## Intuition [Beginner]

DNA and RNA carry genetic information in the sequence of their nucleotide building blocks. Each **nucleotide** has three parts: a sugar (ribose in RNA, deoxyribose in DNA), a phosphate group, and a nitrogen-containing **base**. The bases are the information-carrying part — there are five of them, abbreviated A, T, G, C, and U.

A **nucleoside** is the sugar-plus-base unit without the phosphate. A **nucleotide** is the nucleoside plus one or more phosphates. When nucleotides link together through phosphodiester bonds between the sugar of one and the phosphate of the next, they form a **polynucleotide** strand. DNA is a double-stranded polynucleotide; RNA is usually single-stranded.

The bases come in two families. **Purines** (adenine, A; guanine, G) have a fused two-ring structure. **Pyrimidines** (cytosine, C; thymine, T; uracil, U) have a single six-membered ring. DNA uses A, T, G, C. RNA uses A, U, G, C — uracil replaces thymine. The difference between thymine and uracil is a single methyl group on thymine.

The critical chemical insight is **Watson-Crick base pairing**. Adenine pairs with thymine (in DNA) or uracil (in RNA) through two hydrogen bonds. Guanine pairs with cytosine through three hydrogen bonds. This pairing is the molecular basis for the double helix: two DNA strands run in opposite directions (antiparallel), held together by A-T and G-C hydrogen bonds between the bases. The sequence of one strand determines the sequence of the other.

## Visual [Beginner]

The DNA double helix looks like a twisted ladder. The sides of the ladder are the sugar-phosphate backbones (identical repeating units). The rungs are the base pairs: A pairs with T (two hydrogen bonds), G pairs with C (three hydrogen bonds).

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Draw the structure of adenosine monophosphate (AMP) and show Watson-Crick base pairing for A-T.**

AMP consists of three parts:

1. **Ribose** — a five-carbon sugar with the formula $\mathrm{C_5H_{10}O_4}$ in its open form, cyclised to a furanose ring. In RNA nucleotides the 2'-position has an OH group; in DNA (deoxy) it has only H.

2. **Adenine** — a purine base attached to the ribose via a beta-N-glycosidic bond from N9 of adenine to C1' of the ribose.

3. **Phosphate** — one phosphate group esterified to the 5'-OH of the ribose, forming the 5'-monophosphate.

The full structure: adenine-N9-C1'-ribose-O5'-phosphate.

**A-T base pairing.** Adenine (A) in DNA pairs with thymine (T) through two hydrogen bonds:

- The N6 amino group of adenine ($\mathrm{-NH_2}$) donates a hydrogen bond to the O4 carbonyl oxygen of thymine.
- The N1 nitrogen of adenine accepts a hydrogen bond from the N3 hydrogen of thymine.

The geometry places the two bases in a coplanar arrangement with the hydrogen bonds holding them at a fixed distance, maintaining the uniform width of the double helix regardless of which base pair occupies a given position.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **nucleoside** is an $N$-glycoside formed by the attachment of a purine or pyrimidine base to the anomeric carbon (C1') of a pentose sugar through a beta-glycosidic linkage. For purines the glycosidic bond is N9-C1'; for pyrimidines it is N1-C1'.

A **nucleotide** is a nucleoside phosphate — a phosphoric acid ester of a nucleoside. Monophosphates (AMP, GMP, CMP, UMP, TMP) have one phosphate; diphosphates (ADP, GDP) have two; triphosphates (ATP, GTP) have three in an anhydride chain. The high-energy phosphate bonds of ATP ($\Delta G^\circ$ of hydrolysis $\approx -30.5\,\mathrm{kJ/mol}$) are the universal energy currency of the cell.

**Phosphodiester bond.** In polynucleotides, the phosphate links the 3'-OH of one nucleotide's sugar to the 5'-OH of the next, forming a 3',5'-phosphodiester bond. The backbone has a repeating charge of -1 per phosphate at physiological pH, making nucleic acids polyanions. The directionality is always specified: strand polarity runs 5' to 3'.

**Watson-Crick base pairing.** In the B-form DNA double helix (the most common form under physiological conditions):

- A-T: two hydrogen bonds. Donor-acceptor pairs: (N6-H of A) to (O4 of T), and (N3-H of T) to (N1 of A).
- G-C: three hydrogen bonds. Donor-acceptor pairs: (N1-H of G) to (N3 of C), (N2-H of G) to (O2 of C), and (O6 of G) to (N4-H of C).

**Double-helix geometry (B-form).** 10 base pairs per turn, pitch 3.4 nm, helix diameter 2.0 nm. Major groove (~2.2 nm wide) and minor groove (~1.2 nm wide) provide distinct chemical environments for protein-DNA recognition. The backbone follows a right-handed helical path with the base pairs stacked perpendicular to the helix axis.

**Chemical stability.** RNA is susceptible to base-catalysed hydrolysis at the phosphodiester bond because the 2'-OH can act as an intramolecular nucleophile, attacking the adjacent phosphate and cleaving the backbone. DNA lacks the 2'-OH and is resistant to this mechanism, which is why DNA is the stable long-term genetic storage molecule and RNA serves shorter-lived roles (messenger, catalytic, regulatory).

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **RNA is not just single-stranded.** RNA molecules fold into complex secondary and tertiary structures through intramolecular base pairing, forming stem-loops, hairpins, pseudoknots, and catalytic active sites (ribozymes).

- **Not all base pairing is Watson-Crick.** Wobble base pairs (G-U in RNA), Hoogsteen pairs, and base triples occur in RNA structures and in DNA under certain conditions. The genetic code's degeneracy at the third codon position is mediated by wobble pairing.

- **DNA has three common helical forms.** B-DNA (right-handed, physiological conditions), A-DNA (right-handed, dehydrated conditions, also adopted by RNA duplexes), and Z-DNA (left-handed, GC-rich sequences under high salt).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Proposition (Chargaff's rules and complementarity).** *In double-stranded DNA, the molar amount of adenine equals thymine ($[A] = [T]$), and the molar amount of guanine equals cytosine ($[G] = [C]$). This follows from Watson-Crick base pairing.*

**Proof.** Watson-Crick pairing pairs every A with a T and every G with a C. In a double-stranded DNA molecule, every base on one strand is paired with a complementary base on the other strand. Therefore the total number of A residues equals the total number of T residues (each A-T pair contributes one A and one T), and the total number of G residues equals the total number of C residues (each G-C pair contributes one G and one C). This gives $[A] = [T]$ and $[G] = [C]$. A further consequence: the sum $[A] + [G] = [T] + [C]$, i.e., total purines equal total pyrimidines. $\blacksquare$

Chargaff discovered these ratios experimentally in 1950; Watson and Crick explained them structurally in 1953.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, short-answer).**

Explain why RNA is chemically less stable than DNA, including the specific mechanism by which RNA undergoes base-catalysed hydrolysis.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

RNA has a 2'-OH that DNA lacks. What intramolecular reaction does this enable?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

RNA's 2'-OH acts as an intramolecular nucleophile. Under basic conditions, the 2'-O$^-$ attacks the adjacent phosphorus atom of the phosphodiester bond, forming a 2',3'-cyclic phosphate intermediate and cleaving the bond to the 5'-oxygen. The cyclic phosphate is then hydrolysed to either the 2'- or 3'-monophosphate. DNA lacks the 2'-OH and cannot undergo this intramolecular cleavage, making its backbone resistant to base-catalysed hydrolysis.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Describe the structural difference between the B-form and A-form of DNA. Under what conditions does each form predominate?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

B-form is the standard physiological helix. A-form occurs under dehydrating conditions and is the default for RNA duplexes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

B-DNA has 10 base pairs per turn, a pitch of 3.4 nm, base pairs nearly perpendicular to the helix axis, and a wide major groove suitable for protein binding. It predominates under physiological conditions (high water activity).

A-DNA has 11 base pairs per turn, a pitch of 2.8 nm, base pairs tilted ~20 degrees from perpendicular, and a narrower, deeper major groove. It forms under dehydrating conditions (low water activity, high salt or ethanol). RNA duplexes default to A-form because the 2'-OH sterically prevents the C2'-endo sugar pucker required for B-form; RNA adopts C3'-endo pucker, which produces A-form geometry.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, short-answer).**

The DNA oligonucleotide 5'-CGCGAATTCGCG-3' has been studied by X-ray crystallography (the "Dickerson dodecamer"). Explain why this particular sequence was chosen and what the crystal structure revealed about the structure of DNA that was not predicted by the original Watson-Crick model.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

This sequence is palindromic (self-complementary). What happens to the uniform helix model when you look at real DNA at atomic resolution?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The sequence is palindromic (its complement reads the same 5' to 3'), allowing it to form a self-complementary duplex from a single strand. This makes crystallisation straightforward because every molecule in the crystal has the same sequence.

The Dickerson dodecamer crystal structure (1979) revealed that B-DNA is not the uniform, regular helix predicted by the Watson-Crick model. Instead, it shows **base-pair propeller twist** (base pairs are not perfectly planar but twisted like a propeller), **helical parameter variation** (twist, roll, and slide vary from base pair to base pair depending on sequence context), and a **narrow minor groove** at AT-rich regions with a "spine of hydration" — a string of ordered water molecules that stabilise the narrow groove. These sequence-dependent structural variations are the basis for indirect readout in protein-DNA recognition: proteins sense the shape and flexibility of the DNA helix, not just the pattern of hydrogen-bond donors and acceptors in the grooves.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Discuss the chemical basis for the specificity of Watson-Crick base pairing. Why does A not pair with G under normal conditions?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider the hydrogen-bond donor/acceptor pattern on each base. What happens when you try to pair two purines?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Watson-Crick pairing works because the hydrogen-bond donor and acceptor patterns on each face are complementary. A presents a donor (N6-H) and an acceptor (N1) in the right geometry for T to present a complementary acceptor (O4) and donor (N3-H). G presents two donors (N1-H, N2-H) and one acceptor (O6) in the geometry matching C's two acceptors (N3, O2) and one donor (N4-H).

A-G pairing fails for two reasons. First, a purine-purine pair is too wide to fit within the helix without distorting the backbone — two fused-ring bases side by side exceed the uniform helix width. Second, the hydrogen-bond donor/acceptor pattern is mismatched: A has one donor and one acceptor on the Watson-Crick face, while G has two donors and one acceptor. There is no way to pair all donors with acceptors simultaneously in a geometry that fits the helix. In contrast, A-T (one purine, one pyrimidine) and G-C (one purine, one pyrimidine) both maintain uniform helix width and have perfectly complementary hydrogen-bond patterns.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Describe the mechanism by which ATP hydrolysis releases free energy, and explain why the phosphoanhydride bonds in ATP are termed "high-energy."

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The $\Delta G$ of ATP hydrolysis is about -30.5 kJ/mol. Consider resonance stabilisation, electrostatic repulsion, and solvation effects of the products versus reactants.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

ATP hydrolysis ($\mathrm{ATP + H_2O \to ADP + P_i}$) has $\Delta G^\circ \approx -30.5\,\mathrm{kJ/mol}$ under standard conditions, and up to $-50\,\mathrm{kJ/mol}$ under cellular conditions (where [ATP] is high and [ADP], [Pi] are low). The negative $\Delta G$ arises from three factors:

1. **Resonance stabilisation of the products.** The inorganic phosphate $P_i$ (as $\mathrm{HPO_4^{2-}}$) has four equivalent resonance structures spreading the negative charge over four oxygens. The terminal phosphate on ATP has fewer resonance forms because one oxygen is bridged.

2. **Electrostatic repulsion.** ATP carries four negative charges (at physiological pH) crowded on the triphosphate chain. Hydrolysis relieves this repulsion by separating the charges onto ADP and Pi.

3. **Solvation.** The separated ADP and Pi are better solvated than the intact ATP, because water can interact with the newly exposed charges.

The term "high-energy bond" is a misnomer — it does not mean the bond itself is unusually strong. It means the overall reaction releasing that bond is strongly exergonic due to the factors above. The phosphoanhydride bonds are actually weaker than typical P-O bonds because of the electrostatic destabilisation described in factor 2.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

The enzyme ribozyme catalyses RNA cleavage without any protein component. Describe the chemical mechanism by which the hammerhead ribozyme cleaves its substrate RNA strand.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The hammerhead ribozyme uses a specific nucleotide in its active site to facilitate the same 2'-OH nucleophilic attack that occurs in base-catalysed RNA hydrolysis, but with sequence specificity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The hammerhead ribozyme folds into a three-helix junction that brings the cleavage site into proximity with a conserved guanine residue. The mechanism involves:

1. The 2'-OH of the ribose at the cleavage site is deprotonated, acting as a nucleophile. In the ribozyme active site, a conserved guanine (G12 in the minimal ribozyme) acts as a general base, abstracting the proton from the 2'-OH.

2. The resulting 2'-O$^-$ attacks the adjacent phosphorus atom, forming a pentacoordinate transition state.

3. A conserved second residue (often a divalent metal ion or another nucleobase) acts as a general acid, donating a proton to the 5'-oxyanion leaving group as the P-O5' bond breaks.

4. The products are a 2',3'-cyclic phosphate on the upstream fragment and a 5'-OH on the downstream fragment.

The ribozyme provides both the general base (G12) and general acid functions that in solution would require high pH. It achieves rate enhancements of ~$10^6$ over uncatalysed RNA cleavage, demonstrating that RNA itself has catalytic capability — a central observation for the "RNA world" hypothesis of the origin of life.

</details>

</aside>

## Nucleic acid analogues and therapeutic applications [Master]

Modified nucleic acids are central to modern pharmacology. **Nucleoside analogues** (acyclovir, azidothymidine/AZT, remdesivir) mimic natural nucleosides and are incorporated into growing DNA or RNA strands by viral polymerases, causing chain termination. The key design principle: the analogue must be recognised by the viral polymerase (to be incorporated) but should lack the 3'-OH (to prevent further extension) or should distort the growing strand.

**Antisense oligonucleotides** (ASOs) are short synthetic DNA strands (15–25 nucleotides) that bind to complementary mRNA by Watson-Crick pairing and trigger its degradation by RNase H, or sterically block its translation. Chemical modifications (phosphorothioate backbone, 2'-O-methyl or 2'-O-methoxyethyl ribose) increase nuclease resistance and binding affinity.

**CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing** exploits a guide RNA (gRNA) that directs the Cas9 nuclease to a complementary DNA sequence. The gRNA-DNA base pairing provides the specificity; the Cas9 protein provides the catalytic activity (double-strand break). The system is a direct application of nucleic acid base-pairing chemistry to genome engineering.

## Connections [Master]

- **Amino acids and protein chemistry [15.12.01]** — nucleic acid-protein interactions (DNA-binding proteins, transcription factors, histones) depend on the hydrogen-bonding and electrostatic properties of both nucleotide bases and amino acid side chains.

- **Enzyme mechanism [15.14.01]** — DNA polymerases, RNA polymerases, and ribozymes are enzyme-catalysed (or RNA-catalysed) reactions that operate on nucleic acid substrates. The phosphodiester bond formation mechanism shares features with peptide bond formation.

- **Retrosynthetic analysis [15.10.01]** — automated DNA synthesis (phosphoramidite chemistry) is a solid-phase synthetic method analogous to SPPS, using protecting groups and sequential coupling steps.

- **NMR spectroscopy [15.11.01]** — nucleic acid NMR (imino proton region, 10–15 ppm) characterises base-pairing and secondary structure in solution.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Miescher discovered DNA in 1869, calling it "nuclein." Avery, MacLeod, and McCarty demonstrated in 1944 that DNA (not protein) carries genetic information. Chargaff's rules (1950) established the base-composition regularities. Watson and Crick proposed the double helix in 1953, drawing on Franklin's X-ray diffraction data. Nirenberg and Khorana cracked the genetic code in the 1960s. Sanger and Gilbert developed DNA sequencing methods (Nobel Prize, 1980). Mullis invented PCR (Nobel Prize, 1993). Doudna and Charpentier developed CRISPR-Cas9 gene editing (Nobel Prize, 2020).

The philosophical significance: DNA is the molecule where chemistry becomes information. The linear sequence of four bases encodes three-dimensional protein structures, developmental programs, and heritable traits. The fact that a simple chemical polymer can carry heritable information — and that the information is read, copied, and interpreted by other molecules obeying the same chemical laws — is one of the most consequential discoveries in the history of science.
