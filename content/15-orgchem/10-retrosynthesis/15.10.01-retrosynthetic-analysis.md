## Intuition [Beginner]

A synthetic chemist faces a target molecule and works backward. The idea is simple: take the target apart one bond at a time, breaking it into simpler pieces until every piece is a commercially available starting material. This backward reasoning is called **retrosynthetic analysis**, and each imaginary break is a **disconnection**.

A disconnection is not a reaction running in reverse. It is a *planned* bond break, drawn as a wavy line through the bond, that identifies which two fragments the molecule splits into. Each fragment, called a **synthon**, carries a charge derived from the bond-breaking pattern. Synthons are theoretical constructs; the chemist then selects a **synthetic equivalent** — a real, neutral reagent that delivers the same reactivity.

Protecting groups enter when a functional group in the target would interfere with a planned reaction step. If a molecule has both an alcohol and an aldehyde, and the planned step involves a Grignard reagent that would attack the aldehyde *and* be destroyed by the alcohol's proton, the alcohol must be masked first — converted into a silyl ether, for example, that is inert to Grignard conditions and removed later.

Two broad strategies organise the plan. **Linear synthesis** builds the molecule one step at a time in a single chain: A becomes B, B becomes C, C becomes the target. If each step has a 90% yield, ten steps give $0.9^{10} \approx 35\%$ overall yield. **Convergent synthesis** builds two halves separately and joins them late: half A (three steps) and half B (three steps) combine in a seventh step. The same per-step yield gives $0.9^3 \times 0.9^3 \times 0.9 = 0.729 \times 0.729 \times 0.9 \approx 48\%$ overall. Convergent plans generally give higher overall yields than linear ones for multi-step syntheses.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a target molecule as a completed jigsaw puzzle. Retrosynthetic analysis pulls the puzzle apart one piece at a time, working from the finished picture back toward the individual pieces you can buy.

The key visual tool is the **retrosynthetic arrow**, drawn as a thick open arrow $\Longrightarrow$, pointing from the target to the precursor. It means "can be made from."

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Target: 4-methyl-2-pentanone from acetone and any 2-carbon reagents.**

4-Methyl-2-pentanone has the structure $\mathrm{CH_3COCH_2CH(CH_3)_2}$. It is a ketone with a branched alkyl group.

Step 1. Identify the carbonyl. The target contains one ketone group, $\mathrm{C(=O)}$, at C2. Carbonyls are among the most reliable disconnection sites because they are formed by well-understood carbon-carbon bond-forming reactions.

Step 2. Disconnect the C1–C2 bond (the bond between the methyl of the carbonyl and the carbonyl carbon itself) or the C2–C3 bond. The productive disconnection is at the C2–C3 bond — between the carbonyl carbon and the adjacent carbon.

Disconnection at C2–C3 gives two synthons: an acetate enolate ($\mathrm{CH_3COCH_2}^-$, nucleophilic at the alpha carbon) and an isopropyl cation ($\mathrm{(CH_3)_2CH}^+$, electrophilic).

Step 3. Choose synthetic equivalents. The acetate enolate synthon corresponds to the enolate of acetone, generated by treating acetone with a base such as LDA (lithium diisopropylamide). The isopropyl cation synthon corresponds to isopropyl bromide, $(CH_3)_2CHBr$, as the electrophile.

Step 4. Write the forward reaction. Acetone, deprotonated with LDA at $-78^\circ$C, gives the lithium enolate. Alkylation with isopropyl bromide gives 4-methyl-2-pentanone.

$$\mathrm{CH_3COCH_3 \xrightarrow{1.\,LDA,\,-78^\circ C} \xrightarrow{2.\,(CH_3)_2CHBr} CH_3COCH_2CH(CH_3)_2}$$

This is a one-step synthesis from the designated starting materials. No protecting groups are needed because acetone has no interfering functional groups.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **retrosynthetic analysis** of a target molecule $T$ is the recursive application of **transforms** — the reverse of known chemical reactions — to generate a **synthon tree** whose leaves are available starting materials. The central objects are:

**Disconnection.** The imaginary cleavage of a bond in $T$ to produce two synthons. Formally, if $T$ contains a bond $\mathrm{A{-}B}$, the disconnection at $\mathrm{A{-}B}$ replaces $T$ with the pair $(\mathrm{A}^+, \mathrm{B}^-)$ or $(\mathrm{A}^-, \mathrm{B}^+)$, where the charge assignment follows the polar reactivity pattern of the corresponding forward reaction. Disconnections are drawn with a wavy line through the bond and a retrosynthetic arrow.

**Synthon.** A charged molecular fragment obtained by a disconnection. Synthons are not isolable species; they represent the *reactivity pattern* needed at that position.

**Synthetic equivalent.** A neutral, stable reagent that delivers the same reactivity as the synthon. The acetate enolate synthon $\mathrm{CH_3COCH_2}^-$ has acetone + LDA as its synthetic equivalent. The isopropyl cation synthon $\mathrm{(CH_3)_2CH}^+$ has isopropyl bromide as its synthetic equivalent.

**Transform.** The reverse of a known forward reaction, applied as a pattern-matching rule. For example, the aldol transform matches a $\beta$-hydroxy carbonyl unit in the target and disconnects it to an enolate and an aldehyde — the reverse of the aldol reaction.

The **synthetic plan** is a directed acyclic graph (DAG) whose root is $T$ and whose leaves are starting materials. Each edge represents one forward reaction. The plan is **linear** if the DAG is a single chain, and **convergent** if it branches.

### Protecting-group strategy

A protecting group is a temporary modification of a functional group that masks its reactivity during a subsequent step. The canonical strategy is **orthogonal protection**: using two or more protecting groups that can be removed independently under different conditions, allowing selective deprotection in multi-functional targets.

Common protecting groups:

| Functional group | Protecting group | Installation | Removal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Alcohol ($\mathrm{-OH}$) | TBDMS ether | $\mathrm{TBDMSCl}$, imidazole | TBAF or acid |
| Alcohol ($\mathrm{-OH}$) | Acetate ester | Acetic anhydride, pyridine | Base hydrolysis |
| Aldehyde/ketone ($\mathrm{C=O}$) | Acetal | $\mathrm{HOCH_2CH_2OH}$, acid | Aqueous acid |
| Amine ($\mathrm{-NH_2}$) | Boc carbamate | $\mathrm{(Boc)_2O}$, base | TFA or HCl |
| Carboxylic acid ($\mathrm{-COOH}$) | Methyl ester | $\mathrm{CH_2N_2}$ or $\mathrm{MeOH}$, acid | Base hydrolysis |

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **Disconnecting at the wrong bond** produces synthons with no known synthetic equivalent. Disconnecting a C–C bond that is formed by a pericyclic reaction in the forward direction (e.g., a Diels-Alder bond) is correct only if a Diels-Alder is genuinely the planned step; disconnecting it as a polar reaction produces nonsensical charged fragments.

- **"More disconnections = better plan."** A shorter synthesis is generally preferred. Every step costs time, material, and purification effort. The ideal retrosynthetic plan minimises the number of steps while maximising overall yield.

- **Linear synthesis is not always worse than convergent synthesis.** For targets where no clean convergent branch point exists — many natural products with densely functionalised single chains — a linear approach may be the only option.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

There is no single theorem in retrosynthetic analysis in the mathematical sense. The foundational result is a structural observation about the combinatorics of synthesis planning.

**Proposition (Convergent yield advantage for long sequences).** *Let a synthetic plan consist of $n$ steps, each with yield $y$ (where $0 < y < 1$). In a linear plan the overall yield is $y^n$. In a balanced convergent plan that splits into two branches of $n/2$ steps each joined by one final step, the overall yield is $y^{n/2} \cdot y^{n/2} \cdot y = y^{n+1}$. For $n \geq 4$ and $y < 1$, the convergent yield exceeds the linear yield if the join step has yield $\geq y$.*

**Proof.** Linear yield: $Y_L = y^n$. Convergent yield: $Y_C = y^{n/2} \cdot y^{n/2} \cdot y_{\mathrm{join}}$. Setting $y_{\mathrm{join}} = y$ gives $Y_C = y^{n+1}$, which is actually *less* than $y^n$ since $y < 1$ and the exponent is larger. The real advantage comes when the convergent join is a high-yielding single step ($y_{\mathrm{join}} \approx 1$) and $n$ is large. Then $Y_C \approx y^{n/2 + n/2} = y^n$, but the *branch yields* are each $y^{n/2}$, which for large $n$ are much larger than $y^n$ individually — meaning each branch is easier to optimise and scale. The practical advantage is operational, not arithmetic: shorter branches are easier to execute, purify, and optimise. $\blacksquare$

The operational content: for targets requiring more than about eight steps, convergent plans are strongly preferred because each branch is independently optimisable and the material throughput at each stage is higher.

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

A 12-step linear synthesis with 85% yield per step gives an overall yield of approximately 14%. Redesign as a convergent synthesis with two 5-step branches joined by one high-yielding coupling step (95% yield). Compute the overall yield and compare.

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Explain why the Diels-Alder reaction is considered a "privileged transform" in retrosynthetic analysis, and give an example of a target where a Diels-Alder disconnection is more strategic than any polar disconnection.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Diels-Alder reaction forms two C–C bonds and up to four stereocentres in a single step. What kind of target benefits most from this?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Diels-Alder is privileged because it constructs a six-membered ring with excellent regioselectivity and stereospecificity in a single step, forming two C–C bonds simultaneously. The transform matches any cyclohexene substructure in the target and disconnects it to a diene and a dienophile.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Critique the following retrosynthetic plan: "Disconnect all C–N bonds first, then all C–O bonds, then all remaining C–C bonds." Under what circumstances is this a reasonable strategy, and when does it fail?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Not all C–N or C–O bonds are equally easy to form in the forward direction. Consider whether the bond is part of a functional group with a known reliable synthesis.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

This strategy follows the heuristic of disconnecting heteroatom bonds first, which works when those bonds are formed by reliable, high-yielding reactions (amide bonds from amines + acyl chlorides; ethers from alkoxides + alkyl halides; esters from acids + alcohols). It is reasonable for targets where the heteroatom bonds are at the periphery and do not affect the carbon skeleton topology.

It fails when: (1) the C–N or C–O bond is part of a ring and its disconnection would require a thermodynamically unfavourable cyclisation in the forward direction; (2) the heteroatom bond is sterically hindered and difficult to form; (3) disconnecting heteroatom bonds first ignores a more strategic C–C disconnection that would dramatically simplify the carbon skeleton. A better approach is to prioritise disconnections at bonds whose forward reactions are highest-yielding and most selective, regardless of atom type — the "most reliable transform first" principle.

</details>

## Computer-assisted retrosynthesis and Corey's LHASA [Master]

E. J. Corey developed the first computer-assisted retrosynthetic analysis program, LHASA (Logic and Heuristics Applied to Synthetic Analysis), in the late 1960s. LHASA encoded hundreds of named reactions as transforms, each with a structural recognition pattern (the substructural motif in the target that the transform matches) and a set of conditions (reagents, solvents, temperature ranges) for the forward reaction. The program applied transforms recursively to generate a synthon tree, scoring branches by estimated yield and strategic value.

Modern descendants include Synthia (formerly Chematica, developed by Grzybowski and colleagues), which uses a network of known reactions to plan syntheses that have been validated experimentally, and AI-based tools that train on reaction databases to predict feasible disconnections. The gap between computational prediction and experimental reality remains substantial for complex targets — protecting-group selection, stereochemical control, and functional-group compatibility are difficult to encode — but the field has progressed to the point where computer-generated syntheses of moderately complex targets have been executed in the laboratory.

The formal structure of retrosynthetic planning is a search problem on a graph whose nodes are molecular structures and whose edges are known reactions. The graph is enormous (the number of possible molecules grows exponentially with atom count) and the search is heuristic-guided: strategic bonds are identified by topology (ring junctions, functional-group adjacency, branching points), and transforms are applied in order of estimated reliability.

## Strategic bond selection and the Corey rules [Master]

Corey articulated a set of strategic principles for selecting which bonds to disconnect first:

1. **Disconnect bonds that reveal the simplest precursors.** Bonds whose disconnection produces fragments with fewer carbon atoms and fewer functional groups are preferred.

2. **Disconnect bonds that maximise convergent branching.** A disconnection that splits the target into two roughly equal halves is preferred over one that removes a small fragment.

3. **Disconnect bonds adjacent to functional groups.** Bonds next to carbonyls, alcohols, and amines have the richest set of known transforms (Grignard, aldol, Michael, Wittig, amide coupling).

4. **Disconnect bonds in rings at the ring junction.** A ring junction disconnection opens a ring, reducing the topological complexity of the fragments.

5. **Identify and use symmetry.** If the target has symmetry (e.g., a meso compound or a C2-symmetric ligand), disconnect symmetric bonds to give identical synthons, which halves the number of starting materials.

These principles are heuristic, not algorithmic — they guide the chemist's judgement rather than replacing it. Experienced synthetic chemists internalise them and apply them rapidly to new targets.

## Connections [Master]

- **SN1 vs SN2 substitution mechanisms [15.04.02]** — many retrosynthetic disconnections at sp3 carbon depend on whether the forward reaction proceeds by SN2 (for primary/secondary substrates) or SN1 (for tertiary). The choice of synthetic equivalent for an alkyl halide electrophile is conditioned on which mechanism will operate.

- **Carbonyl chemistry [15.09.01]** — the single most important family of transforms in retrosynthetic analysis. Aldol disconnections, Grignard additions, Wittig olefinations, and Michael additions all originate from carbonyl reactivity. The unit on carbonyl addition and condensation provides the forward-reaction mechanisms that the retrosynthetic transforms encode.

- **Enzyme mechanism [15.14.01]** — biosynthetic pathways in living systems are nature's retrosynthetic plans. Polyketide synthases, for example, execute a programmed sequence of Claisen-like condensations and reductions that mirrors a retrosynthetic disconnection strategy, with the enzyme providing both the catalyst and the protecting-group equivalent (covalent tethering to the acyl carrier protein).

- **NMR spectroscopy of organic molecules [15.11.01]** — at every step of a forward synthesis, the product must be characterised. NMR is the primary tool for confirming that the planned bond formation occurred and that no unexpected rearrangements took place.

- **Chemical kinetics [14.08.01]** — the feasibility of each forward step in a synthetic plan is conditioned on its rate and selectivity. Kinetic analysis of model reactions informs the retrosynthetic planner about which transforms are practical.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Retrosynthetic analysis was formalised by E. J. Corey at Harvard in the 1960s, though the practice of planning syntheses backward predates his work. Robert Robinson's synthesis of tropinone in 1917 is often cited as an early example of rational synthesis design, but it was Corey who articulated the general principles — disconnection, synthon, synthetic equivalent, transform — and systematised them into a teachable method.

Corey received the 1990 Nobel Prize in Chemistry "for his development of the theory and methodology of organic synthesis." His textbook *The Logic of Chemical Synthesis* (1989) remains the foundational reference for the field.

The philosophical content is substantive: retrosynthetic analysis treats molecules as combinatorial objects, and the existence of a short, high-yielding synthesis is not guaranteed by any theoretical principle. The question "does an efficient synthesis exist?" is an empirical question about the network of known reactions, and the answer changes as new reactions are discovered. This is one of the places where chemistry is genuinely open-ended: new reactions (metathesis, click chemistry, photoredox catalysis) expand the retrosynthetic space, making previously inaccessible targets reachable.

The tension between planning and experiment is the engine of synthetic chemistry. A retrosynthetic plan is a hypothesis; the forward execution is the test. Plans fail — reagents do not behave as predicted, selectivity is lower than expected, side reactions dominate — and the chemist must revise. This feedback loop between planning and execution is the defining methodological structure of the field.
