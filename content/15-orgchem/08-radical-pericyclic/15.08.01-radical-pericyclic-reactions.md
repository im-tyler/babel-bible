## Intuition [Beginner]

Most reactions you have studied proceed through ionic or polar mechanisms -- nucleophiles attack electrophiles, bonds break heterolytically, and charges move in pairs. Radical and pericyclic reactions break this pattern. Radicals are neutral species with a single unpaired electron. They react by moving single electrons rather than pairs. Pericyclic reactions are different again: they occur through a cyclic redistribution of bonding electrons in a single concerted step, with no intermediate at all.

Radical reactions are everywhere. Combustion is a radical chain reaction. The halogenation of alkanes proceeds through radicals. Polymerization of ethylene to make polyethylene is radical-initiated. Antioxidants work by intercepting radicals before they can damage biomolecules.

Pericyclic reactions are among the most elegant in all of organic chemistry. The Diels-Alder reaction builds six-membered rings in one step from a diene and a dienophile. Electrocyclic reactions open or close rings with precise stereochemical control. Sigmatropic rearrangements shift hydrogen atoms or groups across conjugated systems. What unites them all is the Woodward-Hoffmann rules, which predict which pericyclic reactions are allowed and which are forbidden, based purely on orbital symmetry.

## Visual [Beginner]

```
   Radical chain mechanism (chlorination of methane):

   Initiation:
   Cl-Cl  --hv-->  2 Cl*

   Propagation:
   Cl* + CH4  -->  HCl + *CH3
   *CH3 + Cl2  -->  CH3Cl + Cl*
   (Cl* regenerated -- chain continues)

   Termination:
   Cl* + Cl*      -->  Cl2
   *CH3 + Cl*     -->  CH3Cl
   *CH3 + *CH3    -->  CH3-CH3


   Diels-Alder reaction (a [4+2] cycloaddition):

         CH2                       CH2
        /    \                    /    \
   CH2        CH2  +  CH=CH2 --> CH     CH--CH2-CH2
        \    /                    \    /
         CH2                       CH


   Frontier orbital picture:

   Diene HOMO (4 pi electrons):        Dienophile LUMO (2 pi electrons):

   +  -  +  -                         -  +  (empty)
   |_|_|_|_|                          |_|_|

   Symmetry match: bonding interaction at both
   new sigma bond-forming sites simultaneously.
   This is a thermally allowed [4+2] cycloaddition
   under the Woodward-Hoffmann rules.
```

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Problem:** Predict the major monobromination product when propane is treated with Br2 under light. Explain the selectivity.

**Solution:**

The radical chain mechanism produces bromine radicals by homolysis of Br2. Propagation steps abstract a hydrogen from propane, generating a propyl radical, which then reacts with Br2 to form the alkyl bromide.

Propane has two types of hydrogen: primary (6H on the terminal carbons) and secondary (2H on the central carbon). The relevant bond dissociation energies and radical stabilities favor the secondary radical because it is more stable than the primary radical by hyperconjugation.

At 125 degrees C, the relative reactivity per hydrogen atom in bromination is approximately:

primary H : secondary H = 1 : 82

Expected product ratio:

1-bromopropane (primary): 6 x 1 = 6
2-bromopropane (secondary): 2 x 82 = 164

Fraction of 2-bromopropane = 164 / (6 + 164) = 0.965

2-bromopropane is the major product by a wide margin. Bromination is highly selective (unlike chlorination, which is much less selective because the chlorine radical is more reactive and less discriminating).

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Radical stability.** Radical stability increases with substitution and delocalization:

methyl < primary < secondary < tertiary < allylic approximately equals benzylic

This order reflects hyperconjugation (for alkyl radicals) and resonance delocalization (for allylic and benzylic radicals). Bond dissociation energies (BDEs) quantify this: the C-H BDE for a tertiary carbon (~ 96 kcal/mol) is lower than for a primary carbon (~ 101 kcal/mol), meaning less energy is required to form the more stable radical.

**Radical chain kinetics.** For a chain reaction with initiation rate R_i, propagation rate constants k_p and k_p', and termination rate constant k_t, the steady-state rate of product formation is:

rate = k_p [R*] [substrate]

where [R*] is determined by the steady-state approximation:

d[R*]/dt = R_i - 2 k_t [R*]^2 = 0

giving [R*] = (R_i / 2 k_t)^(1/2). The chain length (propagations per initiation) is:

chain length = k_p [substrate] / (2 k_t R_i)^(1/2)

**Pericyclic reaction classification.** Pericyclic reactions are classified by the number of electrons involved and the topology of bond reorganization:

1. **Cycloadditions** [m+n]: Two components with m and n pi electrons combine to form a ring. The Diels-Alder reaction is a [4+2] cycloaddition. A [2+2] cycloaddition is thermally forbidden but photochemically allowed.

2. **Electrocyclic reactions:** A single conjugated system opens to or closes from a ring. The stereochemical outcome depends on whether the reaction is thermal or photochemical and on the number of pi electrons. For a thermal reaction with 4n pi electrons, ring closure is conrotatory; for 4n+2 pi electrons, it is disrotatory.

3. **Sigmatropic rearrangements** [m,n]: A sigma bond migrates across a conjugated system. The [3,3]-sigmatropic rearrangements (Cope and Claisen) are the most synthetically important.

**Woodward-Hoffmann rules.** A pericyclic reaction is thermally allowed if the total number of (4q+2) suprafacial components plus (4r) antarafacial components is odd. In practice:

- Thermal [4+2] cycloaddition (Diels-Alder): allowed (suprafacial on both components)
- Thermal [2+2] cycloaddition: forbidden (suprafacial-suprafacial); allowed if one component reacts antarafacially (geometrically difficult for small systems)
- Photochemical [2+2]: allowed (one component is excited, inverting the selection rule)

**Frontier molecular orbital (FMO) analysis.** The Woodward-Hoffmann rules can be understood through orbital symmetry conservation. In a Diels-Alder reaction, the HOMO of the diene and the LUMO of the dienophile have matching symmetry at both reacting termini, allowing constructive overlap and bond formation. For a thermal [2+2] reaction between two ethylenes, the HOMO-LUMO interactions have one bonding and one antibonding overlap, making the reaction symmetry-forbidden.

## Key results [Intermediate+]

1. **Hammond's postulate applied to radical selectivity.** Hydrogen abstraction by a reactive radical (Cl*) has an early transition state resembling the reactants, so stability differences in the product radical barely affect the barrier. This is why chlorination is unselective. Bromination has a late transition state (Br* is less reactive), so radical stability strongly affects the barrier, giving high selectivity. This is a direct application of the Hammond postulate and the reactivity-selectivity principle.

2. **Endo rule for Diels-Alder reactions.** When the dienophile bears electron-withdrawing substituents, the endo transition state is favored over the exo. In the endo TS, secondary (non-bond-forming) orbital interactions between the diene and the dienophile substituents stabilize the TS. The endo product is the kinetic product; the exo is typically more thermodynamically stable. This is the Alder endo rule.

3. **Cope rearrangement.** The [3,3]-sigmatropic rearrangement of 1,5-dienes proceeds through a chair-like or boat-like transition state. The chair TS is favored by 5--6 kcal/mol. For 3,3-dimethyl-1,5-hexadiene, the rearrangement is degenerate (the product is identical to the reactant) and proceeds with Delta G double dagger approximately 33 kcal/mol, making it observable at 200--300 degrees C. Substituent effects can dramatically lower the barrier: oxy-Cope rearrangements (with an OH at C-3) proceed at much lower temperatures, especially when the OH is deprotonated (anionic oxy-Cope, rate acceleration of 10^17).

4. **Electrocyclic ring opening of cyclobutenes.** Cyclobutene opens to 1,3-butadiene in a conrotatory fashion under thermal conditions (4 pi electrons) and a disrotatory fashion under photochemical conditions. The stereochemical outcome is predicted by the Woodward-Hoffmann rules and confirmed experimentally: trans-3,4-dimethylcyclobutene gives (E,Z)-2,4-hexadiene thermally, not the (E,E) isomer.

Predict the products (including stereochemistry) of the Diels-Alder reaction between cyclopentadiene and maleic anhydride. Draw the endo and exo transition states and explain why the endo product is kinetically favored. What happens if the reaction is heated for a prolonged period?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Cyclopentadiene is the diene (s-cis conformation locked by the ring). Maleic anhydride is the dienophile with electron-withdrawing carbonyl groups. The endo rule applies: secondary orbital interactions between the carbonyl pi systems and the diene favor the endo TS.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Diels-Alder reaction between cyclopentadiene and maleic anhydride gives a bicyclo[2.2.1]hept-5-ene-2,3-dicarboxylic anhydride (the "endo adduct"). The product is a norbornene derivative with the anhydride bridge endo (pointing toward the double bond).

The endo TS is favored because the carbonyl pi orbitals of maleic anhydride can interact with the developing pi bond in the cyclopentadiene moiety (secondary orbital interactions). In the endo TS, the anhydride carbonyl groups are oriented toward the diene, allowing these stabilizing interactions. In the exo TS, the carbonyls point away from the diene, and no secondary interactions occur. The endo TS is typically 1-2 kcal/mol lower in energy than the exo TS.

The endo product is the kinetic product (lower activation energy). The exo product is the thermodynamic product (lower overall energy because the bulky anhydride group is on the less congested exo face). Prolonged heating causes retro-Diels-Alder of the endo adduct followed by re-cyclization to the exo adduct, eventually equilibrating to the thermodynamic exo product. However, the kinetic preference for endo is strong, and in practice the endo product often predominates even at elevated temperatures because the retro-DA/re-DA equilibrium is slow.

</details>

**Exercise (hard, symbolic).**

The Norrish Type I reaction involves the photochemical alpha-cleavage of a ketone: R-CO-R' + hv -> R* + *CO-R'. The resulting acyl radical can undergo decarbonylation (R'CO* -> R'* + CO) or recombination. Draw the complete mechanism for the Norrish Type I reaction of dibenzyl ketone (PhCH2COCH2Ph) and predict the major products. Explain why this reaction is synthetically useful for generating specific radical intermediates.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Alpha-cleavage occurs at the bond between the carbonyl carbon and the alpha carbon, generating a benzyl radical and a phenylacetyl radical. The phenylacetyl radical can lose CO to give another benzyl radical. The benzyl radicals can then recombine, disproportionate, or abstract hydrogen.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Mechanism:

Step 1: Photoexcitation. PhCH2COCH2Ph + hv -> PhCH2COCH2Ph* (n -> pi* triplet excited state).

Step 2: Alpha-cleavage (Norrish Type I). PhCH2COCH2Ph* -> PhCH2* + *COCH2Ph. The C-C bond alpha to the carbonyl cleaves homolytically, generating a benzyl radical and a phenylacetyl radical. The driving force is the stability of the benzyl radical (resonance with the phenyl ring).

Step 3: Decarbonylation. *COCH2Ph -> PhCH2* + CO. The phenylacetyl radical loses CO because the resulting benzyl radical is highly stabilized by resonance (three resonance structures: the unpaired electron is delocalized into the ring). This step is fast (k ~ 10^7 s^-1 at room temperature).

Step 4: Radical reactions. The benzyl radicals (two equivalents) can:
(a) Recombine: PhCH2* + *CH2Ph -> PhCH2CH2Ph (1,2-diphenylethane, bibenzyl) -- major product.
(b) Disproportionate: PhCH2* + *CH2Ph -> PhCH3 + PhCH=CH2 (toluene + styrene) -- minor product.
(c) Abstract H from solvent or other H-donor: PhCH2* + RH -> PhCH3 + R* -- depends on conditions.

The major product is bibenzyl (1,2-diphenylethane). The Norrish Type I reaction of dibenzyl ketone is a clean source of benzyl radicals. This is synthetically useful because: (1) the reaction is initiated by light, allowing temporal control, (2) the radical intermediates are well-defined and predictable, (3) the byproduct (CO) is inert and easily removed, and (4) the reaction can be used to initiate radical chain processes in synthesis. The reaction is also important in photopolymerization and in the photodegradation of polymers containing carbonyl groups.

</details>

Predict the product and stereochemistry of the thermal [1,5]-sigmatropic hydrogen shift in (Z)-1,3-pentadiene. Draw the transition state and explain why the migration proceeds suprafacially. The Woodward-Hoffmann rule for [1,j]-sigmatropic shifts of hydrogen: thermal suprafacial migration is allowed when j = 4n+3 (odd number of pairs: 1,5,9,...). For [1,5] with 4 electron pairs, the Hückel topology applies and the reaction is thermally allowed suprafacially. <details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

[1,5]-Sigmatropic hydrogen shift in (Z)-1,3-pentadiene:

The hydrogen at C-5 migrates to C-1 suprafacially (on the same face of the pi system). The sigma bond at C-5 breaks and a new sigma bond forms at C-1, while the pi system rearranges from C1=C2-C3=C4-C5H to H-C1=C2-C3=C4-C5 (i.e., the pentadiene inverts from having the terminal double bond at C1-C2 to having it at C4-C5). The product is (E)-1,3-pentadiene (the stereochemistry inverts at the migration terminus because the suprafacial migration places the hydrogen on the same face, but the overall geometry changes).

Actually, more precisely: the [1,5]-sigmatropic shift of (Z)-1,3-pentadiene gives (E)-1,3-pentadiene via suprafacial migration. The transition state involves the hydrogen bridging C-1 and C-5 on the same face of the pentadienyl system. The six-electron (five carbons plus the migrating hydrogen) transition state has Huckel topology with 4n+2 = 6 electrons, which is aromatic and stabilized. This is why the reaction is thermally allowed.

The [1,3]-sigmatropic hydrogen shift would involve four electrons (three carbons plus the migrating hydrogen). For 4 electrons, the Woodward-Hoffmann rules require antarafacial migration for thermal pericyclic reactions. Antarafacial migration means the hydrogen would have to migrate from one face of the pi system to the opposite face. This is geometrically impossible because the hydrogen 1s orbital cannot simultaneously overlap with p-orbitals on opposite faces of the pi system -- the orbital geometry does not allow it. The hydrogen would need to pass through the plane of the molecule, which has no orbital overlap pathway. Therefore, thermal [1,3]-sigmatropic hydrogen shifts do not occur.

This contrast illustrates the power of the Woodward-Hoffmann rules: [1,5] shifts are common in organic chemistry (observed in vitamin D biosynthesis, sigmatropic rearrangements in terpene biosynthesis) while [1,3] shifts are not observed thermally. The same analysis predicts that [1,7] shifts (8 electrons) require antarafacial migration but are geometrically feasible for long conjugated systems (e.g., previtamin D3 to vitamin D3 involves a [1,7]-sigmatropic hydrogen shift).

</details>

</aside>

**Orbital correlation diagrams.** The Woodward-Hoffmann rules were originally derived using orbital correlation diagrams. For a pericyclic reaction, the molecular orbitals of the reactants are correlated with those of the products, respecting the symmetry elements preserved through the reaction. If all occupied orbitals of the reactants correlate with occupied orbitals of the products, the reaction is symmetry-allowed. If an occupied orbital correlates with an unoccupied orbital (or vice versa), there is a symmetry-imposed barrier, and the reaction is thermally forbidden.

For the [4+2] cycloaddition (Diels-Alder), the relevant symmetry element is a mirror plane bisecting the diene and dienophile. The six pi orbitals of the reactant system (four from the diene, two from the dienophile) are classified as symmetric (S) or antisymmetric (A) with respect to this plane. The six product sigma/pi orbitals are similarly classified. Correlation lines connect reactant orbitals to product orbitals of the same symmetry, from lowest to highest energy. All occupied reactant orbitals correlate with occupied product orbitals: the reaction is symmetry-allowed. For the [2+2] cycloaddition of two ethylenes, the analogous construction shows that the highest occupied reactant orbital correlates with an unoccupied product orbital: the reaction is symmetry-forbidden (a thermal symmetry-imposed barrier exists).

**Aromatic transition state theory (Dewar-Zimmerman).** An alternative formulation classifies the pericyclic transition state as aromatic or antiaromatic. A pericyclic TS with (4n+2) electrons in a Huckel topology (all suprafacial) or 4n electrons in a Mobius topology (one antarafacial component) is aromatic and stabilized. The reverse combination is antiaromatic and destabilized. This framework unifies cycloadditions, electrocyclic reactions, and sigmatropic rearrangements under a single principle. The Dewar-Zimmerman approach is computationally convenient: draw the pericyclic array of orbitals, count the phase inversions in the cycle, and apply the Huckel/Mobius rule. Zero or an even number of phase inversions gives Huckel topology; an odd number gives Mobius topology. A Huckel system with 4n+2 electrons or a Mobius system with 4n electrons is aromatic (allowed).

**Detailed electrocyclic reaction analysis.** Electrocyclic reactions provide the most direct illustration of the Woodward-Hoffmann rules. Consider the ring opening of cyclobutene to 1,3-butadiene. The reaction involves four pi electrons. Under thermal conditions, the selection rule for 4n electrons (n=1) requires conrotatory motion: the two terminal CH2 groups rotate in the same direction (both clockwise or both counterclockwise). Under photochemical conditions, disrotatory motion is required (the two groups rotate in opposite directions). The stereochemical consequence is dramatic. Trans-3,4-dimethylcyclobutene opens thermally to give (E,Z)-2,4-hexadiene by conrotatory motion, not the (E,E) or (Z,Z) isomers. This prediction, confirmed by experimental studies, is a direct test of the Woodward-Hoffmann rules.

For the ring closure of 1,3,5-hexatriene (6 pi electrons = 4n+2, n=1), the thermal selection rule reverses: disrotatory closure is allowed and conrotatory is forbidden. Photochemical reversal again applies. The electrocyclic ring closure of vitamin D precursors in the skin (7-dehydrocholesterol to previtamin D3) is a photochemical electrocyclic reaction that proceeds via conrotatory ring opening (6 pi electrons, photochemically allowed).

**Sigmatropic rearrangements in detail.** The [3,3]-sigmatropic rearrangements are the most synthetically important class. The Cope rearrangement converts 1,5-dienes to isomeric 1,5-dienes through a chair-like or boat-like transition state. For 1,5-hexadiene itself, the rearrangement is degenerate (product = reactant) with a barrier of approximately 33 kcal/mol. The chair TS is favored over the boat by 5-6 kcal/mol, analogous to the cyclohexane conformational preference. Substituent effects on the Cope rearrangement are dramatic. An oxygen at C-3 (the oxy-Cope rearrangement) accelerates the reaction because the product enol is stabilized. When the hydroxyl is deprotonated (anionic oxy-Cope), the rate acceleration reaches 10^17, making the rearrangement feasible at room temperature.

The Claisen rearrangement is the oxygen analogue: an allyl vinyl ether rearranges to a gamma,delta-unsaturated carbonyl compound. This is one of the most reliable methods for forming quaternary stereocenters adjacent to a carbonyl. The Eschenmoser-Claisen variant uses an N,N-dimethylacetamide dimethyl acetal and gives amide products; the Johnson-Claisen uses triethyl orthoacetate and gives ester products. These variants allow the Claisen rearrangement to be applied to a wide range of substrates, including those lacking a pre-formed vinyl ether.

The [1,5]-sigmatropic hydrogen shift is common in conjugated dienes and trienes. The thermal [1,5] shift is suprafacial (allowed for 4n+2 electrons), while the [1,3] shift would require antarafacial geometry (forbidden for small rings). This explains why 1,3-pentadiene undergoes thermal [1,5]-hydrogen shift readily but not [1,3]-shift. Vitamin D biosynthesis involves a photochemical [1,7]-sigmatropic hydrogen shift in the conversion of previtamin D3 to vitamin D3.

**Frontier molecular orbital theory applied to cycloadditions.** The FMO approach provides an intuitive and quantitative framework for understanding cycloaddition reactivity. For the normal electron-demand Diels-Alder reaction, the dominant orbital interaction is between the diene HOMO and the dienophile LUMO. The energy gap Delta E = epsilon_LUMO(dienophile) - epsilon_HOMO(diene) determines the rate: smaller gaps give faster reactions. Electron-withdrawing groups on the dienophile lower the LUMO, reducing the gap and accelerating the reaction. Maleic anhydride (LUMO approximately -2.0 eV) is a much more reactive dienophile than ethylene (LUMO approximately +1.5 eV). Electron-donating groups on the diene raise the HOMO, also reducing the gap.

For inverse electron-demand Diels-Alder reactions, the dominant interaction is between the dienophile HOMO and the diene LUMO. Electron-poor dienes (e.g., hexachlorocyclopentadiene) and electron-rich dienophiles (e.g., vinyl ethers) participate in these reactions. The regioselectivity of both normal and inverse-demand Diels-Alder reactions is predictable from the relative magnitudes of the FMO coefficients at the reacting termini.

**Regioselectivity and stereoselectivity in Diels-Alder reactions.** For unsymmetrical dienes and dienophiles, two regioisomeric products are possible ("ortho" and "meta" substitution patterns). FMO theory predicts the favored regioisomer: the larger coefficient on the diene HOMO pairs with the larger coefficient on the dienophile LUMO. Electron-donating groups on the diene raise its HOMO energy; electron-withdrawing groups on the dienophile lower its LUMO energy, accelerating normal-electron-demand Diels-Alder reactions. Inverse-electron-demand Diels-Alder reactions (electron-poor diene, electron-rich dienophile) proceed via diene LUMO-dienophile HOMO interaction.

**Radical clocks.** The rates of known radical rearrangements serve as internal clocks to measure the lifetime of radical intermediates. For example, the ring opening of the cyclopropylmethyl radical proceeds at k = 1.3 x 10^8 s^-1 at 25 degrees C. If a reaction produces this radical and it is trapped by a hydrogen atom donor faster than it rearranges, the radical lifetime is less than 10^-8 s. Radical clocks have been used to determine whether enzymatic C-H hydroxylation (by cytochrome P450) proceeds through a radical "oxygen rebound" mechanism or a concerted insertion. The observation of ring-opened products from cyclopropyl-containing substrates supports the radical pathway.

**Persistent and stable radicals.** Most radicals are transient, but steric protection or extensive delocalization can produce persistent (long-lived) or even stable radicals. TEMPO (2,2,6,6-tetramethylpiperidin-1-oxyl) is a stable nitroxyl radical used as a radical trap, a mediator in nitroxide-mediated polymerization (NMP), and a catalyst for selective alcohol oxidation (TEMPO oxidation). Galvinoxyl, verdazyl, and triphenylmethyl radicals are other examples. The stability of triphenylmethyl radical, discovered by Gomberg in 1900, established the existence of carbon-centered radicals.

**Radical reactions in biology and industry.** Beyond the examples already discussed, radical chemistry pervades both biological systems and industrial processes. In biology, the enzyme ribonucleotide reductase converts ribonucleotides to deoxyribonucleotides (the building blocks of DNA) using a stable tyrosyl radical to initiate hydrogen atom abstraction from the substrate. Photosynthesis involves radical pair formation in the reaction center; the radical pair mechanism has been proposed as the basis for the avian magnetic compass. Lipid peroxidation, initiated by hydroxyl radicals abstracting hydrogen from polyunsaturated fatty acids, propagates through a radical chain that damages cell membranes and is implicated in aging and disease. Antioxidants (vitamin E, vitamin C, glutathione) terminate these chains by donating hydrogen atoms to peroxyl radicals, forming stabilized radical intermediates that do not propagate.

In industry, radical polymerization produces over 100 million tons of polymers annually. The production of polyethylene (both HDPE and LDPE), poly(vinyl chloride), polystyrene, and poly(methyl methacrylate) all proceed through radical chain mechanisms. Controlled radical polymerization methods (ATRP: atom transfer radical polymerization; RAFT: reversible addition-fragmentation chain transfer; NMP: nitroxide-mediated polymerization) have transformed polymer science by enabling precise control over molecular weight, dispersity, and architecture (block, star, gradient copolymers). These methods work by establishing a dynamic equilibrium between active propagating radicals and dormant species, limiting the steady-state radical concentration and thus reducing termination events.

**The [2+2] photocycloaddition in synthesis.** The photochemical [2+2] cycloaddition is one of the most important methods for forming cyclobutane rings. The Paterno-Buchi reaction (carbonyl + alkene) gives oxetanes. The intramolecular [2+2] photocycloaddition of dienes gives bicyclobutane systems. These reactions are forbidden under thermal conditions but proceed readily under UV irradiation because one component is electronically excited, inverting the orbital symmetry requirements. The de Mayo reaction (enone + alkene, followed by retro-aldol) is a powerful method for 1,5-diketone synthesis. The use of sensitizers (acetophenone, benzophenone) allows selective triplet-state [2+2] cycloadditions with different stereochemical outcomes than the direct singlet-state reaction.

**Computational studies of pericyclic reactions.** Modern DFT calculations (B3LYP/6-31G* and higher) reproduce the activation barriers and stereoselectivities of pericyclic reactions with typical accuracy of 1-3 kcal/mol. The transition states of Diels-Alder reactions have been extensively studied computationally: they are characterized by synchronous or asynchronous bond formation, with the degree of asynchronicity depending on the electronic asymmetry of the reactants. For highly asynchronous Diels-Alder reactions (e.g., with a strongly electron-withdrawing dienophile), the TS resembles a zwitterionic intermediate, blurring the distinction between concerted and stepwise mechanisms. This has led to the concept of a "continuum" of Diels-Alder TS geometries, from synchronous (symmetrical diene/dienophile) to highly asynchronous (strongly polarized pairs).

## Connections [Master]

Radical and pericyclic reactions intersect with many areas of chemistry:

- **Synthesis:** The Diels-Alder reaction is one of the most powerful C-C bond-forming reactions in synthesis, used extensively in natural product total synthesis (e.g., Woodward's synthesis of reserpine, Corey's prostaglandin syntheses). The Claisen rearrangement reliably installs quaternary stereocenters. Radical reactions enable C-C bond formation under mild, neutral conditions that tolerate many functional groups. Intramolecular Diels-Alder reactions (IMDA) construct complex polycyclic systems in a single step, as in the synthesis of the steroid framework. Hetero-Diels-Alder reactions (using C=O or C=N as the dienophile) give access to heterocyclic products.

- **Polymer chemistry:** Radical polymerization accounts for the majority of commercial polymer production (polyethylene, poly(vinyl chloride), polystyrene). Controlled radical polymerization techniques (ATRP, RAFT, NMP) provide molecular weight control and narrow dispersities by maintaining a persistent radical that reversibly deactivates growing chains. The kinetics of controlled radical polymerization follow a different rate law than conventional radical polymerization: the rate is first-order in initiator rather than half-order, because the persistent radical effect suppresses bimolecular termination.

- **Biochemistry:** Radical enzymes (ribonucleotide reductase, pyruvate formate-lyase, galactose oxidase) catalyze difficult transformations using transient organic radicals as cofactors. The adenosylcobalamin (vitamin B12) cofactor generates a 5'-deoxyadenosyl radical that initiates radical rearrangements. DNA damage by hydroxyl radicals (from ionizing radiation) produces strand breaks and base modifications. Radical SAM enzymes (S-adenosylmethionine-dependent) use a [4Fe-4S] cluster to generate a 5'-deoxyadenosyl radical, catalyzing diverse reactions including cofactor biosynthesis, tRNA modification, and antibiotic biosynthesis.

- **Atmospheric chemistry:** The hydroxyl radical (HO*) is the primary oxidant in the troposphere, initiating the degradation of most volatile organic compounds. Chlorine radicals from CFC photolysis catalyze ozone destruction in the stratosphere through the Chapman cycle and its radical chain extensions. The atmospheric lifetime of a compound is determined by its rate of reaction with OH radicals; methane (k_OH approximately 6 x 10^-15 cm^3/molecule/s) has an atmospheric lifetime of about 12 years, while trichloroethylene (k_OH approximately 2 x 10^-12) has a lifetime of about 5 days.

- **Materials science:** Pericyclic reactions are used in photoresist chemistry for semiconductor lithography. Thermal [4+2] cycloadditions are exploited in self-healing polymers (embedded diene/dienophile pairs that react when cracks form). Click chemistry, though not strictly pericyclic, shares the conceptual simplicity of concerted, high-yielding bond formation. Photochromic materials (e.g., spiropyrans, diarylethenes) operate via photochemical electrocyclic ring opening/closing, enabling applications in optical data storage and molecular switches.

- **Theoretical chemistry:** The Woodward-Hoffmann rules were a triumph of molecular orbital theory and one of the first examples where theoretical analysis predicted a broad class of experimental outcomes. The 1981 Nobel Prize to Fukui and Hoffmann recognized this achievement. Modern computational chemistry can now predict pericyclic reaction barriers and selectivities with quantitative accuracy using DFT and coupled cluster methods. The transition state theory of pericyclic reactions connects the orbital symmetry analysis to the potential energy surface, showing that symmetry-allowed reactions have lower barriers because the occupied-occupied orbital crossing that creates the symmetry-imposed barrier in forbidden reactions is absent.

- **Pharmaceutical chemistry:** The Diels-Alder reaction and the Claisen rearrangement are workhorse reactions in pharmaceutical process chemistry. The Diels-Alder approach to the steroid skeleton, the Claisen-based installation of quaternary centers in prostaglandin synthesis, and the use of hetero-Diels-Alder reactions for nitrogen heterocycle construction (prevalent in drug molecules) all illustrate the industrial importance of pericyclic reactions.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Radical chemistry was established experimentally before it was comfortable conceptually. Gomberg's 1900 report of the triphenylmethyl radical forced chemists to accept carbon-centered species with unpaired electrons, and twentieth-century kinetic work turned radical chains into a standard mechanism for combustion, halogenation, polymerization, and atmospheric chemistry.

Pericyclic chemistry became a test case for theoretical organic chemistry after Woodward and Hoffmann formulated orbital-symmetry rules in the 1960s, in parallel with Fukui's frontier-orbital approach. The significance was not merely classificatory. A reaction's stereochemical fate could be predicted from the phase relationships of molecular orbitals before the experiment was run.

The story of the Woodward-Hoffmann rules illustrates how theory and experiment interact in chemistry. In the early 1960s, Woodward (a synthetic chemist) observed unexpected stereochemical outcomes in vitamin B12 synthesis intermediates that suggested a general pattern. He consulted Hoffmann (a theoretical chemist), and together they analyzed the orbital symmetry requirements for pericyclic reactions. Their 1965 series of five papers in the Journal of the American Chemical Society established the rules that bear their names. Independently, Fukui had developed frontier molecular orbital theory in the 1950s, but its application to pericyclic reactions was not widely appreciated until the Woodward-Hoffmann papers provided the context.

The Woodward-Hoffmann rules represented a paradigm shift in organic chemistry. Before their work, the understanding of why certain reactions occurred and others did not was largely empirical. After their work, a unified theoretical framework could predict the outcome of any pericyclic reaction from simple orbital symmetry considerations. This was one of the first instances where a major class of chemical reactions was rationalized by theoretical analysis rather than catalogued by experiment. The 1981 Nobel Prize in Chemistry to Fukui and Hoffmann (Woodward had died in 1979 and was ineligible) recognized this achievement.

The philosophical significance extends beyond chemistry. The Woodward-Hoffmann rules demonstrate that chemical reactivity is governed by the symmetry properties of quantum mechanical wave functions. A reaction is "forbidden" not because of energetic or steric constraints, but because the symmetry of the bonding electrons cannot be conserved through the transformation. This is a statement about the topology of orbital phase relationships, not about energy barriers in the traditional sense. The rules thus provide a rare example in chemistry where a qualitative, predictive theory emerges directly from quantum mechanical principles without numerical computation.

The development of radical chemistry followed a different but equally instructive path. Gomberg's triphenylmethyl radical (1900) was initially met with skepticism; many chemists preferred to explain the observations using molecular complexes rather than free radicals. The gradual acceptance of radical intermediates paralleled the development of kinetic methods for detecting short-lived species (flash photolysis, ESR spectroscopy, spin trapping). By the mid-twentieth century, radical chain mechanisms were established for combustion, halogenation, and polymerization. The discovery of radical enzymes in the 1970s-1980s (ribonucleotide reductase, coenzyme B12-dependent mutases) further expanded the scope of radical chemistry into biochemistry, demonstrating that nature uses radical mechanisms for some of the most chemically challenging transformations in metabolism.

The development of enantioselective pericyclic reactions represents an important extension of the Woodward-Hoffmann rules to the control of absolute stereochemistry. Chiral Lewis acid catalysts can bind to dienophiles and create a chiral environment that favors one facial approach of the diene over the other, producing Diels-Alder adducts with high enantiomeric excess. The Evans oxazolidinone auxiliaries, developed in the 1980s, use a chiral auxiliary covalently attached to the dienophile to control the facial selectivity of the Diels-Alder reaction. The MacMillan organocatalyst (2000) uses iminium ion activation to catalyze Diels-Alder reactions with high enantioselectivity without any metal catalyst. These developments show that the orbital symmetry rules established by Woodward and Hoffmann are compatible with the fine control of three-dimensional molecular architecture, bridging the gap between the electronic and steric aspects of chemical reactivity.

The interplay between concerted and stepwise mechanisms continues to be a subject of active research. The classic distinction -- pericyclic reactions are concerted, radical reactions are stepwise -- breaks down in certain cases. Highly asynchronous Diels-Alder reactions (with strongly polarized diene-dienophile pairs) have transition states that resemble zwitterionic intermediates, and the distinction between a concerted but asynchronous mechanism and a stepwise mechanism with a very short-lived intermediate becomes a matter of definition. Similarly, some radical cyclizations (particularly 5-exo-trig cyclizations) are so fast that the ring-closed radical is formed before the intervening bond can rotate, producing products with stereochemistry reminiscent of concerted reactions. The continuum between concerted and stepwise mechanisms, revealed by ultrafast spectroscopy and high-level computational studies, shows that the traditional mechanistic categories are useful idealizations rather than rigid boundaries.

The frontier orbital approach to predicting regiochemistry and sterechemistry in pericyclic reactions has been extended to heterocyclic and asymmetric systems. Inverse-electron-demand Diels-Alder reactions (IEDDA) between electron-poor 1,2,4,5-tetrazines and strained or electron-rich dienophiles (trans-cyclooctene, norbornene) have emerged as powerful bioorthogonal reactions for labeling biomolecules in living systems. The tetrazine ligation, developed by Fox and coworkers, proceeds at rate constants up to 10^6 M^-1 s^-1 in aqueous buffer at 37 C, making it the fastest known bioorthogonal reaction. The reaction mechanism is a [4+2] cycloaddition followed by retro-[4+2] elimination of N2, giving a dihydropyridazine product. The driving force is the loss of N2 gas (the most stable diatomic molecule), and the rate is enhanced by the LUMO-lowering effect of the nitrogen atoms on the tetrazine frontier orbitals. This application of FMO theory to a practical biological problem demonstrates the enduring power of the Woodward-Hoffmann-Fukui framework.

The emerging field of photochemical pericyclic reactions in synthesis has been revitalized by the development of photocatalytic methods that allow precise control over the excited-state reactivity. The [2+2] photocycloaddition, previously limited by the need for UV light and the formation of complex product mixtures, can now be controlled using visible-light photocatalysts that selectively sensitize one reactant while leaving the other in its ground state. Enantioselective photocycloadditions, using chiral hydrogen-bonding catalysts or chiral Lewis acids, provide access to cyclobutane-containing natural products with high stereoselectivity. The [4+4] photocycloaddition of anthracene derivatives is used in the synthesis of ladder polymers with remarkable electronic properties. These modern applications of photochemical pericyclic reactions demonstrate that the orbital symmetry rules continue to guide synthetic innovation more than half a century after their discovery.

The study of pericyclic reaction mechanisms has been revolutionized by ultrafast spectroscopy, which can directly observe the transition state region of pericyclic reactions. Femtosecond pump-probe experiments on the ring-opening of cyclohexadiene (a photochemical electrocyclic reaction) have measured the reaction time as approximately 80 femtoseconds, confirming the concerted nature of the process. For the Cope rearrangement, dynamic effects (non-statistical behavior) have been observed in highly substituted systems, where the reaction trajectory on the potential energy surface does not pass through the lowest-energy transition state but instead takes a more direct path that bypasses intermediates. These dynamic effects, revealed by trajectory calculations and isotopic labeling experiments, show that pericyclic reactions can exhibit behavior that goes beyond both the Woodward-Hoffmann rules (which predict the allowed pathway) and transition state theory (which assumes statistical distribution of energy at the saddle point).

The computational prediction of pericyclic reaction rates and selectivities has reached a level of accuracy that rivals experiment. Coupled cluster calculations (CCSD(T)/cc-pVTZ) reproduce Diels-Alder activation barriers to within 1 kcal/mol. SCS-MP2 (spin-component-scaled MP2) provides comparable accuracy at lower computational cost, enabling the study of larger systems. Density functional theory (DFT) with modern functionals (M06-2X, wB97X-D) gives reliable results for most pericyclic reactions, although care must be taken with dispersion corrections for reactions where van der Waals interactions play a role in the transition state geometry. Machine learning approaches are beginning to predict pericyclic reactivity from molecular descriptors, potentially enabling the rapid screening of candidate reactions for synthetic applications without explicit quantum chemical calculation.

The Cope rearrangement of 1,5-hexadiene is a [3,3]-sigmatropic rearrangement that proceeds through either a chair-like or boat-like transition state. Using the Woodward-Hoffmann rules and FMO analysis, show that the reaction is allowed under thermal conditions. Explain why the chair transition state is preferred (by about 5.7 kcal/mol) over the boat. Calculate the rate enhancement factor at 25 C that results from this energy difference.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A [3,3]-sigmatropic rearrangement involves 6 electrons in the cyclic transition state. For a thermal reaction, the Woodward-Hoffmann rules require an even number of antarafacial components. In the chair TS, all components are suprafacial. Use the Evans-Dehnhuttlmn rule: for 4n+2 electrons with all suprafacial components, the thermal reaction is allowed. <details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Cope rearrangement is a [3,3]-sigmatropic shift. The suprafacial-suprafacial pathway involves 6 electrons (4n+2 with n=1), so the thermal reaction is allowed by the Woodward-Hoffmann rules (Huckel topology with 4n+2 electrons is aromatic and stabilized in the transition state).

FMO analysis: The HOMO of the allyl radical fragment has orbital coefficients of +0.707, -0.707, and +0.707 on C1, C2, and C3 respectively. In the transition state, the terminal carbons of the two allyl fragments overlap. The bonding interaction requires overlap of orbitals with the same sign, which is satisfied for both chair and boat geometries.

The chair TS is lower in energy because:

1. In the chair TS, the substituents on C2, C3, C4, and C5 are in a staggered arrangement, minimizing gauche interactions. The secondary orbital interactions (HOMO-HOMO overlap) between the inner carbons are optimized.

2. In the boat TS, the substituents are partially eclipsed, introducing torsional strain. Additionally, the boat geometry brings the inner carbons (C2 and C5) into a repulsive interaction that is absent in the chair.

DFT calculations (B3LYP/6-31G*) give activation energies of about 33 kcal/mol for the chair and 39 kcal/mol for the boat, a difference of about 6 kcal/mol. Experimental measurements confirm this: the chair pathway dominates by more than 99:1 at all temperatures.

Rate enhancement: $\Delta\Delta G^\ddagger = 5.7$ kcal/mol. The rate ratio is $k_{\text{chair}}/k_{\text{boat}} = e^{\Delta\Delta G^\ddagger/RT} = e^{5700/(1.987 \times 298)} = e^{9.63} = 1.5 \times 10^4$. The chair pathway is about 15,000 times faster than the boat at 25 C.

For substituted Cope rearrangements (e.g., 3,3-dimethyl-1,5-hexadiene), the substituent effects on the chair vs boat preference can be analyzed using FMO theory: electron-donating groups on C3 and C4 raise the HOMO energy and accelerate the reaction, while electron-withdrawing groups have the opposite effect. The oxy-Cope rearrangement (with an OH group at C3) is accelerated by a factor of 10^17 at 25 C compared to the parent Cope, because the developing enolate character in the product-like TS is stabilized by the oxygen lone pair.

The Barton reaction involves the nitrite photolysis of a steroidal nitrite ester to form an oxime via a 1,5-hydrogen atom transfer (HAT) radical mechanism. Draw the complete mechanism, showing all radical intermediates. Explain why the 1,5-HAT is favored over 1,3-HAT and 1,7-HAT by analyzing the transition state geometry and the orbital overlap requirements. Calculate the approximate rate constant for 1,5-HAT at 25 C given that the activation energy is about 7 kcal/mol and the pre-exponential factor is approximately 10^10 s^-1.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The key step is hydrogen atom transfer from a carbon to an alkoxy radical. The transition state for 1,n-HAT involves a cyclic arrangement of n+1 atoms (including the hydrogen being transferred). For 1,5-HAT, this is a 6-membered ring, which can adopt a chair-like geometry with optimal orbital overlap. For 1,3-HAT (4-membered ring), the geometry is strained. For 1,7-HAT (8-membered ring), the entropy penalty reduces the rate.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Mechanism:

Step 1: Photolysis of the nitrite ester R-ONO --hv--> R-O* + *NO. The weak O-NO bond (about 38 kcal/mol) cleaves homolytically upon UV irradiation.

Step 2: 1,5-Hydrogen atom transfer. The alkoxy radical abstracts a hydrogen atom from a carbon that is 5 atoms away in the chain: R-O* + H-C(5) --> R-OH + *C(5). The resulting carbon-centered radical is delta to the original alkoxy group.

Step 3: Radical recombination. The carbon radical combines with the nitric oxide (*NO) released in Step 1: *C(5) + *NO --> C(5)=NOH (tautomerizes to oxime).

Why 1,5-HAT dominates:

1,3-HAT (4-membered TS): The four-membered ring transition state is geometrically strained and requires severe angle distortions. The orbital overlap between the singly occupied p orbital on oxygen and the C-H sigma* orbital is poor because they cannot achieve the optimal collinear arrangement. Activation energy is typically 12-15 kcal/mol, much higher than for 1,5-HAT.

1,5-HAT (6-membered TS): The six-membered ring can adopt a chair-like geometry where the O, H, and C atoms are nearly collinear (the optimal geometry for H atom transfer). The singly occupied orbital on oxygen and the C-H sigma bonding orbital achieve excellent overlap. Activation energy is about 6-8 kcal/mol.

1,7-HAT (8-membered TS): The eight-membered ring is floppy and has a large entropic penalty for achieving the correct geometry. Although the enthalpic barrier is comparable to 1,5-HAT (about 7-9 kcal/mol), the pre-exponential factor is reduced by 2-3 orders of magnitude due to the unfavorable entropy of activation. In flexible systems, 1,7-HAT can compete with 1,5-HAT, but it is generally slower.

Rate constant for 1,5-HAT: Using the Arrhenius equation $k = A e^{-E_a/RT}$ with $A = 10^{10}$ s^-1 and $E_a = 7$ kcal/mol:

$k = 10^{10} \times e^{-7000/(1.987 \times 298)} = 10^{10} \times e^{-11.83} = 10^{10} \times 7.3 \times 10^{-6} = 7.3 \times 10^4$ s^-1

The half-life is $t_{1/2} = \ln 2/k = 9.5 \times 10^{-6}$ s, confirming that the 1,5-HAT is extremely fast on the chemical timescale. This speed ensures that the alkoxy radical undergoes intramolecular H transfer before it can be trapped by intermolecular reactions, making the Barton reaction highly selective for the delta-position.

**Exercise (medium, symbolic).**

Predict the product of the electrocyclic ring-opening of cyclobutene to 1,3-butadiene under thermal and photochemical conditions. Use orbital correlation diagrams to justify your prediction. Explain why the stereochemistry of the thermal reaction is conrotatory (both methyl groups rotate in the same direction) while the photochemical reaction is disrotatory.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For a 4-electron electrocyclic reaction (4n electrons), the Woodward-Hoffmann rules predict conrotatory ring-opening under thermal conditions and disrotatory under photochemical conditions. Analyze the symmetry of the pi and pi* orbitals of cyclobutene under the two possible modes of rotation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Cyclobutene has two pi orbitals: the bonding pi (C1=C2) and the antibonding pi* (C1=C2). During ring-opening, these become the four pi molecular orbitals of 1,3-butadiene: psi1 (bonding), psi2 (bonding), psi3 (antibonding), psi4 (antibonding).

For thermal ring-opening (ground state), the electron configuration of cyclobutene is pi^2. The Woodward-Hoffmann rules for a 4-electron system (4n with n=1) predict conrotatory motion under thermal conditions. In the conrotatory mode, both CH2 groups rotate in the same direction (both clockwise or both counterclockwise). The orbital symmetry is conserved: the bonding pi orbital of cyclobutene correlates with the bonding psi1 orbital of butadiene under conrotatory motion, so the reaction proceeds without a symmetry-imposed barrier.

For photochemical ring-opening (excited state), the electron configuration is pi^1(pi*)^1. Under disrotatory motion (CH2 groups rotate in opposite directions), the pi* orbital of cyclobutene correlates with the bonding psi2 orbital of butadiene, so the excited state can undergo ring-opening without a symmetry-imposed barrier. The conrotatory mode would correlate the pi* with psi3 (antibonding), creating a barrier.

If the cyclobutene has substituents (e.g., 3,4-dimethylcyclobutene), the stereochemical outcome is predictable:

Thermal (conrotatory): Both methyl groups rotate in the same direction. If they start cis on the ring, they end up trans in the product (E,E or Z,Z depending on the direction). If they start trans on the ring, they end up cis (E,Z).

Photochemical (disrotatory): The methyl groups rotate in opposite directions. If they start cis, they end up cis (E,Z). If they start trans, they end up trans (E,E or Z,Z).

This stereochemical prediction has been confirmed experimentally and is one of the most striking demonstrations of the Woodward-Hoffmann rules: the stereochemistry of the product is determined entirely by the symmetry of the orbitals, not by steric or thermodynamic considerations.
