## Intuition [Beginner]

For nearly half a century, American psychology refused to look inside the head. The behaviorists, led by John Watson and B. F. Skinner, insisted that a real science could measure only what an organism *does* — its responses to stimuli — and that talk of "mind," "idea," or "image" was unscientific. By the 1950s this had become a cage. Psychology's most interesting questions — how we speak, remember, plan, recognize a face — could not even be asked inside the vocabulary behaviorism allowed.

Then a new idea arrived from an unexpected direction: the computer. If a machine could take in information, hold it, transform it, and act on it, then perhaps a person could be described the same way — not as a bundle of stimulus-response links, but as a system that processes information. The mind was back. This change, gathered between 1956 and 1967, is the **cognitive revolution**. Ulric Neisser's 1967 book *Cognitive Psychology* gave it a name and a textbook.

The revolution's boldest claim is the **computational theory of mind**: thinking is computation, and the brain runs something like a program over something like a code. This unit follows that claim from its roots — Turing's computer metaphor, Miller's measurements of memory, Chomsky's attack on behaviorist accounts of language — through Fodor's "language of thought," and on to its challenger, the connectionist tradition of artificial neural networks.

The revolution opened the box and gave each stage inside a name, a measurable capacity, and a model.

## Worked example [Beginner]

In 1956 George Miller measured how much a person can hold in mind at once, and the result became a founding fact of the new psychology [ref: miller1956 Miller 1956].

Imagine you are read a string of random digits at one per second: `7 3 9 1 4 8 2`. When the list ends you repeat it back. Most adults manage about seven digits, sometimes six, sometimes nine. Miller called this the "magical number seven, plus or minus two." A behaviorist would record this as a response rate and stop there. The new psychology asked what the limit revealed about the *machinery inside*.

Now try letters: `C I A F B I I B M T N N`. That is twelve letters, well past the limit — yet most people reproduce them easily. The trick is that the letters re-group into four familiar acronyms: CIA, FBI, IBM, TNN. Miller called this regrouping **chunking**. The limit is not on raw items but on *chunks* — and a chunk is whatever your prior knowledge has already bundled together.

What this tells us: the seven-item limit presupposes internal representations doing the grouping, because the same physical input yields different capacities depending on what the listener already knows. Behaviorism's stimulus-response vocabulary had no place for "what the listener already knows." The information-processing framework did, and that single move reopened the mind as a scientific object.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The cognitive revolution supplied a vocabulary that the rest of this chapter inherits. The definitions below fix the central terms.

An **information-processing model** [ref: neisser1967 Neisser 1967] is a functional decomposition of a cognitive act into a sequence of stages — encoding, storage, transformation, retrieval, response — each characterized by an operation on an internal **representation** and by measurable parameters (capacity, decay rate, latency). The model is a *flow*: stimulus enters at one end, is transformed by a cascade of intermediate processes, and emerges as behavior at the other. A flow diagram is not a metaphor tacked onto prose; each box commits its author to a distinct process with its own properties.

The **computational theory of mind** (CTM) is the stronger thesis that mental processes *are* computations. On the standard statement, a system has a mind, or a mental state, insofar as it implements the right sort of computation over the right sort of representation; the physical substrate matters only in that it must be capable of implementing the computation. The brain's biological details are relevant to *how* the computation is realized, not to *whether* there is a computation at all. This is the sense in which the theory makes the computer more than a metaphor: thinking and computing are claimed to be the same kind of thing.

A **physical symbol system**, in the formulation of Newell and Simon [ref: newell1958 Newell, Shaw, & Simon 1958], is a physical device that manipulates structured patterns (symbols) according to rules. Their hypothesis states that a physical symbol system has the necessary and sufficient means for general intelligent action — that intelligence is, at bottom, rule-governed symbol manipulation. This is the symbolic pole of the computational theory.

The **language of thought** hypothesis, due to Fodor [ref: fodor1975 Fodor 1975], holds that thought takes place in a Mentalese: an internal, compositional system of representations in which the meaning of a complex thought is a function of the meanings of its parts and their mode of combination. Thinking is then computation over Mentalese sentences, and the productivity and systematicity of thought — you can think a thought you have never thought before, and anyone who can think "John loves Mary" can think "Mary loves John" — are explained by the compositional syntax of the medium.

**Connectionism**, or parallel distributed processing [ref: rumelhart1986 Rumelhart & McClelland 1986], is the counter-tradition. A connectionist model is a network of simple units organized in layers, weighted links between units, and a rule for adjusting the weights; information is stored not in discrete symbols but in the *pattern* of weights across the whole network. Knowledge is distributed, retrieval is the reactivation of a pattern, and learning is the gradual reshaping of weights by experience. The neural inspiration is overt; the explanatory primitive is the weighted connection, not the symbol.

The contrast that organizes the rest of the unit is between the **symbolic** tradition (CTM, physical symbol systems, the language of thought) and the **subsymbolic** tradition (connectionism). Both descend from the revolution, and both take the mind to be an information-processing system; they disagree about the basic currency of the processing.

## Key model: the computational theory of mind and the information-processing framework [Intermediate+]

The revolution's model is best seen as two claims layered together: an architectural claim about *what the mind is* and a methodological claim about *how to study it*. Treating them separately exposes where the framework earns its strength and where it inherits behaviorism's unfinished business.

The architectural claim is the computational theory of mind. Turing's 1950 paper proposed that the question "can a machine think?" could be recast as a behavioral test — the imitation game — and that the test admitted no principled line between human and machine reasoning [ref: turing1950 Turing 1950]. McCulloch and Pitts had already shown, in 1943, that networks of simple threshold units could implement any Boolean function and so, in principle, any finite computation [ref: mcculloch1943 McCulloch & Pitts 1943]. By the late 1950s the Newell-Simon programs — the Logic Theory Machine and the General Problem Solver — were proving theorems and playing chess by searching a space of symbol structures under rules [ref: newell1958 Newell, Shaw, & Simon 1958]. These threads converge on the claim: cognition is computation; the brain is the hardware; the mind is the program.

The methodological claim is the information-processing framework [ref: neisser1967 Neisser 1967]. Broadbent's 1958 flow diagram of selective attention — a sensory buffer feeding a selective filter feeding a limited-capacity channel feeding long-term storage — set the template [ref: broadbent1958 Broadbent 1958]: draw boxes for processes, arrows for the flow of information, and attach to each box a measurable property. Miller's magical number seven then quantified one box [ref: miller1956 Miller 1956]; the Sperling partial-report paradigm quantified another; reaction-time methods decomposed a third. The framework turned "the mind" from a philosophical abstraction into a collection of named stages, each admitting an experiment.

The framework's first triumph was that it redescribed behaviorism's own data at a deeper level. A Skinner-box reinforcement schedule, read as a stimulus-response curve, is also the *output* of an animal that encodes the stimulus, retrieves a value, and selects an action; the information-processing account predicts, and in many cases explains, features the flat curve leaves opaque. The critique that Chomsky leveled at Skinner's *Verbal Behavior* made the gain vivid: children produce sentences they have never been rewarded for and make systematic errors like "I goed," which no schedule of imitation and reinforcement can generate [ref: chomsky1959 Chomsky 1959]. Within the behaviorist vocabulary these facts are anomalous; within the computational vocabulary they are exactly what a rule-governed system should produce. This is the sense in which the revolution was a *shift* and not merely an accumulation: the same datum meant a different thing before and after.

The framework's acknowledged limit is the implementation question it leaves open. To say that cognition is computation is to say what the mind *does*; it leaves nearly untouched how wet neural tissue manages to do it. The connectionist tradition, taken up in the Master section, arose precisely to supply the missing layer — and in doing so it reopened the question of whether the symbol is the right primitive at all.

**Bridge.** The information-processing framework **builds toward** every cognition unit that follows: attention, memory, language, and decision are each modeled as stages of this same pipeline, and the computational theory of mind **appears again in** the cognition chapter [29.05.01] and the learning-and-memory chapter [29.04.01]. **This is exactly** what separates a cognitive account from a behaviorist one — the science begins at the box behaviorism refused to open. **The foundational reason** the framework took hold so fast is that it handed psychologists a shared architecture: stimulus, encoding, storage, computation, response, each measurable and each modelable. **Putting these together**, the pipeline **generalises** to non-human animals and to machines, and **the bridge is** that a flow diagram is itself a theory — every box commits its author to a representational claim.

Fodor argues that thought has a *compositional syntax* (the language of thought). State the two phenomena he takes this to explain, and say in one sentence why a pure associationist network struggles with them.

**Exercise 6 (hard, short answer).**

The computational theory of mind is often glossed as "the brain is hardware, the mind is software." State the strongest *philosophical* objection to this gloss, and then say why the gloss can nonetheless be retained as a regulative ideal for cognitive psychology.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider whether the same program can be run on substrates that share none of the brain's biological structure, and what that implies about explanatory levels.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The strongest objection is the worry about *implementation*: a mere equivalence-class of input-output behavior does not fix a unique computation, because any finite behavior can be described under indefinitely many computational interpretations, so "the mind is software" threatens to underdetermine the very states it posits. If multiple programs are compatible with the same behavior, the software description seems to float free of the biology that would adjudicate between them, and the theory risks vacuity.

The gloss survives as a regulative ideal because cognitive psychology does not traffic in unconstrained input-output descriptions. It posits *specific* intermediate representations (working-memory buffers, object files, syntactic trees) whose existence is tested by converging operations — reaction-time dissociations, interference paradigms, neuropsychological deficits, and neural signatures. These constraints pin down particular computations well enough to make them explanatory, even if the philosophical underdetermination remains. The metaphor is a research program, not a finished ontology.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

It nonetheless belongs to the cognitive revolution because the weights are *internal* parameters of a model that posits hidden representations and intermediate transformations. The network is a hypothesis about the machinery between input and output — about what is computed and how it is stored — and its predictions concern internal states (patterns of activation, distributed representations, error gradients) rather than stimulus-response rates alone. Behaviorism refused the inner box; connectionism fills it, just with weights instead of symbols. The disagreement with the symbolic tradition is therefore a family quarrel *inside* the revolution.

## Symbol-processing versus connectionism [Master]

The revolution's internal fault line is the dispute over the mind's basic currency. The symbolic tradition, descending from Turing and McCulloch-Pitts through Newell and Simon to Fodor, takes the rule-governed symbol as its primitive. The subsymbolic tradition, descending from the same McCulloch-Pitts neurons but developed by Rumelhart, McClelland, and the Parallel Distributed Processing group, takes the weighted connection as its primitive. Both are computational; they disagree about what computation looks like inside the head.

The symbolic tradition's strongest argument is systematicity. Fodor pressed the case that thought exhibits a combinatorial structure that any adequate psychology must explain [ref: fodor1975 Fodor 1975]: whoever can think "John loves Mary" can think "Mary loves John," and this covariance is not an accident to be learned case by case but a structural feature of what it is to possess the constituent concepts. A compositional Mentalese accounts for the covariance directly — the constituents are reusable slots in a structured representation — whereas a network trained to produce the first thought must, on the face of it, learn the second as a separate fact. Productivity, the open-ended capacity to entertain novel thoughts, runs on the same engine: a finite stock of constituents and rules yields an unbounded stock of thoughts, and no finite training set can approximate the unbounded by enumeration.

The connectionist reply is that the appearance of separate learning is an artifact of how early networks were trained and evaluated. A network with distributed representations can share structure across training instances: the weights that encode "love" and the weights that encode its argument roles can compose in activation space, so that learning one ordering partially privileges the other. The famous Rumelhart-McClelland model of the English past tense [ref: rumelhart1986 Rumelhart & McClelland 1986] was exhibit A: a network with no explicit rule learned to produce regular past-tense forms and then overgeneralized them to irregulars ("goed," "runned") — reproducing, without a symbolic rule, the very developmental pattern that had been taken as evidence of an internal rule system. The lesson drawn was that rule-like behavior need not issue from explicit rules; the appearance of a rule can be the surface of a distributed statistical regularity.

Each tradition explains a regularity the other finds stubborn. The symbolic side accounts for the productivity, compositionality, and inferential coherence of explicit reasoning — the fact that a single counterexample defeats a universal claim, that logical form tracks validity, that novel sentences are understood on first hearing. The connectionist side accounts for the gradedness, context-sensitivity, and damage-resilience of cognition — the fact that recognition degrades gracefully rather than collapsing, that prototypes rather than definitions seem to govern many categories, that statistical generalization across examples is the rule rather than the exception in learning. A theory that captures logic's crispness struggles with perception's softness; a theory that captures perception's softness struggles with logic's crispness.

The contemporary resolution is level-of-description pluralism. A connectionist network can *implement* a symbol system — the symbol is a stable pattern of activation, the rule is a reliable transformation of such patterns — and a symbol system can be *approximated* by a sufficiently expressive network. On this reading the dispute was never about which primitive is real but about which level of description carries the explanatory load for a given phenomenon. Language parsing and logical inference sit closer to the symbolic level; perception, motor control, and many forms of learning sit closer to the subsymbolic level. The unification, where it has been achieved, treats the brain as connectionist hardware whose operations admit a symbolic description at a higher level of abstraction — a modern echo of the hardware-software distinction Turing's metaphor first imported.

**Synthesis.** The symbol-processing–connectionism debate **appears again in** every computational treatment of cognition — from language parsing [29.05.01] to learning theory [29.04.01] to contemporary deep learning — and it **builds toward** the modern reconciliation in which brains are modeled as connectionist hardware running operations that admit a symbolic description at a higher level. **This is exactly** the level-of-description question the cognitive revolution inherited but could not settle: whether the mind's basic currency is the rule-governed symbol or the weighted connection. **The foundational reason** the dispute persists is that the two traditions explain different regularities — systematic, productive reasoning on one side, graceful degradation and statistical learning on the other. **The central insight** of the contemporary view is that these need not compete: a network can implement a symbol system, and a symbol system can be approximated by a network. **Putting these together**, the revolution's legacy **generalises** across the cognitive sciences, and **the bridge is** the recognition that "how the mind works" admits more than one true level of description.

The learning-and-memory chapter [29.04.01] inherits the revolution's family quarrel: behaviorist reinforcement schedules on one side, cognitive representations and the connectionist account of learning on the other. The Chomsky critique and the Rumelhart-McClelland past-tense model both resurface there as the central evidence the chapter must reconcile.

The neuroscience chapter [29.02.01] carries the implementation question this unit leaves open. The claim that cognition is computation tells what the mind does; cognitive neuroscience asks how neural tissue does it, and the modern answer — populations of neurons encoding distributed representations — is the biological face of the connectionist tradition formalized here.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The revolution has a precise genealogy, and its dating is a genuine historiographical question rather than a convenience. The symbolic-computational root was planted in 1943, when McCulloch and Pitts proved that networks of simple threshold units could compute any Boolean function and so, in principle, realize any finite automaton [ref: mcculloch1943 McCulloch & Pitts 1943]. The information-theoretic root matured in the late 1940s with Shannon's theory of communication and Wiener's cybernetics, which made "information" a measurable currency available to psychology. Turing's 1950 paper then imported the computer as a model of mind [ref: turing1950 Turing 1950], replacing the old metaphors of mind-as telephone-exchange or mind-as hydraulic-press with a metaphor of a kind that could, in principle, be implemented.

The conventional birthday is the Symposium on Information Theory held at MIT in September 1956, at which the threads visibly converged. George Miller presented the magical number seven [ref: miller1956 Miller 1956]; Allen Newell and Herbert Simon presented the Logic Theory Machine, a program that had derived theorems from Whitehead and Russell's *Principia*; Noam Chomsky presented a formal treatment of syntactic structure; and Jerome Bruner and Jacqueline Goodnow presented their work on thinking and categorization [ref: bruner1960 Bruner, Goodnow, & Austin 1956]. Historians of cognitive science, notably Howard Gardner, have taken this simultaneity as the field's founding moment: the disciplines that would compose cognitive science — psychology, linguistics, computer science, philosophy, neuroscience — found, in the same year, a shared vocabulary.

The consolidation followed across a decade. Broadbent's *Perception and Communication* (1958) gave the information-processing framework its canonical flow diagram for attention [ref: broadbent1958 Broadbent 1958]. Chomsky's 1959 review dismantled the behaviorist account of language and supplied, in the poverty-of-the-stimulus argument, the anomaly the old paradigm could not digest [ref: chomsky1959 Chomsky 1959]. In 1960 Miller and Bruner founded the Center for Cognitive Studies at Harvard, institutionalizing the movement. Neisser's *Cognitive Psychology* (1967) then named the field and assembled its findings into a textbook [ref: neisser1967 Neisser 1967], and the discipline that had spent forty years studying only inputs and outputs turned back to the machinery between them.

The revolution's philosophical apotheosis arrived with Fodor's *The Language of Thought* (1975), which argued that the computational theory required an internal, compositional medium of representation — Mentalese — and that the productivity and systematicity of thought could be explained by no other means [ref: fodor1975 Fodor 1975]. The counter-tradition crystallized a decade later in the two-volume *Parallel Distributed Processing* (1986), in which Rumelhart, McClelland, and their collaborators showed that networks of neuron-like units could learn structured tasks without explicit rules [ref: rumelhart1986 Rumelhart & McClelland 1986]. The latter re-opened the question the symbolic tradition had thought settled — whether the symbol is the right primitive — and the debate it ignited still organizes the field.
