## Intuition [Beginner]

Psychology feels ancient, but as a laboratory science it is younger than the telephone. For most of human history, questions about the mind — what is thought, where does knowledge come from, why do people act as they do — belonged to philosophy. The change came in **1879**, when Wilhelm Wundt opened a small room in Leipzig, Germany, fitted it with instruments, and declared that mental life could be measured like anything else in nature.

That date is a landmark, not a wall. The questions did not change overnight; the methods did. Before Wundt, scholars reasoned about the mind from the armchair. After Wundt, they timed reactions, probed sensations, and trained observers to look inward under controlled conditions, and they called the new field *psychology* — the study of the psyche recast as a science.

From that single laboratory the field kept branching. William James in America framed the mind as a tool for survival. Sigmund Freud in Vienna argued that we are driven by wishes we cannot see. Then the behaviorists threw inner experience out entirely and studied only what organisms *do*. Then the cognitive revolution of the 1950s put the mind back at the center, this time modeled on the computer.

Today brain imaging and genetics have added a third layer. Each generation claimed to have found psychology's real object — conscious elements, adaptive functions, observable behavior, information, neural activity — and each sharpened the question for the next.

So the history of psychology is a chain of answers to one stubborn question: *what is the proper object of this science, and how should we study it?* The answer keeps moving because the question itself keeps being redrawn.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Trace one problem through four schools: *how does the mind represent the world?* The question is the same; watch how the *allowed answer* changes.

**Locke (1689).** John Locke held that the mind begins as a blank slate — *tabula rasa* — and that all ideas enter through the senses. A child's representation of an apple is built up from repeated sensations: red, round, sweet, crisp. Representation is a copy of sensation, stored in memory and later recalled.

**Wundt (1879).** Wundt agreed the mind is built from sensations, but he made the question experimental. Trained observers listened to a metronome and reported what they experienced: rhythms, feelings of tension, expectations. The representation was decomposed into elementary sensations plus feelings. His tool was introspection — looking inward under controlled conditions in the laboratory he founded that year.

**Watson (1913).** Watson rejected the question. There is no representation to study, he argued, because there is no inspectable mind — only behavior. A child reaches for the apple because reaching has been rewarded in the past. The scientific object is the *response*, not anything hidden in the head, which cannot be measured by a second observer.

**The cognitivists (1956 onward).** The cognitive revolution put representation back. The mind stores an internal description of the apple — its color, category, location — and computes over it. Neisser's 1967 textbook made "information processing" the framework: sensation is input, memory is storage, thinking is computation, behavior is output.

What this trace shows: psychology did not inch toward one fixed truth. It kept *redefining the question itself*. Locke asked what is in the mind. Wundt asked how to measure it. Watson asked why we should look inside at all. The cognitivists asked how the mind computes. Each answer reset the field's horizon.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **school of psychology** is a research community bound by three shared commitments: (i) a specified *object* of study, (ii) a legitimate *method* for investigating that object, and (iii) a theoretical *vocabulary* in which findings are stated. A school is more than a taste; it constrains what counts as a question worth asking and what counts as an answer worth publishing. To identify a school, name all three.

The major schools of the discipline, defined along these three axes:

**Structuralism** (Wundt; Edward Titchener). Object: the *contents of consciousness* in their adult, trained form. Method: *systematic introspection* by practiced observers under controlled stimulation. Aim: to decompose experience into irreducible elements — sensations, images, and feelings — and the laws of their combination.

**Functionalism** (William James, John Dewey, James Rowland Angell). Object: the *functions* of mental processes — what they do, particularly in adapting the organism to its environment. Method: experiment, but also comparative psychology, developmental observation, and naturalistic study; functionalism is methodologically broader than structuralism.

**Psychoanalysis** (Sigmund Freud). Object: the *dynamic unconscious* — wishes, conflicts, and defenses operating outside awareness. Method: *free association* and clinical interpretation of dreams, slips, and the transference. Vocabulary: drive, repression, conflict, defense, transference.

**Behaviorism** (John B. Watson; B. F. Skinner). Object: *observable behavior*. Method: the *controlled experiment* relating stimuli to responses, with reinforcement and punishment as the chief explanatory variables. Mental states are either redefined as behavior dispositions or excluded as outside a natural science of psychology.

**Cognitivism** (Ulric Neisser, George Miller, Donald Broadbent, Jerome Bruner). Object: *mental processes* treated as *information processing*. Method: experiment supplemented by formal models (flow diagrams, later computational and connectionist models). Vocabulary: encoding, storage, retrieval, representation, capacity, computation.

**Cognitive neuroscience** (late twentieth century). Object: the *neural mechanisms* that subserve mental processes. Method: the cognitive toolkit augmented by functional neuroimaging (fMRI, PET), electrophysiology (EEG, single-unit recording), and the study of patients with focal brain lesions. It positions itself as a mechanistic underlayer for cognitive accounts, not a replacement for them.

A second distinction, due originally to the philosopher Wilhelm Dilthey and revived in twentieth-century historiography, separates two *kinds* of science. A *Naturwissenschaft* (natural science) explains its objects by subsuming them under general laws and seeks causal, preferably measurable, relations. A *Geisteswissenschaft* (human science) understands its objects by interpreting them in terms of meaning, purpose, and lived experience. Psychology has never settled which kind of science it is, and each school can be placed on this axis: behaviorism and cognitive neuroscience are at the Naturwissenschaft pole; psychoanalysis and much of clinical and phenomenological psychology sit toward the Geisteswissenschaft pole. The tension between the two is a persistent source of methodological dispute.

A **paradigm**, following Kuhn [ref: kuhn1970 Kuhn 1970], is the constellation of shared assumptions, exemplary achievements, and accepted methods that defines "normal science" for a research community. Whether psychology has ever possessed a *single* paradigm — or remains, as Kuhn himself suspected, a "pre-paradigmatic" discipline held together by professional organization rather than shared fundamentals — is a genuine historiographical question, taken up in the next section.

## Key model: paradigms, revolutions, and the historiography of psychology [Intermediate+]

The historian's central problem is whether psychology's history is **cumulative** — each school adding to a growing stock of reliable findings — or **revolutionary** — each school replacing its predecessor by redefining what counts as a legitimate finding at all. The choice of historiographical model is itself an interpretation, and it decides in advance what kind of story the discipline tells about itself.

Edwin Boring's *A History of Experimental Psychology* (1929; second edition 1950) [ref: boring1950 Boring 1950] tells the cumulative story. On Boring's account psychology progresses steadily from philosophical speculation to laboratory measurement, each generation refining instruments, quantifying more phenomena, and extending the experimental method into new domains. The heroes are the experimenters who made measurement possible; the plot is the gradual victory of method over speculation. This is the history that treats Wundt as a beginning, the behaviorists as methodological sharpeners, and the cognitive revolution as a broadening of the measurable, not a break.

Thomas Kuhn's *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) supplied the rival template [ref: kuhn1970 Kuhn 1970]. On Kuhn's account mature sciences advance not by accumulation but by *paradigm shifts*: a reigning framework organizes "normal science" until anomalies accumulate that it cannot digest, a crisis opens, and a new framework takes over, reinterpreting old results under a new vocabulary. The change is not additive; the same observation means something different before and after.

Applied to psychology, the Kuhnian reading treats the fall of behaviorism and the rise of cognitivism, roughly 1950 to 1970, as psychology's clearest candidate for a revolution. Behaviorism's anomalies could not be resolved within the stimulus-response vocabulary: Edward Tolman showed that rats learn the *layout* of a maze without reinforcement for any particular route, implying an internal "cognitive map" rather than a chain of stimulus-response bonds; Noam Chomsky argued that children produce sentences they have never been rewarded for and make systematic, rule-governed errors (such as "I goed"), which a pure reinforcement account cannot explain [ref: chomsky1959 Chomsky 1959]; John Garcia showed that some associations are learned in a single trial and others are nearly unlearnable, revealing biological constraints on what conditioning can achieve. Under the cognitive framework, the same organisms were redescribed as computing over internal representations, and these previously anomalous findings became normal science under the new paradigm.

The cumulative reading resists this interpretation. Behaviorism's empirical core — the laws of classical and operant conditioning, the quantification of reinforcement schedules — survived the transition intact and remains in active use in clinical, educational, and organizational settings. What changed, on this view, was the *vocabulary layered on top* of a continuing experimental program, not the program itself. A revolution that leaves most of the old paradigm's experimental results standing looks less like a Kuhnian Gestalt switch and more like a reformulation.

Kurt Danziger's *Constructing the Subject* (1990) [ref: danziger1990 Danziger 1990] advanced a third position that cuts across both. Danziger argued that psychology's basic *objects* — "intelligence," "attitude," "personality," "memory" — are not discovered in nature but *constructed* by the investigative methods used to study them. The intelligence test manufactures "intelligence" as a measurable, rankable quantity; the attitude scale produces "attitude"; the reaction-time experiment produces "mental process" as a temporal measurable. On this view psychology is neither a cumulative march toward truth nor a sequence of paradigm replacements but a succession of *methodological regimes*, each producing the phenomena it then claims to have found. The history to write is therefore a history of methods and their administrative settings — the laboratory, the clinic, the school, the personnel office — not a history of discoveries about a fixed mind.

None of these three readings is now universally accepted, and a fourth — that psychology is *inherently pluralistic*, a federation of approaches addressing different aspects of a many-layered object rather than a single linear science converging on one vocabulary — has gained ground among historians and among working scientists weary of internecine competition. The four readings are not mere tastes; they make different predictions about whether contemporary cognitive neuroscience will absorb, displace, or merely coexist with cognitive and behavioral accounts.

**Bridge.** The paradigm-versus-cumulation question **builds toward** every subsequent chapter of this curriculum, because each unit in §29 inherits the methodological commitments of one school or another; it **appears again in** the learning-and-memory chapter [29.04.01] and the cognition chapter [29.05.01], where behaviorist and cognitive accounts still compete for the same data. **This is exactly** why the same datum — a rat learning a maze — admits two honest descriptions under two vocabularies; **the foundational reason** psychology resists consolidation into a single paradigm is that its object, the human being, is simultaneously a biological system, an information processor, and a social agent, and no one description exhausts all three. **Putting these together**, the historiographical model **generalises** to the other human sciences, and **the bridge is** the recognition that what a psychologist counts as "the phenomenon" decides in advance which school's vocabulary will fit the data.

On Danziger's thesis the IQ test does not merely measure a natural quantity called intelligence; it *operationalizes* a latent trait by fixing a particular set of tasks (verbal, numerical, spatial, and so on), and then the resulting score becomes, by social and administrative practice, the thing called "intelligence." The objection is blunted by noting that the *meaning* of the surviving findings changed even when the findings themselves did not. A reinforcement schedule that behaviorism read as a direct environment-action law is redescribed by cognitivism as the output of an intervening representation, and that redescriptive change alters which questions are worth asking and which explanations count. A revolution in vocabulary is still a revolution if it resets the discipline's explanatory targets, even when it preserves the data.

## The behaviorism–cognitivism debate [Master]

The deepest historiographical dispute in modern psychology is whether the field's proper object is *behavior* or *the mental processes that produce behavior*. This is not a settled question that the cognitive revolution answered; it is the fault line along which the discipline still organizes its methods, its journals, and its therapies. Treating the dispute as a contest between two defensible positions, rather than as a story in which one side simply won, is the precondition for understanding contemporary pluralism.

**Position 1 — Behaviorism (Watson 1913; Skinner 1938).** Psychology can be a science only of what is publicly observable. "Mind," "image," "idea," and "representation" name private events that no second observer can verify, and a science built on such events is condemned to the introspectionists' fate: trained observers disagree, and there is no arbiter. Watson's 1913 manifesto proposed that psychology become "a purely objective experimental branch of natural science" whose theoretical goal is "the prediction and control of behavior" [ref: watson1913 Watson 1913]. Skinner refined the program: behavior is shaped by its consequences — reinforcement increases the frequency of a response, punishment decreases it, extinction returns the rate to baseline when reinforcement ceases — and operant conditioning supplies lawful, replicable relations between environment and action [ref: skinner1938 Skinner 1938]. The yield is substantial and durable: applied behavior analysis remains the most evidence-based intervention for many features of autism spectrum disorder, and reinforcement principles organize animal training, education, and organizational management. On this view the cognitive vocabulary is a speculative detour that re-imports the very unobservables psychology struggled for half a century to escape; what matters scientifically is the functional relation between environment and action, and the rest is interpretation we cannot test.

**Position 2 — Cognitivism (Neisser 1967 and the 1950s synthesis).** The behaviorist ban on the mental left psychology unable to explain its own most striking phenomena: language, planning, selective attention, working memory, problem-solving, and the systematic errors of reasoning documented by the heuristics-and-biases program. Chomsky's 1959 review demonstrated that children produce sentences they have never been rewarded for and make rule-governed errors that no schedule of imitation and reinforcement can reproduce [ref: chomsky1959 Chomsky 1959]. The cognitive synthesis replied by treating the mind as an information-processing system: behavior is the *output* of computations over internal *representations*, and the representations themselves — encoded, stored, retrieved, transformed — are the proper scientific object [ref: neisser1967 Neisser 1967]. On this view excluding the mental is not rigor but a self-imposed blindness; the interesting science begins at precisely the point where behaviorism declared the investigation closed. The cognitive program's productivity — precise models of attention, memory, language, and decision, plus the theoretical foundation of artificial intelligence and the empirical backbone of cognitive-behavioral therapy — is taken as confirmation that the mind was the right object all along.

The two positions are not symmetric in current prestige. Cognitivism dominates academic psychology, the cognitive neurosciences, and the computational disciplines that border them, and its vocabulary is the default of contemporary textbooks. Yet the behaviorist critique retains real force. The replication crisis documented across cognitive and social psychology (see [29.01.01]) struck hardest at exactly the kinds of subtle, memory- and judgment-based findings that the cognitive vocabulary produces, while the behaviorist tradition's tightly controlled single-subject designs and direct rate-of-response measures have often replicated well. The dispute is therefore live rather than archival: it is a disagreement about *what kind of object* psychology is investigating, and because that choice decides which methods and which forms of evidence count, it cannot be settled by any single experiment. A finding that convinces a cognitivist (an internal representation inferred from a reaction-time pattern) is, to a thoroughgoing behaviorist, precisely the kind of inferred, unobservable construct that the manifesto excluded; a finding that convinces a behaviorist (a clean reinforcement-rate curve) is, to a cognitivist, the mere input-output surface of an underlying computation. The same laboratory bench supports both readings.

A pluralist resolution, increasingly common among historians and among practitioners who work across paradigms, treats the two positions as *complementary levels of description* rather than competing claims about a single object. Behavior is real and lawful, and the functional relations behaviorism catalogs are genuine; representations are also real, in the sense that an organism that can be modeled as computing over them is better predicted than one that cannot. On the pluralist view the mistake is the imperial one — the claim that one vocabulary must displace the other — and the history of the discipline reads as an extended argument that no single carving of the human being exhausts the object.

**Synthesis.** The behaviorism–cognitivism dispute **appears again in** every applied chapter of this curriculum — therapy [29.10.01], learning [29.04.01], language within cognition [29.05.01] — and **builds toward** the modern biopsychosocial synthesis, in which behavior, computation, and brain mechanism are treated as levels of one account rather than rival ontologies. **This is exactly** the tension Kuhn predicted: rival paradigms talk past each other because their vocabularies carve the phenomenon differently and assign the burden of proof to different quantities. **The foundational reason** the dispute will not die is that human beings are simultaneously acting bodies and representing minds, and any framework that recognizes only one half mis-predicts the other. **The central insight** of contemporary pluralism is that no single carving exhausts so layered an object. **Putting these together**, the historiographical lesson **generalises** across the human sciences: psychology's history is not a march toward one true vocabulary but a disciplined rotation among partial descriptions, and **the bridge is** that each school, read as a lens rather than a creed, still discloses something the others miss.

## Connections [Master]

This unit supplies the historiographical frame that the methods unit [29.01.01] takes for granted: the scientific method became psychology's default only after Wundt detached the field from philosophy of mind, and the behaviorist-versus-cognitive split still determines which methods a researcher reaches for first.

The learning-and-memory chapter [29.04.01] continues the story begun here in technical detail. Classical and operant conditioning are the empirical core the behaviorist school bequeathed, and the cognitive counter-evidence — Tolman's cognitive maps, Garcia's prepared learning, the encoding-and-retrieval framework — reappears there as the central data the chapter must reconcile.

The cognition and intelligence chapter [29.05.01] inherits the cognitive revolution directly. George Miller's magical number seven, the Atkinson-Shiffrin multi-store model, and the information-processing vocabulary are the school this unit introduces, and the chapter formalizes the very internal representations Watson refused to discuss.

The therapy and treatment chapter [29.10.01] carries the same fault line into the clinic: behavior therapy descends from Watson and Skinner, cognitive-behavioral therapy from the cognitive synthesis, and psychodynamic therapy from Freud, so that three schools founded a century apart still practice side by side in contemporary mental-health care.

The neuroscience chapter [29.02.01] extends the historiography forward: cognitive neuroscience presents itself as the mechanistic underlayer that finally grounds both the behaviorist's laws and the cognitivist's representations in identified neural circuits, and the open question is whether it absorbs its predecessors or merely adds a fourth vocabulary to the federation.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The discipline's name and its experimental birth both belong to Wundt. In the *Grundzüge der physiologischen Psychologie* (1874) he argued that psychology should study immediate experience by experimental means, and in 1879 he established at Leipzig the first laboratory dedicated to the new science, drawing students from across Europe and America who then founded the field's national traditions [ref: wundt1874 Wundt 1874]. Wundt was explicit that experiment could reach only the *lower* mental processes — sensation, perception, simple feeling, reaction time — while the higher processes of language, myth, custom, and culture required a separate *Völkerpsychologie* pursued by comparative-historical methods. That distinction, and the sheer scale of the second program across ten volumes, was largely ignored by his American heirs, who read Wundt as the founder of a unified experimental science and treated his cultural psychology as a footnote.

William James's *The Principles of Psychology* (1890) gave the field a different founding gesture: not a laboratory program but a compendium that absorbed physiology, introspection, and evolutionary theory into a functional account of mental life as the "adjustment of inner to outer relations" [ref: james1890 James 1890]. Where Wundt sought elements, James sought functions; where Wundt built a school around a method and an institution, James wrote a book that made psychology conceivable to a generation of American readers and seeded the functionalist movement that would, through Dewey and Angell at Chicago, become behaviorism's immediate predecessor.

The behaviorist turn came when Watson, frustrated by the irreproducibility of introspective reports and drawn by the practical leverage of conditioning work pioneered by Ivan Pavlov, proposed in 1913 to redefine the field around behavior alone [ref: watson1913 Watson 1913]. The cognitive counter-turn is conventionally dated to a cluster of works in a single decade — Miller's 1956 paper on the magical number seven, Broadbent's 1958 *Perception and Communication*, Chomsky's 1959 review [ref: chomsky1959 Chomsky 1959], and Neisser's 1967 textbook, which named and framed the field [ref: neisser1967 Neisser 1967]. After this convergence "the mind" re-entered psychology as a legitimate object of controlled experiment, now described in the vocabulary of information and computation rather than introspected contents, and the discipline that had spent forty years studying inputs and outputs turned back to the machinery between them.
