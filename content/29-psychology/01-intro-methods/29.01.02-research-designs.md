## Intuition [Beginner]

Psychologists use four main research designs to study behavior and mental processes. Each design answers a different kind of question, and each has distinct strengths and limitations. The choice of design shapes what conclusions a study can support.

In a true experiment, participants are randomly assigned to different conditions. Randomization is the gold standard because it spreads individual differences evenly across groups, ruling out alternative explanations. A well-run experiment can establish that one variable causes another.

Many important questions cannot be studied with experiments. You cannot randomly assign people to experience trauma, grow up in poverty, or smoke for decades. Correlational studies measure naturally occurring relationships between variables without any manipulation. They reveal whether variables move together, but they cannot prove causation.

Quasi-experiments study groups that already exist. A researcher might compare a school that adopted a new curriculum to a similar school that did not. Because students were not randomly assigned, the groups may differ in other ways, which weakens any causal claim.

Meta-analysis takes a different approach. Instead of running one new study, it combines the results of many existing studies statistically. By pooling data across dozens of experiments, meta-analysis produces more reliable conclusions than any single study could, especially when individual studies are small.

The skill of a researcher is matching the design to the question. Some questions demand experiments; others forbid them on ethical or practical grounds. Knowing what each design can and cannot prove is the foundation of reading psychological research critically.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A researcher wonders whether regular exercise reduces anxiety. She can study this question two different ways, and the two designs lead to very different conclusions.

In the experimental version, she recruits 60 volunteers and randomly assigns 30 to an eight-week running program and 30 to a waitlist control group. After eight weeks, the running group reports lower anxiety than the control group. Because assignment was random, she can conclude that the program likely caused the reduction.

In the correlational version, she surveys 200 adults instead, asking how much they exercise and how anxious they feel. She finds that people who exercise more report less anxiety. But she cannot conclude that exercise causes lower anxiety. Naturally calmer people may simply be more drawn to exercise, or a third factor like general health could explain both.

This is the core lesson of research design. The two studies describe the same relationship, but only the first supports a causal claim, because only the first used random assignment. Design determines what a study is allowed to conclude.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **research design** is the complete plan that links how participants are selected and assigned, what is manipulated and measured, and what inferences the data can support. The vocabulary below is shared across all empirical psychology.

### Experimental design

A true experiment requires four components: (1) manipulation of an **independent variable** (IV), (2) **random assignment** of participants to conditions, (3) measurement of a **dependent variable** (DV), and (4) control of known confounds. The **control group** receives the baseline or no-treatment condition; the **experimental group** receives the manipulation.

Two procedural safeguards protect against bias. A **placebo** control gives the control group an inert treatment that mimics the real one, isolating the treatment's specific effect from the psychological effect of receiving any treatment at all. In a **double-blind** design, neither participants nor the staff measuring outcomes know who received which condition, eliminating expectancy effects in both groups.

**Between-subjects designs** place different participants in each condition; each person contributes one score. **Within-subjects designs** place the same participants in every condition; each person serves as their own control, which increases statistical power but exposes the design to **order effects** (practice, fatigue, carryover). **Counterbalancing** the order of conditions across participants neutralizes these confounds.

**Factorial designs** cross two or more independent variables simultaneously. A $2 \times 2$ design has two IVs, each with two levels, producing four conditions. This allows the researcher to estimate **main effects** (the separate effect of each IV) and **interactions** (whether the effect of one IV depends on the level of the other). Interactions are often the most informative result: a treatment that works for one group but not another appears as an interaction, not a main effect.

### Quasi-experimental design

When random assignment is impossible or unethical, a **quasi-experiment** compares groups that already differ on the variable of interest. The three principal variants are the **nonequivalent control group design** (comparing two intact groups, as in the curriculum example above), the **interrupted time series design** (tracking a single group's outcomes across many time points before and after an intervention), and **regression discontinuity** (assigning treatment by a cutoff score on a pre-test, then comparing outcomes just above and just below the cutoff).

Because intact groups are not equivalent by construction, quasi-experiments rest on statistical adjustment rather than randomization. The causal inference is weaker but not absent: a well-analyzed quasi-experiment, especially regression discontinuity, can approach the credibility of a true experiment.

### Correlational design

A correlational design measures two or more variables without manipulation and quantifies their association. The **Pearson correlation coefficient** $r$ measures the strength and direction of a linear relationship:

$$r_{xy} = \frac{\sum_i (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sqrt{\sum_i (x_i - \bar{x})^2}\sqrt{\sum_i (y_i - \bar{y})^2}}$$

$r$ ranges from $-1$ (perfect negative) through $0$ (no linear relationship) to $+1$ (perfect positive). The **coefficient of determination** $r^2$ gives the proportion of variance shared by the two variables.

Correlation does not imply causation for two reasons. The **directionality problem**: even if $X$ and $Y$ are related, the design cannot show which came first or whether one drives the other. The **third-variable problem**: a lurking variable $Z$ may cause both $X$ and $Y$, manufacturing an association between them that reflects no direct link at all.

**Statistical control** partially addresses the third-variable problem. In **partial correlation**, the researcher statistically removes the influence of a suspected confound $Z$ from both $X$ and $Y$, then re-examines their relationship. In **multiple regression**, the outcome is modeled as a function of several predictors simultaneously:

$$\hat{Y} = b_0 + b_1 X_1 + b_2 X_2 + \cdots + b_k X_k$$

Each coefficient $b_j$ estimates the unique association between predictor $X_j$ and the outcome, holding the other predictors constant. Statistical control is powerful but assumes the researcher has measured every important confound, an assumption that randomization does not require.

### Meta-analysis

A **meta-analysis** combines the quantitative results of many studies into a single pooled estimate. The first step is converting each study's result into a common **effect size** on a comparable scale:

- **Cohen's $d$**: the difference between two group means in pooled standard-deviation units, $d = (\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2)/s_{\text{pooled}}$.
- **Hedges' $g$**: a small-sample-corrected version of $d$, preferred when studies have small samples.
- **Odds ratio (OR)**: for binary outcomes, the ratio of the odds of an event in the treatment group to the odds in the control group.

Two pooling models differ in what they assume about the true effect. A **fixed-effect model** assumes every study estimates one identical true effect; differences between studies are pure sampling error. A **random-effects model** assumes each study has its own true effect drawn from a distribution of effects, and the goal is to estimate the mean (and spread) of that distribution. Random-effects models are the default in psychology, where studies almost always differ in samples, procedures, and settings.

## Evidence pattern: threats to validity and meta-analytic evaluation [Intermediate+]

Designing a study is only half the battle. The other half is ruling out the rival explanations that can make a result misleading. Donald Campbell and Julian Stanley's 1963 monograph gave psychology a permanent checklist for this work: the threats to validity.

### Threats to internal validity

**Internal validity** asks whether the study's design supports a causal claim — whether the change in the DV can be attributed to the IV rather than to something else. Campbell and Stanley named seven principal threats:

1. **History** — an outside event, other than the treatment, occurs between the pre-test and post-test and affects the outcome.
2. **Maturation** — participants change over time on their own (growing older, more tired, more experienced), independent of the treatment.
3. **Testing** — taking a pre-test changes performance on the post-test through practice or familiarity.
4. **Instrumentation** — the measuring instrument changes over time (observers get stricter, a test is re-calibrated), altering scores.
5. **Regression to the mean** — groups selected for extreme scores tend to score closer to the average on re-testing, mimicking a treatment effect.
6. **Selection** — in designs without random assignment, pre-existing differences between groups explain the post-test gap.
7. **Attrition** — differential dropout (more troubled participants leave one condition than the other) changes the composition of groups.

Random assignment neutralizes selection and, through equivalence at baseline, blunts history, maturation, and regression threats. This is the structural reason experiments support causal claims and quasi-experiments do not: randomization is a blanket defense against an entire family of threats at once.

### External and ecological validity

**External validity** asks whether the result generalizes beyond the specific participants, setting, and time of the study. **Ecological validity** is a narrower concern: does the laboratory procedure resemble the real-world situation the researcher wants to understand? A memory task using nonsense syllables may have low ecological validity even if it has high internal validity. The two can trade off: tight laboratory control raises internal validity but can lower ecological validity.

### Evaluating a body of evidence: the meta-analytic toolkit

A single study, however well designed, is one noisy estimate. Meta-analysis evaluates the whole body of evidence at once.

A **forest plot** displays each study's effect size and confidence interval as a row, with a square whose size reflects the study's weight and a horizontal line spanning its confidence interval. The bottom row shows the pooled estimate as a diamond. Researchers read a forest plot for three things at once: the pooled effect, its precision, and the spread of individual studies around it.

A **funnel plot** checks for **publication bias** — the tendency for significant results to be published and null results to languish in file drawers. It plots effect size against a measure of precision (or sample size). In an unbiased literature the studies form a symmetric inverted funnel. **Asymmetry** — small studies reporting larger effects than large studies — signals that small studies with null results were never published.

Several statistical tests formalize this diagnosis. **Rosenthal's fail-safe N** estimates how many unpublished null studies would be needed to reduce the pooled effect to non-significance. **Egger's test** regresses standardized effect against precision and tests whether the intercept departs from zero, a formal signal of small-study bias.

**Heterogeneity** asks whether the studies are estimating the same effect at all. The **Cochran $Q$ statistic** tests the null hypothesis that all studies share one true effect; the **$I^2$ statistic** reports the percentage of between-study variance that reflects real differences rather than chance (with rough benchmarks of 25%, 50%, 75% as low, moderate, and high). High heterogeneity does not invalidate a meta-analysis, but it demands explanation through subgroup analysis or meta-regression.

Two infrastructural frameworks govern how this work is reported. The **Cochrane Collaboration** sets the methodological standard for systematic reviews in medicine and behavioral science. The **PRISMA** guidelines (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses) require authors to document every step: the search strategy, the number of records screened and excluded, and the reasons for exclusion, so that the review itself can be audited and replicated.

A weight-loss program selects its participants from the heaviest 5% of a population. After the program, participants weigh less on average. The program director claims the program caused the weight loss. Identify the Campbell-Stanley threat that most undermines this claim, and explain why random assignment would have helped.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If you pick people specifically because their scores are extreme, what happens to their scores the next time you measure them, even with no treatment at all?

The dominant threat is **regression to the mean**. Participants were selected for extremely high weights, and extreme scores tend to move toward the population average on re-measurement purely through statistical regression, with no treatment necessary. Part (or all) of the observed weight loss could reflect this regression rather than any effect of the program.

Random assignment would have helped in two ways. First, a randomly assigned control group drawn from the same extreme subset would also regress toward the mean, so the regression artifact would cancel out in the treatment-minus-control comparison. Second, randomization equalizes the groups at baseline, removing the selection confound entirely. Without a control group, there is no way to separate a real program effect from statistical regression.

A researcher reports $r = -.60$ between weekly hours of social-media use and self-reported life satisfaction in a sample of teenagers. State two distinct reasons this correlation does not justify the claim that social media causes lower life satisfaction, and name one statistical technique that could partially address one of them.

Two reasons:

1. **Directionality problem.** The correlation is symmetric in its information about causation. It is equally consistent with social media reducing satisfaction and with dissatisfied teenagers seeking out more screen time. A correlational design cannot distinguish cause from effect.

2. **Third-variable problem.** A confound such as sleep deprivation, social isolation, or family stress could cause both heavier social-media use and lower satisfaction, manufacturing the observed association without any direct causal link between the two measured variables.

This works only for confounds the researcher has actually measured, which is why random assignment remains the stronger defense.

Interpret the pooled effect, then explain what the funnel-plot asymmetry implies about whether $d = 0.42$ is a trustworthy estimate.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What does a confidence interval tell you about the pooled effect, and what does funnel-plot asymmetry tell you about which studies are missing from the literature?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The pooled effect of $d = 0.42$ is a medium effect by Cohen's conventions, and the confidence interval $[0.28, 0.56]$ excludes zero, so under the pooling model the effect is statistically significant.

However, the funnel-plot asymmetry is a red flag for **publication bias**. The pattern — small studies showing large effects and large studies showing near-null effects — is exactly what you expect when small studies with non-significant results go unpublished while small studies that "got lucky" with large effects enter the literature. Large studies, having more power, are more likely to be published regardless of outcome, so they cluster near the true (smaller) effect. Under this reading, the pooled estimate of $0.42$ is inflated by the selective absence of small null studies, and the true effect is probably closer to the large-study estimates near zero. A sensitivity analysis (fail-safe N, Egger's test, or a trim-and-fill correction) would be the next step.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, conceptual).**

Two researchers study the same question — does a workplace wellness program reduce sick days — using different quasi-experimental designs. Researcher A uses a nonequivalent control group (comparing one company that adopted the program to a similar company that did not). Researcher B uses an interrupted time series (tracking sick days in one company for two years before and two years after adoption). Describe the distinctive threat each design faces, and explain why the two designs provide complementary evidence rather than redundant evidence.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What confound is specific to comparing two different companies, and what confound is specific to a single timeline that happens to include a historical event?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Researcher A's nonequivalent control group design is most vulnerable to **selection**: the two companies may have differed in workforce health, culture, or job demands before the program began, and those differences could explain any gap in sick days. A pre-test helps, but pre-existing group differences remain the central threat.

Researcher B's interrupted time series design is most vulnerable to **history**: any event coinciding with the program's launch — a flu season ending, a management change, a new HR policy — could shift the trend in sick days and masquerade as a program effect. The long pre-program baseline helps rule out maturation and regression, but a single concurrent external shock remains the key threat.

The designs are complementary because their primary threats are different. A selection-based confound in design A would not produce the same artifact in design B (which uses one company as its own control), and a history-based confound in design B would not necessarily appear in design A (which has a concurrent control company). If both designs point to the same conclusion, the rival explanations that plague each one individually are less plausible. This logic — triangulating across designs with different weakness profiles — is the core of the Campbell tradition's argument for converging evidence.

</details>

## Advanced designs and causal inference [Master]

Modern causal inference reframes the question "did $X$ cause $Y$?" as a problem of comparing what happened to what would have happened. This counterfactual framing, developed by Donald Rubin and Paul Holland, unifies the seemingly disparate advanced designs below.

### The Rubin causal model and potential outcomes

The **potential outcomes framework** imagines that each participant has two possible outcomes: $Y_i(1)$ if treated and $Y_i(0)$ if untreated. The **individual treatment effect** is $Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)$. The fundamental problem of causal inference is that both outcomes are never observed for the same person — you either receive the treatment or you do not.

Because individual effects are unobservable, inference targets averages. The **average treatment effect** is $\text{ATE} = E[Y(1) - Y(0)]$ across the population; the **sample average treatment effect on the treated** ($\text{SATT}$) averages over those who actually received treatment. Random assignment solves the identification problem because it makes treatment independent of potential outcomes, so the difference in observed group means is an unbiased estimate of the ATE. Every non-randomized design below is, at root, an attempt to recover this independence by other means.

### Quasi-experimental designs for causal inference

**Propensity score matching** estimates each participant's probability of receiving treatment given observed covariates, then matches treated and untreated participants with similar probabilities. This balances observed confounds between groups, mimicking randomization — but only for confounds that were measured. Unmeasured confounds remain a threat, which is the recurring limit of all selection-on-observables methods.

**Instrumental variables (IV)** tackle unmeasured confounds head-on. An instrument $Z$ affects treatment assignment but has no direct path to the outcome except through treatment. Under the Angrist-Imbens-Rubin assumptions (relevance, exclusion restriction, monotonicity), IV identifies the **local average treatment effect** — the causal effect for the subset of participants whose treatment status was shifted by the instrument ("compliers"). The cost is a sharper but narrower estimand: you learn the effect on compliers, not on everyone.

**Difference-in-differences (DiD)** compares the *change* in outcomes before and after an intervention in a treated group to the same change in an untreated comparison group. By differencing out the time-invariant differences between groups, DiD removes fixed confounds. Its identifying assumption is **parallel trends**: absent treatment, the two groups' outcomes would have evolved along parallel paths. When a treatment is assigned at a sharp cutoff, **regression discontinuity** compares units just above and just below the threshold. A **sharp RDD** assumes perfect compliance at the cutoff; a **fuzzy RDD** treats the cutoff as an instrument for actual treatment receipt. Under smoothness assumptions, units near the cutoff are effectively randomized, and the design can deliver credible local causal estimates.

### Advanced meta-analysis

The simple random-effects model assumes studies exchangeable around a common mean. Two generalizations relax this. **Multilevel (hierarchical) meta-analysis** models variance at three levels — sampling variance within studies, variance between outcomes within a study, and variance between studies — and is essential when a single study contributes multiple effect sizes. **Bayesian meta-analysis** places prior distributions on the pooled effect and heterogeneity, yielding full posterior distributions rather than point estimates and accommodating weak or sparse evidence more gracefully.

**Meta-regression** extends the random-effects model by regressing study-level effect sizes on study-level covariates (year, sample age, design type, setting) to explain heterogeneity rather than merely quantify it.

Heterogeneity is summarized by three statistics. **Cochran's $Q$** tests whether between-study variance exceeds what sampling error alone would produce. **$I^2$** expresses the proportion of total variance due to real between-study differences. **$\tau^2$** estimates the variance of the true effect distribution itself (unlike $I^2$, it is insensitive to the number of studies and is the preferred measure for comparing heterogeneity across meta-analyses).

### Publication bias and its correction

Beyond funnel plots and Egger's test, the **PET-PEESE** framework corrects for publication bias by exploiting the relationship between effect size and standard error. Under the assumption that publication selection favors significant results, the most precise studies carry the least bias. PET (precision-effect test) regresses effect sizes on standard errors; if the intercept is non-zero, it estimates the bias-corrected effect. PEESE (precision-effect estimate with standard error) refines this when a real effect exists. The method remains debated — strong selection can defeat it — but it represents the field's most systematic attempt to estimate the effect behind the published record.

### Designing for replication: the credibility reforms

The Open Science Collaboration's 2015 replication of 100 psychology studies — only 36% replicated significantly, with mean effect sizes roughly halved — turned design quality into an existential concern for the field. The reforms operate at the level of research design itself.

**Pre-registration** fixes the analysis plan before data are seen, eliminating the flexibility that lets researchers manufacture significance (the "garden of forking paths"). **Registered Reports** move peer review to before data collection: the journal accepts the design on its merits, committing to publish the result whatever it is, which eliminates publication bias for null findings by construction. The **ManyLabs** projects run the same protocol across dozens of sites with thousands of participants, producing tight effect-size estimates and revealing how effects vary across samples and settings — a direct empirical attack on the external-validity question.

### The WEIRD critique and cultural variation in effect sizes

Henrich, Heine, and Norenzayan's 2010 argument that psychology's participant pool is overwhelmingly Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, and Democratic applies with special force to research design. An effect estimated in WEIRD samples is not a design flaw in the narrow sense, but it is a generalization claim that itself requires evidential support. Meta-analysis makes this testable: when the same effect is estimated across diverse samples, heterogeneity often rises, and the pooled WEIRD estimate can diverge from the global pattern. The remedy is not to discard WEIRD findings but to treat them as one stratum in a multi-sample design and to meta-analyze across cultures rather than assume transportability.

The replication crisis is a live case study in the social epistemology of science.

- **Sensation and perception [29.03.01].** Perception research relies heavily on within-subjects experimental designs (each observer serves as their own baseline) and on signal-detection theory, a model-based approach to separating true sensitivity from response bias.

- **Psychological disorders [29.09.01].** Clinical trials of therapies and medications use randomized controlled designs, placebo and waitlist controls, and double-blind procedures. Meta-analysis is the standard tool for synthesizing the treatment-outcome literature that informs clinical guidelines.

- **Cross-cultural and indigenous psychology [29.12.01].** The WEIRD critique, developed here at the level of design, becomes the central organizing problem of the cross-cultural unit: which effects transport across populations, and how should research designs change to test transportability rather than assume it?

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

### Fisher and the invention of randomization

The logic of random assignment was systematized by R. A. Fisher at the Rothamsted Experimental Station in the 1920s and codified in *The Design of Experiments* (1935). Fisher's three contributions were inseparable: randomization as the physical basis for valid inference, the analysis of variance as the machinery for partitioning treatment effects from error, and the null-hypothesis testing framework that connected design to probability. Before Fisher, agricultural researchers assigned treatments systematically and argued informally that their layouts were "fair"; Fisher showed that only randomization licenses a valid probability statement about whether an observed difference exceeds chance.

### Campbell and Stanley: validity as a research program

Donald Campbell's career was devoted to a single proposition: the credibility of a causal claim depends on how well the design rules out rival explanations. The 1963 monograph *Experimental and Quasi-Experimental Designs for Research*, written with Julian Stanley, distilled this into the threats-to-validity checklist still taught today. Campbell later expanded the framework with Thomas Cook (1979) into four validity types — internal, external, construct, and statistical conclusion — and championed quasi-experimental designs not as inferior substitutes for experiments but as principled tools for the many real-world questions where randomization is impossible. The Campbell Collaboration, founded in 2000, applies this tradition to social-policy evidence as the behavioral-science counterpart to Cochrane.

### The invention of meta-analysis

The term "meta-analysis" was coined by Gene Glass in a 1976 presidential address, where he argued that the accumulated findings of a research literature could be treated as data to be analyzed rather than narrated. Glass was responding to a furor over psychotherapy outcomes: Hans Eysenck had claimed psychotherapy was no more effective than no treatment, basing this on a narrative review, while Glass's meta-analysis of 375 studies found a substantial average effect. The episode demonstrated that two experts reading the same literature could reach opposite conclusions, and that systematic pooling could resolve what narrative synthesis could not. The synthesis methods were formalized by Hedges and Olkin (1985) and made practical for medicine by the Cochrane Collaboration from 1993 onward.

### From design to credibility

The replication crisis reframed the history of research design. What began as a technical discipline — how to assign participants and measure outcomes — became a movement about the social structure of science: which results get published, which analyses get run, and who gets to check. Pre-registration, Registered Reports, and large-scale replications are design reforms, but they are also institutional reforms: they change the relationship between researchers, journals, and evidence. The arc runs from Fisher's single-experiment logic, through Campbell's multi-design triangulation, to the contemporary vision of a literature whose reliability is itself a designed property rather than a happy accident.

1. Fisher, R. A., *The Design of Experiments* (Oliver & Boyd, 1935). Founded the modern theory of randomization, analysis of variance, and null-hypothesis testing.

2. Campbell, D. T. and Stanley, J. C., *Experimental and Quasi-Experimental Designs for Research* (Rand McNally, 1963). The threats-to-validity checklist that organizes this unit.

3. Cook, T. D. and Campbell, D. T., *Quasi-Experimentation: Design and Analysis Issues for Field Settings* (Houghton Mifflin, 1979). Extended Campbell-Stanley into the four-validity typology.

4. Glass, G. V., "Primary, Secondary, and Meta-Analysis of Research," *Educational Researcher* 5(10) (1976), 3-8. Coined "meta-analysis" and demonstrated its power on the psychotherapy-outcomes debate.

6. Hedges, L. V. and Olkin, I., *Statistical Methods for Meta-Analysis* (Academic Press, 1985). Formalized fixed- and random-effects pooling and the small-sample corrections bearing Hedges' name.

7. Rubin, D. B., "Estimating Causal Effects of Treatments in Randomized and Nonrandomized Studies," *Journal of Educational Psychology* 66(5) (1974), 688-701. Introduced the potential-outcomes framework that grounds modern causal inference.

8. Holland, P. W., "Statistics and Causal Inference," *Journal of the American Statistical Association* 81(396) (1986), 945-960. The accessible exposition of the Rubin model and "no causation without manipulation."

9. Cook, T. D. and Shadish, W. R., "Social Experimentation: A Method for Assessing the Outcomes of Educational and Social Programs," in *The Review of Sociology* (1986). Bridged the Campbell tradition and applied policy research.

11. Thistlethwaite, D. L. and Campbell, D. T., "Regression-Discontinuity Analysis: An Alternative to the Ex Post Facto Experiment," *Journal of Educational Psychology* 51(6) (1960), 309-317. The original regression-discontinuity design.

13. Egger, M., Smith, G. D., Schneider, M., and Minder, C., "Bias in Meta-Analysis Detected by a Simple, Graphical Test," *BMJ* 315(7109) (1997), 629-634. The Egger test for funnel-plot asymmetry.

14. Higgins, J. P. T., Thompson, S. G., Deeks, J. J., and Altman, D. G., "Measuring Inconsistency in Meta-Analyses," *BMJ* 327(7414) (2003), 557-560. Introduced the $I^2$ heterogeneity statistic.

18. Moher, D., Liberati, A., Tetzlaff, J., and Altman, D. G., "Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses: The PRISMA Statement," *PLoS Medicine* 6(7) (2009), e1000097. The reporting standard for meta-analyses.

19. Stanley, T. D. and Doucouliagos, H., "Meta-Regression Approximations to the Publication Bias Correction," *Journal of Economic Surveys* 28(2) (2014), 276-291. The PET-PEESE framework for publication-bias correction.

20. Nosek, B. A., Ebersole, C. R., DeHaven, A. C., and Mellor, D. T., "The Preregistration Revolution," *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 115(11) (2018), 2600-2606. The case for pre-registration and Registered Reports as design-level reforms.
