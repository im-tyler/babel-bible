## Intuition [Beginner]

Classical test theory, the framework of the previous unit, treats a test as a single observed score plus a single lump of error. Item response theory turns the lens around: it models each item on its own. The guiding question shifts from "what is the person's true total score?" to "what is the probability that this particular person answers this particular item correctly?"

The engine behind that shift is a hidden quantity called the **latent trait**, written $\theta$ (theta). A person's $\theta$ is their standing on whatever the test measures — reasoning skill, reading fluency, anxiety level. Each item carries a **difficulty** $b$. The probability of a correct answer rises smoothly as the gap between ability and difficulty grows.

The shape of that rise is an S-shaped curve. When ability sits far below difficulty, the probability of success is near zero. When ability sits far above, the probability is near one. Right at the crossing point — where ability equals difficulty — the probability is exactly one half, a coin flip. This S-curve is the **item characteristic curve**, and it is the basic object of the theory.

Why model the item rather than the total score? Because items differ, and a person's raw score depends on which items they happened to see. Item response theory places every person and every item on one shared scale, so that a comparison of two people does not depend on which items were used, and a comparison of two items does not depend on which people took them. That invariance is something classical test theory cannot offer.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A reasoning item has difficulty $b = 0.5$. Two students attempt it: student A has ability $\theta = 1.0$, student B has ability $\theta = 0.0$. The Rasch rule for the probability of a correct answer is

$$P(\text{correct}) = \frac{e^{\,\theta - b}}{1 + e^{\,\theta - b}}.$$

The quantity $\theta - b$ is the gap between the student's ability and the item's difficulty. A positive gap favours the student; a negative gap favours the item.

For student A the gap is $1.0 - 0.5 = 0.5$. For student B the gap is $0.0 - 0.5 = -0.5$. The arithmetic for each is:

```
Student A (gap = +0.5):  e^0.5  = 1.65   ->  P = 1.65 / (1 + 1.65) = 1.65 / 2.65 = 0.62
Student B (gap = -0.5):  e^-0.5 = 0.61   ->  P = 0.61 / (1 + 0.61) = 0.61 / 1.61 = 0.38
```

The stronger student has a 62% chance of success and the weaker one a 38% chance. Notice that both probabilities stay well away from 0 and 1: even the weaker student has a real chance of being right, and even the stronger student can miss it. That is the forgiving shape of the logistic curve — never a cliff.

The shared unit of $\theta$ and $b$ is the **logit**. A difference of one logit between ability and difficulty multiplies the odds of a correct answer by $e \approx 2.72$, so logits are the natural currency in which ability and difficulty are compared.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Item response theory** (IRT) [ref: TODO_REF Rasch1960] is a family of models for the probability that person $j$ with latent ability $\theta_j$ gives a designated response to item $i$ with parameters $\xi_i$. For the dichotomous case (correct/incorrect, coded $X_{ij} \in \{0,1\}$), the models are nested by adding one parameter at a time.

**1PL — the Rasch model.** Each item carries a single difficulty $b_i$, and [ref: TODO_REF Rasch1960]

$$\Pr(X_{ij} = 1 \mid \theta_j, b_i) = \frac{\exp(\theta_j - b_i)}{1 + \exp(\theta_j - b_i)} = \mathrm{logistic}(\theta_j - b_i).$$

The equivalent **logit form** is the additive identity that defines the model:

$$\mathrm{logit}\,\Pr(X_{ij}=1) = \log\frac{\Pr(X_{ij}=1)}{1 - \Pr(X_{ij}=1)} = \theta_j - b_i.$$

Ability and difficulty share the logit scale, so an item is harder exactly when $b_i$ is larger, and a person is more able exactly when $\theta_j$ is larger. The subtraction $\theta_j - b_i$ is the whole model: the log-odds of success is the person's location minus the item's location.

**2PL — adding discrimination.** [ref: TODO_REF Birnbaum1968] The two-parameter logistic model introduces a slope parameter $a_i > 0$, the **discrimination**, so that

$$\Pr(X_{ij} = 1) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp\!\big(-a_i(\theta_j - b_i)\big)}.$$

A large $a_i$ makes the item characteristic curve steep (the item sharply separates people just above its difficulty from those just below); a small $a_i$ makes it shallow. The Rasch model is the special case $a_i \equiv 1$.

**3PL — adding guessing.** A lower asymptote $c_i \in [0,1)$ accounts for the chance of answering a multiple-choice item correctly by guessing, giving

$$\Pr(X_{ij} = 1) = c_i + \frac{1 - c_i}{1 + \exp\!\big(-a_i(\theta_j - b_i)\big)}.$$

The curve no longer falls to zero as $\theta \to -\infty$; it approaches $c_i$, the success probability of a pure guess. Each added parameter buys flexibility at the cost of more data needed to estimate it stably.

**Polytomous extensions.** For ordered-category items (Likert scales, partial credit), Samejima's **graded response model** [ref: TODO_REF Samejima1969] and the partial-credit and rating-scale models generalise the logistic link to cumulative boundaries between adjacent categories. The taxonomy of Thissen and Steinberg [ref: TODO_REF Thissen1986] unifies these under one family.

**Information and standard error.** The **item information function** measures how much each item reduces uncertainty about $\theta$ at each point of the ability continuum. For the 2PL,

$$I_i(\theta) = a_i^{2}\, P_i(\theta)\big(1 - P_i(\theta)\big),$$

and for the Rasch model this reduces to $I_i(\theta) = P_i(\theta)(1 - P_i(\theta))$ since $a_i = 1$. The **test information** is the sum over items,

$$T(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} I_i(\theta),$$

and the **asymptotic standard error** of the ability estimate is

$$\mathrm{SE}(\theta) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{T(\theta)}}.$$

Information is *local*: a test can be precise near the average ability and imprecise in the tails, which is why well-designed tests spread item difficulties across the range of interest. The standard error is largest where information is smallest, so precision varies with ability rather than being a single number as in classical test theory.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Equal slopes are a model assumption, not an empirical fact.* The Rasch model requires $a_i \equiv 1$; forcing this on data where items genuinely differ in discrimination misfits the curve. The 2PL relaxes this, at the cost of estimating one more parameter per item.
- *Information is local, reliability is global.* A single CTT reliability coefficient summarises the whole test, but $T(\theta)$ varies with ability. Two tests with identical reliability can have very different precision profiles.
- *The latent scale is arbitrary up to a linear transform.* IRT fixes only differences $\theta - b$; the origin and unit of the logit scale are set by a convention (mean $\theta = 0$, or anchoring item difficulties).
- *Guessing breaks specific objectivity.* The 3PL lower asymptote $c_i$ reintroduces an item-specific term that prevents the clean cancellation $\theta_1 - \theta_2$; the strong invariance results hold only for the 1PL.

## Key model [Intermediate+]

The Rasch model is the central object of this unit, and its defining strength is **specific objectivity**. Because the logit of success is the additive identity $\theta_j - b_i$, the comparison of any two persons on a common item depends only on their abilities:

$$\mathrm{logit}\,\Pr(X_{1j}=1) - \mathrm{logit}\,\Pr(X_{2j}=1) = (\theta_1 - b) - (\theta_2 - b) = \theta_1 - \theta_2,$$

which is free of the item difficulty $b$. Symmetrically, the comparison of two items on a common person depends only on their difficulties. This is a strong invariance claim: the ordering of people is independent of the calibre of items used, and the ordering of items is independent of the people who took them — *provided the items fit the model*. Classical test theory cannot match this, because a raw score there is bound to the specific items on the test.

The price of specific objectivity is the restriction $a_i \equiv 1$: all items must discriminate equally well. The 2PL and 3PL buy flexibility by surrendering the clean additive form, and with it the exact cancellation that specific objectivity requires. Choosing between the 1PL and its generalisations is a substantive modelling decision settled by fit statistics (likelihood-ratio tests, $M_2$, item-fit residuals) rather than by fiat.

**Estimation.** Three routes recover $\theta$ and the item parameters from a response matrix. **Joint maximum likelihood** (JML) estimates all person and item parameters simultaneously; it is plain in concept but biased for short tests because the number of parameters grows with the number of people. **Conditional maximum likelihood** exploits the Rasch property that the raw score is a sufficient statistic for $\theta$, conditioning it out and estimating item parameters alone — consistent and unbiased for the 1PL [ref: TODO_REF Rasch1960]. **Marginal maximum likelihood** (MML) assumes an ability distribution across the population, integrates $\theta$ out, and estimates item parameters from the marginal likelihood, typically via the EM algorithm [ref: TODO_REF Lord1980]. Person abilities are then estimated in a second step by maximum likelihood or Bayes' expected a posteriori. MML is the modern default because it handles sparse data and generalises cleanly to 2PL and 3PL.

**Worked information example.** Three Rasch items have difficulties $b = -1, 0, +1$. At ability $\theta = 0$ the success probabilities are $P_1 \approx 0.731$, $P_2 = 0.5$, $P_3 \approx 0.269$, so the item informations are $P_i(1-P_i) \approx 0.197, 0.250, 0.197$. The test information is $T(0) \approx 0.644$, giving $\mathrm{SE}(0) = 1/\sqrt{0.644} \approx 1.25$ logits. A 3-item test is blunt; a 30-item test spreading difficulties across the same range would lift $T(0)$ by roughly an order of magnitude and cut the standard error in proportion.

**Bridge.** The Rasch model builds toward the information-theoretic view of precision that classical test theory could only approximate with a single reliability number, and appears again in the factor-analysis companion as the item loading, since a 2PL discrimination parameter is a rescaled factor loading under linearisation. The foundational reason the logistic link is preferred is that it places ability and difficulty additively on the logit scale, so item and person share one continuum; this is exactly the condition that specific objectivity requires. Putting these together, the item characteristic curve is the basic measuring unit, and the bridge is that test information converts a bank of curves into a local standard error $\mathrm{SE}(\theta) = 1/\sqrt{T(\theta)}$, replacing CTT's constant standard error of measurement with a precision profile that varies with ability.

**Exercise (easy, multiple choice).**

In the 2PL model, what does the discrimination parameter $a_i$ control?

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise (easy, true or false).**

Under the Rasch model, the total number-correct score is a sufficient statistic for the person's ability $\theta$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write the joint likelihood of a response pattern and check whether it depends on the pattern only through the sum of correct responses.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.** For the Rasch model the probability of a response pattern factorises into a term depending on the raw score through $\exp(r\theta)$ and a term depending only on the item difficulties and the specific items answered correctly. The person parameter enters solely via the raw score $r$, so the number-correct score carries all the information about $\theta$ that the response pattern contains. This sufficiency is what makes conditional maximum likelihood possible, and it is a property the 2PL does **not** share.
</details>

</aside>

A Rasch item has difficulty $b = 1.0$ and the person has ability $\theta = 2.0$. Compute $P(\text{correct})$ to two decimal places.

**Exercise (medium, numeric).**

A single Rasch item has difficulty $b = 0$. Compute the item information $I(\theta)$ at $\theta = 0$ and at $\theta = 2$, and give the standard error of ability implied by this one item at each point. Round to three decimal places.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the Rasch model $I(\theta) = P(\theta)(1 - P(\theta))$, and $\mathrm{SE}(\theta) = 1/\sqrt{I(\theta)}$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

At $\theta = 0$, $P = 0.5$, so $I(0) = 0.5 \times 0.5 = 0.250$ and $\mathrm{SE}(0) = 1/\sqrt{0.250} = 2.000$.

A single item is most informative where it is steepest (at $\theta = b$) and nearly useless in the tails. Even at its best, one item leaves a two-logit standard error, which is why reliable ability estimation needs a bank of items.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise (medium, short answer).**

A test designer has two item banks available for a certification exam. Bank A contains 40 items whose difficulties cluster tightly around the cut score $\theta = 0$. Bank B contains 40 items spread evenly across the range $[-3, +3]$. Which bank gives a more precise ability estimate at the cut score, and which gives a more precise estimate across the whole population? Justify with the test information function.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Test information is a sum of item informations, each peaked near that item's own difficulty.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Bank A** gives the more precise estimate *at the cut score*, because clustering difficulties near $\theta = 0$ stacks each item's information peak on top of the others, maximising $T(0)$. For pass/fail decisions made at a single point, this is the efficient design.

**Bank B** gives the more precise estimate *across the whole population*, because spreading difficulties spreads the information peaks across $[-3,+3]$, lifting $T(\theta)$ throughout the range. Bank A is nearly blind in the tails: an examinee at $\theta = 2$ faces 40 items that are all too easy, each contributing little information. The choice depends on the purpose — a credentialing cut score favours Bank A, a survey of the full ability distribution favours Bank B.
</details>

</aside>

Compared with joint maximum likelihood, which is the principal advantage of marginal maximum likelihood (MML) estimation for IRT models?

A. MML gives a closed-form analytic solution for every item parameter.
B. MML treats ability as a random effect drawn from a population distribution, so it estimates item parameters consistently even when many people have sparse response patterns.
C. MML removes the need for any assumption about the distribution of ability.

**Exercise (hard, numeric/symbolic).**

A two-item Rasch test has difficulties $b_1 = 0$ and $b_2 = 1$. Compute the test information $T(\theta)$ at $\theta = 0.5$ and the corresponding standard error. Then explain in one sentence why $T(\theta)$ is maximised near $\theta = 0.5$ rather than at $\theta = 0$ or $\theta = 1$. Round $T$ and the standard error to three decimal places.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute each $P_i(0.5)$ from $P_i = e^{\theta - b_i}/(1 + e^{\theta - b_i})$, form $I_i = P_i(1-P_i)$, and sum. For the second part, recall that each item information peaks at $\theta = b_i$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

At $\theta = 0.5$: $P_1 = e^{0.5}/(1+e^{0.5}) \approx 0.622$ with $I_1 \approx 0.235$; $P_2 = e^{-0.5}/(1+e^{-0.5}) \approx 0.378$ with $I_2 \approx 0.235$. So $T(0.5) \approx 0.470$ and $\mathrm{SE}(0.5) = 1/\sqrt{0.470} \approx 1.459$.

The test information peaks near $\theta = 0.5$ because that is the midpoint between the two item difficulties: each item contributes most near its own $b_i$, and the sum of two humps centred at $0$ and $1$ is largest where they overlap, around $0.5$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise (hard, short answer).**

State precisely the condition under which the comparison of two persons is independent of the item used (specific objectivity), and prove that the 2PL violates this condition even when both items have equal difficulty. Then give one practical reason a psychometrician might still prefer the 2PL.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the 2PL write $\mathrm{logit}\,P = a_i(\theta - b)$. Compute the logit difference between two people on item $i$ and on item $j$ with $a_i \neq a_j$ but $b_i = b_j$. Does the difference stay the same?
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Specific objectivity holds when the logit difference between two people, $\mathrm{logit}\,P_1 - \mathrm{logit}\,P_2$, is the same for every item. For the Rasch model this difference is $(\theta_1 - b) - (\theta_2 - b) = \theta_1 - \theta_2$, independent of $b$.

For the 2PL the logit difference on item $i$ is $a_i(\theta_1 - b_i) - a_i(\theta_2 - b_i) = a_i(\theta_1 - \theta_2)$. On a second item $j$ with $b_j = b_i$ but $a_j \neq a_i$, the difference is $a_j(\theta_1 - \theta_2)$. These differ whenever $a_i \neq a_j$, so the comparison of the two people changes with the item: specific objectivity fails.

A psychometrician may still prefer the 2PL because real item banks contain items that genuinely differ in how sharply they discriminate, and forcing equal discrimination (the Rasch restriction) misfits such data. The 2PL trades the invariance guarantee for descriptive adequacy.
</details>

</aside>

Define differential item functioning (DIF) in a way that distinguishes it cleanly from a real group difference in the underlying trait, and name the two standard statistical tests used to detect it. Why does matching on an ability estimate matter for both?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

DIF is about an item behaving differently across groups *at the same ability*. What goes wrong if you compare raw proportions correct without matching on $\theta$?
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

An item shows **DIF** when two people of equal ability $\theta$ but from different groups have different success probabilities on that item — that is, when the item's parameters are not invariant across groups. This is distinct from a group difference in the trait itself: if one group has higher mean $\theta$, its members will answer more items correctly without any item being unfair. DIF is item-level non-invariance, not a mean shift.

The two standard tests are the **Mantel-Haenszel** procedure [ref: TODO_REF Holland1988], which compares the odds of a correct response across groups within ability-matched score strata and reports a common odds ratio, and the **likelihood-ratio test**, which fits an IRT model with and without a group-specific item parameter and compares the fit. Matching on ability (or on the raw score, which is sufficient for $\theta$ in the Rasch case) is essential for both, because without it a real mean-$\theta$ difference would masquerade as DIF on every item simultaneously.
</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The Rasch model and its generalisations are descriptive once the parameters are in hand; the machinery that puts them there, and that polices their fairness across groups, is the substance of advanced IRT. The results below record how estimation, item fairness, and adaptive testing are actually carried out.

### Estimation: joint, conditional, and marginal likelihood

**Joint maximum likelihood** (JML) writes the likelihood of the full response matrix as a product over persons and items and maximises over every $\theta_j$ and every $\xi_i$ at once. Its defect is the **incidental-parameters problem**: when the number of people grows while each person's number of items stays fixed, the item-parameter estimates are inconsistent, because each person's noisy $\hat\theta$ contaminates the item estimates. JML is intuitive but biased for the test lengths typical of educational measurement.

**Conditional maximum likelihood** (CML) is available only for the Rasch family. The key fact is that the raw score $r_j = \sum_i X_{ij}$ is a sufficient statistic for $\theta_j$, so conditioning on $r_j$ eliminates $\theta_j$ from the likelihood entirely. The conditional likelihood of the response pattern given the score depends only on the item difficulties, which are then estimated consistently. The cost is that CML does not generalise to the 2PL or 3PL, where no sufficient statistic for $\theta$ exists.

**Marginal maximum likelihood** (MML) [ref: TODO_REF Lord1980] is the modern default. It posits a population distribution $g(\theta)$ for ability (typically normal with free mean and variance), writes the marginal likelihood of each response pattern as $\int \prod_i P_i(\theta)^{X_i}(1-P_i(\theta))^{1-X_i}\, g(\theta)\,d\theta$, and maximises over the item parameters and the distribution's moments. The integral has no closed form and is evaluated by the **EM algorithm**: the E-step computes the expected sufficient statistics given current parameters, and the M-step updates the parameters. Bock and Aitkin's 1981 implementation remains the workhorse. Person abilities are recovered afterwards by maximum likelihood or by the **expected a posteriori** (EAP) estimate, which shrinks toward the population mean and is preferred for short tests. MML handles the 2PL and 3PL naturally and underpins every modern item-bank calibration.

### Differential item functioning

**Differential item functioning** (DIF) is the operational definition of item unfairness: an item shows DIF when examinees of equal ability $\theta$ but from different demographic groups have different success probabilities on it. DIF is item-level non-invariance, distinct from a real group difference in the trait, and detecting it is a legal and ethical prerequisite for high-stakes testing.

The **Mantel-Haenszel** procedure [ref: TODO_REF Holland1988] is the simplest robust detector. Examinees are stratified into narrow ability bands (typically by total score), and within each band the odds of a correct response for the focal group are compared to those for the reference group. The **common odds ratio** $\alpha_{\mathrm{MH}}$ pools across strata; $\alpha_{\mathrm{MH}} = 1$ means no DIF, and the transformation $\mathrm{MH}\Delta = -2.35\log(\alpha_{\mathrm{MH}})$ places the magnitude on the ETS delta scale used to flag items (A/B/C categories). The Mantel-Haenszel test is cheap and assumes uniform DIF (the same bias across the ability range).

The **likelihood-ratio test** is the more flexible alternative. It fits two IRT models — one with a common item parameter for both groups, one with a group-specific parameter — and compares the fit via $-2\log$ likelihood. Uniform and non-uniform DIF (where discrimination itself differs across groups) can be tested by nesting models appropriately. The likelihood-ratio framework generalises cleanly to the 2PL and 3PL and is the default for publishable DIF analyses.

### Specific objectivity and the Rasch justification

Georg Rasch [ref: TODO_REF Rasch1960] regarded specific objectivity not as a convenient by-product but as the **defining criterion** of measurement. His argument: a comparison of two objects (people, or items) deserves to be called a measurement only if it yields the same result regardless of which instruments (items, or people) are used within a class. The additive logit form $\theta - b$ is the unique link (up to scale) for which this holds for binary responses, and Rasch took this as a reason to prefer the 1PL even when the 2PL fits marginally better. The position is contested — the 2PL camp argues that forcing equal discrimination sacrifices descriptive truth for an invariance guarantee the data do not support — but it marks a genuine philosophical fork: whether measurement is defined by the model's invariance properties or by its fit to the data.

### Comparison with classical test theory

IRT and CTT answer the same question — how precisely does this instrument measure? — at different resolution. CTT operates on the total score and reports a single reliability coefficient and a single standard error of measurement; IRT operates on each item and reports a precision profile $T(\theta)$ that varies with ability. A CTT score is bound to the specific items on the test, so comparing scores across different forms requires equating; IRT places person and item on a common scale, so scores are comparable across forms by construction. CTT's reliability is population-relative (it depends on the true-score variance in the sample), whereas IRT item parameters are invariant across populations once the scale is fixed. The cost of IRT is larger sample sizes for stable calibration and a parametric model whose assumptions must be checked. For low-stakes group research CTT is often sufficient; for high-stakes individual decisions and adaptive testing IRT is the standard.

### Applications: computerised adaptive testing

**Computerised adaptive testing** (CAT) is the practical payoff of item information. A CAT engine maintains a calibrated item bank, begins from a provisional ability estimate, and at each step selects the item that maximises information at the current $\hat\theta$, administers it, updates $\hat\theta$ by maximum likelihood or Bayes, and stops when the cumulative information $T(\hat\theta)$ crosses a target (equivalently, when $\mathrm{SE}(\hat\theta)$ falls below a threshold). Because each examinee receives items targeted near their own ability, a CAT achieves the precision of a long fixed test with roughly half the items — the GRE, GMAT, and NATO's military aptitude batteries are all CAT implementations built on 3PL item banks. The same machinery underpins the scaling of the SAT and the equating of test forms across administrations: IRT's common logit scale is what allows scores from different item sets to be placed on one reported scale.

**Synthesis.** Putting these together, the Rasch model, its 2PL/3PL generalisations, and the information apparatus form a single measurement engine: the logistic link is the foundational reason item-level probabilities admit a closed-form Fisher information, this is exactly what turns a bank of items into a precision-weighted measuring instrument, and the central insight is that precision varies with ability rather than being a single number as in classical theory. Specific objectivity generalises the parallel-tests idea of the sibling unit into a statement that comparisons are invariant to the calibre of items used, and the bridge is that estimation (joint, conditional, marginal) and DIF testing together operationalise the fairness claims that reliability alone cannot reach. The pattern recurs whenever a latent variable must be measured: the same information-versus-standard-error duality appears again in the standard error of measurement of classical test theory, and item response theory is dual to factor analysis in that the discrimination parameter and the factor loading are rescaled versions of one another under the linearised link.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Fisher information and standard error under the 1PL Rasch model).** Let $X_1, \ldots, X_k$ be the responses of a fixed person with latent ability $\theta$ to $k$ Rasch items with difficulties $b_1, \ldots, b_k$, modelled as independent Bernoulli variables with $P_i(\theta) = \Pr(X_i = 1) = \exp(\theta - b_i)/(1 + \exp(\theta - b_i))$. Then (a) the Fisher information about $\theta$ carried by item $i$ is $I_i(\theta) = P_i(\theta)(1 - P_i(\theta))$; (b) the test information is $T(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} I_i(\theta)$; and (c) the large-sample standard error of the maximum-likelihood estimator $\hat\theta$ satisfies $\mathrm{SE}(\hat\theta) = 1/\sqrt{T(\hat\theta)}$.

*Proof.* For (a), write the log-likelihood of a single response $x_i \in \{0,1\}$ as

$$\ell_i(\theta) = x_i \log P_i(\theta) + (1 - x_i)\log(1 - P_i(\theta)).$$

The logistic identity gives $\mathrm{logit}\,P_i(\theta) = \log\frac{P_i}{1 - P_i} = \theta - b_i$, so $1 - P_i = P_i / e^{\theta - b_i}$. Differentiating the two log-probabilities against $\theta$ yields $P_i'/P_i$ and $-P_i'/(1 - P_i)$ respectively, where $P_i' = \partial P_i / \partial \theta$. Hence the score is

$$\frac{\partial \ell_i}{\partial \theta} = x_i \frac{P_i'}{P_i} - (1 - x_i)\frac{P_i'}{1 - P_i} = \frac{P_i'}{P_i(1 - P_i)}\bigl(x_i - P_i\bigr).$$

For the logistic curve the derivative is $P_i' = P_i(1 - P_i)$, which cancels the denominator and reduces the score to the clean form

$$\frac{\partial \ell_i}{\partial \theta} = x_i - P_i(\theta).$$

The Fisher information is the variance of the score (its mean is zero, since $\mathbb{E}[X_i] = P_i$):

$$I_i(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\left(\frac{\partial \ell_i}{\partial \theta}\right)^{2}\right] = \mathbb{E}\!\left[(X_i - P_i)^{2}\right] = \mathrm{Var}(X_i) = P_i(\theta)\bigl(1 - P_i(\theta)\bigr),$$

using that the variance of a Bernoulli variable with success probability $P_i$ is $P_i(1 - P_i)$. Equivalently, $I_i(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}[\partial^{2}\ell_i/\partial\theta^{2}] = P_i'$, which equals $P_i(1 - P_i)$ by the same logistic derivative.

For (b), independence of the items makes the total log-likelihood a sum $\ell(\theta) = \sum_i \ell_i(\theta)$. Scores of independent components add, so the Fisher information is additive:

$$T(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} I_i(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} P_i(\theta)\bigl(1 - P_i(\theta)\bigr).$$

For (c), the maximum-likelihood estimator $\hat\theta$ is asymptotically normal about the true $\theta$ with variance given by the inverse Fisher information — the Cramér-Rao lower bound, attained in the large-sample limit. Because the total information is $T(\theta)$, the asymptotic variance is $\mathrm{Var}(\hat\theta) \to 1/T(\theta)$, and taking square roots gives

$$\mathrm{SE}(\hat\theta) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{T(\hat\theta)}}.$$

$\square$

The proposition is the formal content of the statement that a test measures precisely where it is informative. Because $P_i(1-P_i)$ peaks at $P_i = 0.5$, i.e. at $\theta = b_i$, each item contributes most where the person sits right at its difficulty; spreading item difficulties spreads the peaks and raises $T(\theta)$ across the range, lowering the standard error there. The identity $\mathrm{SE} = 1/\sqrt{T}$ converts the abstract information curve into a concrete confidence band on a person's ability, and it is the reason adaptive testing targets items near the current $\hat\theta$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Item response theory was founded by the Danish mathematician Georg Rasch, who developed the one-parameter model in the 1950s for the Danish Institute for Educational Research to analyse reading-attainment tests [ref: TODO_REF Rasch1960]. Rasch's *Probabilistic Models for Some Intelligence and Attainment Tests* (1960) introduced not only the model that bears his name but the specific-objectivity criterion he took to be the definition of measurement itself: a comparison deserves to be called measurement only if it is invariant to the instruments used. This philosophical commitment — that the model's invariance properties, not its fit to data, define measurement — set the Rasch tradition apart from the broader IRT community and remains a live fault-line in psychometrics.

The generalisation to multi-parameter models is due to Allan Birnbaum, whose chapters in Lord and Novick's 1968 *Statistical Theories of Mental Test Scores* introduced the 2PL and 3PL by adding discrimination and guessing parameters [ref: TODO_REF Birnbaum1968]. Frederick Lord then built the modern estimation and application apparatus: his *Applications of Item Response Theory to Practical Testing Problems* (1980) [ref: TODO_REF Lord1980] developed marginal maximum likelihood, the EM-based calibration that still underpins item-bank estimation, and the first operational computerised adaptive testing engine for the GRE. Fumiko Samejima's 1969 graded response model [ref: TODO_REF Samejima1969] extended the framework from dichotomous to ordered-category items, opening IRT to Likert-scale personality and attitude measurement.

The modern synthesis — treating IRT, CTT, and factor analysis as three parameterisations of one latent-variable decomposition — was consolidated by Embretson and Reise's *Item Response Theory for Psychologists* (2000, 2nd edition 2013) [ref: TODO_REF Embretson2000], which made the apparatus accessible beyond the educational-measurement community. The 1980s and 1990s added the differential-item-functioning apparatus (Holland and Thayer's Mantel-Haenszel procedure, 1988) [ref: TODO_REF Holland1988] and the Thissen-Steinberg taxonomy of item response models [ref: TODO_REF Thissen1986], both of which turned fairness testing from an aspiration into a routine statistical procedure. The continuing philosophical tension — between those who follow Rasch in restricting measurement to invariant models and those who prefer best-fitting multi-parameter models — is not a technical dispute to be settled but a standing reminder that the choice of model embodies a substantive position about what measurement is for.
