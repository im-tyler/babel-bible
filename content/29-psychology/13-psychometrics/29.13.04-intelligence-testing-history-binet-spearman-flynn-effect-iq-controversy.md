## Intuition [Beginner]

IQ (the intelligence quotient) measures a narrow slice of cognitive ability — the verbal, mathematical, and spatial reasoning that schools reward. The first modern intelligence test was built in 1905 by Alfred Binet, a French psychologist, to identify which Paris schoolchildren needed extra educational help. Binet explicitly rejected the idea that his test measured innate, fixed intelligence. He saw it as a snapshot of current performance, shaped by schooling and opportunity.

When the test crossed the Atlantic, it became what Binet had feared. Lewis Terman's 1916 Stanford-Binet adapted the instrument for American use and turned the IQ score into a popular sorting device. Over the following decades the test was enlisted by the eugenics movement, by immigration restriction, and by the Army's mass testing of recruits in the First World War. A tool designed to help struggling children was repurposed to rank populations.

The history turns on a single empirical fact. Over the twentieth century, average IQ scores rose by roughly $3$ points per decade in every developed country studied — the Flynn effect, after James Flynn's 1984 synthesis. That magnitude cannot be genetic. It demonstrates that IQ is sensitive to environment and reframes every claim about fixed intelligence or group differences in the score.

The picture lays out the intelligence-testing timeline as a horizontal axis from 1869 to the present. ## Worked example [Beginner]

Setting. In 1917, with the United States entering the First World War, the American Psychological Association appointed Robert Yerkes to develop a group-administered intelligence test for screening Army recruits. Yerkes's committee, which included Terman and Henry Goddard, produced two instruments: the Army Alpha for literate English speakers and the Army Beta for recruits who were illiterate or did not speak English.

Step 1. Scale. Between 1917 and 1918 the Army administered the Alpha and Beta to approximately $1{,}700{,}000$ recruits. The results were summarised in Yerkes's 1921 report to the National Academy of Sciences. The reported mean mental age of the native-born white American recruit was $13$ years — only marginally above the cut-off then used for the category of "feeblemindedness."

Step 2. Use. The Army results were widely reported. The reported mean for Southern and Eastern European immigrants was lower than the native-born mean; the reported mean for Black recruits was lower still. These figures were cited in the 1924 Johnson-Reed Immigration Restriction Act, which set national-origin quotas designed to favour Northern Europeans over Southern and Eastern Europeans.

Step 3. Methodology. The Alpha and Beta were culturally loaded (they required familiarity with American domestic objects and currency), language-loaded even in the supposedly non-verbal Beta, and unvalidated against any criterion of actual military performance. Critics at the time, including some within the APA, noted these flaws. The critics were ignored in the policy arena.

What this tells us: a psychometric instrument deployed at scale without validation of its construct for the use to which it was put can produce numerically precise-looking results whose policy consequences outrun the evidence. The Army tests established intelligence testing as a mass-administered instrument. They are also the paradigmatic cautionary tale of psychometric misuse.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The objects of intelligence testing are standardised scores on cognitive instruments, their transformation into the IQ scale, and the latent variables extracted from correlations among them. Three definitions anchor the field, and a fourth governs the heritability concept that underlies the most contested interpretation.

**Definition (Mental age and ratio IQ; Binet-Simon 1905, Stern 1912).** Let $\mathrm{MA}$ denote the mental age assigned to a child whose performance on the Binet-Simon scale matches the median performance of typical children of age $\mathrm{MA}$. Let $\mathrm{CA}$ denote the child's chronological age. Stern's (1912) ratio IQ is
$$
\mathrm{IQ}_{\mathrm{ratio}} \;=\; 100 \cdot \frac{\mathrm{MA}}{\mathrm{CA}}.
$$
The ratio IQ is well-defined only for children (where $\mathrm{MA}$ grows roughly linearly with $\mathrm{CA}$), breaks down for adults (where $\mathrm{MA}$ saturates), and yields a non-stationary distribution because the standard deviation of $\mathrm{MA}/\mathrm{CA}$ varies with age.

**Definition (Deviation IQ; Wechsler 1939).** Let $X$ denote an individual's raw composite score on the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale (WAIS) or the Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children (WISC), referenced to an age-matched standardisation sample with sample mean $\mu_a$ and sample standard deviation $\sigma_a$ for the individual's age band $a$. The deviation IQ is
$$
\mathrm{IQ}_{\mathrm{dev}} \;=\; 100 \;+\; 15 \cdot \frac{X - \mu_a}{\sigma_a},
$$
so the reference distribution has mean $100$ and standard deviation $15$ at every age band. The transformation normalises the score across ages — a given IQ always corresponds to the same percentile within the age band — and is the form used in all modern clinical instruments.

**Definition (Spearman's $g$ and the two-factor model; Spearman 1904).** Let $X_1, \ldots, X_k$ be standardised scores on $k$ cognitive tests, and let $R = (\rho_{ij})$ be their $k \times k$ correlation matrix. Spearman [ref: TODO_REF Spearman1904] observed that $R$ typically exhibits the *positive manifold*: every pairwise correlation $\rho_{ij}$ is positive. The two-factor model posits
$$
X_i \;=\; a_i \, g \;+\; s_i, \qquad i = 1, \ldots, k,
$$
where $g$ is a single latent variable common to all tests (the *general factor*), $a_i \in [0, 1]$ is the loading of test $i$ on $g$, and $s_i$ is a test-specific residual uncorrelated with $g$ and with $s_j$ for $j \neq i$. Under the model, $g$ is recovered (up to sign) as the leading principal component of $R$; the proportion of total variance it accounts for is its largest eigenvalue divided by $k$, and is typically $0.40$ to $0.50$ for diverse cognitive batteries.

**Definition (Heritability, narrow-sense).** Let $V_P$ denote the phenotypic variance of IQ in a specified population, decomposed as $V_P = V_A + V_D + V_I + V_C + V_E$, where $V_A$ is additive genetic variance, $V_D$ is dominance variance, $V_I$ is epistatic variance, $V_C$ is shared-environment variance, and $V_E$ is non-shared-environment variance. The narrow-sense heritability is $h^2 = V_A / V_P$. Twin-study estimates of $h^2$ for adult IQ in modern industrial populations converge on the range $0.5$ to $0.8$, with $h^2$ increasing from childhood to adulthood (the *Wilson effect*). Heritability is a within-population, within-time variance ratio; it places no constraint on the cause of mean differences between populations or across generations.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *IQ tests measure innate intelligence.* No. Binet rejected this interpretation in 1905; the Flynn effect, on which mean IQ rose approximately $30$ points across the twentieth century, refutes it empirically. What IQ tests measure is a composite of reasoning skills that are partially heritable *within* populations and substantially sensitive to environment *between* populations and across generations.

- *The general factor $g$ does not exist.* No. $g$ is one of the most robust findings in psychometrics. Under the positive manifold, the leading principal component of any diverse cognitive battery accounts for a substantial fraction of variance and predicts performance on novel cognitive tasks. The existence of $g$ does not entail hereditarian claims; the contested question is what causes individual differences in $g$, not whether $g$ exists.

- *A heritability of $h^2 = 0.8$ means that $80\%$ of any individual's IQ is genetic.* No. Heritability is a population-level variance ratio, not an individual-level determination. The statement "$h^2 = 0.8$" means that $80\%$ of the *between-individual variance* in the specified population is attributable to additive genetic differences; it says nothing about any one person's score, and it says nothing about the causes of mean differences between populations or across generations.

- *The Army Alpha and Beta were valid measures of intelligence.* No. The Army instruments were culturally loaded, language-loaded, and unvalidated against any criterion of actual military performance. The numerical results were quoted with spurious precision; the policy uses outran the evidence.

- *Binet supported eugenics.* No. Binet explicitly rejected the hereditarian interpretation of his scale and advocated educational intervention for low-scoring children. The eugenicist reading was imposed on the Binet-Simon scale by Goddard, Terman, and Yerkes after the scale crossed the Atlantic.

- *The Flynn effect proves that genetics is irrelevant.* No. Within-population heritability of IQ is high ($h^2 \approx 0.5$ to $0.8$ in adults) and is compatible with massive between-generation environmental gains, because heritability is a within-population variance ratio. The two facts are not in tension (Proposition 2 below).

- *Race differences in IQ are genetic.* No. The mainstream consensus (Nisbett 2009 [ref: TODO_REF Nisbett2009]; the 1996 APA task force led by Neisser) is that observed group differences are heavily environmental, with no evidence for genetic racial differences in $g$. Within-group heritability of IQ places no constraint on the cause of between-group mean differences.

- *Gardner's multiple intelligences have psychometric support.* Weak. The proposed "intelligences" (linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalist) do not meet the standard psychometric criteria for distinct factors. They correlate substantially with each other and with $g$, and several lack standardised measures. The theory is influential in education and negligible in psychometrics.

- *Working-memory training raises intelligence.* Limited. Jaeggi et al. (2008) reported large gains in fluid intelligence after $n$-back training; Redick et al. (2013) and subsequent replications with active control groups found the transfer to be confined to the trained task and not to generalise to fluid intelligence. The "Mozart effect" was similarly debunked.

## Key result: the Flynn effect and the malleability of IQ [Intermediate+]

**Result (The Flynn effect).** *Across all developed countries for which twentieth-century IQ data are available, mean IQ scores rose by approximately $3$ points per decade — roughly $1$ standard deviation every $50$ years — over the period from approximately 1930 to 2000. The gains are concentrated on tests of fluid intelligence (Raven's Progressive Matrices and similar abstract-reasoning instruments) and are smaller on tests of crystallised intelligence (vocabulary, general information). The cumulative gain over the twentieth century is approximately $30$ IQ points.*

**Defence.** Flynn (1984) [ref: TODO_REF Flynn1984] assembled nearly all available United States standardisation samples of the Stanford-Binet and Wechsler scales between 1932 and 1978 and showed that the mean score rose by approximately $13.8$ points over $46$ years — $3$ points per decade. Flynn (1987) extended the analysis to $14$ developed nations and found the same rate everywhere: $5$ to $25$ IQ points per generation, with the largest gains on Raven's-style fluid-intelligence tests and the smallest on vocabulary and information tests. The rate has since been confirmed in dozens of replications across Europe, North America, and East Asia; the Trahan et al. (2014) meta-analysis aggregated $405$ samples totalling approximately $7{,}000{,}000$ participants and estimated the secular trend at $2.31$ points per decade.

The magnitude is the central fact. A $30$-point gain over a century exceeds the present-day gap between any two contemporary racial or socioeconomic groups in the United States (which is on the order of $10$ to $15$ points). If the cause were genetic, the underlying population allele frequencies would have had to shift at a rate incompatible with any plausible selection pressure operating over four generations (Proposition 1 below gives the quantitative argument). The cause is environmental.

The candidate environmental causes are several: improvements in nutrition and the decline of infectious disease (Lynn 1990); the expansion of formal education and the rising duration of schooling (Ceci 1991); the increasing cognitive complexity of everyday environments, including exposure to abstract categories and scientific reasoning (Flynn 2007); the reduction in family size; and the spread of testing itself, which familiarises populations with the task format. The gains are heterogeneous across subtests, which is hard to reconcile with any single-cause account; the Piagetian subtests have even shown declines in some samples. The Flynn effect has reversed in a few Nordic countries since approximately 2000 (Sundet et al. 2004; Dutton et al. 2016), which suggests the underlying causes are not monotone and may interact with cohort-specific conditions.

**Caveat.** The Flynn effect demonstrates that IQ is environmentally malleable at the population level, but it does not entail that any individual's IQ can be raised substantially by intervention. Within-population heritability is high; between-generation change is environmental; the two facts are consistent because heritability is a within-population variance ratio, not a fixed property of the trait.

**Bridge.** The Flynn effect is the foundational reason that mean IQ cannot be read as a fixed biological constant; it identifies the deviation IQ with a population-relative measure that re-calibrates with each standardisation sample, and the bridge is between the within-time measurement scale of classical test theory and the between-time secular trend that any single standardisation conceals. This is exactly the substance of the within-versus-between distinction that the factor-analysis tradition of [29.13.02] presupposes but cannot defend on its own, and it builds toward the item-response framework of [29.13.03], where the latent trait $\theta$ is by construction invariant under re-parameterisation. The pattern appears again in [29.05.01] as the cognitive-psychology companion to this unit's psychometric frame, where the construct of fluid intelligence ($\mathrm{Gf}$) is what the Flynn-effect gains are measured on, and the chapter-level synthesis in [29.13.01] places the present unit within the broader psychometric edifice.

**Exercise 1 (easy, multiple choice).**

Spearman's general factor $g$ is best described as:

A. The total score on the WAIS, divided by the participant's age.
B. The leading principal component of the correlation matrix of a diverse battery of cognitive tests.
C. The ratio of mental age to chronological age.
D. The test-specific residual uncorrelated with all other tests.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Recall that Spearman (1904) observed the positive manifold — all pairwise correlations positive — and proposed a single common latent variable to account for it. Under what mathematical operation does a single common factor emerge from a correlation matrix?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. The leading principal component of the correlation matrix of a diverse battery of cognitive tests.** Under the two-factor model $X_i = a_i g + s_i$ with $g$ uncorrelated with the specific residuals $s_i$, the correlation matrix $R$ has entries $\rho_{ij} = a_i a_j$ for $i \neq j$, so the leading eigenvector of $R$ recovers $g$ (up to sign) and the proportion of variance accounted for equals the largest eigenvalue divided by $k$. The total WAIS score (A) is a sum of subtest scores, not a factor; Stern's ratio (C) is the IQ formula, not a latent variable; the test-specific residual (D) is $s_i$, the *opposite* of $g$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, true-false).**

Stern's ratio IQ, $\mathrm{IQ} = 100 \cdot \mathrm{MA}/\mathrm{CA}$, is well-defined for adults of all ages and yields a stationary distribution across the lifespan.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider what happens to the ratio as $\mathrm{CA}$ grows beyond the point at which mental age continues to increase.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**False.** The ratio IQ is well-defined only for children, in whom $\mathrm{MA}$ grows roughly linearly with $\mathrm{CA}$. In adults, mental age saturates: performance on Binet-style tasks stops increasing past the late teens, so $\mathrm{MA}$ approaches a ceiling while $\mathrm{CA}$ continues to grow. The ratio $\mathrm{MA}/\mathrm{CA}$ therefore declines with age in adults, producing absurd-looking low IQs in older adults. Wechsler's (1939) deviation IQ was introduced specifically to fix this problem: by standardising within age bands, it produces a stationary distribution with mean $100$ and standard deviation $15$ at every age. All modern clinical instruments use the deviation IQ, not Stern's ratio.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).**

A child obtains a standard score of $X = 87$ on the WISC-V. The age-band standardisation sample has mean $\mu_a = 100$ and standard deviation $\sigma_a = 15$. Compute the child's deviation IQ.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the deviation-IQ formula $\mathrm{IQ} = 100 + 15(X - \mu_a)/\sigma_a$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$87$.** The deviation-IQ transformation is
$$
\mathrm{IQ} \;=\; 100 + 15 \cdot \frac{X - \mu_a}{\sigma_a} \;=\; 100 + 15 \cdot \frac{87 - 100}{15} \;=\; 100 + 15 \cdot (-0.867) \;=\; 100 - 13 \;=\; 87.
$$
When the raw score already lies on the $\mu_a = 100, \sigma_a = 15$ metric (as is conventional for the WISC composite scores), the deviation IQ coincides with the raw score. The point of the formula is to guarantee mean $100$ and standard deviation $15$ at every age band even when the underlying raw-score metric varies.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Under the two-factor model $X_i = a_i g + s_i$ with $\mathrm{Var}(g) = 1$, $\mathrm{Var}(s_i) = 1 - a_i^2$, and $g$ uncorrelated with every $s_i$ and $s_i$ uncorrelated with $s_j$ for $j \neq i$, write the correlation $\rho_{ij}$ between $X_i$ and $X_j$ (for $i \neq j$) as a function of the loadings $a_i$ and $a_j$. Show that the model predicts the positive manifold — that is, $\rho_{ij} > 0$ whenever $a_i, a_j > 0$ — and derive the variance accounted for by the general factor.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The covariance of two sums of uncorrelated terms picks out only the contributions of the shared component. Recall $\mathrm{Var}(X_i) = a_i^2 + \mathrm{Var}(s_i)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Under the assumptions, $\mathrm{Cov}(g, s_i) = 0$, $\mathrm{Cov}(s_i, s_j) = 0$ for $i \neq j$, $\mathrm{Var}(g) = 1$, and $\mathrm{Var}(X_i) = a_i^2 + (1 - a_i^2) = 1$ (standardised scores). For $i \neq j$,
$$
\mathrm{Cov}(X_i, X_j) \;=\; \mathrm{Cov}(a_i g + s_i,\; a_j g + s_j) \;=\; a_i a_j \,\mathrm{Var}(g) \;=\; a_i a_j.
$$
Since $\mathrm{Var}(X_i) = \mathrm{Var}(X_j) = 1$, the correlation is $\rho_{ij} = a_i a_j$. Whenever $a_i > 0$ and $a_j > 0$, $\rho_{ij} > 0$, giving the positive manifold. The variance of $X_i$ accounted for by $g$ is $a_i^2 \mathrm{Var}(g) = a_i^2$, so the proportion of variance in test $i$ explained by $g$ is $a_i^2$. Averaging over $k$ tests, the proportion of total variance explained by the general factor is $\overline{a^2} = k^{-1} \sum_i a_i^2$, which is typically $0.40$ to $0.50$ for diverse cognitive batteries. The formal identification of $g$ with the leading principal component of $R$ follows from the fact that $R = a a^\top + \mathrm{diag}(1 - a_i^2)$, which has leading eigenvalue $\|a\|^2 = \sum_i a_i^2$ and leading eigenvector $a / \|a\|$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short answer).**

In plain English, explain why a within-population heritability of $h^2 = 0.8$ for IQ does *not* imply that the mean difference between two populations is $80\%$ genetic.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Heritability is a within-population variance ratio. Consider a thought experiment in which two genetically identical populations are reared in different environments.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Heritability is a within-population variance ratio: $h^2 = V_A / V_P$, the ratio of additive-genetic variance to total phenotypic variance *within a single interbreeding population in a specified range of environments*. It is a number describing one population at one time, not a partition of any individual's score and not a constraint on between-population comparisons.

The standard illustration (Lewontin 1970): take a single bag of genetically diverse seeds and divide it into two batches. Plant one batch in nutrient-poor soil and the other in nutrient-rich soil. Within each batch, height is highly heritable — tall parents have tall offspring — because the genetic variation is preserved. But the mean height difference between the two batches is entirely environmental, because the two batches experience different soils. Within-group heritability of $h^2 \approx 1$ coexists with between-group mean differences that are $0\%$ genetic.

The same logic applies to human IQ. Within any modern industrial population, $h^2$ is high ($0.5$ to $0.8$). But the Flynn effect demonstrates that mean IQ has risen $30$ points across the twentieth century — an environmental change, not a genetic one. And the mean IQ difference between contemporary socioeconomic or racial groups cannot be inferred from the within-group heritability: the difference could in principle be $0\%$, $50\%$, or $100\%$ genetic, and the within-group $h^2$ does not adjudicate. Direct evidence on environmental causation (intervention studies, adoption studies, secular trends) is required.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (medium, numeric).**

A twin study of IQ reports a monozygotic-twin correlation of $r_{\mathrm{MZ}} = 0.86$ and a dizygotic-twin correlation of $r_{\mathrm{DZ}} = 0.60$. Use Falconer's formula $h^2 \approx 2(r_{\mathrm{MZ}} - r_{\mathrm{DZ}})$ to estimate the narrow-sense heritability of IQ in this population. Express the answer as a decimal rounded to two places.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Monozygotic twins share $100\%$ of their segregating genes; dizygotic twins share on average $50\%$. The difference in correlation, doubled, estimates the additive-genetic contribution.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$0.52$.** Applying Falconer's formula,
$$
h^2 \;\approx\; 2(r_{\mathrm{MZ}} - r_{\mathrm{DZ}}) \;=\; 2(0.86 - 0.60) \;=\; 2 \times 0.26 \;=\; 0.52.
$$
The estimate indicates that approximately $52\%$ of the between-individual variance in IQ in this population is attributable to additive-genetic differences. Falconer's formula derives from the assumption that $\mathrm{Cov}_{\mathrm{MZ}} = V_A + V_D + V_C$ and $\mathrm{Cov}_{\mathrm{DZ}} = \tfrac{1}{2} V_A + \tfrac{1}{4} V_D + V_C$ under random mating, so doubling the difference isolates $V_A$. The estimate is in the range reported for adolescent and young-adult samples; adult $h^2$ estimates from meta-analyses of twin and adoption studies converge on the $0.6$ to $0.8$ range, with the Wilson effect (increasing heritability from childhood to adulthood) explaining the age-dependence.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

The causes of the Flynn effect remain debated. Three leading hypotheses are (i) the *nutrition hypothesis* (Lynn 1990): better nutrition and the decline of infectious disease raised IQ directly; (ii) the *education hypothesis* (Ceci 1991): more years of schooling raised IQ through practice with the relevant cognitive skills; (iii) the *cognitive-complexity hypothesis* (Flynn 2007): everyday environments became more cognitively demanding, exercising abstract categorisation and scientific reasoning. Design a study that distinguishes these three hypotheses and predicts a different pattern of subtest gains under each.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each hypothesis predicts a different profile of gains across WAIS subtests. Vocabulary and information (crystallised) are most sensitive to formal schooling; Raven's Matrices and similarities (fluid, abstract) are most sensitive to abstract reasoning practice; purely physiological interventions (nutrition, infection load) predict uniform gains across all subtests. Cross-cohort comparisons that disaggregate subtests are the empirical handle.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The three hypotheses make distinguishable predictions about the *profile* of subtest gains across the twentieth century.

Under the **nutrition hypothesis**, gains should be largest in cohorts and populations with the largest historical incidence of malnutrition and infectious disease, and should be uniform across cognitive subtests (since brain development is a global resource, not specific to abstract reasoning). Predicted profile: roughly equal gains on Raven's, Vocabulary, Arithmetic, and Information. Empirical prediction: the gains should be concentrated in the early twentieth century when nutrition was worst, and should attenuate in well-nourished late-twentieth-century cohorts.

Under the **education hypothesis**, gains should track the expansion of secondary and tertiary schooling, both across time (decades with the largest rises in school attainment should show the largest IQ gains) and across populations (countries with the largest rise in years of schooling should show the largest Flynn gains). Predicted profile: largest gains on subtests closest to the school curriculum (Arithmetic, Information, Vocabulary, Comprehension), and smaller gains on non-curricular tasks like Raven's Matrices.

Under the **cognitive-complexity hypothesis**, gains should be largest on subtests involving abstract categorisation, scientific reasoning, and post-concrete-operational thought: Raven's Progressive Matrices, Similarities (which requires classifying items under abstract categories), and non-verbal analogies. Predicted profile: gains largest on fluid-intelligence subtests, smallest on crystallised-knowledge subtests.

The empirical pattern, summarised by Flynn (2007) and replicated in many countries, fits the cognitive-complexity hypothesis most closely: the largest gains are on Raven's and Similarities, the smallest on Vocabulary and Information. The nutrition hypothesis accounts for the early-twentieth-century portion of the trend (where nutrition gains were largest) and for the diminishing and recent reversal of the trend in well-nourished Nordic populations. The education hypothesis accounts for the smaller crystallised-intelligence gains but not for the massive fluid-intelligence gains. The most defensible synthesis is that all three causes contribute, with weights that vary across the century and across cohorts; no single-cause account fits the full profile.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, numeric).**

In quantitative genetics, the response to selection per generation is $R = h^2 S$, where $h^2$ is narrow-sense heritability and $S$ is the selection differential (the mean of the selected parents minus the population mean). Suppose a strong-hardest-case scenario: a heritability of $h^2 = 0.8$, and a sustained selection differential of $S = 1$ IQ point per generation on the breeding value for IQ. Over $4$ generations (approximately one century), what is the cumulative genetic response in IQ points? Compare with the observed Flynn-effect gain of approximately $30$ points over the same period and state what fraction of the observed gain the genetic response could account for.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The cumulative response over $N$ generations at constant selection differential is $N \cdot h^2 \cdot S$. Divide by the observed gain to get the fraction explained.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$3.2$ IQ points cumulative genetic response; approximately $11\%$ of the observed Flynn-effect gain.** The cumulative response over $N = 4$ generations is
$$
R_{\mathrm{total}} \;=\; N \cdot h^2 \cdot S \;=\; 4 \cdot 0.8 \cdot 1 \;=\; 3.2 \;\text{IQ points}.
$$
The fraction of the observed gain accounted for is
$$
\frac{3.2}{30} \;\approx\; 0.107 \;\approx\; 11\%.
$$
Under an explicitly hard-case selection scenario — heritability at the upper bound of credible estimates, a sustained selection differential of $1$ IQ point per generation (which would require very strong assortative mating or differential fertility by IQ) — genetic change can account for at most about $11\%$ of the observed secular trend. Under more realistic historical scenarios (smaller $S$, gene-by-environment interaction, declining selection pressure), the fraction falls further. The conclusion is that even the most generous genetic-response calculation cannot accommodate the magnitude of the Flynn effect; the residual $89\%$ or more must be environmental. This is the quantitative backbone of the consensus that the Flynn effect is environmental rather than genetic.

</details>

## Interpretive debates and developments [Master]

**Result 1 (Galton 1869: the hereditarian precursor).** Francis Galton's *Hereditary Genius* [ref: TODO_REF Galton1869] proposed that eminence runs in families because intellectual capacity is biologically inherited, and advocated selective breeding to improve the human stock. Galton coined the term *eugenics* in 1883. The book is the founding document of the hereditarian tradition in intelligence research and the conceptual ancestor of every subsequent claim that IQ differences between groups are genetically fixed. Galton had no psychometric instrument and no statistical method for separating genetic from environmental transmission; his data were genealogies of eminent families. The methodological apparatus that would later be invoked to defend Galton's hypothesis — correlation, regression, factor analysis — was in part developed by Galton himself and by his student Karl Pearson for exactly this purpose.

**Result 2 (Binet-Simon 1905: the practical intelligence test).** Binet and Simon's 1905 scale [ref: TODO_REF BinetSimon1905] consisted of thirty tasks of increasing difficulty, calibrated by age: a child whose performance matched the median six-year-old's performance was assigned mental age $6$. The scale was commissioned by the French Ministry of Education to identify Paris schoolchildren needing remedial education. Binet was explicit that the scale measured a developmental outcome shaped by schooling and opportunity, not a fixed biological capacity, and he rejected the hereditarian interpretation that Goddard, Terman, and Yerkes later imposed on it. The 1905 paper is the load-bearing reference of this unit's empirical origin point.

**Result 3 (Stern 1912; Terman 1916: the IQ score and its American adaptation).** William Stern (1912) proposed the ratio $\mathrm{IQ} = 100 \cdot \mathrm{MA}/\mathrm{CA}$ as a single-number summary of mental-age growth relative to chronological age. Lewis Terman's 1916 Stanford-Binet [ref: TODO_REF Terman1916] adapted the Binet-Simon scale for American use, adopted Stern's ratio IQ as the standard reporting metric, and standardised the scale on a California sample. Terman was an active proponent of the hereditarian interpretation and of eugenic immigration restriction; his 1916 manual explicitly advocated the use of the IQ score for classifying and sorting populations, in direct contravention of Binet's stated position.

**Result 4 (Yerkes 1917–1921: the Army Alpha and Beta).** Robert Yerkes, president of the American Psychological Association, chaired the committee that produced the Army Alpha (for literate English speakers) and Beta (for illiterate or non-English-speaking recruits) and oversaw the testing of approximately $1.7$ million United States recruits during the First World War [ref: TODO_REF Yerkes1921]. The reported results — that the mean mental age of the native-born white American recruit was approximately $13$ years, with lower means reported for immigrants and Black recruits — were cited in the 1924 Johnson-Reed Immigration Restriction Act. The methodological flaws (cultural loading, language loading, absence of criterion validation) were known at the time and disregarded in the policy arena. The Army programme established intelligence testing as a mass-administered instrument and made IQ part of public discourse.

**Result 5 (Spearman 1904; Thurstone 1938; Carroll 1993: the structure of intelligence).** Spearman [ref: TODO_REF Spearman1904] used factor analysis on a battery of cognitive tests to extract a single common factor $g$ that predicted performance across all of them, and proposed the two-factor theory $X_i = a_i g + s_i$. Thurstone (1938) argued against a single dominant $g$ and proposed instead a set of *Primary Mental Abilities* — verbal comprehension, word fluency, number facility, spatial visualisation, associative memory, perceptual speed, and reasoning — each a separate factor. Carroll's (1993) [ref: TODO_REF Carroll1993] re-analysis of more than $460$ datasets integrated the two positions in the *three-stratum theory*: a general factor $g$ at the third (top) stratum, eight broad abilities (including fluid intelligence, crystallised intelligence, general memory, visual processing) at the second stratum, and many narrow abilities at the first stratum. The synthesis with Cattell-Horn fluid-crystallised theory produced the Cattell-Horn-Carroll (CHC) model that underwrites contemporary test construction. The empirical existence of $g$ as the leading principal component of any diverse battery is uncontested; the *interpretation* of $g$ — as a biological, educational, or statistical regularity — remains the central theoretical dispute.

**Result 6 (Wechsler 1939: the clinical instrument).** David Wechsler's 1939 Wechsler-Bellevue [ref: TODO_REF Wechsler1939] introduced the deviation IQ (mean $100$, standard deviation $15$ standardised within age band), subdivided the battery into Verbal and Performance scales, and replaced the Binet-style mental-age framework with a stationary score metric. The WAIS (adults), WISC (children), and WPPSI (preschoolers) descendants of the Wechsler-Bellevue are the most-used clinical intelligence instruments in the world. Wechsler's deviation IQ is the form used in all modern reporting; Stern's ratio IQ survives only in historical discussions.

**Result 7 (Burt 1950s–1960s; Jensen 1969: the heritability controversy).** Cyril Burt's twin studies of IQ heritability, conducted between the 1940s and 1960s, reported some of the highest heritability estimates in the literature ($h^2 \approx 0.85$). After Burt's death, his data were found to be statistically anomalous and the allegation of fabrication was widely accepted (though the details remain contested by some). Arthur Jensen's 1969 article in the *Harvard Educational Review* [ref: TODO_REF Jensen1969], "How Much Can We Boost IQ and Scholastic Achievement?", argued that compensatory education programmes had failed because IQ is substantially heritable and that genetic differences might contribute to the Black-white IQ gap. The article ignited the modern heritability controversy. Jensen's heritability estimates were within the mainstream; his extension of within-population heritability to a hypothesis about between-population differences committed the ecological fallacy that Lewontin (1970) and others formalised.

**Result 8 (Flynn 1984: the secular trend).** James Flynn's 1984 paper in *Psychological Bulletin* [ref: TODO_REF Flynn1984] assembled nearly all United States standardisation samples of the Stanford-Binet and Wechsler scales between 1932 and 1978 and showed that the mean score rose by approximately $14$ points over $46$ years — about $3$ points per decade. The 1987 extension to fourteen developed countries found the same rate everywhere. The result, now called the *Flynn effect*, is the single most consequential empirical discovery in the history of intelligence testing. It demonstrates that mean IQ is environmentally malleable on a scale incompatible with any plausible genetic-response calculation (Proposition 1 below), and it reframes every claim about fixed intelligence or group differences. The recent reversal of the effect in some Nordic countries (Sundet et al. 2004; Dutton et al. 2016) shows that the underlying causes are not monotone and may interact with cohort-specific conditions.

**Result 9 (Herrnstein-Murray 1994; Nisbett 2009: the group-differences debate).** Herrnstein and Murray's *The Bell Curve* [ref: TODO_REF HerrnsteinMurray1994] argued that IQ is a major determinant of social outcomes, that it is substantially heritable, and that the Black-white IQ gap may be partly genetic. The book's statistical analyses were heavily criticised (Heckman 1995; Fischer et al. 1996; a 1996 APA task force led by Neisser), and the policy recommendations were widely rejected. The mainstream consensus, synthesised in Nisbett's *Intelligence and How to Get It* [ref: TODO_REF Nisbett2009], is that the observed group differences are heavily environmental: adoption studies, intervention programmes, secular-trend data, and the absence of any credible genetic mechanism for racial differences in $g$ all point in the same direction. Direct genome-wide-association-study evidence (Ritchie et al. 2014 onwards) has failed to identify any allele clusters that would explain the observed group differences.

**Result 10 (Gardner 1983; Sternberg 1985: alternatives to $g$).** Howard Gardner's *Frames of Mind* (1983) proposed eight "multiple intelligences" (linguistic, logical-mathematical, spatial, musical, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, naturalist) as a deliberate rejection of the $g$-factor framework. Robert Sternberg's *Beyond IQ* (1985) proposed a *triarchic theory* dividing intelligence into analytical, creative, and practical components. Both theories are influential in education and in popular discourse. Neither has strong psychometric support: the proposed factors correlate substantially with $g$ and with each other, several lack standardised measures, and the central psychometric findings (positive manifold, leading-principal-component dominance) are not accommodated. The mainstream view, reflected in the Cattell-Horn-Carroll synthesis, is that $g$ is robust and that Gardner's "intelligences" are best understood as domains in which $g$ and broad abilities are applied, rather than as distinct intelligence factors.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason that intelligence testing has been the most-studied psychometric instrument for a century is that $g$ is the most robust individual-differences construct in psychology, and the central insight of the modern synthesis is that $g$'s robustness as a within-population statistical regularity is consistent with massive environmental malleability between generations, because heritability is a within-population variance ratio rather than a fixed biological parameter. Putting these together with the Flynn-effect magnitude, the Cattell-Horn-Carroll hierarchical structure, and the Lewontin formalisation of the within-versus-between distinction, this is exactly the picture that identifies the deviation IQ with a population-relative measure and the general factor $g$ with a stable within-cookbook statistical regularity that is silent on the causes of between-cohort or between-group differences. The bridge is between the factor-analysis tradition of Spearman (1904) and Thurstone (1938), formalised in Carroll's (1993) three-stratum theory, and the environmental-malleability evidence of Flynn (1984); the pattern recurs across every domain in which a within-group regularity has been mistaken for a between-group cause, from gender differences in cognitive tasks to socioeconomic gaps in academic achievement, and the pattern generalises to the broader principle that statistical description and causal attribution require distinct evidence. The field's central methodological lesson — that the existence of $g$, the heritability of IQ within populations, and the malleability of IQ between populations are three consistent facts, not three contradictory ones — is the load-bearing interpretive content of the modern synthesis.

## Full argument set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Flynn-effect magnitude is inconsistent with genetic causation).** *Let $h^2$ denote narrow-sense heritability of IQ in a specified population and let $S$ denote the sustained per-generation selection differential on the breeding value for IQ. Then the cumulative genetic response over $N$ generations is $R_{\mathrm{total}} = N h^2 S$. Under any empirically credible bound on $h^2$ and $S$ over the twentieth century, $R_{\mathrm{total}}$ is small relative to the observed Flynn-effect gain of approximately $30$ IQ points; therefore the secular trend cannot be genetic in origin.*

*Proof.* The breeder's equation $R = h^2 S$ gives the per-generation response to selection under the additive-infinitesimal model of quantitative genetics (Falconer 1981). For the response to be plausibly genetic, the per-generation selection differential must be sustained by some mechanism — typically assortative mating by IQ or differential fertility by IQ.

Upper-bound empirical estimates: $h^2 \leq 0.8$ in modern industrial adult populations (Bouchard 2004 meta-analysis of twin and adoption studies); $|S| \leq 2$ IQ points per generation even under the strongest historically observed assortative mating and differential fertility (Reynolds et al. 2000 analysis of cohort fertility gradients). With these bounds,
$$
R_{\mathrm{total}} \;\leq\; N \cdot h^2 \cdot |S| \;\leq\; 4 \cdot 0.8 \cdot 2 \;=\; 6.4 \;\text{IQ points over a century}.
$$
The observed Flynn-effect gain is approximately $30$ points. Even at the upper bound of credible genetic response, the genetic channel can account for at most $6.4 / 30 \approx 21\%$ of the observed gain. Under more realistic historical scenarios — smaller $S$, gene-by-environment interaction, the well-documented narrowing of historical fertility gradients — the genetic fraction falls to below $10\%$.

The residual $79\%$ to $90\%$ or more of the secular trend cannot be attributed to additive genetic change under any credible model. The cause must therefore be environmental: changes in nutrition, education, infection load, cognitive complexity of the everyday environment, or some combination. This is the quantitative backbone of the modern consensus that the Flynn effect is environmental. The conclusion is robust to the choice of heritability estimate and to the modelling of selection differentials, because the gap between the genetic-response ceiling and the observed gain is more than an order of magnitude. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Within-group heritability does not constrain between-group heritability).** *Let $P_1$ and $P_2$ be two populations reared in environments $E_1$ and $E_2$ respectively, drawn from a common genetic distribution. Let $h^2_{P_1} = h^2_{P_2} = h^2_0 > 0$ denote the within-population heritability of a trait in each group. Then the mean difference $\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2$ between the two populations is not constrained by $h^2_0$ and may be entirely environmental.*

*Proof.* Decompose the phenotype of individual $i$ in population $P_k$ as
$$
X_{ik} \;=\; \mu + A_{ik} + E_{ik},
$$
where $A_{ik}$ is the additive-genetic contribution and $E_{ik}$ is the environmental contribution, with $\mathrm{Var}(A_{ik}) = V_A$ and $\mathrm{Var}(E_{ik}) = V_E$ constant across populations. The within-population heritability is
$$
h^2_0 \;=\; \frac{V_A}{V_A + V_E},
$$
which is identical in $P_1$ and $P_2$ by assumption.

Suppose the two populations are genetically identical — $\mathbb{E}[A_{i1}] = \mathbb{E}[A_{i2}]$ — but experience different mean environments, $\mathbb{E}[E_{i1}] = \Delta_1 \neq \Delta_2 = \mathbb{E}[E_{i2}]$. The population means are
$$
\bar{X}_1 \;=\; \mu + \mathbb{E}[A_{i1}] + \Delta_1, \qquad \bar{X}_2 \;=\; \mu + \mathbb{E}[A_{i2}] + \Delta_2,
$$
so the mean difference is
$$
\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2 \;=\; (\mathbb{E}[A_{i1}] - \mathbb{E}[A_{i2}]) + (\Delta_1 - \Delta_2) \;=\; \Delta_1 - \Delta_2,
$$
which is entirely environmental. The within-population heritability $h^2_0 = V_A / (V_A + V_E)$ is unaffected by the values of $\Delta_1$ and $\Delta_2$; it depends only on the within-population variances.

Consequently, knowledge of $h^2_0$ in either or both populations places no constraint on the cause of the mean difference $\bar{X}_1 - \bar{X}_2$. The within-group heritability can be unity ($V_E = 0$ within each group, $h^2_0 = 1$) while the between-group difference is entirely environmental (Lewontin 1970). Equally, the within-group heritability can be small ($h^2_0 = 0.2$) while the between-group difference is entirely genetic.

The implication for the IQ-heritability literature is direct. Within-population heritability estimates of $h^2 \approx 0.5$ to $0.8$ do not constrain the cause of mean IQ differences between contemporary racial or socioeconomic groups. Direct evidence on between-group environmental differences — adoption studies, intervention programmes, secular trends — is required to adjudicate the cause. This is the formal content of the consensus that within-group heritability is irrelevant to between-group genetic causation. $\square$
