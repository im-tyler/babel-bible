## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine 100 people in a room with \$100 total. If everyone has \$1, perfect equality. If one person has \$99 and the other 99 share \$1, extreme inequality. Most real economies fall between these extremes -- but where, exactly?

**Income inequality** describes how unevenly income is distributed across a population. It exists in every economy, but the degree varies enormously. In the Nordic countries, the richest 10% earn about 5-6 times more than the poorest 10%. In parts of Latin America and sub-Saharan Africa, the ratio can exceed 20 or 30.

Why does it matter? Two reasons. First, extreme inequality may be unfair -- people's incomes depend partly on circumstances they did not choose (family background, health, geography, innate talent). Second, inequality may have economic consequences: it can reduce social mobility, increase political instability, and, according to some economists, reduce overall economic growth.

Measuring inequality requires tools. The simplest is to compare the share of total income going to different groups (e.g., the top 10% versus the bottom 50%). A more comprehensive measure is the **Gini coefficient**, which summarises the entire income distribution in a single number between 0 (perfect equality) and 1 (perfect inequality).

Governments redistribute income through **progressive taxation** (higher earners pay a larger share) and **transfer payments** (welfare, unemployment benefits, social security). The central debate is how much redistribution is appropriate, given the potential trade-off between equity and efficiency.

It is important to distinguish between income inequality and wealth inequality. Income refers to what people earn over a period (wages, salaries, investment returns). Wealth refers to what people own at a point in time (property, savings, financial assets, minus debts). Wealth inequality is typically much larger than income inequality, because wealth accumulates over lifetimes and across generations through inheritance. In the United States, the top 1% of earners receive about 20% of income, but the top 1% of wealth holders own about 40% of all wealth.

Understanding the distinction matters because income and wealth inequality have different causes and consequences. Income inequality is driven by differences in wages, returns to education, and labour market conditions. Wealth inequality is driven by savings rates, investment returns, inheritance, and asset price inflation (particularly housing). The policy tools for addressing each type of inequality differ: progressive income taxes address income inequality, while wealth taxes, inheritance taxes, and property taxes address wealth inequality.

A further distinction is between **relative inequality** (how income shares are distributed) and **absolute inequality** (the dollar gap between rich and poor). In a growing economy, it is possible for relative inequality to fall while absolute inequality rises -- everyone gets richer, but the rich get richer by more in absolute terms. Conversely, during a recession, relative inequality might stay constant while absolute gaps shrink. Which measure matters more is partly an empirical question and partly a normative one.

## Worked Example [Beginner]

A country with 10 households has the following market incomes (in thousands):

| Household | Income | Cumulative Income | Cumulative % Income | Cumulative % Population |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| 1 (poorest) | \$5 | \$5 | 3.3% | 10% |
| 2 | \$8 | \$13 | 8.7% | 20% |
| 3 | \$10 | \$23 | 15.3% | 30% |
| 4 | \$12 | \$35 | 23.3% | 40% |
| 5 | \$14 | \$49 | 32.7% | 50% |
| 6 | \$16 | \$65 | 43.3% | 60% |
| 7 | \$18 | \$83 | 55.3% | 70% |
| 8 | \$22 | \$105 | 70.0% | 80% |
| 9 | \$30 | \$135 | 90.0% | 90% |
| 10 (richest) | \$15 | \$150 | 100% | 100% |

The bottom 50% of households earn 32.7% of total income. The top 10% earn 10.0%. The Lorenz curve bows only slightly below the equality line, indicating moderate inequality.

The Gini coefficient can be approximated from the area between the equality line and the Lorenz curve. A rough calculation gives a Gini of approximately 0.22 -- relatively low by global standards. For comparison: Sweden's Gini is about 0.27, the United States about 0.39, and South Africa about 0.63.

Now consider the effect of a progressive tax system. Suppose the government imposes a 10% tax on income up to \$15,000 and a 30% tax on income above \$15,000. The revenue is distributed equally as a transfer to all households.

Household 1 (income \$5,000) pays \$500 in tax and receives a transfer. Household 10 (income \$15,000) pays \$1,500 in tax and receives the same transfer. Household 9 (income \$30,000) pays \$1,500 + \$4,500 = \$6,000 in tax and receives the transfer. The transfer amount depends on total revenue, but the effect is clear: high earners pay more, low earners pay less, and the transfer equalises post-tax income to some degree. The post-tax Gini will be lower than the pre-tax Gini.

This example illustrates the basic mechanism of redistribution: progressive taxation collects more from high earners, and transfers provide more to low earners. The result is a more equal distribution of post-tax income. The economic question is whether the gains from reduced inequality outweigh the potential efficiency costs of the tax system.

Now consider what happens if the government also provides universal public services -- free primary education, basic healthcare, public transportation -- funded by the same tax revenue. These in-kind transfers are difficult to value precisely, but they disproportionately benefit lower-income households who could not afford these services at market prices. When economists measure inequality after both cash transfers and the value of public services, the reduction in inequality is substantially larger than when only cash transfers are counted. This is why comparisons of inequality across countries should account for differences in public service provision, not just tax and transfer systems.

## Formal Definition [Intermediate+]

### Lorenz curve and Gini coefficient

Let $F(y)$ be the cumulative distribution function of income and $\mu$ be the mean income. The **Lorenz curve** plots the cumulative share of total income against the cumulative share of population:

$$L(p) = \frac{1}{\mu} \int_0^{F^{-1}(p)} y \, dF(y)$$

where $p$ is the cumulative population share. $L(p)$ maps $[0,1] \to [0,1]$ with $L(0) = 0$ and $L(1) = 1$.

The **Gini coefficient** is:

$$G = \frac{A}{A + B} = 1 - 2\int_0^1 L(p) \, dp$$

Equivalently, $G$ is half the mean absolute difference relative to the mean:

$$G = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \sum_{j=1}^{n} |y_i - y_j|}{2n^2 \bar{y}}$$

The Gini coefficient has several properties that make it useful but also limited. It satisfies anonymity (the Gini does not depend on who holds which income), scale independence (multiplying all incomes by a constant does not change Gini), and the principle of transfers (transferring income from a richer to a poorer person reduces Gini). However, it does not uniquely identify the distribution: different income distributions can yield the same Gini coefficient, and it is more sensitive to changes in the middle of the distribution than to changes at the extremes.

### Atkinson index

The **Atkinson index** $A(\epsilon)$ incorporates normative judgments about inequality through the inequality aversion parameter $\epsilon \geq 0$:

$$A(\epsilon) = 1 - \left[ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( \frac{y_i}{\bar{y}} \right)^{1-\epsilon} \right]^{1/(1-\epsilon)}$$

for $\epsilon \neq 1$. Higher $\epsilon$ places more weight on the incomes of the poor. When $\epsilon = 0$, $A = 0$ regardless of distribution (inequality does not matter). As $\epsilon \to \infty$, the index reflects only the income of the poorest individual (Rawlsian).

The Atkinson index can be interpreted as the fraction of total income that a society would be willing to sacrifice to achieve perfect equality. If $A(1) = 0.15$, society would give up 15% of its income to have equal distribution. This interpretation makes explicit the normative judgments embedded in inequality measurement.

### Theil index and generalised entropy

The **Theil index** belongs to the family of generalised entropy measures. The mean log deviation (Theil's L) is:

$$GE_0 = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \ln\left(\frac{\bar{y}}{y_i}\right)$$

The Theil index (Theil's T) is:

$$GE_1 = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{y_i}{\bar{y}} \ln\left(\frac{y_i}{\bar{y}}\right)$$

These measures are additively decomposable: total inequality can be partitioned into "within-group" and "between-group" components. This decomposition is useful for analysing how much of a country's inequality is due to differences between regions, between urban and rural areas, or between educational groups, versus how much exists within each group.

### Redistribution and the equity-efficiency trade-off

A standard formulation of the redistribution problem (following Mirrlees, 1971) models the government as choosing a tax schedule $T(y)$ to maximise a social welfare function:

$$\max_{T(\cdot)} \int W(u_i(c_i, \ell_i)) \, dF(\theta_i)$$

subject to incentive compatibility constraints (individuals choose labour supply $\ell_i$ given the tax schedule). Higher redistribution reduces inequality but may reduce labour supply and output. The optimal tax rate balances the marginal social benefit of redistribution against the marginal efficiency cost of distorting work incentives.

## Key Concepts [Intermediate+]

- **Income inequality**: The unequal distribution of income across individuals or households in an economy.

- **Wealth inequality**: The unequal distribution of net assets. Typically exceeds income inequality.

- **Lorenz curve**: A graphical representation of income distribution, plotting cumulative income share against cumulative population share.

- **Gini coefficient**: A summary measure of inequality from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality). Equals twice the area between the Lorenz curve and the 45-degree line.

- **Progressive taxation**: A tax system in which the average tax rate increases with income. Higher earners pay a larger share of income in tax.

- **Transfer payments**: Government payments to individuals (welfare, unemployment benefits, social security) that redistribute income.

- **Equity-efficiency trade-off**: The idea that redistribution reduces inequality but may reduce economic efficiency by distorting incentives to work, save, and invest.

- **Social mobility**: The degree to which individuals can move between income groups across generations. High inequality and low mobility often coincide.

- **Atkinson index**: A measure of inequality that incorporates normative judgments through an inequality aversion parameter, allowing the analyst to specify how much weight to place on the incomes of the poor.

- **Theil index**: An entropy-based measure of inequality that is additively decomposable into within-group and between-group components.

- **Top income shares**: The share of total income received by the top 1%, 5%, or 10% of earners. A common and intuitive measure of concentration at the top of the distribution.

- **Poverty rate**: The fraction of the population below a defined income threshold (absolute or relative). Related to but distinct from inequality; a society can have low inequality and high poverty, or high inequality and low poverty.

- **Universal basic income (UBI)**: A proposal to provide a fixed cash payment to all citizens regardless of income or employment status, funded by taxation. Advocates argue it simplifies redistribution; critics question its fiscal sustainability and work incentives.

- **r > g**: Piketty's central finding that the rate of return on capital tends to exceed the rate of economic growth, leading to wealth concentration over time.

<strong>Exercise 2.</strong> Suppose a government introduces a flat tax of 20% on all income and redistributes the revenue equally as a lump-sum transfer. Show that this reduces inequality (the post-tax Gini is lower than the pre-tax Gini). Is this system progressive?
<details><summary>Answer</summary>
Let total income be Y and population be N. Each person receives a transfer of 0.2Y/N. A person with income y_i now has 0.8y_i + 0.2Y/N. The transfer is a larger fraction of income for low earners (0.2Y/N divided by a small y_i) than for high earners. This compresses the distribution, reducing the Gini. The flat tax alone is proportional (same rate for everyone), but the flat tax combined with a universal lump-sum transfer is progressive: the net fiscal impact (transfers minus tax) represents a larger share of income for low earners. ## Global Perspectives [Intermediate+]

### Global inequality trends

The story of global inequality over the past four decades is one of divergence within countries and convergence between countries. The economists Branko Milanovic and Christoph Lakner documented what has been called the "elephant curve": between 1988 and 2008, the biggest income gains went to people around the 50th percentile of the global distribution (predominantly the rising middle class in China and India), while those around the 80th percentile (the working and lower-middle class in rich countries, particularly the United States) saw very little growth. The very top of the distribution -- the global top 1% -- also saw enormous gains.

This pattern helps explain the political upheavals in wealthy countries: the people who saw the least benefit from globalisation were precisely the groups that once formed the economic backbone of industrialised nations. The political consequences -- the rise of populism, the backlash against trade and immigration, the erosion of trust in institutions -- are partly rooted in this distributional dynamic.

Within most wealthy countries, inequality has risen since the 1980s. In the United States, the share of pre-tax income going to the top 1% rose from about 10% in 1980 to over 20% by 2015. The UK, Canada, and Ireland experienced similar though less extreme trends. Meanwhile, some continental European countries (France, Germany) and the Nordic nations maintained more stable distributions, partly through stronger labour unions, more progressive tax systems, and more generous welfare states.

### Inequality in the Global South

Latin America has historically been the most unequal region in the world, with Gini coefficients typically between 0.45 and 0.60. This inequality has deep colonial roots: the concentration of land ownership, the exploitation of indigenous and enslaved populations, and the extraction of wealth by colonial powers created structural patterns of inequality that persisted long after independence. Brazil, Mexico, Colombia, and Chile all grapple with the legacy of these colonial structures.

Remarkably, Latin America experienced a significant decline in inequality between 2000 and 2015, driven by commodity-driven growth, expansion of education, and targeted social programmes (such as Brazil's Bolsa Familia and Mexico's Oportunidades). These conditional cash transfer programmes paid poor families to keep their children in school and attend health check-ups, simultaneously reducing current poverty and investing in future human capital. Studies found that Bolsa Familia increased school attendance, improved child nutrition, and reduced child labour, while the cash transfers themselves had minimal negative effects on adult labour supply, suggesting that the equity-efficiency trade-off may be less severe than critics claim when transfers are well designed. However, since 2015, progress has stalled or reversed in several countries due to economic crises and political instability.

Sub-Saharan Africa faces additional challenges. Many countries lack the administrative capacity to collect progressive taxes or deliver transfers effectively. Informal economies dominate, making income measurement difficult. Ethnic and regional inequalities overlap with economic inequality, creating complex patterns of disadvantage. In South Africa, the end of apartheid dismantled legal racial inequality but economic inequality actually increased in the post-apartheid era, with the Gini rising from about 0.60 in 1994 to 0.63 by 2015. The emerging black middle class and elite benefited from economic liberalisation, while the majority of black South Africans saw limited improvement.

### Gender and inequality

Gender is a major axis of inequality in every country. Globally, women earn approximately 77 cents for every dollar earned by men, though this gap varies enormously by country and has been narrowing over time. The gender pay gap reflects multiple factors: occupational segregation (women are overrepresented in lower-paying fields), the motherhood penalty (women's earnings decline after having children while men's do not), unpaid care work (which reduces women's available hours for paid employment), and direct discrimination.

The gender dimension of inequality is often invisible in standard measures. The Gini coefficient and income shares are typically calculated at the household level, obscuring inequality within households. A household with a high-earning male partner and an unpaid female caregiver may appear equally well-off as a household where both partners earn moderate incomes, but the distribution of resources and decision-making power within the household may be very different.

Feminist economists have advocated for measures that account for unpaid care work, time use, and within-household inequality. Diane Elson's work on gender-responsive budgeting shows how fiscal policy can either reduce or reinforce gender inequality depending on how taxes and spending are structured.

### Race, caste, and inequality

Racial and ethnic disparities in income and wealth are persistent features of many economies. In the United States, the median white household holds approximately eight times the wealth of the median Black household, a gap that has barely changed in decades. This disparity reflects historical injustices (slavery, segregation, discriminatory housing policies) as well as ongoing discrimination in labour markets, credit markets, and the criminal justice system.

In India, caste-based inequality remains significant despite legal prohibitions on discrimination. Members of Scheduled Castes (Dalits) and Scheduled Tribes (Adivasis) have substantially lower incomes, educational attainment, and occupational mobility than upper-caste groups. Affirmative action policies (reservations in education and government employment) have created opportunities for some, but the overall pattern of caste-based inequality persists.

Indigenous populations in Australia, Canada, New Zealand, and throughout the Americas experience similarly entrenched disadvantage. The gap in life expectancy, income, and educational outcomes between indigenous and non-indigenous populations in these countries is a stark reminder that inequality is not merely an economic phenomenon but is deeply intertwined with history, culture, and political power.

### Technology and inequality

Technological change has been a major driver of rising inequality in wealthy countries. The automation of routine tasks has reduced demand for middle-skill workers in manufacturing and clerical occupations, hollowing out the income distribution and creating what economists call "job polarisation": growth in high-skill, high-wage jobs (software engineers, financial analysts) and low-skill, low-wage jobs (care workers, food service), with declining opportunities in between.

The rise of artificial intelligence may accelerate this trend. If AI can perform cognitive tasks that were previously the domain of educated professionals -- legal research, medical diagnosis, financial analysis -- the demand for high-skill labour could also fall, concentrating income even further among the owners of capital and technology. Some economists, such as Daron Acemoglu, have argued that the direction of technological change is not inevitable but is shaped by institutions, policies, and power relations. Technologies that augment workers' capabilities can reduce inequality, while technologies that replace workers tend to increase it.

The platform economy (Uber, TaskRabbit, Deliveroo) represents another dimension of technology-driven inequality. These platforms classify workers as independent contractors, avoiding minimum wage laws, benefits, and job security. While they provide flexible earning opportunities, they also shift risk from employers to workers and contribute to the erosion of stable, middle-income employment. The question of how to regulate platform work to protect workers without destroying the flexibility that some value is one of the most active policy debates in labour economics.

### Education and inequality

Education is widely seen as the primary route out of poverty and the great equaliser of opportunity. The data supports this in part: each additional year of schooling is associated with roughly 8-13% higher earnings, and countries with more educated workforces tend to have lower inequality. However, education can also reproduce inequality. Children from wealthy families attend better schools, receive more educational support at home, and are more likely to attend university. The educational attainment gap between children from high-income and low-income families is large and persistent in most countries.

In the United States, the college wage premium (the ratio of college graduate earnings to high school graduate earnings) has risen from about 1.5 in 1980 to over 1.8 today, reflecting both the increasing demand for skilled labour and the slowdown in educational attainment growth since the 1990s. This premium drives inequality because access to college is itself unequal: children from families in the top income quartile are roughly ten times more likely to complete a bachelor's degree than children from the bottom quartile.

Finland's education system, often cited as a model, combines universal high-quality early childhood education, well-trained teachers, minimal standardised testing, and strong support for struggling students. The result is both high overall achievement and relatively small disparities between schools -- suggesting that education policy can reduce inequality, but only when combined with broader social policies that address the conditions (housing, nutrition, healthcare) that affect children's ability to learn.

### Health and inequality

Health inequality is both a cause and a consequence of economic inequality. Low-income individuals have worse health outcomes, shorter life expectancies, and higher rates of chronic disease. In the United States, the gap in life expectancy between the richest 1% and the poorest 1% is approximately 15 years for men and 10 years for women. These health gaps reflect differences in access to healthcare, exposure to environmental hazards, nutrition, stress, and health behaviours shaped by economic circumstances.

The COVID-19 pandemic starkly illustrated the intersection of health and economic inequality. Infection and mortality rates were significantly higher in low-income communities and communities of colour, both because of greater exposure (frontline work, crowded housing) and because of pre-existing health disparities. The pandemic also exacerbated economic inequality: while many low-wage workers lost their jobs or faced dangerous workplace conditions, the wealth of billionaires increased by trillions of dollars as stock markets recovered and digital platforms profited from the shift to remote life.

### Tax havens and global inequality

An often-overlooked dimension of global inequality is the role of tax havens and offshore financial centres. Gabriel Zucman estimates that approximately 8% of global financial wealth -- roughly \$7.6 trillion -- is held in tax havens, much of it unreported and untaxed. This hidden wealth disproportionately belongs to the very rich: in some countries, the top 0.01% of wealth holders hold more than 50% of their wealth offshore.

The existence of tax havens undermines the ability of governments to tax capital income and wealth, which is one of the primary tools for reducing inequality. When the wealthy can move their assets to low-tax jurisdictions, the burden of taxation shifts to less mobile factors -- labour income and consumption -- which are the primary sources of income for the majority of the population. This creates a structural bias in the tax system that favours capital over labour, contributing to the r > g dynamic identified by Piketty.

International efforts to address tax evasion, such as the OECD's Common Reporting Standard and the EU's list of non-cooperative jurisdictions, have made progress but face resistance from both tax haven jurisdictions and the wealthy individuals and corporations that benefit from them. The question of how to achieve international tax cooperation in a world of sovereign states with competing interests parallels the free rider problems discussed in Unit 23.

## Academic Perspectives [Master]

### Neoclassical view

Neoclassical economists analyse redistribution through the lens of the equity-efficiency trade-off. The seminal contribution is Mirrlees (1971), which models optimal income taxation as a problem of maximising social welfare subject to incentive compatibility constraints. The key result is that the optimal top marginal tax rate depends on the elasticity of labour supply: if high earners reduce their work effort significantly in response to higher taxes, the optimal tax rate is lower.

Saez (2001) extended the Mirrlees framework, deriving optimal tax formulas expressed in terms of observable elasticities and the income distribution. The Saez formula gives the optimal top marginal tax rate as:

$$\tau^* = \frac{1 - \bar{g}}{1 - \bar{g} + a \cdot e}$$

where $\bar{g}$ is the average social marginal welfare weight of top earners, $a$ is the Pareto parameter of the income distribution, and $e$ is the elasticity of taxable income. This framework supports top marginal rates in the range of 50-80% under plausible parameter values.

Neoclassical economists disagree on the severity of the trade-off. Some (e.g., Feldstein, 1999) emphasise the deadweight loss of taxation. Others (e.g., Diamond and Saez, 2011) argue that the efficiency costs are modest at current tax levels and that significant redistribution is both feasible and desirable.

The neoclassical framework also provides tools for decomposing inequality into its sources. The Shapley decomposition attributes changes in inequality to changes in different income components (wages, capital income, transfers, taxes). This decomposition reveals that rising wage inequality (the increasing premium for high skills) has been the primary driver of rising income inequality in most wealthy countries, with capital income playing a secondary role. However, the role of capital income is growing, particularly at the very top of the distribution.

The neoclassical tradition also emphasises the distinction between inequality of outcomes and inequality of opportunity. Inequality of outcomes (the distribution of income at a point in time) may be less concerning than inequality of opportunity (the extent to which individuals' economic prospects are determined by circumstances beyond their control, such as family background, race, or gender). Roemer (1998) and others have developed frameworks for measuring inequality of opportunity, finding that a substantial share of income inequality in most countries can be attributed to circumstances rather than effort. This distinction has policy implications: while redistribution addresses inequality of outcomes, investments in education, healthcare, and anti-discrimination policy address inequality of opportunity at its source.

The concept of **predistribution** -- shaping the pre-tax distribution of income through labour market institutions, education policy, and market regulation -- has gained traction as a complement to traditional redistribution. The argument is that relying solely on tax-and-transfer redistribution to address inequality is both economically costly and politically fragile, whereas policies that produce a more equal market distribution in the first place may be more sustainable.

### Keynesian perspective

Keynesians emphasise that inequality can reduce aggregate demand. If marginal propensity to consume falls with income, then transferring income from high earners (who save a large share) to low earners (who spend a larger share) increases total consumption and stimulates economic activity. In this view, redistribution can enhance efficiency, not just equity -- the trade-off may be false during recessions or periods of deficient demand.

Keynesians also point to the macroeconomic instability associated with high inequality. Rajan (2010) argued that rising inequality in the United States led to political pressure for easy credit, contributing to the 2008 financial crisis. Stiglitz (2012) has argued that inequality distorts political institutions, leading to policies that favour the wealthy at the expense of overall economic performance.

The Keynesian perspective also emphasises the role of labour market institutions. Strong labour unions, minimum wages, and collective bargaining can compress the wage distribution and reduce inequality without relying solely on tax-and-transfer redistribution. The decline of unionisation in the United States and the UK since the 1980s correlates closely with rising wage inequality, though causation is debated.

### Austrian school

Austrian economists are generally opposed to government redistribution, arguing that market outcomes reflect individual choices, productivity, and consumer preferences. Inequality, in the Austrian view, is a natural and desirable feature of a dynamic economy: it reflects differences in talent, effort, risk-taking, and time preference. Entrepreneurs who create value for consumers earn high incomes as a reward; redistributing those incomes reduces the incentive to innovate.

Mises and Hayek argued that government cannot determine a "fair" distribution because value is subjective and knowledge is dispersed. Any redistribution scheme requires a central planner with knowledge that no individual or bureaucracy possesses. Hayek did support a basic safety net, but argued that it should be minimal and universal rather than targeted and redistributive.

Critics note that this view does not adequately account for the role of inherited wealth, structural barriers, and market power in generating inequality. Market outcomes reflect initial endowments and institutional rules, not purely individual merit.

### Marxian perspective

Marxian economists view inequality not as a policy problem to be corrected but as an inherent feature of capitalism. The capitalist mode of production, by paying workers less than the value they produce (extracting surplus value), necessarily generates inequality between the class that owns the means of production and the class that sells its labour. Redistribution through taxation and transfers addresses symptoms but not causes.

Piketty (2014), while not strictly Marxian, provided empirical support for a structural tendency toward inequality in capitalism. His central finding -- that the rate of return on capital ($r$) tends to exceed the rate of economic growth ($g$), causing wealth to concentrate over time -- echoes Marxian concerns about the dynamics of capital accumulation. Piketty's solution (a global wealth tax) is reformist rather than revolutionary, but the analysis of structural inequality aligns with Marxian themes.

The Marxian tradition also analyses how inequality is reproduced through institutions. The education system, the media, the legal system, and the state all serve, in this view, to legitimise and reproduce class divisions. Education is supposed to be a great equaliser, but Bourdieu's concept of cultural capital shows how the education system often reinforces existing class advantages by rewarding the cultural knowledge and behaviour of dominant groups.

### Institutional economics

Institutional economists focus on how the "rules of the game" shape the distribution of income. Acemoglu and Robinson (2012) argue that inclusive economic institutions (secure property rights, competitive markets, broad access to opportunities) produce lower inequality and higher growth, while extractive institutions concentrate power and wealth. The distribution of income is not a natural outcome of market forces but a product of political and economic institutions.

This perspective helps explain why similar levels of economic development can be associated with very different levels of inequality. South Korea and Taiwan developed rapidly with relatively equal distributions, while many Latin American and African countries at similar income levels experienced much higher inequality. The difference lies partly in historical institutional choices: land reform, education policy, labour market regulation, and the political power of different social groups.

Walter Scheidel's *The Great Leveler* (2017) takes a longer historical view, arguing that significant reductions in inequality have almost always been associated with what he calls the "Four Horsemen" of levelling: mass mobilisation warfare, transformative revolution, state collapse, and catastrophic pandemics. Peaceful policy instruments, in Scheidel's analysis, have had limited success in reducing inequality compared to these violent disruptions. This pessimistic conclusion has been challenged, but it highlights the deep structural forces that sustain inequality and the difficulty of overcoming them through policy alone.

### Behavioral perspectives

Behavioral economics contributes to the inequality debate by challenging the standard assumption that people's economic outcomes reflect their preferences and abilities. **Implicit bias** in hiring has been documented through audit studies: identical resumes with different-sounding names (e.g., Emily vs. Lakisha) receive different callback rates. **Present bias** and limited self-control can explain why people in poverty make choices (high-interest borrowing, low savings) that perpetuate their disadvantage -- not because they are irrational, but because the cognitive demands of poverty deplete the mental bandwidth available for long-term decision-making.

Mullainathan and Shafir (2013) argue that scarcity creates a "scarcity mindset" that narrows attention to immediate needs, making it harder to plan, invest, and escape poverty. This insight challenges both the neoclassical view that poverty reflects poor choices and the structural view that it reflects poor circumstances: it suggests a feedback loop where adverse circumstances produce cognitive patterns that reinforce those circumstances.

### Public choice perspective

Public choice theorists, notably Gordon Tullock, point out that redistribution programmes do not necessarily flow from the rich to the poor. In practice, much government spending benefits the middle class and the politically connected: agricultural subsidies go to large farms, mortgage interest deductions benefit higher-income homeowners, and professional licensing restricts competition in ways that benefit incumbents. Tullock called these transfers "transfers to the rich" and argued that the political process tends to serve organised interest groups rather than the truly disadvantaged.

This perspective does not argue against all redistribution but cautions that the actual pattern of government transfers may be very different from what the theory of progressive taxation suggests. The design of redistribution programmes matters enormously: poorly designed programmes can redistribute upward rather than downward, creating inequality through the very mechanisms intended to reduce it.

### Feminist economics

Feminist economists have been at the forefront of challenging how inequality is measured and understood. The standard framework measures inequality in market income, which systematically excludes unpaid care work, predominantly performed by women. If unpaid care work were valued at market rates, it would add an estimated \$10.8 trillion to global GDP according to Oxfam (2020), and its distribution would significantly alter measured inequality.

Feminist economists also challenge the individualistic focus of standard inequality measurement. Households are not unitary entities with shared preferences and pooled resources. The distribution of income and resources within households -- who controls spending, who makes financial decisions, whose needs are prioritised -- is a critical dimension of inequality that is invisible in household-level data.

Amartya Sen's capability approach offers a broader framework for understanding inequality. Sen argues that income is an imperfect measure of well-being because people convert income into valuable functioning (being healthy, being educated, participating in community life) at different rates. A person with a disability may need more income to achieve the same level of functioning as an able-bodied person. Two people with the same income may have very different capabilities depending on their health, social support, and political freedoms. The capability approach shifts the focus from income inequality to inequality in the real freedoms people have to lead lives they value.

Martha Nussbaum has extended Sen's framework into a concrete list of central capabilities (life, bodily health, bodily integrity, senses, imagination, thought, emotions, practical reason, affiliation, other species, play, control over one's environment) that every society should guarantee. This approach provides a philosophical foundation for understanding inequality that goes beyond economics while remaining empirically grounded.

## Intergenerational mobility and the Great Gatsby curve [Intermediate+]

The relationship between inequality and social mobility is captured by the **Great Gatsby curve**, named by Alan Krueger in 2012. This curve plots income inequality (measured by the Gini coefficient) against intergenerational earnings elasticity (a measure of how strongly parents' incomes predict their children's incomes). Countries with higher inequality tend to have lower social mobility: the children of the rich stay rich, and the children of the poor stay poor.

The mechanism works through several channels. In unequal societies, wealthy parents can invest more in their children's education, health, and social connections. Residential segregation means that children from different income groups attend different schools with different resources. Social networks that provide access to jobs and opportunities are concentrated among the affluent. The result is a self-reinforcing cycle where inequality in one generation reduces mobility in the next, which perpetuates inequality in the generation after that. Krueger argued that if the Great Gatsby curve holds, rising inequality since the 1980s predicts declining mobility in coming decades, a concerning prospect for societies that value equal opportunity.

The policy implications are significant. If inequality reduces mobility, then redistribution today may promote equality of opportunity tomorrow. Investing in universal education, healthcare, and early childhood programmes may be more effective at breaking the cycle of poverty than transfers to adults, because these investments expand the capabilities of the next generation. This argument reframes the equity-efficiency trade-off: redistribution may sacrifice some current efficiency for greater future efficiency by allowing talent to rise regardless of family background.

Intergenerational mobility also varies dramatically within countries. In the United States, mobility is higher in some regions (the upper Great Plains, the rural Midwest) and much lower in others (the Deep South, the Appalachian region). Raj Chetty's research team has shown that neighbourhood characteristics -- school quality, social capital, family structure, income diversity -- explain much of this variation, suggesting that place-based policies can improve mobility.

## Historical Context [Master]

- **Pareto (1897)**: Vilfredo Pareto observed that income distributions across countries and time periods follow a similar power-law pattern (the Pareto distribution). "Pareto's law" suggested that inequality was a stable feature of economies, resistant to policy intervention.

- **Kuznets (1955)**: Simon Kuznets hypothesised an inverted-U relationship between economic development and inequality: inequality rises in early industrialisation (as workers move from low-productivity agriculture to higher-productivity industry) and falls in later stages (as the service sector expands and education spreads). The "Kuznets curve" was influential but has not been supported by late-20th-century data.

- **Mirrlees (1971)**: James Mirrlees developed the theory of optimal income taxation, formalising the equity-efficiency trade-off. He received the Nobel Prize in 1996.

- **Atkinson (1970)**: Anthony Atkinson developed the Atkinson index and pioneered the normative measurement of inequality. His work emphasised that inequality measurement inevitably involves ethical judgments.

- **Piketty (2014)**: Thomas Piketty's *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* used centuries of tax data from multiple countries to document a long-run trend toward wealth concentration. The book's central claim -- $r > g$ leads to rising inequality -- sparked global debate and brought inequality to the forefront of economic policy discussions. Piketty and his collaborators subsequently launched the World Inequality Database, providing open-access data on the historical evolution of income and wealth distribution across more than a hundred countries.

- **World Inequality Report (2018-present)**: A collaborative effort (Alvaredo, Atkinson, Piketty, Saez, Zucman) providing standardised inequality data for over 100 countries, enabling systematic cross-country and temporal comparisons.

- **The Great Compression (1940s-1970s)**: The period from the 1940s to the 1970s saw a dramatic decline in income inequality in the United States and other wealthy nations. The top marginal income tax rate in the US exceeded 90% in the 1950s. Strong unions, expanding education, and social programmes contributed to what economists call "the great compression." Inequality began to rise again in the late 1970s and 1980s, coinciding with the policy shifts associated with Reagan and Thatcher -- deregulation, tax cuts, and reduced union power.

- **The Gilded Age and the Progressive Era**: The late 19th century in the United States was a period of extreme inequality, with the wealth of industrialists like Rockefeller, Carnegie, and Vanderbilt dwarfing the incomes of ordinary workers. This era gave rise to the Progressive movement, which advocated antitrust regulation, labour protections, and the introduction of the federal income tax (1913). The parallels between the Gilded Age and the present period of rising inequality are frequently noted by historians and economists.

- **Land reform in East Asia**: After World War II, the United States imposed land reform in Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan as part of democratisation efforts. Large estates were broken up and redistributed to tenant farmers. This dramatic redistribution of wealth is often credited with creating the relatively equal societies and broad-based economic growth that characterised East Asian development in the subsequent decades.

- **The Nordic model (20th century)**: The Scandinavian countries developed a distinctive approach to inequality that combines high levels of redistribution with strong economic performance. The "Nordic model" features high marginal tax rates, generous universal welfare programmes, strong labour unions, and active labour market policies. Despite (or because of) high taxes, these countries maintain high labour force participation, strong productivity growth, and high living standards. The Nordic experience challenges the assumption that redistribution necessarily undermines economic performance.

- **The Washington Consensus and rising inequality (1980s-1990s)**: The structural adjustment programmes promoted by the IMF and World Bank in the 1980s and 1990s often required developing countries to cut social spending, liberalise trade, and privatise state enterprises. While these policies were intended to promote growth, they frequently increased inequality by reducing the social safety net and exposing vulnerable populations to international competition without adequate support. The experience contributed to a reassessment of the relationship between market liberalisation and distributional outcomes.

- **The rise of the ultra-rich (2000s-present)**: The emergence of a new class of global billionaires, particularly in the technology sector, has drawn attention to extreme wealth concentration. The combined wealth of the world's 2,700 billionaires exceeds the GDP of all but a handful of nations. This concentration raises questions about the social function of extreme wealth, the adequacy of existing tax systems (which are designed for income rather than wealth), and the political influence of the ultra-rich. The debate over wealth taxes, inheritance taxes, and capital gains taxation is in part a debate about whether the current level of wealth concentration is compatible with democratic governance.

## Connections [Master]

This unit connects to the surrounding world sequence through prerequisites (23.01.16) and successors (none declared). In the wider Babel Bible map, income inequality and redistribution also links to mathematics when models, rates, maps, measurement, or incentives are involved; to philosophy when norms, rights, justice, or political legitimacy are at stake; and to language when categories, narratives, documents, and public arguments structure how people understand the domain. The cross-domain point is that social knowledge needs both evidence and interpretation.

The measurement of inequality connects to **GDP and economic measurement** (Unit 16), which provides the national income data that inequality measures require. **Fiscal policy** (Unit 18) is the primary instrument of redistribution. **Labour markets and wages** (Unit 13) determine the pre-tax distribution of income. **Economic systems** (Unit 27) shapes the institutional framework within which inequality is generated. **Development economics** (Unit 28) examines how inequality interacts with economic growth in low-income countries.

The philosophical dimensions of inequality are profound. The question of whether inequality is unjust, and if so what should be done about it, connects to theories of distributive justice from Rawls (who argued that inequalities are justified only if they benefit the least advantaged) to Nozick (who argued that any distribution arising from voluntary exchange is just, regardless of its shape). The economist's tools -- Gini coefficients, optimal tax formulas, social welfare functions -- can inform but cannot resolve these philosophical disagreements. The choice of inequality aversion parameter in the Atkinson index, the discount rate applied to future generations, and the social welfare function used in optimal tax analysis all embed ethical judgments that economics alone cannot justify.

The relationship between inequality and democracy is another important connection. High inequality can undermine democratic governance by concentrating political power alongside economic power, enabling the wealthy to shape policies in their own interest. This feedback loop between economic inequality and political inequality is a central concern of political economy.

Redistribution also connects to **externalities and public goods** (Unit 23): public goods like education and healthcare generate positive externalities that justify government provision, but their distributional impact is also significant. **Behavioural economics** (Unit 25) challenges standard assumptions about rationality that underlie both the neoclassical equity-efficiency trade-off and the Austrian defence of market outcomes. **Personal finance** (Units 29-30) examines how inequality at the individual level shapes financial decision-making, access to credit, and wealth accumulation.

The ethical question of what constitutes a just distribution is ultimately one that economics can inform but not answer. The choice between Rawls's difference principle (inequalities are just only if they benefit the least advantaged), Nozick's entitlement theory (any distribution arising from just acquisition and voluntary transfer is just), and utilitarianism (the distribution that maximises total welfare) is a philosophical question. Economics can tell us the consequences of each choice, but the choice itself requires ethical reasoning that goes beyond positive analysis.
