# Treaties and international law

## Intuition [Beginner]

Domestic law is enforced by governments: police arrest lawbreakers, courts try them, and prisons hold them. International law has no global police force, no world court with compulsory jurisdiction, and no global executive to enforce its judgments. Yet international law exists, is widely followed, and matters enormously.

**International law** is the body of rules that governs the relations between states (and, increasingly, between states and other actors like international organizations and individuals). Its sources are treaties (written agreements between states), customary law (practices that states follow out of a sense of legal obligation), and general principles of law recognized by civilized nations.

A **treaty** is a written agreement between two or more states that is binding under international law. Treaties go by many names -- convention, protocol, covenant, pact, accord, agreement -- but they all have the same legal character: states voluntarily accept obligations that they are then legally bound to fulfill.

The puzzle of international law is: why do states obey it when there is no global enforcer? Several answers:

- **Reputation**: states that break their agreements are trusted less in future negotiations.
- **Reciprocity**: states follow rules because they want others to follow them too.
- **Domestic enforcement**: many treaties are implemented through domestic law, enforced by domestic courts.
- **Institutional pressure**: international organizations monitor compliance and publicize violations.
- **Habit and legitimacy**: most states most of the time follow most rules because doing so is normal and expected.

International law is not weak -- it governs trade, diplomacy, war, human rights, the environment, aviation, shipping, and much of daily life across borders. But it is imperfect: enforcement is uneven, powerful states sometimes ignore it, and it evolves slowly.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider how the Paris Agreement on climate change (2015) works as a treaty:

1. **Negotiation**: 196 parties negotiated the agreement at the UN Climate Change Conference (COP21) in Paris. The negotiations were complex, involving differences between developed and developing countries, fossil-fuel-dependent economies and vulnerable island states, and countries with different views on how binding the commitments should be.

2. **Adoption**: The agreement was adopted by consensus on December 12, 2015.

3. **Signature and ratification**: States signed and then ratified according to their domestic procedures. The US (under Obama) ratified by executive agreement; the US (under Trump) withdrew in 2020; the US (under Biden) rejoined in 2021. This illustrates how domestic politics affects treaty participation.

4. **Structure**: The Paris Agreement is a "pledge and review" system. Each country submits a Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) -- its own plan for reducing emissions. The NDCs are not legally binding in their specific content (no country is required to hit a specific emissions target), but the process of submitting NDCs, reporting progress, and participating in periodic review is binding.

5. **Enforcement**: There is no enforcement mechanism for failure to meet NDC targets. The agreement relies on transparency (countries must report their emissions), peer pressure (global scrutiny of NDCs), and the expectation that naming and shaming will encourage compliance. Critics argue this is too weak; supporters argue that a stricter treaty would not have been ratified by enough countries.

The Paris Agreement illustrates a central feature of international law: it often reflects what states are willing to agree to, not what the problem demands. A treaty that perfectly solved climate change but that no major emitter would ratify would be legally elegant but practically useless. The Paris Agreement sacrifices binding specificity for broad participation.

## Check your understanding [Beginner]

<aside class="exercises">

**Exercise 1.** A country signs a treaty but never ratifies it. Is the treaty binding on that country?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

Not in the usual sense. Signature alone does not make a treaty binding; ratification (or a similar formal step like acceptance, approval, or accession) is required. However, under the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (Article 18), a state that has signed a treaty must "refrain from acts which would defeat the object and purpose of the treaty" until it has made its intention clear not to become a party. This is a limited obligation of good faith, not the full binding force of the treaty. A state that has signed but not ratified is not required to comply with the treaty's substantive provisions.

</details>

**Exercise 2.** The International Criminal Court (ICC) has issued arrest warrants for sitting heads of state. How can it do this if it has no police force?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

The ICC relies on member states to execute its arrest warrants. Under the Rome Statute (the ICC's founding treaty), states that are parties to the statute are obligated to cooperate with the court, including arresting individuals the court has charged. If a state refuses, the ICC can refer the matter to the Assembly of States Parties or the UN Security Council, but it cannot compel compliance.

In practice, some individuals wanted by the ICC have evaded arrest for years because the states where they live refuse to execute the warrant (Omar al-Bashir of Sudan traveled to several ICC member states without being arrested). The ICC's authority is legal and moral, not coercive. Its effectiveness depends on the willingness of states to enforce its decisions.

</details>

**Exercise 3.** Customary international law binds all states, even those that have not signed any treaty on the subject. How is it possible for a practice to become law without any state formally agreeing to it?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

Customary international law forms through two elements: (a) **general practice**: states consistently behave in a certain way over time; and (b) **opinio juris**: states follow the practice because they believe it is legally required, not just out of habit or convenience. For example, the principle that diplomats cannot be arrested by the host country existed as customary law long before it was codified in the Vienna Convention on Diplomatic Relations (1961). States followed this practice for centuries out of a sense of legal obligation.

Once a practice meets both criteria, it becomes binding on all states, except those that have "persistently objected" to the rule from the beginning (the persistent objector doctrine). Customary law is slow to form and difficult to identify precisely, but it fills gaps where no treaty exists.

</details>

</aside>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**International law** is the set of rules, norms, and principles that govern the conduct of states and other international actors in their relations with one another. Its formal sources are enumerated in Article 38(1) of the Statute of the International Court of Justice:

1. International conventions (treaties)
2. International custom (state practice + opinio juris)
3. General principles of law recognized by civilized nations
4. Judicial decisions and scholarly writings (as subsidiary means)

**Treaty law.** The Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (1969) is the "treaty on treaties," governing how treaties are made, interpreted, amended, and terminated. Key principles:

- **Pacta sunt servanda**: agreements must be kept. A state cannot invoke its domestic law as justification for failing to perform a treaty obligation (Article 27).
- **Consent**: treaties bind only those states that have consented to be bound (Article 11). There is no majority-rule legislation at the international level.
- **Reservations**: states may modify their treaty obligations through reservations, unless the reservation is prohibited by the treaty or incompatible with its object and purpose (Article 19).
- **Interpretation**: treaties are interpreted in good faith according to their ordinary meaning in context, in light of their object and purpose (Article 31).
- **Invalidity**: treaties can be invalidated by corruption, coercion, error, or conflict with a peremptory norm (jus cogens) of international law (Articles 46-53).

**Jus cogens** (peremptory norms) are rules from which no derogation is permitted. No treaty can violate them, and they bind all states regardless of consent. Accepted jus cogens norms include the prohibitions on genocide, slavery, torture, wars of aggression, and crimes against humanity. The concept is controversial because it imposes limits on state consent, which is the normal basis of international obligation.

**Treaty reservations.** A reservation is a unilateral statement by a state, made when signing or ratifying a treaty, that modifies or excludes certain provisions. Reservations allow states with different legal systems, cultural values, or policy preferences to join the same treaty while declining specific obligations. The practice promotes universality (more states join) but at the cost of uniformity (different states are bound by different obligations under the same treaty). Some treaties (like the European Convention on Human Rights) severely restrict reservations; others (like the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women, CEDAW) have attracted extensive reservations.


The comparative study of governance systems reveals that familiar arrangements are not universal. Different societies have developed different institutional solutions to the fundamental problems of political organization: how to make collective decisions, how to constrain power, how to represent diverse interests, and how to hold leaders accountable. Understanding these differences is essential for thinking about governance without assuming that any single country's approach is the default.

This comparative perspective is especially important because the academic study of government has historically been dominated by Western European and North American models. The assumption that familiar institutional arrangements represent the "normal" form of governance is itself a product of particular historical circumstances, including colonial expansion, economic dominance, and the global influence of American political science after World War II. When we look beyond these familiar models, we find that governance takes many forms, and that the range of institutional possibilities is much wider than any single country's experience suggests.

Indigenous governance systems around the world developed sophisticated institutional arrangements that often embodied principles -- distributed authority, accountability mechanisms, consensus-based decision-making -- that Western political theory later claimed as its own inventions. The Iroquois Confederacy practiced a form of federalism with checks and balances centuries before the US Constitution. The Maori developed systems of collective resource management (kaitiakitanga) that integrated environmental governance with political authority. Many sub-Saharan African societies practiced forms of accountable kingship where rulers could be removed by councils of elders. These traditions deserve attention not as curiosities but as genuine contributions to the global inventory of governance design.

**Competing perspectives on governance.** Scholars and citizens disagree about fundamental questions of governance. Should government be large or small? Should it prioritize individual liberty or collective welfare? Should it be democratic or expert-led? These disagreements are not merely matters of preference; they reflect different theoretical frameworks and different interpretations of evidence.

Libertarians argue that government should be minimal, limited to protecting individual rights and enforcing contracts. They contend that free markets, voluntary cooperation, and civil society can solve most collective problems without government intervention. Social democrats argue that government should play an active role in ensuring social welfare, reducing inequality, and providing public goods. They contend that unfettered markets produce unacceptable levels of inequality and social harm. Communitarians argue that government should promote shared values and community bonds, contending that individualistic approaches undermine the social fabric.

These debates are not abstract. They shape practical policy questions: Should health care be provided by government or by markets? Should education be publicly funded or privately chosen? Should environmental protection rely on regulation or market mechanisms? Should social welfare programs be universal or targeted? Different countries answer these questions differently, and the comparative study of government provides evidence about the consequences of different choices. No single approach has proven universally superior, suggesting that effective governance requires pragmatic adaptation to specific circumstances rather than rigid adherence to ideological principles.
## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

**Monism vs. dualism in domestic legal systems.** How does international law interact with domestic law? Two theories:

| Theory | Description | Effect | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| Monism | International and domestic law are one system; international law is automatically part of domestic law | Treaties take effect domestically without implementing legislation | France, Netherlands, Switzerland (varies in practice) |
| Dualism | International and domestic law are separate systems; international law must be "transformed" into domestic law through legislation | Treaties require implementing legislation before they create domestic rights and obligations | UK, Canada, Australia, India |

In practice, most states fall somewhere between pure monism and pure dualism. The US follows a mixed approach: self-executing treaties (those that create directly enforceable rights) take effect without legislation; non-self-executing treaties require implementing legislation. Even in monist systems, courts may be reluctant to give direct effect to treaty provisions that conflict with domestic law.

**The International Court of Justice (ICJ).** The ICJ is the principal judicial organ of the UN. It hears two types of cases:

1. **Contentious cases**: legal disputes between states that have accepted its jurisdiction (either by special agreement, treaty clause, or optional clause declaration). Judgments are binding on the parties.
2. **Advisory opinions**: legal advice requested by UN organs and specialized agencies. Advisory opinions are non-binding but carry legal and moral authority.

The ICJ's jurisdiction is not compulsory. States must consent to its jurisdiction, and many states have made reservations that exclude certain categories of disputes. This means the ICJ can only resolve disputes when both parties agree to let it.

**The International Criminal Court (ICC).** Established by the Rome Statute (1998, entered into force 2002), the ICC has jurisdiction over individuals (not states) accused of:

- Genocide
- Crimes against humanity
- War crimes
- The crime of aggression (since 2018, with restrictions)

The ICC's jurisdiction is limited: it can only act when the crime was committed on the territory of a member state or by a national of a member state, or when the UN Security Council refers a situation. Major states (US, China, Russia, India) are not members, limiting the ICC's reach. The principle of **complementarity** means the ICC acts only when national courts are unwilling or unable to prosecute -- it is a court of last resort, not a replacement for domestic justice.

### Exercises

**Exercise 1.** The US is not a party to the Rome Statute and does not recognize the ICC's jurisdiction over its citizens. Yet the ICC claims jurisdiction over alleged war crimes committed by US nationals on the territory of Afghanistan (an ICC member). How is this legal tension resolved?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

The ICC asserts jurisdiction based on the territorial principle: the alleged crimes occurred in Afghanistan, which is a member state. Under the Rome Statute, the ICC has jurisdiction over crimes committed on the territory of a member state, regardless of the nationality of the accused. The US rejects this claim, arguing that a state cannot be subject to a treaty it has not ratified and that the ICC's assertion of jurisdiction over non-members violates state sovereignty. The US imposed sanctions on ICC officials in 2020 (later lifted). The ICC proceeded with its investigation but faces practical limits: it cannot compel US cooperation, cannot arrest individuals without member state assistance, and cannot try anyone in absentia. The standoff illustrates the limits of international law when powerful non-members resist it.

</details>

**Exercise 2.** A treaty is adopted that contradicts an earlier treaty that some of the same states have ratified. Which treaty prevails?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

Under the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (Article 30), the general rule is that when states are parties to both treaties, the later treaty prevails over the earlier one to the extent of the inconsistency ("lex posterior derogat legi priori"). But if a state is party to the earlier treaty but not the later one, the earlier treaty governs relations between that state and other parties to the earlier treaty. If the earlier treaty establishes a rule of jus cogens, the later treaty is void to the extent of the conflict. In practice, conflicts between treaties are resolved through interpretation (courts try to read treaties as consistent), negotiation (states modify or supplement the treaties), or, if all else fails, the principle that the later expression of state intent prevails.

</details>


Governance in the Global South presents distinctive challenges and innovations that are often underrepresented in political science textbooks. Post-colonial states inherited institutional frameworks designed for extraction and control rather than democratic participation, and many have struggled to adapt these frameworks to serve their populations. However, this experience has also produced innovations: India's constitutional protections for disadvantaged groups, South Africa's constitutional enforcement of socio-economic rights, Botswana's integration of traditional governance with democratic institutions, and Rwanda's community-based justice and reconciliation processes.

The experience of the Global South also challenges some Western assumptions about governance. The assumption that economic development requires democratic institutions has been contradicted by the rapid growth of authoritarian states like China and Singapore. The assumption that strong central government is necessary for effective governance has been challenged by the success of decentralized systems in some developing countries. The assumption that Western-style political parties are the natural mechanism for aggregating interests has been challenged by the persistence of ethnic, religious, and patron-client networks as primary political organizations in many countries. These challenges do not invalidate Western governance models but suggest that they are not the only viable approach.


**Treaties and power asymmetry.** The history of treaty-making is deeply marked by power asymmetries between the parties. European colonial powers used treaties as instruments of domination, extracting concessions from less powerful societies through a combination of military threat, economic pressure, and legal manipulation. Many treaties between colonial powers and indigenous peoples were signed under duress, were based on fundamentally different understandings of sovereignty and property, or were subsequently violated by the more powerful party.

The treaties between the United States and Native American nations illustrate this pattern. The US government signed hundreds of treaties with indigenous nations, recognizing them as sovereign entities, but then violated many of these treaties when they became inconvenient. The Treaty of Fort Laramie (1868) guaranteed the Lakota people control of the Black Hills, but when gold was discovered, the US seized the land. The Supreme Court ruled in United States v. Sioux Nation (1980) that the seizure was illegal and ordered compensation, but the Lakota have refused the money, insisting on return of the land.

International law has evolved to address some of these asymmetries. The Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (1969) provides that treaties obtained by coercion are void. The principle of pacta sunt servanda (agreements must be kept) applies equally to all parties regardless of power. In practice, however, powerful states continue to shape treaty regimes to their advantage, and enforcement mechanisms remain weak. The challenge of creating a truly equitable international legal order remains one of the central challenges of global governance.

The law of the sea provides an example of how treaty law can evolve to accommodate the interests of less powerful states. The Third United Nations Conference on the Law of the Sea (1973-1982) was one of the longest and most complex treaty negotiations in history, producing the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS). A key innovation was the concept of the exclusive economic zone (EEZ), which gives coastal states control over resources within 200 nautical miles of their coast. This principle, championed by developing countries, fundamentally restructured the relationship between maritime powers and coastal states and demonstrated that treaty law could serve the interests of the less powerful as well as the powerful.
## Political theory [Master]

**Is international law really law?**

The legal philosopher John Austin (1832) defined law as the command of a sovereign backed by a sanction. By this standard, international law is not "real" law because there is no global sovereign to command and no reliable sanction for non-compliance. Hart (1961) offered a more sophisticated analysis: law exists when there is a "rule of recognition" -- a shared standard for identifying valid legal rules. International law arguably has such a standard (the sources enumerated in ICJ Statute Article 38), but it lacks secondary rules of change and adjudication that are well-developed in domestic systems.

Modern legal scholars generally reject Austin's narrow definition. International law is treated as law by the institutions that apply it (international courts, domestic courts, treaty bodies), by the states that follow it, and by the legal profession that advises on it. Its compliance rate, while imperfect, is comparable to many domestic legal systems. Henkin (1979) observed that "almost all nations observe almost all principles of international law and almost all of their obligations almost all of the time."

**International law and power.**

Realist scholars argue that international law reflects and reinforces the interests of powerful states. The structure of the UN Security Council (P5 veto) gives major powers legal authority to block action against themselves and their allies. The ICC has primarily prosecuted individuals from African states, leading to accusations of selective justice. Trade law rules (WTO) reflect the preferences of economically dominant states at the time they were negotiated. The corpus of international law was largely developed by European states during the colonial era and initially excluded non-European peoples from its protections.

Critical legal scholars (Koskenniemi, 2001) argue that international law's claims to neutrality and objectivity mask its political function: it provides a vocabulary for justifying the exercise of power while appearing to constrain it. The indeterminacy of legal rules -- their capacity to support multiple interpretations -- means that law can be invoked to support any position, and outcomes depend on which interpreter has authority.

**Compliance theories.** Why do states comply with international law? Several explanations compete:

- **Enforcement theory** (Downs, Rocke, and Barsoom, 1996): states comply when the costs of violation (sanctions, retaliation, loss of reputation) exceed the benefits. Compliance is rational, not virtuous.
- **Managerial theory** (Chayes and Chayes, 1995): most non-compliance results from ambiguity, capacity limitations, or unforeseen circumstances, not deliberate violation. The appropriate response is transparency, technical assistance, and dispute resolution, not punishment.
- **Legitimacy theory** (Franck, 1990): states comply because they perceive the rules as legitimate -- fairly made, plainly stated, and applied consistently. Legitimacy creates a "compliance pull" independent of enforcement.
- **Norm internalization** (Finnemore and Sikkink, 1998): international norms become internalized through socialization, persuasion, and institutionalization. Over time, states follow norms not because of external pressure but because they have come to accept them as appropriate.

Each theory captures part of the truth. States comply for a mixture of reasons that vary across issues, states, and circumstances.

**The enforcement gap.** The most significant weakness of international law is the gap between legal obligation and enforcement. When a state violates international law:

- There is no automatic enforcement mechanism.
- The injured state may retaliate (self-help), but this favors powerful states that can impose costs.
- International courts can issue binding judgments, but cannot compel compliance.
- The UN Security Council can authorize enforcement action, but only if no P5 member vetoes.
- Economic sanctions can be imposed, but their effectiveness is debated and their humanitarian costs can be severe.

This enforcement gap means that international law is strongest when it aligns with powerful states' interests and weakest when it contradicts them. This is not a flaw that can be easily corrected without creating a world government that states do not want.


Indigenous governance systems offer important perspectives on institutional design. Unlike the European state model, which organizes authority through territorial control and bureaucratic hierarchy, many indigenous systems organize authority through kinship, reciprocity, and relationship to land. The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy's Great Law of Peace established governance principles including participatory decision-making, recall mechanisms for leaders, and the requirement that decisions consider their impact on seven future generations. The Maori developed systems where authority (mana) was connected to responsibility (kaitiakitanga), and where leaders who failed to fulfill their obligations could lose their legitimacy.

Aboriginal Australian governance systems, among the oldest continuous governance traditions on Earth, organized authority through songlines, kinship networks, and elder councils. Decision-making was typically consensual, with the emphasis on maintaining social harmony and ecological balance rather than on majority rule. These systems governed complex societies for tens of thousands of years, demonstrating that sophisticated governance does not require the institutional forms that European political theory considers essential.

The incorporation of indigenous governance principles into modern constitutional frameworks is an ongoing process. New Zealand's Waitangi Tribunal interprets the Treaty of Waitangi in light of both Maori and Crown understandings. Canada's recognition of Aboriginal self-government in the Constitution Act, 1982 has led to negotiated self-government agreements. Bolivia's 2009 Constitution establishes a plurinational state that recognizes indigenous justice systems alongside the national legal system. These developments suggest that the future of governance may involve greater integration of indigenous and Western institutional traditions.


**International criminal law and accountability.** The development of international criminal law represents one of the most significant advances in international law since World War II. The Nuremberg and Tokyo tribunals established the principle that individuals -- including heads of state -- can be held criminally responsible for war crimes, crimes against humanity, and genocide. The International Criminal Court (ICC), established by the Rome Statute in 1998, provides a permanent institution for prosecuting these crimes when national courts are unable or unwilling to do so.

The ICC's experience illustrates both the promise and the limitations of international criminal justice. The Court has opened investigations in situations including Uganda, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Sudan, Libya, and Ukraine. It has secured convictions for war crimes and crimes against humanity. However, the Court has been criticized for focusing disproportionately on African situations (though it has also opened investigations in other regions), for its slow and expensive procedures, and for the limited cooperation it receives from powerful states.

The relationship between international criminal law and national sovereignty is deeply contested. The ICC operates on the principle of complementarity, meaning it can only act when national courts are unable or unwilling to prosecute. But what constitutes inability or unwillingness is itself contested. Some countries argue that the ICC's intervention undermines national reconciliation processes. Others argue that accountability for mass atrocities is a non-negotiable requirement of international justice.

The arrest of former Chilean dictator Augusto Pinochet in London in 1998, based on a Spanish warrant for crimes committed in Chile, demonstrated the potential reach of international criminal law. The principle of universal jurisdiction -- the idea that some crimes are so grave that any country can prosecute them regardless of where they were committed -- has been invoked in cases involving torture, genocide, and crimes against humanity. However, universal jurisdiction remains controversial, with critics arguing that it can be abused for political purposes and that it undermines the principle of sovereign equality.

**International environmental law.** Environmental protection has become one of the most active areas of international treaty-making. The Stockholm Conference (1972) placed the environment on the international agenda. The Montreal Protocol (1987) on ozone-depleting substances is often cited as the most successful environmental treaty, having achieved near-universal participation and significant reductions in the use of ozone-depleting chemicals. The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (1992) and the Paris Agreement (2015) have established frameworks for addressing climate change, though their effectiveness in reducing emissions remains uncertain.

International environmental law faces distinctive challenges. Environmental problems are transboundary -- pollution does not respect national borders -- but the legal and political system for addressing them is built on state sovereignty. The principle of "common but differentiated responsibilities," recognized in the Rio Declaration (1992), acknowledges that all countries have obligations to protect the environment but that developed countries, which have contributed more to environmental degradation and have greater resources, should bear a larger share of the burden. This principle is contested by some developed countries that argue that rapidly developing countries like China and India must also take on significant obligations.

The intersection of environmental law and indigenous rights is increasingly important. Indigenous peoples have been among the most effective stewards of biodiversity and forest conservation, and their traditional knowledge offers valuable insights for environmental management. International instruments like the Convention on Biological Diversity (1992) and the Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (2007) recognize the role of indigenous peoples in environmental governance. The challenge is to ensure that environmental protection efforts respect indigenous rights and knowledge rather than imposing top-down conservation measures that displace indigenous communities.

**International law and the use of force.** Perhaps no area of international law is more consequential -- or more contested -- than the rules governing the use of military force. The UN Charter prohibits the use of force except in self-defense or when authorized by the Security Council. This framework, established in 1945, represented a revolutionary attempt to prevent war by requiring collective security rather than unilateral action.

However, the framework has been challenged repeatedly since its creation. The Cold War paralyzed the Security Council, with the US and Soviet vetoes preventing collective action. Humanitarian interventions in Kosovo (1999), Libya (2011), and elsewhere raised questions about whether military force could be justified to prevent atrocities even without Security Council authorization. The US-led invasion of Iraq (2003) was widely regarded as illegal under international law, though the US argued it was justified by Iraq's alleged possession of weapons of mass destruction and previous Security Council resolutions.

The concept of the "responsibility to protect" (R2P), adopted by the UN World Summit in 2005, attempts to address the tension between sovereignty and human rights. R2P holds that states have the primary responsibility to protect their populations from genocide, war crimes, ethnic cleansing, and crimes against humanity, and that if a state fails to meet this responsibility, the international community must take appropriate action, including as a last resort the use of military force through the Security Council. R2P has been invoked in Libya (2011) but not in Syria, revealing the continuing influence of great power politics on humanitarian intervention.

**International law and technology.** Technology presents some of the most challenging new questions for international law. Cyber operations -- including espionage, sabotage, and influence campaigns -- blur the line between peace and war. Does a cyberattack on critical infrastructure constitute an "armed attack" that justifies self-defense under Article 51 of the UN Charter? Most scholars say it can, if the effects are comparable to those of kinetic weapons, but the threshold remains unclear. The Tallinn Manual, produced by a group of experts convened by NATO, attempts to clarify how international law applies to cyber operations, but it is an academic study rather than an authoritative legal document.

Artificial intelligence raises questions about accountability in international law. If an autonomous weapons system commits a war crime, who is responsible -- the programmer, the commander, the manufacturer, or the state that deployed the system? The Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons has hosted discussions on lethal autonomous weapons systems, but agreement on regulation has been elusive. The development of AI capabilities by states and non-state actors is outpacing the development of legal frameworks to govern their use.

Space law, initially developed to govern the peaceful exploration of outer space during the Cold War, faces new challenges as private companies become major space actors and as military competition extends to space. The Outer Space Treaty (1967) prohibits the placement of weapons of mass destruction in orbit and establishes that outer space is not subject to national appropriation. However, the treaty does not address many contemporary issues, including space debris, resource extraction on celestial bodies, and the militarization of space. Updating the legal framework for space activities is an important agenda for international law in the coming decades.


**International law and global challenges.** International law faces a fundamental paradox: it is more necessary than ever in an interconnected world, but it faces greater challenges to its effectiveness and legitimacy. Climate change, pandemics, cyber threats, migration, and economic inequality all require international cooperation underpinned by legal frameworks. Yet the voluntary, consent-based nature of international law means that its effectiveness depends on the willingness of states to comply with obligations they have accepted -- a willingness that is often influenced by considerations of power and interest rather than legal obligation.

The relationship between international law and domestic legal systems varies across countries. Monist systems, like those of France and the Netherlands, treat international law as automatically part of domestic law once ratified. Dualist systems, like those of the United Kingdom and India, require implementing legislation to give international treaties domestic legal effect. The United States takes a hybrid approach, distinguishing between self-executing treaties (which have domestic legal effect upon ratification) and non-self-executing treaties (which require implementing legislation). These different approaches affect the practical impact of international obligations on citizens and governments.

The role of non-state actors in international law is growing. International organizations, corporations, non-governmental organizations, and even individuals increasingly participate in international legal processes. The Paris Agreement's framework for non-state climate action, the role of NGOs in international human rights monitoring, and the development of international standards by private bodies like the International Organization for Standardization all reflect this trend. The traditional state-centric model of international law is being supplemented, though not replaced, by a more pluralistic approach that recognizes multiple sources of international legal authority.

The enforcement of international law remains its greatest challenge. Unlike domestic legal systems, which have police forces, courts, and penalties for non-compliance, international law relies primarily on voluntary compliance, reciprocity, reputation, and the occasional use of economic sanctions or military force. The International Court of Justice can issue binding judgments, but only in cases where states have accepted its jurisdiction. The International Criminal Court can prosecute individuals, but only when national courts are unable or unwilling to do so. Developing more effective enforcement mechanisms without undermining state sovereignty is the central challenge for the future of international law.
## Historical context [Master]

**The origins of international law.** The modern system of international law emerged from the European state system. Hugo Grotius (1583-1645), often called the "father of international law," synthesized earlier traditions (Roman law, canon law, scholastic thought) into a systematic framework for the law of nations. His *De Jure Belli ac Pacis* (1625) addressed when war is justified (jus ad bellum) and how it should be conducted (jus in bello). The Peace of Westphalia (1648) established the principle of territorial sovereignty that underlies the modern international system: each state has exclusive authority over its territory and is legally equal to all other states.

**The 19th century: positivism and colonialism.** 19th-century international law was dominated by legal positivism -- the view that law is created by the will of states, not derived from natural law or divine authority. This period saw the development of rules on diplomacy, neutrality, maritime law, and the laws of war. It also saw international law used to justify colonialism: European states classified non-European societies as "uncivilized" and therefore not entitled to the full protections of international law. The Berlin Conference (1884-85) divided Africa among European powers using legal instruments that excluded the affected populations.

**The Hague and Geneva traditions.** Two parallel traditions developed the law of armed conflict. The Hague Conventions (1899, 1907) addressed the methods and means of warfare (what weapons may be used, how combatants must behave). The Geneva Conventions (1864, 1906, 1929, 1949) addressed the protection of victims of war (wounded soldiers, prisoners of war, civilians). Together they form the modern law of armed conflict (international humanitarian law). The Additional Protocols of 1977 expanded protections for civilians and combatants in both international and non-international conflicts.

**The Nuremberg and Tokyo trials.** After World War II, the victorious Allies prosecuted Nazi and Japanese leaders for crimes against peace (waging aggressive war), war crimes, and crimes against humanity. The defense argued that these were ex post facto laws -- acts that were not criminal when committed. The tribunal rejected this: the principles underlying the charges (that aggressive war is illegal, that individuals can be held criminally responsible under international law) had crystallized into customary international law before the war. The Nuremberg Principles were later affirmed by the UN General Assembly and formed the basis for the International Criminal Court.

**The post-war treaty explosion.** Since 1945, the number of multilateral treaties has grown enormously: human rights treaties (UDHR, ICCPR, ICESCR, CERD, CEDAW, CAT, CRC, CRPD), environmental treaties (UNFCCC, CBD, Montreal Protocol, CITES), trade treaties (GATT/WTO), law of the sea (UNCLOS, 1982), and many others. This "treaty explosion" has greatly expanded the scope of international law but also raised concerns about fragmentation (conflicting treaty regimes), complexity (states face overlapping obligations), and compliance capacity (developing states struggle to implement their commitments).

**The International Criminal Court and individual accountability.** The ad hoc tribunals for the former Yugoslavia (ICTY, 1993) and Rwanda (ICTR, 1994) demonstrated that international criminal prosecution was feasible. The Rome Statute (1998) created the permanent ICC. The ICC has been both praised (for ending impunity for mass atrocities) and criticized (for selective prosecution, for targeting African states, for limited effectiveness). Its existence reflects a fundamental shift in international law: from a system governing only state-to-state relations to one that also holds individuals accountable for international crimes.


Decolonial scholars argue that the study of government must account for the ways in which colonialism shaped -- and continues to shape -- political institutions worldwide. The modern state system, with its assumptions about territorial sovereignty, centralized authority, and the separation of domestic and international politics, was imposed on much of the world through colonialism. Post-colonial states inherited boundaries drawn by colonial powers, administrative structures designed for extraction, and legal systems that often contradicted local governance traditions.

Walter Mignolo and other decolonial theorists argue that the very concepts used to analyze government -- sovereignty, democracy, rights, the rule of law -- carry the imprint of European intellectual history and may not capture the full range of political experience. This does not mean these concepts should be abandoned, but that they should be understood as products of particular historical circumstances rather than as universal truths. The challenge is to develop analytical frameworks that can accommodate both Western and non-Western governance traditions without privileging one over the other.

The practical implications of decolonial analysis are significant. Constitutional design in post-conflict societies increasingly incorporates indigenous governance traditions alongside Western institutional models. International organizations are grappling with how to promote governance standards without imposing Western templates. And the rise of China as an alternative model of development governance challenges the assumption that Western institutional arrangements are universally applicable. The study of government in the 21st century must be genuinely comparative, attending to the full diversity of institutional arrangements that human societies have developed.

**Connections across disciplines.** The study of government connects to philosophy, economics, sociology, history, and psychology. Political philosophy provides the normative frameworks -- justice, equality, liberty, democracy -- that justify and critique governmental arrangements. Economics illuminates the incentives that shape political behavior and the tradeoffs involved in policy choices. Sociology reveals how social structures -- class, race, gender, religion -- shape political participation and institutional design. History shows how present arrangements emerged from particular contingencies and conflicts.

The connection between government and economics is especially important. Different governmental systems create different incentive structures for economic activity. Market economies require legal frameworks for property rights, contract enforcement, and corporate governance that only government can provide. Planned economies require governmental capacity to allocate resources, set production targets, and manage distribution. Mixed economies, which describe most contemporary systems, require government to balance market efficiency with social protection, a balance that is constantly renegotiated through the political process.

The relationship between government and technology is increasingly important. Digital technologies create new possibilities for government surveillance, citizen participation, service delivery, and transparency. At the same time, they create new risks to privacy, security, and democratic deliberation. The governance of artificial intelligence, data privacy, and cybersecurity are emerging policy domains where governmental institutions must adapt to technological change. The speed of technological development challenges traditional governmental processes, which are designed for deliberation rather than rapid response.


**The historical development of international law.** International law has evolved from informal customs governing relations between political communities to a complex body of rules, institutions, and processes that regulate interactions among states, international organizations, and other actors. Early examples of international legal principles can be found in ancient treaties between Mesopotamian city-states, Egyptian-Hittite relations, and the practices of Greek city-states regarding diplomacy, asylum, and the treatment of envoys.

The modern system of international law is usually traced to the Peace of Westphalia (1648), which ended the Thirty Years' War and established the principle of state sovereignty -- the idea that each state has exclusive authority over its territory and domestic affairs. This principle provided the foundation for a legal system based on the coexistence of independent sovereign states. Hugo Grotius, often called the father of international law, developed systematic legal frameworks for war, diplomacy, and maritime law in the early 17th century.

The 19th century saw the development of more formal international legal institutions and processes. The Congress of Vienna (1815) established rules for diplomatic relations. The Hague Conferences (1899, 1907) codified the laws of war. The Alabama Claims arbitration (1872), in which Britain paid compensation to the United States for damage caused by a British-built Confederate warship, demonstrated that international disputes could be resolved through legal processes rather than force.

The 20th century transformed international law in fundamental ways. The creation of the League of Nations and then the United Nations established institutional frameworks for maintaining international peace and security. The Nuremberg and Tokyo tribunals established the principle that individuals could be held criminally responsible for violations of international law. The decolonization movement produced new states that demanded equal treatment under international law and challenged the Eurocentric assumptions that had shaped the discipline. The development of international human rights law, international environmental law, and international criminal law expanded the scope of international law far beyond its traditional focus on relations between states.

The 21st century presents new challenges for international law. Cyber warfare raises questions about how the law of armed conflict applies to digital attacks. Artificial intelligence creates challenges for legal frameworks designed for human decision-makers. Climate change requires international legal responses to a problem that transcends national borders. Space exploration raises questions about the governance of activities beyond Earth. These emerging issues will shape the future development of international law, as the discipline continues to adapt to new circumstances while maintaining its foundational principles.


**Contemporary challenges in treaty-making.** Treaty-making faces several contemporary challenges. The growing complexity of international issues makes it harder to negotiate comprehensive agreements. The increasing number of state and non-state actors with interests in international negotiations makes consensus more difficult. And the rise of populist nationalism in many countries has made governments more reluctant to accept international obligations that might constrain their domestic policy choices.

The failure of the Doha Round of World Trade Organization negotiations, which began in 2001 and was never formally concluded, illustrates these challenges. The negotiations aimed to liberalize trade in ways that would benefit developing countries, but disagreements between developed and developing countries on issues like agricultural subsidies, intellectual property, and special and differential treatment proved impossible to resolve. The failure of the Doha Round has led to a shift toward bilateral and regional trade agreements, which are easier to negotiate but may disadvantage countries that are not party to them.

Climate change negotiations illustrate both the difficulty and the possibility of treaty-making in the contemporary era. The Kyoto Protocol (1997) established binding emissions targets for developed countries, but the United States refused to ratify it and developing countries like China and India were exempt from targets. The Paris Agreement (2015) took a different approach, establishing a framework for nationally determined contributions that allows each country to set its own targets. This more flexible approach achieved near-universal participation but has been criticized for lacking enforcement mechanisms. The Paris Agreement demonstrates that treaty-making can adapt to contemporary challenges, but also that the resulting agreements may reflect the lowest common denominator of international consensus.
