# Electoral systems

## Intuition [Beginner]

An electoral system is the set of rules that determines how votes are translated into seats in a legislature. The same voters, with the same preferences, can produce very different results depending on which rules are used.

Imagine an election where 40% of voters support Party A, 35% support Party B, and 25% support Party C. Under one set of rules, Party A might win every seat. Under another, the three parties might split seats in proportion to their vote shares. Neither result is "wrong" -- they follow from different choices about what an election should achieve.

The basic choices are:

- **Winner-take-all**: the candidate with the most votes in a district wins the single seat. Also called plurality or first-past-the-post.
- **Proportional representation**: parties receive seats in proportion to their share of the vote. Many seats are allocated from each district or nationwide.
- **Mixed systems**: some combination of the two, with some seats filled by winner-take-all and others by proportional allocation.
- **Ranked-choice systems**: voters rank candidates in order of preference rather than choosing just one. Lower-ranked candidates are eliminated and votes are redistributed until someone has a majority.

No electoral system is neutral. Each one rewards certain strategies, favors certain kinds of parties, and disadvantages others. The choice of system is itself a political decision.



**Why do electoral systems matter?** Electoral systems matter because they determine how your vote is translated into political representation. The same distribution of votes can produce dramatically different outcomes depending on the electoral system used. A party that receives 40% of the vote might win a majority of seats under one system and a minority under another. This means that the choice of electoral system is not just a technical matter but a deeply political one that affects who holds power and whose interests are represented.

Understanding electoral systems helps you evaluate the fairness of your own political system and think critically about proposals for reform. If you believe that every vote should count equally, you might favor proportional representation. If you believe that each area should have its own representative, you might favor single-member districts. If you believe that governments should be able to act decisively, you might favor systems that tend to produce single-party majorities. These are legitimate disagreements, and understanding the tradeoffs involved in different electoral systems helps you participate in these debates more effectively.

Electoral systems also shape the incentives that politicians face. Systems that require politicians to appeal to a broad range of voters encourage moderation and compromise. Systems that reward appeals to a narrow base encourage polarization and extremism. Systems that are perceived as fair and legitimate encourage participation and trust in government. Systems that are perceived as rigged or unresponsive encourage disengagement and cynicism. The design of electoral systems thus has consequences far beyond the mechanical translation of votes into seats.

### How the same votes produce different results

Assume a country with 5 districts, each with 100 voters:

| District | Party A | Party B | Party C | FPTP winner | PR seat allocation (5 seats total) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 42 | 35 | 23 | Party A | -- |
| 2 | 40 | 38 | 22 | Party A | -- |
| 3 | 38 | 37 | 25 | Party A | -- |
| 4 | 35 | 40 | 25 | Party B | -- |
| 5 | 45 | 30 | 25 | Party A | -- |
| **Totals** | **200** | **180** | **120** | **A: 4 seats, B: 1 seat** | **A: 2, B: 2, C: 1** |

Under FPTP, Party A wins 4 of 5 seats with 40% of the vote. Under PR, the three parties split seats proportionally: A gets 2, B gets 2, C gets 1. Neither system is wrong -- they measure different things.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider how Germany's mixed-member proportional system works in practice.

Each voter gets two ballots:

1. **First vote (constituency ballot)**: Choose a candidate for your single-member district. Winner takes the seat, just like FPTP.
2. **Second vote (party ballot)**: Choose a party. This determines the overall proportion of seats each party receives in the Bundestag.

The total number of seats a party receives is determined by the second vote. If a party earns 25% of the party vote, it gets roughly 25% of the total seats. The constituency winners from the first vote are counted first. Any remaining seats are filled from party lists to bring the total up to the proportional share.

This means constituency wins are guaranteed (each district has a direct representative), but the overall composition of parliament reflects the national party vote. A party that wins few constituencies but gets 10% of the party vote still enters parliament with roughly 10% of the seats.

Germany also has a 5% threshold: a party must win at least 5% of the national party vote (or 3 constituency seats) to enter parliament. This prevents fragmentation while still allowing smaller parties representation.

Compare this with the United Kingdom, which uses pure FPTP. In 2015, the UK Independence Party (UKIP) received 12.6% of the national vote but won only 1 of 650 seats. Under Germany's MMP system, UKIP would have received roughly 80 seats. The difference is entirely a product of the electoral rules.


### Electoral systems in action: how different systems produce different results

**The 2000 US presidential election.** George W. Bush won the presidency despite losing the popular vote to Al Gore by over 500,000 votes. This occurred because the Electoral College system awards votes by state, and Bush won enough states (particularly Florida, by a margin of just 537 votes) to secure a majority of electoral votes.

**New Zealand electoral reform (1993).** New Zealand voted in a referendum to replace its first-past-the-post electoral system with mixed-member proportional representation (MMP). The reform was driven by growing dissatisfaction with a system that frequently produced wrong winners and underrepresented smaller parties. Since adopting MMP, New Zealand has had coalition governments and more proportional representation.

**The 2014 Indian general election.** India first-past-the-post system produced a decisive victory for the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP), which won 282 of 543 seats with 31% of the popular vote. This illustrates how FPTP can amplify small differences in vote share into large majorities in seats.

**The Dutch general election (2021).** The Netherlands uses a highly proportional system with a single nationwide constituency and no electoral threshold. In 2021, 17 parties won seats in the 150-member House of Representatives. The largest party won just 34 seats (22.7% of the vote). This extreme proportionality ensures virtually all perspectives are represented but makes coalition formation complex.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

The ballot is not discarded. It is transferred to the voter's second choice. This process continues -- the candidate with the fewest first-choice votes is eliminated, and those ballots are redistributed to the remaining candidates based on voter rankings -- until one candidate has a majority (more than 50%) of the active ballots. The result is that the winning candidate has majority support when all preferences are considered, not just a plurality of first preferences. Supporters of IRV argue this produces more legitimate winners; critics argue it is more complex and can produce counterintuitive results.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

An **electoral system** is the set of rules governing how votes are cast, counted, and translated into seats. The key variables are:

1. **Ballot structure**: whether voters choose candidates (candidate-centered) or parties (party-centered), and whether they express a single preference or rank multiple options.

2. **District magnitude**: the number of seats allocated per electoral district. This is the single most important variable. Single-member districts (magnitude 1) produce majoritarian outcomes; larger districts enable proportionality.

3. **Electoral formula**: the mathematical rule for converting votes into seats.

| Formula | Description | District magnitude needed | Disproportionality |
|---|---|---|---|
| Plurality | Most votes wins | Single-member | High |
| Majority (runoff/IRV) | Winner needs >50% | Single-member | Moderate to high |
| D'Hondt (PR) | Divisors: 1, 2, 3, 4... allocate seats sequentially | Multi-member | Low to moderate (favors larger parties) |
| Sainte-Lague (PR) | Divisors: 1, 3, 5, 7... | Multi-member | Low (more neutral) |
| Largest remainder (PR) | Seats = votes / quota; remainders awarded to highest remainders | Multi-member | Low |

4. **Thresholds**: minimum vote share required to receive seats. Common thresholds: 5% (Germany, Poland, Turkey), 4% (Sweden), 3% (Greece), none (Netherlands, South Africa). Higher thresholds reduce fragmentation; lower thresholds increase representativeness.

5. **Assembly size**: the total number of seats. Larger assemblies can be more proportional because each seat represents a smaller share of the total.

**Duverger's law** (Duverger, 1954): Single-member plurality systems tend to produce two-party competition. The logic is psychological: voters avoid "wasting" their vote on a third party unlikely to win, and strategic politicians avoid joining or creating third parties. This is a tendency, not an iron law. India and Canada use FPTP but have multiparty systems, because regional parties can win pluralities in specific districts even without national support.


The comparative study of governance systems reveals that familiar arrangements are not universal. Different societies have developed different institutional solutions to the fundamental problems of political organization: how to make collective decisions, how to constrain power, how to represent diverse interests, and how to hold leaders accountable. Understanding these differences is essential for thinking about governance without assuming that any single country's approach is the default.

This comparative perspective is especially important because the academic study of government has historically been dominated by Western European and North American models. The assumption that familiar institutional arrangements represent the "normal" form of governance is itself a product of particular historical circumstances, including colonial expansion, economic dominance, and the global influence of American political science after World War II. When we look beyond these familiar models, we find that governance takes many forms, and that the range of institutional possibilities is much wider than any single country's experience suggests.

Indigenous governance systems around the world developed sophisticated institutional arrangements that often embodied principles -- distributed authority, accountability mechanisms, consensus-based decision-making -- that Western political theory later claimed as its own inventions. The Iroquois Confederacy practiced a form of federalism with checks and balances centuries before the US Constitution. The Maori developed systems of collective resource management (kaitiakitanga) that integrated environmental governance with political authority. Many sub-Saharan African societies practiced forms of accountable kingship where rulers could be removed by councils of elders. These traditions deserve attention not as curiosities but as genuine contributions to the global inventory of governance design.

**Competing perspectives on governance.** Scholars and citizens disagree about fundamental questions of governance. Should government be large or small? Should it prioritize individual liberty or collective welfare? Should it be democratic or expert-led? These disagreements are not merely matters of preference; they reflect different theoretical frameworks and different interpretations of evidence.

Libertarians argue that government should be minimal, limited to protecting individual rights and enforcing contracts. They contend that free markets, voluntary cooperation, and civil society can solve most collective problems without government intervention. Social democrats argue that government should play an active role in ensuring social welfare, reducing inequality, and providing public goods. They contend that unfettered markets produce unacceptable levels of inequality and social harm. Communitarians argue that government should promote shared values and community bonds, contending that individualistic approaches undermine the social fabric.

These debates are not abstract. They shape practical policy questions: Should health care be provided by government or by markets? Should education be publicly funded or privately chosen? Should environmental protection rely on regulation or market mechanisms? Should social welfare programs be universal or targeted? Different countries answer these questions differently, and the comparative study of government provides evidence about the consequences of different choices. No single approach has proven universally superior, suggesting that effective governance requires pragmatic adaptation to specific circumstances rather than rigid adherence to ideological principles.


**The mathematics of electoral systems.** Electoral systems can be analyzed using mathematical concepts that reveal important properties. The concept of proportionality measures how closely the share of seats won by each party reflects its share of votes. The Gallagher Index (also called the Least Squares Index) quantifies the disproportionality of an electoral result: a score of 0 indicates perfect proportionality, while higher scores indicate greater disproportionality. First-past-the-post systems typically produce Gallagher Index scores of 10-20, while proportional systems typically score below 5.

The concept of the effective number of parties, developed by Markku Laakso and Rein Taagepera, measures how many parties are meaningfully represented in a legislature, weighted by their seat shares. A system with four equally-sized parties has an effective number of 4, while a system with one party holding 90% of seats and nine parties holding 1% each has an effective number close to 1.9. Duverger's law predicts that plurality systems tend to produce two-party systems (effective number close to 2) while proportional systems tend to produce multiparty systems (effective number of 3-8 depending on the specific system).

The concept of the wasted vote describes votes that do not contribute to electing a candidate. In first-past-the-post systems, all votes for losing candidates are wasted, as are votes for the winning candidate beyond the plurality threshold. This means that in a typical FPTP election, a majority of votes may be wasted. Proportional systems waste fewer votes because a higher proportion of votes contribute to electing representatives. Understanding these mathematical properties helps explain why electoral system design matters for representation and governance.

The concept of the electoral threshold sets a minimum share of votes that a party must receive to win seats in a proportional system. The natural threshold is the implicit minimum created by the district magnitude (the number of seats per district). Legal thresholds, typically set at 3-5%, are designed to prevent excessive fragmentation while maintaining proportionality. The German Bundestag uses a 5% threshold, which has prevented small extremist parties from gaining representation while allowing five or six meaningful parties to compete. Turkey 10% threshold, by contrast, has been criticized for being too high, effectively disenfranchising voters who support smaller parties.
## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

**Proportionality indices.** Political scientists measure how closely an electoral system translates votes into seats using disproportionality indices. The most common is the **Gallagher Index** (Least Squares Index), which calculates the square root of half the sum of squared differences between vote shares and seat shares.

| Country (recent election) | Gallagher Index | System |
|---|---|---|
| Netherlands | ~1.0 | PR (no threshold) |
| Germany | ~2.0 | MMP |
| Ireland | ~3.0 | STV |
| France | ~10.0 | Two-round |
| UK | ~12.0 | FPTP |
| US | ~15.0+ | FPTP (plus Electoral College for president) |

Lower values indicate more proportional results. Values below 2 are considered highly proportional; values above 10 indicate significant disproportionality.

**Mechanical vs. psychological effects.** Electoral systems shape party systems through two channels (Duverger, 1954):

- **Mechanical effects**: the direct translation of votes into seats. Under FPTP, a party with 10% of the vote receives roughly 0% of the seats. Under PR, it receives roughly 10%.
- **Psychological effects**: voters and politicians adapt their behavior to the system. Voters desert parties they perceive as non-viable. Politicians join larger parties or form pre-election alliances. Over time, these strategic adaptations reinforce the mechanical effects.

**Gerrymandering**: the practice of drawing electoral district boundaries to favor a particular party or group. FPTP systems are vulnerable to gerrymandering because the distribution of votes across districts determines who wins. PR systems are largely immune because seats are allocated proportionally regardless of district boundaries.

| Feature | FPTP systems | PR systems |
|---|---|---|
| Gerrymandering possible? | Yes, critically important | No (or minimal impact) |
| Who draws boundaries? | Legislature, commission, or court (varies) | Less relevant |
| Examples of reform | US: independent commissions in some states; UK: Boundary Commissions | Less applicable |

**Exercise 1.** New Zealand switched from FPTP to MMP in 1996. Before the switch, parliament was typically dominated by one or two parties. After the switch, six or more parties regularly entered parliament. Explain this outcome using Duverger's framework.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

Under FPTP, Duverger's psychological effect was strong: voters deserted smaller parties (Social Credit, Values Party, Alliance) because they could not win pluralities in single-member districts, even though these parties had significant national support. The mechanical effect then confirmed this: even when smaller parties won 10-20% of the vote, they won few or no seats. After switching to MMP, the mechanical effect changed: a party winning 5% of the party vote was guaranteed proportional seats. The psychological effect followed: voters felt free to support smaller parties, and new parties formed. The result was a transition from a two-party-dominant system to a multiparty system with regular coalition governments.

**Exercise 2.** Some countries use compulsory voting (Australia, Belgium, Brazil). How might compulsory voting interact with the choice of electoral system?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

Compulsory voting reduces the impact of turnout differentials between groups. Under voluntary voting with FPTP, parties can win by mobilizing their base rather than persuading the center, because many voters simply stay home. Compulsory voting forces parties to compete for the entire electorate, which can moderate political discourse. Under PR, compulsory voting ensures that the full spectrum of public opinion is represented in the seat allocation, rather than just the preferences of the most motivated voters. However, compulsory voting can also increase the number of informal (spoiled) ballots from disengaged voters and raises questions about whether forcing participation is compatible with democratic freedom.

Governance in the Global South presents distinctive challenges and innovations that are often underrepresented in political science textbooks. Post-colonial states inherited institutional frameworks designed for extraction and control rather than democratic participation, and many have struggled to adapt these frameworks to serve their populations. However, this experience has also produced innovations: India's constitutional protections for disadvantaged groups, South Africa's constitutional enforcement of socio-economic rights, Botswana's integration of traditional governance with democratic institutions, and Rwanda's community-based justice and reconciliation processes.

The experience of the Global South also challenges some Western assumptions about governance. The assumption that economic development requires democratic institutions has been contradicted by the rapid growth of authoritarian states like China and Singapore. The assumption that strong central government is necessary for effective governance has been challenged by the success of decentralized systems in some developing countries. The assumption that Western-style political parties are the natural mechanism for aggregating interests has been challenged by the persistence of ethnic, religious, and patron-client networks as primary political organizations in many countries. These challenges do not invalidate Western governance models but suggest that they are not the only viable approach.


**Electoral systems and their consequences.** The choice of electoral system is one of the most consequential institutional decisions a democracy makes. Electoral systems determine how votes are translated into seats, shaping party systems, representation patterns, governance outcomes, and public trust in democratic institutions. The two main families of electoral systems are majoritarian (or plurality) systems and proportional representation systems, each with many variants.

First-past-the-post (FPTP) systems, used in the United States, United Kingdom, India, and many former British colonies, award seats to the candidate with the most votes in each district. This tends to produce two-party systems, single-party governments, and strong constituency representation, but can produce "wrong winners" (a party winning the most seats despite losing the popular vote) and leaves small parties underrepresented. Canada has experienced this phenomenon multiple times, with parties winning parliamentary majorities despite receiving less than 40% of the popular vote.

Proportional representation (PR) systems, used in most of continental Europe, aim to ensure that a party's share of seats reflects its share of votes. The Netherlands uses a single nationwide district with an extremely proportional system, resulting in a multiparty parliament where no single party has ever held a majority. Germany's mixed-member proportional system combines single-member districts with party lists, achieving both local representation and proportionality. South Africa uses a closed-list PR system that has ensured representation for multiple parties since the end of apartheid.

Other systems include the alternative vote (used for Australia's House of Representatives), the single transferable vote (used in Ireland and for Northern Ireland's Assembly), and the two-round system (used for French presidential elections). Each system reflects tradeoffs between proportionality, local accountability, governability, and simplicity. Electoral system reform is a live issue in many democracies, with debates about whether changing the system would improve representation, reduce polarization, or increase public trust.


**Voter behavior and political psychology.** Understanding electoral systems requires understanding how voters make decisions. Political science has identified several factors that influence voter behavior: party identification (a long-term psychological attachment to a particular party), issue voting (choosing based on policy positions), retrospective voting (evaluating the incumbent's performance), candidate characteristics (personality, appearance, perceived competence), and social identity (voting based on group memberships like ethnicity, religion, or class).

The Michigan model of voter behavior, developed in the 1950s, argued that party identification is the primary driver of voting behavior, acting as a "perceptual screen" through which voters evaluate candidates and issues. The rational choice model, developed by Anthony Downs, argues that voters rationally choose the party whose platform is closest to their own preferences. The sociological model, associated with Seymour Martin Lipset and Stein Rokkan, argues that social cleavages -- class, religion, ethnicity, region -- determine voting behavior. Each model captures part of the truth, and contemporary research recognizes that voting behavior is influenced by a combination of factors.

The psychology of voting also includes important biases and heuristics. Voters use cognitive shortcuts to simplify complex political decisions: they rely on party labels, endorsements, and media narratives rather than conducting detailed policy analysis. Confirmation bias leads voters to seek information that confirms their existing beliefs. The availability heuristic leads voters to overweight recent and vivid events. These psychological factors mean that electoral outcomes do not always reflect the informed preferences that democratic theory assumes.

Comparative research reveals significant differences in voter behavior across countries. In countries with strong party systems and high levels of party identification (like the UK and Sweden), voters tend to be loyal to their parties over time. In countries with weaker party systems (like Brazil and Italy), voters are more likely to switch parties between elections. In countries where ethnic or religious identities are politically salient (like India and Nigeria), voting often follows identity lines. Understanding these differences is important for evaluating how well electoral systems translate voter preferences into representative outcomes.
## Political theory [Master]

**The fairness question: what should an electoral system achieve?**

Electoral system design rests on competing values that cannot all be maximized simultaneously:

- **Proportionality**: seats should reflect vote shares. Favors PR.
- **Governability**: the system should produce stable, effective governments. Favors FPTP or high thresholds.
- **Accountability**: voters should be able to identify and sanction responsible leaders. Favors systems with clear constituency links.
- **Representation of diversity**: the legislature should reflect the demographic and ideological diversity of the electorate. Favors PR with low thresholds.
- **Simplicity**: voters should understand how their vote translates into representation. Favors FPTP.
- **Local representation**: voters should have a identifiable representative for their area. Favors single-member districts.

Lijphart (1999) argued that proportionality and governability are not actually in tension: PR systems with coalition governments produce policy that is more representative and, empirically, no less stable than FPTP single-party governments. His comparative data showed that consensus democracies (PR systems) outperformed majoritarian democracies (FPTP systems) on measures of democratic quality, economic performance, and social welfare, though this finding is contested.

**Gerrymandering and the problem of neutral districting.** In FPTP systems, the drawing of district boundaries is itself a political act. The question of how to draw districts "fairly" has generated extensive theoretical and mathematical work:

- **Partisan fairness**: the seat-vote curve should be symmetric -- both parties should receive the same share of seats for the same share of votes.
- **Competitiveness**: districts should be drawn to create competitive races rather than safe seats.
- **Community of interest**: districts should group voters with shared interests or identities.
- **Compactness**: districts should be geographically compact (the "ugly district" test).
- **Racial fairness**: in the US, the Voting Rights Act requires districts that give minority voters a chance to elect candidates of their choice.

These criteria can conflict. A competitively drawn map may split communities of interest. A racially fair map may produce irregular shapes. Mathematicians (Duchin, 2018) have developed ensemble methods that generate thousands of possible maps to determine whether a proposed map is an outlier relative to what neutral map-drawing would produce.

**The paradox of electoral reform.** The actors who control electoral system design (incumbent legislators) are precisely the actors who benefit from the existing system. Taagepera and Shugart (1989) observed that electoral reform is rare because it requires the winners of the current game to change the rules in ways that would reduce their advantage. When reform does occur, it is usually driven by external pressure (public demand, judicial rulings, crisis) rather than voluntary action by incumbents. Japan (1994), New Zealand (1996), and Italy (multiple reforms) all reformed their electoral systems after political crises.

**Electoral systems and polarization.** The relationship between electoral systems and political polarization is debated. Some scholars argue that FPTP contributes to polarization because candidates in safe districts appeal to their party base rather than the median voter. Others argue that PR contributes to polarization because extremist parties can enter parliament and pull the discourse toward extremes. The empirical evidence is mixed, suggesting that electoral systems interact with other factors (party rules, media environment, social cleavages) to produce polarization.


Indigenous governance systems offer important perspectives on institutional design. Unlike the European state model, which organizes authority through territorial control and bureaucratic hierarchy, many indigenous systems organize authority through kinship, reciprocity, and relationship to land. The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy's Great Law of Peace established governance principles including participatory decision-making, recall mechanisms for leaders, and the requirement that decisions consider their impact on seven future generations. The Maori developed systems where authority (mana) was connected to responsibility (kaitiakitanga), and where leaders who failed to fulfill their obligations could lose their legitimacy.

Aboriginal Australian governance systems, among the oldest continuous governance traditions on Earth, organized authority through songlines, kinship networks, and elder councils. Decision-making was typically consensual, with the emphasis on maintaining social harmony and ecological balance rather than on majority rule. These systems governed complex societies for tens of thousands of years, demonstrating that sophisticated governance does not require the institutional forms that European political theory considers essential.

The incorporation of indigenous governance principles into modern constitutional frameworks is an ongoing process. New Zealand's Waitangi Tribunal interprets the Treaty of Waitangi in light of both Maori and Crown understandings. Canada's recognition of Aboriginal self-government in the Constitution Act, 1982 has led to negotiated self-government agreements. Bolivia's 2009 Constitution establishes a plurinational state that recognizes indigenous justice systems alongside the national legal system. These developments suggest that the future of governance may involve greater integration of indigenous and Western institutional traditions.


**Gerrymandering and electoral manipulation.** The practice of manipulating electoral district boundaries for political advantage -- known as gerrymandering after Massachusetts Governor Elbridge Gerry (1812) -- is a persistent challenge for electoral democracies. Gerrymandering can be used to concentrate opposition voters in a few districts (packing) or to spread opposition voters thinly across many districts (cracking), both of which can produce outcomes that do not reflect the overall distribution of voter preferences.

The United States has a particularly severe gerrymandering problem because redistricting is typically controlled by state legislatures, creating an inherent conflict of interest. The Supreme Court's decision in Rucho v. Common Cause (2019) declared that partisan gerrymandering is a non-justiciable political question, removing federal courts from the issue and leaving it to states to address. Some states have established independent redistricting commissions, which research suggests produce more competitive districts and more representative outcomes.

Other democracies address boundary-drawing differently. The United Kingdom uses independent boundary commissions that apply neutral criteria. Canada has an independent redistribution process that has largely eliminated gerrymandering. India's Delimitation Commission redraws constituencies based on population changes, though the process has been complicated by the decision to freeze constituency boundaries based on the 1971 census to avoid penalizing states that succeeded in family planning. These comparative examples suggest that independent boundary-drawing processes can reduce (though not eliminate) electoral manipulation.

**Electoral integrity and observation.** The integrity of elections -- the extent to which they reflect the genuine preferences of voters -- is essential for democratic legitimacy. Electoral integrity requires accurate voter rolls, fair ballot access, neutral election administration, secure voting processes, transparent vote counting, and effective mechanisms for resolving disputes. Weaknesses in any of these areas can undermine public confidence in election results.

International election observation has become an important mechanism for promoting electoral integrity. Organizations like the Organization for Security and Co-operation in Europe (OSCE), the European Union, and the Carter Center deploy observers to monitor elections around the world. Observers assess whether elections meet international standards and issue public reports that can legitimize credible elections or call attention to irregularities. However, some authoritarian regimes have learned to "manage" elections in ways that superficially meet observer standards while still producing predetermined outcomes, a practice known as "authoritarian elections."

Technology is transforming election administration in both positive and negative ways. Electronic voting machines can improve efficiency and reduce certain types of fraud but create risks of cyberattacks and software manipulation. Biometric voter registration can improve the accuracy of voter rolls but raises privacy concerns. Online voting offers convenience but presents serious security challenges. Estonia has pioneered internet voting since 2005, but most cybersecurity experts remain skeptical about its security. The challenge is to harness technology to improve elections without introducing new vulnerabilities that could undermine electoral integrity.

**Elections and democratic theory.** Electoral systems are not just technical mechanisms for converting votes into seats. They embody particular theories of representation and democracy. Majoritarian systems embody a theory of democracy as majority rule, where the government should reflect the preferences of the largest group. Proportional systems embody a theory of democracy as inclusive representation, where all significant groups should be represented in proportion to their share of the electorate.

The philosopher Jean-Jacques Rousseau argued that the general will, not the will of the majority, should govern. This distinction raises questions about whether electoral systems can ever truly capture the general will, or whether they inevitably reduce complex political preferences to simplistic choices between packaged platforms. Deliberative democrats like Jurgen Habermas argue that legitimate political decisions require not just voting but genuine public deliberation, where citizens engage in reasoned discussion about common problems. This perspective suggests that the quality of democratic life depends not only on the electoral system but on the broader public sphere in which political discussion takes place.

The relationship between elections and accountability is more complex than it first appears. Elections provide a mechanism for holding incumbents accountable by allowing voters to remove them from office. However, elections are a blunt accountability tool: voters can only choose between the options on the ballot, and they may have many reasons for their choices beyond assessing incumbent performance. Research suggests that voters are influenced by economic conditions, candidate characteristics, party identification, and single-issue concerns, making it difficult to use elections as a precise mechanism for holding officials accountable for specific actions.

The role of money in elections is a persistent concern. Campaign spending has increased dramatically in many democracies, raising questions about whether wealthy donors and interests have disproportionate influence over electoral outcomes. The United States, where campaign finance regulation is relatively permissive, has seen particularly sharp increases in campaign spending. The Supreme Court's decisions equating campaign spending with free speech have limited the ability of Congress and state legislatures to regulate campaign finance. Other democracies address this issue differently, with some countries providing public funding for campaigns, limiting private contributions, and restricting campaign advertising. The comparative study of campaign finance reveals a range of approaches to balancing the need for political competition with the risk of plutocratic influence.
## Historical context [Master]

**Pre-modern elections.** Elections are ancient -- the Catholic Church elected popes, Venice elected its Doge, the Holy Roman Empire elected its emperor -- but these were not popular elections in the modern sense. They involved small elites choosing among candidates, often through complex indirect procedures.

**The expansion of the franchise and the emergence of modern electoral systems.** As suffrage expanded in the 19th century (from propertied elites to all adult men, then to women), the question of how to translate votes into representation became urgent. The British system of single-member districts developed organically over centuries. Proportional representation was developed as a conscious reform proposal in the mid-19th century, most influentially by Thomas Hare (1859) and John Stuart Mill, who argued that FPTP left large minorities unrepresented.

**The 20th century: PR spreads, then retreats.** Many European countries adopted PR in the early 20th century as suffrage expanded and working-class parties demanded representation. Germany's Weimar Republic (1919) used pure PR with no threshold, producing a fragmented parliament that contributed to political instability -- a history that informed the 5% threshold in post-war Germany's MMP system. Some countries that adopted PR later reverted: Italy experimented with PR, then moved toward a mixed system; France shifted between PR and two-round systems several times.

**Post-war institutional design.** Germany's MMP system (1949) was specifically designed to combine the local accountability of FPTP with the proportionality of PR, while avoiding the fragmentation of Weimar. Japan adopted its single non-transferable vote system (SNTV) under American occupation, which produced factional competition within the dominant LDP rather than inter-party competition. After the Cold War, many newly democratic states in Eastern Europe adopted PR systems, influenced by West European models.

**Recent reforms.** New Zealand's switch from FPTP to MMP (1996), approved by referendum, is the most studied case of democratic electoral reform. Japan shifted from SNTV to a mixed system in 1994. The UK held a referendum on adopting IRV in 2011, which was defeated. Several US states and municipalities have adopted ranked-choice voting since 2000 (Maine, Alaska, New York City, San Francisco). The trend in established democracies has been toward systems that increase proportionality or voter choice, though reform remains politically difficult.


Decolonial scholars argue that the study of government must account for the ways in which colonialism shaped -- and continues to shape -- political institutions worldwide. The modern state system, with its assumptions about territorial sovereignty, centralized authority, and the separation of domestic and international politics, was imposed on much of the world through colonialism. Post-colonial states inherited boundaries drawn by colonial powers, administrative structures designed for extraction, and legal systems that often contradicted local governance traditions.

Walter Mignolo and other decolonial theorists argue that the very concepts used to analyze government -- sovereignty, democracy, rights, the rule of law -- carry the imprint of European intellectual history and may not capture the full range of political experience. This does not mean these concepts should be abandoned, but that they should be understood as products of particular historical circumstances rather than as universal truths. The challenge is to develop analytical frameworks that can accommodate both Western and non-Western governance traditions without privileging one over the other.

The practical implications of decolonial analysis are significant. Constitutional design in post-conflict societies increasingly incorporates indigenous governance traditions alongside Western institutional models. International organizations are grappling with how to promote governance standards without imposing Western templates. And the rise of China as an alternative model of development governance challenges the assumption that Western institutional arrangements are universally applicable. The study of government in the 21st century must be genuinely comparative, attending to the full diversity of institutional arrangements that human societies have developed.

**Connections across disciplines.** The study of government connects to philosophy, economics, sociology, history, and psychology. Political philosophy provides the normative frameworks -- justice, equality, liberty, democracy -- that justify and critique governmental arrangements. Economics illuminates the incentives that shape political behavior and the tradeoffs involved in policy choices. Sociology reveals how social structures -- class, race, gender, religion -- shape political participation and institutional design. History shows how present arrangements emerged from particular contingencies and conflicts.

The connection between government and economics is especially important. Different governmental systems create different incentive structures for economic activity. Market economies require legal frameworks for property rights, contract enforcement, and corporate governance that only government can provide. Planned economies require governmental capacity to allocate resources, set production targets, and manage distribution. Mixed economies, which describe most contemporary systems, require government to balance market efficiency with social protection, a balance that is constantly renegotiated through the political process.

The relationship between government and technology is increasingly important. Digital technologies create new possibilities for government surveillance, citizen participation, service delivery, and transparency. At the same time, they create new risks to privacy, security, and democratic deliberation. The governance of artificial intelligence, data privacy, and cybersecurity are emerging policy domains where governmental institutions must adapt to technological change. The speed of technological development challenges traditional governmental processes, which are designed for deliberation rather than rapid response.


**The history of electoral systems.** The concept of using elections to select leaders has ancient roots, but the modern practice of universal suffrage and competitive elections is relatively recent. Ancient Athens used a combination of elections and sortition (random selection) to fill public offices, with elections reserved for positions requiring special expertise (like military commanders) and sortition used for most other offices. The Athenian approach reflected a distrust of election as a mechanism that would favor the wealthy and well-connected.

The development of modern electoral systems was closely tied to the expansion of suffrage. In the 18th and 19th centuries, most "democracies" restricted voting to propertied men. Britain's Reform Acts (1832, 1867, 1884) gradually expanded the franchise, but universal male suffrage was not achieved until 1918, and women gained equal voting rights only in 1928. The United States formally guaranteed voting rights regardless of race in 1870, but Jim Crow laws effectively disenfranchised Black voters in the South until the Voting Rights Act of 1965. Women gained the right to vote nationally in 1920 with the 19th Amendment.

New Zealand was the first country to grant women the right to vote in national elections (1893). Finland was the first to grant women both the right to vote and to stand for election (1906). The expansion of suffrage continued through the 20th century, with Switzerland being one of the last Western democracies to grant women voting rights (1971 at the federal level, 1991 in the last canton). Saudi Arabia granted women the right to vote in 2015, making it one of the most recent countries to do so.

The choice of electoral systems has often been shaped by political calculation. The single-member district plurality system used in the United States and Britain was not deliberately chosen; it was simply the existing practice when these countries democratized. Proportional representation was developed in the 19th century by theorists like Thomas Hare and John Stuart Mill as a more representative alternative. Many European countries adopted PR in the early 20th century, often as a deliberate choice by established parties responding to the rise of new political forces (labor parties, socialist parties) that would have been shut out under plurality systems.

The design of electoral systems continues to evolve. Mixed-member proportional systems, which combine single-member districts with proportional party lists, have been adopted in Germany, New Zealand, and several other countries as a way to balance local representation with overall proportionality. The single transferable vote, used in Ireland and Malta, allows voters to rank candidates, producing proportional outcomes while maintaining a constituency link. These innovations demonstrate that electoral system design is an ongoing process, not a settled question.


**The future of elections.** Elections face several challenges that will shape their future development. Declining voter turnout in many democracies raises questions about the legitimacy of elected governments. The spread of misinformation through social media threatens the informed electorate that democratic theory requires. Cybersecurity threats to election infrastructure create risks of manipulation and loss of public confidence. And the growing influence of money in politics raises concerns about whether elections truly reflect the preferences of citizens.

These challenges have prompted various reform efforts. Some countries are lowering the voting age to 16 (Austria, Scotland, Argentina) to engage young people in politics. Others are adopting automatic voter registration, making election day a holiday, or expanding early voting and mail-in voting to make it easier to participate. Campaign finance reforms aim to reduce the influence of money. And efforts to combat misinformation include fact-checking initiatives, media literacy education, and regulation of political advertising on social media.

The design of electoral systems continues to evolve. Several countries have adopted or considered electoral system reforms in recent years. New Zealand voted in 1993 to switch from first-past-the-post to mixed-member proportional representation, a decision that transformed the party system. The UK held a referendum on electoral reform in 2011, though the alternative vote proposal was rejected. Canada's Trudeau government promised electoral reform but did not implement it. These experiences demonstrate that electoral system reform is possible but politically challenging, as existing parties have incentives to maintain the systems that brought them to power.

The concept of "deliberative democracy" offers an alternative vision for the future of democratic participation. Rather than relying solely on elections to aggregate preferences, deliberative democracy emphasizes informed public discussion as the foundation of legitimate political decisions. Citizens' assemblies, deliberative polls, and mini-publics bring randomly selected citizens together to learn about, discuss, and make recommendations on policy issues. These experiments have produced promising results, with participants often changing their views in response to evidence and argument. Whether deliberative innovations can be scaled up to supplement or replace traditional electoral processes remains an open question.
