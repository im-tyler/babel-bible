# Political parties and interest groups

## Intuition [Beginner]

Political parties and interest groups are both ways that people organize to influence government. The difference is straightforward: parties run candidates for office and seek to govern. Interest groups try to influence those who already hold power.

A **political party** is an organization that nominates candidates for public office, competes in elections, and (if it wins) forms or joins the government. Parties serve as intermediaries between citizens and the state: they aggregate preferences into policy platforms, simplify choices for voters, organize legislatures, and recruit political leaders.

An **interest group** (also called a pressure group, advocacy group, or lobby) is an organization that seeks to influence public policy without directly contesting elections. Interest groups may represent businesses, labor unions, professional associations, environmental causes, civil rights, religious communities, or any other shared interest.

How many parties matter in a country depends partly on the electoral system. Winner-take-all systems tend to produce two dominant parties. Proportional systems tend to produce many parties that must form coalitions to govern. Neither arrangement is inherently better; they create different dynamics.



**Why do political parties and interest groups matter?** Political parties and interest groups are the primary vehicles through which citizens organize to influence government. Parties aggregate diverse interests into coherent policy platforms and compete for the opportunity to govern. Interest groups represent specific constituencies and advocate for particular policies. Together, they form the infrastructure of democratic politics -- the connective tissue between citizens and government.

Understanding how parties and interest groups work helps you be a more effective participant in democratic politics. If you want to influence policy, you need to know which party shares your views, which interest group is working on your issue, and how to engage effectively with both. If you are concerned about the influence of money in politics, you need to understand how campaign finance works and what reforms might reduce the disproportionate influence of wealthy donors. If you are frustrated with political polarization, you need to understand the structural factors that contribute to it.

The health of a democracy depends in significant part on the health of its parties and interest groups. Strong, programmatic parties that offer clear policy choices and are internally democratic provide citizens with meaningful options at the ballot box. Diverse, active interest groups ensure that a wide range of perspectives is represented in the policy-making process. When parties become corrupt, personalistic, or unresponsive, and when interest groups become captured by wealthy interests, democratic governance suffers. Understanding these dynamics is essential for anyone who cares about the quality of democratic life.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider how a coalition government forms in a multiparty system. In Germany's 2021 election, the results were:

- SPD (Social Democrats): 25.7%
- CDU/CSU (Christian Democrats): 24.1%
- Greens: 14.8%
- FDP (Free Democrats): 11.5%
- AfD (Alternative for Germany): 10.3%
- Die Linke (The Left): 4.9%

No party had a majority. The SPD, as the largest party, led negotiations. Over several weeks, the SPD, Greens, and FDP negotiated a coalition agreement -- a detailed document specifying policy compromises each party would accept in exchange for participating in government.

Key trade-offs in the negotiation:
- The SPD wanted higher social spending and a minimum wage increase.
- The Greens wanted accelerated climate policy and a speed limit on autobahns.
- The FDP wanted fiscal discipline and no new debt.

The resulting "traffic light" coalition (SPD red, Greens green, FDP yellow) governed because these three parties together held a majority of seats. The CDU/CSU moved into opposition. The AfD and Die Linke were not considered as coalition partners by the other parties.

In a two-party system like the US, none of this negotiation is visible to voters. One party wins, the other loses, and the winner governs alone (subject to checks and balances). The coalition process reveals the specific compromises that parties make, which voters in multiparty systems can evaluate.


### Interest group influence: case studies

**The National Rifle Association (United States).** The NRA is one of the most influential interest groups in American politics, spending millions on campaign contributions, lobbying, and advertising to oppose gun control legislation. Despite polling that consistently shows majority support for measures like universal background checks, the NRA influence -- combined with structural features of the US legislative process -- has helped prevent significant federal gun legislation for decades.

**Trade unions in Scandinavia.** In Sweden, Denmark, and Norway, trade unions play a formal role in policy-making through corporatist arrangements that bring together government, employers, and labor organizations. This tripartite model has produced policies including generous social welfare benefits, active labor market programs, and high rates of union membership.

**The anti-apartheid movement.** The international campaign against South Africa apartheid system combined boycotts, divestment campaigns, diplomatic pressure, and armed resistance. Universities, cities, and companies divested from companies doing business in South Africa. Musicians and athletes refused to perform or compete in the country. This transnational advocacy network demonstrated how interest group activity across borders can contribute to political change.

**Climate advocacy organizations.** Organizations like Greenpeace, 350.org, and the Sunrise Movement have used a combination of lobbying, litigation, protest, and public education to advocate for stronger climate policies. The Paris Agreement (2015) and various national climate policies reflect, in part, the influence of climate advocacy.

<aside class="exercises">

**Exercise 1.** Why does the United States have only two competitive parties, while the Netherlands has more than a dozen?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

The main reason is the electoral system. The US uses first-past-the-post, single-member districts. Under these rules, a third party that wins 15% of the vote nationwide will likely win 0% of the seats because it does not come first in any district (Duverger's law). Voters learn this and gravitate toward one of the two parties with a chance of winning. The Netherlands uses proportional representation with a very low threshold, so a party that wins 2% of the vote receives approximately 3 seats in the 150-seat parliament. Voters can support a small party without "wasting" their vote. Institutional rules, not voter preferences alone, shape the party system.

</details>

**Exercise 2.** An environmental group pays for television advertisements supporting a candidate who favors renewable energy. Is this group a political party or an interest group?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

It depends on whether the group nominates candidates and seeks to govern. If the group only advocates for environmental policy and supports candidates who agree with its positions, it is an interest group (specifically, a single-issue advocacy group). If the group were to register as a party, nominate its own candidates for office, and attempt to win seats in the legislature, it would be a political party (like the Green parties that exist in many countries). In practice, many environmental organizations in the US are interest groups that endorse candidates, while Green parties in Germany, France, and elsewhere are full political parties that contest elections and sometimes join governing coalitions.

</details>

**Exercise 3.** In a coalition government, a junior partner agrees to support policies it disagrees with in exchange for cabinet positions. Is this democratic?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

This is a matter of ongoing debate. Supporters argue that coalition compromise is democratic because it reflects the reality that no single party won a majority -- voters distributed their support across multiple parties, and those parties must negotiate to form a government. The coalition agreement is a visible, published document that voters can evaluate. Critics argue that coalition deals can betray the specific commitments each party made to its voters, that junior partners gain disproportionate influence through the leverage of being needed for a majority, and that voters cannot easily hold any single party accountable for government performance. Both views have merit. The question illustrates the trade-off between pure majoritarian representation and consensus-based governance.

</details>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **political party** is a formally organized group that (a) articulates a policy program, (b) nominates candidates for public office, and (c) seeks to control or influence government through electoral competition. Sartori (1976) defined a party as "any political group that presents at elections, and is capable of placing through elections, candidates for public office."

An **interest group** is an organized association that seeks to influence public policy without contesting elections or seeking to govern directly.

**Party system classification** (Sartori, 1976) uses two criteria: the number of relevant parties and the degree of ideological polarization:

| Type | Number of parties | Ideological distance | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Predominant party | One party wins repeatedly | Varies | Japan 1955-1993 (LDP) |
| Two-party | Two parties alternate | Moderate | US, UK (historically) |
| Moderate pluralism | 3-5 relevant parties | Moderate | Germany, Sweden |
| Polarized pluralism | 5+ relevant parties | High (anti-system parties present) | Weimar Republic; Italy (First Republic) |

**Coalition formation theory.** In multiparty systems, coalition formation follows identifiable patterns (Laver and Schofield, 1990):

- **Minimum winning coalition**: the smallest coalition that commands a parliamentary majority (maximizes each member's share of cabinet positions).
- **Minimum connected coalition**: parties that are adjacent on the ideological spectrum form a coalition (minimizes internal policy disagreement).
- **Surplus majority coalition**: includes more parties than needed for a majority (provides stability; common in divided societies or times of crisis).
- **Grand coalition**: the two largest parties (often ideological opponents) govern together (used in national emergencies; common in Switzerland).

**Lobbying** is the organized effort to influence legislation or policy decisions. Interest groups use multiple strategies:

1. **Direct lobbying**: meeting with legislators or officials to present arguments and information.
2. **Grassroots lobbying**: mobilizing members or the public to contact representatives.
3. **Litigation**: using the courts to advance policy goals.
4. **Campaign contributions**: donating to sympathetic candidates (regulated differently across systems).
5. **Public relations**: shaping media coverage and public opinion.


The comparative study of governance systems reveals that familiar arrangements are not universal. Different societies have developed different institutional solutions to the fundamental problems of political organization: how to make collective decisions, how to constrain power, how to represent diverse interests, and how to hold leaders accountable. Understanding these differences is essential for thinking about governance without assuming that any single country's approach is the default.

This comparative perspective is especially important because the academic study of government has historically been dominated by Western European and North American models. The assumption that familiar institutional arrangements represent the "normal" form of governance is itself a product of particular historical circumstances, including colonial expansion, economic dominance, and the global influence of American political science after World War II. When we look beyond these familiar models, we find that governance takes many forms, and that the range of institutional possibilities is much wider than any single country's experience suggests.

Indigenous governance systems around the world developed sophisticated institutional arrangements that often embodied principles -- distributed authority, accountability mechanisms, consensus-based decision-making -- that Western political theory later claimed as its own inventions. The Iroquois Confederacy practiced a form of federalism with checks and balances centuries before the US Constitution. The Maori developed systems of collective resource management (kaitiakitanga) that integrated environmental governance with political authority. Many sub-Saharan African societies practiced forms of accountable kingship where rulers could be removed by councils of elders. These traditions deserve attention not as curiosities but as genuine contributions to the global inventory of governance design.

**Competing perspectives on governance.** Scholars and citizens disagree about fundamental questions of governance. Should government be large or small? Should it prioritize individual liberty or collective welfare? Should it be democratic or expert-led? These disagreements are not merely matters of preference; they reflect different theoretical frameworks and different interpretations of evidence.

Libertarians argue that government should be minimal, limited to protecting individual rights and enforcing contracts. They contend that free markets, voluntary cooperation, and civil society can solve most collective problems without government intervention. Social democrats argue that government should play an active role in ensuring social welfare, reducing inequality, and providing public goods. They contend that unfettered markets produce unacceptable levels of inequality and social harm. Communitarians argue that government should promote shared values and community bonds, contending that individualistic approaches undermine the social fabric.

These debates are not abstract. They shape practical policy questions: Should health care be provided by government or by markets? Should education be publicly funded or privately chosen? Should environmental protection rely on regulation or market mechanisms? Should social welfare programs be universal or targeted? Different countries answer these questions differently, and the comparative study of government provides evidence about the consequences of different choices. No single approach has proven universally superior, suggesting that effective governance requires pragmatic adaptation to specific circumstances rather than rigid adherence to ideological principles.


**Party models and typologies.** Political scientists have developed various typologies to categorize political parties and understand their evolution. Maurice Duverger distinguished between cadre parties (organized around a small group of notables), mass parties (with large, dues-paying memberships), and devotee parties (organized around a charismatic leader). Later scholars added catch-all parties (which reduce ideological distinctiveness to appeal to broader electorates), cartel parties (which rely on state resources rather than membership dues), and business firm parties (which operate like corporations with professional staff and centralized control).

The evolution of party models reflects broader social and political changes. Mass parties emerged with industrialization and the expansion of suffrage, as working-class organizations mobilized new voters. Catch-all parties emerged as class-based voting declined and television created new opportunities for direct communication with voters. Cartel parties emerged as states began providing public funding for political parties, reducing dependence on grassroots members. Business firm parties have emerged in some new democracies where wealthy individuals use personal resources to create parties as vehicles for their political ambitions.

The applicability of these Western-derived typologies to parties in the Global South is debated. Many parties in developing countries do not fit neatly into these categories. Ethnic parties, religious parties, personalistic parties, and military-affiliated parties are common forms that are not well captured by typologies developed from European and North American experience. The comparative study of parties requires analytical frameworks that can accommodate the full diversity of party forms found around the world.

The internal governance of political parties is an increasingly important topic. Democratic theory suggests that parties that practice internal democracy -- holding competitive leadership elections, involving members in policy development, and maintaining transparent decision-making processes -- are better able to represent their constituents and contribute to democratic governance. However, many parties are controlled by small elites or individual leaders, with limited member participation. The tension between effective party organization and internal democracy is a persistent challenge for party design.
## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

**Party functions.** Political scientists identify several functions that parties perform in democratic systems:

1. **Integration and mobilization**: bringing citizens into the political process and converting individual preferences into collective demands.
2. **Aggregation**: combining diverse interests into coherent policy platforms.
3. **Recruitment**: selecting and training candidates for public office.
4. **Organization of government**: structuring legislative behavior (party discipline, caucuses) and forming executives.
5. **Opposition**: providing scrutiny of the government and offering alternative policies.
6. **Simplification**: reducing complex policy choices to a manageable number of options for voters.

**Catch-all parties vs. ideological parties** (Kirchheimer, 1966): Some parties (especially in two-party systems) aim to attract the broadest possible electorate, moderating their positions toward the center. Others define themselves by a clear ideology or identity, accepting a smaller base in exchange for sharper differentiation.

| Type | Strategy | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Catch-all | Broad appeal; moderate positions; avoid alienating any group | US Democratic and Republican parties; CDU (Germany) |
| Ideological | Clear programmatic identity; mobilize committed supporters | Die Linke (Germany); Podemos (Spain); Tea Party faction (US) |
| Ethnic/regional | Represent a specific group or region | Scottish National Party; Parti Quebecois (Canada); BJP (India, Hindu nationalism) |
| Single-issue | Organized around one policy question | Prohibition Party (US, historical); Animal Rights parties (Netherlands, Sweden) |
| Personalist | Organized around a specific leader | Many parties in Latin America and Africa; Berlusconi's Forza Italia |

**Interest group systems: pluralism vs. corporatism.**

| Feature | Pluralism | Corporatism (neo-corporatism) |
|---|---|---|
| Number of groups | Many, competing | Few, organized by economic sector |
| Access to government | Open; groups compete for influence | Structured; designated groups negotiate with government |
| Government role | Neutral arbiter | Active participant in interest intermediation |
| Examples | US, UK, France | Sweden, Austria, Germany, Netherlands |
| Strengths | Open access; competition prevents capture | Orderly; reduces conflict; produces bargained outcomes |
| Weaknesses | Unequal resources favor wealthy groups | Excludes unorganized interests; can be rigid |

### Exercises

**Exercise 1.** India has a multiparty system but uses first-past-the-post elections. Why does Duverger's law not fully apply?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

India has many regional parties that win pluralities in specific states or districts even though they have little national presence. The Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) and Indian National Congress are the main national parties, but in many states, regional parties (All India Trinamool Congress in West Bengal, Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam in Tamil Nadu, Telangana Rashtra Samithi in Telangana) dominate. This works because India's social diversity -- linguistic, religious, caste-based -- creates distinct political cleavages in different regions. A party that represents a regional or caste-based majority in one state may win all the seats there under FPTP, even though it has no support elsewhere. Duverger's law applies at the district level (typically one or two parties compete seriously in each district), but the parties that are competitive vary across districts, producing a multiparty parliament.

</details>

**Exercise 2.** In the US, the National Rifle Association (NRA) is an interest group, not a political party. Yet it influences policy significantly. Explain the strategies an interest group like this can use in a system where it does not contest elections.

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

The NRA uses several strategies: (a) direct lobbying of legislators and executive officials; (b) campaign contributions through its political action committee (PAC), which donates to and spends independently on behalf of sympathetic candidates; (c) grassroots mobilization, alerting its members to contact their representatives when gun legislation is proposed; (d) public communications, including advertising, media appearances, and social media; (e) litigation, participating in court cases that affect gun rights; (f) electoral scoring, publishing "grades" for politicians based on their votes, which influence how gun-rights voters cast their ballots. These strategies give the NRA influence comparable to (or exceeding) that of a small political party, without the need to win elections directly. The effectiveness of such groups depends on the openness of the political system, the resources available to the group, and the intensity of its supporters' commitment.

</details>


Governance in the Global South presents distinctive challenges and innovations that are often underrepresented in political science textbooks. Post-colonial states inherited institutional frameworks designed for extraction and control rather than democratic participation, and many have struggled to adapt these frameworks to serve their populations. However, this experience has also produced innovations: India's constitutional protections for disadvantaged groups, South Africa's constitutional enforcement of socio-economic rights, Botswana's integration of traditional governance with democratic institutions, and Rwanda's community-based justice and reconciliation processes.

The experience of the Global South also challenges some Western assumptions about governance. The assumption that economic development requires democratic institutions has been contradicted by the rapid growth of authoritarian states like China and Singapore. The assumption that strong central government is necessary for effective governance has been challenged by the success of decentralized systems in some developing countries. The assumption that Western-style political parties are the natural mechanism for aggregating interests has been challenged by the persistence of ethnic, religious, and patron-client networks as primary political organizations in many countries. These challenges do not invalidate Western governance models but suggest that they are not the only viable approach.


**Comparative party systems.** Political party systems vary dramatically across democracies, and these differences shape governance in fundamental ways. The number of parties, their ideological positions, their internal organization, and their relationships with interest groups all affect how democratic politics operates. Party systems are often classified by the number of relevant parties: two-party systems (US), moderate multiparty systems with three to five parties (Germany, Sweden), and extreme multiparty systems with many parties (Israel, Italy, Brazil).

The relationship between parties and interest groups also varies. In some countries, parties have formal ties to specific interest groups. Germany's Social Democratic Party historically had close ties to trade unions, while the Christian Democratic Union had ties to churches and business associations. In the United States, parties are less ideologically disciplined and less formally connected to interest groups, which instead influence politics through lobbying and campaign contributions.

The rise of populism has transformed party systems in many democracies. Populist parties and movements challenge established parties by claiming to represent "the people" against "the elite." Left-wing populism, as seen in Latin America's pink tide and movements like Podemos in Spain, emphasizes economic redistribution and social justice. Right-wing populism, as seen in movements like the National Rally in France and the Alternative for Germany, emphasizes national identity, immigration restriction, and cultural traditionalism. Both types challenge the centrist consensus that dominated many democracies in the late 20th century.

The role of money in politics remains a central concern across democracies. Campaign finance regulation varies enormously, from relatively strict systems in Canada and the UK to the relatively permissive system in the United States, where the Supreme Court's Citizens United decision (2010) removed limits on independent political expenditures. The challenge is to balance the need for political competition with the risk that wealthy individuals and organizations can buy disproportionate influence.
## Political theory [Master]

**Why parties? The necessity of collective organization.**

Schattschneider (1942) argued that "modern democracy is unthinkable save in terms of political parties." Without parties, individual legislators would have no mechanism for organizing a governing agenda, and voters would have no shorthand for evaluating candidates. Parties reduce the complexity of democratic choice from evaluating individual candidates on dozens of issues to choosing between a small number of organized platforms. This simplification is essential for mass politics.

Aldrich (1995) offered a rational-choice explanation: parties are "endogenous institutions" created by ambitious politicians who need collective organization to achieve their goals (election, legislation, policy influence). Individual legislators form parties because coordinated action is more effective than isolated action. Voters support parties because they provide reliable information about what candidates will do in office.

**The cartel party thesis.** Katz and Mair (1995) argued that parties in established democracies have evolved into "cartel parties" that collude with each other to secure state resources (public funding, privileged ballot access, media access) and exclude new competitors. In this view, parties are no longer intermediary organizations linking civil society to the state but rather agents of the state that have captured public resources for their own survival. Evidence includes the widespread adoption of public party funding, the professionalization of party organizations, and the declining membership of mass parties.

**Interest group power: pluralism and its discontents.**

Pluralist theory (Dahl, 1961; Truman, 1951) holds that competition among interest groups produces roughly democratic outcomes: all interests have a chance to be heard, and policy reflects the balance of organized forces. No single group dominates because groups check each other.

Critiques of pluralism:

- **Olson (1965)**: collective action problems mean that large, diffuse interests (consumers, taxpayers, the environment) are underrepresented, while small, concentrated interests (industries, professional associations) are overrepresented because the costs of organizing are lower and the benefits per member are higher.
- **Schattschneider (1960)**: the "heavenly chorus" of interest groups sings with a distinctly upper-class accent. Pluralist competition is biased toward groups with economic resources, organizational capacity, and social status.
- **Lindblom (1977)**: business occupies a "privileged position" in all market-based democracies because government depends on business investment for economic performance. This structural dependence gives business influence that no amount of countervailing organization can fully offset.

**Campaign finance and democratic equality.** The question of how political campaigns should be funded involves a tension between two values:

- **Freedom of political expression**: spending money to support a candidate or cause is a form of speech that should be protected.
- **Political equality**: each citizen's influence over elections should be roughly equal; wealth should not determine political power.

Different systems resolve this tension differently. The US Supreme Court (in *Buckley v. Valeo*, 1976, and *Citizens United v. FEC*, 2010) treated spending limits as restrictions on speech, striking down many campaign finance regulations. Most other democracies impose stricter limits on campaign spending and donations, treating political equality as a legitimate reason to restrict how money enters politics. The result is that the US has far more private money in politics than comparable democracies.

**The role of media.** Media -- whether state-run, commercial, or social -- plays a critical role in mediating between parties, interest groups, and voters. Hallin and Mancini (2004) identified three models of media-political systems:

1. **Mediterranean/polarized pluralist** (Italy, Spain): media aligned with political factions; low professional autonomy; state intervention common.
2. **North Atlantic/liberal** (US, UK): commercial media; high journalistic autonomy; strong press freedom; adversarial relationship with government.
3. **North/Central European/democratic corporatist** (Germany, Sweden): coexistence of commercial and public media; strong journalistic norms; institutionalized press-state relations.

The rise of social media has disrupted all three models, creating new channels for political communication that bypass traditional gatekeepers, enabling micro-targeted advertising and rapid spread of unverified information.


Indigenous governance systems offer important perspectives on institutional design. Unlike the European state model, which organizes authority through territorial control and bureaucratic hierarchy, many indigenous systems organize authority through kinship, reciprocity, and relationship to land. The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy's Great Law of Peace established governance principles including participatory decision-making, recall mechanisms for leaders, and the requirement that decisions consider their impact on seven future generations. The Maori developed systems where authority (mana) was connected to responsibility (kaitiakitanga), and where leaders who failed to fulfill their obligations could lose their legitimacy.

Aboriginal Australian governance systems, among the oldest continuous governance traditions on Earth, organized authority through songlines, kinship networks, and elder councils. Decision-making was typically consensual, with the emphasis on maintaining social harmony and ecological balance rather than on majority rule. These systems governed complex societies for tens of thousands of years, demonstrating that sophisticated governance does not require the institutional forms that European political theory considers essential.

The incorporation of indigenous governance principles into modern constitutional frameworks is an ongoing process. New Zealand's Waitangi Tribunal interprets the Treaty of Waitangi in light of both Maori and Crown understandings. Canada's recognition of Aboriginal self-government in the Constitution Act, 1982 has led to negotiated self-government agreements. Bolivia's 2009 Constitution establishes a plurinational state that recognizes indigenous justice systems alongside the national legal system. These developments suggest that the future of governance may involve greater integration of indigenous and Western institutional traditions.


**Party systems in new and developing democracies.** The party systems of new and developing democracies often differ significantly from those of established democracies. In many African, Asian, and Latin American countries, parties are organized around ethnic, religious, or regional identities rather than ideological positions. Personalistic parties, built around a single leader rather than a program or organization, are common. Clientelist parties, which exchange material benefits for political support, persist in many contexts where ideological differentiation is limited.

India's party system illustrates the complexity of parties in developing democracies. The Congress Party dominated Indian politics for decades as a broad coalition encompassing diverse social groups. Since the 1990s, the party system has fragmented, with the Hindu nationalist Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP), regional parties like the Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam (DMK) in Tamil Nadu, and caste-based parties like the Bahujan Samaj Party competing for power. This fragmentation reflects the increasing political mobilization of previously marginalized groups and the growing importance of regional and identity-based politics.

Brazil's party system is one of the most fragmented in the world, with over 30 parties represented in Congress. This fragmentation reflects Brazil's electoral system (open-list proportional representation with large districts), federal structure, and the weakness of party discipline. The result is a system where presidents must build broad, shifting coalitions to govern, which can produce both inclusive representation and governance instability.

The quality of democracy depends in part on the quality of its political parties. Strong, programmatic parties that are internally democratic, financially transparent, and responsive to citizens provide essential mechanisms for democratic governance. Weak, personalistic, or clientelist parties undermine democratic accountability and can contribute to democratic erosion. Party reform -- including measures to strengthen party organization, reduce the influence of money, increase transparency, and encourage internal democracy -- is an important agenda for democratic improvement in both established and new democracies.

**Social movements and extra-institutional politics.** Not all political participation occurs through parties and interest groups. Social movements -- organized efforts by large numbers of people to promote or resist social change -- have been powerful forces in political life. The civil rights movement, the women's movement, the environmental movement, and the labor movement all achieved significant policy changes through extra-institutional means: protests, boycotts, strikes, civil disobedience, and public education campaigns.

Social movements often arise when institutional channels of political participation are blocked or unresponsive. The Arab Spring (2010-2012) erupted in countries where authoritarian regimes had suppressed party competition and interest group activity. The Umbrella Movement in Hong Kong (2014) emerged when China restricted democratic reforms. Black Lives Matter (2013-present) arose in response to persistent racial inequality and police violence that conventional politics had failed to address. These movements demonstrate that when institutional channels fail, people will find other ways to make their voices heard.

The digital age has transformed social movements. Social media enables rapid mobilization, decentralized organization, and global solidarity. The #MeToo movement spread virally across countries, cultures, and languages. Climate activism, led by young people like Greta Thunberg, has used digital platforms to coordinate worldwide protests. At the same time, digital activism faces challenges: online engagement can substitute for offline action (the "slacktivism" critique), social media algorithms can create echo chambers, and authoritarian governments can use digital tools for surveillance and repression.

**Parties, interest groups, and democratic theory.** The role of political parties and interest groups in democratic governance raises fundamental questions about representation, pluralism, and the common good. Pluralist theory, associated with thinkers like Robert Dahl, argues that democratic politics is properly a competition among groups representing different interests, with policy outcomes determined by the relative power and skill of competing groups. In this view, no single group dominates, and the resulting policy compromises reflect a rough approximation of the public interest.

Critics of pluralism argue that the playing field among groups is not level. Well-resourced groups -- corporations, professional associations, wealthy individuals -- have far greater capacity to influence policy than poor, marginalized, or unorganized groups. Mancur Olson's theory of collective action explains why: small, concentrated groups with high stakes in a particular policy outcome are easier to organize and motivate than large, diffuse groups with lower individual stakes. This means that policies that concentrate benefits on a few (like tax breaks for specific industries) are easier to achieve than policies that distribute benefits broadly (like environmental protection or public health measures).

The concept of "regulatory capture," developed by economists like George Stigler, describes situations where regulatory agencies come to serve the interests of the industries they are supposed to regulate rather than the public interest. Capture occurs through various mechanisms: the revolving door between industry and government, the information advantage that regulated industries have over their regulators, and the political influence that industries exercise through lobbying and campaign contributions. The 2008 financial crisis, in which regulatory agencies failed to prevent reckless behavior by financial institutions, is often cited as an example of regulatory capture.

The relationship between parties, interest groups, and inequality is increasingly important. Research by political scientists Martin Gilens and Benjamin Page found that economic elites and organized interest groups have substantially more influence over US policy outcomes than average citizens. Similar patterns have been documented in other democracies, though the extent of inequality in political influence varies across countries and policy areas. Addressing this democratic deficit -- the gap between the ideal of equal political voice and the reality of unequal influence -- is one of the central challenges facing democratic governance in the 21st century.


**The future of political participation.** Political participation is being transformed by technology, social change, and generational shifts. Young people in many countries are less likely to join political parties, vote in elections, or follow traditional news media than previous generations. However, they are more likely to participate in online activism, social movements, and issue-based campaigns. This shift from institutional to extra-institutional participation has important implications for the future of democratic governance.

The decline of traditional membership organizations -- political parties, trade unions, churches, civic associations -- has been documented across many democracies. Robert Putnam's "bowling alone" thesis argued that social capital was declining as people became less connected to community organizations. Whether this decline is as dramatic as Putnam suggested, and whether new forms of online connection compensate for the loss of face-to-face association, are matters of ongoing debate. What is clear is that the organizational landscape of democratic politics is changing, and that parties and interest groups must adapt to remain relevant.

The rise of "post-material" values -- environmental protection, human rights, gender equality, LGBTQ+ rights -- has shifted the focus of political participation in many democracies. These issues often cut across traditional left-right party lines and have generated new forms of political organization, including single-issue movements, online campaigns, and transnational advocacy networks. The climate movement, #MeToo, and Black Lives Matter represent different manifestations of this shift toward values-based political participation.

The challenge for democratic governance is to channel these diverse forms of participation into constructive engagement with the political process. When citizens feel that institutional channels -- parties, legislatures, courts -- are unresponsive to their concerns, they may turn to extra-institutional forms of action, ranging from peaceful protest to civil disobedience to political violence. Maintaining the legitimacy of democratic institutions requires that they be responsive enough to address citizens' concerns through established channels. The comparative study of political participation provides valuable evidence about how different institutional designs affect the relationship between citizens and government.
## Historical context [Master]

**The emergence of modern parties.** Political parties in the modern sense emerged in the 19th century as legislatures gained power and suffrage expanded. The earliest mass parties were socialist and labor parties (German Social Democratic Party, 1863; British Labour Party, 1900), which organized workers through unions, newspapers, and social clubs to compete for political power. Conservative and liberal parties adapted by developing their own organizational structures.

**Cleavage structures and party systems.** Lipset and Rokkan (1967) argued that Western party systems were frozen in place by the 1920s, reflecting the social cleavages of the early industrial era: owner vs. worker (class), church vs. state (religion), center vs. periphery (regional identity), and urban vs. rural. These cleavages structured party competition for decades. The "freezing hypothesis" has been modified by subsequent developments: the rise of post-materialist values (Inglehart, 1977) created new cleavages around environmentalism, gender, and cultural liberalism; immigration created a new nationalist-cosmopolitan divide; and deindustrialization weakened the class cleavage that had structured left-right politics.

**The post-war party landscape.** After World War II, Christian Democratic parties became dominant in several European countries (Germany, Italy, Netherlands), uniting religious voters across class lines. Social Democratic parties governed or co-governed in Scandinavia and the UK. The US maintained its two-party system, with the New Deal coalition (urban workers, ethnic minorities, the South) dominating the Democratic Party until the 1960s.

**Late 20th and 21st century developments.** Since the 1990s, established party systems have been disrupted by new parties of the radical right (National Rally in France, Lega in Italy, Sweden Democrats), green parties (first in Germany, then across Europe), and populist movements that challenge established parties from outside (Five Star Movement in Italy, Podemos in Spain). These developments reflect shifts in social cleavages, declining trust in established institutions, and the impact of globalization and immigration on political identities.

**The evolution of lobbying.** Organized interest representation is as old as politics, but systematic lobbying in the modern sense developed alongside representative institutions. Washington's K Street lobbying corridor, Brussels's EU lobbying community, and Westminster's consultant lobbyists all reflect the institutionalization of interest group access. The regulation of lobbying has generally lagged behind its growth, with most systems adopting transparency requirements only in recent decades.


Decolonial scholars argue that the study of government must account for the ways in which colonialism shaped -- and continues to shape -- political institutions worldwide. The modern state system, with its assumptions about territorial sovereignty, centralized authority, and the separation of domestic and international politics, was imposed on much of the world through colonialism. Post-colonial states inherited boundaries drawn by colonial powers, administrative structures designed for extraction, and legal systems that often contradicted local governance traditions.

Walter Mignolo and other decolonial theorists argue that the very concepts used to analyze government -- sovereignty, democracy, rights, the rule of law -- carry the imprint of European intellectual history and may not capture the full range of political experience. This does not mean these concepts should be abandoned, but that they should be understood as products of particular historical circumstances rather than as universal truths. The challenge is to develop analytical frameworks that can accommodate both Western and non-Western governance traditions without privileging one over the other.

The practical implications of decolonial analysis are significant. Constitutional design in post-conflict societies increasingly incorporates indigenous governance traditions alongside Western institutional models. International organizations are grappling with how to promote governance standards without imposing Western templates. And the rise of China as an alternative model of development governance challenges the assumption that Western institutional arrangements are universally applicable. The study of government in the 21st century must be genuinely comparative, attending to the full diversity of institutional arrangements that human societies have developed.

**Connections across disciplines.** The study of government connects to philosophy, economics, sociology, history, and psychology. Political philosophy provides the normative frameworks -- justice, equality, liberty, democracy -- that justify and critique governmental arrangements. Economics illuminates the incentives that shape political behavior and the tradeoffs involved in policy choices. Sociology reveals how social structures -- class, race, gender, religion -- shape political participation and institutional design. History shows how present arrangements emerged from particular contingencies and conflicts.

The connection between government and economics is especially important. Different governmental systems create different incentive structures for economic activity. Market economies require legal frameworks for property rights, contract enforcement, and corporate governance that only government can provide. Planned economies require governmental capacity to allocate resources, set production targets, and manage distribution. Mixed economies, which describe most contemporary systems, require government to balance market efficiency with social protection, a balance that is constantly renegotiated through the political process.

The relationship between government and technology is increasingly important. Digital technologies create new possibilities for government surveillance, citizen participation, service delivery, and transparency. At the same time, they create new risks to privacy, security, and democratic deliberation. The governance of artificial intelligence, data privacy, and cybersecurity are emerging policy domains where governmental institutions must adapt to technological change. The speed of technological development challenges traditional governmental processes, which are designed for deliberation rather than rapid response.


**The historical development of political parties.** Political parties are a relatively modern invention. For most of human history, political authority was exercised through dynastic succession, military conquest, religious hierarchy, or community consensus -- not through organized partisan competition. The emergence of political parties was closely tied to the development of representative government, the expansion of suffrage, and the need to organize mass political participation.

The first recognizable political parties emerged in Britain and the United States in the late 18th century. Britain's Whigs and Tories represented different factions within Parliament, divided over issues like the power of the monarchy, the rights of Parliament, and the role of religion in public life. The Federalists and Democratic-Republicans in the early United States similarly represented different visions of government: a strong central government with commercial interests versus a decentralized republic of agrarian virtue. These early parties were elite organizations, with limited mass participation.

The transformation of parties into mass organizations occurred in the 19th century, driven by the expansion of suffrage and the rise of industrial capitalism. Socialist parties, organized by trade unions and working-class organizations, mobilized millions of new voters across Europe. Conservative parties adapted by building their own mass organizations, often based on religious, rural, or business constituencies. The German Social Democratic Party, founded in 1875, became a model for mass party organization, with local branches, newspapers, educational programs, and social activities that created a comprehensive political subculture.

The 20th century saw further evolution of party types. "Catch-all" parties, described by Otto Kirchheimer, abandoned ideological distinctiveness in favor of broad electoral appeal. "Cartel parties," described by Richard Katz and Peter Mair, became increasingly dependent on state resources rather than mass membership. These developments have been accompanied by declining party membership, weakening party identification among voters, and growing anti-establishment sentiment that fuels populist challenges to established parties.

In the Global South, political parties developed under different conditions. Many parties in post-colonial countries were originally independence movements that transformed themselves into governing parties after independence. The Indian National Congress, the African National Congress, and FRELIMO in Mozambique all made this transition, sometimes maintaining power for decades. Other parties were created by military rulers as instruments of control, or emerged from ethnic, religious, or regional movements. The diversity of party origins in the Global South reflects the diversity of political trajectories in post-colonial states.
