# Federalism and local government

## Intuition [Beginner]

Not all government decisions are made in the capital. Most countries divide power between a central (national) government and regional or local governments. The question is how much power is shared, and who decides what.

There are three basic arrangements:

- **Unitary state**: the central government holds all sovereign power. It may create local governments and give them responsibilities, but it can also take those powers away. Local governments exist at the central government's discretion.
- **Federal state**: sovereignty is divided between a central government and regional governments (states, provinces, lander). The regional governments have constitutionally guaranteed powers that the central government cannot take away. Both levels of government operate directly on citizens.
- **Confederation**: sovereign states delegate limited powers to a central body for specific purposes (defense, trade) but retain ultimate authority. The central body's decisions may require unanimous consent from member states.

No pure type exists in practice. The United Kingdom is formally unitary but has devolved significant powers to Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland. The European Union has confederal origins but exercises some powers that are federal in character. China is formally unitary with a centralized party but has special administrative regions (Hong Kong, Macau) with considerable autonomy.

The choice between these arrangements is usually shaped by a country's size, diversity, history, and political culture. Large, diverse countries tend toward federalism. Small, homogeneous countries tend toward unitary government. But there are exceptions.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider how education policy works in three different systems:

**United States (federal):** The Constitution does not mention education. Under the 10th Amendment, powers not delegated to the federal government are reserved to the states. Education is therefore primarily a state responsibility. Each of the 50 states has its own education laws, standards, curriculum requirements, and funding systems. The federal government influences education through conditional grants (offering states money if they adopt certain standards) but cannot directly set curriculum. This means education policy varies enormously across states.

**France (unitary):** The national government sets curriculum, standards, teacher qualifications, and funding for all schools in France. The Ministry of National Education in Paris makes decisions that apply to every school in the country. Local authorities (communes, departments, regions) have some responsibilities (school buildings, some staffing) but the core educational program is nationally determined. A student moving from Paris to Marseille will find the same curriculum.

**Germany (federal):** Education is primarily the responsibility of the 16 Lander, not the federal government. Each Land has its own education ministry, curriculum, and school system. This means there are effectively 16 different education systems in Germany. The Standing Conference of Ministers of Education (KMK) coordinates to ensure comparability, but the variation is real: some Lander have comprehensive schools, others track students into academic and vocational paths at different ages. Germany's federalism reflects the principle of **subsidiarity**: decisions should be made at the lowest level capable of making them.

All three approaches work. The US approach allows experimentation and responsiveness to local preferences but produces inequality. The French approach ensures uniformity and equal standards but may not account for local needs. The German approach balances regional autonomy with national coordination.


### Federalism in action: comparative case studies

**Education policy in the United States.** Education is primarily a state and local responsibility in the US, with significant variation in funding, standards, and outcomes across states. The federal government influences education policy through conditional grants (providing about 8% of K-12 funding) and legislation like the Every Student Succeeds Act (2015).

**Health care in Canada.** Canada universal health insurance system (Medicare) is administered by provinces within federal guidelines. The Canada Health Act (1984) sets national standards (public administration, comprehensiveness, universality, portability, accessibility), but provinces determine how to deliver services and manage wait times.

**Goods and Services Tax in India.** India 2017 constitutional amendment created a unified national goods and services tax (GST), replacing a complex patchwork of state and federal taxes. The reform required constitutional amendment (needing a two-thirds majority in Parliament and ratification by at least half the states) and created a GST Council where federal and state finance ministers jointly set rates.

**Climate policy in the European Union.** The EU climate policy illustrates multilevel governance in action. The European Commission proposes climate legislation, the European Parliament and Council of Ministers adopt it, and member states implement it through national measures. The EU Emissions Trading System sets a cap on emissions from power plants and industry across all member states. But member states have discretion in how they meet their targets.

**Exercise 1.** The UK is a unitary state, but Scotland has its own parliament that passes laws on health, education, and justice. How is this different from federalism?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

In a federal system, the powers of regional governments are constitutionally guaranteed and cannot be unilaterally taken away by the central government. In the UK, the Scottish Parliament's powers exist because the UK Parliament passed the Scotland Act 1998 (and subsequent acts), and the UK Parliament could, in legal theory, repeal those acts and abolish the Scottish Parliament. This is called "devolution" -- power is devolved (handed down) from the center rather than constitutionally shared. In practice, abolishing the Scottish Parliament against Scotland's wishes would be politically impossible, but the legal distinction matters: the UK Parliament retains legislative sovereignty over all matters.

</details>

**Exercise 2.** If a US state passes a law that contradicts a federal law, which one applies?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

Under the **supremacy clause** of the US Constitution (Article VI), federal law is the "supreme law of the land" and prevails over conflicting state law. If Congress has legislated within its enumerated powers, states cannot override that legislation. However, in areas where the federal government has not legislated (or lacks the constitutional authority to legislate), states are free to set their own policies. This is why states have different laws on marijuana (the federal government prohibits it, but some states have legalized it, creating a tension between federal and state law that remains unresolved). The boundary between federal and state power is continuously negotiated through legislation, court decisions, and political practice.

</details>

**Exercise 3.** The European Union can pass regulations that apply directly in all member states, and member states cannot override them. Does this make the EU a federal state?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

Not in the standard sense. The EU shares some features with federalism: it has a central authority that makes binding law, a court that interprets that law, and mechanisms to enforce it. But the EU lacks other federal features: member states retain sovereignty and can (as the UK demonstrated by leaving) withdraw from the union; the EU's powers are limited to those delegated by treaty and cannot be expanded without unanimous agreement; there is no unified fiscal policy, no common military, and no single demos (people) in the political sense. The EU is best understood as a sui generis (unique) entity that defies simple classification. Scholars debate whether it is moving toward federalism or will remain a hybrid.

</details>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Federalism** is a system of government in which sovereignty is constitutionally divided between a central authority and constituent political units (states, provinces, Lander, cantons). The defining features are:

1. **Two (or more) levels of government** each with independent constitutional status.
2. **Constitutionally guaranteed division of powers** that neither level can unilaterally alter.
3. **Direct governance**: each level operates directly on citizens (taxes, laws, courts).
4. **A dispute resolution mechanism** (typically a supreme or constitutional court) to adjudicate conflicts between levels.
5. **Representation of subnational units** in central decision-making (typically through an upper house of the legislature).

**Subsidiarity** is the principle that decisions should be taken at the lowest level of government capable of addressing the issue effectively. Originating in Catholic social teaching (Pope Pius XI, 1931), it was adopted by the European Union (Article 5, Treaty on European Union) and is a general principle of federal design. The logic: local governments are closer to the people and better informed about local conditions, so they should handle local matters. Higher levels of government should act only when the issue exceeds local capacity.

**Fiscal federalism** (Oates, 1972) addresses the division of taxing and spending powers:

| Revenue source | Typically assigned to | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Income tax | Central (sometimes shared) | Redistribution; mobile tax base |
| Corporate tax | Central (sometimes shared) | Avoids race to the bottom |
| Value-added/sales tax | Central or shared | Uniform rate; efficient collection |
| Property tax | Local | Immobile base; local accountability |
| User fees | Local | Direct link between payment and service |

**Constitutional allocation of legislative powers** takes several forms:

| Model | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Dual federalism | Exclusive lists for each level; no overlap | US (original design; eroded in practice) |
| Concurrent powers | Both levels can legislate; federal prevails in conflict | Germany, India, Australia |
| Residual powers to center | Federal government gets unlisted powers | Canada, India |
| Residual powers to regions | States/provinces get unlisted powers | US, Australia |


The comparative study of governance systems reveals that familiar arrangements are not universal. Different societies have developed different institutional solutions to the fundamental problems of political organization: how to make collective decisions, how to constrain power, how to represent diverse interests, and how to hold leaders accountable. Understanding these differences is essential for thinking about governance without assuming that any single country's approach is the default.

This comparative perspective is especially important because the academic study of government has historically been dominated by Western European and North American models. The assumption that familiar institutional arrangements represent the "normal" form of governance is itself a product of particular historical circumstances, including colonial expansion, economic dominance, and the global influence of American political science after World War II. When we look beyond these familiar models, we find that governance takes many forms, and that the range of institutional possibilities is much wider than any single country's experience suggests.

Indigenous governance systems around the world developed sophisticated institutional arrangements that often embodied principles -- distributed authority, accountability mechanisms, consensus-based decision-making -- that Western political theory later claimed as its own inventions. The Iroquois Confederacy practiced a form of federalism with checks and balances centuries before the US Constitution. The Maori developed systems of collective resource management (kaitiakitanga) that integrated environmental governance with political authority. Many sub-Saharan African societies practiced forms of accountable kingship where rulers could be removed by councils of elders. These traditions deserve attention not as curiosities but as genuine contributions to the global inventory of governance design.

**Competing perspectives on governance.** Scholars and citizens disagree about fundamental questions of governance. Should government be large or small? Should it prioritize individual liberty or collective welfare? Should it be democratic or expert-led? These disagreements are not merely matters of preference; they reflect different theoretical frameworks and different interpretations of evidence.

Libertarians argue that government should be minimal, limited to protecting individual rights and enforcing contracts. They contend that free markets, voluntary cooperation, and civil society can solve most collective problems without government intervention. Social democrats argue that government should play an active role in ensuring social welfare, reducing inequality, and providing public goods. They contend that unfettered markets produce unacceptable levels of inequality and social harm. Communitarians argue that government should promote shared values and community bonds, contending that individualistic approaches undermine the social fabric.

These debates are not abstract. They shape practical policy questions: Should health care be provided by government or by markets? Should education be publicly funded or privately chosen? Should environmental protection rely on regulation or market mechanisms? Should social welfare programs be universal or targeted? Different countries answer these questions differently, and the comparative study of government provides evidence about the consequences of different choices. No single approach has proven universally superior, suggesting that effective governance requires pragmatic adaptation to specific circumstances rather than rigid adherence to ideological principles.


**The constitutional architecture of federalism.** The constitutional design of federal systems involves several key decisions. First is the division of legislative authority: which subjects are assigned to the central government, which to the subnational units, and which are concurrent. The US Constitution enumerates federal powers and reserves the rest to the states. The Indian Constitution provides three lists: Union, State, and Concurrent. The German Basic Law enumerates state powers and gives the federation authority only where explicitly granted. These different approaches reflect different starting assumptions about where authority should reside.

Second is the fiscal framework: how revenue is raised and distributed between levels of government. Federal systems typically give the central government control over the most lucrative tax sources (income tax, customs duties) while assigning states less productive taxes (property taxes, sales taxes). This creates vertical fiscal imbalance, which is addressed through transfer payments from the center to the states. The design of these transfer systems -- whether they are formula-based, discretionary, or conditional -- significantly affects the autonomy and resources of subnational governments.

Third is the representation of subnational units at the federal level. Most federal systems have a bicameral legislature where one chamber represents the population (like the US House of Representatives or Indian Lok Sabha) and the other represents the subnational units (like the US Senate or Indian Rajya Sabha). The degree to which the upper chamber protects subnational interests varies: the US Senate gives equal representation to each state regardless of population, while the Indian Rajya Sabha represents states proportionally to population. The German Bundesrat is unique in that its members are delegates of state governments rather than independently elected representatives, giving state governments direct input into federal legislation.

Fourth is the mechanism for resolving intergovernmental disputes. Federal systems require mechanisms for managing conflicts between levels of government, whether through courts (as in the United States, where the Supreme Court resolves federal-state disputes), political negotiations (as in Germany, where the Bundesrat and conference committees mediate between federal and state interests), or formal intergovernmental councils (as in India, where the Inter-State Council facilitates coordination). The effectiveness of these mechanisms is critical for the smooth functioning of federal governance.
## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

**Cooperative federalism vs. dual federalism.** In theory, federal and state governments operate in separate spheres. In practice, they constantly interact:

- **Dual federalism** (layer-cake): each level operates independently within its assigned sphere. Early US federalism approximated this model.
- **Cooperative federalism** (marble-cake): the levels work together on shared programs. The US New Deal (1930s) created cooperative programs where the federal government funded state-administered programs.
- **Competitive federalism**: states compete to attract residents, businesses, and investment through different policy packages. This is framed as beneficial (laboratory of democracy) or harmful (race to the bottom).
- **Coercive federalism**: the federal government uses conditional grants and mandates to direct state policy, effectively overriding state preferences.

**Fiscal equalization.** Federal systems often include mechanisms to redistribute resources from wealthier to poorer regions, ensuring that all citizens receive comparable public services regardless of where they live:

| Country | Equalization mechanism |
|---|---|
| Germany | Horizontal equalization: wealthy Lander transfer funds to poorer Lander; also federal supplementary grants |
| Canada | Federal equalization payments to provinces with below-average fiscal capacity |
| Australia | Grants Commission recommends distribution of GST revenue to achieve horizontal fiscal equalization |
| US | Limited; mostly through categorical grant programs rather than systematic equalization |
| India | Finance Commission recommends distribution of tax revenue between center and states; grants to poorer states |

**Local government.** Below the state/provincial level, local governments (municipalities, counties, districts, communes) handle day-to-day services: policing, fire protection, water, waste management, local roads, zoning, primary education (in some systems). Local governments are usually created by the state or national government and have only the powers granted to them (Dillon's Rule in the US) or general authority to govern local affairs (home rule).

| Country | Local government structure | Powers |
|---|---|---|
| US | Counties, municipalities, townships, special districts (varies by state) | Granted by state; varies widely |
| France | Communes (36,000+), departments, regions | Limited autonomy; strong central oversight |
| Germany | Gemeinden (municipalities), Kreise (districts) | Constitutional right to self-government (Article 28) |
| Japan | Prefectures (47), municipalities | Limited autonomy; significant central delegation |
| Brazil | States, municipalities (with constitutional status) | Municipalities have federal status; significant autonomy |

### Exercises

**Exercise 1.** During the COVID-19 pandemic, some US states imposed strict lockdowns while neighboring states remained open. What does this reveal about federalism, and what are the arguments for and against allowing this variation?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

The variation reveals that US federalism gives states significant authority over public health measures (a power reserved to the states under the 10th Amendment). Arguments for allowing variation: (a) states could tailor responses to local conditions (population density, hospital capacity, infection rates); (b) federalism allows experimentation -- states could learn from each other's approaches; (c) local preferences differ, and decentralization respects those differences. Arguments against: (a) viruses do not respect state borders, so one state's lax policies could undermine another state's restrictions; (b) variation produced confusion and unequal protection; (c) collective action problems -- states had incentives to free-ride on neighbors' restrictions while keeping their own economies open. The pandemic illustrated the fundamental tension in federalism between local autonomy and coordinated response to problems that cross boundaries.

</details>

**Exercise 2.** Germany's Bundesrat (upper house) represents the Lander, not individual citizens. Land governments appoint Bundesrat members, who vote as a bloc. How does this differ from the US Senate, and what does each system prioritize?

<details>
<summary>Reveal</summary>

The US Senate represents states but its members are directly elected by voters in each state. Senators are independent agents who vote individually and may disagree with their state's governor or legislature. The Bundesrat represents Land governments: members are delegates of the governing coalition in each Land and vote as instructed by that government. The US system prioritizes democratic representation of citizens within states; the German system prioritizes direct participation of subnational governments in federal legislation. The Bundesrat means that when the opposition controls a majority of Lander, it can block federal legislation -- creating a check on central government power that is institutional rather than electoral. Both systems represent states, but one connects to voters and the other to governments.

</details>


Governance in the Global South presents distinctive challenges and innovations that are often underrepresented in political science textbooks. Post-colonial states inherited institutional frameworks designed for extraction and control rather than democratic participation, and many have struggled to adapt these frameworks to serve their populations. However, this experience has also produced innovations: India's constitutional protections for disadvantaged groups, South Africa's constitutional enforcement of socio-economic rights, Botswana's integration of traditional governance with democratic institutions, and Rwanda's community-based justice and reconciliation processes.

The experience of the Global South also challenges some Western assumptions about governance. The assumption that economic development requires democratic institutions has been contradicted by the rapid growth of authoritarian states like China and Singapore. The assumption that strong central government is necessary for effective governance has been challenged by the success of decentralized systems in some developing countries. The assumption that Western-style political parties are the natural mechanism for aggregating interests has been challenged by the persistence of ethnic, religious, and patron-client networks as primary political organizations in many countries. These challenges do not invalidate Western governance models but suggest that they are not the only viable approach.


**Comparative federal and unitary systems.** The distinction between federal and unitary states is one of the most fundamental in comparative government. Federal systems divide sovereignty between central and regional governments, with each level having constitutionally protected powers. Unitary systems concentrate sovereignty in the central government, which may delegate powers to local authorities but can also withdraw them. Between these poles, many countries have developed intermediate arrangements.

The United States, Germany, India, Australia, Brazil, Canada, and Nigeria are examples of federal systems, but they differ significantly in practice. Germany's federalism is cooperative, with the federal and state governments working closely together on implementation. India's federalism is often described as "quasi-federal" because the central government has significant emergency powers and can reorganize state boundaries. Nigeria's federalism was designed to accommodate ethnic and religious diversity but has struggled with resource allocation disputes. Brazil's federalism gives states significant autonomy, including independent tax bases and their own constitutions.

Unitary states like France, Japan, and the United Kingdom have also experimented with decentralization. France's 1982 decentralization reforms transferred significant powers to elected local governments. The UK has devolved substantial authority to Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland, creating an asymmetrical system where different parts of the country have different levels of self-government. Japan's system of prefectures provides local governance within a framework of national standards.

Decentralization and devolution are trends across both federal and unitary systems, driven by demands for local autonomy, efficiency gains from tailoring policies to local conditions, and democratic participation. However, decentralization also carries risks: it can entrench local inequalities, complicate national policy coordination, and create opportunities for local corruption. The challenge is to find the right balance between central coordination and local autonomy, a balance that varies depending on each country's specific circumstances.
## Political theory [Master]

**The federalism trade-off.** Federalism involves a fundamental trade-off between two values:

- **Uniformity and equality**: the same rights, services, and standards apply to all citizens regardless of where they live.
- **Diversity and responsiveness**: different communities can adopt different policies reflecting their preferences, needs, and values.

Unitary systems prioritize uniformity. Federal systems accept some variation in exchange for local control. Neither is objectively superior; the choice reflects political values and circumstances.

**Federalism as a solution to ethnic and national conflict.** In divided societies, federalism can accommodate different groups by giving each territorial autonomy:

- Switzerland's cantonal system accommodates German, French, Italian, and Romansh linguistic communities.
- India's federal structure was reorganized along linguistic lines in the 1950s, defusing language conflicts.
- Belgium evolved from a unitary state to a federal one (1993) to accommodate Flemish and Walloon communities.
- Ethiopia adopted "ethnic federalism" (1995), creating regional states along ethnic lines -- a controversial experiment that some argue reduced conflict and others argue institutionalized ethnic division.

The success of federalism as a conflict-management tool depends on whether group boundaries align with territorial boundaries. When they do (Switzerland, India's linguistic states), territorial autonomy can work. When groups are intermingled (Bosnia, Northern Ireland), territorial federalism may not resolve the conflict and may even entrench divisions.

**The "laboratory of democracy" argument.** Justice Brandeis argued in *New State Ice Co. v. Liebmann* (1932) that "a single courageous state may, if its citizens choose, serve as a laboratory; and try novel social and economic experiments without risk to the rest of the country." This is one of the strongest arguments for federalism: decentralization allows policy experimentation, and successful experiments can be adopted by other jurisdictions.

Evidence supports this: many US policy innovations began at the state level (women's suffrage, environmental regulation, healthcare reform, same-sex marriage). But the laboratory argument has limits: some experiments fail, some produce harmful outcomes for the citizens of the experimenting state, and some problems (climate change, pandemics) cannot be solved through local experimentation.

**Federalism and democratic accountability.** Federalism creates multiple points of access for citizens to influence government, which can enhance democracy. But it also complicates accountability: when something goes wrong, citizens may not know which level of government is responsible. The diffusion of responsibility allows each level to blame the other, reducing electoral accountability. This is a particular problem in systems where responsibilities overlap significantly (cooperative federalism).

**Fiscal federalism and equity.** The economist Albert Oates (1972) formalized the "decentralization theorem": public goods whose benefits are localized should be provided by local governments, while goods with national spillovers should be provided centrally. This is efficient but can produce inequality if local governments have very different fiscal capacities. Federal systems must decide how much equality to guarantee across regions -- a question that is inherently political.

**Federalism and secession.** Federalism raises the question of whether subnational units have the right to leave the federation. Most federal constitutions are silent on secession. The US Civil War established (by force) that states cannot unilaterally secede. Canada's Clarity Act (2000) set conditions under which Quebec could secede: a clear majority on a clear question, followed by constitutional negotiation. The Ethiopian constitution uniquely grants an explicit right to secede (Article 39), though it has never been exercised peacefully.


Indigenous governance systems offer important perspectives on institutional design. Unlike the European state model, which organizes authority through territorial control and bureaucratic hierarchy, many indigenous systems organize authority through kinship, reciprocity, and relationship to land. The Haudenosaunee (Iroquois) Confederacy's Great Law of Peace established governance principles including participatory decision-making, recall mechanisms for leaders, and the requirement that decisions consider their impact on seven future generations. The Maori developed systems where authority (mana) was connected to responsibility (kaitiakitanga), and where leaders who failed to fulfill their obligations could lose their legitimacy.

Aboriginal Australian governance systems, among the oldest continuous governance traditions on Earth, organized authority through songlines, kinship networks, and elder councils. Decision-making was typically consensual, with the emphasis on maintaining social harmony and ecological balance rather than on majority rule. These systems governed complex societies for tens of thousands of years, demonstrating that sophisticated governance does not require the institutional forms that European political theory considers essential.

The incorporation of indigenous governance principles into modern constitutional frameworks is an ongoing process. New Zealand's Waitangi Tribunal interprets the Treaty of Waitangi in light of both Maori and Crown understandings. Canada's recognition of Aboriginal self-government in the Constitution Act, 1982 has led to negotiated self-government agreements. Bolivia's 2009 Constitution establishes a plurinational state that recognizes indigenous justice systems alongside the national legal system. These developments suggest that the future of governance may involve greater integration of indigenous and Western institutional traditions.


**Fiscal federalism and resource distribution.** One of the most practical and contentious aspects of federalism is the distribution of financial resources between levels of government. Subnational governments need revenue to provide services but often lack the tax bases to generate sufficient funds. Central governments have greater taxing capacity but may be reluctant to share resources with subnational governments that are controlled by opposition parties. This tension produces ongoing political conflicts over revenue sharing in virtually every federal system.

In the United States, the federal government uses grants-in-aid to influence state policy in areas like education, transportation, and health care. These grants may be categorical (restricted to specific purposes) or block grants (more flexible). The conditions attached to federal grants have been a source of constitutional controversy, with the Supreme Court setting limits on federal conditional spending in cases like NFIB v. Sebelius (2012), which restricted the federal government's ability to coerce states into expanding Medicaid.

In India, the Finance Commission, a constitutional body appointed every five years, recommends how tax revenue should be divided between the central government and the states. The Goods and Services Tax (GST), introduced in 2017, created a unified national market but also required complex revenue-sharing arrangements between the central government and states. India's fiscal federalism is complicated by vast disparities in state capacity and revenue, with some states like Maharashtra generating far more revenue per capita than poorer states like Bihar.

Nigeria's fiscal federalism revolves around oil revenue. The federal government controls oil resources and distributes revenue to states through a formula that has been the subject of intense political debate. Oil-producing states in the Niger Delta argue that they should receive a larger share of the revenue generated from their territory, while other states argue that national resources should be shared equitably. This dispute has contributed to political instability and conflict in the Niger Delta region.

**Local governance and community participation.** Local government is often described as the level of government closest to the people, and comparative experience suggests that effective local governance is essential for democratic participation and service delivery. Local governments provide services -- water, sanitation, primary education, local roads, land use planning -- that directly affect daily life. They also provide opportunities for citizens to participate in governance and hold officials accountable.

Comparative analysis reveals different models of local governance. The United Kingdom has traditionally had relatively weak local government, with significant central control over local taxation and policy. Scandinavian countries have strong local governments with significant autonomy and tax-raising powers. Brazil has implemented participatory budgeting in many municipalities, allowing citizens to directly decide how a portion of the municipal budget is spent. India's constitutional amendments in 1992 established panchayati raj institutions that devolved significant powers to village, intermediate, and district levels, though implementation has varied across states.

The concept of subsidiarity, developed in Catholic social teaching and incorporated into European Union law, holds that decisions should be made at the lowest level of government capable of making them effectively. This principle supports decentralization but also recognizes that some functions -- national defense, macroeconomic policy, environmental protection -- require central coordination. The challenge is to determine which functions belong at which level of government, a determination that depends on the specific circumstances of each country and policy area.

**Federalism, identity, and conflict management.** Federalism and decentralization are often proposed as solutions to ethnic, religious, and linguistic conflicts. The theory is that granting subnational autonomy allows different groups to govern themselves in areas of cultural importance while maintaining national unity on matters of common concern. Switzerland's successful accommodation of German, French, Italian, and Romansh speakers within a federal framework is often cited as a model.

However, federalism is not a guaranteed solution to identity-based conflict. Yugoslavia's federal structure did not prevent the violent breakup of the country in the 1990s. Nigeria's federal system has not eliminated ethnic and religious tensions. Ethiopia's experiment with ethnic federalism has produced both empowerment and conflict, with some ethnic groups gaining autonomy while others have been marginalized or displaced. The effectiveness of federalism in managing identity-based conflict depends on factors including the specific institutional design, the distribution of resources, the commitment of political leaders to federal principles, and the broader political culture.

The relationship between federalism and democracy is complex. On one hand, federalism creates multiple arenas for democratic participation, allowing citizens to engage with government at different levels. Subnational governments can serve as "laboratories of democracy," experimenting with policy innovations that can be adopted by other jurisdictions if successful. On the other hand, federalism can entrench local inequalities, allow subnational authoritarianism, and complicate efforts to establish national standards for rights protection.

The European Union represents a novel form of multilevel governance that does not fit easily into the category of federalism. The EU has supranational institutions with real governing authority, but it is not a federal state. Member states retain sovereignty in many areas and can veto treaty changes. The ongoing debate about "ever closer union" versus national sovereignty reflects the fundamental tension between the benefits of cooperation at the European level and the desire to maintain national identity and self-government. The EU's experience suggests that multilevel governance can take forms that go beyond traditional federal-unitary categories.

The environmental policy dimension of federalism is increasingly important. Environmental problems like climate change, air pollution, and water management do not respect jurisdictional boundaries, creating challenges for governance systems based on territorial division of authority. Climate policy requires coordination across local, regional, national, and international levels, testing the capacity of federal systems to address transboundary problems. The comparative study of environmental federalism reveals that effective environmental governance requires both centralized standard-setting and decentralized implementation, a balance that different countries strike in different ways.


**Federalism and the global economy.** The relationship between federalism and economic development is complex and contested. Some scholars argue that federalism promotes economic growth by allowing subnational governments to compete for investment, experiment with economic policies, and tailor regulations to local conditions. Others argue that federalism can hinder development by creating barriers to trade between regions, allowing a "race to the bottom" in regulatory standards, and complicating the coordination of national economic policy.

The experience of federal countries supports both views. The United States, Germany, and Switzerland are among the wealthiest countries in the world, suggesting that federalism is compatible with economic success. However, India, Nigeria, and Brazil demonstrate that federalism does not guarantee prosperity and can complicate development in countries with significant regional disparities. The relationship between federalism and economic outcomes depends on factors including the specific institutional design, the distribution of fiscal resources, the quality of governance at different levels, and the broader economic and political context.

The globalization of the economy presents new challenges for federal systems. International trade agreements increasingly set standards in areas like intellectual property, labor rights, and environmental protection that were previously determined at the national or subnational level. This creates a dynamic where international commitments constrain the policy space available to subnational governments, potentially undermining the autonomy that is a core feature of federalism. Navigating this tension between international economic integration and federal governance is one of the central challenges facing federal systems in the 21st century.
## Historical context [Master]

**Pre-modern antecedents.** Federal-like arrangements existed before the modern concept of federalism. The Achaean League and Aetolian League in ancient Greece were confederations of city-states. The Holy Roman Empire was a complex mosaic of principalities, free cities, and bishoprics with a weak central authority. The Dutch Republic (1581-1795) was a confederation of sovereign provinces. The Iroquois Confederacy united six nations under a shared council while preserving each nation's internal autonomy.

**The American invention.** The US Constitution (1787) was the first modern federal system. Under the Articles of Confederation (1781-1789), the US was a confederation with a weak central government that could not tax or regulate commerce. The Constitution created a stronger central government with independent powers, while reserving other powers to the states. *The Federalist Papers* (especially Nos. 10, 39, 45, and 46) articulated the theory: a federal republic could combine the advantages of a large state (defense, commercial power) with those of small republics (responsive government, civic participation).

**19th century federalism.** Switzerland adopted its federal constitution in 1848, creating a system of cantons with considerable autonomy. Canada confederated in 1867, creating a federal system with a stronger central government than the US model (residual powers to the center). Germany unified in 1871 as a federation of monarchies under Prussian leadership; its federal tradition continued through the Weimar Republic and was strengthened in the post-war Basic Law (1949).

**Post-colonial federalism.** India adopted federalism (1950) to accommodate its enormous linguistic, religious, and regional diversity. Nigeria (1960, revised 1999) created a federal system to manage ethnic and regional tensions among its hundreds of ethnic groups. Brazil maintained the federal system inherited from its imperial constitution (1891). Many post-colonial states that adopted federalism did so not out of ideological commitment to decentralization but because no alternative could hold diverse territories together.

**Contemporary trends.** Since the late 20th century, there has been a global trend toward decentralization. Unitary states have devolved powers (UK to Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland; Spain to autonomous communities; Italy to regions). The motivations include democratic participation, efficient service delivery, accommodation of regional identities, and the influence of international institutions (World Bank, EU) that promote decentralization. At the same time, some federal systems have experienced centralization (increased federal power in the US, especially since the New Deal). The overall trend is mixed: formal federal structures have remained relatively stable, while the actual distribution of power between levels has shifted in complex ways.


Decolonial scholars argue that the study of government must account for the ways in which colonialism shaped -- and continues to shape -- political institutions worldwide. The modern state system, with its assumptions about territorial sovereignty, centralized authority, and the separation of domestic and international politics, was imposed on much of the world through colonialism. Post-colonial states inherited boundaries drawn by colonial powers, administrative structures designed for extraction, and legal systems that often contradicted local governance traditions.

Walter Mignolo and other decolonial theorists argue that the very concepts used to analyze government -- sovereignty, democracy, rights, the rule of law -- carry the imprint of European intellectual history and may not capture the full range of political experience. This does not mean these concepts should be abandoned, but that they should be understood as products of particular historical circumstances rather than as universal truths. The challenge is to develop analytical frameworks that can accommodate both Western and non-Western governance traditions without privileging one over the other.

The practical implications of decolonial analysis are significant. Constitutional design in post-conflict societies increasingly incorporates indigenous governance traditions alongside Western institutional models. International organizations are grappling with how to promote governance standards without imposing Western templates. And the rise of China as an alternative model of development governance challenges the assumption that Western institutional arrangements are universally applicable. The study of government in the 21st century must be genuinely comparative, attending to the full diversity of institutional arrangements that human societies have developed.

**Connections across disciplines.** The study of government connects to philosophy, economics, sociology, history, and psychology. Political philosophy provides the normative frameworks -- justice, equality, liberty, democracy -- that justify and critique governmental arrangements. Economics illuminates the incentives that shape political behavior and the tradeoffs involved in policy choices. Sociology reveals how social structures -- class, race, gender, religion -- shape political participation and institutional design. History shows how present arrangements emerged from particular contingencies and conflicts.

The connection between government and economics is especially important. Different governmental systems create different incentive structures for economic activity. Market economies require legal frameworks for property rights, contract enforcement, and corporate governance that only government can provide. Planned economies require governmental capacity to allocate resources, set production targets, and manage distribution. Mixed economies, which describe most contemporary systems, require government to balance market efficiency with social protection, a balance that is constantly renegotiated through the political process.

The relationship between government and technology is increasingly important. Digital technologies create new possibilities for government surveillance, citizen participation, service delivery, and transparency. At the same time, they create new risks to privacy, security, and democratic deliberation. The governance of artificial intelligence, data privacy, and cybersecurity are emerging policy domains where governmental institutions must adapt to technological change. The speed of technological development challenges traditional governmental processes, which are designed for deliberation rather than rapid response.


**The historical development of federalism.** Federalism as a conscious constitutional choice -- rather than as an accidental product of historical fragmentation -- is largely a modern phenomenon. The United States Constitution (1787) is widely considered the first deliberate federal constitution, dividing sovereignty between a national government and state governments, each with constitutionally protected powers. The American experiment was influenced by the Iroquois Confederacy, which practiced a form of federalism for centuries before European contact, as well as by European political theorists including Montesquieu and the authors of the Federalist Papers.

Switzerland's federal system, formalized in the 1848 constitution, emerged from the need to unite diverse linguistic, religious, and cantonal communities under a single national government. The Swiss model emphasizes direct democracy, with citizens able to challenge laws through referendums and propose constitutional amendments through initiatives. Canada's federal system, established in 1867, was designed to balance English and French interests within a single state, though the tension between federal and provincial authority, particularly regarding Quebec, has been a persistent theme.

Germany's federal system, established by the Basic Law in 1949, was designed to prevent the concentration of power that enabled the Nazi dictatorship. The German model is characterized by cooperative federalism, with the federal government and the states (Lander) working closely together on policy implementation. The Bundesrat, the upper house of parliament, represents state governments and must approve many federal laws, giving states significant influence over national legislation.

India's federal system, established by the 1950 constitution, was designed to hold together a vast and diverse country with multiple languages, religions, and ethnic groups. The Indian constitution gives the central government significant emergency powers, including the ability to impose President's Rule in states during periods of constitutional breakdown. This "quasi-federal" design reflects the founders' concern that a weak center could lead to national disintegration in a country as diverse as India.

Post-colonial Africa has produced a variety of federal and decentralized arrangements, often designed to accommodate ethnic diversity. Nigeria has oscillated between federal and unitary arrangements, with the current federal structure designed to balance the interests of its predominantly Muslim north and predominantly Christian south. Ethiopia adopted a distinctive form of ethnic federalism in 1995, organizing regional states along ethnic lines and constitutionally recognizing the right of ethnic groups to self-determination, including secession. South Africa's 1996 constitution established a system of provinces with significant but not constitutionally protected powers. These African experiments demonstrate both the potential of federalism to accommodate diversity and its limitations in contexts of poverty, inequality, and weak institutional capacity.


**The future of multilevel governance.** The traditional federal-unitary distinction is becoming less relevant as governance becomes increasingly multilevel. The European Union, devolution in the United Kingdom, decentralization in unitary states, and the growing importance of city-regions all point toward a future in which governance authority is distributed across multiple levels rather than concentrated at one or two. This "multilevel governance" perspective emphasizes that governance problems do not respect jurisdictional boundaries and that effective governance requires coordination across levels.

Climate governance illustrates the challenges of multilevel governance. Greenhouse gas emissions are a global problem requiring international cooperation, but mitigation and adaptation policies must be implemented at national, regional, and local levels. Cities, which produce over 70% of global CO2 emissions, are increasingly important actors in climate governance, with networks like C40 Cities coordinating municipal climate action across national boundaries. This creates a governance architecture that does not fit neatly into traditional federal-unitary categories.

The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated both the strengths and weaknesses of multilevel governance. In federal systems, different states and provinces could tailor their responses to local conditions, but coordination failures between levels of government sometimes produced inconsistent and ineffective policies. In unitary systems, centralized decision-making produced more uniform responses but sometimes failed to account for local variation. The pandemic revealed that effective governance requires both central coordination and local flexibility, a balance that is difficult to achieve in any institutional framework.

The growing importance of cities and metropolitan areas as governance actors is reshaping federal and unitary systems alike. Over half the world's population lives in urban areas, and cities are responsible for over 80% of global GDP. Yet cities often have limited governance authority compared to national and regional governments. The mismatch between the economic and social importance of cities and their governance powers is a growing challenge that will require institutional innovation in both federal and unitary systems.
