## Intuition [Beginner]

**Migration** is the movement of people from one place to another with the intention of settling, either permanently or temporarily. People have been migrating since the origin of our species -- humans first left Africa roughly 70,000 years ago and eventually populated every continent. Today, roughly 280 million people (about 3.6% of the global population) live outside the country where they were born.

People migrate for a mixture of **push factors** (things that make them want to leave: war, poverty, persecution, environmental disaster, lack of jobs) and **pull factors** (things that attract them to a new place: job opportunities, safety, political freedom, family already there, better schools or healthcare). The decision to migrate is rarely simple. Most people would prefer to stay home if conditions allowed it. Migration is usually a response to necessity or the rational pursuit of a better life.

## Worked Example [Beginner]

In 2015, over a million refugees and migrants arrived in Europe, mostly from Syria, Afghanistan, and Iraq. What drove this movement?

The primary **push factor** was the Syrian civil war, which began in 2011 and had by 2015 produced widespread destruction of cities, infrastructure, and livelihoods. Over 6 million Syrians were internally displaced within Syria, and roughly 6.8 million had fled the country entirely. Afghanistan had been in conflict for decades. Iraq was destabilized by war and the rise of ISIS.

The **pull factors** included relative safety in Europe, existing Syrian diaspora communities in Germany and Sweden, Germany's initial open-door policy in summer 2015, and the perception (not always accurate) of generous welfare systems and job opportunities. Geographic proximity and the existence of smuggling routes through Turkey, the Aegean Sea, and the Western Balkans made the journey physically possible despite its dangers.

The response varied enormously by country. Germany accepted over a million asylum seekers in 2015-2016. Hungary built a border fence. Sweden and Denmark later tightened their asylum policies. The EU attempted to establish a quota system for distributing refugees among member states, but several Central European countries refused to participate. This episode illustrates how migration is shaped by the interaction of conflict, geography, policy, and domestic politics.

## Formal Definition [Intermediate+]

**Human migration** is the spatial movement of individuals or groups from one location to another, involving a change of usual residence. Migration is classified along multiple dimensions:

**By spatial scope**:
- **Internal migration**: Movement within a country's borders. This is quantitatively the largest form of migration globally. In China alone, an estimated 280 million internal migrants have moved from rural to urban areas since economic reforms began in 1978.
- **International migration**: Movement across international borders. The UN International Organization for Migration (IOM) estimated 281 million international migrants in 2020 (3.6% of global population).

**By voluntariness**:
- **Voluntary migration**: Primarily driven by economic opportunity, family reunification, or lifestyle preference. Governed by immigration law of the destination state.
- **Forced migration**: Driven by conflict, persecution, environmental disaster, or state action. The UNHCR counted 108.4 million forcibly displaced people worldwide in 2023, including 35.3 million refugees, 62.5 million internally displaced persons, 6.1 million asylum seekers, and 4.4 million stateless persons.

**Legal categories** (under international law):
- **Refugee**: Defined by the 1951 Convention Relating to the Status of Refugees as a person who, "owing to well-founded fear of being persecuted for reasons of race, religion, nationality, membership of a particular social group or political opinion, is outside the country of his nationality and is unable or, owing to such fear, is unwilling to avail himself of the protection of that country."
- **Asylum seeker**: A person who has applied for refugee status but whose claim has not yet been adjudicated.
- **Internally displaced person (IDP)**: A person forced to flee their home but who has not crossed an international border. IDPs are not covered by the Refugee Convention and remain under the jurisdiction of their own government.

**Push-pull theory** (Everett Lee, 1966) classifies factors into origin push factors (unemployment, conflict, environmental degradation), destination pull factors (jobs, safety, family, political freedom), intervening obstacles (distance, cost, borders, legal barriers), and personal factors (age, education, risk tolerance). This framework is useful but criticized for being overly individualistic and for implying that migration decisions are purely rational.

## Key Concepts [Intermediate+]

**Migration transition theory** (Zelinsky, 1971) posits that as societies develop economically, their migration patterns shift through predictable stages: pre-industrial societies have limited mobility; early industrialization produces rural-to-urban internal migration and international emigration; advanced industrial societies become destinations for immigration; post-industrial societies experience complex patterns including high-skilled immigration and lifestyle migration.

**Diaspora** refers to a scattered population whose origin lies in a separate geographic locale. The term originally described the Jewish diaspora but is now applied broadly: the Armenian diaspora, the African diaspora, the Chinese diaspora, the Indian diaspora, and others. Diasporas maintain collective identity and often ties (economic, cultural, political) to a real or imagined homeland. Diaspora remittances -- money sent by migrants to families in origin countries -- totaled approximately $656 billion in 2022, exceeding foreign aid to developing countries by a factor of roughly three.

**Brain drain** refers to the emigration of highly trained or educated people from a poorer to a richer country. An estimated 15-20% of sub-Saharan Africa's tertiary-educated population lives outside the region. Health worker migration is particularly acute: Ghana, for example, has approximately 0.15 physicians per 1,000 people, while many Ghanaian-trained doctors practice in the UK and US. Counter-arguments note that remittances offset some losses, that diaspora networks facilitate technology transfer, and that the possibility of emigration incentivizes education ("brain gain" through incentive effects).

**Transnationalism** (Glick Schiller et al., 1992) describes how migrants maintain simultaneous connections to origin and destination societies, operating across borders rather than fully assimilating into one national context. This challenges the traditional assumption that migration is a one-way, permanent relocation.

## Geographic Theory [Master+]

**Migration systems theory** (Kritz, Lim, and Zlotnik, 1992) analyzes migration as a system of linked origin and destination countries, connected by flows of people, goods, capital, and information. Once a migration flow is established, it tends to be self-sustaining through network effects: earlier migrants provide information, housing, and social support to later ones, reducing the costs and risks of migration. This explains why specific origin-destination corridors (Morocco-France, Turkey-Germany, Mexico-USA, Philippines-Saudi Arabia) persist over decades even when initial push factors have changed.

**Time-space geography** (Hagerstrand, 1970; applied to migration by Findley, 1987) models the constraints on individual movement as authority constraints (borders, laws), capability constraints (resources, transport), and coupling constraints (the need to be in specific places at specific times). Migration emerges from the interaction of these constraints with individual aspirations. This framework integrates structural and agency-based explanations.

**Political economy of migration** (Castles, de Haas, and Miller, 2014) situates migration within global inequalities. It argues that migration is structural -- produced by the same processes of capitalist development and geopolitical inequality that differentiate sending and receiving countries. The creation of borders and immigration controls, paradoxically, produces "illegal" migration by criminalizing movement that would otherwise follow established labor market demands.

**Environmental migration** has emerged as a major research area. The Foresight Report (2011) estimated that environmental change could displace 150-200 million people by 2050, though these projections are contested. Key issues include: distinguishing environmental drivers from economic and political ones (they are typically intertwined), the legal status of "climate refugees" (not recognized under the 1951 Convention), and whether environmental migration is primarily internal or international (evidence suggests mostly internal and short-distance).

**Critical migration studies** challenge the categories and frameworks used to study migration. The "migration crisis" framing, for instance, is analyzed as a political construction that serves particular policy agendas (De Genova, 2002; Mezzadra and Neilson, 2013). The distinction between "forced" and "voluntary" migration is critiqued as a legal artifact that obscures the continuum of constraint under which migration decisions are made.

## Historical Context [Master+]

Human migration is as old as the species. The **Out of Africa** expansion (~70,000-50,000 BCE) populated the globe. The **Neolithic Revolution** (~10,000 BCE) produced the first major shift in human settlement patterns, as agricultural populations expanded at the expense of hunter-gatherer groups. The **Bantu Expansion** (~3,000 BCE-500 CE) spread Bantu-speaking agriculturalists across sub-Saharan Africa. The **Austronesian expansion** (~5,000-1,000 BCE) settled the Pacific Islands.

The **Atlantic slave trade** (1500s-1800s) forcibly transported approximately 12.5 million Africans to the Americas -- the largest forced migration in history. Its demographic, economic, and cultural effects shaped the Americas and Africa profoundly and persist to the present.

The **Age of Mass Migration** (1815-1914) saw approximately 60 million Europeans emigrate, primarily to the Americas (USA, Canada, Argentina, Brazil, Australia). This was driven by population growth, agricultural displacement, industrialization, and the availability of cheap transatlantic transport. It ended with World War I and the restrictive immigration laws of the 1920s (US Immigration Act of 1924).

**Post-1945 migration** was shaped by decolonization (producing migration flows along former colonial corridors), labor recruitment (European guest worker programs of the 1950s-1970s, Gulf state recruitment), Cold War refugee flows (Hungarian 1956, Czech 1968, Vietnamese 1975, Soviet Jewish 1989-1991), and post-Cold War globalization (rising international migration from ~80 million in 1970 to ~280 million in 2020).

The **2015 European migration crisis** and ongoing displacement from Syria, Afghanistan, South Sudan, Myanmar, Venezuela, and Ukraine have made migration one of the most politically contentious issues in destination countries, driving the rise of anti-immigration political parties and reshaping policy debates around border control, integration, and national identity.
