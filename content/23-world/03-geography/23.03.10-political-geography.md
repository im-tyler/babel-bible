## Intuition [Beginner]

The Earth's land surface is divided into almost 200 countries, each claiming sovereignty over its territory. But borders are not natural features of the landscape -- they are human inventions. Some borders follow natural features like rivers or mountain ranges; others are straight lines drawn on a map by colonial administrators who had never visited the land. The study of how and why territory is divided, governed, and contested is **political geography**.

Three terms that people often confuse are **nation**, **state**, and **nation-state**. A **nation** is a group of people who share a common identity (language, culture, history). A **state** is a political unit with defined territory, a government, and sovereignty (the authority to govern itself). A **nation-state** is a state whose territory corresponds to a single nation -- Japan, for instance, where the Japanese nation and the Japanese state largely overlap. Many states are multinational (India contains dozens of distinct nations), and many nations are stateless (the Kurds, Palestinians, Tibetans).

## Worked Example [Beginner]

The border between the United States and Canada west of the Great Lakes is a straight line along the 49th parallel of latitude. It was established by treaty between the US and Britain (which controlled Canada) in 1818. This is an **artificial border** -- it does not follow any physical feature of the landscape. People living on opposite sides of the line may share the same climate, the same terrain, and even the same economy, but they are citizens of different countries with different laws, taxes, and governments.

Contrast this with the border between Chile and Argentina along the Andes Mountains. This is a **natural border** -- it follows the mountain range that physically separates the two countries. Natural borders are not inherently better or worse than artificial ones, but they tend to create fewer practical problems because the physical barrier already limits interaction.

Some borders are deeply contested. **Western Sahara** is claimed by Morocco, which controls most of the territory, and by the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic, which is recognized by numerous states and controls a smaller portion. **Kashmir** is claimed by India, Pakistan, and China, each controlling different parts. **Taiwan** operates as an independent state with its own government, military, and economy, but the People's Republic of China claims it as a province. These disputes have different historical origins and different international legal statuses, but they all involve competing claims to the same territory.

## Formal Definition [Intermediate+]

**Political geography** is the study of how space is organized into political units and how geographic factors influence political processes. It examines the spatial dimensions of power: how states claim, organize, defend, and contest territory.

**Sovereignty** is the authority of a state to govern itself within its territory without external interference. The modern state system is commonly dated to the Peace of Westphalia (1648), which established the principle that each state has exclusive authority over its territory and domestic affairs.

**Territoriality** (Robert Sack, 1986) is the attempt by an individual or group to affect, influence, or control people, phenomena, and relationships by delimiting and asserting control over a geographic area. States exercise territoriality through borders, citizenship laws, immigration controls, and the monopoly on legitimate force within their borders (Max Weber's definition of the state).

**Maritime boundaries** are governed by the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS, 1982), which defines:

- **Territorial sea**: extends 12 nautical miles from the baseline (usually the low-water line along the coast). The coastal state has full sovereignty over this zone, including the airspace above and the seabed below.
- **Contiguous zone**: extends 12-24 nm from the baseline. The coastal state may enforce customs, fiscal, immigration, and sanitary laws.
- **Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ)**: extends 200 nm from the baseline. The coastal state has exclusive rights to explore, exploit, conserve, and manage natural resources in the water column, seabed, and subsoil. Other states retain freedom of navigation and overflight.
- **Continental shelf**: the natural prolongation of the land territory to the continental margin's outer edge (up to 350 nm or more). The coastal state has exclusive rights to seabed and subsoil resources.

**Enclaves** are territories entirely enclosed within another state's territory. True enclaves require crossing another state's territory to reach the rest of the home state. **Exclaves** are portions of a state geographically separated from the main part by surrounding foreign territory. A territory can be both (an exclave of one state that is an enclave within another). Notable examples include Baarle-Hertog/Baarle-Nassau (Belgian/Dutch border with nested enclaves and counter-enclaves) and the Cooch Behar district (India/Bangladesh, with over 100 enclaves and counter-enclaves, largely resolved by the 2015 Land Boundary Agreement).

## Key Concepts [Intermediate+]

**Geopolitics** examines how geographic factors -- location, resources, terrain, demography -- influence international relations and state power. The term has a fraught history: early geopoliticians like Halford Mackinder (1904) argued that control of the "Heartland" (the Eurasian interior) conferred global dominance, while Alfred Mahan (1890) argued for the primacy of sea power. These ideas influenced -- and were distorted by -- German and Japanese strategic thinking in the 1930s and 1940s, giving "geopolitics" an association with imperialism that lasted decades.

Modern critical geopolitics (Gearoid O Tuathail / Gerard Toal, 1996) examines how geographic knowledge is produced and used to justify political action. It analyzes the discourses through which states construct threats, define regions, and legitimize intervention. The framing of the Middle East as a "region of instability," for instance, is not a neutral geographic fact but a discursive construction with policy consequences.

**Boundary disputes** take several forms: **positional disputes** (disagreement over where exactly the border runs), **territorial disputes** (disagreement over who owns a particular area), **resource disputes** (disagreement over who has rights to resources near or across a border), and **functional disputes** (disagreement over how the border should be administered -- immigration, customs, transit).

**Disputed territories** -- selected cases:

- **Western Sahara**: Former Spanish Sahara, claimed by Morocco (which controls ~80% of the territory and has built a berm separating it from the Polisario Front-controlled area) and by the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic (recognized by ~40 states, mostly African and Latin American). A UN-supervised referendum on independence has been planned since 1991 but never held, primarily over disagreement on who qualifies to vote. - **Kashmir**: The former princely state of Jammu and Kashmir is divided among India (Jammu and Kashmir, Ladakh), Pakistan (Azad Kashmir, Gilgit-Baltistan), and China (Aksai Chin, Shaksgam Valley). India claims the entire former state; Pakistan contests India's control. China holds territory seized in the 1962 Sino-Indian War. The Line of Control between Indian and Pakistani-administered areas is one of the most militarized boundaries in the world. - **Taiwan**: The Republic of China (ROC) governed mainland China from 1912 and retreated to Taiwan in 1949 after the Chinese Civil War. The People's Republic of China (PRC) claims Taiwan as a province. The ROC operates with its own constitution, military, currency, and democratic elections. The international community takes various positions: most states officially recognize the PRC's claim while maintaining unofficial ties with Taiwan. The PRC pursues a "One China" principle; the ROC's major political parties differ on whether to maintain the status quo, pursue formal independence, or seek eventual unification.

- **Israel and Palestine**: Both Israelis and Palestinians claim historical and religious ties to the land between the Mediterranean Sea and the Jordan River. The State of Israel was established in 1948 in part of the former British Mandate of Palestine. The 1948 Arab-Israeli War and the 1967 Six-Day War produced the current territorial situation: Israel controls the entire area but the Palestinian Authority exercises limited self-governance in parts of the West Bank, and Hamas governs Gaza. Competing claims center on borders, the status of Jerusalem, Israeli settlements in the West Bank, the right of return for Palestinian refugees, and security arrangements. The international community supports various frameworks for resolution, most commonly a two-state solution, but the parties have not agreed on terms.

## Geographic Theory [Master+]

**Critical geopolitics** (O Tuathail, 1996; Agnew, 2002) reconceptualizes geopolitics as a set of discursive practices rather than a natural or objective analysis of geographic constraints on power. It draws on Foucault's concept of discourse and Said's Orientalism to examine how geographic knowledge is produced by institutions (military academies, think tanks, media organizations) and deployed to legitimize particular foreign policy positions. The "clash of civilizations" thesis (Huntington, 1996), for instance, is analyzed not as a geographic fact but as a discursive construction that shapes policy by dividing the world into reified cultural blocs.

**Territorial governance** theory examines the rescaling of political authority. The traditional Westphalian model assumes a nested hierarchy: local, regional, national. In practice, authority is increasingly dispersed across scales. The European Union exercises authority above the state; transnational corporations operate below and across state boundaries; indigenous land claims create forms of shared sovereignty; and global governance institutions (UN, WTO, ICC) exercise quasi-governmental functions without territorial control.

**Border studies** has moved from treating borders as fixed lines to analyzing them as processes and practices. The "bordering" perspective (Rumford, 2012; Parker and Vaughan-Williams, 2012) examines how borders are produced through everyday practices of surveillance, classification, and exclusion -- not just at the territorial margin but at airports, workplaces, hospitals, and online. Borders are understood as both material and discursive, operating simultaneously at multiple scales.

**Feminist political geography** (Sharp, 2007; Secor, 2001) examines how gender, sexuality, and embodiment intersect with territorial politics. This includes analyses of how nationalism is gendered, how border enforcement produces gendered vulnerabilities, and how the body itself becomes a site of political contestation (reproductive rights, military conscription, veiling laws).

## Historical Context [Master+]

The modern international state system traces to the Peace of Westphalia (1648), which ended the Thirty Years' War and established the principle of territorial sovereignty. However, Westphalian sovereignty has always been more aspiration than reality -- colonial empires, spheres of influence, and unequal treaties have continuously compromised it.

The 1884-1885 Berlin Conference formalized the European "Scramble for Africa," dividing the continent among colonial powers with minimal regard for existing ethnic, linguistic, or political boundaries. Many of Africa's current borders date to this conference, which is why many African states are multinational and why border disputes are frequent. The Organization of African Unity (1963) and its successor the African Union adopted the principle of *uti possidetis* -- preserving colonial borders upon independence -- to prevent endless territorial conflict, but this has frozen in place borders that cut across ethnic groups and ecological zones.

The 20th century saw the creation of numerous new states through decolonization (1940s-1970s), the dissolution of the Soviet Union and Yugoslavia (1991), and the partition of states (India/Pakistan, 1947; Israel/Palestine, 1948; Sudan/South Sudan, 2011). Each of these events involved territorial disputes, population transfers, and unresolved boundary questions.

The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS, concluded 1982, entered force 1994) established the modern framework for maritime boundaries, creating the EEZ concept and providing mechanisms for dispute resolution. The International Court of Justice and the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea adjudicate boundary disputes, though their rulings require state consent to be binding.
