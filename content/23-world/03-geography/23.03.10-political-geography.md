## Intuition [Beginner]

The Earth's land surface is divided into almost 200 countries, each claiming sovereignty over its territory. But borders are not natural features of the landscape -- they are human inventions. Some borders follow natural features like rivers or mountain ranges; others are straight lines drawn on a map by colonial administrators who had never visited the land. The study of how and why territory is divided, governed, and contested is **political geography**.

Three terms that people often confuse are **nation**, **state**, and **nation-state**. A **nation** is a group of people who share a common identity (language, culture, history). A **state** is a political unit with defined territory, a government, and sovereignty (the authority to govern itself). A **nation-state** is a state whose territory corresponds to a single nation -- Japan, for instance, where the Japanese nation and the Japanese state largely overlap. Many states are multinational (India contains dozens of distinct nations), and many nations are stateless (the Kurds, Palestinians, Tibetans).

A fourth important term is **sovereignty** -- the supreme authority of a state over its own territory and affairs, free from external interference. The modern concept of sovereignty dates to the Peace of Westphalia in 1648, which ended the Thirty Years' War in Europe and established the principle that each ruler had exclusive authority over their territory. This principle underlies the entire international system of states that exists today.

Political geography matters because the way territory is organized affects people's lives. Borders determine what laws you follow, what currency you use, whether you can travel freely, and whether your community is divided or united. Understanding political geography means understanding how power operates through space -- and how the maps we take for granted are products of specific historical and political choices, not natural or inevitable divisions of the Earth's surface.

The study of political geography also reveals how maps and geographic knowledge are themselves tools of power. The decision to draw a border in one location rather than another, to name a territory with one name rather than another, or to represent a disputed region as belonging to one state rather than another -- these are political acts with real consequences for the people who live in those places. Political geography asks us to question the maps we take for granted and to understand the power relations that produced them.

## Worked Example [Beginner]

The border between the United States and Canada west of the Great Lakes is a straight line along the 49th parallel of latitude. It was established by treaty between the US and Britain (which controlled Canada) in 1818. This is an **artificial border** -- it does not follow any physical feature of the landscape. People living on opposite sides of the line may share the same climate, the same terrain, and even the same economy, but they are citizens of different countries with different laws, taxes, and governments.

Contrast this with the border between Chile and Argentina along the Andes Mountains. This is a **natural border** -- it follows the mountain range that physically separates the two countries. Natural borders are not inherently better or worse than artificial ones, but they tend to create fewer practical problems because the physical barrier already limits interaction between the two sides. That said, natural borders can also create problems: rivers change course, mountain watersheds are difficult to survey, and communities on both sides may share economic and social ties that the border disrupts.

Some borders are deeply contested. **Western Sahara** is claimed by Morocco, which controls most of the territory, and by the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic, which is recognized by numerous states and controls a smaller portion. **Kashmir** is claimed by India, Pakistan, and China, each controlling different parts. **Taiwan** operates as an independent state with its own government, military, and economy, but the People's Republic of China claims it as a province. These disputes have different historical origins and different international legal statuses, but they all involve competing claims to the same territory. Understanding these disputes requires understanding how borders were drawn, who drew them, and what interests those borders serve.

## Formal Definition [Intermediate+]

**Political geography** is the study of how space is organized into political units and how geographic factors influence political processes. It examines the spatial dimensions of power: how states claim, organize, defend, and contest territory.

**Sovereignty** is the authority of a state to govern itself within its territory without external interference. The modern state system is commonly dated to the Peace of Westphalia (1648), which established the principle that each state has exclusive authority over its territory and domestic affairs.

**Territoriality** (Robert Sack, 1986) is the attempt by an individual or group to affect, influence, or control people, phenomena, and relationships by delimiting and asserting control over a geographic area. States exercise territoriality through borders, citizenship laws, immigration controls, and the monopoly on legitimate force within their borders (Max Weber's definition of the state).

**Maritime boundaries** are governed by the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS, 1982), which defines:

- **Territorial sea**: extends 12 nautical miles from the baseline (usually the low-water line along the coast). The coastal state has full sovereignty over this zone, including the airspace above and the seabed below.
- **Contiguous zone**: extends 12-24 nm from the baseline. The coastal state may enforce customs, fiscal, immigration, and sanitary laws.
- **Exclusive Economic Zone (EEZ)**: extends 200 nm from the baseline. The coastal state has exclusive rights to explore, exploit, conserve, and manage natural resources in the water column, seabed, and subsoil. Other states retain freedom of navigation and overflight.
- **Continental shelf**: the natural prolongation of the land territory to the continental margin's outer edge (up to 350 nm or more). The coastal state has exclusive rights to seabed and subsoil resources.

**Enclaves** are territories entirely enclosed within another state's territory. True enclaves require crossing another state's territory to reach the rest of the home state. **Exclaves** are portions of a state geographically separated from the main part by surrounding foreign territory. A territory can be both (an exclave of one state that is an enclave within another).

Notable examples include Baarle-Hertog/Baarle-Nassau (Belgian/Dutch border with nested enclaves and counter-enclaves that create a patchwork of jurisdictions at the street level) and the Cooch Behar district (India/Bangladesh, with over 100 enclaves and counter-enclaves, largely resolved by the 2015 Land Boundary Agreement). The existence of enclaves and exclaves demonstrates that borders are not always clean lines but can produce complex, fragmented territorial situations.

### State forms and territorial organization

States organize their internal territory in different ways. **Unitary states** concentrate political power in a central government, which may delegate authority to local governments but can also revoke it. France, Japan, and China are unitary states. **Federal states** divide power between a central government and constituent units (states, provinces, territories) whose authority is constitutionally protected. The United States, Germany, India, Brazil, and Australia are federal states.

The choice between unitary and federal organization often reflects geographic and demographic diversity. Large countries with varied terrain, multiple language groups, or regional economic differences tend to adopt federal systems to accommodate regional variation and prevent any single region from dominating the others. Small, culturally homogeneous countries tend to adopt unitary systems for efficiency of governance. However, these are tendencies rather than rules: China is a large, diverse country with a unitary system, while Switzerland is a small country with a federal system that reflects its multilingual and multi-confessional character.

**Devolution** -- the transfer of powers from a central government to regional governments -- has occurred in several formally unitary states. The United Kingdom has devolved significant powers to Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland while remaining formally unitary. Spain has created autonomous communities with varying degrees of self-governance. These arrangements reflect the political geography of multinational states where regional identities demand recognition and self-governance.

**Asymmetric federalism** describes arrangements where different regions within the same state have different levels of autonomy. In Spain, the Basque Country and Catalonia have greater fiscal autonomy than other autonomous communities. In India, Article 370 (now abrogated) gave Jammu and Kashmir special autonomous status. In Canada, Quebec has distinct civil law and language protections. These asymmetries reflect the geographic distribution of distinct national identities within multinational states and the political compromises necessary to hold such states together.

### Electoral geography

**Electoral geography** examines the spatial dimensions of elections and political representation. The drawing of electoral district boundaries (**gerrymandering**) is a direct exercise of political power through geographic manipulation. When the party in control of redistricting draws boundaries to maximize its own representation, it can translate a minority of votes into a majority of seats.

The United States provides prominent examples. The term "gerrymander" dates to 1812, when Massachusetts Governor Elbridge Gerry approved a district shaped like a salamander to benefit his party. Modern gerrymandering uses sophisticated GIS (Geographic Information Systems) technology to draw districts that pack opposition voters into a few districts (wasting their surplus votes) or crack them across many districts (diluting their concentration). The Supreme Court case *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019) ruled that partisan gerrymandering is a political question beyond federal court review, leaving regulation to individual states.

Electoral geography also examines the spatial patterns of voting behavior. Urban areas in most countries tend to support more progressive or left-leaning parties, while rural areas tend to support more conservative or right-leaning parties. This pattern has geographic causes: cities concentrate diverse populations, service economies, and educational institutions, while rural areas are more homogeneous and economically dependent on extractive industries and agriculture. The geographic distribution of voters interacts with districting to produce legislative outcomes that may not reflect the overall popular vote.

### The geography of conflict

Political geography examines how territory becomes a source and arena of conflict. **Territorial disputes** arise when two or more states claim sovereignty over the same area. **Irredentism** is a political movement that seeks to annex territory based on shared ethnic, cultural, or historical ties -- for example, Somali irredentism seeks to unite all ethnic Somalis in a single state, which would require taking territory from Ethiopia, Kenya, and Djibouti.

**Separatism** is the desire of a region or group to separate from an existing state and form a new one. Catalonia and the Basque Country in Spain, Scotland in the United Kingdom, Quebec in Canada, and Tamil Eelam in Sri Lanka are examples of separatist movements with geographic dimensions. The viability of a separatist state depends on its geographic characteristics: territorial coherence, economic self-sufficiency, access to the sea, and defensibility.

**Geopolitics** examines how geographic factors -- location, resources, terrain, demography -- influence international relations and state power. The term has a fraught history: early geopoliticians like Halford Mackinder (1904) argued that control of the "Heartland" (the Eurasian interior) conferred global dominance, while Alfred Mahan (1890) argued for the primacy of sea power. These ideas influenced -- and were distorted by -- German and Japanese strategic thinking in the 1930s and 1940s, giving "geopolitics" an association with imperialism that lasted decades.

Modern critical geopolitics (Gearoid O Tuathail / Gerard Toal, 1996) examines how geographic knowledge is produced and used to justify political action. It analyzes the discourses through which states construct threats, define regions, and legitimize intervention. The framing of the Middle East as a "region of instability," for instance, is not a neutral geographic fact but a discursive construction with policy consequences.

**Boundary disputes** take several forms: **positional disputes** (disagreement over where exactly the border runs), **territorial disputes** (disagreement over who owns a particular area), **resource disputes** (disagreement over who has rights to resources near or across a border), and **functional disputes** (disagreement over how the border should be administered -- immigration, customs, transit).

**Disputed territories** -- selected cases:

- **Western Sahara**: Former Spanish Sahara, claimed by Morocco (which controls ~80% of the territory and has built a berm separating it from the Polisario Front-controlled area) and by the Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic (recognized by ~40 states, mostly African and Latin American). A UN-supervised referendum on independence has been planned since 1991 but never held, primarily over disagreement on who qualifies to vote.

- **Kashmir**: The former princely state of Jammu and Kashmir is divided among India (Jammu and Kashmir, Ladakh), Pakistan (Azad Kashmir, Gilgit-Baltistan), and China (Aksai Chin, Shaksgam Valley). India claims the entire former state; Pakistan contests India's control. The Line of Control between Indian and Pakistani-administered areas is one of the most militarized boundaries in the world.

- **Taiwan**: The Republic of China (ROC) governed mainland China from 1912 and retreated to Taiwan in 1949 after the Chinese Civil War. The PRC claims Taiwan as a province. The ROC operates with its own constitution, military, currency, and democratic elections. Most states officially recognize the PRC's claim while maintaining unofficial ties with Taiwan.

- **Israel and Palestine**: Both Israelis and Palestinians claim historical and religious ties to the land between the Mediterranean Sea and the Jordan River. Israel was established in 1948 in part of the former British Mandate of Palestine. Competing claims center on borders, the status of Jerusalem, Israeli settlements in the West Bank, the right of return for Palestinian refugees, and security arrangements.

## Geographic Theory [Master+]

**Critical geopolitics** (O Tuathail, 1996; Agnew, 2002) reconceptualizes geopolitics as a set of discursive practices rather than a natural or objective analysis of geographic constraints on power. It draws on Foucault's concept of discourse and Said's Orientalism to examine how geographic knowledge is produced by institutions (military academies, think tanks, media organizations) and deployed to legitimize particular foreign policy positions. The "clash of civilizations" thesis (Huntington, 1996), for instance, is analyzed not as a geographic fact but as a discursive construction that shapes policy by dividing the world into reified cultural blocs.

**Territorial governance** theory examines the rescaling of political authority. The traditional Westphalian model assumes a nested hierarchy: local, regional, national. In practice, authority is increasingly dispersed across scales. The European Union exercises authority above the state; transnational corporations operate below and across state boundaries; indigenous land claims create forms of shared sovereignty; and global governance institutions (UN, WTO, ICC) exercise quasi-governmental functions without territorial control.

**Border studies** has moved from treating borders as fixed lines to analyzing them as processes and practices. The "bordering" perspective (Rumford, 2012; Parker and Vaughan-Williams, 2012) examines how borders are produced through everyday practices of surveillance, classification, and exclusion -- not just at the territorial margin but at airports, workplaces, hospitals, and online. Borders are understood as both material and discursive, operating simultaneously at multiple scales.

**Feminist political geography** (Sharp, 2007; Secor, 2001) examines how gender, sexuality, and embodiment intersect with territorial politics. This includes analyses of how nationalism is gendered, how border enforcement produces gendered vulnerabilities, and how the body itself becomes a site of political contestation (reproductive rights, military conscription, veiling laws).

**Postcolonial political geography** examines how colonial power relations persist in the territorial organization of the contemporary world. The borders, administrative structures, and economic geographies inherited from colonial rule continue to shape political possibilities in the Global South. Scholars in this tradition argue that the Westphalian state system was never truly universal -- it was imposed on colonized peoples through violence and coercion, and its contemporary reproduction through international law and institutions continues to serve the interests of powerful states at the expense of marginalized populations.

### Political geography of the city

Cities are sites of intense political geography. **Municipal boundaries** determine which government provides services and collects taxes. **Zoning laws** control what can be built where, shaping the spatial distribution of housing, industry, commerce, and public space. **Urban planning** is an exercise of political power through spatial organization, determining who lives where, who has access to amenities, and whose interests are served by development decisions.

The politics of urban space reflects and reinforces social inequalities. Wealthy neighborhoods receive better services (schools, parks, public transit) while poor neighborhoods are underserved. **Redlining** -- the practice of denying mortgages or insurance in neighborhoods based on racial or ethnic composition -- shaped the spatial organization of American cities in the 20th century, creating patterns of segregation and disinvestment whose effects persist decades after the practice was banned.

**The right to the city** (Henri Lefebvre, 1968; David Harvey, 2008) is a concept that asserts that all inhabitants, not just property owners or the wealthy, should have a say in how urban space is produced and managed. This concept has influenced social movements advocating for affordable housing, public space, transit equity, and participatory planning. The political geography of the city is ultimately about who has the power to shape the spaces where people live their daily lives.

### The colonial construction of borders

The borders of many countries in Africa, the Middle East, and South Asia were drawn by European colonial powers with minimal knowledge of or regard for local ethnic, linguistic, or political geography. The Berlin Conference of 1884-1885 formalized the "Scramble for Africa," dividing the continent among Britain, France, Germany, Belgium, Portugal, Spain, and Italy. The borders drawn at Berlin and in subsequent colonial agreements were geometric and administrative, designed to serve European strategic and economic interests rather than to reflect African political realities.

The consequences of these colonial borders persist. The Somali people are divided among five states (Somalia, Ethiopia, Kenya, Djibouti, and the disputed Somaliland region). The Yoruba are divided between Nigeria and Benin. The Azeri are split between Azerbaijan and Iran. The Kurdish homeland is divided among Turkey, Iraq, Iran, and Syria. In each case, a single ethnic group straddles an international border that they had no role in drawing, creating political tensions that have contributed to irredentism, separatism, and conflict.

The Organization of African Unity (1963) adopted the principle of *uti possidetis* -- preserving colonial borders upon independence -- to prevent endless territorial conflict as countries gained sovereignty. This pragmatic decision froze colonial borders in place, preventing border wars but also perpetuating the problems created by arbitrary boundary drawing. The African Union continues this policy, and most African borders remain unchanged from their colonial configurations.

### Territory and identity

The relationship between territory and identity is central to political geography. **Ethnonationalism** asserts that the nation (defined by shared ethnicity, language, or culture) should correspond to the state (defined by territorial sovereignty). This principle, known as the principle of national self-determination, was articulated by Woodrow Wilson after World War I and has been a powerful force in international politics.

However, the principle of national self-determination conflicts with the territorial integrity of existing states. Every state contains minority nations or ethnic groups, and granting self-determination to all of them would require redrawing every border on Earth. The international community has generally prioritized territorial integrity over self-determination, recognizing new states only in exceptional circumstances (the dissolution of existing states, as with the breakup of Yugoslavia and the Soviet Union).

The politics of recognition -- which groups are acknowledged as nations deserving self-determination and which are treated as minorities within existing states -- is inherently geographic. It involves mapping ethnic distributions, defining the territorial extent of national homelands, and drawing boundaries that include some people within the nation and exclude others. These maps and boundaries are not neutral descriptions of reality but political acts with consequences for the people they include and exclude.

### Resource geopolitics

Access to and control of natural resources is a major driver of geopolitical competition. **Oil geopolitics** has shaped international relations since the early 20th century, with the concentration of petroleum reserves in the Persian Gulf region creating strategic dependencies for importing nations. The 1973 oil embargo demonstrated the political power that resource control confers, and subsequent conflicts in the Gulf region (the Iran-Iraq War, the Gulf War, the 2003 Iraq War) have been shaped by oil geopolitics.

**Water geopolitics** is becoming increasingly important as climate change alters precipitation patterns and glacial melt. International river basins -- the Nile (11 riparian states), the Mekong (6 states), the Indus (India and Pakistan), the Jordan (Israel, Palestine, Jordan, Lebanon, Syria), the Tigris-Euphrates (Turkey, Syria, Iraq) -- are sites of potential conflict as upstream and downstream states compete for limited water resources. Turkey's dam construction on the Euphrates reduces downstream flow to Syria and Iraq; Ethiopia's Grand Renaissance Dam on the Nile has created tensions with Egypt, which depends on the Nile for nearly all its freshwater.

**Critical mineral geopolitics** is emerging as a new frontier. The transition to renewable energy requires lithium, cobalt, rare earth elements, and other minerals concentrated in specific geographic locations. The Democratic Republic of the Congo produces over 70% of the world's cobalt; China processes over 80% of rare earth elements; the "lithium triangle" of Bolivia, Chile, and Argentina holds over 50% of known lithium reserves. Control of these supply chains is becoming a source of geopolitical competition, with China, the United States, and the European Union competing for access and influence.

### Maritime geopolitics and the South China Sea

The South China Sea illustrates how maritime geography creates geopolitical competition. The sea is bounded by China, Taiwan, the Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia, Brunei, and Indonesia. It contains valuable fisheries, potential oil and gas reserves, and some of the world's busiest shipping lanes (over $3 trillion in trade passes through annually). China claims nearly the entire sea based on the "nine-dash line," a cartographic assertion that predates the modern EEZ framework. The Philippines, Vietnam, Malaysia, and Brunei claim portions that overlap with China's claim and with each other.

China has constructed artificial islands on disputed reefs and equipped them with military installations, extending its territorial control beyond what UNCLOS would permit. In 2016, the Permanent Court of Arbitration ruled in favor of the Philippines, finding that China's claims had no legal basis under UNCLOS. China rejected the ruling and continued its island-building and naval presence.

The dispute illustrates the tension between legal frameworks (UNCLOS) and power politics. China's geographic position, naval capabilities, and economic leverage allow it to assert control that exceeds its legal entitlements. The smaller claimant states lack the military capacity to challenge China directly and must rely on diplomatic and legal mechanisms whose enforcement depends on China's willingness to comply -- a willingness that has not been demonstrated.

### The geopolitics of climate change

Climate change is reshaping political geography in several ways. Rising sea levels threaten the territorial existence of low-lying island states (Tuvalu, Kiribati, the Marshall Islands, the Maldives), which could become uninhabitable within decades. The loss of inhabited territory would create an unprecedented situation: states without territory, populations without a homeland, and the need for new legal frameworks for climate-induced migration and statehood.

The melting of Arctic sea ice is opening new shipping routes and making Arctic resources (oil, gas, minerals, fisheries) more accessible. The five Arctic coastal states (Russia, Canada, the United States, Denmark/Greenland, and Norway) are positioning themselves to claim extended continental shelves under UNCLOS, while non-Arctic states (China, India, Japan) seek influence in Arctic governance. Russia has been the most aggressive in asserting Arctic territorial claims and militarizing its northern coastline.

Climate change is also expected to increase the frequency and severity of extreme weather events, droughts, and agricultural disruptions, which may generate migration pressures, resource competition, and political instability. The geographic distribution of climate impacts is uneven: the countries that have contributed least to greenhouse gas emissions (in the Global South) are generally the most vulnerable to their effects, creating questions of climate justice that have geopolitical dimensions.

### Colonialism and the political geography of extraction

The political geography of the Global South cannot be understood without reference to colonialism. Colonial states were organized to facilitate resource extraction: borders were drawn to give colonial powers access to mineral deposits, agricultural land, and ports; transportation networks were designed to move resources from interior to coast for export rather than to connect populations within the colony; and administrative structures were designed to maintain colonial control rather than to serve local populations.

Post-independence, many of these colonial structures persisted. The boundaries, transportation networks, and administrative systems inherited from colonial rule continue to shape the political geography of formerly colonized states. The concentration of economic activity in coastal cities (Lagos, Mumbai, Jakarta, Buenos Aires) reflects colonial-era investment in port infrastructure for export-oriented economies. The neglect of interior regions reflects colonial disinterest in areas without extractable resources.

The political geography of extraction also manifests in the contemporary global economy. Mining concessions, oil extraction zones, and industrial agriculture are concentrated in the Global South, often in regions inhabited by indigenous peoples and ethnic minorities who bear the environmental and social costs of extraction while receiving few of its benefits. The political geography of extraction is a geography of environmental racism and resource injustice.

### Indigenous sovereignty and territorial rights

Indigenous peoples' claims to territory challenge the Westphalian state system in fundamental ways. Indigenous conceptions of territory often emphasize relationship, stewardship, and collective responsibility rather than ownership, sovereignty, and exclusion. The recognition of indigenous land rights -- through treaties, court decisions, and international declarations (the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples, 2007) -- creates forms of shared sovereignty that do not fit neatly into the Westphalian framework.

In Australia, the Mabo decision (1992) overturned the doctrine of *terra nullius* (the legal fiction that Australia was unoccupied before British colonization) and recognized native title -- the recognition that indigenous Australians have pre-existing rights to land that survive colonization. In Canada, comprehensive land claims have created new forms of territorial governance, including the territory of Nunavut (created in 1999), where the Inuit-majority government exercises significant authority over a vast Arctic territory.

These developments represent partial and contested recognition of indigenous territorial rights. In many countries, indigenous land claims remain unresolved, and extractive industries continue to operate on indigenous territories without consent. The Standing Rock protests against the Dakota Access Pipeline (2016-2017) in the United States and the resistance of the Sami people to mining projects in northern Scandinavia are recent examples of conflicts over indigenous territorial sovereignty.

## Connections [Master]

This unit connects to the surrounding world sequence through prerequisites (none declared) and successors (none declared). In the wider Babel Bible map, political geography also links to mathematics when models, rates, maps, measurement, or incentives are involved; to philosophy when norms, rights, justice, or political legitimacy are at stake; and to language when categories, narratives, documents, and public arguments structure how people understand the domain. The cross-domain point is that social knowledge needs both evidence and interpretation.

Political geography connects to economics through the spatial distribution of resources, the geography of trade routes, and the economic implications of border regimes. The creation of free trade zones, customs unions, and economic blocs (the EU single market, NAFTA/USMCA, ASEAN) reconfigures the economic significance of political borders, creating territories where goods and capital move freely while people may not. The political geography of trade is a geography of inclusion and exclusion, where some spaces are integrated into global markets and others are marginalized.

The connection to international relations is fundamental. Territorial disputes, alliance systems, and geopolitical competition are core concerns of both fields. Political geography contributes to international relations by examining how geographic factors shape state behavior, how spatial discourses construct threats and opportunities, and how the material organization of territory enables and constrains political action.

The connection to environmental geography is increasingly important. Climate change is altering the geographic basis of state power: rising seas threaten territorial existence, changing precipitation patterns create water conflicts, and the energy transition is shifting the geography of strategic resources. The concept of **climate security** -- the recognition that environmental change can create political instability, migration, and conflict -- represents a new frontier in political geography.

Political geography connects to technology through the geography of surveillance, the politics of digital borders, and the spatial implications of cyberwarfare. States exercise territoriality not only through physical borders but through digital infrastructure: data localization laws, internet firewalls (China's Great Firewall), and the geographic distribution of data centers and undersea cables all represent exercises of political power through spatial control. The concept of **digital sovereignty** -- the assertion of state authority over data flows and digital infrastructure -- extends territorial logic into cyberspace.

The connection to demography is also significant. Population distribution, migration patterns, and demographic change have political geographic dimensions. The movement of people across borders -- whether voluntary migration, forced displacement, or climate-induced migration -- challenges state territoriality and creates political tensions about inclusion, citizenship, and national identity. The political geography of migration is a geography of borders, camps, detention centers, and the spatial management of human mobility.

## Historical Context [Master+]

The modern international state system traces to the Peace of Westphalia (1648), which ended the Thirty Years' War and established the principle of territorial sovereignty. However, Westphalian sovereignty has always been more aspiration than reality -- colonial empires, spheres of influence, and unequal treaties have continuously compromised it.

The 1884-1885 Berlin Conference formalized the European "Scramble for Africa," dividing the continent among colonial powers with minimal regard for existing ethnic, linguistic, or political boundaries. Many of Africa's current borders date to this conference, which is why many African states are multinational and why border disputes are frequent. The Organization of African Unity (1963) and its successor the African Union adopted the principle of *uti possidetis* -- preserving colonial borders upon independence -- to prevent endless territorial conflict, but this has frozen in place borders that cut across ethnic groups and ecological zones.

The 20th century saw the creation of numerous new states through decolonization (1940s-1970s), the dissolution of the Soviet Union and Yugoslavia (1991), and the partition of states (India/Pakistan, 1947; Israel/Palestine, 1948; Sudan/South Sudan, 2011). Each of these events involved territorial disputes, population transfers, and unresolved boundary questions.

The United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea (UNCLOS, concluded 1982, entered force 1994) established the modern framework for maritime boundaries, creating the EEZ concept and providing mechanisms for dispute resolution. The International Court of Justice and the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea adjudicate boundary disputes, though their rulings require state consent to be binding.

### Political geography of Antarctica

Antarctica represents a unique case in political geography. The Antarctic Treaty System (1959) reserves the continent for scientific research and prohibits military activity, nuclear testing, and mineral extraction. Seven states have claimed sectors of Antarctica (Argentina, Australia, Chile, France, New Zealand, Norway, and the United Kingdom), but these claims are suspended under the treaty and not universally recognized. The United States and Russia maintain the right to make future claims without having done so.

The governance of Antarctica through the treaty system rather than through territorial sovereignty is often cited as a model for international cooperation. However, the system reflects the power dynamics of the Cold War era in which it was negotiated, and the prohibition on mineral extraction (the Madrid Protocol, 1991) comes up for review in 2048. As climate change makes Antarctic resources more accessible and fishing pressures increase, the political geography of the continent may face new challenges.

### Supranational political geography

The late 20th and early 21st centuries have seen the rise of **supranational institutions** that exercise political authority above the level of the state. The European Union is the most developed example, with a parliament, court of justice, central bank, and common currency used by 20 of its 27 member states. The EU exercises real political authority: EU law takes precedence over national law in areas of EU competence, and member states have pooled sovereignty in trade, competition policy, environmental regulation, and other domains.

Other supranational organizations include the African Union (which has proposed a continental free trade area and a common passport), the Association of Southeast Asian Nations (ASEAN), the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC), and the Southern Common Market (Mercosur). These organizations vary in the degree of authority they exercise over member states, but all represent a partial reconfiguration of the Westphalian state system.

The political geography of supranationalism is complex. Member states retain sovereignty in most areas but surrender it in others. The geographic boundaries of supranational organizations do not coincide with any natural or cultural geography -- they are political constructs that expand (EU enlargement) or contract (Brexit) through negotiation and political decision. The coexistence of state sovereignty and supranational authority creates multi-level governance systems where political power is distributed across geographic scales.

### The partition of India

The partition of British India in 1947 is one of the most consequential border-drawing events in modern history. British lawyer Cyril Radcliffe was given five weeks to draw the border between India and the new state of Pakistan, based on religious demographics -- Muslim-majority areas would go to Pakistan, Hindu- and Sikh-majority areas to India. Radcliffe had never been to India before his appointment.

The partition displaced approximately 15 million people and killed between 1 and 2 million in the communal violence that followed. The border cut through Punjab and Bengal, regions with mixed religious populations and deep historical roots. Radcliffe's line separated villages, divided irrigation systems, and left some people on the "wrong" side of the border based on the religious composition of their district rather than their individual identity.

The geographic consequences of partition persist. India and Pakistan have fought four wars, three of them involving Kashmir. The border in Punjab remains heavily militarized. The administrative boundary Radcliffe drew has become one of the most dangerous borders in the world, with nuclear-armed states facing each other across a line that was drawn in less than five weeks by a man who had never seen the land he was dividing.

### The dissolution of the Soviet Union

The dissolution of the Soviet Union in 1991 created 15 new independent states and reconfigured the political geography of Eurasia. The internal administrative borders of the Soviet Union, drawn by Soviet planners with little regard for ethnic distributions, became international borders overnight. This created several problematic situations: large Russian-speaking populations found themselves in new states (Latvia, Estonia, Kazakhstan), previously internal conflicts became international (Nagorno-Karabakh between Armenia and Azerbaijan), and strategic assets (military bases, nuclear weapons, ports) were distributed among the new states.

The conflict between Russia and Ukraine illustrates the ongoing consequences of Soviet-era border drawing. Crimea was transferred from the Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic to the Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic in 1954 by Nikita Khrushchev, a decision that had little practical significance within the Soviet Union but became momentous when Ukraine became independent in 1991. Russia's annexation of Crimea in 2014 and the ongoing war in eastern Ukraine demonstrate how borders drawn as administrative conveniences can become sources of international conflict when the political context changes.

### Borders and the European Union

The European Union represents a fundamental reconfiguration of political geography. The Schengen Agreement (1985, fully implemented 1995) abolished internal border controls among participating states, allowing free movement of people, goods, and services. This created a new form of political territory -- a zone of free movement that is not a state but exercises some state-like functions.

At the same time, the EU has reinforced its external borders. Frontex, the European Border and Coast Guard Agency, coordinates border security among member states. The Mediterranean has become a deadly border for migrants and refugees attempting to reach Europe from Africa and the Middle East. The contrast between free internal movement and heavily policed external borders illustrates the double logic of political geography in the EU: integration within, exclusion without.

Brexit (2020) represented a reversal of this integration, as the United Kingdom left the EU and re-established a customs and regulatory border with the European Union. The geography of this border -- particularly the land border between Northern Ireland (part of the UK) and the Republic of Ireland (an EU member) -- proved extremely difficult to manage, given the political sensitivity of the Irish border and the history of the Northern Ireland conflict. The Northern Ireland Protocol, which keeps Northern Ireland aligned with EU customs rules to avoid a hard border on the island of Ireland, demonstrates how political geography operates at multiple scales simultaneously.

### The political geography of the Arctic

The Arctic is emerging as a significant zone of political geography. Climate change is melting sea ice at an accelerating rate, opening new shipping routes (the Northern Sea Route along Russia's coast, and potentially the Northwest Passage through the Canadian archipelago) and making underwater resources more accessible. The five Arctic coastal states (Russia, Canada, the United States, Denmark/Greenland, and Norway) are competing to extend their continental shelf claims under UNCLOS, which would give them exclusive rights to seabed resources.

Russia has been the most assertive Arctic actor, reopening Soviet-era military bases, building new icebreakers, and promoting the Northern Sea Route as an alternative to the Suez Canal for shipping between Asia and Europe. Canada and Denmark have submitted overlapping continental shelf claims to the Commission on the Limits of the Continental Shelf. The United States has not ratified UNCLOS but asserts Arctic interests through the Navy and Coast Guard.

The Arctic also raises questions of indigenous territorial rights. The Inuit Circumpolar Council represents Inuit peoples across Alaska, Canada, Greenland, and Russia, asserting indigenous rights to Arctic territory and resources. The creation of Nunavut in 1999 as a self-governing Inuit territory within Canada represents a significant exercise in indigenous political geography. The tension between state territorial claims and indigenous territorial rights is a defining feature of Arctic politics.

### Walls, fences, and fortified borders

Despite predictions that globalization would make borders less important, many states are building new border fortifications. The United States has expanded fencing and wall construction along its border with Mexico. India has built a fence along much of its border with Bangladesh. Hungary constructed a fence along its border with Serbia during the 2015 migration crisis. Saudi Arabia has built a barrier along its border with Iraq. Israel has constructed a separation barrier in the West Bank.

These structures represent the material dimension of bordering -- the physical manifestation of political decisions about who is included and who is excluded. Their effectiveness is debated: walls can deter casual crossing but are expensive to build and maintain, can be circumvented, and often redirect migration flows rather than stopping them. They also have humanitarian consequences, forcing migrants to take more dangerous routes and contributing to deaths at border zones.

The geography of border fortification reflects global inequalities. Rich countries build barriers to keep out people from poor countries. The borders being fortified are not between equals but between the Global North and the Global South, between wealthy and impoverished, between stable and conflict-affected regions. The political geography of border fortification is a geography of inequality.

### Satellite states, buffer zones, and spheres of influence

Not all political geography operates through formal borders. **Spheres of influence** are regions where a powerful state exercises significant political, economic, or military influence without formal sovereignty. During the Cold War, the Soviet Union maintained a sphere of influence in Eastern Europe through political pressure, military presence, and economic integration (the Warsaw Pact and Comecon). The United States maintained a sphere of influence in Latin America through the Monroe Doctrine, military interventions, and economic dominance.

**Buffer states** are countries that lie between more powerful rivals, providing geographic separation that reduces the risk of direct confrontation. Afghanistan served as a buffer between the British and Russian empires during the "Great Game" of the 19th century. Belgium was created as a buffer between France and Germany after the Napoleonic Wars. Mongolia serves as a buffer between Russia and China.

**Satellite states** are formally independent but politically dominated by a more powerful neighbor. The Soviet satellite states of Eastern Europe (Poland, East Germany, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, Bulgaria) had their own governments and flags but followed Soviet direction on major policy decisions. The concept of satellite states demonstrates that political geography operates through influence and dependency as much as through formal sovereignty and territorial control.

### Capital cities and political center-periphery relations

The location of **capital cities** is a significant element of political geography. Capitals are typically chosen for strategic, symbolic, or practical reasons. Many capitals are centrally located to provide equitable access to government (Madrid, Abuja, Brasilia, Islamabad -- all deliberately located away from the largest coastal cities to promote interior development and national integration). Others are located for historical or strategic reasons (London, Paris, Moscow -- all long-established centers of power).

Some countries have deliberately relocated their capitals. Brazil moved its capital from Rio de Janeiro to the newly built city of Brasilia in 1960 to develop the interior. Nigeria moved from Lagos to the purpose-built Abuja in 1991, partly to create a neutral location not associated with any ethnic group. Myanmar moved from Yangon to Naypyidaw in 2006, reportedly for strategic reasons related to military security. Tanzania is in the process of moving from Dar es Salaam to Dodoma. These relocations reflect political geography decisions about where the center of state power should be located.

**Center-periphery relations** describe the political and economic dynamics between a state's core (where political power, economic activity, and population are concentrated) and its periphery (marginalized regions with less political influence and economic development). In many countries, the capital region is disproportionately wealthy and powerful, while peripheral regions feel neglected. This geography of inequality fuels separatist movements (Catalonia's distance from Madrid, Scotland's distance from London, Chechnya's distance from Moscow) and demands for decentralization and regional autonomy.
