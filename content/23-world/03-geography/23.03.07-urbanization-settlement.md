## Intuition [Beginner]

For most of human history, people lived in rural areas -- small farming villages, pastoral camps, or scattered homesteads. Today, more than half the world's population lives in urban areas, and that fraction is still growing. Urbanization is the shift of population from rural to urban places. It happens for economic reasons (cities offer more jobs, especially in industry and services), social reasons (access to education, healthcare, entertainment), and because agricultural mechanization reduces the need for farm labor. Understanding how and why cities grow is essential for addressing the environmental, social, and economic challenges of the 21st century.

Not all cities grow the same way. Some explode into megacities (10 million+ people) like Tokyo, Delhi, and Lagos. Others grow outward in low-density sprawl that consumes farmland and requires cars to navigate. A megacity is not always a wealthy city -- some of the fastest-growing megacities are in developing countries where infrastructure struggles to keep up with population growth.

People choose where to settle based on two geographic concepts. **Site** refers to the exact physical characteristics of a place: its elevation, soil, water supply, and vegetation. **Situation** refers to a place's location relative to other places -- its connectivity to trade routes, ports, highways, and other cities. A city might have a poor site (flood-prone, hilly) but an excellent situation (at the junction of two major rivers), which explains why it grew anyway.

Settlements also differ in their spatial pattern. **Nucleated** (or clustered) settlements group buildings together around a central point -- a village green, a market square, a crossroads. **Dispersed** (or isolated) settlements spread buildings far apart, typical of farmsteads in agricultural regions. **Linear** settlements arrange buildings in a line along a road, river, or valley.

## Worked Example [Beginner]

Consider Lagos, Nigeria. In 1960, Lagos had about 760,000 people. By 2024, its metropolitan area held over 16 million. What drove this growth? Push factors: rural poverty in Nigeria's agricultural regions, limited services, environmental pressures in the north (drought and desertification). Pull factors: Lagos is Nigeria's economic center, its main port, and the hub of the country's oil, entertainment (Nollywood), and tech industries. Lagos has a strong situation -- located on the coast with a natural harbor -- but its site includes low-lying, flood-prone land. The city has sprawled outward, consuming surrounding wetlands and creating enormous traffic congestion. Despite these problems, people continue to arrive because economic opportunity outweighs the costs of poor infrastructure.

Now consider Istanbul, Turkey. Istanbul's site -- straddling the Bosporus strait on steep hills -- is challenging for construction and prone to earthquake risk. But its situation is extraordinary: it sits at the crossroads of Europe and Asia, controlling the passage between the Black Sea and the Mediterranean. This strategic location made it the capital of three empires (Roman, Byzantine, Ottoman) and continues to make it Turkey's largest city and economic engine. The lesson is that situation often matters more than site in determining which settlements grow into major cities.

## Formal Definition [Intermediate+]

**Urbanization** is the increase in the proportion of a population living in urban areas, measured as the urbanization rate (percentage urban). It is distinct from **urban growth** (the absolute increase in urban population) and from **urban sprawl** (the geographic expansion of urban land use at low densities). A country can experience urban growth without urbanization if its rural population grows at the same rate as its urban population.

The **rank-size rule** predicts that in a mature urban system, the population of the nth-ranked city equals 1/n times the population of the largest city. If the largest city has 10 million, the second should have 5 million, the third 3.3 million, and so on. Significant deviation -- where the largest city is far larger than predicted -- indicates a **primate city** pattern, common in countries where political and economic power is concentrated in one center (e.g., Paris in France, Bangkok in Thailand, Lima in Peru).

**Central place theory** (Christaller, 1933) explains the size, spacing, and distribution of settlements based on the range (maximum distance people will travel for a good or service) and threshold (minimum market size needed to sustain a business). Larger settlements provide higher-order goods and services (specialized medicine, universities, luxury retail) and are spaced farther apart; smaller settlements provide lower-order services (groceries, gas stations) and are spaced closer together.

### Urban land use models

Several classic models describe the internal structure of cities:

The **Burgess concentric zone model** (1925) posits that cities grow outward from a central business district in concentric rings: a commercial core, a transition zone of mixed industry and deteriorating housing, a zone of working-class homes, a zone of middle-class residences, and a commuter zone. This model was based on Chicago in the early 20th century and reflects the patterns of industrial cities with strong central business districts.

The **Hoyt sector model** (1939) modifies the concentric model by proposing that cities grow in wedge-shaped sectors radiating from the center, with high-rent residential areas extending along transportation corridors and industrial areas following rail lines and waterways. Low-rent areas tend to cluster near industry and deteriorate outward from there.

The **Harris and Ullman multiple nuclei model** (1945) recognizes that large cities do not have a single center but multiple nodes of activity: a central business district, but also satellite business districts, industrial zones, residential districts, and suburban centers, each attracting compatible land uses and repelling incompatible ones.

These models are useful starting points but have well-known limitations. They were developed for North American and European cities and may not apply well to cities in the Global South, where informal settlements, mixed land uses, and different cultural patterns produce different spatial structures. They also assume market-driven land allocation, neglecting the role of government planning, colonial legacies, and informal economies.

## Key Concepts [Intermediate+]

**Urban sprawl** is the outward expansion of low-density urban development into previously rural land. It is characterized by single-use zoning (separating residential, commercial, and industrial areas), automobile dependency, and fragmented governance across many small municipalities. Sprawl consumes agricultural land, increases infrastructure costs per capita, and generates higher per-capita carbon emissions than compact urban forms.

**Smart growth** is a set of planning principles intended to counter sprawl: compact building design, mixed land uses, walkable neighborhoods, a range of housing choices, preservation of open space, and development directed toward existing communities rather than greenfield sites. New Urbanism is a related design movement that promotes traditional neighborhood patterns (grid streets, mixed uses, public transit). These approaches have been implemented in developments like Seaside, Florida, and Poundbury, England, though critics note that some New Urbanist developments are expensive enclaves rather than the inclusive communities their founders envisioned.

The **gravity model** of spatial interaction predicts that interaction between two urban places is proportional to their populations and inversely proportional to the distance between them. This model, borrowed from physics, is widely used in urban geography, transportation planning, and retail location analysis.

**Counterurbanization** is the process by which people move from urban to rural areas, reversing the historic trend of rural-to-urban migration. This phenomenon has been observed in some wealthy countries (particularly in Europe and North America) where improvements in transportation and telecommunications have made it possible to live in rural areas while maintaining urban employment. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated counterurbanization trends, as remote work enabled some urban residents to relocate to smaller towns and rural areas. However, the scale of this movement has been modest relative to overall urbanization trends, and its long-term trajectory remains uncertain.

**Suburbanization** refers to the outward movement of population and employment from city centers to surrounding areas. In the United States, suburbanization was driven by government policies including Federal Housing Administration (FHA) mortgages (which favored new suburban construction over urban renovation), Interstate Highway construction (which made commuting from suburbs feasible), and tax deductions for mortgage interest (which subsidized homeownership, disproportionately benefiting suburban residents). These policies were racially discriminatory: the FHA refused to insure mortgages in Black neighborhoods (a practice called redlining), while subsidizing suburban development that was explicitly restricted to white buyers.

The racial geography of American cities today reflects these policies. Black households are concentrated in central-city neighborhoods that were denied investment, while white households are overrepresented in suburbs that benefited from federally subsidized mortgages and infrastructure. The wealth gap between Black and white Americans is largely a housing wealth gap, produced by decades of discriminatory policy that enabled white families to build equity in appreciating suburban homes while denying Black families the same opportunity.

### Urbanization in the Global South

The most dramatic urbanization of the 21st century is occurring in the Global South. Sub-Saharan Africa, still the least urbanized major region (about 44% urban in 2024), is urbanizing faster than any other. Cities like Lagos, Kinshasa, Dar es Salaam, and Nairobi are growing by 4-5% per year -- doubling every 15-18 years. This growth outpaces the capacity of governments to provide housing, water, sanitation, electricity, roads, schools, and healthcare.

The result is the proliferation of informal settlements -- areas built without official planning permission, often on marginal land (floodplains, steep slopes, areas adjacent to industrial zones or waste dumps), lacking basic infrastructure. The United Nations estimates that over 1 billion people live in informal settlements, and this number is projected to reach 3 billion by 2050 if current trends continue. These settlements go by many names: favelas (Brazil), bidonvilles (Francophone Africa), bustees (India), barrios (Venezuela), gecekondu (Turkey).

Informal settlements are often characterized as "slums," but this term can be misleading. Many informal settlements are vibrant communities with complex social structures, informal economies, and self-built housing that represents decades of incremental investment by residents. The problem is not that these communities lack social organization but that they lack formal recognition and the infrastructure investment that comes with it. Residents often lack legal tenure, making them vulnerable to eviction and unable to use their homes as collateral for loans. The term "slum" can also be used as a justification for demolition and clearance, treating communities as blights rather than as people's homes.

The **digital divide** within cities mirrors the physical divide. Access to high-speed internet, mobile phone coverage, and digital services is unevenly distributed, with low-income and marginalized communities having less access. This digital divide affects education, employment, healthcare, and civic participation, reinforcing existing spatial inequalities. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the shift to remote learning and telemedicine highlighted these disparities: children in informal settlements without internet access could not participate in online education, while those in wealthier neighborhoods continued learning with minimal disruption.

### Colonial urban legacies

Many cities in the Global South bear the imprint of colonial urban planning. Colonial cities were typically divided into a European quarter (with wide streets, parks, and modern infrastructure) and a native quarter (with narrow streets, inadequate infrastructure, and dense housing). This spatial segregation was not merely physical but also legal: different laws, different services, and different rights applied in each zone.

In some cases, colonial authorities built entirely new capital cities designed to project imperial power. New Delhi, designed by British architects Edwin Lutyens and Herbert Baker, was laid out with grand ceremonial avenues, government buildings, and spacious residential districts for colonial officials. The city was designed to impress and to facilitate control, with wide streets that allowed military parades and made barricade-building difficult. The contrast between planned New Delhi and the organic, dense fabric of Old Delhi remains visible today.

Post-independence, many former colonial cities retained their dual structure. The former European quarter became the wealthy core, occupied by the national elite and international businesses, while the former native quarters remained under-served. Urban planning traditions inherited from colonial regimes often prioritized modernist master plans that were inappropriate for local conditions, favoring automobile-oriented development in cities where most people walked or used public transit.

In many cases, colonial infrastructure was designed for extraction rather than development. Railroads connected mines and plantations to ports, not cities to each other. Roads were built for military and administrative purposes, not for local commerce. This infrastructure legacy continues to shape urbanization patterns: cities that grew as colonial administrative and export centers remain dominant, while the transportation networks that serve them were designed to move raw materials to ports, not to integrate national economies. Post-independence governments have struggled to retrofit this colonial infrastructure for development purposes.

### Gentrification and displacement

Gentrification is the process by which higher-income residents move into a previously low-income neighborhood, driving up property values and rents and displacing existing residents. It is associated with the redevelopment of inner-city areas that were previously disinvested, often neighborhoods with older housing stock and historically marginalized populations.

Gentrification is driven by both demand-side and supply-side factors. Demand-side factors include the preference of younger, educated workers for urban living (particularly in neighborhoods with historic character, cultural amenities, and proximity to employment centers). Supply-side factors include public policies that encourage redevelopment (tax incentives, zoning changes, infrastructure investment) and the availability of capital for real estate investment.

The geographic pattern of gentrification often follows a predictable sequence: artists and students move into a low-cost neighborhood, followed by young professionals, followed by real estate developers and luxury amenities. Each wave increases costs and displaces the previous population. The neighborhoods most vulnerable to gentrification are those with older housing, proximity to city centers or transit, and populations with limited political power to resist displacement -- often communities of color and immigrant communities.

**Climate gentrification** is an emerging phenomenon in which wealthy residents displace poorer residents from areas that are relatively safe from climate impacts. In Miami, high ground neighborhoods like Little Haiti, historically home to low-income communities, are experiencing development pressure because they are less vulnerable to sea level rise than low-lying coastal areas. The communities that contributed least to climate change are being displaced from the areas that offer the most protection from its effects.

## Geographic Theory [Master]

**World-systems theory** (Wallerstein, 1974) frames urbanization within a global hierarchy of core, semi-peripheral, and peripheral countries. Core countries export high-value goods and services; peripheral countries export raw materials and cheap labor. Urbanization in the periphery often takes a different form than in the core: rapid rural-to-urban migration outpaces industrial job creation, producing informal settlements (slums, favelas, bidonvilles) that house a substantial fraction of urban populations in the Global South. Mike Davis (2006) estimated that over 1 billion people lived in slums globally, a figure that has continued to rise.

**Planetary urbanization** (Brenner & Schmid, 2011) argues that the traditional urban/rural binary is obsolete. Urbanization is not merely the growth of dense settlements but a transformative process that reshapes entire territories: extraction frontiers (mines, oil fields), logistics networks (ports, highways, warehouses), and even seemingly rural areas are integrated into urban systems. This framework challenges the conventional definition of "urban" used by census agencies.

The **Alonso-Muth-Mills model** of urban land use predicts that land rents decrease with distance from the city center, producing concentric zones of different land uses (commercial core, mixed transition zone, residential suburbs, agricultural periphery). Critiques include its assumption of a monocentric city (most cities now have multiple employment centers), its neglect of institutional factors (zoning, racial segregation), and its foundation in neoclassical economics.

**Urban political ecology** examines how environmental conditions in cities are produced by political-economic structures. Access to clean water, green space, and clean air is unevenly distributed along lines of class, race, and political power. Flood risk in Lagos, heat islands in Phoenix, and air pollution in Delhi are not merely natural phenomena but outcomes of specific land-use decisions, infrastructure investments, and governance failures.

**Settlement geography** uses quantitative models (nearest-neighbor analysis, quadrat analysis, spatial autocorrelation) to classify and explain settlement patterns. These statistical tools distinguish nucleated, dispersed, and linear patterns objectively rather than impressionistically, and they enable comparison across regions and time periods.

### Urban systems and network theory

Cities do not exist in isolation; they form **urban systems** -- networks of cities connected by flows of people, goods, capital, and information. The structure of urban systems can be analyzed using network theory, which examines the pattern of connections between cities and the flows that pass through them. **World city network** theory (Taylor, 2004) analyzes how global cities are connected through the operations of multinational firms, creating a hierarchy of urban centers that function as command points in the global economy.

The concept of **global cities** (Sassen, 1991) identifies a small number of cities -- London, New York, Tokyo, and increasingly Shanghai, Dubai, and Singapore -- that serve as nodes in global financial and business networks. These cities are characterized not by size but by function: they concentrate the specialized services (finance, law, accounting, consulting) that facilitate global capital flows. The global city hierarchy is distinct from the urban size hierarchy; Zurich and Frankfurt are more important in global finance than much larger cities like Karachi or Manila.

**Megaregions** represent the next scale of urban organization above the individual city. The northeastern United States megalopolis (Boston to Washington, DC), the Pearl River Delta in China (Hong Kong-Shenzhen-Guangzhou), and the Tokyo-Nagoya-Osaka corridor in Japan each contain multiple metropolitan areas that are functionally integrated through commuting, supply chains, and communication networks. These megaregions contain hundreds of millions of people and generate a disproportionate share of global economic output.

### Urban sustainability and resilience

Urban sustainability requires meeting the needs of current residents without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. Cities consume about 75% of global energy and produce about 70% of global CO2 emissions, despite covering only about 3% of the Earth's land surface. The density that makes cities economically productive also creates environmental challenges: air pollution, water contamination, waste generation, and the urban heat island effect.

Urban resilience refers to the capacity of a city to absorb shocks and stresses -- including climate change impacts, natural disasters, economic crises, and pandemics -- while maintaining essential functions. Resilient cities have diversified economies, redundant infrastructure systems, strong social networks, adaptive governance, and the ability to learn from past events. The COVID-19 pandemic tested urban resilience worldwide, revealing stark differences between cities that managed the crisis effectively and those that did not.

The challenge of urban sustainability in the Global South is compounded by the pace of growth. Cities that will house hundreds of millions of additional residents by 2050 are being built today, and the decisions made about their form -- compact or sprawling, transit-oriented or car-dependent, green or grey infrastructure -- will lock in patterns of energy use, emissions, and livability for decades. Getting urbanization "right" in the Global South is therefore one of the most consequential challenges of the 21st century.

### Informality and the right to the city

The concept of the **right to the city** (Lefebvre, 1968) argues that urban space should be produced and managed collectively by its inhabitants, not merely by market forces and state authority. This idea has been influential in urban social movements worldwide, from the shack dwellers' movement (Abahlali baseMjondolo) in South Africa to the participatory budgeting movement that began in Porto Alegre, Brazil.

David Harvey (2008) extended Lefebvre's concept, arguing that the right to the city is not merely a right to existing urban space but a right to reshape the urbanization process itself. This framing highlights how urban redevelopment often serves the interests of capital at the expense of existing residents. When cities compete to attract investment by offering tax breaks, relaxed regulations, and infrastructure development, they often do so at the cost of affordable housing, public services, and democratic accountability.

Informal economies are a defining feature of urbanization in the Global South. The International Labour Organization estimates that about 2 billion workers worldwide are employed in the informal economy -- without legal protection, social security, or formal contracts. In many sub-Saharan African cities, the informal sector accounts for 80-90% of employment. Informality is not a temporary stage that will be eliminated by development; in many cities, it is the dominant form of economic activity, providing essential services (street food, transportation, construction, repair) that the formal sector cannot deliver at prices that most residents can afford.

The concept of **peripheral urbanization** (Caldeira, 2017) describes how urbanization in the Global South often occurs through the actions of residents themselves, building their own housing, creating their own infrastructure, and establishing their own governance structures, often in defiance of official plans and regulations. This autoconstruction produces urban forms that differ radically from the planned cities of the Global North: irregular street patterns, mixed uses, incremental building, and a constantly changing built environment. Rather than seeing this informality as a problem to be solved through formalization, peripheral urbanization theory suggests that it represents an alternative mode of urbanization that may be more appropriate to the conditions of the Global South than imported planning models.

### Urban transportation and spatial justice

Transportation systems shape urban form and access to opportunity. Cities built around public transit (subways, buses, trams) tend to be more compact and equitable than cities built around private automobiles, because transit provides mobility to those who cannot afford cars. The spatial distribution of transit investment -- which neighborhoods get subway stations, which get bus service, which get none -- reflects and reinforces existing patterns of inequality.

In many cities of the Global South, transportation is dominated by informal operators: minibuses (matatus in Kenya, tro-tros in Ghana, colectivos in Argentina), motorcycle taxis (boda bodas in East Africa, okadas in Nigeria), and cycle rickshaws. These systems fill the gap left by inadequate public transit, providing essential mobility to millions of people. They are often unsafe, unregulated, and polluting, but they are also the only affordable option for most residents. Attempts to formalize or replace informal transit often prioritize the interests of middle-class commuters over the needs of the urban poor who depend on informal services.

Urban highways have historically been used to reshape cities in ways that reinforced racial and class segregation. In the United States, the Interstate Highway System (1956 onward) was routed through Black neighborhoods in city after city, destroying homes, dividing communities, and facilitating white flight to the suburbs. The effects of these decisions are still visible: neighborhoods bisected by highways have higher pollution, lower property values, and worse health outcomes than comparable neighborhoods that were not dissected. Recent movements to remove urban highways (the "freeway revolts") represent an attempt to repair this damage.

### Housing and the right to shelter

Access to adequate housing is recognized as a human right under international law (Article 25 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, Article 11 of the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights). Yet an estimated 1.6 billion people worldwide live in inadequate housing, and about 100 million are homeless. The gap between the formal recognition of housing rights and the reality of housing conditions is one of the starkest failures of global governance.

In the Global South, the housing deficit is driven by rapid urbanization, low incomes, inadequate public investment, and land tenure systems that favor the wealthy. Formal housing markets produce units that are far too expensive for the majority of urban residents. In Nairobi, the cheapest formally built house costs about 15 times the average annual income; in Manila, about 20 times. The result is that most urban housing in the Global South is produced informally, by residents themselves, without building codes, permits, or professional design.

Evictions and forced displacement remain widespread. The Centre on Housing Rights and Evictions (COHRE) documented over 15 million forced evictions worldwide between 1998 and 2008, and the practice has continued. Evictions are often carried out to make way for urban redevelopment, infrastructure projects, or private investment, with insufficient compensation or resettlement for displaced residents. In some cases, cities clear informal settlements in advance of international events: Beijing before the 2008 Olympics, Delhi before the 2010 Commonwealth Games, Rio de Janeiro before the 2014 World Cup and 2016 Olympics.

### Smart cities and digital urbanism

The "smart city" concept uses digital technology and data analytics to improve urban management: sensors that monitor traffic flow and air quality, apps that help residents navigate public transit, platforms that enable citizen reporting of infrastructure problems. Proponents argue that smart city technology can make cities more efficient, sustainable, and responsive to residents' needs.

Critics raise several concerns. First, smart city initiatives often serve the interests of technology vendors and urban elites rather than ordinary residents. IBM, Cisco, and Siemens have been among the most aggressive promoters of smart city technology, framing urban problems as technical challenges amenable to technological solutions. This framing depoliticizes fundamentally political questions about whose needs the city should serve.

Second, surveillance is a core component of many smart city systems. Cameras, sensors, and data collection systems that monitor movement, energy use, and social activity create detailed records of urban life that can be used for social control. China's social credit system, which monitors and scores citizens' behavior, represents an extreme version of digital urbanism, but surveillance elements are present in smart city initiatives worldwide.

Third, the digital divide means that the benefits of smart city technology accrue disproportionately to the wealthy and connected. Residents without smartphones, internet access, or digital literacy -- who are disproportionately poor, elderly, and living in the Global South -- are excluded from the systems that smart cities build. A city that manages services through digital platforms risks becoming less accessible, not more, to its most vulnerable residents.

And it connects to environmental science through the ecological footprint of cities, urban heat islands, and the transformation of natural landscapes.

The study of settlement patterns also connects to history and archaeology, as the location and form of settlements reveal how past societies organized themselves in relation to their environments. The transition from nomadic to sedentary lifestyles, the emergence of the first cities, and the ongoing process of urbanization all represent fundamental shifts in the human relationship with space.

Urbanization also connects to climate science through the role of cities in greenhouse gas emissions and through the vulnerability of urban populations to climate impacts. Cities are both a major cause of climate change (through energy consumption, transportation, and industrial activity) and a major site of climate adaptation (through infrastructure investment, building codes, and emergency preparedness). The concept of the "carbon footprint" of cities connects urban planning to global climate policy.

The study of settlement geography connects to mathematics through spatial analysis techniques (nearest-neighbor analysis, spatial autocorrelation, fractal analysis of urban form) and through the geometric models used to describe settlement patterns (central place theory, rank-size distributions). These mathematical tools provide rigor and comparability, but they also risk reducing the richness of human settlement to abstract patterns that may obscure the lived experience of urban residents.

### Urban agriculture and food systems

Cities have always depended on food produced elsewhere, but the scale of urban food dependence has grown enormously. A city of 10 million people requires thousands of tons of food per day, transported from farms that may be thousands of kilometers away. This food system depends on global supply chains, fossil fuel-powered transportation, and refrigeration infrastructure -- all of which are vulnerable to disruption.

Urban agriculture -- the production of food within and around cities -- has gained attention as a strategy for improving food security, reducing food miles, and greening the urban environment. In cities like Havana, Cuba, urban agriculture produces a significant fraction of the city's food supply, partly as a legacy of the Special Period (1991-2000) when the collapse of Soviet trade forced Cuba to develop local food production. In African cities, urban farming is common but often unrecognized by planners, who may view it as a temporary or undesirable land use.

The concept of **food deserts** -- areas where residents lack access to affordable, nutritious food -- highlights the spatial dimension of food insecurity. In many cities, low-income neighborhoods have an abundance of fast food outlets and convenience stores but few or no grocery stores selling fresh produce. The geographic distribution of food access reflects patterns of investment and disinvestment that follow lines of race and class.

## Historical Context [Master]

The first cities emerged around 3500 BCE in Mesopotamia (Uruk, Ur), followed by the Indus Valley (Mohenjo-daro, Harappa), the Nile Valley (Memphis, Thebes), and the Yellow River valley (Erlitou). These early urban centers depended on agricultural surplus, which freed some inhabitants from food production to specialize in governance, craft production, trade, and religion. Defensive walls, monumental architecture, and writing systems are hallmarks of early urbanization.

Uruk, often considered the world's first true city, reached a population of about 40,000-80,000 by 2900 BCE. Its growth was enabled by irrigation agriculture in the fertile plains between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers. The city featured monumental temple complexes, a stratified social hierarchy, and a bureaucratic administration that managed the distribution of grain and labor. The emergence of writing in Uruk (cuneiform) is closely linked to the administrative needs of urban life: recording transactions, tracking grain stores, and managing labor.

In the Americas, urbanization developed independently. The Olmec cities of San Lorenzo and La Venta (1200-400 BCE) were among the earliest urban centers in Mesoamerica. Teotihuacan, near modern Mexico City, reached a population of about 100,000-200,000 by 450 CE, making it one of the largest cities in the world at that time. In the Andes, the Wari and Tiwanaku cultures developed urban centers that rivaled those of Mesoamerica in sophistication, though with very different spatial organization reflecting Andean cultural values.

The Roman Empire established a network of cities connected by roads, aqueducts, and administrative systems across Europe, North Africa, and the Near East. After Rome's fall, European urbanization declined sharply, with cities shrinking to a fraction of their imperial size. Urbanization revived in the medieval period (11th-14th centuries) driven by trade, particularly in Italy, the Low Countries, and the Hanseatic League.

The Industrial Revolution (late 18th to 19th century) produced the first massive wave of urbanization. Manchester grew from about 25,000 in 1770 to over 300,000 by 1850. London, Chicago, Berlin, and other industrial cities expanded rapidly, absorbing rural migrants and immigrants in conditions that were often appalling: overcrowded housing, contaminated water, epidemic disease, and exploitative labor. Public health reforms, housing regulations, and planning movements (Haussmann's Paris, the Garden City movement) emerged as responses.

The 20th century saw urbanization become a global phenomenon. In 1900, only about 14% of the world's population was urban. By 2000, it was 47%. The most dramatic urbanization occurred in the post-colonial developing world after 1950, where colonial economies had suppressed industrial urbanization and post-independence governments concentrated investment in primate cities.

Contemporary urbanization is dominated by the Global South. The UN projects that virtually all net population growth between 2020 and 2050 will be urban, and nearly all of that urban growth will occur in Africa and Asia. This raises fundamental questions about infrastructure, governance, housing, food systems, and environmental sustainability at a scale without historical precedent.

### The Garden City movement and its legacy

Ebenezer Howard's Garden City concept (1898) proposed building self-contained communities of about 30,000 people that combined the economic advantages of cities with the health and environmental benefits of the countryside. Garden cities would be surrounded by green belts, have mixed land uses, and be collectively owned. The first garden cities -- Letchworth (1903) and Welwyn (1920) in England -- were built according to Howard's principles.

The Garden City idea was enormously influential, but it was also distorted. In practice, the greenbelt and low-density principles were often adopted without the collective ownership and social equality that Howard intended. Suburban sprawl -- the antithesis of Howard's vision -- borrowed the garden city's low-density aesthetic while abandoning its social program. The New Towns movement in Britain (1946 onward) built over 30 new communities influenced by garden city principles, with mixed results.

In the Global South, planned capital cities represent a related tradition. Brasilia (built 1956-1960 to replace Rio de Janeiro as Brazil's capital), Chandigarh (designed by Le Corbusier as the capital of Punjab, India), and Abuja (built in the 1980s as Nigeria's capital) were all modernist planned cities intended to redirect national development away from overcrowded coastal primate cities. These planned capitals have had mixed success: they often attracted government functions and wealthy residents but struggled to develop the organic economic and cultural vitality of older cities.

### Urban renewal and its discontents

Urban renewal programs in the mid-20th century sought to transform declining inner-city areas through large-scale demolition and redevelopment. In the United States, the Housing Act of 1949 funded "slum clearance" that destroyed hundreds of thousands of housing units, disproportionately in Black neighborhoods. The displaced residents often had nowhere to go, and the new construction -- typically high-rise public housing or commercial development -- did not replace the affordable housing that was lost.

Jane Jacobs's *The Death and Life of Great American Cities* (1961) launched a devastating critique of urban renewal and modernist planning. Jacobs argued that the dense, mixed-use, walkable neighborhoods that planners condemned as "slums" were actually vibrant communities with complex social networks, diverse economic activities, and effective informal systems of safety and child-rearing. Her advocacy for bottom-up, community-based planning, mixed uses, short blocks, and density has become the foundation of contemporary urbanism.

### Post-colonial urbanization

After independence in the 1940s-1960s, many developing countries experienced explosive urban growth. Colonial restrictions on rural-to-urban migration were lifted, and newly independent governments invested in capital cities as symbols of modern nationhood. The result was a massive influx of rural migrants into cities that lacked the industrial base to absorb them into formal employment.

In Latin America, urbanization occurred rapidly in the mid-20th century, producing some of the world's largest cities (Mexico City, Sao Paulo, Buenos Aires). The region is now the most urbanized in the developing world (about 80% urban), but its cities are characterized by extreme inequality: gated communities and luxury high-rises alongside vast informal settlements with inadequate services. The favelas of Rio de Janeiro and Sao Paulo, the barrios of Caracas, and the pueblos jovenes of Lima are emblematic of this inequality.

In sub-Saharan Africa, urbanization is occurring later but faster. The continent's urban population is projected to triple by 2050, from about 600 million to nearly 1.5 billion. Whether African cities follow the Latin American pattern of unequal urbanization or develop more inclusive models depends on governance, investment, and the choices made in the coming decades.

### Urbanization and industrialization: the East Asian model

East Asia provides the most successful example of rapid urbanization combined with economic development. Japan's urbanization rate rose from about 38% in 1950 to about 78% by 1980, driven by export-oriented industrialization. South Korea followed a similar trajectory, urbanizing from about 20% in 1950 to over 80% by 2000. China's urbanization since 1978 has been the largest rural-to-urban migration in human history: from about 18% urban in 1978 to about 65% in 2024, involving the movement of over 500 million people.

The East Asian model differed from urbanization in Africa and Latin America in several key respects. Industrial job creation kept pace with rural-to-urban migration, providing formal employment to most new urban residents. Strong states invested heavily in urban infrastructure (housing, transportation, water, sanitation) and directed urban growth through planning. Export-oriented manufacturing generated the foreign exchange needed to import food and raw materials, decoupling urban growth from local agricultural capacity. High educational investment ensured that the urban workforce had the skills demanded by industry.

However, East Asian urbanization also had significant costs. Japan's rapid urban growth produced severe pollution in the 1960s-1970s (Minamata disease from mercury poisoning, Yokkaichi asthma from air pollution). South Korea's development concentrated population and investment in the Seoul metropolitan area, which now contains about half the national population and produces almost unbearable housing costs. China's urbanization involved the displacement of millions of rural residents, the destruction of traditional neighborhoods (hutongs in Beijing, lilong in Shanghai), and the construction of entire "ghost cities" -- urban developments built on speculation that stood empty for years before eventually being occupied.

China's **hukou** system (household registration) provides a unique example of state control over urbanization. Introduced in 1958, the hukou system classified every Chinese citizen as either rural or urban and restricted access to social services (education, healthcare, housing) to the place of registration. Rural migrants who moved to cities for work were denied access to urban public services, creating a vast floating population of second-class urban residents. The system has been gradually reformed since the 2010s, but its legacy of institutional inequality persists.

### Water, sanitation, and urban health

Access to clean water and sanitation is one of the most fundamental determinants of urban health and quality of life. The World Health Organization estimates that about 2 billion people worldwide lack access to safely managed drinking water, and about 3.6 billion lack access to safely managed sanitation. The vast majority live in urban areas of the Global South.

Inadequate water and sanitation are not merely technical failures but reflect political choices about investment, governance, and priorities. Cities that invest in universal water and sewer systems (as most wealthy countries did in the 19th and 20th centuries) dramatically reduce waterborne disease and improve overall health. But extending piped water and sewer networks to informal settlements is expensive and often politically contentious, because it requires recognizing the legitimacy of settlements that governments may prefer to ignore or clear.

The consequences of inadequate sanitation fall disproportionately on women and girls, who bear primary responsibility for water collection and household hygiene and who face safety risks when sanitation facilities are shared or distant. In cities like Dhaka, Kinshasa, and Port-au-Prince, women and girls may spend hours each day collecting water from shared taps or managing without adequate sanitation facilities, with cascading effects on education, economic participation, and health.

### Climate change and urban vulnerability

Climate change will reshape cities in profound ways. Sea level rise threatens coastal cities where hundreds of millions of people live. The IPCC estimates that about 1 billion people in low-lying cities and settlements are at risk from climate-related flooding by 2050. Cities like Mumbai, Shanghai, Miami, Jakarta, Lagos, and New York all face significant sea level rise exposure, with the impacts concentrated among low-income residents who live in the most vulnerable areas.

Urban heat is becoming a lethal threat. The urban heat island effect can add 2-5 C to daytime temperatures and 5-10 C to nighttime temperatures compared to surrounding rural areas. As global temperatures rise, urban heat waves are becoming more frequent and more intense. The 2003 European heat wave killed an estimated 70,000 people, mostly elderly urban residents. The 2015 Indian heat wave killed over 2,500 people, many of them outdoor workers in cities. As temperatures approach the limits of human physiological tolerance in some tropical cities, parts of the world may become uninhabitable without air conditioning -- creating a cruel feedback loop where the energy demand for cooling increases the warming that makes cooling necessary.

Informal settlements are particularly vulnerable to climate impacts. Built on marginal land (floodplains, steep slopes, areas adjacent to industrial zones), lacking durable construction, and often without drainage systems, these communities bear the brunt of extreme weather. When floods hit Mumbai, the poorest neighborhoods in low-lying areas are inundated first and deepest. When heat waves strike, residents of informal settlements with corrugated metal roofs and no green space experience the most extreme temperatures. Climate adaptation in cities must therefore center the needs of the most vulnerable residents, not merely protect infrastructure and property.
