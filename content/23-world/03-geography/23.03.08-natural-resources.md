## Intuition [Beginner]

A **natural resource** is anything from the natural environment that people use to survive or build wealth: water to drink, soil to grow food, oil to fuel machines, minerals to make tools, forests for timber and clean air. Resources are not spread evenly across the planet. Some regions are rich in oil, others in minerals, others in fertile farmland. This uneven distribution shapes everything -- which countries become wealthy, where conflicts break out, and how societies develop over time. Understanding resource geography therefore means understanding power, inequality, and the physical foundations of modern civilization.

The most important distinction is between **renewable** resources (ones that replenish naturally on human timescales -- water, sunlight, wind, forests if managed carefully) and **non-renewable** resources (ones that take millions of years to form and are finite -- oil, coal, natural gas, most minerals). Using any non-renewable resource means there is less of it available forever. The challenge of our era is building economies that rely more on renewable resources and less on finite ones.

Resources also differ in how they are distributed. Some, like sunlight and wind, are widely available but diffuse -- they require large areas of solar panels or wind turbines to capture meaningful amounts of energy. Others, like oil and mineral deposits, are highly concentrated in specific locations, creating the geopolitical leverage that comes with controlling a scarce resource. This difference in spatial distribution has profound implications for who benefits from resource wealth and who bears the costs of extraction.

## Worked Example [Beginner]

Nigeria is Africa's largest oil producer and has some of the continent's largest oil reserves. Yet per-capita income is relatively low, corruption is widespread, and many Nigerians live in poverty despite billions of dollars in oil revenue. This is the **resource curse** -- also called the "paradox of plenty." Understanding why resource wealth does not automatically translate into broad-based development is one of the central questions of resource geography.

The resource curse happens when a country has valuable natural resources but fails to translate that wealth into broad-based development. The mechanism works like this: oil revenues flow to the government directly, so leaders do not need to tax citizens broadly. Without taxation, citizens have less leverage to demand accountability. Elite groups compete to control resource revenues rather than building productive industries. The national currency appreciates because of resource exports, making other exports (manufacturing, agriculture) less competitive -- a phenomenon called "Dutch disease," named after the Netherlands' experience after discovering natural gas in the 1960s.

Not all resource-rich countries fall into this trap. Norway, for example, created a sovereign wealth fund that invests oil revenues for future generations, maintained strong institutions, and diversified its economy. The difference is not the resource itself but the institutions that manage it. Botswana, which discovered diamonds after independence in 1966, also avoided the resource curse through competent governance, investment in education, and careful management of mining revenues, becoming one of Africa's most stable and prosperous countries. These cases demonstrate that the resource curse is not inevitable but contingent on institutional quality and political will.

## Formal Definition [Intermediate+]

**Natural resources** are materials and energy sources occurring in the natural environment that have economic value. They are classified along several dimensions:

- **By renewability**: Renewable resources regenerate on human timescales (solar radiation, wind, biomass). Non-renewable resources exist in finite stocks formed over geological time (fossil fuels, metallic ores). Some resources are conditionally renewable -- they renew if extraction does not exceed the regeneration rate (groundwater, fish stocks, timber).
- **By excludability**: Resources may be rivalrous and excludable (private goods like mineral deposits), rivalrous but non-excludable (common-pool resources like ocean fisheries), or non-rivalrous and non-excludable (public goods like clean air).
- **By economic use**: Energy resources (fossil fuels, uranium, wind, solar), material resources (minerals, timber, water for industry), and biological resources (crops, fisheries, livestock).

**Resource distribution** follows geological, climatic, and ecological patterns. Petroleum deposits are concentrated in sedimentary basins where organic material was buried and subjected to heat and pressure over millions of years -- the Persian Gulf, Gulf of Mexico, West Africa, North Sea, and Siberia. Metallic ores concentrate along tectonic plate boundaries, ancient volcanic arcs, and cratonic regions with prolonged mineralization history.

The **resource curse** (Sachs and Warner, 1995; Ross, 2012) is a statistically documented correlation between resource wealth (particularly point-source resources like oil and minerals) and poor economic outcomes, authoritarian governance, and civil conflict. Proposed mechanisms include Dutch disease, rent-seeking, institutional degradation, and the financing of insurgency through resource looting.

**OPEC** (Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries), founded in 1960, coordinates petroleum production among member states to influence global oil prices. Members include Algeria, Angola, Congo, Equatorial Guinea, Gabon, Iran, Iraq, Kuwait, Libya, Nigeria, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Venezuela. OPEC's ability to set prices has varied with member cohesion and the rise of non-OPEC production (US shale, Russian output).

**Water scarcity** is measured by the Falkenmark indicator (renewable freshwater per capita per year): greater than 1,700 m3 = no stress; 1,000-1,700 m3 = water stress; 500-1,000 m3 = water scarcity; below 500 m3 = absolute scarcity. By 2025, an estimated 1.8 billion people will live in countries or regions with absolute water scarcity. Water scarcity is not merely a physical phenomenon but a social one: it results from the interaction of limited supply with growing demand, unequal distribution, and inadequate infrastructure. Some of the most water-scarce countries (Saudi Arabia, UAE) use energy-intensive desalination to produce freshwater, while some water-rich countries (Democratic Republic of Congo) lack the infrastructure to deliver clean water to their populations.

### The energy transition

The transition from fossil fuels to renewable energy is one of the most significant resource shifts in human history. Fossil fuels (coal, oil, natural gas) have powered industrial civilization for 250 years, but their combustion produces carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases that are warming the planet. The Paris Agreement (2015) commits signatories to limiting warming to well below 2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels, which requires reaching net-zero CO2 emissions by about 2050-2070.

The energy transition creates new resource geographies. Solar and wind energy require no fuel but need specific materials: silicon for solar panels, rare earth elements for wind turbine magnets, lithium and cobalt for batteries. These materials are geographically concentrated -- lithium in the "lithium triangle" of Chile, Argentina, and Bolivia; cobalt in the Democratic Republic of Congo; rare earths primarily in China. The transition away from fossil fuel dependence could create new dependencies on critical minerals.

The geopolitics of energy is shifting accordingly. Countries that control critical mineral supplies (China's dominance of rare earth processing, Australia's lithium production) gain strategic leverage, similar to the leverage that oil-producing countries have held since the 1970s. The competition to secure access to these minerals is sometimes called the "new great game," echoing the 19th-century competition for influence in Central Asia.

Renewable energy technologies are decentralizing energy production in ways that have geopolitical implications. Solar panels and wind turbines can be deployed at many scales, from rooftop systems to utility-scale farms, reducing dependence on the concentrated fossil fuel infrastructure (pipelines, refineries, tanker fleets) that has shaped global power relations for a century. Countries that currently depend on fossil fuel exports -- Russia, Saudi Arabia, Nigeria, Venezuela -- face the prospect of declining demand for their primary export, requiring economic diversification that may be politically and socially difficult.

### Fossil fuel divestment and stranded assets

The fossil fuel divestment movement, which began on university campuses in the early 2010s, argues that institutions should sell their holdings in fossil fuel companies because the business model of those companies is incompatible with the goal of limiting global warming. By 2023, institutions representing over $40 trillion in assets had committed to some form of fossil fuel divestment.

A related concept is **stranded assets** -- fossil fuel reserves that will not be burned if the world succeeds in limiting warming to 2 degrees Celsius. The Carbon Tracker Initiative estimated in 2011 that about 80% of declared fossil fuel reserves would be unburnable under a 2-degree scenario, implying that fossil fuel companies were significantly overvalued. This "carbon bubble" argument suggests that financial markets have not priced in the possibility that climate policy will make much of the fossil fuel industry's asset base worthless.

The implications for fossil fuel-producing countries in the Global South are significant. Countries like Nigeria, Angola, Iraq, and Venezuela that depend on oil exports for government revenue face the prospect of declining demand and falling prices before they have diversified their economies. Some of these countries have barely begun to benefit from their fossil fuel reserves, raising questions of fairness: the countries that industrialized first (using fossil fuels) are now calling for an end to the fossil fuel era before latecomers have had the same opportunity.

### Resource mapping and GIS

Modern resource management relies heavily on Geographic Information Systems (GIS) and remote sensing. Satellite imagery enables monitoring of deforestation, mining activity, oil spills, and land use change at global scales. The Landsat program (operating since 1972) provides a continuous record of the Earth's surface at 30-meter resolution, enabling analysis of environmental change over five decades. More recent satellite systems (Sentinel, Planet Labs) provide higher resolution and more frequent revisits.

GIS-based resource mapping enables governments and companies to identify resource deposits, plan extraction operations, and monitor environmental impacts. However, access to resource data is often restricted. Mining companies treat geological survey data as proprietary, and some governments restrict access to resource maps for security reasons. The Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative (EITI), launched in 2002, promotes disclosure of payments from oil, gas, and mining companies to governments, but participation is voluntary, and many resource-rich countries have not joined.

Citizen science and community mapping have emerged as tools for communities affected by resource extraction to document environmental damage and advocate for remediation. In the Niger Delta, communities have used GPS and photography to map oil spills and gas flares, providing evidence that contradicts official accounts of environmental conditions. In the Amazon, indigenous communities use GPS and satellite imagery to monitor deforestation and report illegal logging on their lands.

### Water as a strategic resource

Water is becoming the defining resource challenge of the 21st century. While water covers 71% of the Earth's surface, only about 2.5% is freshwater, and of that, about two-thirds is locked in glaciers and ice caps. The accessible freshwater available for human use is less than 1% of the total. This tiny fraction must supply drinking water, agricultural irrigation (which accounts for about 70% of global freshwater withdrawals), industrial processes, and ecosystem needs.

Water scarcity is worsening due to population growth, urbanization, pollution, and climate change. Many major aquifers -- underground water stores that took millennia to fill -- are being depleted faster than they can recharge. The Ogallala Aquifer beneath the US Great Plains, which supplies irrigation water for about one-fifth of US agricultural production, has declined by about 30% since the 1950s in some areas. The groundwater beneath the North China Plain, which supports wheat and corn production for hundreds of millions of people, is falling by about 1 meter per year.

Transboundary water resources create geopolitical tension. About 60% of the world's freshwater flows across international boundaries in 310 river basins shared by two or more countries. Upstream nations control the flow; downstream nations are vulnerable. Ethiopia's Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam on the Nile, which began filling in 2020, has created tension with Egypt and Sudan, both of which depend on the Nile for agriculture and drinking water. Turkey's dams on the Tigris and Euphrates have reduced flow to Iraq and Syria. China's dam construction on the Mekong affects downstream nations Laos, Cambodia, Thailand, and Vietnam.

### The tragedy of the commons and its critics

Garrett Hardin's "The Tragedy of the Commons" (1968) argued that resources held in common (pastures, fisheries, the atmosphere) are inevitably degraded because individuals have incentives to overuse shared resources even when collective restraint would produce better outcomes for all. Hardin's solution was either privatization (assigning property rights) or state regulation.

Elinor Ostrom's work, which won the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2009, challenged Hardin's pessimistic conclusion. Through extensive field research, Ostrom demonstrated that communities around the world have successfully managed common-pool resources -- irrigation systems, fisheries, forests, grazing lands -- through self-governance institutions. These institutions typically involve well-defined boundaries, rules matched to local conditions, collective decision-making, monitoring, graduated sanctions, conflict resolution mechanisms, and recognition by external authorities.

Ostrom's work is relevant to resource geography because it shows that resource management outcomes depend not only on the physical characteristics of the resource but on the institutional arrangements governing its use. The same fishery can be sustainably managed under one institutional arrangement and devastated under another. The same forest can be preserved by community governance or destroyed by state concession to logging companies.

### Agricultural resources and food security

Agricultural resources -- arable land, freshwater for irrigation, soil fertility, and genetic crop diversity -- are fundamental to human well-being. The Food and Agriculture Organization estimates that about 12 million hectares of agricultural land are degraded or lost each year through soil erosion, salinization, nutrient depletion, and urbanization. At the same time, global food demand is projected to increase by about 50% by 2050 due to population growth and dietary changes.

The Green Revolution of the 1960s-1980s dramatically increased agricultural yields through high-yielding crop varieties, synthetic fertilizers, pesticides, and irrigation. However, these gains came at environmental costs: groundwater depletion, soil degradation, loss of crop genetic diversity, and dependence on fossil fuel-derived inputs. The challenge of feeding a growing population while maintaining the resource base is one of the central problems of environmental geography.

**Land grabs** -- large-scale acquisitions of agricultural land in the Global South by foreign governments and corporations -- emerged as a major concern after the 2007-2008 food price crisis. Countries with limited arable land (Gulf states, China, South Korea) and agribusiness corporations acquired or leased millions of hectares in Africa, Southeast Asia, and Latin America to produce food for export back to the investing country. These acquisitions often displaced smallholder farmers and pastoralists, converting land that had supported local food production to export-oriented agriculture. The International Land Coalition documented over 1,000 large-scale land deals covering more than 40 million hectares between 2000 and 2016.

## Key Concepts [Intermediate+]

**Hubbert's peak theory** predicts that production from a finite resource follows a bell curve: it rises, peaks, and then declines irreversibly. M. King Hubbert correctly predicted US oil production would peak around 1970. Global "peak oil" projections have been repeatedly revised as technology (deepwater drilling, hydraulic fracturing) shifts the production curve, but the finite nature of the resource remains.

**Resource geopolitics** examines how geography and resources intersect with power. Control over oil routes (Strait of Hormuz, Strait of Malacca, Suez Canal), pipeline networks, and critical minerals supply chains are central concerns. The concentration of rare earth element processing in China, for instance, creates strategic vulnerability for electronics and defense industries elsewhere.

**Ecological footprint analysis** measures human demand on natural capital -- how much land and water area a population requires to produce the resources it consumes and absorb the wastes it generates. Humanity's ecological footprint has exceeded Earth's biocapacity since the early 1970s, meaning we are drawing down natural capital stocks rather than living on annual yields.

### Resource extraction and the Global South

The history of resource extraction in the Global South is deeply intertwined with colonialism. European colonial powers extracted raw materials -- gold, silver, rubber, timber, minerals, agricultural products -- from their colonies, shipping them to Europe for processing and consumption. This extraction-oriented economic structure persisted after independence: many post-colonial countries continue to export raw materials and import manufactured goods, reproducing the colonial division of labor.

Mining in the Global South often operates with lower environmental and labor standards than in the Global North, partly because governments competing for foreign investment lower standards to attract mining companies, and partly because enforcement capacity is limited. The result is environmental damage that disproportionately affects local communities -- often indigenous peoples who were never consulted about mining on their traditional lands.

The concept of **ecological unequal exchange** (Hornborg, 1998; Bunker, 1985) describes how the Global North's high consumption levels depend on the net transfer of resources and waste absorption capacity from the Global South. When a ton of copper is mined in Peru, processed in China, and manufactured into electronics in the United States, the environmental costs of extraction (water pollution, habitat destruction, community displacement) remain in Peru, while the economic value of the finished product accrues in the United States. This spatial separation of costs and benefits is a defining feature of the global resource economy.

The concept of the **ecological debt** owed by industrialized nations to the Global South follows from this analysis. Proponents argue that centuries of resource extraction, combined with disproportionate greenhouse gas emissions, have created a debt that wealthy nations owe to the countries whose resources and atmospheric space they have consumed. While this debt is not recognized in international law, it has influenced negotiations on climate finance, technology transfer, and loss and damage compensation.

The Democratic Republic of Congo illustrates these dynamics. The DRC has some of the world's richest mineral deposits, including cobalt (essential for electric vehicle batteries), copper, diamonds, gold, and coltan (used in electronic devices). Yet the country remains one of the poorest in the world, and mineral wealth has financed decades of conflict. Artisanal mining -- small-scale, often unregulated extraction by individual miners -- employs millions of Congolese but involves dangerous working conditions, child labor, and environmental contamination. Major technology and automotive companies that depend on Congolese cobalt face increasing pressure to ensure their supply chains do not involve exploitation.

### Indigenous land and resource rights

Indigenous peoples have historically been displaced from resource-rich lands, often with the complicity or active participation of the state. In many countries, mineral rights belong to the national government regardless of who holds surface land rights, enabling mining on indigenous territories without meaningful consent. The concept of **Free, Prior, and Informed Consent** (FPIC), enshrined in the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (2007), requires that indigenous communities be consulted and give consent before resource development occurs on their lands. Implementation of FPIC remains uneven.

In Canada, the duty to consult indigenous peoples on resource development was established by Supreme Court decisions (Haida Nation v. British Columbia, 2004) and has become a significant factor in pipeline and mining approvals. The conflict over the Dakota Access Pipeline (2016) in the United States, which the Standing Rock Sioux Tribe argued threatened their water supply and sacred sites, drew international attention to the intersection of indigenous rights, resource extraction, and environmental justice.

In Australia, the Mabo decision (1992) overturned the doctrine of terra nullius and recognized native title, providing indigenous Australians with legal grounds to claim traditional lands -- including lands with mineral resources. However, the interaction between native title and mining rights remains contested, with many indigenous communities arguing that consultation processes are superficial and that the economic benefits of mining rarely flow equitably to affected communities.

### The green resource paradox

The energy transition from fossil fuels to renewables creates a paradox: the technologies needed to decarbonize the global economy require mining on a massive scale. Electric vehicles, solar panels, wind turbines, and battery storage all require materials that must be extracted from the Earth. The International Energy Agency projects that reaching net-zero emissions by 2050 would require a sixfold increase in critical mineral production by 2040.

This creates tension between environmental goals in the Global North and environmental and social impacts in the Global South, where many critical minerals are found. Lithium extraction in the Atacama Desert of Chile and Bolivia uses enormous quantities of water in one of the world's driest regions, affecting indigenous communities who depend on scarce water supplies. Cobalt mining in the DRC involves hazardous conditions and child labor. Nickel processing in Indonesia has caused deforestation and water pollution.

The concept of **sacrifice zones** describes areas where environmental damage is accepted as the cost of producing resources for distant consumers. Historically, sacrifice zones were associated with fossil fuel extraction (coal mining regions, oil refinery communities). The energy transition risks creating new sacrifice zones in mining regions of the Global South, reproducing the same patterns of environmental injustice under a green banner.

## Geographic Theory [Master]

Resource geography intersects with several theoretical traditions. **Ostensian location theory** (after Edgar Osten, expanded by Losch and Isard) models the spatial economics of resource extraction, processing, and consumption. The **staples thesis** (Harold Innis, 1930s) argues that resource-exporting economies develop institutional structures shaped by the characteristics of their staple commodities -- bulky, perishable resources create different political economies than compact, durable ones.

The **political ecology** framework (Blaikie and Brookfield, 1987; Bryant and Bailey, 1997) examines how access to and control over resources is shaped by power relations at multiple scales. It rejects the apolitical framing of "resource scarcity" by showing that scarcity is often produced through enclosure, privatization, and exclusion rather than absolute physical shortage. The Gujarat water crisis, for instance, is not a function of low rainfall but of groundwater extraction policies, cash-crop incentives, and caste-based access structures.

**Resource conflicts** are analyzed through the greed vs. grievance debate (Collier and Hoeffler, 2004). The "greed" hypothesis holds that lootable resources (alluvial diamonds, timber, drugs) finance and incentivize rebellion, making conflict more likely and longer-lasting. The "grievance" hypothesis holds that resource exploitation generates injustice and inequality, which motivates rebellion. Empirical evidence supports elements of both: resource wealth correlates with conflict onset, but only under conditions of weak governance, ethnic polarization, and low income.

The concept of **peak everything** (Heinberg, 2007) extends peak oil analysis to phosphorus, rare earths, arable land, and other critical inputs, raising questions about the material throughput requirements of continued economic growth. The **circular economy** model (Pearce and Turner, 1990; Ellen MacArthur Foundation) proposes closing material loops to decouple economic activity from virgin resource extraction.

### Extractivism and post-extractivism

**Extractivism** refers to economic models based on the large-scale extraction of natural resources for export. It is associated with Latin American dependency theory and the critique that resource extraction enriches multinational corporations and national elites while leaving local communities with environmental damage and social disruption. Eduardo Gudynas (2013) distinguishes between progressive extractivism (where resource revenues fund social programs, as in Evo Morales's Bolivia) and conservative extractivism (where revenues accrue to private interests). Both forms, critics argue, share the same fundamental problem: dependence on non-renewable resources and the environmental damage that extraction entails.

**Post-extractivism** proposes economic models that reduce dependence on resource extraction through diversification, value-added processing, and a shift toward renewable resources and knowledge-based economic activities. Achieving post-extractivism is politically difficult because resource revenues fund government budgets and social programs, particularly in countries where alternative economic sectors are underdeveloped. The commodity boom of 2003-2014, driven by Chinese demand for raw materials, reinforced extractivist models across Latin America, Africa, and Central Asia.

### The resource curse debate

The resource curse thesis has been extensively debated. Early cross-national statistical studies (Sachs and Warner, 1995) found a strong negative correlation between resource dependence and economic growth. However, subsequent research has challenged this finding on several grounds. The correlation weakens when different time periods are examined, when different measures of resource dependence are used, and when institutional quality is controlled for. Some scholars argue that the causal direction is reversed: weak institutions cause both resource dependence (because countries with weak institutions fail to diversify their economies) and poor outcomes.

The **institutional quality** argument, advanced by Mehlum, Moene, and Torvik (2006), proposes that the resource curse operates only in countries with "grabber-friendly" institutions, where elites can capture resource rents for personal enrichment. In countries with "producer-friendly" institutions (strong rule of law, transparent governance, checks on executive power), resource wealth can support development. This argument is supported by the success of Botswana, Norway, and Chile -- resource-rich countries with relatively strong institutions that have managed resource revenues effectively.

The **political** dimension of the resource curse extends beyond economic growth. Oil wealth, in particular, correlates with authoritarian governance, reduced civil liberties, and increased conflict risk. The mechanism is straightforward: oil revenues free governments from the need to tax citizens broadly, weakening the social contract that obliges governments to be responsive to citizen demands. Oil-rich governments use resource revenues to fund security forces, subsidize consumption, and co-opt opposition, reducing the pressure for democratic reform.

### Resource nationalism

**Resource nationalism** refers to the assertion of national sovereignty over natural resources, typically through nationalization of foreign-owned extractive operations, increased taxation or royalties on resource companies, or requirements for local processing and local content. Resource nationalism has occurred in waves: the nationalization of oil in Mexico (1938), Iran (1951), and the Arab OPEC states (1970s); the nationalization of copper in Chile (1971) and Zambia (1970); and more recent examples in Bolivia (gas, 2006), Ecuador (oil, 2010), and Tanzania (mining, 2017).

Resource nationalism reflects the legitimate aspiration of resource-rich countries to control their own wealth. However, its effects depend on implementation. Countries with strong state capacity (Norway, Chile) have managed state resource companies effectively. Countries with weak governance have seen nationalized industries become sources of patronage, corruption, and inefficiency. Venezuela's nationalization of oil under Hugo Chavez, for example, initially increased social spending and reduced poverty, but the failure to invest in maintenance and new production capacity led to a dramatic decline in oil output, contributing to the country's economic collapse.

### Sovereign wealth funds

**Sovereign wealth funds** (SWFs) are state-owned investment funds that invest resource revenues in diversified assets, saving wealth for future generations and buffering the economy from commodity price volatility. Norway's Government Pension Fund Global, funded by petroleum revenues, is the world's largest SWF at over $1.4 trillion. It invests globally, is transparently managed, and follows ethical guidelines that exclude companies involved in weapons production, tobacco, and serious environmental damage.

Other resource-rich countries have established SWFs with varying degrees of success. The Gulf states (Abu Dhabi, Kuwait, Qatar) have large SWFs, but their transparency and governance standards vary. Botswana's Pula Fund, capitalized by diamond revenues, is often cited as an African success story. Many resource-rich countries, however, lack the institutional framework to manage SWFs effectively, and resource revenues are spent on current consumption rather than saved or invested productively.

### Critical minerals and the energy transition

The shift from fossil fuels to renewable energy requires a different set of minerals. The International Energy Agency estimates that a typical electric car requires six times the mineral inputs of a conventional car, and an onshore wind plant requires nine times more mineral resources than a gas-fired power plant. The minerals most critical to the energy transition include lithium, cobalt, nickel, copper, rare earth elements, graphite, and manganese.

The geographic concentration of these minerals creates strategic vulnerabilities. China processes about 60-70% of the world's lithium, cobalt, and rare earths. The Democratic Republic of Congo produces about 70% of the world's cobalt. Chile and Australia dominate lithium production. Indonesia is the largest nickel producer. This concentration means that trade restrictions, political instability, or environmental disasters in a small number of countries could disrupt the entire global energy transition.

The concept of **critical minerals** reflects this strategic dimension. Governments in the United States, European Union, Japan, and other major economies have published lists of minerals deemed critical for economic security and the energy transition, and have developed policies to diversify supply sources, increase domestic production, promote recycling, and invest in alternative technologies that reduce dependence on scarce materials.

The relationship between resources and power is bidirectional. Resources create power (oil wealth funds military and diplomatic influence), and power determines who controls resources (military strength enables resource capture). This bidirectionality means that resource geography cannot be understood apart from the political and economic structures that determine how resources are extracted, processed, traded, and consumed.

Resource consumption patterns vary enormously between the Global North and the Global South. The average resident of the United States consumes about 25 times more energy than the average resident of Nigeria. The richest 10% of the global population is responsible for about 50% of global carbon emissions, while the poorest 50% is responsible for about 12%. These disparities mean that discussions of resource scarcity and environmental sustainability must address not only the total demand for resources but also the extremely unequal distribution of consumption.

### Forests as global resources

Tropical forests cover about 6% of the Earth's land surface but contain over 50% of terrestrial species and store about 25% of the carbon in terrestrial vegetation. They also regulate regional rainfall patterns: the Amazon rainforest generates about half of its own rainfall through evapotranspiration, and deforestation threatens to disrupt this cycle, potentially pushing parts of the Amazon toward a tipping point where large areas transition from rainforest to savanna.

Deforestation in the tropics is driven primarily by commercial agriculture (cattle ranching in the Amazon, palm oil plantations in Southeast Asia, soybean production in South America), logging (both legal and illegal), and small-scale shifting cultivation. Global demand for beef, soy, palm oil, and timber drives these activities. Brazil, Indonesia, and the Democratic Republic of Congo account for the majority of tropical deforestation.

The concept of **REDD+** (Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation) was introduced in international climate negotiations as a mechanism for wealthy countries to pay tropical countries to preserve their forests, on the basis that forest conservation provides global climate benefits. However, REDD+ has been criticized for enabling wealthy countries to continue emitting while paying for carbon offsets, for displacing indigenous communities from forests that they have sustainably managed for centuries, and for the difficulty of verifying that deforestation would not have occurred without the payments (the "additionality" problem).

Indigenous peoples manage or hold tenure over about 25% of the world's land surface, including about 40% of all terrestrial protected areas and ecologically intact landscapes. Research consistently shows that indigenous-managed lands have lower deforestation rates than comparable areas managed by governments or private actors. Despite this evidence, indigenous communities are often excluded from forest governance decisions and face criminalization for practicing traditional land management.

### Urbanization and resource demand

Urbanization is transforming the geography of resource demand. Cities consume about 75% of global energy and produce about 70% of global CO2 emissions. As urban populations grow, particularly in the Global South, the demand for construction materials (cement, steel, sand, gravel), energy, water, and food is increasing dramatically.

Sand and gravel are the most extracted materials in the world by volume -- about 40-50 billion tons per year, far exceeding the extraction of fossil fuels or minerals. Sand is essential for construction (concrete, glass, asphalt), and demand has tripled in the last two decades, driven by urbanization in Asia and the Middle East. Sand mining from rivers and coastlines causes erosion, habitat destruction, and increased flood risk. In some regions, sand mafias -- criminal organizations that illegally mine and sell sand -- have become a significant problem.

## Historical Context [Master]

The geopolitics of resources has shaped world history. European colonial expansion was driven substantially by the pursuit of resources -- spices, gold, rubber, oil, timber. The Scramble for Africa (1884-1914) was motivated in part by control over mineral and agricultural resources. Japan's expansion into Southeast Asia in World War II was driven by oil and resource needs.

The 1973 oil crisis -- when OPEC embargoed oil exports to nations supporting Israel in the Yom Kippur War -- demonstrated that resource control could be wielded as a geopolitical weapon, quadrupling oil prices and triggering recessions in industrialized nations. The 1979 Iranian Revolution caused a second oil shock. These events reshaped energy policy worldwide, spurring investment in alternative energy, strategic petroleum reserves, and energy efficiency.

The post-Cold War period saw "resource wars" in Africa: diamonds financed conflicts in Angola, Sierra Leone, and Liberia (the "blood diamonds" or "conflict diamonds" problem, addressed by the Kimberley Process Certification Scheme, 2003). Coltan (columbite-tantalite) financed armed groups in the eastern Democratic Republic of Congo. Water disputes have intensified as populations grow and climate changes -- tensions over the Nile (upstream dam construction by Ethiopia), the Mekong (Chinese dam construction), the Tigris-Euphrates (Turkish dam projects), and the Indus (India-Pakistan tensions) all have geopolitical dimensions.

The concept of the "resource curse" was formally articulated by Richard Auty (1993) and statistically documented by Sachs and Warner (1995). It has been critiqued on methodological grounds (the correlation weakens when different time periods or different resource measures are used) and on causal ones (it may be that weak institutions cause both resource dependence and poor outcomes, rather than resources causing institutional weakness).

### Colonial resource extraction

The modern global resource economy was built on colonial foundations. Spain extracted vast quantities of silver from mines in Potosi (modern Bolivia) and Zacatecas (Mexico) during the 16th-18th centuries, financing the Spanish Empire and flowing through global trade networks to China and Europe. The silver mines were worked by indigenous labor under the brutal mita system, a form of forced labor that caused enormous suffering and population decline.

King Leopold II of Belgium's personal colony in the Congo (1885-1908) was perhaps the most extreme case of resource extraction through violence. The colony's initial wealth came from ivory and wild rubber, harvested under a quota system enforced by mutilation and murder. The population of the Congo Free State declined by an estimated 10 million people during Leopold's rule. After international outcry forced Leopold to relinquish personal control, the Belgian government continued to extract mineral wealth, including uranium (used in the atomic bombs dropped on Hiroshima and Nagasaki), copper, and diamonds.

In Southeast Asia, the Dutch East India Company and later the Dutch colonial government established plantation systems for spices, rubber, and oil palm in Indonesia. The British did the same in Malaya (rubber), India (cotton, jute, tea), and East Africa (tea, coffee). These plantation systems transformed landscapes, displaced indigenous communities, and created economies oriented toward export rather than local needs. The environmental and social legacies of plantation agriculture persist in land tenure conflicts, deforestation, and economic inequality throughout the Global South.

### Oil and the modern Middle East

The discovery of oil in the Middle East transformed the region's political economy. The first major commercial oil discovery in the region was in Iran in 1908, by the Anglo-Persian Oil Company (later British Petroleum). Saudi oil was discovered in 1938 by American companies (later ARAMCO). Iraq, Kuwait, the UAE, and other Gulf states followed.

The initial effect of oil discovery was to deepen colonial control, as Western powers sought to secure access to the newly valuable resource. The 1953 coup that overthrew Iranian Prime Minister Mohammad Mossadegh, who had nationalized the Anglo-Iranian Oil Company, was orchestrated by the CIA and MI6 to restore Western control over Iranian oil. The coup installed the Shah, whose authoritarian rule lasted until the 1979 Iranian Revolution.

After independence, oil revenues transformed the Gulf states. Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, and Kuwait used oil wealth to build modern infrastructure, provide generous social services to citizens (though not to the migrant workers who constitute the majority of the labor force), and accumulate sovereign wealth funds. However, oil dependence also created vulnerabilities: these economies are heavily exposed to oil price fluctuations, their political systems remain authoritarian, and the economic model of trading energy exports for imported goods and labor is unsustainable in the long term as the world transitions away from fossil fuels.

### The Niger Delta and the environmental costs of oil

The Niger Delta, the oil-producing region of Nigeria, illustrates the environmental and social costs of resource extraction in the Global South. Nigeria has produced over 2.5 billion barrels of oil since the 1950s, generating hundreds of billions of dollars in revenue. Yet the Niger Delta remains one of the poorest and most polluted regions in Nigeria. An estimated 13 million barrels of oil have been spilled in the delta since oil operations began -- the equivalent of roughly one Exxon Valdez spill every year for 50 years.

Oil spills have contaminated waterways, destroyed fisheries, damaged agricultural land, and caused health problems for local communities. Gas flaring -- the burning of natural gas associated with oil extraction -- releases toxic pollutants and greenhouse gases. Despite regulations requiring the end of gas flaring, Nigeria flares more gas than almost any other country. The environmental damage has devastated the livelihoods of communities that depend on fishing and farming.

The Ogoni people of the Niger Delta, led by writer and activist Ken Saro-Wiwa, organized against Shell's operations in the 1990s, accusing the company of environmental destruction and the Nigerian government of collaborating with Shell against local interests. Saro-Wiwa and eight other activists were executed by the Nigerian military government in 1995 after a widely condemned trial, drawing international attention to the delta's plight. The movement for environmental justice in the Niger Delta continues, with groups like the Movement for the Emancipation of the Niger Delta (MEND) using both political organizing and, at times, sabotage of oil infrastructure to demand resource control and environmental remediation.

### Fishing and ocean resources

Ocean resources represent a critical and increasingly contested domain. About 3.3 billion people depend on marine resources for at least 20% of their animal protein intake. Global fish catch peaked in the mid-1990s and has since stagnated or declined despite increasing fishing effort, indicating that many fish stocks are overexploited. The Food and Agriculture Organization estimates that about 34% of assessed fish stocks are fished beyond sustainable levels.

Overfishing is driven by a combination of factors: technological improvements (larger vessels, GPS, sonar) that increase fishing capacity; subsidies that keep fishing fleets operating even when stocks decline; illegal, unreported, and unregulated (IUU) fishing, which accounts for an estimated 20% of global catch; and the open-access nature of much of the ocean, which creates incentives to catch fish before competitors do.

Distant-water fishing fleets, primarily from China, Taiwan, South Korea, and Spain, operate far from their home waters, often targeting the EEZs of West African, Pacific Island, and South American nations. These fleets pay licensing fees to host governments but often exceed their quotas, engage in illegal fishing, and compete with local artisanal fishers. In West Africa, where many countries lack the naval capacity to monitor their EEZs effectively, distant-water fleets have depleted fish stocks that coastal communities depend on for food and livelihoods.

Deep-sea mining represents an emerging frontier in ocean resource extraction. The International Seabed Authority, established under UNCLOS, regulates mining in international waters (the "Area"), which contains polymetallic nodules, cobalt-rich crusts, and seafloor massive sulfides rich in manganese, nickel, copper, and cobalt. Proponents argue that deep-sea mining could supply minerals needed for the energy transition with less social disruption than terrestrial mining. Critics warn that mining could cause irreversible damage to deep-sea ecosystems that are poorly understood and extremely slow to recover.
