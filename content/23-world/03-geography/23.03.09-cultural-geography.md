## Intuition [Beginner]

Culture is the collection of beliefs, practices, languages, customs, and ways of life shared by a group of people. **Cultural geography** studies how culture varies across space and how place shapes culture. Different parts of the world speak different languages, practice different religions, eat different foods, build different kinds of houses, and organize their societies differently -- and these differences are not random. They reflect history, environment, migration, trade, conquest, and the movement of ideas.

Language and religion are two of the most powerful cultural forces. There are roughly 7,000 languages spoken in the world today, but they cluster into a much smaller number of **language families** -- groups of languages that share a common ancient ancestor. Similarly, the world's major religions are not spread uniformly; they have distinct geographic patterns shaped by where they originated and how they spread.

## Worked Example [Beginner]

English is spoken as a first language by roughly 380 million people, primarily in the United States, United Kingdom, Canada, Australia, and Ireland. But it is spoken as a second language by over a billion more, making it the most widely used **lingua franca** in history -- a language used for communication between people who do not share a native language.

How did this happen? English originated in England as a Germanic language (Anglo-Saxon), absorbed vocabulary from Norse invaders and Norman French conquerors, then spread globally through British colonial expansion from the 17th through 20th centuries. After World War II, American economic and cultural dominance reinforced English as the language of international business, science, aviation, and the internet. Today, a business meeting between a Japanese executive and a Brazilian engineer is most likely conducted in English -- not because either person is from an English-speaking country, but because it is the shared bridge language.

This pattern is not unique to English. Arabic served as a lingua franca across the Islamic world for centuries. Swahili functions as a lingua franca across East Africa. Mandarin serves this role in China, where hundreds of mutually unintelligible local languages exist. Latin was the lingua franca of Western European scholarship and the Catholic Church for over a thousand years.

## Formal Definition [Intermediate+]

**Cultural geography** is the branch of human geography that studies spatial variations in cultural phenomena and the relationships between culture and place. Key areas of study include language geography, religious geography, ethnic geography, and the cultural landscape.

**Language families** are groups of languages related through descent from a common ancestral language (proto-language). The comparative method reconstructs proto-languages by identifying systematic sound correspondences among descendant languages. Major families include:

- **Indo-European**: The largest family by number of speakers, spanning Europe, Iran, and South Asia. Major branches include Germanic, Romance, Slavic, Indo-Iranian, Celtic, and Hellenic. The proto-language (Proto-Indo-European) was likely spoken in the Pontic-Caspian steppe around 4,000-2,500 BCE.
- **Sino-Tibetan**: The second-largest family, spanning China, Myanmar, and the Himalayas. Sinitic (Chinese) languages and Tibeto-Burman languages are the two major branches.
- **Afro-Asiatic**: Spans North Africa, the Horn of Africa, and the Middle East. Major branches include Semitic (Arabic, Hebrew, Amharic), Berber, Chadic (Hausa), Cushitic, and Omotic.
- **Niger-Congo**: The largest family in Africa by number of languages (~1,500). The Bantu branch expanded across much of sub-Saharan Africa beginning around 3,000 BCE, spreading agriculture and iron-working.
- **Austronesian**: The most geographically widespread family before European colonial expansion, spanning from Madagascar to Easter Island. The expansion began from Taiwan around 5,000-3,000 BCE.

**Religious geography** examines the spatial distribution of religions, their patterns of diffusion, and the imprint of religion on the landscape. Religions are classified as **universalizing** (seeking adherents globally -- Christianity, Islam, Buddhism) or **ethnic** (associated with a particular group and place -- Hinduism, Judaism, Shinto). Sacred spaces, pilgrimage routes, religious architecture, and dietary laws all create distinctive cultural landscapes.

**Cultural diffusion** is the spread of cultural elements from one group or area to another. **Relocation diffusion** occurs when people physically carry a cultural trait to a new place (e.g., immigrants bringing their cuisine). **Expansion diffusion** occurs when a trait spreads outward from its origin while remaining strong there -- this includes hierarchical diffusion (spreading from nodes of power or large cities), contagious diffusion (spreading through direct contact), and stimulus diffusion (the underlying idea spreads but is adapted locally).

A **lingua franca** is a language systematically used for communication between people who do not share a native language. Historically important lingua francas include Akkadian, Aramaic, Greek (Koine), Latin, Arabic, Swahili, Sanskrit, and English. The term itself comes from "Frankish language," a pidgin used in Mediterranean trade during the Crusades.

## Key Concepts [Intermediate+]

**Ethnicity vs. nationality**: Ethnicity refers to a shared cultural identity based on common ancestry, language, religion, or history (e.g., Kurds, Basques, Tamils). Nationality refers to legal citizenship in a state (e.g., Turkish, Spanish, Indian). The two often do not align -- the Kurds are an ethnic group of ~30 million spanning Turkey, Iraq, Iran, and Syria, but they have no sovereign state. A **nation-state** exists when a state's territory corresponds closely to a single ethnic nation (e.g., Japan, Iceland).

**The cultural landscape** (Carl Sauer, 1925) is the visible imprint of human activity on the natural landscape. Barn styles, field patterns, settlement forms, place names, and religious structures all encode cultural information. Sauer argued that the cultural landscape is the central object of geographic study, shaped by the interaction of natural environment and cultural group. The concept has been extended to include "ordinary landscapes" (Peirce Lewis, 1979) and has influenced heritage preservation policy through the UNESCO World Heritage Convention.

**Sequent occupance** (Derwent Whittlesey, 1929) describes how successive cultural groups leave their imprint on a landscape over time. Jerusalem's Old City contains layers from Jewish, Roman, Byzantine, Islamic, Crusader, Ottoman, and modern Israeli periods -- each group built on, modified, or replaced the cultural landscape of its predecessors.

1. The Bantu Expansion is one of the largest migration events in human history, spreading Bantu languages, agriculture, and iron technology across sub-Saharan Africa beginning around 3,000 BCE. How does this illustrate the relationship between language geography, economic practice, and migration?

2. Place names (toponyms) reveal cultural history. Consider: Minnesota (Dakota Sioux), San Francisco (Spanish saint's name), New York (English, previously Nieuw Amsterdam under Dutch rule), Allahabad (Mughal-era, renamed Prayagraj). What layers of sequent occupance do these names reveal?

## Geographic Theory [Master+]

Cultural geography has been reshaped by several theoretical interventions. **New cultural geography** (Cosgrove and Jackson, 1987; Duncan, 1990) moved beyond Sauer's Berkeley School approach by drawing on cultural studies, literary theory, and post-structuralism. It treats culture not as a bounded, coherent system of traits but as a contested field of meaning production, emphasizing representation, power, and discourse.

**Non-representational theory** (Thrift, 2008) challenges the textual metaphor that dominated new cultural geography, arguing that culture is enacted through embodied practices, routines, and affect rather than decoded through symbolic interpretation. This has influenced studies of everyday mobility, soundscapes, and emotional geographies.

**Language endangerment and linguistic geography**: Of the ~7,000 languages currently spoken, between 50% and 90% may go extinct by the end of the 21st century (Krauss, 1992). The **EGIDS** (Expanded Graded Intergenerational Disruption Scale) classifies languages by their vitality. Language shift typically occurs when speakers of a minority language adopt a dominant language for economic opportunity, social mobility, or political pressure. GIS-based mapping of language distributions (Ethnologue, UNESCO Atlas of the World's Languages in Danger) has enabled spatial analysis of language endangerment hotspots, which overlap significantly with regions of high biodiversity -- the "biocultural diversity" correlation (Maffi, 2005).

**Geographies of religion** have expanded from distribution-mapping to the study of secularization, religious fundamentalism as a spatial phenomenon, and the politics of sacred space. The work of Lily Kong (2001, 2010) has been influential in examining how religious practices shape urban space, how competing religious claims to the same territory generate conflict, and how the state regulates religious expression in public space.

## Historical Context [Master+]

Cultural geography as a formal discipline emerged with Carl Sauer and the Berkeley School (1920s-1950s). Sauer's "The Morphology of Landscape" (1925) defined the cultural landscape as the central object of geographic study and argued against environmental determinism -- the idea that the physical environment determines culture -- in favor of viewing culture as an active agent shaping the landscape.

The **environmental determinism** of earlier geographers (Ellen Churchill Semple, Ellsworth Huntington) had posited that climate directly determined cultural achievement, with temperate climates producing "superior" civilizations. This was critiqued as scientifically unfounded and ideologically racist by Sauer and others, and by the mid-20th century it was largely abandoned in academic geography.

The **quantitative revolution** of the 1950s-1960s shifted geography toward spatial analysis and modeling, marginalizing cultural geography for a time. The **humanistic turn** of the 1970s (Yi-Fu Tuan, Edward Relph, Anne Buttimer) brought back attention to experience, meaning, and place -- but with a phenomenological rather than Sauerian framework.

The **cultural turn** of the 1980s-1990s brought the "new cultural geography," which engaged with critical theory, postcolonial studies, and feminist geography. Key themes included landscape as ideology (Cosgrove, 1984), the politics of representation, and the deconstruction of geographic categories (e.g., "the Orient" in Edward Said's sense).
