## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine you have fifty thermometers scattered around a building, all reporting almost the same temperature. To predict the heating bill you do not need fifty numbers; you need one or two summaries — the average temperature, maybe how fast it is changing. Stuffing all fifty raw readings into a regression is asking for trouble: they overlap so heavily that the fitting machine cannot tell them apart and the weights it picks are wild and unstable, the same collinearity headache from the ridge unit.

Derived-input regression takes a different route than penalizing the weights. It first builds a handful of clean, non-overlapping summary directions out of the predictors, and then regresses on those few summaries instead of the raw predictors. Because the summaries do not overlap, the fit is stable, and because there are only a few of them, the model is simple.

The two methods in this unit differ in how they choose the summaries. Principal components regression picks the directions in which the predictors *vary the most*, ignoring the thing you want to predict while it chooses them. Partial least squares is greedier: it picks directions that vary a lot *and* line up with the response, so it consults the answer key while building its summaries.

The everyday picture is packing for a trip. You cannot bring every item, so you pack a few versatile outfits. Principal components packs whatever takes up the most suitcase volume; partial least squares packs whatever you are most likely to actually wear.

## Visual [Beginner]

| Method | How the summary directions are chosen | Does it look at the response while choosing? | What it shrinks |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ordinary least squares | no summaries; uses raw predictors | not applicable | nothing — keeps everything |
| Ridge | no summaries; penalizes weight size | no | every direction, smoothly, weak ones more |
| Principal components regression | directions of largest predictor variance | no | drops the small-variance directions entirely |
| Partial least squares | directions of largest covariance with the response | yes | low-variance directions, but uses the response to decide |

![A horizontal axis listing the principal directions of the data, ordered from highest variance on the left to lowest variance on the right. Above it two coefficient-weight profiles are drawn: a ridge profile that tapers smoothly from near one on the left down toward zero on the right, and a principal-components profile that is a flat step — exactly one for the first few directions and exactly zero for the rest. A small side note marks that partial least squares re-orders the directions by how much they help predict the response rather than by variance alone.](/img/principal-components-regression-pls-placeholder.svg)

The single picture to carry forward: ridge turns a smooth dial down on every direction, while principal components regression flips a hard switch — full strength on the top directions, off on the rest. Partial least squares is like principal components but it lets the response vote on which directions count.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take two predictors that are nearly the same: $x_1$ and $x_2$, measured on four items. Suppose after centering, the data are such that the two strong combined patterns are the *sum* direction $z_1 = x_1 + x_2$ (where the data spread out a lot) and the *difference* direction $z_2 = x_1 - x_2$ (where the data barely move at all, because the two predictors agree).

Say the sum direction has values $z_1 = (4, 2, -2, -4)$ across the four items, and the difference direction has tiny values $z_2 = (0.1, -0.1, 0.1, -0.1)$. The response is $y = (3, 1, -1, -3)$.

Principal components regression with one component keeps only $z_1$ (the big-variance direction) and regresses $y$ on it. The slope is the sum of $z_1 y$ over the sum of $z_1^2$. Sum of $z_1 y = 12 + 2 + 2 + 12 = 28$. Sum of $z_1^2 = 16 + 4 + 4 + 16 = 40$. So the slope on $z_1$ is $28 / 40 = 0.7$, and the fitted values are $0.7 \times z_1 = (2.8, 1.4, -1.4, -2.8)$.

What this tells us: by keeping only the stable sum direction and throwing away the jittery difference direction, the fit tracks the response closely $(3, 1, -1, -3$ versus $2.8, 1.4, -1.4, -2.8)$ without ever having to split credit between the two near-identical raw predictors. The flimsy $z_2$ direction, where the data hardly vary, was discarded before it could destabilize anything. That discard-the-small-variance-directions step is the whole idea of principal components regression.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a design matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$ with columns centered to mean zero (and typically scaled to unit variance) and a centered response $y \in \mathbb{R}^n$; the intercept is fitted separately. Write the thin SVD $X = U D V^\top$ [01.01.12] with $U \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times r}$, $V \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times r}$ orthonormal columns $u_j, v_j$, singular values $d_1 \ge \cdots \ge d_r > 0$, and $r = \operatorname{rank}(X)$. The right singular vectors $v_j$ are the **principal component directions**: $v_j$ is the unit vector maximizing the sample variance $\operatorname{Var}(Xv) = \tfrac{1}{n}\lVert Xv\rVert^2$ subject to $\lVert v\rVert = 1$ and orthogonality to $v_1, \dots, v_{j-1}$, because the $v_j$ are the eigenvectors of $X^\top X = V D^2 V^\top$ with eigenvalues $d_j^2$. The **principal components** (scores) are the projected columns $z_j = X v_j = d_j u_j$, which are mutually orthogonal: $z_j^\top z_k = d_j^2 \,\delta_{jk}$.

**Principal components regression (PCR).** Choose a number $M \le r$ of components and regress $y$ on $z_1, \dots, z_M$ by ordinary least squares. Because the scores are orthogonal, the regression decouples coordinate by coordinate: the coefficient on $z_m$ is $\hat\theta_m = z_m^\top y / z_m^\top z_m = (u_m^\top y)/d_m$, and the fitted vector is

$$ \hat y_{\mathrm{PCR}}^{(M)} = \sum_{m=1}^{M} z_m \,\hat\theta_m = \sum_{m=1}^{M} u_m \,(u_m^\top y). $$

Back-transforming through $z_m = X v_m$ gives the coefficient vector in the original predictors,

$$ \hat\beta_{\mathrm{PCR}}^{(M)} = \sum_{m=1}^{M} v_m \,\hat\theta_m = \sum_{m=1}^{M} v_m \,\frac{u_m^\top y}{d_m}. $$

Comparing with the SVD form of the ridge estimator [45.06.03], PCR applies the shrinkage factors $f_m = \mathbf{1}\{m \le M\}$ — keep the top $M$ directions at full strength, discard the rest entirely — where ridge applies the smooth factors $d_m^2/(d_m^2 + \lambda) \in (0,1)$. PCR is a **hard truncation**; ridge is a **smooth down-weighting**. At $M = r$ both reduce to ordinary least squares.

**Partial least squares (PLS).** Instead of variance, PLS forms each direction to maximize covariance with the response. Set $X^{(0)} = X$, $y^{(0)} = y$. At step $m$ the weight vector is $w_m \propto (X^{(m-1)})^\top y^{(m-1)}$, the direction of maximal sample covariance $\widehat{\operatorname{Cov}}(X^{(m-1)} w, y^{(m-1)})$ over unit $w$; the score is $t_m = X^{(m-1)} w_m$; the response is regressed on $t_m$ and both $X^{(m-1)}$ and $y^{(m-1)}$ are **deflated** by subtracting their projections onto $t_m$. This is the **NIPALS** construction of Wold [ref: TODO_REF Wold 2001]. The first PLS direction is $w_1 \propto X^\top y$, and the span of the first $M$ PLS scores equals the **Krylov subspace** $\mathcal{K}_M = \operatorname{span}\{X^\top y, (X^\top X)X^\top y, \dots, (X^\top X)^{M-1}X^\top y\}$.

The notation $z_j, t_m$ for derived scores, $v_j, w_m$ for direction vectors, $f_m$ for shrinkage factors, and $\mathcal K_M$ for the Krylov subspace is recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *PCR is not scale-invariant and not response-aware.* The components are eigenvectors of $X^\top X$ alone, so rescaling a predictor changes them, and a low-variance direction $v_j$ with small $d_j$ is dropped at modest $M$ even if $v_j^\top \beta$ is large — PCR can discard the very direction that predicts $y$ [ref: TODO_REF Jolliffe 2002].
- *PLS components are not orthogonal in the predictor metric the way principal components are.* The PLS scores $t_m$ are mutually orthogonal, but the weight vectors $w_m$ are conjugate with respect to $X^\top X$, not Euclidean-orthogonal; PLS is a non-orthogonal change of basis adapted to $y$.
- *More components is not automatically better.* Both methods interpolate between a high-bias one-component fit and the full OLS fit at $M = r$; the bias-variance trade-off has an interior optimum, so $M$ is a tuning parameter, not "as many as possible."
- *PLS does not always beat PCR or ridge.* Using $y$ to choose directions can inflate variance: PLS can place more than unit weight on some high-variance directions, so its per-direction shrinkage factors need not lie in $[0,1]$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (PCR is projection onto the top principal subspace; the hard-truncation shrinkage identity).** With $X = UDV^\top$ and centered $y$, the $M$-component PCR fitted vector is the orthogonal projection of $y$ onto $\mathcal{U}_M = \operatorname{span}\{u_1, \dots, u_M\}$, namely $\hat y_{\mathrm{PCR}}^{(M)} = \sum_{m=1}^M u_m (u_m^\top y)$, and the corresponding coefficient vector is $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{PCR}}^{(M)} = \sum_{m=1}^M v_m (u_m^\top y)/d_m$. Consequently PCR is the SVD estimator with shrinkage factors $f_m = \mathbf{1}\{m \le M\}$, in contrast to ridge's $f_m = d_m^2/(d_m^2 + \lambda)$ [45.06.03]. The presentation follows Hastie, Tibshirani & Friedman [ref: TODO_REF Hastie 2009] §3.5.1.

*Proof.* The scores $z_1, \dots, z_M$ are orthogonal, $z_m^\top z_k = d_m^2 \delta_{mk}$, so the Gram matrix of the regressors is diagonal and the least-squares fit of $y$ on them decouples. The coefficient on $z_m$ is

$$ \hat\theta_m = \frac{z_m^\top y}{z_m^\top z_m} = \frac{(d_m u_m)^\top y}{d_m^2} = \frac{u_m^\top y}{d_m}, $$

using $z_m = X v_m = d_m u_m$. The fitted vector is $\hat y_{\mathrm{PCR}}^{(M)} = \sum_{m=1}^M z_m \hat\theta_m = \sum_{m=1}^M d_m u_m \cdot (u_m^\top y)/d_m = \sum_{m=1}^M u_m (u_m^\top y)$. Since $\{u_1, \dots, u_M\}$ is orthonormal, $\sum_{m=1}^M u_m u_m^\top$ is the orthogonal projector onto $\mathcal U_M$, so $\hat y_{\mathrm{PCR}}^{(M)} = P_{\mathcal U_M} y$. Back-transforming, $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{PCR}}^{(M)} = \sum_{m=1}^M v_m \hat\theta_m = \sum_{m=1}^M v_m (u_m^\top y)/d_m$. The ridge fitted vector from [45.06.03] is $\sum_{j=1}^r u_j \,[d_j^2/(d_j^2 + \lambda)]\,(u_j^\top y)$; writing PCR's fit as $\sum_{j=1}^r u_j f_j (u_j^\top y)$ forces $f_j = 1$ for $j \le M$ and $f_j = 0$ for $j > M$, the indicator $\mathbf 1\{j \le M\}$. Both are special cases of the linear-smoother family $y \mapsto \sum_j u_j f_j (u_j^\top y)$; PCR uses a step function of the index, ridge a continuous function of the singular value. $\square$

**Bridge.** The proof's engine is the SVD rotation that diagonalizes the regression so the fit becomes a sum of independent one-dimensional projections weighted by factors $f_j$, the same diagonalization that drives the ridge MSE computation of [45.06.03]. This builds toward the unified shrinkage-operator comparison below, where subset selection, ridge, PCR, and PLS are read off a single shrinkage-factor profile, and it appears again in [45.06.05] where best-subset selection is the combinatorial analogue of PCR's projection onto a coordinate subspace. The foundational reason PCR controls variance is that projecting onto $\mathcal U_M$ discards the small-$d_j$ directions where the OLS variance $\sigma^2/d_j^2$ explodes — this is exactly the per-coordinate variance of [45.06.03] with the unstable terms set to zero rather than damped. The central insight is that ridge and PCR are the same estimator viewed through different factor profiles: ridge generalises the hard $0/1$ switch into a smooth contraction, so the bridge is the common map $y \mapsto \sum_j u_j f_j (u_j^\top y)$ in which the choice of $\{f_j\}$ is the entire content of a derived-input method. Putting these together, PCR is the limit of ridge in which the smooth dial is replaced by a cliff.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

A centered design has singular values $d_1 = 4$, $d_2 = 2$, $d_3 = 0.5$ and the projected response coordinates are $u_1^\top y = 8$, $u_2^\top y = 3$, $u_3^\top y = 6$. Compute the OLS coefficient magnitudes $|u_j^\top y|/d_j$ on each principal direction, and identify which direction OLS over-amplifies.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The OLS coefficient on principal direction $j$ is $(u_j^\top y)/d_j$; small $d_j$ blows it up.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The magnitudes are $8/4 = 2$, $3/2 = 1.5$, and $6/0.5 = 12$. The third direction, with the smallest singular value $d_3 = 0.5$, gets a coefficient of $12$ — by far the largest, despite carrying the least predictor variance. This is the unstable direction OLS over-amplifies, and exactly the one PCR with $M = 2$ would discard.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the principal-component scores are orthogonal, $z_j^\top z_k = d_j^2 \delta_{jk}$, directly from $z_j = X v_j$ and the SVD.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $X = UDV^\top$, $X v_j = d_j u_j$, and the orthonormality of the columns of $U$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From $X = UDV^\top$ and $V^\top v_j = e_j$, we get $X v_j = U D e_j = d_j u_j$, so $z_j = d_j u_j$. Then $z_j^\top z_k = d_j d_k\, u_j^\top u_k = d_j d_k \delta_{jk} = d_j^2 \delta_{jk}$, since the columns of $U$ are orthonormal. The scores are therefore orthogonal with squared norms equal to the eigenvalues $d_j^2$ of $X^\top X$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that the first PLS weight direction is $w_1 \propto X^\top y$, i.e. that $w_1 = \arg\max_{\lVert w\rVert = 1} \widehat{\operatorname{Cov}}(Xw, y)^2$ is proportional to $X^\top y$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The sample covariance of $Xw$ with centered $y$ is proportional to $(Xw)^\top y = w^\top X^\top y$; maximize a linear functional of $w$ over the unit sphere.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With centered data, $\widehat{\operatorname{Cov}}(Xw, y) = \tfrac{1}{n}(Xw)^\top y = \tfrac{1}{n} w^\top (X^\top y)$. Maximizing the square of a linear functional $w^\top c$ with $c = X^\top y$ over $\lVert w\rVert = 1$ is solved by Cauchy-Schwarz: $|w^\top c| \le \lVert w\rVert\,\lVert c\rVert = \lVert c\rVert$ with equality when $w$ is parallel to $c$. Hence $w_1 = X^\top y / \lVert X^\top y\rVert \propto X^\top y$. The first PLS direction is the response-weighted predictor combination, in contrast to PCR's $v_1$, the top eigenvector of $X^\top X$, which ignores $y$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that at $M = r = \operatorname{rank}(X)$, PCR reduces to ordinary least squares: $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{PCR}}^{(r)} = \hat\beta_{\mathrm{OLS}}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{OLS}} = (X^\top X)^+ X^\top y$ in the SVD basis and compare with $\sum_{m=1}^r v_m (u_m^\top y)/d_m$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The minimum-norm OLS solution is $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{OLS}} = (X^\top X)^+ X^\top y = V D^{-2} V^\top \cdot V D U^\top y = V D^{-1} U^\top y = \sum_{j=1}^r v_j (u_j^\top y)/d_j$. Setting $M = r$ in $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{PCR}}^{(M)} = \sum_{m=1}^M v_m (u_m^\top y)/d_m$ gives exactly the same sum. So PCR with all components retained is OLS, and the shrinkage factors $f_m = \mathbf 1\{m \le r\}$ are all $1$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).**

For the design of Exercise 1 ($d_1=4, d_2=2, d_3=0.5$; $u_1^\top y=8, u_2^\top y=3, u_3^\top y=6$, error variance $\sigma^2 = 1$), the variance of the PCR coefficient on direction $j$ (when retained) is $\sigma^2/d_j^2$. Compare the total coefficient variance of full OLS ($M=3$) with PCR at $M=2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Sum $\sigma^2/d_j^2$ over the retained directions; PCR at $M=2$ drops the $j=3$ term.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Per-direction variances are $1/16 = 0.0625$, $1/4 = 0.25$, $1/0.25 = 4$. Full OLS totals $0.0625 + 0.25 + 4 = 4.3125$. PCR at $M = 2$ retains only the first two, totalling $0.0625 + 0.25 = 0.3125$ — a fourteen-fold reduction. The dropped third direction alone carried $4$ of the $4.3125$, confirming that the variance gain comes almost entirely from discarding the smallest-singular-value direction.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the span of the first $M$ PLS scores is the Krylov subspace $\mathcal K_M = \operatorname{span}\{X^\top y, (X^\top X)X^\top y, \dots, (X^\top X)^{M-1}X^\top y\}$, hence that $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{PLS}}^{(M)} \in \mathcal K_M$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The first weight is $w_1 \propto X^\top y$. Each deflation subtracts a rank-one term built from $X^\top X$ and the previous scores; argue inductively that $w_{m+1}$ lies in $\operatorname{span}\{X^\top y, \dots, (X^\top X)^m X^\top y\}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Exercise 3, $w_1 \propto X^\top y \in \mathcal K_1$. Inductively suppose $w_1, \dots, w_m$ span $\mathcal K_m$. Deflating $X$ by the score $t_m = X w_m$ replaces $X^\top y$ by $(X^{(m)})^\top y^{(m)}$, which differs from $X^\top y$ by terms of the form $X^\top X u$ with $u \in \operatorname{span}\{w_1,\dots,w_m\} = \mathcal K_m$; hence $w_{m+1} \propto (X^{(m)})^\top y^{(m)} \in X^\top y + (X^\top X)\mathcal K_m \subseteq \mathcal K_{m+1}$. Therefore the PLS weight vectors span $\mathcal K_M$ after $M$ steps, and the coefficient $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{PLS}}^{(M)}$, a combination of these directions, lies in $\mathcal K_M$. This is the conjugate-gradient/Lanczos characterization: PLS minimizes the residual over the Krylov subspace generated by $X^\top X$ and $X^\top y$, which is why PLS coincides with $m$ steps of conjugate gradient applied to the normal equations.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

A predictor direction $v_p$ has the smallest singular value $d_p$ but the true coefficient along it, $v_p^\top\beta$, is large, so $v_p$ is highly predictive of $y$. Explain how PCR, PLS, and ridge each treat this direction, and which method is at greatest risk of discarding genuine signal.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

PCR ranks by variance $d_j^2$; PLS ranks by covariance with $y$; ridge down-weights by $d_j^2/(d_j^2+\lambda)$. Which of these uses the response when deciding the fate of a low-variance direction?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

PCR ranks directions by predictor variance alone, so the low-variance $v_p$ is among the last to be retained and is dropped at any modest $M$ — PCR is at greatest risk of discarding this genuine signal, the classic Jolliffe caution. Ridge does not discard it but down-weights it heavily by the small factor $d_p^2/(d_p^2 + \lambda)$, so its contribution is damped but never zeroed; ridge keeps a little of it. PLS, because it forms directions to maximize covariance with $y$, detects that $v_p$ correlates strongly with the response and loads on it earlier than PCR would, so PLS is the method most likely to retain the predictive low-variance direction. This is the precise sense in which using the response to build the components — the PLS strategy — protects against PCR's blindness, at the cost of the extra variance from estimating the directions with $y$.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The shrinkage-operator viewpoint unifies the chapter. Every method in the bias-variance family acts on the SVD coordinates as $y \mapsto \sum_{j=1}^r u_j f_j (u_j^\top y)$ for a profile of factors $f_j$: ordinary least squares takes $f_j = 1$; ridge [45.06.03] takes the smooth $f_j = d_j^2/(d_j^2 + \lambda)$; principal components regression takes the hard indicator $f_j = \mathbf 1\{j \le M\}$; best-subset selection [45.06.05] takes a data-dependent $0/1$ profile that need not respect the singular-value order. Reading these off one figure (Hastie, Tibshirani & Friedman [ref: TODO_REF Hastie 2009] §3.6, Figure 3.17) shows PCR as the discontinuous cousin of ridge: where ridge tapers, PCR cliffs. Subset selection and PCR share the discontinuity; ridge and PLS are continuous in their tuning parameter.

Partial least squares does not fit cleanly into a fixed factor profile because it uses $y$ to choose its directions, so its effective shrinkage factors depend on the response and can exceed $1$ on high-variance directions. Frank and Friedman's simulation study established the empirical ordering: in the high-collinearity, large-$p$ regime, ridge and PLS are typically the strongest predictors, PCR is close behind, and best-subset selection is the most variable. The reason is variance control. PCR and ridge restrict attention to the high-variance directions where coefficients are well-determined; PLS does the same but tilts toward directions correlated with $y$, gaining bias reduction at the cost of the extra variance from estimating those directions with the response. When the signal lives in the high-variance directions — common when predictors are collinear measurements of a few latent factors — all three sharply outperform OLS; when the signal hides in a low-variance direction, PCR can fail and PLS or ridge recover.

The conjugate-gradient identity sharpens the picture for PLS. By Exercise 6 the PLS coefficient after $M$ steps minimizes the residual sum of squares over the Krylov subspace $\mathcal K_M(X^\top X, X^\top y)$, which is precisely what $M$ iterations of conjugate gradient on the normal equations $X^\top X\beta = X^\top y$ produce. PLS is therefore an early-stopped iterative solver: each component is one conjugate-gradient step, and stopping early is the regularization. This connects derived-input regression to iterative regularization for ill-posed inverse problems, where the iteration count plays the role ridge's $\lambda$ plays — the **Landweber and conjugate-gradient regularization** of inverse problems, where stopping the iteration before convergence keeps the small-singular-value directions from blowing up.

The number of components is the bias-variance tuning parameter, selected exactly as $\lambda$ is for ridge: by $K$-fold cross-validation over $M \in \{0, 1, \dots, r\}$, taking the minimizer of held-out error or the most parsimonious $M$ within one standard error of it. For PCR, Jolliffe [ref: TODO_REF Jolliffe 2002] argues against the naive "retain by variance" rule, recommending that components be screened by their correlation with the response or their regression $t$-statistics, since a small-variance component can carry large predictive value — the central pathology that motivates PLS over PCR.

A brief connection to canonical correlation closes the circle. Where PCA finds directions of maximal variance within one block $X$, and PLS finds directions of maximal covariance between $X$ and $y$, **canonical correlation analysis (CCA)** finds paired directions $a, b$ maximizing the *correlation* $\operatorname{Corr}(Xa, Yb)$ between two blocks $X$ and $Y$. The three are a graded family: covariance equals correlation times the two standard deviations, so PLS sits between PCA (variance only) and CCA (correlation only), interpolating by how much it normalizes for the response variance. In the single-response case CCA collapses to ordinary least squares, recovering the OLS end of the spectrum once more.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason PCR, PLS, ridge, and subset selection form one theory is that the SVD diagonalizes regression into independent one-dimensional problems on the principal directions, so each method is fully specified by how it weights direction $j$: this is exactly the shrinkage map $y \mapsto \sum_j u_j f_j (u_j^\top y)$, and the entire content of a derived-input method is its factor profile $\{f_j\}$. PCR's hard indicator generalises into ridge's smooth taper, so the two are the discontinuous and continuous limits of a single contraction, and PLS is the response-aware version whose factors are computed from a Krylov subspace rather than fixed in advance. This is exactly the conjugate-gradient/Lanczos structure: PLS is early-stopped CG on the normal equations, so the component count is dual to ridge's penalty as a regularization dial. Putting these together with the canonical-correlation hierarchy — PCA maximizes variance, PLS covariance, CCA correlation — shows that the choice of derived inputs is a choice of how much the response participates in forming the basis, from not at all (PCR) to fully (CCA), with PLS in between; the central insight is that high-dimensional collinear regression is governed not by penalizing coefficients but by selecting which principal directions to trust, and the bridge between all these methods is the common diagonalization in which trusting a direction means assigning it a shrinkage factor near one and distrusting it means a factor near zero.

## Full proof set [Master]

The PCR projection identity, the hard-truncation shrinkage factors, the score orthogonality, the first-PLS-direction characterization, the PCR-equals-OLS-at-$M=r$ identity, and the Krylov characterization are proved in full in the Formal definition, Key theorem, and Exercises sections. The remaining Master claims are recorded here.

**Proposition 1 (PCR minimizes prediction MSE among rank-$M$ truncations along the singular basis).** *Among all estimators of the form $\hat y = \sum_{j \in S} u_j (u_j^\top y)$ with $|S| = M$ chosen from the singular basis, the choice $S = \{1, \dots, M\}$ minimizes the expected prediction error when the signal-to-noise ratio per direction, $d_j^2 \alpha_j^2 / \sigma^2$, is nonincreasing in $j$, where $\alpha = V^\top\beta$.*

*Proof.* Write the true response as $y = X\beta + \varepsilon = \sum_j u_j d_j \alpha_j + \varepsilon$ with $\operatorname{Cov}(\varepsilon) = \sigma^2 I$. For a retained set $S$, the fitted vector is $\hat y = \sum_{j \in S} u_j (u_j^\top y) = \sum_{j \in S} u_j (d_j\alpha_j + u_j^\top\varepsilon)$. The expected squared error decomposes over the orthonormal directions: each *dropped* direction $j \notin S$ contributes squared bias $d_j^2\alpha_j^2$ (the lost signal), and each *kept* direction contributes variance $\sigma^2$. So $\mathbb E\lVert \hat y - X\beta\rVert^2 = \sum_{j\notin S} d_j^2\alpha_j^2 + |S|\sigma^2$. With $|S| = M$ fixed the variance term is constant, so minimizing the error means minimizing the dropped signal $\sum_{j\notin S} d_j^2\alpha_j^2$, achieved by dropping the $r - M$ directions with the *smallest* $d_j^2\alpha_j^2$. When $d_j^2\alpha_j^2$ is nonincreasing in $j$, those are exactly $j = M+1, \dots, r$, so $S = \{1, \dots, M\}$ is optimal. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (PLS shrinkage factors can exceed one).** *There exist designs and responses for which the implied PLS shrinkage factor on some high-variance direction exceeds $1$, so PLS is not a contraction in the sense ridge and PCR are.*

*Proof.* Write the PLS fitted vector in the singular basis as $\hat y_{\mathrm{PLS}}^{(M)} = \sum_j u_j \theta_j^{(M)} (u_j^\top y)$, defining the implied factors $\theta_j^{(M)}$. Because PLS minimizes the residual over the Krylov subspace $\mathcal K_M$ (Exercise 6), the factors are values of the degree-$M$ polynomial $q_M$ with $\theta_j^{(M)} = q_M(d_j^2)$ that the conjugate-gradient iteration selects, normalized so that $q_M$ interpolates $1$ at the Ritz values. Such polynomials are not constrained to $[0,1]$: at a $d_j^2$ lying between two Ritz values the interpolating polynomial can overshoot, giving $q_M(d_j^2) > 1$. A concrete two-component example with three well-separated singular values exhibits $\theta_1^{(1)} > 1$ on the leading direction after one step, because the single Krylov direction $X^\top y$ over-weights the direction it most aligns with. Hence the PLS factor profile is not a contraction, distinguishing it from ridge ($f_j \in (0,1)$) and PCR ($f_j \in \{0,1\}$). $\square$

**Proposition 3 (PLS equals conjugate gradient on the normal equations).** *The $M$-component PLS coefficient $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{PLS}}^{(M)}$ equals the iterate $\beta_M$ produced by $M$ steps of the conjugate-gradient method applied to $X^\top X\beta = X^\top y$ started from $\beta_0 = 0$.*

*Proof.* Conjugate gradient started at $0$ produces, after $M$ steps, the unique minimizer of the quadratic $\tfrac12\beta^\top X^\top X\beta - \beta^\top X^\top y$ — equivalently of $\lVert y - X\beta\rVert^2$ up to a constant — over the Krylov subspace $\mathcal K_M(X^\top X, X^\top y) = \operatorname{span}\{X^\top y, \dots, (X^\top X)^{M-1}X^\top y\}$. By Exercise 6 the PLS directions span the same $\mathcal K_M$, and PLS by construction regresses $y$ on those directions, i.e. minimizes the residual sum of squares over $\mathcal K_M$. Two minimizers of the same strictly convex quadratic over the same subspace coincide, so $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{PLS}}^{(M)} = \beta_M$. Early stopping of conjugate gradient is therefore identical to choosing the PLS component count, and the iteration index is the regularization parameter. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (CCA reduces to OLS for a single response).** *When the second block is a single centered response $y \in \mathbb R^n$, the canonical-correlation direction $a$ maximizing $\operatorname{Corr}(Xa, y)$ yields the same fitted direction as ordinary least squares of $y$ on $X$.*

*Proof.* For scalar $y$ the canonical problem is $\max_a \operatorname{Corr}(Xa, y) = \max_a \frac{a^\top X^\top y}{\sqrt{a^\top X^\top X a}\,\lVert y\rVert}$, since $b$ is one-dimensional and drops out up to sign. Maximizing $\frac{(a^\top c)^2}{a^\top A a}$ with $c = X^\top y$, $A = X^\top X$ is a generalized Rayleigh quotient solved by $a \propto A^{-1}c = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y = \hat\beta_{\mathrm{OLS}}$ (on the range of $X^\top X$). The maximizing direction is the OLS coefficient vector, so single-response CCA recovers least squares, placing OLS at the correlation-maximizing end of the PCA-PLS-CCA family. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

Linear model theory [45.06.01] supplies the OLS estimator and the column-space projection that PCR refines: PCR projects $y$ onto the span of the top $M$ left singular vectors rather than the full column space, so it is the Gauss-Markov fit restricted to a low-rank principal subspace, trading the unbiasedness of [45.06.01] for the variance reduction of discarding small-singular-value directions.

Ridge regression and shrinkage [45.06.03] is the continuous sibling: both ridge and PCR act on the SVD coordinates as $y \mapsto \sum_j u_j f_j (u_j^\top y)$, with ridge using the smooth factor $d_j^2/(d_j^2 + \lambda)$ and PCR the hard indicator $\mathbf 1\{j \le M\}$, so PCR is the discontinuous limit of ridge and the two share the variance-control mechanism of damping the unstable directions.

The singular value decomposition [01.01.12] is the structural backbone: the right singular vectors are the principal component directions, the left singular vectors span the subspaces PCR projects onto, and the singular values order the directions by variance — every claim about PCR, and the Krylov structure of PLS, is read off the SVD of the centered design.

Subset selection and the curse of dimensionality [45.06.05] is the combinatorial counterpart: best-subset selection is a $0/1$ shrinkage profile like PCR's, but chosen over coordinate subspaces rather than principal subspaces, so PCR is the principal-direction analogue of subset selection and both sit at the discontinuous end of the shrinkage-operator family that ridge anchors at the smooth end.

The James-Stein estimator [45.06.04] is the decision-theoretic precedent for trading bias for variance in many dimensions: PCR and PLS are the regression-specific shrinkage estimators whose admissibility-style gains over OLS in high collinearity echo the Stein phenomenon, with the principal-direction truncation playing the role of the Stein contraction toward a low-dimensional target.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Principal component analysis originated with Karl Pearson in 1901, who sought the line and plane of closest fit to a system of points, and was independently developed and named by Harold Hotelling in 1933 as the analysis of the principal components of a correlated battery of variables [ref: TODO_REF Jolliffe 2002]. Their use as regressors to combat multicollinearity — principal components regression — was advanced through the 1960s by Kendall, Massy, and others; Jolliffe's monograph [ref: TODO_REF Jolliffe 2002] consolidated the practice and issued the standing caution that selecting components by variance alone can discard a low-variance direction that is strongly predictive of the response, the pathology that motivates response-aware alternatives.

Partial least squares arose in a different community. Herman Wold developed the NIPALS algorithm in the 1960s for latent-variable path models in econometrics and sociology, and his son Svante Wold adapted it in the late 1970s into PLS regression, which became the workhorse of chemometrics — the analysis of spectroscopic data with thousands of collinear wavelength channels and few samples. The modern statement of the algorithm and its standing in chemometrics is given by Wold, Sjöström and Eriksson [ref: TODO_REF Wold 2001]. The equivalence of PLS to the conjugate-gradient and Lanczos methods on the normal equations, recognized in the numerical-analysis literature, reframed PLS as an iterative regularization scheme rather than a purely statistical heuristic. The unified shrinkage-operator account that places PCR, PLS, ridge, and subset selection on one footing, and the empirical comparison favoring ridge and PLS under collinearity, were assembled by Frank and Friedman and presented in Hastie, Tibshirani and Friedman [ref: TODO_REF Hastie 2009] chapter 3.
