## Intuition [Beginner]

The lasso is a sharp tool, but it has two habits that get in the way. First, when two predictors carry nearly the same information — two columns of data that move together — the lasso will usually keep one and switch the other off entirely, and which one it keeps can flip with a tiny change in the data. That is jumpy and hard to trust. Second, when you have more predictors than data points, the lasso refuses to keep more than a handful: it caps the number it selects at the number of rows you have. In genetics or text, where predictors vastly outnumber samples, that cap is a real limit.

The elastic net fixes both by mixing two charges. It keeps the lasso's absolute-value charge, which produces the clean on-or-off selection, and adds a small dose of ridge's squared-size charge, which gently ties together predictors that move together. The squared charge has no favorites among a correlated group, so instead of picking one and dropping the rest, the elastic net keeps the whole group and splits the weight among them. That is the grouping effect, and it is exactly what you want when several measurements are really pointing at one underlying cause.

The second tool in this unit is about *how* you compute the whole family of solutions, not just one. As you loosen the budget, predictors switch on one at a time at definite thresholds, and between thresholds every weight changes in a perfectly straight line. Least angle regression walks along these straight pieces, finding each switch-on point exactly, so it draws the entire path of solutions for the price of about one ordinary fit.

The everyday image: the lasso is a strict editor that cuts every redundant word; the elastic net is an editor that keeps a useful synonym pair together; and least angle regression is the time-lapse that shows you every edit as the budget relaxes.

| Elastic net | both, mixed | keeps the group together, with exact zeros elsewhere | yes |

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take two predictors that are perfectly correlated: the second column is an exact copy of the first. Standardize both, and suppose the lasso would put a total weight of $6$ on this pair to fit the data. Because the two columns are identical, the lasso cannot tell them apart, and any split that adds to $6$ — all on the first, all on the second, or anything between — fits equally well and costs the same absolute-value charge. The lasso picks one of these arbitrarily, often all $6$ on one column and $0$ on the other.

Now add a small squared-size charge, the ridge part of the elastic net. The squared charge of a split $(a, b)$ with $a + b = 6$ is $a^2 + b^2$. Try the lopsided split $(6, 0)$: its squared charge is $36 + 0 = 36$. Try the even split $(3, 3)$: its squared charge is $9 + 9 = 18$. The even split costs half as much.

Check a middle case, $(4, 2)$: that gives $16 + 4 = 20$, still more than $18$. The smallest squared charge for a fixed total always comes from sharing equally.

What this tells us: adding the squared charge breaks the lasso's tie in favor of *splitting the weight evenly* across identical predictors, instead of dumping it all on one. With real (not identical) predictors the effect is gentler, but the direction is the same — correlated predictors are kept together and share the load. That is the grouping effect in one calculation.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a design matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$ with columns $X_j$ and response $y \in \mathbb{R}^n$, predictors centered and standardized so that $\tfrac1n\lVert X_j\rVert_2^2 = 1$, the intercept fitted unpenalized. For penalty parameters $\lambda_1 \ge 0$ and $\lambda_2 \ge 0$, the **naive elastic net** estimator is the minimizer of the doubly penalized criterion

$$ \hat\beta^{\,\mathrm{naive}}(\lambda_1, \lambda_2) = \arg\min_{\beta \in \mathbb{R}^p} \Big\{ \tfrac12\lVert y - X\beta\rVert_2^2 + \lambda_1\lVert\beta\rVert_1 + \lambda_2\lVert\beta\rVert_2^2 \Big\}, \qquad \lVert\beta\rVert_2^2 = \sum_{j=1}^p \beta_j^2. $$

The criterion is the sum of the lasso objective [45.06.06] and the ridge penalty [45.06.03]. The $\ell_2$ term makes the objective **strongly convex** for $\lambda_2 > 0$: the Hessian of the smooth part is $X^\top X + 2\lambda_2 I \succeq 2\lambda_2 I$, positive definite regardless of the rank of $X^\top X$, so the minimizer is unique even when $p > n$. The optimality condition combines the ridge gradient with the lasso subgradient: $\hat\beta$ is the minimizer if and only if there is $s \in \partial\lVert\hat\beta\rVert_1$ with

$$ X^\top(y - X\hat\beta) = 2\lambda_2\,\hat\beta + \lambda_1\,s, \qquad s_j = \operatorname{sign}(\hat\beta_j)\ \text{if}\ \hat\beta_j \ne 0, \quad s_j \in [-1,1]\ \text{if}\ \hat\beta_j = 0. $$

The inactive-coordinate clause $|X_j^\top(y - X\hat\beta)| \le \lambda_1$ still produces exact zeros, so selection is preserved; the active-coordinate clause now carries the extra ridge shrinkage $2\lambda_2\hat\beta_j$, which is what couples correlated predictors.

The **augmented-design representation** turns the elastic net into a pure lasso. Define
$$ X_* = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+2\lambda_2}}\begin{pmatrix} X \\ \sqrt{2\lambda_2}\,I_p \end{pmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{(n+p)\times p}, \qquad y_* = \begin{pmatrix} y \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{n+p}. $$
A direct computation (Proposition 1) shows the naive elastic net solves a lasso on $(X_*, y_*)$ with parameter $\gamma = \lambda_1/\sqrt{1+2\lambda_2}$. Because $X_*$ has full column rank $p$ for any $\lambda_2 > 0$ — its lower block $\sqrt{2\lambda_2}\,I_p$ alone supplies $p$ independent rows — the elastic net can select up to all $p$ predictors, removing the lasso's cap of $\min(n,p)$ active variables. Following Zou & Hastie [ref: TODO_REF Zou, H. & Hastie, T. — Regularization and Variable Selection via the Elastic Net], the **corrected (proper) elastic net** rescales away the double shrinkage,
$$ \hat\beta(\lambda_1, \lambda_2) = (1 + 2\lambda_2)\,\hat\beta^{\,\mathrm{naive}}(\lambda_1, \lambda_2), $$
since the augmented lasso shrinks once by $\ell_1$ and again by the $1/\sqrt{1+2\lambda_2}$ scaling of $X_*$; undoing the latter restores prediction quality while keeping the support and the grouping. (Zou & Hastie write the criterion as $\lambda_1\lVert\beta\rVert_1 + \lambda_2\lVert\beta\rVert_2^2$ and obtain the correction factor $1+\lambda_2$; the factor $2$ here is the bookkeeping of writing the ridge term as $\lambda_2\lVert\beta\rVert_2^2$ rather than $\tfrac{\lambda_2}{2}\lVert\beta\rVert_2^2$.)

**Least angle regression (LARS)** is the algorithm that constructs the lasso solution *path* $\lambda \mapsto \hat\beta(\lambda)$. With residual $r = y - X\hat\beta$ and active set $A = \{j : \hat\beta_j \ne 0\}$, the lasso KKT condition forces every active predictor to share the maximal absolute correlation $\lvert X_j^\top r\rvert = \lambda$. LARS moves the coefficient vector in the **equiangular direction**
$$ u_A = X_A\, w_A, \qquad w_A = c\,(X_A^\top X_A)^{-1}\operatorname{sign}(X_A^\top r), $$
with $c$ chosen so that $\lVert u_A\rVert$ makes every active predictor's correlation with the residual decrease at the same rate — the direction that keeps all active correlations tied. It proceeds in straight segments, adding the predictor that next ties at the threshold, until a coefficient is about to change sign. The **lasso modification** of Efron, Hastie, Johnstone & Tibshirani [ref: TODO_REF Efron, B., Hastie, T., Johnstone, I. & Tibshirani, R. — Least Angle Regression] is a single rule: whenever an active coefficient would cross zero, remove that index from $A$ at the crossing and recompute $u_A$. With this one change, LARS traces the exact lasso path.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *The naive elastic net is not the corrected estimator.* The naive solution carries double shrinkage and predicts worse than ridge in correlated designs; only after the $(1+2\lambda_2)$ rescaling does prediction improve. Reporting the naive coefficients as "the elastic net" understates the signal.
- *The augmented design must be standardized correctly.* The $1/\sqrt{1+2\lambda_2}$ factor in $X_*$ is not cosmetic — it is what makes the augmented lasso's parameter $\gamma = \lambda_1/\sqrt{1+2\lambda_2}$ reproduce the elastic-net objective exactly. Dropping it changes the effective penalty.
- *LARS is not forward selection.* Greedy forward selection adds a predictor and refits it to its full least-squares value; LARS moves only *partway*, stopping the instant a competitor ties, so it never overcommits to a single predictor. The two coincide only when predictors are orthogonal.
- *The unmodified LARS path is not the lasso path in general.* Plain LARS only adds variables; the lasso can also *drop* a variable when its coefficient hits zero. Without the sign-crossing modification, the LARS and lasso paths diverge precisely at such drop events.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is the elastic-net grouping bound: two predictors that are highly correlated receive nearly identical coefficients, with the gap controlled by the ridge parameter and the sample correlation. This is the precise statement of the grouping effect that the lasso lacks.

**Theorem (grouping effect — Zou & Hastie 2005).** *Let $\hat\beta = \hat\beta^{\,\mathrm{naive}}(\lambda_1,\lambda_2)$ be the naive elastic-net estimator with $\lambda_2 > 0$ on standardized predictors ($\tfrac1n\lVert X_j\rVert_2^2 = 1$, equivalently $\lVert X_j\rVert_2^2 = n$). Suppose two coordinates $i, j$ are active with the same sign, $\hat\beta_i\hat\beta_j > 0$. Let $\rho_{ij} = \tfrac1n X_i^\top X_j$ be the sample correlation of the two predictors. Then*

$$ \big|\hat\beta_i - \hat\beta_j\big| \;\le\; \frac{\lVert y\rVert_2}{\lambda_2}\,\sqrt{2n\,(1 - \rho_{ij})}. $$

*In particular, as $\rho_{ij}\to 1$ the bound tends to zero: nearly identical predictors receive nearly identical coefficients.* The argument follows Zou & Hastie [ref: TODO_REF Zou, H. & Hastie, T. — Regularization and Variable Selection via the Elastic Net].

*Proof.* Because $\hat\beta_i, \hat\beta_j \ne 0$ and have the same sign, the active-coordinate optimality conditions hold with $s_i = s_j = \operatorname{sign}(\hat\beta_i) = \operatorname{sign}(\hat\beta_j)$. Writing $r = y - X\hat\beta$ for the residual, the stationarity equations for the two coordinates read
$$ X_i^\top r = 2\lambda_2\hat\beta_i + \lambda_1 s_i, \qquad X_j^\top r = 2\lambda_2\hat\beta_j + \lambda_1 s_j. $$
Subtract the second from the first. Since $s_i = s_j$, the $\lambda_1$ terms cancel, leaving
$$ (X_i - X_j)^\top r = 2\lambda_2(\hat\beta_i - \hat\beta_j), \qquad\text{so}\qquad \hat\beta_i - \hat\beta_j = \frac{1}{2\lambda_2}(X_i - X_j)^\top r. $$
Bound the right side by Cauchy-Schwarz: $|(X_i - X_j)^\top r| \le \lVert X_i - X_j\rVert_2\,\lVert r\rVert_2$. The squared length of the difference is
$$ \lVert X_i - X_j\rVert_2^2 = \lVert X_i\rVert_2^2 + \lVert X_j\rVert_2^2 - 2X_i^\top X_j = n + n - 2n\rho_{ij} = 2n(1 - \rho_{ij}), $$
using the standardization $\lVert X_i\rVert_2^2 = \lVert X_j\rVert_2^2 = n$. For the residual, evaluate the elastic-net objective at $\hat\beta$ and at $0$: since $\hat\beta$ is the minimizer, $\tfrac12\lVert y - X\hat\beta\rVert_2^2 \le \tfrac12\lVert y - X\hat\beta\rVert_2^2 + \lambda_1\lVert\hat\beta\rVert_1 + \lambda_2\lVert\hat\beta\rVert_2^2 \le \tfrac12\lVert y\rVert_2^2$, the value at $0$. Hence $\lVert r\rVert_2 = \lVert y - X\hat\beta\rVert_2 \le \lVert y\rVert_2$. Combining,
$$ |\hat\beta_i - \hat\beta_j| = \frac{|(X_i - X_j)^\top r|}{2\lambda_2} \le \frac{\sqrt{2n(1-\rho_{ij})}\,\lVert y\rVert_2}{2\lambda_2} = \frac{\lVert y\rVert_2}{\lambda_2}\sqrt{\frac{n(1-\rho_{ij})}{2}}. $$
The displayed bound $\tfrac{\lVert y\rVert_2}{\lambda_2}\sqrt{2n(1-\rho_{ij})}$ is the same inequality with the constant relaxed from $\sqrt{1/2}$ to $\sqrt{2}$, which holds a fortiori. $\square$

**Bridge.** The proof's engine is differencing the two active stationarity equations so that the nondifferentiable $\lambda_1$ subgradient terms cancel for same-sign coefficients, leaving a purely $\ell_2$ relation $\hat\beta_i - \hat\beta_j = (2\lambda_2)^{-1}(X_i - X_j)^\top r$ whose right side is small exactly when the columns are close. This builds toward the augmented-lasso and naive-versus-corrected theory of the Master tier, where the same strong convexity that makes $\lambda_2$ appear in the denominator is what removes the lasso's selection cap, and it appears again in the LARS path analysis, where the active-set stationarity $\lvert X_j^\top r\rvert = \lambda$ for all $j \in A$ is the very condition that makes the path piecewise linear. The foundational reason the elastic net groups while the lasso does not is that the lasso's penalty is linear in each coordinate, so its subgradient carries no information tying two coordinates together, whereas the strictly convex $\ell_2$ term contributes the coupling $2\lambda_2(\hat\beta_i - \hat\beta_j)$; this is exactly the ridge contraction of [45.06.03] acting differentially across a correlated pair. The result generalises the ridge grouping observation rather than contradicting the lasso: at $\lambda_2 = 0$ the bound degenerates ($1/\lambda_2 \to \infty$) and the lasso's arbitrary tie-breaking returns, and the central insight is that a positive $\ell_2$ dose converts the flat lasso ridge of equally optimal splits into a strictly convex valley with a unique, balanced minimizer. Putting these together, the grouping bound is the differential form of the strong convexity that the augmented-design representation makes global.

Then $\lVert y_* - X_*\beta\rVert_2^2 = \lVert y - (1+2\lambda_2)^{-1/2}X\beta\rVert_2^2 + (1+2\lambda_2)^{-1}\cdot 2\lambda_2\lVert\beta\rVert_2^2$ — but the cleaner route is to set $\tilde\beta = (1+2\lambda_2)^{-1/2}\beta$. With $X_*\beta = (1+2\lambda_2)^{-1/2}\begin{psmallmatrix}X\beta\\\sqrt{2\lambda_2}\beta\end{psmallmatrix}$, one computes $\lVert y_* - X_*\beta\rVert_2^2 = \lVert y\rVert_2^2 - 2(1+2\lambda_2)^{-1/2}y^\top X\beta + (1+2\lambda_2)^{-1}(\lVert X\beta\rVert_2^2 + 2\lambda_2\lVert\beta\rVert_2^2)$. Multiplying the augmented objective by $(1+2\lambda_2)$ and writing $b = (1+2\lambda_2)^{-1/2}\beta$ shows the minimizer in $b$ minimizes $\tfrac12\lVert y - Xb\rVert_2^2 + \lambda_2\lVert b\rVert_2^2 + \lambda_1\lVert b\rVert_1$ once $\gamma(1+2\lambda_2)\lVert\beta\rVert_1 = \lambda_1(1+2\lambda_2)^{1/2}\lVert\beta\rVert_1 = \lambda_1\lVert b\rVert_1\cdot(1+2\lambda_2)$ is matched by $\gamma = \lambda_1/\sqrt{1+2\lambda_2}$. The two problems share a minimizer up to the change of variables $b = (1+2\lambda_2)^{-1/2}\beta$, which is the source of the corrected-estimator rescaling.

</details>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the elastic-net solution at orthonormal design $\tfrac1n X^\top X = I$ and show it is soft-thresholding followed by ridge scaling: $\hat\beta_j^{\,\mathrm{naive}} = S_{\lambda_1}(z_j)/(1 + 2\lambda_2)$ where $z_j = X_j^\top y$ and $S_{\lambda_1}$ is the soft-threshold operator of [45.06.06].

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

At orthonormal design the criterion decouples; for each coordinate minimize $\tfrac12(\beta_j - z_j)^2 + \lambda_2\beta_j^2 + \lambda_1|\beta_j|$ after collecting the quadratic terms.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $X^\top X = nI$ but using the standardized form $\tfrac1n X^\top X = I$, the smooth part decouples to $\sum_j\big(\tfrac12(\beta_j - z_j)^2 + \lambda_2\beta_j^2\big)$ up to constants, with $z_j = X_j^\top y$ (absorbing $n$). Per coordinate, minimize $h(\beta_j) = \tfrac12(\beta_j - z_j)^2 + \lambda_2\beta_j^2 + \lambda_1|\beta_j|$. The smooth part is $\tfrac12(1+2\lambda_2)\beta_j^2 - z_j\beta_j + \text{const}$, so optimality is $0 \in (1+2\lambda_2)\beta_j - z_j + \lambda_1\partial|\beta_j|$. For $\beta_j > 0$ this gives $\beta_j = (z_j - \lambda_1)/(1+2\lambda_2)$, valid when $z_j > \lambda_1$; symmetrically for $\beta_j < 0$; and $\beta_j = 0$ when $|z_j| \le \lambda_1$. The numerator is the lasso soft-threshold; the denominator is the extra ridge shrinkage, and multiplying by $(1+2\lambda_2)$ recovers the lasso threshold exactly — the corrected estimator.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, short-answer).**

Explain why the lasso solution path is piecewise linear in $\lambda$ between knots, using the active-set stationarity condition.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On an interval where the active set $A$ and the signs are fixed, solve the active-coordinate KKT equations for $\hat\beta_A$ as a function of $\lambda$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix an interval of $\lambda$ on which the active set $A$ and the signs $s_A = \operatorname{sign}(\hat\beta_A)$ do not change. The active KKT equations are $\tfrac1n X_A^\top(y - X_A\hat\beta_A) = \lambda s_A$, a linear system whose solution is $\hat\beta_A(\lambda) = (X_A^\top X_A)^{-1}(X_A^\top y - n\lambda s_A)$. This is affine in $\lambda$: $\hat\beta_A(\lambda) = a - \lambda b$ with $a = (X_A^\top X_A)^{-1}X_A^\top y$ and $b = n(X_A^\top X_A)^{-1}s_A$. So between events the coefficients move along straight lines. A knot occurs when either an inactive coordinate's correlation reaches $\lambda$ (a variable joins $A$) or an active coefficient hits zero (a variable leaves $A$); at each knot the linear system changes and the slope changes, giving the piecewise-linear path.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Construct the LARS equiangular direction. Given an active set $A$ with $X_A$ of full column rank and signs $s_A = \operatorname{sign}(X_A^\top r)$, find the unit-prediction direction $u_A = X_A w_A$ such that every active predictor makes the same angle with $u_A$, i.e. $X_A^\top u_A = \alpha\, s_A$ for a scalar $\alpha$, with $\lVert u_A\rVert_2 = 1$. Solve for $w_A$ and $\alpha$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Impose $X_A^\top X_A w_A = \alpha s_A$ and then the normalization $w_A^\top X_A^\top X_A w_A = 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Equiangularity $X_A^\top u_A = \alpha s_A$ with $u_A = X_A w_A$ gives $X_A^\top X_A w_A = \alpha s_A$, so $w_A = \alpha (X_A^\top X_A)^{-1}s_A$. The unit-length condition $\lVert u_A\rVert_2^2 = w_A^\top X_A^\top X_A w_A = \alpha^2 s_A^\top(X_A^\top X_A)^{-1}s_A = 1$ fixes $\alpha = \big(s_A^\top(X_A^\top X_A)^{-1}s_A\big)^{-1/2}$, the **equiangular correlation**. Thus $w_A = \alpha(X_A^\top X_A)^{-1}s_A$ and $u_A = \alpha X_A(X_A^\top X_A)^{-1}s_A$. Every active predictor satisfies $X_j^\top u_A = \alpha s_j$, so moving the fit along $u_A$ decreases each active correlation $|X_j^\top r|$ at the identical rate $\alpha$, keeping the active set tied — the defining property of LARS. The next knot is the smallest step at which an inactive predictor's correlation rises to meet the common active value.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

State the LARS-to-lasso modification and explain why it is needed, referring to the difference between adding and dropping variables.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Plain LARS only ever enlarges the active set; what does the lasso KKT condition do when an active coefficient passes through zero?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Plain LARS adds one variable per step and never removes one, so its active set grows monotonically. The lasso, by contrast, requires the sign $s_j = \operatorname{sign}(\hat\beta_j)$ in the active KKT equation $\tfrac1n X_j^\top r = \lambda s_j$; if an active coefficient $\hat\beta_j$ moves through zero, its required sign would have to flip, but the coefficient is now zero, so the predictor must become inactive instead. The modification (Efron, Hastie, Johnstone & Tibshirani) is: as the LARS step proceeds, monitor each active coefficient, and if one is about to reach zero before the next variable joins, stop at that crossing, remove $j$ from the active set $A$, and recompute the equiangular direction with the smaller $A$. Without this rule LARS would carry $\hat\beta_j$ past zero with the wrong sign, violating the lasso KKT condition; with it, LARS traces the exact lasso path, including the drop events the lasso allows but forward methods do not.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The naive elastic net carries a double shrinkage that the augmented-lasso representation makes precise. Solving the lasso on $(X_*, y_*)$ shrinks once through the $\ell_1$ threshold and a second time through the contraction implicit in the $1/\sqrt{1+2\lambda_2}$ scaling of $X_*$, so the naive coefficients underpredict; in the orthonormal case Exercise 4 shows the exact factor $1/(1+2\lambda_2)$. The corrected estimator $\hat\beta = (1+2\lambda_2)\hat\beta^{\,\mathrm{naive}}$ removes the second shrinkage while preserving the support and the grouping, and Zou & Hastie [ref: TODO_REF Zou, H. & Hastie, T. — Regularization and Variable Selection via the Elastic Net] document that on correlated designs the corrected estimator improves prediction over both the lasso (which over-selects and tie-breaks erratically) and ridge (which never selects). The mixing is usually parameterized by $\alpha = \lambda_2/(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2) \in [0,1]$, interpolating from the lasso ($\alpha = 0$) to ridge ($\alpha = 1$), with $\lambda$ controlling overall strength; cross-validation selects the pair, and warm-started coordinate descent solves the whole $(\alpha, \lambda)$ grid.

Least angle regression turns the lasso from a single convex program into a homotopy. The path $\lambda \mapsto \hat\beta(\lambda)$ is continuous and piecewise affine (Exercise 6), with knots $\lambda_1 > \lambda_2 > \cdots > 0$ at which the active set changes. Between knots, $\hat\beta_A(\lambda) = (X_A^\top X_A)^{-1}(X_A^\top y - n\lambda s_A)$ runs along a line; at each knot LARS solves a $|A|\times|A|$ system to update the equiangular direction (Exercise 7) and a one-dimensional search to find the next join-or-drop event. Computing $(X_A^\top X_A)^{-1}$ incrementally by rank-one Cholesky updates costs $O(|A|^2)$ per knot, and with $O(p)$ knots in the typical regime the full lasso path costs $O(p^3 + np^2)$ — the order of a single ordinary least-squares fit, but delivering every solution along the way. This is the computational payoff that made the lasso practical: one does not refit at each $\lambda$; one walks the path. Efron, Hastie, Johnstone & Tibshirani [ref: TODO_REF Efron, B., Hastie, T., Johnstone, I. & Tibshirani, R. — Least Angle Regression] also placed LARS between the greedy forward selection and the infinitesimal forward-stagewise procedure, showing all three trace nearly the same profiles, with LARS the least committal and stagewise the most cautious.

The **degrees of freedom along the path** are exact and remarkably simple. For the LARS algorithm stopped after $k$ steps, the degrees of freedom equal $k$; for the lasso at a fixed $\lambda$, the degrees of freedom equal the expected size of the active set, $\mathbb{E}\lvert\hat S(\lambda)\rvert$. Both follow from Stein's identity applied to the piecewise-affine fitted-value map (Proposition 3), the same divergence computation that gave the lasso df [45.06.06] and that links back to the Stein shrinkage theory of [45.06.04]. The simplicity is structural: on each linear piece the fit is the projection onto the span of the active columns, whose trace is the active-set size, and the knots form a null set, so the expected divergence is the expected active-set cardinality. This makes information criteria (Mallows-type, AIC, BIC) directly computable along the path, turning the lasso/elastic-net path into a model-selection device [45.06.09]: each knot is a candidate model with a known complexity, and one selects among them by the penalized criterion or by cross-validation, reading off the chosen $\lambda$ from the path.

The modern solver is not LARS but **coordinate descent** [44.06.07]. Because the lasso and elastic-net objectives are smooth plus a *separable* nonsmooth penalty, the optimality condition decouples coordinate by coordinate, and cyclically minimizing one coordinate at a time — each subproblem solved in closed form by the soft-threshold-then-ridge-scale update of Exercise 4, the $\ell_1$ proximal step of [44.06.02] — converges to the global minimizer. Computing the path along a decreasing grid of $\lambda$ with warm starts (initialize each fit at the previous solution) makes coordinate descent faster than LARS in practice, especially for large sparse $X$ where touching one column at a time exploits sparsity. The elastic net's $\ell_2$ term improves coordinate descent's conditioning, so the grouping that helps statistically also helps the solver. The two algorithms are complementary: LARS gives the exact knots and the df bookkeeping, coordinate descent gives the fast grid solve.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason the elastic net and LARS belong to one unit is that a single optimality system governs both the penalty and the path: the stationarity condition $X^\top(y - X\hat\beta) = 2\lambda_2\hat\beta + \lambda_1 s$, $s \in \partial\lVert\hat\beta\rVert_1$, produces the grouping bound by differencing two active equations, removes the lasso's selection cap through the strong convexity its $\ell_2$ term supplies, and — specialized to $\lambda_2 = 0$ — yields the piecewise-affine active-set solution $\hat\beta_A(\lambda) = (X_A^\top X_A)^{-1}(X_A^\top y - n\lambda s_A)$ that LARS walks. This is exactly the $\ell_1$-plus-$\ell_2$ specialization of the proximal/subgradient theory of [44.06.02], whose soft-threshold prox is the coordinate update [44.06.07] that solves the path, and whose composite Fermat rule $0 \in \nabla f + \partial g$ is the elastic-net KKT system.

The central insight is that the elastic net generalises ridge [45.06.03] and the lasso [45.06.06] simultaneously — it is the bridge penalty's interior, inheriting ridge's grouping of correlated predictors and the lasso's corner-geometry selection — and that the corrected rescaling $(1+2\lambda_2)$ is precisely the undoing of the augmented-design contraction, so naive and corrected estimators share a support but differ by a known factor. Putting these together, the degrees of freedom along the path is dual to the geometry of the knots: each knot adds or drops one active column, the fit is the projection onto the active span, and Stein's identity [45.06.04] turns that projection trace into the expected active-set size, which is exactly what makes the path a calibrated model-selection ladder [45.06.09]. The bridge from a single penalized program to an entire regularization path is the piecewise linearity that the $\ell_1$ subgradient and the active-set linear system together force.

## Full proof set [Master]

The grouping bound, the augmented-design reduction, the orthonormal elastic-net solution, the piecewise-linear path, and the equiangular direction are proved in full in the Formal definition, Key theorem, and Exercises sections. The remaining Master claims are recorded here.

**Proposition 1 (augmented-design reduction and the $p$-variable capacity).** *With $X_* = (1+2\lambda_2)^{-1/2}[X;\,\sqrt{2\lambda_2}\,I_p]$ and $y_* = [y;\,0]$, the naive elastic net with parameters $(\lambda_1,\lambda_2)$ minimizes, in the variable $b = (1+2\lambda_2)^{-1/2}\beta$, the lasso objective $\tfrac12\lVert y_* - X_* b'\rVert_2^2 + \gamma\lVert b'\rVert_1$ with $\gamma = \lambda_1/\sqrt{1+2\lambda_2}$, and $X_*$ has full column rank $p$ for every $\lambda_2 > 0$, so the estimator may have up to $p$ nonzero coordinates.*

*Proof.* Expand the augmented residual sum of squares with $\beta$ as variable:
$$ \tfrac12\lVert y_* - X_*\beta\rVert_2^2 = \tfrac12\big\lVert y - (1+2\lambda_2)^{-1/2}X\beta\big\rVert_2^2 + \tfrac12(1+2\lambda_2)^{-1}\big\lVert\sqrt{2\lambda_2}\,\beta\big\rVert_2^2. $$
Substitute the change of variable $\beta = (1+2\lambda_2)^{1/2}b$ (so $(1+2\lambda_2)^{-1/2}X\beta = Xb$ and $(1+2\lambda_2)^{-1}\cdot 2\lambda_2\lVert\beta\rVert_2^2 = 2\lambda_2\lVert b\rVert_2^2$):
$$ \tfrac12\lVert y_* - X_*\beta\rVert_2^2 = \tfrac12\lVert y - Xb\rVert_2^2 + \lambda_2\lVert b\rVert_2^2. $$
The penalty becomes $\gamma\lVert\beta\rVert_1 = \gamma(1+2\lambda_2)^{1/2}\lVert b\rVert_1 = \lambda_1\lVert b\rVert_1$ exactly when $\gamma = \lambda_1/\sqrt{1+2\lambda_2}$. So the augmented lasso in $\beta$ equals the naive elastic net in $b$, and the minimizers correspond under $\beta = (1+2\lambda_2)^{1/2}b$. For the rank claim, the lower block $\sqrt{2\lambda_2}\,I_p$ has rank $p$ for $\lambda_2 > 0$; stacking rows can only increase rank, so $\operatorname{rank}(X_*) = p$. A lasso on a full-column-rank design has no a priori cap on the number of active variables (the $\min(n,p)$ bound for the plain lasso comes from $X$ alone having rank $\le n$), so the elastic net can activate all $p$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (strong convexity and uniqueness for $p > n$).** *For $\lambda_2 > 0$ the naive elastic-net objective is $2\lambda_2$-strongly convex on $\mathbb{R}^p$, hence has a unique minimizer for every $X$ (including $p > n$, where $X^\top X$ is singular).*

*Proof.* The objective is $f(\beta) + g(\beta)$ with $f(\beta) = \tfrac12\lVert y - X\beta\rVert_2^2 + \lambda_2\lVert\beta\rVert_2^2$ smooth and $g(\beta) = \lambda_1\lVert\beta\rVert_1$ convex. The Hessian of $f$ is $\nabla^2 f = X^\top X + 2\lambda_2 I$. Since $X^\top X \succeq 0$, $\nabla^2 f \succeq 2\lambda_2 I \succ 0$, so $f$ is $2\lambda_2$-strongly convex; adding the convex $g$ preserves $2\lambda_2$-strong convexity. A strongly convex function on $\mathbb{R}^p$ is coercive and has a unique global minimizer, independent of whether $X^\top X$ is invertible. This is the structural advantage over the lasso ($\lambda_2 = 0$), whose objective is convex but not strictly convex when $p > n$, allowing a polytope of minimizers [45.06.06]. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (degrees of freedom along the path).** *For $y \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2 I_n)$: (i) the LARS fit after $k$ steps has $\mathrm{df} = k$; (ii) the lasso fit at fixed $\lambda$ has $\mathrm{df}(\lambda) = \mathbb{E}\lvert\hat S(\lambda)\rvert$, the expected active-set size.*

*Proof (following Efron-Hastie-Johnstone-Tibshirani via Stein).* Effective degrees of freedom is $\mathrm{df} = \tfrac1{\sigma^2}\sum_i\operatorname{Cov}(\hat y_i, y_i) = \mathbb{E}[\operatorname{div}\hat y(y)]$ by Stein's identity (the Gaussian integration-by-parts of [45.06.04]), valid for the continuous, almost-everywhere-differentiable fitted-value map. For the lasso at fixed $\lambda$, on the open region where the active set $\hat S$ and signs are locally constant, the fit is $\hat y = X_{\hat S}(X_{\hat S}^\top X_{\hat S})^{-1}(X_{\hat S}^\top y - n\lambda\,\operatorname{sign}_{\hat S})$, whose Jacobian in $y$ is the projection $P_{\hat S} = X_{\hat S}(X_{\hat S}^\top X_{\hat S})^{-1}X_{\hat S}^\top$ of rank $\lvert\hat S\rvert$. Its divergence (trace) is $\lvert\hat S\rvert$; the knot set where $\hat S$ changes is Lebesgue-null, so $\operatorname{div}\hat y = \lvert\hat S(\lambda)\rvert$ almost everywhere, giving (ii). For LARS after $k$ steps the active set has size $k$ on each path segment by construction (one variable added per step, modulo drops), and the same projection-trace argument gives divergence $k$; the simple-knot covariance lemma of Efron et al. handles the boundary contributions, yielding $\mathrm{df} = k$, statement (i). $\square$

**Proposition 4 (coordinate descent solves the elastic net).** *For $\lambda_2 \ge 0$ and $\lambda_1 > 0$, cyclic coordinate minimization of the elastic-net objective, with each coordinate update $\hat\beta_j \leftarrow S_{\lambda_1}(z_j^{(-j)})/(1 + 2\lambda_2 + \tfrac1n\lVert X_j\rVert_2^2 - 1)$ where $z_j^{(-j)} = \tfrac1n X_j^\top(y - X_{-j}\hat\beta_{-j})$ is the partial residual correlation, converges to the global minimizer.*

*Proof.* The objective is $f(\beta) + \sum_j g_j(\beta_j)$ with $f$ smooth convex and each $g_j(\beta_j) = \lambda_1|\beta_j|$ convex — a smooth-plus-separable composite, the structure for which block-coordinate descent reaches a global minimizer [44.06.07] (the optimality condition $0 \in \nabla f + \partial g$ decouples across coordinates because $\partial g = \prod_j\partial g_j$). Each one-coordinate subproblem minimizes $\tfrac12 a_j\beta_j^2 - z_j^{(-j)}\beta_j + \lambda_2\beta_j^2 + \lambda_1|\beta_j|$ with $a_j = \tfrac1n\lVert X_j\rVert_2^2 = 1$ for standardized columns; collecting quadratic terms gives coefficient $(a_j + 2\lambda_2)$ and the closed-form soft-threshold-then-scale update $\hat\beta_j = S_{\lambda_1}(z_j^{(-j)})/(a_j + 2\lambda_2)$, the $\ell_1$ proximal step of [44.06.02] specialized to the elastic net. Because the nonsmooth part is separable and the smooth part has a Lipschitz coordinatewise gradient, the cyclic sweeps generate a decreasing objective sequence whose limit points are coordinatewise stationary, hence (by separability) globally optimal. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

The lasso and its oracle theory [45.06.06] is the method the elastic net refines: the elastic net keeps the lasso's $\ell_1$ corner geometry and exact-zero selection but adds an $\ell_2$ dose that repairs the lasso's two structural defects — its cap of $\min(n,p)$ selected variables and its erratic all-but-one behaviour among correlated predictors — while LARS is the homotopy that constructs the lasso solution path, with its one-line sign-crossing modification turning the additive LARS sequence into the exact lasso path that drops as well as adds variables.

Ridge regression and shrinkage [45.06.03] supplies the $\ell_2$ penalty whose differential action across a correlated pair is the grouping effect: the elastic-net stationarity term $2\lambda_2(\hat\beta_i - \hat\beta_j)$ is ridge's smooth contraction made to act on coefficient *differences*, and the corrected estimator $(1+2\lambda_2)\hat\beta^{\,\mathrm{naive}}$ undoes exactly the ridge-induced double shrinkage that the augmented-design lasso introduces.

The proximal operator and proximal-gradient method [44.06.02] gives the closed-form coordinate update: the elastic-net coordinate solution $S_{\lambda_1}(z)/(1+2\lambda_2)$ is the proximal operator of $\lambda_1|\cdot| + \lambda_2(\cdot)^2$, the soft-threshold-then-shrink composition, and the elastic-net KKT system is the composite Fermat rule $0 \in \nabla f + \partial g$ of that unit.

Coordinate descent [44.06.07] is the modern path solver: because the elastic-net penalty is smooth-plus-separable, cyclic coordinate minimization with warm starts along a $\lambda$ grid converges to the global minimizer and outperforms LARS on large sparse designs, and the $\ell_2$ term improves the coordinate subproblems' conditioning, so the statistical grouping device doubles as a numerical preconditioner.

Subset selection and model selection [45.06.09] is where the path becomes a model-selection tool: the piecewise-linear lasso/elastic-net path visits a nested sequence of candidate models at its knots, each with degrees of freedom equal to its active-set size, so information criteria and cross-validation select $\lambda$ by reading complexity directly off the path rather than refitting a combinatorial family of subsets.

The James-Stein estimator and Stein's identity [45.06.04] supplies the integration-by-parts behind the degrees-of-freedom result: the divergence formula $\mathrm{df} = \mathbb{E}[\operatorname{div}\hat y]$ that gives James-Stein its risk accounting is the same identity that equates the lasso path's degrees of freedom with the expected active-set size and the LARS df with the step count.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Least angle regression was introduced by Bradley Efron, Trevor Hastie, Iain Johnstone and Robert Tibshirani in their 2004 *Annals of Statistics* paper [ref: TODO_REF Efron, B., Hastie, T., Johnstone, I. & Tibshirani, R. — Least Angle Regression], read with discussion, which unified three apparently distinct procedures — greedy forward selection, the cautious forward-stagewise method long used in boosting, and the lasso of Tibshirani (1996) — by showing that a single equiangular geometry generates all of them, and that a one-line modification of the LARS algorithm produces the entire lasso regularization path at the computational cost of one ordinary least-squares fit. The same paper proved the degrees-of-freedom identity, that the LARS fit after $k$ steps has exactly $k$ degrees of freedom, via Stein's covariance formula, which retroactively justified counting model complexity by active-set size.

The elastic net was introduced the following year by Hui Zou and Trevor Hastie in their 2005 *Journal of the Royal Statistical Society, Series B* paper [ref: TODO_REF Zou, H. & Hastie, T. — Regularization and Variable Selection via the Elastic Net], motivated explicitly by three failures of the lasso on the microarray and other $p \gg n$ data then driving statistical genetics: the lasso could select at most $n$ variables, it behaved erratically among groups of correlated genes by keeping one and discarding the rest, and it was outperformed in prediction by ridge whenever predictors were correlated. Their combined $\ell_1 + \ell_2$ penalty repaired all three, with the augmented-design representation reducing the problem to a lasso (so LARS-EN computes its path) and the grouping-effect bound giving a precise guarantee that correlated predictors receive similar coefficients. The naive-versus-corrected distinction, and the $(1+\lambda_2)$ rescaling that removes the double shrinkage, were established in the same paper. The synthesis of LARS, the lasso, the elastic net and coordinate-descent path solvers into a single account of regularized regression was consolidated by Hastie, Tibshirani and Friedman [ref: TODO_REF Hastie, T., Tibshirani, R. & Friedman, J. — The Elements of Statistical Learning (2nd ed.)] in chapter 3 of *The Elements of Statistical Learning*.
