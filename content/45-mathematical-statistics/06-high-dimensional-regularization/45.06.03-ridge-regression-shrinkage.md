## Intuition [Beginner]

Least squares hands you the weights that fit your data best, full stop. That sounds ideal, but it has a failure mode: when your predictors are nearly redundant — two columns of data that say almost the same thing — the fitting machine cannot decide how to split the credit between them. It compensates by inventing huge offsetting weights, a giant positive one on the first predictor and a giant negative one on the second. These weights are wildly unstable: collect a fresh dataset and they swing somewhere completely different. The fit looks great on the data you have and predicts terribly on data you do not.

Ridge regression fixes this by adding a penalty for large weights. Instead of asking only "how small can I make the total squared miss?", it asks "how small can I make the total squared miss plus a charge on the size of the weights?" The charge is tuned by a dial. Turn the dial to zero and you are back to plain least squares. Turn it up and the weights are pulled toward zero, gently at first, harder as you keep turning.

Why does pulling the weights toward zero help? Because a small, deliberate amount of being-wrong-on-average buys a large reduction in instability. You accept a fit that is slightly off the true relationship in exchange for a fit that barely moves when the data changes. This is the bias-variance bargain from the previous unit, made into a knob you can turn.

The everyday image is a budget. Least squares spends without limit to match the data; ridge puts the weights on an allowance. Under the allowance, the fit cannot chase every quirk of one dataset, so it tracks the stable, repeatable signal instead.

## Visual [Beginner]

| Dial setting (penalty strength) | What happens to the weights | Bias | Variance | Typical outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Zero | exactly the least-squares weights | none | high (can be huge if predictors overlap) | overfits, unstable |
| Small | weights pulled slightly toward zero | small | lower | usually better on new data |
| Moderate | clear shrinkage, stable weights | moderate | low | often the sweet spot |
| Very large | weights crushed toward zero | large | tiny | underfits, predicts the average |

![A horizontal axis labelled penalty strength running from zero on the left to large on the right. Several wiggly coefficient paths start at their spread-out least-squares values on the left and are drawn smoothly inward toward the zero line as the axis increases, none ever crossing exactly to zero. A second small inset shows a U-shaped test-error curve whose minimum sits at a moderate penalty, marking the best dial setting.](/img/ridge-regression-shrinkage-placeholder.svg)

The single picture to carry forward: every coefficient path slides toward zero as you raise the penalty, but the directions in your data that carry the most information resist the pull, while the flimsy, redundant directions collapse fast. Ridge does not switch predictors off; it quiets the unreliable ones.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take two data points and one predictor with no intercept, so the model is $\hat y = b\,x$. The data are $x = 2$ at $y = 4$, and $x = 1$ at $y = 1$. Plain least squares picks the slope $b$ that minimizes $(4 - 2b)^2 + (1 - b)^2$.

The least-squares slope solves the normal equation. The sum of $x^2$ is $4 + 1 = 5$, and the sum of $x\,y$ is $2 \times 4 + 1 \times 1 = 9$, so $b_{\text{OLS}} = 9 / 5 = 1.8$.

Now add a ridge penalty with dial value $\lambda = 5$. Ridge minimizes $(4 - 2b)^2 + (1 - b)^2 + 5 b^2$. The recipe changes the denominator: it becomes the sum of $x^2$ plus $\lambda$, that is $5 + 5 = 10$, while the numerator stays $9$. So the ridge slope is $b_{\text{ridge}} = 9 / 10 = 0.9$.

The penalty has cut the slope exactly in half, from $1.8$ to $0.9$. Try $\lambda = 1$ instead: the denominator is $5 + 1 = 6$, giving $b = 9/6 = 1.5$. And $\lambda = 20$ gives denominator $25$, so $b = 9/25 = 0.36$.

What this tells us: the ridge slope is always the least-squares slope scaled down by the factor $5 / (5 + \lambda)$ — the original $x^2$-sum over itself-plus-penalty. At $\lambda = 0$ the factor is $1$ (no shrinkage); as $\lambda$ grows the factor shrinks toward $0$. The penalty never flips the sign and never sets the slope to exactly zero; it only damps it, and the more penalty you add, the more it damps.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a design matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$ and response $y \in \mathbb{R}^n$. Assume the predictors are centered and scaled to unit variance and the intercept is fitted separately (so $X$ carries no column of ones); the penalty is applied to the slope coefficients only. For a penalty parameter $\lambda \ge 0$, the **ridge regression** (or **Tikhonov-regularized**) estimator is the minimizer of the penalized residual sum of squares

$$ \hat\beta(\lambda) = \arg\min_{\beta \in \mathbb{R}^p} \Big\{ \lVert y - X\beta \rVert^2 + \lambda \lVert \beta \rVert^2 \Big\}. $$

The criterion is a strictly convex quadratic in $\beta$ whenever $\lambda > 0$. Differentiating and setting the gradient to zero gives the **regularized normal equations** $(X^\top X + \lambda I)\beta = X^\top y$, and since $X^\top X$ is positive semidefinite, $X^\top X + \lambda I$ is positive definite for $\lambda > 0$, hence invertible. The estimator is

$$ \hat\beta(\lambda) = (X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1} X^\top y. $$

The decisive structural fact, sharpening the OLS theory [45.06.01]: this solution **exists and is unique even when $X^\top X$ is singular** — in particular when $p > n$ and ordinary least squares has no unique solution. Adding $\lambda I$ lifts every eigenvalue of $X^\top X$ by $\lambda$, curing the rank deficiency. The fitted values are $\hat y = X\hat\beta(\lambda) = H_\lambda y$ with the **ridge hat matrix**

$$ H_\lambda = X(X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1} X^\top, $$

which is symmetric but *not* idempotent (it is not a projection), and the **effective degrees of freedom** are $\mathrm{df}(\lambda) = \operatorname{tr}(H_\lambda)$, decreasing from $\operatorname{rank}(X)$ at $\lambda = 0$ to $0$ as $\lambda \to \infty$ — the continuous complexity index of [45.06.02].

The **singular value view** makes the mechanism transparent. Write the thin SVD $X = U D V^\top$ [01.01.12] with $U \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times r}$, $V \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times r}$ having orthonormal columns $u_j, v_j$, and $D = \operatorname{diag}(d_1, \dots, d_r)$ the singular values, $r = \operatorname{rank}(X)$. The ridge fitted values decompose along the principal directions as

$$ \hat y = H_\lambda y = \sum_{j=1}^{r} u_j \, \frac{d_j^2}{d_j^2 + \lambda} \, (u_j^\top y). $$

Each coordinate $u_j^\top y$ is multiplied by the **shrinkage factor** $d_j^2/(d_j^2 + \lambda) \in (0, 1]$. Directions with large singular values (where the data carry strong, well-determined signal) are barely touched; directions with small singular values (where the design is nearly collinear and the OLS coefficient is unstable) are shrunk hard. Ordinary least squares is the $\lambda = 0$ case, where every factor equals $1$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *The ridge hat matrix is not a projection.* $H_\lambda$ is symmetric but $H_\lambda^2 \ne H_\lambda$ for $\lambda > 0$; its eigenvalues $d_j^2/(d_j^2 + \lambda)$ lie strictly between $0$ and $1$, so $\hat y$ is a *contraction* of $y$ toward the origin along principal directions, not an orthogonal projection onto a subspace.
- *Ridge is not scale-invariant.* Because the penalty $\lambda \lVert\beta\rVert^2$ depends on the units of each predictor, rescaling a column changes the solution. Standardizing the predictors before fitting is part of the method, not an optional cleanup; the intercept is excluded from the penalty for the same reason.
- *Existence does not require full rank.* The phrase "the ridge solution" is well-posed for any $\lambda > 0$ regardless of $\operatorname{rank}(X)$; the regularizer guarantees a unique minimizer even when $n < p$, which is precisely the high-dimensional regime where OLS fails to define one.
- *Shrinkage toward zero is a modeling choice.* Ridge shrinks toward the origin only because the prior mean is taken to be zero after centering; shrinking toward any fixed $\beta_0$ replaces $\lVert\beta\rVert^2$ by $\lVert\beta - \beta_0\rVert^2$ and changes nothing structural.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (existence of an improving ridge constant — Hoerl & Kennard 1970).** Under the linear model $y = X\beta + \varepsilon$ with $\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon] = 0$, $\operatorname{Cov}(\varepsilon) = \sigma^2 I_n$, and $X$ of full column rank, let $\mathrm{MSE}(\lambda) = \mathbb{E}\big[\lVert \hat\beta(\lambda) - \beta \rVert^2\big]$ be the total mean squared error of the ridge estimator. Then whenever $\sigma^2 > 0$ there exists $\lambda > 0$ with $\mathrm{MSE}(\lambda) < \mathrm{MSE}(0)$: ridge strictly beats ordinary least squares in total MSE. The proof follows Hoerl & Kennard [ref: TODO_REF Hoerl 1970] and the SVD presentation of Hastie, Tibshirani & Friedman [ref: TODO_REF Hastie 2009] §3.4.1.

*Proof.* Rotate to the eigenbasis of $X^\top X$. With the SVD $X = U D V^\top$, write $\alpha = V^\top \beta$ for the true coefficient in singular coordinates and $\hat\alpha_j(\lambda) = V^\top \hat\beta(\lambda)$ for the estimator. A direct computation from $\hat\beta(\lambda) = (X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1} X^\top y$ gives, in coordinate $j$,

$$ \hat\alpha_j(\lambda) = \frac{d_j}{d_j^2 + \lambda}\, u_j^\top y, \qquad \mathbb{E}[\hat\alpha_j(\lambda)] = \frac{d_j^2}{d_j^2 + \lambda}\, \alpha_j, \qquad \operatorname{Var}(\hat\alpha_j(\lambda)) = \frac{d_j^2 \sigma^2}{(d_j^2 + \lambda)^2}, $$

using $u_j^\top y = d_j \alpha_j + u_j^\top\varepsilon$ with $\operatorname{Var}(u_j^\top\varepsilon) = \sigma^2$. Because $V$ is orthogonal, total MSE is the sum of coordinate MSEs, and each coordinate splits as squared bias plus variance [45.06.02]:

$$ \mathrm{MSE}(\lambda) = \sum_{j=1}^{p} \left[ \underbrace{\Big(\frac{\lambda}{d_j^2 + \lambda}\Big)^2 \alpha_j^2}_{\text{squared bias}} + \underbrace{\frac{d_j^2 \sigma^2}{(d_j^2 + \lambda)^2}}_{\text{variance}} \right]. $$

Write $g(\lambda)$ for this sum and differentiate term by term. For the variance part, $\frac{d}{d\lambda}\frac{d_j^2\sigma^2}{(d_j^2+\lambda)^2} = \frac{-2 d_j^2 \sigma^2}{(d_j^2+\lambda)^3}$. For the bias part, $\frac{d}{d\lambda}\frac{\lambda^2 \alpha_j^2}{(d_j^2+\lambda)^2} = \frac{2\lambda d_j^2 \alpha_j^2}{(d_j^2+\lambda)^3}$. Summing,

$$ g'(\lambda) = \sum_{j=1}^{p} \frac{2 d_j^2 \big(\lambda \alpha_j^2 - \sigma^2\big)}{(d_j^2 + \lambda)^3}. $$

Evaluate at $\lambda = 0$: every numerator becomes $2 d_j^2(-\sigma^2) < 0$, so $g'(0) = -\sum_j 2\sigma^2/d_j^2 < 0$. The squared-bias terms enter through $\lambda^2$ and so contribute nothing to the first derivative at the origin, while the variance terms contribute a strictly negative slope. Since $g$ is continuously differentiable and $g'(0) < 0$, there is an interval $(0, \lambda_0)$ on which $g(\lambda) < g(0) = \mathrm{MSE}(0)$. Any $\lambda$ in that interval improves on least squares. $\square$

**Bridge.** The proof's engine is the SVD rotation that diagonalizes the problem so that total MSE becomes a sum of one-dimensional bias-variance trade-offs, each governed by the shrinkage factor $d_j^2/(d_j^2+\lambda)$. This builds toward the full advanced theory below, where the same factor controls effective degrees of freedom and the Bayesian posterior mean, and it appears again in [45.06.06] when the lasso replaces the smooth $\ell_2$ contraction with a thresholding $\ell_1$ map. The foundational reason ridge can win is that least squares sits at the *boundary* of the admissible region — it is the unbiased corner — so its MSE derivative in the bias direction is zero while the variance can only be reduced by moving inward; this is exactly the single-coordinate computation of [45.06.02] Exercise 5 promoted to all $p$ directions at once. The result generalises the Gauss-Markov benchmark of [45.06.01] rather than contradicting it: Gauss-Markov ranks OLS first among *unbiased linear* estimators, and ridge wins only by abandoning unbiasedness, so the central insight is that the unbiasedness constraint — not optimality itself — is what high-dimensional statistics relaxes. Putting these together, ridge is the smooth, always-feasible shrinkage that the bias-variance decomposition predicts must exist.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show directly that $\hat\beta(\lambda) = (X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1} X^\top y$ minimizes $\lVert y - X\beta\rVert^2 + \lambda\lVert\beta\rVert^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set the gradient of the criterion to zero; the gradient of $\lambda\lVert\beta\rVert^2$ is $2\lambda\beta$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The gradient is $-2X^\top(y - X\beta) + 2\lambda\beta = -2X^\top y + 2(X^\top X + \lambda I)\beta$. Setting it to zero gives $(X^\top X + \lambda I)\beta = X^\top y$, and the Hessian $2(X^\top X + \lambda I)$ is positive definite for $\lambda > 0$, so the stationary point is the unique global minimum: $\hat\beta(\lambda) = (X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1}X^\top y$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

A design has singular values $d_1 = 3$, $d_2 = 1$. At $\lambda = 3$, compute the two shrinkage factors $d_j^2/(d_j^2 + \lambda)$ and the effective degrees of freedom $\mathrm{df}(\lambda)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathrm{df}(\lambda)$ is the sum of the shrinkage factors.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Factor 1: $9/(9+3) = 9/12 = 0.75$. Factor 2: $1/(1+3) = 1/4 = 0.25$. Effective degrees of freedom $\mathrm{df}(3) = 0.75 + 0.25 = 1.0$, down from $\mathrm{df}(0) = 2$. The strong direction keeps three-quarters of its signal while the weak direction is cut to a quarter.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove $\mathrm{df}(\lambda) = \operatorname{tr}(H_\lambda) = \sum_{j} d_j^2/(d_j^2 + \lambda)$ from the SVD.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute $X = UDV^\top$ into $H_\lambda = X(X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1}X^\top$ and use $V^\top V = I$, then cyclicity of the trace.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $X = UDV^\top$, $X^\top X = V D^2 V^\top$, so $(X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1} = V(D^2 + \lambda I)^{-1}V^\top$ (on the range, extending $V$ to an orthonormal basis). Then $H_\lambda = U D V^\top V (D^2+\lambda I)^{-1} V^\top V D U^\top = U D(D^2 + \lambda I)^{-1} D\, U^\top = U \operatorname{diag}\!\big(d_j^2/(d_j^2+\lambda)\big) U^\top$. Taking the trace and using $\operatorname{tr}(U M U^\top) = \operatorname{tr}(M)$ for $U$ with orthonormal columns gives $\operatorname{tr}(H_\lambda) = \sum_j d_j^2/(d_j^2+\lambda)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

For an orthonormal design $X^\top X = I$ (so every $d_j = 1$), show $\hat\beta(\lambda) = \hat\beta_{\text{OLS}}/(1 + \lambda)$ and identify the shrinkage factor.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

When $X^\top X = I$, the regularized Gram matrix is $(1+\lambda)I$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $X^\top X = I$, $\hat\beta(\lambda) = (I + \lambda I)^{-1}X^\top y = \frac{1}{1+\lambda}X^\top y$. But $\hat\beta_{\text{OLS}} = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top y = X^\top y$, so $\hat\beta(\lambda) = \hat\beta_{\text{OLS}}/(1+\lambda)$. Every coordinate is scaled by the same factor $1/(1+\lambda) = d_j^2/(d_j^2+\lambda)$ with $d_j = 1$. This is the uniform-shrinkage special case, and it is exactly the single-coordinate model of [45.06.02] Exercise 5.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

The augmented-data trick: show that ridge with penalty $\lambda$ equals ordinary least squares on the stacked design $\tilde X = \begin{psmallmatrix} X \\ \sqrt{\lambda}\,I_p \end{psmallmatrix}$ and stacked response $\tilde y = \begin{psmallmatrix} y \\ 0 \end{psmallmatrix}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\tilde X^\top \tilde X$ and $\tilde X^\top \tilde y$ and compare with the ridge normal equations.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\tilde X^\top \tilde X = X^\top X + (\sqrt{\lambda}I)^\top(\sqrt{\lambda}I) = X^\top X + \lambda I$, and $\tilde X^\top \tilde y = X^\top y + \sqrt{\lambda}I \cdot 0 = X^\top y$. The OLS normal equations for the augmented problem read $(X^\top X + \lambda I)\beta = X^\top y$, identical to the ridge normal equations. So $\hat\beta(\lambda) = (\tilde X^\top \tilde X)^{-1}\tilde X^\top \tilde y$. This also shows existence cleanly: $\tilde X$ has full column rank $p$ for any $\lambda > 0$ (the $\sqrt\lambda I_p$ block alone supplies $p$ independent rows), even when $X$ does not.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the leave-one-out cross-validation shortcut for ridge: $\mathrm{CV}(\lambda) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \big(\frac{y_i - \hat y_i}{1 - (H_\lambda)_{ii}}\big)^2$, where $\hat y = H_\lambda y$ on the full data.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use that $H_\lambda$ is a *linear* smoother. The leave-one-out residual relates to the full residual through the diagonal entry $(H_\lambda)_{ii}$ by the same identity that holds for any linear smoother.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Ridge is a linear smoother, $\hat y = H_\lambda y$ with $H_\lambda$ independent of $y$. For any such smoother whose fit at point $i$ depends on $y_i$ only through the row $(H_\lambda)_{i\cdot}$, the prediction with case $i$ removed, $\hat y_i^{(-i)}$, satisfies $y_i - \hat y_i^{(-i)} = (y_i - \hat y_i)/(1 - (H_\lambda)_{ii})$. (Refit with $y_i$ replaced by its own out-of-fold prediction; the linear smoother then reproduces that value, and solving the resulting one-line identity for the deleted residual gives the formula.) Squaring and averaging gives $\mathrm{CV}(\lambda)$ without $n$ separate refits. Replacing each $(H_\lambda)_{ii}$ by the average $\mathrm{df}(\lambda)/n$ yields generalized cross-validation $\mathrm{GCV}(\lambda) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i (y_i-\hat y_i)^2/(1 - \mathrm{df}(\lambda)/n)^2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Explain why ridge ($\ell_2$ penalty) does not produce exactly-zero coefficients, whereas the lasso ($\ell_1$ penalty) does, using the SVD/coordinate picture for ridge and the constraint geometry for the contrast.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A ridge coordinate is multiplied by a factor strictly between $0$ and $1$; what value of $\lambda$ would make it exactly zero? For the lasso, think about the corners of the constraint ball.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In ridge each coordinate is scaled by $d_j^2/(d_j^2+\lambda)$, which is strictly positive for every finite $\lambda$ and every $d_j > 0$; a positive number times a generic nonzero coordinate is nonzero, so coefficients approach zero only in the limit $\lambda \to \infty$, never reaching it at finite penalty. Geometrically, the ridge constraint region $\lVert\beta\rVert^2 \le t$ is a smooth ball, and the solution is where an error-contour ellipse first touches it — generically on the smooth interior of a face, away from any axis. The lasso constraint $\lVert\beta\rVert_1 \le t$ is a polytope with vertices and edges *on* the axes, so the first contact frequently lands at a corner where some coordinates are exactly zero. The smoothness of the $\ell_2$ ball versus the corners of the $\ell_1$ ball is the geometric source of ridge's pure shrinkage versus the lasso's variable selection; the contrast is developed in [45.06.06].

## Advanced results [Master]

The Bayesian derivation places ridge inside a coherent inferential frame. Take the conjugate Gaussian model $y \mid \beta \sim N(X\beta,\, \sigma^2 I_n)$ with prior $\beta \sim N(0,\, \tau^2 I_p)$. The log-posterior in $\beta$ is, up to additive constants,

$$ -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\lVert y - X\beta \rVert^2 - \frac{1}{2\tau^2}\lVert\beta\rVert^2, $$

and maximizing it over $\beta$ is exactly the ridge criterion with $\lambda = \sigma^2/\tau^2$. The posterior is itself Gaussian, $\beta \mid y \sim N\big(\hat\beta(\lambda),\, \sigma^2(X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1}\big)$, so the ridge estimator is simultaneously the maximum-a-posteriori and the posterior-mean estimate; for a Gaussian posterior the two coincide. The penalty strength is the noise-to-prior variance ratio: a tight prior (small $\tau^2$, strong belief that coefficients are near zero) forces a large $\lambda$ and heavy shrinkage, while a diffuse prior recovers least squares as $\tau^2 \to \infty$. This is the forward connection promised by [45.06.02]: shrinkage is prior information made quantitative, developed fully in the Bayesian treatment [45.03.02].

Effective degrees of freedom give ridge a calibrated complexity. With $\mathrm{df}(\lambda) = \sum_j d_j^2/(d_j^2 + \lambda)$, the optimism identity of [45.06.02] yields the in-sample risk surrogate $\mathbb{E}[\text{in-sample error}] = \mathbb{E}[\overline{\mathrm{err}}] + \frac{2\sigma^2}{n}\mathrm{df}(\lambda)$, and substituting an estimate of $\mathrm{df}$ into Mallows-type criteria or the generalized cross-validation score $\mathrm{GCV}(\lambda) = \frac{n^{-1}\lVert y - H_\lambda y\rVert^2}{(1 - \mathrm{df}(\lambda)/n)^2}$ selects $\lambda$ without a held-out set. In practice $\lambda$ is chosen by $K$-fold cross-validation over a logarithmic grid: for each candidate the average held-out squared error is computed, and the minimizer (or the largest $\lambda$ within one standard error of the minimum, the one-standard-error rule) is taken. Because ridge has a closed form, the entire **ridge trace** $\lambda \mapsto \hat\beta(\lambda)$ is a smooth path computed once from the SVD, so the cross-validation sweep is cheap.

Kernel ridge regression lifts the method to nonlinear and infinite-dimensional feature maps. Replace each $x_i$ by a feature vector $\phi(x_i)$ in a (possibly infinite-dimensional) space and penalize $\lVert\beta\rVert^2$ there. The closed form, after the matrix push-through identity $(\Phi^\top\Phi + \lambda I)^{-1}\Phi^\top = \Phi^\top(\Phi\Phi^\top + \lambda I)^{-1}$, becomes $\hat f(x) = k(x)^\top(K + \lambda I)^{-1} y$, where $K_{ij} = \langle\phi(x_i),\phi(x_j)\rangle = \kappa(x_i,x_j)$ is the kernel Gram matrix and $k(x)_i = \kappa(x, x_i)$. The feature map never appears explicitly — only inner products — which is the kernel trick. The estimator lives in the reproducing-kernel Hilbert space generated by $\kappa$, the setting developed in [45.08.01], and the representer theorem guarantees the solution is a finite combination of kernels at the data points.

Ridge sits at one end of a spectrum of penalties. The general bridge penalty $\lambda\sum_j |\beta_j|^q$ recovers ridge at $q = 2$ and the lasso at $q = 1$. The qualitative break occurs at $q = 1$: for $q > 1$ the penalty is differentiable at the origin and the solution map is a smooth contraction (no exact zeros, as in ridge), while at $q \le 1$ the penalty has a corner at the origin and the solution map thresholds, producing exact zeros and hence variable selection. The elastic net $\lambda_1\lVert\beta\rVert_1 + \lambda_2\lVert\beta\rVert_2^2$ combines both, inheriting ridge's stability under correlated predictors (it shrinks correlated coefficients together rather than arbitrarily picking one) and the lasso's sparsity. The $\ell_2$-versus-$\ell_1$ contrast that organizes this chapter is the contrast between a contraction and a threshold, exactly the SVD shrinkage of ridge against the soft-thresholding of [45.06.06].

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason ridge unifies regularization, Bayesian shrinkage, and complexity control is that the SVD diagonalizes the estimator into independent one-dimensional shrinkages, each with factor $d_j^2/(d_j^2+\lambda)$, so the bias, the variance, the effective degrees of freedom, and the posterior covariance are all functions of that single factor. This is exactly the multivariate promotion of the one-coordinate bias-variance calculation of [45.06.02], and putting these together with the Hoerl-Kennard existence theorem shows that the optimal penalty $\lambda = \sigma^2/\tau^2$ from the Gaussian prior and the MSE-minimizing penalty from the frequentist trade-off are the same quantity viewed from two sides — the noise-to-signal ratio. The central insight is that ridge generalises ordinary least squares by relaxing the Gauss-Markov unbiasedness constraint of [45.06.01]: the contraction toward zero is dual to the prior pull toward zero, and the bridge is the regularized Gram matrix $X^\top X + \lambda I$ whose eigenvalue lift simultaneously cures collinearity, guarantees existence when $p > n$, and quantifies the shrinkage. The same machinery, with inner products replaced by kernels, becomes kernel ridge regression and the RKHS theory of [45.08.01]; with the smooth penalty replaced by a cornered one, it becomes the sparse selection of [45.06.06].

## Full proof set [Master]

The closed form, the SVD shrinkage identity, the effective-degrees-of-freedom formula, and the Hoerl-Kennard existence theorem are proved in full in the Formal definition, Key theorem, and Exercises sections. The remaining Master claims are recorded here.

**Proposition 1 (Ridge as posterior mean under a Gaussian prior).** *Under $y \mid \beta \sim N(X\beta, \sigma^2 I_n)$ and $\beta \sim N(0, \tau^2 I_p)$, the posterior is $\beta \mid y \sim N(\hat\beta(\lambda), \sigma^2(X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1})$ with $\lambda = \sigma^2/\tau^2$, so $\hat\beta(\lambda)$ is both the posterior mean and the MAP estimate.*

*Proof.* The log-posterior is $-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\lVert y - X\beta\rVert^2 - \frac{1}{2\tau^2}\lVert\beta\rVert^2 + \text{const}$. Expanding the quadratic in $\beta$, the coefficient of the quadratic term is $\frac{1}{2}\beta^\top\big(\frac{X^\top X}{\sigma^2} + \frac{I}{\tau^2}\big)\beta$ and of the linear term is $-\frac{1}{\sigma^2}\beta^\top X^\top y$. A Gaussian density $N(m, \Sigma)$ has log-density quadratic form $-\frac{1}{2}\beta^\top\Sigma^{-1}\beta + \beta^\top\Sigma^{-1}m$; matching, $\Sigma^{-1} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}(X^\top X + \frac{\sigma^2}{\tau^2}I) = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}(X^\top X + \lambda I)$ and $\Sigma^{-1}m = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}X^\top y$. Hence $\Sigma = \sigma^2(X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1}$ and $m = (X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1}X^\top y = \hat\beta(\lambda)$. The posterior is Gaussian, so its mean equals its mode, and both equal $\hat\beta(\lambda)$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Ridge bias and the strict MSE gain at orthonormal design).** *For $X^\top X = I_p$ with $\hat\beta_j \sim N(\beta_j, \sigma^2)$ independent across $j$, the ridge MSE is $\mathrm{MSE}(\lambda) = \frac{\lambda^2 \lVert\beta\rVert^2 + p\sigma^2}{(1+\lambda)^2}$, minimized at $\lambda^\star = p\sigma^2/\lVert\beta\rVert^2 > 0$.*

*Proof.* By Exercise 4 each coordinate satisfies $\hat\beta_j(\lambda) = \hat\beta_j/(1+\lambda)$, so its bias is $\frac{\beta_j}{1+\lambda} - \beta_j = \frac{-\lambda\beta_j}{1+\lambda}$ with squared bias $\frac{\lambda^2\beta_j^2}{(1+\lambda)^2}$, and its variance is $\frac{\sigma^2}{(1+\lambda)^2}$. Summing over $j$ gives $\mathrm{MSE}(\lambda) = \frac{\lambda^2\lVert\beta\rVert^2 + p\sigma^2}{(1+\lambda)^2}$. Differentiating, the numerator of $\frac{d}{d\lambda}\mathrm{MSE}$ is proportional to $2\lambda\lVert\beta\rVert^2(1+\lambda) - 2(\lambda^2\lVert\beta\rVert^2 + p\sigma^2) = 2(\lambda\lVert\beta\rVert^2 - p\sigma^2)$, which vanishes at $\lambda^\star = p\sigma^2/\lVert\beta\rVert^2$. Since this is positive whenever $\sigma^2 > 0$, a strictly positive penalty minimizes MSE, recovering the general Hoerl-Kennard conclusion in the orthonormal case with an explicit optimum. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Kernel ridge push-through identity).** *For any feature matrix $\Phi \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$ and $\lambda > 0$, $(\Phi^\top\Phi + \lambda I_m)^{-1}\Phi^\top = \Phi^\top(\Phi\Phi^\top + \lambda I_n)^{-1}$, so the ridge fit is $\hat f = \Phi\hat\beta(\lambda) = K(K + \lambda I_n)^{-1}y$ with $K = \Phi\Phi^\top$.*

*Proof.* Both regularized matrices are invertible for $\lambda > 0$. Start from the identity $\Phi^\top(\Phi\Phi^\top + \lambda I_n) = (\Phi^\top\Phi + \lambda I_m)\Phi^\top$, which holds because each side equals $\Phi^\top\Phi\Phi^\top + \lambda\Phi^\top$. Left-multiply by $(\Phi^\top\Phi + \lambda I_m)^{-1}$ and right-multiply by $(\Phi\Phi^\top + \lambda I_n)^{-1}$ to obtain $(\Phi^\top\Phi + \lambda I_m)^{-1}\Phi^\top = \Phi^\top(\Phi\Phi^\top + \lambda I_n)^{-1}$. Then $\hat\beta(\lambda) = (\Phi^\top\Phi + \lambda I)^{-1}\Phi^\top y = \Phi^\top(K + \lambda I)^{-1}y$, and $\hat f = \Phi\hat\beta(\lambda) = \Phi\Phi^\top(K+\lambda I)^{-1}y = K(K + \lambda I)^{-1}y$. The estimator depends on $\Phi$ only through inner products $K_{ij} = \langle\phi(x_i),\phi(x_j)\rangle$, so an explicit feature map is never needed. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Ridge has no exact zeros at finite $\lambda$).** *If $X$ has no zero singular values and $u_j^\top y \ne 0$ for the relevant $j$, then for every finite $\lambda \ge 0$ each principal-coordinate ridge estimate $\hat\alpha_j(\lambda) = \frac{d_j}{d_j^2+\lambda}u_j^\top y$ is nonzero; coefficients vanish only in the limit $\lambda \to \infty$.*

*Proof.* The shrinkage multiplier $\frac{d_j}{d_j^2 + \lambda}$ is a quotient of a positive number $d_j > 0$ by the positive number $d_j^2 + \lambda$, hence strictly positive for every finite $\lambda$. Multiplying the nonzero data coordinate $u_j^\top y$ by a strictly positive scalar gives a nonzero result, so $\hat\alpha_j(\lambda) \ne 0$. As $\lambda \to \infty$ the multiplier tends to $0$, so the estimate tends to $0$ but attains it only in the limit. The smooth $\ell_2$ penalty therefore contracts without selecting; exact zeros require the cornered $\ell_1$ penalty of [45.06.06]. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

Linear model theory [45.06.01] supplies the OLS estimator, the normal equations, and the hat matrix that ridge regularizes; ridge deliberately relaxes the Gauss-Markov unbiasedness constraint $CX = I$ that makes OLS the BLUE there, replacing $X^\top X$ by $X^\top X + \lambda I$ to trade unbiasedness for a variance reduction, so ridge is the unbiased benchmark of that unit moved inward off the boundary.

The bias-variance decomposition [45.06.02] is the engine of the existence theorem: the SVD turns total MSE into a sum of per-coordinate squared-bias-plus-variance terms, and the single-coordinate shrinkage computation there (Exercise 5) is exactly the $X^\top X = I$ special case proved here as Proposition 2; the effective degrees of freedom $\mathrm{df}(\lambda) = \operatorname{tr}(H_\lambda)$ that index ridge complexity are the same optimism-linked quantity defined there.

The singular value decomposition [01.01.12] is the structural backbone: rotating into the singular basis diagonalizes the ridge estimator so that each principal direction shrinks independently by $d_j^2/(d_j^2 + \lambda)$, which is why collinear (small-$d_j$) directions are damped and well-determined (large-$d_j$) directions are preserved.

Bayesian statistics [45.03.02] reframes ridge as the posterior mean and MAP estimate under a Gaussian prior $\beta \sim N(0, \tau^2 I)$, with the penalty $\lambda = \sigma^2/\tau^2$ equal to the noise-to-prior-variance ratio; the deliberate bias of ridge is the prior pull toward zero, and the closed-form Gaussian posterior covariance $\sigma^2(X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1}$ is the inferential output the frequentist treatment leaves implicit.

The lasso and sparse selection [45.06.06] is the $\ell_1$ counterpart: replacing the smooth $\ell_2$ ball by the cornered $\ell_1$ ball turns ridge's pure contraction into soft-thresholding with exact zeros, so the two methods are the contraction and the threshold ends of the bridge-penalty family, and the elastic net combines them.

Kernel methods and reproducing-kernel Hilbert spaces [45.08.01] are reached by the push-through identity proved here as Proposition 3: kernel ridge regression $\hat f(x) = k(x)^\top(K + \lambda I)^{-1}y$ is ridge in a feature space accessed only through inner products, and the representer theorem of that unit guarantees the solution is a finite kernel expansion at the data points.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Ridge regression was introduced by Arthur Hoerl and Robert Kennard in two companion 1970 *Technometrics* papers [ref: TODO_REF Hoerl 1970], motivated by the practical instability of least squares for nonorthogonal (collinear) industrial design problems. Their central theorem — that there always exists a positive ridge constant $k$ for which the biased estimator has smaller total mean squared error than least squares — turned a numerical regularization device into a statistical method with a precise optimality justification. The diagnostic they proposed, the *ridge trace* plotting each coefficient against $k$, made the stabilization visible and remains in use. The same regularizer had appeared independently in the inverse-problems literature as **Tikhonov regularization**, after Andrey Tikhonov's work on ill-posed problems in the 1940s and 1960s, where $\lambda\lVert\beta\rVert^2$ stabilizes the inversion of an ill-conditioned operator; the two traditions describe the same matrix $X^\top X + \lambda I$.

The Bayesian reading — ridge as the posterior mean under a Gaussian prior on the coefficients — connects the method to the shrinkage estimators that James and Stein had shown in 1961 to dominate the sample mean in three or more dimensions, establishing that biased estimators can be uniformly better and that shrinkage toward a point is not a heuristic but a consequence of decision theory. The synthesis of ridge, subset selection, the lasso, and their Bayesian interpretations into a single account of shrinkage in regression was consolidated by Hastie, Tibshirani and Friedman [ref: TODO_REF Hastie 2009] in chapter 3 of *The Elements of Statistical Learning*, where the singular-value shrinkage factors $d_j^2/(d_j^2 + \lambda)$ are given as the unifying description, and extended to the high-dimensional sparse regime by Hastie, Tibshirani and Wainwright [ref: TODO_REF Hastie 2015].
