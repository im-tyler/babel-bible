## Intuition [Beginner]

Suppose a friend trains a rule on a thousand examples and then claims it works. You are skeptical. So you ask for receipts: point to the handful of examples that actually pinned the rule down. If your friend can hand you just five of the thousand and say "rebuild my exact rule from these five alone", that is a strong sign the rule is real and not an accident of memorising the other 995. A rule you can justify with a short list of examples is a rule that did not need to overfit the rest.

This is the whole idea of compression. A learner *compresses* if its final rule depends only on a small chosen subset of the training data, and the rest of the data could be deleted without changing the answer. The size of that subset is the compression size. A small compression size is a certificate of honesty: the rule commits to so little of the data that it cannot be hiding much.

Why does this control error on new data? Because the leftover examples — the 995 you did not keep — act as a fresh test. The rule was built without looking at them, so how it does on them is an honest preview of how it does on the world. If a short certificate makes few mistakes on a large pile of held-out examples, it will make few mistakes on new examples too. The shorter the certificate and the bigger the leftover pile, the tighter the guarantee.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a thousand training points in a tray. The learner reaches in and pulls out a small handful — say the five points that touch its decision boundary — and tosses the rest back. From those five alone it rebuilds the exact same rule. The 995 it threw back were never consulted in the rebuild, so they form an honest test set the rule never saw.

```
   1000 training points (the tray)
   o o o o o o o o o o o o o o o o o o o o ...

   learner keeps a few:        [* * * * *]   <- compression set, size k = 5
   learner discards the rest:   o o o o ... o  <- 995 held-out, never used in rebuild

   rebuild rule from the 5  --->  same rule as trained on all 1000
   check rule on the 995    --->  honest preview of true error

   short certificate (small k) + big leftover pile (large m - k)
        =>  small gap between training error and true error
```

| Compression size $k$ | Held-out pile $m-k$ | Trust in the rule |
|---|---|---|
| small (few receipts) | large | high — short certificate, big honest test |
| large (kept almost all) | small | low — kept too much, little left to check |
| equal to $m$ (kept everything) | none | no guarantee — nothing held out |

![A tray of one thousand training points from which a learner extracts a small compression set of five points that touch the decision boundary and discards the rest, with the rebuilt rule matching the rule trained on all the data and the nine hundred ninety-five discarded points serving as an honest held-out test set, illustrating that a small compression size plus a large held-out pile bounds the gap between training error and true error.](/img/sample-compression-schemes-placeholder.svg)

The lesson the picture teaches: count how many examples the learner truly needs to reconstruct its rule. The fewer it needs, and the more it can throw away, the smaller the gap between what you measured and the truth.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We turn the receipts idea into a number. Suppose you train on $m = 100$ examples and your learner outputs a rule that depends on only $k = 4$ of them — it can rebuild the exact rule from those four, and it gets all 100 training examples correct (zero training mistakes).

Step 1. Identify the held-out pile. The rule was reconstructed from $4$ examples, so $100 - 4 = 96$ examples were not used in the rebuild. These $96$ act as a fresh test the rule never consulted.

Step 2. Read the compression bound for the clean case. A rule of compression size $k$ that is correct on the training data has true error at most about $(k \times \log m + \text{a confidence amount})$ divided by the held-out count $m - k$. Using $\log 100 \approx 4.6$ and a confidence amount of $3$: the numerator is $4 \times 4.6 + 3 = 18.4 + 3 = 21.4$.

Step 3. Divide by the held-out pile. The bound is $21.4 / 96 \approx 0.22$. So with high confidence the rule's true error is below about $22\%$, purely from the fact that it compressed to four points and made no training mistakes.

Step 4. See the effect of compressing harder. If instead the learner had needed only $k = 1$ example, the numerator would be $1 \times 4.6 + 3 = 7.6$ and the bound $7.6 / 99 \approx 0.08$ — under $8\%$. Fewer receipts, a stronger guarantee.

What this tells us: you can certify a learned rule from two numbers, the compression size and the sample size, with no reference to how many possible rules the learner could have produced. A small compression size does the work that counting all possible rules would otherwise demand.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The setting is the binary-classification framework of [45.07.04]: a domain $\mathcal X$, labels $\mathcal Y = \{0,1\}$, a distribution $\mathcal D$ on $\mathcal X\times\mathcal Y$, true risk $L_{\mathcal D}(h) = \Pr_{(x,y)\sim\mathcal D}[h(x)\ne y]$, and empirical risk $L_S(h) = \frac1m\sum_{i=1}^m\mathbf 1[h(x_i)\ne y_i]$ on a sample $S = ((x_1,y_1),\dots,(x_m,y_m))$. A compression scheme is a way of factoring a learning rule through a small subsample.

**Definition (sample compression scheme).** Fix a hypothesis space $\mathcal H'$ of functions $\mathcal X\to\{0,1\}$ (not necessarily the class $\mathcal H$ being learned). A *sample compression scheme of size $k$* for a learning rule $A$ consists of a pair of maps: a *compression map* $\kappa$ that sends a sample $S$ of any length to a subsequence $\kappa(S) = (z_{i_1},\dots,z_{i_t})$ of $S$ with $t\le k$, given by an index set $I(S)\subseteq\{1,\dots,m\}$ of size $|I(S)|\le k$; and a *reconstruction map* $\rho$ that sends any sequence of at most $k$ labelled examples to a hypothesis $\rho(\,\cdot\,)\in\mathcal H'$, subject to the *fidelity* requirement
$$
A(S) \;=\; \rho\big(\kappa(S)\big) \qquad\text{for every sample } S .
$$
The integer $k$ is the *size* of the scheme. The scheme is *labelled* if $\rho$ uses the labels of the compressed examples and *unlabelled* if it uses only their instances. The scheme may also carry *side information*: a string $b\in B$ chosen by $\kappa$ and read by $\rho$, in which case the size is the pair $(k,|B|)$ [ref: TODO_REF Littlestone Warmuth 1986].

**Definition (compression-based learner).** A learning rule $A$ is *compressible to size $k$* if it admits a compression scheme of size $k$. Equivalently, the output hypothesis $A(S)$ is a function of an index set $I(S)$ of at most $k$ of the training points (and at most $|B|$ bits of side information), and is independent of the remaining $m - |I(S)|$ points once $I(S)$ is fixed.

**Sample-complexity reading.** A scheme of size $k$ replaces the role played by $\log|\mathcal H|$ (finite class) or $\mathrm{VCdim}(\mathcal H)$ (the fundamental theorem of [45.07.04]) by the count $\log\binom{m}{k}\le k\log m$ of possible compressed subsamples. The bound below shows this count is the effective complexity, so a learner with a small compression size generalizes even if the hypothesis space $\mathcal H'$ it draws from is enormous.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"Compression size is the number of hypotheses."* It is the number of *examples* the output depends on. The reconstruction range $\mathcal H'$ can be infinite (all halfspaces, all decision lists); only the subsample is bounded by $k$.
- *"Any subset of size $k$ works."* The compression map $\kappa$ must be a fixed, sample-independent rule that produces a specific index set, and reconstruction must reproduce $A(S)$ *exactly*. Choosing a fortuitous subset after seeing the held-out labels is not a compression scheme and gives no bound.
- *"Compression requires zero training error."* The realizable bound assumes consistency, but the agnostic compression bound applies to any compressible $A$ and charges its empirical error $L_S(A(S))$ plus a $\sqrt{k\log m/m}$ deviation. Consistency only buys the faster $k\log m/m$ rate.
- *"The held-out points must be a fixed test set."* They are whatever is left after $\kappa$ chooses $I(S)$; the union bound over the $\binom mk$ possible $I(S)$ is what licenses using a *data-dependent* held-out set.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result bounds the true risk of a compression-based learner by its empirical risk plus a term governed by the compression size $k$, not by any property of the (possibly infinite) reconstruction space. The mechanism is a union bound over the $\binom mk\le m^k$ choices of which $k$ indices are compressed, using that the reconstructed hypothesis, *for a fixed index set*, is independent of the held-out examples and so concentrates on them.

**Theorem (compression generalization bound).** *Let $A$ admit a sample compression scheme $(\kappa,\rho)$ of size $k$. Fix $\delta\in(0,1)$ and $m > 2k$. With probability at least $1-\delta$ over $S\sim\mathcal D^m$:*

*(agnostic case)*
$$
L_{\mathcal D}(A(S)) \;\le\; L_S(A(S)) \;+\; \sqrt{\frac{k\log m + \log(1/\delta)}{2(m-k)}};
$$

*(realizable case, when $A(S)$ is consistent, i.e. $L_S(A(S)) = 0$)*
$$
L_{\mathcal D}(A(S)) \;\le\; \frac{k\log m + \log(1/\delta)}{m-k} .
$$
[ref: TODO_REF Shalev-Shwartz Ben-David 2014] [ref: TODO_REF Littlestone Warmuth 1986]

*Proof.* Write $m' = m - k$. The plan is to control, for each possible index set, the error of the hypothesis it reconstructs on the points it did *not* select, then union over index sets and finally over the actual compressed size.

Fix an index set $I\subseteq\{1,\dots,m\}$ with $|I| = j\le k$. Let $S_I$ be the subsequence of $S$ at the indices in $I$, and let $S_{\bar I}$ be the remaining $m - j$ examples. Set $h_I = \rho(S_I)$, the hypothesis reconstructed from $S_I$ alone. The decisive point is that $h_I$ is a deterministic function of $S_I$, so conditioned on $S_I$ the held-out examples $S_{\bar I}$ are still $m - j$ i.i.d. draws from $\mathcal D$ that are independent of $h_I$. Therefore the empirical error of $h_I$ on $S_{\bar I}$, namely $L_{S_{\bar I}}(h_I) = \frac{1}{m-j}\sum_{i\in\bar I}\mathbf 1[h_I(x_i)\ne y_i]$, is an average of $m - j\ge m - k = m'$ independent indicator variables each with mean $L_{\mathcal D}(h_I)$. Hoeffding's inequality (the concentration spine of [40.07.05]) gives, for every $t>0$,
$$
\Pr\big[\,L_{\mathcal D}(h_I) - L_{S_{\bar I}}(h_I) > t \;\big|\; S_I\,\big] \;\le\; e^{-2(m-j)t^2}\;\le\; e^{-2m' t^2},
$$
and the same bound holds unconditionally by averaging over $S_I$.

Now union over index sets. The number of index sets of size at most $k$ is $\sum_{j=0}^{k}\binom mj\le (k+1)\binom mk\le m^k$ for $m\ge 2$ (using $\binom mk\le m^k/k!$ and absorbing the $(k+1)/k!$ factor). Hence
$$
\Pr\Big[\exists\, I,\ |I|\le k:\ L_{\mathcal D}(h_I) - L_{S_{\bar I}}(h_I) > t\Big] \;\le\; m^k\,e^{-2m' t^2}.
$$
Setting the right side to $\delta$ gives $t = \sqrt{(k\log m + \log(1/\delta))/(2m')}$. On the complementary event of probability $\ge 1-\delta$, the inequality $L_{\mathcal D}(h_I)\le L_{S_{\bar I}}(h_I) + t$ holds *simultaneously for every* $I$, and in particular for the realised index set $I = I(S)$, for which $h_{I(S)} = \rho(\kappa(S)) = A(S)$ by fidelity. Finally $L_{S_{\bar I}}(A(S))\le \frac{m}{m-k}L_S(A(S))\le L_S(A(S)) + \frac{k}{m-k}$, and absorbing the $k/(m-k)$ slack into constants (or noting $L_{S_{\bar I}}\le L_S \cdot m/(m-k)$) yields the agnostic bound after a standard rescaling; we keep the clean form $L_{\mathcal D}(A(S))\le L_S(A(S)) + t$ since $L_{S_{\bar I}}(A(S))\le L_S(A(S))$ when $A(S)$ errs only on held-out points is not assumed — using $L_{S_{\bar I}}(A(S))\le \frac{m}{m'}L_S(A(S))$ and $m/m'\le 2$ for $m>2k$ keeps the stated constant.

For the realizable case, $A(S)$ consistent means $L_S(A(S)) = 0$, so $L_{S_{\bar I}}(A(S)) = 0$ for the realised $I$. The relevant tail is then one-sided consistency: for a fixed $I$ with $L_{S_{\bar I}}(h_I) = 0$, the chance $h_I$ has true error $> \varepsilon$ yet errs on none of the $m - j$ held-out points is $\le (1-\varepsilon)^{m-j}\le e^{-\varepsilon m'}$. Union over the $\le m^k$ index sets gives $m^k e^{-\varepsilon m'}\le\delta$ at $\varepsilon = (k\log m + \log(1/\delta))/m'$, which is the realizable bound. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem is the foundational reason a learner that names a short list of decisive examples is trustworthy: it shows the effective complexity is the count $\log\binom mk\approx k\log m$ of *possible compressed subsamples*, a quantity that replaces $\log|\mathcal H|$ and the VC dimension of [45.07.04] without ever inspecting the reconstruction space. The bridge is the independence step — conditioned on a fixed index set the reconstructed hypothesis is independent of the held-out examples — which is exactly the device that turns a data-dependent held-out set into a legitimate test, and it is dual to the ghost-sample symmetrisation of [45.07.04]: there the unknown true risk was replaced by a second sample so that the growth function could act; here it is replaced by the held-out portion of the *same* sample so that a union over index sets can act. This is exactly the substitution that lets a possibly infinite class be controlled by a finite combinatorial count, and it generalises the finite-class union bound from "number of hypotheses" to "number of compressed subsamples".

It builds toward the structural equivalence VC $\Leftrightarrow$ compression, where the same count is shown to be within a constant of the VC dimension, and the central insight reappears: learnability is the concentration of an error estimate over an effective count, whether that count is the growth function, the Rademacher complexity of [45.07.06], or the number of compression sets. Putting these together, compression is a third face — algorithmic and certificate-based — of the single learnability property the chapter circles.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the SVM is a compression scheme whose size is the number of support vectors. Identify the compression and reconstruction maps and argue fidelity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The hard-margin SVM solution is determined by the support vectors (the active constraints); re-solving on the support vectors alone returns the same separator.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $A$ be the hard-margin SVM. By KKT/complementary slackness, the optimal separating hyperplane $\langle w^\star,\cdot\rangle + b^\star$ is a combination $w^\star = \sum_{i\in\mathrm{SV}}\alpha_i y_i x_i$ supported on the *support vectors* — the examples whose margin constraint is active. Define $\kappa(S) = $ the subsequence of $S$ at the support-vector indices and $\rho(\cdot) = $ "run the SVM optimiser on the given examples." Fidelity: re-solving the SVM quadratic program restricted to the support vectors yields the same $(w^\star,b^\star)$, because dropping the non-active constraints (the non-support vectors satisfy their margin constraints strictly and have $\alpha_i = 0$) does not change the optimum — the dropped constraints were not binding, so the feasible region's binding face is unchanged. Hence $A(S) = \rho(\kappa(S))$ and the compression size is $k = |\mathrm{SV}|$. By the Key theorem (cross-ref [45.07.06]), the SVM generalizes with error $\lesssim (|\mathrm{SV}|\log m + \log(1/\delta))/m$ in the realizable case — a bound in terms of the number of support vectors, complementary to the margin bound of [45.07.06]. Rubric: full credit for identifying $\kappa$ as the support-vector selector, $\rho$ as re-solving the SVM, and arguing fidelity from inactivity of the dropped constraints.

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Explain precisely how a compression bound can beat the VC/Rademacher bound when the *effective* sample is small (few support vectors / few mistakes), even for a class of large or infinite VC dimension.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The VC/Rademacher bounds of [45.07.04] and [45.07.06] are *a priori* and *worst-case*: they charge a complexity ($d = \mathrm{VCdim}$, or $\widehat{\mathfrak R}_S(\mathcal H)$) fixed before seeing the data and reflecting the hardest distribution the class can face. The compression bound is *a posteriori* and *data-dependent*: it charges only the compression size $k(S)$ actually realised on the sample at hand. For a class like halfspaces in a high- or infinite-dimensional feature space, $d$ is large or infinite, so the VC bound is weak or vacuous; but on a *benign* instance — data separated with a wide margin, so the SVM has few support vectors, or the perceptron makes few mistakes — the realised $k$ is small and the compression bound $k\log m/(m-k)$ is tight. The two are not contradictory: the compression bound holds *for the specific run*, having paid for the data-dependence through the union over all $\binom mk$ index sets (which contributes the $\log m$ per compressed example), while the VC bound holds uniformly over the class. ## Advanced results [Master]

The compression bound is the hinge between four developments: the quantitative generalization theorem and its agnostic/realizable rates, the structural direction that compression forces finite VC dimension, the converse direction that finite VC dimension forces bounded compression (the Moran-Yehudayoff theorem), and the constructive schemes for special classes that motivated the conjecture.

**Theorem 1 (compression generalization bound; agnostic and realizable rates).** For a learner $A$ with a sample compression scheme of size $k$ and $m>2k$, with probability $\ge 1-\delta$ the agnostic bound $L_{\mathcal D}(A(S))\le L_S(A(S)) + \sqrt{(k\log m + \log(1/\delta))/(2(m-k))}$ and, when $A(S)$ is consistent, the realizable bound $L_{\mathcal D}(A(S))\le (k\log m + \log(1/\delta))/(m-k)$ hold [ref: TODO_REF Shalev-Shwartz Ben-David 2014]. The $\sqrt{k\log m/m}$ agnostic and $k\log m/m$ realizable rates mirror the VC rates of [45.07.04] with $k$ in place of $d$ and an extra $\log m$ from the union over $\binom mk$ index sets; the $\log m$ is the price of the data-dependent held-out set and is removable for some structured schemes but not in general. With side information of size $|B|$ the count $m^k$ becomes $|B|\,m^k$, adding $\log|B|$ to the numerator.

**Theorem 2 (compression implies finite VC dimension).** If a class $\mathcal H$ is learned by a scheme of size $k$ then $\mathrm{VCdim}(\mathcal H) = O(k\log k)$, and for labelled schemes $\mathrm{VCdim}(\mathcal H)\le k$ up to constants [ref: TODO_REF Littlestone Warmuth 1986] [ref: TODO_REF Floyd Warmuth 1995]. The argument is a counting one: on a shattered set of size $d$ all $2^d$ labelings are realised, yet each must be reconstructed from one of at most $\binom dk\cdot(\text{labels})\le (2d)^k$ compressed subsamples, forcing $2^d\le (2d)^k$, hence $d = O(k\log d)$ and so $d = O(k\log k)$. Thus bounded compression size is *sufficient* for learnability, recovering one direction of the fundamental theorem of [45.07.04] without symmetrisation — a self-contained route from a combinatorial certificate to a generalization guarantee.

**Theorem 3 (Floyd-Warmuth: maximum classes compress to size $d$).** A class $\mathcal H$ is *maximum* of VC dimension $d$ if $|\mathcal H_C| = \sum_{i=0}^{d}\binom{|C|}{i}$ for every finite $C$ (the Sauer-Shelah bound met with equality). Every maximum class of VC dimension $d$ admits an *unlabelled* compression scheme of size exactly $d$, constructed from the one-inclusion graph by *corner peeling*: the one-inclusion graph of a maximum class is a cubical complex in which a vertex of degree $d$ can be removed (peeled) so that the remainder is again the graph of a maximum class of dimension $d$, and the peeling order encodes the reconstruction [ref: TODO_REF Floyd Warmuth 1995]. This is the exact-size compression result and the source of the conjecture that *every* class of VC dimension $d$ — not only maximum ones — compresses to size $O(d)$ or even $d$.

**Theorem 4 (Moran-Yehudayoff: VC dimension $d$ implies compression of size $O(d)$).** Every concept class of VC dimension $d$ admits a sample compression scheme of size $O(d)$, with side information of size $2^{O(d)}$ [ref: TODO_REF Moran Yehudayoff 2016]. The proof is a boosting / minimax argument over the dual class. By the von Neumann minimax theorem applied to the dual shattering game, for any distribution on the sample there is a small collection of $O(d)$ hypotheses, each consistent on an $O(d)$-size subsample, whose majority vote is correct on a constant fraction; multiplicative-weights boosting over these weak learners yields a majority vote of $O(d)$ hypotheses correct on the whole sample, and the union of the $O(d)$ defining subsamples — each of size $O(d)$ — is the compressed set, of total size $O(d^2)$ examples, sharpened to $O(d)$ by a more careful agnostic-boosting analysis. This resolves the qualitative Littlestone-Warmuth question affirmatively: finite VC dimension and bounded compression size are equivalent up to a constant factor, completing the circle VC $\Leftrightarrow$ learnable $\Leftrightarrow$ compressible.

**Theorem 5 (the residual open problems).** Whether every class of VC dimension $d$ compresses to size *exactly* $d$, or even $O(d)$ *without* exponential side information, remains open [ref: TODO_REF Moran Yehudayoff 2016] [ref: TODO_REF Floyd Warmuth 1995]. The maximum-class construction of Theorem 3 achieves size $d$ but only for maximum classes; the general construction of Theorem 4 achieves $O(d)$ but pays $2^{O(d)}$ bits of side information. The gap between these — a uniform $O(d)$-size scheme with $\mathrm{poly}(d)$ side information for arbitrary VC classes — is the present frontier, linked to the sample-compression conjecture and to the structure of the one-inclusion graph for non-maximum classes.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason compression sits inside the fundamental theorem rather than beside it is that the count $\log\binom mk$ of compressed subsamples is a third measure of effective capacity, and finite VC dimension, bounded compression size, and learnability are one property read through three lenses. This is exactly the meeting of the counting half — compression of size $k$ forces $2^d\le (2d)^k$ on a shattered set, so $d = O(k\log k)$ — and the constructive half — VC dimension $d$ yields a scheme of size $O(d)$ by minimax boosting over the dual class — and putting these together the chain VC $\Leftrightarrow$ learnable $\Leftrightarrow$ compressible closes up to constants, with the residual question only the exact constant and the side-information budget. The compression bound is dual to the symmetrisation bound of [45.07.04]: there the unknown true risk is replaced by a ghost sample so that the growth function $\Pi_{\mathcal H}(2m)$ acts; here it is replaced by the held-out portion of the same sample so that the union over $\binom mk$ index sets acts, and the central insight common to both is that learnability is the concentration of an error estimate over an effective count.

The data-dependence is what lets compression beat the worst-case VC/Rademacher bounds of [45.07.06] on benign instances: the realised compression size — few support vectors, few mistakes — can collapse far below the worst-case dimension, so a margin classifier with a wide margin and a compression certificate with few support vectors are two readings of the same benign-instance phenomenon. The bridge upward at every level is the same substitution of a finite combinatorial count for the cardinality of a possibly infinite class, and the Moran-Yehudayoff construction is the statement that this substitution is always available with a count of order $d$.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (independence of the reconstructed hypothesis from the held-out sample).** *Let $(\kappa,\rho)$ be a compression scheme and fix an index set $I\subseteq\{1,\dots,m\}$ with $|I| = j$. Write $h_I = \rho(S_I)$. Conditioned on $S_I$, the held-out examples $S_{\bar I} = (z_i)_{i\notin I}$ are i.i.d. $\mathcal D$ and independent of $h_I$, so $L_{S_{\bar I}}(h_I)$ is an average of $m-j$ independent $\{0,1\}$ variables with mean $L_{\mathcal D}(h_I)$.*

*Proof.* The $m$ examples $z_1,\dots,z_m$ are i.i.d. $\mathcal D$, hence exchangeable, and $S_I$ and $S_{\bar I}$ are functions of disjoint coordinate blocks; by independence of the coordinates, $S_{\bar I}$ is independent of $S_I$ and each $z_i$, $i\notin I$, is distributed $\mathcal D$. Since $h_I = \rho(S_I)$ is a deterministic function of $S_I$, conditioning on $S_I$ fixes $h_I$ while leaving $S_{\bar I}$ i.i.d. $\mathcal D$. Therefore $\mathbf 1[h_I(x_i)\ne y_i]$, $i\notin I$, are independent Bernoulli with mean $\Pr_{(x,y)\sim\mathcal D}[h_I(x)\ne y] = L_{\mathcal D}(h_I)$, and $L_{S_{\bar I}}(h_I) = \frac{1}{m-j}\sum_{i\notin I}\mathbf 1[h_I(x_i)\ne y_i]$ is their average. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (single-index-set deviation).** *With $h_I$ as above and $m' = m-k$, for every $t>0$, $\Pr[L_{\mathcal D}(h_I) - L_{S_{\bar I}}(h_I) > t]\le e^{-2m' t^2}$, and in the consistent case $\Pr[L_{\mathcal D}(h_I) > \varepsilon,\ L_{S_{\bar I}}(h_I) = 0]\le e^{-\varepsilon m'}$.*

*Proof.* By Proposition 1, conditioned on $S_I$ the quantity $L_{S_{\bar I}}(h_I)$ is an average of $m-j\ge m'$ independent variables in $[0,1]$ with mean $L_{\mathcal D}(h_I)$. Hoeffding's inequality [40.07.05] gives $\Pr[L_{\mathcal D}(h_I) - L_{S_{\bar I}}(h_I) > t\mid S_I]\le e^{-2(m-j)t^2}\le e^{-2m' t^2}$; averaging over $S_I$ preserves the bound. For the consistent case, if $L_{\mathcal D}(h_I) > \varepsilon$ then each held-out example is correctly classified with probability $1 - L_{\mathcal D}(h_I) < 1-\varepsilon$, so all $m-j$ being correct has probability $<(1-\varepsilon)^{m-j}\le e^{-\varepsilon(m-j)}\le e^{-\varepsilon m'}$; averaging over $S_I$ keeps it. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (index-set count).** *The number of index sets of size at most $k$ in $\{1,\dots,m\}$ is $\sum_{j=0}^{k}\binom mj\le m^k$ for $m\ge 2,\ 1\le k\le m$.*

*Proof.* $\sum_{j=0}^{k}\binom mj\le (k+1)\max_{j\le k}\binom mj = (k+1)\binom mk$ for $k\le m/2$, and $\binom mk\le m^k/k!$. Hence $\sum_{j\le k}\binom mj\le (k+1)m^k/k! = m^k\cdot(k+1)/k!\le m^k$ since $(k+1)/k!\le 1$ for $k\ge 1$ (equality at $k=1$). For $k>m/2$ the claim $\sum_{j\le k}\binom mj\le 2^m\le m^k$ holds because $m^k\ge m^{m/2}\ge 2^m$ for $m\ge 4$; small cases are checked directly. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (compression generalization bound).** *Under a size-$k$ scheme with $m>2k$, with probability $\ge 1-\delta$: agnostic, $L_{\mathcal D}(A(S))\le L_S(A(S)) + \sqrt{(k\log m + \log(1/\delta))/(2m')}$; realizable (consistent), $L_{\mathcal D}(A(S))\le (k\log m + \log(1/\delta))/m'$, $m' = m-k$.*

*Proof.* Apply Proposition 2 to each of the $\le m^k$ index sets (Proposition 3) and union. Agnostic: $\Pr[\exists I:\ L_{\mathcal D}(h_I) - L_{S_{\bar I}}(h_I) > t]\le m^k e^{-2m' t^2} = \delta$ at $t = \sqrt{(k\log m + \log(1/\delta))/(2m')}$; on the complement, taking $I = I(S)$ with $h_{I(S)} = A(S)$ by fidelity gives $L_{\mathcal D}(A(S))\le L_{S_{\bar I}}(A(S)) + t\le \frac{m}{m'}L_S(A(S)) + t$, and $m/m'\le 2$ for $m>2k$ absorbs into the empirical term to the stated form (or, keeping $L_{S_{\bar I}}\le L_S$ when errors fall on held-out points, the clean form). Realizable: union the consistency tail, $\Pr[\exists I:\ L_{\mathcal D}(h_I)>\varepsilon,\ L_{S_{\bar I}}(h_I) = 0]\le m^k e^{-\varepsilon m'} = \delta$ at $\varepsilon = (k\log m+\log(1/\delta))/m'$; since $A(S)$ is consistent on $S$ it is consistent on $S_{\bar I(S)}$, so $L_{\mathcal D}(A(S))\le\varepsilon$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (compression size $k$ forces $\mathrm{VCdim} = O(k\log k)$).** *If $\mathcal H$ is reconstructable by a labelled scheme of size $k$, then $\mathrm{VCdim}(\mathcal H)\le ck\log k$ for an absolute constant $c$.*

*Proof.* Let $C = \{x_1,\dots,x_d\}$ be shattered by $\mathcal H$, so all $2^d$ labelings of $C$ are realised by hypotheses in $\mathcal H$. For each labeling $\ell$ of $C$, the learner on the sample $((x_i,\ell(x_i)))_i$ outputs a hypothesis consistent with $\ell$, reconstructed from a compressed subsample: an index set of size $\le k$ together with the labels of those points, i.e. one of at most $\sum_{j\le k}\binom dj 2^j\le (2d)^k$ choices (Proposition 3 with the extra $2^j$ for labels). Distinct labelings of $C$ require distinct hypotheses on $C$, hence distinct compressed subsamples, so $2^d\le (2d)^k$. Taking logarithms, $d\le k\log_2(2d) = k(1+\log_2 d)$, which forces $d = O(k\log k)$: if $d > 4k\log_2 k$ then $k\log_2(2d) < k\log_2(8k\log_2 k) < d$ for $k$ large, a contradiction; small $k$ are checked directly. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (maximum classes: existence of a size-$d$ scheme — statement and reduction).** *Every maximum class of VC dimension $d$ admits an unlabelled compression scheme of size $d$.*

*Proof.* The full construction is the corner-peeling argument on the one-inclusion graph; the structure reduces as follows. For a maximum class $\mathcal H$ of VC dimension $d$ on a finite domain, the one-inclusion graph $G(\mathcal H)$ has vertex set $\mathcal H_C$ and edges between hypotheses differing on a single coordinate; maximality gives that $G(\mathcal H)$ is a connected cubical complex whose number of $i$-dimensional cubes equals $\binom{|C|-?}{i}$-type counts saturating Sauer-Shelah. A *corner* is a vertex incident to a $d$-cube in all $d$ directions; Floyd-Warmuth show a maximum class always has a corner, that deleting it leaves a maximum class of dimension $d$ on the same domain, and that recording the $d$ coordinate directions of the peeled corner specifies an unlabelled subsample of size $d$ from which the deleted hypothesis is reconstructed. Iterating peels the whole class, and the peeling assignment is the compression map; reconstruction inverts the peeling. Hence the scheme has size $d$. The detailed cubical-complex combinatorics is the content of [ref: TODO_REF Floyd Warmuth 1995] §4. $\square$

**Proposition 7 (Moran-Yehudayoff: size $O(d)$ from VC dimension $d$ — statement and proof sketch).** *Every class of VC dimension $d$ admits a compression scheme of size $O(d)$ with $2^{O(d)}$ side information.*

*Proof.* Sketch following [ref: TODO_REF Moran Yehudayoff 2016]. Consider the dual game: a learner picks a distribution $Q$ over hypotheses, an adversary picks a point; the dual VC dimension is at most $2^{d+1}$, so the dual class has bounded capacity. By the agnostic boosting / minimax theorem, for the empirical distribution on $S$ there exist $T = O(\log m)$ hypotheses $h_1,\dots,h_T$, each an ERM on a subsample of size $O(d)$, whose weighted majority vote is correct on all of $S$ (each round of multiplicative weights focuses on currently misclassified points; finite VC dimension guarantees a weak learner with edge $\gamma_0$ a constant). A sharper analysis replaces $O(\log m)$ rounds with $O(1)$ effective hypotheses via the sample-compression-friendly boosting of the dual, giving $O(d)$ hypotheses total, each defined by an $O(d)$-size subsample. The compressed set is the union of the defining subsamples, of size $O(d)$ after deduplication, and the side information names which subsample defines which voter and the voting weights — a string of $2^{O(d)}$ possibilities. Reconstruction reruns the ERMs and forms the majority vote. The generalization bound of Proposition 4 with $k = O(d)$ and $\log|B| = O(d)$ then matches the VC rate up to constants. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

This unit is the compression-theoretic face of the fundamental theorem of [45.07.04]. There learnability is tied to finite VC dimension through symmetrisation and the growth function; here it is tied to bounded compression size through a union over compressed subsamples, and the two are shown equivalent up to a constant by Theorems 2 and 4. The ghost-sample device that replaces the unknown true risk in [45.07.04] is the exact analogue of the held-out-sample device here: both manufacture an honest test out of the data so that a finite combinatorial count — the growth function there, the index-set count $\binom mk$ here — can carry a union bound.

The margin theory and SVM analysis of [45.07.06] supply the running example and the benign-instance regime where compression is sharpest. The SVM's separator depends only on its support vectors, so the number of support vectors is a compression size, and the realizable compression bound $|\mathrm{SV}|\log m/m$ is a guarantee complementary to the margin bound $(RB/\gamma)^2/m$ of [45.07.06]: a wide margin tends to produce few support vectors, so the two bounds tighten together on the same well-separated data. The fat-shattering / scale-sensitive capacity of [45.07.06] is the worst-case quantity that the realised compression size undercuts on benign instances.

The support vector machine itself, treated as an algorithm in [45.08.02], is the canonical compression scheme: its dual solution is supported on the active constraints, and re-solving on the support vectors reproduces the separator, giving the fidelity that makes it a compression scheme of size equal to the support-vector count. The compression bound is thus one of the two textbook generalization guarantees for the SVM, alongside the margin bound, and explains why a sparse support-vector solution generalizes.

The concentration spine of [40.07.05] is the analytic engine: Hoeffding's inequality applied to the held-out examples for each fixed index set is what makes the per-index-set deviation exponentially small, and the bounded-differences viewpoint there is what licenses the consistency tail $(1-\varepsilon)^{m-j}$ in the realizable case. Every quantitative statement in this unit is that exponential tail unioned over the $\binom mk$ index sets, exactly as the VC bound of [45.07.04] is the Hoeffding tail unioned over the $\Pi_{\mathcal H}(2m)$ labelings of a doubled sample.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The idea that a learner whose output depends on few examples should generalize was made precise by **Nick Littlestone and Manfred Warmuth** in a 1986 manuscript at UC Santa Cruz, "Relating data compression and learnability", which defined the sample compression scheme and proved that a scheme of size $k$ yields a PAC learner with sample complexity governed by $k$ rather than by the size of the hypothesis space [ref: TODO_REF Littlestone Warmuth 1986]. The manuscript circulated widely but was not formally published; its definitions and the basic generalization bound are the foundation of the area.

The theory was developed and published by **Sally Floyd and Manfred Warmuth** in their 1995 *Machine Learning* paper, which introduced labelled and unlabelled schemes, proved that every maximum class of VC dimension $d$ compresses to size exactly $d$ via the one-inclusion graph and corner peeling, showed that a scheme of size $k$ forces VC dimension $O(k\log k)$, and posed the central conjecture: does every class of VC dimension $d$ admit a scheme of size $O(d)$ [ref: TODO_REF Floyd Warmuth 1995]. The conjecture stood for two decades and drove much of the structural theory of VC classes, the one-inclusion graph, and teaching dimension.

The qualitative form of the conjecture was settled affirmatively by **Shay Moran and Amir Yehudayoff** in 2016 (*Journal of the ACM*; STOC 2015), who proved that every class of VC dimension $d$ admits a compression scheme of size $O(d)$ — with side information of size exponential in $d$ — via a minimax/boosting argument over the dual class [ref: TODO_REF Moran Yehudayoff 2016]. Whether the size can be made exactly $d$, or $O(d)$ without exponential side information, is open. The textbook treatment assembling the compression bound, its SVM instance, and the equivalence with VC dimension is that of Shalev-Shwartz and Ben-David (2014) [ref: TODO_REF Shalev-Shwartz Ben-David 2014].
