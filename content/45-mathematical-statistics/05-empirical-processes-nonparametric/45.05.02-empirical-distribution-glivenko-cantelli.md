## Intuition [Beginner]

You have a sample of data and you want to know the distribution it came from — not just its average, but its whole shape: where the values pile up, how spread out they are, where the tails sit. The most honest summary you can build is the *empirical distribution*: pretend the only possible outcomes are exactly the data points you saw, each equally likely. If you collected $100$ numbers, you treat the world as a lottery with those $100$ tickets. This turns a sample into a full-fledged distribution you can compute anything from.

The empirical distribution function records, for each threshold, the fraction of your data at or below it. As you collect more data, this staircase of fractions should fill in and match the true distribution function. The law of large numbers already promises that at any single fixed threshold the fraction settles to the true probability. The deeper claim is that this happens *everywhere at once*: the entire empirical curve hugs the true curve, with the worst gap across all thresholds shrinking to zero.

That uniform promise is the Glivenko-Cantelli theorem, sometimes called the fundamental theorem of statistics. It is what lets you treat a sample as a faithful stand-in for the population, and it is the reason plug-in estimates — compute something from the data exactly as you would from the truth — actually work as the sample grows.

The one-sentence takeaway: the empirical distribution built from a sample converges to the true distribution uniformly, so a large sample is a trustworthy picture of the whole population, not just of one feature at a time.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the true distribution function as a smooth rising curve from $0$ on the left to $1$ on the right. The empirical version is a staircase: it jumps up by $1/n$ at each data point and is flat in between. Watch the staircase as the sample size grows.

| Sample size | What the staircase looks like | Worst gap to the true curve |
|---|---|---|
| Small | A few big steps, blocky, only roughly tracking the curve | Large |
| Medium | Many smaller steps hugging the curve more closely | Smaller |
| Large | A fine staircase nearly on top of the smooth curve | Tiny |
| Growing without bound | The staircase merges into the smooth curve everywhere | Shrinks to zero |

![A smooth S-shaped rising curve labeled true distribution function, overlaid with three staircase curves. The palest staircase has a few large steps and strays from the curve, labeled small sample. A medium staircase with more steps hugs the curve more closely, labeled medium sample. The darkest staircase has many fine steps and sits almost exactly on the smooth curve, labeled large sample. The single number to watch is the *worst gap*: the largest vertical distance between the staircase and the smooth curve, taken over every threshold. Glivenko-Cantelli says this worst gap closes to zero as the sample grows, for almost every run of data collection. The same worst gap is what the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test measures to decide whether a sample really came from a proposed distribution.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We build the empirical distribution function from a tiny sample and measure its worst gap to a true distribution. Take five data points drawn from a distribution that is uniform on the interval from $0$ to $1$, whose true distribution function is the straight line $F(t) = t$ on that interval. Suppose the five values are $0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9$.

Step 1. Build the staircase. With $n = 5$ points, each one contributes a jump of $1/5 = 0.2$. The empirical fraction at or below a threshold $t$ counts how many of the five values are $\le t$, divided by $5$. Below $0.1$ the fraction is $0$; at $0.1$ it jumps to $0.2$; at $0.3$ to $0.4$; at $0.5$ to $0.6$; at $0.7$ to $0.8$; at $0.9$ to $1.0$.

Step 2. Compare to the true line just before each jump. The biggest gaps sit just under each data point, where the staircase has not yet jumped but the true line has risen. Just below $0.1$, the staircase is $0$ and the true value is $0.1$, a gap of $0.1$. Just below $0.3$, the staircase is $0.2$ and the true value is $0.3$, a gap of $0.1$. The same pattern repeats: just below $0.5$, $0.7$, $0.9$, each gap is $0.1$.

Step 3. Compare just after each jump. Just above $0.1$, the staircase is $0.2$ and the true value is $0.1$, a gap of $0.1$. The same $0.1$ gap appears just above each data point.

Step 4. Read off the worst gap. Every gap computed is $0.1$, so the largest vertical distance between staircase and true line is $0.1$.

Step 5. What this tells us. With only five evenly spaced points the worst gap is already just $0.1$. Pack in more data and the steps get finer and the worst gap shrinks. Glivenko-Cantelli is the promise that, for genuinely random samples, this worst gap keeps dropping toward zero as the sample grows, so the staircase becomes a faithful copy of the true distribution.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $X_1, X_2, \dots$ are independent and identically distributed random elements of a measurable space $(\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{A})$ with common law $P$. The development follows van der Vaart [ref: TODO_REF van der Vaart 1998] Ch. 19 and van der Vaart-Wellner [ref: TODO_REF van der Vaart Wellner 1996] §2.4. The modes of stochastic convergence and the notion of consistency used below are those fixed in [45.04.01], and the strong law of large numbers [37.02.02] is the single probabilistic input.

**Definition (empirical measure and empirical process).** The *empirical measure* of the first $n$ observations is the random discrete probability measure
$$
P_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n \delta_{X_k},
$$
where $\delta_x$ is the unit point mass at $x$. For a measurable $f : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$ write $P_n f = \int f\,dP_n = n^{-1}\sum_{k=1}^n f(X_k)$ and $Pf = \int f\,dP = \mathbb{E}\,f(X_1)$. The *empirical process* indexed by $f$ is $\mathbb{G}_n f = \sqrt{n}\,(P_n f - Pf)$.

**Definition (empirical distribution function).** When $\mathcal{X} = \mathbb{R}$, the *empirical distribution function* (EDF) is
$$
F_n(t) = P_n(-\infty, t] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{\{X_k \le t\}}, \qquad t \in \mathbb{R}.
$$
It is the EDF that the elementary statistics curriculum estimates $F$ with; it is a right-continuous step function with jumps of size $1/n$ (or multiples, at ties) at the observations, and it is the special case $P_n f$ for $f = \mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t]}$.

**Definition (the supremum norm over a class).** For a class $\mathcal{F}$ of measurable functions $f : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$ with $P|f| < \infty$ for all $f \in \mathcal{F}$, set
$$
\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} = \sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \big| P_n f - Pf \big|.
$$
For the EDF the relevant class is $\mathcal{F} = \{\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t]} : t \in \mathbb{R}\}$, and then $\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} = \sup_t |F_n(t) - F(t)|$, the *Kolmogorov-Smirnov distance*.

**Definition (Glivenko-Cantelli class).** A class $\mathcal{F}$ is *$P$-Glivenko-Cantelli* (or simply Glivenko-Cantelli, GC) if
$$
\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} \xrightarrow{a.s.^*} 0,
$$
where $\xrightarrow{a.s.^*}$ is outer almost-sure convergence (the supremum may fail to be measurable; the outer expectation $\mathbb{E}^*$ handles this without changing anything for the countable or pointwise-separable classes met in practice). Equivalently $\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} \to 0$ in (outer) probability suffices to call $\mathcal{F}$ *Glivenko-Cantelli in probability*; for the classes below the two notions coincide.

**Definition (bracketing number).** Given two functions $\ell \le u$, the *bracket* $[\ell, u]$ is the set of all $g$ with $\ell \le g \le u$ pointwise; it is an *$\varepsilon$-bracket in $L^1(P)$* if $P(u - \ell) \le \varepsilon$. The *bracketing number* $N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, L^1(P))$ is the minimum number of $\varepsilon$-brackets needed to cover $\mathcal{F}$ (every $f \in \mathcal{F}$ lies in at least one bracket). Finiteness of $N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, L^1(P))$ for every $\varepsilon > 0$ is the bracketing entropy condition.

**Definition (Vapnik-Chervonenkis dimension).** A collection $\mathcal{C}$ of subsets of $\mathcal{X}$ *shatters* a finite set $\{x_1, \dots, x_m\}$ if every one of its $2^m$ subsets is cut out as $\{x_1, \dots, x_m\} \cap C$ for some $C \in \mathcal{C}$. The *VC dimension* $V(\mathcal{C})$ is the largest $m$ for which some $m$-point set is shattered (and $\infty$ if arbitrarily large sets are shattered). A class of sets with $V(\mathcal{C}) < \infty$ is a *VC class*; a class of functions is VC if the collection of its subgraphs is a VC class of sets. This is the identical combinatorial notion that governs uniform generalisation bounds in statistical learning theory; the learning-theoretic development is [45.07.03].

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (classical Glivenko-Cantelli).** *Let $X_1, X_2, \dots$ be i.i.d. real random variables with distribution function $F$, and let $F_n$ be the empirical distribution function. Then*
$$
\|F_n - F\|_\infty = \sup_{t \in \mathbb{R}} |F_n(t) - F(t)| \xrightarrow{a.s.} 0.
$$

*Proof.* The argument upgrades the pointwise strong law to uniformity by exploiting monotonicity of $F$ and a finite grid. Fix an integer $m \ge 1$. Using right-continuity and monotonicity of $F$, choose grid points
$$
-\infty = t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_m = +\infty
$$
such that $F(t_j-) - F(t_{j-1}) \le 1/m$ for each $j$; this is possible because $F$ increases from $0$ to $1$ and has at most countably many jumps, so the values $\{i/m : 0 \le i \le m\}$ can be straddled by grid points, splitting any jump exceeding $1/m$ at its location. By the strong law of large numbers [37.02.02] applied to the bounded indicators $\mathbf{1}_{\{X_k \le t_j\}}$ and $\mathbf{1}_{\{X_k < t_j\}}$, for each fixed $j$,
$$
F_n(t_j) \xrightarrow{a.s.} F(t_j), \qquad F_n(t_j-) \xrightarrow{a.s.} F(t_j-),
$$
and since there are only finitely many grid points, all $2m$ of these limits hold simultaneously off a single null set $N_m$.

Fix $\omega \notin N_m$ and any $t \in \mathbb{R}$; say $t \in [t_{j-1}, t_j)$. Monotonicity of both $F_n$ and $F$ gives the two-sided sandwich
$$
F_n(t) - F(t) \le F_n(t_j-) - F(t_{j-1}) = \big(F_n(t_j-) - F(t_j-)\big) + \big(F(t_j-) - F(t_{j-1})\big) \le \big(F_n(t_j-) - F(t_j-)\big) + \tfrac1m,
$$
$$
F_n(t) - F(t) \ge F_n(t_{j-1}) - F(t_j-) = \big(F_n(t_{j-1}) - F(t_{j-1})\big) - \big(F(t_j-) - F(t_{j-1})\big) \ge \big(F_n(t_{j-1}) - F(t_{j-1})\big) - \tfrac1m.
$$
Taking the supremum over $t$ and then $\limsup_n$, every grid-point difference vanishes off $N_m$, leaving
$$
\limsup_{n\to\infty} \sup_{t} |F_n(t) - F(t)| \le \frac1m \quad\text{for all } \omega \notin N_m.
$$
Let $N = \bigcup_m N_m$, a null set. For $\omega \notin N$ the bound holds for every $m$, so the $\limsup$ is $0$, which is uniform almost-sure convergence. $\square$

**Bridge.** The grid-and-monotonicity device builds toward the abstract Glivenko-Cantelli theorem, where monotonicity is replaced by the more flexible *bracketing* of a function class, and it appears again in the uniform law of large numbers that drives M-estimator consistency [45.04.04], where the objective function must be controlled uniformly over the parameter. The foundational reason a pointwise strong law lifts to a uniform one is that a class small enough to be straddled by finitely many brackets of small $L^1(P)$ width can only oscillate, between brackets, by the bracket widths; this is exactly the mechanism by which the indicators $\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty,t]}$ — totally ordered by $t$ and hence bracketable by a coarse grid of quantiles — are tamed. The classical theorem generalises in two directions that the chapter pursues in parallel: the bracketing route, which measures the size of $\mathcal{F}$ by $L^1(P)$-covering with brackets, and the combinatorial Vapnik-Chervonenkis route, which measures it by shattering. Putting these together, the empirical measure $P_n$ is a uniformly consistent estimator of $P$ over any class that is not too rich, and the bridge is the passage from the single distribution function $F$ to an arbitrary index class $\mathcal{F}$, which is the move that opens empirical-process theory.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

For i.i.d. real data with continuous distribution function $F$, the random variable $D_n = \sup_t |F_n(t) - F(t)|$ has a distribution that does not depend on $F$. Briefly say why, and compute the exact value of $D_n$ for the sample $0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9$ against $F(t) = t$ on $[0,1]$ (i.e. $n = 5$). Give the value to two decimal places.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The probability integral transform $U_k = F(X_k)$ makes the data uniform; the worst gap sits just below or just above each order statistic.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Distribution-freeness: $U_k = F(X_k)$ are i.i.d. uniform on $[0,1]$ when $F$ is continuous, and $\sup_t|F_n(t)-F(t)| = \sup_u|G_n(u) - u|$ where $G_n$ is the empirical distribution function of the $U_k$; the law of the latter does not involve $F$. Numeric: with order statistics $t_{(k)} = 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9$ and $n=5$, $D_n = \max_k \max\big(\tfrac{k}{n} - t_{(k)},\, t_{(k)} - \tfrac{k-1}{n}\big)$. Each term equals $0.1$, so $D_5 = \mathbf{0.10}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, true-false).**

The class $\mathcal{F} = \{\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t]} : t \in \mathbb{R}\}$ of all lower-half-line indicators is a Glivenko-Cantelli class for every distribution $P$ on $\mathbb{R}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

This is precisely the classical Glivenko-Cantelli theorem restated in the language of function classes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.** For this class $\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} = \sup_t|F_n(t) - F(t)|$, and the classical Glivenko-Cantelli theorem gives $\sup_t|F_n(t) - F(t)| \xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$ for every $F$. So the half-line indicators form a universal Glivenko-Cantelli class; equivalently, the cells $(-\infty, t]$ have VC dimension $1$, which also forces the GC property by the VC route.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).**

Compute the VC dimension of the class of closed intervals $\mathcal{C} = \{[a, b] : a \le b\}$ on $\mathbb{R}$. Justify both that some $2$-point set is shattered and that no $3$-point set is.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

To shatter a set you must realise *all* of its subsets, including the one omitting the middle of three collinear points.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$V(\mathcal{C}) = \mathbf{2}$. Two points $x_1 < x_2$ are shattered: $\emptyset$ by an interval missing both, $\{x_1\}$ by $[x_1 - 1, x_1]$, $\{x_2\}$ by $[x_2, x_2 + 1]$, and $\{x_1, x_2\}$ by $[x_1, x_2]$. No three points $x_1 < x_2 < x_3$ are shattered: the subset $\{x_1, x_3\}$ cannot be cut out, since any interval containing both $x_1$ and $x_3$ must contain $x_2$ as well. So the largest shattered set has size $2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $\mathcal{F} = \{f_\theta : \theta \in \Theta\}$ with $\Theta \subset \mathbb{R}$ compact, where $(\theta, x) \mapsto f_\theta(x)$ is continuous in $\theta$ for each $x$ and dominated by an envelope $G$ with $PG < \infty$ ($|f_\theta| \le G$). Show $\mathcal{F}$ has finite bracketing numbers $N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, L^1(P))$ for every $\varepsilon > 0$, hence is Glivenko-Cantelli.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Cover $\Theta$ by finitely many small balls; on each ball bracket $f_\theta$ between an infimum and a supremum over the ball, and bound their $L^1(P)$ gap by continuity, an envelope, and dominated convergence.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By compactness cover $\Theta$ with finitely many balls $B_1, \dots, B_N$ of radius $\eta$. On $B_i$ define the bracket $\ell_i(x) = \inf_{\theta \in B_i} f_\theta(x)$ and $u_i(x) = \sup_{\theta \in B_i} f_\theta(x)$; both lie in $[-G, G]$, so they are integrable. Every $f_\theta$ with $\theta \in B_i$ satisfies $\ell_i \le f_\theta \le u_i$, so the $N$ brackets cover $\mathcal{F}$. As $\eta \downarrow 0$, continuity of $\theta \mapsto f_\theta(x)$ gives $u_i(x) - \ell_i(x) \to 0$ pointwise on each ball, and $u_i - \ell_i \le 2G$, so by dominated convergence $P(u_i - \ell_i) \to 0$. Choosing $\eta$ small enough that $\max_i P(u_i - \ell_i) \le \varepsilon$ yields $N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, L^1(P)) \le N < \infty$. The bracketing Glivenko-Cantelli theorem then gives the GC property.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Using the DKW inequality $\mathbb{P}(\sup_t|F_n(t) - F(t)| > \varepsilon) \le 2e^{-2n\varepsilon^2}$, give a non-asymptotic Glivenko-Cantelli proof: show $\sup_t|F_n - F| \to 0$ almost surely by a Borel-Cantelli argument, and state a finite-sample confidence band for $F$ of level $1 - \alpha$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Sum the DKW tail over $n$ for a fixed $\varepsilon$ and apply the first Borel-Cantelli lemma; then invert the tail bound to solve for the band half-width.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. By DKW, $\sum_n \mathbb{P}(\sup_t|F_n - F| > \varepsilon) \le \sum_n 2e^{-2n\varepsilon^2} < \infty$, so by the first Borel-Cantelli lemma $\{\sup_t|F_n - F| > \varepsilon\}$ occurs only finitely often almost surely; hence $\limsup_n \sup_t|F_n - F| \le \varepsilon$ a.s. Intersecting over a sequence $\varepsilon = 1/m$ gives $\sup_t|F_n - F| \xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$. For a band, set $\varepsilon_n = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2n}\log\tfrac{2}{\alpha}}$, so $2e^{-2n\varepsilon_n^2} = \alpha$; then with probability at least $1 - \alpha$ the band $[F_n(t) - \varepsilon_n,\, F_n(t) + \varepsilon_n]$ (clipped to $[0,1]$) contains $F(t)$ simultaneously for all $t$. This is the standard DKW confidence band.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that a finite class $\mathcal{F} = \{f_1, \dots, f_K\}$ with $P|f_j| < \infty$ for each $j$ is Glivenko-Cantelli, directly from the strong law and a union bound. Where does finiteness enter?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each $P_n f_j \to P f_j$ a.s. by the strong law; a finite union of null sets is null.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the strong law of large numbers [37.02.02], for each $j$ there is a null set $N_j$ off which $P_n f_j \to P f_j$. Set $N = \bigcup_{j=1}^K N_j$; as a finite union of null sets, $N$ is null. Off $N$, all $K$ averages converge, so $\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} = \max_{1 \le j \le K}|P_n f_j - P f_j| \to 0$. Finiteness enters exactly at the union: a finite (or countable) union of null sets is null, and the supremum over a finite index set is the max of finitely many convergent sequences. For uncountable classes this fails outright, which is why bracketing or VC control is needed.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the *bracketing Glivenko-Cantelli theorem*: if $N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, L^1(P)) < \infty$ for every $\varepsilon > 0$, then $\mathcal{F}$ is $P$-Glivenko-Cantelli.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Cover $\mathcal{F}$ by finitely many $\varepsilon$-brackets $[\ell_i, u_i]$; bound $P_n f - Pf$ above by $P_n u_i - P u_i + P(u_i - \ell_i)$ and below by $P_n \ell_i - P\ell_i - P(u_i - \ell_i)$, then apply the strong law to the finitely many bracket endpoints.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix $\varepsilon > 0$ and take finitely many $\varepsilon$-brackets $[\ell_i, u_i]$, $i = 1, \dots, N$, covering $\mathcal{F}$, with $P(u_i - \ell_i) \le \varepsilon$ and integrable endpoints. For any $f \in \mathcal{F}$, choose a bracket with $\ell_i \le f \le u_i$. Then
$$
P_n f - Pf \le P_n u_i - Pf = (P_n u_i - P u_i) + (P u_i - P f) \le (P_n u_i - P u_i) + P(u_i - \ell_i) \le (P_n u_i - P u_i) + \varepsilon,
$$
using $Pf \ge P\ell_i \ge Pu_i - P(u_i - \ell_i)$. Symmetrically $P_n f - Pf \ge (P_n \ell_i - P\ell_i) - \varepsilon$. Taking suprema over $f$ (equivalently over the finite bracket list),
$$
\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} \le \max_{1 \le i \le N}\big(|P_n u_i - P u_i| \vee |P_n \ell_i - P\ell_i|\big) + \varepsilon.
$$
By the strong law [37.02.02] applied to the $2N$ integrable functions $u_i, \ell_i$, the max tends to $0$ a.s., so $\limsup_n \|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} \le \varepsilon$ a.s. As $\varepsilon$ ranges over $1/m$ the limsup is $0$ a.s., which is the GC property.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

State Sauer's lemma and use it to explain why a VC class of sets with dimension $V$ has at most polynomially many distinct intersections with any $n$-point sample, and why this polynomial growth (against the exponential $2^n$) is what powers the VC Glivenko-Cantelli theorem through symmetrisation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Sauer's lemma bounds the shattering coefficient $\Delta_n(\mathcal{C}) = \max_{x_1,\dots,x_n}\#\{\{x_1,\dots,x_n\}\cap C : C \in \mathcal{C}\}$ by $\sum_{i=0}^{V}\binom{n}{i}$; compare this to $2^n$ inside a symmetrisation/union bound.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Sauer's lemma.** If $V(\mathcal{C}) = V < \infty$ then the shattering coefficient satisfies $\Delta_n(\mathcal{C}) \le \sum_{i=0}^V \binom{n}{i} \le (n+1)^V$ for all $n$, a polynomial in $n$ rather than $2^n$. Thus on any sample of size $n$ the sets in $\mathcal{C}$ realise at most $(n+1)^V$ distinct subsets. In the VC route to Glivenko-Cantelli one symmetrises: introduce an independent ghost sample and Rademacher signs $\sigma_k$, reducing $\mathbb{E}^*\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{C}}$ to control of the *conditional* Rademacher average $\mathbb{E}_\sigma \sup_{C} |n^{-1}\sum_k \sigma_k \mathbf{1}_C(X_k)|$. Conditionally on the sample, the supremum ranges over only the $\Delta_n(\mathcal{C}) \le (n+1)^V$ distinct sign-patterns, so a finite-class maximal inequality (each $\pm 1$-average is sub-Gaussian) gives a bound of order $\sqrt{V\log(n+1)/n} \to 0$. The polynomial cardinality is decisive: against $2^n$ the union bound is useless, but against $(n+1)^V$ the $\log$ turns it into $V\log n$, which the $1/\sqrt n$ normalisation defeats. Hence finite VC dimension implies the GC property.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (bracketing Glivenko-Cantelli).** If $N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, L^1(P)) < \infty$ for every $\varepsilon > 0$, then $\mathcal{F}$ is $P$-Glivenko-Cantelli, with convergence in outer almost-sure sense [ref: TODO_REF van der Vaart Wellner 1996]. The proof brackets $\mathcal{F}$ by finitely many $L^1(P)$-small brackets and applies the strong law to the integrable endpoints; the bracket width controls the oscillation of $P_n f - Pf$ within each bracket, and the strong law controls the finitely many endpoints. The classical theorem is the special case of the totally ordered class $\{\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty,t]}\}$, where the brackets are quantile cells and the bracketing number is $O(1/\varepsilon)$.

**Theorem 2 (VC Glivenko-Cantelli).** If $\mathcal{C}$ is a VC class of sets with $V(\mathcal{C}) < \infty$ (more generally if $\mathcal{F}$ is a VC class of functions with integrable envelope $F$, $PF < \infty$), then $\mathcal{F}$ is $P$-Glivenko-Cantelli for *every* $P$ — the property is distribution-free [ref: TODO_REF Vapnik Chervonenkis 1971]. The mechanism is symmetrisation followed by Sauer's lemma: the expected uniform deviation is bounded by a Rademacher average over the at most $\sum_{i \le V}\binom{n}{i} \le (n+1)^V$ distinct sample-traces, and the polynomial growth against the $\sqrt n$ normalisation forces $\mathbb{E}^*\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} \to 0$; a concentration (bounded-difference) inequality upgrades the mean convergence to almost-sure. The same finite-VC hypothesis is the exact condition under which empirical risk minimisation generalises in statistical learning theory [45.07.03].

**Theorem 3 (Dvoretzky-Kiefer-Wolfowitz, Massart's constant).** For i.i.d. real data with any distribution function $F$ and every $\varepsilon > 0$,
$$
\mathbb{P}\Big(\sup_t |F_n(t) - F(t)| > \varepsilon\Big) \le 2\,e^{-2n\varepsilon^2},
$$
with the constant $2$ sharp [ref: TODO_REF Massart 1990]; the original DKW bound [ref: TODO_REF Dvoretzky Kiefer Wolfowitz 1956] carried an unspecified constant. This is a non-asymptotic, distribution-free, *uniform* tail for the EDF, strictly stronger than what Hoeffding gives at a single $t$, and it yields the exact-coverage Kolmogorov-Smirnov confidence band $F_n(t) \pm \sqrt{(2n)^{-1}\log(2/\alpha)}$. The asymptotic constant matches: $\sqrt n \sup_t|F_n - F|$ converges in law to the supremum of a Brownian bridge, whose tail is $2\sum_{j\ge1}(-1)^{j-1}e^{-2j^2 x^2} \sim 2e^{-2x^2}$, the Kolmogorov distribution.

**Theorem 4 (uniform-entropy / Pollard's GC theorem).** If $\mathcal{F}$ has integrable envelope $F$ and finite uniform-entropy integral in the weak sense that $\sup_Q N(\varepsilon\|F\|_{Q,1}, \mathcal{F}, L^1(Q)) < \infty$ for each $\varepsilon$ (supremum over finitely supported probability measures $Q$), then $\mathcal{F}$ is $P$-Glivenko-Cantelli [ref: TODO_REF van der Vaart Wellner 1996]. VC classes satisfy the uniform-entropy bound by Sauer's lemma, so Theorem 2 is the combinatorial corollary; bracketing classes satisfy Theorem 1 instead. The two entropy regimes — bracketing in a fixed $L^r(P)$ versus uniform covering across all $Q$ — are the two standard sufficient conditions, neither containing the other.

**Theorem 5 (preservation and consequences).** The Glivenko-Cantelli property is stable under the operations that arise in plug-in statistics: if $\mathcal{F}$ and $\mathcal{G}$ are $P$-GC with integrable envelopes and $\varphi$ is Lipschitz, then $\varphi \circ (\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{G})$, finite sums, finite products of uniformly bounded GC classes, and pointwise minima/maxima are again $P$-GC [ref: TODO_REF van der Vaart 1998]. The statistical payoff is uniform consistency of M-estimators: if the criterion functions $\{m_\theta : \theta \in \Theta\}$ form a GC class, then $\sup_\theta|P_n m_\theta - P m_\theta| \to 0$ a.s., which is exactly the uniform law of large numbers that, with a well-separated maximum, forces argmax consistency [45.04.04].

**Synthesis.** The empirical measure $P_n$ is the most economical estimator of $P$, and the entire chapter is the study of when it is a *uniformly* good one. The foundational reason a pointwise strong law lifts to a uniform law over a class $\mathcal{F}$ is that the class must be small in a sense that admits a finite description at every resolution: either bracketed by finitely many $L^1(P)$-small brackets, or shattering only sets up to a finite VC dimension, and these are two faces of one demand on the metric entropy of $\mathcal{F}$. This is exactly the dichotomy that organises the subject — bracketing entropy controls classes through a fixed reference measure, uniform entropy controls them combinatorially and distribution-free — and the classical Glivenko-Cantelli theorem is the point where both routes degenerate to the totally ordered indicator class with $V = 1$ and $O(1/\varepsilon)$ brackets.

Putting these together, the DKW inequality supplies the sharp non-asymptotic rate that the qualitative theorems leave open, and its asymptotic shadow is the Brownian-bridge supremum that Donsker's empirical-process limit [37.03.03] makes precise, so the EDF's worst gap has a finite-sample exponential tail and a limiting Kolmogorov law that agree in their leading constant. The central insight is that the VC condition that makes $P_n$ uniformly consistent here is identical to the condition that makes empirical risk minimisation generalise in learning theory [45.07.03]: uniform convergence of empirical means to population means over a hypothesis class is simultaneously the engine of nonparametric statistics and of statistical learning, and Glivenko-Cantelli is its qualitative skeleton, with Donsker the quantitative refinement that adds a limit law on top.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (the classical theorem as a bracketing corollary).** *The class $\mathcal{F} = \{\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty,t]} : t \in \mathbb{R}\}$ has bracketing number $N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, L^1(P)) \le \lceil 1/\varepsilon\rceil + 1$, and is therefore $P$-Glivenko-Cantelli for every $P$.*

*Proof.* Pick quantile grid points $-\infty = t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_m = +\infty$ with $F(t_j-) - F(t_{j-1}) \le \varepsilon$, achievable with $m \le \lceil 1/\varepsilon\rceil + 1$ since $F$ rises from $0$ to $1$. Form the brackets $[\ell_j, u_j]$ with $\ell_j = \mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t_{j-1}]}$ and $u_j = \mathbf{1}_{(-\infty, t_j)}$. Any $\mathbf{1}_{(-\infty,t]}$ with $t \in [t_{j-1}, t_j)$ satisfies $\ell_j \le \mathbf{1}_{(-\infty,t]} \le u_j$ pointwise, and $P(u_j - \ell_j) = F(t_j-) - F(t_{j-1}) \le \varepsilon$. So $m$ brackets of $L^1(P)$-width $\le \varepsilon$ cover $\mathcal{F}$, giving the stated bound. Finiteness of the bracketing number for every $\varepsilon$ invokes the bracketing Glivenko-Cantelli theorem (Proposition 2 below), recovering $\sup_t|F_n - F| \to 0$ a.s. without the explicit grid sandwich of the Key theorem. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (bracketing Glivenko-Cantelli).** *If $N_{[\,]}(\varepsilon, \mathcal{F}, L^1(P)) < \infty$ for every $\varepsilon > 0$, then $\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} \xrightarrow{a.s.^*} 0$.*

*Proof.* Fix $\varepsilon > 0$ and brackets $[\ell_i, u_i]_{i=1}^N$ of $L^1(P)$-width $\le \varepsilon$ covering $\mathcal{F}$, endpoints integrable. For $f \in \mathcal{F}$ in bracket $i$,
$$
P_n f - Pf \le P_n u_i - Pf = (P_n u_i - P u_i) + P(u_i - f) \le (P_n u_i - P u_i) + P(u_i - \ell_i),
$$
and the lower bound is symmetric with $\ell_i$. Hence $\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} \le \max_i\big(|P_n u_i - Pu_i| \vee |P_n \ell_i - P\ell_i|\big) + \varepsilon$. The maximum is over $2N$ averages each governed by the strong law of large numbers [37.02.02], so it tends to $0$ almost surely; therefore $\limsup_n \|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} \le \varepsilon$ a.s. Intersecting the corresponding full-measure events over $\varepsilon = 1/k$ gives $\limsup_n \|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{F}} = 0$ a.s. Measurability issues are absorbed into the outer expectation, under which the max of finitely many measurable averages is measurable and the bound is exact. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Sauer-Shelah lemma).** *If $\mathcal{C}$ shatters no set of size $V+1$, then for every $n$ and every $n$-point set, $\#\{\{x_1,\dots,x_n\} \cap C : C \in \mathcal{C}\} \le \sum_{i=0}^{V}\binom{n}{i}$.*

*Proof.* Induct on $n + V$. Write $\Phi(n, V) = \sum_{i=0}^V \binom ni$. Fix points $S = \{x_1, \dots, x_n\}$ and let $\mathcal{T} = \{S \cap C : C \in \mathcal{C}\}$ be the trace, a set system on $S$ shattering no $(V+1)$-subset. Remove $x_n$: let $\mathcal{T}' = \{T \setminus \{x_n\} : T \in \mathcal{T}\}$ be the trace on $S' = S \setminus \{x_n\}$, and let $\mathcal{T}'' = \{T \in \mathcal{T}' : T \in \mathcal{T} \text{ and } T \cup \{x_n\} \in \mathcal{T}\}$ be the family of subsets of $S'$ appearing both with and without $x_n$. Then $|\mathcal{T}| = |\mathcal{T}'| + |\mathcal{T}''|$, because each $T' \in \mathcal{T}'$ lifts to one member of $\mathcal{T}$ unless both extensions are present, contributing the extra count $|\mathcal{T}''|$. Now $\mathcal{T}'$ shatters no $(V+1)$-subset of $S'$ (a shattered subset would be shattered in $\mathcal{T}$), so $|\mathcal{T}'| \le \Phi(n-1, V)$ by induction. And $\mathcal{T}''$ shatters no $V$-subset of $S'$: if it shattered a $V$-set $A \subseteq S'$, then $\mathcal{T}$ would shatter $A \cup \{x_n\}$, a $(V+1)$-set, since every subset of $A$ appears in $\mathcal{T}''$ both with and without $x_n$. Hence $|\mathcal{T}''| \le \Phi(n-1, V-1)$. Adding, $|\mathcal{T}| \le \Phi(n-1,V) + \Phi(n-1,V-1) = \Phi(n,V)$ by Pascal's identity. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (VC Glivenko-Cantelli via symmetrisation).** *Let $\mathcal{C}$ be a VC class of sets with $V(\mathcal{C}) = V < \infty$. Then $\mathbb{E}^*\,\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{C}} \to 0$, and consequently $\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{C}} \xrightarrow{a.s.^*} 0$.*

*Proof.* Let $X_1', \dots, X_n'$ be an independent ghost sample with empirical measure $P_n'$, and $\sigma_1, \dots, \sigma_n$ independent Rademacher signs independent of the data. By the standard symmetrisation inequality,
$$
\mathbb{E}^*\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{C}} \le 2\,\mathbb{E}^*\Big\|\frac1n\sum_{k=1}^n \sigma_k\,\mathbf{1}_{C}(X_k)\Big\|_{\mathcal{C}}.
$$
Condition on $X_1, \dots, X_n$. The sets of $\mathcal{C}$ induce at most $\Delta_n(\mathcal{C}) \le \Phi(n, V) \le (n+1)^V$ distinct vectors $(\mathbf{1}_C(X_1), \dots, \mathbf{1}_C(X_n))$ by Proposition 3. For each fixed such vector $v \in \{0,1\}^n$, $n^{-1}\sum_k \sigma_k v_k$ is a Rademacher average with $\sum_k v_k^2 \le n$, hence sub-Gaussian with parameter $\le 1/n$; the maximal inequality over $M$ sub-Gaussian variables gives
$$
\mathbb{E}_\sigma \max_{C}\Big|\frac1n\sum_k \sigma_k \mathbf{1}_C(X_k)\Big| \le \sqrt{\frac{2\log\big(2\,\Delta_n(\mathcal{C})\big)}{n}} \le \sqrt{\frac{2\,(V\log(n+1) + \log 2)}{n}}.
$$
The right-hand side is deterministic and tends to $0$, so taking expectations over the data, $\mathbb{E}^*\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{C}} \le 2\sqrt{2(V\log(n+1)+\log2)/n} \to 0$. For the almost-sure upgrade, $\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{C}}$ is a function of $n$ independent variables changing by at most $1/n$ when one is altered, so the bounded-difference (McDiarmid) inequality gives $\mathbb{P}(|\,\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{C}} - \mathbb{E}^*\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{C}}| > t) \le 2e^{-2nt^2}$; summability over $n$ and Borel-Cantelli, combined with the mean tending to $0$, yield $\|P_n - P\|_{\mathcal{C}} \to 0$ a.s. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (DKW gives the classical theorem with a rate).** *With the DKW inequality $\mathbb{P}(\sup_t|F_n - F| > \varepsilon) \le 2e^{-2n\varepsilon^2}$ in hand, $\sup_t|F_n - F| \xrightarrow{a.s.} 0$, and moreover $\limsup_n \sqrt{n/\log\log n}\,\sup_t|F_n - F| \le 1/\sqrt2$ almost surely.*

*Proof.* For fixed $\varepsilon$, $\sum_n 2e^{-2n\varepsilon^2} < \infty$, so by the first Borel-Cantelli lemma $\sup_t|F_n - F| > \varepsilon$ finitely often a.s.; intersecting over $\varepsilon = 1/k$ gives a.s. convergence to $0$, a second proof of Glivenko-Cantelli requiring no monotonicity grid, only the uniform tail. For the rate, set $\varepsilon_n = \sqrt{(1+\delta)\log\log n/(2n)}$; then $2e^{-2n\varepsilon_n^2} = 2(\log n)^{-(1+\delta)}$, which is summable along $n_j = \lfloor \rho^j\rfloor$ for $\rho > 1$. Borel-Cantelli along this subsequence plus the monotone control of $\sup_t|F_n - F|$ between consecutive $n_j$ gives $\limsup_n \sqrt{n/\log\log n}\sup_t|F_n - F| \le \sqrt{(1+\delta)/2}$ a.s.; let $\delta \downarrow 0$. (The matching lower bound, giving equality $1/\sqrt2$, is the Smirnov-Chung law of the iterated logarithm for the EDF.) $\square$

## Connections [Master]

The strong law of large numbers [37.02.02] is the sole probabilistic input to every Glivenko-Cantelli theorem in this unit: the classical proof applies it to the finitely many grid indicators, the bracketing proof applies it to the finitely many bracket endpoints, and the VC proof applies a concentration refinement of it (McDiarmid) to upgrade mean convergence to almost-sure. Glivenko-Cantelli is precisely the statement that the strong law holds *uniformly* over an index class, so this unit is the uniform-in-index extension of that one.

Consistency and the modes of stochastic convergence [45.04.01] supply the language: the GC property is uniform-in-$\mathcal{F}$ almost-sure consistency of the plug-in estimator $P_n$ for $P$, and the M-estimator argmax route previewed there is completed here, since the uniform law of large numbers $\sup_\theta|P_n m_\theta - P m_\theta| \to 0$ that the Wald argument needs is exactly the assertion that $\{m_\theta\}$ is a Glivenko-Cantelli class.

Donsker's invariance principle and the functional CLT [37.03.03] is the quantitative successor: where Glivenko-Cantelli gives $\sup_t|F_n - F| \to 0$, the empirical-process invariance principle gives $\sqrt n(F_n - F) \Rightarrow$ an $F$-Brownian bridge in $D[0,1]$, whose supremum is the limiting Kolmogorov-Smirnov law, and the DKW exponential tail $2e^{-2n\varepsilon^2}$ is the finite-sample shadow of that bridge-supremum tail. The Donsker property is to Glivenko-Cantelli what the central limit theorem is to the law of large numbers.

The Vapnik-Chervonenkis theory of learnability [45.07.03] uses the identical VC-dimension and Sauer-lemma machinery proved here: finite VC dimension is simultaneously the condition under which the empirical measure converges uniformly to $P$ (this unit) and the condition under which empirical risk minimisation generalises, so the GC theorem for a VC class and the fundamental theorem of statistical learning are two readings of one uniform-convergence result.

Bernstein's inequality and the sub-exponential regime [45.05.01] furnishes the concentration tools that sharpen these qualitative theorems into rates: the bounded-difference inequality used to upgrade VC mean-convergence to almost-sure convergence, and the sub-Gaussian maximal inequality bounding the Rademacher average over the polynomially many sample-traces, are members of the same concentration family, and they reappear when bracketing entropy is integrated to obtain rates rather than mere convergence.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The uniform convergence of the empirical distribution function was established in 1933 by Valery Glivenko and Francesco Paolo Cantelli in companion papers in the *Giornale dell'Istituto Italiano degli Attuari* [ref: TODO_REF Glivenko 1933]. Glivenko proved the result for continuous distribution functions and Cantelli removed the continuity assumption, giving the general statement that the sample distribution function converges to the population distribution function uniformly with probability one. This was the first uniform law of large numbers and the prototype for all later empirical-process theory; the name *fundamental theorem of statistics* records its role in justifying the sample as a stand-in for the population.

The sharp non-asymptotic form was obtained in 1956 by Aryeh Dvoretzky, Jack Kiefer, and Jacob Wolfowitz [ref: TODO_REF Dvoretzky Kiefer Wolfowitz 1956], who proved an exponential bound $C e^{-2n\varepsilon^2}$ on the uniform deviation in the course of establishing the asymptotic minimax optimality of the empirical distribution function; the optimal constant $C = 2$ resisted proof until Pascal Massart settled it in 1990 [ref: TODO_REF Massart 1990]. The abstraction from the single distribution function to arbitrary classes of sets came from Vladimir Vapnik and Alexey Chervonenkis in 1971 [ref: TODO_REF Vapnik Chervonenkis 1971], whose study of uniform convergence of relative frequencies introduced the combinatorial dimension now bearing their names and the shatter-coefficient bound, work undertaken in the service of pattern recognition and later recognised as the combinatorial core of both nonparametric statistics and statistical learning. Richard Dudley and David Pollard subsequently built the metric-entropy and symmetrisation framework that unifies the bracketing and combinatorial routes, the synthesis codified in van der Vaart and Wellner's monograph [ref: TODO_REF van der Vaart Wellner 1996].
