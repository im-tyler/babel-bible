## Intuition [Beginner]

Two ways of assigning probabilities to the same data can be close or far apart. If they are close, an event that one of them calls nearly impossible the other also calls nearly impossible; if they are far apart, each can call certain what the other calls impossible. Contiguity is the large-sample version of "close." You have a sequence of null laws, one for each sample size, and a matching sequence of alternative laws drifting toward the null. Contiguity says the alternatives never run away: whatever the null deems negligible in the long run, the alternatives also deem negligible.

Why bother? Because it lets you reuse work. Suppose you have computed how some test statistic behaves under the null — its bell curve, its centre, its spread. You would like the same description under a nearby alternative, where the truth might actually lie. Without contiguity you would redo the whole calculation. With contiguity, and one extra ingredient — how the statistic moves together with the likelihood ratio — you simply slide the null bell curve over by a fixed amount and you are done.

The likelihood ratio is the bookkeeper. It records, sample by sample, how much more plausible the alternative makes the data than the null does. Contiguity is exactly the condition that this bookkeeper does not blow up or vanish in a way that severs the two worlds. When it stays well behaved, the two worlds are different views of one scene, and you can pass freely between them.

The takeaway: contiguity is asymptotic absolute continuity; once you have it, the limit behaviour of any statistic under a nearby alternative is the null behaviour shifted by the statistic's covariance with the log-likelihood ratio — no recomputation needed.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture two bell curves for the same test statistic. The first is its distribution under the null: a bell centred at zero. The second is its distribution under a nearby alternative. The lesson of contiguity is that the second bell is the first one slid sideways by a fixed amount, with the same width. You do not reshape the curve; you only translate it. The size of the slide is one number — how strongly the statistic and the likelihood-ratio bookkeeper move together.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We watch the shift appear for a normal mean. Each observation is normal with variance one and unknown mean. The null says the mean is zero; the alternative puts the mean at a small value $h / \sqrt n$, drifting toward zero as the sample grows. The statistic of interest is the standardised sample average $T_n = \sqrt n \, \bar X_n$.

Step 1. The null behaviour. Under the null (mean zero), $T_n = \sqrt n \, \bar X_n$ has mean zero and variance one for every $n$ — a standard bell curve centred at zero.

Step 2. The bookkeeper. The log-likelihood ratio for mean $h/\sqrt n$ against mean zero works out, for this model, to $\Lambda_n = h \, T_n - \tfrac12 h^2$. So the statistic and the log-ratio are linked: $\Lambda_n$ is just $h$ times $T_n$, minus a constant.

Step 3. The shift. The shift is how $T_n$ and $\Lambda_n$ move together. Since $\Lambda_n = h T_n - \tfrac12 h^2$ and $T_n$ has variance one, the covariance of $T_n$ with $\Lambda_n$ is $h \times 1 = h$.

Step 4. Read off the alternative. By the shift rule, under the alternative $T_n$ has the same bell shape, centred now at $h$ instead of zero: variance still one, mean now $h$. Take $h = 2$: under the null $T_n$ centres at $0$; under the alternative it centres at $2$, same spread.

What this tells us: we never recomputed a distribution under the alternative. We took the null bell curve, found one covariance ($h$), and slid the curve over by that amount. That slide is the whole content of the shift rule.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $(\mathcal X_n, \mathcal A_n)$ be measurable spaces and let $P_n, Q_n$ be probability measures on $(\mathcal X_n, \mathcal A_n)$. Convergence in distribution $\xrightarrow{d}$, convergence in probability $\xrightarrow{\mathbb P}$, and the $o_{\mathbb P}/O_{\mathbb P}$ calculus are as in [45.04.01]; the LAN expansion and the central sequence $\Delta_{n,\theta_0}$ are as in [45.04.06].

**Definition (contiguity).** The sequence $(Q_n)$ is *contiguous* with respect to $(P_n)$, written $Q_n \triangleleft P_n$, if for every sequence of events $A_n \in \mathcal A_n$,
$$
P_n(A_n) \to 0 \ \Longrightarrow\ Q_n(A_n) \to 0 .
$$
The sequences are *mutually contiguous*, written $Q_n \triangleleft\triangleright P_n$, if $Q_n \triangleleft P_n$ and $P_n \triangleleft Q_n$. Contiguity is the asymptotic analogue of absolute continuity: $Q \ll P$ means $P(A) = 0 \Rightarrow Q(A) = 0$, and contiguity replaces the single pair by sequences and "$=0$" by "$\to 0$." A fixed pair with $Q \ll P$ gives the constant sequences $Q_n \equiv Q$, $P_n \equiv P$ a contiguous sequence; the genuinely new phenomenon is that $Q_n$ and $P_n$ may be mutually singular for every fixed $n$ yet still be contiguous as sequences.

**Definition (likelihood ratio and its escaping mass).** Assume $Q_n \ll P_n$ (the dominated case; the general case adds the singular part below). Write $W_n = dQ_n/dP_n$ for the likelihood ratio, so $\mathbb{E}_{P_n} W_n = 1$. A sequence of laws *loses mass to $\infty$* under $P_n$ if there exist $M_k \to \infty$ with $\liminf_n Q_n(W_n > M_k) > 0$; equivalently the $P_n$-laws of $W_n$ are not uniformly tight after the change of measure. The relation to the *Hellinger affinity* $\rho_n = \int \sqrt{dP_n\, dQ_n}$ of [37.04.04] is direct: $\rho_n = \mathbb{E}_{P_n}\sqrt{W_n}$, and $\rho_n$ bounded away from $0$ is the affinity form of the no-escaping-mass condition, the asymptotic shadow of the Kakutani dichotomy.

**Definition (the limit log-likelihood ratio under LAN).** When $(P_n, Q_n) = (P_{\theta_0}^{\otimes n}, P_{\theta_0 + h/\sqrt n}^{\otimes n})$ comes from a LAN family at $\theta_0$ [45.04.06], the *log-likelihood ratio* is
$$
\Lambda_n = \log \frac{dQ_n}{dP_n} = \langle h, \Delta_{n,\theta_0}\rangle - \tfrac12\, \langle h, I(\theta_0)\,h\rangle + o_{P_n}(1),
$$
and under $P_n$, $\Lambda_n \xrightarrow{d} N(-\tfrac12\sigma^2, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2 = \langle h, I(\theta_0) h\rangle$. The mean of the limit equals $-\tfrac12$ its variance: the signature of a contiguous log-ratio. This $N(-\tfrac12\sigma^2,\sigma^2)$ law is the canonical limit that drives the lemmas below.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Contiguity is directional.* $Q_n \triangleleft P_n$ does not entail $P_n \triangleleft Q_n$. If $Q_n$ concentrates on a set the null gives small but not vanishing probability while the null spreads onto a set the alternative ignores, one direction can hold and the other fail. Mutual contiguity is the symmetric, two-sided statement, and only it licenses transferring limits in both directions.

- *Fixed-$n$ singularity is compatible with contiguity.* For the Gaussian shift $P_n = N(0, I_n)^{\otimes}$ versus $Q_n = N(\mu_n, I_n)$ with $\sum_k \mu_{n,k}^2 \to c < \infty$, the laws can be mutually singular at each $n$ yet contiguous as sequences. Contiguity is a statement about sequences, not about any single pair; reading it as fixed-$n$ absolute continuity is the standard error.

- *A degenerate limit log-ratio breaks contiguity.* If $\Lambda_n \xrightarrow{d} \Lambda$ with $\mathbb{E}[e^{\Lambda}] < 1$, then mass has escaped to $-\infty$ (equivalently the reciprocal ratio escapes to $+\infty$), and $Q_n$ is *not* contiguous to $P_n$. The criterion is exactly $\mathbb{E}[e^{\Lambda}] = 1$; an inequality, not just convergence, is required.

- *The shift in the third lemma is a covariance, not a mean.* The amount by which a statistic's limit centre moves under the alternative is $\operatorname{Cov}(T, \Lambda)$ in the joint null limit, not the alternative mean of anything computed separately. A statistic asymptotically independent of the log-ratio ($\operatorname{Cov}(T,\Lambda)=0$) has the *same* limit under null and alternative despite the laws differing.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is Le Cam's first lemma: contiguity is completely characterised by the limit law of the likelihood ratio, and in particular by whether that limit conserves total mass. The proof reduces contiguity to uniform integrability of the likelihood ratios via Prohorov's tightness theorem, then identifies the mass-conservation condition $\mathbb{E}[e^\Lambda] = 1$ as the operative criterion. The result is the asymptotic counterpart of the Kakutani affinity dichotomy [37.04.04].

**Theorem (Le Cam's first lemma; van der Vaart Ch. 6).** *Let $P_n, Q_n$ be probability measures with $Q_n \ll P_n$ and likelihood ratio $W_n = dQ_n/dP_n$. The following are equivalent.*

*(a) $Q_n \triangleleft P_n$.*

*(b) If $W_n \xrightarrow{d} W$ along a subsequence under $P_n$, then $\mathbb{E}[W] = 1$.*

*(c) If $1/W_n = dP_n/dQ_n \xrightarrow{d} V$ along a subsequence under $Q_n$, then $V$ places no mass at $0$, i.e. $\mathbb{P}(V = 0) = 0$.*

*In particular, when the log-ratio satisfies $\Lambda_n = \log W_n \xrightarrow{d} N(-\tfrac12\sigma^2, \sigma^2)$ under $P_n$, contiguity holds, because $\mathbb{E}[e^{\Lambda}] = \exp(-\tfrac12\sigma^2 + \tfrac12\sigma^2) = 1$.*

**Proof.** Write $\mathcal L_{P_n}(W_n)$ for the law of $W_n$ under $P_n$.

*(a) $\Rightarrow$ (b).* The family $\{\mathcal L_{P_n}(W_n)\}$ is tight: if not, there are $M_k \to \infty$ and a subsequence with $P_n(W_n > M_k) \ge \varepsilon > 0$ infinitely often — but $P_n(W_n > M) \le \mathbb{E}_{P_n}W_n / M = 1/M \to 0$ uniformly, so the laws *are* tight, and any subsequential weak limit $W$ exists. Suppose along a subsequence $W_n \xrightarrow{d} W$ under $P_n$ with $\mathbb{E}[W] < 1$ (Fatou gives $\le 1$ always). Then mass $1 - \mathbb{E}[W] > 0$ escapes upward: there are $M_k \to \infty$ with $\lim_n \mathbb{E}_{P_n}[W_n \mathbf 1\{W_n > M_k\}] \ge 1 - \mathbb{E}[W] - o(1) > 0$. Setting $A_n = \{W_n > M_{k(n)}\}$ for a slowly increasing $M_{k(n)}$, one has $P_n(A_n) \le 1/M_{k(n)} \to 0$ while $Q_n(A_n) = \mathbb{E}_{P_n}[W_n \mathbf 1_{A_n}] \not\to 0$, contradicting $Q_n \triangleleft P_n$. Hence $\mathbb{E}[W] = 1$.

*(b) $\Rightarrow$ (a).* Suppose $P_n(A_n) \to 0$ but, along a subsequence, $Q_n(A_n) \ge \varepsilon > 0$. By tightness pass to a further subsequence with $W_n \xrightarrow{d} W$ under $P_n$ and $\mathbb{E}[W] = 1$. Since $\mathbb{E}_{P_n}W_n = 1 = \mathbb{E}[W]$ and $W_n \xrightarrow{d} W$, the family $\{W_n\}$ is uniformly integrable under $P_n$ (convergence of means together with weak convergence of non-negative variables forces UI). Then $Q_n(A_n) = \mathbb{E}_{P_n}[W_n \mathbf 1_{A_n}] \to 0$ because $P_n(A_n) \to 0$ and $\{W_n\}$ is UI, contradicting $Q_n(A_n) \ge \varepsilon$. Hence $Q_n \triangleleft P_n$.

*(b) $\Leftrightarrow$ (c).* Under $Q_n$ the variable $1/W_n$ is the likelihood ratio $dP_n/dQ_n$; mass conservation upstairs ($\mathbb{E}[W] = 1$, no escape of $W_n$ to $+\infty$ under $P_n$) is equivalent to no atom of the reciprocal at $0$ under $Q_n$, since $\{W_n > M\}$ corresponds to $\{1/W_n < 1/M\}$ and the escaping $P_n$-mass of $W_n$ is the $Q_n$-mass piling up near $1/W_n = 0$. The change-of-measure bookkeeping $Q_n(W_n > M) = \mathbb{E}_{P_n}[W_n \mathbf 1\{W_n>M\}]$ makes the two escape conditions identical.

*The Gaussian case.* If $\Lambda_n \xrightarrow{d} \Lambda \sim N(-\tfrac12\sigma^2, \sigma^2)$ then $W_n = e^{\Lambda_n} \xrightarrow{d} e^{\Lambda}$ by the continuous-mapping theorem, and for a normal $\Lambda$, $\mathbb{E}[e^{\Lambda}] = \exp(\mathbb{E}\Lambda + \tfrac12\mathrm{Var}\,\Lambda) = \exp(-\tfrac12\sigma^2 + \tfrac12\sigma^2) = 1$. By (b) contiguity holds. $\square$

**Bridge.** The first lemma builds toward the third lemma's transfer of limit laws across measures, and the same likelihood-ratio limit appears again in the local-power computations of [45.04.09], where the non-central chi-squared arises from exactly this contiguous change of measure. The foundational reason contiguity reduces to mass conservation is that the likelihood ratio is a non-negative mean-one variable, so its only way to break the link between $P_n$ and $Q_n$ is to leak mass to $0$ or $\infty$; ruling that out is precisely the uniform-integrability statement that Prohorov tightness and convergence of means supply. This is exactly the asymptotic incarnation of the Kakutani dichotomy [37.04.04]: there the Hellinger product $\prod a_k$ stays positive or collapses, here the Hellinger affinity $\rho_n = \mathbb{E}_{P_n}\sqrt{W_n}$ stays bounded away from $0$ or not, and the $\sqrt{\cdot}$ that linearised the product in Kakutani is the same root-likelihood that linearises the log-ratio under LAN [45.04.06]. Putting these together, contiguity generalises absolute continuity from a single pair to a drifting sequence, and the bridge is that every limit statement proved under the null transports to the contiguous alternative once one knows the joint limit of the statistic with the log-ratio — which is what the third lemma packages.

## Advanced results [Master]

Contiguity is the change-of-measure backbone of the local theory: it certifies that limit statements proved under the null transport to drifting alternatives, and Le Cam's three lemmas are the precise transport rules. The first lemma reduces contiguity to mass conservation of the likelihood-ratio limit; the second supplies the asymptotically normal log-ratio that LAN produces; the third converts any joint null limit into an alternative limit by a Gaussian tilt. The downstream payoff is that local power — the limiting rejection probability of a test under a sequence of alternatives approaching the null at rate $\sqrt n$ — is computed once from the null limit and a covariance, yielding the noncentral chi-squared limits of [45.04.09] uniformly across the Wald, score, and likelihood-ratio statistics.

**Theorem 1 (Le Cam's first lemma; the contiguity characterisation).** Under the hypotheses of the Key theorem, $Q_n \triangleleft P_n$ iff every subsequential limit $W$ of $dQ_n/dP_n$ under $P_n$ satisfies $\mathbb{E}[W] = 1$, iff every subsequential limit $V$ of $dP_n/dQ_n$ under $Q_n$ has $\mathbb{P}(V=0)=0$. Equivalently, in terms of the Hellinger affinity $\rho_n = \mathbb{E}_{P_n}\sqrt{dQ_n/dP_n}$, no escaping mass is the condition that $\rho_n$ stays bounded away from $0$ along the relevant subsequence, the asymptotic refinement of the Kakutani affinity dichotomy [37.04.04] [ref: TODO_REF van der Vaart — Asymptotic Statistics].

**Theorem 2 (Le Cam's second lemma; asymptotic normality of the LAN log-ratio).** In a LAN sequence at $\theta_0$ with central sequence $\Delta_{n,\theta_0} \xrightarrow{d} N(0, I(\theta_0))$, the local log-likelihood ratio $\Lambda_n = \log(dP_{\theta_0+h/\sqrt n}^{\otimes n}/dP_{\theta_0}^{\otimes n})$ satisfies, under $P_{\theta_0}^{\otimes n}$,
$$
\Lambda_n \xrightarrow{d} N\big(-\tfrac12\sigma^2, \sigma^2\big), \qquad \sigma^2 = h^{\mathsf T} I(\theta_0)\, h,
$$
and jointly $(\Delta_{n,\theta_0}, \Lambda_n) \xrightarrow{d} (\Delta, \langle h, \Delta\rangle - \tfrac12\sigma^2)$ with $\Delta \sim N(0, I(\theta_0))$. The mean-equals-minus-half-variance structure makes the alternatives contiguous to the null by Theorem 1, and the joint Gaussian limit is the input to the third lemma [ref: TODO_REF van der Vaart — Asymptotic Statistics].

**Theorem 3 (Le Cam's third lemma; the shifted limit).** Suppose under $P_n$ a sequence of statistics $T_n$ (valued in $\mathbb{R}^m$) and the log-ratio $\Lambda_n$ satisfy
$$
(T_n, \Lambda_n) \xrightarrow{d} N\Big(\begin{pmatrix}\mu\\ -\tfrac12\sigma^2\end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix}\Sigma & c\\ c^{\mathsf T} & \sigma^2\end{pmatrix}\Big),
$$
with $Q_n \triangleleft P_n$. Then under $Q_n$,
$$
T_n \xrightarrow{d} N(\mu + c, \Sigma).
$$
The covariance $\Sigma$ is preserved and the mean is shifted by the cross-covariance $c = \operatorname{Cov}(T, \Lambda)$ of the statistic with the log-ratio in the joint null limit [ref: TODO_REF Le Cam, L. — Locally asymptotically normal families of distributions].

**Theorem 4 (local power of asymptotically linear tests).** Let a test reject when $T_n = a^{\mathsf T}\Delta_{n,\theta_0} \to N(0, a^{\mathsf T} I a)$ exceeds a null-calibrated cutoff. Under the contiguous alternative $\theta_0 + h/\sqrt n$, the third lemma gives $T_n \xrightarrow{d} N(a^{\mathsf T} I h, a^{\mathsf T} I a)$; the limiting power is monotone in the standardised noncentrality $(a^{\mathsf T} I h)/(a^{\mathsf T} I a)^{1/2}$, maximised over directions $a$ by $a \propto h$ (the efficient score direction), and the maximal local power is the Neyman-Pearson power for the limit Gaussian shift experiment $\{N(h, I^{-1})\}$ of [45.04.06]. The multidimensional quadratic statistic has the noncentral chi-squared limit $\chi^2_k(h^{\mathsf T} I h)$ of [45.04.09] [ref: TODO_REF Hájek, J. & Šidák, Z. — Theory of Rank Tests].

**Theorem 5 (the general, possibly singular, first lemma).** Drop $Q_n \ll P_n$. Write the Lebesgue decomposition $Q_n = Q_n^{ac} + Q_n^{\perp}$ with respect to $P_n$ and let $W_n = dQ_n^{ac}/dP_n$. Then $Q_n \triangleleft P_n$ iff (i) every subsequential limit $W$ of $W_n$ under $P_n$ has $\mathbb{E}[W] = 1$ and (ii) $Q_n^{\perp}(\mathcal X_n) \to 0$. Contiguity thus simultaneously controls the escaping mass of the absolutely continuous part and forces the singular part to vanish; the affinity form $\rho_n = \mathbb{E}_{P_n}\sqrt{W_n} \to 1$ (along the subsequence) encodes both [ref: TODO_REF Roussas, G. G. — Contiguity of Probability Measures: Some Applications in Statistics].

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason these lemmas cohere is that the likelihood ratio is a non-negative mean-one martingale-like object whose only pathologies are leakage to $0$ or $\infty$, so contiguity, mass conservation of the limit, and uniform integrability of the ratios are one condition viewed through the negligible-set definition, the Fatou/mean-convergence inequality, and Prohorov tightness respectively. This is exactly the asymptotic incarnation of the Kakutani dichotomy [37.04.04]: the Hellinger affinity that decided absolute continuity versus singularity there decides contiguity versus escape here, and the square root that linearised the product into a sum in Kakutani is the same root-likelihood that linearises the LAN log-ratio of [45.04.06]. The third lemma is dual to the first: where the first rules out escaping mass so that the change-of-measure operator $f \mapsto \mathbb{E}_{P_n}[f e^{\Lambda_n}]$ is asymptotically mass-preserving, the third computes its action on a jointly Gaussian statistic as a mean shift by the covariance $c$, the Gaussian exponential tilt. Putting these together, the central insight is that the entire alternative-distribution theory of the chapter — local power, noncentral chi-squared limits, the asymptotic relative efficiency of competing tests — is the null-distribution theory transported by one covariance, so that no sampling distribution is ever recomputed under a moving alternative. The bridge is convergence of experiments: the local experiments converge to the Gaussian shift [45.04.06], contiguity is what makes that convergence a genuine transfer of risk and power, and the three lemmas are its operational form.

## Full proof set [Master]

The first lemma and the Gaussian special case are proved in the Key theorem; the second and third lemmas and the local-power consequences are recorded here.

**Proposition 1 (second lemma: LAN gives an asymptotically normal log-ratio).** *In a LAN sequence at $\theta_0$, $\Lambda_n \xrightarrow{d} N(-\tfrac12\sigma^2, \sigma^2)$ under $P_{\theta_0}^{\otimes n}$, with $\sigma^2 = h^{\mathsf T} I(\theta_0) h$, and $(\Delta_{n,\theta_0}, \Lambda_n)$ converges jointly to $(\Delta, \langle h, \Delta\rangle - \tfrac12\sigma^2)$, $\Delta \sim N(0, I(\theta_0))$.*

*Proof.* The LAN expansion [45.04.06] gives $\Lambda_n = \langle h, \Delta_{n,\theta_0}\rangle - \tfrac12 h^{\mathsf T} I h + o_{P_n}(1)$. By the multivariate central limit theorem [37.03.02], $\Delta_{n,\theta_0} \xrightarrow{d} \Delta \sim N(0, I)$, so the continuous-mapping theorem applied to the affine map $x \mapsto (x, \langle h, x\rangle - \tfrac12 h^{\mathsf T} I h)$ gives $(\Delta_{n,\theta_0}, \langle h, \Delta_{n,\theta_0}\rangle - \tfrac12 h^{\mathsf T} I h) \xrightarrow{d} (\Delta, \langle h, \Delta\rangle - \tfrac12\sigma^2)$, and Slutsky absorbs the $o_{P_n}(1)$. The second coordinate is normal with mean $-\tfrac12\sigma^2$ and variance $\operatorname{Var}(\langle h, \Delta\rangle) = h^{\mathsf T} I h = \sigma^2$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (third lemma).** *If under $P_n$, $(T_n, \Lambda_n) \xrightarrow{d} N\big((\mu, -\tfrac12\sigma^2), \begin{psmallmatrix}\Sigma & c\\ c^{\mathsf T} & \sigma^2\end{psmallmatrix}\big)$ and $Q_n \triangleleft P_n$, then under $Q_n$, $T_n \xrightarrow{d} N(\mu + c, \Sigma)$.*

*Proof.* By the first lemma the family $\{e^{\Lambda_n}\}$ is uniformly integrable under $P_n$ (mean-one convergence to $e^\Lambda$ with $\mathbb{E}[e^\Lambda]=1$). For bounded continuous $f$ on $\mathbb{R}^m$, the map $(t, \lambda) \mapsto f(t) e^{\lambda}$ is continuous and, by the UI of $e^{\Lambda_n}$ and the joint weak convergence, $\mathbb{E}_{Q_n} f(T_n) = \mathbb{E}_{P_n}[f(T_n) e^{\Lambda_n}] \to \mathbb{E}[f(T) e^{\Lambda}]$. It remains to identify the tilted law $\nu(f) = \mathbb{E}[f(T) e^{\Lambda}]$. The joint density of $(T, \Lambda)$ is the multivariate normal with the stated mean and covariance; multiplying by $e^{\lambda}$ and integrating out $\lambda$ completes the square. Concretely the characteristic function is
$$
\mathbb{E}[e^{\mathrm i u^{\mathsf T} T} e^{\Lambda}] = \exp\Big(\mathrm i u^{\mathsf T}\mu - \tfrac12\sigma^2 + \tfrac12\big(\mathrm i u, 1\big)\begin{psmallmatrix}\Sigma & c\\ c^{\mathsf T} & \sigma^2\end{psmallmatrix}\begin{psmallmatrix}\mathrm i u\\ 1\end{psmallmatrix}\Big),
$$
and expanding the quadratic form gives exponent $\mathrm i u^{\mathsf T}\mu - \tfrac12\sigma^2 - \tfrac12 u^{\mathsf T}\Sigma u + \mathrm i u^{\mathsf T} c + \tfrac12\sigma^2 = \mathrm i u^{\mathsf T}(\mu + c) - \tfrac12 u^{\mathsf T}\Sigma u$. This is the characteristic function of $N(\mu + c, \Sigma)$, and $\nu$ is a probability law since $\nu(1) = \mathbb{E}[e^\Lambda] = 1$. Hence under $Q_n$, $T_n \xrightarrow{d} N(\mu + c, \Sigma)$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (local power of the score test).** *In a LAN family at $\theta_0$, the one-sided score test in direction $h_0$ based on $T_n = (h_0^{\mathsf T} I h_0)^{-1/2}\langle h_0, \Delta_{n,\theta_0}\rangle$ has limiting power $1 - \Phi(z_{1-\alpha} - \delta)$ against $\theta_0 + h_0/\sqrt n$, with $\delta = (h_0^{\mathsf T} I h_0)^{1/2}$.*

*Proof.* Set $\sigma^2 = h_0^{\mathsf T} I h_0$. The pair $(T_n, \Lambda_n)$ with $\Lambda_n = \langle h_0, \Delta_n\rangle - \tfrac12\sigma^2$ has joint null limit $(T, \Lambda)$ Gaussian with $T \sim N(0,1)$, $\operatorname{Cov}(T, \Lambda) = \sigma^{-1}\operatorname{Cov}(\langle h_0,\Delta\rangle, \langle h_0,\Delta\rangle) = \sigma^{-1}\sigma^2 = \sigma = \delta$. By Proposition 2 the alternative limit of $T_n$ is $N(\delta, 1)$. Therefore $\lim_n Q_n(T_n > z_{1-\alpha}) = \mathbb{P}(N(\delta,1) > z_{1-\alpha}) = 1 - \Phi(z_{1-\alpha} - \delta)$, increasing in $\delta = (h_0^{\mathsf T} I h_0)^{1/2}$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (noncentral chi-squared local power).** *For the quadratic score statistic $S_n = \Delta_{n,\theta_0}^{\mathsf T} I(\theta_0)^{-1}\Delta_{n,\theta_0}$ with null limit $\chi^2_k$, the contiguous-alternative limit is $\chi^2_k(\lambda)$ with $\lambda = h^{\mathsf T} I(\theta_0) h$.*

*Proof.* The joint null limit of $(\Delta_n, \Lambda_n)$ is Gaussian with $\operatorname{Cov}(\Delta, \Lambda) = \operatorname{Cov}(\Delta, \langle h, \Delta\rangle) = I h$. By Proposition 2 applied to $T_n = \Delta_n$, under $Q_n$, $\Delta_n \xrightarrow{d} N(Ih, I)$. Setting $Y = I^{-1/2}\Delta_n$'s limit $\sim N(I^{1/2}h, \mathrm{Id})$, $S_n \xrightarrow{d} \|Y\|^2 \sim \chi^2_k(\lambda)$ with $\lambda = \|I^{1/2}h\|^2 = h^{\mathsf T} I h$, since a sum of $k$ squared independent unit-variance normals with mean vector $m$ is noncentral chi-squared with noncentrality $\|m\|^2$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (contiguity from a bounded limiting Hellinger affinity).** *For sequences $P_n, Q_n$ with $\rho_n = \mathbb{E}_{P_n}\sqrt{dQ_n/dP_n}$, if $\liminf_n \rho_n > 0$ then $Q_n \triangleleft P_n$.*

*Proof.* Let $W_n = dQ_n/dP_n$ and suppose along a subsequence $W_n \xrightarrow{d} W$ under $P_n$ (tightness from $\mathbb{E}_{P_n}W_n = 1$). The map $w \mapsto \sqrt w$ is continuous and bounded by $1 + w$, and $\{W_n\}$ has bounded means, so $\sqrt{W_n}$ is uniformly integrable; hence $\mathbb{E}[\sqrt W] = \lim_n \mathbb{E}_{P_n}\sqrt{W_n} = \lim_n \rho_n \ge \delta > 0$. If mass had escaped, $\mathbb{E}[W] < 1$ would force, by the Cauchy-Schwarz relation $\mathbb{E}[\sqrt W] \le \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[W]}\,\sqrt{\mathbb{P}(W>0)}$ together with the escaping-mass accounting, a strictly smaller affinity; the quantitative bound $1 - \rho_n \le h^2(P_n,Q_n)$ and $|Q_n(A_n) - P_n(A_n)| \le \sqrt 2\, h(P_n, Q_n) \le \sqrt{2(1-\delta+o(1))}$ together with the first lemma give $\mathbb{E}[W] = 1$, hence contiguity. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

The local asymptotic normality of [45.04.06] is the source of every concrete contiguity statement in this chapter: the LAN log-likelihood expansion is exactly the hypothesis under which the second lemma produces the $N(-\tfrac12\sigma^2,\sigma^2)$ log-ratio, the first lemma then certifies mutual contiguity of the local alternatives, and the central sequence $\Delta_{n,\theta_0}$ is the statistic whose joint limit with the log-ratio the third lemma tilts; contiguity is what upgrades the convergence of local experiments there into a genuine transfer of risk and power.

Kakutani's theorem on product martingales and absolute continuity [37.04.04] is the finite-$n$ ancestor of the first lemma: the Hellinger affinity $\prod_k a_k$ that decides absolute continuity versus singularity of infinite product measures is the same affinity $\rho_n = \mathbb{E}_{P_n}\sqrt{dQ_n/dP_n}$ whose boundedness away from zero decides contiguity, and the square-root linearisation that turns the product martingale into an $L^2$-bounded object is the root-likelihood that linearises the LAN log-ratio; contiguity is the moving-target, drifting-alternative refinement of the static Kakutani dichotomy.

The chi-squared asymptotics of the Wald, score, and likelihood-ratio tests [45.04.09] are the principal payoff of the third lemma: the noncentral chi-squared limit $\chi^2_k(h^{\mathsf T} I h)$ under contiguous alternatives, and hence the local power and asymptotic relative efficiency of these tests, are computed by the single covariance shift of Proposition 4 rather than by re-deriving each statistic's distribution under each moving alternative, so the entire local-power table of that unit is a corollary of contiguity.

The consistency and modes-of-convergence machinery of [45.04.01] supplies the $o_{\mathbb P}$ calculus, the continuous-mapping and Slutsky lemmas, and the Prohorov tightness that the first lemma's uniform-integrability argument rests on; contiguity is the change-of-measure layer built directly on top of those convergence modes.

The delta method of [45.04.02] composes with the third lemma: a smooth transformation of a statistic whose null limit is known has its alternative limit obtained by first tilting (third lemma) and then linearising (delta method), so the local distribution of a plug-in functional under a contiguous alternative is again a translated Gaussian, with the translation propagated through the derivative of the functional.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The notion of contiguity is due to **Lucien Le Cam**, introduced in his 1960 monograph *Locally asymptotically normal families of distributions* [ref: TODO_REF Le Cam, L. — Locally asymptotically normal families of distributions], where the first and third lemmas appear as tools for transferring asymptotic statements between a null hypothesis and a sequence of local alternatives. Le Cam isolated the property that makes such transfer possible — that the alternatives never place asymptotically non-negligible mass where the null places vanishing mass — and recognised it as the sequence-level analogue of absolute continuity, decided by the limiting behaviour of the likelihood ratio. The connection to the Hellinger affinity, and through it to Kakutani's 1948 dichotomy for product measures [ref: TODO_REF van der Vaart — Asymptotic Statistics], situates contiguity within the older measure-theoretic theory of equivalence and singularity of infinite products.

The three lemmas were systematised and put to work by **Jaroslav Hájek and Zbyněk Šidák** in their 1967 *Theory of Rank Tests* [ref: TODO_REF Hájek, J. & Šidák, Z. — Theory of Rank Tests], where contiguity is the engine that yields the asymptotic distributions and local power of rank statistics under contiguous location-shift alternatives — distributions that are otherwise intractable. George Roussas's 1972 monograph extended contiguity to dependent and Markov data, giving the general possibly-singular first lemma and its applications [ref: TODO_REF Roussas, G. G. — Contiguity of Probability Measures: Some Applications in Statistics]. The modern textbook synthesis, in which the first lemma is read off the LAN log-ratio and the third lemma is a one-line Gaussian tilt, follows van der Vaart's *Asymptotic Statistics*, where contiguity is presented as the bridge between the null-distribution theory and all alternative-distribution computations of the local theory.
