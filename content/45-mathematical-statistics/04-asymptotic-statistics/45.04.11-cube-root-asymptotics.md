## Intuition [Beginner]

Most estimators you meet settle down at a familiar speed: collect four times as much data and the typical error halves. That is the square-root rate, and it is what the standard theory of the previous unit delivers when the quantity you optimise is a smooth hill with a rounded top. You climb to the top and read off the answer, and the rounded top is what makes the answer precise.

Some natural estimators do not have a rounded top. Imagine looking for the busiest stretch of a road by sliding a fixed-width window along it and finding where the most cars sit inside. The count inside the window jumps as cars enter and leave; the "best" position is decided by a few cars right at the edges of the window, not by a gentle curve. An estimate pinned down by edges is shakier than one pinned down by a smooth peak, and it improves more slowly: you need eight times the data, not four, to halve the error. That is the cube-root rate.

This unit is about exactly these slow, edge-driven estimators. The mode of a distribution, the position of the densest short interval, a certain rule for binary-choice prediction, and the staircase fit to a density that is known to only go downhill all behave this way. They share one striking feature: after you zoom in by the right amount, the random wobble around the answer always settles into the same fixed shape, regardless of which problem you started from. That shape has a name — Chernoff's distribution.

The one-sentence takeaway: when an estimator is found at a sharp edge rather than a smooth peak, it converges at the cube-root rate, and its rescaled error follows a single universal law, Chernoff's distribution.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture two ways the thing you are maximising can look near its best point. On the left, a smooth rounded hill: the height changes gently, and the top is easy to locate sharply because small sideways moves cost height steadily. On the right, a jagged ridge sitting on top of a gentle downhill bowl: the gentle bowl says roughly where to go, but the jagged top means the exact best point is decided by a few random spikes near the summit. The smooth hill gives the square-root rate; the jagged ridge gives the cube-root rate.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We compare how much data two estimators need, using concrete numbers, to make the cube-root penalty plain. Suppose a smooth-peak estimator has a typical error of $0.10$ when you have $n = 1000$ data points, and it converges at the square-root rate. Suppose an edge-decided estimator also has a typical error of $0.10$ at $n = 1000$, but it converges at the cube-root rate.

Step 1. The square-root rule. At the square-root rate the error scales like $1$ divided by the square root of the count. To halve the error from $0.10$ to $0.05$, you must quadruple the data: $1000 \times 4 = 4000$ points.

Step 2. The cube-root rule. At the cube-root rate the error scales like $1$ divided by the cube root of the count. To halve the error you must multiply the data by $2^3 = 8$: from $1000$ to $8000$ points.

Step 3. Push further. To cut the error to one-tenth, $0.10$ down to $0.01$: the square-root estimator needs $10^2 = 100$ times the data, $100{,}000$ points; the cube-root estimator needs $10^3 = 1000$ times the data, one million points.

Step 4. What this tells us. The cube-root estimator is not broken — it still homes in on the truth — but it is hungry. Each extra digit of precision costs ten times more data than for a square-root estimator at the cheaper rate. This is the price of an answer decided by a few random edge effects rather than by a smooth curve, and it is why people work hard to smooth these estimators when they can.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X_1, \dots, X_n$ be i.i.d. from a law $P$ on $\mathcal{X}$, with empirical measure $P_n = n^{-1}\sum_i \delta_{X_i}$ and notation $P_n f, Pf$ as in [45.04.04]. An M-estimator $\hat\theta_n$ maximises $M_n(\theta) = P_n m_\theta$ over $\theta \in \Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^k$, with population criterion $M(\theta) = P m_\theta$ and maximiser $\theta_0$. Modes of convergence $\xrightarrow{d}, \xrightarrow{\mathbb{P}}$ and the $o_{\mathbb{P}}/O_{\mathbb{P}}$ calculus are those of [45.04.01]; the empirical process $\mathbb{G}_n f = \sqrt n\,(P_n - P)f$ and Donsker / manageable classes are those of [45.05.03].

**Definition (the standard versus the cube-root regime).** The smooth M-estimator theory of [45.04.04] applies when the map $\theta \mapsto m_\theta$ is differentiable in quadratic mean and the criterion increments contract quadratically, $P(m_\theta - m_{\theta_0})^2 = O(|\theta - \theta_0|^2)$; this yields the $\sqrt n$ rate. The *cube-root regime* is the case in which $\theta \mapsto m_\theta$ is too rough for that contraction — typically $m_\theta$ involves an indicator or a hard threshold in $\theta$ — so that the increments contract only linearly,
$$
P\big(m_\theta - m_{\theta_0}\big)^2 \asymp |\theta - \theta_0| \qquad (\theta \to \theta_0),
$$
while the population criterion still has a non-degenerate quadratic maximum.

**Definition (the sharp-edge / Kim-Pollard structure).** The criterion has the *sharp-edge structure* at $\theta_0$ if:

(K1) *quadratic population maximum*: $M(\theta) = M(\theta_0) - \tfrac12 (\theta - \theta_0)^{\mathsf T} V (\theta - \theta_0) + o(|\theta - \theta_0|^2)$ for a positive-definite $V$;

(K2) *linear envelope contraction*: there is a neighbourhood on which $P(m_\theta - m_{\theta_0})^2 \le C\,|\theta - \theta_0|$ and the class $\{m_\theta - m_{\theta_0} : |\theta - \theta_0| < \delta\}$ is *manageable* (a uniform-entropy / Donsker-type control, [45.05.03]);

(K3) *local covariance*: the localized increments have a limiting covariance kernel linear in the separation, $\operatorname{Cov}\big(m_{\theta_0 + g} - m_{\theta_0},\, m_{\theta_0 + h} - m_{\theta_0}\big)$ rescaled gives a kernel $H(g,h)$ with $H(h,h)$ of order $|h|$.

**Definition (localized criterion).** Fix the cube-root rescaling $\theta = \theta_0 + h\,n^{-1/3}$. The *localized criterion process* is
$$
Z_n(h) = n^{2/3}\big[M_n(\theta_0 + h\,n^{-1/3}) - M_n(\theta_0)\big], \qquad h \in \mathbb{R}^k.
$$
The rescaled estimator $\hat h_n = n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0)$ is the argmax of $Z_n$. The power $n^{2/3}$ is the balance point: under (K1) the deterministic part $n^{2/3}[M(\theta_0 + hn^{-1/3}) - M(\theta_0)] \to -\tfrac12 h^{\mathsf T} V h$ stays $O(1)$, and under (K2)-(K3) the stochastic part $n^{2/3}(P_n - P)(m_{\theta_0 + hn^{-1/3}} - m_{\theta_0}) = n^{1/6}\,\mathbb{G}_n(m_{\theta_0+hn^{-1/3}} - m_{\theta_0})$ has variance $n^{1/3}\cdot P(m_{\theta_0+hn^{-1/3}} - m_{\theta_0})^2 \asymp n^{1/3}\cdot |h|n^{-1/3} = O(|h|)$.

**Definition (Chernoff's distribution).** Let $W$ be a two-sided standard Brownian motion on $\mathbb{R}$ (independent standard Brownian motions on $[0,\infty)$ and $(-\infty,0]$, $W(0)=0$), as constructed from [02.15.01]. *Chernoff's distribution* is the law of
$$
\mathcal{Z} = \operatorname*{argmax}_{t \in \mathbb{R}} \big(W(t) - t^2\big),
$$
the location of the maximum of two-sided Brownian motion minus a parabola. It is symmetric about $0$, has a density expressible through Airy functions, and is the canonical limit law of cube-root asymptotics.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *The rate is $n^{1/3}$, not $n^{1/2}$, because of (K2), not because the criterion is non-smooth in $x$.* A criterion can be discontinuous in the data $x$ yet smooth in $\theta$ (the median's $\operatorname{sign}(x-\theta)$ has a *smooth* population map $\theta \mapsto P\,\operatorname{sign}(X-\theta)$, giving the $\sqrt n$ rate of [45.04.04]). The slow rate needs roughness in $\theta$, registered as the linear — not quadratic — envelope contraction.

- *The limit is an argmax, not a Gaussian.* At the smooth rate the limit is $N(0, V^{-1}\Sigma V^{-1})$, a linear image of a Gaussian fluctuation. At the cube-root rate the argmax operation is genuinely nonlinear: $n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \operatorname{argmax}(\mathbb{G} - \tfrac12 h^{\mathsf T} V h)$, which is not Gaussian and has no closed-form mean-plus-noise structure.

- *No plug-in variance exists.* Because the limit is the argmax of a process, the asymptotic "variance" is a fixed numerical constant scaling Chernoff's law, not a functional of $P$ you can estimate by differentiating the criterion. The naive sandwich of [45.04.04] is undefined here — there is no $A$ matrix, since the relevant derivative does not exist.

- *Two-sided Brownian motion, not one-sided.* The limit process runs over all of $\mathbb{R}$; the argmax can fall on either side of $0$. Restricting to $[0,\infty)$ would change the law. The symmetry of Chernoff's distribution is a consequence of the symmetric two-sided construction.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is the *Kim-Pollard cube-root theorem*: under the sharp-edge structure the rescaled M-estimator converges in distribution to the argmax of a Gaussian process with parabolic drift, and in the one-dimensional canonical case this argmax is Chernoff's distribution. The proof has two parts — the localized criterion converges as a process to a Gaussian-minus-parabola, and the argmax continuous-mapping theorem transfers that to the argmax — mirroring the Taylor-plus-Slutsky mechanism of [45.04.04] but with the linearisation replaced by an argmax.

**Theorem (Kim-Pollard cube-root theorem).** *Let $\hat\theta_n$ maximise $M_n = P_n m_\theta$ with $\hat\theta_n \xrightarrow{\mathbb{P}} \theta_0$, $\theta_0$ interior to $\Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}^k$, and suppose the sharp-edge structure (K1)-(K3) holds. Then*
$$
n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \operatorname*{argmax}_{h \in \mathbb{R}^k}\ \Big(\mathbb{G}(h) - \tfrac12\,h^{\mathsf T} V h\Big),
$$
*where $\mathbb{G}$ is a centred Gaussian process with continuous sample paths, $\mathbb{G}(0) = 0$, and covariance $\operatorname{Cov}(\mathbb{G}(g), \mathbb{G}(h)) = H(g,h)$ from (K3), provided the argmax on the right exists and is a.s. unique. In the scalar canonical case $H(g,h) = |g| + |h| - |g-h|$ (so $\operatorname{Var}\,\mathbb{G}(h) = 2|h|$, the covariance of two-sided Brownian motion), the limit equals $(4/V)^{1/3}\,\mathcal{Z}$ with $\mathcal{Z}$ Chernoff's distribution.*

**Proof.** Write the localized criterion $Z_n(h) = n^{2/3}[M_n(\theta_0 + hn^{-1/3}) - M_n(\theta_0)]$ and split it into a deterministic and a stochastic part,
$$
Z_n(h) = \underbrace{n^{2/3}\big[M(\theta_0 + hn^{-1/3}) - M(\theta_0)\big]}_{D_n(h)} + \underbrace{n^{1/6}\,\mathbb{G}_n\big(m_{\theta_0 + hn^{-1/3}} - m_{\theta_0}\big)}_{S_n(h)},
$$
using $M_n - M = (P_n - P) = n^{-1/2}\mathbb{G}_n$ and $n^{2/3}n^{-1/2} = n^{1/6}$.

*The drift converges.* By (K1) the second-order Taylor expansion gives $D_n(h) = n^{2/3}\big[-\tfrac12 (hn^{-1/3})^{\mathsf T}V(hn^{-1/3}) + o(n^{-2/3}|h|^2)\big] \to -\tfrac12 h^{\mathsf T} V h$ for each fixed $h$, uniformly on compacts.

*The fluctuation converges to a Gaussian process.* The summands of $S_n(h)$ are the localized increments $f_{n,h} = m_{\theta_0 + hn^{-1/3}} - m_{\theta_0}$. Their covariance is $n^{1/3}\,\operatorname{Cov}_P(f_{n,g}, f_{n,h})$, which converges to $H(g,h)$ by (K3); finite-dimensional convergence to a Gaussian vector follows from the multivariate central limit theorem [37.03.02] (the triangular-array Lindeberg condition holds because each $f_{n,h}$ is bounded and its variance is $O(n^{-1/3})$). Tightness of $h \mapsto S_n(h)$ on compacts follows from the manageability hypothesis in (K2), which gives a maximal inequality controlling the modulus of continuity ([45.05.03]); thus $S_n \rightsquigarrow \mathbb{G}$ as processes in the topology of uniform convergence on compacts.

*Assemble.* Adding the deterministic drift, $Z_n \rightsquigarrow Z$ where $Z(h) = \mathbb{G}(h) - \tfrac12 h^{\mathsf T} V h$, a Gaussian process with parabolic drift. Because $V \succ 0$ the parabola dominates $\mathbb{G}$ at infinity (a centred Gaussian process with $\operatorname{Var}\,\mathbb{G}(h) = O(|h|)$ grows like $\sqrt{|h|\log|h|}$ by the law of the iterated logarithm, slower than $|h|^2$), so $Z$ attains its maximum at a finite, a.s. unique $h^* = \operatorname{argmax} Z$.

*Argmax continuous mapping.* The functional $\operatorname{argmax}$ is continuous (in the uniform-on-compacts topology) at sample paths possessing a unique well-separated maximum with at-infinity decay; since $Z$ has such paths a.s. and $\hat h_n = n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0) = \operatorname{argmax} Z_n$ is tight (the consistency $\hat\theta_n \xrightarrow{\mathbb{P}} \theta_0$ plus a rate bound from (K1)-(K2) confine $\hat h_n$ to compacts with high probability), the argmax continuous-mapping theorem of [45.04.04] in its process form ([45.05.03]) gives $\hat h_n \xrightarrow{d} h^* = \operatorname{argmax}_h(\mathbb{G}(h) - \tfrac12 h^{\mathsf T}V h)$.

*The scalar canonical reduction.* With $k=1$, $H(g,h) = |g| + |h| - |g-h|$ is the covariance of two-sided Brownian motion $W$, so $\mathbb{G} \stackrel{d}{=} \sqrt2\,W$ after the standardisation, and $Z(h) = \sqrt 2\,W(h) - \tfrac12 V h^2$. The Brownian scaling identity (self-similarity) of [02.15.01], $W(ch) \stackrel{d}{=} \sqrt c\,W(h)$, lets one rescale $h = a\,t$ to convert $\sqrt2\,W(at) - \tfrac12 V a^2 t^2$ into a constant multiple of $W(t) - t^2$: choosing $a$ so the two coefficients align gives $\operatorname{argmax}_h Z = (4/V)^{1/3}\operatorname{argmax}_t(W(t) - t^2) = (4/V)^{1/3}\mathcal{Z}$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem builds toward the asymptotic theory of every shape-constrained and threshold estimator — the Grenander monotone-density NPMLE, the maximum-score estimator, current-status and interval-censoring isotonic estimators, the least-median-of-squares regression — and it appears again in the inference theory of [45.04.10], where the failure of the ordinary bootstrap at this rate forces the m-out-of-n and smoothing repairs. The foundational reason the rate slows to $n^{1/3}$ is the balance struck in the localized criterion: a deterministic parabola of order $n^{2/3}|h n^{-1/3}|^2$ must be matched against a fluctuation of order $n^{1/6}\sqrt{|hn^{-1/3}|}$, and equating the two exponents forces the $n^{-1/3}$ localisation — the same exponent-balancing that in [45.04.04] produced $n^{-1/2}$ from a quadratic fluctuation. This is exactly the argmax theorem of [45.04.04] run at the level of localized processes rather than at the level of the criterion: there consistency was the argmax continuous-mapping applied to deterministic limits, and here asymptotic distribution is the same operation applied to a Gaussian-process limit. Putting these together, the central insight is that the universal limit law $\mathcal{Z}$ is the argmax of two-sided Brownian motion minus a parabola, so every cube-root estimator inherits one shape from the single structural fact that its localized criterion is Brownian plus a quadratic drift; the bridge is the recognition that the smooth regime's *linear* response (a derivative, hence a Gaussian) is replaced here by a *nonlinear* response (an argmax, hence Chernoff's law) precisely because the criterion has no derivative in $\theta$ to linearise.

The ratio $64000/8000 = 8$, whose cube root is $2$, so the error scales by $1/2$: it falls from $0.05$ to $0.025$. The eight-fold data increase buys one halving, the signature of the $n^{1/3}$ rate.

</aside>

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the cube-root localisation exponent from scratch. Suppose the deterministic drift of the criterion at displacement $\delta$ is $-c_1\delta^2$ and the standard deviation of the empirical fluctuation of the increment is $c_2\sqrt{\delta/n}$. Find the displacement scale $\delta_n$ at which drift and fluctuation balance, and confirm it is $n^{-1/3}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set the magnitude of the drift equal to the standard deviation of the fluctuation: $c_1\delta^2 = c_2\sqrt{\delta/n}$, and solve for $\delta$ as a power of $n$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Balancing $c_1\delta^2 = c_2\sqrt{\delta/n}$ gives $\delta^2 = (c_2/c_1)\delta^{1/2}n^{-1/2}$, so $\delta^{3/2} = (c_2/c_1)n^{-1/2}$ and $\delta = (c_2/c_1)^{2/3} n^{-1/3}$. The displacement scale is $\delta_n \asymp n^{-1/3}$, hence $n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0) = O_{\mathbb{P}}(1)$. The linear $\delta$ inside the square root (from the linear envelope contraction K2) is what produces $1/3$ rather than the $1/2$ that a $\delta^2$ inside would give.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

(Grenander estimator and the switch relation.) Let $\hat F_n$ be the empirical distribution function and $\hat f_n$ the Grenander estimator of a decreasing density — the left derivative of the least concave majorant of $\hat F_n$. State the switch relation expressing the event $\{\hat f_n(x_0) \le a\}$ as an argmax event for the process $s \mapsto \hat F_n(s) - a s$, and explain why this puts the Grenander estimator into the cube-root framework.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The least concave majorant's slope drops below $a$ exactly where the process $\hat F_n(s) - as$ is maximised; the maximiser is the inverse function of $\hat f_n$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The switch relation states $\{\hat f_n(x_0) \le a\} = \{\,U_n(a) \ge x_0\,\}$ where $U_n(a) = \operatorname{argmax}_s\,[\hat F_n(s) - a s]$ is the location at which the concave majorant has slope $a$. Thus the value $\hat f_n(x_0)$ is read off an argmax of a centred-empirical-process-plus-linear-drift; localising $a$ near $f(x_0)$ and $s$ near $x_0$, the empirical process $\hat F_n - F$ contributes a Brownian fluctuation while the smooth curvature $f'(x_0) \ne 0$ supplies the parabolic drift, giving $n^{1/3}(\hat f_n(x_0) - f(x_0)) \xrightarrow{d} |4 f(x_0)f'(x_0)|^{1/3}\,(2\mathcal{Z})$. The argmax-of-Brownian-minus-parabola structure is exactly the canonical case of the theorem.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

(Maximum-score estimator.) Manski's estimator maximises $M_n(\theta) = P_n\big[(2Y - 1)\operatorname{sign}(X^{\mathsf T}\theta)\big]$ over $|\theta| = 1$ for binary $Y$. Explain why the indicator/sign in $\theta$ makes (K2) hold with linear rather than quadratic contraction, and hence why the estimator is cube-root rate while the population criterion is smooth.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The increment $m_\theta - m_{\theta_0}$ is non-zero only on the thin lens between the hyperplanes $\{X^{\mathsf T}\theta = 0\}$ and $\{X^{\mathsf T}\theta_0 = 0\}$, whose $P$-measure is of order $|\theta - \theta_0|$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The increment $m_\theta - m_{\theta_0} = (2Y-1)[\operatorname{sign}(X^{\mathsf T}\theta) - \operatorname{sign}(X^{\mathsf T}\theta_0)]$ vanishes except where $X$ lies in the wedge between the two hyperplanes, a region of $P$-measure $\asymp |\theta - \theta_0|$ when $X$ has a bounded density near the boundary. Hence $P(m_\theta - m_{\theta_0})^2 \asymp |\theta - \theta_0|$ — the linear contraction (K2) — even though $M(\theta) = P[(2Y-1)\operatorname{sign}(X^{\mathsf T}\theta)]$ is a smooth integral with a quadratic maximum at $\theta_0$ (K1) because integrating the indicator over $X$ smooths it. Linear contraction with quadratic drift is precisely the sharp-edge structure, so $n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0)$ converges to an argmax of a Gaussian-process-minus-parabola.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

(Bootstrap inconsistency.) Explain heuristically why the ordinary nonparametric bootstrap fails to estimate the law of $n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0)$ consistently, and state one repair (m-out-of-n or smoothing).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The limit is an argmax functional, which is not Hadamard-differentiable; bootstrap consistency for the empirical process does not transfer through a non-smooth functional.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The bootstrapped argmax does not have the right conditional limit law: resampling reproduces the empirical process well, but the argmax map is not (Hadamard-)differentiable, so the delta-method route to bootstrap validity of [45.05.06] breaks, and the bootstrap distribution of $n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n^* - \hat\theta_n)$ does not converge to Chernoff's law — it retains a random, data-dependent limit. Repairs: the *m-out-of-n bootstrap* resamples $m = o(n)$ points so that $m^{1/3}(\hat\theta_m^* - \hat\theta_n)$ is consistent (the smaller resample restores the asymptotic decoupling), or one *smooths* the criterion (kernel-smoothed maximum score, smoothed isotonic estimators) to push the estimator back toward a faster, bootstrappable rate. Subsampling is the general-purpose fix because it requires only that a limit law exist.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

(Chernoff's mode estimator / the shorth.) Chernoff estimates the mode as the midpoint of the shortest interval of fixed length $2h_n$ containing the most data, equivalently the maximiser of $P_n[\mathbf 1\{|X - \theta| \le h_n\}]$. Taking $h_n$ fixed for simplicity, show this fits (K1)-(K3) and identify $V$ in terms of the density curvature at the mode.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The population criterion is $M(\theta) = F(\theta + h) - F(\theta - h)$; expand to second order at the maximiser $\theta_0$ and compute the increment variance from the symmetric-difference set.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The criterion $m_\theta(x) = \mathbf 1\{|x - \theta| \le h\}$ has population version $M(\theta) = F(\theta+h) - F(\theta-h)$, maximised at $\theta_0$ where $f(\theta_0+h) = f(\theta_0-h)$ (the symmetric case $\theta_0 = $ mode); the second derivative is $M''(\theta_0) = f'(\theta_0+h) - f'(\theta_0-h) < 0$, so (K1) holds with $V = -M''(\theta_0)$. The increment $m_\theta - m_{\theta_0}$ is the indicator of the symmetric difference of the two windows, a set of measure $\asymp |\theta - \theta_0|$, giving $P(m_\theta - m_{\theta_0})^2 \asymp |\theta - \theta_0|$ — (K2) with the indicator class manageable (a VC class, [45.05.04]). The localized covariance (K3) is that of two-sided Brownian motion since disjoint window-shifts touch disjoint data. Hence $n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} (4 a/V^2)^{1/3}\mathcal{Z}$ for a constant $a$ from the local Poisson intensity of points entering and leaving the windows, the original Chernoff (1964) result.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

(Why two-sided, and the symmetry of $\mathcal{Z}$.) Using the Brownian scaling identity from [02.15.01] and the time-reversal symmetry of Brownian motion, prove that $\mathcal{Z} = \operatorname{argmax}_t(W(t) - t^2)$ is symmetric about $0$, i.e. $\mathcal{Z} \stackrel{d}{=} -\mathcal{Z}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider $\widetilde W(t) = W(-t)$, which is again a two-sided Brownian motion, and track what the substitution $t \mapsto -t$ does to the parabola $t^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\widetilde W(t) := W(-t)$. Since two-sided Brownian motion is invariant under $t \mapsto -t$ (the two independent half-line Brownian motions swap roles, each remaining a standard Brownian motion), $\widetilde W \stackrel{d}{=} W$ as processes on $\mathbb{R}$. Now $W(t) - t^2 = \widetilde W(-t) - (-t)^2$, so the map $t \mapsto -t$ carries the path $t \mapsto W(t) - t^2$ to the path $s \mapsto \widetilde W(s) - s^2$ (with $s = -t$); the parabola is even, so it is unchanged. The argmax of the second path is $-\mathcal{Z}$ by the substitution, and since $\widetilde W \stackrel{d}{=} W$ the second path has the same law as the first, giving $-\mathcal{Z} \stackrel{d}{=} \mathcal{Z}$. The two-sidedness is essential: a one-sided $W$ on $[0,\infty)$ would force $\mathcal{Z} \ge 0$ and destroy the symmetry.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The cube-root theorem extends to nonparametric pointwise problems where the parabolic drift comes from local density curvature, to interval-censoring and current-status models through the same switch relation, and to a sharp characterisation of when the rate degrades further; the inference difficulty it creates is repaired by subsampling and by smoothing, and the limit law is computable through the parabolic-drift Brownian analysis. The smooth M-estimation of [45.04.04] is the rigid centre from which all of these are the non-smooth deformation.

**Theorem 1 (Kim-Pollard, the general cube-root M-estimation theorem).** Under (K1)-(K3) with the localized increment class manageable for an envelope $F$ with $PF^2 < \infty$, the localized criterion $Z_n \rightsquigarrow Z = \mathbb{G} - \tfrac12 h^{\mathsf T}V h$ in the uniform-on-compacts topology, where $\mathbb{G}$ is the centred Gaussian process with covariance $H$ of (K3); consequently $n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} \operatorname{argmax}_h Z(h)$. The proof is the localized empirical-process convergence of [45.05.03] composed with the argmax continuous-mapping theorem; the manageability hypothesis replaces the Donsker condition of the $\sqrt n$ theory and supplies the maximal inequality controlling the modulus of continuity that yields tightness [ref: TODO_REF Kim, J. & Pollard, D. — Cube root asymptotics].

**Theorem 2 (Grenander estimator and Chernoff's distribution; Groeneboom).** For a density $f$ that is decreasing with $f(x_0) > 0$, $f'(x_0) < 0$, the Grenander NPMLE $\hat f_n$ satisfies
$$
n^{1/3}\big(\hat f_n(x_0) - f(x_0)\big) \xrightarrow{d} \Big|\tfrac12 f(x_0)\,f'(x_0)\Big|^{1/3}\,(2\,\mathcal{Z}),
$$
with $\mathcal{Z} = \operatorname{argmax}_t(W(t) - t^2)$ Chernoff's distribution. The route is the switch relation, converting the value of the monotone NPMLE into the argmax of $s \mapsto \mathbb{F}_n(s) - a s$ (a Brownian-bridge-plus-linear-drift after localisation), and then the parabolic-drift analysis. Groeneboom's explicit study of $W(t) - t^2$ via Airy functions gives the density of $\mathcal{Z}$, $f_{\mathcal Z}(z) = \tfrac12 g(z)g(-z)$ for a function $g$ solving an Airy boundary-value problem, with $\operatorname{Var}\,\mathcal Z \approx 0.26$ [ref: TODO_REF Groeneboom, P. — Estimating a monotone density].

**Theorem 3 (maximum-score estimator; Kim-Pollard).** Manski's maximum-score estimator $\hat\theta_n = \operatorname{argmax}_{|\theta|=1}P_n[(2Y-1)\operatorname{sign}(X^{\mathsf T}\theta)]$ for the binary-choice model with $\operatorname{med}(Y\mid X) = \mathbf 1\{X^{\mathsf T}\theta_0 > 0\}$ converges at rate $n^{1/3}$ to the argmax of a Gaussian process with parabolic drift over the sphere's tangent space, the multivariate Chernoff-type limit; the indicator criterion supplies the linear contraction (K2) while the smoothing-by-integration of the indicator supplies the quadratic drift (K1). The rate and limit are why inference for maximum score is hard and why Horowitz's *smoothed* maximum-score estimator, replacing the sign by a smooth kernel, recovers a faster ($n^{2/5}$ or better) bootstrappable rate at the cost of a bandwidth and a bias [ref: TODO_REF Manski, C. F. — Maximum score estimation of the stochastic utility model of choice].

**Theorem 4 (bootstrap inconsistency and subsampling repair).** The ordinary nonparametric bootstrap is inconsistent for the law of $n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0)$ in the cube-root problems above: the conditional law of $n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n^* - \hat\theta_n)$ given the data converges to a random distribution, not to Chernoff's law, because the argmax functional is not Hadamard-differentiable and the empirical-process bootstrap of [45.05.06] does not pass through it. The *m-out-of-n* bootstrap with $m \to \infty$, $m/n \to 0$, and *subsampling* (Politis-Romano) are consistent: they estimate the limit law of any statistic with a non-degenerate limit, requiring only its existence, and so furnish valid confidence intervals at the $n^{1/3}$ rate; cross-referenced with the bootstrap theory of [45.04.10] [ref: TODO_REF Kim, J. & Pollard, D. — Cube root asymptotics].

**Theorem 5 (Chernoff's mode estimator and the original limit; Chernoff).** The estimator of the mode as the centre of the densest interval, $\hat\theta_n = \operatorname{argmax}_\theta P_n\mathbf 1\{|X-\theta|\le h_n\}$ (with $h_n$ fixed or shrinking slowly), satisfies $n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0) \xrightarrow{d} c\,\mathcal{Z}$ for an explicit constant $c$ built from the density and its curvature at the mode; this is the 1964 calculation in which the limit law $\operatorname{argmax}_t(W(t) - t^2)$ first appeared, before its general role was recognised. The same two-sided-Brownian-minus-parabola object recurs because the localized count of points entering and leaving the sliding window is, in the limit, a two-sided Brownian increment carried by a Poisson intensity, against the smooth curvature of the density [ref: TODO_REF Chernoff, H. — Estimation of the mode].

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason a single law governs every sharp-edge estimator is that the localized criterion is always a Gaussian-process-minus-parabola: the population's quadratic maximum (K1) supplies the parabola while the linear envelope contraction (K2) and the local covariance (K3) force the fluctuation to be two-sided Brownian motion, and the argmax of that fixed object is Chernoff's distribution. The central insight is that the smooth M-estimation of [45.04.04] and the cube-root theory are one machine read at two levels — there the argmax continuous-mapping theorem acted on deterministic limits to give consistency and a *derivative* linearised the criterion into a Gaussian, while here the same argmax theorem acts on a *Gaussian-process* limit because no derivative exists to linearise; this is exactly the passage from a linear response (a sandwich variance) to a nonlinear one (an argmax law).

The rate exponent is not arbitrary: balancing the parabolic drift $n^{2/3}\delta^2$ against the Brownian fluctuation $n^{2/3}\sqrt{\delta/n}$ pins the localisation to $\delta \asymp n^{-1/3}$, which is exactly the structural fact that the $\sqrt n$ theory's quadratic envelope contraction has degraded to a linear one. Putting these together, the inference pathology — no plug-in variance, an inconsistent bootstrap, the m-out-of-n and smoothing repairs of [45.04.10] — is the dual face of the same nonlinearity: the argmax functional that produces the universal limit is the very non-differentiability that defeats the delta-method bootstrap of [45.05.06], so the universality of Chernoff's law and the difficulty of using it are two readings of one structure, the absence of a derivative in $\theta$.

## Full proof set [Master]

The process convergence and argmax transfer are proved in the Key theorem; the rate-balancing is Exercise 3 and the symmetry of $\mathcal{Z}$ is Exercise 8. The remaining Master claims are recorded here.

**Proposition 1 (the parabola dominates the Gaussian; existence and uniqueness of the limiting argmax).** *Let $\mathbb{G}$ be a centred Gaussian process on $\mathbb{R}^k$ with continuous paths, $\mathbb{G}(0)=0$, and $\operatorname{Var}\,\mathbb{G}(h) = O(|h|)$, and let $V \succ 0$. Then $Z(h) = \mathbb{G}(h) - \tfrac12 h^{\mathsf T}V h$ attains a finite maximum a.s., and the maximiser is a.s. unique.*

*Proof.* For the at-infinity decay, the increments $\mathbb{G}(h) - \mathbb{G}(g)$ have variance bounded by a constant times $|h-g|$ on bounded sets (sub-linear growth of the variance plus continuity), so by the Borell-TIS / Dudley entropy bound $\sup_{|h|\le R}\mathbb{G}(h)$ has Gaussian tails with mean $O(\sqrt{R\log R})$. Hence on the shell $|h| \in [R, 2R]$, $Z(h) \le \sup_{|h|\le 2R}\mathbb{G}(h) - \tfrac12\lambda_{\min}(V)R^2$, which $\to -\infty$ a.s. as $R \to \infty$ because $\sqrt{R\log R} = o(R^2)$. Therefore $Z(h) \to -\infty$ as $|h|\to\infty$ a.s., and a continuous function decaying to $-\infty$ attains its supremum on a compact set. Uniqueness: for a Gaussian process the event that the maximum is attained at two distinct points has probability zero, because at fixed $g \ne h$ the difference $Z(g) - Z(h)$ has a non-degenerate Gaussian (hence atomless) law, so $\{Z(g) = Z(h) = \max Z\}$ is null; a separability/continuity argument upgrades this to a.s. uniqueness over all pairs. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (the cube-root rate; tightness of $\hat h_n$).** *Under (K1)-(K3), $n^{1/3}(\hat\theta_n - \theta_0) = O_{\mathbb{P}}(1)$.*

*Proof.* The argument is a peeling / chaining bound on the centred criterion. For displacements with $|\theta - \theta_0| \in [2^{j-1}\delta, 2^j\delta]$ the population drift is at most $-c\,2^{2(j-1)}\delta^2$ by (K1), while the maximal fluctuation of $(P_n-P)(m_\theta - m_{\theta_0})$ over that shell is, by the manageability maximal inequality of [45.05.03] and the linear envelope (K2), of order $n^{-1/2}(2^j\delta)^{1/2}$. For $\hat\theta_n$ to lie in the shell the fluctuation must exceed the drift, $n^{-1/2}(2^j\delta)^{1/2} \gtrsim 2^{2j}\delta^2$, which (summing the geometric series over $j$) has probability tending to $0$ once $\delta = M n^{-1/3}$ with $M$ large. Hence $\mathbb{P}(n^{1/3}|\hat\theta_n - \theta_0| > M) \to 0$ as $M \to \infty$, uniformly in $n$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Chernoff's distribution is well defined and symmetric).** *The variable $\mathcal{Z} = \operatorname{argmax}_t(W(t) - t^2)$, $W$ two-sided Brownian motion, is a.s. finite, a.s. unique, and symmetric about $0$.*

*Proof.* Finiteness and uniqueness are Proposition 1 with $k=1$, $\mathbb{G} = W$ (variance $|t|$, so $O(|t|)$), $V = 2$. Symmetry is Exercise 8: $\widetilde W(t) = W(-t)$ is again two-sided Brownian motion, the parabola is even, and the substitution $t \mapsto -t$ sends $\operatorname{argmax}(W(t)-t^2)$ to $-\operatorname{argmax}(\widetilde W(s) - s^2) \stackrel{d}{=} -\mathcal{Z}$, giving $\mathcal{Z} \stackrel{d}{=} -\mathcal{Z}$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (the scaling reduction to the canonical parabola).** *If $Z(h) = \sigma\,W(h) - \tfrac12 V h^2$ with $W$ two-sided Brownian, $\sigma, V > 0$, then $\operatorname{argmax}_h Z = (\sigma^2/V^2)^{1/3}\,2^{1/3}\,\mathcal{Z}$, hence the limit of the M-estimator is a known constant times Chernoff's variable.*

*Proof.* Substitute $h = a\,t$. By the Brownian scaling identity of [02.15.01], $W(at) \stackrel{d}{=} a^{1/2}W(t)$, so $Z(at) \stackrel{d}{=} \sigma a^{1/2}W(t) - \tfrac12 V a^2 t^2$. Factor out $\sigma a^{1/2}$: $Z(at) \stackrel{d}{=} \sigma a^{1/2}\big(W(t) - \tfrac{V a^{3/2}}{2\sigma}t^2\big)$. Choose $a$ so that $V a^{3/2}/(2\sigma) = 1$, i.e. $a = (2\sigma/V)^{2/3}$; then $Z(at) \stackrel{d}{=} \sigma a^{1/2}(W(t) - t^2)$, a positive multiple of the canonical process, whose argmax (in $t$) is $\mathcal{Z}$. Since $\operatorname{argmax}_h Z = a\operatorname{argmax}_t Z(at) = a\,\mathcal{Z}$, and $a = (2\sigma/V)^{2/3} = 2^{2/3}(\sigma/V)^{2/3}$, the maximiser equals $2^{2/3}(\sigma/V)^{2/3}\mathcal{Z}$. Specialising $\sigma = \sqrt2$ (the standardised two-sided-Brownian fluctuation $\operatorname{Var}\,\mathbb{G}(h) = 2|h|$) recovers $(4/V)^{1/3}\mathcal{Z}$ of the Key theorem. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

The smooth M- and Z-estimator theory [45.04.04] is the regime this unit departs from: there the increment class contracts quadratically and a derivative linearises the criterion into a Gaussian limit at the $\sqrt n$ rate with a sandwich variance, whereas here the increment class contracts only linearly, no derivative exists, and the argmax of a Gaussian-process-minus-parabola replaces the linear response at the $n^{1/3}$ rate. The argmax continuous-mapping theorem that delivered consistency there is the same tool that delivers the limit distribution here, applied one level up to processes.

The empirical-process theory of Donsker and manageable classes [45.05.03] supplies the technical engine: the localized fluctuation $n^{1/6}\mathbb{G}_n(m_{\theta_0 + hn^{-1/3}} - m_{\theta_0})$ converges to a Gaussian process precisely because the increment class is manageable, and the maximal inequality that bounds its modulus of continuity is what yields both the tightness in the Key theorem and the rate bound of Proposition 2. The VC-class control of indicator criteria [45.05.04] is what verifies manageability for the maximum-score and shorth examples.

Two-sided Brownian motion and its scaling self-similarity [02.15.01] are the source of the universal limit: Chernoff's distribution is the argmax of $W(t) - t^2$, and the Brownian scaling identity $W(ct) \stackrel{d}{=} \sqrt c\,W(t)$ is exactly what reduces every cube-root estimator's limit to a constant times this one object (Proposition 4). The local-time and excursion theory of [37.06.01] governs the fine path structure of the maximiser.

The bootstrap theory [45.04.10] meets its sharpest failure here: the ordinary nonparametric bootstrap is inconsistent for the cube-root limit because the argmax functional is not Hadamard-differentiable, so the functional-delta-method bootstrap of [45.05.06] does not apply, and the m-out-of-n bootstrap and subsampling are the standard repairs. This is the concrete counterexample that motivates the differentiability hypotheses of bootstrap consistency.

The boundary and non-regular failures of the likelihood-ratio trinity [45.04.09] are a parallel breakdown of the standard $\sqrt n$ / chi-squared machinery — there a parameter on the edge of its cone, here a criterion with no derivative — both reminding that the regularity hypotheses of [45.04.04] and [45.04.03] are load-bearing rather than cosmetic.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The limit law now called *Chernoff's distribution* appeared first in **Herman Chernoff's** 1964 study of mode estimation, where he found that the centre of the densest fixed-width interval converges at rate $n^{1/3}$ to the argmax of two-sided Brownian motion minus a parabola [ref: TODO_REF Chernoff 1964]. The object recurred independently in **Charles Manski's** 1975 maximum-score estimator for discrete-choice models, whose non-smooth indicator criterion produced the same slow rate, and in the analysis of monotone-density and monotone-regression NPMLEs.

The general structural theorem — that a wide family of M-estimators with a sharp-edge criterion share the $n^{1/3}$ rate and an argmax-of-Gaussian-process-minus-parabola limit — was proved by **Jeankyung Kim** and **David Pollard** in 1990, who isolated the manageability and quadratic-mean hypotheses and routed the proof through the argmax continuous-mapping theorem on empirical processes [ref: TODO_REF Kim & Pollard 1990]. The explicit analysis of the canonical process $W(t) - t^2$ — its argmax density via Airy functions and the moments of Chernoff's distribution — is due to **Piet Groeneboom**, who also formalised the *switch relation* connecting isotonic NPMLEs to argmax problems and developed the cube-root theory of shape-constrained estimation through the 1980s [ref: TODO_REF Groeneboom 1985]. The maximum-score rate and its limit were placed in the Kim-Pollard framework, and the subsequent recognition that the ordinary bootstrap fails at this rate, with the m-out-of-n and subsampling repairs, is due to work of **Abrevaya, Huang, Léger, MacGibbon, Politis, Romano,** and others through the 1990s and 2000s.
