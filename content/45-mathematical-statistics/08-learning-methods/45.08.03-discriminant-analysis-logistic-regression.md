## Intuition [Beginner]

You have measurements on some objects and a label attached to each one — emails marked spam or not, tumours marked benign or malignant, irises sorted into species. A classifier is a rule that takes a fresh, unlabelled object and guesses its label from its measurements. This unit is about the simplest honest rules: ones that carve the measurement space into regions with a straight (or gently curved) boundary, one region per label.

There are two opposite ways to build such a rule. The first studies each label on its own: it asks, for each species of iris, what petals typically look like, building a little model of "what an object of this label tends to measure." Then a new object is handed to the label whose model fits it best. This is the *generative* way; linear and quadratic discriminant analysis are its standard members.

The second way skips the per-label modelling and goes straight for the boundary: it asks only "given these measurements, how should the odds of the labels tip?" and tunes a dial — the weights on the measurements — until the predicted odds line up with the labels actually seen. This is the *discriminative* way, and logistic regression is its workhorse. It never bothers to describe what a typical object of each label looks like; it only describes where to draw the line.

A surprise sits at the centre of the unit: when the generative models are matched bell-shaped clouds of the same shape, the two ways produce a boundary of the same straight form. They disagree only on how to place it — by fitting the clouds, or by fitting the line directly.

## Visual [Beginner]

| Approach | What it models | Boundary it produces | Member methods |
|---|---|---|---|
| Generative, shared shape | a bell-shaped cloud per label, same spread | a straight line / flat plane | linear discriminant analysis |
| Generative, free shape | a bell-shaped cloud per label, each its own spread | a curved boundary | quadratic discriminant analysis |
| Discriminative | only the odds of the labels given the data | a straight line / flat plane | logistic regression |

![Two overlapping clouds of points, one cloud for each of two labels, on a plane of two measurements. A straight slanted line separates the plane into two regions; points are assigned to the label whose region they fall in. A second small inset shows the same two clouds but with one cloud wider than the other, and there the separating boundary bends into a curve rather than a straight line.](/img/discriminant-analysis-logistic-regression-placeholder.svg)

The picture to keep: two clouds of labelled points. If the clouds have the same shape, the fair dividing line between them is straight; if one cloud is fatter than the other, the fair divider bends. Linear discriminant analysis and logistic regression both draw the straight line, by different means; quadratic discriminant analysis draws the curve.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Two kinds of fish are sorted by a single measurement, body length in centimetres. Kind A lengths average $10$, kind B lengths average $20$, and both kinds spread the same amount around their average. The two kinds are equally common in the catch. A fish $30$ cm long arrives. Which kind is it?

The fair rule, when the two clouds have the same shape and are equally common, is the simplest one: assign the fish to whichever average it is closer to. The midpoint between the averages is $(10 + 20)/2 = 15$. A length above $15$ leans toward kind B; a length below $15$ leans toward kind A.

Our fish is $30$ cm. Its distance to the kind-A average is $|30 - 10| = 20$. Its distance to the kind-B average is $|30 - 20| = 10$. The fish is closer to kind B, so the rule calls it kind B.

Now change the catch. Suppose kind A is four times as common as kind B. Being common shifts the boundary toward the rarer kind: you need stronger length-evidence to overrule the fact that most fish are kind A. The boundary moves up from $15$ to about $17.8$ for this spread, but our $30$ cm fish sits well above even the shifted line, so it is still called kind B.

What this tells us: with equal, same-shaped clouds the boundary is a single cut-point halfway between the averages, and how common each kind is slides that cut-point toward the rarer kind. Everything later in the unit is this one cut-point, dressed up for many measurements at once.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $(X, G)$ be a feature-label pair with $X \in \mathbb{R}^p$ and $G \in \{1, \dots, K\}$. Write the *prior* class probabilities $\pi_k = \Pr(G = k)$ and the *class-conditional densities* $f_k(x) = f(x \mid G = k)$. By Bayes' rule the *posterior* is $\Pr(G = k \mid X = x) = \pi_k f_k(x) / \sum_{\ell} \pi_\ell f_\ell(x)$.

**Definition (Bayes classifier).** Under $0$-$1$ loss the *Bayes classifier* assigns
$$
\hat G(x) = \arg\max_{k} \Pr(G = k \mid X = x) = \arg\max_{k}\ \pi_k f_k(x).
$$
It minimises the misclassification probability among all rules; its risk is the *Bayes rate*. The set $\{x : \pi_k f_k(x) = \pi_\ell f_\ell(x)\}$ is the *decision boundary* between classes $k$ and $\ell$.

**The generative model: discriminant analysis.** Take Gaussian class-conditionals $f_k(x) = (2\pi)^{-p/2}|\Sigma_k|^{-1/2}\exp\!\big(-\tfrac12 (x - \mu_k)^\top \Sigma_k^{-1}(x - \mu_k)\big)$ [26.03.01]. Comparing $\log \pi_k f_k(x)$ across $k$ and discarding the $k$-free term gives the *quadratic discriminant function*
$$
\delta_k(x) = -\tfrac12 \log|\Sigma_k| - \tfrac12 (x - \mu_k)^\top \Sigma_k^{-1}(x - \mu_k) + \log \pi_k,
$$
and the rule $\hat G(x) = \arg\max_k \delta_k(x)$ is *quadratic discriminant analysis* (QDA): its boundaries are quadric surfaces. If all classes share a covariance $\Sigma_k \equiv \Sigma$, the quadratic term $x^\top \Sigma^{-1} x$ is common to every class and cancels, leaving the *linear discriminant function*
$$
\delta_k(x) = x^\top \Sigma^{-1}\mu_k - \tfrac12 \mu_k^\top \Sigma^{-1}\mu_k + \log\pi_k,
$$
linear in $x$. The rule $\hat G(x) = \arg\max_k \delta_k(x)$ is *linear discriminant analysis* (LDA), with hyperplane boundaries. Parameters are estimated by their maximum-likelihood plug-ins [45.01.04]: $\hat\pi_k = n_k/n$, $\hat\mu_k = n_k^{-1}\sum_{g_i = k} x_i$, and for LDA the pooled $\hat\Sigma = (n - K)^{-1}\sum_k \sum_{g_i = k}(x_i - \hat\mu_k)(x_i - \hat\mu_k)^\top$.

**The discriminative model: logistic regression.** For $K = 2$ classes coded $\{0, 1\}$, posit the *logit-linear* (log-odds) model
$$
\log\frac{\Pr(G = 1 \mid x)}{\Pr(G = 0 \mid x)} = \beta_0 + \beta^\top x =: \eta(x), \qquad p(x) := \Pr(G = 1 \mid x) = \sigma(\eta(x)) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\eta(x)}},
$$
with $\sigma$ the *logistic (sigmoid)* function. Absorbing the intercept into $\beta$ and the constant $1$ into $x$, the *log-likelihood* of an i.i.d. sample is
$$
\ell(\beta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \big[ y_i \log p_i + (1 - y_i)\log(1 - p_i)\big] = \sum_{i=1}^n \big[ y_i\, x_i^\top\beta - \log(1 + e^{x_i^\top\beta})\big],
$$
and the MLE $\hat\beta = \arg\max_\beta \ell(\beta)$ is found by maximising it. The *multinomial (softmax)* extension for $K > 2$ classes fixes a reference class $K$ and models $\log[\Pr(G=k\mid x)/\Pr(G=K\mid x)] = \beta_{k0} + \beta_k^\top x$, giving $\Pr(G=k\mid x) = e^{\beta_k^\top x}/\sum_\ell e^{\beta_\ell^\top x}$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *LDA is not nearest-centroid.* The rule "assign to the nearest class mean in Euclidean distance" matches LDA only when $\Sigma = I$ and the priors are equal. In general the relevant distance is the Mahalanobis distance $(x - \mu_k)^\top \Sigma^{-1}(x - \mu_k)$, and unequal priors shift the boundary by $\log\pi_k$.
- *The logit is the log-odds, not the log-probability.* Modelling $\log p(x)$ as linear violates the constraint $p \le 1$; the logit $\log[p/(1-p)]$ maps $(0,1)$ onto all of $\mathbb{R}$, which is what makes a linear model on it coherent.
- *More classes does not always favour QDA.* QDA estimates a separate $\Sigma_k$ per class — $K p(p+1)/2$ covariance parameters — and on small samples this variance can swamp the bias LDA pays for pooling; the pooled estimate is often the better classifier despite its wrong shared-covariance assumption.
- *Perfect separation is not a perfect fit.* If a hyperplane separates the two classes with no overlap, the logistic likelihood has no maximiser: $\|\hat\beta\| \to \infty$ drives the fitted probabilities to $\{0,1\}$ and the likelihood up without bound. The MLE fails to exist exactly when the data are linearly separable.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature computational result is that the maximum-likelihood fit of logistic regression is a sequence of weighted least-squares fits: Newton-Raphson on the log-likelihood is *iteratively reweighted least squares* (IRLS), reusing the normal-equations machinery of the linear model [45.06.01] with a diagonal weight matrix that the current fitted probabilities supply. The result follows Hastie, Tibshirani & Friedman [ref: TODO_REF Hastie 2009] §4.4 and McCullagh & Nelder [ref: TODO_REF McCullagh 1989] Ch. 4.

**Theorem (logistic regression by IRLS).** Let $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times (p+1)}$ have rows $x_i^\top$ (intercept absorbed) and full column rank, $y \in \{0,1\}^n$. With $p_i = \sigma(x_i^\top\beta)$, $p = (p_1, \dots, p_n)^\top$, and the diagonal weight matrix $W = \operatorname{diag}\big(p_i(1 - p_i)\big)$, the gradient and Hessian of the log-likelihood are
$$
\nabla\ell(\beta) = X^\top(y - p), \qquad \nabla^2\ell(\beta) = -\,X^\top W X.
$$
The Newton-Raphson update $\beta^{\mathrm{new}} = \beta - [\nabla^2\ell(\beta)]^{-1}\nabla\ell(\beta)$ equals the weighted least-squares solution
$$
\beta^{\mathrm{new}} = (X^\top W X)^{-1} X^\top W z, \qquad z = X\beta + W^{-1}(y - p),
$$
where $z$ is the *working response* (the *adjusted dependent variable*). Each step solves a weighted least-squares problem; hence the name.

**Proof.** Differentiate $\ell(\beta) = \sum_i [y_i x_i^\top\beta - \log(1 + e^{x_i^\top\beta})]$. The $i$-th summand has gradient $y_i x_i - x_i\, e^{x_i^\top\beta}/(1 + e^{x_i^\top\beta}) = (y_i - p_i)x_i$, since $\sigma(\eta) = e^\eta/(1 + e^\eta)$. Summing, $\nabla\ell(\beta) = \sum_i (y_i - p_i)x_i = X^\top(y - p)$.

For the Hessian, use $\sigma'(\eta) = \sigma(\eta)(1 - \sigma(\eta))$, so $\partial p_i/\partial\beta = p_i(1 - p_i)x_i$. Then
$$
\nabla^2\ell(\beta) = \frac{\partial}{\partial\beta}\sum_i (y_i - p_i)x_i = -\sum_i p_i(1 - p_i)\, x_i x_i^\top = -X^\top W X,
$$
with $W = \operatorname{diag}(p_i(1 - p_i))$. The weights are positive whenever $0 < p_i < 1$, so $W \succ 0$, and full column rank of $X$ makes $X^\top W X \succ 0$, hence invertible.

Substitute into the Newton step:
$$
\beta^{\mathrm{new}} = \beta + (X^\top W X)^{-1} X^\top(y - p).
$$
Factor $X^\top(y - p) = X^\top W\, W^{-1}(y - p)$ and write $\beta = (X^\top W X)^{-1} X^\top W X\beta$, so
$$
\beta^{\mathrm{new}} = (X^\top W X)^{-1}\big[X^\top W X\beta + X^\top W\, W^{-1}(y - p)\big] = (X^\top W X)^{-1} X^\top W\big(X\beta + W^{-1}(y - p)\big),
$$
which is $(X^\top W X)^{-1} X^\top W z$ with $z = X\beta + W^{-1}(y - p)$. This is the normal equation for the weighted least-squares regression of $z$ on $X$ with weights $W$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The proof's engine is that the Hessian $-X^\top W X$ is the weighted Gram matrix of the linear model [45.06.01] with the Bernoulli variance $p_i(1 - p_i)$ supplying the weights, so each Newton step is the generalized-least-squares solve of that unit applied to the working response $z$. This is exactly the Fisher-scoring identity of [45.01.04] specialised to the canonical-link binomial: because the logistic link is canonical, the observed and expected information coincide and Newton equals Fisher scoring, which is the foundational reason the update reduces to clean weighted least squares rather than something messier. The negative log-likelihood is convex — $X^\top W X \succeq 0$ makes $-\ell$ have a positive-semidefinite Hessian everywhere — so the iteration converges to the unique global maximiser whenever one exists. The result builds toward the regularized fits below, where adding a penalty restores existence and uniqueness on separated or high-dimensional data, and it appears again in the generative/discriminative comparison: putting these together, LDA and logistic regression share the linear log-odds form $\beta_0 + \beta^\top x$, and the central insight of the unit is that they differ only in how that single linear function is fitted — generatively from the Gaussian clouds, or discriminatively by this IRLS maximisation of the conditional likelihood.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the logistic function satisfies $\sigma'(\eta) = \sigma(\eta)(1 - \sigma(\eta))$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $\sigma(\eta) = (1 + e^{-\eta})^{-1}$ and differentiate with the chain rule.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\sigma'(\eta) = -(1 + e^{-\eta})^{-2}\cdot(-e^{-\eta}) = \dfrac{e^{-\eta}}{(1 + e^{-\eta})^2} = \dfrac{1}{1 + e^{-\eta}}\cdot\dfrac{e^{-\eta}}{1 + e^{-\eta}} = \sigma(\eta)(1 - \sigma(\eta))$, since $1 - \sigma(\eta) = e^{-\eta}/(1 + e^{-\eta})$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

In a two-class LDA with $p = 1$, the class means are $\mu_0 = 2$, $\mu_1 = 6$, the common variance is $\sigma^2 = 4$, and the priors are equal. Give the decision boundary (the value of $x$ at which the two linear discriminants are equal).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

With equal priors and equal variance the boundary is the midpoint of the means.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$x = 4$.** Setting $\delta_1(x) = \delta_0(x)$ with equal priors gives $x\mu_1/\sigma^2 - \mu_1^2/(2\sigma^2) = x\mu_0/\sigma^2 - \mu_0^2/(2\sigma^2)$, i.e. $x = (\mu_0 + \mu_1)/2 = (2 + 6)/2 = 4$. The common variance scales both sides equally and cancels.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the two-class LDA boundary in $\mathbb{R}^p$: show that $\delta_1(x) = \delta_0(x)$ is the hyperplane $x^\top \Sigma^{-1}(\mu_1 - \mu_0) = \tfrac12(\mu_1 + \mu_0)^\top \Sigma^{-1}(\mu_1 - \mu_0) - \log(\pi_1/\pi_0)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Subtract the two linear discriminant functions $\delta_k(x) = x^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_k - \tfrac12\mu_k^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_k + \log\pi_k$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\delta_1(x) - \delta_0(x) = x^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\mu_1 - \mu_0) - \tfrac12(\mu_1^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_1 - \mu_0^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_0) + \log(\pi_1/\pi_0)$. Using $\mu_1^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_1 - \mu_0^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_0 = (\mu_1 + \mu_0)^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\mu_1 - \mu_0)$ (since $\Sigma^{-1}$ is symmetric) and setting the difference to zero yields the stated hyperplane. It is linear in $x$ with normal direction $\Sigma^{-1}(\mu_1 - \mu_0)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the negative log-likelihood $-\ell(\beta)$ of logistic regression is convex in $\beta$, and strictly convex when $X$ has full column rank and $0 < p_i < 1$ for all $i$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the Hessian $\nabla^2(-\ell) = X^\top W X$ from the Key theorem and the sign of the weights.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Hessian of $-\ell$ is $X^\top W X$ with $W = \operatorname{diag}(p_i(1-p_i))$. Each weight $p_i(1-p_i) > 0$, so for any $v$, $v^\top X^\top W X v = \sum_i p_i(1-p_i)(x_i^\top v)^2 \ge 0$, giving convexity. Equality forces $x_i^\top v = 0$ for all $i$, i.e. $Xv = 0$; full column rank then forces $v = 0$, so the Hessian is positive definite and $-\ell$ is strictly convex. A strictly convex function has at most one minimiser, so the MLE is unique when it exists.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Carry out one IRLS step for a logistic regression with intercept only (a single column of ones), starting from $\beta = 0$. Show the update reproduces the empirical log-odds.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

With $X = \mathbf 1$ and $\beta = 0$, every $p_i = 1/2$, so $W = \tfrac14 I$. Compute $(X^\top W X)^{-1}X^\top W z$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

At $\beta = 0$: $p_i = \tfrac12$, $W = \tfrac14 I$, $z_i = 0 + (y_i - \tfrac12)/\tfrac14 = 4(y_i - \tfrac12) = 4y_i - 2$. Then $X^\top W X = n/4$ and $X^\top W z = \tfrac14\sum_i(4y_i - 2) = \sum_i y_i - n/2$. So $\beta^{\mathrm{new}} = (4/n)(\sum_i y_i - n/2) = 4(\bar y - \tfrac12) = 4\bar y - 2$. (The iteration continues; at convergence the intercept equals the exact empirical log-odds $\log[\bar y/(1 - \bar y)]$, of which $4\bar y - 2$ is the first Newton iterate.)

</details>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive Fisher's linear discriminant as the generalised eigenproblem $\max_a a^\top B a / a^\top W a$, where $B$ is the between-class and $W$ the within-class scatter, and show the optimal direction is the leading generalised eigenvector of $(B, W)$ [01.01.19].

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The ratio is invariant to scaling $a$, so fix $a^\top W a = 1$ and use a Lagrange multiplier; the stationarity condition is a generalised eigenequation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Maximising $J(a) = a^\top B a / a^\top W a$ is scale-invariant, so impose $a^\top W a = 1$ and maximise $a^\top B a$. The Lagrangian $a^\top B a - \lambda(a^\top W a - 1)$ has stationarity $B a = \lambda W a$, the generalised eigenequation for the pencil $(B, W)$. Since $W \succ 0$, this is equivalent to $W^{-1}B\, a = \lambda a$, and the objective at a solution equals $\lambda$, so the maximiser is the eigenvector of the largest generalised eigenvalue. For two classes $B = (\mu_1 - \mu_0)(\mu_1 - \mu_0)^\top$ is rank one, giving the single direction $a \propto W^{-1}(\mu_1 - \mu_0)$, the same normal as the LDA hyperplane of Exercise 3. The simultaneous diagonalisation of $B$ and $W$ [01.01.19] is what reduces the problem to an ordinary eigenproblem after whitening by $W^{-1/2}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Explain precisely when the logistic-regression MLE fails to exist, and how an $\ell_2$ penalty restores existence. State the condition on the data.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider data that a hyperplane separates with no overlap, and what happens to $\|\beta\|$ as the fitted probabilities approach $\{0, 1\}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The MLE fails to exist exactly when the classes are *linearly separable*: there is a $\beta^*$ with $x_i^\top\beta^* > 0$ for all $y_i = 1$ and $x_i^\top\beta^* < 0$ for all $y_i = 0$ (complete separation), or such inequalities with some equalities (quasi-complete separation). Then scaling $t\beta^*$ with $t \to \infty$ pushes every $p_i$ toward the correct $\{0,1\}$ value, driving $\ell$ up to its supremum $0$ without attaining it, so no finite maximiser exists and $\|\hat\beta\| \to \infty$. Adding a ridge penalty $-\tfrac{\lambda}{2}\|\beta\|^2$ ($\lambda > 0$) makes the penalised objective $\ell(\beta) - \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\|\beta\|^2$ strictly concave and coercive (it tends to $-\infty$ as $\|\beta\| \to \infty$), so a finite unique maximiser always exists regardless of separation. The $\ell_1$ penalty likewise restores existence and additionally sets some coefficients exactly to zero [45.06.06].

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

The generative and discriminative routes both produce a posterior of the same functional form, and identifying where they part is the organising fact of the unit.

**Theorem 1 (LDA posterior is logistic).** Under shared-covariance Gaussian class-conditionals with two classes, the LDA posterior log-odds is exactly linear:
$$
\log\frac{\Pr(G = 1 \mid x)}{\Pr(G = 0 \mid x)} = \log\frac{\pi_1}{\pi_0} - \tfrac12(\mu_1 + \mu_0)^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\mu_1 - \mu_0) + x^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\mu_1 - \mu_0) =: \alpha_0 + \alpha^\top x.
$$
This is the logistic model. LDA and logistic regression therefore posit the *same* linear log-odds; they differ only in how $\alpha_0, \alpha$ are estimated. LDA fits the full joint likelihood $\prod_i f_{g_i}(x_i)\pi_{g_i}$ — the *generative* likelihood, which the Gaussian-plus-prior parameters maximise in closed form — whereas logistic regression fits the *conditional* likelihood $\prod_i \Pr(g_i \mid x_i)$, leaving the marginal of $X$ unmodelled. The generative fit uses the marginal information in the $x_i$ (the shape and separation of the clouds) that the conditional fit discards.

**Theorem 2 (efficiency-robustness trade).** When the Gaussian model holds, LDA is asymptotically more efficient: it uses more of the data's information, and Efron [ref: TODO_REF Efron 1975] showed logistic regression is roughly one-half to two-thirds as efficient in the regimes he computed. When the Gaussian model fails — skewed features, outliers, qualitative predictors — the LDA covariance and mean estimates are biased and its boundary degrades, while logistic regression, committing to nothing about the marginal of $X$, remains consistent for the log-odds whenever the log-odds is genuinely linear. The discriminative method buys robustness with the efficiency it forgoes.

**Theorem 3 (Fisher's reduced-rank discriminant).** With $K$ classes in $\mathbb{R}^p$, the class means $\hat\mu_1, \dots, \hat\mu_K$ span an affine subspace of dimension at most $K - 1$. Fisher's discriminant seeks the directions $a$ maximising the *Rayleigh quotient* $a^\top B a / a^\top W a$, where $B = \sum_k n_k(\hat\mu_k - \bar\mu)(\hat\mu_k - \bar\mu)^\top$ is the between-class scatter and $W$ the pooled within-class scatter. The successive maximisers are the leading generalised eigenvectors of the pencil $(B, W)$ [01.01.19]; equivalently, whiten by $W^{-1/2}$ so the problem becomes the ordinary eigenproblem of the transformed $B$. Projecting onto the leading $L \le K - 1$ such directions and applying LDA in the reduced space is *reduced-rank LDA*, a supervised dimension reduction that loses no LDA information when $L = K - 1$.

**Theorem 4 (regularized logistic regression).** Adding a penalty to the deviance,
$$
\hat\beta_\lambda = \arg\min_\beta\ -\ell(\beta) + \lambda\, \Omega(\beta), \qquad \Omega \in \{\tfrac12\|\beta\|_2^2,\ \|\beta\|_1\},
$$
serves two ends. The $\ell_2$ (ridge) penalty makes the objective strictly convex and coercive, so the minimiser exists and is unique even under linear separation or when $p > n$, shrinking coefficients toward zero. The $\ell_1$ (lasso) penalty additionally produces exact zeros, performing simultaneous fitting and variable selection [45.06.06]; the optimisation is convex but non-smooth, solved by coordinate descent or proximal methods rather than plain Newton. Both penalties trade a controlled bias for variance reduction, the same bias-variance bargain that shrinkage strikes for the linear model [45.06.01].

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason the whole subject coheres is the shared linear log-odds $\alpha_0 + \alpha^\top x$: LDA derives it from matched Gaussian clouds and logistic regression posits it directly, so the two are dual estimators of one boundary — generative versus discriminative fits of the same model. This is exactly the efficiency-robustness duality that Efron quantified: the generative fit harvests the marginal information in $X$ and wins on efficiency when its Gaussian assumption is true, while the discriminative fit, indifferent to that marginal, wins on robustness when it is false, and putting these together explains why neither dominates in practice. The central insight extends to dimension reduction: Fisher's quotient $a^\top B a / a^\top W a$ is the generalised eigenproblem of [01.01.19] wearing statistical clothing, the between/within scatters playing the roles of the two quadratic forms, so supervised projection and classification are one optimisation seen twice. The IRLS engine generalises beyond the logit — every canonical-link generalized linear model fits by the same weighted least squares, with the Bernoulli variance $p(1-p)$ replaced by the model's variance function — and the bridge to the high-dimensional regime is regularization, which restores the existence the separated-data MLE loses and connects classification to the sparsity theory of [45.06.06]. The quadratic case sits at the boundary of this picture: dropping the shared-covariance assumption breaks the linearity, turns the discriminant quadratic, and forfeits the logistic identification, which is the precise sense in which QDA is the generative method without a matching discriminative twin.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Bayes-rule optimality for $0$-$1$ loss).** *Among all classifiers $g : \mathbb{R}^p \to \{1, \dots, K\}$, the Bayes classifier $g^*(x) = \arg\max_k \Pr(G = k \mid X = x)$ minimises the misclassification probability $\Pr(g(X) \ne G)$.*

*Proof.* Condition on $X = x$. For any rule $g$, $\Pr(g(X) \ne G \mid X = x) = 1 - \Pr(G = g(x) \mid X = x)$. This conditional error is minimised, pointwise in $x$, by choosing $g(x)$ to maximise $\Pr(G = g(x) \mid X = x)$, which is the Bayes rule $g^*(x)$. Taking expectation over $X$, $\Pr(g(X) \ne G) = \mathbb{E}_X[1 - \Pr(G = g(X) \mid X)] \ge \mathbb{E}_X[1 - \Pr(G = g^*(X) \mid X)] = \Pr(g^*(X) \ne G)$, since the integrand is minimised pointwise. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (the QDA discriminant and its cancellation to LDA).** *For Gaussian class-conditionals $N(\mu_k, \Sigma_k)$, the Bayes rule is $\arg\max_k \delta_k(x)$ with the quadratic discriminant $\delta_k$; if $\Sigma_k \equiv \Sigma$, $\delta_k$ reduces to a linear function of $x$.*

*Proof.* The Bayes rule maximises $\log\pi_k f_k(x)$. Expanding the Gaussian log-density,
$$
\log\pi_k f_k(x) = \log\pi_k - \tfrac{p}{2}\log(2\pi) - \tfrac12\log|\Sigma_k| - \tfrac12(x - \mu_k)^\top\Sigma_k^{-1}(x - \mu_k).
$$
The term $-\tfrac{p}{2}\log(2\pi)$ is $k$-free and drops from the $\arg\max$, leaving $\delta_k(x)$ as stated. When $\Sigma_k \equiv \Sigma$, expand $-\tfrac12(x - \mu_k)^\top\Sigma^{-1}(x - \mu_k) = -\tfrac12 x^\top\Sigma^{-1}x + x^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_k - \tfrac12\mu_k^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_k$. The quadratic $-\tfrac12 x^\top\Sigma^{-1}x$ and the constant $-\tfrac12\log|\Sigma|$ are $k$-free and drop, leaving $\delta_k(x) = x^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_k - \tfrac12\mu_k^\top\Sigma^{-1}\mu_k + \log\pi_k$, linear in $x$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (gradient, Hessian, and convexity of the logistic deviance).** *With $p_i = \sigma(x_i^\top\beta)$ and $W = \operatorname{diag}(p_i(1 - p_i))$, $\nabla\ell = X^\top(y - p)$ and $\nabla^2\ell = -X^\top W X \preceq 0$; under full column rank of $X$ the inequality is strict, so $-\ell$ is strictly convex.*

*Proof.* The per-observation log-likelihood is $\ell_i(\beta) = y_i x_i^\top\beta - \log(1 + e^{x_i^\top\beta})$. Its gradient is $(y_i - p_i)x_i$ using $\partial_\beta\log(1 + e^{x_i^\top\beta}) = p_i x_i$, and its second derivative is $-p_i(1 - p_i)x_i x_i^\top$ using $\sigma' = \sigma(1 - \sigma)$. Summing gives $\nabla\ell = X^\top(y - p)$ and $\nabla^2\ell = -\sum_i p_i(1-p_i)x_i x_i^\top = -X^\top W X$. For any $v$, $v^\top X^\top W X v = \sum_i p_i(1-p_i)(x_i^\top v)^2 \ge 0$ since each weight is positive, so $-\ell$ is convex; equality needs $x_i^\top v = 0$ for every $i$, i.e. $Xv = 0$, which under full column rank forces $v = 0$. Hence $X^\top W X \succ 0$ and $-\ell$ is strictly convex, with at most one minimiser. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (IRLS = Newton-Raphson).** *The Newton step on $\ell$ equals the weighted least-squares fit $\beta^{\mathrm{new}} = (X^\top W X)^{-1}X^\top W z$ with working response $z = X\beta + W^{-1}(y - p)$.*

*Proof.* As established in the Key theorem: $\beta^{\mathrm{new}} = \beta - (\nabla^2\ell)^{-1}\nabla\ell = \beta + (X^\top W X)^{-1}X^\top(y - p)$. Writing $X^\top(y-p) = X^\top W W^{-1}(y-p)$ and $X^\top W X\beta = X^\top W (X\beta)$, factor out $(X^\top W X)^{-1}X^\top W$ to obtain $(X^\top W X)^{-1}X^\top W(X\beta + W^{-1}(y-p))$, the weighted normal equation for regressing $z$ on $X$ with weights $W$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (non-existence of the MLE under separation; ridge restores it).** *If the data are linearly separable then $\sup_\beta\ell(\beta) = 0$ is not attained. For any $\lambda > 0$ the penalised objective $\ell(\beta) - \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\|\beta\|^2$ attains a unique maximum.*

*Proof.* Suppose $\beta^*$ separates: $x_i^\top\beta^* > 0$ when $y_i = 1$ and $< 0$ when $y_i = 0$ (intercept absorbed). For $t > 0$ and $\beta = t\beta^*$, each term $y_i\log p_i + (1-y_i)\log(1-p_i) \to 0$ as $t \to \infty$ because $p_i \to 1$ on the $y_i = 1$ points and $p_i \to 0$ on the $y_i = 0$ points; each term is negative for finite $t$, so $\ell(t\beta^*) \uparrow 0$ without reaching it, and no finite $\beta$ gives $\ell = 0$. Thus the supremum is unattained. For the penalised objective, $-\ell$ is convex (Proposition 3) and $\tfrac{\lambda}{2}\|\beta\|^2$ is strictly convex and coercive, so $-\ell(\beta) + \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\|\beta\|^2 \to +\infty$ as $\|\beta\| \to \infty$ and is strictly convex; a strictly convex coercive function on $\mathbb{R}^{p+1}$ attains a unique minimum. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The linear model and its hat-matrix least-squares machinery [45.06.01] is reused verbatim inside IRLS: each Newton step is the weighted least-squares solve $(X^\top W X)^{-1}X^\top W z$, the generalized-least-squares normal equation of that unit with the Bernoulli variances $p_i(1-p_i)$ as weights and the working response $z$ as the response, so logistic fitting is a loop over linear-model fits.

- The maximum-likelihood and Fisher-scoring theory [45.01.04] supplies the existence-and-uniqueness logic here: the logistic log-likelihood is concave with Hessian $-X^\top W X$, and because the logit is the canonical link the observed and expected information coincide, so the Newton iteration of that unit is exactly the IRLS of this one, and the moment-matching score equation $X^\top(y - p) = 0$ is its specialisation.

- Newton's method for optimization [44.03.03] is the algorithmic backbone of the discriminative fit: IRLS is Newton-Raphson on the concave deviance, inheriting that unit's local quadratic convergence, and the convexity established here is what upgrades local convergence to global convergence toward the unique maximiser.

- Regularized regression and the lasso [45.06.06] extend directly to classification: the $\ell_2$ penalty restores the existence the separated-data logistic MLE loses, while the $\ell_1$ penalty performs variable selection on the coefficients, the corner geometry and subgradient conditions of that unit carrying over to the logistic deviance in place of the squared-error loss.

- The multivariate normal and covariance machinery [26.03.01] is the generative engine of LDA and QDA: the class-conditional Gaussian densities, their Mahalanobis exponents, and the pooled covariance estimate are the objects whose log-ratio produces the discriminant functions, and the same Gaussian transformation rules govern the sampling behaviour of the plug-in estimates.

- The simultaneous diagonalisation of two quadratic forms and the generalised eigenvalue problem [01.01.19] is precisely Fisher's reduced-rank discriminant: maximising the between/within Rayleigh quotient $a^\top B a / a^\top W a$ is the generalised eigenproblem $Ba = \lambda Wa$, and whitening by $W^{-1/2}$ is the simultaneous-diagonalisation step that turns supervised dimension reduction into an ordinary eigenproblem.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Linear discriminant analysis originates with R. A. Fisher's 1936 study of iris measurements, *The use of multiple measurements in taxonomic problems* [ref: TODO_REF Fisher 1936], which posed the problem of finding the linear combination of features that best separates known groups and solved it through the between-to-within scatter ratio now bearing his name. Fisher framed it without an explicit probability model — as an optimal projection — and the equivalence to the Bayes rule under shared-covariance Gaussians was recognised afterward, by Welch and others, tying the geometric and the generative pictures together. The quadratic generalisation, allowing each class its own covariance, follows directly from the same Gaussian Bayes rule and was standard by the mid-century multivariate-analysis literature.

Logistic regression has a separate lineage. The logistic function was studied by Verhulst in the nineteenth century as a population-growth curve and revived for bioassay by Berkson in the 1940s, who advocated the *logit* against the then-dominant probit. Its maturation as a general regression method came through the unifying framework of generalized linear models, introduced by Nelder and Wedderburn in 1972 and consolidated in McCullagh and Nelder's *Generalized Linear Models* [ref: TODO_REF McCullagh 1989], which identified the logit as the canonical link for binomial responses and the Fisher-scoring fit as iteratively reweighted least squares. The generative-versus-discriminative comparison was sharpened by B. Efron's 1975 calculation [ref: TODO_REF Efron 1975] of the asymptotic relative efficiency of logistic regression against normal discriminant analysis, quantifying the cost the conditional likelihood pays when the Gaussian model is true and, implicitly, the robustness it gains when that model is false.
