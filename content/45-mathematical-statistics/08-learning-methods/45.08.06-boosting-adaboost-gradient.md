## Intuition [Beginner]

Boosting builds a strong predictor out of many weak ones, hired one at a time. A weak learner is a rule that is only a little better than a coin flip — a shallow decision tree, sometimes a single yes/no question. On its own it is useless. But boosting runs a hiring process: it keeps a committee, sees where the committee currently gets things wrong, and hires the next weak rule specifically to handle those hard cases.

The trick is the weights. Every training case carries a weight that says how much it matters right now. At the start all cases matter equally. After each new rule joins, the cases it got wrong have their weight raised and the cases it got right have their weight lowered. So the next rule is trained on a reweighted dataset where the still-unsolved cases shout the loudest. Each rule chases the mistakes left behind by the ones before it.

When a rule is finished, it gets a vote in the final committee, and a good rule that made few weighted mistakes gets a louder vote than a barely-better-than-chance one. To predict a new case, every rule votes, the votes are added up with their weights, and the sign of the total is the answer. A flock of stumps, each near-useless, becomes a sharp classifier.

A useful contrast: bagging also combines many trees, but it grows them all in parallel on shuffled copies of the data and averages them to cancel out their noise. Boosting is sequential and corrective — it lowers the committee's mistakes, not just its jitter.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture three rounds on a scatter of plus and minus points. Round one draws a vertical line; a few points land on the wrong side, and we redraw them bigger. Round two draws a horizontal line aimed at those enlarged points; it fixes them but slips on others, which now grow. Round three cleans up again. No single line separates the data, but the weighted vote of the three does.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Ten training points. A weak learner here is a single threshold rule. We run one round of boosting and read off the new vote and weights.

Start: all ten points have weight $0.1$ (they sum to $1$). The best weak rule we can find misclassifies exactly three of the ten points.

Step 1. Weighted error. The three wrong points each carry weight $0.1$, so the weighted error is $\mathrm{err} = 0.1+0.1+0.1 = 0.3$. The seven correct points contribute nothing to the error.

Step 2. The rule's vote. A rule that is right more often than wrong earns a positive vote, computed as $\alpha = \log\!\big(\tfrac{1-\mathrm{err}}{\mathrm{err}}\big) = \log\!\big(\tfrac{0.7}{0.3}\big) = \log(2.333) = 0.847$. A perfect rule ($\mathrm{err}\to 0$) would earn an enormous vote; a coin-flip rule ($\mathrm{err}=0.5$) earns a vote of $0$.

Step 3. Re-weight. Multiply each wrong point's weight by $e^{\alpha} = e^{0.847} = 2.333$ and leave the right points as they are. The three wrong points go from $0.1$ to $0.1 \times 2.333 = 0.233$ each.

Step 4. Renormalize so the weights sum to $1$ again. The new total is $7(0.1) + 3(0.233) = 0.7 + 0.7 = 1.4$. Dividing through, each wrong point now has weight $0.233/1.4 = 0.167$ and each right point $0.1/1.4 = 0.071$.

What this tells us: after one round the three hard points carry $0.167$ each — more than double the $0.071$ of the easy ones — so the next weak rule is pulled toward fixing exactly the points the first rule missed, while the first rule keeps its $0.847$ vote in the final committee.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let the training sample be $\{(x_i,y_i)\}_{i=1}^n$ with $y_i\in\{-1,+1\}$, and let $\mathcal G$ be a class of base ("weak") classifiers $G:\mathcal X\to\{-1,+1\}$. **Boosting** produces an additive ensemble $f_M(x)=\sum_{m=1}^M \beta_m G_m(x)$ and predicts $\hat y(x)=\mathrm{sign}\, f_M(x)$, where the $G_m$ and coefficients $\beta_m>0$ are chosen sequentially.

**AdaBoost.** Maintain weights $w^{(m)}=(w_1^{(m)},\dots,w_n^{(m)})$ on the sample, initialised $w_i^{(1)}=1/n$. At round $m$:

1. Fit $G_m=\arg\min_{G\in\mathcal G}\sum_{i=1}^n w_i^{(m)}\mathbf 1\{y_i\neq G(x_i)\}$, with weighted error $\mathrm{err}_m=\sum_i w_i^{(m)}\mathbf 1\{y_i\neq G_m(x_i)\}/\sum_i w_i^{(m)}$.
2. Set the vote $\alpha_m=\tfrac12\log\!\big(\tfrac{1-\mathrm{err}_m}{\mathrm{err}_m}\big)$ (a factor $\tfrac12$ relative to the Freund-Schapire constant absorbs the symmetric coding; both conventions give the same classifier).
3. Update $w_i^{(m+1)}=w_i^{(m)}\exp\!\big(-\alpha_m y_i G_m(x_i)\big)$ and renormalise to sum to one. Since $y_iG_m(x_i)=+1$ when correct and $-1$ when wrong, this multiplies wrong points by $e^{\alpha_m}$ and right points by $e^{-\alpha_m}$.

The output is $f_M=\sum_m \alpha_m G_m$ and $\hat y=\mathrm{sign}\, f_M$.

**Forward stagewise additive modelling.** Given a loss $L(y,f)$ and a basis $\{G(\,\cdot\,;\gamma)\}$, build $f_m=f_{m-1}+\beta_m G(\,\cdot\,;\gamma_m)$ where, at each step, $(\beta_m,\gamma_m)=\arg\min_{\beta,\gamma}\sum_{i=1}^n L\big(y_i,\,f_{m-1}(x_i)+\beta\,G(x_i;\gamma)\big)$, never revisiting the earlier terms. The **margin** of $f$ at a point is $y f(x)$; positive margin means correct classification with confidence $|f(x)|$.

**Exponential loss.** $L(y,f)=\exp(-yf)$. Its population risk is $\mathbb E[\,e^{-Yf(X)}\,]$, and AdaBoost is forward stagewise additive modelling under this loss (proved below).

**Gradient boosting.** For a differentiable loss $L$, define the empirical risk $\mathcal R(f)=\sum_{i=1}^n L(y_i,f(x_i))$ as a function of the vector $(f(x_1),\dots,f(x_n))$. The **pseudo-residual** at round $m$ is the negative gradient
$$
\tilde r_{im}=-\left[\frac{\partial L(y_i,f(x_i))}{\partial f(x_i)}\right]_{f=f_{m-1}},
$$
a base learner $h_m$ (typically a regression tree) is fit by least squares to $\{(x_i,\tilde r_{im})\}$, and the update is $f_m=f_{m-1}+\nu\,\rho_m h_m$ with line-searched step $\rho_m=\arg\min_\rho\sum_i L(y_i,f_{m-1}(x_i)+\rho h_m(x_i))$ and **shrinkage** (learning rate) $\nu\in(0,1]$.

The notation here — $\mathbf 1\{\cdot\}$ for indicators, $\mathrm{sign}$, the margin $yf(x)$, and the empirical risk $\mathcal R$ — is recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"AdaBoost minimises the training misclassification rate directly."* It does not. Each round minimises a weighted $0/1$ error to choose $G_m$, but the quantity the whole procedure descends is the exponential loss $\sum_i e^{-y_i f_m(x_i)}$, a smooth surrogate. The misclassification rate is only an upper-bounded by-product (the training-error theorem), not the objective.

- *"Exponential loss and binomial deviance give the same boosting algorithm."* They share the same population minimiser (the half-log-odds), but exponential loss penalises large negative margins as $e^{-yf}$, far more aggressively than deviance's $\log(1+e^{-yf})$. On mislabelled data this makes AdaBoost less robust; gradient boosting with deviance is the standard fix.

- *"More rounds always help, because boosting cannot overfit."* The margin theory explains why test error often keeps falling after training error hits zero, but boosting can and does overfit — with noisy labels, deep base trees, or a vast number of rounds. Shrinkage, shallow trees, and early stopping are needed; the no-overfitting claim is a tendency, not a theorem.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is that AdaBoost's training error is squeezed to zero exponentially fast, as long as every weak learner does a little better than chance. This is the quantitative payoff of reweighting, and it is what justifies calling a collection of barely-useful rules a strong learner.

**Theorem (AdaBoost training-error bound; Freund-Schapire).** *Run AdaBoost for $M$ rounds, let $\mathrm{err}_m=\tfrac12-\gamma_m$ be the weighted error at round $m$, and let $Z_m=\sum_i w_i^{(m)}\exp(-\alpha_m y_i G_m(x_i))$ be the round-$m$ normalising constant (before renormalisation). Then the training misclassification rate of $f_M=\sum_m\alpha_m G_m$ satisfies*
$$
\frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf 1\{y_i\neq \mathrm{sign}\,f_M(x_i)\}\;\le\;\prod_{m=1}^M Z_m \;=\;\prod_{m=1}^M 2\sqrt{\mathrm{err}_m(1-\mathrm{err}_m)}\;\le\;\exp\!\Big(-2\sum_{m=1}^M\gamma_m^2\Big).
$$
*In particular, if every weak learner satisfies $\gamma_m\ge\gamma>0$, the training error is at most $e^{-2M\gamma^2}\to 0$.* [ref: TODO_REF Freund 1997]

*Proof.* Unrolling the weight recursion from $w_i^{(1)}=1/n$ and writing each renormalisation factor as $Z_m$,
$$
w_i^{(M+1)}=\frac{1}{n}\,\frac{\exp\!\big(-\sum_{m=1}^M \alpha_m y_i G_m(x_i)\big)}{\prod_{m=1}^M Z_m}=\frac{1}{n}\,\frac{e^{-y_i f_M(x_i)}}{\prod_m Z_m}.
$$
Because the final weights are a probability vector, $\sum_i w_i^{(M+1)}=1$, so $\tfrac1n\sum_i e^{-y_i f_M(x_i)}=\prod_m Z_m$. The misclassification indicator is dominated by the exponential loss: if $y_i\neq\mathrm{sign}\,f_M(x_i)$ then $y_i f_M(x_i)\le 0$ and $e^{-y_i f_M(x_i)}\ge 1$, so $\mathbf 1\{y_i\neq\mathrm{sign}\,f_M(x_i)\}\le e^{-y_i f_M(x_i)}$. Averaging gives
$$
\frac1n\sum_i \mathbf 1\{y_i\neq\mathrm{sign}\,f_M(x_i)\}\le \frac1n\sum_i e^{-y_i f_M(x_i)}=\prod_{m=1}^M Z_m,
$$
the first inequality of the claim. To evaluate $Z_m$, split the sum over correctly and incorrectly classified points. With weights normalised so $\sum_i w_i^{(m)}=1$, the wrong points carry total weight $\mathrm{err}_m$ and the right points $1-\mathrm{err}_m$, and $-\alpha_m y_i G_m(x_i)=+\alpha_m$ on wrong points, $-\alpha_m$ on right points, so
$$
Z_m=(1-\mathrm{err}_m)e^{-\alpha_m}+\mathrm{err}_m\,e^{\alpha_m}.
$$
Minimising over $\alpha_m$: $\tfrac{d Z_m}{d\alpha_m}=-(1-\mathrm{err}_m)e^{-\alpha_m}+\mathrm{err}_m e^{\alpha_m}=0$ gives $e^{2\alpha_m}=(1-\mathrm{err}_m)/\mathrm{err}_m$, i.e. $\alpha_m=\tfrac12\log\frac{1-\mathrm{err}_m}{\mathrm{err}_m}$ — exactly the AdaBoost vote. Substituting back, $e^{-\alpha_m}=\sqrt{\mathrm{err}_m/(1-\mathrm{err}_m)}$ and $e^{\alpha_m}=\sqrt{(1-\mathrm{err}_m)/\mathrm{err}_m}$, so
$$
Z_m=(1-\mathrm{err}_m)\sqrt{\tfrac{\mathrm{err}_m}{1-\mathrm{err}_m}}+\mathrm{err}_m\sqrt{\tfrac{1-\mathrm{err}_m}{\mathrm{err}_m}}=2\sqrt{\mathrm{err}_m(1-\mathrm{err}_m)}.
$$
Writing $\mathrm{err}_m=\tfrac12-\gamma_m$, $4\,\mathrm{err}_m(1-\mathrm{err}_m)=1-4\gamma_m^2$, so $Z_m=\sqrt{1-4\gamma_m^2}$. The elementary inequality $1-u\le e^{-u}$ at $u=4\gamma_m^2$ gives $Z_m\le e^{-2\gamma_m^2}$, and the product telescopes to $\prod_m Z_m\le\exp(-2\sum_m\gamma_m^2)$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The choice $\alpha_m=\tfrac12\log\frac{1-\mathrm{err}_m}{\mathrm{err}_m}$ was not an arbitrary scoring rule: the proof shows it is the unique value that minimises the round's normalising constant $Z_m$, and minimising $Z_m$ each round is exactly greedy minimisation of $\prod_m Z_m=\frac1n\sum_i e^{-y_i f_M(x_i)}$, the exponential loss. This is the foundational reason the algorithm of reweighting-and-voting and the analysis of exponential-loss descent are one object, and it is exactly the equivalence the Advanced results prove in full as forward stagewise additive modelling. The telescoping identity $w_i^{(M+1)}\propto e^{-y_i f_M(x_i)}$ generalises the single-round weight update into a closed form for the cumulative margin, which builds toward the population analysis where minimising $\mathbb E[e^{-Yf}]$ pins $f$ to half the log-odds. The exponential bound appears again in the margin theory of [45.07.06], where the same domination $\mathbf 1\{yf\le 0\}\le e^{-yf}$ is sharpened to control not just the sign of the margin but its distribution; putting these together, the bridge is that boosting drives the empirical margin distribution upward, and the training-error theorem is the crudest reading of that fact.

$\phi'(f)=-p\,e^{-f}+(1-p)e^{f}=0$ gives $e^{2f}=p/(1-p)$, so $f^\*=\tfrac12\log\frac{p}{1-p}$, and $\phi''(f)=p\,e^{-f}+(1-p)e^{f}>0$ confirms the minimum. Hence the minimiser of $\mathbb E[e^{-Yf(X)}]$ is half the log-odds, and inverting, $p(x)=1/(1+e^{-2f^\*(x)})$, so the boosted score is a calibrated estimate of the log-odds. Rubric: full credit for the stationarity computation and the identification of $f^\*$ as half the log-odds, plus the inversion to a probability.

</details>

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Derive the pseudo-residual for squared-error loss $L(y,f)=\tfrac12(y-f)^2$ and explain why gradient boosting with this loss amounts to repeatedly fitting a tree to the current residuals.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $-\partial L/\partial f$ at $f=f_{m-1}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$-\partial L/\partial f=-\partial[\tfrac12(y-f)^2]/\partial f=(y-f)$, so the pseudo-residual is $\tilde r_{im}=y_i-f_{m-1}(x_i)$, the ordinary residual. Gradient boosting under squared error therefore fits each new tree by least squares to the residuals left by the current ensemble, then adds (a shrunken multiple of) it — the LS_Boost procedure. Each round explains a piece of the residual, so the ensemble fits the response progressively. **Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that one round of forward stagewise fitting under exponential loss reproduces the AdaBoost weight update. Fix $f_{m-1}$, set $w_i^{(m)}=e^{-y_i f_{m-1}(x_i)}$, and minimise $\sum_i w_i^{(m)}e^{-\beta y_i G(x_i)}$ over $\beta>0$ and $G\in\mathcal G$. Recover $G_m$ as the weighted-error minimiser, $\beta_m=\tfrac12\log\frac{1-\mathrm{err}_m}{\mathrm{err}_m}$, and the update $w_i^{(m+1)}\propto w_i^{(m)}e^{-\beta_m y_i G_m(x_i)}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Split $\sum_i w_i^{(m)}e^{-\beta y_i G(x_i)}$ into correctly- and incorrectly-classified points, then write it as $(e^{\beta}-e^{-\beta})\sum_i w_i^{(m)}\mathbf 1\{y_i\neq G(x_i)\}+e^{-\beta}\sum_i w_i^{(m)}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $y_iG(x_i)\in\{\pm 1\}$, $e^{-\beta y_iG(x_i)}=e^{-\beta}$ on correct points and $e^{\beta}$ on wrong ones, so
$\sum_i w_i^{(m)}e^{-\beta y_iG(x_i)}=e^{-\beta}\sum_i w_i^{(m)}+(e^{\beta}-e^{-\beta})\sum_i w_i^{(m)}\mathbf 1\{y_i\neq G(x_i)\}.$
For fixed $\beta>0$ the factor $e^{\beta}-e^{-\beta}>0$, so the minimising $G$ is the one minimising the weighted error $\sum_i w_i^{(m)}\mathbf 1\{y_i\neq G(x_i)\}$ — that is $G_m$. With $\mathrm{err}_m$ the normalised weighted error of $G_m$, minimising over $\beta$ gives $\beta_m=\tfrac12\log\frac{1-\mathrm{err}_m}{\mathrm{err}_m}$ exactly as in Exercise 3. Then $f_m=f_{m-1}+\beta_m G_m$ gives $w_i^{(m+1)}=e^{-y_i f_m(x_i)}=w_i^{(m)}e^{-\beta_m y_i G_m(x_i)}$, the AdaBoost update (up to the normalising constant). Rubric: full credit for the correct/wrong split, the identification of $G_m$ as the weighted-error minimiser, the $\beta_m$ formula, and the recovered weight recursion.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Explain, using the bias-variance picture, why boosting and bagging reduce error through different mechanisms, and why this makes deep trees natural base learners for bagging but shallow trees (stumps) natural for boosting.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Bagging averages high-variance, low-bias estimators; boosting adds high-bias, low-variance estimators sequentially.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Bagging averages many trees grown independently on bootstrap samples; averaging leaves bias unchanged but cuts the variance of the unstable, low-bias deep trees, so deep (high-variance) trees are the right base learners — there is variance to remove. Boosting instead builds an additive model in which each term corrects the residual error of the current ensemble; the ensemble's bias falls as terms accumulate, while each term must be low-variance so the sequential fit does not chase noise — hence shallow trees or stumps, which are high-bias but stable. Deep trees in boosting overfit quickly because each already-low-bias term adds variance the procedure cannot average away. Rubric: full credit for assigning variance reduction to bagging and bias reduction to boosting, and for tying tree depth to the bias-variance profile each mechanism needs.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

AdaBoost is one algorithm read two ways: as reweight-and-vote and as coordinate-wise descent on exponential loss. The theory beyond the training-error bound concerns what that descent estimates, why it generalises beyond the point of zero training error, and how the same construction extends from one loss to all differentiable losses.

**Theorem 1 (AdaBoost is forward stagewise additive modelling under exponential loss).** With $L(y,f)=e^{-yf}$, the forward stagewise update $(\beta_m,G_m)=\arg\min_{\beta,G}\sum_i e^{-y_i(f_{m-1}(x_i)+\beta G(x_i))}$ has solution: $G_m$ is the minimiser of the weighted misclassification error with weights $w_i^{(m)}=e^{-y_i f_{m-1}(x_i)}$, and $\beta_m=\tfrac12\log\frac{1-\mathrm{err}_m}{\mathrm{err}_m}$, after which the weights update multiplicatively as $w_i^{(m+1)}=w_i^{(m)}e^{-\beta_m y_i G_m(x_i)}$ [ref: TODO_REF Hastie 2009]. Thus the AdaBoost weights are the exponential-loss residual weights, and the AdaBoost vote is the stagewise-optimal coefficient. AdaBoost performs no explicit reweighting "by design"; the reweighting *is* the gradient of exponential loss.

**Theorem 2 (population minimiser and the log-odds).** The minimiser of the population exponential risk is $f^\*(x)=\arg\min_f \mathbb E[e^{-Yf(X)}\mid X=x]=\tfrac12\log\frac{\Pr(Y=1\mid x)}{\Pr(Y=-1\mid x)}$, half the log-odds [ref: TODO_REF Hastie 2009]. Inverting, $\Pr(Y=1\mid x)=1/(1+e^{-2f^\*(x)})$, so the boosted score, after monotone transformation, is an estimate of the class-probability log-odds; the same minimiser is shared by the binomial deviance $\log(1+e^{-2yf})$, which is why deviance is the robust drop-in replacement that penalises gross misclassification only linearly rather than exponentially. The sign of $f^\*$ is the Bayes classifier, so exponential loss is classification-calibrated (Fisher-consistent).

**Theorem 3 (gradient boosting as steepest descent in function space).** Treat $\mathcal R(f)=\sum_i L(y_i,f(x_i))$ as a function of the vector $\mathbf f=(f(x_1),\dots,f(x_n))$. The unconstrained steepest-descent step is $\mathbf f_m=\mathbf f_{m-1}-\rho_m\nabla\mathcal R(\mathbf f_{m-1})$, with gradient components $-\tilde r_{im}=\partial L(y_i,f(x_i))/\partial f(x_i)$. Restricting the step to the span of a base-learner class, gradient boosting fits $h_m=\arg\min_{h\in\mathcal H}\sum_i(\tilde r_{im}-h(x_i))^2$ — the least-squares projection of the negative gradient onto $\mathcal H$ — and sets $f_m=f_{m-1}+\nu\rho_m h_m$ with line search $\rho_m$ and shrinkage $\nu$ [ref: TODO_REF Friedman 2001]. This is functional gradient descent (cross-ref the first-order optimisation theory of 44.06): the only departures from textbook steepest descent are that the gradient lives in $\mathbb R^n$ over the training inputs and the step is forced into $\mathcal H$ so it generalises off-sample.

**Theorem 4 (margin distribution and resistance to overfitting).** Define the normalised margin of the voting classifier as $\mathrm{margin}(x,y)=y\sum_m\alpha_m G_m(x)/\sum_m\alpha_m\in[-1,1]$. For voting classifiers, with probability $1-\delta$ over the sample, for all $\theta>0$ simultaneously,
$$
\Pr_{\mathcal D}[\,y f(x)\le 0\,]\;\le\;\widehat{\Pr}_S[\,\mathrm{margin}(x,y)\le\theta\,]\;+\;O\!\Big(\frac{1}{\sqrt n}\sqrt{\frac{d}{\theta^2}}\Big),
$$
where $d$ is a complexity measure (e.g. the VC dimension of $\mathcal G$); the bound does not depend on the number of rounds $M$ [ref: TODO_REF Schapire 1998]. Because AdaBoost continues to increase the margins of training points after the training error reaches zero, the right-hand side keeps decreasing, which is the margin-theoretic account of why test error often falls past the zero-training-error point. The account is partial: boosting can overfit when labels are noisy or base learners too rich, and the margin bound is loose; shrinkage, shallow trees, subsampling, and early stopping remain necessary regularisers.

**Synthesis.** The central insight is that AdaBoost is exponential-loss minimisation by coordinate descent over the dictionary of weak learners, and every operational feature follows from that one identification. The vote $\alpha_m=\tfrac12\log\frac{1-\mathrm{err}_m}{\mathrm{err}_m}$ is the stagewise-optimal step (Theorem 1), the reweighting is the loss gradient, and the training-error theorem is just the statement that greedy descent on $\prod_m Z_m$ drives the surrogate — and hence the misclassification rate it dominates — to zero. Putting these together with the population analysis, minimising exponential risk pins the boosted score to half the log-odds (Theorem 2), so boosting is not merely a classifier but a probability estimator, and this is exactly the fact that generalises AdaBoost to gradient boosting: once boosting is descent on a loss, any differentiable loss will do, and the pseudo-residual fit of Theorem 3 is the foundational reason a single framework covers regression, robust regression, and classification. The bridge to generalisation is the margin distribution (Theorem 4): the training-error bound controls only the sign of the margin, while the margin theory controls its spread, and boosting's empirical tendency to keep enlarging margins after zero training error is what the round-independent bound formalises. This is dual to bagging's variance reduction proved for trees in [45.08.04]: bagging averages independent high-variance, low-bias trees to cut variance, whereas boosting adds dependent high-bias, low-variance stumps to cut bias, and the two occupy opposite corners of the bias-variance plane while sharing trees as their base object.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (telescoping of the weights; the empirical exponential loss equals $\prod_m Z_m$).** *With $w_i^{(1)}=1/n$ and the AdaBoost update renormalised by $Z_m=\sum_i w_i^{(m)}e^{-\alpha_m y_i G_m(x_i)}$, one has $w_i^{(M+1)}=\frac1n e^{-y_i f_M(x_i)}/\prod_{m=1}^M Z_m$, hence $\frac1n\sum_i e^{-y_i f_M(x_i)}=\prod_{m=1}^M Z_m$.*

*Proof.* By induction on $M$. At $M=0$, $w_i^{(1)}=1/n=\tfrac1n e^{-y_i f_0(x_i)}$ with empty product $=1$ and $f_0\equiv 0$. Assume $w_i^{(m)}=\tfrac1n e^{-y_i f_{m-1}(x_i)}/\prod_{k<m}Z_k$. The renormalised update is $w_i^{(m+1)}=w_i^{(m)}e^{-\alpha_m y_i G_m(x_i)}/Z_m$, and substituting the inductive hypothesis with $f_m=f_{m-1}+\alpha_m G_m$ gives $w_i^{(m+1)}=\tfrac1n e^{-y_i f_m(x_i)}/\prod_{k\le m}Z_k$. Summing over $i$ and using $\sum_i w_i^{(M+1)}=1$ (the weights are renormalised to a probability vector) yields $\sum_i \tfrac1n e^{-y_i f_M(x_i)}=\prod_{m\le M}Z_m$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (per-round factor and the exponential bound).** *Each $Z_m=2\sqrt{\mathrm{err}_m(1-\mathrm{err}_m)}=\sqrt{1-4\gamma_m^2}\le e^{-2\gamma_m^2}$ for $\mathrm{err}_m=\tfrac12-\gamma_m$, so $\prod_m Z_m\le e^{-2\sum_m\gamma_m^2}$.*

*Proof.* With normalised round weights, $Z_m=(1-\mathrm{err}_m)e^{-\alpha_m}+\mathrm{err}_m e^{\alpha_m}$; substituting $\alpha_m=\tfrac12\log\frac{1-\mathrm{err}_m}{\mathrm{err}_m}$ gives $Z_m=2\sqrt{\mathrm{err}_m(1-\mathrm{err}_m)}$ (the back-substitution of Exercise 3). Then $4\,\mathrm{err}_m(1-\mathrm{err}_m)=4(\tfrac12-\gamma_m)(\tfrac12+\gamma_m)=1-4\gamma_m^2$, so $Z_m=\sqrt{1-4\gamma_m^2}$. The inequality $1-u\le e^{-u}$ with $u=4\gamma_m^2$ gives $1-4\gamma_m^2\le e^{-4\gamma_m^2}$, whence $Z_m\le e^{-2\gamma_m^2}$, and the product bound follows. Combined with Proposition 1 and the domination $\mathbf 1\{y_i\neq\mathrm{sign}\,f_M(x_i)\}\le e^{-y_i f_M(x_i)}$, the training error is at most $\prod_m Z_m\le e^{-2\sum_m\gamma_m^2}$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (exponential-loss population minimiser).** *For $L(y,f)=e^{-yf}$ with $Y\in\{-1,+1\}$ and $p(x)=\Pr(Y=1\mid x)$, the pointwise minimiser of $\mathbb E[e^{-Yf(X)}\mid X=x]$ is $f^\*(x)=\tfrac12\log\frac{p(x)}{1-p(x)}$.*

*Proof.* Conditioning on $X=x$, $\mathbb E[e^{-Yf}\mid x]=p(x)e^{-f}+(1-p(x))e^{f}=:\phi(f)$. Then $\phi'(f)=-p(x)e^{-f}+(1-p(x))e^{f}$, vanishing where $e^{2f}=p(x)/(1-p(x))$, i.e. $f^\*(x)=\tfrac12\log\frac{p(x)}{1-p(x)}$; $\phi''(f)=p(x)e^{-f}+(1-p(x))e^{f}>0$ confirms the global minimum (the objective is strictly convex in $f$). Inverting, $\Pr(Y=1\mid x)=e^{2f^\*}/(1+e^{2f^\*})=1/(1+e^{-2f^\*})$. The Bayes rule $\mathrm{sign}(p(x)-\tfrac12)$ coincides with $\mathrm{sign}\,f^\*(x)$, so exponential loss is classification-calibrated. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (gradient-boosting step is the least-squares projection of the negative gradient).** *Let $\mathcal R(\mathbf f)=\sum_i L(y_i,f(x_i))$ and $\tilde r_{im}=-\partial_{f(x_i)}L(y_i,f(x_i))|_{f_{m-1}}$. The base learner $h_m=\arg\min_{h\in\mathcal H}\sum_i (\tilde r_{im}-h(x_i))^2$ is the element of $\mathcal H$ most aligned with the negative gradient in the empirical inner product, and a positive step along it decreases $\mathcal R$ to first order whenever $h_m\not\perp\nabla\mathcal R$.*

*Proof.* The negative gradient of $\mathcal R$ at $\mathbf f_{m-1}$ has components $\tilde r_{im}$. For a step $\mathbf f_{m-1}+\rho\,\mathbf h$ with $\mathbf h=(h(x_1),\dots,h(x_n))$, a first-order expansion gives $\mathcal R(\mathbf f_{m-1}+\rho\mathbf h)=\mathcal R(\mathbf f_{m-1})-\rho\langle \tilde{\mathbf r}_m,\mathbf h\rangle+o(\rho)$, where $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ is the Euclidean inner product over the $n$ training points. Decreasing $\mathcal R$ at first order requires $\langle \tilde{\mathbf r}_m,\mathbf h\rangle>0$. Minimising $\sum_i(\tilde r_{im}-h(x_i))^2=\|\tilde{\mathbf r}_m\|^2-2\langle\tilde{\mathbf r}_m,\mathbf h\rangle+\|\mathbf h\|^2$ over $\mathcal H$ maximises $\langle\tilde{\mathbf r}_m,\mathbf h\rangle$ relative to $\|\mathbf h\|^2$, i.e. selects the base learner best aligned with the negative gradient; the orthogonality conditions of the least-squares fit make $\mathbf h_m$ the projection of $\tilde{\mathbf r}_m$ onto $\mathcal H$. So long as that projection is non-zero, $\langle\tilde{\mathbf r}_m,\mathbf h_m\rangle=\|\mathbf h_m\|^2>0$ and the line-searched step $\rho_m>0$ strictly decreases $\mathcal R$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The base learners boosting combines are the trees of [45.08.04]: boosting reweights or refits shallow trees (stumps and small trees at the low-$|T|$ end of the cost-complexity chain), and the instability and high-variance profile established there is precisely the property that makes deep trees wrong for boosting and right for bagging, so this unit specialises the tree object of the prior unit into a sequential, bias-reducing ensemble.

- Boosting reduces bias where bagging and random forests reduce variance, the two facing pages of the bias-variance decomposition: the correlated-average variance formula proved for trees in [45.08.04] explains bagging's gain, while the additive forward-stagewise construction here explains boosting's complementary attack on bias, so the same decomposition organises both ensembles and assigns each its corner of the bias-variance plane.

- The margin-distribution bound of Theorem 4 is the boosting instance of the margin generalisation theory of [45.07.06]: the elementary domination $\mathbf 1\{yf\le 0\}\le e^{-yf}$ used in the training-error proof is the seed of the uniform margin bounds there, and the boosting story — test error falling past zero training error — is the empirical phenomenon those bounds were built to explain.

- Gradient boosting is functional gradient descent, an instance of the first-order optimisation methods of [44.06]: the empirical risk $\mathcal R(\mathbf f)$ is descended by steepest steps projected onto a base-learner class, with the learning rate $\nu$ playing the role of a fixed step size and stochastic subsampling mirroring the mini-batching of stochastic gradient methods, so the convergence and step-size intuition from convex optimisation transfers directly to the boosting iteration.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Boosting began as a question in computational learning theory: **Kearns and Valiant (1989)** asked whether a "weak" PAC-learning algorithm — one only slightly better than chance — could always be boosted into a "strong" one with arbitrarily small error. **Schapire (1990)** answered yes with the first boosting algorithm, and **Freund (1995)** simplified it to boost-by-majority. The practical breakthrough was **Freund and Schapire's AdaBoost (1995; full version 1997)** [ref: TODO_REF Freund 1997], which adapted to the weak learners' errors without knowing their edge in advance and came with the exponential training-error bound proved above; it won the 2003 Gödel Prize.

The statistical recasting followed. **Friedman, Hastie, and Tibshirani (2000)** identified AdaBoost as forward stagewise additive modelling under exponential loss with the half-log-odds population minimiser, moving boosting from a margin-game in learning theory into the regression-modelling framework of *The Elements of Statistical Learning* [ref: TODO_REF Hastie 2009]. **Friedman (2001)** generalised the construction to arbitrary differentiable losses as a gradient boosting machine [ref: TODO_REF Friedman 2001], the lineage that runs through MART to the regularised, second-order, sparsity-aware implementations of XGBoost (Chen and Guestrin 2016), LightGBM, and CatBoost that dominate tabular-data competitions. The apparent paradox that AdaBoost keeps improving test error after training error reaches zero prompted the margin theory of **Schapire, Freund, Bartlett, and Lee (1998)** [ref: TODO_REF Schapire 1998] and a long debate, including **Breiman's (1999)** counterexamples, over whether margins fully explain the phenomenon; the resolution is that boosting enlarges the margin distribution but is not immune to overfitting, which is why shrinkage and subsampling became standard.
