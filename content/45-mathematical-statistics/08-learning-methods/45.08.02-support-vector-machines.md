## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine two crowds standing on a field, one wearing red, one wearing blue, and your job is to paint a single straight line that keeps them apart. If the crowds are well separated, many lines work. Which one should you pick? The support vector machine picks the line that leaves the widest empty street between the two crowds. The street is centred on the line, and its width is the distance from the line to the nearest person on either side.

Why prefer the widest street? Because a wide buffer is forgiving. New people will arrive, and a line jammed right up against the existing crowd will misclassify a newcomer who stands slightly inside the gap. A line with a fat margin of safety tolerates that wobble. Maximizing the margin is choosing the most cautious boundary the data allow.

The people who end up touching the edge of the street do all the work. They are the support vectors. Move someone deep inside the red crowd and the line does not budge; move a support vector and the line shifts. The whole boundary is decided by a handful of borderline cases, not by the comfortable majority.

Real crowds overlap, so a perfectly empty street is often impossible. The soft-margin version lets a few people stand inside the street, or even on the wrong side, and charges a penalty for each one. A single dial trades street width against the total penalty: open the street wide and accept some intruders, or keep it narrow and clean. And by measuring closeness with a kernel instead of plain straight-line distance, the same idea bends the boundary into curves.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take four points on a line. Two red points sit at positions $-3$ and $-1$, labelled $-1$. Two blue points sit at $+1$ and $+3$, labelled $+1$. We want the threshold that best separates red from blue.

Any threshold between $-1$ and $+1$ separates them. The widest-street choice is the midpoint, $0$: the nearest red point is at $-1$ and the nearest blue point at $+1$, each a distance $1$ from the boundary, so the street runs from $-1$ to $+1$ and has half-width $1$.

Now read off the support vectors. The points at $-1$ and $+1$ touch the edge of the street; they are the support vectors. The points at $-3$ and $+3$ sit comfortably outside and play no role. Slide the point at $-3$ to $-5$ and the boundary stays at $0$. Slide the support vector at $-1$ to $-0.5$ and the street shrinks: the nearest red point is now $0.5$ away, so the midpoint between $-0.5$ and $+1$ moves to $+0.25$ and the half-width drops to $0.75$.

To put numbers on the rule, write the boundary as $w x + b = 0$. Centring at $0$ means $b = 0$. We scale $w$ so the support vectors satisfy $y(wx + b) = 1$: at $x = 1$, $y = +1$, we need $w \cdot 1 = 1$, so $w = 1$. Check the red support vector: at $x = -1$, $y = -1$, the value is $(-1)(1 \cdot (-1)) = 1$. Both edges sit at value $1$, as required.

What this tells us: the margin half-width equals $1/w = 1$, so making $w$ small makes the street wide. The whole rule rests on the two border points, and the two interior points are spectators.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let the data be $(x_1, y_1), \dots, (x_n, y_n)$ with $x_i \in \mathbb R^d$ and labels $y_i \in \{-1, +1\}$. A **separating hyperplane** is the set $\{x : \langle w, x\rangle + b = 0\}$ with normal $w \in \mathbb R^d$ and offset $b \in \mathbb R$; the classifier is $x \mapsto \operatorname{sign}(\langle w, x\rangle + b)$. The **functional margin** of point $i$ is $y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b)$ and its **geometric margin** is that quantity divided by $\lVert w\rVert$, the signed Euclidean distance from $x_i$ to the hyperplane. The data are **linearly separable** if some $(w, b)$ gives every point a positive functional margin.

**Definition (hard-margin SVM).** When the data are separable, the maximum-margin hyperplane is the minimizer of the convex quadratic program

$$
\min_{w, b}\ \tfrac12 \lVert w\rVert^2 \quad \text{subject to}\quad y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b) \ge 1,\ \ i = 1, \dots, n.
$$

The scale of $(w, b)$ is fixed by the canonical normalization $\min_i y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b) = 1$, under which the geometric margin equals $1/\lVert w\rVert$; minimizing $\lVert w\rVert^2$ therefore maximizes the margin. A **support vector** is a point with $y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b) = 1$, lying exactly on the margin boundary.

**Definition (soft-margin SVM).** For non-separable data, introduce **slack variables** $\xi_i \ge 0$ measuring margin violation and a penalty parameter $C > 0$:

$$
\min_{w, b, \xi}\ \tfrac12\lVert w\rVert^2 + C\sum_{i=1}^n \xi_i \quad \text{subject to}\quad y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b) \ge 1 - \xi_i,\ \ \xi_i \ge 0.
$$

At the optimum $\xi_i = [1 - y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b)]_+$, the **hinge loss** $[\,t\,]_+ = \max(0, t)$ of the margin, so the soft-margin program is the **regularized empirical risk**

$$
\min_{w, b}\ \sum_{i=1}^n \big[1 - y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b)\big]_+ + \frac{1}{2C}\lVert w\rVert^2,
$$

with $\lambda = 1/(2C)$ playing the regularization role. As $C \to \infty$ the penalty on violations grows without bound and the soft-margin solution returns the hard-margin one whenever the data are separable.

The **Lagrange dual** (derived below) replaces the inputs by their inner products $\langle x_i, x_j\rangle$, which is the opening for the **kernel trick**: substituting a positive-definite kernel $K(x_i, x_j) = \langle\Phi(x_i), \Phi(x_j)\rangle$ for the dot product runs the entire machine in the feature space of $K$ without ever forming $\Phi$. Written as a function-fitting problem with $f(x) = \langle w, \Phi(x)\rangle + b$, the soft-margin SVM is

$$
\min_{f \in \mathcal H_K,\, b}\ \sum_{i=1}^n [1 - y_i f(x_i)]_+ + \lambda\lVert f\rVert_{\mathcal H_K}^2,
$$

the **hinge-loss instance of regularized RKHS risk** of [45.08.01], to which the representer theorem applies.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *The margin is geometric, not functional.* The functional margin $y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b)$ scales with $\lVert w\rVert$ and can be inflated by rescaling $(w, b)$; only the geometric margin $1/\lVert w\rVert$ is meaningful, which is why the canonical normalization is imposed.
- *Not every point with $\alpha_i > 0$ sits on the margin.* In the soft-margin dual the multipliers obey $0 \le \alpha_i \le C$; points strictly inside the margin or misclassified hit the upper bound $\alpha_i = C$ and lie off the margin boundary, while the bias-recovering "free" support vectors have $0 < \alpha_i < C$ and sit exactly on it.
- *Maximizing the margin is not minimizing training error.* On non-separable data a tiny $C$ can prefer a wide margin with many violations to a narrow margin with none; the SVM minimizes regularized hinge risk, not the count of mistakes.
- *The hinge loss is not the $0$-$1$ loss.* It is a convex upper bound that is exactly $0$ once the margin exceeds $1$ and grows linearly below; this convexity is what makes the program solvable, and the gap to the $0$-$1$ loss is controlled by the calibration theory rather than being zero.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (the SVM dual and the support-vector characterization).** *The soft-margin primal*
$$
\min_{w, b, \xi}\ \tfrac12\lVert w\rVert^2 + C\sum_i \xi_i \quad\text{s.t.}\quad y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b) \ge 1 - \xi_i,\ \xi_i \ge 0
$$
*has Lagrange dual*
$$
\max_{\alpha}\ \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i - \tfrac12\sum_{i,j} \alpha_i \alpha_j y_i y_j \langle x_i, x_j\rangle \quad\text{s.t.}\quad 0 \le \alpha_i \le C,\ \ \sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0.
$$
*Strong duality holds, the primal optimum is $w = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i x_i$, and the KKT complementary-slackness conditions give the trichotomy: $\alpha_i = 0 \Rightarrow y_i f(x_i) \ge 1$ (correctly outside the margin); $0 < \alpha_i < C \Rightarrow y_i f(x_i) = 1$ (on the margin); $\alpha_i = C \Rightarrow y_i f(x_i) \le 1$ (inside the margin or misclassified). The support vectors are exactly the points with $\alpha_i > 0$.* The derivation follows Cortes & Vapnik [ref: TODO_REF Cortes 1995] and Schölkopf & Smola [ref: TODO_REF Schölkopf 2002] Ch. 7.

*Proof.* Introduce multipliers $\alpha_i \ge 0$ for the margin constraints $1 - \xi_i - y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b) \le 0$ and $\mu_i \ge 0$ for $-\xi_i \le 0$. The Lagrangian is
$$
L = \tfrac12\lVert w\rVert^2 + C\sum_i \xi_i + \sum_i \alpha_i\big(1 - \xi_i - y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b)\big) - \sum_i \mu_i \xi_i.
$$
The problem is convex with affine constraints, so Slater's condition holds and strong duality with KKT optimality follows from [44.02.01] and [44.02.02]. Stationarity in the primal variables:
$$
\nabla_w L = w - \sum_i \alpha_i y_i x_i = 0 \ \Rightarrow\ w = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i x_i, \qquad
\frac{\partial L}{\partial b} = -\sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0,
$$
$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial \xi_i} = C - \alpha_i - \mu_i = 0 \ \Rightarrow\ \mu_i = C - \alpha_i.
$$
Substituting $w = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i x_i$ back into $L$ and using $\sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0$ to kill the $b$-term and $\mu_i = C - \alpha_i \ge 0$ to convert the $\xi$-terms, the $\xi$ contributions cancel ($C\xi_i - \alpha_i\xi_i - \mu_i\xi_i = 0$), leaving the dual objective
$$
g(\alpha) = \sum_i \alpha_i - \tfrac12\sum_{i,j}\alpha_i\alpha_j y_i y_j\langle x_i, x_j\rangle,
$$
because $\tfrac12\lVert w\rVert^2 = \tfrac12\sum_{i,j}\alpha_i\alpha_j y_i y_j\langle x_i, x_j\rangle$ and the linear cross-term doubles it with the opposite sign. The constraint $\mu_i \ge 0$ becomes $\alpha_i \le C$, giving the box $0 \le \alpha_i \le C$ together with $\sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0$.

For the trichotomy, complementary slackness [44.02.02] reads $\alpha_i\big(1 - \xi_i - y_i f(x_i)\big) = 0$ and $\mu_i\xi_i = (C - \alpha_i)\xi_i = 0$, with $f(x) = \langle w, x\rangle + b$. If $\alpha_i = 0$ then $\mu_i = C > 0$ forces $\xi_i = 0$, and the margin constraint gives $y_i f(x_i) \ge 1$. If $0 < \alpha_i < C$ then $\mu_i > 0$ forces $\xi_i = 0$, and $\alpha_i > 0$ forces $y_i f(x_i) = 1 - \xi_i = 1$: the point lies on the margin. If $\alpha_i = C$ then $\mu_i = 0$ permits $\xi_i \ge 0$, and $\alpha_i > 0$ forces $y_i f(x_i) = 1 - \xi_i \le 1$: inside the margin or misclassified. Points with $\alpha_i = 0$ drop out of $w$, so the expansion $w = \sum_{i : \alpha_i > 0}\alpha_i y_i x_i$ runs only over support vectors. $\square$

**Bridge.** This dual is the foundational reason the kernel trick works: every input enters only through the inner product $\langle x_i, x_j\rangle$, so replacing it by $K(x_i, x_j)$ nonlinearizes the classifier without touching the optimization, and this is exactly the [45.08.01] picture with the hinge loss in place of squared error. The result builds toward the kernel SVM and the support-vector regression of the Advanced results, and appears again in every margin-based generalization bound, where the sparse support set controls capacity. The dual is dual to the primal margin program in the precise sense of [44.02.01]: the primal unknown is the feature-space normal $w$, the dual unknown is the data-space coefficient vector $\alpha$, and the stationarity identity $w = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i x_i$ is the same data-space-versus-weight-space correspondence that the representer theorem of [45.08.01] guarantees in general. Putting these together, complementary slackness of [44.02.02] is the central insight that produces sparsity: the box constraint $0 \le \alpha_i \le C$ and the slackness equations sort the data into spectators, margin vectors, and bound vectors, and this generalises the hard-margin case as the limit $C \to \infty$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that under the canonical normalization $\min_i y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b) = 1$, the geometric margin (distance from the hyperplane to the nearest point) equals $1/\lVert w\rVert$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The signed distance from a point $x$ to $\{x : \langle w, x\rangle + b = 0\}$ is $(\langle w, x\rangle + b)/\lVert w\rVert$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The distance from $x_i$ to the hyperplane is $|\langle w, x_i\rangle + b|/\lVert w\rVert$, and for a correctly classified point this equals $y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b)/\lVert w\rVert$. The nearest point has $y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b) = \min_i y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b) = 1$ by the normalization, so its distance is $1/\lVert w\rVert$. Hence the margin half-width is $1/\lVert w\rVert$, and the full street width is $2/\lVert w\rVert$; minimizing $\tfrac12\lVert w\rVert^2$ maximizes it.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

Two points in $\mathbb R^2$: $x_1 = (1, 0)$ with $y_1 = +1$ and $x_2 = (-1, 0)$ with $y_2 = -1$. For the hard-margin SVM, the optimal normal is $w = (1, 0)$ with $b = 0$. Compute the geometric margin half-width.

Use $1/\lVert w\rVert$, and check both points satisfy $y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b) = 1$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the bias $b$ from a free support vector. Given the dual solution $\alpha$ and $w = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i x_i$, show that for any $k$ with $0 < \alpha_k < C$, $b = y_k - \sum_i \alpha_i y_i \langle x_i, x_k\rangle$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A free support vector satisfies $y_k f(x_k) = 1$ exactly; solve for $b$ and use $y_k^2 = 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $0 < \alpha_k < C$ the trichotomy gives $y_k f(x_k) = 1$, i.e. $y_k(\langle w, x_k\rangle + b) = 1$. Multiply by $y_k$ and use $y_k^2 = 1$: $\langle w, x_k\rangle + b = y_k$, so $b = y_k - \langle w, x_k\rangle = y_k - \sum_i \alpha_i y_i\langle x_i, x_k\rangle$. Numerically one averages this over all free support vectors for stability. Bound vectors ($\alpha_k = C$) cannot be used because their margin is $\le 1$, not $= 1$.

</details>

</aside>

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In feature space $w = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i \Phi(x_i)$, so $f(x) = \langle w, \Phi(x)\rangle + b = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i\langle\Phi(x_i), \Phi(x)\rangle + b = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i K(x_i, x) + b$. The sum runs only over support vectors ($\alpha_i > 0$), and $b = y_k - \sum_i \alpha_i y_i K(x_i, x_k)$ for any free support vector $k$. The classifier $\operatorname{sign}(f(x))$ never references $\Phi$, only $K$: this is the kernel SVM. The dual objective likewise becomes $\sum_i \alpha_i - \tfrac12\sum_{ij}\alpha_i\alpha_j y_i y_j K(x_i, x_j)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show the dual objective is concave, so the dual is a concave maximization (equivalently a convex QP). Identify the matrix whose positive semidefiniteness certifies this.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write the quadratic term as $-\tfrac12 \alpha^\top Q \alpha$ with $Q_{ij} = y_i y_j K(x_i, x_j)$, and relate $Q$ to the Gram matrix.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The objective is $\mathbf 1^\top\alpha - \tfrac12\alpha^\top Q\alpha$ with $Q_{ij} = y_i y_j K(x_i, x_j)$. Write $Q = D\mathbf K D$ where $\mathbf K_{ij} = K(x_i, x_j)$ is the Gram matrix and $D = \operatorname{diag}(y)$. Since $K$ is positive-definite, $\mathbf K \succeq 0$, and $D\mathbf K D \succeq 0$ because $\alpha^\top D\mathbf K D\alpha = (D\alpha)^\top\mathbf K(D\alpha) \ge 0$. So $Q \succeq 0$, the quadratic form $-\tfrac12\alpha^\top Q\alpha$ is concave, and the linear term keeps it concave; the feasible set (a box intersected with one hyperplane) is convex, so the dual is a convex program with a global maximizer.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the soft-margin SVM is the representer-theorem instance with hinge loss, i.e. that the optimal $f$ minimizing $\sum_i [1 - y_i f(x_i)]_+ + \lambda\lVert f\rVert_{\mathcal H_K}^2$ over $\mathcal H_K$ has the form $f(x) = \sum_i \beta_i K(x_i, x)$, and relate $\beta_i$ to the dual multipliers.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the representer theorem of [45.08.01] with $L$ the summed hinge loss and $\Omega(t) = \lambda t^2$; then compare with $w = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i \Phi(x_i)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The objective is $L(f(x_1), \dots, f(x_n)) + \Omega(\lVert f\rVert)$ with $L(u_1, \dots, u_n) = \sum_i [1 - y_i u_i]_+$ a function of the fitted values and $\Omega(t) = \lambda t^2$ strictly increasing. The representer theorem of [45.08.01] applies verbatim: any minimizer lies in $\operatorname{span}\{K_{x_i}\}$, so $f(x) = \sum_i \beta_i K(x_i, x)$. Matching to the SVM expansion $f(x) = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i K(x_i, x) + b$ identifies $\beta_i = \alpha_i y_i$ (the bias $b$ corresponds to an unpenalized constant feature, handled by the affine offset rather than the RKHS norm). The dual box $0 \le \alpha_i \le C$ with $\lambda = 1/(2C)$ is the finite-dimensional reduction of the infinite-dimensional variational problem.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Explain in what sense the hinge loss is a convex surrogate for the $0$-$1$ loss, and why convexity of the surrogate is essential for the SVM to be a tractable program.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare $[1 - yf]_+$ with the indicator $\mathbf 1[yf < 0]$ as functions of the margin $yf$; consider what minimizing each over a linear family costs.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The $0$-$1$ loss $\mathbf 1[yf(x) < 0]$ counts misclassifications; as a function of the margin $m = yf(x)$ it is the discontinuous step $\mathbf 1[m < 0]$, which is non-convex and flat almost everywhere, so minimizing the empirical $0$-$1$ risk over a parametric family is NP-hard and has no usable gradient. The hinge loss $[1 - m]_+$ is the convex upper bound that pins to $0$ for $m \ge 1$ and rises linearly for $m < 1$: it dominates the (shifted) $0$-$1$ loss, is convex in $m$ and hence in $(w, b)$ through the affine $f$, and its sub-gradient is informative everywhere. Convexity makes the regularized hinge risk a convex program with a unique global minimum reachable by quadratic programming or sub-gradient methods, replacing an intractable combinatorial search by a tractable convex one. The price is that the hinge minimizer is not the $0$-$1$ minimizer; consistency (next section) controls that gap.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The SVM is the hinge-loss member of the regularized-RKHS-risk family of [45.08.01]. Writing $f(x) = \langle w, \Phi(x)\rangle + b$ with $\Phi$ the feature map of $K$, the soft-margin program is $\min_{f, b}\sum_i[1 - y_i f(x_i)]_+ + \lambda\lVert f\rVert_{\mathcal H_K}^2$ with $\lambda = 1/(2C)$, and the representer theorem forces $f = \sum_i \beta_i K_{x_i}$ with $\beta_i = \alpha_i y_i$ [ref: TODO_REF Steinwart 2008]. Squared error gives kernel ridge regression with dense coefficients; the hinge loss gives the SVM with sparse coefficients, the sparsity being the analytic signature of the loss's flat region $[1 - m]_+ = 0$ for $m \ge 1$, which sends the complementary-slackness multiplier to zero for every comfortably-classified point. The two methods share the projection-onto-the-data-span engine and differ only in $L$, exactly the unification the Bridge of [45.08.01] advertises.

The hinge loss is a *classification-calibrated* (Fisher-consistent) surrogate, which is the precise statement that minimizing hinge risk drives the $0$-$1$ risk to its Bayes optimum [ref: TODO_REF Steinwart 2008]. For a margin loss $\phi$, define the conditional $\phi$-risk $C_\eta(m) = \eta\phi(m) + (1 - \eta)\phi(-m)$ at posterior $\eta = \mathbb P(y = +1 \mid x)$; $\phi$ is calibrated when, for every $\eta \ne 1/2$, the minimizer of $C_\eta$ has the sign of $2\eta - 1$, the Bayes decision. The hinge $\phi(m) = [1 - m]_+$ satisfies this: its conditional minimizer is $\operatorname{sign}(2\eta - 1)$ for $\eta \ne 1/2$, so the population SVM recovers the Bayes classifier, and the self-calibration inequality $R_{0\text{-}1}(f) - R^\star_{0\text{-}1} \le R_\phi(f) - R^\star_\phi$ bounds the excess $0$-$1$ risk by the excess hinge risk without a multiplicative loss. Unlike logistic loss, the hinge minimizer does not estimate $\eta$ itself, only its thresholded sign — the price the SVM pays for sparsity is the loss of calibrated probabilities.

Margin theory supplies the generalization guarantee that motivates margin maximization as structural risk minimization. Vapnik's gap-tolerant bound shows that separating hyperplanes of geometric margin $\Delta$ inside a ball of radius $R$ have VC dimension at most $\min(R^2/\Delta^2, d) + 1$, independent of the (possibly infinite) feature dimension $d$ when $R^2/\Delta^2$ is the binding term [ref: TODO_REF Vapnik 1998]. The Rademacher route is sharper and dimension-free: the empirical Rademacher complexity of the kernel ball $\{f \in \mathcal H_K : \lVert f\rVert \le B\}$ is $\hat{\mathfrak R}_n \le B\sqrt{\operatorname{tr}\mathbf K}/n = B\sqrt{\sum_i K(x_i, x_i)}/n$, and composing with the $1$-Lipschitz hinge loss via Talagrand's contraction lemma gives, with probability $1 - \delta$, a bound of the form $R_{0\text{-}1}(f) \le \widehat R_{\text{hinge}}(f) + 2\hat{\mathfrak R}_n + 3\sqrt{\log(2/\delta)/2n}$. The margin enters through $B \sim 1/\Delta$, so a large margin means small complexity means a tight bound — the generalization counterpart of the foundational regularization control developed in the high-dimensional and learning-theory units.

Support vector regression replaces classification by the $\varepsilon$-insensitive loss $|y - f(x)|_\varepsilon = \max(0, |y - f(x)| - \varepsilon)$, flat inside a tube of half-width $\varepsilon$ and linear outside [ref: TODO_REF Schölkopf 2002]. The primal $\min\tfrac12\lVert w\rVert^2 + C\sum_i(\xi_i + \xi_i^\ast)$ subject to $y_i - f(x_i) \le \varepsilon + \xi_i$, $f(x_i) - y_i \le \varepsilon + \xi_i^\ast$, $\xi_i, \xi_i^\ast \ge 0$ has the dual $\max -\tfrac12\sum_{ij}(\alpha_i - \alpha_i^\ast)(\alpha_j - \alpha_j^\ast)K(x_i, x_j) - \varepsilon\sum_i(\alpha_i + \alpha_i^\ast) + \sum_i y_i(\alpha_i - \alpha_i^\ast)$ over the box $0 \le \alpha_i, \alpha_i^\ast \le C$ with $\sum_i(\alpha_i - \alpha_i^\ast) = 0$, and $f(x) = \sum_i(\alpha_i - \alpha_i^\ast)K(x_i, x) + b$. The $\varepsilon$-tube is the regression analogue of the margin: points inside it are spectators ($\alpha_i = \alpha_i^\ast = 0$), and only the tube-violating points become support vectors, so sparsity persists. Solving these box-constrained QPs at scale is the role of decomposition methods; sequential minimal optimization optimizes the smallest admissible working set of two multipliers at a time, each step solving a one-dimensional problem analytically along the line cut out by the equality constraint $\sum_i\alpha_i y_i = 0$, with working sets chosen by maximal KKT violation [ref: TODO_REF Platt 1999], the optimization story continued in [44.02.02].

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason the support vector machine, the kernel trick, and convex duality cohere into one method is that the maximum-margin primal is a convex quadratic program whose Lagrangian dual of [44.02.01] expresses every input through inner products alone, so the kernel substitution $\langle x_i, x_j\rangle \mapsto K(x_i, x_j)$ nonlinearizes the classifier while the optimization stays convex — this is exactly the [45.08.01] regularized-RKHS-risk picture with the hinge loss in place of squared error. The central insight is that complementary slackness of [44.02.02] sorts the data into the support-vector trichotomy and forces sparsity, which is dual to the dense kernel-ridge solution and generalises the hard-margin classifier as the $C \to \infty$ limit. Putting these together, the representer theorem of [45.08.01] guarantees the finite expansion $f = \sum_i \alpha_i y_i K_{x_i}$, the dual produces its coefficients as box-constrained multipliers, and the KKT conditions identify which coefficients survive; the bridge is the kernel Gram matrix $\mathbf K$, whose positive-semidefiniteness makes the dual a concave maximization, whose entries are the only data the solver ever sees, and whose trace bounds the Rademacher complexity that, together with the margin, certifies generalization. This is exactly the structural-risk-minimization programme — wide margin as low capacity, sparse support set as compressed evidence, convex surrogate as tractable computation — assembled from the duality, KKT, and RKHS layers built in the prerequisite units.

## Full proof set [Master]

The dual derivation, the support-vector trichotomy, the bias recovery, and the representer-theorem instance are established in the Formal definition, Key theorem, and Exercises. The remaining Master claims are recorded here.

**Proposition 1 (hard-margin is the $C \to \infty$ limit of soft-margin on separable data).** *If the data are linearly separable, then for all $C$ large enough the soft-margin solution coincides with the hard-margin solution, with all $\xi_i = 0$.*

*Proof.* Separability gives a feasible $(w_0, b_0)$ with $y_i(\langle w_0, x_i\rangle + b_0) \ge 1$ for all $i$, hence the hard-margin program has a finite optimal value $\tfrac12\lVert w^\star\rVert^2 =: P^\star$. For any $C$, the soft-margin optimum is bounded above by the hard-margin feasible point $(w^\star, b^\star, 0)$ with value $P^\star$, so the soft optimum value is $\le P^\star$. Suppose the soft optimum had some $\xi_i > 0$; its objective would be $\tfrac12\lVert w\rVert^2 + C\sum_i\xi_i \ge C\xi_i$, which exceeds $P^\star$ once $C > P^\star/\xi_{\min}$ for the smallest positive slack achievable — more carefully, the optimal total slack $\sum_i\xi_i$ is a non-increasing function of $C$ that must vanish for $C$ large, since otherwise the $C\sum_i\xi_i$ term would force the objective above the fixed bound $P^\star$. With all $\xi_i = 0$ the soft program reduces to the hard program, so the minimizers coincide. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (the SVM solution depends only on the kernel Gram matrix).** *The dual optimizer $\alpha$, the support set, and the decision function $f(\cdot)$ evaluated at any point depend on the training data only through the Gram matrix $\mathbf K_{ij} = K(x_i, x_j)$, the labels $y$, and the test-point kernel vector $k(x)_i = K(x_i, x)$.*

*Proof.* The dual objective $\sum_i\alpha_i - \tfrac12\alpha^\top(D\mathbf K D)\alpha$ with $D = \operatorname{diag}(y)$ and its feasible set $\{0 \le \alpha \le C,\ y^\top\alpha = 0\}$ reference the inputs only through $\mathbf K$ and $y$; hence the optimizer $\alpha$ and the support set $\{i : \alpha_i > 0\}$ do too. The decision function $f(x) = \sum_i\alpha_i y_i K(x_i, x) + b = \alpha^\top D k(x) + b$ with $b = y_k - \alpha^\top D\, \mathbf K_{\cdot k}$ for a free support vector $k$ references inputs only through $\mathbf K$ and $k(x)$. So no explicit feature representation is ever needed; the feature map $\Phi$ may be infinite-dimensional. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (the SVM is a regularized empirical-risk minimizer with the hinge loss).** *The soft-margin primal is equivalent to $\min_{w, b}\sum_i[1 - y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b)]_+ + \tfrac{1}{2C}\lVert w\rVert^2$, and any minimizer of the latter yields, with $\xi_i = [1 - y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b)]_+$, a minimizer of the former.*

*Proof.* Fix $(w, b)$. The soft-margin program minimizes $\sum_i\xi_i$ over $\xi_i \ge 0$, $\xi_i \ge 1 - y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b)$ for each $i$ separately; the minimum of $\xi_i$ over $\{\xi_i \ge 0,\ \xi_i \ge t_i\}$ is $\max(0, t_i) = [t_i]_+$ with $t_i = 1 - y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b)$. Substituting the optimal slacks turns $\tfrac12\lVert w\rVert^2 + C\sum_i\xi_i$ into $\tfrac12\lVert w\rVert^2 + C\sum_i[1 - y_i(\langle w, x_i\rangle + b)]_+$; dividing by $C$ and writing $\lambda = 1/(2C)$ gives the hinge-plus-ridge form up to the positive scale $C$, which does not move the argmin. Conversely a minimizer of the hinge form with $\xi_i$ set to the hinge values is feasible for the soft-margin program and attains the same objective, hence optimal. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (hinge-loss classification calibration).** *Let $\eta = \mathbb P(y = +1\mid x)$. The conditional hinge risk $C_\eta(m) = \eta[1 - m]_+ + (1 - \eta)[1 + m]_+$ is minimized over $m \in \mathbb R$ at $m^\star = \operatorname{sign}(2\eta - 1)$ for $\eta \ne 1/2$. Consequently the population hinge minimizer has the Bayes sign, and the hinge loss is classification-calibrated.*

*Proof.* $C_\eta$ is convex and piecewise linear in $m$ with breakpoints at $m = \pm 1$. On $m \le -1$, $C_\eta = \eta(1 - m)$ with slope $-\eta \le 0$. On $-1 \le m \le 1$, $C_\eta = \eta(1 - m) + (1 - \eta)(1 + m) = 1 + (1 - 2\eta)m$ with slope $1 - 2\eta$. On $m \ge 1$, $C_\eta = (1 - \eta)(1 + m)$ with slope $1 - \eta \ge 0$. For $\eta > 1/2$ the middle slope $1 - 2\eta < 0$, so $C_\eta$ decreases up to $m = 1$ then increases, with minimum at $m^\star = 1 = \operatorname{sign}(2\eta - 1)$; for $\eta < 1/2$ the middle slope is positive, the minimum is at $m^\star = -1 = \operatorname{sign}(2\eta - 1)$. In both cases $\operatorname{sign}(m^\star) = \operatorname{sign}(2\eta - 1)$, the Bayes decision; calibration follows because the minimizing sign matches the Bayes rule whenever $\eta \ne 1/2$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (Rademacher bound for the kernel ball).** *For the class $\mathcal F_B = \{x \mapsto \langle w, \Phi(x)\rangle : \lVert w\rVert \le B\}$ with kernel $K$, the empirical Rademacher complexity satisfies $\hat{\mathfrak R}_n(\mathcal F_B) \le \tfrac{B}{n}\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n K(x_i, x_i)}$.*

*Proof.* With Rademacher signs $\sigma_i$ independent and uniform on $\{\pm 1\}$,
$$
\hat{\mathfrak R}_n(\mathcal F_B) = \frac1n\mathbb E_\sigma\sup_{\lVert w\rVert \le B}\Big\langle w, \sum_i\sigma_i\Phi(x_i)\Big\rangle = \frac{B}{n}\mathbb E_\sigma\Big\lVert\sum_i\sigma_i\Phi(x_i)\Big\rVert,
$$
the supremum attained by aligning $w$ with $\sum_i\sigma_i\Phi(x_i)$. By Jensen, $\mathbb E_\sigma\lVert\sum_i\sigma_i\Phi(x_i)\rVert \le (\mathbb E_\sigma\lVert\sum_i\sigma_i\Phi(x_i)\rVert^2)^{1/2}$, and $\mathbb E_\sigma\lVert\sum_i\sigma_i\Phi(x_i)\rVert^2 = \sum_{i,j}\mathbb E[\sigma_i\sigma_j]\langle\Phi(x_i), \Phi(x_j)\rangle = \sum_i\lVert\Phi(x_i)\rVert^2 = \sum_i K(x_i, x_i)$ using $\mathbb E[\sigma_i\sigma_j] = \delta_{ij}$. Hence $\hat{\mathfrak R}_n(\mathcal F_B) \le \tfrac{B}{n}\sqrt{\sum_i K(x_i, x_i)}$. Composing with the $1$-Lipschitz hinge loss via Talagrand's contraction inequality leaves this complexity unchanged up to the standard constant, yielding the margin-based generalization bound. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

Kernels, RKHS, and the representer theorem [45.08.01] are the direct parent: the SVM is the hinge-loss instance of regularized RKHS risk, and the representer theorem proved there is exactly what reduces the infinite-dimensional fit to the finite support-vector expansion $f = \sum_i\alpha_i y_i K_{x_i}$. Kernel ridge regression is the squared-error sibling with dense coefficients; the SVM differs only in the loss $L$, and the contrast between dense and sparse solutions is the hinge loss's flat-region signature.

Lagrangian duality [44.02.01] supplies the engine: the Wolfe dual of the margin program is its Lagrange dual, strong duality (via Slater on the convex QP) is what licenses passing from primal to dual, and the dual unknown $\alpha$ is the data-space coefficient vector conjugate to the feature-space normal $w$. The push from primal to dual is precisely what exposes the inner products that the kernel trick then replaces.

The Karush-Kuhn-Tucker conditions [44.02.02] characterize the support vectors: complementary slackness on the margin constraints produces the trichotomy $\alpha_i = 0$, $0 < \alpha_i < C$, $\alpha_i = C$, sorting the data into spectators, free margin vectors, and bound vectors, and the box constraints $0 \le \alpha_i \le C$ are the slackness conditions on the slack-variable multipliers. The same KKT-violation criterion drives the SMO working-set selection.

Empirical process theory and margin-based generalization bounds (forward, 45.07.05) certify why margin maximization controls overfitting: the Rademacher complexity of the kernel ball scales with the RKHS-norm budget $B \sim 1/\Delta$, so a large geometric margin means small complexity and a tight excess-risk bound; the hinge loss's classification calibration links that excess hinge risk back to the excess $0$-$1$ risk, completing the structural-risk-minimization argument.

Convex optimization solvers (forward) implement the SVM at scale: the dual is a box-constrained convex QP with a single linear equality, and decomposition methods such as SMO and its working-set generalizations exploit the KKT structure of [44.02.02], the optimization continuation of this unit's duality content.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The maximum-margin idea entered learning theory through Vladimir Vapnik and Alexey Chervonenkis's work on the optimal separating hyperplane in the 1960s and 1970s, where margin maximization was justified by the capacity-control programme that became structural risk minimization. The modern support vector machine took shape at AT&T Bell Labs: Bernhard Boser, Isabelle Guyon, and Vapnik introduced the kernel trick for the optimal-margin classifier in 1992, and Corinna Cortes and Vapnik published the soft-margin support-vector network for non-separable data in 1995 in *Machine Learning* [ref: TODO_REF Cortes 1995], adding the slack variables and the box-constrained dual that made the method practical on real, overlapping data. Vapnik's 1998 *Statistical Learning Theory* [ref: TODO_REF Vapnik 1998] set the margin-based generalization bounds and the gap-tolerant VC-dimension argument that motivate margin maximization on capacity grounds.

The kernel viewpoint was consolidated by Bernhard Schölkopf, Alexander Smola, and collaborators, whose 2002 *Learning with Kernels* [ref: TODO_REF Schölkopf 2002] unified the SVM, support vector regression, and the broader kernel-methods family under the RKHS regularization framework, and Hastie, Tibshirani, and Friedman placed the SVM as a penalized hinge-loss method alongside the rest of statistical learning. The computational bottleneck of the dense dual QP was broken by John Platt's 1999 sequential minimal optimization [ref: TODO_REF Platt 1999], which reduced training to a sequence of analytically solvable two-variable subproblems. The consistency theory — that the hinge loss is a classification-calibrated surrogate, so the SVM is Bayes-consistent — was settled by Ingo Steinwart and Andreas Christmann and collaborators in the 2000s [ref: TODO_REF Steinwart 2008], placing the convex-surrogate heuristic on a rigorous statistical footing.
