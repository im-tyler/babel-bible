## Intuition [Beginner]

Many of the patterns we want to learn are not straight lines. A spam filter, a handwriting reader, a price predictor — each needs a curved, flexible rule, not a flat one. The blunt fix is to invent new features by hand: square a number, multiply two columns together, take a logarithm. Pile up enough invented features and a straight-line method can fit a curved rule in the enlarged space. The problem is that the useful set of invented features can be enormous, even endless, and computing them all is hopeless.

Kernel methods sidestep the whole pile. They rest on one observation: most fitting methods only ever need to know how *alike* two examples are, not the examples themselves. A kernel is a similarity score — one number for each pair of examples saying how much they resemble each other. If you can compute that score directly, you never have to build the giant feature list. You get the flexibility of a vast feature space at the price of a small similarity table.

The everyday image is a recommendation. To guess whether you will like a film, a good system does not need to list every property of every film; it needs to know which films are similar to ones you already rated. Similarity carries the information. The kernel is that similarity, measured carefully enough that the math behind it stays honest.

The second idea is just as practical. When you fit a flexible rule and ask for the *best* one that does not overreact to noise, the answer is never some wild abstract function. It is always a simple weighted blend of the similarity scores to your training examples — one weight per example. So even a search over an endless space of curves collapses to finding a handful of numbers.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take three one-dimensional training points: $x_1 = 0$, $x_2 = 1$, $x_3 = 2$. We will use the squared-similarity kernel $K(a, b) = (1 + ab)^2$, a standard choice that secretly corresponds to fitting with the features $1$, $a$, and $a^2$.

First, build the similarity table, the $3 \times 3$ Gram matrix. Each entry is $K(x_i, x_j)$. For example $K(x_1, x_1) = (1 + 0)^2 = 1$, and $K(x_2, x_3) = (1 + 1 \times 2)^2 = (3)^2 = 9$. Working through all pairs:

$$ K = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 4 & 9 \\ 1 & 9 & 25 \end{pmatrix}. $$

Notice the table is symmetric: the similarity of $x_2$ to $x_3$ equals the similarity of $x_3$ to $x_2$. That symmetry is required of every kernel.

Now check the secret feature claim by hand for one pair. With features $\phi(a) = (1, a, a^2)$, the dot product is $\phi(a) \cdot \phi(b) = 1 + ab + a^2 b^2$. For $a = 1$, $b = 2$ that is $1 + 2 + 4 = 7$, but our kernel gave $9$. They differ because the matching feature map is actually $\phi(a) = (1, \sqrt 2\, a, a^2)$, giving $1 + 2ab + a^2 b^2 = (1 + ab)^2$. For $a = 1$, $b = 2$: $1 + 4 + 4 = 9$. The numbers agree.

What this tells us: the kernel value $9$ and the dot product $9$ are the same number, computed two ways. The kernel route asks for one multiplication and one squaring. The feature route asks you to build the three-coordinate vectors first. For three features the saving is small; for thousands or infinitely many features, the kernel route is the only one that finishes.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\mathcal X$ be a non-empty set (the input space). A symmetric function $K : \mathcal X \times \mathcal X \to \mathbb R$ is a **positive-definite kernel** if for every finite collection $x_1, \dots, x_n \in \mathcal X$ the **Gram matrix** $\mathbf K \in \mathbb R^{n \times n}$ with entries $\mathbf K_{ij} = K(x_i, x_j)$ is symmetric positive semidefinite, that is $c^\top \mathbf K c = \sum_{i,j} c_i c_j K(x_i, x_j) \ge 0$ for all $c \in \mathbb R^n$ [ref: TODO_REF Schölkopf 2002]. The **kernel trick** is the observation that such a $K$ is exactly an inner product of an implicit **feature map**: there is a Hilbert space $\mathcal H$ and a map $\Phi : \mathcal X \to \mathcal H$ with $K(x, x') = \langle \Phi(x), \Phi(x') \rangle_{\mathcal H}$, so any algorithm written purely in terms of inner products of inputs runs in $\mathcal H$ while touching only $K$.

A **reproducing-kernel Hilbert space** (RKHS) on $\mathcal X$ is a Hilbert space $\mathcal H$ of real-valued functions $f : \mathcal X \to \mathbb R$ in which, for every $x \in \mathcal X$, the evaluation functional $\mathrm{ev}_x : f \mapsto f(x)$ is bounded (continuous). By the Riesz representation theorem [02.11.08], each $\mathrm{ev}_x$ is represented by a unique element $K_x \in \mathcal H$, the **representer of evaluation**, satisfying the **reproducing property**

$$ \langle f, K_x \rangle_{\mathcal H} = f(x) \qquad \text{for all } f \in \mathcal H, \ x \in \mathcal X. $$

Writing $K(x, x') := \langle K_x, K_{x'} \rangle_{\mathcal H} = K_x(x')$ defines the **reproducing kernel** of $\mathcal H$; it is symmetric and positive-definite, with feature map $\Phi(x) = K_x$. The function $x' \mapsto K(x, x')$ is itself an element of $\mathcal H$, namely $K_x$, so the kernel "lives inside" the space it generates.

The correspondence runs both ways. The **Moore-Aronszajn theorem** states that to every symmetric positive-definite kernel $K$ there corresponds a *unique* RKHS $\mathcal H_K$ having $K$ as its reproducing kernel [ref: TODO_REF Aronszajn 1950]. The space is built by taking the linear span $\mathcal H_0 = \operatorname{span}\{K_x : x \in \mathcal X\}$, equipping it with the inner product $\langle \sum_i a_i K_{x_i}, \sum_j b_j K_{x_j} \rangle = \sum_{i,j} a_i b_j K(x_i, x_j)$ (well-defined and positive by positive-definiteness), and completing.

Standard kernels on $\mathcal X = \mathbb R^d$ include the **linear** kernel $K(x, x') = x^\top x'$ (feature map the identity), the **polynomial** kernel $K(x, x') = (1 + x^\top x')^p$ (feature space all monomials of degree $\le p$), the **Gaussian** or **radial basis function** (RBF) kernel $K(x, x') = \exp(-\lVert x - x' \rVert^2 / 2\sigma^2)$ (an infinite-dimensional feature space), and the **Matérn** family interpolating between rough and smooth. **Mercer's theorem** gives the eigen-picture: for a continuous kernel on a compact domain with measure $\mu$, the integral operator $(T_K f)(x) = \int K(x, x') f(x')\, d\mu(x')$ is self-adjoint, compact, and positive, so by the spectral theorem [02.11.08] it has eigenfunctions $\{e_j\}$ and eigenvalues $\lambda_j \ge 0$ with the uniformly convergent expansion $K(x, x') = \sum_j \lambda_j e_j(x) e_j(x')$, exhibiting the feature map $\Phi(x) = (\sqrt{\lambda_j}\, e_j(x))_j$ explicitly.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Symmetry alone is not enough.* The function $K(x, x') = -\lVert x - x'\rVert$ is symmetric but its Gram matrices are not positive semidefinite, so it is not a kernel; only the conditional version (positive on the subspace $\sum c_i = 0$) is admissible, and only after a fix.
- *Positive-definite kernel does not mean positive-definite Gram matrix.* The Gram matrix is required only positive *semi*definite; it is singular whenever two inputs coincide or the points are linearly dependent in feature space. The closed forms below use $\mathbf K + \lambda \mathbf I$ precisely to restore invertibility.
- *The kernel value is not a distance.* A large Gaussian kernel value means inputs are *close*, not far; the induced feature-space distance is $\lVert \Phi(x) - \Phi(x')\rVert^2 = K(x,x) + K(x',x') - 2K(x,x')$, which decreases as the kernel grows.
- *The RKHS norm is not the supremum norm.* A small $\lVert f \rVert_{\mathcal H}$ controls pointwise values only through $|f(x)| = |\langle f, K_x\rangle| \le \lVert f\rVert_{\mathcal H}\sqrt{K(x,x)}$; functions with bounded sup-norm can have unbounded RKHS norm, which is why the penalty $\lVert f\rVert_{\mathcal H}^2$ is a genuine smoothness control rather than a size cap.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (representer theorem — Kimeldorf-Wahba 1971; Schölkopf-Herbrich-Smola 2001).** Let $\mathcal H_K$ be the RKHS of a positive-definite kernel $K$ on $\mathcal X$, let $(x_1, y_1), \dots, (x_n, y_n)$ be data, let $L : \mathbb R^n \to \mathbb R$ be any function of the fitted values $(f(x_1), \dots, f(x_n))$, and let $\Omega : [0, \infty) \to \mathbb R$ be strictly increasing. Then every minimizer of the regularized empirical risk

$$ J(f) = L\big(f(x_1), \dots, f(x_n)\big) + \Omega\big(\lVert f \rVert_{\mathcal H_K}\big) $$

over $f \in \mathcal H_K$ admits a representation $f^\star = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i K_{x_i}$, i.e. $f^\star(x) = \sum_{i=1}^n \alpha_i K(x_i, x)$ for some $\alpha \in \mathbb R^n$. The proof follows the orthogonal-projection argument of Schölkopf & Smola [ref: TODO_REF Schölkopf 2002] §4.2 and the original squared-error case of Kimeldorf & Wahba [ref: TODO_REF Kimeldorf 1971].

*Proof.* Let $V = \operatorname{span}\{K_{x_1}, \dots, K_{x_n}\}$, a finite-dimensional, hence closed, subspace of $\mathcal H_K$. By the projection theorem for Hilbert spaces [02.11.08], every $f \in \mathcal H_K$ decomposes uniquely as $f = f_\parallel + f_\perp$ with $f_\parallel \in V$ and $f_\perp \in V^\perp$. Write $f_\parallel = \sum_i \alpha_i K_{x_i}$.

The fitted values depend only on the parallel part. By the reproducing property, for each data point $x_j$,

$$ f(x_j) = \langle f, K_{x_j} \rangle = \langle f_\parallel + f_\perp, K_{x_j} \rangle = \langle f_\parallel, K_{x_j} \rangle + \langle f_\perp, K_{x_j} \rangle = f_\parallel(x_j), $$

because $K_{x_j} \in V$ and $f_\perp \perp V$ force $\langle f_\perp, K_{x_j} \rangle = 0$. Hence $L(f(x_1), \dots, f(x_n)) = L(f_\parallel(x_1), \dots, f_\parallel(x_n))$: the loss term is blind to $f_\perp$.

The penalty can only grow with $f_\perp$. By orthogonality and the Pythagorean identity,

$$ \lVert f \rVert_{\mathcal H_K}^2 = \lVert f_\parallel \rVert_{\mathcal H_K}^2 + \lVert f_\perp \rVert_{\mathcal H_K}^2 \ge \lVert f_\parallel \rVert_{\mathcal H_K}^2, $$

with equality if and only if $f_\perp = 0$. Since $\Omega$ is strictly increasing, $\Omega(\lVert f \rVert) \ge \Omega(\lVert f_\parallel \rVert)$, again with equality only when $f_\perp = 0$. Combining, $J(f) \ge J(f_\parallel)$, with strict inequality unless $f_\perp = 0$. So any minimizer must have $f_\perp = 0$, hence lies in $V$ and has the claimed finite expansion. When $\Omega$ is merely non-decreasing, $f_\parallel$ is *a* minimizer even if not the unique one. $\square$

**Bridge.** The representer theorem is the foundational reason kernel learning is computationally finite: an optimization over an infinite-dimensional function space reduces to $n$ coefficients $\alpha$, and the engine is nothing more than orthogonal projection onto the span of the data representers, exactly the Hilbert-space decomposition of [02.11.08]. This builds toward the kernel ridge closed form below, where $L$ is squared error and the coefficients solve $(\mathbf K + \lambda \mathbf I)\alpha = y$, and it appears again in the support vector machine [45.08.02], where $L$ is the hinge loss and the same projection argument forces the solution onto the support vectors. The result is dual to the primal weight-space view of ridge regression [45.06.03]: there the unknown is a weight vector $\beta$ in feature space, here it is a coefficient vector $\alpha$ in data space, and the push-through identity that connects them is exactly the central insight that the two parameterizations describe the same fitted function. Putting these together, the kernel trick supplies the inner products, the reproducing property converts evaluation into an inner product, and the projection theorem guarantees the answer is a finite kernel expansion — three facts that generalise from squared error to any pointwise loss with a monotone norm penalty.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the reproducing kernel of an RKHS is symmetric and that $K(x, x) \ge 0$ for every $x$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $K(x, x') = \langle K_x, K_{x'} \rangle$ and the symmetry and positivity of the inner product.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By definition $K(x, x') = \langle K_x, K_{x'} \rangle$. The inner product is symmetric, so $K(x, x') = \langle K_x, K_{x'} \rangle = \langle K_{x'}, K_x \rangle = K(x', x)$. Taking $x' = x$ gives $K(x, x) = \langle K_x, K_x \rangle = \lVert K_x \rVert^2 \ge 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

For the Gaussian kernel $K(x, x') = \exp(-(x - x')^2 / 2)$ on $\mathbb R$, compute $K(0, 0)$ and $K(0, \sqrt{2})$ (give the latter as $e^{-1}$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute and simplify the exponent $-(x - x')^2/2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$K(0,0) = \exp(0) = 1$. $K(0, \sqrt 2) = \exp(-(\sqrt 2)^2/2) = \exp(-2/2) = \exp(-1) = e^{-1} \approx 0.368$. The diagonal self-similarity is $1$, and similarity decays as the inputs separate.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that if $K_1, K_2$ are positive-definite kernels then so are $K_1 + K_2$ and $c K_1$ for $c \ge 0$, and that the pointwise product $K_1 K_2$ is positive-definite (Schur product theorem).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the sum, add the two Gram quadratic forms. For the product, use that the Hadamard product of two PSD matrices is PSD.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For any $x_1, \dots, x_n$ and $c \in \mathbb R^n$, $\sum_{ij} c_i c_j (K_1 + K_2)(x_i, x_j) = c^\top \mathbf K_1 c + c^\top \mathbf K_2 c \ge 0$, so $K_1 + K_2$ is a kernel; scaling by $c \ge 0$ preserves the sign. For the product, the Gram matrix of $K_1 K_2$ is the Hadamard (entrywise) product $\mathbf K_1 \circ \mathbf K_2$. Writing $\mathbf K_1 = \sum_a u_a u_a^\top$ and $\mathbf K_2 = \sum_b v_b v_b^\top$ as PSD decompositions, $\mathbf K_1 \circ \mathbf K_2 = \sum_{a,b} (u_a \circ v_b)(u_a \circ v_b)^\top$, a sum of rank-one PSD matrices, hence PSD. So $K_1 K_2$ is positive-definite.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the kernel ridge regression coefficients: minimizing $\sum_i (y_i - f(x_i))^2 + \lambda \lVert f \rVert_{\mathcal H}^2$ over $f \in \mathcal H_K$, show $\alpha = (\mathbf K + \lambda \mathbf I)^{-1} y$ where $f = \sum_i \alpha_i K_{x_i}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the representer theorem, then express both the fitted values and the norm in terms of the Gram matrix $\mathbf K$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the representer theorem $f = \sum_i \alpha_i K_{x_i}$, so the fitted vector is $(f(x_j))_j = \mathbf K \alpha$ (since $f(x_j) = \sum_i \alpha_i K(x_i, x_j) = (\mathbf K \alpha)_j$), and the norm is $\lVert f \rVert^2 = \sum_{ij}\alpha_i \alpha_j K(x_i, x_j) = \alpha^\top \mathbf K \alpha$. The objective becomes $\lVert y - \mathbf K \alpha \rVert^2 + \lambda \alpha^\top \mathbf K \alpha$. Setting the gradient to zero: $-2\mathbf K(y - \mathbf K \alpha) + 2\lambda \mathbf K \alpha = 0$, i.e. $\mathbf K\big[(\mathbf K + \lambda \mathbf I)\alpha - y\big] = 0$. Solving the bracket, $\alpha = (\mathbf K + \lambda \mathbf I)^{-1} y$ (valid for $\lambda > 0$, where $\mathbf K + \lambda \mathbf I$ is invertible), and $\hat f(x) = \sum_i \alpha_i K(x_i, x) = k(x)^\top(\mathbf K + \lambda \mathbf I)^{-1}y$ with $k(x)_i = K(x_i, x)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify that the polynomial kernel $K(x, x') = (x^\top x')^2$ on $\mathbb R^2$ equals $\langle \Phi(x), \Phi(x')\rangle$ for the feature map $\Phi(x) = (x_1^2, \sqrt 2\, x_1 x_2, x_2^2)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $(x_1 x_1' + x_2 x_2')^2$ and match terms to the dot product of the feature vectors.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$(x^\top x')^2 = (x_1 x_1' + x_2 x_2')^2 = (x_1 x_1')^2 + 2(x_1 x_1')(x_2 x_2') + (x_2 x_2')^2 = x_1^2 (x_1')^2 + 2 x_1 x_2\, x_1' x_2' + x_2^2 (x_2')^2$. The dot product $\langle \Phi(x), \Phi(x')\rangle = x_1^2 (x_1')^2 + (\sqrt 2 x_1 x_2)(\sqrt 2 x_1' x_2') + x_2^2(x_2')^2 = x_1^2(x_1')^2 + 2 x_1 x_2 x_1' x_2' + x_2^2 (x_2')^2$, which matches term by term. The $\sqrt 2$ is exactly the factor that makes the cross term's coefficient $2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the pointwise bound $|f(x)| \le \lVert f \rVert_{\mathcal H} \sqrt{K(x, x)}$ for every $f$ in an RKHS, and deduce that norm convergence in $\mathcal H_K$ implies pointwise convergence.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply Cauchy-Schwarz to the reproducing identity $f(x) = \langle f, K_x \rangle$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the reproducing property $f(x) = \langle f, K_x \rangle$, so Cauchy-Schwarz gives $|f(x)| \le \lVert f \rVert_{\mathcal H} \lVert K_x \rVert_{\mathcal H}$. But $\lVert K_x \rVert_{\mathcal H}^2 = \langle K_x, K_x \rangle = K(x, x)$, so $\lVert K_x \rVert = \sqrt{K(x,x)}$ and $|f(x)| \le \lVert f\rVert_{\mathcal H}\sqrt{K(x,x)}$. If $f_n \to f$ in norm, then $|f_n(x) - f(x)| \le \lVert f_n - f\rVert_{\mathcal H}\sqrt{K(x,x)} \to 0$ for each fixed $x$, so norm convergence forces pointwise convergence — the defining advantage of an RKHS over a generic $L^2$ space, where equality is only almost-everywhere and pointwise values are undefined.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Explain why the representer theorem requires the penalty to be a monotone function of the RKHS norm specifically, and give a penalty for which the conclusion can fail.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The proof needs (a) the loss to depend only on fitted values, and (b) the penalty to be minimized by killing the orthogonal component. Which step breaks if the penalty is not a function of $\lVert f\rVert$?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The proof has two pillars. First, the loss depends only on the fitted values $f(x_j) = \langle f, K_{x_j}\rangle$, which lie in the span $V$, so $f_\perp$ is invisible to it — this needs only that the loss is a function of the evaluations, true for any pointwise loss. Second, the penalty must be a non-decreasing function of $\lVert f\rVert_{\mathcal H} = \sqrt{\lVert f_\parallel\rVert^2 + \lVert f_\perp\rVert^2}$, so that adding any $f_\perp$ strictly increases it; this forces the minimizer to set $f_\perp = 0$. If instead the penalty depended on $f$ through some functional *not* controlled by the RKHS norm — for instance a penalty that rewards energy in a particular off-span direction, such as $-\langle f, g\rangle$ for a fixed $g \notin V$ — then increasing $f_\perp$ along $g$ would lower the objective, and the minimizer would leave $V$. Monotonicity in the RKHS norm is precisely what guarantees the orthogonal complement is dead weight.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

Kernel ridge regression is the squared-error instance and the cleanest bridge to the weight-space view [45.06.03]. With $L(f(x_1),\dots,f(x_n)) = \sum_i (y_i - f(x_i))^2$ and $\Omega(t) = \lambda t^2$, the representer theorem gives $\hat f = \sum_i \alpha_i K_{x_i}$ with $\alpha = (\mathbf K + \lambda \mathbf I)^{-1} y$, so $\hat f(x) = k(x)^\top (\mathbf K + \lambda \mathbf I)^{-1} y$ where $k(x)_i = K(x_i, x)$. This is exactly the push-through identity of [45.06.03]: in the primal feature space the fit is $\Phi(x)^\top (\Phi^\top \Phi + \lambda \mathbf I)^{-1}\Phi^\top y$, and $(\Phi^\top \Phi + \lambda \mathbf I)^{-1}\Phi^\top = \Phi^\top(\Phi\Phi^\top + \lambda \mathbf I)^{-1}$ turns the $m \times m$ feature-space solve (with $m$ possibly infinite) into the $n \times n$ kernel solve. The kernel trick is the statement that $\Phi$ never appears alone, only through $\mathbf K = \Phi\Phi^\top$, so an infinite feature dimension costs nothing.

The Moore-Aronszajn correspondence is sharper than a bare existence claim. The map sending a positive-definite kernel to its RKHS is a bijection onto Hilbert function spaces with continuous evaluations, and it respects structure: the RKHS of $K_1 + K_2$ is the sum space $\{f_1 + f_2 : f_i \in \mathcal H_{K_i}\}$ with the infimal-convolution norm, the RKHS of a product kernel is (a quotient of) the tensor product, and the RKHS of a kernel restricted to a subset is the restriction of the functions [ref: TODO_REF Aronszajn 1950]. Mercer's theorem refines the picture analytically: for a continuous kernel on a compact metric space with a finite Borel measure of full support, the integral operator $T_K$ is trace-class with eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \ge \lambda_2 \ge \cdots \to 0$ and orthonormal eigenfunctions $e_j \in L^2(\mu)$, the kernel expands as $K(x, x') = \sum_j \lambda_j e_j(x) e_j(x')$ uniformly, and the RKHS is the ellipsoid $\mathcal H_K = \{ \sum_j c_j \sqrt{\lambda_j}\, e_j : \sum_j c_j^2 < \infty \}$ with $\lVert \sum_j a_j e_j\rVert_{\mathcal H_K}^2 = \sum_j a_j^2 / \lambda_j$. The eigenvalue decay rate is the precise quantification of the kernel's smoothness and governs the generalization rate of kernel methods through the effective dimension $\sum_j \lambda_j/(\lambda_j + \lambda)$, the kernel analogue of the effective degrees of freedom [45.06.03].

Smoothing splines are the RKHS method that predates the modern kernel vocabulary [ref: TODO_REF Wahba 1990]. Minimizing $\sum_i (y_i - f(x_i))^2 + \lambda \int (f''(t))^2\, dt$ over the Sobolev space $W_2^2$ is a regularized RKHS problem: the penalty is a squared seminorm whose null space is the linear functions, and the reproducing kernel of the complement is a piecewise-cubic Green's function. The representer theorem (in its seminorm-plus-null-space form) forces the minimizer to be a *natural cubic spline* with knots exactly at the data points — an $n$-dimensional solution to an infinite-dimensional variational problem, the original instance of the finite-representation phenomenon. Generalized cross-validation, which selects $\lambda$ by $\mathrm{GCV}(\lambda) = n^{-1}\lVert y - \mathbf S_\lambda y\rVert^2 / (1 - \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf S_\lambda)/n)^2$ with smoother matrix $\mathbf S_\lambda = \mathbf K(\mathbf K + \lambda\mathbf I)^{-1}$, is Wahba's invention and carries over verbatim to kernel ridge regression.

The support vector machine is the same architecture with a different loss [45.08.02]. Taking $L$ to be the hinge loss $\sum_i \max(0, 1 - y_i f(x_i))$ (with $y_i \in \{-1, +1\}$) and $\Omega(t) = \lambda t^2$, the representer theorem again forces $\hat f = \sum_i \alpha_i K_{x_i}$, and the Karush-Kuhn-Tucker conditions of the resulting convex program make most $\alpha_i$ vanish: the surviving terms are the *support vectors*, the points on or inside the margin. Sparsity in $\alpha$ is the hinge-loss signature, absent in the dense kernel-ridge solution, and it is the reason SVMs evaluate quickly at test time. The kernel choice is what makes the SVM nonlinear without changing the convex optimization — the kernel trick applied to a maximum-margin classifier.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason kernels, RKHS, and the representer theorem cohere into one theory is that a positive-definite kernel *is* an inner product in disguise, and the reproducing property converts the act of evaluating a function into taking that inner product — so the entire apparatus of Hilbert-space geometry [02.11.08] becomes available for learning. This is exactly the orthogonal-projection decomposition: the data representers $K_{x_i}$ span a finite subspace, the loss sees only the projection onto it, and the norm penalty kills the orthogonal complement, which is why an infinite-dimensional search collapses to $n$ coefficients. Putting these together with the Moore-Aronszajn correspondence shows the central insight — that choosing a kernel and choosing a hypothesis space with its smoothness penalty are *the same act* viewed from two sides, the kernel side computational and the function-space side statistical, joined by the Mercer eigen-expansion that turns the kernel into an explicit feature map. The representer theorem generalises ridge regression [45.06.03] from a fixed finite design to an arbitrary RKHS: kernel ridge is the squared-error case, the smoothing spline is the Sobolev case, and the SVM is the hinge-loss case, each obtained by changing only the loss $L$ while the projection argument and the finite expansion are dual to the same Hilbert-space fact. The bridge is the Gram matrix $\mathbf K$: its positive-semidefiniteness certifies the kernel, its regularized inverse $(\mathbf K + \lambda \mathbf I)^{-1}$ solves the fit, and its eigenvalues quantify the effective dimension that controls generalization.

## Full proof set [Master]

The reproducing property, the Moore-Aronszajn construction, the representer theorem, and the kernel-ridge closed form are established in the Formal definition, Key theorem, and Exercises sections. The remaining Master claims are recorded here.

**Proposition 1 (Moore-Aronszajn existence and uniqueness).** *To every symmetric positive-definite kernel $K$ on $\mathcal X$ there corresponds a unique Hilbert space $\mathcal H_K$ of functions on $\mathcal X$ with $K_x \in \mathcal H_K$ and the reproducing property $\langle f, K_x\rangle = f(x)$ for all $f, x$.*

*Proof.* Existence: on $\mathcal H_0 = \operatorname{span}\{K_x : x \in \mathcal X\}$ define $\langle \sum_i a_i K_{x_i}, \sum_j b_j K_{x_j}\rangle = \sum_{i,j} a_i b_j K(x_i, x_j)$. This is well-defined (independent of representation, since for $f = \sum_i a_i K_{x_i}$ the value $\sum_i a_i f'(x_i)$ depends only on $f$ via the reproducing identity on $\mathcal H_0$), bilinear, symmetric by symmetry of $K$, and positive semidefinite by positive-definiteness of $K$. For definiteness, the bound $|f(x)|^2 = |\langle f, K_x\rangle|^2 \le \langle f, f\rangle K(x,x)$ (Cauchy-Schwarz, valid for a positive semidefinite form) shows $\langle f, f\rangle = 0 \Rightarrow f \equiv 0$, so the form is an inner product. Complete $\mathcal H_0$ to a Hilbert space $\mathcal H_K$; the same Cauchy-Schwarz bound shows every Cauchy sequence converges pointwise to a function and that evaluation stays continuous, so $\mathcal H_K$ is a function space with the reproducing property extended by continuity. Uniqueness: if $\mathcal H'$ is another RKHS with kernel $K$, it contains each $K_x$, contains their span, and the reproducing property fixes the inner product on that span to agree with $\mathcal H_K$; closures of the same dense subspace under the same norm coincide, so $\mathcal H' = \mathcal H_K$ isometrically. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (kernel trick — feature-map characterisation).** *A symmetric $K : \mathcal X\times\mathcal X \to \mathbb R$ is positive-definite if and only if there exist a Hilbert space $\mathcal F$ and a map $\Phi : \mathcal X \to \mathcal F$ with $K(x, x') = \langle \Phi(x), \Phi(x')\rangle_{\mathcal F}$.*

*Proof.* ($\Leftarrow$) If $K(x,x') = \langle\Phi(x),\Phi(x')\rangle$ then for any $c$, $\sum_{ij} c_i c_j K(x_i,x_j) = \lVert \sum_i c_i \Phi(x_i)\rVert^2 \ge 0$, and symmetry is inherited from the inner product. ($\Rightarrow$) Take $\mathcal F = \mathcal H_K$ and $\Phi(x) = K_x$; then $\langle\Phi(x),\Phi(x')\rangle = \langle K_x, K_{x'}\rangle = K(x, x')$ by definition of the kernel. The canonical feature map is into the RKHS itself, but Proposition 1's Mercer form $\Phi(x) = (\sqrt{\lambda_j}e_j(x))_j$ into $\ell^2$ is an alternative when the eigen-expansion converges. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (kernel ridge equals feature-space ridge).** *Let $\Phi : \mathcal X \to \mathcal F$ realise $K$, $\Phi$ the $n\times m$ design with rows $\Phi(x_i)^\top$ (allow $m = \infty$ with the Hilbert-space interpretation). For $\lambda > 0$ the feature-space ridge fit $\hat f(x) = \Phi(x)^\top \hat\beta$, $\hat\beta = (\Phi^\top\Phi + \lambda\mathbf I)^{-1}\Phi^\top y$, equals the kernel fit $\hat f(x) = k(x)^\top(\mathbf K + \lambda\mathbf I)^{-1}y$ with $\mathbf K = \Phi\Phi^\top$, $k(x)_i = K(x_i, x)$.*

*Proof.* The push-through identity $(\Phi^\top\Phi + \lambda\mathbf I_m)^{-1}\Phi^\top = \Phi^\top(\Phi\Phi^\top + \lambda\mathbf I_n)^{-1}$ holds because both sides equal $\Phi^\top(\Phi\Phi^\top + \lambda\mathbf I_n)^{-1}$ after multiplying the identity $\Phi^\top(\Phi\Phi^\top + \lambda\mathbf I) = (\Phi^\top\Phi + \lambda\mathbf I)\Phi^\top$ on the left and right by the respective inverses (each side of that identity equals $\Phi^\top\Phi\Phi^\top + \lambda\Phi^\top$). Hence $\hat\beta = \Phi^\top(\mathbf K + \lambda\mathbf I)^{-1}y$, and $\hat f(x) = \Phi(x)^\top\hat\beta = \Phi(x)^\top\Phi^\top(\mathbf K + \lambda\mathbf I)^{-1}y$. Since $(\Phi(x)^\top\Phi^\top)_i = \langle\Phi(x),\Phi(x_i)\rangle = K(x_i, x) = k(x)_i$, this is $k(x)^\top(\mathbf K + \lambda\mathbf I)^{-1}y$. The proof uses only inner products of feature vectors, so it is valid for $m = \infty$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Mercer expansion and the RKHS as a weighted ellipsoid).** *Let $K$ be continuous on a compact $\mathcal X$ with a finite Borel measure $\mu$ of full support. Then $T_K$ has eigenpairs $(\lambda_j, e_j)$ with $\lambda_j \ge 0$, $\sum_j \lambda_j < \infty$, $K(x,x') = \sum_j \lambda_j e_j(x)e_j(x')$ uniformly, and $\mathcal H_K = \{f = \sum_j a_j e_j : \sum_j a_j^2/\lambda_j < \infty\}$ with $\lVert f\rVert_{\mathcal H_K}^2 = \sum_j a_j^2/\lambda_j$ (sum over $j$ with $\lambda_j > 0$).*

*Proof.* The operator $T_K$ on $L^2(\mu)$ is self-adjoint (by symmetry of $K$), positive (by positive-definiteness, $\langle T_K f, f\rangle = \iint K(x,x')f(x)f(x')\,d\mu\,d\mu \ge 0$ as a limit of Gram quadratic forms), and compact (a continuous kernel on a compact domain is Hilbert-Schmidt). The spectral theorem for compact self-adjoint operators [02.11.08] supplies the eigensystem with $\lambda_j \to 0$; Mercer's theorem upgrades the $L^2$ expansion to uniform convergence using continuity and positivity. Define $\Phi(x) = (\sqrt{\lambda_j}e_j(x))_j \in \ell^2$; then $\langle\Phi(x),\Phi(x')\rangle = \sum_j\lambda_j e_j(x)e_j(x') = K(x,x')$, so by Proposition 2 this $\Phi$ realises $K$ and its image-space inner product gives the stated RKHS. For $f = \sum_j a_j e_j$, the reproducing property forces $\langle f, K_{x}\rangle = \sum_j (a_j/\lambda_j)\lambda_j e_j(x) = f(x)$ precisely when the inner product is $\langle \sum a_j e_j, \sum b_j e_j\rangle_{\mathcal H_K} = \sum_j a_j b_j/\lambda_j$, giving the ellipsoid norm. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

Ridge regression and shrinkage [45.06.03] is the parent: kernel ridge regression is its kernelized form, obtained by the push-through identity proved here as Proposition 3, and the effective-dimension quantity $\sum_j \lambda_j/(\lambda_j + \lambda)$ that controls kernel generalization is the Mercer-eigenvalue version of the effective degrees of freedom $\operatorname{tr}(H_\lambda)$ defined there; the Gaussian-prior reading of ridge becomes the Gaussian-process prior whose covariance is the kernel.

Hilbert space [02.11.08] supplies the entire engine: the Riesz representation theorem produces the representer $K_x$ of each evaluation functional, the projection theorem yields the orthogonal decomposition $f = f_\parallel + f_\perp$ that drives the representer theorem, and the spectral theorem for compact self-adjoint operators produces the Mercer eigen-expansion of Proposition 4.

Support vector machines [45.08.02] are the next unit: the same representer theorem with the hinge loss in place of squared error gives the sparse support-vector expansion, and the kernel trick makes the maximum-margin classifier nonlinear while keeping the optimization convex; this unit supplies the RKHS and representer-theorem foundation that the SVM specializes.

The spectral theorem and functional-analytic operator theory of [02.11.08] reappear through Mercer's theorem, which is the spectral decomposition of the kernel integral operator; the eigenfunction basis is the infinite-dimensional analogue of the singular-vector basis that diagonalizes ridge regression in [45.06.03].

Gaussian processes and Bayesian nonparametrics (forward) make the kernel a prior covariance: the kernel-ridge predictor $k(x)^\top(\mathbf K + \lambda\mathbf I)^{-1}y$ is exactly the posterior mean of a Gaussian process with covariance $K$ and noise variance $\lambda$, so the RKHS norm penalty and the Gaussian-process prior are the same regularizer, paralleling the ridge/Gaussian-prior identification of [45.06.03].

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The theory of reproducing kernels was unified by Nachman Aronszajn in his 1950 *Transactions of the American Mathematical Society* paper [ref: TODO_REF Aronszajn 1950], which gave the correspondence between symmetric positive-definite kernels and Hilbert function spaces its definitive form and proved the existence-uniqueness theorem now called Moore-Aronszajn, after E. H. Moore's earlier "positive Hermitian matrices" of the 1930s. James Mercer had established the eigen-expansion of a continuous positive-definite kernel in 1909 in the context of integral equations, well before the Hilbert-space language existed; his theorem became the bridge between the analytic and the feature-space pictures. The kernels themselves had appeared across mathematics — in potential theory as Green's functions, in probability as covariance functions of Gaussian processes, and in the theory of integral equations — long before they were recognized as one object.

The statistical use began with smoothing splines. George Kimeldorf and Grace Wahba proved the representer theorem for squared-error spline-smoothing problems in 1971 [ref: TODO_REF Kimeldorf 1971], showing the infinite-dimensional variational problem has a finite-dimensional natural-spline solution, and Wahba's 1990 monograph [ref: TODO_REF Wahba 1990] developed the reproducing-kernel framework for observational data together with generalized cross-validation. The machine-learning rediscovery came through Bernhard Schölkopf, Alexander Smola, and collaborators in the late 1990s, who recognized the kernel trick as a way to nonlinearize the support vector machine of Vapnik and Cortes and generalized the representer theorem to arbitrary pointwise losses with monotone regularizers; their 2002 monograph *Learning with Kernels* [ref: TODO_REF Schölkopf 2002] consolidated the field. Hastie, Tibshirani, and Friedman [ref: TODO_REF Hastie 2009] placed the RKHS regularization framework, kernel ridge regression, and smoothing splines in a single statistical account in *The Elements of Statistical Learning*.
