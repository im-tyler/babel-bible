## Intuition [Beginner]

Every time you use data to make a call — estimate a quantity, choose between two explanations, decide whether a part is defective — you are picking a *rule* that turns observations into an action. Two honest people with the same data can use different rules. Statistical decision theory is the framework for asking which rule is better, and what "better" even means once chance is in the picture.

The first ingredient is a way to score mistakes. A *loss* says how bad it is to take action $a$ when the truth is $\theta$. Guessing a temperature of 19 when it is really 20 should cost less than guessing 5. A common score is the squared miss, but the framework allows any sensible cost. The loss is the value judgment; everything after it is mathematics.

A rule does not have one loss, it has a whole spread of them, because the data are random. So we average the loss over the randomness in the data, holding the truth $\theta$ fixed. That average is the *risk* of the rule at $\theta$. A rule with low risk is one whose mistakes are small on average, whatever specific data happen to arrive.

The catch is that risk depends on the unknown truth $\theta$. One rule might be best when $\theta$ is small, another best when $\theta$ is large. There is rarely a single rule that beats all others everywhere. So the theory offers principles for breaking the tie: avoid rules that are beaten outright (keep only the *admissible* ones), protect against the worst case (*minimax*), or weigh the possible truths by a prior belief and minimise the weighted average (*Bayes*). These three principles, and how they secretly fit together, are the whole subject.

## Visual [Beginner]

## Worked example [Beginner]

You observe one draw $X$ from a population whose unknown mean is $\theta$, with the draw scattered around $\theta$ with variance $4$. You want to estimate $\theta$. Compare two rules: the rule $\delta_1(X) = X$ (just report the data), and the shrink-toward-zero rule $\delta_2(X) = \tfrac{1}{2}X$. Score each by squared error, $L(\theta, a) = (\theta - a)^2$.

**Step 1. Risk of $\delta_1$.** The risk is the average squared error. For $\delta_1(X) = X$, the error is $X - \theta$, whose average square is the variance, $4$. So the risk is $4$ for every $\theta$ — a flat line.

**Step 2. Risk of $\delta_2$.** Now the error is $\tfrac{1}{2}X - \theta$. Split it into a typical-offset part and a wobble part. On average $\tfrac{1}{2}X$ sits at $\tfrac{1}{2}\theta$, so it is off-target by $\tfrac{1}{2}\theta$ (this is the bias). The wobble of $\tfrac{1}{2}X$ has variance $\tfrac{1}{4}\times 4 = 1$. The average squared error is bias-squared plus wobble:

$$
R(\theta, \delta_2) = \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\theta\right)^2 + 1 = \tfrac{1}{4}\theta^2 + 1.
$$

**Step 3. Compare.** When is $\delta_2$ better? Solve $\tfrac{1}{4}\theta^2 + 1 < 4$, i.e. $\theta^2 < 12$, i.e. $|\theta| < 3.46$. So $\delta_2$ wins for truths near zero and loses for truths far out.

**What this tells us.** Neither rule beats the other everywhere: their risk curves cross. Both are admissible — you cannot discard either on dominance grounds alone. Choosing between them needs an extra principle: if you fear a large $|\theta|$, the flat rule $\delta_1$ is safer (minimax); if you believe $\theta$ is probably near zero, the shrinker $\delta_2$ is better on average (Bayes).

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **statistical decision problem** is specified by a parameter space $\Theta$, a family of probability measures $\{P_\theta : \theta \in \Theta\}$ on a sample space $(\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{B})$ (the measure-theoretic substrate of [37.01.01]), an **action space** $\mathcal{A}$, and a **loss function** $L : \Theta \times \mathcal{A} \to [0, \infty]$ that is measurable in its arguments. A (non-randomized) **decision rule** is a measurable map $\delta : \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{A}$; on observing $X \sim P_\theta$ one takes the action $\delta(X)$.

**Definition (risk function).** The **risk** of a rule $\delta$ at $\theta$ is the expected loss
$$
\boxed{\; R(\theta, \delta) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_\theta\big[L(\theta, \delta(X))\big] \;=\; \int_{\mathcal{X}} L(\theta, \delta(x))\, dP_\theta(x). \;}
$$
A rule is identified with the function $\theta \mapsto R(\theta, \delta)$ it induces. For point estimation with $\mathcal{A} = \Theta \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ and squared-error loss $L(\theta, a) = (\theta - a)^2$, the risk is the mean squared error, with the bias-variance decomposition $R(\theta, \delta) = \operatorname{Var}_\theta(\delta) + (\mathbb{E}_\theta\delta - \theta)^2$ [ref: TODO_REF Casella & Berger §7.3].

**Definition (dominance and admissibility).** A rule $\delta'$ **dominates** $\delta$ if $R(\theta, \delta') \leq R(\theta, \delta)$ for all $\theta \in \Theta$ with strict inequality for at least one $\theta$. A rule $\delta$ is **inadmissible** if some $\delta'$ dominates it, and **admissible** otherwise. Admissibility is a minimal requirement, not a recommendation: it only rules out being beaten outright, and many admissible rules are poor.

**Definition (minimax rule).** The **maximum risk** of $\delta$ is $\sup_{\theta} R(\theta, \delta)$. A rule $\delta^*$ is **minimax** if it minimises the maximum risk,
$$
\sup_{\theta} R(\theta, \delta^*) \;=\; \inf_{\delta}\, \sup_{\theta} R(\theta, \delta) \;=:\; \bar V,
$$
the **minimax value** of the problem. Minimaxity is the conservative, worst-case-protective principle.

**Definition (Bayes risk and Bayes rule).** Given a **prior** probability measure $\pi$ on $\Theta$, the **Bayes risk** of $\delta$ is the prior-averaged risk
$$
r(\pi, \delta) \;=\; \int_{\Theta} R(\theta, \delta)\, d\pi(\theta).
$$
A rule $\delta_\pi$ is a **Bayes rule** with respect to $\pi$ if it minimises the Bayes risk, $r(\pi, \delta_\pi) = \inf_\delta r(\pi, \delta) =: r(\pi)$, the **Bayes value**. The posterior $\pi(\cdot \mid x)$ obtained by conditioning $\pi$ on $X = x$ (the Bayesian update of [26.07.01]) gives the **posterior expected loss** $\rho(\pi, a \mid x) = \int_\Theta L(\theta, a)\, d\pi(\theta \mid x)$.

**Definition (randomized rule and the risk set).** A **randomized rule** $\delta$ chooses an action by drawing from a distribution $\delta(\cdot \mid x)$ on $\mathcal{A}$ after seeing $x$; its risk is $R(\theta, \delta) = \int_{\mathcal{X}}\int_{\mathcal{A}} L(\theta, a)\, \delta(da \mid x)\, dP_\theta(x)$. When $\Theta = \{\theta_1, \dots, \theta_k\}$ is finite, the **risk set** is
$$
S \;=\; \{(R(\theta_1, \delta), \dots, R(\theta_k, \delta)) : \delta \text{ a randomized rule}\} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^k,
$$
a convex subset of $\mathbb{R}^k$ in the sense of [44.01.01]: convexity comes from mixing rules, since the $1/2$-$1/2$ random choice between $\delta'$ and $\delta''$ has risk vector the midpoint of theirs.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **Admissible does not mean good.** The constant rule $\delta(x) \equiv c$ (ignore the data, always estimate $c$) is admissible under squared-error loss on $\Theta = \mathbb{R}$: its risk is $0$ at $\theta = c$, so no competitor can be uniformly no-worse without also being $0$ there, which pins it down. A useless rule can be admissible.
- **Minimax is not unique and need not be Bayes for an obvious prior.** Several rules can share the minimax value; the minimax rule is the Bayes rule for a *least favourable* prior, which is often supported on the boundary of $\Theta$, not the prior a practitioner would write down.
- **A Bayes rule can be randomized only on a null set.** When the posterior expected loss has a unique minimiser for almost every $x$, the Bayes rule is essentially non-randomized; randomization buys nothing under strict convexity of the loss. Randomization matters for minimaxity in finite-$\Theta$ problems, not for typical Bayes estimation.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The organising theorem is that the Bayes rule is computed pointwise by minimising posterior expected loss, and that Bayes rules with finite Bayes risk are almost the cheapest possible certificate of admissibility.

**Theorem (Bayes rule via posterior expected loss; admissibility of Bayes rules).** *Fix a prior $\pi$ with finite Bayes value $r(\pi) < \infty$.*

*(i) (Posterior characterization.) Suppose the joint law of $(\theta, X)$ admits the disintegration $d\pi(\theta)\, dP_\theta(x) = dm(x)\, d\pi(\theta \mid x)$ with marginal $m$. If for $m$-almost every $x$ the action $\delta_\pi(x)$ minimises the posterior expected loss $a \mapsto \rho(\pi, a \mid x) = \int_\Theta L(\theta, a)\, d\pi(\theta \mid x)$, then $\delta_\pi$ is a Bayes rule.*

*(ii) (Admissibility.) If the Bayes rule $\delta_\pi$ is unique up to risk equivalence — any other Bayes rule has the same risk function — then $\delta_\pi$ is admissible.*

**Proof of (i).** For any rule $\delta$, Tonelli's theorem (the integrand is non-negative) lets the Bayes risk be reordered:
$$
r(\pi, \delta) = \int_\Theta \int_{\mathcal{X}} L(\theta, \delta(x))\, dP_\theta(x)\, d\pi(\theta) = \int_{\mathcal{X}} \int_\Theta L(\theta, \delta(x))\, d\pi(\theta \mid x)\, dm(x) = \int_{\mathcal{X}} \rho(\pi, \delta(x) \mid x)\, dm(x).
$$
The Bayes risk is thus the $m$-integral of the posterior expected loss evaluated at the chosen action. To minimise the integral it suffices to minimise the integrand $x$ by $x$, which is what $\delta_\pi$ does by hypothesis. Hence $r(\pi, \delta_\pi) \leq r(\pi, \delta)$ for every $\delta$, so $\delta_\pi$ attains $r(\pi)$ and is a Bayes rule. $\square$

**Proof of (ii).** Suppose $\delta_\pi$ is inadmissible. Then some $\delta'$ dominates it: $R(\theta, \delta') \leq R(\theta, \delta_\pi)$ for all $\theta$, strictly for some $\theta_0$. Integrating against $\pi$,
$$
r(\pi, \delta') = \int_\Theta R(\theta, \delta')\, d\pi(\theta) \leq \int_\Theta R(\theta, \delta_\pi)\, d\pi(\theta) = r(\pi).
$$
Because $r(\pi)$ is the minimum Bayes risk, the reverse inequality $r(\pi, \delta') \geq r(\pi)$ also holds, so $r(\pi, \delta') = r(\pi)$ and $\delta'$ is itself a Bayes rule for $\pi$. By the uniqueness hypothesis $\delta'$ has the same risk function as $\delta_\pi$, contradicting the strict inequality at $\theta_0$. Hence no dominating $\delta'$ exists and $\delta_\pi$ is admissible. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem **builds toward** the complete-class theory and the minimax constructions of the Advanced section, and **appears again in** the Rao-Blackwell improvement [45.01.02] and the Bayes-optimality of the Neyman-Pearson test [45.02.01]. **This is exactly** the device that converts a global optimisation over rules into a pointwise optimisation over actions, paid for by the prior, and it **generalises** the elementary posterior-mean estimator of [26.07.01] to an arbitrary loss: under squared error the posterior-expected-loss minimiser is the posterior mean, under absolute error the posterior median, under 0-1 loss the posterior mode. **The foundational reason** Bayes rules are the workhorse is that uniqueness upgrades them to admissible rules, so the Bayes construction simultaneously delivers optimality-on-average and the no-dominance guarantee. **Putting these together** with the convexity of the risk set, the minimax rule turns out to be **dual to** a least-favourable prior — the same prior-averaging integral, now read as a saddle point — which is the connection the Advanced section makes precise via the separating-hyperplane geometry of [44.01.01].

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

For squared-error loss, show that the risk decomposes as $R(\theta, \delta) = \operatorname{Var}_\theta(\delta(X)) + b_\theta(\delta)^2$ where $b_\theta(\delta) = \mathbb{E}_\theta\delta(X) - \theta$ is the bias.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Insert and subtract $\mathbb{E}_\theta\delta(X)$ inside $(\theta - \delta(X))^2$ and expand the cross term.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $\delta - \theta = (\delta - \mathbb{E}_\theta\delta) + (\mathbb{E}_\theta\delta - \theta)$. Squaring and taking $\mathbb{E}_\theta$, the cross term $2(\mathbb{E}_\theta\delta - \theta)\,\mathbb{E}_\theta(\delta - \mathbb{E}_\theta\delta)$ vanishes because $\mathbb{E}_\theta(\delta - \mathbb{E}_\theta\delta) = 0$. The two surviving terms are $\mathbb{E}_\theta(\delta - \mathbb{E}_\theta\delta)^2 = \operatorname{Var}_\theta(\delta)$ and $(\mathbb{E}_\theta\delta - \theta)^2 = b_\theta(\delta)^2$. So $R(\theta, \delta) = \operatorname{Var}_\theta(\delta) + b_\theta(\delta)^2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Let $X \mid \theta \sim N(\theta, \sigma^2)$ with a conjugate prior $\theta \sim N(\mu_0, \tau^2)$. Under squared-error loss, derive the Bayes rule.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The posterior-expected-loss minimiser for squared error is the posterior mean; recall the normal-normal posterior.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The posterior is normal with mean a precision-weighted average,
$$
\delta_\pi(x) = \mathbb{E}[\theta \mid x] = \frac{\sigma^{-2} x + \tau^{-2}\mu_0}{\sigma^{-2} + \tau^{-2}} = \frac{\tau^2}{\sigma^2 + \tau^2}\, x + \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 + \tau^2}\,\mu_0.
$$
By part (i) of the Key theorem, the squared-error Bayes rule is the posterior mean, so this is the Bayes estimator. It shrinks the data $x$ toward the prior mean $\mu_0$ by the factor $\sigma^2/(\sigma^2 + \tau^2)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

For estimating a binomial proportion $p$ from $X \sim \operatorname{Bin}(n, p)$ under squared-error loss, show that the rule $\delta(X) = (X + \sqrt{n}/2)/(n + \sqrt{n})$ has constant risk, and explain why constant risk is the route to minimaxity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

This is the Bayes rule for a $\operatorname{Beta}(\sqrt{n}/2, \sqrt{n}/2)$ prior; compute its mean squared error and check the $p$-dependence cancels.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $\delta = (X + a)/(n + 2a)$ with $a = \sqrt{n}/2$. Its bias is $\mathbb{E}_p\delta - p = (np + a)/(n + 2a) - p = a(1 - 2p)/(n + 2a)$ and its variance is $np(1-p)/(n+2a)^2$. The MSE is
$$
R(p, \delta) = \frac{np(1-p) + a^2(1-2p)^2}{(n + 2a)^2}.
$$
Expanding the numerator, $np(1-p) + a^2(1 - 4p + 4p^2) = (4a^2 - n)p^2 + (n - 4a^2)p + a^2$. With $a = \sqrt n/2$, $4a^2 = n$, so the $p^2$ and $p$ coefficients vanish and the numerator is the constant $a^2 = n/4$. Thus $R(p,\delta) = (n/4)/(n + \sqrt n)^2$, independent of $p$. A rule with constant risk that is also Bayes for some prior is minimax (Proposition 2 in the Full proof set): a flat risk equals its own maximum, and the Bayes property forbids any uniform improvement.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Under absolute-error loss $L(\theta, a) = |\theta - a|$, show the Bayes rule is the posterior median.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Minimise $\rho(a) = \int |\theta - a|\, d\pi(\theta \mid x)$ in $a$; differentiate using $\tfrac{d}{da}|\theta - a| = \operatorname{sgn}(a - \theta)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The posterior expected loss is $\rho(a) = \int_{\theta < a}(a - \theta)\,d\pi(\theta\mid x) + \int_{\theta > a}(\theta - a)\,d\pi(\theta\mid x)$. Differentiating in $a$,
$$
\rho'(a) = \pi(\theta < a \mid x) - \pi(\theta > a \mid x) = 2\,\pi(\theta < a \mid x) - 1 + \pi(\theta = a \mid x).
$$
Setting $\rho'(a) = 0$ gives $\pi(\theta \leq a \mid x) \geq \tfrac12$ and $\pi(\theta \geq a\mid x) \geq \tfrac12$, the definition of a posterior median. Convexity of $|\cdot|$ makes the stationary point a global minimum, so by the Key theorem the median minimises Bayes risk.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that under 0-1 loss on a finite action set, $L(\theta, a) = \mathbf{1}\{a \neq d(\theta)\}$ for a correct-action map $d$, the Bayes rule chooses the action of maximum posterior probability (the posterior mode of the correct action).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The posterior expected loss of action $a$ is $1$ minus the posterior probability that $a$ is correct.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For action $a$, $\rho(a \mid x) = \int_\Theta \mathbf{1}\{a \neq d(\theta)\}\,d\pi(\theta\mid x) = \pi(d(\theta) \neq a \mid x) = 1 - \pi(d(\theta) = a \mid x)$. Minimising $\rho(a\mid x)$ over $a$ is maximising $\pi(d(\theta) = a \mid x)$, the posterior probability that $a$ is the correct action. So the Bayes rule reports the maximum-a-posteriori action. In a $k$-hypothesis testing problem this is the test that picks the hypothesis of highest posterior probability.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that if a rule $\delta_0$ has constant risk and is admissible, then it is minimax.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Suppose a competitor has smaller maximum risk; compare it to $\delta_0$ pointwise using the constant-risk value.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $R(\theta, \delta_0) = c$ for all $\theta$, so $\sup_\theta R(\theta, \delta_0) = c$. Suppose $\delta_0$ is not minimax: some $\delta'$ has $\sup_\theta R(\theta, \delta') < c$. Then $R(\theta, \delta') < c = R(\theta, \delta_0)$ for every $\theta$ — a strict uniform improvement, so $\delta'$ dominates $\delta_0$ and $\delta_0$ is inadmissible, contradicting the hypothesis. Hence $\sup_\theta R(\theta, \delta') \geq c$ for every $\delta'$, so $\delta_0$ achieves the minimax value $c$ and is minimax. (This is the standard route: exhibit an admissible equalizer rule.)

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $\Theta = \{\theta_1, \theta_2\}$. Show that the minimax value equals $\sup_\pi r(\pi)$ over priors $\pi = (\pi_1, \pi_2)$ on the two points, and interpret the maximiser as a least favourable prior, using the convexity of the risk set.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A prior is a point on the line $\pi_1 + \pi_2 = 1$; the Bayes value is the lowest value of $\pi_1 r_1 + \pi_2 r_2$ over the convex risk set $S$. Apply a separating-hyperplane / minimax argument.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The risk set $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ of vectors $(R(\theta_1,\delta), R(\theta_2,\delta))$ is convex and closed-from-below (lower boundary is its admissible frontier). For a prior $\pi$, $r(\pi) = \inf_{s \in S}(\pi_1 s_1 + \pi_2 s_2)$ is the lowest level a line of slope $-\pi_1/\pi_2$ touches $S$, i.e. a supporting line of $S$ — the convex-set support function of [44.01.01]. The minimax value is $\inf_\delta \max(s_1, s_2)$, the lowest corner of the smallest down-set square containing a point of $S$. Sliding a supporting line until it touches $S$ at a point on the diagonal $s_1 = s_2$ shows $\inf_\delta\max(s_1,s_2) = \sup_\pi r(\pi)$, with the touching prior the least favourable one and the touching rule both Bayes-for-$\pi$ and minimax. This is the two-point case of the minimax theorem; the supporting line is the separating hyperplane of [44.01.01].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that a Bayes rule with respect to a prior $\pi$ that has full support and a strictly convex loss is admissible, without assuming uniqueness, provided risk functions are continuous in $\theta$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If a dominating $\delta'$ improves strictly at $\theta_0$, continuity spreads the improvement to a neighbourhood that carries positive prior mass.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $\delta'$ dominates the Bayes rule $\delta_\pi$, with $R(\theta_0, \delta') < R(\theta_0, \delta_\pi)$ and $R(\theta,\delta') \leq R(\theta,\delta_\pi)$ elsewhere. By continuity of both risk functions there is an open neighbourhood $U \ni \theta_0$ on which $R(\theta, \delta') \leq R(\theta,\delta_\pi) - \varepsilon$ for some $\varepsilon > 0$. Since $\pi$ has full support, $\pi(U) > 0$, so
$$
r(\pi, \delta') = \int R(\theta,\delta')\,d\pi \leq \int R(\theta,\delta_\pi)\,d\pi - \varepsilon\,\pi(U) = r(\pi) - \varepsilon\,\pi(U) < r(\pi),
$$
contradicting that $r(\pi)$ is the minimum Bayes risk. Hence $\delta_\pi$ is admissible. Strict convexity of the loss is what made $\delta_\pi$ a genuine (essentially unique pointwise) minimiser so that $r(\pi)$ is attained; full support is what converts a local strict improvement into a global Bayes-risk improvement.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The geometry of the risk set and admissibility

Take $\Theta = \{\theta_1, \dots, \theta_k\}$ finite and let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^k$ be the risk set of all randomized rules, a convex set by mixing [44.01.01]. Three principles become three geometric operations on $S$. A rule is **admissible** if and only if its risk vector lies on the **lower-left boundary** of $S$: the set of points $s \in S$ for which no other point of $S$ is coordinatewise $\leq s$ with a strict inequality, the Pareto frontier of $S$. A rule is **Bayes** for $\pi$ if and only if its risk vector minimises the linear functional $\langle \pi, \cdot\rangle$ over $S$, that is, $S$ has a supporting hyperplane with outer normal $\pi$ at that point; as $\pi$ ranges over the simplex, these supporting points trace out the closed convex lower boundary. A rule is **minimax** if and only if its risk vector minimises $\max_i s_i$, the lowest corner of the smallest down-translate of the positive orthant meeting $S$. The first identification shows that admissible rules are exactly the exposed and supporting points of the lower boundary; the second that every Bayes rule is a supporting point and hence admissible whenever the support is strict; the third that the minimax point sits where the diagonal $s_1 = \cdots = s_k$ meets the lower boundary, which is generically a Bayes point [ref: TODO_REF Ferguson §2].

### The minimax theorem and least favourable priors

The minimax-Bayes duality is the statistical face of von Neumann's minimax theorem applied to the loss game between Nature (choosing $\theta$, or a prior $\pi$) and the statistician (choosing $\delta$). Under conditions guaranteeing a saddle point — compactness of the (randomized) decision space and risk-set convexity — the value of the game exists:
$$
\inf_\delta \sup_\pi r(\pi, \delta) = \sup_\pi \inf_\delta r(\pi, \delta), \qquad \text{i.e.}\quad \inf_\delta\sup_\theta R(\theta,\delta) = \sup_\pi r(\pi).
$$
A prior $\pi^*$ attaining the right side is **least favourable**: it is the prior under which the best achievable Bayes risk is largest, and the Bayes rule against $\pi^*$ is minimax [ref: TODO_REF Berger §5.2]. The standard production recipe inverts this: guess an equalizer rule $\delta_0$ with constant risk, exhibit a prior $\pi^*$ for which $\delta_0$ is Bayes, and conclude $\delta_0$ is minimax — the constant-risk and Bayes conditions together forcing the saddle. When no proper least favourable prior exists, one uses a **least favourable sequence** $\pi_n$ with $r(\pi_n) \to \sup_\theta R(\theta, \delta_0)$, the device by which the sample mean $\bar X$ is shown minimax for the normal mean via priors $N(0, \tau^2)$ with $\tau \to \infty$ [ref: TODO_REF Lehmann & Casella §5.2].

### Stein's phenomenon: admissibility is dimension-sensitive

The sample mean $\bar X$ is admissible as an estimator of a normal mean in dimensions one and two under squared-error loss, but Charles Stein proved in 1956 that for $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^p$ with $p \geq 3$ the estimator $\delta_0(X) = X$ is **inadmissible** [ref: TODO_REF Stein 1956]: the James-Stein estimator $\delta_{JS}(X) = (1 - (p-2)/\|X\|^2)X$ dominates it everywhere, shrinking toward the origin. The risk improvement is computed via Stein's unbiased risk identity $\mathbb{E}_\theta\|\delta(X) - \theta\|^2 = p + \mathbb{E}_\theta[2\,\nabla\cdot g(X) + \|g(X)\|^2]$ for $\delta(X) = X + g(X)$ [ref: TODO_REF Lehmann & Casella §5.5]. The phenomenon shows admissibility is not a local property of a single coordinate: borrowing strength across coordinates strictly reduces total risk despite each coordinate's estimate becoming biased. James-Stein is itself inadmissible (its positive-part truncation dominates it), so the example also illustrates that exhibiting a dominator does not produce an admissible terminus.

### Complete class theorems

A class $\mathcal{C}$ of rules is **complete** if for every rule $\delta \notin \mathcal{C}$ there is a $\delta' \in \mathcal{C}$ dominating it, and **essentially complete** if the dominating $\delta'$ is merely no-worse. The structural payoff is that one may restrict the search for good rules to a complete class without loss. The central results, due to Wald and refined by Stein, Le Cam, and Brown, state that under convexity of the loss and mild regularity the **Bayes rules and their limits form a complete class**, and conversely every admissible rule is a (generalized or limiting) Bayes rule [ref: TODO_REF Berger §8]. For finite $\Theta$ the closed lower boundary of the convex risk set $S$ is the minimal complete class, and its supporting hyperplanes are exactly the priors, so "admissible," "supporting point of $S$," and "Bayes or limit of Bayes" coincide. The complete-class theorems are the converse direction of the admissibility-of-Bayes-rules result: not only is every (unique) Bayes rule admissible, but every admissible rule is, in the limit, Bayes.

**Synthesis.** **The central insight** of decision theory is that risk turns the choice of a statistical procedure into a partial order — dominance — whose maximal elements (admissible rules) are **exactly** the supporting points of the convex risk set, and the three classical principles are three ways of selecting among them. **The foundational reason** Bayes rules dominate the theory is the posterior-expected-loss characterization: it makes the Bayes rule computable pointwise, makes unique Bayes rules admissible, and — through the complete-class theorems — makes *every* admissible rule a limit of Bayes rules, so the Bayes construction **generalises** from one optimality principle into the generating mechanism for the whole admissible frontier. **Putting these together**, the minimax rule **is dual to** a least favourable prior in the literal saddle-point sense, the minimax value equalling $\sup_\pi r(\pi)$, which **is exactly** von Neumann's game value read on the loss matrix and supported by the separating-hyperplane geometry of [44.01.01]. The Stein phenomenon shows the order is genuinely global and dimension-sensitive, and the entire apparatus **appears again in** estimation [45.01.02], testing [45.02.01], and the Bayesian inference of [26.07.01], where these same objects — loss, risk, prior, posterior — recur as the unifying language. **The bridge is** the convex risk set: feasibility, optimality, admissibility, the Bayes-minimax duality, and the complete-class theorems are one theory about supporting hyperplanes of a single convex object.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (the risk set is convex; Bayes points support it).** *For finite $\Theta = \{\theta_1,\dots,\theta_k\}$, the risk set $S = \{R(\delta) := (R(\theta_i,\delta))_i : \delta \text{ randomized}\}$ is convex, and $\delta$ is Bayes for $\pi$ in the simplex if and only if $\langle\pi, R(\delta)\rangle = \min_{s\in S}\langle\pi, s\rangle$.*

*Proof.* Given rules $\delta', \delta''$ and $\alpha \in [0,1]$, the randomized rule that uses $\delta'$ with probability $\alpha$ and $\delta''$ with probability $1-\alpha$ (an independent coin flip before seeing data) has risk $R(\theta_i, \cdot) = \alpha R(\theta_i,\delta') + (1-\alpha)R(\theta_i,\delta'')$ at each $\theta_i$ by linearity of expectation, so $\alpha R(\delta') + (1-\alpha)R(\delta'') = R(\text{mixture}) \in S$; hence $S$ is convex (the mixing operation of [44.01.01]). For the Bayes characterization, $r(\pi,\delta) = \sum_i \pi_i R(\theta_i,\delta) = \langle\pi, R(\delta)\rangle$, so minimising Bayes risk over $\delta$ is minimising the linear functional $\langle\pi,\cdot\rangle$ over $S$. The minimiser is a point at which the hyperplane $\{s : \langle\pi,s\rangle = r(\pi)\}$ supports $S$ from below. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (a Bayes rule with constant risk is minimax).** *If $\delta_0$ is Bayes for some prior $\pi$ and $R(\theta, \delta_0) = c$ is constant in $\theta$, then $\delta_0$ is minimax and $\pi$ is least favourable.*

*Proof.* For any rule $\delta$, since $\delta_0$ is Bayes, $r(\pi,\delta_0) \leq r(\pi,\delta)$. The left side is $\int R(\theta,\delta_0)\,d\pi = c$. The right side is a prior average and so cannot exceed the maximum risk: $r(\pi,\delta) = \int R(\theta,\delta)\,d\pi \leq \sup_\theta R(\theta,\delta)$. Chaining, $c \leq \sup_\theta R(\theta,\delta)$ for every $\delta$. Taking the infimum over $\delta$, $c \leq \inf_\delta\sup_\theta R(\theta,\delta) = \bar V$. But $\delta_0$ itself has $\sup_\theta R(\theta,\delta_0) = c$, so $\bar V \leq c$. Hence $\bar V = c = \sup_\theta R(\theta,\delta_0)$ and $\delta_0$ is minimax. Moreover $\sup_{\pi'} r(\pi') \geq r(\pi) = c = \bar V \geq \sup_{\pi'} r(\pi')$ (the last step is weak duality $r(\pi') \leq \bar V$ for every prior), so $\pi$ attains $\sup_{\pi'} r(\pi')$ and is least favourable. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (weak duality: every Bayes value is below the minimax value).** *For every prior $\pi$, $r(\pi) \leq \bar V = \inf_\delta\sup_\theta R(\theta,\delta)$.*

*Proof.* For any fixed $\delta$, $r(\pi,\delta) = \int R(\theta,\delta)\,d\pi \leq \sup_\theta R(\theta,\delta)$ because an average over $\pi$ is dominated by the supremum. Taking the infimum over $\delta$ on both sides, $r(\pi) = \inf_\delta r(\pi,\delta) \leq \inf_\delta \sup_\theta R(\theta,\delta) = \bar V$. (This is the max-min inequality $\sup_\pi\inf_\delta r \leq \inf_\delta\sup_\theta R$ specialized; equality is the minimax theorem, the saddle realized by a least favourable $\pi^*$ and its Bayes rule.) $\square$

**Proposition 4 (admissibility of a unique Bayes rule).** *If $\delta_\pi$ is Bayes for $\pi$ with $r(\pi) < \infty$ and every Bayes rule for $\pi$ has the same risk function as $\delta_\pi$, then $\delta_\pi$ is admissible.*

*Proof.* This is part (ii) of the Key theorem, reproduced for the proof set. If $\delta'$ dominated $\delta_\pi$, integrating the dominance inequality $R(\theta,\delta')\leq R(\theta,\delta_\pi)$ against $\pi$ gives $r(\pi,\delta')\leq r(\pi) $, hence $r(\pi,\delta')=r(\pi)$ since $r(\pi)$ is minimal, so $\delta'$ is also Bayes for $\pi$. By the uniqueness hypothesis $R(\cdot,\delta')=R(\cdot,\delta_\pi)$, contradicting the strict inequality that domination requires at some $\theta_0$. So no dominator exists. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Unbiased estimation and the Rao-Blackwell / Lehmann-Scheffé theorems [45.01.02] are the squared-error specialization of this framework: conditioning an estimator on a sufficient statistic is a convexity-of-loss argument that lowers risk, the same risk and dominance order defined here, and the minimum-variance-unbiased estimator is the admissible representative inside the unbiased class.

- Hypothesis testing and the Neyman-Pearson lemma [45.02.01] are the two-action decision problem under 0-1 (or weighted 0-1) loss; the most powerful test is the Bayes rule of Exercise 5 for the corresponding prior, and the admissibility and complete-class structure of tests is the same risk-set geometry, now over the power/size plane.

- Bayesian inference [26.07.01] supplies the prior, posterior, and posterior-expected-loss machinery that the Key theorem turns into the Bayes rule; this unit is the decision-theoretic completion of that survey, replacing "report the posterior" with "choose the action minimising posterior expected loss for a stated loss function."

- The convex-set and supporting-hyperplane calculus of [44.01.01] is the geometric engine: the risk set is convex by rule-mixing, Bayes rules are its supporting points, admissible rules are its lower boundary, and the minimax-Bayes duality is the separating-hyperplane / saddle-point structure read on this single convex object, exactly as Lagrangian duality reads it on the value set.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The decision-theoretic reframing of statistics is due to Abraham Wald, whose *Statistical Decision Functions* [ref: TODO_REF Wald 1950] (Wiley, 1950) introduced the loss function, the risk function, randomized decision rules, and the complete-class program, casting estimation and testing as special cases of a single game against Nature; the minimax principle and the proof that Bayes and limiting-Bayes rules form a complete class are Wald's. The game-theoretic substrate traces to John von Neumann's 1928 minimax theorem for zero-sum games, which supplies the saddle-point existence behind the minimax-Bayes duality, and the decision-game reading was sharpened by Wald and by L. J. Savage's subjective-expected-utility foundations in *The Foundations of Statistics* (1954).

Charles Stein's 1956 Berkeley Symposium paper [ref: TODO_REF Stein 1956] (Proc. Third Berkeley Symposium 1, 197-206) proved the inadmissibility of the sample mean in three or more dimensions, a result so counter to intuition that it reoriented the field's understanding of admissibility; the explicit dominating estimator was given by James and Stein in 1961. The systematic theory of admissibility and complete classes was developed through the 1950s-1970s by Stein, Lucien Le Cam, and Lawrence Brown, whose differential-equation characterization of admissibility for the normal mean closed the dimension-one and -two admissibility questions. Thomas Ferguson's *Mathematical Statistics: A Decision Theoretic Approach* [ref: TODO_REF Ferguson §2] (1967) gave the geometric risk-set exposition, and James Berger's *Statistical Decision Theory and Bayesian Analysis* [ref: TODO_REF Berger §4-§5] (1985) and Lehmann and Casella's *Theory of Point Estimation* [ref: TODO_REF Lehmann & Casella §5] (1998) are the standard modern references, the latter integrating the decision framework with the classical optimality theory of estimation.
