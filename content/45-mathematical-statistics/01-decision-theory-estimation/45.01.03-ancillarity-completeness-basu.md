## Intuition [Beginner]

You measure the heights of a small group of people to learn the average height in a population. Two numbers fall out of the data: the sample average, which tracks the unknown population average, and the spread of the heights around their own average. The spread is interesting. If you slid every height up by ten centimetres — pretending the whole population were taller — the average would move, but the spread would stay exactly the same. The spread does not know where the population average sits.

A summary whose distribution does not move when the unknown quantity moves is called an *ancillary* statistic. It is a quantity the experiment produces that carries no direct information about the thing you are chasing. The spread of heights around their own average is ancillary to the population average: it is pure scatter, blind to the level.

This sounds useless, but it is the opposite. Once you have squeezed all the information about the unknown into one faithful summary — a sufficient statistic, the idea from the previous unit — the leftover ancillary scatter ought to be unrelated to that summary. If the summary already holds every drop of information, the ancillary noise can have nothing to say about it.

Basu's theorem makes that hunch a fact, under one extra condition called *completeness*. Completeness says the faithful summary is not carrying any hidden quantity that secretly averages to zero — there is no redundant direction in it. When a summary is both faithful (sufficient) and non-redundant (complete), Basu's theorem guarantees it is *statistically independent* of every ancillary quantity. The payoff is concrete: you can prove that the sample average and the sample spread are independent for normal data without computing a single integral, just by naming one as complete-sufficient and the other as ancillary.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the data as producing two dials. The left dial is the *informative* summary — for height data, the sample average — and its needle swings as the unknown population average changes. The right dial is the *ancillary* summary — the spread around the sample average — and its needle's behaviour is frozen with respect to that unknown: shifting everyone's height up by the same amount leaves the right dial's whole pattern of wobble unchanged.

Basu's theorem is the claim that, when the left dial is faithful and non-redundant, the two needles wobble independently: knowing the right dial's reading tells you nothing about the left dial's reading, and the other way around.

| Summary | Moves with the unknown level? | Role |
|---|---|---|
| Sample average | yes — its whole distribution shifts | informative (sufficient) |
| Spread around the average | no — distribution is frozen in the level | ancillary |
| The two together | independent (Basu), once the average is complete | the punchline |

![Two dials side by side: a left dial labelled sample average whose needle and distribution shift when the unknown population level changes, and a right dial labelled spread whose distribution is frozen against that level; a double-headed arrow between them is crossed out to show the two are statistically independent under Basu's theorem.](/img/ancillarity-completeness-basu-placeholder.svg)

The picture is the whole lesson: a frozen-against-the-unknown dial (ancillary) and a faithful non-redundant dial (complete sufficient) turn independently of each other.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We check independence on the smallest informative case. Take two independent draws $X_1, X_2$ from a population centred at an unknown level $\theta$, where each draw equals $\theta$ plus a fair coin's worth of noise: the noise is $+1$ or $-1$, each with chance one half. So each $X_i$ is $\theta + 1$ or $\theta - 1$.

Form two summaries: the average $M = (X_1 + X_2)/2$ and the gap $D = X_1 - X_2$.

Step 1. The gap is ancillary. Compute $D = X_1 - X_2 = (\theta + \text{noise}_1) - (\theta + \text{noise}_2)$; the two $\theta$'s cancel, so $D = \text{noise}_1 - \text{noise}_2$, which is $-2$, $0$, or $+2$ with chances $1/4$, $1/2$, $1/4$. None of these chances mention $\theta$: the gap's distribution is frozen against the level, so $D$ is ancillary.

Step 2. List the four equally likely outcomes. Each noise pair $(\pm 1, \pm 1)$ has chance $1/4$:

| $(\text{noise}_1, \text{noise}_2)$ | $M = \theta + (\text{noise}_1+\text{noise}_2)/2$ | $D$ |
|---|---|---|
| $(+1,+1)$ | $\theta + 1$ | $0$ |
| $(+1,-1)$ | $\theta$ | $+2$ |
| $(-1,+1)$ | $\theta$ | $-2$ |
| $(-1,-1)$ | $\theta - 1$ | $0$ |

Step 3. Check independence directly. The event $D = 0$ has chance $1/2$ (two of four rows). Given $D = 0$, the average $M$ is $\theta + 1$ or $\theta - 1$, each with chance one half. Given $D = +2$, the average is exactly $\theta$. So the value of $D$ tells you which values $M$ can take — wait, that looks like dependence. Recompute the joint table honestly: the pair $(M, D)$ takes four distinct values $(\theta+1, 0)$, $(\theta, 2)$, $(\theta, -2)$, $(\theta-1, 0)$, each with chance $1/4$. Here $M$ and $D$ are in fact dependent, because this discrete model has a complete sufficient statistic only after more structure is added.

What this tells us: ancillarity of $D$ is real, but independence needs the *completeness* of the informative summary, which this bare two-point model lacks. The clean independence appears for the normal model, worked in full at the Intermediate tier, where the sample mean is complete sufficient and Basu's theorem applies without computation.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a measurable space $(\mathcal{X}, \mathcal{A})$ and a statistical model $\mathcal{P} = \{P_\theta : \theta \in \Theta\}$ of probability measures on it, in the sense of [45.01.02]. Probability spaces, laws, and independence are taken from [37.01.01]; the expectation $\mathbb{E}_\theta[\,\cdot\,]$ is the integral against $P_\theta$ of [26.03.01]. A *statistic* is a measurable map $T : \mathcal{X} \to (\mathcal{T}, \mathcal{B})$, with law $P_\theta^T = T_* P_\theta$.

**Definition (ancillary statistic).** A statistic $A : \mathcal{X} \to (\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{C})$ is *ancillary* for $\mathcal{P}$ if its law $P_\theta^A = A_* P_\theta$ is the same for every $\theta \in \Theta$. It is *first-order ancillary* if only $\mathbb{E}_\theta[A]$ is constant in $\theta$. An ancillary statistic carries no marginal information about $\theta$; its interest is conditional, through Fisher's *conditionality principle*: inference about $\theta$ should be carried out in the conditional model given the observed value of an ancillary configuration statistic, because the ancillary indexes which subexperiment was effectively run [ref: TODO_REF Fisher 1934].

**Definition (complete statistic).** A statistic $T$ is *complete* for $\mathcal{P}$ if, for every measurable $g : \mathcal{T} \to \mathbb{R}$ with $\mathbb{E}_\theta[g(T)]$ defined for all $\theta$,
$$
\mathbb{E}_\theta[g(T)] = 0 \ \text{ for all } \theta \in \Theta
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
g(T) = 0 \ \ P_\theta\text{-a.s. for all } \theta.
$$
Equivalently, the family of laws $\{P_\theta^T\}$ admits no non-zero unbiased estimator of zero. The statistic is *bounded complete* if the implication holds for every *bounded* measurable $g$. Completeness is a property of the family $\{P_\theta^T\}$, not of a single law; it asserts the family is rich enough that no non-zero function of $T$ can average to zero everywhere — there is no redundant direction in $T$.

**Definition (independence of statistics).** Statistics $T$ and $A$ are *independent under $P_\theta$* if $\sigma(T)$ and $\sigma(A)$ are independent $\sigma$-fields under $P_\theta$, i.e. $P_\theta(T \in B,\, A \in C) = P_\theta(T \in B)\,P_\theta(A \in C)$ for all $B \in \mathcal{B}$, $C \in \mathcal{C}$, in the sense of [37.01.01]. Basu's theorem concerns the case where this holds simultaneously for every $\theta$.

The three notions interlock through *minimal sufficiency* of [45.01.02]. A minimal sufficient statistic is the coarsest faithful summary; an ancillary statistic is, informally, a "complementary" direction carrying no information about $\theta$. A complete sufficient statistic is automatically minimal sufficient (proved below), so completeness is a strengthening of minimality.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- **Sufficient does not imply complete.** For an i.i.d. normal sample with *known* variance, $\bar X$ is complete sufficient; but in the location-only uniform family $\mathrm{Unif}(\theta - 1/2, \theta + 1/2)$ the minimal sufficient statistic $(\min_i X_i, \max_i X_i)$ is *not* complete, because the range $\max_i X_i - \min_i X_i$ has $\theta$-free mean and so $g = \text{range} - \mathbb{E}[\text{range}]$ is a non-zero unbiased estimator of zero.

- **Ancillary is not "uninformative once conditioned".** An ancillary statistic has constant *marginal* law but can be highly informative about the *precision* of an estimator. In a regression with random design, the design matrix is ancillary for the slope yet determines the conditional variance of the least-squares estimator — the heart of the conditionality principle.

- **Completeness is needed for Basu, not just sufficiency.** Independence of a sufficient statistic from an ancillary can fail when the sufficient statistic is not complete; the discrete two-point model of the Beginner worked example shows the dependence directly. The completeness hypothesis is not decorative.

- **Bounded completeness is strictly weaker.** There are families that are boundedly complete but not complete; bounded completeness already suffices for the similar-tests application (Lehmann-Romano §4.3) and for many uses of Basu's theorem restricted to bounded functions [ref: TODO_REF Lehmann Romano 2005].

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The organising result is Basu's theorem. We state it for a complete sufficient statistic and prove it from the two defining properties; the proof is the cleanest illustration of why completeness is exactly the missing ingredient.

**Theorem (Basu, 1955).** *Let $\mathcal{P} = \{P_\theta : \theta \in \Theta\}$ be a statistical model, let $T$ be a sufficient statistic that is also complete for $\mathcal{P}$, and let $A$ be an ancillary statistic for $\mathcal{P}$. Then $T$ and $A$ are independent under $P_\theta$ for every $\theta \in \Theta$.*

**Proof.** It suffices to show that for every measurable set $C$ in the range of $A$,
$$
P_\theta(A \in C \mid T) = P_\theta(A \in C) \qquad P_\theta\text{-a.s., for every } \theta,
$$
because the right side then does not depend on $T$, giving independence of $\sigma(T)$ and $\sigma(A)$.

Fix a measurable set $C$. Define two functions. First, the unconditional probability
$$
p_C := P_\theta(A \in C).
$$
Because $A$ is *ancillary*, $p_C$ does not depend on $\theta$: it is a fixed number.

Second, the conditional probability given $T$. Because $T$ is *sufficient*, the conditional distribution of the data given $T$ does not depend on $\theta$, so a version of
$$
\psi(T) := P_\theta(A \in C \mid T) = \mathbb{E}_\theta[\mathbf{1}\{A \in C\} \mid T]
$$
may be chosen as a single function of $T$ that is free of $\theta$. (This is the defining property of sufficiency from [45.01.02]: the conditional law of the sample, hence of any function $\mathbf{1}\{A \in C\}$ of it, given $T$ is parameter-free.)

Now consider the difference $g(T) := \psi(T) - p_C$, a fixed measurable function of $T$. Take its expectation under $P_\theta$, using the tower property:
$$
\mathbb{E}_\theta[g(T)] = \mathbb{E}_\theta[\psi(T)] - p_C = \mathbb{E}_\theta\big[\,\mathbb{E}_\theta[\mathbf{1}\{A \in C\} \mid T]\,\big] - p_C = P_\theta(A \in C) - p_C = p_C - p_C = 0.
$$
This holds for *every* $\theta \in \Theta$. Since $T$ is *complete* and $g$ is a function of $T$ with $\mathbb{E}_\theta[g(T)] = 0$ for all $\theta$, completeness forces
$$
g(T) = 0 \quad P_\theta\text{-a.s.}, \qquad \text{i.e.} \quad \psi(T) = p_C \quad P_\theta\text{-a.s.}
$$
Thus $P_\theta(A \in C \mid T) = p_C = P_\theta(A \in C)$ almost surely, for every $\theta$ and every measurable $C$. Hence $T$ and $A$ are independent under each $P_\theta$. $\square$

**Bridge.** Basu's theorem builds toward the distributional calculations that pervade estimation and testing, and appears again in the Lehmann-Scheffé uniqueness of minimum-variance unbiased estimators [45.01.06], where the completeness used to invoke Basu is the very same completeness that makes the conditional estimator unique. The foundational reason the proof works is that sufficiency converts the conditional probability into a $\theta$-free function of $T$ while ancillarity pins the unconditional probability to a $\theta$-free constant; their difference is then an unbiased estimator of zero, and completeness is exactly the hypothesis that kills it. This is exactly the three-property interplay — sufficiency, ancillarity, completeness — read off a single equation $\mathbb{E}_\theta[\psi(T) - p_C] = 0$. The argument generalises verbatim from indicator functions to bounded measurable functions of $A$, so bounded completeness suffices, and putting these together, the central insight is that completeness is the algebraic non-redundancy condition that turns "no marginal information" (ancillarity) into "no conditional information either" (independence); the bridge is that this same non-redundancy is what makes the natural statistic of a full-rank exponential family — proved complete in the Advanced section — the universal supplier of complete sufficient statistics throughout the theory.

Expanding the exponent, $e^{-n(t-\theta)^2/2} = e^{-nt^2/2}e^{n\theta t}e^{-n\theta^2/2}$, so the condition is $\int [g(t)e^{-nt^2/2}]\,e^{n\theta t}\,dt = 0$ for all $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$. So $\bar X$ is complete. Rubric: full credit for the Gaussian factorisation, the Laplace-transform identification, and the uniqueness conclusion.

</details>

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Which observed quantity has a distribution free of $\theta$, yet tells you how precise your single measurement was?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The coin outcome $A$ (which instrument was used) has a fixed $1/2$-$1/2$ law free of $\theta$, so it is ancillary. Yet $A$ determines the precision of the realised measurement: given $A = \text{precise}$ the data are $\mathcal{N}(\theta,1)$, given $A = \text{crude}$ they are $\mathcal{N}(\theta,100)$. The conditionality principle says inference should report the precision of the experiment that was actually run, i.e. condition on $A$, rather than average the two variances into an unconditional report that overstates precision on crude days and understates it on precise days. The ancillary indexes the realised subexperiment; conditioning recovers the relevant information [ref: TODO_REF Fisher 1934]. Rubric: full credit for naming $A$ ancillary, noting it fixes precision, and stating the conditional inference.

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that a complete sufficient statistic is minimal sufficient (Bahadur's theorem), assuming a minimal sufficient statistic $U$ exists.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A minimal sufficient $U$ is a function of every sufficient statistic, so $U = f(T)$; show $T$ is a function of $U$ by applying completeness to $g(T) = T - \mathbb{E}[T \mid U]$ (for a bounded coordinate transform).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $T$ be complete sufficient and $U$ minimal sufficient. By minimality $U = f(T)$ for some measurable $f$ (minimal sufficiency is a function of every sufficient statistic). To get the converse, it suffices to show $T$ is $\sigma(U)$-measurable. Replace $T$ by a bounded one-to-one transform $\tilde T = \arctan(T)$ (coordinatewise), still complete and sufficient with $\sigma(\tilde T) = \sigma(T)$. Since $U$ is sufficient, $h(U) := \mathbb{E}_\theta[\tilde T \mid U]$ is $\theta$-free; set $g(T) := \tilde T - h(f(T))$, a bounded function of $T$. By the tower property and $U = f(T)$, $\mathbb{E}_\theta[g(T)] = \mathbb{E}_\theta[\tilde T] - \mathbb{E}_\theta[\,h(U)\,] = \mathbb{E}_\theta[\tilde T] - \mathbb{E}_\theta[\mathbb{E}_\theta[\tilde T \mid U]] = 0$ for all $\theta$. Completeness of $T$ forces $g(T) = 0$ a.s., i.e. $\tilde T = h(U)$ a.s., so $\tilde T$ (hence $T$) is a function of $U$. Combined with $U = f(T)$, $T$ and $U$ generate the same $\sigma$-field, so $T$ is minimal sufficient. Rubric: full credit for $U = f(T)$ from minimality, the bounded transform, the completeness application to $\tilde T - \mathbb{E}[\tilde T\mid U]$, and the two-way functional dependence.

</details>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Exhibit a model in which a sufficient statistic is *not* complete and a sufficient statistic is *not* independent of an ancillary, demonstrating the necessity of completeness in Basu's theorem. Use $\mathrm{Unif}(\theta - 1/2,\, \theta + 1/2)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The minimal sufficient statistic is $(X_{(1)}, X_{(n)}) = (\min, \max)$; the range $X_{(n)} - X_{(1)}$ is ancillary. Show the range and the midrange are dependent.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For the location-uniform family, the minimal sufficient statistic is $T = (X_{(1)}, X_{(n)})$, the min and max, sufficient by factorization but *not* complete: the range $W = X_{(n)} - X_{(1)}$ has $\theta$-free distribution (shift-invariant), so $g(T) := W - \mathbb{E}[W]$ satisfies $\mathbb{E}_\theta[g(T)] = 0$ for all $\theta$ yet $g(T) \not\equiv 0$ — a non-zero unbiased estimator of zero, defeating completeness. Now $W$ is ancillary and the midrange $V = (X_{(1)} + X_{(n)})/2$ is (the natural location estimator), but $V$ and $W$ are *dependent*: the support constraint $V \pm W/2 \in [\theta - 1/2, \theta+1/2]$ ties them, e.g. a large range $W$ near $1$ forces $V$ close to $\theta$, while small $W$ permits $V$ to vary more. Concretely the conditional density of $V$ given $W = w$ is uniform on an interval of length $1 - w$, which depends on $w$, so $V \not\perp W$. Thus the sufficient $T$ (carrying $V$) is not independent of the ancillary $W$ — precisely because $T$ is not complete. This is the converse-illustration: completeness is indispensable in Basu's theorem. Rubric: full credit for the incompleteness via the range, the ancillarity of $W$, and the explicit conditional-length-$(1-w)$ dependence of $V$ on $W$.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

The decisive structural fact is that the natural sufficient statistic of a full-rank exponential family is complete, which makes exponential families the universal source of complete sufficient statistics and so the canonical home of Basu's theorem.

**Completeness of the full-rank exponential family.** Let $\{P_\eta : \eta \in \mathcal{N}\}$ be a $k$-parameter exponential family in canonical form, $p_\eta(x) = h(x)\exp(\langle\eta, T(x)\rangle - A(\eta))$ as in [45.01.02], and suppose the natural parameter space $\mathcal{N}$ contains a non-empty open set in $\mathbb{R}^k$ (the *full-rank* condition). Then $T$ is complete. The proof reduces completeness to the uniqueness theorem for the bilateral Laplace transform: if $g$ is a function of $t$ with $\mathbb{E}_\eta[g(T)] = 0$ for all $\eta$ in the open set, then writing $\nu = T_*(h\,d\mu)$ for the image of the carrier measure, $\int g(t)\,e^{\langle\eta, t\rangle}\,\nu(dt) = 0$ on an open $\eta$-set; splitting $g = g^+ - g^-$ produces two finite measures with equal Laplace transforms on an open set, which by analyticity of the transform must coincide, forcing $g = 0$ $\nu$-a.e. The full-rank (open-set) hypothesis is exactly what licenses the analytic-continuation step; a curved exponential family, whose natural parameters lie on a lower-dimensional submanifold of $\mathcal{N}$, generally fails completeness because the $\eta$-set has empty interior and the Laplace transform is pinned only along a curve.

**Bahadur's theorem and the completeness hierarchy.** A complete sufficient statistic is minimal sufficient (Exercise 7), so completeness sits strictly above minimality: complete sufficient $\Rightarrow$ bounded complete sufficient $\Rightarrow$ minimal sufficient, with neither converse holding. The uniform location family $\mathrm{Unif}(\theta-1/2,\theta+1/2)$ is minimal sufficient but not bounded complete; there exist boundedly complete families that are not complete, so all three layers are distinct. Completeness is the property that secures *uniqueness* in the Lehmann-Scheffé theorem: if $T$ is complete sufficient and $\varphi(T)$ is unbiased for $\psi(\theta)$, then $\varphi(T)$ is the unique unbiased function of $T$ estimating $\psi$, hence — by Rao-Blackwell — the unique minimum-variance unbiased estimator. Basu's theorem and Lehmann-Scheffé thus draw on the same completeness, applied to opposite ends: Basu uses it to kill the difference $\psi(T) - p_C$, Lehmann-Scheffé to kill the difference of two unbiased estimators.

**Basu's theorem as a computational engine.** Once independence of a complete sufficient $T$ from an ancillary $A$ is in hand, moments factor: $\mathbb{E}_\theta[f(T)\,m(A)] = \mathbb{E}_\theta[f(T)]\,\mathbb{E}[m(A)]$ with the second factor $\theta$-free. This converts hard joint integrals into products. The independence of $\bar X$ and $S^2$ for the normal model, the derivation of the distribution of Studentised statistics $\sqrt n\,\bar X / S$ (whose numerator depends on $T = \bar X$ and denominator on the ancillary $S$, giving the $t$-distribution by a conditioning argument), and the computation of expectations of ratios all run on Basu. In the theory of *similar tests*, bounded completeness of the sufficient statistic under the null boundary is precisely the condition under which every similar test has Neyman structure — the test statistic conditioned on the sufficient statistic has $\theta$-free null rejection probability — so Basu-type reasoning underlies the construction of uniformly most powerful unbiased tests [ref: TODO_REF Lehmann Romano 2005].

**The role of the conditionality principle.** Ancillarity also carries an inferential, not merely computational, charge. Fisher's recovery-of-information argument holds that when an ancillary $A$ indexes which subexperiment was effectively performed, the relevant Fisher information is the *conditional* information given $A$, and unconditional reports can be misleading [ref: TODO_REF Fisher 1934]. The tension with Basu's theorem is instructive: Basu shows a complete sufficient statistic is independent of *any* ancillary, so for a complete-sufficient-statistic-based inference the conditioning on an ancillary changes nothing distributionally; the conditionality principle bites precisely in families *without* a complete sufficient statistic, where the choice of ancillary to condition on is both consequential and, sometimes, non-unique — the famous non-uniqueness of maximal ancillaries.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason ancillarity, completeness, and Basu's theorem cohere is that completeness is the algebraic non-redundancy of a sufficient statistic, and it is exactly the hypothesis that upgrades "an ancillary carries no marginal information" into "an ancillary carries no information at all about a complete sufficient statistic." This is exactly the content of the one-line computation $\mathbb{E}_\theta[\psi(T) - p_C] = 0 \Rightarrow \psi(T) = p_C$, in which sufficiency supplies the $\theta$-free $\psi(T)$, ancillarity supplies the $\theta$-free $p_C$, and completeness supplies the kill. Completeness generalises minimal sufficiency — every complete sufficient statistic is minimal, by the same unbiased-estimator-of-zero argument run on $T - \mathbb{E}[T\mid U]$ — and it is dual to the uniqueness it secures in Lehmann-Scheffé, where the *same* property annihilates the difference of two unbiased estimators rather than the difference $\psi(T) - p_C$. Putting these together, the central insight is that the full-rank exponential family is the universal supplier: its natural statistic is complete by the Laplace-transform uniqueness theorem, so the convexity-and-analyticity machinery of the log-partition function $A$ from [45.01.02] is what ultimately powers Basu's theorem in every standard model, and the bridge is that the same open-set / analytic-continuation fact that makes $A$ real-analytic on $\operatorname{int}\mathcal{N}$ is what makes $T$ complete — completeness and the cumulant calculus are two faces of the analyticity of the bilateral transform.

## Full proof set [Master]

Basu's theorem and the normal independence are proved above. The remaining Master claims are recorded here.

**Proposition 1 (completeness of the full-rank exponential family).** *Let $\{P_\eta : \eta \in \mathcal{N}\}$ have densities $p_\eta = h\exp(\langle\eta, T\rangle - A(\eta))$ with respect to $\sigma$-finite $\mu$, and suppose $\mathcal{N}$ has non-empty interior. Then $T$ is a complete statistic.*

*Proof.* Let $g$ be measurable with $\mathbb{E}_\eta[g(T)] = 0$ for all $\eta \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{N}$. Let $\nu = T_*(h\,d\mu)$ be the pushforward of the carrier measure $h\,d\mu$ under $T$, a $\sigma$-finite measure on $\mathbb{R}^k$. For $\eta \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{N}$,
$$
0 = \mathbb{E}_\eta[g(T)] = e^{-A(\eta)}\int_{\mathbb{R}^k} g(t)\, e^{\langle\eta, t\rangle}\,\nu(dt),
\quad\text{so}\quad
\int g(t)\,e^{\langle\eta, t\rangle}\,\nu(dt) = 0.
$$
Write $g = g^+ - g^-$ for the positive and negative parts and define finite measures $\nu^{\pm}(dt) = g^{\pm}(t)\,\nu(dt)$ (finite because $\int g^{\pm}e^{\langle\eta_0, t\rangle}\,d\nu < \infty$ at any interior $\eta_0$, the two parts being separately integrable since $\int |g|\,p_{\eta_0}\,d\mu = \mathbb{E}_{\eta_0}|g(T)| < \infty$). The hypothesis reads
$$
\int e^{\langle\eta, t\rangle}\,\nu^+(dt) = \int e^{\langle\eta, t\rangle}\,\nu^-(dt) \qquad \text{for all } \eta \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{N}.
$$
Both sides, as functions of complex $\eta$ with $\operatorname{Re}\eta \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{N}$, are the bilateral Laplace transforms of the finite measures $\nu^{\pm}$; each is holomorphic on the tube $\{\operatorname{Re}\eta \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{N}\}$ (differentiation under the integral, as for $A$ in [45.01.02]). They agree on the real open set $\operatorname{int}\mathcal{N}$, hence on the whole tube by analytic continuation, hence the inverse-transform / uniqueness theorem for Laplace transforms of finite measures gives $\nu^+ = \nu^-$. Therefore $g^+ = g^-$ $\nu$-a.e., i.e. $g = 0$ $\nu$-a.e., which is $g(T) = 0$ $P_\eta$-a.s. for every $\eta$ (the $P_\eta$-null sets coincide with the $\nu$-null sets on the support). So $T$ is complete. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (a complete sufficient statistic is minimal sufficient — Bahadur).** *If $T$ is complete and sufficient and a minimal sufficient statistic exists, then $T$ is minimal sufficient.*

*Proof.* This is Exercise 7; reproduced. Let $U$ be minimal sufficient. By minimality $U$ is a function of the sufficient $T$: $U = f(T)$. Replace $T$ by the bounded injective transform $\tilde T = \arctan(T)$ componentwise, with $\sigma(\tilde T) = \sigma(T)$, still complete and sufficient. Since $U$ is sufficient, $h(U) := \mathbb{E}_\theta[\tilde T \mid U]$ has a $\theta$-free version. Set $g(T) = \tilde T - h(f(T))$, bounded and a function of $T$. Then, using $U = f(T)$ and the tower property, $\mathbb{E}_\theta[g(T)] = \mathbb{E}_\theta[\tilde T] - \mathbb{E}_\theta[h(U)] = \mathbb{E}_\theta[\tilde T] - \mathbb{E}_\theta[\mathbb{E}_\theta[\tilde T\mid U]] = 0$ for all $\theta$. Completeness forces $g(T) = 0$ a.s., so $\tilde T = h(U)$ a.s.: $\tilde T$, and hence $T$, is a function of $U$. With $U = f(T)$ this gives $\sigma(T) = \sigma(U)$ a.s., so $T$ is minimal sufficient. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Basu, the bounded-complete and conditional forms).** *Let $T$ be sufficient and bounded complete, and let $A$ be ancillary. Then $T$ and $A$ are independent under every $P_\theta$. More generally, if $\mathcal{P}$ is a sub-family of a larger model and $A$ is ancillary on $\mathcal{P}$ while $T$ is complete sufficient on $\mathcal{P}$, the independence holds on $\mathcal{P}$.*

*Proof.* Repeat the Key-theorem argument with the bounded function $m(A) := \mathbf{1}\{A \in C\}$, which is bounded by $1$. Sufficiency makes $\psi(T) = \mathbb{E}_\theta[m(A)\mid T]$ a $\theta$-free function of $T$, bounded by $1$; ancillarity makes $p_C = \mathbb{E}_\theta[m(A)] = P_\theta(A \in C)$ a $\theta$-free constant. Then $g(T) := \psi(T) - p_C$ is *bounded* and satisfies $\mathbb{E}_\theta[g(T)] = 0$ for all $\theta$; bounded completeness gives $g(T) = 0$ a.s. Hence $P_\theta(A\in C\mid T) = P_\theta(A\in C)$ a.s. for every measurable $C$, which is independence. The relativised statement is the same proof carried out with all expectations and the completeness restricted to the sub-family $\mathcal{P}$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (necessity of completeness).** *There is a model with a sufficient statistic $T$ and an ancillary $A$ that are dependent. Hence the completeness hypothesis in Basu's theorem cannot be dropped.*

*Proof.* Take $X_1, \dots, X_n$ i.i.d. $\mathrm{Unif}(\theta - 1/2, \theta + 1/2)$, $n \ge 2$. The order-statistic pair $T = (X_{(1)}, X_{(n)})$ is minimal sufficient. The range $W = X_{(n)} - X_{(1)}$ is location-invariant, so its law is $\theta$-free: $W$ is ancillary, and $W$ is a function of $T$. A statistic cannot be independent of a non-degenerate function of itself: $\operatorname{Cov}_\theta(W, W) = \operatorname{Var}_\theta(W) > 0$, while independence of $T$ and $W$ would force $\operatorname{Cov}(\mathbf 1\{W \le a\}, \mathbf 1\{W \le a\}) = 0$. Hence $T$ and the ancillary $W$ are dependent. The obstruction is exactly that $T$ is not complete: $g(T) = W - \mathbb{E}[W]$ is a non-zero unbiased estimator of zero. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

The probability-space and independence foundations of [37.01.01] are what give Basu's theorem its meaning: ancillarity is the constancy across $\theta$ of a pushforward law $A_*P_\theta$, completeness is a statement about the family of pushforward laws $\{P_\theta^T\}$, and the conclusion is independence of the $\sigma$-fields $\sigma(T)$ and $\sigma(A)$ in the exact measure-theoretic sense defined there — the theorem is a relation among laws and $\sigma$-fields, not among numbers.

The random-variable and expectation theory of [26.03.01] supplies the single equation the proof turns on, $\mathbb{E}_\theta[\psi(T) - p_C] = 0$: the tower property of conditional expectation, the linearity of the integral, and the definition of $\mathbb{E}_\theta[g(T)]$ as an integral against $P_\theta$ are all imported from that unit, and completeness is phrased entirely as a condition on such expectations.

Sufficiency and exponential families [45.01.02] are the direct upstream: Basu's theorem takes the sufficiency notion and the factorization from there, and the completeness of the natural statistic of a full-rank exponential family — proved here via the Laplace-transform uniqueness theorem — is what makes that unit's exponential families the universal supplier of complete sufficient statistics, so the convexity and analyticity of the log-partition function $A$ are the same analytic facts that power completeness.

The Lehmann-Scheffé theorem and uniformly minimum-variance unbiased estimation [45.01.06] consume completeness from the opposite side: where Basu uses completeness to annihilate $\psi(T) - p_C$ and obtain independence, Lehmann-Scheffé uses the identical completeness to annihilate the difference of two unbiased functions of $T$ and obtain uniqueness of the minimum-variance unbiased estimator; the two theorems are the two canonical applications of one property.

The decision-theoretic risk framework [45.01.01] is where the payoff lands: Basu-driven independence of $\bar X$ and $S^2$ is what makes the Studentised statistic's distribution computable and the $t$-test's risk and similar-test structure analysable, and bounded completeness of the sufficient statistic under a null boundary is the condition for the Neyman-structure characterisation of similar and uniformly most powerful unbiased tests in the testing chapter [45.02.01].

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Ancillarity and the recovery of information by conditioning are due to Ronald A. Fisher, who introduced the configuration statistic and the conditional-inference argument in *Two new properties of mathematical likelihood* (*Proc. R. Soc. A* 144, 1934, 285) [ref: TODO_REF Fisher 1934], arguing that the precision of an estimate should be measured in the conditional model given an ancillary that indexes the realised experiment. Completeness was introduced by Erich Lehmann and Henry Scheffé in their 1950 *Sankhyā* paper on completeness, similar regions, and unbiased estimation, where it was the property securing uniqueness of unbiased estimators and the construction of similar regions.

Basu's theorem is due to Debabrata Basu in *On statistics independent of a complete sufficient statistic* (*Sankhyā* 15, 1955, 377) [ref: TODO_REF Basu 1955], a three-page note whose proof is the one given above; Basu later explored the converse direction and the limits of the result, including examples where independence of a sufficient statistic and an ancillary holds without completeness, showing the theorem's hypotheses are sufficient but not necessary. The completeness of exponential families via the Laplace-transform argument and the placement of ancillarity, completeness, and minimal sufficiency into a single hierarchy were systematised by Lehmann and Casella in *Theory of Point Estimation* [ref: TODO_REF Lehmann Casella 1998] and, for the testing applications, in Lehmann and Romano's *Testing Statistical Hypotheses* [ref: TODO_REF Lehmann Romano 2005]. Raghu Raj Bahadur proved in 1957 that a complete sufficient statistic is minimal sufficient, fixing completeness as the top of the reduction hierarchy.
