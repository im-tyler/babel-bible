## Intuition [Beginner]

Atoms differ in how tightly they hold their outermost electrons. Moving from left to right across a row of the periodic table, the nuclear charge increases but the shielding from inner electrons stays roughly constant. The net pull on the valence electrons grows, and they are drawn closer to the nucleus. This single idea — that **effective nuclear charge** increases across a period — explains almost every periodic trend.

**Ionization energy** is the energy required to remove the outermost electron from a gaseous atom. Atoms with a large effective nuclear charge hold their electrons tightly, so they have high ionization energies. Atoms with a small effective nuclear charge lose electrons easily, so they have low ionization energies. Across a period, ionization energy rises. Down a group, it falls because the valence electrons sit in shells farther from the nucleus, where the nuclear pull is weaker.

**Atomic radius** is the distance from the nucleus to the outermost electrons. As effective nuclear charge increases across a period, the electron cloud is pulled inward and the atom shrinks. Down a group, each new period adds a shell, so the atom grows larger. Cations (positive ions) are smaller than their parent atoms because removing electrons reduces electron-electron repulsion and the remaining electrons are pulled closer. Anions (negative ions) are larger because added electrons increase repulsion and the cloud expands.

## Visual [Beginner]

Summary of the four major periodic trends:

| Trend | Across a period (L to R) | Down a group |
|---|---|---|
| Ionization energy | Increases | Decreases |
| Electron affinity | Generally becomes more negative | Generally becomes less negative |
| Atomic radius | Decreases | Increases |
| Electronegativity | Increases | Decreases |

![Periodic trend arrows overlaid on a periodic table. A rightward arrow across each period labelled IE increases; a downward arrow along each group labelled radius increases. Color gradient from low IE (left, blue) to high IE (right, red).](/img/14.01.02-periodic-trends-table-placeholder.svg)

Electronegativity is a measure of how strongly an atom attracts electrons in a chemical bond. Fluorine is the most electronegative element (3.98 on the Pauling scale). Francium and cesium are the least electronegative. The trend follows ionization energy closely because both depend on effective nuclear charge.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Rank the following atoms by first ionization energy from lowest to highest: Na, Mg, Al, Si.

Step 1. Identify the period. All four are in period 3, so shielding from inner shells is identical.

Step 2. Apply the across-period trend. Ionization energy generally increases from Na to Si as $Z$ increases from 11 to 14 and the valence electrons feel a stronger nuclear pull.

Step 3. Check for anomalies. Al ($Z = 13$) has configuration $[\text{Ne}]\, 3s^2\, 3p^1$. Its outermost electron is a $3p$ electron, which is higher in energy than the $3s$ electrons of Mg ($[\text{Ne}]\, 3s^2$). So Al has a lower first ionization energy than Mg despite having one more proton.

The ranking: Na (496 kJ/mol) < Al (578 kJ/mol) < Mg (738 kJ/mol) < Si (786 kJ/mol).

The dip at Al is one of the characteristic anomalies caused by subshell boundaries. A similar dip occurs between P and S, where S has a paired $3p$ electron that is easier to remove than one of P's three unpaired $3p$ electrons.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Effective nuclear charge

The **effective nuclear charge** $Z_{\text{eff}}$ is the net positive charge experienced by an electron in an atom, after accounting for shielding by other electrons:

$$Z_{\text{eff}} = Z - \sigma,$$

where $Z$ is the atomic number and $\sigma$ is the shielding constant. **Slater's rules** (1930) provide a systematic procedure for estimating $\sigma$:

1. Write the electron configuration in the order $1s\;|\;2s,2p\;|\;3s,3p\;|\;3d\;|\;4s,4p\;|\;4d\;|\;4f\;|\;\ldots$
2. Electrons in groups to the right of the electron of interest contribute nothing to $\sigma$.
3. Electrons in the same group as the electron of interest contribute $0.35$ each (except $1s$, where the contribution is $0.30$).
4. For $s$ or $p$ electrons: electrons in the $n - 1$ shell contribute $0.85$ each; electrons in $n - 2$ and lower shells contribute $1.00$ each.
5. For $d$ or $f$ electrons: all electrons in lower groups contribute $1.00$ each.

Slater's rules are approximate. More accurate values come from Hartree-Fock calculations, but the rules capture the essential physics with minimal computation.

### Ionization energy

The **first ionization energy** $IE_1$ is the minimum energy required to remove the most loosely bound electron from a gaseous atom:

$$\text{X}(g) \to \text{X}^+(g) + e^- \qquad \Delta E = IE_1 > 0.$$

The **second ionization energy** $IE_2$ removes a second electron: $\text{X}^+(g) \to \text{X}^{2+}(g) + e^-$. Successive ionization energies always increase because each removal leaves fewer electrons to shield and a more positive ion to escape. A large jump in successive ionization energies occurs when electrons are removed from a new (more tightly bound) shell.

In the Hartree-Fock framework, Koopmans' theorem provides the connection: $IE_i \approx -\varepsilon_i$, where $\varepsilon_i$ is the orbital energy of the electron removed. This approximation neglects orbital relaxation and electron correlation but gives the correct qualitative ordering.

### Electron affinity

The **electron affinity** $EA$ is the energy change when a gaseous atom gains an electron:

$$\text{X}(g) + e^- \to \text{X}^-(g) \qquad \Delta E = EA.$$

By convention, if the process releases energy, $EA$ is reported as a negative number (following the IUPAC sign convention). A more negative electron affinity means the atom more readily accepts an electron. Chlorine has the most negative electron affinity ($-349$ kJ/mol) among the main-group elements. Noble gases have positive electron affinities — adding an electron to a closed shell is energetically unfavorable.

Electron affinity does not follow a smooth periodic trend. The general trend is toward more negative values across a period, but anomalies are frequent. The Group 2 elements (Be, Mg, Ca) have less negative electron affinities than the Group 1 elements in the same period because the added electron would enter a higher-energy $p$ orbital rather than a half-filled $s$ orbital. Nitrogen has a less negative electron affinity than carbon because the added electron must pair with an existing $2p$ electron in N's half-filled $2p^3$ subshell.

### Atomic and ionic radius

The **atomic radius** is not a directly measurable quantity because electron densities decay exponentially and have no sharp boundary. Several operational definitions exist:

- **Covalent radius**: half the distance between two identical bonded atoms.
- **Metallic radius**: half the distance between adjacent atoms in a metallic crystal.
- **Van der Waals radius**: half the closest distance between non-bonded atoms in adjacent molecules.

Despite the ambiguity, all definitions give the same qualitative trends. The atomic radius decreases across a period (increasing $Z_{\text{eff}}$ draws electrons inward) and increases down a group (additional electron shells).

For ions, **isoelectronic series** provide clean comparisons. The species $\text{O}^{2-}$, $\text{F}^-$, $\text{Ne}$, $\text{Na}^+$, $\text{Mg}^{2+}$, $\text{Al}^{3+}$ all have 10 electrons. Their radii decrease as $Z$ increases because the same number of electrons is attracted by an increasingly positive nucleus: $\text{O}^{2-}$ (140 pm) $>$ $\text{F}^-$ (133 pm) $>$ $\text{Na}^+$ (102 pm) $>$ $\text{Mg}^{2+}$ (72 pm) $>$ $\text{Al}^{3+}$ (54 pm).

### Electronegativity

**Electronegativity** quantifies an atom's tendency to attract electrons in a chemical bond. Three major scales exist:

**Pauling scale** (1932). Based on bond dissociation energies. If the bond energy of A–B exceeds the geometric mean of A–A and B–B bond energies, the excess is attributed to ionic character:

$$\chi_A - \chi_B = \sqrt{D_{AB} - \sqrt{D_{AA} \cdot D_{BB}}},$$

where $D_{AB}$ is the dissociation energy of the A–B bond in eV. The scale is relative, anchored by $\chi_\text{F} = 3.98$.

**Mulliken scale** (1934). Defined as the average of the first ionization energy and the negative of the electron affinity:

$$\chi_\text{M} = \frac{IE_1 + |EA|}{2}.$$

The Mulliken scale has a clear physical interpretation: an atom is electronegative if it is both hard to ionize (high $IE_1$) and eager to gain an electron (large $|EA|$). Conversion to the Pauling scale is approximately $\chi_\text{P} \approx 0.336\,\chi_\text{M} - 0.207$ when $IE$ and $EA$ are in eV.

**Allen scale** (1989). Based on the average one-electron energy of the valence-shell electrons:

$$\chi_\text{A} = \frac{m\,\varepsilon_s + n\,\varepsilon_p}{m + n},$$

where $m$ and $n$ are the numbers of $s$ and $p$ valence electrons and $\varepsilon_s$, $\varepsilon_p$ are their spectroscopic energies (from Hartree-Fock calculations). The Allen scale is the most directly connected to atomic spectroscopy and avoids the thermodynamic empiricism of the Pauling scale.

### Anomalies and fine structure in periodic trends

**Half-filled subshell stability.** The first ionization energy of nitrogen (1402 kJ/mol) is higher than that of oxygen (1314 kJ/mol), even though oxygen is one column to the right. Nitrogen's $2p^3$ configuration has one electron in each $2p$ orbital — a half-filled subshell with maximum exchange energy. Removing an electron from oxygen means removing a paired $2p$ electron, which experiences additional electron-electron repulsion. This anomaly repeats for P/S and As/Se in later periods.

**d-Block contraction.** The atomic radii of Ga, Ge, As, Se, Br are smaller than expected from simple extrapolation because the intervening $3d$ transition series does not shield the $4s$ and $4p$ electrons very effectively. The poor shielding by $d$ electrons means $Z_{\text{eff}}$ for the period-4 $p$-block elements is larger than a naive model predicts. This is why Ga is approximately the same size as Al despite being one period lower.

**Lanthanide contraction.** The 14 $4f$ electrons added across the lanthanide series (Ce–Lu) shield very poorly. By the time the $5d$ and $6p$ elements are reached, $Z_{\text{eff}}$ has increased substantially, pulling the outer electrons inward. The result is that period-6 transition metals (Hf, Ta, W, Re, Os, Ir, Pt, Au) have nearly the same atomic radii as their period-5 congeners (Zr, Nb, Mo, Tc, Ru, Rh, Pd, Ag). This has profound chemical consequences: Zr and Hf are so similar in size and chemistry that separating them industrially is difficult, and Pt and Pd have overlapping catalytic properties.

**Diagonal relationships.** Elements diagonally adjacent in the periodic table (Li/Mg, Be/Al, B/Si) show unexpected similarities in their chemical properties. The diagonal relationship arises because the increase in $Z_{\text{eff}}$ across a period roughly compensates for the decrease down a group, producing similar charge densities. Lithium and magnesium both form covalent organometallic compounds (Grignard-like behavior), unlike the other Group 1 elements.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Ionic radius in an isoelectronic series).** *Consider a series of ions $\text{X}^{q+}$, all with $N$ electrons. The ionic radius is a decreasing function of the nuclear charge $Z$: if $Z_1 < Z_2$, then $r(Z_1) > r(Z_2)$.*

*Proof.* For an $N$-electron ion with nuclear charge $Z$, the electronic Hamiltonian (in atomic units) is

$$\hat{H} = \sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(-\frac{1}{2}\nabla_i^2 - \frac{Z}{r_i}\right) + \sum_{i<j}\frac{1}{r_{ij}}.$$

By the Feynman-Hellmann theorem, the derivative of the energy with respect to $Z$ is

$$\frac{\partial E}{\partial Z} = \left\langle \frac{\partial \hat{H}}{\partial Z} \right\rangle = -\left\langle \sum_{i=1}^{N}\frac{1}{r_i}\right\rangle < 0.$$

Increasing $Z$ lowers the energy. The virial theorem for Coulomb systems gives $E = -\langle T \rangle = \langle V \rangle / 2$, so $E \propto -Z_{\text{eff}}^2/n^2$ approximately, and the characteristic orbital radius scales as $r \propto n^2 / Z_{\text{eff}}$. For a fixed electron count $N$, increasing $Z$ increases $Z_{\text{eff}}$ for every electron, contracting the charge distribution and reducing $r$. This is rigorous for hydrogen-like ions ($r \propto 1/Z$) and remains qualitatively valid for many-electron systems. $\square$

**Corollary.** *In the isoelectronic series $\text{O}^{2-}$, $\text{F}^-$, Ne, $\text{Na}^+$, $\text{Mg}^{2+}$, $\text{Al}^{3+}$ (all 10 electrons), the ionic radius decreases monotonically with increasing $Z$.*

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Using Slater's rules, calculate $Z_{\text{eff}}$ for a valence $4s$ electron in calcium ($Z = 20$, configuration $[\text{Ar}]\, 4s^2$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The $4s$ electron is shielded by: the $1s^2\, 2s^2\, 2p^6$ core (10 electrons, each contributes $0.85$), the $3s^2\, 3p^6$ subshell (8 electrons, each contributes $0.85$), and the other $4s$ electron ($0.35$).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Shielding: $10 \times 0.85 + 8 \times 0.85 + 1 \times 0.35 = 8.50 + 6.80 + 0.35 = 15.65$.

$Z_{\text{eff}} = 20 - 15.65 = 4.35$.

This moderate effective charge reflects the fact that calcium's two $4s$ electrons are far from the nucleus and heavily shielded, consistent with calcium's low first ionization energy (590 kJ/mol) and strong electropositive character.

</details>

All four cations have lost their single valence electron. Each has a noble-gas electron configuration.

**Exercise 3 (easy, symbolic).**

Explain why the second ionization energy of sodium ($IE_2 = 4562$ kJ/mol) is much larger than the first ($IE_1 = 496$ kJ/mol).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write the electron configurations of Na and Na$^+$. From which shell is the second electron removed?

</details>

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

The first five ionization energies of titanium ($Z = 22$) are: $IE_1 = 658$, $IE_2 = 1310$, $IE_3 = 2653$, $IE_4 = 4175$, $IE_5 = 9573$ kJ/mol. Explain the large jump between $IE_4$ and $IE_5$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write the electron configuration of Ti: $[\text{Ar}]\, 4s^2\, 3d^2$. Track which electrons are removed at each step.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Ti: $[\text{Ar}]\, 4s^2\, 3d^2$. The first four ionizations remove the two $4s$ electrons and two $3d$ electrons, giving Ti$^{4+}$: $[\text{Ar}]$. All four valence electrons are gone.

$IE_5$ must remove an electron from the Ar core ($3p$ subshell). Core electrons are much more tightly bound than valence electrons, producing the jump from 4175 to 9573 kJ/mol. This jump confirms that titanium's common oxidation state is $+4$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Using Slater's rules, compare $Z_{\text{eff}}$ for a $3d$ electron in Cu ($Z = 29$, configuration $[\text{Ar}]\, 4s^1\, 3d^{10}$) with $Z_{\text{eff}}$ for a $4s$ electron. Which electron is more tightly bound?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For a $3d$ electron: all electrons in groups $1s$ through $3p$ shield at $1.00$ each; other $3d$ electrons and the $4s$ electron shield at $0.35$ each.

For a $4s$ electron: electrons in $n = 1, 2, 3$ shells shield at $0.85$ each; $3d$ electrons shield at $1.00$ each.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a $3d$ electron: shielding from $1s^2\, 2s^2\, 2p^6\, 3s^2\, 3p^6$ = 18 at $1.00$ = $18.0$. Shielding from other $3d$ electrons = 9 at $0.35$ = $3.15$. Shielding from $4s^1$ = 1 at $0.35$ = $0.35$. Total $\sigma = 21.5$, so $Z_{\text{eff}}(3d) = 29 - 21.5 = 7.5$.

For a $4s$ electron: shielding from $1s^2\, 2s^2\, 2p^6\, 3s^2\, 3p^6$ = 18 at $0.85$ = $15.3$. Shielding from $3d^{10}$ = 10 at $1.00$ = $10.0$. Total $\sigma = 25.3$, so $Z_{\text{eff}}(4s) = 29 - 25.3 = 3.7$.

The $3d$ electrons ($Z_{\text{eff}} = 7.5$) are far more tightly bound than the $4s$ electron ($Z_{\text{eff}} = 3.7$). This explains why the $4s$ electron is removed first when copper ionizes, and why Cu$^+$ has configuration $[\text{Ar}]\, 3d^{10}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

On the Pauling scale, the electronegativity of hydrogen is 2.20, lithium is 0.98, and fluorine is 3.98. Estimate the percent ionic character of the Li–H bond and the H–F bond using the empirical formula:

$$\%\,\text{ionic} = (1 - e^{-0.25(\chi_A - \chi_B)^2}) \times 100.$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the electronegativity difference $\Delta\chi$ for each pair, then apply the formula.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Li–H: $\Delta\chi = 2.20 - 0.98 = 1.22$. $\%\,\text{ionic} = (1 - e^{-0.25(1.22)^2}) \times 100 = (1 - e^{-0.372}) \times 100 = (1 - 0.689) \times 100 \approx 31\%$.

H–F: $\Delta\chi = 3.98 - 2.20 = 1.78$. $\%\,\text{ionic} = (1 - e^{-0.25(1.78)^2}) \times 100 = (1 - e^{-0.792}) \times 100 = (1 - 0.453) \times 100 \approx 55\%$.

H–F is substantially more ionic, consistent with fluorine's strong electron-attracting ability. Both bonds have significant covalent character, illustrating that the ionic/covalent distinction is a continuum.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

The Mulliken electronegativity of chlorine is $\chi_\text{M} = (IE_1 + |EA|)/2$. Given $IE_1 = 1251$ kJ/mol and $EA = -349$ kJ/mol, compute $\chi_\text{M}$ in eV (1 eV = 96.485 kJ/mol) and convert to the Pauling scale using $\chi_\text{P} \approx 0.336\,\chi_\text{M} - 0.207$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Convert both $IE_1$ and $|EA|$ to eV first, then average, then apply the conversion formula.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$IE_1 = 1251 / 96.485 = 12.97$ eV. $|EA| = 349 / 96.485 = 3.62$ eV.

$\chi_\text{M} = (12.97 + 3.62)/2 = 8.30$ eV.

$\chi_\text{P} \approx 0.336 \times 8.30 - 0.207 = 2.79 - 0.207 = 2.58$.

The accepted Pauling value for chlorine is 3.16, so this linear conversion gives only an approximate result. The discrepancy reflects the empirical nature of the Pauling-to-Mulliken conversion; more sophisticated nonlinear conversions give better agreement.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Zirconium (Zr, $Z = 40$) has an atomic radius of 160 pm. Hafnium (Hf, $Z = 72$) has an atomic radius of 159 pm, despite being 32 protons heavier. Explain this near-identity using the lanthanide contraction.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Between Zr (Group 4, period 5) and Hf (Group 4, period 6), 14 lanthanide elements are inserted (Ce through Lu). Consider the shielding properties of $4f$ electrons.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Zr ($[\text{Kr}]\, 5s^2\, 4d^2$) and Hf ($[\text{Xe}]\, 6s^2\, 4f^{14}\, 5d^2$) are in the same group. Normally, adding a shell (period 5 to period 6) would increase the radius by 10–20 pm. However, the 14 $4f$ electrons filled across the lanthanide series are very poor at shielding the nuclear charge from the outer $5d$ and $6s$ electrons. The $f$ orbitals are diffuse and do not effectively screen the increasing nuclear charge.

The result: $Z_{\text{eff}}$ for Hf's valence electrons is much higher than for Zr's, pulling Hf's electron cloud inward and nearly canceling the radius increase from the additional shell. Hf (159 pm) is essentially the same size as Zr (160 pm). This has major chemical consequences: Zr and Hf compounds have almost identical structures, solubilities, and reactivities, making industrial separation extremely difficult (they are separated by solvent extraction of their fluorides).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

The first ionization energies of the period-2 elements are: Li (520), Be (900), B (801), C (1086), N (1402), O (1314), F (1681), Ne (2081) — all in kJ/mol. Identify the two anomalies and explain each in terms of electron configuration.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare each element's $IE_1$ to its neighbors. Look at the subshell from which the electron is removed.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Anomaly 1: B < Be.** Beryllium ($[\text{He}]\, 2s^2$) has a filled $2s$ subshell. Boron ($[\text{He}]\, 2s^2\, 2p^1$) has one electron in the higher-energy $2p$ subshell. The $2p$ electron is farther from the nucleus on average and more shielded (Slater's rules give lower $Z_{\text{eff}}$ for $2p$ than $2s$), so it is easier to remove. This produces the dip at boron.

**Anomaly 2: O < N.** Nitrogen ($[\text{He}]\, 2s^2\, 2p^3$) has a half-filled $2p$ subshell — one electron in each $2p$ orbital, all unpaired, maximizing exchange energy. Oxygen ($[\text{He}]\, 2s^2\, 2p^4$) has a paired $2p$ electron. Removing an electron from O removes one of a pair, gaining exchange energy from the resulting half-filled $2p^3$ configuration in O$^+$. Additionally, the paired electron experiences extra electron-electron repulsion. Both effects make O's first ionization energy lower than N's.

These anomalies repeat in periods 3 and beyond (Al < Mg, S < P), with the same subshell-structure origin.

</details>

</aside>

The covalent radii of Si (111 pm) and Cl (99 pm) are both smaller than one would predict by simple extrapolation from the period-3 elements Na (186 pm), Mg (160 pm), Al (143 pm). The $d$-block contraction accounts for part of this discrepancy. Estimate the expected radius of Si if the trend from Na to Al were linearly extrapolated, and state how much smaller Si actually is.

Compute the average decrease per element from Na to Al and extrapolate to Si (one more step).

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Na to Mg: $186 - 160 = 26$ pm decrease. Mg to Al: $160 - 143 = 17$ pm decrease. Average decrease per step: $(26 + 17)/2 = 21.5$ pm.

While the linear extrapolation is crude, the 10 pm deficit reflects the cumulative effect of increasing $Z_{\text{eff}}$ more steeply than expected. This is a mild version of the $d$-block contraction, which becomes much more pronounced for period-4 $p$-block elements where a full $3d^{10}$ subshell has been filled. For comparison, Ga (122 pm) is nearly the same size as Al (143 pm) despite being a full period lower — a contraction of about 21 pm against expectation.

## Quantitative periodic-trend models [Master]

The qualitative trends described above follow from three quantitative inputs: (1) the effective nuclear charge $Z_{\text{eff}}$, (2) the principal quantum number $n$ of the valence shell, and (3) the subshell type $l$.

**Ionization energy scaling.** For hydrogen-like ions, $IE \propto Z^2/n^2$. For many-electron atoms, Koopmans' theorem gives $IE_1 \approx -\varepsilon_{\text{outer}}$, where $\varepsilon_{\text{outer}}$ is the Hartree-Fock energy of the highest occupied orbital. A useful semi-empirical approximation for main-group elements is

$$IE_1 \approx \frac{Z_{\text{eff}}^2}{n^{*2}} \times 13.6\;\text{eV},$$

where $n^*$ is an effective principal quantum number that accounts for penetration: $n^* \approx 1$ for $n = 1$, $n^* \approx 2$ for $n = 2$, $n^* \approx 3$ for $n = 3$, but $n^* \approx 3.7$ for $n = 4$ (Slater's values). This formula predicts the correct ordering across periods and groups but fails quantitatively by 10–30% because it neglects electron correlation and the detailed orbital structure.

**Atomic radius scaling.** For hydrogen-like systems, $r \propto n^2/Z$. For many-electron atoms, the covalent radius scales approximately as

$$r_{\text{cov}} \propto \frac{n^2}{Z_{\text{eff}}},$$

with corrections for subshell type. The $s$ electrons penetrate closer to the nucleus than $p$ electrons of the same $n$, so $s$-block elements are somewhat smaller than this formula predicts. The $d$-block and $f$-block elements have inner $d$ or $f$ electrons that shield poorly, making the outer $s$ electrons experience a larger $Z_{\text{eff}}$ than expected — this is the mechanism behind the $d$-block and lanthanide contractions.

**Mulliken electronegativity as a bridge.** The Mulliken scale $\chi_\text{M} = (IE_1 + |EA|)/2$ connects electronegativity to two directly measurable atomic properties. It reveals that electronegativity is not an independent quantity but a composite: it measures the average tendency of an atom to either retain its own electrons (high $IE_1$) or acquire additional ones (large $|EA|$). The Periodic-table trend in $\chi_\text{M}$ follows the trends in both $IE_1$ and $|EA|$, which in turn follow from $Z_{\text{eff}}$ and $n$.

## Relativistic contributions to periodic trends [Master]

For heavy elements, relativistic effects modify the periodic trends in ways that the non-relativistic framework cannot capture. The three principal relativistic corrections — mass-velocity, Darwin, and spin-orbit — affect periodic trends through two mechanisms:

**Orbital contraction of $s$ and $p_{1/2}$ orbitals.** The relativistic mass increase for electrons moving near the speed of light in the deep potential well of a heavy nucleus contracts $s$ and $p_{1/2}$ orbitals. This contraction increases $Z_{\text{eff}}$ for the outer $s$ electrons, raising their ionization energy and electronegativity above the non-relativistic prediction. Gold's $6s$ ionization energy (890 kJ/mol) is about 200 kJ/mol higher than the non-relativistic estimate, and its Pauling electronegativity (2.54) is unusually high for a Group 11 element (compare Cu at 1.90, Ag at 1.93).

**Orbital expansion of $d$ and $f$ orbitals.** The contracted $s$ and $p$ orbitals shield the $d$ and $f$ orbitals more effectively, causing them to expand and rise in energy. This indirect destabilization lowers the ionization energy of $d$ and $f$ electrons and makes them more available for bonding in some cases (e.g., the variable oxidation states of the late transition metals).

Pyykko's 2011 review [ref: TODO_REF Pyykko 2011] provides a comprehensive table of relativistic effects on atomic properties across the periodic table. The key insight: for $Z > 70$, relativistic corrections to atomic radii exceed 5%, and for $Z > 80$, they dominate the chemical behavior. The non-relativistic periodic table is a good approximation for the first five periods but fails qualitatively for the sixth and seventh.

The inert-pair effect is the most chemically visible consequence. In Tl ($Z = 81$), Pb ($Z = 82$), and Bi ($Z = 83$), the $6s^2$ pair is stabilized relativistically, making the lower oxidation state (Tl$^+$, Pb$^{2+}$, Bi$^{3+}$) more stable than the group oxidation state (Tl$^{3+}$, Pb$^{4+}$, Bi$^{5+}$). This is a direct relativistic modification of the ionization-energy trend.

## Historical notes [Master]

The recognition that elements exhibit recurring properties dates to Mendeleev's 1869 periodic table and earlier work by Dobereiner (triads, 1817) and Newlands (law of octaves, 1865). Mendeleev's table organized elements by atomic weight and predicted the properties of undiscovered elements (Ga, Ge, Sc) with remarkable accuracy. The concept of atomic number ($Z$) was introduced by Moseley in 1913 using X-ray spectra, correcting several ordering anomalies in Mendeleev's table.

**Effective nuclear charge.** The concept of shielding was developed by J. C. Slater in 1930 [ref: TODO_REF Slater 1930], who proposed the rules still used in general chemistry. More sophisticated treatments by Clementi and Raimondi (1963) computed $Z_{\text{eff}}$ from Hartree-Fock wave functions, producing the accurate values used in modern computational chemistry.

**Electronegativity scales.** Linus Pauling introduced the first electronegativity scale in 1932 as part of *The Nature of the Chemical Bond*. Robert Mulliken proposed his scale in 1934 [ref: TODO_REF Mulliken 1934], grounding electronegativity in measurable atomic properties. Leland Allen's spectroscopic scale (1989) [ref: TODO_REF Allen 1989] connected electronegativity to orbital energies, providing the most physically transparent definition. All three scales agree qualitatively on the periodic trend but differ in absolute values by scaling factors.

**The lanthanide contraction** was first recognized by Goldschmidt in 1926 from ionic-radius measurements on rare-earth compounds. Its chemical implications — the near-identity of Zr/Hf, Nb/Ta, and Mo/W chemistries — were elaborated by numerous inorganic chemists through the mid-20th century. The relativistic interpretation of the contraction (that poor shielding by $4f$ electrons is augmented by relativistic $6s$ contraction) was developed by Pyykko and Desclaux in the 1970s.
