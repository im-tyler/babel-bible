## Intuition [Beginner]

Most quantum chemistry problems cannot be solved exactly. The variational principle provides a systematic way to approximate the answer. It states a simple but powerful fact: any trial wave function you write down will give an energy that is greater than or equal to the true ground-state energy. You can never undershoot. This means the best wave function is the one that gives the lowest energy -- you minimise to approach the truth from above.

To use the variational principle, you need a flexible family of trial wave functions. The **linear variational method** builds the trial function as a linear combination of fixed basis functions: $\tilde{\psi} = c_1\phi_1 + c_2\phi_2 + \cdots + c_N\phi_N$. The coefficients $c_i$ are the adjustable parameters. Minimising the energy with respect to these coefficients leads to a matrix equation called the **secular equation**, whose solutions are the variational energies and wave functions.

## Visual [Beginner]

The variational principle has a geometric interpretation. The exact eigenstates of the Hamiltonian form an orthonormal basis for the Hilbert space. A trial wave function is some vector in that space. The expectation value $\langle \tilde{\psi} | \hat{H} | \tilde{\psi} \rangle$ is a weighted average of all eigenvalues, with weights given by the overlap of $\tilde{\psi}$ with each eigenstate. Because the weights are positive and sum to one, this weighted average can never fall below the lowest eigenvalue.

![Diagram showing a trial wave function vector in a two-dimensional Hilbert space spanned by the ground state and first excited state. The energy expectation value is a weighted average of E_0 and E_1, shown as a point on a number line that always lies above E_0. Arrows indicate that varying the trial function rotates the vector toward the ground state, lowering the energy toward E_0.](/img/14.04.02-variational-principle-geometry-placeholder.svg)

For the linear variational method with two basis functions, the secular determinant is a $2 \times 2$ matrix. Setting its determinant to zero gives a quadratic equation whose two roots are approximations to the two lowest energies. Adding more basis functions enlarges the matrix and improves the approximation.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Apply the linear variational method to the H$_2^+$ molecular ion using a two-function basis: $\tilde{\psi} = c_1 \phi_{1s}^A + c_2 \phi_{1s}^B$, where $\phi_{1s}^A$ and $\phi_{1s}^B$ are hydrogen 1s orbitals centred on nuclei A and B.

Step 1. Compute the matrix elements. $H_{11} = \langle \phi_{1s}^A | \hat{H} | \phi_{1s}^A \rangle$ is the energy of an electron in orbital A (the **Coulomb integral** $\alpha$). $H_{12} = \langle \phi_{1s}^A | \hat{H} | \phi_{1s}^B \rangle$ is the **resonance integral** $\beta$. $S_{12} = \langle \phi_{1s}^A | \phi_{1s}^B \rangle$ is the **overlap integral** $S$.

Step 2. The secular determinant is

$$
\begin{vmatrix} \alpha - E & \beta - ES \\ \beta - ES & \alpha - E \end{vmatrix} = 0.
$$

Step 3. Expanding: $(\alpha - E)^2 - (\beta - ES)^2 = 0$, giving $E_{\pm} = \frac{\alpha \pm \beta}{1 \pm S}$. Since $\beta < 0$ and $S > 0$, the lower root $E_- = (\alpha + \beta)/(1 + S)$ is the bonding orbital energy and the upper root $E_+ = (\alpha - \beta)/(1 - S)$ is the antibonding energy.

Step 4. The corresponding wave functions are $\psi_- = (\phi_{1s}^A + \phi_{1s}^B)/\sqrt{2(1+S)}$ (bonding, electron density between the nuclei) and $\psi_+ = (\phi_{1s}^A - \phi_{1s}^B)/\sqrt{2(1-S)}$ (antibonding, a node between the nuclei).

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Variational principle (general).** Let $\hat{H}$ be a time-independent Hamiltonian with discrete, ordered eigenvalues $E_0 \leq E_1 \leq E_2 \leq \cdots$ and corresponding orthonormal eigenstates $\{|k\rangle\}$. For any normalised trial state $|\tilde{\psi}\rangle$,

$$
\langle \tilde{\psi} | \hat{H} | \tilde{\psi} \rangle \geq E_0.
$$

**Proof.** Expand $|\tilde{\psi}\rangle = \sum_k c_k |k\rangle$ with $\sum_k |c_k|^2 = 1$. Then

$$
\langle \tilde{\psi} | \hat{H} | \tilde{\psi} \rangle = \sum_k |c_k|^2 E_k \geq E_0 \sum_k |c_k|^2 = E_0.
$$

Equality holds if and only if $|\tilde{\psi}\rangle = |0\rangle$ (up to a phase). $\square$

### The linear variational method

Choose a finite set of $N$ linearly independent basis functions $\{\phi_i\}_{i=1}^N$ and form the trial function $\tilde{\psi} = \sum_{i=1}^N c_i \phi_i$. The energy is

$$
E(\mathbf{c}) = \frac{\sum_{i,j} c_i^* c_j H_{ij}}{\sum_{i,j} c_i^* c_j S_{ij}},
$$

where $H_{ij} = \langle \phi_i | \hat{H} | \phi_j \rangle$ is the **Hamiltonian matrix element** and $S_{ij} = \langle \phi_i | \phi_j \rangle$ is the **overlap matrix element**. Minimising $E$ with respect to each $c_k$ (setting $\partial E / \partial c_k^* = 0$) yields the **secular equations**

$$
\sum_{j=1}^N (H_{ij} - E S_{ij})\, c_j = 0, \qquad i = 1, \ldots, N.
$$

This system has a nontrivial solution if and only if the **secular determinant** vanishes:

$$
\det(\mathbf{H} - E\,\mathbf{S}) = 0.
$$

This is a **generalised eigenvalue problem**. The $N$ roots $E_0 \leq E_1 \leq \cdots \leq E_{N-1}$ are variational upper bounds to the $N$ lowest exact eigenvalues. The corresponding eigenvectors $\mathbf{c}^{(k)}$ give the variational wave functions.

### Basis functions for molecular calculations

The basis functions used in quantum chemistry fall into two families:

**Slater-type orbitals (STOs)** have the form

$$
\chi_{nlm}^\text{STO}(r, \theta, \varphi) = N\, r^{n-1}\, e^{-\zeta r}\, Y_l^m(\theta, \varphi),
$$

where $\zeta$ is the orbital exponent. STOs have the correct cusp at the nucleus ($d\psi/dr \neq 0$ at $r = 0$ for $s$ orbitals) and the correct exponential decay at large $r$. However, the two-electron repulsion integrals $\langle \chi_a \chi_b | r_{12}^{-1} | \chi_c \chi_d \rangle$ over STOs are expensive to evaluate for polyatomic molecules because there is no general analytical formula for multi-centre integrals.

**Gaussian-type orbitals (GTOs)** replace the exponential with a Gaussian:

$$
\chi_{l_x, l_y, l_z}^\text{GTO}(x, y, z) = N\, x^{l_x}\, y^{l_y}\, z^{l_z}\, e^{-\alpha r^2}.
$$

Gaussians lack the nuclear cusp and decay too quickly at large $r$, but the product of two Gaussians centred at different points is another Gaussian centred at an intermediate point. This **Gaussian product theorem** reduces all multi-centre integrals to single-centre integrals, making GTOs computationally efficient. The price is that more Gaussian functions are needed to approach the quality of a single STO.

### Contracted Gaussian basis sets

A **contracted Gaussian function** is a fixed linear combination of primitive Gaussians:

$$
\chi_\mu^\text{CGF} = \sum_{p=1}^K d_{\mu p}\, \chi_p^\text{PGF}(\alpha_p),
$$

where the contraction coefficients $d_{\mu p}$ and exponents $\alpha_p$ are determined once (usually by optimising atomic energies) and then held fixed during molecular calculations. The hierarchy of Gaussian basis sets includes:

| Basis type | Description | Example for C |
|------------|-------------|---------------|
| Minimal (STO-$n$G) | One contracted function per atomic orbital | STO-3G: 5 functions |
| Split-valence | Two (or more) functions per valence AO, one per core AO | 3-21G, 6-31G |
| Triple-zeta | Three functions per valence AO | 6-311G |
| Polarisation | Adds functions with $l$ one higher than valence | 6-31G(d), 6-31G(d,p) |
| Diffuse | Adds small-exponent functions for Rydberg/anion states | 6-31+G(d), aug-cc-pVDZ |

The notation 6-31G(d) means: core orbitals use 6 primitives contracted to 1 function, valence orbitals are split into two contractions (3 primitives + 1 primitive), and $d$-type polarisation functions are added to heavy atoms. The "(d,p)" variant also adds $p$-type functions to hydrogen.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (MacDonald's theorem).** *The $k$-th root $E_k$ of the secular equation $\det(\mathbf{H} - E\,\mathbf{S}) = 0$ is an upper bound to the $(k+1)$-th exact eigenvalue $E_k^\text{exact}$ of $\hat{H}$:*

$$
E_k \geq E_k^\text{exact}, \qquad k = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1.
$$

**Proof.** Let $\hat{H}$ have exact eigenstates $|j\rangle$ with eigenvalues $E_j^\text{exact}$ in non-decreasing order. The variational subspace $\mathcal{V} = \operatorname{span}\{\phi_1, \ldots, \phi_N\}$ has dimension $N$. Consider the subspace $\mathcal{W}$ spanned by the exact eigenstates $|0\rangle, |1\rangle, \ldots, |k\rangle$. Since $\dim \mathcal{W} = k + 1$, the intersection $\mathcal{V} \cap \mathcal{W}^\perp$ has dimension at least $N - k$ (where $\mathcal{W}^\perp$ is the orthogonal complement of $\mathcal{W}$ in $\mathcal{V}$).

For any $|\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{V} \cap \mathcal{W}^\perp$, the expansion $|\psi\rangle = \sum_{j \geq k+1} a_j |j\rangle$ contains only terms with $j \geq k+1$, so

$$
\langle \psi | \hat{H} | \psi \rangle = \sum_{j \geq k+1} |a_j|^2 E_j^\text{exact} \geq E_k^\text{exact} \sum_{j \geq k+1} |a_j|^2 = E_k^\text{exact}.
$$

The minimum of $\langle \psi | \hat{H} | \psi \rangle$ over all normalised $|\psi\rangle$ in $\mathcal{V}$, subject to being orthogonal to the first $k$ variational eigenvectors, equals $E_k$ by the Rayleigh-Ritz variational characterisation. Since $\mathcal{V} \cap \mathcal{W}^\perp$ is a subset of the admissible states, $E_k \geq E_k^\text{exact}$. $\square$

**Corollary (Bracketing theorem).** If the variational eigenvalues $E_0, E_1, \ldots, E_{N-1}$ satisfy $E_k < E_{k+1}$ and there exists a gap between exact eigenvalues such that $E_k^\text{exact} < E_{k+1}^\text{exact}$, then $E_k^\text{exact} \leq E_k < E_{k+1}^\text{exact}$ is possible but $E_k^\text{exact} \leq E_k \leq E_{k+1}^\text{exact}$ always holds.

### Worked example at intermediate level

Construct and solve the secular determinant for a particle in a one-dimensional box ($V = 0$ for $0 < x < L$, $V = \infty$ otherwise) using the two-function basis $\phi_1 = x(L - x)$ and $\phi_2 = x^2(L - x)$.

Both functions vanish at $x = 0$ and $x = L$, satisfying the boundary conditions. The Hamiltonian is $\hat{H} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2}{dx^2}$.

The overlap matrix element is $S_{12} = \int_0^L x(L-x) \cdot x^2(L-x)\,dx = L^6/60$.

The Hamiltonian matrix elements involve the second derivatives: $\hat{H}\phi_1 = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}(-2) = \frac{\hbar^2}{m}$ and $\hat{H}\phi_2 = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}(2L - 6x) = \frac{\hbar^2}{2m}(6x - 2L)$.

Computing $H_{11} = \langle \phi_1 | \hat{H} | \phi_1 \rangle = \frac{\hbar^2}{m}\int_0^L x(L-x)\,dx = \frac{\hbar^2 L^3}{6m}$. Similarly, $H_{12} = \frac{\hbar^2 L^4}{10m}$ and $S_{11} = \frac{L^5}{30}$.

Setting up the $2 \times 2$ secular equation and solving gives the lowest variational energy $E_0 = 0.1250\,\hbar^2\pi^2/(2mL^2)$, compared with the exact $E_0^\text{exact} = \pi^2\hbar^2/(2mL^2)$. The variational result exceeds the exact by $E_0/E_0^\text{exact} = 1.267$, a 26.7% overestimate. This is expected for a small, unoptimised basis.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

State the variational principle in words and explain why it implies that the lowest energy obtained from any trial function is always an upper bound to the true ground-state energy.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand the trial function in the exact eigenbasis and compute the expectation value as a weighted average of eigenvalues.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The variational principle states: for any normalised trial function $|\tilde{\psi}\rangle$, the expectation value $\langle \hat{H} \rangle = \langle \tilde{\psi} | \hat{H} | \tilde{\psi} \rangle$ satisfies $\langle \hat{H} \rangle \geq E_0$. Expanding $|\tilde{\psi}\rangle = \sum_k c_k |k\rangle$, the energy becomes $\sum_k |c_k|^2 E_k$, which is a weighted average of the exact eigenvalues with positive weights summing to 1. Any weighted average of a set of numbers is bounded below by the smallest member of that set, hence $\langle \hat{H} \rangle \geq E_0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, multiple choice).**

Which property do Gaussian-type orbitals have that makes them preferred over Slater-type orbitals for molecular calculations?

A. They have the correct nuclear cusp.

B. They decay exponentially at large $r$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).**

For a system with a $3 \times 3$ Hamiltonian matrix (in an orthonormal basis) $H_{11} = -2$, $H_{22} = -1$, $H_{33} = 0$, $H_{12} = -0.5$, $H_{13} = 0$, $H_{23} = -0.3$ (all other elements by symmetry), find the lowest eigenvalue. Is it above or below $-2$?

The Hamiltonian matrix is:

\mathbf{H} = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & -0.5 & 0 \\ -0.5 & -1 & -0.3 \\ 0 & -0.3 & 0 \end{pmatrix}

The secular equation $\det(\mathbf{H} - E\mathbf{I}) = 0$ gives a cubic. The lowest root is approximately $E_0 \approx -2.12$. This is below the smallest diagonal element ($-2$) because the off-diagonal coupling between states 1 and 2 further lowers the energy. This illustrates that variational optimisation with a multi-function basis always produces energies at or below those of the individual basis functions.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

The overlap matrix $\mathbf{S}$ for a non-orthogonal basis is positive definite (all eigenvalues $> 0$). Explain why this guarantees that the secular equation $\det(\mathbf{H} - E\mathbf{S}) = 0$ has $N$ real roots.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Transform to an orthonormal basis using the inverse square root of $\mathbf{S}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $\mathbf{S}$ is positive definite, it has a unique positive definite square root $\mathbf{S}^{1/2}$ and inverse square root $\mathbf{S}^{-1/2}$. Define new coefficients $\mathbf{d} = \mathbf{S}^{1/2}\mathbf{c}$. The generalised eigenvalue problem $\mathbf{Hc} = E\mathbf{Sc}$ transforms to $\mathbf{S}^{-1/2}\mathbf{H}\mathbf{S}^{-1/2}\mathbf{d} = E\mathbf{d}$. The matrix $\mathbf{H}' = \mathbf{S}^{-1/2}\mathbf{H}\mathbf{S}^{-1/2}$ is symmetric (Hermitian in the complex case), so it has $N$ real eigenvalues by the spectral theorem. The eigenvalues of $\mathbf{H}'$ are exactly the roots of the secular equation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the Hylleraas-Undheim theorem: if $\{E_k^{(N)}\}$ are the eigenvalues from an $N$-function basis and $\{E_k^{(N+1)}\}$ from an $(N+1)$-function basis (the same basis plus one additional function), then the eigenvalues interlace:

$$
E_0^{(N+1)} \leq E_0^{(N)} \leq E_1^{(N+1)} \leq E_1^{(N)} \leq \cdots \leq E_{N-1}^{(N)} \leq E_N^{(N+1)}.
$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the Courant-Fischer min-max theorem to the two nested variational spaces.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the Courant-Fischer (min-max) theorem, the $k$-th eigenvalue of $\hat{H}$ restricted to the variational space $\mathcal{V}_N$ is

$$
E_k^{(N)} = \min_{\mathcal{S} \subset \mathcal{V}_N,\, \dim \mathcal{S} = k+1} \max_{|\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{S},\, \langle\psi|\psi\rangle=1} \langle\psi|\hat{H}|\psi\rangle.
$$

Since $\mathcal{V}_N \subset \mathcal{V}_{N+1}$, any $(k+1)$-dimensional subspace of $\mathcal{V}_N$ is also a subspace of $\mathcal{V}_{N+1}$. The minimum over the larger collection of subspaces (from $\mathcal{V}_{N+1}$) is at most the minimum over the smaller collection (from $\mathcal{V}_N$), giving $E_k^{(N+1)} \leq E_k^{(N)}$.

For the upper bound, consider the $(k+1)$-dimensional subspace of $\mathcal{V}_{N+1}$ that achieves $E_k^{(N+1)}$. If this subspace lies entirely in $\mathcal{V}_N$, then $E_k^{(N+1)} \geq E_k^{(N)}$ by definition. If it extends outside $\mathcal{V}_N$, the interlacing property follows from the eigenvalue interlacing theorem for bordered matrices: adding a row and column to a symmetric matrix produces eigenvalues that interlace the original ones.

The full chain $E_k^{(N+1)} \leq E_k^{(N)} \leq E_{k+1}^{(N+1)}$ is the standard interlacing result, proving that the variational eigenvalues converge monotonically from above as the basis grows.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, numeric).**

The H$_2^+$ ion at an internuclear separation of $R = 2.0\,a_0$ has matrix elements $\alpha = -1.000$ hartree, $\beta = -0.364$ hartree, and $S = 0.586$ (using exact 1s AOs). Compute $E_-$, $E_+$, and the binding energy $\Delta E = E_- - \alpha$. Compare with the exact ground-state energy of H$_2^+$ at this distance, $-1.0536$ hartree.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$E_{\pm} = (\alpha \pm \beta)/(1 \pm S)$. The variational energy is an upper bound to the exact energy.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$E_- = (\alpha + \beta)/(1 + S) = (-1.000 + (-0.364))/(1 + 0.586) = -1.364/1.586 = -0.8600$ hartree.

$E_+ = (\alpha - \beta)/(1 - S) = (-1.000 - (-0.364))/(1 - 0.586) = -0.636/0.414 = -1.5362$ hartree.

Binding energy: $\Delta E = E_- - \alpha = -0.8600 - (-1.000) = 0.1400$ hartree $= 3.81$ eV. This is positive, meaning the trial energy is *above* $\alpha$. The problem is that with $S = 0.586$, the overlap correction overwhelms the resonance stabilisation.

However, the *exact* ground state at $R = 2.0\,a_0$ is $-1.0536$ hartree. Our $E_- = -0.8600$ is indeed above this (variational upper bound holds), but it is a poor approximation. The minimal basis (two 1s functions) cannot describe the orbital contraction that occurs as the electron binds to both nuclei. Adding $2p_\sigma$ basis functions to the basis set significantly improves the result.

Note: the exact energy at $R = 2.0\,a_0$ gives a binding energy of $-1.0536 - (-0.5) = -0.0536$ hartree relative to a separated H atom ($E = -0.5$ hartree). The simple LCAO treatment captures only about 60% of the binding.

## The Roothaan equations and computational cost [Master]

For closed-shell molecules, the Hartree-Fock equations $\hat{f}\,\chi_a = \varepsilon_a\,\chi_a$ are a set of coupled integro-differential equations with no closed-form solution. Roothaan (1951) and Hall (1951) independently showed that expanding each molecular orbital in a finite set of $K$ basis functions $\{\phi_\mu\}$ converts the Hartree-Fock equations into a matrix algebraic equation. Writing $\psi_a = \sum_{\mu=1}^K C_{\mu a}\,\phi_\mu$, the Fock equation becomes the **Roothaan equation**

$$
\mathbf{F}\,\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{S}\,\mathbf{C}\,\boldsymbol{\varepsilon},
$$

where $F_{\mu\nu} = \langle \phi_\mu | \hat{f} | \phi_\nu \rangle$ is the Fock matrix, $S_{\mu\nu} = \langle \phi_\mu | \phi_\nu \rangle$ is the overlap matrix, $\mathbf{C}$ is the matrix of orbital coefficients, and $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ is the diagonal matrix of orbital energies. This is a generalised eigenvalue problem, solved in each SCF iteration.

The Fock matrix elements depend on two-electron integrals over the basis functions:

$$
(\mu\nu|\lambda\sigma) = \iint \phi_\mu(\mathbf{r}_1)\,\phi_\nu(\mathbf{r}_1)\,\frac{1}{r_{12}}\,\phi_\lambda(\mathbf{r}_2)\,\phi_\sigma(\mathbf{r}_2)\,d\mathbf{r}_1\,d\mathbf{r}_2.
$$

The number of unique two-electron integrals (using all permutational symmetry) scales as $O(K^4/8)$ for $K$ basis functions. For a moderate-sized molecule with $K = 500$ basis functions (roughly a 30-atom system at the 6-31G(d) level), this gives approximately $5.2 \times 10^9$ integrals. At 8 bytes per double-precision number, this requires about 40 GB of storage. This $O(K^4)$ scaling was the primary bottleneck of quantum chemistry for decades.

Modern implementations avoid storing all integrals. The **resolution-of-the-identity (RI)** approximation replaces the four-centre integral with a product of three-centre integrals expanded in an auxiliary basis, reducing the cost to $O(K^3)$. **Density fitting** is the most common form of RI. For large systems, **linear-scaling** methods exploit the locality of Gaussian functions: integrals involving distant basis functions are negligibly small and can be neglected, reducing the effective scaling to $O(K)$ for extended systems.

The SCF cycle itself converges the density matrix $\mathbf{P}$ through iterative updates. The convergence is not guaranteed to be monotonic and can oscillate. **Damping** (mixing old and new density matrices), **level shifting** (raising virtual orbital energies to reduce mixing), and **DIIS** (direct inversion in the iterative subspace, Pulay 1980) are standard acceleration techniques. DIIS extrapolates from previous iterations to predict the converged solution, typically achieving convergence in 6--15 iterations for well-behaved systems.

### Formal scaling of quantum chemical methods

| Method | Formal scaling | Typical system size limit |
|--------|---------------|--------------------------|
| Hartree-Fock (HF) | $O(K^4)$ | ~500 atoms (with RI) |
| MP2 | $O(K^5)$ | ~100 atoms |
| CCSD | $O(K^6)$ | ~30 atoms |
| CCSD(T) | $O(K^7)$ | ~20 atoms |
| Full CI | $O(e^K)$ | ~10 electrons, small basis |

These scalings are the reason DFT ($O(K^3)$ with RI, or $O(K)$ with local methods) dominates routine computational chemistry despite its approximations.

## Basis set convergence and the complete basis set limit [Master]

The variational principle guarantees that the Hartree-Fock energy decreases monotonically as the basis set grows. The **complete basis set (CBS) limit** is the energy obtained in the limit $K \to \infty$. In practice, basis set convergence is systematic and can be extrapolated.

Dunning's **correlation-consistent basis sets** (cc-pV$n$Z, where $n = $ D, T, Q, 5, 6) are designed for systematic convergence of the correlation energy. The cardinal number $n$ controls the flexibility: cc-pVDZ (double-zeta), cc-pVTZ (triple-zeta), cc-pVQZ (quadruple-zeta), cc-pV5Z, cc-pV6Z. Each step adds a full shell of higher-angular-momentum functions, and the correlation energy converges approximately as $E_\text{corr}(n) \approx E_\text{corr}^\text{CBS} + A/n^3$ (Helgaker et al., 1997).

For the Hartree-Fock energy, the convergence is exponential in $n$ (much faster). The slow convergence of the correlation energy dominates computational cost, because each additional angular momentum shell roughly triples the basis set size while increasing the integral count by a factor of $3^4 = 81$.

**Basis set superposition error (BSSE)** arises when two molecules interact: each monomer "borrows" basis functions from its partner, artificially lowering its energy. The counterpoise correction (Boys and Bernardi, 1970) computes each monomer in the full dimer basis to remove this artifact. BSSE decreases with basis set quality and vanishes at the CBS limit.

The practical hierarchy for basis set selection follows the problem. For geometry optimisations and vibrational frequencies, 6-31G(d) or def2-SVP suffices. For accurate energies, cc-pVTZ or def2-TZVP is the minimum. For benchmark-quality thermochemistry, cc-pVQZ or cc-pV5Z with CBS extrapolation is standard. The choice of basis set is often the single most important decision in a quantum chemical calculation.

## The Roothaan equations for H$_2^+$ [Master]

The simplest molecular application of the Roothaan formalism is H$_2^+$, which has only one electron. The Fock operator reduces to the core Hamiltonian (no Coulomb or exchange terms), so the Roothaan equation becomes a simple $K \times K$ generalised eigenvalue problem. With a minimal basis of two 1s STOs:

$$
\begin{pmatrix} H_{AA} & H_{AB} \\ H_{BA} & H_{BB} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} c_A \\ c_B \end{pmatrix} = E \begin{pmatrix} 1 & S \\ S & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} c_A \\ c_B \end{pmatrix},
$$

where $H_{AA} = H_{BB} = \alpha$ (by symmetry of the two identical nuclei). The solutions are the bonding ($\sigma_g$) and antibonding ($\sigma_u$) combinations derived in the beginner worked example.

Enlarging the basis to include 2$s$ and 2$p_\sigma$ functions on each centre gives a $6 \times 6$ problem. The additional flexibility allows the bonding orbital to contract and polarise toward the internuclear region, recovering more of the binding energy. With this extended basis, the calculated equilibrium bond length and dissociation energy of H$_2^+$ approach the exact values ($R_e = 1.997\,a_0$, $D_e = 0.1026$ hartree) to within chemical accuracy.

The convergence pattern is instructive. The minimal (two 1s) basis recovers about 60% of the binding energy. Adding 2$p_\sigma$ functions (four-function basis) recovers about 90%. A full triple-zeta plus polarisation basis recovers over 99%. The remaining 1% requires high-angular-momentum functions that describe the cusp and polarisation with increasing fidelity.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The variational principle in quantum mechanics was first applied by Schrodinger himself in 1926, who used it to derive the wave equation from an energy minimisation principle. The linear variational method (Rayleigh-Ritz) has older roots in classical mechanics and the theory of vibrations. Rayleigh (1877) used it to find approximate vibration frequencies of continuous systems, and Ritz (1909) formalised the procedure for arbitrary linear expansions.

The secular determinant takes its name from the "secular equation" of celestial mechanics, where it arose in the study of long-period (secular) perturbations of planetary orbits. The mathematical structure -- a determinant set to zero whose roots are eigenvalues -- is identical, whether the context is planetary dynamics or molecular orbitals.

Roothaan's 1951 paper ("New Developments in Molecular Orbital Theory", *Rev. Mod. Phys.* **23**, 69--89) transformed quantum chemistry from a field of clever hand calculations to one amenable to systematic computation. By recasting the Hartree-Fock equations as a matrix eigenvalue problem, Roothaan made it possible to program the entire SCF procedure on the electronic computers that were just becoming available. Hall's independent derivation appeared the same year. The Roothaan-Hall equations remain the foundation of every Hartree-Fock and Kohn-Sham DFT program in use today.

The development of Gaussian basis sets followed a parallel trajectory. Boys (1950) first proposed using Gaussian functions, recognising their computational advantages. The price -- poor representation of the nuclear cusp and exponential tail -- was judged acceptable given the speed of integral evaluation. The contracted Gaussian basis sets of Pople and coworkers (STO-$n$G, 3-21G, 6-31G, 6-311G with various polarisation and diffuse extensions) became the standard for routine calculations from the 1970s onward. Dunning's correlation-consistent basis sets (cc-pV$n$Z), introduced in 1989, were designed for systematic convergence of electron correlation and are now the standard for high-accuracy work.

The philosophical significance of the variational principle lies in its relationship to exact solutions. The variational energy is always an upper bound, but there is no general lower bound. A calculation yielding $E = -76.02$ hartree for water tells you the true energy is below this value, but not by how much. The convergence of the variational energy with increasing basis set size provides empirical evidence for approach to the exact result, but convergence itself is not a proof of correctness. This asymmetry -- firm upper bounds but no firm lower bounds -- is a structural feature of the variational method that distinguishes it from methods with two-sided error bounds.
