## Intuition [Beginner]

Unit [14.08.01] introduced the rate law: $\text{Rate} = k[A]^m$. The rate law tells you how fast a reaction proceeds at any given concentration. But chemists usually measure concentrations at several times and need to extract the order $m$ and the rate constant $k$ from that data. The **integrated rate law** gives concentration as a function of time, which is the form you need.

Each reaction order produces a distinct mathematical relationship between concentration and time. For a **zeroth-order** reaction, concentration decreases linearly with time. For a **first-order** reaction, the natural log of concentration decreases linearly with time. For a **second-order** reaction, the reciprocal of concentration increases linearly with time.

The **half-life** is the time it takes for the concentration of a reactant to drop to half its starting value. First-order reactions have a special property: the half-life depends only on the rate constant, not on the starting concentration. This is why radioactive decay has a characteristic half-life that never changes regardless of how much material is present.

## Visual [Beginner]

Each reaction order produces a characteristic graphical signature when you plot the data the right way:

| Order | Rate law | Linear plot | Slope | Half-life |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | Rate $= k$ | $[A]$ vs. $t$ | $-k$ | $[A]_0 / (2k)$ |
| 1 | Rate $= k[A]$ | $\ln[A]$ vs. $t$ | $-k$ | $\ln 2 / k$ |
| 2 | Rate $= k[A]^2$ | $1/[A]$ vs. $t$ | $+k$ | $1/(k[A]_0)$ |

![Three panels showing concentration-vs-time curves for zeroth, first, and second order reactions. The left panel shows a straight line decreasing to zero for zeroth order. The centre panel shows exponential decay for first order. The right panel shows a curve that drops steeply at first then levels off for second order. Each panel also shows the corresponding linearised plot inset.](/img/14.08.02-order-plots-placeholder.svg)

The key experimental technique is to try all three linearisations. Whichever plot gives a straight line tells you the reaction order, and the slope gives you $k$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The decomposition of $\text{N}_2\text{O}_5$ at 45$^\circ$C is first-order with $k = 6.2 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{s}^{-1}$. Starting from $[A]_0 = 0.500\,\text{M}$:

**Half-life.** $t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / k = 0.693 / 6.2 \times 10^{-4} = 1118\,\text{s} \approx 18.6\,\text{min}$.

**Concentration after 30 minutes.** Thirty minutes is 1800 seconds.

$$[A]_t = [A]_0 \cdot e^{-kt} = 0.500 \cdot e^{-6.2 \times 10^{-4} \times 1800} = 0.500 \cdot e^{-1.116} = 0.500 \times 0.328 = 0.164\,\text{M}$$

About 33% remains after 30 minutes.

**Time to reach 0.100 M.**

$$\ln(0.100/0.500) = -kt \implies \ln(0.200) = -6.2 \times 10^{-4}\,t \implies -1.609 = -6.2 \times 10^{-4}\,t$$

$t = 1.609 / 6.2 \times 10^{-4} = 2595\,\text{s} \approx 43.2\,\text{min}$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### The general integration

For a reaction $A \rightarrow$ products with rate law $v = k[A]^m$, the differential rate equation is:

$$-\frac{d[A]}{dt} = k[A]^m.$$

Separating variables and integrating from $t = 0$ to $t$ with corresponding concentrations $[A]_0$ and $[A]_t$:

$$\int_{[A]_0}^{[A]_t} \frac{d[A]}{[A]^m} = -k\int_0^t dt' = -kt.$$

The integral on the left evaluates differently depending on $m$.

### Zeroth order ($m = 0$)

$$\int_{[A]_0}^{[A]_t} d[A] = -kt \implies [A]_t = [A]_0 - kt.$$

The concentration decreases linearly. The reaction proceeds at a constant rate regardless of how much reactant is left. This behaviour is observed in surface-catalysed reactions and enzyme-saturated reactions where the catalyst is the limiting factor, not the reactant concentration. The reactant is exhausted at $t = [A]_0 / k$.

Setting $[A]_t = [A]_0 / 2$:

$$t_{1/2} = \frac{[A]_0}{2k}.$$

### First order ($m = 1$)

$$\int_{[A]_0}^{[A]_t} \frac{d[A]}{[A]} = -kt \implies \ln\frac{[A]_t}{[A]_0} = -kt \implies [A]_t = [A]_0\,e^{-kt}.$$

A plot of $\ln[A]$ vs. $t$ is a straight line of slope $-k$. The fraction remaining at time $t$ is $[A]_t/[A]_0 = e^{-kt}$, which depends only on $kt$ -- a dimensionless combination. Setting $[A]_t = [A]_0/2$:

$$t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{k}.$$

The half-life is constant: every successive half-life reduces the concentration by the same factor of 2, regardless of how much reactant is present. This is the unique property of first-order kinetics and explains why radioactive isotopes have a characteristic half-life.

### Second order ($m = 2$)

$$\int_{[A]_0}^{[A]_t} [A]^{-2}\,d[A] = -kt \implies \left[-\frac{1}{[A]}\right]_{[A]_0}^{[A]_t} = -kt \implies \frac{1}{[A]_t} = \frac{1}{[A]_0} + kt.$$

A plot of $1/[A]$ vs. $t$ is a straight line of slope $k$. Setting $[A]_t = [A]_0/2$:

$$t_{1/2} = \frac{1}{k[A]_0}.$$

The half-life depends on the initial concentration: starting from a lower concentration gives a longer half-life. Each successive half-life doubles (because $[A]_0$ halves each time, and $t_{1/2} \propto 1/[A]_0$).

### Graphical determination of order

Given time-concentration data for a reaction of unknown order, the standard procedure is:

1. Plot $[A]$ vs. $t$. If linear, the reaction is zeroth order.
2. Plot $\ln[A]$ vs. $t$. If linear, the reaction is first order.
3. Plot $1/[A]$ vs. $t$. If linear, the reaction is second order.

Only one of these three plots will be a straight line (for reactions of order 0, 1, or 2). The slope of the linear plot gives $k$ (with appropriate sign).

### Pseudo-first-order kinetics

For a bimolecular reaction $A + B \rightarrow$ products with rate law $v = k[A][B]$, the integrated rate law has no simple closed form. If one reactant is present in large excess (typically at least 10-fold, preferably 20-fold or more), its concentration remains approximately constant throughout the reaction: $[B] \approx [B]_0$. The rate law becomes:

$$v = k[A][B]_0 = k'[A], \qquad k' = k[B]_0.$$

The reaction follows first-order kinetics with an effective rate constant $k'$ that depends on the concentration of the excess reactant. Varying $[B]_0$ changes $k'$, and plotting $k'$ vs. $[B]_0$ recovers the true bimolecular rate constant $k$ from the slope.

This technique is ubiquitous in experimental kinetics because first-order data analysis is far simpler than the integrated bimolecular law.

### Radioactive decay

Radioactive decay is a first-order process. Each radioactive nucleus decays independently with a constant probability per unit time $\lambda$ (the decay constant). The number of nuclei remaining after time $t$ is:

$$N(t) = N_0\,e^{-\lambda t}.$$

The half-life is $t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / \lambda$, and the mean lifetime is $\tau = 1/\lambda = t_{1/2} / \ln 2$. The activity (decays per second) is $A = \lambda N$, which also decays exponentially. Carbon-14 dating uses the known half-life of $^{14}\text{C}$ (5730 years) to determine the age of organic materials by measuring the remaining $^{14}\text{C}$ fraction.

### Worked example: determining order from data

The following data were collected for the decomposition of a compound $A$ at constant temperature:

| $t$ (s) | $[A]$ (M) |
|---|---|
| 0 | 0.800 |
| 100 | 0.600 |
| 200 | 0.450 |
| 400 | 0.253 |
| 600 | 0.142 |

Testing first order: compute $\ln[A]$ and check linearity.

| $t$ (s) | $\ln[A]$ |
|---|---|
| 0 | $-0.223$ |
| 100 | $-0.511$ |
| 200 | $-0.799$ |
| 400 | $-1.374$ |
| 600 | $-1.951$ |

$\ln[A]$ vs. $t$ is linear with slope $\approx -2.87 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{s}^{-1}$, so the reaction is first order with $k \approx 2.87 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{s}^{-1}$. Half-life: $t_{1/2} = 0.693 / 2.87 \times 10^{-3} = 241\,\text{s}$.

## Key result [Intermediate+]

### Summary of integrated rate laws and half-lives

**Theorem (Integrated rate laws).** *For a reaction $A \rightarrow$ products with rate law $v = k[A]^m$:*

| $m$ | Integrated law | Linear plot | $t_{1/2}$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| 0 | $[A]_t = [A]_0 - kt$ | $[A]$ vs. $t$, slope $-k$ | $[A]_0 / (2k)$ |
| 1 | $\ln[A]_t = \ln[A]_0 - kt$ | $\ln[A]$ vs. $t$, slope $-k$ | $\ln 2 / k$ |
| 2 | $1/[A]_t = 1/[A]_0 + kt$ | $1/[A]$ vs. $t$, slope $+k$ | $1 / (k[A]_0)$ |

*First-order half-life is independent of $[A]_0$. Zeroth-order and second-order half-lives depend on $[A]_0$.*

**Proof.** All three cases follow from separating variables in $-d[A]/dt = k[A]^m$ and integrating. The zeroth-order integral is straightforward. The first-order integral gives $\ln([A]_t/[A]_0) = -kt$ by the fundamental theorem of calculus applied to $1/[A]$. The second-order integral gives $-1/[A]_t + 1/[A]_0 = -kt$ by integrating $[A]^{-2}$. Half-life formulas follow by substituting $[A]_t = [A]_0/2$ and solving for $t$.

### Fraction remaining for first-order reactions

For a first-order reaction, the fraction remaining after $n$ half-lives is $(1/2)^n$:

| Half-lives elapsed | Fraction remaining |
|---|---|
| 1 | 0.500 |
| 2 | 0.250 |
| 3 | 0.125 |
| 4 | 0.0625 |
| 5 | 0.03125 |

After 10 half-lives, less than 0.1% of the original reactant remains.

## Generalised integration and the $n$th-order case [Master]

The three integrated rate laws treated in the Intermediate tier are the special cases $m = 0, 1, 2$ of the general ODE $-d[A]/dt = k[A]^m$. For arbitrary integer or non-integer order $m \neq 1$, separation of variables gives:

$$\int_{[A]_0}^{[A]_t} [A]^{-m}\,d[A] = -kt \implies \frac{[A]_t^{1-m} - [A]_0^{1-m}}{1 - m} = -kt.$$

Rearranging:

$$[A]_t^{1-m} = [A]_0^{1-m} + (m-1)kt, \qquad m \neq 1.$$

The first-order case $m = 1$ is singular because $\int [A]^{-1}\,d[A] = \ln[A]$, not $[A]^{1-m}/(1-m)$. This is the standard pole at $m = 1$ that separates the power-law family from the exponential family. For fractional orders (which arise in heterogeneous catalysis and radical chain mechanisms), the power-law expression above applies directly.

The half-life for general $m \neq 1$ is:

$$t_{1/2} = \frac{[A]_0^{1-m}(2^{m-1} - 1)}{(m-1)k}.$$

This expression recovers $t_{1/2} = [A]_0/(2k)$ for $m = 0$ (with $2^{-1} - 1 = -1$, but the overall sign from $(m-1)$ corrects it) and $t_{1/2} = 1/(k[A]_0)$ for $m = 2$ (with $2^{1} - 1 = 1$ and $(m-1) = 1$). The dependence on $[A]_0$ is always through $[A]_0^{1-m}$, so only $m = 1$ gives a concentration-independent half-life.

## The full bimolecular integrated law and Guggenheim's method [Master]

The pseudo-first-order approximation of the Intermediate tier avoids the full integrated bimolecular law because that law is algebraically more involved. For $A + B \rightarrow$ products with $v = k[A][B]$ and $[A]_0 \neq [B]_0$, let $x$ be the extent of reaction ($[A]_t = [A]_0 - x$, $[B]_t = [B]_0 - x$). The rate equation becomes:

$$\frac{dx}{dt} = k([A]_0 - x)([B]_0 - x).$$

Separating and integrating by partial fractions:

$$\int_0^x \frac{dx}{([A]_0 - x)([B]_0 - x)} = kt.$$

The partial-fraction decomposition uses $1/(([A]_0 - x)([B]_0 - x)) = [1/([B]_0 - [A]_0)][1/([A]_0 - x) - 1/([B]_0 - x)]$, giving:

$$\frac{1}{[B]_0 - [A]_0}\ln\frac{[A]_0([B]_0 - x)}{[B]_0([A]_0 - x)} = kt,$$

or equivalently:

$$\frac{1}{[B]_0 - [A]_0}\ln\frac{[B]_t/[B]_0}{[A]_t/[A]_0} = kt.$$

A plot of $\ln([B]_t \cdot [A]_0) / ([A]_t \cdot [B]_0)$ vs. $t$ is linear with slope $k([B]_0 - [A]_0)$. When $[A]_0 = [B]_0$, the formula degenerates (the partial-fraction decomposition divides by zero), and the system reduces to second order in a single variable: $1/[A]_t - 1/[A]_0 = kt$.

**Guggenheim's method.** When the initial concentrations are not known precisely (for instance, when the reaction is initiated by rapid mixing and the first measurement comes seconds later), Guggenheim (1924, *Phil. Mag.* 47) proposed measuring $[A]$ at times $t$ and $t + \tau$ where $\tau$ is a fixed interval. For a first-order reaction:

$$\ln([A]_t - [A]_{t+\tau}) = -kt + \text{const}.$$

A plot of $\ln([A]_t - [A]_{t+\tau})$ vs. $t$ is linear with slope $-k$, and the initial concentration never appears. The method eliminates one of the largest sources of systematic error in kinetics experiments: uncertainty in the mixing-time zero point. Guggenheim's method extends to second-order reactions with $[A]_0 = [B]_0$ by replacing $\ln$ with $1/[A]$, but the resulting expressions are less convenient. Mangelsdorf's variant (1955, *J. Appl. Phys.* 26) and the Kezky-Bruice method extend the idea to arbitrary-order reactions by using numerical differentiation of the Guggenheim plot.

## Pseudo-first-order kinetics: the Tikhonov singular-perturbation foundation [Master]

The Intermediate tier introduced pseudo-first-order kinetics through the practical approximation $[B] \approx [B]_0$ when $[B]_0 \gg [A]_0$. The rigorous mathematical basis is Tikhonov's theorem on singular perturbations of ODE systems. For the bimolecular system $d[A]/dt = -k[A][B]$, $d[B]/dt = -k[A][B]$, define $\varepsilon = [A]_0/[B]_0 \ll 1$. Rescaling $A' = [A]/[A]_0$ and $B' = [B]/[B]_0$ with dimensionless time $\tau = k[A]_0 t$ gives:

$$\frac{dA'}{d\tau} = -A'B', \qquad \varepsilon\frac{dB'}{d\tau} = -A'B'.$$

On the fast time scale $\tau/\varepsilon$, $B'$ relaxes to a quasi-steady state. On the slow time scale $\tau$, $B' \approx 1$ to leading order in $\varepsilon$, and $A'$ evolves as $dA'/d\tau = -A'$, recovering the pseudo-first-order law. Tikhonov's theorem guarantees that the error between the true solution and the pseudo-first-order approximation is $O(\varepsilon)$ on time scales of order $1/(k[B]_0)$, and the approximation improves as $\varepsilon \to 0$. This is the same singular-perturbation framework that underlies the steady-state approximation in enzyme kinetics (see [14.08.01] and [17.04.01]).

The practical consequence: pseudo-first-order conditions are justified when $[B]_0 \geq 10\,[A]_0$ (error below 5% throughout the reaction) and are excellent when $[B]_0 \geq 20\,[A]_0$ (error below 2.5%). Below 10-fold excess, the approximation degrades and the full bimolecular integrated law should be used.

## Radioactive decay and the stochastic-deterministic connection [Master]

Radioactive decay provides the cleanest physical realisation of first-order kinetics. Each unstable nucleus decays independently with a constant probability $\lambda\,dt$ in an infinitesimal time interval $dt$. The number of nuclei $N(t)$ in a sample of initial size $N_0$ follows:

$$N(t) = N_0\,e^{-\lambda t}.$$

This deterministic equation is the law-of-large-numbers limit of an underlying stochastic process. Individual decay events are independent and exponentially distributed with mean waiting time $\tau = 1/\lambda$. The total count $N(t)$ is a binomial random variable with $N_0$ trials and success probability $p = e^{-\lambda t}$, giving standard deviation $\sigma = \sqrt{N_0 p(1-p)}$. For macroscopic samples ($N_0 \sim 10^{23}$), the relative fluctuation $\sigma/N$ is of order $10^{-12}$, so the deterministic law is effectively exact.

Three time scales are used:

- **Half-life:** $t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / \lambda$. The time for half the sample to decay.
- **Mean lifetime:** $\tau = 1/\lambda = t_{1/2}/\ln 2 \approx 1.443\,t_{1/2}$. The average time a nucleus survives before decaying.
- **Tenth-life:** $t_{1/10} = \ln 10 / \lambda \approx 3.322\,t_{1/2}$. The time for 90% of the sample to decay.

**Successive radioactive decay.** Many decay chains involve a parent isotope decaying to a radioactive daughter: $A \xrightarrow{\lambda_A} B \xrightarrow{\lambda_B} C$ (stable). The ODE system is:

$$\frac{dN_A}{dt} = -\lambda_A N_A, \qquad \frac{dN_B}{dt} = \lambda_A N_A - \lambda_B N_B.$$

The solution for $N_A$ is $N_A(t) = N_{A,0}\,e^{-\lambda_A t}$. Substituting into the equation for $N_B$ and solving (by integrating factor or Laplace transform):

$$N_B(t) = \frac{\lambda_A N_{A,0}}{\lambda_B - \lambda_A}\left(e^{-\lambda_A t} - e^{-\lambda_B t}\right), \qquad \lambda_A \neq \lambda_B.$$

Three regimes emerge:

1. **Secular equilibrium** ($\lambda_A \ll \lambda_B$, parent much longer-lived): After a transient of order $1/\lambda_B$, $N_B$ reaches a plateau where $N_B/N_A \approx \lambda_A/\lambda_B$. The daughter activity equals the parent activity.

2. **Transient equilibrium** ($\lambda_A < \lambda_B$, but comparable): The ratio $N_B/N_A$ approaches a constant $\lambda_A/(\lambda_B - \lambda_A)$, but both activities decay measurably.

3. **No equilibrium** ($\lambda_A > \lambda_B$, parent shorter-lived): The daughter outlives the parent. $N_B$ rises to a maximum at $t_{\max} = \ln(\lambda_A/\lambda_B)/(\lambda_A - \lambda_B)$ and then decays with its own half-life.

The $^{238}\text{U}$ decay chain to $^{206}\text{Pb}$ involves 14 successive alpha and beta decays. At secular equilibrium (achieved after $\sim 10^6$ years in a closed system), every isotope in the chain has equal activity. This principle underlies uranium-thorium dating and uranium-lead geochronology, which can date rocks from the early solar system with uncertainties below 1 Myr on ages of 4.5 Gyr.

- **Statistical mechanics [14.07.01].** The exponential decay law for first-order reactions is the macroscopic manifestation of the Poisson process at the molecular level. The connection between stochastic single-molecule kinetics and deterministic rate laws is a law-of-large-numbers result.

- **Acid-base chemistry [14.10.01].** Pseudo-first-order conditions appear naturally in acid-catalysed reactions where $[\text{H}^+]$ is buffered to a constant value, making the reaction effectively first-order in the substrate.

## Historical notes [Master]

The study of reaction rates dates to Wilhelmy (1850), who measured the acid-catalysed inversion of sucrose and found it followed a first-order law. Harcourt and Esson (1866-1867) established the first second-order rate law from the oxidation of oxalic acid by potassium permanganate. Arrhenius (1889) provided the temperature-dependence framework. The systematic classification into zeroth, first, and second order came from Ostwald and van't Hoff in the 1880s. Guggenheim (1926) introduced his method for analysing first-order reactions without needing the infinite-time concentration.
