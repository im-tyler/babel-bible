## Intuition [Beginner]

A **dipole moment** measures how unequally electrons are shared in a molecule. When two atoms with different electronegativities form a bond, the bonding electrons shift toward the more electronegative atom. This creates partial charges: a $\delta+$ on the less electronegative atom and a $\delta-$ on the more electronegative one. This charge separation is a **bond dipole**.

A bond dipole is a vector. It has magnitude (how strong the charge separation is) and direction (which way the electrons are pulled). The dipole points from $\delta+$ to $\delta-$, conventionally drawn as an arrow with a plus sign at the tail.

A **molecular dipole moment** is the vector sum of all bond dipoles in the molecule. Whether a molecule is polar depends on both the polarity of its individual bonds and its 3D geometry. Two identical bond dipoles pointing in opposite directions cancel, giving zero net dipole. Two bond dipoles at an angle add to give a nonzero resultant.

$\text{CO}_2$ is linear with two equal C=O dipoles pointing in opposite directions. They cancel, so $\text{CO}_2$ is nonpolar. $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ is bent, so its two O-H dipoles do not cancel. Water is polar, with a dipole moment of 1.85 D (debye units). This is why water dissolves ionic compounds but $\text{CO}_2$ does not.

Molecular polarity controls **intermolecular forces** -- the attractions between molecules. Polar molecules attract each other through dipole-dipole interactions. Nonpolar molecules interact only through weaker London dispersion forces. When a hydrogen atom is bonded to N, O, or F, the resulting **hydrogen bond** is an especially strong dipole-dipole interaction that dramatically raises boiling points.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Determine whether $\text{NH}_3$ (ammonia) is polar.

Step 1. Identify the geometry. Nitrogen has 5 valence electrons. Three form N-H bonds, leaving one lone pair. Steric number = 4, so the electron-domain geometry is tetrahedral. The molecular geometry is **trigonal pyramidal** (one lone pair).

Step 2. Evaluate bond dipoles. Each N-H bond has a dipole pointing from H ($\delta+$) toward N ($\delta-$), because nitrogen (3.04) is more electronegative than hydrogen (2.20).

Step 3. Add the bond-dipole vectors. Three N-H bond dipoles point upward toward the nitrogen. The lone pair also contributes a dipole in the same direction. Because the molecule is not planar (the nitrogen sits above the plane of the three hydrogens), the horizontal components of the three bond dipoles cancel by symmetry, but the vertical components add.

Step 4. Conclusion. $\text{NH}_3$ has a net dipole moment of 1.47 D directed along the $C_3$ axis from the H-plane toward the nitrogen. **Ammonia is polar.**

Compare with $\text{BF}_3$: trigonal planar, three identical B-F bond dipoles at $120^\circ$. The horizontal components cancel completely. $\text{BF}_3$ is nonpolar despite having polar bonds. The geometry makes the difference.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Bond polarity and electronegativity

A **bond dipole moment** $\vec{\mu}_\text{bond}$ arises when two bonded atoms differ in electronegativity. The magnitude is

$$
\mu_\text{bond} = q \cdot d,
$$

where $q$ is the effective charge separation (in units of $e$) and $d$ is the bond length. The SI unit is coulomb-metres (C$\cdot$m), but the practical unit is the **debye** (D), defined as $1\,\text{D} = 3.336 \times 10^{-30}\,\text{C}\cdot\text{m}$. A single elementary charge separated by 1 Angstrom gives $4.80\,\text{D}$.

Bond polarity correlates with the electronegativity difference $\Delta\chi$ between the bonded atoms:

| $\Delta\chi$ | Bond character | Dipole magnitude |
|---|---|---|
| 0.0 - 0.4 | Nonpolar covalent | $\approx 0$ |
| 0.4 - 1.7 | Polar covalent | Moderate |
| > 1.7 | Mostly ionic | Large |

These boundaries are approximate. The Pauling electronegativity scale assigns $\chi = 2.20$ for H and $\chi = 3.98$ for F, giving $\Delta\chi = 1.78$ for H-F -- a highly polar bond with $\mu = 1.82\,\text{D}$.

### Molecular dipole moments: vector addition

The **molecular dipole moment** is the vector sum of all bond dipoles and lone-pair contributions:

$$
\vec{\mu}_\text{total} = \sum_i \vec{\mu}_i.
$$

For a molecule with two identical bond dipoles of magnitude $\mu_\text{bond}$ at angle $\theta$ to each other, the resultant is

$$
\mu_\text{total} = 2\,\mu_\text{bond}\cos\!\left(\frac{\theta}{2}\right).
$$

For $\text{H}_2\text{O}$: $\mu_\text{bond}(\text{O-H}) \approx 1.51\,\text{D}$, $\theta = 104.5^\circ$, giving $\mu_\text{total} = 2(1.51)\cos(52.25^\circ) = 2(1.51)(0.612) = 1.85\,\text{D}$, matching the experimental value.

For $\text{CO}_2$: $\mu_\text{bond}(\text{C=O}) \approx 2.40\,\text{D}$, $\theta = 180^\circ$, giving $\mu_\text{total} = 2(2.40)\cos(90^\circ) = 0$. The bond dipoles cancel.

### Symmetry and polarity: point-group criteria

A molecule is nonpolar if and only if its point group contains either:

1. A centre of inversion ($i$), or
2. A rotation axis $C_n$ with perpendicular mirror planes $\sigma_h$ (i.e., the point group is one of $D_{nh}$ or $D_{nd}$ for $n \geq 2$).

More practically: if a molecule has symmetry such that all bond dipoles cancel by vector addition, it is nonpolar. The common cases:

- **Linear, symmetric** ($D_{\infty h}$): $\text{CO}_2$, $\text{BeCl}_2$, $\text{XeF}_2$ -- nonpolar.
- **Trigonal planar** ($D_{3h}$): $\text{BF}_3$, $\text{SO}_3$ -- nonpolar.
- **Tetrahedral** ($T_d$): $\text{CCl}_4$, $\text{CH}_4$, $\text{SF}_4$ is seesaw ($C_{2v}$) -- polar.
- **Octahedral** ($O_h$): $\text{SF}_6$, $\text{PF}_6^-$ -- nonpolar.

Any molecule belonging to $C_n$ or $C_{nv}$ (lacking a horizontal mirror plane or inversion centre) is polar, provided it has polar bonds.

### Intermolecular forces

**London dispersion forces** exist between all molecules. They arise from instantaneous dipole-induced dipole interactions. The magnitude depends on molecular polarizability (larger, more electron-rich molecules are more polarizable) and shape (more surface contact increases dispersion). The interaction energy between two polarizable molecules scales as

$$
V_{\text{disp}} \propto -\frac{\alpha_1 \alpha_2}{r^6},
$$

where $\alpha_1, \alpha_2$ are the polarizabilities and $r$ is the intermolecular distance.

**Dipole-dipole interactions** occur between permanent molecular dipoles. The interaction energy between two aligned permanent dipoles scales as

$$
V_{\text{dip-dip}} \propto -\frac{\mu_1^2 \mu_2^2}{r^6\, k_B T}.
$$

The temperature dependence arises because thermal motion disrupts dipole alignment.

**Hydrogen bonding** is a special case of dipole-dipole interaction requiring:

1. A hydrogen atom bonded to N, O, or F (the donor).
2. A lone pair on N, O, or F of another molecule (the acceptor).

Hydrogen bond energies range from 5-40 kJ/mol (conventional hydrogen bonds) up to $\sim$160 kJ/mol for the strongest short, symmetric hydrogen bonds in ions like $\text{HF}_2^-$.

**The ion-dipole interaction** is the strongest of the electrostatic intermolecular forces and is critical for solvation. The energy scales as $V \propto -\mu q / r^2$, where $q$ is the ionic charge and $\mu$ the solvent dipole moment. This is why polar solvents dissolve ionic compounds.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **"Symmetric molecules are always nonpolar."** Only geometric symmetry with identical substituents guarantees cancellation. $\text{CHCl}_3$ is tetrahedral but polar because the three Cl atoms and one H atom are chemically different, breaking the $T_d$ symmetry.

- **"Lone pairs do not contribute to the dipole moment."** Lone pairs carry electron density and contribute to the molecular dipole. In $\text{NH}_3$, the lone pair on nitrogen enhances the dipole moment along the $C_3$ axis. In $\text{NF}_3$, the lone pair dipole partially opposes the N-F bond dipoles, giving a surprisingly small molecular dipole of 0.24 D.

- **"Larger molecules always have larger dipole moments."** Dipole moment depends on the vector arrangement of bond dipoles, not on molecular size. $\text{CCl}_4$ (MW 154) has zero dipole; $\text{HCl}$ (MW 36.5) has a dipole of 1.08 D.

- **"Hydrogen bonds only form between different molecules."** Intramolecular hydrogen bonds occur within a single molecule when the donor and acceptor are in the right geometric arrangement. The enol form of acetylacetone is stabilised by an intramolecular O-H$\cdots$O hydrogen bond.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Dipole cancellation by symmetry).** *A molecule belonging to any point group that contains either an inversion centre $i$ or a $C_n$ axis with $\sigma_h$ mirror planes ($D_{nh}$, $D_{nd}$, $D_{\infty h}$) has zero permanent electric dipole moment. Conversely, molecules belonging to $C_n$ or $C_{nv}$ point groups ($n \geq 1$) generally possess a nonzero dipole moment along the $C_n$ axis, provided the individual bonds are polar.*

**Argument.** An electric dipole moment $\vec{\mu}$ transforms as a vector under the symmetry operations of the molecular point group. For $\vec{\mu}$ to be nonzero, it must transform as the totally symmetric irreducible representation of the point group.

If the point group contains an inversion centre $i$, then the dipole vector transforms as $\vec{\mu} \to -\vec{\mu}$ under inversion (dipole is an odd-parity, or $u$, quantity). For $\vec{\mu}$ to be invariant, it must be zero. All centrosymmetric molecules ($\text{CO}_2$, $\text{SF}_6$, $\text{XeF}_4$, benzene) are therefore nonpolar.

If the point group contains a $C_n$ axis with a perpendicular $\sigma_h$ mirror plane (i.e., $D_{nh}$ or $D_{nd}$), then the component of $\vec{\mu}$ parallel to $C_n$ is reversed by $\sigma_h$ and must vanish. The components perpendicular to $C_n$ vanish by the $C_n$ rotational symmetry (they must be invariant under $C_n$ rotation, which is impossible for a vector perpendicular to the axis unless it is zero, since a rotation by $2\pi/n$ maps it to a different direction). All components are zero, so $\vec{\mu} = 0$.

For $C_{nv}$ groups: the $C_n$ axis does not reverse the parallel component of $\vec{\mu}$. The $\sigma_v$ mirror planes leave the component along $C_n$ unchanged. The perpendicular components vanish by $C_n$ symmetry. Therefore $\vec{\mu}$ is nonzero along $C_n$. Molecules like $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ ($C_{2v}$), $\text{NH}_3$ ($C_{3v}$), and $\text{BrF}_5$ ($C_{4v}$) are polar. $\square$

**Bridge.** This symmetry argument connects to [14.05.01] molecular orbital theory, where the same point-group symmetry determines whether a molecule can have a nonzero transition dipole moment and hence whether an electronic transition is allowed. The dipole moment is a ground-state property; the transition dipole is a transition property; but both are governed by the same group-theoretic selection rules. The classification also connects to [14.12.01] spectroscopy, where IR activity requires a change in dipole moment during a vibration, and Raman activity requires a change in polarizability.

**Exercise 1 (easy, multiple choice).**

$\text{SF}_4$ has a seesaw geometry. Is it polar or nonpolar?

A. Nonpolar, because it has four equal S-F bonds

B. Polar, because the axial and equatorial F atoms are not symmetrically equivalent

C. Nonpolar, because sulfur can expand its octet

D. Polar, because sulfur is more electronegative than fluorine

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In a seesaw geometry, two F atoms are axial and two are equatorial. The molecule has $C_{2v}$ symmetry. Does this symmetry cancel the S-F bond dipoles?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** $\text{SF}_4$ has $C_{2v}$ symmetry (one lone pair in an equatorial position of the trigonal bipyramidal electron-domain geometry). The axial S-F bonds (1.547 A) are longer than the equatorial S-F bonds (1.545 A in gas phase, but substantially different in some phases). More importantly, the lone pair breaks the symmetry: there is no $\sigma_h$ plane and no inversion centre. The S-F bond dipoles do not cancel. The molecular dipole moment is 0.63 D, directed from the lone-pair side toward the axial fluorine side.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

The dipole moment of HF is 1.82 D and the bond length is 91.7 pm. Calculate the effective charge separation $q$ in units of the elementary charge $e$. ($1\,\text{D} = 3.336 \times 10^{-30}\,\text{C}\cdot\text{m}$; $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,\text{C}$.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Convert $\mu$ to C$\cdot$m, divide by $d$ to get charge in coulombs, then divide by $e$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mu = 1.82\,\text{D} = 1.82 \times 3.336 \times 10^{-30} = 6.072 \times 10^{-30}\,\text{C}\cdot\text{m}$.

$d = 91.7\,\text{pm} = 91.7 \times 10^{-12}\,\text{m} = 9.17 \times 10^{-11}\,\text{m}$.

$q = \mu / d = 6.072 \times 10^{-30} / 9.17 \times 10^{-11} = 6.62 \times 10^{-20}\,\text{C}$.

$q / e = 6.62 \times 10^{-20} / 1.602 \times 10^{-19} = 0.413\,e$.

The effective charge separation is about 41% of a full elementary charge. HF is highly polar but not fully ionic.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Predict whether $\text{XeF}_4$ is polar. Justify using both the VSEPR geometry and the point-group symmetry argument.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\text{XeF}_4$ has steric number 6 with 4 bonding pairs and 2 lone pairs in trans positions. What is the molecular geometry and point group?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\text{XeF}_4$ is **square planar** with $D_{4h}$ symmetry. The four Xe-F bonds are equivalent and coplanar. By the symmetry theorem, $D_{4h}$ contains a $\sigma_h$ plane (the molecular plane) and a $C_4$ axis, so the molecule must have zero dipole moment. Alternatively, by vector addition: the four Xe-F bond dipoles cancel pairwise (opposite pairs are equal and antiparallel). The two trans lone pairs also cancel by symmetry. $\text{XeF}_4$ is nonpolar.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

The O-H bond dipole in water is approximately 1.51 D. The H-O-H angle is $104.5^\circ$. Calculate the expected molecular dipole moment using vector addition and compare with the experimental value of 1.85 D.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $\mu_\text{total} = 2\,\mu_\text{bond}\cos(\theta/2)$ where $\theta$ is the H-O-H angle.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mu_\text{total} = 2(1.51)\cos(104.5^\circ / 2) = 2(1.51)\cos(52.25^\circ) = 2(1.51)(0.6126) = 1.850\,\text{D}$.

This matches the experimental value of 1.85 D almost exactly. The agreement suggests that the bond dipole model works well for water, with the lone-pair contribution either small or already incorporated into the effective bond dipole value.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Explain why $\text{NF}_3$ (dipole moment 0.24 D) has a much smaller dipole moment than $\text{NH}_3$ (1.47 D) despite having three polar N-F bonds.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider the direction of the N-F bond dipoles relative to the lone-pair dipole. Fluorine is more electronegative than nitrogen. In which direction do the N-F bond dipoles point? In which direction does the lone-pair dipole point?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In $\text{NH}_3$, the N-H bond dipoles point from H toward N (since N is more electronegative). These bond dipoles point upward, in the same direction as the lone-pair dipole (which also points away from the hydrogens, toward the lone pair). The two effects reinforce each other.

In $\text{NF}_3$, the N-F bond dipoles point from N toward F (since F is more electronegative). These bond dipoles point downward, away from the lone pair. The lone-pair dipole still points upward (away from the fluorines), but now the N-F bond dipoles oppose it. The two effects nearly cancel, leaving only 0.24 D.

This is one of the most striking examples of how geometry and electronegativity combine to determine molecular polarity. Both molecules have the same shape (trigonal pyramidal), but the reversal in bond-dipole direction produces opposite effects.

</details>

</aside>

B. $\text{CH}_4 < \text{CH}_3\text{Cl} < \text{CH}_3\text{OH}$

C. $\text{CH}_4 < \text{CH}_3\text{OH} < \text{CH}_3\text{Cl}$

D. $\text{CH}_3\text{Cl} < \text{CH}_4 < \text{CH}_3\text{OH}$

Identify the intermolecular forces present in each. $\text{CH}_4$ has only London dispersion. $\text{CH}_3\text{Cl}$ has dipole-dipole interactions plus dispersion. $\text{CH}_3\text{OH}$ has hydrogen bonding.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** $\text{CH}_4$ (bp $-162^\circ$C) has only London dispersion forces. $\text{CH}_3\text{Cl}$ (bp $-24^\circ$C) is polar and has dipole-dipole interactions in addition to dispersion. $\text{CH}_3\text{OH}$ (bp $65^\circ$C) can form hydrogen bonds (O-H donor, O lone-pair acceptor), which are the strongest intermolecular force among the three. The boiling point ordering reflects the intermolecular force hierarchy: dispersion < dipole-dipole < hydrogen bonding.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

$\text{PCl}_5$ is trigonal bipyramidal. Determine whether it has a dipole moment. Then predict the polarity of $\text{PCl}_3\text{F}_2$ assuming the two fluorines occupy (a) both axial positions and (b) both equatorial positions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\text{PCl}_5$ has $D_{3h}$ symmetry. For the substituted versions, consider whether the axial-equatorial asymmetry in bond dipoles cancels. P-F bonds are more polar than P-Cl bonds.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\text{PCl}_5$: $D_{3h}$ symmetry with a $C_3$ axis and $\sigma_h$ plane. By the symmetry theorem, it is nonpolar.

(a) Both F axial: $D_{3h}$ symmetry is retained (the three equatorial Cl atoms are equivalent, and the two axial F atoms are equivalent). The axial P-F dipoles cancel (equal and opposite). The equatorial P-Cl dipoles cancel by $C_3$ symmetry. **Nonpolar.**

(b) Both F equatorial: The molecule now has $C_{2v}$ symmetry (the two F atoms define a $C_2$ axis in the equatorial plane, with $\sigma_v$ planes). The three equatorial positions are no longer equivalent (two F, one Cl). The axial P-Cl dipoles still cancel (equal and opposite). But the equatorial bond dipoles do not cancel: two P-F dipoles and one P-Cl dipole in the equatorial plane. The P-F dipoles are larger than the P-Cl dipole, so there is a net dipole in the equatorial plane, pointing from Cl toward the F-F midpoint. **Polar.**

</details>

**Exercise 8 (hard, numeric).**

The boiling points of the hydrogen halides are: HF $20^\circ$C, HCl $-85^\circ$C, HBr $-67^\circ$C, HI $-35^\circ$C. Explain why HF has the highest boiling point, and why the trend for HCl, HBr, HI increases despite decreasing bond polarity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

HF can form hydrogen bonds. For the other three, hydrogen bonding is negligible. What intermolecular force dominates for HCl, HBr, and HI, and how does it change with molecular size?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

HF forms hydrogen bonds (F-H$\cdots$F interactions), giving it an anomalously high boiling point. The other three hydrogen halides cannot form significant hydrogen bonds because Cl, Br, and I are not electronegative enough.

For HCl, HBr, and HI, the dominant intermolecular force is London dispersion, which increases with molecular size and polarizability. The polarizability increases HCl < HBr < HI as the electron cloud gets larger and more diffuse. Despite the decreasing bond polarity ($\mu$: HCl 1.08 D, HBr 0.82 D, HI 0.44 D), the increasing dispersion forces more than compensate, raising the boiling points from $-85^\circ$C to $-35^\circ$C.

This is a classic example where the dispersion force dominates over dipole-dipole interactions for larger molecules. The cross-over occurs around HBr: dipole-dipole interactions are still important for HCl but become secondary to dispersion for HBr and HI.

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

cis-1,2-Dichloroethene ($\text{ClHC=CHCl}$, cis) has a dipole moment of 1.90 D. trans-1,2-Dichloroethene has zero dipole moment. Explain using vector addition of the C-Cl and C-H bond dipoles and the geometry imposed by the $sp^2$ hybridization at each carbon.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each carbon in the C=C double bond is $sp^2$, making the molecule planar. In the cis isomer, both Cl atoms are on the same side. In the trans isomer, they are on opposite sides.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Each carbon has one C-Cl bond and one C-H bond in addition to the C=C bond. The C-Cl bond dipole (pointing from C toward Cl, since Cl is more electronegative) and the C-H bond dipole (pointing from H toward C) are both in the molecular plane due to $sp^2$ hybridization.

**cis isomer:** Both Cl atoms are on the same side of the C=C bond. The two C-Cl dipoles have a component perpendicular to the C=C axis that adds constructively (both point in the same direction). The two C-H dipoles have a small perpendicular component in the opposite direction but are much weaker. The net result is a molecular dipole of 1.90 D perpendicular to the C=C axis, pointing from the H-side toward the Cl-side.

**trans isomer:** The Cl atoms are on opposite sides of the C=C bond. The two C-Cl dipoles have perpendicular components that cancel exactly (they are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction). The two C-H dipoles also cancel. The molecular plane is a symmetry element that forces the dipole to be zero. The trans isomer has $C_{2h}$ symmetry (centrosymmetric), guaranteeing zero dipole moment.

This is a direct application of the symmetry theorem: $C_{2h}$ contains an inversion centre, so the trans isomer must be nonpolar.

## Multipole expansion and beyond-dipole interactions [Master]

The dipole moment is the first nonvanishing term in the **multipole expansion** of a charge distribution. For a general charge distribution $\rho(\mathbf{r})$, the electrostatic potential at a distant point $\mathbf{R}$ is

$$
\Phi(\mathbf{R}) = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\left[\frac{Q}{R} + \frac{\vec{\mu}\cdot\hat{R}}{R^2} + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{\alpha,\beta}\frac{Q_{\alpha\beta}\hat{R}_\alpha\hat{R}_\beta}{R^3} + \cdots\right],
$$

where $Q$ is the total charge (zero for neutral molecules), $\vec{\mu}$ is the dipole moment, and $Q_{\alpha\beta}$ is the quadrupole moment tensor. The expansion converges for $R$ much larger than the molecular dimensions.

**Quadrupole moments.** Molecules with zero dipole moment may still have substantial quadrupole moments that affect physical behaviour. The quadrupole moment tensor is

$$
Q_{\alpha\beta} = \int \rho(\mathbf{r})\,(3r_\alpha r_\beta - r^2\delta_{\alpha\beta})\,d\mathbf{r}.
$$

$\text{CO}_2$ has zero dipole but a large negative quadrupole moment ($Q_{zz} \approx -4.3 \times 10^{-40}\,\text{C}\cdot\text{m}^2$). This arises from the charge distribution $(-\delta)(+2\delta)(-\delta)$ along the molecular axis. The quadrupole drives $\text{CO}_2$'s solubility in water (quadrupole-dipole interaction with water's permanent dipole) and the quadrupole-quadrupole interactions between $\text{CO}_2$ molecules in the solid and liquid phases.

Benzene has zero dipole but a large positive quadrupole moment from the $\pi$-electron cloud distributed above and below the ring plane. The ring carbons are partially positive; the $\pi$ cloud is negative. This quadrupole drives the characteristic face-to-face and parallel-displaced stacking geometries of aromatic rings and the cation-$\pi$ interactions important in protein-ligand binding.

**Polarizability and induced dipoles.** When a molecule is placed in an external electric field $\mathbf{E}$, its electron cloud distorts, creating an **induced dipole**:

$$
\vec{\mu}_\text{ind} = \alpha \mathbf{E},
$$

where $\alpha$ is the (scalar, for isotropic molecules) **polarizability**. For anisotropic molecules, $\alpha$ is a tensor. The polarizability determines the London dispersion interaction energy between two molecules:

$$
V_{\text{disp}} = -\frac{3}{2}\frac{\alpha_1 \alpha_2}{r^6}\frac{I_1 I_2}{I_1 + I_2},
$$

where $I_1, I_2$ are the ionisation energies. This is the London formula, showing that larger, more polarizable molecules have stronger dispersion forces.

**The Lennard-Jones potential.** The combined repulsion (Pauli exclusion at short range) and dispersion attraction gives the Lennard-Jones 12-6 potential:

$$
V(r) = 4\epsilon\left[\left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^{12} - \left(\frac{\sigma}{r}\right)^{6}\right],
$$

where $\epsilon$ is the well depth and $\sigma$ is the distance at which $V = 0$. The $r^{-6}$ attractive term encompasses both dispersion and dipole-dipole (orientation-averaged) contributions. The $r^{-12}$ repulsive term is empirical. This potential is the workhorse of molecular dynamics simulations and captures the essential physics of intermolecular interactions for simple fluids.

## Dipole moment measurement: experimental methods [Master]

**Debye's method (solution measurement).** The dipole moment of a molecule dissolved in a nonpolar solvent is determined from the **Debye equation**, which relates the molar polarisation $P_m$ to the dielectric constant $\epsilon_r$ of the solution:

$$
P_m = \frac{\epsilon_r - 1}{\epsilon_r + 2}\frac{M}{\rho} = \frac{N_A}{3\epsilon_0}\left(\alpha + \frac{\mu^2}{3k_B T}\right),
$$

where $M$ is the molar mass, $\rho$ is the density, $N_A$ is Avogadro's number, and $\alpha$ is the polarizability. By measuring $P_m$ at several temperatures and plotting $P_m$ vs $1/T$, the slope gives $\mu^2$ and the intercept gives $\alpha$. This is the classic method, developed by Debye in the 1910s and refined by Sutton and others [ref: TODO_REF Sutton 1958].

**Microwave spectroscopy.** The rotational spectrum of a polar molecule provides a direct measurement of the dipole moment. The Stark effect (splitting of rotational lines in an applied electric field) depends on $\mu^2$. The magnitude of the splitting gives the dipole moment to high precision ($\pm 0.01\,\text{D}$). This method works for gas-phase molecules and gives the dipole moment along each principal axis of the inertia tensor separately.

**Molecular beam electric resonance.** Developed by Ramsey and colleagues in the 1950s, this technique measures the dipole moment of isolated molecules in a molecular beam, free from solvent effects. It achieves the highest precision ($\pm 0.001\,\text{D}$) and can resolve the dipole moment in different vibrational states.

**Computational prediction.** Modern quantum-chemical methods (DFT, coupled-cluster) compute dipole moments from the electron density: $\vec{\mu} = \int \rho(\mathbf{r})\,\mathbf{r}\,d\mathbf{r}$. The accuracy depends on the basis set and electron correlation treatment. At the CCSD(T)/aug-cc-pVTZ level, computed dipole moments agree with experiment to within 0.05 D for small molecules.

## Boiling point trends: a quantitative analysis [Master]

The boiling point of a liquid is the temperature at which the vapour pressure equals the external pressure. The enthalpy of vaporisation, $\Delta H_\text{vap}$, reflects the total intermolecular energy that must be overcome. For non-hydrogen-bonded liquids, $\Delta H_\text{vap}$ is dominated by dispersion and dipole-dipole contributions.

**Group contributions and polarity.** Within a homologous series, boiling points increase with molecular weight due to increasing dispersion forces. The polarity effect appears as a constant offset: for molecules of similar molecular weight, the more polar one has the higher boiling point. This offset is typically 30-80$^\circ$C for molecules with $\mu \approx 1-2\,\text{D}$ compared to nonpolar analogues.

The boiling points of the first-row hydrides illustrate the hydrogen-bonding anomaly:

| Compound | MW | $\mu$ (D) | bp ($^\circ$C) |
|---|---|---|---|
| $\text{CH}_4$ | 16 | 0 | $-162$ |
| $\text{NH}_3$ | 17 | 1.47 | $-33$ |
| $\text{H}_2\text{O}$ | 18 | 1.85 | $+100$ |
| HF | 20 | 1.82 | $+20$ |

$\text{H}_2\text{O}$ has the highest boiling point despite being the lightest among the heavier three, because each water molecule can donate two hydrogen bonds (two O-H groups) and accept two (two lone pairs), forming an extensive 3D hydrogen-bond network. HF can donate one and accept three but forms linear chains rather than a 3D network. $\text{NH}_3$ can donate one and accept one, giving a less extensive network.

**Trouton's rule and its exceptions.** For many nonpolar liquids, $\Delta S_\text{vap} = \Delta H_\text{vap} / T_\text{bp} \approx 88\,\text{J/(mol}\cdot\text{K)}$ (Trouton's rule). Hydrogen-bonded liquids deviate significantly: water has $\Delta S_\text{vap} = 109\,\text{J/(mol}\cdot\text{K)}$ at its normal boiling point, reflecting the large enthalpy cost of breaking the hydrogen-bond network. The deviation from Trouton's rule is a diagnostic for hydrogen bonding.

## Connections [Master]

- **Lewis structures and VSEPR [14.02.01].** The molecular geometry determined by VSEPR is the primary input for predicting polarity. Symmetric geometries ($D_{nh}$, $T_d$, $O_h$) cancel bond dipoles; asymmetric geometries ($C_{nv}$) generally produce nonzero molecular dipoles. The steric number and lone-pair count determine the geometry, which determines the polarity.

- **Hybridization [14.02.02].** The hybridization at each atom determines the bond angles, which in turn determine the vector sum of bond dipoles. $sp^3$ centres with different substituents produce asymmetric charge distributions; $sp^2$ centres in double bonds constrain the substituents to a plane, enabling cis/trans polarity differences (as in cis- vs trans-dichloroethene).

- **Atomic structure and electronegativity [14.01.02].** Electronegativity differences between bonded atoms determine bond polarity. The periodic trends in electronegativity (increasing across a period, decreasing down a group) predict which bonds are polar and the direction of the bond dipole. These are the building blocks from which molecular dipoles are constructed.

- **Solutions and phase equilibria [14.09.01].** Molecular polarity determines solubility ("like dissolves like"), miscibility, and phase behaviour. The intermolecular forces discussed here (dispersion, dipole-dipole, hydrogen bonding) are the microscopic origin of macroscopic properties like vapour pressure, boiling point, and surface tension.

- **Acid-base chemistry [14.10.01].** Solvent polarity affects acid-base equilibria. Water's high dielectric constant ($\epsilon_r = 80.1$ at $20^\circ$C) and hydrogen-bonding ability stabilise ions, making it an excellent solvent for ionic reactions. The leveling effect in water (no acid stronger than $\text{H}_3\text{O}^+$ can exist in aqueous solution) is a direct consequence of water's polarity.

- **Molecular orbital theory [14.05.01].** MO theory provides the quantitative framework for computing dipole moments from the electron density distribution. The vector-addition model of bond dipoles is an approximation; the MO approach computes $\vec{\mu} = \int \rho(\mathbf{r})\,\mathbf{r}\,d\mathbf{r}$ directly from the molecular wave function, capturing lone-pair contributions and electron delocalisation that the simple bond-dipole model misses.

- **Spectroscopy [14.12.01].** IR activity requires a change in dipole moment during a vibration. $\text{CO}_2$ has symmetric and antisymmetric C=O stretches: the symmetric stretch does not change the dipole (it remains zero) and is IR-inactive; the antisymmetric stretch creates a transient dipole and is IR-active. Raman activity requires a change in polarizability. The mutual exclusion rule for centrosymmetric molecules (IR-active modes are Raman-inactive and vice versa) follows directly from the symmetry analysis of the dipole moment.

## Historical notes [Master]

**Peter Debye** (1884-1966) introduced the concept of the permanent electric dipole moment of molecules in 1912 and developed the theory of polar molecules in his 1929 monograph *Polar Molecules* [ref: TODO_REF Debye 1929]. Debye showed that the temperature dependence of the dielectric constant of a gas or dilute solution reveals the molecular dipole moment, providing the first experimental method for measuring molecular polarity. He received the Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1936 for this work.

**Johannes van der Waals** (1837-1923) formulated the equation of state that bears his name in 1873, incorporating intermolecular attractive forces (the $a/V^2$ term) and molecular volume (the $b$ term). The attractive forces between neutral molecules are now called van der Waals forces, encompassing London dispersion, dipole-dipole, and dipole-induced dipole interactions. Van der Waals received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1910.

**Fritz London** (1900-1954) provided the quantum-mechanical explanation of dispersion forces in 1930, showing that fluctuations in the electron distribution of one molecule induce a dipole in a neighbouring molecule, producing an attractive interaction that varies as $r^{-6}$. This resolved the puzzle of why noble gases (with no permanent dipole) still condense to liquids at low temperatures.

**Walter Kossel** (1888-1956) and **G.N. Lewis** independently developed the concept that chemical bonding involves electron sharing (1916). Kossel extended this to ionic bonding and recognised that electronegativity differences produce charge separation in bonds, the physical origin of bond dipoles.

**Linus Pauling** formalised the electronegativity scale in 1932, providing the quantitative basis for predicting bond polarity from atomic properties. His scale, based on bond-energy differences, assigns $\chi$ values to every element and enables prediction of bond-dipole magnitudes from atomic data alone.

**The hydrogen bond** was first recognised by **Wendell Latimer and Worth Rodebush** in 1920, who noted that the anomalous properties of water and HF could only be explained by an attractive interaction between the hydrogen on one molecule and the lone pair on another. The term "hydrogen bond" was coined by **Maurice Huggins** in 1921.

**A.D. Buckingham** extended the multipole-moment framework to quadrupole and octupole moments in the 1950s and 1960s [ref: TODO_REF Buckingham 1959], providing the theoretical tools for understanding the intermolecular forces of nonpolar but quadrupolar molecules like $\text{CO}_2$ and benzene. Buckingham's work connected the experimentally measurable dielectric and refractive properties to the underlying multipole-moment tensor components.
