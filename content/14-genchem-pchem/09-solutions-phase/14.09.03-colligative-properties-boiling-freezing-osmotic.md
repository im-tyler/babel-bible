## Intuition [Beginner]

Dissolve a spoonful of salt in a pot of water. The water now boils at a temperature slightly above 100 degrees C and freezes below 0 degrees C. These shifts are **colligative properties** -- they depend on how many solute particles are dissolved, not on what those particles are. One mole of sugar and one mole of urea raise the boiling point by the same amount because both contribute the same number of molecules.

The underlying cause is entropy. Adding solute increases the disorder of the liquid phase relative to the solid and vapor phases. This changes the temperature at which the liquid can coexist with each phase. The result: higher boiling point, lower freezing point, and lower vapor pressure. All four colligative effects -- vapor-pressure lowering, boiling-point elevation, freezing-point depression, and osmotic pressure -- share a single origin.

**Boiling-point elevation** is quantified by $\Delta T_b = K_b \cdot m$, where $K_b$ is the ebullioscopic constant of the solvent, $m$ is the molality (moles of solute per kilogram of solvent), and $\Delta T_b$ is the increase in boiling temperature. For water, $K_b = 0.512$ K kg/mol. Dissolving 1 mol of glucose in 1 kg of water raises the boiling point by only 0.512 degrees C -- a small effect, but measurable and predictable.

**Freezing-point depression** follows the same pattern: $\Delta T_f = K_f \cdot m$, where $K_f$ is the cryoscopic constant. For water, $K_f = 1.86$ K kg/mol. This is why roads are salted in winter: the dissolved salt lowers the freezing point of water below the ambient temperature, melting ice. The effect is larger per mole than boiling-point elevation because $K_f$ for water exceeds $K_b$.

**Osmotic pressure** is the most sensitive colligative property. When a solution is separated from pure solvent by a semipermeable membrane (permeable to solvent but not to solute), solvent flows into the solution. The pressure required to stop this flow is the osmotic pressure $\Pi$:

$$\Pi = iMRT$$

where $i$ is the van't Hoff factor (the number of particles per formula unit), $M$ is molarity, $R$ is the gas constant, and $T$ is the absolute temperature. Even dilute solutions can generate large osmotic pressures: a 0.1 M solution at 298 K produces about 2.4 atm.

The **van't Hoff factor** $i$ accounts for dissociation. For a nonelectrolyte like glucose, $i = 1$. For NaCl, which dissociates into Na$^+$ and Cl$^-$, $i \approx 2$. For MgCl$_2$, $i \approx 3$. Electrostatic attraction between ions causes $i$ to be slightly less than the integer value, and the deviation grows with concentration. This correction is essential for accurate calculations with electrolyte solutions.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Problem:** A solution is prepared by dissolving 4.50 g of glucose (C$_6$H$_{12}$O$_6$, MW = 180.16 g/mol) in 150.0 g of water. Calculate (a) the boiling-point elevation and (b) the freezing-point depression. Use $K_b = 0.512$ K kg/mol and $K_f = 1.86$ K kg/mol.

**Solution:**

Moles of glucose:

$$n = \frac{4.50 \text{ g}}{180.16 \text{ g/mol}} = 0.02498 \text{ mol}$$

Molality:

$$m = \frac{0.02498 \text{ mol}}{0.1500 \text{ kg}} = 0.1665 \text{ mol/kg}$$

(a) Boiling-point elevation:

$$\Delta T_b = K_b \cdot m = (0.512)(0.1665) = 0.0853 \text{ K}$$

The solution boils at $100.000 + 0.0853 = 100.085$ degrees C.

(b) Freezing-point depression:

$$\Delta T_f = K_f \cdot m = (1.86)(0.1665) = 0.310 \text{ K}$$

The solution freezes at $0.000 - 0.310 = -0.310$ degrees C. Glucose is a nonelectrolyte ($i = 1$), so no van't Hoff correction is needed. Note that the freezing-point effect is larger than the boiling-point effect because $K_f > K_b$ for water.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Colligative properties** are those properties of a dilute solution that depend on the number of solute particles but not on their chemical identity. All four colligative properties follow from the reduction of the solvent's chemical potential when a solute is present.

For an ideal dilute solution, the chemical potential of the solvent (component 1) is:

$$\mu_1 = \mu_1^* + RT \ln x_1$$

where $\mu_1^*$ is the chemical potential of the pure solvent at the same $T$ and $P$, and $x_1$ is the mole fraction of the solvent. Since $x_1 = 1 - x_2$ where $x_2$ is the solute mole fraction, the solvent chemical potential is reduced by $-RT \ln(1 - x_2) \approx RT x_2$ for dilute solutions ($x_2 \ll 1$).

This reduction in chemical potential shifts every phase equilibrium involving the solvent.

**Boiling-point elevation.** At the boiling point, the solvent in the solution is in equilibrium with its vapor. Adding a nonvolatile solute lowers the solvent's chemical potential in the liquid phase. To restore equilibrium with the vapor, the temperature must increase. Expanding to first order in $x_2$:

$$\Delta T_b = \frac{RT_b^{*2}}{\Delta H_{\text{vap}}} x_2 = K_b \cdot m$$

where $K_b = \dfrac{RT_b^{*2} M_{\text{solvent}}}{1000 \cdot \Delta H_{\text{vap}}}$ is the ebullioscopic constant and $m$ is the molality.

**Freezing-point depression.** At the freezing point, the solvent in the solution is in equilibrium with the pure solid solvent. The same chemical-potential argument gives:

$$\Delta T_f = \frac{RT_f^{*2}}{\Delta H_{\text{fus}}} x_2 = K_f \cdot m$$

where $K_f = \dfrac{RT_f^{*2} M_{\text{solvent}}}{1000 \cdot \Delta H_{\text{fus}}}$ is the cryoscopic constant.

**Osmotic pressure.** When a solution and pure solvent are separated by a semipermeable membrane, the chemical potential of the solvent in the solution is lower than that of the pure solvent. Solvent flows into the solution until the applied pressure restores equilibrium:

$$\Pi V = -RT \ln x_1 \approx n_2 RT$$

For dilute solutions, this gives the **van't Hoff equation**:

$$\Pi = iMRT$$

where $i$ is the van't Hoff factor, $M$ is the molar concentration of solute, $R = 0.0821$ L atm/(mol K), and $T$ is absolute temperature.

**Van't Hoff factor.** For a solute that dissociates into $\nu$ ions:

$$i = 1 + (\nu - 1)\alpha$$

where $\alpha$ is the degree of dissociation. For strong electrolytes at infinite dilution, $\alpha = 1$ and $i = \nu$. At finite concentrations, ionic interactions reduce the effective $i$ below $\nu$. The Debye-Huckel theory quantifies this deviation.

**Determination of molar mass.** Freezing-point depression and osmotic pressure are the two standard methods. Freezing-point depression is convenient for small molecules (measurable $\Delta T_f$ at reasonable molalities). Osmotic pressure is preferred for large molecules (polymers, proteins) because the osmotic pressure of a given mass concentration is inversely proportional to molar mass, making it measurable even for very large solutes where freezing-point depression would be undetectably small.

## Key result [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Colligative equations from chemical potential).** *For an ideal dilute binary solution with solvent mole fraction $x_1 = 1 - x_2$ and nonvolatile solute:*

*1. Boiling-point elevation: $\Delta T_b = K_b \cdot m$ where $K_b = RT_b^{*2} M_1 / (1000 \Delta H_{\text{vap}})$.*

*2. Freezing-point depression: $\Delta T_f = K_f \cdot m$ where $K_f = RT_f^{*2} M_1 / (1000 \Delta H_{\text{fus}})$.*

*3. Osmotic pressure: $\Pi = (n_2 RT)/V \approx cRT$ for dilute solutions.*

**Proof sketch (boiling-point elevation).** The solvent vapor-liquid equilibrium condition is $\mu_1^{\text{vap}}(T_b + \Delta T_b, P) = \mu_1^{\text{liq}}(T_b + \Delta T_b, P, x_1)$. Expanding both sides about $T_b^*$ (the pure-solvent boiling point) to first order in $\Delta T_b$, using $(\partial \mu / \partial T)_P = -S_m$, and noting that for the pure solvent at $T_b^*$ the chemical potentials are equal, gives $-S_m^{\text{vap}} \Delta T_b = -S_m^{\text{liq}} \Delta T_b + RT_b^* \ln x_1$. Rearranging and using $\Delta S_{\text{vap}} = \Delta H_{\text{vap}} / T_b^*$ yields $\Delta T_b = (RT_b^{*2} / \Delta H_{\text{vap}}) x_2$. Converting mole fraction to molality produces the $K_b \cdot m$ form.

The freezing-point and osmotic-pressure derivations follow the same strategy: write the equilibrium condition, expand to first order, and use the relevant enthalpy or volume change.

### Ebullioscopic and cryoscopic constants for common solvents

| Solvent | $T_b$ (K) | $K_b$ (K kg/mol) | $T_f$ (K) | $K_f$ (K kg/mol) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Water | 373.15 | 0.512 | 273.15 | 1.86 |
| Benzene | 353.25 | 2.53 | 278.68 | 5.12 |
| Camphor | -- | -- | 452.15 | 40.0 |
| Ethanol | 351.44 | 1.22 | 158.85 | -- |
| Acetic acid | 391.15 | 3.07 | 289.75 | 3.90 |

Camphor's large $K_f$ makes it the preferred solvent for Rast's method of molar-mass determination via freezing-point depression. The large $K_f$ arises from camphor's low $\Delta H_{\text{fus}}$ relative to its high $T_f$.

### Reverse osmosis

If a pressure greater than the osmotic pressure is applied to the solution side of a semipermeable membrane, solvent flows from the solution into the pure solvent -- the reverse of normal osmosis. This is **reverse osmosis**, the basis of seawater desalination.

Seawater ($\sim$0.6 M total ions, $i \approx 2$) has an osmotic pressure of approximately:

$$\Pi = iMRT \approx (2)(0.6)(0.0821)(298) \approx 29 \text{ atm}$$

Practical reverse-osmosis desalination operates at 50--80 atm to drive water through the membrane at an economically useful rate. The membrane must reject dissolved ions while allowing water molecules to pass -- typically a thin-film composite polyamide membrane. Energy consumption is dominated by the high-pressure pumping, making reverse osmosis one of the most energy-intensive water-treatment processes, though still less energy-intensive than thermal distillation.

## Derivation of colligative equations from chemical potential [Master]

All four colligative properties are consequences of a single fact: dissolving a solute reduces the chemical potential of the solvent in the liquid phase. Each colligative equation follows from applying the phase-equilibrium condition $\mu_1^{\text{phase A}} = \mu_1^{\text{phase B}}$ to a specific pair of phases, expanding about the pure-solvent reference state, and retaining the first nonvanishing term in the solute mole fraction $x_2$.

The chemical potential of the solvent in an ideal dilute solution is:

$$\mu_1^{\text{liq}}(T, P, x_1) = \mu_1^{*\,\text{liq}}(T, P) + RT \ln x_1 = \mu_1^{*\,\text{liq}}(T, P) + RT \ln(1 - x_2)$$

For $x_2 \ll 1$, the Taylor expansion $\ln(1 - x_2) \approx -x_2 - x_2^2/2 - \cdots$ gives $\mu_1^{\text{liq}} \approx \mu_1^{*\,\text{liq}} - RT x_2$. The leading correction is linear in $x_2$ and independent of solute identity. This is the mathematical origin of colligativity.

**Boiling-point elevation (detailed derivation).** At the boiling point of the pure solvent ($T_b^*$, $P$), the liquid and vapor chemical potentials are equal: $\mu_1^{*\,\text{liq}}(T_b^*, P) = \mu_1^{*\,\text{vap}}(T_b^*, P)$. With solute present, equilibrium requires:

$$\mu_1^{\text{vap}}(T_b^* + \Delta T_b, P) = \mu_1^{*\,\text{liq}}(T_b^* + \Delta T_b, P) + RT \ln(1 - x_2)$$

Expand each chemical potential about $T_b^*$ using $(\partial \mu / \partial T)_P = -S_m$:

$$\mu_1^{*\,\text{vap}}(T_b^*, P) - S_m^{\text{vap}} \Delta T_b = \mu_1^{*\,\text{liq}}(T_b^*, P) - S_m^{\text{liq}} \Delta T_b + RT_b^* \ln(1 - x_2)$$

The pure-solvent terms cancel. Rearranging:

$$\Delta S_{\text{vap}} \cdot \Delta T_b = -RT_b^* \ln(1 - x_2) \approx RT_b^* x_2$$

where $\Delta S_{\text{vap}} = S_m^{\text{vap}} - S_m^{\text{liq}} = \Delta H_{\text{vap}} / T_b^*$. Therefore:

$$\Delta T_b = \frac{RT_b^{*2}}{\Delta H_{\text{vap}}} x_2$$

Converting mole fraction to molality ($x_2 \approx m M_1 / 1000$ where $M_1$ is the solvent molar mass in g/mol) gives $\Delta T_b = K_b \cdot m$.

**Freezing-point depression (detailed derivation).** The argument is structurally identical, with solid-liquid equilibrium replacing liquid-vapor:

$$\mu_1^{*\,\text{solid}}(T_f^* + \Delta T_f, P) = \mu_1^{*\,\text{liq}}(T_f^* + \Delta T_f, P) + RT \ln(1 - x_2)$$

Expanding and using $\Delta S_{\text{fus}} = \Delta H_{\text{fus}} / T_f^*$:

$$\Delta T_f = \frac{RT_f^{*2}}{\Delta H_{\text{fus}}} x_2 = K_f \cdot m$$

The sign is opposite to boiling-point elevation because $\Delta S_{\text{fus}}$ appears with opposite sign in the equilibrium condition. Note that $K_f > K_b$ for water because $\Delta H_{\text{fus}} = 6.01$ kJ/mol is much smaller than $\Delta H_{\text{vap}} = 40.7$ kJ/mol, while $T_f^*$ and $T_b^*$ are of comparable magnitude.

**Osmotic pressure (detailed derivation).** At equilibrium between pure solvent (at pressure $P$) and solution (at pressure $P + \Pi$) across a semipermeable membrane:

$$\mu_1^{*\,\text{liq}}(T, P) = \mu_1^{*\,\text{liq}}(T, P + \Pi) + RT \ln(1 - x_2)$$

The pressure dependence of chemical potential is $(\partial \mu_1 / \partial P)_T = V_m$, the partial molar volume. Expanding:

$$\mu_1^{*\,\text{liq}}(T, P + \Pi) \approx \mu_1^{*\,\text{liq}}(T, P) + V_m \Pi$$

Substituting and cancelling pure-solvent terms:

$$V_m \Pi = -RT \ln(1 - x_2) \approx RT x_2 \approx \frac{n_2 RT}{n_1}$$

For dilute solutions, $V \approx n_1 V_m$, so $\Pi = n_2 RT / V = cRT$ where $c$ is the molar concentration. The van't Hoff equation $\Pi = iMRT$ adds the dissociation factor.

**Limitations and corrections.** The first-order expansion in $x_2$ is accurate only for $x_2 \lesssim 0.01$. At higher concentrations, the full $\ln(1 - x_2)$ must be retained, activity coefficients replace mole fractions, and solute-solute interactions become nontrivial. The virial expansion of osmotic pressure, $\Pi / c = RT(1 + B_2 c + B_3 c^2 + \cdots)$ where $B_2$ is the second osmotic virial coefficient, extends the framework to moderate concentrations. For macromolecules, $B_2$ carries information about solute-solvent interactions and polymer dimensions (the theta condition corresponds to $B_2 = 0$).

**Reverse osmosis and desalination.** Normal osmosis drives solvent from low to high solute concentration. Applying external pressure $P_{\text{ext}} > \Pi$ reverses the flow. The thermodynamic minimum work to desalinate a volume $V$ of seawater is $W_{\min} = \Pi V \approx n_2 RT$. Real reverse-osmosis plants operate at 2--3 times this minimum because of membrane resistance, concentration polarization (solute buildup at the membrane surface), and pumping inefficiencies. Modern seawater reverse-osmosis plants consume 3--4 kWh per cubic meter of fresh water produced, compared to the thermodynamic minimum of about 1.1 kWh/m$^3$ for typical seawater.

## Determination of molar mass from colligative properties [Master]

The practical importance of colligative properties lies in molar-mass determination. Each equation relates a measurable physical quantity ($\Delta T_b$, $\Delta T_f$, or $\Pi$) to the number of moles of solute $n_2$. Given a known mass $w_2$ of solute, the molar mass follows from $M_2 = w_2 / n_2$.

**Freezing-point depression** is used for small molecules ($M \lesssim 500$ g/mol). The procedure: dissolve a known mass of solute in a known mass of solvent, measure the freezing point with a Beckmann thermometer or thermistor (precision $\sim 0.001$ K), and compute:

$$M_2 = \frac{K_f \cdot w_2}{\Delta T_f \cdot w_{\text{solvent}}}$$

Rast's method uses camphor ($K_f = 40.0$ K kg/mol) as the solvent, producing large depressions that can be measured with an ordinary thermometer. The trade-off is accuracy: camphor solutions are less ideal, and the method introduces systematic errors at higher concentrations.

**Osmotic pressure** is used for large molecules ($M \gtrsim 10{,}000$ g/mol). The osmotic pressure of a 1 g/L protein solution with $M = 50{,}000$ g/mol at 298 K is:

$$\Pi = \frac{w_2 RT}{M_2 V} = \frac{(1 \text{ g/L})(0.0821 \text{ L atm/mol K})(298 \text{ K})}{50{,}000 \text{ g/mol}} = 4.9 \times 10^{-4} \text{ atm} \approx 0.37 \text{ torr}$$

This is small but measurable with a capillary manometer. The corresponding freezing-point depression would be:

$$\Delta T_f = K_f \cdot m = 1.86 \times \frac{1/50000}{0.001} = 3.7 \times 10^{-5} \text{ K}$$

which is below the detection limit of most instruments. Osmotic pressure wins for macromolecules because $\Pi$ depends on $c = n/V$ (molarity), which is the same number of moles per liter regardless of molecular size, while $\Delta T_f$ depends on molality, which becomes vanishingly small for heavy solutes at practical mass concentrations.

**Non-ideality corrections.** Real solutions deviate from the ideal dilute limit. The corrected freezing-point equation is $\Delta T_f = K_f \cdot m \cdot \gamma_1$ where $\gamma_1$ is the solvent activity coefficient (not the solute coefficient). For osmotic pressure, the virial expansion gives:

$$\frac{\Pi}{c} = RT\left(\frac{1}{M_2} + B_2 c + B_3 c^2 + \cdots\right)$$

Plotting $\Pi / c$ against $c$ and extrapolating to $c = 0$ yields $1/M_2$ as the intercept. The slope gives $B_2$, which measures solute-solvent interactions. This is the standard method for determining both the molar mass and the thermodynamic quality of the solvent for polymer solutions.

**Molar-mass distribution.** A synthetic polymer is not a single molecular species but a distribution of chain lengths. The osmotic-pressure measurement gives the **number-average** molar mass $M_n = \sum n_i M_i / \sum n_i$ because $\Pi$ depends on the total number of solute particles regardless of their individual masses. Light scattering, by contrast, gives the **weight-average** molar mass $M_w = \sum w_i M_i / \sum w_i$. The ratio $M_w / M_n$ (the polydispersity index) quantifies the breadth of the molar-mass distribution. A monodisperse sample has $M_w / M_n = 1$; typical step-growth polymers have $M_w / M_n \approx 2$.

## Historical notes [Master]

The quantitative study of colligative properties began with Raoult's systematic measurements of vapor-pressure lowering (Compt. Rend. **95**, 187, 1882) and freezing-point depression (Compt. Rend. **95**, 1040, 1882). Raoult established that the depression of freezing point is proportional to the weight concentration of solute divided by its molecular weight -- a relationship now expressed as $\Delta T_f = K_f \cdot m$. His cryoscopic constant $K_f$ was determined empirically for each solvent. Raoult's experimental work was meticulous: he measured the freezing points of dozens of solutions in water, benzene, acetic acid, and other solvents, establishing the universality of the proportionality.

Jacobus van't Hoff's 1887 paper in *Zeitschrift fur physikalische Chemie* (Vol. 1, pp. 481--508) provided the theoretical framework. Van't Hoff recognized that the osmotic-pressure equation $\Pi V = nRT$ is formally identical to the ideal-gas law, with the osmotic pressure of the solute playing the role of gas pressure. This analogy was provocative: it suggested that solute molecules in dilute solution behave as independent particles, just as gas molecules in an ideal gas. The analogy is exact for ideal dilute solutions and breaks down at higher concentrations where solute-solute interactions become significant.

Van't Hoff's paper is remarkable for its boldness. He extended the gas analogy to electrolytes by introducing the factor $i$ (now called the van't Hoff factor), noting that electrolyte solutions produce osmotic pressures larger than predicted for undissociated solutes. He recognized that $i > 1$ implied dissociation but did not himself propose a theory of ionic dissociation. That step was taken by Arrhenius in his 1887 doctoral dissertation, which argued that electrolytes dissociate into ions in solution. The colligative evidence -- the van't Hoff factor consistently approaching integer values ($i = 2$ for NaCl, $i = 3$ for CaCl$_2$) at high dilution -- was among the strongest evidence for Arrhenius's theory, alongside the independent electrical-conductivity measurements of Kohlrausch and the Faraday-law experiments that connected current to ion transport.

The colligative framework also played a role in establishing Avogadro's number. Perrin's 1908 experiments on Brownian motion used Einstein's 1905 theoretical analysis to extract $N_A$ from the osmotic pressure of suspended colloidal particles, providing one of the first reliable determinations. The connection is direct: the osmotic pressure of a suspension of particles is $\Pi = nRT/V$ where $n = N_{\text{particles}}/N_A$, so measuring $\Pi$ and counting particles determines $N_A$. This was one of several independent methods (radioactive decay, X-ray diffraction, oil-drop experiments) that converged on the modern value $N_A = 6.022 \times 10^{23}$ mol$^{-1}$, finally putting the atomic hypothesis on quantitative footing.

The derivation of colligative equations from chemical potential came later, with the development of chemical thermodynamics by Gibbs (1875--1878), Lewis (1907--1923), and Guggenheim (1933). The chemical-potential derivation unifies all four colligative properties under a single principle and makes clear why they are "colligative" (dependent on the number of particles): the first-order correction to $\mu_1$ is $RT x_2$, which depends on the count of solute particles and nothing else. Higher-order corrections, activity coefficients, and specific ion effects break colligativity and carry information about intermolecular forces.

Reverse osmosis has its own history. The feasibility of pressure-driven desalination through synthetic membranes was first demonstrated by Sidney Loeb and Srinivasa Sourirajan at UCLA in 1959, using an asymmetric cellulose acetate membrane. Their membrane had a thin dense skin (the rejecting layer) supported by a porous substructure, achieving 98% salt rejection at practical flow rates. The first commercial reverse-osmosis plant was built in Coalinga, California, in 1965. Modern thin-film composite membranes, developed by Cadotte in the 1970s, use interfacial polymerization to form a polyamide rejecting layer on a polysulfone support, achieving 99.5%+ salt rejection. Global desalination capacity exceeded 100 million m$^3$/day by 2020, with reverse osmosis accounting for approximately 70% of installed capacity.

## Connections [Master]

- **Solutions and phase equilibria (14.09.01-14.09.02):** All colligative properties originate from the reduction of solvent chemical potential by a solute, which is the dilute-solution limit of the phase-equilibrium framework developed in the preceding units on Raoult's law and Henry's law.
- **Thermodynamics (14.06):** The colligative equations are derived by expanding the chemical-potential equality about the pure-solvent reference state — a first-order Taylor expansion that mirrors the linearisation techniques used in the thermodynamics of phase transitions.
- **Electrochemistry (14.11):** The van't Hoff factor for electrolytes and the Debye-Huckel theory of ionic activity coefficients connect directly to the electrochemistry units, where ionic activities govern electrode potentials and cell voltages.
- **Biological membranes (17.02):** Osmotic pressure and the van't Hoff equation are the physicochemical basis for understanding osmoregulation, cell turgor, and water transport across biological membranes.

**Exercise (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the exact (non-approximate) expression for the freezing-point depression in terms of the solute mole fraction $x_2$ by retaining the full $\ln(1 - x_2)$ term rather than the linearised $-x_2$ approximation. Show that the exact result is:

$$\Delta T_f = \frac{RT_f^{*2}}{\Delta H_{\text{fus}}} \left(-\ln(1 - x_2)\right)$$

and compute the fractional error in the linearised formula for $x_2 = 0.01$ and $x_2 = 0.10$. At what solute mole fraction does the linearised approximation differ from the exact result by more than 5%?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $-\ln(1 - x_2) = x_2 + x_2^2/2 + x_2^3/3 + \cdots$. The linearised approximation retains only $x_2$. The fractional error is $(x_2^2/2 + x_2^3/3 + \cdots) / (x_2 + x_2^2/2 + \cdots)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The exact freezing-point equation comes from $\mu_1^{*\,\text{solid}}(T_f) = \mu_1^{*\,\text{liq}}(T_f) + RT_f \ln(1 - x_2)$. Expanding $\mu_1^{*\,\text{solid}}$ and $\mu_1^{*\,\text{liq}}$ about $T_f^*$ and using $\Delta S_{\text{fus}} = \Delta H_{\text{fus}} / T_f^*$:

$$\frac{\Delta H_{\text{fus}}}{T_f^*} \cdot \Delta T_f = -RT_f \ln(1 - x_2)$$

For small $\Delta T_f$, $T_f \approx T_f^*$, giving:

$$\Delta T_f = \frac{RT_f^{*2}}{\Delta H_{\text{fus}}} \left(-\ln(1 - x_2)\right)$$

The linearised approximation replaces $-\ln(1 - x_2)$ with $x_2$.

At $x_2 = 0.01$: $-\ln(0.99) = 0.0100503...$, while $x_2 = 0.01$. Fractional error = $(0.0100503 - 0.01)/0.0100503 = 0.50\%$.

At $x_2 = 0.10$: $-\ln(0.90) = 0.10536...$, while $x_2 = 0.10$. Fractional error = $(0.10536 - 0.10)/0.10536 = 5.08\%$.

The linearised approximation differs from the exact result by more than 5% at $x_2 \approx 0.10$. This corresponds to a molality of approximately $m = 1000 x_2 / M_1 = (1000)(0.10)/18.015 = 5.6$ mol/kg for water -- a concentrated solution well outside the dilute regime.

</details>

**Exercise (hard, symbolic).**

The virial expansion of osmotic pressure for a polymer solution is:

$$\frac{\Pi}{c} = RT\left(\frac{1}{M_n} + A_2 c + A_3 c^2 + \cdots\right)$$

where $c$ is the mass concentration (g/mL), $M_n$ is the number-average molar mass, and $A_2$ is the second virial coefficient. Osmotic-pressure measurements for a polystyrene sample in toluene at 25 degrees C gave:

| $c$ (g/L) | $\Pi$ (torr) |
|---|---|
| 2.0 | 0.62 |
| 4.0 | 1.34 |
| 6.0 | 2.18 |
| 8.0 | 3.14 |
| 10.0 | 4.22 |

(a) Plot $\Pi / c$ vs $c$ and determine $M_n$ from the intercept. (b) Calculate $A_2$ from the slope. (c) Is toluene a good solvent for this polystyrene sample? (A good solvent has $A_2 > 0$; theta conditions correspond to $A_2 = 0$.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Convert $\Pi$ from torr to atm (1 atm = 760 torr). Calculate $\Pi / c$ for each concentration. Plot $\Pi / c$ vs $c$ and fit a line. The intercept is $RT/M_n$; the slope is $RT \cdot A_2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Converting pressures to atm and computing $\Pi / c$:

| $c$ (g/L) | $\Pi$ (atm) | $\Pi/c$ (atm L/g) |
|---|---|---|
| 2.0 | 0.000816 | 4.08 $\times 10^{-4}$ |
| 4.0 | 0.001763 | 4.41 $\times 10^{-4}$ |
| 6.0 | 0.002868 | 4.78 $\times 10^{-4}$ |
| 8.0 | 0.004132 | 5.16 $\times 10^{-4}$ |
| 10.0 | 0.005553 | 5.55 $\times 10^{-4}$ |

Linear fit of $\Pi/c$ vs $c$: intercept $= 3.7 \times 10^{-4}$ atm L/g, slope $= 1.85 \times 10^{-5}$ atm L$^2$/g$^2$.

(a) $RT/M_n = 3.7 \times 10^{-4}$ atm L/g. $M_n = RT / (3.7 \times 10^{-4}) = (0.0821)(298) / (3.7 \times 10^{-4}) = 24.5 / 3.7 \times 10^{-4} = 66{,}100$ g/mol.

(b) $RT \cdot A_2 = 1.85 \times 10^{-5}$. $A_2 = 1.85 \times 10^{-5} / (0.0821 \times 298) = 1.85 \times 10^{-5} / 24.5 = 7.6 \times 10^{-7}$ atm L$^2$/g$^2$. Converting to the more standard mol mL/g$^2$ units: $A_2 \approx 2.3 \times 10^{-4}$ mol mL / g$^2$.

(c) $A_2 > 0$, so toluene is a good solvent for this polystyrene sample. The positive $A_2$ indicates favorable polymer-solvent interactions that cause the polymer chains to expand beyond their theta dimensions. At the theta temperature, $A_2 = 0$ and the chains adopt ideal random-walk conformations.

**Exercise (hard, symbolic).**

A reverse-osmosis membrane has a water permeability of $A = 2.0 \times 10^{-6}$ L/(m$^2$ s atm) and a salt rejection of 99.2%. Feed water has a total dissolved solids concentration of 35,000 mg/L (seawater). The feed pressure is 55 atm and the permeate pressure is 1 atm. The osmotic pressure of the feed is approximately 27 atm. (a) Calculate the water flux through the membrane in L/(m$^2$ h). (b) What is the salt concentration in the permeate? (c) If the feed-side concentration polarization raises the effective osmotic pressure at the membrane surface to 35 atm, calculate the new water flux and the percentage reduction.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Water flux $J_w = A(\Delta P - \Delta \Pi)$ where $\Delta P$ is the transmembrane pressure difference and $\Delta \Pi$ is the transmembrane osmotic pressure difference. Salt rejection $R = 1 - c_p / c_f$ where $c_p$ and $c_f$ are permeate and feed concentrations.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) Net driving pressure: $\Delta P - \Delta \Pi = (55 - 1) - 27 = 27$ atm.

Water flux: $J_w = A \times 27 = (2.0 \times 10^{-6})(27) = 5.4 \times 10^{-5}$ L/(m$^2$ s) $= 0.194$ L/(m$^2$ h).

(b) Salt rejection: $R = 0.992 = 1 - c_p/c_f$. Therefore $c_p = c_f (1 - R) = 35{,}000 \times 0.008 = 280$ mg/L. This meets drinking-water standards (< 500 mg/L TDS).

(c) With concentration polarization, $\Delta \Pi = 35$ atm (assuming permeate osmotic pressure is negligible). Net driving pressure: $(55 - 1) - 35 = 19$ atm.

New flux: $J_w = (2.0 \times 10^{-6})(19) = 3.8 \times 10^{-5}$ L/(m$^2$ s) $= 0.137$ L/(m$^2$ h).

Percentage reduction: $(0.194 - 0.137)/0.194 \times 100 = 29.4\%$. Concentration polarization reduces output by nearly 30%. This is why membrane modules include spacers and cross-flow designs to minimize boundary-layer buildup.

</details>
