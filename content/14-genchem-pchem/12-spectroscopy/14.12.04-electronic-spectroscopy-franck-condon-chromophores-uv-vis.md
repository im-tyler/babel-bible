## Intuition [Beginner]

When a molecule absorbs a photon of ultraviolet or visible light, an electron jumps from a lower-energy orbital to a higher-energy one. The energy of the photon — a few electron-volts — matches the gap between the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO). Different electronic transitions have different energies, so different molecules absorb at different wavelengths. The pattern of absorption across the UV and visible spectrum is a fingerprint of the molecule's electronic structure.

A solution of potassium permanganate is deep purple because it absorbs green light around 550 nm and transmits red and blue. A solution of copper sulfate is blue because it absorbs orange-red light around 800 nm. Chlorophyll is green because it absorbs strongly in the blue (around 430 nm) and the red (around 660 nm), transmitting green. In each case, the colour we see is the complement of the colour absorbed. UV-Vis spectroscopy quantifies this absorption: the instrument measures how much light of each wavelength passes through the sample, and the resulting spectrum identifies and quantifies the absorbing species.

The Beer-Lambert law connects the measured absorption to concentration: $A = \varepsilon c \ell$, where $A$ is the absorbance (dimensionless), $\varepsilon$ is the molar absorptivity (a property of the molecule at that wavelength), $c$ is the concentration, and $\ell$ is the path length of the light through the sample. A concentrated solution absorbs more than a dilute one, a thicker sample absorbs more than a thin one, and a molecule with a large $\varepsilon$ absorbs more than one with a small $\varepsilon$. This simple linear relationship makes UV-Vis spectroscopy one of the most widely used quantitative techniques in chemistry.

## Visual [Beginner]

Electronic transitions are classified by the orbitals involved. In a simple organic molecule, the occupied orbitals are sigma bonds, pi bonds, and nonbonding lone pairs (n); the unoccupied orbitals are sigma antibonding and pi antibonding. Transitions from each occupied to each unoccupied orbital produce absorption bands at characteristic energies.

![Electronic transition energy diagram showing four types of transitions in a molecule with pi and n orbitals. From left to right: sigma to sigma-star at highest energy (far UV, below 200 nm), n to sigma-star in the mid-UV (150-250 nm), pi to pi-star in the near-UV to visible (200-400 nm for simple chromophores, red-shifted by conjugation), and n to pi-star at lowest energy (near-UV, 250-350 nm). Each transition is drawn as a vertical arrow on a molecular orbital energy diagram. A second panel shows a schematic UV-Vis absorption spectrum of a conjugated aldehyde, with a strong pi-pi-star band at 220 nm (epsilon approx 10000) and a weak n-pi-star band at 320 nm (epsilon approx 20).](/img/14.12.04-electronic-transitions-placeholder.svg)

The two most important transitions in organic UV-Vis are $\pi \to \pi^{*}$ and $n \to \pi^{*}$. The $\pi \to \pi^{*}$ transition is strong ($\varepsilon = 10^{3}$ to $10^{5}$) because the orbital overlap between $\pi$ and $\pi^{*}$ is large, producing a large transition dipole moment. The $n \to \pi^{*}$ transition is weak ($\varepsilon = 10$ to $100$) because the nonbonding orbital and $\pi^{*}$ orbital are nearly perpendicular, giving a small transition dipole. Both transitions are diagnostic: the $\pi \to \pi^{*}$ band identifies conjugation, and the $n \to \pi^{*}$ band identifies a lone pair adjacent to a pi system.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A solution of benzene in ethanol is placed in a 1.0-cm cuvette in a UV-Vis spectrometer. The absorbance at 255 nm (the $\pi \to \pi^{*}$ band of benzene) is measured as 0.450. The molar absorptivity of benzene at 255 nm is 200 inverse molar inverse centimetre. Calculate the concentration.

Step 1. Apply the Beer-Lambert law: $A = \varepsilon c \ell$. Rearranging: $c = A/(\varepsilon \ell) = 0.450/(200 \times 1.0) = 2.25 \times 10^{-3}$ mol/L.

Step 2. The concentration is 2.25 millimolar. The absorbance is well within the linear range ($0.1 < A < 2.0$), where Beer-Lambert is reliable. Below $A = 0.1$, instrumental noise dominates; above $A = 2.0$, so little light is transmitted that the measurement becomes inaccurate.

Step 3. The molar absorptivity of 200 is relatively low for a $\pi \to \pi^{*}$ transition. This reflects the symmetry of benzene (which partially forbids the transition by group-theoretic selection rules) and the brevity of the conjugated system (only one aromatic ring). Extending conjugation — as in naphthalene or anthracene — dramatically increases $\varepsilon$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Electronic spectroscopy probes transitions between the electronic states of a molecule. The relevant Hamiltonian is the molecular electronic Hamiltonian $\hat{H}_{\text{el}}$ whose eigenstates are Born-Oppenheimer-separated electronic wave functions parametrised by the nuclear coordinates. A transition from electronic state $|i\rangle$ to $|f\rangle$ absorbs a photon of energy $h\nu = E_{f} - E_{i}$.

**Definition (Beer-Lambert law).** For a sample of concentration $c$ and path length $\ell$, the transmitted intensity at wavelength $\lambda$ is $I = I_{0}10^{-\varepsilon(\lambda)c\ell}$, and the absorbance is

$$
A(\lambda) = \log_{10}\frac{I_{0}}{I} = \varepsilon(\lambda)\, c\, \ell,
$$

where $\varepsilon(\lambda)$ is the molar absorptivity. The molar absorptivity is related to the transition dipole moment by $\varepsilon \propto \nu\, |\boldsymbol{\mu}_{fi}|^{2}$, where $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{fi} = \langle f | \hat{\boldsymbol{\mu}} | i \rangle$ is the electric-dipole transition moment.

**Definition (Franck-Condon principle).** The electronic transition is vertical on the potential-energy diagram: the nuclear coordinates do not change during the electronic transition because the nuclei are much heavier than the electrons. The intensity of a vibronic transition from $|i, v_{i}\rangle$ to $|f, v_{f}\rangle$ is proportional to the **Franck-Condon factor**

$$
\text{FCF}(v_{i}, v_{f}) = \left|\langle \chi_{v_{f}}^{(f)} | \chi_{v_{i}}^{(i)} \rangle\right|^{2},
$$

the squared overlap integral between the vibrational wave functions of the initial and final electronic states evaluated at the same nuclear geometry.

**Definition (chromophore).** A **chromophore** is a structural unit that absorbs at a characteristic wavelength and whose absorption properties are transferable across different molecular environments. An **auxochrome** is a substituent that modifies the chromophore's absorption (e.g., -OH, -NH$_{2}$) by donating electron density into the conjugated system, typically red-shifting and intensifying the absorption.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Absorbance is not transmittance.* $T = I/I_{0}$ ranges from 0 to 1; $A = -\log_{10}T$ ranges from 0 to infinity. An absorbance of 1 means 10 percent transmittance; an absorbance of 2 means 1 percent transmittance. Reporting $T$ when $A$ is needed (or vice versa) is the most common quantitative error in UV-Vis spectroscopy.

- *Colour is complementary.* A compound that absorbs at 400 nm (violet) appears yellow-green, not violet. The observed colour is the complement of the absorbed colour. This is why strong absorbers at long wavelength (like chlorophyll absorbing at 660 nm) appear the complement (green), not the absorbed colour (red).

- *Lambda-max shifts with solvent.* The position of the absorption maximum depends on the solvent because the solvent stabilises ground and excited states to different extents. Solvatochromism — the solvent-dependent shift of $\lambda_{\text{max}}$ — is a diagnostic of the charge-transfer character of the transition: a blue shift (hypsochromic shift) with increasing solvent polarity indicates less polarisation in the excited state, while a red shift (bathochromic shift) indicates greater polarisation.

## Key result [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Franck-Condon intensity distribution).** *Consider two electronic states with harmonic-oscillator vibrational potentials characterised by frequencies $\omega_{i}$, $\omega_{f}$ and equilibrium displacements $\Delta R = R_{e}^{(f)} - R_{e}^{(i)}$. The Franck-Condon factor for the $0 \to v_{f}$ transition is*

$$
\text{FCF}(0, v_{f}) = \frac{e^{-S} S^{v_{f}}}{v_{f}!}, \qquad S = \frac{\mu\omega_{f}}{2\hbar}(\Delta R)^{2},
$$

*where $S$ is the Huang-Rhys factor. The maximum-intensity vibronic band occurs at $v_{f}^{*} \approx S$.*

*Proof.* In the harmonic approximation with equal frequencies ($\omega_{i} = \omega_{f} = \omega$), the overlap integral between displaced harmonic-oscillator states is evaluated using the generating-function technique. The ground-state vibrational wave function $\chi_{0}^{(i)}$ is a Gaussian centred at $R_{e}^{(i)}$; the excited-state wave functions $\chi_{v_{f}}^{(f)}$ are centred at $R_{e}^{(f)}$. The vertical transition projects $\chi_{0}^{(i)}$ onto the displaced harmonic-oscillator basis of the upper state.

The displacement $\Delta R$ enters through the dimensionless Huang-Rhys parameter $S = \mu\omega(\Delta R)^{2}/(2\hbar)$, which counts the mean number of vibrational quanta excited by the electronic transition. The overlap integral $\langle \chi_{v_{f}}^{(f)} | \chi_{0}^{(i)} \rangle$ for the displaced harmonic oscillator evaluates to a closed form whose square gives the Poisson distribution $e^{-S}S^{v_{f}}/v_{f}!$. The distribution peaks at $v_{f} = S$ for $S \geq 1$ and at $v_{f} = 0$ for $S < 1$.

For $S = 0$ (no displacement), the $0 \to 0$ transition dominates — the absorption is a sharp line. For $S = 3$ (moderate displacement), the maximum intensity is at $v_{f} = 3$ — the absorption is a broad progression of vibronic bands. For large $S$ ($S \gg 1$), the envelope of the progression is approximately Gaussian, and the absorption band appears as a broad featureless peak. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Franck-Condon principle explains why some electronic transitions are sharp (small geometry change between states) and others are broad (large geometry change). This connects directly to the vibronic structure of UV-Vis spectra: gas-phase small molecules show resolved vibronic progressions (measurable $S$), while large molecules in solution show broad featureless bands (large $S$ convolved with solvent broadening). The same framework describes the fluorescence spectrum (emission from the relaxed excited state to the ground state), which is a mirror image of the absorption spectrum when the potential-energy surfaces have the same curvature.

### Worked example at intermediate level

The UV-Vis spectrum of molecular iodine I$_{2}$ in the gas phase shows a vibronic progression in the visible (around 500 nm). The spacing between vibronic peaks is 130 cm$^{-1}$, and the progression spans about 15 peaks. Calculate the Huang-Rhys factor and the bond-length change upon excitation.

The peak of the progression at $v_{f}^{*} \approx 7$ gives $S \approx 7$. The vibrational frequency of the excited state is 130 cm$^{-1}$ (from the spacing). Using $S = \mu\omega(\Delta R)^{2}/(2\hbar)$:

$\mu(\text{I}_{2}) = (127 \times 127)/(254) = 63.5$ amu $= 1.054 \times 10^{-25}$ kg. $\omega = 2\pi c \times 130 = 2\pi \times 2.998 \times 10^{10} \times 130 = 2.45 \times 10^{13}$ rad/s.

$\Delta R = \sqrt{2\hbar S/(\mu\omega)} = \sqrt{2 \times 1.055 \times 10^{-34} \times 7/(1.054 \times 10^{-25} \times 2.45 \times 10^{13})} = \sqrt{1.477 \times 10^{-33}/2.582 \times 10^{-12}} = \sqrt{5.72 \times 10^{-22}} = 2.39 \times 10^{-11}$ m $= 23.9$ pm.

The I-I bond lengthens by about 24 pm upon electronic excitation, from 267 pm in the ground state to about 291 pm in the excited state. This substantial lengthening reflects the promotion of an antibonding electron, weakening the bond.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

1,3-Butadiene absorbs at 217 nm and 1,3,5-hexatriene absorbs at 268 nm. Use the particle-in-a-box model to estimate the absorption wavelength of 1,3,5,7-octatetraene, assuming each additional double bond adds 240 pm to the box length and the box length for butadiene is 560 pm.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In the particle-in-a-box model, $E = n^{2}h^{2}/(8mL^{2})$. For a conjugated polyene with $N$ double bonds, the HOMO has quantum number $n = N$ and the LUMO has $n = N+1$. The transition energy is $\Delta E = (2N+1)h^{2}/(8mL^{2})$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For butadiene ($N = 2$): $L = 560$ pm. For hexatriene ($N = 3$): $L = 560 + 240 = 800$ pm. For octatetraene ($N = 4$): $L = 560 + 3 \times 240 = 1280$ pm.

The transition energy scales as $(2N+1)/L^{2}$:
- Butadiene: $(2 \times 2 + 1)/560^{2} = 5/313{,}600$
- Hexatriene: $(2 \times 3 + 1)/800^{2} = 7/640{,}000$

Ratio of hexatriene to butadiene wavelengths: $\lambda_{\text{hex}}/\lambda_{\text{but}} = (E_{\text{but}}/E_{\text{hex}}) = [5 \times 640{,}000]/[7 \times 313{,}600] = 3{,}200{,}000/2{,}195{,}200 = 1.458$. Predicted: $217 \times 1.458 = 316$ nm. Observed: 268 nm — the crude model overestimates because the effective box length does not grow linearly.

For octatetraene: $E_{\text{oct}}/E_{\text{but}} = [5 \times (560)^{2}]/[9 \times (1280)^{2}] = 5 \times 313{,}600/(9 \times 1{,}638{,}400) = 1{,}568{,}000/14{,}745{,}600 = 0.1063$. $\lambda_{\text{oct}} = 217/0.1063 = 2042$ nm — far from the expected range. The model breaks down for longer chains because the effective conjugation length saturates.

A better estimate: observe that $\lambda$ for $N = 2$ is 217 nm and $N = 3$ is 268 nm. The increment is about 51 nm per double bond. So $N = 4$: $\lambda \approx 268 + 51 = 319$ nm. The experimental value is 304 nm. The simple free-electron model captures the red-shift trend but not the quantitative details.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Formaldehyde has both $\pi \to \pi^{*}$ and $n \to \pi^{*}$ transitions. Using the $C_{2v}$ character table, show that the $\pi \to \pi^{*}$ transition is symmetry-allowed and identify the polarisation direction, while the $n \to \pi^{*}$ transition is symmetry-forbidden (weakly allowed through vibronic coupling).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In $C_{2v}$: $B_{1}$ contains $x$, $B_{2}$ contains $y$, $A_{1}$ contains $z$. The $\pi$ and $\pi^{*}$ orbitals transform as $B_{1}$; the nonbonding $n$ orbital on oxygen transforms as $B_{2}$. Apply the direct-product selection rule: $\Gamma_{i} \otimes \Gamma_{\mu} \otimes \Gamma_{f}$ must contain $A_{1}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In $C_{2v}$, the symmetry species and their characters for the relevant orbitals: $\pi$ is $B_{1}$, $\pi^{*}$ is $B_{1}$, $n$ (lone pair on O) is $B_{2}$.

For $\pi \to \pi^{*}$ ($B_{1} \to B_{1}$): The direct product is $\Gamma_{i} \otimes \Gamma_{f} = B_{1} \otimes B_{1} = A_{1}$. Since $A_{1}$ matches the $z$ component of the dipole ($A_{1}$ contains $z$), the transition is $z$-polarised and symmetry-allowed.

For $n \to \pi^{*}$ ($B_{2} \to B_{1}$): The direct product is $\Gamma_{i} \otimes \Gamma_{f} = B_{2} \otimes B_{1} = A_{2}$. Checking the character table: $A_{2}$ does not contain $x$, $y$, or $z$ — none of the dipole components transform as $A_{2}$. Therefore the transition is symmetry-forbidden by electric-dipole selection rules.

The weak $n \to \pi^{*}$ absorption observed experimentally ($\varepsilon \approx 20$) arises from vibronic coupling: the simultaneous excitation of a vibration that breaks the $C_{2v}$ symmetry mixes in a small allowed component, relaxing the strict electronic selection rule.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

A molecule in its first excited electronic state relaxes to the ground state via fluorescence. Explain why the fluorescence emission maximum is always at longer wavelength (lower energy) than the absorption maximum (the Stokes shift), and identify the three contributions to the Stokes shift.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider what happens between absorption and emission: nuclear geometry relaxes, the solvent reorganises, and the excited-state vibrational levels thermalise.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Stokes shift has three contributions:

1. **Geometry relaxation.** Absorption is a vertical transition from the ground-state equilibrium geometry to the excited-state potential at that same geometry (Franck-Condon principle). The excited molecule then relaxes to the excited-state equilibrium geometry, which has a different bond length (typically longer for antibonding excitation). Emission is a vertical transition from this relaxed geometry back to the ground state. The energy gap between the relaxed geometries is smaller than the energy gap between the unrelaxed geometries, producing a net red shift.

2. **Solvent reorganisation.** The excited state typically has a different charge distribution from the ground state. After excitation, the solvent shell reorganises to stabilise the new charge distribution, lowering the excited-state energy. Before emission, the solvent has already relaxed around the excited state, so emission occurs from the solvationally stabilised excited state to the unrelaxed ground state. The solvent contribution to the Stokes shift measures the reorganisation energy of the solvent, which is connected to the Marcus theory of electron transfer.

3. **Vibrational relaxation.** The vibrationally excited ground state produced by emission relaxes to $v = 0$, but this does not affect the emission wavelength directly — it affects the vibrational envelope shape.

The total Stokes shift is typically 20-200 nm, with larger shifts for more polar molecules in more polar solvents. The Stokes shift is the reason fluorescent dyes can be excited at one wavelength and detected at a longer wavelength, enabling sensitive fluorescence detection in analytical chemistry and microscopy.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the quantitative relationship between the molar absorptivity $\varepsilon$ and the transition dipole moment $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{fi}$, showing that $\varepsilon(\tilde{\nu}) = (8\pi^{3}N_{A}\tilde{\nu}/(3hc \times 1000 \times \ln 10))|\boldsymbol{\mu}_{fi}|^{2}g(\tilde{\nu})$, where $g(\tilde{\nu})$ is a normalised lineshape function. Evaluate this for a transition with $|\boldsymbol{\mu}_{fi}| = 5$ debye to predict the peak $\varepsilon$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Start from Fermi's golden rule for the absorption rate, integrate over the lineshape, convert to molar absorptivity using the relation between absorption cross-section and $\varepsilon$, and use the orientational average $\langle\cos^{2}\theta\rangle = 1/3$ for randomly oriented molecules.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The absorption cross-section for a single molecule is obtained by integrating the Fermi golden rule rate over the photon density of states and the lineshape:

$\sigma(\tilde{\nu}) = \frac{8\pi^{3}\tilde{\nu}}{3hc}|\boldsymbol{\mu}_{fi}|^{2}g(\tilde{\nu}),$

where the factor of $1/3$ comes from the orientational average over randomly oriented molecules ($\langle\cos^{2}\theta\rangle = 1/3$ for isotropic solution). Converting to molar absorptivity via $\varepsilon = \sigma N_{A}/(\ln 10 \times 1000)$ (the factor of 1000 converts from m$^{3}$ to litres, and $\ln 10$ converts from natural to base-10 logarithm):

$\varepsilon(\tilde{\nu}) = \frac{8\pi^{3}N_{A}}{3hc \times 1000 \times \ln 10}\tilde{\nu}|\boldsymbol{\mu}_{fi}|^{2}g(\tilde{\nu}).$

For a narrow band at $\tilde{\nu}_{0}$, $g(\tilde{\nu})$ is approximately $\delta(\tilde{\nu} - \tilde{\nu}_{0})$ and the integrated $\varepsilon$ gives the oscillator strength. For a Gaussian band with FWHM $\Delta\tilde{\nu}$: $\varepsilon_{\text{max}} = (8\pi^{3}N_{A}\tilde{\nu}_{0}/(3hc \times 1000 \times \ln 10))|\boldsymbol{\mu}_{fi}|^{2} \times (1/\Delta\tilde{\nu})\sqrt{2/\pi}$.

For $|\boldsymbol{\mu}_{fi}| = 5$ debye $= 5 \times 3.336 \times 10^{-30} = 1.668 \times 10^{-29}$ C m, $\tilde{\nu}_{0} = 25{,}000$ cm$^{-1}$ (400 nm), $\Delta\tilde{\nu} = 5000$ cm$^{-1}$:

$\varepsilon_{\text{max}} \approx 9.78 \times 10^{-3} \times \tilde{\nu}_{0} \times |\boldsymbol{\mu}_{fi}|^{2}/\Delta\tilde{\nu}$

$= 9.78 \times 10^{-3} \times 25{,}000 \times (1.668 \times 10^{-29})^{2}/5000 = 9.78 \times 10^{-3} \times 25{,}000 \times 2.78 \times 10^{-58}/5000 = 1.36 \times 10^{4}$ inverse molar inverse centimetre.

A transition dipole of 5 debye gives $\varepsilon \approx 14{,}000$, characteristic of a strong $\pi \to \pi^{*}$ transition. Typical ranges: $n \to \pi^{*}$ ($\mu \approx 0.5$-1 D, $\varepsilon \approx 10$-100); $\pi \to \pi^{*}$ ($\mu \approx 3$-7 D, $\varepsilon \approx 5{,}000$-50 000); charge transfer ($\mu \approx 5$-10 D, $\varepsilon \approx 10{,}000$-50 000).

## Transition-metal d-d transitions and charge-transfer spectra [Master]

The electronic spectra of transition-metal complexes are dominated by two types of transitions: d-d transitions within the metal d-orbital manifold, and charge-transfer transitions between metal-centred and ligand-centred orbitals. The d-d transitions carry information about the crystal-field splitting and hence the geometry and ligand field strength; the charge-transfer transitions carry information about the redox properties of the metal and ligand.

In an octahedral complex, the five d orbitals split into a lower-energy $t_{2g}$ triplet ($d_{xy}$, $d_{xz}$, $d_{yz}$) and a higher-energy $e_{g}$ doublet ($d_{z^{2}}$, $d_{x^{2}-y^{2}}$) with splitting $\Delta_{o}$ (or $10Dq$). A d-d transition promotes an electron from $t_{2g}$ to $e_{g}$, absorbing a photon of energy $\Delta_{o}$. For Ti(H$_{2}$O)$_{6}^{3+}$ ($d^{1}$), this transition produces a single broad band at about 500 nm (green absorption, purple colour), and the band position directly measures $\Delta_{o}$.

d-d transitions in centrosymmetric octahedral complexes are parity-forbidden: both $t_{2g}$ and $e_{g}$ have $g$ (gerade) symmetry, and the electric-dipole operator is $u$ (ungerade), so the matrix element $\langle e_{g} | \hat{\mu} | t_{2g}\rangle$ vanishes by Laporte's rule. The observed bands ($\varepsilon \approx 10$-200) are weakly allowed through vibronic coupling — vibrations that destroy the inversion centre mix in a small allowed component. Tetrahedral complexes lack an inversion centre, so d-d transitions are parity-allowed and much more intense ($\varepsilon \approx 100$-1000). This symmetry difference is the spectroscopic signature that distinguishes octahedral from tetrahedral coordination: weak d-d bands indicate octahedral geometry, strong d-d bands indicate tetrahedral geometry.

The spectrochemical series orders ligands by their ability to split the d orbitals: I$^{-}$ < Br$^{-}$ < Cl$^{-}$ < F$^{-}$ < OH$^{-}$ < H$_{2}$O < NH$_{3}$ < en < NO$_{2}^{-}$ < CN$^{-}$ < CO. The position of a ligand in this series determines $\Delta_{o}$ and hence the colour of the complex. For Cr$^{3+}$ ($d^{3}$), the three d-d transitions shift systematically with ligand strength: Cr(H$_{2}$O)$_{6}^{3+}$ is violet ($\Delta_{o} = 17{,}800$ cm$^{-1}$), Cr(NH$_{3}$)$_{6}^{3+}$ is yellow ($\Delta_{o} = 21{,}500$ cm$^{-1}$), and Cr(CN)$_{6}^{3-}$ absorbs in the UV ($\Delta_{o} = 26{,}700$ cm$^{-1}$). The spectrochemical series was historically established by measuring these UV-Vis band positions across many complexes.

Charge-transfer (CT) transitions involve electron movement between metal and ligand. **Ligand-to-metal charge transfer** (LMCT) promotes an electron from a ligand orbital to a metal orbital — the deep purple of MnO$_{4}^{-}$ arises from O$^{2-} \to$ Mn$^{7+}$ LMCT with $\varepsilon \approx 10{,}000$ in the visible. **Metal-to-ligand charge transfer** (MLCT) promotes an electron from a metal d orbital to a ligand $\pi^{*}$ orbital — the intense red of Ru(bpy)$_{3}^{2+}$ comes from Ru(d) $\to$ bpy($\pi^{*}$) MLCT at 450 nm with $\varepsilon \approx 14{,}000$. CT transitions are fully allowed (they involve orbitals of different parity and location), so their intensities are orders of magnitude larger than d-d transitions. The energy of a CT band measures the redox potential difference between donor and acceptor, connecting electronic spectroscopy to electrochemistry.

## Solvent effects and solvatochromism [Master]

The electronic absorption spectrum of a molecule depends on the solvent because the ground and excited states are stabilised to different extents by solute-solvent interactions. The primary mechanisms are:

1. **Dielectric stabilisation.** A more polar excited state is stabilised more by a polar solvent, red-shifting the absorption (bathochromic shift). This is the dominant effect for charge-transfer transitions.

2. **Hydrogen bonding.** Hydrogen-bonding solvents interact preferentially with lone pairs and polar functional groups, stabilising states that concentrate electron density on the hydrogen-bond-accepting atoms. For $n \to \pi^{*}$ transitions, hydrogen bonding stabilises the ground state (where the lone pair is fully occupied) more than the excited state (where the electron has been promoted to $\pi^{*}$), producing a blue shift (hypsochromic shift) with increasing hydrogen-bond donor ability.

3. **Polarisability.** Highly polarisable solvents (aromatics, carbon disulfide) provide dispersion stabilisation that favours the more polarisable state, typically the excited state with its larger, more diffuse electron cloud.

These effects are quantified by **solvatochromic parameters** such as the Kamlet-Taft $\pi^{*}$ scale (dipolarity/polarisability), $\alpha$ scale (hydrogen-bond donor ability), and $\beta$ scale (hydrogen-bond acceptor ability). The Dimroth-Reichardt $E_{T}(30)$ scale uses the solvatochromic shift of a pyridinium-N-phenolate betaine dye as a single-number measure of solvent polarity, spanning from $E_{T}(30) = 30.7$ for hexane to $E_{T}(30) = 63.1$ for water. These scales are indispensable for predicting and rationalising solvent effects on UV-Vis spectra.

The most dramatic solvent effects occur in charge-transfer complexes. Reichardt's dye shows a solvatochromic shift of over 350 nm between nonpolar and polar solvents — from blue-green in methanol to red-orange in toluene — making it a direct visual indicator of solvent polarity. This extreme solvatochromism reflects the zwitterionic character of the ground state, which is stabilised enormously by polar solvents.

## Fluorescence, phosphorescence, and the Kasha rule [Master]

After electronic excitation, a molecule can return to the ground state by emitting a photon (radiative decay) or by dissipating the energy as heat (nonradiative decay). Two types of emission are distinguished by their timescales and mechanisms.

**Fluorescence** is emission from the lowest vibrational level of the first excited singlet state ($S_{1}$) to the ground singlet state ($S_{0}$). It occurs on nanosecond timescales ($10^{-9}$ to $10^{-7}$ s) and preserves spin multiplicity. The fluorescence spectrum is a mirror image of the absorption spectrum when the ground and excited state potential curves have similar curvature, because the same Franck-Condon progression governs both absorption ($v_{i} = 0 \to v_{f}$) and emission ($v_{f} = 0 \to v_{i}$).

**Phosphorescence** is emission from the first triplet state ($T_{1}$) to the ground singlet ($S_{0}$). It involves a spin-forbidden transition ($\Delta S \neq 0$), so it is much slower (microseconds to seconds) and typically weaker than fluorescence. Phosphorescence is enhanced by heavy atoms (spin-orbit coupling) and by rigid environments (reducing nonradiative decay).

The **Kasha rule** [ref: TODO_REF Kasha1950] states that emission occurs predominantly from the lowest excited state of a given multiplicity, regardless of which excited state was initially populated. The reason is that internal conversion (nonradiative relaxation between electronic states of the same multiplicity) is much faster than radiative emission. A molecule excited to $S_{3}$ relaxes rapidly through $S_{2}$ to $S_{1}$ (in picoseconds), and then emits from $S_{1}$ (in nanoseconds). The fluorescence spectrum is therefore characteristic of the $S_{1} \to S_{0}$ transition, independent of the excitation wavelength. Exceptions to the Kasha rule are rare but known — for example, azulene emits from $S_{2}$ because the $S_{2} \to S_{1}$ internal conversion is unusually slow due to the large energy gap.

The quantum yield of fluorescence $\Phi_{f}$ is the fraction of absorbed photons that are re-emitted as fluorescence: $\Phi_{f} = k_{f}/(k_{f} + k_{\text{nr}})$, where $k_{f}$ is the radiative rate constant and $k_{\text{nr}}$ is the total nonradiative rate constant. Fluorescent dyes like fluorescein have $\Phi_{f} > 0.9$ — nearly every absorbed photon is re-emitted — while quenchers have $\Phi_{f} < 0.01$. Fluorescence quenching by collisional encounters with a quencher molecule (Stern-Volmer kinetics) or by resonance energy transfer (Forster resonance energy transfer, FRET) to a nearby acceptor provides the basis for fluorescence-based molecular rulers that measure distances of 1-10 nm in biological systems.

## Connections [Master]

- **Rotational spectroscopy [14.12.02].** High-resolution gas-phase electronic spectra show rotational fine structure in each vibronic band. The P-branch ($\Delta J = -1$), Q-branch ($\Delta J = 0$, present when the electronic angular momentum changes), and R-branch ($\Delta J = +1$) give the rotational constants of both ground and excited electronic states. Comparing $B'$ and $B''$ reveals whether the excited-state bond is longer or shorter.

- **Vibrational spectroscopy [14.12.03].** The Franck-Condon principle connects electronic spectroscopy to vibrational wave functions: the vibronic structure of an electronic band encodes the vibrational frequencies of the excited state and the displacement between ground and excited-state equilibrium geometries. The harmonic-oscillator and Morse-potential models from vibrational spectroscopy provide the framework for interpreting vibronic progressions.

- **Molecular orbital theory [14.05.02].** The classification of electronic transitions ($\sigma \to \sigma^{*}$, $\pi \to \pi^{*}$, $n \to \pi^{*}$) rests on the MO description of bonding. The HOMO-LUMO gap, the symmetry labels of the orbitals, and the spatial overlap between initial and final orbitals all originate in MO theory. The symmetry selection rules that determine whether a transition is allowed or forbidden require the group-theoretic analysis developed in MO theory.

- **Crystal-field theory [16.04.02].** The d-d spectra of transition-metal complexes directly measure the crystal-field splitting parameter $\Delta_{o}$ (or $\Delta_{t}$ for tetrahedral complexes). The spectrochemical series was established by measuring these spectra across many complexes. The parity selection rule (Laporte rule) and its relaxation by vibronic coupling are the central spectroscopic concepts in inorganic electronic spectroscopy.

- **Quantum chemistry: hydrogen atom [14.04.01].** The selection rules for atomic spectroscopy ($\Delta l = \pm 1$, $\Delta m = 0, \pm 1$) are the atomic analogues of the molecular selection rules discussed here. The transition dipole moment matrix elements between hydrogen-atom orbitals are the prototypes for the molecular transition dipoles that govern UV-Vis absorption intensities.

## Historical notes [Master]

The study of electronic spectroscopy began with the observation that coloured substances absorb specific wavelengths of light. Beer's 1852 law [ref: TODO_REF Beer1852] quantified the concentration dependence, building on the path-length observations of Bouguer (1729) [ref: TODO_REF Bouguer1729] and Lambert (1760) [ref: TODO_REF Lambert1760]. The combined Beer-Lambert law became the quantitative foundation of absorption spectroscopy, though its molecular-theoretic explanation awaited the development of quantum mechanics.

The Franck-Condon principle originated with James Franck's 1925 study [ref: TODO_REF Franck1925] of photochemical processes in diatomic molecules, where he observed that the intensity distribution in electronic band systems could be understood if the electronic transition was "vertical" — the nuclear positions did not change during the transition. Edward Condon in 1928 [ref: TODO_REF Condon1928] gave the quantum-mechanical formulation, expressing the vibronic intensity as the square of the overlap integral between vibrational wave functions of the two electronic states. The resulting Franck-Condon factor became the standard tool for analysing the vibrational structure of electronic spectra.

The development of UV-Vis instrumentation followed the Beer-Lambert framework. The first commercial UV-Vis spectrometer (Beckman DU, 1941) used a quartz prism and photomultiplier detector, making electronic spectroscopy accessible to routine chemical analysis. The introduction of double-beam instruments (automatically comparing sample and reference) improved accuracy, and the development of diode-array detectors in the 1980s enabled simultaneous multi-wavelength measurement without mechanical scanning.

The spectrochemical series was established empirically in the 1930s-1950s by measuring the d-d absorption bands of transition-metal complexes with different ligands. Hartmann and Ilse (1951) provided the crystal-field interpretation, and Tanabe and Sugano (1954) published the correlation diagrams that predict the number and positions of d-d bands for any $d^{n}$ configuration in octahedral and tetrahedral symmetry. These diagrams remain the standard tool for interpreting the electronic spectra of transition-metal complexes.

The development of fluorescence spectroscopy expanded the scope of electronic spectroscopy from steady-state absorption to time-resolved emission. The discovery of GFP (green fluorescent protein) by Shimomura, Chalfie, and Tsien (Nobel Prize 2008) transformed biology by enabling fluorescent labelling of specific proteins in living cells. The expansion into single-molecule fluorescence spectroscopy (Moerner, Nobel Prize 2014) pushed detection sensitivity to its ultimate limit and opened the field of super-resolution microscopy.
