## Intuition [Beginner]

Entropy measures how spread out energy and matter are across possible arrangements. A gas in a small flask has lower entropy than the same gas in a large room because the molecules in the room can occupy many more positions. When a process increases the number of accessible arrangements, entropy increases.

The **second law of thermodynamics** states that the total entropy of the universe always increases for a spontaneous process. For a chemical reaction, this means $\Delta S_\text{universe} = \Delta S_\text{system} + \Delta S_\text{surroundings} > 0$. Entropy is a **state function**: its change depends only on the initial and final states, not on the path taken.

But tracking the entropy of the entire universe is impractical. The **Gibbs free energy** $G$ collapses everything into a single number for the system. It is defined as $G = H - TS$, where $H$ is enthalpy, $T$ is temperature, and $S$ is entropy. At constant temperature and pressure, $\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$. A reaction is spontaneous when $\Delta G < 0$, at equilibrium when $\Delta G = 0$, and non-spontaneous when $\Delta G > 0$.

The sign of $\Delta G$ is a competition between enthalpy and entropy. An exothermic reaction ($\Delta H < 0$) with increasing disorder ($\Delta S > 0$) is spontaneous at all temperatures. An endothermic reaction ($\Delta H > 0$) with decreasing disorder ($\Delta S < 0$) is never spontaneous. The two mixed cases depend on temperature.

## Visual [Beginner]

The spontaneity of a reaction depends on the signs of $\Delta H$ and $\Delta S$. The table below summarises all four combinations:

| | $\Delta S > 0$ | $\Delta S < 0$ |
|---|---|---|
| $\Delta H < 0$ | Spontaneous at all $T$ | Spontaneous at low $T$ |
| $\Delta H > 0$ | Spontaneous at high $T$ | Never spontaneous |

The crossover temperature where spontaneity changes is $T = \Delta H / \Delta S$, where $\Delta G = 0$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Calculate the standard entropy change for the combustion of methane at 298 K:

$$\text{CH}_4(g) + 2\,\text{O}_2(g) \to \text{CO}_2(g) + 2\,\text{H}_2\text{O}(l)$$

Standard molar entropies $S^\circ$ at 298 K (all in J/(mol K)): $\text{CH}_4(g) = 186.3$, $\text{O}_2(g) = 205.2$, $\text{CO}_2(g) = 213.8$, $\text{H}_2\text{O}(l) = 69.9$.

$\Delta S^\circ$ = (total standard entropy of all products) $-$ (total standard entropy of all reactants)
$$\Delta S^\circ = [213.8 + 2(69.9)] - [186.3 + 2(205.2)]$$
$$\Delta S^\circ = 353.6 - 596.7 = -243.1 \;\text{J/(mol K)}$$

The entropy of the system decreases. Three moles of gas produce one mole of gas and two moles of liquid, so the system becomes more ordered. But the reaction releases a large amount of heat ($\Delta H^\circ = -890.3$ kJ/mol), and this heat increases the entropy of the surroundings enormously. The total $\Delta G^\circ = -890\,300 - 298(-243.1) = -890\,300 + 72\,444 = -817\,856$ J/mol. The reaction is strongly spontaneous despite the entropy decrease of the system.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Entropy as a state function and the second law

Entropy $S$ is a state function. Its change between two states depends only on the endpoints, not on the path. For a reversible process at constant temperature, the entropy change of the system is

$$\Delta S = \frac{q_\text{rev}}{T},$$

where $q_\text{rev}$ is the heat transferred in a reversible process connecting the initial and final states. Because $S$ is a state function, $\Delta S$ can be computed along any convenient reversible path, even if the actual process is irreversible.

The **second law of thermodynamics** states that for any spontaneous process,

$$\Delta S_\text{universe} = \Delta S_\text{system} + \Delta S_\text{surroundings} > 0.$$

The entropy change of the surroundings is

$$\Delta S_\text{surroundings} = \frac{-q_\text{system}}{T_\text{surroundings}}$$

for a process occurring at constant external temperature. For a reaction at constant pressure, $q_\text{system} = \Delta H$, so

$$\Delta S_\text{universe} = \Delta S_\text{system} - \frac{\Delta H}{T}.$$

Multiplying both sides by $-T$:

$$-T\,\Delta S_\text{universe} = \Delta H - T\,\Delta S_\text{system} = \Delta G.$$

Since $\Delta S_\text{universe} > 0$ for a spontaneous process, $-T\,\Delta S_\text{universe} < 0$, giving $\Delta G < 0$. This is the fundamental link: the Gibbs free energy criterion $\Delta G < 0$ is equivalent to the second law expressed for constant-$(T, P)$ processes.

### The third law and absolute entropy

The **third law of thermodynamics** (Nernst heat theorem, 1906; Planck formulation, 1911) states that the entropy of a perfect crystalline substance approaches zero as temperature approaches absolute zero:

$$\lim_{T \to 0} S(T) = 0 \quad \text{(perfect crystal)}.$$

A perfect crystal has a unique ground-state arrangement. There is only one way to arrange the atoms, so the number of microstates is $\Omega = 1$, and by Boltzmann's entropy formula $S = k_B \ln \Omega = 0$.

The third law provides an absolute reference point for entropy. Unlike enthalpy (where only changes are measurable), entropy has a natural zero. The absolute entropy of a substance at any temperature $T$ is obtained by integrating the heat capacity:

$$S(T) = \int_0^T \frac{C_P(T')}{T'}\,dT'.$$

In practice, heat-capacity measurements from near 0 K up to the temperature of interest are used. Phase transitions (melting, boiling) contribute $\Delta S_\text{trans} = \Delta H_\text{trans} / T_\text{trans}$ at each transition temperature.

### Standard molar entropies

The **standard molar entropy** $S^\circ$ is the absolute entropy of one mole of a substance in its standard state (pure substance at 1 bar pressure and a specified temperature, usually 298 K). Key trends:

- $S^\circ$ increases with molecular complexity. $\text{CH}_4$ ($186.3$ J/(mol K)) has higher entropy than Ar ($154.8$ J/(mol K)) because more atoms mean more vibrational and rotational degrees of freedom.
- $S^\circ$ increases from solid to liquid to gas. $\text{H}_2\text{O}(l)$ has $S^\circ = 69.9$ J/(mol K); $\text{H}_2\text{O}(g)$ has $S^\circ = 188.8$ J/(mol K).
- $S^\circ$ increases with temperature because higher $T$ populates more molecular energy levels.
- Dissolved ions have standard entropies referenced to $S^\circ(\text{H}^+, \text{aq}) = 0$ by convention, not from the third law directly.

For a chemical reaction $\sum_i \nu_i A_i = 0$, the standard entropy change is

$$\Delta_r S^\circ = \sum_i \nu_i S_i^\circ,$$

with $\nu_i > 0$ for products and $\nu_i < 0$ for reactants. This is the same bookkeeping pattern used for enthalpy changes via Hess's law.

### Standard Gibbs energy of reaction

The **standard Gibbs energy of reaction** is

$$\Delta_r G^\circ = \Delta_r H^\circ - T\,\Delta_r S^\circ.$$

Alternatively, using standard Gibbs energies of formation $\Delta_f G^\circ$ (tabulated for most compounds; $\Delta_f G^\circ = 0$ for elements in their reference form):

$$\Delta_r G^\circ = \sum_i \nu_i \Delta_f G_i^\circ.$$

The two methods give identical results. The formation-enthalpy-plus-entropy route is useful when you want to analyse how temperature affects spontaneity; the formation-Gibbs-energy route is useful when you only need $\Delta_r G^\circ$ at 298 K.

### Worked example at intermediate level

Predict the spontaneity of the decomposition of calcium carbonate at 298 K and at 1200 K:

$$\text{CaCO}_3(s) \to \text{CaO}(s) + \text{CO}_2(g)$$

Data at 298 K: $\Delta_f H^\circ(\text{CaCO}_3) = -1206.9$, $\Delta_f H^\circ(\text{CaO}) = -635.1$, $\Delta_f H^\circ(\text{CO}_2) = -393.5$ kJ/mol. $S^\circ(\text{CaCO}_3) = 92.9$, $S^\circ(\text{CaO}) = 38.1$, $S^\circ(\text{CO}_2) = 213.8$ J/(mol K).

**Step 1: Enthalpy change.**

$$\Delta_r H^\circ = (-635.1 - 393.5) - (-1206.9) = +178.3 \;\text{kJ/mol}$$

**Step 2: Entropy change.**

$$\Delta_r S^\circ = (38.1 + 213.8) - 92.9 = +159.0 \;\text{J/(mol K)}$$

The positive $\Delta S^\circ$ makes sense: a solid produces a solid plus a gas, increasing the number of accessible molecular arrangements.

**Step 3: Gibbs energy at 298 K.**

$$\Delta_r G^\circ_{298} = 178\,300 - 298(159.0) = 178\,300 - 47\,382 = +130\,918 \;\text{J/mol} = +130.9 \;\text{kJ/mol}$$

Non-spontaneous at 298 K.

**Step 4: Crossover temperature.**

$$T = \Delta_r H^\circ / \Delta_r S^\circ = 178\,300 / 159.0 = 1121 \;\text{K}$$

**Step 5: At 1200 K.**

$$\Delta_r G^\circ_{1200} = 178\,300 - 1200(159.0) = 178\,300 - 190\,800 = -12\,500 \;\text{J/mol} = -12.5 \;\text{kJ/mol}$$

Spontaneous at 1200 K. Calcium carbonate decomposes above about 1121 K (848$^\circ$C), consistent with industrial lime production in kilns at 900-1100$^\circ$C.

## Key result [Intermediate+]

### Calorimetric vs spectroscopic entropy: testing the third law

The third law predicts that calorimetric entropy (from heat-capacity integration) and spectroscopic entropy (from partition functions) should agree for perfect crystals. Experimental data confirm this and reveal exceptions with physical explanations:

| Substance | $S^\circ_\text{cal}$ (J/(mol K)) | $S^\circ_\text{spec}$ (J/(mol K)) | Deviation |
|---|---|---|---|
| $\text{N}_2(g)$ | 192.0 | 191.6 | 0.4 |
| $\text{O}_2(g)$ | 205.4 | 205.2 | 0.2 |
| $\text{HCl}(g)$ | 186.2 | 186.8 | 0.6 |
| $\text{CO}(g)$ | 193.3 | 197.9 | 4.6 |

Nitrogen, oxygen, and hydrogen chloride agree within experimental uncertainty. Carbon monoxide deviates by 4.6 J/(mol K), close to $R\ln 2 = 5.76$ J/(mol K), revealing residual entropy from disordered CO/OC orientations frozen into the crystal lattice.

### Trouton's rule across substances

Trouton's rule ($\Delta S_\text{vap} \approx 88$ J/(mol K) for most liquids) holds across diverse substances, confirming that the entropy increase on vaporisation is governed by a common mechanism (gain of translational freedom):

| Substance | $T_\text{bp}$ (K) | $\Delta S_\text{vap}$ (J/(mol K)) |
|---|---|---|
| Benzene | 353 | 87.3 |
| $\text{CCl}_4$ | 350 | 85.7 |
| Diethyl ether | 308 | 84.4 |
| Water | 373 | 109.0 |

Water is a notable outlier because hydrogen bonding lowers the entropy of the liquid, making the entropy gain on vaporisation larger than typical.

### CaCO3 decomposition: Gibbs energy predicts industrial practice

The worked example above predicts that calcium carbonate decomposes spontaneously above 1121 K. Industrial lime kilns operate at 900--1100 $^\circ$C (1173--1373 K), consistent with this prediction. The thermodynamic crossover temperature directly determines the minimum operating temperature for one of the world's oldest and largest-scale chemical processes.

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the Gibbs-Helmholtz equation from $G = H - TS$ and the thermodynamic identity $(\partial G / \partial T)_P = -S$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Start with $G/T$ as a function of $T$. Differentiate with respect to $T$ at constant $P$ and substitute $-S = (\partial G / \partial T)_P$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From $G = H - TS$, we have $S = (H - G)/T$. The temperature derivative of $G$ at constant $P$ is $(\partial G/\partial T)_P = -S$. Now compute:

$$\left(\frac{\partial (G/T)}{\partial T}\right)_P = \frac{T(\partial G/\partial T)_P - G}{T^2} = \frac{-TS - G}{T^2} = \frac{-H}{T^2},$$

where the last step uses $-TS - G = -TS - (H - TS) = -H$. This gives the **Gibbs-Helmholtz equation**:

$$\left(\frac{\partial (G/T)}{\partial T}\right)_P = -\frac{H}{T^2}.$$

Applied to a reaction: $[\partial(\Delta_r G/T)/\partial T]_P = -\Delta_r H/T^2$. This identity underpins the van't Hoff equation for the temperature dependence of the equilibrium constant.

</details>

## The third law, residual entropy, and the thermodynamic temperature scale [Master]

### Nernst heat theorem and Planck's formulation

The Nernst heat theorem (1906) states that for any isothermal process between equilibrium states of a condensed system (solid or liquid), the entropy change approaches zero as temperature approaches absolute zero:

$$\lim_{T \to 0} \Delta S(T) = 0.$$

Nernst arrived at this by extrapolating measurements of the Gibbs energy and enthalpy of chemical reactions at low temperatures. He observed that $\Delta G$ and $\Delta H$ converge as $T \to 0$ (since $\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$, this means $T\Delta S \to 0$), and he postulated the stronger statement that $\Delta S \to 0$ itself, not merely $T\Delta S \to 0$.

Planck (1911) strengthened the theorem to an absolute statement about individual substances: the entropy of every chemically homogeneous solid or liquid approaches zero at $T = 0$. Combined with Boltzmann's $S = k_B \ln \Omega$, this means that a perfect crystal at $T = 0$ has exactly one microstate ($\Omega = 1$). The Planck formulation converts entropy from a quantity defined only up to an additive constant into one with an absolute zero, making standard molar entropies physical quantities that can be measured and tabulated without ambiguity.

The third law has consequences beyond providing an entropy zero. It implies that heat capacities vanish at absolute zero: $C_P \to 0$ and $C_V \to 0$ as $T \to 0$. This follows because $S = \int_0^T (C_P/T')\,dT'$ must converge to a finite value, which requires the integrand to vanish at least as fast as $T'$. The Debye $T^3$ law for the low-temperature heat capacity of insulators ($C_V = (12\pi^4/5)R(T/\theta_D)^3$) satisfies this requirement. The coefficient of thermal expansion $\alpha$ also vanishes as $T \to 0$, a result that follows from the Maxwell relation $(\partial S/\partial P)_T = -(\partial V/\partial T)_P$ and the third law: since $S \to 0$ independent of $P$, $(\partial S/\partial P)_T \to 0$, forcing $(\partial V/\partial T)_P = V\alpha \to 0$.

### Residual entropy and disorder in crystals

Not all crystals satisfy $\Omega = 1$ at $T = 0$. Carbon monoxide, CO, crystallises with the molecules oriented nearly randomly as either CO or OC in the lattice. The resulting residual entropy is $S_\text{residual} = k_B \ln 2$ per molecule $= R \ln 2 = 5.76$ J/(mol K), close to the measured discrepancy between calorimetric and spectroscopic entropies. Ice Ih has residual entropy from the proton disorder on the hydrogen-bond network: each water molecule donates two and accepts two hydrogen bonds, but there are multiple configurations satisfying this constraint. Pauling (1935) estimated $S_\text{residual} = R \ln(3/2) = 3.37$ J/(mol K), in good agreement with experiment ($3.41$ J/(mol K)).

These exceptions do not violate the third law. The law applies to a **perfect** crystal in its unique ground state. Real crystals that freeze in configurational disorder retain residual entropy because they are kinetically trapped in metastable states, not because the true ground state has $\Omega > 1$. The distinction between thermodynamic equilibrium (the perfect crystal) and the kinetically frozen state is essential: the third law describes the entropy at equilibrium, not the entropy of whatever configuration the laboratory sample happens to adopt.

### Absolute entropy from calorimetry and spectroscopy

The absolute entropy of a gas at temperature $T$ is obtained by integrating $C_P / T$ from 0 K through all phase transitions up to $T$, with each phase transition contributing $\Delta H_\text{trans} / T_\text{trans}$:

$$S^\circ(T) = \int_0^{T_\text{fus}} \frac{C_P(\text{solid})}{T'}\,dT' + \frac{\Delta H_\text{fus}}{T_\text{fus}} + \int_{T_\text{fus}}^{T_\text{vap}} \frac{C_P(\text{liquid})}{T'}\,dT' + \frac{\Delta H_\text{vap}}{T_\text{vap}} + \int_{T_\text{vap}}^T \frac{C_P(\text{gas})}{T'}\,dT'.$$

For ideal gases, the calorimetric entropy can be compared with the **statistical entropy** computed from molecular partition functions. The Sackur-Tetrode equation gives the translational entropy of a monatomic ideal gas:

$$S_\text{trans} = R\left[\frac{5}{2} + \ln\!\left(\frac{(2\pi m k_B T)^{3/2} k_B T}{P^\circ h^3}\right)\right],$$

and the rotational and vibrational contributions are added from the corresponding partition-function factors. Agreement between calorimetric and statistical entropies to within experimental uncertainty is a stringent test of the consistency of the third law, heat-capacity data, and spectroscopic constants. Discrepancies reveal residual entropy (CO, ice) or the need for revised spectroscopic assignments.

## Gibbs free energy, spontaneity, and the Gibbs-Helmholtz equation [Master]

### The Gibbs energy as a thermodynamic potential

The Gibbs free energy is the fourth thermodynamic potential obtained by Legendre-transforming the internal energy twice:

$$G(T, P, \{N_i\}) = U(S, V, \{N_i\}) - TS + PV.$$

Its natural variables are $T$, $P$, and the mole numbers $\{N_i\}$. The differential form for an open multicomponent system is

$$dG = -S\,dT + V\,dP + \sum_i \mu_i\,dn_i,$$

where $\mu_i = (\partial G / \partial n_i)_{T, P, n_{j \neq i}}$ is the chemical potential of species $i$. At constant $T$ and $P$, $dG = \sum_i \mu_i\,dn_i$, and the equilibrium condition is $dG = 0$ (stationarity) with $d^2G > 0$ (stability, i.e., a true minimum).

The Gibbs energy is the maximum non-expansion work obtainable from a process at constant $T$ and $P$. For a reversible electrochemical cell, $\Delta_r G = -nFE$, where $n$ is the number of moles of electrons transferred, $F$ is the Faraday constant, and $E$ is the cell potential. This identification bridges chemical thermodynamics to electrochemistry.

### The Gibbs-Helmholtz equation

The **Gibbs-Helmholtz equation** gives the temperature dependence of $G/T$:

$$\left(\frac{\partial (G/T)}{\partial T}\right)_P = -\frac{H}{T^2}.$$

**Proof.** Compute $(G/T)$ as a function of $T$ at constant $P$:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial T}\!\left(\frac{G}{T}\right) = \frac{T(\partial G/\partial T)_P - G}{T^2} = \frac{-TS - G}{T^2}.$$

Since $G = H - TS$, we have $-TS - G = -TS - H + TS = -H$. Substituting:

$$\left(\frac{\partial (G/T)}{\partial T}\right)_P = -\frac{H}{T^2}. \quad \square$$

Applied to a chemical reaction, the Gibbs-Helmholtz equation becomes

$$\left(\frac{\partial (\Delta_r G / T)}{\partial T}\right)_P = -\frac{\Delta_r H}{T^2}.$$

Combined with $\Delta_r G^\circ = -RT \ln K$, this yields the van't Hoff equation:

$$\frac{d \ln K}{dT} = \frac{\Delta_r H^\circ}{RT^2}.$$

The Gibbs-Helmholtz equation is the thermodynamic engine behind all predictions of how equilibrium shifts with temperature. It requires only the enthalpy (measurable by calorimetry) and links it to the temperature dependence of the Gibbs energy (measurable by equilibrium measurements or electrochemistry).

### Temperature dependence of spontaneity

For a reaction with approximately temperature-independent $\Delta_r H^\circ$ and $\Delta_r S^\circ$, the Gibbs energy is linear in temperature:

$$\Delta_r G^\circ(T) = \Delta_r H^\circ - T\,\Delta_r S^\circ.$$

The four cases follow directly:

1. $\Delta_r H^\circ < 0$, $\Delta_r S^\circ > 0$: $\Delta_r G^\circ < 0$ at all $T$. Both terms favour spontaneity.
2. $\Delta_r H^\circ > 0$, $\Delta_r S^\circ < 0$: $\Delta_r G^\circ > 0$ at all $T$. Neither term favours spontaneity.
3. $\Delta_r H^\circ < 0$, $\Delta_r S^\circ < 0$: $\Delta_r G^\circ < 0$ at low $T$, $\Delta_r G^\circ > 0$ at high $T$. Crossover at $T = \Delta_r H^\circ / \Delta_r S^\circ$.
4. $\Delta_r H^\circ > 0$, $\Delta_r S^\circ > 0$: $\Delta_r G^\circ > 0$ at low $T$, $\Delta_r G^\circ < 0$ at high $T$. Crossover at $T = \Delta_r H^\circ / \Delta_r S^\circ$.

When $\Delta_r H^\circ$ and $\Delta_r S^\circ$ are temperature-dependent (through the difference in heat capacities $\Delta_r C_P$), the linear approximation breaks down and $\Delta_r G^\circ(T)$ becomes a nontrivial function. The Kirchhoff equations give $d\Delta_r H^\circ/dT = \Delta_r C_P$ and $d\Delta_r S^\circ/dT = \Delta_r C_P / T$. Integrating these with a temperature-dependent $\Delta_r C_P$ (often expressed as a polynomial $\Delta_r C_P = a + bT + cT^2 + \ldots$ from the Shomate or NIST-JANAF tables) gives accurate predictions of $\Delta_r G^\circ(T)$ over wide temperature ranges.

### The pressure dependence of Gibbs energy

From $dG = -S\,dT + V\,dP$ at constant composition, the pressure dependence of the Gibbs energy at constant temperature is

$$\left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial P}\right)_T = V.$$

For an ideal gas, $V = nRT/P$, so integrating from standard pressure $P^\circ = 1$ bar to arbitrary pressure $P$:

$$G(T, P) = G^\circ(T) + nRT \ln\!\left(\frac{P}{P^\circ}\right).$$

For a pure liquid or solid, $V$ is approximately constant, giving $G(T, P) \approx G^\circ(T) + V(P - P^\circ)$. Because molar volumes of condensed phases are small ($\sim 10{-}100$ cm$^3$/mol), the pressure dependence of $G$ for solids and liquids is negligible at moderate pressures. This justifies setting the activity of pure solids and liquids to unity in the equilibrium-constant expression.

For gas-phase reactions with a change in the number of moles ($\Delta n_\text{gas} \neq 0$), pressure affects $\Delta_r G$ through the reaction quotient $Q$. At constant $T$, increasing total pressure shifts equilibrium toward the side with fewer gas moles because the $RT \ln(P/P^\circ)$ contribution per mole of gas adds up more on the side with more gas moles. This is the quantitative basis of Le Chatelier's pressure principle.

## Historical context [Master]

The concept of entropy was introduced by Rudolf Clausius in 1865 as the thermodynamic quantity that always increases in spontaneous processes. Clausius chose the name from the Greek $\eta\ \tau\rho o\pi\eta$ ("the transformation"), reflecting entropy's role as the measure of energy's dispersal. His statement "the entropy of the universe tends to a maximum" was the first clear articulation of the second law in terms of entropy rather than heat engines.

The third law emerged from Walther Nernst's 1906 investigations of chemical equilibria at low temperatures. Nernst observed that the Gibbs energies and enthalpies of reactions converged as temperature decreased, and he postulated that $\Delta S \to 0$ as $T \to 0$ for condensed-phase reactions. Max Planck strengthened this in 1911 to the absolute statement that $S \to 0$ for a perfect crystal, giving entropy a natural zero point. The Nernst-Planck formulation resolved a fundamental problem: without the third law, thermodynamics defines only entropy *changes*, and the absolute entropy of any substance is undetermined. With the third law, $S^\circ$ becomes a measurable, tabulated quantity.

The Gibbs energy was introduced by J. Willard Gibbs in his 1876-78 monograph *On the Equilibrium of Heterogeneous Substances*, where he defined the chemical potential and derived the conditions for chemical and phase equilibrium. Gibbs's $G$ (he called it the "thermodynamic potential at constant temperature and pressure") unified the enthalpy-entropy tension into a single criterion for spontaneity. The Gibbs-Helmholtz equation was developed independently by Gibbs and Helmholtz in the early 1880s; Helmholtz's formulation emphasised the connection between the free energy and the maximum work obtainable from a chemical process.

Trouton's rule (1884), which states that $\Delta S_\text{vap} \approx 88$ J/(mol K) for most liquids at their normal boiling points, was an early empirical observation that finds its explanation in the statistical mechanics of translational entropy. Water's anomalously high $\Delta S_\text{vap} \approx 109$ J/(mol K) reflects the low entropy of liquid water due to its hydrogen-bond network.
