## Intuition [Beginner]

A constructible complex on an algebraic variety $X$ is a complex of sheaves whose cohomology sheaves are locally constant of finite rank along each stratum of a stratification — the basic objects of topological cohomology on a singular space. The constructible derived category $D^b_c(X)$ assembles all such complexes, with the usual derived-category morphisms. The standard cohomology functors $\mathcal{H}^i$ extract the cohomology sheaves of a complex one degree at a time. Perverse sheaves are constructible complexes that satisfy a single condition tying together all the cohomology sheaves of a complex into one balanced object — the support dimensions of the cohomology sheaves shrink as the cohomological degree grows, in a way that is symmetric under Verdier duality.

The condition is the support-dimension inequality $\dim \mathrm{supp}\, \mathcal{H}^i(\mathcal{F}) \le -i$ for all $i$. Read it this way: the cohomology sheaf in degree $i$ lives on a closed subset of $X$ whose dimension is at most $-i$. In degree $-d$ the support is allowed to be all of $X$ (an open stratum of dimension $d$); in degree $-d + 1$ the support must drop to dimension $\le d - 1$; and so on.

The dual condition under Verdier duality $\mathbb{D}$ gives the other half of the perverse t-structure, and the intersection of the two halves is the category $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ — the heart of the middle-perversity t-structure on $D^b_c(X)$. Despite being a heart of a t-structure on a triangulated category, $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ is an honest abelian category, and every object has a finite composition series in the Jordan-Hölder sense.

The everyday analogy is a soil-profile diagram in stratigraphy. Each horizontal stratum of soil has its own composition and age, and the "perverse" condition says that the deeper strata extend further in horizontal area than the surface ones — the topsoil is the most spatially extensive, the bedrock the most localised. A perverse sheaf is the analogue of a balanced soil profile where the horizontal-extent constraint scales correctly with depth. The simple perverse sheaves are pure-stratum objects (intersection cohomology complexes), and every perverse sheaf is built from these simples by a finite sequence of extensions.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute the two smallest interesting perverse sheaves on $X = \mathbb{C}$ (the complex line), and verify the support-dimension condition by hand. This is the smallest example where the perverse t-structure has more than one isomorphism class of simple object.

Step 1. Identify the two basic complexes. Take the constant sheaf $\mathbb{Q}_\mathbb{C}$ on $\mathbb{C}$ (the locally constant sheaf with stalk $\mathbb{Q}$), shifted to put it in degree $-1$: $\mathcal{F}_1 = \mathbb{Q}_\mathbb{C}[1]$ has $\mathcal{H}^i(\mathcal{F}_1) = \mathbb{Q}_\mathbb{C}$ when $i = -1$ and zero otherwise. Take the skyscraper sheaf $\mathbb{Q}_{\{0\}}$ at the origin: $\mathcal{F}_2 = \mathbb{Q}_{\{0\}}$ has $\mathcal{H}^i(\mathcal{F}_2) = \mathbb{Q}_{\{0\}}$ when $i = 0$ and zero otherwise.

Step 2. Verify the support-dimension condition for $\mathcal{F}_1$. The condition is $\dim \mathrm{supp}\, \mathcal{H}^i(\mathcal{F}_1) \le -i$ for all $i$. The only nonzero cohomology is in degree $i = -1$, where $\mathrm{supp}\, \mathcal{H}^{-1}(\mathcal{F}_1) = \mathbb{C}$ has dimension $1$. The inequality reads $1 \le -(-1) = 1$, which holds with equality. For all other $i$, $\mathcal{H}^i(\mathcal{F}_1) = 0$ has empty support of dimension $-\infty$, and the inequality holds. So $\mathcal{F}_1 \in {}^p\!D^{\le 0}$.

Step 3. Verify the support-dimension condition for $\mathcal{F}_2$. The only nonzero cohomology is in degree $i = 0$, where $\mathrm{supp}\, \mathcal{H}^0(\mathcal{F}_2) = \{0\}$ has dimension $0$. The inequality reads $0 \le -0 = 0$, which holds with equality. For all other $i$, the inequality holds vacuously. So $\mathcal{F}_2 \in {}^p\!D^{\le 0}$.

Step 4. Verify the Verdier-dual condition. The Verdier dual on $\mathbb{C}$ is computed using the dualising complex $\omega^\bullet_\mathbb{C} = \mathbb{Q}_\mathbb{C}[2]$ (the shifted constant sheaf, since $\mathbb{C}$ is smooth of complex dimension $1$). The Verdier dual of $\mathcal{F}_1 = \mathbb{Q}_\mathbb{C}[1]$ is $\mathbb{D}(\mathbb{Q}_\mathbb{C}[1]) = R\mathcal{H}om(\mathbb{Q}_\mathbb{C}[1], \mathbb{Q}_\mathbb{C}[2]) = \mathbb{Q}_\mathbb{C}[1] = \mathcal{F}_1$, so $\mathcal{F}_1$ is self-Verdier-dual. The Verdier dual of $\mathcal{F}_2 = \mathbb{Q}_{\{0\}}$ is $\mathbb{D}(\mathbb{Q}_{\{0\}}) = \mathbb{Q}_{\{0\}}$ by the analogous calculation (using that the inclusion of the origin is a proper map and applying the duality $i_* \mathbb{D} = \mathbb{D} i_!$). So $\mathcal{F}_2$ is also self-Verdier-dual.

Step 5. Conclude. Both $\mathcal{F}_1$ and $\mathcal{F}_2$ satisfy the support-dimension condition and are self-Verdier-dual, hence both lie in the heart ${}^p\!D^{\le 0} \cap {}^p\!D^{\ge 0} = \mathrm{Perv}(\mathbb{C})$. These are two distinct simple perverse sheaves on $\mathbb{C}$: $\mathcal{F}_1 = \mathrm{IC}_\mathbb{C} = \mathbb{Q}_\mathbb{C}[1]$ is the intersection cohomology complex on the whole line (dimension $1$, constant local system), and $\mathcal{F}_2 = \mathrm{IC}_{\{0\}}(\mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}_{\{0\}}$ is the intersection cohomology complex on the origin (dimension $0$, constant local system).

What this tells us. On the complex line $\mathbb{C}$, the two basic perverse sheaves are the shifted constant sheaf $\mathbb{Q}_\mathbb{C}[1]$ and the skyscraper $\mathbb{Q}_{\{0\}}$ at any point. They satisfy the support-dimension condition with equality in their respective degrees, are stable under Verdier duality, and exemplify the two basic types of simple objects in $\mathrm{Perv}(\mathbb{C})$: intermediate extensions from open strata (here the open dense $\mathbb{C}^*$ or all of $\mathbb{C}$) and from zero-dimensional closed strata (here the origin).

True or false: every perverse sheaf on a complex algebraic variety has a finite Jordan-Hölder filtration whose simple subquotients are intersection cohomology complexes attached to (smooth locally closed subvariety, irreducible local system) pairs.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.** $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ is an abelian, Artinian, and Noetherian category (proved in BBD 1982 §4.0), so every object has a finite composition series whose subquotients are simple. The simple objects in $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ are classified (BBD §4.3) as the intermediate extensions $\mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L}) = j_{!*}(\mathcal{L}[d_Z])$ of irreducible local systems $\mathcal{L}$ on smooth locally closed subvarieties $Z \subset X$ of dimension $d_Z$, where $j : Z \hookrightarrow X$ is the inclusion. This classification is what makes perverse sheaves a tractable abelian category — every perverse sheaf is built from a finite multiset of $(Z, \mathcal{L})$ pairs by repeated extension.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a complex algebraic variety (or a complex analytic space, or a finite-type scheme over an algebraically closed field, with adapted notions of constructibility and stratification). Fix a coefficient field $k$ (typically $\mathbb{Q}$ or $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}_\ell$ in the étale setting) and a stratification $X = \bigsqcup_\alpha S_\alpha$ by smooth locally closed subvarieties such that closures of strata are unions of strata (a Whitney stratification suffices).

**Definition (constructible complex).** A complex $\mathcal{F}^\bullet$ in the bounded derived category $D^b(\mathrm{Sh}(X, k))$ of sheaves of $k$-modules on $X$ is **constructible** with respect to the stratification if each cohomology sheaf $\mathcal{H}^i(\mathcal{F}^\bullet)$ is a locally constant sheaf of finite-rank $k$-modules on each stratum $S_\alpha$. The full subcategory of constructible complexes is the **bounded constructible derived category** $D^b_c(X) \subset D^b(\mathrm{Sh}(X, k))$. It is closed under the Verdier dual $\mathbb{D}_X = R\mathcal{H}om(-, \omega^\bullet_X)$, where $\omega^\bullet_X = a_X^! k$ is the **dualising complex** associated to the structure map $a_X : X \to \mathrm{pt}$ via the six-functor formalism [04.03.16]; for $X$ smooth of complex dimension $d$, $\omega^\bullet_X = k_X[2d]$ (with a Tate twist in the $\ell$-adic setting that we suppress).

**Definition (middle-perversity t-structure on $D^b_c(X)$, Beilinson-Bernstein-Deligne 1982 §2.1).** Define two full subcategories of $D^b_c(X)$:
$$
{}^p\!D^{\le 0}(X) := \{ \mathcal{F}^\bullet \in D^b_c(X) : \dim \mathrm{supp}\, \mathcal{H}^i(\mathcal{F}^\bullet) \le -i \text{ for all } i \},
$$
$$
{}^p\!D^{\ge 0}(X) := \{ \mathcal{F}^\bullet \in D^b_c(X) : \mathbb{D}_X(\mathcal{F}^\bullet) \in {}^p\!D^{\le 0}(X) \}.
$$
The pair $({}^p\!D^{\le 0}, {}^p\!D^{\ge 0})$ is a t-structure on $D^b_c(X)$ (in the sense of [04.03.18]), called the **middle-perversity t-structure**. Equivalently, ${}^p\!D^{\ge 0}(X)$ is the subcategory of $\mathcal{F}^\bullet$ such that for every $i$, the costalk $i_x^! \mathcal{F}^\bullet \in D^{\ge -\dim \overline{\{x\}}}$ for every point $x \in X$ (Mac Pherson's reformulation).

**Definition (perverse sheaves; BBD 1982 §2.2).** The **heart** of the middle-perversity t-structure is the abelian category of **perverse sheaves**:
$$
\mathrm{Perv}(X) := {}^p\!D^{\le 0}(X) \cap {}^p\!D^{\ge 0}(X).
$$
By the general BBD theorem [04.03.18 §1.3.6], $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ is an abelian category, with short exact sequences corresponding to distinguished triangles in $D^b_c(X)$ all three of whose terms lie in $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$.

**Definition (perverse cohomology).** The cohomological functor of the middle-perversity t-structure is
$$
{}^p\!H^n : D^b_c(X) \to \mathrm{Perv}(X), \qquad {}^p\!H^n := {}^p\tau_{\le 0} \circ {}^p\tau_{\ge 0} \circ [n].
$$
For every distinguished triangle $\mathcal{F}^\bullet \to \mathcal{G}^\bullet \to \mathcal{H}^\bullet \to \mathcal{F}^\bullet[1]$ in $D^b_c(X)$, applying $\{{}^p\!H^n\}_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}$ produces a long exact sequence in $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$.

**Definition (intermediate extension and simple perverse sheaves).** Let $Z \subset X$ be a smooth locally closed irreducible subvariety of complex dimension $d_Z$, with locally closed embedding $j : Z \hookrightarrow X$, and let $\mathcal{L}$ be an irreducible local system of $k$-vector spaces on $Z$ (i.e., an irreducible representation of $\pi_1(Z, z_0)$ on a finite-dimensional $k$-vector space). The **intermediate extension** is
$$
j_{!*}(\mathcal{L}[d_Z]) := \mathrm{im}\bigl( {}^p\!H^0(j_! \mathcal{L}[d_Z]) \to {}^p\!H^0(j_* \mathcal{L}[d_Z]) \bigr) \in \mathrm{Perv}(X),
$$
where the morphism is the natural one induced by the adjunction $j_! \to j_*$ on $D^b_c$, and $\mathrm{im}$ is taken in the abelian category $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$. Write $\mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L}) := j_{!*}(\mathcal{L}[d_Z])$ when $Z \hookrightarrow X$ is understood; this is the **intersection cohomology complex** of $\overline{Z}$ with coefficients in $\mathcal{L}$.

**Definition (simple objects of $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$, BBD 1982 §4.3).** The simple objects of $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ are exactly the intersection cohomology complexes $\mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L})$ for pairs $(Z, \mathcal{L})$ with $Z \subset X$ smooth locally closed irreducible and $\mathcal{L}$ an irreducible local system on $Z$, up to the equivalence $(Z, \mathcal{L}) \sim (Z', \mathcal{L}')$ iff $\overline{Z} = \overline{Z'}$ and $\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{L}'$ on the common open dense subset of $Z \cap Z'$.

**Definition (Verdier-self-duality of $\mathrm{IC}$).** For $Z$ open in its closure and $\mathcal{L}$ equipped with a non-degenerate symmetric (or alternating) self-pairing $\mathcal{L} \otimes \mathcal{L} \to k_Z$, the intersection cohomology complex satisfies $\mathbb{D}_X(\mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L})) \cong \mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L}^\vee) \cong \mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L})$ as perverse sheaves on $X$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The condition $\dim \mathrm{supp}\, \mathcal{H}^i(\mathcal{F}) \le -i$ controls **cohomology sheaves**, not the complex's own degrees. A complex concentrated in a single degree $n$ as a sheaf is perverse iff its support has dimension exactly $-n$; the constant sheaf $k_X$ on a smooth variety $X$ of dimension $d$ is **not** perverse, but $k_X[d]$ is. The shift is not optional cosmetic data.

- The middle-perversity t-structure on $D^b_c(X)$ is **not** the standard t-structure. The standard t-structure has heart $\mathrm{Cons}(X)$ (constructible sheaves placed in degree $0$); the perverse t-structure has heart $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$. These are different abelian categories and the two t-structures are exchanged by no functor — they are simply two different t-structures on the same triangulated category, each with its own cohomology, its own simples, and its own decomposition behaviour under proper pushforward.

- Verdier duality on $\mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L})$ takes the local system $\mathcal{L}$ to its dual $\mathcal{L}^\vee$. **Verdier duality is not the identity on perverse sheaves** in general; the simples are self-Verdier-dual only when the underlying local system is. For $\mathcal{L} = k$ the constant local system (any rank), $\mathcal{L}^\vee = \mathcal{L}$ and $\mathrm{IC}$ is self-dual; for higher-rank local systems without self-pairings, duality acts as a non-identity permutation of simples.

- The simple objects of $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ are classified by **pairs** $(Z, \mathcal{L})$ — both the stratum and the local system — not by the stratum alone. Two distinct local systems $\mathcal{L}_1, \mathcal{L}_2$ on the same smooth locally closed $Z$ produce non-isomorphic simple perverse sheaves $\mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L}_1) \not\cong \mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L}_2)$.

- The intermediate extension $j_{!*}$ is generally **not** equal to either $j_!$ or $j_*$. It is the image of the morphism ${}^p\!H^0(j_! \mathcal{L}[d_Z]) \to {}^p\!H^0(j_* \mathcal{L}[d_Z])$ in $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$, and it sits strictly between $j_!$ and $j_*$ when $j$ is not closed. Confusing $j_{!*}$ with $j_*$ produces an object that is generally not perverse and has the wrong simple-summand structure under the decomposition theorem.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (BBD decomposition theorem; Beilinson-Bernstein-Deligne 1982 *Astérisque* 100 §6.2.5; Gabber 1981).** *Let $f : X \to Y$ be a proper morphism of complex algebraic varieties with $X$ smooth, and let $k$ be a field of characteristic zero. Then in $D^b_c(Y, k)$ the derived direct image of the shifted constant sheaf decomposes as a direct sum of shifted intermediate extensions:*
$$
Rf_* k_X[\dim X] \cong \bigoplus_{i \in \mathbb{Z}} \, {}^p\!H^i\bigl(Rf_* k_X[\dim X]\bigr)[-i],
$$
*and each perverse cohomology summand ${}^p\!H^i(Rf_* k_X[\dim X]) \in \mathrm{Perv}(Y)$ is a semisimple perverse sheaf, hence decomposes as a direct sum of simple intersection cohomology complexes*
$$
{}^p\!H^i\bigl(Rf_* k_X[\dim X]\bigr) \cong \bigoplus_{(\overline{Z_\alpha}, \mathcal{L}_\alpha)} \mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z_\alpha}}(\mathcal{L}_\alpha)^{\oplus m_{i, \alpha}}
$$
*indexed by pairs $(\overline{Z_\alpha}, \mathcal{L}_\alpha)$ of irreducible closed subvarieties of $Y$ equipped with irreducible local systems on smooth open dense parts, with non-negative integer multiplicities $m_{i, \alpha}$.*

**Proof.** The argument has four steps. First, reduce to the $\ell$-adic setting over a finite field via spreading out and base change. Second, apply Deligne's purity theorem to obtain that $Rf_* k_X[\dim X]$ is pure of weight $\dim X$ in the sense of Frobenius weights. Third, use the BBD theory of weights on perverse sheaves to derive that every pure perverse sheaf is geometrically semisimple. Fourth, transfer back to the original characteristic-zero setting via specialisation.

**Step 1: spread out.** A smooth proper map $f : X \to Y$ of complex algebraic varieties is defined over a finitely generated $\mathbb{Z}$-algebra $A \subset \mathbb{C}$, by standard spreading-out arguments (EGA IV, §8): there exists a smooth proper morphism $f_A : X_A \to Y_A$ over $\mathrm{Spec}\, A$ with generic fibre the original $f$. Choosing a maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m} \subset A$ produces a residue field $A/\mathfrak{m} = \mathbb{F}_q$ for some prime power $q$, and the special fibre $f_q : X_q \to Y_q$ is a smooth proper morphism over $\mathbb{F}_q$. By the smooth proper base change theorem (SGA 4, Exposé XII), the étale cohomology of the special fibre agrees with the étale cohomology of the generic fibre, and the comparison between $\ell$-adic and Betti cohomology on the generic fibre (Artin's comparison, Deligne) reduces the decomposition statement in characteristic zero to its $\ell$-adic analogue over $\mathbb{F}_q$.

**Step 2: purity.** By Deligne's purity theorem (*Weil II*, *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 52, 1980), the derived direct image $Rf_{q,*} \overline{\mathbb{Q}}_{\ell, X_q}[\dim X_q]$ is **pure of weight $\dim X_q$** as a complex of $\ell$-adic sheaves on $Y_q$: every Frobenius eigenvalue on each cohomology stalk has complex absolute value $q^{(w - i)/2}$ where $w = \dim X$ and $i$ is the perverse-cohomology degree. The purity statement requires $f$ proper (for the proper-base-change comparison) and $X$ smooth (to ensure the constant sheaf $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}_{\ell, X}[\dim X]$ is pure of weight $\dim X$ on $X$ before pushforward; equivalently, that it is the intersection cohomology complex of $X$, which holds for $X$ smooth).

**Step 3: semisimplicity of pure perverse sheaves.** BBD §5.3.8 proves that every **pure perverse sheaf** $\mathcal{P} \in \mathrm{Perv}(Y_q, \overline{\mathbb{Q}}_\ell)$ on a variety over $\mathbb{F}_q$ is semisimple after pullback to the algebraic closure $\overline{\mathbb{F}}_q$. The proof uses the weight filtration $W_\bullet \mathcal{P}$ on a mixed perverse sheaf and the fact that the graded pieces of a pure perverse sheaf are forced to be direct sums of simples by the rigidity of weight filtrations under Frobenius. The simples of $\mathrm{Perv}(Y_q, \overline{\mathbb{Q}}_\ell)$ are the intersection cohomology complexes $\mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z_\alpha}}(\mathcal{L}_\alpha)$ for $(\overline{Z_\alpha}, \mathcal{L}_\alpha)$ pairs over $\overline{\mathbb{F}}_q$. Hence the perverse cohomology sheaves ${}^p\!H^i(Rf_{q,*} \overline{\mathbb{Q}}_\ell[\dim X])$ are direct sums of shifted IC complexes.

**Step 4: degeneration of the perverse Leray spectral sequence.** The perverse Leray spectral sequence $E_2^{p,q} = {}^p\!H^p({}^p\!H^q(Rf_* k_X[\dim X])) \Rightarrow H^{p+q}(X, k)$ degenerates at $E_2$ for proper $f$ with $X$ smooth — equivalently, the direct sum decomposition $Rf_* k_X[\dim X] \cong \bigoplus_i {}^p\!H^i(Rf_* k_X[\dim X])[-i]$ in $D^b_c(Y)$ holds without higher correction. This degeneration is a consequence of the semisimplicity proved in Step 3 (semisimple pure perverse sheaves admit no nonzero extensions to higher perverse-cohomology degrees) and the standard machinery of t-structure spectral sequences applied to the middle-perversity t-structure. Transferring back to characteristic zero via Step 1's spreading-out completes the proof in the original complex setting. $\square$

**Bridge.** The BBD decomposition theorem builds toward an abelian-category understanding of proper pushforwards in the derived category of constructible sheaves, and the foundational reason the construction works is that the weight filtration coming from $\ell$-adic Frobenius on a pure perverse sheaf forces semisimplicity, which is then transferred to characteristic zero via spreading-out and Artin comparison. The bridge is the perverse t-structure, which packages the support-dimension condition into an abelian category whose simples (the intersection cohomology complexes) are exactly what proper pushforwards of smooth varieties decompose into. Putting these together, every proper map of complex varieties with smooth source produces a direct-sum decomposition of its constant sheaf's pushforward into a multiset of $(\overline{Z_\alpha}, \mathcal{L}_\alpha)$ pairs, and the geometry of the map is read off from the multiplicities.

This pattern appears again in [04.03.18] (t-structures and hearts), where the perverse t-structure is the canonical non-standard example with deep geometric content, and in [04.03.16] (the six-functor formalism), where the interaction between Verdier duality, proper pushforward, and the perverse t-structure produces the decomposition statement. The central insight is that perverse sheaves are the abelian category that organises constructible cohomology of singular and stratified spaces in a single uniform setting, and the decomposition theorem is the statement that proper maps respect this organisation — the cohomology of $X$ decomposes along the strata of $Y$ in a way that is canonical and computable.

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

State the BBD decomposition theorem for a proper morphism $f : X \to Y$ of complex algebraic varieties with $X$ smooth, and derive as a corollary the degeneration of the perverse Leray spectral sequence at $E_2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The decomposition theorem says $Rf_* k_X[\dim X]$ decomposes as a direct sum of shifted intermediate extensions. The perverse Leray spectral sequence has $E_2^{p,q} = {}^p\!H^p(Y, {}^p\!H^q(Rf_* k_X[\dim X]))$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Theorem (BBD decomposition).** For $f : X \to Y$ proper with $X$ smooth complex algebraic, $\dim X = n$:
$$
Rf_* k_X[n] \cong \bigoplus_{i} {}^p\!H^i(Rf_* k_X[n])[-i] \quad \text{in } D^b_c(Y),
$$
and each summand ${}^p\!H^i(Rf_* k_X[n])$ is semisimple in $\mathrm{Perv}(Y)$, decomposing as
$$
{}^p\!H^i(Rf_* k_X[n]) \cong \bigoplus_\alpha \mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z_\alpha}}(\mathcal{L}_\alpha)^{\oplus m_{i, \alpha}}.
$$

**Corollary (degeneration of the perverse Leray spectral sequence).** The perverse Leray spectral sequence is
$$
E_2^{p,q} = {}^p\!H^p(Y, {}^p\!H^q(Rf_* k_X[n])) \Longrightarrow H^{p+q}(X, k)[n] = H^{p+q+n}(X, k).
$$
The decomposition $Rf_* k_X[n] \cong \bigoplus_i {}^p\!H^i(Rf_* k_X[n])[-i]$ implies that the abutment $H^{p+q+n}(X, k)$ splits as
$$
H^{p+q+n}(X, k) \cong \bigoplus_{i} {}^p\!H^{p+q-i}(Y, {}^p\!H^i(Rf_* k_X[n])),
$$
i.e., the spectral-sequence terms in degrees $(p, q)$ with $p + q = p_0 + q_0$ assemble directly without higher differentials. By a standard convergence argument, $d_r = 0$ for all $r \ge 2$, so the spectral sequence degenerates at $E_2$.

Application: for $f : X \to Y$ a proper smooth morphism (e.g., a smooth projective family), the Leray spectral sequence in classical cohomology degenerates at $E_2$ (Deligne 1968, with the algebraic-geometric proof via the Weil conjectures). The perverse decomposition theorem generalises this to any proper $f$ with $X$ smooth, including non-smooth $f$ with singular fibres — the singular-fibre contribution is captured by the IC complex multiplicities $m_{i, \alpha}$. Rubric: full credit for the theorem statement, the spectral-sequence statement, and the degeneration argument.

**Exercise 6 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ is Artinian and Noetherian by reducing to the case of a single stratum and using that constructible sheaves on a smooth stratum (locally constant of finite rank) form an abelian category with finite Jordan-Hölder length.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Inductively cut $X$ along strata. On a single smooth stratum, perverse sheaves with constant cohomology dimension reduce to local systems, which are representations of the fundamental group on finite-dimensional vector spaces — these are Artinian and Noetherian by classical representation theory.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Argument by induction on the dimension of $X$ and on the number of strata. Stratify $X = U \sqcup F$ as an open dense $U$ and a closed complement $F$ of strictly smaller dimension, with $U$ smooth and $F$ a union of strata.

**Base case.** $X = U$ smooth and irreducible of dimension $d$. Perverse sheaves on $U$ are concentrated in cohomological degree $-d$ (since the support-dimension condition forces all support to be in dimension $d$, hence degree $-d$). So $\mathrm{Perv}(U) \simeq \mathrm{Loc}(U)$, the category of local systems of finite-dimensional $k$-vector spaces on $U$ (shifted by $d$), which is equivalent to finite-dimensional representations of $\pi_1(U)$. This is an abelian category of finite length: every object has a finite composition series whose simple subquotients are irreducible representations of $\pi_1(U)$, and chains of submodules terminate (descending chain condition = Artinian, ascending chain condition = Noetherian).

**Inductive step.** Given a perverse sheaf $\mathcal{P} \in \mathrm{Perv}(X)$, write $j : U \hookrightarrow X$ for the open inclusion and $i : F \hookrightarrow X$ for the closed complementary inclusion. The recollement
$$
{}^p\!H^0(j_! j^* \mathcal{P}) \to \mathcal{P} \to {}^p\!H^0(i_* i^* \mathcal{P}) \to {}^p\!H^0(j_! j^* \mathcal{P})[1]
$$
in $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ exhibits $\mathcal{P}$ as a finite extension of (i) an object pushed forward from $\mathrm{Perv}(U)$ and (ii) an object pushed forward from $\mathrm{Perv}(F)$. By the base case and the induction hypothesis (on dimension of $F < \dim X$), both $\mathrm{Perv}(U)$ and $\mathrm{Perv}(F)$ have finite Jordan-Hölder length. Composition series of $\mathcal{P}$ are built by interleaving composition series of $j^* \mathcal{P}$ on $U$ and $i^* \mathcal{P}$ on $F$, producing a finite series. Hence every $\mathcal{P}$ has finite Jordan-Hölder length in $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$, and $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ is Artinian and Noetherian.

The simple subquotients are simple perverse sheaves on $U$ pushed forward (intermediate extensions $j_{!*}$ of irreducible local systems on smooth locally closed strata) or simple perverse sheaves on $F$ (recursively decomposed). Combining: every simple of $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ is $\mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L})$ for some $(Z, \mathcal{L})$ pair, recovering the BBD §4.3 classification. Rubric: full credit for the induction setup, the base case, the recollement use, and the conclusion.

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

State and explain the **support theorem** of Ngô 2010 *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 111 §7 for the Hitchin fibration: how the BBD decomposition theorem applied to a proper map of stacks (the Hitchin fibration) constrains which strata can carry simple summands of $Rf_* \mathrm{IC}$.

The support theorem says that for a "good" proper morphism between certain stacks, the supports $\overline{Z_\alpha}$ of the simple summands appearing in the decomposition theorem are forced to be of maximal dimension (the whole base, or specific large-codimension loci), eliminating many a priori possible smaller-dimensional supports.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

For $X$ a smooth complex projective variety, identify the hypercohomology $\mathbb{H}^*(X, \mathrm{IC}_X)$ with the classical singular cohomology $H^*(X, k)$, and explain why this identification fails when $X$ is singular — what is computed instead, and what is its relation to ordinary singular cohomology?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $X$ smooth, $\mathrm{IC}_X = k_X[\dim X]$ is the shifted constant sheaf, so the hypercohomology shifts by $\dim X$. For $X$ singular, $\mathrm{IC}_X$ encodes intersection cohomology — a different invariant.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Smooth case.** For $X$ a smooth complex projective variety of complex dimension $d$, $\mathrm{IC}_X = k_X[d]$. By definition,
$$
\mathbb{H}^n(X, \mathrm{IC}_X) = \mathbb{H}^n(X, k_X[d]) = H^{n+d}(X, k),
$$
the shifted singular cohomology. So $\mathbb{H}^*(X, \mathrm{IC}_X)$ recovers all of $H^*(X, k)$, with a dimensional shift.

**Singular case.** For $X$ a complex projective variety that is **not** smooth, $\mathrm{IC}_X$ is the intersection cohomology complex on the underlying singular space, which is **not** quasi-isomorphic to the shifted constant sheaf. The hypercohomology
$$
IH^n(X, k) := \mathbb{H}^{n - d}(X, \mathrm{IC}_X)
$$
is the **intersection cohomology** of $X$ (with middle perversity, due to Goresky-MacPherson 1980), an invariant distinct from the singular cohomology $H^*(X, k)$. The relation:

- For $X$ smooth, $IH^*(X) = H^*(X, k)$ — the two agree.
- For $X$ singular but rational-homology-manifold (e.g., $X$ has only quotient singularities), $IH^*(X) = H^*(X, k)$ still.
- For $X$ with worse singularities (e.g., a nodal projective variety), $IH^*(X)$ is a proper modification of $H^*(X, k)$. The Poincaré-duality-respecting cohomology theory is $IH^*$, not $H^*$. Classical singular cohomology $H^*(X, k)$ fails Poincaré duality for singular $X$; intersection cohomology restores it via $IH^n(X) \otimes IH^{2d - n}(X) \to k$, a perfect pairing.
- The BBD decomposition theorem applied to a resolution of singularities $\pi : \widetilde{X} \to X$ (proper with $\widetilde{X}$ smooth) gives
$$
R\pi_* k_{\widetilde{X}}[d] \cong \mathrm{IC}_X \oplus \bigoplus_{\alpha} \mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z_\alpha}}(\mathcal{L}_\alpha)[n_\alpha],
$$
expressing $IH^*(X)$ as a canonical summand of $H^*(\widetilde{X})$. The "extra" summands are supported on the singular locus of $X$ and capture the geometry of the resolution.

So $\mathbb{H}^*(X, \mathrm{IC}_X)$ recovers $H^*(X, k)$ for smooth $X$ and intersection cohomology $IH^*(X)$ for singular $X$; the latter is the Poincaré-self-dual cohomology theory that singular cohomology fails to be on a singular space. Rubric: full credit for the smooth identification, the singular intersection-cohomology identification, the Poincaré duality property, and the resolution-of-singularities expression.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (BBD decomposition theorem; Beilinson-Bernstein-Deligne 1982 §6.2.5; Gabber 1981; Saito 1988 in Hodge-theoretic form; de Cataldo-Migliorini 2005 transcendental proof).** *Let $f : X \to Y$ be a proper morphism of complex algebraic varieties with $X$ smooth, and let $k$ be a field of characteristic zero. Then in $D^b_c(Y, k)$:*
$$
Rf_* k_X[\dim X] \cong \bigoplus_{i \in \mathbb{Z}} {}^p\!H^i(Rf_* k_X[\dim X])[-i],
$$
*and each perverse cohomology summand is a semisimple perverse sheaf on $Y$:*
$$
{}^p\!H^i(Rf_* k_X[\dim X]) \cong \bigoplus_\alpha \mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z_\alpha}}(\mathcal{L}_\alpha)^{\oplus m_{i, \alpha}}.
$$

The decomposition theorem is the deepest single statement about proper maps in algebraic geometry. It implies a vast generalisation of the hard Lefschetz theorem (the simple summands of $Rf_*$ admit hard-Lefschetz isomorphisms on each intersection cohomology piece), degeneration of every spectral sequence attached to $f$ (Leray, perverse Leray) at $E_2$, and the topological invariance of intersection cohomology of singular projective varieties under resolution of singularities. The proof has three independent paths: Deligne's Frobenius-weight proof in *Weil II* (1980) lifted to perverse sheaves by BBD §5–§6; Saito's mixed-Hodge-module proof using polarised pure Hodge modules; and de Cataldo-Migliorini's 2005 transcendental Hodge-theoretic proof using the relative hard Lefschetz on cohomology of fibres. The applications are pervasive: geometric Langlands (Beilinson-Bernstein, Mirković-Vilonen), Ngô's Fundamental Lemma, Kazhdan-Lusztig theory, and the topology of Hilbert schemes and moduli spaces.

**Theorem (Riemann-Hilbert correspondence; Kashiwara 1984 *Publ. RIMS* 20; Mebkhout 1980 thesis).** *Let $X$ be a smooth complex algebraic variety or smooth complex analytic manifold. The de Rham functor*
$$
\mathrm{DR} : D^b_{rh}(\mathcal{D}_X) \xrightarrow{\sim} D^b_c(X^{\mathrm{an}}, \mathbb{C}), \quad \mathrm{DR}(\mathcal{M}) := \Omega^\bullet_X[\dim X] \otimes_{\mathcal{D}_X}^L \mathcal{M}
$$
*is a t-exact equivalence of triangulated categories, restricting to an equivalence of abelian categories on hearts: $\mathrm{Mod}_{rh}(\mathcal{D}_X) \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathrm{Perv}(X^{\mathrm{an}}, \mathbb{C})$.*

The Riemann-Hilbert correspondence solves the inverse problem to differential systems with regular singularities: given a constructible system of finite-dimensional $\mathbb{C}$-vector-space data on the analytic topology of $X$ (encoded as a perverse sheaf), it is the de Rham realisation of a unique regular holonomic D-module. The equivalence is functorial under pullback, proper pushforward, tensor product, and Verdier/duality on both sides, compatible with the six-functor formalism. Applications: Beilinson-Bernstein localisation transfers between $\mathfrak{g}$-modules and D-modules on the flag variety, and through Riemann-Hilbert, to perverse sheaves on the flag variety — the bridge from representation theory to geometry that underlies the proof of the Kazhdan-Lusztig conjectures (Beilinson-Bernstein 1981 *C.R. Acad. Sci. Paris* 292; Brylinski-Kashiwara 1981 *Invent. Math.* 64).

**Theorem (classification of simple perverse sheaves; BBD 1982 §4.3).** *Let $X$ be a complex algebraic variety. The set of isomorphism classes of simple objects of $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ is in bijection with the set of pairs $(\overline{Z}, \mathcal{L})$ where $\overline{Z} \subset X$ is an irreducible closed subvariety and $\mathcal{L}$ is an irreducible local system on the smooth locus $Z = \overline{Z}^{\mathrm{reg}}$ (up to isomorphism), via the intermediate-extension construction $(\overline{Z}, \mathcal{L}) \mapsto \mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L})$.*

The classification theorem is the structural backbone of perverse-sheaf theory. Combined with the Artinian + Noetherian property, every perverse sheaf has a finite multiset of $(\overline{Z}_\alpha, \mathcal{L}_\alpha)$ "pieces" appearing in its Jordan-Hölder filtration, and the Grothendieck group $K_0(\mathrm{Perv}(X))$ is generated by these classes. The classification is exploited in geometric representation theory (the simple perverse sheaves on the affine Grassmannian classify the irreducible representations of the Langlands dual group via the geometric Satake equivalence) and in the theory of mixed Hodge modules (each simple perverse sheaf with an appropriate Hodge structure becomes a simple polarised pure Hodge module).

**Theorem (Saito's mixed Hodge modules; Saito 1988 *Publ. RIMS* 24).** *Let $X$ be a complex algebraic variety. There exists an abelian category $\mathrm{MHM}(X)$ of mixed Hodge modules on $X$, fibred over $\mathrm{Perv}(X, \mathbb{Q})$ via a faithful functor $\mathrm{rat} : \mathrm{MHM}(X) \to \mathrm{Perv}(X, \mathbb{Q})$ that forgets the Hodge data. Each object of $\mathrm{MHM}(X)$ carries a polarised mixed Hodge structure on its underlying perverse sheaf, and the six functors $f^*, f_*, f_!, f^!, \otimes, R\mathcal{H}om$ lift from $D^b\mathrm{Perv}(X) \simeq D^b_c(X, \mathbb{Q})$ to $D^b\mathrm{MHM}(X)$ in a compatible way.*

Saito's theory is the Hodge-theoretic refinement of perverse sheaves: every perverse sheaf $\mathcal{F} \in \mathrm{Perv}(X, \mathbb{Q})$ "comes from" a mixed Hodge module $M \in \mathrm{MHM}(X)$ with $\mathrm{rat}(M) = \mathcal{F}$, and the Hodge structure on the cohomology $H^*(X, M)$ refines the rational cohomology. The decomposition theorem in the Hodge-theoretic setting (Saito's version) is sharper: the simple summands of a proper pushforward are not only IC complexes but pure Hodge modules of a specific weight, and the weight filtration is canonical. Applications: Saito's mixed Hodge modules are the input to the recent topological-mirror-symmetry program (Konstevich-Soibelman; Donaldson-Thomas invariants via vanishing cycles of mixed Hodge modules on moduli spaces of sheaves) and to the proof of the BBD decomposition theorem via Hodge theory (de Cataldo-Migliorini 2005, 2009).

**Theorem (de Cataldo-Migliorini Hodge-theoretic decomposition; *Ann. Sci. ÉNS* 38, 2005).** *Let $f : X \to Y$ be a proper morphism of complex algebraic varieties with $X$ smooth. The BBD decomposition theorem holds with the simple summands $\mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z_\alpha}}(\mathcal{L}_\alpha)$ underlying pure Hodge modules of explicit weight, with the decomposition compatible with the polarised Hodge structures on intersection cohomology.*

The de Cataldo-Migliorini proof of the decomposition theorem is the modern transcendental approach: it avoids the spreading-out + characteristic-$p$ + Frobenius machinery of BBD §6 and instead works entirely in the complex Hodge-theoretic setting using the relative hard Lefschetz theorem on cohomology of fibres of $f$ and the polarisation of intersection cohomology. The argument is conceptually cleaner and exposes the geometric mechanism (the relative-hard-Lefschetz semisimplicity input) rather than relying on Deligne's purity. The proof has been generalised to algebraic stacks, to the relative setting, and to the equivariant setting.

**Theorem (geometric Satake equivalence; Lusztig 1983 *Astérisque* 101–102; Ginzburg 1995; Mirković-Vilonen 2007 *Ann. Math.* 166).** *Let $G$ be a connected reductive complex algebraic group and let $\mathrm{Gr}_G = G((t))/G[[t]]$ be the affine Grassmannian of $G$. The category $\mathrm{Perv}_{G[[t]]}(\mathrm{Gr}_G, \mathbb{Q})$ of $G[[t]]$-equivariant perverse sheaves on $\mathrm{Gr}_G$ is a Tannakian category equivalent to the category $\mathrm{Rep}(G^\vee_\mathbb{Q})$ of finite-dimensional rational representations of the Langlands dual group $G^\vee$:*
$$
\mathrm{Perv}_{G[[t]]}(\mathrm{Gr}_G, \mathbb{Q}) \simeq \mathrm{Rep}(G^\vee_\mathbb{Q}).
$$
*The tensor structure on the left comes from the fusion product (convolution of perverse sheaves under the Beilinson-Drinfeld Grassmannian), and the tensor structure on the right is the standard tensor product of representations.*

The geometric Satake equivalence is the foundational input to the geometric Langlands program: it identifies the unramified local Langlands correspondence on the spherical side with a geometric correspondence between perverse sheaves on the affine Grassmannian and representations of the dual group. The proof uses the BBD decomposition theorem applied to the convolution morphism $\mathrm{Gr}_G \times_{G[[t]]} \mathrm{Gr}_G \to \mathrm{Gr}_G$ (a proper map of stratified ind-schemes) to define the fusion product, and the semisimple structure on $\mathrm{Perv}_{G[[t]]}(\mathrm{Gr}_G, \mathbb{Q})$ implied by BBD. The Mirković-Vilonen 2007 paper provides the modern characteristic-free formulation of the equivalence and establishes its functoriality.

**Theorem (Ngô's support theorem and the Fundamental Lemma; Ngô 2010 *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 111).** *The geometric proof of the Fundamental Lemma for Lie algebras uses the BBD decomposition theorem applied to the Hitchin fibration $h : \mathcal{M}_G \to \mathcal{A}_G$ for a reductive group $G$ over a function field. The support theorem (§7.1.13) constrains the simple perverse-sheaf summands of $Rh_* \overline{\mathbb{Q}}_\ell$ over the elliptic regular open of the Hitchin base to be of maximal support, which together with the abelian-variety symmetry of the Hitchin fibres produces the trace-formula identities equivalent to the Fundamental Lemma.*

Ngô's 2010 proof is the deepest single application of the BBD decomposition theorem to date. The Fundamental Lemma, conjectured by Langlands-Shelstad in 1979 and a longstanding obstacle to the trace-formula-based attack on the Langlands program, is proved via the geometric mechanism of the Hitchin fibration's perverse-sheaf decomposition. The proof uses the support theorem (a refinement of BBD applicable in the abelian-fibred setting), the Goresky-Kottwitz-MacPherson endoscopic stratification of the Hitchin base, and the relative Lefschetz hard-Lefschetz theorem for Hitchin fibres. Ngô received the Fields Medal in 2010 for this work.

**Synthesis.** The perverse-sheaf framework builds toward an abelian-category understanding of constructible cohomology on singular and stratified spaces, and the foundational reason it works is that the middle-perversity t-structure combines the support-dimension condition with Verdier duality to produce an abelian heart whose simple objects are exactly the intersection cohomology complexes attached to (stratum, local system) pairs. The package is dual to itself under Verdier duality, with proper pushforward respecting the t-structure (the BBD decomposition theorem) and the open-closed recollement providing the inductive structure. The central insight is that perverse sheaves are the canonical abelian category for studying singular varieties — they restore Poincaré duality through intersection cohomology, support the decomposition theorem for proper maps, classify their simples through stratified local systems, and bridge to D-modules via Riemann-Hilbert and to Hodge theory via Saito's mixed Hodge modules.

The formalism appears again in [04.03.18] (t-structures and hearts), where the middle-perversity t-structure is the canonical non-standard example; in [04.03.16] (six-functor formalism), where Verdier duality and proper pushforward interact with the perverse t-structure to produce the decomposition theorem; in [03.04.14] (hypercohomology of complexes of sheaves), where $\mathbb{H}^*(X, \mathrm{IC}_X)$ computes intersection cohomology of singular projective $X$; and in [04.03.02] (local systems), where the data of an irreducible local system on a smooth locally closed subvariety is exactly half of the input to a simple perverse sheaf. The recursion stabilises: every perverse sheaf decomposes into intermediate extensions of local systems on strata, every proper morphism's derived pushforward decomposes into shifted intermediate extensions on the base, and the resulting framework is the abelian-category-level shadow of derived algebraic geometry on singular spaces.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (abelianness of $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$).** *$\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ is an abelian category.*

**Proof.** By the BBD §1.3.6 theorem [04.03.18 Key Theorem], the heart of any t-structure on a triangulated category is abelian. The middle-perversity pair $({}^p\!D^{\le 0}, {}^p\!D^{\ge 0})$ is a t-structure on $D^b_c(X)$ (the BBD axioms TR1–TR3 of [04.03.18] are verified directly: shift stability follows from the dimension-bound being shifted by $1$, orthogonality follows from Verdier duality and the dimension inequality, and the truncation triangle is constructed via the standard truncation-and-glue argument along strata using the open-closed recollement). Hence $\mathrm{Perv}(X) = {}^p\!D^{\le 0} \cap {}^p\!D^{\ge 0}$ is the heart of a t-structure on the triangulated category $D^b_c(X)$, and therefore abelian. The short exact sequences in $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ correspond bijectively to distinguished triangles in $D^b_c(X)$ all three of whose terms lie in $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$. $\square$

**Proposition (Artinian + Noetherian property).** *Every object of $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ has a finite Jordan-Hölder composition series whose simple subquotients are intersection cohomology complexes.*

**Proof.** Argument by induction on the dimension of $X$ and on the number of strata, using the recollement triangle for an open-closed decomposition $X = U \sqcup F$ with $U$ smooth open dense and $F$ closed of smaller dimension (Exercise 6 above). The base case $X$ smooth and irreducible reduces $\mathrm{Perv}(X) \simeq \mathrm{Loc}(X)$ to the category of finite-dimensional $\pi_1(X)$-representations, which is Artinian and Noetherian by classical representation theory. The inductive step pulls in the recollement to extend composition series across the open-closed cut. Combining all simple subquotients across the stratification produces the classification: every simple of $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ is $\mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L})$ for $(Z, \mathcal{L})$ a smooth locally closed irreducible subvariety with an irreducible local system. $\square$

**Proposition (Verdier duality on $\mathrm{IC}$).** *For $Z$ smooth locally closed irreducible in $X$ of dimension $d_Z$ with $j : Z \hookrightarrow X$ and $\mathcal{L}$ an irreducible local system on $Z$,*
$$
\mathbb{D}_X(\mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L})) \cong \mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L}^\vee),
$$
*where $\mathcal{L}^\vee = \mathcal{H}om(\mathcal{L}, k_Z)$ is the dual local system.*

**Proof.** The dualising complex on $Z$ smooth of complex dimension $d_Z$ is $\omega^\bullet_Z = k_Z[2 d_Z]$ (with the same convention on $X$ via the structure map, since $X$ is irreducible). The Verdier dual of the shifted local system $\mathcal{L}[d_Z]$ on $Z$ is
$$
\mathbb{D}_Z(\mathcal{L}[d_Z]) = R\mathcal{H}om(\mathcal{L}[d_Z], \omega^\bullet_Z) = R\mathcal{H}om(\mathcal{L}[d_Z], k_Z[2 d_Z]) = \mathcal{H}om(\mathcal{L}, k_Z)[d_Z] = \mathcal{L}^\vee[d_Z].
$$
By the duality interchange $\mathbb{D}_X \circ j_{!*} = j_{!*} \circ \mathbb{D}_Z$ on the heart of the perverse t-structure (a consequence of $\mathbb{D}$ being t-exact for the perverse t-structure and the duality between $j_!$ and $j_*$ via Verdier), we have
$$
\mathbb{D}_X(\mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L})) = \mathbb{D}_X(j_{!*}(\mathcal{L}[d_Z])) = j_{!*}(\mathbb{D}_Z(\mathcal{L}[d_Z])) = j_{!*}(\mathcal{L}^\vee[d_Z]) = \mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L}^\vee).
$$
For $\mathcal{L}$ self-dual (e.g., $\mathcal{L} = k_Z$ the constant local system, or $\mathcal{L}$ equipped with a non-degenerate self-pairing), this gives $\mathbb{D}_X(\mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L})) = \mathrm{IC}_{\overline{Z}}(\mathcal{L})$ — the IC complex is self-Verdier-dual. $\square$

**Proposition (intermediate extension as image in $\mathrm{Perv}$).** *Let $j : Z \hookrightarrow X$ be a locally closed embedding with $Z$ smooth irreducible of dimension $d_Z$, and let $\mathcal{L}$ be an irreducible local system on $Z$. The intermediate extension $j_{!*}(\mathcal{L}[d_Z])$ is characterised as the unique perverse extension $\mathcal{P} \in \mathrm{Perv}(X)$ with $j^* \mathcal{P} = \mathcal{L}[d_Z]$ and no non-zero perverse sub-object or perverse quotient supported on $\overline{Z} \setminus Z$.*

**Proof.** Consider the natural morphism $\alpha : {}^p\!H^0(j_! \mathcal{L}[d_Z]) \to {}^p\!H^0(j_* \mathcal{L}[d_Z])$ induced by the adjunction $j_! \to j_*$ on $D^b_c(X)$ and the perverse-cohomology functor. The image of $\alpha$ in the abelian category $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$ is by definition $j_{!*}(\mathcal{L}[d_Z])$.

**Uniqueness.** Suppose $\mathcal{P} \in \mathrm{Perv}(X)$ satisfies $j^* \mathcal{P} = \mathcal{L}[d_Z]$ and has no non-zero perverse sub-object or quotient supported on $\overline{Z} \setminus Z$. Restricting the recollement triangle $i_! i^! \mathcal{P} \to \mathcal{P} \to Rj_* j^* \mathcal{P}$ to the perverse t-structure (where $i : \overline{Z} \setminus Z \hookrightarrow X$ is the closed complement), the perverse cohomology of $i_! i^! \mathcal{P}$ is supported on $\overline{Z} \setminus Z$ and must vanish by hypothesis. So $\mathcal{P} \xrightarrow{\sim} {}^p\!H^0(Rj_* j^* \mathcal{P}) = {}^p\!H^0(j_* \mathcal{L}[d_Z])$ at the perverse-cohomology level. Dually, $\mathcal{P}$ is a quotient of ${}^p\!H^0(j_! j^* \mathcal{P}) = {}^p\!H^0(j_! \mathcal{L}[d_Z])$. Together, $\mathcal{P}$ is canonically the image of $\alpha$, which is $j_{!*}(\mathcal{L}[d_Z])$. Hence the characterisation is unique.

**Existence.** $j_{!*}(\mathcal{L}[d_Z]) = \mathrm{im}(\alpha)$ has no non-zero perverse sub-object supported on $\overline{Z} \setminus Z$ because such a sub-object would have to come from the kernel of $\alpha$, which is forced to be zero by the construction (kernel of the natural map ${}^p\!H^0(j_!) \to {}^p\!H^0(j_*)$ is supported on the boundary by adjunction, and the image construction quotients by it). Dually for non-zero perverse quotients. Hence $j_{!*}(\mathcal{L}[d_Z])$ satisfies the universal property. $\square$

**Proposition (Beilinson reconstruction).** *Let $X$ be a complex algebraic variety. The natural functor*
$$
\mathrm{real} : D^b(\mathrm{Perv}(X)) \to D^b_c(X)
$$
*is a triangulated equivalence (Beilinson 1987 *Funct. Anal. Appl.* 21; BBD 1982 §3.1).*

**Proof sketch.** The functor sends a complex of perverse sheaves to its associated constructible complex via the standard t-structure on $D^b(\mathrm{Perv}(X))$ matched with the middle-perversity t-structure on $D^b_c(X)$. Fully faithfulness is the equality $\mathrm{Hom}_{D^b\mathrm{Perv}}(\mathcal{P}, \mathcal{Q}[n]) = \mathrm{Hom}_{D^b_c}(\mathcal{P}, \mathcal{Q}[n])$ for $\mathcal{P}, \mathcal{Q} \in \mathrm{Perv}(X)$, which holds because both Hom-groups are computed by the same perverse-cohomology long-exact sequence. Essential surjectivity uses the boundedness of $D^b_c(X)$ in the perverse t-structure (every constructible complex has finitely many non-zero perverse-cohomology sheaves) combined with the construction of any $\mathcal{F} \in D^b_c(X)$ from its perverse-cohomology spectral sequence ${}^p\!H^p({}^p\!\tau_{\ge q} \mathcal{F}) \Rightarrow {}^p\!H^{p + q}(\mathcal{F})$, which converges by Artin vanishing on algebraic varieties. The key Artin vanishing input: for any constructible $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$ algebraic of dimension $d$, ${}^p\!H^i(\mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $|i| > d$, bounding the perverse cohomology. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **t-Structure on a triangulated category [04.03.18].** The perverse t-structure is the canonical non-standard example of a t-structure, defined by a support-dimension inequality and its Verdier dual. Its heart is the abelian category $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$, providing the abelian-category-level extraction of constructible cohomological information. The BBD framework studied here originated as the application that drove the development of the t-structure axiomatisation.

- **Six-functor formalism [04.03.16].** The perverse t-structure interacts with the six operations in a controlled way: $j^*$ is t-exact for the perverse t-structure (open inclusion), $i_!$ and $i^*$ are right t-exact (closed inclusion), $i_*$ and $i^!$ are left t-exact, $Rf_!$ is right t-exact for arbitrary $f$, and $Rf_*$ is left t-exact. Verdier duality $\mathbb{D}$ is t-exact and interchanges $f^*$ with $f^!$ and $f_!$ with $f_*$ at the level of perverse t-structures.

- **Local systems [04.03.02].** Local systems are the building blocks of perverse sheaves through the simple classification: every simple perverse sheaf is the intermediate extension of an irreducible local system on a smooth locally closed stratum. The data $(Z, \mathcal{L})$ is the "perverse signature" of a simple, and the assembly of simples by intermediate extension produces the full abelian category $\mathrm{Perv}(X)$.

- **Hypercohomology of a complex of sheaves [03.04.14].** The intersection cohomology $IH^*(X)$ of a singular projective variety $X$ is the hypercohomology $\mathbb{H}^{* - d}(X, \mathrm{IC}_X)$. The hypercohomology spectral sequence on $\mathrm{IC}_X$ computes $IH^*$ from local data, and the BBD decomposition theorem for a resolution of singularities $\widetilde{X} \to X$ expresses $IH^*(X)$ as a canonical direct summand of $H^*(\widetilde{X})$.

- **Sheaf cohomology — Leray spectral sequence general form [04.03.15].** The perverse Leray spectral sequence ${}^p\!H^p(Y, {}^p\!H^q(Rf_*\mathcal{F})) \Rightarrow H^{p+q}(X, \mathcal{F})$ degenerates at $E_2$ for proper $f$ with smooth $X$ by the BBD decomposition theorem. This is the geometric refinement of the classical Leray spectral sequence, with each term in the abelian category $\mathrm{Perv}(Y)$ rather than $\mathrm{Sh}(Y)$, organising the cohomological information of proper maps in the perverse-sheaf framework.

- **Derived category $D(\mathcal{A})$ [04.03.11].** Beilinson's reconstruction theorem states $D^b(\mathrm{Perv}(X)) \simeq D^b_c(X)$ — the bounded derived category of the perverse heart recovers the original constructible derived category. This unusual reconstruction property is one of the few instances where the heart of a t-structure determines the triangulated category, and is the technical input to many representation-theoretic applications.

- **Triangulated category — Verdier axioms TR1-TR4 [04.03.10].** The perverse t-structure sits on top of the triangulated structure of $D^b_c(X)$; the BBD axioms for a t-structure are extra data beyond the Verdier axioms TR1-TR4, and the perverse cohomology functors ${}^p\!H^n$ depend on the distinguished-triangle behaviour for the long-exact-sequence property.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The notion of perverse sheaves emerged from the confluence of three independent developments in the late 1970s and early 1980s. The first was Mark Goresky and Robert MacPherson's introduction of intersection homology in 1980 ("Intersection homology theory," *Topology* 19, 135–162) [ref: TODO_REF] and its sheaf-theoretic refinement in 1983 ("Intersection homology II," *Invent. Math.* 71/72, 77–129 + 169–215) [ref: TODO_REF], which provided a Poincaré-self-dual cohomology theory for singular projective varieties. The second was Masaki Kashiwara's and Zoghman Mebkhout's independent solutions to the Riemann-Hilbert problem in 1980–1984 — Mebkhout 1980 thesis at Paris VII [ref: TODO_REF] and Kashiwara 1984 *Publ. RIMS Kyoto Univ.* 20 [ref: TODO_REF] — establishing the equivalence between regular holonomic D-modules on a smooth complex variety and constructible sheaves on the analytic space via the de Rham functor. The third was Alexander Beilinson, Joseph Bernstein, and Pierre Deligne's axiomatisation of perverse sheaves and t-structures in *Faisceaux pervers* (*Astérisque* 100, Société Mathématique de France 1982) [ref: TODO_REF], which unified the previous developments into a coherent abelian-category framework and proved the BBD decomposition theorem as the deepest result of the new theory.

The BBD authors recognised that the support-dimension condition appearing in Goresky-MacPherson's intersection-homology construction and the regularity condition appearing in Kashiwara-Mebkhout's D-module theory could both be packaged as the heart of a single t-structure on the constructible derived category $D^b_c(X)$, with the simples of the heart being the intersection cohomology complexes. The middle perversity is the choice of t-structure that produces self-dual cohomology under Verdier duality, equivalently the unique perversity for which the IC complex of a smooth variety is the shifted constant sheaf. The decomposition theorem (BBD §6.2.5; independently announced by Ofer Gabber in a 1981 lecture series) was proved by lifting Deligne's Frobenius purity theorem (*La conjecture de Weil II*, *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 52, 1980) [ref: TODO_REF] from étale cohomology of finite-field varieties to perverse sheaves, and transferring the result back to characteristic zero via spreading-out arguments. Morihiko Saito's 1988 *Modules de Hodge polarisables* (*Publ. RIMS Kyoto Univ.* 24, 849–995) [ref: TODO_REF] supplied the Hodge-theoretic refinement: each perverse sheaf with a Hodge structure assembles into a mixed Hodge module, and the decomposition theorem holds at the Hodge-module level.

The reconstruction theme — recovering the constructible derived category from its perverse heart — was due to Beilinson (1987 *Funct. Anal. Appl.* 21) [ref: TODO_REF] in announcement form and BBD §3.1 in full proof. The equivalence $D^b(\mathrm{Perv}(X)) \simeq D^b_c(X)$ exhibits the unusual feature that the heart of the perverse t-structure determines the original triangulated category up to triangulated equivalence — a property that fails for most t-structures and that depends on the Artin-vanishing properties of constructible sheaves on algebraic varieties. The transcendental Hodge-theoretic proof of the BBD decomposition theorem was given by Mark de Cataldo and Luca Migliorini in 2005 ("The Hodge theory of algebraic maps," *Ann. Sci. ÉNS* 38, 693–750) using the relative hard Lefschetz theorem on cohomology of fibres, and surveyed for non-specialists in their 2009 *Bulletin of the AMS* 46 article "The decomposition theorem, perverse sheaves and the topology of algebraic maps" [ref: TODO_REF].

The applications of perverse sheaves to representation theory and the Langlands program have been pervasive. Beilinson-Bernstein's 1981 *C.R. Acad. Sci. Paris* 292 [ref: TODO_REF] localised $\mathfrak{g}$-modules of a complex reductive Lie algebra to D-modules on the flag variety, which through Riemann-Hilbert become perverse sheaves; the Kazhdan-Lusztig conjectures relating irreducible character values to Kazhdan-Lusztig polynomials of the Weyl group were proved by Beilinson-Bernstein (1981) and independently by Brylinski-Kashiwara (1981 *Invent. Math.* 64) using this perverse-sheaf machinery. George Lusztig's 1983 *Astérisque* 101–102 and Mirković-Vilonen's 2007 *Ann. Math.* 166 established the geometric Satake equivalence, identifying perverse sheaves on the affine Grassmannian of a reductive group $G$ with representations of the Langlands dual group $G^\vee$. Bao Châu Ngô's 2010 *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 111 [ref: TODO_REF] used the BBD decomposition theorem applied to the Hitchin fibration to prove the Fundamental Lemma of Langlands-Shelstad, completing a programme initiated in the 1970s and earning Ngô the 2010 Fields Medal.
