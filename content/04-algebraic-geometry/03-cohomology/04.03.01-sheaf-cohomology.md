## Intuition [Beginner]

*Sheaf cohomology* measures the global obstructions to assembling local data into global objects. A sheaf gives consistent local data; if you can always glue locally-compatible data into global sections, the sheaf has zero cohomology in degree 1 and above. When gluing fails — when locally-compatible data exists but no global section assembles — you have a nonzero cohomology class.

The simplest example: on the punctured plane $\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{0\}$, the sheaf of "potential functions for the angular 1-form $d\theta$" has local sections everywhere (locally, $\theta$ exists) but no global section (the angle $\theta$ wraps around the origin, so it can't be a global function). This is a nonzero class in $H^1$.

Sheaf cohomology unifies de Rham cohomology, singular cohomology, group cohomology, and Galois cohomology into a single framework. It is the central computational tool of algebraic geometry, where computing cohomology of coherent sheaves on a scheme drives Riemann-Roch, Hodge theory, and much more.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The classic example: the *Mittag-Leffler problem* on a Riemann surface. Given specified poles and Laurent tails on a Riemann surface, when can you find a global meromorphic function with exactly that prescribed singular behaviour?

For genus 0 (the sphere): always possible — no obstruction. For higher genus: there are obstructions, captured by $H^1$ of an appropriate sheaf. This higher-genus obstruction is exactly the Riemann-Roch defect.

A concrete numerical example: on the genus-1 torus, you cannot have a meromorphic function with a single simple pole — Riemann-Roch / cohomology says the obstruction lives in $H^1(\text{torus}, \mathcal{O})$, which is nonzero (one-dimensional).

The sheaf cohomology framework makes such obstructions computable through systematic methods (Čech, derived functors, spectral sequences).

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a topological space [02.01.01] and $\mathcal{F}$ a sheaf of abelian groups on $X$ [04.01.01]. The **sheaf cohomology groups** $H^i(X; \mathcal{F})$ for $i \geq 0$ are defined as the *right-derived functors* of the global sections functor $\Gamma(X, -) : \mathbf{Sh}_{\mathbf{Ab}}(X) \to \mathbf{Ab}$:

$$
H^i(X; \mathcal{F}) := R^i \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}).
$$

Concretely: choose an injective resolution $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{I}^\bullet$ in $\mathbf{Sh}_{\mathbf{Ab}}(X)$, apply $\Gamma$ to get the cochain complex $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{I}^\bullet)$, and take cohomology:

$$
H^i(X; \mathcal{F}) := H^i\big(\Gamma(X, \mathcal{I}^\bullet)\big) = \frac{\ker(d^i)}{\mathrm{im}(d^{i-1})}.
$$

The sheaf-cohomology groups are independent of the choice of injective resolution (standard derived-functor argument).

**Properties:**

- $H^0(X; \mathcal{F}) = \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}) = \mathcal{F}(X)$.
- $H^i(X; -)$ is a covariant functor for each $i$.
- A short exact sequence of sheaves $0 \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G} \to \mathcal{H} \to 0$ induces a *long exact sequence in cohomology*:

$$
0 \to H^0(\mathcal{F}) \to H^0(\mathcal{G}) \to H^0(\mathcal{H}) \to H^1(\mathcal{F}) \to H^1(\mathcal{G}) \to H^1(\mathcal{H}) \to H^2(\mathcal{F}) \to \cdots
$$

This is the central computational tool: short exact sequences of sheaves give long exact sequences in cohomology.

**Čech cohomology.** A more concrete definition for "nice" sheaves: for an open cover $\mathcal{U} = \{U_i\}$ of $X$, the **Čech complex** of $\mathcal{F}$ with respect to $\mathcal{U}$ is

$$
\check{C}^p(\mathcal{U}; \mathcal{F}) := \prod_{i_0 < i_1 < \cdots < i_p} \mathcal{F}(U_{i_0} \cap \cdots \cap U_{i_p})
$$

with **Čech differential** $\delta^p : \check{C}^p \to \check{C}^{p+1}$ the alternating-sum-of-restriction map. The *Čech cohomology* $\check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}; \mathcal{F})$ is the cohomology of this complex; *Čech cohomology of $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$* is the colimit over refining covers, $\check{H}^p(X; \mathcal{F})$.

### Čech vs de Rham differentials — typographic discipline

When sheaf cohomology is computed via Čech cohomology of a covering and the sheaf in question is itself a complex of sheaves (e.g., the de Rham complex $\Omega^\bullet$), two different differentials appear: the Čech differential $\delta$ on $\check{C}^p$, and the internal differential of the sheaf complex (here the exterior derivative $d$ on $\Omega^q$). Bott-Tu §8 fixes this typographic discipline:

- $\delta$ for the Čech differential — alternating-sum restriction on $\check{C}^*$.
- $d$ for the exterior derivative on $\Omega^*$.

The total differential of the Čech-de Rham double complex is $D = d + (-1)^p \delta$ on bidegree $(p, q)$, with sign convention chosen so that $D^2 = 0$. Codex pre-Pass-4 collapsed both into a generic $d^p$; the present unit (D2) and Babel Bible's Čech-de Rham double-complex unit [03.04.11] adopt the $\delta$ vs $d$ convention throughout, both because it is Bott-Tu's originator-text discipline and because it is load-bearing for the tic-tac-toe pedagogy of §9.

**Comparison.** For "nice" $X$ (paracompact Hausdorff) and any sheaf $\mathcal{F}$, $\check{H}^p(X; \mathcal{F}) \cong H^p(X; \mathcal{F})$. So Čech cohomology computes derived-functor sheaf cohomology in the cases of interest.

**Acyclic sheaves.** A sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ is *acyclic* if $H^i(X; \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $i > 0$. Important examples: flabby sheaves (every section extends globally), soft sheaves, fine sheaves (admit partitions of unity). On smooth manifolds, sheaves of $C^\infty$-functions and their tensor-power variants are fine; this implies de Rham cohomology computes singular cohomology.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Long exact sequence in sheaf cohomology).** *Given a short exact sequence of sheaves of abelian groups $0 \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G} \to \mathcal{H} \to 0$ on a topological space $X$, there is a long exact sequence*

$$
0 \to H^0(X; \mathcal{F}) \to H^0(X; \mathcal{G}) \to H^0(X; \mathcal{H}) \xrightarrow{\delta} H^1(X; \mathcal{F}) \to H^1(X; \mathcal{G}) \to \cdots
$$

**Proof sketch.** This is the standard derived-functor long exact sequence applied to $\Gamma$.

The global sections functor $\Gamma$ is *left exact*: if $0 \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G} \to \mathcal{H} \to 0$ is short exact, then $0 \to \Gamma(\mathcal{F}) \to \Gamma(\mathcal{G}) \to \Gamma(\mathcal{H})$ is exact (but the rightmost map need not be surjective).

To extend to a long exact sequence: pick injective resolutions $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{I}^\bullet$ and $\mathcal{H} \to \mathcal{J}^\bullet$. The horseshoe lemma constructs an injective resolution $\mathcal{G} \to \mathcal{K}^\bullet$ fitting into a short exact sequence of complexes $0 \to \mathcal{I}^\bullet \to \mathcal{K}^\bullet \to \mathcal{J}^\bullet \to 0$. Apply $\Gamma$: a short exact sequence of complexes of abelian groups (since each $\mathcal{I}^p, \mathcal{K}^p, \mathcal{J}^p$ is injective and $\Gamma$ preserves products of injectives in each degree). The snake lemma then produces the long exact sequence connecting cohomologies. $\square$

The long exact sequence is the workhorse of sheaf-cohomology computation: it converts a "vertical" exact sequence of sheaves into a "horizontal" exact sequence of cohomology groups, allowing iterative computation by attacking simpler sheaves.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [04.04.01] (riemann-roch theorem for curves), where the same data is developed in the next layer of the strand. The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Show that $H^0(X; \mathcal{F}) = \mathcal{F}(X)$ for any sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

This is the definition of $R^0 \Gamma$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The 0-th right-derived functor of any left-exact functor on its domain equals the functor itself: $R^0 \Gamma(\mathcal{F}) = \Gamma(\mathcal{F}) = \mathcal{F}(X)$. This is the start of the resolution: $\mathcal{F} = \ker(\mathcal{I}^0 \to \mathcal{I}^1)$ in the injective resolution, and $\Gamma$ commutes with kernels of left-exact functors. So $H^0(X; \mathcal{F}) = \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})$.

</details>

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

What is $H^0(\mathrm{pt}; \mathcal{F})$ for a sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on a one-point space?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A sheaf on a point is just an abelian group.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A sheaf on $\mathrm{pt}$ is determined by its value on the unique non-empty open set, $\mathcal{F}(\mathrm{pt})$. So $H^0(\mathrm{pt}; \mathcal{F}) = \mathcal{F}(\mathrm{pt})$. Higher cohomology $H^i(\mathrm{pt}; \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $i \geq 1$ — every higher cohomology of a point vanishes.

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that for the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}$ on $S^1$, $H^0(S^1; \underline{\mathbb{Z}}) = \mathbb{Z}$ and $H^1(S^1; \underline{\mathbb{Z}}) = \mathbb{Z}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Constant-sheaf cohomology equals singular cohomology with constant coefficients.

For nice spaces, constant-sheaf cohomology equals singular cohomology: $H^i(X; \underline{A}) \cong H^i_{\mathrm{sing}}(X; A)$.

For $S^1$: $H^0_{\mathrm{sing}}(S^1; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}$ (constant functions are integer-valued and connected components count) and $H^1_{\mathrm{sing}}(S^1; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}$ (winding number, generated by the angular form's class).

So $H^0(S^1; \underline{\mathbb{Z}}) = \mathbb{Z}$ and $H^1(S^1; \underline{\mathbb{Z}}) = \mathbb{Z}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

For a sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$, suppose every open cover has a refinement to a *good cover* (every finite intersection is contractible, or appropriately acyclic). Show that Čech cohomology computes sheaf cohomology.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Čech-to-derived-functor spectral sequence collapses when intersections are acyclic.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a good cover $\mathcal{U}$, the higher cohomology of every finite intersection $U_{i_0} \cap \cdots \cap U_{i_p}$ vanishes. The *Čech-to-derived-functor spectral sequence* has $E_2^{p, q} = \check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}; \mathcal{H}^q(\mathcal{F}))$ where $\mathcal{H}^q(\mathcal{F})$ is the presheaf $U \mapsto H^q(U; \mathcal{F})$. By the good-cover hypothesis, $\mathcal{H}^q(\mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $q > 0$ on all finite intersections. So the spectral sequence has $E_2^{p, 0} = \check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}; \mathcal{F})$ and zero elsewhere; it collapses to give $\check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}; \mathcal{F}) = H^p(X; \mathcal{F})$. Refining to good covers takes the colimit and identifies Čech cohomology with sheaf cohomology.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why does the de Rham cohomology of a smooth manifold compute sheaf cohomology of the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the fine resolution by $\Omega^k$ sheaves and the Poincaré lemma.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$ has a *fine resolution* by sheaves of differential forms:

$$
0 \to \underline{\mathbb{R}} \to \Omega^0 \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^1 \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^2 \xrightarrow{d} \cdots
$$

This is exact at each stalk by the Poincaré lemma (locally, every closed form is exact). The sheaves $\Omega^k$ are *fine* (admit partitions of unity), hence *acyclic*. So the fine resolution computes the sheaf cohomology of $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$ via global sections of $\Omega^\bullet$:

$$
H^p(M; \underline{\mathbb{R}}) = H^p(\Gamma(M, \Omega^\bullet)) = H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(M).
$$

This is *de Rham's theorem*: de Rham cohomology computes constant-coefficient sheaf cohomology. By the comparison with singular cohomology, $H^p_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \cong H^p_{\mathrm{sing}}(M; \mathbb{R})$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the Čech cohomology of the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$ on $S^1$ via the two-arc cover, and verify it agrees with $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(S^1)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Cover $S^1$ with two slightly-overlapping open arcs $U, V$. Each $U, V$ is contractible, $U\cap V$ has two contractible components.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take $\mathcal{U} = \{U, V\}$ with $U, V$ open arcs covering $S^1$ and $U\cap V = A\sqcup B$ a disjoint union of two contractible components. The Čech complex of $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$ is

\check{C}^0 = \underline{\mathbb{R}}(U)\oplus \underline{\mathbb{R}}(V) = \mathbb{R}\oplus \mathbb{R},\quad \check{C}^1 = \underline{\mathbb{R}}(U\cap V) = \mathbb{R}\oplus \mathbb{R},

with Čech differential $\delta(a, b) = (b - a, b - a)\in \mathbb{R}_A\oplus \mathbb{R}_B$ (the alternating-sum-of-restriction sends each pair $(a, b)$ on $(U, V)$ to its difference, repeated on each connected component of $U\cap V$).

- $\ker\delta = \{(a, a)\} \cong \mathbb{R}$, the constants — so $\check{H}^0(\mathcal{U}; \underline{\mathbb{R}}) = \mathbb{R}$.
- $\mathrm{im}\,\delta = \{(c, c) : c\in \mathbb{R}\}\subseteq \mathbb{R}\oplus \mathbb{R}$, so $\check{H}^1 = (\mathbb{R}\oplus \mathbb{R})/\{(c, c)\} \cong \mathbb{R}$.

This matches $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(S^1) = (\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{R})$ in degrees zero and one.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the Čech-de Rham double-complex cohomology of $S^2$ via the two-disk cover and identify cocycle representatives in degree two.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Cover $S^2$ with two open disks $U, V$ overlapping in an annulus $U\cap V$ (homotopy equivalent to $S^1$). The Čech-de Rham double complex has bidegrees $(p, q)$ with $p\in\{0, 1\}$ (since $\mathcal{U} = \{U, V\}$).

{}_I E_1 = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbb{R}\oplus \mathbb{R} & \mathbb{R} \\ 0 & \mathbb{R} \\ 0 & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots \end{pmatrix},

with the $\delta$ map on the bottom row $\mathbb{R}\oplus \mathbb{R}\to \mathbb{R}$ sending $(a, b)\mapsto a - b$ (kernel $\mathbb{R}$, image $\mathbb{R}$).

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).**

State Serre's theorem: on an affine scheme, all higher cohomology of quasi-coherent sheaves vanishes.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Quasi-coherent sheaves on $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ correspond to $R$-modules; higher cohomology vanishes on affine schemes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Serre's theorem.** Let $X = \mathrm{Spec}(R)$ be an affine scheme and $\mathcal{F}$ a quasi-coherent sheaf on $X$ corresponding to $R$-module $M$. Then $H^i(X; \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for all $i \geq 1$.

In particular: a short exact sequence of quasi-coherent sheaves on an affine scheme is short exact in global sections (no first-cohomology obstruction). The proof reduces to the corresponding statement on $R$-modules: every short exact sequence $0 \to M_1 \to M_2 \to M_3 \to 0$ of $R$-modules is exact in global sections (taking global sections is a left-exact functor, and the right-derived functors vanish on affine schemes by Serre's theorem).

This is the main reason affine schemes are computationally tractable: quasi-coherent cohomology vanishes. On a *projective* scheme, the analogous statement is the *Serre formula* for cohomology of line bundles, which is also computable.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, conceptual).**

State the relationship between sheaf cohomology, étale cohomology, and singular cohomology for a complex algebraic variety.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Comparison theorems link these in the analytic / algebraic settings.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a complex algebraic variety $X$ (proper smooth):

- **Sheaf cohomology of constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{C}}$ in the analytic topology** equals **singular cohomology** $H^i_{\mathrm{sing}}(X^{\mathrm{an}}; \mathbb{C})$ (de Rham + comparison theorems).
- **Coherent sheaf cohomology** $H^i(X; \mathcal{O}_X)$ in the Zariski topology computes the algebraic cohomology, agreeing with the Hodge component $H^{0, i}$ of the analytic cohomology by *Serre's GAGA*.
- **Étale cohomology** $H^i_{\mathrm{ét}}(X; \mathbb{Z}/\ell)$ for $\ell$ coprime to characteristic recovers singular cohomology mod $\ell$ via the étale-singular comparison (proved by Artin-Grothendieck, refined by Deligne).

These three perspectives (analytic / algebraic / arithmetic) give different computations but agree on the underlying invariant. The bridge between them is the deep content of Hodge theory and the Weil conjectures.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (Leray on good covers).** *Let $X$ be a paracompact Hausdorff space and $\mathcal{F}$ a sheaf of abelian groups on $X$. If $\mathcal{U} = \{U_i\}$ is an open cover such that every finite intersection $U_{i_0}\cap\cdots\cap U_{i_p}$ is $\mathcal{F}$-acyclic — meaning $H^q(U_{i_0}\cap\cdots\cap U_{i_p}; \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for all $q\geq 1$ — then the canonical map*

$$
\check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}; \mathcal{F})\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;} H^p(X; \mathcal{F})
$$

*is an isomorphism for every $p\geq 0$.*

**Proof sketch.** The Čech-to-derived-functor spectral sequence has $E_2^{p, q} = \check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}; \mathcal{H}^q(\mathcal{F}))$, where $\mathcal{H}^q(\mathcal{F})$ is the presheaf $U\mapsto H^q(U; \mathcal{F})$. The acyclic-cover hypothesis forces $\mathcal{H}^q(\mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $q\geq 1$ on every finite intersection, so the spectral sequence collapses on the $q = 0$ row, yielding $\check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}; \mathcal{F}) = H^p(X; \mathcal{F})$.

This is the **Leray cover theorem**: when intersections are acyclic, Čech computes derived-functor cohomology *on a single cover*, no refinement needed. The corollary on smooth manifolds: a good cover (every finite intersection contractible) is acyclic for the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$, so de Rham cohomology and Čech cohomology of $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$ on a good cover coincide.

**Grothendieck's vanishing theorem.** For a Noetherian topological space $X$ of dimension $n$ and any sheaf of abelian groups $\mathcal{F}$, $H^i(X; \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $i > n$.

**Leray spectral sequence.** For a continuous map $f : X \to Y$ and a sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$, there is a spectral sequence $E_2^{p, q} = H^p(Y; R^q f_* \mathcal{F}) \Rightarrow H^{p+q}(X; \mathcal{F})$. This decomposes the cohomology of $X$ in terms of cohomology on $Y$ with coefficients in the higher direct images.

**Hodge decomposition.** For a compact Kähler manifold (in particular, a smooth complex projective variety) $X$, the singular cohomology decomposes:

$$
H^n(X; \mathbb{C}) = \bigoplus_{p + q = n} H^q(X; \Omega^p_X)
$$

where $\Omega^p_X$ is the sheaf of holomorphic $p$-forms. This Hodge decomposition is one of the deepest results linking sheaf cohomology to topology.

### Tic-tac-toe — alternative proof of Leray on good covers (constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$)

For the case of the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$ on a smooth manifold $M$ with a finite good cover $\mathcal{U} = \{U_\alpha\}$, the Leray theorem admits a second proof via the Čech-de Rham double complex [03.04.11]. The proof avoids the spectral-sequence machinery in favour of a direct diagonal-ascent argument — Bott-Tu's *tic-tac-toe* pedagogy.

Form the double complex

$$
K^{p, q} = \prod_{\alpha_0 < \cdots < \alpha_p} \Omega^q(U_{\alpha_0}\cap\cdots\cap U_{\alpha_p}),
$$

with vertical $d$ (de Rham) and horizontal $\delta$ (Čech). The total differential $D = d + (-1)^p \delta$ satisfies $D^2 = 0$.

*First filtration (rows first).* Take cohomology of the vertical $d$ first. By the Poincaré lemma on each contractible intersection, every column collapses: only the degree-zero rows of constant functions survive. The remaining horizontal complex is the Čech complex of the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$:

$$
{}_I E_2^{p, q} = \begin{cases}\check{H}^p(\mathcal{U}; \underline{\mathbb{R}}) & q = 0 \\ 0 & q\geq 1.\end{cases}
$$

The total cohomology of the double complex is therefore $\check{H}^*(\mathcal{U}; \underline{\mathbb{R}})$.

*Second filtration (columns first).* Take cohomology of the horizontal $\delta$ first. Each sheaf $\Omega^q$ is fine, hence acyclic, so the Čech complex of $\Omega^q$ on the cover collapses to the global sections in degree zero:

$$
{}_{II} E_2^{p, q} = \begin{cases}H^q_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) & p = 0 \\ 0 & p\geq 1.\end{cases}
$$

The total cohomology is therefore $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$.

The two filtrations of the same total complex give the same total cohomology, so $\check{H}^*(\mathcal{U}; \underline{\mathbb{R}}) \cong H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$. Combined with sheaf cohomology agreeing with de Rham cohomology of the constant sheaf via the fine resolution $\Omega^\bullet$ (Exercise 5), this gives Leray's theorem for $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$ on good covers.

The tic-tac-toe pedagogy is the same machinery as the spectral-sequence proof, with the bookkeeping done graphically on a 2D grid rather than symbolically as $E_r$ pages. Bott-Tu §9 carries the full diagonal-ascent diagram. The dual-proof discipline — Leray's theorem proved twice, once by spectral sequence and once by tic-tac-toe — is the same pattern that recurs in the Künneth formula [03.04.12] and in Poincaré duality.

**Coherent cohomology of projective space.** For projective space $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ over a field $k$ and the line bundle $\mathcal{O}(d)$:

$$
H^i(\mathbb{P}^n_k; \mathcal{O}(d)) =
\begin{cases}
\binom{n + d}{n} & i = 0, d \geq 0 \\
\binom{-d - 1}{-d - n - 1} & i = n, d \leq -n - 1 \\
0 & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
$$

This is the foundational computation in projective algebraic geometry — every coherent-cohomology calculation eventually reduces to this and Riemann-Roch.

**Synthesis.** Sheaf cohomology $H^i(X, \mathcal{F})$ measures the failure of local-to-global extension for sections of a sheaf: the zeroth group is global sections, and higher groups detect obstructions such as the non-exactness of the global-sections functor on an acyclic resolution. The derived-functor perspective identifies cohomology with right-derived functors of $\Gamma$, while Cech cohomology provides a concrete combinatorial approximation via open covers. Across algebraic geometry, the cohomological dimension of a scheme controls embedding theorems, vanishing results like Kodaira vanishing and Serre duality turn cohomology groups into computable invariants, and the Leray spectral sequence decomposes cohomology along arbitrary morphisms.

## Connections [Master]

- **De Rham cohomology [03.04.06]** — sheaf cohomology of the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$ on a smooth manifold; computed by the fine resolution $\Omega^\bullet$.

- **Čech-de Rham double complex [03.04.11]** — the tic-tac-toe alternative proof of Leray on good covers proceeds via the double complex of $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$ on a finite good cover. Connection type: foundation-of.

- **Singular cohomology and de Rham theorem [03.04.13]** — sheaf cohomology of the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{R}}$ on a paracompact Hausdorff space agrees with singular cohomology with $\mathbb{R}$ coefficients; this is route 3 of the de Rham theorem. Connection type: bridging-theorem.

- **Local systems and monodromy [04.03.02]** — local systems are special locally constant sheaves; twisted cohomology is sheaf cohomology of these. Connection type: specialisation.

- **Singular cohomology** — for paracompact Hausdorff $X$ and constant sheaf $\underline{A}$, $H^i_{\mathrm{sing}}(X; A) \cong H^i(X; \underline{A})$.

- **Riemann-Roch theorem for curves [04.04.01]** — direct application of sheaf cohomology of line bundles on a curve, computing $\dim H^0(C; \mathcal{L}) - \dim H^1(C; \mathcal{L})$.

- **Riemann-Roch for compact Riemann surfaces [06.04.01]** — analytic version, equivalent to the algebraic statement via GAGA.

- **Étale cohomology** — Grothendieck-topology refinement of sheaf cohomology, the foundation of arithmetic geometry and the Weil conjectures.

- **Galois cohomology** — sheaf cohomology in the étale topology of $\mathrm{Spec}(k)$ for a field $k$.

- **Group cohomology** — sheaf cohomology on the classifying space $BG$ of a discrete group $G$, with constant coefficients.

**Throughlines and forward promises.** Sheaf cohomology is the foundational organising language for cohomology with coefficients. We will see local systems generalise the constant sheaf in [04.03.02]; we will see Leray's theorem for Čech cohomology on a good cover identify Čech and sheaf cohomology in [03.04.11]; we will later see Hodge theory and Dolbeault cohomology specialise to holomorphic-coefficient sheaf cohomology. This pattern recurs throughout algebraic and complex geometry. The foundational reason sheaf cohomology vanishes on affines is exactly Serre's vanishing theorem; this is precisely the statement that affine geometry is module theory in disguise. Putting these together: sheaf cohomology is an instance of derived-functor cohomology, the generalisation of singular cohomology to non-constant coefficients, and the bridge between local algebraic data and global topological invariants. This is exactly the Grothendieck reformulation that organises modern algebraic geometry.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Sheaf cohomology emerged in the 1940s–50s through the work of Jean Leray (in a Nazi prison camp), Henri Cartan, Jean-Pierre Serre, and Alexander Grothendieck. Leray invented the concept while imprisoned to hide his expertise in fluid dynamics; he developed sheaves and their cohomology as part of a self-imposed program to "do harmless mathematics."

Cartan and Serre formalised the theory in the *Cartan seminar* (1948–64), and Serre's foundational paper *Faisceaux Algébriques Cohérents* (FAC, 1955) introduced sheaf cohomology to algebraic geometry. The framework was completed by Grothendieck's *Tôhoku* paper (1957), which defined sheaf cohomology in any abelian category with enough injectives via right-derived functors.

The conceptual revolution was the realisation that *cohomology is local-to-global* — every classical cohomology theory is sheaf cohomology of an appropriate sheaf. De Rham cohomology, singular cohomology, group cohomology, Galois cohomology — all become unified examples of derived functor cohomology of sheaves on appropriately chosen spaces (or sites).

This unification is one of the great conceptual achievements of 20th-century mathematics: a single framework explains the deep similarities across topology, complex analysis, algebraic geometry, and number theory.
