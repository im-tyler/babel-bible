## Intuition [Beginner]

*Serre duality* is a beautiful symmetry on cohomology of a smooth projective variety: top cohomology and bottom cohomology of a sheaf are paired together, with the canonical sheaf providing the pairing. On a smooth projective curve, Serre duality says that the first cohomology of one line bundle equals the dual of the zeroth cohomology of a partner line bundle — the partner obtained by twisting the inverse against the canonical bundle.

So the $H^1$ of one line bundle equals the dual of the $H^0$ of another — a duality flipping cohomological degree and pairing against the canonical bundle. This is the algebraic-geometry analogue of *Poincaré duality* in topology, and it makes much of cohomology computable. Riemann-Roch on a curve is the difference between $\dim H^0$ and $\dim H^1$; via Serre duality, $\dim H^1$ converts into $\dim H^0$ of a partner, recovering the classical $\ell(D) - \ell(K - D)$ form.

Jean-Pierre Serre proved the theorem in 1955 in *Un théorème de dualité* (Comm. Math. Helv.); Grothendieck extended it to a vast relative version (*Residues and Duality*, 1966) that powers most of modern algebraic geometry.

## Worked example [Beginner]

For the projective line $\mathbb{P}^1_k$ (a smooth projective curve of genus 0), Serre duality gives

$$
H^1(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(d)) \cong H^0(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(-d - 2))^*.
$$

For $d = 0$: $H^1(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}) \cong H^0(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(-2))^* = 0^* = 0$. So $H^1(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}) = 0$ — confirming genus 0.

For $d = -3$: $H^1(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(-3)) \cong H^0(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(1))^*$. The right side is the dual of the 2-dimensional space of linear forms, hence 2-dimensional. So $H^1(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(-3)) = 2$.

For an elliptic curve $E$ (genus 1) and its canonical sheaf $\omega_E \cong \mathcal{O}_E$: $H^1(E; \mathcal{O}) \cong H^0(E; \omega_E)^* \cong H^0(E; \mathcal{O})^* = k^* = k$. So $\dim H^1(E; \mathcal{O}) = 1$ — confirming genus 1.

The duality computes $H^1$ from $H^0$ of a different bundle — turning hard $H^1$ computations into straightforward $H^0$ counts.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a smooth projective variety of dimension $n$ over an algebraically closed field $k$, and let $\mathcal{F}$ be a coherent sheaf on $X$.

**Theorem (Serre duality, classical form).** *For each $0 \leq i \leq n$, there is a canonical perfect pairing of finite-dimensional $k$-vector spaces*

$$
H^i(X; \mathcal{F}) \times \mathrm{Ext}^{n-i}(\mathcal{F}, \omega_X) \to k,
$$

*equivalently a canonical isomorphism*

$$
H^i(X; \mathcal{F})^\vee \cong \mathrm{Ext}^{n-i}(\mathcal{F}, \omega_X).
$$

For locally free $\mathcal{F}$ (i.e., $\mathcal{F}$ a vector bundle), $\mathrm{Ext}^{n-i}(\mathcal{F}, \omega_X) \cong H^{n-i}(X; \mathcal{F}^\vee \otimes \omega_X)$, recovering the form

$$
H^i(X; \mathcal{F}) \cong H^{n-i}(X; \mathcal{F}^\vee \otimes \omega_X)^\vee.
$$

**Trace map.** The pairing is given by composition with the *trace map*

$$
\mathrm{tr}_X : H^n(X; \omega_X) \xrightarrow{\sim} k,
$$

a canonical isomorphism (the trace map is the algebraic analogue of integration over the fundamental class). The pairing is

$$
H^i(X; \mathcal{F}) \otimes H^{n-i}(X; \mathcal{F}^\vee \otimes \omega_X) \xrightarrow{\cup} H^n(X; \omega_X) \xrightarrow{\mathrm{tr}_X} k.
$$

The cup product on cohomology, followed by the trace, gives the perfect pairing.

**Curve case.** For $C$ a smooth projective curve of genus $g$:

$$
H^1(C; \mathcal{L}) \cong H^0(C; \omega_C \otimes \mathcal{L}^{-1})^\vee.
$$

For $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{O}_C$: $H^1(C; \mathcal{O}_C)^\vee \cong H^0(C; \omega_C)$. Both have dimension $g$, the genus.

**Surface case.** For $X$ a smooth projective surface:

$$
H^2(X; \mathcal{L}) \cong H^0(X; \omega_X \otimes \mathcal{L}^{-1})^\vee, \quad H^1(X; \mathcal{L}) \cong H^1(X; \omega_X \otimes \mathcal{L}^{-1})^\vee.
$$

The middle case ($i = 1$) gives a self-pairing on $H^1$ via the canonical class.

**Generalisation: dualising sheaf.** For a proper variety $X$ over a field that may not be smooth, the *dualising sheaf* $\omega_X$ is the unique coherent sheaf making Serre duality hold:

$$
H^i(X; \mathcal{F})^\vee \cong \mathrm{Ext}^{n-i}(\mathcal{F}, \omega_X).
$$

For Cohen-Macaulay $X$, $\omega_X$ is a line bundle (resp. coherent locally free of expected rank). For general $X$, $\omega_X$ is a coherent sheaf or, more generally, the *dualising complex* $\omega_X^\bullet$ — an object of the derived category. Grothendieck duality (1966) is the relative-and-derived version.

**Grothendieck duality.** For a proper morphism $f : X \to Y$ between Noetherian schemes, there is a derived adjunction

$$
Rf_* R\mathcal{H}\!om_X(\mathcal{F}^\bullet, f^! \mathcal{G}^\bullet) \cong R\mathcal{H}\!om_Y(Rf_* \mathcal{F}^\bullet, \mathcal{G}^\bullet),
$$

with $f^! : D(Y) \to D(X)$ the *exceptional inverse image* (right-adjoint to $Rf_*$ on the derived category). Serre duality is the case $Y = \mathrm{Spec}\,k$ with $\mathcal{G}^\bullet = k$, giving $f^! k = \omega_X^\bullet[n]$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Serre duality on curves).** *Let $C$ be a smooth projective curve of genus $g$ over an algebraically closed field $k$, and $\mathcal{L}$ a line bundle on $C$. Then there is a canonical perfect pairing*

$$
H^0(C; \mathcal{L}) \otimes H^1(C; \omega_C \otimes \mathcal{L}^{-1}) \to k,
$$

*equivalently $H^1(C; \omega_C \otimes \mathcal{L}^{-1}) \cong H^0(C; \mathcal{L})^\vee$.*

**Proof sketch.** *Step 1 — trace map.* On a smooth projective curve, $H^1(C; \omega_C) \cong k$ canonically. This isomorphism (the *trace map* $\mathrm{tr}_C$) is the residue pairing: a Čech 1-cocycle representing a class in $H^1(\omega_C)$ is integrated by summing local residues at the cocycle's support. The total residue is independent of representative (the cocycle relations imply additivity).

For example, on $\mathbb{P}^1$ with cover $\{U_0, U_1\}$ where $U_i = \mathbb{P}^1 \setminus \{[1 : 0], [0 : 1]\}^c$: a Čech cocycle is a section of $\omega_{\mathbb{P}^1}$ on $U_0 \cap U_1$, i.e., a meromorphic 1-form on the affine line minus $\{0\}$. Its trace is its residue at the point $[0 : 0]$ (or equivalently $[1 : 0]$), a nonzero element of $k$, giving $H^1(\mathbb{P}^1; \omega_{\mathbb{P}^1}) \cong k$.

*Step 2 — cup product.* The cup product $H^0(\mathcal{L}) \otimes H^1(\omega_C \otimes \mathcal{L}^{-1}) \to H^1(C; \mathcal{L} \otimes \omega_C \otimes \mathcal{L}^{-1}) = H^1(C; \omega_C)$ is the standard pairing on Čech cohomology.

Composing with $\mathrm{tr}_C$:

$$
H^0(\mathcal{L}) \otimes H^1(\omega_C \otimes \mathcal{L}^{-1}) \xrightarrow{\cup} H^1(C; \omega_C) \xrightarrow{\mathrm{tr}_C} k.
$$

*Step 3 — perfect pairing.* The pairing is perfect — i.e., induces an isomorphism $H^1(\omega_C \otimes \mathcal{L}^{-1}) \cong H^0(\mathcal{L})^\vee$. This requires showing the pairing is non-degenerate.

Non-degeneracy on the left: a section $s \in H^0(\mathcal{L})$ that pairs to zero with all of $H^1(\omega \otimes \mathcal{L}^{-1})$ must be zero. This uses *Serre's vanishing theorem* (sufficiently positive line bundles have vanishing higher cohomology) plus the long-exact-sequence machinery — see Hartshorne §III.7 Theorem 7.6 for the full argument, or Forster §17 for the analytic version.

The proof reduces to checking the pairing on a generating set, where it is computed directly via Čech cocycles and residue theory.

*Step 4 — globalisation to higher-dimensional smooth projective varieties.* The same machinery — trace map $\mathrm{tr}_X : H^n(X; \omega_X) \cong k$, cup product, perfect pairing — extends to dimension $n$. The trace map is the algebraic Stokes formula applied to top-degree forms; the perfect pairing follows from Serre's vanishing on twists by ample line bundles. $\square$

The theorem ties together three deep structures: *cohomology* (for the cohomological pairing), *the canonical sheaf* (for the dualising twist), and *the trace map* (for the integration to $k$).

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Compute $H^1(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(d))$ for $d \geq 0$ using Serre duality.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\omega_{\mathbb{P}^1} = \mathcal{O}(-2)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Serre duality: $H^1(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(d)) \cong H^0(\mathbb{P}^1; \omega_{\mathbb{P}^1} \otimes \mathcal{O}(-d))^\vee = H^0(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(-d - 2))^\vee$.

For $d \geq 0$: $-d - 2 < 0$, so $H^0(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(-d - 2)) = 0$ (negative-degree line bundles on $\mathbb{P}^1$ have no global sections). Hence $H^1(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(d)) = 0$ for $d \geq 0$.

For $d \leq -2$: $-d - 2 \geq 0$, so $H^0(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(-d - 2)) = (-d - 1)$-dimensional (polynomials of degree $\leq -d - 2$). Hence $H^1(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(d)) = (-d - 1)$-dimensional.

For $d = -1$: $H^1(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(-1)) = H^0(\mathbb{P}^1; \mathcal{O}(-1))^\vee = 0$.

Use Serre duality to compute $H^1(C; \mathcal{O}_C)$ for any smooth projective curve $C$.

Apply Serre duality with $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{O}_C$.

$H^1(C; \mathcal{O}_C) \cong H^0(C; \omega_C \otimes \mathcal{O}_C^{-1})^\vee = H^0(C; \omega_C)^\vee$.

This is the standard *Hodge-theoretic* identification of the genus: $g = \dim H^0(\omega_C) = \dim H^1(\mathcal{O}_C)$, which is the $(0, 1)$-Hodge component of $H^1(C; \mathbb{C})$ via the Hodge decomposition.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that Serre duality on curves recovers the Riemann-Roch formula $\ell(D) - \ell(K - D) = \deg D - g + 1$ from the Euler-characteristic form $\chi(\mathcal{O}(D)) = \deg D + 1 - g$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\chi(\mathcal{O}(D)) = h^0(\mathcal{O}(D)) - h^1(\mathcal{O}(D))$, and Serre duality reformulates $h^1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Euler-characteristic form: $\chi(\mathcal{O}(D)) = h^0(\mathcal{O}(D)) - h^1(\mathcal{O}(D)) = \deg D + 1 - g$.

By Serre duality on a curve: $h^1(\mathcal{O}(D)) = h^0(\omega_C \otimes \mathcal{O}(-D)) = h^0(\mathcal{O}(K - D)) = \ell(K - D)$.

Substituting: $\ell(D) - \ell(K - D) = \deg D + 1 - g$, equivalently $\ell(D) - \ell(K - D) = \deg D - g + 1$ — the classical Riemann-Roch.

So Serre duality is exactly the bridge from the categorical / cohomological Euler-characteristic version to the classical Riemann-Roch with a correction term. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why is Serre duality the algebraic-geometry analogue of Poincaré duality?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Both pair top and bottom cohomology via integration of a top-degree form.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Poincaré duality* (algebraic topology): for a closed oriented $n$-manifold $M$, $H^i(M; \mathbb{R}) \cong H^{n-i}(M; \mathbb{R})^\vee$ via cup product and integration of the fundamental class.

*Serre duality* (algebraic geometry): for a smooth projective variety $X$ of dimension $n$, $H^i(X; \mathcal{F}) \cong H^{n-i}(X; \mathcal{F}^\vee \otimes \omega_X)^\vee$ via cup product and the trace map on $H^n(X; \omega_X)$.

The analogy:

- *Top cohomology* in topology = top cohomology of the canonical sheaf in algebraic geometry. The fundamental class corresponds to the trace map.
- *Volume form / orientation* in topology = canonical sheaf $\omega_X$ in algebraic geometry. Both encode "volume forms": orientation in topology, top-degree algebraic differential forms in algebraic geometry.
- *Cup product + integration* gives the pairing in both settings.

The key sharper analogy: for a smooth complex projective variety $X$ of complex dimension $n$, the *underlying real manifold* has real dimension $2n$. Poincaré duality on this manifold pairs $H^i$ and $H^{2n - i}$. Hodge decomposition $H^i = \bigoplus_{p + q = i} H^{p, q}$ refines the pairing: $H^{p, q}(X) \cong H^{n - p, n - q}(X)^\vee$. The Hodge-component $(p, q) = (0, q)$ is $H^q(X; \mathcal{O}_X)$, paired against $(n, n - q) = H^{n - q}(X; \omega_X)$ — exactly Serre duality with $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{O}_X$.

Serre duality is the *holomorphic / algebraic* refinement of Poincaré duality. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Outline the proof of Serre duality via the *trace map and Yoneda lemma* approach.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show $H^n(X; -)$ is co-represented by $\omega_X$ via Yoneda; trace map is the universal element.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Approach (Hartshorne / Grothendieck).** Show that the contravariant functor $\mathcal{F} \mapsto H^n(X; \mathcal{F})^\vee$ is *representable*: there exists a coherent sheaf $\omega_X$ and a *trace* $\mathrm{tr}_X \in H^n(X; \omega_X)^\vee$ such that for any coherent $\mathcal{F}$,

$$
\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{O}_X}(\mathcal{F}, \omega_X) \xrightarrow{\sim} H^n(X; \mathcal{F})^\vee, \quad \phi \mapsto \mathrm{tr}_X \circ H^n(\phi).
$$

The sheaf $\omega_X$ representing this functor is *the dualising sheaf*. By the Yoneda lemma, it is unique up to canonical isomorphism. Hartshorne's proof: explicitly construct $\omega_X$ as $\det \Omega^1_X$ for smooth $X$, verify by direct computation in the projective space case ($\omega_{\mathbb{P}^n} = \mathcal{O}(-n - 1)$ is the right answer), extend to general smooth projective $X$ via a Veronese embedding and the projection formula.

**Higher-cohomology pairings.** Once the $H^n(X; \omega_X) \cong k$ trace map is established, the lower cohomology pairings follow:

$$
H^i(X; \mathcal{F}) \times H^{n-i}(X; \mathcal{F}^\vee \otimes \omega_X) \xrightarrow{\cup} H^n(X; \omega_X) \xrightarrow{\mathrm{tr}} k.
$$

Non-degeneracy comes from: (i) the case $i = 0, n - i = n$ being the trace pairing; (ii) for general $i$, reduce to the case $i = 0$ via a Koszul-like resolution of $\mathcal{F}$ by sums of $\mathcal{O}(-m)$'s. By Serre's vanishing theorem, twisting by $\mathcal{O}(m)$ for $m \gg 0$ kills higher cohomology of $\mathcal{F}$, so the cohomology of $\mathcal{F}$ is computed by a finite resolution where the pairing reduces to the case of $\mathcal{O}(d)$, computed directly. $\square$

This is the modern *categorical* approach to Serre duality, due to Grothendieck. The classical approach (Serre 1955) uses analytic Hodge-theoretic methods specific to compact Kähler manifolds; the categorical approach extends to arbitrary smooth proper varieties over arbitrary fields.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

State Grothendieck duality and explain how it generalises Serre duality.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Grothendieck duality is a relative version: derived adjunction for proper morphisms.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Grothendieck duality (1966, *Residues and Duality*).** For a proper morphism $f : X \to Y$ between Noetherian schemes, the derived pushforward $Rf_* : D(X) \to D(Y)$ has a *right adjoint* $f^! : D(Y) \to D(X)$ on the bounded derived categories of coherent sheaves. The adjunction is

$$
Rf_* R\mathcal{H}\!om_X(\mathcal{F}^\bullet, f^! \mathcal{G}^\bullet) \cong R\mathcal{H}\!om_Y(Rf_* \mathcal{F}^\bullet, \mathcal{G}^\bullet),
$$

equivalently

$$
\mathrm{Hom}_{D(X)}(\mathcal{F}^\bullet, f^! \mathcal{G}^\bullet) = \mathrm{Hom}_{D(Y)}(Rf_* \mathcal{F}^\bullet, \mathcal{G}^\bullet).
$$

The *exceptional inverse image* $f^!$ encodes a "dualising data" attached to $f$. Concretely:

- For $f : X \to \mathrm{Spec}(k)$ (so $Y = \mathrm{Spec}(k)$): $f^! k = \omega_X^\bullet$, the *dualising complex*. For smooth $X$ of dimension $n$: $\omega_X^\bullet = \omega_X[n]$, the canonical sheaf shifted to degree $-n$.
- For a smooth proper morphism: $f^! \mathcal{G}^\bullet = f^* \mathcal{G}^\bullet \otimes \omega_{X/Y}[n]$, where $n$ is the relative dimension.
- For a closed immersion $i : X \hookrightarrow Y$: $i^! \mathcal{G}^\bullet$ is given by the dual of the conormal complex (Koszul resolution).

**Recovering Serre duality.** Take $Y = \mathrm{Spec}(k)$ and $\mathcal{G}^\bullet = k$. Then the adjunction reads:

$$
\mathrm{Hom}_{D(X)}(\mathcal{F}^\bullet, \omega_X^\bullet) = \mathrm{Hom}_{D(\mathrm{Spec}\,k)}(R\Gamma(X; \mathcal{F}^\bullet), k) = R\Gamma(X; \mathcal{F}^\bullet)^\vee.
$$

For $\mathcal{F}$ a coherent sheaf in degree 0 on smooth $X$ of dimension $n$: $R\Gamma(X; \mathcal{F}) = \bigoplus_i H^i(X; \mathcal{F})[-i]$, $\omega_X^\bullet = \omega_X[n]$, and the adjunction gives

$$
\mathrm{Ext}^{n - i}(\mathcal{F}, \omega_X) = H^i(X; \mathcal{F})^\vee,
$$

which is exactly Serre duality.

**Why generalise?** Grothendieck duality works for:

- *Arbitrary proper $f$* (not just $f$ to a point) — *relative* Serre duality.
- *Singular $X$* via the dualising complex $\omega_X^\bullet$ replacing $\omega_X$.
- *Derived categories* — coherent sheaves replaced by complexes, allowing inductive constructions and use of resolutions.

This generality is essential for: families of varieties (relative duality over a base), non-smooth varieties (dualising complexes), homological mirror symmetry (Fukaya category dualities mirror dualising complexes). The 1966 *Residues and Duality* (Hartshorne LNM 20) and Lipman's 2009 *Foundations of Grothendieck Duality* are the standard references. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Grothendieck duality.** For a proper morphism $f : X \to Y$, the derived adjunction $Rf_* \dashv f^!$ on derived categories. The exceptional inverse image $f^!$ encodes the "dualising data," generalising both Serre duality (case $Y = \mathrm{pt}$) and the Verdier duality of constructible sheaves. References: Hartshorne *Residues and Duality* (1966), Lipman *Foundations of Grothendieck Duality* (2009).

**Verdier duality.** The constructible-sheaves analogue of Grothendieck duality: for $f : X \to Y$ with appropriate properness hypotheses, $f^!$ encodes Poincaré duality on stratified spaces. Recovers classical Poincaré duality as $f : X \to \mathrm{pt}$ with constant sheaf coefficients. Verdier 1965, refined by Bernstein-Lunts and Beilinson-Bernstein-Deligne.

**Serre duality and Hodge theory.** On a smooth projective complex variety, Hodge decomposition $H^k(X; \mathbb{C}) = \bigoplus H^{p, q}(X)$ with $H^{p, q} = H^q(X; \Omega^p)$. Serre duality: $H^q(X; \Omega^p) \cong H^{n - q}(X; \Omega^{n - p})^\vee$, equivalently $H^{p, q} \cong H^{n - p, n - q \vee}$. The Hodge symmetry $\overline{H^{p, q}} = H^{q, p}$ together with Serre duality gives the *Hodge diamond* symmetry and the equality $h^{p, q} = h^{q, p} = h^{n - p, n - q} = h^{n - q, n - p}$.

**Self-dual line bundles.** For $X$ smooth projective of dimension $n$, a line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ is *self-dual* with respect to Serre duality iff $\mathcal{L}^{\otimes 2} = \omega_X$. Such line bundles are *theta characteristics* on curves (where $\mathcal{L}^{\otimes 2} = \omega_C$ has $\binom{2g}{1}$ solutions in $\mathrm{Pic}^{g-1}(C)$). Theta characteristics underlie the modular geometry of curves and the half-canonical bundles of physics applications.

**Bondal-Kapranov enhancement.** The dualising complex provides the *Serre functor* on the derived category of coherent sheaves: $S(\mathcal{F}^\bullet) = \mathcal{F}^\bullet \otimes \omega_X[n]$ satisfies $\mathrm{Hom}(\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{G}) = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathcal{G}, S\mathcal{F})^\vee$. Serre functors are central to the modern theory of triangulated categories and homological mirror symmetry (Bondal-Kapranov 1989; Kontsevich 1995).

**Duality in arithmetic and étale cohomology.** *Poitou-Tate duality* and *Artin-Verdier duality* are arithmetic analogues for étale cohomology of arithmetic schemes. Foundational for the Birch-Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture, the Bloch-Kato conjectures, and the Tamagawa-number conjectures.

## Full proof set [Master]

The classical curve case is sketched in the formal-definition section. The full proof for smooth projective $X$ requires Serre's vanishing theorem on twists by ample line bundles, the projection formula, and Koszul resolutions — see Hartshorne §III.7 Theorem 7.6 for the complete argument, occupying ~10 pages with technical lemmas. Grothendieck duality is stated without proof — see Hartshorne *Residues and Duality* (1966, LNM 20) or Lipman *Foundations of Grothendieck Duality for Diagrams of Schemes* (2009, LNM 1960).

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Jean-Pierre Serre's 1955 paper *Un théorème de dualité* (*Comm. Math. Helv.* 29, 9–26) was the first general statement of what is now called Serre duality. Serre considered a compact complex manifold $X$ of complex dimension $n$ and proved the duality

$$
H^q(X; \Omega^p) \cong H^{n - q}(X; \Omega^{n - p})^\vee
$$

via Hodge theory: harmonic representatives, the Hodge-Laplace operator, and a precise Hodge-theoretic identification of $H^{p, q}$. The proof used the finiteness of cohomology on compact complex manifolds (Cartan-Serre 1953) and the *Dolbeault resolution* of $\Omega^p$ by anti-holomorphic forms. Serre's approach was strongly influenced by Hodge's 1941 monograph *Theory and Applications of Harmonic Integrals* and by the Cartan seminar's developing sheaf-cohomology framework (1948–55).

Alexander Grothendieck generalised Serre's duality enormously in *Residues and Duality* (1966, written by Hartshorne from Grothendieck's notes, published as Springer LNM 20). Grothendieck's vision: duality is a *relative* phenomenon attached to a proper morphism $f : X \to Y$, encoded in a derived adjunction $Rf_* \dashv f^!$ between derived categories. The exceptional inverse image $f^!$ unifies many classical dualities (Serre, Poincaré, residue formulas, local cohomology) into a single framework. The construction of $f^!$ via *dualising complexes* and *residual complexes* introduced new homological-algebraic tools that became standard.

Two parallel developments deepened the theory. *Verdier duality* (Verdier 1965, *Catégories dérivées*) gave the constructible-sheaves analogue, foundational for modern intersection cohomology and the Beilinson-Bernstein-Deligne theory of perverse sheaves. *Lipman's reformulation* (1979 onwards, culminating in *Foundations of Grothendieck Duality for Diagrams of Schemes*, 2009) cleaned up the technical foundations using modern higher-categorical tools.

Serre duality and its generalisations sit at the centre of modern algebraic geometry. Riemann-Roch for curves uses it directly; higher-dimensional Riemann-Roch (Hirzebruch, Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch) uses the *Riemann-Roch transform* compatible with duality; the Atiyah-Singer index theorem is its analytic generalisation to elliptic operators on smooth manifolds. In homological mirror symmetry, the Serre functor $S = - \otimes \omega_X[n]$ on the derived category of coherent sheaves corresponds, on the mirror, to a categorical shift on the Fukaya category — making the Calabi-Yau condition $\omega_X = \mathcal{O}_X[n]$ central to mirror symmetry's categorical formulation.
