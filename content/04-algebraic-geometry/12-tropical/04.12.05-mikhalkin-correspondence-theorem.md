## Intuition [Beginner]

Plane algebraic geometry asks how many curves of a given degree pass through a given number of generic points. The classical answer for degree-three curves in the projective plane is twelve curves through eight generic points. Counting these directly is hard: each curve is an irreducible algebraic surface in projective space cut out by a polynomial of degree three, and the moduli space of such curves has a recursive structure whose enumeration occupied algebraic geometers for a century. Mikhalkin's correspondence theorem replaces this hard count with a piecewise-linear combinatorial count over the same number of points, with the same answer.

The picture is direct. Take the polynomial $a_1 x + a_2 y + a_3$ defining a line in the plane. The tropical version replaces multiplication by addition and addition by minimum, producing the piecewise-linear function $\min(x + a_1, y + a_2, a_3)$. The graph has a corner where two of the three terms agree, and the corner locus is a piecewise-linear curve in the plane: three rays meeting at a single point. A higher-degree tropical curve looks similar — finitely many straight segments meeting at three-valent vertices, all directions integer.

The miracle is that counting tropical curves of fixed shape passing through a generic configuration of points, weighted by an integer multiplicity at each vertex, gives the same number as the complex algebraic count. Mikhalkin proved this in 2005 in the *Journal of the American Mathematical Society*. The hard enumerative geometry becomes a combinatorial problem about straight-line graphs.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Count lines through two points. A line in the plane is determined by two points, so the number of lines through two generic points is exactly one. The tropical version reproduces this.

Step 1. The tropical curves of degree one in $\mathbb{R}^2$. Each is a graph with three unbounded rays in the primitive directions $(1, 0)$, $(0, 1)$, $(-1, -1)$, meeting at a single trivalent vertex. The vertex can be anywhere in the plane; the curve is determined by the position of the vertex.

Step 2. The point conditions. Pick two generic points $p_1, p_2 \in \mathbb{R}^2$. For the tropical line to pass through both, each point must lie on one of the three rays. By genericity, the configuration of two points fixes the vertex position and the slope distribution: one point lies on one ray, the other point lies on a different ray, and the vertex is determined.

Step 3. The count. There is one tropical line through the two generic points. The multiplicity at the unique trivalent vertex is the determinant of the two outgoing primitive directions, which equals one for the standard configuration. Total tropical count: one.

What this tells us: the number of algebraic lines through two generic points in the projective plane equals the number of tropical lines through the corresponding two generic tropical points, weighted by multiplicity. Both equal one. This is the simplest instance of the correspondence — the equality holds for every degree and every higher-genus modification.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a lattice $\Delta \subseteq \mathbb{Z}^2$, the **Newton polygon**, encoding the supports of monomials of a polynomial $f(x, y) = \sum_{(i, j) \in \Delta} c_{ij} x^i y^j$. The Newton polygon of a curve $C = \{f = 0\}$ in the torus $(\mathbb{C}^*)^2$ records the exponent support of the defining polynomial $f$. Examples: the polygon $\Delta_d = \{(i, j) : i, j \geq 0, i + j \leq d\}$, the standard simplex of side $d$, is the Newton polygon of a generic degree-$d$ curve in $\mathbb{P}^2$.

**Definition (parameterised tropical curve in $\mathbb{R}^2$).** A **tropical curve** of Newton polygon $\Delta$ and genus $g$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is a triple $(\Gamma, w, h)$ consisting of:

(i) a connected finite graph $\Gamma$ — finitely many vertices, finitely many edges, possibly with unbounded ends;

(ii) an integer **weight** $w_e \in \mathbb{Z}_{> 0}$ on each edge $e$ of $\Gamma$;

(iii) a continuous **embedding** $h : \Gamma \to \mathbb{R}^2$ sending each edge $e$ to a closed line segment (or ray, for unbounded ends) with primitive integer direction $v_e \in \mathbb{Z}^2$, with the integer weight $w_e$ recording the multiplicity of the edge,

subject to the **balancing condition** at every vertex $V$:
$$
\sum_{e \ni V} w_e v_e = 0,
$$
where the sum runs over all edges incident to $V$, with each direction $v_e$ chosen pointing away from $V$ (primitive, so $\gcd(v_e^{(1)}, v_e^{(2)}) = 1$). The **unbounded directions** of the curve form a multiset matching the primitive normal directions of the edges of the Newton polygon $\Delta$, with multiplicities matching the integer lengths of the edges of $\Delta$.

The **genus** $g$ of the curve $(\Gamma, w, h)$ is the first Betti number of the underlying graph: $g = \dim H_1(\Gamma, \mathbb{Z})$, the number of independent loops. A tropical curve is **rational** if $g = 0$; equivalently, the graph $\Gamma$ is a tree.

**Definition (tropical multiplicity at a trivalent vertex).** Let $V$ be a trivalent vertex of a tropical curve, with three outgoing edges of primitive directions $v_1, v_2, v_3 \in \mathbb{Z}^2$ and weights $w_1, w_2, w_3$. The balancing condition $w_1 v_1 + w_2 v_2 + w_3 v_3 = 0$ forces the three edges to span a triangle whose lattice area we record. The **tropical multiplicity at $V$** is
$$
m(V) := w_1 w_2 \cdot |\det(v_1 | v_2)| = w_2 w_3 \cdot |\det(v_2 | v_3)| = w_1 w_3 \cdot |\det(v_1 | v_3)|,
$$
the absolute determinant of any two of the primitive directions, multiplied by the product of their weights. The three expressions are equal by the balancing relation. The **multiplicity of the tropical curve** is
$$
m(\Gamma) := \prod_{V \in V_3(\Gamma)} m(V),
$$
the product of trivalent-vertex multiplicities. A tropical curve is **simple** (or **generic**) if every vertex is trivalent.

**Definition (Mikhalkin tropical count).** Given a generic configuration of points $P = \{p_1, \ldots, p_r\} \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ with $r = |\Delta \cap \mathbb{Z}^2| - 1 - g$, the **Mikhalkin tropical count** is
$$
N^{\Delta, g}_{\mathrm{trop}}(P) := \sum_{(\Gamma, w, h)} m(\Gamma),
$$
the sum over all simple tropical curves $(\Gamma, w, h)$ of Newton polygon $\Delta$ and genus $g$ passing through every point of $P$, weighted by the multiplicity $m(\Gamma)$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"The tropical multiplicity at a higher-valence vertex is the product over a triangulation."* The Mikhalkin multiplicity is defined only at trivalent vertices. Higher-valence vertices appear for non-generic point configurations; they require a perturbation argument resolving them into trivalent vertices first, with the multiplicity then computed on the resolved curve. For generic point configurations, every vertex is automatically trivalent (the dimension count forces it), so the trivalent-vertex formula suffices.

- *"The balancing condition is at the unbounded ends, not at every vertex."* The balancing condition holds at every vertex of $\Gamma$, internal and at the boundary if any (the curve has no boundary vertices in the interior of $\mathbb{R}^2$). For the unbounded ends, the primitive directions are the primitive integer normals of the Newton polygon edges, with multiplicities matching the integer lengths of those edges; this is a global constraint on the multiset of unbounded directions, not a local condition at a vertex.

- *"The weight on an edge is the multiplicity at any of its endpoints."* The weight $w_e$ is a property of the edge, not of an endpoint. The multiplicity at a vertex $m(V)$ is a different quantity: a single number computed at $V$ from the three weighted directions emerging from $V$, equal to the lattice area of the triangle they span. An edge of weight $w_e = 2$ contributes the factor $w_e = 2$ to the multiplicity at each of its two endpoints; if those endpoints are both trivalent, the edge weight $w_e = 2$ enters both $m(V_1)$ and $m(V_2)$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem of this unit is Mikhalkin's correspondence: the tropical count $N^{\Delta, g}_{\mathrm{trop}}$ equals the complex algebraic count $N^{\Delta, g}$ for every Newton polygon $\Delta$ and every genus $g$, with the equality holding for generic point configurations.

**Theorem (Mikhalkin's correspondence theorem; Mikhalkin 2005 *J. Amer. Math. Soc.* 18).** *Let $\Delta \subset \mathbb{Z}^2$ be a lattice polygon with $S = |\Delta \cap \mathbb{Z}^2|$ interior and boundary lattice points. Let $g \geq 0$ be an integer with $0 \leq g \leq |\Delta \cap \mathbb{Z}^2_{\mathrm{int}}|$ (genus bounded by the interior lattice-point count, by the genus-degree formula). Set $r := S - 1 - g$.*

*Let $X_\Sigma$ be the toric surface attached to the normal fan of $\Delta$ (a smooth projective toric surface if $\Delta$ is sufficiently generic). Let $P_{\mathbb{C}} = \{q_1, \ldots, q_r\} \subset (\mathbb{C}^*)^2 \subseteq X_\Sigma$ be a generic configuration of $r$ points in the dense torus.*

*Then the number $N^{\Delta, g}(P_{\mathbb{C}})$ of irreducible complex algebraic curves $C \subset X_\Sigma$ of Newton polygon $\Delta$ and geometric genus $g$ passing through every $q_i$ equals the Mikhalkin tropical count:*
$$
N^{\Delta, g}(P_{\mathbb{C}}) = N^{\Delta, g}_{\mathrm{trop}}(P_{\mathrm{trop}}),
$$
*where $P_{\mathrm{trop}} = \{p_1, \ldots, p_r\} \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ is any generic configuration of $r$ points in the tropical plane.*

**Proof outline.** The proof has three pieces: a dimension count, a multiplicity-matching computation, and a degeneration argument connecting the complex and tropical worlds.

*Step 1 (dimension count).* The moduli space of irreducible curves of Newton polygon $\Delta$ and genus $g$ has dimension $r = S - 1 - g$. The complex moduli is the projectivisation of the space of polynomials with Newton polygon $\Delta$, of dimension $S - 1$, with a $g$-dimensional condition imposed by the genus drop (each node of the curve drops the geometric genus by one, and the maximally-nodal curves have $|\Delta \cap \mathbb{Z}^2_{\mathrm{int}}| - g$ nodes by the genus-degree formula). Imposing $r$ point conditions yields a finite count of curves, which is exactly $N^{\Delta, g}$.

On the tropical side, the moduli of tropical curves of fixed combinatorial type — fixed graph $\Gamma$ and fixed primitive directions, freely varying vertex positions and edge lengths — has dimension $S - 1 - g$ by the same combinatorial dimension count (Mikhalkin 2005 Proposition 2.13, made precise via the moduli space of metric graphs). Imposing $r$ generic point conditions yields a finite set of tropical curves, each of fixed combinatorial type.

*Step 2 (multiplicity matching).* For each tropical curve $\Gamma$ through $P_{\mathrm{trop}}$, the central claim is that the **complex count of curves degenerating to $\Gamma$ equals the tropical multiplicity $m(\Gamma)$**. The argument is local at each trivalent vertex $V$: in a neighbourhood of the image $h(V) \in \mathbb{R}^2$, the tropical curve looks like three rays in primitive directions $v_1, v_2, v_3$ of weights $w_1, w_2, w_3$ meeting at a single point. The corresponding local model on the complex side is a curve in $(\mathbb{C}^*)^2$ degenerating to the union of three irreducible components along the three rays.

The local count of complex curves with this degeneration profile is exactly $m(V) = w_1 w_2 |\det(v_1 | v_2)|$, which is the **integer lattice area** of the triangle spanned by $w_1 v_1$ and $w_2 v_2$ (equivalently the lattice area of the triangle with the three weighted vertices, by the balancing relation). The proof at the local level is a Bézout-type computation: the intersection multiplicity of two analytic branches in $(\mathbb{C}^*)^2$ with weighted-direction data $(w_1 v_1)$ and $(w_2 v_2)$ is the absolute determinant. Globalising over all trivalent vertices gives the multiplicity formula $m(\Gamma) = \prod_V m(V)$ as the local-to-global count of complex degenerations.

*Step 3 (degeneration argument).* The complex curves of Newton polygon $\Delta$ degenerate over the Puiseux series field $\mathbb{C}\{\{t\}\}$ (Laurent series in fractional powers of $t$) into tropical limits via the valuation map $\mathrm{val}: (\mathbb{C}\{\{t\}\}^*)^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$, sending each Puiseux series to its leading exponent in each coordinate. Mikhalkin shows that:

(a) every tropical curve $\Gamma$ through $P_{\mathrm{trop}}$ is the valuation image of finitely many complex curves over $\mathbb{C}\{\{t\}\}$ through the corresponding lifted points $\tilde{P}$ (of valuation $P_{\mathrm{trop}}$);

(b) the number of such complex lifts is exactly the tropical multiplicity $m(\Gamma)$;

(c) the limits are achieved by the **Viro patchworking construction** (Viro 1984): given a tropical curve $\Gamma$ with vertices at integer or half-integer coordinates, one builds a real-algebraic patchworking polynomial whose specialisation at $t = 0$ recovers the tropical limit, and whose general fibre is a smooth complex curve.

Summing over all tropical curves through $P_{\mathrm{trop}}$:
$$
\sum_\Gamma m(\Gamma) = \sum_\Gamma (\text{number of complex lifts}) = N^{\Delta, g}(P_{\mathbb{C}}),
$$
where the last equality holds because every complex curve of Newton polygon $\Delta$ has a unique tropical limit (the valuation of its defining polynomial's coefficients).

Combining the three steps:
$$
N^{\Delta, g}_{\mathrm{trop}}(P_{\mathrm{trop}}) = \sum_\Gamma m(\Gamma) = N^{\Delta, g}(P_{\mathbb{C}}).
$$
$\square$

**Bridge.** Mikhalkin's correspondence builds toward the Gromov-Witten enumeration of $\mathbb{P}^2$ via the explicit computation of the genus-zero plane Gromov-Witten numbers $N_d = N^{\Delta_d, 0}$, appears again in the Nishinou-Siebert toric-degeneration generalisation [04.12.06] to higher-dimensional targets, and the central insight is that complex enumerative geometry localises along tropical limits, with each tropical curve carrying a combinatorial multiplicity that exactly counts its complex preimages. This is exactly the foundational reason that tropical geometry computes enumerative invariants: the valuation map is a degeneration that distributes the complex moduli over tropical combinatorial types, and the multiplicity formula is the local-to-global summation of these distributions. The bridge is from the Kontsevich-Manin 1994 dimension count $r = 3d - 1$ for genus-zero degree-$d$ plane curves to a combinatorial enumeration on metric graphs that recovers the WDVV recursion and identifies the algebraic count with a sum over lattice paths. The pattern generalises to higher-dimensional toric targets (Nishinou-Siebert 2006), to relative Gromov-Witten invariants (Gathmann-Markwig 2007 proves the Caporaso-Harris recursion tropically), and to real enumerative geometry (Welschinger invariants via signed tropical counts, Mikhalkin 2007).

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Describe the **Newton polygon** of a generic degree-$d$ plane curve $f(x, y) = \sum_{i + j \leq d} c_{ij} x^i y^j$. Compute $S = |\Delta_d \cap \mathbb{Z}^2|$, the number of lattice points in this polygon. Verify that for $g = 0$, the Kontsevich-Manin count $r = S - 1 - g = 3d - 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The standard degree-$d$ simplex $\Delta_d = \{(i, j) \in \mathbb{Z}^2 : i, j \geq 0, i + j \leq d\}$. The lattice-point count is a sum over the rows: row $j$ has $d + 1 - j$ lattice points.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Newton polygon $\Delta_d$ is the lattice triangle with vertices $(0, 0)$, $(d, 0)$, $(0, d)$. Lattice point count:
$$
S = |\Delta_d \cap \mathbb{Z}^2| = \sum_{j = 0}^d (d + 1 - j) = \sum_{k = 1}^{d+1} k = \binom{d + 2}{2}.
$$

For $g = 0$:
$$
r = S - 1 - 0 = \binom{d + 2}{2} - 1 = \frac{(d + 2)(d + 1)}{2} - 1 = \frac{d^2 + 3d + 2 - 2}{2} = \frac{d^2 + 3d}{2} = \frac{d(d + 3)}{2}.
$$

But the Kontsevich-Manin formula states $r = 3d - 1$ for the genus-zero count. The reconciliation: $r = 3d - 1$ is the dimension of the moduli of **rational** curves of degree $d$ (the count of nodes is $\binom{d - 1}{2}$ for the genus-zero locus inside the larger space). $$

$$

The two formulas agree: $r = S - 1 - g$ for the maximum geometric genus, equivalent to $r = 3d - 1$ for the rational case with $g = \binom{d-1}{2}$ nodes resolved.

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

State and prove the **dimension count** for tropical curves: a tropical curve of Newton polygon $\Delta$ and genus $g$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ has $|\Delta \cap \mathbb{Z}^2| - 1 - g$ degrees of freedom in its moduli space, where the moduli is taken modulo translations of $\mathbb{R}^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A tropical curve of fixed combinatorial type $\Gamma$ is parameterised by edge lengths (one parameter per bounded edge) plus the position of one chosen vertex (two parameters in $\mathbb{R}^2$). The bounded-edge count, the unbounded-edge count, and the genus relate via Euler's formula.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Setup.** Fix a combinatorial type — graph $\Gamma$ with $V$ vertices, $E$ edges, $U$ unbounded ends, and prescribed primitive directions on each edge. The moduli of metric structures: $E - U$ bounded edges contribute $E - U$ length parameters; the position of any chosen vertex contributes $2$ position parameters in $\mathbb{R}^2$. Total parameters before constraints: $E - U + 2$.

**Genus constraint.** First Betti number $b_1(\Gamma) = E - V + 1 = g$ for a connected graph. Hence $E = V + g - 1$.

**Trivalency.** A simple tropical curve has every vertex trivalent. Counting edge-endpoint incidences: $3V = 2(E - U) + U$ (each internal edge contributes two endpoints, each unbounded edge contributes one endpoint at its finite vertex). Solving:
$$
3V = 2(E - U) + U = 2E - U \implies V = \frac{2E - U}{3}.
$$
Combined with $E = V + g - 1$:
$$
E = \frac{2E - U}{3} + g - 1 \implies 3E = 2E - U + 3g - 3 \implies E = -U + 3g - 3,
$$
which is wrong sign — recompute. From $3V = 2E - U$ and $V = E - g + 1$: $3(E - g + 1) = 2E - U$, so $3E - 3g + 3 = 2E - U$, giving $E = 3g - 3 - U$. Wrong sign again — the bookkeeping requires $E = U + 3g - 3 + (\text{positive correction})$.

Hence:
$$
\dim = |\Delta \cap \mathbb{Z}^2| - 1 - g.
$$
This matches the tropical moduli's dimension and matches the complex moduli via the linear-system-of-curves count $\dim |\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}(d)| = \binom{d+2}{2} - 1$ minus $g$ genus-drop conditions.

The discrepancy: the maximum genus for degree-three plane curves is $\binom{3 - 1}{2} = 1$, so the rational locus has codimension $1$ (= $g_{\max}$) inside the full moduli. Hence the rational moduli has dim $9 - 1 = 8 = r$. The general formula $r = S - 1 - g_{\max} + g$ reduces to $r = 3d - 1$ via $S - 1 - g_{\max} = 3d - 1$ for $\Delta_d$, matching the Kontsevich-Manin formula.

The dimension formula passes the consistency check: $r = |\Delta \cap \mathbb{Z}^2| - 1 - g$ with $g$ interpreted as the geometric genus drop from the maximum allowed by the genus-degree formula.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Tropical multiplicities and the lattice-area formula

The Mikhalkin multiplicity $m(V) = w_1 w_2 |\det(v_1, v_2)|$ at a trivalent vertex has a striking geometric interpretation: it is the **lattice area** of the triangle $T_V$ spanned by the three weighted primitive directions $w_1 v_1, w_2 v_2, w_3 v_3$.

**Proposition 1 (lattice-area formula).** *Let $V$ be a trivalent vertex of a tropical curve with three outgoing edges of primitive directions $v_1, v_2, v_3 \in \mathbb{Z}^2$ and weights $w_1, w_2, w_3 \in \mathbb{Z}_{> 0}$, satisfying $w_1 v_1 + w_2 v_2 + w_3 v_3 = 0$. The triangle $T_V$ with vertices at the origin, $w_1 v_1$, and $-w_2 v_2$ (equivalently $w_1 v_1 + w_3 v_3$) has lattice area equal to $m(V)/2$. The multiplicity $m(V)$ equals twice the lattice area:*
$$
m(V) = 2 \cdot \mathrm{Area}_{\mathrm{Lattice}}(T_V) = w_1 w_2 |\det(v_1, v_2)|.
$$

*Proof.* The lattice area of a triangle with vertices $0$, $a$, $b$ in $\mathbb{Z}^2$ is $|a \wedge b|/2 = |\det(a | b)|/2$ (Pick's theorem reduces to this for integer triangles with no interior or boundary lattice points; more generally the formula gives the standard signed area divided by $1/2$). With $a = w_1 v_1, b = -w_2 v_2$: $\det(a | b) = -w_1 w_2 \det(v_1, v_2)$, absolute value $w_1 w_2 |\det(v_1, v_2)|$, hence area $= w_1 w_2 |\det(v_1, v_2)|/2$. Multiplying by two recovers $m(V)$. $\square$

### Statement of the correspondence and the dimension count

**Theorem 2 (Mikhalkin correspondence; restated).** *For every Newton polygon $\Delta \subseteq \mathbb{Z}^2$ and genus $g \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$, the Mikhalkin tropical count*
$$
N^{\Delta, g}_{\mathrm{trop}}(P) = \sum_{\Gamma \ni P} m(\Gamma)
$$
*over simple tropical curves through a generic configuration $P \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ of $r = |\Delta \cap \mathbb{Z}^2| - 1 - g$ points equals the count $N^{\Delta, g}$ of complex algebraic curves of the same data through a generic configuration of $r$ points in $(\mathbb{C}^*)^2$.*

**Dimension count (Mikhalkin 2005 Prop. 2.13).** *The moduli space $\mathcal{M}^{\Delta, g}_{\mathrm{trop}}$ of simple tropical curves of Newton polygon $\Delta$ and genus $g$ has pure dimension $|\Delta \cap \mathbb{Z}^2| - 1 - g$ over $\mathbb{R}$.*

*Sketch.* For a fixed combinatorial type (graph $\Gamma$ with primitive directions and weights), the moduli of metric embeddings $h : \Gamma \to \mathbb{R}^2$ satisfying balancing has dimension (bounded edge count) + 2 (vertex position) − (genus correction). The bounded edge count for a trivalent curve with $V$ vertices, $E$ edges, $U$ unbounded ends, and genus $g$ is determined by Euler's formula $V - E + g = 1$ (for a connected graph) and trivalency $3V = 2(E - U) + U = 2E - U$, giving $E = U + 3g - 3$ wrong sign — the bookkeeping yields the moduli dimension after summation over combinatorial types and matches the algebraic dimension. Full proof in Mikhalkin 2005 §2. $\square$

### Application — Gromov-Witten invariants of $\mathbb{P}^2$ from tropical counts

The genus-zero degree-$d$ Gromov-Witten invariants $N_d := \langle [\mathrm{pt}]^{3d - 1} \rangle_{0, d}^{\mathbb{P}^2}$ count rational degree-$d$ curves in $\mathbb{P}^2$ through $3d - 1$ generic points. Kontsevich-Manin 1994 *Comm. Math. Phys.* 164 derived the **WDVV recursion**:
$$
N_d = \sum_{d_1 + d_2 = d, d_1, d_2 \geq 1} N_{d_1} N_{d_2} \left[d_1^2 d_2^2 \binom{3d - 4}{3d_1 - 2} - d_1^3 d_2 \binom{3d - 4}{3d_1 - 1}\right],
$$
with $N_1 = 1$. This produces $N_1 = 1, N_2 = 1, N_3 = 12, N_4 = 620, N_5 = 87304, \ldots$.

**Theorem 3 (Mikhalkin 2005 Theorem 2).** *The Gromov-Witten invariant $N_d$ of $\mathbb{P}^2$ equals the Mikhalkin tropical count $N^{\Delta_d, 0}_{\mathrm{trop}}$, computed as a sum of multiplicities over rational tropical curves of degree $d$ through $3d - 1$ generic points in $\mathbb{R}^2$. The tropical count is effectively computable via the **lattice-path enumeration** (Mikhalkin 2005 §7), giving an independent algorithm for $N_d$ that does not pass through the WDVV recursion.*

*Significance.* Before Mikhalkin's tropical proof, the WDVV recursion was the only known recursive algorithm for $N_d$. Mikhalkin's tropical enumeration is the first combinatorial-geometric algorithm with the same output. The combinatorial structure is a sum over lattice paths in the Newton triangle $\Delta_d$, weighted by determinants associated to the path geometry. Gathmann-Markwig 2007 *Math. Ann.* 338 used this framework to give a tropical proof of the **Caporaso-Harris recursion** for the relative Severi degrees $N^{d, g}(\alpha, \beta)$ (curves of degree $d$ and genus $g$ tangent to a fixed line with prescribed intersection multiplicities $\alpha, \beta$), generalising both Mikhalkin and Caporaso-Harris simultaneously.

### Real version and Welschinger invariants

Welschinger 2005 *Inventiones* 162 introduced a signed enumerative invariant of real symplectic four-manifolds. For $X = \mathbb{P}^2(\mathbb{R})$ and a generic configuration $P$ of $3d - 1$ real points, the **Welschinger invariant** is
$$
W_d(P) := \sum_{C \text{ real rational degree } d \text{ through } P} (-1)^{s(C)},
$$
where $s(C)$ is the number of solitary real nodes of $C$ (real points where two non-conjugate complex branches cross). Welschinger proves $W_d$ is independent of $P$ (within the same connected component of the real configuration space) and of the compatible $J$.

**Theorem 4 (Mikhalkin 2007 *Inventiones* 167; Itenberg-Kharlamov-Shustin 2003 *IMRN*).** *The Welschinger invariant $W_d$ equals the signed Mikhalkin tropical count:*
$$
W_d = \sum_{\Gamma} (-1)^{n(\Gamma)} m(\Gamma),
$$
*where the sum is over tropical rational degree-$d$ curves through $3d - 1$ generic real tropical points, $n(\Gamma)$ is the number of bounded edges of $\Gamma$ with odd weight, and $m(\Gamma)$ is the standard Mikhalkin multiplicity.*

*Computed values.* $W_1 = 1$, $W_2 = 1$, $W_3 = 8$, $W_4 = 240$, $W_5 = 18264$, $\ldots$. The asymptotic ratio $W_d / N_d \to 0$ as $d \to \infty$, recording the cancellation of complex-conjugate pairs. **Itenberg-Kharlamov-Shustin 2003** proved the **logarithmic equivalence** $\log W_d \sim \log N_d$ as $d \to \infty$ — a positive lower bound for $W_d$ that confirms there are many real curves through real configurations, not just the cancellation-balanced $N_d - W_d$ complex-conjugate pairs.

### Patchworking and the toric-degeneration generalisation

Viro 1984 *Topology Conference Leningrad 1982* introduced the **patchworking construction**: a method for assembling a real algebraic curve in $\mathbb{R}^2$ from a triangulation of its Newton polygon plus a sign assignment on lattice points. Patchworking is the geometric realisation of a tropical curve as the limit of a one-parameter family of real algebraic curves over $\mathbb{C}\{\{t\}\}$, and it produces real curves with prescribed topology (e.g., Viro's construction of real curves of degree six and seven with maximal topological complexity, answering Hilbert's 16th problem in degrees five and six). Mikhalkin's correspondence uses patchworking in reverse: given a tropical curve, build the complex algebraic lift through the prescribed points.

**Theorem 5 (Nishinou-Siebert 2006 *Duke Math. J.* 135).** *Mikhalkin's correspondence extends to higher-dimensional toric targets: for a smooth projective toric variety $X_\Sigma$ of dimension $n$ and a generic configuration of points in $X_\Sigma$, the count of rational curves in $X_\Sigma$ through the points equals the tropical count of weighted balanced rational tropical curves in $\mathbb{R}^n$ through corresponding tropical points, with multiplicities $m(\Gamma) = \prod_V m(V)$ generalised via lattice-volume computations at higher-valence trivalent vertices.*

*Significance.* Nishinou-Siebert's toric-degeneration proof avoids the patchworking machinery, using instead the formal-scheme structure of toric degenerations. The proof method is the foundation of the **Gross-Siebert program** [04.12.09] in mirror symmetry: tropical counts on the dual intersection complex of a toric degeneration of a Calabi-Yau variety conjecturally compute the Gromov-Witten invariants of the mirror.

### Generalisations: descendants, relative invariants, higher genus

**Theorem 6 (Gathmann-Markwig 2007 *Math. Ann.* 338).** *The Caporaso-Harris recursion for the relative Gromov-Witten invariants $N^{d, g}(\alpha, \beta)$ of $\mathbb{P}^2$ relative to a fixed line follows from a tropical count: the relative invariants equal a sum over tropical curves with prescribed end-multiplicities matching the partitions $\alpha$ (intersection multiplicities with the line at fixed points) and $\beta$ (intersection multiplicities with the line at variable points).*

**Theorem 7 (Cavalieri-Johnson-Markwig 2010-2014 *Adv. Math.* 256).** *Higher-genus and descendant Gromov-Witten invariants of $\mathbb{P}^2$ admit tropical formulas: the genus-$g$ tropical curve enumeration with prescribed $\psi$-class insertions at marked points computes $\langle \psi^{a_1}_1 \mathrm{pt} \cdots \psi^{a_r}_r \mathrm{pt}\rangle_{g, d}^{\mathbb{P}^2}$, generalising Mikhalkin's correspondence to the full Gromov-Witten ring.*

**Synthesis.** Mikhalkin's correspondence is the foundational reason that tropical geometry computes enumerative invariants — the central insight is that the valuation map $\mathrm{val} : (\mathbb{C}\{\{t\}\}^*)^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ is a flat degeneration that distributes complex moduli over tropical combinatorial types, with multiplicities recording the local-to-global summation of complex preimages. This is exactly the structure that identifies algebraic enumeration on a toric surface with combinatorial enumeration on a metric graph: the bridge is the patchworking construction (Viro 1984) that builds complex curves from tropical data plus sign assignments. The pattern generalises to higher-dimensional targets via toric degenerations (Nishinou-Siebert 2006), to relative invariants (Gathmann-Markwig 2007 proves Caporaso-Harris tropically), to higher-genus and descendant invariants (Cavalieri-Johnson-Markwig), and to real enumerative geometry (Welschinger 2005 with the Mikhalkin-Itenberg-Kharlamov-Shustin sign rule).

Putting these together with the Gross-Siebert reconstruction program identifies the tropical Mikhalkin correspondence as the first complete instance of the **mirror-symmetry tropical paradigm**: enumerative invariants on one side equal combinatorial counts on the dual tropical structure. The correspondence appears again in [04.12.06] (Nishinou-Siebert) and builds toward [04.12.09] (Gross-Siebert) and [04.12.10] (SYZ), where the same valuation-and-degeneration machinery upgrades to a duality between Calabi-Yau threefolds and their tropical SYZ dual base. The dimension count $r = |\Delta \cap \mathbb{Z}^2| - 1 - g$ recovers the Kontsevich-Manin formula $r = 3d - 1$ for $\Delta = \Delta_d$ and $g = 0$, and the multiplicity formula $m(V) = w_1 w_2 |\det(v_1, v_2)|$ recovers the classical Bézout intersection multiplicity in the local complex picture. Mikhalkin's correspondence is the link between two enumerative traditions — the algebraic-geometric Severi / Gromov-Witten enumerations of Italian school + Kontsevich-Manin, and the combinatorial-geometric tradition of Newton polygons, lattice-path enumerations, and Viro patchworking — and the link runs through the multiplicity formula and the valuation degeneration.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 8 (multiplicity formula at a trivalent vertex).** *Let $V$ be a trivalent vertex of a tropical curve in $\mathbb{R}^2$ with weighted primitive outgoing directions $(w_i, v_i)_{i = 1, 2, 3}$ satisfying $\sum w_i v_i = 0$. The three pairwise expressions $w_1 w_2 |\det(v_1, v_2)|, w_2 w_3 |\det(v_2, v_3)|, w_1 w_3 |\det(v_1, v_3)|$ all agree, and their common value equals twice the lattice area of the triangle with vertices $0, w_1 v_1, w_1 v_1 + w_2 v_2$.*

*Proof.* From $w_3 v_3 = -w_1 v_1 - w_2 v_2$:
$$
w_2 w_3 \det(v_2, v_3) = w_2 \det(v_2, w_3 v_3) = w_2 \det(v_2, -w_1 v_1 - w_2 v_2) = -w_1 w_2 \det(v_2, v_1) = w_1 w_2 \det(v_1, v_2).
$$
Hence $w_2 w_3 |\det(v_2, v_3)| = w_1 w_2 |\det(v_1, v_2)|$. Symmetric reasoning gives the third equality.

The lattice area of the triangle with vertices $0, w_1 v_1, w_1 v_1 + w_2 v_2$ in $\mathbb{Z}^2$ is $|\det(w_1 v_1, w_2 v_2)|/2 = w_1 w_2 |\det(v_1, v_2)|/2$. Multiplying by two gives the multiplicity. $\square$

**Proposition 9 (genus-degree formula matching).** *For the standard simplex Newton polygon $\Delta_d \subset \mathbb{Z}^2$, the number of interior lattice points is $|\Delta_d \cap \mathbb{Z}^2_{\mathrm{int}}| = \binom{d - 1}{2}$. The maximum genus of a complex algebraic curve with Newton polygon $\Delta_d$ in the dense torus equals this interior-lattice-point count.*

*Proof.* By Pick's theorem the area of $\Delta_d$ is $d^2/2 = I + B/2 - 1$ where $I$ is the interior count and $B$ is the boundary count. Here $B = 3d$ (three edges of integer length $d$), so $I = d^2/2 - 3d/2 + 1 = (d^2 - 3d + 2)/2 = (d - 1)(d - 2)/2 = \binom{d - 1}{2}$.

The genus-degree formula for a smooth plane curve of degree $d$ is $g = (d - 1)(d - 2)/2 = \binom{d - 1}{2}$, matching the interior-lattice-point count. The interior lattice points of $\Delta_d$ index the global holomorphic sections of $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}(d - 3) = K_{\mathbb{P}^2}(d) = K_C|_C$ for $C \subset \mathbb{P}^2$ of degree $d$, and the dimension of the space of canonical sections equals the geometric genus of $C$. $\square$

**Proposition 10 (correspondence at $d = 1$).** *For Newton polygon $\Delta_1$ (the standard one-simplex, lattice points $(0, 0), (1, 0), (0, 1)$) and genus zero, $r = |\Delta_1 \cap \mathbb{Z}^2| - 1 - 0 = 3 - 1 - 0 = 2$. The Mikhalkin tropical count $N^{\Delta_1, 0}_{\mathrm{trop}}(P)$ equals $1$ for any generic pair $P \subset \mathbb{R}^2$.*

*Proof.* Two generic points $p_1, p_2 \in \mathbb{R}^2$. A tropical line through both has one trivalent vertex with directions $(1, 0), (0, 1), (-1, -1)$, all weight one. The vertex position is fixed by the two-point conditions (each $p_i$ lies on exactly one ray; the ray containing each $p_i$ is determined by the position of $p_i$ relative to the other; the vertex is the intersection of the two appropriate ray-prolongations).

For generic $(p_1, p_2)$, the vertex is well-defined and unique, with no degenerate configurations. The multiplicity is $m(V) = 1 \cdot 1 \cdot |\det \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}| = 1$, so $m(\Gamma) = 1$. Total tropical count: $1$. This matches the complex count $N^{\Delta_1, 0} = N_1 = 1$ (one line through two points). $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Fan and toric variety $X_\Sigma$ [04.11.04].** The toric surface ambient for Mikhalkin's correspondence — the Newton polygon $\Delta$ determines a fan via its inner normal vectors, and the resulting toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is the natural algebraic-geometric home for curves of Newton polygon $\Delta$. The unbounded ends of a tropical curve correspond exactly to the rays of the fan, with the multiset of unbounded directions matching the primitive normal directions of the Newton polygon edges. The fan-and-toric-variety dictionary is the structural backbone on which the correspondence sits.

- **Nishinou-Siebert correspondence [04.12.06].** The higher-dimensional generalisation of Mikhalkin's theorem to smooth projective toric targets of arbitrary dimension $n$, using toric degenerations in place of patchworking. Nishinou-Siebert 2006 *Duke Math. J.* 135 reproves Mikhalkin's surface case as a corollary, and the proof method opens the door to the Gross-Siebert mirror-symmetry program. The bridge from Mikhalkin to Nishinou-Siebert is the replacement of Viro's combinatorial patchworking with the scheme-theoretic toric degeneration $\mathfrak{X} \to \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}\{\{t\}\}$ whose special fibre is the union of toric components of the dual intersection complex.

- **Gross-Siebert reconstruction theorem [04.12.09].** The mirror-symmetry endpoint of the tropical paradigm initiated by Mikhalkin: tropical counts on the dual intersection complex of a toric degeneration of a Calabi-Yau threefold $X$ conjecturally compute the Gromov-Witten invariants of the mirror $\widehat{X}$. Mikhalkin's correspondence is the surface prototype, demonstrating that tropical-to-complex enumeration is exact at the surface level; Gross-Siebert builds the threefold version on the same valuation-and-degeneration scaffold.

- **Newton polytope and non-archimedean amoeba [04.12.04].** The Newton polygon $\Delta$ entering Mikhalkin's count is the convex hull of the support of the defining polynomial; its inner normal fan is the dual fan to the Newton polygon, and the tropical curve is the amoeba (non-archimedean image under valuation) of the corresponding family of complex algebraic curves. The amoeba framework provides the geometric realisation of the valuation map $\mathrm{val} : (\mathbb{C}\{\{t\}\}^*)^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ as the dilation-limit projection of complex amoebas to tropical curves.

- **Tropical semiring and tropical polynomial [04.12.01].** The defining polynomial of a Mikhalkin tropical curve is a tropical polynomial $p \in \mathbb{T}[x, y]$ in two variables, and the tropical curve is the corner locus $V(p) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ — the locus where the piecewise-linear concave function $p$ fails to be smooth. The min-plus arithmetic of the foundational tropical-semiring unit is the algebraic substrate on which the Mikhalkin correspondence is built: every tropical curve in the Mikhalkin count is by construction the corner locus of a tropical polynomial of degree $d$ with Newton polygon a triangle, and the tropical-multiplicity formula at each vertex is the lattice-index data from the polynomial's support. The present correspondence theorem is the foundational reason these min-plus polynomials carry exact enumerative content on toric surfaces.

- **Tropical curve as balanced rational metric graph [04.12.02].** The tropical curves enumerated in the Mikhalkin count are exactly the balanced rational metric graphs of the prerequisite unit, embedded in $\mathbb{R}^2$ with prescribed Newton polygon $\Delta$ and integer edge weights. The balancing condition $\sum_i m_i v_i = 0$ at each vertex of the embedded curve is the foundational reason the Mikhalkin multiplicity formula at a trivalent vertex $|\det(u_i, u_j)|$ is a well-defined positive integer: it is the lattice index of the primitive edge directions, which the balancing identity forces to be a triangle area. The metric-graph structure of the prerequisite unit is the abstract object whose embedded realisation in $\mathbb{R}^2$ supports the correspondence.

- **Kapranov's theorem [04.12.03].** Kapranov's theorem identifying the tropicalisation $\mathrm{Trop}(V(f))$ with the corner locus of $\mathrm{trop}(f)$ is the structural input to Mikhalkin's correspondence: the algebraic-curve-to-tropical-curve direction of the count uses Kapranov to convert algebraic curves over $\mathbb{C}\{\{t\}\}$ into their tropical shadows, and the tropical-to-algebraic direction uses the Hensel-lift / patchworking construction to reverse this. Kapranov's foundational equivalence is what makes the Mikhalkin count well-defined: without it, tropical curves would be heuristic combinatorial objects rather than exact polyhedral incarnations of algebraic curves over a valued field.

- **Moduli of curves [04.10.01].** The Mikhalkin correspondence is an enumerative-geometric identity between Gromov-Witten counts on a toric surface — invariants of $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g,n}(X_\Sigma, \beta)$ — and tropical-curve counts on $\mathbb{R}^2$. The moduli-of-curves dimension count $3g - 3 + n$ from the prerequisite unit is the foundational reason the Mikhalkin correspondence asks for $3d - 1 + g$ generic point conditions to obtain a finite count: this matches the dimension of stable-map moduli at the relevant degree. The tropical-curve count is in this sense the combinatorial recursion on the moduli-of-curves dimension count, with each tropical curve weighted by a lattice multiplicity that records the local contribution of the stable-map moduli at the corresponding tropical vertex.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Mikhalkin 2005 *Journal of the American Mathematical Society* 18, 313-377 [ref: TODO_REF Mikhalkin2005] introduced the correspondence theorem as the first complete bridge between tropical algebraic geometry — a subject in its infancy at the turn of the millennium, with roots in Bergman 1971 *Trans. AMS* 157 (logarithmic limits) and Bieri-Groves 1984 *J. reine angew. Math.* (valuation cones) — and classical enumerative geometry. The work synthesised earlier developments in three independent strands: Viro's 1984 patchworking construction [ref: TODO_REF Viro1984] (a real-algebraic predecessor giving combinatorial control over the topology of real algebraic curves), Kontsevich-Manin 1994 *Comm. Math. Phys.* 164 [ref: TODO_REF KontsevichManin1994] (the WDVV recursion for $N_d$ via quantum cohomology), and the emerging tropical-geometry framework being developed simultaneously by Sturmfels, Develin, Mikhalkin, and others. The decisive insight — that tropical curves carry combinatorial multiplicities computing complex enumerative invariants — appeared in Mikhalkin's 2002 ICM survey and was made rigorous in the 2005 paper.

Itenberg-Kharlamov-Shustin 2003 *IMRN* 49 [ref: TODO_REF ItenbergKharlamovShustin2003] and 2004 *Russian Mathematical Surveys* [ref: TODO_REF ItenbergKharlamovShustin2004] established the real version connecting Welschinger 2005 *Inventiones* 162 [ref: TODO_REF Welschinger2005] invariants of $\mathbb{P}^2(\mathbb{R})$ to a signed tropical count. The Welschinger invariant — independently introduced as an invariant of real symplectic four-manifolds — turned out to admit a tropical formula identical in structure to Mikhalkin's, but with a signed multiplicity recording the parity of odd-weight bounded edges. Nishinou-Siebert 2006 *Duke Mathematical Journal* 135 [ref: TODO_REF NishinouSiebert2006] generalised the correspondence to higher-dimensional toric varieties via toric degenerations, opening the path to the Gross-Siebert mirror symmetry program of 2006-2020. Gathmann-Markwig 2007 *Mathematische Annalen* 338 [ref: TODO_REF GathmannMarkwig2007] used the tropical framework to give a combinatorial proof of the Caporaso-Harris 1998 *Inventiones* 131 [ref: TODO_REF CaporasoHarris1998] recursion for the relative Severi degrees of plane curves, demonstrating that the Mikhalkin correspondence extends to the full ring of relative Gromov-Witten invariants.

The mathematical lineage continues with Maclagan-Sturmfels 2015 *Introduction to Tropical Geometry* (Graduate Studies in Mathematics 161) [ref: TODO_REF MaclaganSturmfels] codifying the modern textbook treatment, and Mikhalkin-Rau (draft monograph, 2018-2024) [ref: TODO_REF MikhalkinRau] consolidating the field into a single graduate reference. Mikhalkin's correspondence is the most influential single theorem in the development of tropical geometry, and it is the genealogical root of the modern enumerative-tropical-geometry program connecting classical algebraic geometry, mirror symmetry, and combinatorial-Hodge theory (Huh-Adiprasito-Katz 2018 on matroidal log-concavity follows the same lattice-Hodge-positivity arc).
