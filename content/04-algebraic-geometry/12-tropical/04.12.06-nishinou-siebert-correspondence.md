## Intuition [Beginner]

Some questions in algebraic geometry ask how many curves there are of a prescribed shape that pass through a prescribed number of points. The simplest version is the question of how many straight lines pass through two distinct points in the plane: exactly one. A harder version asks how many smooth conics pass through five points in general position: a single answer, five. Even harder: how many rational curves of degree $d$ in the plane pass through $3d - 1$ generic points. These numbers are the **Gromov-Witten invariants** of the plane, and they grow rapidly with $d$.

The Nishinou-Siebert correspondence theorem is the discovery that these counting problems have a faithful **tropical mirror**: every count of complex curves on a toric variety is matched by a count of certain combinatorial graphs in a piecewise-linear space, with the same answer on both sides. The complex curves live in a smooth complex variety. The tropical graphs live in a piecewise-linear space built out of the combinatorial skeleton of the toric variety. The miracle is that translating the question from the complex side to the tropical side reduces an analytic problem to a finite combinatorial one — and the finite answer agrees with the analytic answer exactly.

Mikhalkin discovered this miracle in 2005 for toric **surfaces**: the plane, the projective plane, Hirzebruch surfaces, weighted projective spaces in dimension two. The full picture for toric varieties in **arbitrary dimension** is the contribution of Nishinou and Siebert in 2006: their generalisation handles curves on a toric threefold, a toric fourfold, and so on, with the same precision the Mikhalkin theorem achieved in two dimensions. The key new technical input is **toric degenerations** — flat families of varieties in which the smooth toric variety is the generic fibre and a piecewise-linear combinatorial object is the central fibre.

The deeper reason the correspondence works is that a toric variety carries the combinatorial structure of its fan in its bones. Curves on the variety, in a limiting regime, behave like graphs that respect the fan. Track the right limit carefully — through log Gromov-Witten theory — and the count of complex curves becomes a count of these graphs. The Nishinou-Siebert theorem is the precise statement of this principle, and the bridge from enumerative algebraic geometry to mirror symmetry runs through it.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the projective plane $\mathbb{P}^2$ as the simplest toric surface, and count rational curves of degree $1$ through $2$ generic points. The answer on the complex side is the number of lines through two points: a single line, so the count is $1$.

Step 1. The fan and the degeneration. The fan of $\mathbb{P}^2$ has three rays in the lattice $\mathbb{Z}^2$ at $e_1$, $e_2$, $-e_1 - e_2$. A toric degeneration produces a one-parameter family with $\mathbb{P}^2$ as the generic fibre. The central fibre is the union of three toric strata glued along their common boundaries — the three projective lines that are the toric divisors.

Step 2. The dual intersection complex. The cell complex dual to the central fibre is the **standard simplex** of dimension 2: a triangle in the plane, with three vertices, three edges, one two-dimensional cell. Tropical curves in this complex are balanced graphs in the triangle.

Step 3. The tropical count. A tropical line through two generic points of the triangle is a single trivalent vertex with three unbounded legs in directions $e_1$, $e_2$, $-e_1 - e_2$, positioned so that the two finite edges connecting the vertex to the two marked points have matching lengths. The Mikhalkin multiplicity at the trivalent vertex is $|\det(e_1, e_2)| = 1$. There is exactly one such configuration. The tropical count: $1$.

What this tells us: the two sides match. The classical count of $1$ line through $2$ points is recovered as the tropical count of $1$ balanced trivalent graph through $2$ tropical points in the triangle. This is the Mikhalkin / Nishinou-Siebert correspondence at its smallest possible example. The same translation procedure works for any toric variety, any genus, any class — and produces substantive answers reflecting the deep arithmetic of complex enumerative geometry.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a lattice $N \cong \mathbb{Z}^n$ of rank $n$ with dual $M = \mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(N, \mathbb{Z})$, and a fan $\Sigma$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$ in the sense of `[04.11.04]`. Write $X_\Sigma$ for the associated toric variety over $\mathbb{C}$, and $T = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M] \cong (\mathbb{C}^*)^n$ for the dense torus.

**Definition (toric degeneration).** A **toric degeneration** of $X_\Sigma$ is a flat morphism of $\mathbb{C}$-schemes
$$
\pi : \mathcal{X} \longrightarrow \mathbb{A}^1_\mathbb{C}
$$
together with a polyhedral subdivision $\Xi$ of a lattice polytope $\Delta \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ associated to a line bundle on $X_\Sigma$, satisfying:

(i) The generic fibre $\mathcal{X}_t$ for $t \neq 0$ is isomorphic to $X_\Sigma$.

(ii) The central fibre $\mathcal{X}_0$ is a reduced union $\bigcup_{\Delta_i \in \Xi} X_{\Delta_i}$ of irreducible toric strata, where each $X_{\Delta_i}$ is the toric variety whose Newton polytope is the polyhedral cell $\Delta_i$.

(iii) The total space $\mathcal{X}$ carries a torus action of $T$ extending the action on the generic fibre, and $\pi$ is $T$-equivariant where $T$ acts by the identity on $\mathbb{A}^1$.

(iv) The intersection pattern of the toric strata of $\mathcal{X}_0$ is recorded by the polyhedral subdivision $\Xi$: two strata $X_{\Delta_i}, X_{\Delta_j}$ intersect along the toric stratum corresponding to the polyhedral face $\Delta_i \cap \Delta_j$.

**Definition (dual intersection complex).** The **dual intersection complex** $B(\Xi)$ of the polyhedral subdivision $\Xi$ is the integral affine manifold (with singularities along codimension-2 cells) obtained as the cone complex dual to $\Xi$:
$$
B(\Xi) := \mathrm{Cone}(\Xi)^\vee = \bigcup_{\Delta_i \in \Xi} (\sigma_{\Delta_i}),
$$
where $\sigma_{\Delta_i} \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ is the cone in $N_\mathbb{R}$ dual to the cell $\Delta_i$, and the cones are glued along the dual incidences of the subdivision. The result is a polyhedral $n$-dimensional cell complex carrying integral affine charts on its top-dimensional cells.

**Definition (parametrised tropical curve).** A **parametrised tropical curve** of genus $g$ in $B(\Xi)$ with $r$ marked legs is a pair $(\Gamma, h)$ where:

(a) $\Gamma$ is a connected metric graph with first Betti number $b_1(\Gamma) = g$, a set $L(\Gamma)$ of $r$ marked unbounded legs (rays going to infinity), and a positive real edge length $\ell(e) \in \mathbb{R}_{> 0} \cup \{\infty\}$ on every edge (finite for internal edges, infinite for legs).

(b) $h : \Gamma \to B(\Xi)$ is a continuous map taking each edge to an integral affine segment in some top-dimensional cell of $B(\Xi)$, with primitive integer **direction vector** $u_{e, v} \in N$ at the endpoint $v$ of every edge $e$.

(c) **Balancing condition.** At every internal vertex $v \in V(\Gamma)$, the sum of the direction vectors of the edges incident at $v$ vanishes:
$$
\sum_{e \ni v} u_{e, v} \;=\; 0 \;\in\; N.
$$

(d) **Class data.** The unbounded leg directions $(u_{e, \infty})_{e \in L(\Gamma)}$ are prescribed: their multiset records the **tropical class** $\Delta_{\text{trop}}(\Gamma, h)$.

**Definition (Nishinou-Siebert multiplicity).** The **multiplicity** of a parametrised tropical curve $(\Gamma, h)$ at a trivalent vertex $v$ with edge direction vectors $u_1, u_2, u_3 \in N$ is the absolute value of the **lattice determinant** of any two of the three vectors:
$$
m_v(\Gamma, h) \;:=\; |\det(u_i, u_j)| \;\in\; \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}.
$$
By the balancing relation $u_1 + u_2 + u_3 = 0$, this is independent of the chosen pair $\{i, j\} \subseteq \{1, 2, 3\}$. For higher-valence vertices in dimension $\geq 3$, the multiplicity is the Nishinou-Siebert higher-determinantal formula: a product of subdeterminants weighted by the combinatorial structure of the vertex; see §3 of the Master tier. The **multiplicity of the curve** $(\Gamma, h)$ is the product over its trivalent (or higher-valence) vertices:
$$
m(\Gamma, h) \;:=\; \prod_{v \in V(\Gamma)} m_v(\Gamma, h).
$$

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *The polyhedral subdivision $\Xi$ is not the fan $\Sigma$.* These are dual objects. The fan $\Sigma$ lives in $N_\mathbb{R}$ and encodes the affine charts of $X_\Sigma$; the polyhedral subdivision $\Xi$ lives in $M_\mathbb{R}$ and refines the polytope associated to a chosen line bundle on $X_\Sigma$. The dual intersection complex $B(\Xi)$ recovers the fan-like polyhedral structure but in the dual $N$ lattice.

- *Tropical curves are not embedded tropical objects.* A parametrised tropical curve is a map $h : \Gamma \to B(\Xi)$, not a subset of $B(\Xi)$. Two distinct parametrised tropical curves can have the same image but differ in their domain graphs $\Gamma$, edge multiplicities, or vertex weights. The enumeration counts maps, not images, exactly as Gromov-Witten counts pointed maps from curves to a target variety rather than embedded subschemes.

- *Multiplicity is not a binary "real / not real" attribute.* The Nishinou-Siebert (and Mikhalkin) multiplicity is a positive integer typically larger than $1$, recording the local algebraic multiplicity of the corresponding family of complex curves. Tropical curves of multiplicity $5$ correspond to $5$ distinct complex curves degenerating to the same combinatorial graph in the family limit; the count is honest enumeration weighted by these collisions.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Nishinou-Siebert correspondence; Nishinou-Siebert 2006).** *Let $X_\Sigma$ be a smooth projective toric variety of dimension $n$ over $\mathbb{C}$, let $\pi : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{A}^1$ be a toric degeneration of $X_\Sigma$ with central fibre corresponding to a polyhedral subdivision $\Xi$ of an associated lattice polytope, let $g \geq 0$ be a non-negative integer, and let $\Delta \in H_2(X_\Sigma, \mathbb{Z})$ be an effective curve class. Let $r$ be the expected dimension of the moduli space of genus-$g$ stable maps to $X_\Sigma$ of class $\Delta$. Then for $r$ generic points $p_1, \ldots, p_r \in X_\Sigma$ and $r$ tropically generic points $\bar{p}_1, \ldots, \bar{p}_r \in B(\Xi)$:*
$$
N_{g, \Delta}(X_\Sigma) \;=\; \sum_{(\Gamma, h)} m(\Gamma, h) \;=\; N^{\text{trop}}_{g, \Delta}(\Xi),
$$
*where the algebraic count $N_{g, \Delta}(X_\Sigma)$ enumerates genus-$g$ stable maps $f : C \to X_\Sigma$ of class $\Delta$ with $f(C)$ passing through every $p_i$, and the tropical count enumerates parametrised tropical curves $(\Gamma, h)$ in $B(\Xi)$ of the same genus and class data, with $h(\Gamma)$ passing through every $\bar{p}_i$, weighted by Nishinou-Siebert multiplicity.*

**Proof.** The proof factors into four steps. We describe each in the intermediate-tier version; the Master tier elaborates the log Gromov-Witten foundations.

*Step 1 (algebraic side as log GW count).* The expected dimension $r = \dim \overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g, 0}(X_\Sigma, \Delta) = (\dim X_\Sigma - 3)(1 - g) + \int_\Delta c_1(T X_\Sigma) + n_{\text{markings}}$ matches the codimension condition imposed by the $r$ point constraints, so the algebraic count is the integral of the constraint class against the virtual fundamental class $[\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g, r}(X_\Sigma, \Delta)]^{\text{vir}}$:
$$
N_{g, \Delta}(X_\Sigma) \;=\; \int_{[\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g, r}(X_\Sigma, \Delta)]^{\text{vir}}} \prod_{i = 1}^r \mathrm{ev}_i^*[\mathrm{pt}].
$$
This is the standard definition of a Gromov-Witten invariant. For toric varieties with the symmetric obstruction theory available in the projective case, the virtual count is finite and integral.

*Step 2 (degeneration to log smooth central fibre).* The toric degeneration $\pi : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{A}^1$ has central fibre $\mathcal{X}_0 = \bigcup X_{\Delta_i}$, which is log smooth in the sense of Kato — the singular locus is the codimension-$1$ stratum of toric divisors, and the natural log structure on $\mathcal{X}$ makes the family log smooth. The **log Gromov-Witten invariants** of Abramovich-Chen (2014 *Asian J. Math.* 18; *Annals* 180) and Gross-Siebert (2013 *J. AMS* 26) extend Gromov-Witten counts to log smooth targets and prove a **degeneration formula**: the GW invariant of the smooth generic fibre equals the sum of log GW invariants of the central fibre weighted by combinatorial gluing data. Explicitly:
$$
N_{g, \Delta}(X_\Sigma) \;=\; \sum_{\text{log curves on }\mathcal{X}_0} N^{\log}_{g, \Delta}(\mathcal{X}_0, \text{combinatorial data}).
$$
Each summand on the right is the log GW count of a stable log map $f : (C, M_C) \to (\mathcal{X}_0, M_{\mathcal{X}_0})$ from a log smooth curve to the log smooth central fibre.

*Step 3 (tropicalisation of log smooth central-fibre curves).* The **tropicalisation map** takes a log smooth stable log map $(C, M_C) \to (\mathcal{X}_0, M_{\mathcal{X}_0})$ to a parametrised tropical curve $(\Gamma_C, h_C)$ in $B(\Xi)$. The construction:

(a) The graph $\Gamma_C$ is the dual graph of the central-fibre curve $C \subseteq \mathcal{X}_0$, with one vertex per irreducible component and one edge per nodal intersection point. The leg structure is determined by the marked points on $C$.

(b) Each irreducible component $C_i \subseteq C$ maps to one toric stratum $X_{\Delta_j}$, and the **balancing condition** at each vertex of $\Gamma_C$ records the algebraic intersection numbers of $C_i$ with the codimension-1 toric divisors of $X_{\Delta_j}$.

(c) The map $h_C : \Gamma_C \to B(\Xi)$ sends each vertex to the dual-cone point representing its irreducible component, and each edge to the dual segment between adjacent strata; the direction vector along an edge encodes the local intersection multiplicity.

The tropicalisation is balanced by the **toric residue theorem**: the divisor class of $C_i$ in $X_{\Delta_j}$ is principal in its top homology (since irreducible toric components are simply connected), forcing the sum of intersection numbers with toric divisors to vanish — which is exactly the balancing condition on $\Gamma_C$.

*Step 4 (local multiplicity match).* For each parametrised tropical curve $(\Gamma, h)$ in $B(\Xi)$, the inverse tropicalisation produces a finite set of log smooth central-fibre curves whose tropicalisation is $(\Gamma, h)$. The cardinality of this set is the **Nishinou-Siebert multiplicity** $m(\Gamma, h)$ defined above. The local-multiplicity computation reduces, by a coordinate change to the toric chart of each cell, to a Mikhalkin-style lattice-determinant calculation:
$$
m_v(\Gamma, h) \;=\; |\det(u_1, u_2)|, \qquad \text{at trivalent vertex } v \text{ with edge directions } u_1, u_2, u_3, u_1 + u_2 + u_3 = 0.
$$
For higher-valence vertices in dimension $\geq 3$, the multiplicity is the absolute value of a higher Plücker determinant, expressing the local lattice volume change of a polyhedral cone subdivision.

Combining the four steps:
$$
N_{g, \Delta}(X_\Sigma) \;\stackrel{\text{Step 2}}{=}\; \sum_{(C, M_C)} N^{\log}_{g, \Delta} \;\stackrel{\text{Step 3}}{=}\; \sum_{(\Gamma, h)} m(\Gamma, h) \;=\; N^{\text{trop}}_{g, \Delta}(\Xi).
$$
The middle equality records the bijection (with multiplicity) between log smooth central-fibre curves and parametrised tropical curves in $B(\Xi)$, established by Nishinou-Siebert 2006 §4-§8. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Nishinou-Siebert correspondence builds toward `[04.12.07]` toric degenerations of Calabi-Yau varieties, where the same degeneration mechanism produces the Gross-Siebert mirror construction, and appears again in `[04.12.09]` Gross-Siebert reconstruction theorem, where tropical curves are precisely the perturbative data assembled into scattering diagrams. The foundational reason is that the log Gromov-Witten theory of Abramovich-Chen / Gross-Siebert provides a degeneration-stable enumerative count, and this is exactly the bridge between the smooth enumerative invariants of $X_\Sigma$ and the combinatorial enumerative invariants of the dual intersection complex $B(\Xi)$.

Putting these together identifies the enumerative content of toric Gromov-Witten theory with the combinatorial enumeration of balanced tropical graphs, and this identification is the foundational pattern: the same mechanism generalises in `[04.12.15]` log Gromov-Witten invariants to non-toric log smooth targets, in `[04.12.10]` Strominger-Yau-Zaslow conjecture to special-Lagrangian fibrations, and in `[04.12.12]` theta functions to the construction of canonical bases on Calabi-Yau varieties. The Mikhalkin 2005 surface case of `[04.12.05]` is the $n = 2$ specialisation; the Nishinou-Siebert generalisation lifts every aspect of the surface picture to arbitrary dimension.

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

The moduli space $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g, m}(X_\Sigma, \Delta)$ of stable maps of genus $g$ and class $\Delta$ with $m$ marked points has expected (= virtual) dimension
$$
\dim^{\text{vir}} = (1 - g)(\dim X_\Sigma - 3) + \int_\Delta c_1(T X_\Sigma) + m
$$
by the deformation theory of stable maps. Each point constraint $f(p_i) = p_i \in X_\Sigma$ imposes a codimension-$\dim X_\Sigma$ condition on the evaluation map $\mathrm{ev}_i : \overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g, m}(X_\Sigma, \Delta) \to X_\Sigma$. Imposing $r := \dim^{\text{vir}}|_{m = 0} = (1 - g)(\dim X_\Sigma - 3) + \int_\Delta c_1(T X_\Sigma)$ point constraints — distributed over $r$ marked points, hence $m = r$ — gives total codimension $r \cdot \dim X_\Sigma + r$, which exceeds the virtual dimension when $\dim X_\Sigma > 1$. The constraint class therefore intersects the virtual fundamental class in dimension $0$, yielding a finite integer count.

## Toric degenerations and the dual intersection complex [Master]

The technical heart of Nishinou-Siebert is the construction of an explicit toric degeneration of the smooth target $X_\Sigma$ to a piecewise-linear central fibre, and the construction of the dual intersection complex $B(\Xi)$ as the combinatorial home for tropical curves. We make the construction explicit in three layers.

**Layer 1 — the polytope and its subdivision.** A smooth projective toric variety $X_\Sigma$ together with a $T$-equivariant ample line bundle $L$ corresponds, by the polytope-fan dictionary [ref: TODO_REF Nishinou-Siebert 2006] (cf. `[04.11.10]`), to a lattice polytope $\Delta \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$. The dimension of $\Delta$ equals the dimension of $X_\Sigma$, and the integer points $\Delta \cap M$ are the weights of the $T$-representation on $H^0(X_\Sigma, L)$. A **polyhedral subdivision** $\Xi$ of $\Delta$ is a partition of $\Delta$ into finitely many lattice polytopes $\Delta_i$ meeting along common faces, with vertices in $M$. Subdivisions arise from concave piecewise-linear functions $\varphi : \Delta \to \mathbb{R}$ taking integer values at lattice points — the **Mumford / Newton-Okounkov function** — via the lower-convex-hull construction: the cells $\Delta_i$ are the projections to $M_\mathbb{R}$ of the maximal faces of the lower envelope of $\{(m, \varphi(m)) : m \in \Delta \cap M\}$.

**Layer 2 — the degeneration $\pi : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{A}^1$.** Given $(\Delta, \varphi)$, the polynomial ring $\mathbb{C}[\Delta \cap M][t]$ is graded by lifting each monomial $\chi^m$ to weight $\varphi(m)$ in $t$:
$$
\widetilde{\chi^m} \;:=\; t^{\varphi(m)} \chi^m \;\in\; \mathbb{C}[\Delta \cap M, t].
$$
The relations defining $X_\Sigma$ as a closed subscheme of $\mathbb{P}^{|\Delta \cap M| - 1}$ — given by lattice-relation binomials $\chi^{m_1 + m_2} = \chi^{m_3 + m_4}$ for $m_1 + m_2 = m_3 + m_4$ in $\Delta \cap M$ — lift to binomials $t^{\varphi(m_1) + \varphi(m_2)} \widetilde{\chi^{m_1}} \widetilde{\chi^{m_2}} = t^{\varphi(m_3) + \varphi(m_4)} \widetilde{\chi^{m_3}} \widetilde{\chi^{m_4}}$, defining the total space $\mathcal{X} \subseteq \mathbb{P}^{|\Delta \cap M| - 1}_{\mathbb{A}^1} \to \mathbb{A}^1$.

The generic fibre $\mathcal{X}_t$ for $t \neq 0$ is isomorphic to $X_\Sigma$: setting $t = 1$ recovers the original relations. The central fibre $\mathcal{X}_0$ is a reducible toric variety: setting $t = 0$ in the lifted relations kills the terms with $\varphi(m_1) + \varphi(m_2) > \varphi(m_3) + \varphi(m_4)$, breaking the global toric relations into local relations valid only on individual cells of $\Xi$. The result is that $\mathcal{X}_0 = \bigcup_{\Delta_i} X_{\Delta_i}$, with each $X_{\Delta_i}$ the toric variety of the polytope $\Delta_i$ and the union glued along the common toric strata corresponding to the polyhedral faces.

**Layer 3 — the dual intersection complex and its integral affine structure.** The **dual intersection complex** $B(\Xi)$ is built directly from the combinatorics of $\Xi$. For each cell $\Delta_i \subseteq \Xi$, the dual cone $\sigma_{\Delta_i}^* := \{u \in N_\mathbb{R} : \langle u, m\rangle \geq \langle u, m'\rangle \text{ for all } m, m' \in \Delta_i\}$ is the cone of linear functionals constant on $\Delta_i$; this is a cone of dimension $n - \dim \Delta_i$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$. The dual intersection complex glues these cones according to the face incidences of $\Xi$:
$$
B(\Xi) \;:=\; \bigcup_{\Delta_i \in \Xi} \sigma_{\Delta_i}^* \;/\; \sim,
$$
where $\sim$ identifies $\sigma_{\Delta_i}^*$ with $\sigma_{\Delta_j}^*$ along the dual of the face $\Delta_i \cap \Delta_j$ whenever $\Delta_i$ and $\Delta_j$ share a face.

The complex $B(\Xi)$ inherits an **integral affine structure** on each top-dimensional cell: the lattice $N \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ restricts to a lattice on each dual cone $\sigma_{\Delta_i}^*$, and the gluings respect lattice identifications along common faces. The integral affine structure has **monodromy** along the codimension-$2$ singular locus of $B(\Xi)$ — closed faces of cells where the local affine transition matrices generate a non-identity element of $\mathrm{GL}(N) = \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z})$. The monodromy is exactly the obstruction to gluing the local affine charts of $B(\Xi)$ into a global integral affine manifold; in the Calabi-Yau case studied by Gross-Siebert it encodes the topological mirror symmetry information.

## Log Gromov-Witten theory and tropical-to-log degeneration [Master]

The technical bridge from the smooth algebraic side to the tropical combinatorial side is the **log Gromov-Witten theory** of Abramovich-Chen and Gross-Siebert. We describe the package, its degeneration formula, and the tropicalisation construction.

**Log smooth schemes and log smooth morphisms.** A **fine saturated (fs) log structure** on a scheme $X$ is an étale sheaf of monoids $M_X$ together with a homomorphism $\alpha : M_X \to (\mathcal{O}_X, \cdot)$ inducing $M_X^{\times} \cong \mathcal{O}_X^{\times}$. A scheme with log structure is **log smooth** over a base if it is étale-locally isomorphic to a chart $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{Z}[P]$ for a fine saturated monoid $P$, with the natural log structure $P \hookrightarrow \mathbb{Z}[P]$. The classic examples are: a smooth scheme with the identity log structure; a smooth variety with a normal-crossings divisor and the divisorial log structure; the central fibre $\mathcal{X}_0$ of a toric degeneration with its log structure induced from the inclusion in the total space $\mathcal{X}$.

**Stable log maps.** A **stable log map** $f : (C, M_C) \to (X, M_X)$ is a morphism of log smooth schemes from a log smooth curve $C$ (a nodal curve with log structure encoding the nodes as smoothing data) to a log smooth target $X$, satisfying a stability condition analogous to the ordinary Deligne-Mumford stability. The Abramovich-Chen and Gross-Siebert constructions both produce a proper Deligne-Mumford moduli stack
$$
\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g, m}^{\log}(X / S, \Delta)
$$
of stable log maps of fixed genus $g$, marking number $m$, and combinatorial class data $\Delta$, with a virtual fundamental class of expected dimension. The Abramovich-Chen-Marcus-Wise comparison theorem (*Asian J. Math.* 2017) proves that the two constructions agree.

**Log GW invariants and the degeneration formula.** For $X \to S$ log smooth proper with $S = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}$, the integral of cohomology classes on $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g, m}^{\log}(X, \Delta)$ against the virtual class defines the **log Gromov-Witten invariants** $N^{\log}_{g, \Delta}(X)$. The degeneration formula for log GW says: for a one-parameter degeneration $\pi : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{A}^1$ with smooth generic fibre $\mathcal{X}_t$ and log smooth central fibre $\mathcal{X}_0$, and curve-class data deforming compatibly, the ordinary GW invariant of $\mathcal{X}_t$ equals the log GW invariant of $\mathcal{X}_0$ summed over combinatorial type classes [ref: TODO_REF Gross-Siebert 2013]:
$$
N_{g, \Delta}(\mathcal{X}_t) \;=\; \sum_{\text{type } \tau} N^{\log, \tau}_{g, \Delta}(\mathcal{X}_0).
$$
The combinatorial type $\tau$ records the dual graph of the central-fibre curve, the irreducible component each piece maps to, and the tangency profile at the boundary divisors.

**Tropicalisation.** The **tropicalisation** of a stable log map $f : (C, M_C) \to (\mathcal{X}_0, M_{\mathcal{X}_0})$ is a parametrised tropical curve $(\Gamma_f, h_f)$ in the dual intersection complex $B(\Xi)$:
- **Vertices.** One vertex per irreducible component $C_i$ of $C$, located at the dual point of the toric stratum $X_{\Delta_j(i)}$ in $B(\Xi)$ to which $C_i$ maps.
- **Edges.** One edge per node of $C$, connecting the vertices of the two components meeting at the node. The edge direction $u_{e, v} \in N$ at the endpoint $v$ is the primitive integer vector recording the contact order of $C_i$ at the node.
- **Legs.** One unbounded leg per marked point of $C$, in the direction of the contact order with the appropriate toric divisor.
- **Balancing.** At each vertex, the sum of edge directions equals zero in $N$, as a consequence of the toric residue theorem applied to the toric component $X_{\Delta_j(i)}$.

The tropicalisation is functorial in the moduli problem: the moduli of stable log maps of type $\tau$ tropicalises onto the moduli of parametrised tropical curves of dual type $(\Gamma_\tau, h_\tau)$, and the log GW invariant of type $\tau$ equals the multiplicity $m(\Gamma_\tau, h_\tau)$ of the corresponding tropical curve.

## The Nishinou-Siebert theorem and dimension counts [Master]

The Nishinou-Siebert theorem combines the log Gromov-Witten degeneration formula with the tropicalisation map and the multiplicity formula into a clean enumerative statement. We state the precise dimension counts and multiplicity formula here.

**The dimension count.** For $X_\Sigma$ a smooth projective toric variety of complex dimension $n$ and curve-class $\Delta \in H_2(X_\Sigma, \mathbb{Z})$, the virtual dimension of the moduli of genus-$g$ stable maps with $m$ markings is
$$
\dim^{\text{vir}} \overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g, m}(X_\Sigma, \Delta) \;=\; (1 - g)(n - 3) + \langle c_1(T X_\Sigma), \Delta\rangle + m.
$$
Setting $m = r$ and demanding zero net dimension after imposing $r$ point constraints (each codimension $n$ in the evaluation map):
$$
\dim^{\text{vir}} - r(n - 1) \;=\; 0 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad r \;=\; \frac{(1 - g)(n - 3) + \langle c_1, \Delta\rangle}{n - 2} \;\text{(adjusted by incidence convention)}.
$$
For $n = 2$ (Mikhalkin's case), $r = 3d + g - 1$ for $\Delta = d L$ on $\mathbb{P}^2$, recovering the classical count of $3d + g - 1$ point conditions for genus-$g$ plane curves of degree $d$. For $n \geq 3$, the dimension count is sensitive to the curvature class $\langle c_1, \Delta\rangle$ — equivalently, to how the curve class sits in $H_2(X_\Sigma, \mathbb{Z})$ relative to the canonical class.

**The Nishinou-Siebert multiplicity in arbitrary dimension.** At a $k$-valent vertex $v$ of a parametrised tropical curve $(\Gamma, h)$ in $B(\Xi)$ with edge direction vectors $u_1, \ldots, u_k \in N$, the Nishinou-Siebert multiplicity generalises the Mikhalkin lattice determinant:
$$
m_v(\Gamma, h) \;=\; \frac{|\langle \omega, [v]\rangle|}{\prod_i (\text{lattice length of edge } u_i)} \;\cdot\; \text{combinatorial factor},
$$
where $\omega \in \wedge^{k-1} N^\vee$ is a balancing-class form and $[v]$ is the local cycle class. In the trivalent case ($k = 3$) the formula reduces to $|\det(u_i, u_j)|$ for any pair $i, j$, recovering Mikhalkin. For $k = 4$ in dimension $n = 3$, the formula uses the Plücker coordinates of the $4$-tuple $(u_1, u_2, u_3, u_4)$ in the Grassmannian $\mathrm{Gr}(3, 4)$ and counts a weighted Plücker volume.

**The theorem (precise form).** *Let $X_\Sigma$ be a smooth projective toric variety of complex dimension $n$ over $\mathbb{C}$. Let $\pi : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{A}^1$ be a toric degeneration determined by a polyhedral subdivision $\Xi$ of an ample polytope $\Delta$. Let $g \geq 0$ and $\Delta \in H_2(X_\Sigma, \mathbb{Z})$ be a curve class with $r := (1 - g)(n - 3) + \langle c_1(T X_\Sigma), \Delta\rangle$ being the expected dimension. Then for $r$ generic points $p_1, \ldots, p_r \in X_\Sigma$ and corresponding tropically generic points $\bar{p}_1, \ldots, \bar{p}_r \in B(\Xi)$:*
$$
N_{g, \Delta}(X_\Sigma; p_1, \ldots, p_r) \;=\; \sum_{(\Gamma, h)} m(\Gamma, h),
$$
*the sum on the right being over all parametrised tropical curves $(\Gamma, h)$ in $B(\Xi)$ of genus $g$, tropical class $\Delta_{\mathrm{trop}}$ matched to $\Delta$, and image passing through every $\bar{p}_i$, weighted by the multiplicity formula above.*

**Witnesses in low dimension.** The theorem reproduces the classical Mikhalkin counts in dimension $2$ (e.g., $N_{0, 3}(\mathbb{P}^2) = 12$, $N_{0, 4}(\mathbb{P}^2) = 620$). In dimension $3$, the count $N_{0, 1}(\mathbb{P}^3) = 1$ recovers the classical fact that there is a unique line through $2$ generic points of $\mathbb{P}^3$, and the count $N_{0, 2}(\mathbb{P}^3)$ — rational conics through $5$ points — is recovered as a tropical count over three-dimensional balanced graphs in the dual intersection complex of $\mathbb{P}^3$.

**Synthesis.** The Nishinou-Siebert correspondence is the foundational reason that enumerative toric Gromov-Witten theory has a faithful combinatorial mirror, and the central insight is that toric degenerations carry the smooth enumerative problem to a log smooth central fibre whose log Gromov-Witten count is computable by direct combinatorial enumeration in the dual intersection complex. The bridge is the tropicalisation map — a piecewise-linear functor from log smooth moduli to parametrised tropical curves — and the multiplicity formula identifies the algebraic local multiplicity with the lattice-determinantal weight at each combinatorial vertex.

Putting these together generalises Mikhalkin's 2005 surface theorem to arbitrary dimension and identifies the tropical counts with the wall-crossing data of the Gross-Siebert mirror reconstruction; this pattern recurs in `[04.12.07]` toric Calabi-Yau degenerations, in `[04.12.10]` Strominger-Yau-Zaslow conjecture, and in `[04.12.15]` log Gromov-Witten invariants. The correspondence pattern generalises to arbitrary log smooth targets (Gross-Siebert programme), and the resulting log GW theory builds toward an enumerative-combinatorial dictionary that is one of the most active research currents in algebraic geometry.

## Mirror symmetry application via Gross-Siebert [Master]

The Nishinou-Siebert correspondence is not only an enumerative result; it is the analytic-algebraic input to the **Gross-Siebert programme** for constructing mirror Calabi-Yau pairs. We describe the role here, with attention to the scattering-diagram mechanism.

**The Strominger-Yau-Zaslow heuristic.** Strominger-Yau-Zaslow 1996 (*Nuclear Physics B* 479) proposed that mirror Calabi-Yau pairs $(X, X^\vee)$ arise as dual special-Lagrangian torus fibrations: $X \to B$ and $X^\vee \to B$ over the same base $B$, with the fibres of one fibration being the dual tori of the other. The base $B$ carries an integral affine structure with singularities — exactly the structure of a dual intersection complex of a toric degeneration. The SYZ heuristic motivates the search for an algebraic realisation in which $B$ is constructed combinatorially and the smooth Calabi-Yau pair is recovered by a deformation procedure.

**The Gross-Siebert programme.** Gross-Siebert 2011 (*Annals of Math.* 174) provides the algebraic realisation. Starting from an integral affine manifold with singularities $B$ — the dual intersection complex of a toric degeneration of a degenerate Calabi-Yau $\mathcal{X}_0$ — the programme constructs a smoothing $\mathcal{X}_t$ to a one-parameter family whose generic fibre $\mathcal{X}_t^\vee$ is the mirror Calabi-Yau. The smoothing is constructed order-by-order in the deformation parameter $t$:
1. At each order $k$, the central fibre $\mathcal{X}_0$ admits a candidate deformation $\mathcal{X}_0^{(k)}$ over $\mathbb{C}[t]/(t^{k + 1})$.
2. The obstruction to extending to order $k + 1$ is encoded in **walls** of a **scattering diagram** on $B$ — codimension-$1$ subsets of $B$ where the local trivialisations of $\mathcal{X}_0^{(k)}$ disagree.
3. The scattering diagram is consistent — meaning every closed loop in $B$ produces the identity wall-crossing automorphism — if and only if the wall-crossing automorphisms can be **balanced** by additional walls accounting for the curve contributions.
4. The additional walls are exactly the **rays of tropical curves** in $B$ enumerated by the Nishinou-Siebert correspondence, with each tropical curve contributing a wall whose wall-crossing automorphism is determined by its Nishinou-Siebert multiplicity.

**Theta functions and canonical bases.** A consequence of the consistency of the scattering diagram is the construction of **theta functions** $\vartheta_q$ indexed by integer points $q \in B(\mathbb{Z})$ — global sections of an ample line bundle on the smooth mirror $\mathcal{X}_t^\vee$. The theta functions form a canonical basis of the polarised section space, and their structure constants under multiplication encode the Nishinou-Siebert curve counts via an explicit formula:
$$
\vartheta_{q_1} \cdot \vartheta_{q_2} \;=\; \sum_{q_3, (\Gamma, h)} m(\Gamma, h) \cdot t^{\text{tropical area}(\Gamma)} \cdot \vartheta_{q_3},
$$
where the sum is over tropical curves $(\Gamma, h)$ with three marked legs in directions determined by $q_1, q_2, q_3$. This is the **structure-constant formula** of Gross-Hacking-Keel-Kontsevich [ref: TODO_REF Gross-Siebert 2011], and it makes the link between Nishinou-Siebert curve counting and mirror-symmetric Calabi-Yau geometry maximally explicit.

**Synthesis of the application.** The Nishinou-Siebert correspondence supplies the integer counts that govern the Gross-Siebert reconstruction; equivalently, the wall-crossing scattering diagram on $B$ is built from the tropical-curve enumeration of $B$. This is the bridge between enumerative algebraic geometry — counting curves on a toric variety — and the construction of mirror Calabi-Yau pairs — building the smooth dual Calabi-Yau from combinatorial-tropical data. The same pattern recurs in `[04.12.10]` SYZ conjecture, in `[04.12.09]` Gross-Siebert reconstruction theorem statement, in `[04.12.11]` slab functions and tropical-manifold structure, and in `[04.12.12]` theta functions on polarised tropical manifolds. The Nishinou-Siebert correspondence is the foundational structural input to the entire programme.

## Connections [Master]

- **Mikhalkin's correspondence theorem [04.12.05].** The Nishinou-Siebert correspondence is the higher-dimensional generalisation of Mikhalkin's 2005 theorem for toric surfaces. The Mikhalkin case is recovered as the dimension-$2$ specialisation: when $\Sigma$ is a fan in $\mathbb{R}^2$, the dual intersection complex $B(\Xi)$ is two-dimensional and the Nishinou-Siebert multiplicity formula at each trivalent vertex reduces to $|\det(u_i, u_j)|$ — the original Mikhalkin lattice multiplicity. The Nishinou-Siebert lift uses toric degenerations instead of Mikhalkin's complex tropicalisation argument, and the new toolkit extends seamlessly to dimension $n \geq 3$.

- **Fan and the toric variety [04.11.04].** The Nishinou-Siebert setup is built on the cone-and-fan formalism of `[04.11.04]`. The toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is constructed from the fan $\Sigma$; the polyhedral subdivision $\Xi$ refines the polytope associated to a line bundle on $X_\Sigma$; the dual intersection complex $B(\Xi)$ is the cone complex dual to $\Xi$. The toric structure of $X_\Sigma$ is what makes the tropical correspondence possible — non-toric targets require the more general Gross-Siebert log GW framework but do not admit the clean Nishinou-Siebert reduction.

- **Gross-Siebert reconstruction theorem [04.12.09].** The mirror-symmetry application appears again in `[04.12.09]` Gross-Siebert reconstruction theorem statement, where the Nishinou-Siebert tropical curves are exactly the wall-crossing data assembling the smooth mirror Calabi-Yau from the dual intersection complex. The two units pair: `[04.12.06]` proves the curve-counting correspondence, `[04.12.09]` consumes it as input to mirror construction.

- **Strominger-Yau-Zaslow conjecture [04.12.10].** The SYZ heuristic of dual special-Lagrangian torus fibrations is the physics-motivated picture behind the Nishinou-Siebert / Gross-Siebert programme. The dual intersection complex $B(\Xi)$ is the algebraic realisation of the SYZ base $B$, and the Nishinou-Siebert correspondence is the algebraic mechanism by which the SYZ heuristic descends to a precise enumerative-combinatorial theorem on the base.

- **Log Gromov-Witten invariants (pointer) [04.12.15].** The log Gromov-Witten foundations of Abramovich-Chen 2014 and Gross-Siebert 2013 are the technical bridge from algebraic enumeration to tropical enumeration. The Nishinou-Siebert proof uses log GW theory essentially: ordinary GW theory cannot handle the singular central fibre of the toric degeneration. The pointer unit `[04.12.15]` records the log GW infrastructure that Nishinou-Siebert applies.

- **Tropical semiring and tropical polynomial [04.12.01].** The min-plus arithmetic of the foundational tropical-semiring unit is the algebraic substrate of the Nishinou-Siebert correspondence: the polyhedral subdivision $\Xi$ from which the dual intersection complex is built is itself a regular subdivision of a Newton polytope induced by the lower hull of a tropical polynomial $p \in \mathbb{T}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$, and the integer-slope structure of $p$ determines the integer multiplicities at vertices of the embedded tropical curves on $B(\Xi)$. The corner-locus formalism of the foundational unit generalises in this setup to the dual-intersection-complex setting, with vertices of $B(\Xi)$ playing the role of corners.

- **Tropical curve as balanced rational metric graph [04.12.02].** The Nishinou-Siebert tropical curves on the dual intersection complex $B(\Xi)$ are balanced rational metric graphs in the precise sense of the foundational unit, with edge weights given by integer multiplicities and the balancing condition holding at every vertex when read against primitive edge directions in the integral affine structure on $B(\Xi)$. The Mikhalkin trivalent-vertex multiplicity $|\det(u_i, u_j)|$ recovered as the dimension-$2$ specialisation of the Nishinou-Siebert vertex-multiplicity formula is exactly the lattice-multiplicity datum carried by the balanced metric graphs of the prerequisite unit. The metric-graph framework supplies the abstract combinatorial object; the dual intersection complex supplies the polyhedral ambient.

- **Kapranov's theorem [04.12.03].** Kapranov's foundational equivalence between algebraic and tropical hypersurfaces is the structural input that makes the Nishinou-Siebert lift well-defined: tropical curves on $B(\Xi)$ are valuation-images of algebraic curves on the generic fibre of the toric degeneration, and the Hensel-lift construction at each tropical vertex inverts the valuation map locally via the residue-tropical structure. Without Kapranov's theorem, the Nishinou-Siebert count would be a combinatorial heuristic; with it, the tropical-to-algebraic lift is an exact equivalence at the level of stable maps.

- **Newton polytope and non-archimedean amoeba [04.12.04].** The polytope subdivision $\Xi$ entering the Nishinou-Siebert construction is a regular subdivision of a polytope of an ample line bundle on the toric variety $X_\Sigma$ — exactly the Newton-polytope subdivision formalism of the prerequisite unit specialised to a global polytope. The non-archimedean amoeba framework of the prerequisite unit provides the dilation-limit geometry that the toric degeneration realises scheme-theoretically: the special fibre of $\mathfrak{X} \to \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}\{\{t\}\}$ is the limiting amoeba of the generic fibre, and the dual intersection complex $B(\Xi)$ is its tropical skeleton.

- **Moduli of curves [04.10.01].** The Nishinou-Siebert correspondence is implemented in the log Gromov-Witten moduli space $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g,n}^{\log}(\mathfrak{X}, \beta)$ — a log-enhancement of the moduli-of-curves space from the prerequisite unit. The moduli-of-stable-maps construction (curve plus marked points plus map to target) generalises here to log-smooth families of pointed curves with toric-degeneration target, and the tropical-to-algebraic lift of the present unit is the combinatorial recursion on this enhanced moduli space. The dimension count of the prerequisite unit is the foundational reason Nishinou-Siebert dimension counts come out integer: at each tropical vertex, the contribution to the virtual fundamental class of $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g,n}^{\log}$ is a lattice index, matched against the local moduli-dimension of stable maps at that vertex.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The Nishinou-Siebert correspondence sits at the convergence of three twentieth-century mathematical threads. Tropical geometry as an autonomous subject was launched by Mikhalkin in the early 2000s, but its roots reach back to Bergman 1971 *J. Algebraic Combinatorics* (tropical varieties as logarithmic limits), Sturmfels 1996 *Gröbner Bases and Convex Polytopes* (algebraic-combinatorial duality), and the older Bieri-Groves 1984 *J. Reine Angew. Math.* construction of tropical varieties as polyhedral complexes [ref: TODO_REF Bieri-Groves 1984]. Mikhalkin's 2005 *J. AMS* 18 paper [ref: TODO_REF Mikhalkin 2005] crystallised the field by proving the first correspondence theorem — relating complex enumerative invariants on toric surfaces to tropical enumeration — and won him the Clay Research Award in 2008.

The toric degeneration technology underpinning Nishinou-Siebert traces to Mumford 1972 *Compositio Mathematica* (the first explicit construction of degenerating Abelian varieties) and Kempf-Knudsen-Mumford-Saint-Donat 1973 *Toroidal Embeddings I*, which formalised the gluing of toric varieties from polyhedral subdivision data. The application of toric degenerations to enumerative geometry as a computational tool was developed in the 1990s and early 2000s by Sturmfels, Gathmann, and others. Tian-Symington 2003 connected toric degenerations to the Strominger-Yau-Zaslow heuristic in the smooth Lagrangian framework, providing a physics-side motivation parallel to the algebraic one.

The third thread is log Gromov-Witten theory itself. Kato's 1989 paper on logarithmic geometry [ref: TODO_REF Kato 1989] provided the basic framework of log smooth schemes, and Li-Ruan 2001 introduced the first version of relative Gromov-Witten theory adapted to degenerations. The full log GW edifice was constructed in parallel by Abramovich-Chen (*Asian J. Math.* 2014; Chen *Annals* 2014) and Gross-Siebert (*J. AMS* 2013) [ref: TODO_REF Gross-Siebert 2013], with the comparison theorem of Abramovich-Chen-Marcus-Wise (2017) showing the two constructions agree. Nishinou-Siebert 2006 [ref: TODO_REF Nishinou-Siebert 2006] is the precise instance where these three threads — tropical geometry, toric degenerations, log Gromov-Witten — converge into a single enumerative correspondence theorem in arbitrary dimension.

The Gross-Siebert programme that subsumes the Nishinou-Siebert correspondence into mirror symmetry was published as a sequence of papers between 2006 and the present; the foundational *Annals* 2011 paper [ref: TODO_REF Gross-Siebert 2011] proved the reconstruction theorem assembling a smoothing of a degenerate Calabi-Yau from real-affine data, and ongoing work by Gross, Hacking, Keel, Kontsevich, and collaborators extends the picture to log Calabi-Yau pairs, cluster algebras, and Fukaya category considerations. Gross's CBMS lecture notes 2011 [ref: TODO_REF Gross 2011 CBMS] provide the textbook synthesis of the Nishinou-Siebert correspondence in the broader mirror-symmetric context.
