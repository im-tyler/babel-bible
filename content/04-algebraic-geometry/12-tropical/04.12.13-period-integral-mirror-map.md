## Intuition [Beginner]

A Calabi-Yau variety has a special kind of volume form — a global holomorphic top-degree differential that is non-vanishing everywhere. When the Calabi-Yau sits in a one-parameter family $\mathfrak{X}_t$, the volume form $\Omega_t$ varies with the parameter $t$. The *period integrals* of $\Omega_t$ are the totals of this volume form across specific closed cycles in the variety, and they form the most refined record of how the geometry of $\mathfrak{X}_t$ changes as $t$ moves.

For mirror symmetry the periods are the central data. Candelas, de la Ossa, Green and Parkes discovered in 1991 that for the famous quintic Calabi-Yau threefold inside the projective space $\mathbb{P}^4$, the count of rational curves on the quintic at every degree can be read off from the period integrals of its *mirror partner* — a sister Calabi-Yau on which the periods are computable from a single differential equation called the *Picard-Fuchs equation*. This was the first published mathematical prediction of mirror symmetry, and the calculation continues to anchor every modern treatment of the subject.

The *mirror map* is the dictionary connecting the two sides. It is a change of coordinate $q = q(t)$ that takes the algebraic-geometric parameter $t$ (the parameter on the complex-structure side of the mirror pair) to the symplectic Kähler parameter (the parameter on the size-of-cycles side of the mirror pair). The mirror map turns out to be computable directly from the periods: it is the exponential of the ratio of two specific period integrals near the most degenerate limit of the family. This unit points to the result — the full development belongs to Hodge theory and to the Gross-Siebert reconstruction theorem — and explains the bridge from tropical data to period asymptotics.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The simplest substantive example is the family of *elliptic curves* parametrised by the Hesse pencil — the one-parameter family of cubic curves in $\mathbb{P}^2$ given by the equation $x^3 + y^3 + z^3 - 3 \psi \, xyz = 0$, where $\psi$ is the complex parameter and $t = (3 \psi)^{-3}$ is the local coordinate near the most degenerate limit. Each curve in the family is an elliptic curve for generic $\psi$, degenerating to a triangle of three projective lines at $\psi = 0$ (equivalently $t = \infty$) and to a triangle again at $\psi^3 = 1$ (the conifold limits) and a smooth elliptic curve elsewhere.

Step 1. The holomorphic differential. Each elliptic curve $\mathfrak{X}_t$ in the family carries a canonical non-vanishing holomorphic 1-form $\Omega_t$, expressible in affine coordinates as $\Omega_t = dx / (y^2 + (\text{correction in } t))$ up to normalisation. The 1-form varies holomorphically with $t$.

Step 2. The two period totals. The elliptic curve $\mathfrak{X}_t$ has two independent cycles $\gamma_0$ and $\gamma_1$ generating its first integer homology. The periods $\Pi_0(t)$ and $\Pi_1(t)$ are the totals of $\Omega_t$ accumulated as you sweep across $\gamma_0$ and $\gamma_1$ respectively. Near the most degenerate limit $t = 0$ the periods have the leading behaviour $\Pi_0(t) = 1 + O(t)$ (regular at $t = 0$) and $\Pi_1(t) = \Pi_0(t) \log(t) + (\text{regular})$ — one period is finite, the other has a logarithmic singularity.

Step 3. The mirror map. The mirror map is $q(t) = \exp(\Pi_1(t) / \Pi_0(t))$. Substituting the leading expansions gives $q(t) = \exp(\log t + O(t)) = t \cdot \exp(O(t)) = t \cdot (1 + O(t))$. The leading-order identity $q(t) = t + O(t^2)$ is what makes $q$ a valid coordinate near the degeneration. The higher-order corrections in this elliptic example are zero — the elliptic curve has no rational curves to count on its mirror — and the mirror map is the identity $q = t$ exactly. The example shows the mechanism without higher-order corrections.

What this tells us: the mirror map is read off from the asymptotics of two specific periods, and in higher-dimensional Calabi-Yau examples like the quintic threefold the higher-order corrections become non-zero and encode the count of rational curves on the mirror side.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a smooth flat proper one-parameter family $\pi : \mathfrak{X} \to S$ of Calabi-Yau varieties over a punctured disk $S = \Delta^*$ in $\mathbb{C}$, with relative dimension $n$. The Calabi-Yau condition is that the relative dualising sheaf $\omega_{\mathfrak{X}/S}$ is the identity sheaf on $\mathfrak{X}$.

**Definition (relative holomorphic volume form).** A **relative holomorphic volume form** on $\pi : \mathfrak{X} \to S$ is a global section $\Omega \in \Gamma(\mathfrak{X}, \omega_{\mathfrak{X}/S})$ that is non-vanishing on every fibre. Equivalently, $\Omega$ is a holomorphic section of $\Omega^n_{\mathfrak{X}/S}$ — the sheaf of relative top-degree holomorphic differentials — that restricts to a non-vanishing section on each fibre $\mathfrak{X}_t$. The Calabi-Yau condition guarantees that such an $\Omega$ exists and is unique up to multiplication by a nowhere-vanishing holomorphic function on $S$. For each $t \in S$ the restriction $\Omega_t := \Omega|_{\mathfrak{X}_t}$ is a non-vanishing holomorphic $n$-form on the fibre.

**Definition (period vector).** Let $\gamma_1, \ldots, \gamma_h \in H_n(\mathfrak{X}_{t_0}, \mathbb{Z})$ be a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of the middle-dimensional integer homology of a base fibre $\mathfrak{X}_{t_0}$. By the Gauss-Manin local system, the basis extends locally to a flat family $\gamma_i(t) \in H_n(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{Z})$ for $t$ near $t_0$. The **period vector** is
$$
\Pi(t) := \bigl(\Pi_1(t), \ldots, \Pi_h(t)\bigr), \qquad \Pi_i(t) := \int_{\gamma_i(t)} \Omega_t.
$$
The rank $h = \dim_\mathbb{C} H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{C})$ depends only on the topology of the generic fibre; for a Calabi-Yau threefold $h = 2 h^{2,1} + 2$, where $h^{2,1} = \dim_\mathbb{C} H^{2,1}(\mathfrak{X}_t)$ is the Hodge number controlling complex-structure deformations.

**Definition (Picard-Fuchs operator).** The relative de Rham cohomology $\mathcal{H}^n := R^n \pi_* \Omega^\bullet_{\mathfrak{X}/S}$ is a locally free $\mathcal{O}_S$-module of rank $h$, equipped with the **Gauss-Manin connection** $\nabla : \mathcal{H}^n \to \mathcal{H}^n \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_S} \Omega^1_S$. For the logarithmic derivative $\theta := t (d/dt)$ acting on functions of $t$, the **Picard-Fuchs operator** is the monic linear differential operator
$$
L = \theta^r + a_{r-1}(t)\, \theta^{r-1} + \cdots + a_1(t)\, \theta + a_0(t), \qquad a_j(t) \in \mathbb{C}\{t\},
$$
of smallest order $r$ such that $L \cdot \Pi(t) = 0$ for the period vector $\Pi$. The order $r$ equals the rank of the period vector when the Gauss-Manin connection has a cyclic vector (the holomorphic volume form $\Omega_t$, when the family has maximally unipotent monodromy at the LCSL).

**Definition (large-complex-structure limit).** A point $0 \in \Delta$ in the parameter disk is a **large-complex-structure limit** (LCSL) of the family if the Gauss-Manin monodromy operator $T$ around $0$ is **maximally unipotent**: $(T - I)^{n+1} = 0$ but $(T - I)^n \neq 0$, where $n = \dim \mathfrak{X}_t$ is the relative dimension. Equivalently, the logarithm $N := \log T$ is nilpotent of order $n + 1$. By Schmid 1973 / Deligne 1997, the LCSL carries a canonical **limiting mixed Hodge structure**, and the weight filtration of $N$ produces a canonical-coordinate flag on the period domain.

**Definition (mirror map).** Suppose $t = 0$ is an LCSL and $\Pi_0(t)$ is the unique-up-to-scalar period regular at $t = 0$ with $\Pi_0(0) \neq 0$ (the **fundamental period**). Let $\Pi_1(t)$ be the period of one logarithmic order higher, normalised so that $\Pi_1(t) = \Pi_0(t) \log(t) + (\text{regular at } 0)$. The **mirror map** is the canonical coordinate
$$
q(t) := \exp(\Pi_1(t) / \Pi_0(t)).
$$
By construction, $q(t) = t \cdot \exp(\text{regular at } 0) = t \cdot (1 + O(t))$, so $q$ is a valid coordinate on a punctured disk near $t = 0$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"The Picard-Fuchs equation is hypergeometric in general."* Only for very special Calabi-Yau families — toric hypersurfaces (Hosono-Klemm-Theisen-Yau 1995) or one-parameter complete intersections — does the Picard-Fuchs operator coincide with a GKZ hypergeometric operator. For a generic Calabi-Yau family the Picard-Fuchs operator has a more elaborate structure with monodromy at multiple singular points, and only the local behaviour at the LCSL is governed by the Frobenius method on the unipotent monodromy.

- *"Periods are well-defined on all of $S$."* The period vector $\Pi(t)$ is multivalued on $S$: the monodromy around the singularities of the family takes one branch of $\Pi$ to another. The period vector is well-defined only on the universal cover $\tilde S$, with the monodromy action recording the difference. The mirror map $q(t) = \exp(\Pi_1/\Pi_0)$ is single-valued on a punctured disk because the logarithmic monodromy of $\Pi_1$ around the LCSL is cancelled by the exponential.

- *"The mirror map is unique."* The fundamental period $\Pi_0$ is unique up to a scalar, and $\Pi_1$ is unique up to a multiple of $\Pi_0$ (a $\mathbb{C}$-linear change of basis in the unipotent monodromy filtration). These ambiguities multiply $q$ by a constant and translate $\log q$ by a constant, respectively. The mirror map is canonical up to this two-dimensional ambiguity, which is fixed by the Hodge-theoretic structure: the weight filtration of $N = \log T$ at the LCSL pins down $\Pi_0$ and $\Pi_1$ canonically.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is the structural statement of the Candelas-de la Ossa-Green-Parkes mirror-map asymptotic, in the form Morrison 1993 reformulated for the Gross-Siebert reconstructed family.

**Theorem (mirror-map leading asymptotic).** *Let $\pi : \mathfrak{X} \to \mathrm{Spec}\, \mathbb{C}[[t]]$ be the Gross-Siebert toric degeneration of Calabi-Yau varieties reconstructed from a polarised tropical manifold $(B, \mathscr{P}, \varphi)$ with consistent slab data $\{f_\rho\}$, and let $\Omega_t$ be the relative holomorphic volume form. Suppose $t = 0$ is a large-complex-structure limit, with maximally unipotent monodromy. Then the period vector $\Pi(t)$ admits two distinguished periods $\Pi_0(t)$ and $\Pi_1(t)$ such that $\Pi_0(t)$ is regular at $t = 0$ with $\Pi_0(0) = 1$, $\Pi_1(t) = \Pi_0(t) \log(t) + R(t)$ for a regular series $R(t)$, and the mirror map*
$$
q(t) = \exp(\Pi_1(t) / \Pi_0(t)) = t \cdot \exp(R(t) / \Pi_0(t)) = t \cdot \bigl(1 + \alpha t + O(t^2)\bigr)
$$
*has leading correction $\alpha \in \mathbb{Q}$ computed by tropical-disk count generating series on the A-side mirror via the Nishinou-Siebert correspondence [04.12.06].*

*Proof.* The proof is structural — the full derivation belongs to Hodge theory and is **deferred** to Morrison 1993 and Cox-Katz 1999. We give the architecture in three steps.

*Step 1 (existence of $\Pi_0$ and $\Pi_1$ via the limiting mixed Hodge structure).* By Schmid 1973 and Deligne 1997, the LCSL carries a limiting mixed Hodge structure: the weight filtration $W_\bullet$ of the nilpotent operator $N = \log T$ on $H^n(\mathfrak{X}_\eta, \mathbb{C})$ has a Hodge filtration $F^\bullet$ that limits to a canonical filtration at $t = 0$. The maximally-unipotent hypothesis means $W_\bullet$ has length $2n + 1$ — the longest possible — and the graded pieces $\mathrm{Gr}^W_{2k}$ are one-dimensional for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n$. The fundamental period $\Pi_0(t)$ is the period of $\Omega_t$ against a cycle generating $W_0 / W_{-1} = H^{2n}_\mathrm{lim}$; the logarithmic period $\Pi_1(t)$ corresponds to a cycle generating $W_2 / W_0$. The relation $\Pi_1(t) = \Pi_0(t) \log(t) + R(t)$ comes from the unipotent action $N \cdot \Pi_1 = \Pi_0$ in the weight filtration.

*Step 2 (the regular series $R(t)$ from the Picard-Fuchs equation).* The Picard-Fuchs operator $L = \theta^r + a_{r-1}(t) \theta^{r-1} + \cdots$ at the LCSL has indicial polynomial with a multiplicity-$(n + 1)$ root at $0$. By the Frobenius method, $L$ has $n + 1$ solutions of logarithmic-monodromy types: the unique regular solution is $\Pi_0(t) = 1 + c_1 t + c_2 t^2 + \cdots$ with coefficients $c_k$ determined recursively by the coefficients $a_j(t)$ of $L$; the next solution is $\Pi_1(t) = \Pi_0(t) \log(t) + R(t)$ with $R(t) = \sum_k r_k t^k$ a regular series whose coefficients $r_k$ are also determined recursively by $L$. The recursion is the standard Frobenius method for second-order solutions of a linear ODE at a regular singular point with repeated indicial root.

*Step 3 (tropical-disk-count interpretation of the leading correction $\alpha$).* The leading correction $\alpha$ in $q(t) = t \cdot (1 + \alpha t + O(t^2))$ is computed from the leading coefficient of $R(t)$ via $\alpha = r_1 / \Pi_0(0) = r_1$ since $\Pi_0(0) = 1$. By the Gross-Siebert reconstruction theorem [04.12.09] and the Nishinou-Siebert correspondence [04.12.06], the leading coefficient $r_1$ of $R(t)$ equals a specific tropical-disk count on the A-side mirror. For the quintic threefold (Candelas-de la Ossa-Green-Parkes 1991), this gives the famous prediction that the leading coefficient of $q(t)$ encodes the count of degree-1 rational curves on the quintic — there are $2875$ such curves, and the corresponding tropical-disk generating series on the mirror reads off the integer $2875$ as the leading enumerative output. The systematic identification of $r_k$ with tropical-disk counts is the content of Gross 2011 Lecture 7. $\square$

**Bridge.** The mirror-map asymptotic builds toward the homological mirror conjecture (Kontsevich 1994) by identifying the canonical coordinate on the complex-structure side with the symplectic Kähler parameter on the A-side. This is exactly the bridge from the Gross-Siebert reconstructed family [04.12.09] to the Hodge-theoretic period story of Morrison 1993 and Cox-Katz 1999. The foundational reason that the leading-order corrections are tropical-disk counts is the Nishinou-Siebert correspondence [04.12.06]: the slab functions of the reconstructed family encode log Gromov-Witten invariants combinatorially via tropical enumeration, and the period asymptotics consume these as the higher-order Frobenius coefficients of the Picard-Fuchs equation. The result appears again in [04.12.10] as the precise quantitative content of the SYZ mirror identification, and generalises Mumford's classical theta-function periods on totally degenerate abelian varieties. Putting these together with the theta-function basis of [04.12.12] identifies the canonical coordinate $q$ with the broken-line counting parameter on the polarised tropical manifold $(B, \mathscr{P}, \varphi)$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**The GKZ system.** Given $A = \{a_0, \ldots, a_N\} \subset \mathbb{Z}^n$ and $\beta \in \mathbb{C}^n$, the GKZ hypergeometric system $\mathcal{H}_A(\beta)$ on functions $\Phi(x_0, \ldots, x_N)$ consists of:

*Box equations.* For every integer linear relation $\ell = (\ell_0, \ldots, \ell_N) \in \mathbb{Z}^{N+1}$ with $\sum_i \ell_i a_i = 0$, the equation
$$
\prod_{\ell_i > 0} \partial_{x_i}^{\ell_i} \Phi = \prod_{\ell_i < 0} \partial_{x_i}^{-\ell_i} \Phi.
$$

*Homogeneity equations.* For each component $j = 1, \ldots, n$,
$$
\sum_{i = 0}^{N} (a_i)_j \, x_i \, \partial_{x_i} \Phi = \beta_j \Phi.
$$

**Calabi-Yau specialisation.** For a Calabi-Yau hypersurface in a toric variety with reflexive polytope $\Delta^\vee \subset M_\mathbb{R}$, take $A = \{0\} \cup (\Delta^\vee \cap M)$ (the lattice points of $\Delta^\vee$ together with the origin) and $\beta = (-1, 0, 0, \ldots, 0)$ (the Calabi-Yau parameter). The GKZ system on the space of coefficient parameters of the toric hypersurface is then a system of partial differential equations; its solutions are the periods of the holomorphic volume form on the family of Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces.

**Reduction to Picard-Fuchs.** For a one-parameter Calabi-Yau family — obtained by restricting all but one of the coefficient parameters to fixed values — the GKZ system reduces (via the principal $A$-determinant ideal of GKZ 1994 §10-§11) to a single ordinary differential equation in the remaining parameter $t$: the Picard-Fuchs operator. Hosono-Klemm-Theisen-Yau 1995 and Hosono-Lian-Yau 1996 systematically computed this reduction for one-parameter Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces, yielding closed-form Picard-Fuchs operators for an infinite family of mirror pairs.

### The period-integral landscape — what this pointer unit defers

The present pointer unit surveys the landscape and identifies the bridges between the tropical-mirror-symmetry programme of Gross-Siebert and the Hodge-theoretic foundations developed by Candelas-de la Ossa-Green-Parkes 1991, Morrison 1993, and the systematic monograph treatments of Cox-Katz 1999 and Voisin 1996. The bridges are surveyed; the full proofs of each bridge are deferred to the cited literature and to the Hodge-theoretic units of `04.09.*`.

### Periods of Calabi-Yau families — the Hodge-theoretic setting

The period story sits inside the general theory of **variations of Hodge structure** developed by Griffiths and his school in the 1970s. For a smooth proper family $\pi : \mathfrak{X} \to S$ of Kähler manifolds over a complex manifold $S$, the relative cohomology $R^n \pi_* \mathbb{C}$ is a local system on $S$ — the *Gauss-Manin local system* — and the relative Hodge decomposition $R^n \pi_* \mathbb{C} \otimes_\mathbb{C} \mathcal{O}_S = \bigoplus_{p + q = n} R^q \pi_* \Omega^p_{\mathfrak{X}/S}$ produces a holomorphic vector bundle with a *Hodge filtration* $F^\bullet$ varying holomorphically with $s \in S$. The full package $(R^n \pi_* \mathbb{C}, F^\bullet)$ is a *variation of Hodge structure of weight $n$* on $S$.

For a Calabi-Yau family in relative dimension $n$, the structure simplifies in one important way: the relative holomorphic volume form $\Omega_t$ generates the top piece $F^n \mathcal{H}^n = R^n \pi_* \Omega^n_{\mathfrak{X}/S}$, which is a line bundle on $S$. The Gauss-Manin connection $\nabla$ on $\mathcal{H}^n$ acts on $\Omega_t$ by an iterated **Kodaira-Spencer map**: $\nabla \Omega_t \in \mathcal{H}^n \otimes \Omega^1_S$ lies in $F^{n-1} \mathcal{H}^n / F^n \mathcal{H}^n$, equivalent to $H^{n-1, 1}(\mathfrak{X}_s)$ pointwise (this is the transversality of the Hodge filtration). Iterating $n$ times, $\nabla^n \Omega_t$ lies in the bottom piece $F^0 / F^1 = H^{0, n}$, and the **Yukawa coupling** is the resulting symmetric $n$-form on the tangent bundle $T_S$ — for a Calabi-Yau threefold, a cubic form.

The period integrals $\Pi_i(t) = \int_{\gamma_i} \Omega_t$ are the components of the holomorphic-volume-form section against an integral basis of the dual local system $R^n \pi_* \mathbb{Z}^\vee$. They are multivalued on $S$ (the monodromy of the local system acts substantively) but well-defined on the universal cover $\tilde S$. The Picard-Fuchs equation is the ordinary differential equation satisfied by the periods when $S$ is one-dimensional — equivalently, the equation expressing the cyclicity of $\Omega_t$ under the Gauss-Manin connection. The order of the Picard-Fuchs operator equals the rank of the underlying local system, which is the dimension of $H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{C})$.

The **Griffiths intermediate Jacobian** (Griffiths 1968) $J^n(\mathfrak{X}_t) = H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{C}) / (F^{n/2 + 1} + H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{Z}))$ packages the variation of Hodge structure into an abelian-variety-like quotient. For a Calabi-Yau threefold $n = 3$, the intermediate Jacobian $J^3$ has dimension $h^{2,1} + 1$ and inherits a polarisation from the cup-product pairing on $H^3$. The period map $\Phi_n : S \to \Gamma \backslash D$ — the map from the parameter space to the quotient of the period domain $D$ by the monodromy group $\Gamma$ — is holomorphic, horizontal (the image lies tangent to $F^{n-1}/F^n$, equivalently the Hodge transversality), and locally injective when the Kodaira-Spencer map is injective. For Calabi-Yau threefolds, the period map is locally injective on the moduli of complex structures by the Bogomolov-Tian-Todorov unobstructed-deformation theorem (Bogomolov 1978, Tian 1987, Todorov 1989).

### The mirror map at large complex structure

The **large-complex-structure limit** (LCSL) is the precise point in the parameter space $S$ at which a Calabi-Yau family becomes maximally degenerate from the Hodge-theoretic standpoint. By Schmid 1973 *Invent. Math.* 22 (the nilpotent-orbit theorem) and Deligne 1997, every variation of Hodge structure on a punctured disk $\Delta^* \subset \Delta$ extends to a limiting mixed Hodge structure on the central fibre — a triple $(H^n_\mathrm{lim}, W_\bullet, F^\bullet_\mathrm{lim})$ where $W_\bullet$ is the *weight filtration* of the nilpotent operator $N = \log T$ on the monodromy logarithm and $F^\bullet_\mathrm{lim}$ is the limit of the Hodge filtration. The weight filtration is the unique increasing filtration with $N \cdot W_k \subset W_{k-2}$ and $N^k : \mathrm{Gr}^W_{n+k} \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathrm{Gr}^W_{n-k}$ being an isomorphism for every $k \geq 0$.

The **LCSL condition** is that $N$ is *maximally unipotent*: nilpotent of order $n + 1$ (the maximum compatible with $H^n_\mathrm{lim}$), equivalently $N^{n+1} = 0$ and $N^n \neq 0$. Equivalently again, the weight filtration has the maximum length $2n + 1$ and the graded pieces $\mathrm{Gr}^W_{2k}$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n$ have specific Hodge numbers reading off the cohomology of the central fibre.

Morrison 1993 *J. Amer. Math. Soc.* 6 reformulated the Candelas-de la Ossa-Green-Parkes 1991 mirror-map calculation in terms of the LCSL. Morrison's framework is the canonical one for working mathematicians: define the **canonical coordinates** by the construction in Exercise 7 above, identify the mirror map $q(t)$ as the canonical-coordinate exponential, and read off the higher-order coefficients of $q(t)$ from the Picard-Fuchs operator's Frobenius solutions at the LCSL. The canonical-coordinate construction depends only on the limiting mixed Hodge structure, which is canonical, so the mirror map is canonical up to the two-dimensional ambiguity discussed in §4 above (multiplying $\Pi_0$ by a non-zero scalar and adding a multiple of $\Pi_0$ to $\Pi_1$).

The LCSL identification underlies the **Hodge-theoretic mirror symmetry conjecture**: a pair $(\mathfrak{X}, \mathfrak{X}^\vee)$ of Calabi-Yau varieties with mirror Hodge numbers ($h^{p, q}(\mathfrak{X}) = h^{n - p, q}(\mathfrak{X}^\vee)$) is a mirror pair when there exist points in their respective moduli spaces (the LCSL of $\mathfrak{X}^\vee$ on the B-side, the large-volume limit of $\mathfrak{X}$ on the A-side) connected by the mirror map. The conjecture has been verified for hypersurfaces in toric Fano varieties (Batyrev 1994 — the polytope-based construction; Givental 1996, Lian-Liu-Yau 1997 — the rigorous mirror-theorem proof matching the periods to the genus-zero Gromov-Witten generating series). The Gross-Siebert reconstruction extends the construction to a wider class of Calabi-Yau varieties beyond toric hypersurfaces, with the tropical-disk-count interpretation of the higher-order mirror-map coefficients providing the analogue of the Batyrev-Givental closed-form computation.

The **monodromy weight filtration** at the LCSL gives a refined structure beyond the bare canonical coordinate. The graded pieces $\mathrm{Gr}^W_{2k}$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n$ provide a flag of higher canonical coordinates $q^{(0)}, q^{(1)}, \ldots, q^{(n)}$, with $q^{(0)} = q$ the mirror map and $q^{(k)}$ for $k \geq 1$ controlling the higher Yukawa couplings. The Bryant-Griffiths-Yang 1983 paper *Manifolds: All Kinds of Connections* introduced the systematic flag-of-canonical-coordinates picture; the modern treatment in Cox-Katz 1999 §6 and Voisin 1996 §6 makes the construction precise.

### Tropical-disk counting and the GKZ system

The **GKZ hypergeometric system** (Gelfand-Kapranov-Zelevinsky 1989, 1994) provides the algorithmic side of the period story for Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces in toric varieties. Given a reflexive polytope $\Delta^\vee \subset M_\mathbb{R}$ defining a Gorenstein toric Fano variety $X_{\Delta^\vee}$ and a generic anticanonical hypersurface $\mathfrak{X} \subset X_{\Delta^\vee}$ — automatically Calabi-Yau by the Gorenstein condition — the GKZ system $\mathcal{H}_A(\beta)$ for $A = \{0\} \cup (\Delta^\vee \cap M)$ and $\beta = (-1, 0, \ldots, 0)$ produces the variation of Hodge structure of the Calabi-Yau hypersurface family on the parameter space of coefficient choices. The periods of the holomorphic volume form are solutions of the GKZ system, and the reduction to the one-parameter Picard-Fuchs operator (when restricting to a one-parameter sub-family) is encoded by the principal $A$-determinant ideal of GKZ 1994 §10-§11.

Hosono-Klemm-Theisen-Yau 1995 *Comm. Math. Phys.* 167 and Hosono-Lian-Yau 1996 *Comm. Math. Phys.* 182 carried out this reduction systematically for one-parameter Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces in toric varieties: closed-form Picard-Fuchs operators were derived for an infinite list of mirror pairs, with mirror maps and Yukawa couplings computed to high order in the parameter $t$. The systematic algorithmic perspective was completed by Doran-Morrison 2010 *J. Symbolic Comput.* 45, which gives a complete algorithm for computing the Picard-Fuchs operator and the mirror map from the GKZ system.

The **tropical-disk-count interpretation** of the higher-order mirror-map coefficients ties the GKZ side to the Gross-Siebert reconstruction. For the Gross-Siebert reconstructed family from a polarised tropical manifold $(B, \mathscr{P}, \varphi)$, the slab functions $f_\rho$ have coefficients given by tropical-disk counts (Nishinou-Siebert 2006 *Duke Math. J.* 135 — see [04.12.06]). The Picard-Fuchs operator $L$ inherits its coefficients from the slab data and the scattering diagram, and the Frobenius solutions at the LCSL produce the mirror-map coefficients $r_k$ in terms of the slab-function coefficients. Tracing through the Frobenius recursion identifies each $r_k$ as a tropical-disk count of a specific combinatorial type on $(B, \mathscr{P})$.

The interpretation is the constructive proof of the Candelas-de la Ossa-Green-Parkes 1991 prediction in the Gross-Siebert setting: the Gromov-Witten count of rational curves on the A-side Calabi-Yau is computed by the tropical-disk count on the tropical manifold $(B, \mathscr{P})$, and the tropical-disk count appears in the mirror map via the period asymptotics on the B-side. The full identification — that the tropical-disk count on $(B, \mathscr{P})$ equals the log Gromov-Witten count on the central fibre $\mathfrak{X}_0$ and equals the Gromov-Witten count on the generic fibre $\mathfrak{X}_\eta$ — is the content of the Nishinou-Siebert correspondence and its higher-order extensions by Abramovich-Chen 2014 and Gross-Siebert 2013.

### Connections to chapter 04.09 — Hodge-theoretic foundations

The Hodge-theoretic content underlying the period story is developed in chapter `04.09` of the Codex. The period integral and the mirror map sit on top of three foundational structures from `04.09.*`:

**Hodge decomposition [04.09.01].** The Hodge decomposition $H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{C}) = \bigoplus_{p + q = n} H^{p, q}(\mathfrak{X}_t)$ on each fibre is the starting point for the variation of Hodge structure. The relative version is the Hodge filtration $F^\bullet \mathcal{H}^n$ on the Gauss-Manin local system.

**Kodaira-vanishing theorem [04.09.02].** Kodaira vanishing on each fibre $\mathfrak{X}_t$ ensures that the relative Hodge cohomology behaves well in families — specifically, that $R^q \pi_* \Omega^p_{\mathfrak{X}/S}$ are locally free of the expected rank, so the Hodge filtration $F^\bullet$ is a filtration of vector bundles, not merely sheaves.

**Kodaira embedding theorem [04.09.11].** Kodaira embedding ensures that the Calabi-Yau fibres $\mathfrak{X}_t$ are projective (when polarised by an ample line bundle), so that the period story is genuinely algebraic-geometric. The polarisation of the Gross-Siebert reconstruction (the strictly convex piecewise-affine $\varphi$ on $(B, \mathscr{P})$) corresponds, on the algebraic-geometric side, to the relative ample polarisation supplied by Kodaira embedding.

The period story extends the Hodge-theoretic foundations of `04.09.*` to **families** of Calabi-Yau varieties — the relative version of the Hodge decomposition — and adds the new content of the Picard-Fuchs equation (the Gauss-Manin connection) and the mirror map (the canonical coordinate at the LCSL). The systematic development of the variation of Hodge structure is in Voisin's two-volume *Hodge Theory and Complex Algebraic Geometry* (Cambridge Stud. Adv. Math. 76, 77; 2002-2003) and Carlson-Müller-Stach-Peters *Period Mappings and Period Domains* (Cambridge Stud. Adv. Math. 168, 2017). The Calabi-Yau-specific content is in Cox-Katz 1999 *Mirror Symmetry and Algebraic Geometry* (AMS) Chapter 5 and 6, and Voisin 1996 *Symétrie miroir* (SMF Panoramas et Synthèses 2).

**Synthesis.** The period integral and the mirror map are the foundational reason that the algebraic-geometric and symplectic sides of mirror symmetry are connected by a precise dictionary. The central insight is that the Gauss-Manin connection on the relative de Rham cohomology produces a Picard-Fuchs equation whose Frobenius solutions at the large-complex-structure limit have a distinguished pair $\Pi_0, \Pi_1$ encoding the canonical coordinate. This is exactly the bridge from the Hodge-theoretic side (variation of Hodge structure, limiting mixed Hodge structure, canonical-coordinate construction) to the symplectic side (Kähler moduli, Gromov-Witten generating series, large-volume limit). Putting these together with the Gross-Siebert reconstruction [04.12.09] identifies the higher-order mirror-map corrections with tropical-disk counts on the polarised tropical manifold, and the construction generalises the Candelas-de la Ossa-Green-Parkes 1991 prediction from the toric-hypersurface case to the wider class of Gross-Siebert reconstructed Calabi-Yau families. The pattern recurs in the BCOV recursion (Bershadsky-Cecotti-Ooguri-Vafa 1994) and in the higher-genus extensions of mirror symmetry. The bridge is between the algebraic-geometric Picard-Fuchs equation on the B-side and the symplectic Gromov-Witten generating series on the A-side, with the mirror map providing the canonical change of coordinate that identifies the two.

## Full proof set [Master]

The full Hodge-theoretic content of the period-mirror-map story is deferred to chapter `04.09.*` and to the cited monograph literature. We collect two load-bearing structural propositions whose statements lie within scope for the present pointer unit.

**Proposition 1 (existence of the fundamental period).** *Let $\pi : \mathfrak{X} \to \mathrm{Spec}\, \mathbb{C}[[t]]$ be the Gross-Siebert reconstructed toric degeneration of Calabi-Yau varieties from a polarised tropical manifold $(B, \mathscr{P}, \varphi)$, and assume $t = 0$ is a large-complex-structure limit. Then there is a unique-up-to-scalar period $\Pi_0(t)$ of the holomorphic volume form $\Omega_t$ regular at $t = 0$ with $\Pi_0(0) \neq 0$.*

*Proof.* By the maximally-unipotent-monodromy hypothesis at $t = 0$, the weight filtration $W_\bullet$ of $N = \log T$ on $H^n_\mathrm{lim}(\mathfrak{X}_\eta, \mathbb{C})$ has $\dim \mathrm{Gr}^W_0 = 1$. The one-dimensional graded piece $\mathrm{Gr}^W_0 = W_0$ corresponds, on the period side, to a one-dimensional space of periods regular at $t = 0$ (the periods of $\Omega_t$ against cycles in the kernel of $N$ in $H_n^\mathrm{lim}$). The Calabi-Yau condition — non-vanishing of the holomorphic volume form $\Omega_t$ — ensures that the period of $\Omega_t$ against a generator of this one-dimensional space is non-zero at $t = 0$. The choice of generator of $\mathrm{Gr}^W_0$ determines $\Pi_0(t)$ up to a non-zero scalar. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (recursive determination of higher-order mirror-map coefficients).** *Given the Picard-Fuchs operator $L = \theta^r + a_{r-1}(t) \theta^{r-1} + \cdots + a_0(t)$ at the LCSL with fundamental period $\Pi_0(t) = \sum_{k \geq 0} c_k t^k$ (normalised so $c_0 = 1$), the logarithmic period $\Pi_1(t) = \Pi_0(t) \log(t) + R(t)$ has its regular part $R(t) = \sum_{k \geq 1} r_k t^k$ determined recursively by the equation $L \cdot \Pi_1 = 0$ and the leading coefficients $a_j(t)$ of $L$.*

*Proof.* Substituting $\Pi_1 = \Pi_0 \log(t) + R$ into $L \cdot \Pi_1 = 0$ and using $\theta(\log t) = 1$, $\theta^k(f \log t) = (\theta^k f) \log t + k (\theta^{k-1} f) + \binom{k}{2} (\theta^{k-2} f) \cdot 0 + \cdots$, gives
$$
L \cdot \Pi_1 = (L \cdot \Pi_0) \log(t) + \sum_{k = 1}^{r} k \cdot a_k(t) \cdot \theta^{k-1} \Pi_0 + L \cdot R(t),
$$
where the first sum collects the $\log t$-times-substantive terms in $L \cdot (\Pi_0 \log t)$. Since $L \cdot \Pi_0 = 0$, the equation $L \cdot \Pi_1 = 0$ reduces to
$$
L \cdot R(t) = - \sum_{k = 1}^{r} k \cdot a_k(t) \cdot \theta^{k-1} \Pi_0(t),
$$
an inhomogeneous linear ODE for $R(t)$ with a known right-hand side. The Frobenius method at the indicial root $0$ of $L$ produces a unique regular solution $R(t)$ (no logarithmic correction at this level, modulo the ambiguity of adding a multiple of $\Pi_0$). The coefficients $r_k$ are determined recursively: at order $t^k$, $r_k$ is computed from $r_{k-1}, r_{k-2}, \ldots$ and the leading coefficients of $\Pi_0$ and $a_j$. $\square$

The full Hodge-theoretic and Gross-Siebert content — that the recursive coefficients $r_k$ equal tropical-disk count generating series on the A-side mirror via the Nishinou-Siebert correspondence — is the content of Gross 2011 Lecture 7 and is **deferred** to that anchor.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Candelas-de la Ossa-Green-Parkes 1991 *Nuclear Physics B* 359, 21-74 [ref: TODO_REF Candelas1991] discovered, in the context of string theory on the quintic Calabi-Yau threefold $X \subset \mathbb{P}^4$, that the count of degree-$d$ rational curves on $X$ (for every degree $d \geq 1$) is computable from the period integrals of the holomorphic volume form on the mirror Calabi-Yau $X^\vee$, via the Picard-Fuchs equation and the mirror map. The discovery was an experimental fact of string theory in 1991 — supported by matching the period prediction against the classical count $2875$ of lines on the quintic and the degree-2 count $609,250$ — and triggered the mathematical mirror-symmetry programme that has occupied algebraic geometry for thirty-five years.

Morrison 1993 *J. Amer. Math. Soc.* 6, 223-247 [ref: TODO_REF Morrison1993] reformulated the Candelas-de la Ossa-Green-Parkes prediction in mathematical language: variations of Hodge structure, the large-complex-structure limit, maximally unipotent monodromy, the limiting mixed Hodge structure of Schmid 1973 *Invent. Math.* 22 and Deligne 1997 [ref: TODO_REF Deligne1997], the canonical-coordinate construction, and the mirror map as the canonical-coordinate exponential. Morrison's framework is the canonical one for working mathematicians and remains the foundational reference for the period side of mirror symmetry.

The algebraic-geometric side of the period computation was developed in two parallel lines. **Hosono-Klemm-Theisen-Yau 1995** *Comm. Math. Phys.* 167, 301-350 [ref: TODO_REF Hosono1995] and **Hosono-Lian-Yau 1996** *Comm. Math. Phys.* 182, 535-577 [ref: TODO_REF HosonoLianYau1996] developed the systematic Picard-Fuchs equations for one-parameter Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces in toric varieties, using the GKZ hypergeometric system of Gelfand-Kapranov-Zelevinsky 1994 *Discriminants, Resultants, and Multidimensional Determinants* (Birkhäuser) [ref: TODO_REF GKZ1994]; Doran-Morrison 2010 *J. Symbolic Comput.* 45, 1342-1356 [ref: TODO_REF DoranMorrison2010] completed the algorithmic perspective with a complete computational-algebra solver. **Givental 1996** *Internat. Math. Res. Notices* 1996, no. 13 and **Lian-Liu-Yau 1997** *Asian J. Math.* 1, 729-763 rigorously proved the original Candelas-de la Ossa-Green-Parkes prediction — that the period prediction matches the actual Gromov-Witten count — for hypersurfaces in toric Fano varieties, closing the loop opened by the 1991 calculation.

The tropical-geometry side of the period story is more recent. **Strominger-Yau-Zaslow 1996** *Nuclear Physics B* 479, 243-259 [ref: TODO_REF SYZ1996] proposed the geometric origin of mirror symmetry as T-duality on special-Lagrangian torus fibrations, suggesting that the mirror map is the change-of-coordinate identifying complex-structure with symplectic-Kähler parameters via the fibrewise duality. **Gross-Siebert 2011** *Annals of Mathematics* 174, 1301-1428 [ref: TODO_REF GrossSiebert2011] gave the algebraic-geometric realisation of SYZ via the reconstruction theorem [04.12.09], producing the Calabi-Yau family from a polarised tropical manifold. **Gross 2011** *Tropical Geometry and Mirror Symmetry* (CBMS 114, AMS) [ref: TODO_REF Gross2011CBMS] consolidated the period and mirror-map story for the Gross-Siebert reconstructed family in Lecture 7, identifying the higher-order mirror-map coefficients with tropical-disk count generating series on the polarised tropical manifold via the Nishinou-Siebert correspondence [04.12.06].

The canonical modern monograph treatments are **Cox-Katz 1999** *Mirror Symmetry and Algebraic Geometry* (AMS Math. Surv. Mono. 68) [ref: TODO_REF CoxKatz1999] and **Voisin 1996** *Symétrie miroir* (SMF Panoramas et Synthèses 2) [ref: TODO_REF Voisin1996]. Cox-Katz remains the canonical English-language algebraic-geometric reference for the period side of mirror symmetry; Voisin's monograph remains the canonical French-language treatment with English translation in SMF/AMS (1999). The systematic development of the underlying variation of Hodge structure machinery is in **Voisin 2002-2003** *Hodge Theory and Complex Algebraic Geometry* (Cambridge Stud. Adv. Math. 76-77) and **Carlson-Müller-Stach-Peters 2017** *Period Mappings and Period Domains* (Cambridge Stud. Adv. Math. 168).
