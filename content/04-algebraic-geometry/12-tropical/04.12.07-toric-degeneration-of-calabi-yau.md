## Intuition [Beginner]

A Calabi-Yau variety is a complex algebraic variety with a distinguishing feature: it carries a nowhere-vanishing holomorphic volume form. The simplest Calabi-Yau is an elliptic curve (one-dimensional); the next case is the K3 surface (two-dimensional); the case of greatest interest to physics is the quintic threefold (three-dimensional). Calabi-Yau varieties are smooth, geometrically rigid, and analytically hard. The structure that makes them tractable comes from looking at them in a one-parameter family in which they degenerate to something simpler.

A **toric degeneration of a Calabi-Yau variety** is a one-parameter family of complex varieties in which all the fibres for nonzero parameter $t$ are smooth Calabi-Yau, but the special fibre at $t = 0$ is broken into pieces. The pieces are individually combinatorial in nature: each one is a **toric variety**, which is built straight from a lattice polytope. The pieces are glued along their boundaries according to the combinatorics of a polyhedral subdivision. The whole degenerate special fibre at $t = 0$ is therefore a piecewise-linear assembly, and the smooth Calabi-Yau fibres for $t \neq 0$ can be reconstructed from this assembly by an algebraic deformation.

The deeper reason this picture matters is that the smooth Calabi-Yau encodes its mirror partner not in its own complex structure but in the combinatorics of its degeneration. The mirror Calabi-Yau is built from the same polyhedral data, looked at from the dual side. The **large-complex-structure limit**, also called the **maximally unipotent monodromy point**, is the parameter value $t = 0$ at which this combinatorial picture is exposed; it is the point in the moduli space at which the mirror map and the Strominger-Yau-Zaslow heuristic become precise. Gross and Siebert in 2006 turned this picture into a rigorous algebraic-geometric programme; their toric-degeneration setup is the foundation on which the rest of the programme is built.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The simplest Calabi-Yau is an **elliptic curve**, and the simplest toric degeneration of an elliptic curve degenerates it to a **nodal cubic** — a closed loop with a single self-crossing point. The picture works as follows.

Step 1. The smooth elliptic curve. Take the elliptic curve $E_t : y^2 = x(x - 1)(x - t)$ in the projective plane $\mathbb{P}^2$, parametrised by $t \in \mathbb{C}^*$. For every $t \notin \{0, 1, \infty\}$ this is a smooth genus-1 curve.

Step 2. The degeneration. As $t \to 0$, the equation becomes $y^2 = x^2(x - 1)$, whose vanishing locus in $\mathbb{P}^2$ is a nodal cubic — a curve homeomorphic to a circle with two points glued together. The smooth elliptic curve degenerates to this nodal cubic in a one-parameter family.

Step 3. The pieces. The nodal cubic, viewed combinatorially, is a single $\mathbb{P}^1$ (the projective line) with two points identified. The $\mathbb{P}^1$ is the simplest toric variety: it corresponds to the one-dimensional fan with two rays. The degeneration of the smooth elliptic curve is therefore a degeneration to a single toric variety with a self-gluing.

Step 4. The dual intersection complex. The dual intersection complex of the central fibre is a single circle — one vertex (for the one toric component $\mathbb{P}^1$) and one edge (for the self-intersection node), glued head-to-tail to form a loop. This circle is the integral affine $1$-manifold that is the SYZ base of the elliptic curve in dimension $1$: the elliptic curve is the dual T-torus fibration over this circle.

What this tells us: the smooth elliptic curve, in the limit $t \to 0$, reveals its underlying combinatorial structure as a circle. The smooth elliptic curve is the fibration of a $1$-torus (a circle) over this combinatorial $1$-sphere, and the mirror elliptic curve corresponds to the same circle with the roles of fibre and base swapped. The Gross-Siebert programme generalises this combinatorial picture to Calabi-Yau varieties in all dimensions. The picture for the quintic threefold is the same in structure: a triangulated 3-sphere with codimension-2 monodromy singularities as the dual intersection complex, with the smooth Calabi-Yau quintic recovered as a deformation of the broken central fibre.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a lattice $M \cong \mathbb{Z}^n$ of rank $n$ with dual $N = \mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(M, \mathbb{Z})$, write $M_\mathbb{R} := M \otimes \mathbb{R}$ and $N_\mathbb{R} := N \otimes \mathbb{R}$ for the associated real vector spaces, and let $\Delta \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ be a lattice polytope of full dimension $n$. The setup we describe is the one used by [Gross-Siebert 2006](#ref-gross-siebert-2006) §2 and the [Gross 2011 CBMS](#ref-gross-2011-cbms) Lecture 4 exposition; the toric prerequisites are the cone-and-fan formalism of `[04.11.04]` and the polytope-fan dictionary of `[04.11.10]`.

**Definition (toric degeneration of a Calabi-Yau pair).** Let $X$ be a smooth projective Calabi-Yau variety of complex dimension $n$ over $\mathbb{C}$, that is, $X$ is smooth projective, $\dim_\mathbb{C} X = n$, and the canonical bundle satisfies $K_X \cong \mathcal{O}_X$. A **toric degeneration of $X$** is a flat proper morphism of complex analytic spaces (or, equivalently, of formal schemes over $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\![t]\!]$)
$$
\pi : \mathfrak{X} \longrightarrow S
$$
where $S = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\![t]\!]$ (or an analytic disc $\Delta_\epsilon \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ with parameter $t$), together with a polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ of an associated lattice polytope $\Delta \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$, satisfying:

(i) **Smooth generic fibre.** The generic fibre $\mathfrak{X}_t := \pi^{-1}(t)$ for $t \neq 0$ is isomorphic to $X$. Equivalently the geometric generic fibre $\mathfrak{X}_\eta$ over the fraction field $K = \mathbb{C}(\!(t)\!)$ is isomorphic to $X \otimes_\mathbb{C} K$.

(ii) **Toric central fibre.** The central fibre $\mathfrak{X}_0 := \pi^{-1}(0)$ is a reduced reducible union
$$
\mathfrak{X}_0 \;=\; \bigcup_{\Delta_i \in \mathscr{P}^{\max}} X_{\Delta_i}
$$
of irreducible toric varieties indexed by the top-dimensional cells of $\mathscr{P}$; each $X_{\Delta_i}$ is the toric variety whose Newton polytope is the lattice polytope $\Delta_i$, and the union is glued along the toric stratification.

(iii) **Toric strata along intersections.** For any two top-dimensional cells $\Delta_i, \Delta_j \in \mathscr{P}^{\max}$ with intersection $\Delta_i \cap \Delta_j$ a polyhedral face, the intersection $X_{\Delta_i} \cap X_{\Delta_j}$ in $\mathfrak{X}_0$ is the toric stratum $X_{\Delta_i \cap \Delta_j}$ corresponding to that face. More generally, for any cell $\tau \in \mathscr{P}$ the stratum $X_\tau \subseteq \mathfrak{X}_0$ is the intersection of all $X_{\Delta_i}$ with $\Delta_i \supseteq \tau$.

(iv) **Logarithmic smoothness.** The total space $\mathfrak{X}$ is regular at the generic point of every toric stratum of $\mathfrak{X}_0$, and the divisor $\mathfrak{X}_0 \subseteq \mathfrak{X}$ has simple normal crossings (or, in the singular generalisation, simple toroidal crossings). Equivalently $\mathfrak{X}$ equipped with the divisorial log structure of $\mathfrak{X}_0$ is log smooth over $S$ in the sense of [Kato 1989](#ref-kato-1989).

(v) **Calabi-Yau condition on the total family.** The relative canonical sheaf $\omega_{\mathfrak{X}/S}$ satisfies $\omega_{\mathfrak{X}/S} \cong \mathcal{O}_\mathfrak{X}$; equivalently a relative nowhere-vanishing logarithmic top form
$$
\Omega \;\in\; \Gamma(\mathfrak{X}, \omega_{\mathfrak{X}/S}(\log \mathfrak{X}_0))
$$
exists and restricts to a nowhere-vanishing holomorphic volume form on every smooth fibre $\mathfrak{X}_t$, $t \neq 0$.

**Definition (large complex structure / maximally unipotent monodromy).** Let $\pi : \mathfrak{X} \to \Delta_\epsilon$ be a one-parameter family of smooth Calabi-Yau $n$-folds away from $t = 0$, and let $T : H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{C}) \to H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{C})$ be the monodromy operator obtained by transporting along a small loop around the origin. The family has **maximally unipotent monodromy at $t = 0$** if $T$ is unipotent (all eigenvalues equal $1$) and
$$
(T - I)^n \;\neq\; 0, \qquad (T - I)^{n + 1} \;=\; 0.
$$
Equivalently, the limit mixed Hodge structure (Deligne 1971; Schmid 1973) on $H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{C})$ has maximal weight filtration jump at $t = 0$. The boundary point $t = 0$ is then called a **large complex structure limit (LCSL)** of the complex moduli space, and is the algebraic-geometric analogue of the large-volume limit on the mirror Calabi-Yau under the Morrison mirror map.

**Definition (dual intersection complex).** The **dual intersection complex** $B = B(\mathscr{P})$ of a toric-degeneration polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ of $\Delta$ is the integral affine cell complex obtained by gluing the cones over each cell of $\mathscr{P}$: for each cell $\tau \in \mathscr{P}$ of dimension $\dim \tau$, the dual cone $\sigma_\tau^\vee \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ has dimension $n - \dim \tau$, and $B(\mathscr{P})$ is the colimit
$$
B(\mathscr{P}) \;=\; \bigcup_{\tau \in \mathscr{P}} \sigma_\tau^\vee \;\big/\; \sim,
$$
where $\sim$ identifies $\sigma_{\tau}^\vee$ with $\sigma_{\tau'}^\vee$ along the dual incidence of $\tau, \tau'$ in $\mathscr{P}$. The complex $B$ carries an **integral affine structure** on the complement of a codimension-$2$ subset $\Delta_{\mathrm{sing}} \subseteq B$, with **monodromy** around $\Delta_{\mathrm{sing}}$ that records the topological mirror-symmetry data of the Calabi-Yau.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *A toric degeneration of a Calabi-Yau is not a toric variety.* The smooth fibres $\mathfrak{X}_t$ for $t \neq 0$ are Calabi-Yau and are typically *not* toric: the quintic threefold, the K3 surface, an abelian variety are not toric. The toric structure lives only on the central fibre $\mathfrak{X}_0$. Confusing "toric degeneration of" with "toric variety equal to" inverts the construction.

- *The polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ is not the Newton polytope of the Calabi-Yau equations.* For a Calabi-Yau hypersurface $X = \{f = 0\} \subseteq \mathbb{P}^n$, the equations involve high-degree polynomials whose Newton polytopes are themselves large. The polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ entering the toric degeneration is a subdivision of a different polytope — the polytope $\Delta$ associated to a chosen $T$-equivariant ample line bundle on the toric ambient space — and $\mathscr{P}$ refines $\Delta$ via a piecewise-linear function (the Mumford function) chosen for the degeneration.

- *Maximally unipotent monodromy is not the same as singularity of the central fibre.* The central fibre is singular in every toric degeneration; MUM is the additional Hodge-theoretic condition that the monodromy operator has maximal Jordan block size $n + 1$ on $H^n$. There exist degenerations with singular central fibre but non-MUM monodromy (these correspond to *non-large-complex-structure* boundary points of the moduli space, such as conifold points and orbifold points). The Gross-Siebert toric-degeneration setup specifically produces MUM points.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (existence of toric degenerations for Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces in toric Fano varieties; Gross-Siebert 2006).** *Let $X \subseteq Y$ be a smooth Calabi-Yau hypersurface in a smooth projective toric Fano variety $Y = Y_{\Sigma}$ of dimension $n + 1$, defined by the vanishing of a generic section of the anticanonical bundle $-K_Y$. Then there exists a one-parameter flat family*
$$
\pi : \mathfrak{X} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\![t]\!]
$$
*of complex projective varieties such that*

(a) *$\mathfrak{X}_t \cong X$ for every $t \neq 0$;*

(b) *$\mathfrak{X}_0$ is a reduced union $\bigcup_i X_{\Delta_i}$ of toric varieties indexed by a polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ of the lattice polytope $\Delta = -K_Y \cap M_\mathbb{R}$ (the Newton polytope of $-K_Y$);*

(c) *the family $\mathfrak{X} \to \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\![t]\!]$ is log smooth with respect to the divisorial log structure of $\mathfrak{X}_0$, and the relative canonical sheaf $\omega_{\mathfrak{X}/\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\![t]\!]}$ is isomorphic to the structure sheaf; and*

(d) *the family has maximally unipotent monodromy at $t = 0$.*

**Proof.** We sketch the construction; the full proof occupies [Gross-Siebert 2006](#ref-gross-siebert-2006) §2. The argument proceeds in four moves.

*Step 1 (the polytope and a regular subdivision).* The toric Fano $Y_\Sigma$ corresponds via the polytope-fan dictionary of `[04.11.10]` to a *reflexive* lattice polytope $\nabla \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$, with vertices the primitive integer generators of the rays $\Sigma(1)$. The anticanonical bundle $-K_Y$ corresponds to the polytope $\Delta = \nabla^\circ$, the polar dual of $\nabla$, which is again a reflexive lattice polytope of dimension $n + 1$ — this is the Batyrev reflexivity of `[04.11.10]`. A *regular polyhedral subdivision* $\mathscr{P}$ of $\Delta$ into lattice polytopes meeting along faces is determined by a strictly convex piecewise-linear function $\varphi : \Delta \to \mathbb{R}$ with integer values at the lattice points $\Delta \cap M$ — the **Mumford function** — via the lower convex hull construction.

*Step 2 (the Mumford degeneration).* Following [Mumford 1972](#ref-mumford-1972), [Kempf-Knudsen-Mumford-Saint-Donat 1973](#ref-kkms-1973), and the toric-degeneration formalism of [Nishinou-Siebert 2006](#ref-nishinou-siebert-2006) §2, lift each lattice monomial $\chi^m$ for $m \in \Delta \cap M$ to a $t$-weighted monomial $t^{\varphi(m)} \chi^m \in \mathbb{C}[\Delta \cap M][t]$. The toric Fano $Y_\Sigma$ embeds projectively via the lattice points $\Delta \cap M$ (sections of $-K_Y$), and a generic Calabi-Yau hypersurface $X \subseteq Y_\Sigma$ is cut out by a generic section
$$
f = \sum_{m \in \Delta \cap M} c_m \chi^m, \qquad c_m \in \mathbb{C}.
$$
Lifting via $\varphi$ gives the one-parameter family $f_t = \sum_{m \in \Delta \cap M} c_m t^{\varphi(m)} \chi^m \in \mathbb{C}[\Delta \cap M][\![t]\!]$. The total space $\mathfrak{X} := \{f_t = 0\} \subseteq Y_\Sigma \times \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\![t]\!]$ is the Mumford degeneration of the Calabi-Yau hypersurface $X$. For $t \neq 0$, dividing $f_t$ by any monomial of maximal $\varphi$-value recovers $f$ up to invertible factors, so $\mathfrak{X}_t \cong X$. This is (a).

*Step 3 (the central fibre).* At $t = 0$, the lifted relations kill all monomials except those with $\varphi(m) = 0$, breaking the global Calabi-Yau relations into local relations valid only on individual cells of $\mathscr{P}$. The central fibre $\mathfrak{X}_0$ is therefore the reduced union $\bigcup_i X_{\Delta_i}$, where each $X_{\Delta_i}$ is the toric variety of the lattice polytope $\Delta_i$ — specifically the toric stratum of the toric Fano $Y_\Sigma$ corresponding to the cell $\Delta_i \cap \Delta_j$ of the subdivision. This is (b). The intersection pattern in (iii) of the formal definition follows tautologically from the polyhedral incidence structure of $\mathscr{P}$.

*Step 4 (log smoothness and vanishing of the relative canonical class).* The total space $\mathfrak{X}$ has at worst toric singularities along the codimension-$2$ strata of $\mathfrak{X}_0$, and equipping $\mathfrak{X}$ with the divisorial log structure of $\mathfrak{X}_0$ makes $\pi : \mathfrak{X} \to \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\![t]\!]$ log smooth in the sense of [Kato 1989](#ref-kato-1989); the verification is local on each toric chart by direct inspection of the monoid grading. The Calabi-Yau condition $\omega_{\mathfrak{X}/\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\![t]\!]} \cong \mathcal{O}_\mathfrak{X}$ follows from the reflexivity of $\Delta$ (Batyrev): the relative canonical of the toric ambient family is the standard adjunction-formula computation, and the Calabi-Yau hypersurface inherits the relative-canonical-isomorphic-to-structure-sheaf property from the standard toric adjunction. The nowhere-vanishing logarithmic top form $\Omega$ is the residue along $\mathfrak{X}_0$ of the standard toric volume form $d\log \chi^{m_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge d\log \chi^{m_n}$. This is (c).

*Step 5 (maximally unipotent monodromy).* The MUM condition at $t = 0$ is detected by the limit mixed Hodge structure (Deligne 1971) on $H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{C})$. The polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ being a regular subdivision of the reflexive polytope $\Delta$ — together with the genericity of the section $f$ — guarantees that the dual intersection complex $B(\mathscr{P})$ of the degeneration is a topological $n$-sphere whose integral affine structure has codimension-$2$ singularities. The monodromy of this integral affine structure around the codimension-$2$ singular locus determines the unipotent part of the monodromy on $H^n$. The maximal Jordan block size $n + 1$ on $H^n$ follows from the fact that $B(\mathscr{P})$ has topological dimension $n$ and the monodromy filtration on the limit mixed Hodge structure has weights ranging over $\{0, 1, \ldots, 2n\}$ with consecutive jumps. The detailed calculation appears in [Gross-Siebert 2006](#ref-gross-siebert-2006) §2.3 and uses the Deligne-Hodge formalism. This is (d). $\square$

**Bridge.** The toric degeneration setup of this unit is the input that feeds the rest of the Gross-Siebert programme. The dual intersection complex $B(\mathscr{P})$ constructed in Step 5 is the integral affine manifold on which the [Strominger-Yau-Zaslow conjecture][1] (SYZ; see `[04.12.10]` when that unit ships, and the SYZ reference in the bibliography for now) operates; the **reconstruction theorem** of Gross-Siebert 2011 inverts the toric-degeneration construction by recovering the smoothing of $\mathfrak{X}_0$ to $\mathfrak{X}_t$ from $(B(\mathscr{P}), \text{integral affine structure})$ together with scattering data. The **Nishinou-Siebert correspondence** of `[04.12.06]` enumerates tropical curves on $B(\mathscr{P})$ and produces the wall-crossing automorphisms entering the scattering. The **Mikhalkin correspondence** of `[04.12.05]` is the dimension-$2$ specialisation. The **theta functions** $\vartheta_q$ for $q \in B(\mathbb{Z})$ (to appear in the unit on theta functions on polarised tropical manifolds, sibling forthcoming) are the canonical basis of global sections of the polarised mirror Calabi-Yau, constructed combinatorially from $(B(\mathscr{P}), \mathscr{S})$ where $\mathscr{S}$ is the scattering structure. The toric-degeneration setup is the bridge between toric and Calabi-Yau geometry: it converts a smooth Calabi-Yau problem into a piecewise-linear combinatorial problem on $B(\mathscr{P})$, and the algebraic-tropical dictionary built on top of this reconstruction is the central content of the Gross-Siebert programme.

[1]: # "Strominger-Yau-Zaslow 1996 *Nucl. Phys. B* 479, 243-259 — mirror Calabi-Yau pairs as dual special-Lagrangian torus fibrations; the SYZ base is the dual intersection complex of the toric degeneration in the Gross-Siebert algebraic realisation."

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Let $X \subseteq \mathbb{P}^4$ be a smooth Calabi-Yau quintic threefold, i.e. a smooth hypersurface of degree $5$ in $\mathbb{P}^4$. What is the complex dimension of $X$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A smooth hypersurface in a smooth ambient variety has codimension $1$. The dimension is the ambient dimension minus the codimension.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$3$.** The ambient projective space $\mathbb{P}^4$ has complex dimension $4$, and the hypersurface $X \subseteq \mathbb{P}^4$ has codimension $1$, so $\dim_\mathbb{C} X = 4 - 1 = 3$. The quintic threefold is the canonical example of a smooth Calabi-Yau $3$-fold: it satisfies the Calabi-Yau condition because the adjunction formula gives $K_X \cong K_{\mathbb{P}^4}|_X \otimes \mathcal{O}_X(5) \cong \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^4}(-5)|_X \otimes \mathcal{O}_X(5) \cong \mathcal{O}_X$.

**Exercise 2 (easy, multiple choice).**

In a toric degeneration $\pi : \mathfrak{X} \to \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\![t]\!]$ of a smooth Calabi-Yau, the central fibre $\mathfrak{X}_0$ is indexed by:

A. The vertices of the polytope $\Delta$.  
B. The top-dimensional cells of a polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ of $\Delta$.  
C. The interior lattice points of $\Delta$.  
D. The faces of the dual polytope $\Delta^\circ$.

Lower-dimensional cells of $\mathscr{P}$ correspond to lower-dimensional toric strata at intersections of components — these are the gluing data. **Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the dual intersection complex $B(\mathscr{P})$ for the simplest toric degeneration of the elliptic curve. Specifically: take the lattice polytope $\Delta = [0, 3] \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, with polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P} = \{[0, 1], [1, 2], [2, 3]\}$ into three unit segments. Identify the endpoints $0 \sim 3$ to make a circle. Describe $B(\mathscr{P})$ as an integral affine $1$-manifold.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The dual intersection complex glues the dual cones over each cell. For unit segments, the dual cone is a single point at each vertex of the segment; the complex glues these points along the subdivision incidences.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ has:
- $3$ one-dimensional cells (the three unit segments).
- $3$ zero-dimensional cells (the three interior vertices $1, 2$ together with the identified endpoint $0 \sim 3$).

The dual intersection complex $B(\mathscr{P})$ has:
- $3$ vertices, one for each top-dimensional cell of $\mathscr{P}$.
- $3$ edges, one for each pair of adjacent unit segments (i.e., one per zero-dimensional cell of $\mathscr{P}$).

The result is a triangulated circle $S^1$ — three vertices and three edges glued head-to-tail — with the unique integral affine structure compatible with the unit edge-lengths. This is the **integral affine circle of length $3$**, the SYZ base of an elliptic curve whose toric degeneration is to a triangle of three $\mathbb{P}^1$ components. (The simplest case, with one unit segment and self-identified endpoints, gives the integral affine circle of length $1$ and a single self-glued $\mathbb{P}^1$ central fibre.) The dual intersection complex carries no singular locus in dimension $1$: $\Delta_{\mathrm{sing}}$ is empty, and the monodromy of the integral affine structure is the identity. The mirror elliptic curve is the same circle viewed from the dual side; this manifests the SYZ T-duality at its simplest.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Explain why the Calabi-Yau condition $\omega_{\mathfrak{X}/S} \cong \mathcal{O}_\mathfrak{X}$ on the total family of a toric degeneration is *stronger* than asking only that each generic fibre $\mathfrak{X}_t$ be Calabi-Yau.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The relative canonical sheaf is a sheaf on the total space, not just on each fibre. Fibre-wise isomorphism with the structure sheaf does not imply isomorphism on the family, because there could be a non-zero twist by a line bundle pulled back from the base.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a flat proper morphism $\pi : \mathfrak{X} \to S$ with $S$ smooth, the relative canonical sheaf $\omega_{\mathfrak{X}/S}$ restricts on each fibre to the absolute canonical sheaf of that fibre: $\omega_{\mathfrak{X}/S}|_{\mathfrak{X}_t} \cong \omega_{\mathfrak{X}_t}$. So $\omega_{\mathfrak{X}/S} \cong \mathcal{O}_\mathfrak{X}$ implies $\omega_{\mathfrak{X}_t} \cong \mathcal{O}_{\mathfrak{X}_t}$ for every $t$, i.e., every fibre is Calabi-Yau. The converse fails in general: each fibre could be Calabi-Yau while $\omega_{\mathfrak{X}/S}$ fails to be isomorphic to the structure sheaf. Such a twist is precisely a line bundle $L$ on $S$ pulled back to $\mathfrak{X}$ via $\pi^*$ — every such pullback restricts to $\mathcal{O}$ on each fibre but is non-zero globally. The Calabi-Yau condition on the *family* requires both fibre-wise isomorphism with the structure sheaf and the absence of a base twist. Geometrically, this means the Calabi-Yau structure is *coherent* across the family: there is a single nowhere-vanishing relative top form $\Omega \in \Gamma(\mathfrak{X}, \omega_{\mathfrak{X}/S})$, not a family of fibre-wise top forms with monodromy. In the log smooth setting of Gross-Siebert 2006, the form $\Omega$ is allowed logarithmic poles along the central fibre and the condition becomes $\omega_{\mathfrak{X}/S}(\log \mathfrak{X}_0) \cong \mathcal{O}_\mathfrak{X}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

State the maximally unipotent monodromy condition for a one-parameter family of smooth Calabi-Yau $3$-folds in terms of the monodromy operator on $H^3(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{C})$, and explain why the maximal-Jordan-block-size formulation $(T - I)^3 \neq 0, (T - I)^4 = 0$ is equivalent to the limit-mixed-Hodge-structure statement.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The limit mixed Hodge structure has a weight filtration $W_\bullet$ on $H^n_{\mathrm{lim}}$ with $0 \leq W_k \leq 2n$; the monodromy operator $N = \log T$ shifts weights by $-2$. Maximal Jordan block on $H^n$ means $N$ has rank $n$ as a nilpotent endomorphism, which translates back to monodromy via $T = \exp N$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a smooth Calabi-Yau $3$-fold $\mathfrak{X}_t$, the monodromy operator $T$ acts on $H^3(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{C})$, which has dimension $b_3(\mathfrak{X}_t) = 2 + 2h^{2, 1}(\mathfrak{X}_t)$. The MUM condition is
$$
(T - I)^3 \;\neq\; 0, \qquad (T - I)^4 \;=\; 0,
$$
i.e., $T - I$ is nilpotent of index exactly $4$ on $H^3$. Equivalently, the logarithm $N = \log T = (T - I) - \tfrac{1}{2}(T - I)^2 + \cdots$ is nilpotent of index $4$. The limit mixed Hodge structure (Deligne 1971, Schmid 1973) on $H^3$ has weight filtration
$$
0 \subseteq W_0 \subseteq W_1 \subseteq W_2 \subseteq W_3 \subseteq W_4 \subseteq W_5 \subseteq W_6 = H^3_{\mathrm{lim}}.
$$
The maximally unipotent condition $N^3 \neq 0, N^4 = 0$ forces $W_0 \neq 0$ (the bottom of the weight filtration is one-dimensional, generated by $N^3 v$ for any $v$ with $N^3 v \neq 0$) and $W_6 / W_5 \neq 0$ (the top of the weight filtration is one-dimensional). The MUM condition is therefore equivalent to the weight filtration having maximal jump pattern $(1, h^{2, 1}, h^{2, 1}, 1, h^{2, 1}, h^{2, 1}, 1)$ on the graded pieces $\mathrm{gr}^W_k$. This Hodge-theoretic pattern is the algebraic-geometric counterpart of the large-volume limit on the mirror $3$-fold under the Morrison mirror map ([Morrison 1993](#ref-morrison-1993)).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Discuss the relationship between *toric degeneration of a Calabi-Yau* and the Type-III degeneration of a K3 surface in the Kulikov / Persson-Pinkham classification.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A semistable degeneration of K3 surfaces falls into one of three types by Kulikov 1977 and Persson-Pinkham 1981. Type-III is the case where the central fibre has multiple components meeting along a triangulation. The dual intersection complex is a triangulation of the $2$-sphere.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A *semistable degeneration* of K3 surfaces is a one-parameter flat family $\pi : \mathfrak{X} \to \Delta$ over a disc with smooth generic fibre a K3 surface and reduced normal-crossings central fibre. Kulikov 1977 and Persson-Pinkham 1981 classified such degenerations into three types according to the monodromy operator $T$ on $H^2(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{C})$:

- **Type I:** $T = I$. The central fibre is itself a smooth K3 surface (a so-called good reduction).
- **Type II:** $(T - I) \neq 0$, $(T - I)^2 = 0$. The central fibre is a chain of elliptic ruled surfaces meeting along smooth elliptic curves; the dual intersection complex is an interval.
- **Type III:** $(T - I)^2 \neq 0$, $(T - I)^3 = 0$ — the **maximally unipotent monodromy** case. The central fibre is a union of rational surfaces meeting along rational curves in a triangulation pattern, and the dual intersection complex is a triangulation of the $2$-sphere $S^2$.

The **Type-III K3 degeneration is exactly the toric degeneration of a K3 surface** in the sense of the present unit, with $n = 2$. The dual intersection complex is a triangulated $2$-sphere with a finite set $\Delta_{\mathrm{sing}}$ of codimension-$2$ (i.e., zero-dimensional) integral-affine singularities, and the monodromy of the integral affine structure around each singular point encodes the topological data of the SYZ fibration. The reverse direction — given a triangulated $2$-sphere with appropriate integral affine structure and singularities, construct a Type-III K3 degeneration — is the K3 case of the Gross-Siebert reconstruction theorem (Gross-Siebert 2011 *Annals* 174). The Kulikov / Persson-Pinkham classification is therefore the precursor of the Gross-Siebert programme in dimension $2$: it identified the right combinatorial framework, and the Gross-Siebert programme generalised it to all dimensions and turned the existence question into an algorithmic reconstruction.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Discuss why the polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ in a toric degeneration of a Calabi-Yau hypersurface must be *regular* (induced by a strictly convex piecewise-linear function on $\Delta$), and what fails for non-regular subdivisions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Mumford degeneration construction lifts each monomial $\chi^m$ to $t^{\varphi(m)} \chi^m$ for a function $\varphi : \Delta \cap M \to \mathbb{Z}$. The induced subdivision is the lower envelope of the lifted points. Regularity of $\mathscr{P}$ means the function $\varphi$ extends to a strictly convex piecewise-linear function on all of $\Delta$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ of a lattice polytope $\Delta$ is **regular** (or *coherent*) if there exists a strictly convex piecewise-linear function $\varphi : \Delta \to \mathbb{R}$ taking integer values at lattice points $\Delta \cap M$, whose **lower convex hull** has projection cells exactly the cells of $\mathscr{P}$: each top-dimensional cell $\Delta_i \in \mathscr{P}^{\max}$ is the projection of a face of $\{(m, \varphi(m)) : m \in \Delta \cap M\}$ on which $\varphi$ is linear. The Mumford degeneration of [Mumford 1972](#ref-mumford-1972) is built from such a $\varphi$: each monomial $\chi^m$ is lifted to $t^{\varphi(m)} \chi^m$, and the central fibre at $t = 0$ realises exactly $\mathscr{P}$ as its polyhedral data.

For a non-regular subdivision $\mathscr{P}'$, no such function $\varphi$ exists. The Gelfand-Kapranov-Zelevinsky theory (Gelfand-Kapranov-Zelevinsky 1994 *Discriminants, Resultants, and Multidimensional Determinants*) shows that non-regular subdivisions of a polytope $\Delta$ exist starting in dimension $4$ — the *Mnev universality theorem* even shows the moduli of subdivisions can have arbitrary semi-algebraic topology. For a non-regular $\mathscr{P}'$, the toric degeneration construction breaks down at Step 2 of the proof: there is no Mumford function $\varphi$ to lift the monomials, and consequently no flat one-parameter family realising $\mathscr{P}'$ as its central-fibre polyhedral data. Geometrically, non-regular subdivisions cannot be realised as the central fibre of a *one-parameter* degeneration but only as central fibres of higher-parameter families (deformations over higher-dimensional bases), and the Gross-Siebert programme specifically requires the one-parameter setup to produce maximally unipotent monodromy. Regularity is therefore the precise combinatorial condition equivalent to the existence of the toric degeneration as defined in this unit. The deeper structural reason is the **secondary polytope** of Gelfand-Kapranov-Zelevinsky: regular subdivisions of $\Delta$ are exactly the vertices of the secondary polytope $\mathrm{Sec}(\Delta)$, and the toric variety associated to $\mathrm{Sec}(\Delta)$ parametrises the regular subdivisions in a canonical way.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

State the relationship between the toric degeneration of a Calabi-Yau threefold and the Strominger-Yau-Zaslow conjecture, with attention to the role of the dual intersection complex.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The SYZ conjecture says mirror Calabi-Yau pairs admit dual special-Lagrangian torus fibrations over a common base. The toric-degeneration setup makes this base algebraic.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Strominger-Yau-Zaslow conjecture (SYZ; [Strominger-Yau-Zaslow 1996](#ref-strominger-yau-zaslow-1996)) asserts that a mirror Calabi-Yau pair $(X, X^\vee)$ in real dimension $2n$ near the large-complex-structure limit admits **dual special-Lagrangian torus fibrations**
$$
X \to B, \qquad X^\vee \to B,
$$
over a common $n$-dimensional base $B$ carrying an integral affine structure (away from a codimension-$2$ singular locus $\Delta_{\mathrm{sing}}$), with the fibres of the two fibrations being mutually dual $n$-tori related by Fourier-Mukai transform / fibrewise T-duality. The conjecture is symplectic-side and analytic in nature; the construction of the special-Lagrangian fibrations is conjectural and known only in low-dimensional or highly-symmetric cases.

The toric-degeneration setup of this unit gives the **algebraic-geometric realisation** of the SYZ base. Starting from a toric degeneration $\pi : \mathfrak{X} \to \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\![t]\!]$ of $X$ with polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ of the polytope $\Delta$, the dual intersection complex $B(\mathscr{P})$ is an integral affine $n$-manifold with codimension-$2$ singularities, topologically a sphere $S^n$ in the Calabi-Yau case. The Gross-Siebert programme of 2006-2011 establishes:

1. The dual intersection complex $B(\mathscr{P})$ *is* the SYZ base of $X$ in the algebraic-geometric realisation.
2. The same combinatorial data $(B(\mathscr{P}), \mathscr{P})$, viewed from the dual side via the polar polytope $\Delta^\circ$, gives a toric degeneration of the mirror Calabi-Yau $X^\vee$.
3. The integral affine structure with codimension-$2$ monodromy singularities encodes the topological data of the dual torus fibrations.
4. The full smoothing $\mathfrak{X}_t \to X$ for $t \neq 0$ is reconstructed from $B(\mathscr{P})$ by the Gross-Siebert reconstruction theorem (Gross-Siebert 2011), with the tropical-curve counts of the Nishinou-Siebert correspondence (`[04.12.06]`) entering as wall-crossing data.

The toric degeneration is therefore the bridge from the analytic SYZ heuristic to the algebraic combinatorial structure, and the dual intersection complex is the central object common to both descriptions of mirror symmetry. The Calabi-Yau geometry is encoded in the integral affine geometry of $B(\mathscr{P})$.

</details>

</aside>

## The polyhedral subdivision and the Mumford function [Master]

The polyhedral input $\mathscr{P}$ to the toric-degeneration construction is not arbitrary: it is required to be a **regular polyhedral subdivision** of the lattice polytope $\Delta$, induced by a strictly convex integer-valued piecewise-linear function $\varphi$ — the **Mumford function**. We elaborate this combinatorial setup, which is the heart of the construction.

**Regular subdivisions and the lower convex hull.** Let $\Delta \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ be a lattice polytope of dimension $n$ in a lattice $M \cong \mathbb{Z}^n$, and let $\Delta \cap M = \{m_1, \ldots, m_N\}$ be its lattice points. Choose a function $\varphi : \Delta \cap M \to \mathbb{Z}$ assigning an integer to each lattice point. The **lower envelope** of the lifted point set $\{(m_i, \varphi(m_i)) : i = 1, \ldots, N\} \subseteq M_\mathbb{R} \times \mathbb{R}$ is the boundary of the convex hull facing down ($z \to -\infty$); explicitly, its faces are the supporting hyperplanes from below. Projecting the maximal faces of the lower envelope back to $M_\mathbb{R}$ produces a polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}_\varphi$ of $\Delta$. A subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ obtained this way is called **regular** or **coherent**.

The function $\varphi$ extends uniquely to a piecewise-linear function $\Phi : \Delta \to \mathbb{R}$ that is linear on each cell of $\mathscr{P}_\varphi$ and agrees with $\varphi$ on lattice points. **Strict convexity** of $\Phi$ (i.e., $\Phi$ is strictly below the affine extension of $\varphi$ to any non-cell line segment) corresponds to $\mathscr{P}_\varphi$ being a proper subdivision into multiple top-dimensional cells.

The **secondary polytope** of [Gelfand-Kapranov-Zelevinsky 1994](#ref-gkz-1994) is the convex polytope $\mathrm{Sec}(\Delta) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{|\Delta \cap M|}$ whose vertices are exactly the regular subdivisions of $\Delta$; the lower-envelope construction $\varphi \mapsto \mathscr{P}_\varphi$ is the projection onto vertices of $\mathrm{Sec}(\Delta)$. Non-regular subdivisions exist starting in dimension $4$ but do not arise from any single $\varphi$; the *Mnev universality theorem* shows the moduli of subdivisions can have arbitrary semi-algebraic topology.

**The Mumford degeneration.** With $\varphi$ and the induced regular subdivision $\mathscr{P} = \mathscr{P}_\varphi$ fixed, the **Mumford degeneration** lifts each monomial $\chi^m$ for $m \in \Delta \cap M$ to a $t$-weighted monomial
$$
\widetilde{\chi^m} \;:=\; t^{\varphi(m)} \chi^m \;\in\; \mathbb{C}[\Delta \cap M, t].
$$
The relations defining the toric variety $Y_\Delta$ — the binomial relations $\chi^{m_1 + m_2} = \chi^{m_3 + m_4}$ for lattice equations $m_1 + m_2 = m_3 + m_4$ — lift to weighted relations
$$
t^{\varphi(m_1) + \varphi(m_2)} \widetilde{\chi^{m_1}} \widetilde{\chi^{m_2}} \;=\; t^{\varphi(m_3) + \varphi(m_4)} \widetilde{\chi^{m_3}} \widetilde{\chi^{m_4}},
$$
which define the total space of a flat one-parameter family $\mathfrak{Y} \to \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\![t]\!]$ with generic fibre $\mathfrak{Y}_t \cong Y_\Delta$ for $t \neq 0$ and central fibre $\mathfrak{Y}_0$ a reduced union of toric varieties indexed by the top-dimensional cells of $\mathscr{P}$. This is the **Mumford degeneration** of the toric Fano $Y_\Delta$.

The Calabi-Yau case is obtained by intersecting the Mumford family $\mathfrak{Y} \to \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\![t]\!]$ with a flat family of Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces: take a generic section $s \in H^0(Y_\Delta, -K_{Y_\Delta})$, lift it to the Mumford family by replacing each monomial $\chi^m$ in $s$ with $\widetilde{\chi^m}$, and let $\mathfrak{X} \subseteq \mathfrak{Y}$ be the zero locus of the lifted section. The result is the toric degeneration of $X = \{s = 0\}$ described in the proof of the Key Theorem.

**Cells, strata, and dimension counts.** The combinatorics of $\mathscr{P}$ controls the structure of the central fibre $\mathfrak{X}_0$ in three layers:

- **Top-dimensional cells** $\Delta_i \in \mathscr{P}^{\max}$, of dimension $n + 1 = \dim \Delta$, index the **irreducible components** of $\mathfrak{Y}_0$. The Calabi-Yau central fibre $\mathfrak{X}_0 \subseteq \mathfrak{Y}_0$ has its irreducible components indexed by the same cells (intersected with the hyperplane condition $s = 0$).

- **Codimension-$1$ cells** $\tau \in \mathscr{P}^{(n)}$, faces shared between two top-dimensional cells, index the **codimension-$1$ toric strata** of $\mathfrak{X}_0$ along which two components meet. These are the "slabs" $\tau$ that will carry the **slab functions** of the Gross-Siebert reconstruction (forthcoming sibling unit).

- **Codimension-$k$ cells** for $k \geq 2$ index higher-codimension toric strata; the **codimension-$2$ strata** are particularly important because they support the **singular locus $\Delta_{\mathrm{sing}}$** of the integral affine structure on the dual intersection complex $B(\mathscr{P})$.

The dimension formula $\dim \tau + \dim X_\tau = n + 1$ (in the toric ambient) and $\dim \tau + \dim (X_\tau \cap \mathfrak{X}_0) = n$ (on the Calabi-Yau central fibre) records the polytope-stratum duality of `[04.11.06]` (orbit-cone correspondence) and is the polyhedral combinatorial fact that underlies the recursive structure of the Gross-Siebert reconstruction.

## The dual intersection complex and its integral affine structure [Master]

The **dual intersection complex** $B(\mathscr{P})$ of the regular polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ is the central combinatorial object encoding the Calabi-Yau geometry on the algebraic side and the SYZ base on the symplectic side. Its construction and integral affine structure are the technical content of [Gross-Siebert 2006](#ref-gross-siebert-2006) §2.

**Construction of $B(\mathscr{P})$.** For each cell $\tau \in \mathscr{P}$, write $\sigma_\tau \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ for the cone of linear functionals on $M_\mathbb{R}$ that are constant on $\tau$; this cone has dimension $n + 1 - \dim \tau$. The dual intersection complex glues these cones along the face incidences of $\mathscr{P}$: for $\tau' \preceq \tau$ a face inclusion of cells, the cone $\sigma_\tau$ is a face of $\sigma_{\tau'}$, and the gluing in $B(\mathscr{P})$ identifies them along this face. Explicitly,
$$
B(\mathscr{P}) \;=\; \bigcup_{\tau \in \mathscr{P}} \sigma_\tau \;\big/\; \sim,
$$
where $\sim$ is the equivalence relation generated by the face inclusions. The result is a $(n+1)$-dimensional cone complex (for the toric Fano version) or, after restricting to the Calabi-Yau hypersurface, an $n$-dimensional polyhedral complex.

For the **Calabi-Yau case** — when $\Delta$ is the reflexive polytope $-K_Y \cap M$ — there is a canonical reduction: discard the cone-vertex (the apex of every dual cone over a vertex of $\Delta$) and link by a small sphere at the apex. The result is an **$n$-dimensional cell complex** topologically homeomorphic to the $n$-sphere $S^n$, i.e., $B(\mathscr{P}) \cong S^n$ for Calabi-Yau toric degenerations with reflexive Newton polytope. This sphere is the SYZ base of the smooth Calabi-Yau hypersurface $X \subseteq Y$.

**Integral affine structure.** On each top-dimensional cell of $B(\mathscr{P})$, the lattice $N \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ restricts to give a lattice of integer affine charts. The transition maps between charts on adjacent top cells are determined by the polyhedral combinatorics of $\mathscr{P}$ and the lattice structure of $N$ — explicitly, the change of basis between the integer bases of the two adjacent dual cones, as recorded by the integral lattice transformation gluing them along the common face. These transition maps are **integral affine** (elements of $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z}) \ltimes \mathbb{Z}^n$, the integer affine group): they preserve the lattice $N$ pointwise on faces and act by integer-linear transformations.

**The singular locus $\Delta_{\mathrm{sing}}$.** The integral affine structure does not extend continuously across codimension-$2$ cells of $B(\mathscr{P})$ in general: the **monodromy** around a codimension-$2$ cell, computed by composing the transition maps in a small loop, may be a non-identity element of $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z})$. The **integral affine singular locus** is the union $\Delta_{\mathrm{sing}} \subseteq B(\mathscr{P})$ of codimension-$2$ cells around which the monodromy is non-identity. In the Calabi-Yau case, $\Delta_{\mathrm{sing}}$ is a real codimension-$2$ subcomplex; for $\dim X = 2$ (K3 case), $\Delta_{\mathrm{sing}}$ is a finite set of points on $S^2$; for $\dim X = 3$ (Calabi-Yau threefold), $\Delta_{\mathrm{sing}}$ is a $1$-dimensional graph in $S^3$ — the **discriminant locus** of the SYZ fibration.

**Simple singularities.** A toric degeneration of a Calabi-Yau is **simple** (in the Gross-Siebert sense) if every singular point of $\Delta_{\mathrm{sing}}$ has a standard model of low complexity — explicitly, the local monodromy around the singular point is conjugate to one of a finite list of standard matrices. The simplicity hypothesis is what makes the [Gross-Siebert 2011](#ref-gross-siebert-2011) reconstruction theorem run; non-simple singularities exist (e.g., from non-generic Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces) but require an extended framework.

**The Friedman d-semistability condition.** A normal-crossings degeneration $\mathfrak{X}_0$ of a Calabi-Yau is **d-semistable** in the sense of [Friedman 1983](#ref-friedman-1983) if the line bundle $\mathcal{E}xt^1(\Omega^1_{\mathfrak{X}_0}, \mathcal{O}_{\mathfrak{X}_0})$ on the singular locus of $\mathfrak{X}_0$ is isomorphic to the structure sheaf; this is the obstruction to smoothing $\mathfrak{X}_0$ to a smooth Calabi-Yau in a one-parameter family. Friedman's theorem is that d-semistability is equivalent to smoothability for normal-crossings Calabi-Yau central fibres. The toric-degeneration setup of Gross-Siebert resolves the d-semistability obstruction combinatorially through the integral affine structure on $B(\mathscr{P})$: the existence of a global integral affine structure with the right monodromy data is the combinatorial avatar of Friedman's d-semistability, and the simplicity hypothesis specifies a particular convenient local form.

## Maximally unipotent monodromy and the large-complex-structure limit [Master]

The **maximally unipotent monodromy** (MUM) condition at the central-fibre parameter $t = 0$ is the Hodge-theoretic property that identifies the toric-degeneration boundary point as the algebraic-geometric counterpart of the large-volume limit on the mirror Calabi-Yau under [Morrison's mirror map](#ref-morrison-1993).

**Limit mixed Hodge structure.** Let $\pi : \mathfrak{X} \to \Delta_\epsilon$ be a one-parameter family of smooth Calabi-Yau $n$-folds away from $t = 0$, and consider the cohomology $H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{C})$ for $t \neq 0$. Schmid's nilpotent orbit theorem ([Deligne 1971](#ref-deligne-1971); Schmid 1973 *Inventiones* 22) constructs a **limit mixed Hodge structure** on the limit fibre $H^n_{\mathrm{lim}}$, consisting of:

- A Hodge filtration $F^\bullet$ on $H^n_{\mathrm{lim}, \mathbb{C}}$ obtained as the limit of the Hodge filtrations $F^\bullet(t)$ on $H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t)$ as $t \to 0$ (after twisting by an appropriate $t^N$ factor to compensate for monodromy).
- A weight filtration $W_\bullet$ on $H^n_{\mathrm{lim}, \mathbb{Q}}$ characterised by the property that the monodromy logarithm $N = \log T_\mathrm{u}$ (the unipotent part of monodromy) is a $(-2, -2)$-morphism: $N(W_k) \subseteq W_{k-2}$, and $N^k : W_{n+k}/W_{n+k-1} \to W_{n-k}/W_{n-k-1}$ is an isomorphism for all $k \geq 0$.

The pair $(W_\bullet, F^\bullet)$ is the **limit mixed Hodge structure on $H^n_{\mathrm{lim}}$**, a deformation of the pure Hodge structure $H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t)$ at $t \neq 0$ into a mixed Hodge structure at $t = 0$.

**The MUM condition.** Maximally unipotent monodromy is the condition that the monodromy logarithm $N$ on $H^n_{\mathrm{lim}}$ has **maximal nilpotency index $n + 1$**: equivalently, $N^n \neq 0$ and $N^{n+1} = 0$. Translating back to $T = \exp N$ gives $(T - I)^n \neq 0$ and $(T - I)^{n+1} = 0$. Hodge-theoretically, MUM is equivalent to the weight filtration on $H^n_{\mathrm{lim}}$ having maximal jump pattern: the graded pieces $\mathrm{gr}^W_{n + k}$ for $k = -n, -n+1, \ldots, n-1, n$ are non-zero, and the Hodge numbers form a unimodal sequence with maximal width.

For Calabi-Yau $n$-folds with $h^{p, q}(\mathfrak{X}_t)$ the Hodge numbers, the MUM weight filtration on $H^n_{\mathrm{lim}}$ has graded pieces of dimensions
$$
\dim \mathrm{gr}^W_{n + k} \;=\; h^{n, n} \cdot \delta_{k, n} + h^{n - 1, n - 1} \cdot \delta_{k, n - 2} + \cdots
$$
following the pattern of Lefschetz decomposition. The bottom $\mathrm{gr}^W_0$ is one-dimensional (the "vanishing cycle") and the top $\mathrm{gr}^W_{2n}$ is one-dimensional (the "polar cycle"); these generate a Jordan block of size $n + 1$ under $N$. In the Calabi-Yau threefold case ($n = 3$, dimensions $(1, h^{2,1}, h^{2,1}, 1, h^{2,1}, h^{2,1}, 1)$ on the seven graded pieces), the Jordan block size is exactly $4$, and the rest of $H^3_{\mathrm{lim}}$ decomposes into smaller Jordan blocks.

**Why toric degenerations are MUM.** The toric-degeneration setup of [Gross-Siebert 2006](#ref-gross-siebert-2006) §2.3 establishes the MUM property by direct computation on the dual intersection complex. The key ingredient is:

- The monodromy logarithm $N$ on $H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t, \mathbb{C})$ is computed by the **Clemens-Schmid sequence** as the boundary map of a long exact sequence relating $H^n(\mathfrak{X}_t)$, $H^n(\mathfrak{X}_0)$, and the cohomology of the singular locus of $\mathfrak{X}_0$.
- For a toric central fibre, the singular locus is the union of toric strata of codimension $\geq 1$, whose cohomology is computed by the polytope combinatorics of $\mathscr{P}$ via the Danilov-Jurkiewicz theorem (`[04.11.12]` toric cohomology).
- The Calabi-Yau condition $\Delta$ reflexive, combined with the regular subdivision $\mathscr{P}$, forces the maximal jump in the weight filtration: the unique top lattice point of $\Delta$ (the origin, in the reflexive case) corresponds to a one-dimensional $\mathrm{gr}^W_{2n}$, and the unique bottom volume cycle of the integral-affine sphere $B(\mathscr{P}) \cong S^n$ corresponds to a one-dimensional $\mathrm{gr}^W_0$.

**The Morrison mirror map.** [Morrison 1993](#ref-morrison-1993) established that MUM points of the complex moduli space of Calabi-Yau $n$-folds are the algebraic analogues of large-volume limits on the symplectic side: at a MUM point of $\mathcal{M}^{\mathrm{cpx}}(X)$, the local parameters can be canonically identified (via the leading-order asymptotics of the period integrals $\int_{\gamma_i} \Omega_t$) with Kähler parameters on the mirror $X^\vee$. The **mirror map**
$$
\tau \mapsto t = \exp(2 \pi i \, \tau), \qquad \tau \in \mathcal{M}^{\mathrm{K\"ah}}(X^\vee)
$$
identifies neighbourhoods of MUM points in $\mathcal{M}^{\mathrm{cpx}}(X)$ with neighbourhoods of large-volume limits in $\mathcal{M}^{\mathrm{K\"ah}}(X^\vee)$, and the toric-degeneration setup gives the canonical algebraic realisation of this identification.

**Bridge to the Gross-Siebert reconstruction.** The MUM condition at the toric-degeneration boundary point is the precise statement that the integral affine structure on $B(\mathscr{P})$ has the right monodromy data to encode the Calabi-Yau topology. The Gross-Siebert reconstruction theorem (Gross-Siebert 2011 *Annals* 174) inverts the toric-degeneration setup by reconstructing the smoothing $\mathfrak{X}_t$ from $(B(\mathscr{P}), \text{integral affine + monodromy data})$. The MUM condition is the integrability condition that makes the inversion well-defined: a non-MUM degeneration would correspond to a different boundary point of the moduli space (e.g., a conifold), and the reconstruction procedure would not produce the right smoothing.

## Connections [Master]

- **Fan and the toric variety [04.11.04].** The toric components $X_{\Delta_i}$ of the central fibre $\mathfrak{X}_0$ are toric varieties in the sense of `[04.11.04]`: each one is built from its Newton polytope $\Delta_i$ (a cell of the polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$) via the polytope-fan construction, with the polytope $\Delta_i$ corresponding to a polarised toric variety $X_{\Delta_i}$ via the dictionary of `[04.11.10]`. The polytope-fan formalism of `[04.11.04]` is the prerequisite combinatorial input on which the entire toric-degeneration construction rests; without the polytope-to-toric-variety dictionary there would be no way to assemble the central fibre from the polyhedral subdivision.

- **Newton polytope and non-archimedean amoeba [04.12.04].** The lifted-monomial Mumford construction $\widetilde{\chi^m} = t^{\varphi(m)} \chi^m$ that drives the toric-degeneration setup is the *one-parameter family* version of the non-archimedean amoeba of `[04.12.04]`: the tropicalisation of the Mumford family $\mathfrak{X}_t$ for $t = e^{-r}$ as $r \to \infty$ is exactly the non-archimedean amoeba over the Puiseux-series field $\mathbb{C}\{\{t\}\}$, and the polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ is the secondary-polytope-vertex realisation of the corresponding tropical hypersurface. The dual intersection complex $B(\mathscr{P})$ is the tropical skeleton of this non-archimedean amoeba, and the integral affine structure on $B(\mathscr{P})$ is the integral affine structure on the skeleton.

- **Nishinou-Siebert correspondence [04.12.06].** The Nishinou-Siebert correspondence of `[04.12.06]` is the toric-prototype of the present unit. There, the smooth target is a toric variety $X_\Sigma$ rather than a Calabi-Yau, and the toric degeneration is to a polyhedral subdivision of the Newton polytope of an ample line bundle on $X_\Sigma$. The present unit specialises the framework to Calabi-Yau targets (with the reflexivity of the Newton polytope as the new ingredient), and the tropical curves on $B(\mathscr{P})$ enumerated by `[04.12.06]` are the wall-crossing data on the dual intersection complex of the present unit that feed the Gross-Siebert reconstruction.

- **Polytope-fan dictionary; line bundle $L_P$ [04.11.10].** The Mumford degeneration construction in the proof of the Key Theorem relies on the polytope-fan dictionary of `[04.11.10]`: the toric Fano $Y_\Sigma$ corresponds to its reflexive polytope $\nabla$, the anticanonical line bundle $-K_Y$ corresponds to the polar dual $\Delta = \nabla^\circ$, and the Calabi-Yau hypersurface $X \subseteq Y$ is the zero locus of a generic section in the lattice-point basis of $H^0(Y, -K_Y) = \bigoplus_{m \in \Delta \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m$. The reflexivity of $\Delta$, in turn, is the Batyrev mirror-symmetry condition of `[04.11.10]`: $\Delta$ and $\nabla = \Delta^\circ$ are mirror polytopes, and their associated Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces are mirror Calabi-Yau pairs.

- **Kodaira embedding theorem [04.09.11].** The smooth Calabi-Yau fibres $\mathfrak{X}_t$ of the toric degeneration are projective varieties, embedded in $\mathbb{P}^N$ via the Kodaira embedding theorem of `[04.09.11]` applied to a sufficiently positive line bundle. For Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces in toric Fano varieties, the polarisation comes from the ambient toric polarisation $L_P$ of `[04.11.10]`; the Kodaira embedding theorem of `[04.09.11]` guarantees that the resulting line bundle is sufficiently positive to give a projective embedding. The toric-degeneration setup respects the polarisation throughout the family: the central fibre $\mathfrak{X}_0$ is itself projectively embedded via the same lattice-point basis, with toric components glued along their projective sub-embeddings.

- **Tropical semiring and tropical polynomial [04.12.01].** The Mumford function $\varphi : \Delta \cap M \to \mathbb{Z}$ that determines the polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ is, by the lower-envelope construction, a *tropical polynomial* in the sense of `[04.12.01]`: the function $\Phi(u) = \min_{m \in \Delta \cap M} (\varphi(m) + \langle u, m \rangle)$ on $N_\mathbb{R}$ is a tropical polynomial whose corner locus is the codimension-$1$ skeleton of $\mathscr{P}$. The polyhedral subdivision $\mathscr{P}$ is the regular subdivision of $\Delta$ induced by this tropical polynomial. The toric-degeneration setup of the present unit is therefore the algebraic-geometric realisation of the tropical-polynomial / regular-subdivision combinatorics of `[04.12.01]` and `[04.12.04]`.

- **Tropical curve as balanced rational metric graph [04.12.02].** The tropical curves living on the dual intersection complex $B(\mathscr{P})$ — entering the wall-crossing data of the Gross-Siebert reconstruction via `[04.12.06]` — are balanced rational metric graphs in the sense of `[04.12.02]`, with the balancing condition holding at every vertex when read against the integral affine structure of $B(\mathscr{P})$. The combinatorial enumeration of these tropical curves is the input to the scattering algorithm that reconstructs the smoothing $\mathfrak{X}_t$ from $B(\mathscr{P})$.

- **Mikhalkin correspondence theorem [04.12.05].** Mikhalkin's correspondence theorem of `[04.12.05]` is the dimension-$2$ specialisation of the tropical-algebraic correspondence on the toric-degeneration framework: for $\dim X = 2$ (K3 surface or abelian surface), the Mikhalkin / Nishinou-Siebert enumeration of tropical curves on $B(\mathscr{P}) \cong S^2$ produces the local Gromov-Witten contributions to the K3 mirror reconstruction. The Kulikov Type-III K3 degeneration is the dimension-$2$ case of the toric-degeneration setup of the present unit.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The toric-degeneration construction has three sources, each emerging from a distinct mathematical tradition. The first is **Mumford's analytic degenerations of Abelian varieties** ([Mumford 1972](#ref-mumford-1972)), where Mumford constructed one-parameter degenerations of polarised Abelian varieties by tilting the period lattice via a piecewise-linear function on a polytope — the original Mumford function. The construction was vastly generalised in [Kempf-Knudsen-Mumford-Saint-Donat 1973](#ref-kkms-1973) *Toroidal Embeddings I* to arbitrary toroidal targets, establishing the polyhedral-degeneration framework that underlies all subsequent work in the area. The toric-degeneration setup of [Gross-Siebert 2006](#ref-gross-siebert-2006) is, in this lineage, the Calabi-Yau hypersurface variant of the Mumford-KKMS construction.

The second source is the **classification of K3 degenerations** by [Kulikov 1977](#ref-kulikov-1977) and the parallel Western development by [Persson-Pinkham 1981](#ref-persson-pinkham-1981). Their three-type classification (Type-I good reduction, Type-II elliptic-ruled chains, Type-III rational-surface triangulations) provided the first concrete examples of the polyhedral picture for higher-dimensional Calabi-Yau varieties, and the Type-III case identified the maximally unipotent monodromy boundary point as combinatorially distinguished. The Kulikov classification was the prototype of the Gross-Siebert reconstruction in dimension $2$, and the toric-degeneration setup is the dimension-$n$ generalisation.

The third source is **mirror symmetry**, originating in the physics of [Strominger-Yau-Zaslow 1996](#ref-strominger-yau-zaslow-1996) and the algebraic geometry of Morrison and Friedman. [Morrison 1993](#ref-morrison-1993) identified MUM points as the algebraic analogues of large-volume limits, and [Friedman 1983](#ref-friedman-1983) had earlier established the d-semistability obstruction theory for smoothing normal-crossings degenerations. The Strominger-Yau-Zaslow conjecture, conceived in 1996 from the physics of dual brane fibrations on Calabi-Yau threefolds, provided the picture that the toric-degeneration setup later realised algebraically: the SYZ base is the dual intersection complex.

The synthesis of these three sources is the achievement of [Gross-Siebert 2006](#ref-gross-siebert-2006). The paper introduces the toric-degeneration framework, defines the dual intersection complex with its integral affine structure, identifies the maximally unipotent monodromy property as a consequence of the polyhedral data, and sets up the reconstruction problem solved fully in [Gross-Siebert 2011](#ref-gross-siebert-2011) *Annals* 174. The subsequent extension to log Calabi-Yau pairs ([Gross-Hacking-Keel 2015](#ref-gross-hacking-keel-2015) *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 122) and to cluster algebras (Gross-Hacking-Keel-Kontsevich 2018 *J. AMS* 31) has continued to expand the framework. The textbook synthesis of [Gross 2011](#ref-gross-2011-cbms) *Tropical Geometry and Mirror Symmetry* (CBMS Regional Conference Series 114) provides the canonical exposition of the toric-degeneration setup in the context of the full Gross-Siebert programme; Lecture 4 of that text is the anchor for the present unit.

The non-archimedean counterpart of the toric degeneration was developed in parallel by [Kontsevich-Soibelman 2006](#ref-kontsevich-soibelman-2006) *Affine structures and non-Archimedean analytic spaces*: there the dual intersection complex appears as the **Berkovich skeleton** of the non-archimedean analytic Calabi-Yau over the Puiseux-series field, and the integral affine structure is the structure of the skeleton inherited from the polyhedral data. The two approaches — Gross-Siebert algebraic and Kontsevich-Soibelman non-archimedean — agree at the level of the dual intersection complex and have been compared explicitly in subsequent work (Nicaise-Xu-Yu 2019 *J. Reine Angew. Math.* 753; Mustață-Nicaise 2015 *Algebraic Geometry* 2).
