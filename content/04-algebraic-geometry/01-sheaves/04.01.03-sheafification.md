## Intuition [Beginner]

A *presheaf* is a system of local data: an assignment of a set (or group, or ring) to each open set, with restriction maps. A *sheaf* is a presheaf where the local data glues consistently — sections agreeing on overlaps assemble to a unique global section. Many natural constructions (kernels, images, quotients) produce a presheaf that is *not* a sheaf — the local data is correct, but the gluing axiom fails.

*Sheafification* is the universal way to fix this: it takes a presheaf and produces the *closest sheaf*. The result is a sheaf $\mathcal{F}^+$ together with a presheaf morphism $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}^+$, satisfying the universal property that any presheaf morphism $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ to a sheaf $\mathcal{G}$ factors uniquely through $\mathcal{F}^+$.

The key fact: sheafification *preserves stalks*. The germs of $\mathcal{F}^+$ at any point equal the germs of $\mathcal{F}$ at that point. So sheafification is *not* about adding new local data — it is purely about reorganising the global structure to satisfy the gluing axiom. This makes sheafification the central technical tool for working with presheaves: kernels, cokernels, images, tensor products, and pullbacks of sheaves all involve sheafifying an intermediate presheaf.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The *constant presheaf* $\mathcal{F}_A$ on a topological space $X$ assigns the abelian group $A$ to every nonempty open set, with restriction maps the identity:

$$
\mathcal{F}_A(U) = A \quad (\text{for nonempty } U); \qquad \mathcal{F}_A(\emptyset) = 0.
$$

This is a presheaf, but it is *not* a sheaf in general. Consider $X = \{p, q\}$ with the discrete topology. The opens are $\emptyset, \{p\}, \{q\}, \{p, q\}$. Sections of the constant presheaf are: $A, A, A, A$ over the four opens.

The sheaf gluing axiom requires that for the cover $\{p, q\} = \{p\} \cup \{q\}$, sections on $\{p\}$ and $\{q\}$ that "agree on the overlap" $\emptyset$ glue to a unique section on $\{p, q\}$. Two elements of $A$ on $\{p\}$ and $\{q\}$ vacuously agree on $\emptyset$ (which has section group 0), so the sheaf would need $\mathcal{F}_A(\{p, q\}) = A \times A$, not $A$.

The *constant sheaf* $\underline{A}$ is the sheafification: $\underline{A}(U) = A^{\pi_0(U)}$, one copy of $A$ per connected component of $U$. On the discrete two-point space, $\underline{A}(\{p, q\}) = A^2$, matching the gluing requirement.

The constant presheaf and constant sheaf agree on every connected open and on every stalk (each stalk equals $A$); they differ on disconnected opens like $\{p, q\}$. Sheafification fixes the disconnected case while preserving the local (stalk) structure.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a topological space and $\mathcal{F}$ a presheaf on $X$ (of sets, abelian groups, rings, modules, etc.). The **sheafification** of $\mathcal{F}$, denoted $\mathcal{F}^+$, is the sheaf characterised by the following universal property: there is a presheaf morphism $\eta : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}^+$ such that for any sheaf $\mathcal{G}$ and presheaf morphism $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$, there exists a unique sheaf morphism $\widetilde{\varphi} : \mathcal{F}^+ \to \mathcal{G}$ with $\widetilde{\varphi} \circ \eta = \varphi$.

In categorical language: sheafification is the left adjoint to the inclusion functor $\iota : \mathbf{Sh}(X) \hookrightarrow \mathbf{PSh}(X)$,

$$
(-)^+ \dashv \iota, \qquad \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Sh}}(\mathcal{F}^+, \mathcal{G}) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{PSh}}(\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{G}).
$$

**Construction via étale space.** The most concrete construction: form the *étale space*

$$
\mathrm{\acute{E}t}(\mathcal{F}) := \bigsqcup_{x \in X} \mathcal{F}_x,
$$

with the topology making continuous all sections $s : U \to \mathrm{\acute{E}t}(\mathcal{F})$, $x \mapsto s_x$, induced by sections $s \in \mathcal{F}(U)$. Then $\mathcal{F}^+$ is the sheaf of continuous local sections of the projection $\pi : \mathrm{\acute{E}t}(\mathcal{F}) \to X$:

$$
\mathcal{F}^+(U) := \{ \sigma : U \to \mathrm{\acute{E}t}(\mathcal{F}) \mid \sigma \text{ continuous}, \pi \circ \sigma = \mathrm{id}_U \}.
$$

The natural map $\eta : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}^+$ sends $s \in \mathcal{F}(U)$ to the section $x \mapsto s_x$.

**Construction via plus-construction (Grothendieck).** For a general site, sheafification is performed twice, by the *plus construction* $\mathcal{F}^+_0(U) := \varinjlim_{\mathcal{U} \text{ cover of } U} \check{H}^0(\mathcal{U}; \mathcal{F})$, then $\mathcal{F}^+ := \mathcal{F}^+_0 \circ \mathcal{F}^+_0$. On topological spaces, the étale-space construction gives the same result more directly.

**Properties.**

(SF1) *Sheafification preserves stalks.* For every $x \in X$, $\mathcal{F}_x = (\mathcal{F}^+)_x$ as the colimit construction commutes with the locality completion.

(SF2) *Sheafification is the identity on sheaves.* If $\mathcal{F}$ is already a sheaf, then $\eta : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}^+$ is an isomorphism.

(SF3) *Sheafification is exact.* As a left adjoint, $(-)^+$ preserves colimits — including coequalisers, hence cokernels in the abelian-group case. As a left exact functor (filtered colimits commute with finite limits), $(-)^+$ is also left exact. Hence *sheafification is an exact functor* between abelian categories.

(SF4) *Functoriality.* A morphism $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ of presheaves induces a morphism $\varphi^+ : \mathcal{F}^+ \to \mathcal{G}^+$ of sheaves. The plus construction is a functor.

**Use cases.**

1. *Cokernels of sheaf morphisms.* The presheaf cokernel $U \mapsto \mathrm{coker}(\varphi(U))$ is generally not a sheaf; the sheafification produces the cokernel in $\mathbf{Sh}(X; \mathbf{Ab})$.

2. *Image sheaves.* The presheaf image $U \mapsto \mathrm{im}(\varphi(U))$ is generally not a sheaf; sheafification produces the image sheaf, used in the sheaf-version of the first isomorphism theorem.

3. *Tensor products.* For sheaves $\mathcal{F}, \mathcal{G}$ of $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules, the presheaf tensor $U \mapsto \mathcal{F}(U) \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_X(U)} \mathcal{G}(U)$ is generally not a sheaf; sheafification gives the sheaf tensor product $\mathcal{F} \otimes_{\mathcal{O}_X} \mathcal{G}$.

4. *Pullback / inverse image.* For $f : X \to Y$ continuous and $\mathcal{G}$ a sheaf on $Y$, the inverse-image presheaf $U \mapsto \varinjlim_{V \supseteq f(U)} \mathcal{G}(V)$ is generally not a sheaf; sheafification gives $f^{-1}\mathcal{G}$.

5. *Constant sheaf.* The constant presheaf with value $A$ sheafifies to the constant sheaf $\underline{A}$, with $\underline{A}(U) = A^{\pi_0(U)}$.

In each of these constructions, the *presheaf* version is the natural pointwise definition, but the resulting object fails the gluing axiom; *sheafification* is what produces the right sheaf.

**Sheafification on sites.** For a Grothendieck site $(\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{T})$, the sheafification functor still exists as the left adjoint to the inclusion $\mathbf{Sh}(\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{T}) \hookrightarrow \mathbf{PSh}(\mathcal{C})$. Construction via plus-plus or Verdier's stalkwise approximation. This generality is essential in étale cohomology and Grothendieck topologies.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (existence and stalk-preservation of sheafification).** *For any presheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on a topological space $X$, there exists a sheaf $\mathcal{F}^+$ and a presheaf morphism $\eta : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}^+$ satisfying the universal property: any presheaf morphism $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ to a sheaf factors uniquely through $\eta$. Moreover, $\eta$ induces an isomorphism on every stalk: $\mathcal{F}_x \cong (\mathcal{F}^+)_x$ for every $x \in X$.*

**Proof.** *Construction.* Define $\mathcal{F}^+(U)$ as the set of "matching families of germs": functions $\sigma : U \to \bigsqcup_{x \in U} \mathcal{F}_x$ with $\sigma(x) \in \mathcal{F}_x$ for every $x$, locally lifted to a section: every $x \in U$ has an open neighbourhood $V \subseteq U$ and a section $s \in \mathcal{F}(V)$ with $\sigma(y) = s_y$ for all $y \in V$.

Equivalently: $\mathcal{F}^+$ is the sheaf of continuous sections of the étale space $\pi : \mathrm{\acute{E}t}(\mathcal{F}) \to X$, where $\mathrm{\acute{E}t}(\mathcal{F}) = \bigsqcup_x \mathcal{F}_x$ has the topology generated by section-images.

Restriction maps: $\sigma|_V (x) := \sigma(x)$ for $x \in V$ — this is a function $V \to \mathrm{\acute{E}t}(\mathcal{F})$ inheriting the locality property.

*Sheaf axioms.* (Locality) Two elements of $\mathcal{F}^+(U)$ agreeing on every member of an open cover agree pointwise (germs match), hence are equal. (Gluing) A locally given matching family of germs glues to a global matching family, since the locality condition is local — if it holds on each member of a cover, it holds on the union.

*Universal property.* The natural map $\eta : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}^+$ sends $s \in \mathcal{F}(U)$ to $\sigma_s(x) := s_x$ — the matching family of germs of $s$.

For a sheaf $\mathcal{G}$ and a presheaf map $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$, define $\widetilde{\varphi}(U) : \mathcal{F}^+(U) \to \mathcal{G}(U)$ as follows: given $\sigma \in \mathcal{F}^+(U)$, the matching-family condition gives an open cover $\{V_i\}$ of $U$ and sections $s_i \in \mathcal{F}(V_i)$ with $\sigma(x) = (s_i)_x$ for $x \in V_i$. Apply $\varphi$ to get $\varphi(s_i) \in \mathcal{G}(V_i)$. The germs of $\varphi(s_i)$ at any $x \in V_i \cap V_j$ agree (both equal $\varphi_x(\sigma(x))$ via the pointwise compatibility), so by locality of $\mathcal{G}$, $\varphi(s_i)|_{V_i \cap V_j} = \varphi(s_j)|_{V_i \cap V_j}$. By gluing on $\mathcal{G}$, the $\varphi(s_i)$ patch to a unique $\widetilde{\varphi}(\sigma) \in \mathcal{G}(U)$.

This $\widetilde{\varphi}$ is well-defined, satisfies $\widetilde{\varphi} \circ \eta = \varphi$, and is unique by the gluing-uniqueness on $\mathcal{G}$.

*Stalk preservation.* For $x \in X$, both $\mathcal{F}_x$ and $(\mathcal{F}^+)_x$ are computed as colimits over open neighbourhoods. The map $\eta_x : \mathcal{F}_x \to (\mathcal{F}^+)_x$ sends a germ $s_x$ to the germ $\sigma_x$ where $\sigma$ is the matching-family-of-germs section.

Surjectivity: a germ in $(\mathcal{F}^+)_x$ is represented by a matching family $\sigma$ on some neighbourhood $U$ of $x$. By the locality condition, on a smaller $V \subseteq U$ containing $x$, $\sigma$ agrees with the germs of a section $s \in \mathcal{F}(V)$. So $\sigma_x = (\eta(s))_x = \eta_x(s_x)$.

Injectivity: if $\eta_x(s_x) = \eta_x(t_x)$, then on some neighbourhood $V \ni x$, the matching families $\sigma_s$ and $\sigma_t$ agree pointwise. So $s_y = t_y$ for every $y \in V$, hence by the colimit definition $s_x = t_x$ in $\mathcal{F}_x$.

So $\eta_x : \mathcal{F}_x \to (\mathcal{F}^+)_x$ is a bijection. $\square$

This theorem articulates *why* sheafification is the right notion: it takes a presheaf to the unique sheaf with the same stalks, and it is universal among such constructions. The stalk-preservation property is what makes sheafification harmless from the local viewpoint while fixing the global structure.

The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Show that if $\mathcal{F}$ is already a sheaf, then $\eta : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}^+$ is an isomorphism.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The universal property gives $\mathcal{F}^+ \cong \mathcal{F}$ via the identity factorisation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $\mathcal{F}$ is a sheaf, the identity $\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{F}} : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}$ is a presheaf morphism to a sheaf. By the universal property of sheafification, it factors uniquely through $\eta$: there is a unique sheaf morphism $\rho : \mathcal{F}^+ \to \mathcal{F}$ with $\rho \circ \eta = \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{F}}$.

Conversely, $\eta \circ \rho : \mathcal{F}^+ \to \mathcal{F}^+$ and the identity $\mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{F}^+}$ both satisfy: post-composition with $\eta$ gives $\eta$. By the universal property's uniqueness clause, $\eta \circ \rho = \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{F}^+}$.

So $\eta$ and $\rho$ are mutually inverse, $\eta$ is an isomorphism. $\square$

This is an instance of the general fact: a left adjoint, restricted to the image of the right adjoint, is the identity on isomorphism. The category of sheaves is a *reflective subcategory* of presheaves, with sheafification the reflector.

</details>

</aside>

Constant sheaf has values $A^{\pi_0(U)}$ — one copy per connected component.

$U$ has 3 connected components, so $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}(U) = \mathbb{Z}^3$.

In contrast, the constant *presheaf* (without sheafification) would have value $\mathbb{Z}$ on $U$ — only a single integer, ignoring connectedness. Sheafification corrects this to the connectedness-aware $\mathbb{Z}^3$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that sheafification is an exact functor: if $0 \to \mathcal{F}' \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}'' \to 0$ is an exact sequence of presheaves, then $0 \to \mathcal{F}'^+ \to \mathcal{F}^+ \to \mathcal{F}''^+ \to 0$ is an exact sequence of sheaves.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use stalk preservation and stalkwise exactness for sheaves.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Exactness in $\mathbf{Sh}(X; \mathbf{Ab})$ is detected stalkwise: a sequence is exact iff every stalk sequence is exact (this is the abelian-category property of sheaves).

Exactness in $\mathbf{PSh}(X; \mathbf{Ab})$ is also detected stalkwise: stalks are filtered colimits of presheaf-section-data, and filtered colimits are exact in $\mathbf{Ab}$.

Sheafification preserves stalks: $(\mathcal{F})_x = (\mathcal{F}^+)_x$ for every $x$.

Combining these: if $0 \to \mathcal{F}' \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}'' \to 0$ is exact in $\mathbf{PSh}$, every stalk sequence $0 \to \mathcal{F}'_x \to \mathcal{F}_x \to \mathcal{F}''_x \to 0$ is exact. By stalk preservation, the corresponding sheaf sequence has the same stalks, hence the same stalk sequences. So $0 \to \mathcal{F}'^+ \to \mathcal{F}^+ \to \mathcal{F}''^+ \to 0$ is stalkwise exact, hence exact in $\mathbf{Sh}(X; \mathbf{Ab})$. $\square$

This exactness is the key technical property of sheafification: it makes sheafification well-behaved on long exact sequences and homological constructions. As a left adjoint, sheafification is automatically right-exact; the surprise is that it is also left-exact. The combination gives full exactness, making the category of sheaves of abelian groups behave well.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

For the morphism $\varphi : \mathcal{O} \to \mathcal{O}$, $f \mapsto f^2$, on the structure sheaf of $\mathbb{C}^\times$ (nonzero complex numbers, with the analytic topology), compute the cokernel sheaf.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Stalkwise, $\varphi_z$ has cokernel $\mathbb{Z}/2$ — but the cokernel sheaf is more subtle.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The map $\varphi : \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{C}^\times} \to \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{C}^\times}$, $f \mapsto f^2$, on multiplicative units (so we should write this on $\mathcal{O}^\times$):

Stalkwise, on $\mathcal{O}^\times_z$ (units of holomorphic germs at $z$): every nonzero germ is a square locally (using a local logarithm), so $\varphi_z$ is *surjective* and the cokernel stalk is 0.

The presheaf cokernel $U \mapsto \mathcal{O}^\times(U) / \mathcal{O}^\times(U)^2$ is generally nonzero (for example $U = \mathbb{C}^\times$ itself: the function $z$ is not globally a square on $\mathbb{C}^\times$, so $z \mod (\mathcal{O}^\times)^2$ is a nontrivial element of the presheaf cokernel).

Sheafification kills these nontrivial presheaf-cokernel elements: since the stalks are zero, the sheaf cokernel is the zero sheaf.

The "obstruction" to global squaring on $\mathbb{C}^\times$ is detected not by the sheaf cokernel but by *higher cohomology*: $H^1(\mathbb{C}^\times; \underline{\mathbb{Z}/2}) = \mathbb{Z}/2$, the Brauer-like obstruction class of $z$. This is the prototype example of how sheafification kills local data while leaving the global obstruction in higher cohomology.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why does the cokernel of a sheaf morphism need to be sheafified?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The presheaf cokernel may fail the gluing axiom even when both inputs are sheaves.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ be a morphism of sheaves. The *presheaf cokernel* is $U \mapsto \mathrm{coker}(\varphi(U)) = \mathcal{G}(U) / \mathrm{im}(\varphi(U))$.

This presheaf can fail the gluing axiom. Concrete example on $\mathbb{C}^\times$: $\varphi : \mathcal{O}^\times \to \mathcal{O}^\times$, $f \mapsto f^2$. Locally on simply connected opens, $\varphi(U)$ is surjective (every unit has a local square root), so the presheaf cokernel is 0 on these opens. But $\varphi(\mathbb{C}^\times)$ is not surjective: $z$ is not a global square. So the presheaf cokernel on $\mathbb{C}^\times$ is nonzero, while on every simply connected piece of an open cover, the presheaf cokernel is 0. This violates gluing: nonzero on the union, zero on the cover pieces.

Sheafification fixes this: it forces the sheaf cokernel to match the (stalkwise) zero data on simply connected opens, killing the non-locally-detectable global obstruction. The sheaf cokernel of $f \mapsto f^2$ on $\mathcal{O}^\times_{\mathbb{C}^\times}$ is the zero sheaf, even though the presheaf cokernel is nonzero on $\mathbb{C}^\times$.

The "lost" global obstruction reappears in *higher cohomology*: the long exact sequence

$$
0 \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G} \to \mathrm{coker}\, \varphi \to 0
$$

induces a long exact sequence in cohomology, and the global element of the presheaf cokernel that vanishes in the sheaf cokernel manifests as a connecting-homomorphism class in $H^1$. This is the formal mechanism by which sheafification, sheaf cohomology, and obstruction theory fit together.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that sheafification commutes with finite limits but generally not with infinite limits.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Sheafification is left adjoint, so commutes with colimits; finite limits commute with filtered colimits in $\mathbf{Ab}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Sheafification commutes with colimits.* As a left adjoint, $(-)^+$ commutes with all (small) colimits.

*Sheafification commutes with finite limits.* Stalks commute with finite limits (filtered colimits and finite limits commute in $\mathbf{Ab}$ and similar locally finitely presentable categories). Stalks commute with sheafification (stalk preservation). Therefore: a finite limit of presheaves has the same stalks as the corresponding finite limit of sheafifications, hence the sheafifications agree.

More precisely: for a finite diagram of presheaves $\mathcal{F}_i$ with limit $\mathcal{L} = \varprojlim \mathcal{F}_i$:
- Each stalk $\mathcal{L}_x = \varprojlim (\mathcal{F}_i)_x$ (filtered colimit over $U \ni x$ commutes with finite limit).
- $\mathcal{L}^+$ has stalks $\mathcal{L}_x = \varprojlim (\mathcal{F}_i)_x$.
- $\varprojlim \mathcal{F}_i^+$ has stalks $\varprojlim (\mathcal{F}_i^+)_x = \varprojlim (\mathcal{F}_i)_x$.
- By stalkwise determination of sheaves, $\mathcal{L}^+ \cong \varprojlim \mathcal{F}_i^+$.

*Sheafification does not commute with infinite limits.* Filtered colimits do not commute with arbitrary infinite limits in $\mathbf{Ab}$. Concrete failure: an infinite product of sheaves' stalks is generally not the stalk of an infinite product of presheaves' stalks (the issue is whether infinite products commute with filtered colimits — they do not).

Example: take a discrete infinite topological space $X = \mathbb{N}$ and the constant sheaves $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}_n$ for each $n \in \mathbb{N}$. The infinite product $\prod_n \underline{\mathbb{Z}}_n$ in $\mathbf{Sh}(X)$ has sections $\prod_n \mathbb{Z}^{|U|}$, but stalkwise behaviour differs from the product of presheaf stalks.

This *finite-limit / infinite-limit asymmetry* is one of the subtle but important properties of sheafification. It is the categorical reason behind many failures of "naive" stalkwise constructions for infinite operations. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Explain how sheafification on a Grothendieck site differs from sheafification on a topological space.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The plus construction must be applied twice on a general site; once suffices on a topological space.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On a topological space, the étale-space construction gives the sheafification $\mathcal{F}^+$ in one step. The étale space topology makes continuous local sections automatically satisfy gluing.

On a general Grothendieck site $(\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{T})$, there is no topological-space backing for $\mathcal{C}$ — covers are abstract families of morphisms — and the étale-space construction does not apply directly.

The *plus construction* on a site is

$$
(\mathcal{F}^+_0)(U) := \varinjlim_{\mathcal{U} \in \mathrm{Cov}(U)} \check{H}^0(\mathcal{U}; \mathcal{F}),
$$

where the colimit is over covers $\mathcal{U}$ of $U$ in the Grothendieck topology, and $\check{H}^0$ is the equaliser of the two restriction maps $\prod_i \mathcal{F}(U_i) \rightrightarrows \prod_{i,j} \mathcal{F}(U_i \times_U U_j)$.

This $\mathcal{F}^+_0$ is *separated* (the locality axiom holds: equal-on-cover implies equal) but not generally a sheaf. Applying the plus construction *again*, $\mathcal{F}^{+} := \mathcal{F}^+_0 \circ \mathcal{F}^+_0$, gives a sheaf — Grothendieck's two-step procedure.

For a topological-space site (covers = open covers), the two-step procedure simplifies: one application of plus is already a sheaf, recovering the étale-space construction. This is because topological covers are "well-behaved" in a way that general site covers may not be (e.g., étale topology, fppf topology, fpqc topology).

The general site-theoretic sheafification is foundational for:

- *Étale cohomology* (Grothendieck-Artin-Verdier 1960s): $\mathbf{Sh}(\mathbf{X}_{\acute{e}t})$ is the abelian category for étale cohomology of schemes.
- *Faithfully flat descent* (Grothendieck): the fppf and fpqc topoi have their own sheafifications.
- *Higher topos theory* (Lurie): $\infty$-categorical sheaves on sites generalise classical sheaves; sheafification becomes an $\infty$-categorical localisation functor.
- *Crystalline cohomology* (Berthelot, Ogus): sheaves on the crystalline site of a scheme require general site-theoretic sheafification.

So sheafification is the universal tool for completing local data to satisfy any prescribed gluing axiom — its full power emerges in the abstract site-theoretic setting, where the topological intuition is replaced by categorical formal data.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Sheafification on Grothendieck sites.** For a Grothendieck site $(\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{T})$, the sheafification functor exists as the left adjoint to $\mathbf{Sh}(\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{T}) \hookrightarrow \mathbf{PSh}(\mathcal{C})$. Construction via the *plus-plus* procedure: $\mathcal{F}^+ := \mathcal{F}^{+_0 +_0}$, where $\mathcal{F}^{+_0}(U) = \varinjlim_\mathcal{U} \check{H}^0(\mathcal{U}; \mathcal{F})$ over covers $\mathcal{U}$ of $U$. The two-fold application is necessary on a general site (one application separates, two applications produce a sheaf).

**Reflective localisation.** The category of sheaves is a *reflective localisation* of presheaves: it is obtained by formally inverting the morphisms $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}^+$ for all presheaves $\mathcal{F}$. This is the *Bousfield localisation* of $\mathbf{PSh}$ at the class of sheafification maps.

**Sheafification in derived algebraic geometry.** Lurie's formulation: in the $\infty$-category of presheaves of spaces (or spectra) on a site, sheaves are the local objects with respect to the Grothendieck topology, and *sheafification* is an $\infty$-categorical localisation. The construction generalises to *hypercomplete* sheaves and presents the foundation for $\infty$-categorical algebraic geometry (Lurie's *Higher Topos Theory* and *Spectral Algebraic Geometry*).

**Topos-theoretic sheafification.** A *topos* is a category equivalent to $\mathbf{Sh}(\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{T})$ for some site. The sheafification functor exhibits every topos as the localisation of the presheaf topos $\mathbf{PSh}(\mathcal{C})$ at the local-isomorphism class. Subtoposes correspond to *Lawvere-Tierney topologies*, which are the inner-language characterisations of Grothendieck topologies.

**Constructive aspects.** Sheafification is constructive in the sense of topos theory: the axiom of choice is needed only to choose representatives in the colimit, and the construction is uniform across the topos. This makes sheafification a tool not only for algebraic geometry but for *type theory* (homotopy type theory, with sheaves as univalent groupoids) and *constructive analysis*.

**Verdier's perspective.** Verdier (1965) gave a homotopical reformulation: the derived category of sheaves on a topological space is a Bousfield localisation of the derived category of presheaves. This connects sheafification to Quillen model structures and modern stable homotopy theory.

**Sheafification in $p$-adic geometry.** The pro-étale, pro-finite, and perfectoid topologies (Scholze 2012-) come with their own sheafification procedures, often requiring careful handling of size and the overconvergent or perfectoid conditions. Sheafification on these sites is the foundation of $p$-adic Hodge theory and the recent geometrisation of the local Langlands programme.

**Synthesis.** Sheafification is the left-exact left adjoint to the inclusion of sheaves into presheaves, and it works by replacing a presheaf with the sheaf that has the same stalks but satisfies the gluing axiom — the universal repair that adds exactly the local-to-global coherence a presheaf may lack. The construction via stalks or the etale space reveals that sheafification preserves stalks pointwise while altering only the global section functor, which is why it interacts well with colimits but not with limits. Across algebraic and differential geometry, sheafification is the mechanism by which quotient presheaves, image presheaves, and tensor products are corrected into genuine sheaves, and it is the technical foundation underlying the definition of quasi-coherent sheaves on schemes and the descent theory that glues objects along covers.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The construction of *sheafification* emerged in the late 1940s and early 1950s through the seminars of Henri Cartan at the École Normale Supérieure. Cartan's exposés (1948–54) developed the precise definitions of sheaves and the étale-space construction, with Jean-Pierre Serre, Armand Borel, and Alexander Grothendieck among the participants. The étale-space description — sheaves as local homeomorphisms over the base space — gave a topological meaning to sheaves and provided the most direct construction of sheafification. The seminars were a key locus for the diffusion of sheaf-theoretic methods through French and international mathematics.

Roger Godement's 1958 monograph *Topologie algébrique et théorie des faisceaux* gave the first systematic textbook treatment of sheaves and sheafification. Godement's approach was foundational: the étale-space (espace étalé) construction, the comparison of presheaves and sheaves, the plus construction on a topological space, and the consequences for cohomology. Godement's book remains a model of clarity in the foundational period of sheaf theory and is still consulted today.

Alexander Grothendieck's transformation in the late 1950s and 1960s extended sheafification far beyond the topological case. In *Sur quelques points d'algèbre homologique* (Tôhoku, 1957) he showed that the abelian category of sheaves of abelian groups on a topological space has enough injectives — making derived-functor sheaf cohomology well-defined. The 1960s SGA seminars (especially SGA 4 with Artin and Verdier) introduced *Grothendieck topologies* and *sites*, with sheafification as a fundamental construction on any site. The plus-plus procedure for general sites was the technical core, and it enabled étale cohomology, faithfully flat descent, and the rich array of cohomology theories now standard in arithmetic geometry. Grothendieck's perspective: sheafification is *not* a topological-space-specific notion but a categorical / topos-theoretic operation, the universal completion of any presheaf-style local data into the sheaf satisfying the prescribed gluing.

In contemporary research, sheafification appears in many forms: as a key step in derived algebraic geometry (Lurie's $\infty$-categorical sheaves), as the foundation of perfectoid spaces (Scholze, requiring sheafification on the pro-étale and pro-finite sites), as a tool in homotopy type theory (univalent sheaves and Bousfield localisations), and as a method in microlocal sheaf theory (Kashiwara-Schapira). Cartan's 1948–55 exposés and Godement's 1958 monograph remain the foundational sources, with Grothendieck-Artin-Verdier SGA 4 the canonical reference for the general site-theoretic version.
