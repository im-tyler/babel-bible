## Intuition [Beginner]

A *sheaf* is the formal notion of "consistent local data with a gluing rule." For a topological space $X$, a sheaf assigns to every open subset $U \subseteq X$ a set (or group, or ring) of "sections over $U$." The key requirement: if you have compatible sections on a covering of $U$, they glue to a unique section on $U$.

The motivating example: continuous functions on a topological space. To every open $U$, assign $\mathcal{C}(U)$, the set of continuous real-valued functions on $U$. If you have continuous functions on each piece of an open cover of $U$ that agree on overlaps, they assemble into a single continuous function on $U$. That's the sheaf axiom.

Sheaves are the universal language for "objects on a topological space whose local information determines global information": holomorphic functions, smooth differential forms, sections of vector bundles, solutions to PDEs locally. They are the central organising concept of modern algebraic geometry, complex analysis, and parts of differential geometry.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The sheaf of continuous real-valued functions on the real line $\mathbb{R}$. To each open subset $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, assign $\mathcal{C}(U) = \{f : U \to \mathbb{R} \text{ continuous}\}$.

The *restriction map*: for $V \subseteq U$, send $f \in \mathcal{C}(U)$ to $f|_V \in \mathcal{C}(V)$ — restrict the function to the smaller domain. This is functorial: restricting twice equals restricting once with the composite inclusion.

The *gluing axiom*: if $U = U_1 \cup U_2$ and we have $f_1 \in \mathcal{C}(U_1)$, $f_2 \in \mathcal{C}(U_2)$ with $f_1|_{U_1 \cap U_2} = f_2|_{U_1 \cap U_2}$, then there is a unique $f \in \mathcal{C}(U)$ with $f|_{U_1} = f_1$ and $f|_{U_2} = f_2$. Define $f$ piecewise: $f(x) := f_1(x)$ if $x \in U_1$, $f_2(x)$ if $x \in U_2$. Continuity follows from continuity on each piece and agreement on the overlap.

This is the prototypical sheaf. Other examples: smooth functions, holomorphic functions, sections of a vector bundle, locally constant functions — all are sheaves on appropriate spaces.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a topological space [02.01.01]. A **presheaf** $\mathcal{F}$ of sets on $X$ is the data:

1. For each open $U \subseteq X$, a set $\mathcal{F}(U)$ — the set of *sections* of $\mathcal{F}$ over $U$. By convention $\mathcal{F}(\emptyset)$ is a singleton (or the empty set in some conventions).
2. For each inclusion $V \subseteq U$ of opens, a *restriction map* $\mathrm{res}_{U, V} : \mathcal{F}(U) \to \mathcal{F}(V)$.

These satisfy:

- $\mathrm{res}_{U, U} = \mathrm{id}_{\mathcal{F}(U)}$ for every $U$.
- $\mathrm{res}_{V, W} \circ \mathrm{res}_{U, V} = \mathrm{res}_{U, W}$ for $W \subseteq V \subseteq U$.

Equivalently, a presheaf is a contravariant functor from the category of open subsets of $X$ (with morphisms = inclusions) to the category $\mathbf{Set}$.

A presheaf can be valued in any category $\mathbf{C}$ (groups, abelian groups, rings, modules, etc.) instead of $\mathbf{Set}$. We write **presheaf of abelian groups**, **presheaf of $R$-modules**, etc.

A **sheaf** is a presheaf satisfying two additional axioms (the **sheaf axioms** or *gluing axioms*):

- **(Locality / Identity)**: For an open $U$ with cover $\{U_i\}$, if $s, t \in \mathcal{F}(U)$ satisfy $\mathrm{res}_{U, U_i}(s) = \mathrm{res}_{U, U_i}(t)$ for every $i$, then $s = t$.
- **(Gluing)**: For an open $U$ with cover $\{U_i\}$ and a family of sections $\{s_i \in \mathcal{F}(U_i)\}$ such that $\mathrm{res}_{U_i, U_i \cap U_j}(s_i) = \mathrm{res}_{U_j, U_i \cap U_j}(s_j)$ for every $i, j$, there exists $s \in \mathcal{F}(U)$ with $\mathrm{res}_{U, U_i}(s) = s_i$ for every $i$.

Combining, the sheaf axiom can be phrased as exactness of

$$
0 \to \mathcal{F}(U) \to \prod_i \mathcal{F}(U_i) \rightrightarrows \prod_{i, j} \mathcal{F}(U_i \cap U_j),
$$

where the two parallel maps are restriction to first and second factors of the intersection.

A **morphism** of sheaves $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ is a natural transformation: for each open $U$, a map $\varphi_U : \mathcal{F}(U) \to \mathcal{G}(U)$ commuting with restriction. **Sheaves on $X$** form a category $\mathbf{Sh}(X)$; sheaves of abelian groups form an abelian category $\mathbf{Sh}_{\mathbf{Ab}}(X)$.

The **stalk** of a sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ at a point $x \in X$ is

$$
\mathcal{F}_x := \mathrm{colim}_{x \in U} \mathcal{F}(U),
$$

the direct limit of sections over neighbourhoods of $x$. A morphism $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ is an isomorphism iff every stalk map $\varphi_x : \mathcal{F}_x \to \mathcal{G}_x$ is an isomorphism.

**Key examples.**

- **Sheaf of continuous functions** $\mathcal{C}_X$: $U \mapsto \{f : U \to \mathbb{R} \text{ continuous}\}$.
- **Sheaf of smooth functions** $\mathcal{C}^\infty_M$ on a smooth manifold $M$.
- **Sheaf of holomorphic functions** $\mathcal{O}_X$ on a complex manifold $X$.
- **Sheaf of sections of a vector bundle** $\mathcal{E} = \Gamma(E, -)$ on a manifold.
- **Constant sheaf** $\underline{A}_X$ for an abelian group $A$: $U \mapsto $ locally constant functions $U \to A$.
- **Skyscraper sheaf** at $x \in X$ with stalk $A$: $U \mapsto A$ if $x \in U$, $0$ otherwise.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Sheafification).** *Every presheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on a topological space $X$ has a *sheafification* $\mathcal{F}^+$ — a sheaf together with a map of presheaves $\eta : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}^+$ universal among presheaf maps to sheaves: any presheaf map $\mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ with $\mathcal{G}$ a sheaf factors uniquely through $\eta$.*

**Proof sketch.** Construct $\mathcal{F}^+$ via stalks. For an open $U$, define

$$
\mathcal{F}^+(U) := \big\{ (s_x)_{x \in U} \in \prod_{x \in U} \mathcal{F}_x : \forall x \in U, \exists \text{ open } V \ni x, V \subseteq U, \exists t \in \mathcal{F}(V) \text{ with } t_y = s_y \forall y \in V \big\}.
$$

A section of $\mathcal{F}^+$ over $U$ is a tuple of stalks that is *locally* representable by an actual section of $\mathcal{F}$. Restriction maps are restriction of stalk-tuples. Verify the sheaf axioms:

- Locality: agreement on a cover means agreement of stalks at each point, hence equal stalk-tuples.
- Gluing: given compatible $\{s_i\}$ on a cover, the union of their stalk values gives a stalk-tuple that is locally representable (by the local representations of the $s_i$).

The natural map $\eta : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}^+$ sends $s \in \mathcal{F}(U)$ to the stalk-tuple $(s_x)_{x \in U}$. It is universal: any presheaf map $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ to a sheaf factors uniquely through $\eta$ by sheaf-axiom-driven extension to stalk-tuples. $\square$

The sheafification construction is the right adjoint to the inclusion $\mathbf{Sh}(X) \hookrightarrow \mathbf{Pr}(X)$ of sheaves into presheaves. It is the "best sheaf approximation" of a presheaf — the sheaf that has the same stalks but satisfies the gluing axiom.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Show that the constant presheaf $U \mapsto A$ (with all restriction maps the identity) is *not* a sheaf when $A$ has more than one element and $X$ is disconnected.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take $X = U_1 \sqcup U_2$ disjoint, and pick different elements $a_1 \neq a_2$ in $A$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $X = U_1 \sqcup U_2$ disjoint and $a_1 \neq a_2 \in A$, then $a_1 \in \mathcal{F}(U_1)$ and $a_2 \in \mathcal{F}(U_2)$ have *empty* overlap to check (since $U_1 \cap U_2 = \emptyset$). The gluing axiom would require a section $s \in \mathcal{F}(X) = A$ that restricts to both $a_1$ on $U_1$ and $a_2$ on $U_2$. But the constant presheaf has identity restriction maps, so $s = a_1$ and $s = a_2$ force $a_1 = a_2$. Contradiction. The constant presheaf fails the gluing axiom; the *constant sheaf* $\underline{A}$ uses locally constant functions instead.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).**

Show that the stalk of a sheaf at a point $x$ is well-defined as a direct limit.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Direct limits exist in $\mathbf{Set}$ (and in $\mathbf{Ab}$, $\mathbf{Ring}$, etc.); use the directed system of opens containing $x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The set of opens $\{U : x \in U\}$ is a directed system under reverse inclusion (the intersection of two opens containing $x$ contains $x$). The presheaf $\mathcal{F}$ assigns to each $U$ a set $\mathcal{F}(U)$, with restriction maps $\mathrm{res}_{U, V} : \mathcal{F}(U) \to \mathcal{F}(V)$ for $V \subseteq U$ — going *up* the directed system. The colimit is the disjoint union of $\mathcal{F}(U)$ modulo: $s \in \mathcal{F}(U)$ and $t \in \mathcal{F}(V)$ are equivalent if $\mathrm{res}_{U, W}(s) = \mathrm{res}_{V, W}(t)$ for some $W \subseteq U \cap V$ containing $x$. This colimit is well-defined as long as such a $W$ always exists — which it does by directedness. Hence $\mathcal{F}_x$ is well-defined.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Prove that a morphism of sheaves $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ is an isomorphism iff every stalk map $\varphi_x$ is an isomorphism.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For one direction: stalks are colimits, and isomorphisms commute with colimits. For the other: use the sheaf axiom to lift stalk-iso to section-iso.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $\varphi$ is a sheaf isomorphism, taking stalks gives stalk isomorphisms (functor commutes with colimits up to natural iso).

For the converse, suppose $\varphi_x$ is an iso for every $x$. To show $\varphi$ is an iso, we show each $\varphi_U : \mathcal{F}(U) \to \mathcal{G}(U)$ is bijective.

*Injectivity*: if $\varphi_U(s) = \varphi_U(t)$, then $\varphi_x(s_x) = \varphi_x(t_x)$ for every $x \in U$. Stalk-injectivity gives $s_x = t_x$. Locality of $\mathcal{F}$ then gives $s = t$.

*Surjectivity*: given $r \in \mathcal{G}(U)$, the stalks $r_x \in \mathcal{G}_x$ have preimages $s_x \in \mathcal{F}_x$ via $\varphi_x^{-1}$. Each $s_x$ is locally representable by some $t \in \mathcal{F}(V)$ for an open $V \ni x$. The local sections fit together to form a global section by the sheaf axiom (Gluing) applied to $\mathcal{F}$. Then $\varphi_U(s) = r$ by construction.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Show that the sheaf of continuous functions on $\mathbb{R}$ has the same stalk at every point: $(\mathcal{C})_0 \cong (\mathcal{C})_x$ for every $x \in \mathbb{R}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The stalk consists of germs — equivalence classes of functions defined near $x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The stalk $(\mathcal{C})_x$ is the set of germs of continuous functions at $x$: equivalence classes $[f]$ where $f, g$ are equivalent if they agree on some open neighbourhood of $x$. Translation $T_x : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$, $y \mapsto y - x$ gives an isomorphism $T_x^* : (\mathcal{C})_x \to (\mathcal{C})_0$, $[f] \mapsto [f \circ T_x^{-1}]$. So all stalks are canonically isomorphic to $(\mathcal{C})_0$. (More precisely: the stalk depends only on the local topological/smooth structure at the point, which is the same everywhere on $\mathbb{R}$.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why is the *presheaf cokernel* of a morphism of sheaves of abelian groups generally not a sheaf, and what is the resolution?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Pointwise quotient on opens may fail the gluing axiom.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a morphism $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ of sheaves, the pointwise cokernel $U \mapsto \mathcal{G}(U) / \varphi(\mathcal{F}(U))$ is a presheaf but generally fails locality / gluing. A cohomology class might be locally exact but globally non-exact — this is precisely the failure that sheaf cohomology measures.

The resolution: the *sheaf cokernel* is the **sheafification** of the presheaf cokernel. This recovers a sheaf with the right local properties. The disagreement between the presheaf cokernel and its sheafification — the global obstruction — is the content of $H^1(X; \mathcal{F})$ in sheaf cohomology [04.03.01].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that pushforward and pullback are adjoint functors between sheaves on related spaces: for a continuous map $f : X \to Y$, $f_\ast : \mathbf{Sh}(X) \to \mathbf{Sh}(Y)$ has left adjoint $f^{-1} : \mathbf{Sh}(Y) \to \mathbf{Sh}(X)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The pushforward is $(f_\ast \mathcal{F})(V) := \mathcal{F}(f^{-1}(V))$. The pullback is constructed via the sheafification of "stalks pulled back."

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define the *pushforward* $(f_\ast \mathcal{F})(V) := \mathcal{F}(f^{-1}(V))$ for $V \subseteq Y$ open. This is a sheaf on $Y$ because $\mathcal{F}$ is a sheaf on $X$ and $f^{-1}$ commutes with intersections.

Define the *pullback* $f^{-1} \mathcal{G}$ on $X$ by sheafifying the presheaf $U \mapsto \mathrm{colim}_{V \supseteq f(U)} \mathcal{G}(V)$ — sections on $X$ are stalks of $\mathcal{G}$ pulled back along $f$, glued by the sheaf axiom.

The adjunction $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Sh}(X)}(f^{-1}\mathcal{G}, \mathcal{F}) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Sh}(Y)}(\mathcal{G}, f_\ast \mathcal{F})$ is verified by tracing through the universal properties: a map $f^{-1}\mathcal{G} \to \mathcal{F}$ corresponds (by the universal property of sheafification + colimit) to a map $\mathcal{G}(V) \to \mathcal{F}(f^{-1}(V)) = (f_\ast \mathcal{F})(V)$ for every $V$, naturally — that's a map $\mathcal{G} \to f_\ast \mathcal{F}$ of sheaves on $Y$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

State the *espace étalé* (étale space) construction of a sheaf and explain its geometric meaning.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The étale space topology gives a covering-space-like geometric realization of a sheaf.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$, the **étale space** $\mathrm{Et}(\mathcal{F}) := \bigsqcup_{x \in X} \mathcal{F}_x$ (disjoint union of stalks) carries a topology making the projection $\pi : \mathrm{Et}(\mathcal{F}) \to X$ a *local homeomorphism*. Sections of $\mathcal{F}$ over $U$ correspond to continuous sections of $\pi$ over $U$ — i.e., continuous maps $s : U \to \mathrm{Et}(\mathcal{F})$ with $\pi \circ s = \mathrm{id}_U$.

This identifies sheaves on $X$ with local-homeomorphism spaces over $X$, and gives a geometric realization of sheaves as "stacked-sheets" objects (similar to covering spaces but more general — fibres can be sets / groups / rings, not just discrete sets, and the local structure can be more flexible). The étale-space perspective is the foundation of *étale cohomology* in algebraic geometry.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Sheaves form a topos.** $\mathbf{Sh}(X)$ is a *Grothendieck topos* — a category with finite limits, exponentials, a subobject classifier, and small colimits. This topos-theoretic perspective unifies sheaf theory with logic and category theory; classical sheaves on a topological space are a special case of sheaves on a *Grothendieck site*.

**Étale fundamental group.** For a connected scheme $X$, the étale fundamental group $\pi_1^{\text{ét}}(X, \overline{x})$ classifies finite étale covers of $X$ — the algebraic analogue of $\pi_1$ for topological covers. It is the Galois group of the maximal unramified extension of the function field, and underlies arithmetic geometry.

**Sheaves of modules and quasi-coherent sheaves.** On a ringed space $(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$ — a topological space with a sheaf of rings — *modules* are sheaves $\mathcal{F}$ with each $\mathcal{F}(U)$ an $\mathcal{O}_X(U)$-module compatibly. *Quasi-coherent* sheaves on a scheme are those locally given by modules over the ring. Coherent sheaves are quasi-coherent + finite-type — they are the algebraic-geometric analogue of vector bundles, and their cohomology is the central computational tool [04.03.01].

**Direct and inverse images.** For a morphism $f : X \to Y$ of ringed spaces, the pushforward $f_\ast$ and pullback $f^\ast$ functors are central to all higher constructions: composition of morphisms, base change, projection formulas. Adjoint pairs are the structural backbone of sheaf-theoretic functoriality.

**Soft, fine, flabby sheaves.** Various flavours of "acyclic" sheaves used to compute sheaf cohomology: soft sheaves (extension property for closed sets), fine sheaves (admit partitions of unity), flabby sheaves (every section extends globally). On smooth manifolds, the sheaves $\mathcal{C}^\infty$, $\Omega^k$, etc., are all fine; this is why de Rham cohomology computes singular cohomology.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Sheafification existence.** Proved in §"Key theorem".

**Stalks compute everything.** Proved in Exercise 3.

**Sheaves of abelian groups form an abelian category.** The kernel of a morphism $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ is the pointwise kernel $\ker(\varphi)(U) := \ker(\varphi_U)$ — this *is* a sheaf (verify: kernels are local). The image is the sheafification of the pointwise image (which fails to be a sheaf in general). The cokernel similarly. With these constructions, $\mathbf{Sh}_{\mathbf{Ab}}(X)$ has all kernels, cokernels, and images, and the category is abelian.

**Adjunction $f^{-1} \dashv f_\ast$.** Proved in Exercise 6 with the universal-property argument.

**Stalks under $f^{-1}$.** $(f^{-1} \mathcal{G})_x \cong \mathcal{G}_{f(x)}$. Proof: stalks are colimits over neighbourhoods, and pullback along $f$ realises the directed system of opens around $f(x)$ as a sub-system of opens around $x$ via pullback. The colimit is preserved.

**Étale-space topology.** Sheaves on $X$ correspond to *étale spaces* over $X$ (local homeomorphisms). The bijection sends $\mathcal{F}$ to $\mathrm{Et}(\mathcal{F}) = \bigsqcup_x \mathcal{F}_x$ topologised by *germs over neighbourhoods*. Continuous sections over $U$ correspond to elements of $\mathcal{F}(U)$ via the colimit structure.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The notion of a sheaf was introduced by Jean Leray in 1946 in the context of homological methods for studying topological invariants of fibre bundles. Henri Cartan's seminar at the École Normale Supérieure (1948–1953) systematised the theory, with Cartan, Weil, Eilenberg, and Steenrod establishing the now-canonical axiomatics.

Roger Godement's 1958 *Topologie algébrique et théorie des faisceaux* gave the first systematic textbook treatment. Grothendieck's mid-century revolution in algebraic geometry — the *Éléments de géométrie algébrique* (EGA, 1960s) — built the entire theory of schemes on the foundation of sheaves of rings on a topological space, recasting algebraic geometry as commutative algebra applied to spectra of rings.

In modern mathematics, sheaves are the universal language for "local structure on a space": they appear in algebraic geometry (sheaves on schemes), differential geometry (sheaves of smooth functions, forms, sections of bundles), complex analysis (sheaves of holomorphic functions), algebraic topology (cosheaves of chains), category theory (Grothendieck topoi), and logic (Lawvere-Tierney elementary topoi). The dual perspectives of "local data with gluing" and "geometric realisation as étale spaces" make the concept bridge analytic, geometric, and algebraic intuitions.
