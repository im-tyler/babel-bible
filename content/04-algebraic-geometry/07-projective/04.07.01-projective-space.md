## Intuition [Beginner]

*Projective space* $\mathbb{P}^n$ is the space of *lines through the origin* in $(n+1)$-dimensional space. Equivalently: ordinary $n$-dimensional space, plus a sphere of "points at infinity" — one point at infinity for every direction you might travel.

Why care? Many natural geometric statements that have annoying exceptions in ordinary affine space become clean and exception-free in projective space. *Two distinct lines in the plane meet at exactly one point* — except when they're parallel; in projective space, "parallel" means "meeting at the same point at infinity," and the statement holds with no exception. The number of intersections of curves of given degrees is constant in projective space (Bézout's theorem), again with no exception.

Projective space is the natural home for algebraic geometry: smooth projective varieties are *compact* (in the analytic topology over $\mathbb{C}$), so they admit clean cohomological invariants, finite-dimensional function spaces, and the full power of Riemann-Roch. Affine space is a punctured version of projective space; projective space is the cleaner and more symmetric object.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The *projective line* $\mathbb{P}^1$ over the real numbers: points are equivalence classes $[x : y]$ of pairs $(x, y) \neq (0, 0)$, where $[x : y] \sim [\lambda x : \lambda y]$ for any $\lambda \neq 0$.

Two charts cover $\mathbb{P}^1$:

- $U_0 = \{[x : y] : x \neq 0\}$ with coordinate $u = y/x$. This sends $[x:y]$ to $u$ and recovers the real line $\mathbb{R}$ as $\{[1:u] : u \in \mathbb{R}\}$.
- $U_1 = \{[x:y] : y \neq 0\}$ with coordinate $v = x/y$.

The transition function on the overlap $U_0 \cap U_1$ is $v = 1/u$. Topologically, $\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{R})$ is a circle (real line plus one point at infinity), and $\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{C})$ is the *Riemann sphere*.

The *projective plane* $\mathbb{P}^2$ over $\mathbb{R}$ is a 2-dimensional surface obtained from the sphere $S^2$ by identifying antipodal points. Over $\mathbb{C}$, $\mathbb{P}^2(\mathbb{C})$ is a smooth complex surface (real dimension 4) — a basic and important compact complex manifold.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $k$ be a field. The **projective space** $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ has three equivalent constructions.

**(1) Set-theoretic / quotient construction.** As a set,

$$
\mathbb{P}^n(k) := (k^{n+1} \setminus \{0\}) / k^\times,
$$

where $k^\times$ acts by scalar multiplication. A point is denoted $[x_0 : x_1 : \cdots : x_n]$, the equivalence class of $(x_0, \ldots, x_n)$.

**(2) Open-affine cover.** Cover $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ by $n + 1$ open subsets $U_i := \{[x_0 : \cdots : x_n] : x_i \neq 0\}$ for $i = 0, \ldots, n$. Each $U_i$ is isomorphic to affine $n$-space:

$$
U_i \cong \mathbb{A}^n_k, \qquad [x_0 : \cdots : x_n] \mapsto (x_0/x_i, \ldots, \widehat{x_i/x_i}, \ldots, x_n/x_i).
$$

The transition functions on overlaps $U_i \cap U_j$ are rational maps of the form $u_l \mapsto u_l / u_j$ — algebraic regular functions on the open subsets.

**(3) Proj construction (scheme-theoretic).** As a scheme,

$$
\mathbb{P}^n_k := \mathrm{Proj}\,k[x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n],
$$

where $\mathrm{Proj}\,S$ for a graded ring $S$ is constructed analogously to $\mathrm{Spec}\,R$ but with homogeneous prime ideals (those not containing the irrelevant ideal $S_+ = \bigoplus_{d > 0} S_d$). Topologically, points of $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ are homogeneous prime ideals of $k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]$ other than the irrelevant ideal.

**(4) Functor of points.** Most modern: $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ represents the contravariant functor

$$
\mathbb{P}^n_k(R) := \{(L, \varphi) : L \text{ invertible } R\text{-module}, \varphi : R^{n+1} \twoheadrightarrow L\} / \sim,
$$

i.e., surjections from $R^{n+1}$ onto invertible $R$-modules, modulo isomorphism. This characterises $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ as the moduli space of *line bundles together with $n+1$ generating sections*.

**Twisting sheaves.** Define the *twisting sheaf* $\mathcal{O}(d)$ on $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ for each $d \in \mathbb{Z}$ as the sheaf associated to the graded module $k[x_0, \ldots, x_n](d)$ (degree shift by $d$). For $d \geq 0$:

$$
H^0(\mathbb{P}^n_k; \mathcal{O}(d)) = k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]_d \quad \text{(homogeneous polynomials of degree } d\text{)}.
$$

For $d < 0$, $H^0 = 0$.

**Properties of $\mathbb{P}^n_k$:**

- *Connected, irreducible, reduced* — $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ is a smooth projective variety.
- *Dimension*: $\dim \mathbb{P}^n_k = n$.
- *Picard group*: $\mathrm{Pic}(\mathbb{P}^n_k) = \mathbb{Z}$, generated by $\mathcal{O}(1)$.
- *Cohomology of $\mathcal{O}(d)$*: foundational computation, given by the Serre vanishing-and-formula:

$$
H^i(\mathbb{P}^n_k; \mathcal{O}(d)) = \begin{cases} k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]_d & i = 0, d \geq 0 \\ k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]_{-d-n-1}^\vee & i = n, d \leq -n - 1 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}
$$

- *Canonical sheaf*: $\omega_{\mathbb{P}^n_k} = \mathcal{O}(-n - 1)$.
- *Automorphism group*: $\mathrm{Aut}(\mathbb{P}^n_k) = \mathrm{PGL}_{n+1}(k)$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Picard group of projective space).** *For an algebraically closed field $k$ and $n \geq 1$, $\mathrm{Pic}(\mathbb{P}^n_k) = \mathbb{Z}$, generated by $\mathcal{O}(1)$. Every line bundle on $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ is isomorphic to $\mathcal{O}(d)$ for a unique $d \in \mathbb{Z}$.*

**Proof sketch.** Use the cover $U_i = \{x_i \neq 0\}$, on each of which $\mathbb{P}^n$ restricts to $\mathbb{A}^n$. The Picard group of $\mathbb{A}^n_k$ vanishes (every line bundle on affine space vanishes — by Quillen-Suslin / the polynomial ring case of $\mathrm{Pic}(\mathrm{Spec}\,R) = 0$ when $R$ is a polynomial ring over a field). So a line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ on $\mathbb{P}^n$ vanishes on each $U_i$.

A line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ on $\mathbb{P}^n$ is then determined by its *transition functions* on overlaps $U_i \cap U_j$ — invertible regular functions $g_{ij} \in \mathcal{O}^\times(U_i \cap U_j)$. Compute $\mathcal{O}^\times(U_i \cap U_j) = k^\times \cdot (x_j / x_i)^{\mathbb{Z}}$ — Laurent monomials in $x_j / x_i$ with leading $k^\times$ scalar.

The cocycle condition $g_{ij} g_{jk} = g_{ik}$ on triple overlaps forces $g_{ij}(x) = c_{ij} \cdot (x_j/x_i)^{d}$ for a fixed integer $d$ and constants $c_{ij} \in k^\times$ satisfying $c_{ij} c_{jk} = c_{ik}$ — but these constants are coboundaries (any cocycle of $k^\times$-valued data on a contractible cover is a coboundary), so they can be normalised to 1. The remaining data is the integer $d$, the *degree* of $\mathcal{L}$.

So $\mathcal{L} \cong \mathcal{O}(d)$ for unique $d \in \mathbb{Z}$. $\square$

This computation is the foundational *first nonzero Picard-group calculation* in algebraic geometry. Every later Picard-group computation builds on similar Čech-cocycle reasoning.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [04.05.05] (ample and very ample line bundle), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [04.07.02] (blowup). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Compute $\dim_k H^0(\mathbb{P}^2_k; \mathcal{O}(3))$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Global sections are homogeneous polynomials of degree $d$ in $n + 1$ variables.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$H^0(\mathbb{P}^2_k; \mathcal{O}(3)) = k[x_0, x_1, x_2]_3$ — homogeneous degree-3 polynomials in 3 variables.

The dimension is $\binom{3 + 3 - 1}{3 - 1} = \binom{5}{2} = 10$.

(Monomials $x_0^a x_1^b x_2^c$ with $a + b + c = 3$, $a, b, c \geq 0$: there are 10 such.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, conceptual).**

Why does $\mathcal{O}(d)$ have no global sections for $d < 0$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A global section of $\mathcal{O}(d)$ is a homogeneous polynomial of degree $d$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Global sections of $\mathcal{O}(d)$ on $\mathbb{P}^n$ are homogeneous polynomials of degree $d$ in $x_0, \ldots, x_n$. Polynomials only have non-negative degree, so $H^0(\mathbb{P}^n; \mathcal{O}(d)) = 0$ for $d < 0$.

Equivalently: $\mathcal{O}(-1)$ is the *tautological sub-bundle* on $\mathbb{P}^n$, whose fibre at a point $[v]$ is the line $kv \subset k^{n+1}$. A global section would be a globally non-vanishing assignment of vectors in these lines, but the bundle is nonzero (it's the canonical example of a nonzero line bundle), so no such global section exists.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Prove that $\omega_{\mathbb{P}^n} = \mathcal{O}(-n - 1)$ on $\mathbb{P}^n_k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the Euler sequence and compute determinants.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The *Euler sequence* on $\mathbb{P}^n$:

$$
0 \to \mathcal{O} \to \mathcal{O}(1)^{n+1} \to T_{\mathbb{P}^n} \to 0,
$$

where $T_{\mathbb{P}^n}$ is the tangent sheaf. Taking determinants:

$$
\det T_{\mathbb{P}^n} = \det \mathcal{O}(1)^{n+1} = \mathcal{O}(n + 1),
$$

so $T_{\mathbb{P}^n} = \omega_{\mathbb{P}^n}^{-1}$ has determinant $\mathcal{O}(n+1)$, giving $\omega_{\mathbb{P}^n} = \mathcal{O}(-n - 1)$. $\square$

This is one of the most important facts about projective space: the canonical bundle is *negative* (i.e., $-\omega_{\mathbb{P}^n}$ is ample), which is why $\mathbb{P}^n$ is a *Fano variety*.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

Compute the Hilbert polynomial $p(t) = \chi(\mathbb{P}^n; \mathcal{O}(t))$ of $\mathbb{P}^n_k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $t \geq 0$, $H^0(\mathcal{O}(t)) = \binom{t + n}{n}$ and higher cohomology vanishes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $t$ large positive, $\chi = h^0 = \binom{t + n}{n}$. As a polynomial in $t$, $p(t) = \binom{t + n}{n} = \frac{(t+1)(t+2) \cdots (t+n)}{n!}$.

This polynomial encodes:

- $\deg p(t) = n = \dim \mathbb{P}^n$.
- *Leading coefficient* $1/n!$ × *degree of $\mathcal{O}(1)$* on $\mathbb{P}^n$, where $\deg(\mathcal{O}(1))^n = 1$ (the class number).
- *Constant term* $p(0) = 1 = \chi(\mathbb{P}^n; \mathcal{O})$, which equals the *arithmetic genus* contribution $1 - g$ for $g = 0$.

The Hilbert polynomial of any closed subvariety $X \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}^n$ encodes its dimension, degree, and arithmetic genus.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Explain the Plücker embedding of the Grassmannian $\mathrm{Gr}(2, 4)$ into $\mathbb{P}^5$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Send a 2-plane $W \subset k^4$ to the wedge $w_1 \wedge w_2 \in \Lambda^2 k^4 \cong k^6$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathrm{Gr}(2, 4)$ is the variety of 2-dimensional subspaces of $k^4$. Choose a basis $w_1, w_2$ of $W$. The exterior product $w_1 \wedge w_2 \in \Lambda^2 k^4$ is well-defined up to scalar (changing basis multiplies by a scalar = $\det$ of change-of-basis matrix). So $W \mapsto [w_1 \wedge w_2]$ defines a map $\mathrm{Gr}(2, 4) \to \mathbb{P}(\Lambda^2 k^4) = \mathbb{P}^5$.

The image is cut out by the *Plücker relations*: a vector $\sum p_{ij} e_i \wedge e_j \in \Lambda^2 k^4$ is decomposable as $w_1 \wedge w_2$ iff $p_{12} p_{34} - p_{13} p_{24} + p_{14} p_{23} = 0$. So $\mathrm{Gr}(2, 4)$ is a smooth quadric hypersurface in $\mathbb{P}^5$.

This Plücker embedding generalises: every Grassmannian $\mathrm{Gr}(k, n)$ embeds in $\mathbb{P}\bigl(\Lambda^k k^n\bigr)$ as a smooth subvariety, providing a uniform way to study these spaces.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

State Bézout's theorem for plane curves and explain why projective space is the natural setting.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Affine intersections can have lower count due to "intersections at infinity" or parallel curves.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Bézout's theorem (plane curves).** Let $C, D \subset \mathbb{P}^2_k$ be plane projective curves of degrees $d, e$ respectively, with no common irreducible component. Then their intersection $C \cap D$ has exactly $de$ points, counted with multiplicity.

In affine $\mathbb{A}^2$, this can fail: two lines (degrees 1 and 1) "should" meet in $1 \cdot 1 = 1$ point, but parallel lines meet in *zero* affine points. Adding the line at infinity to make $\mathbb{P}^2$, parallel lines meet at the point at infinity in their common direction — restoring the count.

More generally: a smooth conic (degree 2) and a line (degree 1) "should" meet in 2 points; in $\mathbb{A}^2$ they can have 0, 1, or 2 intersection points depending on geometry. In $\mathbb{P}^2$, the count is always exactly 2 (with multiplicity), thanks to:

1. *Compactness*: $\mathbb{P}^2$ is compact, so all "limit" intersections are captured.
2. *Multiplicity*: tangencies and singular intersections are weighted properly.
3. *No exceptions for "infinity"*: every direction has a single point at infinity.

Bézout's theorem generalises to *intersection numbers* on smooth projective varieties, the foundation of intersection theory (Fulton, Eisenbud-Harris).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Explain how $\mathbb{P}^n$ represents the functor of "line bundles with $n + 1$ generating sections."

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A point of $\mathbb{P}^n(R)$ is the data of a surjection $R^{n+1} \to L$ where $L$ is invertible.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a ring $R$, a morphism $\varphi : \mathrm{Spec}\,R \to \mathbb{P}^n_k$ is equivalently:

- *(scheme map)* A morphism of schemes $\mathrm{Spec}\,R \to \mathbb{P}^n_k$ over $\mathrm{Spec}\,k$.
- *(functorial point)* An equivalence class $(L, s_0, \ldots, s_n)$ where $L$ is an invertible $R$-module, $s_0, \ldots, s_n \in L$ are global sections that *generate* $L$ (i.e., the map $R^{n+1} \to L$, $(a_0, \ldots, a_n) \mapsto \sum a_i s_i$, is surjective), modulo isomorphism of the data.

Concretely: pulling back $\mathcal{O}(1)$ gives the line bundle $L = \varphi^* \mathcal{O}(1)$, and the global sections $x_0, \ldots, x_n \in H^0(\mathbb{P}^n; \mathcal{O}(1))$ pull back to generators $s_i = \varphi^* x_i$ of $L$. The surjectivity (no common zeroes) corresponds to the morphism being well-defined everywhere.

This functorial description is *the* modern way to think about $\mathbb{P}^n$: it represents the moduli problem "line bundle equipped with $n + 1$ generators." Higher Grassmannians and flag varieties have analogous moduli interpretations, and this perspective generalises to all projective bundles and varieties.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Cohomology of line bundles on $\mathbb{P}^n$.** The complete computation of $H^i(\mathbb{P}^n_k; \mathcal{O}(d))$ for all $i, d$ — given in the formal-definition section — is the foundational cohomology calculation in algebraic geometry. Every projective-variety cohomology computation eventually reduces to this via long exact sequences.

**Hilbert polynomial.** Every coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on a closed subscheme $X \subset \mathbb{P}^n$ has a *Hilbert polynomial* $p_{\mathcal{F}}(t) = \chi(X; \mathcal{F}(t))$. The polynomial is a numerical invariant whose leading coefficient encodes the dimension and degree of $X$, and whose constant term encodes the arithmetic genus. The *Castelnuovo-Mumford regularity* refines this with bounds on when $H^i(\mathcal{F}(t)) = 0$.

**Cox rings.** The Cox ring of $\mathbb{P}^n$ is $\bigoplus_{d \geq 0} H^0(\mathbb{P}^n; \mathcal{O}(d)) = k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]$. For more general Mori-dream spaces and toric varieties, the Cox ring construction provides a uniform projective-coordinate description.

**Toric perspective.** $\mathbb{P}^n$ is a *toric variety* — it admits an action by the torus $(k^\times)^n$ with a dense open orbit. The combinatorial description: $\mathbb{P}^n$ corresponds to the fan in $\mathbb{R}^n$ generated by $e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n, -e_1 - \cdots - e_n$. This generalises to toric varieties via fans and polytopes (Fulton *Introduction to Toric Varieties*).

**Grassmannians and flag varieties.** $\mathbb{P}^n = \mathrm{Gr}(1, n+1)$ is the simplest Grassmannian. The general Grassmannian $\mathrm{Gr}(k, n)$ parametrises $k$-dimensional subspaces of $k^n$ and embeds in $\mathbb{P}\bigl(\Lambda^k k^n\bigr)$ via Plücker coordinates. *Flag varieties* $G/B$ for $G$ semisimple are the natural generalisation.

**Brauer group and twisted projective forms.** Over non-algebraically-closed fields, "projective space" admits *Brauer-Severi twists*: forms that become $\mathbb{P}^n$ over the algebraic closure but are not $\mathbb{P}^n$ over $k$. The Brauer-Severi variety $X$ corresponds to a class in $\mathrm{Br}(k)$ — central simple algebras over $k$.

**Crepant resolutions and $\mathbb{P}^n$ as a target.** Many natural moduli problems and singularities are resolved by maps to $\mathbb{P}^n$ (Hirzebruch-Jung, weighted projective spaces, projective bundles).

**Synthesis.** Projective space $\mathbb{P}^n$ is the compactification of affine space that adds a hyperplane at infinity, and this single geometric act has far-reaching algebraic consequences: every regular function on $\mathbb{P}^n$ is constant (forcing the use of sheaf cohomology to study global sections of twisted line bundles $\mathcal{O}(d)$), and every morphism from $\mathbb{P}^n$ to a lower-dimensional variety is constant (a rigidity that underpins the classification of maps between projective varieties). The homogeneous coordinate ring and the $\mathrm{Proj}$ construction extend the dictionary between commutative algebra and geometry from affine to projective settings. Through the Euler sequence, the cohomology of $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^n}(d)$, and the interpretation of $\mathbb{P}^n$ as the moduli space of lines through the origin in $\mathbb{A}^{n+1}$, projective space serves as the ambient geometry in which virtually every classification and embedding theorem in algebraic geometry takes place.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The geometric idea of projective space is older than the formalism. Renaissance painters (Alberti, Brunelleschi, ca. 1413–1450) discovered the *vanishing point* and developed the geometry of perspective. Gérard Desargues (1639) introduced *Desargues's theorem* and the synthetic projective plane, but the work was largely lost until rediscovered in the 19th century.

The modern algebraic formulation began with Hermann Grassmann's 1844 *Die lineale Ausdehnungslehre* — a remarkable but largely-unread work introducing what we now call exterior algebra and projective coordinates. August Möbius's 1827 *Der barycentrische Calcul* introduced *homogeneous coordinates*. Julius Plücker (1830s–60s) developed analytic projective geometry systematically, including the *Plücker embedding* of Grassmannians.

Arthur Cayley and Felix Klein in the late 19th century unified projective and Euclidean geometry through the *Erlangen Programme*: Euclidean geometry is the geometry preserved by a fixed quadric (the absolute) inside a projective space. David Hilbert's 1899 *Grundlagen der Geometrie* gave the modern axiomatic treatment.

The 20th century's algebraic and scheme-theoretic perspective is due to:

- *Severi, van der Waerden, Weil* (1930s–40s): projective varieties as zero sets of homogeneous polynomial systems.
- *Cartan-Serre* (FAC, 1955): the sheaf-cohomology computation of $H^i(\mathbb{P}^n; \mathcal{O}(d))$ was the prototype for the entire theory of coherent cohomology.
- *Grothendieck-Dieudonné* (EGA, 1960s): the $\mathrm{Proj}$ construction, projective morphisms, and the functorial characterisation. Grothendieck's relative perspective ($\mathbb{P}^n_S$ over an arbitrary base $S$) made projective space a *universal* moduli object.

Today projective space is the most-studied nonzero variety in mathematics, with deep ties to representation theory (flag varieties, Schubert calculus), number theory (heights, rational points, Mordell-Weil), physics (gauge theory's projective Higgs branches, twistor space in Penrose's programme), and combinatorics (matroid theory, tropical geometry). The single phrase "lines through the origin" generates an essentially endless mathematical landscape.
