## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Hartshorne Chapter V develops the theory of smooth projective surfaces over an algebraically closed field $k$ through one organising structure: the intersection pairing $\mathrm{Pic}(X)\times\mathrm{Pic}(X)\to\mathbb{Z}$ [04.05.06]. From it flow the adjunction formula [04.05.07], surface Riemann-Roch [04.05.08], and the Hodge index theorem [04.05.09]; the geometric content is carried by blow-ups [04.07.02], ruled and rational surfaces, and the showcase example of the cubic surface with its 27 lines. The chapter's exercises continually combine these — an intersection count feeds an adjunction genus, which feeds a Riemann-Roch dimension, which constrains a linear system.

This pack collects nine exercises — two easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units rather than as a standalone development. The exercises group loosely by Hartshorne section: intersection-number and self-intersection warm-ups (easy), adjunction and blow-up computations (medium), and Nakai-Moishezon, Hodge-index, and cubic-surface arguments (hard).

The conventions throughout are Hartshorne's: $X$ is a smooth projective surface; $D\cdot D'$ the intersection number; $K_X$ the canonical divisor; for an irreducible curve $C\subset X$, adjunction reads $2p_a(C)-2=C\cdot(C+K_X)$ with $p_a$ the arithmetic genus. Blowing up a point $P$ produces $\pi:\widetilde X\to X$ with exceptional curve $E\cong\mathbb{P}^1$, $E^2=-1$, and $K_{\widetilde X}=\pi^*K_X+E$.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining eight follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *State and prove the Nakai-Moishezon criterion's content on a surface: a divisor $D$ on a smooth projective surface $X$ is ample iff $D^2>0$ and $D\cdot C>0$ for every irreducible curve $C\subset X$.*

**Solution.** Ampleness means some multiple $nD$ is very ample, embedding $X$ in projective space [04.05.05]. We prove the surface case of Nakai-Moishezon (Hartshorne V.1.10).

*Necessity.* If $D$ is ample, embed $X\hookrightarrow\mathbb{P}^N$ by $|nD|$. Then $nD$ is a hyperplane class $H$; $H\cdot C=\deg C>0$ for every curve $C$ (a curve has positive degree under a projective embedding), and $H^2=\deg X>0$ (the surface has positive degree). Dividing by $n^2,n$ gives $D^2>0$ and $D\cdot C>0$.

*Sufficiency.* Suppose $D^2>0$ and $D\cdot C>0$ for all irreducible $C$. By surface Riemann-Roch [04.05.08], $\chi(\mathcal{O}_X(nD))=\chi(\mathcal{O}_X)+\tfrac12 nD\cdot(nD-K_X)$ grows like $\tfrac12 n^2 D^2\to+\infty$. Serre duality bounds $h^2(nD)=h^0(K_X-nD)$, which vanishes for $n\gg 0$ since $(K_X-nD)\cdot D<0$ rules out effectivity against the positive $D$. Hence $h^0(nD)\geq\chi(nD)\to\infty$, so $|nD|$ is nonempty and grows; a positivity-against-every-curve argument (Nakai's induction on subvarieties) upgrades this to base-point-freeness and then very-ampleness of a large multiple. Therefore $D$ is ample. $\square$

This is the **Nakai-Moishezon criterion** specialised to surfaces — the numerical characterisation of ampleness. It turns "ample" (a hard geometric condition about embeddings) into two intersection inequalities, computable from the Néron-Severi lattice [04.05.02]. Most ampleness checks downstream reduce to it.

## Exercises [Intermediate]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).** *Intersection numbers on $\mathbb{P}^1\times\mathbb{P}^1$.*

On $Q=\mathbb{P}^1\times\mathbb{P}^1$ let $f,g$ be the two ruling classes (fibers of the two projections). Compute $f^2$, $g^2$, $f\cdot g$, and the self-intersection of the diagonal $\Delta$, whose class is $f+g$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Two distinct fibers of the same ruling are disjoint, so $f^2=g^2=0$; one fiber of each ruling meets in a single point, so $f\cdot g=1$ [04.05.06].

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fibers of one projection are disjoint (move one fiber to another value of the parameter), so $f^2=0$ and $g^2=0$. A fiber of the first projection and a fiber of the second meet in exactly one point transversally, so $f\cdot g=1$.

The diagonal $\Delta\subset Q$ has class $f+g$ (it projects isomorphically to each factor, meeting each ruling once). Its self-intersection:

$$
\Delta^2=(f+g)^2=f^2+2f\cdot g+g^2=0+2\cdot 1+0=2.
$$

Check via adjunction [04.05.07]: $\Delta\cong\mathbb{P}^1$ has $p_a=0$, and $K_Q=-2f-2g$, so $2p_a-2=\Delta\cdot(\Delta+K_Q)=(f+g)\cdot(-f-g)=-(f+g)^2=-2$, giving $p_a=0$. Consistent.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Self-intersection of the exceptional curve.*

Let $\pi:\widetilde X\to X$ be the blow-up of a smooth surface $X$ at a point $P$, with exceptional curve $E$. Compute $E^2$ and $E\cdot\pi^*D$ for any divisor $D$ on $X$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\pi^*D$ is supported away from $E$ generically; $E^2=-1$ is the defining numerical feature of a $(-1)$-curve [04.07.02].

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The pullback $\pi^*D$ of a divisor on $X$ meets the exceptional curve in degree zero: $\pi^*D\cdot E=D\cdot\pi_*E=D\cdot 0=0$ (the exceptional curve maps to the point $P$, so $\pi_*E=0$). Hence

$$
E\cdot\pi^*D=0\quad\text{for every divisor }D\text{ on }X.
$$

The self-intersection of the exceptional curve is

$$
E^2=-1.
$$

This is the hallmark of the blow-up [04.07.02]: $E\cong\mathbb{P}^1$ with $E^2=-1$, a "$(-1)$-curve". One way to see it: $\mathcal{O}_{\widetilde X}(E)|_E\cong\mathcal{O}_E(-1)=\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(-1)$, whose degree is $E\cdot E=-1$. The Picard group of $\widetilde X$ is $\pi^*\mathrm{Pic}(X)\oplus\mathbb{Z}E$, orthogonal with $E^2=-1$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).** *Genus of a curve through a blown-up point.*

Let $C\subset X$ be a smooth curve passing through $P$ with multiplicity $m$ (an ordinary $m$-fold point). On the blow-up $\pi:\widetilde X\to X$, the strict transform is $\widetilde C=\pi^*C-mE$. Compute $\widetilde C^2$ in terms of $C^2$ and $m$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $(\pi^*C-mE)^2$ using $\pi^*C\cdot E=0$ and $E^2=-1$ from Exercise 2.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Using the blow-up intersection rules from Exercise 2 — $(\pi^*C)^2=C^2$, $\pi^*C\cdot E=0$, $E^2=-1$:

$$
\widetilde C^2=(\pi^*C-mE)^2=(\pi^*C)^2-2m\,(\pi^*C\cdot E)+m^2E^2=C^2-0+m^2(-1)=C^2-m^2.
$$

So passing through $P$ with multiplicity $m$ and taking the strict transform drops the self-intersection by $m^2$:

$$
\widetilde C^2=C^2-m^2.
$$

This is the standard tool for resolving singularities of plane curves: blowing up a node ($m=2$) of a plane curve drops $C^2$ by $4$ and separates the branches. Combined with $K_{\widetilde X}=\pi^*K_X+E$ and adjunction, it gives the genus-drop formula $p_a(\widetilde C)=p_a(C)-\binom{m}{2}$ when $\widetilde C$ is smooth (Hartshorne V.3.7).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).** *Adjunction on the projective plane.*

Compute the arithmetic genus of a smooth plane curve $C\subset\mathbb{P}^2$ of degree $d$ using adjunction, and verify against the genus-degree formula.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On $\mathbb{P}^2$, $K_{\mathbb{P}^2}=-3H$ where $H$ is the line class, $H^2=1$, and $C\sim dH$. Apply $2p_a-2=C\cdot(C+K)$ [04.05.07].

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On $\mathbb{P}^2$: the hyperplane (line) class $H$ has $H^2=1$, the canonical class is $K_{\mathbb{P}^2}=-3H$ [04.08 canonical sheaf; $\omega=\mathcal{O}(-3)$], and a degree-$d$ curve has class $C=dH$. Adjunction [04.05.07] gives

$$
2p_a(C)-2=C\cdot(C+K_{\mathbb{P}^2})=dH\cdot(dH-3H)=d(d-3)H^2=d(d-3).
$$

Hence

$$
p_a(C)=\frac{d(d-3)}{2}+1=\frac{d^2-3d+2}{2}=\frac{(d-1)(d-2)}{2}.
$$

This matches the genus-degree formula derived cohomologically [04.03.E1 Exercise 3]: a smooth plane curve of degree $d$ has genus $\binom{d-1}{2}$. For $d=1,2$: $p_a=0$ (lines and conics are rational). For $d=3$: $p_a=1$ (cubics are elliptic). For $d=4$: $p_a=3$ (quartics realise the genus-$3$ canonical curve, Exercise 6 of the curves pack).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).** *Riemann-Roch on a surface.*

Let $X$ be a smooth projective surface with $\chi(\mathcal{O}_X)=1$ and $D$ a divisor with $D^2=6$, $D\cdot K_X=-8$. Compute $\chi(\mathcal{O}_X(D))$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Surface Riemann-Roch [04.05.08]: $\chi(\mathcal{O}_X(D))=\chi(\mathcal{O}_X)+\tfrac12 D\cdot(D-K_X)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Riemann-Roch theorem for surfaces [04.05.08] reads

$$
\chi(\mathcal{O}_X(D))=\chi(\mathcal{O}_X)+\tfrac12\,D\cdot(D-K_X).
$$

Compute the intersection term:

$$
D\cdot(D-K_X)=D^2-D\cdot K_X=6-(-8)=14.
$$

Therefore

$$
\chi(\mathcal{O}_X(D))=1+\tfrac12\cdot 14=1+7=8.
$$

So $h^0(D)-h^1(D)+h^2(D)=8$. If, in addition, $D$ is ample with $D-K_X$ ample (so Kodaira vanishing gives $h^1=h^2=0$), then $h^0(\mathcal{O}_X(D))=8$, the dimension of the embedding's linear system. The data $\chi(\mathcal{O}_X)=1$ is consistent with a rational surface (e.g. a blow-up of $\mathbb{P}^2$).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).** *The Hodge index theorem.*

Let $X$ be a smooth projective surface and $H$ an ample divisor. Show that if a divisor $D$ satisfies $D\cdot H=0$ and $D\not\equiv 0$ (numerically), then $D^2<0$. Conclude the intersection form on $\mathrm{NS}(X)\otimes\mathbb{R}$ has signature $(1,\rho-1)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

This is the Hodge index theorem [04.05.09]. Suppose $D^2\geq 0$; combine $D$ with $H$ and use Riemann-Roch to produce effective divisors, contradicting $D\cdot H=0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $D\cdot H=0$, $D\not\equiv 0$, and toward a contradiction $D^2\geq 0$.

*Case $D^2>0$.* Consider $D_n=D+nH$ for varying $n$. We have $D_n^2=D^2+2n(D\cdot H)+n^2H^2=D^2+n^2H^2>0$. By surface Riemann-Roch [04.05.08] and the growth argument (lead exercise), for $|n|$ large $D_n$ or $-D_n$ is effective. But $D_n\cdot H=D\cdot H+nH^2=nH^2$, which is positive for $n>0$ and negative for $n<0$; an effective divisor meets the ample $H$ non-negatively, so $D_n$ effective forces $n>0$ and $-D_n$ effective forces $n<0$ — both achievable, yet then $D=\tfrac12(D_n+D_{-n}\text{-type})$ relations force $D\cdot H\neq 0$ unless $D\equiv 0$. More directly: $D^2>0$ and $D\not\equiv0$ make $D$ or $-D$ effective (big), so $D\cdot H\neq 0$ for ample $H$ — contradicting $D\cdot H=0$.

*Case $D^2=0$.* Pick any divisor $E$ with $E\cdot D\neq 0$ (exists since $D\not\equiv 0$ and the form is non-degenerate on $\mathrm{NS}$). For real $t$, consider $D'=tD+E'$ where $E'=E-\frac{E\cdot H}{H^2}H$ satisfies $E'\cdot H=0$. Then $(tD+E')\cdot H=0$ and $(tD+E')^2=2t(D\cdot E')+E'^2$, which is linear in $t$ with nonzero slope $2(D\cdot E')\neq 0$, so it takes a positive value for some $t$ — reducing to the previous case and yielding a contradiction.

Hence $D^2<0$. This says: on the hyperplane $H^\perp\subset\mathrm{NS}(X)\otimes\mathbb{R}$, the intersection form is negative definite. Since $H^2>0$, the form is positive on the line $\mathbb{R}H$ and negative on its orthogonal complement of dimension $\rho-1$. The signature is $(1,\rho-1)$ — the **Hodge index theorem** (Hartshorne V.1.9). It underlies the structure of the Néron-Severi lattice and the spin-geometry $b_2^+=1$ phenomenon for many algebraic surfaces.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).** *The cubic surface is $\mathbb{P}^2$ blown up at six points.*

Show that a smooth cubic surface $X\subset\mathbb{P}^3$ is isomorphic to $\mathbb{P}^2$ blown up at $6$ points in general position, and use this to count $K_X^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The anticanonical class: by adjunction on $\mathbb{P}^3$, $K_X=(K_{\mathbb{P}^3}+X)|_X=(-4H+3H)|_X=-H|_X$, so $X$ is anticanonically embedded. Blowing up $6$ general points of $\mathbb{P}^2$ has $K^2=9-6=3$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By adjunction for the surface $X=\{F_3=0\}\subset\mathbb{P}^3$ [04.05.07]:

$$
K_X=(K_{\mathbb{P}^3}+X)\big|_X=(-4H+3H)\big|_X=-H\big|_X,
$$

so $-K_X$ is the hyperplane class — $X$ is embedded by its anticanonical system $|-K_X|$, a del Pezzo surface of degree $K_X^2=H^2|_X=\deg X=3$.

Now blow up $6$ points $p_1,\dots,p_6\in\mathbb{P}^2$ in general position (no $3$ collinear, not all $6$ on a conic), giving $\pi:Y\to\mathbb{P}^2$ with exceptional curves $E_1,\dots,E_6$. Then $\mathrm{Pic}(Y)=\mathbb{Z}\langle \ell,E_1,\dots,E_6\rangle$ where $\ell=\pi^*H$, with $\ell^2=1$, $E_i^2=-1$, $\ell\cdot E_i=E_i\cdot E_j=0$ ($i\neq j$). The canonical class is

$$
K_Y=-3\ell+E_1+\cdots+E_6,\qquad K_Y^2=9\cdot 1+6\cdot(-1)=9-6=3.
$$

The anticanonical system $|-K_Y|=|3\ell-\sum E_i|$ is the system of plane cubics through the $6$ points; for points in general position it is very ample of dimension $\ell(-K_Y)-1=3$ (a net of cubics through $6$ points has $\binom{3+2}{2}-6=10-6=4$ dimensions of sections), embedding $Y\hookrightarrow\mathbb{P}^3$ as a cubic surface. Both $X$ and $Y$ are anticanonically embedded del Pezzo surfaces of degree $3$; by the uniqueness of the minimal model and the classification (Hartshorne V.4.7), $X\cong Y$.

Thus every smooth cubic surface is $\mathbb{P}^2$ blown up at $6$ general points, with $K_X^2=3$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *The 27 lines on a cubic surface.*

Using the blow-up model of Exercise 7, enumerate the $27$ lines on a smooth cubic surface and verify the count.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Lines on $X$ are the $(-1)$-curves: classes $C$ with $C^2=-1$ and $C\cdot K_X=-1$. Sort them by type on the blow-up $\mathbb{P}^2$ at $6$ points: the $6$ exceptional curves, the $\binom{6}{2}$ lines through pairs, the $6$ conics through five points.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On a cubic surface a line $L$ has $L\cdot(-K_X)=L\cdot H=1$ (lines have degree $1$), and by adjunction [04.05.07] $2p_a(L)-2=L\cdot(L+K_X)$ with $p_a=0$ gives $-2=L^2-1$, so $L^2=-1$. Lines are exactly the $(-1)$-curves: $C^2=-1$, $C\cdot K_X=-1$.

On $Y=\mathrm{Bl}_6\mathbb{P}^2$ with $K_Y=-3\ell+\sum E_i$, solve $C=a\ell-\sum b_iE_i$ with $C^2=a^2-\sum b_i^2=-1$ and $-C\cdot K_Y=3a-\sum b_i=1$. The non-negative solutions (effective irreducible $(-1)$-classes) are:

- **The $6$ exceptional curves** $E_i$: class $E_i$, with $E_i^2=-1$. Count: $\binom{6}{1}=6$.
- **The $\binom{6}{2}=15$ strict transforms of lines** through two of the points: class $\ell-E_i-E_j$, with $(\ell-E_i-E_j)^2=1-1-1=-1$. Count: $15$.
- **The $6$ strict transforms of conics** through five of the six points: class $2\ell-\sum_{k\neq i}E_k$, with $(2\ell-\sum_{k\neq i}E_k)^2=4-5=-1$. Count: $\binom{6}{5}=6$.

Total:

$$
6+15+6=27.
$$

These are the famous **27 lines** (Cayley-Salmon, 1849). Their intersection pattern — each line meets $10$ others — is governed by the root system $E_6$: the $27$ lines correspond to the $27$ weights of the minuscule representation of $E_6$, and the Weyl group $W(E_6)$ (order $51840$) acts as the symmetry group permuting them (Hartshorne V.4.9–4.11). The configuration is the prototype example in the deformation theory of del Pezzo surfaces.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (medium, proof).** *Castelnuovo's contractibility — the numerical criterion.*

Let $E\subset X$ be a curve on a smooth projective surface with $E\cong\mathbb{P}^1$ and $E^2=-1$. Show that $E\cdot K_X=-1$, the numerical signature of an exceptional curve of a blow-up.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply adjunction [04.05.07] to $E$ with $p_a(E)=0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $E\cong\mathbb{P}^1$, its arithmetic genus is $p_a(E)=0$. Adjunction [04.05.07] on the surface $X$ gives

$$
2p_a(E)-2=E\cdot(E+K_X)=E^2+E\cdot K_X.
$$

Substituting $p_a(E)=0$ and $E^2=-1$:

$$
-2=-1+E\cdot K_X\quad\Longrightarrow\quad E\cdot K_X=-1.
$$

So a smooth rational curve with $E^2=-1$ automatically has $E\cdot K_X=-1$ — it is a **$(-1)$-curve**, exactly the numerical type of the exceptional curve of a single blow-up (Exercise 2). **Castelnuovo's contractibility criterion** (Hartshorne V.5.7) is the converse direction: any such $(-1)$-curve can be contracted, i.e. there is a morphism $\pi:X\to X'$ to a smooth projective surface realising $X$ as the blow-up of $X'$ at a point with $E$ as exceptional curve. This is the engine of the minimal-model program for surfaces: contract $(-1)$-curves until none remain, reaching a minimal model. The $27$ lines of Exercise 8 are precisely the $27$ ways to contract a cubic surface back down toward $\mathbb{P}^2$.

</details>

</aside>
