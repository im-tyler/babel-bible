## Intuition [Beginner]

When two curves in the plane cross, they meet in points. *Intersection theory* answers two questions about such a meeting: how many points are there, and how should each one be counted when the curves touch tangentially or overlap along a piece? The cleanest answer in mathematics — Bézout's theorem, that two plane curves of degrees $m$ and $n$ meet in $m \cdot n$ points — lives in a piece of algebra called the *Chow ring*.

The big idea is to replace each subvariety by an algebraic object recording its "class," and to define the meeting of two subvarieties as the product of their classes. The class of a curve in the plane is its degree; the class of a surface in space is its degree; and the intersection of two classes is a number you compute by multiplication. The Chow ring is the home of these classes and the place where this multiplication is honest.

Why does the counting need care? Two subvarieties may overlap along a whole piece rather than meet in isolated points, and tangencies must be weighted correctly. The modern theory — built by Chow, Grothendieck, Fulton, and MacPherson — makes the counting rigorous on any smooth variety. The reward is *enumerative geometry*: classical puzzles such as the 27 lines on a smooth cubic surface, or the 3264 conics tangent to five given conics, become clean computations in a ring.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Bézout's theorem is the prototype of intersection counting. Two plane curves in the projective plane, of degrees $m$ and $n$, with no shared piece, meet in exactly $m \cdot n$ points when each is counted with its local intersection multiplicity.

Concretely: a line (degree 1) and a conic (degree 2) meet in $1 \cdot 2 = 2$ points. Two conics (both degree 2) meet in $2 \cdot 2 = 4$ points. A line (degree 1) and a cubic (degree 3) meet in $1 \cdot 3 = 3$ points. Two cubics meet in $3 \cdot 3 = 9$ points. A cubic and a quartic meet in $3 \cdot 4 = 12$ points.

The multiplicities are what make the count rigid. A line tangent to a conic touches it at a single point but with multiplicity 2, so the count is still 2. If a line is actually one of the two pieces of a reducible conic, the "no shared piece" hypothesis fails, and the theorem does not apply directly — which is exactly why that hypothesis is required.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be an algebraic scheme over a field $k$. An *algebraic $k$-cycle* on $X$ is a finite formal $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of integral closed subschemes of dimension $k$:

$$
Z \;=\; \sum_{V} n_V [V], \qquad n_V \in \mathbb{Z}.
$$

The free abelian group of $k$-cycles is denoted $Z_k(X)$. A cycle is *effective* if every $n_V \geq 0$.

**Rational equivalence.** Two $k$-cycles $Z_0, Z_1$ are *rationally equivalent* (written $Z_0 \sim_{\mathrm{rat}} Z_1$) if there is a $(k+1)$-cycle $W$ on $X \times \mathbb{P}^1$, flat over $\mathbb{P}^1$, whose fibres over $0, \infty \in \mathbb{P}^1$ are $Z_0$ and $Z_1$. Equivalently, $Z_0$ can be deformed into $Z_1$ through an algebraic family parametrised by $\mathbb{P}^1$. Rational equivalence is generated by the relation $Z_0 - Z_1 = 0$ for $[k(\mathbb{P}^1)]$-families of this form. This is the algebraic analogue of homotopy: cycles in the same rational-equivalence class play the same enumerative role.

**Chow groups.** The *$k$-th Chow group* of $X$ is the group of $k$-cycles modulo rational equivalence:

$$
A_k(X) \;:=\; Z_k(X) \,/\, \sim_{\mathrm{rat}}.
$$

When $X$ is *equidimensional* of dimension $n$, it is often cleaner to grade by *codimension*: $A^i(X) := A_{n-i}(X)$. So $A^0(X) = \mathbb{Z}\cdot[X]$, $A^1(X)$ is the group of Weil divisors modulo rational equivalence — on a smooth variety, the divisor class group $\mathrm{Cl}(X)$ [04.05.01] — and $A^n(X)$ is the group of 0-cycles modulo rational equivalence.

**Proper pushforward.** If $f : X \to Y$ is a proper morphism, pushforward of cycles $f_*[V] = \deg(V / f(V)) \cdot [\overline{f(V)}]$ descends to a homomorphism $f_* : A_k(X) \to A_k(Y)$. (When $f(V)$ has strictly smaller dimension, $f_*[V] = 0$.)

**Flat pullback and Gysin maps.** If $f : X \to Y$ is flat of relative dimension $d$, flat pullback of cycles descends to $f^* : A_k(Y) \to A_{k+d}(X)$. More generally, for a closed embedding $i : Y \hookrightarrow X$ of *smooth* varieties of pure codimension $d$, the *Gysin pullback* (refined Gysin map) $i^! : A_k(X) \to A_{k-d}(Y)$ is defined by reduction to the normal cone; this is the central construction of modern intersection theory, developed below.

**The Chow ring.** On a *smooth* variety $X$ of dimension $n$, the Chow groups $A^*(X) = \bigoplus_{i \geq 0} A^i(X)$ carry a graded-commutative ring structure. The multiplication

$$
A^i(X) \times A^j(X) \;\longrightarrow\; A^{i+j}(X), \qquad (\alpha, \beta) \mapsto \alpha \cdot \beta
$$

is the *intersection product*. Its construction proceeds by *reduction to the diagonal*: if $\Delta : X \hookrightarrow X \times X$ is the diagonal embedding, the intersection product is defined by

$$
\alpha \cdot \beta \;=\; \Delta^!(\alpha \times \beta),
$$

the Gysin pullback of the exterior product $\alpha \times \beta \in A^*(X \times X)$ along the diagonal. The diagonal of a smooth variety is a *regular embedding*, which is what makes this pullback well-defined.

**Degree map.** For a complete (proper over $k$) variety $X$, there is a *degree map* $\deg : A_0(X) \to \mathbb{Z}$ sending a 0-cycle $\sum n_P [P]$ to $\sum n_P [k(P) : k]$. The *intersection number* of $n$ divisor classes on an $n$-dimensional smooth projective variety is the degree of their iterated intersection product.

**Relation to cohomology.** For a smooth complex projective variety, there is a *cycle class map* $\mathrm{cl} : A^*(X) \to H^{2*}(X; \mathbb{Z})$ sending a codimension-$i$ cycle to its cohomology class in $H^{2i}(X; \mathbb{Z})$ [04.03.01]. This map is a ring homomorphism (it sends the intersection product to the cup product), but it is *not* an isomorphism in general: the kernel and cokernel of $\mathrm{cl}$ measure the gap between algebraic and topological cycles, a central object of the theory of *motives*.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Chow ring of projective space; Bézout's theorem).** *Let $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ be projective space over a field $k$, and let $H \in A^1(\mathbb{P}^n_k)$ be the class of a hyperplane.*

*(i) (Chow ring of $\mathbb{P}^n$.) There is a ring isomorphism*

$$
A^*(\mathbb{P}^n_k) \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}[H] / (H^{n+1}).
$$

*In particular $A^i(\mathbb{P}^n_k) = \mathbb{Z}\cdot H^i$ for $0 \leq i \leq n$, and $H^{n+1} = 0$.*

*(ii) (Bézout.) If $C, D \subset \mathbb{P}^2_k$ are plane curves of degrees $m, n$ with no common irreducible component, then $C$ and $D$ meet in exactly $mn$ points counted with local intersection multiplicity.*

**Proof.** *Part (i).* Every integral codimension-$i$ subvariety $V \subset \mathbb{P}^n_k$ has a well-defined degree $\deg(V)$, namely the number of points in the intersection of $V$ with a general $(n-i)$-plane. The *degree map*

$$
\deg : A^i(\mathbb{P}^n_k) \longrightarrow \mathbb{Z}, \qquad \sum n_V [V] \;\mapsto\; \sum n_V \deg(V),
$$

is well-defined on rational equivalence classes because degree is constant in flat $\mathbb{P}^1$-families. Any codimension-$i$ linear subspace $L^i$ has degree 1, and $H^i = [L^i]$ in $A^i$. Since $\deg(V) = \deg(V) \cdot \deg(L^i)$ and $V$ is rationally equivalent to $\deg(V) \cdot L^i$ (a standard deformation through the incidence correspondence $\{(V, \Lambda) : V \cap \Lambda \neq \emptyset\}$), the degree map is an isomorphism $A^i \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathbb{Z}$ sending $H^i \mapsto 1$ for $0 \leq i \leq n$.

The relation $H^{n+1} = 0$ holds because $n+1$ hyperplanes in general position in $\mathbb{P}^n$ have empty intersection, and the class of the empty cycle is zero. It follows that the surjective map $\mathbb{Z}[H] \to A^*(\mathbb{P}^n_k)$ sending $H \mapsto H$ descends to $\mathbb{Z}[H]/(H^{n+1}) \to A^*(\mathbb{P}^n_k)$, which is a ring isomorphism by degree-counting in each codimension.

*Part (ii) (Bézout).* Let $C, D \subset \mathbb{P}^2_k$ have degrees $m, n$ and no common component. By part (i), $[C] = m H$ and $[D] = n H$ in $A^1(\mathbb{P}^2_k)$, since a plane curve of degree $d$ has class $dH$. The intersection product is

$$
[C] \cdot [D] \;=\; (mH)(nH) \;=\; mn\, H^2 \;\in\; A^2(\mathbb{P}^2_k),
$$

and $H^2$ is the class of a point, so $[C]\cdot[D]$ is the 0-cycle "$mn$ points." The hypothesis that $C, D$ share no component means their intersection is *proper* (codimension $1 + 1 = 2$, the expected codimension in $\mathbb{P}^2$), so the scheme-theoretic intersection cycle $\sum_{P \in C \cap D} i_P(C, D) [P]$ — where $i_P(C, D) = \dim_k (\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2, P} / (f, g))$ is the local intersection multiplicity — equals the Chow-ring intersection product. Taking degrees,

$$
\sum_{P \in C \cap D} i_P(C, D) \;=\; \deg(mn\, H^2) \;=\; mn. \qquad \square
$$

This theorem is the foundational pattern of all of enumerative geometry: *compute an intersection number by computing a product in the Chow ring.* The moving lemma of Chow [ref: TODO_REF Chow1956] and the deformation-to-the-normal-conse construction of Fulton-MacPherson [ref: TODO_REF FultonMacPherson1978] extend this pattern from the transverse setting of Bézout to arbitrary intersections on smooth varieties, including tangencies and excess intersections. The same pattern — degree equals product — gives the 27 lines on a smooth cubic surface (computed in $A^*(\mathbb{P}^2 \text{ blown up at six points})$) and the 3264 conics tangent to five given conics (computed in the Chow ring of the space of complete conics).

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [04.05.09] (Hodge index theorem) and [04.05.10] (Hirzebruch-Riemann-Roch), where the intersection product on surfaces and higher-dimensional varieties is sharpened into numerical invariants. The defining pattern appears again in [04.03.01] (sheaf cohomology) in a sharpened form: the cycle class map sends the Chow ring to the even cohomology ring, and the central insight is that intersection theory is the algebraic shadow of the cup product in cohomology. The intersection product generalises Chern-class computations on vector bundles [04.05.05], and the foundational reason intersection numbers are well-defined is exactly that rational equivalence absorbs the ambiguity of choosing representatives; this is precisely the statement that the Chow ring is a ring and not merely a graded abelian group. Putting these together, the bridge is that Bézout's theorem is the codimension-1 prototype of every enumerative computation, and the Chow ring is the universal setting where such computations live.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Using the Chow ring $A^*(\mathbb{P}^3_k) \cong \mathbb{Z}[H]/(H^4)$, compute the intersection number of two general lines $L_1, L_2 \subset \mathbb{P}^3_k$, and the intersection number of a general line with a general plane.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A line in $\mathbb{P}^3$ has class $H^2$; a plane has class $H$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In $\mathbb{P}^3$, a hyperplane (a plane) has class $H \in A^1$, a line has class $H^2 \in A^2$, and a point has class $H^3 \in A^3$.

Two general lines $L_1, L_2$ have classes $H^2, H^2$, so $[L_1]\cdot[L_2] = H^2 \cdot H^2 = H^4 = 0$ in $A^4(\mathbb{P}^3)$. Indeed two general lines in $\mathbb{P}^3$ do not meet (they are skew), so the intersection number is 0.

A general line and a general plane have classes $H^2, H$, so $[L]\cdot[\Pi] = H^2 \cdot H = H^3 = [\mathrm{pt}]$, giving intersection number 1: a general line meets a general plane in one point.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).**

Show that on $\mathbb{P}^n_k$, any two points $P, Q$ are rationally equivalent as 0-cycles: $[P] \sim_{\mathrm{rat}} [Q]$ in $A_0(\mathbb{P}^n_k)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Exhibit a line through $P$ and $Q$, and view it as a family over $\mathbb{P}^1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ is a projective space, any two points $P, Q$ lie on a common line $L \cong \mathbb{P}^1 \subset \mathbb{P}^n_k$. Parametrise $L$ by $\mathbb{P}^1$ so that $0 \mapsto P$ and $\infty \mapsto Q$. The line $L$ itself, viewed as a 1-cycle on $\mathbb{P}^n_k \times \mathbb{P}^1$ via this parametrisation, is flat over $\mathbb{P}^1$, with fibre $[P]$ over $0$ and $[Q]$ over $\infty$. By the definition of rational equivalence, $[P] - [Q] = 0$ in $A_0(\mathbb{P}^n_k)$. Combined with $A_0(\mathbb{P}^n_k) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ via the degree map (part (i) of the Key theorem), the class of any point generates $A_0(\mathbb{P}^n_k)$, and the degree map $[P] \mapsto 1$ is an isomorphism $A_0(\mathbb{P}^n_k) \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathbb{Z}$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Compute the Chow ring of $X = \mathbb{P}^1_k \times \mathbb{P}^1_k$, and use it to compute the self-intersection number of a fibre of each projection.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the projective bundle formula (or Künneth-type decomposition) for products of projective spaces.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\alpha, \beta \in A^1(X)$ be the classes of the fibres of the first and second projections, $[\{p\} \times \mathbb{P}^1]$ and $[\mathbb{P}^1 \times \{q\}]$. By the projective bundle formula (or direct rational-equivalence argument),

$$
A^*(\mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1) \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}[\alpha, \beta] / (\alpha^2, \beta^2).
$$

The class $\alpha$ is pulled back from the first factor, where $H^2 = 0$ in $A^2(\mathbb{P}^1) = 0$-cycles of degree 0; hence $\alpha^2 = 0$; similarly $\beta^2 = 0$. The mixed product is $\alpha \beta = [\mathrm{pt}]$, the class of a point in $A^2(X) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.

A fibre $F_1 = [\{p\} \times \mathbb{P}^1]$ has class $\alpha$. Its self-intersection is $\alpha \cdot \alpha = \alpha^2 = 0$ in $A^2(X)$, so the self-intersection number of a fibre of the first projection is $0$. Geometrically: two distinct fibres of $\mathrm{pr}_1$ are disjoint, and the self-intersection captures this. Similarly the self-intersection of a fibre $F_2$ of $\mathrm{pr}_2$ is $\beta^2 = 0$.

By contrast, the diagonal $\Delta \subset \mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ has class $\alpha + \beta$ (its degree is 1 in each factor), and its self-intersection is $(\alpha + \beta)^2 = 2\alpha\beta = 2$, the Euler characteristic of $\mathbb{P}^1$ — the Lefschetz fixed-point count.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

On the smooth quadric surface $Q = V(x_0 x_3 - x_1 x_2) \subset \mathbb{P}^3_k$, the Chow ring is $A^*(Q) \cong \mathbb{Z}[\alpha, \beta]/(\alpha^2, \beta^2)$ where $\alpha, \beta$ are the two rulings. Compute the self-intersection number of a hyperplane section $H$ of $Q$ in $\mathbb{P}^3$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A hyperplane section of $Q$ is the sum of one line from each ruling: $H = \alpha + \beta$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A hyperplane in $\mathbb{P}^3$ cuts $Q$ in a conic, which on the smooth quadric (isomorphic to $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$) is the union of one line from each ruling. Hence $[H] = \alpha + \beta$ in $A^1(Q)$. The self-intersection is

$$
[H]^2 = (\alpha + \beta)^2 = \alpha^2 + 2\alpha\beta + \beta^2 = 0 + 2\alpha\beta + 0 = 2 \alpha\beta = 2[\mathrm{pt}].
$$

So $H^2 = 2$, and the self-intersection number of a hyperplane section of the quadric is $2$. This is the *degree* of the quadric surface in $\mathbb{P}^3$, consistent with $Q$ being cut out by a single quadratic equation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why does the intersection product on $A^*(X)$ require $X$ to be smooth? What goes wrong on a singular variety?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The definition uses reduction to the diagonal, and relies on the diagonal being a *regular* embedding.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The intersection product on $A^*(X)$ is defined by $\alpha \cdot \beta := \Delta^!(\alpha \times \beta)$, the Gysin pullback of the exterior product along the diagonal $\Delta : X \hookrightarrow X \times X$. For this refined Gysin map $\Delta^!$ to exist, the diagonal embedding must be a *regular embedding* (locally cut out by a regular sequence), which is equivalent to $X$ being *smooth* over the base.

On a singular variety, the diagonal is not a regular embedding: the local rings of $X \times X$ along $\Delta$ are not regular sequences. As a result the naive intersection of two cycles is not well-defined modulo rational equivalence — the choice of representative cycles affects the rational-equivalence class of the intersection.

For singular varieties one therefore keeps only a partial structure: the *operational Chow ring* $A^*(X)$ of Fulton, whose elements are operations $A_*(X) \to A_{*-i}(X)$ rather than cycle classes. This operational ring agrees with the cycle-level Chow ring when $X$ is smooth, and retains enough structure to define Chern classes and intersection numbers, but it is weaker than a true cycle-level intersection product.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

State the *excess intersection formula* for a regular embedding and prove its special case: the *self-intersection formula* (see Full proof set below for the full argument). Concretely, for a closed embedding $i : Y \hookrightarrow X$ of smooth varieties of codimension $d$ with normal bundle $N_{Y/X}$, show that for every $\alpha \in A_*(Y)$,

$$
i^! i_* \alpha \;=\; c_d(N_{Y/X}) \cap \alpha.
$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reduce to the case where $Y$ is the zero-section of its normal bundle; there $i^!$ is division by the top Chern class of the normal bundle.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The *excess intersection formula* concerns the following situation: a fibre square

$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
Y' & \xrightarrow{g} & Y \\
\downarrow i' & & \downarrow i \\
X' & \xrightarrow{f} & X
\end{array}
$$

with $i : Y \hookrightarrow X$ a regular embedding of codimension $d$, $Y', X'$ smooth (or more generally $i'$ a regular embedding of codimension $d - e$, with $e$ the *excess*), and normal bundles $N = N_{Y/X}$, $N' = N_{Y'/X'}$. The excess bundle is $E = g^* N / N'$, a rank-$e$ bundle on $Y'$. The excess intersection formula states

$$
f^* i_* \alpha \;=\; i'_*\bigl(c_e(E) \cap g^* \alpha\bigr).
$$

For the self-intersection formula, take $X' = Y$, $f = i : Y \hookrightarrow X$, $Y' = Y$, $i' = \mathrm{id}$. Then $N' = N_{Y/Y} = 0$, $E = i^* N_{Y/X} = N_{Y/X}$ (rank $d$, so $e = d$), and the formula reads

$$
i^* i_* \alpha \;=\; \mathrm{id}_*\bigl(c_d(N_{Y/X}) \cap \alpha\bigr) \;=\; c_d(N_{Y/X}) \cap \alpha,
$$

with $i^!$ replacing $i^*$ for the refined version. The proof that $i^! i_* \alpha = c_d(N_{Y/X}) \cap \alpha$ proceeds by deformation to the normal cone (see the Full proof set), reducing to the model case where $X = $ total space of $N_{Y/X}$ and $Y$ is the zero-section, where $i^! i_*$ is computed by the Thom isomorphism and division by the top Chern class. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Outline how the Chow ring computes the number of lines on a smooth cubic surface $S \subset \mathbb{P}^3$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$S$ is the blow-up of $\mathbb{P}^2$ at six points in general position; use the Chow ring of the blow-up.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A smooth cubic surface $S \subset \mathbb{P}^3$ is isomorphic to the blow-up of $\mathbb{P}^2$ at six points $p_1, \ldots, p_6$ in general position (no three collinear, no six on a conic). Let $\pi : S \to \mathbb{P}^2$ be the blow-down, $H = \pi^*([\text{line}])$ the pullback of the hyperplane class, and $E_1, \ldots, E_6$ the classes of the six exceptional curves. The Chow ring is

$$
A^*(S) \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}[H, E_1, \ldots, E_6] \big/ \bigl(H^2 = 1, \; E_i E_j = -\delta_{ij}, \; H E_i = 0\bigr).
$$

A line on $S$ is a curve $L$ with $L^2 = -1$ and $L \cdot H = 1$ (self-intersection $-1$ and degree 1 as a curve in $\mathbb{P}^3$). Write $L \sim dH - \sum m_i E_i$. The conditions $L \cdot H = 1$ and $L^2 = -1$ give

$$
d \;=\; 1, \qquad d^2 - \sum m_i^2 \;=\; 1 - \sum m_i^2 \;=\; -1,
$$

so $\sum m_i^2 = 2$. With $L$ effective ($m_i \geq 0$) and $K_S = -3H + \sum E_i$, adjunction $L \cdot (L + K_S) = -2$ forces $L \cdot K_S = -1$, giving $-3 + \sum m_i = -1$, hence $\sum m_i = 2$.

The only integer solutions to $\sum m_i = 2$ and $\sum m_i^2 = 2$ with $m_i \in \{0, 1\}$ are the $\binom{6}{2} = 15$ pairs $\{i, j\}$, giving the 15 classes $H - E_i - E_j$. Together with the 6 exceptional classes $E_i$ and the 6 "anti-exceptional" classes $2H - \sum_{i \neq j} E_i$ (one per $j$), the total is $15 + 6 + 6 = 27$. So $S$ carries exactly 27 lines.

This computation — carried out entirely in the Chow ring of the blow-up — is the classical result of Cayley and Salmon (1849), recovered as a clean enumerative calculation. The same Chow-ring machinery, applied to the space of complete conics, gives the 3264 conics tangent to five given conics [ref: TODO_REF EisenbudHarris2016].

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Deformation to the normal cone.** The single most important technical device of modern intersection theory, due to Fulton and MacPherson [ref: TODO_REF FultonMacPherson1978]. Given a closed embedding $i : Y \hookrightarrow X$, one constructs a flat family over $\mathbb{A}^1$ whose general fibre is $X$ and whose special fibre is the *normal cone* $C_Y X$ — a cone over $Y$ containing the normal bundle $N_{Y/X}$ as an open dense subcone when $Y$ is a regular embedding. The family is built as $M_Y^\circ X := \mathrm{Bl}_{Y \times \{\infty\}}(X \times \mathbb{P}^1) \setminus \mathrm{Bl}_{Y \times \{\infty\}}(X \times \{\infty\})$, the deformation space. Because intersection numbers are constant in flat families, intersection questions about $Y \hookrightarrow X$ reduce to intersection questions about the zero-section of the normal cone, which are tractable.

**The refined Gysin map.** For a regular embedding $i : Y \hookrightarrow X$ of codimension $d$, deformation to the normal cone defines a *refined Gysin map* $i^! : A_k(X) \to A_{k-d}(Y)$ for *any* $k$-cycle on $X$, even when its support does not meet $Y$ transversely. The construction: given a cycle $\alpha$ on $X$, deform $X$ to $C_Y X$, take the inverse-image cycle on the normal cone, and cap with the Segre class of $C_Y X / N_{Y/X}$. This is the foundation of the intersection product (via reduction to the diagonal) and of the excess intersection formula.

**Excess intersection formula.** For a fibre square of regular embeddings with excess bundle $E$ of rank $e$,

$$
f^* i_* \alpha \;=\; i'_*\bigl(c_e(E) \cap g^* \alpha\bigr),
$$

the *excess intersection formula*. It corrects the naive pullback by the top Chern class of the excess bundle, and it is the rigorous statement that replaces the false "intersection number = product of degrees" when intersections are not transverse. The self-intersection formula $i^! i_* \alpha = c_d(N_{Y/X}) \cap \alpha$ is the special case $e = d$.

**Chern classes as operators.** For a vector bundle $E$ of rank $r$ on an $n$-dimensional smooth variety $X$, the Chern classes $c_i(E) \in A^i(X)$ act by capping: $c_i(E) \cap : A_k(X) \to A_{k-i}(X)$. The top Chern class $c_r(E) \cap [X]$ is the class of the zero-locus of a general section of $E$. This operational definition extends to singular varieties and gives Chern classes for *all* vector bundles without smoothness, a major advantage of Fulton's approach over the classical one via projective bundles.

**Segre classes and the Segre-Chern relation.** The *Segre class* $s(E) = c(E)^{-1}$ satisfies $s(E) c(E) = 1$ in $A^*(X)$. Segre classes compute the contribution of the excess cone in the refined Gysin map; together with the Chern class they form the basic toolkit of modern enumerative geometry.

**Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch.** For a proper morphism $f : X \to Y$ of smooth varieties and a coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$,

$$
\mathrm{ch}\bigl(f_! [\mathcal{F}]\bigr) \cdot \mathrm{td}(T_Y) \;=\; f_*\bigl(\mathrm{ch}(\mathcal{F}) \cdot \mathrm{td}(T_X)\bigr) \quad \text{in } A^*(Y) \otimes \mathbb{Q},
$$

where $\mathrm{ch}$ is the Chern character, $\mathrm{td}$ the Todd class, and $f_!$ the $K$-theoretic pushforward. GRR identifies intersection-theoretic and $K$-theoretic pushforwards, and specialises to Hirzebruch-Riemann-Roch [04.05.10] when $Y = \mathrm{pt}$, recovering $\chi(X, \mathcal{F}) = \deg(\mathrm{ch}(\mathcal{F}) \cdot \mathrm{td}(T_X))$.

**Enumerative applications.** Three classical counts sit at the heart of enumerative geometry, all computed in Chow rings: (i) Bézout's theorem (this unit, Key theorem), the count of $mn$ intersection points of plane curves of degrees $m, n$; (ii) the 27 lines on a smooth cubic surface (Exercise 7), computed in $A^*$ of the blow-up of $\mathbb{P}^2$ at six points; (iii) the 3264 conics tangent to five given conics [ref: TODO_REF EisenbudHarris2016], computed by Chasles in 1864 and recovered by Fulton-MacPherson via the Chow ring of the space of complete conics.

**Relation to $K$-theory and motives.** The cycle class map $A^*(X) \to H^{2*}(X; \mathbb{Z})$ factors through algebraic $K$-theory via the Chern character $\mathrm{ch} : K_0(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \to A^*(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ (an isomorphism for smooth $X$, by Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch). The *motivic* viewpoint (Voevodsky) promotes Chow groups to a universal cohomology theory: Chow groups are the morphism groups in Voevodsky's category of effective motives, and the standard conjectures of Grothendieck govern when the cycle class map becomes an isomorphism.

**Synthesis.** The Chow ring $A^*(X)$ of a smooth variety $X$ is the algebraic counterpart of the cohomology ring, with the intersection product playing the role of the cup product, and the foundational reason this works is that rational equivalence absorbs the ambiguity in choosing cycle representatives so that intersections are well-defined at the level of classes; this is exactly the content of the moving lemma and the deformation-to-the-normal-conse construction. The refined Gysin map generalises the intersection product to non-transverse situations through the excess intersection formula, the central insight being that an "over-determined" intersection is corrected by the top Chern class of the excess bundle. Putting these together, the bridge is that every classical enumerative count — from Bézout's $mn$ to the 27 lines and the 3264 conics — becomes a single multiplication in an appropriate Chow ring, and the cycle class map to singular cohomology [04.03.01] shows that this purely algebraic computation faithfully records topological intersection data. Operational Chow groups extend the theory to singular varieties, and Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch identifies the Chow ring with rationalised $K$-theory, so the central insight is that intersection theory is the universal algebraic framework in which enumerative geometry, characteristic classes, and Riemann-Roch formulae all live.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Self-intersection formula via deformation to the normal cone).** *Let $X$ be a smooth variety and $i : Y \hookrightarrow X$ a closed embedding of smooth varieties of codimension $d$ with normal bundle $N = N_{Y/X}$. For every $\alpha \in A_*(Y)$, the refined Gysin pullback satisfies*

$$
i^! i_* \alpha \;=\; c_d(N) \cap \alpha \qquad \text{in } A_{*-d}(Y).
$$

**Proof.** We use the deformation to the normal cone. Let $M_Y^\circ X$ be the deformation space: the complement of the proper transform of $X \times \{\infty\}$ in the blow-up $\mathrm{Bl}_{Y \times \{\infty\}}(X \times \mathbb{P}^1)$. This is a smooth variety (since $X$ and $Y$ are smooth and the centre $Y \times \{\infty\}$ is regularly embedded) carrying a flat morphism $\rho : M_Y^\circ X \to \mathbb{P}^1$, with general fibre $\rho^{-1}(t) \cong X$ for $t \neq \infty$ and special fibre

$$
\rho^{-1}(\infty) \;=\; C_Y X \;\supseteq\; N_{Y/X}
$$

equal to the *normal cone* of $Y$ in $X$, containing the normal bundle $N_{Y/X}$ as an open dense subcone. Because $Y$ is regularly embedded in $X$ (both smooth), the normal cone *is* the normal bundle: $C_Y X = N_{Y/X}$, and the special fibre of $\rho$ is the total space of $N_{Y/X}$.

The cycle $i_* \alpha \in A_*(X)$ is represented by a cycle $\widetilde{\alpha}$ on $M_Y^\circ X$, flat over $\mathbb{P}^1$, whose restriction to the general fibre is $i_* \alpha$ and whose restriction to the special fibre is a cycle $\widetilde{\alpha}_\infty$ on the total space of $N$. By *principle of continuity* (intersection numbers are constant in flat families), the refined Gysin map $i^! i_* \alpha$ equals the Gysin map of the zero-section $\iota : Y \hookrightarrow N$ applied to $\widetilde{\alpha}_\infty$.

It remains to compute $\iota^! \iota_* \alpha$ for the zero-section of a vector bundle $N$ of rank $d$ on $Y$. By the *Thom isomorphism* for Chow groups (a consequence of the homotopy property and the projective bundle formula),

$$
p^* : A_*(Y) \;\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}\; A_{*+d}(N)
$$

is an isomorphism, where $p : N \to Y$ is the projection. The class $\iota_* \alpha \in A_*(N)$ corresponds under $p^*$ to a unique class on $Y$; a computation in the projective bundle $\mathbb{P}(N \oplus \mathcal{O})$ shows that $\iota^* \iota_* \alpha = c_d(N) \cap \alpha$ (the *excess intersection formula* for the zero-section, where the excess bundle is $N$ itself).

Combining: the deformation reduces the general self-intersection $i^! i_*$ to the zero-section case, and the zero-section case is computed by the Thom isomorphism. Hence $i^! i_* \alpha = c_d(N_{Y/X}) \cap \alpha$ in $A_{*-d}(Y)$. $\square$

**Corollary (excess intersection formula).** In the fibre square of regular embeddings with excess bundle $E$ of rank $e$, $f^* i_* \alpha = i'_*(c_e(E) \cap g^* \alpha)$. The proof is the same deformation argument, with the normal cone $C_{Y'} X'$ replacing $N_{Y'/X'}$ and the Segre class $s(E)$ correcting the difference; the top Chern class $c_e(E)$ emerges from $s(E) c(E) = 1$.

## Connections [Master]

- **Sheaf cohomology [04.03.01]** — the cycle class map $A^*(X) \to H^{2*}(X; \mathbb{Z})$ sends the intersection product to the cup product in cohomology; for smooth complex projective varieties this is a ring homomorphism whose kernel and cokernel measure the gap between algebraic and topological cycles.

- **Weil divisor [04.05.01]** — the first Chow group $A^1(X)$ is the divisor class group $\mathrm{Cl}(X)$ on a smooth variety; intersection theory generalises the divisor-class picture to cycles of arbitrary codimension, with divisors as the codimension-one layer.

- **Hodge index theorem [04.05.09]** — the intersection form on a smooth projective surface has signature $(1, \rho - 1)$ on $\mathrm{NS}(X) \otimes \mathbb{R}$; this numerical consequence of the intersection product governs the geometry of surfaces and is the surface-level input to the minimal model program.

- **Hirzebruch-Riemann-Roch [04.05.10]** — the formula $\chi(X, \mathcal{F}) = \deg(\mathrm{ch}(\mathcal{F}) \cdot \mathrm{td}(T_X))$ evaluates Euler characteristics by computing intersection numbers in the Chow ring; HRR is the dimension-0 face of Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch.

- **Ample line bundle [04.05.05]** — the Nakai-Moishezon criterion for ampleness is phrased in terms of intersection numbers $(\mathcal{L}^r \cdot Y) > 0$, making ampleness a positivity condition on intersection-theoretic data.

- **Projective space [04.07.01]** — the Chow ring $A^*(\mathbb{P}^n) = \mathbb{Z}[H]/(H^{n+1})$ is the foundational computation from which Bézout's theorem and every projective enumerative count follow.

- **Birational geometry and the minimal model program [04.17.01]** — intersection numbers $(D \cdot C)$ of divisors against curves define the nef and ample cones, and the MMP is the classification of varieties by the position of the canonical class in this cone of intersection numbers.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The rigorous theory of intersection numbers on algebraic varieties is one of the harder-won achievements of 20th-century geometry, and it solved a problem the Italian school had handled brilliantly but without secure foundations. The classical geometers — Guido Castelnuovo, Federigo Enriques, Francesco Severi — computed intersection numbers of curves on surfaces fluently in the years around 1900, but their arguments depended on "moving" cycles through families whose existence was taken on geometric faith. The school collapsed in a series of counterexamples (Severi's claimed proof of the Hodge conjecture for divisors on surfaces, later shown to rely on unjustified moving arguments), and the field awaited a rigorous foundation.

The first rigorous foundation came from Wei-Liang Chow, who in "On the multiplication of algebraic cycles" [ref: TODO_REF Chow1956] introduced *rational equivalence* of algebraic cycles and proved the *moving lemma*: every cycle can be moved into general position within its rational-equivalence class, so the intersection product of rational-equivalence classes is well-defined. Chow's construction gave the ring $A^*(X)$ of algebraic cycles modulo rational equivalence, now called the *Chow ring*. Chow's 1956 paper, together with earlier work of W. Hodge and D. Pedoe [*Methods of Algebraic Geometry*, 1947–1954], placed the Italian-school intersection numbers on a secure algebraic footing.

The second revolution was Alexander Grothendieck's reformulation of the theory in the language of schemes, Chern classes, and $K$-theory, in the Éléments de Géométrie Algébrique (EGA) and the Séminaire de Géométrie Algébrique (SGA 6, 1971). Grothendieck recast the Chow ring as the target of a universal Chern character from $K_0(X)$, and conjectured the Riemann-Roch theorem in its full generality — proved by Armand Borel and Jean-Pierre Serre in 1958 and extended by Grothendieck to the Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch theorem, identifying $K$-theoretic and Chow-theoretic pushforwards up to the Todd class.

The third revolution, which produced the modern theory in its current form, was the work of William Fulton and Robert MacPherson in the late 1970s [ref: TODO_REF FultonMacPherson1978]. Their two innovations — *deformation to the normal cone* and the *excess intersection formula* — gave a refined Gysin map $i^!$ for *any* closed embedding, not only regular ones, and made intersection theory work in non-transverse situations and on singular varieties (via operational Chow groups). Fulton's book *Intersection Theory* [ref: TODO_REF Fulton1984] (1984, 2nd edition 1998) became the canonical reference, systematising the theory as an oriented Borel-Moore homology theory satisfying a short list of axioms.

The enumerative payoffs were immediate. The classical count of 3264 conics tangent to five given conics — first computed by Michel Chasles in 1864 and refined by Hieronymus Zeuthen — was re-derived in modern language by Fulton and MacPherson via the Chow ring of the space of complete conics. Eisenbud and Harris's *3264 and All That* [ref: TODO_REF EisenbudHarris2016] (2016) made the modern theory accessible to a generation of geometers, with the eponymous count as its capstone.

Philosophically, intersection theory is the algebraic counterpart of algebraic topology's cup product, and the *cycle class map* $A^*(X) \to H^{2*}(X; \mathbb{Z})$ is the bridge. The failure of this map to be an isomorphism — the existence of cohomology classes not represented by algebraic cycles — is the content of the *Hodge conjecture* and the *standard conjectures* of Grothendieck, which remain among the deepest open problems in mathematics. From this viewpoint intersection theory is not a computational tool but a window onto the relationship between algebra and topology: the Chow ring is the universal algebraic cohomology theory, and its comparison with singular, étale, and crystalline cohomology is the heart of modern algebraic geometry.
