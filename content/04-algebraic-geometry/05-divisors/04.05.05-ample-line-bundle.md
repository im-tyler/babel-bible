## Intuition [Beginner]

A line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ on a scheme $X$ is *very ample* if its global sections embed $X$ as a closed subscheme of projective space. It is *ample* if some tensor power $\mathcal{L}^n$ is very ample. Ample bundles are the *positively curved* line bundles of algebraic geometry: they witness the projectivity of $X$ and produce its embeddings.

Why does this matter? Projective embeddings are the foundational tool for studying varieties: a projective embedding gives a Hilbert polynomial, a degree, an arithmetic genus, and a host of cohomological invariants computable from the embedding. A variety is *projective* (in the sense of admitting a closed embedding into some projective space) iff it carries an ample line bundle. So ampleness encodes projectivity in line-bundle language.

The technical version is best understood via the *Cartan-Serre-Grothendieck criterion*: a line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ on a complete scheme is ample iff for every coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$, the higher cohomology of the twist $\mathcal{F}(n) := \mathcal{F}$-tensored-with-$\mathcal{L}^n$ vanishes (i.e., $H^i$ vanishes for $i \geq 1$ and $n$ large). Ampleness is thus a *cohomological positivity* condition. It generalises to nef, big, and effective cones in birational geometry, and is at the heart of the minimal model program.

## Worked example [Beginner]

On the projective line $\mathbb{P}^1_k$, the line bundle $\mathcal{O}(1)$ is very ample. Its global sections are the linear forms in two variables, spanned by $x_0, x_1$. The map

$$
\mathbb{P}^1_k \to \mathbb{P}^1_k, \quad [a : b] \mapsto [x_0(a, b) : x_1(a, b)] = [a : b]
$$

is the identity embedding — $\mathbb{P}^1_k$ embeds into itself by $\mathcal{O}(1)$.

The line bundle $\mathcal{O}(2)$ on $\mathbb{P}^1_k$ has global sections the homogeneous quadratics: $x_0^2, x_0 x_1, x_1^2$. These three sections give a map $\mathbb{P}^1_k \to \mathbb{P}^2_k$, $[a:b] \mapsto [a^2 : ab : b^2]$, the *Veronese embedding* of degree 2. Its image is the smooth conic $V(y_0 y_2 - y_1^2) \subset \mathbb{P}^2_k$.

For each $n \geq 1$, $\mathcal{O}(n)$ on $\mathbb{P}^1_k$ is very ample, embedding $\mathbb{P}^1_k$ as the *rational normal curve of degree $n$* in $\mathbb{P}^n_k$. The line bundle $\mathcal{O}(0) = \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1_k}$ is ample-but-not-very-ample? No: $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}$ is not even ample, since its powers all stay structure-sheaf and produce no embedding. Only $\mathcal{O}(d)$ for $d \geq 1$ is ample on $\mathbb{P}^1_k$; for $d \geq 1$ it is also very ample.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a scheme over a field $k$ (or more generally, over a Noetherian base $S$), and let $\mathcal{L}$ be a line bundle on $X$.

**Very ample.** $\mathcal{L}$ is *very ample* (over $k$) if there exists a closed immersion $\iota : X \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}^N_k$ for some $N$ such that $\iota^* \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^N}(1) \cong \mathcal{L}$. Equivalently, the natural map

$$
\varphi_{\mathcal{L}} : X \to \mathbb{P}(H^0(X; \mathcal{L})^\vee), \qquad x \mapsto [\text{evaluation of sections at } x]
$$

is a closed immersion. This requires that $\mathcal{L}$ is *globally generated* (the sections $H^0(X; \mathcal{L})$ have no common zero, equivalently $\varphi_{\mathcal{L}}$ is defined everywhere) and that $\varphi_{\mathcal{L}}$ separates points and tangent vectors.

**Ample.** $\mathcal{L}$ is *ample* if there exists a positive integer $n$ such that $\mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}$ is very ample. Equivalently, $\mathcal{L}$ is ample iff for every coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$, there exists $n_0$ depending on $\mathcal{F}$ such that $\mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}$ is globally generated for all $n \geq n_0$.

**Cartan-Serre-Grothendieck criterion.** Let $X$ be a Noetherian scheme proper over a Noetherian base ring $A$. A line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ on $X$ is ample iff one of the following equivalent conditions holds:

(A1) For every coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$, $\mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}$ is globally generated for $n \gg 0$.

(A2) For every coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$ and every $i \geq 1$, $H^i(X; \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}) = 0$ for $n \gg 0$.

(A3) Some tensor power $\mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}$ is very ample (over $A$).

This trio of conditions — global generation, cohomological vanishing, and projective embedding — is the heart of ampleness.

**Numerical (Nakai-Moishezon) criterion.** Let $X$ be a complete (proper over $k$) scheme. A line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ is ample iff for every irreducible closed subscheme $Y \subseteq X$ of dimension $r \geq 1$,

$$
(\mathcal{L}^r \cdot Y) := \int_Y c_1(\mathcal{L})^r > 0.
$$

(Intersection number positive on every positive-dimensional subvariety.)

This says ampleness is a *numerical* condition — it depends only on the class of $\mathcal{L}$ in $\mathrm{NS}(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, the rational Néron-Severi group.

**Globally generated.** $\mathcal{L}$ is *globally generated* (or *base-point-free*) if the evaluation map

$$
H^0(X; \mathcal{L}) \otimes_k \mathcal{O}_X \to \mathcal{L}
$$

is surjective. Equivalently, for every $x \in X$, there is a global section $s$ with $s(x) \neq 0$.

**Examples.**

1. *Projective space.* $\mathcal{O}(d)$ on $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ is ample iff $d > 0$. It is very ample iff $d \geq 1$ (Veronese embeddings).
2. *Affine schemes.* Affine schemes do not carry ample line bundles (they admit no closed embedding into projective space, since they are non-projective).
3. *Smooth projective curves.* On a curve $C$ of genus $g$, a line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ is ample iff $\deg \mathcal{L} > 0$. It is very ample iff $\deg \mathcal{L} \geq 2g + 1$ (Castelnuovo-Riemann-Roch criterion).
4. *Abelian varieties.* On an abelian variety $A$, an ample line bundle exists iff $A$ is projective (always true for abelian varieties over fields). The classical *theta divisor* on a Jacobian is the canonical ample bundle.
5. *Failure of ampleness.* On $\mathbb{P}^1_k \times \mathbb{P}^1_k$, the bundle $\mathcal{O}(1, 0)$ (pullback from one factor) has structure-sheaf restriction to the fibres of the other projection, so $(\mathcal{O}(1, 0)^2 \cdot \text{fibre}) = 0$, violating Nakai-Moishezon. It is not ample, only *nef* (numerically effective).

**Ample cone.** The set of ample classes in $\mathrm{NS}(X) \otimes \mathbb{R}$ forms an open convex cone, the *ample cone* $\mathrm{Amp}(X)$. Its closure is the *nef cone*, dual under the intersection pairing to the *Mori cone* of effective curve classes (Kleiman's criterion). The geometry of these cones drives the minimal model program.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Cartan-Serre-Grothendieck criterion for ampleness).** *Let $X$ be a Noetherian scheme proper over a Noetherian ring $A$, and let $\mathcal{L}$ be a line bundle on $X$. The following are equivalent.*

*(i) Some tensor power $\mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}$ is very ample (over $A$).*

*(ii) For every coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$, $\mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}$ is globally generated for $n \gg 0$.*

*(iii) For every coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$ and every $i \geq 1$, $H^i(X; \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}) = 0$ for $n \gg 0$.*

**Proof.** The key implications are (i) $\Rightarrow$ (iii) $\Rightarrow$ (ii) $\Rightarrow$ (i).

*(i) $\Rightarrow$ (iii)*: Suppose $\mathcal{L}^{\otimes m}$ is very ample, giving a closed immersion $\iota : X \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}^N_A$ with $\iota^* \mathcal{O}(1) = \mathcal{L}^{\otimes m}$. Serre's projective vanishing theorem on $\mathbb{P}^N_A$: for any coherent sheaf $\mathcal{G}$ on $\mathbb{P}^N_A$, $H^i(\mathbb{P}^N_A; \mathcal{G} \otimes \mathcal{O}(d)) = 0$ for $i \geq 1$ and $d \gg 0$. Apply this to $\mathcal{G} = \iota_* \mathcal{F}$ on $\mathbb{P}^N_A$:

$$
H^i(X; \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes md}) = H^i(\mathbb{P}^N_A; \iota_* \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{O}(d)) = 0
$$

for $d \gg 0$. Splitting tensor powers $\mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}$ as $\mathcal{L}^{\otimes md+r}$ with $0 \leq r < m$ and applying the bound to each shift covers all $n \gg 0$. So (iii) holds.

*(iii) $\Rightarrow$ (ii)*: Given $\mathcal{F}$ coherent, the global sections functor is exact for sheaves with $H^1 = 0$. For $n \gg 0$, $H^1(X; \mathfrak{m}_x \cdot \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}) = 0$ for every closed point $x \in X$ (where $\mathfrak{m}_x$ is the ideal sheaf), so the evaluation map

$$
H^0(X; \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}) \to \mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}|_x
$$

is surjective at $x$. Thus $\mathcal{F} \otimes \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}$ is globally generated near $x$. Noetherian compactness gives finitely many $x_i$ exhausting $X$; taking $n$ large enough for all gives (ii). (A finer argument using vanishing at higher orders and the openness of generation handles uniformity.)

*(ii) $\Rightarrow$ (i)*: Apply (ii) with $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{O}_X$. For $n \geq n_0$, $\mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}$ is globally generated. The map $\varphi_n : X \to \mathbb{P}(H^0(X; \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n})^\vee)$ is then defined everywhere. For larger $n$ — applying (ii) to $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{I}_x \otimes \mathcal{I}_y$ for pairs of distinct closed points and to $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{I}_x^2$ for tangent-vector separation — $\varphi_n$ separates points and tangent vectors, hence is a closed immersion. So $\mathcal{L}^{\otimes n}$ is very ample for $n$ sufficiently large. $\square$

This theorem is the foundational equivalence: ampleness equals projective embedding equals cohomological positivity equals eventual global generation. Each formulation is useful in different contexts; the Cartan-Serre-Grothendieck triad organises them.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Show that $\mathcal{O}(d)$ on $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ is very ample for $d \geq 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the global sections of $\mathcal{O}(d)$ — homogeneous polynomials of degree $d$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The global sections $H^0(\mathbb{P}^n_k; \mathcal{O}(d))$ form the space of homogeneous polynomials of degree $d$ in $x_0, \ldots, x_n$, of dimension $\binom{n+d}{n}$.

The induced map $\varphi_d : \mathbb{P}^n_k \to \mathbb{P}^N_k$ with $N = \binom{n+d}{n} - 1$ is given by all degree-$d$ monomials. This is the *$d$-th Veronese embedding*.

To verify it is a closed immersion: separation of points requires that for distinct $[a], [b] \in \mathbb{P}^n_k$, some monomial of degree $d$ takes different values — for $d \geq 1$ this is satisfied (the linear forms already separate, since two distinct points differ in at least one coordinate). Separation of tangent vectors requires injectivity on tangent spaces — also holds for $d \geq 1$.

The image is a closed subvariety of $\mathbb{P}^N_k$ cut out by the *Veronese relations*: the degree-2 polynomials in monomial-coordinates that vanish on the image, expressing relations like $y_{i+j} y_{k+l} = y_{i+k} y_{j+l}$ when $i + j = i + k$ and so on (the polynomial-ring relations).

For $d = 1$, the Veronese is the identity. For $d \geq 2$, the image is the *Veronese variety* of dimension $n$ and degree $d^n$ in $\mathbb{P}^N_k$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Prove that a line bundle on a smooth projective curve $C$ of genus $g$ is very ample if its degree is $\geq 2g + 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use Riemann-Roch and the criterion that very ample = separates points and tangent vectors.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\mathcal{L}$ be a line bundle on $C$ with $\deg \mathcal{L} = d$. By Riemann-Roch, $h^0(\mathcal{L}) - h^1(\mathcal{L}) = d - g + 1$, and Serre duality gives $h^1(\mathcal{L}) = h^0(\omega_C \otimes \mathcal{L}^{-1})$.

If $d \geq 2g + 1 > 2g - 2 = \deg \omega_C$, then $\deg(\omega_C \otimes \mathcal{L}^{-1}) = 2g - 2 - d < 0$, so $h^0(\omega_C \otimes \mathcal{L}^{-1}) = 0$. Hence $h^1(\mathcal{L}) = 0$ and $h^0(\mathcal{L}) = d - g + 1$.

*Separation of points.* For distinct points $p, q \in C$, the divisor $D = p + q$ has $h^0(\mathcal{L}(-D)) = h^0(\mathcal{L} \otimes \mathcal{O}(-p-q))$, with $\deg(\mathcal{L}(-D)) = d - 2 \geq 2g - 1$. By Riemann-Roch and the same argument, $h^0(\mathcal{L}(-D)) = d - 2 - g + 1 = d - g - 1$. So the codimension of sections vanishing at both $p, q$ inside all sections is $h^0(\mathcal{L}) - h^0(\mathcal{L}(-D)) = 2$, i.e., the evaluation map $H^0(\mathcal{L}) \to \mathcal{L}|_p \oplus \mathcal{L}|_q$ is surjective, separating $p$ and $q$.

*Separation of tangent vectors.* Similar: for $p \in C$, the divisor $2p$ has $h^0(\mathcal{L}(-2p)) = d - 2 - g + 1 = d - g - 1$, and the codimension is 2 again, giving surjectivity of $H^0(\mathcal{L}) \to \mathcal{L}|_p \oplus T_p^* C \otimes \mathcal{L}|_p$ — separating tangent vectors at $p$.

Both conditions hold for $d \geq 2g + 1$, so $\varphi_{\mathcal{L}} : C \to \mathbb{P}(H^0(\mathcal{L})^\vee)$ is a closed immersion: $\mathcal{L}$ is very ample. $\square$

For $g = 0$: $d \geq 1$ suffices (the rational normal curve in $\mathbb{P}^d$). For $g = 1$: $d \geq 3$ suffices; the elliptic-curve embedding by $\mathcal{O}(3p)$. For $g \geq 2$: the canonical bundle $\omega_C$ has degree $2g - 2$, but $\omega_C$ is not very ample for hyperelliptic curves (the canonical map is 2:1 onto a rational curve); for non-hyperelliptic $g \geq 3$, $\omega_C$ is very ample.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why does the Nakai-Moishezon criterion characterise ampleness numerically?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Ample classes form an open cone in the Néron-Severi group; the criterion gives the cone via inequalities.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Nakai-Moishezon criterion: on a complete scheme $X$, $\mathcal{L}$ is ample iff $(\mathcal{L}^r \cdot Y) > 0$ for every irreducible closed subscheme $Y$ of dimension $r \geq 1$.

This is *numerical* because the intersection number $(\mathcal{L}^r \cdot Y)$ depends only on the *numerical class* of $\mathcal{L}$ — the image of $\mathcal{L}$ in $\mathrm{NS}(X) := \mathrm{Pic}(X)/\equiv$ (numerical equivalence). Numerically equivalent line bundles have identical intersection numbers with every subvariety.

So ampleness factors through $\mathrm{NS}(X)$: it depends only on the class $[\mathcal{L}] \in \mathrm{NS}(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, not on the specific bundle.

Combined with Kleiman's criterion for *nef*ness ($\mathcal{L}$ nef iff $(\mathcal{L} \cdot C) \geq 0$ for every irreducible curve $C$), and the fact that ample = interior of nef, one obtains the *ample cone* in $\mathrm{NS}(X) \otimes \mathbb{R}$ as an open convex cone whose closure is the nef cone. The Mori cone of effective curve classes is dual.

This *cone of curves* picture is the foundation of the minimal model program: the geometry of $X$ is encoded in the structure of these convex cones in $\mathrm{NS}(X) \otimes \mathbb{R}$, and the cone theorem (Mori, Kawamata) classifies the extremal rays.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

State and outline a proof of the Kodaira embedding theorem: a compact Kähler manifold is projective iff it carries a positive line bundle.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Positive line bundle = ample, by Kodaira vanishing and Cartan-Serre-Grothendieck.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Kodaira embedding theorem.** A compact complex Kähler manifold $X$ admits a closed embedding into some $\mathbb{P}^N(\mathbb{C})$ iff there exists a holomorphic line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ on $X$ whose first Chern class $c_1(\mathcal{L}) \in H^{1,1}(X; \mathbb{R})$ is represented by a *positive* real $(1,1)$-form (Kähler form, equivalently $c_1(\mathcal{L})$ inside the Kähler cone).

**Outline.** A positive line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ has, by Kodaira's vanishing theorem,

$$
H^i(X; \mathcal{L}^n \otimes \omega_X) = 0 \quad \text{for } i \geq 1, n \gg 0
$$

(equivalently $H^i(X; \mathcal{L}^n) = 0$ for $i \geq 1$ and $n \geq 1$ when $\mathcal{L}$ is positive enough). This is the analytic version of the Cartan-Serre-Grothendieck cohomological criterion.

By the Cartan-Serre-Grothendieck criterion (in its analytic GAGA form), positivity of $\mathcal{L}$ implies that $\mathcal{L}^n$ is globally generated and separates points and tangent vectors for $n$ large. So $\mathcal{L}^n$ is *very ample*: the map $\varphi_n : X \to \mathbb{P}(H^0(X; \mathcal{L}^n)^\vee)$ is a closed analytic embedding.

Conversely, a projective embedding $\iota : X \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}^N$ pulls back the Fubini-Study form $\omega_{FS}$ — a positive $(1,1)$-form representing $c_1(\mathcal{O}(1))$ — to a positive form on $X$ representing $c_1(\iota^* \mathcal{O}(1))$. So $X$ has a positive line bundle.

This theorem links three different perspectives:

1. *Algebraic geometry*: ample line bundle.
2. *Differential geometry*: positive curvature on a holomorphic line bundle.
3. *Topology*: projective embedding (compactness + closed in $\mathbb{P}^N$).

Combined with GAGA (Serre 1956), Kodaira embedding shows: smooth compact Kähler manifolds with a positive line bundle = smooth complex projective varieties (analytically). The equivalence of the algebraic and analytic perspectives in this setting is one of the deepest results of mid-20th-century mathematics. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Explain the role of ample line bundles in the minimal model program.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The MMP runs flips and divisorial contractions until the canonical bundle becomes nef.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The *minimal model program* (MMP, also Mori program) seeks to classify higher-dimensional algebraic varieties up to birational equivalence. The key player is the *canonical bundle* $K_X = \omega_X$ (or its $\mathbb{Q}$-Cartier extension on mildly singular varieties).

A variety is a *minimal model* if $K_X$ is *nef* (numerically effective: $(K_X \cdot C) \geq 0$ for every irreducible curve $C$). It is a *Mori fibre space* if $K_X$ is anti-ample on the fibres of a structure morphism.

**The MMP procedure.** Start with a smooth projective $X$. If $K_X$ is nef, $X$ is a minimal model and stop. If not, by the *cone theorem* (Mori-Kawamata) there is a $K_X$-negative extremal ray $R$ in the Mori cone $\overline{\mathrm{NE}}(X)$, and an associated *contraction morphism* $\varphi_R : X \to Y$. Three cases:

- *Divisorial contraction*: $\varphi_R$ contracts a divisor; replace $X$ by $Y$.
- *Small contraction*: $\varphi_R$ contracts a subvariety of codimension $\geq 2$; replace $X$ by a *flip* $X^+ \to Y$ (existence of flips is the BCHM theorem, 2010, by Birkar-Cascini-Hacon-McKernan).
- *Mori fibre space*: $\dim Y < \dim X$; stop with the Mori fibre space $X \to Y$.

Iterate. Termination is conjectural in general (proved up to dimension 4) but the existence of flips (BCHM) and abundance (in low dimension) are major theorems.

**Role of ample bundles.** Ample classes are extremal points of the Kähler / nef cone. The MMP modifies $X$ to push the canonical class $K_X$ towards nef-ness, equivalently *out of* the anti-ample directions. The cone theorem identifies $K_X$-negative extremal rays as exactly the directions to contract; ampleness of an auxiliary divisor $H + \epsilon K_X$ (for $H$ ample, $\epsilon > 0$ small) is what guarantees the contraction exists and is projective.

In summary: ampleness controls the projective and cohomological behaviour of the MMP, the cone of effective curves provides the dual data, and the canonical bundle is the central object whose positivity is the goal.

This perspective — varieties classified up to birational equivalence by the position of $K_X$ in the cone of divisors — is the major conceptual achievement of late-20th-century algebraic geometry and remains under active development (in characteristic $p$, in the relative setting, for moduli of higher-dimensional varieties).

## Advanced results [Master]

**Kodaira embedding theorem.** A compact Kähler manifold $X$ is projective iff it carries a holomorphic line bundle whose first Chern class is positive. The proof uses Kodaira's vanishing theorem: a positive line bundle has all higher cohomology of $\mathcal{L}^n \otimes \omega_X$ vanishing for $n \gg 0$. By Cartan-Serre-Grothendieck (in its analytic form), this forces eventual very ampleness.

**Kleiman's criterion.** A line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ on a complete scheme is *nef* (numerically effective) iff $(\mathcal{L} \cdot C) \geq 0$ for every irreducible curve $C \subseteq X$. The nef cone is the closure of the ample cone. Together they form the foundational pair of cones in $\mathrm{NS}(X) \otimes \mathbb{R}$.

**Seshadri's criterion.** $\mathcal{L}$ on a complete scheme is ample iff there exists $\epsilon > 0$ such that for every closed point $x \in X$ and every irreducible curve $C$ through $x$, $(\mathcal{L} \cdot C) \geq \epsilon \cdot \mathrm{mult}_x(C)$. The supremum of such $\epsilon$ is the *Seshadri constant* $\epsilon(\mathcal{L}, x)$, a refinement of ampleness measuring local positivity. Seshadri constants are major invariants in modern positivity theory (Lazarsfeld *Positivity*).

**Big and effective cones.** Beyond ample/nef: a line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ is *big* if $h^0(\mathcal{L}^n) \sim c \cdot n^{\dim X}$ for $c > 0$. The big cone is open in $\mathrm{NS}(X) \otimes \mathbb{R}$, and it contains the ample cone. The *effective cone* of classes admitting nonzero sections is even larger but typically not closed.

**Iitaka dimension.** For a line bundle $\mathcal{L}$, the Iitaka dimension $\kappa(\mathcal{L}) := \limsup_{n \to \infty} \log h^0(\mathcal{L}^n) / \log n$ takes values in $\{-\infty, 0, 1, \ldots, \dim X\}$. $\mathcal{L}$ ample $\Rightarrow \kappa = \dim X$ (maximal); $\mathcal{L}$ big $\Leftrightarrow \kappa = \dim X$.

**Castelnuovo-Mumford regularity.** A coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $\mathbb{P}^N_k$ is *$m$-regular* if $H^i(\mathcal{F}(m - i)) = 0$ for $i \geq 1$. Regularity bounds when $\mathcal{F}(d)$ becomes globally generated and has higher cohomology vanishing — a quantitative refinement of ampleness statements. Regularity controls Hilbert-scheme constructions and resolution-of-singularities estimates.

**Effective ampleness on abelian varieties.** Lefschetz's theorem: on an abelian variety $A$, a line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ is *very ample* iff $\mathcal{L}^{\otimes 3}$ has the property that $|\mathcal{L}^3|$ separates points and tangent vectors. The *Lefschetz-Mukai* criterion and Mumford's index theorem on theta divisors give effective bounds.

**Rationality and ampleness in characteristic $p$.** In positive characteristic, Kodaira vanishing can fail (Raynaud counterexamples), so Kodaira embedding requires modification. The *Mehta-Ramanathan theorem* and *Frobenius-positivity* substitutes (Hara, Smith, Schwede) provide replacement positivity criteria.

**Asymptotic invariants.** Volumes of line bundles $\mathrm{vol}(\mathcal{L}) := \limsup n^{-\dim X} \cdot n! \cdot h^0(\mathcal{L}^n)$ are continuous invariants on $\mathrm{NS}(X) \otimes \mathbb{R}$ (Boucksom-Demailly-Paun-Peternell), giving a real-analytic refinement of ampleness.

**Synthesis.** An ample line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ on a scheme $X$ is one whose high tensor powers define embeddings into projective space, so the geometry of $X$ can be recovered from the graded ring $\bigoplus_{n \geq 0} H^0(X, \mathcal{L}^{\otimes n})$. Ampleness is the algebraic-geometric substitute for positive curvature: the Kodaira embedding theorem identifies ample line bundles on complex manifolds with those admitting positively curved Hermitian metrics, and the Nakai-Moishezon criterion translates ampleness into a numerical condition on intersection numbers with subvarieties. Through ampleness, projective geometry, cohomology vanishing (Kodaira, Serre), and the minimal model programme all converge on the question of which varieties admit projective embeddings.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The notion of ampleness emerged from the post-war French school's reformulation of projective geometry in sheaf-theoretic language. Jean-Pierre Serre's 1955 *Faisceaux Algébriques Cohérents* (FAC) introduced the *twisting sheaves* $\mathcal{O}(d)$ on $\mathbb{P}^n$ and proved the foundational vanishing theorem: for any coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $\mathbb{P}^n$, $H^i(\mathbb{P}^n; \mathcal{F}(d)) = 0$ for $i \geq 1$ and $d \gg 0$. This was the prototypical example of cohomological positivity, and it implied that $\mathcal{F}(d)$ is globally generated for $d \gg 0$. Serre's twisting sheaves *were* the first ample line bundles, and FAC implicitly identified the cohomological-positivity property that defines ampleness.

Alexander Grothendieck and Jean Dieudonné, in *Éléments de Géométrie Algébrique II* (EGA II, 1961), developed the systematic theory of ample and very ample line bundles in scheme theory. EGA II §4 gives the formal definitions; §5 proves the Cartan-Serre-Grothendieck equivalence (often credited to Grothendieck, generalising Serre's projective-space case). Grothendieck's approach was characteristically functorial: ampleness is a property of a relative line bundle that controls the projectivity of a morphism, and the equivalence with cohomological vanishing is the natural way to articulate it within his sheaf-theoretic framework. EGA II also introduced the notion of *ampleness relative to a base* — a line bundle $\mathcal{L}$ on $X \to S$ ample relative to $S$ — which is the right notion in moduli theory and arithmetic geometry.

Yozo Nakai (1963) and Boris Moishezon (1964) gave the numerical criterion: ampleness is detected by intersection numbers on subvarieties. The Nakai-Moishezon theorem reduced ampleness to a geometric inequality — a positivity condition on the *class* of the line bundle in the Néron-Severi group — and connected ampleness to the nascent theory of cones of divisors and curves.

Kunihiko Kodaira's 1953 vanishing theorem and the resulting *Kodaira embedding theorem* (also 1953) established the analytic counterpart: a compact Kähler manifold is projective iff it has a positive holomorphic line bundle. Kodaira's proof used differential-geometric methods (the Bochner technique on harmonic forms) and provided the bridge between algebraic and analytic perspectives via GAGA (Serre 1956). The Kodaira embedding theorem remains one of the deepest theorems in 20th-century complex analysis and algebraic geometry.

In contemporary research, ample line bundles are central to the *minimal model program* (Mori, Kawamata, Reid, Shokurov; modern work of Birkar-Cascini-Hacon-McKernan, Birkar 2016 BCHM-style finiteness). The ample, nef, big, and effective cones organise the geometry of higher-dimensional varieties. The *Birkar-Cascini-Hacon-McKernan* theorem (2010) — existence of minimal models / finite generation of canonical rings for varieties of general type — relies on systematic positivity arguments rooted in the theory developed in EGA II and Lazarsfeld's *Positivity in Algebraic Geometry*. Ampleness in arithmetic (height theory, Arakelov geometry, $p$-adic positivity) and in derived algebraic geometry (Lurie) extends the framework to richer settings. Robert Lazarsfeld's two-volume *Positivity in Algebraic Geometry* (2004) is the modern canonical reference and remains under active extension.
