## Intuition [Beginner]

The Hilbert-Mumford numerical criterion is the computable test for stability in geometric invariant theory. The setup: a reductive group $G$ acts on a projective variety $X$, and we want to know which points of $X$ behave well enough to survive into the quotient $X /\!/ G$. Mumford's framework labels each point of $X$ as stable, semistable, or unstable; the numerical criterion turns this distinction, which is defined in terms of orbit closures and invariant sections, into a one-parameter calculation.

Here is the picture. A *one-parameter subgroup* of $G$ is a map $\lambda$ from the multiplicative group $\mathbb{G}_m$ — the nonzero scalars under multiplication — into $G$. Sending the scalar to zero, the path $\lambda(t) \cdot x$ traces a curve in $X$, and its limit point $x_0$ is where the action of $\lambda$ pulls $x$ as $t$ shrinks. The numerical criterion says: $x$ is unstable if and only if there is some one-parameter subgroup whose limit $x_0$ is a point where the chosen linearisation is positively weighted. Stability is the opposite: the limit point is never positively weighted, no matter which one-parameter subgroup we try.

The one-sentence takeaway: stability is detected by walking out along all one-parameter subgroups and watching where the point goes, plus the weight that the line bundle picks up on the limit.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The classical example: the group $\mathrm{SL}_2$ acts on the space of binary quartic forms — homogeneous polynomials of degree 4 in two variables. A binary quartic is $f(x, y) = a_0 x^4 + a_1 x^3 y + a_2 x^2 y^2 + a_3 x y^3 + a_4 y^4$. The diagonal one-parameter subgroup of $\mathrm{SL}_2$ is the scaling $\lambda(t)$ sending $x$ to $t \cdot x$ and $y$ to $t^{-1} \cdot y$, with $t$ a nonzero scalar.

Step 1. Write the action of $\lambda(t)$ on each monomial. Under $\lambda(t)$, the monomial $x^{4 - i} y^i$ is sent to $t^{4 - i} \cdot t^{-i} \cdot x^{4 - i} y^i = t^{4 - 2i} \cdot x^{4 - i} y^i$. So the monomial $x^{4 - i} y^i$ carries weight $4 - 2i$ under the action of $\lambda$. The list of monomial weights, as $i$ ranges from 0 to 4, is $4, 2, 0, -2, -4$.

Step 2. The limit point. Let $f$ be a binary quartic. As $t$ approaches zero, $\lambda(t) \cdot f$ has each monomial $a_i \cdot x^{4 - i} y^i$ multiplied by $t^{4 - 2i}$. The dominant term as $t$ shrinks is the one with the smallest exponent in $t$. If $a_0$ is nonzero, the dominant term is $a_0 \cdot x^4$ — the monomial of weight 4 — and the limit point is $a_0 \cdot x^4$, with weight 4 on the line-bundle fibre.

Step 3. Diagnose stability. The minimum weight in the expansion of $f$ is the smallest $4 - 2i$ such that $a_i$ is nonzero. The Hilbert-Mumford function $\mu(f, \lambda)$ is the negation of this minimum weight. If $a_0 = a_1 = a_2 = 0$ but $a_3$ is nonzero, the minimum weight is $-2$, so $\mu = 2 > 0$, and $f$ is destabilised by $\lambda$. The geometric content: $a_0 = a_1 = a_2 = 0$ means $f = a_3 x y^3 + a_4 y^4$ has the root $[0 : 1]$ with multiplicity at least 3. Varying $\lambda$ over all $\mathrm{SL}_2$-conjugates corresponds to choosing a different root, so a binary quartic is unstable when some root has multiplicity at least 3.

What this tells us: a binary form of degree $d$ is unstable exactly when one of its roots has multiplicity strictly greater than $d / 2$ — Hilbert's nineteenth-century theorem, recovered from the modern numerical criterion.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $G$ be a reductive algebraic group over an algebraically closed field $k$ acting on a projective variety $X \subset \mathbb{P}^N$ with a $G$-linearised ample line bundle $L = \mathcal{O}(1)|_X$. The data of a $G$-linearised line bundle includes a lift of the $G$-action on $X$ to the total space of $L$ compatible with scalar multiplication on the fibres.

**One-parameter subgroup.** A *one-parameter subgroup* of $G$ is a homomorphism of algebraic groups $\lambda : \mathbb{G}_m \to G$. Equivalently, $\lambda$ is a $k^\times$-action on each finite-dimensional rational $G$-representation $V$ through the chosen homomorphism. The set of one-parameter subgroups is denoted $X_*(G)$. For a torus $T \subset G$, $X_*(T) \cong \mathbb{Z}^r$ as a free abelian group of rank equal to the rank of $T$.

**The limit point.** Let $\lambda : \mathbb{G}_m \to G$ be a one-parameter subgroup and $x \in X$. The orbit map $\mathbb{G}_m \to X$ sending $t \mapsto \lambda(t) \cdot x$ extends uniquely to a morphism $\mathbb{A}^1 \to X$ when $X$ is projective. Set

$$
x_0 := \lim_{t \to 0} \lambda(t) \cdot x \in X.
$$

This limit exists and is the image of $t = 0$ under the extended morphism. The point $x_0$ is a fixed point of $\lambda$: that is, $\lambda(t) \cdot x_0 = x_0$ for all $t$.

**The Hilbert-Mumford weight.** The fibre $L_{x_0}$ of the line bundle at the fixed point $x_0$ is a one-dimensional $\mathbb{G}_m$-representation through $\lambda$; let $\mu^L(x, \lambda)$ denote the weight of this representation. Explicitly, $\lambda(t)$ acts on the fibre $L_{x_0}$ by multiplication by $t^{-\mu^L(x, \lambda)}$ — the sign convention is Mumford's: positive $\mu$ corresponds to instability.

Equivalent computation via affine cones. Choose a lift $\tilde{x} \in \mathbb{A}^{N+1} \setminus \{0\}$ of $x \in \mathbb{P}^N$, and let $\mathbb{G}_m$ act on $\mathbb{A}^{N+1}$ through $\lambda$. The action diagonalises with weights $m_0, m_1, \ldots, m_N$ on a chosen weight-eigenbasis. Decompose $\tilde{x} = \sum_i \tilde{x}_i$ with $\tilde{x}_i$ in the $m_i$-weight piece. Then

$$
\mu^L(x, \lambda) = - \min \{ m_i : \tilde{x}_i \neq 0 \}.
$$

**Definition.** Let $X \subset \mathbb{P}^N$ be a projective variety carrying a linearised reductive $G$-action with linearisation $L$. A point $x \in X$ is:

- *semistable* with respect to $L$ if $\mu^L(x, \lambda) \geq 0$ for every one-parameter subgroup $\lambda : \mathbb{G}_m \to G$;
- *stable* if $\mu^L(x, \lambda) > 0$ for every $\lambda$ that is not contained in the stabiliser of $x$, and additionally the orbit $G \cdot x$ is closed in the semistable locus $X^{ss}$;
- *unstable* if some $\lambda$ has $\mu^L(x, \lambda) < 0$.

The set of semistable points is denoted $X^{ss}(L)$, the stable points $X^s(L)$, and the unstable locus $X^{us}(L) = X \setminus X^{ss}(L)$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Stability is not closed-orbit alone.* A closed orbit with infinite stabiliser is semistable but not stable. The stability definition combines closed-orbit with finite-stabiliser.
- *The sign convention varies.* Some authors define $\mu$ with the opposite sign so that semistability is "$\mu^L \leq 0$." We follow Mumford-Fogarty-Kirwan §2.1: positive $\mu$ destabilises.
- *Reductivity is essential.* For a non-reductive $G$, the invariant ring need not be finitely generated (Nagata 1959), and the numerical criterion as stated does not characterise GIT semistability. Modern non-reductive GIT requires additional structure.
- *One-parameter subgroups over a single point.* The limit $\lim_{t \to 0} \lambda(t) \cdot x$ is a fixed point of $\lambda$, but not necessarily a fixed point of all of $G$. Different one-parameter subgroups generally limit to different points.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Hilbert-Mumford numerical criterion, Mumford 1965).** *Let $G$ be a reductive algebraic group acting on a projective variety $X$ over an algebraically closed field $k$, with $L$ a $G$-linearised ample line bundle. A point $x \in X$ is GIT-semistable with respect to $L$ if and only if $\mu^L(x, \lambda) \geq 0$ for every one-parameter subgroup $\lambda : \mathbb{G}_m \to G$. The point $x$ is GIT-stable if and only if $\mu^L(x, \lambda) > 0$ for every one-parameter subgroup $\lambda$ not in the identity component of the stabiliser of $x$.*

*Proof.* The strategy: reduce from arbitrary reductive $G$ to a maximal torus $T \subset G$, where the action diagonalises and stability becomes a question about convex hulls of weight vectors. The reduction is the harder direction; we first dispatch the easier converse.

*Step 1 — semistability implies the numerical inequality.* Suppose $x$ is GIT-semistable: there exists a $G$-invariant section $s \in H^0(X; L^{\otimes n})^G$ for some $n \geq 1$ with $s(x) \neq 0$. Choose a one-parameter subgroup $\lambda$ and form the limit $x_0 = \lim_{t \to 0} \lambda(t) \cdot x$. Since $\lambda(t) \cdot x \to x_0$ in $X$ and $s$ is $G$-invariant, $s(\lambda(t) \cdot x) = s(x)$ for all $t \neq 0$. Continuity (or properness of $X$) gives $s(x_0) = s(x) \neq 0$. The section $s$ is $\lambda$-invariant on the line bundle $L^{\otimes n}$, so the fibre $L^{\otimes n}|_{x_0}$ carries the zero $\lambda$-weight: $n \cdot \mu^L(x, \lambda) = \mu^{L^{\otimes n}}(x, \lambda) = 0$, hence $\mu^L(x, \lambda) \geq 0$. The inequality is in fact equality for the specific $\lambda$ stabilising $s$ but the proof gives $\geq 0$ uniformly across all one-parameter subgroups by varying $s$.

*Step 2 — reduction to a maximal torus.* Suppose $\mu^L(x, \lambda) \geq 0$ for every one-parameter subgroup. We claim $x$ is GIT-semistable. The key observation: every one-parameter subgroup of $G$ is conjugate, by an element $g \in G$, into a fixed maximal torus $T \subset G$. So it suffices to prove that, after replacing $x$ with $g \cdot x$ for any $g$, the numerical inequality $\mu^L(g \cdot x, \lambda_T) \geq 0$ for every $\lambda_T \in X_*(T)$ implies the existence of a $G$-invariant section not vanishing at $g \cdot x$. By $G$-equivariance, finding such a section at $g \cdot x$ is the same as finding one at $x$.

*Step 3 — diagonalising the torus action.* Restrict the action of $T$ on a chosen $G$-equivariant projective embedding $X \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}(V)$, with $V$ a finite-dimensional $G$-representation. Decompose $V = \bigoplus_{\chi \in X^*(T)} V_\chi$ into $T$-weight spaces, with $X^*(T)$ the character lattice of $T$. Lift $x \in X$ to $\tilde{x} \in V \setminus \{0\}$ and write $\tilde{x} = \sum_\chi \tilde{x}_\chi$. The set

$$
\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x}) := \{ \chi \in X^*(T) : \tilde{x}_\chi \neq 0 \}
$$

is the *weight polytope* of $\tilde{x}$ at $T$; its convex hull in $X^*(T) \otimes \mathbb{R}$ encodes the $T$-stability behaviour.

*Step 4 — the convex-hull lemma.* The numerical inequality $\mu^L(x, \lambda_T) \geq 0$ for every $\lambda_T \in X_*(T)$ is equivalent to the convex hull of $\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x})$ in $X^*(T) \otimes \mathbb{R}$ containing the origin $0$. This is a standard convex-geometry statement: a finite point set in a real vector space has the origin in its convex hull if and only if no linear functional on the space is strictly negative on the entire set. Here, one-parameter subgroups $\lambda_T \in X_*(T)$ pair with characters $\chi \in X^*(T)$ via the natural integer pairing, and $\mu^L(\tilde{x}, \lambda_T) = - \min_\chi \langle \chi, \lambda_T \rangle$ over the support of $\tilde{x}$. The numerical inequality becomes "no $\lambda_T$ has $\langle \chi, \lambda_T \rangle < 0$ on the entire support," equivalent to $0 \in \mathrm{conv}(\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x}))$.

*Step 5 — invariant sections from a $T$-invariant.* Suppose $0$ is in the convex hull of $\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x})$. Then $0$ is a positive rational combination $\sum_\chi c_\chi \cdot \chi$ with $c_\chi \geq 0$ and $\sum c_\chi > 0$. Clearing denominators, there exist integers $n_\chi \geq 0$, not all zero, with $\sum n_\chi \cdot \chi = 0$. The product $\prod_\chi \tilde{x}_\chi^{n_\chi}$ is a $T$-invariant element of $\mathrm{Sym}^{n}(V)$ with $n = \sum n_\chi$. By construction it does not vanish at $\tilde{x}$ — it is the explicit monomial whose exponents we just chose.

*Step 6 — $G$-equivariant averaging.* We now construct a $G$-invariant section out of the $T$-invariant. In characteristic zero, the Reynolds operator $R : \mathrm{Sym}^n(V)^* \to \mathrm{Sym}^n(V)^{*G}$ — the $G$-equivariant projection onto the $G$-invariants — averages any $T$-invariant function over the cosets $G/T$ to produce a $G$-invariant function. The output $R(s)$ is $G$-invariant and, by careful normalisation, does not vanish at $\tilde{x}$ provided the original $s$ did not. (Reynolds-operator argument: write $R$ as the projection associated to the unique $G$-invariant probability measure on $G/T$ in characteristic zero; in arbitrary characteristic Mumford uses *good filtrations* with the same conclusion.) The resulting $R(s) \in \mathrm{Sym}^n(V)^{*G}$ corresponds to a $G$-invariant section of $L^{\otimes n}$ not vanishing at $x$, proving $x$ is GIT-semistable.

*Step 7 — stability from strict inequality.* The stable case adapts the same argument with strict inequalities: $\mu^L(x, \lambda_T) > 0$ for every $\lambda_T \notin \mathrm{Stab}(x)^0$ translates, via Step 4, into the strict containment $0 \in \mathrm{int}(\mathrm{conv}(\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x})))$. The strict containment guarantees a separating $G$-invariant section that not only does not vanish at $x$ but also has $x$ in the open semistable locus where the orbit is closed and the stabiliser is finite. The orbit-closedness follows from the strict-positivity argument applied to all conjugates of $\lambda$; the finite-stabiliser condition is a separate input on the dimension of $\mathrm{Stab}(x)$.

The full proof, with the detailed handling of arbitrary characteristic via good filtrations and the convex-geometric formulation of stable versus semistable, occupies Mumford-Fogarty-Kirwan *GIT* (3rd ed.) §2.1, Theorem 2.1. The Kempf 1978 refinement provides, for an unstable point, the *optimal destabilising one-parameter subgroup* — the one minimising $\mu^L / \| \lambda \|$ for a chosen norm on $X_*(T)$, with this $\lambda$ unique up to $\mathrm{Stab}(x)$-conjugacy. $\square$

**Bridge.** The proof identifies GIT semistability with a convex-geometric containment statement on the weight polytope. The bridge is from algebraic (existence of an invariant section) to combinatorial (origin in a convex hull), and this is exactly the architectural pattern that generalises to symplectic reduction via the moment map. The convex-hull formulation builds toward [04.10.04] Kempf-Ness, where the same picture is restated as moment-map vanishing, and appears again in [03.09.08] symplectic toric varieties, where the Delzant polytope encodes the entire variety from its torus weight data. The central insight is that for reductive group actions on projective varieties, every analytic question about stability reduces to a finite list of linear inequalities in the character lattice, and the numerical criterion is the dictionary translating between the two languages.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the Hilbert-Mumford function $\mu^L$ satisfies $\mu^L(x, \lambda^n) = n \cdot \mu^L(x, \lambda)$ for positive integers $n$, and that it is invariant under the $G$-conjugation $\lambda \mapsto g \lambda g^{-1}$: that is, $\mu^L(x, g \lambda g^{-1}) = \mu^L(g^{-1} x, \lambda)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The first identity uses $L^{\otimes n}$ scaling on the line bundle. The second uses $G$-equivariance of the linearisation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Power identity.* The one-parameter subgroup $\lambda^n : \mathbb{G}_m \to G$ sends $t$ to $\lambda(t^n)$. Acting on the line bundle $L$ at the limit point $x_0 = \lim_{t \to 0} \lambda^n(t) \cdot x$ — which equals $\lim_{t \to 0} \lambda(t) \cdot x$ since both paths terminate at the same fixed point — the action is $\lambda^n(t) \cdot \xi = \lambda(t^n) \cdot \xi$, with weight $\mu^L(x, \lambda^n) = n \cdot \mu^L(x, \lambda)$ by the scaling rule for $\mathbb{G}_m$-weights.

*Conjugation identity.* Let $\lambda' := g \lambda g^{-1}$. The limit point of $\lambda'$ acting on $x$ is

$$
\lim_{t \to 0} \lambda'(t) \cdot x = \lim_{t \to 0} g \cdot \lambda(t) \cdot g^{-1} \cdot x = g \cdot \lim_{t \to 0} \lambda(t) \cdot (g^{-1} x),
$$

since $g$ acts continuously. Set $y = g^{-1} x$ and $y_0 = \lim_{t \to 0} \lambda(t) \cdot y$. Then the limit of $\lambda'$ on $x$ is $g \cdot y_0$. The weight of $\lambda'$ on the fibre $L_{g \cdot y_0}$ is the weight of $\lambda$ on $L_{y_0}$ — by $G$-equivariance of the linearisation, the two fibres are isomorphic as $\lambda$-representations after transport by $g$. Thus $\mu^L(x, g \lambda g^{-1}) = \mu^L(g^{-1} x, \lambda)$ as claimed.

The two identities together establish that $\mu^L$ is a rational homogeneous function on the cone of one-parameter subgroups, equivariant under $G$-conjugation. These are the inputs Kempf needs to define the optimal destabilising one-parameter subgroup of an unstable point. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Prove that on the affine line $\mathbb{A}^1$ with the $\mathbb{G}_m$-action $t \cdot x = t \cdot x$, the origin is the unique unstable point and every nonzero point is stable. Compute the Hilbert-Mumford function explicitly.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The single relevant one-parameter subgroup is the identity $\lambda(t) = t$ itself; compute its limit and weight at each point.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The group $\mathbb{G}_m$ acts on $\mathbb{A}^1$ via $t \cdot x = t \cdot x$. The only one-parameter subgroups of $\mathbb{G}_m$ are $\lambda_n(t) = t^n$ for $n \in \mathbb{Z}$; the "positive" one-parameter subgroups (those that limit to the origin) are $\lambda_n$ for $n \geq 1$.

Take the standard linearisation: $L = \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{A}^1}$ trivialised, with $\mathbb{G}_m$ acting on the fibre by $t$ (the *standard* lift).

*At the origin $x = 0$.* The point is already fixed by $\mathbb{G}_m$, so the limit is itself: $x_0 = 0$. The weight of $\lambda_1(t) = t$ on the fibre $L_0$ is $1$, so $\mu^L(0, \lambda_1) = -1 < 0$. Hence the origin is unstable.

*At a nonzero point $x = c \neq 0$.* The orbit map $t \mapsto t \cdot c$ extends to $\mathbb{A}^1 \to \mathbb{A}^1$ sending $0$ to $0$. So the limit is $x_0 = 0$. The weight of $\lambda_1$ on $L_0$ is $1$, but the trick is the lift: the lift of $c$ to the total space of $L$ is the element $c \in L_c$, mapped under the $\lambda$-extended action to $\lim_{t \to 0} t \cdot t \cdot c = \lim t^2 c = 0$. Equivalently, on the affine cone $\mathbb{A}^1 \setminus \{0\} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{A}^1$ with weight-$1$ action, the weight of $c$ itself under $\lambda$ is $1$, so $\mu^L(c, \lambda_1) = -1 \cdot 1 = -1 < 0$ in the unrestricted convention; with the projective adjustment the relevant statement is that the *projectivisation* $\mathbb{P}^0 = \mathrm{pt}$ has the unique point semistable.

The cleaner statement: with the linearisation $L = \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{A}^1}(1)$ (treating $\mathbb{A}^1$ as the affine cone over $\mathrm{Spec}\, k$), the semistable locus is $\mathbb{A}^1 \setminus \{0\}$, the unstable locus is the origin, and the GIT quotient $\mathbb{A}^1 /\!/ \mathbb{G}_m$ is a single point — every nonzero scalar is equivalent under $\mathbb{G}_m$, so the quotient identifies them. $\square$

</details>

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).**

Show that the weight $\mu^L(x, \lambda)$ depends $\mathbb{Z}$-linearly on the line bundle $L$: if $L_1, L_2$ are two $G$-linearised line bundles with weights $\mu_1, \mu_2$ at the limit, then $\mu^{L_1 \otimes L_2}(x, \lambda) = \mu_1 + \mu_2$.

Tensor product of two one-dimensional $\mathbb{G}_m$-representations has weight the sum of the individual weights.

Let $x_0 = \lim_{t \to 0} \lambda(t) \cdot x$; the limit is the same for any linearisation (it lives in $X$, not in $L$). The fibres $L_1|_{x_0}$ and $L_2|_{x_0}$ are one-dimensional representations of $\mathbb{G}_m$ via $\lambda$, with weights $\mu^{L_1}(x, \lambda)$ and $\mu^{L_2}(x, \lambda)$ respectively (under Mumford's sign convention, $\lambda(t)$ acts by $t^{-\mu^L}$).

The tensor product line bundle $L_1 \otimes L_2$ has fibre $L_1|_{x_0} \otimes L_2|_{x_0}$. The $\mathbb{G}_m$-action on a tensor product of one-dimensional representations is the tensor product of the actions, so $\lambda(t) \cdot (v_1 \otimes v_2) = (\lambda(t) v_1) \otimes (\lambda(t) v_2) = t^{-\mu_1} v_1 \otimes t^{-\mu_2} v_2 = t^{-\mu_1 - \mu_2} (v_1 \otimes v_2)$. **Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

State and prove the convex-hull lemma at the heart of the proof of the numerical criterion: for a torus $T$ acting on a vector $\tilde{x} \in V = \bigoplus_\chi V_\chi$ with weight support $\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x})$, the numerical inequality $\mu^L(\tilde{x}, \lambda_T) \geq 0$ for every $\lambda_T \in X_*(T)$ is equivalent to $0 \in \mathrm{conv}(\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x}))$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use Farkas's lemma or the hyperplane separation theorem for convex sets.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Setup.* Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional $T$-representation with weight decomposition $V = \bigoplus_{\chi \in X^*(T)} V_\chi$. Write $\tilde{x} = \sum_\chi \tilde{x}_\chi$ and let $S = \mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x}) = \{\chi : \tilde{x}_\chi \neq 0\}$. For $\lambda_T \in X_*(T)$, the limit of $\lambda_T(t) \cdot \tilde{x}$ as $t \to 0$ keeps only the terms with $\langle \chi, \lambda_T \rangle = \min_{\chi' \in S} \langle \chi', \lambda_T \rangle$, with the convention that $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ is the natural pairing $X^*(T) \times X_*(T) \to \mathbb{Z}$. The Hilbert-Mumford weight is $\mu^L(\tilde{x}, \lambda_T) = -\min_{\chi \in S} \langle \chi, \lambda_T \rangle$.

*Forward direction.* Suppose $0 \in \mathrm{conv}(S)$. Then $0 = \sum_\chi c_\chi \chi$ for some non-negative reals $c_\chi$ with $\sum c_\chi = 1$ and the sum is over $\chi \in S$. For any $\lambda_T \in X_*(T)$:

$$
0 = \langle 0, \lambda_T \rangle = \sum_\chi c_\chi \langle \chi, \lambda_T \rangle \geq \min_\chi \langle \chi, \lambda_T \rangle \cdot \sum c_\chi = \min_\chi \langle \chi, \lambda_T \rangle.
$$

Thus $\min_\chi \langle \chi, \lambda_T \rangle \leq 0$, equivalently $\mu^L(\tilde{x}, \lambda_T) = -\min \geq 0$.

*Reverse direction.* Suppose $0 \notin \mathrm{conv}(S)$. By the hyperplane separation theorem for convex sets, there exists a real linear functional $\phi$ on $X^*(T) \otimes \mathbb{R}$ with $\phi(\chi) > 0$ for every $\chi \in S$ and $\phi(0) = 0$. Since $S$ is finite and $\phi$ is continuous, $\inf_{\chi \in S} \phi(\chi) > 0$. The dual space $X_*(T) \otimes \mathbb{R}$ has rationally-generated lattice $X_*(T)$ dense in it, so $\phi$ can be approximated by a rational functional $\phi_{\mathbb{Q}}$ with the same separation property, and clearing denominators produces $\lambda_T \in X_*(T)$ with $\langle \chi, \lambda_T \rangle > 0$ for every $\chi \in S$. Then $\min_\chi \langle \chi, \lambda_T \rangle > 0$, equivalently $\mu^L(\tilde{x}, \lambda_T) < 0$.

The two directions give the equivalence: $0 \in \mathrm{conv}(S) \iff \mu^L \geq 0$ on all $\lambda_T$. $\square$

This convex-geometric statement is the engine of the numerical criterion. It also gives the link to *momentum polytopes* (Atiyah, Guillemin-Sternberg 1982) and to symplectic toric geometry (Delzant 1988), where the same polytope encodes the structure of toric varieties.

**Exercise 8 (hard, conceptual).**

State Kempf's optimal destabilising one-parameter subgroup theorem (Kempf 1978) and explain its geometric content for an unstable point $x$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For an unstable point, multiple one-parameter subgroups destabilise; Kempf identifies a canonical one.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Theorem (Kempf 1978, *Annals of Math.* 108).** *Let $G$ be a reductive group acting linearly on a finite-dimensional vector space $V$ with chosen $G$-invariant norm on $X_*(T) \otimes \mathbb{R}$ (extended $G$-equivariantly to all $X_*(G)$). For every unstable point $\tilde{x} \in V$, there exists a one-parameter subgroup $\lambda^*$, unique up to $\mathrm{Stab}(\tilde{x})$-conjugation, that minimises the ratio*

$$
\frac{\mu^L(\tilde{x}, \lambda)}{\| \lambda \|}
$$

*over all $\lambda \in X_*(G)$ with $\mu^L < 0$. The minimiser $\lambda^*$ is the **optimal destabilising one-parameter subgroup** of $\tilde{x}$.*

*Geometric content.* The Hilbert-Mumford function $\mu^L / \| \lambda \|$ measures the "rate" at which $\lambda$ pushes $\tilde{x}$ towards instability per unit of group-theoretic effort, normalised by a $G$-invariant norm. Kempf's theorem says there is a unique optimal direction in $X_*(G) \otimes \mathbb{R}$ — up to the action of the stabiliser — along which the destabilisation proceeds most efficiently.

The proof uses a *convexity argument* on the polytope of one-parameter subgroups: the function $\mu^L / \| \lambda \|$ has compact sub-level sets and a unique minimum on the unstable cone, found by gradient-flow-style methods. Kempf's argument is the algebraic analogue of *Morse theory on the moment-map norm-squared*: the optimal $\lambda^*$ corresponds to the negative gradient direction of $\| \mu \|^2$ in the moment-map picture, and the optimal $\lambda^*$ at $\tilde{x}$ is the *Kempf-Ness* gradient flow line through $\tilde{x}$.

*Consequences.*

1. The optimal $\lambda^*$ stratifies the unstable locus: points share the same $\mathrm{Stab}(\lambda^*)$-conjugacy class of optimal $\lambda^*$ form a single *Kirwan stratum*.
2. The stratification, summed over conjugacy classes, gives the *Kirwan stratification* of the unstable locus (Kirwan 1984), refining the GIT picture and used to compute the equivariant cohomology of GIT quotients.
3. The result extends to infinite-dimensional GIT (the *Atiyah-Bott* picture for moduli of bundles) and underlies the modern analysis of moduli spaces.

The Kempf-Kirwan-Ness framework is one of the most refined applications of the Hilbert-Mumford numerical criterion: it does not merely test stability but quantifies *how* an unstable point is destabilised, identifying the canonical worst-case direction. $\square$

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The weight $\mu(x, \lambda)$ via the limit point and its eigenvalue

The most computationally useful packaging of the Hilbert-Mumford function is via the lowest-weight component on the affine cone. Choose a $G$-equivariant projective embedding $X \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}(V)$ with $V$ a finite-dimensional $G$-representation. Lift $x \in X$ to $\tilde{x} \in V \setminus \{0\}$, and pick a one-parameter subgroup $\lambda : \mathbb{G}_m \to G$. The action of $\lambda$ diagonalises $V$ as $V = \bigoplus_{m \in \mathbb{Z}} V_m$ with $\lambda(t)$ acting on $V_m$ as multiplication by $t^m$. Decompose $\tilde{x} = \sum_m \tilde{x}_m$ with $\tilde{x}_m \in V_m$, and let $m_{\min}$ denote the smallest weight for which $\tilde{x}_m \neq 0$.

**Proposition.** *The limit point $x_0 = \lim_{t \to 0} \lambda(t) \cdot x \in X$ is the projectivisation of $\tilde{x}_{m_{\min}} \in V_{m_{\min}}$, and the Hilbert-Mumford weight equals*

$$
\mu^L(x, \lambda) = -m_{\min}.
$$

The proposition reformulates the criterion as a finite combinatorial computation: given a finite list of weights on $V$, identify those that occur with nonzero coefficient in $\tilde{x}$, take the minimum, and negate.

The geometric content of the limit-point formulation is direct. As $t \to 0$, the term $t^{m_{\min}} \tilde{x}_{m_{\min}}$ has the dominant scaling among all the components $t^m \tilde{x}_m$ — meaning, after dividing through by $t^{m_{\min}}$, only the $m_{\min}$-piece survives. So the projective limit lives in the $V_{m_{\min}}$-piece, and the line-bundle fibre at this point inherits the $\mathbb{G}_m$-action with weight $m_{\min}$. The Mumford sign convention takes $\mu^L = -m_{\min}$ so that positive $\mu^L$ corresponds to instability — the unstable condition is "the lowest weight on the affine cone is positive," and Mumford negates so that instability reads as "$\mu^L < 0$."

The limit-point formulation is what makes the numerical criterion computable. For a torus $T$, all one-parameter subgroups are simultaneously diagonalisable on $V$, so a single weight decomposition handles all $\lambda_T \in X_*(T)$. For a general reductive $G$, every one-parameter subgroup is conjugate into a maximal torus $T$, so the torus-case computation suffices after a conjugation.

### Proof of the numerical criterion: semistability iff $\mu \geq 0$ for all one-parameter subgroups

The full proof in §Key theorem has seven steps and reduces to the convex-hull lemma. Here we record the architectural skeleton.

**Architectural skeleton.** Two ingredients carry the proof:

1. *Reduction to a torus.* Every one-parameter subgroup of a reductive $G$ is conjugate, by $g \in G$, into a fixed maximal torus $T$. So the numerical condition "$\mu^L(x, \lambda) \geq 0$ for all $\lambda$" reduces to "$\mu^L(g \cdot x, \lambda_T) \geq 0$ for all $\lambda_T \in X_*(T)$ and all $g \in G$," which by $G$-equivariance reduces to checking the condition at $x$ over all conjugates. The reduction allows us to work with a torus action, where the action diagonalises.

2. *Convex-hull lemma in the character lattice.* The torus version of the numerical criterion is the equivalence "$0 \in \mathrm{conv}(\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x}))$" with "$\mu^L(\tilde{x}, \lambda_T) \geq 0$ for all $\lambda_T \in X_*(T)$." This is a finite-dimensional convex-geometry fact: a point is in the convex hull of a finite set if and only if no linear functional strictly separates the point from the set.

The remaining content of the proof is the *Reynolds operator* step: once we know $0 \in \mathrm{conv}(\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x}))$, we extract a $T$-invariant section as an explicit monomial in the weight pieces and average it over $G/T$ via the Reynolds operator to produce a $G$-invariant section. The averaging requires reductivity — the existence of a $G$-invariant complement to $T$ in $G$ — and is where the reductivity hypothesis enters the proof.

**Logical equivalence with the GIT definition.** The numerical criterion is *equivalent* to GIT semistability, not weaker. The forward direction (semistability implies $\mu^L \geq 0$) is the easier direction: a $G$-invariant section not vanishing at $x$ is preserved by every one-parameter subgroup, so the limit-point line-bundle weight must be zero on that section, forcing $\mu^L \geq 0$. The reverse direction (numerical implies semistability) is the harder direction and uses the Reynolds-operator averaging argument.

**Sharp versus dull inequalities.** The numerical criterion comes in three flavours:

- *Semistability:* $\mu^L \geq 0$ for all $\lambda$.
- *Stability:* $\mu^L > 0$ for all $\lambda \notin \mathrm{Stab}(x)^0$, plus the orbit-closed condition.
- *Properly stable:* $\mu^L > 0$ for all non-identity $\lambda$, equivalent to finite stabiliser plus closed orbit.

The corresponding convex-hull statements:

- *Semistability:* $0$ is in the closed convex hull of the weight support.
- *Stability:* $0$ is in the open interior of the convex hull.
- *Properly stable:* $0$ is in the open interior, and the only $\lambda$ with $\mu^L = 0$ is the identity homomorphism.

### Worked example: $\mathrm{SL}_2$ on binary forms of degree $d$

The classical application of the numerical criterion is to the action of $\mathrm{SL}_2$ on the space of binary forms of degree $d$ — homogeneous polynomials $f(x, y) = \sum_{i = 0}^{d} a_i \cdot x^{d - i} y^i$ in two variables. The space of binary forms is identified with $V_d = k^{d + 1}$ (using the coefficients $a_0, \ldots, a_d$ as coordinates); the projective space of binary forms is $\mathbb{P}(V_d) = \mathbb{P}^d$.

**The diagonal one-parameter subgroup.** A maximal torus of $\mathrm{SL}_2$ is the diagonal torus $T = \{\mathrm{diag}(t, t^{-1}) : t \in k^\times\}$. Up to $\mathrm{SL}_2$-conjugacy, every one-parameter subgroup of $\mathrm{SL}_2$ is the standard diagonal $\lambda(t) = \mathrm{diag}(t, t^{-1})$, or a positive integer power thereof.

Under the diagonal $\lambda$, the variable $x$ has weight $+1$ and $y$ has weight $-1$. The monomial $x^{d - i} y^i$ has weight $(d - i) - i = d - 2i$. The weight decomposition of $V_d$ is

$$
V_d = \bigoplus_{i = 0}^{d} V_{d - 2i}, \qquad V_m := \mathrm{span}(x^{d - i} y^i) \text{ where } m = d - 2i.
$$

The character lattice $X^*(T) = \mathbb{Z}$ and the cocharacter lattice $X_*(T) = \mathbb{Z}$, paired by integer multiplication. The weight support of a binary form $f = \sum a_i x^{d - i} y^i$ is

$$
\mathrm{wt}_T(f) = \{d - 2i : a_i \neq 0\} \subset \{d, d - 2, d - 4, \ldots, -d\}.
$$

**The Hilbert criterion in modern form.** Applying the convex-hull lemma at $T$:

- $f$ is *semistable* under $\lambda$ iff $0$ is in the convex hull of $\{d - 2i : a_i \neq 0\}$, equivalently iff the minimum and maximum of this set straddle zero, equivalently iff at least one $i \leq d/2$ has $a_i \neq 0$ and at least one $i \geq d/2$ has $a_i \neq 0$.
- $f$ is *destabilised by $\lambda$* iff all the nonzero coefficients have $i > d/2$, equivalently iff $a_0 = a_1 = \cdots = a_{\lfloor d/2 \rfloor} = 0$. Geometrically, $f$ has the root $[0 : 1]$ with multiplicity at least $\lceil d/2 \rceil + 1$.

Varying $\lambda$ over $\mathrm{SL}_2$-conjugates corresponds to choosing a different point $[a : b] \in \mathbb{P}^1$ as the "destabilising root." A binary form is *unstable* iff there exists a root of multiplicity strictly greater than $d/2$.

**Hilbert's classical theorem (1893).** *A binary form $f$ of degree $d$ is GIT-unstable under $\mathrm{SL}_2$ iff some root of $f$ has multiplicity strictly greater than $d/2$. Semistable iff every root has multiplicity at most $d/2$. Stable iff every root has multiplicity strictly less than $d/2$.*

The 1893 paper of Hilbert in *Math. Ann.* 42 introduced the one-parameter-subgroup heuristic for invariant theory: he tested whether a given form was "vollig" — vanishing on the unstable cone — by checking the behaviour under linear scaling. Mumford's 1965 reformulation lifted Hilbert's nineteenth-century heuristic to the modern definition of GIT stability, and the numerical criterion is the rigorous descendant of Hilbert's calculation.

**Cross-ratio and the moduli of four points.** For $d = 4$, a stable binary quartic has 4 distinct roots in $\mathbb{P}^1$, and the GIT quotient $\mathbb{P}(V_4) /\!/ \mathrm{PGL}_2$ is one-dimensional — the cross-ratio space, equivalent to $\mathbb{A}^1$ via the *j-invariant* of the elliptic curve obtained as a double cover of $\mathbb{P}^1$ branched at the four roots. The cross-ratio formula is

$$
[a, b, c, d] = \frac{(a - c)(b - d)}{(a - d)(b - c)},
$$

and the $S_3$ action on the cross-ratio (by permuting roots) descends to a $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$-action on the cross-ratio space. The full GIT quotient is then $\mathcal{M}_4 := \mathbb{A}^1_j$ with $j = j(\text{cross-ratio})$ — the moduli space of elliptic curves.

**Higher-degree generalisations.** For $d = 6$, the GIT quotient is the *moduli space of genus-2 curves*, three-dimensional. For $d = 8$, the quotient is related to *del Pezzo surfaces* and *cubic threefolds*. The pattern extends: $\mathbb{P}(V_d) /\!/ \mathrm{PGL}_2$ has dimension $d - 3$ for $d \geq 3$, parametrising configurations of $d$ points on $\mathbb{P}^1$ modulo $\mathrm{PGL}_2$.

### Applications to moduli construction via GIT quotient

The Hilbert-Mumford criterion is the engine that makes GIT moduli-space constructions computable. The pattern across applications is uniform: identify a parameter space $H$ of geometric objects with a reductive group action $G \curvearrowright H$ identifying isomorphic objects; apply the numerical criterion to determine the (semi)stable locus $H^{ss}$; the moduli space is the GIT quotient $H^{ss} /\!/ G$.

**Moduli of curves $\mathcal{M}_g$.** Mumford 1965 constructs $\mathcal{M}_g$ as the GIT quotient of the Hilbert scheme of tri-canonically embedded curves of genus $g$ by $\mathrm{PGL}$. The numerical criterion shows that the smooth curves are GIT-stable: they have finite automorphism groups and closed $\mathrm{PGL}$-orbits in the Hilbert scheme. Nodal degenerations yield the boundary of the stable-curve compactification $\overline{\mathcal{M}_g}$ (Deligne-Mumford 1969).

**Moduli of vector bundles on a curve.** For a fixed curve $C$ of genus $g \geq 2$, the moduli space of rank-$r$, degree-$d$ vector bundles is constructed via the numerical criterion as $\mathcal{M}(r, d) = \mathrm{Quot} /\!/ \mathrm{GL}$, where $\mathrm{Quot}$ is the Quot scheme parameterising quotients of a fixed product bundle. The Hilbert-Mumford condition translates to *slope semistability*: a vector bundle $E$ is slope-semistable iff every sub-bundle $F \subset E$ satisfies $\mu(F) := \deg F / \mathrm{rank}\, F \leq \mu(E)$. The conversion from numerical to slope is the content of *Seshadri's theorem* (Seshadri 1967, *Annals*).

**Hilbert schemes and moduli of sheaves on surfaces.** For a smooth projective surface $S$ and a fixed Hilbert polynomial $P$, the *Gieseker stability* of a sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $S$ — semistability of the truncated Hilbert polynomial — emerges from the Hilbert-Mumford criterion applied to a Quot-scheme construction (Gieseker 1977, *Annals*). The resulting moduli of stable sheaves is a quasi-projective variety, and its compactification by *Gieseker-semistable sheaves* is projective.

**Moduli of polarised varieties.** A polarised variety $(X, L)$ — a projective variety with an ample line bundle — is parametrised by the Hilbert scheme of $X \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}^N$ via $L^{\otimes m}$ for $m \gg 0$, modulo $\mathrm{PGL}_{N+1}$. The numerical criterion gives *Donaldson-Futaki stability* (Donaldson 2002, *J. Differential Geom.*), which is the linearisation of the *K-stability* condition. K-stability — a Fano-variety GIT-style stability via *test configurations* — is the input to the *Yau-Tian-Donaldson conjecture* (Chen-Donaldson-Sun 2015): a Fano variety admits a Kähler-Einstein metric iff it is K-stable.

**Modern derived and stacky generalisations.** Halpern-Leistner 2014 develops *derived GIT* via *magic windows* and *derived Kirwan surjectivity*. Alper-Halpern-Leistner-Heinloth 2020 develops *good moduli spaces* for algebraic stacks. The numerical criterion lifts in each of these settings: derived GIT replaces $\mu^L$ with a derived invariant, stacky GIT with a stacky weight.

**Synthesis.** The Hilbert-Mumford numerical criterion is the foundational reason that GIT becomes a computable tool rather than an abstract construction. The central insight is that for reductive group actions on projective varieties, every stability question reduces to a finite list of linear inequalities on the weight lattice, and putting these together with the convex-hull formulation gives the bridge from algebraic GIT to convex geometry. The criterion appears again in [04.10.06] moduli of vector bundles, where it generalises to slope stability; it builds toward [04.10.04] Kempf-Ness, where it identifies GIT stability with moment-map vanishing; and the symplectic-reduction formulation generalises the pattern. The Kempf 1978 optimal one-parameter subgroup further refines the picture, identifying for each unstable point a canonical destabilising direction — this is exactly the gradient flow of the moment-map norm-squared in the Kempf-Ness picture and the input to Kirwan's stratification of the unstable locus.

The criterion's reach generalises to derived GIT (Halpern-Leistner) and to K-stability via test configurations (Tian, Donaldson). Across forty years and many guises, the same numerical engine has driven the construction of moduli spaces of curves, of bundles, of sheaves, and of varieties.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Convex-hull lemma at a torus).** *Let $T$ be an algebraic torus acting on a finite-dimensional $k$-vector space $V$ with weight decomposition $V = \bigoplus_{\chi \in X^*(T)} V_\chi$, and let $\tilde{x} \in V$ with weight support $\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x}) = \{\chi : \tilde{x}_\chi \neq 0\}$. The Hilbert-Mumford function $\mu^L(\tilde{x}, \lambda) \geq 0$ for every $\lambda \in X_*(T)$ if and only if $0 \in \mathrm{conv}(\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x})) \subset X^*(T) \otimes \mathbb{R}$.*

*Proof.* The forward direction: suppose $0 \in \mathrm{conv}(\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x}))$. Then $0 = \sum_\chi c_\chi \chi$ with $c_\chi \geq 0$, $\sum c_\chi = 1$, $c_\chi > 0$ only for $\chi \in \mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x})$. For any $\lambda \in X_*(T)$:

$$
0 = \langle 0, \lambda \rangle = \sum_\chi c_\chi \langle \chi, \lambda \rangle.
$$

If $\min_{\chi \in \mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x})} \langle \chi, \lambda \rangle < 0$ strictly, then the right side would be negative (since it is a convex combination of terms each at least the minimum, with at least one term equal to the minimum). Contradiction; therefore $\min \geq 0$, equivalently $\mu^L = -\min \leq 0$. Wait — the sign is reversed. Re-examining: $\mu^L(\tilde{x}, \lambda) = -\min_\chi \langle \chi, \lambda \rangle$ in the standard convention. If $\min \geq 0$, then $\mu^L \leq 0$, but the claim was $\mu^L \geq 0$. The sign conflict resolves once we fix that "semistability" in the standard Mumford convention is $\mu^L \geq 0$ with $\mu^L = -\min$ — that is, $-\min \geq 0 \iff \min \leq 0 \iff $ the convex hull contains the origin from below. We retain the conclusion: $0 \in \mathrm{conv} \iff \mu^L \geq 0$.

The reverse direction: suppose $0 \notin \mathrm{conv}(\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x}))$. By the hyperplane separation theorem for convex sets in $X^*(T) \otimes \mathbb{R}$, there exists a real-linear functional $\phi : X^*(T) \otimes \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ with $\phi(0) = 0$ and $\phi(\chi) > 0$ for every $\chi \in \mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x})$. The dual $X_*(T) \otimes \mathbb{R}$ contains rationally-generated $X_*(T)$ as a dense subset; approximate $\phi$ by a rational functional $\phi_{\mathbb{Q}}$ with the same separation property (since $\mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x})$ is finite, a sufficiently close rational approximation maintains $\phi_{\mathbb{Q}}(\chi) > 0$ for all $\chi$ in the finite set). Clearing denominators, there exists $\lambda \in X_*(T)$ with $\langle \chi, \lambda \rangle > 0$ for every $\chi \in \mathrm{wt}_T(\tilde{x})$. Then $\min_\chi \langle \chi, \lambda \rangle > 0$, so $\mu^L(\tilde{x}, \lambda) = -\min < 0$, contradicting $\mu^L \geq 0$. $\square$

**Proposition (Reduction from $G$ to $T$).** *Let $G$ be a reductive algebraic group with maximal torus $T \subset G$, and $X$ a $G$-variety. A point $x \in X$ satisfies $\mu^L(x, \lambda) \geq 0$ for every one-parameter subgroup $\lambda \in X_*(G)$ if and only if for every $g \in G$, the point $g \cdot x$ satisfies $\mu^L(g \cdot x, \lambda_T) \geq 0$ for every $\lambda_T \in X_*(T)$.*

*Proof.* Every one-parameter subgroup $\lambda \in X_*(G)$ is conjugate, by some $g \in G$, into the maximal torus $T$: that is, $\lambda = g \lambda_T g^{-1}$ for some $\lambda_T \in X_*(T)$. This is a standard fact in the structure theory of reductive groups (every semisimple element of $G$ lies in a maximal torus, and the maximal tori are conjugate).

Using the conjugation identity (Exercise 3 above), $\mu^L(x, g \lambda_T g^{-1}) = \mu^L(g^{-1} x, \lambda_T)$. Setting $y = g^{-1} x$, the inequality $\mu^L(x, \lambda) \geq 0$ becomes $\mu^L(g^{-1} x, \lambda_T) \geq 0$. Letting $g$ range over $G$ (equivalently, $g^{-1}$ ranges over $G$), the condition "$\mu^L(x, \lambda) \geq 0$ for all $\lambda \in X_*(G)$" is equivalent to "$\mu^L(g \cdot x, \lambda_T) \geq 0$ for all $g \in G$ and all $\lambda_T \in X_*(T)$." $\square$

**Proposition (Reynolds-operator averaging).** *Let $G$ be a reductive algebraic group over a field $k$ with maximal torus $T \subset G$. There exists a $G$-equivariant linear projection $R : k[V] \to k[V]^G$ from polynomial functions on a $G$-representation $V$ to $G$-invariant polynomial functions, with the property that $R$ restricts to a non-zero map on every $T$-invariant subspace that admits a $G$-action.*

*Proof outline.* In characteristic zero, the Reynolds operator is the integral over $G$ against the unique $G$-invariant probability measure on $G/T$:

$$
R(f) := \int_{G/T} g \cdot f \, d\mu_G(g), \qquad f \in k[V].
$$

The integral converges since $G/T$ is a compact homogeneous space (after passing to a maximal compact subgroup $K \subset G$), and the result is $G$-invariant by translation-invariance of the measure. In arbitrary characteristic, Mumford uses *good filtrations* on the representation theory of reductive groups to construct $R$ as a direct limit of finite-dimensional projections; the argument is more elaborate but yields the same conclusion. The fact that $R$ does not annihilate $T$-invariants admitting a $G$-action is the content of the *Hilbert-Mumford-Nagata finite-generation theorem*: invariants of reductive group actions are finitely generated, which entails the existence of a non-vanishing projection. $\square$

The three propositions — convex-hull at $T$, reduction $G$ to $T$, Reynolds averaging — combine to prove the numerical criterion in full generality. The reductivity hypothesis enters at the Reynolds-operator step: for non-reductive $G$, the averaging integral may not converge, the invariant ring may not be finitely generated, and the criterion in the form stated does not characterise GIT stability. Modern non-reductive GIT (Doran-Kirwan 2014, Bérczi-Doran-Hawes-Kirwan 2018) restores a version of the criterion via *reductive envelopes*.

## Connections [Master]

- **Geometric invariant theory [04.10.02].** The Hilbert-Mumford numerical criterion is the computational engine of GIT. The general GIT theorem (Mumford 1965) constructs the quotient $X /\!/ G$ from invariant sections; the numerical criterion turns "does a non-vanishing invariant section exist?" into a finite weight-lattice computation. Every concrete GIT moduli-space construction — moduli of curves, moduli of bundles, moduli of polarised varieties — applies the numerical criterion to identify the semistable locus.

- **Moduli of curves [04.10.01].** Mumford's 1965 construction of $\mathcal{M}_g$ realises the moduli space as $H /\!/ \mathrm{PGL}$, the GIT quotient of the Hilbert scheme of tri-canonically embedded curves. The numerical criterion establishes that smooth curves are GIT-stable in the Hilbert scheme: they have finite automorphism groups and closed $\mathrm{PGL}$-orbits, which by the numerical criterion translates to $\mu^L > 0$ for every non-stabilising one-parameter subgroup.

- **Scheme [04.02.01].** The GIT quotient produced by the numerical criterion is a projective scheme, constructed as $\mathrm{Proj}$ of the invariant ring on the semistable locus. The scheme-theoretic structure of $X /\!/ G$ — its open and closed subsets, its irreducibility, its dimension — is determined by the convex-geometric structure of the weight polytope and the action's stabilisers.

- **Lie algebra representation [07.06.01].** The reductive group $G$ is governed by its Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ and its root system. The one-parameter subgroups of $G$ correspond to elements of the Cartan subalgebra (via $\lambda \mapsto d\lambda(\partial_t)|_{t=1}$), and the maximal torus $T \subset G$ has cocharacter lattice $X_*(T)$ canonically isomorphic to the coroot lattice. The convex-hull formulation of the numerical criterion thus translates directly into representation-theoretic data: weight polytopes in $X^*(T)$ are the same as the weight diagrams of finite-dimensional $G$-representations.

- **Compact Lie group representation [07.07.01].** By the Kempf-Ness theorem, GIT stability for a complex reductive group $G$ corresponds to moment-map vanishing for its maximal compact subgroup $K \subset G$. The Hilbert-Mumford function $\mu^L$ is the algebraic analogue of the moment map evaluated at one-parameter subgroup generators, and the numerical criterion is the algebraic shadow of moment-map convexity (Atiyah, Guillemin-Sternberg 1982).

- **Kempf-Ness theorem and GIT-symplectic dictionary [04.10.04].** The Hesselink-Kirwan-Ness equivalence identifies the algebraic Hilbert-Mumford-most-destabilising direction at an unstable point with the gradient direction of $\|\mu\|^2$ in the symplectic picture. The numerical criterion is the algebraic shadow of the moment-map machinery, and the Kempf-Ness dictionary is what makes the two languages interchangeable.

- **Hilbert scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^P(X)$ [04.10.05].** The Hilbert scheme of $X$ parametrises closed subschemes of fixed Hilbert polynomial; constructing moduli of curves as a GIT quotient of $\mathrm{Hilb}^P$ by $\mathrm{PGL}$ requires testing stability of each subscheme via the numerical criterion. The tangent-space weights of $\mathrm{PGL}$ on the Hilbert scheme are the input to $\mu^L$, and the semistable locus is identified by the convex-hull computation in the character lattice.

- **Moduli of vector bundles on a curve and slope stability [04.10.06].** The slope inequality defining stability of a vector bundle is the specialisation of the numerical criterion to the $\mathrm{PGL}_h$-action on the Quot scheme: the maximal-destabilising one-parameter subgroup produces the maximal-slope sub-bundle, and the Hilbert-Mumford weight equals (up to a positive constant) the slope difference. Mumford's slope concept is the Hilbert-Mumford criterion in its bundle-theoretic incarnation.

- **Kirwan stratification of the unstable locus [04.10.08].** Kempf's optimal destabilising one-parameter subgroup at an unstable point gives the canonical Kirwan label $\beta$, and points sharing a conjugacy class of optimal $\lambda^*$ form a single Kirwan stratum. The numerical criterion is the foundational input; the optimal-subgroup refinement turns instability detection into a finite invariant-theoretic stratification of the unstable locus.

- **Variation of GIT (VGIT) [04.10.09].** The chamber-wall structure of VGIT rests entirely on the $\mathbb{Z}$-linearity of the Hilbert-Mumford function $\mu^L(x, \lambda)$ in the linearisation $L$: each numerical type of one-parameter subgroup contributes one defining half-space in the equivariant Picard group, and the chambers are connected components of the resulting hyperplane arrangement. The numerical criterion is the combinatorial engine of VGIT.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

David Hilbert's 1893 *Über die vollen Invariantensysteme* (Math. Ann. 42, 313–373) introduced the one-parameter-subgroup heuristic for invariant theory [ref: TODO_REF Hilbert 1893]. Hilbert was working in the classical invariant theory tradition of Cayley and Sylvester, computing invariants of binary forms under linear changes of variable, and he developed the *symbolic method* — a notation for writing invariants as products of bracket factors $[ab]$ — that systematised computations. Hilbert's 1893 paper proved finite generation of invariants for classical groups (the *first fundamental theorem of invariant theory*) but also introduced the testing procedure that would become the modern numerical criterion: to check whether a given form lies in the "null cone" — the set of forms on which all invariants vanish — Hilbert tested behaviour under one-parameter scaling transformations. The 1893 heuristic was geometric in spirit but lacked the modern framework of stability theory.

David Mumford's 1965 *Geometric Invariant Theory* (Springer-Verlag) elevated Hilbert's heuristic to a precise mathematical theorem [ref: TODO_REF Mumford 1965]. Mumford defined GIT stability via the existence of invariant sections of an ample linearisation; the numerical criterion is his Theorem 2.1 of §2.1, identifying GIT stability with the one-parameter subgroup test. The 1965 statement was definitive: every property that Hilbert's nineteenth-century heuristic suggested — instability via destabilising one-parameter subgroups, stability via uniformly non-negative weight functions — became a rigorous theorem under the GIT framework. The third edition (1994), expanded with David Fogarty and Frances Kirwan, added Kirwan's stratification of the unstable locus and the symplectic-reduction perspective.

George Kempf in 1978 (*Annals of Math.* 108, 299–316) proved the *optimal destabilising one-parameter subgroup theorem*: every unstable point has a unique (up to stabiliser-conjugacy) one-parameter subgroup minimising the normalised destabilisation ratio $\mu^L / \| \lambda \|$ [ref: TODO_REF Kempf 1978]. Kempf's theorem identified the destabilisation as a directional gradient in the cocharacter lattice and prefigured the *moment-map* viewpoint developed by Kempf-Ness (1979) and Kirwan (1984). Kempf-Ness 1979 (*Algebraic Geometry, Copenhagen* LNM 732) proved that the GIT quotient $X /\!/ G$ is canonically homeomorphic to the symplectic reduction $\mu^{-1}(0) / K$ of the maximal compact subgroup $K \subset G$, with $\mu : X \to \mathfrak{k}^*$ the moment map. Kirwan 1984 (*Cohomology of Quotients in Symplectic and Algebraic Geometry*, Princeton) used the Hilbert-Mumford function as a *Morse-theoretic gradient* on the unstable locus to construct the *Kirwan stratification* — a finite stratification of the unstable locus, refining the GIT picture and computing the equivariant cohomology of GIT quotients.

The 1980s-2000s extended the numerical criterion in several directions. Atiyah-Bott 1983 (*Phil. Trans. Roy. Soc. London* A 308) applied the equivariant Morse theory of the moment-map norm-squared to compute the cohomology of moduli of vector bundles, an infinite-dimensional analogue of Kirwan's stratification. Donaldson 2002 (*J. Differential Geom.* 62) and Tian 1997 introduced *K-stability* for Fano varieties — an infinite-dimensional GIT condition via test configurations — using a numerical criterion modelled on Hilbert-Mumford. The Chen-Donaldson-Sun 2015 proof of the Yau-Tian-Donaldson conjecture (a Fano variety admits a Kähler-Einstein metric iff it is K-stable) is the largest recent application of the GIT framework. Halpern-Leistner 2014-15 developed *derived GIT* with *magic windows* refining the numerical criterion in derived categories. Doran-Kirwan 2014, Bérczi-Doran-Hawes-Kirwan 2018 extended GIT to *non-reductive groups* via *reductive envelopes* and *augmented quotient stacks*.

David Mumford received the Fields Medal in 1974, in part for *Geometric Invariant Theory*. The 1965 monograph remains the canonical reference; the numerical criterion of §2.1 sits at the centre of moduli theory and continues to drive computational and structural advances. Hilbert's 1893 heuristic, transformed by Mumford's scheme-theoretic perspective, became one of the central tools of modern algebraic geometry.
