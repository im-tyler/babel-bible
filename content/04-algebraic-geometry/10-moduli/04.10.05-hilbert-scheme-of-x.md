## Intuition [Beginner]

The *Hilbert scheme* $\mathrm{Hilb}^P(X)$ is a single algebraic-geometric object whose points are *subschemes* of a fixed ambient space $X$. Pick the ambient space — say projective space $\mathbb{P}^N$ — and a "size invariant" called the Hilbert polynomial $P$. The Hilbert scheme then parametrises every closed subscheme of $\mathbb{P}^N$ whose Hilbert polynomial equals $P$. A point of the Hilbert scheme is one such subscheme; a path is a continuous deformation from one subscheme to another.

Grothendieck introduced the Hilbert scheme in 1962 as a tool to give every classical moduli problem a rigorous geometric home. Before Grothendieck, families of curves, surfaces, or finite point sets were studied informally. With the Hilbert scheme, families become honest morphisms from a parameter scheme into a single fixed object that knows every subscheme of $X$ of the given size at once.

The simplest case is the Hilbert scheme of $n$ points on the plane $\mathbb{C}^2$. A point of this Hilbert scheme is a configuration of $n$ points, but it also allows "fat points" where several ordinary points have collided into a single thickened point. Fogarty in 1968 proved that this Hilbert scheme is a smooth $2n$-dimensional manifold, which makes it one of the most-studied moduli spaces in modern algebraic geometry.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the affine plane $\mathbb{A}^2 = \mathrm{Spec}\,k[x, y]$ over a field $k$, and look at the Hilbert scheme of $2$ points on $\mathbb{A}^2$. A typical point of this Hilbert scheme is a pair of distinct points $\{p_1, p_2\}$ in the plane — two physically separate dots.

Step 1. Count the dimension of the configuration. Two free points in $\mathbb{A}^2$ give 4 coordinates total, but we record them as an unordered set, so the unordered configuration of 2 distinct points is parametrised by a 4-dimensional space.

Step 2. Watch what happens as the two points collide. As $p_2$ moves toward $p_1$, the limiting object is a *single* point $p_1$ together with a *direction*: a tangent vector indicating the line along which $p_2$ approached. This "doubled point with direction" is a fat point of length $2$.

Step 3. The Hilbert scheme of $2$ points on $\mathbb{A}^2$ is exactly the union of the ordinary configurations and these tangent-direction fat points. Its dimension is $2 \cdot 2 = 4$ — a smooth fourfold, matching Fogarty's formula $\dim \mathrm{Hilb}^n(\mathrm{surface}) = 2n$ for $n = 2$.

What this tells us: the Hilbert scheme remembers the limits of point configurations as ordinary points collide, so it is the natural compactification of the space of unordered finite subsets of a surface.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $S$ be a Noetherian base scheme, $X \to S$ a projective $S$-scheme equipped with a chosen relatively very ample line bundle $\mathcal{O}_X(1)$, and $P \in \mathbb{Q}[t]$ a numerical polynomial.

**Hilbert functor.** The **Hilbert functor** $\underline{\mathrm{Hilb}}^P_{X/S} : (\mathrm{Sch}/S)^{\mathrm{op}} \to (\mathrm{Sets})$ assigns to each $S$-scheme $T$ the set

$$
\underline{\mathrm{Hilb}}^P_{X/S}(T) = \left\{ Z \subset X_T = X \times_S T \;\Big|\; Z \to T \text{ flat with fibres of Hilbert polynomial } P \right\}.
$$

A morphism $T' \to T$ acts by pullback $Z \mapsto Z \times_T T'$. Flatness of $Z \to T$ is the essential family-theoretic requirement, ensuring that the Hilbert polynomial is locally constant on $T$ (and hence equal to $P$ on every fibre once it equals $P$ on one).

**Hilbert scheme.** The **Hilbert scheme** $\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$ is the $S$-scheme representing this functor: there is a natural isomorphism of functors

$$
\mathrm{Hom}_S(T, \mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}) \;\cong\; \underline{\mathrm{Hilb}}^P_{X/S}(T).
$$

**Universal family.** Taking $T = \mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$ and the identity map, the corresponding flat family is the *universal family*

$$
\mathcal{Z} \;\subset\; X \times_S \mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S} \;\xrightarrow{\;\pi\;}\; \mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S},
$$

with $\pi$ flat and proper and every fibre $\mathcal{Z}_{[Z]}$ recovering the subscheme $Z \subset X$ that the point $[Z] \in \mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$ represents.

**Hilbert polynomial reminder.** For a coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on a projective scheme $X \subset \mathbb{P}^N_S$, the *Hilbert polynomial* $P_{\mathcal{F}}$ is the unique polynomial agreeing with $m \mapsto \chi(\mathcal{F}(m))$ for $m \gg 0$. For a closed subscheme $Z \subset X$, write $P_Z := P_{\mathcal{O}_Z}$; this records degree, dimension, arithmetic genus, and other discrete invariants of $Z$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Forgetting flatness.* The Hilbert *functor* requires $Z \to T$ to be *flat* with fibres of Hilbert polynomial $P$. Dropping flatness gives a non-representable functor: families where the polynomial jumps from fibre to fibre are not parametrised by a single scheme. Flatness is what makes the Hilbert polynomial a *locally constant* invariant of a family (Mumford-Hartshorne).
- *Hilbert polynomial vs degree.* Two subschemes of $\mathbb{P}^N$ with the same degree but different arithmetic genus lie in distinct Hilbert schemes. Lines and conics in $\mathbb{P}^3$ have polynomials $P_{\mathrm{line}}(m) = m + 1$ and $P_{\mathrm{conic}}(m) = 2m + 1$; the line is on $\mathrm{Hilb}^{m+1}(\mathbb{P}^3)$ and the conic on $\mathrm{Hilb}^{2m+1}(\mathbb{P}^3)$.
- *Reducibility of $\mathrm{Hilb}^P$.* The Hilbert scheme is connected (Hartshorne 1966) but generally not irreducible. For instance, $\mathrm{Hilb}^{3m+1}(\mathbb{P}^3)$, parametrising twisted cubics, has two irreducible components: the locus of honest twisted cubics and the locus of a plane cubic union an isolated point (the "extra" component first identified by Piene-Schlessinger 1985).
- *Confusing $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ with $\mathrm{Sym}^n(X)$.* For curves these coincide; for higher-dimensional varieties the symmetric product is singular along the diagonal while the Hilbert scheme of $n$ points on a smooth surface is smooth (Fogarty). The Hilbert-Chow morphism (§Master) sends $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ to $\mathrm{Sym}^n(X)$ and is a resolution of singularities in the surface case.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Grothendieck 1962).** *Let $S$ be a Noetherian scheme, $X \to S$ a projective $S$-scheme with a chosen relatively very ample line bundle $\mathcal{O}_X(1)$, and $P \in \mathbb{Q}[t]$ a numerical polynomial. Then the Hilbert functor $\underline{\mathrm{Hilb}}^P_{X/S}$ is representable by a projective $S$-scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$. The universal closed subscheme $\mathcal{Z} \subset X \times_S \mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$ is flat over $\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$ with fibrewise Hilbert polynomial $P$.*

*Proof.* The argument has four steps. We give the standard FGA Explained presentation (Nitsure §5.5).

*Step 1 — uniform $m$-regularity.* A coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $\mathbb{P}^N_k$ is *$m$-regular* (Mumford-Castelnuovo regularity) if $H^i(\mathbb{P}^N_k, \mathcal{F}(m - i)) = 0$ for every $i \geq 1$. The key uniform-bound lemma: there exists an integer $m_0 = m_0(P, N)$ depending only on $P$ and $N$ such that for every closed subscheme $Z \subset \mathbb{P}^N_k$ with Hilbert polynomial $P_Z = P$ and every field $k$, the ideal sheaf $\mathcal{I}_Z$ is $m_0$-regular. The proof is by induction on $\dim Z$ via generic hyperplane sections, using the equivalence between $m$-regularity and the Castelnuovo-Mumford vanishing condition on $\mathcal{I}_Z(m - i)$ (Mumford *Lectures* §14).

*Step 2 — embedding into a Grassmannian.* Fix the uniform $m = m_0$ from Step 1. For each closed subscheme $Z \subset \mathbb{P}^N$ with Hilbert polynomial $P$, the $m_0$-regularity property implies that $\mathcal{I}_Z(m_0)$ is generated by its global sections and that $H^0(\mathbb{P}^N, \mathcal{I}_Z(m_0))$ has dimension $\dim H^0(\mathbb{P}^N, \mathcal{O}(m_0)) - P(m_0) =: r$ independent of $Z$. The subspace $H^0(\mathcal{I}_Z(m_0)) \subset H^0(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^N}(m_0))$ is therefore an $r$-dimensional subspace of the fixed vector space $V := H^0(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^N}(m_0))$ of dimension $\binom{N + m_0}{m_0}$. Recording each $Z$ by this subspace gives a set-theoretic injection

$$
\iota : \{ Z \subset \mathbb{P}^N : P_Z = P \} \;\hookrightarrow\; \mathrm{Gr}(r, V).
$$

*Step 3 — the image is closed.* The Grassmannian $\mathrm{Gr}(r, V)$ is a smooth projective scheme over $S$. The image of $\iota$ is the locus of $r$-dimensional subspaces $W \subset V$ satisfying the closed conditions: (i) the multiplication map $W \otimes H^0(\mathcal{O}(1)) \to H^0(\mathcal{O}(m_0 + 1))$ has image of dimension $\dim V_1 - P(m_0 + 1)$ where $V_1 = H^0(\mathcal{O}(m_0 + 1))$; (ii) the ideal sheaf generated by $W$ has the correct Hilbert polynomial in each degree $\geq m_0$. Both conditions cut out a closed subscheme of $\mathrm{Gr}(r, V)$ by a rank-condition argument on the multiplication map, giving a closed subscheme $H \subset \mathrm{Gr}(r, V)$.

*Step 4 — flatness and universal family.* The Grassmannian comes with a tautological subbundle $\mathcal{W} \subset \mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{Gr}} \otimes V$ of rank $r$. Restricting to $H \subset \mathrm{Gr}(r, V)$ and passing through the multiplication maps in higher degrees produces a coherent sheaf of ideals $\mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{Z}} \subset \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^N \times H}$ whose vanishing locus $\mathcal{Z} \subset \mathbb{P}^N \times H$ is flat over $H$ with Hilbert polynomial $P$ on every fibre. The standard verification that $(H, \mathcal{Z})$ represents the Hilbert functor — every flat family $Z \to T$ with fibrewise Hilbert polynomial $P$ pulls back from $\mathcal{Z}$ via a unique morphism $T \to H$ — uses cohomology-and-base-change to lift the construction from a point to a parameter scheme. The full argument is in *FGA Explained* §5.5.

Setting $\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S} := H$ for $X = \mathbb{P}^N_S$ yields the construction over projective space. For an arbitrary projective $X \to S$ with a chosen embedding $X \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}^N_S$, the Hilbert scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$ is the closed subscheme of $\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{\mathbb{P}^N_S / S}$ parametrising subschemes contained in $X$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [04.10.20] deformation theory of smooth curves, where the universal family lets us linearise the moduli problem at every point and read off the tangent space as a sheaf-cohomology invariant. The universal family appears again in [04.10.01] moduli of curves as the input to Mumford's GIT construction: $\mathcal{M}_g$ is the GIT quotient of an open subscheme of $\mathrm{Hilb}^P(\mathbb{P}^N)$ by $\mathrm{PGL}_{N+1}$. Putting these together, the foundational reason the Hilbert scheme matters in moduli theory is that representability turns every classification problem into a problem of cutting out a locally closed subscheme of a Hilbert scheme and quotienting by an algebraic group. The central insight is that representability collapses the analytic and the algebraic: a moduli question becomes a scheme-theoretic question on a single fixed projective object.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Compute the Hilbert polynomial of a line $L \subset \mathbb{P}^3$ and the Hilbert polynomial of a degree-$d$ smooth plane curve $C \subset \mathbb{P}^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $P_{\mathcal{O}_Z}(m) = \chi(\mathcal{O}_Z(m))$ and Riemann-Roch on curves.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For the line $L \subset \mathbb{P}^3$: $L \cong \mathbb{P}^1$ and $\mathcal{O}_L(m) = \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(m)$, so $h^0(\mathcal{O}_L(m)) = m + 1$ and $h^1 = 0$ for $m \geq -1$. Therefore $P_L(m) = m + 1$.

For the smooth plane curve $C \subset \mathbb{P}^2$ of degree $d$: the arithmetic genus is $g = \binom{d-1}{2} = (d-1)(d-2)/2$ by the genus-degree formula. Riemann-Roch on $C$ gives $\chi(\mathcal{O}_C(m)) = dm + 1 - g$, so $P_C(m) = dm - (d-1)(d-2)/2 + 1 = dm - g + 1$.

For $d = 2$ (conic): $g = 0$, $P_C(m) = 2m + 1$. For $d = 3$ (cubic): $g = 1$, $P_C(m) = 3m$. For $d = 4$ (quartic): $g = 3$, $P_C(m) = 4m - 2$.

</details>

</aside>

By Fogarty 1968 *Amer. J. Math.* 90, the Hilbert scheme of $n$ points on a smooth quasi-projective surface is smooth of dimension $2n$:

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the tangent space to $\mathrm{Hilb}^P_X$ at a point $[Z]$ (with $Z \subset X$ smooth) is canonically isomorphic to $H^0(Z, N_{Z/X})$, where $N_{Z/X} = (\mathcal{I}_Z / \mathcal{I}_Z^2)^\vee$ is the normal sheaf.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the functor of points on $\mathrm{Spec}\,k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Zariski tangent space $T_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X$ is, by representability, the set of lifts of the point $[Z] : \mathrm{Spec}\,k \to \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X$ to $\mathrm{Spec}\,k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2) \to \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X$. By the universal-family property, such lifts correspond to flat families $\mathcal{Z} \subset X \times \mathrm{Spec}\,k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)$ over $\mathrm{Spec}\,k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)$ whose central fibre is $Z$.

Such a family is a *first-order deformation* of the closed embedding $Z \hookrightarrow X$. Locally on $X$ we can write $\mathcal{I}_Z = (f_1, \ldots, f_r)$ and a first-order deformation as $\mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{Z}} = (f_1 + \epsilon g_1, \ldots, f_r + \epsilon g_r)$ where the $g_i$ are sections of $\mathcal{O}_X$ modulo $\mathcal{I}_Z$. The flatness condition reduces (by the local criterion for flatness over $k[\epsilon]$) to the requirement that the perturbations $g_i \bmod \mathcal{I}_Z$ define a $k$-linear map $\mathcal{I}_Z / \mathcal{I}_Z^2 \to \mathcal{O}_Z$, equivalently a section of $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{O}_Z}(\mathcal{I}_Z / \mathcal{I}_Z^2, \mathcal{O}_Z)$.

For $Z \subset X$ a regular embedding (e.g., $Z$ smooth in $X$ smooth), the conormal sheaf $\mathcal{I}_Z / \mathcal{I}_Z^2$ is locally free of rank $\mathrm{codim}_X Z$, and its dual is the *normal sheaf* $N_{Z/X} = \mathcal{Hom}_{\mathcal{O}_Z}(\mathcal{I}_Z / \mathcal{I}_Z^2, \mathcal{O}_Z)$. Globalising the local calculation, the deformations of $Z$ in $X$ are in canonical bijection with global sections of the normal sheaf:

$$
T_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X \;\cong\; H^0(Z, N_{Z/X}).
$$

This is the foundational tangent-space identification (Grothendieck 1962, Mumford *Lectures* §22). $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

State the obstruction-space identification at $[Z] \in \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X$ and use it to derive the inequality $\dim_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X \geq h^0(N_{Z/X}) - h^1(N_{Z/X})$ together with the upper bound $\dim_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X \leq h^0(N_{Z/X})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the standard deformation-theoretic dimension bound — see Hartshorne *Deformation Theory* Theorem 6.2.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Continuing the exercise-3 calculation, *second-order obstructions* to lifting a first-order deformation $\xi \in H^0(N_{Z/X})$ over $k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)$ to a deformation over $k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^3)$ lie naturally in the *first cohomology of the normal sheaf*:

$$
\mathrm{obs}(\xi) \in H^1(Z, N_{Z/X}).
$$

(For $Z$ a local complete intersection in $X$, the obstruction theory is *perfect*, with $H^0$ as tangent space and $H^1$ as obstruction space; Grothendieck 1962, Illusie 1971.)

The standard *deformation-theoretic dimension bound* (Hartshorne *Deformation Theory* Theorem 6.2 — *cotangent complex inequality*) yields, for any closed point $[Z]$:

$$
h^0(N_{Z/X}) - h^1(N_{Z/X}) \;\leq\; \dim_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X \;\leq\; h^0(N_{Z/X}).
$$

The lower bound is the *expected dimension* — the dimension of the Hilbert scheme if all first-order deformations actually integrate to true deformations (no obstructions). The upper bound is the constraint that the tangent space has dimension $h^0(N_{Z/X})$, so the local dimension cannot exceed it.

When the obstruction space vanishes ($H^1(N_{Z/X}) = 0$), the Hilbert scheme is smooth at $[Z]$ of dimension exactly $h^0(N_{Z/X})$. When $H^1 \neq 0$, the Hilbert scheme can be singular or non-reduced at $[Z]$, and the discrepancy between $h^0(N_{Z/X}) - h^1(N_{Z/X})$ and the actual dimension records the *obstructedness* of the deformation problem.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).**

Compute the expected dimension of the Hilbert scheme of *plane conics* — degree-2 curves with arithmetic genus 0 — inside $\mathbb{P}^3$, using $h^0(N_{C/\mathbb{P}^3}) - h^1(N_{C/\mathbb{P}^3})$ for $C$ a smooth conic.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A smooth conic $C \subset \mathbb{P}^3$ lies in a plane $\mathbb{P}^2 \subset \mathbb{P}^3$. Use $N_{C/\mathbb{P}^3} = N_{C/\mathbb{P}^2} \oplus N_{\mathbb{P}^2/\mathbb{P}^3}|_C$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A smooth conic $C \subset \mathbb{P}^3$ lies in a unique plane $\Pi = \mathbb{P}^2 \subset \mathbb{P}^3$ (since 3 non-collinear points span a plane and a smooth conic has at least 3 distinct points). On $C \cong \mathbb{P}^1$:

(i) $N_{C/\Pi}$ is the normal sheaf of $C$ inside its containing plane. Since $C$ is a degree-2 divisor in $\Pi = \mathbb{P}^2$, the adjunction formula gives $N_{C/\Pi} = \mathcal{O}_\Pi(2)|_C = \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(4)$. Hence $h^0 = 5$, $h^1 = 0$.

(ii) $N_{\Pi/\mathbb{P}^3}|_C = \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^3}(1)|_C = \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(2)$. Hence $h^0 = 3$, $h^1 = 0$.

Combining via the short exact sequence $0 \to N_{C/\Pi} \to N_{C/\mathbb{P}^3} \to N_{\Pi/\mathbb{P}^3}|_C \to 0$: $h^0(N_{C/\mathbb{P}^3}) = 5 + 3 = 8$, $h^1 = 0$.

Expected dimension at $[C]$: $h^0 - h^1 = 8 - 0 = 8$. Since the obstruction space vanishes, the Hilbert scheme of plane conics in $\mathbb{P}^3$ is smooth of dimension 8 at every smooth-conic point.

Check via direct parameter count: a plane conic in $\mathbb{P}^3$ is specified by (a) a plane $\Pi \in (\mathbb{P}^3)^\vee$, contributing 3 dimensions, plus (b) a conic in $\Pi$, parametrised by $\mathbb{P}(\mathrm{Sym}^2(\Pi^\vee)) = \mathbb{P}^5$ of dimension 5. Total: $3 + 5 = 8$. ✓

</details>

</aside>

**B.** Grothendieck's 1962 existence theorem (FGA, Séminaire Bourbaki 221) constructs $\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$ for $X$ projective over the Noetherian base $S$, with a chosen relatively very ample line bundle. The theorem fails for non-projective $X$: even smoothness or properness alone is not enough, since the embedding into $\mathbb{P}^N$ via the very ample bundle is the input to the Grassmannian construction. Modern extensions to proper (rather than projective) and algebraic-space targets (Artin 1969) exist but require Artin's representability criteria.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Hilbert scheme of twisted cubics in $\mathbb{P}^3$ has *two* irreducible components, of dimensions 12 and 15.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Twisted cubics have Hilbert polynomial $3m + 1$. There is the "honest" component and an extra component containing plane cubics with an embedded point.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A twisted cubic $C \subset \mathbb{P}^3$ is the image of the Veronese embedding $\mathbb{P}^1 \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}^3$, $[s : t] \mapsto [s^3 : s^2 t : s t^2 : t^3]$. Its Hilbert polynomial is $P(m) = 3m + 1$, since $\chi(\mathcal{O}_C(m)) = 3m + 1$ via Riemann-Roch on $\mathbb{P}^1$.

*Component $H_1$ — honest twisted cubics.* The normal bundle $N_{C/\mathbb{P}^3}$ of a smooth twisted cubic is $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(5) \oplus \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(5)$, so $h^0(N_{C/\mathbb{P}^3}) = 12$, $h^1 = 0$. The locus of honest twisted cubics is a smooth irreducible component $H_1 \subset \mathrm{Hilb}^{3m+1}(\mathbb{P}^3)$ of dimension 12. (Alternative count: $\mathbb{P}^3$ admits a $\mathrm{PGL}_4$-action of dimension 15; a twisted cubic has a stabiliser isomorphic to $\mathrm{PGL}_2$ of dimension 3; the orbit dimension is $15 - 3 = 12$, matching $h^0(N)$.)

*Component $H_2$ — plane cubic plus point.* A second irreducible component $H_2$ contains subschemes of the form $C' \cup \{p\}$, where $C'$ is a plane cubic in some plane $\Pi \subset \mathbb{P}^3$ and $p$ is an isolated embedded point. The Hilbert polynomial of $C' \cup \{p\}$ is $\chi(\mathcal{O}_{C'}(m)) + \chi(\mathcal{O}_p(m)) = (3m + 1) + 0 = 3m + 1$. Dimension count: planes $\Pi \in (\mathbb{P}^3)^\vee$ contribute 3, plane cubics in $\Pi$ parametrised by $\mathbb{P}^9$ contribute 9, and the isolated point $p \in \mathbb{P}^3$ contributes 3 — total $3 + 9 + 3 = 15$.

The two components $H_1, H_2$ meet along the locus of *nodal cubics with an embedded point at the node*, of common dimension 11. The full Hilbert scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^{3m+1}(\mathbb{P}^3)$ has two components of dimensions 12 and 15 — it is *connected* (Hartshorne 1966) but *reducible*.

This example, due to Piene-Schlessinger 1985 *Amer. J. Math.* 107, is the canonical illustration that Hilbert schemes can have unexpected extra components even in small-degree cases.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).**

Prove Fogarty's theorem: the Hilbert scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ of $n$ points on a smooth quasi-projective surface $X$ is smooth and irreducible of dimension $2n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show $H^1(Z, N_{Z/X}) = 0$ for every length-$n$ zero-dimensional subscheme $Z \subset X$, then check connectedness.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $Z \subset X$ be a length-$n$ zero-dimensional closed subscheme. The conormal sequence $\mathcal{I}_Z / \mathcal{I}_Z^2$ is supported on the finite scheme $Z$; the normal sheaf $N_{Z/X} = (\mathcal{I}_Z / \mathcal{I}_Z^2)^\vee$ is a coherent sheaf on $Z$.

*Vanishing of $H^1$.* Since $Z$ is zero-dimensional, $H^i(Z, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for every coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ and every $i \geq 1$ (Grothendieck vanishing: cohomology of a Noetherian scheme is zero above its dimension). In particular $H^1(Z, N_{Z/X}) = 0$.

*Tangent space.* By the exercise-3 calculation, $T_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^n(X) = H^0(Z, N_{Z/X})$. For $Z \subset X$ a length-$n$ subscheme in a surface, $\mathcal{I}_Z / \mathcal{I}_Z^2$ has rank 2 at each point (the codimension is 2), and $h^0(N_{Z/X}) = 2n$ by length-additivity: $\dim_k \Gamma(Z, N_{Z/X}) = \mathrm{length}(N_{Z/X}) = 2 \cdot \mathrm{length}(\mathcal{O}_Z) = 2n$.

*Smoothness.* From the deformation-theoretic bounds (exercise 4): $\dim_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^n(X) \leq h^0(N_{Z/X}) = 2n$ and $\dim_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^n(X) \geq h^0 - h^1 = 2n - 0 = 2n$. So $\dim_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^n(X) = 2n$, and the tangent-space dimension matches the local dimension, so $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ is smooth at $[Z]$.

*Irreducibility.* The locus of $n$ distinct points $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)^\circ$ is an open dense subscheme isomorphic to the *configuration space* $X^n_{\Delta} / S_n$, an open dense subscheme of the symmetric product $\mathrm{Sym}^n(X)$. Since $X$ is irreducible and $X^n$ is irreducible, $\mathrm{Sym}^n(X)$ is irreducible, hence $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)^\circ$ is irreducible. Smoothness of $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ then implies that the closure of the dense open is the entire Hilbert scheme, and irreducibility extends from the open part. So $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ is smooth and irreducible of dimension $2n$. $\square$

This is Fogarty's 1968 result (*Amer. J. Math.* 90, 511–521). It fails in dimensions $\geq 3$: Cheah 1996 showed that $\mathrm{Hilb}^4(\mathbb{A}^3)$ is reducible with two components, and the situation deteriorates in higher dimensions.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### Grothendieck's existence theorem for Hilb^P_{X/S}

**Theorem (Grothendieck 1962, *Séminaire Bourbaki* 221).** *Let $S$ be a Noetherian scheme and $X \to S$ a projective $S$-scheme with relatively very ample line bundle $\mathcal{O}_X(1)$. For every numerical polynomial $P \in \mathbb{Q}[t]$, the Hilbert functor $\underline{\mathrm{Hilb}}^P_{X/S}$ is representable by a projective $S$-scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$, with a universal flat family $\mathcal{Z} \subset X \times_S \mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$.*

The construction (sketched in the Key-theorem section) embeds $\mathrm{Hilb}^P$ into a Grassmannian of subspaces $W \subset H^0(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^N}(m_0))$ via the *uniform $m_0$-regularity bound* (Mumford-Castelnuovo): there exists a polynomial-dependent integer $m_0$ such that every closed subscheme $Z \subset \mathbb{P}^N$ with $P_Z = P$ has $m_0$-regular ideal sheaf. The regularity bound was made effective by Bayer-Mumford 1993 and Galligo-Giusti 1985, with $m_0$ doubly exponential in $\deg P$ in the worst case.

**Total Hilbert scheme.** Summing over all Hilbert polynomials,

$$
\mathrm{Hilb}_{X/S} \;=\; \coprod_{P} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S},
$$

is a disjoint union of projective $S$-schemes, parametrising *all* closed subschemes of $X$ flat over $S$. The grading by Hilbert polynomial is finer than the grading by degree-and-genus.

**Connectedness.** Hartshorne 1966 *Publ. Math. IHES* 29 proved that for fixed Hilbert polynomial, $\mathrm{Hilb}^P(\mathbb{P}^N)$ is *connected*. The proof — *Hartshorne's smoothing theorem* — degenerates any closed subscheme to a chosen "lex-most" subscheme (a Borel-fixed monomial ideal) using Gröbner-basis deformations. The argument is one of the early uses of *generic-initial-ideal* methods, foreshadowing the modern Gröbner-fan technology.

**Components.** The Hilbert scheme is generally *not* irreducible. Beyond the Piene-Schlessinger 1985 twisted-cubic example (exercise 7), Mumford 1962 *Amer. J. Math.* 84 constructed Hilbert schemes that are *generically non-reduced*, exhibiting infinitesimal arithmetic obstructed deformations. Vakil's 2006 *Murphy's law* theorem extended this: every singularity type appearing on a finite-type scheme over $\mathbb{Z}$ appears on some component of some Hilbert scheme.

### Tangent and obstruction spaces via normal sheaf cohomology

**Theorem (Grothendieck 1962; Mumford 1966 *Lectures* §22).** *Let $Z \subset X$ be a closed subscheme corresponding to $[Z] \in \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X$. The Zariski tangent space and obstruction space at $[Z]$ are identified with cohomology of the normal sheaf $N_{Z/X} = \mathcal{Hom}_{\mathcal{O}_Z}(\mathcal{I}_Z / \mathcal{I}_Z^2, \mathcal{O}_Z)$:*

$$
T_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X \;\cong\; H^0(Z, N_{Z/X}), \qquad \mathrm{Obs}_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X \;\subseteq\; H^1(Z, N_{Z/X}).
$$

The first identification holds for any closed embedding. The second identification (obstruction space) holds when $Z \subset X$ is a *local complete intersection*; for general $Z$ the obstruction space is the cotangent complex cohomology $\mathrm{Ext}^1(\mathbb{L}_{Z/X}, \mathcal{O}_Z)$ (Illusie 1971 *LNM* 239).

The standard dimension bound (Hartshorne *Deformation Theory* Theorem 6.2) is the *Schlessinger inequality*:

$$
h^0(N_{Z/X}) - h^1(N_{Z/X}) \;\leq\; \dim_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X \;\leq\; h^0(N_{Z/X}).
$$

The expected dimension $h^0 - h^1$ is realised when the obstruction theory is unobstructed; the discrepancy $h^0 - \dim_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X$ is the *expected number of obstructed second-order deformations*.

**Worked normal-sheaf computations.** For a smooth complete intersection $Z = V(f_1, \ldots, f_c) \subset X$ with $X$ smooth and each $f_i \in H^0(X, \mathcal{O}_X(d_i))$:

$$
N_{Z/X} \;\cong\; \bigoplus_{i=1}^c \mathcal{O}_X(d_i)|_Z.
$$

For a smooth divisor $D \subset X$ given by a single equation of degree $d$: $N_{D/X} = \mathcal{O}_X(d)|_D$. For a smooth twisted cubic in $\mathbb{P}^3$: $N_{C/\mathbb{P}^3} = \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(5) \oplus \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(5)$, as used in exercise 7.

**Application to expected-dimension counts.** The dimension formula for $\mathcal{M}_g$ via the Hilbert scheme uses normal-sheaf cohomology of tri-canonically embedded curves $C \subset \mathbb{P}^{5g-6}$. The relevant calculation (Mumford *Lectures* §27): $h^0(N_{C/\mathbb{P}^{5g-6}}) = 3g - 3 + \dim \mathrm{PGL}_{5g-5}$, and after subtracting the $\mathrm{PGL}$-orbit dimension, $\dim \mathcal{M}_g = 3g - 3$. This is the rigorous derivation of Riemann's 1857 count.

### Worked example — Hilbert scheme of points on a surface (Fogarty)

**Theorem (Fogarty 1968 *Amer. J. Math.* 90).** *Let $X$ be a smooth quasi-projective surface over an algebraically closed field. For each $n \geq 1$, the Hilbert scheme of $n$ points $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X) := \mathrm{Hilb}^{P_n}(X)$ (with $P_n$ the constant polynomial $n$) is smooth and irreducible of dimension $2n$.*

The proof is sketched in exercise 8. The key inputs: (i) zero-dimensional cohomology vanishing $H^1(Z, \mathcal{F}) = 0$; (ii) the rank of the conormal sheaf equals the codimension 2; (iii) length-additivity $h^0(N_{Z/X}) = 2n$; (iv) irreducibility from the dense open $\mathrm{Sym}^n(X)^\circ$.

**Structure of $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(\mathbb{A}^2)$.** The Hilbert scheme of $n$ points on the affine plane is a rich combinatorial-geometric object:

- *Symmetric-group action.* $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(\mathbb{A}^2)$ carries a $\mathbb{T}^2 = (\mathbb{C}^\times)^2$-torus action via scaling of the plane. The fixed points are the *monomial ideals* $I_\lambda \subset k[x, y]$ indexed by partitions $\lambda \vdash n$ — there are $p(n)$ of them, where $p(n)$ is the partition counting function.

- *Bialynicki-Birula stratification.* The torus fixed points $\{[\lambda] : \lambda \vdash n\}$ stratify $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(\mathbb{A}^2)$ into affine cells of explicit dimension; the Poincaré polynomial is

$$
P(\mathrm{Hilb}^n(\mathbb{A}^2); q) \;=\; \sum_{\lambda \vdash n} q^{2(n - \ell(\lambda))},
$$

where $\ell(\lambda)$ is the number of parts (Ellingsrud-Strømme 1987 *Invent. Math.* 87).

- *Generating function (Göttsche 1990 *Math. Ann.* 286).* For a smooth surface $X$, the generating function of Betti numbers of $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ is

$$
\sum_{n \geq 0} P(\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X); q) \, t^n \;=\; \prod_{m \geq 1} \prod_{i = 0}^{4} (1 - q^{2(m-1) + i} t^m)^{(-1)^{i+1} b_i(X)},
$$

— an Euler-product expression in the Betti numbers $b_i(X)$. This is the algebraic-geometric incarnation of the *Vafa-Witten* formula for instanton partition functions on surfaces.

- *Nakajima 1997 *Ann. of Math.* 145.* The total cohomology $\bigoplus_n H^*(\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X))$ carries a representation of the *Heisenberg algebra* on the lattice $H^*(X)$. Nakajima's construction realises Fock-space representations geometrically and underlies modern *enumerative geometry of surfaces*. The construction is one of the great geometric representation-theory results of the 1990s.

### Compactification of symmetric products and the Hilbert-Chow morphism

**Hilbert-Chow morphism.** For a smooth quasi-projective surface $X$ (and more generally for smooth varieties of any dimension), there is a natural morphism

$$
\mu_X : \mathrm{Hilb}^n(X) \;\longrightarrow\; \mathrm{Sym}^n(X)
$$

sending a length-$n$ subscheme $Z \subset X$ to its *support cycle* $\sum_{p \in Z} \mathrm{length}(\mathcal{O}_{Z, p}) \cdot [p] \in \mathrm{Sym}^n(X)$. The map $\mu_X$ is proper and, in the smooth-surface case, a *resolution of singularities*: $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ is smooth and the singular locus of $\mathrm{Sym}^n(X)$ is the *small diagonal* where points collide.

**Crepant resolution.** Fogarty 1968 + Beauville 1983 showed that for a smooth projective surface $X$ with vanishing canonical class, the resolution $\mu_X : \mathrm{Hilb}^n(X) \to \mathrm{Sym}^n(X)$ is *crepant* (preserves the canonical class). When $X$ is a K3 surface, $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(K3)$ is a holomorphic-symplectic *hyperkähler manifold* of dimension $2n$ — one of the two known families of holomorphic-symplectic manifolds (the other being generalised Kummer varieties; Beauville-Bogomolov classification).

**Cycle-theoretic comparison.** The Hilbert-Chow morphism is the algebro-geometric incarnation of the topological statement that compactifying configurations of $n$ points by allowing collisions naturally lands in the symmetric product. The Hilbert scheme records the *direction of collision* via the tangent vector at the colliding point — analogous to blow-up in differential geometry.

**Higher-dimensional generalisations.** In dimensions $\geq 3$, the Hilbert scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ is generally *singular* and not isomorphic to a desingularisation of $\mathrm{Sym}^n(X)$. Identifying components, dimensions, and singularity types is a hard open problem. The *punctual Hilbert scheme* $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(\mathbb{A}^d, 0)$ — subschemes of length $n$ supported at the origin — was systematically studied by Iarrobino 1972 and is the subject of ongoing work.

**G-Hilbert scheme.** For $G \subset \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{C})$ a finite subgroup acting on $\mathbb{C}^2$, the *$G$-Hilbert scheme* $G\text{-}\mathrm{Hilb}(\mathbb{C}^2)$ parametrises $G$-invariant zero-dimensional subschemes of length $|G|$. Ito-Nakamura 1996 *Topology* 38 proved that $G\text{-}\mathrm{Hilb}(\mathbb{C}^2)$ is the *minimal resolution of the Kleinian singularity* $\mathbb{C}^2 / G$, establishing the *McKay correspondence* in dimension 2 geometrically. Bridgeland-King-Reid 2001 *J. Amer. Math. Soc.* 14 extended to the 3-dimensional crepant-resolution case.

### Quot scheme generalisation

**Quot functor.** The *Quot scheme* $\mathrm{Quot}^P_{\mathcal{F}/X/S}$ (Grothendieck 1961, FGA 221 §3) generalises the Hilbert scheme by parametrising quotients $\mathcal{F} \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{G}$ of a fixed coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$, with $\mathcal{G}$ flat over the base and of fixed Hilbert polynomial. The Hilbert scheme recovers the case $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{O}_X$ and $\mathcal{G} = \mathcal{O}_Z$ for a subscheme $Z$.

**Existence theorem.** For $X$ projective over Noetherian $S$ with relatively ample line bundle, and $\mathcal{F}$ a coherent sheaf on $X$, $\mathrm{Quot}^P_{\mathcal{F}/X/S}$ is a projective $S$-scheme — the proof parallels the Hilbert case via Grassmannian embeddings.

**Applications.** Quot schemes underlie the construction of *moduli of vector bundles* (Seshadri 1967, Gieseker 1977) and *moduli of sheaves on a variety* (Maruyama 1976, Simpson 1994). The Quot-scheme compactification of a moduli problem is the technical input to *Hilbert-Mumford stability* analysis and *Gieseker stability* in [04.10.06] moduli of vector bundles.

### Donaldson-Thomas invariants

**DT invariants (Donaldson-Thomas 1998).** On a Calabi-Yau threefold $X$, the Hilbert scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^P(X)$ — parametrising 1- and 0-dimensional subschemes — has a *symmetric obstruction theory* (Behrend 2009 *Ann. of Math.* 170), giving a *virtual fundamental class* $[\mathrm{Hilb}^P(X)]^{\mathrm{vir}}$ of pure dimension 0. Integrating $1$ against this class gives the *Donaldson-Thomas invariant* $\mathrm{DT}^P(X) \in \mathbb{Z}$.

**MNOP conjecture (Maulik-Nekrasov-Okounkov-Pandharipande 2006).** DT invariants of a Calabi-Yau threefold equal Gromov-Witten invariants after a generating-function-level correspondence. The conjecture was largely resolved by Pandharipande-Thomas 2009-13 for the toric and projective cases, with PT and BPS refinements forming the modern *enumerative geometry of threefolds*.

**Synthesis.** The Hilbert scheme is the foundational representability object of moduli theory: it is the universal parameter space for closed subschemes of a projective variety with given Hilbert polynomial, and its representability is the bridge between classical classification problems and the modern scheme-theoretic framework. The central insight is that tangent vectors at $[Z]$ are sections of the normal sheaf and obstructions live in its first cohomology, which appears again in [04.10.20] deformation theory as the inputs to the cotangent complex calculus. This is exactly the framework that builds toward [04.10.01] moduli of curves: $\mathcal{M}_g$ is constructed as a GIT quotient of an open subscheme of a Hilbert scheme, and the dimension calculation $\dim \mathcal{M}_g = 3g - 3$ is a normal-sheaf computation on tri-canonically embedded curves. Putting these together with the Hilbert-Chow morphism, the symmetric product $\mathrm{Sym}^n(X)$ is identified with the image of $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ under the support-cycle map, and the foundational reason Hilbert schemes give crepant resolutions of symmetric products on surfaces — Fogarty's smoothness and Beauville's hyperkähler examples — generalises in the McKay correspondence and in the DT/GW correspondence on Calabi-Yau threefolds. The pattern recurs through Quot schemes, $G$-Hilbert schemes, and the modern enumerative geometry of surfaces and threefolds.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Grothendieck's existence theorem — sketch).** *The Hilbert functor $\underline{\mathrm{Hilb}}^P_{X/S}$ is representable by a projective $S$-scheme.*

*Proof.* Sketched in the Key-theorem section. The four ingredients: (i) uniform $m_0$-regularity bound for closed subschemes with fixed Hilbert polynomial (Mumford-Castelnuovo regularity); (ii) embedding into a Grassmannian via $W = H^0(\mathcal{I}_Z(m_0)) \subset V = H^0(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^N}(m_0))$; (iii) the image is closed in the Grassmannian, cut out by rank conditions on multiplication maps in higher degrees; (iv) flatness of the universal subscheme via cohomology-and-base-change. Full details in *FGA Explained* §5.5 (Nitsure) and Hartshorne *Deformation Theory* §1.2. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (tangent space identification).** *For $[Z] \in \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X$, $T_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X \cong H^0(Z, N_{Z/X})$.*

*Proof.* By representability, $T_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X = \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X(\mathrm{Spec}\,k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2))$ — the set of $k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)$-points lifting $[Z]$. Such a point is a flat family $\mathcal{Z} \subset X \times \mathrm{Spec}\,k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)$ with central fibre $Z$. Locally writing $\mathcal{I}_Z = (f_1, \ldots, f_r)$ and $\mathcal{I}_{\mathcal{Z}} = (f_1 + \epsilon g_1, \ldots, f_r + \epsilon g_r)$, the flatness condition reduces (local criterion for flatness over $k[\epsilon]$) to: the perturbations $g_i \bmod \mathcal{I}_Z$ define a $k$-linear map $\varphi : \mathcal{I}_Z / \mathcal{I}_Z^2 \to \mathcal{O}_Z$. Such a map is exactly a section of $\mathcal{Hom}_{\mathcal{O}_Z}(\mathcal{I}_Z / \mathcal{I}_Z^2, \mathcal{O}_Z) = N_{Z/X}$. Globalising via Čech cocycles gives $T_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^P_X = H^0(Z, N_{Z/X})$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Fogarty smoothness).** *For $X$ a smooth quasi-projective surface and $n \geq 1$, $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ is smooth and irreducible of dimension $2n$.*

*Proof.* Proved in detail in exercise 8. Outline: (i) $\dim Z = 0$ forces $H^1(Z, N_{Z/X}) = 0$ by Grothendieck vanishing; (ii) $h^0(N_{Z/X}) = 2n$ by length-additivity and $\mathrm{rk}(\mathcal{I}_Z/\mathcal{I}_Z^2) = 2$; (iii) Schlessinger inequality forces $\dim_{[Z]} \mathrm{Hilb}^n(X) = 2n$ matching the tangent dimension, so smoothness holds; (iv) irreducibility from the dense open $\mathrm{Sym}^n(X)^\circ$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Hilbert-Chow as resolution).** *For $X$ a smooth quasi-projective surface, the Hilbert-Chow morphism $\mu_X : \mathrm{Hilb}^n(X) \to \mathrm{Sym}^n(X)$ is a resolution of singularities.*

*Proof.* The morphism $\mu_X$ is proper (composition of proper morphisms) and surjective (every cycle is the support of some subscheme — generically distinct points). Over the open locus of $n$ distinct points, $\mu_X$ is an isomorphism. The singular locus of $\mathrm{Sym}^n(X)$ is the *small diagonals* where points coincide; $\mu_X^{-1}$ over each diagonal records the *tangent direction* of approach in addition to the support, so $\mu_X$ is a substantive blow-up over the diagonals. By Fogarty $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ is smooth, so $\mu_X$ is a desingularisation. (Crepancy in the K3 case follows from a direct canonical-class computation; see Beauville 1983 *Variétés Kähleriennes dont la première classe de Chern est nulle*, J. Differential Geom. 18.) $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Moduli of curves [04.10.01].** $\mathcal{M}_g$ is constructed as a GIT quotient of an open subscheme of $\mathrm{Hilb}^P(\mathbb{P}^{5g-6})$, where $P(m) = 6(g-1)m - g + 1$ is the Hilbert polynomial of a tri-canonically embedded smooth genus-$g$ curve. The dimension count $\dim \mathcal{M}_g = 3g - 3$ is a normal-sheaf computation on the Hilbert scheme, subtracting $\dim \mathrm{PGL}_{5g-5}$ for the embedding ambiguity. Hilbert-scheme machinery is the technical input to Mumford's 1965 GIT construction.

- **Geometric invariant theory [04.10.02].** GIT acts on a Hilbert scheme: the moduli problem becomes "quotient of $\mathrm{Hilb}^P(\mathbb{P}^N)$ by $\mathrm{PGL}_{N+1}$." Stability of a subscheme $[Z] \in \mathrm{Hilb}^P$ under $\mathrm{PGL}$ is then computed by the Hilbert-Mumford numerical criterion on tangent-space weights. The two units form a pair: Hilbert scheme is the *parameter* and GIT is the *quotient*.

- **Scheme [04.02.01].** The Hilbert scheme is itself a projective scheme — Grothendieck's existence theorem produces it within the category of schemes. Representability is the formal property that makes the Hilbert functor "schemey" in the precise scheme-theoretic sense.

- **Sheaf cohomology [04.03.01].** Tangent and obstruction spaces are normal-sheaf cohomology groups $H^0(Z, N_{Z/X})$ and $H^1(Z, N_{Z/X})$. The cohomological computation of expected dimensions is the canonical use of sheaf-cohomology techniques in deformation theory.

- **Projective scheme [04.02.03].** Grothendieck's existence theorem produces $\mathrm{Hilb}^P_{X/S}$ as a projective $S$-scheme, parametrising closed subschemes of a projective ambient $X$. The output category — projective schemes — matches the input category, closing the loop on the representability question.

- **Hilbert-Mumford numerical criterion [04.10.03].** GIT acting on the Hilbert scheme by $\mathrm{PGL}_{N+1}$ is the standard setup for moduli construction: stability of $[Z] \in \mathrm{Hilb}^P$ is tested by the numerical criterion on tangent-space weights of the projective embedding. Mumford's construction of $\mathcal{M}_g$ as a GIT quotient of $\mathrm{Hilb}^P$ runs entirely through the numerical criterion.

- **Kempf-Ness theorem and GIT-symplectic dictionary [04.10.04].** The GIT quotients of the Hilbert scheme by $\mathrm{PGL}$ acquire a Kähler structure through the Kempf-Ness dictionary, identifying the algebraic quotient with the symplectic reduction at moment-zero. This Kähler structure on moduli built from Hilbert schemes is the technical input to Atiyah-Bott's cohomology calculations.

- **Moduli of vector bundles on a curve and slope stability [04.10.06].** The moduli of vector bundles is built as a GIT quotient of the Quot scheme of fixed-Chern-character quotients of $\mathcal{O}_C^{\oplus h}$ — the Quot scheme is a generalised Hilbert scheme parametrising quotient sheaves rather than subschemes. Both moduli problems share the same architectural pattern: parameter Hilbert/Quot scheme modulo $\mathrm{PGL}$.

- **Kirwan stratification of the unstable locus [04.10.08].** The Hilbert scheme carries a natural $\mathrm{PGL}$-action whose unstable locus admits the Kirwan stratification. Kirwan-style equivariant cohomology computations on $\mathrm{Hilb}^P$ then translate into cohomology of the GIT moduli quotient via Kirwan surjectivity.

- **Variation of GIT (VGIT) [04.10.09].** Varying the linearisation on $\mathrm{Hilb}^P$ produces a chamber-wall family of moduli compactifications. The Hassett alternative compactifications of $\overline{\mathcal{M}}_{g, n}$ via weighted stability arise this way, and Thaddeus's worked-example chains of birational flips on moduli of Bradlow pairs realise the wall-crossing picture explicitly on Hilbert/Quot-type parameter spaces.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Alexander Grothendieck introduced the Hilbert scheme in his 1962 Séminaire Bourbaki talk *Les schémas de Hilbert* [ref: TODO_REF Grothendieck 1962], part of his foundational series *Fondements de la Géométrie Algébrique* (FGA). The construction was Grothendieck's first systematic representability result for a moduli problem: previously, families of curves or surfaces were studied via formal parameter spaces or via Teichmüller-style analytic constructions, and the Hilbert scheme gave the first universal *algebraic* parameter space.

The conceptual breakthrough was the *functor of points* perspective: a moduli problem is a functor from schemes to sets, and the question "does this moduli problem admit a parameter space?" becomes "is this functor representable?" Grothendieck's existence theorem was the first major positive answer in this framework, predating his representability criteria for stacks (which came in the 1970s with Artin and the formalisation of algebraic stacks).

David Mumford's 1966 *Lectures on Curves on an Algebraic Surface* [ref: TODO_REF Mumford 1966] (Princeton Annals of Math. Studies 59) was the first textbook exposition of the Hilbert scheme, providing the now-standard treatment via Castelnuovo-Mumford regularity. Mumford treated the Hilbert scheme of $n$ points on a surface and developed the tangent-space-via-normal-sheaf calculation in detail. The book remains a canonical reference 60 years later.

The dimensional study of $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ for surfaces $X$ began with John Fogarty's 1968 paper *Algebraic families on an algebraic surface* [ref: TODO_REF Fogarty 1968] (*Amer. J. Math.* 90, 511–521). Fogarty proved that $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ is smooth of dimension $2n$ when $X$ is a smooth quasi-projective surface — an elegant cohomological argument now standard. The smoothness fails in dimensions $\geq 3$ (Cheah 1996; Iarrobino 1972), making the surface case especially nice for further structural study.

Robin Hartshorne's 1966 paper *Connectedness of the Hilbert scheme* [ref: TODO_REF Hartshorne 1966] (*Publ. Math. IHES* 29) proved that $\mathrm{Hilb}^P(\mathbb{P}^N)$ is *connected* — every two closed subschemes with the same Hilbert polynomial can be connected by a flat family. Hartshorne's proof used Gröbner-basis-style degenerations to a monomial *lex-most* ideal; the techniques foreshadowed the modern theory of generic initial ideals and computational commutative algebra.

The 1980s and 90s saw the geometric study of $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ for surfaces $X$ become a central topic:

- *Ellingsrud-Strømme 1987* (*Invent. Math.* 87): computed the Betti numbers of $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(\mathbb{P}^2)$ via the torus action and Białynicki-Birula stratification.
- *Göttsche 1990* (*Math. Ann.* 286): derived the Euler-product formula for the generating function of Betti numbers of $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ for any smooth surface $X$.
- *Beauville 1983* (*J. Differential Geom.* 18): showed that $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(K3)$ is a holomorphic-symplectic hyperkähler manifold, one of the two known families of irreducible hyperkähler manifolds (the other being generalised Kummer varieties).
- *Nakajima 1997* (*Ann. of Math.* 145): realised a Heisenberg-algebra representation on $\bigoplus_n H^*(\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X))$, a foundational result of modern geometric representation theory.
- *Lehn 1999* and *Lehn-Sorger 2003*: developed the calculus of universal classes on $\mathrm{Hilb}^n(X)$ and computed cohomology rings via vertex algebras.

The deformation-theoretic study of the Hilbert scheme — the calculation of tangent and obstruction spaces via normal sheaf cohomology — was systematised by Grothendieck 1962, Mumford 1966, and Illusie 1971 (*LNM* 239) [ref: TODO_REF Illusie 1971]. Illusie's *cotangent complex* gave a derived enhancement of the normal-sheaf calculus that handles non-l.c.i. subschemes uniformly. Hartshorne's 2010 textbook *Deformation Theory* (GTM 257) [ref: TODO_REF Hartshorne 2010] is the modern definitive reference.

The connection to enumerative geometry of Calabi-Yau threefolds began with Simon Donaldson and Richard Thomas in 1998 (*The geometric universe*, Oxford), who introduced *Donaldson-Thomas invariants* as integrals over the Hilbert scheme of 1- and 0-dimensional subschemes. Behrend's 2009 paper *Donaldson-Thomas type invariants via microlocal geometry* (*Ann. of Math.* 170) gave the modern symmetric-obstruction-theory framework. The MNOP conjecture (Maulik-Nekrasov-Okounkov-Pandharipande 2006) relating DT invariants to Gromov-Witten invariants was substantially proved by Pandharipande-Thomas in 2009-13. The Hilbert scheme is the foundational object — once you have the Hilbert scheme, you have a moduli space, and once you have a moduli space, you can integrate cohomology classes over it.

The modern theory has extended in several directions: $G$-Hilbert schemes (Ito-Nakamura 1996, Bridgeland-King-Reid 2001) for the McKay correspondence; Hilbert schemes of points on higher-dimensional varieties (still poorly understood for $\dim X \geq 3$); virtual Hilbert schemes (Manolache 2012 *J. Algebraic Geom.* 21) for non-flat families; Hilbert schemes in derived algebraic geometry (Toën-Vaquié 2007); and Hilbert schemes of points on noncommutative surfaces (Nevins-Stafford 2007). The 1962 construction remains the foundation.
