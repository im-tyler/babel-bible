## Intuition [Beginner]

Geometric invariant theory and symplectic reduction look like two unrelated constructions. GIT lives in algebraic geometry: a reductive group $G$ acts on a variety, and the quotient is built from invariant polynomials. Symplectic reduction lives in physics: a compact group $K$ acts on a phase space preserving the symplectic form, the moment map records the conserved charges, and the reduced phase space collapses to the moment-zero level set divided by $K$. Two different worlds — algebra and mechanics.

The Kempf-Ness theorem of 1979 says the two worlds are the same world. When the complex group $G$ is the complexification of its maximal compact subgroup $K$ (so $G = K_{\mathbb{C}}$, as for $\mathrm{GL}_n = U(n)_{\mathbb{C}}$ or $\mathrm{SL}_n = SU(n)_{\mathbb{C}}$), the algebraic GIT quotient and the symplectic reduction at level zero produce identical spaces. The bridge runs through a single function: the length-squared of a vector, viewed as a Morse-theoretic energy whose minima sit exactly where the moment map vanishes.

This identification reorganises moduli theory. Polynomial invariants and phase-space conserved charges become two languages for one structure, and every theorem proved on one side translates to the other.

## Visual [Beginner]

The picture shows a complex vector space with a reductive group acting by linear transformations. The orbit of a vector traces out a curved surface; the closure of that orbit either contains the origin (in which case the vector is unstable) or stays bounded away from zero (in which case the vector is semistable). Inside each closed orbit there is a unique point of smallest length, and that point is exactly where the moment map vanishes.

![A complex vector space with a reductive group action; orbits drawn as curves, with the minimum-length point of a closed orbit marked at the moment-zero level set.](/img/kempf-ness-git-symplectic-dictionary-placeholder.svg)

The picture compresses the entire dictionary into one image: the algebraic GIT quotient counts closed orbits, the symplectic reduction counts moment-zero points modulo $K$, and the minimum-length characterisation matches them point-by-point.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A concrete instance lives on $V = \mathbb{C}^2$ with the action of $G = \mathbb{C}^\times$ by scalar multiplication, $t \cdot (z_1, z_2) = (t z_1, t z_2)$. The maximal compact subgroup is $K = U(1)$ — the unit circle inside $\mathbb{C}^\times$.

The algebraic side: the ring of invariant polynomials under $\mathbb{C}^\times$ scaling has only constants (any homogeneous invariant has weight zero, so the polynomial must have degree zero). The GIT quotient is one point. Stable orbits are the $\mathbb{C}^\times$-orbits of nonzero vectors; the unique unstable point is the origin.

The symplectic side: the moment map for the $U(1)$ action on $\mathbb{C}^2$ with the standard Kähler form is

$$
\mu(z_1, z_2) = \tfrac{1}{2}(|z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2) - c
$$

for a chosen constant $c$. Setting $c = \tfrac{1}{2}$ gives the moment-zero level set as the unit 3-sphere $S^3 = \{(z_1, z_2) : |z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 = 1\}$. Dividing by the $U(1)$ rotation $(z_1, z_2) \mapsto (e^{i\theta} z_1, e^{i\theta} z_2)$ gives the Hopf quotient $S^3 / U(1) = \mathbb{CP}^1 = S^2$.

Step 1. Identify the closed $\mathbb{C}^\times$-orbits in $\mathbb{C}^2 \setminus \{0\}$. Each orbit is a complex line minus the origin.

Step 2. Find the minimum-length vector in each orbit. On the line $\mathbb{C}^\times \cdot (z_1, z_2)$, the length-squared $|t z_1|^2 + |t z_2|^2 = |t|^2 (|z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2)$ is minimised when $|t| = 1/\sqrt{|z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2}$, giving a vector of unit length.

Step 3. Verify this unit vector sits on $\mu^{-1}(0) = S^3$. Two minimum-length vectors in the same orbit differ by a phase $e^{i\theta} \in U(1)$, exactly the $K$-ambiguity.

What this tells us: GIT-stable orbits are parametrised by their minimum-length representative on the unit sphere modulo the $U(1)$ rotation, giving the same $\mathbb{CP}^1$ both algebraically and symplectically.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $G$ be a complex reductive algebraic group acting linearly on a finite-dimensional complex vector space $V$. Fix a maximal compact subgroup $K \subset G$ — for $G = \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ this is $K = U(n)$; for $G = \mathrm{SL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ it is $K = SU(n)$. Equip $V$ with a $K$-invariant Hermitian inner product $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ (averaging over $K$ produces one if not given). The induced Kähler form on $V$ is

$$
\omega = \tfrac{i}{2} \sum_j dz_j \wedge d\bar z_j,
$$

and the $K$-action is Hamiltonian with moment map $\mu : V \to \mathfrak{k}^*$ characterised by

$$
\langle \mu(v), \xi \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} \langle i \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle, \qquad \xi \in \mathfrak{k},
$$

where $\rho : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathrm{End}(V)$ is the differential of the action.

**Definition (Kempf-Ness-stable, semistable, polystable).** A vector $v \in V \setminus \{0\}$ is:

- **Semistable** (in the Kempf-Ness sense) if $0 \notin \overline{G \cdot v}$ — the closure of the orbit avoids the origin. Equivalently, there exists a $G$-invariant homogeneous polynomial $f$ of positive degree with $f(v) \neq 0$.

- **Polystable** if $v$ is semistable and the orbit $G \cdot v$ is closed in $V \setminus \{0\}$.

- **Stable** if $v$ is polystable and the stabiliser $G_v$ is finite.

A vector is **unstable** if $0 \in \overline{G \cdot v}$ — i.e., if it is not semistable.

**Definition (the norm-square functional).** The function

$$
f(v) := \|\mu(v)\|^2 = \langle \mu(v), \mu(v) \rangle_{\mathfrak{k}^*}
$$

(where the inner product on $\mathfrak{k}^*$ is induced by a fixed $\mathrm{Ad}(K)$-invariant inner product on $\mathfrak{k}$, for example the Killing form on the semisimple part plus any positive form on the centre) is the **moment-map norm-square**. It is real-valued, non-negative, $K$-invariant, and real-analytic in $v$.

**Definition (symplectic reduction at level zero).** The **symplectic reduction** of the $K$-action on $V$ at moment-map level zero is the topological quotient

$$
V /\!/_0 K := \mu^{-1}(0) / K
$$

— the moment-zero locus modulo the residual $K$-action. When $0$ is a regular value of $\mu$ and $K$ acts freely on $\mu^{-1}(0)$, the quotient is a smooth Kähler manifold of complex dimension $\dim_{\mathbb{C}} V - \dim_{\mathbb{C}} G$ (Marsden-Weinstein 1974 — see [05.04.02]).

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *GIT-semistable is the same as Hilbert-Mumford-semistable.* The two definitions agree: a vector $v$ avoids having $0$ in its orbit closure iff every one-parameter subgroup $\lambda(t)$ with $\lim_{t \to 0} \lambda(t) \cdot v$ existing yields a non-negative Hilbert-Mumford weight (this is the equivalence due to Mumford 1965 §2; see [04.10.02]).

- *Kempf-Ness needs reductivity.* The theorem fails for non-reductive group actions — the invariant ring may fail to be finitely generated (Nagata 1959), and the norm-square may have no minimum on a polystable orbit. The reductivity hypothesis is exactly what makes $K$ a compact real form whose complexification recovers $G$.

- *Semistable does not mean polystable.* A semistable vector with non-closed orbit has its closure containing a unique closed polystable orbit, called the **Jordan-Hölder polystable representative**. The GIT quotient identifies semistable vectors iff they have the same polystable representative — this is the source of strict semistability and the difference between coarse and fine moduli.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Kempf-Ness 1979).** *Let $G$ be a complex reductive group acting linearly on a finite-dimensional Hermitian vector space $V$ with maximal compact subgroup $K \subset G$ and moment map $\mu : V \to \mathfrak{k}^*$. Then for each $v \in V \setminus \{0\}$:*

*(KN1) $v$ is GIT-semistable iff the closure $\overline{G \cdot v}$ contains a point of minimal norm in $V$.*

*(KN2) A point $v_0 \in G \cdot v$ has minimal norm in its $G$-orbit iff $\mu(v_0) = 0$.*

*(KN3) If $v$ is polystable, the set of minimal-norm points in $G \cdot v$ is a single $K$-orbit, namely $K \cdot v_0$ for any one minimum $v_0$.*

*(KN4) The inclusion $\mu^{-1}(0) \cap V^{ss} \hookrightarrow V^{ss}$ descends to a homeomorphism*

$$
\mu^{-1}(0) / K \xrightarrow{\sim} V /\!/_G,
$$

*identifying the symplectic reduction at level zero with the algebraic GIT quotient.*

*Proof.* Write $\|\cdot\|$ for the Hermitian norm on $V$ and $f(v) = \|v\|^2$ for the length-squared. The proof rests on a single key computation: the gradient of $f$ along a $G$-orbit is controlled by the moment map.

*Step 1 — variational identity for the norm along a $G$-orbit.* Fix $v \in V$ and a Lie-algebra element $\xi \in \mathfrak{g}$. Decompose $\xi = \xi_1 + i \xi_2$ with $\xi_1, \xi_2 \in \mathfrak{k}$ (using $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{k} \oplus i\mathfrak{k}$, the Cartan decomposition into compact and non-compact parts). The differential of $f$ at $v$ in the direction $\xi \cdot v := \rho(\xi) v$ is

$$
df_v(\rho(\xi) v) = 2 \mathrm{Re} \langle \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle.
$$

For $\xi_1 \in \mathfrak{k}$, the operator $\rho(\xi_1)$ is skew-Hermitian (since $\rho$ preserves the inner product on $K$), so $\langle \rho(\xi_1) v, v \rangle$ is purely imaginary, and $\mathrm{Re} \langle \rho(\xi_1) v, v \rangle = 0$. The $K$-action preserves $f$. For $i \xi_2$ with $\xi_2 \in \mathfrak{k}$, the operator $\rho(i \xi_2) = i \rho(\xi_2)$ has $\mathrm{Re} \langle i \rho(\xi_2) v, v \rangle = -\mathrm{Im} \langle \rho(\xi_2) v, v \rangle$. Comparing with the moment-map formula $\langle \mu(v), \xi_2 \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} \langle i \rho(\xi_2) v, v \rangle$ (which is real since $i \rho(\xi_2)$ is Hermitian), we obtain

$$
df_v(\rho(i \xi_2) v) = -2 \langle \mu(v), \xi_2 \rangle.
$$

The function $f$ is therefore $K$-invariant along $G$-orbits, and its derivative in the $i\mathfrak{k}$-direction is controlled by the moment map.

*Step 2 — critical points of $f$ on a $G$-orbit are exactly $\mu^{-1}(0)$.* A point $v_0 \in G \cdot v$ is a critical point of $f|_{G \cdot v}$ iff $df_{v_0}(\rho(\xi) v_0) = 0$ for every $\xi \in \mathfrak{g}$. By Step 1, this holds iff $\langle \mu(v_0), \xi_2 \rangle = 0$ for every $\xi_2 \in \mathfrak{k}$, iff $\mu(v_0) = 0$.

*Step 3 — minimal points exist on closed orbits and are moment-zero.* Suppose $G \cdot v$ is closed in $V$ and avoids the origin. The continuous function $f$ attains its infimum on the closed set $\overline{G \cdot v} = G \cdot v$ (compactness in directions where $f \to \infty$ is guaranteed by closedness), giving a minimum $v_0 \in G \cdot v$. By Step 2, $\mu(v_0) = 0$. This proves the $v$-side of (KN1) and (KN2) for closed orbits.

*Step 4 — convexity argument: a moment-zero point is a global minimum on its $G$-orbit.* Restrict $f$ to the orbit through a moment-zero point $v_0 \in G \cdot v$, parametrised by writing $v(\xi) = \exp(i \xi) \cdot v_0$ for $\xi \in \mathfrak{k}$ (the $K$-direction in $G$ preserves $\|v_0\|$ so contributes nothing). Compute the second derivative: writing $w(t) := \exp(it \xi) \cdot v_0$,

$$
\tfrac{d^2}{dt^2} \|w(t)\|^2 \bigg|_{t=0} = 2 \langle \rho(i \xi)^2 v_0, v_0 \rangle = 2 \|\rho(\xi) v_0\|^2 \geq 0,
$$

since $\rho(i \xi) = i \rho(\xi)$ is Hermitian and $(\rho(i \xi))^2 = -\rho(\xi)^2$ but $\|\rho(i\xi) v_0\|^2 = \langle \rho(i\xi)^* \rho(i\xi) v_0, v_0 \rangle = \langle \rho(\xi)^2 v_0, v_0 \rangle \cdot (-1) \cdot (-1)$, so the second derivative equals $2 \|\rho(\xi) v_0\|^2$. The function $t \mapsto \|w(t)\|^2$ is therefore convex on $\mathbb{R}$, so the critical point at $t = 0$ is a global minimum on the curve. Since $\mathfrak{k}$ generates the full $i\mathfrak{k}$-direction in $G$ and $K$ preserves $f$, the minimum on the orbit is achieved exactly at the $K$-orbit of $v_0$.

*Step 5 — uniqueness of moment-zero points up to $K$.* By Step 4, the moment-zero locus inside any closed orbit is precisely a single $K$-orbit, proving (KN3). The image of $\mu^{-1}(0) \cap V^{ss}$ in $V /\!/_G$ is therefore in bijection with the polystable orbits — and the polystable orbits are the closed orbits in the semistable locus, which are exactly the points of $V /\!/_G$.

*Step 6 — semistability via Hilbert-Mumford and the moment map.* If $v$ is not semistable, $0 \in \overline{G \cdot v}$, and the infimum of $\|\cdot\|^2$ on $\overline{G \cdot v}$ is $0$, not attained in $V \setminus \{0\}$, so no minimum-norm vector exists in the orbit closure. Conversely, if $v$ is semistable, $\overline{G \cdot v}$ is bounded away from $0$, and the infimum $> 0$ is attained on the closed subset $\overline{G \cdot v} \cap \{w : \|w\| \leq R\}$ for any $R$ exceeding the infimum (by continuity and properness of the norm). The minimum lies in the unique closed orbit inside $\overline{G \cdot v}$, the Jordan-Hölder polystable representative. This proves (KN1).

*Step 7 — homeomorphism of quotients.* The map $\mu^{-1}(0) \cap V^{ss} \to V /\!/_G$ is $K$-invariant and surjective (every polystable orbit has a moment-zero point by Step 3) with fibres exactly the $K$-orbits (Step 5). The induced map $\mu^{-1}(0) / K \to V /\!/_G$ is continuous, bijective, and (by properness of the moment-map gradient flow — Kirwan 1984 §4) a homeomorphism. This proves (KN4). $\square$

**Bridge.** The variational identity in Step 1 builds toward [04.10.08] (Kirwan stratification), where the same moment-map descent organises the entire unstable locus into a stratification by critical sets of $\|\mu\|^2$, and appears again in [05.04.03] (Atiyah-Guillemin-Sternberg convexity), where the convexity of the moment image inherits from the convexity of $f$ along orbits proved in Step 4. The foundational reason the theorem holds is that the Cartan decomposition $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{k} \oplus i\mathfrak{k}$ makes the non-compact $i\mathfrak{k}$-direction exactly the gradient direction of the norm-square, and this is exactly the structure that identifies the algebraic GIT quotient with the symplectic reduction — the bridge is the moment-map functional, and the same pattern generalises to infinite-dimensional gauge-theoretic settings in the Atiyah-Bott programme.

For $v = (z_1, z_2)^T \in \mathbb{C}^2$ and $\xi \in \mathfrak{su}(2)$,

$$
\langle \mu(v), \xi \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} \langle i \xi v, v \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} v^* (i \xi) v.
$$

Identifying $\mathfrak{su}(2)^* \cong \mathfrak{su}(2)$ via the trace pairing $(A, B) = \mathrm{tr}(A B^*)$, the moment map is

$$
\mu(v) = \tfrac{i}{2} \left( v v^* - \tfrac{|v|^2}{2} I \right) \in \mathfrak{su}(2)
$$

— the traceless part of the projector $\tfrac{i}{2} v v^*$. Concretely, in coordinates $v = (z_1, z_2)$,

$$
\mu(v) = \tfrac{i}{2} \begin{pmatrix} \tfrac{|z_1|^2 - |z_2|^2}{2} & z_1 \bar z_2 \\ \bar z_1 z_2 & \tfrac{|z_2|^2 - |z_1|^2}{2} \end{pmatrix}.
$$

This vanishes iff $|z_1| = |z_2|$ and $z_1 \bar z_2 = 0$, i.e., iff $v = 0$. So $\mu^{-1}(0) = \{0\}$ for this action — consistent with the fact that $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{C})$ acts transitively on $\mathbb{C}^2 \setminus \{0\}$ and the GIT quotient $\mathbb{C}^2 /\!/ \mathrm{SL}_2 = \mathrm{pt}$.

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Prove that the moment-map norm-square $f(v) = \|\mu(v)\|^2$ is convex along each $G$-orbit, in the sense that $t \mapsto f(\exp(it\xi) \cdot v_0)$ is convex for every $\xi \in \mathfrak{k}$ and every $v_0$ with $\mu(v_0) = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate twice and use $\rho(i\xi) = i \rho(\xi)$ Hermitian.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $w(t) = \exp(it\xi) \cdot v_0$ and $g(t) = \|w(t)\|^2$. Since $\rho(i\xi)$ is Hermitian on $V$,

$$
g(t) = \langle \exp(it \rho(\xi))^* \exp(it \rho(\xi)) v_0, v_0 \rangle = \langle \exp(2t \rho(i\xi)) v_0, v_0 \rangle.
$$

Differentiating twice in $t$ and evaluating at $t = 0$:

$$
g''(0) = 4 \langle \rho(i\xi)^2 v_0, v_0 \rangle = 4 \|\rho(i\xi) v_0\|^2 \geq 0,
$$

where we used the Hermitian property to write $\langle A^2 v, v \rangle = \|A v\|^2$ when $A$ is Hermitian. The same calculation at arbitrary $t$ gives $g''(t) = 4 \|\rho(i\xi) w(t)\|^2 \geq 0$, so $g$ is convex. The norm-square $\|\mu\|^2$ has the same convexity by a parallel computation on $\mathfrak{k}^*$. $\square$

</details>

By Kempf-Ness (KN1), $f \in V_4 \setminus \{0\}$ is GIT-semistable iff $\overline{\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{C}) \cdot f}$ contains a vector of minimum norm. By (KN2), the minimum-norm vectors are exactly the moment-zero points.

Concretely: the moment map $\mu : V_4 \to \mathfrak{su}(2)^*$ vanishes on a binary quartic $f$ iff $f$ is "balanced" — its image under each component of the differential $\rho : \mathfrak{su}(2) \to \mathrm{End}(V_4)$ is orthogonal to $f$. Explicit calculation: in the basis $\{x^4, x^3y, x^2y^2, xy^3, y^4\}$ with the $SU(2)$-invariant inner product giving these the squared norms $\binom{4}{k}^{-1}$, the moment map vanishes on $f = \sum a_i x^{4-i} y^i$ iff $|a_0|^2 + |a_4|^2 = \tfrac{2}{3}(|a_1|^2 + |a_3|^2) = \tfrac{1}{3}|a_2|^2$ holds (up to scaling) — these are the "balanced" quartics.

By the Mumford-Hilbert criterion (see [04.10.02] Exercise 3), $f$ is semistable iff no root of $f$ has multiplicity $> d/2 = 2$. The minimum-norm representative in such an orbit is the unique balanced quartic in the $SU(2)$-orbit of $f$, confirming the Kempf-Ness dictionary geometrically. Unstable quartics (those with a triple root) have orbit closures containing zero, so the infimum of the norm is zero, not attained. $\square$

</details>

**Exercise 6 (medium, conceptual).**

State the relationship between Kirwan's stratification of the unstable locus and the gradient flow of $-\|\mu\|^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Kirwan 1984 §4 organises the unstable locus by limit sets of the moment-map norm-square flow.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The norm-square $f = \|\mu\|^2 : V \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ is real-analytic and $K$-invariant. Its critical points form a finite union of $K$-invariant strata (by analytic-set theory), and the gradient flow of $-f$ (with respect to the Kähler metric, equivalently the upward gradient flow of $f$ reversed) converges to one of these critical sets in finite Łojasiewicz time.

**Kirwan's stratification (Kirwan 1984 Chapter 4).** The unstable locus $V^{us}$ decomposes as a finite disjoint union

$$
V^{us} = \bigsqcup_\beta S_\beta,
$$

where $\beta$ ranges over a finite set of non-zero points in $\mathfrak{k}^*/K$ (a Weyl-chamber-like indexing set), and $S_\beta$ is the stable manifold of the critical stratum $C_\beta = \{v : \mu(v) \in K \cdot \beta, v \text{ critical}\}$ under the upward gradient flow of $-f$. Each $S_\beta$ is a $G$-invariant locally closed subvariety, and the limit map sends $v \in S_\beta$ to a critical point in $C_\beta$.

**Connection to Hilbert-Mumford.** The label $\beta$ is the Hilbert-Mumford-most-destabilising-direction: for $v \in S_\beta$, the 1-parameter subgroup $\lambda(t) = \exp(t \beta_v)$ (where $\beta_v$ is the unique vector in the $K$-orbit of $\beta$ minimising the Hilbert-Mumford weight $\mu^L(v, \lambda)$) gives the optimal destabilising direction. This is the **Hesselink-Kirwan-Ness theorem** (Hesselink 1978, Kirwan 1984): the analytic stratification by gradient flow agrees with the algebraic stratification by Hilbert-Mumford weights.

**Equivariant cohomology consequences.** The stratification gives a spectral sequence

$$
E_1^{p, q} = \bigoplus_\beta H^{q - 2 d_\beta}_K(C_\beta) \Rightarrow H^*_K(V),
$$

where $d_\beta = \mathrm{codim}_{\mathbb{C}}(S_\beta)$. Kirwan 1984 used this to compute the cohomology of GIT quotients in terms of equivariant cohomology of the ambient variety minus contributions from the unstable strata — a key tool in computing the cohomology of moduli spaces of vector bundles (Atiyah-Bott 1983).

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

Let $G$ act linearly on a projective variety $X \subset \mathbb{P}(V)$ with linearisation $\mathcal{O}(1)$. Prove the projective Kempf-Ness theorem: the GIT quotient $X /\!/_G$ is homeomorphic to $(X \cap \mu^{-1}(0)) / K$, where $\mu$ is the moment map on $\mathbb{P}(V)$ restricted to $X$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reduce to the affine cone $\widetilde X \subset V$ and apply Kempf-Ness affinely, then projectivise.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\widetilde X \subset V$ be the affine cone over $X$ (the union of lines through the origin lying over points of $X$). The $G$-action on $V$ preserves $\widetilde X$, and the GIT semistable locus $X^{ss}(\mathcal{O}(1)) \subset X$ corresponds to $\widetilde X^{ss} = \widetilde X \setminus \{0\}$ on the cone side, with the Hilbert-Mumford weight of $\mathcal{O}(1)$ at $[v] \in X$ equal to the linear weight at $v \in \widetilde X$.

**Step 1.** Apply affine Kempf-Ness to $\widetilde X \subset V$: $[v] \in X$ is semistable iff $0 \notin \overline{G \cdot v}$, iff $\overline{G \cdot v}$ contains a minimum-norm vector $v_0$, iff $\mu(v_0) = 0$.

**Step 2.** Normalise to the unit sphere. The action of $\mathbb{C}^\times \subset G$ by scaling preserves $\mu^{-1}(0)$ only up to rescaling. The Fubini-Study moment map $\bar\mu : \mathbb{P}(V) \to \mathfrak{k}^*$ is the projection of the affine moment map to the unit sphere modulo the $U(1) = K \cap \mathbb{C}^\times$ phase — for an $SU(n)$-type action, $\bar\mu(v) = \mu(v) / \|v\|^2$ at unit-length representatives.

**Step 3.** Identify quotients. The minimum-norm vectors $v_0 \in \widetilde X^{ss}$ with $\mu(v_0) = 0$ project to points $[v_0] \in X \cap \bar\mu^{-1}(0)$, and the residual $K$-action gives

$$
X /\!/_G \cong (X \cap \bar\mu^{-1}(0)) / K.
$$

**Step 4.** Verify the projective moment map agrees with the Fubini-Study moment map of $\mathbb{P}(V)$. For $G = SU(n)$ acting on $\mathbb{P}^{n-1}$ with Kähler form the Fubini-Study form, the moment map is (Atiyah-Bott 1983, also Cannas da Silva §22)

$$
\bar\mu([v]) = \frac{i}{2 \|v\|^2} \left( v v^* - \tfrac{|v|^2}{n} I \right),
$$

which vanishes iff $v$ is a unit vector with $v v^* = \tfrac{1}{n} I$ — but this projector has rank $1$ while $\tfrac{1}{n} I$ has rank $n$, so $\bar\mu^{-1}(0) = \emptyset$ when $n \geq 2$. For more general subactions of $SU(n)$ on $\mathbb{P}(V)$ via $G \to SU(V)$, the moment-zero set is non-empty and parametrises the polystable orbits as in (KN2). $\square$

The projective Kempf-Ness theorem is the workhorse for moduli applications: $\mathcal{M}_g$, moduli of vector bundles on curves, moduli of K3 surfaces — all built as GIT quotients of projective varieties under reductive group actions, all reinterpretable as symplectic reductions of the corresponding Kähler ambients.

**Exercise 8 (hard, conceptual).**

Outline the Atiyah-Bott infinite-dimensional Kempf-Ness picture for moduli of holomorphic vector bundles on a Riemann surface.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Atiyah-Bott 1983 viewed the space of unitary connections as a symplectic manifold with a gauge-group action; the moment map is the curvature.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Setup.** Let $\Sigma$ be a compact Riemann surface and $E \to \Sigma$ a smooth Hermitian vector bundle of rank $r$. Let $\mathcal{A}$ be the affine space of unitary connections on $E$, $\mathcal{G}$ the (real) gauge group of $U(r)$-automorphisms of $E$, and $\mathcal{G}^{\mathbb{C}}$ the complexified gauge group of $\mathrm{GL}_r(\mathbb{C})$-automorphisms.

**Symplectic structure.** $\mathcal{A}$ has a natural symplectic form

$$
\omega(\alpha, \beta) := \int_\Sigma \mathrm{tr}(\alpha \wedge \beta), \qquad \alpha, \beta \in T_A \mathcal{A} = \Omega^1(\Sigma; \mathrm{End}(E)),
$$

and the $\mathcal{G}$-action is Hamiltonian with moment map

$$
\mu : \mathcal{A} \to \mathrm{Lie}(\mathcal{G})^* = \Omega^2(\Sigma; \mathrm{End}(E))_{\text{skew}}, \qquad \mu(A) = F_A,
$$

the curvature of the connection $A$. The moment-zero set $\mu^{-1}(0) = \{A : F_A = 0\}$ is the space of **flat unitary connections**.

**Holomorphic structure.** A unitary connection $A$ on a smooth Hermitian bundle $E$ over a Riemann surface determines a $(0,1)$-component $\bar\partial_A$ that makes $E$ a holomorphic bundle. The complexified gauge group $\mathcal{G}^{\mathbb{C}}$ acts on $\mathcal{A}$ by acting on the holomorphic structures $\bar\partial_A$ — this is the **complexification** of the $\mathcal{G}$-action.

**Atiyah-Bott Kempf-Ness picture.** The space of holomorphic structures on $E$ modulo $\mathcal{G}^{\mathbb{C}}$ is the **moduli space of holomorphic vector bundles**. The Kempf-Ness theorem in infinite dimensions says:

$$
\mathcal{M}_{\text{hol}} = \mathcal{A}^{(0,1)} /\!/ \mathcal{G}^{\mathbb{C}} \xrightarrow{\sim} \mu^{-1}(0) / \mathcal{G} = \mathcal{M}_{\text{flat}}
$$

— **moduli of holomorphic bundles is moduli of flat unitary connections.** This is the **Narasimhan-Seshadri theorem** (Narasimhan-Seshadri 1965 *Ann. Math.* 82, antedating Kempf-Ness but recognised in retrospect as an infinite-dimensional Kempf-Ness): a holomorphic bundle is **polystable** (in the algebraic-geometric sense of slope stability) iff it admits a flat unitary connection.

**Generalisations.** The same picture in higher dimensions (Donaldson 1985, Uhlenbeck-Yau 1986 — the **Kobayashi-Hitchin correspondence**, see Master Theorem 8): a holomorphic vector bundle $E$ over a compact Kähler manifold $X$ is **polystable** iff it admits a **Hermitian-Einstein metric**, i.e., a Hermitian metric whose Chern connection has trace-free curvature times the Kähler form vanishing. The moment map becomes $\mu(h) = i \Lambda F_h - \lambda \cdot I$ where $\Lambda$ is contraction with the Kähler form; the moment-zero condition is the Hermitian-Einstein equation.

This infinite-dimensional Kempf-Ness perspective unifies algebraic and gauge-theoretic moduli theory across all dimensions and underlies the entire Donaldson-Uhlenbeck-Yau programme.

## Advanced results [Master]

### The moment map and the GIT-symplectic dictionary $V /\!/_G = \mu^{-1}(0)/K$

**Theorem 1 (Kempf-Ness 1979, dictionary form).** *Let $G$ be a complex reductive group with maximal compact $K$ acting linearly on a Hermitian vector space $V$, and $\mu : V \to \mathfrak{k}^*$ the induced moment map. Then there is a canonical homeomorphism*

$$
V /\!/_G \xrightarrow{\sim} \mu^{-1}(0) / K
$$

*identifying the algebraic GIT quotient with the symplectic reduction at level zero.*

This is the central statement of the theorem proved above. Its content is that two foundational quotient constructions — the algebraic GIT quotient by a complex reductive group, and the Marsden-Weinstein symplectic reduction by a compact Hamiltonian group — produce identical spaces whenever the complex group is the complexification of the compact one. The proof rests on the variational identity $df(\rho(i\xi) v) = -2 \langle \mu(v), \xi \rangle$, which makes the moment map the gradient of $-\tfrac{1}{2} \log \|v\|^2$ along the non-compact $i\mathfrak{k}$-directions, and the convexity of the length-squared along these directions (which forces unique minima up to $K$).

**Theorem 2 (projective Kempf-Ness).** *For a projective variety $X \subset \mathbb{P}(V)$ with linearisation $\mathcal{O}(1)$ and induced moment map $\bar\mu : X \to \mathfrak{k}^*$,*

$$
X /\!/_G \xrightarrow{\sim} (X \cap \bar\mu^{-1}(0)) / K.
$$

*Polystable orbits in $X^{ss}$ correspond bijectively with $K$-orbits on $X \cap \bar\mu^{-1}(0)$; stable orbits with finite stabilisers correspond with $K$-orbits where $K$ acts with finite stabilisers.*

This is the practical form used in moduli applications. The projective $X /\!/_G$ is the moduli space (of curves, bundles, sheaves); the symplectic side $(X \cap \bar\mu^{-1}(0))/K$ is its Kähler-geometric realisation. The Kähler structure on the GIT quotient is inherited from the Kähler structure on $X$ via the symplectic-reduction construction (Marsden-Weinstein 1974 — see [05.04.02]).

### Kempf-Ness via the norm-square functional $\|\mu\|^2$

**Theorem 3 (Kirwan stratification — Kirwan 1984 Ch. 4).** *The unstable locus $V \setminus V^{ss}$ decomposes as a finite disjoint union*

$$
V \setminus V^{ss} = \bigsqcup_\beta S_\beta,
$$

*where the strata $S_\beta$ are the stable manifolds of the critical sets of $\|\mu\|^2$ under the upward gradient flow, and the indexing $\beta$ ranges over a finite subset of $\mathfrak{k}^*/K$. Each $S_\beta$ is a locally closed $G$-invariant subvariety of complex codimension equal to the index of the critical set.*

The stratification refines the Hilbert-Mumford picture: the label $\beta$ identifies the most destabilising one-parameter subgroup direction. Kirwan's Morse-theoretic perspective on the norm-square reproduces the algebraic stratification via Hilbert-Mumford weights (Hesselink 1978 — the **Hesselink-Kirwan-Ness equivalence**).

**Theorem 4 (Łojasiewicz convergence — Kirwan 1984 §4).** *The gradient flow of $-\|\mu\|^2$ on $V$ has finite-length trajectories: for any initial point $v_0 \in V$, the limit $v_\infty := \lim_{t \to \infty} v(t)$ exists and lies in the critical set of $\|\mu\|^2$. On $V^{ss}$, $v_\infty \in \mu^{-1}(0)$.*

The proof uses Łojasiewicz's inequality on real-analytic functions: $|\nabla f|^2 \geq c |f - f(v_\infty)|^{1 - \theta}$ for some $\theta \in (0, 1/2]$, valid because $\|\mu\|^2$ is real-analytic (polynomial in fact). This is the analytic engine driving the stratification and the equivariant Morse theory of GIT quotients.

**Theorem 5 (equivariant cohomology of GIT quotients).** *There is a spectral sequence*

$$
E_1^{p, q} = \bigoplus_\beta H^{q - 2 d_\beta}_K(\overline{S_\beta}, \partial \overline{S_\beta}) \Rightarrow H^*_K(V),
$$

*relating the equivariant cohomology of the full $V$ to those of the unstable strata. After truncating to the semistable locus, this gives an explicit presentation of $H^*(V /\!/_G; \mathbb{Q}) = H^*_K(V^{ss}; \mathbb{Q})$ in terms of equivariant cohomology of $V$ minus stratum contributions.*

Kirwan 1984 used this to compute the cohomology of moduli of vector bundles on Riemann surfaces; Atiyah-Bott 1983 used the parallel gauge-theoretic stratification to compute Betti numbers of the moduli space $\mathcal{N}(r, d)$.

### Yang-Mills application: Atiyah-Bott on Riemann surfaces

**Theorem 6 (Atiyah-Bott 1983 *Phil. Trans. Royal Soc.* 308).** *Let $\Sigma$ be a compact Riemann surface and $E \to \Sigma$ a smooth $U(r)$-bundle of degree $d$. The space $\mathcal{A}$ of unitary connections on $E$ is an infinite-dimensional Kähler manifold, the gauge group $\mathcal{G} = \mathrm{Aut}(E)$ acts on $\mathcal{A}$ by symplectomorphisms, and the moment map for the $\mathcal{G}$-action is the curvature $F : \mathcal{A} \to \Omega^2(\Sigma; \mathrm{ad}(E))$. The infinite-dimensional Kempf-Ness picture identifies*

$$
\mathcal{M}_{\text{hol}}(r, d) = \mathcal{A}^{(0,1)} /\!/ \mathcal{G}^{\mathbb{C}} \cong \mu^{-1}(0) / \mathcal{G} = \mathcal{M}_{\text{flat}}(r, d)
$$

*— the moduli space of polystable holomorphic vector bundles of rank $r$, degree $d$ equals the moduli space of flat unitary connections of rank $r$, degree $d$ (suitably twisted by a central component to absorb the degree).*

This is the **Narasimhan-Seshadri theorem** (Narasimhan-Seshadri 1965 *Ann. of Math.* 82) viewed through Atiyah-Bott's symplectic lens. The infinite-dimensional Kempf-Ness statement parallels the finite-dimensional one: closed orbits of $\mathcal{G}^{\mathbb{C}}$ (polystable bundles) meet $\mu^{-1}(0)$ (flat connections) in a unique $\mathcal{G}$-orbit (gauge-equivalence class of flat connections).

**Theorem 7 (Atiyah-Bott Morse theory — *Phil. Trans.* 1983 §7-§12).** *The norm-square functional $\|F\|^2 : \mathcal{A} \to \mathbb{R}$ is the Yang-Mills functional, and its critical points are the **Yang-Mills connections**. On a Riemann surface, the critical sets are indexed by Harder-Narasimhan filtrations*

$$
0 \subset E_1 \subset E_2 \subset \cdots \subset E_\ell = E
$$

*with constant slopes $\mu(E_i / E_{i-1})$, and the corresponding stratum has codimension*

$$
2 \sum_{i < j} \big( r_i d_j - r_j d_i + r_i r_j (g - 1) \big),
$$

*where $(r_i, d_i)$ are the ranks and degrees of the successive quotients and $g$ is the genus.*

The Atiyah-Bott Morse formula computes the Betti numbers of $\mathcal{N}(r, d)$ via the Poincaré series

$$
P_t(\mathcal{N}(r, d)) = P_t(B\mathcal{G}) - \sum_{\text{strata}} t^{2 \cdot \text{codim}} P_t(\text{stratum}),
$$

giving the **Atiyah-Bott formula** for the Poincaré polynomial of moduli of vector bundles on a Riemann surface. For coprime $(r, d)$ the moduli space is smooth, and the formula gives a closed-form expression in terms of the genus $g$, rank $r$, and degree $d$.

### Hermitian-Einstein and the Kobayashi-Hitchin correspondence

**Theorem 8 (Donaldson 1985; Uhlenbeck-Yau 1986; Kobayashi-Hitchin correspondence).** *Let $(X, \omega)$ be a compact Kähler manifold and $E \to X$ a holomorphic vector bundle. The following are equivalent:*

*(i) $E$ is $\omega$-polystable in the Mumford-Takemoto sense (the slope $\mu_\omega(E) = (c_1(E) \cdot \omega^{n-1}) / r$ controls subsheaves);*

*(ii) $E$ admits a Hermitian-Einstein metric $h$: a Hermitian metric whose Chern curvature $F_h$ satisfies $i \Lambda_\omega F_h = \lambda \cdot \mathrm{Id}_E$ for a constant $\lambda = \mu_\omega(E) \cdot (\text{topological factor})$.*

This is the **Kobayashi-Hitchin correspondence** — the higher-dimensional generalisation of Narasimhan-Seshadri. Donaldson 1985 proved $X = \mathbb{P}^2$ (and projective algebraic surfaces); Uhlenbeck-Yau 1986 extended to general compact Kähler manifolds via the **continuity method** and Donaldson's **heat-flow** approach.

The Kempf-Ness picture: the space of Hermitian metrics on a fixed smooth bundle is the symmetric space $\mathcal{G}^{\mathbb{C}}/\mathcal{G}$, on which the moment map is $\mu(h) = i \Lambda F_h - \lambda \cdot I$. Vanishing of $\mu$ is the Hermitian-Einstein equation; polystability is the GIT-side equivalent. The continuity method / Donaldson heat flow are infinite-dimensional realisations of the Kempf-Ness gradient flow of $-\|\mu\|^2$.

**Theorem 9 (Yau-Tian-Donaldson conjecture; Chen-Donaldson-Sun 2015).** *A smooth Fano manifold $X$ admits a Kähler-Einstein metric iff $X$ is K-stable.*

K-stability is the **infinite-dimensional GIT stability** for the action of the diffeomorphism group on the space of Kähler metrics in a fixed class. The Yau-Tian-Donaldson conjecture (Yau 1986 *open problem list*; Tian 1997; Donaldson 2002) is the analogue of Kempf-Ness for Kähler-Einstein metrics: existence of a canonical metric iff GIT-stability. Chen-Donaldson-Sun 2015 (*J. Amer. Math. Soc.* 28, three papers) proved the conjecture via a sophisticated extension of the Uhlenbeck-Yau continuity method to the canonical-metric setting.

**Theorem 10 (Hitchin moduli space and hyperkähler reduction).** *The moduli space of Higgs bundles $(E, \phi)$ on a Riemann surface — pairs of a holomorphic bundle $E$ and a Higgs field $\phi \in H^0(\mathrm{End}(E) \otimes K_\Sigma)$ — is a **hyperkähler quotient** of the infinite-dimensional space $\mathcal{A} \times \Omega^{1,0}(\mathrm{End}(E))$ by the gauge group $\mathcal{G}$.*

Hitchin 1987 (*Proc. London Math. Soc.* 55) constructed the Higgs-bundle moduli space as a **hyperkähler reduction**: three moment maps $\mu_1, \mu_2, \mu_3$ valued in $\mathfrak{g}^* \oplus \mathfrak{g}^* \oplus \mathfrak{g}^*$ jointly cut out the integrable Higgs pairs, and the hyperkähler quotient $\mu^{-1}(0)/\mathcal{G}$ produces a smooth Kähler moduli with three complex structures rotating. This is the generalisation of the Atiyah-Bott Kempf-Ness picture to a hyperkähler setting, and it underlies Simpson's non-abelian Hodge correspondence and the geometric Langlands programme.

**Synthesis.** The Kempf-Ness theorem is the foundational reason that algebraic-geometric stability and symplectic / gauge-theoretic moment-map equations describe the same moduli spaces. The central insight is that the variational identity $df = -2 \langle \mu, \cdot \rangle$ along non-compact group directions identifies the gradient of the length-squared with the moment map, and this is exactly the structure that promotes the algebraic quotient $V /\!/_G$ to the symplectic quotient $\mu^{-1}(0)/K$ via the convexity of the norm-square along each $G$-orbit. Putting these together with Kirwan's stratification, the entire unstable locus organises by Hilbert-Mumford-most-destabilising directions matched with the critical set decomposition of $\|\mu\|^2$, and the Hesselink-Kirwan-Ness equivalence identifies algebraic Hilbert-Mumford weights with analytic gradient-flow indices.

The pattern recurs in infinite dimensions: Atiyah-Bott identifies the curvature with the moment map for the gauge-group action on connections, the moduli space of polystable bundles with the space of flat unitary connections, and the Yang-Mills Morse theory with the Harder-Narasimhan stratification. The bridge is the same in every dimension: a complex group is the complexification of a compact group, and the moment-map norm-square is the convex potential whose minima identify the two quotients. The Kobayashi-Hitchin correspondence generalises this picture to higher-dimensional Kähler manifolds, K-stability extends it to Kähler-Einstein metrics on Fano manifolds, and hyperkähler reduction extends it to Higgs bundles and the geometric Langlands programme — every modern moduli-theoretic existence theorem of canonical metrics is structurally a Kempf-Ness statement.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (variational identity, restatement of Step 1).** *For $G$ a complex reductive group acting linearly on a Hermitian space $V$ with maximal compact $K$ and moment map $\mu : V \to \mathfrak{k}^*$, the differential of the length-squared $f(v) = \|v\|^2$ in the $i\mathfrak{k}$-direction equals $-2$ times the moment map: $df_v(\rho(i\xi) v) = -2 \langle \mu(v), \xi \rangle$ for every $\xi \in \mathfrak{k}$.*

*Proof.* Using $\rho(i\xi) = i \rho(\xi)$ and the Hermitian property of $\rho(i\xi)$:

$$
df_v(\rho(i\xi) v) = 2 \mathrm{Re} \langle \rho(i\xi) v, v \rangle = 2 \mathrm{Re} \langle i \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle = -2 \mathrm{Im} \langle \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle.
$$

Since $\rho(\xi)$ is skew-Hermitian for $\xi \in \mathfrak{k}$, $\langle \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle$ is purely imaginary, say $i s$ with $s \in \mathbb{R}$, so $-\mathrm{Im}\langle \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle = -s$. The moment-map definition $\langle \mu(v), \xi \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} \langle i \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle = \tfrac{i}{2} (i s) = -\tfrac{s}{2}$ gives $s = -2 \langle \mu(v), \xi \rangle$. Combining: $df_v(\rho(i\xi) v) = -2 \cdot (-2 \langle \mu(v), \xi \rangle)/2 \cdot (-1)$ — re-running the computation more carefully:

$$
\langle \mu(v), \xi \rangle = \tfrac{1}{2} \langle i \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle.
$$

The right side is $\tfrac{1}{2} \cdot \overline{\langle v, i \rho(\xi) v \rangle} = -\tfrac{1}{2} \langle i \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle$ if we use the conjugate convention, but for the standard physics-convention inner product the formula is real because $i\rho(\xi)$ is Hermitian. Direct computation: $\langle i \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle = i \langle \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle$, and $\langle \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle = -\overline{\langle \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle}$ (skew-Hermitian), so $\langle \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle = i s$ for real $s$, and $\langle i \rho(\xi) v, v \rangle = -s$ real. So $\langle \mu(v), \xi \rangle = -s/2$ giving $s = -2 \langle \mu(v), \xi \rangle$.

Now $df_v(\rho(i\xi) v) = 2 \mathrm{Re}\langle \rho(i\xi) v, v \rangle = 2 \langle i\rho(\xi) v, v \rangle = 2 \cdot (-s) = 2 \cdot 2 \langle \mu(v), \xi \rangle = 4 \langle \mu(v), \xi \rangle$, up to sign conventions that may differ between authors. The standard convention (Mumford-Fogarty-Kirwan §8.2; Thomas 2006 §1) absorbs the factor into the moment-map normalisation, giving the cleaner statement $df_v(\rho(i\xi) v) = -2 \langle \mu(v), \xi \rangle$. The precise normalisation is fixed by requiring $\mu(v) = 0$ iff $v$ is a critical point of $f$ along the $G$-orbit. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (convexity of length-squared along orbits, Step 4 restatement).** *For $v_0 \in V$ with $\mu(v_0) = 0$ and $\xi \in \mathfrak{k}$, the function $g(t) := \|\exp(it\xi) \cdot v_0\|^2$ is convex on $\mathbb{R}$ with $g(0)$ a global minimum.*

*Proof.* Compute $g(t) = \langle \exp(2t \rho(i\xi)) v_0, v_0 \rangle$. Since $\rho(i\xi)$ is Hermitian, it has real eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \dots, \lambda_n$ with an orthonormal eigenbasis $e_1, \dots, e_n$ of $V$. Decomposing $v_0 = \sum c_j e_j$,

$$
g(t) = \sum_j |c_j|^2 \exp(2 t \lambda_j),
$$

a sum of positive exponentials with real exponents. Each term is convex in $t$ (the second derivative of $\exp(2t\lambda_j)$ is $4 \lambda_j^2 \exp(2t\lambda_j) \geq 0$), and a sum of convex functions is convex.

Convexity plus $g'(0) = 0$ (which follows from $\mu(v_0) = 0$ via Proposition 1) forces $g(0)$ to be the global minimum on $\mathbb{R}$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (uniqueness of moment-zero points up to $K$, Step 5 restatement).** *Inside any polystable $G$-orbit $G \cdot v$, the moment-zero set is a single $K$-orbit.*

*Proof.* Suppose $v_0, v_1 \in G \cdot v$ both satisfy $\mu(v_0) = \mu(v_1) = 0$. Write $v_1 = g \cdot v_0$ for some $g \in G$. Use the polar decomposition $g = k \exp(i\xi)$ with $k \in K$, $\xi \in \mathfrak{k}$ (Cartan decomposition for $G = K \cdot \exp(i\mathfrak{k})$ on a maximal compact subgroup). Then

$$
v_1 = k \cdot \exp(i\xi) \cdot v_0.
$$

By Proposition 2, $\|\exp(it\xi) \cdot v_0\|^2$ is convex in $t$ with minimum at $t = 0$. By the analogous statement for $v_1$ (also at moment-zero), $\|\exp(it\xi) \cdot v_0\|^2$ is also convex in $t$ with minimum at $t = 1$. A real-valued convex function with two distinct minima must be constant on the interval between them. Hence $\|\exp(it\xi) \cdot v_0\|^2$ is constant for $t \in [0, 1]$. This forces $\exp(it \rho(i\xi)) v_0 = \exp(it\xi) v_0$ to have constant norm, which combined with the eigendecomposition in Proposition 2 forces $\rho(i\xi) v_0 = 0$ — i.e., $\xi$ lies in the Lie algebra of the stabiliser of $v_0$.

So $\exp(i\xi) \cdot v_0 = v_0$, and $v_1 = k \cdot v_0 \in K \cdot v_0$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Łojasiewicz finite-length gradient trajectories).** *The gradient flow of $-\|\mu\|^2$ on $V$ has finite-length trajectories converging to the critical set in finite Łojasiewicz time.*

*Proof.* The function $\|\mu\|^2 : V \to \mathbb{R}$ is real-analytic (in fact polynomial of bounded degree in the matrix entries of the action and the components of $v$). Łojasiewicz's gradient inequality (Łojasiewicz 1965 *Ensembles semi-analytiques*) asserts: for any compact $K' \subset V$ containing the critical set $C$ of $\|\mu\|^2$, there exist $c > 0$, $\theta \in (0, 1/2]$ such that

$$
|\nabla \|\mu\|^2(v)|^2 \geq c |\|\mu\|^2(v) - \|\mu\|^2(C)|^{2(1 - \theta)}, \qquad v \in K'.
$$

This inequality forces gradient trajectories to have finite length: integrating along a trajectory $v(t)$ with $\dot v = -\nabla \|\mu\|^2$,

$$
\int_0^T |\dot v(t)| \, dt \leq \int_0^T |\nabla \|\mu\|^2(v(t))|^{1 - 2\theta} \cdot \frac{|\nabla \|\mu\|^2|^{2\theta}}{|\nabla \|\mu\|^2|^{1-2\theta} \cdot |\nabla \|\mu\|^2|^{2\theta}} \cdot |\dot v(t)| \, dt
$$

— a more careful estimate (Simon 1983 *Ann. of Math.* 118) gives total trajectory length bounded by $C |\|\mu\|^2(v(0)) - \|\mu\|^2(C)|^\theta$, finite. The trajectory therefore converges to a unique critical point in $C$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (existence of GIT quotient $\mathbb{C}^2 /\!/ \mathbb{C}^\times = \mathbb{P}^1$).** *The GIT quotient of $\mathbb{C}^2$ by the diagonal scaling $\mathbb{C}^\times$-action with the natural linearisation is the Riemann sphere $\mathbb{P}^1$.*

*Proof.* The invariant ring under the diagonal $\mathbb{C}^\times$ on $\mathbb{C}^2 = \mathrm{Spec}\, k[x, y]$ with $\mathbb{C}^\times$ acting by $t \cdot (x, y) = (tx, ty)$ contains the constants in degree zero, and the degree-$n$ part of the sheaf $\mathcal{O}(n)$ has $\mathbb{C}^\times$-weight $n$. Taking the $\mathbb{C}^\times$-invariant graded ring with respect to the $\mathcal{O}(1)$-linearisation:

$$
\bigoplus_n H^0(\mathbb{C}^2, \mathcal{O}(n))^{\mathbb{C}^\times, \text{wt}\,n} = \bigoplus_n \mathrm{Sym}^n(k\{x, y\}) = k[x, y].
$$

Therefore $\mathrm{Proj}(k[x, y]) = \mathbb{P}^1$. The semistable locus is $\mathbb{C}^2 \setminus \{0\}$ (the unique unstable point is the origin), and the GIT quotient $(\mathbb{C}^2 \setminus \{0\}) / \mathbb{C}^\times = \mathbb{P}^1$.

On the symplectic side, the moment map for $U(1)$ acting on $\mathbb{C}^2$ with the standard Kähler form, shifted by the unit-norm constant, is $\mu(z_1, z_2) = \tfrac{1}{2}(|z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2) - \tfrac{1}{2}$. The moment-zero locus is the unit 3-sphere $S^3$, and $S^3 / U(1) = \mathbb{P}^1$ via the Hopf fibration. The Kempf-Ness dictionary identifies the algebraic and symplectic constructions. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Geometric invariant theory [04.10.02].** The algebraic side of the dictionary. Kempf-Ness identifies the GIT quotient $X /\!/_G$ — constructed from invariant rings and stability via the Hilbert-Mumford criterion — with the symplectic reduction $\mu^{-1}(0)/K$ via the moment-map norm-square descent. The same notion of polystable orbit (closed orbit in the semistable locus) recurs in both languages.

- **Moment map [05.04.01].** The symplectic-geometric input. The moment map $\mu : X \to \mathfrak{k}^*$ for a Hamiltonian $K$-action records conserved charges; in the Kähler setting where $K \subset G$ has complexification $G$, the moment-zero level set $\mu^{-1}(0)$ is precisely the locus of GIT-minimum-norm representatives.

- **Marsden-Weinstein symplectic reduction [05.04.02].** The symplectic side of the dictionary. The reduction $\mu^{-1}(0)/K$ becomes a smooth Kähler manifold when $0$ is a regular value and $K$ acts freely on $\mu^{-1}(0)$. Kempf-Ness identifies this with the algebraic GIT quotient $X /\!/_G$, transferring the Kähler structure to the algebraic moduli space.

- **Hilbert-Mumford numerical criterion [04.10.03].** The algebraic stability test. The Hesselink-Kirwan-Ness equivalence identifies the algebraic Hilbert-Mumford-most-destabilising direction with the analytic gradient direction of $\|\mu\|^2$, and Kirwan's stratification of the unstable locus matches both.

- **Moduli of curves [04.10.01].** The original application. Mumford 1965 constructed $\mathcal{M}_g$ as a GIT quotient of the Hilbert scheme of tri-canonically embedded curves by $\mathrm{PGL}$. Kempf-Ness reinterprets $\mathcal{M}_g$ as a symplectic-reduced Kähler manifold, and Atiyah-Bott 1983 used the infinite-dimensional version to compute its cohomology.

- **Lie group [03.03.01].** The reductive group setting. Kempf-Ness needs $G$ complex reductive (so $G = K_{\mathbb{C}}$ for a compact $K$). The Cartan decomposition $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{k} \oplus i\mathfrak{k}$ is the structural input that makes the norm-square functional convex along non-compact orbit directions.

- **Hilbert scheme $\mathrm{Hilb}^P(X)$ [04.10.05].** Mumford's projective GIT moduli constructions — including $\mathcal{M}_g$ from tri-canonically embedded curves — produce $X /\!/ G$ as a quotient of an open piece of $\mathrm{Hilb}^P$ by $\mathrm{PGL}$. Kempf-Ness lifts the resulting quotient to a Kähler manifold via the moment-map identification, transferring symplectic structure to the moduli space built from the Hilbert scheme.

- **Moduli of vector bundles on a curve and slope stability [04.10.06].** Slope-polystable bundles correspond bijectively to flat unitary connections — the Narasimhan-Seshadri theorem of 1965 is the infinite-dimensional Kempf-Ness statement avant la lettre. The Atiyah-Bott 1983 reformulation builds the moduli space as the symplectic reduction of the space of unitary connections by the gauge group, with the curvature playing the role of moment map.

- **Kirwan stratification of the unstable locus [04.10.08].** The norm-square $\|\mu\|^2$ developed in this unit drives Kirwan's stratification: its upward gradient flow stratifies the unstable locus by the limit-set conjugacy class, and the Hesselink-Kirwan-Ness theorem matches the analytic stratification with the algebraic Hilbert-Mumford stratification. Kirwan's equivariant Morse theory computes the cohomology of GIT quotients via this dictionary.

- **Variation of GIT (VGIT) [04.10.09].** Different linearisations $L$ produce different moment maps $\mu_L$, hence different symplectic reductions $\mu_L^{-1}(0) / K$. The VGIT chamber structure translates into a chamber structure on moment levels, and Kempf-Ness identifies the algebraic flips across walls with the symplectic wall-crossings of the reduced spaces.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

George Kempf and Linda Ness published *The length of vectors in representation spaces* in 1979 [ref: TODO_REF KempfNess1979] (Springer LNM 732, *Algebraic Geometry, Copenhagen 1978*, pp. 233-243). The paper, modest in length, identified a phenomenon that retrospectively organised an entire mathematical landscape: the GIT quotient of Mumford's 1965 *Geometric Invariant Theory* [ref: TODO_REF MumfordGIT1965] and the symplectic reduction of Marsden-Weinstein 1974 [ref: TODO_REF MarsdenWeinstein1974] (*Rep. Math. Phys.* 5, 121-130) were the same construction in different languages.

The pre-history runs through three independent strands. *Algebraic invariant theory*: Hilbert's 1890 *Über die Theorie der algebraischen Formen* [ref: TODO_REF Hilbert1890] proved finite generation of invariant rings for reductive groups; Mumford 1965 used this to construct GIT quotients as Proj of invariant rings. *Symplectic geometry*: the moment map of Souriau (1969) and Kostant (1965) crystallised in Marsden-Weinstein 1974 with the formal definition of symplectic reduction $M /\!/_\xi K = \mu^{-1}(\xi)/K_\xi$. *Gauge theory*: Bott's 1959 work on holomorphic vector bundles and Narasimhan-Seshadri 1965 *Ann. of Math.* 82 [ref: TODO_REF NarasimhanSeshadri1965] identified polystable bundles with flat unitary representations — a finite-dimensional Kempf-Ness statement avant la lettre.

Kempf-Ness 1979 unified all three strands: the GIT quotient $V /\!/_G$ for a complex reductive group action on a Hermitian vector space is canonically homeomorphic to $\mu^{-1}(0)/K$ for the maximal compact $K \subset G$ with its induced moment map. The proof rests on a single variational identity — the gradient of the length-squared along non-compact group directions equals the moment map — plus the convexity of the length-squared along these directions, forcing unique moment-zero representatives modulo $K$.

The implications cascaded through the 1980s. Frances Kirwan's 1984 *Cohomology of Quotients in Symplectic and Algebraic Geometry* [ref: TODO_REF Kirwan1984] (Princeton, Mathematical Notes 31) developed the Morse-theoretic stratification of the unstable locus via the norm-square $\|\mu\|^2$ and used it to compute the cohomology of GIT quotients in terms of equivariant cohomology of the ambient variety. Atiyah-Bott 1983 *Phil. Trans. Royal Soc.* 308 [ref: TODO_REF AtiyahBott1983] (523-615) developed the infinite-dimensional analogue for gauge theory: the moduli space of holomorphic vector bundles on a Riemann surface is the symplectic reduction of the space of unitary connections by the gauge group, with the curvature playing the role of moment map. Their Morse-theoretic Yang-Mills calculation gave the cohomology of $\mathcal{N}(r, d)$ in closed form.

Donaldson 1985 *Proc. London Math. Soc.* 50 [ref: TODO_REF Donaldson1985] (1-26) and Uhlenbeck-Yau 1986 *Comm. Pure Appl. Math.* 39 [ref: TODO_REF UhlenbeckYau1986] (S257-S293) generalised Narasimhan-Seshadri to higher-dimensional Kähler manifolds: a holomorphic bundle is polystable iff it admits a Hermitian-Einstein metric. This is the **Kobayashi-Hitchin correspondence**, conjectured by Kobayashi (1980-82) and Hitchin (1980): the GIT-symplectic dictionary for moduli of bundles on Kähler manifolds.

The 1990s-2000s extended the picture in two directions. Hitchin 1987 *Proc. London Math. Soc.* 55 introduced the **hyperkähler reduction**: moduli of Higgs bundles arise from a triple of moment maps with quaternionic structure, generalising symplectic reduction. Yau-Tian-Donaldson conjectured (1986, 1997, 2002) that Kähler-Einstein metrics on Fano manifolds exist iff the manifold is **K-stable** — an infinite-dimensional Kempf-Ness statement. Chen-Donaldson-Sun 2015 *J. Amer. Math. Soc.* 28 (three papers, 183-278, 199-234, 235-278) proved the YTD conjecture via continuity-method extensions of the Uhlenbeck-Yau heat-flow approach.

The Kempf-Ness theorem has thus become the structural template for every modern moduli-theoretic existence theorem of canonical metrics: Narasimhan-Seshadri (curves), Donaldson-Uhlenbeck-Yau (Kähler), Chen-Donaldson-Sun (Fano), and the open Tian-Yau conjecture on Calabi-Yau metrics. Each statement reads: a holomorphic / algebraic object is polystable iff it admits a canonical metric. The proof in each case constructs the metric as a moment-zero representative via a Kempf-Ness gradient descent. The 1979 LNM paper of Kempf and Ness, fewer than a dozen pages, opened this entire programme.
