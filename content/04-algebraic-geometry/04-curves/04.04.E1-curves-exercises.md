## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Hartshorne Chapter IV is the worked-example chapter of the book: smooth projective curves over an algebraically closed field $k$, studied through Riemann-Roch and Serre duality. It opens with Riemann-Roch [04.04.01] and the basic vanishing/special-divisor dictionary, proves Hurwitz's ramification formula [04.04.02], develops the criteria for a divisor to embed a curve in projective space, treats elliptic curves [04.04.03] with their group law and $j$-invariant, and closes with the canonical embedding and the resulting classification of curves by genus. Many exercises braid several of these threads at once.

This pack collects nine exercises — two easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units rather than as a standalone development. The exercises group loosely by Hartshorne section: numerical Riemann-Roch warm-ups (easy), Hurwitz and embedding computations (medium), and elliptic-curve and canonical-embedding arguments (hard).

The conventions throughout are Hartshorne's: $X$ is a smooth projective curve of genus $g$ over $k=\bar k$; $K_X$ a canonical divisor with $\deg K_X=2g-2$ and $\ell(K_X)=g$; $\ell(D)=h^0(\mathcal{O}_X(D))$; Riemann-Roch reads $\ell(D)-\ell(K_X-D)=\deg D+1-g$. A divisor $D$ is *very ample* when $\mathcal{O}_X(D)$ embeds $X$ in $\mathbb{P}^{\ell(D)-1}$; the canonical divisor is very ample exactly when $X$ is non-hyperelliptic of genus $\geq 3$.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining eight follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *Prove that a divisor $D$ of degree $\geq 2g+1$ on a smooth projective curve $X$ of genus $g$ is very ample.*

**Solution.** A complete linear system $|D|$ is very ample iff it **separates points** and **separates tangent vectors**: for all points $P,Q\in X$ (including $P=Q$),

$$
\ell(D-P-Q)=\ell(D)-2.
$$

Separating points ($P\neq Q$) means no hyperplane through the image of $P$ is forced through $Q$; separating tangents ($P=Q$) means the embedding is an immersion at $P$. Both conditions are captured by the displayed equality (Hartshorne IV.3.1).

Apply Riemann-Roch [04.04.01] to $D$ and to $D-P-Q$. Since $\deg D\geq 2g+1$, also $\deg D\geq 2g-1$, so $\deg(K_X-D)<0$ and $\ell(K_X-D)=0$; thus $\ell(D)=\deg D+1-g$. Likewise $\deg(D-P-Q)=\deg D-2\geq 2g-1$, so $\ell(K_X-D+P+Q)=0$ and

$$
\ell(D-P-Q)=(\deg D-2)+1-g=\ell(D)-2.
$$

The equality holds for every pair $(P,Q)$, so $|D|$ separates points and tangents, hence $D$ is very ample. $\square$

This is **Hartshorne IV.3.2(b)**, the everyday embedding criterion. Two consequences route through it: every curve of genus $g$ embeds in $\mathbb{P}^3$ (take a generic $D$ of degree $2g+1$, then project), and any line bundle of large enough degree is automatically very ample, which is why ampleness and very-ampleness coincide asymptotically [04.05.05].

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).** *Genus from the canonical degree.*

A smooth projective curve $X$ has canonical divisor $K_X$ with $\deg K_X=12$. Find the genus $g$ and the dimension $\ell(K_X)$ of the space of global differentials.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\deg K_X=2g-2$ and $\ell(K_X)=g$ [04.08.02], [04.04.01].

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The canonical degree is $2g-2$. Setting $2g-2=12$ gives $g=7$.

The space of global differentials $H^0(X,\omega_X)$ has dimension equal to the genus, so $\ell(K_X)=g=7$. (One can also confirm via Riemann-Roch applied to $D=K_X$: $\ell(K_X)-\ell(0)=\deg K_X+1-g=12+1-7=6$, and $\ell(0)=1$, giving $\ell(K_X)=7$.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Riemann-Roch for a generic divisor.*

On a curve of genus $4$, compute $\ell(D)$ for a divisor $D$ of degree $10$, and for a divisor $D'$ of degree $5$ in general position.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

When $\deg D>2g-2$, the index $\ell(K_X-D)$ vanishes and Riemann-Roch is exact. For $\deg D'=5$ between $g-1$ and $2g-2$, "general position" means $\ell(K_X-D')=0$ as well.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Here $g=4$, so $\deg K_X=2g-2=6$.

For $\deg D=10>6$: $\deg(K_X-D)=6-10=-4<0$, so $\ell(K_X-D)=0$ and

$$
\ell(D)=\deg D+1-g=10+1-4=7.
$$

For $\deg D'=5$: now $\deg(K_X-D')=6-5=1\geq 0$, so $\ell(K_X-D')$ could be nonzero. For a *general* effective divisor of degree $5$, the special divisor $K_X-D'$ of degree $1$ is non-effective (a general degree-$1$ class has $\ell=0$ unless $g=0$), so $\ell(K_X-D')=0$ and

$$
\ell(D')=\deg D'+1-g=5+1-4=2.
$$

(For a *special* $D'$ — e.g. if $K_X-D'$ happens to be an effective point — $\ell(D')$ could jump to $3$; the genus-$4$ curve is the canonical curve in $\mathbb{P}^3$ and its $g^1_3$ is the special system.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).** *Hurwitz for a hyperelliptic curve.*

A hyperelliptic curve $X$ of genus $g$ admits a degree-$2$ map $f:X\to\mathbb{P}^1$. Use the Riemann-Hurwitz formula to determine the number of branch points.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Riemann-Hurwitz [04.04.02]: $2g-2=\deg f\cdot(2\cdot 0-2)+\sum_P(e_P-1)$. A double cover in characteristic $\neq 2$ has only simple ($e_P=2$) ramification.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The target $\mathbb{P}^1$ has genus $0$. With $\deg f=2$, Riemann-Hurwitz reads

$$
2g-2=2\,(2\cdot 0-2)+\sum_{P}(e_P-1)=-4+\sum_P(e_P-1).
$$

In characteristic $0$ (or $\neq 2$) a degree-$2$ map is tamely ramified, and every ramification point has $e_P=2$ (the fiber over a branch point is a single point counted twice), contributing $e_P-1=1$ each. If there are $r$ branch points,

$$
2g-2=-4+r\quad\Longrightarrow\quad r=2g+2.
$$

So a hyperelliptic curve of genus $g$ is a double cover of $\mathbb{P}^1$ branched over exactly $2g+2$ points — the Weierstrass points. For $g=1$ this gives $4$ branch points, matching the four roots (including $\infty$) of a Legendre-form elliptic curve [04.04.03]; for $g=2$, six branch points.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).** *The $j$-invariant of an elliptic curve.*

For the Weierstrass curve $y^2=x^3+ax+b$ over $k$ ($\mathrm{char}\,k\neq 2,3$), recall the discriminant $\Delta=-16(4a^3+27b^2)$ and the $j$-invariant $j=-1728\,(4a)^3/\Delta$. Show that two such curves are isomorphic iff they have the same $j$, and compute $j$ for $y^2=x^3+x$ and $y^2=x^3+1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The only coordinate changes preserving Weierstrass form are $x\mapsto u^2x,\ y\mapsto u^3 y$, scaling $(a,b)\mapsto(u^4a,u^6b)$. Check $j$ is invariant and that it pins the pair $(a,b)$ up to this scaling.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

An admissible change of variable preserving short Weierstrass form is $x=u^2x'$, $y=u^3y'$ for $u\in k^\times$, sending $(a,b)\mapsto(u^4a,u^6b)$ and $\Delta\mapsto u^{12}\Delta$. Under this, $(4a)^3/\Delta\mapsto u^{12}(4a)^3/(u^{12}\Delta)$ is unchanged, so $j$ is an isomorphism invariant.

Conversely, given equal $j$, the ratio $a^3:b^2$ is determined (since $j$ is a function of $4a^3/(4a^3+27b^2)$). If both $a,b\neq 0$, solve $u^4=a/a'$ and check the same $u$ gives $u^6=b/b'$ using equal $j$; the degenerate cases $a=0$ ($j=0$) and $b=0$ ($j=1728$) are handled by the corresponding scalings. So equal $j$ implies isomorphic curves over $\bar k$.

Compute. For $y^2=x^3+x$: $a=1,b=0$, so $\Delta=-16\cdot 4=-64$ and

$$
j=-1728\cdot\frac{(4)^3}{-64}=-1728\cdot\frac{64}{-64}=1728.
$$

For $y^2=x^3+1$: $a=0,b=1$, so $(4a)^3=0$ and $j=0$.

Thus $y^2=x^3+x$ has $j=1728$ (the curve with extra automorphism $i$, of order $4$) and $y^2=x^3+1$ has $j=0$ (the curve with the order-$6$ automorphism). These are exactly the two curves with automorphism groups larger than $\{\pm 1\}$, Hartshorne IV.4.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).** *Genus of a curve of bidegree $(a,b)$ on a quadric.*

Let $X\subset\mathbb{P}^1\times\mathbb{P}^1$ be a smooth curve of bidegree $(a,b)$ (class $a\,\{pt\}\times\mathbb{P}^1+b\,\mathbb{P}^1\times\{pt\}$). Compute its genus.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Adjunction on the surface $Q=\mathbb{P}^1\times\mathbb{P}^1$: $2g-2=X\cdot(X+K_Q)$, with $K_Q$ of bidegree $(-2,-2)$ and the intersection product on $Q$ given by $(a,b)\cdot(c,d)=ad+bc$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On $Q=\mathbb{P}^1\times\mathbb{P}^1$ the intersection pairing on divisor classes is $(a,b)\cdot(c,d)=ad+bc$, and the canonical class is $K_Q=(-2,-2)$ [04.08.02]. The adjunction formula gives $2g-2=X\cdot(X+K_Q)$ for the smooth curve $X$ of class $(a,b)$.

Compute $X+K_Q=(a-2,b-2)$, so

$$
X\cdot(X+K_Q)=(a,b)\cdot(a-2,b-2)=a(b-2)+b(a-2)=2ab-2a-2b.
$$

Hence $2g-2=2ab-2a-2b$, i.e.

$$
g=ab-a-b+1=(a-1)(b-1).
$$

Checks: bidegree $(1,1)$ (a "diagonal" conic, rational) gives $g=0$; bidegree $(2,2)$ gives $g=1$ (an elliptic curve as a $(2,2)$ on the quadric); bidegree $(2,3)$ gives $g=2$. This is Hartshorne IV.1 Example / Exercise, the quadric-surface analogue of the plane genus-degree formula [from 04.03.E1 Exercise 3].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).** *The canonical embedding for non-hyperelliptic curves.*

Let $X$ be a smooth projective curve of genus $g\geq 3$. Prove that the canonical divisor $K_X$ is very ample iff $X$ is not hyperelliptic, embedding non-hyperelliptic $X$ as a curve of degree $2g-2$ in $\mathbb{P}^{g-1}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

By the point-and-tangent criterion (lead exercise), $K_X$ fails to separate $P,Q$ iff $\ell(K_X-P-Q)=\ell(K_X)-1=g-1$. By Riemann-Roch this is equivalent to $\ell(P+Q)=2$, i.e. a $g^1_2$, i.e. hyperelliptic.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The complete canonical system $|K_X|$ has $\ell(K_X)=g$, so it maps $X\to\mathbb{P}^{g-1}$. By the very-ampleness criterion (lead exercise) $|K_X|$ is very ample iff for all points $P,Q$,

$$
\ell(K_X-P-Q)=\ell(K_X)-2=g-2.
$$

Apply Riemann-Roch [04.04.01] to $D=P+Q$ (degree $2$):

$$
\ell(P+Q)-\ell(K_X-P-Q)=\deg(P+Q)+1-g=2+1-g=3-g.
$$

Since $\ell(K_X)=g$, the failure $\ell(K_X-P-Q)=g-1$ (one bigger than the very-ample value $g-2$) is equivalent, by the displayed equation, to

$$
\ell(P+Q)=(g-1)+(3-g)=2.
$$

But $\ell(P+Q)=2$ means the degree-$2$ divisor $P+Q$ moves in a pencil — a $g^1_2$ — which by definition makes $X$ **hyperelliptic** (the pencil gives a degree-$2$ map $X\to\mathbb{P}^1$).

So $|K_X|$ fails to separate some pair $P,Q$ iff $X$ is hyperelliptic. Contrapositive: $X$ non-hyperelliptic $\Rightarrow K_X$ very ample, giving an embedding $X\hookrightarrow\mathbb{P}^{g-1}$ of degree $\deg K_X=2g-2$. This is the **canonical embedding** (Hartshorne IV.5.2). For $g=3$ it realizes a non-hyperelliptic curve as a smooth plane quartic; for $g=4$, as the complete intersection of a quadric and a cubic in $\mathbb{P}^3$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).** *Every genus-1 curve with a point is a plane cubic.*

Let $(X,P_0)$ be a smooth projective curve of genus $1$ with a chosen point $P_0$. Show that the linear system $|3P_0|$ embeds $X$ as a smooth cubic curve in $\mathbb{P}^2$, and that $P_0$ becomes an inflection point.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\ell(nP_0)$ by Riemann-Roch for small $n$. Degree $3=2g+1$ on a genus-$1$ curve is very ample (lead exercise). The image has degree $3$ in $\mathbb{P}^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For genus $g=1$, $\deg K_X=0$ and $\ell(K_X)=1$, so $K_X\sim 0$ (the canonical class is the zero class, $\mathcal{O}_X$). Riemann-Roch [04.04.01] applied to $D=nP_0$ ($n\geq 1$) gives $\deg(K_X-nP_0)=-n<0$, so $\ell(K_X-nP_0)=0$ and

$$
\ell(nP_0)=n+1-g=n\quad(n\geq 1).
$$

Thus $\ell(P_0)=1$, $\ell(2P_0)=2$, $\ell(3P_0)=3$. The degree $\deg(3P_0)=3=2g+1$, so $|3P_0|$ is very ample by the lead exercise, embedding $X\hookrightarrow\mathbb{P}^{\ell(3P_0)-1}=\mathbb{P}^2$. The image has degree equal to $\deg(3P_0)=3$, a smooth plane cubic.

A basis of $L(3P_0)$ is $\{1,x,y\}$ with $x\in L(2P_0)\setminus L(P_0)$ (a pole of order $2$ at $P_0$) and $y\in L(3P_0)\setminus L(2P_0)$ (pole of order $3$). The seven functions $1,x,y,x^2,xy,y^2,x^3$ lie in the $6$-dimensional $L(6P_0)$, so satisfy one linear relation — a Weierstrass cubic $y^2+\cdots=x^3+\cdots$ after scaling. The point $P_0$ maps to the unique point at infinity $[0:1:0]$, where the tangent line $\{Z=0\}$ meets the cubic with multiplicity $3$: $P_0$ is an inflection (flex) point. This is exactly the construction of the group law [04.04.03]: $P_0$ is the identity, and collinearity of three points means their sum is $0$ (Hartshorne IV.4.6).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Clifford-type bound and the genus-2 classification.*

Show that every smooth projective curve of genus $2$ is hyperelliptic, and that its canonical map is the degree-$2$ map to $\mathbb{P}^1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $g=2$, $\deg K_X=2$ and $\ell(K_X)=2$, so $|K_X|$ is a $g^1_2$. A base-point-free $g^1_2$ is a degree-$2$ map.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For genus $g=2$: $\deg K_X=2g-2=2$ and $\ell(K_X)=g=2$. So the canonical system $|K_X|$ is a linear system of dimension $\ell(K_X)-1=1$ and degree $2$ — a $g^1_2$.

First, $|K_X|$ is **base-point free**. If $P$ were a base point, then $\ell(K_X-P)=\ell(K_X)=2$; but $\deg(K_X-P)=1$, and Riemann-Roch gives $\ell(K_X-P)-\ell(P)=1+1-2=0$, so $\ell(K_X-P)=\ell(P)$. On a curve of genus $\geq 1$, $\ell(P)=1$ (no nonconstant function with a single simple pole, else $X\cong\mathbb{P}^1$). Hence $\ell(K_X-P)=1\neq 2$, contradiction. So $|K_X|$ has no base points.

A base-point-free $g^1_2$ defines a morphism $\varphi_{|K_X|}:X\to\mathbb{P}^1$ of degree $2$ (the degree equals $\deg K_X=2$). A degree-$2$ map to $\mathbb{P}^1$ is exactly a hyperelliptic structure. Therefore **every genus-$2$ curve is hyperelliptic**, and its canonical map *is* the hyperelliptic double cover.

This also shows the canonical map is not an embedding for $g=2$ (it is $2$-to-$1$), consistent with Exercise 6: the canonical embedding requires non-hyperelliptic, which forces $g\geq 3$. By Riemann-Hurwitz [04.04.02] (Exercise 3) this double cover branches over $2g+2=6$ points, so every genus-$2$ curve is $y^2=f_6(x)$ for a sextic $f_6$ with distinct roots. Hartshorne IV.5.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (medium, proof).** *Curves of genus 0 are projective lines.*

Let $X$ be a smooth projective curve of genus $0$ over $k=\bar k$ with a $k$-rational point $P$. Show $X\cong\mathbb{P}^1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\ell(P)$ by Riemann-Roch. A degree-$1$ divisor with $\ell=2$ gives a degree-$1$ map to $\mathbb{P}^1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For genus $g=0$, $\deg K_X=-2$. Apply Riemann-Roch [04.04.01] to $D=P$ (degree $1$): $\deg(K_X-P)=-3<0$, so $\ell(K_X-P)=0$ and

$$
\ell(P)=\deg P+1-g=1+1-0=2.
$$

So $L(P)$ is $2$-dimensional, spanned by $1$ and a nonconstant function $f$ with a single simple pole at $P$. The map $f:X\to\mathbb{P}^1$ (sending $P\mapsto\infty$) has degree equal to the number of poles counted with multiplicity, namely $1$. A degree-$1$ morphism of smooth projective curves is an isomorphism (it is finite, birational, and the target is normal). Hence $X\cong\mathbb{P}^1$.

This completes the bottom of the **classification by genus**: $g=0$ gives $\mathbb{P}^1$; $g=1$ gives the plane cubics / elliptic curves (Exercise 7); $g=2$ the hyperelliptic sextic double covers (Exercise 8); $g\geq 3$ splits into hyperelliptic and canonically-embedded non-hyperelliptic curves (Exercise 6). Over $k=\bar k$ every genus-$0$ curve has a rational point, so the hypothesis is automatic.

</details>

</aside>
