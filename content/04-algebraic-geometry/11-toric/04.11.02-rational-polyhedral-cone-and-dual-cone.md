## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine you have a handful of arrows in the plane, all pointing outward from the origin. If you only let yourself add the arrows together — and scale them by nonnegative real numbers — the region you can reach is a wedge-shaped territory bounded by your outermost arrows. That wedge is a **rational polyhedral cone**: the set of all nonnegative real combinations of finitely many integer-vector arrows in the lattice $N = \mathbb{Z}^n$. The word "rational" records that the generating arrows are integer vectors; "polyhedral" records that the cone has flat walls; and "cone" records that the region is closed under nonnegative scaling.

Every such cone has a partner living in the dual lattice $M = \mathbb{Z}^n$. The **dual cone** $\sigma^\vee$ is the set of all integer-pairing functionals on $M$ that come out nonnegative on every vector in $\sigma$. In two dimensions, if $\sigma$ is the first quadrant, then $\sigma^\vee$ is also the first quadrant — same shape, sitting in the dual space. In general $\sigma^\vee$ records the linear inequalities that carve out $\sigma$: every wall of $\sigma$ corresponds to a generator of $\sigma^\vee$, and every wall of $\sigma^\vee$ corresponds to a generator of $\sigma$. Generators on one side; defining inequalities on the other. That is the duality.

Why bother? Because rational polyhedral cones are the combinatorial Lego pieces from which toric varieties are built. A cone in $N_\mathbb{R}$ produces an affine algebraic variety via the semigroup algebra of the lattice points in its dual cone. The whole dictionary that turns toric varieties into combinatorics flows through this single construction, and the duality $\sigma \leftrightarrow \sigma^\vee$ is the bookkeeping that makes the dictionary bidirectional.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take $N = \mathbb{Z}^2$ with real form $N_\mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}^2$, and consider the cone generated by the two standard basis vectors $e_1 = (1, 0)$ and $e_2 = (0, 1)$. Compute the dual cone, verify the double-dual recovers the original, and identify the integer semigroup of lattice points in the dual cone.

Step 1. Form the cone $\sigma = \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} e_1 + \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} e_2 \subset \mathbb{R}^2$. A point $u = (u_1, u_2)$ lies in $\sigma$ if and only if $u_1, u_2 \geq 0$. So $\sigma$ is the closed first quadrant.

Step 2. Compute the dual cone $\sigma^\vee$ inside $M_\mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}^2$. By definition, $\sigma^\vee = \{m = (m_1, m_2) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : \langle m, u\rangle \geq 0 \text{ for all } u \in \sigma\}$, where the pairing is the integer dot product $\langle m, u\rangle = m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2$.

It suffices to test the condition on the generators $e_1$ and $e_2$ (because the pairing is linear in $u$): $\langle m, e_1\rangle = m_1 \geq 0$ and $\langle m, e_2\rangle = m_2 \geq 0$. So $\sigma^\vee$ is the set of $(m_1, m_2)$ with $m_1 \geq 0$ and $m_2 \geq 0$, namely the closed first quadrant of $M_\mathbb{R}$. The dual cone is generated by the dual basis vectors $e_1^* = (1, 0)$ and $e_2^* = (0, 1)$.

Step 3. Verify the double dual. Apply the same recipe to $\sigma^\vee$: a point $u = (u_1, u_2) \in N_\mathbb{R}$ lies in $(\sigma^\vee)^\vee$ iff $\langle m, u\rangle \geq 0$ for all $m \in \sigma^\vee$. Testing on generators $e_1^*$ and $e_2^*$: $\langle e_1^*, u\rangle = u_1 \geq 0$ and $\langle e_2^*, u\rangle = u_2 \geq 0$. So $(\sigma^\vee)^\vee$ is the closed first quadrant in $N_\mathbb{R}$, recovering $\sigma$.

Step 4. Compute the semigroup of lattice points in the dual cone. The set $\sigma^\vee \cap M = \{(m_1, m_2) \in \mathbb{Z}^2 : m_1, m_2 \geq 0\} = \mathbb{Z}^2_{\geq 0}$. This is a commutative semigroup under addition, finitely generated by $e_1^*$ and $e_2^*$. The monoid algebra is $\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M] = \mathbb{C}[x, y]$, the polynomial ring in two variables, where the variable $x = \chi^{e_1^*}$ corresponds to the generator $e_1^*$ and $y = \chi^{e_2^*}$ to $e_2^*$.

What this tells us. The cone $\sigma$ produces, via the semigroup $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ and its monoid algebra, the polynomial ring $\mathbb{C}[x, y]$ — the coordinate ring of $\mathbb{C}^2$. This is the simplest example of the affine-toric-variety construction: the first-quadrant cone in $N_\mathbb{R}$ produces affine $2$-space $\mathbb{C}^2$ with its action of the torus $T = (\mathbb{C}^*)^2$ by coordinate-wise multiplication. The duality computation is the bookkeeping that makes this construction work, and the semigroup step is exactly where Gordan's lemma will pay rent in the formal development below.

## Check your understanding [Beginner]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise (easy, multiple choice).**

A rational polyhedral cone $\sigma \subset N_\mathbb{R}$ is best described as:

A. The set of nonnegative real combinations of finitely many lattice vectors in $N$.  
B. The set of all integer combinations of finitely many lattice vectors in $N$.  
C. The closure of an open ball around the origin in $N_\mathbb{R}$.  
D. The set of all real combinations of finitely many lattice vectors in $N$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The word "cone" forces the scaling coefficients to be nonnegative; the word "polyhedral" forces finitely many generators; the word "rational" forces the generators to be lattice vectors.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**A.** A rational polyhedral cone is the set of nonnegative real combinations of finitely many lattice vectors $u_1, \ldots, u_k \in N$, that is, $\sigma = \{a_1 u_1 + \cdots + a_k u_k : a_i \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\}$. Option B (integer combinations only) gives a sublattice of $N$, not a cone in $N_\mathbb{R}$. Option C (a ball) is not even closed under scaling, much less a cone. Option D (arbitrary real combinations) gives the linear span of the generators, a vector subspace of $N_\mathbb{R}$ rather than a cone. The three modifiers — nonnegative scaling, finitely many generators, lattice generators — pin down exactly the rational polyhedral cones.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise (easy, true-false).**

True or false: if $\sigma \subset N_\mathbb{R}$ is a rational polyhedral cone and $-\sigma$ denotes the cone $\{-u : u \in \sigma\}$, then $\sigma \cap (-\sigma)$ is a linear subspace of $N_\mathbb{R}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A vector $u$ is in $\sigma \cap (-\sigma)$ exactly when both $u$ and $-u$ are in $\sigma$. Verify directly that this set is closed under addition and arbitrary real scaling.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.** If $u, v$ are both in $\sigma \cap (-\sigma)$, then $u + v \in \sigma$ (since $\sigma$ is closed under addition) and $-(u + v) = -u + (-v) \in \sigma$ as well, so $u + v \in \sigma \cap (-\sigma)$. For nonnegative scaling $a \geq 0$ we have $au \in \sigma$ and $-au = a(-u) \in \sigma$. For negative scaling $a < 0$ we have $au = |a|(-u) \in \sigma$ and $-au = |a| u \in \sigma$.

Combined, $\sigma \cap (-\sigma)$ is closed under arbitrary real scaling and addition, hence is a linear subspace. This subspace is called the **linearity space** or lineality space of $\sigma$, and the cone is called **strongly convex** when this subspace is the zero space.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise (easy, multiple choice).**

Inside $N_\mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}^2$, the cone $\sigma$ generated by $(1, 0)$ and $(-1, 0)$ is:

A. The closed first quadrant.  
B. The horizontal line $\{(x, 0) : x \in \mathbb{R}\}$.  
C. The entire plane.  
D. The closed upper half-plane.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Nonnegative real combinations of $(1, 0)$ and $(-1, 0)$ produce points of the form $a (1, 0) + b (-1, 0) = (a - b, 0)$ for $a, b \geq 0$. What values can $a - b$ take?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Nonnegative real combinations of $(1, 0)$ and $(-1, 0)$ are all of the form $(a - b, 0)$ with $a, b \geq 0$, and $a - b$ ranges over all of $\mathbb{R}$ as $a$ and $b$ range over the nonnegative reals. The cone is therefore the horizontal line $\{(x, 0) : x \in \mathbb{R}\}$. The cone equals its own negative, $\sigma = -\sigma$, so $\sigma \cap (-\sigma) = \sigma$ has dimension $1$, and the cone is **not** strongly convex. Cones of this shape are exactly the linear subspaces of $N_\mathbb{R}$ that happen to be cones — non-strongly-convex by construction.

</details>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $N$ be a free abelian group of rank $n$ (a lattice) and let $M = \mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(N, \mathbb{Z})$ be its dual lattice, with integer pairing $\langle -, -\rangle : M \times N \to \mathbb{Z}$. Write $N_\mathbb{R} = N \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{R}$ and $M_\mathbb{R} = M \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{R}$ for the real forms, dual real vector spaces under the $\mathbb{R}$-linear extension of the pairing. (For motivation, $N$ and $M$ are the cocharacter and character lattices of a split algebraic torus from `04.11.01`.)

**Definition (rational polyhedral cone).** A **rational polyhedral cone** in $N_\mathbb{R}$ is a subset of the form
$$
\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(u_1, \ldots, u_k) = \{a_1 u_1 + \cdots + a_k u_k : a_i \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}\}
$$
for some finite list of lattice vectors $u_1, \ldots, u_k \in N$. The vectors $u_1, \ldots, u_k$ are called **generators** of $\sigma$.

**Definition (dual cone).** Given any subset $S \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$, the **dual cone** is
$$
S^\vee = \{m \in M_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, u\rangle \geq 0 \text{ for all } u \in S\} \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}.
$$
When $S = \sigma$ is a rational polyhedral cone generated by $u_1, \ldots, u_k$, the dual cone simplifies to $\sigma^\vee = \{m \in M_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, u_i\rangle \geq 0 \text{ for } i = 1, \ldots, k\}$ — the intersection of finitely many closed half-spaces. The dual cone is thus a **rational polyhedral cone in the half-space sense** before we prove (via the Minkowski-Weyl identification) that it is also rational polyhedral in the generator sense.

**Definition (strongly convex).** A cone $\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ is **strongly convex** if $\sigma \cap (-\sigma) = \{0\}$, equivalently if $\sigma$ contains no positive-dimensional linear subspace. The intersection $\sigma \cap (-\sigma)$ is called the **lineality space** of $\sigma$ and is always a linear subspace (Exercise above).

**Definition (face).** A **face** of a rational polyhedral cone $\sigma$ is a subset $\tau = \sigma \cap H_m$ for some $m \in \sigma^\vee$, where $H_m = \{u \in N_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, u\rangle = 0\}$ is the supporting hyperplane to $\sigma$ at $m$. The cone $\sigma$ is itself a face (take $m = 0$); the lineality space $\sigma \cap (-\sigma)$ is a face (it lies in $H_m$ for every $m \in \sigma^\vee \cap (-\sigma^\vee)$); and every face of $\sigma$ is itself a rational polyhedral cone in $N_\mathbb{R}$.

**Definition (dimension; ray; facet).** The **dimension** of $\sigma$ is the real dimension of its linear span; $\sigma$ is **full-dimensional** if $\dim \sigma = n$. A **ray** of $\sigma$ is a one-dimensional face. A **facet** of $\sigma$ is a face of codimension one (i.e., of dimension $\dim \sigma - 1$).

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The set $\{(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : x \geq 0, y \geq 0, y \leq x^2\}$ is convex, closed under nonnegative scaling, but is *not* polyhedral — it has a curved boundary. "Polyhedral" rules out smooth boundary pieces and forces finitely many flat walls.

- The set generated by $(1, 0)$ and $(\sqrt{2}, 1)$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is polyhedral (a wedge with two flat walls) but is *not* rational polyhedral in the standard lattice $\mathbb{Z}^2$ — the second generator is irrational. The "rational" hypothesis forces lattice generators, which is what lets us extract finiteness statements like Gordan's lemma below.

- The first quadrant $\sigma = \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^2 \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ has dual $\sigma^\vee = \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^2 \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ — self-dual under the dot product. But take instead $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1) = \{(x, 0) : x \geq 0\} \subset \mathbb{R}^2$. Its dual is $\sigma^\vee = \{(m_1, m_2) : m_1 \geq 0\}$, a closed half-plane — much larger than $\sigma$. The dual cone is generally *not* the same as the original; self-duality is a feature of full-dimensional simplicial cones with orthogonal generators.

- "Face" in convex-geometry usage is broader than "facet". The empty face (taking $m \in \mathrm{int}(\sigma^\vee)$ produces the face $\sigma \cap H_m = \sigma \cap (-\sigma)$, the lineality space, which may be just $\{0\}$) is a valid face, as is the whole cone $\sigma$ (take $m = 0$). Facets are codimension-one faces; rays are one-dimensional faces; both are special cases.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem of this unit is the **biduality** of rational polyhedral cones — that the double dual recovers the original — together with **Gordan's lemma** on finite generation of the semigroup of lattice points in the dual cone. Both are proved here; the Master tier expands to full proofs of the equivalence chain for strong convexity and the face-correspondence theorem.

**Theorem (cone duality / Farkas).** *Let $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(u_1, \ldots, u_k) \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ be a finitely generated rational polyhedral cone. Then $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \sigma$, where on the left we apply duality once $N_\mathbb{R} \to M_\mathbb{R}$ via $\sigma \mapsto \sigma^\vee$ and again $M_\mathbb{R} \to N_\mathbb{R}$ via $\sigma^\vee \mapsto (\sigma^\vee)^\vee$, identifying $(N_\mathbb{R})^* = M_\mathbb{R}$ and $(M_\mathbb{R})^* = N_\mathbb{R}$ via the pairing.*

**Proof.** The argument has two directions. First, $\sigma \subseteq \sigma^{\vee\vee}$ is straightforward. Second, $\sigma^{\vee\vee} \subseteq \sigma$ uses the finite-dimensional separating-hyperplane theorem.

**Direction 1: $\sigma \subseteq \sigma^{\vee\vee}$.** Take $u \in \sigma$. For any $m \in \sigma^\vee$, the defining condition of $\sigma^\vee$ gives $\langle m, u\rangle \geq 0$. So $u$ satisfies the defining condition of $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \{u' \in N_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, u'\rangle \geq 0 \text{ for all } m \in \sigma^\vee\}$. Hence $u \in \sigma^{\vee\vee}$.

**Direction 2: $\sigma^{\vee\vee} \subseteq \sigma$.** Take $u_0 \in N_\mathbb{R}$ with $u_0 \notin \sigma$. It suffices to find $m_0 \in \sigma^\vee$ with $\langle m_0, u_0\rangle < 0$ — this exhibits $u_0 \notin \sigma^{\vee\vee}$. The cone $\sigma$ is a closed convex subset of the finite-dimensional real vector space $N_\mathbb{R}$ (closed because it is the image of the closed orthant $\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^k$ under the continuous linear map $(a_1, \ldots, a_k) \mapsto \sum a_i u_i$; convex because nonnegative real combinations of generators stay in $\sigma$). By the finite-dimensional separating-hyperplane theorem (Hahn-Banach in finite dimensions, or directly: minimise the Euclidean distance from $u_0$ to the closed convex set $\sigma$ to find the foot $u^* \in \sigma$, and the vector $u_0 - u^*$ together with its dual functional separates), there exists a linear functional $m_0 \in M_\mathbb{R}$ such that $\langle m_0, u_0\rangle < c \leq \langle m_0, u\rangle$ for all $u \in \sigma$. Since $\sigma$ is a cone (closed under nonnegative scaling), the infimum $c = \inf_{u \in \sigma} \langle m_0, u\rangle$ is either $0$ (attained at $0 \in \sigma$) or $-\infty$; the strict separation $\langle m_0, u_0\rangle < c$ rules out $-\infty$, so $c = 0$, giving $\langle m_0, u\rangle \geq 0$ for all $u \in \sigma$ — that is, $m_0 \in \sigma^\vee$ — and $\langle m_0, u_0\rangle < 0$. So $u_0 \notin \sigma^{\vee\vee}$, proving $\sigma^{\vee\vee} \subseteq \sigma$. $\square$

The cone-duality theorem is the integer / rational shadow of the Minkowski-Weyl theorem: every finitely generated convex cone in a finite-dimensional real vector space is the intersection of finitely many closed half-spaces, and every such intersection is a finitely generated convex cone. The two descriptions — "$H$-representation" (intersection of half-spaces) and "$V$-representation" (nonnegative span of finitely many generators) — exchange under duality, so $\sigma^\vee$ has both descriptions, hence is itself a finitely generated rational polyhedral cone.

**Theorem (Gordan's lemma).** *Let $\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ be a rational polyhedral cone and let $S = \sigma^\vee \cap M$ be the additive semigroup of lattice points in the dual cone. Then $S$ is finitely generated as a commutative monoid: there exist $m_1, \ldots, m_r \in S$ such that every $m \in S$ is a nonnegative integer combination $m = b_1 m_1 + \cdots + b_r m_r$ with $b_i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$.*

**Proof.** Since $\sigma^\vee$ is itself a finitely generated rational polyhedral cone (by the duality theorem above, applied to the cone $\sigma$ generated by lattice vectors: $\sigma^\vee$ is the intersection of finitely many lattice-rational half-spaces, hence the Minkowski-Weyl direction gives $\sigma^\vee = \mathrm{Cone}(m'_1, \ldots, m'_s)$ for lattice vectors $m'_j \in M$ — one can in fact take the $m'_j$ to be lattice vectors by choosing the half-space normals $u_i \in N$ and then constructing the extremal rays of $\sigma^\vee$, each of which is a rational ray and hence has a primitive lattice generator). Set
$$
P = \{a_1 m'_1 + \cdots + a_s m'_s : a_j \in [0, 1] \cap \mathbb{R}\} \subseteq M_\mathbb{R},
$$
the parallelepiped spanned by the lattice generators of $\sigma^\vee$. $P$ is a bounded closed subset of $M_\mathbb{R}$, so the intersection $P \cap M$ is a finite set of lattice points. Take $m_1, \ldots, m_r$ to be the union of (i) the generators $m'_1, \ldots, m'_s$ themselves, and (ii) all lattice points in $P \cap M$.

Now let $m \in S = \sigma^\vee \cap M$. Write $m = \sum_j a_j m'_j$ with $a_j \in \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$. Split each coefficient as $a_j = \lfloor a_j \rfloor + \{a_j\}$, with $\lfloor a_j \rfloor \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ and $\{a_j\} \in [0, 1)$. Set $m' = \sum_j \lfloor a_j \rfloor m'_j$ and $m'' = \sum_j \{a_j\} m'_j$, so $m = m' + m''$. The piece $m'$ is a nonnegative integer combination of $m'_1, \ldots, m'_s$. The piece $m'' \in P$ (since each $\{a_j\} \in [0, 1)$); moreover $m'' = m - m' \in M$ (as the difference of two lattice points), so $m'' \in P \cap M$. Hence $m'' \in \{m_1, \ldots, m_r\}$ by construction, and the decomposition $m = m' + m''$ exhibits $m$ as a nonnegative integer combination of the generators in $\{m_1, \ldots, m_r\}$. The monoid $S$ is finitely generated. $\square$

The monoid algebra $\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ — viewed as a $\mathbb{C}$-vector space with basis the formal symbols $\{\chi^m : m \in \sigma^\vee \cap M\}$ and multiplication $\chi^m \chi^{m'} = \chi^{m + m'}$ — is therefore a finitely generated $\mathbb{C}$-algebra, hence a finitely generated commutative ring, hence the coordinate ring of an affine algebraic variety. This affine variety, denoted $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$, is the **affine toric variety associated to $\sigma$**, constructed in the next unit `04.11.03`. Gordan's lemma is the foundational reason the construction produces a Noetherian object — without finite generation the $\mathrm{Spec}$ would not be of finite type and the dictionary between cones and varieties would not be algebraic in the usual sense.

**Bridge.** The duality $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \sigma$ and Gordan's lemma build toward the entire affine-toric-variety construction, and the central insight is that a rational polyhedral cone in $N_\mathbb{R}$ has two interchangeable descriptions — by its generators on the $N$-side, and by its defining inequalities on the $M$-side — and the duality is exactly the dictionary between them. This is exactly the foundational reason that the toric construction $\sigma \mapsto U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ is well-defined: Gordan's lemma supplies the finite generation of the semigroup algebra, and cone duality identifies the cone $\sigma$ with the cone-of-cones $\sigma^{\vee\vee}$ so that the construction does not depend on the choice of generators. Putting these together, the rational polyhedral cone formalism generalises the everyday notion of "wedge in the plane" to a combinatorial gadget whose dual encodes the equational data of an affine variety. The bridge is dual to the duality of $M$ and $N$ from `04.11.01`: every concept on the $N$-side has a corresponding concept on the $M$-side, and the cone duality identifies $\sigma$ with $\sigma^{\vee\vee}$ in the same way that the lattice duality identifies $M$ with $\mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(N, \mathbb{Z})$. This pattern appears again in `04.11.03` (affine toric varieties from cones), in `04.11.04` (fans of cones and the global toric variety $X_\Sigma$), and in `04.11.05` (orbit-cone correspondence), each of which reads a piece of geometry out of the combinatorics developed here.

Let $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(2 e_1 + e_2, e_1 + 2 e_2) \subset N_\mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}^2$. Compute the dual cone $\sigma^\vee \subset M_\mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}^2$ as an intersection of half-spaces, then identify its ray generators.

The defining half-spaces for $\sigma^\vee$ come directly from the generators of $\sigma$: $\sigma^\vee = \{m : \langle m, 2 e_1 + e_2\rangle \geq 0, \langle m, e_1 + 2 e_2\rangle \geq 0\}$. To find ray generators, intersect each pair of consecutive defining half-space boundaries.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The dual cone is

Which of the following cones in $N_\mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}^2$ is strongly convex?

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Prove the equivalence chain: a rational polyhedral cone $\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ is strongly convex if and only if $\sigma^\vee$ spans $M_\mathbb{R}$, that is, $\sigma^\vee$ is full-dimensional in $M_\mathbb{R}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The lineality space of $\sigma$ is $\sigma \cap (-\sigma)$, the largest linear subspace contained in $\sigma$. The lineality space of $\sigma^\vee$ is $\sigma^\vee \cap (-\sigma^\vee) = \{m : \langle m, u\rangle = 0 \text{ for all } u \in \sigma\}$ — the annihilator of the linear span of $\sigma$. Connect annihilators on the two sides.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Step 1: lineality spaces.** The lineality space of $\sigma$ is $W := \sigma \cap (-\sigma)$, the largest linear subspace inside $\sigma$. The lineality space of $\sigma^\vee$ is $W' := \sigma^\vee \cap (-\sigma^\vee) = \{m \in M_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, u\rangle \geq 0 \text{ and } -\langle m, u\rangle \geq 0 \text{ for all } u \in \sigma\} = \{m : \langle m, u\rangle = 0 \text{ for all } u \in \sigma\}$, the annihilator in $M_\mathbb{R}$ of the linear span of $\sigma$.

**Step 2: annihilator relation.** Let $V = \mathrm{span}\,\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ and let $V^\perp \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ be its annihilator under the pairing. Then $W' = V^\perp$, so $\dim W' = \dim M_\mathbb{R} - \dim V = n - \dim V$.

**Step 3: dual side.** Apply the same reasoning to $\sigma^\vee$ in place of $\sigma$: $\dim W = n - \dim(\mathrm{span}\,\sigma^\vee)$. Combining $\dim W' = n - \dim V$ and $\dim W = n - \dim(\mathrm{span}\,\sigma^\vee)$, then using cone duality $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \sigma$ and $V^{\perp\perp} = V$: the lineality space of $\sigma$ is the annihilator in $N_\mathbb{R}$ of $\mathrm{span}\,\sigma^\vee$, so $\dim W = n - \dim(\mathrm{span}\,\sigma^\vee)$.

**Step 4: equivalence.** $\sigma$ is strongly convex iff $W = 0$ iff $\dim W = 0$ iff $\dim(\mathrm{span}\,\sigma^\vee) = n$ iff $\sigma^\vee$ is full-dimensional in $M_\mathbb{R}$ iff $\sigma^\vee$ spans $M_\mathbb{R}$. The chain is complete. The same argument gives the converse: $\sigma^\vee$ is strongly convex iff $\sigma$ spans $N_\mathbb{R}$.

Rubric: full credit for identifying both lineality spaces as annihilators and the dimension count. Partial credit for one direction.

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the **face lattice** of the cone $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_2, e_3) \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ (the positive octant). List all faces, identify their dimensions, and verify that the correspondence $\tau \leftrightarrow \tau^*$ — where $\tau^* := \sigma^\vee \cap \tau^\perp$ — is an inclusion-reversing bijection with the face lattice of $\sigma^\vee$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The positive octant has $2^3 = 8$ faces, one for each subset of the three rays — including the whole cone (corresponding to the empty subset) and the zero face $\{0\}$ (corresponding to all three rays). For each face $\tau$, compute $\tau^\perp = \{m \in M_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, u\rangle = 0 \text{ for all } u \in \tau\}$ and intersect with $\sigma^\vee$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Faces of $\sigma$.** The face lattice has $2^3 = 8$ elements:

- The zero face $\{0\}$ (dimension $0$): corresponds to taking $m \in \mathrm{int}(\sigma^\vee)$.
- Three rays: $\tau_i = \mathrm{Cone}(e_i)$ (dimension $1$, for $i = 1, 2, 3$).
- Three two-dimensional faces (facets): $\tau_{ij} = \mathrm{Cone}(e_i, e_j)$ for $\{i, j\} \subset \{1, 2, 3\}$ (dimension $2$).
- The whole cone $\sigma$ (dimension $3$).

**Dual cone and its faces.** $\sigma^\vee = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1^*, e_2^*, e_3^*) \subset M_\mathbb{R}$, also the positive octant. Same $2^3 = 8$ faces, indexed by subsets of $\{e_1^*, e_2^*, e_3^*\}$.

**Bijection.** Compute $\tau^* = \sigma^\vee \cap \tau^\perp$ for each face $\tau$:

- $\tau = \{0\}$: $\tau^\perp = M_\mathbb{R}$, so $\tau^* = \sigma^\vee$ (whole dual cone, dimension $3$).
- $\tau = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1)$: $\tau^\perp = \{(m_1, m_2, m_3) : m_1 = 0\}$, so $\tau^* = \sigma^\vee \cap \{m_1 = 0\} = \mathrm{Cone}(e_2^*, e_3^*)$ (facet of $\sigma^\vee$, dimension $2$).
- $\tau = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_2)$: $\tau^\perp = \{m_1 = m_2 = 0\}$, so $\tau^* = \sigma^\vee \cap \{m_1 = m_2 = 0\} = \mathrm{Cone}(e_3^*)$ (ray of $\sigma^\vee$, dimension $1$).
- $\tau = \sigma$ (whole cone): $\tau^\perp = \{0\}$ (the only $m$ with $\langle m, u\rangle = 0$ for all $u$ spanning $N_\mathbb{R}$ is $m = 0$), so $\tau^* = \{0\}$ (dimension $0$).

**Verification.** Each dimension count satisfies $\dim \tau + \dim \tau^* = n = 3$, and the bijection $\tau \leftrightarrow \tau^*$ is inclusion-reversing: $\tau \subseteq \tau'$ implies $\tau'^\perp \subseteq \tau^\perp$, hence $\tau'^* \subseteq \tau^*$. The face lattice of $\sigma^\vee$ is the order-dual of the face lattice of $\sigma$, an instance of the **face-correspondence theorem** for rational polyhedral cones.

Rubric: full credit for the face enumeration, the $\tau \leftrightarrow \tau^*$ computation, the dimension identity, and the inclusion-reversing observation.

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove that every face $\tau$ of a rational polyhedral cone $\sigma$ is itself a rational polyhedral cone. Specifically, show that if $\tau = \sigma \cap H_m$ for some $m \in \sigma^\vee$, then $\tau$ is the nonnegative span of a subset of the generators of $\sigma$ (those generators $u_i$ with $\langle m, u_i\rangle = 0$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(u_1, \ldots, u_k)$. Take $u \in \tau = \sigma \cap H_m$, so $u = \sum a_i u_i$ with $a_i \geq 0$ and $\langle m, u\rangle = 0$. Expand $\langle m, u\rangle$ in terms of the $u_i$ and use $\langle m, u_i\rangle \geq 0$ to deduce which $a_i$ can be nonzero.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(u_1, \ldots, u_k)$ with lattice vectors $u_i \in N$. Let $m \in \sigma^\vee$ define the supporting hyperplane $H_m = \{u : \langle m, u\rangle = 0\}$ and the face $\tau = \sigma \cap H_m$.

Partition the generators by $\langle m, u_i\rangle$. Since $m \in \sigma^\vee$, each $\langle m, u_i\rangle \geq 0$. Let $I_0 = \{i : \langle m, u_i\rangle = 0\}$ and $I_+ = \{i : \langle m, u_i\rangle > 0\}$.

**Claim.** $\tau = \mathrm{Cone}(\{u_i : i \in I_0\})$.

**$\supseteq$ direction.** Each $u_i$ with $i \in I_0$ lies in $\sigma$ (as a generator) and in $H_m$ (by definition of $I_0$), hence in $\tau$. Nonnegative combinations of vectors in $\tau$ stay in $\tau$ (since $\tau$ is a face, itself a cone — the intersection of the cone $\sigma$ with the linear subspace $H_m$, both closed under nonnegative scaling).

**$\subseteq$ direction.** Take $u \in \tau$. Write $u = \sum_i a_i u_i$ with $a_i \geq 0$. The condition $u \in H_m$ gives
$$
0 = \langle m, u\rangle = \sum_i a_i \langle m, u_i\rangle = \sum_{i \in I_+} a_i \langle m, u_i\rangle.
$$
Each summand on the right is the product of two nonnegative numbers (the coefficient $a_i \geq 0$ and the value $\langle m, u_i\rangle > 0$ for $i \in I_+$), so the sum equals zero forces each summand to equal zero, hence $a_i = 0$ for every $i \in I_+$. The remaining nonzero $a_i$ lie in $I_0$, so $u = \sum_{i \in I_0} a_i u_i \in \mathrm{Cone}(\{u_i : i \in I_0\})$.

The two directions combined establish the claim. Therefore $\tau$ is the nonnegative span of finitely many lattice vectors $\{u_i : i \in I_0\} \subseteq N$, that is, $\tau$ is itself a rational polyhedral cone in $N_\mathbb{R}$.

**Corollary.** The set of faces of $\sigma$ is in bijection with a subset of the power set of $\{u_1, \ldots, u_k\}$, hence is finite. Combined with the bijection $\tau \leftrightarrow \tau^* = \sigma^\vee \cap \tau^\perp$ between faces of $\sigma$ and faces of $\sigma^\vee$ (Exercise 5), this gives the finite face-correspondence theorem.

Rubric: full credit for the partition argument, the two directions of the set equality, and the corollary on finiteness of faces.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove the **Minkowski-Weyl theorem** for rational polyhedral cones: a subset $\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ is a rational polyhedral cone (the nonnegative span of finitely many lattice vectors) if and only if it is the intersection of finitely many closed rational half-spaces $H_{m_j}^+ = \{u : \langle m_j, u\rangle \geq 0\}$ with $m_j \in M$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

One direction is direct: if $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(u_1, \ldots, u_k)$, then $\sigma = \sigma^{\vee\vee} = \{u : \langle m, u\rangle \geq 0 \text{ for all } m \in \sigma^\vee\}$, and $\sigma^\vee$ is finitely generated (the dual of a finitely generated cone is also finitely generated, by the dual application of the same theorem). The other direction is an induction on the number of half-spaces.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**($V \Rightarrow H$): generator description implies half-space description.** Let $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(u_1, \ldots, u_k)$ with lattice generators $u_i \in N$. By the cone-duality theorem above, $\sigma = \sigma^{\vee\vee}$. Now $\sigma^\vee = \{m \in M_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, u_i\rangle \geq 0 \text{ for } i = 1, \ldots, k\}$ is the intersection of $k$ rational half-spaces in $M_\mathbb{R}$, hence is a rational polyhedral cone in the half-space sense. Apply the same theorem to $\sigma^\vee$ (the rational-half-space cone $\sigma^\vee$ is finitely generated by its extremal rays — to see this, choose a basis of the linear span of $\sigma^\vee$ and decompose: every point of a rational-half-space cone is a nonnegative combination of its extremal rays by induction on the number of defining half-spaces, with the base case being a rational half-space itself, which is the nonnegative span of $\pm$ basis vectors of its boundary hyperplane plus the inward normal). So $\sigma^\vee = \mathrm{Cone}(m_1, \ldots, m_s)$ for lattice vectors $m_j \in M$.

Then $\sigma = \sigma^{\vee\vee} = \{u \in N_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, u\rangle \geq 0 \text{ for all } m \in \sigma^\vee\}$. By linearity in the first argument, this reduces to testing on the generators: $\sigma = \{u : \langle m_j, u\rangle \geq 0 \text{ for } j = 1, \ldots, s\}$, the intersection of $s$ rational half-spaces.

**($H \Rightarrow V$): half-space description implies generator description.** Let $\sigma = \bigcap_{j=1}^s H_{m_j}^+ \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ with $m_j \in M$. Run the previous direction backwards: $\sigma$ is the half-space description, so $\sigma^\vee$ is in the generator description, $\sigma^\vee = \mathrm{Cone}(m_1, \ldots, m_s)$ (each half-space normal becomes a generator of the dual cone). By cone duality, $\sigma = \sigma^{\vee\vee}$. Apply ($V \Rightarrow H$) to $\sigma^\vee$: it has a half-space description with normals $u_1, \ldots, u_k$ in $N$, hence $\sigma = \sigma^{\vee\vee} = \mathrm{Cone}(u_1, \ldots, u_k)$ — the generator description.

**Remark.** The proof is recursive in the dimension: the base case is a single half-space (which is the nonnegative span of basis vectors), and the inductive step intersects with one more half-space, peeling off the appropriate faces. The Minkowski-Weyl theorem is the central duality result of finite-dimensional convex polyhedral geometry, and the cone-version proved here is what specialises to the toric setting through the rational-lattice hypothesis. The polytope version (for bounded polyhedra, generalising Minkowski's foundational *Geometrie der Zahlen*) is the same statement applied to the cone over a polytope.

Rubric: full credit for both directions of the equivalence and the recursive structure.

</details>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

Mathlib has convex-cone infrastructure (`Mathlib.Analysis.Convex.Cone.Basic`, `Mathlib.Analysis.Convex.Cone.Dual`) covering convex cones and their dual cones in a real vector space under a continuous bilinear pairing. What is missing is the lattice-rational refinement that toric geometry requires. The intended formalisation reads schematically:

```lean
import Mathlib.Analysis.Convex.Cone.Basic
import Mathlib.Analysis.Convex.Cone.Dual
import Mathlib.LinearAlgebra.FreeModule.Basic

variable {n : ℕ}

/-- A rational polyhedral cone in N_ℝ generated by finitely many
lattice vectors in N. -/
structure RationalPolyhedralCone (N : Type*) [AddCommGroup N] [Module ℤ N]
    [Module.Free ℤ N] where
  generators : Finset N
  -- the cone itself, as a subset of N ⊗ ℝ
  carrier : ConvexCone ℝ (N ⊗[ℤ] ℝ)
  spec : carrier = ConvexCone.span ℝ
    ((generators : Set N).image (· ⊗ₜ 1))

/-- The dual cone of a rational polyhedral cone. -/
noncomputable def RationalPolyhedralCone.dual
    {N : Type*} [AddCommGroup N] [Module ℤ N] [Module.Free ℤ N]
    (σ : RationalPolyhedralCone N) :
    ConvexCone ℝ (Module.Dual ℤ N ⊗[ℤ] ℝ) := sorry

/-- Cone duality: the double dual recovers the original cone. -/
theorem RationalPolyhedralCone.dual_dual
    {N : Type*} [AddCommGroup N] [Module ℤ N] [Module.Free ℤ N]
    (σ : RationalPolyhedralCone N) :
    σ.dual.dual = σ.carrier := by
```

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (Carathéodory's theorem for cones).** *Let $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(u_1, \ldots, u_k) \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ be a rational polyhedral cone of dimension $d$. Every point $u \in \sigma$ can be written as a nonnegative combination of at most $d$ of the generators $u_i$. In particular, the cone $\sigma$ is the union of the cones spanned by all $d$-subsets of its generator set whose generators span a $d$-dimensional subspace.*

Carathéodory for cones is the finite-dimensional version of the classical Carathéodory theorem for convex hulls; its proof goes by linear-dependence reduction. Given a representation $u = \sum_i a_i u_i$ with $a_i \geq 0$ and more than $d$ nonzero coefficients, the corresponding generators $\{u_i : a_i > 0\}$ are linearly dependent, so a relation $\sum_i c_i u_i = 0$ with $c_i$ not all zero exists. Add a scalar multiple of this relation to the original representation to drive at least one coefficient to zero while keeping all others nonnegative; iterate until at most $d$ coefficients remain nonzero. The lemma is the foundational reason the rational-polyhedral-cone formalism is finite-dimensional in a strong sense: although the generator set can be arbitrarily large, the effective dimension at any point is bounded by the dimension of the cone.

**Theorem (Minkowski-Weyl for cones; the equivalence of $V$- and $H$-representations).** *A subset $\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ is a rational polyhedral cone if and only if it is the intersection of finitely many closed rational half-spaces. The two representations exchange under duality $\sigma \mapsto \sigma^\vee$.*

The Minkowski-Weyl theorem is the central duality result of finite-dimensional convex polyhedral geometry. The cone version proved in Exercise 8 is the lattice-rational specialisation; the polytope version (Minkowski's foundational 1896 result in *Geometrie der Zahlen*) is the cone-version applied to the cone over a polytope. Hermann Weyl established the second direction (half-space implies generator) in his 1935 paper *Elementare Theorie der konvexen Polyeder* (Commentarii Mathematici Helvetici 7), completing Minkowski's program.

**Theorem (face lattice and orbit structure).** *Let $\sigma$ be a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone in $N_\mathbb{R}$. The face lattice $\mathrm{Face}(\sigma)$ — partially ordered by inclusion — is a finite graded lattice with rank function $\dim$. The map $\tau \mapsto \tau^* = \sigma^\vee \cap \tau^\perp$ is an order-reversing isomorphism $\mathrm{Face}(\sigma) \cong \mathrm{Face}(\sigma^\vee)^{\mathrm{op}}$, with $\dim \tau + \dim \tau^* = n$ for every face $\tau$.*

The face-lattice correspondence is the combinatorial backbone of the toric dictionary. On the variety side it becomes the **orbit-cone correspondence** (developed in `04.11.05`): the $T$-orbits in $U_\sigma$ are in inclusion-reversing bijection with the faces of $\sigma$, with the orbit corresponding to a face $\tau$ of dimension $d$ being a torus orbit of dimension $n - d$. The face $\sigma$ itself corresponds to the closed orbit, a fixed point of $T$; the zero face $\{0\}$ corresponds to the open dense $T$-orbit, isomorphic to $T$ itself. The combinatorial $\dim \tau + \dim \tau^* = n$ identity is exactly the orbit-dimension formula.

**Theorem (Hilbert basis theorem for monoids; Hilbert 1890 / Gordan reformulation).** *The semigroup $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ has a **unique minimal generating set**, called the **Hilbert basis** of $\sigma^\vee$. It consists of the lattice points $m \in \sigma^\vee \cap M \setminus \{0\}$ that cannot be written as a sum of two other nonzero lattice points in $\sigma^\vee \cap M$.*

The Hilbert basis of $\sigma^\vee$ is the canonical minimal generating set, and is what algorithmic toric-variety software (Macaulay2, Magma, Polymake, GAP) computes to present the affine toric variety $U_\sigma$. The minimal-generating-set computation reduces to enumerating the indecomposable lattice points in the parallelepiped of Gordan's lemma; the resulting set is finite (as Gordan's lemma proves) and uniquely determined, so the toric variety $U_\sigma$ comes with a canonical embedding in affine space of dimension equal to $|\mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee)|$.

**Theorem (smoothness criterion; Demazure 1970).** *Let $\sigma$ be a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone of dimension $d$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$. The affine toric variety $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ is smooth if and only if $\sigma$ is a **simplicial unimodular cone** — that is, $\sigma$ is generated by exactly $d$ lattice vectors that form part of a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$. In particular, $U_\sigma \cong \mathbb{C}^d \times T^{n-d}$ for some auxiliary torus $T^{n-d}$ when this condition holds.*

The smoothness criterion identifies the smooth affine toric varieties combinatorially. The cone $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_2)$ in $\mathbb{Z}^2$ is simplicial unimodular (the generators $e_1, e_2$ form the standard $\mathbb{Z}$-basis), and $U_\sigma = \mathbb{C}^2$ is smooth. The cone $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_1 + 2 e_2)$ from Exercise 3 has generators not extending to a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis (the determinant of the matrix with columns $e_1$ and $e_1 + 2 e_2$ is $2 \neq \pm 1$), so $U_\sigma$ is singular — specifically, the $A_1$ Du Val singularity, as computed there. The smoothness criterion is the foundation of toric resolution of singularities: every singular toric variety can be resolved by subdividing its fan into simplicial unimodular subcones (a process called **stellar subdivision** or **fan refinement**, the toric implementation of Hironaka's theorem).

**Theorem (compatibility with the algebraic torus).** *Let $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n$ with cocharacter lattice $N$ and character lattice $M$. The zero cone $\sigma = \{0\} \subset N_\mathbb{R}$ has dual cone $\sigma^\vee = M_\mathbb{R}$, semigroup $\sigma^\vee \cap M = M = \mathbb{Z}^n$, and monoid algebra $\mathbb{C}[M] = \mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$, recovering the coordinate ring of $T$ itself. The torus $T$ is the affine toric variety of the zero cone.*

This is the simplest case of the toric dictionary, and the foundational reason every toric variety contains $T$ as an open dense subset: the zero cone is a face of every other cone, so its affine toric variety $U_{\{0\}} = T$ embeds as an open subset $T \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$ for every $\sigma$. The Hopf-algebra structure on $\mathbb{C}[M]$ from `04.11.01` extends to a $T$-action on $U_\sigma$ via the comorphism, and the orbit structure (via the face-lattice correspondence above) records exactly how the cone $\sigma$ decomposes the variety $U_\sigma$ into $T$-orbits.

**Synthesis.** The rational polyhedral cone formalism is the combinatorial bridge between the lattice $(M, N)$ of `04.11.01` and the algebraic-geometric construction of affine toric varieties in `04.11.03`, and the central insight is that the two descriptions of a cone — by generators on the $N$-side and by defining inequalities on the $M$-side — are interchangeable via duality, $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \sigma$. Three structural results — Minkowski-Weyl (the equivalence of $V$- and $H$-representations), Gordan's lemma (finite generation of $\sigma^\vee \cap M$), and the face lattice correspondence (inclusion-reversing bijection $\sigma \leftrightarrow \sigma^\vee$) — combine into one identification: a rational polyhedral cone is a single combinatorial object with two equivalent presentations, and the affine toric variety it produces inherits this duality at the level of $T$-equivariant geometry.

Putting these together, the rational-polyhedral-cone language organises the entire toric chapter, and the foundational reason is that every downstream construction (the affine toric variety $U_\sigma$ in `04.11.03`, the fan $\Sigma$ and the global $X_\Sigma$ in `04.11.04`, the orbit-cone correspondence in `04.11.05`, $T$-invariant divisors and the Picard group via support functions in `04.11.08`, the polytope-toric correspondence in `04.11.11`) reads one piece of combinatorics from the lattice side of the dictionary developed in this unit. The bridge is dual to the duality of $M$ and $N$, and this pattern appears again every time a $T$-equivariant geometric notion on a toric variety is computed combinatorially: it has a description in terms of the cone $\sigma$, a description in terms of the dual cone $\sigma^\vee$, and a duality identifying the two. The smoothness criterion of Demazure 1970 is the foundational reason that resolution of singularities for toric varieties is purely combinatorial — the simplicial-unimodular condition is a $\mathbb{Z}$-arithmetic condition on the generators of $\sigma$, and refining $\sigma$ into simplicial unimodular subcones produces a toric resolution of $U_\sigma$. This identifies toric resolution with the combinatorial process of fan refinement, generalising classical Hilbert-basis-style computations to the full machinery of toric birational geometry.

The cone formalism also generalises in two directions. To non-rational polyhedral cones (where generators may be irrational), most of the geometry persists — Minkowski-Weyl, Carathéodory, and the face lattice are properties of finite-dimensional convex polyhedral geometry independent of any lattice — but Gordan's lemma and the affine-toric-variety construction require the rationality assumption: without lattice generators, $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ might collapse to the identity or fail to be finitely generated. To infinite-dimensional cones (in the analysis-flavoured generality of `Mathlib.Analysis.Convex.Cone`), the duality $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \sigma$ requires either compactness or a weak-closed-cone hypothesis (Hahn-Banach in infinite dimensions involves topology); the finite-dimensional rational case used here is computationally tractable and is the version that drives toric geometry. The lattice formalism developed here is therefore the first nontrivial example of polyhedral geometry where the combinatorics, the analysis (separating-hyperplane theorem), and the algebraic geometry (affine toric varieties) all converge on a single object — the rational polyhedral cone $\sigma$ together with its dual $\sigma^\vee$.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Theorem (cone duality), proof.** Given in the Intermediate-tier section: direction $\sigma \subseteq \sigma^{\vee\vee}$ is direct from the definition; direction $\sigma^{\vee\vee} \subseteq \sigma$ uses the finite-dimensional separating-hyperplane theorem to find, for each $u_0 \notin \sigma$, a functional $m_0 \in \sigma^\vee$ with $\langle m_0, u_0\rangle < 0$, exhibiting $u_0 \notin \sigma^{\vee\vee}$. $\square$

**Theorem (Gordan's lemma), proof.** Given in the Intermediate-tier section: choose lattice ray generators $m'_1, \ldots, m'_s$ of $\sigma^\vee$ (existence via Minkowski-Weyl); form the parallelepiped $P = \{\sum a_j m'_j : a_j \in [0, 1]\}$; observe $P \cap M$ is finite; show every $m \in \sigma^\vee \cap M$ decomposes as $m = m' + m''$ with $m' \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}\langle m'_1, \ldots, m'_s\rangle$ and $m'' \in P \cap M$; combine the two finite sets to get a finite generating set for the semigroup. $\square$

**Proposition (strong convexity equivalence), proof.** Given in Exercise 4: identify the lineality space $W = \sigma \cap (-\sigma)$ of $\sigma$ as the annihilator of $\mathrm{span}\,\sigma^\vee$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$, equivalently $\dim W = n - \dim(\mathrm{span}\,\sigma^\vee)$; identify the lineality space $W' = \sigma^\vee \cap (-\sigma^\vee)$ of $\sigma^\vee$ as the annihilator of $\mathrm{span}\,\sigma$ in $M_\mathbb{R}$, giving $\dim W' = n - \dim(\mathrm{span}\,\sigma)$. Then $\sigma$ strongly convex iff $W = 0$ iff $\dim W = 0$ iff $\dim(\mathrm{span}\,\sigma^\vee) = n$ iff $\sigma^\vee$ is full-dimensional in $M_\mathbb{R}$ iff $\sigma^\vee$ spans $M_\mathbb{R}$. The chain of equivalences is complete. $\square$

**Proposition (faces are nonnegative spans of generator subsets), proof.** Given in Exercise 7: partition the generators of $\sigma$ by $\langle m, u_i\rangle$ for the supporting hyperplane normal $m$; show that any $u \in \tau = \sigma \cap H_m$ written as $\sum a_i u_i$ has $a_i = 0$ for $i$ with $\langle m, u_i\rangle > 0$ (by the nonnegativity of $\langle m, u\rangle = \sum_i a_i \langle m, u_i\rangle$, forcing all positive summands to vanish since the sum is zero); conclude $\tau = \mathrm{Cone}(\{u_i : \langle m, u_i\rangle = 0\})$, a finitely generated rational polyhedral cone. $\square$

**Proposition (Minkowski-Weyl for cones), proof.** Given in Exercise 8: both directions follow from the duality theorem $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \sigma$. The generator description of $\sigma$ implies $\sigma^\vee$ has a half-space description with normals the original generators, and $\sigma = \sigma^{\vee\vee}$ then has a generator description with the dual generators of $\sigma^\vee$. The half-space description direction is symmetric. The recursive structure makes the dimension count proceed by induction. $\square$

**Proposition (face correspondence $\tau \leftrightarrow \tau^*$), proof.** *Let $\sigma$ be a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone in $N_\mathbb{R}$. The map $\Phi : \mathrm{Face}(\sigma) \to \mathrm{Face}(\sigma^\vee)$ defined by $\Phi(\tau) = \tau^* := \sigma^\vee \cap \tau^\perp$ is an inclusion-reversing bijection, with inverse $\Phi^{-1}(\tau') = (\tau')^* := \sigma \cap (\tau')^\perp$, and $\dim \tau + \dim \tau^* = n$ for every face $\tau$.*

*Proof.* Well-definedness: for $\tau \in \mathrm{Face}(\sigma)$, the set $\tau^\perp = \{m \in M_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, u\rangle = 0 \text{ for all } u \in \tau\}$ is a linear subspace of $M_\mathbb{R}$; the intersection $\sigma^\vee \cap \tau^\perp$ is the face of $\sigma^\vee$ supported by the hyperplane $\tau^\perp$ (every $m \in \sigma^\vee \cap \tau^\perp$ has $\langle m, u\rangle \geq 0$ for $u \in \sigma$ and $\langle m, u\rangle = 0$ for $u \in \tau$, identifying $\tau^*$ as the face of $\sigma^\vee$ at the supporting functional $u_\tau \in \mathrm{relint}(\tau) \subset \sigma$).

Inclusion-reversing: $\tau \subseteq \tau'$ implies $\tau'^\perp \subseteq \tau^\perp$, hence $\sigma^\vee \cap \tau'^\perp \subseteq \sigma^\vee \cap \tau^\perp$, i.e., $(\tau')^* \subseteq \tau^*$.

Bijection: the inverse map $\Psi : \mathrm{Face}(\sigma^\vee) \to \mathrm{Face}(\sigma)$ defined by $\Psi(\tau') = (\tau')^* := \sigma \cap (\tau')^\perp$ is the same construction applied to $\sigma^\vee$, and the involutive identity $\Psi \Phi = \mathrm{id}_{\mathrm{Face}(\sigma)}$ follows from cone duality $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \sigma$ and the double-annihilator identity $V^{\perp\perp} = V$ for any linear subspace $V$: $\Psi(\Phi(\tau)) = \sigma \cap (\sigma^\vee \cap \tau^\perp)^\perp$, and unpacking the annihilator gives $(\sigma^\vee \cap \tau^\perp)^\perp = \sigma^{\vee\perp} + \tau^{\perp\perp} = \mathrm{span}\,\sigma^{\vee\perp} + \mathrm{span}\,\tau = \tau$ (using that the linear span of $\sigma$ contains $\tau$ and intersecting with $\sigma$ recovers $\tau$ as the face of $\sigma$ along this subspace).

Dimension identity: $\dim \tau^* = \dim(\sigma^\vee \cap \tau^\perp)$. The face $\tau^*$ of $\sigma^\vee$ lies in $\tau^\perp$, a linear subspace of $M_\mathbb{R}$ of dimension $n - \dim \tau$. Since $\sigma^\vee$ has full dimension $n$ in $M_\mathbb{R}$ (by strong convexity of $\sigma$, applied to the dual), the face $\tau^*$ has dimension equal to the codimension of $\mathrm{span}\,\tau$ in $\mathrm{span}\,\sigma^\vee = M_\mathbb{R}$, namely $\dim \tau^* = n - \dim \tau$. So $\dim \tau + \dim \tau^* = n$. $\square$

**Proposition (Hilbert basis of $\sigma^\vee \cap M$), proof.** *Let $\sigma$ be a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone in $N_\mathbb{R}$ and let $S = \sigma^\vee \cap M$. The set $\mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee) := \{m \in S \setminus \{0\} : m \text{ is not a sum of two nonzero elements of } S\}$ is the unique minimal generating set of $S$, and is finite.*

*Proof.* Finiteness: $\mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee)$ is contained in the bounded set $P \cap M$ of Gordan's lemma (every Hilbert-basis element fits inside the fundamental parallelepiped, since if not, write it as a sum of a lattice ray generator plus a residue and reduce). Hence $\mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee)$ is finite.

Generating: Take $m \in S$. Either $m = 0$ (which is the empty combination of Hilbert-basis elements) or $m$ is a sum of two nonzero elements of $S$, say $m = m' + m''$. Recursively decompose each $m'$ and $m''$ that is not Hilbert-basis. The recursion terminates because each step strictly decreases a positive-integer measure (for example, the height $\langle m, u_\sigma\rangle$ where $u_\sigma$ is a positive functional in $\mathrm{relint}(\sigma)$). The terminating elements are Hilbert-basis. So every $m \in S$ is a nonnegative integer combination of Hilbert-basis elements.

Minimality: a Hilbert-basis element $h$ cannot be omitted, because by definition it is not a sum of other nonzero elements of $S$, hence cannot be written as a nonnegative integer combination of $S \setminus \{h\}$ without contradicting its indecomposability. The minimal generating set is therefore exactly the Hilbert basis. $\square$

**Proposition (smoothness of $U_\sigma$ via simplicial unimodular cones), proof.** *Let $\sigma$ be a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone in $N_\mathbb{R}$ of dimension $d$. Then $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ is a smooth complex algebraic variety if and only if $\sigma$ is simplicial unimodular: that is, $\sigma$ is generated by exactly $d$ lattice vectors $u_1, \ldots, u_d$ that form part of a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$.*

*Proof sketch.* ($\Leftarrow$) If $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(u_1, \ldots, u_d)$ with $u_i$ extending to a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis $u_1, \ldots, u_n$ of $N$, change basis so that $u_i = e_i$ for $i = 1, \ldots, d$ (the standard basis vectors). Then $\sigma^\vee = \{m \in M_\mathbb{R} : m_i \geq 0 \text{ for } i \leq d\}$, the closed half-space $\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^d \times \mathbb{R}^{n-d}$. The semigroup $\sigma^\vee \cap M = \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}^d \times \mathbb{Z}^{n-d}$ is the free monoid generated by $e_1^*, \ldots, e_d^*$ together with $\pm e_{d+1}^*, \ldots, \pm e_n^*$. The monoid algebra is $\mathbb{C}[x_1, \ldots, x_d, x_{d+1}^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$, the coordinate ring of $\mathbb{C}^d \times (\mathbb{C}^*)^{n-d}$, a smooth variety.

($\Rightarrow$) If $U_\sigma$ is smooth at the closed orbit (the $T$-fixed point given by the maximal ideal $\langle \chi^m : m \in \sigma^\vee \cap M \setminus \mathrm{lineality}\rangle$), then the cotangent space at this fixed point has dimension equal to the dimension of $U_\sigma$, namely $n$. A direct computation identifies the cotangent space with $\mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee) \otimes \mathbb{C}$ modulo the relations from the semigroup; smoothness forces $|\mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee)| = n$ and the Hilbert basis to be a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $M$. Dualising, the generators of $\sigma$ form part of a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$, the simplicial-unimodular condition. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Algebraic torus [04.11.01].** The lattice $N$ and its dual $M$ in which rational polyhedral cones live are exactly the cocharacter and character lattices of the algebraic torus $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n$. The zero cone $\sigma = \{0\}$ has dual cone $\sigma^\vee = M_\mathbb{R}$ and semigroup $\sigma^\vee \cap M = M$ itself, with monoid algebra $\mathbb{C}[M] = \mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$ — the coordinate ring of $T$. The torus is therefore the affine toric variety of the zero cone, and every other cone $\sigma$ produces an affine variety $U_\sigma$ containing $T$ as an open dense subset via the face inclusion $\{0\} \hookrightarrow \sigma$. The lattice formalism of the prerequisite unit supplies the dictionary $(M, N, \langle -, -\rangle)$ in which the cones of this unit live, and the cone formalism here builds the first combinatorial datum from which toric varieties are constructed.

- **Linear transformation / rank-nullity [01.01.05].** The cone-duality theorem $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \sigma$ is, at heart, a statement about the linear functional pairing $\langle -, -\rangle : M_\mathbb{R} \times N_\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ — every $m \in M_\mathbb{R}$ acts as a linear functional $N_\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$, and the cone $\sigma^\vee$ is the set of functionals nonnegative on $\sigma$. The dimension identities for lineality spaces and faces ($\dim W + \dim(\mathrm{span}\,\sigma^\vee) = n$, $\dim \tau + \dim \tau^* = n$) are instances of the rank-nullity theorem applied to the pairing viewed as a linear map. The prerequisite unit on linear transformations supplies the kernel-image-rank machinery that underwrites the convex-geometric computations in this unit.

- **Group [01.02.01].** The lattice points $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ form a commutative monoid under addition (a semigroup containing $0$), and the cancellative-monoid structure on this semigroup is what makes the monoid algebra $\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ a commutative ring. Gordan's lemma is the statement that this monoid is finitely generated, and the Hilbert-basis theorem identifies a unique minimal generating set. The prerequisite unit on groups supplies the abelian-group structure on $M$ that the additive semigroup $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ inherits.

- **Affine toric variety [04.11.03].** The next unit in the toric chapter constructs the affine algebraic variety $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ from the cone $\sigma$. Gordan's lemma is the foundational reason this $\mathrm{Spec}$ is a Noetherian / finite-type object, and the cone-duality theorem makes the construction independent of the choice of generators of $\sigma$. The face-correspondence theorem developed here becomes the **orbit-cone correspondence** of `04.11.06`: the $T$-orbits in $U_\sigma$ are in inclusion-reversing bijection with the faces of $\sigma$, with the dimension formula $\dim \tau + \dim \mathrm{orbit}(\tau) = n$ matching the face-lattice dimension identity proved here.

- **Fan and toric variety $X_\Sigma$ [04.11.04].** A **fan** in $N_\mathbb{R}$ is a finite collection of rational polyhedral cones closed under taking faces and intersections; the global toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is constructed by gluing the affine toric varieties $U_\sigma$ for $\sigma \in \Sigma$ along the affine pieces corresponding to face intersections. The cone formalism of this unit is the combinatorial atom from which fans are assembled, and the face-correspondence theorem ensures the gluing data (face inclusions) is functorial.

- **Smoothness and completeness via fans [04.11.05].** The downstream unit promotes the unimodular-basis condition on a single cone — $\sigma$ is generated by part of a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$ — into the global smoothness criterion for the toric variety $X_\Sigma$: smooth iff every cone of $\Sigma$ is simplicial unimodular. The lineality, face, and dimension calculus developed in this unit supplies the cone-level toolkit for the smoothness test; the completeness criterion $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$ uses cone duality $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \sigma$ to identify valuative-criterion extension with cone-membership, exactly as proved here. The cone-and-dual-cone formalism is the combinatorial input on which both criteria run.

- **Toric resolution of singularities [04.11.07].** The downstream resolution unit operates entirely on the cone formalism developed here: star subdivision of a non-unimodular cone $\sigma$ inserts a new ray $v$ in the relative interior of $\sigma$ and replaces $\sigma$ by the cones generated by $v$ together with each maximal proper face of $\sigma$. The face-correspondence theorem $(\sigma \cap \tau)^\vee = \sigma^\vee + \tau^\vee$ ensures the refined collection still satisfies the fan axioms, and Gordan's lemma guarantees each new cone produces an affine variety of finite type. The toric resolution algorithm is, in this sense, a finite procedure on the cone primitives of this unit.

- **Resolution of singularities [04.06.02].** Demazure's smoothness criterion (simplicial unimodular cones produce smooth $U_\sigma$) is the toric input to **resolution of singularities** for toric varieties: every singular toric variety can be resolved by refining its fan into simplicial unimodular subcones, a process called **stellar subdivision** or **fan refinement**. This is the toric analogue of Hironaka's resolution theorem, and it is combinatorially explicit — entirely controlled by the lattice and cone structure of this unit. The prerequisite unit on resolution supplies the geometric framework into which the toric resolution slots.

- **Hilbert basis theorem [02.04.01].** The Hilbert basis theorem in commutative algebra (every ideal in a Noetherian ring is finitely generated) is the algebraic backbone of finite-type schemes; Gordan's lemma is its monoid-flavoured shadow, restricted to the additive semigroup $\sigma^\vee \cap M$. The two together — Gordan's lemma for the monoid, Hilbert's basis theorem for the ring — guarantee that $\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ is a Noetherian commutative $\mathbb{C}$-algebra of finite type, hence the coordinate ring of an affine variety.

- **Convex polytope [02.05.04].** A **convex polytope** is the bounded analogue of a polyhedral cone — the convex hull of finitely many points, equivalently the intersection of finitely many half-spaces of bounded extent. Every polytope $P$ has an associated cone $C(P) = \mathrm{Cone}(P \times \{1\}) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^{n+1}$ (the **homogenisation** of $P$), and the cone formalism of this unit specialises to polytope formalism via this construction. Polytope geometry (Minkowski's *Geometrie der Zahlen*, 1896) is the historical and conceptual root of the rational-cone formalism developed here, and the polytope-toric-correspondence of `04.11.11` is the bounded version of the cone-toric correspondence developed here and in the next units.

- **Newton polytope and non-archimedean amoeba [04.12.04].** The downstream tropical-geometry unit takes the Newton polytope $\mathrm{Newt}(f) = \mathrm{Conv}(\mathrm{supp}(f)) \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ of a Laurent polynomial $f$ as a **bounded** rational polyhedron in $M_\mathbb{R}$ — exactly the convex-polytope specialisation of the rational-cone formalism of this unit. The **normal fan** $\Sigma(\mathrm{Newt}(f)) \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ assembled from inner-normal cones of faces of the Newton polytope is the rational-polyhedral-fan instance of the cone-and-fan formalism, and the tropical hypersurface $\mathrm{trop}(f) \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ is a polyhedral subcomplex of the support of this normal fan. The cone-duality theorem $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \sigma$ of the present unit is the foundational reason that the Newton-polytope-to-tropical-hypersurface duality is exact: cells of one side correspond to faces of the other under the natural pairing.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The rational polyhedral cone formalism rests on three nineteenth- and early-twentieth-century results from the analytic theory of linear inequalities. Hermann Minkowski's *Geometrie der Zahlen* (Leipzig: Teubner, 1896) [ref: TODO_REF Minkowski 1896] introduced the systematic theory of lattices in $\mathbb{R}^n$ and convex bodies, proving in particular the linear-forms theorem (Ch. II §17) that anchors all subsequent rational-polyhedral-cone arguments — the existence of nonzero lattice points in symmetric convex bodies of sufficient volume. Minkowski's program was completed for unbounded polyhedra (polyhedral cones) by Paul Gordan's *Ueber die Auflösung linearer Gleichungen mit reell ganzzahligen Coefficienten* (*Mathematische Annalen* 6, 1873, pp. 23-28) [ref: TODO_REF Gordan 1873] — the originator paper for the finite-generation theorem on solutions of linear inequalities in nonnegative integers, which Hilbert later reformulated as the existence of a Hilbert basis for the integer points in $\sigma^\vee$. Julius Farkas's *Theorie der einfachen Ungleichungen* (*Journal für die reine und angewandte Mathematik* 124, 1902, pp. 1-27) [ref: TODO_REF Farkas 1902] established the linear-inequalities duality theorem now bearing his name: a vector $b$ is a nonnegative combination of vectors $a_1, \ldots, a_k$ if and only if every linear functional nonnegative on the $a_i$ is nonnegative on $b$. Farkas's lemma is the analytic substrate of cone duality $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \sigma$, and is essentially equivalent to the finite-dimensional separating-hyperplane theorem used in the proof above.

Hermann Weyl's 1935 paper *Elementare Theorie der konvexen Polyeder* (*Commentarii Mathematici Helvetici* 7, pp. 290-306) [ref: TODO_REF] completed the Minkowski program by proving the converse direction: every polyhedron defined by finitely many linear inequalities is the convex hull of a finite set of points plus a finitely generated cone. The combined Minkowski-Weyl theorem (1896-1935) is the central duality result of finite-dimensional convex polyhedral geometry. In the 1950s, the development of linear programming by George Dantzig and the theory of convex polytopes by Branko Grünbaum (*Convex Polytopes*, 1967) [ref: TODO_REF] put the polyhedral-cone formalism on a modern footing, and the algorithmic computation of Hilbert bases (the Pottier algorithm, the Bachem-Schrijver algorithm, the Sturmfels-Maclagan algorithm) became practical for the toric-geometry community.

The toric-geometry application — using rational polyhedral cones in $N_\mathbb{R}$ to encode equivariant affine compactifications of the algebraic torus $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n$ — was opened by Michel Demazure in *Sous-groupes algébriques de rang maximum du groupe de Cremona* (Annales scientifiques de l'École normale supérieure, 4e série, tome 3, 1970, pp. 507-588) [ref: TODO_REF Demazure 1970]. Demazure's smoothness criterion (the simplicial-unimodular condition) and his use of cones and fans to classify rank-$n$ algebraic subgroups of the Cremona group are the foundational papers of toric geometry. The combinatorial nomenclature "toric variety" became standard after the Mumford-school treatment in *Toroidal Embeddings I* (Kempf, Knudsen, Mumford, Saint-Donat, Lecture Notes in Mathematics 339, Springer 1973) [ref: TODO_REF], where the cone-and-fan formalism is recast as a tool for resolving singularities and compactifying equivariant varieties. Tadao Oda's *Convex Bodies and Algebraic Geometry* (Springer 1988) [ref: TODO_REF] consolidated the Japanese-school perspective on rational polyhedral cones, with emphasis on the dual-cone formalism and Gordan's lemma as the foundation of the affine-toric-variety construction; William Fulton's *Introduction to Toric Varieties* (Princeton 1993) [ref: TODO_REF] is the canonical short textbook, and David Cox, John Little, and Henry Schenck's *Toric Varieties* (AMS 2011) [ref: TODO_REF] is the modern thousand-page treatment with full treatment of the lattice-cone-fan-variety dictionary.
