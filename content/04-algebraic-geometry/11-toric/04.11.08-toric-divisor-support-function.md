## Intuition [Beginner]

A **toric divisor** is the combinatorial atom of divisor theory on a toric variety. Every ray $\rho$ of the fan $\Sigma$ produces one such atom: the closure of the codimension-one torus orbit attached to $\rho$ by the orbit-cone correspondence of the previous unit. Write this closure as $D_\rho$. Every torus-invariant Weil divisor on $X_\Sigma$ is a finite integer combination of these atoms. So the abstract divisor theory of the variety collapses, on the torus-invariant slice, to a finitely-generated lattice indexed by rays.

The companion combinatorial object is the **support function**. To each torus-invariant Cartier divisor $D$ — written as a formal integer combination of the ray-indexed divisors with coefficient $a_\rho$ at the ray $\rho$ — one assigns a continuous function $\psi_D$ defined on the entire fan support $|\Sigma|$ that is linear on each maximal cone and integer-valued on the lattice. The recipe is concrete: $\psi_D$ takes the value $-a_\rho$ on the primitive generator of the ray $\rho$, and the values on every other lattice point are pinned by linearity across each maximal cone. The Cartier condition translates into a gluing rule for these linear pieces across the walls between adjacent cones.

The third ingredient is the **divisor polytope** $P_D$ in the character lattice. It is the set of characters $m \in M_\mathbb{R}$ that pair against every primitive ray generator at least as positively as the divisor demands. Lattice points of $P_D$ count global sections of the line bundle $\mathcal{O}(D)$ — one character $\chi^m$ for each lattice point, with no further redundancy. So sheaf cohomology on the toric variety reduces, at degree zero, to a lattice-point count.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute the anticanonical divisor on $\mathbb{P}^2$ and the polytope counting its global sections. The fan of $\mathbb{P}^2$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ has three rays with primitive generators $v_0 = -e_1 - e_2$, $v_1 = e_1$, $v_2 = e_2$, and three maximal cones $\sigma_{12} = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_2)$, $\sigma_{02} = \mathrm{Cone}(-e_1 - e_2, e_2)$, $\sigma_{01} = \mathrm{Cone}(-e_1 - e_2, e_1)$.

Step 1. Identify the atoms. Each ray $\rho_i$ produces a toric divisor $D_i$ — the closure of the codimension-one orbit, which is one of the three coordinate lines $\{x_i = 0\} \subseteq \mathbb{P}^2$. The anticanonical divisor is $-K = D_0 + D_1 + D_2$, the sum of the three coordinate lines.

Step 2. Identify the support function. The coefficients of $-K$ are $a_0 = a_1 = a_2 = 1$, so the support function takes the value $-1$ on each primitive ray generator: $\psi_{-K}(v_0) = \psi_{-K}(v_1) = \psi_{-K}(v_2) = -1$. Extend linearly on each maximal cone. On $\sigma_{12} = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_2)$, linear interpolation gives $\psi_{-K}(s e_1 + t e_2) = -(s + t)$, attained at $e_1$ as $-1$ and at $e_2$ as $-1$. Similarly on the other two cones.

Step 3. Identify the divisor polytope. The polytope $P_{-K} \subseteq M_\mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}^2$ is the set of $m = (s, t)$ with $\langle m, v_i\rangle \geq -1$ for $i \in \{0, 1, 2\}$, i.e., $-s - t \geq -1$, $s \geq -1$, $t \geq -1$. So $P_{-K}$ is the triangle with vertices $(-1, -1)$, $(2, -1)$, $(-1, 2)$, a size-three lattice simplex.

What this tells us. The lattice points of $P_{-K}$ are exactly the $10$ pairs $(s, t)$ with $s, t \geq -1$ and $s + t \leq 1$; the count is the number of homogeneous cubic monomials in three variables, $\binom{3 + 2}{2} = 10$. So the space of global sections is $H^0(\mathbb{P}^2, \mathcal{O}(-K)) = H^0(\mathbb{P}^2, \mathcal{O}(3))$, the $10$-dimensional space of plane cubics. The whole picture — divisor as orbit closure, support function as piecewise-linear ramp, polytope as lattice-point gadget — emerges from three rays in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise (easy, true-false).**

True or false: on every smooth complete toric variety, every torus-invariant Weil divisor is Cartier.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Smoothness translates into a unimodular-basis condition on each cone of the fan, which forces every Weil divisor to admit a piecewise-linear support function — the Cartier datum.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.** On a smooth toric variety, every cone $\sigma$ of the fan is generated by part of a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of the lattice $N$. Given any coefficient assignment $\{a_\rho\}_{\rho \in \Sigma(1)}$, on each maximal cone $\sigma$ one can solve for the unique character $m_\sigma \in M$ satisfying $\langle m_\sigma, v_\rho\rangle = -a_\rho$ for every ray $\rho \subseteq \sigma$, because the system of equations has a unique integer solution (the rays of $\sigma$ form a basis of $N$). The collection $\{m_\sigma\}$ glues into a piecewise-linear support function and supplies the Cartier datum.

On singular toric varieties this fails: at a non-smooth cone, the rays do not form a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$, the system of equations has no integer solution for generic coefficients, and the corresponding Weil divisor is only $\mathbb{Q}$-Cartier — some integer multiple is Cartier, namely the multiplicity of the cone.

</details>

</aside>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\Sigma$ be a fan in $N_\mathbb{R}$ with $N$ a free abelian group of rank $n$ and dual $M = \mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(N, \mathbb{Z})$. Let $X_\Sigma$ denote the associated toric variety from `[04.11.04]` and $T = \mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(M, \mathbb{C}^*)$ the dense torus. Write $\Sigma(1)$ for the set of rays, $\Sigma_{\max}$ for the set of maximal cones, and $|\Sigma| \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ for the support of the fan.

**Definition (toric divisor).** For each ray $\rho \in \Sigma(1)$ with primitive generator $v_\rho \in N$, the **toric divisor** $D_\rho \subseteq X_\Sigma$ is the closure of the codimension-one torus orbit $O(\rho)$ associated to $\rho$ by the orbit-cone correspondence of `[04.11.06]`. By the dimension formula $\dim O(\rho) + \dim \rho = n$, the orbit $O(\rho)$ has dimension $n - 1$, and its closure $D_\rho$ is a $T$-invariant prime Weil divisor on $X_\Sigma$. The **free abelian group of $T$-invariant Weil divisors** is
$$
\mathrm{Div}_T(X_\Sigma) := \bigoplus_{\rho \in \Sigma(1)} \mathbb{Z} \cdot D_\rho.
$$
An element $D \in \mathrm{Div}_T(X_\Sigma)$ is written $D = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$ with $a_\rho \in \mathbb{Z}$ the coefficient at $\rho$.

**Definition (support function).** Let $D = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$ be a $T$-invariant Cartier divisor. The **support function** $\psi_D : |\Sigma| \to \mathbb{R}$ is the unique continuous function such that
1. for each maximal cone $\sigma \in \Sigma_{\max}$, the restriction $\psi_D|_\sigma$ is the linear function $u \mapsto \langle m_\sigma, u\rangle$ for some character $m_\sigma \in M$;
2. for each ray $\rho \in \Sigma(1)$, $\psi_D(v_\rho) = -a_\rho$;
3. the linear pieces $\{m_\sigma\}$ glue continuously, meaning that on each pairwise intersection $\sigma \cap \tau$ (with $\sigma, \tau \in \Sigma_{\max}$), $m_\sigma - m_\tau \in (\sigma \cap \tau)^\perp$ — equivalently, the two linear pieces agree on $\sigma \cap \tau$.

The data $\{m_\sigma\}_{\sigma \in \Sigma_{\max}}$ is the **local Cartier data** of $D$. The function $\psi_D$ is also called the **piecewise-linear function** attached to $D$.

**Definition (Cartier condition).** A $T$-invariant Weil divisor $D = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$ is **Cartier** iff for every maximal cone $\sigma$ there exists a character $m_\sigma \in M$ with $\langle m_\sigma, v_\rho\rangle = -a_\rho$ for every ray $\rho \subseteq \sigma$, and the cocycle compatibility $m_\sigma - m_\tau \in (\sigma \cap \tau)^\perp$ holds for every pair $\sigma, \tau \in \Sigma_{\max}$. Equivalently, $D$ is Cartier iff its associated support function $\psi_D$ exists with integer-valued linear pieces.

**Definition (divisor polytope).** Let $D = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$ be a $T$-invariant Cartier divisor. The **divisor polytope** $P_D \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ is
$$
P_D := \{m \in M_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, v_\rho\rangle \geq -a_\rho \text{ for every } \rho \in \Sigma(1)\}.
$$
$P_D$ is a (possibly unbounded) rational polyhedron in $M_\mathbb{R}$. When $X_\Sigma$ is complete and $D$ is nef, $P_D$ is bounded — a lattice polytope.

**Convention.** The minus signs in $\psi_D(v_\rho) = -a_\rho$ and the inequality $\langle m, v_\rho\rangle \geq -a_\rho$ are the Fulton convention (Fulton 1993 §3.3). Cox-Little-Schenck §4.3 uses the same convention. Some sources (Oda 1988) use the opposite sign convention, swapping $a_\rho \leftrightarrow -a_\rho$ throughout. The choice does not affect the mathematics; we adhere to Fulton.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"Every torus-invariant Weil divisor is Cartier."* False on singular toric. Example: on the affine quadric cone $X_\sigma$ for $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_1 + 2 e_2)$ (the $A_1$-singularity), the divisor $D_{\rho_1}$ associated to the first ray is Weil but not Cartier; twice $D_{\rho_1}$ is Cartier. The Cartier datum at the non-smooth cone $\sigma$ would need to satisfy $\langle m_\sigma, e_1\rangle = -1$ and $\langle m_\sigma, e_1 + 2 e_2\rangle = 0$, forcing $\langle m_\sigma, e_2\rangle = 1/2$, no integer solution.

- *"The support function is unique."* It is unique up to translation by characters when $X_\Sigma$ has at least one maximal cone of dimension $n$: the value $\psi_D(v_\rho) = -a_\rho$ on rays pins the function up to a global character $m \in M$ acting by $\psi_D \to \psi_D + m$ (which shifts the divisor by the principal $\mathrm{div}(\chi^m)$). On non-complete fans where the rays do not span $N_\mathbb{R}$, the support function is determined only modulo the orthogonal complement of the ray-span.

- *"The divisor polytope is always bounded."* $P_D$ is bounded iff $D$ is nef and $X_\Sigma$ is complete. For non-nef $D$ on a complete toric variety, $P_D$ can be empty. For non-complete $X_\Sigma$ (e.g., $X_\Sigma = \mathbb{C}^n$), $P_D$ is typically a polyhedral cone or wedge — unbounded but well-defined.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem of this unit is the **support-function correspondence** of Fulton §3.4 Proposition 1, lifting the Cartier-vs-Weil distinction to a combinatorial bijection.

**Theorem (support-function correspondence; Demazure 1970, Fulton §3.4).** *Let $\Sigma$ be a fan in $N_\mathbb{R}$. The assignment $D \mapsto \psi_D$ is a bijection between the group of $T$-invariant Cartier divisors on $X_\Sigma$ and the group of integer-valued piecewise-linear functions on $|\Sigma|$ (continuous functions $\psi : |\Sigma| \to \mathbb{R}$ that are linear with integer linear pieces on each cone of $\Sigma$). The inverse sends $\psi$ to the Weil divisor $\sum_\rho (-\psi(v_\rho)) D_\rho$. Under this bijection, the group of principal Cartier divisors corresponds to the group of globally linear functions on $|\Sigma|$, i.e., functions of the form $u \mapsto \langle m, u\rangle$ for some $m \in M$.*

*Proof.*

*(Setup: $T$-invariant Cartier divisors are locally given by characters.)* A $T$-invariant Cartier divisor $D$ on $X_\Sigma$ is a Weil divisor with a Cartier-cocycle structure: an open cover $\{U_i\}$, a section $f_i \in K(X_\Sigma)^*$ on each $U_i$, and transitions $f_i / f_j \in \mathcal{O}_{X_\Sigma}^*(U_i \cap U_j)$ giving units on overlaps. For the canonical torus-invariant affine cover $\{U_\sigma\}_{\sigma \in \Sigma}$ of $X_\Sigma$ (from `[04.11.04]`), $T$-invariance forces each local section $f_\sigma$ to be a character: $f_\sigma = \chi^{m_\sigma}$ for some $m_\sigma \in M$. The cocycle condition $f_\sigma / f_\tau \in \mathcal{O}_{X_\Sigma}^*(U_\sigma \cap U_\tau)$ becomes $\chi^{m_\sigma - m_\tau} \in \mathcal{O}^*(U_{\sigma \cap \tau})$, equivalently $m_\sigma - m_\tau \in (\sigma \cap \tau)^\perp \cap M$ — the Cartier-cocycle compatibility.

*(From a Cartier divisor to a support function.)* Given a $T$-invariant Cartier divisor with local data $\{m_\sigma\}_{\sigma \in \Sigma_{\max}}$ satisfying the cocycle compatibility, define $\psi_D : |\Sigma| \to \mathbb{R}$ by $\psi_D(u) := \langle m_\sigma, u\rangle$ for $u \in \sigma$. The cocycle compatibility $m_\sigma - m_\tau \in (\sigma \cap \tau)^\perp$ ensures the pieces agree on overlaps, so $\psi_D$ is well-defined and continuous on $|\Sigma|$. By construction $\psi_D$ is linear on each maximal cone with integer-valued linear piece $m_\sigma \in M$. The value at a primitive ray generator $v_\rho \in \sigma$ is $\psi_D(v_\rho) = \langle m_\sigma, v_\rho\rangle$, which equals $-a_\rho$ by the local equation of $D$ on $U_\sigma$: the multiplicity of $D$ along $D_\rho$ is the order of vanishing of $\chi^{m_\sigma}$ at the generic point of $D_\rho \cap U_\sigma$, and this order equals $\langle m_\sigma, v_\rho\rangle$ on the toric chart. Setting $a_\rho = -\psi_D(v_\rho)$ recovers the Weil-divisor coefficients.

*(From a support function to a Cartier divisor.)* Conversely, given an integer-valued piecewise-linear function $\psi$ on $|\Sigma|$, let $m_\sigma \in M$ be the linear piece on each maximal cone $\sigma$. Continuity of $\psi$ across $\sigma \cap \tau$ forces $m_\sigma - m_\tau \in (\sigma \cap \tau)^\perp$. Define the Weil divisor $D_\psi := \sum_\rho (-\psi(v_\rho)) D_\rho$; the local sections $\chi^{m_\sigma}$ on each affine chart $U_\sigma$ supply Cartier data with the divisor $D_\psi$ as their associated Weil divisor.

*(Bijection.)* The two assignments $D \mapsto \psi_D$ and $\psi \mapsto D_\psi$ are mutually inverse by construction: starting from $D = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$ with Cartier data $\{m_\sigma\}$, the resulting $\psi_D$ has $\psi_D(v_\rho) = \langle m_\sigma, v_\rho\rangle = -a_\rho$, and so $D_{\psi_D} = \sum_\rho (-\psi_D(v_\rho)) D_\rho = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho = D$. Conversely $\psi_{D_\psi} = \psi$ on every maximal cone by the linear-piece identification.

*(Principal Cartier divisors correspond to globally linear functions.)* A principal $T$-invariant Cartier divisor is $\mathrm{div}(\chi^m) = \sum_\rho \langle m, v_\rho\rangle D_\rho$ for some $m \in M$. Its associated support function is $\psi_{\mathrm{div}(\chi^m)}(u) = -\langle m, u\rangle$, the globally linear function with global linear piece $-m$. Conversely, if $\psi$ is globally linear on $|\Sigma|$ with linear piece $m \in M$ (so $\psi(u) = \langle m, u\rangle$ for all $u$), then the associated divisor $D_\psi = \sum_\rho (-\langle m, v_\rho\rangle) D_\rho = -\mathrm{div}(\chi^m)$ is principal. So the bijection restricts to an identification of principal Cartier divisors with global linear functions on $|\Sigma|$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The support-function correspondence builds toward the polytope-fan dictionary of `[04.11.10]` and appears again in `[04.11.09]` as the supporting calculus for the toric Picard exact sequence. The central insight is that the entire Cartier-vs-Weil distinction on a toric variety, normally treated as a sheaf-theoretic question requiring local-ring arguments, reduces to a combinatorial gluing condition on piecewise-linear functions over a polyhedral fan.

This is exactly the foundational reason that line-bundle geometry on a toric variety becomes algorithmic: where on a general variety the Cartier condition requires checking local equations on every affine chart, on a toric variety the check collapses to a finite linear-algebra computation across walls between maximal cones. The bridge is to the projectivity criterion of `[04.11.04]`: $X_\Sigma$ is projective iff some support function is strictly convex, and the projective embedding is supplied directly by the lattice points of the divisor polytope $P_D$. Putting these together with the orbit-cone correspondence of `[04.11.06]`, the support-function correspondence identifies the entire $T$-equivariant divisor calculus of a toric variety with the combinatorics of piecewise-linear functions on its fan, generalises in the equivariant setting to higher-dimensional moment-map data, and is dual to the global-sections lattice-point formula via the polytope construction $D \leftrightarrow P_D$.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the support function $\psi_D$ explicitly for $D = D_0 + D_1 + D_2$ (the anticanonical divisor) on $\mathbb{P}^2$, and verify that its linear pieces $\{m_\sigma\}$ on the three maximal cones satisfy the Cartier-cocycle compatibility on pairwise intersections.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On each maximal cone $\sigma_{ij} = \mathrm{Cone}(v_i, v_j)$, solve the linear system $\langle m_{ij}, v_i\rangle = -1$ and $\langle m_{ij}, v_j\rangle = -1$. Then verify $m_{ij} - m_{ik} \in \rho_i^\perp$ for adjacent cones sharing the ray $\rho_i$.

- $\sigma_{12}$: $\langle m_{12}, e_1\rangle = -1$, $\langle m_{12}, e_2\rangle = -1$, so $m_{12} = -e_1^* - e_2^* = (-1, -1)$.
- $\sigma_{02}$: $\langle m_{02}, -e_1 - e_2\rangle = -1$, $\langle m_{02}, e_2\rangle = -1$. With $m_{02} = (a, b)$, the first gives $-a - b = -1$, the second gives $b = -1$, so $a = 2$. $m_{02} = (2, -1)$.

- $\sigma_{12}$ vs $\sigma_{02}$ on $\rho_2$: $m_{12} - m_{02} = (-1, -1) - (2, -1) = (-3, 0)$. Pair against $v_2 = e_2$: $\langle (-3, 0), e_2\rangle = 0$. Compatible.

- $\sigma_{01}$ vs $\sigma_{12}$ on $\rho_1$: $m_{01} - m_{12} = (-1, 2) - (-1, -1) = (0, 3)$. Pair against $v_1 = e_1$: $\langle (0, 3), e_1\rangle = 0$. Compatible.

So the three linear pieces glue continuously, and the support function $\psi_{-K}$ is well-defined.

**Geometric interpretation.** The support function $\psi_{-K}$ on $\mathbb{P}^2$ takes the value $-1$ at each $v_i$ and rises linearly on each maximal cone. Inside $\sigma_{12}$, $\psi(u) = \langle (-1, -1), u\rangle = -(s + t)$ for $u = s e_1 + t e_2$, reaching $\psi(e_1) = -1, \psi(e_2) = -1$. **Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

On the singular toric variety $\mathbb{P}(1, 2, 3)$ (fan in $\mathbb{R}^2$ with rays $v_0 = (1, 0), v_1 = (0, 1), v_2 = (-2, -3)$), determine whether the divisor $D_2$ (the toric divisor at the third ray) is Cartier. If not, find the smallest integer multiple of $D_2$ that is Cartier.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For each maximal cone $\sigma$, check whether there exists an integer character $m_\sigma \in M = \mathbb{Z}^2$ with $\langle m_\sigma, v_\rho\rangle = -a_\rho$ for every ray $\rho \subseteq \sigma$ — where $a_\rho$ is $1$ at $\rho = \rho_2$ and $0$ at other rays. The bottleneck is the cone containing $\rho_2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Maximal cones.** The fan of $\mathbb{P}(1, 2, 3)$ has three maximal cones $\sigma_{01} = \mathrm{Cone}(v_0, v_1), \sigma_{02} = \mathrm{Cone}(v_0, v_2), \sigma_{12} = \mathrm{Cone}(v_1, v_2)$.

**Cartier test for $D_2$.** Coefficients $a_0 = a_1 = 0, a_2 = 1$.

- $\sigma_{01}$ (does not contain $\rho_2$): need $\langle m_{01}, v_0\rangle = 0$, $\langle m_{01}, v_1\rangle = 0$, so $m_{01} = (0, 0)$ — integer, fine.
- $\sigma_{02}$ (contains $\rho_2$): need $\langle m_{02}, v_0\rangle = 0$, $\langle m_{02}, v_2\rangle = -1$. With $m_{02} = (a, b)$: $a = 0$, $-2a - 3b = -1$, so $-3b = -1$, $b = 1/3$. **Not an integer** — $D_2$ fails the Cartier condition at $\sigma_{02}$.
- $\sigma_{12}$ (contains $\rho_2$): need $\langle m_{12}, v_1\rangle = 0$, $\langle m_{12}, v_2\rangle = -1$. With $m_{12} = (a, b)$: $b = 0$, $-2a - 3b = -1$, so $-2a = -1$, $a = 1/2$. **Not an integer** — $D_2$ fails at $\sigma_{12}$.

**Exercise 5 (medium, multiple choice).**

Let $X_\Sigma$ be a smooth complete toric variety of dimension $n$ and let $D = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$ be a $T$-invariant Cartier divisor with associated support function $\psi_D$ and divisor polytope $P_D$. Which of the following statements is **always true**?

A. $\dim H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = |P_D \cap M|$ when $D$ is nef.
B. The support function $\psi_D$ is constant on each maximal cone of $\Sigma$.
C. The divisor polytope $P_D$ is empty whenever $D$ has any negative coefficient $a_\rho < 0$.
D. Every torus-invariant Weil divisor on $X_\Sigma$ is principal.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

One of these is the Demazure global-sections formula. The others have subtle counterexamples.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**A.** The Demazure global-sections formula (Fulton §3.4 Proposition 1, Cox-Little-Schenck Theorem 4.3.3) asserts that when $D$ is nef on a smooth complete toric $X_\Sigma$, the lattice points of $P_D$ give a basis of $H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D))$: one character $\chi^m$ per lattice point $m \in P_D \cap M$, with $\dim H^0 = |P_D \cap M|$ exactly.

Option B is wrong: $\psi_D$ is **linear** (not constant) on each maximal cone, with linear piece $m_\sigma \in M$. Constants come up only when $\psi_D \equiv 0$, the divisor zero.

Option C is wrong: $P_D$ depends on all coefficients jointly, and a negative $a_\rho < 0$ tightens the inequality $\langle m, v_\rho\rangle \geq -a_\rho$ to $\langle m, v_\rho\rangle \geq |a_\rho| > 0$, which can be satisfied. Example: on $\mathbb{P}^1$, the divisor $D = 2 D_0 - D_1$ has $P_D = \{m \in \mathbb{R} : m \geq -2, -m \geq 1\} = [-2, -1]$, with two lattice points $\{-2, -1\}$, giving $h^0 = 2$ (the divisor is $\mathcal{O}(1)$ on $\mathbb{P}^1$, which has two sections).

Option D is wrong: principal divisors are exactly the image of $\mathrm{div} : M \to \mathrm{Div}_T(X_\Sigma)$, which has rank $n$ inside the rank-$|\Sigma(1)|$ group $\mathrm{Div}_T$. For most $X_\Sigma$, $|\Sigma(1)| > n$, so there are non-principal Weil divisors. Example: on $\mathbb{P}^2$, the divisor $D_0$ is not principal; only $D_0 - D_1$ (and integer multiples) are.

Rubric: full credit for identifying A and noting the counterexamples for B, C, D.

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

On the Hirzebruch surface $F_a$ with fan $v_1 = e_1, v_2 = e_2, v_3 = -e_1 + a e_2, v_4 = -e_2$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$, compute the divisor polytope $P_D$ for $D = D_1 + D_3 + b D_2$ and identify for which integer $b$ the divisor is nef.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write out the four ray inequalities; the polytope is bounded iff the four constraints close up into a finite region. The nef condition translates into the polytope being non-empty and full-dimensional.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Inequalities.** Coefficients $(a_1, a_2, a_3, a_4) = (1, b, 1, 0)$. For $m = (s, t)$:
- $\langle m, v_1\rangle = s \geq -1$;
- $\langle m, v_2\rangle = t \geq -b$;
- $\langle m, v_3\rangle = -s + a t \geq -1$, i.e., $s - a t \leq 1$;
- $\langle m, v_4\rangle = -t \geq 0$, i.e., $t \leq 0$.

**Polytope.** The constraints $t \leq 0$ and $t \geq -b$ require $b \geq 0$ for the polytope to be non-empty (vertically). With $b \geq 0$, $t$ ranges over $[-b, 0]$. The horizontal extent: $s \geq -1$ and $s \leq 1 + a t$. The polytope is non-empty iff $1 + a t \geq -1$ for some $t \in [-b, 0]$, i.e., $a t \geq -2$. For $a \geq 0$ and $t \in [-b, 0]$, $a t \in [-a b, 0]$, so $\min a t = -a b$. The non-emptiness condition becomes $-a b \geq -2$, i.e., $a b \leq 2$. But more importantly we want $P_D$ to be non-empty for **every** $t \in [-b, 0]$, not just some — for the polytope to be full-dimensional, we need $-1 \leq 1 + a t$ for all $t \in [-b, 0]$, i.e., $a t \geq -2$ for all $t \in [-b, 0]$. With $a \geq 0$ this reduces to $-a b \geq -2$, i.e., $a b \leq 2$.

**Nef condition.** The divisor $D = D_1 + D_3 + b D_2$ is nef iff its support function $\psi_D$ is convex on $|\Sigma| = \mathbb{R}^2$. For $a = 1$ ($F_1 = \mathrm{Bl}_p \mathbb{P}^2$): nef iff $0 \leq b \leq 2$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove the Demazure global-sections formula on a smooth complete toric variety $X_\Sigma$: for a $T$-invariant Cartier divisor $D = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$,
$$
H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = \bigoplus_{m \in P_D \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m,
$$
where $P_D = \{m \in M_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, v_\rho\rangle \geq -a_\rho \text{ for all } \rho \in \Sigma(1)\}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Decompose $H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D))$ into $T$-weight spaces under the natural torus action. For each character $m \in M$, identify when $\chi^m$ extends to a section of $\mathcal{O}(D)$ in terms of the divisor of $\chi^m$ versus the divisor $D$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Step 1: weight decomposition.** The torus $T$ acts on $X_\Sigma$, hence on $\mathcal{O}(D)$ ($T$-linearised), and on its space of global sections. By the standard theorem of finite-dimensional torus representations, $H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D))$ decomposes into a direct sum of weight spaces:
$$
H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = \bigoplus_{m \in M} H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D))_m,
$$
where $H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D))_m$ is the subspace of $\chi^m$-equivariant sections.

**Step 2: each weight space is at most one-dimensional.** A weight-$m$ global section of $\mathcal{O}(D)$ corresponds to a $T$-equivariant rational function $f$ on $X_\Sigma$ with $\chi^m$-equivariance and $\mathrm{div}(f) + D \geq 0$. Equivariance forces $f$ to be a scalar multiple of $\chi^m$ as a rational function on the dense torus $T \subset X_\Sigma$. So $H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D))_m$ is either $\mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m$ or $0$, depending on whether $\chi^m$ extends to a section.

**Step 3: extension condition.** $\chi^m \in H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D))$ iff $\mathrm{div}(\chi^m) + D \geq 0$ as a Weil divisor. Recall $\mathrm{div}(\chi^m) = \sum_\rho \langle m, v_\rho\rangle D_\rho$. Adding: $\mathrm{div}(\chi^m) + D = \sum_\rho (\langle m, v_\rho\rangle + a_\rho) D_\rho$. The condition $\mathrm{div}(\chi^m) + D \geq 0$ becomes $\langle m, v_\rho\rangle + a_\rho \geq 0$ for every $\rho \in \Sigma(1)$, i.e., $\langle m, v_\rho\rangle \geq -a_\rho$. This is exactly the defining inequality of the divisor polytope $P_D$.

**Step 4: conclusion.** $H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D))_m = \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m$ iff $m \in P_D \cap M$, and zero otherwise. Summing over all weights $m \in M$:
$$
H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = \bigoplus_{m \in P_D \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m. \qquad \square
$$

**Remark.** The proof uses only the $T$-action and the divisor calculus on the toric variety, not any specific smoothness or completeness. For singular toric $X_\Sigma$ the same formula applies with the same polytope $P_D$; for non-complete $X_\Sigma$ the polytope $P_D$ can be unbounded but the formula still holds (with the sum over lattice points of an unbounded polytope giving an infinite-dimensional cohomology when appropriate). The smoothness assumption is needed when one further wants to identify higher cohomology — the Demazure vanishing theorem $H^i(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = 0$ for $i \geq 1, D$ nef — but for $H^0$ alone the polytope formula is universal.

Rubric: full credit for the weight decomposition, the one-dimensionality of each weight space, the extension criterion, and the identification with $P_D \cap M$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove that on a smooth toric variety $X_\Sigma$, every $T$-invariant Weil divisor is Cartier. (Equivalently: the inclusion $\mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma) \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$ is an equality.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Smoothness means every cone $\sigma$ of $\Sigma$ is generated by part of a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$. Use this to solve the Cartier-cocycle system on each maximal cone.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Step 1: reduce to maximal cones.** A $T$-invariant Weil divisor $D = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$ is Cartier iff it has a piecewise-linear support function $\psi_D$ with integer linear pieces. The linear pieces are pinned by the values at primitive ray generators: $\psi_D(v_\rho) = -a_\rho$. The question is whether, on each maximal cone $\sigma$, there exists a character $m_\sigma \in M$ realising these prescribed values on the rays of $\sigma$.

**Step 2: smoothness gives unique solution on each cone.** Suppose $X_\Sigma$ is smooth, so every cone $\sigma$ is generated by part of a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$. Let $\sigma$ be a maximal cone of dimension $k \leq n$ with primitive ray generators $v_{\rho_1}, \ldots, v_{\rho_k}$ forming part of a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis $\{v_{\rho_1}, \ldots, v_{\rho_k}, w_{k+1}, \ldots, w_n\}$ of $N$. The dual basis $\{v_{\rho_1}^*, \ldots, v_{\rho_k}^*, w_{k+1}^*, \ldots, w_n^*\}$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $M$. Define
$$
m_\sigma := -\sum_{i=1}^{k} a_{\rho_i} \cdot v_{\rho_i}^*.
$$
This is an element of $M$ (integer linear combination of integer-basis elements). It satisfies $\langle m_\sigma, v_{\rho_j}\rangle = -a_{\rho_j}$ for $j = 1, \ldots, k$ by dual-basis duality, and $\langle m_\sigma, w_l\rangle$ can be any integer (the rays of $\sigma$ do not constrain those coordinates). The choice above (setting the $w_l$-components to zero) is one valid solution.

For maximal $\sigma$ (dimension $n$), the rays of $\sigma$ form a full $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$ and the choice of $m_\sigma$ is unique.

**Step 3: cocycle compatibility.** On a pairwise intersection $\sigma \cap \tau$ between two maximal cones, the common rays $\rho \subseteq \sigma \cap \tau$ have $\langle m_\sigma, v_\rho\rangle = \langle m_\tau, v_\rho\rangle = -a_\rho$ by construction. So $\langle m_\sigma - m_\tau, v_\rho\rangle = 0$ for every common ray, hence $m_\sigma - m_\tau \in (\sigma \cap \tau)^\perp$. This is exactly the Cartier-cocycle compatibility.

**Step 4: conclusion.** Every $T$-invariant Weil divisor $D = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$ on a smooth toric $X_\Sigma$ admits a piecewise-linear support function $\psi_D$ with integer linear pieces, hence is Cartier. Equivalently, $\mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma) = \mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$ on smooth toric. $\square$

**Converse.** If $X_\Sigma$ is not smooth, there exists a non-smooth cone $\sigma$ with primitive ray generators not forming a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis. Pick coefficients $a_\rho$ at the rays of $\sigma$ that have no integer solution for $m_\sigma$ (generic coefficients work). The corresponding Weil divisor is not Cartier. So the equality $\mathrm{Pic} = \mathrm{Cl}$ is **equivalent** to smoothness, a stronger statement going both directions.

Rubric: full credit for the cone-by-cone construction of $m_\sigma$, the dual-basis decomposition, the cocycle compatibility, and the converse.

## Advanced results [Master]

### §A. T-invariant prime divisors and the support-function correspondence

**Theorem (support-function correspondence — full form; Demazure 1970, Fulton §3.4 Proposition 1).** *Let $\Sigma$ be a fan in $N_\mathbb{R}$ and let $X_\Sigma$ be the associated toric variety. There is a canonical isomorphism of abelian groups*
$$
\mathrm{CDiv}_T(X_\Sigma) \cong \mathrm{PL}_\mathbb{Z}(\Sigma),
$$
*where $\mathrm{CDiv}_T(X_\Sigma)$ denotes the group of $T$-invariant Cartier divisors on $X_\Sigma$ and $\mathrm{PL}_\mathbb{Z}(\Sigma)$ denotes the group of integer-valued piecewise-linear functions on $|\Sigma|$ that are linear on each cone of $\Sigma$ with linear piece in $M$. The isomorphism sends $D = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho \mapsto \psi_D$ with $\psi_D(v_\rho) = -a_\rho$ and linear pieces $\{m_\sigma\}_{\sigma \in \Sigma_{\max}}$ satisfying the Cartier-cocycle compatibility.*

The full Demazure correspondence reorganises Cartier-divisor theory on $X_\Sigma$ around the combinatorial object $\mathrm{PL}_\mathbb{Z}(\Sigma)$. The key conceptual move is that local Cartier data — a chart-by-chart choice of $\chi^{m_\sigma}$ giving the local equation of the divisor — is encoded globally by a single continuous piecewise-linear function. Continuity is the cocycle compatibility; integrality is the Cartier integrality.

**Theorem (principal Cartier divisors $\leftrightarrow$ linear functions; Fulton §3.4).** *Under the isomorphism above, the subgroup of principal $T$-invariant Cartier divisors* $\{\mathrm{div}(\chi^m) : m \in M\}$ *corresponds to the subgroup* $M \subseteq \mathrm{PL}_\mathbb{Z}(\Sigma)$ *of globally linear functions* $u \mapsto \langle m, u\rangle$.

The principal-vs-Cartier distinction translates, via the correspondence, into the elementary observation that globally linear functions form a sub-lattice of piecewise-linear functions. The quotient $\mathrm{PL}_\mathbb{Z}(\Sigma) / M$ is the **Picard group** $\mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma)$ for smooth $X_\Sigma$, and a finer Cartier-cocycle-graded quotient for singular $X_\Sigma$ (cross-reference `[04.11.09]`).

**Theorem (orbit-cone refinement; Cox-Little-Schenck §4.1).** *Let $D_\rho \subseteq X_\Sigma$ be the closure of the codimension-one orbit attached to the ray $\rho$ under the orbit-cone correspondence of `[04.11.06]`. Then $D_\rho$ is a $T$-invariant prime Weil divisor, and the assignment $\rho \mapsto D_\rho$ is a bijection between rays of $\Sigma$ and $T$-invariant prime Weil divisors on $X_\Sigma$. The orbit-closure decomposition*
$$
D_\rho = \bigsqcup_{\tau \succeq \rho} O(\tau)
$$
*expresses $D_\rho$ as the disjoint union of all torus orbits indexed by cones $\tau \in \Sigma$ containing $\rho$.*

The orbit-closure decomposition supplies the geometric meaning of the divisor $D_\rho$: it is the closure of a single orbit and stratifies into smaller orbits indexed by cones in the star of $\rho$. This is the divisor-level statement of the orbit-cone correspondence and supplies the bridge from the cone combinatorics of $\Sigma$ to the divisor geometry of $X_\Sigma$.

### §B. Cartier vs Weil — when they agree

**Theorem (smoothness criterion at the divisor level; Fulton §3.4 Proposition 2).** *Let $X_\Sigma$ be a toric variety. Then $X_\Sigma$ is smooth iff every $T$-invariant Weil divisor on $X_\Sigma$ is Cartier. Equivalently, the inclusion $\mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma) \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$ is an equality iff $X_\Sigma$ is smooth iff every cone $\sigma \in \Sigma$ is unimodular (generated by part of a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$).*

The proof in the forward direction follows from the smoothness criterion of `[04.11.05]` (the cones are unimodular) together with the dual-basis Cartier construction sketched in Exercise 8 above: every Weil divisor admits a piecewise-linear support function by solving the Cartier-cocycle system on each cone using the dual basis. The reverse direction follows by exhibiting, at any non-smooth cone, a Weil divisor whose Cartier system has no integer solution.

**Theorem ($\mathbb{Q}$-Cartier refinement; Cox-Little-Schenck §4.2).** *Let $X_\Sigma$ be a simplicial toric variety (every cone has $\dim$-many rays — possibly not forming a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis). Then every $T$-invariant Weil divisor on $X_\Sigma$ is $\mathbb{Q}$-Cartier: some positive integer multiple is Cartier. The smallest such multiplier is the **multiplicity** of the worst non-smooth cone, defined as $\mathrm{mult}(\sigma) = |\det(v_{\rho_1}, \ldots, v_{\rho_n})|$ for the primitive ray generators of $\sigma$.*

The simplicial case interpolates between smooth and general singular. For $\mathbb{Q}$-factorial toric (= simplicial), the Picard group is a finite-index sublattice of the divisor class group, with index controlled by cone multiplicities. For non-simplicial toric, even rational Cartier divisors are obstructed, and one requires the full divisor class group machinery of `[04.11.09]`.

**Theorem (Cartier-cocycle exact sequence; Fulton §3.4, KKMS Ch. I §2).** *There is a short exact sequence of abelian groups*
$$
0 \to M \to \mathrm{PL}_\mathbb{Z}(\Sigma) \to \mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma) \to 0,
$$
*with the first map sending $m \mapsto \psi_m$ (the globally linear function $u \mapsto \langle m, u\rangle$, corresponding to the principal divisor $-\mathrm{div}(\chi^m)$) and the second the natural quotient (a piecewise-linear function to its associated Cartier-divisor class modulo principal).*

This is the support-function-version of the toric Picard exact sequence in `[04.11.09]`. The middle term $\mathrm{PL}_\mathbb{Z}(\Sigma)$ replaces the abstract divisor group, supplying a combinatorial parameterisation of Cartier divisors that is more directly amenable to ampleness analysis (where strict convexity of the piecewise-linear function characterises ample divisors, per `[04.11.09]` §C).

### §C. Sections via lattice-point inequalities — the polytope of sections

**Theorem (Demazure global-sections formula; Fulton §3.4 Proposition 1, Cox-Little-Schenck Theorem 4.3.3).** *Let $X_\Sigma$ be a toric variety and $D = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$ a $T$-invariant Cartier divisor. Then*
$$
H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = \bigoplus_{m \in P_D \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m,
$$
*where $P_D = \{m \in M_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, v_\rho\rangle \geq -a_\rho \text{ for all } \rho \in \Sigma(1)\}$ is the divisor polytope. In particular,*
$$
\dim H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = |P_D \cap M|
$$
*— the number of lattice points of $P_D$.*

The proof is the $T$-equivariant decomposition argument from Exercise 7: $H^0$ decomposes into weight spaces by the torus action, each weight space is at most one-dimensional (spanned by a $\chi^m$), and the extension criterion $\mathrm{div}(\chi^m) + D \geq 0$ reduces to the polytope-defining inequalities. The smoothness or completeness of $X_\Sigma$ is not needed for $H^0$; the same formula holds for arbitrary toric varieties.

**Theorem (Demazure vanishing; Fulton §3.4, Cox-Little-Schenck Theorem 9.2.3).** *Let $X_\Sigma$ be a smooth complete toric variety and $D$ a $T$-invariant Cartier divisor that is **nef** (i.e., $\psi_D$ is convex on $|\Sigma|$). Then $H^i(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = 0$ for every $i \geq 1$.*

Demazure vanishing supplies the higher-cohomology counterpart to the global-sections formula: when $D$ is nef, the Euler characteristic $\chi(\mathcal{O}(D)) = \sum_i (-1)^i \dim H^i(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D))$ reduces to $\dim H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = |P_D \cap M|$, a lattice-point count. This realises Hirzebruch-Riemann-Roch on a smooth complete toric variety as a purely combinatorial Ehrhart-polynomial computation (Brion-Vergne 1997 *J. Amer. Math. Soc.* 10).

**Theorem (Ehrhart polynomial; Ehrhart 1962, Brion-Vergne 1997).** *Let $X_\Sigma$ be a projective smooth toric variety and $L = \mathcal{O}(D)$ a nef line bundle with divisor polytope $P = P_D$. For each integer $k \geq 1$, the lattice-point count*
$$
L(P, k) := |kP \cap M|
$$
*is a polynomial in $k$ of degree $n = \dim X_\Sigma$, called the **Ehrhart polynomial** of $P$. Its leading coefficient is the lattice volume of $P$, $\mathrm{vol}(P) / n!$, and the polynomial satisfies the Hilbert-function identity*
$$
L(P, k) = \dim H^0(X_\Sigma, L^{\otimes k}) = \chi(X_\Sigma, L^{\otimes k}).
$$

The Ehrhart polynomial is the toric realisation of the Hilbert polynomial in classical projective geometry. Its leading coefficient (the volume) recovers the degree of $X_\Sigma$ under the polarisation $L$; its lower-order coefficients encode the Todd class times the Chern character, exhibiting Hirzebruch-Riemann-Roch as a polynomial identity in $k$.

### §D. Worked example — anticanonical on $\mathbb{P}^2$

**Theorem (anticanonical divisor on $\mathbb{P}^n$; Fulton §3.5).** *Let $\mathbb{P}^n$ have its standard toric fan with rays $v_0 = -e_1 - \cdots - e_n, v_1 = e_1, \ldots, v_n = e_n$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Then:*

1. *The canonical class is $K_{\mathbb{P}^n} = -(D_0 + D_1 + \cdots + D_n) \in \mathrm{Pic}(\mathbb{P}^n)$.*
2. *The anticanonical divisor $-K = D_0 + D_1 + \cdots + D_n$ has divisor polytope $P_{-K}$ the size-$(n+1)$ simplex in $M_\mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}^n$ with vertices $(n, -1, -1, \ldots, -1), (-1, n, -1, \ldots, -1), \ldots, (-1, \ldots, -1, n)$ and $(-1, -1, \ldots, -1)$.*
3. *The lattice-point count is $|P_{-K} \cap M| = \binom{2n+1}{n}$.*
4. *Equivalently, $\mathcal{O}(-K_{\mathbb{P}^n}) = \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^n}(n+1)$, with global sections of dimension $\binom{2n+1}{n}$ — the space of homogeneous degree-$(n+1)$ polynomials in $n+1$ variables.*

For $n = 2$: $-K_{\mathbb{P}^2} = D_0 + D_1 + D_2 = 3 H$, polytope is the size-three simplex with $\binom{5}{2} = 10$ lattice points, dimension of plane cubics. For $n = 3$: $-K_{\mathbb{P}^3} = 4 H$, polytope is the size-four simplex with $\binom{7}{3} = 35$ lattice points, dimension of homogeneous quartics in four variables.

The anticanonical example is the cleanest illustration of the entire divisor-and-support-function framework. The divisor $-K_{\mathbb{P}^2}$ is an honest geometric object (the sum of the three coordinate lines); the support function $\psi_{-K}$ is a piecewise-linear ramp pinned at $-1$ on each ray; the divisor polytope $P_{-K}$ is a lattice triangle with exactly ten integer points; and the resulting global sections are the ten monomials forming a basis of the cubic plane curves linear system.

**Theorem (anticanonical on Hirzebruch surfaces; Fulton §3.4).** *On the Hirzebruch surface $F_a = X_{\Sigma_a}$ with the four-ray fan $v_1 = e_1, v_2 = e_2, v_3 = -e_1 + a e_2, v_4 = -e_2$, the canonical class is $K_{F_a} = -(D_1 + D_2 + D_3 + D_4) + (a - 2) D_2$, the anticanonical divisor is $-K = D_1 + (2 - a) D_2 + D_3 + D_4$, and $-K$ is ample iff $a \leq 1$ (i.e., $F_0 = \mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ and $F_1 = \mathrm{Bl}_p \mathbb{P}^2$ are del Pezzo surfaces; $F_a$ for $a \geq 2$ is not Fano).*

The Hirzebruch-surface anticanonical example illustrates that the support-function framework distinguishes ample from non-ample with a single combinatorial check (strict convexity vs convexity of the piecewise-linear function on the fan). The transition $a = 1 \to a = 2$ — where $-K$ drops from ample to merely nef — corresponds to a wall-crossing in the support-function space, with the Hirzebruch surface losing its Fano status as the tilt $a$ exceeds the del Pezzo threshold.

**Theorem (anticanonical on toric Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces; Batyrev 1994).** *Let $X_\Sigma$ be a projective smooth toric variety such that the anticanonical class $-K_{X_\Sigma}$ is associated to a **reflexive polytope** $P^\circ$ (the polar dual of the polytope $P = P_{-K}$ defining $X_\Sigma$). Then generic anticanonical hypersurfaces $Y \subset X_\Sigma$ are smooth Calabi-Yau varieties, and the polar duality $P \leftrightarrow P^\circ$ supplies the **Batyrev mirror Calabi-Yau** $Y^\circ \subset X_{\Sigma^\circ}$. The 473,800,776 reflexive 4-polytopes classified by Kreuzer-Skarke 2000-2002 account for the bulk of explicitly known Calabi-Yau 3-folds and their mirrors.*

The Batyrev construction lifts the anticanonical divisor from a worked example to a mirror-symmetry generator. The reflexive-polytope condition on $P_{-K}$ — that the polar dual is also a lattice polytope — is the combinatorial signature of Gorenstein toric Calabi-Yau geometry, and the polar-duality involution becomes the mirror-symmetry exchange of A-side and B-side Calabi-Yau geometries (cross-reference `[04.11.10]` for the polytope-fan dictionary and `[04.12.09]` for the Gross-Siebert reconstruction generalisation).

**Synthesis.** The toric divisor and support function form the foundational reason that line-bundle and section geometry on a toric variety reduces to combinatorial calculations on a polyhedral fan: prime Weil divisors are atoms indexed by rays, Cartier divisors are encoded by piecewise-linear functions, and global sections are enumerated by lattice points of a polytope. The central insight is that the three different presentations — divisor, support function, polytope — are three avatars of the same Cartier-divisor object, with the bijection furnished by Fulton §3.4 Proposition 1 making the whole calculus algorithmic.

This is exactly the foundational fact that identifies toric divisor theory with algorithmic algebraic geometry: where a general variety's divisor theory requires sheaf-cohomological computation via spectral sequences or derived categories, on a toric variety the entire calculus reduces to lattice arithmetic. The bridge is the global-sections formula $H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = \bigoplus_{m \in P_D \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m$ — sheaf cohomology becomes lattice-point counting, and Hirzebruch-Riemann-Roch becomes a polynomial identity in Ehrhart polynomials.

Putting these together with the toric Picard exact sequence of `[04.11.09]`, the support-function correspondence is the bridge between fan combinatorics and divisor / line-bundle geometry. The pattern recurs in three generalisations. To **higher cohomology**, Demazure vanishing supplies $H^i(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = 0$ for nef $D$ and $i \geq 1$, reducing Euler characteristics to Ehrhart polynomials and Hirzebruch-Riemann-Roch to lattice-point identities (Brion-Vergne 1997). To **singular toric**, the Cartier-Weil distinction widens; one still has support functions, but the Cartier subgroup is a finite-index sublattice of the piecewise-linear function lattice, with index controlled by cone multiplicities (Cox-Little-Schenck §4.2). To **equivariant cohomology**, the support-function description lifts to a description of equivariant Cartier divisors in terms of $M$-graded sheaves on the fan, and the Demazure formula becomes an equivariant Riemann-Roch theorem in $T$-equivariant K-theory (Vergne 1999, Brion-Vergne 1997). Each generalisation preserves the divisor-support-function-polytope triad and identifies the line-bundle theory of the toric variety with a piece of combinatorial polytope theory.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (support-function correspondence), proof.** Given in the key theorem. The forward direction sends a $T$-invariant Cartier divisor to its piecewise-linear support function by reading off local Cartier data $\{m_\sigma\}$ from each affine chart $U_\sigma$ and gluing into a continuous $\psi_D$. The reverse direction reads off Weil-divisor coefficients from $-\psi(v_\rho)$ at each primitive ray generator and reconstructs the Cartier data $\{m_\sigma\}$ from the integer linear pieces. Mutual inverses are confirmed by the chart-by-chart identification $a_\rho = -\psi_D(v_\rho) = -\langle m_\sigma, v_\rho\rangle$ for $\rho \subseteq \sigma$. $\square$

**Proposition (smoothness criterion at divisor level; Fulton §3.4 Prop 2), proof.** *On a smooth toric $X_\Sigma$, every $T$-invariant Weil divisor is Cartier; conversely, if every $T$-invariant Weil divisor on $X_\Sigma$ is Cartier, then $X_\Sigma$ is smooth.*

*Proof.* Given in Exercise 8. Forward direction: on a smooth cone $\sigma$, the primitive ray generators $v_{\rho_1}, \ldots, v_{\rho_k}$ extend to a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$. The dual basis $\{v_{\rho_i}^*\}$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $M$, and one constructs $m_\sigma = -\sum_i a_{\rho_i} v_{\rho_i}^* \in M$ as an explicit integer character satisfying $\langle m_\sigma, v_{\rho_j}\rangle = -a_{\rho_j}$ for every $j$. Cocycle compatibility follows on shared rays.

Reverse direction: if $X_\Sigma$ has a non-smooth cone $\sigma$, the primitive ray generators of $\sigma$ do not form a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N \cap \mathbb{R}\langle\sigma\rangle$. Pick coefficients $a_\rho$ at the rays of $\sigma$ such that the linear system $\langle m_\sigma, v_\rho\rangle = -a_\rho$ has no integer solution (generic coefficients work — the system has a unique $\mathbb{Q}$-solution, and irrationality of denominators in the basis matrix of the rays forces some $a_\rho$ to fail integer-divisibility). The resulting Weil divisor $D = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$ is not Cartier. So smoothness at the cone level is equivalent to all-Weil-Cartier. $\square$

**Proposition (Demazure global-sections formula; Fulton §3.4 Prop 1), proof.** Given in Exercise 7. The weight-decomposition argument shows $H^0(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = \bigoplus_m H^0_m$ with each $H^0_m$ at most one-dimensional, spanned by $\chi^m$ when $m \in P_D \cap M$ (the extension criterion $\mathrm{div}(\chi^m) + D \geq 0$ rewrites as the polytope inequalities $\langle m, v_\rho\rangle \geq -a_\rho$) and zero otherwise. $\square$

**Proposition (Demazure vanishing; Cox-Little-Schenck Theorem 9.2.3), proof sketch.** *On a smooth complete toric $X_\Sigma$, $H^i(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = 0$ for $i \geq 1$ when $D$ is nef.*

*Proof sketch.* The cohomology $H^i(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D))$ decomposes into $T$-weight pieces $H^i_m$. For each $m \in M$, the weight piece is computed by the cellular cohomology of a complex of sub-fans of $\Sigma$ depending on $m$ — concretely, the simplicial complex $\Sigma_m := \{\sigma \in \Sigma : m \notin \sigma^\vee + \sum_\rho a_\rho m_\rho^\sigma\}$ where $m_\rho^\sigma$ is the local Cartier datum. When $D$ is nef (equivalently $\psi_D$ is convex), $\Sigma_m$ is either empty (giving zero contribution to all $H^i$) or contractible (giving zero contribution to $H^i$ for $i \geq 1$ by the cellular cohomology of a contractible complex). Either way, $H^i_m = 0$ for $i \geq 1$, hence $H^i(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D)) = 0$ for $i \geq 1$. The full argument uses the toric Čech-cohomology computation of Demazure 1970 refined by Mavlyutov 2000 *Compositio Math.* 124. $\square$

**Proposition (Ehrhart polynomiality; Ehrhart 1962, Brion-Vergne 1997), proof sketch.** *For a lattice polytope $P \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ of dimension $n$, the function $L(P, k) = |kP \cap M|$ is a polynomial in $k$ of degree $n$ with rational coefficients.*

*Proof sketch.* Two complementary arguments. The classical Ehrhart 1962 *C. R. Acad. Sci.* 254 proof uses the generating function $\sum_{k \geq 0} L(P, k) t^k = h^*(P, t) / (1 - t)^{n + 1}$ with $h^*(P, t)$ a polynomial of degree $\leq n$, giving polynomiality of $L(P, k)$ by direct power-series manipulation. The Brion-Vergne 1997 proof uses the Atiyah-Bott-Berline-Vergne equivariant localisation on the toric variety $X_P$: the Euler characteristic $\chi(X_P, L_P^{\otimes k})$ localises to a sum over toric-fixed points, each contribution being a rational function of $k$, with the cancellations giving polynomiality. Equivalently, by the Demazure global-sections formula and Demazure vanishing, $L(P, k) = \chi(X_P, L_P^{\otimes k})$ is the Hilbert polynomial of the polarised toric variety $(X_P, L_P)$ in degree $k$ — polynomial in $k$ by Hilbert's theorem on Hilbert polynomials. $\square$

**Proposition (anticanonical on $\mathbb{P}^n$), proof.** *On $\mathbb{P}^n$ with the standard toric fan, $-K_{\mathbb{P}^n} = D_0 + D_1 + \cdots + D_n$ has $|P_{-K} \cap M| = \binom{2n+1}{n}$.*

*Proof.* The fan has $n + 1$ rays $v_0 = -e_1 - \cdots - e_n, v_1 = e_1, \ldots, v_n = e_n$, with the sum identity $v_0 + v_1 + \cdots + v_n = 0$. The anticanonical polytope $P_{-K}$ is defined by $\langle m, v_i\rangle \geq -1$ for $i = 0, 1, \ldots, n$. The constraints $\langle m, v_i\rangle = m_i \geq -1$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$ give $m_i \geq -1$. The constraint $\langle m, v_0\rangle = -m_1 - \cdots - m_n \geq -1$ gives $m_1 + \cdots + m_n \leq 1$. So $P_{-K} = \{m \in \mathbb{R}^n : m_i \geq -1 \text{ for } i = 1, \ldots, n, \text{ and } \sum_i m_i \leq 1\}$, the size-$(n+1)$ lattice simplex translated.

Translate by $m \mapsto m + (1, 1, \ldots, 1)$: in new coordinates $m'_i = m_i + 1 \geq 0$, $\sum_i m'_i \leq n + 1$. So $P_{-K}$ becomes the standard simplex $\{m' \in \mathbb{R}^n : m'_i \geq 0, \sum_i m'_i \leq n + 1\}$. The lattice points in this simplex are tuples $(m'_1, \ldots, m'_n) \in \mathbb{Z}^n_{\geq 0}$ with $m'_1 + \cdots + m'_n \leq n + 1$, equivalent to $(m'_0, m'_1, \ldots, m'_n) \in \mathbb{Z}^{n+1}_{\geq 0}$ with $m'_0 + m'_1 + \cdots + m'_n = n + 1$ (introducing $m'_0 := n + 1 - \sum_i m'_i$).

The count of non-negative integer tuples summing to $n + 1$ is the multinomial coefficient $\binom{(n+1) + n}{n} = \binom{2n+1}{n}$. So $|P_{-K} \cap M| = \binom{2n+1}{n}$. For $n = 2$: $\binom{5}{2} = 10$. For $n = 3$: $\binom{7}{3} = 35$. For $n = 4$: $\binom{9}{4} = 126$. $\square$

**Proposition ($\mathbb{Q}$-Cartier on $\mathbb{P}(1, 2, 3)$), proof.** *On the singular weighted projective space $\mathbb{P}(1, 2, 3)$ with fan rays $v_0 = (1, 0), v_1 = (0, 1), v_2 = (-2, -3)$, the toric divisor $D_2$ is not Cartier, but $6 D_2$ is.*

*Proof.* Given in Exercise 4. The Cartier-cocycle system at $\sigma_{02} = \mathrm{Cone}(v_0, v_2)$ requires $\langle m_{02}, v_0\rangle = 0$ and $\langle m_{02}, v_2\rangle = -1$. With $m_{02} = (a, b)$: $a = 0, -2a - 3b = -1$, giving $b = 1/3$, not integer. Similarly $\sigma_{12} = \mathrm{Cone}(v_1, v_2)$ requires $b = 0, -2a - 3b = -1$, giving $a = 1/2$, not integer. So $D_2$ fails Cartier at both singular cones. For $6 D_2$, the constraints become $\langle m_{02}, v_2\rangle = -6$ with $m_{02} = (0, 2)$ (integer) and $\langle m_{12}, v_2\rangle = -6$ with $m_{12} = (3, 0)$ (integer). Cocycle compatibility on shared rays is direct. So $6 D_2$ is Cartier, and $6 = \mathrm{lcm}(2, 3)$ is the minimal positive multiplier matching the toric Picard / class group index calculation from `[04.11.09]`. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Fan and toric variety $X_\Sigma$ [04.11.04].** The structural prerequisite. The toric divisor $D_\rho$ for $\rho \in \Sigma(1)$ is defined relative to the gluing $X_\Sigma = \bigcup_\sigma U_\sigma$ from `[04.11.04]`, and the Cartier-cocycle compatibility of a support function uses the chart-overlap structure $U_{\sigma \cap \tau} \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$ from the fan construction. The smoothness criterion that lifts smoothness from $X_\Sigma$ to "every Weil divisor is Cartier" passes through the unimodular-cone condition of `[04.11.04]`.

- **Orbit-cone correspondence [04.11.06].** The geometric realisation of $D_\rho$ as the closure of the codimension-one torus orbit $O(\rho)$ uses the orbit-cone correspondence directly. The dimension formula $\dim O(\rho) + \dim \rho = n$ identifies $D_\rho$ as a divisor (dimension $n - 1$) since rays are one-dimensional cones. The orbit-closure decomposition $D_\rho = \bigsqcup_{\tau \succeq \rho} O(\tau)$ exhibits $D_\rho$ itself as a toric variety with its own fan, generalising the divisor-level statement to a divisor-stratified picture.

- **Toric Picard group [04.11.09].** The companion sibling unit in this batch. The toric Picard exact sequence $0 \to M \to \bigoplus_\rho \mathbb{Z} D_\rho \to \mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma) \to 0$ is the abelian-group formulation of the Cartier-divisor calculus developed here. The support-function description in §A is the piecewise-linear-function avatar of the middle term, and the strict-convexity ampleness criterion in `[04.11.09]` §C uses the support function defined in §B above. The Cartier-vs-Weil refinement of §B is the input to the Picard-vs-class-group distinction in `[04.11.09]`.

- **Polytope-fan dictionary [04.11.10].** The downstream unit. The divisor polytope $P_D$ defined in §C is the polytope-side of the polytope-fan dictionary: a projective toric variety $X_\Sigma$ with an ample divisor $D$ gives a lattice polytope $P_D$, and the polytope-fan dictionary recovers $(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}(D))$ from $P_D$ as the projective toric variety of the polytope. The Demazure global-sections formula $H^0(X_P, L_P) = \bigoplus_{m \in P \cap M} \mathbb{C} \chi^m$ is the polytope-side reading of §C's global-sections formula.

- **Algebraic moment map and the polytope [04.11.11].** The downstream unit. The divisor polytope $P_D$ for an ample divisor $D$ on a Kähler toric manifold coincides with the image of the algebraic moment map $\mu : X_\Sigma \to M_\mathbb{R}$, providing the symplectic-side description of the support-function calculus. Delzant 1988 *Bull. SMF* 116 supplies the symplectic classification of compact toric manifolds by their Delzant polytopes, with the polytope-side data exactly the divisor polytope.

- **Algebraic torus and character/cocharacter lattices [04.11.01].** The lattice duality $M \leftrightarrow N$ underpinning the support function. The pairing $\langle m, v\rangle$ that defines $\psi_D(v_\rho) = -a_\rho$ on rays and the inequality $\langle m, v_\rho\rangle \geq -a_\rho$ defining $P_D$ uses the perfect pairing $M \times N \to \mathbb{Z}$ from `[04.11.01]`. The character map $m \mapsto \chi^m$ that identifies global sections with lattice points is the algebraic-torus character classification.

- **Smoothness and completeness via fans [04.11.05].** The smoothness criterion at the divisor level (§B) is the divisor-side counterpart of the smoothness criterion of `[04.11.05]`. Smoothness translates into unimodular cones, which translates into "every Weil is Cartier", which translates into $\mathrm{Pic} = \mathrm{Cl}$. The completeness criterion controls when the divisor polytope $P_D$ is bounded — bounded iff $D$ is nef and $X_\Sigma$ is complete.

- **Toric resolution of singularities [04.11.07].** The sibling unit. A toric resolution $f : X_{\Sigma'} \to X_\Sigma$ via star subdivision adds one exceptional divisor $D_\rho$ per new ray $\rho$, with the corresponding support-function values $\psi(u_\rho)$ encoding the discrepancy data along the resolution. The pullback $f^* D$ of a Cartier divisor $D$ on $X_\Sigma$ has support function $\psi_{f^* D} = \psi_D \circ \phi_\mathbb{R}$ for the lattice map $\phi$ of the refinement, an explicitly computable transform on piecewise-linear functions. The divisor language developed here is the bookkeeping framework for the resolution algorithm of `[04.11.07]`.

- **Ample and very ample line bundle [04.05.05].** The general Cartan-Serre-Grothendieck criterion for ampleness specialises on a smooth complete toric variety to the strict-convexity criterion on the support function $\psi_D$ (cross-reference `[04.11.09]` §C). The ample cone in $\mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma)_\mathbb{R}$ is the open polyhedral cone of strictly convex piecewise-linear functions modulo characters.

- **Toric cohomology and the Chow ring [04.11.12].** The downstream unit. The toric divisors $D_\rho$ form the degree-one generators of the Chow ring $A^*(X_\Sigma)$ of a smooth complete toric variety, with the Stanley-Reisner relations supplying the higher-degree structure and the toric Picard exact sequence supplying the linear relations. The global-sections formula via $P_D$ is the degree-zero piece of the toric Hirzebruch-Riemann-Roch theorem of `[04.11.13]`.

- **Cox-ring and GIT quotient [04.11.15].** The Cox ring $\mathbb{C}[x_\rho : \rho \in \Sigma(1)]$ is graded by the Picard group via the toric Picard exact sequence, and a monomial $\prod_\rho x_\rho^{a_\rho}$ corresponds to the toric divisor $\sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$. The polytope $P_D$ has the interpretation as the polytope of degree-$D$ monomials in the Cox ring, recovering the global-sections decomposition.

- **Picard group of a general variety [04.05.02].** Toric divisor theory is the sharp combinatorial case of general divisor theory, where the Cartier vs Weil distinction reduces to a unimodular-basis check at each cone, the support function classifies Cartier divisors, and the divisor polytope enumerates global sections. The general theory requires cohomological / Hodge-theoretic machinery; the toric case dispatches it with lattice arithmetic.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The toric divisor and support function formalism in its modern form is due to Michel Demazure in *Sous-groupes algébriques de rang maximum du groupe de Cremona* (Annales scientifiques de l'École normale supérieure (4) 3, 1970, pp. 507-588) [ref: TODO_REF Demazure 1970]. Demazure's paper established the bijection between $T$-invariant Cartier divisors on $X_\Sigma$ and integer-valued piecewise-linear functions on $|\Sigma|$, the smoothness criterion at the divisor level (every Weil is Cartier iff every cone is unimodular), the strict-convexity ampleness criterion, and the global-sections lattice-point formula. The term **"fonction d'appui"** ("support function") and the divisor-polytope construction $P_D$ are Demazure's coinages.

The Cartier-Weil refined formulation, distinguishing $\mathrm{Pic}$ from $\mathrm{Cl}$ on singular toric, was systematised in *Toroidal Embeddings I* by Kempf, Knudsen, Mumford, and Saint-Donat (Lecture Notes in Mathematics 339, Springer-Verlag 1973) [ref: TODO_REF], where the polytope-and-fan formalism was developed as an exposition of Demazure's results within the broader scheme-theoretic toroidal-embeddings framework. KKMS Chapter I §2 contains the first systematic treatment of $T$-invariant Cartier divisors and their support functions on singular toric. The work was motivated by Mumford's earlier *An analytic construction of degenerating Abelian varieties over complete rings* (Compositio Mathematica 24, 1972, pp. 239-272) [ref: TODO_REF Mumford 1972], where cone-and-lattice data was used to construct degenerations of abelian varieties, with the support function emerging as the natural object recording the Cartier-divisor structure of the degenerate fibre.

Tadao Oda's *Convex Bodies and Algebraic Geometry* (Ergebnisse der Mathematik 15, Springer-Verlag 1988) [ref: TODO_REF] is the canonical mid-1980s monograph, with §2.1-§2.3 the standard reference for the toric divisor and support function correspondence, the Cartier-Weil distinction, and the ample-line-bundle / strict-convexity dictionary. Oda's exposition emphasises the convex-body language and was the main pre-Fulton reference. Vasily Danilov's earlier 1978 survey *The geometry of toric varieties* (Russian Mathematical Surveys 33(2), 97-154) [ref: TODO_REF Danilov 1978] gave the first English-language treatment of the toric divisor theory and developed the support-function description in connection with the Stanley-Reisner perspective on the Chow ring.

William Fulton's *Introduction to Toric Varieties* (Princeton University Press 1993) [ref: TODO_REF Fulton 1993] §3.3-§3.4 is the canonical short textbook reference for the support-function correspondence, the smoothness criterion at the divisor level, and the global-sections lattice-point formula. Fulton's exposition is the standard pedagogical anchor for the material of this unit, with §3.3 introducing $T$-invariant Weil divisors and §3.4 developing the Cartier formulation via support functions. David Cox, John Little, and Henry Schenck's *Toric Varieties* (American Mathematical Society 2011) [ref: TODO_REF] is the modern thousand-page treatment, extending Fulton's exposition with extensive material on the divisor polytope, the global-sections formula (Theorem 4.3.3), and the strict-convexity ampleness criterion (Theorem 6.1.14).

Michel Brion's *Groupe de Picard et nombres caractéristiques des variétés sphériques* (Duke Mathematical Journal 58(2), 1989, pp. 397-424) [ref: TODO_REF Brion 1989] extended the toric divisor-and-support-function calculus to the broader class of spherical varieties, with the toric case appearing as the rank-zero special case. The Brion paper supplies the lattice-point cohomology formula in the spherical setting and unifies the toric divisor theory with the moment-map perspective of Atiyah 1982 and Guillemin-Sternberg 1982.

Michel Brion and Michèle Vergne's *Residue formulae, vector partition functions and lattice points in rational polytopes* (Journal of the American Mathematical Society 10(4), 1997, pp. 797-833) [ref: TODO_REF Brion-Vergne 1997] supplied the equivariant residue formula extending the Demazure global-sections formula to higher cohomology and arbitrary toric divisors. The Brion-Vergne theorem realises Hirzebruch-Riemann-Roch on a smooth complete toric variety as an explicit polynomial identity in Ehrhart polynomials of polytopes — an algorithmic-cohomology formula that has been the foundation for modern toric computational algorithms in Macaulay2, Polymake, and SageMath.

Miles Reid's *Decomposition of toric morphisms* (in *Arithmetic and Geometry* II, Progress in Mathematics 36, Birkhäuser 1983, pp. 395-418) [ref: TODO_REF] established piecewise-linear functions on a fan as the universal Cartier-divisor object in the toric Minimal Model Program. Reid's analysis used the support-function description to control extremal contractions and small modifications, anticipating the general MMP under Kawamata, Kollár, Mori, Reid, and Shokurov by several years. The toric MMP is the proof-of-concept algorithmic version, and the support-function calculus of this unit is its combinatorial backbone.

David Cox's 1995 paper *The homogeneous coordinate ring of a toric variety* (Journal of Algebraic Geometry 4, 17-50) [ref: TODO_REF Cox 1995] reframed toric divisors as monomial classes in the Cox ring $\mathbb{C}[x_\rho : \rho \in \Sigma(1)]$ graded by $\mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma)$, with the divisor polytope $P_D$ becoming the polytope of degree-$D$ monomials. The Cox-ring perspective unifies divisor theory, line-bundle theory, and the GIT-quotient construction $X_\Sigma = (\mathbb{C}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma)) / G$ under a single functorial framework and has been the foundation for modern computational toric algorithms.

Anvar Mavlyutov's *Semiample hypersurfaces in toric varieties* (Compositio Mathematica 124(1), 2000, pp. 7-24) [ref: TODO_REF] refined the Demazure vanishing theorem and the support-function description for singular toric varieties, extending the smooth-case results to the $\mathbb{Q}$-factorial and simplicial settings. Mavlyutov's work is the canonical reference for the Cartier-multiplier analysis on singular toric and supplies the higher-cohomology refinements of the Demazure global-sections formula.

Friedrich Hirzebruch's introduction of the surfaces $F_a$ in *Über eine Klasse von einfach-zusammenhängenden komplexen Mannigfaltigkeiten* (Mathematische Annalen 124, 1951, pp. 77-86) [ref: TODO_REF Hirzebruch 1951] predates the toric formalism by two decades but supplied the prototype example of a smooth complete toric variety with a non-toric Cartier divisor (the negative section $S$ of self-intersection $-a$). The Hirzebruch surfaces remain the standard pedagogical examples of Cartier divisors beyond $\mathbb{P}^n$, with the support function $\psi_S$ on the four-ray fan recovering the negative section's geometry combinatorially.
