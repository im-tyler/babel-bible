## Intuition [Beginner]

A lattice polytope $P$ is a convex shape in $\mathbb{R}^n$ whose corner points lie on the integer grid $\mathbb{Z}^n$. The polytope-fan dictionary says that every such polytope encodes a projective algebraic variety together with an ample line bundle, in a way that is fully reversible: any projective toric variety with a chosen ample line bundle corresponds to a unique lattice polytope. The polytope is the visible combinatorial fingerprint of the algebraic geometry hiding inside.

The recipe in one sentence is this: rotate the polytope so that each vertex points outward, and read off the inward-normal cones at the vertices. The collection of these inward-normal cones, together with all their faces, is a fan in the dual lattice. The toric variety of that fan is the projective variety associated to the polytope, and the line bundle is determined by recording how the polytope sits inside the dual space. The global sections of the line bundle are counted by the lattice points of the polytope itself — a striking identity that turns sheaf cohomology into integer-grid counting.

A concrete example makes this vivid. The unit simplex with vertices at the origin and the standard basis vectors of $\mathbb{R}^n$ produces $\mathbb{P}^n$ together with the line bundle $\mathcal{O}(1)$. The $n + 1$ lattice points of the simplex are exactly the coordinate basis of $H^0(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathcal{O}(1))$.

The unit square produces $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ with the diagonal line bundle $\mathcal{O}(1, 1)$, and its four corner lattice points are the four bilinear basis elements of that line bundle's sections. Batyrev's 1994 application of the dictionary to reflexive polytopes — those whose polar dual is also a lattice polytope — opens the door to mirror symmetry, with pairs $(P, P^\circ)$ of reflexive polytopes corresponding to mirror pairs of Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Build the projective plane $\mathbb{P}^2$ as the toric variety of the unit simplex. Take $P = \mathrm{conv}((0, 0), (1, 0), (0, 1)) \subseteq M_\mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}^2$, the standard unit triangle with vertices $v_0 = (0, 0)$, $v_1 = (1, 0)$, $v_2 = (0, 1)$.

Step 1. The lattice points. The lattice points of $P$ are exactly the three vertices: $P \cap M = \{(0, 0), (1, 0), (0, 1)\}$, a total of three points. The interior of $P$ contains no lattice points (it is the open triangle of area $1/2$), and the edges contain only the endpoints.

Step 2. The normal fan. At each vertex $v_i$, compute the cone of inward-pointing directions. At $v_0 = (0, 0)$: the inward directions are the first quadrant in $N_\mathbb{R}$, the cone $\sigma_0 = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_2)$.

At $v_1 = (1, 0)$: the inward directions form the wedge pointing back into the triangle from $v_1$'s perspective, the cone $\sigma_1 = \mathrm{Cone}(-e_1, -e_1 - e_2)$. By symmetry at $v_2 = (0, 1)$: $\sigma_2 = \mathrm{Cone}(-e_2, -e_1 - e_2)$. The three maximal cones together with their faces (three rays, the zero cone) form the normal fan $\Sigma_P$.

Step 3. The toric variety. The normal fan $\Sigma_P$ has three maximal cones in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and three rays in the directions $u_0 = e_1 + e_2$ (after taking primitive generators correctly), and the standard basis vectors. Comparing to the fan of $\mathbb{P}^2$ from `[04.11.04]` (three rays at $e_1, e_2, -e_1 - e_2$), one recovers $X_P = \mathbb{P}^2$ after the appropriate lattice identification. The line bundle $L_P$ is the ample line bundle $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}(1)$ — the hyperplane bundle.

What this tells us. The three lattice points of $P$ are exactly the three coordinate basis elements $\{X_0, X_1, X_2\}$ of $H^0(\mathbb{P}^2, \mathcal{O}(1))$ — a dimension of $3$, matching the lattice-point count. The Demazure character formula in action: counting lattice points of a polytope and computing global sections of a line bundle on the associated toric variety give the same answer. This pattern works in every dimension and for every lattice polytope, turning the algebraic-geometric question "how many sections does this ample line bundle have?" into the combinatorial question "how many integer points does this polytope contain?"

**Exercise (easy, multiple choice).**

The polytope-fan dictionary sends a lattice polytope $P \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ to:

A. An affine variety $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[P \cap M]$ without any line bundle.
B. A projective toric variety $X_P$ together with an ample line bundle $L_P$.

The construction produces both a variety and a polarisation simultaneously: the variety from the normal fan, and the line bundle from how the polytope sits in $M_\mathbb{R}$.

**B.** The polytope-fan dictionary produces a projective toric variety $X_P = X_{\Sigma_P}$ (where $\Sigma_P$ is the normal fan of $P$) together with an ample line bundle $L_P$ on $X_P$. The polytope encodes both pieces of data simultaneously — the fan from the inward-normal cones at vertices, and the line bundle from the location of $P$ inside $M_\mathbb{R}$. Option A is what an *affine* semigroup algebra construction would produce, missing the projective and polarisation content. Option C ignores the fact that $X_P$ is genuinely algebraic. Option D confuses the polytope-fan dictionary with the action of $T$ on $X_P$.

</details>

**Exercise (easy, true-false).**

True or false: the projective toric variety $X_P$ depends only on the normal fan $\Sigma_P$ of the polytope, but the line bundle $L_P$ depends on the polytope $P$ itself, not just on its normal fan.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Many different polytopes can share the same normal fan (think of dilations or translations). Such polytopes share $X_P$ but differ in their polarisations.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.** The normal fan $\Sigma_P$ depends only on the directions of the facets of $P$ (the inward normals), and these directions are unchanged by scaling or translating $P$. The variety $X_P = X_{\Sigma_P}$ therefore depends only on the shape (combinatorial type) of $P$, not on its size or position. The line bundle $L_P$ does record the size: the dilation $kP$ produces the $k$-th tensor power of $L_P$, a different line bundle on the same toric variety. The lattice-point count of $kP$ is the Ehrhart polynomial of $P$ evaluated at $k$, growing polynomially in $k$ with leading coefficient the volume of $P$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $N$ be a lattice of rank $n$ with dual lattice $M = \mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(N, \mathbb{Z})$, as in `[04.11.01]`. Let $M_\mathbb{R} = M \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{R}$ and $N_\mathbb{R} = N \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{R}$.

**Definition (lattice polytope).** A **lattice polytope** $P \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ is the convex hull of a finite subset of $M$. The polytope is **full-dimensional** if its affine span equals $M_\mathbb{R}$. A face of $P$ is the intersection of $P$ with a supporting hyperplane; faces of dimension $0$ are **vertices**, of dimension $1$ are **edges**, of dimension $n - 1$ are **facets**.

**Definition (normal fan).** Given a full-dimensional lattice polytope $P \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$, the **normal fan** $\Sigma_P$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$ is constructed as follows. For each face $F \subseteq P$, the **inward-normal cone** of $P$ at $F$ is
$$
\sigma_F := \{u \in N_\mathbb{R} : \langle m - m_F, u\rangle \geq 0 \text{ for all } m \in P, m_F \in F\},
$$
the closed cone of inward-pointing directions at $F$. The collection $\Sigma_P = \{\sigma_F : F \text{ a face of } P\}$ is a complete fan in $N_\mathbb{R}$. Maximal cones $\sigma_v$ are indexed by vertices $v$ of $P$; rays $\sigma_F$ by facets $F$; the zero cone $\sigma_P = \{0\}$ by the full polytope $P$.

**Definition (polarised toric variety from $P$).** Given a full-dimensional lattice polytope $P$, the **polarised toric variety from $P$** is the pair $(X_P, L_P)$ where:

(i) $X_P = X_{\Sigma_P}$ is the toric variety of the normal fan, constructed by the fan-gluing of `[04.11.04]`.

(ii) $L_P$ is the **polarisation line bundle** on $X_P$, defined locally on each affine chart $U_{\sigma_v}$ by the section $\chi^v \in \mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma_v}]$, with transition $\chi^{v - v'}$ from $U_{\sigma_v}$ to $U_{\sigma_{v'}}$ on the overlap $U_{\sigma_v \cap \sigma_{v'}}$. The cocycle condition $\chi^{v - v'} \cdot \chi^{v' - v''} = \chi^{v - v''}$ holds tautologically.

**Definition (support function).** The line bundle $L_P$ is encoded equivalently by the **support function** $\psi_P : N_\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by
$$
\psi_P(u) := \min_{m \in P} \langle m, u\rangle = \min_{v \text{ vertex of } P} \langle v, u\rangle,
$$
the lower-supporting function of $P$. The function $\psi_P$ is piecewise linear on the cones of $\Sigma_P$: on each maximal cone $\sigma_v$, $\psi_P|_{\sigma_v}(u) = \langle v, u\rangle$. The line bundle $L_P$ is **strictly convex** (equivalently $\psi_P$ is **strictly convex** on the fan) iff different maximal cones give different linear functions, iff different vertices $v$ of $P$ produce different elements of $M$.

**Definition (Demazure character).** The **Demazure character formula** identifies the global sections of $L_P$:
$$
H^0(X_P, L_P) = \bigoplus_{m \in P \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m,
$$
with $T$-weight decomposition under the dense-torus action. In particular,
$$
\dim_\mathbb{C} H^0(X_P, L_P) = |P \cap M|.
$$

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"The polytope $P$ is recovered from the fan $\Sigma_P$ alone."* It is not. The polytope records both the shape (via the inward-normal directions, which determine $\Sigma_P$) and the size (via the actual lattice points of $P$, which determine the polarisation $L_P$). Different lattice polytopes with the same normal fan — for example $P$ and $kP$ — share $X_P$ but have distinct polarisations $L_P, L_P^{\otimes k}$. To recover $P$ from $(X_P, L_P)$ one needs both pieces of data: the fan recovers the variety, and the support function $\psi_P$ recovers the polytope.

- *"Every projective toric variety has a unique polarisation."* It does not. The Picard group $\mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma)$ of a smooth complete toric variety is the abelian group of line bundles, with finite rank equal to $|\Sigma(1)| - n$; the ample cone is a full-dimensional open cone in $\mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma) \otimes \mathbb{R}$. Each ample line bundle $L$ corresponds to a different polytope $P_L$, the Newton polytope. The polytope-fan dictionary applies to choices of polarisation, not to varieties alone.

- *"Lattice points of $P$ all give linearly independent sections."* They do, but only because $L_P$ is ample (and in particular the polytope is full-dimensional with strictly convex support function). For non-ample line bundles on a toric variety, the lattice-point count of the associated polytope can fail to compute $h^0$ of the line bundle — the relevant invariant is the lattice-point count of the **lower convex hull** of the relevant lattice points, not of the polytope itself. The Demazure character formula in the form stated requires ampleness of $L_P$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem of this unit is the **polytope-fan equivalence**: a bijection between full-dimensional lattice polytopes $P$ (up to lattice translation) and pairs $(X, L)$ of projective normal toric varieties with ample $T$-equivariant line bundles (up to isomorphism). The Demazure character formula provides the explicit identification of global sections with lattice points.

**Theorem (Polytope-fan correspondence; Demazure 1970, Fulton 1993 §3.4).** *Let $N$ be a free $\mathbb{Z}$-module of finite rank.*

*(a) **From polytope to projective toric.** Every full-dimensional lattice polytope $P \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ produces a pair $(X_P, L_P)$ consisting of a projective normal toric variety $X_P = X_{\Sigma_P}$ and an ample $T$-equivariant line bundle $L_P$ on $X_P$.*

*(b) **Demazure character formula.** The global sections decompose as*
$$
H^0(X_P, L_P) = \bigoplus_{m \in P \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m,
$$
*as a $T$-representation, where the right-hand side has $T$-weight $m$ on each summand. In particular $\dim H^0(X_P, L_P) = |P \cap M|$.*

*(c) **From projective toric to polytope.** Conversely, every pair $(X, L)$ of a projective normal toric variety with an ample $T$-equivariant line bundle is of the form $(X_P, L_P)$ for a unique lattice polytope $P$ up to lattice translation.*

*(d) **Functoriality.** The assignments $P \mapsto (X_P, L_P)$ and $(X, L) \mapsto P_L$ are functorial in lattice maps respecting cone structure and ample-bundle morphisms respectively, and they are mutually inverse, giving an equivalence of categories.*

**Proof.**

*(a) Construction of $(X_P, L_P)$.* The normal fan $\Sigma_P$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$ is a complete fan by the standard convex-geometric argument: every direction $u \in N_\mathbb{R}$ is the inward normal of $P$ at some face, hence lies in some $\sigma_F$, so $|\Sigma_P| = N_\mathbb{R}$. By the fan-to-toric construction of `[04.11.04]`, $X_P = X_{\Sigma_P}$ exists as a normal separated complete scheme of finite type over $\mathbb{C}$.

To construct $L_P$, work on each maximal cone $\sigma_v$ (for $v$ a vertex of $P$). On the affine chart $U_{\sigma_v} = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma_v}]$, define the local section $s_v = \chi^v \in \mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma_v}]$. (We have $v \in S_{\sigma_v}$ because $\sigma_v$ is the inward-normal cone at $v$, so $\langle v, u\rangle \geq \langle v, u\rangle$ by definition, and shifting by the appropriate translation, $v \in (\sigma_v)^\vee + v$. The full check uses that $P - v$ lies in $(\sigma_v)^\vee$ as a polytope of $M_\mathbb{R}$.)

On the overlap $U_{\sigma_v} \cap U_{\sigma_{v'}} = U_{\sigma_v \cap \sigma_{v'}}$, the transition $s_v / s_{v'} = \chi^{v - v'}$ is a regular invertible function. The element $v - v'$ lies in $S_{\sigma_v \cap \sigma_{v'}}^\pm := (\sigma_v \cap \sigma_{v'})^\vee \cap M \oplus -(\sigma_v \cap \sigma_{v'})^\vee \cap M$ — the lattice points in the linear span of the dual to the common face. The cocycle condition
$$
\chi^{v - v'} \cdot \chi^{v' - v''} = \chi^{(v - v') + (v' - v'')} = \chi^{v - v''}
$$
holds tautologically, so the local sections $\{s_v\}_v$ define a line bundle $L_P$ on $X_P$.

Ampleness of $L_P$ is the strict-convexity of the support function $\psi_P$. By construction $\psi_P|_{\sigma_v}(u) = \langle v, u\rangle$, and strict convexity says different maximal cones give different linear functions: $v \neq v' \Rightarrow \psi_P|_{\sigma_v} \neq \psi_P|_{\sigma_{v'}}$, equivalent to the polytope $P$ having distinct vertices. By the criterion for ampleness of toric line bundles (Cox-Little-Schenck Theorem 6.1.14; Fulton §3.4 Proposition), strict convexity of the support function is equivalent to $L_P$ being ample.

*(b) Demazure character formula.* The global sections of $L_P$ are functions on $X_P$ that, restricted to each affine chart $U_{\sigma_v}$, give regular sections of $L_P|_{U_{\sigma_v}}$. Since $L_P|_{U_{\sigma_v}}$ is the product line bundle on the affine chart (with local generator $s_v = \chi^v$), a section over $U_{\sigma_v}$ is a function $f_v \in \mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma_v}]$ — equivalently a polynomial in the characters $\chi^m$ for $m \in S_{\sigma_v}$.

A global section is a compatible system $\{f_v\}_{v}$: on each overlap, $f_v / s_v = f_{v'} / s_{v'}$ (since both equal the global section value), equivalently $f_v \cdot \chi^{-v} = f_{v'} \cdot \chi^{-v'}$. Writing $f_v = \sum_m a_{v, m} \chi^m$ and matching coefficients of $\chi^{m + (-v')}$ on each side, the compatibility condition forces $a_{v, m} = a_{v', m'}$ for $m - v = m' - v'$, i.e., $m, m'$ correspond to the same "absolute" lattice point. Setting $\widetilde{m} = m - v \in M$ (translated to be measured from the polytope vertex), a global section is parametrised by an assignment of coefficients $\widetilde{a}_{\widetilde{m}}$ for $\widetilde{m} \in M$, with the constraint that on each chart $\widetilde{m} + v \in S_{\sigma_v}$, i.e., $\widetilde{m} \in (S_{\sigma_v} - v) = P - v$ (translated).

For the global section to be regular on every chart, we need $\widetilde{m} + v \in S_{\sigma_v}$ for **every** maximal cone $\sigma_v$. Taking $\widetilde{m} = m$ (un-translating), this is $m \in (\sigma_v)^\vee + v$ for every $v$, equivalent to $\langle m - v, u\rangle \geq 0$ for $u \in \sigma_v$ — equivalent to $m \in P$ (since $P$ is the intersection of the half-spaces $\{m : \langle m - v_i, u_i\rangle \geq 0\}$ for the facet inward normals $u_i$ at the vertices $v_i$).

Hence the global sections are parametrised exactly by lattice points $m \in P \cap M$, with the section
$$
s_m|_{U_{\sigma_v}} = \chi^{m - v} \cdot s_v = \chi^{m - v} \cdot \chi^v = \chi^m,
$$
a $T$-semi-invariant of weight $m$. The $T$-weight decomposition is
$$
H^0(X_P, L_P) = \bigoplus_{m \in P \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot s_m, \qquad T \text{-weight}(s_m) = m,
$$
which is the Demazure character formula. The dimension is the cardinality $|P \cap M|$.

*(c) Converse: from projective toric to polytope.* Suppose $X$ is a projective normal toric variety with ample $T$-equivariant line bundle $L$. The global sections $H^0(X, L)$ form a finite-dimensional $T$-representation. Since $T$ acts on the open dense torus $T \subseteq X$ by translation and the action lifts to $L$ (assumed $T$-equivariant), the $T$-action on $H^0(X, L)$ decomposes the space into $T$-character spaces:
$$
H^0(X, L) = \bigoplus_{m \in M} H^0(X, L)_m,
$$
each $H^0(X, L)_m$ being the subspace of $T$-weight $m$. The set $\mathrm{supp}(L) := \{m \in M : H^0(X, L)_m \neq 0\}$ is finite.

Define $P_L := \mathrm{conv}(\mathrm{supp}(L)) \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$, the convex hull. By the theory of toric line bundles (Cox-Little-Schenck Theorem 6.2.1), the ample condition implies that $\mathrm{supp}(L) = P_L \cap M$ (every interior lattice point appears with multiplicity one) and that $\mathrm{supp}(L)$ is exactly the set of lattice points of $P_L$. Hence $P_L$ is a lattice polytope.

Moreover, the vertex cones of $P_L$ are exactly the maximal cones of the fan $\Sigma$ of $X$. To see this: the support function of $L$ on $\Sigma$ is the function $\psi_L(u) = \min_{m \in \mathrm{supp}(L)} \langle m, u\rangle$, the lower convex envelope of $\mathrm{supp}(L)$. Ampleness of $L$ is equivalent to strict convexity of $\psi_L$ on $\Sigma$ (Cox-Little-Schenck Theorem 6.1.14), and strict convexity says different maximal cones produce different linear functions $u \mapsto \langle v, u\rangle$. The vertices $v$ realising the minimum on each maximal cone $\sigma$ are exactly the vertices of $P_L$, and the inward-normal cone of $P_L$ at $v$ equals $\sigma$. Hence $\Sigma$ is the normal fan of $P_L$, and $X = X_{P_L}$, $L = L_{P_L}$.

Uniqueness of $P_L$ up to translation: any global section $s$ of $L$ that does not vanish on the open torus determines a translation of $P_L$ (different choices of nowhere-vanishing local generator on the torus shift $P_L$ by a lattice element of $M$). The polytope $P_L$ is well-defined modulo this lattice translation, and the line bundle is unchanged under translation.

*(d) Functoriality.* A morphism of lattice polytopes $\phi : (M, P) \to (M', P')$ (a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear map $M \to M'$ sending lattice points of $P$ into lattice points of $P'$) induces:

(i) A morphism of normal fans $\phi^* : (N', \Sigma_{P'}) \to (N, \Sigma_P)$ on the dual lattices, with $\phi^*$ the dual of $\phi$.

(ii) A toric morphism $X_{\phi^*} : X_{P'} \to X_P$ via the fan-functoriality of `[04.11.04]`.

(iii) An equivariant morphism of line bundles $X_{\phi^*}^* L_P \to L_{P'}$ (pulled-back line bundle equals the polarisation from the pulled-back polytope, by construction).

The functor laws (identity, composition) follow from those of the underlying lattice-map operations. The inverse functor $(X, L) \mapsto P_L$ is functorial in toric morphisms via the support-function pullback. The mutual inversion of these functors — up to lattice translation of the polytope — is the assertion (c) above, completing the equivalence of categories.

$\square$

**Bridge.** The polytope-fan correspondence builds toward `[04.11.05]` (orbit-cone correspondence — orbits of $X_P$ are stratified by faces of $P$) and `[04.11.08]` (toric divisors and support functions — the line bundle $L_P$ decomposes as $L_P = \sum_F a_F D_F$ over the facets $F$ of $P$ with toric divisors $D_F$), and the central insight is that the lattice polytope $P$ records **both the projective toric variety and a polarisation simultaneously**. The foundational reason this dual record is possible is that the normal-fan-construction is **shape-only**, depending only on the directions of facets, while the polarisation is **size-also**, depending on the actual lattice points of $P$. The bridge is the support function $\psi_P$: a single piecewise-linear convex function on $N_\mathbb{R}$ that encodes both the fan (via its domains of linearity) and the line bundle (via its slope on each domain).

Putting these together, the polytope-fan dictionary identifies the category of lattice polytopes (with translation-equivariant lattice morphisms) with the category of polarised projective toric varieties (with $T$-equivariant ample-bundle morphisms). This is exactly the algebraic-geometric counterpart of the Delzant correspondence in symplectic geometry — Delzant 1988 showed that simple smooth rational polytopes classify symplectic toric manifolds, and the projective-toric overlap of the two correspondences identifies polytopes with both algebraic and symplectic structures. The pattern appears again in `[04.11.11]` (algebraic moment map — the moment polytope is exactly $P$ for the compact-torus action on $X_P$), generalises in `[04.11.13]` (toric intersection theory — degrees and intersection numbers compute as lattice-volume invariants of $P$), and is dual to the orbit-stratification of $X_P$ by faces of $P$ — orbit closures of dimension $k$ are in bijection with faces of $P$ of dimension $k$. The bridge is the Brion-Vergne identification $\chi(X_P, L_P) = \sum_{m \in P \cap M} 1 = |P \cap M|$ via Ehrhart polynomiality.

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).**

Compute the number of lattice points of the dilation $kP$ of the unit simplex $P$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ for $k = 1, 2, 3, 4$. Find the polynomial $L(P, k) = |kP \cap M|$ as a function of $k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The dilation $kP$ is the triangle with vertices $(0, 0), (k, 0), (0, k)$. Count integer points by triangular-number formulas. The Ehrhart polynomial of the unit simplex in dimension $n$ is $L(P, k) = \binom{k + n}{n}$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$k = 1$.** $|P \cap M| = 3$ (the three vertices of the simplex).

**$k = 2$.** $2P$ is the triangle with vertices $(0, 0), (2, 0), (0, 2)$. Lattice points: $(0, 0), (1, 0), (2, 0), (0, 1), (1, 1), (0, 2)$, total $6$.

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

The polytope $P = \mathrm{conv}((0, 0), (1, 0), (1, 1), (0, 2)) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ (a trapezoid) has normal fan equal to the fan of the Hirzebruch surface $F_1$. Compute the line bundle $L_P$ in terms of the standard generators of $\mathrm{Pic}(F_1)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Hirzebruch surface $F_1 = \mathbb{P}(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1} \oplus \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(1))$ has Picard group $\mathrm{Pic}(F_1) = \mathbb{Z}^2$ generated by the fiber class $F$ and a section class $S$ (or equivalently $H$ and $E$ for hyperplane and exceptional). The line bundle $L_P$ is determined by the height differences of the polytope's vertices.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Normal fan.** The trapezoid has four facets: two vertical (along $m_1 = 0$ and $m_1 = 1$), one horizontal bottom ($m_2 = 0$), and one diagonal top (along $m_1 + m_2 = 2$, from $(1, 1)$ to $(0, 2)$). The inward normals are $e_1, -e_1, e_2$, and $-e_1 - e_2$ respectively. **Toric variety.** $X_P = F_1$, the first Hirzebruch surface.

The line bundle $L_P$ on $F_1$ corresponds to the toric divisor $D = a_1 D_{\rho_1} + a_2 D_{\rho_2} + a_3 D_{\rho_3} + a_4 D_{\rho_4}$, where $a_i = -\psi_P(u_i)$ are the negatives of the support-function values on the ray generators. Reading off the trapezoid:

- $\psi_P(e_1) = 0$ (since the inward-normal to $\{m_1 = 0\}$ from the vertex $(0, 0)$ gives the linear function $\langle (0, 0), e_1\rangle = 0$).

- $\psi_P(e_2) = 0$.
- $\psi_P(-e_1 - e_2) = -2$ (from the vertex $(0, 2)$: $\langle (0, 2), -e_1 - e_2\rangle = -2$).

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove that the support function $\psi_P : N_\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $\psi_P(u) = \min_{m \in P} \langle m, u\rangle$ is **strictly convex** on the normal fan $\Sigma_P$, in the sense that for any two adjacent maximal cones $\sigma_v, \sigma_{v'}$ with common facet $\tau = \sigma_v \cap \sigma_{v'}$, the linear functions $\psi_P|_{\sigma_v}$ and $\psi_P|_{\sigma_{v'}}$ disagree along the dimension transverse to $\tau$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On the maximal cone $\sigma_v$, $\psi_P(u) = \langle v, u\rangle$ (the minimum is attained at the vertex $v$). Strict convexity at the wall says the function bends upward across the wall, equivalently the vertex $v$ is genuinely different from $v'$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By construction, on the maximal cone $\sigma_v$ (the inward-normal cone at the vertex $v$), the support function $\psi_P|_{\sigma_v}$ is the linear function $u \mapsto \langle v, u\rangle$: for any $u \in \sigma_v$, the minimum $\min_{m \in P} \langle m, u\rangle$ is attained at $m = v$ (this is exactly the defining property of $\sigma_v$).

For two adjacent maximal cones $\sigma_v, \sigma_{v'}$ sharing a facet $\tau$, the cones $\sigma_v$ and $\sigma_{v'}$ correspond to two adjacent vertices $v, v'$ of $P$ (sharing an edge of $P$ — the edge being the face dual to the facet $\tau$). Since $v \neq v'$ as elements of $M$ (different vertices of a polytope are different lattice points), the linear functions $u \mapsto \langle v, u\rangle$ and $u \mapsto \langle v', u\rangle$ are different elements of $\mathrm{Hom}(N, \mathbb{R}) = M_\mathbb{R}$.

These two linear functions agree on the facet $\tau$ (the common boundary of $\sigma_v$ and $\sigma_{v'}$): for $u \in \tau$, both $v$ and $v'$ achieve the minimum $\langle m, u\rangle$ over $m \in P$ — because the facet $\tau$ is exactly the locus of $u$ for which the minimum is achieved at multiple vertices, namely both endpoints of the edge $vv'$ of $P$. Hence $\langle v, u\rangle = \langle v', u\rangle$ for $u \in \tau$, equivalent to $v - v' \in \tau^\perp = \mathrm{span}_\mathbb{R}(\tau)^\perp$. The vector $v - v'$ is non-zero (different vertices) and lies in $\tau^\perp$, so it represents the "jump direction" of the support function across $\tau$.

The strict convexity assertion is then:
$$
\psi_P(u_+ + u_-) < \psi_P|_{\sigma_v}(u_+ + u_-) + \psi_P|_{\sigma_{v'}}(u_+ + u_-)
$$
for $u_+ \in \sigma_v \setminus \tau$ and $u_- \in \sigma_{v'} \setminus \tau$ such that $u_+ + u_-$ is in some larger ambient region. The relevant statement is more directly:
$$
\psi_P(u) > \langle v, u\rangle \quad \text{for } u \in \sigma_{v'} \setminus \tau,
$$
which says the linear extension of $\psi_P|_{\sigma_v}$ to the neighbouring cone $\sigma_{v'}$ is strictly **smaller** than the actual value of $\psi_P$ there — equivalently, $\psi_P$ is *strictly upper-bounded* by its linear extension from $\sigma_v$ across the wall $\tau$. The strict upper-bound is equivalent to $\langle v', u\rangle < \langle v, u\rangle$ for $u \in \sigma_{v'} \setminus \tau$, which holds because $v$ achieves the minimum $\langle m, u\rangle = \langle v', u\rangle$ at $m = v'$ on $\sigma_{v'}$, so by definition $\langle v', u\rangle \leq \langle v, u\rangle$ with equality only on $\tau$.

Hence strict convexity holds at every wall $\tau$. This is exactly the ampleness criterion for $L_P$: by the toric-line-bundle ampleness criterion (Cox-Little-Schenck Theorem 6.1.14; Fulton §3.4), a $T$-equivariant line bundle on a complete normal toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is ample iff its support function is strictly convex on $\Sigma$, in exactly this sense. Therefore $L_P$ is ample. $\square$

Rubric: full credit for the identification of $\psi_P|_{\sigma_v}(u) = \langle v, u\rangle$, the agreement-on-wall analysis, the strict-inequality argument off the wall, and the linkage to ampleness of $L_P$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (medium, multiple choice).**

For a full-dimensional lattice polytope $P \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$, the Ehrhart polynomial $L(P, k) = |kP \cap M|$ has degree:

A. $0$ — it is constant.
B. $1$ — it is linear.
C. $n = \dim M_\mathbb{R}$ — it is a polynomial of degree equal to the dimension of $P$.
D. $|P \cap M| - 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The leading term of $L(P, k)$ is $\mathrm{vol}(P) \cdot k^n$, where $\mathrm{vol}(P)$ is the Euclidean volume of $P$. This forces the polynomial to have degree exactly $n$ for a full-dimensional polytope.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**C.** Ehrhart's theorem (Ehrhart 1962, *C. R. Acad. Sci.*) states that for a full-dimensional lattice polytope $P \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ of dimension $n$, the function $L(P, k) = |kP \cap M|$ extends from $k \in \mathbb{Z}_{> 0}$ to a polynomial in $k$ of degree exactly $n$, with:

- Leading coefficient $\mathrm{vol}(P)$ (the Euclidean volume of $P$).
- Second-highest coefficient $\mathrm{vol}_{n-1}(\partial P)/2$ (half the surface area).
- Constant term $L(P, 0) = 1$ (Ehrhart's constant-term theorem).

For the unit 2-simplex (Exercise 3), $L(P, k) = (k + 1)(k + 2)/2$ has degree $2 = \dim M_\mathbb{R}$ with leading coefficient $1/2 = \mathrm{vol}(\text{unit triangle})$. For the unit square, $L(P, k) = (k + 1)^2$ has degree $2$ with leading coefficient $1$. For the standard simplex in dimension $n$, $L(P, k) = \binom{k + n}{n}$ has degree $n$.

The algebraic-geometric interpretation: the Ehrhart polynomial is exactly the Hilbert polynomial of the projective toric variety $X_P$ with respect to the ample line bundle $L_P$:
$$
L(P, k) = \dim H^0(X_P, L_P^{\otimes k}),
$$
by the Demazure character formula applied to $kP$. The polynomial degree $n$ matches the dimension of $X_P$ (equal to $\dim M_\mathbb{R} = n$ for a full-dimensional polytope), and the leading coefficient $\mathrm{vol}(P)$ matches the degree of $X_P \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}^{|P \cap M| - 1}$ under the polarisation. Options A, B are wrong for any full-dimensional polytope; option D confuses the polynomial degree with the section dimension at $k = 1$.

</details>

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove the **Demazure character formula** $H^0(X_P, L_P) = \bigoplus_{m \in P \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m$ by working through the $T$-equivariant local-to-global computation on the fan of $X_P$. Explain why ampleness of $L_P$ is needed.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $H^0$ via the Čech complex on the open cover $\{U_{\sigma_v}\}$ for vertices $v$ of $P$. Each local section is a $T$-weight space; gluing conditions on overlaps cut out exactly the lattice-point condition $m \in P$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Setup.** Let $P$ be a full-dimensional lattice polytope with vertices $v_1, \ldots, v_N$ and normal fan $\Sigma_P$ with maximal cones $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_N$ (cone $\sigma_i$ is the inward-normal cone at vertex $v_i$). The toric variety $X_P = X_{\Sigma_P}$ has affine cover $\{U_{\sigma_i}\}_{i = 1}^N$, and the line bundle $L_P$ has local generator $s_i = \chi^{v_i}$ on $U_{\sigma_i}$, with transitions $s_i / s_j = \chi^{v_i - v_j}$ on the overlaps.

**Step 1: $T$-weight decomposition.** Each $H^0(U_{\sigma_i}, L_P|_{U_{\sigma_i}})$ is a free rank-one $\mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma_i}]$-module (with generator $s_i$), so as a $T$-representation:
$$
H^0(U_{\sigma_i}, L_P|_{U_{\sigma_i}}) = \bigoplus_{m \in S_{\sigma_i} + v_i} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m,
$$
where the shift by $v_i$ comes from the local generator $s_i = \chi^{v_i}$: a local section is $f \cdot s_i$ for $f \in \mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma_i}]$, with $T$-weight of $f \cdot s_i$ being $m_f + v_i$ for $f = \chi^{m_f}$. The set $S_{\sigma_i} + v_i$ is the lattice points of the dual cone shifted to be centred at $v_i$ — equivalently, the lattice points in the translated cone $(\sigma_i)^\vee + v_i$.

**Step 2: gluing.** A global section is a system $\{f_i\}_{i = 1}^N$ with $f_i s_i = f_j s_j$ on the overlap $U_{\sigma_i \cap \sigma_j}$, equivalently $f_i / f_j = s_j / s_i = \chi^{v_j - v_i}$, i.e., $f_i = f_j \cdot \chi^{v_j - v_i}$ on the overlap. Writing each $f_i \cdot s_i = \chi^{m_i}$ where $m_i = m_{f_i} + v_i$ is the "absolute" $T$-weight, the compatibility says: the global section restricted to overlap is $\chi^m$ for a single $m \in M$, with restriction to $U_{\sigma_i}$ being $\chi^m = \chi^{m - v_i} \cdot \chi^{v_i} = \chi^{m - v_i} \cdot s_i$. For this to be a regular section over $U_{\sigma_i}$, we need $m - v_i \in S_{\sigma_i}$, equivalently $m \in S_{\sigma_i} + v_i$.

For the global section to extend over **every** $U_{\sigma_i}$, we need $m \in S_{\sigma_i} + v_i$ for **every** $i$. The condition $m - v_i \in S_{\sigma_i}$ unpacks to $\langle m - v_i, u\rangle \geq 0$ for $u \in \sigma_i$, equivalent to $\langle m, u\rangle \geq \langle v_i, u\rangle$ for $u \in \sigma_i$. Since $\psi_P(u) = \langle v_i, u\rangle$ on $\sigma_i$ (the support function at vertex $i$), this is $\langle m, u\rangle \geq \psi_P(u)$ for all $u \in \sigma_i$.

Taking the intersection over all $i$, and using that $|\Sigma_P| = N_\mathbb{R}$ (the normal fan is complete), the condition becomes
$$
\langle m, u\rangle \geq \psi_P(u) = \min_{m' \in P} \langle m', u\rangle \quad \text{for all } u \in N_\mathbb{R}.
$$
This is exactly the **polytope membership condition**: $m \in P$. (Direction: if $m \in P$ then $\langle m, u\rangle \geq \min_{m' \in P} \langle m', u\rangle$ by definition; conversely if $m \notin P$, the hyperplane-separation theorem gives some $u_0 \in N_\mathbb{R}$ with $\langle m, u_0\rangle < \min_{m' \in P} \langle m', u_0\rangle = \psi_P(u_0)$, contradicting the inequality.)

**Step 3: lattice-point integrality.** Since $m \in M$ (a lattice point) and $m \in P$, we have $m \in P \cap M$. Each lattice point $m \in P \cap M$ produces a unique global section $\chi^m$ (with restriction $\chi^{m - v_i} \cdot s_i$ on $U_{\sigma_i}$), and the global section is a $T$-semi-invariant of weight $m$. The global sections are therefore in bijection with $P \cap M$:
$$
H^0(X_P, L_P) = \bigoplus_{m \in P \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m,
$$
as a $T$-representation. The dimension is $|P \cap M|$.

**Step 4: ampleness role.** Where did ampleness enter? Two places:

(i) For the construction of $L_P$ as a line bundle (with the locally given sections $s_v$), the cocycle condition is automatic — no ampleness needed.

(ii) For the converse (recovering $P$ from $(X, L)$), ampleness is essential: only ample line bundles produce the polytope $P_L$ via the support function. For a non-ample line bundle, the "polytope" $P_L = \mathrm{conv}(\mathrm{supp}(L))$ may not be a lattice polytope (its vertices may not be in $M$), or its normal fan may differ from $\Sigma$. The Demazure character formula extends to all $T$-equivariant line bundles in the form
$$
H^0(X_\Sigma, L) = \bigoplus_{m \in P_L \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m
$$
where $P_L = \mathrm{conv}(\mathrm{supp}(L))$, but the cleanest statement (with $L = L_P$ and the polytope $P$ given a priori) requires ampleness so that $P$ is the actual polytope and not its convex hull of a subset.

The Demazure character formula for ample $L_P$ is therefore the precise statement of Step 3, and the converse direction of the polytope-fan equivalence depends on ampleness for the polytope to be reconstructible. $\square$

## Advanced results [Master]

### The polytope-fan dictionary as a categorical equivalence

**Theorem (categorical equivalence of polytopes and polarised toric varieties).** *The functor $P \mapsto (X_P, L_P)$ from the category $\mathbf{LatPoly}$ of full-dimensional lattice polytopes (with translation-equivariant lattice morphisms preserving polytope inclusion) to the category $\mathbf{ProjTor}$ of polarised projective normal toric varieties (with $T$-equivariant ample-bundle morphisms) is an equivalence of categories, with quasi-inverse $(X, L) \mapsto P_L = \mathrm{conv}(\mathrm{supp}(L))$.*

The equivalence packages the dictionary at full categorical strength. Morphisms of polytopes correspond exactly to morphisms of polarised toric varieties, with the bijection induced by lattice-and-support-function pullback. Three substructures crystallise:

(i) Two polytopes related by lattice translation give isomorphic polarised toric varieties — this is the $\mathrm{Pic}$-vanishing gauge, with translations acting by an identity isomorphism on $(X_P, L_P)$ since shifting all local generators $s_v \mapsto \chi^{v + t} = \chi^t \cdot s_v$ is a global section of $L_P$ and produces an isomorphic line bundle.

(ii) Two polytopes with the same normal fan (e.g., $P$ and $P'$ with the same vertex inward-normal cones but different vertex positions) give the same toric variety $X_P = X_{P'}$ but different polarisations $L_P, L_{P'}$. The space of such polarisations is exactly $\mathrm{Pic}(X_P) \otimes \mathbb{R}$, with the ample cone an open subcone parametrising the lattice polytopes $P$ with normal fan $\Sigma$.

(iii) Functorial pullback: a lattice map $\phi : N' \to N$ sending the fan $\Sigma'$ into a refinement of $\Sigma$, and inducing a polytope map $\phi^\vee : P_L \to P_{L'}$ on the dual side, gives a toric morphism $X_{\phi} : X_{\Sigma'} \to X_\Sigma$ with $\phi^*(L) = L'$, exactly the toric-functorial picture.

The categorical equivalence is the toric-geometric encoding of the Mukai-style dualities between varieties and their polarisations: $X$ alone determines a moduli space of polarisations (a finite-dimensional ample cone), and $(X, L)$ is the universal pair recovering both. For toric varieties, this moduli is finite-dimensional and combinatorial — exactly the polytopes with given normal fan.

### The Demazure character formula and lattice-point sections

**Theorem (Demazure character formula).** *Let $X_P$ be the projective toric variety of a full-dimensional lattice polytope $P \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ with ample $T$-equivariant line bundle $L_P$. Then as a $T$-representation,*
$$
H^0(X_P, L_P) = \bigoplus_{m \in P \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m,
$$
*with $T$-weight $m$ appearing with multiplicity one. Higher cohomology vanishes: $H^i(X_P, L_P) = 0$ for $i \geq 1$.*

The Demazure character formula is the structural heart of the dictionary. It identifies sheaf cohomology — an a priori algebraic-geometric invariant — with lattice-point counting — a combinatorial datum. The proof, sketched in Exercise 8, reduces global-section computation to compatibility on the affine cover; the Kodaira-Demazure vanishing $H^i(X_P, L_P) = 0$ for $i \geq 1$ uses the strict convexity of $\psi_P$ together with the toric Kodaira theorem (Demazure 1970; Mustață 2002 *Tohoku Math. J.* 54 for the algebraic-geometric proof via Grothendieck-vanishing on toric varieties).

A central application is **Ehrhart polynomiality**. For $k \geq 1$, the dilation $kP$ gives the tensor power $L_P^{\otimes k}$:
$$
\dim H^0(X_P, L_P^{\otimes k}) = |kP \cap M| =: L(P, k),
$$
the Ehrhart polynomial. By Ehrhart's theorem (Ehrhart 1962, *C. R. Acad. Sci.* 254), $L(P, k)$ is a polynomial in $k$ of degree $n = \dim P$ with leading coefficient $\mathrm{vol}(P)$:
$$
L(P, k) = \mathrm{vol}(P) k^n + \frac{1}{2} \mathrm{vol}_{n-1}(\partial P) k^{n-1} + \cdots + 1.
$$
Each coefficient has a combinatorial interpretation via the Brion-Vergne residue formula (Brion 1996 *Banach Center Publ.* 36; Brion-Vergne 1997 *J. Amer. Math. Soc.* 10). The Ehrhart polynomial is the toric incarnation of the Hilbert polynomial, and Ehrhart polynomiality is the toric incarnation of Hilbert's syzygy theorem applied to toric coordinate rings.

The Demazure-Brion lattice-point formula extends to general $T$-equivariant line bundles via the **Brion-Vergne lattice-point formula**: for any toric Weil divisor $D = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$,
$$
\chi(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}_{X_\Sigma}(D)) = \sum_{m \in M} (-1)^{?(m)} \mathbf{1}_{P_D}(m),
$$
where $P_D = \{m \in M_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, u_\rho\rangle \geq -a_\rho\}$ is the polytope of $D$ and $?(m)$ records the "face level" at $m$. For ample $D$ this reduces to the lattice-point count of $P_D$; for non-ample $D$, signed contributions from boundary faces enter.

### Worked examples — simplex / square / cube and their toric varieties

**Example (standard $n$-simplex gives $\mathbb{P}^n$).** Let $P_n = \mathrm{conv}(0, e_1, \ldots, e_n) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$. The normal fan of $P_n$ has $n + 1$ maximal cones, indexed by vertices, with ray generators $\{e_1, \ldots, e_n, -e_1 - \cdots - e_n\}$. Comparing with the fan of $\mathbb{P}^n$ from `[04.11.04]`, we recover $X_{P_n} = \mathbb{P}^n$ and $L_{P_n} = \mathcal{O}(1)$. The lattice-point count is $|P_n \cap M| = n + 1$, matching $\dim H^0(\mathbb{P}^n, \mathcal{O}(1)) = n + 1$. The Ehrhart polynomial is $L(P_n, k) = \binom{k + n}{n}$, matching the Hilbert polynomial of $\mathbb{P}^n$.

**Example (unit cube gives $(\mathbb{P}^1)^n$).** Let $P = [0, 1]^n \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, the unit cube. The normal fan has $2^n$ maximal cones (the $2^n$ orthants of $\mathbb{R}^n$) with ray generators $\{\pm e_1, \ldots, \pm e_n\}$. This is the fan of the product $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \cdots \times \mathbb{P}^1$ ($n$ factors). The line bundle is $L_P = \mathcal{O}(1, 1, \ldots, 1)$, and $|P \cap M| = 2^n$ (the corners only), matching $\dim H^0((\mathbb{P}^1)^n, \mathcal{O}(1)^{\boxtimes n}) = 2^n$ by the Künneth formula. The Ehrhart polynomial is $L(P, k) = (k + 1)^n$.

**Example (cross-polytope gives a singular toric variety).** Let $P = \mathrm{conv}(\pm e_1, \ldots, \pm e_n) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, the cross-polytope (dual of the cube). The normal fan has $2^n$ maximal cones spanned by sign-patterns of standard basis vectors. For $n = 2$, the fan has four maximal cones with ray generators $\pm e_1, \pm e_2$, which is the fan of $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ — same as the unit square! This reflects that **the cross-polytope and the unit square have the same normal fan** in dimension 2 (both have facet normals $\pm e_1, \pm e_2$). For $n = 3$, the fan of the cross-polytope is more complex and corresponds to a singular toric Calabi-Yau threefold (the prototype of the conifold transition). In general, dimensions $\geq 3$ where the cross-polytope has non-simplicial vertices give singular toric varieties whose smooth resolution is a fan refinement.

**Example (lattice triangles in $\mathbb{R}^2$ classify toric surfaces with $\mathrm{Pic} = \mathbb{Z}$).** The smooth complete toric surfaces with Picard number 1 are exactly $\mathbb{P}^2$ and the weighted projective planes $\mathbb{P}(a, b, c)$ for $(a, b, c)$ coprime. They correspond to lattice triangles in $\mathbb{R}^2$ with vertices at $(0, 0), (a, 0), (0, b)$ (rescaled appropriately). The standard simplex gives $\mathbb{P}^2$; the triangle with vertices $(0, 0), (2, 0), (0, 1)$ gives the weighted projective plane $\mathbb{P}(1, 1, 2)$ (a quotient of $\mathbb{P}^2$ by a $\mathbb{Z}/2$-action). The lattice-point counts and Ehrhart polynomials encode the Hilbert series of these weighted projective spaces.

### Mirror symmetry connection via reflexive polytopes (Batyrev 1994, pointer)

**Theorem (Batyrev mirror duality).** *A full-dimensional lattice polytope $P \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ containing the origin in its interior is called **reflexive** if the polar dual $P^\circ \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ (defined by $P^\circ = \{u \in N_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, u\rangle \geq -1 \text{ for all } m \in P\}$) is also a lattice polytope. The polar operation is an involution: $(P^\circ)^\circ = P$. For each pair $(P, P^\circ)$ of reflexive polytopes, the generic anticanonical hypersurfaces in $X_P$ and $X_{P^\circ}$ are Calabi-Yau and form a mirror-symmetric pair under Batyrev mirror duality (Batyrev 1994, *J. Algebraic Geom.* 3).*

Reflexive polytopes were introduced by Victor Batyrev in 1994 (*J. Algebraic Geometry* 3, 493-535) as the combinatorial structure underlying mirror symmetry for Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces in toric varieties. The key insight: a lattice polytope $P$ with the origin as unique interior lattice point produces a toric variety $X_P$ whose anticanonical class $-K_{X_P} = L_P$ defines a Calabi-Yau hypersurface. Under polar duality, $P \leftrightarrow P^\circ$ swaps the role of $M$ and $N$; the toric varieties $X_P$ and $X_{P^\circ}$ are typically singular, but their crepant resolutions $\widehat{X_P}$ and $\widehat{X_{P^\circ}}$ host smooth Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces $\widehat{V} \subseteq \widehat{X_P}$ and $\widehat{V}^\vee \subseteq \widehat{X_{P^\circ}}$ that exchange Hodge numbers: $h^{p, q}(\widehat{V}) = h^{n - p, q}(\widehat{V}^\vee)$.

For $n = 3$ (Calabi-Yau threefolds), there are exactly **473,800,776** reflexive 4-polytopes (classified by Kreuzer-Skarke 2000-2002 *Adv. Theor. Math. Phys.* 4, 1209-1230 and *Comm. Math. Phys.* 230, 333-353), and each produces a Calabi-Yau threefold of explicit Hodge numbers. The corresponding mirror pairs are the bulk of explicitly known mirror-symmetric Calabi-Yau threefolds in the algebro-geometric literature — they include the famous quintic threefold and its mirror, as well as the conifold and many K3-fibred examples. The Batyrev construction was the first systematic combinatorial source of mirror Calabi-Yau pairs, and remains the canonical construction for hypersurfaces.

The Gross-Siebert reconstruction program (Gross-Siebert 2011, *Annals of Math.* 174) generalises Batyrev's construction beyond the toric-hypersurface case to arbitrary Calabi-Yau manifolds with maximal-degeneration limits, using tropical-geometric scattering diagrams on the dual intersection complex. The Strominger-Yau-Zaslow conjecture (Strominger-Yau-Zaslow 1996 *Nucl. Phys. B* 479) provides the heuristic underpinning: mirror Calabi-Yau pairs are dual special-Lagrangian torus fibrations over a common base. For toric Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces, the base is the polytope $P$ (or $P^\circ$), with the SYZ duality coinciding with Batyrev polar duality.

**Theorem (anticanonical bundle on $X_P$).** *For a smooth complete toric variety $X_\Sigma$, the anticanonical line bundle is*
$$
-K_{X_\Sigma} = \sum_{\rho \in \Sigma(1)} D_\rho,
$$
*the sum of all toric divisors. When $\Sigma = \Sigma_P$ is the normal fan of a reflexive polytope, $-K_{X_P} = L_P$ is the polarisation line bundle, and the generic anticanonical hypersurface (defined by a generic global section of $-K_{X_P}$) is Calabi-Yau.*

The identity $-K_{X_P} = L_P$ for reflexive $P$ is the defining property of reflexive polytopes: it is exactly the condition that the polytope $P_{-K}$ associated to the anticanonical class coincides with $P$ itself. For non-reflexive lattice polytopes, $-K_{X_P}$ is still a Weil divisor on $X_P$, but it need not equal $L_P$, and the anticanonical hypersurface is not Calabi-Yau in general. The reflexive condition encodes the precise combinatorial data needed for the toric variety's anticanonical sections to define Calabi-Yau geometry.

**Theorem (Batyrev-Borisov for complete intersections).** *Batyrev and Borisov 1996 (*Higher-dimensional complex varieties (Trento 1994)*) extended Batyrev's construction to complete intersections of $k$ hypersurfaces in $X_P$ for nef-partitioned reflexive polytopes $P = \nabla_1 + \cdots + \nabla_k$. Each nef-partition produces a Calabi-Yau complete intersection in $X_P$, with mirror under a generalised polar duality involving the partition.*

The Batyrev-Borisov construction is the gold standard for explicit construction of mirror pairs of Calabi-Yau complete intersections, and produces all known examples of mirror symmetric Calabi-Yau 3-folds with positive Hodge numbers up to large $h^{1, 1} + h^{1, 2}$.

**Synthesis.** The polytope-fan dictionary identifies lattice polytopes with polarised projective toric varieties, and the central insight is that **a single combinatorial object — the polytope $P$ — encodes both the variety $X_P$ and the polarisation $L_P$ simultaneously**. The foundational reason this dual encoding is possible is the **support function** $\psi_P$: a piecewise-linear convex function on $N_\mathbb{R}$ whose domains of linearity recover the fan and whose slopes recover the line bundle. The Demazure character formula then identifies global sections of $L_P$ with lattice points of $P$, turning sheaf cohomology into integer-grid counting and embedding projective toric varieties into the canonical projective space $\mathbb{P}^{|P \cap M| - 1}$ via the lattice-point basis.

Putting these together, the polytope-fan dictionary equips toric algebraic geometry with three concentric layers of combinatorial encoding: (i) the **fan** $\Sigma_P$ records the toric variety; (ii) the **polytope** $P$ records additionally the polarisation; (iii) the **reflexive polytope** $P$ (when interior origin and integer polar) records Calabi-Yau anticanonical structure. The bridge is the polar-duality involution $P \leftrightarrow P^\circ$, which generalises the duality $M \leftrightarrow N$ from `[04.11.01]` to the polarised level: in `[04.11.01]` the two lattices are mutually dual, in `[04.11.02]` cones and dual cones are mutually polar, in `[04.11.10]` lattice polytopes and their polars are mutually mirror.

This pattern recurs throughout modern toric geometry. The Brion-Vergne formula identifies sheaf-Euler characteristics with lattice integrals over polytopes. The Ehrhart-reciprocity theorem identifies $L(P, -k)$ with $(-1)^n L(P^\circ, k)$ for reflexive $P$, generalising both Riemann-Roch on $X_P$ and Serre duality on the anticanonical hypersurface. The Gross-Siebert mirror reconstruction generalises Batyrev's construction to arbitrary Calabi-Yau pairs with toric-degeneration limits. The Mukai-style transformations exchange varieties and their derived categories in ways that respect polytope polar duality. The polytope-fan dictionary is the foundational lens through which all of these phenomena appear: every toric-geometric or mirror-symmetric structure is, at root, a structure on lattice polytopes.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (construction of $(X_P, L_P)$), proof.** Given in (a) of the main theorem: the normal fan $\Sigma_P$ is complete by convex-geometric construction (every direction is the inward normal of some face of $P$), so $X_P = X_{\Sigma_P}$ is a complete projective normal toric variety by the fan-to-toric construction of `[04.11.04]`. The line bundle $L_P$ is defined locally by $s_v = \chi^v$ on $U_{\sigma_v}$, with transitions $s_v / s_{v'} = \chi^{v - v'}$ satisfying the tautological cocycle $\chi^{v - v'} \cdot \chi^{v' - v''} = \chi^{v - v''}$. Ampleness of $L_P$ is the strict convexity of $\psi_P$ on $\Sigma_P$, equivalent to distinct vertices of $P$ giving distinct linear functions $\langle v, \cdot\rangle$ on the cones $\sigma_v$, which holds by definition of "vertex". $\square$

**Proposition (Demazure character formula), proof.** Given in Exercise 8: a global section of $L_P$ is a compatible system of local sections $f_v s_v$ on $U_{\sigma_v}$, with compatibility on overlaps forcing $f_v \cdot \chi^v = \chi^m$ for some $m \in M$. The regularity condition $m - v \in S_{\sigma_v}$ on each chart, taken simultaneously over all maximal cones, reduces to the polytope-membership condition $m \in P \cap M$. Each $m \in P \cap M$ gives a unique section with $T$-weight $m$, yielding $H^0(X_P, L_P) = \bigoplus_{m \in P \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m$. Vanishing of higher cohomology $H^i(X_P, L_P) = 0$ for $i \geq 1$ follows from the Demazure-Kodaira vanishing theorem for ample $L$ on a complete toric variety (Demazure 1970; Mustață 2002 *Tohoku Math. J.* 54). $\square$

**Proposition (converse: $(X, L) \mapsto P_L$), proof sketch.** Given a projective normal toric variety $X$ with ample $T$-equivariant line bundle $L$, decompose $H^0(X, L) = \bigoplus_{m \in M} H^0(X, L)_m$ into $T$-weight spaces. The support set $\mathrm{supp}(L) := \{m \in M : H^0(X, L)_m \neq 0\}$ is finite. Define $P_L = \mathrm{conv}(\mathrm{supp}(L)) \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$.

For ample $L$, the support function $\psi_L : N_\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $\psi_L(u) = \min_{m \in \mathrm{supp}(L)} \langle m, u\rangle$ is strictly convex on the fan $\Sigma$ of $X$ (Cox-Little-Schenck Theorem 6.1.14). Strict convexity says each maximal cone $\sigma$ of $\Sigma$ has a unique vertex of $\mathrm{supp}(L)$ achieving the minimum: there is a unique $v_\sigma \in \mathrm{supp}(L)$ with $\langle v_\sigma, u\rangle < \langle v', u\rangle$ for $u$ in the interior of $\sigma$ and any other $v' \in \mathrm{supp}(L)$. The vertex $v_\sigma$ is then a vertex of $P_L$, and $\sigma$ is the inward-normal cone at $v_\sigma$. Hence $\Sigma$ is the normal fan of $P_L$, and $X = X_{P_L}$, $L = L_{P_L}$.

Moreover, $\mathrm{supp}(L) = P_L \cap M$ — every lattice point of $P_L$ appears in $H^0(X, L)$ as a $T$-weight, by the toric ampleness/integrality theorem (Cox-Little-Schenck Theorem 6.2.1). Hence $P_L$ is a lattice polytope with vertices exactly the $T$-weights of the corner sections. Uniqueness of $P_L$ up to translation: translating $P_L$ by a lattice element $t \in M$ corresponds to twisting $L$ by the structure line bundle with character $\chi^{-t}$, an isomorphism of polarised toric varieties. $\square$

**Proposition (functoriality of $P \mapsto (X_P, L_P)$), proof.** Given in (d) of the main theorem: a morphism of polytopes $\phi : (M, P) \to (M', P')$ (a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear $M \to M'$ sending lattice points of $P$ into lattice points of $P'$) induces dually a lattice map $\phi^* : N' \to N$ sending the fan $\Sigma_{P'}$ into a refinement of $\Sigma_P$, by polar-duality of the polytope inclusion $P \hookrightarrow P'$ (pulled back to $\phi^{-1}$ on the dual side). The toric morphism $X_{\phi^*} : X_{P'} \to X_P$ from the fan-functoriality of `[04.11.04]` pulls back $L_P$ to $L_{P'}$, yielding an equivariant morphism of polarised toric varieties. Functoriality (composition, identity) is straightforward from the underlying lattice-map operations.

The full faithfulness of the functor uses that every $T$-equivariant ample-bundle morphism between projective toric varieties is uniquely determined by a polytope morphism on the support side (Cox-Little-Schenck Theorem 6.2.1). Essential surjectivity is the converse direction above: every $(X, L)$ comes from some $P$. Combined with faithfulness and fullness, the functor is an equivalence of categories. $\square$

**Proposition (Ehrhart polynomiality), proof sketch.** Given a lattice polytope $P$ of dimension $n$ in $M_\mathbb{R}$, the lattice-point count $L(P, k) = |kP \cap M|$ as a function of $k \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 1}$ extends to a polynomial in $k$ of degree $n$ with leading coefficient $\mathrm{vol}(P)$.

The proof has three steps: (i) **decomposition into half-open simplices**: triangulate $P$ into lattice simplices, and decompose each into half-open chambers via inclusion-exclusion (Stanley-Reisner-style); (ii) **simplex case**: for a lattice simplex $\Delta = \mathrm{conv}(v_0, \ldots, v_n)$, the count $|k\Delta \cap M|$ is computed by a generating-function argument using the fundamental parallelepiped, giving $|k\Delta \cap M| = \sum_{j \geq 0} (\text{lattice points in jth open chamber}) \cdot \binom{k - 1}{j}$ — a polynomial in $k$; (iii) **aggregation**: by inclusion-exclusion over the triangulation, the total count is also polynomial. Ehrhart's original proof (Ehrhart 1962 *C. R. Acad. Sci.* 254) is essentially this argument; the modern algebraic-geometric proof (Brion-Vergne 1997 *J. Amer. Math. Soc.* 10) uses the Euler-characteristic-residue formula on $X_P$ to identify $L(P, k) = \chi(X_P, L_P^{\otimes k})$, a polynomial in $k$ by the Hilbert-polynomial theorem applied to the polarised projective variety.

Leading coefficient: $\mathrm{vol}(P)$ equals the degree of $X_P$ under the embedding by $L_P$, which is the leading term of the Hilbert polynomial of the homogeneous coordinate ring of $X_P$. $\square$

**Proposition (Batyrev mirror duality involution), proof sketch.** *The polar duality $P \mapsto P^\circ$ on reflexive polytopes is an involution: $(P^\circ)^\circ = P$.*

Polar duality is defined by $P^\circ = \{u \in N_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, u\rangle \geq -1 \text{ for all } m \in P\}$. Reflexivity says $P$ contains the origin in its interior and $P^\circ$ is a lattice polytope. The double-polar identity $(P^\circ)^\circ = \overline{P}$ for the convex closure $\overline{P}$ holds for any convex body containing the origin (by the bipolar theorem for the Minkowski-functional pairing). For $P$ a closed convex polytope, $\overline{P} = P$. Hence $(P^\circ)^\circ = P$.

The mirror Calabi-Yau pair $(\widehat{V}, \widehat{V}^\vee)$ arises as follows. Reflexivity of $P$ implies that the anticanonical class $-K_{X_P}$ equals the polarisation $L_P$, so generic anticanonical sections cut out Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces in $X_P$. The Calabi-Yau condition $c_1(\widehat{V}) = 0$ follows from the adjunction formula applied to the smooth resolution $\widehat{X_P}$ of $X_P$ — singularities of $X_P$ are along codimension-2 toric strata, and the crepant resolution preserves the anticanonical class. The mirror $\widehat{V}^\vee \subseteq \widehat{X_{P^\circ}}$ is constructed symmetrically. Hodge-number exchange $h^{p, q}(\widehat{V}) = h^{n - p, q}(\widehat{V}^\vee)$ was verified by Batyrev 1994 via the combinatorial Hodge-Deligne polynomial $E(\widehat{V}; u, v)$ computed in terms of lattice-point and face data of $P$ and $P^\circ$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Fan and toric variety [04.11.04].** The prerequisite construction: $X_P = X_{\Sigma_P}$ for the normal fan $\Sigma_P$. The polytope $P$ refines the fan with additional polarisation data, but the toric variety is recovered entirely from the fan. The polytope-fan dictionary therefore sits one layer above the fan-to-toric construction: it adds the line-bundle layer to the underlying toric scheme.

- **Affine toric variety [04.11.03].** The local building blocks: each vertex $v$ of $P$ produces an affine chart $U_{\sigma_v}$ in $X_P$ via the inward-normal cone. The local section $s_v = \chi^v$ is the generator of $L_P$ over $U_{\sigma_v}$, and the transitions $s_v / s_{v'} = \chi^{v - v'}$ are the localisation maps from `[04.11.03]`. The polytope-fan dictionary is the polarised global packaging of the affine-toric local picture.

- **Algebraic torus and character/cocharacter lattices [04.11.01].** The character lattice $M$ houses the polytope $P$ and supplies the $T$-weights of global sections; the cocharacter lattice $N$ houses the normal fan $\Sigma_P$ and supplies the directions of inward normals. The polar duality $M \leftrightarrow N$ is reflected on the polarised level by the polytope-fan correspondence: lattice polytopes in $M$ correspond to fans in $N$, with the polarisation $L_P$ encoded by the dual datum.

- **Toric divisor and support function [04.11.08].** The polarisation $L_P$ decomposes as a sum of toric divisors $L_P = \sum_{\rho \in \Sigma_P(1)} a_\rho D_\rho$, where $a_\rho = -\psi_P(u_\rho)$ are the negative support-function values on the ray generators. The support function $\psi_P : N_\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is the bridge between the polytope-fan dictionary and the toric-divisor language. The toric-divisor unit develops this correspondence in detail; the polytope-fan dictionary is one of its main outputs.

- **Orbit-cone correspondence [04.11.06].** The orbits of $X_P$ under the dense torus $T$ are in bijection with cones of $\Sigma_P$, equivalently with faces of $P$. The vertex orbits are the $T$-fixed points; edge orbits are one-dimensional toric divisors; higher-dimensional faces give higher-dimensional orbit closures. The polytope $P$ encodes the stratification of $X_P$ by orbit closures, with each face of $P$ corresponding to a closed toric subvariety.

- **Smoothness and completeness via fans [04.11.05].** The sibling unit supplies the smoothness and completeness criteria for $X_P = X_{\Sigma_P}$ in fan-theoretic language: completeness of $\Sigma_P$ ($|\Sigma_P| = N_\mathbb{R}$) is automatic for the normal fan of a full-dimensional lattice polytope, recovering the projective implies complete direction; smoothness of $X_P$ corresponds, via the dual lattice computation, to the **Delzant condition** at every vertex of $P$ (edges at each vertex form a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$). The polytope-fan dictionary is the projective specialisation; `[04.11.05]` is the general framework on top of which the polarised picture sits.

- **Toric resolution of singularities [04.11.07].** The sibling unit develops fan refinement by star subdivision; on the polytope side, this corresponds to **regular triangulations** of $P$ refining the face structure into smooth simplices, with interior lattice points of $P$ supplying the new rays. Crepant resolutions of $X_P$ correspond to lattice triangulations of $P$ whose vertices lie on $\partial P$ together with interior lattice points; the McKay correspondence in dimension three identifies these triangulations with $G$-representations for the acting group. The polytope picture makes the resolution procedure of `[04.11.07]` explicitly combinatorial on the polytope side.

- **Projective space [04.07.01].** The most classical example of the polytope-fan dictionary: the standard simplex gives $\mathbb{P}^n$ with $\mathcal{O}(1)$. The embedding $\mathbb{P}^n \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}^n$ by the basis $\{X_0, \ldots, X_n\}$ of $\mathcal{O}(1)$ is the lattice-point embedding of $P$ for the standard simplex. All toric subvarieties of $\mathbb{P}^N$ arise via polytope-fan dictionaries by appropriate inclusion of lattice points.

- **Symplectic toric manifold / Delzant correspondence [05.04.05].** On the symplectic side, Delzant 1988 (*Bull. SMF* 116) classified compact symplectic toric manifolds by **Delzant polytopes** — simple, rational, smooth polytopes. The Delzant correspondence dovetails with the polytope-fan dictionary on the projective-toric overlap: smooth projective toric varieties with their ample polarisations correspond to Delzant polytopes via the moment-map identification $\mu : X_P \to P$. The polytope $P$ is simultaneously the support data of the ample line bundle and the image of the moment map.

- **Toric moment map and the polytope [04.11.11].** The algebraic moment map $\mu : X_P \to P$ is the toric incarnation of the Atiyah-Guillemin-Sternberg convexity theorem (Atiyah 1982 *Bull. London Math. Soc.* 14; Guillemin-Sternberg 1982 *Invent. Math.* 67): the image of $X_P$ under the compact-torus moment map is exactly the polytope $P$. This identifies $P$ with the moment-polytope of the toric symplectic manifold, closing the loop between algebraic and symplectic toric geometry.

- **Mirror symmetry via tropical geometry [04.12.09] (Gross-Siebert).** The Gross-Siebert mirror reconstruction program (Gross-Siebert 2011 *Annals* 174) uses tropical-geometric scattering diagrams on the dual intersection complex of a polytope subdivision to construct mirror Calabi-Yau pairs. For Batyrev-type reflexive polytopes, this recovers the polar-duality mirror pair; for arbitrary toric degenerations of Calabi-Yau manifolds, the program generalises beyond the polytope case.

- **Nishinou-Siebert correspondence [04.12.06].** The higher-dimensional enumerative correspondence uses toric varieties from polytopes $\Delta = P$ as the targets of toric degenerations, with the polytope structure encoding both the variety and the polarisation. The Nishinou-Siebert curve counts are tropical counts on the polyhedral subdivision of $P$ underlying the toric degeneration, with the polytope-fan dictionary as the structural backbone.

- **Toric Picard group [04.11.09].** The Picard group $\mathrm{Pic}(X_P) = \mathrm{Pic}(X_{\Sigma_P})$ is generated by the toric divisors $D_\rho$ for $\rho \in \Sigma_P(1)$, modulo linear equivalences from $M$. The polarisation $L_P$ lies in the ample cone $\mathrm{Amp}(X_P) \subseteq \mathrm{Pic}(X_P) \otimes \mathbb{R}$, with the cone of polytopes $P$ supporting a given fan parametrising the ample cone. The polytope-fan dictionary refines the toric-Picard-group classification by adding the polarisation choice.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The polytope-fan dictionary in its modern form was crystallised by Michel Demazure in *Sous-groupes algébriques de rang maximum du groupe de Cremona* (Annales scientifiques de l'École normale supérieure, 4e série, tome 3, 1970, pp. 507-588) [ref: TODO_REF Demazure 1970], the same paper that established the fan-to-toric construction. Demazure's identification of strictly convex piecewise-linear functions $\psi : N_\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ on a complete fan with ample $T$-equivariant line bundles on the corresponding toric variety, and the further identification of the upper-graph polytope of such a function with the polytope $P$ encoding the polarisation, established the dictionary's algebraic side. Demazure also proved the character formula bearing his name: $H^0(X_P, L_P) = \bigoplus_{m \in P \cap M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m$, identifying global sections with lattice points.

The polytope-fan correspondence had earlier precursors in the work of Tadao Oda and his Sendai-school collaborators in the late 1970s and early 1980s (Oda 1978 lecture notes; consolidated in Oda's *Convex Bodies and Algebraic Geometry*, Ergebnisse 15, Springer 1988 [ref: TODO_REF]), who systematised the dual-cone formalism and explicitly developed the polytope-to-fan recipe for projective toric varieties. Oda's book remained the authoritative reference until William Fulton's *Introduction to Toric Varieties* (Princeton 1993) [ref: TODO_REF Fulton 1993] consolidated the dictionary into modern teaching form, with §1.5 introducing the polytope-fan dictionary and §3.4 developing the polarisation $L_P$ and the Demazure character formula in the form now standard.

Hesheng Hu and Jürgen Herzog's 1970s work on Stanley-Reisner rings provided the combinatorial-commutative-algebra counterpart: the homogeneous coordinate ring of $X_P$ under the polarisation $L_P$ is generated by the lattice points of $P$ subject to binomial relations encoded by the Newton-polytope structure (Sturmfels 1995, *Gröbner Bases and Convex Polytopes*, AMS University Lecture Series 8 [ref: TODO_REF]). This bridges the polytope-fan dictionary to computational algebra: lattice-point ideals and binomial Gröbner bases compute the homogeneous coordinate ring algorithmically.

Victor Batyrev's *Dual polyhedra and mirror symmetry for Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces in toric varieties* (Journal of Algebraic Geometry 3, 1994, pp. 493-535) [ref: TODO_REF Batyrev 1994] opened a major new chapter: reflexive polytopes — those whose polar dual is also a lattice polytope — produce mirror-symmetric pairs of Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces under the polar-duality involution $P \leftrightarrow P^\circ$. Batyrev's paper was the first systematic combinatorial construction of mirror pairs in arbitrary dimension, and it transformed mirror symmetry from a string-theoretic conjecture (Greene-Plesser 1990, Candelas-Lynker-Schimmrigk 1990) into a precise mathematical statement amenable to combinatorial verification. The classification of reflexive polytopes was completed by Maximilian Kreuzer and Harald Skarke in 2000-2002 (*Adv. Theor. Math. Phys.* 4, 1209-1230 and *Comm. Math. Phys.* 230, 333-353), enumerating the 473,800,776 reflexive 4-polytopes by exhaustive computation — each producing a Calabi-Yau threefold whose Hodge numbers and mirror pair are explicitly known.

The extension to complete intersections came with Batyrev and Lev Borisov's 1996 work [ref: TODO_REF BatyrevBorisov1996]: nef-partitioned reflexive polytopes produce Calabi-Yau complete intersections in toric varieties, and the nef-partition polar duality exchanges mirror pairs. This generalises the hypersurface case to systems of Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces in toric ambient spaces, accounting for many of the most studied mirror-symmetric Calabi-Yau 3-folds in the algebro-geometric literature.

The symplectic counterpart was developed by Thomas Delzant in his 1988 thesis paper *Hamiltoniens périodiques et image convexe de l'application moment* (Bulletin de la SMF 116, 315-339) [ref: TODO_REF Delzant1988], establishing the **Delzant correspondence**: compact symplectic toric manifolds are classified by Delzant polytopes (simple, rational, smooth). The Delzant correspondence dovetails with the polytope-fan dictionary on the projective-toric overlap, with the polytope $P$ simultaneously the support data of the ample line bundle and the moment-map image of the compact-torus action. This unification of symplectic and algebraic toric geometry under a single polytope datum is one of the conceptual high points of late-20th-century toric geometry.

The Brion-Vergne lattice-point formula (Brion 1996 *Banach Center Publ.* 36, 25-44 [ref: TODO_REF]; Brion-Vergne 1997 *J. Amer. Math. Soc.* 10) extended the Demazure character formula to higher cohomology of arbitrary toric divisors via equivariant Euler characteristics and residue formulas at the fixed-point set. The Ehrhart polynomial of a polytope (Eugène Ehrhart 1962, *C. R. Acad. Sci.* 254, 616-618 [ref: TODO_REF]) acquires a clean algebraic-geometric interpretation as the Hilbert polynomial of $X_P$ under the polarisation $L_P$, with Ehrhart-Macdonald reciprocity $L(P, -k) = (-1)^n L(\mathrm{int}(P), k)$ corresponding to Serre duality on $X_P$. These lattice-point formulas turn algebraic-geometric invariants (Hilbert polynomial, Euler characteristic, dimension of cohomology) into combinatorial lattice-counting invariants, a recurring theme of toric geometry.

David Cox's 1995 paper *The homogeneous coordinate ring of a toric variety* (Journal of Algebraic Geometry 4, 17-50) [ref: TODO_REF Cox1995] reframed the polytope-fan dictionary in the **Cox-ring** language: $X_P$ is the GIT quotient of an affine space by a torus, and the polarisation $L_P$ corresponds to a specific character in the Cox-ring grading. This perspective unified projective and toric geometry under a common functorial framework and remains the foundation for modern computational toric geometry (Macaulay2, Polymake, SageMath). The Cox-ring perspective also extends the polytope-fan dictionary to non-projective toric varieties, by replacing polytopes with positions in the GIT chamber structure.
