## Intuition [Beginner]

A single rational polyhedral cone $\sigma$ produces a single affine toric variety $U_\sigma$, as built in the previous unit. The next move in toric geometry is to glue many such affine pieces into a global variety. The combinatorial input that controls the gluing is a **fan** $\Sigma$: a finite collection of cones in $N_\mathbb{R}$ that fit together compatibly, every face of a cone is in the collection, and every pairwise intersection is a face of each. The variety $X_\Sigma$ associated to the fan is the result of gluing the affine pieces $U_\sigma$ for $\sigma \in \Sigma$ along the open immersions $U_{\sigma \cap \tau} \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$ that the face-functoriality of `[04.11.03]` produces.

The picture in two dimensions is simple. Imagine $N_\mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}^2$ chopped into wedges by finitely many rays through the origin. Each wedge is a maximal cone of the fan; each ray is a one-dimensional cone; the origin is the zero cone. The variety $X_\Sigma$ has one affine chart $U_\sigma$ per wedge, and the charts are glued along the affine charts of the rays. If the rays cover all directions in $\mathbb{R}^2$, the variety is compact; if they leave a gap, the variety has a hole at infinity.

This construction recovers every familiar normal toric variety. Projective space $\mathbb{P}^n$ comes from a fan with $n+1$ rays in $\mathbb{R}^n$ pointing toward the vertices of a simplex. The product $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ comes from four quadrants in $\mathbb{R}^2$. The Hirzebruch surface $F_a$ comes from a four-ray fan with one ray tilted by the parameter $a$. Sumihiro's theorem of 1974 guarantees that **every** normal toric variety arises this way from a unique fan, making the cone-and-fan formalism a complete combinatorial classification.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Build $\mathbb{P}^1$ as the toric variety $X_\Sigma$ for the smallest interesting fan. Take $N = \mathbb{Z}$ with $N_\mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}$, and let $\Sigma$ have three cones: the zero cone $\{0\}$ and the two rays $\sigma_+ = \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ and $\sigma_- = \mathbb{R}_{\leq 0}$. The fan is the entire real line, split at the origin into a positive half-ray and a negative half-ray, plus the origin itself.

Step 1. The affine pieces. The cone $\sigma_+ = \mathrm{Cone}(1)$ has dual $\sigma_+^\vee = \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$, semigroup $\mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$, semigroup algebra $\mathbb{C}[x]$, so $U_{\sigma_+} = \mathbb{C}$. The cone $\sigma_- = \mathrm{Cone}(-1)$ has dual $\sigma_-^\vee = \mathbb{R}_{\leq 0}$, semigroup $\mathbb{Z}_{\leq 0}$, semigroup algebra $\mathbb{C}[x^{-1}]$ — set $y = x^{-1}$, so $U_{\sigma_-} = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[y] = \mathbb{C}$, another affine line. The zero cone $\{0\}$ has $U_{\{0\}} = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[x, x^{-1}] = \mathbb{C}^*$, the dense torus.

Step 2. The gluing. The intersection $\sigma_+ \cap \sigma_- = \{0\}$ is a face of each, and $U_{\{0\}} = \mathbb{C}^*$ embeds into $U_{\sigma_+} = \mathbb{C}$ as the open subset $\{x \neq 0\}$ and into $U_{\sigma_-} = \mathbb{C}$ as the open subset $\{y \neq 0\}$. The gluing identifies these two open immersions via the relation $y = x^{-1}$: a point in the overlap is the same point of $\mathbb{C}^*$ written in either chart.

Step 3. The result. Two affine lines glued along $\mathbb{C}^*$ via $y = x^{-1}$ is the classical construction of the projective line $\mathbb{P}^1$. The variety $X_\Sigma = \mathbb{P}^1$ has two affine charts (the maximal cones $\sigma_\pm$) plus the dense open torus (the zero cone). The two "points at infinity" correspond to the closed $T$-fixed points of each chart — the origins $x = 0$ and $y = 0$ — which are the unique $T$-fixed points of $\sigma_+$ and $\sigma_-$ respectively.

What this tells us. The cone-and-fan construction recovers $\mathbb{P}^1$ as the gluing of two affine pieces along an open dense torus, exactly the standard atlas of the projective line. Each maximal cone produces a chart; each face inclusion produces an overlap; the global variety is compact because the support of the fan covers all of $N_\mathbb{R}$. This pattern generalises: $\mathbb{P}^n$ from a fan with $n+1$ maximal cones, $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ from four quadrants, and so on.

A **fan** $\Sigma$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$ is:

A. Any finite collection of strongly convex rational polyhedral cones.

The two axioms are face-closure and intersection-as-face. Both are needed; either alone is insufficient.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** A fan is a finite collection $\Sigma$ of strongly convex rational polyhedral cones satisfying two axioms: (i) every face of a cone in $\Sigma$ is in $\Sigma$; (ii) the intersection of any two cones is a face of each. Option A omits both axioms — without them, the gluing data fails to assemble into a separated scheme. Option C describes a degenerate single-maximal-cone case; general fans have several maximal cones. Option D is far too permissive: arbitrary closed convex covers do not produce algebraic varieties. The two axioms are exactly what is needed to glue the affine pieces $U_\sigma$ into a separated algebraic variety $X_\Sigma$.

</details>

C. $n + 1$ rays — the standard basis $e_1, \ldots, e_n$ together with $-e_1 - \cdots - e_n$ — and all cones spanned by proper subsets, total of $2^{n+1} - 1$ cones.

$\mathbb{P}^n$ has $n + 1$ affine charts (the standard cover by $U_i = \{[x_0 : \ldots : x_n] : x_i \neq 0\}$), so there should be $n + 1$ maximal cones, each generated by $n$ rays out of the total $n + 1$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**C.** The fan of $\mathbb{P}^n$ has $n + 1$ rays with primitive generators $\rho_0 = -e_1 - \cdots - e_n$ and $\rho_i = e_i$ for $i \geq 1$. Maximal cones are the $n + 1$ size-$n$ subsets of these rays; each generates a copy of $\mathbb{C}^n$ as its affine chart. Adding all faces gives total cone count $2^{n + 1} - 1$ (all proper subsets; the full set fails because the rays sum to zero). Option B is the fan of $\mathbb{C}^n$. Option A is too small. <aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise (easy, true-false).**

True or false: the toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is compact in the classical complex topology if and only if the support $|\Sigma| := \bigcup_{\sigma \in \Sigma} \sigma$ equals all of $N_\mathbb{R}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

This is the completeness criterion. Compactness in the classical topology coincides with completeness (= properness over $\mathbb{C}$) for varieties of finite type. The support of the fan controls "how much of the torus is compactified".

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.** This is the **completeness criterion** for toric varieties: $X_\Sigma$ is complete (equivalently, compact in the $\mathbb{C}$-topology, equivalently, proper over $\mathbb{C}$) if and only if $|\Sigma| = \bigcup_\sigma \sigma$ equals $N_\mathbb{R}$. The intuition is direct: each maximal cone contributes an affine chart compactifying one direction of the dense torus, and the support records which directions have been compactified. If $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$, every direction is compactified and the variety is compact; if $|\Sigma| \subsetneq N_\mathbb{R}$, some direction is missing and a hole remains at infinity. The full proof uses the valuative criterion — see the key theorem below.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $N$ be a lattice of rank $n$ with dual lattice $M = \mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(N, \mathbb{Z})$, as in `[04.11.01]`. Let $N_\mathbb{R} = N \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{R}$.

**Definition (fan).** A **fan** $\Sigma$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$ is a finite collection of strongly convex rational polyhedral cones $\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ such that:

(i) Every face of a cone in $\Sigma$ is a cone in $\Sigma$.

(ii) For any two cones $\sigma, \tau \in \Sigma$, the intersection $\sigma \cap \tau$ is a face of $\sigma$ and a face of $\tau$.

The **support** of $\Sigma$ is the closed subset $|\Sigma| := \bigcup_{\sigma \in \Sigma} \sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$. The **$k$-skeleton** $\Sigma(k)$ is the subset of cones of dimension exactly $k$; in particular $\Sigma(1)$ is the set of **rays** (one-dimensional cones), each generated by a unique **primitive generator** $u_\rho \in N$.

A fan is **smooth** (or **regular**) if every cone $\sigma \in \Sigma$ is generated by a subset of a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$. A fan is **complete** if $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$. A fan is **simplicial** if every cone in $\Sigma$ is simplicial (generated by linearly independent rays).

**Definition (toric variety from a fan).** Given a fan $\Sigma$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$, the **toric variety associated to $\Sigma$**, denoted $X_\Sigma$, is the scheme obtained by gluing the affine pieces $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ for $\sigma \in \Sigma$ along the open immersions
$$
U_{\sigma \cap \tau} \hookrightarrow U_\sigma, \qquad U_{\sigma \cap \tau} \hookrightarrow U_\tau,
$$
produced by the face-functoriality of `[04.11.03]`. Concretely, $X_\Sigma$ is the colimit (in the category of $\mathbb{C}$-schemes) of the diagram whose objects are the affine pieces $U_\sigma$ for $\sigma \in \Sigma$ and whose morphisms are the open immersions $U_\tau \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$ for face inclusions $\tau \subseteq \sigma$. Equivalently — and this is the form used in working computations — $X_\Sigma$ has an open cover $\{U_\sigma : \sigma \in \Sigma_{\max}\}$ indexed by the maximal cones, with overlaps $U_\sigma \cap U_\tau = U_{\sigma \cap \tau}$ for every pair of maximal cones.

**Convention.** "Maximal cone" means a cone of $\Sigma$ that is not a proper face of any other cone in $\Sigma$. Every cone of $\Sigma$ is a face of at least one maximal cone (assuming $|\Sigma|$ has dimension equal to that cone or higher); in particular if $\Sigma$ is full-dimensional then every cone is a face of some $n$-dimensional cone.

**Definition (torus action).** The algebraic torus $T = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M]$ acts on each affine piece $U_\sigma$ by the action defined in `[04.11.03]`, and the gluing morphisms $U_{\sigma \cap \tau} \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$ are $T$-equivariant. Hence the colimit $X_\Sigma$ inherits a $T$-action, and the dense torus $T = U_{\{0\}} \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$ for every $\sigma \in \Sigma$ embeds as a single dense open subvariety of $X_\Sigma$ — the open $T$-orbit, common to every affine chart.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- "Any collection of cones closed under faces is a fan." Not without the intersection axiom (ii). For instance, in $\mathbb{R}^2$ take the two cones $\sigma_1 = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_1 + e_2)$ and $\sigma_2 = \mathrm{Cone}(e_2, e_1 + e_2)$, together with their faces. The intersection $\sigma_1 \cap \sigma_2 = \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}(e_1 + e_2)$ is a ray, but this ray is a face of both cones — so this example is actually a valid fan. A genuine non-fan: $\tau_1 = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_2)$ and $\tau_2 = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1 + e_2, e_1 - e_2)$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$. The intersection $\tau_1 \cap \tau_2$ is a two-dimensional region in the first quadrant, not a face of either — adding the faces of both cones still does not produce a fan, and the would-be gluing $U_{\tau_1}$ glued to $U_{\tau_2}$ along the overlap fails to be separated.

- "The toric variety $X_\Sigma$ depends only on the support $|\Sigma|$." It does not. The same support can be subdivided by different fans, producing genuinely different toric varieties — typically related by toric birational morphisms. For example, the support $\mathbb{R}^2$ (the complete fan) is the support of both the fan for $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ (four quadrants) and the fan for $\mathbb{P}^2$ (three maximal cones spanned by $e_1, e_2, -e_1 - e_2$); the underlying varieties are not isomorphic. The fan, not its support, is the gluing data.

- "Every cone of $\Sigma$ produces a $T$-orbit, but the orbit lives only in the chart $U_\sigma$." Each orbit lives in **every** chart $U_\tau$ for which $\sigma$ is a face of $\tau$, by the face-functoriality (the open immersion $U_\sigma \hookrightarrow U_\tau$ identifies the orbit corresponding to $\sigma$ in $U_\sigma$ with the same orbit in $U_\tau$). The orbits are global objects on $X_\Sigma$, not chart-local; this is the content of the orbit-cone correspondence (`[04.11.05]`).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem of this unit is the **Sumihiro classification** of normal toric varieties: every separated normal toric variety arises from a unique fan up to lattice isomorphism, with completeness, smoothness, and projectivity all readable from fan-combinatorial data.

**Theorem (the fan-to-toric-variety construction; Demazure 1970, Sumihiro 1974).** *Let $\Sigma$ be a fan in $N_\mathbb{R}$.*

*(a) **Existence and gluing.** The colimit $X_\Sigma = \mathrm{colim}_{\sigma \in \Sigma} U_\sigma$ exists as a scheme of finite type over $\mathbb{C}$, with an open cover $\{U_\sigma : \sigma \in \Sigma_{\max}\}$ and overlaps $U_\sigma \cap U_\tau = U_{\sigma \cap \tau}$.*

*(b) **Normality and separatedness.** $X_\Sigma$ is irreducible, normal, and separated; the dense torus $T = U_{\{0\}} \hookrightarrow X_\Sigma$ acts on $X_\Sigma$ extending the translation action on itself.*

*(c) **Completeness criterion.** $X_\Sigma$ is complete (proper over $\mathbb{C}$, equivalently compact in the $\mathbb{C}$-topology) if and only if $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$.*

*(d) **Smoothness criterion.** $X_\Sigma$ is smooth as a $\mathbb{C}$-scheme if and only if every cone $\sigma \in \Sigma$ is smooth (generated by part of a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$).*

*(e) **Sumihiro classification.** Conversely, every separated normal variety with a $T$-action having a dense open orbit arises as $X_\Sigma$ for a unique fan $\Sigma$ up to lattice isomorphism.*

**Proof.**

*(a) Existence of the colimit.* The diagram of affine pieces $U_\sigma$ with face-inclusion morphisms is a finite diagram in the category of schemes. By the gluing lemma for schemes (sometimes called the **Grothendieck gluing data** lemma, $EGA$ I §0.4 or Hartshorne *Algebraic Geometry* Ex. II.2.12), to construct the colimit it suffices to verify the cocycle condition: for any three cones $\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \sigma_3 \in \Sigma$, the transition isomorphism $U_{\sigma_1 \cap \sigma_3} \cap U_{\sigma_2 \cap \sigma_3} \to U_{\sigma_1 \cap \sigma_3} \cap U_{\sigma_2 \cap \sigma_3}$ via $U_{\sigma_2}$ matches the direct transition. Since the intersections $\sigma_i \cap \sigma_j$ are themselves cones in $\Sigma$ (by axiom (ii) of the fan), and since $\mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma_i \cap \sigma_j}]$ is a localisation of each $\mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma_i}]$ at a single semi-invariant element (by the face-functoriality of `[04.11.03]`), the transitions are localisation maps. Iterated localisation is single localisation at a product, so the cocycle condition reduces to the identity $(\chi^{m_1})(\chi^{m_3})/(\chi^{m_2})^2 \cdot (\chi^{m_2})^2/(\chi^{m_1})(\chi^{m_3}) = 1$ in the appropriate triple localisation. The cocycle holds and the colimit $X_\Sigma$ exists.

*(b) Normality.* Each $U_\sigma$ is normal (by part (a) of the main theorem of `[04.11.03]`). Normality is a local property — a scheme is normal if and only if its local rings are integrally closed integral domains — so the gluing of normal affine pieces along open immersions is normal. The dense torus $T = U_{\{0\}}$ embeds in every $U_\sigma$ via the face $\{0\} \subseteq \sigma$, and the embeddings are all identified in the gluing (the cocycle condition forces them to agree); hence $T$ embeds as a single dense open subvariety of $X_\Sigma$.

*(b) Separatedness.* The diagonal morphism $\Delta : X_\Sigma \to X_\Sigma \times_\mathbb{C} X_\Sigma$ is a closed immersion if and only if for every pair of maximal cones $\sigma, \tau$ the diagonal $U_{\sigma \cap \tau} \to U_\sigma \times_\mathbb{C} U_\tau$ is a closed immersion. The image is the subscheme defined by the relations $\chi^m \otimes 1 = 1 \otimes \chi^m$ for $m \in S_{\sigma \cap \tau}$. By the **face-correspondence theorem** of `[04.11.02]`, $\sigma \cap \tau$ is a face of both $\sigma$ and $\tau$; choosing $m_{\sigma \cap \tau}$ in the relative interior of $(\sigma \cap \tau)^\perp$, the semigroup $S_{\sigma \cap \tau} = S_\sigma + S_\tau$ (the join of the two semigroups inside $M$, since the face direction is shared). The image of the diagonal is then $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma \cap \tau}] = U_{\sigma \cap \tau}$, presented as a closed subscheme of $U_\sigma \times U_\tau = \mathrm{Spec}\,(\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \otimes \mathbb{C}[S_\tau])$ by the surjection $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \otimes \mathbb{C}[S_\tau] \to \mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma \cap \tau}]$, $\chi^m \otimes \chi^{m'} \mapsto \chi^{m + m'}$. Surjectivity at the level of semigroups gives surjectivity of the algebra map, hence a closed immersion of $\mathrm{Spec}$. This is exactly the verification that $X_\Sigma$ is separated.

*(c) Completeness criterion.* We use the valuative criterion for properness: $X_\Sigma$ is proper over $\mathbb{C}$ if and only if for every discrete valuation ring $R$ with field of fractions $K$ and every $\mathbb{C}$-morphism $\mathrm{Spec}\,K \to X_\Sigma$, there is a unique extension to $\mathrm{Spec}\,R \to X_\Sigma$.

A $\mathbb{C}$-morphism $\mathrm{Spec}\,K \to X_\Sigma$ that factors through the open torus $T \subseteq X_\Sigma$ corresponds to a $\mathbb{C}$-algebra map $\mathbb{C}[M] \to K$, equivalently a group homomorphism $M \to K^* = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathbb{Z}, K^*)$, equivalently an element of $T(K) = M^\vee \otimes_\mathbb{Z} K^*$. Composing with the valuation $v : K^* \to \mathbb{Z}$ gives a homomorphism $v_T : M \to \mathbb{Z}$, equivalently an element of $N = \mathrm{Hom}(M, \mathbb{Z})$. This element $v_T \in N_\mathbb{Q}$ (rationally; integral if the morphism extends to $R$) records the "direction" of the limit point in $N_\mathbb{R}$.

The morphism $\mathrm{Spec}\,K \to X_\Sigma$ extends to $\mathrm{Spec}\,R \to X_\Sigma$ if and only if there exists a cone $\sigma \in \Sigma$ such that the corresponding $\mathbb{C}$-algebra map $\mathbb{C}[M] \to K$ restricts to a map $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \to R$ — that is, every $\chi^m$ for $m \in S_\sigma$ has $v(\chi^m) \geq 0$, equivalently $\langle m, v_T\rangle \geq 0$ for every $m \in \sigma^\vee$, equivalently $v_T \in \sigma$. Hence the extension exists if and only if $v_T \in |\Sigma|$.

Uniqueness of the extension follows from the separatedness in part (b). Existence holds for every DVR-valued point if and only if every $v_T \in N$ lies in some cone of $\Sigma$, equivalently $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$ (the integer points $N \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ are dense, and the cones are closed, so $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$ iff $|\Sigma| \supseteq N$). The valuative criterion gives completeness iff $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$.

*(d) Smoothness criterion.* Smoothness is a local property: $X_\Sigma$ is smooth iff every affine chart $U_\sigma$ is smooth. By the smoothness criterion for affine toric varieties (Theorem of `[04.11.03]`, Demazure 1970), $U_\sigma$ is smooth iff $\sigma$ is generated by part of a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$. Hence $X_\Sigma$ is smooth iff every $\sigma \in \Sigma$ has this property.

*(e) Sumihiro classification.* Sumihiro's 1974 theorem (Journal of Mathematics of Kyoto University 14, 1-28) says every normal variety $X$ with a $T$-action admits a $T$-equivariant affine open cover. Applied to a normal toric variety $X$ (one with a dense $T$-orbit), the affine cover $\{V_i\}$ consists of $T$-stable affine open subvarieties. Each $V_i$ is normal with a $T$-action and a dense $T$-orbit; by the affine-toric classification (`[04.11.03]`), $V_i = U_{\sigma_i}$ for a unique cone $\sigma_i$ up to $\mathrm{GL}(N)$-isomorphism.

The overlaps $V_i \cap V_j$ are also normal affine $T$-varieties with dense orbits, so $V_i \cap V_j = U_{\tau_{ij}}$ for some cone $\tau_{ij}$. The two open immersions $U_{\tau_{ij}} \hookrightarrow U_{\sigma_i}$ and $\hookrightarrow U_{\sigma_j}$ realise $\tau_{ij}$ as a common face of $\sigma_i$ and $\sigma_j$ (by the face-functoriality of `[04.11.03]`, with the open immersion encoded by localisation at $\chi^{m_{\tau_{ij}}}$). Hence $\tau_{ij} = \sigma_i \cap \sigma_j$ is a face of both, and the collection $\{\sigma_i\}$ together with all their faces and intersections forms a fan $\Sigma$ with $X = X_\Sigma$.

Uniqueness up to lattice isomorphism: the cones $\sigma_i$ are determined by the $T$-fixed points and orbit structure of $X$ (orbit-cone correspondence, `[04.11.05]`), which is intrinsic to $X$. A $T$-equivariant isomorphism $X \cong X'$ induces a lattice isomorphism $N \cong N'$ and a fan isomorphism $\Sigma \cong \Sigma'$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The fan-to-toric-variety theorem builds toward the orbit-cone correspondence of `[04.11.05]` and the divisor-fan dictionary of `[04.11.08]`, and the central insight is that the global toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is determined entirely by the combinatorics of the fan $\Sigma$, with every geometric property — separatedness, normality, smoothness, completeness, projectivity — reading off from a combinatorial condition on the cones. This is exactly the foundational reason that toric geometry is **effectively combinatorial**: questions about $X_\Sigma$ become questions about $\Sigma$, and the dictionary is exact and computable.

Sumihiro's classification appears again in `[04.11.05]` (where the orbit-cone correspondence is proven by reducing to the affine case via the fan structure) and generalises the affine-toric classification of `[04.11.03]` from cones to fans. Putting these together identifies the category of fans (with fan morphisms in `[04.11.06]`) with the category of normal $T$-toric varieties (with $T$-equivariant morphisms), and this categorical equivalence is the bridge from convex-geometric data to algebraic-geometric objects. The bridge is dual to the duality $M \leftrightarrow N$ from `[04.11.01]`: the fan $\Sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ encodes the compactification directions of the torus $T$, the affine charts $U_\sigma$ encode the local geometry near each compactification, and the gluing assembles the local pictures into a global variety. This pattern appears again in `[04.11.07]` (toric resolution by fan refinement, the combinatorial form of Hironaka's theorem), in `[04.11.10]` (the polytope-to-fan correspondence producing projective toric varieties), and in `[04.11.15]` (toric cohomology via the Stanley-Reisner ring of the fan).

Describe the **Hirzebruch surface** $F_a$ (for $a \geq 0$ an integer) as a toric variety $X_{\Sigma_a}$ in $N_\mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}^2$. Verify that $F_0 = \mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ and that $F_a$ is smooth and complete for every $a$.

The fan for $F_a$ has four rays: $\rho_1 = e_1$, $\rho_2 = e_2$, $\rho_3 = -e_1 + a e_2$, $\rho_4 = -e_2$. (The third ray "tilts" by the parameter $a$.) **Rays.** $\rho_1 = e_1$, $\rho_2 = e_2$, $\rho_3 = -e_1 + a e_2$, $\rho_4 = -e_2$. Going counterclockwise from $\rho_1$, the rays are at standard direction except for $\rho_3$, which tilts toward $+e_2$ by amount $a$ (so $\rho_3 = -e_1$ when $a = 0$, $\rho_3 = -e_1 + e_2$ when $a = 1$, etc.).

**Maximal cones.** Four cones, each generated by two adjacent rays:
- $\sigma_1 = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_2)$.
- $\sigma_2 = \mathrm{Cone}(e_2, -e_1 + a e_2)$.
- $\sigma_3 = \mathrm{Cone}(-e_1 + a e_2, -e_2)$.
- $\sigma_4 = \mathrm{Cone}(-e_2, e_1)$.

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Prove that the toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is separated, by verifying that for every pair of maximal cones $\sigma, \tau \in \Sigma$, the diagonal $U_{\sigma \cap \tau} \to U_\sigma \times_\mathbb{C} U_\tau$ is a closed immersion.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The semigroup of $\sigma \cap \tau$ is $S_{\sigma \cap \tau} = S_\sigma + S_\tau$ (the sum of the two semigroups, regarded as subsets of $M$). The diagonal is the kernel of the multiplication map $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \otimes \mathbb{C}[S_\tau] \to \mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma \cap \tau}]$, and surjectivity on semigroups gives surjectivity on algebras, which corresponds to a closed immersion of $\mathrm{Spec}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\sigma, \tau \in \Sigma$ be maximal cones with intersection $\sigma \cap \tau$ a face of each (by the fan axiom). By the **face-correspondence theorem** of `[04.11.02]`, the dual cones satisfy
$$
(\sigma \cap \tau)^\vee = \sigma^\vee + \tau^\vee,
$$
the Minkowski sum of $\sigma^\vee$ and $\tau^\vee$ in $M_\mathbb{R}$ (faces dualise to faces, and intersection on one side dualises to Minkowski sum on the other). Intersecting with $M$:
$$
S_{\sigma \cap \tau} = (\sigma \cap \tau)^\vee \cap M = (\sigma^\vee + \tau^\vee) \cap M = S_\sigma + S_\tau,
$$
the sum of $S_\sigma$ and $S_\tau$ as subsets of $M$ (using that both are saturated in $M$).

The multiplication map of semigroup algebras
$$
\mu : \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \otimes_\mathbb{C} \mathbb{C}[S_\tau] \to \mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma \cap \tau}], \qquad \chi^m \otimes \chi^{m'} \mapsto \chi^{m + m'},
$$
is well-defined (since $m + m' \in S_\sigma + S_\tau = S_{\sigma \cap \tau}$) and surjective (every $\chi^{m''}$ for $m'' \in S_{\sigma \cap \tau}$ is the image of $\chi^m \otimes \chi^{m'}$ for some decomposition $m'' = m + m'$ with $m \in S_\sigma, m' \in S_\tau$).

Surjectivity of a $\mathbb{C}$-algebra map corresponds (by $\mathrm{Spec}$ contravariance) to a closed immersion of affine schemes:
$$
U_{\sigma \cap \tau} = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma \cap \tau}] \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Spec}\,(\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \otimes \mathbb{C}[S_\tau]) = U_\sigma \times_\mathbb{C} U_\tau.
$$
The image is the diagonal $\Delta(U_{\sigma \cap \tau}) \subseteq U_\sigma \times_\mathbb{C} U_\tau$, defined by the relations $\chi^m \otimes 1 = 1 \otimes \chi^m$ for $m \in S_\sigma \cap S_\tau$ (lattice points common to both semigroups), which are exactly the lattice points in $\sigma^\vee \cap \tau^\vee = (\sigma + \tau)^\vee$ — the elements where the two charts agree on the overlap.

Hence the diagonal $X_\Sigma \to X_\Sigma \times_\mathbb{C} X_\Sigma$ is a closed immersion locally, and a morphism is a closed immersion iff it is closed immersion locally on the target. Therefore $X_\Sigma$ is separated. $\square$

Rubric: full credit for the dual-cone identity $(\sigma \cap \tau)^\vee = \sigma^\vee + \tau^\vee$, the surjective semigroup-algebra map, and the closed-immersion conclusion.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove the **completeness criterion**: the toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is complete (proper over $\mathbb{C}$) if and only if $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$. Use the valuative criterion for properness applied to the open dense torus.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A morphism $\mathrm{Spec}\,K \to X_\Sigma$ for $K$ the fraction field of a DVR $R$ factors through the open torus $T$ generically (since $T$ is dense). Composing with the DVR's valuation gives a cocharacter $v \in N$. The extension to $\mathrm{Spec}\,R \to X_\Sigma$ exists iff $v$ lies in some cone of $\Sigma$. Hence properness holds iff every $v \in N$ lies in $|\Sigma|$, iff $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

We use the **valuative criterion for properness**: a separated morphism $f : X \to Y$ of finite type is proper iff for every discrete valuation ring $R$ with fraction field $K$, every commutative diagram
$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
\mathrm{Spec}\,K & \to & X \\
\downarrow & & \downarrow_f \\
\mathrm{Spec}\,R & \to & Y
\end{array}
$$
admits a unique diagonal filling $\mathrm{Spec}\,R \to X$. For $X = X_\Sigma$ and $Y = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}$, we test all $\mathbb{C}$-morphisms $\mathrm{Spec}\,K \to X_\Sigma$.

**Step 1: reduce to torus-factored morphisms.** Since $T = U_{\{0\}} \subseteq X_\Sigma$ is dense and open, every morphism $\mathrm{Spec}\,K \to X_\Sigma$ with $K$ a field factors generically through some maximal cone's chart $U_\sigma$. The morphism factors through $T$ iff its image is in the dense torus — which is the generic case for an integral domain such as $K$. We handle this generic case; the case of morphisms with image in a proper closed subset reduces to a smaller toric variety (a closure of a $T$-orbit) and is handled by induction on the orbit stratification.

**Step 2: translate the morphism to a cocharacter.** A $\mathbb{C}$-morphism $\mathrm{Spec}\,K \to T = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M]$ corresponds to a $\mathbb{C}$-algebra map $\mathbb{C}[M] \to K$, equivalently a group homomorphism $\phi : M \to K^*$. Compose with the discrete valuation $v_R : K^* \to \mathbb{Z}$, $v_R(c) = $ the integer such that $c$ generates $\mathfrak{m}_R^{v_R(c)}$:
$$
v_\phi := v_R \circ \phi : M \to \mathbb{Z}.
$$
This is a group homomorphism $M \to \mathbb{Z}$, i.e., an element $v_\phi \in N = \mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(M, \mathbb{Z})$.

**Step 3: extension condition.** The morphism $\mathrm{Spec}\,K \to T \hookrightarrow X_\Sigma$ extends to $\mathrm{Spec}\,R \to X_\Sigma$ iff it factors through some affine chart $U_\sigma$ at the level of $R$, i.e., $\phi : \mathbb{C}[M] \to K$ restricts to a map $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \to R$.

This restriction is equivalent to: $\phi(\chi^m) \in R$ for every $m \in S_\sigma$, equivalently $v_R(\phi(\chi^m)) \geq 0$ for every $m \in S_\sigma = \sigma^\vee \cap M$. Since $v_R \circ \phi = v_\phi$ (the cocharacter from Step 2), this says $\langle m, v_\phi \rangle = v_\phi(m) \geq 0$ for every $m \in \sigma^\vee$, equivalently $v_\phi \in \sigma$ (by cone duality $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \sigma$).

Hence the extension exists iff $v_\phi \in \sigma$ for some $\sigma \in \Sigma$, iff $v_\phi \in |\Sigma|$.

**Step 4: range over all $v$.** As $R$ ranges over all DVRs containing $\mathbb{C}$ and $\phi$ ranges over all $\mathbb{C}$-morphisms $\mathrm{Spec}\,K \to T$, the cocharacter $v_\phi$ ranges over all elements of $N$ (every element $v \in N$ arises as $v_\phi$ for the homomorphism $\phi : M \to K^* = \mathbb{C}((t))^*$ defined by $\phi(m) = t^{v(m)}$, using the DVR $R = \mathbb{C}[[t]]$).

The valuative criterion holds for every choice iff every $v \in N$ lies in $|\Sigma|$, iff $|\Sigma| \supseteq N$. Since the cones of $\Sigma$ are closed in $N_\mathbb{R}$ and $N$ is dense in $N_\mathbb{R}$, this is equivalent to $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$.

**Step 5: uniqueness.** Uniqueness of the extension follows from the separatedness theorem in part (b) of the key theorem.

**Conclusion.** $X_\Sigma$ is proper over $\mathbb{C}$ iff $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$. Properness over $\mathbb{C}$ for a finite-type scheme is equivalent to compactness in the classical $\mathbb{C}$-topology (by GAGA and the Chow-Heisuke theorem), so $X_\Sigma$ is compact iff $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$. $\square$

Rubric: full credit for the cocharacter translation, the cone-membership characterisation of extendability, the range-over-$N$ argument, and the conclusion.

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove the **projectivity criterion**: the toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is projective if and only if $\Sigma$ is the **normal fan** of a lattice polytope $P \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$. Sketch the construction of an ample line bundle from $P$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The normal fan of a polytope $P$ is the fan in $N_\mathbb{R}$ whose maximal cones $\sigma_v$ are indexed by vertices $v$ of $P$, with $\sigma_v$ the closed cone of inward normals to $P$ at the vertex $v$. The polarisation line bundle $L_P$ is constructed from $P$ via the lattice-point basis of global sections.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Normal fan construction.** Let $P \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ be a lattice polytope (vertices in $M$). For each vertex $v$ of $P$, let
$$
\sigma_v := \{u \in N_\mathbb{R} : \langle m - v, u\rangle \geq 0 \text{ for all } m \in P\},
$$
the cone of inward normal directions to $P$ at $v$. The collection $\{\sigma_v : v \in P\}$, together with all their faces, is a fan in $N_\mathbb{R}$ called the **normal fan** $\Sigma_P$ of $P$.

The normal fan is **complete** ($|\Sigma_P| = N_\mathbb{R}$, since every direction in $N_\mathbb{R}$ is the inward normal to some face of $P$) and is determined uniquely by $P$ up to translation (translating $P$ does not change the inward normal cones at vertices).

**($\Leftarrow$): polytope gives projective.** Given a lattice polytope $P$ with normal fan $\Sigma = \Sigma_P$, we construct the polarisation line bundle $L_P$ on $X_\Sigma$ and show it is ample (hence $X_\Sigma$ is projective).

For each vertex $v \in P$, define a local section: on the chart $U_{\sigma_v}$, the local section is $\chi^v \in \mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma_v}]$. The transition between two charts $U_{\sigma_v} \to U_{\sigma_{v'}}$ on the overlap $U_{\sigma_v \cap \sigma_{v'}}$ uses the transition function $\chi^{v - v'}$ — this is well-defined on the overlap since $v - v' \in S_{\sigma_v \cap \sigma_{v'}}$ (the edge of $P$ from $v$ to $v'$ corresponds to the face $\sigma_v \cap \sigma_{v'}$ shared by the two normal cones).

The cocycle condition $\chi^{v - v'} \cdot \chi^{v' - v''} = \chi^{v - v''}$ holds tautologically, so the local sections define a line bundle $L_P$ on $X_\Sigma$.

**Sections of $L_P$.** The global sections $H^0(X_\Sigma, L_P)$ are parametrised by the lattice points $P \cap M$: each lattice point $m \in P \cap M$ defines a global section by $s_m|_{U_{\sigma_v}} = \chi^{m - v}$, with the cocycle $s_m|_{U_{\sigma_{v'}}} = \chi^{m - v'} = \chi^{m - v} \cdot \chi^{v - v'} = s_m|_{U_{\sigma_v}} \cdot (\text{transition})$, consistent.

The dimension $h^0(X_\Sigma, L_P) = |P \cap M|$ is the number of lattice points in $P$. The associated morphism $X_\Sigma \to \mathbb{P}^{|P \cap M| - 1}$ sending a point to $[s_{m_1}(x) : s_{m_2}(x) : \ldots]$ for the lattice points $m_i \in P \cap M$ is a closed embedding when $P$ is "very ample" — when $P$ has full $n$-dimensional interior and the lattice points $m_i - v$ for fixed vertex $v$ generate $S_{\sigma_v}$. Hence $L_P$ is very ample and $X_\Sigma$ is projective.

**($\Rightarrow$): projective gives polytope.** Conversely, suppose $X_\Sigma$ is projective with an ample $T$-equivariant line bundle $L$. The global sections $H^0(X_\Sigma, L)$ form a finite-dimensional $T$-representation, decomposing into character spaces $H^0(X_\Sigma, L)_m$ for $m \in M$. The set of $m$ such that the character space is nonzero is a finite subset of $M$, and its convex hull is a lattice polytope $P_L \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$.

By the theory of toric line bundles (`[04.11.09]`), the ampleness of $L$ implies that the vertex cones of $P_L$ are exactly the maximal cones of $\Sigma$ (the support function of $L$ is strictly convex on the fan, and its Newton polytope is the dual polytope of $\Sigma$). Hence $\Sigma$ is the normal fan of $P_L$.

**Conclusion.** $X_\Sigma$ is projective iff $\Sigma$ is the normal fan of a lattice polytope. The polarisation $L_P$ is the line bundle whose Newton polytope is $P$. $\square$

Rubric: full credit for the normal fan construction, the construction of $L_P$ via the cocycle, the lattice-point basis of sections, and the converse via Newton polytopes of ample line bundles.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (functoriality of the fan-to-toric construction).** *The assignment $\Sigma \mapsto X_\Sigma$ extends to a functor from the category $\mathbf{Fan}$ of fans (with **fan morphisms** $(N, \Sigma) \to (N', \Sigma')$: lattice maps $\phi : N \to N'$ such that for every $\sigma \in \Sigma$ there exists $\sigma' \in \Sigma'$ with $\phi_\mathbb{R}(\sigma) \subseteq \sigma'$) to the category of normal $T$-toric varieties (with $T$-equivariant morphisms). The functor is fully faithful and essentially surjective (by Sumihiro 1974), making it an equivalence of categories.*

The functoriality theorem packages the fan-to-toric correspondence as an equivalence of categories. The morphism side is the content of `[04.11.06]` (toric morphisms): a lattice map $\phi : N \to N'$ that sends each cone $\sigma$ into some cone $\sigma'$ induces a $T$-equivariant morphism $X_\Sigma \to X_{\Sigma'}$, with the morphism on the affine pieces $U_\sigma \to U_{\sigma'}$ given by the comorphism on semigroup algebras $\mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma'}] \to \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ dual to $\phi^* : (\sigma')^\vee \cap M' \to \sigma^\vee \cap M$. The essential surjectivity is Sumihiro's classification: every normal $T$-toric variety arises from a fan, with the fan recovered from the equivariant affine cover.

**Theorem (Sumihiro's equivariant covering, refined).** *Let $X$ be a normal algebraic variety over $\mathbb{C}$ with an action of an algebraic torus $T$ such that there is a dense $T$-orbit. Then $X$ admits a unique-up-to-lattice-isomorphism fan $\Sigma$ such that $X \cong X_\Sigma$ $T$-equivariantly.*

Sumihiro's 1974 theorem in its refined form for toric varieties (Journal of Mathematics of Kyoto University 14, 1-28, with refinement in Sumihiro 1975 *Equivariant completion II*) is the foundational classification theorem of toric geometry. It identifies the category of normal toric varieties with the category of fans on free $\mathbb{Z}$-modules. The proof has two parts: (i) every normal $T$-variety admits a $T$-equivariant affine open cover (general Sumihiro), and (ii) for normal toric varieties (with dense $T$-orbit), each affine piece is an affine toric variety $U_\sigma$ for a unique cone $\sigma$ (classification of normal affine $T$-varieties with dense orbit, due to Demazure 1970). Combining these gives the fan as the collection of cones from the affine cover, with faces and intersections coming from the gluing data.

**Theorem (projectivity ↔ normal fan of a polytope).** *Let $\Sigma$ be a complete fan in $N_\mathbb{R}$. Then $X_\Sigma$ is projective if and only if $\Sigma$ is the normal fan of a full-dimensional lattice polytope $P \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$. In this case, $P$ is unique up to lattice translation, and the polarisation line bundle $L_P$ on $X_\Sigma$ has global sections $H^0(X_\Sigma, L_P) = \bigoplus_{m \in P \cap M} \mathbb{C} \chi^m$ with basis the lattice points of $P$.*

This is the **polytope-fan correspondence** on the projective side, proven in part above (Exercise 8) and developed in detail in `[04.11.10]` (Fulton §3.4; Cox-Little-Schenck §6.1). The bijection between polytopes and projective toric varieties is the algebraic side of the **Delzant correspondence** in symplectic geometry (Delzant 1988), which classifies symplectic toric manifolds by their moment polytopes. The two correspondences coincide on the projective-symplectic-toric overlap, and the moment map $\mu : X_\Sigma \to P$ identifies the polytope with the image of the compact-torus action's moment map.

**Theorem (toric resolution; Demazure 1970, KKMS 1973).** *Every toric variety $X_\Sigma$ admits a $T$-equivariant resolution of singularities $X_{\Sigma'} \to X_\Sigma$, where $\Sigma'$ is a smooth refinement of $\Sigma$ obtained by iterated star subdivision. The resolution is proper, birational, and constructive: a finite algorithm produces $\Sigma'$ from $\Sigma$ by subdividing non-smooth cones along interior lattice points.*

The toric resolution theorem is the combinatorial form of Hironaka's theorem on resolution of singularities (Hironaka 1964, *Annals of Mathematics* 79, 109-326), and is **algorithmically explicit** in the toric setting. For surfaces, the toric resolution is the **Hirzebruch-Jung resolution** via continued-fraction expansion of the singularity's index (Hirzebruch 1953, *Mathematische Annalen* 126, 1-22). For higher dimensions, the algorithm is more elaborate but still combinatorial (Knudsen 1973, Mumford 1976). The toric setting was the first context where resolution of singularities was understood algorithmically — a fact that motivated much of the development of toric geometry as a testbed for general resolution conjectures.

**Theorem (intersection theory on toric varieties; Danilov 1978, Fulton-Sturmfels 1997).** *For a smooth complete toric variety $X_\Sigma$, the Chow ring $A^*(X_\Sigma) = \bigoplus_{k} A^k(X_\Sigma)$ is generated by the classes of toric divisors $D_\rho$ for $\rho \in \Sigma(1)$, with relations the **linear equivalence** (from the exact sequence $M \to \mathbb{Z}^{\Sigma(1)} \to \mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma) \to 0$) and the **Stanley-Reisner relations** $D_{\rho_1} \cdots D_{\rho_k} = 0$ for $\{\rho_1, \ldots, \rho_k\}$ not generating a cone of $\Sigma$.*

The Chow-ring formula for smooth complete toric varieties is the **Stanley-Reisner ring** of the fan $\Sigma$, modulo linear relations from $M$. Danilov's 1978 survey (*Russian Math. Surveys* 33, 97-154) established this for the rational-coefficient Chow ring; Fulton-Sturmfels 1997 (*Topology* 36, 335-353) extended it to integer coefficients and gave a combinatorial proof via the **localisation theorem** for $T$-equivariant cohomology (Atiyah-Bott 1984, *Topology* 23, 1-28; Goresky-Kottwitz-MacPherson 1998, *Inventiones Mathematicae* 131, 25-83). The Stanley-Reisner description makes cohomology computations on toric varieties effective and combinatorial, including Hirzebruch-Riemann-Roch (Brion-Vergne 1997).

**Theorem (Cox-quotient presentation; Cox 1995).** *Let $X_\Sigma$ be a toric variety with $\mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma)$ free abelian. Then $X_\Sigma$ is the GIT quotient*
$$
X_\Sigma = (\mathbb{C}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma)) / G,
$$
*where $G = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma), \mathbb{C}^*)$ acts on $\mathbb{C}^{\Sigma(1)}$ via the exact sequence $0 \to M \to \mathbb{Z}^{\Sigma(1)} \to \mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma) \to 0$, and $Z(\Sigma)$ is the **irrelevant locus** $\bigcup_{C \notin \Sigma} V(C)$ for $C$ ranging over coordinate subspaces.*

Cox's 1995 paper (*Journal of Algebraic Geometry* 4, 17-50) reframed toric geometry through the **homogeneous coordinate ring** $\mathbb{C}[x_\rho : \rho \in \Sigma(1)]$ — a polynomial ring with one variable per ray of the fan, graded by $\mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma)$. The toric variety $X_\Sigma$ becomes a GIT quotient of an affine space by a torus, with the quotient stratification controlled by the fan combinatorics. This perspective unifies projective and toric geometry under a single functorial framework and has been the foundation for modern toric mirror symmetry (Batyrev-Borisov 1996), Cox-ring methods in algebraic geometry (Hu-Keel 2000, *Michigan Math. Journal* 48, 331-348), and computational toric algorithms (Macaulay2, Polymake, SageMath).

**Synthesis.** The toric variety from a fan $X_\Sigma$ is the combinatorial bridge from convex geometry to global algebraic geometry, and the central insight is that the fan $\Sigma$ — a finite collection of cones in $N_\mathbb{R}$ subject to face-closure and intersection-as-face axioms — encodes a global normal $T$-variety whose every geometric property reads off from the cones. Three structural results — gluing existence (the cocycle condition holds tautologically), separatedness (semigroup-algebra surjectivity at intersections), and Sumihiro's classification (every normal $T$-toric variety arises this way) — combine into a single equivalence of categories: fans $\leftrightarrow$ normal $T$-toric varieties. The bridge is exactly the duality of `[04.11.01]`: the fan in $N_\mathbb{R}$ encodes which characters of $T$ extend to regular functions on which open piece of $X_\Sigma$, the dual cones $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ supply the local coordinate rings, and the global variety is assembled from the local pictures along face inclusions.

Putting these together, the four criteria — separatedness, completeness, smoothness, projectivity — fully classify the geometric type of $X_\Sigma$ in terms of $\Sigma$: separatedness holds automatically for fans (axiom (ii)), completeness iff $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$, smoothness iff every cone is simplicial unimodular, projectivity iff $\Sigma$ is the normal fan of a polytope. The foundational reason this **combinatorial classification is exhaustive** is Sumihiro's equivariant-covering theorem: every normal $T$-variety with a dense orbit is captured by a fan, so no normal toric variety escapes the combinatorial framework. This is exactly what makes toric geometry an effectively combinatorial laboratory for global algebraic geometry, generalises the affine-local picture of `[04.11.03]` to a global picture, and is dual to the local-to-global passage from cones to fans.

The fan-to-toric construction generalises in three directions worth recording. To **non-simplicial fans** with non-rational cones, the construction $X_\Sigma$ may still exist as an algebraic space or stack rather than a scheme — the regime of **toroidal embeddings** (KKMS 1973) and **logarithmic toric stacks** (Olsson 2008). To **infinite fans** with countably many cones, the construction extends to **ind-toric varieties** used in affine Grassmannians and infinite-dimensional Lie theory (Kac-Moody groups, Mathieu 1988). To **arbitrary base schemes** $S$ instead of $\mathbb{C}$, the construction $X_\Sigma / S$ produces **toric schemes over $S$**, specialising to toric varieties over each fibre and degenerating to combinatorial Stanley-Reisner schemes over $\mathbb{Z}$; this is the foundation for **integral models** of toric varieties in arithmetic geometry, used by Demazure and Grothendieck in the development of reductive group schemes. The pattern recurs — each generalisation preserves the cone-to-variety dictionary developed here, with technical modifications recording the deviation from the rational case over $\mathbb{C}$.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (separatedness of $X_\Sigma$), proof.** Given in Exercise 4: for any two maximal cones $\sigma, \tau \in \Sigma$, the face-correspondence theorem of `[04.11.02]` gives $(\sigma \cap \tau)^\vee = \sigma^\vee + \tau^\vee$ (Minkowski sum), hence $S_{\sigma \cap \tau} = S_\sigma + S_\tau$; the multiplication map $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \otimes \mathbb{C}[S_\tau] \to \mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma \cap \tau}]$ is surjective, dualising to a closed immersion of $\mathrm{Spec}$, which is the diagonal $U_{\sigma \cap \tau} \to U_\sigma \times U_\tau$; gluing these closed immersions gives the diagonal of $X_\Sigma$, so $X_\Sigma$ is separated. $\square$

**Proposition (completeness criterion), proof.** Given in Exercise 7: the valuative criterion applied to torus-valued points translates a $\mathrm{Spec}\,K \to T$ to a cocharacter $v \in N$; the extension to $\mathrm{Spec}\,R \to X_\Sigma$ exists iff $v$ lies in some cone of $\Sigma$, iff $v \in |\Sigma|$; ranging over all $v \in N$ (achievable by varying the DVR $R$), properness holds iff $|\Sigma| \supseteq N$, iff $|\Sigma| = N_\mathbb{R}$. $\square$

**Proposition (smoothness criterion), proof.** Given in part (d) of the main theorem: smoothness is local, $X_\Sigma$ is smooth iff every chart $U_\sigma$ is smooth, and $U_\sigma$ is smooth iff $\sigma$ is simplicial unimodular by the smoothness criterion of `[04.11.03]` (Demazure 1970). Hence $X_\Sigma$ is smooth iff every cone is simplicial unimodular. $\square$

**Proposition (projectivity criterion), proof.** Given in Exercise 8: ($\Leftarrow$) The normal fan $\Sigma_P$ of a lattice polytope $P$ gives a complete fan; the polarisation line bundle $L_P$ has lattice-point basis $H^0(X_\Sigma, L_P) = \bigoplus_{m \in P \cap M} \mathbb{C} \chi^m$, and when $P$ has full $n$-dimensional interior the associated morphism $X_\Sigma \to \mathbb{P}^{|P \cap M| - 1}$ is a closed embedding. ($\Rightarrow$) Conversely, an ample $T$-equivariant line bundle on $X_\Sigma$ has Newton polytope $P_L$ a lattice polytope, and the vertex cones of $P_L$ recover the maximal cones of $\Sigma$, exhibiting $\Sigma$ as the normal fan of $P_L$. $\square$

**Proposition (Sumihiro's classification), proof sketch.** *Let $X$ be a normal algebraic variety over $\mathbb{C}$ with a $T$-action having a dense open orbit. Then there is a fan $\Sigma$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$, unique up to lattice isomorphism, with $X \cong X_\Sigma$ $T$-equivariantly.*

*Proof.* By Sumihiro's general equivariant covering theorem (1974, Journal of Mathematics of Kyoto University 14, 1-28), $X$ admits a $T$-equivariant cover by $T$-stable affine open subvarieties $\{V_i\}$. Each $V_i$ is normal (subvariety of $X$), affine (by construction), and has a $T$-action; the dense $T$-orbit of $X$ restricts to a dense $T$-orbit of $V_i$ (since $V_i$ is open and contains points of the dense orbit). Hence each $V_i$ is a normal affine $T$-variety with dense $T$-orbit.

By the classification of affine toric varieties from `[04.11.03]` (Demazure 1970), every normal affine $T$-variety with dense orbit is of the form $U_\sigma$ for a unique strongly convex rational polyhedral cone $\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ (where $N$ is the cocharacter lattice of $T$). Hence $V_i = U_{\sigma_i}$ for some cone $\sigma_i$.

For the overlaps: $V_i \cap V_j$ is open in both $V_i$ and $V_j$, $T$-stable, and normal — hence also a normal affine $T$-variety with dense orbit (the dense orbit of $X$ restricts to a dense orbit of the overlap). By the classification, $V_i \cap V_j = U_{\tau_{ij}}$ for some cone $\tau_{ij}$. The two open immersions $U_{\tau_{ij}} \hookrightarrow U_{\sigma_i}$ and $U_{\tau_{ij}} \hookrightarrow U_{\sigma_j}$ correspond (by face-functoriality, `[04.11.03]`) to face inclusions $\tau_{ij} \hookrightarrow \sigma_i$ and $\tau_{ij} \hookrightarrow \sigma_j$. The cone $\tau_{ij}$ is therefore a common face of $\sigma_i$ and $\sigma_j$, and by uniqueness of the affine cone for a normal affine $T$-variety with dense orbit, $\tau_{ij} = \sigma_i \cap \sigma_j$ (intersection as cones in $N_\mathbb{R}$).

The collection $\Sigma := \{\text{all faces of } \sigma_i \text{ for all } i\}$ is closed under faces (by definition) and intersection (since $\sigma_i \cap \sigma_j$ is a common face, hence belongs to $\Sigma$). Hence $\Sigma$ is a fan, and the gluing of the $V_i$ along the overlaps $V_i \cap V_j = U_{\sigma_i \cap \sigma_j}$ produces $X = X_\Sigma$.

Uniqueness: the cones $\sigma_i$ are determined by the closed $T$-fixed points of $X$ (each $\sigma_i$ has its unique $T$-fixed point at the closed orbit) and the orbit structure (orbits in bijection with faces of $\sigma_i$, orbit-cone correspondence `[04.11.05]`). A $T$-equivariant isomorphism $X \cong X'$ induces an isomorphism of cocharacter lattices $N \cong N'$ and a bijection of cones $\Sigma \cong \Sigma'$ preserving the face structure — that is, a fan isomorphism. $\square$

**Proposition (functoriality $\Sigma \mapsto X_\Sigma$), proof.** *The assignment $\Sigma \mapsto X_\Sigma$ extends to a functor $\mathbf{Fan} \to \mathbf{TorVar}$ to the category of normal $T$-toric varieties, with morphisms induced by lattice maps respecting cones.*

*Proof.* Given a fan morphism $\phi : (N, \Sigma) \to (N', \Sigma')$ (a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear map $\phi : N \to N'$ such that for every $\sigma \in \Sigma$ there exists $\sigma' \in \Sigma'$ with $\phi_\mathbb{R}(\sigma) \subseteq \sigma'$), construct the toric morphism $X_\phi : X_\Sigma \to X_{\Sigma'}$ on each affine piece by the comorphism on semigroup algebras
$$
\phi^* : \mathbb{C}[S_{\sigma'}] \to \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma], \qquad \chi^{m'} \mapsto \chi^{\phi^*(m')},
$$
where $\phi^* : M' \to M$ is the dual lattice map. The map $\phi^*$ sends $S_{\sigma'} = (\sigma')^\vee \cap M'$ into $S_\sigma = \sigma^\vee \cap M$ by the condition $\phi_\mathbb{R}(\sigma) \subseteq \sigma'$: for $m' \in (\sigma')^\vee$, $\langle m', \phi_\mathbb{R}(u)\rangle \geq 0$ for $u \in \sigma$, equivalently $\langle \phi^*(m'), u\rangle \geq 0$, equivalently $\phi^*(m') \in \sigma^\vee$.

The local morphisms $U_\sigma \to U_{\sigma'}$ are compatible with face inclusions (since $\phi_\mathbb{R}$ preserves face containment by linearity), so they glue to a global morphism $X_\phi : X_\Sigma \to X_{\Sigma'}$. The functor laws (identity, composition) follow from the corresponding laws on lattice maps. $T$-equivariance follows from the $M$-grading being preserved by $\phi^*$.

The functor is **faithful** (lattice maps are determined by their action on $M$, recoverable from the morphism of varieties via the $T$-character group of the open orbit) and **full** (every $T$-equivariant morphism $X_\Sigma \to X_{\Sigma'}$ arises from a fan morphism, by the classification of $T$-equivariant morphisms between normal toric varieties — Cox-Little-Schenck Theorem 3.3.4). Hence the functor is fully faithful.

**Essential surjectivity** is Sumihiro's classification: every normal $T$-toric variety arises as $X_\Sigma$ for some fan. Combined with full faithfulness, the functor is an equivalence of categories. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Affine toric variety [04.11.03].** The local building blocks of $X_\Sigma$. Each cone $\sigma \in \Sigma$ contributes an affine chart $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$, and the face-functoriality $\tau \subseteq \sigma \Rightarrow U_\tau \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$ developed in the prerequisite unit supplies the open immersions along which the affine pieces glue. The toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is exactly the colimit of these affine pieces along their face-inclusion morphisms. Sumihiro's classification reduces the global toric problem to the affine case by the equivariant covering theorem, making `[04.11.03]` the technical core of this unit.

- **Rational polyhedral cone and dual cone [04.11.02].** The combinatorial input of the fan-to-toric construction. The face-correspondence theorem of the prerequisite unit gives $(\sigma \cap \tau)^\vee = \sigma^\vee + \tau^\vee$, which is the foundational reason that the diagonal $U_{\sigma \cap \tau} \to U_\sigma \times U_\tau$ is a closed immersion — the central step in the separatedness theorem for $X_\Sigma$. Gordan's lemma supplies finite generation of the dual semigroups, hence finite-type-ness of $X_\Sigma$.

- **Algebraic torus and character/cocharacter lattices [04.11.01].** The torus $T = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M]$ acts on $X_\Sigma$ with a dense open orbit equal to $T$ itself (the chart $U_{\{0\}}$ corresponding to the zero cone, shared by every $U_\sigma$). The character lattice $M$ encodes the regular functions on $X_\Sigma$ that are $T$-semi-invariant — namely those characters $m \in M$ such that $\chi^m$ extends to a regular function on $U_\sigma$ for some $\sigma$, equivalently $m \in \sigma^\vee \cap M$ for that cone. The cocharacter lattice $N$ encodes the one-parameter subgroups of $T$, whose limit behaviour in $X_\Sigma$ records the orbit-cone correspondence of `[04.11.05]`.

- **Projective space [04.07.01].** The most fundamental toric variety beyond affine space: $\mathbb{P}^n = X_{\Sigma_{\mathbb{P}^n}}$ for the fan $\Sigma_{\mathbb{P}^n}$ with $n + 1$ rays in $\mathbb{R}^n$ (the standard basis together with $-e_1 - \cdots - e_n$) and all maximal cones spanned by proper subsets of these rays. The standard affine cover of $\mathbb{P}^n$ by $n + 1$ copies of $\mathbb{C}^n$ is recovered as the affine cover $\{U_\sigma\}$ for the maximal cones of $\Sigma_{\mathbb{P}^n}$. The Segre embedding of $\mathbb{P}^a \times \mathbb{P}^b$ corresponds to the product fan, illustrating the general pattern that products of toric varieties correspond to products of fans.

- **Orbit-cone correspondence [04.11.05].** The next unit in the toric chapter: $T$-orbits in $X_\Sigma$ are in inclusion-reversing bijection with cones of $\Sigma$, with the orbit corresponding to a cone $\sigma$ of dimension $k$ having dimension $n - k$. The orbit-closure stratification of $X_\Sigma$ matches the face-inclusion stratification of $\Sigma$. The proof uses the face-functoriality of `[04.11.03]` together with the gluing developed here to identify orbits as locally affine pieces of the global toric variety.

- **Toric resolution of singularities [04.11.07].** Every toric variety $X_\Sigma$ admits a $T$-equivariant resolution $X_{\Sigma'} \to X_\Sigma$ by smooth refinement of the fan via iterated star subdivision (Demazure 1970, KKMS 1973). The combinatorial nature of toric resolution — algorithmic where Hironaka's general resolution is non-constructive — made toric geometry the first testbed for resolution-of-singularities conjectures, and remains a working laboratory for understanding the geometry of birational maps. For toric surfaces, the resolution is the Hirzebruch-Jung continued-fraction algorithm.

- **Polytope-fan correspondence [04.11.10].** Projective toric varieties are classified by lattice polytopes via $P \mapsto \Sigma_P$ (normal fan) and $\Sigma \mapsto P$ (Newton polytope of an ample line bundle). This is the algebraic side of the **Delzant correspondence** on the symplectic side (Delzant 1988), and the polytope $P$ records both the toric variety and an ample polarisation simultaneously. The lattice points $P \cap M$ are an explicit basis of global sections of the polarisation $L_P$, making polytopes a working tool for computing cohomology of projective toric varieties.

- **Scheme [04.02.01] and affine scheme [04.02.02].** The toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is a separated normal scheme of finite type over $\mathbb{C}$, with the standard scheme-theoretic structure inherited from the gluing of affine schemes $U_\sigma$. The gluing lemma for schemes (Grothendieck's EGA I §0.4 or Hartshorne Ex. II.2.12) is the foundational tool for the fan-to-toric construction, and the cocycle condition is verified automatically by the fan axioms together with the localisation structure of the affine pieces from `[04.11.03]`.

- **Resolution of singularities [04.06.02].** The toric resolution theorem (every $X_\Sigma$ admits a $T$-equivariant resolution by fan refinement) is the toric implementation of Hironaka's 1964 theorem on resolution of singularities over fields of characteristic zero. The toric case is **algorithmic**: a constructive procedure produces the smooth refinement in finitely many steps, whereas Hironaka's general proof is a non-constructive Noetherian-induction argument. The toric setting was the first context where resolution was understood algorithmically, and the algorithmic perspective has since motivated significant research on constructive resolution (Bierstone-Milman 1997, Encinas-Villamayor 2003, Włodarczyk 2005).

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The toric variety from a fan was first constructed in modern form by Michel Demazure in *Sous-groupes algébriques de rang maximum du groupe de Cremona* (Annales scientifiques de l'École normale supérieure, 4e série, tome 3, 1970, pp. 507-588) [ref: TODO_REF Demazure 1970]. Demazure's motivation was the classification of algebraic subgroups of the Cremona group $\mathrm{Cr}_n = \mathrm{Bir}(\mathbb{P}^n)$ of maximal rank, and the fan-to-toric construction arose as the combinatorial language for enumerating the $T$-equivariant compactifications of the torus $T = (\mathbb{C}^*)^n$. Demazure's paper established the gluing construction, the smoothness criterion (every cone simplicial unimodular), and the basic functoriality on fan morphisms. The term **"espace torique"** ("toric space" in English) is due to Demazure.

The scheme-theoretic foundations were systematised by George Kempf, Finn Faye Knudsen, David Mumford, and Bernard Saint-Donat in *Toroidal Embeddings I* (Lecture Notes in Mathematics 339, Springer 1973) [ref: TODO_REF], where the fan formalism was recast as a tool for resolving singularities and compactifying equivariant varieties over arbitrary base schemes. KKMS's "toroidal embedding" is the local-étale model for varieties that look étale-locally like toric varieties; the global toric setting (where the variety is genuinely a fan-gluing) is the rigid combinatorial special case. The KKMS volume also established the **toric resolution theorem** — every fan admits a smooth refinement obtained by iterated star subdivision — making toric geometry the first testbed for resolution-of-singularities algorithms.

Hideyasu Sumihiro's *Equivariant completion* (Journal of Mathematics of Kyoto University 14, 1974, pp. 1-28) [ref: TODO_REF Sumihiro 1974] proved the foundational equivariant-covering theorem: every normal $T$-variety admits a $T$-equivariant affine open cover. Applied to normal toric varieties, this classifies them as gluings $X_\Sigma$ from fans, completing the bijection between fans and normal toric varieties. Sumihiro's paper is the bridge from the affine-local picture of Demazure to the global classification — without Sumihiro's theorem, one would have only the affine-local picture; with it, every normal toric variety is captured by a fan. Sumihiro's 1975 follow-up *Equivariant completion II* (Journal of Mathematics of Kyoto University 15, 573-605) extended the theorem to non-toric reductive-group actions.

The Japanese-school perspective was consolidated by Tadao Oda in *Convex Bodies and Algebraic Geometry* (Ergebnisse 15, Springer 1988) [ref: TODO_REF], an authoritative monograph emphasising the dual-cone formalism, the global toric construction, the orbit-cone correspondence, and the polytope-fan dictionary. Oda's book is the canonical mid-1980s reference and was the standard text until Fulton's 1993 volume superseded it for many uses. Vasily Danilov's 1978 survey *The geometry of toric varieties* (Russian Mathematical Surveys 33(2), 97-154) [ref: TODO_REF Danilov 1978] is the canonical pre-Fulton English survey, explicitly cited by Fulton as the immediate predecessor; Danilov's article also established the Stanley-Reisner description of toric cohomology.

William Fulton's *Introduction to Toric Varieties* (Princeton 1993) [ref: TODO_REF Fulton 1993] is the canonical short textbook of modern toric geometry, organising the cone-fan-variety dictionary into a coherent narrative covering smoothness, completeness, projectivity, divisors, line bundles, cohomology, and the polytope correspondence. David Cox, John Little, and Henry Schenck's *Toric Varieties* (American Mathematical Society 2011) [ref: TODO_REF] is the modern thousand-page treatment, extending Fulton with additional material on Cox-quotients, toric mirror symmetry, GKZ-systems, and arithmetic-geometric applications.

The fan-to-toric construction took its modern functorial form in David Cox's 1995 paper *The homogeneous coordinate ring of a toric variety* (Journal of Algebraic Geometry 4, 17-50) [ref: TODO_REF Cox 1995], where the **Cox quotient** $X_\Sigma = (\mathbb{C}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma)) / G$ presents the toric variety as a GIT quotient of an affine space by a torus. This perspective unifies projective and toric geometry under a common functorial framework and has been the foundation for modern toric mirror symmetry (Batyrev-Borisov 1996), Cox-ring methods in algebraic geometry, and computational toric algorithms in Macaulay2, Polymake, and SageMath.

Friedrich Hirzebruch's introduction of the surfaces $F_a$ in *Über eine Klasse von einfach-zusammenhängenden komplexen Mannigfaltigkeiten* (Mathematische Annalen 124, 1951, pp. 77-86) [ref: TODO_REF Hirzebruch 1951] predates the toric formalism by two decades but was identified as a toric example by Demazure (1970), illustrating the general pattern that classical examples from algebraic surface theory often have toric models with explicit fan presentations. The toric interpretation of the Hirzebruch surfaces $F_a = X_{\Sigma_a}$ (Exercise 3) is now a standard fixture of introductory toric-variety pedagogy.
