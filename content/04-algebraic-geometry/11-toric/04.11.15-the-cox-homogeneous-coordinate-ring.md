## Intuition [Beginner]

Projective space has a beautiful one-line description: a point of $\mathbb{P}^n$ is a list of homogeneous coordinates $[x_0 : \ldots : x_n]$, not all zero, where two lists count as the same point when one is a nonzero scalar multiple of the other. In other words $\mathbb{P}^n$ is the space $\mathbb{A}^{n+1}$ with the origin removed, then collapsed by the rule "rescale everything at once." A single polynomial ring in $n+1$ variables, plus one scaling rule, describes the whole variety.

David Cox discovered in 1995 that **every** toric variety has a description of exactly this shape. Recall from the fan unit that a toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is built from a fan, a fan-out of cones whose one-dimensional edges are called rays. Cox's idea: introduce one variable $x_\rho$ for each ray $\rho$. These variables generate a single polynomial ring $S$, the **total coordinate ring** (also called the Cox ring). It is the toric replacement for the homogeneous coordinate ring of projective space.

The scaling rule for $\mathbb{P}^n$ was one copy of $\mathbb{C}^*$ acting by simultaneous multiplication. For a general toric variety the rule is richer: a group $G$ acts on the variable space, and $G$ is read off from the geometry of the fan. The toric variety is then the space of variable-tuples, minus a forbidden set, collapsed by the action of $G$.

So the slogan is short. One polynomial ring, one clever grading, one group action: a whole toric variety presented as a single quotient, just like projective space.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Build the projective plane $\mathbb{P}^2$ as a Cox quotient. The fan of $\mathbb{P}^2$ has three rays, with primitive generators $u_0 = -e_1 - e_2$, $u_1 = e_1$, $u_2 = e_2$, as recalled in the fan unit. One variable per ray gives three variables $x_0, x_1, x_2$, so the total coordinate ring is $S = \mathbb{C}[x_0, x_1, x_2]$.

The grading. The three rays satisfy one relation, $u_0 + u_1 + u_2 = 0$. This single relation says that the three variables share **one** common scaling degree. So $S$ is graded by $\mathbb{Z}$, with each variable in degree one, and a monomial $x_0^{a} x_1^{b} x_2^{c}$ sits in degree $a + b + c$. This is the ordinary grading of the homogeneous coordinate ring of $\mathbb{P}^2$.

The forbidden set. The rule is that we delete the locus where the coordinates fail to lie in any cone of the fan. For $\mathbb{P}^2$ this deleted locus is the single point $x_0 = x_1 = x_2 = 0$, the origin of $\mathbb{A}^3$. So we work on $\mathbb{A}^3 \setminus 0$.

The group. One $\mathbb{Z}$ grading direction corresponds to one copy of $\mathbb{C}^*$. The group $G = \mathbb{C}^*$ acts by $t \cdot (x_0, x_1, x_2) = (t x_0, t x_1, t x_2)$, simultaneous scaling.

The result. The quotient $(\mathbb{A}^3 \setminus 0)/\mathbb{C}^*$ is the standard construction of $\mathbb{P}^2$. The Cox machine has reproduced homogeneous coordinates on $\mathbb{P}^2$, with $x_0, x_1, x_2$ the familiar coordinates. What the machine adds is that the recipe — one variable per ray, grade by the relations among rays, delete a forbidden set, collapse by the matching group — works for **any** toric variety, not only projective space.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X_\Sigma$ be the toric variety of a fan $\Sigma$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$, as constructed in `[04.11.04]`, with $\Sigma(1)$ the set of rays and $u_\rho \in N$ the primitive generator of $\rho$. Write $M = \mathrm{Hom}(N, \mathbb{Z})$ for the character lattice.

**Definition (total coordinate ring).** The **total coordinate ring** (or **Cox ring**) of $X_\Sigma$ is the polynomial ring
$$
S = \mathbb{C}[x_\rho : \rho \in \Sigma(1)],
$$
one variable per ray. A monomial $x^a = \prod_\rho x_\rho^{a_\rho}$ corresponds to the divisor $D_a = \sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$ in the group $\mathbb{Z}^{\Sigma(1)} = \bigoplus_\rho \mathbb{Z} D_\rho$ of $T$-invariant Weil divisors of `[04.11.08]`.

**Definition (the class-group grading).** Assume $X_\Sigma$ has no torus factor, so that the rays span $N_\mathbb{R}$. The divisor-class exact sequence of `[04.11.08]`,
$$
0 \to M \xrightarrow{\ \mathrm{div}\ } \mathbb{Z}^{\Sigma(1)} \xrightarrow{\ \pi\ } \mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma) \to 0, \qquad m \mapsto \sum_\rho \langle m, u_\rho\rangle D_\rho,
$$
defines a grading of $S$ by the class group: the monomial $x^a$ has degree $\deg(x^a) := \pi(D_a) = [D_a] \in \mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$. This makes $S = \bigoplus_{\beta \in \mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)} S_\beta$ a $\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$-graded ring, with $S_\beta = \bigoplus_{[D_a] = \beta} \mathbb{C} \, x^a$.

**Definition (irrelevant ideal).** For each cone $\sigma \in \Sigma$, write $x^{\hat\sigma} = \prod_{\rho \notin \sigma(1)} x_\rho$ for the product of the variables of the rays **not** in $\sigma$. The **irrelevant ideal** is
$$
B(\Sigma) = \big\langle x^{\hat\sigma} : \sigma \in \Sigma \big\rangle \subseteq S,
$$
generated by these monomials (it suffices to range over maximal cones). Its vanishing locus $Z(\Sigma) = V(B(\Sigma)) \subseteq \mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} = \mathrm{Spec}\,S$ is the **exceptional set** (or **irrelevant locus**). Concretely a point lies in $Z(\Sigma)$ when the set of rays $\{\rho : x_\rho = 0\}$ fails to be contained in the rays of any single cone of $\Sigma$.

**Definition (the quotient group).** Applying $\mathrm{Hom}(-, \mathbb{C}^*)$ to the exact sequence and using $\mathrm{Hom}(M, \mathbb{C}^*) = T$ gives
$$
1 \to G \to (\mathbb{C}^*)^{\Sigma(1)} \to T \to 1, \qquad G = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma), \mathbb{C}^*).
$$
The group $G$ is a diagonalisable group (a product of a torus and a finite abelian group), and it acts on $\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)}$ as the subgroup of the diagonal torus $(\mathbb{C}^*)^{\Sigma(1)}$ that maps to the identity of $T$. Explicitly $g = (g_\rho) \in G$ iff $\prod_\rho g_\rho^{\langle m, u_\rho\rangle} = 1$ for all $m \in M$.

**Definition (the Cox quotient).** The **quotient presentation** of $X_\Sigma$ is
$$
X_\Sigma \;=\; \big(\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma)\big) /\!\!/ G,
$$
the **categorical quotient** of the open set $\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma)$ by the action of $G$. When $\Sigma$ is simplicial this is a **geometric quotient** (the orbits are closed in the open set and the quotient is an orbit space); the quotient is identified with a GIT quotient in the sense of `[04.10.02]` for a suitable character linearisation. The variables $x_\rho$ are the **homogeneous coordinates** on $X_\Sigma$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- "The grading is always by $\mathbb{Z}$." Only when $\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, as for $\mathbb{P}^n$. For $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ the class group is $\mathbb{Z}^2$, so the grading is bigraded; for a singular toric surface the class group can carry torsion, and the grading lands in a group with a finite part.

- "The forbidden set is always the origin." Only for fans like that of $\mathbb{P}^n$ where every proper coordinate subspace meets a cone. For $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ the exceptional set $Z(\Sigma)$ is the union of two coordinate planes, $\{x_1 = x_2 = 0\} \cup \{x_3 = x_4 = 0\}$, not a single point.

- "The quotient is always geometric." For non-simplicial $\Sigma$ the action of $G$ has non-closed orbits, and the construction yields only a categorical (good) quotient that identifies non-separated orbits; the geometric-quotient statement needs the simplicial hypothesis.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Cox quotient presentation; Cox 1995).** *Let $X_\Sigma$ be the toric variety of a fan $\Sigma$ whose rays span $N_\mathbb{R}$, with total coordinate ring $S$, irrelevant ideal $B(\Sigma)$, exceptional set $Z(\Sigma) = V(B(\Sigma))$, and group $G = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma), \mathbb{C}^*)$.*

*(a) **Quotient.** There is a canonical morphism $\pi : \mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma) \to X_\Sigma$ that is a good categorical quotient for the action of $G$. When $\Sigma$ is simplicial, $\pi$ is a geometric quotient, and $X_\Sigma$ is the orbit space.*

*(b) **GIT.** The quotient $\pi$ agrees with the GIT quotient $(\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)})^{ss}(\mathcal{L}) /\!\!/ G$ for the linearisation $\mathcal{L}$ given by an ample class $\beta \in \mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$, with semistable locus $(\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)})^{ss}(\mathcal{L}) = \mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma)$.*

*(c) **Sheaves.** For $X_\Sigma$ simplicial, the functor $\widetilde{(-)}$ sending a graded $S$-module $F$ to the sheaf $\widetilde{F}$ on $X_\Sigma$ induces an equivalence between the category of finitely generated graded $S$-modules modulo those supported on $Z(\Sigma)$ and the category of coherent sheaves on $X_\Sigma$. In particular $\widetilde{S(\beta)} = \mathcal{O}_{X_\Sigma}(\beta)$ for every $\beta \in \mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$.*

**Proof.**

*(a) Construction of the map.* For each maximal cone $\sigma \in \Sigma$ let $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ be the affine chart of `[04.11.04]`. Consider the principal open set $W_\sigma = \{x^{\hat\sigma} \neq 0\} \subseteq \mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)}$, where every variable $x_\rho$ with $\rho \notin \sigma(1)$ is invertible. The ring of $G$-invariant functions on $W_\sigma$ is exactly the degree-zero part of the localisation $S_{x^{\hat\sigma}}$, namely
$$
\big(S_{x^{\hat\sigma}}\big)_0 = \mathbb{C}[x_\rho^{\pm 1} (\rho \notin \sigma), x_\rho (\rho \in \sigma)]_0 .
$$
A Laurent monomial $\prod_\rho x_\rho^{a_\rho}$ has degree zero in $\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$ iff $\sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho = \mathrm{div}(\chi^m)$ for some $m \in M$, that is iff $a_\rho = \langle m, u_\rho\rangle$. Restricting to monomials regular on $W_\sigma$ (so $a_\rho \geq 0$ for $\rho \in \sigma(1)$) forces $\langle m, u_\rho\rangle \geq 0$ for $\rho \in \sigma(1)$, equivalently $m \in \sigma^\vee \cap M$. Hence
$$
\big(S_{x^{\hat\sigma}}\big)_0 = \mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M],
$$
which is the coordinate ring of $U_\sigma$. Taking $\mathrm{Spec}$ produces a morphism $W_\sigma /\!\!/ G \to U_\sigma$ that is an isomorphism. These glue over the rays into a global morphism $\pi : \mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma) \to X_\Sigma$, since $\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma) = \bigcup_\sigma W_\sigma$ by the definition of $Z(\Sigma)$ as the locus lying in no cone.

*(a) Quotient properties.* On each $W_\sigma$ the map is the affine GIT quotient $\mathrm{Spec}(S_{x^{\hat\sigma}}) \to \mathrm{Spec}((S_{x^{\hat\sigma}})_0)$, which is a good categorical quotient by the basic theory of `[04.10.02]`. Categorical quotients glue, so $\pi$ is a good categorical quotient globally. When $\Sigma$ is simplicial, each $\sigma$ has linearly independent ray generators, so the isotropy of $G$ at a point of $W_\sigma$ is finite and the orbits are closed in $W_\sigma$; a good quotient with closed orbits is geometric. Hence $X_\Sigma$ is the orbit space $(\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma))/G$.

*(b) GIT identification.* Choose $\beta \in \mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$ ample, corresponding to an ample line bundle $\mathcal{O}_{X_\Sigma}(\beta)$. Linearise the structure-sheaf bundle on $\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)}$ by the character of $G$ dual to $\beta$ under $\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma) = \widehat{G}$. The Hilbert-Mumford numerical criterion of `[04.10.02]` selects as semistable exactly those points whose closure of the $G$-orbit avoids the locus where every section of the linearised bundle vanishes; for the toric character this locus is precisely $Z(\Sigma)$. Thus $(\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)})^{ss}(\mathcal{L}) = \mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma)$, and the GIT quotient is the categorical quotient of part (a).

*(c) Sheaves.* The localisations $(S_{x^{\hat\sigma}})_0 = \mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ are the structure rings of the charts $U_\sigma$; a graded $S$-module $F$ localises to a module over each, gluing to a quasi-coherent sheaf $\widetilde{F}$. The shift $S(\beta)$ localises on $U_\sigma$ to the rank-one free module generated in degree $-\beta$, which is the chart description of the divisorial sheaf $\mathcal{O}_{X_\Sigma}(\beta)$ from `[04.11.08]`; hence $\widetilde{S(\beta)} = \mathcal{O}_{X_\Sigma}(\beta)$. Two modules give the same sheaf iff they agree after inverting each $x^{\hat\sigma}$, that is iff they differ by a submodule supported on $Z(\Sigma)$; this is the stated equivalence, the toric analogue of Serre's $\mathrm{Proj}$ correspondence. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Cox quotient theorem builds toward the GIT-symplectic dictionary and appears again in the Delzant construction of `[04.10.04]`; the central insight is that the global gluing of affine charts from `[04.11.04]` is repackaged as a single quotient of one affine space by one diagonalisable group, and this is exactly the structure $\mathbb{P}^n = (\mathbb{A}^{n+1}\setminus 0)/\mathbb{C}^*$ written for an arbitrary fan. The foundational reason the construction works is the divisor-class exact sequence of `[04.11.08]`: it simultaneously fixes the grading of $S$, the group $G$ by duality, and the irrelevant ideal $B(\Sigma)$ that carves out the forbidden set. Putting these together, the fan combinatorics of `[04.11.04]` and the divisor theory of `[04.11.08]` fuse into one GIT problem in the sense of `[04.10.02]`, and the bridge is dual to the symplectic-reduction shadow recorded by Kempf-Ness in `[04.10.04]`. This generalises the homogeneous-coordinate description of projective space to all of toric geometry, and is the structure that recurs whenever sheaves on $X_\Sigma$ are computed through graded $S$-modules.

Write down the total coordinate ring, its grading, the irrelevant ideal, and the group $G$ for $\mathbb{P}^2$. Confirm the quotient is $(\mathbb{A}^3 \setminus 0)/\mathbb{C}^*$.

Three rays, one relation $u_0 + u_1 + u_2 = 0$, so $\mathrm{Cl}(\mathbb{P}^2) = \mathbb{Z}$. The irrelevant ideal is generated by the products $x^{\hat\sigma}$ for the three maximal cones.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Ring.** $S = \mathbb{C}[x_0, x_1, x_2]$.

**Grading.** The sequence $0 \to M \to \mathbb{Z}^3 \to \mathrm{Cl}(\mathbb{P}^2) \to 0$ has $M = \mathbb{Z}^2 \hookrightarrow \mathbb{Z}^3$ via $m \mapsto (\langle m, u_0\rangle, \langle m, u_1\rangle, \langle m, u_2\rangle)$, and the cokernel is $\mathbb{Z}$ with each $D_\rho \mapsto 1$ (since $u_0 + u_1 + u_2 = 0$ gives $D_0 \sim D_1 \sim D_2$). So $\deg x_i = 1$, the standard grading.

**Group.** $\mathrm{Cl}(\mathbb{P}^2) = \mathbb{Z}$, so $G = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{C}^*) = \mathbb{C}^*$, acting diagonally by $t \cdot (x_0, x_1, x_2) = (t x_0, t x_1, t x_2)$.

**Quotient.** $X_\Sigma = (\mathbb{A}^3 \setminus 0)/\mathbb{C}^* = \mathbb{P}^2$.

**Exercise 2 (medium, symbolic).**

Carry out the Cox construction for $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$. Give the bigrading, the irrelevant ideal, the group $G$, and the exceptional set $Z(\Sigma)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Four rays $\pm e_1, \pm e_2$; rank-two lattice; class group $\mathbb{Z}^2$. The four maximal cones are the quadrants; each omits two opposite rays.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Ring and bigrading.** $S = \mathbb{C}[x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4]$ with $\rho_1 = e_1, \rho_2 = -e_1, \rho_3 = e_2, \rho_4 = -e_2$. The relations $u_1 + u_2 = 0$ and $u_3 + u_4 = 0$ give $\mathrm{Cl} = \mathbb{Z}^2$ with $\deg x_1 = \deg x_2 = (1,0)$ and $\deg x_3 = \deg x_4 = (0,1)$.

**Irrelevant ideal.** The four maximal cones are the quadrants $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(\pm e_1, \pm e_2)$, each containing two rays and omitting the other two; the omitted pairs are $\{x_1, x_2\}$-complements. Working through the four cones, $B(\Sigma) = \langle x_1, x_2\rangle \cap \langle x_3, x_4\rangle = \langle x_1 x_3, x_1 x_4, x_2 x_3, x_2 x_4\rangle$.

**Group.** $G = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathbb{Z}^2, \mathbb{C}^*) = (\mathbb{C}^*)^2$, acting by $(s, t) \cdot (x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4) = (s x_1, s x_2, t x_3, t x_4)$.

**Exceptional set.** $Z(\Sigma) = V(B(\Sigma)) = \{x_1 = x_2 = 0\} \cup \{x_3 = x_4 = 0\}$, the union of two disjoint lines in $\mathbb{A}^4$.

**Quotient.** $X_\Sigma = (\mathbb{A}^4 \setminus Z(\Sigma))/(\mathbb{C}^*)^2 = \mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$, the product of two copies of the projective-line quotient.

Rubric: full credit for the bigrading, the irrelevant ideal as an intersection of two ideals, the group $(\mathbb{C}^*)^2$, and the two-component exceptional set.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

The Hirzebruch surface $F_a$ has fan rays $\rho_1 = e_1$, $\rho_2 = e_2$, $\rho_3 = -e_1 + a e_2$, $\rho_4 = -e_2$. Compute its class group and the degrees of the four Cox variables.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the map $\mathbb{Z}^4 \to \mathrm{Cl}$ killing the image of $M = \mathbb{Z}^2$. The columns of the matrix $[u_1\,u_2\,u_3\,u_4]$ give the relations $\sum_\rho \langle m, u_\rho\rangle D_\rho = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Relations.** For $m = e_1^*$: $\langle e_1^*, u_\rho\rangle = (1, 0, -1, 0)$, giving $D_1 = D_3$ in $\mathrm{Cl}$. For $m = e_2^*$: $\langle e_2^*, u_\rho\rangle = (0, 1, a, -1)$, giving $D_2 + a D_3 = D_4$, that is $D_4 = D_2 + a D_3$.

**Class group.** $\mathrm{Cl}(F_a) = \mathbb{Z}^4 / \langle D_1 - D_3,\ D_2 + a D_3 - D_4\rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}^2$, free of rank two (no torsion, since $F_a$ is smooth). Take $\{[D_3], [D_2]\}$ as a basis.

**Degrees.** With $\beta_3 = [D_3] = (1,0)$ and $\beta_2 = [D_2] = (0,1)$: $\deg x_1 = [D_1] = [D_3] = (1, 0)$; $\deg x_3 = [D_3] = (1, 0)$; $\deg x_2 = [D_2] = (0, 1)$; $\deg x_4 = [D_4] = [D_2] + a[D_3] = (a, 1)$. So the bidegrees are $x_1, x_3 \mapsto (1,0)$, $x_2 \mapsto (0,1)$, $x_4 \mapsto (a, 1)$.

**Check $a = 0$.** When $a = 0$, $\deg x_4 = (0,1) = \deg x_2$, and the degrees reduce to the $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$ pattern of Exercise 2, consistent with $F_0 = \mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$.

Rubric: full credit for the two relations, $\mathrm{Cl}(F_a) = \mathbb{Z}^2$, the four bidegrees, and the $a = 0$ consistency check.

</details>

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that for $\Sigma$ simplicial the action of $G$ on $\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma)$ has finite stabilisers, so the quotient is geometric.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A point in $\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma)$ has nonvanishing coordinates on a set of rays containing the complement of some cone $\sigma$. Restrict the $G$-action to those coordinates and use that simplicial cones have linearly independent generators.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $p \in \mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma)$, so the rays $\rho$ with $p_\rho = 0$ all lie in a single cone $\sigma \in \Sigma$, equivalently $p \in W_\sigma$ where $x_\rho \neq 0$ for $\rho \notin \sigma(1)$. An element $g = (g_\rho) \in G$ fixes $p$ iff $g_\rho = 1$ for every $\rho$ with $p_\rho \neq 0$, in particular for every $\rho \notin \sigma(1)$.

Recall $G = \{(g_\rho) : \prod_\rho g_\rho^{\langle m, u_\rho\rangle} = 1 \ \forall m \in M\}$. Setting $g_\rho = 1$ for $\rho \notin \sigma(1)$ leaves the constraints $\prod_{\rho \in \sigma(1)} g_\rho^{\langle m, u_\rho\rangle} = 1$. Because $\sigma$ is simplicial, the generators $\{u_\rho : \rho \in \sigma(1)\}$ are linearly independent over $\mathbb{Q}$, so the linear forms $m \mapsto \langle m, u_\rho\rangle$ for $\rho \in \sigma(1)$ span a finite-index subgroup of $\mathbb{Z}^{\sigma(1)}$. Hence the only solutions $(g_\rho)_{\rho \in \sigma(1)}$ form a finite group (roots of unity determined by the cone multiplicity $\mathrm{mult}(\sigma)$).

Therefore the stabiliser of $p$ is finite, of order dividing $\mathrm{mult}(\sigma)$. A good categorical quotient with finite stabilisers and closed orbits is geometric, so $X_\Sigma = (\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma))/G$ is an orbit space. The stabiliser order $\mathrm{mult}(\sigma)$ is exactly the order of the local fundamental group of the quotient singularity at the corresponding torus-fixed point.

Rubric: full credit for reducing the stabiliser to the $\sigma$-coordinates, using simplicial linear independence to force finiteness, and concluding geometric quotient.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove that $(S_{x^{\hat\sigma}})_0 = \mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ for a maximal cone $\sigma$, the localisation identity at the heart of the quotient theorem.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A Laurent monomial $\prod_\rho x_\rho^{a_\rho}$ is degree zero in $\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$ iff $\sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho$ is the divisor of a character $\chi^m$, i.e. $a_\rho = \langle m, u_\rho\rangle$. Regularity on $W_\sigma$ forces $a_\rho \geq 0$ for $\rho \in \sigma(1)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The localisation $S_{x^{\hat\sigma}}$ inverts every $x_\rho$ with $\rho \notin \sigma(1)$, so its elements are spanned by Laurent monomials $x^a = \prod_\rho x_\rho^{a_\rho}$ with $a_\rho \geq 0$ for $\rho \in \sigma(1)$ and $a_\rho \in \mathbb{Z}$ otherwise.

Such a monomial has degree zero in $\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$ iff $[\sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho] = 0$, iff $\sum_\rho a_\rho D_\rho = \mathrm{div}(\chi^m)$ for some $m \in M$ by exactness of the divisor-class sequence of `[04.11.08]`. Since $\mathrm{div}(\chi^m) = \sum_\rho \langle m, u_\rho\rangle D_\rho$ and the $D_\rho$ are independent in $\mathbb{Z}^{\Sigma(1)}$, this reads $a_\rho = \langle m, u_\rho\rangle$ for every $\rho$.

The constraint $a_\rho \geq 0$ for $\rho \in \sigma(1)$ becomes $\langle m, u_\rho\rangle \geq 0$ for all $\rho \in \sigma(1)$. Since $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(u_\rho : \rho \in \sigma(1))$ for a maximal cone, this is precisely the condition $m \in \sigma^\vee$. For $\rho \notin \sigma(1)$ there is no sign constraint, and $a_\rho = \langle m, u_\rho\rangle$ is automatically determined by $m$.

The map $m \mapsto x^a$ with $a_\rho = \langle m, u_\rho\rangle$ is therefore a bijection from $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ onto the degree-zero monomials of $S_{x^{\hat\sigma}}$, and it is multiplicative: $m + m'$ maps to $x^a x^{a'}$. Hence $(S_{x^{\hat\sigma}})_0 = \mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M] = \mathcal{O}(U_\sigma)$, the coordinate ring of the chart. $\square$

Rubric: full credit for the degree-zero characterisation via the divisor sequence, the translation of regularity into $m \in \sigma^\vee$, and the multiplicative bijection onto $\sigma^\vee \cap M$.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (GIT chambers and the secondary fan; Cox 1995, Cox-Little-Schenck §14).** *Fix the affine space $\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)}$ with its $G$-action. As the linearising character $\beta \in \mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)_\mathbb{R}$ varies, the semistable locus $(\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)})^{ss}(\beta)$, hence the GIT quotient, changes only when $\beta$ crosses a wall of the **secondary fan** (the fan of GIT chambers in $\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)_\mathbb{R}$). Each top-dimensional chamber yields a simplicial toric variety, all sharing the same total coordinate ring $S$ and group $G$; the distinct quotients are related by toric flips and are the **variation of GIT** (VGIT) of the $G$-action.*

The secondary-fan theorem turns the choice of fan refinement into the choice of a GIT chamber. The varieties $X_\Sigma$ obtained for different chambers all have the same Cox data; they differ only in the irrelevant ideal $B(\Sigma)$, equivalently in which forbidden set $Z(\Sigma)$ is removed before quotienting. This is the algebraic incarnation of the toric minimal model program: crossing a wall performs a flip, and the wall-and-chamber structure of $\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)_\mathbb{R}$ records the birational geometry of the family. The construction is functorial in the lattice data and realises the Mori-theoretic picture combinatorially.

**Theorem (sheaf cohomology via graded $S$-modules; Cox 1995 §3, Mustață 2002).** *For $X_\Sigma$ simplicial and complete, the cohomology of a sheaf $\mathcal{O}_{X_\Sigma}(\beta)$ is computed by the local cohomology of $S$ with support in the irrelevant ideal: there is an isomorphism*
$$
H^i(X_\Sigma, \mathcal{O}_{X_\Sigma}(\beta)) \;\cong\; H^{i+1}_{B(\Sigma)}(S)_\beta
$$
*for $i \geq 1$, the graded local cohomology in degree $\beta$, the toric analogue of the local-cohomology computation of sheaf cohomology on $\mathbb{P}^n$.*

This theorem makes cohomology on a toric variety into commutative algebra over the single ring $S$. The local cohomology $H^\bullet_{B(\Sigma)}(S)$ is computed from the Koszul or Čech complex on the generators of the irrelevant ideal, exactly as the cohomology of line bundles on $\mathbb{P}^n$ is computed from $H^\bullet_{\mathfrak{m}}(\mathbb{C}[x_0, \ldots, x_n])$. The grading slices out the contribution of each class $\beta$, and the result recovers the lattice-point formulas of `[04.11.08]` for global sections while extending them to all cohomological degrees.

**Theorem (Kempf-Ness shadow; Audin Ch. VI, Cox-Little-Schenck §14.3).** *Let $X_\Sigma$ be a smooth projective toric variety presented as $(\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma))/G$. Let $K \subseteq G$ be the maximal compact subgroup, a real torus. Then the GIT quotient is canonically homeomorphic to the symplectic (Marsden-Weinstein) quotient $\mu_K^{-1}(c)/K$ of $\mathbb{C}^{\Sigma(1)}$ by $K$ at a regular value $c$, where $\mu_K$ is the moment map of the linear $K$-action. This is the algebraic form of the **Delzant construction** of `[04.10.04]`.*

The Kempf-Ness shadow identifies the Cox quotient with a symplectic reduction. The same affine space $\mathbb{C}^{\Sigma(1)}$ carries a linear Hamiltonian action of the compact torus $K$, and reducing at a level $c$ produces a symplectic toric manifold whose moment polytope is the polytope of the polarisation. The GIT-symplectic dictionary of `[04.10.04]` says the algebraic quotient and the symplectic quotient coincide, so the Cox ring is the algebraic engine sitting beneath Delzant's symplectic construction. The level $c$ corresponds to the ample class $\beta$, and varying $c$ across walls reproduces the secondary-fan chambers on the symplectic side.

**Theorem (functoriality and toric morphisms in homogeneous coordinates; Cox 1995 §4).** *A toric morphism $X_\Sigma \to X_{\Sigma'}$ induced by a fan map $\phi : N \to N'$ is given in homogeneous coordinates by monomial maps $x'_{\rho'} \mapsto \prod_\rho x_\rho^{b_{\rho' \rho}}$, the exponents determined by how the rays of $\Sigma$ map under $\phi$. The total coordinate rings assemble into a functor, and the morphism is encoded by a graded ring homomorphism compatible with the class-group gradings.*

Homogeneous coordinates make toric morphisms into explicit monomial maps, exactly as a morphism $\mathbb{P}^n \to \mathbb{P}^m$ given by degree-$d$ forms is written in homogeneous coordinates. This is the computational payoff of the Cox construction: maps, embeddings, and rational maps between toric varieties become polynomial bookkeeping in the variables $x_\rho$, and the class-group grading tracks which bundles are pulled back. The functoriality refines the fan-morphism functoriality of `[04.11.04]` to the level of coordinate rings.

**Synthesis.** The Cox homogeneous coordinate ring is the central insight that unifies the affine-chart picture of `[04.11.04]`, the divisor theory of `[04.11.08]`, and the GIT machinery of `[04.10.02]` into a single statement: every toric variety is one affine space, minus a forbidden set, modulo one diagonalisable group. The foundational reason this works is the divisor-class exact sequence, which is dual to the torus sequence $1 \to G \to (\mathbb{C}^*)^{\Sigma(1)} \to T \to 1$; the grading of $S$ by $\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$ and the group $G = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma), \mathbb{C}^*)$ are the two faces of this duality, and the irrelevant ideal supplies the semistable locus on which the quotient is well-behaved. Putting these together, the secondary fan organises every chamber-quotient of the fixed $G$-action, sheaf cohomology becomes local cohomology of $S$ supported on $B(\Sigma)$, and the whole apparatus is the algebraic shadow of the symplectic Delzant reduction of `[04.10.04]`. This is exactly the structure $\mathbb{P}^n = (\mathbb{A}^{n+1}\setminus 0)/\mathbb{C}^*$ generalised to an arbitrary fan, and it generalises further to Cox rings of non-toric varieties (Mori dream spaces), where the same total-coordinate-ring-plus-irrelevant-ideal pattern recurs and the bridge is again a GIT quotient by the Picard torus.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (the group $G$ is the kernel of $(\mathbb{C}^*)^{\Sigma(1)} \to T$), proof.** Apply the exact functor $\mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(-, \mathbb{C}^*)$ (exact because $\mathbb{C}^*$ is divisible, hence injective as an abelian group) to the divisor-class sequence $0 \to M \to \mathbb{Z}^{\Sigma(1)} \to \mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma) \to 0$. This yields the exact sequence
$$
1 \to \mathrm{Hom}(\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma), \mathbb{C}^*) \to \mathrm{Hom}(\mathbb{Z}^{\Sigma(1)}, \mathbb{C}^*) \to \mathrm{Hom}(M, \mathbb{C}^*) \to 1.
$$
Now $\mathrm{Hom}(\mathbb{Z}^{\Sigma(1)}, \mathbb{C}^*) = (\mathbb{C}^*)^{\Sigma(1)}$ and $\mathrm{Hom}(M, \mathbb{C}^*) = T$, so $G = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma), \mathbb{C}^*)$ is exactly the kernel of the surjection $(\mathbb{C}^*)^{\Sigma(1)} \to T$. The defining equations of $G$ inside $(\mathbb{C}^*)^{\Sigma(1)}$ are $\prod_\rho g_\rho^{\langle m, u_\rho\rangle} = 1$ for $m$ ranging over a basis of $M$, the image of the dual map. $\square$

**Proposition (the charts $W_\sigma$ cover $\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma)$), proof.** By definition $W_\sigma = \{x^{\hat\sigma} \neq 0\} = \{x_\rho \neq 0 \text{ for all } \rho \notin \sigma(1)\}$. A point $p$ lies in some $W_\sigma$ iff the set $R(p) = \{\rho : p_\rho = 0\}$ is contained in $\sigma(1)$ for some cone $\sigma$, that is iff the rays where $p$ vanishes are among the rays of a single cone. The exceptional set is $Z(\Sigma) = V(B(\Sigma))$ with $B(\Sigma) = \langle x^{\hat\sigma}\rangle$; a point $p$ lies in $Z(\Sigma)$ iff $x^{\hat\sigma}(p) = 0$ for every $\sigma$, iff for every cone $\sigma$ some ray $\rho \notin \sigma(1)$ has $p_\rho = 0$, iff $R(p)$ is contained in **no** cone. Negating, $p \notin Z(\Sigma)$ iff $R(p) \subseteq \sigma(1)$ for some $\sigma$, iff $p \in W_\sigma$ for some $\sigma$. Hence $\bigcup_\sigma W_\sigma = \mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma)$. $\square$

**Proposition (gluing of the chart quotients), proof.** For maximal cones $\sigma, \tau$, the overlap $W_\sigma \cap W_\tau = W_{\sigma \cap \tau}$ inverts the variables of rays outside $\sigma \cap \tau$, and by the localisation identity (Exercise 5) the degree-zero ring is $(S_{x^{\widehat{\sigma \cap \tau}}})_0 = \mathbb{C}[(\sigma \cap \tau)^\vee \cap M] = \mathcal{O}(U_{\sigma \cap \tau})$. The face $\sigma \cap \tau$ is a common face of $\sigma$ and $\tau$ by the fan axioms of `[04.11.04]`, and the localisation maps $\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M] \to \mathbb{C}[(\sigma \cap \tau)^\vee \cap M]$ are exactly the open-immersion comorphisms used in `[04.11.04]` to glue $U_{\sigma \cap \tau} \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$. Hence the chart quotients $W_\sigma /\!\!/ G \cong U_\sigma$ glue along the same data that glue the $U_\sigma$ into $X_\Sigma$, producing a global isomorphism $(\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma))/\!\!/G \cong X_\Sigma$. $\square$

**Proposition (the sheaf $\widetilde{S(\beta)}$ is $\mathcal{O}_{X_\Sigma}(\beta)$), proof.** On the chart $U_\sigma$ the module $S(\beta)$ localises to $(S_{x^{\hat\sigma}})$ shifted by $\beta$, and its degree-zero part $(S_{x^{\hat\sigma}}(\beta))_0 = (S_{x^{\hat\sigma}})_\beta$. A degree-$\beta$ Laurent monomial regular on $W_\sigma$ corresponds, by the argument of Exercise 5, to an element $m \in M$ with $\langle m, u_\rho\rangle \geq -a_\rho$ for $\rho \in \sigma(1)$, where $\beta = [\sum a_\rho D_\rho]$. These are precisely the sections of $\mathcal{O}_{X_\Sigma}(D)$ over $U_\sigma$ for any divisor $D$ with $[D] = \beta$, by the local description of divisorial sheaves in `[04.11.08]`. The identification is compatible with restriction to overlaps, so $\widetilde{S(\beta)} = \mathcal{O}_{X_\Sigma}(\beta)$ globally. $\square$

**Proposition (the secondary fan governs VGIT), proof sketch.** *As $\beta$ varies in $\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)_\mathbb{R}$, the semistable locus is locally constant on chambers and jumps across walls.*

*Proof.* By the Hilbert-Mumford criterion of `[04.10.02]`, semistability of a point $p$ for the character $\beta$ depends only on the sign pattern of the pairings $\langle \beta, \lambda\rangle$ as $\lambda$ ranges over one-parameter subgroups of $G$ with $\lim_{t\to 0}\lambda(t)\cdot p$ existing. For the toric linear action these one-parameter subgroups are indexed by lattice vectors, and the relevant pairings are linear functionals of $\beta$. The set of $\beta$ giving a fixed sign pattern is the relative interior of a rational polyhedral cone — a chamber of the **secondary fan**, the normal fan of the secondary polytope of the vector configuration $\{u_\rho\}$. Within one chamber the semistable locus, and so the GIT quotient, does not change. Crossing a wall flips at least one sign, changing which $x^{\hat\sigma}$ generators lie in the irrelevant ideal, hence changing $Z(\Sigma)$ and performing a toric flip on the quotient. The chambers thus enumerate the simplicial fans supported on $\bigcup_\rho u_\rho$, and the wall-crossings realise the toric minimal model program combinatorially. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Fan and the toric variety $X_\Sigma$ [04.11.04].** The Cox quotient is the global gluing of `[04.11.04]` rewritten as a single quotient. Each affine chart $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ reappears as the degree-zero ring $(S_{x^{\hat\sigma}})_0$ of a localisation of the Cox ring, and the chart overlaps glue along exactly the face-inclusion comorphisms of the fan unit. This resolves the forward reference to `[04.11.15]` made in `[04.11.04]`: the homogeneous-coordinate presentation promised there is constructed here.

- **Toric divisor and support function [04.11.08].** The divisor-class exact sequence $0 \to M \to \mathbb{Z}^{\Sigma(1)} \to \mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma) \to 0$ of `[04.11.08]` is the structural input that fixes the grading of $S$, the group $G$ by duality, and the divisorial sheaves $\mathcal{O}_{X_\Sigma}(\beta) = \widetilde{S(\beta)}$. The variable $x_\rho$ is the homogeneous equation of the toric divisor $D_\rho$, so the monomials of $S$ are the effective $T$-invariant divisors, and the lattice-point global-sections formula of `[04.11.08]` becomes the degree-$\beta$ piece $S_\beta$. This resolves the forward reference to `[04.11.15]` in `[04.11.08]`.

- **Geometric invariant theory (GIT) quotients [04.10.02].** The Cox quotient is a GIT quotient of $\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)}$ by the diagonalisable group $G$, linearised by an ample class $\beta$. The semistable locus is $\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma)$, computed by the Hilbert-Mumford criterion of `[04.10.02]`, and the chamber structure of the linearisation is the secondary fan governing variation of GIT. The toric case is the most explicit testing ground for GIT, since every ingredient — group, semistable locus, quotient — is read off from fan combinatorics.

- **Kempf-Ness theorem [04.10.04].** The GIT quotient $(\mathbb{A}^{\Sigma(1)} \setminus Z(\Sigma))/G$ is homeomorphic to the symplectic quotient $\mu_K^{-1}(c)/K$ of $\mathbb{C}^{\Sigma(1)}$ by the maximal compact $K \subseteq G$, by the Kempf-Ness identification of `[04.10.04]`. This realises the Cox ring as the algebraic engine beneath the symplectic Delzant construction of toric manifolds, with the ample class $\beta$ corresponding to the moment-map level $c$ and the secondary-fan walls corresponding to the walls of the symplectic chamber structure.

- **Projective space [04.07.01].** The prototype: $\mathbb{P}^n = (\mathbb{A}^{n+1}\setminus 0)/\mathbb{C}^*$ is the Cox quotient of the fan with $n+1$ rays, total coordinate ring $\mathbb{C}[x_0, \ldots, x_n]$, irrelevant ideal $\langle x_0, \ldots, x_n\rangle$, and group $\mathbb{C}^*$. The Cox construction is exactly the statement that every toric variety admits homogeneous coordinates in this projective-space sense, and Serre's $\mathrm{Proj}$ correspondence between graded modules and sheaves on $\mathbb{P}^n$ generalises to the toric module-sheaf equivalence proved here.

- **Toric Picard group [04.11.09].** The class group $\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$ grading the Cox ring is computed in `[04.11.09]`, where the Picard subgroup of Cartier classes sits inside $\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)$. The ample cone in $\mathrm{Cl}(X_\Sigma)_\mathbb{R}$ that selects the linearisation $\beta$ is the same nef/ample cone studied there, and the secondary-fan chambers refine the Picard-group geometry into GIT chambers.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The homogeneous coordinate ring of a toric variety was introduced by David Cox in *The homogeneous coordinate ring of a toric variety* (Journal of Algebraic Geometry 4, 1995, pp. 17-50) [ref: TODO_REF Cox 1995]. Cox's stated aim was to give toric varieties the same computational footing as projective space, where a single graded polynomial ring controls coordinates, line bundles, sheaves, and cohomology through Serre's $\mathrm{Proj}$ correspondence. The construction supplied exactly this: one polynomial ring $S$ graded by the class group, one irrelevant ideal $B(\Sigma)$, and a presentation of $X_\Sigma$ as a quotient of affine space by a diagonalisable group. The quotient form generalises the classical $\mathbb{P}^n = (\mathbb{A}^{n+1}\setminus 0)/\mathbb{C}^*$ and made toric geometry tractable for symbolic computation, underpinning the toric packages of Macaulay2, Polymake, and SageMath.

The quotient was independently anticipated from the symplectic side. The construction of a toric manifold as a symplectic reduction $\mathbb{C}^d /\!\!/ K$ of affine space by a real torus is the **Delzant construction** (Thomas Delzant, *Hamiltoniens périodiques et image convexe de l'application moment*, Bulletin de la Société Mathématique de France 116, 1988, pp. 315-339) [ref: TODO_REF Delzant 1988], and Michèle Audin's monograph *The Topology of Torus Actions on Symplectic Manifolds* (Birkhäuser 1991) [ref: TODO_REF Audin 1991] developed this reduction in detail. The bridge between Cox's algebraic quotient and Delzant's symplectic quotient is the Kempf-Ness theorem (George Kempf and Linda Ness, *The length of vectors in representation spaces*, Springer LNM 732, 1979, pp. 233-243), so the Cox ring is the algebraic shadow of a symplectic construction discovered along a parallel route.

The underlying global toric variety had been constructed two decades earlier by Michel Demazure in his 1970 study of the Cremona group [ref: TODO_REF Demazure 1970], and the GIT framework that makes the quotient rigorous is Mumford's geometric invariant theory (Mumford-Fogarty-Kirwan, *Geometric Invariant Theory*, 3rd ed., Springer 1994) [ref: TODO_REF MumfordFogartyKirwan 1994]. Cox's contribution was to recognise that these strands — Demazure's gluing, Mumford's quotients, and the class-group bookkeeping of toric divisors — combine into a single homogeneous-coordinate description. The philosophical lesson is one of unification: the same variety has a combinatorial description (the fan), an algebraic description (the graded Cox ring with its quotient), and a symplectic description (the Delzant reduction), and the equivalence of these three is the structural backbone of modern toric geometry, extending through Mori dream spaces to the Cox rings of non-toric varieties.
