## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine the rational polyhedral cone $\sigma$ from the previous unit as a blueprint. The cone sits inside the lattice space $N_\mathbb{R}$, and its dual cone $\sigma^\vee$ sits inside $M_\mathbb{R}$. The lattice points $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ inside the dual cone form a tidy bag of integer vectors — closed under addition, containing zero, finitely generated by Gordan's lemma. From this combinatorial bag we are about to build an actual geometric shape: an affine algebraic variety, denoted $U_\sigma$, whose coordinate ring records the addition law on the bag.

The recipe is short. Each lattice point $m$ in $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ produces a formal monomial $\chi^m$. When you add two lattice points you multiply their monomials: $\chi^m \cdot \chi^{m'} = \chi^{m + m'}$. The set of all finite $\mathbb{C}$-linear combinations of these monomials forms a commutative ring — the **semigroup algebra** $\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ — and the affine algebraic variety $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ is the geometric shape encoded by that ring. Different cones $\sigma$ produce different shapes, and the dictionary between cone combinatorics and affine-variety geometry is the heart of toric geometry.

Why bother? Because $U_\sigma$ has two simultaneous descriptions — combinatorial (the cone $\sigma$) and algebraic-geometric (an affine variety with a torus action) — and every question you ask about the variety becomes a question about the cone. Is the variety smooth? Look at whether the cone generators form part of a lattice basis. How many torus orbits does the variety have? Count the faces of the cone. The whole geometry of $U_\sigma$ is readable from the shape of $\sigma$, which makes toric varieties the playground where algebraic geometry, combinatorics, and convex geometry meet on equal terms.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take $N = \mathbb{Z}^2$ with cone $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_2) = \mathbb{R}^2_{\geq 0}$, the closed first quadrant. Compute the affine toric variety $U_\sigma$ step by step and identify it with a familiar algebraic variety.

Step 1. Compute the dual cone. As shown in the previous unit, $\sigma^\vee = \{(m_1, m_2) \in \mathbb{R}^2 : m_1 \geq 0, m_2 \geq 0\}$ is the closed first quadrant of $M_\mathbb{R}$, generated by the dual basis vectors $e_1^*$ and $e_2^*$.

Step 2. Enumerate lattice points in the dual cone. The semigroup $S_\sigma = \sigma^\vee \cap M = \{(m_1, m_2) \in \mathbb{Z}^2 : m_1, m_2 \geq 0\} = \mathbb{Z}^2_{\geq 0}$ is the additive monoid of nonnegative integer pairs. It is generated by $(1, 0)$ and $(0, 1)$ as a commutative monoid — every element $(a, b)$ is uniquely $a \cdot (1, 0) + b \cdot (0, 1)$.

Step 3. Form the semigroup algebra. Set $x = \chi^{(1, 0)}$ and $y = \chi^{(0, 1)}$. Each monomial $\chi^{(a, b)}$ becomes $x^a y^b$, and the semigroup algebra $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ becomes the polynomial ring $\mathbb{C}[x, y]$. The multiplication rule $\chi^{(a, b)} \cdot \chi^{(c, d)} = \chi^{(a + c, b + d)}$ matches $x^a y^b \cdot x^c y^d = x^{a + c} y^{b + d}$, the ordinary multiplication of monomials.

Step 4. Take the spectrum. $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[x, y] = \mathbb{C}^2$, the affine plane. The closed points of $\mathbb{C}^2$ are the maximal ideals $(x - a, y - b)$ for $(a, b) \in \mathbb{C}^2$, and the dense open torus $T = (\mathbb{C}^*)^2 \subset \mathbb{C}^2$ corresponds to the locus where $x \neq 0$ and $y \neq 0$.

Step 5. Identify the torus action. The torus $T = (\mathbb{C}^*)^2$ acts on $U_\sigma = \mathbb{C}^2$ by $(s, t) \cdot (a, b) = (s a, t b)$, coordinate-wise multiplication. The dual action on the coordinate ring sends $x \mapsto s x$, $y \mapsto t y$, recording the $M$-grading: $x$ is in degree $(1, 0)$ and $y$ in degree $(0, 1)$.

What this tells us. The cone $\sigma = \mathbb{R}^2_{\geq 0}$ produces the affine plane $\mathbb{C}^2$ with its standard $T$-action by coordinate-wise multiplication. The dense torus $T = (\mathbb{C}^*)^2 \subset \mathbb{C}^2$ corresponds to the zero face $\{0\} \subset \sigma$ — confirming the rule that the affine toric variety of the zero cone is the torus itself. The construction $\sigma \mapsto U_\sigma$ is a recipe for building affine $T$-varieties from cones, and the first-quadrant cone produces the smoothest possible example: the affine plane.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $N$ be a lattice of rank $n$ with dual lattice $M = \mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(N, \mathbb{Z})$ and integer pairing $\langle -, -\rangle : M \times N \to \mathbb{Z}$, as in `04.11.01`. Let $\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ be a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone, as in `04.11.02`. Write $S_\sigma := \sigma^\vee \cap M$ for the additive semigroup of lattice points in the dual cone.

**Definition (semigroup algebra).** The **semigroup algebra** of $S_\sigma$ over $\mathbb{C}$, denoted $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$, is the $\mathbb{C}$-vector space
$$
\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] = \bigoplus_{m \in S_\sigma} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^m
$$
with formal basis $\{\chi^m : m \in S_\sigma\}$, equipped with the commutative-ring multiplication
$$
\chi^m \cdot \chi^{m'} = \chi^{m + m'}
$$
extended $\mathbb{C}$-bilinearly. The identity element is $\chi^0 = 1$. The grading by $M$ records the $M$-degree of each monomial — $\chi^m$ has degree $m \in M$ — and this grading will encode the $T$-action below.

By Gordan's lemma (`04.11.02`), $S_\sigma$ is finitely generated as a commutative monoid. Choose a finite generating set $\{m_1, \ldots, m_r\} \subseteq S_\sigma$. Then $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is generated as a $\mathbb{C}$-algebra by the elements $\chi^{m_1}, \ldots, \chi^{m_r}$. Hence $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is a finitely generated commutative $\mathbb{C}$-algebra, and there is a surjective $\mathbb{C}$-algebra map
$$
\mathbb{C}[X_1, \ldots, X_r] \twoheadrightarrow \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma], \qquad X_i \mapsto \chi^{m_i}.
$$
The kernel is the **toric ideal** $I_\sigma \subseteq \mathbb{C}[X_1, \ldots, X_r]$ associated to $\sigma$ and the choice of generators. The toric ideal is generated by binomial relations $X_1^{a_1} \cdots X_r^{a_r} - X_1^{b_1} \cdots X_r^{b_r}$, one for each integer dependency $\sum_i a_i m_i = \sum_i b_i m_i$ in $S_\sigma$ with $a_i, b_i \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$.

**Definition (affine toric variety).** The **affine toric variety** associated to $\sigma$ is the affine algebraic variety
$$
U_\sigma := \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M].
$$
By the surjection above, $U_\sigma$ is naturally embedded as the vanishing locus of the toric ideal: $U_\sigma \cong V(I_\sigma) \subseteq \mathbb{A}^r_\mathbb{C}$. When the generating set is taken to be the **Hilbert basis** $\mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee)$, the embedding dimension is $r = |\mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee)|$, which is the minimal embedding dimension realisable from this construction.

**Definition (torus action).** The algebraic torus $T = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M]$ from `04.11.01` acts on $U_\sigma$ via the comorphism
$$
\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \to \mathbb{C}[M] \otimes_\mathbb{C} \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma], \qquad \chi^m \mapsto \chi^m \otimes \chi^m,
$$
which encodes the $M$-grading on $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ as an algebraic group action of $T$ on $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$. The action restricts to the dense open subscheme $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M] \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ coming from the inclusion $S_\sigma \hookrightarrow M$ of monoids (every $m \in S_\sigma$ extends to all of $M$ when we adjoin inverses); this dense open is the torus $T$ itself, acting on itself by translation.

**Convention.** For the rest of this unit we assume $\sigma$ is strongly convex — that is, $\sigma \cap (-\sigma) = \{0\}$ — unless explicitly stated otherwise. When $\sigma$ is not strongly convex, the same construction works but $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ contains units corresponding to the lineality space of $\sigma^\vee$, and the resulting $U_\sigma$ is a torus bundle over the strongly convex case. The strongly convex hypothesis is the standard setup in toric geometry and is what makes $U_\sigma$ have a unique closed $T$-fixed point.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- "The semigroup $\sigma \cap N$ should work as well as $\sigma^\vee \cap M$." It does not. The lattice points in $\sigma$ form an additive semigroup, but the resulting semigroup algebra encodes the wrong functor — the contravariance of $\mathrm{Spec}$ requires lattice points in the dual cone. Concretely, for $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_2)$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$, both $\sigma \cap N$ and $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ give $\mathbb{Z}^2_{\geq 0}$ (since the cone is self-dual), so the two constructions coincidentally agree; for $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1)$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ they differ — $\sigma \cap N = \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0} e_1$ gives $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[x] = \mathbb{A}^1$, but $\sigma^\vee \cap M = \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0} e_1^* + \mathbb{Z} e_2^*$ gives $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[x, y^{\pm 1}] = \mathbb{A}^1 \times \mathbb{G}_m$, the correct affine toric variety.

- "The Hilbert basis equals the ray generators of $\sigma^\vee$." It does not in general. The ray generators of $\sigma^\vee$ are part of the Hilbert basis (every ray generator is indecomposable), but additional lattice points in the interior of the parallelepiped of fractional parts may also be indecomposable. For $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_1 + 2 e_2)$, the dual has rays $(0, 1)$ and $(2, -1)$ but the Hilbert basis is $\{(0, 1), (2, -1), (1, 0)\}$ — the interior lattice point $(1, 0)$ is indecomposable because no two nonzero elements of $S_\sigma$ sum to it.

- "Replacing $\sigma$ by a $\mathrm{GL}(N)$-equivalent cone gives an isomorphic variety." This is correct when "$\mathrm{GL}(N)$-equivalent" means equivalent under $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z})$, the lattice automorphisms of $N$. Two cones differing by a $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z})$ change of basis produce isomorphic affine toric varieties. But cones differing by an arbitrary $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{R})$ change of basis (involving irrational coefficients) may produce non-isomorphic varieties — the lattice structure is load-bearing.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem of this unit identifies the affine toric variety $U_\sigma$ as a genuinely $T$-equivariant affine algebraic variety, classifies it up to $T$-equivariant isomorphism by the cone $\sigma$, and makes the construction $\sigma \mapsto U_\sigma$ functorial in $\sigma$.

**Theorem (the affine-toric-variety construction).** *Let $\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ be a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone in a lattice $N$ of rank $n$, with dual cone $\sigma^\vee \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ and lattice-point semigroup $S_\sigma = \sigma^\vee \cap M$.*

*(a) **Existence.** The semigroup algebra $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is a finitely generated commutative $\mathbb{C}$-algebra; the affine variety $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is irreducible, normal, and of dimension $n$.*

*(b) **Torus.** The torus $T = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M]$ embeds as an open dense subvariety $T \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$ via the inclusion $S_\sigma \hookrightarrow M$; the embedding is the affine toric variety of the zero face $\{0\} \subseteq \sigma$, identifying $T = U_{\{0\}}$.*

*(c) **Action.** The torus $T$ acts on $U_\sigma$ via the $M$-grading $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] = \bigoplus_{m \in S_\sigma} \mathbb{C} \chi^m$, restricting to the translation action on $T \subseteq U_\sigma$, with $T \subseteq U_\sigma$ a $T$-orbit.*

*(d) **Functoriality.** If $\tau$ is a face of $\sigma$, the inclusion $\tau \hookrightarrow \sigma$ induces a $T$-equivariant open immersion $U_\tau \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$ corresponding to the localisation $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \hookrightarrow \mathbb{C}[S_\tau]$ at the monoid element $\chi^{m_\tau}$, where $m_\tau \in S_\sigma$ is any lattice point in the relative interior of the face $\sigma \cap \tau^\perp$ of $\sigma^\vee$.*

**Proof.**

*(a) Finite generation and normality.* By Gordan's lemma (`04.11.02`), $S_\sigma$ is a finitely generated commutative monoid, with finite generating set $m_1, \ldots, m_r$. The corresponding monomials $\chi^{m_1}, \ldots, \chi^{m_r}$ generate $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ as a $\mathbb{C}$-algebra, so the algebra is finitely generated; its $\mathrm{Spec}$ is therefore an affine variety of finite type over $\mathbb{C}$.

For irreducibility, note that $S_\sigma$ embeds in the group $M = \mathbb{Z}^n$, so the semigroup is **cancellative** and **torsion-free**. Hence the localisation of $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ at the multiplicative set $\{\chi^m : m \in S_\sigma\}$ is $\mathbb{C}[M] = \mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$, which is an integral domain (the Laurent polynomial ring is the localisation of $\mathbb{C}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$, a UFD). A subring of an integral domain is an integral domain, so $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is an integral domain, and $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is irreducible.

For normality, we show $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is integrally closed in its fraction field $\mathbb{C}(M)$. The fraction field of $\mathbb{C}[M]$ is $\mathbb{C}(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$; the fraction field of $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is the same, since $S_\sigma$ generates $M$ as a group when $\sigma$ is strongly convex (the dual cone $\sigma^\vee$ is full-dimensional in $M_\mathbb{R}$, hence $S_\sigma$ spans $M_\mathbb{R}$ over $\mathbb{R}$, hence contains a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $M$). Now $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is the intersection of valuation rings: write $\sigma^\vee = \bigcap_j H_{u_j}^+$ as an intersection of rational half-spaces with $u_j$ the primitive ray generators of $\sigma$; then $S_\sigma = M \cap \bigcap_j \{m : \langle m, u_j\rangle \geq 0\}$, so
$$
\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] = \bigcap_j \mathbb{C}[M]_{\langle u_j, -\rangle \geq 0},
$$
where $\mathbb{C}[M]_{\langle u_j, -\rangle \geq 0}$ is the subring of $\mathbb{C}[M]$ consisting of Laurent monomials nonnegatively paired with $u_j$. Each such subring is a valuation subring of $\mathbb{C}(M)$ (the valuation $v_{u_j}(\chi^m) = \langle u_j, m\rangle$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-valued valuation on $\mathbb{C}(M)$). An intersection of valuation rings is integrally closed, so $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is integrally closed in $\mathbb{C}(M)$, hence normal.

The dimension of $U_\sigma$ is the transcendence degree of $\mathbb{C}(M)$ over $\mathbb{C}$, namely $n$.

*(b) Torus embedding.* The inclusion $S_\sigma \hookrightarrow M$ of monoids extends $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ to $\mathbb{C}[M]$ by adjoining the inverses $(\chi^m)^{-1} = \chi^{-m}$ for $m \in S_\sigma \setminus (-S_\sigma)$. Concretely, $\mathbb{C}[M]$ is the localisation of $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ at the multiplicative set $\{\chi^m : m \in S_\sigma\}$, and this localisation is an open immersion of affine varieties $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M] = T \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] = U_\sigma$. The image $T \subseteq U_\sigma$ is open (the complement is the vanishing locus of the product of the generators $\chi^{m_i}$) and dense (since $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \hookrightarrow \mathbb{C}[M]$ is injective into an integral domain, generic points coincide).

The zero cone $\{0\} \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ has dual cone $\{0\}^\vee = M_\mathbb{R}$ and semigroup $S_{\{0\}} = M$, so $U_{\{0\}} = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M] = T$. The zero cone is a face of $\sigma$ (in fact the unique minimal face when $\sigma$ is strongly convex, since the lineality space of a strongly convex cone is $\{0\}$), so the embedding $T \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$ is an instance of the face-functoriality from part (d).

*(c) Torus action.* The comorphism
$$
\mu^* : \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \to \mathbb{C}[M] \otimes_\mathbb{C} \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma], \qquad \chi^m \mapsto \chi^m \otimes \chi^m,
$$
is a $\mathbb{C}$-algebra map (extends multiplicatively from the generators by the rule $\chi^m \cdot \chi^{m'} \mapsto \chi^{m + m'} \otimes \chi^{m + m'} = (\chi^m \chi^{m'}) \otimes (\chi^m \chi^{m'})$), and the coassociativity and counit axioms for a Hopf-algebra coaction follow from the Hopf-algebra structure on $\mathbb{C}[M]$ from `04.11.01` (the comultiplication on $\mathbb{C}[M]$ is exactly $\chi^m \mapsto \chi^m \otimes \chi^m$, so the coaction on $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is the restriction of the regular Hopf-coaction to a sub-comodule). Dualising, $T \times U_\sigma \to U_\sigma$ is a morphism of affine $\mathbb{C}$-schemes, and the axioms for a group action follow from the Hopf axioms.

The restriction to $T \subseteq U_\sigma$ is the translation action of $T$ on itself: the comorphism $\mathbb{C}[M] \to \mathbb{C}[M] \otimes \mathbb{C}[M]$, $\chi^m \mapsto \chi^m \otimes \chi^m$, is the standard Hopf-coaction defining the regular $T$-action. Hence $T$ is a single $T$-orbit inside $U_\sigma$, and this orbit is dense.

*(d) Functoriality on face inclusions.* Let $\tau \subseteq \sigma$ be a face. By the face-correspondence theorem (`04.11.02`), $\tau = \sigma \cap H_m$ for some $m \in \sigma^\vee \cap (-\tau)^\vee$, equivalently for any $m_\tau$ in the relative interior of the face $\sigma \cap \tau^\perp$ of $\sigma^\vee$. The dual cone of $\tau$ contains $\sigma^\vee$ and is obtained from $\sigma^\vee$ by adjoining the inverses of $\chi^{m_\tau}$:
$$
\tau^\vee = \sigma^\vee + \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \cdot (-m_\tau),
$$
since the face direction $m_\tau$ lies in $\sigma^\vee$ but $-m_\tau$ does not (it lies in $-\tau^\vee$). At the level of semigroups,
$$
S_\tau = \tau^\vee \cap M = S_\sigma + \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0} \cdot (-m_\tau) = S_\sigma[\chi^{m_\tau} \text{ inverted}],
$$
the localisation of $S_\sigma$ at the single element $m_\tau$ (regarding $S_\sigma \subseteq M$, the result of inverting one element produces a larger sub-monoid of $M$, namely $S_\tau$).

At the level of algebras, $\mathbb{C}[S_\tau] = \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma][\chi^{-m_\tau}] = \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]_{\chi^{m_\tau}}$, the localisation of $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ at the element $\chi^{m_\tau}$. Dualising, $U_\tau = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_\tau] = D(\chi^{m_\tau}) \subseteq U_\sigma$ is the basic open subscheme of $U_\sigma$ where $\chi^{m_\tau}$ is nonzero. Open immersions of affine schemes corresponding to localisation at a single element are $T$-equivariant (since $\chi^{m_\tau}$ is a $T$-semi-invariant element of weight $m_\tau$, the localisation preserves the $M$-grading). Hence the face inclusion $\tau \hookrightarrow \sigma$ induces a $T$-equivariant open immersion $U_\tau \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$.

The composition law $\tau_1 \subseteq \tau_2 \subseteq \sigma$ produces the composition $U_{\tau_1} \hookrightarrow U_{\tau_2} \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$ as a sequence of open immersions, by the localisation rule $\mathbb{C}[S_{\tau_1}] = (\mathbb{C}[S_{\tau_2}])_{\chi^{m_{\tau_1}}}$. The construction $\sigma \mapsto U_\sigma$ is therefore functorial on the category of strongly convex rational polyhedral cones with face inclusions, landing in the category of affine $T$-toric varieties with $T$-equivariant open immersions. $\square$

**Bridge.** The affine-toric-variety theorem builds toward the global toric construction of `04.11.04` and the orbit-cone correspondence of `04.11.05`, and the central insight is that the semigroup algebra $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is the coordinate ring of an affine variety with a built-in torus action, with the action read off mechanically from the $M$-grading on the algebra. This is exactly the foundational reason that toric geometry is **combinatorial algebraic geometry**: every geometric question about $U_\sigma$ (dimension, smoothness, orbits, divisors, line bundles) becomes a combinatorial question about the cone $\sigma$ and its dual, and the dictionary between the two sides is exact and computable.

The functoriality on face inclusions identifies the face poset of $\sigma$ with a poset of $T$-equivariant open subvarieties of $U_\sigma$, generalising the orbit decomposition of an affine $T$-variety into a stratification by torus orbits. Putting these together, the affine toric variety is the universal $T$-equivariant affine extension of $T$ along the cone direction $\sigma$, in a sense made precise by Sumihiro's 1974 theorem on equivariant completions. The bridge is dual to the duality $M \leftrightarrow N$ from `04.11.01`: the cone $\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ encodes which characters $m \in M$ extend to regular functions on $U_\sigma$ (namely those in $\sigma^\vee$) and which characters extend only to rational functions, and this asymmetry is the entire reason the toric variety $U_\sigma$ is a $T$-equivariant *partial* compactification of $T$ rather than the full torus itself. This pattern appears again in `04.11.04` (gluing the $U_\sigma$ into a global $X_\Sigma$ via face inclusions), in `04.11.05` (the orbit-cone correspondence identifying $T$-orbits in $U_\sigma$ with faces of $\sigma$), and in `04.11.08` (the divisor-support-function dictionary identifying $T$-invariant divisors on $X_\Sigma$ with piecewise-linear functions on the support of the fan).

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the affine toric variety for $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(2 e_1 - e_2, e_2) \subset N_\mathbb{R} = \mathbb{R}^2$. Identify it with a quadric surface in $\mathbb{C}^3$ and discuss whether it is smooth.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the dual cone and the Hilbert basis of $\sigma^\vee \cap M$. The dual will have two rays plus possibly an interior generator. Then find the binomial relations among the Hilbert basis elements.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Step 1: dual cone.** $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(2 e_1 - e_2, e_2)$ generators have determinant $\det \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = 2 \neq \pm 1$, so the generators do not form a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis; this anticipates singularity. Compute $\sigma^\vee$ in half-space form: $\sigma^\vee = \{(m_1, m_2) : 2 m_1 - m_2 \geq 0, m_2 \geq 0\}$.

**Step 2: ray generators of $\sigma^\vee$.** Boundary lines $2 m_1 - m_2 = 0$ (primitive lattice direction $(1, 2)$, check: $m_2 = 2 \geq 0$, valid) and $m_2 = 0$ (primitive lattice direction $(1, 0)$, check: $2 m_1 - m_2 = 2 \geq 0$, valid). So $\sigma^\vee = \mathrm{Cone}((1, 0), (1, 2))$.

**Step 3: Hilbert basis.** The parallelepiped $P = \{a (1, 0) + b (1, 2) : a, b \in [0, 1]\} = \{(a + b, 2 b) : a, b \in [0, 1]\}$. Lattice points $(m_1, m_2) \in P \cap \mathbb{Z}^2$: $m_2 = 2 b$ forces $b \in \{0, 1/2, 1\}$. For $b = 0$: $m_1 = a \in [0, 1]$, so $(0, 0)$ and $(1, 0)$. For $b = 1/2$: $m_1 = a + 1/2 \in [1/2, 3/2]$, so $m_1 = 1$, giving $(1, 1)$. For $b = 1$: $m_1 = a + 1 \in [1, 2]$, so $(1, 2)$ and $(2, 2)$. It is singular at the origin and smooth elsewhere. The singular point corresponds to the closed $T$-orbit of $U_\sigma$ — the $T$-fixed point given by the maximal ideal $(x, y, z)$. By Demazure's criterion, $U_\sigma$ is singular because the generators of $\sigma$ do not form a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis (determinant $2$). This is an alternative presentation of the same $A_1$ singularity obtained from $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_1 + 2 e_2)$ in the prerequisite unit — the cone-classification of $A_1$ singularities is up to $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ change of basis of $N$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Prove that the affine toric variety $U_\sigma$ is irreducible. Specifically, show that the semigroup algebra $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ has no zero divisors.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The semigroup $S_\sigma$ is contained in the group $M$, so it is cancellative and torsion-free. The semigroup algebra of a cancellative torsion-free monoid embeds into the group algebra, which is a Laurent polynomial ring.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The semigroup $S_\sigma = \sigma^\vee \cap M$ is a sub-monoid of the abelian group $M = \mathbb{Z}^n$. Hence $S_\sigma$ is **cancellative** ($a + b = a + c$ implies $b = c$ in $S_\sigma$, by the cancellation law in $M$) and **torsion-free** (no element has finite additive order, since $M$ has no torsion).

The semigroup algebra $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ embeds into the group algebra $\mathbb{C}[M] = \mathbb{C}[\mathbb{Z}^n] = \mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$ via the inclusion of monoids $S_\sigma \hookrightarrow M$. This injection is a $\mathbb{C}$-algebra map (preserves $\chi^m \chi^{m'} = \chi^{m + m'}$), and is injective on basis elements (different $m, m' \in S_\sigma$ map to different basis monomials in $\mathbb{C}[M]$).

The Laurent polynomial ring $\mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$ is a localisation of $\mathbb{C}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$, which is an integral domain. A localisation of an integral domain is an integral domain, so $\mathbb{C}[M]$ is an integral domain. A subring of an integral domain is an integral domain, hence $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ has no zero divisors.

Therefore $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is irreducible — the spectrum of any integral domain is irreducible (the zero ideal is prime). $\square$

Rubric: full credit for the cancellative + torsion-free observation, the embedding into the group algebra, and the integral-domain conclusion.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove the **smoothness criterion**: the affine toric variety $U_\sigma$ of a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone $\sigma$ of dimension $d \leq n$ is smooth as a complex algebraic variety if and only if $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(u_1, \ldots, u_d)$ with $\{u_1, \ldots, u_d\}$ extendable to a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the easy direction, assume the simplicial-unimodular hypothesis and change basis to set $u_i = e_i$ for $i \leq d$; show $U_\sigma \cong \mathbb{C}^d \times (\mathbb{C}^*)^{n-d}$. For the hard direction, compute the Zariski cotangent space at the closed $T$-fixed point and identify it with the Hilbert basis of $\sigma^\vee$; smoothness forces the Hilbert basis to be a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $M$, which dualises to the simplicial unimodular condition on $\sigma$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**($\Leftarrow$): simplicial unimodular implies smooth.** Assume $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(u_1, \ldots, u_d)$ with $\{u_1, \ldots, u_d\}$ extending to a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis $u_1, \ldots, u_n$ of $N$. Change basis so that $u_i = e_i$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$. Then $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, \ldots, e_d) = \{(a_1, \ldots, a_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n : a_i \geq 0 \text{ for } i \leq d, a_i = a_i \text{ free for } i > d\}$. Strictly, $\sigma = \mathbb{R}^d_{\geq 0} \times \{0\}$ inside $\mathbb{R}^n$, which is strongly convex of dimension $d$.

Compute the dual cone: $\sigma^\vee = \{m \in M_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, e_i\rangle \geq 0 \text{ for } i \leq d\} = \mathbb{R}^d_{\geq 0} \times \mathbb{R}^{n-d}$. The semigroup $S_\sigma = \sigma^\vee \cap M = \mathbb{Z}^d_{\geq 0} \times \mathbb{Z}^{n-d}$, a free monoid with $d$ "polynomial" generators $e_1^*, \ldots, e_d^*$ and $n - d$ "Laurent" generators $\pm e_{d+1}^*, \ldots, \pm e_n^*$.

The semigroup algebra is $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] = \mathbb{C}[x_1, \ldots, x_d, x_{d+1}^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$, and $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] = \mathbb{C}^d \times (\mathbb{C}^*)^{n-d}$. This is a smooth algebraic variety (a product of smooth varieties — affine space and a torus).

**($\Rightarrow$): smooth implies simplicial unimodular.** Conversely, suppose $U_\sigma$ is smooth. Let $\sigma$ have rays $\rho_1, \ldots, \rho_k$ with primitive ray generators $u_1, \ldots, u_k \in N$, with $k$ rays in $\sigma$ of dimension $d = \dim \sigma$. We show $k = d$ (simplicial) and $\{u_1, \ldots, u_d\}$ extends to a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$ (unimodular).

The closed $T$-orbit of $U_\sigma$ is the $T$-fixed point corresponding to the maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}_0 = \langle \chi^m : m \in S_\sigma, m \neq 0\rangle$ (the augmentation ideal). This is the unique maximal ideal stable under the $T$-action with all $\chi^m$ in $\mathfrak{m}_0$ for $m \neq 0$; it corresponds to evaluating all monomials to zero except the identity $\chi^0 = 1$.

The Zariski cotangent space at this fixed point is $\mathfrak{m}_0 / \mathfrak{m}_0^2$. Compute: $\mathfrak{m}_0 = \bigoplus_{m \in S_\sigma \setminus \{0\}} \mathbb{C} \chi^m$ and $\mathfrak{m}_0^2 = \bigoplus_{m = m_1 + m_2, m_i \in S_\sigma \setminus \{0\}} \mathbb{C} \chi^m$, so
$$
\mathfrak{m}_0 / \mathfrak{m}_0^2 \cong \bigoplus_{m \in \mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee)} \mathbb{C} \chi^m,
$$
the $\mathbb{C}$-vector space spanned by the Hilbert basis of $\sigma^\vee$ (the indecomposable elements of $S_\sigma \setminus \{0\}$).

Smoothness at the fixed point requires $\dim_\mathbb{C} \mathfrak{m}_0/\mathfrak{m}_0^2 = \dim U_\sigma = n$ (since the dimension at a smooth point equals the Krull dimension of the local ring, equivalently the cotangent dimension). Hence $|\mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee)| = n$. But the Hilbert basis must generate $S_\sigma$ as a monoid and $S_\sigma$ spans $M_\mathbb{R}$, so the Hilbert basis must span $M_\mathbb{R}$. Hence the Hilbert basis is exactly $n$ vectors spanning $M_\mathbb{R}$, so it is a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $M$ — for if it spanned $M_\mathbb{R}$ with $n$ vectors but the $\mathbb{Z}$-span were a proper sublattice $M' \subsetneq M$, the index $[M : M']$ would be $\geq 2$ and the semigroup $S_\sigma$ would not generate all of $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ from these $n$ vectors (interior lattice points would be missing), contradicting that the Hilbert basis generates $S_\sigma$.

Now $\sigma^\vee = \mathrm{Cone}(\mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee))$ is generated by $n$ vectors forming a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $M$, so $\sigma^\vee$ is simplicial unimodular in $M_\mathbb{R}$. Dualising (the dual of a simplicial unimodular cone is simplicial unimodular, by cone duality and the $\mathbb{Z}$-basis property), $\sigma$ is simplicial unimodular in $N_\mathbb{R}$ — generated by $d$ rays $u_1, \ldots, u_d$ that extend to a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$.

The two directions combine into the smoothness criterion. $\square$

Rubric: full credit for both directions, the cotangent-space identification with the Hilbert basis, and the dimension count $|\mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee)| = n$.

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove the **toric ideal presentation**: for $\sigma$ a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone with Hilbert basis $\mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee) = \{m_1, \ldots, m_s\}$, the affine toric variety $U_\sigma$ is isomorphic to the vanishing locus $V(I_\sigma) \subseteq \mathbb{C}^s$ of the toric ideal
$$
I_\sigma = \langle X^a - X^b : a, b \in \mathbb{Z}^s_{\geq 0}, \sum_i a_i m_i = \sum_i b_i m_i \text{ in } M \rangle,
$$
the ideal generated by all binomial relations among the Hilbert basis elements. Equivalently, $I_\sigma$ is the kernel of the surjection $\mathbb{C}[X_1, \ldots, X_s] \twoheadrightarrow \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$, $X_i \mapsto \chi^{m_i}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The surjection $\mathbb{C}[X_1, \ldots, X_s] \twoheadrightarrow \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ exists because the $m_i$ generate $S_\sigma$. The kernel is a homogeneous prime ideal — homogeneous in the $\mathbb{Z}^s$ multi-grading by $\deg X_i = e_i$, prime because $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is an integral domain. Generators of the kernel are differences of monomials with the same image in $S_\sigma$, namely binomials.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\phi : \mathbb{C}[X_1, \ldots, X_s] \to \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ be the $\mathbb{C}$-algebra map sending $X_i \mapsto \chi^{m_i}$. Since the $m_i$ generate $S_\sigma$ as a monoid, $\phi$ is surjective: every $\chi^m$ with $m \in S_\sigma$ is the image of some monomial $X^a = X_1^{a_1} \cdots X_s^{a_s}$ with $\sum_i a_i m_i = m$.

**Kernel is binomial.** Let $I_\sigma = \ker \phi$. Take any element $f \in I_\sigma$ and write it as a $\mathbb{C}$-linear combination of monomials, $f = \sum_a c_a X^a$. The image $\phi(f) = \sum_a c_a \chi^{\phi_M(a)} = 0$ in $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$, where $\phi_M : \mathbb{Z}^s_{\geq 0} \to S_\sigma$ is the monoid map $a \mapsto \sum_i a_i m_i$.

Group the monomials by their image under $\phi_M$: let $A_m = \{a \in \mathbb{Z}^s_{\geq 0} : \phi_M(a) = m\}$ for each $m \in S_\sigma$. Then $\phi(f) = \sum_{m} \left(\sum_{a \in A_m} c_a\right) \chi^m = 0$, and since the $\chi^m$ are $\mathbb{C}$-linearly independent in $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$, we get $\sum_{a \in A_m} c_a = 0$ for each $m$.

For each $m$ with $|A_m| > 1$, fix one $a^* \in A_m$ and write $\sum_{a \in A_m} c_a X^a = \sum_{a \in A_m, a \neq a^*} c_a (X^a - X^{a^*}) + (\sum_{a \in A_m} c_a) X^{a^*}$. The last term vanishes (since the inner sum is zero), so $\sum_{a \in A_m} c_a X^a = \sum_{a \neq a^*} c_a (X^a - X^{a^*})$, a $\mathbb{C}$-linear combination of binomials $X^a - X^{a^*}$ with $\phi_M(a) = \phi_M(a^*) = m$. Summing over all $m$, $f$ is a $\mathbb{C}$-linear combination of binomials $X^a - X^b$ with $\phi_M(a) = \phi_M(b)$.

Hence $I_\sigma$ is generated (as a $\mathbb{C}$-vector space, and a fortiori as an ideal) by the binomials $X^a - X^b$ with $\sum_i a_i m_i = \sum_i b_i m_i$ in $S_\sigma$. The toric ideal is binomial.

**Geometric interpretation.** $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] = \mathrm{Spec}\,(\mathbb{C}[X_1, \ldots, X_s]/I_\sigma) = V(I_\sigma) \subseteq \mathbb{C}^s$. The embedding is $T$-equivariant: each $X_i$ has $M$-weight $m_i$, and the action $T \curvearrowright \mathbb{C}^s$ is via the weights. The dimension of $V(I_\sigma)$ is $n$ (the dimension of $U_\sigma$), and the codimension in $\mathbb{C}^s$ is $s - n$, the number of independent binomial relations.

**Concrete example.** For $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, e_1 + 2 e_2)$ from `04.11.02` Exercise 3, the Hilbert basis is $\{(0, 1), (1, 0), (2, -1)\}$ with $X_1, X_2, X_3$ corresponding monomials, and the binomial relation $(1, 0) + (1, 0) = (0, 1) + (2, -1)$ gives $X_2^2 - X_1 X_3 \in I_\sigma$. The toric ideal is principal here: $I_\sigma = (X_2^2 - X_1 X_3)$, and $U_\sigma = V(X_2^2 - X_1 X_3) \subseteq \mathbb{C}^3$ is the affine quadric — the $A_1$ Du Val singularity.

Rubric: full credit for the surjection, the binomial-kernel argument, the geometric identification, and the concrete-example verification.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (functoriality of the affine-toric construction).** *The assignment $\sigma \mapsto U_\sigma$ is a contravariant functor from the category $\mathbf{Cone}_N$ of strongly convex rational polyhedral cones in $N_\mathbb{R}$ (with face inclusions as morphisms) to the category $\mathbf{AffToric}_T$ of affine $T$-toric varieties (with $T$-equivariant morphisms). The functor is fully faithful on face inclusions and produces $T$-equivariant open immersions on morphisms.*

The functoriality theorem is the formal content of part (d) of the main theorem above. Its categorical packaging is the proper home for the cone-to-variety dictionary: every face inclusion $\tau \hookrightarrow \sigma$ gives a $T$-equivariant open immersion $U_\tau \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$, and composition of face inclusions corresponds to composition of open immersions. The dual functor $\mathbf{Cone}_N^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{AffToric}_T$ is what allows the global toric variety $X_\Sigma$ of `04.11.04` to be constructed by gluing along these open immersions — the **gluing lemma** for $X_\Sigma$ is exactly the statement that the functor $\sigma \mapsto U_\sigma$ extends from cones to fans by colimit.

**Theorem (Sumihiro's equivariant covering; Sumihiro 1974).** *Let $X$ be a normal algebraic variety with an action of an algebraic torus $T$. Then $X$ admits an open cover by $T$-stable affine subvarieties.*

Sumihiro's theorem (originally proved in *Equivariant completion*, Journal of Mathematics of Kyoto University 14, 1974, pp. 1-28) is the foundational result that every normal $T$-variety is "locally toric": the affine pieces $U_\sigma$ developed here are sufficient to reconstruct any normal $T$-variety. In the toric setting (where $T$ acts with a dense open orbit), Sumihiro's theorem combined with the affine-toric construction implies that every normal toric variety is of the form $X_\Sigma$ for some fan $\Sigma$. This is the foundational reason that the cone-and-fan formalism is exhaustive — it captures all normal toric varieties, not just a special class.

**Theorem (universal property of $U_\sigma$).** *Let $T = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M]$ be a torus and let $S_\sigma = \sigma^\vee \cap M$ for a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone $\sigma$. The affine toric variety $U_\sigma$ represents the functor*
$$
\mathbf{Aff}_\mathbb{C}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}, \qquad R \mapsto \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Mon}}(S_\sigma, (R, \cdot))
$$
*from commutative $\mathbb{C}$-algebras (opposite of affine schemes) to sets, sending $R$ to the set of monoid homomorphisms from the additive semigroup $S_\sigma$ to the multiplicative semigroup $(R, \cdot)$. Equivalently, $U_\sigma(R) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Mon}}(S_\sigma, (R, \cdot))$ for every $\mathbb{C}$-algebra $R$.*

The universal property identifies $U_\sigma$ as the **moduli space of monoid homomorphisms from $S_\sigma$**. This is exactly what $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ does in general for any monoid: $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbb{C}\text{-alg}}(\mathbb{C}[S], R) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Mon}}(S, (R, \cdot))$. The toric specialisation makes this universal property concrete and geometrically meaningful: a $\mathbb{C}$-point of $U_\sigma$ is a choice of complex number $\zeta^m \in \mathbb{C}$ for each $m \in S_\sigma$ such that $\zeta^{m + m'} = \zeta^m \zeta^{m'}$ and $\zeta^0 = 1$ — that is, a multiplicative "evaluation" of $S_\sigma$ in $\mathbb{C}$.

**Theorem (normality and Cohen-Macaulayness; Hochster 1972).** *Let $\sigma$ be a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone. Then $\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ is a normal Cohen-Macaulay domain. In particular, $U_\sigma$ is normal and Cohen-Macaulay.*

Hochster's theorem (*Rings of invariants of tori, Cohen-Macaulay rings generated by monomials, and polytopes*, Annals of Mathematics 96, 1972, pp. 318-337) is the foundational structure theorem for affine semigroup rings of the form $\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$. Normality is essentially the content of the main theorem above; Cohen-Macaulayness is a stronger property requiring the depth of the maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}_0$ (the augmentation ideal at the $T$-fixed point) to equal the Krull dimension $n$. Hochster's proof uses the $T$-action and the structure of the semigroup $S_\sigma$ to construct a regular sequence of length $n$ in $\mathfrak{m}_0$, exhibiting Cohen-Macaulayness directly. Cohen-Macaulayness has substantial consequences for toric singularities: every singular toric variety is rational and Cohen-Macaulay, hence has well-behaved local cohomology and good intersection-theoretic properties.

**Theorem (resolution of toric singularities via simplicial subdivision).** *Let $\sigma$ be a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone of dimension $d$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$. There exists a fan $\Sigma'$ refining the fan $\{\text{faces of } \sigma\}$ such that every cone in $\Sigma'$ is simplicial unimodular. The corresponding gluing $X_{\Sigma'}$ is a smooth toric variety, and the natural map $X_{\Sigma'} \to U_\sigma$ is a $T$-equivariant projective birational morphism — a **toric resolution** of $U_\sigma$.*

The toric resolution theorem (in this form due to Demazure 1970 and KKMS 1973, with algorithmic refinement by Bouvier-Gonzalez-Sprinberg 1995) makes resolution of singularities **combinatorially explicit** for toric varieties. The simplicial subdivision is performed by iteratively adding rays to $\sigma$, splitting non-simplicial-unimodular subcones into smaller pieces. The result is a smooth toric variety mapping properly and birationally to the original $U_\sigma$. For surfaces (dimension $2$), the simplicial-unimodular subdivision is exactly the **continued-fraction expansion** of the cone's index, recovering the classical Hirzebruch-Jung resolution of cyclic-quotient singularities $\mathbb{C}^2/\mu_n$. Hirzebruch's 1953 paper *Über vierdimensionale Riemannsche Flächen mehrdeutiger analytischer Funktionen von zwei komplexen Veränderlichen* (Mathematische Annalen 126, 1-22) computed these resolutions for surface singularities, and the connection to toric geometry was made explicit by Demazure.

**Theorem (orbit-cone correspondence; foreshadowing `04.11.05`).** *Let $\sigma$ be a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone in $N_\mathbb{R}$ of dimension $d$. The $T$-orbits in $U_\sigma$ are in inclusion-reversing bijection with the faces of $\sigma$: a face $\tau$ of dimension $k$ corresponds to a $T$-orbit $O_\tau \subseteq U_\sigma$ of dimension $n - k$. The closures of orbits stratify $U_\sigma$, and the closure $\overline{O_\tau}$ contains $O_{\tau'}$ if and only if $\tau \subseteq \tau'$ (as faces of $\sigma$).*

This is the **orbit-cone correspondence**, the central structural result connecting the combinatorics of $\sigma$ to the equivariant geometry of $U_\sigma$. Its proof (deferred to `04.11.05`) uses the face-functoriality from this unit together with the universal property of $U_\sigma$ to identify each orbit with the spectrum of a localisation of the semigroup algebra. The orbit corresponding to the zero face $\{0\}$ is the open dense orbit $T \subseteq U_\sigma$; the orbit corresponding to the full cone $\sigma$ (when $\sigma$ has dimension $n$) is the unique closed $T$-fixed point.

**Synthesis.** The affine toric variety $U_\sigma$ is the combinatorial bridge from rational polyhedral cones to algebraic geometry, and the central insight is that the semigroup algebra $\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ — manifestly a finitely generated commutative $\mathbb{C}$-algebra by Gordan's lemma — has a coordinate-ring interpretation as the regular functions on a $T$-equivariant affine algebraic variety, with the $T$-action read off mechanically from the $M$-grading. Three structural results — finite generation (Gordan), normality (Hochster), and the smoothness criterion (Demazure) — combine into a single dictionary: the geometric properties of $U_\sigma$ are determined by the lattice combinatorics of the cone $\sigma$ and its dual. The bridge is exactly the duality of `04.11.01`: the cone $\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ encodes which characters of $T$ extend to regular functions on $U_\sigma$ (namely those in $\sigma^\vee \cap M$) and which extend only to rational functions, and this asymmetry is the entire reason that the toric variety $U_\sigma$ is a $T$-equivariant *partial* compactification of $T$ rather than the full torus itself.

Putting these together, the construction $\sigma \mapsto U_\sigma$ identifies the category of strongly convex rational polyhedral cones (with face inclusions) with a category of affine $T$-toric varieties (with $T$-equivariant open immersions), and this functor extends to the global toric construction $X_\Sigma$ of `04.11.04` via colimits over fans. The foundational reason that toric geometry is **effectively combinatorial** is that this functor is fully faithful and structure-preserving — every geometric question about a normal $T$-variety reduces, via Sumihiro's theorem on equivariant covers and the affine model developed here, to a combinatorial question about cones and their lattice-point semigroups. This is exactly what makes toric geometry a working laboratory for testing conjectures in algebraic geometry: the geometric side is rich enough to model substantive phenomena (resolution of singularities, intersection theory, Hodge theory, mirror symmetry), while the combinatorial side is concrete enough to compute by hand or by software (Macaulay2, Polymake, Normaliz, GAP).

The affine model also generalises in three directions worth recording. To **non-strongly-convex cones** (where $\sigma$ contains a positive-dimensional linear subspace), the construction $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ still produces an affine variety, but the lineality space of $\sigma$ corresponds to a sub-torus acting by the identity action, so $U_\sigma$ becomes a torus bundle over the strongly-convex quotient $\sigma / (\sigma \cap (-\sigma))$. The pattern recurs in the **toroidal-embeddings** setting of KKMS 1973, where non-strongly-convex cones encode degenerate toroidal compactifications. To **non-rational cones** (where the cone generators are irrational), the semigroup $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ may fail to be finitely generated (Gordan's lemma requires rationality), so the affine model can degenerate to an infinite-type scheme; this is the regime of **Berkovich analytic toric geometry** developed by Berkovich (1990) and used in tropical geometry. To **arbitrary base rings** (replacing $\mathbb{C}$ by a commutative ring $R$), the construction $\mathrm{Spec}\,R[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ produces an affine $R$-scheme with a $T_R$-action; the resulting **toric schemes over $R$** specialise to toric varieties over fields and degenerate to combinatorial Stanley-Reisner schemes over $\mathbb{Z}$, providing the foundation for the **integral models** of toric varieties used in arithmetic geometry. Each generalisation preserves the cone-to-variety dictionary developed here, with technical modifications recording the deviation from the strongly-convex rational case over $\mathbb{C}$.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (irreducibility of $U_\sigma$), proof.** Given in Exercise 4: the semigroup $S_\sigma \subseteq M$ is cancellative and torsion-free; the semigroup algebra $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ embeds into the group algebra $\mathbb{C}[M] = \mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$, a Laurent polynomial ring which is a localisation of $\mathbb{C}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$ and hence an integral domain; therefore $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is an integral domain and $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is irreducible. $\square$

**Proposition (smoothness criterion), proof.** Given in Exercise 7: ($\Leftarrow$) When $\sigma = \mathrm{Cone}(e_1, \ldots, e_d)$ with $\{e_i\}$ part of a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $N$, computation gives $U_\sigma = \mathbb{C}^d \times (\mathbb{C}^*)^{n-d}$, smooth. ($\Rightarrow$) When $U_\sigma$ is smooth at the closed $T$-fixed point, the Zariski cotangent space identifies with the Hilbert basis of $\sigma^\vee$, smoothness forces $|\mathrm{Hilb}(\sigma^\vee)| = n$ and the Hilbert basis to be a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $M$; dualising gives the simplicial-unimodular condition on $\sigma$. $\square$

**Proposition (toric ideal presentation), proof.** Given in Exercise 8: the surjection $\mathbb{C}[X_1, \ldots, X_s] \twoheadrightarrow \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$, $X_i \mapsto \chi^{m_i}$, has kernel generated by binomials $X^a - X^b$ with $\sum a_i m_i = \sum b_i m_i$ in $S_\sigma$, by the monomial-grouping argument; hence $U_\sigma \cong V(I_\sigma) \subseteq \mathbb{C}^s$ for the toric ideal $I_\sigma$. $\square$

**Proposition (normality of $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$), proof.** Given in part (a) of the main theorem: $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] = \bigcap_j \mathbb{C}[M]_{\langle u_j, -\rangle \geq 0}$ where $u_j$ are the primitive ray generators of $\sigma$; each $\mathbb{C}[M]_{\langle u_j, -\rangle \geq 0}$ is a valuation subring of $\mathbb{C}(M)$ with valuation $v_{u_j}(\chi^m) = \langle u_j, m\rangle$; an intersection of valuation rings is integrally closed; hence $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is normal. $\square$

**Proposition (face-functoriality), proof.** Given in part (d) of the main theorem: for $\tau$ a face of $\sigma$ with $\tau = \sigma \cap H_{m_\tau}$ for $m_\tau$ in the relative interior of $\sigma^\vee \cap \tau^\perp$, the semigroup relation $S_\tau = S_\sigma + \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0} \cdot (-m_\tau)$ realises $\mathbb{C}[S_\tau]$ as the localisation $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]_{\chi^{m_\tau}}$; the corresponding open immersion $U_\tau = D(\chi^{m_\tau}) \subseteq U_\sigma$ is $T$-equivariant since $\chi^{m_\tau}$ is a $T$-semi-invariant of weight $m_\tau$, and localisation at a semi-invariant preserves the $M$-grading. $\square$

**Proposition (universal property of $U_\sigma$), proof.** *Let $T = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M]$ and let $S_\sigma$ be the semigroup associated to a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone $\sigma$. For every commutative $\mathbb{C}$-algebra $R$, there is a natural bijection $U_\sigma(R) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbb{C}\text{-alg}}(\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma], R) \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Mon}}(S_\sigma, (R, \cdot))$ given by $\phi \mapsto (m \mapsto \phi(\chi^m))$.*

*Proof.* The forward map sends a $\mathbb{C}$-algebra homomorphism $\phi : \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \to R$ to the function $\psi : S_\sigma \to R$ defined by $\psi(m) = \phi(\chi^m)$. This function is a monoid homomorphism to the multiplicative monoid $(R, \cdot)$: $\psi(m + m') = \phi(\chi^{m + m'}) = \phi(\chi^m \chi^{m'}) = \phi(\chi^m) \phi(\chi^{m'}) = \psi(m) \psi(m')$, and $\psi(0) = \phi(\chi^0) = \phi(1) = 1$.

The inverse map sends a monoid homomorphism $\psi : S_\sigma \to (R, \cdot)$ to the $\mathbb{C}$-algebra homomorphism $\phi : \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \to R$ extending $\chi^m \mapsto \psi(m)$ by $\mathbb{C}$-linearity: $\phi(\sum_m c_m \chi^m) = \sum_m c_m \psi(m)$ (a finite sum, since elements of $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ have finite support).

The two maps are mutually inverse and natural in $R$, by direct computation: starting with $\phi$, the round trip is $\phi \mapsto \psi \mapsto (\sum c_m \chi^m \mapsto \sum c_m \psi(m) = \sum c_m \phi(\chi^m) = \phi(\sum c_m \chi^m))$, the original $\phi$. Naturality in $R$ records that pre-composition with a $\mathbb{C}$-algebra map $R \to R'$ commutes with the bijection.

Hence $U_\sigma$ represents the functor $R \mapsto \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Mon}}(S_\sigma, (R, \cdot))$, the moduli space of monoid homomorphisms from $S_\sigma$. $\square$

**Proposition (functoriality of $\sigma \mapsto U_\sigma$), proof.** *The assignment $\sigma \mapsto U_\sigma$ extends to a contravariant functor $\mathbf{Cone}_N \to \mathbf{AffToric}_T$, with the morphism induced by a face inclusion $\tau \hookrightarrow \sigma$ being the $T$-equivariant open immersion $U_\tau \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$.*

*Proof.* The face-functoriality (proven in part (d) of the main theorem) gives the action on morphisms. We verify the functor laws:

*Identity.* The identity face inclusion $\sigma \hookrightarrow \sigma$ corresponds to the identity $U_\sigma \to U_\sigma$, since $\sigma^\vee \cap \sigma^\perp = \sigma^\vee \cap N_\mathbb{R}^\perp = \sigma^\vee$ has $m_\sigma = 0$ in its relative interior, giving the identity localisation $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]_{\chi^0} = \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]_{1} = \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$.

*Composition.* Let $\tau_1 \subseteq \tau_2 \subseteq \sigma$ be a chain of face inclusions. By the face-correspondence theorem of `04.11.02`, $\tau_1$ is a face of $\tau_2$ and $\tau_2$ is a face of $\sigma$. Choose $m_{\tau_2} \in \mathrm{relint}(\sigma^\vee \cap \tau_2^\perp)$ and $m_{\tau_1} \in \mathrm{relint}(\tau_2^\vee \cap \tau_1^\perp) = \mathrm{relint}(\sigma^\vee \cap \tau_1^\perp)$, with appropriate adjustment so that $m_{\tau_1}$ is also in the closure of $\sigma^\vee \cap \tau_2^\perp$ (achievable by perturbation if necessary). Then $\mathbb{C}[S_{\tau_1}] = \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]_{\chi^{m_{\tau_1}}}$, which factors through $\mathbb{C}[S_{\tau_2}] = \mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]_{\chi^{m_{\tau_2}}}$ since $\chi^{m_{\tau_1}}$ is already invertible in the larger localisation: explicitly, the inclusion $\tau_1 \subseteq \tau_2$ on faces gives $\sigma^\vee \cap \tau_2^\perp \subseteq \sigma^\vee \cap \tau_1^\perp$ (the larger face has the smaller annihilator), so inverting $\chi^{m_{\tau_1}}$ in $\mathbb{C}[S_{\tau_2}]$ is a further localisation. Composition of open immersions of affine schemes corresponds to iterated localisation, which is a single localisation at the product of the two generators, matching the composite face inclusion $\tau_1 \hookrightarrow \sigma$.

*Contravariance.* The functor reverses arrows: a face inclusion $\tau \hookrightarrow \sigma$ in $\mathbf{Cone}_N$ produces an open immersion $U_\tau \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$ in $\mathbf{AffToric}_T$ — both arrows going the same direction, since both face inclusion and open immersion are "into the larger object". The contravariance shows up at the level of coordinate rings: $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma] \hookrightarrow \mathbb{C}[S_\tau]$ is an inclusion going in the *opposite* direction to the face inclusion, since the semigroup gets bigger when the cone gets smaller (more lattice points become available in the dual).

Hence $\sigma \mapsto U_\sigma$ is a (contravariant on coordinate rings, covariant on schemes) functor from $\mathbf{Cone}_N$ to $\mathbf{AffToric}_T$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Algebraic torus and character/cocharacter lattices [04.11.01].** The torus $T = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M]$ and its character/cocharacter lattices $(M, N)$ developed in the prerequisite unit supply the ambient algebraic-group setting for the affine toric variety. The torus is itself the affine toric variety of the zero cone: $U_{\{0\}} = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M] = T$. The $M$-grading on the semigroup algebra $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is exactly the Hopf-comodule structure under the $T$-Hopf-algebra $\mathbb{C}[M]$, and the $T$-action on $U_\sigma$ is the dualised comodule structure. Every step of the affine-toric-variety construction reads directly from the lattice data $(M, N, \sigma)$ developed in `04.11.01` and `04.11.02`.

- **Rational polyhedral cone and dual cone [04.11.02].** The cone $\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ and its dual $\sigma^\vee \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ from the prerequisite unit are the combinatorial input data of the affine-toric-variety construction. Gordan's lemma (finite generation of $\sigma^\vee \cap M$) is the foundational reason that $\mathbb{C}[S_\sigma]$ is a finitely generated $\mathbb{C}$-algebra and hence that $U_\sigma$ is an affine variety of finite type. The face-correspondence theorem (inclusion-reversing bijection $\sigma \leftrightarrow \sigma^\vee$) becomes the face-functoriality of $U_\sigma$ developed here: a face $\tau \subseteq \sigma$ produces a $T$-equivariant open immersion $U_\tau \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$ via localisation of the semigroup algebra.

- **Affine scheme [04.02.02].** The affine toric variety $U_\sigma$ is by definition an affine scheme: $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$, with closed points the maximal ideals of the semigroup algebra. The general affine-scheme formalism — $\mathrm{Spec}$, the equivalence between commutative rings and affine schemes, the structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_{U_\sigma}$ — supplies the geometric apparatus that makes the toric construction algebraic-geometric rather than purely combinatorial. The functoriality $\sigma \mapsto U_\sigma$ inherits the contravariance of $\mathrm{Spec}$ from this unit's formalism.

- **Fan and toric variety $X_\Sigma$ [04.11.04].** The next unit in the toric chapter constructs the global toric variety $X_\Sigma$ from a **fan** $\Sigma$ — a finite collection of rational polyhedral cones in $N_\mathbb{R}$ closed under taking faces and intersections — by gluing the affine pieces $U_\sigma$ for $\sigma \in \Sigma$ along the open immersions $U_{\sigma \cap \sigma'} \hookrightarrow U_\sigma, U_{\sigma'}$ produced by the face-functoriality developed here. The affine-toric-variety construction is therefore the **local building block** of toric geometry, and the global construction reads $X_\Sigma$ as the colimit of the affine pieces in the category of $T$-toric varieties.

- **Orbit-cone correspondence [04.11.05].** The orbit-cone correspondence (developed in the unit two ahead) identifies the $T$-orbits in $U_\sigma$ with the faces of $\sigma$ via $\tau \leftrightarrow O_\tau$, with the orbit corresponding to a face of dimension $k$ having dimension $n - k$. The closure ordering on orbits matches the reverse face-inclusion on $\sigma$. This identification specialises the face-correspondence theorem of `04.11.02` to the equivariant geometry of $U_\sigma$, and the proof uses the face-functoriality and universal property developed in this unit.

- **Smoothness, étaleness, and unramified morphisms [04.02.05].** The smoothness criterion of Demazure 1970 — $U_\sigma$ is smooth iff $\sigma$ is simplicial unimodular — is a special case of the general smoothness theory of affine schemes from this unit. The cotangent-space computation at the closed $T$-fixed point identifies the cotangent dimension with the size of the Hilbert basis of $\sigma^\vee$, and smoothness forces this to equal $n$. The toric resolution theorem (refining a fan to make every cone simplicial unimodular) is the toric implementation of Hironaka's resolution of singularities, and reduces resolution to combinatorial fan refinement.

- **Spec of a commutative ring [04.02.01].** The $\mathrm{Spec}$ functor from commutative rings to affine schemes is the foundation of the affine-toric-variety construction: $U_\sigma$ is literally $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$. The functoriality of $\mathrm{Spec}$ (contravariant from commutative rings to affine schemes) supplies the contravariance of $\sigma \mapsto U_\sigma$ on coordinate rings, with the face-inclusion-to-open-immersion correspondence being a specialisation of the localisation-to-open-immersion correspondence in $\mathrm{Spec}$.

- **Polytope-toric correspondence [04.11.11].** The polytope-toric correspondence (developed several units ahead) builds projective toric varieties $X_P$ from rational lattice polytopes $P \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ via the cone $C(P) = \mathrm{Cone}(P \times \{1\}) \subseteq M_\mathbb{R} \oplus \mathbb{R}$ and the projective spectrum $\mathrm{Proj}\,\mathbb{C}[C(P)^\vee \cap (N \oplus \mathbb{Z})]$. The affine pieces of $X_P$ at each vertex are exactly affine toric varieties $U_{\sigma_v}$ for the corresponding "vertex cone" $\sigma_v$ of $P$, so the polytope-toric construction reduces to gluing affine toric varieties developed in this unit.

- **Resolution of singularities [04.06.02].** The toric resolution theorem (every singular toric variety admits a $T$-equivariant resolution by refining the fan) is the toric implementation of Hironaka's theorem on resolution of singularities. The simplicial-unimodular subdivision is performed combinatorially on the fan, and the resulting smooth toric variety maps properly and birationally to the original. For toric surfaces, this recovers the **Hirzebruch-Jung resolution** of cyclic-quotient singularities via continued-fraction expansion, a classical result that predates toric geometry by two decades.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The affine toric variety $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ was first written down in modern form by Michel Demazure in *Sous-groupes algébriques de rang maximum du groupe de Cremona* (Annales scientifiques de l'École normale supérieure, 4e série, tome 3, 1970, pp. 507-588) [ref: TODO_REF Demazure 1970]. Demazure's program was to classify the algebraic subgroups of the Cremona group $\mathrm{Cr}_n = \mathrm{Bir}(\mathbb{P}^n)$ of maximal rank, and the cone-and-fan formalism arose as the combinatorial language needed to enumerate the affine $T$-equivariant compactifications of the torus. The smoothness criterion (simplicial unimodular cones produce smooth $U_\sigma$) and the functoriality on face inclusions are due to Demazure, who also introduced the term "espace torique" — the French original of "toric variety".

The scheme-theoretic foundation of the affine-toric-variety construction was systematised by George Kempf, Finn Faye Knudsen, David Mumford, and Bernard Saint-Donat in *Toroidal Embeddings I* (Lecture Notes in Mathematics 339, Springer 1973) [ref: TODO_REF], where the cone-and-fan formalism is recast as a tool for resolving singularities and compactifying equivariant varieties over arbitrary base schemes. KKMS's "toroidal embedding" is the local-affine model for varieties that look étale-locally like affine toric varieties; the global toric setting (where the variety is genuinely a torus embedding) is the rigid combinatorial special case. Hideyasu Sumihiro's *Equivariant completion* (Journal of Mathematics of Kyoto University 14, 1974, pp. 1-28) [ref: TODO_REF Sumihiro 1974] established the foundational theorem that every normal $T$-variety admits a $T$-equivariant affine open cover — a result that, applied to normal toric varieties, identifies the affine toric pieces $U_\sigma$ developed here as **the** building blocks of toric geometry. Without Sumihiro's theorem, one would have only the affine-local picture; with it, every normal toric variety is captured by a fan.

The semigroup-ring perspective on $\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ has roots earlier, in the **invariant theory** of finite group actions on polynomial rings. Hilbert's 1890 paper *Über die Theorie der algebraischen Formen* (Mathematische Annalen 36) [ref: TODO_REF] proved the finite generation of invariant rings of finite groups, the **First Fundamental Theorem of invariant theory**; Gordan's 1873 paper [ref: TODO_REF Gordan 1873] proved the finite generation of nonnegative integer solutions to linear inequalities, the **semigroup version** that became Gordan's lemma. Melvin Hochster's *Rings of invariants of tori, Cohen-Macaulay rings generated by monomials, and polytopes* (Annals of Mathematics 96, 1972, pp. 318-337) [ref: TODO_REF] unified these threads, proving that the semigroup ring $\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ is normal Cohen-Macaulay for every strongly convex rational polyhedral cone — the foundational structure theorem for toric singularities.

Tadao Oda's *Convex Bodies and Algebraic Geometry* (Springer 1988) [ref: TODO_REF] consolidated the Japanese-school perspective, with emphasis on the dual-cone formalism, the affine-toric construction, and the orbit-cone correspondence; William Fulton's *Introduction to Toric Varieties* (Princeton 1993) [ref: TODO_REF Fulton 1993] is the canonical short textbook, and David Cox, John Little, and Henry Schenck's *Toric Varieties* (AMS 2011) [ref: TODO_REF] is the modern thousand-page treatment. David Cox's 1995 paper *The homogeneous coordinate ring of a toric variety* (Journal of Algebraic Geometry 4, 17-50) [ref: TODO_REF Cox 1995] introduced the **total coordinate ring** of a toric variety, reframing the affine pieces $U_\sigma$ as localisations of a single graded ring at irrelevant ideals — a perspective that unifies projective and toric geometry under a common functorial roof.
