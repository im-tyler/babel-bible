## Intuition [Beginner]

An algebraic torus is the multiplicative group $(\mathbb{C}^*)^n$ — $n$-tuples of nonzero complex numbers, multiplied coordinate-by-coordinate — viewed not just as a Lie group but as an algebraic variety, the open subset of $\mathbb{C}^n$ on which every coordinate is nonzero. The single most important fact about this variety is that its symmetries — that is, its algebraic homomorphisms into and out of the one-dimensional torus $\mathbb{C}^*$ — are classified by two copies of the integer lattice $\mathbb{Z}^n$. One lattice records the maps out of $T$ (the characters); the other records the maps into $T$ (the one-parameter subgroups). The two lattices are dual: pair them and you get an integer.

Why bother? Because every piece of geometry built from an algebraic torus — affine toric varieties, fans, projective toric varieties, mirror polytopes — is encoded combinatorially in these two lattices and the duality between them. A polyhedral cone in one lattice produces an affine variety on which the torus acts; a fan of such cones glues them into a global variety. The lattice formalism turns the geometry of toric varieties into the combinatorics of polytopes, and the dictionary works because the characters and one-parameter subgroups of the torus are already integer-valued.

A second reason: the algebraic torus is the simplest shape-bearing algebraic group beyond affine space and the additive group. Every result about general algebraic groups specialises cleanly on a torus, and most of the structure theory of reductive algebraic groups (Borel, Chevalley, Demazure) is organised by maximal-torus data — the same characters and cocharacters that anchor toric geometry.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the two-dimensional torus $T = (\mathbb{C}^*)^2$ with coordinates $(x, y)$. Pick a character — a Laurent monomial — and a one-parameter subgroup, and compute how they compose.

Step 1. The character $\chi^{(1, -2)}$ is the map $T \to \mathbb{C}^*$ sending $(x, y) \mapsto x^1 y^{-2} = x / y^2$. It is determined by the integer pair $(1, -2)$ in the character lattice $M = \mathbb{Z}^2$.

Step 2. The one-parameter subgroup $\lambda_{(3, 1)}$ is the map $\mathbb{C}^* \to T$ sending $t \mapsto (t^3, t^1)$. It is determined by the integer pair $(3, 1)$ in the cocharacter lattice $N = \mathbb{Z}^2$.

Step 3. Compose them: starting with $t \in \mathbb{C}^*$, the one-parameter subgroup sends it to $(t^3, t) \in T$, and then the character sends that to $(t^3)^1 \cdot (t)^{-2} = t^3 / t^2 = t$. So $\chi^{(1, -2)}(\lambda_{(3, 1)}(t)) = t^1$.

Step 4. Cross-check with the integer pairing. The dot product of $(1, -2)$ and $(3, 1)$ is $1 \cdot 3 + (-2) \cdot 1 = 3 - 2 = 1$. The composition is exponentiation to the power of this dot product: $\chi^{(1, -2)} \circ \lambda_{(3, 1)} = t \mapsto t^1$. The exponent matches.

What this tells us. Composing a character and a one-parameter subgroup always produces a map $\mathbb{C}^* \to \mathbb{C}^*$, and every such map is exponentiation $t \mapsto t^k$ for some integer $k$. That integer $k$ is the dot product of the character's integer label and the one-parameter subgroup's integer label. The pairing between the two integer lattices is the dot product, and it is the bookkeeping that makes the algebraic torus into a combinatorial object — two lattices with an integer pairing.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $k$ be an algebraically closed field; in toric geometry one usually takes $k = \mathbb{C}$, and this development is written with $k = \mathbb{C}$ for definiteness. All schemes are taken over $\mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}$.

**Definition (algebraic torus).** A **split algebraic torus** of dimension $n$ over $\mathbb{C}$ is the affine algebraic group
$$
T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}],
$$
whose underlying set of $\mathbb{C}$-points is $(\mathbb{C}^*)^n$ with the coordinate-wise multiplication group structure, and whose group structure is encoded by the Hopf-algebra structure on the Laurent polynomial ring $A = \mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$ with comultiplication $\Delta(x_i) = x_i \otimes x_i$, counit $\varepsilon(x_i) = 1$, and antipode $S(x_i) = x_i^{-1}$. The multiplicative group $\mathbb{G}_m = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[t^{\pm 1}]$ is the one-dimensional case $n = 1$. Throughout this unit, by "torus" we mean a split algebraic torus.

**Definition (character).** A **character** of $T$ is an algebraic group homomorphism $\chi : T \to \mathbb{G}_m$. The set of characters, with operation given by pointwise multiplication of homomorphisms $(\chi \cdot \chi')(t) = \chi(t) \chi'(t)$, is a commutative group, denoted $M(T) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{grp-sch}}(T, \mathbb{G}_m)$ and called the **character lattice** of $T$.

**Definition (cocharacter / one-parameter subgroup).** A **cocharacter**, or **one-parameter subgroup**, of $T$ is an algebraic group homomorphism $\lambda : \mathbb{G}_m \to T$. The set of cocharacters, with operation given by pointwise multiplication in $T$, is denoted $N(T) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{grp-sch}}(\mathbb{G}_m, T)$ and called the **cocharacter lattice** of $T$.

**Definition (pairing).** The **natural pairing** $M(T) \times N(T) \to \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{grp-sch}}(\mathbb{G}_m, \mathbb{G}_m) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ sends a pair $(\chi, \lambda)$ to the composition $\chi \circ \lambda : \mathbb{G}_m \to \mathbb{G}_m$, which is necessarily $t \mapsto t^k$ for a unique integer $k$, called $\langle \chi, \lambda\rangle$.

**Notation for the standard torus.** For $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n$, write $\chi^u$ for the character corresponding to $u = (u_1, \ldots, u_n) \in \mathbb{Z}^n$, defined as $\chi^u(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = x^u = x_1^{u_1} \cdots x_n^{u_n}$. Write $\lambda_v$ for the cocharacter corresponding to $v = (v_1, \ldots, v_n) \in \mathbb{Z}^n$, defined as $\lambda_v(t) = (t^{v_1}, \ldots, t^{v_n})$. The natural pairing is then the integer dot product $\langle u, v\rangle = u_1 v_1 + \cdots + u_n v_n$. After the structure theorem below, these notations exhibit $M$ and $N$ each as $\mathbb{Z}^n$ and the pairing as the dot product.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The set-theoretic group $(\mathbb{C}^*)^n$ has continuous and smooth automorphisms vastly more numerous than its algebraic automorphisms. The full group of continuous characters of the compact torus $(S^1)^n$ is also $\mathbb{Z}^n$, but the route to that identification uses Pontryagin duality of locally compact abelian groups; the algebraic identification uses the Hopf-algebra structure of the coordinate ring. The two identifications agree numerically but are conceptually distinct.

- "Character" in representation theory often means the trace of a representation. For the algebraic torus, every irreducible algebraic representation is one-dimensional (by the structure theorem) and the trace of a one-dimensional representation is the representation itself, so the two senses agree for tori — but the equivalence is a property of the torus, not a general fact.

- The character group of a non-split form of $\mathbb{G}_m$ (for example $\mathrm{Res}_{\mathbb{C}/\mathbb{R}}\,\mathbb{G}_m$ viewed as a real algebraic group, whose $\mathbb{R}$-points form the circle $S^1$) is a rank-one abelian group with Galois action, not just $\mathbb{Z}$. The lattice formalism developed here applies to split tori; the non-split case requires the additional Galois-cohomology data of Borel §III.8.

- The duality $M \cong \mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(N, \mathbb{Z})$ is a duality of free abelian groups, not of vector spaces. Tensoring with $\mathbb{R}$ produces dual real vector spaces $M_\mathbb{R}$ and $N_\mathbb{R}$, the ambient spaces for the rational polyhedral cones of toric geometry. The lattice-level duality is the load-bearing piece; the vector-space duality is a corollary.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (structure of characters and cocharacters of the algebraic torus).** *Let $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$ be the split algebraic torus of dimension $n$ over $\mathbb{C}$.*

*(i) The character lattice $M = M(T) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{grp-sch}}(T, \mathbb{G}_m)$ is a free abelian group of rank $n$. The map $\mathbb{Z}^n \to M$ sending $u = (u_1, \ldots, u_n)$ to the Laurent monomial $\chi^u(x) = x^u = x_1^{u_1} \cdots x_n^{u_n}$ is a group isomorphism.*

*(ii) The cocharacter lattice $N = N(T) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{grp-sch}}(\mathbb{G}_m, T)$ is a free abelian group of rank $n$. The map $\mathbb{Z}^n \to N$ sending $v = (v_1, \ldots, v_n)$ to $\lambda_v(t) = (t^{v_1}, \ldots, t^{v_n})$ is a group isomorphism.*

*(iii) The natural pairing $M \times N \to \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{grp-sch}}(\mathbb{G}_m, \mathbb{G}_m) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ identifies $M$ with $\mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(N, \mathbb{Z})$ and $N$ with $\mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(M, \mathbb{Z})$. Under the isomorphisms in (i) and (ii), the pairing is the integer dot product, $\langle \chi^u, \lambda_v\rangle = \sum_{i=1}^n u_i v_i$, equivalently $\chi^u(\lambda_v(t)) = t^{\langle u, v\rangle}$ for all $t \in \mathbb{G}_m$.*

**Proof.** The argument has four steps. First, identify characters with group-like units in the Laurent ring. Second, identify the group-like units with Laurent monomials. Third, identify cocharacters by the same Hopf-algebra technique applied in the other direction. Fourth, verify the pairing is the dot product by direct computation.

**Step 1: characters correspond to group-like elements.** Affine algebraic group homomorphisms $T \to \mathbb{G}_m$ correspond, by the contravariant equivalence between affine group schemes and commutative Hopf algebras, to Hopf-algebra homomorphisms $\mathbb{C}[t^{\pm 1}] \to A$, where $A = \mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$ is the coordinate ring of $T$ and the comultiplication on $\mathbb{C}[t^{\pm 1}]$ is $\Delta(t) = t \otimes t$. A Hopf-algebra map from $\mathbb{C}[t^{\pm 1}]$ is determined by the image $\chi(t) \in A$, which must be a unit (because $t$ is a unit and the map is a ring homomorphism) and must be **group-like**, meaning $\Delta(\chi(t)) = \chi(t) \otimes \chi(t)$ in $A \otimes_\mathbb{C} A$. So characters are in bijection with the group-like units of $A$.

**Step 2: group-like units of $A$ are Laurent monomials.** The comultiplication on $A$ sends $x_i \mapsto x_i \otimes x_i$ and extends multiplicatively. The units of $A$ are $\mathbb{C}^* \cdot \{$ Laurent monomials $\}$ — any nonzero Laurent polynomial that is a unit must be a single nonzero scalar multiple of a monomial, since $A$ is a unique factorisation domain and the only units of a Laurent UFD are scalar times monomials. Among these units, the group-like ones $f \in A$ satisfy $\Delta(f) = f \otimes f$. Write $f = c \cdot x^u$ for $c \in \mathbb{C}^*$ and $u \in \mathbb{Z}^n$. Then $\Delta(f) = c \cdot x^u \otimes x^u$, while $f \otimes f = c^2 \cdot x^u \otimes x^u$. Equality forces $c = c^2$, so $c = 1$. Thus the group-like units of $A$ are exactly the Laurent monomials $\{x^u : u \in \mathbb{Z}^n\}$, and the multiplication of group-like units is given by addition of exponents: $x^u \cdot x^{u'} = x^{u + u'}$. The map $u \mapsto \chi^u$ from $\mathbb{Z}^n$ to $M$ is therefore a group isomorphism, proving (i).

**Step 3: cocharacters identified by Hopf-algebra duality applied dually.** An algebraic group homomorphism $\lambda : \mathbb{G}_m \to T$ corresponds to a Hopf-algebra map $A \to \mathbb{C}[t^{\pm 1}]$. A Hopf-algebra map out of $A$ is determined by the images of the generators $x_1, \ldots, x_n$, which must each be a group-like unit of $\mathbb{C}[t^{\pm 1}]$. By Step 2 applied to the one-variable case (the same argument with $n = 1$), the group-like units of $\mathbb{C}[t^{\pm 1}]$ are exactly $\{t^{v} : v \in \mathbb{Z}\}$. So each generator $x_i$ maps to some $t^{v_i}$ for a unique integer $v_i$, and the cocharacter is $\lambda(t) = (t^{v_1}, \ldots, t^{v_n})$. The map $v \mapsto \lambda_v$ from $\mathbb{Z}^n$ to $N$ is a group isomorphism: pointwise multiplication of cocharacters $(\lambda_v \cdot \lambda_{v'})(t) = (t^{v_1 + v'_1}, \ldots, t^{v_n + v'_n})$ corresponds to addition of integer vectors. This proves (ii).

**Step 4: pairing computation.** Given $u \in \mathbb{Z}^n$ and $v \in \mathbb{Z}^n$, compute $\chi^u(\lambda_v(t)) = \chi^u(t^{v_1}, \ldots, t^{v_n}) = (t^{v_1})^{u_1} \cdots (t^{v_n})^{u_n} = t^{u_1 v_1 + \cdots + u_n v_n} = t^{\langle u, v\rangle}$, where $\langle u, v\rangle = \sum u_i v_i$ is the integer dot product. The composition $\chi^u \circ \lambda_v$ is therefore the integer power $t \mapsto t^{\langle u, v\rangle}$, identifying the abstract pairing $M \times N \to \mathbb{Z}$ with the dot product under the isomorphisms of (i) and (ii). The pairing exhibits $M$ as $\mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(N, \mathbb{Z})$ and $N$ as $\mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(M, \mathbb{Z})$ because the dot product on $\mathbb{Z}^n \times \mathbb{Z}^n$ is a perfect integer pairing: for each $v \in \mathbb{Z}^n$ the linear functional $u \mapsto \langle u, v\rangle$ ranges over all $\mathbb{Z}$-linear maps $\mathbb{Z}^n \to \mathbb{Z}$ as $v$ ranges over $\mathbb{Z}^n$, and the assignment $v \mapsto \langle -, v\rangle$ is a group isomorphism $\mathbb{Z}^n \to \mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(\mathbb{Z}^n, \mathbb{Z})$. This proves (iii). $\square$

**Bridge.** The structure theorem builds toward the entire combinatorial dictionary of toric geometry, and the central insight is that the geometry of a split algebraic torus is faithfully recorded by two dual integer lattices and the perfect pairing between them — there is no further structure to discover at the level of $T$ itself. This is exactly the foundational reason why toric varieties, defined as equivariant compactifications of $T$, can be classified combinatorially: every piece of $T$-equivariant geometry lives on the lattice side of the dictionary. The dual lattice $N_\mathbb{R} = N \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{R}$ is the ambient space for rational polyhedral cones, the basic combinatorial datum of the toric construction; the dictionary identifies a strongly convex rational polyhedral cone $\sigma \subset N_\mathbb{R}$ with the affine toric variety $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ built from the dual cone $\sigma^\vee \subset M_\mathbb{R}$ and the semigroup $\sigma^\vee \cap M \subset M$.

Putting these together, the structure theorem proved here is the foundational reason that the categorical anti-equivalence between split tori and free abelian groups extends to a full anti-equivalence between toric varieties and fans, which in turn identifies the geometry of toric varieties with the combinatorics of integer-valued polyhedral data. This pattern appears again in `04.11.02` (rational polyhedral cones in $N_\mathbb{R}$), in `04.11.03` (affine toric varieties from cones), and in `04.11.04` (fans and the global toric variety $X_\Sigma$); the present unit supplies the lattice $N$ and the dual lattice $M$ in which all subsequent combinatorial data lives. The bridge is dual to the duality of $M$ and $N$: each downstream toric concept has a pair of equivalent descriptions, one on the $M$-side and one on the $N$-side, and the structure theorem is what makes those descriptions interchangeable.

**Exercise 2 (easy, multiple choice).**

Which of the following Laurent polynomials in $\mathbb{C}[x^{\pm 1}, y^{\pm 1}]$ defines an algebraic group homomorphism $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^2 \to \mathbb{G}_m$?

A. $f(x, y) = x + y$  
B. $f(x, y) = x^2 y^{-3}$  
C. $f(x, y) = x^2 y^{-3} + 1$  
D. $f(x, y) = 2 x^2 y^{-3}$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A character must be a group-like unit of the Laurent ring. The proof of part (i) of the structure theorem showed group-like units are monic monomials.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** A character is a group-like unit, and the group-like units of $\mathbb{C}[x^{\pm 1}, y^{\pm 1}]$ are exactly the monic Laurent monomials $x^a y^b$ with $(a, b) \in \mathbb{Z}^2$ and leading coefficient $1$. Option A is not a unit ($x + y$ is irreducible and not a monomial, so not invertible). Option C is a sum of two terms — not a unit ($x^2 y^{-3} + 1$ has zeros). Option D is a unit but is not group-like: its comultiplication is $\Delta(2 x^2 y^{-3}) = 2 (x \otimes x)^2 (y \otimes y)^{-3} = 2 x^2 y^{-3} \otimes x^2 y^{-3}$, whereas $f \otimes f = 4 x^2 y^{-3} \otimes x^2 y^{-3}$, so $\Delta(f) = f \otimes f$ would require $2 = 4$. Only B is both a unit and group-like.

The Pontryagin dual of $(S^1)^n$ as a compact abelian topological group is $\mathbb{Z}^n$, with the continuous characters being $(z_1, \ldots, z_n) \mapsto z_1^{u_1} \cdots z_n^{u_n}$ for $u \in \mathbb{Z}^n$. Match this with the algebraic character calculation.

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove the **functoriality** of the character lattice: a homomorphism of split algebraic tori $\phi : T \to T'$ induces a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear map $\phi^* : M(T') \to M(T)$ on character lattices, and the assignment $T \mapsto M(T)$, $\phi \mapsto \phi^*$ is a contravariant functor from the category of split algebraic tori over $\mathbb{C}$ to the category of finitely generated free abelian groups. State and prove the analogous covariant functoriality for cocharacter lattices.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the Yoneda perspective: $M(T) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{grp-sch}}(T, \mathbb{G}_m)$, so a map $\phi : T \to T'$ pulls back characters of $T'$ to characters of $T$ by precomposition. Verify $\mathbb{Z}$-linearity from the pointwise-multiplication group structure.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Character functoriality.** Given a homomorphism of tori $\phi : T \to T'$, define $\phi^* : M(T') \to M(T)$ by pullback: $\phi^*(\chi') := \chi' \circ \phi$ for $\chi' \in M(T') = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{grp-sch}}(T', \mathbb{G}_m)$. The composition $\chi' \circ \phi$ is itself an algebraic group homomorphism $T \to \mathbb{G}_m$, hence in $M(T)$.

For $\mathbb{Z}$-linearity, given $\chi', \chi'' \in M(T')$ and $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}$, the pointwise-multiplication group structure gives $(a \chi' + b \chi'')(t') = \chi'(t')^a \chi''(t')^b$, and pulling back: $\phi^*(a \chi' + b \chi'')(t) = (a \chi' + b \chi'')(\phi(t)) = \chi'(\phi(t))^a \chi''(\phi(t))^b = \phi^*(\chi')(t)^a \phi^*(\chi'')(t)^b = (a \phi^*(\chi') + b \phi^*(\chi''))(t)$. So $\phi^*$ is $\mathbb{Z}$-linear.

For functoriality: given a second map $\psi : T' \to T''$, the composition $(\psi \circ \phi)^* (\chi'') = \chi'' \circ \psi \circ \phi = \phi^*(\psi^*(\chi'')) = (\phi^* \circ \psi^*)(\chi'')$ for $\chi'' \in M(T'')$. So $(\psi \phi)^* = \phi^* \psi^*$, and the identity map on $T$ pulls back to the identity on $M(T)$. The assignment $T \mapsto M(T)$, $\phi \mapsto \phi^*$ is contravariant.

**Cocharacter functoriality.** Define $\phi_* : N(T) \to N(T')$ by pushforward: $\phi_*(\lambda) := \phi \circ \lambda$ for $\lambda \in N(T) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{grp-sch}}(\mathbb{G}_m, T)$. The composition $\phi \circ \lambda$ is itself an algebraic group homomorphism $\mathbb{G}_m \to T'$, hence in $N(T')$. $\mathbb{Z}$-linearity: $(\phi_*(a \lambda + b \mu))(t) = \phi(\lambda(t)^a \mu(t)^b) = \phi(\lambda(t))^a \phi(\mu(t))^b = (a \phi_*(\lambda) + b \phi_*(\mu))(t)$. Composition: $(\psi \phi)_*(\lambda) = \psi \phi \lambda = \psi_*(\phi_*(\lambda))$, hence $(\psi \phi)_* = \psi_* \phi_*$, and identity maps preserve. The assignment is covariant.

**Compatibility with the pairing.** The induced maps are mutually adjoint: $\langle \phi^*(\chi'), \lambda\rangle = \langle \chi', \phi_*(\lambda)\rangle$ for all $\chi' \in M(T')$ and $\lambda \in N(T)$. Direct verification: $\langle \phi^*(\chi'), \lambda\rangle$ is the integer $k$ with $\chi'(\phi(\lambda(t))) = t^k$, which is also the integer $k$ with $\chi'(\phi_*(\lambda)(t)) = t^k$, equal to $\langle \chi', \phi_*(\lambda)\rangle$.

**Anti-equivalence of categories.** The two functors $T \mapsto M(T)$ and $T \mapsto N(T)$ exhibit the category of split tori over $\mathbb{C}$ as anti-equivalent to the category of finitely generated free abelian groups (each $\phi$ corresponds to its matrix on $N$, and dually to the transpose matrix on $M$). Rubric: full credit for both functoriality statements, the adjoint pairing identity, and the categorical-anti-equivalence remark.

</details>

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Let $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n$ and let $V$ be a finite-dimensional algebraic $T$-representation. Show that $V$ decomposes as a direct sum of one-dimensional weight subspaces $V = \bigoplus_{u \in M} V_u$, where $V_u = \{v \in V : t \cdot v = \chi^u(t) v \text{ for all } t \in T\}$ is the **weight-$u$ subspace**, and only finitely many $V_u$ are nonzero. Conclude that the algebraic representations of $T$ are classified by finitely supported functions $M \to \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A finite-dimensional algebraic $T$-representation is a Hopf-algebra comodule for the Laurent ring $A = \mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$. The Hopf-algebra structure on $A$ has counit and comultiplication preserving the monomial-basis decomposition, and a comodule decomposes by the monomial basis.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A finite-dimensional algebraic representation of $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n$ corresponds, by the antiequivalence between affine group schemes and Hopf algebras, to a right comodule structure $\rho : V \to V \otimes_\mathbb{C} A$ on the finite-dimensional vector space $V$, where $A = \mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$.

The Laurent ring $A$ has the monomial $\mathbb{C}$-basis $\{x^u : u \in \mathbb{Z}^n\}$, and the comultiplication acts by $\Delta(x^u) = x^u \otimes x^u$. The comodule axiom $(\mathrm{id}_V \otimes \Delta) \circ \rho = (\rho \otimes \mathrm{id}_A) \circ \rho$ implies, after writing $\rho(v) = \sum_u v_u \otimes x^u$ with $v_u \in V$ uniquely determined by $v$, that the assignment $v \mapsto v_u$ is the projection onto a subspace $V_u \subset V$, with $V = \bigoplus_u V_u$ as a direct sum of subspaces. The counit axiom $(\mathrm{id}_V \otimes \varepsilon) \circ \rho = \mathrm{id}_V$ with $\varepsilon(x^u) = 1$ gives $\sum_u v_u = v$, so the decomposition is honest.

Finite-dimensionality of $V$ forces only finitely many $V_u$ to be nonzero (the comodule structure constants live in a finite span of monomials, since $\rho$ has image in $V \otimes A$ and $V$ is finite-dimensional). On each $V_u$, the comodule action specialises to $\rho(v) = v \otimes x^u$, which is the $\mathbb{C}$-points statement $t \cdot v = \chi^u(t) v$ for all $t \in T$; this is the definition of the weight-$u$ subspace.

Therefore the representation is determined by the multiplicity function $u \mapsto \dim_\mathbb{C} V_u$, a finitely supported function $M \to \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$. Conversely, every finitely supported function arises from some representation, by taking the direct sum of the prescribed multiplicities of the one-dimensional characters $\chi^u$. The classification is therefore exactly the set of finitely supported functions $M \to \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$.

**Consequences.** Every algebraic representation of $T$ is completely reducible (a sum of one-dimensional pieces); the irreducible representations are exactly the one-dimensional characters $\chi^u$ for $u \in M$; the Grothendieck ring of finite-dimensional algebraic representations of $T$ is the group algebra $\mathbb{Z}[M]$. Rubric: full credit for the weight decomposition, the finiteness, and the multiplicity-function classification.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (anti-equivalence of categories; Borel III.8 / Demazure-Gabriel 1970).** *The functor $T \mapsto M(T)$ from the category of split algebraic tori over an algebraically closed field $k$ to the opposite of the category of finitely generated free abelian groups is an anti-equivalence of categories. Equivalently, $T \mapsto N(T)$ is an equivalence between split tori and free abelian groups. The pairing $M(T) \times N(T) \to \mathbb{Z}$ exhibits the two as mutually dual.*

The anti-equivalence statement is the categorical refinement of the structure theorem proved above: not only do $M(T)$ and $N(T)$ classify a single torus, the assignment is a categorical equivalence under which torus homomorphisms correspond to $\mathbb{Z}$-linear maps in the opposite (respectively, same) direction. This is the foundational reason the lattice formalism works for toric varieties: every $T$-equivariant geometric construction has a faithful combinatorial shadow on the lattice side, and the shadow contains exactly the same information. The anti-equivalence specialises to the case of $T = \mathbb{G}_m$, where $M = N = \mathbb{Z}$, and recovers the standard fact that algebraic homomorphisms $\mathbb{G}_m \to \mathbb{G}_m$ are exactly $t \mapsto t^k$ for $k \in \mathbb{Z}$.

**Theorem (rationality and field of definition).** *A split algebraic torus over a field $k$ has $M(T)$ and $N(T)$ as free abelian groups of rank $n$ with the Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(\bar k / k)$ acting as the identity. A non-split torus over $k$ (a $k$-form of $(\mathbb{G}_m)^n$ that becomes split after passing to $\bar k$) has $M(T \otimes_k \bar k) = \mathbb{Z}^n$ as a $\mathbb{Z}$-module but carries a non-identity Galois action, and the isomorphism class of the non-split torus over $k$ is classified by the continuous Galois module $M(T \otimes \bar k)$ together with the Galois action.*

The split / non-split dichotomy is implicit in the very name "split torus" used in the formal definition above. Over $\mathbb{C}$ every torus is automatically split (because $\bar \mathbb{C} = \mathbb{C}$), so the dichotomy does not arise. Over $\mathbb{R}$ the non-split form $\mathrm{Res}_{\mathbb{C}/\mathbb{R}}\,\mathbb{G}_m$ has $\mathbb{R}$-points equal to the circle $S^1$ (rather than $\mathbb{R}^* = \mathbb{R} \setminus \{0\}$); its character group over $\mathbb{R}$ is $\mathbb{Z}$ with the complex-conjugation Galois action by negation, $\sigma \cdot u = -u$. The toric-variety framework of `04.11.02`–`04.11.04` is developed for split tori; the non-split case requires the additional Galois-cohomology language of Borel §III.8 and is one of the entry points to Voskresenskii's theory of algebraic tori in arithmetic geometry.

**Theorem (lattice quotients and torus quotients).** *Let $T$ be a split algebraic torus with character lattice $M$ and let $M' \subset M$ be a sublattice of finite index $d$. Then the quotient $M / M'$ is a finite abelian group of order $d$, and there is a corresponding short exact sequence of algebraic groups*
$$
1 \to F \to T \to T' \to 1,
$$
*where $T'$ is the torus with character lattice $M'$, and $F = \mathrm{Hom}(M / M', \mathbb{C}^*)$ is the Cartier dual of the finite group $M / M'$, a finite subgroup of $T$ of order $d$.*

The lattice-quotient theorem is the bridge between integer-lattice arithmetic and the geometry of torus quotients. It says that every finite quotient of a torus $T \to T'$ corresponds to a finite-index inclusion of lattices $M' \hookrightarrow M$, and the kernel of the quotient is the finite subgroup $F$ of $T$ whose character group is the cokernel $M / M'$. The Cartier-dual / Pontryagin-dual flavour of this — replacing the discrete finite group $M/M'$ by the dual finite group — is the canonical algebraic-geometry version of Pontryagin duality for finite abelian groups, and is the load-bearing piece in many computations on weighted projective spaces and orbifold toric varieties.

**Theorem (one-parameter subgroups generate the rational structure on the algebra of functions).** *Let $T$ be a split algebraic torus with cocharacter lattice $N$ and character lattice $M$. The coordinate ring $A = \mathbb{C}[T] = \bigoplus_{u \in M} \mathbb{C} \cdot \chi^u$ decomposes as an $M$-graded algebra, with the grading determined by the action of $T$ on itself by translation. Every one-parameter subgroup $\lambda \in N$ induces a $\mathbb{Z}$-grading on $A$ via the integer-valued homomorphism $u \mapsto \langle u, \lambda\rangle : M \to \mathbb{Z}$, and these $\mathbb{Z}$-gradings are the GIT data for taking quotients of toric varieties.*

The $M$-grading of the Laurent-polynomial ring is the foundational example of a multi-graded ring, and the $\mathbb{Z}$-grading via a cocharacter $\lambda$ is the input to GIT quotients in the toric setting (Mumford's geometric invariant theory, in the toric special case worked out by Białynicki-Birula and refined by Cox in his homogeneous-coordinate-ring construction). The cocharacter $\lambda$ supplies a "GIT linearisation" of the $T$-action, and the resulting GIT quotient is a toric variety whose lattice is the cokernel of a $\lambda$-controlled map of lattices.

**Theorem (functorial moduli interpretation; Demazure-Gabriel).** *The functor $\mathrm{Sch}/\mathbb{C} \to \mathrm{Set}$ sending a scheme $S$ to the set of algebraic group homomorphisms $S \to T$ over $\mathbb{C}$ is represented by the affine scheme $T$ itself; equivalently, $T(S) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{Sch}/\mathbb{C}}(S, T)$ is a multiplicative-monoid-valued functor, naturally a group with values in abelian groups. The character lattice is the Cartier dual of $T$: $M(T) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{grp-sch}/\mathbb{C}}(T, \mathbb{G}_m)$, and the cocharacter lattice is $N(T) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{grp-sch}/\mathbb{C}}(\mathbb{G}_m, T)$. Cartier duality is the duality of finite-rank diagonalisable group schemes; for a torus it specialises to the lattice-level duality $M = \mathrm{Hom}(N, \mathbb{Z})$ proved above.*

This is the universal-property formulation of the character / cocharacter functors, and is the input to Demazure's anti-equivalence theorem. It also is the categorical content of the lattice-level pairing: $\mathbb{G}_m$ is the dualising object, and Cartier duality $T \mapsto T^\vee$ in the category of affine commutative group schemes sends a torus to its Cartier dual — for a split torus this is the lattice $\mathrm{Hom}(M, \mathbb{Z}) = N$, viewed as a discrete diagonalisable group scheme, and vice versa.

**Theorem (Lie algebra of the torus; Borel III.8).** *The Lie algebra of the split torus $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n$ is the abelian Lie algebra $\mathfrak{t} = \mathbb{C}^n$, canonically identified with $N \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{C}$ via the differential of one-parameter subgroups. Under this identification, the differential of a character $\chi^u \in M$ is the linear functional $u : \mathfrak{t} \to \mathbb{C}$, $v \mapsto \langle u, v\rangle$, exhibiting $M$ as a $\mathbb{Z}$-lattice in $\mathfrak{t}^*$ (the integer dual of $\mathfrak{t}$).*

The differential identification is what makes the toric-symplectic dictionary work: the moment map of a symplectic toric manifold takes values in $\mathfrak{t}^*$, and the rational structure of the polytope it produces is exactly the integer lattice $M \subset \mathfrak{t}^*$. The lattice formalism developed in this unit is therefore not only the foundation for algebraic-toric geometry but also for the symplectic-toric / Delzant-theorem viewpoint (see `05.04.04` for the Delzant theorem in the symplectic category).

**Synthesis.** The algebraic torus $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n$ over $\mathbb{C}$ is the simplest shape-bearing connected algebraic group, and the central insight is that all of its geometry is encoded by a pair of dual integer lattices $(M, N)$ with the integer-valued perfect pairing $\langle -, -\rangle : M \times N \to \mathbb{Z}$. Three apparently distinct descriptions — Hopf-algebra duality on the Laurent ring, the functorial moduli interpretation via Cartier duality, and the differential / Lie-algebra picture — fit into one identification, and the structure theorem proved here is what makes the three descriptions interchangeable.

Putting these together, the lattice formalism organises the toric chapter: every downstream concept (cones in `04.11.02`, affine toric varieties in `04.11.03`, fans and the global toric variety $X_\Sigma$ in `04.11.04`, divisors and line bundles via support functions in `04.11.08`–`04.11.10`, the polytope dictionary and the moment map in `04.11.11`) has a description on the $M$-side, a description on the $N$-side, and a duality between them; this is exactly the content of the lattice machinery introduced here. The bridge is dual to the duality of $M$ and $N$, and the foundational reason the toric dictionary is bidirectional.

This pattern appears again in symplectic toric geometry (see `05.04.04` for the Delzant theorem), where the same lattice $M$ becomes the integer lattice in $\mathfrak{t}^*$ that polarises the moment polytope, and the cocharacter lattice $N$ becomes the integer lattice in the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{t}$ along which the Hamiltonian torus acts. The symplectic and algebraic-geometric viewpoints share their combinatorial input — the lattice $(M, N)$ and its pairing — and produce two equivalent classifications of toric data, one polytope-flavoured (symplectic) and one fan-flavoured (algebraic). This is the foundational reason the symplectic Delzant theorem and the algebraic polytope-toric correspondence agree on the manifold underlying a smooth projective toric variety.

The structure theorem also generalises in two directions. To non-split tori over a non-algebraically-closed field $k$, the lattice $M(T \otimes_k \bar k)$ carries an additional continuous Galois action, and the isomorphism class of $T$ over $k$ is classified by the Galois module structure on $M$; the toric-variety construction extends to non-split tori but the descended polytopes become "rational polytopes with Galois action", a setting developed by Voskresenskii. To the more general setting of reductive algebraic groups, the maximal torus of a connected reductive group is a split torus (after passing to the algebraic closure), and its character / cocharacter lattices, together with the root and coroot data inside them, classify the reductive group by Chevalley's theorem (the root-datum classification). The lattice formalism developed here for tori is therefore the first piece of the root-datum machinery that classifies all reductive groups, and through this is connected to the entire structure theory of Lie algebras and algebraic groups (see the Borel and Demazure-Gabriel anchors). The recursion stabilises after one round: the lattice $(M, N)$ of a torus is its complete classifying data, and the additional data of a reductive group beyond its maximal torus consists of root systems inside $M$ and coroot systems inside $N$, all integer-lattice objects, all reading the same pairing developed in this unit.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Theorem (structure of characters and cocharacters), proof.** Given in the Intermediate-tier section: characters of $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n$ correspond to group-like units of the coordinate Hopf algebra $A = \mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$, which by direct calculation on the comultiplication are exactly the Laurent monomials $\{x^u : u \in \mathbb{Z}^n\}$ with coefficient $1$; cocharacters are identified by the same Hopf-algebra calculation applied to maps out of $A$ into $\mathbb{C}[t^{\pm 1}]$; and the natural pairing is computed by direct substitution as the integer dot product $\langle u, v\rangle = \sum u_i v_i$. The three parts together establish $M \cong N \cong \mathbb{Z}^n$ with the dot-product pairing. $\square$

**Proposition (group-like units of a Laurent polynomial ring), proof.** *Let $A = \mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$ with comultiplication $\Delta(x_i) = x_i \otimes x_i$ extended multiplicatively. The group-like units of $A$ — that is, units $f$ with $\Delta(f) = f \otimes f$ — are exactly $\{x^u : u \in \mathbb{Z}^n\}$.*

*Proof.* First, every Laurent monomial $x^u$ is a unit (with inverse $x^{-u}$) and is group-like: $\Delta(x^u) = \Delta(x_1)^{u_1} \cdots \Delta(x_n)^{u_n} = (x_1 \otimes x_1)^{u_1} \cdots (x_n \otimes x_n)^{u_n} = x^u \otimes x^u$. Second, every unit of $A$ is a scalar times a Laurent monomial: write $f = \sum_u c_u x^u$ in the monomial basis; $f$ is a unit iff there is some $g = \sum_u d_u x^u$ with $fg = 1$, and matching coefficients in the monomial basis of $A$ forces the supports of $f$ and $g$ to be single integer vectors with $c_u d_{-u} = 1$ (more carefully: $A$ is the localisation of the polynomial ring $\mathbb{C}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$ at the variables, and as the ring of fractions $\mathbb{C}[x_1, \ldots, x_n][x_1^{-1}, \ldots, x_n^{-1}]$ it is a UFD whose units are products of units of $\mathbb{C}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$ — which are $\mathbb{C}^*$ — and inverses of the variables, so units of $A$ are $\mathbb{C}^* \cdot \{$ Laurent monomials $\}$). Third, among units $c \cdot x^u$ with $c \in \mathbb{C}^*$, the group-like ones satisfy $\Delta(c x^u) = c \cdot x^u \otimes x^u$, while $(c x^u) \otimes (c x^u) = c^2 \cdot x^u \otimes x^u$. Equality of $\Delta(f)$ with $f \otimes f$ forces $c = c^2$, so $c \in \{0, 1\}$; since $f$ is a unit, $c \neq 0$, hence $c = 1$. The group-like units are therefore exactly the Laurent monomials. $\square$

**Proposition (functoriality of $M$ and $N$), proof.** Given in Exercise 7. The pullback $\phi^*(\chi') = \chi' \circ \phi$ defines a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear map $M(T') \to M(T)$ contravariantly; the pushforward $\phi_*(\lambda) = \phi \circ \lambda$ defines a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear map $N(T) \to N(T')$ covariantly. The adjoint identity $\langle \phi^*(\chi'), \lambda\rangle_T = \langle \chi', \phi_*(\lambda)\rangle_{T'}$ follows by direct substitution. $\square$

**Proposition (anti-equivalence of categories), proof.** *The functor $\mathrm{Tori}^{\mathrm{split}}_\mathbb{C} \to (\mathrm{FreeAb}_{\mathrm{fg}})^{\mathrm{op}}$ sending $T \mapsto M(T)$ and $\phi \mapsto \phi^*$ is an anti-equivalence of categories.*

*Proof.* Faithfulness: a torus map $\phi : T \to T'$ is uniquely determined by its pullback $\phi^*$, because $\phi^*$ determines the Hopf-algebra map $A' \to A$ on group-like-unit generators of $A'$, and the Hopf-algebra map determines $\phi$ by the antiequivalence between affine group schemes and commutative Hopf algebras. Fullness: given a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear map $\psi : M(T') \to M(T)$, construct a Hopf-algebra map $A' \to A$ by sending the group-like generator $\chi^{u'}$ to $\chi^{\psi(u')}$; this defines a Hopf-algebra map (the comultiplication compatibility is automatic on group-like elements) and hence a torus map $\phi : T \to T'$ with $\phi^* = \psi$. Essential surjectivity: every finitely generated free abelian group is $\mathbb{Z}^n$ for some $n$, and $\mathbb{Z}^n = M((\mathbb{G}_m)^n)$, so the functor hits every isomorphism class. The functor is therefore an anti-equivalence. $\square$

**Proposition (compatibility of algebraic and topological characters), stated in Exercise 6 — full proof.** The continuous character group of the compact abelian Lie group $(S^1)^n$ is $\mathbb{Z}^n$ by Pontryagin duality: every continuous homomorphism $(S^1)^n \to S^1$ is of the form $(z_1, \ldots, z_n) \mapsto z_1^{u_1} \cdots z_n^{u_n}$ for a unique $u \in \mathbb{Z}^n$. The algebraic character group of $T = (\mathbb{C}^*)^n$ is also $\mathbb{Z}^n$ by the structure theorem of this unit. Restricting an algebraic character $\chi^u : T \to \mathbb{C}^*$ to the compact subgroup $T_\mathbb{R} = (S^1)^n$ produces the continuous character $(z_1, \ldots, z_n) \mapsto z_1^{u_1} \cdots z_n^{u_n}$ — exactly the Pontryagin-dual character with the same label $u$. The restriction is the identity on $\mathbb{Z}^n$, hence a bijection. The algebraic and topological character lattices agree. $\square$

**Proposition (weight decomposition of an algebraic torus representation), stated in Exercise 8 — full proof.** A finite-dimensional algebraic representation of $T$ is a finite-dimensional right $A$-comodule, where $A$ is the Hopf algebra of $T$. The structure map $\rho : V \to V \otimes A$ has image expressible as $\rho(v) = \sum_{u \in \mathbb{Z}^n} v_u \otimes x^u$ for uniquely determined $v_u \in V$ (only finitely many nonzero by finite-dimensionality). The comodule axiom forces the $v_u$ to define mutually orthogonal projection operators $V \to V_u$ onto subspaces, and $V = \bigoplus_u V_u$ as a vector-space direct sum. Each $V_u$ is the weight-$u$ subspace, on which $T$ acts by $\chi^u$. The classification by multiplicity functions $u \mapsto \dim V_u$ follows. The representation theory of the torus is therefore "diagonal" in the sense of being a direct sum of one-dimensional weight characters; this is the foundational example of a diagonalisable algebraic group, and tori are sometimes called diagonalisable groups in the older literature for exactly this reason. $\square$

**Proposition (lattice quotients and torus quotients), proof.** Let $M' \subset M = \mathbb{Z}^n$ be a sublattice of finite index $d$; pick a basis exhibiting $M'$ as $\bigoplus_i a_i \mathbb{Z}$ with $a_i \in \mathbb{Z}_{> 0}$ (Smith normal form). The quotient $M / M' \cong \bigoplus_i \mathbb{Z}/a_i \mathbb{Z}$ is a finite abelian group of order $\prod_i a_i = d$. Apply the contravariant Hom-functor $\mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(-, \mathbb{C}^*)$ to the short exact sequence of abelian groups $0 \to M' \to M \to M/M' \to 0$. Since $\mathbb{C}^*$ is divisible, the Hom-functor is exact, producing the short exact sequence of algebraic groups $1 \to \mathrm{Hom}(M/M', \mathbb{C}^*) \to \mathrm{Hom}(M, \mathbb{C}^*) \to \mathrm{Hom}(M', \mathbb{C}^*) \to 1$. The left term is the Cartier-dual finite group $F$ of order $d$; the middle is $T$; the right is the torus $T'$ with character lattice $M'$. The displayed sequence $1 \to F \to T \to T' \to 1$ identifies $T'$ as $T / F$. $\square$

**Proposition (Lie algebra of the torus), stated in the Advanced-results section — full proof.** The Lie algebra of an algebraic group $G$ is the tangent space at the identity, $T_e G = \mathrm{Der}_\mathbb{C}(\mathcal{O}_{G, e}, \mathbb{C})$. For $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n$ with local coordinates $(x_1 - 1, \ldots, x_n - 1)$ at the identity, the tangent space is canonically $\mathbb{C}^n$, with the $i$-th basis vector given by the derivation $\partial / \partial x_i |_{x_i = 1}$. The differential of the one-parameter subgroup $\lambda_v(t) = (t^{v_1}, \ldots, t^{v_n})$ at $t = 1$ is the tangent vector $(v_1, \ldots, v_n) \in \mathbb{C}^n$, identifying $N \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{C}$ with $\mathfrak{t}$ (and integrally, $N$ with the integer lattice inside $\mathfrak{t}$). The differential of the character $\chi^u(x) = x^u$ at the identity is the linear functional $\mathbb{C}^n \to \mathbb{C}$, $w \mapsto \sum_i u_i w_i = \langle u, w\rangle$, identifying $M$ with the integer lattice inside $\mathfrak{t}^*$ and the pairing $M \times N \to \mathbb{Z}$ with the integer dot product on $\mathbb{Z}^n \times \mathbb{Z}^n$. The Lie-algebra picture and the lattice picture agree. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Group [01.02.01].** The algebraic torus $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n$ is in particular an abelian group, and its character lattice $M$ and cocharacter lattice $N$ are themselves abelian groups under pointwise multiplication of homomorphisms. The lattice structure on $M$ and $N$ is the abelian-group structure of the corresponding homomorphism sets, refined by the additional fact that the homomorphism sets carry $\mathbb{Z}$-module structure (any abelian group is a $\mathbb{Z}$-module). The foundational group-theoretic input from the prerequisite unit is the very notion of group homomorphism that defines characters and cocharacters; the lattice formalism then specialises that notion to algebraic group homomorphisms between specific affine algebraic groups.

- **Sheaf [04.01.01].** The algebraic torus carries the structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_T$ of the affine scheme $T = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$, and the Hopf-algebra-comultiplication structure that makes $T$ a group scheme is recorded as a sheaf morphism $\mathcal{O}_T \to \mathcal{O}_T \otimes \mathcal{O}_T$. The character / cocharacter description proved in this unit can be reformulated sheaf-theoretically as a description of the group-scheme-homomorphism functor of $T$, and the entire toric-variety construction of `04.11.02`–`04.11.04` builds further global sheaves (the structure sheaves of the affine pieces $U_\sigma$, the sheaf of regular functions on the global $X_\Sigma$) on top of this lattice formalism.

- **Affine scheme [04.02.02].** The algebraic torus $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n$ is the affine scheme associated to the Laurent-polynomial ring $\mathbb{C}[x_1^{\pm 1}, \ldots, x_n^{\pm 1}]$, an instance of the affine-scheme construction of the prerequisite unit. Characters of $T$ correspond to Hopf-algebra maps on the coordinate ring, and the structure theorem proved here is exactly a calculation of the Hopf-algebra-map set in the affine-scheme category. The toric-variety chapter that follows is built by gluing affine schemes $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ that include $T$ itself (corresponding to $\sigma = \{0\}$) as the open dense orbit; the present unit supplies the lattice $M$ in which the semigroups $\sigma^\vee \cap M$ live.

- **Scheme [04.02.01].** The algebraic torus is a particular affine scheme with the additional structure of a group scheme; toric varieties are global schemes built from gluing affine pieces of this form. The structure theorem reduces the geometry of $T$ to the integer lattice $M$ and gives the lattice in which the combinatorial fan data of `04.11.02`–`04.11.04` lives; the result is that the entire chapter `04.11.*` is a chapter of scheme-theoretic algebraic geometry seen through the lens of integer-lattice combinatorics, with the present unit supplying the dictionary between the two viewpoints.

- **Delzant theorem [05.04.04].** The compact subgroup $T_\mathbb{R} = (S^1)^n \subset T$ acts symplectically on a symplectic toric manifold with moment map taking values in $\mathfrak{t}^* = N^* \otimes \mathbb{R} = M_\mathbb{R}$, and the image polytope is the polarised polytope of the Delzant theorem. The integer lattice $M \subset M_\mathbb{R}$ from the present unit is exactly the integral structure that makes the moment polytope Delzant (rational, simple, smooth at every vertex), and the symplectic Delzant theorem and the algebraic polytope-toric correspondence of `04.11.10`–`04.11.11` agree because they share the lattice $(M, N)$ developed here. The bridge between the two viewpoints is faithful precisely because the algebraic-character lattice $M$ and the topological-character lattice of the compact torus $T_\mathbb{R}$ coincide as $\mathbb{Z}^n$, by the compatibility proved in Exercise 6.

- **Picard group [04.05.02].** The Picard group of a smooth projective toric variety $X_\Sigma$ is computed combinatorially from the lattice $(M, N)$ and the fan $\Sigma$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$: $\mathrm{Pic}(X_\Sigma)$ is the quotient of the group of $\Sigma$-piecewise-linear functions on $N_\mathbb{R}$ by the group of globally linear functions, the latter being a copy of $M$. The character lattice $M$ thus appears as a canonical sublattice of the divisor / line-bundle group of every toric variety, and the duality $M \leftrightarrow N$ underwrites the duality of divisor data (Cartier vs Weil, $T$-invariant vs general) on toric varieties. The present unit supplies the lattice $M$ and the duality machinery; `04.11.08`–`04.11.10` supply the toric Picard computation.

- **Sheaf of differentials [04.08.01].** The cotangent sheaf $\Omega^1_T$ of the algebraic torus is free of rank $n$, with basis the logarithmic differentials $dx_i / x_i$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$; equivalently $\Omega^1_T \cong M \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathcal{O}_T$ (every character $\chi^u$ has logarithmic differential $d \log \chi^u = \sum u_i dx_i / x_i$). The character lattice $M$ appears directly as the lattice of integer logarithmic-differential classes, and this identification extends to toric varieties: the cotangent sheaf of a smooth toric variety admits a description in terms of $M$ and the fan combinatorics, generalising the free-of-rank-$n$ statement for the torus itself.

- **Projective space [04.07.01].** Projective space $\mathbb{P}^n$ is a toric variety, with torus $T = (\mathbb{G}_m)^n$ acting on the open chart $\mathbb{C}^n \subset \mathbb{P}^n$ by coordinate-wise multiplication and on the complementary boundary toric divisors by extension. The character lattice $M$ of this torus is $\mathbb{Z}^n$, and the fan of $\mathbb{P}^n$ in $N_\mathbb{R}$ has $n + 1$ rays generated by the standard basis vectors and their sum's negative. The present unit's lattice formalism is the combinatorial input that turns $\mathbb{P}^n$ into a toric variety; `04.11.04` develops the fan-construction in generality, and the projective-space example will be the first worked instance.

- **Canonical sheaf [04.08.02].** The canonical sheaf of the algebraic torus is $\omega_T \cong \mathcal{O}_T$, the structure sheaf, because $\Omega^1_T$ is free of rank $n$ and $\omega_T = \det \Omega^1_T$ is the line bundle of the volume form $dx_1 / x_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge dx_n / x_n$, which is nowhere zero. Equivalently, the torus is a Calabi-Yau open piece, and the canonical class of a smooth complete toric variety extending $T$ is computed combinatorially from the lattice $(M, N)$ and the rays of the fan as $-\sum_\rho D_\rho$ (the negative of the sum of the toric prime divisors). The lattice formalism developed here is the foundation for that canonical-class computation, which is the toric-variety counterpart of the Calabi-Yau condition for general algebraic varieties.

- **Rational polyhedral cone and dual cone [04.11.02].** The first downstream unit promotes the lattice pair $(M, N)$ into a convex-geometric framework: a rational polyhedral cone $\sigma \subseteq N_\mathbb{R}$ and its dual $\sigma^\vee = \{m \in M_\mathbb{R} : \langle m, n\rangle \geq 0 \ \forall n \in \sigma\} \subseteq M_\mathbb{R}$ live entirely inside the lattices developed here, with the pairing $\langle -, -\rangle : M \times N \to \mathbb{Z}$ extended $\mathbb{R}$-linearly. Gordan's lemma (finite generation of $\sigma^\vee \cap M$) and the cone-duality theorem $\sigma^{\vee\vee} = \sigma$ are the two foundational facts of toric combinatorics, both expressed in this unit's lattice language. The zero cone $\sigma = \{0\}$ has dual $\sigma^\vee = M_\mathbb{R}$ and semigroup $\sigma^\vee \cap M = M$, recovering the present unit's identification $T = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[M]$ as the affine toric variety of the zero cone.

- **Affine toric variety $U_\sigma$ [04.11.03].** The second downstream unit constructs the affine variety $U_\sigma = \mathrm{Spec}\,\mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ from each rational polyhedral cone $\sigma$, with $T$ acting on $U_\sigma$ by the $M$-grading of the semigroup algebra and embedded as the dense open subset $U_{\{0\}} = T \hookrightarrow U_\sigma$. The torus-character formalism developed here is the foundational reason the $T$-action on $U_\sigma$ is recorded by the $M$-grading: every monomial $x^u \in \mathbb{C}[\sigma^\vee \cap M]$ for $u \in \sigma^\vee \cap M$ is a $T$-eigenvector with weight $\chi^u$, and the entire toric coordinate ring is the direct sum $\bigoplus_{u \in \sigma^\vee \cap M} \mathbb{C}\cdot \chi^u$ of $T$-eigenlines indexed by the lattice points of $\sigma^\vee$. The toric variety is, in this sense, the geometric incarnation of the character-lattice $M$ together with a chosen semigroup of "allowed" characters.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The term "algebraic torus" enters algebraic geometry in the work of the Italian school in the early twentieth century, where the multiplicative group $(\mathbb{C}^*)^n$ appears as the open subset of $\mathbb{C}^n$ on which all coordinates are nonzero. The modern lattice formalism — viewing the algebraic torus through its character group $M = \mathrm{Hom}(T, \mathbb{G}_m)$ and cocharacter group $N = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathbb{G}_m, T)$ — was crystallised by Armand Borel in his foundational work on linear algebraic groups in the 1950s, culminating in the textbook *Linear Algebraic Groups* (Benjamin 1969, with a substantially expanded second edition Springer 1991) [ref: TODO_REF], where §III.8 develops the structure of split tori, characters, cocharacters, and their pairing as the foundation for the entire root-datum classification of reductive algebraic groups. Borel's work absorbed and synthesised earlier contributions by Claude Chevalley (in his Séminaire Chevalley 1956–58 reconstructing reductive groups), André Weil (in *Adèles and Algebraic Groups*, 1961), and the Bourbaki-school formalisation of Lie groups and algebraic groups.

The toric-geometry application — using the lattice $(M, N)$ to encode equivariant compactifications of $T$ via cones and fans — was opened by Michel Demazure in *Sous-groupes algébriques de rang maximum du groupe de Cremona* (Annales scientifiques de l'École normale supérieure, 4e série, tome 3, 1970, pp. 507–588) [ref: TODO_REF], where the rank-$n$ algebraic subgroups of the Cremona group $\mathrm{Bir}(\mathbb{P}^n)$ are classified through what Demazure calls the "diagram" — essentially a fan in $N_\mathbb{R}$ — and the corresponding ambient toric variety. The combinatorial nomenclature "toric variety" became standard after Mumford and his collaborators (George Kempf, Finn Faye Knudsen, David Mumford, Bernard Saint-Donat) developed the broader theory of toroidal embeddings in *Toroidal Embeddings I* (Lecture Notes in Mathematics 339, Springer 1973) [ref: TODO_REF], where the lattice-and-fan formalism is recast as a general technique for resolving singularities and compactifying equivariant varieties. Tadao Oda's *Convex Bodies and Algebraic Geometry* (Springer 1988) [ref: TODO_REF] consolidated the Japanese-school perspective on toric varieties, with extensive emphasis on the lattice category and the dual-cone formalism.

William Fulton's *Introduction to Toric Varieties* (Annals of Mathematics Studies 131, Princeton 1993) [ref: TODO_REF] is the canonical short textbook on the subject, distilled from the William H. Roever Lectures at Washington University, St. Louis (1989). Fulton's §1.1 develops the algebraic torus, the lattices $M$ and $N$, and the pairing in approximately the form presented in this unit. The expanded modern textbook treatment, David Cox, John Little, and Henry Schenck's *Toric Varieties* (Graduate Studies in Mathematics 124, AMS 2011) [ref: TODO_REF], extends Fulton's exposition to nearly a thousand pages, with §1.1 providing the canonical reference for the algebraic-torus and lattice formalism in characteristic zero, and substantial later chapters covering Cox's homogeneous-coordinate-ring construction (Cox 1995 *The homogeneous coordinate ring of a toric variety*, J. Algebraic Geom.), mirror symmetry for toric Calabi-Yau hypersurfaces (Batyrev 1994), and the equivariant cohomology of toric varieties (Brion-Vergne 1997).
