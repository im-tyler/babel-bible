## Intuition [Beginner]

A *coherent sheaf* on a Noetherian scheme is a quasi-coherent sheaf with a *finiteness* property — locally, it is described by a finitely-generated module rather than an arbitrary one. Coherent sheaves are the algebraic-geometric analogue of "finite-dimensional vector spaces" or "finitely-generated modules": well-behaved enough to admit a clean cohomological theory.

Why coherent? The key reason is *cohomology*. On a projective scheme over a Noetherian base, coherent sheaves have *finite-dimensional* cohomology groups — $H^i(X; \mathcal{F})$ is a finitely-generated module over the base. This is the foundational input to Riemann-Roch, the Hilbert polynomial, and most computational tools in algebraic geometry. Quasi-coherent sheaves, in contrast, can have infinite-dimensional cohomology.

Examples of coherent sheaves: line bundles, vector bundles, ideal sheaves of closed subschemes, structure sheaves $\mathcal{O}_Y$ of closed subschemes $Y \subset X$, the canonical sheaf $\omega_X$, the sheaf of differentials $\Omega^1_{X/k}$. Almost every "algebraic-geometrically natural" sheaf is coherent.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X$ of any Noetherian scheme is coherent: it is locally generated by a single element ($1$). Line bundles $\mathcal{L}$ are coherent: they are locally free of rank 1 over $\mathcal{O}_X$.

For a closed subscheme $Y = V(I) \hookrightarrow X = \mathrm{Spec}(R)$ defined by a finitely-generated ideal $I \subset R$ (which $R$ Noetherian guarantees), the *ideal sheaf* $\mathcal{I}_Y \subset \mathcal{O}_X$ is coherent. The *quotient* $\mathcal{O}_Y = \mathcal{O}_X / \mathcal{I}_Y$ is also coherent. This is how subschemes give rise to coherent sheaves systematically.

Non-example: the sheaf $\widetilde{\bigoplus_n \mathbb{Z}}$ on $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$ corresponding to a countably infinite direct sum is quasi-coherent but *not* coherent — the underlying $\mathbb{Z}$-module is not finitely generated.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a *Noetherian scheme*. A **coherent sheaf** on $X$ is a quasi-coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$ such that on every affine open $U = \mathrm{Spec}(R) \subseteq X$, $\mathcal{F}|_U \cong \widetilde{M}$ where $M$ is a *finitely-generated* $R$-module.

Equivalently (over Noetherian schemes): $\mathcal{F}$ is coherent iff:

(C1) $\mathcal{F}$ is *locally finitely generated*: every point has a neighbourhood with a finite generating set.

(C2) For every open $U$ and every finite collection of sections $s_1, \ldots, s_k \in \mathcal{F}(U)$, the *kernel* of the induced map $\mathcal{O}_U^k \to \mathcal{F}|_U$, $(a_1, \ldots, a_k) \mapsto \sum a_i s_i$, is finitely generated.

The second condition (C2) is the key technical content: kernels of finite-rank maps from $\mathcal{O}^k$ should themselves be finitely generated. This is automatic over Noetherian schemes (where the structure sheaf is *itself* coherent). Over non-Noetherian schemes, coherent sheaves are a more restrictive notion than just "locally finitely generated."

**Equivalence on affine schemes (Noetherian).** For $X = \mathrm{Spec}(R)$ with $R$ Noetherian:

$$
\mathbf{Mod}^{\mathrm{fg}}(R) \xrightleftharpoons[\Gamma]{\widetilde{(\cdot)}} \mathbf{Coh}(\mathrm{Spec}(R)),
$$

an equivalence of abelian categories between *finitely-generated* $R$-modules and coherent sheaves on $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$.

**Properties.**

- *Closure under standard operations*: kernel, cokernel, image (via Noetherianness), tensor product, $\mathrm{Hom}$ (between coherent sheaves, on Noetherian schemes).
- *Pullback*: for a morphism $f : X \to Y$ of locally Noetherian schemes, $f^* \mathcal{G}$ of a coherent $\mathcal{G}$ is coherent on $X$.
- *Pushforward*: requires *proper* hypotheses (Grothendieck's *coherence-of-pushforward* theorem). For a proper morphism $f : X \to Y$ of locally Noetherian schemes and a coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$, $f_* \mathcal{F}$ is coherent on $Y$. The right derived $R^i f_* \mathcal{F}$ are also coherent.
- *Cohomology*: for a coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on a *projective* scheme $X$ over a Noetherian base, $H^i(X; \mathcal{F})$ is a finitely-generated module over the base.

**Examples.**

1. *Structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X$*: locally generated by 1, coherent.
2. *Ideal sheaf $\mathcal{I}_Y$*: coherent for closed subscheme $Y$ defined by a finitely-generated ideal.
3. *Quotient sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X / \mathcal{I}_Y = \mathcal{O}_Y$* of a closed subscheme: coherent.
4. *Line bundles $\mathcal{L}$*: locally free of rank 1, coherent.
5. *Vector bundles $\mathcal{E}$*: locally free of finite rank, coherent.
6. *Twisting sheaves $\mathcal{O}(d)$ on projective space*: line bundles, coherent.
7. *Sheaf of differentials $\Omega^1_{X/k}$*: coherent for $X$ a finite-type $k$-scheme.
8. *Skyscraper sheaves at closed points*: coherent (finite-dimensional fibre, zero elsewhere).

**Non-examples.**

- *Constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}$* on a positive-dimensional scheme: not even quasi-coherent.
- *Stalks $i_{x,*} \mathcal{O}_{X, x}$* at non-closed points: quasi-coherent but not coherent (the stalk is generally not finitely generated as an $\mathcal{O}_X$-module).
- *Direct sums $\bigoplus_{n \in \mathbb{N}} \mathcal{O}_X$*: quasi-coherent but not coherent.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (finiteness of coherent cohomology on projective schemes).** *Let $X$ be a projective scheme over a Noetherian ring $A$, and $\mathcal{F}$ a coherent sheaf on $X$. Then for every $i \geq 0$, $H^i(X; \mathcal{F})$ is a finitely-generated $A$-module.*

**Proof sketch.** Reduce to $X = \mathbb{P}^n_A$ and $\mathcal{F}$ a coherent sheaf on $\mathbb{P}^n_A$ (any projective scheme embeds as a closed subscheme of $\mathbb{P}^n_A$, and pushforward of coherent is coherent under closed embeddings, with cohomology preserved).

For $\mathcal{F}$ on $\mathbb{P}^n_A$ coherent: by *Serre's theorem*, there exists $d_0$ such that $\mathcal{F}(d)$ is generated by global sections for $d \geq d_0$. So we get a surjection $\mathcal{O}^N \to \mathcal{F}(d_0)$ for some finite $N$. Twisting back: $\mathcal{O}(-d_0)^N \to \mathcal{F}$ surjects, so $\mathcal{F}$ is a quotient of a coherent sheaf with known cohomology.

The cohomology of $\mathcal{O}(d)$ on $\mathbb{P}^n_A$ is computed explicitly: $H^0(\mathbb{P}^n_A; \mathcal{O}(d)) = A[x_0, \ldots, x_n]_d$ for $d \geq 0$ (a free $A$-module of finite rank), $H^n(\mathbb{P}^n_A; \mathcal{O}(d))$ is the dual for $d \leq -n - 1$, all other cohomology zero. Each cohomology group is a *finitely-generated $A$-module*.

By induction on the resolution length (using Hilbert's syzygy theorem for finitely-generated modules over a Noetherian ring), the long exact sequences in cohomology preserve finite generation, so $H^i(X; \mathcal{F})$ is finitely generated. $\square$

This is the *finiteness theorem* for coherent cohomology on projective schemes — the foundational result powering Riemann-Roch, Hilbert polynomials, and computational algebraic geometry.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [04.05.03] (line bundle on a scheme), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [04.04.01] (riemann-roch theorem for curves). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Show that the ideal sheaf $\mathcal{I}_Y$ of a closed subscheme $Y \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is coherent when $R$ is Noetherian.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The ideal $I \subset R$ is finitely generated by Noetherianness.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A closed subscheme of $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ corresponds to an ideal $I \subseteq R$. The ideal sheaf is $\mathcal{I}_Y = \widetilde{I}$, the sheafification of $I$ as an $R$-module. Since $R$ is Noetherian, $I$ is finitely generated as an $R$-module, so $\mathcal{I}_Y$ is coherent. $\square$

This shows the closed-subscheme construction, central to algebraic geometry, always produces coherent ideal sheaves.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, conceptual).**

Why is the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}$ not coherent on a positive-dimensional scheme?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

It's not even quasi-coherent.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}$ is not quasi-coherent on a positive-dimensional scheme (see the discussion in 04.06.01). Coherent sheaves are by definition quasi-coherent + finiteness, so failing quasi-coherence already rules out coherence.

The intuition: $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}$ has the same value $\mathbb{Z}$ on every connected open, regardless of the structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}$ on those opens. The value doesn't "scale" with the rings, so it cannot arise from any $\mathcal{O}$-module structure.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that on a Noetherian scheme, coherent sheaves are closed under tensor products.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Tensor product of finitely-generated modules over a Noetherian ring is finitely generated.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Locally on $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ with $R$ Noetherian, coherent sheaves correspond to finitely-generated $R$-modules. The tensor product $M \otimes_R N$ of two finitely-generated $R$-modules is finitely generated (generated by tensors $m_i \otimes n_j$ for finite generating sets $\{m_i\}, \{n_j\}$).

So $\widetilde{M} \otimes_{\mathcal{O}} \widetilde{N} = \widetilde{M \otimes_R N}$ is coherent. By the affine cover, this extends globally. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

Compute $\dim_k H^0(\mathbb{P}^2_k; \mathcal{I}_p)$ for $\mathcal{I}_p$ the ideal sheaf of a $k$-point $p \in \mathbb{P}^2_k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the short exact sequence $0 \to \mathcal{I}_p \to \mathcal{O} \to k_p \to 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The short exact sequence $0 \to \mathcal{I}_p \to \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2} \to k_p \to 0$ gives, in cohomology,

$$
0 \to H^0(\mathcal{I}_p) \to H^0(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}) \to H^0(k_p) \to H^1(\mathcal{I}_p) \to H^1(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}) \to \cdots.
$$

Now $H^0(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}) = k$, $H^0(k_p) = k$ (skyscraper at one point), $H^1(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}) = 0$. The map $k \to k$ is non-zero (it's the evaluation at $p$), hence an isomorphism. So $H^0(\mathcal{I}_p) = 0$ and $H^1(\mathcal{I}_p) = 0$.

So $\dim_k H^0(\mathbb{P}^2_k; \mathcal{I}_p) = 0$. (This is the statement that there are no non-zero global functions vanishing at $p$.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

State the *Cartan-Serre coherence theorem* in the analytic setting.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Proper holomorphic maps preserve coherence of analytic sheaves.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Cartan-Serre coherence theorem (1953).** Let $f : X \to Y$ be a *proper* holomorphic map of complex analytic spaces, and $\mathcal{F}$ a *coherent analytic sheaf* on $X$. Then the higher direct images $R^i f_* \mathcal{F}$ are coherent analytic sheaves on $Y$.

This was a foundational achievement of the Cartan seminars (1951–54), generalising classical theorems of Oka and Cartan. The algebraic version (Grothendieck's *Direct Image Theorem* in EGA III) extends this to proper morphisms of locally Noetherian schemes.

The geometric content: for a *family* $\{X_y\}_{y \in Y}$ of compact complex manifolds (a proper map), the cohomology $H^i(X_y; \mathcal{F}_y)$ varies *coherently* in $y$ — it forms a coherent sheaf on $Y$, hence is finite-dimensional fibrewise. This finiteness is the foundational input to Hodge theory, Riemann-Roch, and modern algebraic-analytic geometry.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that on a Noetherian scheme, every coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ admits a *finite locally free resolution* on every regular open subscheme.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Hilbert's syzygy theorem for finitely-generated modules over a regular ring of finite Krull dimension.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $X$ be a Noetherian scheme and $U \subseteq X$ a regular open subset (every local ring is a regular Noetherian ring). On any affine $\mathrm{Spec}(R) \subseteq U$, $R$ is a regular Noetherian ring of finite Krull dimension $d$. By *Hilbert's syzygy theorem*, every finitely-generated $R$-module has a finite resolution by free $R$-modules of length at most $d$:

$$
0 \to F_d \to F_{d-1} \to \cdots \to F_0 \to M \to 0
$$

with each $F_i$ a finite-rank free $R$-module. Sheafifying gives a finite locally-free resolution of $\widetilde{M}$ on $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$, hence (by gluing) on all of $U$. $\square$

This *resolution by locally-free sheaves* is the technical input to defining *Chern classes*, *K-theory*, and many cohomological invariants of coherent sheaves.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Explain why coherence is the *right* finiteness condition for algebraic geometry.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Coherence interacts well with kernels, cokernels, pushforward (under proper maps), and cohomology.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Coherence is *exactly* the finiteness condition that:

1. *Closes under standard operations*: kernels, cokernels (over Noetherian schemes), images, tensor products, $\mathrm{Hom}$ between coherent sheaves.
2. *Closes under pullback*: $f^* \mathcal{G}$ coherent for any morphism, $\mathcal{G}$ coherent.
3. *Closes under pushforward for proper maps*: $f_* \mathcal{F}$ and $R^i f_* \mathcal{F}$ are coherent for $f$ proper, $\mathcal{F}$ coherent.
4. *Has finite-dimensional cohomology on projective schemes*: $H^i(X; \mathcal{F})$ finitely-generated over the base.
5. *Admits resolutions by locally free sheaves* (on regular schemes), enabling Chern classes and K-theory.
6. *Forms an abelian category* with all the standard limit / colimit constructions.

Quasi-coherent sheaves are too general (infinite-dim cohomology); locally-free sheaves alone are too restrictive (not closed under cokernels). Coherent sheaves are the *Goldilocks zone*: just finite enough to be tractable, just general enough to capture all geometrically natural sheaves.

This is why every textbook in algebraic geometry returns again and again to coherent sheaves: they are the natural objects of study, the foundation of cohomological invariants, and the bridge between algebra (modules) and geometry (sheaves on schemes).

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Grothendieck's coherence-of-pushforward theorem (EGA III).** For a *proper morphism* $f : X \to Y$ between locally Noetherian schemes and a coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$, the right derived images $R^i f_* \mathcal{F}$ are coherent for all $i \geq 0$.

**Generic flatness.** A coherent sheaf on a Noetherian integral scheme is *generically flat*: there is a dense open over which the sheaf is flat (locally free over a torsion-free base).

**Resolution by locally-free sheaves.** Over regular Noetherian schemes, every coherent sheaf has a finite resolution by locally-free coherent sheaves (Hilbert's syzygy theorem). This is the foundation of algebraic *K-theory* (Quillen) and *Chow rings*.

**Hilbert polynomial.** For a coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on a closed subscheme $X \subseteq \mathbb{P}^n$, the Hilbert polynomial $p_{\mathcal{F}}(t) = \chi(X; \mathcal{F}(t))$ is a polynomial of degree $\dim X$. Its leading coefficient gives the degree, the constant term encodes the Euler characteristic of $\mathcal{O}_X$ (related to genus), and intermediate coefficients capture intersection numbers.

**Castelnuovo-Mumford regularity.** A coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $\mathbb{P}^n$ is *$m$-regular* if $H^i(\mathbb{P}^n; \mathcal{F}(m - i)) = 0$ for all $i \geq 1$. The *regularity* $\mathrm{reg}(\mathcal{F})$ is the minimal such $m$. Mumford's theory provides effective bounds.

**Six-functor formalism.** Coherent sheaves form the right setting for Grothendieck's six-functor formalism: $f_*, f^*, f_!, f^!, \otimes, \mathrm{Hom}$. The functors interact via duality (Serre, Grothendieck) and base-change theorems, providing a complete framework for cohomological algebra.

**Bondal-Orlov reconstruction.** A smooth projective variety $X$ over an algebraically closed field is determined up to isomorphism by its derived category $D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\, X)$ (when $X$ has ample (anti-)canonical class). This is one of the deepest "reconstruction" results in algebraic geometry.

**Bridgeland stability.** Stability conditions on $D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\, X)$ form a complex manifold $\mathrm{Stab}(X)$ — a moduli space of "stability conditions" with deep connections to symplectic geometry (Donaldson-Thomas theory) and string theory (Calabi-Yau varieties).

**Synthesis.** Coherent sheaves — quasi-coherent sheaves locally finitely generated over the structure sheaf — are the algebraic-geometric counterparts of finite-rank vector bundles and finitely generated modules simultaneously: they carry enough finiteness for cohomology to be finite-dimensional and for Euler characteristics to be well-defined. On smooth projective varieties, coherent sheaves decompose (via the Harder-Narasimhan filtration) into semistable pieces with controlled slope, and the moduli spaces of stable sheaves are themselves projective varieties whose geometry reflects the original variety. Through GAGA, derived categories, and the Hitchin-Kobayashi correspondence, coherent sheaves unify complex geometry, representation theory, and gauge-theoretic moduli problems.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The notion of *coherent sheaf* originates in the Cartan seminars of the early 1950s, in the context of complex analytic geometry. Henri Cartan's 1953 paper *Faisceaux analytiques cohérents* established the *Cartan-Oka coherence theorems*: the structure sheaf of a complex manifold is coherent, and proper holomorphic images of coherent sheaves are coherent. These were foundational achievements at the boundary of complex analysis and algebraic geometry.

Jean-Pierre Serre's *Faisceaux Algébriques Cohérents* (FAC, 1955) introduced the algebraic version: coherent sheaves on algebraic varieties (in the Zariski topology), with their finite-dimensional cohomology, twisting sheaves $\mathcal{O}(d)$, and the foundational vanishing-and-formula theorem for $\mathbb{P}^n$. FAC was a watershed: it moved algebraic geometry from the pre-war classical perspective into the modern sheaf-theoretic framework.

Alexander Grothendieck's *Éléments de Géométrie Algébrique* (EGA, 1960–67) extended coherent sheaf theory to schemes:

- *EGA I §1*: quasi-coherent and coherent modules on locally ringed spaces.
- *EGA III*: cohomology of coherent sheaves, including the coherence-of-pushforward theorem for proper morphisms.
- *EGA IV*: coherent sheaves and morphisms of schemes, including flatness, smoothness, and étale properties.

The conceptual revolution: *coherent sheaves are the right notion of "finite-dimensional algebraic-geometric object."* Just as finite-dimensional vector spaces have a clean theory (rank, eigenvalues, characteristic polynomial), coherent sheaves have a clean theory (Hilbert polynomial, Chern classes, Euler characteristic). Most of modern algebraic geometry is fundamentally about *coherent sheaves on schemes*: classification (via Hilbert schemes, moduli problems), invariants (via cohomology, K-theory), and structure (via derived categories, Bondal-Orlov reconstruction).

Today coherent sheaves continue to be central:

- *Derived categories of coherent sheaves* $D^b(\mathrm{Coh}\, X)$ encode the geometry of $X$ in a triangulated framework (Bondal, Orlov, Kontsevich).
- *Geometric Langlands* studies $D$-modules (a derived enhancement of coherent sheaves) on moduli spaces.
- *Donaldson-Thomas theory* studies coherent sheaves on Calabi-Yau threefolds, with deep connections to string theory and enumerative geometry.
- *Perfectoid spaces* (Scholze) extend coherent-sheaf-theoretic methods to $p$-adic geometry.

The deep insight of the Cartan-Serre-Grothendieck era — that coherent sheaves are the *right* finiteness class — has shaped algebraic geometry for 70 years and continues to drive its most exciting modern developments.
