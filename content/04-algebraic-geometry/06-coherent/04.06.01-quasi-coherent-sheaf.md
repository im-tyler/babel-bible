## Intuition [Beginner]

A *quasi-coherent sheaf* on a scheme $X$ is a sheaf that, locally on every affine open $U = \mathrm{Spec}(R) \subseteq X$, looks like a module over the ring $R$. It is the natural notion of a sheaf adapted to the algebraic structure of a scheme — sheaves whose sections behave like modules.

The slogan: on an affine scheme $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$, quasi-coherent sheaves correspond bijectively to $R$-modules. Module $M$ goes to the sheaf $\widetilde{M}$, characterised by $\widetilde{M}(D(f)) = M[f^{-1}]$. Conversely, the global sections of $\widetilde{M}$ recover $M$.

The class of quasi-coherent sheaves is *exactly the right class* of sheaves on a scheme. Coherent sheaves (a stricter subclass) are well-behaved analogues of finitely-generated modules. Vector bundles, line bundles, and structure sheaves are all quasi-coherent. The theory of cohomology and many key constructions (pullback, pushforward, tensor product) work cleanly only for quasi-coherent sheaves.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X$ is the canonical quasi-coherent sheaf. On an affine $U = \mathrm{Spec}(R)$, $\mathcal{O}_X|_U = \widetilde{R}$ — the sheaf associated to $R$ as an $R$-module over itself.

Another example: for a homogeneous ideal $I \subseteq k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]$, the *ideal sheaf* $\mathcal{I}_X \subseteq \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^n}$ of the closed subscheme $X = V_+(I)$ is quasi-coherent. On affine charts, it is the sheaf associated to the ideal in the local ring.

A non-example: the *constant sheaf* $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}$ on a scheme. This is a sheaf, and well-defined topologically, but does *not* arise from any module structure on the affine charts (since modules over different rings on different affines don't naturally agree on overlaps). It is sheaf-cohomology-meaningful but not a quasi-coherent sheaf.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a scheme. A sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ of $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules is **quasi-coherent** if every point $x \in X$ has an affine open neighbourhood $U = \mathrm{Spec}(R) \ni x$ such that $\mathcal{F}|_U \cong \widetilde{M}$ for some $R$-module $M$, where $\widetilde{M}$ denotes the sheaf associated to $M$:

$$
\widetilde{M}(D(f)) := M[f^{-1}] = M \otimes_R R[f^{-1}], \qquad f \in R.
$$

Equivalently: $\mathcal{F}$ is quasi-coherent iff for every affine open $U = \mathrm{Spec}(R) \subseteq X$, $\mathcal{F}|_U \cong \widetilde{\mathcal{F}(U)}$.

**Equivalence of categories on affine schemes.** The functors

$$
\mathbf{Mod}(R) \xrightleftharpoons[\Gamma]{\widetilde{(\cdot)}} \mathbf{QCoh}(\mathrm{Spec}(R))
$$

are mutually inverse equivalences. The forward functor sheafifies an $R$-module; the backward functor takes global sections.

**Properties.**

- *Closure under standard operations*: kernel, cokernel, image, direct sum, tensor product, $\mathrm{Hom}$ (for finite presentation in the second slot).
- *Pullback*: for a morphism $f : X \to Y$ and quasi-coherent $\mathcal{G}$ on $Y$, $f^* \mathcal{G}$ is quasi-coherent on $X$.
- *Pushforward*: for *quasi-compact and quasi-separated* $f : X \to Y$ and quasi-coherent $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$, $f_* \mathcal{F}$ is quasi-coherent on $Y$.
- *Cohomology*: on an affine scheme, quasi-coherent sheaves have *zero higher cohomology* (Serre's vanishing). On projective schemes, the higher cohomology is governed by the Serre formula and Hilbert polynomial.

**Affine criterion.** A scheme $X$ is *affine* iff for every quasi-coherent ideal sheaf $\mathcal{I} \subseteq \mathcal{O}_X$, $H^1(X; \mathcal{I}) = 0$. This characterises affine schemes cohomologically.

**Examples.**

1. *Structure sheaf.* $\mathcal{O}_X$ is quasi-coherent.
2. *Ideal sheaves.* For a closed subscheme $Y \hookrightarrow X$, the ideal sheaf $\mathcal{I}_Y \subset \mathcal{O}_X$ is quasi-coherent.
3. *Twisting sheaves on $\mathbb{P}^n$.* $\mathcal{O}(d)$ for $d \in \mathbb{Z}$ — quasi-coherent (and even coherent).
4. *Module sheafification.* For any $R$-module $M$, $\widetilde{M}$ is quasi-coherent on $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$.
5. *Direct images.* For a morphism $f : X \to Y$ with mild hypotheses, $f_* \mathcal{O}_X$ is quasi-coherent.

**On Proj.** A graded $S$-module $M$ has an associated quasi-coherent sheaf $\widetilde{M}$ on $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$:

$$
\widetilde{M}(D(f)) := (M[f^{-1}])_0 \quad \text{for homogeneous } f \in S_+.
$$

The functor $\widetilde{(\cdot)}$ from graded $S$-modules to quasi-coherent sheaves on $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$ is *not* an equivalence — *graded modules differing only in low degrees give the same sheaf*. The kernel is the *torsion category* of modules supported in low degrees. Quotienting out gives the equivalence

$$
\mathbf{grMod}(S) / \mathbf{Tor} \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathbf{QCoh}(\mathrm{Proj}(S)).
$$

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (equivalence of $R$-modules and quasi-coherent sheaves on $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$).** *The functor $\widetilde{(\cdot)} : \mathbf{Mod}(R) \to \mathbf{QCoh}(\mathrm{Spec}(R))$, $M \mapsto \widetilde{M}$, is an equivalence of abelian categories. Its inverse is the global sections functor $\Gamma$.*

**Proof sketch.**

*Step 1. $\widetilde{(\cdot)}$ produces a quasi-coherent sheaf.* By construction, $\widetilde{M}|_{D(f)} = \widetilde{M[f^{-1}]}$ is the sheafification of the $R[f^{-1}]$-module $M[f^{-1}]$, which is the affine-cover quasi-coherent property.

*Step 2. $\Gamma \circ \widetilde{(\cdot)} = \mathrm{id}$ on modules.* For an $R$-module $M$, $\Gamma(\mathrm{Spec}(R), \widetilde{M}) = \widetilde{M}(D(1)) = M[1^{-1}] = M$.

*Step 3. $\widetilde{(\cdot)} \circ \Gamma = \mathrm{id}$ on quasi-coherent sheaves.* For a quasi-coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$, set $M = \mathcal{F}(\mathrm{Spec}(R))$. The natural map $\widetilde{M} \to \mathcal{F}$ is constructed by sending $m/f^n \in M[f^{-1}]$ to the corresponding section in $\mathcal{F}(D(f))$ (using the localisation property of quasi-coherent sheaves). It is an isomorphism on every $D(f)$ by the affine quasi-coherent property, hence on $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ globally.

*Step 4. Exactness.* Both functors preserve exact sequences (sheafification is exact on the affine cover; global sections is exact for quasi-coherent sheaves on affine schemes by Serre's vanishing). $\square$

This equivalence is the fundamental dictionary of algebraic geometry: *commutative algebra (modules over rings) on one side, sheaf theory (quasi-coherent sheaves on schemes) on the other.* Most algebraic-geometric constructions are translations between the two languages.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Show that the structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X$ of any scheme $X$ is quasi-coherent.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On an affine open, $\mathcal{O}_X$ is by definition $\widetilde{R}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For any scheme $X$, every point has an affine neighbourhood $U = \mathrm{Spec}(R)$ on which $\mathcal{O}_X|_U \cong \widetilde{R}$ — this is the definition of the structure sheaf on affines. Quasi-coherence is precisely this local property. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

Compute $\widetilde{M}(D(f))$ for $M = \mathbb{Z}/6$ as a $\mathbb{Z}$-module, $R = \mathbb{Z}$, $f = 2 \in \mathbb{Z}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Localisation $M[f^{-1}]$ inverts powers of $f$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\widetilde{M}(D(2)) = M[2^{-1}] = (\mathbb{Z}/6)[2^{-1}]$. In $\mathbb{Z}/6$, $2$ has $2 \cdot 3 = 0$, so $2$ is a zero-divisor. $(\mathbb{Z}/6)[2^{-1}] = \mathbb{Z}/6 \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} \mathbb{Z}[2^{-1}]$. The element $3$ in $\mathbb{Z}/6$ becomes 0 after inverting 2: $3 = 2 \cdot 3 / 2 = 0/2 = 0$. So $(\mathbb{Z}/6)[2^{-1}] = \mathbb{Z}/3 \otimes \mathbb{Z}[2^{-1}] = \mathbb{Z}/3$ (since 2 is invertible mod 3).

So $\widetilde{\mathbb{Z}/6}(D(2)) = \mathbb{Z}/3$. The localisation has killed the "2-part" of $\mathbb{Z}/6$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that quasi-coherent sheaves are closed under kernels and cokernels.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Kernels and cokernels of $R$-module maps are $R$-modules; sheafification commutes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\varphi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{G}$ be a morphism of quasi-coherent sheaves on $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$. Write $\mathcal{F} = \widetilde{M}$, $\mathcal{G} = \widetilde{N}$ for $R$-modules, and $\varphi$ corresponds to an $R$-linear $\phi : M \to N$.

The kernel of $\varphi$ in sheaves is the sheafification of $\ker(\phi)$, which is also an $R$-module. So $\ker \varphi = \widetilde{\ker \phi}$, quasi-coherent.

Similarly, the cokernel in sheaves is the sheafification of $\mathrm{coker}(\phi)$, an $R$-module. So $\mathrm{coker}\, \varphi = \widetilde{\mathrm{coker}\, \phi}$, quasi-coherent.

By the affine cover, this extends to general schemes. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

For the closed embedding $i : \{[1:0]\} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{P}^1$, compute the ideal sheaf $\mathcal{I}_{[1:0]}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$[1:0]$ corresponds to the homogeneous prime $(x_1)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The point $[1:0] \subset \mathbb{P}^1 = \mathrm{Proj}(k[x_0, x_1])$ corresponds to the homogeneous prime $(x_1)$. The ideal sheaf is the ideal-sheaf-version of $\mathcal{O}(-1)$:

$$
\mathcal{I}_{[1:0]} = \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(-1) \cdot x_1 / (\text{tautological vanishing on } [1:0]) \cong \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(-1).
$$

(Equivalently: $\mathcal{I}_{[1:0]}$ is the kernel of the surjection $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1} \twoheadrightarrow k_{[1:0]}$ to the skyscraper sheaf at $[1:0]$. The kernel is $\mathcal{O}(-1)$ via the section $x_1$.)

So the ideal sheaf is $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^1}(-1)$, a line bundle of degree $-1$ on $\mathbb{P}^1$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why isn't the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}$ quasi-coherent on a scheme of positive dimension?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The constant sheaf does not arise from a single module on each affine.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ for $R$ Noetherian and connected, the constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}$ has *all* sections equal to $\mathbb{Z}$ (one copy per connected component — by definition of constant sheaf). So $\Gamma(\mathrm{Spec}(R); \underline{\mathbb{Z}}) = \mathbb{Z}$.

If $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}$ were quasi-coherent, it would equal $\widetilde{\mathbb{Z}}$ as a sheaf of $\mathbb{Z}$-modules. But $\widetilde{\mathbb{Z}}(D(f)) = \mathbb{Z}[f^{-1}]$ for $f \in R$, whereas the constant sheaf gives $\mathbb{Z}$ on every connected open.

For example on $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$: $D(2) = \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}) \setminus \{(2)\}$ is connected. $\widetilde{\mathbb{Z}}(D(2)) = \mathbb{Z}[2^{-1}]$, but $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}(D(2)) = \mathbb{Z}$. So $\underline{\mathbb{Z}} \neq \widetilde{\mathbb{Z}}$, and $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}$ is *not* quasi-coherent.

The constant sheaf is well-defined as a sheaf of abelian groups but doesn't have the "module-over-the-ring" property that quasi-coherence requires.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

State Serre's vanishing theorem on affine schemes for quasi-coherent sheaves.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Higher sheaf cohomology vanishes on affines.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Serre's vanishing theorem.** Let $X = \mathrm{Spec}(R)$ be an affine scheme and $\mathcal{F}$ a quasi-coherent sheaf on $X$. Then $H^i(X; \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $i \geq 1$.

This is the foundational vanishing theorem for affine schemes. The proof uses that $\widetilde{M}$ has zero higher cohomology because $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ has a basis of distinguished opens with vanishing higher Čech cohomology.

This implies: a short exact sequence of quasi-coherent sheaves on an affine scheme remains short exact in global sections. The $H^1$ obstruction (Mittag-Leffler problem in topological terms) vanishes.

The dual statement (cohomology of projective schemes via twists) involves Serre's *projective vanishing*: for coherent $\mathcal{F}$ on $\mathbb{P}^n$, $H^i(\mathbb{P}^n; \mathcal{F}(d)) = 0$ for $i \geq 1$ and $d$ large.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Why is the equivalence "graded modules ↔ quasi-coherent sheaves on $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$" only modulo torsion?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Modules supported in low degrees vanish on Proj.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Proj construction excludes the irrelevant ideal $S_+$. So a graded $S$-module $M$ supported only in low degrees (every element annihilated by some power of $S_+$) sheafifies to *zero* on $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$:

$$
\widetilde{M}(D(f)) = (M[f^{-1}])_0 = 0 \quad \text{for } f \in S_+.
$$

This is because any element $m \in M$ has $f^n \cdot m = 0$ for some $n$ (since $M$ is torsion at $S_+$), hence $m/f^k = 0$ in $M[f^{-1}]$.

So *torsion modules* in the graded sense — those supported in low degrees, equivalently $H^0_{S_+}(M) = M$ — go to zero in $\mathbf{QCoh}(\mathrm{Proj}(S))$. The equivalence is

$$
\mathbf{grMod}(S) / \mathbf{Tor}_{S_+} \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathbf{QCoh}(\mathrm{Proj}(S)).
$$

This phenomenon — that *low-degree* graded data is forgotten by Proj — is one of the fundamental subtleties of projective algebraic geometry. It is the algebraic shadow of "the irrelevant ideal corresponds to the origin we removed before projectivising." Castelnuovo-Mumford regularity quantifies how much of the low-degree data can be safely forgotten.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Quasi-coherent sheaves and module categories.** $\mathbf{QCoh}(X)$ is a Grothendieck abelian category — has all colimits, filtered colimits exact, generators. On a quasi-compact and quasi-separated scheme, $\mathbf{QCoh}$ has enough injectives.

**Pushforward exact sequences.** For $f : X \to Y$ a morphism, the pushforward $f_*$ on quasi-coherent sheaves preserves limits but not in general colimits. The right-derived $R^i f_*$ are quasi-coherent under quasi-compact-quasi-separated hypotheses; this is the foundation of Grothendieck's six-functor formalism.

**Quasi-coherent sheaves with extra structure.** Sheaves of $\mathcal{O}_X$-algebras (sheaves of rings extending $\mathcal{O}_X$); sheaves of Lie algebroids; D-modules. All are quasi-coherent with extra structure.

**Spec / QCoh duality.** For a Noetherian scheme $X$ with sufficient finiteness conditions, the *spectrum* of $\mathbf{QCoh}(X)$ as a tensor-triangulated category recovers $X$ — a *reconstruction theorem* (Bondal-van den Bergh, Balmer). $\mathbf{QCoh}$ thus contains all geometric information.

**Tannakian formalism.** Quasi-coherent sheaves with extra structure (e.g., flat connection) form a tannakian category whose Tannaka dual is a (group) scheme. This extends the Spec-construction one categorical level up.

**Geometric Langlands.** $D$-modules on $\mathrm{Bun}_G$ (the moduli of $G$-bundles on a curve) are conjecturally dual to quasi-coherent sheaves on $\mathrm{Loc}_{G^\vee}$ (the moduli of $G^\vee$-local systems). This is the *categorical Langlands correspondence*, with quasi-coherent sheaves at its heart.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The notion of *coherent sheaf* was introduced by Henri Cartan and Jean-Pierre Serre in the early 1950s, in the context of complex-analytic geometry. Cartan's seminars (1951–54) developed coherent analytic sheaves and the *Cartan-Serre-Grauert* coherence theorems for proper holomorphic maps.

The algebraic version — *quasi-coherent and coherent sheaves on schemes* — was formalised by Serre's foundational 1955 paper *Faisceaux Algébriques Cohérents* (FAC). FAC introduced sheaf-theoretic methods to algebraic geometry, including:

- Quasi-coherent and coherent sheaves on a variety.
- Cohomology of coherent sheaves (later understood as sheaf cohomology in the Grothendieck framework).
- Serre's vanishing theorem on affine varieties.
- The Serre-twist sheaves $\mathcal{O}(d)$ and the cohomology of $\mathcal{O}(d)$ on $\mathbb{P}^n$.

FAC was a foundational achievement, providing the framework that Grothendieck's *EGA* (Éléments de Géométrie Algébrique, 1960–67) extended to schemes. EGA Chapter I §1 systematically develops quasi-coherent modules in scheme-theoretic generality.

The conceptual revolution: *algebraic geometry is fundamentally the study of quasi-coherent sheaves on schemes.* Every important geometric object — varieties, line bundles, vector bundles, ideal sheaves, moduli spaces — is built from quasi-coherent data. The category $\mathbf{QCoh}(X)$ contains essentially all the algebraic-geometric information about $X$.

Today, quasi-coherent sheaves are ubiquitous: in derived algebraic geometry (Lurie's $\infty$-categorical refinement), in geometric Langlands (D-modules and quasi-coherent sheaves on moduli stacks), in derived categories of coherent sheaves (Bondal-Orlov reconstruction, Bridgeland stability conditions), and in arithmetic geometry (étale sheaves, perverse sheaves, motives). The legacy of Cartan-Serre-Grothendieck's foundational work permeates modern mathematics.
