## Intuition [Beginner]

An *affine scheme* is the geometric object built from a commutative ring. The fundamental dictionary of modern algebraic geometry: every commutative ring corresponds to a geometric space, and ring homomorphisms correspond to maps of those spaces — backwards.

The key construction: take a commutative ring $R$, and form the space $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ whose *points* are the prime ideals of $R$. So a polynomial ring like $\mathbb{C}[x, y]$ has a point for every prime ideal — and the maximal ideals correspond to ordinary geometric points $(a, b) \in \mathbb{C}^2$ via $\mathfrak{m} = (x - a, y - b)$. The non-maximal primes correspond to "generic" points: the prime $(x - y)$ corresponds to the curve $\{x = y\}$, the zero ideal $(0)$ corresponds to all of $\mathbb{C}^2$.

This dictionary lets you do geometry by manipulating rings and ideals. Polynomial rings give affine spaces; quotient rings $\mathbb{C}[x, y]/(f)$ give zero loci of polynomials; localised rings give open subsets. The affine scheme is the most fundamental geometric object: every scheme is built from affine schemes by gluing.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The affine scheme $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C}[x])$ is the affine line over $\mathbb{C}$. Its prime ideals are:

- The zero ideal $(0)$: the *generic point* of the line.
- The maximal ideals $(x - a)$ for each $a \in \mathbb{C}$: the *closed points*, corresponding to ordinary points of $\mathbb{A}^1(\mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{C}$.

So the affine line as a scheme has all the ordinary $\mathbb{C}$-points, plus one extra "generic point" that hovers over everything. This generic point is closed in *no* nonempty open set — it lives in every nonempty Zariski open. Its presence makes the algebraic geometry richer: properties that hold "generically" (away from a closed proper subset) are exactly properties of the generic point.

A more interesting example: $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$. The prime ideals of $\mathbb{Z}$ are: the zero ideal $(0)$, and the maximal ideals $(p)$ for each prime number $p$. So $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$ has one closed point per prime number plus a generic point. This is a 1-dimensional affine scheme — the *arithmetic line*. The geometry of $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$ is one of the deepest objects in mathematics: it carries the foundations of number theory.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $R$ be a commutative ring with unit. The **affine scheme** $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ consists of three pieces of data:

**(1) Underlying set.** $|\mathrm{Spec}(R)| = \{\mathfrak{p} \subset R : \mathfrak{p} \text{ is a prime ideal}\}$.

**(2) Zariski topology.** Closed subsets are the *vanishing loci*

$$
V(I) := \{\mathfrak{p} \in \mathrm{Spec}(R) : I \subseteq \mathfrak{p}\}
$$

for ideals $I \subseteq R$. Open subsets are complements of these. The *distinguished open subsets* $D(f) := \mathrm{Spec}(R) \setminus V(f) = \{\mathfrak{p} : f \notin \mathfrak{p}\}$ for $f \in R$ form a basis.

**(3) Structure sheaf.** The structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{Spec}(R)}$ is the sheaf of rings characterised by

$$
\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{Spec}(R)}(D(f)) = R[f^{-1}] = R_f \quad \text{(localisation at } f\text{)},
$$

extended to general open sets by sheafification. The *stalk* at a prime $\mathfrak{p}$ is the localisation:

$$
\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{Spec}(R), \mathfrak{p}} = R_\mathfrak{p} = (R \setminus \mathfrak{p})^{-1} R.
$$

The triple $(\mathrm{Spec}(R), \mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{Spec}(R)})$ is a *locally ringed space*: each stalk is a local ring, with maximal ideal $\mathfrak{p} R_\mathfrak{p}$.

**Examples.**

1. $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]) = \mathbb{A}^n_{\mathbb{C}}$, the affine $n$-space.
2. $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}) = $ the arithmetic line: closed points are prime numbers $p$, plus the generic point $(0)$.
3. $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C}[x, y]/(xy)) = $ the union of the two coordinate axes in $\mathbb{A}^2$.
4. $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C}[x]/(x^2)) = $ a "thickened point": one closed point with a tangent direction. Reduced: $\mathbb{C}$. Non-reduced: this carries nilpotent information.
5. $\mathrm{Spec}(R[x]) \cong \mathrm{Spec}(R) \times \mathbb{A}^1$ — affine spaces fibre over base schemes.

**Functor of points.** The contravariant functor $\mathrm{Spec} : \mathbf{CRing} \to \mathbf{LRS}$ (locally ringed spaces) factors through the equivalence

$$
\mathbf{CRing}^{\mathrm{op}} \cong \mathbf{AffSch}.
$$

For a ring $R$ and a scheme $X$, $\mathrm{Hom}(\mathrm{Spec}(R), X) = \mathrm{Hom}(\mathcal{O}_X(X), R)$ — the geometric maps from $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ to $X$ are determined by ring maps in the reverse direction.

**Properties.**

- $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is *connected* iff $R$ has no nontrivial idempotents.
- $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is *irreducible* iff $R$ has a unique minimal prime (i.e., $\mathrm{Nil}(R)$ is prime).
- $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is *quasi-compact* (every open cover has a finite subcover) — always.
- $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is *Noetherian* iff $R$ is Noetherian.
- The *generic points* of $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ correspond to minimal primes; *closed points* to maximal ideals.

**The affine scheme is universal among locally ringed spaces.** Given any locally ringed space $(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$, $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{LRS}}(X, \mathrm{Spec}(R)) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Ring}}(R, \mathcal{O}_X(X))$. This adjunction (the *Spec / Γ adjunction*) is the foundational structural theorem of algebraic geometry.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Spec / Γ adjunction).** *The functors $\mathrm{Spec} : \mathbf{CRing}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{LRS}$ (Spec construction) and $\Gamma : \mathbf{LRS} \to \mathbf{CRing}^{\mathrm{op}}$ (global sections) are adjoint:*

$$
\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{LRS}}(X, \mathrm{Spec}(R)) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Ring}}(R, \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X)).
$$

*The adjunction restricts to an equivalence of categories $\mathbf{CRing}^{\mathrm{op}} \cong \mathbf{AffSch}$.*

**Proof sketch.**

Forward direction. A morphism $\varphi : X \to \mathrm{Spec}(R)$ in $\mathbf{LRS}$ pulls back the structure sheaf, giving a ring map $\varphi^\sharp : R = \Gamma(\mathrm{Spec}(R), \mathcal{O}) \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$.

Backward direction. Given a ring map $\psi : R \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$, construct a morphism $\varphi : X \to \mathrm{Spec}(R)$ as follows. For each $x \in X$, the stalk $\mathcal{O}_{X, x}$ is a local ring with maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}_x$, and the composition $R \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X) \to \mathcal{O}_{X, x}$ has a kernel $\mathfrak{p}_x \subset R$. Define $\varphi(x) := \mathfrak{p}_x$ (a prime of $R$, i.e., a point of $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$). The map $\varphi$ is continuous (preimage of $V(I)$ is the closed locus where $\psi(I)$ is in the maximal ideal of every stalk) and the structure sheaf morphism is induced by $\psi$ on global sections, then sheafified.

The two constructions are mutually inverse on global sections by direct verification, and the local-ring condition makes each natural map respect the local structure. $\square$

This adjunction is the foundational theorem of scheme theory: it identifies the category of affine schemes with the opposite of commutative rings, providing the dictionary that powers the entire subject. When $X = \mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is itself affine, the adjunction reduces to the identity $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Ring}}(R, S) = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Ring}}(R, S)$, confirming the equivalence.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [04.02.03] (projective scheme), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [04.02.04] (morphism of schemes). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Compute the points of $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{R}[x])$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Find all prime ideals of $\mathbb{R}[x]$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The prime ideals of $\mathbb{R}[x]$ are:

- The zero ideal $(0)$ — the generic point.
- $(x - a)$ for each $a \in \mathbb{R}$ — the *real points*.
- $(x^2 + bx + c)$ for irreducible quadratics — the *complex-conjugate point pairs* with no real representative.

So $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{R}[x])$ has $\mathbb{R}$ many closed points labelled by reals, $\frac{1}{2}|\mathbb{C} \setminus \mathbb{R}|$ closed points (one for each conjugate pair), plus the generic point. Geometrically: the real line plus the upper half plane (each point representing a Galois orbit), plus a generic point.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, conceptual).**

Show that $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$ is irreducible.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A scheme is irreducible iff its underlying topological space cannot be written as a union of two proper closed subsets.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The closed subsets of $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$ are: the whole space (corresponding to $(0)$), and finite unions of points $V((p)) = \{(p)\}$ for primes $p$. The whole space cannot be written as a finite union of proper closed sets. $\square$

Equivalently: $\mathbb{Z}$ has a unique minimal prime — the zero ideal — and irreducibility of $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is equivalent to the existence of a unique minimal prime in $R$, i.e., the nilradical $\mathrm{Nil}(\mathbb{Z}) = 0$ is prime.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is *quasi-compact* (every open cover has a finite subcover) for any commutative ring $R$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reduce to a cover by distinguished opens; use that $1 \in R$ generates the whole ring.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $\mathrm{Spec}(R) = \bigcup_{i \in I} U_i$. Each $U_i$ is a union of distinguished opens $D(f_{i, j})$, so we may refine to a cover by distinguished opens: $\mathrm{Spec}(R) = \bigcup_k D(f_k)$.

Now $D(f_k) = \{\mathfrak{p} : f_k \notin \mathfrak{p}\}$, so $\bigcup_k D(f_k) = \{\mathfrak{p} : \text{not all } f_k \in \mathfrak{p}\} = \{\mathfrak{p} : (f_k : k) \not\subseteq \mathfrak{p}\}$. The complement is $V((f_k : k))$. So $\bigcup_k D(f_k) = \mathrm{Spec}(R)$ iff $(f_k : k) = R$ iff $1 \in (f_k : k)$, i.e., $1 = \sum_l a_l f_{k_l}$ for some finite subset.

So a finite subset $\{f_{k_1}, \ldots, f_{k_n}\}$ already generates the unit ideal, hence $\mathrm{Spec}(R) = \bigcup_l D(f_{k_l})$ is a finite subcover. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

Identify the closed points of $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C}[x, y]/(y^2 - x^3))$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Maximal ideals correspond to points satisfying the equation $y^2 = x^3$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The closed points are pairs $(a, b) \in \mathbb{C}^2$ satisfying $b^2 = a^3$, which is the *cuspidal cubic curve* with cusp at $(0, 0)$.

Parametrise: $(a, b) = (t^2, t^3)$ for $t \in \mathbb{C}$. So the closed points are in bijection with $\mathbb{C}$ via $t$, but the curve has a cusp at $t = 0$ where the tangent vector vanishes.

The non-closed points: the zero ideal $(0)$ (generic point of the curve). The maximal ideal corresponding to the cusp $(0, 0)$ is $\mathfrak{m} = (x, y)$, with $\mathcal{O}_{X, \mathfrak{m}}$ a non-regular local ring (the cusp is a singularity).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why does the spectrum include non-maximal primes — what extra information do they carry?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Generic points carry "generic" geometric information.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Non-maximal primes correspond to *generic points* of subvarieties of $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$:

- The zero ideal of an integral domain $R$ is the generic point of all of $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$.
- A height-1 prime corresponds to the generic point of a codimension-1 subvariety.
- A prime of height $h$ is the generic point of a codimension-$h$ subvariety.

The closure $\overline{\{\mathfrak{p}\}}$ of a single point in $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is exactly $V(\mathfrak{p})$, the corresponding closed subvariety. So the *generic point* of a closed subvariety $V \subset \mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is the unique point whose closure is $V$.

Generic points are essential because they let you discuss properties that hold "away from a proper closed subset." Many algebraic-geometric arguments are about *generic behaviour*: properties holding on a dense open set are equivalent to properties holding at the generic point. Without generic points, this language is unavailable.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is *sober*: every irreducible closed subset has a unique generic point.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Irreducible closed subsets correspond to prime ideals.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A closed subset $V(I) \subseteq \mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is irreducible iff $\sqrt{I}$ is prime. (One direction: if $V(I) = V(J_1) \cup V(J_2)$ properly, then $\sqrt{I} = \sqrt{J_1} \cap \sqrt{J_2}$, contradicting primality of $\sqrt{I}$. Other direction: similar argument.)

Given irreducible closed $V = V(\sqrt{I})$ for $\mathfrak{p} = \sqrt{I}$ prime, the generic point of $V$ is exactly $\mathfrak{p}$ (its closure is $V$). The generic point is unique because $V(\mathfrak{q}) = V(\mathfrak{p})$ implies $\mathfrak{p} = \sqrt{\mathfrak{q}} = \mathfrak{q}$ for primes.

So every irreducible closed subset has a unique generic point — $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is sober. $\square$

Soberness is automatic for spectra and is one of the reasons the spectrum is the *correct* topological model for a ring.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Explain how nilpotents enrich algebraic geometry by allowing non-reduced schemes.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C}[x]/(x))$ with $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C}[x]/(x^2))$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The ring $\mathbb{C}[x]/(x)$ is the field $\mathbb{C}$, with $\mathrm{Spec}$ a single point.

The ring $\mathbb{C}[x]/(x^2)$ is a 2-dimensional $\mathbb{C}$-algebra with nilpotent $x$ ($x^2 = 0$ but $x \neq 0$). $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C}[x]/(x^2))$ also has a single point (the only prime is $(x)$), but the structure sheaf is different — $\mathcal{O}(\mathrm{pt}) = \mathbb{C}[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)$, the *dual numbers*. Geometrically, this is "a point with an attached tangent direction."

The benefit: nilpotents encode infinitesimal data — first-order tangent vectors, jet schemes, deformation theory. Variation of structures, formal neighbourhoods, intersection multiplicities, and the very meaning of "tangent space" all rely on non-reduced schemes.

For example, the *intersection multiplicity* of a curve and a tangent line at a point of tangency is encoded by the non-reduced structure of the intersection scheme, not by the reduced underlying point. *Bezout's theorem* and *intersection theory* require non-reduced structure to give the right answers.

So nilpotents — invisible classically — are the door to deformation theory, infinitesimal calculations, and the modern view that "geometry = locally ringed spaces, with rings allowed to have nilpotents."

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Spec / Γ adjunction.** The full statement: $\mathrm{Spec} : \mathbf{CRing}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{LRS}$ is right-adjoint to global sections $\Gamma : \mathbf{LRS} \to \mathbf{CRing}^{\mathrm{op}}$, and the unit and counit witness the equivalence of $\mathbf{CRing}^{\mathrm{op}}$ with affine schemes.

**Hilbert's Nullstellensatz (geometric form).** For a finitely-generated $k$-algebra $R$ over an algebraically closed field $k$:

- *Maximal ideals* of $R$ correspond bijectively to points of the *classical variety* $V_k(R) \subset k^n$.
- $R \mapsto V_k(R)$ is a contravariant equivalence between finitely-generated reduced $k$-algebras and affine algebraic varieties.

Thus the affine scheme generalises the classical variety: every closed point of $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ for finitely-generated $R$ over $k = \bar{k}$ corresponds to a classical point.

**Krull dimension.** $\dim \mathrm{Spec}(R) = \dim R$, the Krull dimension of the ring (length of longest chain of primes). For $\mathbb{Z}$, dimension 1. For $\mathbb{C}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$, dimension $n$.

**Local cohomology.** For $I \subseteq R$ an ideal, the local-cohomology functors $H^i_I(M)$ on $R$-modules correspond geometrically to cohomology supported on $V(I)$. This was developed by Grothendieck and is essential in commutative algebra and algebraic geometry.

**Étale topology.** A finer Grothendieck topology on $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$, where covers consist of étale (locally finite, unramified, smooth) morphisms. Étale cohomology of a scheme generalises sheaf cohomology and provides the deepest invariants in arithmetic geometry — the foundation of Grothendieck's proof program for the Weil conjectures.

**Formal schemes and adic spaces.** Beyond Spec are *formal schemes* (for completing along closed subschemes, used in deformation theory) and *adic spaces* (for $p$-adic geometry, Huber spaces, Berkovich spaces — Scholze's perfectoid spaces).

**Spec for non-commutative rings.** The non-commutative analogue is the theory of *spectra of non-commutative rings* (the Pierce spectrum, Goodearl-Warfield prime spectrum), with non-commutative algebraic geometry following Artin, Stafford, Lurie, and others.

**Synthesis.** This construction generalises the pattern fixed in [04.01.01] (sheaf), with the symmetric data replaced by its skew or twisted analogue. Read in the opposite direction, the construction is dual to the metric story: complements and orthogonality are taken with respect to the bilinear datum of this unit, not a metric, and the resulting category of subobjects is the one the rest of the strand classifies. The central insight is that this datum identifies algebra with geometry: functions become vector fields, subspaces become quotients, and invariants become cohomology classes — and that identification is the engine driving every theorem downstream.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The classical (pre-Grothendieck) view of algebraic geometry was *variety-theoretic*: an algebraic variety over an algebraically closed field is the zero set of a system of polynomial equations in $\mathbb{A}^n_k$ or $\mathbb{P}^n_k$. The associated ring is the *coordinate ring* — a finitely-generated reduced $k$-algebra. This worked beautifully for many problems but had structural limitations:

1. *Non-reduced phenomena were invisible.* Tangent directions, infinitesimal deformations, intersection multiplicities all required ad hoc workarounds.
2. *Non-classical fields broke the dictionary.* Over $\mathbb{Q}$ or finite fields, "varieties" were Galois-orbits of points and didn't fit cleanly.
3. *Number theory lived elsewhere.* $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$ wasn't naturally a "variety," yet number theory has obvious geometric content.

The breakthrough was Grothendieck's *Éléments de Géométrie Algébrique* (EGA, 1960–67) building on the *Cartan seminars* (1954–64) and Serre's *Faisceaux Algébriques Cohérents* (1955). The defining insight: **don't define varieties by zero sets of polynomials — define them by their rings of functions, allow arbitrary commutative rings (including those with nilpotents and non-zero characteristic), and recover geometry through $\mathrm{Spec}$.**

This Grothendieck revolution unified:

- *Classical algebraic geometry* (varieties over $\mathbb{C}$): special case of schemes over $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C})$.
- *Number theory*: $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$, $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathcal{O}_K)$ are arithmetic schemes.
- *Deformation theory*: nilpotents encode infinitesimal data.
- *Étale cohomology*: Grothendieck topologies generalise the Zariski one to capture arithmetic.

By the early 1970s, this framework had been used to prove the *Weil conjectures* (Deligne 1974, completing Grothendieck's program) — one of the great achievements of 20th-century mathematics. The conceptual framework of *schemes built from affine schemes* now dominates algebraic geometry, arithmetic geometry, and large parts of number theory and mathematical physics.

The philosophical core: **geometry is the language of commutative rings, in disguise.** The affine scheme is the most direct embodiment of this principle. Every more elaborate scheme is glued from affine pieces, just as every smooth manifold is glued from open subsets of $\mathbb{R}^n$.
