## Intuition [Beginner]

A *scheme* is a geometric object built from commutative rings. The slogan: a commutative ring is the ring of functions on some "geometric space," and that space is the *scheme* associated to the ring.

The basic example: the ring $\mathbb{C}[x, y]$ of polynomials in two variables. Geometrically, this ring is the "ring of functions on the plane $\mathbb{C}^2$." The corresponding scheme $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C}[x, y])$ has points corresponding to ideals of the polynomial ring — including the maximal ideals (corresponding to actual points of the plane) and the prime ideals (corresponding to subvarieties like curves).

Schemes generalize the algebraic varieties of classical algebraic geometry by allowing arbitrary commutative rings — including rings with nilpotents (e.g., $k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2)$ for "infinitesimal" calculations) and rings of integers (for arithmetic geometry). They are the universal language of modern algebraic geometry, enabling Grothendieck's revolutionary unification of algebra and geometry.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The simplest scheme: $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$, the spectrum of the integers. Its points are the prime ideals of $\mathbb{Z}$:

- The *maximal ideals* $(p) = p\mathbb{Z}$ for each prime number $p$ — these correspond to "ordinary" closed points.
- The *zero ideal* $(0)$ — the *generic point*, geometrically the "whole space."

So $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$ has one point per prime number, plus a "generic point." The closed points are like a discrete set indexed by primes; the generic point is dense (its closure is the whole space).

The structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}$ assigns to each open set the appropriate ring of "rational" functions — for example, $\mathcal{O}(\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})) = \mathbb{Z}$, the ring of integers itself.

This is the prototype of all schemes. More generally, $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}[x, y, \ldots])$ adds more polynomial dimensions; $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}[x]/(f(x)))$ corresponds to the "scheme of $f = 0$." Every commutative ring has its own scheme.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $R$ be a commutative ring (with unit) [03.01.02]. The **spectrum** of $R$, denoted $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$, is the topological space whose points are the prime ideals of $R$, with the **Zariski topology**: closed sets are $V(I) := \{\mathfrak{p} \in \mathrm{Spec}(R) : I \subseteq \mathfrak{p}\}$ for ideals $I \subseteq R$.

The **structure sheaf** $\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{Spec}(R)}$ on $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ assigns to a basic open $D(f) := \{\mathfrak{p} : f \notin \mathfrak{p}\}$ (for $f \in R$) the localisation $R_f$. For a general open $U$, $\mathcal{O}(U)$ is determined by the sheaf condition. The pair $(\mathrm{Spec}(R), \mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{Spec}(R)})$ is an **affine scheme**.

A **scheme** is a *locally ringed space* $(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$ — a topological space with a sheaf of commutative rings whose stalks $\mathcal{O}_{X, x}$ are local rings — such that $X$ is locally isomorphic to an affine scheme. Concretely: every $x \in X$ has an open neighbourhood $U$ with $(U, \mathcal{O}_X|_U) \cong (\mathrm{Spec}(R), \mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{Spec}(R)})$ for some commutative ring $R$.

A **morphism of schemes** $f : X \to Y$ is a morphism of locally ringed spaces — a continuous map of underlying spaces together with a compatible map of sheaves of rings $\mathcal{O}_Y \to f_\ast \mathcal{O}_X$ such that the induced map on stalks is a *local* ring homomorphism (preserves the maximal ideal).

Key constructions:

- **Affine scheme**: $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ for a ring $R$.
- **Projective scheme**: $\mathrm{Proj}(R)$ for a graded ring $R$. Examples: $\mathbb{P}^n_k = \mathrm{Proj}(k[x_0, \ldots, x_n])$.
- **Affine line over a ring** $R$: $\mathbb{A}^1_R := \mathrm{Spec}(R[t])$.
- **Fibre product**: schemes admit pullbacks $X \times_Z Y$ for any morphisms $X \to Z \leftarrow Y$.

A scheme over a field $k$ (or more generally a base scheme $S$) is a scheme $X$ together with a structural morphism $X \to \mathrm{Spec}(k)$. For algebraically closed $k$, classical algebraic varieties are *reduced, irreducible, separated, finite-type* schemes over $k$ — a substantial restriction of the general scheme notion.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Anti-equivalence of affine schemes and commutative rings).** *The functor $\mathrm{Spec} : \mathbf{CRing}^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{AffSch}$ from commutative rings (opposite category) to affine schemes is an equivalence of categories. Its inverse is the global-sections functor $\Gamma : \mathbf{AffSch} \to \mathbf{CRing}^{\mathrm{op}}$, which sends $(\mathrm{Spec}(R), \mathcal{O}) \mapsto \mathcal{O}(\mathrm{Spec}(R)) = R$.*

**Proof sketch.** Two directions:

*Affine schemes are determined by their global sections.* For an affine scheme $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$, $\Gamma(\mathrm{Spec}(R), \mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{Spec}(R)}) = R$ by construction (the structure sheaf is glued from localisations, and the global sections are the original ring). So $\Gamma \circ \mathrm{Spec} = \mathrm{id}_{\mathbf{CRing}^{\mathrm{op}}}$.

*Morphisms of affine schemes correspond to ring homomorphisms.* A morphism $\mathrm{Spec}(R) \to \mathrm{Spec}(S)$ as locally ringed spaces gives a ring map $S \to R$ (apply $\Gamma$). Conversely, a ring map $\varphi : S \to R$ induces a continuous map $\mathrm{Spec}(R) \to \mathrm{Spec}(S)$ by $\mathfrak{p} \mapsto \varphi^{-1}(\mathfrak{p})$, with the structure-sheaf map coming from $\varphi$. Verifying that these are inverse uses the local-ring-map condition (which lifts to the maximal-ideal-preserving condition on the corresponding prime ideal).

The full equivalence follows by combining objects and morphisms; the locally-ringed-space framework ensures the structure-sheaf data is preserved correctly. $\square$

This anti-equivalence is the foundation of Grothendieck's algebraic geometry: every commutative ring becomes a geometric object, and every algebraic operation has a geometric counterpart. It is what makes "schemes" the right generalisation of varieties.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [04.03.01] (sheaf cohomology), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [04.04.01] (riemann-roch theorem for curves). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

How many points does $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z})$ have?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Find the prime ideals of $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The prime ideals of $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ correspond to the maximal ideals (since $\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z}$ is finite, all primes are maximal) — there are 2 of them: $(2) = \{0, 2, 4\}$ and $(3) = \{0, 3\}$. So $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}/6\mathbb{Z})$ has **2 points**, both closed (no generic point because the ring is not an integral domain).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (medium, proof).**

Show that the Zariski topology on $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is well-defined: closed sets $V(I)$ for ideals $I$ form a topology.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Verify $V(0) = \mathrm{Spec}(R)$, $V(R) = \emptyset$, $V(\sum I_\alpha) = \bigcap V(I_\alpha)$, $V(IJ) = V(I) \cup V(J)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$V(0) = \{\mathfrak{p} : 0 \in \mathfrak{p}\} = \mathrm{Spec}(R)$ (every ideal contains 0). $V(R) = \{\mathfrak{p} : R \subseteq \mathfrak{p}\} = \emptyset$ (no proper ideal is the whole ring).

For arbitrary intersections: $\bigcap V(I_\alpha) = \{\mathfrak{p} : I_\alpha \subseteq \mathfrak{p} \forall \alpha\} = V(\sum I_\alpha)$ (the ideal generated by all $I_\alpha$).

For finite unions: $V(I) \cup V(J) = \{\mathfrak{p} : I \subseteq \mathfrak{p} \text{ or } J \subseteq \mathfrak{p}\}$. We claim this equals $V(IJ) = \{\mathfrak{p} : IJ \subseteq \mathfrak{p}\}$. If $I \subseteq \mathfrak{p}$ then $IJ \subseteq \mathfrak{p}$; same for $J$. So $V(I) \cup V(J) \subseteq V(IJ)$. Conversely, if $IJ \subseteq \mathfrak{p}$ but $I \not\subseteq \mathfrak{p}$, pick $i \in I \setminus \mathfrak{p}$; for every $j \in J$, $ij \in IJ \subseteq \mathfrak{p}$, so by primality of $\mathfrak{p}$, $j \in \mathfrak{p}$. Hence $J \subseteq \mathfrak{p}$. So $V(IJ) \subseteq V(I) \cup V(J)$.

Thus the closed sets satisfy the topology axioms.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}[x])$ is two-dimensional in the sense that it has chains of prime ideals of length 2.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Find a chain $(0) \subset \mathfrak{p}_1 \subset \mathfrak{p}_2$ with strict inclusions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$(0) \subset (p) \subset (p, x)$ for any prime $p \in \mathbb{Z}$. Each is prime (the quotients are $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$, $\mathbb{F}_p$ respectively, all integral domains). The chain has length 2, demonstrating $\dim \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z}[x]) \geq 2$. In fact $\dim = 2$ (Hilbert's basis theorem + Krull dimension theory).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, conceptual).**

What is the difference between a *reduced* and *non-reduced* scheme?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reduced means the structure sheaf has no nilpotent local sections.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A scheme $X$ is **reduced** if the structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X$ has no nilpotent local sections — equivalently, every stalk $\mathcal{O}_{X, x}$ is a reduced ring (no $r \neq 0$ with $r^n = 0$).

Non-reduced schemes have "infinitesimal thickenings": $\mathrm{Spec}(k[\epsilon]/(\epsilon^2))$ is the *dual numbers* scheme, a single point with a 1-dimensional tangent direction realised algebraically. It is not reduced because $\epsilon \neq 0$ but $\epsilon^2 = 0$.

Non-reduced schemes are essential for moduli theory and deformation theory: tangent vectors at a point of a moduli space correspond to morphisms from $\mathrm{Spec}(k[\epsilon]/\epsilon^2)$. In classical algebraic geometry (varieties over algebraically closed fields), schemes are usually assumed reduced.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

State the *functor of points* perspective: every scheme is determined by what it represents.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For a scheme $X$ and a ring $R$, $X(R) := \mathrm{Hom}(\mathrm{Spec}(R), X)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Each scheme $X$ defines a functor $X(-) : \mathbf{CRing} \to \mathbf{Set}$ by

$$
X(R) := \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Sch}}(\mathrm{Spec}(R), X).
$$

This functor encodes the scheme up to isomorphism (Yoneda lemma applied to the category of schemes). For example, the affine line $\mathbb{A}^1_S$ has $\mathbb{A}^1_S(R) = \mathrm{Hom}_S(\mathrm{Spec}(R), \mathbb{A}^1) = R$ — the "points" of $\mathbb{A}^1$ valued in $R$ are just elements of $R$.

The functor-of-points perspective recasts schemes as *representable functors* on commutative rings, making algebraic geometry into a flavour of functorial commutative algebra. It is the foundation of *moduli problems* (representable by moduli schemes) and of *stacks* (functors that aren't quite representable but represented by groupoids).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that the projective space $\mathbb{P}^n_k = \mathrm{Proj}(k[x_0, \ldots, x_n])$ is covered by $n+1$ affine opens, each isomorphic to $\mathbb{A}^n_k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The basic open $D_+(x_i)$ corresponds to "$x_i \neq 0$" — set $x_i = 1$ to get an affine chart.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The basic open $D_+(x_i) \subseteq \mathbb{P}^n_k$ corresponds to homogeneous prime ideals of $k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]$ not containing $x_i$. Localising at $x_i$ and taking the degree-0 part: $D_+(x_i) \cong \mathrm{Spec}(k[x_0/x_i, \ldots, \widehat{x_i/x_i}, \ldots, x_n/x_i]) = \mathbb{A}^n_k$ — the polynomial ring in $n$ variables (the ratios $x_j/x_i$ for $j \neq i$). The $n+1$ opens $D_+(x_0), \ldots, D_+(x_n)$ cover $\mathbb{P}^n_k$ because every homogeneous prime not containing the irrelevant ideal $(x_0, \ldots, x_n)$ misses at least one $x_i$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

State Grothendieck's "scheme = locally ringed space locally isomorphic to $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$" definition and explain why the local-ringed-space condition is essential.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Without local rings, morphisms wouldn't preserve the relevant geometric structure.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A *locally ringed space* is a topological space $X$ with a sheaf of rings $\mathcal{O}_X$ such that every stalk $\mathcal{O}_{X, x}$ is a local ring (has a unique maximal ideal). A *morphism* of locally ringed spaces $f : (X, \mathcal{O}_X) \to (Y, \mathcal{O}_Y)$ is a continuous map plus a sheaf morphism $\mathcal{O}_Y \to f_\ast \mathcal{O}_X$ such that the induced stalk map at every point sends maximal ideals to maximal ideals.

A scheme is a locally ringed space locally isomorphic to an affine scheme.

The local-ringed-space condition is essential because:

- Stalks of structure sheaves at points are *local rings* — the unique maximal ideal corresponds to "functions vanishing at that point."
- Morphisms must preserve this structure: a function vanishing at $f(x)$ in $Y$ should pull back to a function vanishing at $x$ in $X$.

Without the locality condition, morphisms would forget which functions vanish where — the geometric content of "evaluating a function at a point" would be lost.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Functor of points.** Detailed in Exercise 5. Schemes form a full subcategory of *functors* $\mathbf{CRing} \to \mathbf{Set}$ via the representability $X \mapsto X(-)$. Yoneda's lemma identifies scheme morphisms with natural transformations of functors.

**Properties of schemes / morphisms.** Hartshorne and Vakil classify properties: *separated* (diagonal closed), *proper* (universally closed), *flat*, *smooth*, *étale*, *finite type*, *finite presentation*, etc. These properties characterise the morphisms one cares about; they are the algebraic-geometric analogues of "compact," "surjective," "differentiable," etc., from differential topology.

**Quasi-coherent sheaves on schemes.** A sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on a scheme $X$ is **quasi-coherent** if locally it is given by an $\mathcal{O}_X(U)$-module via the localisation pattern. Quasi-coherent sheaves on $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ correspond to $R$-modules (the *Serre-Swan theorem* for affine schemes). They are the natural "vector-bundle-like" objects.

**Coherent sheaves.** Quasi-coherent + finite-type. On Noetherian schemes, coherent sheaves are well-behaved (kernels, cokernels, images are coherent) and have computable cohomology.

**Cohomological properties.** Affine schemes have vanishing higher cohomology of quasi-coherent sheaves (Serre's vanishing theorem). Projective schemes have computable coherent cohomology via the Serre formula. These properties drive Riemann-Roch and Hodge-theoretic computations [04.04.01].

**Connection to other geometries.** Smooth schemes over $\mathbb{C}$ are complex manifolds; schemes over $\mathbb{R}$ extend to the real-algebraic setting. Arithmetic schemes (over $\mathbb{Z}$) connect algebraic geometry with number theory. Stacks generalise schemes to allow "geometric objects with automorphisms."

**Synthesis.** This construction generalises the pattern fixed in [04.01.01] (sheaf), with the symmetric data replaced by its skew or twisted analogue. Read in the opposite direction, the construction is dual to the metric story: complements and orthogonality are taken with respect to the bilinear datum of this unit, not a metric, and the resulting category of subobjects is the one the rest of the strand classifies. The central insight is that this datum identifies algebra with geometry: functions become vector fields, subspaces become quotients, and invariants become cohomology classes — and that identification is the engine driving every theorem downstream.

## Full proof set [Master]

**$\mathrm{Spec}(R) = \mathrm{CRing}^{\mathrm{op}}$.** Proved in §"Key theorem".

**Zariski topology axioms.** Proved in Exercise 2.

**Krull dimension and chains.** Sketched in Exercise 3.

**Projective space affine cover.** Proved in Exercise 6.

**Local ring structure of stalks.** For an affine scheme $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ at point $\mathfrak{p}$, the stalk $\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{Spec}(R), \mathfrak{p}}$ is the localisation $R_{\mathfrak{p}}$ — a local ring with maximal ideal $\mathfrak{p} R_\mathfrak{p}$. The "local ring at $\mathfrak{p}$" captures the local algebraic information at that point.

**Quasi-coherent ↔ module.** On $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$, the category of quasi-coherent sheaves is equivalent to the category of $R$-modules: $\mathcal{F} \mapsto \mathcal{F}(\mathrm{Spec}(R))$ and conversely $M \mapsto \widetilde{M}$ via the localisation construction.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The notion of a scheme was developed by Alexander Grothendieck and Jean Dieudonné in the *Éléments de géométrie algébrique* (EGA, 1960–1967). Grothendieck's revolutionary idea: replace the classical varieties (subsets of $\mathbb{A}^n$ defined by polynomial equations) with the more flexible notion of locally ringed spaces locally isomorphic to spectra of rings. This generalisation includes nilpotents, arithmetic rings, and arbitrary base schemes, dramatically expanding the scope of algebraic geometry.

Earlier algebraic geometers (Weil, Zariski, Serre) had developed the theory of varieties in a functorial language but stopped short of the full ring-theoretic generalisation. Grothendieck's EGA, together with the *Séminaire de géométrie algébrique* (SGA) volumes, set the modern technology in place: schemes, morphisms, properties, sheaves, cohomology, étale cohomology.

The scheme-theoretic perspective transformed not just algebraic geometry but adjacent fields: arithmetic geometry (Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem; the Langlands programme), derived algebraic geometry (Lurie, Toën-Vezzosi), and category theory (sheaves on a Grothendieck site). Schemes are the universal language for "spaces studied via their rings of functions."
