## Intuition [Beginner]

A *projective scheme* is the global, scheme-theoretic generalisation of projective space. Where the affine scheme $\mathrm{Spec}(R)$ is built from a single commutative ring, the projective scheme $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$ is built from a *graded* commutative ring, and its points are *homogeneous* prime ideals — exactly the right structure to model projective varieties.

Why graded rings? Because projective space is built from polynomials with the homogeneity property: a polynomial $f(x_0, \ldots, x_n)$ defines a hypersurface in $\mathbb{P}^n$ only if it is homogeneous (the same total degree in every term). The vanishing locus of a non-homogeneous polynomial isn't well-defined in projective space because the polynomial doesn't transform consistently under rescaling.

The Proj construction takes any graded ring $S = \bigoplus_d S_d$ and produces a scheme whose points correspond to homogeneous primes (other than the irrelevant ideal $S_+ = \bigoplus_{d > 0} S_d$). This way every quotient of a polynomial ring by a homogeneous ideal — which is exactly the setting of classical projective varieties — becomes a scheme. The output is the modern algebraic-geometric view of projective varieties.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The simplest example: $\mathbb{P}^n_k = \mathrm{Proj}(k[x_0, \ldots, x_n])$, the standard projective space we already know. The graded ring $k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]$ has its grading by total degree.

A more interesting example: a *plane projective curve*. Take a homogeneous polynomial $f(x_0, x_1, x_2) \in k[x_0, x_1, x_2]$ of degree $d$. The quotient ring $S = k[x_0, x_1, x_2]/(f)$ inherits a grading. The projective scheme

$$
X = \mathrm{Proj}(k[x_0, x_1, x_2]/(f)) \subset \mathbb{P}^2_k
$$

is a *plane projective curve of degree $d$*. For $d = 2$ and $f = x_0 x_2 - x_1^2$, this is a smooth conic, isomorphic to $\mathbb{P}^1$. For $d = 3$ and $f = x_0 x_2^2 - x_1^3 - x_0^2 x_1$ (or similar), this is an *elliptic curve*.

Every smooth projective curve over $\mathbb{C}$ embeds as a closed subscheme of $\mathbb{P}^N$ for some $N$ — projective schemes are the fundamental geometric objects of classical algebraic geometry.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $S = \bigoplus_{d \geq 0} S_d$ be a *graded commutative ring* (with $S_d \cdot S_e \subseteq S_{d + e}$, $1 \in S_0$). The **projective scheme** $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$ is constructed as follows.

**Underlying set.** Define the *irrelevant ideal*

$$
S_+ := \bigoplus_{d > 0} S_d.
$$

Then

$$
\mathrm{Proj}(S) := \{\mathfrak{p} \subset S : \mathfrak{p} \text{ is a homogeneous prime ideal with } S_+ \not\subseteq \mathfrak{p}\}.
$$

A *homogeneous* ideal is one generated by homogeneous elements; equivalently, $\mathfrak{p} = \bigoplus_d (\mathfrak{p} \cap S_d)$.

**Topology.** The Zariski topology has closed subsets

$$
V(I) := \{\mathfrak{p} \in \mathrm{Proj}(S) : I \subseteq \mathfrak{p}\}
$$

for *homogeneous* ideals $I \subseteq S$. The *distinguished opens* are $D(f) := \{\mathfrak{p} : f \notin \mathfrak{p}\}$ for $f \in S_d$ a *homogeneous element of positive degree*.

**Affine cover.** The key fact: each $D(f)$ is *naturally affine*. For $f \in S_d$ (with $d > 0$),

$$
D(f) \cong \mathrm{Spec}\bigl((S[f^{-1}])_0\bigr),
$$

the spectrum of the *degree-zero part* of the localisation at $f$. As $f$ ranges over homogeneous elements of positive degree, the $D(f)$ cover $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$, giving an affine cover.

**Structure sheaf.** The structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{Proj}(S)}$ on the affine cover is given by

$$
\mathcal{O}_{\mathrm{Proj}(S)}(D(f)) = (S[f^{-1}])_0.
$$

This makes $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$ a locally ringed space; the affine charts identify it with affine schemes locally, hence with a scheme globally.

**Examples.**

1. $\mathrm{Proj}(k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]) = \mathbb{P}^n_k$, the standard projective space.
2. $\mathrm{Proj}(k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]/I)$ for a homogeneous ideal $I$: a *closed subscheme* of $\mathbb{P}^n_k$, the *projective variety* defined by $I$.
3. $\mathrm{Proj}(\bigoplus_{d \geq 0} k[x_0, x_1, x_2]_{ad}) = \mathbb{P}^2_k$ via the *Veronese embedding* of degree $a$.
4. $\mathrm{Proj}(k[x_0, x_1, x_2, x_3]/(x_0 x_3 - x_1 x_2))$: a smooth quadric surface in $\mathbb{P}^3$, isomorphic to $\mathbb{P}^1 \times \mathbb{P}^1$.

**Twisting sheaves.** For each $d \in \mathbb{Z}$, the *twisting sheaf* $\mathcal{O}(d)$ on $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$ is the sheaf associated to the graded $S$-module $S(d)$ (degree shift by $d$). Global sections:

$$
H^0(\mathrm{Proj}(S); \mathcal{O}(d)) = S_d \quad \text{(degree-}d\text{ component, when $S$ is generated by $S_1$ over $S_0$)}.
$$

**Properties.**

- $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$ is *quasi-compact* and Noetherian if $S$ is finitely generated over $S_0$.
- *Closed subschemes of $\mathbb{P}^n_k$* correspond to homogeneous ideals of $k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]$.
- A morphism of graded rings (degree-preserving) $S \to T$ induces a morphism of schemes $\mathrm{Proj}(T) \dashrightarrow \mathrm{Proj}(S)$ — defined where the morphism doesn't kill the irrelevant ideal.

**Veronese and Segre embeddings.** Important explicit projective embeddings:

- *Veronese $v_d : \mathbb{P}^n \to \mathbb{P}^N$* with $N = \binom{n + d}{n} - 1$: $[x_0 : \cdots : x_n] \mapsto [\text{all degree-}d \text{ monomials}]$. The image is a smooth projective variety.
- *Segre $\sigma : \mathbb{P}^m \times \mathbb{P}^n \to \mathbb{P}^{(m+1)(n+1) - 1}$*: $([x] , [y]) \mapsto [x_i y_j]$. Realises products of projective spaces inside larger projective spaces.

These embeddings are foundational: they let you reduce many problems about projective varieties to study of subvarieties of $\mathbb{P}^N$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (every projective scheme is a closed subscheme of some $\mathbb{P}^n_k$).** *Let $X = \mathrm{Proj}(S)$ for $S$ a finitely-generated graded $k$-algebra with $S_0 = k$ and $S$ generated by $S_1$ over $S_0$. Then $X$ is isomorphic to a closed subscheme of $\mathbb{P}^N_k$, where $N + 1 = \dim_k S_1$.*

**Proof sketch.** Pick a basis $f_0, f_1, \ldots, f_N$ of the degree-1 component $S_1$ as a $k$-vector space. The $f_i$ generate $S$ as a graded $k$-algebra (by hypothesis). Define a graded $k$-algebra homomorphism

$$
\varphi : k[x_0, \ldots, x_N] \to S, \qquad x_i \mapsto f_i.
$$

This is degree-preserving (each $x_i$ has degree 1 and goes to $f_i \in S_1$). Surjectivity follows because $f_i$'s generate $S$. The kernel $I = \ker(\varphi) \subset k[x_0, \ldots, x_N]$ is a *homogeneous ideal*, giving an isomorphism

$$
S \cong k[x_0, \ldots, x_N]/I.
$$

Applying Proj:

$$
X = \mathrm{Proj}(S) \cong \mathrm{Proj}(k[x_0, \ldots, x_N]/I) = V_+(I) \subset \mathbb{P}^N_k,
$$

where $V_+(I) \subset \mathbb{P}^N_k$ is the closed subscheme cut out by the homogeneous ideal $I$. $\square$

This theorem is foundational: it identifies projective schemes (over a field) with closed subschemes of projective space. Combined with the *Hilbert basis theorem* (every ideal is finitely generated), it shows that projective schemes are exactly the schemes of the form $V_+(f_1, \ldots, f_r) \subset \mathbb{P}^N$ for finitely many homogeneous polynomials. Every such object is a *projective variety* in the classical sense.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [04.05.05] (ample and very ample line bundle), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [04.06.02] (coherent sheaf). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

What is $\mathrm{Proj}(k[x, y]/(xy))$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The polynomial $xy$ vanishes when either coordinate vanishes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The graded ring $k[x, y]/(xy)$ has homogeneous primes $\mathfrak{p}$ that contain $xy$, hence either $x \in \mathfrak{p}$ or $y \in \mathfrak{p}$.

The locus $V_+(x) = \{[x : y] : x = 0\} = \{[0 : 1]\}$ is a single point in $\mathbb{P}^1$. Similarly $V_+(y) = \{[1 : 0]\}$. So

$$
\mathrm{Proj}(k[x, y]/(xy)) = \{[1:0], [0:1]\} \subset \mathbb{P}^1_k.
$$

This is a "two-point scheme": the two coordinate points of $\mathbb{P}^1_k$. Reduced, since $xy$ is a radical ideal in this case.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, conceptual).**

Why must we exclude the irrelevant ideal $S_+$ from $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Geometrically, what does $S_+$ correspond to in projective space?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The irrelevant ideal $S_+$ corresponds to "the origin" in the affine cone associated to $S$. When we form projective space $\mathbb{P}^n = (\mathbb{A}^{n+1} \setminus \{0\}) / k^\times$, the origin is *removed* before quotienting by the scaling action.

Concretely: the irrelevant ideal of $k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]$ is $(x_0, \ldots, x_n)$, the ideal of polynomials vanishing at the origin. This origin is precisely the point removed in passing from $\mathbb{A}^{n+1}$ to $\mathbb{P}^n$. Excluding it from Proj makes the construction match projective space.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that $\mathrm{Proj}(k[x, y, z]/(xz - y^2))$ is isomorphic to $\mathbb{P}^1_k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Veronese map $v_2 : \mathbb{P}^1 \to \mathbb{P}^2$ has image the smooth conic.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Veronese map $v_2 : \mathbb{P}^1_k \to \mathbb{P}^2_k$ sends $[s : t] \mapsto [s^2 : st : t^2]$. The image is the conic $\{[x : y : z] : xz - y^2 = 0\}$, since $s^2 \cdot t^2 - (st)^2 = 0$. The map is injective (a quadratic Veronese is an embedding) and its image is exactly $V_+(xz - y^2)$.

So $v_2 : \mathbb{P}^1_k \xrightarrow{\sim} V_+(xz - y^2) = \mathrm{Proj}(k[x, y, z]/(xz - y^2))$ is an isomorphism. $\square$

This shows: smooth conics in $\mathbb{P}^2$ are isomorphic to $\mathbb{P}^1$. More generally, any smooth rational curve embedded in projective space is a Veronese-style image of $\mathbb{P}^1$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

Compute the Hilbert polynomial of the smooth projective conic $C = V_+(xz - y^2) \subset \mathbb{P}^2_k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the short exact sequence $0 \to \mathcal{O}(t-2) \to \mathcal{O}(t) \to \mathcal{O}_C(t) \to 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The conic is cut out by a single polynomial $xz - y^2$ of degree 2 in $\mathbb{P}^2$. From the short exact sequence:

$$
0 \to \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}(t - 2) \xrightarrow{\cdot (xz - y^2)} \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}(t) \to \mathcal{O}_C(t) \to 0.
$$

Taking Euler characteristics:

$$
\chi(\mathcal{O}_C(t)) = \chi(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}(t)) - \chi(\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{P}^2}(t - 2)) = \binom{t + 2}{2} - \binom{t}{2}.
$$

Compute: $\binom{t+2}{2} - \binom{t}{2} = \frac{(t+2)(t+1) - t(t-1)}{2} = \frac{t^2 + 3t + 2 - t^2 + t}{2} = \frac{4t + 2}{2} = 2t + 1$.

So $p_C(t) = 2t + 1$, of degree 1 (so $\dim C = 1$, a curve). Leading coefficient $2 = \deg C$ in $\mathbb{P}^2$. Constant term $1 = 1 - g$, so genus $g = 0$ (a rational curve, as expected for a smooth conic).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why does the Proj construction work over $\mathbb{Z}$ and not just over a field?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Proj construction makes no use of field-specific properties.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Proj construction takes any graded commutative ring $S$ and produces a scheme $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$. No field hypothesis is required. So $\mathrm{Proj}(\mathbb{Z}[x_0, \ldots, x_n]) = \mathbb{P}^n_{\mathbb{Z}}$ is a perfectly good projective scheme over $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$.

This generality matters for *arithmetic geometry*: schemes over $\mathbb{Z}$ are the natural objects of number theory. A projective scheme over $\mathbb{Z}$ has, for each prime $p$, a "reduction mod $p$" giving a projective scheme over $\mathbb{F}_p$ — this is the foundation of the *Weil conjectures*, the $p$-adic Hodge theory, and modern arithmetic geometry.

The projective scheme over $\mathbb{Z}$ is a richer object than over any single field: it bundles together all the characteristic-$p$ behaviours simultaneously.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

State Serre's theorem on cohomology of coherent sheaves on a projective scheme.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Serre's theorem says high twists kill higher cohomology.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Serre's theorem.** Let $X$ be a projective scheme over a Noetherian ring $A$, and $\mathcal{F}$ a coherent sheaf on $X$. Then there exists $d_0 = d_0(\mathcal{F})$ such that for $d \geq d_0$:

(1) $\mathcal{F}(d)$ is generated by global sections.
(2) $H^i(X; \mathcal{F}(d)) = 0$ for $i \geq 1$ and $d \geq d_0$.

The first part says that twisting by sufficiently positive line bundles makes any coherent sheaf "manageable" — its global sections capture its full structure. The second part is the cornerstone of *Castelnuovo-Mumford regularity* and the *Hilbert polynomial* theory: high cohomology of twists vanishes, making the Euler characteristic equal to $h^0$.

This theorem is the foundational *cohomological vanishing* on projective schemes, complementing Serre's vanishing on affine schemes.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Explain how Proj generalises classical algebraic geometry's "projective varieties" to schemes over arbitrary base rings.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare classical zero-set definitions to the Proj definition.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Classical*: A projective variety $X \subset \mathbb{P}^n_{\bar k}$ over an algebraically closed field $\bar k$ is the zero set of a finite collection of homogeneous polynomials $f_1, \ldots, f_r \in \bar k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]$. Points of $X$ are $\bar k$-points satisfying these polynomial equations.

*Proj*: $X = \mathrm{Proj}(\bar k[x_0, \ldots, x_n]/(f_1, \ldots, f_r))$ is a scheme. Its $\bar k$-points are the same as the classical points, but it carries additional structure:

- *Generic points* of subvarieties.
- *Non-reduced structure* if $(f_1, \ldots, f_r)$ has nilpotents in the quotient.
- *Functorial behaviour* over arbitrary base rings (not just $\bar k$).
- *Morphisms* defined by graded ring maps, not just polynomial maps in coordinates.
- *Cohomological invariants* (Picard group, Hodge numbers, etc.) that depend on the scheme structure.

Over $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$, the same Proj construction gives an *arithmetic* projective scheme that fibres over arithmetic primes, with a fibre at $p$ being a projective scheme over $\mathbb{F}_p$. This is the universal home for projective varieties over all fields and characteristics simultaneously.

The Proj construction is the modern conceptual foundation of projective algebraic geometry: every classical projective variety is a Proj of a quotient of a polynomial ring, and the Proj construction extends to arbitrary graded rings, opening up the world of arithmetic, formal, and infinitesimal projective geometry.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Functorial Proj.** The Proj construction is functorial for *graded ring homomorphisms in negative degrees*: a graded ring map $S \to T$ that doesn't kill the irrelevant ideal induces a morphism $\mathrm{Proj}(T) \to \mathrm{Proj}(S)$. Properly understood as a *partial functor*.

**Proj of a graded module.** For a graded $S$-module $M$, define the *coherent sheaf* $\widetilde{M}$ on $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$ by $\widetilde{M}(D(f)) = (M[f^{-1}])_0$. This gives the equivalence

$$
\widetilde{(\cdot)} : \mathbf{grMod}(S) / (\text{torsion}) \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathbf{Coh}(\mathrm{Proj}(S))
$$

between coherent sheaves on $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$ and graded modules modulo torsion (Serre's theorem).

**Castelnuovo-Mumford regularity.** A coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on $\mathbb{P}^n$ has *regularity* $\mathrm{reg}(\mathcal{F})$, the smallest $m$ such that $H^i(\mathbb{P}^n; \mathcal{F}(m - i)) = 0$ for all $i \geq 1$. Bounds on regularity give effective control on global sections of twists.

**Hilbert scheme.** The moduli space $\mathrm{Hilb}^P(\mathbb{P}^n)$ of closed subschemes of $\mathbb{P}^n$ with given Hilbert polynomial $P$ is itself a projective scheme. Hilbert schemes are foundational objects in moduli theory, their geometry studied extensively by Mumford, Hartshorne, Grothendieck.

**Severi-Brauer varieties.** Over non-algebraically-closed fields, "twisted forms" of $\mathbb{P}^n$ exist — these are the Severi-Brauer varieties, parametrised by the Brauer group of the base field.

**Toric projective schemes.** The toric varieties associated to *projective polytopes* are projective. The Cox ring construction gives a uniform Proj-like description.

**Kontsevich moduli of stable maps.** Refining Hilbert schemes, $\overline{\mathcal{M}_{g, n}}(\mathbb{P}^N, \beta)$ — the moduli of stable maps from genus-$g$, $n$-marked curves to $\mathbb{P}^N$ representing class $\beta$ — is a projective scheme central to *Gromov-Witten theory* and *mirror symmetry*.

**$\mathrm{Proj}$ vs $\mathrm{Spec}$.** A graded ring $S$ has both: $\mathrm{Spec}(S)$ is the affine cone (the zero locus of $S_+$ removed), and $\mathrm{Proj}(S)$ is its projectivisation. The relation: $\mathrm{Spec}(S) \setminus V(S_+) \to \mathrm{Proj}(S)$ is a $\mathbb{G}_m$-torsor.

**Synthesis.** A projective scheme is one that embeds as a closed subscheme of $\mathbb{P}^n_S$ for some base $S$, and this compactness property ensures finite-dimensional cohomology, the existence of Hilbert polynomials, and the validity of Serre duality and the Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch formula. The $\mathrm{Proj}$ construction builds projective schemes from graded rings, generalising the passage from homogeneous polynomials to projective varieties while accommodating non-reduced and arithmetic structures. Projective schemes are the setting for intersection theory (Chow rings), the classification of varieties via projective embeddings and very ample line bundles, and the geometric invariant theory that constructs moduli spaces as projective quotients.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Classical algebraic geometry (1850s–1950s) focused on projective varieties as zero sets of homogeneous polynomial systems in $\mathbb{P}^n$. Pioneers from Cayley to Severi to Zariski developed an enormous body of techniques, but the foundational language was unsystematic — different texts used different definitions, and rigour was sometimes lacking.

The reformulation came in two stages:

1. *Cartan-Serre 1953–55.* Henri Cartan's seminars and Jean-Pierre Serre's *Faisceaux Algébriques Cohérents* (FAC, 1955) introduced the *sheaf-theoretic* perspective: a projective variety is a topological space equipped with a sheaf of regular functions, and coherent sheaves on it carry the essential geometric information. Serre's vanishing theorem (every coherent sheaf has a high enough twist with vanishing higher cohomology) was a foundational achievement.

2. *Grothendieck-Dieudonné 1960–67.* The *Éléments de Géométrie Algébrique* (EGA) introduced *schemes* in full generality, with $\mathrm{Spec}$ and $\mathrm{Proj}$ as the foundational constructions. Grothendieck's relative perspective ($\mathrm{Proj}_S$ over an arbitrary base $S$, projective morphisms, projective bundles) made projective schemes the natural setting for relative algebraic geometry.

The Proj construction is *the* algebraic-geometric formulation of projective space and projective varieties, working uniformly over arbitrary base rings (including $\mathbb{Z}$, finite fields, $p$-adic rings, formal rings, etc.). This generality is what makes modern arithmetic algebraic geometry possible.

The 20th century's deepest theorems — Weil conjectures (Deligne 1974), Mordell conjecture (Faltings 1983), Wiles's modularity theorem (1995), Lafforgue / Drinfeld / V. Lafforgue Langlands (1990s–2010s) — all live on projective schemes over arithmetic bases. The Proj construction is the conceptual foundation.

Today projective schemes are studied through their cohomology, Hilbert polynomials, intersection theory, motivic invariants, and moduli problems. The reach of projective schemes extends from classical curve and surface theory to mirror symmetry, geometric Langlands, and string-theoretic predictions. The single phrase "Proj of a graded ring" generates an essentially endless geometric landscape, just as $\mathrm{Spec}$ does for affine geometry.
