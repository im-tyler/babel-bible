## Intuition [Beginner]

Microeconomics studies how individuals and firms make choices under scarcity, and how those choices interact through markets to set prices and allocate goods. Its core insight is that constrained optimisation — doing the best you can within limits — describes behaviour across buyers, sellers, workers, and firms.

A consumer with limited income chooses among goods to maximise satisfaction. A firm with limited inputs chooses output to maximise profit. Markets bring these choices together: prices adjust until the quantity buyers want equals the quantity sellers offer.

Microeconomics is the foundation of every applied economic question, from tax policy to climate policy to platform competition.

## Visual [Beginner]

The supply-and-demand diagram: two curves crossing at the equilibrium price where the quantities offered and sought coincide.

![A standard supply (upward-sloping) and demand (downward-sloping) diagram with the intersection marked as the equilibrium price and quantity.](/img/microeconomics-placeholder.svg)

Above the equilibrium, surplus; below it, shortage. The crossing is the market clearing point.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A market has demand $Q_d = 100 - 2P$ and supply $Q_s = 20 + 2P$, where $P$ is price. Find the equilibrium.

Step 1. Set $Q_d = Q_s$: $100 - 2P = 20 + 2P$.

Step 2. Solve: $80 = 4P$, so $P = 20$.

Step 3. Quantity at equilibrium: $Q = 20 + 2(20) = 60$.

What this tells us: equilibrium is the price at which the plans of buyers and sellers are mutually consistent; neither surplus nor shortage remains.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **consumer's preference relation** $\succeq$ on a consumption set $X$ is complete, transitive, and continuous; under standard regularity it is representable by a utility function $u: X \to \mathbb{R}$ such that $x \succeq y$ iff $u(x) \ge u(y)$ [ref: TODO_REF Mas-Colell Whinston Green Ch. 1–3]. The consumer's problem is

$$
\max_{x \in X} u(x) \quad \text{subject to} \quad p \cdot x \le m, \quad x \ge 0,
$$

where $p$ is the price vector and $m$ is income. The solution defines the **Marshallian demand** $x(p, m)$ and the **indirect utility** $v(p, m) = u(x(p, m))$.

A **firm** produces output $y = f(z)$ from inputs $z$ via a production function $f$. Profit $\pi = p_y y - w \cdot z$ is maximised over feasible inputs; the solution gives input demands and output supply.

A **competitive equilibrium** is a price vector $p^*$ and allocations $(x_i^*)$, $(y_j^*)$ such that every consumer maximises utility, every firm maximises profit, and markets clear: $\sum_i x_i = \sum_j y_j$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Utility is ordinal, not cardinal.* Only the ranking matters for demand; doubling the utility function leaves choices unchanged.
- *Equilibrium need not be efficient without further assumptions.* Externalities and public goods can make competitive outcomes Pareto-inefficient.
- *Demand curves slope down only under restrictions.* Giffen goods, though rare, are demand curves that slope up.

## Economic theory [Intermediate+]

**First welfare theorem.** Under completeness, transitivity, continuity, and local non-satiation of preferences, every competitive equilibrium is Pareto efficient.

*Argument sketch.* Suppose an allocation $(x_i)$ is feasible and Pareto-dominates the equilibrium $(x_i^*)$. Then each consumer $i$ weakly prefers $x_i$ to $x_i^*$, and at least one strictly prefers. Local non-satiation implies each $x_i$ costs at least as much as $x_i^*$ at equilibrium prices, with one strict inequality. Summing gives that the dominating allocation costs strictly more than the equilibrium allocation — contradicting feasibility. $\square$

**Bridge.** This result builds toward [52.04.01] (game theory), where strategic interaction replaces price-taking and the welfare theorems need re-examination, and appears again in [52.03.01] (econometrics), where the competitive model's predictions are tested empirically. The foundational reason the welfare theorem matters is that it gives the competitive market a normative defence — efficiency without a planner — and putting these together, this is the bridge from the positive description of markets to the normative analysis of policy, and the pattern generalises to the mechanism-design results that extend the competitive framework to informationally constrained environments.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Set up the Lagrangian for the consumer's problem with utility $u(x_1, x_2) = x_1 x_2$, prices $(p_1, p_2)$, and income $m$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Constraint is $p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2 = m$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathcal{L} = x_1 x_2 - \lambda(p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2 - m)$. First-order conditions: $\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial x_1 = x_2 - \lambda p_1 = 0$, $\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial x_2 = x_1 - \lambda p_2 = 0$, plus the budget constraint. Dividing the first two gives $x_2/x_1 = p_1/p_2$; substituting into the budget gives $x_1 = m/(2p_1)$ and $x_2 = m/(2p_2)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Explain why a Giffen good's demand curve slopes upward.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Income effect dominates substitution effect and the good is inferior.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For an inferior good, the income effect of a price increase reduces real income and increases demand for the good (since the consumer can afford less of better substitutes). When this income effect outweighs the substitution effect, the net response of demand to a price increase is positive, giving an upward-sloping demand curve. Giffen goods are rare but theoretically possible.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that if preferences are Cobb-Douglas $u(x_1, x_2) = x_1^\alpha x_2^{1-\alpha}$, the consumer spends a constant fraction $\alpha$ of income on good 1.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Solve the Lagrangian; expenditure on good 1 is $p_1 x_1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

First-order conditions give $MRS = (1-\alpha)x_2/(\alpha x_1) = p_1/p_2$, so $p_1 x_1 = ((1-\alpha)/\alpha) \cdot (\alpha/(1-\alpha)) p_2 x_2$... rearranging more carefully: from $\alpha x_2 p_2 = (1-\alpha) x_1 p_1$ and the budget $p_1 x_1 + p_2 x_2 = m$, substituting $p_2 x_2 = ((1-\alpha)/\alpha) p_1 x_1$ gives $p_1 x_1 (1 + (1-\alpha)/\alpha) = m$, so $p_1 x_1 / \alpha = m$, hence $p_1 x_1 = \alpha m$. Thus a constant fraction $\alpha$ of income is spent on good 1.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

State the Second Welfare Theorem and the key assumption it requires beyond those of the First.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Convexity; redistribution of endowments.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Second Welfare Theorem: under convexity of preferences and production sets, every Pareto-efficient allocation can be achieved as a competitive equilibrium after a suitable redistribution of initial endowments. ## Advanced results [Master]

The competitive model extends in several directions. **General equilibrium** (Walras, Arrow, Debreu) establishes existence and welfare properties of simultaneous equilibrium across all markets; the **Arrow-Debreu model** handles uncertainty and time via contingent-claim markets [ref: TODO_REF Debreu Theory of Value]. **Mechanism design** (Hurwicz, Maskin, Myerson) characterises the institutions that implement desired outcomes under private information, formalising the trade-offs the Second Welfare Theorem surfaces.

**Market failures** — externalities, public goods, asymmetric information, market power — are the standard cases where the First Welfare Theorem's assumptions fail. Pigouvian taxes, Coasian bargaining, and auction design address them respectively. Behavioural economics (covered in [52.06.01]) documents systematic deviations from the rational-agent model, motivating richer preference specifications.

**Synthesis.** Microeconomics is the load-bearing analytical framework of the economics curriculum: the constrained-optimisation framework established here builds toward [52.02.01] (macroeconomics), where aggregates of micro-decisions produce macro-outcomes, appears again in [52.03.01] (econometrics), where the model's predictions are estimated empirically, the foundational reason the welfare theorems matter is that they give a precise normative benchmark against which real markets and policies are judged, the bridge is that the same optimisation-and-equilibrium pattern recurs from individual choice to general equilibrium to mechanism design, and the pattern generalises to the game-theoretic analyses of strategic interaction in [52.04.01]; putting these together, microeconomics is the foundation of every applied economic question, the chapter whose results the other economics chapters presuppose.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Existence of consumer optimum).** If the consumption set $X \subset \mathbb{R}^n_+$ is closed, convex, and bounded below, and the utility function $u$ is continuous, then for any price vector $p \gg 0$ and income $m > 0$, the budget set $\{x \in X : p \cdot x \le m\}$ is compact and the consumer's problem attains its maximum.

*Argument.* The budget set is closed (intersection of closed sets) and bounded (because all prices are strictly positive and income is finite), hence compact by Heine-Borel. A continuous function on a compact set attains its maximum (Weierstrass). Hence an optimal consumption bundle exists. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Macroeconomics [52.02.01].** Aggregate outcomes — output, employment, inflation — are built up from microeconomic decisions of consumers and firms; modern macro models are explicitly micro-founded.

- **Econometrics [52.03.01].** The empirical estimation of supply, demand, and consumer-preference parameters uses the structural predictions developed here.

- **Game theory [52.04.01].** When agents are large enough to affect prices, the competitive paradigm gives way to strategic analysis, the subject of game theory.

- **Optimisation and duality [44.02.01].** The mathematical machinery of constrained optimisation (Lagrangian, KKT, duality) that microeconomics deploys is the formal subject of the optimisation chapter.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Adam Smith's *Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations* (1776) introduced the idea that self-interested agents in a competitive market produce socially coordinated outcomes — the "invisible hand" [ref: TODO_REF Smith 1776]. The formalisation of this intuition into the welfare theorems was the work of the twentieth-century general-equilibrium school: Walras's *Éléments d'économie politique pure* (1874), Arrow and Debreu's 1954 existence proof, and Debreu's *Theory of Value* (1959).

The constrained-optimisation approach to consumer theory was systematised by Hicks (*Value and Capital*, 1939) and Samuelson (*Foundations of Economic Analysis*, 1947), who made the Lagrangian and duality framework standard. Mas-Colell, Whinston, and Green's *Microeconomic Theory* (1995) is the canonical graduate synthesis, integrating general equilibrium, game theory, and information economics into a single framework.

The discipline's ongoing development — behavioural economics, mechanism design, market design — continues to refine the rational-agent foundation while preserving the optimisation-and-equilibrium core that the First Welfare Theorem crystallises.
