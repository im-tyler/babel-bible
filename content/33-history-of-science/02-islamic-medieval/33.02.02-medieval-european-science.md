## Intuition [Beginner]

The "Dark Ages" were not actually dark. Between roughly 500 and 1500 CE, European monasteries preserved ancient knowledge by patiently copying manuscripts onto parchment, century after century. Without these monks, much of Greek and Roman science would have been lost entirely.

Starting in the 11th century, a new kind of institution appeared: the university. Bologna (1088), Paris (around 1150), Oxford (around 1167), and later Cambridge were Europe's first communities of higher learning since antiquity. They had faculties, examinations, and degrees — a structure still recognizable today. These self-governing corporations gave Europe a durable home for sustained inquiry.

In the 12th century, scholars flocked to Toledo in Spain, then recently retaken from Muslim rule, to translate Arabic and Greek texts into Latin. Through this work Europe recovered Aristotle, Euclid, Ptolemy, and Galen at a level of sophistication unmatched in Latin for centuries. Gerard of Cremona alone translated dozens of these works.

Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274) then synthesized Aristotle's philosophy with Christian theology. This system, called Scholasticism, used rigorous logical disputation: pose a question, gather authoritative arguments on both sides, and resolve the contradiction through careful distinctions. The method prized logical precision above all else.

The Condemnations of 1277, in which Bishop Étienne Tempier banned 219 Aristotelian propositions at Paris, had an unexpected effect. If Aristotle could be wrong about the eternity of the world or the necessity of nature, then his authority was not absolute. This paradoxically freed medieval thinkers to explore alternatives.

The Oxford Calculators, led by Thomas Bradwardine around 1300, developed a mathematical physics at Merton College. Jean Buridan's impetus theory (around 1340) proposed that a thrown object keeps moving because of an internal force impressed on it, not because the air pushes it around — a genuine precursor to the modern idea of inertia.

## Worked example [Beginner]

One of the genuine achievements of medieval mathematical physics is the **Merton mean speed theorem**, proved at Merton College, Oxford, in the 1330s. It says: a body that speeds up uniformly from rest to a final velocity travels, in that time, exactly as far as a body moving steadily at half that final velocity.

Suppose a cart rolls down a slope, accelerating smoothly from rest to 8 metres per second over 4 seconds. What is the fastest way to find the distance travelled without calculus?

Step 1: Identify the final velocity, $v = 8$ metres per second.

Step 2: Take the mean (middle) velocity between rest and the final value: $8 / 2 = 4$ metres per second.

Step 3: Multiply this mean velocity by the time: $4 \times 4 = 16$ metres.

So the cart travels 16 metres. The result is exact, not approximate, provided the acceleration is uniform. Nicole Oresme later justified this rule by drawing velocity against time: the speeding cart traces a triangle of base 4 seconds and height 8 metres per second, whose area is $\tfrac{1}{2} \times 4 \times 8 = 16$ square units — matching the rectangle of the mean-speed body. Equal areas mean equal distances. Galileo would rediscover this same relation three centuries later.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Medieval European science is best understood through three interlocking formal concepts: the **university** as a legal institution, the **scholastic method** as a disciplined form of inquiry, and the **mathematical natural philosophy** developed at Oxford and Paris in the 14th century.

**The university.** A *universitas* was a self-governing corporation — a guild of masters or students possessing a charter that granted legal autonomy, the right to set its own curriculum, and the power to confer universally recognized degrees (*bicellarius*, *magister*, *doctor*). It was organized into faculties (arts, law, medicine, theology). This corporate-legal form had no precise parallel in the Islamic *madrasa* or the Chinese academy, and it gave European scholars a measure of independence from both Crown and Church that proved decisive for later inquiry.

**The scholastic method.** Inquiry proceeded by the *quaestio*: a question is posed ("Whether the world is eternal"); authorities for and against are marshalled (*sed contra*); a reasoned resolution is given (*respondeo*); and objections are answered. Its engine was the *disputatio*, a formal oral debate governed by strict logical rules. Peter Abelard's *Sic et Non* (Yes and No) set the template by juxtaposing contradictory authorities and demanding their reconciliation through distinction. Aquinas's *Summa Theologiae* is the canonical artifact of the method.

**Impetus (Buridan).** Jean Buridan defined the impressed force quantitatively:

$$ I = m \cdot v $$

where $m$ measures the "quantity of matter" of the body and $v$ its velocity. The impetus $I$ is conserved in the absence of resistance and is gradually consumed by it. This is a precise, if qualitative, precursor to momentum and inertia. Buridan applied it to explain projectile motion, the acceleration of falling bodies (gravity continually impresses new impetus), and the indefinite rotation of the celestial spheres — each sphere carrying the impetus God imparted at creation.

**Bradwardine's law of motion.** In *Tractatus de Proportionibus Velocitatum in Motibus* (1328), Thomas Bradwardine proposed a nontrivial functional relationship between motive force $F$, resistance $R$, and velocity $V$. Against the Aristotelian reading that $V$ is proportional to $F - R$ (or to $F/R$ taken arithmetically), Bradwardine held that velocity follows the *ratio* $F/R$ by geometrical (multiplicative) composition:

$$ V \;\propto\; \log(F/R), $$

equivalently, if a ratio $F/R = n$ produces velocity $V$, then the ratio $n^{2}$ produces $2V$, $n^{3}$ produces $3V$, and so on. Bradwardine lacked logarithms, but he described the same composition of ratios that the modern logarithm captures. The law is physically wrong (it admits no motion when $F \le R$, and misdescribes the actual dependence), yet it is the first explicit attempt to write a mathematical *law* of motion — a function relating measurable physical quantities. The methodological leap matters more than the specific formula.

## Key result with derivation [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Merton mean speed theorem).** *A body uniformly accelerated from rest to velocity $v_{f}$ over a time interval of duration $t$ travels the same distance it would travel moving uniformly at velocity $v_{f}/2$ for the same duration. Equivalently, the distance travelled is*

$$ d = \tfrac{1}{2}\, v_{f}\, t. $$

*Derivation (Oresme's geometrical proof, c. 1350).* Represent the motion on a diagram in which a horizontal line measures time and vertical "latitudes" measure instantaneous velocity, drawn perpendicular to the time axis at each instant. This is the earliest known instance of a graph of a function against its independent variable.

For uniform acceleration from rest, the latitudes grow linearly with time. Their tops lie on a straight line from the origin to the point $(t, v_{f})$, so the region swept out is a right triangle of base $t$ and height $v_{f}$. Nicole Oresme identified the total "quantity of motion" — the distance travelled — with the *area* of this region, an identification later made rigorous in integral calculus:

$$ d = \mathrm{area}(\triangle) = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot t \cdot v_{f}. $$

Now consider a second body moving *uniformly* at the mean velocity $v_{f}/2$ for the same time $t$. Its latitudes are all equal to $v_{f}/2$, so its region is a rectangle of width $t$ and height $v_{f}/2$, with area

$$ d' = t \cdot \frac{v_{f}}{2} = \tfrac{1}{2}\, v_{f}\, t = d. $$

Equal areas give equal distances, which establishes the theorem.

*Significance.* The result is identical in content to Galileo's law $d = \tfrac{1}{2} a t^{2}$ (using $v_{f} = a t$), published in the *Discorsi* of 1638 — roughly three centuries after the Merton school proved it. William Heytesbury, John Dumbleton, and Richard Swineshead gave verbal and logical proofs at Oxford in the 1330s; Oresme's geometrical version is the most visually transparent. This is the cleanest piece of evidence for the *continuity thesis*: the mathematical tools of classical mechanics were not invented ex nihilo in the 17th century but developed, in recognizable form, within the medieval university.

**Exercise (medium, short answer).**

State Bradwardine's law relating velocity to force and resistance, and explain in what sense it was methodologically revolutionary even though its specific formula is physically incorrect.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Bradwardine held that velocity depends on the ratio $F/R$ by multiplicative composition: doubling the velocity requires *squaring* the ratio $F/R$ (in modern terms, $V \propto \log(F/R)$). This differs from the Aristotelian view, which treated the dependence arithmetically ($V \propto F - R$ or $V \propto F/R$ linearly). The formula is wrong — it predicts no motion whenever $F \le R$ and distorts the dependence — but it was revolutionary because it was the first explicit attempt to formulate a *mathematical law* of motion: a determinate function linking measurable quantities (force, resistance, velocity). It established the very idea that motion could be governed by a precise functional relation, the template Galileo and Newton would later exploit.

Explain how the *quaestio*–*disputatio* structure of the scholastic method could both support and constrain scientific inquiry. Give one concrete historical example in which it helped, and one in which it acted as an obstacle.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The scholastic method supports inquiry by forcing every claim into a dialectical structure: a question is stated, the strongest arguments on each side are collected, and a resolution is argued on reasoned grounds rather than asserted. This trained a generation of scholars in careful distinction, counterexample, and logical rigor — habits indispensable to later natural philosophy. Aquinas's treatment of whether the world is eternal exhibits the method at its best: the arguments for and against are stated fairly and the resolution turns on a genuine conceptual distinction (between eternity of duration and eternity of origin).

The same structure constrained inquiry when authorities were treated as unrevisable. Because every *respondeo* had to be reconciled with Aristotle and Galen, conclusions that contradicted them — that the vacuum is possible, that the Earth moves, that the heart's septum is impermeable — were often deflected into verbal compromises rather than rejected on empirical grounds. Galen's erroneous anatomy persisted in medieval medical faculties long after Mondino de Liuzzi's dissections might have exposed it, precisely because the *quaestio* demanded reconciliation with the authoritative text. The Condemnations of 1277 loosened this constraint by demonstrating that Aristotle himself could be overruled.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="essay" markdown="1">

**Exercise (hard, essay).**

Buridan applied his impetus theory both to projectiles and to the celestial spheres. Reconstruct that second application and explain why it is conceptually significant for the later unification of terrestrial and celestial physics.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Buridan argued that God impressed an impetus on each celestial sphere at the moment of creation. Because the heavens offer no resistance (they are, on the Aristotelian cosmology, perfect and incorruptible), that impetus is never dissipated, and the spheres rotate indefinitely without the intervention of intelligences or external movers. The significance is twofold. First, it eliminates the need for separate causes for celestial motion: the same *vis impressa* that moves a stone moves a planet. Second, it dissolves the sharp Aristotelian boundary between the sublunary world of change and resistance and the superlunary world of eternal circular motion, replacing it with a single dynamical principle whose effects differ only because resistance differs. This is precisely the conceptual move — one physics for heaven and earth — that Galileo, Descartes, and Newton would complete. Buridan did not take the further step of heliocentrism, but he removed a major obstacle to it.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The central historiographical claim of this unit is the **continuity thesis**: that the Scientific Revolution of the 17th century was not a rupture imposed on a dormant Europe but the maturation of traditions developed inside the medieval university. The thesis was most forcefully argued by Pierre Duhem in *Le Système du Monde* (1913–1959), who traced Galileo's mechanics directly to the 14th-century Parisian nominalists and held that modern science was "born from the ashes of the Condemnations of 1277." Edward Grant's *The Foundations of Modern Science in the Middle Ages* (1996) restated the case institutionally, arguing that the autonomous, chartered university was Europe's distinctive contribution — an institutional novelty without parallel in Islamic or Chinese civilization — and that without it the Scientific Revolution could not have occurred.

Against continuity stands Alexandre Koyré's **discontinuity thesis**, which locates the break not in methods or institutions but in a transformation of thought: the replacement of a closed, hierarchical, qualitative cosmos with an infinite, homogeneous, mathematical one. On this view the medieval achievement was real but preparatory; what mattered was the conceptual revolution achieved by Galileo, Descartes, and Newton. The contemporary consensus, reflected in Cohen's *How Modern Science Came into the World* (2010), borrows from both: medieval natural philosophy supplied indispensable tools and institutions, but the 17th-century synthesis involved genuine conceptual innovation that cannot be reduced to a continuation of scholasticism.

### The 12th-century Renaissance

Charles Haskins's *The Renaissance of the Twelfth Century* (1927) established that the century preceding the universities was itself a period of intense intellectual revival. Cathedral schools — particularly Chartres, under Thierry of Chartres and William of Conches — pursued naturalistic explanations of creation, reading Genesis as a cosmological process governed by underlying regularities. The recovery of Roman law (the rediscovery of Justinian's *Corpus Juris Civilis*), the standardization of scripts (Carolingian minuscule), and the beginnings of the Toledo translation movement all belong to this century. The 12th-century Renaissance created the literate, textually disciplined culture in which the university could crystallize.

### The university as institutional innovation

Toby Huff's *The Rise of Early Modern Science* (1993) emphasizes that the European university was a legally autonomous corporation with the right to govern its members, set its curriculum, and grant degrees valid across Christendom. This legal structure — rooted in the Roman-law concept of the *corporation* and protected by papal and royal privilege — insulated scholars from local political control in a way that neither the Islamic *madrasa* (typically governed by the endower's deed) nor the Chinese academy (an arm of the imperial bureaucracy) achieved. The legal fact of autonomy, Huff argues, was a necessary condition for the free inquiry that the Scientific Revolution required. Whether one accepts Huff's comparative claim in full, the institutional contrast is real: Europe's universities produced a self-reproducing community of natural philosophers whose disputes could not be settled by appeal to a sovereign.

### Medieval technology and its dynamism

Lynn White's *Medieval Technology and Social Change* (1962) and Jean Gimpel's *The Medieval Machine* (1976) document a Europe that was technologically dynamic, not stagnant. The heavy plow, the three-field system, the horse collar, the stirrup, the mechanical clock (13th century), the windmill, and the widespread use of waterpower for industrial milling collectively transformed agriculture, warfare, time-consciousness, and manufacture. White controversially argued that the stirrup produced feudalism; the broader and less contestable point is that medieval Europe generated labor-saving mechanical innovations at a rate that, by the late Middle Ages, exceeded that of any other civilization. This technological base — in precision metalwork, gearing, and timekeeping — supplied both the instruments and the artisanal skills on which the Scientific Revolution's experimental apparatus would depend.

### Optics from Grosseteste to Kepler

Robert Grosseteste (c. 1168–1253), first chancellor of Oxford, held that light (*lux*) is the first corporeal form and that natural processes are mathematically tractable because they propagate by radiation. His student Roger Bacon (c. 1214–1292) developed an experimental and mathematical *perspectiva* drawing on Ptolemy and Ibn al-Haytham (Alhazen), and wrote on optics, gunpowder, and the possibility of mechanical vehicles. The Polish-born Witelo's *Perspectiva* (c. 1270–1280) systematized the Alhazenian tradition for Latin readers. Kepler read this literature carefully before writing *Astronomia Pars Optica* (1604) and *Dioptrice* (1611); his theory of retinal imaging is a direct descendant of the medieval *perspectiva* tradition. Medieval optics also seeded Renaissance linear perspective: Brunelleschi's demonstration and Alberti's *De Pictura* (1435) translate the geometry of sight into the geometry of the picture plane, fusing art and optics in a way that would prove consequential for the culture of exact representation.

### Astronomy from Peurbach to Copernicus

The direct astronomical ancestry of Copernicus runs through Georg von Peurbach (1423–1461) and his student Johannes Regiomontanus (1436–1476), whose *Epitome of the Almagest* (1496) provided the clearest Latin exposition of Ptolemy and the Alfonsine Tables then in use. Copernicus studied at Bologna and Padua, read the *Epitome* intensively, and adopted mathematical devices — including constructions equivalent to the Maragha *Ṭūsī couple* and *ʿUrḍī lemma* developed by al-Ṭūsī and Ibn al-Shāṭir — that had been transmitted, by routes still debated, from Islamic astronomy. Whether Copernicus saw the Maragha models directly is unresolved, but the mathematical parallel is unmistakable and shows that the resources for reforming Ptolemy were a multi-civilizational achievement.

### Alchemy and proto-chemistry

Medieval Latin alchemy, exemplified by the *Summa Perfectionis* attributed to the Pseudo-Geber (c. 1310), developed practical laboratory techniques — distillation, calcination, the preparation of mineral acids (nitric, sulfuric) — that became the operational foundation of later chemistry. Alchemical aims (transmutation, the elixir of life) belonged to a worldview in which matter was spiritually and astrologically ordered, but the laboratory practice was empirical and cumulative. The demarcation between alchemy and chemistry, much debated in philosophy of science, is best treated historically: the same furnaces and the same distillation apparatus produced both the alchemist's transmutation fantasies and the chemist's identification of new substances. Lavoisier's chemical revolution of the late 18th century reorganized the discipline conceptually but inherited its techniques from the medieval and early modern alchemical tradition.

### Medicine: Galenism, dissection, and the plague

Medieval academic medicine was Galenic: it explained health and disease through the balance of the four humors, and diagnosis rested on urine inspection, pulse, and astrological conditions. The medical faculties at Salerno, Montpellier, and Bologna transmitted Ibn Sīna's *Canon* and Galen's corpus. Mondino de Liuzzi's *Anathomia* (1316) reintroduced public dissection into European medical education, but the practice remained constrained by deference to Galenic authority and by the legal and religious limits on anatomical work. The Black Death of 1347–1351, killing between thirty and fifty percent of Europe's population, exposed the inadequacy of Galenic explanation (miasma theory failed to predict or control the epidemic) and prompted the development of quarantine as a public-health measure — an institutional response that belongs to the prehistory of modern epidemiology.

### Magic, astrology, and the boundaries of natural philosophy

Astrology, grounded in Ptolemy's *Tetrabiblos*, was not sharply distinguished from legitimate natural philosophy in the medieval period; planetary influence on terrestrial events was a routine assumption of medical and agricultural practice. *Natural magic*, systematized in the 15th century by Marsilio Ficino's Neoplatonic Hermeticism, sought to harness hidden correspondences in nature and would later influence Kepler and Newton, both of whom took astrological and alchemical concerns seriously. The demarcation problem — which inquiries count as "science" and which as "magic" — is thus historically recent, and imposing the modern boundary retrospectively distorts the medieval intellectual landscape. The productive interpenetration of magic, astrology, and natural philosophy was itself a precondition for the eventual separation of legitimate science from its esoteric neighbors.

### The medieval roots of the Scientific Revolution

The cumulative argument is that the 17th-century synthesis drew on at least four medieval inheritances: the institutional (the autonomous university), the methodological (the *quaestio* and the mathematical analysis of motion), the empirical (alchemical and optical practice), and the conceptual (impetus, the relativity of motion, the possibility that Aristotle was wrong). Whether one calls this continuity or revolution is largely a question of emphasis. What is no longer defensible is the older picture of a thousand-year void between antiquity and Galileo. The medieval university produced the people, the texts, the logical habits, and the mathematical tools that the Scientific Revolution reorganized and extended.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

The historiography of medieval European science has undergone two great reversals. The first, accomplished roughly between 1900 and 1960, dismantled the "Dark Ages" myth. Pierre Duhem's discovery that 14th-century Parisian natural philosophers had proved a recognizable form of the mean speed theorem — and that Galileo had likely read their work in Jesuit editions — forced a recognition that medieval science was neither empty nor merely preservative. Lynn Thorndike's eight-volume *History of Magic and Experimental Science* (1923–1958), the work of Haskins, and later Marshall Clagett's editions of the Merton school cemented the picture of a period of genuine, if uneven, achievement.

The second reversal, still in progress, concerns the *comparative* question. For most of the 20th century the continuity thesis was framed internally to Europe: what did medieval Europe inherit, and what did it bequeath to Galileo? More recent work, drawing on the scholarship surveyed in unit 33.02.01, reframes the question globally. The Aristotle that Aquinas synthesized was a Greek text recovered through Arabic translation and enriched by Islamic commentary (notably Ibn Rushd). The Ptolemy that Regiomontanus epitomized had been criticized and improved by the Maragha school. The algebra Bradwardine used to compose ratios descended from al-Khwārizmī. Medieval European science was not a self-contained European achievement but the European branch of a Mediterranean-wide intellectual network — and the institutions and methods developed in the European universities were one, but only one, route through that network.

The philosophical stakes are highest in the Duhem–Koyré debate. Duhem, a Catholic physicist and historian of unusual range, argued that modern science emerged from the internal development of medieval natural philosophy and that the Condemnations of 1277 — by breaking Aristotle's necessity — opened the logical space in which alternatives could be entertained. On this reading, science has a continuous institutional and conceptual history within the Latin West, and the 17th-century "revolution" is a phase in a longer evolution. Koyré, working in the phenomenological tradition, denied this: the decisive change was not the accumulation of techniques but the replacement of one conception of reality (the Aristotelian cosmos of places and natures) with another (the homogeneous, mathematical *universum* of classical physics). The debate is not merely antiquarian, because it bears on whether scientific change is best understood as continuous refinement or as conceptual rupture — the very question Kuhn's *Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) would make central to philosophy of science.

A related controversy concerns the **Huff thesis**: that the legal autonomy of the European university explains why modern science emerged in Europe rather than in the Islamic world or China, despite the latter's earlier scientific sophistication. Critics object that this is a one-factor explanation for a many-caused outcome and that it understates the real — if differentially organized — inquiry that occurred in *madrasas*, observatories, and Chinese state academies. Defenders reply that no other civilization developed an institution with precisely the combination of legal autonomy, standardized curriculum, and transregional degree recognition that the European university possessed, and that this combination mattered specifically for the cumulative, disputatious, trans-generational inquiry that modern science requires. The honest position is that the question is open and that institutional, economic, religious, and contingent factors all plausibly contributed.

Finally, a methodological caution. Medieval natural philosophy was conducted almost entirely within the framework of Aristotelian categories and Christian theology, and its central genre — the *quaestio* — demanded reconciliation with authority. This makes it tempting either to read medieval figures as closet moderns (anticipating Galileo, Newton, or entropy) or to dismiss them as scholastic pedants. Both readings flatten a genuinely different intellectual practice. The right approach, exemplified by Grant and Clagett, is to reconstruct medieval natural philosophy in its own terms: to ask what questions its practitioners took seriously, what counts as a good answer within their genre, and what they achieved given their premises. Judged that way, the period is neither a void nor a dress rehearsal but a sophisticated and consequential chapter in the history of reason.
