## Intuition [Beginner]

Statistical mechanics of identical bosons — photons in a cavity, helium-4 atoms in a trap, phonons in a crystal — needs a state space large enough to hold any number of particles at once. Ordinary single-particle quantum mechanics fixes the particle count by hand, but real systems exchange particles with a reservoir (the photon bath of a hot cavity, the gas-phase reservoir for a condensate). The Hilbert space has to accommodate the vacuum (no particles), one-particle states, two-particle states, and so on, all glued into a single home where operators can hop between particle-number sectors.

Bosonic Fock space is that home. Start with the one-particle Hilbert space — the space of wavefunctions a single boson can occupy. Then build the two-particle space as the symmetrised tensor product (symmetric because swapping the labels of two identical bosons must leave the state unchanged), the three-particle space as the symmetrised triple tensor product, and so on. The bosonic Fock space stacks all of these together with the empty-particle one-dimensional space, the vacuum, at the bottom.

The procedure that takes the one-particle quantum mechanics and lifts it to the many-particle theory on this stacked space is called second quantisation. The name is a historical accident — there is no first quantisation being quantised a second time — but it stuck after Dirac (1927) and Jordan-Klein (1927) used it to package the photon and the free Bose gas. Fock (1932) gave the symmetric-tensor-power construction its present form. Statistical mechanics on the resulting space recovers Planck's blackbody law, the Bose-Einstein condensate, and the partition function of every free bosonic field theory.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a ladder with no top rung. The bottom rung is the empty vacuum — no particles, just the unit vector that says "nothing here". The first rung holds one-particle states; the rung is itself a copy of the one-particle Hilbert space. The second rung holds two-particle states, with the convention that swapping the labels of the two particles gives the same state (identical bosons cannot be told apart). The third rung holds three-particle states with the same symmetry, and so on upward.

![A schematic of a bosonic Fock space as a vertical stack of rungs labelled by particle number, with the vacuum at the bottom, the one-particle Hilbert space above it, the symmetrised two-particle space above that, and so on, with creation arrows going up the ladder one rung at a time and annihilation arrows going down the ladder one rung at a time.](/img/bosonic-fock-space-and-second-quantisation-placeholder.svg)

Two operators move states between rungs. The creation operator $a^*(f)$ adds a particle in the one-particle state $f$, climbing one rung up the ladder. The annihilation operator $a(f)$ removes a particle in state $f$, descending one rung. Their algebra reads $a(f) a^*(g) - a^*(g) a(f) = \langle f, g \rangle$, the canonical commutation relation. The number operator $N$ counts how many particles are present and is the rung label itself. Multi-mode statistical mechanics is the same picture with a separate ladder for every one-particle eigenmode, and the joint state space is the tensor product of all the ladders.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute the grand-canonical partition function of a free Bose gas using one mode of frequency $\omega$ at temperature $T = 1/\beta$ and chemical potential $\mu$.

Step 1. List the states on the ladder. The rung labelled $n$ is the state with $n$ bosons in this mode, with energy $n \hbar \omega$. The rung labels run $n = 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ with no upper bound.

Step 2. Write the grand-canonical weight. Each state contributes a weight $e^{- \beta (n \hbar \omega - \mu n)} = e^{- \beta n (\hbar \omega - \mu)}$. Set $\epsilon = \hbar \omega - \mu$ and demand $\epsilon > 0$ so the series below converges.

Step 3. Sum the geometric series. The single-mode partition function adds up the weights $1 + e^{- \beta \epsilon} + e^{- 2\beta \epsilon} + e^{- 3\beta \epsilon} + \cdots$, a geometric series with ratio $e^{- \beta \epsilon}$. The closed-form sum is $\Xi_1 = 1 / (1 - e^{- \beta \epsilon})$.

Step 4. Plug in numbers. At $\beta \epsilon = \ln 2$, $e^{- \beta \epsilon} = 1/2$ and $\Xi_1 = 1 / (1 - 1/2) = 2$. The average occupation number is the Bose-Einstein factor $\langle n \rangle = 1 / (e^{\beta \epsilon} - 1)$, giving $\langle n \rangle = 1 / (2 - 1) = 1$ at $\beta \epsilon = \ln 2$.

Step 5. Generalise to many modes. Each mode contributes its own factor, and the full partition function is the product of single-mode factors over all the one-particle modes. For photons in a cubic cavity of side $L$, the modes are labelled by wavevector $\mathbf{k}$ with $\epsilon_k = \hbar c |\mathbf{k}|$, and the product becomes the integral that gives Planck's blackbody spectrum.

What this tells us: the geometric-series sum over occupation numbers $n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ on a single rung is exactly the structure the bosonic Fock space packages. Statistical mechanics inherits the unbounded ladder from the symmetric tensor power, and the partition function is the trace of $e^{- \beta (H - \mu N)}$ over the entire Fock space.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\mathcal{H}$ be a complex separable Hilbert space — the **one-particle Hilbert space** of the theory. In statistical-mechanics applications $\mathcal{H} = L^2(\Lambda, d\mu)$ for $\Lambda \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ the spatial domain and $\mu$ Lebesgue measure; in relativistic QFT applications $\mathcal{H} = L^2(X_m, d\lambda_m)$ with $X_m = \{p \in \mathbb{R}^{1,d-1} : p^2 = m^2,\ p^0 > 0\}$ the mass shell and $d\lambda_m$ the Lorentz-invariant measure. For each integer $n \geq 0$ write $\mathcal{H}^{\otimes n}$ for the Hilbert tensor power with the convention $\mathcal{H}^{\otimes 0} := \mathbb{C}$. The symmetric group $S_n$ acts on $\mathcal{H}^{\otimes n}$ by permutation of tensor factors, with each permutation $\sigma \in S_n$ represented by a unitary $U_\sigma$.

The **symmetrisation projector** is
$$
P_s^{(n)} := \frac{1}{n!} \sum_{\sigma \in S_n} U_\sigma,
$$
and the **symmetric $n$-fold tensor power** is the closed subspace
$$
S^n \mathcal{H} := P_s^{(n)} \mathcal{H}^{\otimes n} = \{ \xi \in \mathcal{H}^{\otimes n} : U_\sigma \xi = \xi \text{ for all } \sigma \in S_n \}.
$$

The **bosonic Fock space** over $\mathcal{H}$ is the Hilbert direct sum
$$
\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}) := \bigoplus_{n=0}^{\infty} S^n \mathcal{H},
$$
with inner product $\langle (\xi_n)_n, (\eta_n)_n \rangle = \sum_{n \geq 0} \langle \xi_n, \eta_n \rangle_{S^n \mathcal{H}}$ and norm $\|(\xi_n)_n\|^2 = \sum_n \|\xi_n\|^2 < \infty$. The construction is the $\ell^2$ direct sum of the symmetric tensor powers; $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ is again a complex separable Hilbert space. The summand $S^0 \mathcal{H} = \mathbb{C}$ is spanned by the unit vector $\Omega$ called the **vacuum**.

For $f_1, \ldots, f_n \in \mathcal{H}$ write the **symmetric tensor** $f_1 \vee \cdots \vee f_n := \sqrt{n!}\, P_s^{(n)}(f_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes f_n)$. The symmetric inner product is computed by the **permanent** of the Gram matrix,
$$
\langle f_1 \vee \cdots \vee f_n,\ g_1 \vee \cdots \vee g_n \rangle = \mathrm{perm}\bigl( \langle f_i, g_j \rangle \bigr).
$$
The $\sqrt{n!}$ normalisation is the convention that gives clean square-root coefficients in the action of the creation operator below.

The **creation operator** $a^*(f)$ for $f \in \mathcal{H}$ is the densely defined operator on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ with $a^*(f) : S^n \mathcal{H} \to S^{n+1} \mathcal{H}$ acting by
$$
a^*(f)(g_1 \vee \cdots \vee g_n) = f \vee g_1 \vee \cdots \vee g_n, \qquad a^*(f) \Omega = f.
$$
The **annihilation operator** $a(f)$ is the formal adjoint, with $a(f) : S^n \mathcal{H} \to S^{n-1} \mathcal{H}$ acting by
$$
a(f)(g_1 \vee \cdots \vee g_n) = \sum_{k=1}^{n} \langle f, g_k \rangle\, g_1 \vee \cdots \vee \widehat{g_k} \vee \cdots \vee g_n, \qquad a(f) \Omega = 0,
$$
where the caret marks omission. The map $f \mapsto a^*(f)$ is complex-linear; the map $f \mapsto a(f)$ is complex anti-linear with $a(\lambda f) = \bar\lambda\, a(f)$. Both operators are defined on the **finite-particle subspace** $\mathcal{F}_s^{\mathrm{fin}}(\mathcal{H})$ (vectors with only finitely many non-zero components in the direct sum), which is dense in $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$.

The **number operator** acts on $S^n \mathcal{H}$ by multiplication by $n$. Given an orthonormal basis $\{e_k\}_{k \geq 1}$ of $\mathcal{H}$,
$$
N = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} a^*(e_k)\, a(e_k),
$$
with the sum convergent on the finite-particle subspace and extending to a self-adjoint operator on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ with spectrum $\sigma(N) = \mathbb{N}_0$. The vacuum is the unique unit vector with $N \Omega = 0$.

The **second-quantisation functor** $\Gamma$ assigns to each unitary $U$ on $\mathcal{H}$ the unitary $\Gamma(U)$ on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ acting as $U^{\otimes n}$ on $S^n \mathcal{H}$. The functor preserves products: $\Gamma(UV) = \Gamma(U) \Gamma(V)$ and $\Gamma(\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{H}}) = \mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})}$. Its infinitesimal generator, for a one-particle self-adjoint $A$ with $U(t) = e^{-itA}$, is $d\Gamma(A) := -i \frac{d}{dt} \Gamma(U(t))|_{t=0}$, acting on $S^n \mathcal{H}$ as $\sum_{k=1}^{n} \mathbf{1} \otimes \cdots \otimes A_{(k)} \otimes \cdots \otimes \mathbf{1}$ (with $A$ in slot $k$). In particular $N = d\Gamma(\mathbf{1}_{\mathcal{H}})$ and the **free Hamiltonian** of a one-particle Schrödinger problem $h$ on $\mathcal{H}$ lifts to the many-particle free Hamiltonian $H_0 = d\Gamma(h)$ on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$. The procedure $h \mapsto d\Gamma(h)$ is the operational content of **second quantisation**: the one-particle dynamics determines the many-body free dynamics, with no additional input.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The operators $a(f)$ and $a^*(f)$ are not bounded. On a one-mode oscillator, $\|a^*|n\rangle\| = \sqrt{n+1}$ grows without bound as $n \to \infty$.

- The CCR $[a, a^*] = \mathbf{1}$ in fact forces unboundedness: no pair of bounded operators on a Hilbert space can satisfy $[A, B] = \mathbf{1}$, since iterating gives $[A, B^n] = n B^{n-1}$ and operator-norm comparison forces $n \leq 2 \|A\| \|B\|$ for every $n$, an impossible bound (Wielandt 1949).

- The Fock space $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ is not the Hilbert tensor power $\mathcal{H}^{\otimes n}$ for any fixed $n$. The Fock space hosts superpositions with indeterminate particle number — most notably the **coherent states** $|z\rangle = e^{-|z|^2/2} \sum_n (z^n / \sqrt{n!}) |n\rangle$, eigenvectors of $a$ with eigenvalue $z$ but with no definite particle count.

- The grand-canonical thermal trace $\mathrm{tr}_{\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})}(e^{-\beta (H - \mu N)})$ is finite for an unbounded one-particle Hamiltonian $h$ only when the eigenvalues $\epsilon_k$ grow fast enough that $\sum_k \ln(1 - e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)})^{-1} < \infty$. For $\mu < \min_k \epsilon_k$ with a discrete spectrum tending to infinity, the sum converges; the partition function diverges as $\mu \to \min_k \epsilon_k$ from below, signalling Bose-Einstein condensation.

- The free Bose gas at chemical potential $\mu > \min_k \epsilon_k$ is **not** a state on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ — the formal partition function is infinite. The thermodynamic limit places the condensate in a coherent state of the lowest mode, and the relevant representation is no longer the standard Fock representation but a Bogoliubov-transformed one (Bratteli-Robinson Vol. II §5.2.5 [ref: TODO_REF Bratteli-Robinson Vol. II]).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (canonical commutation relations on Fock space; Cook 1953, [ref: TODO_REF Cook 1953]).** *Let $\mathcal{H}$ be a complex separable Hilbert space. The creation and annihilation operators $a^*(f), a(g)$ on the bosonic Fock space $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ satisfy*
$$
[a(f),\, a^*(g)] = \langle f, g \rangle\, \mathbf{1}, \qquad [a(f),\, a(g)] = [a^*(f),\, a^*(g)] = 0,
$$
*on the finite-particle subspace $\mathcal{F}_s^{\mathrm{fin}}(\mathcal{H})$, where $\mathbf{1}$ is the identity operator. Furthermore, the number operator $N = \sum_k a^*(e_k)\, a(e_k)$ for any orthonormal basis $\{e_k\}$ of $\mathcal{H}$ is independent of the basis and extends to a self-adjoint operator on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ with spectrum $\sigma(N) = \mathbb{N}_0$ and eigenspace $N^{-1}(\{n\}) = S^n \mathcal{H}$.*

**Proof.** Fix $f, g \in \mathcal{H}$ and $\xi = g_1 \vee \cdots \vee g_n \in S^n \mathcal{H}$. Apply $a^*(g)$ first:
$$
a^*(g) \xi = g \vee g_1 \vee \cdots \vee g_n \in S^{n+1} \mathcal{H}.
$$
Now apply $a(f)$. By the definition of annihilation as a sum over removals,
$$
a(f) a^*(g) \xi = \langle f, g \rangle\, g_1 \vee \cdots \vee g_n + \sum_{k=1}^{n} \langle f, g_k \rangle\, g \vee g_1 \vee \cdots \vee \widehat{g_k} \vee \cdots \vee g_n.
$$
The first term comes from removing the $g$ that was just inserted; the second sum comes from removing each of the original $g_k$'s, with $g$ left behind in the result.

Apply the operators in the opposite order:
$$
a(f) \xi = \sum_{k=1}^{n} \langle f, g_k \rangle\, g_1 \vee \cdots \vee \widehat{g_k} \vee \cdots \vee g_n,
$$
and $a^*(g)$ now inserts $g$ at the front of each term:
$$
a^*(g)\, a(f) \xi = \sum_{k=1}^{n} \langle f, g_k \rangle\, g \vee g_1 \vee \cdots \vee \widehat{g_k} \vee \cdots \vee g_n.
$$
Subtract: $a(f) a^*(g) \xi - a^*(g) a(f) \xi = \langle f, g \rangle\, g_1 \vee \cdots \vee g_n = \langle f, g \rangle\, \xi$. This proves the first CCR on the dense finite-particle subspace.

For $[a^*(f), a^*(g)] = 0$: both $a^*(f) a^*(g) \xi$ and $a^*(g) a^*(f) \xi$ equal $f \vee g \vee g_1 \vee \cdots \vee g_n$, since the symmetric product is unchanged by transposing two factors. For $[a(f), a(g)] = 0$: both $a(f) a(g) \xi$ and $a(g) a(f) \xi$ produce the same double sum
$$
\sum_{j \neq k} \langle g, g_j \rangle \langle f, g_k \rangle\, g_1 \vee \cdots \vee \widehat{g_j} \vee \cdots \vee \widehat{g_k} \vee \cdots \vee g_n,
$$
with the role of $f$ and $g$ exchanged in the inner products. The two double sums coincide after relabelling, so the commutator vanishes.

For the number operator: choose an orthonormal basis $\{e_k\}$ of $\mathcal{H}$ and consider $N = \sum_k a^*(e_k) a(e_k)$. On the symmetric tensor $e_{k_1} \vee \cdots \vee e_{k_n}$ (with repetition allowed), the operator $a^*(e_k) a(e_k)$ counts the number of $e_k$-factors. Summing over $k$ counts the total number of factors, which is $n$. So $N$ acts on $S^n \mathcal{H}$ by multiplication by $n$, basis-independent. Self-adjointness follows from the spectral-theorem packaging of the orthogonal direct sum decomposition $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}) = \bigoplus_n S^n \mathcal{H}$, with $N$ diagonal with real eigenvalues $n$ on each summand. The spectrum is pure point and equals $\mathbb{N}_0$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This Fock-space CCR algebra builds toward every stat-mech / QFT application that follows, and appears again in [08.10.03] as the substrate on which the Wick theorem and the Dyson series are proved. The foundational reason the construction works is exactly the symmetric tensor power: $S^n \mathcal{H}$ is the space of indistinguishable $n$-boson states, the eigenspace $N = n$, and the creation operator lifts an $n$-state to an $(n+1)$-state by symmetric adjunction with no further input. The central insight is that the many-body Hilbert space and the many-body free dynamics are determined by the one-particle data alone — the procedure $\mathcal{H} \mapsto \mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ and $h \mapsto d\Gamma(h)$ is functorial in the unitary group of $\mathcal{H}$. Putting these together, second quantisation generalises the Schrödinger picture of one particle to the same picture for indefinitely many indistinguishable particles, and it is dual to the path-integral / Gaussian-measure picture developed in [08.07.01] under the Wick rotation of [08.09.01]. The bridge is that the Euclidean Gaussian measure of [08.06.01] reproduces the same generating functional of correlation functions as the vacuum expectation of normal-ordered field products on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ — operator-side and measure-side computations of the same physics.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (Reed-Simon N-bound; Reed & Simon Vol. II §X.7 [ref: TODO_REF Reed-Simon Vol. II]).** *For $f \in \mathcal{H}$ and $\xi \in D(N^{1/2})$,*
$$
\|a(f) \xi\| \leq \|f\|\, \|(N + 1)^{1/2} \xi\|, \qquad \|a^*(f) \xi\| \leq \|f\|\, \|(N + 1)^{1/2} \xi\|.
$$
*Consequently $a(f), a^*(f)$ extend to closed operators on the natural domain $D(N^{1/2})$, and the symmetric field operator $\phi(f) = (a(f) + a^*(f))/\sqrt{2}$ is essentially self-adjoint on the finite-particle subspace whenever $f \in \mathcal{H}$ is real (in a Hermitian-conjugation-friendly real structure on $\mathcal{H}$).*

The N-bound is the technical workhorse: every estimate on Fock space that controls a creation or annihilation operator by the number operator goes through this inequality. The essential self-adjointness of the symmetric field operator is the content needed to exponentiate $\phi(f)$ to the bounded unitary Weyl operator $W(f) = e^{i \phi(f)}$, and the Weyl operators satisfy the bounded $C^*$-algebraic version of the CCR — the gateway to the Bratteli-Robinson treatment of free Bose-gas thermal states.

**Theorem (second quantisation of one-particle dynamics; Cook 1953 [ref: TODO_REF Cook 1953]).** *Let $h$ be a self-adjoint operator on $\mathcal{H}$ with $h \geq 0$. The many-body free Hamiltonian $H_0 := d\Gamma(h)$ is self-adjoint and non-negative on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ with the finite-particle subspace as a core. The unitary group $\Gamma(e^{-i t h})$ generated by $H_0$ acts on creation operators by Heisenberg evolution: for every $f \in \mathcal{H}$ and $t \in \mathbb{R}$,*
$$
e^{i t H_0}\, a^*(f)\, e^{-i t H_0} = a^*(e^{i t h} f).
$$
*The corresponding identity holds for $a(f)$ with $f$ replaced by $e^{-ith} f$ on the right.*

This is the operational meaning of second quantisation as a functor: the one-particle dynamics $e^{-ith}$ on $\mathcal{H}$ is promoted to the many-body dynamics $e^{-i t H_0}$ on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ by acting separately on every one-particle factor, and the creation operators intertwine the two dynamics with no extra structure required.

**Theorem (grand-canonical free Bose gas; Bratteli-Robinson Vol. II §5.2.4 [ref: TODO_REF Bratteli-Robinson Vol. II]).** *Let $h$ be a self-adjoint operator on $\mathcal{H}$ with $\sigma(h) = \{\epsilon_k\}_{k \geq 1}$ a discrete spectrum and $\inf_k \epsilon_k > 0$. For $\beta > 0$ and $\mu < \min_k \epsilon_k$ the grand-canonical density matrix*
$$
\rho_{\beta, \mu} := \frac{1}{\Xi} e^{-\beta (d\Gamma(h) - \mu N)}, \qquad \Xi := \mathrm{tr}_{\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})}\bigl(e^{-\beta (d\Gamma(h) - \mu N)}\bigr) = \prod_{k} \frac{1}{1 - e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)}}
$$
*is a positive trace-class operator with trace one. Its expectation of the number operator is*
$$
\langle N \rangle_{\beta, \mu} = \sum_{k} \frac{1}{e^{\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)} - 1},
$$
*the Bose-Einstein distribution summed over one-particle modes.*

The Fock-space construction is what makes the grand-canonical ensemble computationally tractable: the partition function factors over one-particle modes, each contributing the geometric series $(1 - e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)})^{-1}$. The thermodynamic limit (continuous spectrum) gives the integral form $\langle N \rangle / V = \int (d^{d-1} k / (2\pi)^{d-1}) / (e^{\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)} - 1)$, which exhibits Bose-Einstein condensation as $\mu \to \min_k \epsilon_k$ from below: the integral remains finite (with a singularity at $k = 0$ controlled in $d > 2$), and the macroscopic occupation of the ground mode appears as a separate condensate term outside the integral.

**Theorem (mode expansion and the free scalar field; Folland Ch. 5 [ref: TODO_REF Folland Tourist Guide]).** *Let $m > 0$ and let $\mathcal{H}_m := L^2(X_m, d\lambda_m)$ be the relativistic one-particle Hilbert space of positive-energy mass-$m$ solutions on the mass shell $X_m \subset \mathbb{R}^{1,d-1}$. The operator-valued tempered distribution*
$$
\phi(x) = \int \frac{d^{d-1} k}{(2\pi)^{d-1} 2\omega_k} \bigl[ a(\mathbf{k}) e^{-i k \cdot x} + a^*(\mathbf{k}) e^{i k \cdot x} \bigr]
$$
*on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}_m)$, with $k \cdot x = \omega_k x^0 - \mathbf{k} \cdot \mathbf{x}$ and $\omega_k = (|\mathbf{k}|^2 + m^2)^{1/2}$, satisfies the Wightman axioms for a free scalar field of mass $m$: it is Poincaré-covariant under the natural Fock-space representation of the Poincaré group, satisfies local commutativity $[\phi(x), \phi(y)] = 0$ for $x - y$ spacelike, and has the vacuum $\Omega$ as a cyclic and Poincaré-invariant vector.*

This is the bridge from the Fock-space construction to relativistic QFT proper. Wigner's classification of relativistic one-particle Hilbert spaces by mass and spin [ref: TODO_REF Wigner 1939] supplies the input $\mathcal{H}_m$, and the Fock promotion to $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}_m)$ delivers the free-field theory. The construction is the unique covariant operator-valued tempered distribution with the given two-point function $\langle \Omega, \phi(x) \phi(y) \Omega \rangle = \int (d^{d-1} k / (2\pi)^{d-1} 2\omega_k) e^{-i k \cdot (x - y)}$ — Streater-Wightman's reconstruction theorem applied to the free case.

**Theorem (Gaussian-Fock correspondence; Glimm-Jaffe Ch. 6 [ref: TODO_REF Glimm-Jaffe]).** *Let $C := (-\Delta_E + m^2)^{-1}$ be the Euclidean Green function in $d$ dimensions and $d\mu_C$ the centred Gaussian measure on $\mathcal{S}'(\mathbb{R}^d)$ with covariance $C$ (the free scalar field measure of [08.06.01]). The generating functional of vacuum expectations of normal-ordered field products on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}_m)$ equals, after Wick rotation, the moment-generating functional of the Gaussian measure $d\mu_C$:*
$$
\langle \Omega, e^{i \phi(f)} \Omega \rangle_{\mathrm{Mink}} \xrightarrow{\mathrm{Wick}} \int e^{i \phi(f_E)} d\mu_C(\phi) = e^{-\frac{1}{2} \langle f_E, C f_E \rangle}.
$$
*The right-hand side is the standard Gaussian characteristic function; the equivalence holds for every test function $f_E \in \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^d)$ and identifies the operator-side and measure-side computations of free-field correlators.*

The Gaussian-Fock correspondence is the bridge between the operator algebraic and the path-integral / measure-theoretic formulations of free-field theory. It is the reason Parisi-Wu stochastic quantisation [ref: TODO_REF Parisi-Wu 1981] — which produces $d\mu_C$ as the equilibrium of a Langevin equation in a fictitious fifth time — generates the same physics as the canonical Fock-space construction. Stat-mech-flavoured perturbation theory in [08.07.01] takes the right-hand side as primitive; canonical Fock-space perturbation theory in [08.10.03] takes the left.

**Theorem (Stone-von Neumann uniqueness; von Neumann 1931 [ref: TODO_REF von Neumann 1931]).** *Up to unitary equivalence, the irreducible representation of the canonical commutation relations on a finite-dimensional one-particle space $\mathcal{H} = \mathbb{C}^n$ is unique, and is the Fock representation $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathbb{C}^n)$ with the standard creation and annihilation operators. The Weyl form of the CCR — $W(f) W(g) = e^{-i \mathrm{Im} \langle f, g \rangle} W(f + g)$ for bounded unitaries $W(f)$ — extends the statement to a generator-side classification: a strongly continuous irreducible representation of the Weyl algebra on a finite-dimensional symplectic space is unitarily equivalent to the Schrödinger / Fock representation.*

Stone-von Neumann is a one-mode-at-a-time theorem; on an infinite-dimensional one-particle Hilbert space, infinitely many inequivalent CCR representations exist (Haag's theorem [ref: TODO_REF Haag 1955 unstated]: the interacting and free vacuums live in inequivalent representations). For the stat-mech / QFT applications of the present unit, the Fock representation is the canonical choice and the uniqueness fails only at the **thermodynamic limit** — finite-volume free Bose gas at $\mu < \min_k \epsilon_k$ lives on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$, and the infinite-volume limit at fixed density requires a Bogoliubov-transformed representation, the natural setting of Bratteli-Robinson Vol. II §5.2.5.

**Synthesis.** The bosonic Fock space packages the symmetric tensor algebra into a functor $\mathcal{H} \mapsto \mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ that lifts one-particle quantum mechanics to many-body quantum statistical mechanics and to quantum field theory without further input, and this is exactly the foundational reason every free-field calculation that follows can be done. The central insight is that creation and annihilation operators on the symmetric tensor power satisfy the canonical commutation relation $[a(f), a^*(g)] = \langle f, g \rangle$, the algebraic content of bosonic indistinguishability, and the number operator $N = d\Gamma(\mathbf{1})$ is the rung-counter of the Fock-space ladder. Putting these together, second quantisation generalises the Schrödinger picture of one particle to the many-body picture of indefinitely many indistinguishable particles, and it is dual to the path-integral / Gaussian-measure picture: the Wightman two-point function on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}_m)$ Wick-rotates to the Euclidean Gaussian-free-field covariance $(-\Delta_E + m^2)^{-1}$ of [08.06.01], and the Fock-space normal-ordered moments coincide with the Gaussian measure moments via Isserlis-Wick. The bridge is that operator-side and measure-side computations agree on every observable a free theory exposes: the Bose distribution comes out of the trace formula and out of the saddle-point of the Gaussian functional; the Klein-Gordon mode expansion comes out of the Fock-space construction and out of the spectral resolution of the Euclidean two-point function; the partition function comes out of the geometric series and out of the Gaussian integral.

This functorial picture generalises in two complementary directions. On the operator side, replacing the symmetric tensor power by the antisymmetric one gives **fermionic Fock space** — the antisymmetric algebra $\Lambda^n \mathcal{H}$ in place of $S^n \mathcal{H}$, anticommutators $\{a(f), a^*(g)\} = \langle f, g \rangle$ in place of commutators, and the Pauli exclusion principle as the bound $a^*(f)^2 = 0$. The same functorial second-quantisation $d\Gamma$ lifts one-particle dynamics, and the same thermal trace gives the Fermi-Dirac distribution. The bridge is that bosonic and fermionic Fock spaces are dual under the spin-statistics correspondence, with the Pauli exclusion of fermions and the unbounded occupation of bosons as the two faces of one statistical-mechanics framework. On the measure side, the Gaussian free-field measure on $\mathcal{S}'(\mathbb{R}^d)$ generalises to non-Gaussian measures with weight $e^{-V[\phi]} d\mu_C$ for an interaction $V$; the Fock-space side correspondingly develops the Dyson series of [08.10.03] with interaction Hamiltonian $H_I = V(\phi)$ normal-ordered with respect to the free vacuum. The bridge between the two sides is the Wick rotation of [08.09.01], and the bridge identifies operator-side Feynman diagrams with measure-side moment expansions in one universal perturbative language.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (factorisation of the Fock space over orthogonal direct sums).** *For Hilbert spaces $\mathcal{H}_1, \mathcal{H}_2$ with $\mathcal{H} = \mathcal{H}_1 \oplus \mathcal{H}_2$, there is a canonical unitary isomorphism*
$$
\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}_1 \oplus \mathcal{H}_2) \cong \mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}_1) \otimes \mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}_2).
$$

*Under this isomorphism, $a(f_1 \oplus f_2) = a(f_1) \otimes \mathbf{1} + \mathbf{1} \otimes a(f_2)$ and similarly for $a^*$; the vacuum $\Omega_{\mathcal{H}}$ corresponds to $\Omega_{\mathcal{H}_1} \otimes \Omega_{\mathcal{H}_2}$.*

**Proof.** The symmetric tensor power factors via the multinomial expansion: for $n \geq 0$,
$$
S^n(\mathcal{H}_1 \oplus \mathcal{H}_2) \cong \bigoplus_{n_1 + n_2 = n} S^{n_1} \mathcal{H}_1 \otimes S^{n_2} \mathcal{H}_2.
$$
The isomorphism on the right is the symmetrisation of the natural inclusion $\mathcal{H}_1^{\otimes n_1} \otimes \mathcal{H}_2^{\otimes n_2} \hookrightarrow (\mathcal{H}_1 \oplus \mathcal{H}_2)^{\otimes n}$ followed by the bijection of orthogonal direct sums. Summing over $n$:
$$
\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}_1 \oplus \mathcal{H}_2) = \bigoplus_{n \geq 0} S^n(\mathcal{H}_1 \oplus \mathcal{H}_2) \cong \bigoplus_{n_1, n_2 \geq 0} S^{n_1} \mathcal{H}_1 \otimes S^{n_2} \mathcal{H}_2 = \mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}_1) \otimes \mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}_2).
$$
The action of $a(f_1 \oplus f_2)$ on $\xi_1 \vee \cdots \vee \xi_n$ (with $\xi_j \in \mathcal{H}_1 \oplus \mathcal{H}_2$) is a sum over removals; sorting the removals by whether they come from the $\mathcal{H}_1$-component or the $\mathcal{H}_2$-component decomposes the operator as $a(f_1) \otimes \mathbf{1} + \mathbf{1} \otimes a(f_2)$. The same decomposition applies to $a^*$. The vacuum identification is immediate from $S^0 = \mathbb{C}$. $\square$

The factorisation Proposition is the proof underlying the multi-mode partition-function formula $\Xi = \prod_k (1 - e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)})^{-1}$ in the Bose-gas computation: the trace over the full Fock space factors as the product of traces over single-mode Fock spaces, exactly because the Fock space itself factors as the tensor product of one-mode Fock spaces over an orthonormal basis of $\mathcal{H}$.

**Proposition (the Reed-Simon N-bound).** *For every $f \in \mathcal{H}$ and every $\xi \in D(N^{1/2})$,*
$$
\|a(f) \xi\|^2 \leq \|f\|^2\, \|N^{1/2} \xi\|^2, \qquad \|a^*(f) \xi\|^2 \leq \|f\|^2\, \|(N + 1)^{1/2} \xi\|^2.
$$

**Proof.** Decompose $\xi = (\xi_n)_n$ with $\xi_n \in S^n \mathcal{H}$. For $\xi = g_1 \vee \cdots \vee g_n \in S^n \mathcal{H}$,
$$
\|a(f) \xi\|^2 = \Bigl\| \sum_{k=1}^{n} \langle f, g_k \rangle\, g_1 \vee \cdots \vee \widehat{g_k} \vee \cdots \vee g_n \Bigr\|^2.
$$
Apply Cauchy-Schwarz on the sum: $\|a(f) \xi\|^2 \leq n \sum_{k=1}^{n} |\langle f, g_k \rangle|^2 \|g_1 \vee \cdots \vee \widehat{g_k} \vee \cdots \vee g_n\|^2$. Bound $|\langle f, g_k \rangle|^2 \leq \|f\|^2 \|g_k\|^2$. The symmetrised norm of the omission satisfies the inductive identity $\|g_1 \vee \cdots \vee \widehat{g_k} \vee \cdots \vee g_n\|^2 = \|\xi\|^2 / (n \|g_k\|^2)$ on average over $k$. Putting these together with the orthogonality of distinct $S^n$ summands yields $\|a(f) \xi\|^2 \leq n \|f\|^2 \|\xi\|^2$. Summing over $n$ with $\xi = (\xi_n)$ gives $\|a(f) \xi\|^2 \leq \|f\|^2 \sum_n n \|\xi_n\|^2 = \|f\|^2 \|N^{1/2} \xi\|^2$. The bound for $a^*(f)$ is analogous, replacing $n$ by $n + 1$ in the level-shift. $\square$

The N-bound formalises the unboundedness of $a(f), a^*(f)$ as a controlled growth in $N$. The same proof, applied to $a(f)^* a(f) = $ a sum over double removals, gives the second-quantisation bound $\|d\Gamma(h) \xi\| \leq \|h\| \|N \xi\|$ for a bounded $h$ — the substrate for the perturbation theory of $H_0 = d\Gamma(h)$ in the Fock-space picture.

**Proposition (grand-canonical free Bose gas partition function).** *Let $h$ be self-adjoint on $\mathcal{H}$ with discrete spectrum $\{\epsilon_k\}_{k \geq 1}$, $\inf_k \epsilon_k > 0$, and $\sum_k e^{-\beta \epsilon_k} < \infty$ for some $\beta > 0$. For $\mu < \min_k \epsilon_k$ the operator $e^{-\beta(d\Gamma(h) - \mu N)}$ is trace class on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})$ with*
$$
\Xi = \mathrm{tr}_{\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H})}\bigl( e^{-\beta(d\Gamma(h) - \mu N)} \bigr) = \prod_k \frac{1}{1 - e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)}}.
$$

**Proof.** Diagonalise $h$ in an orthonormal eigenbasis $\{e_k\}$ with $h e_k = \epsilon_k e_k$. By the factorisation Proposition, $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}) = \bigotimes_k \mathcal{F}_s(\mathbb{C} e_k)$, and the Hamiltonian $K := d\Gamma(h) - \mu N$ acts on the $k$-th factor as $(\epsilon_k - \mu) a_k^* a_k$. The full Boltzmann operator factors as $e^{-\beta K} = \bigotimes_k e^{-\beta (\epsilon_k - \mu) a_k^* a_k}$, and the trace factors:
$$
\Xi = \prod_k \mathrm{tr}_{\mathcal{F}_s(\mathbb{C} e_k)}\bigl( e^{-\beta (\epsilon_k - \mu) a_k^* a_k} \bigr).
$$
Each single-mode trace is computed on the orthonormal basis $\{|n\rangle_k\}_{n \geq 0}$ of $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathbb{C} e_k)$ as $\sum_{n \geq 0} e^{-\beta n (\epsilon_k - \mu)} = (1 - e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)})^{-1}$, the geometric series convergent under $\epsilon_k > \mu$. The infinite product converges by the assumption $\sum_k e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)} < \infty$ (which gives $\sum_k \ln(1 - e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)})^{-1} \leq c \sum_k e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)} < \infty$). Trace-class property of $e^{-\beta K}$ follows from the convergence of the product of trace-norms. $\square$

The factored product is the operational target of the bosonic Fock space in equilibrium statistical mechanics: every free-boson thermodynamic quantity — internal energy, average occupation, specific heat — is computed by differentiating $\ln \Xi$ with respect to $\beta$ or $\mu$, and the differentiations commute with the infinite product mode by mode.

**Proposition (Heisenberg evolution of creation operators under $d\Gamma(h)$).** *Let $h$ be self-adjoint on $\mathcal{H}$. For every $f \in \mathcal{H}$ and $t \in \mathbb{R}$,*
$$
e^{i t d\Gamma(h)}\, a^*(f)\, e^{-i t d\Gamma(h)} = a^*(e^{i t h} f).
$$

**Proof.** Both sides are densely defined on the finite-particle subspace; show they agree there. For $\xi = g_1 \vee \cdots \vee g_n \in S^n \mathcal{H}$, the action of $\Gamma(U) := e^{-i t d\Gamma(h)}$ with $U = e^{-i t h}$ is $\Gamma(U)(g_1 \vee \cdots \vee g_n) = (U g_1) \vee \cdots \vee (U g_n)$ (by definition of the functor). Apply $a^*(f) e^{-i t d\Gamma(h)} \xi = a^*(f)(U g_1 \vee \cdots \vee U g_n) = f \vee U g_1 \vee \cdots \vee U g_n$. Apply $e^{i t d\Gamma(h)}$ to this, with $U^{-1} = e^{i t h}$:
$$
e^{i t d\Gamma(h)} (f \vee U g_1 \vee \cdots \vee U g_n) = (U^{-1} f) \vee (U^{-1} U g_1) \vee \cdots \vee (U^{-1} U g_n) = (e^{i t h} f) \vee g_1 \vee \cdots \vee g_n = a^*(e^{i t h} f) \xi.
$$
Hence the identity on a dense set, and by continuity on the full domain. $\square$

The Heisenberg evolution formula is what makes the mode expansion of the free Klein-Gordon field a consistent operator-valued tempered distribution: the time-translation generator $H_0 = d\Gamma(h)$ with $h = (m^2 - \nabla^2)^{1/2}$ on $\mathcal{H}_m$ acts on the plane-wave creation operator $a^*(\mathbf{k})$ by multiplication by the phase $e^{i \omega_k t}$, exactly matching the explicit time-dependence in the mode expansion.

## Connections [Master]

- **φ⁴ theory and the Dyson series [08.10.03].** The downstream stat-mech / QFT unit on the Dyson series uses the bosonic Fock space as the substrate on which Wick's theorem and the time-ordered exponential are proved. The interaction Hamiltonian $H_I = (\lambda / 4!) \int : \phi^4 : dx$ is normal-ordered with respect to the free Fock vacuum constructed here, and the perturbative expansion of $\langle \Omega, T(\phi(x_1) \cdots \phi(x_n)) \Omega \rangle$ reduces to a sum over Feynman diagrams with edges given by the two-point function computed in Exercise 8.

- **Gaussian field theory and free boson [08.06.01].** The Gaussian free-field measure on $\mathcal{S}'(\mathbb{R}^d)$ with covariance $C = (-\Delta_E + m^2)^{-1}$ is the Euclidean dual of the Fock-space construction here. Vacuum expectations of normal-ordered field products on $\mathcal{F}_s(\mathcal{H}_m)$ Wick-rotate to the Gaussian moments of $d\mu_C$ via Isserlis-Wick, and the two computations agree on every free-field observable.

- **Path integral formulation of statistical mechanics [08.07.01].** The path-integral / functional-measure framework uses the Gaussian free-field measure as its starting point; the present Fock-space unit supplies the operator-side counterpart. The thermal trace $\mathrm{tr}(e^{-\beta H_0})$ over the bosonic Fock space equals, after Wick rotation, the partition function of the periodic Euclidean field on $S^1_\beta \times \mathbb{R}^{d-1}$, and the equivalence is the operator-side / path-integral-side bridge of finite-temperature QFT.

- **Quantum-classical correspondence (Wick rotation) [08.09.01].** The Minkowski-side and Euclidean-side computations of free-field observables are related by Wick rotation, and the present unit is the canonical Minkowski-side construction. The mode expansion's time-dependence $e^{-i \omega_k t}$ continues to $e^{- \omega_k \tau}$ under $t \to -i \tau$, producing the heat-kernel form of the Euclidean two-point function and identifying the operator-side Fock-space picture with the measure-side Gaussian-free-field picture.

- **Bosonic Fock space and second quantisation [12.13.01].** The quantum-mechanics-side companion unit develops the same construction with an analytic / functional-analytic emphasis: Stone-von Neumann uniqueness, the Bargmann-Fock holomorphic representation, the Weyl-algebra $C^*$-formulation. The present stat-mech / QFT-side unit takes the same algebraic substrate and emphasises the operational uses — the free Bose gas partition function, the Klein-Gordon mode expansion, the Gaussian-Fock correspondence. Read in parallel for a complete picture; cite the QM-side unit for the foundational uniqueness theorems and the holomorphic representation, cite the present unit for the stat-mech / QFT applications.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Second quantisation was introduced by Paul Dirac in his 1927 paper *The quantum theory of the emission and absorption of radiation* (Proc. Roy. Soc. A 114, 243) [ref: TODO_REF Dirac 1927]. Dirac quantised the radiation field by treating each Fourier mode of the electromagnetic vector potential as an independent quantum harmonic oscillator, with creation and annihilation operators raising and lowering the photon occupation number. The "second" in second quantisation is a misnomer — there is no first quantisation being quantised again — but it stuck after Pascual Jordan and Oskar Klein's contemporaneous *Zum Mehrkörperproblem der Quantentheorie* (Z. Phys. 45, 751) [ref: TODO_REF Jordan-Klein 1927] used the same creation-annihilation formalism to package the many-body Schrödinger equation for indistinguishable bosons. The combinatorial substrate had already been built by Bose's 1924 letter to Einstein on Planck's law (Z. Phys. 26, 178) [ref: TODO_REF Bose 1924] and Einstein's two-paper extension to massive bosons (Sitzungsber. Preuss. Akad. Wiss. Berlin 1924, 1925) [ref: TODO_REF Einstein 1924], which introduced what is now called Bose-Einstein statistics by counting indistinguishable configurations.

Vladimir Fock's 1932 paper *Konfigurationsraum und zweite Quantelung* (Z. Phys. 75, 622) [ref: TODO_REF Fock 1932] packaged the symmetric tensor algebra as the multi-particle Hilbert space — what is now called Fock space. The mathematical rigorisation came two decades later in Joseph Cook's 1953 *Trans. Amer. Math. Soc.* paper *The mathematics of second quantization* (74, 222) [ref: TODO_REF Cook 1953], which established the bosonic Fock space as a Hilbert direct sum of symmetric tensor powers, the creation and annihilation operators as densely defined unbounded operators on the finite-particle subspace, and the canonical commutation relations as identities on the appropriate domain. Cook's construction is the version that survives in modern functional-analytic accounts — Michael Reed and Barry Simon's *Methods of Modern Mathematical Physics*, Vol. II (Academic Press, 1975, §X.7) [ref: TODO_REF Reed-Simon Vol. II], James Glimm and Arthur Jaffe's *Quantum Physics: A Functional Integral Point of View* (Springer, 1987) [ref: TODO_REF Glimm-Jaffe], Ola Bratteli and Derek Robinson's *Operator Algebras and Quantum Statistical Mechanics*, Vol. II (Springer, 1997) [ref: TODO_REF Bratteli-Robinson Vol. II].

The stat-mech-flavoured applications developed in parallel. Lev Landau and Evgeny Lifshitz's *Statistical Physics, Part 1*, Vol. 5 of the *Course of Theoretical Physics* (3rd ed., Pergamon, 1980) [ref: TODO_REF Landau-Lifshitz Stat Phys 1] presents the occupation-number representation as the practical calculational tool for the grand-canonical ensemble of Bose and Fermi gases, with the partition function $\Xi = \prod_k (1 \mp e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)})^{\mp 1}$ (upper sign Bose, lower sign Fermi) as the operational target. The QFT-flavoured applications also developed in parallel, with Eugene Wigner's 1939 *Ann. Math.* paper *On unitary representations of the inhomogeneous Lorentz group* (40, 149) [ref: TODO_REF Wigner 1939] classifying the relativistic one-particle Hilbert spaces by mass and spin, then Streater-Wightman's *PCT, Spin and Statistics, and All That* (Princeton, 1964) packaging the resulting Fock-space free field as the canonical free Wightman theory. The path-integral / Gaussian-measure side, opened by Feynman in the 1940s and rigorised by Glimm-Jaffe in the 1970s, identifies the Fock-space vacuum expectation with the moment generating functional of the Euclidean Gaussian free-field measure, a duality made operationally explicit by Parisi and Wu's 1981 stochastic-quantisation paper *Perturbation theory without gauge fixing* (Sci. Sin. 24, 483) [ref: TODO_REF Parisi-Wu 1981]. The bosonic Fock space is the common substrate of all three frameworks: canonical operator-side QFT, Euclidean measure-theoretic QFT, and stochastic-quantisation QFT.
