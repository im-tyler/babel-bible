## Intuition [Beginner]

A leaf faces a dilemma baked into physics. To make sugar it must take in carbon dioxide from the air, and the only door is a pore — the stoma — that also lets water vapour escape. Every time a plant opens its pores to feed, it bleeds water. The whole story of how plants survive deserts, farms, and rainforests is a story of how they manage that door.

Evolution found three answers. The simplest, called C3, opens the door and runs the basic sugar-making cycle directly; most trees, wheat, and rice work this way. A second group, the C4 plants (maize, sugarcane, sorghum), bolt on a pump that concentrates carbon dioxide where it is needed, so the leaf can keep working with the door nearly shut in the heat. A third group, the CAM plants (cacti, pineapple, orchids), opens the door only at night and stores carbon dioxide until daylight returns.

The payoff is water. A C3 plant loses several hundred grams of water for every gram of carbon it fixes; a C4 plant loses far less; a CAM plant less still. The cost is energy, because the pump and the night shift each demand extra fuel. So the three pathways are one trade-off between saving water and spending energy — and that trade-off decides which plant grows where.

## Visual [Beginner]

| Pathway | Where CO2 first fixed | When stomata open | Water lost per g carbon | Typical plants |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| C3 | Inside the leaf, by Rubisco | Day | ~400–1000 g | Wheat, rice, soy, trees |
| C4 | Mesophyll, by PEP carboxylase; refixed in the bundle sheath | Day | ~250–350 g | Maize, sugarcane, sorghum |
| CAM | Same cells, by PEP carboxylase in the dark | Night | ~50–100 g | Cacti, pineapple, orchids |

The defining picture contrasts two engineering strategies. In C4 (left), carbon dioxide is captured in the outer mesophyll cells, packed into a four-carbon acid, shipped inward to the bundle-sheath cells, and released there at high concentration behind nearly closed pores. In CAM (right), the same pump runs in the same cell but in time rather than space: pores open at night to collect carbon dioxide into malic acid, then close for the day while the stored carbon is converted to sugar in the heat.

![C3/C4/CAM pathway comparison: spatial versus temporal carbon dioxide concentration, and the stomatal water trade-off](/img/photosynthesis-pathways-water-relations-placeholder.svg)

A third panel ties the biochemistry back to water: a single stoma flanked by two guard cells, wired to a drought sensor. When the roots run dry, the hormone abscisic acid travels to the guard cells and commands them to deflate, closing the pore. That signal is what lets a C4 leaf keep its pores narrow at noon and a CAM leaf shut them entirely through the day.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Three plants stand side by side in the same hot field. A C3 soybean loses about $500$ grams of water for each gram of carbon it fixes; a C4 maize plant loses about $250$ grams; a CAM pineapple about $80$ grams. If each fixes $10$ grams of carbon in a day, the soybean gives up $10 \times 500 = 5000$ grams of water, the maize $10 \times 250 = 2500$ grams, and the pineapple $10 \times 80 = 800$ grams. What this tells us: the C4 plant makes the same sugar for half the water, and the CAM plant for a sixth — the dividend of concentrating carbon dioxide.

Here is the CAM trick in numbers. A pineapple leaf holds about $100$ micromoles of malic acid per gram at dawn, after a night of fixing carbon with its pores open. Through the day the pores close and the acid falls to about $10$ micromoles by dusk, the missing carbon flowing into sugar. The drop of $100 - 10 = 90$ micromoles per gram is the day's carbon dividend, earned while the pores stayed shut against the heat.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The three photosynthetic pathways share a common engine — the Calvin–Benson cycle and its carboxylating enzyme Rubisco — and differ in how they deliver carbon dioxide to that engine and how they defend its substrate supply against the competing reaction of Rubisco with oxygen. The formalisation below follows Taiz et al. [ref: TODO_REF Taiz2015] and Nobel [ref: TODO_REF Nobel2009].

### The C3 pathway and the Calvin–Benson cycle

In C3 photosynthesis the first stable product of carbon-dioxide fixation is a three-carbon compound, 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PGA), formed when Rubisco (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase) carboxylates ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate (RuBP). The stoichiometry per molecule of carbon dioxide fixed and one molecule of RuBP regenerated is

$$
3\,\text{CO}_2 \;+\; 9\,\text{ATP} \;+\; 6\,\text{NADPH} \;+\; 6\,\text{H}_2\text{O}
\;\longrightarrow\; 1\,\text{G3P} \;+\; 9\,\text{ADP} \;+\; 8\,\text{P}_i \;+\; 6\,\text{NADP}^+,
$$

so the per-carbon cost is $3$ ATP and $2$ NADPH. Rubisco also catalyses an oxygenase reaction: RuBP plus $\text{O}_2$ yields one molecule of 3-PGA and one of 2-phosphoglycolate, the latter processed by the photorespiratory (C2) cycle at an energetic cost of roughly $3.5$ ATP and $2$ NADPH per oxygenation, with release of one molecule of $\text{CO}_2$ that must be refixed. The carboxylation : oxygenation ratio at the active site is governed by the ratio of dissolved $\text{CO}_2$ to $\text{O}_2$ and by temperature, since the relative solubility of $\text{CO}_2$ falls faster than that of $\text{O}_2$ as temperature rises.

### The C4 pathway: the CO2-concentrating cycle

C4 photosynthesis [ref: TODO_REF HatchSlack1966] adds a biochemical pump upstream of the Calvin cycle. Initial fixation is performed in the mesophyll by **PEP carboxylase**, which uses bicarbonate rather than $\text{CO}_2$ and has no oxygenase:

$$
\text{PEP} + \text{HCO}_3^- \;\longrightarrow\; \text{oxaloacetate} + \text{P}_i.
$$

Oxaloacetate is converted to the four-carbon acid malate (or aspartate) and transported to the anatomically distinct **bundle-sheath** cells, where it is decarboxylated to release $\text{CO}_2$ at a concentration several-fold above atmospheric immediately at Rubisco. The residual three-carbon product (pyruvate, or alanine) returns to the mesophyll, where **pyruvate, $P_i$ dikinase (PPDK)** regenerates PEP at the cost of one ATP converted to AMP — equivalent to two ATP equivalents per $\text{CO}_2$. Hence the per-carbon cost of C4 is that of C3 plus an additional $2$ ATP:

$$
\text{C4 cost} \;=\; 5\,\text{ATP} \;+\; 2\,\text{NADPH} \;\;\text{per CO}_2\text{ fixed.}
$$

The concentrating effect raises the $\text{CO}_2$ at Rubisco from the C3 value of roughly $250\,\mu\mathrm{mol\,mol}^{-1}$ (intercellular $c_i$) to $1000$–$3000\,\mu\mathrm{mol\,mol}^{-1}$ in the bundle sheath, near-saturating Rubisco and suppressing photorespiration to a small leak. The associated leaf anatomy is **Kranz anatomy**: radiating rings of mesophyll around bundle-sheath cells enclosing each vein, providing the short diffusion path and low-permeability bundle-sheath walls that the spatial separation requires.

### The CAM pathway: temporal carbon concentration

Crassulacean acid metabolism runs the same PEP-carboxylase pump as C4 but in a single photosynthetic cell separated in time rather than space [ref: TODO_REF Taiz2015]. Stomata open at night; atmospheric $\text{CO}_2$ is fixed by PEP carboxylase into oxaloacetate and malate, which is stored in the vacuole, acidifying the cell to titratable-acidity values often above $100\,\mu\mathrm{eq\,g}^{-1}$ fresh mass at dawn. Through the day the stomata close, malate is decarboxylated (by NADP-ME, NAD-ME, or PEPCK depending on subtype), and the released $\text{CO}_2$ is refixed by the Calvin cycle behind closed stomata. The day-night acidity swing,

$$
\Delta H^+ \;=\; H^+_{\text{dawn}} \;-\; H^+_{\text{dusk}},
$$

is the operational diagnostic of CAM activity. The energetic cost equals that of C4 (an additional $\sim 2$ ATP per $\text{CO}_2$ for PEP regeneration), but the temporal separation decouples carbon gain from transpiration almost entirely, which is why CAM water-use efficiency exceeds C3 by a factor of three to six.

### Stomatal regulation and water-use efficiency

Instantaneous **water-use efficiency** is the ratio of net assimilation $A$ to transpiration $E$,

$$
W \;=\; \frac{A}{E} \;=\; \frac{g_t\,(c_a - c_c)}{g_s\,e/P} \;\approx\; \frac{P\,(c_a - c_c)}{e}\,\frac{g_t}{g_s},
$$

with total conductance $g_t$ (stomatal $g_s$ in series with boundary layer $g_b$ and mesophyll $g_m$), leaf-to-air vapour-pressure difference $e$, and atmospheric pressure $P$. Stomatal regulation closes the aperture (lowers $g_s$) under dry or hot air to limit $E$, at the price of lowering $c_c$ and hence $A$. A C4 leaf can operate at lower $g_s$ than a C3 leaf for the same $A$, because the bundle-sheath pump sustains $c_c$ at Rubisco even when intercellular $\text{CO}_2$ is depleted; this is the mechanistic origin of its higher $W$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **C4 does not "make more sugar per unit light" at all temperatures.** C4 carries a $2$-ATP surcharge per $\text{CO}_2$, so at low temperature and high $\text{CO}_2$, where photorespiration is negligible, C3 has the higher quantum yield. C4 wins only above the temperature and light thresholds at which the suppressed-photorespiration benefit exceeds the surcharge.
- **CAM is not a distinct sugar-making cycle.** It is the Calvin cycle with a temporally separated PEP-carboxylase pump in front of it. The Calvin cycle and Rubisco are still required.
- **PEP carboxylase does not replace Rubisco in C4.** Rubisco still performs the net carbon fixation; PEP carboxylase only concentrates the substrate. A C4 mutant lacking functional bundle-sheath Rubisco is lethal.
- **High water-use efficiency is not high productivity.** CAM plants are water-thrifty but slow-growing; the nocturnal pump and daytime closure cap maximum assimilation. Drought tolerance and yield are decoupled traits.

## Key mechanism [Intermediate+]

### The C4 ATP-surcharge mechanism and its temperature-dependent payoff

The C4 pathway is best read as a regulated investment: the leaf spends two extra ATP per $\text{CO}_2$ to suppress a loss that grows with temperature. Quantifying both sides of the ledger gives the precise conditions under which the investment pays.

**Proposition (C4 energetic break-even under photorespiration).** *Let the rate of carboxylation be $v_c$ and the rate of oxygenation be $v_o$, both catalysed by Rubisco. In C3 the net assimilation is*

$$
A_{C3} \;=\; v_c \;-\; 0.5\,v_o,
$$

*because the photorespiratory C2 cycle releases half a molecule of $\text{CO}_2$ per oxygenation. The total ATP cost of the C3 leaf is*

$$
\Theta_{C3} \;=\; 3\,v_c \;+\; 3.5\,v_o \quad (\text{ATP consumed per unit time}),
$$

*with $3$ ATP per carboxylation and $3.5$ ATP of photorespiratory overhead per oxygenation. In C4 the bundle-sheath $\text{CO}_2$ concentration suppresses $v_o$ to a near-vanishing leak $v_o^{C4} \ll v_o^{C3}$, and the pump adds a surcharge of $2$ ATP per $\text{CO}_2$ delivered, so*

$$
\Theta_{C4} \;=\; 5\,v_c \;+\; 3.5\,v_o^{C4}.
$$

*At high temperature, where $v_o/v_c$ in C3 approaches unity (so $A_{C3} \approx 0.5\,v_c$ and $\Theta_{C3} \approx 6.5\,v_c$), the C4 leaf achieves the same net assimilation at $\Theta_{C4} \approx 5\,v_c$, a lower ATP cost per net carbon fixed. At low temperature, where $v_o \to 0$ in C3 and $\Theta_{C3} \to 3\,v_c$, the C4 surcharge of $2$ ATP per $\text{CO}_2$ is uncompensated and C3 wins on energetic efficiency.*

**Derivation.** The carboxylation and oxygenation rates follow Michaelis–Menten competition at the Rubisco active site:

$$
v_c \;=\; \frac{V_c\,[\text{CO}_2]/K_c}{1 + [\text{CO}_2]/K_c + [\text{O}_2]/K_o}, \qquad
v_o \;=\; \frac{V_o\,[\text{O}_2]/K_o}{1 + [\text{CO}_2]/K_c + [\text{O}_2]/K_o},
$$

so the ratio $v_o/v_c = ([\text{O}_2]/K_o)/([\text{CO}_2]/K_c)$ is set by the substrate specificity factor and the dissolved-gas ratio at the active site. Because the Henry's-law solubility of $\text{CO}_2$ falls more steeply with temperature than that of $\text{O}_2$, the dissolved ratio $[\text{CO}_2]/[\text{O}_2]$ drops, and $v_o/v_c$ rises, with temperature. Net assimilation in C3 follows from the carbon-balance of the Calvin cycle plus the C2 cycle: each carboxylation yields one net carbon into 3-PGA, and each oxygenation yields 2-phosphoglycolate that the C2 cycle converts to one 3-PGA plus one released $\text{CO}_2$, i.e. a net loss of one carbon refixed at Calvin-cycle cost. Hence $A_{C3} = v_c - 0.5\,v_o$ (the conventional Farquhar–von Caemmerer–Berry formulation, where $0.5$ is the fraction of two oxygenation events whose carbon is lost as $\text{CO}_2$). The C3 energetic ledger sums $3$ ATP per carboxylation (the Calvin cycle) and $3.5$ ATP per oxygenation (photorespiratory recovery), giving $\Theta_{C3} = 3\,v_c + 3.5\,v_o$.

In C4 the bundle-sheath pump raises $[\text{CO}_2]$ at Rubisco from $\sim 250$ to $\sim 1500\,\mu\mathrm{mol\,mol}^{-1}$, so the same competition formula yields $v_o^{C4}/v_c \approx (v_o/v_c)_{C3}/6$, a sixfold suppression of oxygenation. Setting $v_o^{C4} \approx 0$ and adding the $2$-ATP PPDK surcharge per $\text{CO}_2$ fixed gives $\Theta_{C4} = 5\,v_c$. Comparing per unit net assimilation: at low temperature ($v_o \approx 0$), C3 needs $3\,v_c/(v_c) = 3$ ATP per net carbon while C4 needs $5$ ATP per net carbon — C3 is more efficient by two ATP. At high temperature ($v_o \approx v_c$), C3 nets only $0.5\,v_c$ of assimilation while spending $6.5\,v_c$ ATP, i.e. $13$ ATP per net carbon, whereas C4 still nets $\approx v_c$ at $5\,v_c$ ATP, i.e. $5$ ATP per net carbon — C4 is now more efficient by eight ATP per net carbon fixed. The crossover temperature at which the two ATP ledgers cross is the **quantum-yield break-even**, observed near $25$–$30\,^\circ\text{C}$ for the NADP-ME subtype. $\square$

**Bridge.** The C4 pump **builds toward** [19.03.01] the convergent evolution of the Kranz syndrome across sixty-six-plus independent lineages, and **appears again in** [18.12.01] the whole-plant gas-exchange budget where the two-ATP surcharge is repaid many times over in hot, bright, water-limited climates. **The foundational reason** C4 outperforms C3 in the heat is that raising bundle-sheath $\text{CO}_2$ competitively suppresses Rubisco's oxygenase; **this is exactly** the biochemical lever that CAM operates in time rather than in space, and **the central insight** is that both strategies buy water-use efficiency at the price of extra energy. **Putting these together**, the three photosynthetic pathways become a single trade-off curve between carbon gain and water loss, set by how much extra ATP a leaf is willing to spend to keep its stomata narrower.

The Rubisco carboxylation : oxygenation ratio in a C3 leaf at $35\,^\circ\text{C}$ is $v_o/v_c = 0.4$. Using the Farquhar–von Caemmerer–Berry relation $A = v_c - 0.5\,v_o$ and $v_c = 40\,\mu\mathrm{mol\,m^{-2}\,s^{-1}}$, compute the net assimilation $A$. Compare it with the gross carboxylation $v_c$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $v_o = 0.4\,v_c$, then subtract half of it from $v_c$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$v_o = 0.4 \times 40 = 16\,\mu\mathrm{mol\,m^{-2}\,s^{-1}}$. $A = 40 - 0.5 \times 16 = 40 - 8 = 32\,\mu\mathrm{mol\,m^{-2}\,s^{-1}}$. The gross carboxylation is $40$ but photorespiration throws away $8\,\mu\mathrm{mol\,m^{-2}\,s^{-1}}$ of freshly fixed carbon. A C4 leaf with $v_o \approx 0$ at the same $v_c$ would retain the full $40$ — the photorespiratory penalty that the C4 pump is built to suppress.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Bundle-sheath leakiness. Let PEP carboxylase in the mesophyll supply four-carbon acid to the bundle sheath at rate $V_p$ ($\mu\mathrm{mol\,m^{-2}\,s^{-1}}$), let $A$ be the true net assimilation (Calvin-cycle uptake in the bundle sheath), and let $\text{CO}_2$ leak back out of the bundle sheath at rate $L = g_L (c_B - c_M)$, where $c_B$ is the bundle-sheath $\text{CO}_2$ concentration, $c_M$ the mesophyll concentration, and $g_L$ the bundle-sheath conductance to $\text{CO}_2$. (a) Show that at steady state $V_p = A + L$. (b) Define leakiness $\phi = L/V_p$. Solve for $c_B$ in terms of $V_p$, $\phi$, $g_L$, and $c_M$. (c) With $V_p = 45$, $\phi = 0.25$, $g_L = 0.003$ (in consistent units giving $c_B$ in $\mu\mathrm{mol\,mol}^{-1}$), and $c_M = 150\,\mu\mathrm{mol\,mol}^{-1}$, compute $c_B$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

At steady state the carbon entering the bundle sheath as four-carbon acid (decarboxylated to $\text{CO}_2$ at rate $V_p$) must equal the carbon leaving by Calvin-cycle fixation ($A$) plus the $\text{CO}_2$ diffusing back out ($L$). Substitute $L = \phi V_p$ into $L = g_L(c_B - c_M)$ and solve.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) Carbon entering the bundle sheath (as decarboxylated malate, rate $V_p$) equals carbon fixed by the Calvin cycle ($A$) plus carbon leaking back to the mesophyll ($L$): at steady state $V_p = A + L$.

(b) With $L = \phi V_p$ and $L = g_L(c_B - c_M)$, solve:
$$
c_B \;=\; c_M \;+\; \frac{\phi\,V_p}{g_L}.
$$
This is the CO2-concentration formula: the bundle-sheath $\text{CO}_2$ rises with the PEP-carboxylase supply $V_p$ and with the leakiness $\phi$, and falls with the leak conductance $g_L$. The same expression explains why a low-permeability bundle sheath (small $g_L$, achieved by suberised cell walls in Kranz anatomy) is essential to sustain high $c_B$.

(c) $c_B = 150 + (0.25 \times 45)/0.003 = 150 + 11.25/0.003 = 150 + 3750 = 3900\,\mu\mathrm{mol\,mol}^{-1}$. The bundle-sheath $\text{CO}_2$ is an order of magnitude above atmospheric, near-saturating Rubisco and driving $v_o$ to a negligible rate — the quantitative payoff of the spatial concentration mechanism.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

C4 break-even. Using the energetic ledgers $\Theta_{C3} = 3\,v_c + 3.5\,v_o$ and $\Theta_{C4} = 5\,v_c$ with net assimilation $A_{C3} = v_c - 0.5\,v_o$ and $A_{C4} \approx v_c$, let $r = v_o/v_c \geq 0$. (a) Show that the ATP cost per net carbon fixed is $\Theta_{C3}/A_{C3} = (3 + 3.5\,r)/(1 - 0.5\,r)$ for C3 and $\Theta_{C4}/A_{C4} = 5$ for C4. (b) Solve for the critical ratio $r^\star$ at which the two costs are equal. (c) Interpret: is C4 favoured when $r$ is large (hot) or small (cool)?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set the two per-net-carbon costs equal and solve the resulting equation for $r$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) Direct division:
$$
\frac{\Theta_{C3}}{A_{C3}} \;=\; \frac{3\,v_c + 3.5\,v_o}{v_c - 0.5\,v_o} \;=\; \frac{3 + 3.5\,r}{1 - 0.5\,r}, \qquad
\frac{\Theta_{C4}}{A_{C4}} \;=\; \frac{5\,v_c}{v_c} \;=\; 5.
$$

(b) Set $(3 + 3.5\,r)/(1 - 0.5\,r) = 5$. Then $3 + 3.5\,r = 5 - 2.5\,r$, so $6\,r = 2$, hence $r^\star = 1/3 \approx 0.33$.

(c) For $r > r^\star$ (hot, high photorespiration), C3's cost per net carbon rises above $5$ and C4 is favoured. For $r < r^\star$ (cool, low photorespiration), C3's cost is below $5$ and C3 wins on efficiency. The break-even $v_o/v_c \approx 0.33$ corresponds to a leaf temperature near $25$–$30\,^\circ\text{C}$ at current atmospheric $\text{CO}_2$ — the observed climatic boundary below which C3 grasses dominate and above which C4 grasses take over.

</details>

</aside>

Given the typical ranges $\delta^{13}\text{C}_{C3} = -20$ to $-35\,\textperthousand$ and $\delta^{13}\text{C}_{C4} = -9$ to $-16\,\textperthousand$, classify the plant and comment on the palaeoenvironmental inference.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Result 1 — The quantum-yield crossover and the climatic boundary of C4

The energetic break-even of the Key mechanism translates into a measurable **quantum-yield crossover**: the slope of $A$ versus absorbed photons, $\phi_{\text{CO}_2}$, is higher for C3 at low temperature (no surcharge, no photorespiration) and higher for C4 above $25$–$30\,^\circ\text{C}$ (suppressed photorespiration outweighs the $2$-ATP surcharge) [ref: TODO_REF Taiz2015]. The crossover temperature falls with rising atmospheric $\text{CO}_2$, because elevated $\text{CO}_2$ itself suppresses $v_o/v_c$ in C3 and erodes the C4 advantage. This is the mechanistic basis for the prediction that C4 grasslands contract under high-$\text{CO}_2$ futures and for the geological observation that the global C4 expansion lagged the late-Miocene $\text{CO}_2$ decline — the C4 advantage only becomes worthwhile when atmospheric $\text{CO}_2$ drops far enough to leave C3 photorespiration unsuppressed in warm climates.

### Result 2 — Why C4 requires Kranz anatomy: the leakiness constraint

The expression $c_B = c_M + \phi V_p / g_L$ derived in Exercise 6 shows that the concentration mechanism is only effective if the bundle-sheath leak conductance $g_L$ is small. A high-$g_L$ bundle sheath vents the concentrated $\text{CO}_2$ back to the mesophyll, collapsing $c_B$ toward $c_M$ and wasting the $2$-ATP surcharge on refixing recycled carbon. Evolution's solution is **Kranz anatomy**: thick, suberised bundle-sheath cell walls with few plasmodesmata, a tight mesophyll wreath shortening the diffusion path, and large, starch-filled bundle-sheath chloroplasts positioned to receive the decarboxylated $\text{CO}_2$. The single-cell C4 species (e.g. *Bienertia*, *Suaeda aralocaspica*) achieve the same partition by polarised chloroplast positioning within one cell, confirming that the deep requirement is spatial separation of the pump from the Calvin cycle — Kranz anatomy is the most common, not the only, solution. The leakiness $\phi$ is empirically constrained to $0.2$–$0.3$ in productive C4 leaves, a compromise between the photosynthetic benefit of high $c_B$ and the cost of pumping carbon that ultimately leaks.

### Result 3 — Carbon isotope discrimination and the isotopic signature of pathway

Rubisco discriminates against $^{13}\text{C}$ by roughly $29\textperthousand$ against the source, while PEP carboxylase discriminates by only $\sim 2\textperthousand$. Because C4 plants fix nearly all their carbon through PEP carboxylase first, their biomass carries a diagnostic signature $\delta^{13}\text{C} \approx -9$ to $-16\textperthousand$, distinct from the C3 range of $-20$ to $-35\textperthousand$ [ref: TODO_REF Nobel2009]. CAM plants span both ranges: obligate CAM with strong nocturnal fixation clusters near the C4 signature, while weak or facultative CAM (C3-CAM intermediates) overlaps C3. The isotopic record makes pathway detectable in fossils, herbivore tooth enamel, palaeosol carbonates, and modern food webs, and is the empirical backbone of the reconstruction that C4 grasslands rose globally in the late Miocene. The same physics underlies the use of $\delta^{13}\text{C}$ to trace sugar adulteration (C4 maize or cane sugar in C3 honey) in food chemistry.

### Result 4 — Convergent origins of C4: a metabolic pipeline, recruited independently

C4 photosynthesis has evolved in at least sixty-six independent lineages across nineteen families of angiosperms [ref: TODO_REF Sage2016]. Each origin re-recruits the same enzymatic toolkit — PEP carboxylase, malate dehydrogenase, a decarboxylating enzyme (NADP-ME, NAD-ME, or PEPCK), and PPDK — from ancestral C3 metabolism, up-regulating them in the mesophyll and bundling the decarboxylation into the bundle sheath. The phylogenetic density of C4 origins (concentrated in grasses, sedges, and a handful of eudicot clades) reflects a "C4 pipeline" through which a C3 leaf can be re-engineered by changes in expression and cell-specific localisation of pre-existing enzymes, given an anatomical precondition of close vein spacing. The repeated, independent arrival at the same Kranz solution is one of the strongest cases of convergent molecular engineering in the history of life, and is dual to the single evolutionary origin of CAM-like physiology in Isoetes and multiple origins in Caryophyllales.

### Result 5 — Stomatal regulation: the ABA–OST1–SLAC1 guard-cell signalling pathway

The hormonal effector that translates water deficit into stomatal closure is **abscisic acid (ABA)**. The molecular pathway is now mapped in detail [ref: TODO_REF Taiz2015]: under drought, ABA accumulates in the leaf and root vasculature and binds the PYR/PYL/RCAR receptor family; the receptor sequesters the PP2C phosphatases (ABI1/ABI2) that otherwise inhibit the SNF1-related kinase **OST1**; active OST1 phosphorylates the anion channel **SLAC1** in the guard-cell plasma membrane; anion efflux depolarises the membrane, driving $\text{K}^+$ efflux through voltage-gated outward-rectifying channels and releasing the osmolytes that hold guard-cell turgor; the guard cells deflate and the stoma closes, all within minutes. The same ABA module up-regulates stress genes (LEA, dehydrins) and underlies the integration of hydraulic and chemical signals across the root–leaf axis. Stomatal regulation by ABA is therefore the molecular instantation of the optimisation principle of Result 6 — a closed pore is the leaf's first-line response whenever the marginal water cost of an open pore exceeds the carbon benefit.

### Result 6 — The stomatal optimisation theorem and the marginal water cost of carbon

The Cowan–Farquhar optimality hypothesis [ref: TODO_REF CowanFarquhar1977] states that, over a day, stomata regulate aperture so that the marginal water cost of carbon is constant:

$$
\frac{\partial E / \partial g_s}{\partial A / \partial g_s} \;=\; \lambda,
$$

with $\lambda$ the marginal water cost of carbon (the Lagrange multiplier on the water constraint). A C4 leaf, whose assimilation saturates at lower $g_s$ because the bundle-sheath pump supplies $c_c$ internally, satisfies this optimum at smaller aperture and larger $\lambda$ than a C3 leaf, mechanically realising higher water-use efficiency. The Ball–Berry–Leuning model $g_s = g_0 + a\,A\,h_s/(c_s - \Gamma)$ is the operational form this principle takes in land-surface and crop models. The integration of pathway biochemistry (Results 1–4) with stomatal signalling (Result 5) and the optimality theorem (Result 6) is what closes the loop from enzyme to biome.

**Synthesis.** The mastery-level view **generalises** the three pathways into one continuum of carbon–water economics. **The foundational reason** C4 and CAM succeed in hot, dry habitats is that decoupling $\text{CO}_2$ supply from stomatal aperture lets the leaf keep assimilating while transpiration falls; **this is exactly** the engineering principle behind the convergent recruitment of PEP carboxylase across more than sixty lineages [19.03.01], and the same pump **appears again in** [17.04.03] the thylakoid-level biochemistry where the extra ATP is supplied by cyclic electron flow around Photosystem I. **Putting these together** with the cohesion–tension hydraulics and the ABA guard-cell signalling analysed above, stomatal aperture, xylem tension, and metabolic mode become three coupled actuators of a single homeostat; **the bridge is** the marginal water cost of carbon, $\lambda$, which sets the operating point of all three. **The central insight** is that a plant's photosynthetic pathway and its drought strategy are one optimisation, not two separable problems, and the biochemical pump — spatial in C4, temporal in CAM, absent in C3 — is the variable that locates the plant along the trade-off.

## Full proof set [Master]

### Proposition (bundle-sheath CO$_2$ steady state under leakiness)

Let PEP carboxylase supply four-carbon acid to the bundle sheath at rate $V_p$ (mol $\text{CO}_2$-equivalent $\mathrm{m^{-2}\,s^{-1}}$), let $A$ denote net Calvin-cycle assimilation in the bundle sheath, and let $L = g_L (c_B - c_M)$ be the $\text{CO}_2$ leak from bundle sheath to mesophyll, with bundle-sheath conductance $g_L > 0$ and mesophyll concentration $c_M \geq 0$. Assume steady state and that all decarboxylated four-carbon acid releases its $\text{CO}_2$ inside the bundle sheath. Then (i) $V_p = A + L$, and (ii) the bundle-sheath concentration satisfies $c_B = c_M + (V_p - A)/g_L = c_M + \phi V_p / g_L$ with leakiness $\phi = L/V_p = 1 - A/V_p \in [0,1)$.

**Proof.** Carbon conservation in the bundle-sheath compartment at steady state requires that the rate at which carbon enters as decarboxylated four-carbon acid ($V_p$) equal the rate at which it leaves by Calvin-cycle fixation ($A$) plus the rate at which it diffuses back to the mesophyll as $\text{CO}_2$ ($L$). This gives the steady-state balance $V_p = A + L$, claim (i). Substituting $L = g_L(c_B - c_M)$ into (i) and solving for $c_B$,

$$
c_B \;=\; c_M \;+\; \frac{V_p - A}{g_L}.
$$

Defining leakiness $\phi = L/V_p = (V_p - A)/V_p = 1 - A/V_p$, the net assimilation satisfies $A = (1 - \phi) V_p$, and substituting $V_p - A = \phi V_p$ yields $c_B = c_M + \phi V_p / g_L$, claim (ii). The bound $\phi \in [0,1)$ holds because $A \geq 0$ (net Calvin-cycle uptake cannot be negative at steady state under illumination) gives $\phi \leq 1$, and $A \leq V_p$ (net assimilation cannot exceed the carbon supplied by the pump) gives $\phi \geq 0$, with strict inequality wherever the Calvin cycle assimilates positive carbon. The result is the quantitative statement that the C4 concentration mechanism is governed by the ratio of pump activity to bundle-sheath leak conductance: a low-permeability bundle sheath (small $g_L$) is the anatomical prerequisite for sustaining $c_B$ well above $c_M$, and hence for suppressing photorespiration at Rubisco. $\square$

### Proposition (diurnal malic-acid balance of CAM at steady state)

Let $M_{\text{dawn}}$ and $M_{\text{dusk}}$ denote the vacuolar malic-acid content of a CAM cell at dawn and dusk respectively. Assume that all nocturnal carbon fixation proceeds through PEP carboxylase into malate at rate $V_p^{\text{night}}$ over a dark period of length $T_d$, and that all daytime decarboxylation proceeds at rate $V_{\text{dec}}^{\text{day}}$ over a light period of length $T_l$, with no other source or sink of malate. At steady state over a full day–night cycle, $M_{\text{dawn}}$ and $M_{\text{dusk}}$ are constant from one cycle to the next, and $V_p^{\text{night}}\,T_d = V_{\text{dec}}^{\text{day}}\,T_l = \Delta M$, where $\Delta M = M_{\text{dawn}} - M_{\text{dusk}}$ is the diurnal acidity swing.

**Proof.** Integrating the rate of change of vacuolar malic acid over the dark period gives $M_{\text{dawn}} - M_{\text{dusk}} = V_p^{\text{night}}\,T_d$, since PEP carboxylase is the only nocturnal source of malate. Over the light period the sign reverses: $M_{\text{dusk, next}} - M_{\text{dawn}} = -V_{\text{dec}}^{\text{day}}\,T_l$, since decarboxylation is the only daytime sink. Steady state over a full cycle requires that the dawn content not drift, $M_{\text{dawn, next cycle}} = M_{\text{dawn}}$, which by composition of the two integrals is $V_p^{\text{night}}\,T_d - V_{\text{dec}}^{\text{day}}\,T_l = 0$, i.e. $V_p^{\text{night}}\,T_d = V_{\text{dec}}^{\text{day}}\,T_l$. Equating each side with $\Delta M$ from the nocturnal integral gives the stated identity. The carbon dividend to the Calvin cycle over the light period equals one $\text{CO}_2$ per malate decarboxylated, i.e. $V_{\text{dec}}^{\text{day}}\,T_l = \Delta M$ moles of $\text{CO}_2$ per unit leaf area, supplied internally behind closed stomata. This is the formal statement that CAM is a closed-loop carbon reservoir: at steady state the night-time PEP-carboxylase pump and the daytime Calvin-cycle drain balance exactly, and the titratable-acidity swing measures the throughput of the pump. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The discovery that the three-carbon compound 3-PGA is the first product of photosynthesis, and the mapping of the cycle that regenerates its acceptor, are the work of **Melvin Calvin, Andrew Benson, and James Bassham** in Berkeley, using $^{14}\text{C}$ labelling and two-dimensional paper chromatography in the late 1940s and 1950s [ref: TODO_REF Calvin1962]; Calvin received the 1961 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for the pathway now called the Calvin–Benson cycle. The C4 pathway was uncovered a decade later in a parallel discovery: **Hugo Kortschak, Conrad Hartt and George Burr** in Hawaii noted in the mid-1960s that radio-labelled carbon in sugarcane first appears in four-carbon acids rather than 3-PGA, and **Marshall (Hal) Hatch and Roger Slack** in Brisbane established the full C4 cycle, the role of PEP carboxylase, and the bundle-sheath decarboxylation in a 1966 *Biochemical Journal* paper that gave the pathway its name [ref: TODO_REF HatchSlack1966]. The recognition that C4 is not a curiosity but a globally dominant syndrome in tropical grasses came with **Gerald Edwards and David Hatch** in the 1970s and matured through the isotopic and phylogenetic synthesis of **Rowan Sage, Colin Osborne, and Elizabeth Edwards** [ref: TODO_REF Sage2016], who established the $\geq 66$ independent evolutionary origins.

Crassulacean acid metabolism was observed as a phenomenon — the nocturnal acidification of succulent leaves — long before it was understood as a photosynthetic pathway: the acid-by-night, sweet-by-day rhythm of succulents was recorded by **Julius von Sachs** and others in the nineteenth century, and the mechanism was crystallised in the mid-twentieth century by **Irving Ting, Barry Osmond, and Park Nobel** [ref: TODO_REF Nobel2009], who showed that the night-time acidification is PEP-carboxylase fixation into malate and the daytime deacidification is its decarboxylation feeding the Calvin cycle.

The water-relations half of the unit rests on the cohesion–tension theory articulated by **Henry Dixon and John Joly** in Dublin in 1894 [ref: TODO_REF DixonJoly1894], resisted for half a century on the grounds that water could not sustain large negative pressures, and vindicated by **Per Scholander's** pressure-bomb measurements on tall trees and mangroves in the 1960s. The quantitative bridge between the photosynthetic pathway and the hydraulic system is the **Cowan–Farquhar stomatal-optimality theorem** of 1977 [ref: TODO_REF CowanFarquhar1977], which recast stomatal behaviour as an economic optimum and remains the backbone of every modern land-surface and crop model. Together these strands turn plant carbon and water economy from a catalogue of adaptations into a predictive science, in which the choice among C3, C4, and CAM is read as a single optimisation over carbon gain, water loss, and energy cost.
