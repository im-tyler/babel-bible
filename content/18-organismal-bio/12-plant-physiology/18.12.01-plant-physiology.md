## Intuition [Beginner]

Plants look still, but they are not passive. A plant is a quiet hydraulic machine: it pumps water from soil to sky, moves sugar from leaves to roots and fruit, and constantly senses and responds to light, gravity, drought, and attack. There is no heart and no brain, yet the whole organism stays coordinated through pressure, chemistry, and electrical pulses.

Water moves up a plant along a single chain of pulls. Roots absorb water from the soil, the water climbs through hollow conducting cells called xylem, and most of it evaporates out of tiny leaf pores called stomata. That evaporation is the engine. Because water molecules cling to one another (cohesion) and to the cell walls (adhesion), a pull at the top drags the whole column upward. This is why a tall tree can lift water a hundred metres with no pump.

Sugar moves through a second set of tubes called phloem, and in the opposite direction: from leaves (sources, where sugar is made) to roots, fruits, and growing buds (sinks, where sugar is used or stored). The leaf loads sugar into the phloem, water follows by osmosis, pressure builds, and the sugary sap is pushed along the tube toward the lower-pressure sink. The same physics pushes and pulls every drop.

Leaves also run the factory that feeds the plant: photosynthesis. Sunlight splits water and drives the assembly of sugar from carbon dioxide. To get carbon dioxide, the leaf must open its stomata, but open pores also let water escape. Every plant walks a tightrope between eating and drinking — open the pores to gain carbon, close them to save water. That single trade-off shapes deserts, rainforests, and farm yields.

Coordination comes from hormones — chemical messengers made in one tissue that change growth elsewhere. Auxin bends a shoot toward light. Gibberellin stretches stems. Cytokinin drives cell division. Abscisic acid closes stomata in a drought. Ethylene ripens fruit and sheds leaves. A handful of compounds let the plant act as one integrated system, responding to its world in minutes or across seasons.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A coast redwood can lift water to leaves roughly $100\,\text{m}$ above the ground. How hard does the leaf have to pull? Use the rule of thumb that atmospheric pressure ($0.1\,\text{MPa}$) lifts a water column about $10\,\text{m}$. To reach $100\,\text{m}$, the xylem must sustain a tension of about $100/10 \times 0.1\,\text{MPa} \approx 1.0\,\text{MPa}$, that is, a negative pressure of roughly one megapascal. Direct measurements with pressure bombs give leaf xylem tensions of $-1$ to $-2\,\text{MPa}$ for tall trees — large enough to do the job and consistent with the cohesion-tension idea.

Now count the breathing pores. A typical sun leaf carries about $300$ stomata per square millimetre, mostly on the underside. A leaf of area $50\,\text{cm}^2$ equals $5000\,\text{mm}^2$. Multiplying gives $5000 \times 300 = 1{,}500{,}000$ stomata on that single leaf. A large tree with $200{,}000$ leaves therefore owns roughly $3 \times 10^{11}$ stomata — three hundred billion tiny valves, each opening and closing to trade carbon dioxide for water.

Compare the two photosynthetic strategies. A C3 plant like rice loses about $400$ to $1000$ grams of water for every gram of carbon it fixes. A C4 plant like maize, which pre-concentrates carbon dioxide before the main reaction, runs at roughly $250$ to $300$ grams of water per gram of carbon. In a dry field, the C4 plant can keep its stomata narrower, save water, and still photosynthesise fast — the reason maize and sugarcane dominate hot, bright climates.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Water potential

The thermodynamic driver of water movement in plants is the **water potential** $\Psi$ (units of pressure, MPa), defined so that water moves spontaneously from high $\Psi$ to low $\Psi$. For a plant cell or solution,

$$
\Psi \;=\; \Psi_p + \Psi_s + \Psi_g,
$$

where $\Psi_p$ is the **pressure (turgor) potential** (positive inside living cells, negative under tension in xylem), $\Psi_s$ is the **solute (osmotic) potential**, and $\Psi_g$ is the **gravitational potential** $\rho g h$ (about $0.01\,\text{MPa/m}$ of height). Pure water in an open container at the reference height has $\Psi = 0$ by convention.

The solute potential follows the van 't Hoff relation

$$
\Psi_s \;=\; -R\,T\,c_s,
$$

where $R$ is the gas constant, $T$ the absolute temperature, and $c_s$ the total solute osmolarity. Adding solute lowers $\Psi_s$ below zero, so water tends to flow into more concentrated solutions across a semipermeable membrane until opposed by turgor.

### Xylem transport and the cohesion-tension state

In the transpiring plant, xylem water is under **tension**: $\Psi_p < 0$. The vertical gradient set by gravity and hydraulic resistance produces a water-potential drop from soil ($\Psi \approx -0.1$ to $-0.3\,\text{MPa}$ in moist soil) through the root, stem, and leaf apoplast ($\Psi \approx -1$ to $-2\,\text{MPa}$ at the evaporating surface of a tall tree). The continuous water column is metastable: it persists only while cohesive intermolecular forces defeat nucleation of vapour bubbles (cavitation).

### Phloem transport and the pressure-flow state

Phloem sap in the sieve tubes is under **positive pressure**: $\Psi_p > 0$, typically $0.5$ to $2.5\,\text{MPa}$. A source loads sucrose into the sieve element (raising $c_s$, lowering $\Psi_s$); water follows osmotically from the xylem; turgor rises. At a sink, sucrose is unloaded and water exits, lowering local turgor. The resulting longitudinal pressure gradient drives bulk flow of sap from source to sink.

### Stomatal conductance and gas exchange

The rate of net $\mathrm{CO}_2$ assimilation $A$ (units $\mu\mathrm{mol\,m^{-2}\,s^{-1}}$) is proportional to the conductance of the diffusion path from the bulk air to the chloroplast:

$$
A \;=\; g_t\,(c_a - c_c),
$$

where $c_a$ is atmospheric $\mathrm{CO}_2$, $c_c$ is the $\mathrm{CO}_2$ concentration at the carboxylation site, and $g_t$ is the total conductance (stomatal $g_s$ and mesophyll $g_m$ in series, with the leaf boundary layer). Opening stomata raises $g_s$ and $c_c$ (raising $A$) but also raises transpiration $E \approx g_s \,e/(P)$ for a leaf-to-air vapour-pressure difference $e$.

### The principal hormone classes

| Hormone | Site of synthesis | Principal effects |
|---|---|---|
| Auxin (IAA) | Shoot apical meristem, young leaves | Cell elongation, apical dominance, phototropism and gravitropism |
| Gibberellin (GA) | Young leaves, roots, developing seeds | Stem elongation, seed germination, flowering |
| Cytokinin (CK) | Root apical meristem | Cell division, delay of senescence, nutrient signalling |
| Abscisic acid (ABA) | Leaves, roots (under stress) | Stomatal closure, seed dormancy, stress tolerance |
| Ethylene (C2H4) | Most tissues (ripening, stress) | Fruit ripening, leaf and flower senescence, triple response in seedlings |

### Photosynthesis in one line

Net assimilation is the smaller of two capacities: the light-limited rate of electron transport $J$ and the carboxylation-limited rate of the Calvin-Benson cycle enzyme Rubisco, $V_{c,\max}$. The C3, C4, and CAM pathways differ in how they deliver $\mathrm{CO}_2$ to Rubisco and how they manage the competing oxygenation reaction (photorespiration).

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **Root pressure is not what lifts water up tall trees.** Root pressure (guttation) is a positive push of at most $0.1$–$0.2\,\text{MPa}$, produced by solute accumulation in the root stele. It can lift water only a few metres and is absent in most actively transpiring trees. Long-distance ascent is driven by the negative tension at the leaf, not by a push from below.
- **Water potential is not the same as water content.** A dry soil and a salty wet soil can both have very negative $\Psi$ and both resist giving up water to the root. Movement depends on the potential gradient, not on the amount of water present.
- **Stomata are not always fully open in daylight.** In dry or hot conditions the plant closes them partly to limit water loss, accepting lower photosynthesis. A well-watered C3 crop rarely operates at maximum $g_s$.
- **Phloem flow is not driven by photosynthesis directly.** It is driven by the turgor gradient created by loading and unloading. A detached leaf in the dark can still translocate stored sugar if loading continues.
- **Hormones are not on/off switches.** Responses depend on concentration, on tissue sensitivity, and on cross-talk with other hormones. Auxin promotes elongation in shoots but inhibits it at high concentration in roots.

## Key mechanism [Intermediate+]

### The cohesion-tension and pressure-flow hypotheses

The two long-distance transport systems of a vascular plant share one physical backbone: a pressure gradient along a conduit drives viscous bulk flow, and the gradient is set by the leaf's interaction with the atmosphere. In xylem the gradient is negative (tension set by transpiration); in phloem it is positive (turgor set by osmotic loading). Treating both as Poiseuille flow in cylindrical conduits unifies them with the same resistance law already met in the cardiovascular system [18.02.03].

**Proposition (xylem tension from transpiration).** *Let a vertical xylem conduit of radius $r$ and length $L$ connect the root (pressure $P_{\text{root}}$) to an evaporating leaf surface (pressure $P_{\text{leaf}}$). The pressure drop required to lift water to height $L$ against gravity and to sustain steady volumetric flux $Q$ is*

$$
\Delta P \;=\; P_{\text{root}} - P_{\text{leaf}} \;=\; \rho\,g\,L \;+\; \frac{8\,\eta\,L}{\pi\,r^4}\,Q,
$$

*so that $P_{\text{leaf}}$ falls to large negative values (tension) in tall, rapidly transpiring plants.*

**Derivation.** For a steady incompressible flow in a vertical conduit, the Navier–Stokes momentum balance reduces to a force balance per unit volume between the pressure gradient, the gravitational body force, and the viscous resistance:

$$
-\frac{dP}{dz} \;-\; \rho\,g \;=\; \eta\,\frac{1}{r'}\frac{d}{dr'}\!\left(r'\,\frac{du}{dr'}\right).
$$

Integrating the viscous term over the circular cross-section (the parabolic Poiseuille profile $u(r') = (\Delta P_{\text{fric}}/(4\eta L))(R^2 - r'^2)$) recovers the volumetric flow

$$
Q \;=\; \frac{\pi\,r^4}{8\,\eta\,L}\,\Delta P_{\text{fric}},
$$

where $\Delta P_{\text{fric}}$ is the frictional pressure drop. Separating the gravitational and frictional contributions gives the stated $\Delta P = \rho g L + (8\eta L/\pi r^4)\,Q$. Solving for the leaf pressure,

$$
P_{\text{leaf}} \;=\; P_{\text{root}} \;-\; \rho\,g\,L \;-\; \frac{8\,\eta\,L}{\pi\,r^4}\,Q.
$$

For a $100\,\text{m}$ tree, $\rho g L = 1000 \times 9.8 \times 100 \approx 9.8 \times 10^{5}\,\text{Pa} \approx 0.98\,\text{MPa}$ from gravity alone, before the frictional term. Hence $P_{\text{leaf}} \approx -1$ to $-2\,\text{MPa}$, matching the pressure-bomb measurements. The column holds because narrow xylem conduits (radius $10$–$40\,\mu\text{m}$) suppress bubble nucleation, and because water's cohesive tensile strength (theoretically $> 20\,\text{MPa}$ in defect-free water) far exceeds the operating tension, although pit-membrane pores and dissolved gas set the practical cavitation threshold near $-2$ to $-5\,\text{MPa}$ for most species. $\square$

**Proposition (Münch pressure-flow in the phloem).** *Let a sieve tube of radius $r$ and length $L$ connect a source (turgor $P_S$) to a sink (turgor $P_K$, with $P_S > P_K$). The steady volumetric sap flow is*

$$
Q \;=\; \frac{\pi\,r^4}{8\,\eta\,L}\,(P_S - P_K),
$$

*directed from source to sink, where the pressure difference is maintained osmotically by sucrose loading at the source and unloading at the sink.*

**Derivation.** Loading sucrose into the source sieve element lowers the local solute potential $\Psi_s$. Water follows osmotically from the adjacent xylem (which is at a much lower, negative water potential but is held at near-atmospheric reference along the way) until the rising turgor $\Psi_p$ equilibrates the total water potential across the loading membrane. The result is a source turgor $P_S \approx 1$–$2.5\,\text{MPa}$. At the sink, sucrose unloading and subsequent use (respiration, starch synthesis, growth) lowers the local solute concentration; water exits to the xylem, and turgor falls to $P_K \approx 0.3$–$1\,\text{MPa}$. The longitudinal gradient $(P_S - P_K)/L$ drives bulk flow obeying the same Poiseuille law as blood in an arteriole. Reversibility is the signature of the mechanism: if a tissue becomes a net source (a storage root exporting starch in spring), its turgor rises and the flow direction reverses. $\square$

**Bridge.** The two transport systems are one hydraulic circuit viewed from two sides: xylem under tension feeds the leaf, the leaf spends water to gain carbon, and phloem under positive pressure redistributes that carbon. **This is exactly** the same $r^4$ Poiseuille resistance law that governs blood flow in the cardiovascular system [18.02.03], so the central insight of haemodynamics carries over: a small change in conduit radius changes resistance by the fourth power. The cohesion–tension result **builds toward** the quantitative theory of maximum tree height and drought-induced cavitation, and the Münch pressure-flow model **appears again in** the source–sink economics that governs how a crop partitions carbon among roots, shoots, grain, and fruit. **Putting these together**, transport, photosynthesis, and carbon allocation collapse into a single coupled circuit whose currency is water potential; **the bridge is** the pressure gradient, negative on one side, positive on the other, that a sessile organism uses to behave like a coherent whole.

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A sieve tube has radius $r = 6\,\mu\text{m}$, length $L = 0.5\,\text{m}$, sap viscosity $\eta = 1.5 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{Pa}\cdot\text{s}$, with source turgor $P_S = 1.8\,\text{MPa}$ and sink turgor $P_K = 0.8\,\text{MPa}$. (a) Derive the sap velocity (mean speed) from Poiseuille flow. (b) Compute its numerical value in $\mu\text{m/s}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Mean velocity $\bar{u} = Q/(\pi r^2)$ with $Q = \pi r^4 \Delta P / (8 \eta L)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) Substituting Poiseuille flow and dividing by the cross-sectional area,

$$
\bar{u} \;=\; \frac{Q}{\pi r^2} \;=\; \frac{r^2\,\Delta P}{8\,\eta\,L}.
$$

(b) With $\Delta P = P_S - P_K = 1.0 \times 10^{6}\,\text{Pa}$, $r = 6 \times 10^{-6}\,\text{m}$:

$$
\bar{u} \;=\; \frac{(6 \times 10^{-6})^2 \times 10^{6}}{8 \times 1.5 \times 10^{-3} \times 0.5} \;=\; \frac{3.6 \times 10^{-5}}{6 \times 10^{-3}} \;=\; 6 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{m/s} \;=\; 6000\,\mu\text{m/s}.
$$

Real phloem sap moves a few thousand $\mu\text{m/s}$, in good agreement. Note the strong sensitivity to $r$: a sieve tube of half the radius would carry one quarter the speed at the same pressure gradient.

</details>

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).**

Estimate the tension needed to lift water to the top of the world's tallest tree, a coast redwood of height $116\,\text{m}$, ignoring friction. Compare it with the typical cavitation threshold of $-2.5\,\text{MPa}$ for that species. Is the column safely below the failure point?

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Cowan–Farquhar optimality. Let assimilation $A(g_s)$ be an increasing, concave function of stomatal conductance and transpiration $E(g_s) = k\,g_s$ (constant vapour-pressure difference, $k > 0$). Show that the stomatal aperture maximising the ratio $A/E$ satisfies $dA/dg_s = A/g_s$, and that if the plant instead maximises $A - \lambda E$ for a marginal water cost $\lambda > 0$, the optimum obeys $dA/dg_s = \lambda\,k$. Hence argue that a plant in dry soil (large $\lambda$) should operate at lower $g_s$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the ratio, differentiate $A/E = A/(k g_s)$ and set the derivative to zero. For the constrained form, take $d(A - \lambda E)/dg_s = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**(Ratio criterion.)** Maximise $f(g_s) = A/(k g_s)$. Setting $f'(g_s) = 0$:

$$
f'(g_s) \;=\; \frac{A'(g_s)\,k g_s - A\,k}{(k g_s)^2} \;=\; 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; A'(g_s)\,g_s - A \;=\; 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; \frac{dA}{dg_s} \;=\; \frac{A}{g_s}.
$$

This is the condition that the marginal gain of opening stomata equals the average gain per unit conductance.

**(Lagrangian criterion.)** Maximise $\mathcal{L} = A(g_s) - \lambda E(g_s) = A(g_s) - \lambda k g_s$. Then $d\mathcal{L}/dg_s = 0$ gives

$$
\frac{dA}{dg_s} \;=\; \lambda\,k.
$$

Because $A$ is concave, $dA/dg_s$ is decreasing in $g_s$. Therefore larger $\lambda$ forces the optimum to sit where $dA/dg_s$ is large, which (by monotonicity of the derivative) means smaller $g_s$. Physiologically, when water is costly (dry soil, large $\lambda$), the plant closes its stomata more, trading photosynthesis for safety. The empirical Ball–Berry–Leuning model of stomatal conductance is the operational form of this optimality principle.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Münch mass balance. A source leaf loads sucrose into a sieve tube at molar rate $L_s$ and a sink root unloads at rate $L_k$, both per unit tube length. Let the sap have sucrose concentration $C$ and move at speed $\bar{u}$. Assume steady state and a uniform cross-section. (a) Derive the continuity equation for $C$ along the tube. (b) Show that if loading and unloading are balanced ($L_s, L_k$ matched so that the total sucrose inventory is constant), then a gradient in $\bar{u}$ accompanies a gradient in $C$, consistent with a pressure-flow profile from source to sink.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write conservation of sucrose in a slab of length $dx$: accumulation equals flux in minus flux out plus sources/sinks. At steady state the accumulation term vanishes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) Let $\phi(x,t) = C(x,t)\,\bar{u}(x,t)$ be the sucrose flux (mol per unit area per unit time). Conservation in a slab of thickness $dx$ with lateral loading/unloading per unit volume $\ell(x)$ (positive at source, negative at sink) gives, at steady state,

$$
\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \;=\; \ell(x) \;\Longrightarrow\; \frac{\partial (C\,\bar{u})}{\partial x} \;=\; \ell(x).
$$

In the loading zone $\ell > 0$, so $C\bar{u}$ increases along the tube; in the unloading zone $\ell < 0$, it decreases.

(b) Expanding the derivative,

$$
\bar{u}\,\frac{dC}{dx} \;+\; C\,\frac{d\bar{u}}{dx} \;=\; \ell(x).
$$

At the source, sucrose accumulates in the sap ($dC/dx$ changes), water follows osmotically and raises turgor, and Poiseuille's law links the local pressure gradient to $\bar{u} = r^2 (dP/dx)/(8\eta)$. Because turgor is high at the source and low at the sink, $dP/dx < 0$ and $\bar{u}$ is directed from source to sink throughout the transport zone. If total sucrose inventory is constant over time, the loading and unloading rates must balance the longitudinal flux divergence: $L_s - L_k = \phi_{\text{out}} - \phi_{\text{in}}$, the steady Münch relation. This is the formal statement that the pressure-flow mechanism conserves both water and sucrose.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Stomatal diffusion and the boundary layer. Let the total conductance from bulk air to the stomatal cavity be the boundary-layer conductance $g_b$ in series with stomatal conductance $g_s$. (a) Show the total conductance is $g_t = (1/g_b + 1/g_s)^{-1}$. (b) If $g_b = 3.0$ is fixed by wind and the plant opens its stomata so that $g_s$ grows from $0.3$ to $3.0$ (all in $\text{mol\,m}^{-2}\,\text{s}^{-1}$), by what factor does $g_t$ increase? (c) Argue why, in still air ($g_b$ small), opening stomata further barely raises assimilation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Series conductances add as reciprocals. Compute $g_t$ at each value of $g_s$ and take the ratio. The limiting case $g_s \gg g_b$ makes $g_t \to g_b$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) In series, resistances add: $1/g_t = 1/g_b + 1/g_s$, hence $g_t = (1/g_b + 1/g_s)^{-1} = g_b g_s/(g_b + g_s)$.

(b) At $g_s = 0.3$: $g_t = (3.0 \times 0.3)/3.3 = 0.9/3.3 \approx 0.273$. At $g_s = 3.0$: $g_t = (3.0 \times 3.0)/6.0 = 9/6 = 1.5$. The ratio is $1.5/0.273 \approx 5.5$. A tenfold increase in stomatal aperture yields only a $5.5$-fold increase in total conductance — the boundary layer already limits flow even with moderate wind.

(c) When $g_b$ is small (still air), $1/g_b$ dominates $1/g_t$, so $g_t \approx g_b$ regardless of how wide $g_s$ becomes. The plant then gains little carbon by opening stomata further but still loses water roughly proportional to $g_s$, so the optimal behaviour is to keep stomata partly closed. This is the physical reason still, humid air encourages stomatal opening (the boundary layer is the limit only when it is thick), whereas the same plant in a breeze can profitably open more — wind reduces the boundary-layer resistance and lets stomatal aperture actually matter.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The stomatal optimisation theorem and water-use efficiency

The single deepest theoretical result in plant physiology is the **Cowan–Farquhar optimality hypothesis** [ref: TODO_REF CowanFarquhar1977]: over a day, stomata regulate aperture so as to maximise cumulative carbon gain for a fixed amount of water lost. The necessary condition is that the marginal water cost of carbon be constant,

$$
\frac{\partial E / \partial g_s}{\partial A / \partial g_s} \;=\; \lambda,
$$

where $\lambda$ is the Lagrange multiplier (the "marginal water cost"). Plants in dry soil operate at larger $\lambda$, smaller $g_s$, and higher water-use efficiency; plants in wet soil operate at smaller $\lambda$ and larger $g_s$. The empirically successful Ball–Berry–Leuning model $g_s = g_0 + a\,A\,h_s/(c_s - \Gamma)$ (with relative humidity $h_s$, surface $\mathrm{CO}_2$ $c_s$, and the $\mathrm{CO}_2$ compensation point $\Gamma$) is the operational form this optimality principle takes in land-surface and crop models. It links a leaf-level economic decision to the global water and carbon cycles.

### C4, CAM, and the bioengineering of the CO2 pump

Rubisco's oxygenase reaction (photorespiration) wastes fixed carbon and energy, and the loss grows with temperature because the solubility of $\mathrm{CO}_2$ falls faster than that of $\mathrm{O}_2$. The C4 pathway (maize, sugarcane, sorghum) separates the initial carboxylation by PEP carboxylase in the mesophyll from the Calvin cycle in the bundle-sheath, raising the $\mathrm{CO}_2$ concentration at Rubisco to two to three times atmospheric and suppressing photorespiration. The cost is one extra ATP per $\mathrm{CO}_2$ fixed, repaid many times over in hot, bright conditions. CAM plants (cacti, pineapple, orchids) run the same PEP-carboxylase pump but temporally: they open stomata at night, fix $\mathrm{CO}_2$ into malate, close stomata through the heat of the day, and release the $\mathrm{CO}_2$ internally for the Calvin cycle. Water-use efficiency rises by a factor of three to six compared with C3, at the price of a slow maximum growth rate. The convergence of C4 photosynthesis in at least sixty-six independent lineages is one of evolution's most striking cases of repeated molecular engineering.

### Phytochrome, photoperiodism, and shade avoidance

Light is not only an energy source but an informational signal. The **phytochrome** pigment exists in two interconvertible forms, $P_r$ (absorbing red light, $\sim 660\,\text{nm}$) and $P_{fr}$ (absorbing far-red, $\sim 730\,\text{nm}$); the ratio $P_{fr}/P_{\text{total}}$ reports the red : far-red balance of the incident light. Sunlight is red-rich; light filtered through a leaf canopy is far-red-rich because chlorophyll absorbs red. A plant measuring a low $P_{fr}$ fraction therefore deduces that it is shaded and responds by stem elongation, petiole raising, and accelerated flowering — the **shade-avoidance syndrome**. Photoperiodism (the measurement of night length by the circadian-gated accumulation of $P_{fr}$) controls flowering in long-day and short-day species through the CONSTANS–FT regulatory module, integrating a pigment-level biophysics with a whole-plant developmental decision.

### Hormone cross-talk and signalling networks

Hormones do not act in isolation. The auxin signalling pathway (TIR1/AFB receptor, Aux/IAA repressors, ARF transcription factors) interacts with cytokinin (two-component histidine-kinase phosphorelay), with gibberellin (DELLA-repressor degradation via the GA receptor GID1), with jasmonate and salicylate (antagonistic in defence against chewing insects versus biotrophic pathogens), and with ethylene (the ETR receptors and EIN3 transcription factor). A single growth response, such as root gravitropism, typically requires auxin redistribution (PIN auxin-efflux carriers relocalised by gravity-sensing statoliths in the root cap), modulation by cytokinin and ethylene, and feedback through DELLA proteins that integrate nutrient and stress status. The architecture is a network, not a list, and this is why hormone biology resists clean single-cause explanations.

### Systemic signalling in stress: ABA, calcium waves, and systemic acquired resistance

Drought perceived in the root triggers ABA synthesis and ABA-mediated guard-cell closure within minutes. But the signal also travels. A leaf wounded by a herbivore or infected by a pathogen emits electrical signals (glutamate-receptor-like channels, action potentials), calcium waves moving at hundreds of $\mu\text{m/s}$, reactive-oxygen species waves, and hydraulic signals through the xylem; distal leaves prepare their defences before the threat arrives. **Systemic acquired resistance** (salicylic-acid dependent, the NPR1 transcription factor, expression of pathogenesis-related proteins) provides broad-spectrum, long-lasting resistance against subsequent infections across the whole plant. This is a sessile organism's alternative to running away: a distributed immune and stress network that uses the vascular highways of xylem and phloem as a nervous system.

**Synthesis.** The mastery-level view **generalises** the single-conduit transport laws into an integrated plant–environment control system. **The foundational reason** vascular plants dominate nearly every terrestrial biome is that they couple a passive hydraulic engine (cohesion–tension) to an active biochemical one (photosynthesis) and regulate both through hormonal and electrical feedback. **This is exactly** the architecture that lets a C4 grass fix carbon at low stomatal conductance in the noon heat while a CAM cactus does so at night, and the same source–sink logic **appears again in** the phloem unloading that fills a tuber or swells a fruit. **Putting these together**, transport, photosynthesis, hormones, and stress responses cease to be separate chapters and become one feedback network, in which the stomatal-optimisation theorem **is dual to** the carbon-allocation principles that govern whole-plant growth. **The central insight** is that a plant is a homeostatic automaton whose set-points are set by water potential, hormone concentration, and redox state, and whose actuators are the same pressure gradients and biochemical pathways analysed in each section above.

## Full proof set [Master]

### Proposition (Münch pressure-flow, mass-conserving form)

Let a phloem sieve tube of uniform cross-sectional area $A_c = \pi r^2$ and length $L$ connect a source at $x = 0$ to a sink at $x = L$. Let $C(x)$ denote the sucrose concentration in the sap and $\bar{u}(x)$ the mean sap velocity. Assume: (i) steady state, (ii) incompressible Newtonian sap of viscosity $\eta$, (iii) the sap obeys Poiseuille's law locally so that $\bar{u}(x) = -r^2 (dP/dx)/(8\eta)$, (iv) lateral sucrose flux per unit length $\ell(x) \geq 0$ near the source and $\ell(x) \leq 0$ near the sink with $\int_0^L \ell(x)\,dx = 0$ (balanced loading and unloading). Then $\bar{u}(x) > 0$ throughout the transport zone, and the sucrose flux $\phi(x) = C(x)\bar{u}(x)$ is constant on the interval between source and sink loading/unloading shoulders.

**Proof.** Steady-state conservation of sucrose in a slab $[x, x + dx]$ gives

$$
\frac{d\phi}{dx} \;=\; \ell(x), \qquad \phi(x) = C(x)\,\bar{u}(x).
$$

In the transport zone (neither loading nor unloading, $\ell = 0$), $\phi(x) = \phi_0$ is constant. Expanding, $\bar{u}\,dC/dx + C\,d\bar{u}/dx = 0$. For the hydraulic field, integrate Poiseuille's law over $[0, x]$:

$$
\bar{u}(x) \;=\; -\frac{r^2}{8\eta}\frac{dP}{dx}.
$$

At the source, osmotic water influx driven by sucrose loading raises $P(0) = P_S$ above the sink value $P(L) = P_K$. Since $P$ is monotonically decreasing from source to sink (the gradient that drives flow), $dP/dx < 0$ and therefore $\bar{u}(x) > 0$ everywhere — flow is strictly from source to sink. Integrating $\bar{u}$ over the tube recovers the total volumetric flow $Q = A_c \langle \bar{u} \rangle = \pi r^4 (P_S - P_K)/(8 \eta L)$, the source-to-sink Poiseuille formula. Because the sucrose flux $\phi_0 = C\bar{u}$ is constant in the transport zone while $\bar{u}$ is positive, the sucrose concentration $C$ there is also constant, consistent with the observed near-uniform sugar content of phloem sap sampled by aphid stylets along the transport path. Finally, the balanced-loading condition $\int_0^L \ell\,dx = 0$ ensures the total sucrose inventory does not drift, so the steady state is self-consistent and the pressure gradient is maintained indefinitely by the continuing biochemistry of loading and unloading. $\square$

### Proposition (stomatal optimality under a water constraint)

Let assimilation $A(g_s)$ be a strictly increasing, strictly concave, differentiable function of stomatal conductance $g_s > 0$, and let transpiration be $E(g_s) = k g_s$ for a fixed leaf-to-air vapour-pressure term $k > 0$. Then the conductance $g_s^\star$ maximising $A(g_s) - \lambda E(g_s)$ for a given marginal water cost $\lambda > 0$ exists, is unique, and satisfies

$$
A'(g_s^\star) \;=\; \lambda\,k.
$$

Moreover, $g_s^\star$ is strictly decreasing in $\lambda$.

**Proof.** Define $\mathcal{L}(g_s) = A(g_s) - \lambda k g_s$. Strict concavity of $A$ makes $\mathcal{L}$ strictly concave, so any critical point is the unique global maximiser. The first-order condition is $\mathcal{L}'(g_s^\star) = A'(g_s^\star) - \lambda k = 0$, giving $A'(g_s^\star) = \lambda k$. For monotonicity in $\lambda$, differentiate the first-order condition implicitly: $A''(g_s^\star)\,dg_s^\star/d\lambda = k$. Because $A'' < 0$ (strict concavity) and $k > 0$, we have $dg_s^\star/d\lambda = k/A''(g_s^\star) < 0$, so $g_s^\star$ strictly decreases as $\lambda$ rises. The interpretation: when water is scarce the plant assigns it a high shadow price $\lambda$, the optimal aperture shrinks, and water-use efficiency $A/E = A(g_s^\star)/(k g_s^\star)$ rises. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The realisation that plants "purify" air began with **Jan Ingenhousz**, who in 1779 showed that only the green parts of plants, and only in sunlight, restore the "goodness" of air that animals had spoilt [ref: TODO_REF Ingenhousz1779]. Ingenhousz had been the court physician to Empress Maria Theresa; his experiments in a sealed-glass, candle-and-mouse tradition established photosynthesis as a light-driven gas exchange and ended the older view that plants simply "dephlogisticate" air by a vital principle. The complementary discovery that plants feed on the atmosphere, not the soil, matured over the next century with von Sachs and Pfeffer, who turned physiology into an experimental science of solutions and pressures.

The cohesion–tension theory was articulated by **Henry Dixon and John Joly** in Dublin in 1894, against the then-dominant "vital force" and capillarity schools. The theory required that water sustain large negative pressures — a state many chemists of the time held to be impossible, since water was thought to boil or cavitate before reaching such tension. The controversy persisted until the mid-twentieth century, when **Per Scholander's** pressure-bomb measurements on tall trees and mangroves demonstrated xylem tensions consistent with the theory, and modern centrifuge and pressure-probe work has pushed the measured tensile strength of water in narrow conduits to the predicted range. The episode is a clean example of how a quantitative physical prediction, resisted on a priori grounds, was settled by instrumentation.

The pressure-flow hypothesis for the phloem is due to **Ernst Münch**, whose 1930 monograph *Die Stoffbewegungen in der Pflanze* laid out the osmotic-loading, turgor-driven, source-to-sink model still taught today [ref: TODO_REF Munch1930]. Münch's proposal competed with several "activated diffusion" and protoplasmic-streaming theories; the decisive tests came from aphid-stylet exudation (sap continues to flow out under pressure long after the aphid is severed) and from radiotracer experiments in the 1950s and 1960s showing directional, pressure-dependent movement of $^{14}$C-labelled sucrose.

Plant hormones began with **Charles and Francis Darwin's** 1880 experiments in *The Power of Movement in Plants*, which showed that the tip of a grass coleoptile senses light and transmits a "fluence" downward to the growing region where bending occurs [ref: TODO_REF Darwin1880]. **Boysen Jensen** (1913) and **Paál** (1919) showed the signal was a diffusible chemical substance; **Fritz Went** isolated and named it **auxin** in 1928. The stomatal-optimisation theorem of **Cowan and Farquhar** (1977) closed the loop by giving the field a quantitative principle connecting a molecular actuator (guard-cell turgor) to an economic optimum operating across biomes and climates [ref: TODO_REF CowanFarquhar1977]. Together these strands turn plant physiology from a catalogue of "what plants do" into a predictive science: a sessile organism that solves transport, energy, and information problems with mechanisms a physicist can model and a formalist can, in principle, check.
