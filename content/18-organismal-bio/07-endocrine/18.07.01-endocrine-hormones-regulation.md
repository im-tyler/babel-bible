## Intuition [Beginner]

The endocrine system is the body's chemical messaging network. Where the nervous system uses electrical signals travelling along wires (neurons), the endocrine system uses chemical signals — **hormones** — carried through the bloodstream to target cells throughout the body.

Hormones are produced by **endocrine glands** and released into the blood. A hormone travels everywhere the blood goes, but it affects only cells that have the right **receptor** — the molecular lock for which the hormone is the key. This is why thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) acts on the thyroid gland but not on the kidneys: only thyroid cells express TSH receptors.

Three main classes of hormones exist, distinguished by their chemical structure and how they signal:

**Peptide and protein hormones** (e.g., insulin, growth hormone) are large, water-soluble molecules that cannot cross the cell membrane. They bind to receptors on the cell surface and trigger signalling cascades inside the cell. Their effects are rapid (seconds to minutes) but generally short-lived.

**Steroid hormones** (e.g., cortisol, testosterone, oestrogen) are derived from cholesterol. They are lipid-soluble and cross the cell membrane to bind intracellular receptors, which then act as transcription factors to alter gene expression. Their effects are slower (hours to days) but longer-lasting.

**Amine hormones** (e.g., adrenaline, thyroxine) are derived from single amino acids. Adrenaline behaves like a peptide hormone (surface receptor); thyroxine behaves like a steroid (intracellular receptor, gene regulation).

## Visual [Beginner]

The **hypothalamic-pituitary axis** is the master control centre of the endocrine system. The hypothalamus, a small region at the base of the brain, produces releasing hormones that travel a short distance to the pituitary gland. The pituitary, in turn, releases tropic hormones that stimulate other endocrine glands throughout the body.

![The hypothalamic-pituitary axis showing the hierarchical control: hypothalamus releases CRH, TRH, GnRH, etc., which stimulate the anterior pituitary to release ACTH, TSH, FSH/LH, etc., which then act on target glands (adrenal cortex, thyroid, gonads). Negative feedback from target gland hormones inhibits both the pituitary and hypothalamus.](/img/18.07.01-hpa-axis-placeholder.svg)

**Negative feedback** is the dominant regulatory pattern. When the target gland produces enough hormone, that hormone travels back to the pituitary and hypothalamus and suppresses further stimulation. This is analogous to a thermostat: when the room is warm enough, the heater turns off. When the room cools, the heater turns back on. The result is a hormone level that oscillates around a set point.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Trace the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis in response to stress:

1. A stressful stimulus activates neurons in the hypothalamus.
2. **Hypothalamus** releases corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) into the portal blood vessels connecting the hypothalamus to the anterior pituitary.
3. **Anterior pituitary** responds to CRH by releasing adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) into the systemic circulation.
4. **Adrenal cortex** responds to ACTH by synthesising and releasing cortisol.
5. **Cortisol** acts on tissues throughout the body: mobilises glucose, suppresses the immune response, and prepares the body for sustained stress.
6. **Negative feedback**: Cortisol travels back to both the pituitary and the hypothalamus and inhibits further CRH and ACTH release, preventing overproduction.

Each step amplifies the signal. A small amount of CRH triggers a larger release of ACTH, which triggers an even larger release of cortisol. The negative feedback loop at the end ensures the response is proportionate and self-limiting.

Explain why oral administration of peptide hormones (e.g., insulin) is ineffective, while oral administration of steroid hormones (e.g., cortisol) can work.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Hypothalamic-pituitary axis

The hypothalamus produces the following releasing and inhibiting hormones, delivered to the anterior pituitary via the **hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal system** (a local capillary network):

| Hypothalamic hormone | Pituitary hormone released | Target gland |
|---|---|---|
| CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone) | ACTH | Adrenal cortex (cortisol) |
| TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone) | TSH | Thyroid (T3, T4) |
| GnRH (gonadotropin-releasing hormone) | FSH, LH | Gonads (sex steroids) |
| GHRH (growth hormone-releasing hormone) | GH | Liver (IGF-1), tissues |
| Dopamine (Prolactin-inhibiting hormone) | inhibits PRL | Breast (milk production) |

The **posterior pituitary** stores and releases two hormones produced in the hypothalamus and transported down axons: **antidiuretic hormone (ADH/vasopressin)**, which promotes water reabsorption in the kidney, and **oxytocin**, which promotes uterine contraction and milk ejection.

### Thyroid axis

The thyroid gland produces **thyroxine (T4)** and **triiodothyronine (T3)** from iodine and the amino acid tyrosine. T4 is the major secretory product (about 90%); T3 is the more active form (3-5 times greater receptor affinity). Peripheral tissues convert T4 to T3 via deiodinase enzymes.

Thyroid hormones set the basal metabolic rate by increasing the expression of metabolic enzymes and uncoupling proteins. They are essential for normal growth, brain development, and thermoregulation. The axis is regulated by negative feedback: T3 and T4 inhibit both TRH release from the hypothalamus and TSH release from the pituitary.

### Adrenal axis

The adrenal cortex produces three classes of steroid hormones, each from a distinct zone:
- **Glomerulosa zone**: mineralocorticoids (aldosterone) — regulate sodium and potassium balance.
- **Fasciculata zone**: glucocorticoids (cortisol) — regulate metabolism and stress response.
- **Reticularis zone**: androgens (DHEA) — weak sex steroids.

Cortisol follows a **circadian rhythm**, peaking in the early morning and reaching a nadir at midnight. This rhythm is driven by pulsatile CRH release from the hypothalamus, which itself is entrained by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (the brain's circadian clock).

### Insulin and glucagon

The pancreatic islets of Langerhans contain alpha cells (glucagon) and beta cells (insulin). These two hormones form a push-pull system regulating blood glucose:

- **Insulin** (released when blood glucose rises) promotes glucose uptake by cells, glycogen synthesis, fat storage, and protein synthesis. It lowers blood glucose.
- **Glucagon** (released when blood glucose falls) promotes glycogen breakdown, gluconeogenesis, and fat breakdown. It raises blood glucose.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Negative feedback stability).** *In a hormone cascade regulated by negative feedback, the steady-state concentration of the final hormone is determined by the set point of the feedback receptor sensitivity. Perturbations that increase or decrease the final hormone concentration produce corrective responses that restore the concentration toward the set point.*

**Proof.** Consider a simplified two-level cascade: the hypothalamus releases CRH at rate $r_C$, which stimulates ACTH release at rate $r_A = k_1 \cdot r_C$, which stimulates cortisol release at rate $r_{F} = k_2 \cdot r_A$. Cortisol exerts negative feedback by inhibiting both CRH and ACTH release: $r_C = r_{C,0} - \alpha \cdot [F]$ and $r_A = k_1 r_C - \beta \cdot [F]$, where $[F]$ is cortisol concentration, $r_{C,0}$ is the baseline CRH release rate, and $\alpha, \beta$ are feedback sensitivities.

At steady state, cortisol production equals cortisol clearance: $r_F = k_2 \cdot r_A = \gamma \cdot [F]$, where $\gamma$ is the clearance rate constant. Substituting: $\gamma [F] = k_2(k_1(r_{C,0} - \alpha [F]) - \beta [F])$. Solving for $[F]$:

$$
[F]_{ss} = \frac{k_1 k_2 r_{C,0}}{\gamma + k_2(k_1 \alpha + \beta)}.
$$

The denominator includes the feedback terms ($k_1 \alpha + \beta$), ensuring that $[F]_{ss}$ is finite and self-limiting. If $[F]$ transiently exceeds $[F]_{ss}$, the feedback terms increase suppression of $r_C$ and $r_A$, reducing production until $[F]$ returns to $[F]_{ss}$. The converse applies if $[F]$ falls below $[F]_{ss}$. The equilibrium is stable. $\square$

This analysis explains the clinical utility of the **dexamethasone suppression test**: administering a synthetic glucocorticoid should suppress ACTH and cortisol production in a normal subject (negative feedback intact). Failure to suppress suggests an autonomous cortisol-producing tumour (feedback-insensitive).

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, multiple choice).**

The anterior pituitary releases TSH in response to which hypothalamic hormone?

A. CRH
B. TRH
C. GnRH
D. GHRH

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

"T" in TSH stands for thyroid. Which hypothalamic hormone stimulates thyroid-related pituitary release?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone).** TRH from the hypothalamus stimulates the anterior pituitary to release TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone). CRH stimulates ACTH release; GnRH stimulates FSH/LH release; GHRH stimulates growth hormone release.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (medium, short answer).**

A patient has a pituitary tumour that produces excess ACTH. Predict the effect on: (a) CRH levels, (b) cortisol levels, (c) the response to a dexamethasone suppression test.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Excess ACTH drives excess cortisol. What does negative feedback do to CRH? Can dexamethasone suppress the tumour's ACTH production?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) CRH levels are **suppressed** — the excess cortisol from the tumour-driven ACTH exerts negative feedback on the hypothalamus. (b) Cortisol levels are **elevated** — the tumour produces excess ACTH regardless of feedback, driving the adrenal cortex to overproduce cortisol (Cushing disease). (c) Low-dose dexamethasone **fails to suppress** cortisol, because the pituitary tumour is partially autonomous. High-dose dexamethasone may partially suppress ACTH in pituitary-driven Cushing disease (distinguishing it from ectopic ACTH or adrenal tumours, where even high-dose dexamethasone fails).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, short answer).**

In type 1 diabetes mellitus, pancreatic beta cells are destroyed by an autoimmune process. Explain why blood glucose rises and why ketoacidosis may develop.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Without insulin, what happens to glucose uptake? What does the body use as an alternative fuel, and what are the by-products?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Without insulin, glucose cannot enter most cells (particularly muscle and adipose tissue), so blood glucose rises (hyperglycaemia). The glucose is excreted in urine (glycosuria) when the renal threshold is exceeded. Meanwhile, cells starved of glucose switch to metabolising fat. The liver converts fatty acids to ketone bodies (acetoacetate, beta-hydroxybutyrate), which are acidic. Accumulation of ketone bodies in the blood produces **diabetic ketoacidosis** — a dangerous fall in blood pH that can be fatal if untreated.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short answer).**

Explain how the same hormone (adrenaline) can cause different effects in different tissues. For example, it relaxes airway smooth muscle but contracts blood vessel smooth muscle.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The receptor type matters, not just the hormone. Which adrenergic receptor subtypes are expressed in each tissue?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Different tissues express different adrenergic receptor subtypes. Airway smooth muscle expresses beta-2 adrenergic receptors, which are coupled to relaxation (via cAMP increase). Blood vessel smooth muscle in the skin and gut expresses alpha-1 adrenergic receptors, which are coupled to contraction (via IP3 and calcium release). The hormone (adrenaline) is the same signal; the response depends on the receptor and its downstream signalling pathway. This principle — the same ligand producing different effects via different receptor subtypes — is called **receptor heterogeneity** and is a general feature of endocrine signalling.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

Model the thyroid axis as: TRH stimulates TSH at rate $\alpha \cdot [\text{TRH}]$; TSH stimulates T4 at rate $\beta \cdot [\text{TSH}]$; T4 feeds back to inhibit TRH at rate $-\gamma \cdot [\text{T4}]$. Assume TRH and TSH are cleared at rates $d_1$ and $d_2$ respectively, and T4 is cleared at rate $d_3$. Write the system of differential equations and find the steady-state T4 concentration.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

At steady state, production = clearance for each variable. Start with the T4 equation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The system:
$$
\frac{d[\text{TRH}]}{dt} = r_0 - \gamma [\text{T4}] - d_1 [\text{TRH}]
$$
$$
\frac{d[\text{TSH}]}{dt} = \alpha [\text{TRH}] - d_2 [\text{TSH}]
$$
$$
\frac{d[\text{T4}]}{dt} = \beta [\text{TSH}] - d_3 [\text{T4}]
$$

At steady state (all derivatives = 0):
From the T4 equation: $[\text{T4}]_{ss} = \beta [\text{TSH}]_{ss} / d_3$.
From the TSH equation: $[\text{TSH}]_{ss} = \alpha [\text{TRH}]_{ss} / d_2$.
From the TRH equation: $[\text{TRH}]_{ss} = (r_0 - \gamma [\text{T4}]_{ss}) / d_1$.

Substituting backward: $[\text{TSH}]_{ss} = \alpha(r_0 - \gamma [\text{T4}]_{ss}) / (d_1 d_2)$. Then $[\text{T4}]_{ss} = \beta \alpha (r_0 - \gamma [\text{T4}]_{ss}) / (d_1 d_2 d_3)$. Solving: $[\text{T4}]_{ss}(1 + \alpha \beta \gamma / (d_1 d_2 d_3)) = \alpha \beta r_0 / (d_1 d_2 d_3)$. Therefore:

$$
[\text{T4}]_{ss} = \frac{\alpha \beta r_0}{d_1 d_2 d_3 + \alpha \beta \gamma}.
$$

The feedback parameter $\gamma$ appears in the denominator, confirming that stronger feedback reduces steady-state T4 levels. In hypothyroidism (reduced $\beta$), $[\text{T4}]_{ss}$ falls, and $[\text{TSH}]_{ss}$ rises (due to reduced negative feedback) — the pattern seen in laboratory testing.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short answer).**

A patient takes high-dose exogenous glucocorticoids for an autoimmune condition. If the medication is abruptly stopped, the patient develops adrenal crisis (dangerously low cortisol). Explain why.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What does chronic high cortisol do to the HPA axis? How long does the suppressed axis take to recover?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Chronic exogenous glucocorticoids suppress the HPA axis at both the hypothalamic (CRH) and pituitary (ACTH) levels via negative feedback. With ACTH chronically suppressed, the adrenal cortex receives no stimulation and **atrophies** (shrinks) due to lack of tropic support. When the exogenous glucocorticoid is abruptly withdrawn, the atrophied adrenal cortex cannot immediately resume cortisol production. The result is **adrenal insufficiency** — dangerously low cortisol levels causing hypotension, hypoglycaemia, and potentially death. Recovery requires weeks to months as the adrenal cortex gradually regrows under restored ACTH stimulation. This is why glucocorticoids must be tapered slowly rather than stopped abruptly.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

Growth hormone (GH) excess in childhood causes gigantism; in adulthood, it causes acromegaly. Explain why the physical manifestations differ.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What structural change occurs at the growth plates (epiphyseal plates) at puberty that prevents further longitudinal bone growth?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In childhood, the epiphyseal (growth) plates at the ends of long bones are open. Excess GH (acting via IGF-1) stimulates continued longitudinal bone growth, producing gigantism (excessive height). After puberty, sex steroids cause the growth plates to fuse (epiphyseal closure). Once fused, long bones can no longer grow in length regardless of GH levels. However, GH still stimulates bone growth in width and soft tissue growth. In adults with GH excess, the result is **acromegaly**: enlarged hands, feet, and facial bones (particularly the jaw and brow ridges), along with enlargement of internal organs (heart, liver, tongue).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, short answer).**

ADH (antidiuretic hormone) is produced in the hypothalamus and released from the posterior pituitary. If the posterior pituitary is surgically removed, would ADH production cease? Explain.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

ADH is synthesised in the hypothalamus but only stored and released from the posterior pituitary. What happens to the producing neurons?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

ADH production would **not** completely cease, but its regulated release would be disrupted. ADH is synthesised by neurons in the supraoptic and paraventricular nuclei of the hypothalamus. These neurons project axons down the pituitary stalk to the posterior pituitary, where ADH is stored in nerve terminals and released into the blood. Removing the posterior pituitary eliminates the storage and release site, but the hypothalamic neurons can eventually reorganise and release ADH from their cut axon endings (which may grow into adjacent vascular tissue). In practice, posterior pituitary removal causes **transient diabetes insipidus** (excessive urination due to ADH deficiency), which often resolves as the axons regenerate functional release sites.

</details>

</aside>

## Endocrine pathophysiology and clinical integration [Master]

Endocrine disorders fundamentally reflect disturbances in feedback regulation:

**Hypothyroidism** can arise at any level of the thyroid axis. Primary hypothyroidism (thyroid gland failure) shows **low T4 with elevated TSH** (loss of negative feedback from T4). Secondary hypothyroidism (pituitary failure) shows **low T4 with low or inappropriately normal TSH**. The pattern of TSH relative to T4 reveals the anatomical level of the lesion — a principle applicable to all endocrine axes.

**Cushing syndrome** (cortisol excess) from an adrenal cortical adenoma shows **high cortisol with low ACTH** (suppressed by negative feedback). Cushing disease (pituitary ACTH-secreting adenoma) shows **high cortisol with high or inappropriately normal ACTH**. Ectopic ACTH production (e.g., small cell lung cancer) shows **high cortisol with very high ACTH**. The dexamethasone suppression test exploits the differential sensitivity of these sources to glucocorticoid feedback.

**Multiple endocrine neoplasia (MEN)** syndromes illustrate that a single genetic defect can produce tumours in multiple endocrine glands simultaneously, reflecting the shared developmental origin and regulatory interconnections of endocrine tissues.

## Connections [Master]

- **Cell signalling [17.07.01]** is the molecular basis of all hormone action. Peptide hormones use G-protein-coupled receptors and second messengers (cAMP, IP3/DAG); steroid hormones use nuclear receptors and transcriptional regulation; the same signalling pathways are used in non-endocrine contexts.

- **Nervous system [18.05.01]** and endocrine system are linked at the hypothalamus. Neuroendocrine cells in the hypothalamus are neurons that secrete hormones into the blood rather than neurotransmitters at synapses. The pituitary is the anatomical interface between the two systems.

- **Cardiovascular physiology [18.02.01]** is modulated by adrenaline (heart rate and contractility), ADH (blood volume and pressure via water retention), aldosterone (blood volume via sodium retention), and atrial natriuretic peptide (blood pressure reduction).

- **Renal physiology [18.08.01]** is regulated by ADH (water reabsorption), aldosterone (sodium reabsorption and potassium excretion), and parathyroid hormone (calcium and phosphate handling). The kidney is both a target and an endocrine organ (producing erythropoietin and renin).

- **Cell cycle [17.08.01]** is influenced by growth hormone, IGF-1, thyroid hormones, and sex steroids, all of which promote cell division and differentiation. Hormonal dysregulation can contribute to uncontrolled cell proliferation (cancer).

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Bayliss and Starling coined the term "hormone" (from the Greek "to excite") in 1902 after discovering that pancreatic secretion could be stimulated by a blood-borne chemical (secretin) rather than only by neural signals. This established the principle of chemical signalling as distinct from neural communication and opened the field of endocrinology.

The hypothalamic control of the pituitary was established by Geoffrey Harris in the 1940s-50s, who demonstrated that the portal blood vessels linking the hypothalamus to the anterior pituitary carried releasing factors. The specific releasing hormones (TRH, GnRH, CRH, GHRH) were isolated and characterised by Schally and Guillemin in the 1960s-70s (Nobel Prize 1977).

The concept of negative feedback in biology predates its formalisation in engineering. Claude Bernard's concept of the "milieu interieur" (internal environment, 1865) and Walter Cannon's "homeostasis" (1932) identified the regulatory principle without the mathematical framework. The formalisation came from control theory (Wiener's cybernetics, 1948), which provided the quantitative tools for analysing hormone cascades.

Philosophically, the endocrine system illustrates **decentralised control**: there is no single "master gland" in the simplistic sense. The pituitary is controlled by the hypothalamus, which is modulated by neural inputs from higher brain centres and by hormonal feedback from target glands. The result is a distributed regulatory network in which no single node has complete control — a pattern familiar from engineering and computer science.
