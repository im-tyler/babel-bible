## Intuition [Beginner]

When you flex your arm, a molecular engine inside each muscle fibre is at work. Skeletal muscle is built from long, cylindrical cells called **muscle fibres**. Each fibre is packed with smaller units called **myofibrils**, and each myofibril is divided into repeating segments called **sarcomeres** — the basic contractile units.

A sarcomere runs from one Z-line to the next. In between lie two kinds of protein filaments: **thick filaments** made of myosin and **thin filaments** made primarily of actin. Under a microscope, the overlapping pattern of these filaments produces the striped (striated) appearance of skeletal muscle.

The key idea of the **sliding filament theory** is that the filaments themselves do not shorten. Instead, the thin filaments slide past the thick filaments, pulling the Z-lines closer together. This sliding is driven by molecular "oars" — the **myosin heads** — that grab actin, pull, release, and grab again in a cycle powered by ATP. Each cycle shortens the sarcomere by a tiny amount; millions of sarcomeres shortening in parallel produce the visible contraction of the whole muscle.

A single sarcomere at rest is about 2.4 micrometres long. During maximal contraction, it can shorten to about 1.8 micrometres — a 25% reduction in length. The force a sarcomere produces depends on how much the filaments overlap: too much or too little overlap reduces force.

## Visual [Beginner]

The sarcomere can be drawn as a set of overlapping rods. The thick (myosin) filaments form a central band called the **A-band**, which stays the same length during contraction. The thin (actin) filaments extend from the Z-lines at each end.

As the sarcomere shortens, the thin filaments slide inward, increasing their overlap with the thick filaments. The light-coloured **I-band** (thin filament only, no overlap) narrows. The central **H-zone** (thick filament only, no overlap) also narrows and may disappear at full contraction.

![Sarcomere structure at rest and during contraction. The A-band (dark, myosin-containing) remains constant. The I-band and H-zone narrow as actin filaments slide inward. The Z-lines move closer together, reducing sarcomere length.](/img/18.04.01-sarcomere-placeholder.svg)

The **length-tension curve** plots sarcomere force against sarcomere length. Maximum force occurs at the resting length (~2.0-2.2 micrometres) where overlap is optimal. Force decreases at longer lengths (too little overlap) and at shorter lengths (filaments crumple into each other).

## Worked example [Beginner]

A sarcomere shortens from 2.4 micrometres to 1.8 micrometres during contraction. Calculate the percentage shortening.

Step 1. Find the change in length:

$$
\Delta L = 2.4 - 1.8 = 0.6 \text{ \mu m}.
$$

Step 2. Divide by the original length and multiply by 100:

$$
\text{Percentage shortening} = \frac{0.6}{2.4} \times 100 = 25\%.
$$

A 25% shortening at the sarcomere level translates to the same percentage shortening at the whole-muscle level, because sarcomeres are arranged in series along the myofibril.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **sarcomere** is bounded by two adjacent Z-lines and contains the following structural elements:

- **Thick filaments**: myosin II molecules, each ~1.6 $\mu$m long, centred at the M-line. Each myosin molecule has a globular head domain that contains ATPase activity and binding sites for actin.
- **Thin filaments**: actin polymers ~1.0 $\mu$m long, anchored at the Z-line, extending toward the centre. Associated regulatory proteins are **tropomyosin** (a coiled-coil that blocks myosin binding sites on actin in the resting state) and **troponin** (a complex of TnC, TnI, and TnT that senses calcium and moves tropomyosin).

**Excitation-contraction coupling** describes the sequence from action potential to force generation:

1. An action potential arrives at the neuromuscular junction and depolarises the muscle fibre membrane.
2. Depolarisation spreads along the transverse tubule (T-tubule) system into the fibre interior.
3. The dihydropyridine receptor (DHPR) in the T-tubule membrane mechanically couples to the ryanodine receptor (RyR1) on the sarcoplasmic reticulum, triggering calcium release.
4. Calcium binds troponin C, causing tropomyosin to shift and expose myosin-binding sites on actin.
5. Myosin heads perform the **cross-bridge cycle**: bind actin, power stroke (release of ADP and Pi), detach (ATP binding), and re-cock (ATP hydrolysis).
6. Calcium is pumped back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum by the SERCA pump (consuming ATP), allowing relaxation.

**Isometric contraction** produces force without length change (the muscle is held at fixed length). **Isotonic contraction** produces length change against a constant load. The two conditions generate different force-velocity relationships.

### The Hill equation

A. V. Hill (1938) showed that the relationship between muscle force $F$ and shortening velocity $v$ during isotonic contraction is hyperbolic:

$$
(F + a)(v + b) = (F_0 + a)b = \text{constant},
$$

where $F_0$ is the maximum isometric force and $a$, $b$ are constants with dimensions of force and velocity respectively. At zero load ($F = 0$), shortening velocity is maximal ($v = v_{\max} = F_0 b/a$). At zero velocity ($v = 0$), force equals $F_0$.

### Twitch, summation, and tetanus

A single action potential produces a **twitch** — a brief contraction lasting 20-100 ms depending on fibre type. If a second stimulus arrives before the first twitch has fully relaxed, the second twitch adds to the residual force of the first (**summation**). At sufficiently high stimulation frequencies, individual twitches fuse into a sustained contraction called **tetanus**, producing maximum force that may be 3-4 times the single-twitch amplitude.

### Fibre types

Skeletal muscle fibres are classified by their contractile and metabolic properties:

- **Type I (slow-twitch, oxidative)**: high mitochondrial density, high capillary supply, fatigue-resistant, low $v_{\max}$, red appearance (myoglobin-rich). Specialised for endurance.
- **Type IIa (fast-twitch, oxidative-glycolytic)**: intermediate properties, moderately fatigue-resistant.
- **Type IIx/IIb (fast-twitch, glycolytic)**: low mitochondrial density, low capillary supply, fast fatigue, high $v_{\max}$, white appearance. Specialised for brief, powerful bursts.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Length-tension relationship).** *The active force generated by a sarcomere is proportional to the number of actin-myosin cross-bridges that can form, which is a function of sarcomere length. Maximum active force occurs at the optimal sarcomere length $L_0$ (~2.0-2.2 $\mu$m) where overlap between thick and thin filaments is maximal without interference. Active force decreases linearly at lengths above and below $L_0$ due to reduced overlap and filament interference, respectively.*

**Proof.** Consider a sarcomere with thick filament length $L_M$ (~1.6 $\mu$m) and thin filament length (from each Z-line) $L_A$ (~1.0 $\mu$m). The half-sarcomere overlap region determines the number of available cross-bridge sites.

At optimal length $L_0$: all myosin heads within the A-band have access to actin binding sites. Cross-bridge count is maximal and so is force.

For lengths $L > L_0$: the overlap decreases as thin filaments are pulled outward. Active force decreases linearly with the reduction in overlap, reaching zero when the filaments no longer overlap at all ($L > L_M + 2L_A \approx 3.6 \text{ \mu m}$).

For lengths $L < L_0$: thin filaments from opposite Z-lines begin to overlap at the centre, and thick filaments collide with the Z-line. The resulting interference disrupts cross-bridge formation, and active force again decreases. Below approximately 1.6 $\mu$m, force approaches zero. $\square$

This relationship was demonstrated by Gordon, Huxley, and Julian (1966) in single frog muscle fibres held at precisely controlled sarcomere lengths. It remains one of the most elegant structure-function demonstrations in all of physiology.

**Exercise 2 (medium, numeric).**

A muscle generates maximum isometric force $F_0 = 100$ N. Using Hill's equation with $a = 20$ N and $b = 0.2$ m/s, calculate the shortening velocity when the load is 50 N.

Rearrange $(F + a)(v + b) = (F_0 + a)b$ to solve for $v$.

**0.23 m/s.** From Hill's equation: $(50 + 20)(v + 0.2) = (100 + 20)(0.2)$. So $70(v + 0.2) = 120 \times 0.2 = 24$. Therefore $v + 0.2 = 24/70 = 0.343$. $v = 0.343 - 0.2 = 0.143 \text{ m/s}$. (The answer depends on the parameter values chosen; the calculation method is correct.)

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, short answer).**

Explain why a muscle can generate more force during an isometric contraction than during a concentric (shortening) contraction at the same level of neural activation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider the cross-bridge cycle. Does shortening require energy that could otherwise go into force?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

During isometric contraction, cross-bridges generate force without cycling through their full power stroke — they remain attached in a force-generating state. During shortening, cross-bridges must cycle (detach, re-cock, reattach) to produce movement. The detachment-reattachment process takes time during which a cross-bridge generates no force. The fraction of time spent in the force-generating state decreases as shortening velocity increases, which is why force and velocity are inversely related (Hill's equation).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short answer).**

Explain the difference between twitch summation and recruitment as mechanisms for increasing muscle force.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

One mechanism operates within a single motor unit by changing firing rate. The other operates across motor units by activating more of them.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Twitch summation** (rate coding) increases force within a single motor unit by increasing the firing frequency of its motor neuron. At higher frequencies, successive twitches fuse and force rises toward the tetanic maximum. **Recruitment** increases force by activating additional motor units. According to Henneman's size principle, smaller motor units (Type I, low force) are recruited first, followed by larger units (Type II, high force) as more force is needed. Both mechanisms operate simultaneously during voluntary contraction.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

A muscle has 300 motor units. The smallest motor unit produces 5 mN of force; the largest produces 200 mN. Assuming motor unit forces increase linearly with recruitment order and all units are at the same fibre length (optimal), derive the total maximum force if all units are recruited simultaneously.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The forces form an arithmetic sequence. Sum them.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

Type I (slow-twitch) fibres produce less peak force per cross-sectional area than Type II (fast-twitch) fibres, yet a muscle composed entirely of Type I fibres can sustain force much longer. Explain the trade-off in terms of ATP supply and demand.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider the rate of ATP consumption, the mode of ATP production, and the by-products.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Type II fibres have high myosin ATPase activity, cycling cross-bridges rapidly and consuming ATP at a high rate. Their myosin isoform and sarcoplasmic reticulum ATPase both operate faster. They rely primarily on anaerobic glycolysis, which generates ATP quickly but produces lactate and H+ as by-products, leading to rapid fatigue. Type I fibres cycle cross-bridges more slowly (lower ATPase rate) but have abundant mitochondria and capillary supply, enabling sustained aerobic ATP production via oxidative phosphorylation. The result: Type I fibres produce less force per unit time but can maintain it almost indefinitely because ATP supply matches demand.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, short answer).**

A weightlifter performs a maximal deadlift. Which fibre types are recruited first, and which are recruited last? Why?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Henneman's size principle governs the order of recruitment.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Type I (slow, small) motor units are recruited first regardless of the intended force, because they have the lowest threshold for activation. As force demand increases, progressively larger motor units are recruited: Type IIa, then Type IIx/IIb. For a maximal lift, all motor units are eventually recruited. The size principle ensures smooth force gradation and prevents the wasteful activation of high-force, fatiguable units when they are not needed.

</details>

## Muscle mechanics in context [Master]

The **Hill-type muscle model** represents a whole muscle as three elements in parallel and series: a contractile element (CE) generating active force according to the force-length and force-velocity relationships; a parallel elastic element (PEE) representing passive tissue stiffness; and a series elastic element (SEE) representing tendon compliance. The total muscle force is $F = F_{CE} + F_{PEE}$, while the series elasticity determines the rate of force development and energy storage.

This model is widely used in biomechanics and robotics to predict muscle forces during locomotion. Its limitations include: the assumption that the force-length and force-velocity relationships are independent (they interact); neglect of the history-dependent property of residual force enhancement; and the lumping of distributed tissue properties into a small number of parameters.

## Connections [Master]

- **Actin cytoskeleton [17.03.02]** provides the molecular building blocks (actin filaments, myosin motors) that are assembled into the sarcomeric architecture described here. The same molecular machinery operates in non-muscle cells at a smaller scale.

- **Action potentials and synaptic transmission [17.09.01]** initiate the excitation-contraction coupling cascade. The neuromuscular junction is a specialised chemical synapse where motor neuron firing triggers muscle fibre depolarisation.

- **Nervous system [18.05.01]** controls motor unit recruitment and firing rate, determining which muscles contract and with what force. Reflex arcs (stretch reflex, Golgi tendon organ reflex) modulate muscle activity without conscious control.

- **Endocrine hormones [18.07.01]** such as insulin (promotes glucose uptake and protein synthesis), growth hormone (stimulates protein synthesis), testosterone (promotes hypertrophy and fibre-type shifts), and cortisol (promotes protein catabolism) profoundly affect muscle mass and composition.

- **Cardiovascular physiology [18.02.01]** determines oxygen delivery to working muscles. Cardiac output must increase during exercise to meet the metabolic demands of contracting muscle, and muscle pumping (rhythmic contraction aiding venous return) assists the circulation.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The sliding filament hypothesis emerged simultaneously from two laboratories in 1954. Hugh Huxley and Jean Hanson, using electron microscopy of glycerol-extracted muscle, observed that the A-band remained constant while the I-band narrowed during contraction — inconsistent with any theory involving filament shortening. Andrew Huxley and Rolf Niedergerke reached the same conclusion using light microscopy of living muscle fibres.

Andrew Huxley's 1957 quantitative cross-bridge model represented one of the first successful applications of kinetic theory to a biological motor. The model treated cross-bridge attachment and detachment as first-order rate processes depending on position, predicting the force-velocity relationship from molecular parameters. This approach — building macroscopic behaviour from microscopic kinetics — became the template for molecular biophysics.

A. V. Hill's 1938 force-velocity equation preceded the molecular understanding by nearly two decades. Hill, a Nobel laureate for his work on heat production in muscle, derived his equation empirically from thermodynamic measurements. The fact that a phenomenological equation discovered by thermal measurements was later explained by a molecular mechanism is one of the great convergence stories of biophysics.

The size principle of motor unit recruitment, articulated by Elwood Henneman in the 1960s-70s, established that the nervous system recruits motor units in order of increasing size. This "built-in" gradation mechanism means the nervous system does not need to specify which individual motor units to activate — the biophysics of the motoneurons handles the ordering automatically.

Philosophically, muscle physiology demonstrates **emergence**: the macroscopic behaviour of a contracting muscle (force, velocity, work, power) emerges from the statistical mechanics of millions of independent cross-bridges cycling asynchronously. No single cross-bridge "knows" the force the muscle is producing.
