## Intuition [Beginner]

The nervous system is the body's communication network. It gathers information from the environment and from inside the body, processes that information, and coordinates responses. Three functions define it: **sensory input** (detecting stimuli), **integration** (interpreting the input), and **motor output** (commanding a response).

The nervous system has two major divisions. The **central nervous system (CNS)** consists of the brain and spinal cord. It is the integration and command centre. The **peripheral nervous system (PNS)** consists of all the nerves that connect the CNS to the rest of the body. It carries sensory information in and motor commands out.

The PNS itself has two functional subdivisions. The **somatic nervous system** controls voluntary movements of skeletal muscles. The **autonomic nervous system** controls involuntary functions such as heart rate, digestion, and pupil dilation. The autonomic division further splits into the **sympathetic** branch (activates the body for "fight or flight") and the **parasympathetic** branch (promotes "rest and digest" activities).

Information travels through the nervous system as electrical signals (action potentials) along neurons, and as chemical signals (neurotransmitters) across synapses. A single neuron can receive inputs from thousands of other neurons, integrate those signals, and pass the result along. This massive parallel processing is what makes the nervous system so powerful.

## Visual [Beginner]

The brain can be divided into four main regions, each with distinct functions. The **cerebrum** (the largest part, divided into left and right hemispheres) handles conscious thought, language, and voluntary movement. The **cerebellum** coordinates balance and fine motor control. The **brainstem** (midbrain, pons, medulla) controls vital functions such as breathing and heart rate. The **diencephalon** (thalamus and hypothalamus) relays sensory information and regulates homeostasis.

![Sagittal section of the brain showing the four main regions: cerebrum, cerebellum, brainstem, and diencephalon. The spinal cord exits from the medulla. Major landmarks labelled.](/img/18.05.01-brain-sagittal-placeholder.svg)

The **spinal cord** runs inside the vertebral column from the brainstem to the lower back. It carries sensory information up to the brain and motor commands down from the brain. It also contains local circuits called **reflex arcs** that can produce rapid responses without waiting for input from the brain.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Trace the knee-jerk (patellar) reflex:

1. A tap on the patellar tendon stretches the quadriceps muscle.
2. **Sensory neuron**: Muscle stretch receptors (muscle spindles) detect the stretch and fire action potentials.
3. **Spinal cord**: The sensory neuron synapses directly onto a motor neuron in the spinal cord (a monosynaptic reflex).
4. **Motor neuron**: The motor neuron fires and sends action potentials back to the quadriceps muscle.
5. **Response**: The quadriceps contracts, extending the lower leg.

This entire loop takes about 30-50 milliseconds. It does not involve the brain — the decision is made in the spinal cord. This is why the knee jerk happens before you are consciously aware of the tap.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The nervous system is organised hierarchically:

**Central nervous system (CNS):**
- **Cerebrum**: Paired hemispheres with an outer layer of grey matter (**cerebral cortex**, ~2-4 mm thick) and inner white matter. The cortex is divided into four lobes (frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital) and contains functional areas for motor control (primary motor cortex, precentral gyrus), sensory processing (primary somatosensory cortex, postcentral gyrus), vision (occipital cortex), audition (temporal cortex), and language (Broca's area, Wernicke's area).
- **Basal ganglia**: Deep nuclei (caudate, putamen, globus pallidus, subthalamic nucleus, substantia nigra) involved in movement selection and habit learning.
- **Thalamus**: Relay station for nearly all sensory information ascending to the cortex.
- **Hypothalamus**: Master regulator of homeostasis — controls body temperature, hunger, thirst, circadian rhythm, and the pituitary gland.
- **Cerebellum**: Coordinates motor timing, balance, and motor learning. Contains more neurons than the rest of the brain combined.
- **Brainstem**: Midbrain, pons, and medulla oblongata. Contains cranial nerve nuclei, reticular formation (arousal), and vital centres for breathing, heart rate, and blood pressure.

**Spinal cord:**
- Segmented into 31 pairs of spinal nerves (8 cervical, 12 thoracic, 5 lumbar, 5 sacral, 1 coccygeal).
- Grey matter (butterfly-shaped central region) contains neuronal cell bodies: dorsal horn (sensory), ventral horn (motor), lateral horn (autonomic, thoracolumbar only).
- White matter (surrounding region) contains ascending (sensory) and descending (motor) tracts of myelinated axons.

**Peripheral nervous system (PNS):**
- **Somatic**: Motor axons to skeletal muscle; sensory axons from skin, muscle, and joints.
- **Autonomic**: Two-neuron chains (preganglionic and postganglionic) innervating smooth muscle, cardiac muscle, and glands.
  - **Sympathetic**: Preganglionic fibres originate in thoracolumbar spinal cord (T1-L2). Short preganglionic, long postganglionic fibres. Primary neurotransmitter at target: norepinephrine. Function: mobilise body for action.
  - **Parasympathetic**: Preganglionic fibres originate in brainstem (cranial nerves III, VII, IX, X) and sacral spinal cord (S2-S4). Long preganglionic, short postganglionic fibres. Primary neurotransmitter at target: acetylcholine. Function: conserve and restore body resources.

### Reflex arcs

A **reflex arc** is the simplest neural circuit producing a stereotyped response to a specific stimulus. Its components are:

1. **Receptor** — detects the stimulus.
2. **Sensory (afferent) neuron** — carries signal to the CNS.
3. **Integration centre** — one or more synapses in the CNS (monosynaptic or polysynaptic).
4. **Motor (efferent) neuron** — carries command to the effector.
5. **Effector** — the muscle or gland that responds.

Monosynaptic reflexes (stretch reflex) have the shortest latency. Polysynaptic reflexes (withdrawal reflex, cross-extensor reflex) involve interneurons and can produce more complex, coordinated responses.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Autonomic dual innervation and antagonism).** *Most internal organs receive dual innervation from both the sympathetic and parasympathetic divisions. These two inputs generally produce opposite effects on the target organ. The resting level of organ activity is determined by the balance (tonic firing rates) of the two autonomic branches.*

**Proof.** Consider the heart as a paradigmatic example. The parasympathetic input arrives via the vagus nerve (cranial nerve X), releasing acetylcholine onto muscarinic receptors on the pacemaker cells. This slows the heart rate. The sympathetic input arrives via postganglionic fibres from the cervical and upper thoracic ganglia, releasing norepinephrine onto beta-1 adrenergic receptors. This increases heart rate and contractility.

At rest, the vagus fires tonically at a moderate rate, keeping the heart rate around 70-80 bpm. Cutting the vagus (vagotomy) raises heart rate to about 100 bpm, revealing the intrinsic pacemaker rate. Cutting sympathetic input lowers heart rate slightly. Cutting both leaves the heart beating at its intrinsic rate (~100 bpm). The resting heart rate is therefore below the intrinsic rate because vagal (parasympathetic) tone dominates at rest.

The same pattern of dual, antagonistic innervation applies to the gastrointestinal tract (sympathetic inhibits motility; parasympathetic promotes it), the pupils (sympathetic dilates; parasympathetic constricts), and the respiratory airways (sympathetic dilates bronchi; parasympathetic constricts them). $\square$

This antagonistic organisation provides fine-grained control: rather than a single input that can only be turned up or down, the two divisions allow bidirectional adjustment around a set point.

**Exercise 2 (medium, short answer).**

A patient has a stroke that damages the left side of the brainstem. Would you expect motor deficits on the left or right side of the body? Explain why.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Most motor pathways cross over (decussate) from one side to the other. At what level does this crossing occur?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Right side.** The corticospinal tract, which carries motor commands from the cortex to the spinal cord, decussates (crosses) at the pyramids in the lower medulla. A lesion in the left brainstem is below the cortical level but above or at the level of decussation, so motor deficits appear on the contralateral (right) side. <aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, short answer).**

The vagus nerve (cranial nerve X) carries parasympathetic fibres to the heart, lungs, and much of the GI tract. Explain why a drug that blocks muscarinic acetylcholine receptors (an anticholinergic) would increase heart rate.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What is the normal effect of vagal acetylcholine on the heart? Blocking this receptor removes that effect.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The vagus nerve tonically releases acetylcholine onto muscarinic receptors on the heart's pacemaker cells, slowing the heart rate below its intrinsic rate (~100 bpm). An anticholinergic drug blocks these muscarinic receptors, removing the vagal brake. Heart rate increases toward the intrinsic rate. At high doses, this produces tachycardia (abnormally fast heart rate), which is one of the side effects of anticholinergic medications.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short answer).**

Trace the neural pathway for the withdrawal reflex (touching a hot object): from receptor to effector. Is this reflex monosynaptic or polysynaptic?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The withdrawal reflex involves both pulling the hand away and stabilising the posture. Does it involve interneurons?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

1. Nociceptors (pain receptors) in the skin detect the heat. 2. Sensory neurons carry the signal to the dorsal horn of the spinal cord. 3. Interneurons in the spinal cord process the signal: excitatory interneurons activate motor neurons to the flexor muscles of the affected limb (withdrawal); inhibitory interneurons suppress motor neurons to the extensor muscles of the same limb (reciprocal inhibition); and via commissural interneurons, excitatory interneurons activate motor neurons to the extensor muscles of the contralateral limb (cross-extensor reflex for postural support). 4. Motor neurons command the muscles. This is a polysynaptic reflex because it involves interneurons between the sensory and motor neurons.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, short answer).**

The sympathetic and parasympathetic systems both use acetylcholine at the ganglion (the synapse between pre- and postganglionic neurons). However, the sympathetic system uses norepinephrine at the target organ while the parasympathetic uses acetylcholine at the target organ. Explain the pharmacological significance of this difference.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If the neurotransmitters differ at the target organ, drugs can selectively affect one branch without affecting the other. How is this used clinically?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Because the sympathetic postganglionic fibres release norepinephrine onto adrenergic receptors while parasympathetic fibres release acetylcholine onto muscarinic receptors, drugs can selectively target one branch. Beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol) block sympathetic beta-adrenergic receptors, reducing heart rate and blood pressure without affecting parasympathetic functions. Similarly, muscarinic agonists or antagonists selectively modulate parasympathetic target organs. This pharmacological selectivity is the basis for treating hypertension, asthma, glaucoma, and many other conditions by targeting one branch of the autonomic nervous system.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short answer).**

The somatotopic map in the primary motor cortex devotes disproportionate area to the hands, face, and tongue compared to the trunk and legs, even though the trunk and legs are much larger body parts. What does this reveal about the neural control of movement?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What kind of movements do the hands, face, and tongue perform compared to the trunk and legs?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Cortical representation is proportional not to body part size but to **motor precision** (the number of independently controllable motor units). The hands have many small muscles with fine graduated control (typing, writing, tool use); the face and tongue control speech articulation and facial expression. The trunk and legs, despite being larger, have fewer independently controlled degrees of freedom. The somatotopic map therefore reflects the computational demands of motor control: brain regions that require fine-grained, fractionated movements receive more cortical processing resources. This is often depicted as a distorted human figure (the motor homunculus) with giant hands and lips.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Suppose a spinal cord injury severs all ascending (sensory) tracts at the T10 level but spares all descending (motor) tracts. Would the patient be able to: (a) sense touch on the left foot, (b) voluntarily move the left foot, (c) exhibit the knee-jerk reflex? Explain each.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Ascending tracts carry sensory information to the brain. Descending tracts carry motor commands from the brain. Reflex arcs are contained within the spinal cord itself.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) **No.** Sensory information from the left foot (below T10) must travel up the ascending tracts to reach the brain. These are severed, so conscious sensation is lost below the level of injury. (b) **No.** Although the descending motor tracts are anatomically intact at the lesion site, voluntary movement requires both an intact motor command pathway (descending) AND sensory feedback (ascending) for effective motor control. More precisely: if only ascending tracts are cut, the motor cortex can still send commands down the intact descending tracts, so the patient should retain voluntary movement. However, without proprioceptive feedback, the movement would be poorly coordinated. Actually, re-reading the problem: if descending tracts are spared, then yes, the patient **can** voluntarily move the foot, but without sensory feedback they would not feel the movement. (c) **Yes.** The knee-jerk reflex is a spinal reflex — its entire circuit (sensory neuron to motor neuron) is contained within the spinal cord below T10. Neither ascending nor descending tracts are required for the reflex itself.

</details>

</aside>

## Neural circuits and information processing [Master]

The cerebral cortex is organised into six layers (I-VI) with distinct cell types and connectivity patterns. Layer IV receives thalamic input (sensory); layers II and III make corticocortical connections; layer V sends output to subcortical structures (including the spinal cord); layer VI sends feedback to the thalamus. This **canonical microcircuit** is repeated across cortical areas with variations that produce functional specialisation.

The **basal ganglia** implement a selection circuit: competing motor programs are proposed by the cortex, and the basal ganglia select one by disinhibiting the appropriate motor pathway. Dopamine from the substantia nigra pars compacta modulates this selection. Loss of dopaminergic neurons in Parkinson disease impairs the ability to initiate movements; excess dopaminergic signalling in Huntington disease produces unwanted movements.

The **cerebellum** functions as an adaptive filter and error-correction system. It receives copies of motor commands (via the inferior olive and pontine nuclei) and sensory feedback, computes the discrepancy (motor error), and adjusts the motor output to reduce error on subsequent attempts. This is the neural substrate of motor learning — the process by which a clumsy, effortful movement becomes smooth and automatic.

## Connections [Master]

- **Action potentials and synaptic transmission [17.09.01]** are the cellular mechanisms underlying every function described in this unit. Gross anatomy is meaningful because it reveals how populations of neurons are wired together.

- **Cell signalling and signal transduction [17.07.01]** operates at synapses. Neurotransmitters binding to receptors trigger second-messenger cascades identical to those in non-neural cells.

- **Skeletal muscle physiology [18.04.01]** is the output side of the motor system. Motor neurons activate muscle fibres, and sensory feedback from muscles (muscle spindles, Golgi tendon organs) returns to the spinal cord.

- **Endocrine hormones [18.07.01]** are regulated by the hypothalamus, which controls the pituitary gland. The hypothalamic-pituitary axis is the anatomical bridge between the nervous and endocrine systems.

- **Cardiovascular physiology [18.02.01]** depends on autonomic control of heart rate, contractility, and vascular tone. The medullary cardiovascular centre integrates baroreceptor input and adjusts autonomic output.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Santiago Ramon y Cajal, using the silver staining method developed by Camillo Golgi, established the **neuron doctrine** in the late 19th century: the nervous system is composed of individual cells (neurons) that communicate at specialised junctions (synapses), rather than being a continuous reticulum. Cajal's 1904 *Textura del Sistema Nervioso* remains one of the most influential works in neuroscience. Golgi, ironically, argued against the neuron doctrine despite providing the tool that proved it.

Charles Sherrington's *The Integrative Action of the Nervous System* (1906) introduced the concepts of synaptic integration, excitation, inhibition, and the reflex arc. Sherrington coined the term "synapse" and demonstrated reciprocal inhibition experimentally. His work established the spinal cord as an integration centre, not merely a conduit.

The somatotopic map was first demonstrated by Wilder Penfield in the 1930s-50s using direct electrical stimulation of exposed human cortex during neurosurgery. His motor and sensory "homunculi" remain iconic representations of cortical organisation.

The autonomic nervous system was characterised by John Langley in the 1890s-1900s, who distinguished sympathetic from parasympathetic divisions based on anatomical origin, ganglion location, and pharmacological responses. Langley introduced the terms "autonomic" and the concept of receptor specificity.

Philosophically, the nervous system raises the **mind-body problem** in its sharpest form: how does physical neural activity give rise to subjective experience (consciousness)? The brain regions described in this unit — particularly the cerebral cortex and thalamus — are the neural correlates of conscious awareness, but the explanatory gap between neural firing patterns and first-person experience remains open. This problem is taken up in the philosophy of mind units [20.x].
