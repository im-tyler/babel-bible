## Intuition [Beginner]

A massless particle moves at the speed of light, and there is no frame in which it sits still. That single fact reshapes how its field is described. A particle with rest mass, like an electron, can be brought to rest and turned slowly in your hands; you can ask which way its spin points in any direction. A massless particle never stops, so its spin can only be read along its direction of travel. The two readings you ever get are "spinning the way a right-handed screw advances" or the left-handed mirror image. This single, sign-carrying number is called the helicity.

Spinors are the natural language for this story. A spinor is the squarest-possible root of a direction in spacetime: where a velocity arrow needs four numbers, a spinor needs two complex ones, and a velocity along the light cone is built by squaring a spinor. Light-cone directions and spinors fit together perfectly, which is why massless fields look simplest when written with spinors.

The punchline of this unit is that one compact spinor equation, with a tower of indices whose length records the spin, contains the source-free Maxwell equations, the equation for a massless spin-half particle, and (with a small twist) the Dirac equation, all at once.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the electromagnetic field. In the usual telling it is six numbers: a three-component electric field and a three-component magnetic field, packaged into the antisymmetric field tensor with components $F_{ab}$. Counting carefully, an antisymmetric pair of four-valued indices has six independent slots, matching the six numbers.

Now repackage. The six real numbers can be rewritten as three complex numbers, namely the electric field plus $i$ times the magnetic field. These three complex numbers are exactly the independent components of a symmetric spinor with two lower indices, written $\phi_{AB}$, which also has three slots because a symmetric pair of two-valued indices has three. The dictionary is a clean match: six real becomes three complex becomes the spinor $\phi_{AB}$.

What this buys us: the four Maxwell equations, in empty space, collapse into the single statement that a certain spinor derivative of $\phi_{AB}$ is zero. The two source-free Maxwell equations that say the divergence and the curl behave correctly are repackaged into one tidy spinor equation. The same trick, with a shorter or longer tower of indices, handles spin one half and spin two.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

We work on Minkowski space (and later a curved Lorentzian $4$-manifold) equipped with the two-spinor calculus of [48.02.41]: a complex rank-two spin space $\mathbb{S}$ with symplectic form $\varepsilon_{AB}$, its complex conjugate $\bar{\mathbb{S}}$ with primed indices, and the soldering isomorphism $V^a \leftrightarrow V^{AA'}$ between complexified tangent vectors and elements of $\mathbb{S}\otimes\bar{\mathbb{S}}$. Indices are raised and lowered with $\varepsilon_{AB}$, $\varepsilon^{AB}$ (and their primed versions) by the conventions $\xi^A = \varepsilon^{AB}\xi_B$, $\xi_B = \xi^A\varepsilon_{AB}$. The spinor covariant derivative $\nabla_{AA'}$ is the soldered form of the Levi-Civita connection, annihilating $\varepsilon_{AB}$.

**Definition (zero-rest-mass field).** A *zero-rest-mass free field of spin $s = \tfrac{n}{2}$* ($n$ a positive integer) is a totally symmetric spinor field
$$
\phi_{AB\cdots L} = \phi_{(AB\cdots L)}, \qquad n = 2s \ \text{indices},
$$
satisfying the **zero-rest-mass field equation**
$$
\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB\cdots L} = 0 .
$$
The derivative is contracted against the *first* index; by total symmetry the choice of contracted index is immaterial. The conjugate field $\bar\phi_{A'B'\cdots L'}$ describes the opposite helicity. The number of unprimed versus primed indices is the **helicity grading**: $\phi_{AB\cdots L}$ with $n$ unprimed indices has helicity $+s$, and $\bar\phi_{A'\cdots L'}$ has helicity $-s$.

**The Maxwell spinor ($s=1$).** A real antisymmetric two-form $F_{ab} = F_{[ab]}$ decomposes, via the dictionary of [48.02.41], as
$$
F_{ab} = \phi_{AB}\,\varepsilon_{A'B'} + \bar\phi_{A'B'}\,\varepsilon_{AB},
\qquad \phi_{AB} = \tfrac12 F_{ab}\,\varepsilon^{A'B'} = \phi_{(AB)},
$$
where $\phi_{AB}$ is the **Maxwell spinor**. The first term is the anti-self-dual part of $F$ and the second its self-dual part (the complex conjugate); the split of a real two-form into $\phi_{AB}\varepsilon_{A'B'}$ plus complex conjugate *is* the (anti-)self-dual decomposition. The source-free Maxwell equations $\nabla^a F_{ab} = 0$ and $\nabla_{[a}F_{bc]} = 0$ together read, in spinor form, $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB} = 0$ — the $s=1$ case of the master equation.

**The Weyl (neutrino) field ($s=\tfrac12$).** A single unprimed spinor field $\phi_A$ obeying
$$
\nabla^{AA'}\phi_A = 0
$$
is the *Weyl field*, the massless spin-half field of one definite helicity. This is the chiral, two-component reduction of the Dirac field at zero mass [12.11.01].

**The Dirac field as a coupled pair.** A Dirac $4$-spinor splits into a pair $(\phi_A,\ \chi^{A'})$ — an unprimed and a primed two-spinor, the two chiral halves [12.11.01]. With mass parameter $\mu = m/\sqrt2$ (in natural units) the Dirac equation becomes the **coupled first-order pair**
$$
\nabla^{AA'}\phi_A = \mu\,\chi^{A'}, \qquad \nabla_{AA'}\chi^{A'} = \mu\,\phi_A .
$$
Setting $\mu = 0$ decouples the pair into two independent Weyl equations, recovering the $s=\tfrac12$ massless case for each chirality.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **Symmetry is not optional.** A solution of $\nabla^{AA'}\psi_{AB\cdots L} = 0$ with $\psi$ *not* totally symmetric is not a spin-$s$ zero-rest-mass field; its antisymmetric parts carry lower-spin content and obey different equations. The symmetric projection is part of the definition.
- **Helicity is the index imbalance, not the index count.** A field with equal numbers of primed and unprimed indices (a tensor field) has helicity zero in this grading; helicity is the *difference* of unprimed and primed counts, halved. Doubling all indices does not double the helicity if you add them symmetrically on both index types.
- **The massive case needs two fields.** One cannot write the massive Dirac equation as a single symmetric spinor equation. Mass couples the two chiralities, so a primed partner is mandatory; a lone $\phi_A$ with a mass term on the right is inconsistent unless a $\chi^{A'}$ is supplied to receive it.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Maxwell recovery and helicity).** Let $\phi_{AB} = \phi_{(AB)}$ be a Maxwell spinor on Minkowski space and let $F_{ab} = \phi_{AB}\varepsilon_{A'B'} + \bar\phi_{A'B'}\varepsilon_{AB}$ be the associated real two-form. Then the single spinor equation $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB} = 0$ is equivalent to the full set of source-free Maxwell equations $\nabla^a F_{ab} = 0$ and $\nabla_{[a}F_{bc]} = 0$. Moreover the positive- and negative-helicity photon states correspond to $\phi_{AB}$ and $\bar\phi_{A'B'}$ respectively.

*Proof.* Apply $\nabla^{AA'}$ to the decomposition of $F_{ab}$. In two-spinor form the divergence and the exterior-derivative parts of $\nabla^a F_{ab}$ are governed by the symmetric and antisymmetric parts of $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB}$ in the index $A$. Compute $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB}$ and split it on the free unprimed index $B$ and free primed index $A'$. The contraction $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB}$ is a spinor with one lower unprimed index $B$ and one upper primed index $A'$, hence corresponds to a covector $G_{b}{}'$, and the real and imaginary parts of the equation $G = 0$ are precisely the divergence equation $\nabla^a F_{ab} = 0$ and the Bianchi/curl equation $\nabla_{[a}F_{bc]} = 0$. Concretely, writing $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB}$ and using $F_{ab} = \phi_{AB}\varepsilon_{A'B'} + \text{c.c.}$ together with $\nabla_{AA'}\varepsilon_{BC} = 0$, the four-vector $\nabla^a F_{ab}$ equals $\nabla^{A}{}_{B'}\phi_{AB}\cdot(\text{soldering}) + \text{c.c.}$, which vanishes for all $b$ iff $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB} = 0$; the homogeneous Maxwell pair is the conjugate statement, automatically included because $F$ is real. The two-to-one correspondence between $F_{ab}$ and the pair $(\phi_{AB},\bar\phi_{A'B'})$ shows the field carries exactly the two photon polarisations, the self-dual part $\bar\phi$ and the anti-self-dual part $\phi$, which are the helicity $-1$ and $+1$ states. $\blacksquare$

This is the prototype: a tensor field equation has been turned into a contracted-derivative condition on a symmetric spinor, and the (anti-)self-dual split that is awkward in tensor language is the plain real-and-imaginary split of $\phi_{AB}$.

**Bridge.** This recovery builds toward the unified spin-$s$ picture and appears again in the linearised gravity case $\phi_{ABCD}$, where the same contracted-derivative equation governs the Weyl spinor of [48.02.18]. The foundational reason the spinor form is cleaner is that the irreducible pieces of a Lorentz tensor are exactly the totally symmetric spinors, so the (anti-)self-dual decomposition is dual to the split of $\phi_{AB}$ into itself and its conjugate; this is exactly the pattern by which the Maxwell field, the Weyl neutrino, and the linearised graviton become a single equation graded by index count. Putting these together, the central insight is that helicity is read directly off the imbalance between primed and unprimed indices, and the zero-rest-mass equation generalises the source-free Maxwell system to every half-integer spin.

## Advanced results [Master]

**The full spin-$s$ tower and conserved currents.** For each $s = \tfrac{n}{2}$ the equation $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB\cdots L} = 0$ on Minkowski space admits a positive-frequency solution space carrying the irreducible massless helicity-$\pm s$ representation of the Poincaré group [ref: TODO_REF Penrose-Rindler §5.7], matching the Wigner classification [07.07.06]. The field carries a conserved, symmetric stress-energy-type current quadratic in $\phi$; for $s=1$ this is the Maxwell stress tensor, and for $s=2$ the linearised Bel-Robinson tensor of [48.02.18]. The contour-integral solution via the Penrose transform produces exactly these helicity-$\pm s$ fields as cohomology classes on twistor space [03.07.14], so the present equation is the spacetime shadow of that complex-analytic construction.

**Maxwell as $s=1$ and linearised gravity as $s=2$.** The Maxwell spinor $\phi_{AB}$ and its equation reproduce source-free electromagnetism, with the principal null directions of $\phi_{AB}$ (the spinors $\alpha_A$ with $\phi_{AB}\alpha^A\alpha^B = 0$) marking the special null directions of the field. For $s=2$, the totally symmetric $\phi_{ABCD}$ is the **linearised Weyl curvature spinor**, and $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{ABCD} = 0$ is the linearised vacuum Bianchi identity; its four principal null spinors and their coincidence pattern are the Petrov classification of [48.02.18]. The peeling behaviour at null infinity — the $r^{-k}$ falloff sorted by Petrov type — is a statement about this spinor field along outgoing null geodesics.

**The Buchdahl consistency condition.** In a general curved Lorentzian background the equation $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB\cdots L} = 0$ for $s > 1$ is *overdetermined*: cross-differentiating and applying the spinor Ricci identities yields an algebraic constraint coupling $\phi$ to the conformal curvature spinor $\Psi_{ABCD}$. For $s>1$ this **Buchdahl condition** $\Psi_{(AB}{}^{EF}\phi_{CD\cdots)EF\cdots} = 0$ generically forces $\phi$ to vanish unless the background is algebraically special or conformally flat [ref: TODO_REF Buchdahl 1958]. Thus higher-spin massless fields do not propagate freely on a generic curved metric — a genuine obstruction with no analogue for $s \le 1$, where Maxwell and Weyl theory remain consistent on any background.

**The massive case and the Dirac pair.** Mass is the off-diagonal coupling $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_A = \mu\chi^{A'}$, $\nabla_{AA'}\chi^{A'} = \mu\phi_A$. Eliminating $\chi^{A'}$ gives the spinor wave equation $(\Box + 2\mu^2)\phi_A = 0$ up to a curvature term, recovering the Klein-Gordon mass shell $m^2 = 2\mu^2$. The same coupling pattern generalises to higher-spin massive fields as towers of symmetric spinors linked by mass terms, the spinor incarnation of the Bargmann-Wigner equations.

**Synthesis.** The zero-rest-mass equation is the foundational reason a single object unifies three classical field theories: this is exactly the statement that the irreducible content of a massless relativistic field is a totally symmetric spinor, graded by helicity. Putting these together, Maxwell ($s=1$) generalises to the linearised graviton ($s=2$) and reduces to the Weyl neutrino ($s=\tfrac12$), while the massive Dirac field is dual to a coupled primed-unprimed pair whose decoupling at zero mass returns the Weyl case. The central insight is that helicity is the primed-versus-unprimed index imbalance, the (anti-)self-dual split is the real-imaginary split of the field spinor, and the Buchdahl obstruction shows precisely where this clean picture breaks under curvature for $s>1$ — the bridge from flat-space field theory to curved-space relativity, and the spacetime end of the twistor correspondence of [03.07.14].

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (component count and helicity content).** *The totally symmetric spinor $\phi_{AB\cdots L}$ with $n=2s$ indices has $n+1$ independent complex components, and the solution space of $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB\cdots L}=0$ carries helicity $+s$ (its conjugate, helicity $-s$).*

*Proof.* A symmetric spinor in $n$ two-valued indices is determined by how many of its indices equal $0$ (the rest equal $1$), giving the values $0,1,\dots,n$, hence $n+1$ complex components. For the helicity, decompose a plane-wave solution $\phi_{AB\cdots L} = \pi_A\pi_B\cdots\pi_L\, e^{i k\cdot x}$ with $k_a = \pi_A\bar\pi_{A'}$ a null covector (this is the general null-datum ansatz). The field equation $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB\cdots L}=0$ forces $k^{AA'}\pi_A = 0$ on the wave, i.e. the propagation direction is the repeated principal spinor; the response of $\phi$ to a rotation about $k$ multiplies it by $e^{is\theta}$, the defining transformation of helicity $+s$. The conjugate field transforms by $e^{-is\theta}$. $\blacksquare$

**Proposition (Maxwell equivalence).** *For real $F_{ab} = \phi_{AB}\varepsilon_{A'B'}+\bar\phi_{A'B'}\varepsilon_{AB}$, the system $\{\nabla^aF_{ab}=0,\ \nabla_{[a}F_{bc]}=0\}$ holds iff $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB}=0$.*

*Proof.* Using $\nabla_{AA'}\varepsilon_{BC}=0$, the four-vector $\nabla^aF_{ab}$ in spinor form is $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB}\,\bar\delta + \text{c.c.}$, where the soldering identifies the free indices $(B,A')$ with the covector index $b$. Its vanishing is the condition $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB}=0$. The homogeneous pair $\nabla_{[a}F_{bc]}=0$ is the complex conjugate of the same spinor equation, automatically holding because $F$ is real and $\phi_{AB}$ is symmetric: the antisymmetric part of $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB}$ on the indices $A,B$ vanishes by symmetry of $\phi$, leaving only the symmetric part, whose reality content is the inhomogeneous equation and whose imaginary content is the Bianchi equation. Hence the single complex spinor equation encodes both Maxwell pairs. $\blacksquare$

**Proposition (Buchdahl obstruction for $s>1$).** *On a curved background, $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB\cdots L}=0$ with $n=2s\ge 3$ implies the algebraic identity $\Psi_{(AB}{}^{EF}\phi_{C\cdots L)EF}=0$, where $\Psi_{ABCD}$ is the Weyl conformal spinor.*

*Proof.* Apply $\nabla_{C'}{}^{C}$ to the field equation and antisymmetrise the two unprimed derivative indices to form the spinor curvature operator $\Box_{AB} = \nabla_{A'(A}\nabla_{B)}{}^{A'}$. The spinor Ricci identities give $\Box_{AB}$ acting on a spinor index as a contraction with $\Psi_{ABCD}$ (the conformally invariant part) plus a Ricci-spinor term that cancels in vacuum or by the trace structure. Feeding the field equation in to remove the symmetric-derivative part leaves the purely algebraic curvature contraction; total symmetry of $\phi$ projects it onto $\Psi_{(AB}{}^{EF}\phi_{C\cdots L)EF}=0$. For $n\ge 3$ this is a nonempty constraint, the Buchdahl condition [ref: TODO_REF Buchdahl 1958]; for $n\le 2$ the symmetrisation kills it and no obstruction arises. $\blacksquare$

The detailed spinor Ricci identities and the full Bargmann-Wigner generalisation to massive higher-spin fields are stated above without proof — see Penrose-Rindler [ref: TODO_REF Penrose-Rindler §5.8] and Wald [ref: TODO_REF Wald §13.2].

## Connections [Master]

- **The two-spinor calculus and the spinor-tensor dictionary [48.02.41].** That unit (co-produced in this wave) builds the $\varepsilon$-spinor, the soldering form $V^a\leftrightarrow V^{AA'}$, and the decomposition of an antisymmetric two-form $F_{ab} = \phi_{AB}\varepsilon_{A'B'}+\text{c.c.}$ that this unit uses to write Maxwell as $\phi_{AB}$. The spinor covariant derivative $\nabla_{AA'}$ appearing in every field equation here is the soldered Levi-Civita connection defined there.

- **The Newman-Penrose spin-coefficient formalism [48.02.43].** That unit (co-produced in this wave) turns the abstract $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB\cdots L}=0$ into component equations along a null tetrad, expressing the Maxwell scalars $\phi_0,\phi_1,\phi_2$ and the Weyl scalars $\Psi_0\ldots\Psi_4$ and their NP-equation dynamics; the present equation is the covariant statement that the NP formalism makes computational.

- **The Dirac equation, relativistic spin [12.11.01].** This unit recasts the four-component Dirac equation built there as the coupled two-spinor pair $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_A = \mu\chi^{A'}$, $\nabla_{AA'}\chi^{A'} = \mu\phi_A$, and identifies the massless chiral halves with the $s=\tfrac12$ Weyl field. The chiral projectors there become the split into primed and unprimed spinors here.

- **Free Maxwell and the Proca field [12.05.06].** The source-free Maxwell system established there is exactly the $s=1$ case $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB}=0$; the photon's two helicities are the Maxwell spinor and its conjugate, and the electric-magnetic duality rotation is the phase freedom of $\phi_{AB}$.

- **The Petrov classification and the Weyl spinor [48.02.18].** The $s=2$ field $\phi_{ABCD}$ is the linearised Weyl spinor classified there; its four principal null directions and their coincidences are the Petrov types, and the peeling theorem describes this spinor field at null infinity.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The spinor form of the massless field equations grew out of the van der Waerden two-spinor calculus of the late 1920s and reached its mature, geometrically explicit form in the work of Roger Penrose and Wolfgang Rindler, whose *Spinors and Space-Time, Vol. 1* (Cambridge University Press, 1984) devotes its fifth chapter to the zero-rest-mass fields and presents the equation $\nabla^{AA'}\phi_{AB\cdots L}=0$ as a single object subsuming Maxwell, Weyl, and (in coupled form) Dirac [ref: TODO_REF Penrose-Rindler §5.7]. The viewpoint that helicity is the fundamental label of a massless field — and that this label is read directly off the imbalance of primed and unprimed spinor indices — became the conceptual seed of the twistor programme, in which these fields are repackaged as cohomology on a complex projective space.

The obstruction to propagating higher-spin massless fields on a curved background was identified by H. A. Buchdahl, whose 1958 paper in *Il Nuovo Cimento* showed that the wave equations for particles of spin greater than one are inconsistent in a general gravitational field unless an algebraic curvature condition holds [ref: TODO_REF Buchdahl 1958]. This result anticipated the modern no-go theorems for interacting higher-spin fields and sharpened the special status of spins $\tfrac12$, $1$, and $2$ — the spins realised by the known fundamental fields. Wald's *General Relativity* (University of Chicago Press, 1984) and Stewart's *Advanced General Relativity* (Cambridge University Press, 1990) gave the now-standard textbook treatments tying the spinor field equations to the tetrad and Newman-Penrose machinery [ref: TODO_REF Wald §13.2].
