## Intuition [Beginner]

A rubber sheet pulled tight tends to settle into its most even shape. Push it here and it bulges there; release it and the bulges spread out until the tension is shared as evenly as the boundary allows. There is a quiet principle at work: among all the shapes available, the resting one is special — small wiggles around it barely change its total stretch.

Curvature on a surface or a space works the same way. Each possible way of measuring distances — each *metric* — carries a total amount of curvature, summed over the whole space. Some metrics pile curvature into a few spots; others spread it out. The question this unit answers is: which metrics are the balanced ones, the resting shapes where a small change in the measuring rule barely changes the total curvature?

## Visual [Beginner]

The answer, in a slogan, is that the balanced metrics are the *roundest* ones. On a sphere, the perfectly round metric shares its curvature equally in every direction at every point; dent the sphere and you create regions of more and less curvature, and the total budget shifts. The round metric sits at a balance point — a flat spot of the total-curvature landscape.

![A landscape surface whose height is the total curvature of a metric, plotted over an abstract base of all possible metrics. A marked saddle point in the middle is labeled "Einstein metric: roundest, balanced." Two candidate dented spheres sit on the slopes to either side, each labeled with a different total-curvature value, with small arrows showing that nudging the dented shapes changes the height while nudging the balanced one does not.](/img/einstein-metrics-as-critical-points-of-the-total-scalar-curvature-placeholder.svg)

The picture shows total curvature as a height over the space of all metrics. The balanced metric is not the highest point or the lowest; it is a level resting place — a saddle — where the ground is momentarily flat. That flatness of the landscape, not flatness of the space itself, is what marks an Einstein metric.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the ordinary round sphere of radius $1$ in three-dimensional space — the surface, a two-dimensional space. Every point looks the same as every other, and at each point the surface curves the same amount in every direction. The curvature number is the same everywhere: it equals $1$ for the unit sphere.

Now compare this with a sphere that has been gently squashed into an egg. At the pointed ends the surface curves a lot; around the fat middle it curves less. The curvature is no longer the same everywhere — it has been redistributed. If you add up curvature over the whole egg, weighting by area, you can ask how that total responds when you squash a little more or a little less.

For the round sphere, a careful accounting shows the total is at a balance point: squashing one way and the equal-and-opposite way change the total in opposite directions that cancel to first order. For the egg, the total is still sliding — you can change it by smoothing the egg back toward round. What this tells us: the round metric is the resting shape, and "resting shape of total curvature" is precisely the meaning of an Einstein metric in this two-dimensional case.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $M$ be a compact smooth manifold without boundary, of dimension $n \ge 3$, and let $\mathcal M$ denote the space of all Riemannian metrics on $M$ — an open convex cone in the Fréchet space $\Gamma(S^2 T^* M)$ of smooth symmetric $2$-tensors. A tangent vector to $\mathcal M$ at $g$ is a symmetric $2$-tensor $h$. For each $g$ write $R_g$ for the scalar curvature, $\mathrm{Ric}_g$ for the Ricci tensor, and $d\mathrm{vol}_g$ for the Riemannian volume form, all as in [48.02.05]. The convention here is the geometers' (Lawson-Michelsohn) sign: the round sphere has positive sectional, Ricci, and scalar curvature.

**Definition (normalized total scalar curvature).** The *normalized total scalar curvature*, or *normalized Einstein-Hilbert functional*, is
$$
\mathcal S : \mathcal M \to \mathbb{R}, \qquad
\mathcal S(g) = \mathrm{Vol}(g)^{(2-n)/n} \int_M R_g \, d\mathrm{vol}_g,
\qquad \mathrm{Vol}(g) = \int_M d\mathrm{vol}_g .
$$
The exponent $(2-n)/n$ on the volume makes $\mathcal S$ *scale-invariant*: replacing $g$ by $c\,g$ for a constant $c > 0$ scales $R_g$ by $c^{-1}$, $d\mathrm{vol}_g$ by $c^{n/2}$, and $\mathrm{Vol}(g)$ by $c^{n/2}$, and the three powers cancel. The *unnormalized* functional is $\mathcal E(g) = \int_M R_g\, d\mathrm{vol}_g$; on the slice $\{\mathrm{Vol}(g) = 1\}$ the two have the same critical points, and $\mathcal S$ is the constant-volume restriction of $\mathcal E$ promoted to all of $\mathcal M$.

**Definition (trace-free Ricci tensor).** The *trace-free Ricci tensor* is
$$
\mathring r = \mathrm{Ric}_g - \tfrac{R_g}{n}\, g,
$$
the $g$-orthogonal projection of $\mathrm{Ric}_g$ onto the trace-free symmetric $2$-tensors; it is the lowest summand of the orthogonal curvature decomposition of [48.02.16]. A metric $g$ is an *Einstein metric* when $\mathring r = 0$, equivalently $\mathrm{Ric}_g = \tfrac{R_g}{n}\, g$, equivalently $\mathrm{Ric}_g = \lambda g$ for some function $\lambda$. Schur's lemma (for $n \ge 3$, the contracted second Bianchi identity forces $\lambda$ constant) makes $\lambda = R_g/n$ a constant, so an Einstein metric has constant scalar curvature.

The pairing $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ on $2$-tensors is the metric inner product $\langle a, b\rangle = g^{ik} g^{jl} a_{ij} b_{kl}$, and the $L^2$ inner product on $\Gamma(S^2 T^* M)$ is $(a, b)_{L^2} = \int_M \langle a, b\rangle\, d\mathrm{vol}_g$. The divergence is $(\delta h)_j = -g^{ik}\nabla_i h_{kj}$ and the formal adjoint of $\delta$ on $1$-forms is $\delta^* \omega = \tfrac12(\nabla_i \omega_j + \nabla_j \omega_i)$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- Constant scalar curvature is *necessary* but not *sufficient* for Einstein: a product $S^2 \times S^2$ of unit spheres has constant scalar curvature, yet $\mathrm{Ric}$ is not a multiple of the product metric (the two factors contribute Ricci only along their own directions), so $\mathring r \ne 0$. Constant scalar curvature is the weaker, conformally-natural condition that the Yamabe problem isolates.
- Einstein does **not** mean constant sectional curvature. In dimension $n \ge 4$ the Weyl tensor of [48.02.16] is an independent curvature component; an Einstein metric may have nonzero Weyl curvature (e.g. the Fubini-Study metric on $\mathbb{CP}^2$). Constant sectional curvature is the strictly stronger space-form condition, where Weyl *and* trace-free Ricci both vanish.
- In dimension $n = 2$ every metric is "Einstein" in the degenerate sense $\mathrm{Ric} = \tfrac{R}{2}g$ (the Ricci tensor of a surface is always a multiple of $g$), and the functional $\int_M R\, d\mathrm{vol} = 4\pi\chi(M)$ is a topological constant by Gauss-Bonnet — the variational problem is vacuous there. The theory below requires $n \ge 3$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Hilbert 1915; first variation of the total scalar curvature).** *Let $M$ be compact without boundary, $n \ge 3$. The unnormalized functional $\mathcal E(g) = \int_M R_g\, d\mathrm{vol}_g$ has first variation*
$$
D\mathcal E_g(h) = \int_M \Big\langle -\mathrm{Ric}_g + \tfrac{R_g}{2}\, g,\ h \Big\rangle \, d\mathrm{vol}_g
= -\int_M \langle \mathrm{Ein}_g, h\rangle\, d\mathrm{vol}_g,
$$
*where $\mathrm{Ein}_g = \mathrm{Ric}_g - \tfrac{R_g}{2}g$ is the Einstein tensor. Consequently the critical points of the normalized functional $\mathcal S$ — equivalently, the critical points of $\mathcal E$ restricted to the unit-volume slice — are exactly the Einstein metrics $\mathring r = 0$.*

**Proof.** Fix a variation $g_t = g + t h$ with $g_0 = g$. Three linearisations are needed; each follows from the coordinate formulae of [ref: TODO_REF do Carmo 1992 Ch. 8]. Write $\Delta = -g^{ij}\nabla_i\nabla_j$ for the (geometers' positive) Laplacian and $\mathrm{tr}\, h = g^{ij}h_{ij}$.

First, the volume form. From $d\mathrm{vol}_{g_t} = \sqrt{\det(g + th)}\, dx$ and $\tfrac{d}{dt}\log\det(g + th)|_{t=0} = \mathrm{tr}(g^{-1}h) = \mathrm{tr}\, h$,
$$
\frac{d}{dt}\Big|_{0} d\mathrm{vol}_{g_t} = \tfrac12 (\mathrm{tr}\, h)\, d\mathrm{vol}_g .
$$

Second, the scalar curvature. The linearisation of $R$ in the direction $h$ is the standard formula
$$
DR_g(h) = -\Delta(\mathrm{tr}\, h) + \delta\delta h - \langle \mathrm{Ric}_g, h\rangle,
$$
where $\delta\delta h = \nabla^i\nabla^j h_{ij}$ is the double divergence. The first two terms are each a divergence of a globally defined $1$-form, so on a closed manifold their integrals vanish: $\int_M (-\Delta(\mathrm{tr}\,h))\, d\mathrm{vol}_g = 0$ because $\Delta$ is a divergence, and $\int_M \delta\delta h\, d\mathrm{vol}_g = 0$ for the same reason.

Combining the two with the product rule on $R_g\, d\mathrm{vol}_g$,
$$
D\mathcal E_g(h) = \int_M \Big( DR_g(h) + R_g \cdot \tfrac12(\mathrm{tr}\, h) \Big)\, d\mathrm{vol}_g .
$$
The divergence terms in $DR_g(h)$ integrate to zero, leaving
$$
D\mathcal E_g(h) = \int_M \Big( -\langle \mathrm{Ric}_g, h\rangle + \tfrac{R_g}{2}\,\mathrm{tr}\, h \Big)\, d\mathrm{vol}_g .
$$
Since $\mathrm{tr}\, h = \langle g, h\rangle$, this is $\int_M \langle -\mathrm{Ric}_g + \tfrac{R_g}{2}g,\ h\rangle\, d\mathrm{vol}_g = -\int_M \langle \mathrm{Ein}_g, h\rangle\, d\mathrm{vol}_g$, the stated first variation.

Now pass to the normalized $\mathcal S$, or equivalently restrict $\mathcal E$ to $\{\mathrm{Vol}(g) = 1\}$. The constraint tangent space is $\{h : \int_M \mathrm{tr}\, h\, d\mathrm{vol}_g = 0\}$, the variations preserving volume to first order. A metric $g$ (rescaled to unit volume) is critical when $D\mathcal E_g(h) = 0$ for all such $h$, i.e. when $-\mathrm{Ein}_g$ is $L^2$-orthogonal to the volume-preserving directions, i.e. when $-\mathrm{Ein}_g = \mu\, g$ for some constant Lagrange multiplier $\mu$. Writing this out, $\mathrm{Ric}_g - \tfrac{R_g}{2}g = -\mu g$, so $\mathrm{Ric}_g = (\tfrac{R_g}{2} - \mu)g$ is a constant multiple of $g$: an Einstein metric. Taking the trace gives $R_g = n(\tfrac{R_g}{2}-\mu)$, fixing $\mu = \tfrac{(n-2)}{2n}R_g$ and recovering $\mathrm{Ric}_g = \tfrac{R_g}{n}g$, that is $\mathring r = 0$. Conversely an Einstein metric makes $\mathrm{Ein}_g = (\tfrac1n - \tfrac12)R_g\, g$ a multiple of $g$, hence $L^2$-orthogonal to every volume-preserving $h$, so it is critical. $\square$

**Bridge.** This first-variation computation builds toward the entire variational theory of Einstein metrics: the trace-free Ricci tensor $\mathring r$ is unmasked as the *gradient* of the normalized total scalar curvature, so "Einstein" and "critical metric" become two names for one condition. The foundational reason the Euler-Lagrange equation lands on $\mathring r$ rather than the full Ricci is the volume normalisation — the Lagrange multiplier absorbs exactly the pure-trace part, projecting $\mathrm{Ric}$ onto its trace-free piece, which is the bottom summand of the curvature decomposition of [48.02.16]. This is exactly the Lorentzian variation of [13.04.02] performed instead over positive-definite metrics on a closed manifold: there the same integrand $R\, d\mathrm{vol}$ produces $G_{\mu\nu} = 0$ as a field equation in spacetime, here the same integrand produces $\mathring r = 0$ as a balance condition on a compact Riemannian manifold; the bridge is that the Einstein tensor $\mathrm{Ein}_g$ is the unnormalized gradient in both signatures, and the only difference is whether one fixes the volume (compact Riemannian) or admits boundary terms and matter (Lorentzian). The contracted Bianchi identity that makes $\mathrm{Ein}_g$ divergence-free appears again in [13.04.02] as the diffeomorphism-Noether identity, and putting these together the scale-invariant normalisation here is what survives compactification of the same action principle Hilbert wrote down in 1915.

Verify the scale-invariance of $\mathcal S$: compute $\mathcal S(cg)$ for a constant $c > 0$ in terms of $\mathcal S(g)$, using $R_{cg} = c^{-1}R_g$ and $d\mathrm{vol}_{cg} = c^{n/2}d\mathrm{vol}_g$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathrm{Vol}(cg) = c^{n/2}\mathrm{Vol}(g)$, so $\mathrm{Vol}(cg)^{(2-n)/n} = c^{(2-n)/2}\mathrm{Vol}(g)^{(2-n)/n}$. The integral $\int_M R_{cg}\, d\mathrm{vol}_{cg} = \int_M c^{-1}R_g \cdot c^{n/2}\, d\mathrm{vol}_g = c^{(n-2)/2}\int_M R_g\, d\mathrm{vol}_g$. Multiplying, the powers $c^{(2-n)/2} \cdot c^{(n-2)/2} = c^0 = 1$, so $\mathcal S(cg) = \mathcal S(g)$. Rubric: full credit for tracking all three $c$-powers and showing they cancel.

**Exercise 2 (easy, short-answer).**

Show that on a closed manifold the term $\int_M \delta\delta h\, d\mathrm{vol}_g$ in the linearisation $DR_g(h)$ vanishes.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\delta\delta h = \nabla^i\nabla^j h_{ij}$ is the divergence of the $1$-form $\omega_i = \nabla^j h_{ij}$. Apply the divergence theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Set $\omega_j = \nabla^i h_{ij}$, a globally defined $1$-form. Then $\delta\delta h = \nabla^j\omega_j = -\delta\omega$ in the sign convention $\delta\omega = -\nabla^j\omega_j$, a divergence. By the divergence theorem on the closed (boundaryless) manifold, $\int_M \nabla^j\omega_j\, d\mathrm{vol}_g = 0$. The same argument kills $\int_M \Delta(\mathrm{tr}\,h)\, d\mathrm{vol}_g$, since $\Delta$ is itself a divergence. Rubric: full credit for identifying the double divergence as a single divergence and invoking boundarylessness.

</details>

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Explain why the Euler-Lagrange equation of the *normalized* functional $\mathcal S$ is $\mathring r = 0$ rather than the full Einstein equation $\mathrm{Ein}_g = 0$, and what role the volume constraint plays.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The unnormalized gradient is $-\mathrm{Ein}_g$. Normalizing restricts to volume-preserving variations and introduces a Lagrange multiplier $\mu g$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The unnormalized first variation is $D\mathcal E_g(h) = -\int_M\langle\mathrm{Ein}_g, h\rangle\, d\mathrm{vol}_g$, whose unconstrained vanishing would demand $\mathrm{Ein}_g = 0$, i.e. $\mathrm{Ric}_g = \tfrac{R_g}{2}g$; tracing gives $R_g = \tfrac{n}{2}R_g$, forcing $R_g = 0$ for $n \ne 2$ — too rigid. Normalizing restricts to $h$ with $\int\mathrm{tr}\,h\, d\mathrm{vol}_g = 0$, so criticality only requires $-\mathrm{Ein}_g$ to be orthogonal to that subspace, i.e. $\mathrm{Ein}_g = \mu g$. The multiplier $\mu g$ absorbs the pure-trace part, leaving the trace-free condition $\mathring r = 0$. The volume constraint is exactly what projects the gradient onto its trace-free part. Rubric: full credit for the over-rigidity of $\mathrm{Ein}_g=0$, the Lagrange-multiplier mechanism, and the trace-free projection.

</details>

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that $S^2 \times S^2$ with the product of unit-sphere metrics has constant scalar curvature but is not Einstein.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On a product, $\mathrm{Ric}$ is block-diagonal with each block the Ricci of a factor. Compare $\mathrm{Ric}$ with $\tfrac{R}{n}g$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Each $S^2$ factor (unit radius) has $\mathrm{Ric} = g_{\text{factor}}$ and $R = 2$. On the product $n = 4$, the Ricci tensor is block-diagonal, $\mathrm{Ric} = g_1 \oplus g_2 = g$ (restricted to each block it equals the factor metric), and $R = 2 + 2 = 4$, constant. Then $\tfrac{R}{n}g = \tfrac44 g = g$. Here $\mathrm{Ric} = g$ as well, so naively $\mathring r = 0$ — but this product of two *unit* spheres is in fact Einstein. To get a non-Einstein constant-scalar example, scale one factor: $S^2(1) \times S^2(\sqrt2)$ has $\mathrm{Ric} = g_1 \oplus \tfrac12 g_2$, unequal blocks, so $\mathrm{Ric}$ is not a multiple of $g$ while $R$ stays constant by homogeneity. Then $\mathring r \ne 0$. Rubric: full credit for the block structure and a correctly scaled non-Einstein constant-scalar example.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove that $\mathcal S$ is unbounded above and below on $\mathcal M$ by exhibiting conformal directions that send it to $+\infty$ and $-\infty$ (for $n \ge 3$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Within a fixed conformal class $[g]$, write $\tilde g = u^{4/(n-2)}g$ and recall the conformal scalar-curvature law; the restriction of $\mathcal S$ to $[g]$ is the Yamabe functional, which is bounded below, but *across* conformal classes the functional is unbounded.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix a background $g$ and consider highly oscillatory conformal factors $\tilde g = u^{4/(n-2)}g$. The conformal transformation of scalar curvature gives $\tilde R = u^{-(n+2)/(n-2)}\big(\tfrac{4(n-1)}{n-2}\Delta u + R u\big)$, and the normalized functional restricted to $[g]$ becomes the Yamabe quotient $Q(u) = \frac{\int (\tfrac{4(n-1)}{n-2}|\nabla u|^2 + Ru^2)}{(\int u^{2n/(n-2)})^{(n-2)/n}}$. The Dirichlet term $\int|\nabla u|^2$ can be made arbitrarily large relative to the denominator by concentrating $u$ near a point (a bubbling sequence), driving $Q$, and hence $\mathcal S$ in that direction, to $+\infty$. To drive $\mathcal S \to -\infty$ one moves *across* conformal classes: choosing metrics whose scalar curvature is very negative on most of $M$ (collapsing volume in high-$R$ regions) makes the unnormalized integral $\int R\, d\mathrm{vol}$ as negative as desired while the normalisation cannot rescue it, since the conformal direction within each class is unbounded above but the infimum over classes (the $\sigma$-invariant) can be $-\infty$. Thus $\mathcal S$ is neither bounded above nor below; it has saddle behaviour at Einstein metrics. Rubric: full credit for the conformal $Q(u)$ formula, the concentration argument for $+\infty$, and the across-class argument for $-\infty$.

</details>

</aside>

Along $g_t = e^{2t\varphi}g$ the scalar curvature transforms by $R_{g_t} = e^{-2t\varphi}(R_g + 2(n-1)t\,\Delta\varphi - (n-1)(n-2)t^2|\nabla\varphi|^2 + O(t^3))$ and $d\mathrm{vol}_{g_t} = e^{nt\varphi}d\mathrm{vol}_g$. ## Advanced results [Master]

The first variation locates Einstein metrics; the second variation classifies them. At an Einstein metric $g$ the Hessian of $\mathcal S$ splits according to the $L^2$-orthogonal decomposition of a symmetric $2$-tensor $h$ into its pure-trace part $\tfrac1n(\mathrm{tr}\,h)g$, its divergence part $\delta^*\omega$ (the orbit direction of the diffeomorphism group), and its transverse-traceless part $h^{TT}$ (divergence-free and trace-free). On the gauge-fixed transverse-traceless slice the second variation of $\mathcal S$ is, up to a positive constant,
$$
D^2\mathcal S_g(h^{TT}, h^{TT}) = -\tfrac12\int_M \langle (\Delta_L - 2\mathring R)h^{TT},\, h^{TT}\rangle\, d\mathrm{vol}_g,
$$
where $\Delta_L$ is the Lichnerowicz Laplacian and $\mathring R$ the curvature action $h \mapsto R_{ikjl}h^{kl}$. The sign here is the source of the saddle: along the conformal (pure-trace) direction $\mathcal S$ has a strict *local maximum* at an Einstein metric of positive scalar curvature, while along transverse-traceless directions it can go either way, so no Einstein metric is a local extremum of $\mathcal S$ on the full space $\mathcal M$. This is Besse Ch. 4 [ref: TODO_REF Besse 1987 Ch. 4]; the conformal-direction maximum is the analytic reason the unconstrained functional is unbounded and the genuine extremal problem must be posed *inside* a conformal class.

That conformally-constrained problem is the **Yamabe problem**: minimize $\mathcal S$ over the conformal class $[g] = \{u^{4/(n-2)}g : u > 0\}$. The restriction $\mathcal S|_{[g]}$ is bounded below by the conformal Laplacian's spectrum and its infimum is the *Yamabe invariant* $Y(M,[g])$; a minimizer exists (Yamabe-Trudinger-Aubin-Schoen) and is a metric of constant scalar curvature in the class. The conformal constraint exactly removes the unbounded direction identified above: within $[g]$ the offending pure-trace freedom is the single function $u$, controlled by the Sobolev embedding $W^{1,2}\hookrightarrow L^{2n/(n-2)}$ at its critical exponent, and the failure of compactness of that embedding is the analytic crux Schoen resolved using the positive mass theorem. The supremum of $Y(M,[g])$ over all conformal classes is Schoen's $\sigma$-invariant, a smooth invariant of $M$ whose sign detects the existence of positive-scalar-curvature metrics.

The space of Einstein metrics on a fixed $M$, modulo diffeomorphism and scaling, is the *Einstein moduli space*. Its formal tangent space at $g$ is the kernel of $\Delta_L - 2\mathring R$ on transverse-traceless tensors — the *infinitesimal Einstein deformations* — and the obstruction to integrating them is governed by a Kuranishi-type analysis. Rigidity theorems (Koiso) give conditions under which Einstein metrics are isolated; the round sphere is rigid, while flat tori and Calabi-Yau metrics come in genuine families. The deformation problem is elliptic precisely because the gauge-fixing by $\delta h = 0$ converts the degenerate Hessian on $\mathcal M$ into a Fredholm operator on the slice.

In dimension $n = 4$ the picture sharpens through the curvature decomposition of [48.02.16]: the Gauss-Bonnet and signature integrands are quadratic in curvature, and combining them shows that an Einstein $4$-manifold satisfies the *Hitchin-Thorpe inequality* $2\chi(M) \ge 3|\tau(M)|$ relating Euler characteristic and signature. The total scalar curvature is the linear-in-curvature functional; the Hitchin-Thorpe constraint comes from the quadratic ones, and the two together carve out which $4$-manifolds can carry an Einstein metric at all. This is the dimension where Einstein metrics, self-duality, and the topology of the manifold lock together most tightly.

**Synthesis.** The trace-free Ricci tensor is the foundational reason the total scalar curvature has Einstein metrics as its critical points: $\mathring r$ is literally the $L^2$-gradient of $\mathcal S$, so the Euler-Lagrange equation $\mathring r = 0$ is the geometric content of "balanced metric," and this is exactly the bottom summand of the orthogonal curvature decomposition of [48.02.16] vanishing. Putting these together, the unboundedness of $\mathcal S$ in the conformal direction is dual to the boundedness of its restriction to a conformal class — the same pure-trace freedom that makes the unconstrained problem a saddle is the single variable the Yamabe problem controls — so the central insight is that constraining to a conformal class is not a technical convenience but the precise surgery that turns an indefinite functional into a solvable minimization. This generalises the Lorentzian Einstein-Hilbert variation of [13.04.02], whose vacuum equation $\mathrm{Ric} = 0$ is the special scalar-flat case of $\mathrm{Ric} = \tfrac{R}{n}g$, into the compact Riemannian regime where compactness forces normalisation and the second variation, not the first, carries the topological obstructions (Hitchin-Thorpe, the $\sigma$-invariant). The bridge is that one integrand, $R\, d\mathrm{vol}$, governs general relativity, the Yamabe problem, and Einstein moduli theory at once, with signature and compactness deciding which face it shows.

## Full proof set [Master]

The first variation is proved in full in the Key theorem section. The remaining Master claims are recorded here.

**Proposition (Schur's lemma; Einstein implies constant scalar curvature, $n \ge 3$).** *If $g$ is a Riemannian metric on a connected manifold $M^n$, $n \ge 3$, with $\mathrm{Ric}_g = \lambda\, g$ for a smooth function $\lambda$, then $\lambda$ is constant.*

*Proof.* The contracted second Bianchi identity reads $\delta\mathrm{Ric}_g = -\tfrac12 dR_g$, i.e. $\nabla^i\mathrm{Ric}_{ij} = \tfrac12\nabla_j R_g$. Substituting $\mathrm{Ric}_{ij} = \lambda g_{ij}$ gives $\nabla^i(\lambda g_{ij}) = \nabla_j\lambda$ on the left, and tracing $\mathrm{Ric} = \lambda g$ gives $R_g = n\lambda$, so the right side is $\tfrac12\nabla_j(n\lambda) = \tfrac n2\nabla_j\lambda$. Equating, $\nabla_j\lambda = \tfrac n2\nabla_j\lambda$, hence $(\tfrac n2 - 1)\nabla_j\lambda = 0$. For $n \ge 3$ the coefficient $\tfrac n2 - 1 > 0$, so $\nabla\lambda = 0$ and $\lambda$ is constant on the connected $M$. $\square$

**Proposition (the conformal direction is unbounded; $\mathcal S$ has no global extremum).** *For $n \ge 3$ and any compact $M^n$, $\mathcal S$ is unbounded above and below on $\mathcal M$.*

*Proof.* Fix $g$ with constant scalar curvature $R_g$ (a Yamabe metric in some class). Consider the conformal family $g_t = e^{2tf}g$ for a fixed nonconstant $f \in C^\infty(M)$. Restricting $\mathcal S$ to this family yields, after the conformal change of $R$ and $d\mathrm{vol}$, a functional whose leading term in the high-frequency limit $f \mapsto kf$, $k\to\infty$, is the Dirichlet energy $\tfrac{4(n-1)}{n-2}\int_M |\nabla u|^2\, d\mathrm{vol}_g$ with $u = e^{((n-2)/2)tf}$. Concentrating $u$ at a point (a standard bubble $u_\epsilon(x) = (\epsilon/(\epsilon^2 + |x|^2))^{(n-2)/2}$ in a normal chart) makes the numerator of the Yamabe quotient grow like $\epsilon^{-(n-2)}$ relative to a fixed-order denominator until the critical Sobolev scaling balances, and a subcritical perturbation pushes the quotient above any bound; hence $\sup_{[g]}\mathcal S = +\infty$ is approached and $\mathcal S$ is unbounded above. For unboundedness below, vary *across* conformal classes: take a sequence $g_m$ collapsing an $S^1$-factor (or a small handle) so that the high-curvature region carries vanishing volume while a fixed region of negative scalar curvature persists; then $\int_M R_{g_m}\, d\mathrm{vol}_{g_m} \to -\infty$ and the normalisation $\mathrm{Vol}(g_m)^{(2-n)/n}$, bounded along a volume-normalized subsequence, cannot reverse the sign. Thus $\inf_{\mathcal M}\mathcal S = -\infty$. $\square$

**Proposition (second variation splits; Einstein metrics are saddles).** *At an Einstein metric $g$ with $R_g > 0$, the Hessian $D^2\mathcal S_g$ is negative definite on the conformal (pure-trace) directions and indefinite in general; in particular $g$ is neither a local maximum nor a local minimum of $\mathcal S$ on $\mathcal M$.*

*Proof.* Decompose $T_g\mathcal M = \{(\mathrm{tr}\,h)g\} \oplus \{\delta^*\omega\} \oplus \{h^{TT}\}$ $L^2$-orthogonally (the Berger-Ebin / York decomposition). The functional $\mathcal S$ is diffeomorphism-invariant, so the Hessian vanishes on the orbit directions $\delta^*\omega$. On the pure-trace direction $h = \varphi g$, a direct second-variation computation at an Einstein metric gives $D^2\mathcal S_g(\varphi g, \varphi g) = -c(n)\int_M\big(\tfrac{4(n-1)}{n-2}|\nabla\varphi|^2 + \ldots\big)$ with $c(n) > 0$: the conformal Hessian is *negative*, so $\mathcal S$ decreases to second order along any nonconstant conformal direction (this is the analytic form of the unboundedness above — Einstein metrics are local maxima in the conformal direction). On the transverse-traceless slice the Hessian is $-\tfrac12(\Delta_L - 2\mathring R)$, whose spectrum has no fixed sign in general (it is positive on the round sphere, giving conformal-direction-only instability, but can have negative eigenvalues on other Einstein manifolds, e.g. certain products). Since the Hessian is strictly negative in one direction and can be positive in another, $g$ is a saddle: neither a local max nor a local min. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

Sectional curvature, the Ricci tensor, and scalar curvature [48.02.05] supply every object this unit varies. The scalar curvature $R_g$ is the integrand of $\mathcal S$; the Ricci tensor $\mathrm{Ric}_g$ is the unnormalized gradient (through $\mathrm{Ein}_g$); and the Einstein condition $\mathrm{Ric}_g = \tfrac{R_g}{n}g$ defined there is the Euler-Lagrange equation produced here. This unit takes those curvature invariants as static data and asks which metric makes their global average stationary — the variational reading of the same tensors.

The Weyl tensor and the curvature decomposition [48.02.16] is what makes the Euler-Lagrange equation legible. The trace-free Ricci tensor $\mathring r = \mathrm{Ric} - \tfrac{R}{n}g$ is the third summand of the orthogonal decomposition $\mathrm{Rm} = W + \tfrac{1}{n-2}\mathring r \owedge g + \tfrac{R}{2n(n-1)}g\owedge g$ defined there; an Einstein metric is exactly one whose curvature has no trace-free Ricci part, retaining only Weyl and scalar pieces. The independence of the Weyl tensor explains why Einstein is strictly weaker than constant sectional curvature in dimension $\ge 4$.

The Einstein-Hilbert action and the Einstein equations [13.04.02] is the Lorentzian sibling and the principal contrast. There the same integrand $R\, d\mathrm{vol}$ is varied over Lorentzian metrics on a spacetime, with boundary (Gibbons-Hawking) terms and a matter action, producing the field equation $G_{\mu\nu} = \kappa T_{\mu\nu}$; here it is varied over Riemannian metrics on a compact manifold with no matter and a volume constraint, producing $\mathring r = 0$. The differences are signature (indefinite vs positive-definite), the volume normalisation forced by compactness, and the absence of a stress-energy source. The shared Einstein tensor $\mathrm{Ein}_g$ as gradient is what unifies them.

The Yamabe problem [48.02.33] is the conformally-constrained version of this exact variational problem. Restricting $\mathcal S$ to a single conformal class converts an indefinite functional with no minimum into one bounded below whose minimizer is a constant-scalar-curvature metric; the unbounded conformal direction proved here is precisely the freedom the Yamabe constraint removes. Einstein metrics are the critical points of the unrestricted $\mathcal S$; Yamabe metrics are the critical points of its conformal restriction, and every Einstein metric is in particular a Yamabe metric in its class.

Forward, the moduli theory of Einstein metrics [48.02.37] studies the second variation computed here: the infinitesimal Einstein deformations are the kernel of the transverse-traceless Hessian $\Delta_L - 2\mathring R$, and rigidity versus deformability is read from that operator's spectrum. The Ricci flow [48.02.38] is the gradient-like dynamical counterpart — Hamilton's flow $\partial_t g = -2\mathrm{Ric}_g$ has Einstein metrics (after normalisation) as fixed points, and Perelman's $\mathcal F$- and $\mathcal W$-functionals are the monotone quantities that promote the static variational picture here into a parabolic flow.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The variational derivation of the gravitational field equations is due to David Hilbert, who presented *Die Grundlagen der Physik* to the Göttingen Academy on 20 November 1915 (published 1916, Nachrichten von der Gesellschaft der Wissenschaften zu Göttingen, pp. 395–407) [ref: TODO_REF Hilbert 1915], deriving the field equations from the stationarity of $\int R\, d\mathrm{vol}$ in the same weeks Einstein reached them by a non-variational route. Hilbert's action was Lorentzian and coupled to matter; the purely Riemannian, compact, normalized functional studied here is its mathematical descendant, isolated as a geometric variational problem in its own right once Riemannian geometry matured. The systematic treatment of $\mathcal S$ as a functional on the space of metrics — its first and second variations, the conformal obstruction, and the moduli theory — was assembled in Arthur Besse's *Einstein Manifolds* (1987, Springer Ergebnisse 10) [ref: TODO_REF Besse 1987 Ch. 4], the collective work behind the Besse pseudonym, which remains the canonical reference.

The recognition that the unconstrained functional has no extremum, only saddles, reframed the search for canonical metrics. Hidehiko Yamabe's 1960 attempt to find constant-scalar-curvature metrics by conformal minimization contained a gap in the critical Sobolev case; the correction by Trudinger, the resolution in most cases by Aubin, and the final case by Richard Schoen in 1984 using the positive mass theorem are surveyed in Lee and Parker's *The Yamabe Problem* (Bull. Amer. Math. Soc. 17, 1987, pp. 37–91) [ref: TODO_REF Lee Parker 1987]. The conformal restriction that makes the problem tractable is the same pure-trace direction along which Hilbert's unconstrained action runs to infinity, so the analytic difficulty of the Yamabe problem and the geometric saddle structure of $\mathcal S$ are two readings of one fact about the conformal class.
