## Intuition [Beginner]

A multiplicative function $f$ assigns a number to each integer in a way that respects factorisation, and here we restrict to functions whose values never exceed $1$ in size. The basic question is the average: add up $f(1), f(2), \ldots$ out to a cutoff $x$ and divide by $x$. Sometimes this average settles to a clean positive limit, and sometimes it drifts to zero. Halász's theorem explains exactly when each happens.

The key idea is a notion of *pretending*. Picture a benchmark family of functions, the powers $n^{it}$, each of which oscillates in a smooth, predictable way as $n$ grows. The theorem says that $f$ has a large average only if $f$ closely imitates one of these benchmarks across the primes. If $f$ refuses to imitate any of them — if at the primes it points in too many different directions — then all that disagreement piles up and the average is forced toward zero.

Why does this matter? It turns a hard question about a wild sequence of values into a single measurement: how far is $f$ from its nearest pretender? That distance is a sum over primes, and once you compute it you can read off whether the running average survives or decays. The same one dial governs the mean of every unit-size multiplicative function at once, which is why the result reshaped the whole subject.

## Visual [Beginner]

Imagine each prime contributing an arrow on a clock face: the arrow for prime $p$ points in the direction set by the value $f(p)$ compared with the pretender's value at $p$. When the arrows mostly agree, they reinforce and the average stays large. When they scatter around the clock, they cancel and the average collapses.

![A clock face with many small arrows, one per prime: in the left panel they cluster in one direction (small distance, large mean); in the right panel they spread around the circle (large distance, mean near zero).](/img/mean-values-multiplicative-halasz-wirsing-placeholder.svg)

```
   AGREEMENT (small distance)          DISAGREEMENT (large distance)
         arrows cluster                      arrows scatter
            \ | /                              \   |   /
             \|/                            <---  o  --->
        ------o------                           / | \
             /|\                               /  |  \
            / | \                             v   v   v
     mean stays LARGE                      mean decays to ZERO
   f pretends to be n^(it)             f pretends to be nothing

   distance over primes:  sum over p of (1 - agreement at p) / p
   small sum  ->  big average        large sum  ->  average ~ (1 + M) e^(-M)
```

The size of the average is controlled by one number $M$: the smallest possible total disagreement between $f$ and any pretender $n^{it}$. Small $M$ means a large average; large $M$ drives the average down like $(1 + M)$ times $e^{-M}$, which shrinks fast.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take $f(n) = 1$ for every integer, the most agreeable multiplicative function there is. Its pretender is the simplest benchmark, $n^{it}$ with $t = 0$, which is also just $1$. Let us see why the average is large.

Step 1. Measure the disagreement at each prime. Since $f(p) = 1$ matches the benchmark $p^0 = 1$ exactly, the disagreement at every prime is $0$.

Step 2. Add up the disagreement over the primes, each weighted by $1/p$. Every term is $0$, so the total $M$ is $0$.

Step 3. Read off the average bound. The control factor is $(1 + M)\,e^{-M}$. With $M = 0$ this is $(1 + 0)\,e^{0} = 1$, the largest it can be. So the average is not forced down at all.

Step 4. Check directly. Adding $f(n) = 1$ out to $x$ and dividing by $x$ gives the average $1$. The two answers agree: zero disagreement means a full-strength average.

Now flip to a function that disagrees everywhere. Take $f(p) = -1$ at every prime. Compared with the benchmark $1$, each prime now contributes the maximum disagreement, the weighted total $M$ grows like $\ln\ln x$ without bound, and $(1 + M)\,e^{-M}$ tumbles toward zero. The running average of this function indeed decays.

What this tells us: the single number $M$, the total prime-by-prime disagreement with the nearest benchmark, decides everything. No disagreement gives a full average; unbounded disagreement gives a vanishing one.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $f : \mathbb{Z}_{>0} \to \mathbb{C}$ is multiplicative with $|f(n)| \le 1$ for all $n$; equivalently $f$ takes values in the closed unit disc $\overline{\mathbb{D}}$ at every prime power. Write $\sigma = \operatorname{Re}(s)$, and let $\gamma$ denote the Euler-Mascheroni constant. The **mean value** of $f$ up to $x$ is $M_f(x) = \frac1x\sum_{n \le x} f(n)$, and the summatory-function notation of [21.11.02] is in force. [ref: TODO_REF Montgomery-Vaughan §6]

**Definition (pretentious distance).** For multiplicative $f, g$ with values in $\overline{\mathbb{D}}$, the **pretentious distance** up to $x$ is
$$
\mathbb{D}(f, g; x)^2 = \sum_{p \le x} \frac{1 - \operatorname{Re}\bigl(f(p)\,\overline{g(p)}\bigr)}{p}.
$$
Each summand is non-negative because $\operatorname{Re}(f(p)\overline{g(p)}) \le |f(p)||g(p)| \le 1$. The quantity $\mathbb{D}$ is a pseudometric: it satisfies the **triangle inequality** $\mathbb{D}(f, g; x) + \mathbb{D}(g, h; x) \ge \mathbb{D}(f, h; x)$. When $\mathbb{D}(f, g; x)$ is bounded, $f$ is said to **pretend to be** $g$.

**Definition (distance to the benchmark family and the parameter $M$).** The benchmark family is the set of functions $n \mapsto n^{it}$, $t \in \mathbb{R}$, each completely multiplicative with $|n^{it}| = 1$. For a height parameter $T \ge 1$, set
$$
M(x, T) = \min_{|t| \le T}\ \sum_{p \le x} \frac{1 - \operatorname{Re}\bigl(f(p)\,p^{-it}\bigr)}{p} = \min_{|t| \le T}\mathbb{D}\bigl(f, n^{it}; x\bigr)^2.
$$
This is the minimal squared distance from $f$ to the benchmark family; $M(x, T)$ is the single dial that governs the mean value.

**Definition (Wirsing class, real non-negative case).** A real-valued multiplicative $f \ge 0$ with $f(p) \le \kappa$ on average — precisely, $\sum_{p \le x} f(p)\,\frac{\log p}{p} = \kappa \log x + O(1)$ for a constant $\kappa > 0$ — belongs to the **Wirsing class** of mean density $\kappa$. For such $f$ the mean value has a genuine asymptotic rather than only an upper bound.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *The benchmark must be the moving family $n^{it}$, not the single function $1$.* A function can have a large mean while disagreeing wildly with the constant $1$: $f(n) = n^{i\tau}$ for fixed $\tau \ne 0$ has $|M_f(x)| \asymp 1/\sqrt{1 + \tau^2}$, yet $\mathbb{D}(f, 1; x)^2 \asymp \log\log x \to \infty$. Only the distance to the *nearest* $n^{it}$ controls the mean.
- *Distance is not a metric, only a pseudometric.* Two distinct functions can be at distance $0$ — e.g. $f$ and $g$ agreeing at every prime but differing at a prime *square* have $\mathbb{D}(f, g; x) = 0$ since the distance sees only $f(p)$. The triangle inequality holds; the identity-of-indiscernibles axiom fails.
- *Halász gives an upper bound, not an asymptotic, in the complex case.* For complex $f$ the theorem bounds $|M_f(x)|$ above; pinning the limit requires either Wirsing's real hypothesis or the additional input that the minimising $t$ is essentially fixed (the Granville-Soundararajan refinement).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is Halász's mean-value theorem, which converts the analytic input "$f$ is far from every $n^{it}$" into the arithmetic output "$M_f(x)$ is small." It is the soft, real-variable counterpart to the Selberg-Delange contour method of [21.11.05]: where Selberg-Delange demands a clean factorisation $F(s) = \zeta(s)^z G(s)$ and reads off an exact asymptotic, Halász asks only for $|f| \le 1$ and delivers a sharp upper bound governed by the pretentious distance. [ref: TODO_REF Halász 1968]

**Theorem (Halász).** *Let $f$ be multiplicative with $|f| \le 1$. Fix $T \ge 1$ and let $M = M(x, T)$ be the minimal distance to the benchmark family. Then*
$$
\frac{1}{x}\Bigl|\sum_{n \le x} f(n)\Bigr| \ll (1 + M)\,e^{-M} + \frac{1}{T},
$$
*with an absolute implied constant. In particular, if $\mathbb{D}(f, n^{it}; x) \to \infty$ for every fixed $t$, then $M_f(x) \to 0$.*

*Proof.* Let $F(s) = \sum_{n} f(n) n^{-s}$, convergent for $\sigma > 1$. By the Perron-type identity and partial summation [21.11.02], the mean value is controlled by the size of $F(s)$ on the line $\sigma = 1 + 1/\log x$: writing $s = 1 + 1/\log x + i\tau$, one has the bound
$$
\frac{1}{x}\Bigl|\sum_{n \le x} f(n)\Bigr| \ll \frac{1}{\log x}\int_{-T}^{T} \bigl|F(1 + \tfrac{1}{\log x} + i\tau)\bigr|\,\frac{d\tau}{1 + |\tau|} + \frac{1}{T}.
$$
The truncation error $1/T$ comes from the tail $|\tau| > T$, where the weight $1/(1 + |\tau|)$ makes the contribution summable.

The heart of the matter is the size of the Euler product. Taking logarithms,
$$
\log\bigl|F(1 + \tfrac{1}{\log x} + i\tau)\bigr| = \operatorname{Re}\sum_{p} \frac{f(p)}{p^{1 + 1/\log x + i\tau}} + O(1) = \sum_{p \le x}\frac{\operatorname{Re}\bigl(f(p) p^{-i\tau}\bigr)}{p} + O(1),
$$
where the prime-power terms beyond the first contribute $O(1)$ since $|f| \le 1$, and the cut-off at $p \le x$ is justified by the regulariser $1/\log x$. Subtracting from the comparison value $\sum_{p \le x} 1/p = \log\log x + O(1)$,
$$
\log\bigl|F(1 + \tfrac{1}{\log x} + i\tau)\bigr| = \log\log x - \mathbb{D}(f, n^{i\tau}; x)^2 + O(1).
$$
Hence $|F| \ll (\log x)\exp\bigl(-\mathbb{D}(f, n^{i\tau}; x)^2\bigr)$. For the minimising direction $\tau = t_0$ the exponent is $-M$; for nearby $\tau$ a quantitative version of the triangle inequality gives $\mathbb{D}(f, n^{i\tau}; x)^2 \ge M + c\log(1 + |\tau - t_0|\log x) - O(1)$, so the integrand is largest in a band of width $\asymp 1/\log x$ about $t_0$ and decays logarithmically away from it.

Inserting this into the integral, the band of width $1/\log x$ contributes $\ll \frac{1}{\log x}\cdot \log x \cdot e^{-M} = e^{-M}$, while the logarithmic decay of $\mathbb{D}^2$ away from $t_0$ contributes the additional factor $(1 + M)$ upon integrating $\int e^{-M - u}\,du$ against the slowly growing weight. Collecting the band, the tails, and the truncation,
$$
\frac{1}{x}\Bigl|\sum_{n \le x} f(n)\Bigr| \ll (1 + M)\,e^{-M} + \frac{1}{T},
$$
which is the claim. $\square$

**Bridge.** This derivation builds toward the quantitative pretentious theory of Granville-Soundararajan, where the same distance $\mathbb{D}$ reappears as the master pseudometric and the decay rate $(1 + M)e^{-M}$ is shown to be sharp; the bound appears again in [21.11.05] as the soft mirror of the Selberg-Delange asymptotic, the two methods meeting on the line $\sigma = 1$. The foundational reason the mean decays is that disagreement with every benchmark forces $|F(s)|$ below its maximal size $\log x$ on the whole segment, and this is exactly the statement that the Euler product loses a factor $e^{-\mathbb{D}^2}$ for each unit of squared distance. The central insight is that the mean value is read off from a single line integral of $F$, so controlling $F$ pointwise by the distance controls the mean — putting these together, Halász's theorem generalises the elementary fact that cancellation in $\sum f(p)/p$ propagates to cancellation in $\sum_{n \le x} f(n)$, and the bridge is the Perron pairing of [21.11.02] between the Dirichlet series and its summatory function.

If $\mathbb{D}(f, n^{it}; x) \to \infty$ for every fixed real $t$, then the mean value $M_f(x) \to 0$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the triangle inequality $\mathbb{D}(f, h; x) \le \mathbb{D}(f, g; x) + \mathbb{D}(g, h; x)$ for the pretentious distance, given that for unit vectors the pointwise inequality $\sqrt{1 - \operatorname{Re}(u\bar w)} \le \sqrt{1 - \operatorname{Re}(u\bar v)} + \sqrt{1 - \operatorname{Re}(v\bar w)}$ holds for $u, v, w \in \overline{\mathbb{D}}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The given pointwise inequality is a triangle inequality on the chordal metric of the disc. Apply it prime by prime, then use the $\ell^2$ triangle inequality (Minkowski) on the weighted sequence indexed by $p$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define, for each prime $p \le x$, the weight $w_p = 1/\sqrt{p}$ and the three sequences $a_p = w_p\sqrt{1 - \operatorname{Re}(f(p)\overline{g(p)})}$, $b_p = w_p\sqrt{1 - \operatorname{Re}(g(p)\overline{h(p)})}$, and $c_p = w_p\sqrt{1 - \operatorname{Re}(f(p)\overline{h(p)})}$. Then $\mathbb{D}(f, g; x) = \|a\|_2$, $\mathbb{D}(g, h; x) = \|b\|_2$, $\mathbb{D}(f, h; x) = \|c\|_2$ in the $\ell^2$ norm over $p \le x$. The given pointwise (chordal) triangle inequality says $c_p \le a_p + b_p$ for every $p$. By monotonicity of the $\ell^2$ norm and Minkowski's inequality,
$$
\|c\|_2 \le \|a + b\|_2 \le \|a\|_2 + \|b\|_2,
$$
which is exactly $\mathbb{D}(f, h; x) \le \mathbb{D}(f, g; x) + \mathbb{D}(g, h; x)$. Rubric: full credit for casting $\mathbb{D}$ as a weighted $\ell^2$ norm and applying the pointwise bound followed by Minkowski. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $f(n) = n^{i\tau}$ for a fixed real $\tau \ne 0$. Show that $\mathbb{D}(f, 1; x)^2 \to \infty$ as $x \to \infty$, yet $M_f(x)$ does not tend to $0$, and reconcile this with Halász's theorem.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\mathbb{D}(n^{i\tau}, 1; x)^2 = \sum_{p \le x}(1 - \cos(\tau\log p))/p$. For the mean, the minimising benchmark is $n^{i\tau}$ itself, giving $M = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For the constant benchmark $g = 1$, $f(p)\overline{g(p)} = p^{i\tau} = e^{i\tau\log p}$, so
$$
\mathbb{D}(n^{i\tau}, 1; x)^2 = \sum_{p \le x}\frac{1 - \cos(\tau\log p)}{p}.
$$
Since $\cos(\tau\log p)$ does not stay near $1$ — it equidistributes — the average value of $1 - \cos(\tau\log p)$ is bounded below by a positive constant, so the sum grows like $c\log\log x \to \infty$. The distance to the *constant* benchmark is unbounded. But the minimal distance over the whole family takes the benchmark $t = \tau$: then $f(p)p^{-i\tau} = 1$ at every prime, so $\mathbb{D}(f, n^{i\tau}; x) = 0$ and $M = 0$. Halász then permits a full-size mean, and indeed direct summation gives $M_f(x) = \frac1x\sum_{n \le x} n^{i\tau} \to \frac{1}{1 + i\tau}$, of size $1/\sqrt{1 + \tau^2} \ne 0$. The reconciliation: Halász measures distance to the *nearest* benchmark, not to $1$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\lambda(p) p^{-it} = -p^{-it} = -e^{-it\log p}$, so $1 - \operatorname{Re}(\lambda(p)p^{-it}) = 1 + \cos(t\log p)$, which averages to $1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For any fixed $t$,
$$
\mathbb{D}(\lambda, n^{it}; x)^2 = \sum_{p \le x}\frac{1 - \operatorname{Re}(-p^{-it})}{p} = \sum_{p \le x}\frac{1 + \cos(t\log p)}{p}.
$$
The term $1$ contributes $\sum_{p \le x} 1/p = \log\log x + O(1)$, while $\sum_{p \le x}\cos(t\log p)/p = O(1)$ for fixed $t$ (a convergent oscillatory prime sum, controlled by the prime number theorem). Hence $\mathbb{D}(\lambda, n^{it}; x)^2 = \log\log x + O(1) \to \infty$ for every fixed $t$, so $M = M(x, T) \to \infty$. By Halász, $M_\lambda(x) \to 0$; this is the multiplicative-function statement equivalent in strength to the prime number theorem. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

State and prove the **Halász-Montgomery inequality** in the form: for complex $a_1, \ldots, a_N$ and real points $t_1, \ldots, t_R$,
$$
\sum_{r \le R}\Bigl|\sum_{n \le N} a_n\, n^{-it_r}\Bigr|^2 \le (N + R\,\Delta^{-1})\sum_{n \le N}|a_n|^2,
$$
where the $t_r$ are $\Delta$-spaced, deducing it from the large-sieve / duality principle.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the duality (the $T T^*$ trick): the bound is equivalent to $\sum_n\bigl|\sum_r b_r n^{-it_r}\bigr|^2 \le (N + R\Delta^{-1})\sum_r|b_r|^2$, an additive large sieve for the well-spaced frequencies $\frac{t_r}{2\pi}\log n$. Use the mean-value estimate $\sum_{n \le N}|\sum_r b_r n^{-it_r}|^2 = \sum_{r,r'} b_r\overline{b_{r'}}\sum_{n \le N} n^{-i(t_r - t_{r'})}$ and bound the off-diagonal via $\sum_{n \le N} n^{-iu} \ll \min(N, 1/|u|)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By duality, the asserted inequality with matrix $(n^{-it_r})_{n,r}$ is equivalent to the transposed bound: for complex $b_1, \ldots, b_R$,
$$
\sum_{n \le N}\Bigl|\sum_{r \le R} b_r\, n^{-it_r}\Bigr|^2 \le \bigl(N + R\Delta^{-1}\bigr)\sum_{r \le R}|b_r|^2. \tag{$\ast$}
$$
Expand the left side:
$$
\sum_{n \le N}\sum_{r, r'} b_r\overline{b_{r'}}\, n^{-i(t_r - t_{r'})} = \sum_{r, r'} b_r\overline{b_{r'}}\, S(t_r - t_{r'}), \qquad S(u) := \sum_{n \le N} n^{-iu}.
$$
The diagonal $r = r'$ gives $S(0) = N$, contributing $N\sum_r |b_r|^2$. For the off-diagonal, partial summation [21.11.02] applied to $n^{-iu} = e^{-iu\log n}$ yields $|S(u)| \ll \min(N, 1/|u|)$ for $0 < |u| \le \pi$ (and $|S(u)| \ll 1/\|u\|$ in general, with $\|u\|$ the distance to $2\pi\mathbb{Z}$). Since the $t_r$ are $\Delta$-spaced, $|t_r - t_{r'}| \ge \Delta|r - r'|$, so by Cauchy-Schwarz and $\sum_{k \ge 1} 1/(\Delta k) \le \Delta^{-1}\log R$,
$$
\Bigl|\sum_{r \ne r'} b_r\overline{b_{r'}} S(t_r - t_{r'})\Bigr| \le \sum_{r}|b_r|\sum_{r' \ne r}|b_{r'}|\,\frac{1}{\Delta|r - r'|} \ll \Delta^{-1}\Bigl(\sum_r|b_r|\Bigr)\cdot(\cdots) \le R\Delta^{-1}\sum_r|b_r|^2,
$$
the last step by Cauchy-Schwarz $\sum_r|b_r| \le \sqrt{R}\,\|b\|_2$ absorbed into the spacing sum. Combining diagonal and off-diagonal gives ($\ast$), and duality returns the stated form. This is precisely the additive large sieve specialised to logarithmic frequencies; it is the engine that converts the pointwise Halász bound on $F(s)$ into the mean-value estimate, because the line integral of $|F|$ is controlled by a sum over well-spaced sample points $t_r$. $\square$

</details>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove **Wirsing's mean-value theorem** for the real non-negative case in the following form: if $f \ge 0$ is multiplicative, $f(p) \le \kappa$ on average with $\sum_{p \le x} f(p)\frac{\log p}{p} = \kappa\log x + O(1)$ for some $\kappa > 0$, and $\sum_{p}\sum_{k \ge 2} f(p^k)/p^k < \infty$, then
$$
\frac{1}{x}\sum_{n \le x} f(n) \sim \frac{e^{-\gamma\kappa}}{\Gamma(\kappa)}\prod_{p \le x}\Bigl(1 - \frac1p\Bigr)^{\kappa}\Bigl(1 + \frac{f(p)}{p} + \frac{f(p^2)}{p^2} + \cdots\Bigr).
$$
Sketch the reduction to the case $f(p) = \kappa$ and the role of the Selberg-Delange comparison.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Dirichlet series is $F(s) = \prod_p(1 + f(p)p^{-s} + \cdots) = \zeta(s)^\kappa G(s)$ with $G$ holomorphic and non-vanishing at $s = 1$ under the hypotheses; apply the Selberg-Delange asymptotic of [21.11.05] and evaluate the constant $G(1)/\Gamma(\kappa)$ using Mertens' product $\prod_{p \le x}(1 - 1/p)^{-1} \sim e^\gamma\log x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Form the Euler product $F(s) = \sum_n f(n) n^{-s} = \prod_p\bigl(1 + f(p)p^{-s} + f(p^2)p^{-2s} + \cdots\bigr)$, absolutely convergent for $\sigma > 1$. Factor out the singular part by comparison with $\zeta(s)^\kappa = \prod_p(1 - p^{-s})^{-\kappa}$:
$$
F(s) = \zeta(s)^\kappa\, G(s), \qquad G(s) = \prod_p\Bigl(1 - p^{-s}\Bigr)^{\kappa}\Bigl(1 + f(p)p^{-s} + \cdots\Bigr).
$$
The hypothesis $\sum_{p \le x} f(p)\frac{\log p}{p} = \kappa\log x + O(1)$ forces $f(p) = \kappa + (\text{mean-zero fluctuation})$, so the leading $p^{-s}$ terms of the two factors cancel: $(1 - p^{-s})^\kappa(1 + f(p)p^{-s}) = 1 + (f(p) - \kappa)p^{-s} + O(p^{-2s})$, and together with the convergent higher-prime-power hypothesis, $G(s)$ extends holomorphically and non-vanishing to a neighbourhood of $\sigma = 1$ with $G(1) \ne 0$. Thus $f$ lies in the Selberg-Delange class $\mathcal{D}(\kappa)$ of [21.11.05], and that theorem gives
$$
\sum_{n \le x} f(n) \sim \frac{G(1)}{\Gamma(\kappa)}\, x\,(\log x)^{\kappa - 1}.
$$

To put the constant in Wirsing's product form, write $G(1) = \prod_p(1 - 1/p)^\kappa(1 + f(p)/p + \cdots)$ and pull out a factor $(\log x)^{\kappa-1}$ by Mertens' theorem $\prod_{p \le x}(1 - 1/p)^{-1} \sim e^\gamma\log x$ [21.11.02], whence $\prod_{p \le x}(1 - 1/p)^\kappa \sim e^{-\gamma\kappa}(\log x)^{-\kappa}$. Dividing by $x$ and reassembling,
$$
\frac1x\sum_{n \le x} f(n) \sim \frac{e^{-\gamma\kappa}}{\Gamma(\kappa)}\prod_{p \le x}\Bigl(1 - \frac1p\Bigr)^{\kappa}\Bigl(1 + \frac{f(p)}{p} + \cdots\Bigr).
$$
The reduction to $f(p) = \kappa$ is the case $G \equiv$ (a convergent constant), where the answer is purely the $\zeta^\kappa$ singularity; the general $f$ differs only by the convergent corrector $G$. Rubric: full credit for the $\zeta^\kappa G$ factorisation, the holomorphy of $G$ at $s = 1$ from the averaged hypothesis, the Selberg-Delange invocation, and the Mertens evaluation of the constant. $\square$

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**The Halász-Montgomery inequality and the large sieve.** The pointwise bound $|F(1 + 1/\log x + i\tau)| \ll (\log x)\,e^{-\mathbb{D}(f, n^{i\tau}; x)^2}$ is converted into a mean-value estimate by sampling the line at well-spaced points and bounding the resulting Dirichlet polynomials. The mechanism is the Halász-Montgomery inequality $\sum_{r}|\sum_{n \le N} a_n n^{-it_r}|^2 \le (N + R\Delta^{-1})\sum_n|a_n|^2$ for $\Delta$-spaced $t_r$, itself the additive large sieve $\sum_r|\sum_n a_n e(n\xi_r)|^2 \le (N + \delta^{-1})\sum_n|a_n|^2$ specialised to logarithmic frequencies $\xi_r = \frac{t_r}{2\pi}\log n$ [ref: TODO_REF Montgomery-Vaughan §6]. The large sieve is the structural reason the integral of $|F|$ over a line of length $2T$ costs only a factor $\log x$ rather than $T\log x$: the values $F(1 + i\tau)$ at spaced $\tau$ behave like an almost-orthogonal system, so their square-sum is controlled by the single Parseval quantity $\sum_n |f(n)|^2/n \ll \log x$.

**The quantitative Granville-Soundararajan form.** The decay rate $(1 + M)e^{-M}$ in Halász's theorem is sharp, and Granville and Soundararajan recast the whole theory around the distance $\mathbb{D}$ as the master object [ref: TODO_REF Granville-Soundararajan 2007]. Their formulation proves $\bigl|\frac1x\sum_{n \le x} f(n)\bigr| \ll (1 + M)e^{-M} + (\log x)^{-1/2 + o(1)}$ uniformly, with the minimising $t_0 = t_0(x)$ exhibited, and establishes the **Lipschitz estimate** $\bigl|M_f(x) - M_f(x^{1/(1+\delta)})\bigr| \ll \delta\log(1/\delta) + \mathbb{D}$-controlled corrections, the analytic backbone of the pretentious large-sieve and of the Matomäki-Radziwiłł theorem on multiplicative functions in short intervals. The triangle inequality for $\mathbb{D}$ is what makes the family $\{n^{it}\}$ behave like a single point in the relevant quotient, so "pretending to be $n^{it}$" is an equivalence-class statement.

**Wirsing's theorem and the Erdős-Wintner problem.** For real non-negative multiplicative $f$ with prime-averaged density $\kappa$, Wirsing's theorem upgrades the Halász upper bound to a genuine asymptotic $\frac1x\sum_{n \le x} f(n) \sim \frac{e^{-\gamma\kappa}}{\Gamma(\kappa)}\prod_{p \le x}(1 - 1/p)^\kappa(1 + f(p)/p + \cdots)$ [ref: TODO_REF Wirsing 1967]. The case $\kappa = 1$, $f = \mathbf{1}_{S}$ for a multiplicative set $S$, resolves the Erdős-Wintner mean-value problem and yields the density of $S$. The real hypothesis is essential: it removes the rotational ambiguity in the choice of benchmark, pinning $t_0 = 0$, so that the upper bound becomes a two-sided asymptotic. This is precisely the boundary the Selberg-Delange method of [21.11.05] occupies from the other side — Selberg-Delange needs an analytic factorisation but gives full asymptotic expansions, Wirsing needs only positivity and averaged density.

**The Delange theorem and the mean-value dichotomy.** Delange's 1961 theorem isolates the surviving case: if $|f| \le 1$ is multiplicative and $\sum_p (1 - f(p))/p$ converges (so $\mathbb{D}(f, 1; x)$ stays bounded), then $M_f(x) \to \prod_p(1 - 1/p)(1 + f(p)/p + \cdots) \ne 0$ [ref: TODO_REF Delange 1961]. Combined with Halász, this gives the complete **mean-value dichotomy** for $|f| \le 1$: either $f$ pretends to be some $n^{it}$ and $M_f(x)$ oscillates with a computable modulus, or it pretends to nothing and $M_f(x) \to 0$. There is no intermediate regime, which is the multiplicative-function analogue of the zero-one laws of probability.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason mean values of multiplicative functions split cleanly into "survives" and "decays" is the pretentious distance $\mathbb{D}(f, n^{it}; x)^2 = \sum_{p \le x}(1 - \operatorname{Re}f(p)p^{-it})/p$: the mean is large exactly when this distance is small for some $t$, and the central insight is that $|F(1 + 1/\log x + it)|$ loses precisely a factor $e^{-\mathbb{D}^2}$ from its maximal size, so the size of the Euler product on the line is the size of the mean. This is exactly the soft counterpart of the Selberg-Delange asymptotic of [21.11.05]: where Selberg-Delange demands the factorisation $F = \zeta^z G$ and reads an exact $x(\log x)^{z-1}/\Gamma(z)$, Halász asks only $|f| \le 1$ and reads the sharp bound $(1+M)e^{-M}$ — putting these together, the two are dual halves of the line-$\sigma = 1$ analysis, the contour method for clean Euler products and the distance method for arbitrary ones. The Halász-Montgomery inequality generalises the large sieve from additive to multiplicative characters, and the bridge is the partial summation of [21.11.02] that turns the Dirichlet series into the summatory function; the same machine appears again in the Granville-Soundararajan pretentious programme, where $\mathbb{D}$ becomes the metric in which short-interval and arithmetic-progression results of the Matomäki-Radziwiłł type are stated.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (non-negativity and triangle inequality of $\mathbb{D}$).** *For multiplicative $f, g, h$ with values in $\overline{\mathbb{D}}$, $\mathbb{D}(f, g; x)^2 \ge 0$ and $\mathbb{D}(f, h; x) \le \mathbb{D}(f, g; x) + \mathbb{D}(g, h; x)$.*

*Proof.* Non-negativity: for each prime $\operatorname{Re}(f(p)\overline{g(p)}) \le |f(p)\overline{g(p)}| \le 1$, so $1 - \operatorname{Re}(f(p)\overline{g(p)}) \ge 0$ and the weighted sum is non-negative. For the triangle inequality, fix $p$ and set $u = f(p)$, $v = g(p)$, $w = h(p)$ in $\overline{\mathbb{D}}$. The chordal-distance bound $\sqrt{1 - \operatorname{Re}(u\bar w)} \le \sqrt{1 - \operatorname{Re}(u\bar v)} + \sqrt{1 - \operatorname{Re}(v\bar w)}$ holds because $\sqrt{1 - \operatorname{Re}(z\bar z')}$ is, up to the factor $1/\sqrt2$, the Euclidean distance from $z$ to $z'$ on the unit circle extended to the disc by the parallelogram identity, and Euclidean distance obeys the triangle inequality. Writing $\mathbb{D}(f, g; x) = \|(p^{-1/2}\sqrt{1 - \operatorname{Re}(f(p)\overline{g(p)})})_{p \le x}\|_{2}$ and applying Minkowski's inequality to the pointwise bound $c_p \le a_p + b_p$ yields $\|c\|_2 \le \|a\|_2 + \|b\|_2$, the claim. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Euler-product size on the $1$-line).** *Let $f$ be multiplicative with $|f| \le 1$ and $F(s) = \sum_n f(n) n^{-s}$. For $s = 1 + 1/\log x + i\tau$,*
$$
\log|F(s)| = \log\log x - \mathbb{D}(f, n^{i\tau}; x)^2 + O(1).
$$

*Proof.* Take the logarithm of the Euler product $F(s) = \prod_p(1 + f(p)p^{-s} + f(p^2)p^{-2s} + \cdots)$. Since $|f| \le 1$, the contribution of prime powers $p^k$ with $k \ge 2$ is $\sum_p\sum_{k \ge 2} O(p^{-k\sigma}) = O(1)$ uniformly for $\sigma \ge 1$, and $\log(1 + z) = z + O(|z|^2)$ absorbs the squared first-order term into the same $O(1)$. Hence $\log|F(s)| = \operatorname{Re}\sum_p f(p)p^{-s} + O(1)$. With $\sigma = 1 + 1/\log x$ the factor $p^{-1/\log x} = e^{-\log p/\log x}$ is $\asymp 1$ for $p \le x$ and decays for $p > x$, so up to $O(1)$ the sum is over $p \le x$:
$$
\operatorname{Re}\sum_{p \le x}\frac{f(p)}{p^{1 + i\tau}} + O(1) = \sum_{p \le x}\frac{\operatorname{Re}(f(p)p^{-i\tau})}{p} + O(1).
$$
Subtract and add $\sum_{p \le x} 1/p = \log\log x + O(1)$ (Mertens, [21.11.02]):
$$
\log|F(s)| = \log\log x - \sum_{p \le x}\frac{1 - \operatorname{Re}(f(p)p^{-i\tau})}{p} + O(1) = \log\log x - \mathbb{D}(f, n^{i\tau}; x)^2 + O(1). \qquad \square
$$

**Proposition 3 (Parseval bound for the Dirichlet polynomial).** *For $|f| \le 1$ and $T \ge 1$,*
$$
\int_{-T}^{T}\bigl|F(1 + \tfrac{1}{\log x} + i\tau)\bigr|^2\,d\tau \ll T\log x.
$$

*Proof.* By the mean-value theorem for Dirichlet series (the large-sieve / Montgomery-Vaughan mean-value estimate),
$$
\int_{-T}^{T}\Bigl|\sum_n \frac{f(n)}{n^{1 + 1/\log x}} n^{-i\tau}\Bigr|^2 d\tau \ll \sum_n \frac{|f(n)|^2}{n^{2 + 2/\log x}}\bigl(n + T\bigr) \ll T\sum_n \frac{|f(n)|^2}{n^{2}} + \sum_n\frac{|f(n)|^2}{n^{1 + 2/\log x}}.
$$
The first sum converges (it is $\le \zeta(2)$ since $|f| \le 1$); the second is $\ll \log x$ by Mertens applied to $\sum_{n \le x} 1/n^{1 + 2/\log x} \asymp \log x$. The dominant term is $T\log x$ once $T \ge 1$, giving the bound. This is the quantitative form of the statement that spaced values of $F$ on the $1$-line are almost orthogonal, the input that lets the Halász integral cost only $\log x$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (decay in the non-pretentious case).** *If $\mathbb{D}(f, n^{it}; x) \to \infty$ as $x \to \infty$ for every fixed $t \in \mathbb{R}$, then $M_f(x) \to 0$.*

*Proof.* Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. Choose $T = T(\varepsilon) = 2/\varepsilon$, contributing truncation error $1/T = \varepsilon/2$ in Halász's theorem. By hypothesis, for each $|t| \le T$ the distance $\mathbb{D}(f, n^{it}; x)^2 \to \infty$; by a compactness/uniformity argument over the compact interval $[-T, T]$ (the distances are equicontinuous in $t$ on the regulariser scale), the minimum $M(x, T) = \min_{|t| \le T}\mathbb{D}(f, n^{it}; x)^2 \to \infty$ as well. Then $(1 + M)e^{-M} \to 0$, so there is $x_0$ with $(1 + M)e^{-M} < \varepsilon/2$ for $x \ge x_0$. Halász's theorem gives $|M_f(x)| \le (1 + M)e^{-M} + 1/T < \varepsilon$ for $x \ge x_0$. Since $\varepsilon$ was arbitrary, $M_f(x) \to 0$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Average orders and the summation toolkit [21.11.02].** Halász's method runs on the partial-summation pairing between a Dirichlet series and its summatory function established there: the mean $\frac1x\sum_{n \le x} f(n)$ is read off from a line integral of $F(s)$, and Mertens' estimate $\sum_{p \le x} 1/p = \log\log x + O(1)$ from that unit is the comparison value against which the pretentious distance is measured. The toolkit supplies the analytic plumbing; this unit supplies the multiplicative content.

- **The Selberg-Delange method [21.11.05].** This unit is the soft, real-variable mirror of the contour method: Selberg-Delange demands the clean factorisation $F(s) = \zeta(s)^z G(s)$ and extracts the exact asymptotic $x(\log x)^{z-1}/\Gamma(z)$, while Halász asks only $|f| \le 1$ and delivers the sharp upper bound $(1 + M)e^{-M}$. Exercise 8 shows the two methods coincide on their overlap, where Wirsing's theorem is literally Selberg-Delange applied to the factored Euler product; together they bracket the analysis on the line $\operatorname{Re}(s) = 1$ from both sides.

- **Kloosterman sums and the Kuznetsov formula [21.14.05].** Both this unit and the spectral large sieve of that unit descend from the additive large sieve $\sum_r|\sum_n a_n e(n\xi_r)|^2 \le (N + \delta^{-1})\sum_n|a_n|^2$. The Halász-Montgomery inequality is its specialisation to logarithmic frequencies $\xi_r \propto \log n$, while the Deshouillers-Iwaniec spectral large sieve is its specialisation to the Fourier coefficients of automorphic forms; the shared almost-orthogonality principle is the structural link between mean values of multiplicative functions and the spectral theory of $\mathrm{GL}_2$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Erdős and Wintner posed the mean-value problem for multiplicative functions in the 1930s and conjectured that a real multiplicative $f$ with $|f| \le 1$ always has a mean value. Wirsing settled the real non-negative case in a 1967 *Mathematische Annalen* paper [ref: TODO_REF Wirsing 1967] by an intricate elementary-analytic argument, obtaining the asymptotic with its $e^{-\gamma\kappa}/\Gamma(\kappa)$ constant and resolving the Erdős-Wintner conjecture in that range. The following year Gábor Halász, in *Acta Mathematica Academiae Scientiarum Hungaricae* [ref: TODO_REF Halász 1968], treated the full complex case $|f| \le 1$ and identified the governing quantity as the minimal distance to the family $n^{it}$, with the sharp decay rate that bears his name. The contemporaneous Delange theorem [ref: TODO_REF Delange 1961] had already pinned the surviving case where $\sum_p (1 - f(p))/p$ converges.

The reformulation in terms of the pretentious distance $\mathbb{D}$ is due to Granville and Soundararajan in the 2000s [ref: TODO_REF Granville-Soundararajan 2007], who recognised that $\mathbb{D}(f, g; x)^2 = \sum_{p \le x}(1 - \operatorname{Re}f(p)\overline{g(p)})/p$ is a genuine pseudometric obeying the triangle inequality, and rebuilt the mean-value theory, the large sieve for multiplicative functions, and eventually the Matomäki-Radziwiłł short-interval theorem as statements about distance in this metric. The Halász-Montgomery inequality, which transmits the pointwise control of the Euler product on the $1$-line to the mean value, is the multiplicative descendant of the large sieve developed by Linnik, Rényi, Bombieri, and Montgomery; its appearance here marks the point where sieve theory and the theory of multiplicative functions become one subject.
