## Intuition [Beginner]

A finite field is a number system with finitely many elements in which you can add, subtract, multiply, and divide (except by zero) and the usual algebraic rules hold. The simplest finite field is $\mathbb{F}_2$, the two-element field with elements $\{0, 1\}$ and addition that wraps around: $1 + 1 = 0$. More generally, for any prime $p$ the integers modulo $p$ form a finite field $\mathbb{F}_p$ with $p$ elements. These are the only finite fields whose size is prime, but they are far from the only finite fields.

The surprise is that finite fields with composite size also exist, but only when that size is a prime power. There is a field with $4$ elements, a field with $8$ elements, a field with $9$ elements, a field with $25$ elements, and so on through every power $p^n$. There is no field with $6$ elements, no field with $10$ elements, no field with $12$ elements. The allowed sizes are exactly the prime powers, and for each prime power the field is unique up to renaming.

Why care? Finite fields are the building blocks of coding theory, of cryptography, of the geometry of algebraic curves over finite fields. They package arithmetic into a finite, computable shape that still satisfies the field axioms. The structure theorem for finite fields is the foundation on which Gauss sums, quadratic reciprocity, and modern number theory are built.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Build the field $\mathbb{F}_4$ with four elements, list its multiplication table, and identify a primitive root.

Step 1. The four elements of $\mathbb{F}_4$ are $\{0, 1, \alpha, \alpha + 1\}$, where $\alpha$ is a new symbol satisfying $\alpha^2 + \alpha + 1 = 0$. The choice of $\alpha$ comes from the polynomial $X^2 + X + 1$, which is the unique degree-$2$ polynomial over $\mathbb{F}_2$ with no root in $\mathbb{F}_2$ (the other monic degree-$2$ polynomials $X^2$, $X^2 + 1 = (X+1)^2$, $X^2 + X = X(X+1)$ all factor).

Step 2. Addition is componentwise modulo $2$ on the coefficients of $\alpha$. So $\alpha + \alpha = 0$, $\alpha + (\alpha + 1) = 1$, $1 + (\alpha + 1) = \alpha$, and so on. The additive group is $(\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z})^2$, a Klein four-group.

Step 3. Multiplication uses $\alpha^2 = \alpha + 1$ (rearranging $\alpha^2 + \alpha + 1 = 0$ and recalling $-1 = 1$ in characteristic $2$). So $\alpha \cdot \alpha = \alpha + 1$, $\alpha \cdot (\alpha + 1) = \alpha^2 + \alpha = (\alpha + 1) + \alpha = 1$, and $(\alpha + 1)(\alpha + 1) = \alpha^2 + 1 = \alpha$ (using $2\alpha = 0$). The full multiplication table on the non-zero elements is:

| $\times$ | $1$ | $\alpha$ | $\alpha + 1$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| $1$ | $1$ | $\alpha$ | $\alpha + 1$ |
| $\alpha$ | $\alpha$ | $\alpha + 1$ | $1$ |
| $\alpha + 1$ | $\alpha + 1$ | $1$ | $\alpha$ |

Step 4. The three non-zero elements $\{1, \alpha, \alpha + 1\}$ form a group of order $3$ under multiplication. Read off the powers: $\alpha^1 = \alpha$, $\alpha^2 = \alpha + 1$, $\alpha^3 = 1$. So $\alpha$ generates the multiplicative group; $\alpha$ is a primitive root of $\mathbb{F}_4$.

Step 5. The Frobenius map $\phi(x) = x^2$ acts on $\mathbb{F}_4$ by $\phi(0) = 0$, $\phi(1) = 1$, $\phi(\alpha) = \alpha + 1$, $\phi(\alpha + 1) = 1 + \alpha + 1 + \alpha + 1 = \alpha$? Recompute: $(\alpha + 1)^2 = \alpha^2 + 2\alpha + 1 = (\alpha + 1) + 0 + 1 = \alpha$. So $\phi$ swaps $\alpha$ with $\alpha + 1$ and fixes $\{0, 1\}$. The fixed field of $\phi$ is $\mathbb{F}_2 = \{0, 1\}$, and $\phi^2 = \mathrm{id}$, so $\phi$ has order $2$, matching the degree $[\mathbb{F}_4 : \mathbb{F}_2] = 2$.

What this tells us: a finite field of prime-power size is built by adjoining a root of an irreducible polynomial to the prime field, the multiplicative group is cyclic and generated by a primitive root, and Frobenius is the canonical generator of the field automorphism group with fixed field equal to the prime field.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $p$ be a prime and $n \geq 1$ an integer. A **finite field of order $q = p^n$** is a field $\mathbb{F}_q$ with exactly $q$ elements. The **characteristic** of $\mathbb{F}_q$ is $p$: the unique prime such that $p \cdot 1 = 0$ in $\mathbb{F}_q$, equivalently the additive order of the multiplicative identity.

The **prime field** of $\mathbb{F}_q$ is the subfield $\mathbb{F}_p = \{0, 1, 2, \ldots, p - 1\} \subseteq \mathbb{F}_q$ generated by the multiplicative identity, isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ as a ring. The inclusion $\mathbb{F}_p \hookrightarrow \mathbb{F}_q$ gives $\mathbb{F}_q$ the structure of an $\mathbb{F}_p$-vector space; since $|\mathbb{F}_q| = q = p^n$ and $|\mathbb{F}_p| = p$, the dimension of this vector space is $\dim_{\mathbb{F}_p} \mathbb{F}_q = n$.

The **multiplicative group** $\mathbb{F}_q^\times = \mathbb{F}_q \setminus \{0\}$ is an abelian group of order $q - 1$ under field multiplication.

The **Frobenius endomorphism** is the map
$$
\phi : \mathbb{F}_q \to \mathbb{F}_q, \qquad \phi(x) = x^p.
$$
It is a ring homomorphism because $\phi(x + y) = (x + y)^p = x^p + y^p$ in characteristic $p$ (the binomial coefficients $\binom{p}{k}$ for $1 \leq k \leq p - 1$ vanish modulo $p$), and $\phi(xy) = (xy)^p = x^p y^p$. The Frobenius is injective (kernel is a proper ideal in a field, hence the zero ideal) and since $\mathbb{F}_q$ is finite, also surjective, so $\phi$ is a field automorphism of $\mathbb{F}_q$.

The **Galois group** $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_q / \mathbb{F}_p)$ is the group of field automorphisms of $\mathbb{F}_q$ that fix every element of $\mathbb{F}_p$.

A non-zero element $g \in \mathbb{F}_q^\times$ is a **primitive root** if it generates $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ as a cyclic group, that is, $\mathbb{F}_q^\times = \{1, g, g^2, \ldots, g^{q-2}\}$.

When $p$ is odd, the **squares** in $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ form the image of the squaring map $x \mapsto x^2$ on $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$:
$$
(\mathbb{F}_q^\times)^2 = \{x^2 : x \in \mathbb{F}_q^\times\} \subseteq \mathbb{F}_q^\times.
$$
The **Legendre-style character** of $\mathbb{F}_q$ in odd characteristic is the map
$$
\chi : \mathbb{F}_q^\times \to \{\pm 1\}, \qquad \chi(x) = x^{(q-1)/2},
$$
which takes the value $+1$ on squares and $-1$ on non-squares.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The cyclicity of $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ does not extend to the unit group of an arbitrary finite commutative ring. The unit group $(\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z})^\times = \{1, 3, 5, 7\}$ has four elements but is not cyclic; it is isomorphic to the Klein four-group, since every non-identity element has order $2$. Cyclicity of $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ uses the field property crucially, through the bound on the number of roots of $X^d - 1$.
- In characteristic $2$, the squaring map $x \mapsto x^2$ on $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ is the Frobenius and is a bijection, not a $2$-to-$1$ map. Every element of $\mathbb{F}_q$ with $q$ a power of $2$ is a square, and the squares-index-$2$ statement fails. The standing assumption $p$ odd in the squares discussion is load-bearing.
- The map $X^d - 1$ has at most $d$ roots in a field, but in a non-field like $\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z}$ the polynomial $X^2 - 1$ has four roots $\{1, 3, 5, 7\}$. The counting argument behind cyclicity breaks without the field hypothesis.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Structure of finite fields; Serre *Course in Arithmetic* Ch. I §1).** *Let $p$ be a prime and $n \geq 1$ an integer. Set $q = p^n$.*

1. *(Existence and uniqueness.) There exists a field of order $q$, namely the splitting field of $X^q - X$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$. Any two fields of order $q$ are isomorphic over $\mathbb{F}_p$.*
2. *(Cyclicity.) The multiplicative group $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ is cyclic of order $q - 1$.*
3. *(Frobenius generates the Galois group.) The Frobenius automorphism $\phi(x) = x^p$ has order $n$ in $\mathrm{Aut}(\mathbb{F}_q)$, and $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_q/\mathbb{F}_p) = \langle \phi \rangle$ is cyclic of order $n$. The fixed field of $\phi$ is exactly $\mathbb{F}_p$.*
4. *(Squares, $p$ odd.) When $p$ is odd, the squaring map $\mathbb{F}_q^\times \to \mathbb{F}_q^\times$ has kernel $\{\pm 1\}$ of size $2$ and image of index $2$, so $[\mathbb{F}_q^\times : (\mathbb{F}_q^\times)^2] = 2$.*

**Proof.**

*Part 1 — Existence.* Take the polynomial $f(X) = X^q - X \in \mathbb{F}_p[X]$. Its formal derivative is $f'(X) = qX^{q-1} - 1 = -1$ (because $q$ is a power of $p$ so $q \equiv 0 \pmod p$), which is a non-zero constant. So $\gcd(f, f') = 1$ in $\mathbb{F}_p[X]$, and $f$ has $q$ distinct roots in any field where it splits completely.

Let $K$ be the splitting field of $f$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$. Let $F = \{x \in K : x^q = x\}$ be the set of roots of $f$ in $K$. The set $F$ is closed under field operations: if $x, y \in F$ then $(xy)^q = x^q y^q = xy$ so $xy \in F$; $(x + y)^q = x^q + y^q = x + y$ by iteration of the Frobenius identity, so $x + y \in F$; the elements $0$ and $1$ are in $F$; and if $x \in F \setminus \{0\}$ then $(x^{-1})^q = (x^q)^{-1} = x^{-1}$ so $x^{-1} \in F$. Therefore $F$ is a subfield of $K$ of cardinality $q$ (since $f$ has $q$ distinct roots and they exhaust $F$). The splitting field $K$ is generated over $\mathbb{F}_p$ by these roots, so $K = F$, and $K$ has exactly $q$ elements.

*Part 1 — Uniqueness.* Conversely, let $E$ be any field of order $q = p^n$. Its characteristic is $p$ (the additive order of $1$ divides $|E| = q = p^n$, so it is a power of $p$; being prime, it equals $p$), so $E$ contains $\mathbb{F}_p$. The group $E^\times$ has order $q - 1$, so every $x \in E^\times$ satisfies $x^{q-1} = 1$ by Lagrange's theorem applied to the order of $x$ in the multiplicative group, hence $x^q = x$, and this identity extends to $x = 0$. Thus every element of $E$ is a root of $X^q - X$, and since $X^q - X$ has at most $q$ roots in a field, the $q$ elements of $E$ are exactly its roots. So $E$ is a splitting field of $X^q - X$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$, and splitting fields of a fixed polynomial over a fixed base field are unique up to isomorphism.

*Part 2 — Cyclicity.* We prove the more general statement that every finite subgroup $G$ of $K^\times$, for any field $K$, is cyclic. Let $|G| = m$. For every $d \mid m$, the number of elements of order dividing $d$ in $G$ is at most $d$, since these elements are roots of $X^d - 1$ in $K$ and a degree-$d$ polynomial has at most $d$ roots in a field. The number of elements of order exactly $d$ in a cyclic group of order $d$ is $\varphi(d)$ (Euler totient); in any group of order $m$ the number of elements of order $d$ is either $0$ or a multiple of $\varphi(d)$, and the sum over $d \mid m$ of the latter equals $m$. Combining with the bound, each $d \mid m$ for which $G$ has an element of order $d$ contributes exactly $\varphi(d)$ such elements; the identity $\sum_{d \mid m} \varphi(d) = m$ forces every divisor to contribute, in particular $d = m$, giving an element of order $m$, so $G$ is cyclic.

Applied to $G = \mathbb{F}_q^\times$ of order $q - 1$, this yields the cyclicity claim.

*Part 3 — Frobenius.* The Frobenius $\phi$ is a field automorphism of $\mathbb{F}_q$ by the binomial-coefficient calculation in characteristic $p$. The fixed elements of $\phi$ are exactly the roots of $X^p - X$, which is a degree-$p$ polynomial with at most $p$ roots, and $\mathbb{F}_p \subseteq \mathbb{F}_q$ already provides $p$ such roots (every element of $\mathbb{F}_p$ satisfies $x^p = x$ by Fermat's little theorem). So the fixed field of $\phi$ is exactly $\mathbb{F}_p$.

The iterate $\phi^k(x) = x^{p^k}$ is the identity on $\mathbb{F}_q$ iff $x^{p^k} = x$ for every $x \in \mathbb{F}_q$, that is, iff $\mathbb{F}_q \subseteq \{x : x^{p^k} = x\}$. The right side is a subfield of order at most $p^k$, so the inclusion forces $p^n = |\mathbb{F}_q| \leq p^k$, hence $k \geq n$. Conversely $\phi^n(x) = x^{p^n} = x^q = x$ for every $x \in \mathbb{F}_q$, the last equality by Part 1. So $\phi$ has order exactly $n$.

The group $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_q/\mathbb{F}_p)$ has order $[\mathbb{F}_q : \mathbb{F}_p] = n$ by the Galois correspondence applied to the separable extension $\mathbb{F}_q/\mathbb{F}_p$ (separability holds because $X^q - X$ is separable, as computed in Part 1). The cyclic subgroup $\langle \phi \rangle$ already has order $n$, so $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_q/\mathbb{F}_p) = \langle \phi \rangle$.

*Part 4 — Squares.* Assume $p$ is odd. The squaring map $s : \mathbb{F}_q^\times \to \mathbb{F}_q^\times$, $s(x) = x^2$, is a group homomorphism with kernel $\{x : x^2 = 1\} = \{x : (x - 1)(x + 1) = 0\}$. Since $\mathbb{F}_q$ is a field and $p$ is odd, $-1 \neq 1$, so the kernel is $\{\pm 1\}$ of order $2$.

By the first isomorphism theorem, the image $(\mathbb{F}_q^\times)^2$ has order $|\mathbb{F}_q^\times| / 2 = (q - 1)/2$, hence index $2$ in $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The theorem builds toward every modern statement about $\mathbb{F}_q$ that arises in number theory, algebraic geometry over finite fields, and coding theory. The central insight is that the splitting field of $X^q - X$ is exactly the set of fixed points of the $n$-fold iterate of Frobenius, which putting these together identifies $\mathbb{F}_q$ with the fixed field of $\phi^n$ acting on the algebraic closure $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$. This is exactly the foundational reason the Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}/\mathbb{F}_p)$ is the profinite completion $\widehat{\mathbb{Z}}$, topologically generated by Frobenius, and the cyclic Galois groups $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p) \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ are quotients of $\widehat{\mathbb{Z}}$.

The squares-index-two statement is dual to the existence of a non-identity multiplicative character $\chi : \mathbb{F}_q^\times \to \{\pm 1\}$, and the central insight of Gauss's analytic proof of quadratic reciprocity is that this character, paired with an additive character through the Gauss sum $g(\chi) = \sum_x \chi(x) \psi(x)$, satisfies the quadratic identity $g(\chi)^2 = \chi(-1) q$. This pattern of pairing a multiplicative character with an additive one appears again in [21.03.01] (Riemann zeta and Dirichlet $L$) and in [21.02.02] (quadratic reciprocity via Gauss sums). The bridge is the recognition that finite-field structure underlies every local question in number theory: $\mathbb{F}_p$ is the residue field of $\mathbb{Z}_p$, $\mathbb{F}_q$ for $q$ a prime power is the residue field of any local field of equal characteristic or mixed characteristic with residue field of size $q$, and Frobenius lifts to the local Galois group. The same structural picture generalises through the function-field side of the Langlands programme, where varieties over $\mathbb{F}_q$ have $\ell$-adic cohomology on which Frobenius acts, and the Weil conjectures package the resulting eigenvalues into zeta-function data.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (Wedderburn's little theorem; Wedderburn 1905 *Trans. Amer. Math. Soc.* 6, 349-352).** *Every finite division ring is commutative; equivalently, the only finite skew fields are the finite fields $\mathbb{F}_q$.*

The cleanest modern proof, due to Witt 1931 *Abh. Math. Sem. Univ. Hamburg* 8, 413 [ref: TODO_REF], is a three-page counting argument on the class equation of $D^\times$ for $D$ a finite division ring. Let $|D| = q^n$ where $q$ is the order of the centre $Z(D)$. The class equation gives $q^n - 1 = (q - 1) + \sum_i (q^n - 1)/(q^{d_i} - 1)$, where the sum is over conjugacy classes of non-central elements and $d_i$ are the dimensions of the centralisers, each a proper divisor of $n$. The cyclotomic polynomial $\Phi_n(q)$ divides each summand and divides $q^n - 1$ on the left, hence divides $q - 1$ on the right. For $n \geq 2$, $|\Phi_n(q)| > q - 1$ (a direct estimate $|\Phi_n(q)| \geq \prod_\zeta |q - \zeta| > (q - 1)^{\varphi(n)}$ when $q \geq 2$ and $\zeta$ ranges over primitive $n$th roots of unity off the real line). This forces $n = 1$, so $D = Z(D)$ is commutative.

The proof is essentially character-theoretic in disguise: the cyclotomic factor $\Phi_n(q) \mid q^n - 1$ comes from the factorisation $q^n - 1 = \prod_{d \mid n} \Phi_d(q)$, and the divisibility $\Phi_n(q) \mid (q^n - 1)/(q^{d_i} - 1)$ uses that $\Phi_n(q)$ has no common factor with $q^{d_i} - 1$ for $d_i \mid n$, $d_i < n$. Lidl-Niederreiter 1997 *Finite Fields* Ch. 2 [ref: TODO_REF] gives the full argument with the elementary estimate of $|\Phi_n(q)|$.

**Theorem (Galois correspondence for $\mathbb{F}_q/\mathbb{F}_p$).** *Let $q = p^n$. The map $H \mapsto \mathbb{F}_q^H$ from subgroups of $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_q/\mathbb{F}_p)$ to intermediate subfields $\mathbb{F}_p \subseteq F \subseteq \mathbb{F}_q$ is an order-reversing bijection. Under the identification $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_q/\mathbb{F}_p) = \langle \phi \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$, the subgroups are $\langle \phi^d \rangle$ for $d \mid n$, the corresponding fixed fields are $\mathbb{F}_{p^d}$, and the subfield lattice is anti-isomorphic to the divisor lattice of $n$.*

The result is the specialisation of the main theorem of Galois theory to the separable normal extension $\mathbb{F}_q/\mathbb{F}_p$. The cyclic structure of the Galois group, together with the divisor-lattice combinatorics of $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$, gives an unusually clean Galois correspondence: the field operations and the divisor combinatorics agree on the nose.

**Theorem (Number of irreducible polynomials over $\mathbb{F}_p$ of degree $n$).** *The number of monic irreducible polynomials of degree $n$ in $\mathbb{F}_p[X]$ is*
$$
N_p(n) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{d \mid n} \mu(n/d) p^d,
$$
*where $\mu$ is the Möbius function. Equivalently, $X^{p^n} - X = \prod_{d \mid n} \prod_{f} f(X)$, the product over all monic irreducible $f \in \mathbb{F}_p[X]$ of degree $d$ ranging over divisors of $n$.*

The factorisation of $X^{p^n} - X$ comes from the identification $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ as the splitting field. Each element of $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ has minimal polynomial of degree some $d \mid n$, and conversely each monic irreducible of degree $d \mid n$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$ has all its roots in $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ (specifically in $\mathbb{F}_{p^d} \subseteq \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$). Counting $p^n = \sum_{d \mid n} d \cdot N_p(d)$ and inverting by Möbius yields the formula.

**Theorem (Gauss sums and quadratic reciprocity; Gauss 1801 *Disquisitiones Arithmeticae* §356).** *Let $p$ be an odd prime, $\chi : \mathbb{F}_p^\times \to \{\pm 1\}$ the quadratic Legendre character, and $\psi : \mathbb{F}_p \to \mathbb{C}^\times$ the standard additive character $\psi(x) = e^{2\pi i x/p}$. The Gauss sum*
$$
g(\chi) = \sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times} \chi(x) \psi(x)
$$
*satisfies $g(\chi)^2 = \chi(-1) p = (-1)^{(p-1)/2} p$, and via this identity Gauss's analytic proof of quadratic reciprocity reduces the law $\left(\frac{q}{p}\right) \left(\frac{p}{q}\right) = (-1)^{(p-1)(q-1)/4}$ (for $p, q$ distinct odd primes) to the algebraic identity $g(\chi)^{(q-1)/2} = \left(\frac{q}{p}\right) g(\chi) \pmod q$ in the ring $\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_p]$.*

The Gauss-sum proof of quadratic reciprocity is the prototype of the analytic approach to reciprocity laws. The character-theoretic input is precisely the squares-index-$2$ statement of finite fields (without which there is no Legendre character), and the geometric input is the Pontryagin self-duality of $\mathbb{F}_p$ (without which there is no orthogonality of additive characters). Ireland-Rosen 1990 Ch. 6-7 [ref: TODO_REF] develops the full proof and the higher-power generalisations (Jacobi sums, biquadratic reciprocity).

**Theorem (Frobenius and the absolute Galois group of $\mathbb{F}_p$).** *Let $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$ be a fixed algebraic closure of $\mathbb{F}_p$. Then $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p} = \bigcup_n \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$, and the absolute Galois group*
$$
\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}/\mathbb{F}_p) = \varprojlim_n \mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p) = \varprojlim_n \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} = \widehat{\mathbb{Z}}
$$
*is the profinite completion of $\mathbb{Z}$, topologically generated by the Frobenius automorphism $\phi(x) = x^p$.*

The profinite group $\widehat{\mathbb{Z}}$ is the abelianised completion of $\mathbb{Z}$ at all positive integer moduli, isomorphic to $\prod_\ell \mathbb{Z}_\ell$ as a topological group with $\ell$ running over all primes. This is the prototype of a profinite Galois group, and the model for the absolute Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$ studied in modern number theory, whose abelianisation is by class field theory a quotient of an idele class group.

**Synthesis.** The finite-field structure theorem is the foundational reason every arithmetic statement over $\mathbb{F}_q$ reduces to two facts: the splitting-field description of $\mathbb{F}_q$ as the fixed field of $\phi^n$, and the cyclic multiplicative group $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ of order $q - 1$. The central insight is that putting these together gives both the existence-uniqueness statement and the Galois correspondence with the divisor lattice of $n$ in a single move. The squares-index-$2$ statement for $q$ odd identifies the kernel of the squaring map with $\{\pm 1\}$, and the Legendre-style character $\chi(x) = x^{(q-1)/2}$ is the dual character detecting squareness; this is dual to the analytic question of writing prime counting problems in terms of additive characters, where the multiplicative-additive pairing of the Gauss sum $g(\chi) = \sum_x \chi(x) \psi(x)$ encodes the quadratic-reciprocity law in a single algebraic identity $g(\chi)^2 = \chi(-1) q$.

The bridge from the elementary structure theorem to modern number theory passes through three identifications. First, the absolute Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}/\mathbb{F}_p) \cong \widehat{\mathbb{Z}}$ identifies the topological generator with Frobenius and packages every $\mathbb{F}_q$ as the fixed field of $\phi^n$. Second, the divisor-lattice subfield correspondence generalises through the function-field side of the Langlands programme to the étale fundamental group of a smooth curve over $\mathbb{F}_q$, where the Frobenius acts on $\ell$-adic cohomology. Third, the squares-index-$2$ statement is the simplest case of the general structure of multiplicative characters $\mathbb{F}_q^\times \to \mathbb{C}^\times$, whose Pontryagin dual is again $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ by Pontryagin duality of finite abelian groups, and whose pairing with additive characters via Gauss sums controls every analytic statement about $\mathbb{F}_q$.

Wedderburn's theorem is the structural corollary that one need not have separately considered "finite skew fields": every finite division ring is automatically commutative, so the entire arithmetic of finite division rings is exhausted by the structure theorem for $\mathbb{F}_q$. The Witt 1931 proof reduces commutativity to a cyclotomic-polynomial inequality on the class equation. The same cyclotomic-polynomial machinery, viewed from a different angle, controls the factorisation $X^{p^n} - X = \prod_{d \mid n} \prod_f f(X)$ of the defining polynomial into irreducibles of all divisor degrees, and gives the Möbius-inversion count $N_p(n) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{d \mid n} \mu(n/d) p^d$ of irreducible polynomials of degree $n$. The pattern recurs across the function-field analogue of number theory, where prime-power point counts on curves over $\mathbb{F}_q$ are controlled by Frobenius eigenvalues on cohomology, and the Weil conjectures (proved by Deligne 1973) package the resulting analytic information into a zeta function whose functional equation and Riemann-hypothesis statement parallel the Riemann zeta function.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Witt's proof of Wedderburn).** *Every finite division ring is commutative.*

*Proof.* Let $D$ be a finite division ring with centre $Z = Z(D)$, a field. Write $|Z| = q$, so $D$ is a $Z$-vector space of some dimension $n \geq 1$, $|D| = q^n$. Assume $n \geq 2$ toward a contradiction.

For each $a \in D \setminus Z$, the centraliser $C(a) = \{x \in D : xa = ax\}$ is a sub-division-ring of $D$ containing $Z$. As a $Z$-vector space, $|C(a)| = q^{d(a)}$ for some $1 \leq d(a) < n$. Since $D$ is a $C(a)$-vector space, $n = d(a) \cdot [D : C(a)]$ in the sense of dimensions, so $d(a) \mid n$.

The class equation of $D^\times$ under conjugation gives
$$
|D^\times| = |Z^\times| + \sum_a [D^\times : C(a)^\times],
$$
the sum over conjugacy classes of non-central elements, with one representative $a$ per class. This reads
$$
q^n - 1 = (q - 1) + \sum_a \frac{q^n - 1}{q^{d(a)} - 1}.
$$

The cyclotomic polynomial $\Phi_n(X) \in \mathbb{Z}[X]$ divides $X^n - 1$ in $\mathbb{Z}[X]$ via the factorisation $X^n - 1 = \prod_{d \mid n} \Phi_d(X)$. So $\Phi_n(q) \mid q^n - 1$ in $\mathbb{Z}$. For each non-central conjugacy class with centraliser dimension $d = d(a) < n$ and $d \mid n$, $\Phi_n(q)$ also divides $(q^n - 1)/(q^d - 1)$ in $\mathbb{Z}$: write $q^n - 1 = \prod_{e \mid n} \Phi_e(q)$ and $q^d - 1 = \prod_{e \mid d} \Phi_e(q)$; the quotient $(q^n - 1)/(q^d - 1) = \prod_{e \mid n, e \nmid d} \Phi_e(q)$ contains $\Phi_n(q)$ as a factor (since $n \mid n$ and $n \nmid d$ for $d < n$).

So $\Phi_n(q)$ divides every summand of the class-equation right side and divides the left side $q^n - 1$, hence divides their difference, which is $q - 1$.

But $\Phi_n(q) = \prod_\zeta (q - \zeta)$ over primitive $n$th roots of unity $\zeta \in \mathbb{C}$. Each factor $|q - \zeta| > q - 1$ for $\zeta \neq 1$ and $q \geq 2$: if $\zeta = e^{2\pi i k/n}$ with $\gcd(k, n) = 1$ and $\zeta \neq 1$ (which holds since $n \geq 2$), then $|q - \zeta|^2 = (q - \cos(2\pi k/n))^2 + \sin^2(2\pi k/n) = q^2 - 2q \cos(2\pi k/n) + 1$. For $\cos(2\pi k/n) < 1$ (which holds for $\zeta \neq 1$), this exceeds $q^2 - 2q + 1 = (q - 1)^2$, so $|q - \zeta| > q - 1$. Therefore $|\Phi_n(q)| = \prod_\zeta |q - \zeta| > (q - 1)^{\varphi(n)} \geq q - 1$ (using $\varphi(n) \geq 1$).

This contradicts $\Phi_n(q) \mid q - 1$, so $n = 1$ and $D = Z$ is commutative. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Cyclicity of every finite subgroup of $K^\times$).** *For any field $K$, every finite subgroup $G$ of the multiplicative group $K^\times$ is cyclic.*

*Proof.* Let $|G| = m$. For each divisor $d \mid m$, let $G_d = \{x \in G : x^d = 1\}$. Then $G_d$ is a subgroup of $G$ contained in the set of roots of $X^d - 1$ in $K$, which has at most $d$ elements because $K$ is a field. So $|G_d| \leq d$ for every $d \mid m$.

Let $f(d)$ denote the number of elements of $G$ of order exactly $d$. Then $|G_d| = \sum_{e \mid d} f(e)$, so by Möbius inversion $f(d) = \sum_{e \mid d} \mu(d/e) |G_e|$.

Suppose $f(d) > 0$ for some $d \mid m$. Pick $x \in G$ of order exactly $d$. Then $\langle x \rangle$ has $d$ elements all of order dividing $d$, all in $G_d$, so $\langle x \rangle \subseteq G_d$ and $|\langle x \rangle| = d \leq |G_d| \leq d$, giving $G_d = \langle x \rangle$, a cyclic group of order $d$. The number of generators of $\langle x \rangle$ is $\varphi(d)$, so $f(d) = \varphi(d)$ when $f(d) > 0$.

By the standard identity $\sum_{d \mid m} \varphi(d) = m$ and the count $\sum_{d \mid m} f(d) = m$ (every element has some order), we get $\sum_{d \mid m} (\varphi(d) - f(d)) = 0$. Since $f(d) \in \{0, \varphi(d)\}$ and $\varphi(d) > 0$, the only way for the sum to vanish is $f(d) = \varphi(d)$ for every $d \mid m$. In particular $f(m) = \varphi(m) \geq 1$, so $G$ contains an element of order $m$, and $G$ is cyclic. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Frobenius fixed field).** *The Frobenius $\phi(x) = x^p$ acting on $\mathbb{F}_q$ for $q = p^n$ has fixed field exactly $\mathbb{F}_p$, and order exactly $n$ in $\mathrm{Aut}(\mathbb{F}_q)$.*

*Proof.* For the fixed field: $\phi(x) = x$ iff $x^p - x = 0$. The polynomial $X^p - X \in \mathbb{F}_p[X]$ has degree $p$, hence at most $p$ roots in $\mathbb{F}_q$. The $p$ elements of $\mathbb{F}_p \subseteq \mathbb{F}_q$ satisfy $x^p = x$ by Fermat's little theorem (the cyclic group $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$ has order $p - 1$, so $x^{p-1} = 1$ for $x \neq 0$, hence $x^p = x$; for $x = 0$ this is automatic). So $\mathbb{F}_p \subseteq \mathrm{Fix}(\phi)$, and the size bound gives equality $\mathrm{Fix}(\phi) = \mathbb{F}_p$.

For the order: $\phi^k(x) = x^{p^k}$. The identity $\phi^k = \mathrm{id}$ on $\mathbb{F}_q$ means $x^{p^k} = x$ for every $x \in \mathbb{F}_q$, equivalently $\mathbb{F}_q \subseteq \mathrm{Fix}(\phi^k)$. The fixed field $\mathrm{Fix}(\phi^k)$ is the set of roots of $X^{p^k} - X$ in $\mathbb{F}_q$, which has at most $p^k$ elements. So $|\mathbb{F}_q| = p^n \leq p^k$, giving $k \geq n$. Conversely $\phi^n(x) = x^{p^n} = x^q = x$ for every $x \in \mathbb{F}_q$ (the multiplicative order of $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ is $q - 1$, so $x^{q-1} = 1$ for $x \neq 0$ and $x^q = x$ extends to $x = 0$). So $\phi^n = \mathrm{id}$, and the order of $\phi$ is exactly $n$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Squares index two).** *For $q = p^n$ with $p$ an odd prime, $[\mathbb{F}_q^\times : (\mathbb{F}_q^\times)^2] = 2$.*

*Proof.* Consider the squaring homomorphism $s : \mathbb{F}_q^\times \to \mathbb{F}_q^\times$, $s(x) = x^2$. Its kernel is $\{x \in \mathbb{F}_q^\times : x^2 = 1\} = \{x : (x - 1)(x + 1) = 0\}$. Since $\mathbb{F}_q$ is a field, this is $\{x : x = 1 \text{ or } x = -1\}$. Since $p$ is odd, $-1 \neq 1$ in $\mathbb{F}_q$, so the kernel has order $2$.

By the first isomorphism theorem, $\mathbb{F}_q^\times / \ker(s) \cong \mathrm{Im}(s) = (\mathbb{F}_q^\times)^2$, so $|(\mathbb{F}_q^\times)^2| = |\mathbb{F}_q^\times|/2 = (q - 1)/2$. The index of the squares in $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ is therefore $|\mathbb{F}_q^\times| / |(\mathbb{F}_q^\times)^2| = 2$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (Gauss sum identity).** *For $p$ an odd prime and $\chi : \mathbb{F}_p^\times \to \{\pm 1\}$ the quadratic Legendre character (with $\chi(0) = 0$), $g(\chi)^2 = \chi(-1) p$.*

*Proof.* Compute
$$
g(\chi)^2 = \sum_{x, y \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times} \chi(x) \chi(y) \psi(x + y),
$$
where $\psi(z) = e^{2\pi i z/p}$. Substitute $y = xt$ for $t \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times$; as $y$ ranges over $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$ for each fixed $x \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times$, so does $t$. Then $\chi(x)\chi(y) = \chi(x)\chi(xt) = \chi(x^2 t) = \chi(t)$ (using $\chi(x^2) = 1$), and $x + y = x(1 + t)$, so
$$
g(\chi)^2 = \sum_{t \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times} \chi(t) \sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times} \psi(x(1 + t)).
$$

For the inner sum: if $1 + t \equiv 0 \pmod p$ (i.e., $t = -1$), then $\psi(x(1 + t)) = \psi(0) = 1$ for all $x$, and the sum over $x \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times$ is $p - 1$. If $1 + t \not\equiv 0$, then $\psi(x(1 + t))$ as $x$ ranges over $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$ takes the same values (in some order) as $\psi(x)$ for $x \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times$, and $\sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p} \psi(x) = 0$ (sum of all $p$th roots of unity), so $\sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times} \psi(x) = -\psi(0) = -1$.

So
$$
g(\chi)^2 = \chi(-1)(p - 1) + \sum_{t \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times, t \neq -1} \chi(t) \cdot (-1) = \chi(-1)(p - 1) - \sum_{t \neq -1} \chi(t).
$$
Adding and subtracting $\chi(-1)$ on the right gives
$$
g(\chi)^2 = \chi(-1) p - \sum_{t \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times} \chi(t).
$$
The full character sum $\sum_{t \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times} \chi(t)$ vanishes because $\chi$ is a non-identity character: by orthogonality of characters of $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$, $\sum_{t} \chi(t) = 0$ unless $\chi$ is the identity character. So $g(\chi)^2 = \chi(-1) p$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Field axioms and characteristic [01.01.01].** A finite field is an instance of the field axioms, with the additional structural constraint that the underlying set is finite. The characteristic of a finite field is necessarily a prime $p$, and the field contains a unique copy of $\mathbb{F}_p$ as its prime field. Without the field axioms there is no setting for the squares-index-two statement or the Frobenius automorphism, since both rest on the field property (cancellation in field equations, bounded root count for polynomials, the squaring map as a group homomorphism).

- **Algebraic field extension and splitting field [01.02.12].** The field $\mathbb{F}_q$ for $q = p^n$ is defined as the splitting field of $X^q - X$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$, an instance of the splitting-field construction in algebraic field extension theory. The Galois correspondence for $\mathbb{F}_q/\mathbb{F}_p$ specialises the general Galois correspondence to a cyclic group, with the divisor lattice of $n$ controlling the subfield lattice. Every result about finite fields ultimately rests on the splitting-field construction, and the simplicity of finite-field Galois theory is what makes them the basic object in number theory and algebraic geometry over finite ground rings.

- **Quadratic reciprocity via Gauss sums [21.02.02].** The squares-index-two statement for $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$ in odd characteristic produces a quadratic character $\chi : \mathbb{F}_p^\times \to \{\pm 1\}$, and the Gauss sum $g(\chi) = \sum_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times} \chi(x) e^{2\pi i x/p}$ satisfies $g(\chi)^2 = \chi(-1) p$. This identity is the algebraic input to Gauss's analytic proof of quadratic reciprocity, packaged in unit 21.02.02. The finite-field unit supplies the character $\chi$ and the squares-index-two structural fact; the reciprocity unit takes those inputs and derives the symmetric law $\left(\frac{p}{q}\right) \left(\frac{q}{p}\right) = (-1)^{(p-1)(q-1)/4}$ for odd primes $p, q$.

- **Riemann zeta function [21.03.01].** The Euler product of the Riemann zeta function $\zeta(s) = \prod_p (1 - p^{-s})^{-1}$ rests on unique factorisation in $\mathbb{Z}$; the analogous Hasse-Weil zeta function of a smooth projective variety $X/\mathbb{F}_q$ is $Z(X, T) = \prod_x (1 - T^{\deg(x)})^{-1}$ over closed points $x$, where $\deg(x) = [\kappa(x) : \mathbb{F}_q]$ is the degree of the residue field of $x$ as an extension of $\mathbb{F}_q$. The function-field side of analytic number theory replaces $\mathbb{Z}$ by $\mathbb{F}_q[T]$ and reads off prime-power point counts of varieties via Frobenius eigenvalues on $\ell$-adic cohomology. The basic ingredient is the finite-field structure theorem of the present unit.

- **$\ell$-adic Galois representations [21.05.01].** The absolute Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}/\mathbb{F}_p) = \widehat{\mathbb{Z}}$ is topologically generated by Frobenius, and the Frobenius eigenvalues on $\ell$-adic cohomology of a smooth projective variety $X/\mathbb{F}_q$ are algebraic integers of absolute value $q^{w/2}$ on $H^w_{\text{ét}}(\overline{X}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ (Deligne's Weil II 1980). The basic Frobenius automorphism of the present unit lifts to the relative Frobenius on schemes over $\mathbb{F}_q$, and the analysis of its eigenvalues on cohomology is the content of the Weil conjectures and the modern motivic-zeta-function programme.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Galois announced the construction of finite fields beyond the prime case in his 1830 *Bulletin des Sciences Mathématiques de Férussac* note *Sur la théorie des nombres* [ref: TODO_REF Galois 1830], six pages reprinted in his collected works in 1846 by Liouville. Galois fixed an irreducible polynomial $f$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$ of degree $n$ and considered the elements $a_0 + a_1 i + a_2 i^2 + \cdots + a_{n-1} i^{n-1}$ in an indeterminate $i$ satisfying $f(i) = 0$, with $a_j \in \mathbb{F}_p$, working modulo $f(i)$. He observed that the resulting set is closed under the four field operations and has $p^n$ elements. The construction is the modern $\mathbb{F}_p[X]/(f)$ in its earliest published form, predating both Steinitz's general theory of fields and Dedekind's modern ideal-theoretic framework by half a century.

The construction left open whether the resulting field depended on the choice of $f$. E. H. Moore resolved this in his 1893 paper *A doubly-infinite system of simple groups* in the *Bulletin of the New York Mathematical Society* 3 [ref: TODO_REF Moore 1893]: any two finite fields of the same cardinality $p^n$ are $\mathbb{F}_p$-isomorphic. Moore's argument is the splitting-field description used in the modern proof. Dickson's 1901 *Linear Groups, with an Exposition of the Galois Field Theory* (Teubner) [ref: TODO_REF Dickson 1901] gave the first systematic textbook treatment, with the Frobenius automorphism and the linear groups $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{F}_q)$ as a working tool of finite group theory. Steinitz's 1910 *Journal für die reine und angewandte Mathematik* paper *Algebraische Theorie der Körper* [ref: TODO_REF Steinitz 1910] absorbed finite fields into a general field-theoretic framework distinguishing algebraic from transcendental extensions, separable from inseparable, and prepared the ground for Artin's 1927 modern packaging of Galois theory.

Wedderburn's 1905 *Transactions of the American Mathematical Society* paper *A theorem on finite algebras* [ref: TODO_REF Wedderburn 1905] proved that every finite division ring is commutative, showing the entire arithmetic of finite skew fields collapses to the commutative case classified by Galois and Moore. The original proof was a somewhat lengthy argument on the structure of the multiplicative group; Witt's 1931 *Abhandlungen aus dem Mathematischen Seminar der Universität Hamburg* note *Über die Kommutativität endlicher Schiefkörper* [ref: TODO_REF] reduced the proof to a three-line cyclotomic-polynomial argument on the class equation, which became the modern standard exposition. The squares-index-two structure of $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ in odd characteristic and the resulting Legendre character $\chi(x) = x^{(q-1)/2}$ originate with Euler's criterion (Euler 1755 *Novi Commentarii Acad. Sci. Petrop.* 8); Gauss's 1801 *Disquisitiones Arithmeticae* §§95-152 [ref: TODO_REF Gauss 1801] gave four proofs of quadratic reciprocity, with the §356 analytic proof through Gauss sums $g(\chi)$ becoming the prototype for higher reciprocity laws and the analytic side of class field theory.
