## Intuition [Beginner]

How many primes are there below a million? The answer is $78{,}498$, and the striking thing is that it is close to a simple prediction: a million divided by the natural logarithm of a million, which is about $72{,}400$. The primes thin out as you climb, but they thin out at a steady, predictable rate. Counting them exactly is hard; bounding how many there are turns out to be much easier, and you can do it with nothing more than careful bookkeeping about factorials.

The trick is to stop counting primes one at a time and instead add up a weight attached to each. Two running totals do the heavy lifting. The first adds the logarithm of every prime up to $x$. The second adds that same logarithm once for the prime, again for its square, again for its cube, and so on. These two totals, written $\vartheta(x)$ and $\psi(x)$, grow almost exactly like $x$ itself. Pin them between two multiples of $x$ and you have pinned the count of primes.

Chebyshev did this in $1852$, decades before anyone could prove the exact growth rate. From the same bookkeeping falls a clean fact you can state to a child: between any whole number and its double there is always a prime. And Mertens showed that if you add up one-over-prime for the primes below $x$, the total grows like the logarithm of the logarithm of $x$ — slow, but never stopping.

The staircase never wanders far from the diagonal line $y = x$. That single picture is the whole content of Chebyshev's bounds: $\psi(x)$ is squeezed between a fixed fraction of $x$ and a fixed multiple of $x$, so the primes can be neither far too dense nor far too sparse.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Check the squeeze directly for a small value, and watch Bertrand's postulate fall out.

Step 1. Compute $\psi(10)$ by listing the prime powers up to $10$. They are $2, 4, 8$ (powers of $2$), $3, 9$ (powers of $3$), $5$, and $7$. Each contributes the logarithm of its underlying prime: $2, 4, 8$ each add $\log 2$; $3, 9$ each add $\log 3$; $5$ adds $\log 5$; $7$ adds $\log 7$. So
$$
\psi(10) = 3\log 2 + 2\log 3 + \log 5 + \log 7 = \log\!\big(2^3 \cdot 3^2 \cdot 5 \cdot 7\big) = \log 2520 \approx 7.83.
$$

Step 2. Compare with $x = 10$. The ratio is $7.83 / 10 = 0.78$, comfortably between, say, $\tfrac12$ and $2$. The squeeze holds.

Step 3. Read off Bertrand for $n = 5$: is there a prime between $5$ and $10$? Yes — the number $7$. The bookkeeping behind the squeeze, sharpened, guarantees such a prime exists for every $n$, with no gaps allowed.

What this tells us: $\psi(x)$ is just the logarithm of the product of all prime powers up to $x$, and that product grows at a rate locked to $x$. Bounding a product of primes — something factorials make easy — bounds the primes themselves.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $p$ ranges over primes, $\log$ is the natural logarithm, and $\Lambda$ is the von Mangoldt function of [21.11.01]: $\Lambda(n) = \log p$ if $n = p^m$ for a prime $p$ and integer $m \geq 1$, and $\Lambda(n) = 0$ otherwise. We follow Apostol [ref: TODO_REF Apostol Ch. 4].

**Definition (Chebyshev functions).** For real $x \geq 1$, the **Chebyshev theta function** and **Chebyshev psi function** are
$$
\vartheta(x) = \sum_{p \leq x} \log p, \qquad \psi(x) = \sum_{n \leq x} \Lambda(n) = \sum_{p^m \leq x} \log p = \sum_{m \geq 1} \vartheta\!\big(x^{1/m}\big).
$$
The last equality holds because $\Lambda(p^m) = \log p$ contributes to $\psi(x)$ exactly when $p^m \leq x$, i.e. $p \leq x^{1/m}$; collecting the contribution at fixed exponent $m$ gives $\vartheta(x^{1/m})$, and the sum over $m$ is finite since $\vartheta(x^{1/m}) = 0$ once $x^{1/m} < 2$, that is once $m > \log_2 x$.

**Definition (prime-counting function).** As in [21.01.02], $\pi(x) = \#\{p \leq x\}$.

**Definition (asymptotic relations).** Write $f(x) \sim g(x)$ for $\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x)/g(x) = 1$, and $f(x) \asymp g(x)$ for the existence of constants $0 < c_1 \leq c_2$ with $c_1 g(x) \leq f(x) \leq c_2 g(x)$ for all large $x$. The **average order** of an arithmetic function $f$ refers to the asymptotics of the summatory function $\sum_{n \leq x} f(n)$.

The three functions are linked at the level of size. Since $\log p \leq \log x$ for $p \leq x$, summing gives $\vartheta(x) \leq \pi(x) \log x$. The reverse comparison, restricting to primes in $(x^{1/2}, x]$, shows $\vartheta(x) \geq \big(\pi(x) - \pi(x^{1/2})\big)\big(\tfrac12 \log x\big)$. The two Chebyshev functions differ by a lower-order amount: $0 \leq \psi(x) - \vartheta(x) = \sum_{m \geq 2}\vartheta(x^{1/m}) = O(\sqrt{x}\,\log x)$, because $\vartheta(t) \leq t \log t$ and only $m \leq \log_2 x$ terms are nonzero. Hence $\psi(x) \asymp x \iff \vartheta(x) \asymp x \iff \pi(x) \asymp x/\log x$, and likewise with $\asymp$ replaced by $\sim$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"$\psi$ and $\vartheta$ are the same function."* They differ by the prime-power tail $\sum_{m \geq 2}\vartheta(x^{1/m})$. At $x = 10$, $\vartheta(10) = \log 210 \approx 5.35$ while $\psi(10) = \log 2520 \approx 7.83$; the gap $\log 12 = \log(4 \cdot 9 \cdot \ldots)$ comes from the powers $4, 8, 9$. The gap is $O(\sqrt x \log x)$, negligible against the main term $x$ but not zero.
- *"$\pi(x) \sim x/\log x$ is an elementary theorem."* The asymptotic equivalence with constant $1$ is the prime number theorem, which the elementary Chebyshev method does not deliver — it gives only the two-sided bound $\pi(x) \asymp x/\log x$. The step from $\asymp$ to $\sim$ requires either complex analysis (Hadamard-de la Vallée Poussin) or the Selberg-Erdős elementary argument, both deferred to [21.12.01].
- *"$\sum_{p \leq x} 1/p$ converges because primes are sparse."* It diverges, by Mertens' second theorem, at rate $\log\log x$. Sparsity bounds the count but not the reciprocal sum; the harmonic weighting $1/p$ decays slowly enough that the divergence survives.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem is Chebyshev's two-sided bound, proved by the elementary method: extract $\psi$ from the prime factorisation of factorials, with no appeal to $\zeta(s)$ or complex analysis.

**Theorem (Chebyshev's bounds).** *There exist absolute constants $0 < c_1 < 1 < c_2$ such that for all sufficiently large $x$,*
$$
c_1\, x \;\leq\; \psi(x) \;\leq\; c_2\, x.
$$
*One may take $c_1 = \log 2 \approx 0.693$ and $c_2 = 2\log 2 \approx 1.386$. Equivalently $\vartheta(x) \asymp x$ and $\pi(x) \asymp x/\log x$.*

*Proof.* The engine is the identity, valid for every integer $N \geq 1$,
$$
\log N! = \sum_{n \leq N} \Lambda(n) \left\lfloor \frac{N}{n} \right\rfloor,
$$
which holds because $\log N! = \sum_{n \leq N}\log n = \sum_{n \leq N}\sum_{d \mid n}\Lambda(d)$ (using $\log = \Lambda * \mathbf{1}$ from [21.11.01]), and reindexing the divisor sum by $d$ counts, for each $d$, the multiples $d, 2d, \ldots \leq N$, of which there are $\lfloor N/d\rfloor$.

*Upper bound.* Consider the central binomial coefficient $\binom{2N}{N} = (2N)!/(N!)^2$. Its logarithm is
$$
\log \binom{2N}{N} = \log(2N)! - 2\log N! = \sum_{n \leq 2N}\Lambda(n)\left(\left\lfloor \frac{2N}{n}\right\rfloor - 2\left\lfloor \frac{N}{n}\right\rfloor\right).
$$
Each bracket $\lfloor 2y\rfloor - 2\lfloor y\rfloor$ equals $0$ or $1$, so every term is at most $\Lambda(n)$, and the bracket vanishes for $n > 2N$ (outside the summation range). Hence
$$
\log\binom{2N}{N} \;\leq\; \sum_{n \leq 2N}\Lambda(n) = \psi(2N).
$$
On the other hand $\binom{2N}{N}$ is the largest of the $2N+1$ terms summing to $(1+1)^{2N} = 4^N$, so $\binom{2N}{N} \geq 4^N/(2N+1)$, giving $\log\binom{2N}{N} \geq 2N\log 2 - \log(2N+1)$. Combining, $\psi(2N) \geq 2N\log 2 - \log(2N+1)$, which is the lower bound at even arguments; we return to it below. For the *upper* bound we instead use that the bracket $\lfloor 2N/n\rfloor - 2\lfloor N/n\rfloor$ is $1$ for $N < n \leq 2N$ (there $\lfloor 2N/n\rfloor = 1$, $\lfloor N/n\rfloor = 0$), so those terms alone contribute $\vartheta(2N) - \vartheta(N)$ plus prime-power corrections; summing the telescoping inequality $\vartheta(2N) - \vartheta(N) \leq \log\binom{2N}{N} \leq 2N\log 2$ over $N, 2N, 4N, \ldots$ from a dyadic decomposition yields $\vartheta(x) \leq (2\log 2)\,x$ for all $x \geq 1$. Since $\psi(x) = \vartheta(x) + O(\sqrt x \log x)$, also $\psi(x) \leq (2\log 2)x + O(\sqrt x\log x) \leq c_2 x$ for large $x$.

*Lower bound.* From $\psi(2N) \geq 2N\log 2 - \log(2N+1)$ established above, and $\psi$ nondecreasing, every $x$ satisfies $\psi(x) \geq \psi(2\lfloor x/2\rfloor) \geq 2\lfloor x/2\rfloor\log 2 - \log(2\lfloor x/2\rfloor + 1) \geq (\log 2)\,x - O(\log x) \geq c_1 x$ for large $x$. This gives the lower bound. Together, $c_1 x \leq \psi(x) \leq c_2 x$. The transfer to $\vartheta$ and to $\pi(x) \asymp x/\log x$ uses the size comparisons of the Formal definition. $\square$

**Bridge.** Chebyshev's bounds build toward [21.12.01] the prime number theorem, where the same factorial identity $\log N! = \sum_n \Lambda(n)\lfloor N/n\rfloor$ reappears as the elementary input to the Selberg symmetry formula, and this is exactly the elementary shadow of the analytic statement $\psi(x) \sim x$ that the contour integral of $-\zeta'(s)/\zeta(s)$ delivers; the bounds appear again in [21.11.04] Perron's formula as the convergence estimates that make the truncated contour integral controllable. The central insight is that $\psi$ is recoverable from factorials because $\log = \Lambda * \mathbf{1}$ in the convolution ring of [21.11.01], so divisor-sum identities convert directly into prime-counting estimates. Putting these together, the bound $\psi(x) \asymp x$ generalises into the precise $\psi(x) \sim x$ once one knows $\zeta(1+it) \neq 0$, the bridge being the Mertens estimate $\sum_{n \leq x}\Lambda(n)/n = \log x + O(1)$ proved next, which is dual to the average order $\sum_{n \leq x}\log n = x\log x - x + O(\log x)$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that $\psi(x) - \vartheta(x) = O(\sqrt{x}\,\log x)$, and conclude $\psi(x) \sim x \iff \vartheta(x) \sim x$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $\psi(x) - \vartheta(x) = \sum_{m \geq 2}\vartheta(x^{1/m})$. Use $\vartheta(t) \leq t\log t$ and count how many $m$ give a nonzero term.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From $\psi(x) = \sum_{m \geq 1}\vartheta(x^{1/m})$, the difference is $\psi(x) - \vartheta(x) = \sum_{m \geq 2}\vartheta(x^{1/m})$. The summand vanishes once $x^{1/m} < 2$, i.e. $m > \log_2 x$, so at most $\log_2 x$ terms survive. Each is bounded by $\vartheta(x^{1/m}) \leq \vartheta(\sqrt x) \leq \sqrt x \log \sqrt x \leq \tfrac12 \sqrt x \log x$ for $m \geq 2$. Hence
$$
0 \leq \psi(x) - \vartheta(x) \leq (\log_2 x)\cdot \tfrac12\sqrt x \log x = O(\sqrt x \,(\log x)^2),
$$
and a sharper count (only $m = 2$ reaches order $\sqrt x$; the rest are $O(x^{1/3}\log x)$) gives $O(\sqrt x \log x)$. Dividing by $x$, the difference is $o(x)$, so $\psi(x)/x$ and $\vartheta(x)/x$ have the same limit; the equivalence of $\psi(x)\sim x$ and $\vartheta(x)\sim x$ follows. Rubric: full credit for the tail estimate and the $o(x)$ conclusion.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the lower Chebyshev bound $\psi(2N) \geq (\log 2)\cdot 2N - \log(2N+1)$ from the central binomial coefficient.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The $2N+1$ binomial coefficients $\binom{2N}{k}$ sum to $4^N$ and $\binom{2N}{N}$ is the largest, so $\binom{2N}{N} \geq 4^N/(2N+1)$, giving $\log\binom{2N}{N} \geq 2N\log 2 - \log(2N+1)$. For the upper comparison, $\log\binom{2N}{N} = \sum_{n \leq 2N}\Lambda(n)(\lfloor 2N/n\rfloor - 2\lfloor N/n\rfloor)$ with each bracket equal to $0$ or $1$; hence $\log\binom{2N}{N} \leq \sum_{n \leq 2N}\Lambda(n) = \psi(2N)$. Chaining, $\psi(2N) \geq \log\binom{2N}{N} \geq 2N\log 2 - \log(2N+1)$. Rubric: full credit for the two inequalities and the chain; the bracket-is-$0$-or-$1$ fact is $\lfloor 2y\rfloor - 2\lfloor y\rfloor \in \{0,1\}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove Mertens' first theorem $\sum_{n \leq x}\frac{\Lambda(n)}{n} = \log x + O(1)$, and deduce $\sum_{p \leq x}\frac{\log p}{p} = \log x + O(1)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Start from $\log\lfloor x\rfloor! = \sum_{n \leq x}\Lambda(n)\lfloor x/n\rfloor$ and the elementary estimate $\log\lfloor x\rfloor! = x\log x - x + O(\log x)$. Replace $\lfloor x/n\rfloor$ by $x/n + O(1)$ and use $\psi(x) = O(x)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the factorial identity, $\sum_{n \leq x}\Lambda(n)\lfloor x/n\rfloor = \log\lfloor x\rfloor! = x\log x - x + O(\log x)$ (Stirling, elementary form). Write $\lfloor x/n\rfloor = x/n - \{x/n\}$ with $0 \leq \{x/n\} < 1$. Then
$$
x\sum_{n \leq x}\frac{\Lambda(n)}{n} - \sum_{n \leq x}\Lambda(n)\{x/n\} = x\log x - x + O(\log x).
$$
The error sum is bounded by $\sum_{n \leq x}\Lambda(n) = \psi(x) = O(x)$ (Chebyshev). Dividing through by $x$:
$$
\sum_{n \leq x}\frac{\Lambda(n)}{n} = \log x - 1 + O(1) + O(\tfrac{\log x}{x}) = \log x + O(1).
$$
To pass to primes, the prime-power tail $\sum_{n \leq x}\Lambda(n)/n - \sum_{p \leq x}\log p/p = \sum_{p^m \leq x, m \geq 2}\log p / p^m$ converges (dominated by $\sum_p \log p \sum_{m \geq 2}p^{-m} = \sum_p \log p/(p(p-1)) < \infty$), so it is $O(1)$. Hence $\sum_{p \leq x}\log p/p = \log x + O(1)$. Rubric: full credit for the floor-removal, the $\psi(x) = O(x)$ input, and the convergent prime-power tail.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Assuming Mertens' first theorem and Abel summation, derive the form of Mertens' second theorem $\sum_{p \leq x}1/p = \log\log x + M + o(1)$ (you need not evaluate $M$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply partial summation with $a_p = (\log p)/p$ and weight $1/\log p$, so $\sum_{p \leq x}1/p = \sum_{p \leq x} a_p \cdot \frac{1}{\log p}$. Use $A(t) = \sum_{p \leq t}(\log p)/p = \log t + R(t)$ with $R(t) = O(1)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Set $A(t) = \sum_{p \leq t}(\log p)/p = \log t + R(t)$, $R(t) = O(1)$ by Mertens I. Abel summation against the decreasing weight $w(t) = 1/\log t$ gives
$$
\sum_{p \leq x}\frac1p = \sum_{p \leq x}\frac{\log p}{p}\cdot\frac{1}{\log p} = \frac{A(x)}{\log x} + \int_2^x \frac{A(t)}{t(\log t)^2}\,dt.
$$
Substituting $A(t) = \log t + R(t)$: the boundary term is $1 + O(1/\log x)$, and the integral splits as $\int_2^x \frac{dt}{t\log t} + \int_2^x\frac{R(t)}{t(\log t)^2}dt = \log\log x - \log\log 2 + C + o(1)$, since the first integral is $\log\log x + O(1)$ and the second converges (its integrand is $O(1/(t(\log t)^2))$, integrable to $\infty$). Collecting the constants into $M$ gives $\sum_{p \leq x}1/p = \log\log x + M + o(1)$. Rubric: full credit for the correct partial-summation setup and identifying the $\int dt/(t\log t) = \log\log x$ main term and the convergent remainder.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove Bertrand's postulate by Erdős's method: for every integer $n \geq 1$ there is a prime $p$ with $n < p \leq 2n$. (You may assume the primorial bound $\prod_{p \leq m}p < 4^m$ and verify small $n$ by exhibiting primes $2,3,5,7,13,23,43,83,163,317,631$.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Suppose no prime lies in $(n, 2n]$. Bound the prime factorisation of $\binom{2n}{n}$: every prime factor is $\leq 2n/3$ (no prime in $(n,2n]$, and primes in $(2n/3, n]$ do not divide $\binom{2n}{n}$); prime powers $p^r \| \binom{2n}{n}$ satisfy $p^r \leq 2n$; only primes $\leq \sqrt{2n}$ can appear with exponent $\geq 2$. Contradict $\binom{2n}{n} \geq 4^n/(2n+1)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $\binom{2n}{n} = \prod_p p^{r_p}$. By Kummer/Legendre, $p^{r_p} \leq 2n$ for every $p$ (the exponent $r_p = \sum_{j \geq 1}(\lfloor 2n/p^j\rfloor - 2\lfloor n/p^j\rfloor)$ is a sum of $0/1$ brackets, nonzero only for $p^j \leq 2n$). Three facts cut the product down. First, primes $p > \sqrt{2n}$ have $r_p \leq 1$. Second, primes in $(2n/3, n]$ satisfy $r_p = 0$: for such $p$, $2p \leq 2n < 3p$, so in the factorials only $\lfloor 2n/p\rfloor = 2$, $\lfloor n/p\rfloor = 1$, bracket $0$, and higher powers exceed $2n$. Third, by hypothesis no prime lies in $(n, 2n]$. Hence every prime factor is $\leq 2n/3$, and
$$
\binom{2n}{n} \;\leq\; \underbrace{\prod_{p \leq \sqrt{2n}} 2n}_{\text{powers}} \cdot \underbrace{\prod_{\sqrt{2n} < p \leq 2n/3} p}_{\text{exponent }1} \;\leq\; (2n)^{\sqrt{2n}} \cdot \prod_{p \leq 2n/3} p \;<\; (2n)^{\sqrt{2n}}\, 4^{2n/3},
$$
using the primorial bound. Against the lower bound $\binom{2n}{n} \geq 4^n/(2n+1)$:
$$
\frac{4^n}{2n+1} < (2n)^{\sqrt{2n}}\,4^{2n/3} \;\Longrightarrow\; 4^{n/3} < (2n+1)(2n)^{\sqrt{2n}} \leq (2n)^{\sqrt{2n}+1}.
$$
Taking logarithms, $(n/3)\log 4 < (\sqrt{2n}+1)\log(2n)$, which fails for all $n \geq 468$ (the left side is linear in $n$, the right side $O(\sqrt n \log n)$). So for $n \geq 468$ a prime must lie in $(n, 2n]$. For $n < 468$, the explicit chain $2,3,5,7,13,23,43,83,163,317,631$ — each less than twice its predecessor — covers every interval $(n, 2n]$. $\square$ Rubric: full credit for the three exponent facts, the product bound, and the asymptotic contradiction; the small-$n$ chain may be cited.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the product form of Mertens' third theorem, $\prod_{p \leq x}(1 - 1/p)^{-1} \sim e^{\gamma}\log x$, *assuming* the second theorem $\sum_{p \leq x}1/p = \log\log x + M + o(1)$ with $M$ the Meissel-Mertens constant, and the identity $M = \gamma - \sum_p\big(\log(1 - 1/p)^{-1} - 1/p\big)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take logarithms of the product and expand $\log(1 - 1/p)^{-1} = 1/p + \big(\log(1-1/p)^{-1} - 1/p\big)$. The correction sum converges; combine with Mertens II.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $P(x) = \prod_{p \leq x}(1 - 1/p)^{-1}$. Then
$$
\log P(x) = \sum_{p \leq x}\log(1 - 1/p)^{-1} = \sum_{p \leq x}\frac1p + \sum_{p \leq x}\Big(\log(1-1/p)^{-1} - \frac1p\Big).
$$
The correction terms are $\log(1-1/p)^{-1} - 1/p = \sum_{k \geq 2}1/(kp^k) = O(1/p^2)$, so the second sum converges to a constant $C = \sum_p(\log(1-1/p)^{-1} - 1/p)$, with tail $o(1)$. By Mertens II the first sum is $\log\log x + M + o(1)$. Hence
$$
\log P(x) = \log\log x + M + C + o(1).
$$
The stated identity $M = \gamma - C$ gives $M + C = \gamma$, so $\log P(x) = \log\log x + \gamma + o(1)$. Exponentiating, $P(x) = e^{\gamma}\log x \cdot e^{o(1)} \sim e^{\gamma}\log x$. Rubric: full credit for the convergent-correction split, the use of Mertens II for the main term, and the identification $M + C = \gamma$. Note the $e^{\gamma}$ (not $e^{-\gamma}$) appears because the product is the reciprocal $(1-1/p)^{-1}$; the form $\prod_{p \leq x}(1 - 1/p) \sim e^{-\gamma}/\log x$ is the same statement inverted.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (the equivalence of the three asymptotic forms).** The relations $\psi(x) \sim x$, $\vartheta(x) \sim x$, and $\pi(x) \sim x/\log x$ are equivalent [ref: TODO_REF Apostol Ch. 4]. The first two are equivalent because $\psi - \vartheta = O(\sqrt x \log x) = o(x)$. The equivalence with $\pi(x) \sim x/\log x$ follows from Abel summation in both directions: $\vartheta(x) = \sum_{p \leq x}\log p = \pi(x)\log x - \int_2^x \pi(t)/t\,dt$, so $\vartheta(x)/x \to 1$ forces $\pi(x)\log x / x \to 1$, and conversely $\pi(x) = \vartheta(x)/\log x + \int_2^x \vartheta(t)/(t(\log t)^2)\,dt$ recovers $\pi(x) \sim x/\log x$ from $\vartheta(x) \sim x$. This equivalence reduces the prime number theorem to the single analytic statement $\psi(x) \sim x$, which is the form the contour method of [21.12.01] proves.

**Theorem 2 (Chebyshev's explicit constants).** Chebyshev 1852 [ref: TODO_REF Chebyshev 1852] obtained the sharper bounds $A\,x/\log x \leq \pi(x) \leq \tfrac65 A\,x/\log x$ for large $x$, with $A = \log(2^{1/2}3^{1/3}5^{1/5}30^{-1/30}) \approx 0.92129$, by combining the factorial identity at the four arguments $x, x/2, x/3, x/5, x/30$ into the linear form $\psi(x) - \psi(x/2) - \psi(x/3) - \psi(x/5) + \psi(x/30)$, whose coefficients are chosen so the associated combination of $\log\lfloor\cdot\rfloor!$ telescopes. These constants straddle $1$ — Chebyshev's method cannot bring them together — which is exactly why $\pi(x) \sim x/\log x$ (constant exactly $1$) lies beyond the elementary range and required the analytic methods of [21.12.01].

**Theorem 3 (Bertrand's postulate; Chebyshev 1852, Erdős 1932).** For every integer $n \geq 1$ there is a prime in $(n, 2n]$ [ref: TODO_REF Chebyshev 1852] [ref: TODO_REF Erdős 1932]. Chebyshev deduced it from his lower bound on $\pi(2n) - \pi(n)$; Erdős's elementary proof (Exercise 7) reads the postulate off the prime factorisation of $\binom{2n}{n}$, using $\binom{2n}{n} \geq 4^n/(2n+1)$, the vanishing of $r_p$ for $p \in (2n/3, n]$, the prime-power cap $p^{r_p} \leq 2n$, and the primorial bound $\prod_{p \leq m}p < 4^m$. The same machinery yields the refinement that for any $\varepsilon > 0$ there is a prime in $(n, (1+\varepsilon)n]$ for all large $n$, since the Chebyshev band has multiplicative width below $2$.

**Theorem 4 (Mertens' three theorems; Mertens 1874).** With $\gamma$ the Euler-Mascheroni constant and $M = 0.2614972128\ldots$ the Meissel-Mertens constant [ref: TODO_REF Mertens 1874],
$$
\sum_{p \leq x}\frac{\log p}{p} = \log x + O(1), \quad \sum_{p \leq x}\frac1p = \log\log x + M + O\!\Big(\frac{1}{\log x}\Big), \quad \prod_{p \leq x}\Big(1 - \frac1p\Big)^{-1} \sim e^{\gamma}\log x.
$$
The first is proved (Exercise 5) from $\log\lfloor x\rfloor! = x\log x - x + O(\log x)$ and $\psi(x) = O(x)$; the second from the first by Abel summation (Exercise 6); the third from the second by expanding the logarithm of the product (Exercise 8). All three use only Chebyshev-grade input — no PNT, no zeta zeros. The product form is the prime-product backbone of sieve theory: the heuristic "probability that $n \leq x$ survives sieving by all $p \leq z$" is $\prod_{p \leq z}(1 - 1/p) \sim e^{-\gamma}/\log z$, and the factor $e^{-\gamma}$ is the source of the celebrated parity-barrier constant in the linear sieve.

**Theorem 5 (Shapiro's Tauberian theorem).** Apostol's Theorem 4.8 [ref: TODO_REF Apostol Ch. 4]: if $\{a_n\}$ are nonnegative and $\sum_{n \leq x}a_n\lfloor x/n\rfloor = x\log x + O(x)$, then $\sum_{n \leq x}a_n/n = \log x + O(1)$, and moreover $\sum_{n \leq x}a_n \asymp x$. Applied to $a_n = \Lambda(n)$ it produces Mertens I and the Chebyshev order $\psi(x) \asymp x$ in one stroke from the factorial identity, abstracting the elementary core of this unit. Shapiro's theorem is the precise statement of how an average-order estimate against $\lfloor x/n\rfloor$ upgrades to a $1/n$-weighted estimate, the recurring move from [21.11.01] average orders to prime counting.

**Synthesis.** The elementary prime-counting estimates are the foundational reason the prime number theorem is *plausible* before it is *provable*: Chebyshev's $\psi(x) \asymp x$ already locates $\pi(x)$ within a constant factor of $x/\log x$, and this is exactly the bridge from the convolution identity $\log = \Lambda * \mathbf{1}$ of [21.11.01] to the analytic $\psi(x) \sim x$ of [21.12.01]. The central insight is that all of this content is squeezed out of one identity, $\log N! = \sum_n \Lambda(n)\lfloor N/n\rfloor$, by elementary manipulation of factorials and binomial coefficients; the factorial encodes the multiplicative structure of the integers, and differencing it (as in $\binom{2N}{N}$) isolates the primes. Putting these together, Mertens' theorems are dual to the average-order estimates of [21.11.01]: where $\sum_{n \leq x}d(n) \sim x\log x$ measures divisors, $\sum_{p \leq x}(\log p)/p \sim \log x$ measures primes, and both descend from partial summation against a Dirichlet-convolution identity. The pattern generalises: Shapiro's Tauberian theorem is the abstract engine, the Selberg symmetry formula $\sum_{n \leq x}\Lambda(n)\log n + \sum_{mn \leq x}\Lambda(m)\Lambda(n) = 2x\log x + O(x)$ is its second-order refinement, and the elementary proof of the full PNT in [21.12.01] is what one obtains by pushing this differencing one level deeper. The bridge is everywhere the factorial identity; Chebyshev saw it, Mertens summed against it, Erdős differenced it, and the analytic theory only sharpens the constant from $\asymp$ to $\sim$.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (factorial identity).** *For every integer $N \geq 1$, $\log N! = \sum_{n \leq N}\Lambda(n)\lfloor N/n\rfloor$.*

*Proof.* From $\log = \Lambda * \mathbf{1}$ in the convolution ring of [21.11.01], for each $m$ we have $\log m = \sum_{d \mid m}\Lambda(d)$. Summing over $m \leq N$,
$$
\log N! = \sum_{m \leq N}\log m = \sum_{m \leq N}\sum_{d \mid m}\Lambda(d) = \sum_{d \leq N}\Lambda(d)\,\#\{m \leq N : d \mid m\} = \sum_{d \leq N}\Lambda(d)\left\lfloor \frac{N}{d}\right\rfloor,
$$
where the third equality swaps the order of summation, grouping by the divisor $d$, and the count of multiples of $d$ up to $N$ is $\lfloor N/d\rfloor$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Abel/partial summation).** *Let $a_n$ be complex, $A(t) = \sum_{n \leq t}a_n$, and $g$ continuously differentiable on $[1, x]$. Then*
$$
\sum_{n \leq x}a_n g(n) = A(x)g(x) - \int_1^x A(t)g'(t)\,dt.
$$

*Proof.* Write $a_n = A(n) - A(n-1)$ and sum by parts over $1 \leq n \leq \lfloor x\rfloor$:
$$
\sum_{n \leq x}a_n g(n) = \sum_{n}(A(n) - A(n-1))g(n) = A(\lfloor x\rfloor)g(\lfloor x\rfloor) - \sum_{n \leq \lfloor x\rfloor - 1}A(n)(g(n+1) - g(n)).
$$
Since $A$ is constant on $[n, n+1)$, $A(n)(g(n+1) - g(n)) = \int_n^{n+1}A(t)g'(t)\,dt$, and $A(\lfloor x\rfloor)g(\lfloor x\rfloor) = A(x)g(x) - \int_{\lfloor x\rfloor}^x A(t)g'(t)\,dt$ because $A(t) = A(x)$ there. Assembling the integrals over $[1, x]$ yields the stated formula. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (the prime-power tail is bounded).** *$\sum_{p}\sum_{m \geq 2}\frac{\log p}{p^m}$ converges; hence $\psi(x) - \vartheta(x) = \sum_{p^m \leq x, m \geq 2}\log p$ has the bound $O(\sqrt x \log x)$ and the reciprocal tail $\sum_{p, m \geq 2}\log p/p^m = O(1)$.*

*Proof.* For fixed $p$, $\sum_{m \geq 2}p^{-m} = p^{-2}/(1 - p^{-1}) = 1/(p(p-1))$, so $\sum_p \log p \sum_{m \geq 2}p^{-m} = \sum_p \frac{\log p}{p(p-1)}$, which converges by comparison with $\sum_n (\log n)/n^2 < \infty$. For the unweighted difference, $\psi(x) - \vartheta(x) = \sum_{m \geq 2}\vartheta(x^{1/m})$; the term $m = 2$ contributes $\vartheta(\sqrt x) \leq \sqrt x\log\sqrt x = O(\sqrt x \log x)$, and the remaining $m \geq 3$ terms total $O(x^{1/3}\log x)$, with at most $\log_2 x$ nonzero terms. Hence the difference is $O(\sqrt x\log x)$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (lower bound on $\pi(x) - \pi(x/2)$ refines Bertrand).** *For all large $x$, $\pi(x) - \pi(x/2) \geq c\,x/\log x$ for an absolute constant $c > 0$; in particular $(x/2, x]$ contains $\gg x/\log x$ primes, a quantitative Bertrand.*

*Proof.* From $\vartheta(x) - \vartheta(x/2) = \sum_{x/2 < p \leq x}\log p \leq (\pi(x) - \pi(x/2))\log x$, it suffices to bound $\vartheta(x) - \vartheta(x/2)$ below by $\gg x$. The central binomial gives $\log\binom{2N}{N} \geq 2N\log 2 - \log(2N+1)$, and the only primes contributing with exponent forcing a lower bound lie in $(N, 2N]$ together with prime-power corrections of size $O(\sqrt N\log N)$; isolating them, $\vartheta(2N) - \vartheta(N) \geq \log\binom{2N}{N} - O(\sqrt N (\log N)^2) \geq 2N\log 2 - O(\sqrt N(\log N)^2)$. Thus $\vartheta(x) - \vartheta(x/2) \geq (\log 2)\tfrac{x}{2} \cdot (1 + o(1)) \gg x$, whence $\pi(x) - \pi(x/2) \geq c\,x/\log x$. The strict positivity for every $n$ (not merely large $n$) is Bertrand's postulate, with small cases checked directly. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The factorial identity and the von Mangoldt function used throughout are the convolution identity $\log = \Lambda * \mathbf{1}$ of [21.11.01]; the entire Chebyshev method is partial summation against that single Dirichlet-convolution relation, and the average-order machinery (hyperbola method, $\sum_{n \leq x}d(n) \sim x\log x$) developed there is the divisor-counting analogue of the prime-counting estimates here.

- These elementary bounds are the prelude to [21.12.01] the prime number theorem: Chebyshev's $\psi(x) \asymp x$ is sharpened to $\psi(x) \sim x$ by the contour integral of $-\zeta'(s)/\zeta(s)$, and the factorial identity reappears in the Selberg symmetry formula underlying the elementary (complex-analysis-free) PNT proof.

- The convergence estimate $\psi(x) = O(x)$ supplies the absolute-convergence and truncation control needed in [21.11.04] Perron's formula and Mellin inversion, where the summatory function of a Dirichlet series is recovered by a vertical contour integral whose error term is governed by Chebyshev-grade bounds on the coefficients.

- The primes $p \leq x$ counted here index the Euler product of [21.03.01] the Riemann zeta function; Mertens' third theorem $\prod_{p \leq x}(1 - 1/p)^{-1} \sim e^{\gamma}\log x$ is the partial Euler product at $s = 1$, and the divergence rate $\log\log x$ of $\sum 1/p$ is the elementary trace of the pole of $\zeta$ at $s = 1$.

- The infinitude and fundamental-theorem apparatus of [21.01.02] is the substrate: the factorisation of $\binom{2n}{n}$ that powers Bertrand, and the unique-factorisation count behind $\log N! = \sum_n\Lambda(n)\lfloor N/n\rfloor$, both rest on the prime factorisation theory established there.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Pafnuty Chebyshev's 1852 memoir *Sur les nombres premiers* [ref: TODO_REF Chebyshev 1852] gave the first rigorous control on the density of primes, three decades after Legendre and Gauss had conjectured $\pi(x) \sim x/\log x$ from numerical tables. Chebyshev introduced the functions now bearing his name and proved $0.921\,x/\log x \leq \pi(x) \leq 1.106\,x/\log x$ for large $x$, together with the first proof of the conjecture that Joseph Bertrand had stated in 1845 and verified by hand to three million. Chebyshev's method was entirely elementary, resting on the arithmetic of factorials, and it established the *order* of $\pi(x)$ while leaving the exact constant out of reach.

Franz Mertens's 1874 paper in Crelle's journal [ref: TODO_REF Mertens 1874] sharpened the elementary theory into the three asymptotic identities for $\sum (\log p)/p$, $\sum 1/p$, and $\prod(1 - 1/p)^{-1}$, isolating the constants $M$ and $e^{\gamma}$. Mertens's second theorem made precise Euler's 1737 observation that $\sum 1/p$ diverges, pinning the rate at $\log\log x$. The young Paul Erdős, in his 1932 debut paper [ref: TODO_REF Erdős 1932], recast Bertrand's postulate as a transparent estimate on the prime factorisation of the central binomial coefficient, a proof so clean it became the canonical one. The gap between the Chebyshev band and the true constant $1$ stood until 1896, when Hadamard and de la Vallée Poussin proved $\pi(x) \sim x/\log x$ using the complex analysis of $\zeta(s)$; the elementary route to the same limit was found by Selberg and Erdős in 1948-49.
