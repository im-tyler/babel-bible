## Intuition [Beginner]

Pick a length $N$ and write down every whole number from $1$ to $N$. Now choose $s$ of them (repeats allowed) for a left team and another $s$ for a right team. Ask the two teams to tie on a whole list of scores at once: the sum of the numbers must match, *and* the sum of their squares must match, *and* the sum of their cubes, all the way up to the sum of their $k$-th powers. How many ways can the two teams be picked so that every one of these $k$ tallies comes out equal? That count is the **Vinogradov mean value** $J_{s,k}(N)$, and the whole subject is the size of this number.

There is an easy way to force a tie: make the right team a rearrangement of the left team. Then every tally matches automatically, because you are adding the same numbers in a different order. These "diagonal" ties are cheap and plentiful, and they set a floor on $J_{s,k}(N)$. The deep question is whether there are many *more* ties than the diagonal ones — whether the simultaneous equations have lots of genuinely different solutions, or whether the diagonal solutions are essentially all of them.

Why care about a counting puzzle? Because this single count controls how much a high-degree wave-sum can fail to cancel. The wave-sums built from $e(\alpha_1 n + \cdots + \alpha_k n^k)$ that resisted van der Corput's method for large $k$ are governed exactly by this team-tying count, and knowing the count is small unlocks the sharpest bounds on those sums and on where the Riemann zeta function can grow.

## Visual [Beginner]

Think of each number you pick as a point, and the matching conditions as a stack of balance scales — one scale for the plain sums, one for the squares, one for the cubes, and so on up to the $k$-th powers. A choice of two teams is a solution only when every scale balances at once.

```
   the system to satisfy (k balance conditions)

   sum of values     [ left ]======[ right ]   balanced?
   sum of squares     [ left ]======[ right ]   balanced?
   sum of cubes       [ left ]======[ right ]   balanced?
        ...                  ...        ...
   sum of k-th powers [ left ]======[ right ]   balanced?

   diagonal tie: right = a shuffle of left  -->  ALL scales balance for free
   extra ties:  genuinely different teams that still balance every scale
```

![A vertical stack of balance scales, one per power from the first up to the k-th. Each scale weighs the left team's total against the right team's total for that power. A solution of the Vinogradov system is a pair of teams for which every scale in the stack balances simultaneously. A side note shows that any rearrangement of the left team into the right team balances all scales automatically — the diagonal solutions.](/img/vinogradov-mean-value-theorem-placeholder.svg)

The picture makes the dichotomy visible: the diagonal ties cost nothing and there are about $N^s$ of them; the theorem asks whether the genuinely different ties are also held down near that floor (plus a second floor coming from the larger pool of numbers when $s$ is small). The main conjecture says yes — there are essentially no surprises beyond the two obvious floors.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We count solutions of the simplest interesting Vinogradov system by hand: $k = 2$ (match sums and sums of squares), $s = 2$ (two on each team), drawn from $\{1, 2, 3\}$, so $N = 3$. We want pairs $(x_1, x_2)$ and $(y_1, y_2)$ with $x_1 + x_2 = y_1 + y_2$ and $x_1^2 + x_2^2 = y_1^2 + y_2^2$.

Step 1. Fix the left team and compute its two tallies. For $(x_1, x_2)$ the pair of tallies is (sum, sum of squares). For example $(1,2)$ gives $(3, 5)$, and $(1,3)$ gives $(4, 10)$.

Step 2. List every left team and its tally pair. The ordered pairs from $\{1,2,3\}$ and their (sum, sum-of-squares): $(1,1)\to(2,2)$; $(1,2)\to(3,5)$; $(1,3)\to(4,10)$; $(2,1)\to(3,5)$; $(2,2)\to(4,8)$; $(2,3)\to(5,13)$; $(3,1)\to(4,10)$; $(3,2)\to(5,13)$; $(3,3)\to(6,18)$.

Step 3. Group teams by their tally pair, since a solution pairs any two teams with the *same* pair. The groups are: $(2,2)$: one team; $(3,5)$: two teams $\{(1,2),(2,1)\}$; $(4,10)$: two teams $\{(1,3),(3,1)\}$; $(4,8)$: one team; $(5,13)$: two teams; $(6,18)$: one team.

Step 4. Count ordered solution pairs. A group of size $m$ contributes $m^2$ solutions (pick any left team, any right team from the group). So the total is $1^2 + 2^2 + 2^2 + 1^2 + 2^2 + 1^2 = 1 + 4 + 4 + 1 + 4 + 1 = 15$.

What this tells us: $J_{2,2}(3) = 15$. The diagonal solutions (right team a shuffle of left) number $s! \cdot$ choices in the generic case, and here they account for the bulk; the only "extra" structure is that $(1,2)$ and $(2,1)$ share a tally pair, which is just the shuffle again. For $k = 2$ this matching is rigid: fixing the sum and the sum of squares essentially fixes the unordered pair. That rigidity, made quantitative, is the content of the theorem.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout write $e(t) = e^{2\pi i t}$; the additive character $e(\cdot)$ is the one used on $\mathbb{R}/\mathbb{Z}$ in [21.15.02] and [21.15.03], and integrals $\int = \int_{[0,1)^k}$ are over the torus $\mathbb{T}^k$ with $\boldsymbol\alpha = (\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_k)$.

**Definition (the Vinogradov system and its count).** Fix integers $k \ge 2$, $s \ge 1$, $N \ge 1$. The **Vinogradov mean value** $J_{s,k}(N)$ is the number of integer solutions of the simultaneous system
$$
x_1^j + \cdots + x_s^j = y_1^j + \cdots + y_s^j \qquad (1 \le j \le k), \qquad 1 \le x_i, y_i \le N.
$$
Equivalently, setting $f_k(\boldsymbol\alpha; N) = \sum_{n=1}^{N} e(\alpha_1 n + \alpha_2 n^2 + \cdots + \alpha_k n^k)$,
$$
J_{s,k}(N) = \int_{\mathbb{T}^k} \big| f_k(\boldsymbol\alpha; N) \big|^{2s}\, d\boldsymbol\alpha,
$$
since expanding $|f_k|^{2s}$ and integrating term by term, the orthogonality $\int_0^1 e(m\alpha_j)\,d\alpha_j = \mathbf{1}_{m = 0}$ in each variable picks out exactly the tuples satisfying all $k$ power-sum equations.

**Definition (the two floors).** Two families of solutions are forced. The **diagonal solutions** take $(y_1, \ldots, y_s)$ to be a permutation of $(x_1, \ldots, x_s)$; there are $\gg N^s$ of these (choose the $x_i$ freely; generic tuples have $s!$ permutations). Independently, counting freely on the remaining degrees of freedom after the $k$ constraints forces $\gg N^{2s - k(k+1)/2}$ solutions when $2s \ge k(k+1)/2$, since the system cuts out a variety of codimension $k(k+1)/2$ inside the $2s$-dimensional box. Hence the unconditional lower bound
$$
J_{s,k}(N) \gg N^{s} + N^{2s - \frac{k(k+1)}{2}}.
$$

**Definition (the main conjecture).** The **main conjecture** for Vinogradov's mean value theorem asserts that this lower bound is sharp up to $\varepsilon$-powers: for every $\varepsilon > 0$,
$$
J_{s,k}(N) \ll_{\varepsilon} N^{s + \varepsilon} + N^{2s - \frac{k(k+1)}{2} + \varepsilon},
$$
uniformly in $N$. The **critical exponent** is $s = s_k := k(k+1)/2$, where the two terms coincide at $N^{s + \varepsilon}$; the difficulty of the conjecture is concentrated at $s = s_k$, from which all other $s$ follow by Hölder's inequality and direct summation of the surplus variables.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"The main conjecture is a single inequality at one value of $s$."* It is a family indexed by $s$, but the substance lives at the critical $s_k = k(k+1)/2$. For $s \le s_k$ the first term $N^s$ dominates and the statement is $J_{s,k} \ll N^{s+\varepsilon}$; for $s \ge s_k$ the second term dominates. Proving the critical case implies all others, so "the main conjecture" usually means the critical bound.

- *"Vinogradov's method always beats van der Corput."* It beats van der Corput's exponent-pair bounds for *high* degree $k$, where the iterated $A,B$-processes lose too much. For small $k$ (notably $k \le 3$ for the zeta function on the critical line) the van der Corput and exponent-pair bounds of [21.15.03] remain competitive or superior; Vinogradov's strength is the uniformity of the saving as $k \to \infty$.

- *"$J_{s,k}(N) \ll N^{s+\varepsilon}$ holds for all $s$."* Only for $s \le s_k$. Once $2s$ exceeds $k(k+1)$, the second floor $N^{2s - k(k+1)/2}$ overtakes $N^s$ and is genuinely larger; claiming $N^{s+\varepsilon}$ there contradicts the lower bound. The two-term shape of the conjecture is essential, not cosmetic.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is the main conjecture itself, now a theorem; below is the statement together with the structure of the proof, and a complete proof of the orthogonality identity and the lower bound that frame it [ref: TODO_REF Bourgain Demeter Guth 2016].

**Theorem (Vinogradov main conjecture; Bourgain-Demeter-Guth 2016, Wooley 2016).** *For all integers $k \ge 2$, $s \ge 1$ and every $\varepsilon > 0$,*
$$
J_{s,k}(N) \ll_{k, s, \varepsilon} N^{s + \varepsilon} + N^{2s - \frac{k(k+1)}{2} + \varepsilon}.
$$
*In particular at the critical exponent $s = s_k = k(k+1)/2$ one has $J_{s_k, k}(N) \ll_{k,\varepsilon} N^{s_k + \varepsilon}$.*

*Proof.* The lower bound matching this is elementary and is proved below as Proposition 2; the content is the upper bound, established by two independent routes.

*The orthogonality framing.* Expand $|f_k(\boldsymbol\alpha;N)|^{2s} = \sum_{\mathbf x, \mathbf y} e\big(\sum_{j=1}^k \alpha_j (\sum_i x_i^j - \sum_i y_i^j)\big)$ over $\mathbf x, \mathbf y \in \{1,\ldots,N\}^s$. Integrating over $\mathbb{T}^k$ and using $\int_0^1 e(m\alpha_j)\,d\alpha_j = \mathbf{1}_{m=0}$ in each coordinate kills every term except those with $\sum_i x_i^j = \sum_i y_i^j$ for all $j$, i.e. counts exactly the solutions of the system. So $\int_{\mathbb{T}^k}|f_k|^{2s} = J_{s,k}(N)$, reducing the arithmetic count to a $2s$-th moment of a Weyl sum of the type bounded in [21.15.02].

*Decoupling route (Bourgain-Demeter-Guth).* Partition $[0,1]$ into $N^{-1}$-arcs and consider the moment curve $\Gamma_k = \{(t, t^2, \ldots, t^k) : t \in [0,1]\}$. The $\ell^2$ **decoupling inequality** for $\Gamma_k$ states that for any function $g$ with Fourier support in an $N^{-1}$-neighbourhood of $\Gamma_k$, decomposed as $g = \sum_\theta g_\theta$ over $N^{-1/k}$-caps $\theta$,
$$
\| g \|_{L^p(\mathbb{R}^k)} \ll_\varepsilon N^\varepsilon \Big( \sum_\theta \|g_\theta\|_{L^p}^2 \Big)^{1/2}, \qquad p = k(k+1).
$$
Specialising $g$ to an exponential sum over the caps and using $\|g_\theta\|_{L^p} \asymp$ a single-cap contribution converts this analytic inequality, at $p = 2s_k = k(k+1)$, into the critical mean value bound $J_{s_k,k}(N) \ll N^{s_k + \varepsilon}$. The decoupling inequality itself is proved by induction on the scale $N$: a **multilinear Kakeya / Bennett-Carbery-Tao** transversality estimate handles the broad (well-spread) case, the **Bourgain-Guth** broad-narrow decomposition reduces the general case to the multilinear one, and **parabolic rescaling** of the moment curve closes the induction. The lower-degree decoupling for $\Gamma_{k-1}$ feeds the induction at each scale.

*Efficient congruencing route (Wooley).* Argue $p$-adically. Choosing a prime $p \asymp N^{1/k}$, classify solutions by the congruence classes of the variables modulo powers of $p$; a solution of the full system forces strong congruence conditions, because a Vandermonde determinant in the variables (the same determinant that makes the system rigid) is divisible by a high power of $p$. This lets one bound $J_{s,k}(N)$ by a version of itself at a smaller scale with extra congruence weight — an **efficient congruencing** iteration $J(N) \lesssim N^{\delta} J(N^{1-1/k})^{1-\eta}$ in schematic form. Iterating drives the exponent to the conjectured value; the **nested** efficient congruencing of Wooley's 2019 paper organises the bookkeeping to reach the sharp bound for all $k$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The mean value theorem builds toward the sharpest Weyl-sum bounds and the Vinogradov-Korobov zero-free region, and it appears again in the application to Waring's problem below, where $J_{s,k}$ is exactly the minor-arc moment fed into the circle method of [21.15.02]. The foundational reason the diagonal floor $N^s$ is so close to the truth is that the simultaneous power-sum equations are governed by a Vandermonde determinant: fixing the first $k$ power sums of a tuple of size $\le k$ fixes the tuple up to permutation, so genuinely non-diagonal solutions are forced to be sparse. This is exactly the rigidity that both proofs exploit — decoupling reads it as curvature of the moment curve $\Gamma_k$, efficient congruencing reads it as $p$-adic Vandermonde divisibility, and the two are dual descriptions of one geometric fact. The central insight is that a high-degree exponential sum's size is *controlled by* a counting problem, so the analytic question of cancellation generalises into the arithmetic question of how many solutions the system has; putting these together, the resolution of the main conjecture is simultaneously a statement in incidence geometry, in $p$-adic analysis, and in the theory of $\zeta$.

At the critical exponent $s = s_k$, the two floors $N^s$ and $N^{2s - k(k+1)/2}$ are equal. Verify this for $k = 2$ by computing both exponents at $s = s_2 = 3$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the orthogonality identity $J_{s,k}(N) = \int_{\mathbb{T}^k} |f_k(\boldsymbol\alpha; N)|^{2s}\, d\boldsymbol\alpha$ where $f_k(\boldsymbol\alpha; N) = \sum_{n \le N} e(\alpha_1 n + \cdots + \alpha_k n^k)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand the $2s$-th power as a sum over $s$-tuples $\mathbf x$ (from $f_k$) and $s$-tuples $\mathbf y$ (from $\overline{f_k}$), then integrate using $\int_0^1 e(m\alpha)\,d\alpha = \mathbf{1}_{m=0}$ in each of the $k$ variables.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $f_k = \sum_{n\le N} e\big(\sum_{j=1}^k \alpha_j n^j\big)$. Then
$$
|f_k|^{2s} = f_k^s\, \overline{f_k}^{\,s} = \sum_{\mathbf x, \mathbf y \in \{1,\ldots,N\}^s} e\Big( \sum_{j=1}^k \alpha_j \Big( \sum_{i=1}^s x_i^j - \sum_{i=1}^s y_i^j \Big) \Big).
$$
Integrate over $\boldsymbol\alpha \in [0,1)^k$. The integral factors across the $k$ coordinates, and in the $j$-th coordinate $\int_0^1 e(\alpha_j m_j)\,d\alpha_j = \mathbf{1}_{m_j = 0}$ with $m_j = \sum_i x_i^j - \sum_i y_i^j$. The product of these indicators is $1$ precisely when $\sum_i x_i^j = \sum_i y_i^j$ for all $j = 1, \ldots, k$ simultaneously, and $0$ otherwise. Hence
$$
\int_{\mathbb{T}^k} |f_k|^{2s}\,d\boldsymbol\alpha = \#\{ (\mathbf x, \mathbf y) : \textstyle\sum_i x_i^j = \sum_i y_i^j,\ 1 \le j \le k \} = J_{s,k}(N). \qquad \square
$$
This is the analytic-arithmetic bridge: a moment of a Weyl sum *is* a solution count, and it is why bounding $J_{s,k}$ is equivalent to bounding the sum on average.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the diagonal lower bound $J_{s,k}(N) \gg N^s$ by exhibiting that many solutions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take $\mathbf y$ to be a permutation of $\mathbf x$. How many tuples $\mathbf x$ have $s$ distinct entries, and how many permutations does each admit?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For any $\mathbf x = (x_1, \ldots, x_s) \in \{1, \ldots, N\}^s$ and any permutation $\sigma$ of $\{1, \ldots, s\}$, set $\mathbf y = (x_{\sigma(1)}, \ldots, x_{\sigma(s)})$. Then $\sum_i y_i^j = \sum_i x_{\sigma(i)}^j = \sum_i x_i^j$ for every $j$, so $(\mathbf x, \mathbf y)$ is a solution. The number of tuples $\mathbf x$ with $s$ pairwise distinct entries is $N(N-1)\cdots(N-s+1) \gg N^s$ once $N \ge 2s$, and each such $\mathbf x$ yields $s!$ distinct $\mathbf y$. Hence
$$
J_{s,k}(N) \ge s! \cdot N(N-1)\cdots(N-s+1) \gg_s N^s.
$$
These diagonal solutions use only the permutation symmetry of the equations and are present for every $k$; they are the first of the two floors. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).**

The main conjecture gives the Weyl-sum bound: for $k \ge 2$ and $|\alpha_k - a/q| \le q^{-2}$ with $\gcd(a,q) = 1$, Vinogradov's method yields $\sum_{n \le N} e(\alpha_k n^k) \ll N^{1 - \rho(k) + \varepsilon}$ with the saving $\rho(k) \asymp 1/(k^2 \log k)$ on a minor arc. Compare the order of magnitude of this saving with Weyl's classical saving $2^{1-k}$ for $k = 10$: which is larger, $1/(k^2\log k)$ or $2^{1-k}$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Estimate $2^{1-10} = 2^{-9} \approx 0.002$ against $1/(100 \cdot \log 10) \approx 1/(100 \cdot 2.3)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**The Vinogradov saving $1/(k^2\log k)$ is larger.** For $k = 10$: Weyl gives $2^{1-k} = 2^{-9} \approx 0.00195$, while Vinogradov gives $\asymp 1/(k^2 \log k) = 1/(100 \cdot \ln 10) \approx 1/230 \approx 0.0043$, roughly twice as large, and the gap widens fast: Weyl's $2^{1-k}$ decays exponentially in $k$, whereas Vinogradov's saving decays only polynomially (times a log). This is precisely why Vinogradov's method is the high-degree tool — the saving from Weyl differencing of [21.15.02] becomes negligible as $k$ grows, while the mean value theorem keeps a usable power saving uniformly in $k$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the critical case $s = s_k$ implies the case $s > s_k$: assuming $J_{s_k, k}(N) \ll N^{s_k + \varepsilon}$, deduce $J_{s,k}(N) \ll N^{2s - k(k+1)/2 + \varepsilon}$ for $s \ge s_k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each of the extra $2(s - s_k)$ variables ranges over $\{1,\ldots,N\}$. Bound the larger sum by directly summing the extra variables: $|f_k|^{2s} = |f_k|^{2s_k}\cdot |f_k|^{2(s-s_k)}$ and $|f_k| \le N$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $2s = 2s_k + 2(s - s_k)$. Pointwise $|f_k(\boldsymbol\alpha;N)| \le N$, so
$$
J_{s,k}(N) = \int_{\mathbb{T}^k} |f_k|^{2s}\,d\boldsymbol\alpha = \int_{\mathbb{T}^k} |f_k|^{2s_k}\, |f_k|^{2(s-s_k)}\,d\boldsymbol\alpha \le N^{2(s - s_k)} \int_{\mathbb{T}^k} |f_k|^{2s_k}\,d\boldsymbol\alpha = N^{2(s-s_k)} J_{s_k,k}(N).
$$
By the critical bound this is $\ll N^{2(s - s_k)} N^{s_k + \varepsilon} = N^{2s - 2s_k + s_k + \varepsilon} = N^{2s - s_k + \varepsilon}$. Since $s_k = k(k+1)/2$, the exponent is $2s - k(k+1)/2 + \varepsilon$, the second floor. Thus the critical case controls all larger $s$ by direct summation of the surplus variables; a Hölder interpolation handles $s < s_k$ similarly, so the whole conjecture reduces to its value at $s = s_k$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the Vandermonde rigidity behind the floor: if two tuples $(x_1,\ldots,x_k)$ and $(y_1,\ldots,y_k)$ of length exactly $k$ satisfy $\sum_i x_i^j = \sum_i y_i^j$ for all $j = 1, \ldots, k$, then $(y_1,\ldots,y_k)$ is a permutation of $(x_1,\ldots,x_k)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Equal power sums $p_1, \ldots, p_k$ force equal elementary symmetric polynomials $e_1, \ldots, e_k$ via Newton's identities, hence equal characteristic polynomials $\prod(T - x_i) = \prod(T - y_i)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $p_j = \sum_i x_i^j$ and $p_j' = \sum_i y_i^j$ be the power sums, and $e_m, e_m'$ the elementary symmetric polynomials of the two tuples. Newton's identities express each $e_m$ as a polynomial in $p_1, \ldots, p_m$ (for $m \le k$):
$$
e_1 = p_1, \quad 2e_2 = e_1 p_1 - p_2, \quad \ldots, \quad m\,e_m = \sum_{i=1}^{m} (-1)^{i-1} e_{m-i}\, p_i.
$$
Given $p_j = p_j'$ for $1 \le j \le k$, induction on $m$ gives $e_m = e_m'$ for all $1 \le m \le k$ (the recursion determines $e_m$ from $e_1,\ldots,e_{m-1}$ and $p_1,\ldots,p_m$, all equal by hypothesis and the inductive step). Therefore the monic polynomials
$$
P(T) = \prod_{i=1}^k (T - x_i) = T^k - e_1 T^{k-1} + \cdots + (-1)^k e_k
$$
and $Q(T) = \prod_i (T - y_i)$ have identical coefficients, so $P = Q$. A monic polynomial determines its multiset of roots, so $\{y_i\}$ equals $\{x_i\}$ as multisets, i.e. $\mathbf y$ is a permutation of $\mathbf x$. Hence at length $s = k$ the *only* solutions are diagonal — the rigidity that propagates, with losses, to the floor $J_{s,k}(N) \approx N^s$ for $s$ near $s_k$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Deduce the Weyl-sum bound from the mean value theorem in skeleton form: assuming $J_{s,k}(N) \ll N^{2s - k(k+1)/2 + \varepsilon}$ at a suitable $s$, show how Hölder's inequality and a major/minor-arc passage bound an individual sum $\sum_{n\le N} e(\alpha_k n^k)$ on a minor arc by $N^{1 - \rho + \varepsilon}$ for some $\rho = \rho(k) > 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The mean value bounds the *average* of $|f_k|^{2s}$ over all $\boldsymbol\alpha$. To bound a *single* sum, combine the mean value with a major/minor-arc dissection in the lower coefficients $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_{k-1}$, so that a large value of the pure sum $\sum e(\alpha_k n^k)$ would force the full $f_k$ to be large on a set too big for the mean value to allow.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $|\sum_{n\le N} e(\alpha_k n^k)| \ge N^{1 - \rho}$ on a minor arc (where $\alpha_k$ has a denominator $q$ in the range $N^\delta \le q \le N^{k - \delta}$). One relates this single sum to the full Weyl sum $f_k(\boldsymbol\alpha;N)$ as follows. By translating $n \mapsto n + m$ and averaging over shifts $m$, a lower bound on the pure degree-$k$ sum produces, for many choices of the lower coefficients $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_{k-1}$ (a set of measure $\gg N^{-O(1)}$ in $\mathbb{T}^{k-1}$), a lower bound $|f_k(\boldsymbol\alpha; N)| \gg N^{1 - \rho - o(1)}$. Integrating $|f_k|^{2s}$ over just that set of $\boldsymbol\alpha$ gives
$$
J_{s,k}(N) = \int_{\mathbb{T}^k} |f_k|^{2s} \ge (\text{measure of the set}) \cdot (N^{1 - \rho})^{2s} \gg N^{-O(1)} N^{2s(1 - \rho)}.
$$
Comparing with the mean value upper bound $J_{s,k}(N) \ll N^{2s - k(k+1)/2 + \varepsilon}$ forces
$$
2s(1 - \rho) - O(1) \le 2s - \tfrac{k(k+1)}{2} + \varepsilon, \qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad 2s\,\rho \ge \tfrac{k(k+1)}{2} - O(1) - \varepsilon.
$$
Choosing $s \asymp k^2$ (the critical range) gives $\rho \gg \frac{k(k+1)/2}{2s} \asymp \frac{k^2}{k^2} \cdot \frac{1}{(\text{log factors})}$; the careful bookkeeping in [ref: TODO_REF Iwaniec Kowalski 2004] yields $\rho(k) \asymp 1/(k^2 \log k)$. Hence the assumption $|\sum e(\alpha_k n^k)| \ge N^{1-\rho}$ is untenable for $\rho$ smaller than this, giving $\sum_{n\le N} e(\alpha_k n^k) \ll N^{1 - \rho(k) + \varepsilon}$ on the minor arc. The pure power sum's cancellation is thus extracted from the on-average control of the full sum. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The critical exponent and the shape of the bound

The entire difficulty of the main conjecture concentrates at $s = s_k = k(k+1)/2$ [ref: TODO_REF Demeter 2020]. For $s \le s_k$ the conjecture reads $J_{s,k}(N) \ll N^{s + \varepsilon}$ (the diagonal floor), and for $s \ge s_k$ it reads $J_{s,k}(N) \ll N^{2s - s_k + \varepsilon}$ (the dimension floor); the critical case interpolates between them at $J_{s_k,k}(N) \ll N^{s_k + \varepsilon}$, and Exercise 6 shows it implies the rest. Before the resolution, Vinogradov's original 1935 method and its refinements by Linnik, Karatsuba, and Stechkin reached bounds of the shape $J_{s,k}(N) \ll N^{2s - k(k+1)/2 + \eta_k}$ with $\eta_k \to 0$ but $\eta_k > 0$ for fixed $s$ near critical — strong enough for the zero-free region but not the exact main conjecture. The gap $\eta_k$ was closed unconditionally only in 2015-2016, first for $k = 3$ by Wooley's efficient congruencing (2014) and then for all $k$ by Bourgain-Demeter-Guth's decoupling (2016) and Wooley's nested congruencing (2019).

### Decoupling for the moment curve

The Bourgain-Demeter-Guth proof factors through a statement in Euclidean harmonic analysis with no number theory in it [ref: TODO_REF Bourgain Demeter Guth 2016]. The $\ell^2$ **decoupling inequality** for the moment curve $\Gamma_k = \{(t, t^2, \ldots, t^k) : t \in [0,1]\} \subset \mathbb{R}^k$ asserts that for $g$ Fourier-supported in the $\delta$-neighbourhood of $\Gamma_k$ and decomposed into $\delta^{1/k}$-caps $\theta$,
$$
\| g \|_{L^p(\mathbb{R}^k)} \le C_\varepsilon \delta^{-\varepsilon} \Big( \sum_\theta \| g_\theta \|_{L^p(\mathbb{R}^k)}^2 \Big)^{1/2}
$$
for all $2 \le p \le k(k+1)$, sharp at the endpoint $p = k(k+1) = 2 s_k$. Taking $g_\theta$ to be (smoothed) characters concentrated on each cap and $\delta = N^{-1}$ recovers the critical mean value bound. The proof is a Bourgain-Guth induction on scales: a **multilinear Kakeya** estimate (Bennett-Carbery-Tao, with the Guth polynomial-method proof) controls the transversal/broad case, the broad-narrow dichotomy reduces the general sum to the multilinear case plus a lower-dimensional contribution handled by decoupling for $\Gamma_{k-1}$, and parabolic rescaling restores the scale. Curvature of $\Gamma_k$ — the non-vanishing of the torsion, equivalently the Vandermonde determinant of the tangent, osculating, and higher flags — is the geometric input that makes decoupling hold, and it is the exact analytic avatar of the arithmetic rigidity of Exercise 7.

### Efficient congruencing and the $p$-adic route

Wooley's method bypasses harmonic analysis entirely, working $p$-adically [ref: TODO_REF Wooley 2012]. The idea is to count solutions by conditioning on the $p$-adic distances between the variables: solutions in which many variables are congruent modulo high powers of a prime $p \asymp N^{1/k}$ are forced by a Vandermonde divisibility — the same determinant — and this lets the count at scale $N$ be bounded by a weighted count at scale $N^{1 - 1/k}$, giving a self-improving recursion. Each iteration "efficiently congruences" a block of variables, trading scale for congruence concentration; the **nested** version tracks two interleaved blocks and reaches the sharp exponent for every $k$. Efficient congruencing also extends to systems over number fields and function fields where the decoupling machinery is harder to deploy, and it gives explicit (non-$\varepsilon$-power) bounds in some ranges. The two proofs are now understood as parallel: decoupling is the archimedean and efficient congruencing the non-archimedean reading of the curvature/Vandermonde rigidity of the moment curve.

### Consequences: Weyl sums, the zero-free region, and Waring's problem

The mean value theorem is a tool, and its three classical payoffs are sharp [ref: TODO_REF Iwaniec Kowalski 2004]. First, the **Weyl-sum bound**: for the high-degree minor-arc sum $\sum_{n\le N} e(\alpha_k n^k + \cdots)$ one obtains a saving $N^{1 - \rho(k) + \varepsilon}$ with $\rho(k) \asymp 1/(k^2 \log k)$, beating the van der Corput exponent-pair and Weyl-differencing bounds of [21.15.03] and [21.15.02] for all large $k$. Second, the **Vinogradov-Korobov zero-free region**: feeding these bounds into the explicit formula gives that $\zeta(\sigma + it) \ne 0$ for $\sigma \ge 1 - c/(\log|t|)^{2/3}(\log\log|t|)^{1/3}$, the widest known zero-free region and the source of the best unconditional error term in the prime number theorem. Third, **Waring's problem**: the mean value bound controls the minor arcs in the circle method for sums of $k$-th powers, yielding $G(k) \le k(\log k + \log\log k + O(1))$ for the least $s$ such that every large integer is a sum of $s$ positive $k$-th powers — a bound that the resolution of the main conjecture brought to within a constant of the conjectured truth.

**Synthesis.** Vinogradov's mean value theorem, the decoupling inequality for the moment curve, efficient congruencing, and the Vinogradov-Korobov zero-free region are one circle of ideas, and the bridge is the rigidity of the moment curve $\Gamma_k$ — its curvature, equivalently the Vandermonde determinant of its flags. The foundational reason the count is pinned to its two floors is that fixing the first $k$ power sums of a short tuple fixes the tuple up to permutation (Exercise 7), and this is exactly the curvature that makes $\ell^2$ decoupling sharp and the $p$-adic Vandermonde divisibility that drives efficient congruencing; the two resolutions are dual — archimedean and non-archimedean — readings of one geometric fact. The central insight is that a high-degree exponential sum's cancellation *is* a counting problem, so the analytic Weyl-sum bound of [21.15.02] generalises into the arithmetic mean value, and putting these together, the same bound that resolves the count delivers the sharpest Weyl bounds, the Vinogradov-Korobov region for $\zeta$, and the near-optimal $G(k)$ in Waring's problem. This is exactly where the smooth-phase exponent-pair calculus of [21.15.03] runs out of strength and the polynomial-system viewpoint takes over: for high degree the central insight is that one should count solutions, not estimate sums directly, and the moment curve's geometry then does the rest.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (orthogonality and the mean value identity).** *With $f_k(\boldsymbol\alpha;N) = \sum_{n\le N} e(\alpha_1 n + \cdots + \alpha_k n^k)$, one has $J_{s,k}(N) = \int_{\mathbb{T}^k} |f_k(\boldsymbol\alpha;N)|^{2s}\,d\boldsymbol\alpha$.*

*Proof.* Expanding $|f_k|^{2s} = f_k^s \overline{f_k}^s$ as a sum over $(\mathbf x, \mathbf y) \in \{1,\ldots,N\}^{2s}$ gives the integrand $e(\sum_{j=1}^k \alpha_j(\sum_i x_i^j - \sum_i y_i^j))$. Integration over $\mathbb{T}^k$ factors over the $k$ variables, and $\int_0^1 e(\alpha_j m_j)\,d\alpha_j = \mathbf{1}_{m_j = 0}$ with $m_j = \sum_i x_i^j - \sum_i y_i^j$. The product of indicators equals $1$ exactly for the solutions of the system, so the integral counts them. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (the two-term lower bound).** *For $N \ge 2s$, $J_{s,k}(N) \gg_s N^s + N^{2s - k(k+1)/2}$.*

*Proof.* The diagonal contribution $\gg N^s$ is Exercise 4: permutations of distinct tuples give $s! \cdot N(N-1)\cdots(N-s+1) \gg N^s$ solutions. For the second term, suppose $2s \ge k(k+1)/2$. The map $(\mathbf x, \mathbf y) \mapsto (\sum_i x_i^j - \sum_i y_i^j)_{1\le j\le k}$ from $\{1,\ldots,N\}^{2s}$ to $\mathbb{Z}^k$ takes values $m_j$ with $|m_j| \le 2s N^j$, so the number of possible target vectors is $\ll \prod_{j=1}^k N^j = N^{1+2+\cdots+k} = N^{k(k+1)/2}$. By pigeonhole, some fibre (in fact the fibre over $\mathbf 0$, by a standard averaging since $\mathbf 0$ is the most populous value) has at least
$$
\frac{N^{2s}}{C\,N^{k(k+1)/2}} \gg N^{2s - k(k+1)/2}
$$
preimages, and the fibre over $\mathbf 0$ is exactly the solution set counted by $J_{s,k}(N)$. (That $\mathbf 0$ is the densest fibre follows from $J_{s,k}(N) = \int|f_k|^{2s} \ge |\int |f_k|^{2s} e(\cdot)|$-type positivity, or directly from the Cauchy-Schwarz / convexity bound $\sum_{\mathbf m} r(\mathbf m)^2 \le r(\mathbf 0)\sum_{\mathbf m} r(\mathbf m)$ where $r(\mathbf m)$ counts representations.) Adding the two contributions gives the stated floor. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (the $k=1$ and small-$s$ cases are exact).** *For $k = 1$, $J_{s,1}(N) = \int_0^1 |\sum_{n\le N} e(\alpha n)|^{2s}\,d\alpha$ counts solutions of the single equation $\sum x_i = \sum y_i$, and the main conjecture $J_{s,1}(N) \ll N^{s + \varepsilon} + N^{2s - 1 + \varepsilon}$ holds with $J_{s,1}(N) \asymp N^{2s-1}$ for $s \ge 1$. For $s \le k$, $J_{s,k}(N) = s!\,N^s + O(N^{s-1})$ is purely diagonal to leading order.*

*Proof.* For $k = 1$ the count is the number of $(\mathbf x, \mathbf y)$ with equal sums; the number of ways to write a value $v \in [s, sN]$ as $x_1 + \cdots + x_s$ is a bounded-degree quasi-polynomial peaking at $\asymp N^{s-1}$ near $v \asymp sN/2$, and $J_{s,1}(N) = \sum_v r(v)^2 \asymp N \cdot (N^{s-1})^2 / N = N^{2s-1}$ by Cauchy-Schwarz being sharp for the near-uniform $r$, consistent with the second floor (here $k(k+1)/2 = 1$). For $s \le k$: by the Vandermonde rigidity of Exercise 7 (Proposition 4 below), every solution with all $x_i$ distinct is diagonal, and tuples with a repeated coordinate number $O(N^{s-1})$; so $J_{s,k}(N) = (\text{distinct diagonal}) + O(N^{s-1}) = s!\binom{N}{s}s! /s! \cdots = s!\,N^s(1 + O(1/N))$, the diagonal floor with no excess. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Vandermonde rigidity at length $k$).** *If $\mathbf x, \mathbf y \in \mathbb{Z}^k$ satisfy $\sum_i x_i^j = \sum_i y_i^j$ for $1 \le j \le k$, then $\mathbf y$ is a permutation of $\mathbf x$.*

*Proof.* Equal power sums $p_1, \ldots, p_k$ force, via Newton's identities $m e_m = \sum_{i=1}^m (-1)^{i-1} e_{m-i} p_i$, equal elementary symmetric functions $e_1, \ldots, e_k$ (induction on $m \le k$). Hence the monic degree-$k$ polynomials $\prod_i(T - x_i)$ and $\prod_i(T - y_i)$ have equal coefficients, so they are equal, and a monic polynomial determines its root multiset. Thus $\{x_i\} = \{y_i\}$ as multisets. This is the determinantal heart of the subject: the Jacobian of $(\sum x_i, \ldots, \sum x_i^k)$ in the $x_i$ is a Vandermonde determinant $\prod_{i<i'}(x_i - x_{i'})$ times $k!$, non-vanishing off the diagonal, which is the local form of the rigidity. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

The mean value $J_{s,k}(N)$ is a high-moment refinement of the Weyl-sum estimates of [21.15.02]: where Weyl differencing bounds a single sum $\sum e(\alpha n^k)$ with the lossy saving $2^{1-k}$, the mean value controls the $2s$-th moment of the full polynomial Weyl sum sharply, and the resulting minor-arc bound feeds the same Hardy-Littlewood circle method that Weyl's inequality serves.

The method supersedes the van der Corput exponent-pair calculus of [21.15.03] in the high-degree regime: the iterated $A,B$-processes lose a constant factor of cancellation at each step and become inert as $k$ grows, whereas the mean value keeps a saving $\rho(k) \asymp 1/(k^2\log k)$ that decays only polynomially, so for large $k$ one counts solutions of the system rather than estimating the sum through stationary phase.

The sharp Weyl bounds produced here yield the Vinogradov-Korobov zero-free region, the widest known for $\zeta$, which is the deepest input to the growth, zero-density, and prime-counting theory of the Riemann zeta function in [21.03.01]; the same minor-arc control underlies the circle-method treatment of Waring's problem and links back to the complete exponential sums of [21.15.04] that govern the major arcs.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Ivan Matveevich Vinogradov introduced the mean value method in 1935 [ref: TODO_REF Vinogradov 1935] as the engine of his "method of trigonometric sums," using it to bound Weyl sums of high degree far beyond what Weyl's differencing or van der Corput's processes could reach, and thereby to establish the zero-free region for $\zeta$ that Korobov and Vinogradov independently sharpened to the $(\log t)^{2/3}$ form around 1958. The original bounds carried an unavoidable loss $\eta_k > 0$ from the exponent $k(k+1)/2$; closing that loss to the conjectured $\varepsilon$ was the central open problem of the area for eighty years. Linnik's 1943 $p$-adic reformulation anticipated the congruencing idea, and Karatsuba and Stechkin pushed the classical method to its limit.

The resolution came from two directions almost simultaneously. Trevor Wooley's **efficient congruencing**, beginning in 2012 [ref: TODO_REF Wooley 2012], proved the main conjecture for $k = 3$ and then, in nested form, for all $k$; it is a $p$-adic descent that conditions on congruences between the variables, made precise by Vandermonde divisibility. Jean Bourgain, Ciprian Demeter, and Larry Guth proved the full conjecture for all $k$ in 2016 [ref: TODO_REF Bourgain Demeter Guth 2016] via $\ell^2$ **decoupling** for the moment curve, an inequality in Euclidean harmonic analysis with no arithmetic content, resting on the multilinear Kakeya estimate and the Bourgain-Guth induction on scales. Demeter's 2020 book [ref: TODO_REF Demeter 2020] gives the systematic decoupling account. The two proofs are now read as archimedean and non-archimedean expressions of the same curvature of the moment curve, and the episode is a case where a number-theoretic conjecture was settled by, and in turn reshaped, the geometry of the Fourier restriction problem.
