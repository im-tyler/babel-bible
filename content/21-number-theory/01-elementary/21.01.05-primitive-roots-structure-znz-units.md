## Intuition [Beginner]

A **primitive root modulo $p$** is a single nonzero residue whose powers, taken modulo $p$, sweep out every other nonzero residue exactly once before returning to $1$. For the prime $p = 5$, the nonzero residues are $1, 2, 3, 4$. Take $2$ as the candidate: $2^1 = 2$; $2^2 = 4$; $2^3 = 8 \equiv 3 \pmod 5$; $2^4 = 16 \equiv 1 \pmod 5$. The four powers $2, 4, 3, 1$ are exactly the four nonzero residues, in a different order. So $2$ is a primitive root modulo $5$.

Not every element is a primitive root. For the same prime $p = 5$, take the candidate $4$: $4^1 = 4$; $4^2 = 16 \equiv 1 \pmod 5$. The powers of $4$ cycle back to $1$ after only $2$ steps, missing $2$ and $3$ entirely. The element $4$ has **order $2$** in $(\mathbb{Z}/5\mathbb{Z})^\times$, not the full order $4 = p - 1$ needed to be a primitive root.

Why does this concept exist? Because primitive roots reveal that $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times$ — the multiplicative group of nonzero residues modulo $p$ — has a single generator. Once you know a primitive root $g$, every other nonzero residue is some power of $g$. This is the prime-modulus version of the deeper fact that the unit group is **cyclic**: built out of one generating element, like the hours on a clock.

The classification of which moduli admit primitive roots is a celebrated theorem of Gauss in his 1801 *Disquisitiones Arithmeticae*. The moduli $n$ for which $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ is cyclic — equivalently, the moduli for which a primitive root exists — are exactly $n = 1, 2, 4$, the odd prime powers $p^k$, and twice the odd prime powers $2 p^k$. For every other $n$, the unit group splits as a product of two or more cyclic pieces, and no single element generates the whole group.

The applied importance of primitive roots is the **discrete logarithm problem**. Fix a prime $p$ and a primitive root $g$ modulo $p$. Every nonzero residue $a$ is then $g^k$ for a unique $k$ with $0 \leq k < p - 1$. Computing $a$ from $k$ is fast (repeated squaring takes about $\log_2 k$ multiplications). Recovering $k$ from $a$ — the discrete logarithm — appears to be hard: no classical algorithm is known that runs in time polynomial in $\log p$.

This asymmetry is the cornerstone of Diffie-Hellman key exchange (1976), ElGamal encryption (1985), and the Digital Signature Algorithm — every secure VPN, every TLS handshake done with a non-elliptic-curve cipher suite, every Tor circuit. The quantum algorithm of Peter Shor 1994 would break this asymmetry on a sufficiently large quantum computer, and the post-quantum cryptography programme exists precisely to plan for that future.

A diagram of the cyclic orbit of a primitive root modulo a small prime. For $p = 7$ the primitive root $3$ generates the orbit $3^1 = 3$, $3^2 = 9 \equiv 2$, $3^3 \equiv 6$, $3^4 \equiv 4$, $3^5 \equiv 5$, $3^6 \equiv 1$. The six powers $3, 2, 6, 4, 5, 1$ form a complete cycle through every nonzero residue modulo $7$, then close back to $1$.

The picture captures the defining property of a primitive root: the orbit visits every nonzero residue exactly once, and then returns to $1$ after exactly $p - 1$ steps. Compare with the orbit of $2$ modulo $7$: $2, 4, 1, 2, 4, 1$ — a shorter cycle of length $3$ that misses three of the residues. The element $2$ has order $3$ modulo $7$, and $3$ has order $6$; only the latter is a primitive root.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Find a primitive root modulo $p = 11$, and verify it by listing its orbit.

Step 1. The order of a primitive root must equal $p - 1 = 10$. By a basic group fact, the order of any element divides $10$, so the possible orders are $1, 2, 5, 10$. A candidate $g$ is a primitive root if and only if its order is $10$ — equivalently, $g^5 \not\equiv 1 \pmod{11}$ and $g^2 \not\equiv 1 \pmod{11}$.

Step 2. Test $g = 2$. Compute $2^2 = 4 \not\equiv 1 \pmod{11}$ and $2^5 = 32 = 2 \cdot 11 + 10 \equiv 10 \equiv -1 \pmod{11}$. Both order-failing conditions are avoided, so the order of $2$ is exactly $10$. The element $2$ is a primitive root modulo $11$.

Step 3. List the orbit: $2^1 = 2$; $2^2 = 4$; $2^3 = 8$; $2^4 = 16 \equiv 5 \pmod{11}$; $2^5 \equiv 10 \pmod{11}$; $2^6 \equiv 20 \equiv 9 \pmod{11}$; $2^7 \equiv 18 \equiv 7 \pmod{11}$; $2^8 \equiv 14 \equiv 3 \pmod{11}$; $2^9 \equiv 6 \pmod{11}$; $2^{10} \equiv 12 \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$.

Step 4. The ten powers $2, 4, 8, 5, 10, 9, 7, 3, 6, 1$ exhaust the ten nonzero residues modulo $11$, confirming $2$ is a primitive root.

What this tells us: to test whether $g$ is a primitive root modulo $p$, you do not need to compute the full orbit. You only need to check that $g^{(p-1)/q} \not\equiv 1 \pmod p$ for every prime $q$ dividing $p - 1$. For $p = 11$ the prime divisors of $10$ are $2$ and $5$, and the two checks $g^5 \not\equiv 1$ and $g^2 \not\equiv 1$ are enough.

Which of the following is a primitive root modulo $7$?

**Exercise (easy, true-false).**

True or false: there exists a primitive root modulo $8$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The classification says primitive roots exist modulo $n$ exactly when $n$ is $1, 2, 4$, an odd prime power, or twice an odd prime power. Is $8 = 2^3$ on that list?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**False.** The classification theorem of Gauss says $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ is cyclic — equivalently, a primitive root exists — only for $n \in \{1, 2, 4, p^k, 2 p^k\}$ with $p$ an odd prime. Since $8 = 2^3$ with $k \geq 3$, the modulus $8$ is not on the list. Direct check: the units modulo $8$ are $\{1, 3, 5, 7\}$, and every one of them squares to $1$ — for instance $3^2 = 9 \equiv 1$, $5^2 = 25 \equiv 1$, $7^2 = 49 \equiv 1$. So every unit has order $1$ or $2$, never the full group order $4$. The group $(\mathbb{Z}/8)^\times$ is in fact the Klein four-group $\mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2$.

**Exercise (easy, numeric).**

How many primitive roots are there modulo $p = 13$? Enter a single integer.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The count of primitive roots modulo a prime $p$ is the Euler totient of $p - 1$. For $p = 13$, $p - 1 = 12$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

We restate the order of an element and the notion of a primitive root in the precise group-theoretic language inherited from [21.01.04].

**Definition (multiplicative order modulo $n$).** Let $n \geq 1$ be an integer and $a$ an integer with $\gcd(a, n) = 1$. The **multiplicative order** of $a$ modulo $n$, written $\mathrm{ord}_n(a)$, is the least positive integer $k$ such that $a^k \equiv 1 \pmod n$.

Such a $k$ exists because $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ is a finite group of order $\varphi(n)$, so the cyclic subgroup generated by $[a]_n$ is finite. By Lagrange's theorem applied to the finite group $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$,
$$
\mathrm{ord}_n(a) \;\big|\; \varphi(n).
$$

**Definition (primitive root modulo $n$).** A **primitive root modulo $n$** is an integer $g$ with $\gcd(g, n) = 1$ and $\mathrm{ord}_n(g) = \varphi(n)$. Equivalently, $[g]_n$ generates $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ as a cyclic group: every $[a]_n \in (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ is $[g^k]_n$ for some integer $k$.

The existence of a primitive root modulo $n$ is equivalent to $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ being **cyclic** — generated by a single element. The classification of such $n$ is the structure theorem proved in the next subsection.

**Definition (discrete logarithm / index).** Let $n$ be a modulus admitting a primitive root $g$, so $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ is cyclic of order $\varphi(n)$ with generator $[g]_n$. For every $a$ coprime to $n$, the **discrete logarithm** (or **index**) of $a$ to base $g$ modulo $n$ is the unique integer $k$ with $0 \leq k < \varphi(n)$ such that $g^k \equiv a \pmod n$. Notation: $k = \mathrm{ind}_g(a) = \log_g(a)$.

The discrete logarithm is the inverse function of the exponentiation map $k \mapsto g^k$, which is a group isomorphism $\mathbb{Z}/\varphi(n)\mathbb{Z} \to (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$. The Gauss index calculus exploits this isomorphism: $\mathrm{ind}_g(ab) \equiv \mathrm{ind}_g(a) + \mathrm{ind}_g(b) \pmod{\varphi(n)}$ — multiplicative structure modulo $n$ becomes additive structure modulo $\varphi(n)$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"Every $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ is cyclic."* False already for $n = 8$: the unit group has order $\varphi(8) = 4$ but every unit squares to $1$, so $(\mathbb{Z}/8)^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2$ is not cyclic. The classification theorem restricts cyclicity to $n \in \{1, 2, 4, p^k, 2 p^k\}$ for $p$ an odd prime.

- *"The order of $a$ modulo $n$ equals $\varphi(n)$ for some $a$ in every modulus."* False — the previous slip restated. For $n = 8$ the maximum order is $2$, strictly below $\varphi(8) = 4$. The maximum achievable order is the **Carmichael function** $\lambda(n)$ of [21.01.04], which can be strictly less than $\varphi(n)$.

- *"The discrete logarithm is well-defined for every nonzero residue."* Only when the residue is coprime to the modulus. The residue $4$ modulo $6$ has no discrete logarithm to base any primitive root of $(\mathbb{Z}/6)^\times = \{1, 5\}$ because $\gcd(4, 6) = 2 \neq 1$. The discrete log lives on the unit group, not on all of $\mathbb{Z}/n$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem of this unit is the existence of a primitive root modulo every prime. The proof technique — counting elements of each order via the field structure of $\mathbb{F}_p$ and the totient sum $\sum_{d \mid m} \varphi(d) = m$ — is the prototype for every cyclicity proof in elementary number theory.

**Theorem (Gauss; existence of primitive roots modulo $p$).** *For every prime $p$, the group $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times$ is cyclic of order $p - 1$. The number of primitive roots modulo $p$ is $\varphi(p - 1)$.*

*Proof.* Let $G = (\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times$, a finite abelian group of order $p - 1$. For each positive divisor $d$ of $p - 1$, let
$$
N(d) \;=\; \# \{\, [a]_p \in G : \mathrm{ord}([a]_p) = d \,\}.
$$
The element orders are exactly the divisors of $p - 1$ (Lagrange), so
$$
\sum_{d \mid p - 1} N(d) \;=\; p - 1. \quad (\star)
$$

We bound $N(d)$ by the field property of $\mathbb{F}_p = \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$. An element of order dividing $d$ is a root of the polynomial $x^d - 1 \in \mathbb{F}_p[x]$. A polynomial of degree $d$ over a field has at most $d$ roots, so the number of elements of order dividing $d$ is at most $d$. Hence
$$
\sum_{e \mid d} N(e) \;\leq\; d. \quad (\star\star)
$$

The crucial step: when $N(d) > 0$, the bound $(\star\star)$ becomes an equality. Suppose $[a]_p$ has order exactly $d$; then the powers $[a]_p, [a]_p^2, \ldots, [a]_p^d = [1]_p$ are $d$ distinct elements of order dividing $d$, so they are all of them by $(\star\star)$. Among these $d$ elements, the number of order exactly $d$ is the number of $k \in \{1, \ldots, d\}$ with $\gcd(k, d) = 1$ — namely $\varphi(d)$. Hence:
$$
N(d) \in \{0, \varphi(d)\} \quad \text{for every } d \mid p - 1.
$$

Combining with $(\star)$ and the elementary totient identity $\sum_{d \mid m} \varphi(d) = m$ (proved in [21.01.03] Proposition 1):
$$
p - 1 \;=\; \sum_{d \mid p - 1} N(d) \;\leq\; \sum_{d \mid p - 1} \varphi(d) \;=\; p - 1.
$$
The inequality is an equality. Hence $N(d) = \varphi(d)$ for every $d \mid p - 1$. In particular $N(p - 1) = \varphi(p - 1) > 0$ since $p \geq 2$, so there exists an element of order exactly $p - 1$ — a primitive root. The cyclic subgroup generated by any such element has order $p - 1 = |G|$, so it is all of $G$, and $G$ is cyclic.

The count of primitive roots is $N(p - 1) = \varphi(p - 1)$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The polynomial-degree bound $(\star\star)$ is the foundational reason that finite subgroups of multiplicative groups of fields are cyclic — and the central insight is exactly this: the field property of $\mathbb{F}_p$ forces every order-$d$ root-locus to have exactly $\varphi(d)$ elements of order $d$ (or zero), and the totient summation $\sum_{d \mid m} \varphi(d) = m$ forces every divisor to be hit. This is the bridge to [01.02.18] finite fields $\mathbb{F}_q$: every $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ is cyclic of order $q - 1$ by the same argument (no exception, unlike the integer-modulus case), and the unit group of any field has every finite subgroup cyclic. The proof generalises to the lift from $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ to $(\mathbb{Z}/p^k)^\times$ via Hensel: a primitive root modulo $p$ lifts to a primitive root modulo $p^k$ for $p$ odd (the technical content of Theorem 1 below), identifying the orbit structure modulo $p^k$ with the orbit structure modulo $p$ times a $p^{k-1}$-cyclic radical. The same divisor-counting pattern recurs in [21.01.06] quadratic residues, where the count of squares is $(p - 1)/2$ via the kernel-image computation of the squaring map.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that the count of primitive roots modulo a prime $p$ is $\varphi(p - 1)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the cyclic structure of $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ established by the Key Theorem. The generators of a cyclic group of order $m$ are precisely the elements $g^k$ with $\gcd(k, m) = 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix a primitive root $g$ modulo $p$. By the Key Theorem, $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times = \{g^0, g^1, \ldots, g^{p - 2}\}$ is cyclic of order $p - 1$ generated by $g$.

The element $g^k$ has order $\mathrm{ord}(g^k) = (p - 1) / \gcd(k, p - 1)$ — a standard cyclic-group computation. So $g^k$ is a primitive root if and only if $\mathrm{ord}(g^k) = p - 1$, equivalent to $\gcd(k, p - 1) = 1$.

The number of $k \in \{0, 1, \ldots, p - 2\}$ with $\gcd(k, p - 1) = 1$ is $\varphi(p - 1)$ by definition of the totient. (The case $k = 0$ corresponds to the identity $g^0 = 1$, which has $\gcd(0, p - 1) = p - 1 \neq 1$ for $p > 2$, so it is not counted — consistent with $1$ not being a primitive root.)

Hence there are exactly $\varphi(p - 1)$ primitive roots modulo $p$.

For $p = 7$: $\varphi(6) = 2$ primitive roots, namely $3$ and $5 = 3^5$. For $p = 11$: $\varphi(10) = 4$ primitive roots, namely $2, 6, 7, 8$. For $p = 13$: $\varphi(12) = 4$ primitive roots: $2, 6, 7, 11$.

Rubric: full credit for the cyclic-group identification of generators with $\gcd(k, m) = 1$.

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that $(\mathbb{Z}/8)^\times$ is not cyclic. Identify its isomorphism type as a product of cyclic groups.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The units modulo $8$ are $\{1, 3, 5, 7\}$. Compute the order of each. A cyclic group of order $4$ has an element of order $4$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The units modulo $8$ are $\{1, 3, 5, 7\}$, four in number, matching $\varphi(8) = 4$.

Compute squares: $1^2 = 1$; $3^2 = 9 \equiv 1 \pmod 8$; $5^2 = 25 \equiv 1 \pmod 8$; $7^2 = 49 \equiv 1 \pmod 8$. Every unit squares to $1$, so every non-identity unit has order exactly $2$, and the identity has order $1$.

A cyclic group of order $4$ has an element of order $4$; since $(\mathbb{Z}/8)^\times$ has no such element, it is not cyclic. Every element has order dividing $2$, so $(\mathbb{Z}/8)^\times$ is an elementary abelian $2$-group. With four elements, this forces $(\mathbb{Z}/8)^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2$ — the Klein four-group $V_4$.

Explicit isomorphism: $1 \leftrightarrow (0, 0)$, $3 \leftrightarrow (1, 0)$, $5 \leftrightarrow (0, 1)$, $7 \leftrightarrow (1, 1)$. (Or any other identification consistent with the group structure.)

This is the base case of the general structure $(\mathbb{Z}/2^k)^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2^{k - 2}$ for $k \geq 3$ — non-cyclic for $k \geq 3$, in contrast to $(\mathbb{Z}/p^k)^\times$ which is cyclic for $p$ odd. This is the only family of exceptions in the cyclicity classification.

Rubric: full credit for the squaring computation, the order-determination of every unit, and the identification of the resulting $\mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2$ structure.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that if $g$ is a primitive root modulo an odd prime $p$, then either $g$ or $g + p$ is a primitive root modulo $p^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The lift from modulo $p$ to modulo $p^2$ is the simplest case of Hensel's lemma. The order of $g$ modulo $p^2$ is either $p - 1$ or $p(p - 1) = \varphi(p^2)$. Show that it is exactly $p(p - 1)$ unless $g^{p - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p^2}$ — in which case $(g + p)^{p - 1} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p^2}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $g$ be a primitive root modulo $p$. The order $d$ of $g$ modulo $p^2$ divides $\varphi(p^2) = p(p - 1)$. Reducing modulo $p$, $g^d \equiv 1 \pmod{p^2}$ forces $g^d \equiv 1 \pmod p$, so the order $p - 1$ of $g$ modulo $p$ divides $d$. The divisors of $p(p - 1)$ that are multiples of $p - 1$ are $p - 1$ and $p(p - 1)$, so $d \in \{p - 1, p(p - 1)\}$.

If $d = p(p - 1)$, then $g$ is already a primitive root modulo $p^2$.

If $d = p - 1$, i.e., $g^{p - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p^2}$, replace $g$ by $g' = g + p$. Then $g' \equiv g \pmod p$, so $g'$ is still a primitive root modulo $p$. Expand by the binomial theorem (using $p$ odd so $p - 1 \geq 2$):
$$
(g + p)^{p - 1} \;=\; g^{p - 1} + (p - 1) g^{p - 2} p + \binom{p - 1}{2} g^{p - 3} p^2 + \cdots.
$$
Modulo $p^2$, every term with $p^2$ or higher vanishes, so
$$
(g + p)^{p - 1} \;\equiv\; g^{p - 1} + (p - 1) g^{p - 2} p \;\equiv\; 1 - p g^{p - 2} \pmod{p^2}.
$$
Since $p \nmid g$ (as $\gcd(g, p) = 1$), $p \nmid g^{p - 2}$, so $p g^{p - 2} \not\equiv 0 \pmod{p^2}$. Hence $(g + p)^{p - 1} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p^2}$. The order of $g + p$ modulo $p^2$ is therefore $p(p - 1)$, and $g + p$ is a primitive root modulo $p^2$.

In either case, at least one of $g, g + p$ is a primitive root modulo $p^2$. This is the inductive step that lifts a primitive root modulo $p$ to a primitive root modulo $p^k$ for every $k \geq 1$, with $p$ odd.

Rubric: full credit for the $d \in \{p - 1, p(p - 1)\}$ dichotomy and the binomial-expansion lifting argument.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the full **structure theorem for $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$**: the unit group is cyclic if and only if $n \in \{1, 2, 4, p^k, 2 p^k\}$ for $p$ an odd prime and $k \geq 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use CRT to reduce to the prime-power case. The case $p$ odd uses Exercise 6 (Hensel lifting); the case $p = 2$ separates $k = 1, 2$ (cyclic) from $k \geq 3$ (Klein-times-cyclic structure). The "only if" direction: a CRT product of two non-identity cyclic groups with even orders is not cyclic.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By CRT [21.01.03], $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times \cong \prod_{p \mid n}(\mathbb{Z}/p^{e_p})^\times$ where $n = \prod p^{e_p}$.

**Step 1 — Prime powers $p$ odd.** By Exercise 6 and induction, $(\mathbb{Z}/p^k)^\times$ is cyclic of order $\varphi(p^k) = p^{k - 1}(p - 1)$ for every $p$ odd and $k \geq 1$.

**Step 2 — Powers of $2$.** $(\mathbb{Z}/2)^\times$ is the identity group (cyclic of order $1$). $(\mathbb{Z}/4)^\times = \{1, 3\}$ is cyclic of order $2$. For $k \geq 3$, claim $(\mathbb{Z}/2^k)^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2^{k - 2}$ with generators $-1$ and $5$.

The order of $-1$ modulo $2^k$ is $2$ for $k \geq 1$. The order of $5$ modulo $2^k$ is $2^{k - 2}$ for $k \geq 3$: by induction, $5^{2^{k - 2}} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^k}$ but $5^{2^{k - 3}} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{2^k}$. The subgroup generated by $5$ does not contain $-1$ because every power of $5$ is $\equiv 1 \pmod 4$ (as $5 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$), while $-1 \equiv 3 \pmod 4$. Hence the two subgroups intersect in only the identity, and the direct product has order $2 \cdot 2^{k - 2} = 2^{k - 1} = \varphi(2^k) = |(\mathbb{Z}/2^k)^\times|$.

So $(\mathbb{Z}/2^k)^\times$ is cyclic for $k \in \{1, 2\}$ and non-cyclic for $k \geq 3$.

**Step 3 — Cyclicity classification via CRT.** $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ is cyclic iff its CRT decomposition has at most one non-identity cyclic factor with even order. A product of two cyclic groups of orders $m_1, m_2$ is cyclic iff $\gcd(m_1, m_2) = 1$. The orders $\varphi(p^{e_p})$ for distinct prime powers are mostly even (every $\varphi(p^k) = p^{k - 1}(p - 1)$ is even for $p$ odd or for $p = 2, k \geq 2$); the only odd values are $\varphi(2) = 1$ and the degenerate $\varphi(1) = 1$.

So at most one factor of orders $\varphi(p^{e_p}) > 1$ can appear in the CRT decomposition without producing an even-even gcd. The allowed configurations:
- $n = 1$: cyclic by definition (the identity group).
- $n = 2$: cyclic by definition (order $1$).
- $n = 4$: cyclic of order $2$.
- $n = p^k$ for $p$ odd: cyclic by Step 1.
- $n = 2 p^k$ for $p$ odd: $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times \cong (\mathbb{Z}/2)^\times \times (\mathbb{Z}/p^k)^\times \cong \{1\} \times \mathbb{Z}/\varphi(p^k)$, cyclic.

Every other $n$ has at least two CRT factors with even orders $> 1$, and the product is therefore not cyclic.

Conversely, for $n \in \{1, 2, 4, p^k, 2 p^k\}$ with $p$ odd, the CRT decomposition produces a cyclic group, so $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ is cyclic.

Rubric: full credit for the three-step structure: prime-power cyclicity via Hensel lifting, the $2^k$ split, and the CRT-cyclicity gcd condition.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the **discrete-logarithm homomorphism**: for $p$ prime and $g$ a primitive root modulo $p$, the map $\mathrm{ind}_g : (\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times \to \mathbb{Z}/(p - 1)\mathbb{Z}$, $a \mapsto \mathrm{ind}_g(a)$, is a group isomorphism. Use it to solve the congruence $7^x \equiv 5 \pmod{11}$ by translating to a linear congruence modulo $10$, using the orbit table from the worked example.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the isomorphism, observe $g^{\mathrm{ind}(a)} \cdot g^{\mathrm{ind}(b)} = g^{\mathrm{ind}(a) + \mathrm{ind}(b)} = g^{\mathrm{ind}(ab)}$ as elements of $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$, then read off the additive structure modulo $p - 1$. For $7^x \equiv 5$ modulo $11$: take $\mathrm{ind}_2$ of both sides, using $\mathrm{ind}_2(7) = 7$ (from Exercise 3) and $\mathrm{ind}_2(5) = 4$ (from the orbit table).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Isomorphism.** Define $\mathrm{ind}_g : (\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times \to \mathbb{Z}/(p - 1)$ by sending $a$ to the unique $k \in \{0, 1, \ldots, p - 2\}$ with $g^k \equiv a \pmod p$. Well-defined because $g$ is a primitive root and $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times = \{g^0, g^1, \ldots, g^{p - 2}\}$.

Group homomorphism: $g^{\mathrm{ind}(a) + \mathrm{ind}(b)} = g^{\mathrm{ind}(a)} \cdot g^{\mathrm{ind}(b)} = a \cdot b$, so $\mathrm{ind}(ab) \equiv \mathrm{ind}(a) + \mathrm{ind}(b) \pmod{p - 1}$.

Injective because $\mathrm{ind}(a) = \mathrm{ind}(b)$ forces $g^{\mathrm{ind}(a)} = g^{\mathrm{ind}(b)}$, hence $a = b$.

Surjective because every $k \in \{0, 1, \ldots, p - 2\}$ is the index of $a = g^k$.

So $\mathrm{ind}_g$ is a group isomorphism $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathbb{Z}/(p - 1)\mathbb{Z}$.

**Solving $7^x \equiv 5 \pmod{11}$.** Take $\mathrm{ind}_2$ of both sides:
$$
\mathrm{ind}_2(7^x) \;\equiv\; \mathrm{ind}_2(5) \pmod{10}.
$$
By the homomorphism property and the orbit table from the worked example, $\mathrm{ind}_2(7^x) = x \cdot \mathrm{ind}_2(7) = 7 x$, and $\mathrm{ind}_2(5) = 4$. So
$$
7 x \;\equiv\; 4 \pmod{10}.
$$
Solve via extended Euclidean: $\gcd(7, 10) = 1$, and $7 \cdot 3 = 21 \equiv 1 \pmod{10}$, so $7^{-1} \equiv 3 \pmod{10}$. Multiplying:
$$
x \;\equiv\; 3 \cdot 4 \;=\; 12 \;\equiv\; 2 \pmod{10}.
$$
So $x \equiv 2 \pmod{10}$, with solutions $x = 2, 12, 22, \ldots$ Direct verification: $7^2 = 49 = 4 \cdot 11 + 5 \equiv 5 \pmod{11}$, confirming $x = 2$.

This is the discrete-logarithm reduction in operation: an exponential congruence in $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ becomes a linear congruence modulo $p - 1$. The same trick underlies the Pohlig-Hellman algorithm and the index-calculus methods of Theorem 5 below.

Rubric: full credit for the homomorphism verification and the index-translation solution $x = 2$.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (existence of primitive roots modulo every $n \in \{1, 2, 4, p^k, 2 p^k\}$; Gauss 1801).** *For $p$ an odd prime and $k \geq 1$, the group $(\mathbb{Z}/p^k)^\times$ is cyclic of order $\varphi(p^k) = p^{k - 1}(p - 1)$. The group $(\mathbb{Z}/2 p^k)^\times$ is cyclic of the same order $\varphi(2 p^k) = \varphi(p^k)$.* [ref: TODO_REF Gauss 1801]. The proof is Hensel lifting: a primitive root $g$ modulo $p$ (Key Theorem) lifts to a primitive root modulo $p^k$, with the explicit replacement $g \to g + p$ used when $g^{p - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p^2}$ (Exercise 6 above). The CRT decomposition $(\mathbb{Z}/2 p^k)^\times \cong (\mathbb{Z}/2)^\times \times (\mathbb{Z}/p^k)^\times \cong \{1\} \times \mathbb{Z}/\varphi(p^k)$ then makes $2 p^k$ cyclic at no extra cost. Cyclicity fails for $n = 2^k$ with $k \geq 3$ (Theorem 2), and for $n$ with at least two distinct odd-prime factors or with $4 \mid n$ and another odd-prime factor — these are the only obstructions. Gauss 1801 arts. 53-89 give the entire classification with constructive proofs.

**Theorem 2 (structure of $(\mathbb{Z}/2^k)^\times$ for $k \geq 3$).** *For $k \geq 3$, $(\mathbb{Z}/2^k\mathbb{Z})^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}/2^{k - 2}\mathbb{Z}$, generated by $-1$ (order $2$) and $5$ (order $2^{k - 2}$).* The first factor is the kernel of reduction modulo $4$ (so $-1$ generates it); the second is generated by $5$, with $5^{2^{k - 2}} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^k}$ but $5^{2^{k - 3}} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{2^k}$ (verifiable by induction with $5 = 1 + 4$ and $(1 + 4)^{2^j}$ expansions). The non-cyclicity for $k \geq 3$ is the unique structural exception to the Gauss cyclicity classification and the operational reason that the Carmichael function $\lambda(2^k) = 2^{k - 2}$ is strictly smaller than $\varphi(2^k) = 2^{k - 1}$ for $k \geq 3$. See Hardy-Wright 2008 §VI.10 [ref: TODO_REF Hardy-Wright 2008] for the standard induction.

**Theorem 3 (Artin's primitive-root conjecture; Artin 1927, Hooley 1967).** *Let $a$ be an integer that is not $-1$ and not a perfect square. Then $a$ is a primitive root modulo $p$ for infinitely many primes $p$, with density*
$$
\delta(a) \;=\; A \cdot \prod_p \left(1 - \frac{1}{p(p - 1)}\right) \;=\; A \cdot 0.3739558\ldots \,,
$$
*where $A$ is a rational correction factor depending on the squarefree part of $a$.* [ref: TODO_REF Artin 1927; Hooley 1967]. The constant $C_A = \prod_p (1 - 1/(p(p - 1))) = 0.3739558...$ is the **Artin constant**. Artin 1927 formulated the conjecture in a letter to Hasse based on heuristic considerations: $a$ is a primitive root modulo $p$ iff for every prime $q \mid p - 1$, $a$ is not a $q$-th power modulo $p$; under independence assumptions, this has probability $\prod_q (1 - 1/q) \cdot (q/(q - 1))$ corrections.

Hooley 1967 *J. reine angew. Math.* 225, 209 [ref: TODO_REF Hooley 1967] proved Artin's conjecture conditionally on the generalised Riemann hypothesis for the Dedekind zeta functions of the Kummer-cyclotomic fields $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_q, a^{1/q})$, using Chebotarev density theorem to count primes by splitting. The unconditional progress: Gupta-Murty 1984 *Invent. Math.* 78, 127 [ref: TODO_REF Gupta-Murty 1984] proved that infinitely many integers $a$ have a positive density of primitive-root primes; Heath-Brown 1986 *Quart. J. Math.* (2) 37, 27 [ref: TODO_REF Heath-Brown 1986] proved that among any three multiplicatively independent integers, at least one is a primitive root modulo infinitely many primes — in particular at least one of $2, 3, 5$ is.

**Theorem 4 (baby-step giant-step algorithm for the discrete logarithm; Shanks 1971).** *In a cyclic group $G$ of order $N$ with generator $g$, the discrete logarithm $\mathrm{ind}_g(a) \in \mathbb{Z}/N$ can be computed in time and space $O(\sqrt N)$ via baby-step giant-step.* [ref: TODO_REF Shanks 1971]. The algorithm: set $m = \lceil \sqrt N \rceil$; precompute and store the **baby steps** $\{(g^j, j) : 0 \leq j < m\}$ in a hash table; for $i = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, m$ compute the **giant step** $a \cdot g^{-i m}$ and check whether it appears in the hash table — if $g^j = a \cdot g^{-i m}$, then $\mathrm{ind}_g(a) = i m + j$. Time and space $\tilde O(\sqrt N)$. Combined with Pohlig-Hellman (Theorem 5), this is the best classical generic algorithm for DLP in groups whose order has no large prime factor.

**Theorem 5 (Pohlig-Hellman algorithm; Pohlig-Hellman 1978).** *Let $G$ be a cyclic group of order $N = \prod_i q_i^{e_i}$. The discrete logarithm in $G$ reduces by CRT to the discrete logarithm in each cyclic subgroup of order $q_i^{e_i}$, each solvable by baby-step giant-step in $O(e_i \sqrt{q_i})$ steps. Total complexity: $O(\sum_i e_i (\log N + \sqrt{q_i}))$.* [ref: TODO_REF Pohlig-Hellman 1978]. Practical consequence: a generic DLP in a group of order $N$ with a large prime factor $q \mid N$ requires at least $O(\sqrt q)$ work, so secure cryptographic groups choose $N$ with a single large prime factor — the **safe prime** condition $p = 2 q + 1$ for $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$, or pairing-friendly curves for elliptic-curve DLP. RFC 3526 standardises safe primes for Diffie-Hellman.

**Theorem 6 (index-calculus method for DLP in $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$; Adleman 1979).** *There exists a probabilistic sub-exponential algorithm for the discrete logarithm in $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ running in time $L_p[1/2, c]$, refined to $L_p[1/3, c]$ via the number-field sieve, where $L_p[\alpha, c] = \exp(c (\log p)^\alpha (\log \log p)^{1 - \alpha})$* [ref: TODO_REF Adleman 1979]. The algorithm exploits the multiplicative structure of $\mathbb{Z}$: fix a smoothness bound $B$ and a factor base $\mathcal{F} = \{p_1, \ldots, p_k\}$ of primes $\leq B$; for random exponents $r$, factor $g^r \bmod p$ over $\mathcal{F}$ (keeping only the smooth values); each successful factorisation $g^r \equiv \prod_j p_j^{e_j} \pmod p$ gives a linear relation $r \equiv \sum_j e_j \mathrm{ind}_g(p_j) \pmod{p - 1}$ in the unknowns $\mathrm{ind}_g(p_j)$. After collecting $\geq k$ linearly independent relations, solve the linear system. Then compute $\mathrm{ind}_g(a)$ via one more smooth-factorisation step. The number-field sieve adaptation [Gordon 1993 *SIAM J. Discrete Math.* 6, 124] reaches $L_p[1/3, c]$ asymptotic complexity. Pre-computation makes $1024$-bit DH insecure (the Logjam attack of Adrian et al. 2015 *CCS* 2015), motivating the $\geq 2048$-bit modern minimum.

**Theorem 7 (Shor's quantum polynomial-time DLP; Shor 1994/1997).** *There exists a quantum algorithm computing the discrete logarithm in any cyclic group of order $N$ in time $O((\log N)^3)$ on a quantum computer, given oracle access to the group operation* [ref: TODO_REF Shor 1994]. The algorithm uses the quantum Fourier transform over $\mathbb{Z}/N$ to extract the period of the function $f(x, y) = g^x a^{-y}$, whose period along the line $g^x = a^y$ encodes $\mathrm{ind}_g(a)$. The same period-finding subroutine factors integers via the order $\mathrm{ord}_N(a)$ of a random element. Polynomial-time quantum factoring breaks RSA; polynomial-time quantum DLP breaks Diffie-Hellman, ElGamal, DSA, ECDH, ECDSA. Motivates the entire post-quantum cryptography programme: NIST PQC standardisation 2016-2024, with the first standards FIPS 203 (ML-KEM / Kyber, lattice-based key encapsulation) and FIPS 204 (ML-DSA / Dilithium, lattice-based signatures) announced August 2024.

**Theorem 8 (Lucas primality test; Lucas 1878).** *An integer $n > 1$ is prime if and only if there exists $a$ with $a^{n - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod n$ and $a^{(n - 1)/q} \not\equiv 1 \pmod n$ for every prime $q \mid n - 1$.* [ref: TODO_REF Lucas 1878]. The condition is exactly that $a$ has order $n - 1$ in $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$, hence $|(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times| \geq n - 1$, hence $|(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times| = n - 1$ (the unit group has at most $n - 1$ elements), which forces every residue $1, 2, \ldots, n - 1$ to be a unit, forcing $n$ to be prime. The certificate $a$ (a primitive root witness) gives a deterministic polynomial-time-verifiable proof of primality — Pratt 1975 *SIAM J. Comput.* 4, 214 showed that the recursive certificate (a primitive root for $n$, plus primality certificates for each $q \mid n - 1$) has polynomial size, putting PRIMES in NP. The Lucas-Lehmer specialisation to Mersenne primes $M_p = 2^p - 1$ is the algorithm used by GIMPS to discover the largest known primes (currently $2^{82{,}589{,}933} - 1$, discovered 2018).

**Synthesis.** The primitive-root framework is the foundational reason that the multiplicative structure of $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ admits a clean discrete-logarithm identification with $\mathbb{Z}/\varphi(n)$ — when it does, and the obstruction $n \notin \{1, 2, 4, p^k, 2 p^k\}$ is precisely when the unit group fails to be cyclic and a single primitive root is unavailable. The central insight is exactly that the field property of $\mathbb{F}_p$ supplies the polynomial-degree bound $|x^d - 1 \text{ roots}| \leq d$, which combined with the totient summation $\sum_{d \mid m} \varphi(d) = m$ forces every divisor of $p - 1$ to have exactly $\varphi(d)$ elements of order $d$ — and the unique $d = p - 1$ branch produces $\varphi(p - 1) > 0$ primitive roots. The bridge to [01.02.18] finite fields is this very argument: every $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ is cyclic of order $q - 1$ without exception, and the failure-mode $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ non-cyclic for non-prime-power $n$ traces to $\mathbb{Z}/n$ not being a field.

The applied half of the unit is dominated by the discrete-logarithm problem. Putting these together with the Pohlig-Hellman reduction and the baby-step giant-step + index-calculus + number-field-sieve hierarchy, the best classical DLP algorithm in $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ for safe-prime $p$ runs in $L_p[1/3, c]$ sub-exponential time, fast enough to break $1024$-bit Diffie-Hellman in pre-computation (Logjam 2015) but slow enough to keep $2048$-bit secure against classical attacks for the foreseeable future. The pattern recurs in elliptic-curve cryptography: DLP in $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ has no known sub-exponential classical attack (no index-calculus analogue), allowing $256$-bit keys to provide $128$-bit security — the modern default in TLS 1.3 and Signal. Shor 1994's quantum polynomial-time DLP identifies all these classical hardness assumptions with a single quantum-Fourier-transform period-finding routine, and the post-quantum transition (NIST FIPS 203 + 204, 2024) replaces the discrete-log + factoring hardness assumptions with lattice-shortest-vector + module-LWE hardness assumptions whose quantum complexity is conjecturally still exponential.

The historical bridge connects 1801 Gauss to 1976 Diffie-Hellman to 1994 Shor in a single arc. Gauss's classification of cyclic $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ identifies the moduli for which the discrete logarithm is well-defined; Diffie-Hellman's 1976 *IEEE Trans. Inform. Theory* paper repurposed the index calculus as the cornerstone of the first public-key cryptosystem; Lucas's 1878 primality test built on the same primitive-root structure as the first deterministic certificate scheme; the entire post-1976 cryptographic ecosystem inherited the cyclic-group framework, with Artin's 1927 conjecture identifying the candidate primitive roots and Hooley 1967 the conditional density. The bridge is everywhere the same: a single generator of the cyclic unit group converts modular arithmetic into discrete additive arithmetic modulo $\varphi(n)$, and every cryptographic protocol since 1976 has rested on the asymmetry between forward exponentiation (fast) and reverse logarithm extraction (slow).

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (cyclicity of $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$).** *For every prime $p$, $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ is cyclic of order $p - 1$.*

*Proof.* Reprised from the Key Theorem with sharpened notation. Let $G = (\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$. For each $d \mid p - 1$ let $N(d) = \#\{x \in G : \mathrm{ord}(x) = d\}$. The element orders divide $|G|$ by Lagrange, so
$$
\sum_{d \mid p - 1} N(d) = p - 1. \tag{1}
$$

An element $x$ has $\mathrm{ord}(x) \mid d$ iff $x^d = 1$, i.e., $x$ is a root of $T^d - 1 \in \mathbb{F}_p[T]$. By the field property of $\mathbb{F}_p$, this polynomial has at most $d$ roots in $\mathbb{F}_p$, so $\sum_{e \mid d} N(e) \leq d$.

Suppose $N(d) > 0$ with witness $x$ of order $d$. The cyclic subgroup $\langle x \rangle$ has $d$ elements, all satisfying $y^d = 1$, so $\langle x \rangle$ accounts for the full root set of $T^d - 1$ — equality $\sum_{e \mid d} N(e) = d$. Among the $d$ elements of $\langle x \rangle$, the number of order exactly $d$ is the number of $k$ with $\gcd(k, d) = 1$, namely $\varphi(d)$.

So $N(d) \in \{0, \varphi(d)\}$ for every $d \mid p - 1$. Combined with (1) and $\sum_{d \mid m} \varphi(d) = m$:
$$
p - 1 = \sum_{d \mid p - 1} N(d) \leq \sum_{d \mid p - 1} \varphi(d) = p - 1.
$$
Equality forces $N(d) = \varphi(d)$ for every $d$, in particular $N(p - 1) = \varphi(p - 1) \geq 1$. A primitive root exists; $G$ is cyclic. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Hensel lift to $(\mathbb{Z}/p^k)^\times$).** *For $p$ an odd prime and $k \geq 1$, $(\mathbb{Z}/p^k)^\times$ is cyclic of order $\varphi(p^k) = p^{k - 1}(p - 1)$.*

*Proof.* By Proposition 1 there exists a primitive root $g$ modulo $p$. By Exercise 6 above, either $g$ or $g + p$ is a primitive root modulo $p^2$; without loss assume $g$ itself is.

Induction on $k$. For the step $k \to k + 1$: assume $g$ has order exactly $\varphi(p^k) = p^{k - 1}(p - 1)$ modulo $p^k$, with $k \geq 2$. The order of $g$ modulo $p^{k + 1}$ divides $\varphi(p^{k + 1}) = p^k(p - 1)$, and reducing modulo $p^k$ shows that $\varphi(p^k) = p^{k - 1}(p - 1)$ divides this order. So the order modulo $p^{k + 1}$ is either $p^{k - 1}(p - 1)$ or $p^k(p - 1)$.

Suppose for contradiction the order is the smaller value $p^{k - 1}(p - 1)$. Then $g^{p^{k - 1}(p - 1)} \equiv 1 \pmod{p^{k + 1}}$. By induction hypothesis $g^{p^{k - 2}(p - 1)} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p^k}$ (it has order exactly $p^{k - 1}(p - 1)$ modulo $p^k$), so $g^{p^{k - 2}(p - 1)} = 1 + c p^{k - 1}$ with $\gcd(c, p) = 1$. Raise to the $p$-th power and expand:
$$
g^{p^{k - 1}(p - 1)} = (1 + c p^{k - 1})^p = 1 + p \cdot c p^{k - 1} + \binom{p}{2} (c p^{k - 1})^2 + \cdots.
$$
The second term is $c p^k$; subsequent terms have $p^{2k - 1}$ or higher. Since $p$ is odd and $k \geq 2$, $2k - 1 \geq k + 1$, so modulo $p^{k + 1}$:
$$
g^{p^{k - 1}(p - 1)} \equiv 1 + c p^k \pmod{p^{k + 1}}.
$$
Since $\gcd(c, p) = 1$, $c p^k \not\equiv 0 \pmod{p^{k + 1}}$, so $g^{p^{k - 1}(p - 1)} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{p^{k + 1}}$ — contradiction. Hence the order is $p^k(p - 1) = \varphi(p^{k + 1})$, and $g$ is a primitive root modulo $p^{k + 1}$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (structure of $(\mathbb{Z}/2^k)^\times$ for $k \geq 3$).** *For $k \geq 3$, $(\mathbb{Z}/2^k)^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2^{k - 2}$, generated by $-1$ and $5$.*

*Proof.* The order of $-1$ modulo $2^k$ is $2$ for every $k \geq 1$. Claim the order of $5$ modulo $2^k$ is $2^{k - 2}$ for $k \geq 3$, by induction.

Base case $k = 3$: $5^2 = 25 = 3 \cdot 8 + 1 \equiv 1 \pmod 8$, so the order of $5$ modulo $8$ divides $2$. Also $5 \not\equiv 1 \pmod 8$, so the order is exactly $2 = 2^{3 - 2}$.

Step $k \to k + 1$ for $k \geq 3$: assume $5^{2^{k - 2}} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^k}$ and $5^{2^{k - 3}} \not\equiv 1 \pmod{2^k}$. Write $5^{2^{k - 2}} = 1 + a \cdot 2^k$ for some integer $a$. Square:
$$
5^{2^{k - 1}} = (1 + a \cdot 2^k)^2 = 1 + 2 a \cdot 2^k + a^2 \cdot 2^{2k} \equiv 1 + a \cdot 2^{k + 1} \pmod{2^{k + 2}}.
$$
So $5^{2^{k - 1}} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{k + 1}}$ iff $a \cdot 2^{k + 1} \equiv 0 \pmod{2^{k + 1}}$, which holds unconditionally. So $5^{2^{k - 1}} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{k + 1}}$, i.e., the order of $5$ modulo $2^{k + 1}$ divides $2^{k - 1}$. Strict-or-equal: suppose for contradiction $5^{2^{k - 2}} \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{k + 1}}$. Then $1 + a \cdot 2^k \equiv 1 \pmod{2^{k + 1}}$, forcing $a \equiv 0 \pmod 2$. But then $5^{2^{k - 3}} \equiv \pm 1 + (a/2) \cdot 2^{k - 1}$ (square-root analysis) lands inside $1 + 2^{k - 1} \mathbb{Z}$ already at $k - 1$, contradicting the inductive non-equality. Hence the order modulo $2^{k + 1}$ is exactly $2^{k - 1} = 2^{(k + 1) - 2}$.

So $\langle 5 \rangle \subseteq (\mathbb{Z}/2^k)^\times$ is cyclic of order $2^{k - 2}$ for $k \geq 3$. The subgroup $\langle -1 \rangle$ has order $2$.

Intersection: every power of $5$ is $\equiv 1 \pmod 4$ (since $5 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ and powers preserve residues mod $4$), while $-1 \equiv 3 \pmod 4$. So $\langle 5 \rangle \cap \langle -1 \rangle = \{1\}$.

Product: $|\langle -1 \rangle \cdot \langle 5 \rangle| = |\langle -1 \rangle| \cdot |\langle 5 \rangle| = 2 \cdot 2^{k - 2} = 2^{k - 1} = \varphi(2^k) = |(\mathbb{Z}/2^k)^\times|$. So the product subgroup equals the full unit group.

Hence $(\mathbb{Z}/2^k)^\times \cong \langle -1 \rangle \times \langle 5 \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2^{k - 2}$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (discrete-logarithm isomorphism).** *For $n \in \{1, 2, 4, p^k, 2 p^k\}$ with $p$ an odd prime, fix a primitive root $g$ modulo $n$. The map $\mathrm{ind}_g : (\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times \to \mathbb{Z}/\varphi(n)\mathbb{Z}$, $a \mapsto \mathrm{ind}_g(a)$, is a group isomorphism.*

*Proof.* For $a \in (\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$, $g$ generates the unit group, so there exists a unique $k \in \{0, 1, \ldots, \varphi(n) - 1\}$ with $g^k \equiv a \pmod n$. This defines $\mathrm{ind}_g(a) = k$.

Group homomorphism: $\mathrm{ind}_g(ab) \equiv \mathrm{ind}_g(a) + \mathrm{ind}_g(b) \pmod{\varphi(n)}$ because $g^{\mathrm{ind}(a) + \mathrm{ind}(b)} = g^{\mathrm{ind}(a)} \cdot g^{\mathrm{ind}(b)} = a \cdot b = g^{\mathrm{ind}(ab)}$, and $g$-exponentials are equal modulo $n$ iff exponents are congruent modulo $\varphi(n)$.

Injective: $\mathrm{ind}_g(a) = \mathrm{ind}_g(b)$ gives $a = g^{\mathrm{ind}(a)} = g^{\mathrm{ind}(b)} = b$.

Surjective: $k \in \mathbb{Z}/\varphi(n)$ is the index of $a = g^k$.

So $\mathrm{ind}_g$ is a bijective homomorphism, hence an isomorphism. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Fermat-Euler-Wilson theorems [21.01.04].** Just shipped. The order-divides-group-order fact underlying Fermat ($a^{p - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod p$) and Euler ($a^{\varphi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n$) is sharpened in the present unit to the existence of an element of order exactly $\varphi(n)$ when $n$ has the right shape — a primitive root. The Key Theorem here builds on the Lagrange-divides framework of [21.01.04] and the totient identity $\sum_{d \mid m} \varphi(d) = m$ that appears in the totient calculus of [21.01.03]. The Carmichael function $\lambda(n)$ from [21.01.04] is the tight exponent of $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ and equals $\varphi(n)$ exactly when a primitive root exists — the cyclic case.

- **Congruences and the Chinese remainder theorem [21.01.03].** Shipped. The CRT decomposition $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times \cong \prod_p (\mathbb{Z}/p^{e_p})^\times$ is the operational tool that reduces the cyclicity classification to prime powers (Theorem 1, Theorem 2), and the discrete-logarithm Pohlig-Hellman reduction (Theorem 5) is essentially CRT applied to the cyclic-group order $\varphi(n)$. The structure theorem for $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ proved here gives the explicit CRT factor decomposition: the cyclic factors $\mathbb{Z}/\varphi(p^{e_p})$ for $p$ odd, the Klein-times-cyclic factor for $p = 2, k \geq 3$.

- **Primes and the fundamental theorem of arithmetic [21.01.02].** Shipped. The classification of $n$ admitting primitive roots — $n \in \{1, 2, 4, p^k, 2 p^k\}$ — is stated in terms of the prime factorisation of $n$ supplied by the fundamental theorem of arithmetic. Lucas's 1878 primality test (Theorem 8) uses primitive-root certificates to give a deterministic primality proof, refining the Fermat test of [21.01.04]; Pratt 1975 showed the resulting recursive certificate has polynomial size, placing PRIMES in NP. The Lucas-Lehmer specialisation to Mersenne primes is the algorithm underlying every record-holding prime since 1952.

- **Quadratic residues and reciprocity [21.01.06].** Pending. A nonzero residue $a$ is a quadratic residue modulo a prime $p$ iff $\mathrm{ind}_g(a)$ is even for any primitive root $g$ modulo $p$. The squaring map $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times \to (\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$, $x \mapsto x^2$, has kernel $\{\pm 1\}$ and image of index $2$; the image is exactly the subgroup of quadratic residues. Euler's criterion $a^{(p - 1)/2} \equiv \pm 1 \pmod p$ for the Legendre symbol is the primitive-root identification $\mathrm{ind}_g(a) \cdot (p - 1)/2 \pmod{p - 1}$. Gauss's quadratic reciprocity (1801, six independent proofs) builds on the primitive-root framework as its operational substrate.

- **Group [01.02.01].** Shipped. The proofs of the cyclicity classification rest on the standard finite-group structure theorems: Lagrange's theorem (order divides group order), the cyclic-group classification (subgroups of cyclic groups are cyclic, generators are $g^k$ for $\gcd(k, \mathrm{ord}(g)) = 1$), CRT for products of finite cyclic groups (cyclic iff orders are pairwise coprime). The discrete-logarithm isomorphism $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}/\varphi(n)$ for cyclic $n$ is the prototype of every isomorphism from a finite cyclic group to its additive realisation $\mathbb{Z}/N$.

- **Finite fields $\mathbb{F}_q$ and Frobenius [01.02.18].** Pending. Every finite subgroup of the multiplicative group of a field is cyclic — a generalisation of the prime-case Key Theorem proved here. In particular $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ is cyclic of order $q - 1$ for every prime power $q$, without the moduli restriction that holds for $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ (since $\mathbb{F}_q$ is a field but $\mathbb{Z}/n$ for non-prime-power $n$ is not). A generator of $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ is also called a primitive root (or primitive element) of $\mathbb{F}_q$, and the construction of $\mathbb{F}_q$ via irreducible polynomials of degree $\log_p q$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$ uses primitive-root existence implicitly. The Frobenius endomorphism $\mathrm{Frob}_p : \mathbb{F}_q \to \mathbb{F}_q$, $x \mapsto x^p$, generates the cyclic Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_q / \mathbb{F}_p)$, and the discrete-logarithm framework lifts to finite-field DLP — the basis of pairing-based cryptography and Schoof's elliptic-curve point-counting algorithm.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Gauss 1801 *Disquisitiones Arithmeticae* arts. 53-94 [ref: TODO_REF Gauss 1801] is the canonical originator of the primitive-root theory. Articles 53-54 define the order of a residue modulo $p$ — Gauss writes "the period of $a$" — and observe that the period divides $p - 1$ via the geometric-series argument $1 + a + a^2 + \cdots + a^{p - 2} \equiv 0 \pmod p$. Articles 55-56 prove the polynomial-root bound: $x^d - 1$ has at most $d$ roots modulo a prime, a consequence Gauss attributes to himself but which appears earlier in Lagrange 1768 in a less general form. Articles 57-72 give the existence proof of primitive roots modulo every odd prime via the divisor-counting argument of the Key Theorem above. Articles 73-89 lift the existence to odd prime powers $p^k$ and to $2 p^k$, classifying the moduli admitting primitive roots — and showing $(\mathbb{Z}/2^k)^\times$ is the lone non-cyclic case among prime powers for $k \geq 3$. Articles 90-94 develop the **index calculus** (Gauss's term for the discrete logarithm) as a working tool: a primitive root $g$ modulo $p$ gives a table of indices $\mathrm{ind}_g(a)$ for every $a$, reducing multiplicative computations modulo $p$ to additive computations modulo $p - 1$. Gauss exhibits explicit tables for $p \leq 100$ and uses them in the proofs of quadratic and biquadratic reciprocity.

The applied legacy is the discrete-logarithm problem. The earliest substantive DLP algorithm is the **baby-step giant-step** method of Shanks 1971 *Proc. Symp. Pure Math.* 20, 415 [ref: TODO_REF Shanks 1971], originally introduced for class-group computations. Pohlig-Hellman 1978 *IEEE Trans. Inform. Theory* IT-24, 106 [ref: TODO_REF Pohlig-Hellman 1978] showed that for $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ with $p - 1 = \prod q_i^{e_i}$, the DLP reduces by CRT to subgroup DLPs of order $q_i^{e_i}$, each solvable by Shanks in $O(e_i \sqrt{q_i})$ steps. The Adleman 1979 *FOCS 20*, 55 [ref: TODO_REF Adleman 1979] index-calculus method achieves sub-exponential complexity $L_p[1/2, c]$ via factor-base smooth-relation collection; the number-field sieve adaptation (Gordon 1993, Joux-Lercier 2003) refines this to $L_p[1/3, c]$. The 2015 **Logjam attack** (Adrian et al. *CCS* 2015) used pre-computed $1024$-bit safe-prime DH discrete logs to break TLS connections in real time, establishing $2048$-bit as the modern Diffie-Hellman minimum.

The 1976 invention of public-key cryptography by Diffie-Hellman 1976 *IEEE Trans. Inform. Theory* IT-22, 644 [ref: TODO_REF Diffie-Hellman 1976] is the watershed moment in modern applied cryptography, and rests entirely on the discrete-logarithm asymmetry in $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$. Alice and Bob agree on a large prime $p$ and a primitive root $g$; Alice publishes $A = g^a \bmod p$, Bob publishes $B = g^b \bmod p$; the shared secret is $K = g^{ab} = A^b = B^a \bmod p$, computable by either party from one secret and the other's public value, but apparently uncomputable by an eavesdropper who sees only $(g, p, A, B)$ — the "computational Diffie-Hellman" assumption. ElGamal 1985 *IEEE Trans. Inform. Theory* IT-31, 469 [ref: TODO_REF ElGamal 1985] extended the framework to public-key encryption and signatures; the U.S. **Digital Signature Algorithm** (NIST FIPS PUB 186, 1991) is the standardised ElGamal-signature variant; elliptic-curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH) and ECDSA (NIST FIPS PUB 186-2, 2000) replace $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ by an elliptic-curve group $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ with no known sub-exponential DLP algorithm. Every TLS handshake, every Signal Protocol initial key exchange, every Tor circuit setup uses some descendant of Diffie-Hellman 1976.

The unconditional density of primitive-root primes is the open conjecture of **Artin 1927** [ref: TODO_REF Artin 1927], communicated in a letter to Hasse: every integer $a$ that is not $-1$ and not a perfect square is a primitive root modulo infinitely many primes, with positive density. The conjectured density is the Artin constant $A = \prod_p (1 - 1/(p(p - 1))) = 0.3739558\ldots$ (corrected by a rational factor for $a$ a power), derived from independence heuristics: $a$ is a primitive root modulo $p$ iff for every prime $q \mid p - 1$, $a$ is not a $q$-th power modulo $p$. Hooley 1967 *J. reine angew. Math.* 225, 209 [ref: TODO_REF Hooley 1967] proved Artin's conjecture conditional on the generalised Riemann hypothesis applied to the Kummer-cyclotomic fields $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_q, a^{1/q})$. Gupta-Murty 1984 *Invent. Math.* 78, 127 [ref: TODO_REF Gupta-Murty 1984] gave the first unconditional positive-density result: there are infinitely many $a$ for which $a$ has a positive density of primitive-root primes. Heath-Brown 1986 *Quart. J. Math.* (2) 37, 27 [ref: TODO_REF Heath-Brown 1986] proved that among any three multiplicatively independent rationals, at least one satisfies Artin's conjecture — in particular at least one of $\{2, 3, 5\}$ is a primitive root modulo infinitely many primes, but no specific witness is known unconditionally.

The quantum-computing future is Shor 1994 *Proc. FOCS 35*, 124 [ref: TODO_REF Shor 1994] (expanded as Shor 1997 *SIAM J. Comput.* 26, 1484): polynomial-time quantum algorithms for both factoring and the discrete logarithm. The core subroutine is **quantum period-finding** via the quantum Fourier transform over $\mathbb{Z}/N$: the function $x \mapsto a^x \bmod N$ is periodic with period $\mathrm{ord}_N(a)$, and the QFT extracts this period in $O((\log N)^3)$ quantum gates. Combined with continued-fraction post-processing, this gives polynomial-time integer factoring (via the order $\mathrm{ord}_N(a)$ of a random $a$) and polynomial-time DLP (via the period of $f(x, y) = g^x a^{-y}$). On a sufficiently large fault-tolerant quantum computer, Shor's algorithm would break RSA, Diffie-Hellman, ElGamal, DSA, ECDH, ECDSA — the entire post-1976 public-key cryptographic infrastructure. NIST's Post-Quantum Cryptography standardisation process (2016-2024) culminated in FIPS 203 (ML-KEM / Kyber, lattice-based key encapsulation) and FIPS 204 (ML-DSA / Dilithium, lattice-based signatures) announced August 2024, with FIPS 205 (SLH-DSA / SPHINCS+, hash-based signatures) as a backup; the underlying hard problems (module-LWE, module-SIS, hash collisions) replace the discrete-log + factoring framework but inherit the same cyclic-group / discrete-additive-structure backbone that Gauss 1801 first identified.
