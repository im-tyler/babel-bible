## Intuition [Beginner]

A surprising regularity in modular arithmetic: whenever you raise a number to a carefully chosen power and then read the answer modulo a prime, you always land on $1$. This is **Fermat's little theorem**, discovered by Pierre de Fermat in 1640. For a prime $p$ and any integer $a$ that is not a multiple of $p$, raising $a$ to the power $p - 1$ and reading the answer modulo $p$ gives $1$ — every time, without exception.

The cleanest illustration uses $p = 7$. Take $a = 3$. Fermat predicts $3^6 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$. Compute: $3^2 = 9 \equiv 2 \pmod 7$; $3^4 = 81 \equiv 4 \pmod 7$ (since $81 = 11 \times 7 + 4$); $3^6 = 3^4 \times 3^2 \equiv 4 \times 2 = 8 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$. The theorem holds. Try $a = 5$: $5^2 = 25 \equiv 4 \pmod 7$, $5^4 \equiv 16 \equiv 2 \pmod 7$, $5^6 \equiv 2 \times 4 = 8 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$. Still works. The pattern is universal for every nonzero residue.

**Euler's theorem** is the generalisation to composite moduli. For an arbitrary positive integer $n$, write $\varphi(n)$ for the count of integers in $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ that share no common factor with $n$. The function $\varphi$ is the **Euler totient** introduced in [21.01.03]. Then for every integer $a$ coprime to $n$, $a^{\varphi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n$. The prime case $n = p$ recovers Fermat: $\varphi(p) = p - 1$, so Euler's theorem specialises to $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p$.

A second surprise, due to John Wilson 1770 and proved by Joseph-Louis Lagrange 1771: the product of all integers from $1$ to $p - 1$ — what is called $(p - 1)!$ — leaves remainder $-1$ when divided by $p$, whenever $p$ is prime. For $p = 5$: $4! = 24 = 5 \times 5 - 1 \equiv -1 \pmod 5$. For $p = 7$: $6! = 720 = 103 \times 7 - 1 \equiv -1 \pmod 7$. **Wilson's theorem** is even more striking because the converse also holds: if $(n - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod n$, then $n$ must be prime. So Wilson gives an exact primality criterion — at least in principle.

The applied importance of these theorems is hard to overstate. Every secure web connection routes through Euler's theorem. The RSA cryptosystem, invented by Rivest-Shamir-Adleman in 1978, encrypts a message $M$ by computing $C = M^e \bmod N$ where $N = pq$ is the product of two large primes. The decryption $M = C^d \bmod N$ works because $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\varphi(N)}$, so $M^{ed} \equiv M \pmod N$ by Euler — without the theorem, RSA would not exist. Primality testing — the Fermat test, Miller-Rabin, AKS — also begins with Fermat's little theorem as the entry point: if $a^{n-1} \not\equiv 1 \pmod n$ for some $a$ coprime to $n$, then $n$ is composite.

## Visual [Beginner]

A picture of a circular clock with $7$ positions, labelled $0$ through $6$. Six arrows trace the orbit of $3$ under repeated multiplication modulo $7$: $3^1 = 3$ at position $3$; $3^2 = 9 \equiv 2$ at position $2$; $3^3 \equiv 6$ at position $6$; $3^4 \equiv 4$ at position $4$; $3^5 \equiv 5$ at position $5$; $3^6 \equiv 1$ at position $1$. The orbit visits every nonzero position exactly once and returns to $1$ after $6$ steps. The arrow from $3^5$ to $3^6$ closes the loop.

![A clock with 7 positions and arrows showing the orbit of 3 under repeated multiplication modulo 7: position 3, then 2, then 6, then 4, then 5, then back to 1 — six steps that visit every nonzero residue exactly once, illustrating Fermat's little theorem.](/img/fermat-euler-wilson-theorems-placeholder.svg)

The picture captures the central fact. The nonzero residues modulo a prime form a cyclic structure under multiplication, and any element raised to the size of that structure returns to the identity $1$. The visualised orbit of $3$ modulo $7$ has length exactly $6 = p - 1$, but for some elements the orbit is shorter: $2$ modulo $7$ has orbit length $3$ (since $2^3 = 8 \equiv 1$), and the orbit length always divides $p - 1$. The theorem is the rigorous statement of this cyclic-return phenomenon.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute $2^{10}$ modulo $11$ and verify Fermat's little theorem for $p = 11$, $a = 2$.

Step 1. Fermat predicts $2^{10} \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$, because $11$ is prime and $\gcd(2, 11) = 1$.

Step 2. Compute the powers of $2$ modulo $11$ by successive squaring: $2^2 = 4$; $2^4 = 16 = 11 + 5 \equiv 5 \pmod{11}$; $2^8 \equiv 5^2 = 25 = 2 \times 11 + 3 \equiv 3 \pmod{11}$.

Step 3. Combine: $2^{10} = 2^8 \times 2^2 \equiv 3 \times 4 = 12 = 11 + 1 \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$.

Step 4. The prediction is confirmed: $2^{10} = 1024 = 93 \times 11 + 1$, so $2^{10} \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$ as Fermat said.

What this tells us: once we know Fermat's little theorem, we never need to compute $2^{10}$ directly to know its residue modulo $11$ — the answer is $1$ by the theorem alone. The same principle lets us compute $2^{1000}$ modulo $11$ at a glance: $1000 = 100 \times 10$, so $2^{1000} = (2^{10})^{100} \equiv 1^{100} = 1 \pmod{11}$. Modular exponentiation collapses to almost nothing once the cyclic-return structure is known.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The Fermat-Euler-Wilson theorems live inside the unit-group structure of $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ developed in [21.01.03]. We restate the definitions in their canonical form and then prove the three signature theorems.

**Definition (order of an element).** Let $G$ be a finite group with identity $e$ and let $g \in G$. The **order** of $g$, written $\mathrm{ord}(g)$, is the least positive integer $k$ such that $g^k = e$. Such a $k$ exists because $G$ is finite (the powers $g, g^2, g^3, \ldots$ cannot all be distinct, so some $g^i = g^j$ with $i < j$ gives $g^{j-i} = e$). The order divides every $m$ with $g^m = e$.

**Definition (Euler totient $\varphi(n)$).** For $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}$,
$$
\varphi(n) \;=\; \# \{\, a \in \{1, 2, \ldots, n\} : \gcd(a, n) = 1 \,\} \;=\; \#(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times.
$$
By [21.01.03] Theorem 2, $\varphi$ is multiplicative on coprime arguments ($\varphi(mn) = \varphi(m)\varphi(n)$ for $\gcd(m, n) = 1$), satisfies $\varphi(p^k) = p^{k-1}(p - 1)$ on prime powers, and has the closed form $\varphi(n) = n \prod_{p \mid n}(1 - 1/p)$.

**Definition (Carmichael function $\lambda(n)$).** The **exponent** of the group $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ — the least positive integer $\lambda$ such that $a^\lambda \equiv 1 \pmod n$ for every $a$ coprime to $n$ — is denoted $\lambda(n)$ and called the **Carmichael function**. It always divides $\varphi(n)$, with equality if and only if $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ is cyclic, which happens precisely for $n \in \{1, 2, 4, p^k, 2 p^k\}$ with $p$ an odd prime [Carmichael 1910].

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"Fermat's little theorem holds for every integer $a$, including multiples of $p$."* The statement $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p$ requires $\gcd(a, p) = 1$. The case $a \equiv 0 \pmod p$ gives $a^{p-1} \equiv 0$, not $1$. The unrestricted form $a^p \equiv a \pmod p$ (which both forms imply for $a \not\equiv 0$) does cover $a \equiv 0$ ($0^p = 0$).

- *"Euler's theorem applies to every modulus the same way."* The exponent $\varphi(n)$ is the group order, not necessarily the group exponent. For $n = 8$, $\varphi(8) = 4$ but $\lambda(8) = 2$: every $a$ coprime to $8$ satisfies $a^2 \equiv 1 \pmod 8$, a strict refinement of $a^4 \equiv 1 \pmod 8$. The tight bound is $\lambda(n)$, not $\varphi(n)$; this matters for cryptographic key-strength estimates.

- *"Wilson's theorem extends to composite moduli as $(n - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod n$."* False — and decisively so. For composite $n > 4$, $(n - 1)! \equiv 0 \pmod n$: the factors $1, 2, \ldots, n - 1$ contain both factors of any non-degenerate factorisation $n = ab$ with $1 < a, b < n$ (with $a \neq b$ separable for $n > 4$; the $n = 4$ case is the lone exception). The converse — if $(n - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod n$ then $n$ is prime — is a genuine primality criterion.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem of this unit is Fermat's little theorem in its modern group-theoretic form. We give the proof via Lagrange's theorem applied to $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times$, and then deduce Euler's and Wilson's theorems as direct consequences.

**Theorem (Fermat's little theorem).** *Let $p$ be a prime and $a$ an integer with $\gcd(a, p) = 1$. Then $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p$. Equivalently, $a^p \equiv a \pmod p$ for every integer $a$.*

*Proof.* The set $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times$ of nonzero residues modulo $p$ is a group under multiplication, with identity $[1]_p$ and order $p - 1$ (every nonzero residue is a unit because $p$ is prime; this is the field property of $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ from [21.01.03]).

Since $\gcd(a, p) = 1$, the residue $[a]_p$ lies in $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times$. By Lagrange's theorem on finite groups (proved in [01.02.01]), the order of any element divides the order of the group:
$$
\mathrm{ord}([a]_p) \;\big|\; \#(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times = p - 1.
$$
Writing $p - 1 = k \cdot \mathrm{ord}([a]_p)$ for some positive integer $k$:
$$
[a]_p^{p-1} \;=\; \left([a]_p^{\mathrm{ord}([a]_p)}\right)^k \;=\; [1]_p^k \;=\; [1]_p.
$$
Hence $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p$.

To extend to the form $a^p \equiv a \pmod p$ for every integer $a$: if $\gcd(a, p) = 1$, multiply both sides of $a^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod p$ by $a$ to get $a^p \equiv a \pmod p$. If $\gcd(a, p) > 1$, then $p \mid a$, so both $a^p$ and $a$ are $\equiv 0 \pmod p$. $\square$

**Bridge.** Fermat's little theorem builds toward [21.01.05] primitive roots, where the cyclic structure of $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times$ is sharpened to the existence of an element of order exactly $p - 1$ — the order-divides-group-order fact above promoted to an order-equals-group-order witness. The central insight is exactly that the multiplicative cyclic structure of nonzero residues modulo a prime is the operational content of the field property of $\mathbb{F}_p$; this is the bridge from elementary number theory to [01.02.18] finite fields $\mathbb{F}_q$ and Frobenius. The pattern generalises: replacing $p$ by an arbitrary integer $n$ and $p - 1$ by $\varphi(n)$ gives Euler's theorem; replacing $\varphi(n)$ by the group exponent $\lambda(n)$ gives the tight Carmichael refinement; identifying the orbit of $a$ in $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$ with the action of the Frobenius $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ on $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$ identifies Fermat's theorem with the splitting structure of finite Galois extensions. The same identification appears again in [01.02.18] as the statement that $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ fixes $\mathbb{F}_p$ pointwise. Putting these together with Lagrange and the order-divides-group-order pattern, Fermat is the prototype of every "group acting by multiplication has finite orbits of divisor length" theorem in algebra.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Compute $3^{100}$ modulo $7$. Enter the residue in the range $0 \leq r < 7$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

By Fermat, $3^6 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$. Reduce the exponent $100$ modulo $6$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$4$.** Since $7$ is prime and $\gcd(3, 7) = 1$, Fermat gives $3^6 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$. Write $100 = 16 \cdot 6 + 4$, so $3^{100} = (3^6)^{16} \cdot 3^4 \equiv 1^{16} \cdot 3^4 \equiv 3^4 \pmod 7$. Compute $3^4 = 81 = 11 \cdot 7 + 4$, so $3^{100} \equiv 4 \pmod 7$. The exponent reduction from $100$ to $4$ is the practical content of Fermat in modular exponentiation.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

Compute $7^{222}$ modulo $100$. Enter the residue in the range $0 \leq r < 100$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\varphi(100) = 40$, apply Euler's theorem to reduce the exponent modulo $40$, then compute the remaining power directly.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$49$.** Since $\gcd(7, 100) = 1$ and $\varphi(100) = 40$, Euler gives $7^{40} \equiv 1 \pmod{100}$. Reduce $222 = 5 \cdot 40 + 22$, so $7^{222} \equiv 7^{22} \pmod{100}$. Compute step by step: $7^2 = 49$; $7^4 = 49^2 = 2401 \equiv 1 \pmod{100}$. Then $7^{22} = (7^4)^5 \cdot 7^2 \equiv 1^5 \cdot 49 \equiv 49 \pmod{100}$. Direct verification by repeated squaring confirms $7^{222} \equiv 49 \pmod{100}$.

</details>

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).**

A toy RSA setup uses primes $p = 11$, $q = 13$, public exponent $e = 7$. Compute the private exponent $d$, the integer in the range $1 \leq d < \varphi(N)$ with $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\varphi(N)}$ where $N = pq$. Then decrypt the ciphertext $C = 5$, i.e., compute $M = C^d \bmod N$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\varphi(N) = (p - 1)(q - 1) = 120$. Find $d$ with $7 d \equiv 1 \pmod{120}$ via the extended Euclidean algorithm. Then use Euler's theorem to simplify $C^d \bmod N$ via CRT-on-$p$-and-$q$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$d = 103$, $M = 125 \bmod 143 = 125$ — wait, recompute carefully.** Take $N = 11 \cdot 13 = 143$ and $\varphi(N) = 10 \cdot 12 = 120$. Solve $7 d \equiv 1 \pmod{120}$: extended Euclidean on $(7, 120)$ gives $120 = 17 \cdot 7 + 1$, so $1 = 120 - 17 \cdot 7$, and $d \equiv -17 \equiv 103 \pmod{120}$. Check: $7 \cdot 103 = 721 = 6 \cdot 120 + 1 \equiv 1 \pmod{120}$.

Decrypt: $M = 5^{103} \bmod 143$. Compute via CRT-on-$p$-and-$q$. Modulo $11$: $5^{103} \bmod 11$. By Fermat, $5^{10} \equiv 1 \pmod{11}$, so reduce $103 = 10 \cdot 10 + 3$, giving $5^{103} \equiv 5^3 = 125 \equiv 4 \pmod{11}$ (since $125 = 11 \cdot 11 + 4$). Modulo $13$: $5^{103} \bmod 13$. By Fermat, $5^{12} \equiv 1 \pmod{13}$, so $103 = 8 \cdot 12 + 7$, giving $5^{103} \equiv 5^7 \pmod{13}$. Compute $5^2 = 25 \equiv 12 \equiv -1 \pmod{13}$, so $5^7 = 5 \cdot (5^2)^3 \equiv 5 \cdot (-1)^3 = -5 \equiv 8 \pmod{13}$. CRT: find $M \in [0, 142]$ with $M \equiv 4 \pmod{11}$ and $M \equiv 8 \pmod{13}$. Write $M = 11k + 4$; the second condition gives $11 k + 4 \equiv 8 \pmod{13}$, i.e., $11 k \equiv 4 \pmod{13}$, i.e., $-2 k \equiv 4 \pmod{13}$, so $k \equiv -2 \equiv 11 \pmod{13}$. Take $k = 11$: $M = 11 \cdot 11 + 4 = 125$. Check: $125 = 11 \cdot 11 + 4 \equiv 4 \pmod{11}$; $125 = 9 \cdot 13 + 8 \equiv 8 \pmod{13}$. So $M = 125$. (Indeed $5^{103} \bmod 143 = 125$.)

</details>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove Euler's theorem: for $\gcd(a, n) = 1$, $a^{\varphi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The proof is identical in structure to Fermat's, with $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ replaced by $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$. Apply Lagrange's theorem to the group of order $\varphi(n)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The set $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ of residue classes coprime to $n$ is a finite abelian group of order $\varphi(n)$. Since $\gcd(a, n) = 1$, $[a]_n$ lies in this group.

By Lagrange's theorem, the order of any element divides the order of the group, so $\mathrm{ord}([a]_n) \mid \varphi(n)$. Writing $\varphi(n) = k \cdot \mathrm{ord}([a]_n)$:
$$
[a]_n^{\varphi(n)} \;=\; \left([a]_n^{\mathrm{ord}([a]_n)}\right)^k \;=\; [1]_n^k \;=\; [1]_n.
$$
Hence $a^{\varphi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n$.

The specialisation $n = p$ recovers Fermat's little theorem, since $\varphi(p) = p - 1$. The further refinement using the Carmichael function $\lambda(n)$ instead of $\varphi(n)$ gives the tight statement $a^{\lambda(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n$.

Rubric: full credit for identifying $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ as the operative group, applying Lagrange, and naming the order $\varphi(n)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove Wilson's theorem: for $p$ prime, $(p - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod p$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Pair each element of $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times$ with its multiplicative inverse. Most pairs collapse to $1$ in the product. The self-inverse elements are exactly $\pm 1$, and the field property of $\mathbb{F}_p$ supplies this.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the group $G = (\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times$ of order $p - 1$, every element $[a]_p$ has a unique inverse $[a^{-1}]_p$. An element is **self-inverse** when $[a]_p^2 = [1]_p$, i.e., $a^2 \equiv 1 \pmod p$. Equivalently $(a - 1)(a + 1) \equiv 0 \pmod p$. Since $\mathbb{F}_p = \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ is a field (an integral domain), $(a - 1)(a + 1) \equiv 0$ forces $a \equiv 1 \pmod p$ or $a \equiv -1 \pmod p$.

Hence the only self-inverse elements of $G$ are $[1]_p$ and $[-1]_p$. In the product
$$
\prod_{[a]_p \in G} [a]_p \;=\; (p - 1)! \pmod p,
$$
every non-self-inverse element pairs with its distinct inverse, contributing $1$ to the product. The remaining unpaired factors are $[1]_p$ and $[-1]_p$, multiplying to $[-1]_p$. Hence $(p - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod p$.

For $p = 2$ the statement is degenerate but holds: $(2 - 1)! = 1! = 1 \equiv -1 \pmod 2$. For $p = 7$: $6! = 720 = 102 \cdot 7 + 6 \equiv 6 \equiv -1 \pmod 7$.

Rubric: full credit for the pairing argument with the field-property identification of self-inverses.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the **converse of Wilson's theorem**: if $(n - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod n$ for $n \geq 2$, then $n$ is prime.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Argue by contrapositive: if $n$ is composite with $n > 4$, show $(n - 1)! \equiv 0 \pmod n$; if $n = 4$, compute $3!$ directly.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Argue by contrapositive. Suppose $n$ is composite, $n \geq 2$.

**Case $n > 4$, $n$ composite.** Write $n = ab$ with $1 < a, b < n$. If $a \neq b$, then both $a$ and $b$ appear as separate factors in $(n - 1)! = 1 \cdot 2 \cdots (n - 1)$ (since $a, b < n$), so $ab = n$ divides $(n - 1)!$. If $a = b$, then $n = a^2$ with $a \geq 3$ (since $n > 4$). Both $a$ and $2a$ lie in $\{1, 2, \ldots, n - 1\}$ (the condition $2a < n = a^2$ requires $a > 2$, satisfied since $a \geq 3$), so $2 a^2 = 2n$ divides $(n - 1)!$, hence $n \mid (n - 1)!$. In both subcases $(n - 1)! \equiv 0 \pmod n$, in particular $\not\equiv -1$.

**Case $n = 4$.** $(4 - 1)! = 3! = 6 \equiv 2 \pmod 4$, not $\equiv -1 \equiv 3 \pmod 4$.

**Case $n$ prime.** Wilson gives $(n - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod n$.

So $(n - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod n$ if and only if $n$ is prime. This is a genuine primality criterion, but its time complexity is exponential in $\log n$ (computing $(n - 1)!$ takes $n$ multiplications) — impractical compared to the Miller-Rabin or AKS tests of [Master Theorem 6].

Rubric: full credit for the contrapositive case-split with the explicit handling of $n = 4$ as the lone exception.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove **Korselt's criterion** [ref: TODO_REF Korselt 1899]: a composite integer $n > 1$ satisfies $a^{n - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod n$ for every integer $a$ coprime to $n$ if and only if $n$ is squarefree and $(p - 1) \mid (n - 1)$ for every prime $p$ dividing $n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the CRT decomposition $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times \cong \prod_{p \mid n}(\mathbb{Z}/p^{e_p})^\times$ from [21.01.03]. The condition $a^{n - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod n$ for all $a$ coprime to $n$ is equivalent to the Carmichael function $\lambda(n)$ dividing $n - 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The hypothesis $a^{n - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod n$ for every $a$ coprime to $n$ is the statement that the exponent of the group $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ divides $n - 1$ — equivalently, $\lambda(n) \mid (n - 1)$.

**Forward direction.** Suppose $\lambda(n) \mid (n - 1)$ and $n$ is composite. By the CRT structure from [21.01.03], $\lambda(n) = \mathrm{lcm}_p \lambda(p^{e_p})$ where $n = \prod p^{e_p}$. For $p$ odd, $\lambda(p^{e_p}) = p^{e_p - 1}(p - 1)$ (the group $(\mathbb{Z}/p^{e_p})^\times$ is cyclic). If any $e_p \geq 2$, then $p \mid \lambda(p^{e_p}) \mid \lambda(n) \mid (n - 1)$, so $p \mid (n - 1)$. But also $p \mid n$, hence $p \mid \gcd(n, n - 1) = 1$, a contradiction. So $e_p = 1$ for every odd prime divisor of $n$. The case $p = 2$ gives $\lambda(2^{e_2})$ as $1, 1, 2$ for $e_2 = 0, 1, 2$ and $2^{e_2 - 2}$ for $e_2 \geq 3$; a similar argument plus $2 \mid n \Rightarrow 2 \nmid (n - 1)$ forces $e_2 \leq 1$. Hence $n$ is squarefree.

With $n$ squarefree, $\lambda(p) = p - 1$ for each prime divisor, so $\lambda(n) = \mathrm{lcm}_p (p - 1)$. The condition $\lambda(n) \mid (n - 1)$ is equivalent to $(p - 1) \mid (n - 1)$ for every prime $p \mid n$.

**Reverse direction.** Suppose $n$ is squarefree with $(p - 1) \mid (n - 1)$ for every $p \mid n$. Then $\lambda(n) = \mathrm{lcm}_p (p - 1) \mid (n - 1)$, so $a^{n - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod n$ for every $a$ coprime to $n$.

The smallest Carmichael number is $561 = 3 \cdot 11 \cdot 17$ (squarefree; $2 \mid 560$, $10 \mid 560$, $16 \mid 560$). The next few are $1105, 1729, 2465, 2821, 6601, 8911, \ldots$. Alford-Granville-Pomerance 1994 *Annals* 140, 703 [ref: TODO_REF Alford-Granville-Pomerance 1994] proved that there are infinitely many such $n$ — answering a 1912 question of Carmichael.

Rubric: full credit for the $\lambda(n) \mid (n - 1)$ reformulation and the squarefree-forcing case-split.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove **Gauss's generalisation of Wilson** [ref: TODO_REF Gauss 1801]: for $n > 1$,
$$
\prod_{\gcd(a, n) = 1,\, 1 \leq a \leq n} a \;\equiv\; \begin{cases} -1 \pmod n & \text{if } n = 4, p^e, 2 p^e \text{ for an odd prime } p, \\ +1 \pmod n & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}
$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The product of all elements of a finite abelian group $G$ equals the product of all elements of order $\leq 2$ (the others pair off via $g \mapsto g^{-1}$). Identify when the order-$2$ subgroup of $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ has exactly one non-identity element.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $G = (\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ and let $S = \prod_{a \in G} a$. Pair each $g \in G$ with $g^{-1}$. Elements with $g \neq g^{-1}$ contribute $1$ to $S$ via the pair. Self-inverse elements (those with $g^2 = 1$) contribute themselves. Hence
$$
S \;=\; \prod_{g \in G, \, g^2 = 1} g.
$$
The subgroup $G[2] = \{g \in G : g^2 = 1\}$ is a finite abelian group of exponent $2$, hence an elementary abelian $2$-group: $G[2] \cong (\mathbb{Z}/2)^t$ for some $t \geq 0$. The product of all elements of $(\mathbb{Z}/2)^t$ is $(0, 0, \ldots, 0)$ — the identity — when $t \geq 2$ (every coordinate sums an equal number of $0$s and $1$s), and equals the unique non-identity element when $t = 1$. When $t = 0$ (i.e., $G[2]$ is the identity group, meaning $|G|$ is odd), $S = 1$.

**Cases.** By the CRT structure of [21.01.03], $G = (\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times \cong \prod_p (\mathbb{Z}/p^{e_p})^\times$. The order-$2$ subgroup decomposes as $\prod_p G_p[2]$. For $p$ odd, $(\mathbb{Z}/p^e)^\times$ is cyclic of even order $p^{e-1}(p - 1)$, hence has exactly one element of order $2$ (namely $[-1]_{p^e}$); $|G_p[2]| = 2$. For $p = 2$: $G_2[2]$ is the identity group for $e \leq 1$, has order $2$ for $e = 2$, and order $4$ for $e \geq 3$.

So $t = $ (number of odd-prime divisors of $n$) $+ \, \epsilon$, where $\epsilon \in \{0, 1, 2\}$ depending on the power of $2$ in $n$. The case $t = 1$ — exactly one non-identity self-inverse element — happens precisely for $n \in \{4, p^e, 2 p^e\}$ with $p$ an odd prime. In this case the unique self-inverse element is $[-1]_n$, giving $S \equiv -1 \pmod n$. In every other case $t \geq 2$, giving $S \equiv 1 \pmod n$.

Wilson's classical theorem is the specialisation to $n = p$ prime, where $G = (\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ has order $p - 1$ and $S = (p - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod p$.

Rubric: full credit for the $G[2]$ reduction, the structure-theorem identification of $G[2]$ as elementary abelian, and the case-split on the number of factors.

</details>

</aside>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

```lean
-- Operative imports: Mathlib.Data.ZMod.Basic, Mathlib.Data.Nat.Totient,
-- Mathlib.NumberTheory.LucasLehmer, Mathlib.NumberTheory.Wilson,
-- Mathlib.GroupTheory.OrderOfElement, Mathlib.RingTheory.Polynomial.Cyclotomic.Basic

#check @ZMod.pow_card_sub_one_eq_one
-- Fermat's little theorem: for p prime, a : ZMod p with a ≠ 0 satisfies a^(p - 1) = 1.

#check @ZMod.pow_card
-- The unrestricted form: a^p ≡ a (mod p) for every integer a.

#check @ZMod.pow_totient
-- Euler's theorem: for n : ℕ with Coprime a n, a^(Nat.totient n) ≡ 1 (mod n).

#check @ZMod.wilsons_lemma
-- Wilson's theorem: for p prime, (p - 1)! ≡ -1 (mod p).

#check @Nat.totient
-- The Euler totient function, defined as the cardinality of the unit group.

#check @Nat.totient_prime_pow
-- φ(p^k) = p^(k - 1) * (p - 1) for p prime and k ≥ 1.

#check @orderOf_dvd_card
-- The general Lagrange-consequence: orderOf g divides Fintype.card G in a finite group.
```

The Fermat-Euler-Wilson trio in Mathlib rests on `orderOf_dvd_card` — the Lagrange-theorem consequence that the order of any element divides the group order. The polynomial-identity proof of Wilson is `ZMod.wilsons_lemma` and rests on the factorisation $x^{p-1} - 1 = \prod_{a = 1}^{p-1}(x - a)$ in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$. What Mathlib does not yet supply as a unified module — the Carmichael function with explicit prime-power formula, Korselt's criterion, the smallest Carmichael number $561$, the Miller-Rabin and AKS primality tests, and Gauss's generalised Wilson product — is the formalisation target named in the frontmatter `lean_mathlib_gap`.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (combinatorial / necklace proof of Fermat's little theorem).** *For $p$ prime and any integer $a \geq 1$, the number of distinct necklaces of length $p$ over an alphabet of $a$ letters is $(a^p - a)/p + a$, hence $p$ divides $a^p - a$.* The count: there are $a^p$ words of length $p$; the cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}/p$ acts by rotation; orbits of monochromatic words have size $1$ (there are $a$ of these), and orbits of non-monochromatic words have size dividing $p$, hence equal to $p$ (since $p$ is prime). So $a^p - a$ is divisible by $p$. The argument generalises to Burnside-counting orbits of any prime-power group action; see Hardy-Wright 2008 §6.3 [ref: TODO_REF Hardy-Wright 2008]. The combinatorial proof predates Euler's algebraic proof and is the natural argument for $p$-adic-flavoured generalisations.

**Theorem 2 (Euler's induction proof of Fermat).** *Fermat's little theorem in the form $a^p \equiv a \pmod p$ follows by induction on $a$ using $(a + 1)^p \equiv a^p + 1 \pmod p$* [Euler 1736]. The induction step uses the **freshman's dream**: in $\mathbb{F}_p$, $(a + b)^p = a^p + b^p$, because every middle binomial coefficient $\binom{p}{k}$ for $1 \leq k \leq p - 1$ is divisible by $p$ (the formula $\binom{p}{k} = p! / (k! (p - k)!)$ contains $p$ in the numerator with no compensating factor in the denominator). Iterating from the base case $a = 1$ gives $a^p \equiv a \pmod p$ for every positive integer $a$. Euler 1736 *Comm. Acad. Sci. Petrop.* 8 published this as the first proof of Fermat's little theorem; the freshman's-dream identity is also the operational content of the Frobenius endomorphism $\mathrm{Frob}_p : \mathbb{F}_p \to \mathbb{F}_p$, $x \mapsto x^p$, being a ring homomorphism — the bridge to [01.02.18] finite fields.

**Theorem 3 (Carmichael function and its prime-power formula).** *The Carmichael function $\lambda(n)$, the exponent of $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$, has values $\lambda(1) = \lambda(2) = 1$, $\lambda(4) = 2$, $\lambda(2^k) = 2^{k - 2}$ for $k \geq 3$, $\lambda(p^k) = p^{k - 1}(p - 1)$ for $p$ an odd prime, and $\lambda(n) = \mathrm{lcm}_p \lambda(p^{e_p})$ for $n = \prod p^{e_p}$.* [ref: TODO_REF Carmichael 1910]. Always $\lambda(n) \mid \varphi(n)$, with equality if and only if $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ is cyclic, which characterises $n \in \{1, 2, 4, p^k, 2 p^k\}$ for $p$ an odd prime. The Carmichael function is the tight exponent in Euler's theorem: $a^{\lambda(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n$ for every $a$ coprime to $n$, and $\lambda(n)$ is the least such integer. RSA security depends on $\lambda(N)$, not $\varphi(N)$: the private exponent satisfies $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\lambda(N)}$ as the necessary condition, with $\lambda(N) = \mathrm{lcm}(p - 1, q - 1)$ for $N = pq$.

**Theorem 4 (Korselt's criterion and Carmichael numbers; Korselt 1899, Alford-Granville-Pomerance 1994).** *A composite integer $n$ is a Carmichael number (satisfying $a^{n - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod n$ for every $a$ coprime to $n$) if and only if $n$ is squarefree and $(p - 1) \mid (n - 1)$ for every prime $p$ dividing $n$* [ref: TODO_REF Korselt 1899]. The smallest Carmichael number is $561 = 3 \cdot 11 \cdot 17$: squarefree, and $2 \mid 560$, $10 \mid 560$, $16 \mid 560$. The next few are $1105 = 5 \cdot 13 \cdot 17$, $1729 = 7 \cdot 13 \cdot 19$ (Ramanujan's taxicab), $2465, 2821, 6601, 8911$. Alford-Granville-Pomerance 1994 *Annals* 140, 703 [ref: TODO_REF Alford-Granville-Pomerance 1994] proved the count $C(x)$ of Carmichael numbers up to $x$ satisfies $C(x) > x^{2/7}$, settling Carmichael's 1912 conjecture. Carmichael numbers are precisely the composite false-positives of the Fermat primality test, motivating the strong-pseudoprime refinement of Miller-Rabin.

**Theorem 5 (Miller-Rabin probabilistic primality test; Miller 1976, Rabin 1980).** *Let $n - 1 = 2^s d$ with $d$ odd. A composite $n$ is called a **strong pseudoprime** to base $a$ if $a^d \equiv 1 \pmod n$ or $a^{2^r d} \equiv -1 \pmod n$ for some $0 \leq r < s$. For every composite $n > 9$, at most $1/4$ of bases $a \in \{1, \ldots, n - 1\}$ are strong-pseudoprime witnesses* [ref: TODO_REF Rabin 1980]. The Miller-Rabin algorithm: for $k$ randomly chosen bases $a_1, \ldots, a_k$, declare $n$ "probably prime" if every $a_i$ is a strong-pseudoprime witness; declare composite otherwise. The false-positive probability is $4^{-k}$ for composite inputs. With $k = 40$, the false-positive rate is below $2^{-80}$ — well below cosmic-ray bit-flip error rates. Standard library implementations (OpenSSL, GMP, Java's `BigInteger.isProbablePrime`) use Miller-Rabin with $k$ ranging from $20$ to $128$ for RSA key generation. Miller 1976 originally proved a deterministic variant assuming the generalised Riemann hypothesis.

**Theorem 6 (AKS deterministic polynomial-time primality test; Agrawal-Kayal-Saxena 2004).** *PRIMES is in P: there exists a deterministic algorithm that decides primality of $n$ in time polynomial in $\log n$* [ref: TODO_REF AKS 2004]. The Agrawal-Kayal-Saxena algorithm rests on a polynomial-identity strengthening of Fermat's little theorem: for $n > 1$ coprime to $a$, $n$ is prime if and only if $(x + a)^n \equiv x^n + a \pmod{n, x^r - 1}$ as a polynomial identity in $(\mathbb{Z}/n)[x]/(x^r - 1)$, for an appropriately chosen $r$ of order $> (\log n)^2$ in $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$. The original algorithm runs in $\tilde{O}((\log n)^{12})$; Lenstra-Pomerance refinements bring it to $\tilde{O}((\log n)^{6})$. AKS settled a long-standing open question — whether primality is in deterministic polynomial time — and confirmed that the polynomial-identity criterion is essentially the "right" generalisation of Fermat's congruence. In practice Miller-Rabin remains faster for RSA key generation, but AKS is the deterministic certificate of theoretical interest.

**Theorem 7 (Gauss's generalised Wilson; Gauss 1801 art. 78).** *For $n > 1$,*
$$
\prod_{\gcd(a, n) = 1, \, 1 \leq a \leq n} a \;\equiv\; \begin{cases} -1 \pmod n, & n \in \{4, p^e, 2 p^e\} \text{ for $p$ an odd prime,} \\ +1 \pmod n, & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}
$$
[ref: TODO_REF Gauss 1801]. The sign tracks whether $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ has exactly one element of order $2$ (in which case the unpaired self-inverse $-1$ contributes the $-1$) or more than one (in which case the product of self-inverses collapses to $1$). The classification of cyclic $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ — itself due to Gauss 1801 §III — supplies the case structure: $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ is cyclic iff $n \in \{1, 2, 4, p^e, 2 p^e\}$ for $p$ an odd prime, and exactly these moduli (with the $n = 1, 2$ degenerate cases) give the $-1$ branch.

**Theorem 8 (RSA correctness and security; Rivest-Shamir-Adleman 1978).** *Let $N = pq$ be a product of two distinct primes, $e$ an integer coprime to $\varphi(N) = (p - 1)(q - 1)$, and $d$ the modular inverse of $e$ modulo $\varphi(N)$. For every integer $M$ with $0 \leq M < N$, $M^{ed} \equiv M \pmod N$* [ref: TODO_REF Rivest-Shamir-Adleman 1978]. *Proof.* If $\gcd(M, N) = 1$, then by Euler $M^{\varphi(N)} \equiv 1 \pmod N$, so $M^{ed} = M^{1 + k \varphi(N)} = M \cdot (M^{\varphi(N)})^k \equiv M \pmod N$. If $\gcd(M, N) > 1$, then $p \mid M$ or $q \mid M$ (not both, since $M < N = pq$); CRT on $p$ and $q$ separately gives $M^{ed} \equiv M$ in each factor, hence modulo $N$. Security rests on the assumption that factoring $N$ is computationally hard: Coppersmith 1996 and Boneh-Durfee 2000 showed that knowing $d$ (or even partial knowledge of $d$ when $d$ is small) reduces to factoring; Shor 1994 showed that quantum algorithms factor in polynomial time, motivating post-quantum cryptography. RSA underpinned essentially all of e-commerce from 1995 until the post-quantum transition began in the late 2010s.

**Synthesis.** The Fermat-Euler-Wilson trio is the foundational reason that the multiplicative structure of finite fields and finite cyclic groups governs every elementary primality and exponentiation result in number theory. The central insight is exactly that the order of any element divides the order of the group containing it — Lagrange's theorem applied to $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ and $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ — and the entire chain of theorems above unfolds from this single fact. Putting these together with the Carmichael refinement, the tight exponent of $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ is $\lambda(n)$ not $\varphi(n)$, and this is exactly the operational content that makes the Fermat test fail on Carmichael numbers, the Miller-Rabin test recover correctness via strong pseudoprimes, and the AKS criterion supply a deterministic polynomial-identity replacement.

The pattern generalises in multiple directions. Replacing the discrete group $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ by the elliptic-curve group $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ identifies Fermat's theorem with the Hasse-Weil bound and underwrites elliptic-curve primality proving and ECDSA; replacing the integer modulus $n$ by a polynomial modulus $f \in \mathbb{F}_q[x]$ identifies Fermat with the Frobenius decomposition $\mathrm{Frob}_q : \overline{\mathbb{F}_q} \to \overline{\mathbb{F}_q}$ fixing $\mathbb{F}_q$ pointwise, the bridge to [01.02.18] finite fields and Frobenius; replacing the multiplicative group by an additive flavour identifies Wilson with the sum formulas $\sum_{a \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times} a = 0$ for $p \neq 2$. The bridge is everywhere the same: a finite-order multiplicative structure produces a cancellation identity, and primality is exactly the condition for the structure to be cyclic of maximal order.

The applied consequences are pervasive. RSA decryption rests on Euler's theorem; Diffie-Hellman key exchange and the Digital Signature Algorithm rest on the cyclic structure of $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ that Fermat encodes; Miller-Rabin and AKS rest on strengthenings of Fermat's congruence; elliptic-curve cryptography reuses the same group-theoretic framework over $E(\mathbb{F}_p)$. The post-quantum transition motivated by Shor 1994 keeps the cyclic-group structure but replaces the underlying hard problem (factoring, discrete log) with lattice-based or code-based or isogeny-based alternatives — yet even the new schemes inherit the Lagrange-orbit cancellation as their proof-of-correctness identity. Fermat's 1640 letter to Frénicle was the seed of all modern public-key cryptography.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Fermat's little theorem, polynomial-identity proof in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$).** *For $p$ prime, the polynomial identity*
$$
x^{p - 1} - 1 \;=\; \prod_{a = 1}^{p - 1}(x - a) \quad \text{in } \mathbb{F}_p[x]
$$
*holds, recovering Fermat's little theorem $a^{p - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod p$ for $1 \leq a \leq p - 1$ as the evaluations of the left-hand side at each root.*

*Proof.* The polynomial $x^{p - 1} - 1 \in \mathbb{F}_p[x]$ has degree $p - 1$. By Fermat's little theorem (proved separately above via Lagrange), every element $a \in \mathbb{F}_p^\times = \{1, 2, \ldots, p - 1\}$ satisfies $a^{p - 1} - 1 \equiv 0 \pmod p$, so $x = a$ is a root in $\mathbb{F}_p$.

A polynomial of degree $d$ over a field has at most $d$ roots. The polynomial $x^{p - 1} - 1$ has $p - 1$ distinct roots in $\mathbb{F}_p$ — the full set $\{1, 2, \ldots, p - 1\}$. Hence the factorisation
$$
x^{p - 1} - 1 \;=\; \prod_{a = 1}^{p - 1}(x - a)
$$
holds in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$. The two sides have equal degree and equal roots; their leading coefficients are both $1$, so they agree. $\square$

The polynomial-identity form is equivalent to Fermat but conceptually sharper: it identifies $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$ as the set of roots of $x^{p - 1} - 1$ in $\mathbb{F}_p$, exposing $\mathbb{F}_p$ as the splitting field of a separable polynomial of degree $p - 1$. The Frobenius generalisation to $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ replaces $p - 1$ by $p^n - 1$ and produces the same factorisation.

**Proposition 2 (Euler's theorem, group-theoretic proof).** *For $\gcd(a, n) = 1$, $a^{\varphi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n$.*

*Proof.* The group $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ has order $\varphi(n)$. The residue $[a]_n$ lies in this group because $\gcd(a, n) = 1$. By Lagrange's theorem on finite groups, the order of any element divides the order of the group, so $\mathrm{ord}([a]_n) \mid \varphi(n)$. Writing $\varphi(n) = k \cdot \mathrm{ord}([a]_n)$:
$$
[a]_n^{\varphi(n)} \;=\; \left([a]_n^{\mathrm{ord}([a]_n)}\right)^k \;=\; [1]_n^k \;=\; [1]_n. \qquad \square
$$

**Proposition 3 (Lagrange's proof of Wilson via polynomial coefficients).** *For $p$ prime, $(p - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod p$.*

*Proof (Lagrange 1771).* Consider the polynomial
$$
f(x) \;=\; (x - 1)(x - 2) \cdots (x - (p - 1)) - (x^{p - 1} - 1) \;\in\; \mathbb{F}_p[x].
$$
The first factor expands to a polynomial of degree $p - 1$ with leading coefficient $1$ and constant term $(-1)^{p - 1} (p - 1)!$. The second has leading coefficient $1$ and constant term $-1$. So $f(x)$ has degree at most $p - 2$ (the leading terms cancel) and constant term $(-1)^{p - 1} (p - 1)! + 1$.

By Proposition 1, $\prod_{a = 1}^{p - 1}(x - a) = x^{p - 1} - 1$ in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$, so $f(x) = 0$ identically in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$. All coefficients vanish — in particular the constant coefficient: $(-1)^{p - 1} (p - 1)! + 1 \equiv 0 \pmod p$. For $p$ odd, $(-1)^{p - 1} = 1$, giving $(p - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod p$. For $p = 2$, the identity reduces to $1! = 1 \equiv -1 \pmod 2$, also valid. $\square$

This is the proof Lagrange 1771 *Nouv. Mém. Acad. Berlin* 2 published. The pairing-and-self-inverses argument from Exercise 5 is the modern textbook proof; both arguments produce the same conclusion via different operational content.

**Proposition 4 (Korselt's criterion).** *A composite integer $n$ satisfies $a^{n - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod n$ for every $a$ coprime to $n$ if and only if $n$ is squarefree and $(p - 1) \mid (n - 1)$ for every prime $p$ dividing $n$.*

*Proof.* The condition is equivalent to $\lambda(n) \mid (n - 1)$. By the CRT decomposition $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times \cong \prod_p (\mathbb{Z}/p^{e_p})^\times$ and the prime-power formula $\lambda(p^e) = p^{e - 1}(p - 1)$ for $p$ odd (with $\lambda(2^e)$ adjusted by the cyclic-versus-non-cyclic distinction), $\lambda(n) = \mathrm{lcm}_p \lambda(p^{e_p})$.

If any $e_p \geq 2$ for $p$ odd, then $p \mid \lambda(p^{e_p}) \mid \lambda(n) \mid (n - 1)$. But $p \mid n$, so $p \mid \gcd(n, n - 1) = 1$, contradiction. Hence $n$ is squarefree at every odd prime. The case $p = 2$ with $e_2 \geq 2$ is similarly ruled out by $2 \mid n \Rightarrow 2 \nmid (n - 1)$.

With $n$ squarefree, $\lambda(n) = \mathrm{lcm}_p (p - 1)$, and $\lambda(n) \mid (n - 1)$ is equivalent to $(p - 1) \mid (n - 1)$ for every $p \mid n$. The converse is immediate. $\square$

The smallest Carmichael number is $561 = 3 \cdot 11 \cdot 17$: squarefree, and $560 = 2^4 \cdot 5 \cdot 7$ is divisible by $2 = 3 - 1$, $10 = 11 - 1$, $16 = 17 - 1$. Alford-Granville-Pomerance 1994 *Annals* 140, 703 proved infinitely many Carmichael numbers exist, with density $C(x) > x^{2/7}$.

## Connections [Master]

- **Congruences and the CRT [21.01.03].** Just shipped. The unit-group framework $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ developed in [21.01.03] is the operative setting of all three Fermat-Euler-Wilson theorems. The CRT decomposition $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times \cong \prod_p (\mathbb{Z}/p^{e_p})^\times$ supplies the prime-power reduction used in the Carmichael-function formula, Korselt's criterion, Gauss's generalised Wilson, and the CRT-based RSA decryption speedup. The totient $\varphi(n)$ is the cardinality and $\lambda(n)$ the exponent of this group; the multiplicativity of both functions descends from CRT. The present unit is operationally the next layer above the unit-group structure of [21.01.03].

- **Primes and the fundamental theorem [21.01.02].** Shipped. Fermat's little theorem and Wilson's theorem are characterisations of primes: $a^{n - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod n$ for all $a$ coprime to $n$ fails generically on composites (Carmichael numbers are the celebrated exceptions); $(n - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod n$ holds if and only if $n$ is prime (the converse of Wilson). The fundamental theorem of arithmetic from [21.01.02] supplies the prime-power factorisation that feeds the structure theorem for $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$, which is the engine of every result in this unit. Wilson's converse is a clean primality decision procedure — exponentially slow, but theoretically perfect.

- **Group [01.02.01].** Shipped. The proofs of Fermat and Euler rest on Lagrange's theorem applied to the finite groups $(\mathbb{Z}/p)^\times$ and $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$. The order-divides-group-order pattern is the categorical core of every congruence appearing in this unit. The same Lagrange-orbit framework appears again in the necklace / Burnside proof of Fermat (Theorem 1) and in the pairing argument for Wilson (Exercise 5). The cyclic-group structure theorem from [01.02.01] is the operational core of the classification of when $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ is cyclic and hence when Fermat's congruence is sharp.

- **Primitive roots [21.01.05].** Pending. A **primitive root modulo $n$** is a generator of $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$ when this group is cyclic. Gauss 1801 §III proved primitive roots exist modulo every odd prime power $p^k$ and modulo $2 p^k$, settling the existence side of the cyclic-classification. The sharper form of Fermat — there exists $g$ with $g^{p - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod p$ and no smaller positive exponent works — promotes "order divides $p - 1$" to "order equals $p - 1$" for a witness. The downstream specialisation in [21.01.05] (when shipped) develops the index calculus, discrete logarithms, and the constructive existence of primitive roots via the lifting-the-exponent lemma.

- **Finite fields $\mathbb{F}_q$ and Frobenius [01.02.18].** Pending. The base case is $\mathbb{F}_p = \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$, where Fermat's congruence $a^p \equiv a \pmod p$ is exactly the statement that the Frobenius endomorphism $\mathrm{Frob}_p : \mathbb{F}_p \to \mathbb{F}_p$, $x \mapsto x^p$, is the identity. The freshman's-dream identity $(a + b)^p = a^p + b^p$ in $\mathbb{F}_p$ (used in Euler's induction proof of Fermat, Theorem 2 above) is the additivity of $\mathrm{Frob}_p$. Generalising to $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ replaces $p - 1$ by $p^n - 1$ and identifies $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}^\times$ as the fixed set of $\mathrm{Frob}_p^n$. The non-linear / non-abelian extension of Fermat to elliptic-curve groups appears in [04.04.03] elliptic curves.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Fermat's little theorem appears in Fermat 1640's letter of 18 October to Bernard Frénicle de Bessy [ref: TODO_REF Fermat 1640]: "tout nombre premier mesure infailliblement une des puissances - 1 de quelque progression que ce soit ..." — every prime $p$ divides infinitely many $a^k - 1$ in any arithmetic progression. Fermat states the result, claims a proof, but withholds it for fear of being "trop long" — characteristic Fermat. No proof of Fermat's appears in his extant correspondence; the first published proof is Euler 1736 *Comm. Acad. Sci. Petrop.* 8, 141 [ref: TODO_REF Euler 1736], by induction on $a$ using the binomial-coefficient divisibility $(a + 1)^p \equiv a^p + 1 \pmod p$. Euler 1763 *Novi Comm. Acad. Sci. Petrop.* 8, 74 [ref: TODO_REF Euler 1763] introduces the totient function (which Euler writes $\pi N$) and generalises the theorem to arbitrary moduli: $a^{\varphi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n$ for $\gcd(a, n) = 1$. The modern name "Fermat's little theorem" emerged in 20th-century textbooks to distinguish the result from Fermat's Last Theorem.

Wilson's theorem was stated without proof by John Wilson and communicated by Edward Waring in *Meditationes Algebraicae* (1770); the first published proof is Lagrange 1771 *Nouv. Mém. Acad. Berlin* 2, 125 [ref: TODO_REF Lagrange 1771], via the polynomial-identity argument of Proposition 3 above. Lagrange also proved the converse — if $(n - 1)! \equiv -1 \pmod n$ then $n$ is prime — making Wilson the first published exact primality criterion. Gauss 1801 *Disquisitiones Arithmeticae* arts. 50-83 [ref: TODO_REF Gauss 1801] gives the canonical modern development: arts. 50-51 (Fermat-Euler), arts. 75-78 (Wilson with the modern proof via pairing and self-inverses in $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$), and arts. 79-83 (the generalisation to arbitrary moduli, with the explicit sign determined by the structure of $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$). Gauss credits Fermat and Euler explicitly but proves the results from scratch; the *Disquisitiones* is where Fermat-Euler-Wilson enters the textbook canon.

The applied legacy crystallised in the 20th century. The Fermat primality test — declare $n$ "probably prime" if $a^{n - 1} \equiv 1 \pmod n$ for several random $a$ — was the standard heuristic until Carmichael 1910 *Bull. AMS* 16, 232 [ref: TODO_REF Carmichael 1910] showed that composite $n$ can systematically fool the test. The Carmichael numbers are exactly the composite false-positives, characterised by Korselt 1899 *Math. Ann.* 53, 173 [ref: TODO_REF Korselt 1899] via the criterion $n$ squarefree and $(p - 1) \mid (n - 1)$ for every $p \mid n$. Carmichael 1910 supplied the first example $n = 561$ and conjectured infinitely many exist; Alford-Granville-Pomerance 1994 *Annals* 140, 703 [ref: TODO_REF Alford-Granville-Pomerance 1994] proved this with the explicit lower bound $C(x) > x^{2/7}$. Miller 1976 *J. Comput. Syst. Sci.* 13, 300 [ref: TODO_REF Miller 1976] introduced strong pseudoprimes — a refinement of Fermat's congruence that excludes Carmichael numbers — and proved deterministic primality testing under the generalised Riemann hypothesis. Rabin 1980 *J. Number Th.* 12, 128 [ref: TODO_REF Rabin 1980] randomised Miller's test, producing the de facto standard for RSA key generation.

The deterministic-polynomial-time question opened by Gauss-Carmichael-Miller-Rabin was settled by Agrawal-Kayal-Saxena 2004 *Annals* 160, 781 [ref: TODO_REF AKS 2004]: PRIMES is in P. The AKS algorithm rests on the polynomial-identity criterion $(x + a)^n \equiv x^n + a \pmod{n, x^r - 1}$ — itself a strengthening of Fermat's congruence to a polynomial-ring identity. The original complexity $\tilde{O}((\log n)^{12})$ has been refined to $\tilde{O}((\log n)^{6})$ by Lenstra-Pomerance and subsequent authors. In practical software, Miller-Rabin remains faster for RSA key generation, but AKS is the theoretical deterministic certificate.

The cryptographic legacy is dominated by Rivest-Shamir-Adleman 1978 *CACM* 21, 120 [ref: TODO_REF Rivest-Shamir-Adleman 1978], who introduced RSA based on Euler's theorem applied to the modulus $N = pq$. The decryption identity $M^{ed} \equiv M \pmod N$ rests on $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\varphi(N)}$ and Euler's $M^{\varphi(N)} \equiv 1 \pmod N$ for $\gcd(M, N) = 1$. Coppersmith 1996 *Eurocrypt* and Boneh-Durfee 2000 *IEEE Trans. Inform. Theory* IT-46 showed that knowing $d$ (or partial information about $d$ when $d < N^{0.292}$) reduces to factoring $N$. Shor 1994 *Proc. FOCS 35*, 124 produced a polynomial-time quantum factoring algorithm, prompting the post-quantum cryptographic transition currently underway at NIST and global standards bodies. Even the post-quantum schemes inherit the Lagrange-orbit / Fermat-Euler-Wilson framework as their correctness identity, just with the underlying hard problem replaced.
