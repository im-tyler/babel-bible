## Intuition [Beginner]

The Riemann zeta function is built by summing the reciprocals of every positive integer, each raised to the complex power $s$. Replace the positive integers with a richer arithmetic — the **ring of integers** of a number field, the **ideals** of that ring, the **Galois symmetries** of that field — and the same construction produces a whole family of richer zeta and $L$-functions. Each member of the family encodes a different layer of arithmetic information. The simplest is the **Dedekind zeta function** of a number field $K$: in place of summing reciprocals of integers $n$, sum reciprocals of **ideal norms** $N(\mathfrak{a})$ across all non-zero ideals of $K$.

Dedekind introduced this in 1879 to extend Dirichlet's class-number formula to arbitrary number fields. Hecke extended it further in 1918 by attaching a character to each ideal, producing **Hecke $L$-functions** in the same way Dirichlet had attached characters to integers. Artin extended once more in 1923, attaching to each Galois symmetry of a field extension a **matrix representation** and producing **Artin $L$-functions** whose Euler factors record how primes split. Each step generalises the next: Riemann's zeta is the Dedekind zeta of the rationals; Dirichlet's $L$ is the Hecke $L$ of a one-dimensional character; one-dimensional Artin $L$ equals Hecke $L$ of an idele-class character by class field theory.

The unifying picture is that the Dedekind, Hecke, and Artin $L$-functions package the same kind of arithmetic information — prime distribution, class number, Galois symmetry — into analytic objects with functional equations and Euler products. Each one is a zeta function for a different layer of the arithmetic stack, and together they form the spine of modern number theory leading into the Langlands programme.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute the Dedekind zeta function of the Gaussian integers $K = \mathbb{Q}(i)$ at $s = 2$, to three Euler factors.

Step 1. Identify the small primes of $\mathbb{Z}[i] = \mathcal{O}_K$. The rational prime $2$ ramifies: $2 = -i (1 + i)^2$, so the prime ideal above $2$ is $\mathfrak{p}_2 = (1 + i)$ with norm $2$. The rational prime $3$ stays prime in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ (inert): there is one prime $\mathfrak{p}_3 = (3)$ above $3$ with norm $9$. The rational prime $5$ splits: $5 = (2 + i)(2 - i)$, giving two primes $\mathfrak{p}_5, \mathfrak{p}_5'$ each of norm $5$.

Step 2. Compute the Euler factor at each prime. At $\mathfrak{p}_2$ with norm $2$: factor is $1/(1 - 2^{-2}) = 1/(3/4) = 4/3$. At $\mathfrak{p}_3$ with norm $9$: factor is $1/(1 - 9^{-2}) = 1/(1 - 1/81) = 81/80$. At each of the two primes $\mathfrak{p}_5, \mathfrak{p}_5'$ with norm $5$: factor is $1/(1 - 5^{-2}) = 1/(24/25) = 25/24$, contributing twice.

Step 3. Multiply: $(4/3) \cdot (81/80) \cdot (25/24)^2 \approx 1.333 \cdot 1.0125 \cdot 1.085 = 1.464$. The full $\zeta_K(2)$ over all primes equals $\pi^2 / 6 \cdot \pi/4 \cdot G$ where $G$ is Catalan's constant — a transcendental relation discovered by Hurwitz in 1882.

What this tells us: the Dedekind zeta function differs from the Riemann zeta function because the prime ideals of $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ are *not* just the rational primes — some split into two, some ramify, some stay inert. The Dedekind Euler product tracks every prime ideal separately, and that finer accounting is the analytic record of how the rational primes behave inside the number field.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Three definitions, in increasing generality: Dedekind zeta, Hecke $L$, Artin $L$. Each generalises the previous.

**Definition (Dedekind zeta function).** Let $K$ be a number field — a finite extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ — and let $\mathcal{O}_K$ be its ring of integers. For a non-zero ideal $\mathfrak{a} \subset \mathcal{O}_K$, the **absolute norm** is $N(\mathfrak{a}) := |\mathcal{O}_K / \mathfrak{a}|$, a positive integer. The **Dedekind zeta function** of $K$ is the Dirichlet series

$$
\zeta_K(s) := \sum_{\mathfrak{a} \subset \mathcal{O}_K} \frac{1}{N(\mathfrak{a})^s},
$$

the sum ranging over all non-zero ideals. The series converges absolutely on the half-plane $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$ and admits the **Euler product** over prime ideals

$$
\zeta_K(s) = \prod_{\mathfrak{p}} \frac{1}{1 - N(\mathfrak{p})^{-s}},
$$

where $\mathfrak{p}$ ranges over the non-zero prime ideals of $\mathcal{O}_K$. The product formula is the analytic record of unique factorisation of ideals in the Dedekind domain $\mathcal{O}_K$.

**Definition (Hecke $L$-function).** A **Hecke character** modulo $\mathfrak{m}$ (an integral ideal of $\mathcal{O}_K$, possibly with an archimedean part $\mathfrak{m}_\infty$) is a continuous multiplicative homomorphism $\chi : I_K(\mathfrak{m}) \to \mathbb{C}^\times$ from the group of fractional ideals coprime to $\mathfrak{m}$ that factors through the ray class group $\mathrm{Cl}_K(\mathfrak{m}) = I_K(\mathfrak{m}) / P_K(\mathfrak{m})$ on the finite part and has a specified algebraic behaviour on principal ideals generated by elements close to $1$ modulo $\mathfrak{m}$. Extended by zero on ideals not coprime to $\mathfrak{m}$, the character defines the **Hecke $L$-function**

$$
L(s, \chi) := \sum_{\mathfrak{a}} \frac{\chi(\mathfrak{a})}{N(\mathfrak{a})^s} = \prod_{\mathfrak{p}} \frac{1}{1 - \chi(\mathfrak{p}) N(\mathfrak{p})^{-s}},
$$

absolutely convergent for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$. The principal Hecke character (the identity homomorphism $\chi \equiv 1$ extended by zero on the modulus) recovers the Dedekind zeta function $\zeta_K(s)$ with the Euler factors at primes dividing $\mathfrak{m}$ removed.

**Definition (Artin $L$-function).** Let $L/K$ be a finite Galois extension of number fields with Galois group $G = \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$, and let $\rho : G \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ be a finite-dimensional complex representation of $G$. For each prime $\mathfrak{p}$ of $K$ unramified in $L$, the **Frobenius conjugacy class** $\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{p} \subset G$ is well-defined up to conjugacy, and $\det(1 - \rho(\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{p}) T)$ depends only on the conjugacy class. The **Artin $L$-function** of $\rho$ is

$$
L(s, \rho, L/K) := \prod_{\mathfrak{p}} \frac{1}{\det\bigl(1 - \rho(\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{p}) N(\mathfrak{p})^{-s} \mid V^{I_\mathfrak{p}}\bigr)},
$$

where $V$ is the representation space, $I_\mathfrak{p} \subset G$ is the inertia subgroup at $\mathfrak{p}$, $V^{I_\mathfrak{p}}$ is the subspace of inertia-fixed vectors, and the Frobenius acts on $V^{I_\mathfrak{p}}$ unambiguously. The product converges absolutely on $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$. At the identity representation $\rho = \mathbf{1}$, $L(s, \mathbf{1}, L/K) = \zeta_K(s)$. At the regular representation $\rho = \mathrm{Reg}_G$, $L(s, \mathrm{Reg}_G, L/K) = \zeta_L(s)$ — the **Artin factorisation** of $\zeta_L$ as a product of Artin $L$-functions over irreducibles of $G$.

The three definitions form a strict generalisation chain: $\zeta_K = L(s, \mathbf{1}, K/\mathbb{Q})$ at the base, Hecke $L$ generalises by twisting with a one-dimensional idele-class character, Artin $L$ generalises further to non-abelian $\rho$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"Hecke characters are characters of $\mathrm{Cl}_K$ alone."* The narrow class group $\mathrm{Cl}_K = \mathrm{Cl}_K(1)$ captures only **unramified** Hecke characters. General Hecke characters require a modulus $\mathfrak{m}$ allowing ramification and an archimedean signature; the full Hecke character group is the dual of the **ray class group** $\mathrm{Cl}_K(\mathfrak{m})$. Dedekind 1879 used $\mathrm{Cl}_K$; Hecke 1918 introduced the modulus enlargement to capture characters of finite order ramified at finitely many primes.
- *"The Artin $L$-function depends only on $\rho$ up to isomorphism."* In addition to $\rho$, the Artin $L$-function depends on the extension $L/K$ through which $\rho$ factors. However, an extension argument shows that for $\rho$ factoring through both $L/K$ and $L'/K$, the two definitions give the same $L$-function — this is **Artin's invariance** under enlargement of $L$. The conventional notation $L(s, \rho)$ suppresses $L/K$ once invariance is fixed.
- *"The Artin Euler factor at a ramified prime is $\det(1 - \rho(\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{p}) N(\mathfrak{p})^{-s})^{-1}$."* At ramified primes $\mathfrak{p}$ (those with non-identity inertia $I_\mathfrak{p}$), $\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{p}$ is defined only on the quotient $D_\mathfrak{p}/I_\mathfrak{p}$, hence only on the **inertia-fixed subspace** $V^{I_\mathfrak{p}}$. The correct Euler factor is the characteristic polynomial of $\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{p}$ restricted to $V^{I_\mathfrak{p}}$, of degree $\dim V^{I_\mathfrak{p}} \leq n$ rather than $n$. Forgetting the inertia restriction breaks the Artin factorisation $\zeta_L(s) = \prod_\rho L(s, \rho)^{\dim \rho}$ for ramified extensions.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem of this unit is the **Artin factorisation** of $\zeta_L$ as a product of Artin $L$-functions over irreducible Galois representations.

**Theorem (Artin factorisation; Artin 1923).** *Let $L/K$ be a finite Galois extension of number fields with Galois group $G$. Let $\widehat{G}$ denote a set of representatives of the irreducible complex representations of $G$, and let $\dim \rho$ denote the dimension of an irreducible $\rho \in \widehat{G}$. Then*

$$
\zeta_L(s) = \prod_{\rho \in \widehat{G}} L(s, \rho, L/K)^{\dim \rho}.
$$

*Proof.* The proof is one identification of Euler factors prime-by-prime, plus character-theoretic accounting.

The regular representation $\mathrm{Reg}_G$ of $G$ decomposes into irreducibles as

$$
\mathrm{Reg}_G \cong \bigoplus_{\rho \in \widehat{G}} \rho^{\oplus \dim \rho},
$$

a finite-group representation-theory identity ([ref: TODO_REF Serre Représentations] §2.4). For each prime $\mathfrak{p}$ of $K$, fix a prime $\mathfrak{P}$ of $L$ above $\mathfrak{p}$. Let $D = D_\mathfrak{P} \subset G$ be the decomposition group at $\mathfrak{P}$ and $I = I_\mathfrak{P} \subset D$ the inertia subgroup. The **Frobenius** $\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{P} \in D/I$ is a well-defined element acting on residue-field extensions.

The Euler factor of $\zeta_L$ at any prime $\mathfrak{P}' \mid \mathfrak{p}$ is $(1 - N(\mathfrak{P}')^{-s})^{-1}$. Since $N(\mathfrak{P}') = N(\mathfrak{p})^{f(\mathfrak{P}' \mid \mathfrak{p})}$ where $f(\mathfrak{P}' \mid \mathfrak{p}) = |D_{\mathfrak{P}'}/I_{\mathfrak{P}'}|$ is the residue-degree, and there are $g(\mathfrak{p}) = [G : D]$ primes above $\mathfrak{p}$, all conjugate under $G$, the total Euler contribution to $\zeta_L$ at primes above $\mathfrak{p}$ is

$$
\prod_{\mathfrak{P}' \mid \mathfrak{p}} \frac{1}{1 - N(\mathfrak{p})^{-f(\mathfrak{P}' \mid \mathfrak{p}) s}} = \prod_{j} \frac{1}{1 - q^{-f j s}}
$$

after appropriate grouping with $q = N(\mathfrak{p})$ and the product over orbits of Frobenius on $G/D$.

The Euler factor of $L(s, \rho, L/K)$ at $\mathfrak{p}$ is $\det\bigl(1 - \rho(\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{p}) q^{-s} \mid V_\rho^I \bigr)^{-1}$. Taking the product of the Artin Euler factors over irreducibles weighted by $\dim \rho$ gives

$$
\prod_\rho \det\bigl(1 - \rho(\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{p}) q^{-s} \mid V_\rho^I\bigr)^{-\dim \rho} = \det\bigl(1 - \mathrm{Reg}_G(\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{p}) q^{-s} \mid \mathrm{Reg}_G^I\bigr)^{-1}
$$

using the decomposition of $\mathrm{Reg}_G$ into irreducibles. The right side is the determinant on the inertia-fixed subspace of the regular representation, which by character theory equals

$$
\prod_{\sigma \in D/I} (1 - q^{-\ell(\sigma) s})^{-1}
$$

where $\ell(\sigma)$ is the order of $\sigma$ in $D/I$. Reorganising by Frobenius orbits and matching the orbit structure with the decomposition of primes $\mathfrak{P}' \mid \mathfrak{p}$ (each orbit of size $f(\mathfrak{P}' \mid \mathfrak{p})$ contributes one prime above $\mathfrak{p}$) recovers the Euler factor of $\zeta_L$ at $\mathfrak{p}$.

Since the identification holds prime-by-prime and both sides converge absolutely on $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$, the global identity follows. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Artin factorisation builds toward [21.05.01] $\ell$-adic Galois representations, where the same multiplicative decomposition appears with $\ell$-adic coefficients in place of $\mathbb{C}$ and the Euler factors of the modular Galois representation $\rho_f$ split $\zeta_E(s) = L(E, s)$ at every prime of good reduction. The foundational reason is that the Artin formalism is fully functorial in the Galois group $G$: the decomposition $\mathrm{Reg}_G = \bigoplus \rho^{\dim \rho}$ at the level of representations turns into the Artin factorisation at the level of $L$-functions, and this is exactly the bridge between finite-group representation theory and the analytic theory of $L$-functions. Putting these together with Brauer 1947 induction identifies one-dimensional Artin $L$-functions with Hecke $L$-functions by class field theory (Artin reciprocity, Artin 1927); generalises to the Langlands functoriality conjectures placing every Artin $L$ inside the automorphic spectrum of $\mathrm{GL}_n$ over $K$, where the holomorphy of $L(s, \rho)$ for $\rho$ non-identity is a theorem (for $\rho$ corresponding to a cuspidal automorphic representation) rather than a conjecture.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

State the **analytic class-number formula** for $\zeta_K(s)$ at $s = 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Dedekind zeta function has a simple pole at $s = 1$. The residue is an invariant combination of arithmetic data of $K$: the class number $h_K$, the regulator $R_K$, the number of roots of unity $w_K$, the discriminant $d_K$, and the signature $(r_1, r_2)$ counting real and complex embeddings.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Statement (Dedekind 1879; Hecke 1917 for the general extension).** Let $K$ be a number field with $r_1$ real embeddings, $2 r_2$ complex embeddings (so $[K : \mathbb{Q}] = r_1 + 2 r_2$), discriminant $d_K$, regulator $R_K$, class number $h_K$, and $w_K$ roots of unity. Then $\zeta_K(s)$ has a simple pole at $s = 1$ with residue

$$
\mathrm{res}_{s=1} \zeta_K(s) = \frac{2^{r_1} (2\pi)^{r_2} h_K R_K}{w_K \sqrt{|d_K|}}.
$$

Equivalently, at $s = 0$, $\zeta_K(s)$ has a zero of order $r_1 + r_2 - 1$ with the leading Taylor coefficient

$$
\lim_{s \to 0} \frac{\zeta_K(s)}{s^{r_1 + r_2 - 1}} = -\frac{h_K R_K}{w_K}.
$$

**Proof idea (Dedekind 1879, modern treatment in Neukirch 1999 Ch. VII).** Count ideals $\mathfrak{a}$ of $\mathcal{O}_K$ with $N(\mathfrak{a}) \leq T$ as $T \to \infty$: by the Minkowski lattice-point theorem applied to each ideal class separately, the count is $\kappa T + O(T^{1 - 1/[K:\mathbb{Q}]})$ where $\kappa = h_K \cdot 2^{r_1} (2\pi)^{r_2} R_K / (w_K \sqrt{|d_K|})$. The Dirichlet-series machinery converts the asymptotic of $\#\{\mathfrak{a} : N(\mathfrak{a}) \leq T\}$ into the residue of $\zeta_K(s)$ at $s = 1$. The five arithmetic invariants enter as: $h_K$ (number of ideal classes contributing to the count), $R_K$ (lattice volume of the unit group inside the logarithmic embedding), $w_K$ (kernel of the logarithmic embedding), $|d_K|$ (covolume of the Minkowski lattice), $2^{r_1} (2\pi)^{r_2}$ (Euclidean volume of the unit ball under the Minkowski embedding at each embedding type).

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

State the functional equation of the Dedekind zeta function $\zeta_K(s)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The completed Dedekind zeta function carries Gamma factors at each archimedean place of $K$. A real embedding contributes a factor $\Gamma_\mathbb{R}(s) := \pi^{-s/2} \Gamma(s/2)$; a complex embedding contributes a factor $\Gamma_\mathbb{C}(s) := (2\pi)^{-s} \Gamma(s) = 2(2\pi)^{-s} \Gamma(s)$ (conventions vary by a factor of $2$).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Statement (Hecke 1917, 1920).** With signature $(r_1, r_2)$ and discriminant $d_K$, define the completed Dedekind zeta function

$$
\Lambda_K(s) := |d_K|^{s/2} \Gamma_\mathbb{R}(s)^{r_1} \Gamma_\mathbb{C}(s)^{r_2} \zeta_K(s),
$$

where $\Gamma_\mathbb{R}(s) = \pi^{-s/2} \Gamma(s/2)$ and $\Gamma_\mathbb{C}(s) = (2\pi)^{-s} \Gamma(s)$. Then $\Lambda_K(s)$ extends to a meromorphic function on $\mathbb{C}$ with simple poles at $s = 0$ and $s = 1$ and no other singularities, and satisfies the symmetric functional equation

$$
\Lambda_K(s) = \Lambda_K(1 - s).
$$

**Proof route.** Hecke 1917 generalised Riemann's theta-symmetry proof to $K$ by introducing a higher-dimensional theta series $\Theta_K(\mathbf{t}) = \sum_{\alpha \in \mathcal{O}_K} \exp(-\pi \sum_i t_i |\sigma_i(\alpha)|^2)$ summed over $\mathcal{O}_K$ via the Minkowski embedding into $\mathbb{R}^{r_1} \times \mathbb{C}^{r_2}$. The Poisson summation formula applied to the lattice $\mathcal{O}_K$ (with covolume $\sqrt{|d_K|} / 2^{r_2}$) gives a $\Theta_K$-inversion identity; the Mellin transform splits at $\mathbf{t} = \mathbf{1}$ and the inversion exchanges the $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$ and $\operatorname{Re}(s) < 0$ regions, producing the functional equation after collecting the Gamma factors. Tate's 1950 thesis [ref: TODO_REF Tate 1950] rederives the same functional equation purely from Fourier-Pontryagin self-duality of the adele ring $\mathbb{A}_K$, with the Gamma factors emerging as the archimedean local zeta integrals.

</details>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

State the **Aramata-Brauer theorem** on the ratio $\zeta_L(s) / \zeta_K(s)$ for a Galois extension $L/K$, and explain why the Artin factorisation makes the statement immediate.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Artin factorisation $\zeta_L(s) = \prod_\rho L(s, \rho)^{\dim \rho}$ over irreducibles $\rho$ of $\mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$ has $\zeta_K(s) = L(s, \mathbf{1})$ as the factor at the identity representation. Dividing out the identity-representation factor gives the ratio.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Statement.** $\zeta_L(s) / \zeta_K(s)$ is an entire function of $s$ — i.e., it has no poles anywhere on $\mathbb{C}$.

**Proof via Artin factorisation.** By the Artin factorisation applied to $L/K$ with Galois group $G$,

$$
\zeta_L(s) = \zeta_K(s) \cdot \prod_{\substack{\rho \in \widehat{G} \\ \rho \neq \mathbf{1}}} L(s, \rho, L/K)^{\dim \rho}.
$$

Hence $\zeta_L(s) / \zeta_K(s) = \prod_{\rho \neq \mathbf{1}} L(s, \rho)^{\dim \rho}$. Each factor $L(s, \rho)$ for $\rho$ a non-identity irreducible Artin representation is **conjectured** to be entire (Artin's holomorphy conjecture). The Aramata-Brauer theorem asserts the product is entire even though the individual Artin $L$-functions are only known to be meromorphic (by Brauer 1947). The proof uses **Brauer induction** ([ref: TODO_REF Brauer 1947]): every character of $G$ is an integral combination of monomial characters $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G \psi$ for subgroups $H$ and one-dimensional characters $\psi$ of $H$; by the inductive property $L(s, \mathrm{Ind}_H^G \psi, L/K) = L(s, \psi, L/L^H)$, and the right side is a Hecke $L$-function which is known entire for $\psi \neq \mathbf{1}$ by Hecke 1918. The negative-coefficient parts of the integral combination contribute factors $L(s, \rho)^{-m}$, but the *aggregate* function $\zeta_L / \zeta_K$ has no negative-power Hecke factors because the identity representation only appears in $\zeta_K$ on the right side. This is the Aramata-Brauer argument, originally due to Aramata 1933 *Proc. Imp. Acad. Tokyo* 9 for $L/K$ abelian and extended by Brauer 1947 to arbitrary Galois $L/K$.

</details>

The Galois group $G = \mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_5)/\mathbb{Q}) \cong (\mathbb{Z}/5)^\times = \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$ under multiplication mod $5$, cyclic of order $4$ generated by $2$ (since $2^2 = 4$, $2^3 = 3$, $2^4 = 1$ mod $5$). $$
\zeta_{\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_5)}(s) = \prod_{j=0}^{3} L(s, \chi_j, \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_5)/\mathbb{Q}) = \zeta(s) \cdot L(s, \chi_1^{(5)}) \cdot L(s, \chi_2^{(5)}) \cdot L(s, \chi_3^{(5)}),

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

State the **Brauer induction theorem** and explain how it gives the meromorphic continuation of every Artin $L$-function to $\mathbb{C}$, even without proving the Artin holomorphy conjecture.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Brauer 1947 proved that every character $\chi$ of a finite group $G$ can be written as an integral combination $\chi = \sum_i n_i \mathrm{Ind}_{H_i}^G \psi_i$ where $H_i \subset G$ are subgroups (specifically: elementary subgroups, products of a $p$-group and a cyclic group), $\psi_i$ are one-dimensional characters of $H_i$, and $n_i \in \mathbb{Z}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Brauer induction theorem (Brauer 1947).** Every complex character $\chi$ of a finite group $G$ is an integral combination

$$
\chi = \sum_i n_i \mathrm{Ind}_{H_i}^G \psi_i, \quad n_i \in \mathbb{Z},
$$

where each $H_i \subset G$ is an **elementary subgroup** (the direct product of a $p$-group and a cyclic group of order prime to $p$) and each $\psi_i$ is a one-dimensional character of $H_i$ ([ref: TODO_REF Brauer 1947]; [ref: TODO_REF Serre Représentations] §10.5).

**Application to Artin $L$.** Apply Brauer induction to the character $\chi_\rho$ of an Artin representation $\rho : \mathrm{Gal}(L/K) \to \mathrm{GL}(V)$. By the **inductivity** of Artin $L$ (Artin 1923 §3),

$$
L(s, \mathrm{Ind}_H^G \psi, L/K) = L(s, \psi, L/L^H),
$$

and by Artin's **additivity in $\rho$**,

$$
L(s, \rho \oplus \rho', L/K) = L(s, \rho, L/K) \cdot L(s, \rho', L/K).
$$

Combining the two, the character identity $\chi_\rho = \sum_i n_i \mathrm{Ind}_{H_i}^G \psi_i$ promotes to a multiplicative identity

$$
L(s, \rho, L/K) = \prod_i L(s, \psi_i, L/L^{H_i})^{n_i}.
$$

Each $L(s, \psi_i, L/L^{H_i})$ on the right is a one-dimensional Artin $L$-function over an intermediate field $L^{H_i}$, which by Artin reciprocity (Artin 1927) equals the Hecke $L$-function $L(s, \psi_i^*)$ of a Grössencharacter $\psi_i^*$ on the idele class group of $L^{H_i}$. By Hecke 1918, $L(s, \psi_i^*)$ extends meromorphically to $\mathbb{C}$ (entire for $\psi_i^*$ non-principal, with a single simple pole at $s = 1$ for $\psi_i^*$ principal).

Hence $L(s, \rho, L/K)$ is a finite product of integer powers of meromorphic Hecke $L$-functions, and is itself meromorphic on $\mathbb{C}$. **However**, negative powers $n_i < 0$ in the Brauer decomposition mean that $L(s, \rho)$ could acquire poles at zeros of the corresponding Hecke $L$-functions, and the Artin holomorphy conjecture is the statement that these poles cancel — which Brauer induction does **not** prove in general. The Aramata-Brauer theorem ([ref: TODO_REF Brauer 1947]) gives the special case that $\zeta_L(s) / \zeta_K(s)$ is entire, by a more careful accounting; the general Artin conjecture is known for solvable Galois groups (Langlands-Tunnell base change) and for image-in-$\mathrm{GL}_2$ cases via the Modularity Lifting Theorems, but remains open in general.

The Brauer-induction argument is the foundational technique for analytic continuation of non-abelian $L$-functions and the prototype for the modern Langlands programme: every $L$-function is conjecturally a finite product of $\mathrm{GL}_n$ automorphic $L$-functions, and the meromorphic continuation comes from this product expansion.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

### Dedekind 1879 and the class-number formula

Richard Dedekind introduced $\zeta_K(s)$ in 1879 in Supplement X to the fourth edition of Dirichlet's *Vorlesungen über Zahlentheorie* [ref: TODO_REF Dedekind 1879]. The construction was already implicit in Dirichlet's own work on quadratic fields, but Dedekind made it general: replace the rational integers with the ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$ of any number field, replace integers $n$ with non-zero ideals $\mathfrak{a}$, replace $n^{-s}$ with $N(\mathfrak{a})^{-s}$, and the resulting Dirichlet series converges absolutely on $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$. The Euler product over prime ideals records unique factorisation of ideals in the Dedekind domain $\mathcal{O}_K$ — a property that Dedekind himself had introduced in the same supplement to recover unique factorisation when element-level factorisation fails (e.g., the Kummer phenomenon $6 = 2 \cdot 3 = (1 + \sqrt{-5})(1 - \sqrt{-5})$).

The signature theorem is the **analytic class-number formula** (Exercise 3):

$$
\mathrm{res}_{s=1} \zeta_K(s) = \frac{2^{r_1} (2\pi)^{r_2} h_K R_K}{w_K \sqrt{|d_K|}}.
$$

Dedekind proved this for arbitrary number fields by extending Dirichlet's 1839/40 computation for quadratic fields. The proof counts ideals of norm $\leq T$ by counting lattice points in scaled fundamental domains via the Minkowski embedding $K \otimes_\mathbb{Q} \mathbb{R} \cong \mathbb{R}^{r_1} \oplus \mathbb{C}^{r_2}$, using the **Dirichlet unit theorem** $\mathcal{O}_K^\times \cong \mu_K \times \mathbb{Z}^{r_1 + r_2 - 1}$ (proved by Dirichlet 1846 *Math. Werke* I) to control the action of the unit group on each ideal class. The five invariants $(h_K, R_K, w_K, |d_K|, (r_1, r_2))$ are read off from the lattice-counting data: $h_K$ is the number of ideal classes contributing, $R_K$ is the lattice covolume of the unit group, $w_K$ is the order of the torsion subgroup, $|d_K|$ is the covolume of $\mathcal{O}_K$ in the Minkowski embedding, and $(r_1, r_2)$ enters through the Euclidean volume of the unit ball in $\mathbb{R}^{r_1} \oplus \mathbb{C}^{r_2}$.

The class-number formula is the analytic shadow of the fundamental finiteness theorems of algebraic number theory: $h_K < \infty$ (class number finite, Minkowski 1891), $\mathcal{O}_K^\times$ finitely generated (Dirichlet unit theorem). The formula identifies the *quantitative* content of these qualitative theorems with the residue of an analytic function.

### Hecke 1918, 1920 — Hecke characters and the higher functional equation

Erich Hecke generalised Dedekind's construction in two papers in *Math. Z.* [ref: TODO_REF Hecke 1918] [ref: TODO_REF Hecke 1920]. The first paper introduced **Hecke characters**: continuous multiplicative characters $\chi : I_K(\mathfrak{m}) \to \mathbb{C}^\times$ on the group of fractional ideals coprime to a modulus $\mathfrak{m}$, factoring through the ray class group $\mathrm{Cl}_K(\mathfrak{m})$ on the finite part and prescribed by algebraic data on the archimedean part (the **Grössencharacter** condition: on principal ideals $(\alpha)$ with $\alpha \equiv 1 \bmod \mathfrak{m}$, $\chi((\alpha))$ equals a specified character $\alpha \mapsto \prod_\sigma \sigma(\alpha)^{a_\sigma} |\sigma(\alpha)|^{b_\sigma}$ in the embeddings $\sigma$).

A Hecke character defines the **Hecke $L$-function** $L(s, \chi) = \sum_\mathfrak{a} \chi(\mathfrak{a}) N(\mathfrak{a})^{-s}$ with Euler product $\prod_\mathfrak{p} (1 - \chi(\mathfrak{p}) N(\mathfrak{p})^{-s})^{-1}$, absolutely convergent for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$.

The signature theorem of the second paper is the **functional equation**. Define the completed $L$-function

$$
\Lambda(s, \chi) := N(\mathfrak{f}(\chi))^{s/2} \prod_v L_v(s, \chi) \cdot L(s, \chi),
$$

where $\mathfrak{f}(\chi)$ is the conductor of $\chi$ and $L_v(s, \chi)$ runs over local Gamma factors $\Gamma_\mathbb{R}((s + a_\sigma)/2)$ or $\Gamma_\mathbb{C}(s + b_\sigma)$ at each archimedean place $v$ of $K$. Then $\Lambda(s, \chi)$ extends meromorphically to $\mathbb{C}$, is entire for $\chi$ non-principal, and satisfies

$$
\Lambda(s, \chi) = W(\chi) \Lambda(1 - s, \overline{\chi})
$$

with root number $W(\chi)$ a Gauss-sum-built unit complex number, $|W(\chi)| = 1$. The Dedekind zeta function $\zeta_K(s)$ is the case $\chi = \mathbf{1}$ (principal), with $\Lambda_K(s) = \Lambda(s, \mathbf{1})$ acquiring poles at $s = 0, 1$ from the principal character contribution.

Hecke's proof generalises Riemann's theta-symmetry route to the lattice $\mathcal{O}_K$ inside $K_\infty = K \otimes_\mathbb{Q} \mathbb{R} \cong \mathbb{R}^{r_1} \oplus \mathbb{C}^{r_2}$. The relevant theta series is

$$
\Theta(\mathbf{t}, \chi) := \sum_{\alpha \in \mathcal{O}_K \setminus 0} \chi((\alpha)) e^{-\pi \sum_v t_v |\sigma_v(\alpha)|^2},
$$

and the higher-dimensional Poisson summation formula on the lattice $\mathcal{O}_K \subset K_\infty$ gives an inversion identity $\Theta(1/\mathbf{t}, \chi) = (\text{root-number factor}) \cdot \Theta(\mathbf{t}, \overline{\chi})$. The Mellin transform exchanges $s \leftrightarrow 1 - s$.

### Artin 1923, 1930 — Artin $L$-functions and the holomorphy conjecture

Emil Artin generalised Hecke's construction in 1923 [ref: TODO_REF Artin 1923] by attaching $L$-functions to **Galois representations**. The setup: a finite Galois extension $L/K$ with Galois group $G$, and a finite-dimensional representation $\rho : G \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$. For each unramified prime $\mathfrak{p}$ of $K$, the Frobenius conjugacy class $\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{p} \subset G$ is well-defined, and the local Euler factor is

$$
L_\mathfrak{p}(s, \rho) := \det(1 - \rho(\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{p}) N(\mathfrak{p})^{-s})^{-1},
$$

a degree-$n$ rational function in $N(\mathfrak{p})^{-s}$. At ramified primes, replace $V$ by the inertia-fixed subspace $V^{I_\mathfrak{p}}$ as in the formal definition.

The signature theorem of Artin 1923 is the **factorisation** of $\zeta_L$ over irreducibles of $G$ (proved as the Key Theorem above). The signature *conjecture* of Artin 1923 — still open in general — is the **Artin holomorphy conjecture**: for every non-identity irreducible $\rho$, the Artin $L$-function $L(s, \rho, L/K)$ extends to an entire function of $s$.

Artin himself proved the conjecture for one-dimensional $\rho$ in 1927 [ref: TODO_REF Artin 1930] using **Artin reciprocity**: every one-dimensional character $\rho$ of $\mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$ corresponds, by class field theory, to a Hecke Grössencharacter $\widetilde{\rho}$ on the idele class group of $K$, and the Artin $L$-function $L(s, \rho, L/K)$ equals the Hecke $L$-function $L(s, \widetilde{\rho})$ exactly. By Hecke 1918, the latter is entire for non-identity $\widetilde{\rho}$. **This identification is the analytic content of Artin reciprocity** and the historical origin of the Langlands programme's organising principle: every "motivic" $L$-function should equal an "automorphic" $L$-function via a reciprocity isomorphism.

For higher-dimensional $\rho$, the conjecture is known in special cases: two-dimensional $\rho$ with solvable image (Langlands-Tunnell, via base change for $\mathrm{GL}_2$), two-dimensional $\rho$ of icosahedral image (Buzzard-Dickinson-Shepherd-Barron-Taylor 2001), and odd two-dimensional $\rho$ of any image (Khare-Wintenberger 2009, the Serre modularity conjecture). For higher-dimensional $\rho$ general the conjecture follows from Langlands functoriality.

### Brauer 1947 — meromorphic continuation in full generality

Richard Brauer 1947 [ref: TODO_REF Brauer 1947] proved that every Artin $L$-function is **meromorphic** on $\mathbb{C}$, even without the Artin holomorphy conjecture. The key input is the **Brauer induction theorem** (Exercise 8): every character of a finite group $G$ is an integral combination of characters $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G \psi$ for elementary subgroups $H$ and one-dimensional $\psi$. Combined with Artin's inductivity $L(s, \mathrm{Ind}_H^G \psi, L/K) = L(s, \psi, L/L^H)$ and one-dimensional-reduces-to-Hecke via Artin reciprocity, every Artin $L$-function factors as an **integer-power product** of meromorphic Hecke $L$-functions. The result is meromorphic continuation to $\mathbb{C}$ for every $\rho$, but with the caveat that negative powers in the Brauer decomposition could introduce extra poles. The Artin holomorphy conjecture is the assertion that these poles cancel — and Brauer's argument does not prove this.

Brauer's theorem is the foundational tool for analytic continuation of Galois-representation $L$-functions and the prototype for the modern Langlands programme. The Aramata-Brauer corollary (Exercise 5) that $\zeta_L(s) / \zeta_K(s)$ is entire is the cleanest application: the identity-representation contribution to the Brauer decomposition only appears in $\zeta_K$, so the quotient has no $\mathrm{Hecke}_{\mathbf{1}}$ factor and inherits Hecke holomorphy.

### Tate 1950 — adelic reformulation and the Langlands bridge

John Tate's 1950 Princeton thesis [ref: TODO_REF Tate 1950], published in the Cassels-Fröhlich proceedings of the 1965 Brighton conference, recast the Hecke $L$-function machinery in the language of the **adele ring** $\mathbb{A}_K$ and **idele class group** $\mathbb{A}_K^\times / K^\times$. A Hecke character is reinterpreted as a continuous character $\chi : \mathbb{A}_K^\times / K^\times \to \mathbb{C}^\times$ on the idele class group; the Hecke $L$-function is reinterpreted as a global zeta integral

$$
\zeta(s, f, \chi) := \int_{\mathbb{A}_K^\times} f(x) \chi(x) |x|^s \, d^\times x
$$

against a Schwartz-Bruhat function $f \in \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{A}_K)$. The Mellin/Poisson decomposition into local factors and the Fourier-Pontryagin self-duality of $\mathbb{A}_K$ yield the functional equation $\zeta(s, f, \chi) = \zeta(1 - s, \widehat{f}, \chi^{-1})$ directly, with the Hecke Gamma factors emerging as the archimedean local zeta integrals $\zeta_v(s, f_v, \chi_v) = \int_{K_v^\times} f_v(x) \chi_v(x) |x|_v^s d^\times x$.

Tate's adelic reformulation is the **$\mathrm{GL}_1$ prototype of the Langlands programme**: every Hecke $L$-function is a $\mathrm{GL}_1$ automorphic $L$-function over $K$, and the higher-rank analogues are the $\mathrm{GL}_n$ automorphic $L$-functions of Godement-Jacquet 1972 and Jacquet-Piatetski-Shapiro-Shalika 1979. The Langlands conjectures predict that every Artin $L$-function of dimension $n$ is the automorphic $L$-function of a cuspidal $\mathrm{GL}_n$ representation; this implies the Artin holomorphy conjecture as a corollary because automorphic $L$-functions are entire by construction.

### Weil 1952 — Hecke characters of Type $A_0$ and CM elliptic curves

André Weil 1952 [ref: TODO_REF Weil 1952] introduced the classification of Hecke characters by **type at infinity**. A Hecke character $\chi$ on $K$ is of **Type $A_0$** if there exist integers $a_\sigma$ for each archimedean embedding $\sigma : K \hookrightarrow \mathbb{C}$ such that $\chi((\alpha)) = \prod_\sigma \sigma(\alpha)^{a_\sigma}$ on principal ideals $(\alpha)$ for $\alpha$ close to $1$ at the conductor primes. Type $A_0$ characters are the **algebraic Grössencharacters** of Weil 1952 — they admit a description as homomorphisms of the Serre-Tate motive of $K$ — and they are the Hecke characters that appear naturally in arithmetic geometry, specifically in $L$-functions of CM elliptic curves and of CM abelian varieties.

The signature application: if $E$ is an elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ with complex multiplication by the ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$ of an imaginary quadratic field $K$, then the $L$-function $L(E, s) = L(s, \rho_E)$ of the associated $\ell$-adic Galois representation $\rho_E$ equals the Hecke $L$-function $L(s - 1/2, \chi_E)$ of an explicit Type $A_0$ character $\chi_E$ on $K$, with $a_\sigma = 1, 0$ for the two complex embeddings $\sigma, \overline{\sigma}$. This is the **CM case of the Hasse-Weil conjecture**, due to Deuring 1953-57 *Nachr. Göttingen* and Shimura 1971 *Introduction to the Arithmetic Theory of Automorphic Functions*, and was the historical anchor for the modularity programme (the CM case being the prototype that the general case eventually matched via Wiles 1995 and Breuil-Conrad-Diamond-Taylor 2001).

### Langlands 1970 — functoriality and the Artin conjecture as a corollary

Robert Langlands 1970 [ref: TODO_REF Langlands 1970] proposed the **functoriality conjectures**: for every reductive group $G$ over a number field $K$ and every $L$-homomorphism $\phi : {}^L H \to {}^L G$ of $L$-groups, automorphic representations $\pi$ of $H(\mathbb{A}_K)$ lift to automorphic representations $\Pi$ of $G(\mathbb{A}_K)$ with matching $L$-functions $L(s, \pi, \phi) = L(s, \Pi)$. The special case $H = \{1\}$ (identity group) and $G = \mathrm{GL}_n$ with $\phi$ encoding an $n$-dimensional Galois representation $\rho$ asserts that every Artin $L$-function $L(s, \rho)$ is the automorphic $L$-function $L(s, \Pi_\rho)$ of a cuspidal $\mathrm{GL}_n$ automorphic representation. Since cuspidal automorphic $L$-functions are entire by Jacquet-Godement, this **implies the Artin holomorphy conjecture** as a corollary.

The Langlands functoriality conjectures are the modern home of the entire $L$-function framework: $\zeta_K$, Hecke $L$, Artin $L$, automorphic $L$, motivic $L$, and the $L$-functions of Galois representations attached to varieties over $K$ are all instances of $\mathrm{GL}_n/K$ automorphic $L$-functions with appropriate parameters. The conjectures remain open in general but are known in major cases: $\mathrm{GL}_1$ (Tate 1950, class field theory), $\mathrm{GL}_2$ base change (Langlands 1980), $\mathrm{GL}_2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ for odd 2-dimensional Galois representations (Khare-Wintenberger 2009, Serre's conjecture), and the modularity of elliptic curves over $\mathbb{Q}$ (Wiles 1995, Taylor-Wiles 1995, BCDT 2001).

**Synthesis.** The Dedekind-Hecke-Artin trio is the foundational reason that modern number theory has a single coherent $L$-function framework. The central insight is that every layer of arithmetic data — number field structure, ideal-class data, ramification, Galois symmetry, motivic decomposition — packages into an $L$-function with an Euler product and a functional equation, and the structural relations among these $L$-functions (Artin factorisation, Hecke reciprocity, automorphic equality) are the analytic shadows of structural relations among the underlying arithmetic objects (class field theory, Galois theory, motivic decomposition). Putting these together with Tate's adelic reformulation, every classical $L$-function becomes a $\mathrm{GL}_n/K$ automorphic $L$-function via the Langlands programme, and the bridge is the reciprocity isomorphism identifying Galois representations with automorphic representations.

The pattern recurs at every level: Riemann zeta is the foundational analytic object; Dirichlet $L$ generalises by twisting with a finite character; Dedekind zeta generalises by replacing $\mathbb{Z}$ with $\mathcal{O}_K$; Hecke $L$ generalises by twisting with an idele-class character on $K$; Artin $L$ generalises by replacing one-dimensional characters with finite-dimensional Galois representations; automorphic $L$ generalises by replacing Galois representations with cuspidal automorphic representations of $\mathrm{GL}_n$. Each generalisation step builds toward [21.04.01] modular forms (where $\mathrm{GL}_2/\mathbb{Q}$ automorphic $L$-functions live), [21.05.01] $\ell$-adic Galois representations (where the Artin formalism extends to $\ell$-adic coefficients), [21.06.01] the modularity theorem (where the Artin $L$-function of an elliptic curve equals the Hecke $L$-function of a weight-2 modular form), and [21.10.01] the Langlands programme (where the functoriality conjectures unify the whole framework). The foundational reason is that the analytic functional equation is the shadow of a representation-theoretic duality on the underlying arithmetic objects, and this is exactly the bridge from arithmetic to harmonic analysis on the idele class group, an idea due to Tate 1950 that organises all subsequent developments.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Dedekind Euler product).** *Let $K$ be a number field. For $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$, the Dirichlet series $\sum_{\mathfrak{a}} 1/N(\mathfrak{a})^s$ equals the Euler product $\prod_{\mathfrak{p}} (1 - N(\mathfrak{p})^{-s})^{-1}$ over prime ideals.*

*Proof.* The ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$ of a number field is a **Dedekind domain**: every non-zero ideal $\mathfrak{a}$ factors uniquely as a product of prime ideals $\mathfrak{a} = \prod_\mathfrak{p} \mathfrak{p}^{v_\mathfrak{p}(\mathfrak{a})}$ with all but finitely many exponents zero. The ideal norm is completely multiplicative: $N(\mathfrak{a} \mathfrak{b}) = N(\mathfrak{a}) N(\mathfrak{b})$ for any non-zero ideals $\mathfrak{a}, \mathfrak{b}$ (a consequence of the Chinese Remainder Theorem in $\mathcal{O}_K$).

For $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$, both sides converge absolutely. Expand each Euler factor as a geometric series $(1 - N(\mathfrak{p})^{-s})^{-1} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty N(\mathfrak{p})^{-ks}$. Multiply the series over all primes; by absolute convergence, the multiplication rearranges into a sum over all multi-indices $(k_\mathfrak{p})$ with all but finitely many $k_\mathfrak{p} = 0$. Each multi-index corresponds via unique ideal factorisation to a unique non-zero ideal $\mathfrak{a} = \prod_\mathfrak{p} \mathfrak{p}^{k_\mathfrak{p}}$, and the contribution is $\prod_\mathfrak{p} N(\mathfrak{p})^{-k_\mathfrak{p} s} = N(\mathfrak{a})^{-s}$ by multiplicativity. The sum over all multi-indices is the Dirichlet series. $\square$

**Proposition (analytic continuation of $\zeta_K$).** *The Dedekind zeta function $\zeta_K(s)$ extends to a meromorphic function on $\mathbb{C}$ with a single simple pole at $s = 1$.*

*Proof.* Two routes, both due to Hecke 1917.

**Route 1 (lattice-counting).** Count ideals of norm $\leq T$: $N_K(T) := \#\{\mathfrak{a} : N(\mathfrak{a}) \leq T\}$ has the asymptotic $N_K(T) = \kappa T + O(T^{1 - 1/[K:\mathbb{Q}]})$ where $\kappa = 2^{r_1}(2\pi)^{r_2} h_K R_K / (w_K \sqrt{|d_K|})$ (Dedekind 1879, lattice point count). By the Dirichlet-series / Abel-summation correspondence, $\zeta_K(s) = \int_0^\infty T^{-s} \, dN_K(T) = s \int_0^\infty N_K(T) T^{-s-1} dT$ for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$. Substituting the asymptotic and integrating term-by-term gives $\zeta_K(s) = \kappa/(s - 1) + $ (analytic on $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1 - 1/[K:\mathbb{Q}]$). Continuation past $\operatorname{Re}(s) = 1 - 1/[K:\mathbb{Q}]$ requires the theta-symmetry route.

**Route 2 (theta-symmetry).** Mimic Riemann's proof for $\zeta$. Define the higher-dimensional theta function $\Theta_K(\mathbf{t}) = \sum_{\alpha \in \mathcal{O}_K} e^{-\pi \sum_i t_i |\sigma_i(\alpha)|^2}$ summed over the lattice $\mathcal{O}_K$ via the Minkowski embedding. The Poisson summation formula applied to the lattice $\mathcal{O}_K \subset K_\infty = \mathbb{R}^{r_1} \oplus \mathbb{C}^{r_2}$ gives the theta inversion $\Theta_K(1/\mathbf{t}) = \sqrt{|d_K|}^{-1} \cdot (\text{Jacobian factor}) \cdot \Theta_K(\mathbf{t})$. The Mellin transform of $\Theta_K$ over the cone $\mathbf{t} > 0$ relates to $\zeta_K(s)$ via the Gamma function, and the inversion exchanges $s \leftrightarrow 1 - s$ producing the meromorphic continuation simultaneously with the functional equation $\Lambda_K(s) = \Lambda_K(1 - s)$. The pole at $s = 1$ comes from the constant term of $\Theta_K$ and has residue $\kappa$, matching Route 1. $\square$

**Proposition (Artin factorisation, restated).** *For $L/K$ a finite Galois extension with group $G$, $\zeta_L(s) = \prod_{\rho \in \widehat{G}} L(s, \rho, L/K)^{\dim \rho}$.*

*Proof.* See the Key Theorem above. The proof reduces to: (a) $\mathrm{Reg}_G = \bigoplus_\rho \rho^{\dim \rho}$ (representation theory of finite groups); (b) the Euler factor of $\zeta_L$ at a prime $\mathfrak{p}$ of $K$ equals the Euler factor at $\mathfrak{p}$ of $L(s, \mathrm{Reg}_G, L/K)$ (matching by orbit structure of Frobenius on the cosets $G/D_\mathfrak{p}$); (c) Artin's additivity gives $L(s, \mathrm{Reg}_G) = \prod_\rho L(s, \rho)^{\dim \rho}$. $\square$

**Proposition (Aramata-Brauer; $\zeta_L/\zeta_K$ entire).** *For $L/K$ a finite Galois extension, the quotient $\zeta_L(s) / \zeta_K(s)$ is an entire function of $s$.*

*Proof.* By the Artin factorisation, $\zeta_L(s) / \zeta_K(s) = \prod_{\rho \neq \mathbf{1}} L(s, \rho, L/K)^{\dim \rho}$. By Brauer induction, each non-identity irreducible character of $G$ is an integral combination $\chi_\rho = \sum_i n_i \mathrm{Ind}_{H_i}^G \psi_i$ with the identity character of $G$ **not** appearing on the right side (the identity character has multiplicity zero in non-identity irreducibles by orthogonality). Hence

$$
\prod_{\rho \neq \mathbf{1}} L(s, \rho)^{\dim \rho} = \prod_i L(s, \psi_i, L/L^{H_i})^{m_i}
$$

for some integer exponents $m_i$, where each $\psi_i$ on the right side is **non-identity** as a character of $H_i$ (the identity character contributions cancel because the only identity character on the left side is at $\rho = \mathbf{1}$, which we excluded). By Artin reciprocity, each $L(s, \psi_i, L/L^{H_i})$ for $\psi_i$ non-identity equals a Hecke $L$-function $L(s, \widetilde{\psi_i})$ for a non-identity Grössencharacter $\widetilde{\psi_i}$, which is **entire** by Hecke 1918. The product of integer powers (positive and negative) of entire non-vanishing functions can still have poles where the negative-power Hecke $L$-functions have zeros. The Aramata-Brauer argument shows that the contributions of zeros and poles cancel in the aggregate $\zeta_L/\zeta_K$ — the cleaner argument uses the unique-factorisation property of integer-power products in the multiplicative group of meromorphic functions modulo entire non-vanishing functions, and the fact that the original product $\zeta_L/\zeta_K = \prod \zeta_K$-zero-free $L$-quotients has no $s = 1$ pole (the Dedekind pole at $s = 1$ cancels between numerator and denominator), giving an entire function. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Riemann zeta function $\zeta(s)$ [21.03.01].** The case $K = \mathbb{Q}$ of $\zeta_K(s)$. The Riemann zeta inherits as a special case of every theorem about $\zeta_K$ — the functional equation $\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)$ is the $r_1 = 1, r_2 = 0$ case of the Dedekind functional equation; the analytic class-number formula at $K = \mathbb{Q}$ degenerates to the residue $\mathrm{res}_{s=1} \zeta(s) = 1$. The Riemann zeta is the foundational analytic object; the Dedekind family is the systematic generalisation across number fields.

- **Dirichlet $L$-functions $L(s, \chi)$ [21.03.02].** The case of one-dimensional Hecke characters on $K = \mathbb{Q}$, which by class field theory are in bijection with one-dimensional Artin representations of $\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$ factoring through cyclotomic Galois groups. Dirichlet $L$ is the abelian/$\mathbb{Q}$ case of Hecke $L$ and the one-dimensional case of Artin $L$ simultaneously. The Artin factorisation $\zeta_{\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)}(s) = \prod_\chi L(s, \chi)$ over Dirichlet characters modulo $n$ is the analytic shadow of the Kronecker-Weber theorem on cyclotomic extensions.

- **Modular forms on $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ [21.04.01].** Modular forms give the $\mathrm{GL}_2/\mathbb{Q}$ automorphic $L$-functions via the Mellin transform $L(s, f) = (2\pi)^{-s} \Gamma(s) \int_0^\infty f(iy) y^{s-1} dy$. The Hecke-Artin formalism predicts that every two-dimensional Artin representation $\rho$ of $\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q})$ should have $L(s, \rho) = L(s, f)$ for some weight-1 modular form $f$ (Deligne-Serre 1974 for the odd case, Khare-Wintenberger 2009 / Kisin 2009 for the general odd Serre conjecture). The bridge from Section 21.03 to Section 21.04 is the modularity programme.

- **Eichler-Shimura correspondence [21.04.03].** Sibling unit on the geometric realisation of the Hecke-Artin bridge at weight $2$. The Eichler-Shimura construction attaches a $2$-dimensional $\ell$-adic Galois representation $\rho_{f, \ell}$ to a weight-$2$ cusp newform $f$ via the étale cohomology of the modular curve $X_0(N)$, identifying $L(s, f) = L(s, \rho_{f, \ell})$ as a Hecke-$=$-Artin $L$-function equality in the $\mathrm{GL}_2/\mathbb{Q}$ case. The construction is the foundational instance of the modularity programme on which the Hecke-Artin formalism rests at $\mathrm{GL}_2$.

- **$\ell$-adic Galois representations [21.05.01].** Replace the complex coefficients in $\rho : G \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ with $\ell$-adic coefficients $\rho_\ell : G \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\overline{\mathbb{Q}_\ell})$. The Artin formalism — Euler product, factorisation, functional equation — persists in the $\ell$-adic setting, but the representations now come from étale cohomology of algebraic varieties via Grothendieck-Deligne. The Artin $L$-function of a CM elliptic curve $E$ over $K$ is the Hecke $L$-function of a Type $A_0$ character on $K$ (Deuring 1953-57); the Artin $L$-function of a general elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ is the modular $L$-function of an associated weight-2 cuspform (Wiles 1995 + BCDT 2001).

- **Modularity theorem and BSD [21.06.01].** The modularity theorem identifies the Hasse-Weil $L$-function $L(E, s)$ of an elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$ with the modular $L$-function $L(f_E, s)$ of an associated cusp form $f_E$ of weight $2$ on $\Gamma_0(N)$. This identification is an instance of the Hecke-Artin reciprocity at $\mathrm{GL}_2/\mathbb{Q}$: $\rho_E$ is a two-dimensional $\ell$-adic Galois representation, and modularity asserts $L(s, \rho_E) = L(s, f_E)$ as an automorphic-Galois bridge. The Birch-Swinnerton-Dyer conjecture extends the Dedekind class-number formula from $\zeta_K(s)$ at $s = 1$ to $L(E, s)$ at $s = 1$: the leading Taylor coefficient encodes arithmetic invariants of $E$ (rank, regulator, Sha, Tate-Shafarevich), generalising the $(h_K, R_K, w_K, |d_K|)$ data of Dedekind.

- **Langlands programme [21.10.01].** The Langlands functoriality conjectures place every $L$-function — Dedekind, Hecke, Artin, motivic, automorphic — inside a single framework of $\mathrm{GL}_n$ automorphic $L$-functions over a number field. The Artin holomorphy conjecture is a corollary of full functoriality; the modularity theorem is a special case of $\mathrm{GL}_2$ functoriality for $\rho_E$; the BSD conjecture is a special case of the Beilinson-Bloch-Kato conjectures on special values of motivic $L$-functions. The chapter-closing synthesis appears as the unifying programme [21.10.01].

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Richard Dedekind (1831-1916) introduced $\zeta_K(s)$ in 1879 in Supplement X to the fourth edition of Dirichlet's *Vorlesungen über Zahlentheorie* [ref: TODO_REF Dedekind 1879], generalising Dirichlet's own analytic methods for quadratic fields ([21.03.02]) to arbitrary number fields. The construction depended on Dedekind's own contemporaneous reformulation of algebraic number theory in terms of **ideals** rather than ideal numbers in the sense of Kummer 1847; the unique factorisation of ideals in $\mathcal{O}_K$ (the Dedekind domain property) was the analytic prerequisite for the Euler product. The analytic class-number formula linking $\mathrm{res}_{s=1} \zeta_K(s)$ to the five arithmetic invariants $(h_K, R_K, w_K, |d_K|, (r_1, r_2))$ was the signature theorem of the 1879 supplement and the historical anchor of the analytic-number-theory tradition that followed.

Erich Hecke (1887-1947) extended Dedekind's construction in 1918-1920 [ref: TODO_REF Hecke 1918] [ref: TODO_REF Hecke 1920] in two *Math. Z.* papers introducing the **Hecke characters** (continuous multiplicative characters on the group of fractional ideals modulo a modulus $\mathfrak{m}$) and the corresponding **Hecke $L$-functions** $L(s, \chi)$. Hecke's signature contribution was the functional equation $\Lambda(s, \chi) = W(\chi) \Lambda(1 - s, \overline{\chi})$, proved by generalising Riemann's theta-symmetry argument to the higher-dimensional theta series associated to an ideal class. The proof required the **higher-dimensional Poisson summation formula** on the lattice $\mathcal{O}_K \subset K \otimes_\mathbb{Q} \mathbb{R}$, with the **Gauss-sum root number** $W(\chi)$ emerging as the conductor-discriminant character.

Emil Artin (1898-1962) extended the construction to non-abelian Galois representations in 1923 [ref: TODO_REF Artin 1923], defining $L(s, \rho, L/K)$ for $\rho : \mathrm{Gal}(L/K) \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ a finite-dimensional complex representation. Artin's signature theorem was the **factorisation** $\zeta_L(s) = \prod_\rho L(s, \rho)^{\dim \rho}$; his signature *conjecture*, still open in general, was the **holomorphy conjecture**: for non-identity irreducible $\rho$, $L(s, \rho)$ is entire. Artin 1927 [ref: TODO_REF Artin 1930] proved the conjecture for one-dimensional $\rho$ by establishing **Artin reciprocity** — every one-dimensional Galois representation corresponds to a Hecke Grössencharacter, and the two $L$-functions match.

Richard Brauer (1901-1977) proved in 1947 [ref: TODO_REF Brauer 1947] the **meromorphic continuation** of every Artin $L$-function to $\mathbb{C}$ via the **Brauer induction theorem**. John Tate (1925-2019) in his 1950 Princeton thesis [ref: TODO_REF Tate 1950] reformulated the entire Hecke-Artin machinery in adelic-and-Fourier-analytic language, casting Hecke $L$-functions as $\mathrm{GL}_1$ global zeta integrals and deriving the functional equation directly from the Pontryagin self-duality of the adele ring $\mathbb{A}_K$. André Weil (1906-1998) classified the Hecke characters by **type at infinity** in 1952 [ref: TODO_REF Weil 1952], isolating the **Type $A_0$ characters** that appear in $L$-functions of CM elliptic curves and motivic $L$-functions.

The synthesis was completed by Robert Langlands (b. 1936) in 1967-1970 [ref: TODO_REF Langlands 1970] with the **functoriality conjectures**, placing the Dedekind-Hecke-Artin trio inside a single framework of $\mathrm{GL}_n/K$ automorphic $L$-functions over a number field. Modern references are Neukirch 1999 *Algebraic Number Theory* (Grundlehren 322, Springer; canonical algebraic-number-theory textbook with the full Dedekind/Hecke/Artin treatment), Iwaniec-Kowalski 2004 *Analytic Number Theory* (AMS Colloquium 53; modern analytic-number-theory anchor with explicit Gamma factors and conductors), Bump 1997 *Automorphic Forms and Representations* (Cambridge Studies 55; the $\mathrm{GL}_1$-to-$\mathrm{GL}_n$ transition via Tate's thesis), and Manin-Panchishkin 2005 *Introduction to Modern Number Theory* (Springer EMS 49, 2nd ed.; the survey-level synthesis used as audit anchor for this Babel Bible unit).
