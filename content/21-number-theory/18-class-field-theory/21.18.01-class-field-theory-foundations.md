## Intuition [Beginner]

A number field such as $\mathbb{Q}$ or $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$ carries symmetries. Pick the symmetries that commute with each other — where carrying out $a$ then $b$ gives the same result as carrying out $b$ then $a$. These commuting symmetries are called **abelian**, and the field extensions they govern are **abelian extensions**. Class field theory is the complete classification of these extensions.

The remarkable claim is a perfect dictionary. Every abelian extension of a number field $K$ matches, one for one, a finite object living inside $K$ itself — a generalised ideal class group. The match sends each abelian extension $L$ over $K$ to a quotient of the class-group data of $K$. So the "nice" symmetries of $K$ are classified by arithmetic already present in $K$.

The simplest case is $\mathbb{Q}$ itself. Every abelian extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ sits inside a field built from roots of unity — a cyclotomic field. This is the Kronecker-Weber theorem, and it is the blueprint for the whole theory. For other number fields the dictionary deepens: the class group itself measures the unramified abelian extensions, and bigger quotients measure ramified ones.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The field $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$ has class number $h_K = 2$: its ideal class group $\mathrm{Cl}(K)$ has two elements, as computed in [21.17.01]. Class field theory predicts that $K$ has a single largest abelian extension $H$ in which no prime ideal of $K$ breaks apart (no prime gets squared when lifted). This $H$ is the **Hilbert class field**, and the theory forces its degree over $K$ to equal the class number: $[H : K] = h_K = 2$.

So there must be a degree-2 extension of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$ with no ramified primes. The answer is $H = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5},\, i)$, obtained by adjoining the square root of $-1$. The new symmetry sending $i \mapsto -i$ commutes with every symmetry of $K$, so the extension $H/K$ is abelian — exactly as the theory requires.

To verify there is no ramification, compare discriminants. The discriminant of $K$ is $d_K = -20$. The discriminant of $H$ is $400$, which equals $(-20)^{2} = d_K^{2}$. Since $d_H = d_K^{2}$, the relative discriminant of $H$ over $K$ is the unit ideal: no prime of $K$ ramifies in $H$. The reciprocity isomorphism $\mathrm{Cl}(K) \cong \mathrm{Gal}(H/K)$ — both groups have two elements — is the first concrete instance of the dictionary.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **modulus** (or **cycle**) in a number field $K$ is a formal product $\mathfrak{m} = \mathfrak{m}_0\, \mathfrak{m}_\infty$, where $\mathfrak{m}_0$ is a nonzero integral ideal of $\mathcal{O}_K$ and $\mathfrak{m}_\infty$ is a subset of the real embeddings $\sigma : K \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}$ [ref: TODO_REF Childress Ch. 2]. The **ray** modulo $\mathfrak{m}$ is the subgroup of $K^\times$ defined by
$$K_{\mathfrak{m},1} = \bigl\{\, x \in K^\times \;:\; v_\mathfrak{p}(x - 1) \geq v_\mathfrak{p}(\mathfrak{m}_0)\ \text{for all}\ \mathfrak{p} \mid \mathfrak{m}_0,\ \ \sigma(x) > 0\ \text{for all}\ \sigma \in \mathfrak{m}_\infty \,\bigr\},$$
that is, elements congruent to $1$ modulo the finite part and strictly positive at each chosen real place. Two fractional ideals lie in the same **ray class** when they differ by a principal ideal $(x)$ with $x \in K_{\mathfrak{m},1}$.

The **ray class group** modulo $\mathfrak{m}$ is the quotient
$$\mathrm{Cl}_\mathfrak{m}(K) \;=\; I_K^\mathfrak{m} \big/ P_{K,1}^\mathfrak{m},$$
where $I_K^\mathfrak{m}$ is the group of fractional ideals of $K$ coprime to $\mathfrak{m}_0$ and $P_{K,1}^\mathfrak{m}$ is the subgroup of principal ideals $(x)$ with $x \in K_{\mathfrak{m},1}$ [ref: TODO_REF Neukirch Ch. VI]. For the empty modulus $\mathfrak{m} = 1$ the congruence condition is vacuous and $\mathrm{Cl}_1(K)$ recovers the ordinary ideal class group $\mathrm{Cl}(K)$ of [21.17.01]. Every ray class group is finite.

**Artin symbol (Frobenius).** Let $L/K$ be a finite Galois extension and let $\mathfrak{p}$ be a prime of $K$ unramified in $L$. For a prime $\mathfrak{P} \mid \mathfrak{p}$ of $L$, the **Artin symbol** is the unique element $\left(\frac{L/K}{\mathfrak{p}}\right) \in \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$ acting on residue classes by
$$\left(\frac{L/K}{\mathfrak{p}}\right)(\bar\alpha) \;=\; \bar\alpha^{\,N\mathfrak{p}}, \qquad N\mathfrak{p} = |\mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{p}|,$$
for every $\bar\alpha \in \mathcal{O}_L/\mathfrak{P}$. Because $\mathfrak{p}$ is unramified the symbol is independent of the choice of $\mathfrak{P}$ above $\mathfrak{p}$ when $L/K$ is abelian. Extending multiplicatively over factorisations of ideals defines the **Artin map**
$$\Phi_{L/K} : I_K^\mathfrak{m} \longrightarrow \mathrm{Gal}(L/K), \qquad \prod_\mathfrak{p} \mathfrak{p}^{v_\mathfrak{p}} \;\mapsto\; \prod_\mathfrak{p} \left(\frac{L/K}{\mathfrak{p}}\right)^{v_\mathfrak{p}},$$
defined on the group of ideals coprime to $\mathfrak{m}$, where $\mathfrak{m}_0$ is divisible by every finite prime of $K$ that ramifies in $L$ [ref: TODO_REF Artin 1927].

The **Hilbert class field** $H_K$ of $K$ is the maximal abelian extension of $K$ unramified at every finite prime. The **narrow Hilbert class field** additionally requires unramification at the real places. The **ray class field** $K_\mathfrak{m}$ modulo $\mathfrak{m}$ is the maximal abelian extension of $K$ whose conductor divides $\mathfrak{m}$; equivalently the extension cut out by the Artin map on $\mathrm{Cl}_\mathfrak{m}(K)$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- Class field theory classifies only **abelian** extensions. A non-abelian extension such as the splitting field of a generic irreducible quintic with group $S_5$ lies outside the theory; its symmetries do not commute, and the classification passes to the Langlands programme [21.10.01].
- "Unramified" in the Hilbert class field means unramified at every **finite** prime. At a real place of $K$ the extension may turn it complex; the narrow Hilbert class field forbids this extra behaviour.
- The Artin symbol is defined only at primes **unramified** in $L/K$. Ramified primes carry no Frobenius, which is why the modulus $\mathfrak{m}$ must absorb every ramified finite prime before the map $\Phi_{L/K}$ is defined on $I_K^\mathfrak{m}$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Artin reciprocity, global).** *Let $L/K$ be a finite abelian extension of number fields with Galois group $G = \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$, and let $\mathfrak{m}$ be a modulus divisible by every finite prime of $K$ that ramifies in $L$. Then the Artin map $\Phi_{L/K} : I_K^\mathfrak{m} \to G$ is surjective with kernel containing the ray $P_{K,1}^\mathfrak{m}$ and the norms $N_{L/K}(I_L^\mathfrak{m})$. It descends to an isomorphism*
$$I_K^\mathfrak{m} \big/ P_{K,1}^\mathfrak{m}\, N_{L/K}(I_L^\mathfrak{m}) \;\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}\; G.$$

*Proof.* The argument proceeds in five stages: the Artin map is a homomorphism; its kernel contains the ray-norm subgroup; the first inequality bounds the index from below; surjectivity follows by a group-theoretic reduction; the second inequality bounds the index from above and closes the kernel.

**Stage 1 — the Artin map is a homomorphism.** For unramified primes $\mathfrak{p}, \mathfrak{p}'$ of $K$, the Frobenius at the product $\mathfrak{p}\mathfrak{p}'$ is the product of the Frobenii: $\left(\frac{L/K}{\mathfrak{p}\mathfrak{p}'}\right) = \left(\frac{L/K}{\mathfrak{p}}\right)\left(\frac{L/K}{\mathfrak{p}'}\right)$. Indeed the residue ring $\mathcal{O}_L/\mathfrak{P}$ has $\mathfrak{p}$-Frobenius raising to the power $N\mathfrak{p}$ and $\mathfrak{p}'$-Frobenius raising to $N\mathfrak{p}'$, and these commute and compose to the power $N\mathfrak{p}\cdot N\mathfrak{p}'$ on the separate residue factors. Multiplicative extension over general ideals in $I_K^\mathfrak{m}$ gives a well-defined homomorphism.

**Stage 2 — the kernel contains the ray and the norms.** If $(x) \in P_{K,1}^\mathfrak{m}$ with $x \equiv 1 \pmod{\mathfrak{m}_0}$ and $\sigma(x) > 0$ for $\sigma \in \mathfrak{m}_\infty$, then locally at each ramified prime the element $x$ is a unit congruent to $1$, and the local norm-residue computation (the Hilbert symbol of [21.02.05]) forces $\left(\frac{L/K}{(x)}\right)$ to act as the identity. Likewise every ideal norm $N_{L/K}(\mathfrak{A})$ lies in the kernel: at an unramified prime $\mathfrak{q}$ of $K$, a prime $\mathfrak{Q}$ of $L$ above it contributes $f(\mathfrak{Q}/\mathfrak{q})$ copies of $\left(\frac{L/K}{\mathfrak{q}}\right)$ to the Artin symbol of $N\mathfrak{A}$, and this contribution is $\mathrm{Frob}_\mathfrak{q}^{f(\mathfrak{Q}/\mathfrak{q})} = 1$ in $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathcal{O}_L/\mathfrak{Q}\,/\,\mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{q})$ because the residue extension has degree $f$ and Frobenius has order $f$. Hence $P_{K,1}^\mathfrak{m}\, N_{L/K}(I_L^\mathfrak{m}) \subseteq \ker\Phi_{L/K}$.

**Stage 3 — the first inequality.** Let $h_\mathfrak{m}(L/K) = (I_K^\mathfrak{m} : P_{K,1}^\mathfrak{m}\, N_{L/K}(I_L^\mathfrak{m}))$. The **first inequality** (Takagi) asserts $h_\mathfrak{m}(L/K) \geq [L : K]$. In the idelic formulation of Chevalley it is a direct count: the kernel of the norm on idele classes $C_L \to C_K$ has index at least $[L:K]$ by the cohomology of the cyclic group $\mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$ acting on $C_L$, the bound falling out of the Herbrand quotient $(C_L) \cong [L:K]$ [ref: TODO_REF Artin-Tate Ch. 7]. This is the inequality whose proof the modern cohomological machine absorbs.

**Stage 4 — surjectivity.** The Artin map has image $G' = \mathrm{im}(\Phi_{L/K}) \subseteq G$. Let $E = L^{G'}$ be its fixed field; then $E \supseteq K$ and $\Phi_{L/K}(\mathfrak{p})|_E = 1$ for every $\mathfrak{p} \in I_K^\mathfrak{m}$, so every prime of $K$ splits completely in $E/K$. A prime that splits completely is a norm from $E$, hence $I_K^\mathfrak{m} = P_{K,1}^\mathfrak{m}\, N_{E/K}(I_E^\mathfrak{m})$ and $h_\mathfrak{m}(E/K) = 1$. If $E \neq K$ the first inequality (Stage 3) applied to $E/K$ gives $1 = h_\mathfrak{m}(E/K) \geq [E:K] > 1$, a contradiction. Hence $E = K$, $G' = G$, and $\Phi_{L/K}$ is surjective.

**Stage 5 — the second inequality and the kernel.** The **second inequality** (Takagi, Chevalley) asserts $h_\mathfrak{m}(L/K) \leq [L : K]$. Its proof is the analytic-cohomological heart of the theory: in the cohomological form, the Herbrand quotient of $C_L$ under $\mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$ and the vanishing of $H^1$ (Hilbert's theorem 90 for ideles) force the index of the norm subgroup in $C_K$ down to $[L:K]$ [ref: TODO_REF Artin-Tate Ch. 7]. Combining Stages 3 and 5 yields $h_\mathfrak{m}(L/K) = [L:K] = |G|$. Stage 4 gives $G \cong I_K^\mathfrak{m}/\ker\Phi_{L/K}$ with $P_{K,1}^\mathfrak{m} N(I_L^\mathfrak{m}) \subseteq \ker\Phi_{L/K}$, and the two subgroups now have equal finite index, so $\ker\Phi_{L/K} = P_{K,1}^\mathfrak{m}\, N_{L/K}(I_L^\mathfrak{m})$. The induced map $I_K^\mathfrak{m}/P_{K,1}^\mathfrak{m} N(I_L^\mathfrak{m}) \xrightarrow{\sim} G$ is the reciprocity isomorphism. $\square$

**Bridge.** Artin reciprocity builds toward the Langlands programme [21.10.01], where the abelian Galois group is replaced by the full non-abelian Galois group and ray class groups are replaced by automorphic representations, and appears again in the Galois-representation theory of [21.05.01], where compatible families of $\ell$-adic representations are the non-abelian shadows of the abelian Artin map. The foundational reason the theory works is that Frobenius elements already form a complete set of symmetries for unramified primes: this is exactly what the Artin map exploits, reading the entire Galois group off residue-field arithmetic. The central insight is that congruence conditions inside $K$ (the ray) and splitting data outside $K$ (the extension) determine each other, the bridge is that this reciprocity is dual to the local norm-residue pairing of [21.02.05], and putting these together the whole theory generalises from the ideal-theoretic form to the idele-theoretic formulation of Chevalley that the modern cohomological proof rests on.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, true-false).**

If a number field $K$ has class number $h_K = 1$, then its Hilbert class field equals $K$ itself.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Hilbert class field has degree $h_K$ over $K$, and degree $1$ means no proper extension.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.** Artin reciprocity gives $\mathrm{Gal}(H_K/K) \cong \mathrm{Cl}(K)$, so $[H_K : K] = h_K$. When $h_K = 1$ this degree is $1$, meaning $H_K = K$: the field admits no proper unramified abelian extension. For example the Hilbert class field of $\mathbb{Q}(i)$, which has class number $1$, is $\mathbb{Q}(i)$ itself.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

For the cyclotomic extension $L = \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$ over $K = \mathbb{Q}$ and a rational prime $p \nmid n$, show that the Artin symbol $\left(\frac{L/K}{(p)}\right)$ is the automorphism $\zeta_n \mapsto \zeta_n^{\,p}$. Deduce that the Artin map identifies $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)/\mathbb{Q})$ with $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reduce $\zeta_n$ modulo a prime $\mathfrak{P}$ of $\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_n]$ above $p$, and use the defining property $\bar\alpha \mapsto \bar\alpha^{\,N(p)}$ of the Frobenius, noting $N(p) = p$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $p \nmid n$, the prime $(p)$ is unramified in $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$. For a prime $\mathfrak{P} \mid p$ of $\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_n]$, the Artin symbol satisfies $\overline{\left(\frac{L/K}{(p)}\right)(\alpha)} = \bar\alpha^{\,N(p)} = \bar\alpha^{\,p}$ for all $\alpha \in \mathbb{Z}[\zeta_n]$. Applying this to $\alpha = \zeta_n$ gives $\overline{\sigma(\zeta_n)} = \bar\zeta_n^{\,p}$ where $\sigma = \left(\frac{L/K}{(p)}\right)$. The assignment $\zeta_n \mapsto \zeta_n^{\,p}$ is itself a Galois automorphism of $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$ (well-defined because $\gcd(p,n)=1$, so $\zeta_n^p$ remains a primitive $n$th root of unity), and it has the same reduction modulo $\mathfrak{P}$ as $\sigma$. By injectivity of reduction on automorphisms at unramified primes, $\sigma(\zeta_n) = \zeta_n^{\,p}$. The map $\sigma \mapsto a$ where $\sigma(\zeta_n) = \zeta_n^{\,a}$ is the standard isomorphism $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)/\mathbb{Q}) \xrightarrow{\sim} (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$, so the Artin map is exactly this identification.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).**

The Hilbert class field of $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$ is $H = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5},\, i)$. Verify the discriminant identity $d_H = d_K^{2}$ used to prove $H/K$ is unramified, by computing $d_H$ as the product of the discriminants of the three quadratic subfields of $H$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The biquadratic field $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5},\, i)$ has three quadratic subfields: $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$, $\mathbb{Q}(i)$, and $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{5})$ (since $\sqrt{-5}\cdot i = \sqrt{5}$ up to sign). Multiply their discriminants.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The discriminants are $d(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})) = -20$ (since $-5 \equiv 3 \pmod 4$), $d(\mathbb{Q}(i)) = -4$, and $d(\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{5})) = 5$ (since $5 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$). The discriminant of a biquadratic field is the product of its three quadratic-subfield discriminants, so
$$d_H = (-20)\cdot(-4)\cdot 5 = 400 = (-20)^{2} = d_K^{2}.$$
Since $d_H = d_K^{[H:K]} N_{K/\mathbb{Q}}(\mathfrak{d}_{H/K}) = d_K^{2} N(\mathfrak{d}_{H/K})$, the equality $d_H = d_K^{2}$ forces $N(\mathfrak{d}_{H/K}) = 1$, hence $\mathfrak{d}_{H/K} = \mathcal{O}_K$, and $H/K$ is unramified at all finite primes.

</details>

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

In $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$ the prime ideal $\mathfrak{p} = (2,\, 1+\sqrt{-5})$ satisfies $(2) = \mathfrak{p}^{2}$ (see [21.17.01]). The Hilbert class field is $H = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5},\, i)$ with $\mathrm{Gal}(H/K) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$. Determine whether $\mathfrak{p}$ splits or remains inert in $H$, given that $\mathfrak{p}$ is unramified in $H/K$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A prime of $K$ unramified in an abelian extension splits completely exactly when its Artin symbol is the identity. Compute the order of the Artin symbol $(\mathfrak{p}, H/K)$ in the two-element group $\mathrm{Gal}(H/K)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Because $H/K$ is unramified at every finite prime and has Galois group $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, an unramified prime $\mathfrak{q}$ of $K$ either splits as $\mathfrak{q}\mathcal{O}_H = \mathfrak{Q}_1\mathfrak{Q}_2$ (when its Artin symbol is the identity, residue degree $1$) or stays inert as $\mathfrak{q}\mathcal{O}_H = \mathfrak{Q}$ (when its Artin symbol is the non-identity, residue degree $2$). For $\mathfrak{p} = (2, 1+\sqrt{-5})$ a direct computation shows the non-identity automorphism $\sigma$ of $H/K$ (sending $i \mapsto -i$) does not fix the residue of $\mathcal{O}_H/\mathfrak{P}$ for $\mathfrak{P}\mid\mathfrak{p}$, so the Artin symbol $(\mathfrak{p}, H/K) = \sigma \neq 1$. Hence $\mathfrak{p}$ is **inert** in $H$: $\mathfrak{p}\mathcal{O}_H$ remains a single prime with residue degree $2$. This is the Frobenius at the non-identity class of $\mathrm{Cl}(K) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $L/\mathbb{Q}$ be a finite abelian extension of prime degree $\ell$. Show that the only rational prime that can ramify wildly in $L$ is $\ell$ itself, and conclude that every tame ramified prime $p \neq \ell$ has ramification index dividing $p - 1$. Use this to explain why $L$ embeds into a cyclotomic field (the Kronecker-Weber theorem in the prime-degree case).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

At a tamely ramified prime $p$, the inertia group injects into $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$, hence has order dividing $p - 1$. Since $G = \mathrm{Gal}(L/\mathbb{Q})$ has order $\ell$, tame inertia at $p \neq \ell$ forces $\ell \mid (p-1)$. Cyclotomic fields $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_p)$ realise the unique degree-$\ell$ quotient precisely when $\ell \mid (p-1)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Wild ramification at a rational prime $p$ occurs only when $p$ divides $[L:\mathbb{Q}] = \ell$, so the sole wildly ramified prime is $\ell$ itself. At a tamely ramified prime $p \neq \ell$, the inertia group $I_p$ is a subgroup of $\mathrm{Gal}(L/\mathbb{Q})$ of order dividing $\ell$ (a prime), so either $I_p = 1$ ($p$ unramified) or $I_p \cong \mathbb{Z}/\ell\mathbb{Z}$. The tame inertia group at $p$ also injects into $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$ (its action on the residue-field extension), so $|I_p|$ divides $p - 1$; in the non-identity case $\ell \mid (p-1)$. The cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_p)$ has Galois group $(\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z})^\times$ of order $p - 1$, which admits a quotient of order $\ell$ exactly when $\ell \mid (p-1)$; its unique degree-$\ell$ subfield realises the same ramification data as $L$ at $p$. Assembling the ramified primes $p$ with $\ell \mid (p-1)$ into a cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_m)$ and matching the degree-$\ell$ character embeds $L$ into $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_m)$, which is the Kronecker-Weber theorem for prime degree.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $L/K$ be a finite **unramified** extension of $p$-adic fields of degree $n$. Prove that the local Artin map sends a uniformiser $\pi_K$ to the Frobenius generator of $\mathrm{Gal}(L/K) \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ and kills the units $\mathcal{O}_K^\times$, giving $K^\times / N_{L/K} L^\times \cong \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For an unramified extension the norm map on residue fields $\mathbb{F}_{q^n}^\times \to \mathbb{F}_q^\times$ is surjective, and a uniformiser of $K$ is also a uniformiser of $L$. Use Hensel's lemma to lift residue-field norms to unit norms.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Because $L/K$ is unramified, a uniformiser $\pi_K$ of $K$ remains a uniformiser of $L$, the ramification index is $e = 1$, and the residue-field extension $\mathbb{F}_{q^n}/\mathbb{F}_q$ has degree $f = n$. The Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$ is cyclic of order $n$, generated by the Frobenius $\mathrm{Frob}: x \mapsto x^{q}$ on residues. The norm of $\pi_K \in L$ is $N_{L/K}(\pi_K) = \pi_K^{n}$ (the residue-field norm of $1$). For the units: the norm $N : \mathbb{F}_{q^n}^\times \to \mathbb{F}_q^\times$ is surjective (both are cyclic and the norm is $\bar v \mapsto \bar v^{1+q+\cdots+q^{n-1}}$), so every $\bar u \in \mathbb{F}_q^\times$ lifts to a unit norm; Hensel's lemma lifts this residue-level surjectivity to $\mathcal{O}_K^\times \subseteq N_{L/K}(\mathcal{O}_L^\times)$. Hence $N_{L/K}(L^\times) = \langle \pi_K^{n}\rangle \cdot \mathcal{O}_K^\times$, and
$$K^\times / N_{L/K}L^\times \;\cong\; \langle\pi_K\rangle / \langle\pi_K^{n}\rangle \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \;\cong\; \mathrm{Gal}(L/K),$$
with the class of $\pi_K$ mapping to $\mathrm{Frob}$. This is the local reciprocity isomorphism in the unramified case.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (Takagi existence theorem, 1920).** *Let $K$ be a number field. Every congruence subgroup $H$ with $P_{K,1}^\mathfrak{m} \subseteq H \subseteq I_K^\mathfrak{m}$ (equivalently, every open subgroup of finite index in the idele class group $C_K$) is the kernel of the Artin map of a unique finite abelian extension $L/K$. The map $L \mapsto \ker\Phi_{L/K}$ is a bijection between finite abelian extensions of $K$ (inside a fixed closure) and such congruence subgroups, reversing inclusion* [ref: TODO_REF Takagi 1920]. This is the converse of Artin reciprocity: not only does every abelian extension produce a congruence subgroup via the Artin map, but every congruence subgroup comes from a unique abelian extension. The existence theorem turns reciprocity from a property of given extensions into a complete classification.

**Theorem (Kronecker-Weber).** *Every finite abelian extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ is contained in a cyclotomic field $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$ for some $n$.* This is the case $K = \mathbb{Q}$ of the existence theorem: the ray class fields of $\mathbb{Q}$ are precisely the cyclotomic fields (and their subfields), so every abelian extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ is cyclotomic. The proof reduces, via the structure theorem for finite abelian groups and induction on degree, to the prime-power case, where a ramification analysis (Exercise 7) forces the extension into the cyclotomic field of its conductor. This is the simplest case from which the whole general theory grew, and the explicit reciprocity map for cyclotomic fields is worked out in the Full proof set.

**Theorem (local class field theory).** *Let $L/K$ be a finite abelian extension of $p$-adic fields. The local Artin map $K^\times \to \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$ is surjective with kernel $N_{L/K}(L^\times)$, inducing*
$$K^\times \big/ N_{L/K}(L^\times) \;\xrightarrow{\;\sim\;}\; \mathrm{Gal}(L/K).$$
*The global reciprocity law is obtained by assembling these local maps over all places of $K$ via the idèle class group* [ref: TODO_REF Serre 1979 Ch. XIII-XV]. The local law is cleaner than the global one because $K^\times$ is a single explicit group (a uniformiser times units), and the unramified case is fully proved in the Full proof set.

**Theorem (Hasse norm theorem).** *For a cyclic extension $L/K$ of number fields, an element $x \in K^\times$ is a global norm from $L$ if and only if it is a local norm in every completion $K_v$, that is, $x \in N_{L_v/K_v}(L_v^\times)$ for every place $v$* [ref: TODO_REF Hasse 1930]. For non-cyclic extensions the local-global principle can fail. This theorem is the archetype of the local-to-global philosophy that pervades modern arithmetic geometry, and it knits the local reciprocity laws into the global theory.

**Theorem (conductor-discriminant formula).** *For a finite abelian extension $L/K$ with Galois group $G$, the relative discriminant $\mathfrak{d}_{L/K}$ equals the product of the conductors $\mathfrak{f}(\chi)$ over all characters $\chi \in \hat{G}$:* $\mathfrak{d}_{L/K} = \prod_\chi \mathfrak{f}(\chi)$. *Equivalently the Dedekind zeta function of $L$ factors as $\zeta_L(s) = \prod_\chi L(s,\chi)$ over the Hecke $L$-functions attached to the characters of $G$* [ref: TODO_REF Neukirch Ch. VII]. This links the conductor — the modulus measuring ramification in class field theory — to the analytic factorisation of zeta functions developed in [21.03.03], and is the seed of the non-abelian factorisation predicted by the Langlands programme.

**Synthesis.** Class field theory is governed by the dictionary between congruence subgroups inside $K$ and abelian extensions above $K$, and the foundational reason the dictionary closes is exactly the two inequalities: the first bounds the index of the norm subgroup from below and the second from above, pinning it to $[L:K] = |G|$. The central insight is that the Artin map — reading the whole Galois group off Frobenius elements at unramified primes — is dual to the local norm-residue pairing of [21.02.05], and this is exactly what forces congruence data inside $K$ to determine splitting data in $L$. The bridge is that the abelian theory is the degree-one layer of the Langlands programme [21.10.01], where abelian characters of the Galois group generalise to automorphic representations; putting these together, the existence theorem shows reciprocity is not merely a property of given extensions but a complete classification, and the whole theory builds toward the non-abelian Galois-representation theory of [21.05.01] where compatible $\ell$-adic families appear again in place of the finite abelian groups classified here.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (the Artin map for cyclotomic fields).** *Let $L = \mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$, $K = \mathbb{Q}$, and let $(p)$ be a rational prime with $p \nmid n$. The Artin map $\Phi_{L/\mathbb{Q}} : I_\mathbb{Q}^{(n)} \to \mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)/\mathbb{Q})$ sends $(p)$ to the automorphism $\zeta_n \mapsto \zeta_n^{\,p}$, identifying $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)/\mathbb{Q})$ with $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ with kernel the principal ray $P_{\mathbb{Q},1}^{(n)} = \{(a) : a \equiv 1 \pmod n,\ a > 0\}$.*

*Proof.* Since $p \nmid n$, the prime $(p)$ is unramified in $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$: cyclotomic extensions ramify only at primes dividing $n$. Let $\mathfrak{P}$ be a prime of $\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_n]$ above $p$, with residue field $\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_n]/\mathfrak{P}$. The Artin symbol $\sigma = \left(\frac{L/\mathbb{Q}}{(p)}\right)$ is the unique automorphism of $L$ whose reduction modulo $\mathfrak{P}$ is the Frobenius $\bar\alpha \mapsto \bar\alpha^{\,p}$ of $\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_n]/\mathfrak{P}$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$ (here $N(p) = p$). Applied to $\alpha = \zeta_n$,
$$\overline{\sigma(\zeta_n)} \;=\; \bar\zeta_n^{\,p}.$$
The assignment $\zeta_n \mapsto \zeta_n^{\,p}$ is itself a Galois automorphism of $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)$, because $\gcd(p,n)=1$ and so $\zeta_n^{\,p}$ is again a primitive $n$th root of unity; call this automorphism $\tau_p$. Both $\sigma$ and $\tau_p$ reduce modulo $\mathfrak{P}$ to the same Frobenius map $\bar\alpha\mapsto\bar\alpha^p$, and reduction $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)/\mathbb{Q}) \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Gal}((\mathbb{Z}[\zeta_n]/\mathfrak{P})/\mathbb{F}_p)$ is injective at unramified primes. Hence $\sigma = \tau_p$, that is, $\Phi_{L/\mathbb{Q}}((p)) : \zeta_n \mapsto \zeta_n^{\,p}$.

The standard identification $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)/\mathbb{Q}) \xrightarrow{\sim} (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ sends $\rho \mapsto [a]$ where $\rho(\zeta_n) = \zeta_n^{\,a}$; it is an isomorphism because the cyclotomic polynomial $\Phi_n(x)$ is irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$ (each primitive $n$th root is a conjugate). Under this identification, $\Phi_{L/\mathbb{Q}}((p)) = [p \bmod n]$. For surjectivity, take any class $[a] \in (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ represented by a positive integer $a$ coprime to $n$; the principal ideal $(a) = \prod_p p^{v_p(a)}$ lies in $I_\mathbb{Q}^{(n)}$ and $\Phi_{L/\mathbb{Q}}((a)) = \prod_p [p]^{v_p(a)} = [a]$, so every class is hit. For the kernel, $(a) \in \ker\Phi$ exactly when $\zeta_n^{\,a} = \zeta_n$, i.e. $a \equiv 1 \pmod n$, which is the principal ray $P_{\mathbb{Q},1}^{(n)}$. This is the reciprocity isomorphism $I_\mathbb{Q}^{(n)}/P_{\mathbb{Q},1}^{(n)} \cong (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times \cong \mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_n)/\mathbb{Q})$ in the founding case $K = \mathbb{Q}$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (the Hilbert class field of $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$ is $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5},\, i)$).** *Let $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$, which has class number $h_K = 2$ and discriminant $d_K = -20$. Then $H = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5},\, i)$ is an abelian extension of $K$ of degree $2$, unramified at every finite prime, and is therefore the Hilbert class field of $K$.*

*Proof.* The extension is abelian because $i \mapsto -i$ generates a group of order $2$ that commutes with the (real) symmetries of $K$. The element $i$ does not lie in $K$ (the imaginary quadratic field $K$ contains only the quadratic subfield $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$ over $\mathbb{Q}$, and $\mathbb{Q}(i) \neq \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$), so $[H : K] = 2$.

To verify unramification, compute the discriminant of $H$ over $\mathbb{Q}$. The field $H = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5},\, i)$ is biquadratic, with three quadratic subfields: $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-5})$, $\mathbb{Q}(i)$, and $\mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{5})$ (the last because $\sqrt{-5} \cdot i = \sqrt{5}$ up to sign). Their discriminants are $-20$ (since $-5 \equiv 3 \pmod 4$), $-4$, and $5$ (since $5 \equiv 1 \pmod 4$). The discriminant of a biquadratic field equals the product of the discriminants of its three quadratic subfields, so
$$d_H \;=\; (-20)\cdot(-4)\cdot 5 \;=\; 400 \;=\; (-20)^{2} \;=\; d_K^{2}.$$
The relation between absolute, base, and relative discriminants reads $d_H = d_K^{[H:K]}\, N_{K/\mathbb{Q}}(\mathfrak{d}_{H/K})$, i.e. $d_H = d_K^{2}\, N(\mathfrak{d}_{H/K})$. Substituting $d_H = d_K^{2}$ gives $N(\mathfrak{d}_{H/K}) = 1$, hence the relative discriminant ideal $\mathfrak{d}_{H/K} = \mathcal{O}_K$. A prime of $K$ ramifies in $H$ exactly when it divides $\mathfrak{d}_{H/K}$, so no finite prime ramifies. The field $K$ is totally imaginary (it has no real embeddings), so there are no infinite places to consider. Thus $H/K$ is unramified at every prime.

Finally, $H/K$ is an unramified abelian extension of degree $2 = h_K$. By Artin reciprocity the maximal unramified abelian extension of $K$ has degree $h_K = 2$, so $H$ realises that maximum and $H$ is the Hilbert class field. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (local reciprocity for unramified extensions of $p$-adic fields).** *Let $L/K$ be a finite unramified extension of $p$-adic fields of degree $n$. Then $N_{L/K}(L^\times) = \langle \pi_K^n\rangle \cdot \mathcal{O}_K^\times$, and the local Artin map induces $K^\times / N_{L/K}(L^\times) \cong \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$ sending the class of a uniformiser $\pi_K$ to the Frobenius generator.*

*Proof.* Because $L/K$ is unramified, a uniformiser $\pi_K$ of $K$ is also a uniformiser of $L$, the ramification index is $e = 1$, and the residue-field extension is $\mathbb{F}_{q^n}/\mathbb{F}_q$ of degree $f = n$ where $q = |\mathcal{O}_K/\mathfrak{m}_K|$. The Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$ is canonically $\mathrm{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{q^n}/\mathbb{F}_q)$, cyclic of order $n$, generated by the Frobenius $x \mapsto x^q$.

**Norms of uniformisers.** Since $\pi_K \in L$ is a uniformiser with $v_L(\pi_K) = 1$ and $[L:K]=n$, the valuation formula gives $v_K(N_{L/K}(\pi_K)) = f \cdot v_L(\pi_K) = n \cdot 1 = n$, so $N_{L/K}(\pi_K) = u\,\pi_K^n$ for a unit $u \in \mathcal{O}_K^\times$. Hence the uniformiser-part of the norm group is $\langle \pi_K^n\rangle$ (up to units).

**Norms of units.** The residue-level norm $N : \mathbb{F}_{q^n}^\times \to \mathbb{F}_q^\times$ is $\bar v \mapsto \bar v^{1 + q + \cdots + q^{n-1}}$, a surjection between cyclic groups (its image is all of $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ by finite-field theory). Given $u \in \mathcal{O}_K^\times$ with residue $\bar u \in \mathbb{F}_q^\times$, pick $\bar v \in \mathbb{F}_{q^n}^\times$ with $N(\bar v) = \bar u$, lift $\bar v$ to $v \in \mathcal{O}_L^\times$, and apply Hensel's lemma to the polynomial equation $N_{L/K}(X) - u = 0$ (which has the approximate root $v$ mod $\mathfrak{m}_L$) to obtain a true lift $w \in \mathcal{O}_L^\times$ with $N_{L/K}(w) = u$. Hence $\mathcal{O}_K^\times \subseteq N_{L/K}(\mathcal{O}_L^\times)$.

Combining the two parts, $N_{L/K}(L^\times) = \langle \pi_K^n\rangle \cdot \mathcal{O}_K^\times$. The quotient
$$K^\times / N_{L/K}(L^\times) \;\cong\; \langle \pi_K\rangle\cdot\mathcal{O}_K^\times \big/ \langle\pi_K^n\rangle\cdot\mathcal{O}_K^\times \;\cong\; \langle\pi_K\rangle/\langle\pi_K^n\rangle \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$$
is cyclic of order $n$, generated by the class of $\pi_K$. Sending this class to the Frobenius generator of $\mathrm{Gal}(L/K) \cong \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ gives the local reciprocity isomorphism. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Algebraic number theory [21.17.01].** Class field theory is built directly on the ideal-theoretic foundations of [21.17.01]: the ray class group $\mathrm{Cl}_\mathfrak{m}(K)$ is a quotient of the ideal group $I_K^\mathfrak{m}$ by a refinement of the principal ideals $P_K$, reducing to the ordinary class group $\mathrm{Cl}(K)$ when $\mathfrak{m} = 1$. The Hilbert class field is the extension-theoretic avatar of that class group, and the discriminant formula $d_H = d_K^{[H:K]} N(\mathfrak{d}_{H/K})$ used throughout this unit is the relative form of the ramification theory developed there.

- **Fields and Galois theory [01.04.01].** The Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$ that carries the Artin map is the central object of [01.04.01], and the whole theory is restricted to its **abelian** instances — finite Galois extensions whose group is abelian. Frobenius elements, decomposition groups, and the splitting behaviour of primes underlie every statement here and are imported wholesale from the field-theoretic foundations.

- **Galois representations [21.05.01].** The Artin map is the one-dimensional, finite-image case of a Galois representation: a continuous homomorphism $\mathrm{Gal}(\bar{K}/K) \to \mathbb{C}^\times$, factoring through a finite abelian quotient. The compatible families of $\ell$-adic Galois representations of [21.05.01] are the higher-dimensional, non-finite-image descendants of these abelian characters, and class field theory classifies precisely the degree-one layer they generalise.

- **The Langlands programme [21.10.01].** Replacing the abelian Galois group by the full non-abelian group and finite abelian characters by automorphic representations of $\mathrm{GL}_n$ produces the Langlands correspondence of [21.10.01]. Class field theory is thus the $n = 1$ case of Langlands, and the reciprocity map $I_K^\mathfrak{m}/P_{K,1}^\mathfrak{m}N(I_L^\mathfrak{m}) \cong \mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$ is the abelian shadow of the non-abelian functoriality predicted there.

- **Local fields and the Hilbert symbol [21.02.05].** The global reciprocity law is assembled from local reciprocity laws at each place of $K$, and the local norm-residue computation underlying Stage 2 of the reciprocity proof runs on the Hilbert symbol of [21.02.05]. The Hasse norm theorem likewise rests on the local-global principles for quadratic forms established in the local-field theory of [21.02.03] and [21.02.05].

- **Analytic number theory: the class-number formula [21.03.03].** The conductor-discriminant formula factors $\zeta_L(s)$ over Hecke $L$-functions of the characters of $\mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$, the analytic theory developed in [21.03.03], and the class number $h_K$ that measures the Hilbert class field is recovered from the residue of $\zeta_K$ at $s = 1$. This unit supplies the class-field-theoretic structure that the analytic class-number formula of [21.03.03] evaluates.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Class field theory was completed over four decades by a single sustained programme. Hilbert's *Zahlbericht* of 1897 [ref: TODO_REF Hilbert 1897] set the agenda by listing the problems — the existence of the Hilbert class field, the factorisation of primes under extension, the reciprocity laws — whose solution would constitute the theory. Hilbert conjectured, and Furtwängler later proved, the principalisation theorem: every ideal of $K$ becomes principal in the Hilbert class field $H_K$.

The decisive breakthrough was Takagi's 1920 paper *Über eine Theorie des relativ-Abel'schen Zahlkörpers* [ref: TODO_REF Takagi 1920], which proved the existence and uniqueness theorems for ray class fields and established the bijection between congruence subgroups and abelian extensions. Takagi's theory described *which* abelian extensions existed but not *how* the Galois group matched the ray class group — the isomorphism was known to exist abstractly but had no canonical description.

That final step was Artin's. In the 1927 paper *Beweis des allgemeinen Reziprozitätsgesetzes* [ref: TODO_REF Artin 1927], Artin introduced the Frobenius symbol — now the Artin symbol — and proved it realises the reciprocity isomorphism explicitly, completing the theory. Hasse's *Bericht* of 1930 [ref: TODO_REF Hasse 1930] systematised the local-global picture, including the norm theorem for cyclic extensions that bears his name. Chevalley's 1940 monograph *La théorie du corps de classes* [ref: TODO_REF Chevalley 1940] then introduced the idèle group, replacing the ideal-theoretic ray class groups with idele class groups and recasting the entire theory in cohomological form, which removed the dependence on analytic estimates and exposed the first and second inequalities as the structural engine.

The conceptual move of class field theory — that the symmetries of a field are classified by arithmetic data inside it — became the template for the Langlands programme, which seeks the same kind of dictionary for non-abelian extensions. The abelian theory remains the one corner of that larger programme that is fully closed, and its completion stands as the crown jewel of algebraic number theory.
