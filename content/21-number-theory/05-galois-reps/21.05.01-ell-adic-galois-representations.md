## Intuition [Beginner]

The absolute Galois group $\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}} / \mathbb{Q})$ is the group of all field-theoretic symmetries of the algebraic numbers that fix every rational number. It is enormous: there is no finite collection of elements that generates it, no finite presentation, and no concrete list of all its elements.

But the Galois group acts on every collection of algebraic numbers that can be described purely in terms of the rationals — the roots of any polynomial with rational coefficients, the torsion points of an elliptic curve over the rationals, the étale cohomology of any variety defined over the rationals. Each such action is a representation of the Galois group on a vector space. The question that organises modern number theory is: what do these representations look like, and what arithmetic information do they encode?

An $\ell$-adic Galois representation is a continuous action of the absolute Galois group on a finite-dimensional vector space over the field of $\ell$-adic numbers $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$. The choice of $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$ as the coefficient field, rather than the complex numbers or the rationals, is essential. The Galois group is profinite and totally disconnected, so its only continuous representations on complex vector spaces have finite image, whereas its continuous representations on $\ell$-adic vector spaces are far richer and capture genuine arithmetic information.

The simplest examples come from elliptic curves. Fix an elliptic curve $E$ over the rationals and a prime $\ell$. The $\ell$-power torsion points $E[\ell^n]$ form a group isomorphic to $(\mathbb{Z}/\ell^n)^2$, and the Galois group acts on each $E[\ell^n]$ by permuting these algebraic points. Assembling these actions across all $n$ gives a continuous representation of the Galois group on a free $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-module of rank $2$, called the Tate module. This single object encodes the way the Galois group sees the elliptic curve. Modular forms produce a second class of examples; the étale cohomology of any smooth projective variety produces a third. The Langlands philosophy claims that all reasonable $\ell$-adic Galois representations come from these geometric sources.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute the Galois action on the $\ell = 5$ torsion of the elliptic curve $E : y^2 = x^3 - x$ over the rationals, restricted to a small Galois extension.

Step 1. The curve $E : y^2 = x^3 - x$ has $\mathbb{Q}$-rational points $\{O, (0, 0), (1, 0), (-1, 0)\}$, all of order $1$ or $2$. The $5$-torsion subgroup $E[5]$ is a group of order $25$, of which $24$ are non-identity points, and these new $5$-torsion points live over a finite extension of $\mathbb{Q}$.

Step 2. The Galois group of the splitting field of $E[5]$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ acts on $E[5] \cong (\mathbb{Z}/5)^2$ by linear automorphisms. The Weil pairing on $E[5]$ takes values in the $5$-th roots of unity, so the determinant of the action of $\sigma \in G_\mathbb{Q}$ on $E[5]$ equals the cyclotomic character $\chi_{\text{cyc}}(\sigma) \bmod 5$ — that is, $\sigma$ sends a fixed primitive $5$-th root of unity $\zeta_5$ to $\zeta_5^{\det(\sigma | E[5])}$.

Step 3. The image of the representation $\bar\rho_{E, 5} : G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_5)$ is constrained to land inside the subgroup of $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_5)$ whose determinant equals the cyclotomic character. This is a subgroup of index $4$ (since $\mathbb{F}_5^\times$ has order $4$ and the determinant lands in $\mathbb{F}_5^\times$). For the curve $E : y^2 = x^3 - x$ this image is *not* the full subgroup — the curve has complex multiplication by $\mathbb{Z}[i]$, which forces the image to be much smaller (essentially contained in the normaliser of a non-split Cartan subgroup).

What this tells us: the Galois representation on $\ell$-torsion is not arbitrary. Its determinant is forced by the Weil pairing to equal the cyclotomic character, and special arithmetic of the curve (such as complex multiplication) further constrains the image. Serre's open-image theorem says that *for elliptic curves without complex multiplication*, the image is as large as possible for almost every prime $\ell$.

An $\ell$-adic Galois representation of $G_K = \mathrm{Gal}(\overline{K}/K)$ is, by definition:

D. Any abstract group homomorphism $G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The two essential features are: continuity with respect to the profinite topology on $G_K$ and the $\ell$-adic topology on $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$, and the choice of coefficient field $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** An $\ell$-adic Galois representation is a continuous group homomorphism $\rho : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ for some finite $n$. Continuity is with respect to the profinite topology on $G_K$ and the $\ell$-adic topology on the matrix group. Option A describes Artin representations, which are the continuous complex representations of $G_K$; these have finite image because $G_K$ is profinite and $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ has no small open subgroups containing only the identity. Option C is not the standard setting; continuous infinite-dimensional representations live in the automorphic theory, not the $\ell$-adic Galois theory. Option D drops the continuity requirement, which is the load-bearing condition.

</details>

**True.** For an elliptic curve $E$ over a number field $K$ and a prime $\ell$ not dividing the residue characteristic (which is automatic in the number-field case), the $\ell^n$-torsion subgroup $E[\ell^n]$ is isomorphic to $(\mathbb{Z}/\ell^n \mathbb{Z})^2$ as an abstract group. The Tate module is the inverse limit $T_\ell E = \varprojlim_n E[\ell^n]$, taken along the multiplication-by-$\ell$ maps $E[\ell^{n+1}] \to E[\ell^n]$. The result is a free $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-module of rank $2$. The absolute Galois group of $K$ acts on each $E[\ell^n]$ by permuting its algebraic points, and the actions are compatible across the tower, so the limit carries a continuous $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-linear $G_K$-action — the $\ell$-adic Galois representation $\rho_{E, \ell} : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a number field $K$ with absolute Galois group $G_K := \mathrm{Gal}(\overline{K}/K)$. Equip $G_K$ with its profinite topology, the inverse limit of the finite quotients $\mathrm{Gal}(L/K)$ over all finite Galois extensions $L/K$. Fix a rational prime $\ell$.

**Definition (continuous $\ell$-adic Galois representation).** An **$\ell$-adic Galois representation** of $G_K$ is a continuous group homomorphism
$$
\rho : G_K \longrightarrow \mathrm{GL}(V),
$$
where $V$ is a finite-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$ and $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ carries the $\ell$-adic topology inherited from $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_\ell) \subset M_n(\mathbb{Q}_\ell) = \mathbb{Q}_\ell^{n^2}$. Equivalently, after choosing a basis of $V$ one writes $\rho : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$.

**Equivalent formulation (via a $G_K$-stable lattice).** Every continuous $\rho : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ stabilises a $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-lattice $T \subset V$; equivalently, the image of $\rho$ is contained in some conjugate of $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$. The proof uses compactness of $G_K$ and the fact that $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ is an open subgroup of $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$. Lattice choice gives the **$\ell$-adic integral representation** $\rho : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ and, reducing modulo $\ell$, the **mod-$\ell$ representation** $\bar\rho : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{F}_\ell)$.

**Definition (ramification at a place).** For a finite place $v$ of $K$, fix a decomposition group $D_v \subset G_K$ and its inertia subgroup $I_v \subset D_v$. The representation $\rho$ is **unramified at $v$** if $\rho(I_v) = \{\mathrm{id}\}$; equivalently, $\rho$ factors through the quotient $D_v / I_v$, which is procyclic and topologically generated by the **Frobenius element** $\mathrm{Frob}_v$. The unramified condition makes the conjugacy class of $\rho(\mathrm{Frob}_v)$ in $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ well-defined (independent of the choice of decomposition group). The **conductor** $\mathfrak{N}(\rho)$ records the ramification at primes where $\rho$ is ramified; it is a product over places of local conductor exponents, generalising the conductor of an Artin representation. The representation $\rho$ is **unramified outside a finite set** if there exists a finite set $S$ of places of $K$ such that $\rho$ is unramified at every $v \notin S$.

**Examples.** The most basic examples are:

**Example 1 (cyclotomic character).** The action of $G_\mathbb{Q}$ on the $\ell$-power roots of unity $\mu_{\ell^\infty} = \bigcup_n \mu_{\ell^n}$ gives the **cyclotomic character**
$$
\chi_{\mathrm{cyc}} : G_\mathbb{Q} \longrightarrow \mathbb{Z}_\ell^\times \subset \mathrm{GL}_1(\mathbb{Q}_\ell),
$$
characterised by $\sigma(\zeta) = \zeta^{\chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}(\sigma)}$ for every primitive $\ell^n$-th root of unity $\zeta$ and every $\sigma \in G_\mathbb{Q}$. The character is continuous and surjective onto $\mathbb{Z}_\ell^\times$. It is unramified at every prime $p \neq \ell$, and $\chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = p$ for $p \neq \ell$.

**Example 2 (Tate module of an elliptic curve).** Let $E/K$ be an elliptic curve and $\ell$ a prime. The **$\ell$-adic Tate module** is
$$
T_\ell E := \varprojlim_n E[\ell^n], \qquad V_\ell E := T_\ell E \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}_\ell} \mathbb{Q}_\ell,
$$
where the inverse limit is along the multiplication-by-$\ell$ maps. As a $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-module, $T_\ell E$ is free of rank $2$, and $V_\ell E$ is a $2$-dimensional $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$-vector space. The Galois group $G_K$ acts continuously on $T_\ell E$, yielding the representation
$$
\rho_{E, \ell} : G_K \longrightarrow \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell) \subset \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}_\ell).
$$
By the Néron-Ogg-Shafarevich criterion, $\rho_{E, \ell}$ is unramified at every prime $v$ of $K$ where $E$ has good reduction and $v \nmid \ell$; the determinant equals the cyclotomic character, $\det \rho_{E, \ell} = \chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}$, by the Weil pairing on $T_\ell E$.

**Example 3 (modular Galois representation).** Let $f \in S_k(\Gamma_0(N), \varepsilon)$ be a normalised cuspidal Hecke eigenform of weight $k \geq 2$, level $N$, and Nebentypus character $\varepsilon$. Deligne 1971 constructed a continuous representation
$$
\rho_{f, \ell} : G_\mathbb{Q} \longrightarrow \mathrm{GL}_2(\overline{\mathbb{Q}_\ell})
$$
characterised, for every prime $p \nmid N\ell$, by unramifiedness at $p$ together with the **Eichler-Shimura identities**:
$$
\mathrm{tr}\, \rho_{f, \ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = a_p(f), \qquad \det \rho_{f, \ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = \varepsilon(p) p^{k - 1}.
$$
The case $k = 1$ was completed by Deligne-Serre 1974 via congruences to higher-weight forms.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"Continuous representations of $G_K$ on complex vector spaces are richer than continuous representations on $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$-vector spaces."* The reverse is the case. Since $G_K$ is profinite and totally disconnected, and $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ contains no small open subgroups apart from $\{\mathrm{id}\}$, every continuous $\rho : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ factors through a finite quotient — these are the **Artin representations**. By contrast, $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ has plenty of small open subgroups (the principal congruence subgroups $1 + \ell^k M_n(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$), so continuous $\ell$-adic representations can have infinite image and capture far more information. This asymmetry is the technical reason the $\ell$-adic theory exists.

- *"$\rho$ ramified at $v$ means $\rho$ is undefined at $\mathrm{Frob}_v$."* The Frobenius element $\mathrm{Frob}_v$ is well-defined as a coset in $D_v / I_v$ regardless of ramification, but at a ramified prime the inertia subgroup acts non-identically, so $\rho(\mathrm{Frob}_v)$ is only defined up to the action of $\rho(I_v)$. At an unramified prime $\rho(I_v) = \{\mathrm{id}\}$ and the Frobenius conjugacy class is sharp. The conductor of $\rho$ records the precise way the inertia subgroup acts at each ramified prime, generalising the conductor of an Artin representation.

- *"$\det \rho_{E, \ell}$ is independent of $E$."* Across all elliptic curves $E$ over a fixed number field $K$, the determinant of $\rho_{E, \ell}$ is always the cyclotomic character — this is the **Weil-pairing consequence** $\det \rho_{E, \ell} = \chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}$. What varies with $E$ is the trace of Frobenius (which equals $a_p(E) = p + 1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ for $p$ of good reduction by the Hasse-Weil bound) and the image of $\rho_{E, \ell}$ (which is described by Serre's open-image theorem for non-CM curves).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem of this unit is the unramified-outside-finite-set property of $\ell$-adic Galois representations arising from elliptic curves, due to the Néron-Ogg-Shafarevich criterion of good reduction.

**Theorem (Néron-Ogg-Shafarevich 1968).** *Let $E$ be an elliptic curve over a number field $K$ and $\ell$ a prime. Then the $\ell$-adic Galois representation $\rho_{E, \ell} : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ is unramified at every prime $v$ of $K$ where $E$ has good reduction and $v \nmid \ell$. Consequently, $\rho_{E, \ell}$ is unramified outside the finite set $S = \{v : v \mid \ell\} \cup \{v : E \text{ has bad reduction at } v\}$.*

*Proof.* The strategy: reduce mod $v$, observe that good reduction means the reduction is an elliptic curve over the residue field $k(v) = \mathcal{O}_K / v$, and use that the reduction map on $\ell^n$-torsion is injective for $v \nmid \ell$.

Fix a prime $v$ of $K$ where $E$ has good reduction and $v \nmid \ell$. Let $K_v$ be the completion of $K$ at $v$, with ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_v$ and residue field $k(v) = \mathcal{O}_v / \mathfrak{m}_v$. Let $\bar{K}_v$ be a fixed algebraic closure of $K_v$, with valuation ring $\overline{\mathcal{O}_v}$. By good reduction, the elliptic curve $E$ extends to an elliptic scheme $\mathcal{E}$ over $\mathrm{Spec}\, \mathcal{O}_v$ whose special fibre $\bar{E} := \mathcal{E} \otimes k(v)$ is an elliptic curve over $k(v)$.

The decomposition group $D_v \subset G_K$ at $v$ is naturally identified with $\mathrm{Gal}(\bar{K}_v / K_v)$, and the inertia subgroup $I_v$ is the kernel of the map $D_v \to \mathrm{Gal}(\overline{k(v)} / k(v))$, where $\overline{k(v)}$ is the residue field of $\bar{K}_v$. We need to show $\rho_{E, \ell}(I_v) = \{\mathrm{id}\}$, i.e., $I_v$ acts by the identity on $T_\ell E$.

For each $n \geq 1$, consider the **reduction map** on $\ell^n$-torsion:
$$
\mathrm{red}_v : E[\ell^n](\bar{K}_v) \longrightarrow \bar{E}[\ell^n](\overline{k(v)}).
$$
The map sends an algebraic $\ell^n$-torsion point $P \in E(\bar{K}_v)$ to its reduction $\bar{P} \in \bar{E}(\overline{k(v)})$ obtained by extending $P$ to a section of $\mathcal{E}$ over $\overline{\mathcal{O}_v}$ and restricting to the special fibre.

**Claim.** For $v \nmid \ell$, the reduction map $\mathrm{red}_v$ is an isomorphism of finite abelian groups.

Since both sides are abstractly isomorphic to $(\mathbb{Z}/\ell^n)^2$ (the $\ell^n$-torsion of an elliptic curve over any algebraically closed field of characteristic prime to $\ell$), it suffices to show $\mathrm{red}_v$ is injective. Suppose $\mathrm{red}_v(P) = \bar{O}$ (the identity in the reduction). Then $P$ reduces to the origin, so $P$ lies in the **formal group** $\widehat{E}(\overline{\mathcal{O}_v})$ — the kernel of the reduction map. The formal group $\widehat{E}$ has the structure of a one-dimensional commutative formal Lie group over $\mathcal{O}_v$; its underlying set is the maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}_v(\overline{\mathcal{O}_v})$, and its group law is given by a formal power series. The crucial fact is that multiplication by $\ell$ on $\widehat{E}$ is given by a power series whose linear term is $\ell$ (a unit in $\mathcal{O}_v$ since $v \nmid \ell$). Hence multiplication by $\ell$ on $\widehat{E}(\overline{\mathcal{O}_v})$ is a power-series automorphism whose only fixed point is the identity, so $\widehat{E}(\overline{\mathcal{O}_v})$ has vanishing $\ell$-power torsion. The point $P$, being $\ell^n$-torsion and in $\widehat{E}$, must equal the identity. Injectivity follows.

Now use the claim. The reduction map $\mathrm{red}_v$ is equivariant for the decomposition group $D_v$ acting on $E[\ell^n](\bar{K}_v)$ via $\rho_{E, \ell}$ and acting on $\bar{E}[\ell^n](\overline{k(v)})$ via the Galois action of $\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{k(v)}/k(v)) = D_v / I_v$. The inertia subgroup $I_v$, mapping to the identity in $D_v/I_v$, acts by the identity on the right-hand side $\bar{E}[\ell^n](\overline{k(v)})$. Since $\mathrm{red}_v$ is an isomorphism, $I_v$ acts by the identity on the left-hand side $E[\ell^n](\bar{K}_v)$ as well, for every $n$. Passing to the inverse limit, $I_v$ acts by the identity on $T_\ell E$, so $\rho_{E, \ell}(I_v) = \{\mathrm{id}\}$. Hence $\rho_{E, \ell}$ is unramified at $v$.

Finally, an elliptic curve $E$ over a number field $K$ has good reduction at all but finitely many primes (the bad-reduction primes are those dividing the discriminant of a minimal model), so $S = \{v : v \mid \ell\} \cup \{v : \text{bad reduction}\}$ is finite. The representation $\rho_{E, \ell}$ is unramified at every $v \notin S$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Néron-Ogg-Shafarevich criterion builds toward [21.04.03] the Eichler-Shimura correspondence, where the same unramified-outside-finite-set property holds for modular Galois representations $\rho_{f, \ell}$, with the unramified set being $\{v : v \mid N \ell\}$ for $f$ of level $N$, and appears again in [21.06.01] modularity, where the matching of unramified-outside-$S$ conditions between elliptic-curve and modular representations is one of the consistency checks for the modularity statement $\rho_{E, \ell} \cong \rho_{f_E, \ell}$. The foundational reason is that good-reduction smooth-proper varieties admit smooth proper integral models, and the étale cohomology of the integral model is unramified at the prime of good reduction by the smooth-proper base change theorem; this is exactly the geometric reason that representations arising from $H^i_{\text{ét}}$ are unramified outside finitely many primes, generalising the elliptic-curve case via the identification $V_\ell E \cong H^1_{\text{ét}}(E_{\bar{K}}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)^\vee$. The central insight is that ramification of $\ell$-adic Galois representations of geometric origin is controlled by the bad-reduction locus of the underlying variety, and putting these together identifies the conductor of $\rho_{E, \ell}$ with the arithmetic conductor of $E$ — a key compatibility used in [21.06.01].

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

For the cyclotomic character $\chi_{\mathrm{cyc}} : G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathbb{Z}_\ell^\times$, compute $\chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$ for an unramified prime $p \neq \ell$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Frobenius $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ at an unramified prime acts on roots of unity of order prime to $p$ by raising to the $p$-th power.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$p$.** For $p \neq \ell$ unramified in $\mathbb{Q}(\zeta_{\ell^\infty})/\mathbb{Q}$, the Frobenius element $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ acts on the $\ell^n$-th roots of unity by $\zeta \mapsto \zeta^p$. By the defining relation $\sigma(\zeta) = \zeta^{\chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}(\sigma)}$, this means $\chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) \equiv p \pmod{\ell^n}$ for every $n$, hence $\chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = p$ in $\mathbb{Z}_\ell^\times$. The cyclotomic character is unramified at every prime $p \neq \ell$ and ramified at $p = \ell$ (where the cyclotomic tower itself ramifies).

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

For an elliptic curve $E$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ with good reduction at $p$ and a prime $\ell \neq p$, what is $\det \rho_{E, \ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Weil pairing identifies $\det \rho_{E, \ell}$ with the cyclotomic character.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$p$.** By the Weil pairing on the Tate module, $\det \rho_{E, \ell} = \chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}$, so $\det \rho_{E, \ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = \chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = p$. The trace, by contrast, equals $a_p(E) = p + 1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ (Hasse-Weil), and the Hasse bound $|a_p(E)| \leq 2\sqrt{p}$ controls its size.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

State the **Weil pairing** on $T_\ell E$ and show how it implies $\det \rho_{E, \ell} = \chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Weil pairing is a perfect, alternating, Galois-equivariant pairing $T_\ell E \times T_\ell E \to T_\ell \mu = \mathbb{Z}_\ell(1)$. The Galois action on a perfect alternating pairing controls the determinant of the action on either factor.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Weil pairing.** For each $n \geq 1$, there is a non-degenerate alternating bilinear pairing
$$
e_{\ell^n} : E[\ell^n] \times E[\ell^n] \longrightarrow \mu_{\ell^n}
$$
into the $\ell^n$-th roots of unity, characterised by Galois-equivariance: $\sigma(e_{\ell^n}(P, Q)) = e_{\ell^n}(\sigma P, \sigma Q)$ for every $\sigma \in G_K$. Passing to the inverse limit gives the **$\ell$-adic Weil pairing**
$$
e_\ell : T_\ell E \times T_\ell E \longrightarrow T_\ell \mu = \mathbb{Z}_\ell(1),
$$
where $\mathbb{Z}_\ell(1) := T_\ell \mu = \varprojlim_n \mu_{\ell^n}$ is the **Tate twist** of $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$, the rank-$1$ free $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-module on which $G_K$ acts via the cyclotomic character.

**Determinant computation.** Pick a basis $(P, Q)$ of $T_\ell E$ as a free $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-module of rank $2$. The Weil pairing satisfies $e_\ell(P, Q) = \zeta$ for a generator $\zeta$ of $\mathbb{Z}_\ell(1)$. For $\sigma \in G_K$ acting by the matrix $\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}$ in this basis, Galois-equivariance gives
$$
\sigma(e_\ell(P, Q)) = e_\ell(\sigma P, \sigma Q) = e_\ell(a P + c Q, b P + d Q) = (a d - b c)\, e_\ell(P, Q) = \det(\sigma)\, \zeta,
$$
using bilinearity and alternation $e_\ell(P, P) = e_\ell(Q, Q) = 0$ (so cross terms cancel except for the determinantal one). On the other hand, $\sigma(\zeta) = \chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}(\sigma) \zeta$ by the definition of the cyclotomic character. Comparing, $\det \rho_{E, \ell}(\sigma) = \chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}(\sigma)$ for every $\sigma$, hence $\det \rho_{E, \ell} = \chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

State the **characteristic polynomial of Frobenius** for $\rho_{E, \ell}$ at a prime $p$ of good reduction, $p \neq \ell$, and derive the **Hasse-Weil bound** $|a_p(E)| \leq 2\sqrt{p}$ from positivity of a discriminant.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The characteristic polynomial of $\rho_{E, \ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$ has trace $a_p(E)$ and determinant $p$ by the Weil pairing. The Hasse-Weil bound is equivalent to the eigenvalues having absolute value $\sqrt{p}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Characteristic polynomial.** For $E/\mathbb{Q}$ with good reduction at $p$ and $p \neq \ell$, the Frobenius $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ acts on $T_\ell E$ via a matrix in $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ with
$$
\mathrm{tr}\, \rho_{E, \ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = a_p(E), \qquad \det \rho_{E, \ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = p,
$$
where $a_p(E) = p + 1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ is the **trace of Frobenius**. The characteristic polynomial is
$$
\mathrm{charpoly}_{\mathrm{Frob}_p}(X) = X^2 - a_p(E) X + p \in \mathbb{Z}[X].
$$

**Hasse-Weil bound.** The two roots of the characteristic polynomial are $\alpha, \beta$ with $\alpha \beta = p$ and $\alpha + \beta = a_p$. The **Weil conjecture for elliptic curves** (Hasse 1933, generalising to higher dimension via Weil 1948 and Deligne 1974) asserts that $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are complex conjugate algebraic integers with $|\alpha| = |\beta| = \sqrt{p}$. Then $|a_p| = |\alpha + \beta| \leq |\alpha| + |\beta| = 2\sqrt{p}$, the Hasse-Weil bound.

Equivalently, the discriminant of the characteristic polynomial is
$$
a_p^2 - 4 p = (a_p - 2\sqrt{p})(a_p + 2\sqrt{p}),
$$
and the Hasse-Weil bound $|a_p| \leq 2\sqrt{p}$ is the statement that this discriminant is $\leq 0$ — that is, the roots are complex conjugate rather than two real distinct values. Equivalently the trace lies in the Hasse interval $[-2\sqrt{p}, 2\sqrt{p}]$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

State **Serre's open-image theorem** (Serre 1972 *Invent. Math.* 15) and explain its content for an elliptic curve $E$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ without complex multiplication.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Galois representation $\rho_{E, \ell} : G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ has image constrained by the Weil pairing to land in the subgroup of $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ with determinant the cyclotomic character. Serre's theorem describes how close to the full constrained subgroup the image lies.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Theorem (Serre 1972).** *Let $E$ be an elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ without complex multiplication. Then:*

(a) *For every prime $\ell$, the image $\rho_{E, \ell}(G_\mathbb{Q}) \subseteq \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ is open.*

(b) *For all but finitely many primes $\ell$, the image equals the full group $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$.*

(c) *The product representation $\rho_E : G_\mathbb{Q} \to \prod_\ell \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ has open image in the product topology.*

**Content for non-CM $E/\mathbb{Q}$.** The Weil pairing forces $\rho_{E, \ell}(G_\mathbb{Q})$ to map onto $\mathbb{Z}_\ell^\times$ via the determinant (since $\chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}$ is surjective onto $\mathbb{Z}_\ell^\times$ for $\ell \geq 3$). The open-image theorem says that for non-CM $E$, this is essentially the only constraint: the image is as large as possible — open in $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ for every $\ell$, equal to $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ for almost every $\ell$, and the simultaneous representation across all $\ell$ has open image in the full product.

For elliptic curves with complex multiplication (by an order in an imaginary quadratic field $K$), the image of $\rho_{E, \ell}$ is much smaller: it commutes with the action of $\mathcal{O}_K \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} \mathbb{Z}_\ell$ on $V_\ell E$, so it is contained in the normaliser of a Cartan subgroup of $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ (the normaliser of a split Cartan if $\ell$ splits in $K$, of a non-split Cartan if $\ell$ is inert). The CM case is governed by class field theory of $K$ rather than by Serre's theorem.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Define **semisimplicity** for an $\ell$-adic Galois representation and state when a representation $\rho_{E, \ell}$ attached to an elliptic curve is irreducible.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A representation is semisimple if it is a direct sum of irreducibles. For elliptic curves, the irreducibility of $\rho_{E, \ell}$ is essentially equivalent to the absence of an isogeny to a curve with a rational $\ell$-isogeny.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Semisimplicity.** An $\ell$-adic Galois representation $\rho : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}(V)$ is **semisimple** if $V$ decomposes as a direct sum of $G_K$-stable subspaces $V = \bigoplus_i V_i$ with each $V_i$ irreducible (no proper non-zero $G_K$-stable subspace). For finite-dimensional representations over a field of characteristic $0$, semisimplicity is the property of being decomposable into irreducibles.

**Irreducibility of $\rho_{E, \ell}$.** For an elliptic curve $E/K$, the representation $\rho_{E, \ell} : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ on $V_\ell E$ is reducible if and only if there is a non-zero proper $G_K$-stable subspace $W \subset V_\ell E$. Since $\dim V_\ell E = 2$, the only possibility is $\dim W = 1$, a $G_K$-stable line. A $G_K$-stable line in $V_\ell E$ corresponds (via the lattice $T_\ell E$) to a $G_K$-stable saturated $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-submodule of rank $1$, equivalently a sub-tower of cyclic $\ell^n$-subgroups of $E[\ell^n]$ stable under $G_K$ — that is, a **rational cyclic $\ell$-isogeny** $E \to E'$ over $K$.

By a theorem of Mazur 1978 *Invent. Math.* 44 (for $K = \mathbb{Q}$), $E/\mathbb{Q}$ admits a rational cyclic $\ell$-isogeny for only finitely many primes $\ell$ (in fact $\ell \in \{2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, 37, 43, 67, 163\}$ for non-CM $E$). So $\rho_{E, \ell}$ is irreducible for almost every $\ell$ when $E/\mathbb{Q}$ has no complex multiplication.

**Semisimplicity of $\rho_{E, \ell}$.** By the work of Tate, $\rho_{E, \ell}$ is **semisimple** in all cases (irreducible, or a sum of two characters when reducible). The semisimplicity is a deep theorem and uses the action of the Hecke algebra plus the rigidity properties of the Galois action on the Néron model.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

State the **Tate conjecture** for elliptic curves over a number field $K$, and explain how Faltings 1983 proved it.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Tate conjecture relates morphisms of abelian varieties to morphisms of their Tate modules as Galois modules. Faltings's proof uses height functions and the finiteness of isogeny classes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Tate conjecture (Tate 1966 conjecture, Faltings 1983 theorem for abelian varieties over number fields).** *For abelian varieties $A$ and $B$ over a number field $K$, the natural map*
$$
\mathrm{Hom}(A, B) \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{Z}_\ell \longrightarrow \mathrm{Hom}_{G_K}(T_\ell A, T_\ell B)
$$
*is an isomorphism.*

**Specialised to elliptic curves.** For elliptic curves $E, E'$ over $K$, the natural map
$$
\mathrm{Hom}(E, E') \otimes \mathbb{Z}_\ell \to \mathrm{Hom}_{G_K}(T_\ell E, T_\ell E')
$$
is an isomorphism. Equivalently, two elliptic curves over $K$ are isogenous (over $K$) if and only if their $\ell$-adic Galois representations are isomorphic for some (equivalently, every) $\ell$ different from the residue characteristics in $K$.

**Faltings's proof.** The argument has three steps. **Step 1 (Faltings height).** Define the Faltings height $h_F(A)$ of an abelian variety $A/K$, a height function on the moduli space $\mathcal{A}_g$ of principally polarised abelian varieties of dimension $g$. **Step 2 (finiteness of isogeny classes).** Prove that the isogeny class of $A$ contains only finitely many isomorphism classes — this is the Shafarevich conjecture for abelian varieties, proved by Faltings 1983 *Invent. Math.* 73. The key estimate bounds the Faltings height of isogenous abelian varieties in terms of the conductor and the $\ell$-adic Galois representation. **Step 3 (Tate-style argument).** Given a $G_K$-equivariant map $T_\ell A \to T_\ell B$, lift it to a $G_K$-equivariant map of $\ell$-adic schemes, descend via étale-descent to a morphism $A \to B$ over $K$ using the finiteness from Step 2.

**Companion results.** Faltings's paper simultaneously proved the **Mordell conjecture** (a curve of genus $\geq 2$ over a number field has only finitely many rational points), the **semisimplicity** of $V_\ell A$ as a $G_K$-representation for every abelian variety $A/K$, and the **Tate conjecture** for abelian varieties as above. These three results together constitute one of the high-water marks of $20$th-century arithmetic geometry.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

State the **Deligne-Serre theorem** (Deligne-Serre 1974 *Ann. Sci. ENS* 7) attaching $\ell$-adic Galois representations to weight-1 cuspidal Hecke eigenforms.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For weight $k \geq 2$, Deligne 1971 constructed the Galois representation via étale cohomology of Kuga-Sato varieties. The weight-1 case is fundamentally different — the étale-cohomology construction fails — and Deligne-Serre's approach uses congruences.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Theorem (Deligne-Serre 1974).** *Let $f \in S_1(\Gamma_1(N), \varepsilon)$ be a normalised cuspidal Hecke eigenform of weight $1$, level $N$, and Nebentypus character $\varepsilon$, with Fourier expansion $f(z) = \sum_{n \geq 1} a_n(f) q^n$. Then there exists a continuous, irreducible, odd Artin representation*
$$
\rho_f : G_\mathbb{Q} \longrightarrow \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{C})
$$
*with finite image, unramified outside $N$, such that for every prime $p \nmid N$:*
$$
\mathrm{tr}\, \rho_f(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = a_p(f), \qquad \det \rho_f(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = \varepsilon(p).
$$

**Comments.** "Odd" means $\det \rho_f(c) = -1$ for complex conjugation $c$, which follows from $\det \rho_f = \varepsilon$ being an odd character (since weight-1 forms with $a_0 = 0$ have odd Nebentypus, by a parity argument). The representation $\rho_f$ has **finite image**, so it is in particular an Artin representation (a continuous complex representation factoring through a finite quotient of $G_\mathbb{Q}$). Embedding $\mathbb{C} \hookrightarrow \overline{\mathbb{Q}_\ell}$ gives the $\ell$-adic incarnation $\rho_{f, \ell} : G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\overline{\mathbb{Q}_\ell})$ with the same trace data.

**Construction (sketch).** The strategy is to construct $\rho_f$ as a Galois representation **modulo $\ell^n$** for every $\ell$ and $n$, by congruences of $f$ to higher-weight forms whose Galois representations are given by Deligne 1971. Specifically: for every $\ell$ and $n$, find a weight-$k$ eigenform $g$ (with $k \geq 2$) such that $g \equiv f \pmod{\ell^n}$ in Fourier coefficients; then $\rho_{g, \ell} \pmod{\ell^n}$ provides the mod-$\ell^n$ reduction of the desired $\rho_f$. Patching across all $\ell$ and using the finite-image constraint completes the construction. The hardest technical step is the existence of the congruence partners $g$, which Deligne-Serre proved using Eisenstein-series perturbations and the Hida theory of $p$-adic families of modular forms (in its 1974 pre-history).

**Significance.** The Deligne-Serre theorem completes the modular-Galois-representation correspondence for the full weight range $k \geq 1$. The case $k = 1$ also resolves a sub-case of the Artin conjecture: the Artin $L$-function $L(\rho_f, s)$ extends to an entire function on $\mathbb{C}$, since it equals the modular $L$-function $L(f, s)$, which is entire by Hecke 1937.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The category of $\ell$-adic Galois representations

Fix a number field $K$, a prime $\ell$, and consider the category $\mathrm{Rep}_{\mathbb{Q}_\ell}(G_K)$ of finite-dimensional continuous $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$-representations of $G_K$. Morphisms are $G_K$-equivariant $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$-linear maps. The category is:

- **Abelian.** Kernels, cokernels, images, and direct sums are formed at the level of underlying vector spaces, with the Galois action restricted or induced.
- **$\mathbb{Q}_\ell$-linear.** Hom sets are $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$-vector spaces.
- **Tensor.** The tensor product $V \otimes_{\mathbb{Q}_\ell} W$ of two representations carries the diagonal Galois action $\sigma(v \otimes w) = \sigma(v) \otimes \sigma(w)$.
- **Rigid (duals exist).** The dual $V^\vee = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbb{Q}_\ell}(V, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ carries the contragredient action $(\sigma \cdot \phi)(v) = \phi(\sigma^{-1} v)$, satisfying $(V^\vee)^\vee = V$ and the standard adjunction $\mathrm{Hom}(V \otimes W, U) = \mathrm{Hom}(V, U \otimes W^\vee)$.
- **Has Tate twists.** The cyclotomic character $\chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}$ defines a $1$-dimensional representation $\mathbb{Q}_\ell(1)$, with $V(n) := V \otimes \mathbb{Q}_\ell(1)^{\otimes n}$ the **$n$-th Tate twist** for any integer $n \in \mathbb{Z}$. Tate twists shift the weight (when weights are defined): if $V$ has weight $w$, then $V(n)$ has weight $w - 2n$.

A representation is **semisimple** if it decomposes as a direct sum of irreducibles. Representations of geometric origin (étale cohomology, Tate modules of abelian varieties) are semisimple by deep theorems of Faltings 1983 for abelian varieties [ref: TODO_REF Faltings 1983]. The semisimplicity of $H^i_{\text{ét}}(X_{\bar K}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ for general smooth proper $X$ is an open conjecture (the **conservativity** part of the Tate conjecture in higher dimension).

**Sub-category of geometric representations.** A representation $V \in \mathrm{Rep}_{\mathbb{Q}_\ell}(G_K)$ is **geometric** if it is unramified outside a finite set $S$ of places and is **de Rham at every place above $\ell$**. The de Rham condition is a strong constraint introduced by Fontaine 1994 [ref: TODO_REF Fontaine 1994] (see the Fontaine-classification subsection below). The Fontaine-Mazur conjecture (Mazur 1989, refined by Fontaine-Mazur 1995 *Proc. Conf. in Honor of Iwasawa*) says: every irreducible geometric representation arises from a sub-quotient of $H^i_{\text{ét}}(X_{\bar K}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ for some smooth proper $X/K$.

### Geometric origin via étale cohomology

The deepest examples of $\ell$-adic Galois representations come from the **$\ell$-adic étale cohomology** of smooth proper varieties. For $X/K$ smooth proper and $\ell$ a prime, the étale cohomology
$$
H^i_{\text{ét}}(X_{\bar K}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell), \qquad i = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, 2 \dim X
$$
is a finite-dimensional $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$-vector space carrying a continuous action of $G_K$. The construction is due to Grothendieck and developed in SGA 4, SGA 4.5, and SGA 5 [ref: TODO_REF Grothendieck SGA].

**Key properties of $H^i_{\text{ét}}$:**

(a) **Comparison with singular cohomology.** For $X$ a smooth proper variety over a number field $K$ embedded in $\mathbb{C}$, there is a comparison isomorphism
$$
H^i_{\text{ét}}(X_{\bar K}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell) \otimes_{\mathbb{Q}_\ell} \mathbb{C} \cong H^i_{\text{sing}}(X(\mathbb{C}), \mathbb{Q}) \otimes_\mathbb{Q} \mathbb{C}
$$
(Artin's comparison theorem, SGA 4 Exposé XI; or more directly via base change to $\mathbb{C}$ and the comparison with singular cohomology of the complex-analytic space).

(b) **Functoriality.** Every morphism $f : X \to Y$ of smooth proper varieties over $K$ induces $G_K$-equivariant maps $f^* : H^i_{\text{ét}}(Y_{\bar K}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell) \to H^i_{\text{ét}}(X_{\bar K}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$.

(c) **Unramified outside bad reduction $\cup \{\ell\}$.** By smooth-proper base change in SGA 4, $H^i_{\text{ét}}(X_{\bar K}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ is unramified at every place $v$ of $K$ where $X$ has good reduction and $v \nmid \ell$.

(d) **Frobenius eigenvalues are Weil numbers (Weil conjectures).** At a place $v$ of good reduction with residue field $k(v)$ of size $q = N(v)$, the eigenvalues of $\rho_v(\mathrm{Frob}_v)$ on $H^i_{\text{ét}}(X_{\bar K}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ are algebraic integers of absolute value $q^{i/2}$. This is the **Riemann hypothesis** for the local zeta function of $X$ at $v$, proved by Deligne 1974 (Weil I) for proper smooth $X$ over $k(v)$ and extended in Deligne 1980 (Weil II) [ref: TODO_REF Deligne 1980] to a general framework of mixed Hodge structures on $\ell$-adic sheaves.

**Example (Tate module of an elliptic curve as étale $H^1$).** For an elliptic curve $E/K$, there is a canonical isomorphism of $G_K$-modules
$$
H^1_{\text{ét}}(E_{\bar K}, \mathbb{Z}_\ell) \cong (T_\ell E)^\vee = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbb{Z}_\ell}(T_\ell E, \mathbb{Z}_\ell),
$$
the dual Tate module. The Weil-Frobenius eigenvalues on $H^1_{\text{ét}}$ (of weight $1$) are the inverses of the Frobenius eigenvalues on $T_\ell E$ (of weight $-1$), consistent with the duality. This is the source of the identification of elliptic-curve $L$-functions $L(E, s) = L(H^1_{\text{ét}}(E), s)$ in the étale-cohomology framework.

### Fontaine's classification: $p$-adic Hodge theory

The deepest subject in the theory of $\ell$-adic Galois representations is the study of representations of the **local** Galois group $G_K = \mathrm{Gal}(\bar K / K)$ when $K$ is a $p$-adic field and the coefficient field is $\mathbb{Q}_p$ (so $\ell = p$). Fontaine 1982 [ref: TODO_REF Fontaine 1982] introduced the **Hodge-Tate decomposition** for such representations: when $V$ is a $p$-adic representation of $G_K$ arising from a $p$-divisible group, the completed scalar extension decomposes as
$$
V \otimes_{\mathbb{Q}_p} \mathbb{C}_p \cong \bigoplus_{i \in \mathbb{Z}} \mathbb{C}_p(-i)^{h_i},
$$
where $\mathbb{C}_p$ is the completion of $\bar K$, the action of $G_K$ on the right side is via Tate twists of the cyclotomic character, and the **Hodge-Tate weights** $\{i \in \mathbb{Z} : h_i > 0\}$ are the indices where the decomposition has non-zero component. Fontaine 1994 [ref: TODO_REF Fontaine 1994] introduced the **period rings**
$$
B_{\mathrm{HT}} \subset B_{\mathrm{dR}}, \qquad B_{\mathrm{cris}} \subset B_{\mathrm{st}} \subset B_{\mathrm{dR}}
$$
as $G_K$-stable subrings of a large $\mathbb{C}_p$-algebra. A $p$-adic representation $V$ is:

(a) **Hodge-Tate** if $V \otimes_{\mathbb{Q}_p} B_{\mathrm{HT}}$ is $B_{\mathrm{HT}}^{G_K}$-free of rank $\dim V$;

(b) **de Rham** if $V \otimes_{\mathbb{Q}_p} B_{\mathrm{dR}}$ is $B_{\mathrm{dR}}^{G_K} = K$-free of rank $\dim V$;

(c) **semistable** if $V \otimes_{\mathbb{Q}_p} B_{\mathrm{st}}$ is $B_{\mathrm{st}}^{G_K} = K_0$-free of rank $\dim V$ (where $K_0$ is the maximal unramified subfield of $K$);

(d) **crystalline** if $V \otimes_{\mathbb{Q}_p} B_{\mathrm{cris}}$ is $B_{\mathrm{cris}}^{G_K} = K_0$-free of rank $\dim V$.

The inclusions $B_{\mathrm{cris}} \subset B_{\mathrm{st}} \subset B_{\mathrm{dR}}$ yield the implications
$$
\text{crystalline} \implies \text{semistable} \implies \text{de Rham} \implies \text{Hodge-Tate}.
$$

**Connection to geometry.** Representations of geometric origin (étale cohomology of a smooth proper variety over $K$, Tate modules of abelian varieties with good reduction) are **crystalline at $p$**. Representations from varieties with semistable but not good reduction are **semistable but not crystalline**. The **$p$-adic comparison theorem** of Fontaine-Messing 1987 / Faltings 1989 / Tsuji 1999 makes this precise: for $X/K$ smooth proper with good reduction, there is a $G_K$-equivariant isomorphism
$$
H^i_{\text{ét}}(X_{\bar K}, \mathbb{Q}_p) \otimes_{\mathbb{Q}_p} B_{\mathrm{cris}} \cong H^i_{\mathrm{cris}}(X_{k}/W) \otimes_W B_{\mathrm{cris}},
$$
where the right side is crystalline cohomology of the special fibre, identifying $\ell = p$-adic étale cohomology with crystalline cohomology after extending scalars to $B_{\mathrm{cris}}$. This is the $p$-adic analogue of the de Rham comparison theorem $H^i_{\mathrm{dR}}(X/\mathbb{C}) \cong H^i_{\text{sing}}(X(\mathbb{C}), \mathbb{C})$ in the complex-analytic case.

### Deformation theory and the Mazur framework

Mazur 1989 [ref: TODO_REF Mazur 1989] introduced the deformation theory of $\ell$-adic Galois representations as the framework underlying the modularity programme. Fix a residual mod-$\ell$ representation $\bar\rho : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{F})$ (for $\mathbb{F}$ a finite field) and consider **deformations** of $\bar\rho$: pairs $(\rho, R)$ consisting of a complete local Noetherian $W(\mathbb{F})$-algebra $R$ with residue field $\mathbb{F}$ and a continuous representation $\rho : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_n(R)$ reducing to $\bar\rho$ modulo the maximal ideal of $R$.

**Theorem (Mazur 1989, universal deformations).** *If $\bar\rho$ is absolutely irreducible, there exists a universal deformation ring $R^{\mathrm{univ}}_{\bar\rho}$ and a universal deformation $\rho^{\mathrm{univ}}$ such that every deformation factors uniquely through $\rho^{\mathrm{univ}}$.*

The universal deformation ring is the central object of the **Taylor-Wiles patching method** used by Wiles 1995 [ref: TODO_REF Wiles 1995] in the proof of Fermat's Last Theorem. The Taylor-Wiles strategy proves the modularity of $\bar\rho$ by establishing an **$R = T$ isomorphism** between the universal deformation ring $R^{\mathrm{univ}}_{\bar\rho}$ and the appropriate Hecke algebra $\mathbb{T}$ acting on modular forms whose mod-$\ell$ Galois representations match $\bar\rho$. The isomorphism $R \cong T$ converts the deformation question into a question about the structure of the Hecke algebra, which can be answered explicitly via congruences of modular forms.

### Khare-Wintenberger and Serre's conjecture

Serre 1987 *Duke Math. J.* 54 conjectured that every irreducible odd continuous mod-$\ell$ Galois representation $\bar\rho : G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\bar{\mathbb{F}}_\ell)$ arises from a modular form: there exists a normalised cuspidal Hecke eigenform $f$ of some weight $k(\bar\rho)$ and level $N(\bar\rho)$ with $\bar\rho \cong \bar\rho_{f, \ell}$. The optimal weight and level are predicted explicitly by Serre's formulae.

**Theorem (Khare-Wintenberger 2008-09, Kisin 2009).** *Serre's modularity conjecture is true.*

The proof, published in Khare-Wintenberger 2009 *Invent. Math.* 178 (parts I and II) [ref: TODO_REF Khare-Wintenberger 2009] together with Kisin's 2009 paper on modularity lifting, proceeds by induction on the Artin conductor, using the modularity-lifting theorems of Wiles, Taylor, Skinner, and Diamond, together with the Khare-Wintenberger "killing ramification" technique. The proof completes the local-to-global program in the case of $2$-dimensional mod-$\ell$ Galois representations of $G_\mathbb{Q}$ and is the residual-characteristic counterpart to the Wiles modularity theorem for elliptic curves.

**Synthesis.** The category of $\ell$-adic Galois representations is the foundational arithmetic object linking algebraic number theory to the geometry of varieties over $K$, and the central insight is that continuous representations of the profinite group $G_K$ on $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$-vector spaces capture far more arithmetic information than complex representations, since the latter have finite image while the former can have infinite image with rich ramification structure. The cyclotomic character is the prototype, the Tate module of an elliptic curve is the first $2$-dimensional example, the modular Galois representation $\rho_{f, \ell}$ generalises to all weight-$k$ Hecke eigenforms, and étale cohomology $H^i_{\text{ét}}(X_{\bar K}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ produces every example of geometric origin. The foundational reason that these representations are unramified outside a finite set is the existence of integral models with good reduction at all but finitely many primes — the Néron-Ogg-Shafarevich criterion for elliptic curves, smooth-proper base change for general varieties.

Putting these together with Fontaine's $p$-adic Hodge theory identifies the local structure at $\ell$ as crystalline / semistable / de Rham / Hodge-Tate, and this is exactly the bridge from arithmetic (the global Galois action) to geometry (the de Rham and crystalline cohomologies of the underlying variety), with the comparison theorems of Fontaine-Messing-Faltings-Tsuji making the identification explicit. The pattern builds toward [21.04.03] the Eichler-Shimura correspondence (the prototype of the modular-Galois-representation correspondence in dimension $2$), [21.06.01] the modularity theorem (every elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ arises from a modular form, equivalently $\rho_{E, \ell} \cong \rho_{f, \ell}$ for some weight-$2$ cuspidal eigenform), [21.07.01] Iwasawa theory (the deformation-theoretic study of $\ell$-adic representations along the cyclotomic $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-extension), and the Langlands programme generally, where automorphic representations of $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{A}_K)$ are conjectured to correspond to $n$-dimensional $\ell$-adic Galois representations of $G_K$ via the Fontaine-Mazur dictionary, reciprocally proved in many low-dimensional cases through Wiles-Taylor-Wiles, Khare-Wintenberger, and Calegari-Geraghty 2018.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (existence of a $G_K$-stable lattice).** *Let $\rho : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ be a continuous representation of a profinite group $G_K$. Then there exists a $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-lattice $T \subset V = \mathbb{Q}_\ell^n$ stable under $\rho$.*

*Proof.* The subgroup $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z}_\ell) \subset \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ is open (it is the preimage of $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z}_\ell / \ell^k)$ under any continuous reduction map, hence open by continuity of $\mathrm{GL}_n$ operations). By continuity, $\rho^{-1}(\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z}_\ell))$ is open in $G_K$, hence has finite index $[G_K : \rho^{-1}(\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z}_\ell))] < \infty$ (since open subgroups of a profinite group have finite index).

Let $H := \rho^{-1}(\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Z}_\ell))$. Pick coset representatives $g_1, \ldots, g_r$ for $G_K / H$. Then the standard lattice $T_0 := \mathbb{Z}_\ell^n \subset V$ is $H$-stable, and the lattice
$$
T := \sum_{i = 1}^r \rho(g_i) T_0
$$
is $G_K$-stable: for any $g \in G_K$, $\rho(g) T = \sum_i \rho(g g_i) T_0$, and since each $g g_i$ lies in some coset $g_{i'} H$, we have $\rho(g g_i) T_0 = \rho(g_{i'}) \rho(h_i) T_0 = \rho(g_{i'}) T_0$ for some $h_i \in H$. Hence $\rho(g) T = \sum_{i'} \rho(g_{i'}) T_0 = T$, completing the proof. $\square$

**Proposition (Weil pairing gives $\det \rho_{E, \ell} = \chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}$).** *For an elliptic curve $E$ over a number field $K$ and a prime $\ell$, the determinant of the Tate-module Galois representation equals the cyclotomic character: $\det \rho_{E, \ell} = \chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}$.*

*Proof.* See Exercise 3. The Weil pairing $e_\ell : T_\ell E \times T_\ell E \to \mathbb{Z}_\ell(1)$ is non-degenerate, alternating, and $G_K$-equivariant; non-degeneracy and rank-$2$ imply that the induced map $\wedge^2 T_\ell E \to \mathbb{Z}_\ell(1)$ is an isomorphism, identifying $\wedge^2 T_\ell E \cong \mathbb{Z}_\ell(1)$. The Galois action on the top exterior power equals the determinant of the representation, hence $\det \rho_{E, \ell} = \chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}$. $\square$

**Proposition (Néron-Ogg-Shafarevich criterion).** *Let $E/K$ be an elliptic curve, $v$ a finite place of $K$ with $v \nmid \ell$. Then $\rho_{E, \ell}$ is unramified at $v$ if and only if $E$ has good reduction at $v$.*

*Proof.* The forward direction is the Key theorem above. The reverse direction (criterion of good reduction): if $\rho_{E, \ell}$ is unramified at $v$, then the inertia $I_v$ acts by the identity on $T_\ell E$ for some (equivalently, every) $\ell \neq p$. By a theorem of Serre-Tate 1968 *Ann. Math.* 88, this is equivalent to $E$ having good reduction at $v$ — the reduction map $T_\ell E \to T_\ell \bar{E}$ is then an isomorphism, the Néron model of $E$ has abelian-variety special fibre, and the curve extends smoothly across $v$. The proof of the reverse direction uses the moduli theory of $p$-divisible groups (Tate 1967) and is substantially deeper than the forward direction. $\square$

**Proposition (Tate module of a CM elliptic curve has image in a Cartan normaliser).** *Let $E$ be an elliptic curve over $K$ with complex multiplication by an order $\mathcal{O}$ in an imaginary quadratic field $F$. Then $\rho_{E, \ell}(G_K)$ is contained in the normaliser of a Cartan subgroup of $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$.*

*Proof.* The order $\mathcal{O}$ acts on $T_\ell E$ by $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-linear endomorphisms commuting with the $G_K$-action. Tensoring with $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$, the action of $\mathcal{O} \otimes \mathbb{Z}_\ell$ on $V_\ell E = T_\ell E \otimes \mathbb{Q}_\ell$ exhibits $V_\ell E$ as a free module of rank $1$ over $F \otimes_\mathbb{Q} \mathbb{Q}_\ell$. If $\ell$ splits in $F$, then $F \otimes \mathbb{Q}_\ell \cong \mathbb{Q}_\ell \times \mathbb{Q}_\ell$ acts diagonally, so $V_\ell E$ decomposes as a direct sum of two $G_{K'}$-stable lines (after possibly enlarging $K$ to a degree-$2$ extension $K' = K \cdot F$), and the Galois action is contained in a split Cartan subgroup of $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$. The full $G_K$-action permutes the two lines via the quadratic character of $K'/K$, so the image is contained in the normaliser of the split Cartan. If $\ell$ is inert in $F$, then $F \otimes \mathbb{Q}_\ell$ is a quadratic field extension of $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$ acting irreducibly on $V_\ell E$, and the Galois action lies in the normaliser of the non-split Cartan. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Hecke operators and Hecke algebra [21.04.02].** The mechanism by which the modular Galois representation $\rho_{f, \ell}$ is attached to a Hecke eigenform $f$ runs through the Hecke algebra: the Fourier coefficients $a_n(f)$ are the eigenvalues of the Hecke operators $T_n$, and the Eichler-Shimura identities $\mathrm{tr}\, \rho_{f, \ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = a_p(f)$ and $\det \rho_{f, \ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = p^{k - 1} \varepsilon(p)$ identify the trace of Frobenius with the Hecke-operator eigenvalue. The Hecke algebra $\mathbb{T}$ acts on the space of cusp forms, and the systems of eigenvalues give the Galois representations. This compatibility is the backbone of the modularity programme.

- **Eichler-Shimura correspondence [21.04.03].** The sibling-in-flight unit. The correspondence is the construction of the modular Galois representation $\rho_{f, \ell}$ for weight $k = 2$ cuspidal Hecke eigenforms via the étale cohomology of the modular curve $X_0(N)$ (equivalently, the Tate module of the Jacobian $J_0(N) = \mathrm{Jac}(X_0(N))$). The signature identity $\mathrm{tr}\, \rho_{f, \ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = a_p(f)$ for $p \nmid N\ell$ is the **Eichler-Shimura relation**. The construction generalises to higher weights $k \geq 3$ via Deligne 1971's Kuga-Sato varieties (étale cohomology of fibre powers of the universal elliptic curve over $X_0(N)$), and to weight $k = 1$ via Deligne-Serre 1974's congruence argument.

- **Modularity theorem and BSD [21.06.01].** Sibling-in-flight. The modularity theorem of Wiles 1995, Taylor-Wiles 1995, and Breuil-Conrad-Diamond-Taylor 2001 *J. AMS* 14 asserts that every elliptic curve $E$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ is modular: there exists a normalised cuspidal Hecke eigenform $f_E$ of weight $2$ and level $N(E)$ (the conductor of $E$) with $\rho_{E, \ell} \cong \rho_{f_E, \ell}$ for every prime $\ell$. Equivalently, $L(E, s) = L(f_E, s)$. The proof is the $R = T$ theorem of Wiles via the deformation theory of mod-$\ell$ Galois representations; the modular Galois representation $\rho_{f, \ell}$ defined in this unit is the key technical input.

- **Iwasawa theory [21.07.01].** Sibling-in-flight. Iwasawa theory studies the variation of an $\ell$-adic Galois representation along the cyclotomic $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-extension of $K$ — the unique $\mathbb{Z}_\ell$-extension contained in $K(\mu_{\ell^\infty})$. The deformation-theoretic perspective on Iwasawa modules is part of the broader framework of $\ell$-adic Galois deformations introduced by Mazur 1989. The cyclotomic character $\chi_{\mathrm{cyc}}$ defined in this unit is the prototype Iwasawa object.

- **Étale cohomology and Weil conjectures [04-algebraic-geometry].** Lateral connection. The étale cohomology $H^i_{\text{ét}}(X_{\bar K}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ of a smooth proper variety $X/K$ is the canonical source of $\ell$-adic Galois representations; the Weil conjectures (Deligne 1974 Weil I, Deligne 1980 Weil II) determine the Frobenius eigenvalues. The $\ell$-adic perspective on étale cohomology developed in SGA 4, SGA 4.5, SGA 5 is the geometric foundation of this unit. See `[04-algebraic-geometry]` for the étale-cohomology pointer (forthcoming).

- **Representation theory [07-rep-theory].** Lateral connection. The category of continuous $\ell$-adic representations of $G_K$ is an instance of the general theory of representations of topological groups; the Tannakian-categorical formalism (Deligne 1990 *Catégories tannakiennes*) recovers $G_K$ from its category of representations under suitable conditions. The structure of mod-$\ell$ representations $\bar\rho : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\bar{\mathbb{F}}_\ell)$ uses modular representation theory of finite groups via the residual finite quotients of $G_K$. See `[07-rep-theory]` for the representation-theoretic background.

- **Dedekind / Hecke / Artin $L$-functions [21.03.03].** Sibling unit on the complex-coefficient analogue. Artin $L$-functions $L(s, \rho)$ attached to a continuous complex Galois representation $\rho : G_K \to \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$ are the $\ell = \infty$ shadow of the present unit's $\ell$-adic framework: an Artin $L$-function corresponds to a Galois representation with finite image (after the $\ell$-adic completion at infinity, recovered as the Frobenius eigenvalue pattern); $\ell$-adic Galois representations of motivic origin extend the Artin formalism to representations with infinite image arising from étale cohomology. The Artin-conjecture holomorphy statement is the $\ell$-adic-to-complex transfer of the geometric origin of $\rho_\ell$.

- **$p$-adic $L$-functions and Iwasawa Main Conjecture [21.07.02].** Sibling unit on the $p$-adic analytic shadow of $\ell$-adic Galois representations at $\ell = p$. The Mazur-Wiles 1984 proof of the Main Conjecture and the Skinner-Urban 2014 extension to elliptic curves run through the deformation theory of $p$-adic Galois representations developed in the present unit, with the universal deformation ring $R_{\bar\rho}$ paired against the Hecke / Eisenstein-ideal side. Greenberg's 1989 framework for Selmer groups of $p$-adic Galois representations generalises the Iwasawa Main Conjecture to arbitrary motivic representations, with the present unit's $\rho_\ell$ as the canonical input.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The systematic study of $\ell$-adic Galois representations begins with Tate's 1966 paper [ref: TODO_REF Tate 1966] in *Inventiones Mathematicae* 2, where the Tate module $T_\ell A$ of an abelian variety $A$ over a finite field was introduced together with the conjectures asserting that morphisms of abelian varieties are recovered from their $G$-equivariant Tate-module maps. Tate proved his conjectures over finite fields in the same paper; the generalisation to number fields was Faltings 1983 [ref: TODO_REF Faltings 1983], whose paper simultaneously settled the Mordell conjecture, the Shafarevich conjecture, and the Tate conjecture for abelian varieties over number fields. Serre 1968 [ref: TODO_REF Serre 1968] in *Abelian ℓ-adic representations and elliptic curves* (W. A. Benjamin) consolidated the formalism of $\ell$-adic Galois representations into a coherent theory, introducing the notions of continuity, ramification, and semisimplicity in the form used here, and using the Tate module of an elliptic curve as the running example. Serre 1972 [ref: TODO_REF Serre 1972] in *Inventiones Mathematicae* 15 then proved the open-image theorem for the $\ell$-adic Galois representations attached to non-CM elliptic curves over $\mathbb{Q}$, identifying the image of $\rho_{E, \ell}$ as essentially all of $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Z}_\ell)$ for almost every $\ell$.

The geometric foundation comes from Grothendieck and his collaborators in the SGA seminars: SGA 4 (1972-73), SGA 4.5 (1977), and SGA 5 (1977) [ref: TODO_REF Grothendieck SGA] developed étale cohomology and $\ell$-adic sheaf theory in the generality required to define $H^i_{\text{ét}}(X_{\bar K}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ for arbitrary smooth proper varieties $X/K$. The Weil conjectures on the Frobenius eigenvalues were proved by Deligne 1974 (Weil I) and refined into the framework of weights and mixed Hodge structures in Deligne 1980 [ref: TODO_REF Deligne 1980] (Weil II). The construction of $\rho_{f, \ell}$ attached to a weight-$k$ cuspidal Hecke eigenform with $k \geq 2$ is due to Deligne 1971 (*Séminaire Bourbaki* 355), via étale cohomology of Kuga-Sato varieties; the weight-$1$ case was completed by Deligne-Serre 1974 [ref: TODO_REF Deligne-Serre 1974] in *Annales scientifiques de l'École Normale Supérieure* 7.

The local theory at $\ell$ (when the coefficient field is $\mathbb{Q}_\ell$ and the residue characteristic of the place is also $\ell$) was developed by Fontaine in a long series of papers culminating in Fontaine 1982 [ref: TODO_REF Fontaine 1982] (Hodge-Tate decomposition for representations from $p$-divisible groups) and Fontaine 1994 [ref: TODO_REF Fontaine 1994] (the period rings $B_{\mathrm{cris}}, B_{\mathrm{st}}, B_{\mathrm{dR}}, B_{\mathrm{HT}}$ and the categories of crystalline, semistable, de Rham, Hodge-Tate $p$-adic representations). The $p$-adic comparison theorems of Fontaine-Messing 1987, Faltings 1989, and Tsuji 1999 made precise the identification of $\ell = p$-adic étale cohomology with crystalline / de Rham cohomology of the special / generic fibre, the $p$-adic counterpart of the classical de Rham-Hodge identification in characteristic zero.

The deformation-theoretic perspective is due to Mazur 1989 [ref: TODO_REF Mazur 1989], who introduced universal deformation rings for residual representations. Wiles 1995 [ref: TODO_REF Wiles 1995] used this framework, together with the Taylor-Wiles patching method, to prove modularity of semistable elliptic curves over $\mathbb{Q}$ and hence Fermat's Last Theorem. Khare-Wintenberger 2009 [ref: TODO_REF Khare-Wintenberger 2009] in *Inventiones Mathematicae* 178 proved Serre's modularity conjecture, completing the modularity correspondence at the level of mod-$\ell$ Galois representations. These developments place $\ell$-adic Galois representations at the centre of modern arithmetic geometry, and the Langlands programme — to which the modular-Galois-representation correspondence is the prototype — is the conjectural extension of the modularity statement to all reductive groups over a global field.
