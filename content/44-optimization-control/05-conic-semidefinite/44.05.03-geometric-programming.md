## Intuition [Beginner]

Many engineering quantities multiply rather than add. The power a chip burns scales with the product of its width, its voltage squared, and its switching rate; the volume of a box is length times width times height; signal strength falls off as a product of gains and distances raised to powers. When the things you can tune enter as products of powers, ordinary linear thinking does not fit. Geometric programming is the optimization method built exactly for this multiply-and-power world.

The two building blocks have plain meanings. A *monomial* here is a single product term: a positive number times each variable raised to some power, like $3\,x^{2}\,y^{-1}$. A *posynomial* is a sum of such terms, all with positive coefficients, like $3\,x^{2}\,y^{-1} + 5\,x\,y$. Posynomials are the natural language for costs, areas, powers, and delays that pile up from several multiplicative effects. A geometric program asks you to make one posynomial as small as possible while keeping other posynomials below their budgets.

Why is this special? Because of one magic trick. If you stop working with the variables themselves and instead work with their logarithms, every one of these multiply-and-power expressions straightens out. Products turn into sums, powers turn into multipliers, and the whole tangled problem becomes a bowl-shaped one ŌĆö the kind with a single global lowest point and no hidden traps. So a problem that looked hopelessly nonlinear is secretly one of the friendly problems we can always solve.

That is the appeal of geometric programming: a large, practical family of design problems that look forbidding in their raw form but become reliably solvable the moment you take logarithms.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We design a rectangular open-top storage box of cheapest material, a classic geometric program small enough to do by hand. Let the base be $x$ by $y$ and the height be $z$, all positive lengths in meters. The box must hold at least one cubic meter: $x\,y\,z \geq 1$. The material cost counts the base and the four sides: cost $= x\,y + 2\,x\,z + 2\,y\,z$ (the base area plus the two pairs of opposite walls). We want the cost as small as possible.

**Step 1. Read off the pieces.** The cost $x y + 2 x z + 2 y z$ is a sum of three positive product terms, so it is a posynomial. The volume rule $x y z \geq 1$ rearranges to $x^{-1} y^{-1} z^{-1} \leq 1$, also a posynomial budget. This is a geometric program.

**Step 2. Use symmetry to guess.** The two side-wall terms treat $x$ and $y$ the same way, so try a square base $x = y$. The volume rule at the boundary gives $x^{2} z = 1$, so $z = 1 / x^{2}$.

**Step 3. Reduce to one variable.** Substituting, the cost becomes $x^{2} + 2 x \cdot (1/x^{2}) + 2 x \cdot (1/x^{2}) = x^{2} + 4/x$.

**Step 4. Find the lowest point.** As $x$ grows the $x^{2}$ term climbs; as $x$ shrinks the $4/x$ term blows up, so a single best $x$ sits in between. Testing values: at $x = 1$ the cost is $1 + 4 = 5$; at $x = 1.25$ it is $1.5625 + 3.2 = 4.7625$; at $x = 1.26$ it is about $1.588 + 3.175 = 4.763$. The minimum is near $x = 1.26$ with cost about $4.76$.

**What this tells us.** The cheapest box has a square base of side about $1.26$ m and height $z = 1/x^{2} \approx 0.63$ m. A multiply-and-power design problem that looked nonlinear had one clean best answer, and posynomials were the exact language to write it down.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, the optimization variable is $x = (x_1, \dots, x_n)$ with each $x_i > 0$; the domain is the positive orthant $\mathbb{R}^n_{++}$.

**Definition (monomial).** A **monomial** is a function $f : \mathbb{R}^n_{++} \to \mathbb{R}_{++}$ of the form
$$
f(x) = c\, x_1^{a_1} x_2^{a_2} \cdots x_n^{a_n}, \qquad c > 0,\ a_i \in \mathbb{R}.
$$
The coefficient $c$ is positive but the exponents $a_i$ may be any real numbers (negative, fractional). This is the geometric-programming sense of "monomial" and differs from the algebraic sense (no nonnegative-integer-exponent restriction, single term, positive coefficient).

**Definition (posynomial).** A **posynomial** is a finite sum of monomials,
$$
p(x) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} c_k\, x_1^{a_{1k}} x_2^{a_{2k}} \cdots x_n^{a_{nk}}, \qquad c_k > 0,\ a_{ik} \in \mathbb{R}.
$$
Posynomials are closed under addition, multiplication, positive scaling, and raising to a nonnegative power; a monomial is the special case $K = 1$, and the ratio of a posynomial by a monomial is again a posynomial.

**Definition (geometric program in standard form).** A **geometric program (GP)** is the optimization problem
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{minimize} \quad & p_0(x) \\
\text{subject to} \quad & p_i(x) \leq 1, \quad i = 1, \dots, m, \\
& g_j(x) = 1, \quad j = 1, \dots, r,
\end{aligned}
$$
where $p_0, p_1, \dots, p_m$ are posynomials, $g_1, \dots, g_r$ are monomials, and the variable ranges over $x \in \mathbb{R}^n_{++}$ [ref: TODO_REF Boyd & Vandenberghe ┬¦4.5]. Inequality constraints use posynomials bounded above by $1$, equality constraints use monomials equal to $1$. A constraint $p(x) \leq b$ with $b > 0$ is brought to standard form by dividing through, $p(x)/b \leq 1$; a constraint $m_1(x) \leq m_2(x)$ between monomials becomes $m_1(x)/m_2(x) \leq 1$.

**Definition (the log-log change of variables).** Substitute $y_i = \log x_i$, so $x_i = e^{y_i}$, and replace each posynomial by the logarithm of its value. A monomial $c\, \prod_i x_i^{a_i}$ becomes
$$
\log\!\Big(c \prod_i e^{a_i y_i}\Big) = a^\top y + \log c,
$$
an **affine** function of $y$. A posynomial $p(x) = \sum_{k} c_k \prod_i x_i^{a_{ik}}$ becomes
$$
\tilde p(y) = \log \sum_{k=1}^{K} e^{a_k^\top y + b_k}, \qquad a_k = (a_{1k}, \dots, a_{nk}),\ b_k = \log c_k,
$$
a **log-sum-exp** of affine functions, which is convex by the convexity of log-sum-exp ([44.01.01]) composed with affine maps.

**Definition (convex form of a GP).** Applying the substitution and taking logarithms throughout, the GP becomes
$$
\begin{aligned}
\text{minimize} \quad & \tilde p_0(y) = \log\textstyle\sum_{k} e^{a_{0k}^\top y + b_{0k}} \\
\text{subject to} \quad & \tilde p_i(y) = \log\textstyle\sum_{k} e^{a_{ik}^\top y + b_{ik}} \leq 0, \quad i = 1, \dots, m, \\
& \tilde g_j(y) = g_j^\top y + h_j = 0, \quad j = 1, \dots, r.
\end{aligned}
$$
This is a convex optimization problem: the objective and inequality functions are convex (log-sum-exp of affine), and the equality constraints are affine. Solving the original GP is equivalent to solving this convex program, after which $x_i = e^{y_i}$ recovers the design.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **A single negative coefficient breaks the posynomial structure.** $x^2 - 3xy + y^2$ is *not* a posynomial; the $-3xy$ term has a negative coefficient. Such expressions are **signomials**, and the log-log trick does not convexify them ŌĆö log-sum-exp requires every coefficient positive.
- **Equality of two posynomials is not a GP constraint.** A constraint $p(x) = 1$ with $p$ a genuine ($K \geq 2$) posynomial is generally nonconvex after the substitution, because $\log\sum_k e^{(\cdot)} = 0$ is not an affine equality. Only *monomial* equalities ($K = 1$) stay affine in $y$; posynomial equalities must be relaxed or restructured.
- **Maximizing a posynomial is not a GP.** GP minimizes a posynomial. Maximizing a posynomial, or minimizing $1/p$ for a posynomial $p$, makes the objective concave-after-substitution and is outside the class.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The organizing result is that the log-log substitution converts every geometric program into an equivalent convex program, so the global solvability of GP rests entirely on the convexity of log-sum-exp from [44.01.01].

**Theorem (GP convexification).** *Let a GP be given in standard form with posynomial objective and constraints $p_0, \dots, p_m$ and monomial equalities $g_1, \dots, g_r$, feasible over $x \in \mathbb{R}^n_{++}$. Under the bijection $y_i = \log x_i$ between $\mathbb{R}^n_{++}$ and $\mathbb{R}^n$, the transformed problem with objective $\tilde p_0(y) = \log\sum_k e^{a_{0k}^\top y + b_{0k}}$, inequality constraints $\tilde p_i(y) \leq 0$, and affine equality constraints $\tilde g_j(y) = 0$ is a convex optimization problem, and $x^\star$ solves the GP if and only if $y^\star = \log x^\star$ (componentwise) solves the convex problem, with equal optimal values up to the monotone map $\log p_0^\star = \tilde p_0(y^\star)$.*

**Proof.** The map $\Phi : \mathbb{R}^n_{++} \to \mathbb{R}^n$, $\Phi(x)_i = \log x_i$, is a bijection with inverse $\Phi^{-1}(y)_i = e^{y_i}$, both continuous and strictly monotone in each coordinate. Feasibility is preserved exactly: a monomial constraint $g_j(x) = 1$ holds iff $\log g_j(x) = 0$ iff $g_j^\top y + h_j = 0$ (an affine equality), and a posynomial constraint $p_i(x) \leq 1$ holds iff $\log p_i(x) \leq \log 1 = 0$ iff $\tilde p_i(y) \leq 0$, using that $\log$ is strictly increasing on $\mathbb{R}_{++}$ so the inequality direction is retained.

For convexity, fix any posynomial $p(x) = \sum_{k=1}^K c_k \prod_i x_i^{a_{ik}}$ with $c_k > 0$. After substitution each term is $c_k \prod_i e^{a_{ik} y_i} = e^{a_k^\top y + b_k}$ with $b_k = \log c_k$, so
$$
\tilde p(y) = \log p(e^{y_1}, \dots, e^{y_n}) = \log \sum_{k=1}^K e^{a_k^\top y + b_k}.
$$
The log-sum-exp function $\ell(z) = \log \sum_k e^{z_k}$ is convex on $\mathbb{R}^K$ ([44.01.01], where its Hessian $\tfrac{1}{Z}\operatorname{diag}(z) - \tfrac{1}{Z^2} z z^\top$ with $z_k = e^{z_k}$, $Z = \sum_k z_k$ was shown positive semidefinite by Cauchy-Schwarz). Each argument $z_k = a_k^\top y + b_k$ is affine in $y$, so $\tilde p = \ell \circ A$ for the affine map $A : y \mapsto (a_1^\top y + b_1, \dots, a_K^\top y + b_K)$. Composition of a convex function with an affine map is convex (the operation calculus of [44.01.01]), so $\tilde p$ is convex. Applying this to $p_0$ gives a convex objective and to each $p_i$ a convex inequality function with the sublevel constraint $\tilde p_i(y) \leq 0$ defining a convex feasible set; the $\tilde g_j(y) = 0$ are affine. The transformed problem therefore minimizes a convex function over a convex set, which is a convex program.

Equivalence of solutions follows because $\Phi$ is a feasibility-preserving bijection and $\log$ is strictly increasing: minimizing $p_0(x)$ over the GP-feasible set is the same as minimizing $\log p_0(x) = \tilde p_0(\Phi(x))$, so $x^\star$ is a minimizer iff $y^\star = \Phi(x^\star)$ minimizes $\tilde p_0$ over the transformed feasible set, with $\log p_0^\star = \tilde p_0(y^\star)$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem **builds toward** the interior-point solvers of [44.05.04] and **appears again in** every practical use of GP, from circuit sizing to power control. **This is exactly** the convexity of log-sum-exp from [44.01.01] doing all the work: the multiply-and-power structure of posynomials is the precise structure that the logarithm linearizes, and the only nonlinearity left, the sum inside the log, is the one function [44.01.01] already certified convex. **The foundational reason** GP is a tractable modeling class while general signomial programming is not is visible here ŌĆö the proof needs every coefficient $c_k$ positive so that $b_k = \log c_k$ is real and the expression is a genuine log-sum-exp; a single negative coefficient destroys this and the convexity with it. **Putting these together**, GP **generalises** the linear program in the log domain (a GP whose every posynomial is a single monomial becomes an LP in $y$, since each $\tilde p_i$ is then affine), and the convex form **is dual to** the original posynomial form through the bijection $x \leftrightarrow e^y$; the convexified GP is precisely the problem class that the self-concordant-barrier machinery of [44.05.04] solves to global optimality in polynomial time.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the product of two monomials is a monomial, and the ratio of two monomials is a monomial.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Multiply $c\,\prod_i x_i^{a_i}$ by $d\,\prod_i x_i^{b_i}$ and add exponents.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $f = c\prod_i x_i^{a_i}$ and $g = d\prod_i x_i^{b_i}$ with $c, d > 0$, the product $fg = (cd)\prod_i x_i^{a_i + b_i}$ has positive coefficient $cd$ and real exponents $a_i + b_i$, so it is a monomial. The ratio $f/g = (c/d)\prod_i x_i^{a_i - b_i}$ likewise has positive coefficient $c/d$ and real exponents $a_i - b_i$, so it is a monomial. This closure is what lets a constraint $m_1(x) \leq m_2(x)$ between monomials be rewritten as the standard-form monomial constraint $m_1(x)/m_2(x) \leq 1$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Bring the constraint $2x^2 z + 3 y^{-1} \leq 5$ into GP standard form.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Divide both sides by the right-hand bound to make the right side $1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Dividing by $5$ gives $\tfrac{2}{5}x^2 z + \tfrac{3}{5}y^{-1} \leq 1$. The left side is a sum of two positive-coefficient power-product terms, so it is a posynomial, and the constraint is now in the standard posynomial $\leq 1$ form. Any constraint $p(x) \leq b$ with $b > 0$ and $p$ a posynomial reduces this way, since dividing a posynomial by the positive constant $b$ yields a posynomial.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Write the convex form of the constraint $x\,y^{-1} + 2 z \leq 1$ under the substitution $u = \log x$, $v = \log y$, $w = \log z$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each monomial term becomes $e^{(\text{affine in } u,v,w)}$; take the log of the sum.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The first term $x y^{-1} = e^{u - v}$ and the second $2z = e^{w + \log 2}$. The posynomial constraint $\leq 1$ becomes $\log(e^{u - v} + e^{w + \log 2}) \leq \log 1 = 0$, i.e.
$$
\log\!\big(e^{u - v} + e^{w + \log 2}\big) \leq 0,
$$
a log-sum-exp of affine functions bounded above by zero. By the convexity of log-sum-exp ([44.01.01]) the left side is convex, so the constraint set is convex.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that a GP in which every posynomial (objective and constraints) is in fact a single monomial reduces, after the log substitution, to a linear program.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A monomial becomes an affine function of $y$; what kind of problem has an affine objective and affine constraints?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A monomial $c\prod_i x_i^{a_i}$ becomes $a^\top y + \log c$, affine in $y$. If the objective and every constraint function is a single monomial, then $\tilde p_0(y)$ is affine, each inequality $\tilde p_i(y) \leq 0$ is an affine inequality, and each equality $\tilde g_j(y) = 0$ is an affine equality. Minimizing an affine objective over affine inequalities and equalities is a linear program ([44.05.01]). So monomial GPs are exactly LPs in the log domain, which is why GP is sometimes described as "LP with curved (posynomial) terms allowed."

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

A wireless link has signal-to-interference ratio $\text{SIR} = \dfrac{G_{11} P_1}{\sigma + G_{12} P_2}$, where powers $P_1, P_2 > 0$ are the variables and the $G$'s and $\sigma$ are positive constants. Show that requiring $\text{SIR} \geq \gamma$ for a target $\gamma > 0$ is a posynomial constraint in GP standard form.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Cross-multiply and divide so the constraint reads (posynomial) $\leq 1$. Note $1/P_1$ is a monomial.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\text{SIR} \geq \gamma$ means $G_{11} P_1 \geq \gamma(\sigma + G_{12} P_2)$, i.e. $\gamma\sigma + \gamma G_{12} P_2 \leq G_{11} P_1$. Dividing by $G_{11} P_1$ gives
$$
\frac{\gamma\sigma}{G_{11}} P_1^{-1} + \frac{\gamma G_{12}}{G_{11}} P_2 P_1^{-1} \leq 1.
$$
The left side is a sum of two positive-coefficient power-product terms in $P_1, P_2$, hence a posynomial, and the constraint is in standard $\leq 1$ form. This is the basic reason power control in interference-limited networks is a geometric program [ref: TODO_REF Chiang 2005 ┬¦2].

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that a *generalized posynomial* ŌĆö built from variables and posynomials by addition, multiplication, positive powers, and the $\max$ of posynomials ŌĆö becomes a convex function of $y = \log x$. (You may use that a $\max$ of convex functions is convex.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Induct on the construction. Each operation must be shown to preserve convexity after the substitution.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A plain posynomial becomes a convex log-sum-exp by the Key theorem. Proceed by structural induction on the generalized-posynomial expression tree, tracking the transformed function $\tilde f(y) = \log f(e^y)$. Sum and product: if $f, g$ become convex $\tilde f, \tilde g$, then $fg$ gives $\widetilde{fg} = \tilde f + \tilde g$ (convex), and $f + g$ gives $\log(e^{\tilde f} + e^{\tilde g})$, a log-sum-exp of the convex $\tilde f, \tilde g$, convex because log-sum-exp is convex and nondecreasing in each argument composed with convex arguments ([44.01.01] composition rule). Positive power: $f^r$ for $r > 0$ gives $r\tilde f$, convex. Maximum: $\max(f, g)$ gives $\max(\tilde f, \tilde g)$ after the increasing $\log$, and a pointwise maximum of convex functions is convex. Every construction step preserves convexity, so the transformed generalized posynomial is convex; this is what makes *generalized geometric programming* still convex and solvable.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Condensation. Given a posynomial $p(x) = \sum_{k=1}^K u_k(x)$ (each $u_k$ a monomial) and any weights $\theta_k > 0$ with $\sum_k \theta_k = 1$, prove the monomial lower bound
$$
p(x) \;\geq\; \prod_{k=1}^K \left(\frac{u_k(x)}{\theta_k}\right)^{\theta_k},
$$
and explain why the right side is a monomial. This is the arithmetic-geometric-mean step used to approximate a posynomial by a monomial in signomial programming.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the weighted AM-GM inequality $\sum_k \theta_k t_k \geq \prod_k t_k^{\theta_k}$ to $t_k = u_k(x)/\theta_k$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the weighted arithmetic-geometric-mean inequality, for positive $t_k$ and weights $\theta_k > 0$ summing to $1$, $\sum_k \theta_k t_k \geq \prod_k t_k^{\theta_k}$. Set $t_k = u_k(x)/\theta_k$; then $\sum_k \theta_k t_k = \sum_k u_k(x) = p(x)$, giving $p(x) \geq \prod_k (u_k(x)/\theta_k)^{\theta_k}$. The right side is a product of monomials each raised to a positive power $\theta_k$, hence a monomial (closure under products and positive powers, Exercise 1). Choosing $\theta_k = u_k(x_0)/p(x_0)$ at a point $x_0$ makes the bound tight at $x_0$, so a posynomial can be locally replaced by a tight monomial under-estimate; iterating this *condensation* turns a signomial program into a sequence of GPs, the standard heuristic for the nonconvex signomial case [ref: TODO_REF Boyd, Kim, Vandenberghe & Hassibi 2007 ┬¦9].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Explain why a constraint of the form $p(x)/q(x) \leq 1$, with $p$ a posynomial and $q$ a posynomial (not a monomial), is generally *not* a GP constraint, but becomes one if $q$ is a monomial. What goes wrong, and how does this motivate signomial programming?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A posynomial divided by a monomial is a posynomial; a posynomial divided by a posynomial is not.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $q$ is a monomial, $p/q$ is a sum of (monomial)$/$(monomial) terms, each a monomial (Exercise 1), so $p/q$ is a posynomial and $p/q \leq 1$ is a standard GP constraint. If $q$ is a genuine posynomial ($\geq 2$ terms), $p/q$ is a ratio of posynomials, which is not in general a posynomial: clearing denominators gives $p(x) \leq q(x)$, i.e. $p(x) - q(x) \leq 0$, and $p - q$ has terms of both signs ŌĆö a *signomial*, not a posynomial. The log substitution then yields $\log\sum e^{(\cdot)} - \log\sum e^{(\cdot)}$, a difference of convex functions, which is generally nonconvex. This is exactly the boundary of the GP class: ratio-of-posynomial constraints push a model into signomial programming, handled by the condensation heuristic of Exercise 7 rather than by a single convex solve.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The GP dual and the degree of difficulty

Geometric programming was born with a dual, predating the convex-analytic viewpoint. For the unconstrained primal $\min_x \sum_{k=1}^K c_k \prod_i x_i^{a_{ik}}$, the **GP dual** is the maximization, over dual weights $\delta = (\delta_1, \dots, \delta_K)$, of the product form
$$
\max_{\delta}\ \prod_{k=1}^{K} \left(\frac{c_k}{\delta_k}\right)^{\delta_k} \quad \text{s.t.}\quad \delta_k \geq 0,\ \sum_{k} \delta_k = 1\ (\text{normality}),\ \sum_{k} \delta_k a_{ik} = 0\ \forall i\ (\text{orthogonality}),
$$
derived directly from the arithmetic-geometric-mean inequality [ref: TODO_REF Duffin, Peterson & Zener 1967 Ch. 3]. Weak duality is the AM-GM bound of Exercise 7; strong duality holds under the primal-feasibility (Slater) hypothesis. The constrained case attaches one block of weights per posynomial and an extra normalization per constraint. The **degree of difficulty** is $K - (n + 1)$, the number of dual variables minus the number of normalization-plus-orthogonality equations; when it is zero the orthogonality system pins $\delta$ uniquely and the GP is solved in closed form by linear algebra, with the optimal primal recovered from the monomial-term ratios $\delta_k = u_k(x^\star)/p_0(x^\star)$. This dual is the modern conic dual of [44.05.01] read through the exponential cone, but its closed-form low-degree-of-difficulty cases remain the reason GP duality is taught on its own terms.

### Generalized geometric programming and the modeling reach of the class

The convexity-after-logarithm property survives a substantial enlargement of the syntax. **Generalized posynomials** are formed from ordinary posynomials by addition, multiplication, positive powers, maximum, and composition with posynomials of posynomials; Exercise 6 shows each becomes convex in $y$. **Generalized geometric programming** minimizes a generalized posynomial subject to generalized-posynomial $\leq 1$ constraints, and remains a convex problem after the substitution, hence globally solvable [ref: TODO_REF Boyd, Kim, Vandenberghe & Hassibi 2007 ┬¦8]. The practical payoff is that maxima of posynomials (worst-case delays, worst-case power), fractional powers (norms, RMS quantities), and nested posynomial-of-posynomial expressions all stay inside the tractable class. Modeling tools exploit this by certifying a model as a generalized GP through a parse-tree check identical in spirit to the disciplined-convex-programming ruleset of [44.01.01], so a designer writes the multiply-and-power model directly and the convexification is automatic.

### Signomial programming, condensation, and the loss of convexity

A **signomial** is a sum of monomial terms with coefficients of *either* sign, and a **signomial program** allows signomial objective and constraints. The class is genuinely larger and genuinely harder: after the log substitution a signomial constraint becomes a *difference* of log-sum-exp functions, a difference-of-convex constraint, and the problem is in general nonconvex and NP-hard. The dominant heuristic is **condensation** (Exercise 7): at the current iterate, replace each posynomial appearing in a denominator or on the larger side of a signomial inequality by its tight monomial AM-GM under-estimate, producing a GP whose solution becomes the next iterate. This is a convex-concave / inner-approximation scheme; each GP solve is a global step on a tightened convex surrogate, and the iteration converges to a point satisfying the KKT conditions of the signomial program though not necessarily its global optimum [ref: TODO_REF Boyd, Kim, Vandenberghe & Hassibi 2007 ┬¦9]. The contrast with the GP case is the central structural fact of the subject: positive coefficients buy global solvability, mixed signs cost it.

### Applications: where multiply-and-power structure is native

The reach of GP is widest precisely where physical and economic quantities combine multiplicatively. In **analog and digital circuit design**, transistor delays, areas, and power are monomials in device widths and lengths, so gate and op-amp sizing is a GP solved to global optimality over hundreds of devices [ref: TODO_REF Hershenson, Boyd & Lee 2001]. In **wireless power control**, signal-to-interference constraints are posynomial (Exercise 5) and network utility maximization is a GP or, with rate-dependent interference, a signomial program attacked by condensation [ref: TODO_REF Chiang 2005]. Other native domains include chemical-equilibrium and process design (the original Zener applications), structural and mechanical sizing under stress and deflection limits, information-theoretic and queueing performance bounds, and statistical maximum-likelihood for log-concave models. The common thread is that the design law arrives as products of powers, the form for which GP is not an approximation but the exact convex reformulation.

**Synthesis.** Geometric programming **is exactly** the convex optimization hiding behind multiply-and-power design laws: the log-log substitution maps posynomials to log-sum-exp of affine functions, and **the central insight** is that one convexity fact from [44.01.01] ŌĆö that log-sum-exp is convex ŌĆö globalizes the entire class. **The foundational reason** GP is a distinct and durable modeling category, rather than a footnote to general convex programming, is its closure algebra: monomials and posynomials are closed under the operations designers actually use, so a parse-tree check certifies convexity without a Hessian, and the convexified program feeds the self-concordant-barrier interior-point machinery of [44.05.04] that solves it at scale. **Putting these together**, the GP dual **generalises** the AM-GM inequality into a duality theory whose zero-degree-of-difficulty cases close in algebra, and it **is dual to** the conic exponential-cone formulation of [44.05.01], the two viewpoints meeting at the same optimum. **This is exactly** where the boundary of tractability sits: positive coefficients keep the transformed problem convex, while a single negative coefficient drops the model into signomial programming, where condensation replaces global solvability with a sequence of GP surrogates; **the bridge is** the logarithm, the one map through which posynomial algebra, log-sum-exp convexity, the AM-GM dual, and the interior-point solvers of [44.05.04] become a single story, and it **appears again in** circuit sizing, power control, and every engineering design whose governing law is a product of powers.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (posynomials form a convex cone under the log substitution).** *For a fixed exponent structure, the set of functions $\{\tilde p(y) = \log\sum_k e^{a_k^\top y + b_k}\}$ obtained from posynomials is closed under the log-domain image of posynomial addition and positive scaling, and each such $\tilde p$ is convex.*

*Proof.* Convexity of each $\tilde p$ is the Key theorem: $\tilde p = \ell \circ A$ with $\ell$ the convex log-sum-exp and $A$ affine. For the algebra, if $p = \sum_k u_k$ and $q = \sum_j w_j$ are posynomials then $p + q$ is the posynomial with the concatenated term list, and its log-domain image is $\log(\sum_k e^{a_k^\top y + b_k} + \sum_j e^{c_j^\top y + d_j})$, a log-sum-exp over the union of terms; positive scaling $\alpha p$ ($\alpha > 0$) shifts each $b_k$ by $\log\alpha$. Both stay log-sum-exp of affine functions, hence convex. Multiplication $pq = \sum_{k,j} u_k w_j$ is again a posynomial (its log-domain image is the log-sum-exp over the product terms with exponents $a_k + c_j$), so the posynomial set is closed under addition, positive scaling, and multiplication, and the log substitution carries each into the convex cone of log-sum-exp functions. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (GP weak duality via AM-GM).** *For the unconstrained primal $p_0(x) = \sum_{k=1}^K c_k \prod_i x_i^{a_{ik}}$ and any dual-feasible $\delta$ (i.e. $\delta_k \geq 0$, $\sum_k \delta_k = 1$, $\sum_k \delta_k a_{ik} = 0$ for all $i$), the dual objective lower-bounds the primal optimum: $\prod_k (c_k/\delta_k)^{\delta_k} \leq p_0(x)$ for every $x \in \mathbb{R}^n_{++}$.*

*Proof.* Apply the weighted AM-GM inequality $\sum_k \delta_k t_k \geq \prod_k t_k^{\delta_k}$ (weights $\delta_k \geq 0$ summing to $1$) to $t_k = u_k(x)/\delta_k$, where $u_k(x) = c_k\prod_i x_i^{a_{ik}}$ is the $k$-th monomial term. The left side is $\sum_k \delta_k \cdot u_k(x)/\delta_k = \sum_k u_k(x) = p_0(x)$. The right side is
$$
\prod_k \left(\frac{u_k(x)}{\delta_k}\right)^{\delta_k} = \prod_k \left(\frac{c_k}{\delta_k}\right)^{\delta_k} \prod_k \Big(\textstyle\prod_i x_i^{a_{ik}}\Big)^{\delta_k} = \prod_k \left(\frac{c_k}{\delta_k}\right)^{\delta_k} \prod_i x_i^{\sum_k \delta_k a_{ik}}.
$$
The orthogonality conditions $\sum_k \delta_k a_{ik} = 0$ make every $x_i$ exponent vanish, so $\prod_i x_i^{0} = 1$ and the right side equals $\prod_k (c_k/\delta_k)^{\delta_k}$, independent of $x$. Therefore $\prod_k (c_k/\delta_k)^{\delta_k} \leq p_0(x)$ for all feasible $x$, which is GP weak duality. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (zero degree of difficulty gives a closed-form solution).** *If the unconstrained GP has $K = n + 1$ terms and the orthogonality-plus-normality system $\sum_k \delta_k a_{ik} = 0\ (i=1,\dots,n),\ \sum_k \delta_k = 1$ has a unique nonnegative solution $\delta^\star$, then the primal optimum equals $\prod_k (c_k/\delta_k^\star)^{\delta_k^\star}$ and is attained.*

*Proof.* The system is $n + 1$ linear equations in the $K = n+1$ unknowns $\delta_k$; uniqueness of the nonnegative solution $\delta^\star$ makes the dual feasible set a single point, so the dual maximization is the evaluation $\prod_k (c_k/\delta_k^\star)^{\delta_k^\star}$. By Proposition 2 this value lower-bounds the primal for every $x$. AM-GM holds with equality iff all the $t_k = u_k(x)/\delta_k^\star$ are equal, i.e. $u_k(x)/\delta_k^\star = u_j(x)/\delta_j^\star$ for all $k, j$. These ratio conditions are monomial equalities in $x$; taking logarithms gives a linear system in $y = \log x$ that is solvable because the exponent matrix has full row rank (its kernel is the one-dimensional normality direction already fixed). The resulting $x^\star$ attains $p_0(x^\star) = \prod_k (c_k/\delta_k^\star)^{\delta_k^\star}$, so the bound is met and is the optimum. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (condensation produces a monomial under-estimate tight at the chosen point).** *Let $p(x) = \sum_{k=1}^K u_k(x)$ be a posynomial and $x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n_{++}$. With weights $\theta_k = u_k(x_0)/p(x_0)$, the monomial $\hat p(x) = \prod_k (u_k(x)/\theta_k)^{\theta_k}$ satisfies $\hat p(x) \leq p(x)$ for all $x$, with equality at $x = x_0$, and $\nabla \hat p(x_0) = \nabla p(x_0)$.*

*Proof.* The inequality $\hat p \leq p$ is Proposition 2 / Exercise 7 with this weight choice. Equality at $x_0$: the AM-GM equality condition requires all $u_k(x_0)/\theta_k$ equal, and $u_k(x_0)/\theta_k = u_k(x_0) \cdot p(x_0)/u_k(x_0) = p(x_0)$ for every $k$, a common value, so the bound is tight and $\hat p(x_0) = \prod_k p(x_0)^{\theta_k} = p(x_0)^{\sum_k \theta_k} = p(x_0)$. For the gradient match, work in the log domain where $\widehat{\log p}(y) = \sum_k \theta_k \log u_k = \sum_k \theta_k (a_k^\top y + b_k)$ is the affine first-order term of $\tilde p(y) = \log\sum_k e^{a_k^\top y + b_k}$ at $y_0 = \log x_0$: indeed $\partial_i \tilde p(y_0) = \sum_k \tfrac{e^{a_k^\top y_0 + b_k}}{\sum_j e^{a_j^\top y_0 + b_j}} a_{ik} = \sum_k \theta_k a_{ik}$, matching the gradient of the condensed affine form. Equal value and equal log-domain gradient at $x_0$ give $\nabla \hat p(x_0) = \nabla p(x_0)$, so condensation is a tight, first-order-accurate monomial under-estimate ŌĆö the property that makes the condensation iteration a convergent inner-approximation method. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The convex-modeling unit [44.01.01] is the direct parent: the entire tractability of geometric programming is the convexity of the log-sum-exp function proved there, applied after the bijection $x_i = e^{y_i}$. Posynomial addition becomes log-sum-exp, monomials become affine maps, and the convexity-preserving operation calculus of [44.01.01] is what certifies the convex form of a GP and of every generalized GP without computing a Hessian.

- Conic optimization [44.05.01] situates GP in the broader convex-programming landscape: the convexified GP is representable over the exponential cone, so GP joins LP, SOCP, and SDP as a named convex modeling class, and the GP dual is the conic dual of [44.05.01] read through that cone. A monomial GP collapses to an LP in the log domain, exhibiting the LP $\subset$ GP-in-log-form inclusion alongside the conic ladder of that unit.

- Interior-point methods [44.05.04] are how GP is actually solved: once convexified, a GP is a smooth convex problem with log-sum-exp objective and constraints, and the self-concordant-barrier primal-dual path-following machinery of [44.05.04] returns the global optimum in polynomial time, which is the reason GP scales to circuit-sizing problems with thousands of devices.

- The signomial extension links GP to the difference-of-convex and sequential-convex-programming circle: a signomial program is a difference-of-log-sum-exp problem solved by condensation, an inner-approximation scheme that produces a sequence of GPs and converges to a KKT point, connecting GP to the constraint-qualification and KKT theory consumed throughout the optimization-control section [44.05.01].

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Geometric programming originated not in convex analysis but in engineering economics. Clarence Zener, studying the cost minimization of equipment whose terms were products of powers, observed in the early 1960s that the arithmetic-geometric-mean inequality gave a clean lower bound, and with Richard Duffin and Elmor Peterson developed this into a full duality theory in the 1967 monograph *Geometric Programming: Theory and Application* [ref: TODO_REF Duffin, Peterson & Zener 1967]. Their formulation was dual-first: the posynomial primal was attacked through the AM-GM dual, with the *degree of difficulty* measuring how far the orthogonality system fell short of determining the dual weights, and zero-degree-of-difficulty problems solved in closed form. The name "geometric programming" honors the geometric mean at the center of the inequality.

The recognition that the log-log substitution turns a GP into a convex program reframed the subject within the convex-optimization theory codified by Boyd and Vandenberghe [ref: TODO_REF Boyd & Vandenberghe ┬¦4.5], and the 2007 tutorial of Boyd, Kim, Vandenberghe, and Hassibi [ref: TODO_REF Boyd, Kim, Vandenberghe & Hassibi 2007] consolidated the modern view: GP as a convex modeling class solved by interior-point methods, with generalized GP and the signomial/condensation heuristics as principled extensions. The applications that revived the field were technological ŌĆö analog circuit sizing by Hershenson, Boyd, and Lee [ref: TODO_REF Hershenson, Boyd & Lee 2001], and wireless power control and network utility maximization synthesized by Chiang [ref: TODO_REF Chiang 2005] ŌĆö each exploiting that delays, powers, and rates are native posynomials, so global optimization replaced the local heuristics that had dominated those design disciplines.
