## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine you are trying to find the lowest point of a huge landscape, but the landscape is the average of millions of smaller bumpy surfaces — one per training example. Measuring the true downhill direction means touching all millions of bumps at once, which is far too slow. So you cheat. You pick one bump at random, feel its slope, and step against that. On its own this single slope is a poor guess at the true downhill direction. But averaged over many random picks, the guesses point the right way, and you drift toward the bottom. This is stochastic gradient descent, and it is how almost every large model is trained.

The price of cheating is noise. Each random slope wobbles around the true direction, so your path is jittery, like walking downhill in fog while someone keeps nudging you sideways. Two things tame the wobble. Averaging several random bumps before stepping — a mini-batch — gives a steadier guess. And shrinking your stride over time lets the early big strides cover ground while the later small strides stop you from bouncing around the bottom forever. Clever variants even remember old slopes to cancel the noise, restoring the fast steady progress of the slow exact method while keeping the cheap steps.

The second idea changes not *what* you measure but *how you measure distance*. Ordinary steps treat all directions the same, as if the world were flat. But some problems live on a special shape — a set of probabilities that must be positive and sum to one. There, "moving the same amount in every direction" is the wrong notion of a step. Mirror descent picks a distance ruler matched to the shape: you carry your point into a mirror world where stepping is easy, step there, and carry the result back. The famous case uses a ruler built from entropy, and its update multiplies each coordinate by a factor and renormalises — the multiplicative-weights rule behind many learning algorithms.

Why bother with a different ruler? Because the right ruler makes the cost of a problem grow far more slowly with the number of variables. With the flat ruler, working over $n$ probabilities costs like the square root of $n$. With the entropy ruler, it costs like the square root of the logarithm of $n$ — almost flat. Choosing the geometry, not just the step, is the whole point.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We average out noise by hand. Suppose the true slope at our point is the number $g = 4$, but we can only see noisy readings: each reading equals $4$ plus a random nudge that is $+2$ half the time and $-2$ half the time. So a single reading is either $6$ or $2$, never exactly $4$.

**Step 1. One reading is a poor guess.** If we step against a single reading of $6$, we over-step; against a reading of $2$, we under-step. Either way we miss the true slope $4$.

**Step 2. Average two readings.** Take a mini-batch of two readings. The possible pairs are $(6,6)$, $(6,2)$, $(2,6)$, $(2,2)$, each with the same chance. Their averages are $6$, $4$, $4$, $2$. So two of the four times we land exactly on $4$, and the other two times we are off by only $2$ instead of by $2$ every time.

**Step 3. Average four readings.** With four readings the average clusters even tighter around $4$. The spread of a $k$-reading average shrinks like $1$ over the square root of $k$: four readings halve the wobble of one.

**Step 4. A multiplicative-weights step.** Now switch rulers. Say we have two choices with current weights $(0.5, 0.5)$ and observed costs $(1, 0)$ — the first choice did worse. The entropy update multiplies each weight by $e^{-\eta\,\text{cost}}$ with stride $\eta = 1$: the new unnormalised weights are $(0.5 \cdot e^{-1}, 0.5 \cdot e^{0}) = (0.184, 0.5)$. Renormalising to sum to one gives about $(0.269, 0.731)$. The worse choice lost weight, the better choice gained, and the two still sum to one.

**What this tells us.** Noisy slopes are useless one at a time but reliable on average, and averaging more readings tightens the guess at a square-root rate. Switching to the entropy ruler turns a step into a multiply-and-renormalise rule that automatically keeps weights positive and summing to one.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be convex (notation from [44.01.01]) with optimal value $f^\star$ and a minimiser $x^\star$ on a closed convex feasible set $C$. The subgradient inequality $g \in \partial f(x) \Rightarrow f(y) \ge f(x) + \langle g, y - x\rangle$ and the subgradient method are as in [44.06.01].

**Definition (stochastic first-order oracle).** A **stochastic subgradient oracle** at $x$ returns a random vector $\tilde g$ with
$$
\mathbb{E}\big[\tilde g \mid x\big] \in \partial f(x), \qquad \mathbb{E}\big[\|\tilde g\|^2 \mid x\big] \le B^2,
$$
that is, $\tilde g$ is an **unbiased** estimate of some subgradient with bounded second moment $B^2$. The canonical instance is the **finite-sum / expectation objective** $f(x) = \mathbb{E}_{\xi}[\ell(x; \xi)]$ or $f(x) = \tfrac1m\sum_{j=1}^m \ell_j(x)$, where drawing $\xi$ (or an index $j$) uniformly and returning $\nabla_x \ell(x;\xi)$ gives such an oracle; the unbiasedness is the sample-average / law-of-large-numbers fact of [37.02.02].

**Definition (stochastic gradient descent).** Choose $x_0 \in C$ and step sizes $\eta_k > 0$. With $P_C$ the Euclidean projection, **stochastic gradient descent (SGD)** generates
$$
x_{k+1} = P_C\big(x_k - \eta_k\, \tilde g_k\big), \qquad \mathbb{E}[\tilde g_k \mid \mathcal F_k] \in \partial f(x_k),
$$
where $\mathcal F_k = \sigma(x_0, \tilde g_0, \dots, \tilde g_{k-1})$ is the history. The convergence object is the **averaged iterate** $\bar x_k = \big(\sum_{i=0}^{k}\eta_i\big)^{-1}\sum_{i=0}^{k}\eta_i x_i$ (or the uniform average), not the last iterate.

**Definition (mini-batch).** A **mini-batch** oracle averages $b$ independent draws, $\tilde g = \tfrac1b\sum_{s=1}^b \nabla_x\ell(x;\xi_s)$; it is unbiased with second moment reduced to $\sigma^2/b + \|\nabla f(x)\|^2$ when $\sigma^2$ is the per-sample variance, trading the per-iteration cost $b$ against a smaller noise term.

**Definition (mirror map / distance-generating function).** Let $\|\cdot\|$ be a norm with dual norm $\|\cdot\|_*$. A **mirror map** (or **distance-generating function**) on $C$ is a function $\Phi : \mathcal D \to \mathbb{R}$, with $C \subseteq \overline{\mathcal D}$, that is differentiable and **$\sigma$-strongly convex** with respect to $\|\cdot\|$:
$$
\Phi(y) \ge \Phi(x) + \langle \nabla\Phi(x), y - x\rangle + \tfrac{\sigma}{2}\|y - x\|^2.
$$
Its **Bregman divergence** is
$$
D_\Phi(x, y) = \Phi(x) - \Phi(y) - \langle \nabla\Phi(y), x - y\rangle \;\ge\; \tfrac{\sigma}{2}\|x - y\|^2 \ge 0.
$$
Taking $\Phi(x) = \tfrac12\|x\|_2^2$ ($\sigma = 1$) recovers $D_\Phi(x,y) = \tfrac12\|x-y\|_2^2$ and the Euclidean geometry of [44.06.01]-[44.06.02].

**Definition (mirror descent).** With a subgradient $g_k \in \partial f(x_k)$ (or a stochastic $\tilde g_k$) and step $\eta_k > 0$, **mirror descent** is the **proximal-mirror update**
$$
x_{k+1} = \arg\min_{x \in C}\Big\{\, \eta_k\langle g_k, x\rangle + D_\Phi(x, x_k) \,\Big\},
$$
equivalently the **mirror step then Bregman projection** $\nabla\Phi(\hat y_{k+1}) = \nabla\Phi(x_k) - \eta_k g_k$ followed by $x_{k+1} = \arg\min_{x\in C} D_\Phi(x, \hat y_{k+1})$. The Euclidean map gives projected subgradient descent of [44.06.01]; replacing $D_\Phi$ by a general Bregman distance is the non-Euclidean generalisation of the proximal step of [44.06.02].

**Definition (entropic mirror map).** On the probability simplex $\Delta_n = \{x \ge 0,\ \sum_i x_i = 1\}$ take the **negative entropy** $\Phi(x) = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i\log x_i$, which is $1$-strongly convex with respect to $\|\cdot\|_1$ (Pinsker). Its Bregman divergence is the **Kullback-Leibler divergence** $D_\Phi(x,y) = \sum_i x_i\log(x_i/y_i)$, and the update becomes the **exponentiated-gradient / multiplicative-weights** rule
$$
x_{k+1, i} = \frac{x_{k,i}\,\exp(-\eta_k g_{k,i})}{\sum_{j} x_{k,j}\,\exp(-\eta_k g_{k,j})}.
$$

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"SGD converges in the last iterate."* In general it does not: with constant step the last iterate fluctuates in a noise ball forever. The averaged iterate $\bar x_k$ (or diminishing steps) is what converges; reporting $x_k$ alone can be badly off.

- *"A biased gradient estimate is fine if it is close."* The analysis uses $\mathbb{E}[\tilde g_k \mid \mathcal F_k] \in \partial f(x_k)$ to make the cross-term collapse to $f(x_k) - f^\star$ in expectation. A fixed bias does not average away and changes the fixed point; unbiasedness, not mere closeness, is the load-bearing hypothesis.

- *"Mirror descent is just a change of variables in subgradient descent."* The step $\nabla\Phi(\hat y_{k+1}) = \nabla\Phi(x_k) - \eta_k g_k$ updates in the **dual** space ($\nabla\Phi$ is the mirror to the dual); the rate constant depends on the radius of $C$ measured by $\Phi$ and on $\sigma$ for the chosen norm, not on the Euclidean radius. Choosing $\Phi$ changes the dimension-dependence, which a coordinate change cannot.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The stochastic and non-Euclidean analyses both rest on one Bregman recursion that generalises the fundamental inequality $(\ast)$ of [44.06.01]; taking expectations or telescoping then reads off each rate.

**Theorem (mirror-descent / stochastic fundamental inequality and the $O(1/\sqrt{k})$ rate).** *Let $f$ be convex and let $\Phi$ be a mirror map that is $\sigma$-strongly convex with respect to $\|\cdot\|$ on $C$. Run mirror descent $x_{k+1} = \arg\min_{x\in C}\{\eta\langle g_k, x\rangle + D_\Phi(x, x_k)\}$ with $g_k \in \partial f(x_k)$, $\|g_k\|_* \le B$, and constant step $\eta$. Then for every $x \in C$ and each $k$,*
$$
\eta\big(f(x_k) - f(x)\big) \;\le\; D_\Phi(x, x_k) - D_\Phi(x, x_{k+1}) + \frac{\eta^2 B^2}{2\sigma}. \tag{$\ast$}
$$
*Consequently, with $D_\Phi(x^\star, x_0) \le R^2$ and $\bar x_k = \tfrac1{k+1}\sum_{i=0}^{k} x_i$,*
$$
f(\bar x_k) - f^\star \;\le\; \frac{R^2}{\eta(k+1)} + \frac{\eta B^2}{2\sigma}, \qquad
\text{and } \eta = \frac{R}{B}\sqrt{\frac{2\sigma}{k+1}} \text{ gives } f(\bar x_k) - f^\star \le BR\sqrt{\frac{2}{\sigma(k+1)}}. \tag{$\ast\ast$}
$$
*In the stochastic case $g_k = \tilde g_k$ with $\mathbb{E}[\tilde g_k\mid\mathcal F_k]\in\partial f(x_k)$ and $\mathbb{E}\|\tilde g_k\|_*^2 \le B^2$, the same bound holds for $\mathbb{E}[f(\bar x_k)] - f^\star$.*

**Proof.** Let $x_{k+1}$ minimise $\psi(x) = \eta\langle g_k, x\rangle + D_\Phi(x, x_k)$ over $C$. The optimality condition is $\langle \nabla\psi(x_{k+1}), x - x_{k+1}\rangle \ge 0$ for all $x \in C$, and since $\nabla_x D_\Phi(x, x_k) = \nabla\Phi(x) - \nabla\Phi(x_k)$,
$$
\big\langle \eta g_k + \nabla\Phi(x_{k+1}) - \nabla\Phi(x_k),\; x - x_{k+1}\big\rangle \ge 0 \quad\text{for all } x \in C. \tag{1}
$$
The **three-point identity** for any $x$ reads
$$
\langle \nabla\Phi(x_{k+1}) - \nabla\Phi(x_k),\, x - x_{k+1}\rangle = D_\Phi(x, x_k) - D_\Phi(x, x_{k+1}) - D_\Phi(x_{k+1}, x_k), \tag{2}
$$
which is a direct expansion of the three Bregman terms. Substituting (2) into (1) gives, for every $x \in C$,
$$
\eta\langle g_k, x_{k+1} - x\rangle \le D_\Phi(x, x_k) - D_\Phi(x, x_{k+1}) - D_\Phi(x_{k+1}, x_k).
$$
Split $\langle g_k, x_{k+1} - x\rangle = \langle g_k, x_k - x\rangle + \langle g_k, x_{k+1} - x_k\rangle$. The subgradient inequality gives $\langle g_k, x_k - x\rangle \ge f(x_k) - f(x)$. For the remaining term, Fenchel-Young / Cauchy-Schwarz in the pair $(\|\cdot\|, \|\cdot\|_*)$ and $D_\Phi(x_{k+1}, x_k) \ge \tfrac\sigma2\|x_{k+1}-x_k\|^2$ give
$$
\eta\langle g_k, x_{k+1} - x_k\rangle - D_\Phi(x_{k+1}, x_k) \le \eta\|g_k\|_*\|x_{k+1}-x_k\| - \tfrac\sigma2\|x_{k+1}-x_k\|^2 \le \frac{\eta^2\|g_k\|_*^2}{2\sigma},
$$
the last step maximising the quadratic in $\|x_{k+1}-x_k\|$. Collecting terms and using $\|g_k\|_* \le B$ yields $(\ast)$.

Sum $(\ast)$ over $i = 0,\dots,k$ to telescope the Bregman differences; dropping the nonnegative $D_\Phi(x, x_{k+1})$ and using convexity $f(\bar x_k) \le \tfrac1{k+1}\sum_i f(x_i)$,
$$
\eta(k+1)\big(f(\bar x_k) - f(x)\big) \le D_\Phi(x, x_0) + (k+1)\frac{\eta^2 B^2}{2\sigma}.
$$
Putting $x = x^\star$, $D_\Phi(x^\star, x_0) \le R^2$, dividing by $\eta(k+1)$, and optimising $\eta$ gives $(\ast\ast)$. In the stochastic case, take conditional expectation given $\mathcal F_k$: $\mathbb{E}[\langle \tilde g_k, x_k - x^\star\rangle \mid \mathcal F_k] = \langle \mathbb{E}[\tilde g_k\mid\mathcal F_k], x_k - x^\star\rangle \ge f(x_k) - f^\star$ by unbiasedness, and $\mathbb{E}\|\tilde g_k\|_*^2 \le B^2$ bounds the noise term; the tower property over the telescoped sum reproduces $(\ast\ast)$ in expectation. $\square$

**Bridge.** This single Bregman recursion $(\ast)$ is the foundational reason both stochastic and non-Euclidean first-order methods converge, and it builds toward the strongly-convex $O(1/k)$ rate and the simplex dimension-dependence of the Advanced results, where the entropy map is read off $(\ast\ast)$ directly. This is exactly the non-Euclidean analogue of the fundamental inequality of [44.06.01]: where that argument used the Euclidean distance as the Lyapunov quantity, here the Bregman divergence $D_\Phi$ plays the role, and the proof generalises projected subgradient descent to an arbitrary geometry with no loss of rate. The mirror update is dual to the proximal step of [44.06.02] — both minimise a linearised objective plus a divergence — and the central insight is that the rate constant $R^2/\sigma$, set by the *geometry* of $C$ rather than its Euclidean radius, is what the entropy map shrinks from $\sqrt n$ to $\sqrt{\log n}$. Putting these together, the stochastic and mirror generalisations share one recursion, and it appears again in the regret analysis of online convex optimization and in the variance-reduced methods that recover linear rates.

## Advanced results [Master]

Mirror descent is the update $x_{k+1} = \arg\min_{x\in C}\{\eta_k\langle g_k, x\rangle + D_\Phi(x, x_k)\}$, and the results below extend the single recursion $(\ast)$ to the strongly-convex rate, the simplex dimension-dependence, variance reduction, and the regret viewpoint that unifies the stochastic and online settings.

**Theorem 1 (rates of stochastic gradient descent).** For convex $L$-Lipschitz $f$ (so $B = L$) and a stochastic oracle with $\mathbb{E}\|\tilde g_k\|^2 \le B^2$, SGD with $\eta_k = R/(B\sqrt{k+1})$ (or constant $\eta = (R/B)\sqrt{2/(k+1)}$ on a fixed horizon) attains $\mathbb{E}[f(\bar x_k)] - f^\star = O(BR/\sqrt{k})$. If $f$ is additionally $\alpha$-strongly convex, the step $\eta_k = 2/(\alpha(k+1))$ with the weighted average $\bar x_k = \sum_i (i+1) x_i / \sum_i (i+1)$ gives $\mathbb{E}[f(\bar x_k)] - f^\star = O(B^2/(\alpha k))$ (Exercise 6) [ref: TODO_REF Bubeck, S. — Convex Optimization: Algorithms and Complexity (2015)]. Both rates are minimax-optimal for the respective stochastic first-order classes, so SGD is the rate-optimal stochastic method; the only freedom is in the constant, which mini-batching and variance reduction improve.

**Theorem 2 (the simplex and the entropic dimension-dependence).** On $\Delta_n$ with $f$ convex and $\|g\|_\infty \le L_\infty$, take the entropy map $\Phi(x) = \sum_i x_i\log x_i$, $1$-strongly convex with respect to $\|\cdot\|_1$ (Pinsker), with $D_\Phi(x^\star, x_0) = \mathrm{KL}(x^\star\|x_0) \le \log n$ for the uniform start. Then $(\ast\ast)$ gives $f(\bar x_k) - f^\star \le L_\infty\sqrt{2\log n / (k+1)} = O\big(L_\infty\sqrt{\log n / k}\big)$ [ref: TODO_REF Nemirovski, A. & Yudin, D. — Problem Complexity and Method Efficiency in Optimization (1983)]. Projected (Euclidean) subgradient descent on $\Delta_n$ achieves only $O(L_2\sqrt{1/k})$ with $L_2$ the $\ell_2$ subgradient bound, and since $L_\infty \le L_2 \le \sqrt n\,L_\infty$ the worst-case ratio is $\sqrt{n/\log n}$ in favour of the entropic map. The improvement is the original motivation of the mirror-descent scheme: the rate constant tracks the prox-radius of $C$ in the *intrinsic* geometry, and entropy is the geometry of the simplex.

**Theorem 3 (variance reduction recovers linear rates).** For the finite sum $f = \tfrac1m\sum_{j=1}^m f_j$ with each $f_j$ convex $L$-smooth and $f$ $\alpha$-strongly convex, plain SGD with constant step converges only to a noise ball and with diminishing step only at $O(1/k)$. **SVRG** periodically computes a full gradient $\nabla f(\tilde x)$ at a snapshot $\tilde x$ and uses the corrected estimate $\tilde g_k = \nabla f_{j}(x_k) - \nabla f_{j}(\tilde x) + \nabla f(\tilde x)$, which is unbiased and has variance $\to 0$ as $x_k, \tilde x \to x^\star$; **SAG/SAGA** maintain a running table of per-component gradients to the same effect. Each attains a **linear** rate $\mathbb{E}[f(x_k) - f^\star] \le (1 - c/\kappa)^k\,(f(x_0)-f^\star)$ with $\kappa = L/\alpha$, matching full-batch gradient descent in rate at $O(1)$ per-iteration cost amortised [ref: TODO_REF Bubeck, S. — Convex Optimization: Algorithms and Complexity (2015)]. The variance term, not the bias, is what the snapshot cancels; once it vanishes the strongly-convex contraction of the deterministic method is restored.

**Theorem 4 (regret and online convex optimization).** In the online model, against any comparator $x \in C$, mirror descent on linearised losses satisfies $\mathrm{Reg}_T = \sum_{t=1}^T \langle g_t, x_t - x\rangle \le \tfrac{R^2}{\eta} + \tfrac{\eta B^2 T}{2\sigma}$, optimised to $\mathrm{Reg}_T = O(BR\sqrt{T/\sigma})$; on the simplex with the entropy map this is the $O(L_\infty\sqrt{T\log n})$ regret of **Hedge / multiplicative weights** [ref: TODO_REF Beck, A. — First-Order Methods in Optimization (SIAM, 2017)]. Online-to-batch conversion (Exercise 8) turns any $O(\sqrt T)$ regret into an $O(1/\sqrt T)$ stochastic-optimization rate, so the offline rates of Theorem 1 and the online regret are the *same* statement read in two directions. The strongly-convex refinement uses $\eta_t = 1/(\alpha t)$ to reach $O(\log T)$ regret, the online face of the $O(1/k)$ rate.

**Synthesis.** The single Bregman recursion $(\ast)$ is the foundational reason the entire stochastic and non-Euclidean theory coheres, and the central insight is that the Bregman divergence $D_\Phi$, not the Euclidean distance, is the Lyapunov quantity — this is exactly why the rate constant tracks the intrinsic geometry of $C$ and why the entropy map turns the simplex factor from $\sqrt n$ into $\sqrt{\log n}$. The stochastic rates of Theorem 1 are the in-expectation form of $(\ast\ast)$, with unbiasedness the load-bearing hypothesis that makes the cross-term collapse and the bounded second moment the noise floor; variance reduction is dual to this, cancelling precisely the noise term so the deterministic strongly-convex contraction reappears, and the foundational reason it works is that the snapshot correction leaves the estimate unbiased while driving its variance to zero. The regret viewpoint of Theorem 4 is not a separate theory but the same recursion summed against a fixed comparator, and online-to-batch conversion shows the offline $O(1/\sqrt k)$ and the online $O(\sqrt T)$ are one fact; putting these together, mirror descent generalises the projected subgradient method of [44.06.01] and is dual to the proximal-gradient method of [44.06.02], both being minimisers of a linearised objective plus a divergence, and the bridge to the rest of large-scale optimization is precisely the freedom to choose $\Phi$ — the geometry, not merely the step, is the design variable that powers large-scale machine-learning training.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (three-point Bregman identity).** *For differentiable $\Phi$ and any $x, y, z$, $\langle\nabla\Phi(z)-\nabla\Phi(y), x - z\rangle = D_\Phi(x,y) - D_\Phi(x,z) - D_\Phi(z,y)$.*

*Proof.* This is Exercise 7; the computation expands the three divergences from the definition $D_\Phi(a,b) = \Phi(a)-\Phi(b)-\langle\nabla\Phi(b),a-b\rangle$, cancels all $\Phi$ values, combines the two $\nabla\Phi(y)$ inner products into $\langle\nabla\Phi(y), z - x\rangle$, and adds $\langle\nabla\Phi(z), x - z\rangle$; the residual $\langle\nabla\Phi(y),(z-x)+(x-z)\rangle$ vanishes, leaving $\langle\nabla\Phi(z)-\nabla\Phi(y), x-z\rangle$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (mirror-descent fundamental inequality).** *Let $\Phi$ be $\sigma$-strongly convex with respect to $\|\cdot\|$, $g_k\in\partial f(x_k)$ with $\|g_k\|_*\le B$, and $x_{k+1} = \arg\min_{x\in C}\{\eta\langle g_k,x\rangle + D_\Phi(x,x_k)\}$. Then for all $x\in C$, $\eta(f(x_k)-f(x)) \le D_\Phi(x,x_k) - D_\Phi(x,x_{k+1}) + \eta^2 B^2/(2\sigma)$.*

*Proof.* The constrained optimality condition for the convex problem defining $x_{k+1}$ is $\langle \eta g_k + \nabla\Phi(x_{k+1}) - \nabla\Phi(x_k), x - x_{k+1}\rangle \ge 0$ for all $x\in C$. Rearranged, $\eta\langle g_k, x_{k+1} - x\rangle \le \langle \nabla\Phi(x_{k+1}) - \nabla\Phi(x_k), x - x_{k+1}\rangle = D_\Phi(x,x_k) - D_\Phi(x,x_{k+1}) - D_\Phi(x_{k+1},x_k)$ by Proposition 1 (with $z = x_{k+1}$, $y = x_k$). Write $\eta\langle g_k, x_{k+1}-x\rangle = \eta\langle g_k, x_k - x\rangle + \eta\langle g_k, x_{k+1}-x_k\rangle$. The subgradient inequality gives $\eta\langle g_k, x_k - x\rangle \ge \eta(f(x_k)-f(x))$. By the generalised Cauchy-Schwarz $\langle g_k, x_{k+1}-x_k\rangle \le \|g_k\|_*\|x_{k+1}-x_k\|$ and strong convexity $D_\Phi(x_{k+1},x_k)\ge\tfrac\sigma2\|x_{k+1}-x_k\|^2$,
$$
\eta\langle g_k, x_{k+1}-x_k\rangle - D_\Phi(x_{k+1},x_k) \le \max_{s\ge0}\big(\eta B s - \tfrac\sigma2 s^2\big) = \frac{\eta^2 B^2}{2\sigma}.
$$
Combining the displayed inequalities, $\eta(f(x_k)-f(x)) \le D_\Phi(x,x_k) - D_\Phi(x,x_{k+1}) + \eta^2 B^2/(2\sigma)$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 ($O(1/\sqrt k)$ averaged-iterate rate).** *Under Proposition 2 with constant $\eta$ and $D_\Phi(x^\star,x_0)\le R^2$, $f(\bar x_k)-f^\star \le R^2/(\eta(k+1)) + \eta B^2/(2\sigma)$, minimised over $\eta$ at $f(\bar x_k)-f^\star \le BR\sqrt{2/(\sigma(k+1))}$.*

*Proof.* Sum Proposition 2 with $x = x^\star$ over $i = 0,\dots,k$: the Bregman terms telescope to $D_\Phi(x^\star,x_0) - D_\Phi(x^\star,x_{k+1}) \le R^2$, so $\eta\sum_{i=0}^k(f(x_i)-f^\star) \le R^2 + (k+1)\eta^2 B^2/(2\sigma)$. Convexity gives $f(\bar x_k)\le\frac1{k+1}\sum_i f(x_i)$, hence $\eta(k+1)(f(\bar x_k)-f^\star) \le R^2 + (k+1)\eta^2 B^2/(2\sigma)$; divide by $\eta(k+1)$. Minimising $h(\eta) = R^2/(\eta(k+1)) + \eta B^2/(2\sigma)$ over $\eta>0$: $h'(\eta) = -R^2/(\eta^2(k+1)) + B^2/(2\sigma) = 0$ gives $\eta = R\sqrt{2\sigma}/(B\sqrt{k+1})$, and $h$ at this $\eta$ is $2\sqrt{R^2 B^2/(2\sigma(k+1))} = BR\sqrt{2/(\sigma(k+1))}$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (stochastic rate in expectation).** *With $g_k = \tilde g_k$, $\mathbb{E}[\tilde g_k\mid\mathcal F_k]\in\partial f(x_k)$, $\mathbb{E}[\|\tilde g_k\|_*^2\mid\mathcal F_k]\le B^2$, the bound of Proposition 3 holds for $\mathbb{E}[f(\bar x_k)]-f^\star$.*

*Proof.* The deterministic step of Proposition 2 uses $\langle g_k, x_k - x^\star\rangle \ge f(x_k)-f^\star$ and $\|g_k\|_*\le B$. Replace these by conditional expectations: $\mathbb{E}[\langle\tilde g_k, x_k-x^\star\rangle\mid\mathcal F_k] = \langle\mathbb{E}[\tilde g_k\mid\mathcal F_k], x_k-x^\star\rangle \ge f(x_k)-f^\star$ since $x_k$ is $\mathcal F_k$-measurable and $\mathbb{E}[\tilde g_k\mid\mathcal F_k]$ is a subgradient, and the noise term is bounded by $\eta^2\mathbb{E}[\|\tilde g_k\|_*^2\mid\mathcal F_k]/(2\sigma)\le\eta^2 B^2/(2\sigma)$. Thus $\eta\,\mathbb{E}[f(x_k)-f^\star\mid\mathcal F_k] \le \mathbb{E}[D_\Phi(x^\star,x_k)-D_\Phi(x^\star,x_{k+1})\mid\mathcal F_k] + \eta^2 B^2/(2\sigma)$. Take total expectation, sum over $i\le k$ (the telescope survives by the tower property since $D_\Phi(x^\star,x_{k+1})$ is integrable), and apply Jensen $\mathbb{E} f(\bar x_k)\le\frac1{k+1}\sum_i\mathbb{E} f(x_i)$ to reach the stated bound. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (entropy map is $1$-strongly convex in $\ell_1$; the simplex rate).** *On $\Delta_n$, $\Phi(x) = \sum_i x_i\log x_i$ satisfies $D_\Phi(x,y) = \mathrm{KL}(x\|y) \ge \tfrac12\|x-y\|_1^2$, and mirror descent with $\|g\|_\infty\le L_\infty$ attains $f(\bar x_k)-f^\star\le L_\infty\sqrt{2\log n/(k+1)}$.*

*Proof.* The Bregman divergence of the negative entropy is $D_\Phi(x,y) = \sum_i x_i\log x_i - \sum_i y_i\log y_i - \sum_i(\log y_i + 1)(x_i - y_i)$; on $\Delta_n$ the $+1$ terms sum to $\sum_i(x_i-y_i) = 0$, leaving $\sum_i x_i\log(x_i/y_i) = \mathrm{KL}(x\|y)$. **Pinsker's inequality** $\mathrm{KL}(x\|y)\ge\tfrac12\|x-y\|_1^2$ is exactly $1$-strong convexity of $\Phi$ with respect to $\|\cdot\|_1$ (so $\sigma = 1$ and the dual norm is $\|\cdot\|_\infty$). The prox-radius is $\sup_{x\in\Delta_n}\mathrm{KL}(x\|x_0)$ at the uniform $x_0 = (1/n,\dots,1/n)$: $\mathrm{KL}(x\|x_0) = \sum_i x_i\log(n x_i) = \log n - H(x) \le \log n$, since entropy $H(x)\ge0$. Hence $R^2 = \log n$, $B = L_\infty$, $\sigma = 1$ in Proposition 3, giving $f(\bar x_k)-f^\star \le L_\infty\sqrt{2\log n/(k+1)}$. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (Euclidean mirror descent is projected subgradient descent).** *For $\Phi(x) = \tfrac12\|x\|_2^2$, the mirror update equals $x_{k+1} = P_C(x_k - \eta g_k)$.*

*Proof.* Here $D_\Phi(x,x_k) = \tfrac12\|x-x_k\|_2^2$, so $x_{k+1} = \arg\min_{x\in C}\{\eta\langle g_k,x\rangle + \tfrac12\|x-x_k\|^2\}$. Completing the square, $\eta\langle g_k,x\rangle + \tfrac12\|x-x_k\|^2 = \tfrac12\|x - (x_k - \eta g_k)\|^2 + \text{const}$, whose constrained minimiser over $C$ is by definition the Euclidean projection $P_C(x_k - \eta g_k)$. The mirror scheme contains projected subgradient descent of [44.06.01] as the case $\Phi = \tfrac12\|\cdot\|_2^2$, $\sigma = 1$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Mirror descent generalises the projected subgradient method of [44.06.01]: the Euclidean mirror map $\Phi = \tfrac12\|\cdot\|_2^2$ reduces the Bregman fundamental inequality $(\ast)$ to that unit's recursion $\|x_{k+1}-x^\star\|^2 \le \|x_k-x^\star\|^2 - 2\eta(f(x_k)-f^\star) + \eta^2\|g_k\|^2$ and recovers the $O(1/\sqrt k)$ rate, while the stochastic version randomises that unit's deterministic subgradient into an unbiased oracle and keeps the rate in expectation. The lower bound proved there caps the deterministic nonsmooth class, and SGD/mirror descent are the rate-optimal members of the stochastic and non-Euclidean refinements of it.

- The proximal-gradient method of [44.06.02] and mirror descent are dual constructions: both minimise a linearised objective plus a regulariser — the Euclidean prox $\arg\min\{\eta\langle\nabla f,x\rangle + \tfrac12\|x-x_k\|^2\} + g$ versus the Bregman prox $\arg\min\{\eta\langle g_k,x\rangle + D_\Phi(x,x_k)\}$ — so the mirror update is the non-Euclidean proximal step, and the Moreau-envelope smoothing of that unit corresponds to the strong convexity of $\Phi$ here. The composite mirror-prox and the proximal-gradient method meet in the Bregman proximal-gradient algorithm.

- Stochastic gradient descent rests on the unbiased-estimator mechanism formalised by the strong law of large numbers in [37.02.02]: the sample-average gradient $\tfrac1b\sum_s\nabla\ell(x;\xi_s)$ converges to the true gradient $\nabla f(x) = \mathbb{E}_\xi[\nabla\ell(x;\xi)]$, and the variance term $B^2$ in the rate is the second-moment quantity that unit's law controls; mini-batching is the finite-sample form of that convergence, and variance reduction engineers the error to vanish along the trajectory.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The stochastic-approximation idea — replacing an exact gradient by a noisy unbiased estimate and shrinking the step — originates with **Herbert Robbins and Sutton Monro** in 1951 [ref: TODO_REF Robbins & Monro 1951], whose convergence conditions $\sum\eta_k = \infty$, $\sum\eta_k^2 < \infty$ are still the canonical diminishing-step regime; **Kiefer and Wolfowitz** (1952) extended it to gradient estimation by finite differences. Mirror descent is due to **Arkadi Nemirovski and David Yudin**, whose 1983 monograph introduced the prox-function / distance-generating-function scheme and proved that the geometry of the feasible set, not the Euclidean norm, governs the complexity — the demonstration that the entropy prox-function gives a $\sqrt{\log n}$ rather than $\sqrt n$ dependence on the simplex is theirs [ref: TODO_REF Nemirovski & Yudin 1983]. The multiplicative-weights / exponentiated-gradient instance was rediscovered repeatedly across learning theory, notably by **Littlestone and Warmuth** (1994) for the weighted-majority algorithm and by **Freund and Schapire** (1997) for Hedge and boosting [ref: TODO_REF Freund & Schapire 1997]. The modern synthesis — mirror descent as the canonical non-Euclidean and online method, the regret / online-to-batch viewpoint, and the SGD rates — is presented by Bubeck [ref: TODO_REF Bubeck 2015] and by Beck [ref: TODO_REF Beck 2017], the source whose Bregman-prox formulation this unit follows. Variance reduction (SAG, SVRG, SAGA) was developed by **Le Roux-Schmidt-Bach** (2012), **Johnson-Zhang** (2013), and **Defazio-Bach-Lacoste-Julien** (2014).
