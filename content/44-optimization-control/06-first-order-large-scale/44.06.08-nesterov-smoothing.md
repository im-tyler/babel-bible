## Intuition [Beginner]

A subgradient method treats a kinked cost as a black box: at each point it asks only for a downhill direction and steps. It works for any convex shape, but it is slow — to cut the error in half you may need four times the steps. The kinks are the problem. A smooth bowl can be descended quickly; a creased one cannot, because near a crease the method keeps zig-zagging across the fold.

Many kinked costs are not arbitrary, though. They are built from a hidden "worst-case" rule: the cost at a point is the largest payoff over a menu of choices an adversary could pick. The absolute value, the largest entry of a vector, the size of the biggest error — each is a maximum over simple options. That hidden menu is extra information the black box throws away.

Nesterov's smoothing uses the menu. Instead of letting the adversary pick the single best option, you charge a small fee for picking an extreme option — a fee that grows the more lopsided the choice. The adversary now prefers a soft blend of options, and the resulting cost is a rounded version of the original: the kinks are buffed out into gentle curves. You control how rounded with one knob.

The payoff is a speed-class jump. Run a fast smooth-problem method on the rounded cost, and tune the rounding to match the accuracy you want. The kinked problem that needed about one-over-error-squared steps now needs about one-over-error steps. You did not break the rule that black-box kinked problems are slow — you stopped treating the problem as a black box and used the menu you knew was there.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the absolute-value cost, a sharp V with a corner at zero. Smoothing replaces the corner with a small smooth arc that hugs the V everywhere else. Make the arc tighter and the rounded cost matches the V more closely but curves harder near zero (steeper to descend); make it wider and the cost is easier to descend but sits a little above the true V.

```
   sharp cost (a kinked V)        smoothed cost (corner rounded)

   |\           /|                 |\           /|
   | \         / |                 | \         / |
   |  \       /  |                 |  \       /  |
   |   \     /   |                 |   \_   _/   |   <- rounded arc
   |    \   /    |                 |     \_/     |      (no kink)
   |     \ /     |                 |              |
   +------V------+                 +------_------+
        corner                        smooth bottom

   error ~ 1/k^2 to descend         error ~ mu (rounding gap)
   (kink forces zig-zag)            balance mu with accuracy -> 1/k overall
```

| approach | kinked problem | rounded problem |
|---|---|---|
| steps to reach error | about $1/\text{error}^2$ | about $1/\text{error}$ |
| extra information used | none (black box) | the hidden max-menu |
| knob | none | rounding amount $\mu$ |

## Worked example [Beginner]

Round off the absolute value $f(x) = |x|$. The key fact is that the absolute value is a maximum over a menu: $|x|$ equals the largest of $x \cdot u$ as $u$ ranges over the choices $-1$ to $1$. The adversary picks $u = +1$ when $x > 0$ and $u = -1$ when $x < 0$, recovering $|x|$, with the kink at $x = 0$ where the choice flips.

**Step 1. Charge a fee.** Subtract a small fee $\tfrac{\mu}{2} u^2$ from the adversary's payoff, with $\mu = 1$ for a clean number. The adversary now maximizes $x u - \tfrac12 u^2$ over $u$ in $-1$ to $1$.

**Step 2. Solve the adversary's choice.** Ignoring the $-1$ to $1$ limits, the best $u$ sets the slope $x - u$ to zero, so $u = x$. That stays inside the limits when $x$ is between $-1$ and $1$. There the rounded cost is $x \cdot x - \tfrac12 x^2 = \tfrac12 x^2$ — a smooth bowl.

**Step 3. Far from zero.** When $x > 1$ the unfettered choice $u = x$ exceeds the limit, so the adversary picks $u = 1$, giving $x \cdot 1 - \tfrac12 = x - \tfrac12$. By symmetry, for $x < -1$ the cost is $-x - \tfrac12 = |x| - \tfrac12$.

**Step 4. Read the rounded cost.** It is $\tfrac12 x^2$ for $|x| \le 1$ and $|x| - \tfrac12$ for $|x| \ge 1$ — a parabola spliced smoothly into the two straight arms of the V. This is the Huber function.

**What this tells us.** The rounded cost never exceeds the true $|x|$ by more than the fee budget (here $\tfrac12$ at $x = 0$), and it has no kink, so a fast smooth-problem method can descend it. Shrink $\mu$ and the rounding gap shrinks too; the whole art is choosing $\mu$ to match the accuracy you need.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $f$ is a structured nonsmooth convex function presented through a saddle (max-type) representation, the conjugate-style modeling form of [44.02.03]. Let $Q_1 \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ and $Q_2 \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$ be nonempty closed convex sets with $Q_2$ bounded, let $A : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ be linear with adjoint $A^\top$, and let $\hat\phi : Q_2 \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous convex. The Euclidean inner product is $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ and $\|A\|$ is the operator norm.

**Definition (structured nonsmooth model).** The objective is
$$
f(x) = \max_{u \in Q_2}\,\big\{\langle Ax, u\rangle - \hat\phi(u)\big\}, \qquad x \in Q_1.
$$
The maximum is a supremum of affine functions of $x$, so $f$ is convex; it is generally nonsmooth, the kinks occurring where the maximiser in $u$ switches. This is exactly the conjugate/saddle form of [44.02.03]: $f(x) = \hat\phi^*(A^\top \cdot)$-type composition, and $f$ is the support-style value of an inner concave maximization in $u$.

**Definition (prox-function).** A **prox-function** for $Q_2$ is a continuous function $d_2 : Q_2 \to \mathbb{R}$ that is $\sigma_2$-strongly convex on $Q_2$ ($\sigma_2 > 0$), with prox-center $u_0 = \arg\min_{u \in Q_2} d_2(u)$ normalised so $d_2(u_0) = 0$, and prox-diameter
$$
D_2 = \max_{u \in Q_2} d_2(u) < \infty.
$$
A symmetric prox-function $d_1$ with parameters $\sigma_1, D_1$ is fixed on $Q_1$ for the outer accelerated method.

**Definition (smooth approximation).** For smoothing parameter $\mu > 0$, the **Nesterov smoothing** of $f$ is
$$
f_\mu(x) = \max_{u \in Q_2}\,\big\{\langle Ax, u\rangle - \hat\phi(u) - \mu\, d_2(u)\big\}.
$$
Subtracting the strongly convex penalty $\mu d_2$ makes the inner objective strongly concave in $u$, so the maximiser $u_\mu(x)$ is unique and depends smoothly on $x$. Write $L_\mu = \|A\|^2/(\mu\sigma_2)$.

**Definition (smoothable function, Beck-Teboulle abstraction).** A convex $g$ is **$(\alpha,\beta)$-smoothable** if for each $\mu > 0$ there is a convex $g_\mu$ with $g_\mu(x) \le g(x) \le g_\mu(x) + \beta\mu$ for all $x$ and $\nabla g_\mu$ Lipschitz with constant $\alpha/\mu$. The conjugate-smoothing $f_\mu$ above realises this with $\alpha = \|A\|^2/\sigma_2$ and $\beta = D_2$ [ref: TODO_REF Beck & Teboulle 2012].

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"Smoothing beats the nonsmooth lower bound, so the $\Omega(1/\varepsilon^2)$ subgradient lower bound is wrong."* It is not a contradiction. The $\Omega(1/\varepsilon^2)$ bound of [44.06.01] is for the **black-box** oracle model, where the method sees only $f$ and a subgradient. Smoothing is not a black-box method: it consumes the explicit max-representation $(A, \hat\phi, Q_2)$ as side information the oracle never supplied. The structure is the extra input that escapes the lower bound's hypotheses.

- *"Just send $\mu \to 0$ to get the exact problem cheaply."* As $\mu \to 0$ the gradient-Lipschitz constant $L_\mu = \|A\|^2/(\mu\sigma_2) \to \infty$, so an accelerated method's per-target iteration count $\sqrt{L_\mu}/\sqrt\varepsilon$ blows up. The error $\mu D_2$ and the conditioning $L_\mu$ pull in opposite directions; the rate comes only from balancing them, $\mu \sim \varepsilon$.

- *"This is the same as Moreau-Yosida / proximal smoothing."* The Moreau envelope $e_\gamma f = f \,\square\, \tfrac1{2\gamma}\|\cdot\|_2^2$ of [44.06.02] smooths via infimal convolution in the **primal** variable and needs a computable proximal operator. Nesterov smoothing regularizes the **dual/inner** variable $u$ in the saddle representation by a prox-function. The two coincide for special $d_2$ but differ in general, and the conjugate route gives the explicit $\nabla f_\mu(x) = A^\top u_\mu(x)$ even when $\operatorname{prox}_f$ is unavailable.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The result that powers the whole technique is that the regularized inner maximum is not merely continuous but continuously differentiable with a controlled Lipschitz gradient, and that it approximates $f$ uniformly with error governed by the single parameter $\mu$. These two facts let an accelerated smooth-class method run on $f_\mu$ and a balance of $\mu$ against accuracy convert its $O(1/k^2)$ rate into $O(1/\varepsilon)$ on the nonsmooth $f$.

**Theorem (smoothness, approximation, and the $O(1/\varepsilon)$ complexity).** *With the data above, the smoothing $f_\mu$ is convex and continuously differentiable on $\mathbb{R}^n$ with*
$$
\nabla f_\mu(x) = A^\top u_\mu(x), \qquad u_\mu(x) = \arg\max_{u \in Q_2}\big\{\langle Ax, u\rangle - \hat\phi(u) - \mu d_2(u)\big\},
$$
*and $\nabla f_\mu$ is Lipschitz with constant $L_\mu = \|A\|^2/(\mu\sigma_2)$. Moreover $f_\mu$ approximates $f$ uniformly,*
$$
f_\mu(x) \le f(x) \le f_\mu(x) + \mu D_2 \qquad \text{for all } x.
$$
*Running the optimal accelerated gradient method of [44.06.03] on $f_\mu$ over $Q_1$ for $N$ steps and choosing*
$$
\mu = \frac{2\|A\|}{N+1}\sqrt{\frac{D_1}{\sigma_1\sigma_2 D_2}}
$$
*yields*
$$
f(x_N) - f^\star \;\le\; \frac{4\|A\|}{N+1}\sqrt{\frac{D_1 D_2}{\sigma_1\sigma_2}} \;=\; O\!\left(\frac{1}{N}\right),
$$
*so $N = O(1/\varepsilon)$ iterations suffice for an $\varepsilon$-accurate value.*

**Proof.** *Differentiability and gradient formula.* Fix $x$. The inner objective $\psi_x(u) = \langle Ax, u\rangle - \hat\phi(u) - \mu d_2(u)$ is strongly concave in $u$ with modulus $\mu\sigma_2$ (since $\hat\phi$ is convex and $\mu d_2$ is $\mu\sigma_2$-strongly convex), so its maximiser $u_\mu(x)$ over the closed convex $Q_2$ is unique. The value $f_\mu(x) = \max_{u\in Q_2}\psi_x(u)$ is a maximum of functions affine in $x$, hence convex. Danskin's theorem for a unique maximiser gives differentiability with $\nabla f_\mu(x) = \nabla_x\psi_x(u)\big|_{u = u_\mu(x)} = A^\top u_\mu(x)$.

*Gradient Lipschitz constant.* Let $x, x' \in \mathbb{R}^n$ with maximisers $u = u_\mu(x)$, $u' = u_\mu(x')$. First-order optimality of the strongly concave inner problem on $Q_2$ gives, for the supergradient $A x - \nabla\hat\phi(u) - \mu\nabla d_2(u)$ pointing out of $Q_2$ at $u$,
$$
\langle Ax - \partial\hat\phi(u) - \mu\nabla d_2(u),\, u' - u\rangle \le 0, \qquad
\langle Ax' - \partial\hat\phi(u') - \mu\nabla d_2(u'),\, u - u'\rangle \le 0.
$$
Adding the two and using monotonicity of $\partial\hat\phi$ ($\langle \partial\hat\phi(u) - \partial\hat\phi(u'), u - u'\rangle \ge 0$) and $\mu\sigma_2$-strong monotonicity of $\mu\nabla d_2$,
$$
\langle A(x - x'),\, u - u'\rangle \ge \mu\sigma_2\|u - u'\|^2.
$$
By Cauchy-Schwarz the left side is $\le \|A\|\,\|x - x'\|\,\|u - u'\|$, so $\|u - u'\| \le \dfrac{\|A\|}{\mu\sigma_2}\|x - x'\|$. Then
$$
\|\nabla f_\mu(x) - \nabla f_\mu(x')\| = \|A^\top(u - u')\| \le \|A\|\,\|u - u'\| \le \frac{\|A\|^2}{\mu\sigma_2}\|x - x'\| = L_\mu\|x - x'\|.
$$

*Uniform approximation.* For every $x$, since $d_2 \ge 0$ on $Q_2$ (as $d_2(u_0) = 0$ is the minimum), subtracting $\mu d_2 \ge 0$ inside the max can only lower the value: $f_\mu(x) \le f(x)$. Conversely, evaluating the $f$-defining max at $u_\mu(x)$ and using $0 \le d_2 \le D_2$,
$$
f(x) \ge \langle Ax, u_\mu(x)\rangle - \hat\phi(u_\mu(x)) = f_\mu(x) + \mu d_2(u_\mu(x)) \le f_\mu(x) + \mu D_2,
$$
and taking the maximiser in $f$ gives $f(x) \le f_\mu(x) + \mu D_2$. Hence $f_\mu \le f \le f_\mu + \mu D_2$.

*Complexity balance.* Accelerated gradient on $f_\mu$ over $Q_1$ (the prox-function $d_1$ supplying the distance term) attains, after $N$ steps, $f_\mu(x_N) - f_\mu^\star \le \dfrac{2 L_\mu D_1}{\sigma_1(N+1)^2}$ — the $O(1/k^2)$ rate of [44.06.03] specialized to the $L_\mu$-smooth $f_\mu$, with $D_1/\sigma_1$ bounding the squared distance to the optimum via the outer prox-function. Pass the rate to $f$ using the approximation bound: $f(x_N) - f^\star \le \big(f_\mu(x_N) - f_\mu^\star\big) + \mu D_2 \le \dfrac{2 L_\mu D_1}{\sigma_1(N+1)^2} + \mu D_2$. Substitute $L_\mu = \|A\|^2/(\mu\sigma_2)$:
$$
f(x_N) - f^\star \le \frac{2\|A\|^2 D_1}{\mu\sigma_1\sigma_2(N+1)^2} + \mu D_2.
$$
The right side is convex in $\mu$ and minimized at $\mu^\star = \dfrac{2\|A\|}{N+1}\sqrt{\dfrac{D_1}{\sigma_1\sigma_2 D_2}}$, where both terms equal $\dfrac{2\|A\|}{N+1}\sqrt{\dfrac{D_1 D_2}{\sigma_1\sigma_2}}$, summing to the stated bound $\dfrac{4\|A\|}{N+1}\sqrt{\dfrac{D_1 D_2}{\sigma_1\sigma_2}}$. This is $O(1/N)$, so $N = O(1/\varepsilon)$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The two halves of the theorem — the $L_\mu = \|A\|^2/(\mu\sigma_2)$ smoothness from strong concavity of the regularized inner max, and the $\mu D_2$ uniform error — are the foundational reason structure beats the black-box barrier; this builds toward the excessive-gap technique and the entropy/$\ell_1$/$\ell_\infty$ smoothings of the Advanced results, where the same balance reappears with explicit prox-functions. This is exactly the conjugate/saddle representation of [44.02.03] with the inner variable regularized rather than the primal, and it is dual to the Moreau smoothing of [44.06.02], which regularizes the primal; putting these together, the central insight is that the per-step accuracy of the accelerated method [44.06.03] and the smoothing error are two error sources balanced at $\mu \sim \varepsilon$, which generalises the single-error analysis of smooth problems to the structured-nonsmooth class and appears again in primal-dual saddle-point solvers [44.06.04].

Take $f(x) = |x|$ written as $\max_{u\in[-1,1]}\{xu\}$, prox-function $d_2(u) = \tfrac12 u^2$ (so $\sigma_2 = 1$, $D_2 = \tfrac12$), $A = 1$ ($\|A\| = 1$). With $\mu = 0.1$, what is the gradient-Lipschitz constant $L_\mu$ and the worst-case approximation error $\mu D_2$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$L_\mu = \|A\|^2/(\mu\sigma_2)$ and the error bound is $\mu D_2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$L_\mu = 10$, error $= 0.05$.** $L_\mu = 1/(0.1\cdot 1) = 10$ and $\mu D_2 = 0.1\cdot 0.5 = 0.05$. Shrinking $\mu$ tenfold to $0.01$ would cut the error to $0.005$ but raise $L_\mu$ to $100$ — the conditioning-versus-accuracy trade-off the balance resolves.

</details>

**Exercise 2 (easy, true-false).**

True or false: Nesterov smoothing contradicts the $\Omega(1/\varepsilon^2)$ lower bound for nonsmooth convex minimization.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Recall what oracle model the lower bound assumes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**False.** The $\Omega(1/\varepsilon^2)$ bound of [44.06.01] holds for the black-box subgradient oracle. Smoothing uses the explicit max-representation $(A,\hat\phi,Q_2)$ — structural information outside the black-box model — so it is not subject to that lower bound. Feedback-correct: right — the structure is extra information, not a violation. Feedback-wrong: the bound's hypothesis (black-box oracle) is exactly what smoothing drops.

</details>

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Smooth the max-function $f(x) = \max_{1\le i\le m} x_i = \max_{u\in\Delta_m}\langle x, u\rangle$ ($\Delta_m$ the simplex) using the entropy prox-function $d_2(u) = \sum_i u_i\log u_i + \log m$. Derive the closed form of $f_\mu$ and $\nabla f_\mu$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Maximize $\langle x,u\rangle - \mu\sum_i u_i\log u_i$ over the simplex; the result is the log-sum-exp / softmax of [44.02.03] Exercise 6.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The regularized inner problem $\max_{u\in\Delta_m}\{\langle x,u\rangle - \mu(\sum_i u_i\log u_i + \log m)\}$ has maximiser $u_\mu(x)_i = e^{x_i/\mu}/\sum_j e^{x_j/\mu}$ (the softmax), giving
$$
f_\mu(x) = \mu\log\Big(\tfrac1m\sum_{i=1}^m e^{x_i/\mu}\Big) = \mu\log\sum_i e^{x_i/\mu} - \mu\log m,
$$
the temperature-$\mu$ log-sum-exp, and $\nabla f_\mu(x) = u_\mu(x) = \operatorname{softmax}(x/\mu)$. Here $\sigma_2 = 1$ (entropy is $1$-strongly convex in $\ell_1$ by Pinsker) and $D_2 = \log m$, so $L_\mu = \|A\|^2/\mu$ with $A = I$, and the error is $\mu\log m$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Smooth the $\ell_\infty$ norm $f(x) = \|x\|_\infty = \max_{\|u\|_1\le 1}\langle x, u\rangle$ with the squared-Euclidean prox-function on the $\ell_1$-ball. State $f_\mu$'s gradient-Lipschitz constant and approximation error in terms of $\mu$, $\sigma_2$, $D_2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\|x\|_\infty$ is the support function of the $\ell_1$ unit ball $Q_2 = \{\|u\|_1\le 1\}$; apply the theorem directly with $A = I$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $Q_2 = \{u : \|u\|_1\le 1\}$, $A = I$ ($\|A\| = 1$), and a $\sigma_2$-strongly convex $d_2$ on $Q_2$ with $D_2 = \max_{Q_2} d_2$, the smoothing $f_\mu(x) = \max_{\|u\|_1\le 1}\{\langle x,u\rangle - \mu d_2(u)\}$ has $\nabla f_\mu(x) = u_\mu(x)$, Lipschitz constant $L_\mu = 1/(\mu\sigma_2)$, and uniform error $\|x\|_\infty - \mu D_2 \le f_\mu(x) \le \|x\|_\infty$. Choosing $d_2(u) = \tfrac12\|u\|_2^2$ gives $\sigma_2 = 1$ and a thresholded-projection $u_\mu(x) = \operatorname{proj}_{\{\|u\|_1\le1\}}(x/\mu)$; the dual $\ell_1$-norm smoothing of $\|x\|_1 = \max_{\|u\|_\infty\le1}\langle x,u\rangle$ is the entrywise Huber computed in the Beginner worked example.

</details>

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify the balance step: minimize $h(\mu) = \tfrac{C_1}{\mu(N+1)^2} + C_2\mu$ over $\mu > 0$ and show both terms are equal at the optimum, where $C_1 = 2\|A\|^2 D_1/(\sigma_1\sigma_2)$ and $C_2 = D_2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set $h'(\mu) = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$h'(\mu) = -\tfrac{C_1}{\mu^2(N+1)^2} + C_2 = 0$ gives $\mu^2 = \tfrac{C_1}{C_2(N+1)^2}$, so $\mu^\star = \tfrac{1}{N+1}\sqrt{C_1/C_2}$. At $\mu^\star$ the first term is $\tfrac{C_1}{\mu^\star(N+1)^2} = \tfrac{C_1}{(N+1)^2}\cdot\tfrac{N+1}{\sqrt{C_1/C_2}} = \tfrac{\sqrt{C_1 C_2}}{N+1}$ and the second is $C_2\mu^\star = \tfrac{\sqrt{C_1 C_2}}{N+1}$ — equal, summing to $\tfrac{2\sqrt{C_1 C_2}}{N+1}$. Substituting $C_1, C_2$ recovers $\mu^\star = \tfrac{2\|A\|}{N+1}\sqrt{D_1/(\sigma_1\sigma_2 D_2)}$ and the total $\tfrac{4\|A\|}{N+1}\sqrt{D_1 D_2/(\sigma_1\sigma_2)}$.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the gradient-Lipschitz bound $\|\nabla f_\mu(x) - \nabla f_\mu(x')\| \le \tfrac{\|A\|^2}{\mu\sigma_2}\|x - x'\|$ from the strong concavity of the inner problem, filling in the monotonicity step.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Add the two first-order optimality inequalities at the maximisers $u = u_\mu(x)$, $u' = u_\mu(x')$; use monotonicity of $\partial\hat\phi$ and $\mu\sigma_2$-strong monotonicity of $\mu\nabla d_2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Optimality of $u$ for $\psi_x$ over $Q_2$: $\langle Ax - g - \mu\nabla d_2(u), u' - u\rangle \le 0$ with $g \in \partial\hat\phi(u)$; similarly $\langle Ax' - g' - \mu\nabla d_2(u'), u - u'\rangle \le 0$ with $g'\in\partial\hat\phi(u')$. Add: $\langle A(x - x'), u - u'\rangle \ge \langle g - g', u - u'\rangle + \mu\langle\nabla d_2(u) - \nabla d_2(u'), u - u'\rangle$. Convex-subgradient monotonicity gives $\langle g - g', u - u'\rangle \ge 0$, and $\mu\sigma_2$-strong convexity of $\mu d_2$ gives the second term $\ge \mu\sigma_2\|u - u'\|^2$. Hence $\langle A(x-x'), u - u'\rangle \ge \mu\sigma_2\|u-u'\|^2$. Cauchy-Schwarz on the left: $\|A\|\|x-x'\|\|u-u'\| \ge \mu\sigma_2\|u-u'\|^2$, so $\|u-u'\|\le\tfrac{\|A\|}{\mu\sigma_2}\|x-x'\|$. Then $\|\nabla f_\mu(x)-\nabla f_\mu(x')\| = \|A^\top(u-u')\| \le \|A\|\|u-u'\| \le \tfrac{\|A\|^2}{\mu\sigma_2}\|x-x'\|$.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The smoothing $f_\mu(x) = \max_{u\in Q_2}\{\langle Ax, u\rangle - \hat\phi(u) - \mu d_2(u)\}$ over $x \in Q_1$ admits a self-contained development: the structured complexity bound that beats the black-box barrier, the resolution of the apparent conflict with the nonsmooth lower bound, the excessive-gap primal-dual scheme that drops the need to fix $\mu$, the entropy smoothing as the canonical instance, and the placement against Moreau-Yosida smoothing.

**Theorem 1 (structured $O(1/\varepsilon)$ complexity).** With prox-functions $d_1, d_2$ of parameters $(\sigma_1, D_1)$, $(\sigma_2, D_2)$, the accelerated method on $f_\mu$ with $\mu = \tfrac{2\|A\|}{N+1}\sqrt{D_1/(\sigma_1\sigma_2 D_2)}$ produces $x_N \in Q_1$ with $f(x_N) - f^\star \le \tfrac{4\|A\|}{N+1}\sqrt{D_1 D_2/(\sigma_1\sigma_2)}$ [ref: TODO_REF Nesterov 2005]. Equivalently $N(\varepsilon) = \big\lceil 4\|A\|\sqrt{D_1 D_2/(\sigma_1\sigma_2)}/\varepsilon\big\rceil$ iterations reach value accuracy $\varepsilon$, each costing one gradient $A^\top u_\mu(x)$ — one inner maximization over $Q_2$. The complexity is $O(\|A\|/\varepsilon)$, linear in $\|A\|$ and in $1/\varepsilon$, against the $O(1/\varepsilon^2)$ of the subgradient method.

**Theorem 2 (structure versus the black-box lower bound).** The $\Omega(1/\varepsilon^2)$ first-order lower bound for the nonsmooth convex class [44.06.01] is an information bound in the **black-box oracle model**: the method is charged only with calls returning $f(x)$ and one subgradient, and the bound exhibits a function on which any such method is slow. Smoothing operates in a **structured model**: the triple $(A, \hat\phi, Q_2)$ and the prox-function $d_2$ are inputs, so the method can compute $f_\mu$ and its exact gradient $A^\top u_\mu(x)$ — information no subgradient oracle returns. The $O(1/\varepsilon)$ rate therefore lives outside the lower bound's hypothesis class; there is no contradiction, only a strictly more informative model [ref: TODO_REF Nesterov 2005]. The boundary between $\Theta(1/\varepsilon)$ and $\Theta(1/\varepsilon^2)$ is exactly the boundary between structured and black-box access.

**Theorem 3 (excessive-gap technique).** Fix smoothings of both the primal $f_{\mu_2}(x) = \max_{u}\{\langle Ax,u\rangle - \hat\phi(u) - \mu_2 d_2(u)\}$ and the dual $\phi_{\mu_1}(u) = \min_x\{\langle Ax, u\rangle + \hat f(x) + \mu_1 d_1(x)\}$, where $\hat f$ is the primal part. A pair $(x, u)$ satisfies the **excessive-gap condition** if $f_{\mu_2}(x) \le \phi_{\mu_1}(u)$. Nesterov's primal-dual scheme generates $(x_k, u_k)$ keeping the excessive-gap condition invariant while shrinking $\mu_1^{(k)}, \mu_2^{(k)} = O(1/k)$, yielding
$$
0 \le f(x_k) - \phi(u_k) \le \mu_1^{(k)} D_1 + \mu_2^{(k)} D_2 = O(1/k),
$$
a genuine primal-dual gap certificate of size $O(1/\varepsilon)$ in $k$, without committing $\mu$ in advance [ref: TODO_REF Nesterov 2005]. The scheme alternates a gradient step on the smoothed primal and a gradient step on the smoothed dual, each step decreasing the relevant $\mu$, so the method self-tunes the smoothing as it converges and returns a verifiable duality gap rather than only a primal value.

**Theorem 4 (entropy smoothing of the max / softmax).** For $f(x) = \max_{1\le i\le m} x_i = \max_{u\in\Delta_m}\langle x, u\rangle$ with entropy prox-function $d_2(u) = \sum_i u_i\log u_i + \log m$ ($\sigma_2 = 1$ in $\ell_1$ by Pinsker's inequality, $D_2 = \log m$), the smoothing is the temperature-$\mu$ log-sum-exp
$$
f_\mu(x) = \mu\log\sum_{i=1}^m e^{x_i/\mu} - \mu\log m, \qquad \nabla f_\mu(x) = \operatorname{softmax}(x/\mu),
$$
with $\|\nabla f_\mu(x) - \nabla f_\mu(x')\|_\infty$ governed by $L_\mu = 1/\mu$ and uniform error $\mu\log m$ [ref: TODO_REF Beck & Teboulle 2012]. The $\log m$ (rather than $m$) dependence of $D_2$ under the entropy prox is the discrete-simplex analogue of mirror descent's logarithmic dimension dependence; it is why softmax smoothing is the preferred relaxation of the max in high-dimensional structured-prediction and attention models.

**Theorem 5 (smoothing versus Moreau-Yosida).** The Moreau envelope $e_\gamma f = f\,\square\,\tfrac1{2\gamma}\|\cdot\|_2^2$ of [44.06.02] is the special case of conjugate smoothing in which the dual prox-function is the squared norm: $e_\gamma f(x) = \max_u\{\langle x, u\rangle - f^*(u) - \tfrac\gamma2\|u\|_2^2\}$, so Moreau smoothing regularizes the conjugate $f^*$ by $\tfrac\gamma2\|\cdot\|_2^2$ on all of $\mathbb{R}^n$ [ref: TODO_REF Beck & Teboulle 2012]. Nesterov smoothing generalizes this in two ways: the prox-function $d_2$ need not be the squared norm (entropy on a simplex, for instance), and the inner domain $Q_2$ may be a proper bounded set, so the prox-diameter $D_2$ and modulus $\sigma_2$ can be tuned to the geometry of $Q_2$. The Moreau route needs a computable $\operatorname{prox}_f$; the conjugate route needs only a computable inner maximization $u_\mu(x)$ — available whenever the max-representation is explicit, even when $\operatorname{prox}_f$ is not.

**Synthesis.** The smoothing $f_\mu$ is exactly the conjugate/saddle representation of [44.02.03] with the inner variable regularized by a prox-function, and this single move is the foundational reason the structured nonsmooth class falls to $O(1/\varepsilon)$: strong concavity of the regularized inner max delivers $\nabla f_\mu = A^\top u_\mu$ with $L_\mu = \|A\|^2/(\mu\sigma_2)$, the accelerated method [44.06.03] turns that smoothness into an $O(L_\mu/N^2)$ value gap, and balancing it against the $\mu D_2$ approximation error at $\mu\sim\varepsilon$ is what produces the rate. The two error sources — algorithmic and approximation — are dual faces of one trade-off, and putting these together is what beats the $\Omega(1/\varepsilon^2)$ black-box barrier of [44.06.01] without contradicting it: the lower bound is a black-box statement and smoothing is a structured method, so the central insight is that the complexity boundary is the boundary of the oracle model, not of convexity. Moreau-Yosida smoothing [44.06.02] is the squared-norm special case, so Nesterov smoothing generalises it; the excessive-gap technique is the primal-dual refinement that self-tunes $\mu$ and certifies a duality gap, and it is the bridge to the saddle-point and operator-splitting solvers [44.06.04] that attack the same min-max structure. The entropy smoothing realises all of this with the softmax, where the $\log m$ prox-diameter is dual to mirror descent's logarithmic geometry and the temperature parameter is exactly $\mu$.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (gradient of the smoothing).** *Under the standing hypotheses, $f_\mu$ is convex and differentiable with $\nabla f_\mu(x) = A^\top u_\mu(x)$, where $u_\mu(x)$ is the unique maximiser of the regularized inner problem.*

*Proof.* The inner objective $\psi_x(u) = \langle Ax, u\rangle - \hat\phi(u) - \mu d_2(u)$ is $\mu\sigma_2$-strongly concave (convex $\hat\phi$, $\sigma_2$-strongly convex $d_2$), so over the closed convex $Q_2$ it has a unique maximiser $u_\mu(x)$. As a pointwise maximum of functions affine in $x$, $f_\mu$ is convex. Danskin's theorem for a strongly concave inner problem with unique maximiser gives that $f_\mu$ is differentiable with $\nabla f_\mu(x) = \nabla_x\psi_x(u)|_{u=u_\mu(x)} = A^\top u_\mu(x)$, since $\psi_x$ depends on $x$ only through the bilinear term $\langle Ax, u\rangle$ whose $x$-gradient is $A^\top u$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (gradient Lipschitz constant).** *$\nabla f_\mu$ is Lipschitz with constant $L_\mu = \|A\|^2/(\mu\sigma_2)$.*

*Proof.* For $u = u_\mu(x)$, $u' = u_\mu(x')$, first-order optimality on $Q_2$ gives $\langle Ax - g - \mu\nabla d_2(u), u'-u\rangle\le0$ and $\langle Ax' - g' - \mu\nabla d_2(u'), u-u'\rangle\le0$ with $g\in\partial\hat\phi(u)$, $g'\in\partial\hat\phi(u')$. Summing and using $\langle g-g', u-u'\rangle\ge0$ (monotonicity of $\partial\hat\phi$) and $\langle\nabla d_2(u)-\nabla d_2(u'),u-u'\rangle\ge\sigma_2\|u-u'\|^2$ (strong convexity of $d_2$) gives $\langle A(x-x'),u-u'\rangle\ge\mu\sigma_2\|u-u'\|^2$. Cauchy-Schwarz bounds the left by $\|A\|\|x-x'\|\|u-u'\|$, so $\|u-u'\|\le\tfrac{\|A\|}{\mu\sigma_2}\|x-x'\|$, and $\|\nabla f_\mu(x)-\nabla f_\mu(x')\|=\|A^\top(u-u')\|\le\|A\|\|u-u'\|\le\tfrac{\|A\|^2}{\mu\sigma_2}\|x-x'\|$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (uniform approximation).** *For all $x$, $f_\mu(x)\le f(x)\le f_\mu(x)+\mu D_2$.*

*Proof.* Since $d_2(u)\ge d_2(u_0)=0$ on $Q_2$, subtracting $\mu d_2\ge0$ inside the inner max only decreases it, so $f_\mu(x)\le f(x)$. For the upper bound, $f(x)\ge\langle Ax,u_\mu(x)\rangle-\hat\phi(u_\mu(x))=f_\mu(x)+\mu d_2(u_\mu(x))$, and $d_2(u_\mu(x))\le D_2$, while taking the maximiser of the $f$-defining problem gives $f(x)\le\max_u\{\langle Ax,u\rangle-\hat\phi(u)-\mu d_2(u)\}+\mu\max_u d_2(u)=f_\mu(x)+\mu D_2$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 ($O(1/\varepsilon)$ complexity).** *Accelerated gradient on $f_\mu$ over $Q_1$ for $N$ steps with $\mu=\tfrac{2\|A\|}{N+1}\sqrt{D_1/(\sigma_1\sigma_2 D_2)}$ gives $f(x_N)-f^\star\le\tfrac{4\|A\|}{N+1}\sqrt{D_1 D_2/(\sigma_1\sigma_2)}$.*

*Proof.* The accelerated method [44.06.03] on the $L_\mu$-smooth $f_\mu$ with prox-function $d_1$ attains $f_\mu(x_N)-f_\mu^\star\le\tfrac{2L_\mu D_1}{\sigma_1(N+1)^2}$. By Proposition 3, $f(x_N)-f^\star\le(f_\mu(x_N)-f_\mu^\star)+\mu D_2\le\tfrac{2L_\mu D_1}{\sigma_1(N+1)^2}+\mu D_2$. Insert $L_\mu=\|A\|^2/(\mu\sigma_2)$ to get $\tfrac{2\|A\|^2 D_1}{\mu\sigma_1\sigma_2(N+1)^2}+\mu D_2$. Minimizing the convex-in-$\mu$ expression (Exercise 6) at $\mu^\star=\tfrac{2\|A\|}{N+1}\sqrt{D_1/(\sigma_1\sigma_2 D_2)}$ equalizes the two terms at $\tfrac{2\|A\|}{N+1}\sqrt{D_1 D_2/(\sigma_1\sigma_2)}$ each, summing to the bound. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (entropy smoothing closed form).** *For $f(x)=\max_i x_i=\max_{u\in\Delta_m}\langle x,u\rangle$ and $d_2(u)=\sum_i u_i\log u_i+\log m$, $f_\mu(x)=\mu\log\sum_i e^{x_i/\mu}-\mu\log m$ and $\nabla f_\mu(x)=\operatorname{softmax}(x/\mu)$, with $\sigma_2=1$ in $\ell_1$, $D_2=\log m$.*

*Proof.* The inner problem $\max_{u\in\Delta_m}\{\langle x,u\rangle-\mu\sum_i u_i\log u_i-\mu\log m\}$ has Lagrangian stationarity (over the simplex constraint $\sum_i u_i=1$, $u\ge0$) $x_i-\mu(\log u_i+1)-\nu=0$, giving $u_i\propto e^{x_i/\mu}$, normalised $u_\mu(x)_i=e^{x_i/\mu}/\sum_j e^{x_j/\mu}$ — the softmax. Substituting back, the entropy and linear terms combine to $f_\mu(x)=\mu\log\sum_j e^{x_j/\mu}-\mu\log m$ (the conjugate of the negative entropy is log-sum-exp, [44.02.03] Proposition 4). By Proposition 1, $\nabla f_\mu(x)=u_\mu(x)=\operatorname{softmax}(x/\mu)$. Pinsker's inequality makes the negative entropy $1$-strongly convex in $\|\cdot\|_1$, so $\sigma_2=1$; $D_2=\max_{u\in\Delta_m}(\sum_i u_i\log u_i+\log m)=\log m$ at a vertex. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (Moreau envelope as squared-norm smoothing).** *For proper closed convex $f$ with conjugate $f^*$, the Moreau envelope satisfies $e_\gamma f(x)=\max_u\{\langle x,u\rangle-f^*(u)-\tfrac\gamma2\|u\|_2^2\}$, i.e. it is the conjugate smoothing of $f$ with $A=\operatorname{Id}$, $\hat\phi=f^*$, $d_2=\tfrac12\|\cdot\|_2^2$.*

*Proof.* By definition $e_\gamma f(x)=\min_z\{f(z)+\tfrac1{2\gamma}\|x-z\|_2^2\}$. The infimal-convolution duality of [44.02.03] gives $(f\,\square\,\tfrac1{2\gamma}\|\cdot\|_2^2)^*=f^*+(\tfrac1{2\gamma}\|\cdot\|_2^2)^*=f^*+\tfrac\gamma2\|\cdot\|_2^2$, since the conjugate of $\tfrac1{2\gamma}\|\cdot\|_2^2$ is $\tfrac\gamma2\|\cdot\|_2^2$. Biconjugating (both sides closed convex), $e_\gamma f=f\,\square\,\tfrac1{2\gamma}\|\cdot\|_2^2=(f^*+\tfrac\gamma2\|\cdot\|_2^2)^*=\max_u\{\langle x,u\rangle-f^*(u)-\tfrac\gamma2\|u\|_2^2\}$. This is $f_\mu$ with $\mu=\gamma$, $\hat\phi=f^*$, $d_2=\tfrac12\|\cdot\|_2^2$ ($\sigma_2=1$), and $\nabla e_\gamma f(x)=u_\gamma(x)=\tfrac1\gamma(x-\operatorname{prox}_{\gamma f}(x))$ recovers the standard envelope gradient. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The smoothing $f_\mu(x)=\max_{u\in Q_2}\{\langle Ax,u\rangle-\hat\phi(u)-\mu d_2(u)\}$ is the conjugate/saddle representation of [44.02.03] with the inner (dual) variable regularized by a prox-function; the support-function and dual-norm conjugate pairs assembled there are exactly the max-representations smoothed here, and the softmax/log-sum-exp smoothing of the max is the temperature-$\mu$ instance of the negative-entropy/log-sum-exp conjugate pair proved in that unit.

- The outer engine is the accelerated gradient method of [44.06.03]: smoothing produces an $L_\mu$-smooth $f_\mu$ precisely so that the optimal $O(1/k^2)$ method applies, and the $O(1/\varepsilon)$ complexity is its $O(L_\mu/N^2)$ rate balanced against the smoothing error at $\mu\sim\varepsilon$. Without the accelerated rate the same balance would give only $O(1/\varepsilon^2)$ from a plain gradient method on $f_\mu$, so the acceleration is load-bearing.

- The optimality story is dual to the lower-bound theory of [44.06.01]: that unit proves the $\Omega(1/\varepsilon^2)$ black-box bound for nonsmooth convex minimization and shows the subgradient method attains it, while this unit shows that admitting the explicit max-structure as side information lets a structured method reach $O(1/\varepsilon)$ — the two results partition the nonsmooth landscape by oracle model, structured versus black-box, with the smoothing technique on the structured side.

- Nesterov smoothing generalizes the Moreau-Yosida / proximal smoothing of [44.06.02]: the Moreau envelope is the special case in which the dual prox-function is the squared norm and the inner domain is all of the space, so the proximal smoothing of that unit is the squared-norm conjugate smoothing, and the excessive-gap primal-dual scheme is the precursor of the saddle-point and operator-splitting solvers [44.06.04] that attack the same min-max structured model directly.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The smoothing technique was introduced by **Yurii Nesterov** in 2005 in *Smooth minimization of non-smooth functions* (Mathematical Programming 103(1), 127-152), which showed that a large class of nonsmooth convex problems with explicit max-structure can be solved in $O(1/\varepsilon)$ iterations rather than the $O(1/\varepsilon^2)$ dictated by the black-box subgradient lower bound of Nemirovski and Yudin [ref: TODO_REF Nesterov 2005]. The paper's resolution of the apparent conflict — that the lower bound governs the black-box oracle model while smoothing exploits known structure — clarified that complexity in optimization is a property of the access model, not of the function class alone. The same paper introduced the **excessive-gap technique** as a primal-dual scheme producing the rate with a verifiable duality-gap certificate and without fixing the smoothing parameter in advance.

The unifying abstraction — the notion of a **smoothable function** with the $\alpha/\mu$ versus $\beta\mu$ trade-off, covering both the conjugate smoothing and the Moreau envelope as instances — was given by **Amir Beck and Marc Teboulle** in 2012 in *Smoothing and First Order Methods: A Unified Framework* (SIAM Journal on Optimization 22(2), 557-580), who packaged the smoothing-then-FISTA combination (S-FISTA) and made the constant trade-off explicit for the $\ell_1$, $\ell_\infty$, hinge, and max smoothings [ref: TODO_REF Beck & Teboulle 2012]. The entropy smoothing of the max, equivalent to the temperature-controlled log-sum-exp, predates the optimization framing in the exponential-family and statistical-mechanics literature and connects the technique to the negative-entropy/log-sum-exp conjugate duality and to mirror descent's logarithmic geometry. Nesterov returned to the technique in the smoothing chapter of his *Lectures on Convex Optimization* [ref: TODO_REF Nesterov 2018].
