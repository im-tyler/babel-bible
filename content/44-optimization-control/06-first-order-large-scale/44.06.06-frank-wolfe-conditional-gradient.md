## Intuition [Beginner]

Many real problems ask you to find the best point while staying inside a fenced region: a budget you cannot exceed, weights that must add to one, a matrix that must stay "small" in a structured sense. The usual large-step method handles the fence by taking a free step and then projecting — snapping back to the nearest legal point. For some fences that snap is cheap. For others it is the most expensive part of the whole computation.

The Frank-Wolfe method never projects. Instead it asks a different, often much cheaper question at every step: "Looking only at the downhill direction right now, which single corner of the fenced region is most downhill?" Finding the best corner for a straight-line cost is a simple search, even when projecting would be costly. The method walks a little way from where it is toward that best corner, and repeats. Because it always heads for a corner, it is sometimes called the conditional-gradient method, and its key tool is named the linear-minimization oracle — the gadget that returns the most-downhill corner.

Two everyday pictures make the move concrete. If your fence is a triangle, the best corner is one of the three vertices, found by checking which one the slope points toward — no projection needed. If your fence is "weights that sum to one," the best corner puts all the weight on a single option, the one the slope likes most. So each step adds at most one new active ingredient to the answer.

That last fact is the quiet payoff. After a handful of steps the answer is a blend of only a handful of corners. The solution stays simple — sparse, made of few pieces — which is exactly what you want when the corners are sparse vectors or low-rank matrices. The method builds a good answer out of a short list of clean building blocks.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the fenced region as a polygon. At your current point the slope points some way downhill. The method does not step downhill freely; it finds the corner of the polygon the slope most favours, then slides part way toward that corner. Each step pulls toward a vertex, and the answer becomes a blend of the few vertices visited.

```
   project-then-step                 Frank-Wolfe (toward a corner)

   x_k                               x_k ------------------> corner s_k
    |  free downhill step             \      (best corner for
    v                                   \      the current slope)
   point outside fence                   \  slide PART way
    |  PROJECT back (costly)              v
    v                               x_{k+1} = blend of x_k and s_k
   x_{k+1} (nearest legal point)    (no projection ever needed)
```

| fenced region | the best corner (LMO) returns |
|---|---|
| weights summing to one (a simplex) | put all weight on the single best option |
| a budget $\|x\|_1 \le \tau$ ($\ell_1$ ball) | one signed coordinate at the budget limit |
| "small" matrices (nuclear-norm ball) | one rank-one piece from the top singular direction |

## Worked example [Beginner]

We run two Frank-Wolfe steps by hand. The cost is the simple bowl $f(x) = \tfrac12\big((x-3)^2 + (y-3)^2\big)$ on the plane, whose lowest point is at $(3,3)$. The fence is the triangle with corners $A=(0,0)$, $B=(2,0)$, $C=(0,2)$. The point $(3,3)$ sits outside the triangle, so the best legal answer lies on the fence.

**Step 1. Start at a corner.** Begin at $x_0 = A = (0,0)$. The slope of the bowl at a point pushes away from the bottom; at $(0,0)$ the most-downhill direction points toward $(3,3)$. Check the three corners: heading from $(0,0)$, corner $B=(2,0)$ and corner $C=(0,2)$ are both equally downhill, and either beats staying at $A$. Pick $s_0 = B = (2,0)$.

**Step 2. Slide part way.** The first step size is $\tfrac{2}{0+2} = 1$, so we go all the way to the corner: $x_1 = (0,0) + 1\cdot\big((2,0)-(0,0)\big) = (2,0)$.

**Step 3. Find the next corner.** At $x_1 = (2,0)$ the bottom $(3,3)$ is still up and to the right. The most-downhill corner is now $C=(0,2)$ versus $A$ and $B$; the slope favours moving toward more height in $y$, so $s_1 = C = (0,2)$.

**Step 4. Slide a smaller amount.** The second step size is $\tfrac{2}{1+2} = \tfrac23$. So $x_2 = (1-\tfrac23)(2,0) + \tfrac23(0,2) = (\tfrac23, \tfrac43)$.

**What this tells us.** No projection was ever computed — each step only compared three corners and averaged. The answer $x_2 = (\tfrac23,\tfrac43)$ is already a blend of two corners and is creeping toward the best edge of the triangle. Shorter slides later on let the blend settle onto the true constrained minimum.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is a nonempty compact convex set and $f : D \to \mathbb{R}$ is convex and continuously differentiable, with $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ the Euclidean inner product and $\|\cdot\|$ its norm. The problem is $\min_{x \in D} f(x)$, with optimal value $f^\star = f(x^\star)$ for some minimiser $x^\star \in D$ (which exists by compactness and continuity). Convex sets, extreme points, and the supporting-hyperplane picture are as in [44.01.01].

**Definition (linear-minimization oracle).** The **linear-minimization oracle** (LMO) for $D$ returns a minimiser of a linear functional over $D$:
$$
\operatorname{LMO}_D(c) \in \arg\min_{s \in D}\, \langle s, c\rangle.
$$
Because a linear function over a compact convex set attains its minimum at an extreme point, $\operatorname{LMO}_D(c)$ may always be taken to be a vertex (extreme point) of $D$. The LMO replaces the Euclidean projection $P_D$ used by projected and proximal-gradient methods [44.06.02]; for many structured sets it is far cheaper, since it is a linear program (over a polytope) or a single eigen/singular-vector computation (over a spectral ball).

**Definition (Frank-Wolfe / conditional-gradient method).** Given $x_0 \in D$, the **Frank-Wolfe method** iterates
$$
s_k = \operatorname{LMO}_D\big(\nabla f(x_k)\big) = \arg\min_{s \in D}\langle s, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle,
\qquad
x_{k+1} = (1 - \gamma_k)\, x_k + \gamma_k\, s_k,
$$
with step size $\gamma_k \in [0,1]$ chosen by the open-loop schedule $\gamma_k = \tfrac{2}{k+2}$, by exact line search $\gamma_k = \arg\min_{\gamma \in [0,1]} f\big(x_k + \gamma(s_k - x_k)\big)$, or by the short-step rule. Since $x_{k+1}$ is a convex combination of $x_k \in D$ and $s_k \in D$, every iterate stays in $D$: feasibility is maintained without projection. The direction $s_k - x_k$ minimises the **linearisation** $f(x_k) + \langle \nabla f(x_k), s - x_k\rangle$ of $f$ over $D$, which is the defining "conditional gradient" idea.

**Definition (curvature constant).** The **curvature constant** of $f$ over $D$ measures how much $f$ exceeds its own linearisation along feasible chords:
$$
C_f = \sup_{\substack{x, s \in D,\ \gamma \in (0,1] \\ y = x + \gamma(s - x)}} \frac{2}{\gamma^2}\Big( f(y) - f(x) - \langle y - x, \nabla f(x)\rangle \Big).
$$
If $\nabla f$ is $L$-Lipschitz on $D$ then $C_f \le L\,\operatorname{diam}(D)^2$, so $C_f$ is finite whenever $f$ is smooth on the compact $D$. The constant $C_f$ is **affine invariant**: it is unchanged under any invertible affine reparametrisation of the variables, matching the affine invariance of the method itself.

**Definition (Frank-Wolfe / duality gap).** At any $x \in D$ the **Frank-Wolfe gap** (or duality gap) is
$$
g(x) = \max_{s \in D}\, \langle x - s, \nabla f(x)\rangle = \langle x - s_{\mathrm{LMO}}, \nabla f(x)\rangle, \qquad s_{\mathrm{LMO}} = \operatorname{LMO}_D(\nabla f(x)).
$$
It is computed for free as a by-product of the LMO call. By convexity it is an **upper bound on the suboptimality**, $g(x) \ge f(x) - f^\star \ge 0$, and $g(x) = 0$ exactly when $x$ satisfies the first-order optimality condition $\langle \nabla f(x), s - x\rangle \ge 0$ for all $s \in D$. It is therefore a computable optimality certificate and stopping criterion.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"The LMO returns a point near $x_k$."* It returns a vertex of $D$, typically far from $x_k$; the step size $\gamma_k$, not the oracle, controls how far you actually move. The early step $\gamma_0 = 1$ jumps all the way to a vertex.

- *"Frank-Wolfe is just projected gradient with a cheaper projection."* It solves a *linear* subproblem (minimise $\langle s, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle$ over $D$), not the *quadratic* subproblem $\min_{s\in D}\|s - (x_k - t\nabla f(x_k))\|^2$ that projection solves. The two subproblems have different minimisers and different costs; the FW direction can be nearly orthogonal to the projected-gradient direction near a constrained optimum.

- *"The rate $C_f$ is the Lipschitz constant of $\nabla f$."* It is bounded by $L\operatorname{diam}(D)^2$ but is generally smaller and, unlike $L$, is affine invariant. Rescaling the variables changes $L$ but leaves $C_f$ and the entire FW trajectory unchanged.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The convergence rests on one per-step descent inequality driven by the curvature constant, fed into a deterministic recursion on the suboptimality. The Frank-Wolfe gap supplies both the descent term and a certificate that brackets the unknown $f^\star$.

**Theorem (curvature descent and the $O(1/k)$ rate).** *Let $D$ be compact convex, $f$ convex and differentiable with finite curvature constant $C_f$, and run Frank-Wolfe with $\gamma_k = \tfrac{2}{k+2}$. Then:*

*(i) (Descent inequality.) For each $k$ and any $\gamma_k \in [0,1]$,*
$$
f(x_{k+1}) \le f(x_k) - \gamma_k\, g(x_k) + \frac{\gamma_k^2}{2}\, C_f. \tag{$\dagger$}
$$

*(ii) ($O(1/k)$ rate.) With $\gamma_k = \tfrac{2}{k+2}$, the suboptimality $h_k = f(x_k) - f^\star$ satisfies*
$$
h_k \le \frac{2 C_f}{k + 2}.
$$

*(iii) (Gap certificate.) $\min_{0 \le i \le k} g(x_i) \le \tfrac{1}{k+1}\sum_{i=0}^{k} g(x_i)$ is $O(1/k)$, so the freely computed gaps also converge to zero at rate $O(1/k)$ and certify suboptimality.*

**Proof.** *(i)* Write $d_k = s_k - x_k$, so $x_{k+1} = x_k + \gamma_k d_k$. By the definition of the curvature constant with $x = x_k$, $s = s_k$, $y = x_{k+1}$,
$$
f(x_{k+1}) \le f(x_k) + \gamma_k\langle d_k, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle + \frac{\gamma_k^2}{2} C_f.
$$
Now $s_k$ minimises $\langle s, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle$ over $D$, so $\langle d_k, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle = \langle s_k - x_k, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle = -\,g(x_k)$ by the definition of the Frank-Wolfe gap. Substituting gives $(\dagger)$.

*(ii)* By convexity and the definition of $g$,
$$
g(x_k) = \langle x_k - s_k, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle \ge \langle x_k - x^\star, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle \ge f(x_k) - f^\star = h_k,
$$
the first inequality because $s_k$ minimises $\langle s, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle$ over $D \ni x^\star$, the second by the gradient inequality for convex $f$. Insert $g(x_k) \ge h_k$ into $(\dagger)$ and subtract $f^\star$:
$$
h_{k+1} \le h_k - \gamma_k h_k + \frac{\gamma_k^2}{2} C_f = (1 - \gamma_k) h_k + \frac{\gamma_k^2}{2} C_f. \tag{R}
$$
We show $h_k \le \tfrac{2 C_f}{k+2}$ by induction. For $k = 0$, $(R)$ with $\gamma_0 = 1$ gives $h_1 \le \tfrac12 C_f \le \tfrac{2 C_f}{3}$; for the base index $k = 0$ itself the bound $h_0 \le \tfrac{2C_f}{2} = C_f$ holds because $(\dagger)$ at $k=0$ shows $f$ decreases from $x_0$ and, more directly, $h_0 = g(x_0) - \langle x_0 - x^\star,\nabla f(x_0)\rangle \le g(x_0) \le C_f$ when $\gamma=1$ is admissible. Assume $h_k \le \tfrac{2 C_f}{k+2}$. With $\gamma_k = \tfrac{2}{k+2}$, $(R)$ gives
$$
h_{k+1} \le \Big(1 - \frac{2}{k+2}\Big)\frac{2 C_f}{k+2} + \frac{1}{2}\Big(\frac{2}{k+2}\Big)^2 C_f
= \frac{2 C_f}{k+2}\Big(\frac{k}{k+2} + \frac{1}{k+2}\Big) = \frac{2 C_f}{k+2}\cdot\frac{k+1}{k+2}.
$$
Since $\tfrac{k+1}{k+2} = \tfrac{k+1}{k+2} \le \tfrac{k+2}{k+3}$ (cross-multiplying, $(k+1)(k+3) = k^2 + 4k + 3 \le k^2 + 4k + 4 = (k+2)^2$), we get $h_{k+1} \le \tfrac{2 C_f}{k+2}\cdot\tfrac{k+2}{k+3} = \tfrac{2 C_f}{k+3}$, completing the induction.

*(iii)* Rearrange $(\dagger)$ to $\gamma_k g(x_k) \le h_k - h_{k+1} + \tfrac{\gamma_k^2}{2}C_f$. Summing $i = 0,\dots,k$ telescopes the $h_i$ terms: $\sum_{i=0}^{k}\gamma_i g(x_i) \le h_0 + \tfrac{C_f}{2}\sum_{i=0}^{k}\gamma_i^2$. With $\gamma_i = \tfrac{2}{i+2}$ the right side is $O(1)$ in the leading order divided against $\sum\gamma_i = \Theta(\log k)$ growth; the standard refinement weighting by $\gamma_i$ yields $\min_{0\le i\le k} g(x_i) \le \tfrac{2 C_f}{k+1}$ for $k \ge 1$ (the gap at the best iterate inherits the $O(1/k)$ rate of the suboptimality). Thus the freely available gaps certify $f(x_i) - f^\star \le g(x_i)$ down to $O(1/k)$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The single inequality $(\dagger)$ is the foundational reason the method converges, and it builds toward the away-step and pairwise variants of the rest of the chapter, where the same curvature descent is reused after enlarging the set of admissible directions. This is exactly the conditional-gradient analogue of the proximal-gradient sufficient-decrease lemma of [44.06.02]: where that argument folds the nonsmooth term into a quadratic comparison via its prox, here the linear oracle replaces the quadratic projection and the curvature constant $C_f$ replaces the smoothness $L$, so the proof generalises projected gradient to projection-free first-order optimisation with the same $O(1/k)$ order. The Frank-Wolfe gap $g(x)$ appears again in the linear-rate theory and in stopping rules, and the central insight — that minimising the linearisation over $D$ both supplies a feasible descent direction and computes a free suboptimality certificate — is dual to the projection viewpoint: putting these together, the method trades the cheaper linear subproblem for the loss of the accelerated $O(1/k^2)$ rate that projection-based acceleration [44.06.03] achieves.

Use the descent lemma $f(y) \le f(x) + \langle\nabla f(x), y-x\rangle + \tfrac{L}{2}\|y-x\|^2$ and $y - x = \gamma(s-x)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The descent lemma for $L$-smooth $f$ gives $f(y) - f(x) - \langle y-x,\nabla f(x)\rangle \le \tfrac{L}{2}\|y-x\|^2$. With $y - x = \gamma(s-x)$, the right side is $\tfrac{L}{2}\gamma^2\|s-x\|^2 \le \tfrac{L}{2}\gamma^2\operatorname{diam}(D)^2$. Multiplying the curvature integrand by $\tfrac{2}{\gamma^2}$ gives $\tfrac{2}{\gamma^2}\cdot\tfrac{L}{2}\gamma^2\operatorname{diam}(D)^2 = L\operatorname{diam}(D)^2$, a uniform bound over $x, s, \gamma$, so the supremum $C_f \le L\operatorname{diam}(D)^2$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

For $D = \Delta_n = \{x \ge 0 : \sum_i x_i = 1\}$ the probability simplex, show the LMO is $\operatorname{LMO}_{\Delta_n}(c) = e_{j^\star}$ with $j^\star = \arg\min_j c_j$, the indicator of the smallest coordinate of $c$. Conclude the iterate sparsity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A linear function over the simplex is minimised at a vertex; the vertices of $\Delta_n$ are the standard basis vectors $e_j$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $x \in \Delta_n$, $\langle x, c\rangle = \sum_i x_i c_i \ge \min_j c_j$ since $x$ is a convex combination, with equality at $x = e_{j^\star}$, $j^\star = \arg\min_j c_j$. So $\operatorname{LMO}_{\Delta_n}(c) = e_{j^\star}$, a single coordinate. Each FW step adds at most one new nonzero coordinate to $x_k$, so after $k$ steps $x_k$ has at most $k+1$ nonzeros: the iterates are sparse, matching the $k+1$-atom convex-combination bound.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

For $D = \{X \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n} : \|X\|_* \le \tau\}$ the nuclear-norm ball (sum of singular values bounded), show $\operatorname{LMO}_D(G) = -\tau\, u_1 v_1^\top$, where $(u_1, v_1)$ is the top left/right singular-vector pair of $G$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The vertices of the nuclear-norm ball are rank-one matrices $\tau\, uv^\top$ with unit $u, v$; minimise $\langle X, G\rangle = \operatorname{tr}(X^\top G)$ over them.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The extreme points of the nuclear-norm ball of radius $\tau$ are the rank-one matrices $\tau\, uv^\top$ with $\|u\| = \|v\| = 1$. For such $X$, $\langle X, G\rangle = \tau\operatorname{tr}(v u^\top G) = \tau\, u^\top G v$, minimised over unit $u, v$ at $u^\top G v = -\sigma_{\max}(G)$, attained by the top singular pair with a sign flip: $u = u_1$, $v = v_1$ give $u_1^\top G v_1 = \sigma_1$, so the minimiser is $X = -\tau\, u_1 v_1^\top$. Thus the LMO is a single top-singular-vector computation — far cheaper than the full SVD a projection onto the nuclear-norm ball would need, and each step adds one rank-one atom, keeping iterates low-rank.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the recursion $h_{k+1} \le (1 - \gamma_k) h_k + \tfrac{\gamma_k^2}{2}C_f$ implies $h_k \le \tfrac{2C_f}{k+2}$ for $\gamma_k = \tfrac{2}{k+2}$, supplying the induction step in full.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Assume $h_k \le \tfrac{2C_f}{k+2}$. With $\gamma_k = \tfrac{2}{k+2}$, $1 - \gamma_k = \tfrac{k}{k+2}$ and $\tfrac{\gamma_k^2}{2} = \tfrac{2}{(k+2)^2}$, so

$$
Since $(k+1)(k+3) = k^2 + 4k + 3 \le k^2 + 4k + 4 = (k+2)^2$, we have $\tfrac{k+1}{(k+2)^2} \le \tfrac{1}{k+3}$, hence $h_{k+1} \le \tfrac{2C_f}{k+3}$. The base case $h_0 \le \tfrac{2C_f}{2} = C_f$ holds since one full step $\gamma = 1$ is admissible from $x_0$, closing the induction.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that vanilla Frank-Wolfe cannot converge linearly in general by exhibiting the obstruction: when $x^\star$ lies in the relative interior of a face of a polytope $D$, the FW direction $s_k - x_k$ becomes asymptotically orthogonal to $-\nabla f(x_k)$, so the per-step decrease $\gamma_k g(x_k)$ degrades. State qualitatively how away-steps repair this.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Near such an $x^\star$, the LMO vertex $s_k$ and the iterate $x_k$ straddle the optimal face, so $g(x_k) = \langle x_k - s_k, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle$ shrinks faster than $\|x_k - x^\star\|$, forcing tiny effective progress.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $x^\star$ lies in the relative interior of a proper face $F$ of the polytope, the optimal active vertices all lie on $F$, but as $x_k \to x^\star$ the LMO still returns a vertex $s_k$ of $F$ whose direction $s_k - x_k$ runs *along* the face, nearly orthogonal to $\nabla f(x_k)$ (which is nearly normal to $F$ by optimality). Then $g(x_k) = \langle x_k - s_k, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle = \Theta(\|x_k - x^\star\|^2)$ while $\|x_k - x^\star\|$ itself is only $\Theta(\|x_k - x^\star\|)$, so the descent $\gamma_k g(x_k)$ is too small to halve the distance at a fixed rate — the iterates zig-zag and converge at $\Theta(1/k)$, not geometrically. Away-step FW adds the move *away* from the worst currently-active vertex (the away direction $x_k - v_k$, $v_k = \arg\max_{v \in S_k}\langle v, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle$ over the active set $S_k$), which lets the method drop weight from a bad atom and so realign with $-\nabla f$; over a polytope with strongly convex $f$ this restores a linear rate $(1-\rho)^k$ with $\rho$ set by the polytope's pyramidal width.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The Frank-Wolfe method is $s_k = \operatorname{LMO}_D(\nabla f(x_k))$, $x_{k+1} = (1-\gamma_k)x_k + \gamma_k s_k$, and the results below sharpen the rate's tightness, certify optimality through the gap, characterise the atomic sparsity of iterates, and recover linear convergence by enlarging the direction set.

**Theorem 1 (affine invariance).** The Frank-Wolfe iterates, the curvature constant $C_f$, and the convergence bound $h_k \le \tfrac{2 C_f}{k+2}$ are invariant under any invertible affine reparametrisation $x = M\tilde x + b$. Replacing $f$ by $\tilde f(\tilde x) = f(M\tilde x + b)$ and $D$ by $\tilde D = M^{-1}(D - b)$, the LMO commutes with the affine map, the iterates correspond exactly, and $C_{\tilde f} = C_f$ because the curvature integrand $f(y) - f(x) - \langle y-x,\nabla f(x)\rangle$ and the feasible chords transform consistently [ref: TODO_REF Jaggi, M. — Revisiting Frank-Wolfe: Projection-Free Sparse Convex Optimization]. No norm or inner product enters the convergence statement, in contrast to projection-based methods whose rate depends on the metric through $L$ and the projection; this is why $C_f$, not the Lipschitz constant, is the structurally correct rate constant.

**Theorem 2 (duality-gap certificate and stopping).** For every $x \in D$, the Frank-Wolfe gap $g(x) = \max_{s\in D}\langle x-s,\nabla f(x)\rangle$ satisfies $g(x) \ge f(x) - f^\star \ge 0$ with equality $g(x) = 0$ iff $x$ is a constrained minimiser, and it is obtained for free from the LMO call that the iteration already performs [ref: TODO_REF Jaggi, M. — Revisiting Frank-Wolfe: Projection-Free Sparse Convex Optimization]. Consequently the algorithm carries its own a-posteriori error bound: stopping when $g(x_k) \le \varepsilon$ guarantees $f(x_k) - f^\star \le \varepsilon$. The averaged/best gap converges at $O(1/k)$, the same order as the suboptimality, so the certificate is never asymptotically weaker than the rate it certifies; $g(x)$ is precisely the Wolfe dual gap for the conic/linear reformulation, the convex-duality object specialised to the LMO.

**Theorem 3 (sparsity and atomic decomposition).** If $x_0$ is a vertex of $D$, then after $k$ Frank-Wolfe steps $x_k$ is a convex combination of at most $k+1$ vertices (atoms) of $D$, since each step appends a single LMO vertex $s_k$ and reweights. For atomic-norm balls — the $\ell_1$ ball (atoms: signed unit coordinates), the simplex (atoms: basis vectors), the nuclear-norm ball (atoms: rank-one matrices) — this means $x_k$ is $(k+1)$-sparse, $(k+1)$-sparse, or rank $\le k+1$ respectively [ref: TODO_REF Jaggi, M. — Revisiting Frank-Wolfe: Projection-Free Sparse Convex Optimization]. The method thus produces a controlled atomic decomposition $x_k = \sum_{i} \alpha_i a_i$ with few atoms $a_i$ and $\sum_i \alpha_i = 1$, $\alpha_i \ge 0$: the iteration count directly bounds the model complexity, and the $O(1/k)$ rate becomes a sparsity-accuracy trade-off in which $k$ atoms buy accuracy $O(1/k)$.

**Theorem 4 (tightness of the $O(1/k)$ rate).** The bound $h_k \le \tfrac{2 C_f}{k+2}$ is order-optimal for the vanilla method: there are convex smooth instances over polytopes on which $h_k = \Omega(1/k)$, so no faster rate holds without modifying the algorithm [ref: TODO_REF Lacoste-Julien, S. & Jaggi, M. — On the Global Linear Convergence of Frank-Wolfe Optimization Variants]. The obstruction is geometric: when $x^\star$ lies in the relative interior of a face, the FW direction $s_k - x_k$ becomes nearly orthogonal to $-\nabla f(x_k)$, so the gap $g(x_k) = \Theta(\|x_k - x^\star\|^2)$ while the distance is $\Theta(\|x_k - x^\star\|)$, forcing the iterates to zig-zag toward the optimal face. This is the structural reason vanilla FW cannot match the accelerated $O(1/k^2)$ of projection-based methods [44.06.03], and the reason away-steps are needed for a linear rate.

**Theorem 5 (away-steps and pairwise FW: linear convergence over polytopes).** Maintaining an active set $S_k$ of vertices whose convex hull contains $x_k$, the **away-step** variant computes, alongside the FW vertex $s_k$, the away vertex $v_k = \arg\max_{v\in S_k}\langle v, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle$ and moves along whichever of the FW direction $s_k - x_k$ or the away direction $x_k - v_k$ has larger inner product with $-\nabla f(x_k)$, with a step capped so that no atom weight goes negative; the **pairwise** variant shifts mass directly from $v_k$ to $s_k$. For $\mu$-strongly convex $L$-smooth $f$ over a polytope $D$, both attain a linear rate $h_{k} \le h_0\,(1 - \rho)^{k}$ with $\rho = \Theta\!\big(\tfrac{\mu}{L}\cdot\tfrac{\delta^2}{\operatorname{diam}(D)^2}\big)$, where $\delta$ is the **pyramidal width** (a vertex/facial-geometry constant of the polytope) [ref: TODO_REF Lacoste-Julien, S. & Jaggi, M. — On the Global Linear Convergence of Frank-Wolfe Optimization Variants]. The away step lets weight on a poorly chosen atom shrink, breaking the zig-zag of Theorem 4; the geometric quantity $\delta$ replaces the projection-based contraction of [44.06.02], and the LMO remains the only oracle.

**Synthesis.** The conditional-gradient method is exactly projected/proximal first-order optimisation [44.06.02] with the metric projection replaced by a linear-minimization oracle, and this single substitution is the foundational reason the whole apparatus is affine invariant: the curvature constant $C_f$, not the Lipschitz constant $L$, is the rate constant because the LMO and the chord difference transform consistently under affine maps, and the descent inequality $(\dagger)$ is the conditional-gradient face of the sufficient-decrease lemma. The Frank-Wolfe gap $g(x) = \langle x - s_{\mathrm{LMO}}, \nabla f(x)\rangle$ is dual to the projection residual: it is the Wolfe duality gap read off the LMO, simultaneously the descent term in $(\dagger)$ and a free certificate $g(x) \ge f(x) - f^\star$, so the method carries its own a-posteriori error bound. The central insight is that minimising the *linearisation* over $D$ keeps every iterate a short convex combination of vertices, giving the atomic-sparsity bound — $k$ atoms buy accuracy $O(1/k)$ — which is exactly the structure that makes the method the solver of choice for nuclear-norm and atomic-norm constraints where projection is an SVD and the LMO is a single singular pair.

The $O(1/k)$ rate is tight for the vanilla method because the optimum on a face turns the FW direction orthogonal to the gradient; putting these together, the away-step and pairwise variants enlarge the direction set to break that degeneracy and recover a linear rate $(1-\rho)^k$ governed by the polytope's pyramidal width, generalising the projection-based linear rate to the projection-free setting. This is exactly the trade the chapter makes: the cheaper linear oracle and sparse iterates in exchange for forgoing the accelerated $O(1/k^2)$ of [44.06.03], with the gap and the active set as the bridge to the splitting and stochastic methods [44.06.04] that reuse the same per-step certificate.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (descent inequality).** *Let $f$ be convex differentiable with finite curvature constant $C_f$ over the compact convex $D$. For the Frank-Wolfe step $x_{k+1} = x_k + \gamma_k(s_k - x_k)$ with $s_k = \operatorname{LMO}_D(\nabla f(x_k))$ and $\gamma_k \in [0,1]$, $f(x_{k+1}) \le f(x_k) - \gamma_k g(x_k) + \tfrac{\gamma_k^2}{2}C_f$.*

*Proof.* Set $y = x_{k+1} = x_k + \gamma_k(s_k - x_k)$, $x = x_k$, $s = s_k$, $\gamma = \gamma_k$ in the curvature-constant definition: $f(y) - f(x) - \langle y - x, \nabla f(x)\rangle \le \tfrac{\gamma^2}{2}C_f$, i.e. $f(x_{k+1}) \le f(x_k) + \gamma_k\langle s_k - x_k, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle + \tfrac{\gamma_k^2}{2}C_f$. By definition of the LMO, $s_k$ minimises $\langle s, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle$ over $D$, so $\langle s_k - x_k, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle = -\max_{s\in D}\langle x_k - s, \nabla f(x_k)\rangle = -g(x_k)$. Substituting gives the claim. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (gap dominates suboptimality).** *For convex $f$ and any $x \in D$, $g(x) \ge f(x) - f^\star \ge 0$, with $g(x) = 0$ iff $x \in \arg\min_{D} f$.*

*Proof.* Let $s_x = \operatorname{LMO}_D(\nabla f(x))$. Since $x^\star \in D$ and $s_x$ minimises $\langle s, \nabla f(x)\rangle$ over $D$, $\langle x - s_x, \nabla f(x)\rangle \ge \langle x - x^\star, \nabla f(x)\rangle$. Convexity gives $f(x^\star) \ge f(x) + \langle\nabla f(x), x^\star - x\rangle$, i.e. $\langle x - x^\star, \nabla f(x)\rangle \ge f(x) - f^\star$. Chaining, $g(x) = \langle x - s_x, \nabla f(x)\rangle \ge f(x) - f^\star \ge 0$. If $g(x) = 0$ then $\langle x - s, \nabla f(x)\rangle \le 0$ for all $s \in D$, i.e. $\langle\nabla f(x), s - x\rangle \ge 0$ for all $s \in D$, the first-order optimality condition over $D$, equivalent for convex $f$ to $x \in \arg\min_D f$; conversely optimality forces $g(x) = 0$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 ($O(1/k)$ rate).** *Under Propositions 1-2 with $\gamma_k = \tfrac{2}{k+2}$, $h_k = f(x_k) - f^\star \le \tfrac{2 C_f}{k+2}$.*

*Proof.* Proposition 1 with $g(x_k) \ge h_k$ (Proposition 2) gives the recursion $h_{k+1} \le (1 - \gamma_k)h_k + \tfrac{\gamma_k^2}{2}C_f$. Base case: at $k=0$, $\gamma_0 = 1$ is admissible, and Proposition 1 with $g(x_0) \ge h_0 \ge 0$ together with $h_0 = f(x_0) - f^\star \le g(x_0) \le \sup_{x,s,\gamma}\tfrac{2}{\gamma^2}(\cdots) \cdot \tfrac12 \le C_f$ gives $h_0 \le C_f = \tfrac{2C_f}{0+2}$. Induction step: assuming $h_k \le \tfrac{2C_f}{k+2}$, substitute $\gamma_k = \tfrac{2}{k+2}$:
$$
h_{k+1} \le \frac{k}{k+2}\cdot\frac{2C_f}{k+2} + \frac{2}{(k+2)^2}C_f = \frac{2C_f(k+1)}{(k+2)^2} \le \frac{2C_f}{k+3},
$$
the last step from $(k+1)(k+3) \le (k+2)^2$. Hence $h_k \le \tfrac{2C_f}{k+2}$ for all $k$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (affine invariance).** *Let $M$ be invertible, $\tilde f(\tilde x) = f(M\tilde x + b)$, $\tilde D = M^{-1}(D - b)$. Frank-Wolfe on $(\tilde f, \tilde D)$ from $\tilde x_0 = M^{-1}(x_0 - b)$ produces $\tilde x_k = M^{-1}(x_k - b)$, and $C_{\tilde f} = C_f$.*

*Proof.* $\nabla\tilde f(\tilde x) = M^\top\nabla f(M\tilde x + b)$. The LMO over $\tilde D$ minimises $\langle s, \nabla\tilde f(\tilde x)\rangle = \langle s, M^\top\nabla f(x)\rangle = \langle Ms, \nabla f(x)\rangle$ over $s \in \tilde D$, i.e. over $Ms + b \in D$; the minimiser $\tilde s$ satisfies $M\tilde s + b = \operatorname{LMO}_D(\nabla f(x)) = s_k$, so the oracle commutes with the map. The convex update $\tilde x_{k+1} = (1-\gamma_k)\tilde x_k + \gamma_k\tilde s_k$ maps to $x_{k+1} = (1-\gamma_k)x_k + \gamma_k s_k$ under $x = M\tilde x + b$ by affinity, so iterates correspond and the schedule $\gamma_k$ is shared. For the curvature constant, with $\tilde y = \tilde x + \gamma(\tilde s - \tilde x)$ mapping to $y = x + \gamma(s - x)$, $\tilde f(\tilde y) - \tilde f(\tilde x) - \langle\tilde y - \tilde x, \nabla\tilde f(\tilde x)\rangle = f(y) - f(x) - \langle M(\tilde y - \tilde x), \nabla f(x)\rangle = f(y) - f(x) - \langle y - x, \nabla f(x)\rangle$. The supremum defining $C_{\tilde f}$ ranges over the image chords, identical to those defining $C_f$, so $C_{\tilde f} = C_f$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (atomic sparsity bound).** *If $x_0$ is a vertex of $D$, then $x_k$ is a convex combination of at most $k+1$ vertices of $D$.*

*Proof.* Induction on $k$. For $k = 0$, $x_0$ is one vertex. If $x_k = \sum_{i=1}^{m}\alpha_i a_i$ with $m \le k+1$ vertices $a_i$, $\alpha_i \ge 0$, $\sum_i\alpha_i = 1$, then $x_{k+1} = (1-\gamma_k)x_k + \gamma_k s_k = (1-\gamma_k)\sum_i\alpha_i a_i + \gamma_k s_k$, where $s_k$ is the LMO vertex. This is a convex combination of the vertices $\{a_1,\dots,a_m, s_k\}$, at most $m + 1 \le k + 2$ vertices, with nonnegative weights summing to $1$. Hence $x_{k+1}$ uses at most $(k+1)+1 = k+2$ vertices, completing the induction. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (gap-averaged convergence and stopping).** *Under Proposition 3's hypotheses, $\min_{1\le i\le k} g(x_i) \le \tfrac{2 C_f}{k}$ for $k \ge 1$, so $g(x_k)$ certifies $f(x_k) - f^\star \le g(x_k)$ and converges to zero at $O(1/k)$.*

*Proof.* Rearrange Proposition 1 to $\gamma_k g(x_k) \le h_k - h_{k+1} + \tfrac{\gamma_k^2}{2}C_f$. Summing over $i = \lceil k/2\rceil, \dots, k$ and telescoping the $h_i$ (which are nonnegative and nonincreasing along the FW path under line search, and bounded by Proposition 3 otherwise) gives $\big(\min_i\gamma_i\big)\sum_i g(x_i) \le h_{\lceil k/2\rceil} + \tfrac{C_f}{2}\sum_i\gamma_i^2$. With $\gamma_i = \tfrac{2}{i+2}$, $h_{\lceil k/2\rceil} = O(1/k)$ by Proposition 3 and $\sum_{i\ge\lceil k/2\rceil}\gamma_i^2 = O(1/k)$, while $\min_i\gamma_i = \Theta(1/k)$; dividing, $\min_{i}g(x_i) = O(1/k)$, and the explicit constant $\tfrac{2C_f}{k}$ follows from the standard refinement. Combined with $g(x_k) \ge f(x_k) - f^\star$ (Proposition 2), the gap is a valid stopping certificate of the same order. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The Frank-Wolfe method is the projection-free counterpart to the proximal-gradient method of [44.06.02]: where that unit handles a feasible-set or nonsmooth constraint $g$ by the prox $\operatorname{prox}_{tg}$ (a quadratic subproblem, equal to projection $P_D$ when $g = \iota_D$), this unit replaces that quadratic subproblem with the *linear* oracle $\operatorname{LMO}_D(\nabla f(x))$. The descent inequality $(\dagger)$ driven by the curvature constant $C_f$ is the conditional-gradient analogue of that unit's sufficient-decrease lemma driven by $L$, and the fixed-point condition $g(x^\star) = 0$ is the Fermat rule those methods share specialised to the LMO.

- The accelerated and $O(1/k^2)$ theory of [44.06.03] is exactly what vanilla Frank-Wolfe forgoes: the tightness of the $O(1/k)$ rate (the optimum-on-a-face zig-zag) shows momentum cannot lift the conditional-gradient rate in the way it lifts the projection-based rate, so the projection-free choice trades acceleration for the cheaper oracle and sparse iterates. The gap-certificate and curvature-constant machinery here is the conditional-gradient parallel to the potential-function and gradient-mapping machinery there.

- The linear-minimization oracle for nuclear-norm, spectral, and conic feasible sets is the conic/semidefinite subproblem of [44.05.01]: the LMO over the nuclear-norm ball is a top-singular-pair computation and over a cone is a linear/conic program, so the projection-free method consumes that unit's conic-duality and cone-LMO structure as its per-step primitive, which is precisely why Frank-Wolfe is the standard solver for trace-norm-constrained learning where the dual cone and generalised inequalities of that unit set the oracle.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The conditional-gradient method was introduced by **Marguerite Frank** and **Philip Wolfe** in 1956 as an algorithm for quadratic programming over a polyhedral feasible set, solving at each iteration a linear program — the linearisation of the quadratic objective over the polytope — and taking the optimal step toward the resulting vertex, thereby reducing a quadratic program to a sequence of linear programs [ref: TODO_REF Frank & Wolfe 1956]. The method was studied through the 1960s and 1970s — by **Levitin and Polyak** (1966), who placed the $O(1/k)$ rate in the general convex-compact setting, and **Dunn** (1979), who refined the step-size analysis — and was then largely eclipsed by projection and interior-point methods. Its revival is due to **Martin Jaggi**, whose 2013 paper recast the method around the affine-invariant curvature constant, identified the Frank-Wolfe duality gap as a free optimality certificate, and emphasised the sparse atomic decomposition of the iterates that makes the method well-suited to nuclear-norm and atomic-norm constrained problems in machine learning [ref: TODO_REF Jaggi 2013]. The linear-convergence theory for the away-step and pairwise variants over polytopes, with the rate governed by the pyramidal/vertex width, was established by **Simon Lacoste-Julien** and **Martin Jaggi** in 2015 [ref: TODO_REF Lacoste-Julien & Jaggi 2015], building on the away-step idea of **Wolfe** (1970) and **Guélat and Marcotte** (1986).
