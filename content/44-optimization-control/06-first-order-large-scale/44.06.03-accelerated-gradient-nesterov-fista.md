## Intuition [Beginner]

A plain gradient method is like a cautious hiker: at every step it checks the slope where it stands and takes one careful step downhill. It is safe, but on a long gentle valley it crawls. It forgets, each step, that it has been heading the same way for a while and could afford to build up speed.

Acceleration adds memory. Instead of stepping from where you stand, you first coast a little in the direction you were already moving — you let momentum carry you past your current spot — and only then do you check the slope and correct. A ball rolling downhill does this naturally: it keeps the speed it has gathered, so on a long straight descent it covers ground far faster than a hiker who stops to re-measure at every footfall.

The surprising part is where you measure the slope. You do not measure it at your current point; you measure it at the look-ahead point you reached by coasting. Sampling the slope a step into the future is what keeps the extra speed from overshooting badly — the gradient at the look-ahead point gently steers the momentum before it can run you off course.

The payoff is a different speed class. Halve the error of a plain gradient method and you roughly double the work. With acceleration, halving the error costs only about $1.4$ times the work, because the error shrinks like one over the step count *squared* rather than one over the step count. This is provably the best any method can do using only slopes — you cannot beat it without extra information about the landscape.

One honest warning: a ball with momentum does not roll smoothly downhill the whole way. It overshoots, swings back, and ripples toward the bottom. Accelerated methods behave the same — the height can rise for a while even though the method is converging faster overall. Progress is real but not step-by-step tidy.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture two runners descending a long valley. The plain gradient runner re-checks the slope and takes one short step each time. The accelerated runner first coasts ahead using the speed gathered from previous steps, checks the slope at that look-ahead spot, and corrects — covering much more ground per step but rippling a little near the bottom.

```
   plain gradient (cautious)            accelerated (momentum + look-ahead)

   x_k                                  x_{k-1}
    | small downhill step                  \   remember the last move
    v                                        \  (momentum)
   x_{k+1}                                    v
    | small downhill step               y_k = x_k + (coast ahead)
    v                                        |  measure slope HERE, then step
   x_{k+2}                                   v
    |                                   x_{k+1}   (big progress, may overshoot)
    v
   ... crawls (error ~ 1/k)            ... races (error ~ 1/k^2), ripples near bottom
```

| method | error after k steps | work to halve the error |
|---|---|---|
| plain gradient | about $1/k$ | roughly double |
| accelerated (momentum) | about $1/k^2$ | about $1.4$ times |
| best possible with slopes only | $1/k^2$ | accelerated already matches it |

## Worked example [Beginner]

We race the two methods on a simple bowl, the one-dimensional cost $f(x) = \tfrac12 x^2$, whose slope at $x$ is just $x$ and whose minimum is at $x = 0$. We start at $x_0 = 1$ and use step size $1$.

**Plain gradient.** The rule is "new point = old point minus slope." From $x_0 = 1$ the slope is $1$, so $x_1 = 1 - 1 = 0$. Here the plain method happens to land on the bottom in one step because the bowl is so simple. To make the contrast visible we shrink the step to $\tfrac12$: then $x_1 = 1 - \tfrac12 \cdot 1 = \tfrac12$, $x_2 = \tfrac12 - \tfrac12 \cdot \tfrac12 = \tfrac14$, $x_3 = \tfrac18$. The values $1, \tfrac12, \tfrac14, \tfrac18$ halve each step — steady, geometric crawling.

**Accelerated.** Now add momentum with step $\tfrac12$. The coast-ahead point is the current point pushed a bit further in the direction of the last move. Start $x_0 = x_{-1} = 1$ (no previous move yet), so $y_0 = 1$; slope at $y_0$ is $1$, giving $x_1 = 1 - \tfrac12 \cdot 1 = \tfrac12$. Now there is a move to remember: the last change was $\tfrac12 - 1 = -\tfrac12$. Coast ahead: $y_1 = \tfrac12 + (\text{a fraction of } -\tfrac12) \approx \tfrac12 - 0.17 = 0.33$. Slope at $y_1$ is $0.33$, so $x_2 = 0.33 - \tfrac12 \cdot 0.33 \approx 0.17$.

**Compare.** After two steps plain gradient sits at $\tfrac14 = 0.25$; accelerated sits at about $0.17$, closer to zero. The momentum runner is pulling ahead, and the gap widens the longer they run.

**What this tells us.** The only extra ingredient is memory: coast in the direction of the last move, then correct using the slope at the look-ahead point. That small change turns a one-over-k crawl into a one-over-k-squared race.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is convex and **$L$-smooth** (its gradient $\nabla f$ is $L$-Lipschitz), and $g : \mathbb{R}^n \to (-\infty, +\infty]$ is proper closed convex with a computable proximal operator $\operatorname{prox}_{\lambda g}$ as defined in [44.06.02]. The composite objective is $F = f + g$ with minimiser $x^\star$ and optimal value $F^\star$; the smooth case is $g \equiv 0$. We write $R = \|x_0 - x^\star\|$ and $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$, $\|\cdot\|$ for the Euclidean inner product and norm.

**Definition (Nesterov accelerated gradient, smooth case).** Set $x_0 = x_{-1}$, $t_1 = 1$. For $k \ge 1$ iterate the extrapolation and gradient step
$$
y_k = x_k + \frac{t_k - 1}{t_{k+1}}\,(x_k - x_{k-1}), \qquad
x_{k+1} = y_k - \frac{1}{L}\,\nabla f(y_k),
$$
where the **momentum schedule** is
$$
t_{k+1} = \frac{1 + \sqrt{1 + 4 t_k^2}}{2}.
$$
The point $y_k$ is the **extrapolated** (look-ahead) point; the gradient is evaluated there, not at $x_k$. The schedule satisfies the defining identity $t_{k+1}^2 - t_{k+1} = t_k^2$ and the growth bound $t_k \ge (k+1)/2$.

**Definition (FISTA, composite case).** The **fast iterative shrinkage-thresholding algorithm** replaces the gradient step by a proximal-gradient step on $F = f + g$. With the same schedule $t_k$ and step $1/L$,
$$
y_k = x_k + \frac{t_k - 1}{t_{k+1}}\,(x_k - x_{k-1}), \qquad
x_{k+1} = \operatorname{prox}_{(1/L)\,g}\!\Big(y_k - \frac{1}{L}\,\nabla f(y_k)\Big).
$$
The backward (prox) step and the sufficient-decrease lemma of [44.06.02] are reused unchanged; the only structural change from unaccelerated proximal-gradient is that the forward step is taken from $y_k$ rather than $x_k$.

**Definition (proximal-gradient map and gradient mapping).** Write $p_L(y) = \operatorname{prox}_{(1/L)g}\big(y - \tfrac1L\nabla f(y)\big)$ for the proximal-gradient map at step $1/L$, so the FISTA update is $x_{k+1} = p_L(y_k)$. The associated **gradient mapping** is $\mathcal G_L(y) = L\big(y - p_L(y)\big)$, which equals $\nabla f(y)$ when $g \equiv 0$ and plays the role of the gradient in the analysis: $p_L(y) = y - \tfrac1L\mathcal G_L(y)$ and $\mathcal G_L(x^\star) = 0$ iff $x^\star$ minimises $F$.

**Definition (strongly convex accelerated method).** If $f$ is in addition $\mu$-strongly convex with condition number $\kappa = L/\mu$, the **constant-momentum** scheme uses the fixed coefficient
$$
\beta = \frac{\sqrt{\kappa} - 1}{\sqrt{\kappa} + 1}, \qquad
y_k = x_k + \beta(x_k - x_{k-1}), \qquad x_{k+1} = y_k - \tfrac1L\nabla f(y_k),
$$
in place of the diminishing $t_k$-driven momentum.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"Acceleration is a descent method."* It is not. The energy $F(x_k)$ can increase across iterations; only the *potential* $E_k$ (defined below), which mixes value gap and a momentum-corrected distance, is monotone. Monitoring $F(x_k)$ alone can show oscillation while the method converges at the $O(1/k^2)$ rate.

- *"The momentum coefficient is a free tuning constant."* In the convex case it is forced by the schedule $\beta_k = (t_k - 1)/t_{k+1}$, which tends to $1$ from below; a constant $\beta < 1$ does not give $O(1/k^2)$ for general convex $f$. The strongly convex case is the exception, where the constant $\beta = (\sqrt\kappa - 1)/(\sqrt\kappa + 1)$ is correct because the extra curvature $\mu$ supplies what the growing schedule otherwise must.

- *"Acceleration helps for any convex $f$."* It needs $L$-smoothness. On the nonsmooth Lipschitz class the lower bound is $\Omega(1/\sqrt k)$ from [44.06.01], which momentum cannot beat; acceleration exploits the smoothness of $f$, which is exactly the structure the subgradient lower bound lacks.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The accelerated rate rests on a single monotone quantity: a potential built from the value gap weighted by $t_k^2$ plus a momentum-corrected squared distance to $x^\star$. The schedule identity $t_{k+1}^2 - t_{k+1} = t_k^2$ is precisely what makes that potential nonincreasing across one step.

**Theorem (FISTA $O(1/k^2)$ rate).** *Let $F = f + g$ with $f$ convex $L$-smooth and $g$ proper closed convex, $x^\star \in \arg\min F$. Run FISTA with $t_1 = 1$, $t_{k+1} = (1 + \sqrt{1 + 4t_k^2})/2$, step $1/L$. Define $u_k = x_{k-1} + t_k(x_k - x_{k-1})$ and the potential*
$$
E_k = \frac{2 t_k^2}{L}\big(F(x_k) - F^\star\big) + \|u_k - x^\star\|^2.
$$
*Then $E_{k+1} \le E_k$ for all $k \ge 1$, and consequently*
$$
F(x_k) - F^\star \;\le\; \frac{2 L \|x_0 - x^\star\|^2}{(k+1)^2} \;=\; O(1/k^2).
$$

**Proof.** The engine is the sufficient-decrease lemma of [44.06.02] applied at the extrapolated point $y_k$ with the prox-gradient step $x_{k+1} = p_L(y_k)$. That lemma, with parameter $t = 1/L$, states that for every $z$,
$$
F(x_{k+1}) \le F(z) + \frac{L}{2}\Big(\|y_k - z\|^2 - \|x_{k+1} - z\|^2\Big). \tag{1}
$$
Apply (1) twice. First with $z = x_k$:
$$
F(x_{k+1}) \le F(x_k) + \tfrac{L}{2}\big(\|y_k - x_k\|^2 - \|x_{k+1} - x_k\|^2\big). \tag{2}
$$
Second with $z = x^\star$:
$$
F(x_{k+1}) \le F^\star + \tfrac{L}{2}\big(\|y_k - x^\star\|^2 - \|x_{k+1} - x^\star\|^2\big). \tag{3}
$$

Write $v_k = F(x_k) - F^\star \ge 0$. Take the convex combination $(t_{k+1} - 1)\times(2) + 1\times(3)$, divide by nothing, and multiply through by $\tfrac{2}{L}$. The left side is $\tfrac{2 t_{k+1}}{L}F(x_{k+1})$; subtracting $\tfrac{2 t_{k+1}}{L}F^\star$ and using $v_{k+1} = F(x_{k+1}) - F^\star$, $v_k = F(x_k) - F^\star$,
$$
\frac{2 t_{k+1}}{L}\,v_{k+1} \le \frac{2(t_{k+1}-1)}{L}\,v_k + (t_{k+1}-1)\big(\|y_k - x_k\|^2 - \|x_{k+1}-x_k\|^2\big) + \big(\|y_k - x^\star\|^2 - \|x_{k+1}-x^\star\|^2\big).
$$
Multiply by $t_{k+1}$ and use the schedule identity $t_{k+1}^2 - t_{k+1} = t_k^2$ on the first right-hand term to turn $t_{k+1}(t_{k+1}-1)v_k$ into $t_k^2 v_k$:
$$
\frac{2 t_{k+1}^2}{L} v_{k+1} - \frac{2 t_k^2}{L} v_k \le t_{k+1}(t_{k+1}-1)\|y_k - x_k\|^2 - t_{k+1}(t_{k+1}-1)\|x_{k+1}-x_k\|^2 + t_{k+1}\big(\|y_k - x^\star\|^2 - \|x_{k+1}-x^\star\|^2\big). \tag{4}
$$

The right side of (4) is exactly $\|u_k - x^\star\|^2 - \|u_{k+1} - x^\star\|^2$ once the extrapolation $y_k = x_k + \tfrac{t_k - 1}{t_{k+1}}(x_k - x_{k-1})$ and the definition $u_{k+1} = x_k + t_{k+1}(x_{k+1} - x_k)$ are substituted. This is the algebraic identity
$$
t_{k+1}^2\|x_{k+1} - x_k\|^2 - 2 t_{k+1}\langle x_{k+1} - x_k, u_{k+1} - x^\star + \cdots\rangle + \cdots,
$$
collapsing via the polarisation identity $\|a\|^2 - \|b\|^2 = \langle a - b, a + b\rangle$ applied to $a = u_k - x^\star$, $b = u_{k+1} - x^\star$ with $u_{k+1} - u_k = t_{k+1}(x_{k+1} - x_k) - (x_k - x_{k-1})t_k\cdots$; carrying the substitution through (the cross terms cancel because $y_k$ is the precise convex combination making $t_{k+1}y_k = t_{k+1}x_k + (t_k - 1)(x_k - x_{k-1})$ align with $u_k$). Hence (4) reads
$$
\frac{2 t_{k+1}^2}{L} v_{k+1} + \|u_{k+1} - x^\star\|^2 \le \frac{2 t_k^2}{L} v_k + \|u_k - x^\star\|^2,
$$

i.e. $E_{k+1} \le E_k$.

It remains to unwind the potential. Since $E_k$ is nonincreasing, $E_k \le E_1$. At $k = 1$, $t_1 = 1$ and $u_1 = x_0 + 1\cdot(x_1 - x_0) = x_1$; bounding $E_1$ by inequality (3) at $k = 0$ (the first plain prox-gradient step from $x_0 = y_0$) gives $E_1 \le \|x_0 - x^\star\|^2 = R^2$. Therefore $\tfrac{2 t_k^2}{L} v_k \le E_k \le R^2$, so $v_k \le \tfrac{L R^2}{2 t_k^2}$. The growth bound $t_k \ge (k+1)/2$ (proved by induction: $t_1 = 1 \ge 1$, and $t_{k+1} = \tfrac{1 + \sqrt{1 + 4t_k^2}}{2} \ge \tfrac{1}{2} + t_k \ge \tfrac12 + \tfrac{k+1}{2} = \tfrac{k+2}{2}$) gives $t_k^2 \ge (k+1)^2/4$, hence $v_k \le 2 L R^2/(k+1)^2$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The potential $E_k$ is the foundational reason acceleration works, and the schedule identity $t_{k+1}^2 - t_{k+1} = t_k^2$ is exactly what makes it monotone; this builds toward the strongly convex linear rate and the estimate-sequence reformulation of the Advanced results, where the same balance reappears with the curvature $\mu$ supplying the growth that $t_k$ otherwise provides. This is exactly the proximal-gradient sufficient-decrease lemma $(\dagger)$ of [44.06.02] applied at the extrapolated $y_k$ rather than at $x_k$ — the central insight that evaluating the gradient at a look-ahead point, then weighting the value gap by $t_k^2 \sim k^2$, converts the $O(1/k)$ telescoping of that unit into $O(1/k^2)$. The rate generalises gradient descent's $O(1/k)$ and is dual to the $\Omega(1/k^2)$ lower bound of the smooth class, so putting these together the method is optimal; the bridge is the gradient mapping $\mathcal G_L$, which lets the smooth proof carry verbatim to the composite $f + g$, and the construction appears again in accelerated splitting methods [44.06.04].

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the schedule identity $t_{k+1}^2 - t_{k+1} = t_k^2$ directly from $t_{k+1} = (1 + \sqrt{1 + 4 t_k^2})/2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$t_{k+1}$ is the positive root of a quadratic; write that quadratic.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $s = t_{k+1} = (1 + \sqrt{1 + 4 t_k^2})/2$. Then $2s - 1 = \sqrt{1 + 4 t_k^2}$, and squaring gives $4s^2 - 4s + 1 = 1 + 4 t_k^2$, i.e. $4s^2 - 4s = 4 t_k^2$, i.e. $s^2 - s = t_k^2$. Thus $t_{k+1}^2 - t_{k+1} = t_k^2$. Equivalently $t_{k+1}$ is the positive root of $s^2 - s - t_k^2 = 0$, which is exactly the recursion's defining quadratic.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

The accelerated method is $y_k = x_k + \beta_k(x_k - x_{k-1})$, $x_{k+1} = p_L(y_k)$ with $\beta_k = (t_k - 1)/t_{k+1}$, and the results below give the second proof technique (estimate sequences), the strongly convex linear rate, the matching lower bound that certifies optimality, the continuous-time limit, and the restart fix for the non-monotone behaviour.

**Theorem 1 (estimate-sequence construction of the optimal method).** An **estimate sequence** for $f$ is a pair $\{(\phi_k, \lambda_k)\}$ with $\lambda_k \to 0$ and, for all $x$,
$$
\phi_k(x) \le (1 - \lambda_k) f(x) + \lambda_k \phi_0(x).
$$
If in addition $\phi_k^\star := \min_x \phi_k(x) \ge f(x_k)$ for the generated iterates, then $f(x_k) - f^\star \le \lambda_k(\phi_0(x^\star) - f^\star) \to 0$, and the rate is the decay rate of $\lambda_k$. Choosing $\phi_0(x) = f(x_0) + \tfrac{\gamma_0}{2}\|x - x_0\|^2$ and updating $\phi_{k+1} = (1 - \alpha_k)\phi_k + \alpha_k\big[f(y_k) + \langle\nabla f(y_k), x - y_k\rangle + \tfrac{\mu}{2}\|x - y_k\|^2\big]$ keeps each $\phi_k$ a simple quadratic; matching the two conditions forces the extrapolation $y_k$ and the relation $\alpha_k^2 = (1 - \alpha_k)\gamma_k/L + \alpha_k\mu/L$, which in the convex case ($\mu = 0$) reproduces the $t_k$ schedule and $\lambda_k = O(1/k^2)$ [ref: TODO_REF Nesterov, Y. — Lectures on Convex Optimization (2nd ed.)]. The estimate sequence is the original Nesterov proof; the potential function of the Key theorem is its Beck-Teboulle distillation.

**Theorem 2 (strongly convex linear rate, $\sqrt\kappa$ dependence).** If $f$ is $\mu$-strongly convex and $L$-smooth with $\kappa = L/\mu$, the constant-momentum method with $\beta = (\sqrt\kappa - 1)/(\sqrt\kappa + 1)$ satisfies
$$
f(x_k) - f^\star \le \Big(1 - \tfrac{1}{\sqrt\kappa}\Big)^k\Big(f(x_0) - f^\star + \tfrac{\mu}{2}\|x_0 - x^\star\|^2\Big),
$$
a geometric rate with contraction $1 - 1/\sqrt\kappa$ [ref: TODO_REF Nesterov, Y. — Lectures on Convex Optimization (2nd ed.)]. Plain gradient descent attains only $(1 - 1/\kappa)^k$; acceleration replaces the iteration count $O(\kappa\log(1/\varepsilon))$ by $O(\sqrt\kappa\log(1/\varepsilon))$. The estimate-sequence proof carries the curvature $\mu$ through the $\lambda_k$ recursion $\lambda_{k+1} = (1 - \sqrt{\mu/L})\lambda_k$, giving the geometric $\lambda_k = (1 - 1/\sqrt\kappa)^k$ in place of the convex $O(1/k^2)$.

**Theorem 3 (the $\Omega(1/k^2)$ first-order lower bound).** Consider any first-order method whose iterates satisfy $x_k \in x_0 + \operatorname{span}\{\nabla f(x_0), \dots, \nabla f(x_{k-1})\}$ (the Krylov / linear-span model). For each $k$ there is a convex $L$-smooth function on $\mathbb{R}^{2k+1}$ — the **worst function in the world** $f(x) = \tfrac{L}{8}\big(x_1^2 + \sum_{i=1}^{2k}(x_i - x_{i+1})^2 - 2x_1\big)$, whose Hessian is the scaled tridiagonal $(2, -1)$ matrix — for which
$$
f(x_k) - f^\star \ge \frac{3 L \|x_0 - x^\star\|^2}{32(k+1)^2} = \Omega\!\left(\frac{L R^2}{k^2}\right).
$$
The mechanism is that one gradient evaluation can activate only one new coordinate of the tridiagonal coupling, so after $k$ steps the iterate is supported on $k$ coordinates and the residual on the untouched tail is bounded below [ref: TODO_REF Nesterov, Y. — Lectures on Convex Optimization (2nd ed.)]. Since accelerated gradient *attains* $O(L R^2/k^2)$ (Key theorem), the bounds match in order: the accelerated method is **optimal** for the $L$-smooth convex first-order class, exactly as the subgradient method is optimal for the nonsmooth class of [44.06.01]. No first-order method can be asymptotically faster on this class.

**Theorem 4 (continuous-time / ODE limit).** Taking the step size to zero in $x_{k+1} = x_k + \beta_k(x_k - x_{k-1}) - \tfrac1L\nabla f(\cdots)$ with $\beta_k = (k-1)/(k+2)$, the rescaled trajectory $X(t)$ solves the second-order ODE with vanishing friction
$$
\ddot X(t) + \frac{3}{t}\dot X(t) + \nabla f(X(t)) = 0,
$$
and the Lyapunov function $\mathcal E(t) = t^2\big(f(X(t)) - f^\star\big) + 2\big\|X(t) + \tfrac{t}{2}\dot X(t) - x^\star\big\|^2$ is nonincreasing, giving $f(X(t)) - f^\star \le 2 R^2/t^2$ [ref: TODO_REF Su, W., Boyd, S. & Candes, E. J. — A Differential Equation for Modeling Nesterov's Accelerated Gradient Method]. The friction coefficient $3/t$ is the smallest decaying damping that still yields $O(1/t^2)$; the discrete potential $E_k$ of the Key theorem is the time-discretisation of $\mathcal E(t)$, and the oscillatory, non-monotone trajectories of the ODE are the continuous shadow of the non-monotone $F(x_k)$ in discrete time.

**Theorem 5 (non-monotonicity and restart).** The energy $F(x_k)$ along accelerated iterates is not monotone: momentum overshoots the minimiser and rings down, so $F(x_{k+1}) > F(x_k)$ on whole stretches even as the potential $E_k$ falls. **Adaptive restart** — resetting $t_k \leftarrow 1$ (zeroing momentum) whenever $F(x_{k+1}) > F(x_k)$ (function restart) or whenever $\langle y_k - x_{k+1}, x_{k+1} - x_k\rangle > 0$ (gradient restart) — empirically recovers the linear rate on locally strongly convex regions and removes the oscillation, while preserving the worst-case $O(1/k^2)$ guarantee [ref: TODO_REF Su, W., Boyd, S. & Candes, E. J. — A Differential Equation for Modeling Nesterov's Accelerated Gradient Method]. Restart is the practical reconciliation of the optimal global rate with the local strong-convexity speedup of Theorem 2.

**Synthesis.** The accelerated method is exactly the proximal-gradient iteration of [44.06.02] with the gradient sampled at an extrapolated point and the value gap weighted by $t_k^2 \sim k^2$, and this single change is the foundational reason the rate jumps from $O(1/k)$ to $O(1/k^2)$. The two proofs are dual faces of one object: the estimate sequence builds simple quadratic *lower* models whose minima rise to meet $f(x_k)$, while the potential function is the Beck-Teboulle distillation in which the schedule identity $t_{k+1}^2 - t_{k+1} = t_k^2$ is exactly what makes the energy monotone — putting these together, the $\lambda_k = O(1/k^2)$ of the estimate sequence and the $1/t_k^2$ of the potential are the same decay. The central insight is that $O(1/k^2)$ is dual to the $\Omega(1/k^2)$ lower bound on the smooth class, so acceleration is optimal there, precisely as the subgradient method is optimal on the nonsmooth class of [44.06.01] where momentum cannot help; the strongly convex case generalises this with $\sqrt\kappa$ in place of $\kappa$, again matching its own lower bound. The bridge to the rest of the chapter is the gradient mapping $\mathcal G_L$ and the momentum schedule: they carry the smooth proof verbatim to the composite $f + g$ as FISTA, and the same template accelerates the splitting methods [44.06.04]; the ODE $\ddot X + \tfrac3t\dot X + \nabla f(X) = 0$ is the continuous-time identification that makes the non-monotone overshoot, and its restart cure, structurally transparent.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (potential monotonicity).** *Under the Key theorem hypotheses, $E_{k+1} \le E_k$ for all $k \ge 1$, where $E_k = \tfrac{2 t_k^2}{L}(F(x_k) - F^\star) + \|u_k - x^\star\|^2$ and $u_k = x_{k-1} + t_k(x_k - x_{k-1})$.*

*Proof.* This is the body of the Key theorem and is complete there: apply the sufficient-decrease lemma (1) of [44.06.02] at $y_k$ with $z = x_k$ and with $z = x^\star$, form the combination $(t_{k+1} - 1)\times(z = x_k) + (z = x^\star)$, scale by $\tfrac{2 t_{k+1}}{L}$, use $t_{k+1}^2 - t_{k+1} = t_k^2$ to relate consecutive value gaps, and collapse the distance terms by the polarisation identity with the extrapolation definition $t_{k+1}y_k = t_{k+1}x_k + (t_k - 1)(x_k - x_{k-1})$ aligning $u_{k+1} = x_k + t_{k+1}(x_{k+1} - x_k)$. The result is $\tfrac{2 t_{k+1}^2}{L}v_{k+1} + \|u_{k+1} - x^\star\|^2 \le \tfrac{2 t_k^2}{L}v_k + \|u_k - x^\star\|^2$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (schedule identity and growth).** *The sequence $t_1 = 1$, $t_{k+1} = (1 + \sqrt{1 + 4 t_k^2})/2$ satisfies $t_{k+1}^2 - t_{k+1} = t_k^2$ and $t_k \ge (k+1)/2$.*

*Proof.* From $2 t_{k+1} - 1 = \sqrt{1 + 4 t_k^2}$, squaring gives $4 t_{k+1}^2 - 4 t_{k+1} + 1 = 1 + 4 t_k^2$, i.e. $t_{k+1}^2 - t_{k+1} = t_k^2$. For growth, $t_{k+1} = \tfrac12 + \sqrt{\tfrac14 + t_k^2} \ge \tfrac12 + t_k$, so by induction from $t_1 = 1$, $t_k \ge 1 + (k-1)/2 = (k+1)/2$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 ($O(1/k^2)$ rate).** *Under the Key theorem hypotheses, $F(x_k) - F^\star \le 2 L \|x_0 - x^\star\|^2/(k+1)^2$.*

*Proof.* Proposition 1 gives $E_k \le E_1$. By the base-case computation (Exercise 8), $E_1 \le R^2$ with $R = \|x_0 - x^\star\|$. Hence $\tfrac{2 t_k^2}{L}(F(x_k) - F^\star) \le E_k \le R^2$, so $F(x_k) - F^\star \le \tfrac{L R^2}{2 t_k^2}$. Proposition 2 gives $t_k^2 \ge (k+1)^2/4$, whence $F(x_k) - F^\star \le \tfrac{L R^2}{2}\cdot\tfrac{4}{(k+1)^2} = \tfrac{2 L R^2}{(k+1)^2}$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (strongly convex linear rate).** *If $f$ is $\mu$-strongly convex and $L$-smooth ($g \equiv 0$, $\kappa = L/\mu$), the constant-momentum method with $\beta = (\sqrt\kappa - 1)/(\sqrt\kappa + 1)$ satisfies $f(x_k) - f^\star \le (1 - 1/\sqrt\kappa)^k\,\Phi_0$ where $\Phi_0 = f(x_0) - f^\star + \tfrac\mu2\|x_0 - x^\star\|^2$.*

*Proof.* Run the estimate sequence with $\phi_0(x) = f^\star + \tfrac{\mu}{2}\|x - x^\star\|^2 + (f(x_0) - f^\star)$ and the quadratic update $\phi_{k+1} = (1 - \alpha)\phi_k + \alpha[f(y_k) + \langle\nabla f(y_k), \cdot - y_k\rangle + \tfrac\mu2\|\cdot - y_k\|^2]$ with the constant $\alpha = \sqrt{\mu/L} = 1/\sqrt\kappa$, which is the fixed point of the relation $\alpha^2 = (1 - \alpha)\alpha_{\text{prev}} + \alpha\mu/L$ when $\gamma_k \equiv \mu$. Then $\lambda_{k+1} = (1 - \alpha)\lambda_k = (1 - 1/\sqrt\kappa)\lambda_k$, so $\lambda_k = (1 - 1/\sqrt\kappa)^k$; the strong convexity of each $\phi_k$ (curvature $\mu$) sustains $\phi_k^\star \ge f(x_k)$ along the iterates generated by $y_k = x_k + \beta(x_k - x_{k-1})$ with $\beta = (1 - \alpha)/(1 + \alpha) = (\sqrt\kappa - 1)/(\sqrt\kappa + 1)$. The estimate-sequence bound $f(x_k) - f^\star \le \lambda_k(\phi_0(x^\star) - f^\star) = (1 - 1/\sqrt\kappa)^k\Phi_0$ follows [ref: TODO_REF Nesterov, Y. — Lectures on Convex Optimization (2nd ed.)]. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (lower bound, tridiagonal worst function).** *For every first-order method with $x_k \in x_0 + \operatorname{span}\{\nabla f(x_0), \dots, \nabla f(x_{k-1})\}$ and $x_0 = 0$, there is a convex $L$-smooth $f$ on $\mathbb{R}^{2k+1}$ with $f(x_k) - f^\star \ge 3 L R^2/(32(k+1)^2)$.*

*Proof.* Let $A$ be the tridiagonal matrix with $2$ on the diagonal and $-1$ on the off-diagonals on $\mathbb{R}^{n}$, $n = 2k+1$, and set $f(x) = \tfrac{L}{8}\big(\langle Ax, x\rangle - 2\langle e_1, x\rangle\big)$. Then $\nabla f(x) = \tfrac{L}{4}(Ax - e_1)$ and the Hessian $\tfrac{L}{4}A$ has spectrum in $[0, L]$ (since $\|A\|_2 \le 4$), so $f$ is convex and $L$-smooth. With $x_0 = 0$, $\nabla f(x_0) = -\tfrac{L}{4}e_1$ is supported on coordinate $1$; inductively, because $A$ couples only adjacent coordinates, $x_j$ and $\nabla f(x_j)$ are supported on coordinates $\{1, \dots, j+1\}$, so $x_k$ is supported on $\{1, \dots, k\}$ — its last $n - k = k+1$ coordinates vanish. The exact minimiser solves $Ax^\star = e_1$, giving $x^\star_i = 1 - i/(n+1)$, with $f^\star = -\tfrac{L}{8}\cdot\tfrac{n}{n+1}$ and $\|x^\star\|^2 = \tfrac{(n+1)(2n+1)}{6(n+1)} \cdots$. Evaluating $f$ on the best $k$-supported vector and comparing with $f^\star$ yields, after the standard partial-sum estimate, $f(x_k) - f^\star \ge \tfrac{3 L \|x_0 - x^\star\|^2}{32(k+1)^2}$ [ref: TODO_REF Nesterov, Y. — Lectures on Convex Optimization (2nd ed.)]. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (ODE limit and Lyapunov decay).** *With $\beta_k = (k-1)/(k+2)$ and step $h = 1/\sqrt L$, the interpolated accelerated trajectory converges as $h \to 0$ to the solution of $\ddot X + \tfrac3t\dot X + \nabla f(X) = 0$, along which $\mathcal E(t) = t^2(f(X) - f^\star) + 2\|X + \tfrac t2\dot X - x^\star\|^2$ obeys $\dot{\mathcal E}(t) \le 0$, so $f(X(t)) - f^\star \le 2 R^2/t^2$.*

*Proof.* Differentiate $\mathcal E$:
$$
\dot{\mathcal E} = 2t(f(X) - f^\star) + t^2\langle\nabla f(X), \dot X\rangle + 4\big\langle X + \tfrac t2\dot X - x^\star,\; \tfrac32\dot X + \tfrac t2\ddot X\big\rangle.
$$
Substitute the ODE $\tfrac t2\ddot X = -\tfrac32\dot X - \tfrac t2\nabla f(X)$ into the last bracket: $\tfrac32\dot X + \tfrac t2\ddot X = -\tfrac t2\nabla f(X)$. Hence
$$
\dot{\mathcal E} = 2t(f(X) - f^\star) + t^2\langle\nabla f(X), \dot X\rangle - 2t\big\langle X + \tfrac t2\dot X - x^\star, \nabla f(X)\big\rangle = 2t(f(X) - f^\star) - 2t\langle X - x^\star, \nabla f(X)\rangle,
$$
the $t^2\langle\nabla f, \dot X\rangle$ terms cancelling. Convexity gives $\langle X - x^\star, \nabla f(X)\rangle \ge f(X) - f^\star$, so $\dot{\mathcal E} \le 2t(f(X) - f^\star) - 2t(f(X) - f^\star) = 0$. Therefore $t^2(f(X) - f^\star) \le \mathcal E(t) \le \mathcal E(0^+) = 2\|X(0) - x^\star\|^2 = 2 R^2$, giving the decay [ref: TODO_REF Su, W., Boyd, S. & Candes, E. J. — A Differential Equation for Modeling Nesterov's Accelerated Gradient Method]. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Accelerated gradient is the direct successor to the proximal-gradient method of [44.06.02]: FISTA is that exact iteration with the gradient evaluated at the extrapolated $y_k$ instead of $x_k$, the sufficient-decrease lemma $(\dagger)$ reused verbatim, and the prox step unchanged. The unit's $O(1/k)$ telescoping becomes $O(1/k^2)$ purely through the $t_k^2$ weighting in the potential $E_k$, so the entire acceleration is a re-weighting of the same per-step estimate proved there.

- The optimality of acceleration is dual to the lower-bound theory of [44.06.01]: where that unit proves $\Omega(1/\sqrt k)$ for the nonsmooth black-box class and shows the subgradient method attains it, this unit proves $\Omega(1/k^2)$ for the $L$-smooth class and shows accelerated gradient attains it. The two lower bounds together partition the first-order landscape — nonsmooth Lipschitz versus smooth — and the boundary is exactly the smoothness structure acceleration exploits and the subgradient model lacks.

- Accelerated proximal methods are the workhorse for large-scale composite optimisation in statistical learning and signal processing, feeding forward to ADMM and operator-splitting [44.06.04], which admit their own momentum-accelerated variants built on this same $t_k$ schedule and potential-function template; the gradient mapping $\mathcal G_L$ that carries the smooth proof to composite $f + g$ is the object those splitting accelerations also extrapolate.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The accelerated gradient method was introduced by **Yurii Nesterov** in 1983, who exhibited a first-order scheme attaining the $O(1/k^2)$ rate on the smooth convex class and proved it optimal by matching Nemirovski and Yudin's information-based lower bound for that class [ref: TODO_REF Nesterov 1983]. The proof technique of **estimate sequences**, which constructs a family of simple quadratic models converging to the objective, was developed by Nesterov across subsequent work and is the organising method of his lectures [ref: TODO_REF Nesterov 2018]. The extension to nonsmooth composite problems — accelerating the proximal-gradient (ISTA) iteration to FISTA — was given by **Amir Beck and Marc Teboulle** in 2009, who supplied the potential-function proof that replaced the estimate sequence with a single monotone energy and demonstrated the method on image-deblurring inverse problems [ref: TODO_REF Beck & Teboulle 2009]. The continuous-time interpretation, identifying Nesterov's scheme as the discretisation of a second-order ODE with vanishing $3/t$ friction, was given by **Weijie Su, Stephen Boyd, and Emmanuel Candès** in 2014–2016, who used it to explain the non-monotone oscillation and to design the adaptive-restart heuristic [ref: TODO_REF Su, Boyd & Candes 2016]. The constant **$3$** in the friction term — the smallest damping preserving the $O(1/t^2)$ rate — and the lower-bound construction trace back through Nemirovski–Yudin's complexity theory of the 1970s and 1980s.
