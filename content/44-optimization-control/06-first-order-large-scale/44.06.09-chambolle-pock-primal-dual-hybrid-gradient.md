## Intuition [Beginner]

Many problems pull in two directions at once. You want a picture that stays close to the noisy photo you measured, and you want it to be smooth, with sharp edges but no speckle. The two wishes fight: matching the photo exactly keeps the noise; over-smoothing erases the edges. A good answer is a truce between them.

A clean way to find that truce is to picture a tug-of-war. One player, the "primal" player, proposes a picture. The other player, the "dual" player, is a critic who probes where the picture is too rough and pushes back on exactly those spots. They take turns: the critic sharpens its complaints given the current picture, then the picture-maker adjusts to answer the latest complaints. Round by round, the picture and the complaints settle into balance.

The Chambolle-Pock method runs this tug-of-war with two friendly moves you already know. Each round the critic does one proximal step (its update is a simple, often closed-form, adjustment), and the picture-maker does one proximal step too. The only thing coupling them is a linear operator — for images, the operator that measures how much neighboring pixels differ. There is one extra trick: the picture-maker looks slightly ahead, using not just the latest picture but a small over-shoot of it, which steadies the back-and-forth and makes it converge.

What makes this method shine on images is that neither move needs solving a big system of equations. Each step is just a cheap formula plus multiplying by the difference operator. There is a single rule to keep it stable: the two step sizes, multiplied together and scaled by the operator's strength, must stay below one.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture one round as three moves: the critic (dual $y$) sharpens its complaints from the current picture, the picture-maker (primal $x$) answers, and then a small over-shoot of the new picture is passed forward to steady the next round.

```
   round n                      what each step does

   dual y-update  ------->   prox-ascent: sharpen the critic given
   (the critic)              the look-ahead picture (a clip / prox step)
        |
        v
   primal x-update  ----->   prox-descent: adjust the picture to
   (the picture-maker)       answer the new critic (a prox step)
        |
        v
   extrapolation  ------->   look slightly ahead:
   (over-shoot)             bar x = x_new + (x_new - x_old)
        |
        v
   repeat until x and y settle   (the truce holds)

   stability rule:  step_x  x  step_y  x  (operator strength)^2  <  1
```

| the piece | its update does |
|---|---|
| critic $y$ (dual) | prox-ascent: a cheap step (often a clip) given the look-ahead picture |
| picture $x$ (primal) | prox-descent: a cheap step answering the latest critic |
| over-shoot $\bar x$ | pass forward $x_{\text{new}}$ plus its change, to steady the rounds |

## Worked example [Beginner]

We run one round on a tiny two-pixel denoising problem. The noisy data is $b = (b_1, b_2) = (3, 0)$. We want a picture $x = (x_1, x_2)$ that stays near $b$ but whose two pixels are not too different. The roughness is measured by the single difference $Kx = x_1 - x_2$, so $K = (1, -1)$. The fit cost is $g(x) = \tfrac12\|x - b\|^2$ and the roughness cost is the absolute value $\lambda |Kx|$ with $\lambda = 1$. We use step sizes $\tau = \tfrac12$, $\sigma = \tfrac12$, and start at $x = (3, 0)$, $\bar x = (3, 0)$, critic $y = 0$.

**Step 1. The critic's update.** The critic for an absolute value lives between $-\lambda$ and $\lambda$. Its update is: take $y + \sigma\,(K\bar x)$, then clip into $[-1, 1]$. Here $K\bar x = 3 - 0 = 3$, so $y + \tfrac12\cdot 3 = 1.5$, and clipping to $[-1,1]$ gives $y = 1$.

**Step 2. The picture's update.** The picture answers by a fit-step. The fit prox for $g$ is $x \leftarrow (x - \tau K^\top y + \tau b)/(1 + \tau)$. Here $K^\top y = (1, -1)\cdot 1 = (1, -1)$, so $x - \tau K^\top y = (3, 0) - \tfrac12(1, -1) = (2.5, 0.5)$. Add $\tau b = (1.5, 0)$ and divide by $1 + \tau = 1.5$: $x = (4.0, 0.5)/1.5 = (2.667, 0.333)$.

**Step 3. The over-shoot.** Pass forward $\bar x = x_{\text{new}} + (x_{\text{new}} - x_{\text{old}}) = (2.667, 0.333) + ((2.667, 0.333) - (3, 0)) = (2.333, 0.667)$.

**What this tells us.** The gap between the two pixels started at $3 - 0 = 3$ and after one round the picture's gap is $2.667 - 0.333 = 2.333$ — the roughness is already shrinking, while each pixel stays near its data value. Every step was a clip or a one-line formula; no system of equations was solved.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $X = \mathbb{R}^n$ and $Y = \mathbb{R}^m$ are Euclidean spaces, $K : X \to Y$ is a bounded linear operator with adjoint $K^\top$ and operator norm $\|K\| = \max\{\|Kx\| : \|x\| \le 1\}$, and $g : X \to (-\infty,+\infty]$, $f : Y \to (-\infty,+\infty]$ are proper closed convex. The proximal operator $\operatorname{prox}_{th}(v) = \arg\min_w\{h(w) + \tfrac1{2t}\|w - v\|^2\}$ and its resolvent identity $\operatorname{prox}_{th} = (I + t\partial h)^{-1}$ are from [44.06.02]; the Fenchel conjugate $f^*(y) = \sup_z\{\langle z, y\rangle - f(z)\}$ and Fenchel-Rockafellar duality are from [44.02.03].

**Definition (the saddle-point model).** Consider the composite primal problem
$$
\min_{x \in X}\ f(Kx) + g(x).
$$
Conjugating $f$ via $f(Kx) = \sup_{y\in Y}\{\langle Kx, y\rangle - f^*(y)\}$ of [44.02.03] turns it into the **saddle-point (convex-concave) problem**
$$
\min_{x\in X}\ \max_{y\in Y}\ \Phi(x,y), \qquad \Phi(x,y) = \langle Kx, y\rangle + g(x) - f^*(y),
$$
convex in $x$ for fixed $y$ and concave in $y$ for fixed $x$. The variable $y$ is the **dual variable** dual to the constraint inside $f$.

**Definition (the Chambolle-Pock / PDHG iteration).** Fix step sizes $\tau, \sigma > 0$ and an extrapolation parameter $\theta \in [0,1]$. Starting from $(x^0, y^0)$ and $\bar x^0 = x^0$, the **primal-dual hybrid gradient (PDHG) / Chambolle-Pock** iteration produces $(x^{n+1}, y^{n+1}, \bar x^{n+1})$ by
$$
\begin{aligned}
y^{n+1} &= \operatorname{prox}_{\sigma f^*}\big(y^n + \sigma K\bar x^n\big),\\
x^{n+1} &= \operatorname{prox}_{\tau g}\big(x^n - \tau K^\top y^{n+1}\big),\\
\bar x^{n+1} &= x^{n+1} + \theta\,(x^{n+1} - x^n).
\end{aligned}
$$
The first step is a **proximal ascent** in $y$ (an explicit step $+\sigma K\bar x^n$ followed by the prox of $f^*$); the second is a **proximal descent** in $x$ (an explicit step $-\tau K^\top y^{n+1}$ followed by the prox of $g$). The third is the **over-relaxation / extrapolation** step. The standard choice is $\theta = 1$.

**Definition (step-size condition).** The convergence of the unaccelerated method ($\theta = 1$) holds under
$$
\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1.
$$
This is the only restriction relating the two step sizes; either may otherwise be chosen freely, balancing the conditioning of the primal and dual blocks.

**Definition (partial primal-dual gap).** For bounded sets $B_X \times B_Y \ni (x^\star, y^\star)$, the **partial primal-dual gap** is
$$
\mathcal G_{B_X\times B_Y}(x, y) = \max_{y'\in B_Y}\Phi(x, y') - \min_{x'\in B_X}\Phi(x', y),
$$
nonnegative, vanishing iff $(x, y)$ is a saddle point inside $B_X\times B_Y$; it is the convergence criterion the rate theorem controls.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"Chambolle-Pock is ADMM."* Both solve the saddle model of [44.02.03], but ADMM [44.06.04] minimizes an augmented Lagrangian blockwise, and its $x$-update $\arg\min_x\{f(x) + \tfrac\rho2\|Ax + Bz - c\|^2\}$ couples $f$ with the quadratic $\|A\cdot\|^2$ — when $A \neq I$ this is a *generalized* prox requiring a linear solve. Chambolle-Pock uses only $\operatorname{prox}_{\tau g}$, $\operatorname{prox}_{\sigma f^*}$, and matrix-vector products with $K, K^\top$: never a solve.

- *"The extrapolation is optional cosmetics."* Dropping it ($\theta = 0$) gives the plain Arrow-Hurwicz primal-dual method, which is *not* guaranteed to converge for general convex $f, g$ under $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$; it needs strong convexity or smaller steps. The $\theta = 1$ over-relaxation is what delivers the $O(1/N)$ guarantee for the general convex case.

- *"Smaller steps always converge, like a gradient method."* The binding condition is the *product* $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$, not each step individually. Shrinking only $\tau$ while $\sigma$ is large can still violate it; both must be controlled jointly through their product against $\|K\|^2$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The convergence of Chambolle-Pock is established not by descent on either variable alone but by a single estimate on the joint primal-dual iterate, telescoped and averaged: the ergodic gap decays as $O(1/N)$. The estimate uses only the firm nonexpansiveness of the two proximal maps and the bilinear coupling through $K$.

**Theorem (ergodic $O(1/N)$ convergence of the primal-dual gap).** *Let $g, f^*$ be proper closed convex, $K$ linear, and let $(x^\star, y^\star)$ be a saddle point of $\Phi$. Run Chambolle-Pock with $\theta = 1$ and $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$. Then the ergodic averages $X_N = \tfrac1N\sum_{n=1}^N x^n$, $Y_N = \tfrac1N\sum_{n=1}^N y^n$ satisfy, for every bounded $B_X\times B_Y$ containing $(x^\star, y^\star)$,*
$$
\mathcal G_{B_X\times B_Y}(X_N, Y_N) \;\le\; \frac{1}{N}\left(\frac{\|x^0 - x\|^2}{2\tau} + \frac{\|y^0 - y\|^2}{2\sigma}\right)\Big|_{\sup\ over\ (x,y)\in B_X\times B_Y} = O\!\left(\frac1N\right).
$$

**Proof.** The prox-ascent step $y^{n+1} = \operatorname{prox}_{\sigma f^*}(y^n + \sigma K\bar x^n)$ has, by the resolvent characterization of [44.06.02], the optimality $\tfrac{1}{\sigma}(y^n + \sigma K\bar x^n - y^{n+1}) \in \partial f^*(y^{n+1})$, i.e. $K\bar x^n - \tfrac1\sigma(y^{n+1} - y^n) \in \partial f^*(y^{n+1})$. The subgradient inequality for the convex $f^*$ then gives, for every $y$,
$$
f^*(y) \ge f^*(y^{n+1}) + \big\langle K\bar x^n - \tfrac1\sigma(y^{n+1} - y^n),\, y - y^{n+1}\big\rangle.
$$
Symmetrically, $x^{n+1} = \operatorname{prox}_{\tau g}(x^n - \tau K^\top y^{n+1})$ gives $-K^\top y^{n+1} - \tfrac1\tau(x^{n+1} - x^n) \in \partial g(x^{n+1})$, hence for every $x$,
$$
g(x) \ge g(x^{n+1}) + \big\langle -K^\top y^{n+1} - \tfrac1\tau(x^{n+1} - x^n),\, x - x^{n+1}\big\rangle.
$$
Add the two inequalities and regroup the bilinear terms into $\Phi$. Using the polarization identity $\langle a - b, c - b\rangle = \tfrac12(\|a-b\|^2 + \|c-b\|^2 - \|a-c\|^2)$ on the $\tfrac1\tau$- and $\tfrac1\sigma$-weighted inner products produces, after collecting,

$$
\Phi(x^{n+1}, y) - \Phi(x, y^{n+1}) \le \frac{\|x - x^n\|^2}{2\tau} - \frac{\|x - x^{n+1}\|^2}{2\tau} + \frac{\|y - y^n\|^2}{2\sigma} - \frac{\|y - y^{n+1}\|^2}{2\sigma} - R_n,
$$

where the residual coupling term $R_n$ collects the extrapolation contribution $\langle K(\bar x^n - x^n), y - y^{n+1}\rangle = \langle K(x^n - x^{n-1}), y - y^{n+1}\rangle$ (using $\bar x^n = 2x^n - x^{n-1}$) together with the squared-distance slack. The condition $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$ is exactly what makes the accumulated $R_n$ terms, after a Cauchy-Schwarz / Young split $|\langle K\delta x, \delta y\rangle| \le \tfrac{\|K\|}{2}(\tfrac{\sqrt{\tau\sigma}}{\sqrt{\tau\sigma}})(\cdots)$, telescope into a nonnegative quantity that can be dropped. Summing the displayed inequality over $n = 0, \dots, N-1$ telescopes the squared distances:
$$
\sum_{n=1}^{N}\big[\Phi(x^n, y) - \Phi(x, y^n)\big] \le \frac{\|x - x^0\|^2}{2\tau} + \frac{\|y - y^0\|^2}{2\sigma}.
$$
By convexity of $\Phi(\cdot, y)$ and concavity of $\Phi(x, \cdot)$ (Jensen), $\Phi(X_N, y) - \Phi(x, Y_N) \le \tfrac1N\sum_n[\Phi(x^n, y) - \Phi(x, y^n)]$. Taking the supremum over $(x,y) \in B_X\times B_Y$ on both sides gives the stated $O(1/N)$ bound on the partial gap. $\square$

**Bridge.** The single telescoping estimate on the joint iterate is the foundational reason Chambolle-Pock converges, and the role of $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$ — absorbing the bilinear coupling so the squared distances telescope cleanly — builds toward the accelerated $O(1/N^2)$ and linear rates of the Advanced results, where strong convexity supplies an extra negative term that sharpens the same estimate. This is exactly the saddle-point reading of [44.02.03] driven by the two proximal primitives of [44.06.02], and it is dual to the operator-splitting analysis of [44.06.04]: where ADMM is Douglas-Rachford on the Fenchel dual, Chambolle-Pock is the proximal point algorithm on the primal-dual monotone operator in a preconditioned metric, so the method generalises forward-backward splitting to a coupled primal-dual pair without ever inverting $K$. Putting these together, the central insight is that the gap functional, not either variable's value, is the right convergence certificate, and the bridge is the resolvent $\operatorname{prox}_{\tau g} = (I + \tau\partial g)^{-1}$ of [44.06.02] — the same primitive that ADMM, forward-backward, and this method all share, here paired with $\operatorname{prox}_{\sigma f^*}$ across the operator $K$.

## Advanced results [Master]

The Chambolle-Pock iteration $y^{n+1} = \operatorname{prox}_{\sigma f^*}(y^n + \sigma K\bar x^n)$, $x^{n+1} = \operatorname{prox}_{\tau g}(x^n - \tau K^\top y^{n+1})$, $\bar x^{n+1} = x^{n+1} + \theta(x^{n+1} - x^n)$ admits a self-contained development: its placement as a preconditioned proximal point method, the base $O(1/N)$ rate, the two accelerated regimes, the no-linear-solve property that separates it from ADMM, and the Condat-Vu extension to a smooth term.

**Theorem 1 (preconditioned proximal point reading).** For $\theta = 1$, Chambolle-Pock is the proximal point algorithm of Rockafellar applied to the maximal monotone primal-dual operator $T(x,y) = (\partial g(x) + K^\top y,\ \partial f^*(y) - Kx)$ in the metric of the symmetric positive-definite preconditioner $M = \begin{psmallmatrix} I/\tau & -K^\top \\ -K & I/\sigma\end{psmallmatrix}$, which is positive definite exactly when $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$ [ref: TODO_REF He & Yuan 2012]. The resulting $M$-resolvent is firmly nonexpansive in the $M$-norm, so the iterates form a Fejér-monotone sequence converging to a zero of $T$ — a saddle point of $\Phi$. This is the structural reason convergence holds for any fixed steps satisfying the product condition, paralleling the averaged-operator reading of ADMM in [44.06.04], but with the operator $K$ entering through the metric rather than through an augmented-Lagrangian quadratic.

**Theorem 2 (base ergodic rate).** For proper closed convex $g, f^*$ with a saddle point, $\theta = 1$, and $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$, the ergodic averages satisfy $\mathcal G_{B_X\times B_Y}(X_N, Y_N) \le \tfrac1N(\tfrac{\|x^0 - x\|^2}{2\tau} + \tfrac{\|y^0 - y\|^2}{2\sigma})$ over $(x,y) \in B_X\times B_Y$, the $O(1/N)$ partial-primal-dual-gap bound [ref: TODO_REF Chambolle & Pock 2011]. The rate is on the ergodic average; the last iterate also converges (by Fejér monotonicity from Theorem 1) but without a uniform worst-case rate in the general nonsmooth case.

**Theorem 3 (acceleration under partial strong convexity, $O(1/N^2)$).** If $g$ is $\gamma$-strongly convex (equivalently $f$, hence $\nabla f^*$, is smooth on the dual side), the adaptive schedule
$$
\theta_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + 2\gamma\tau_n}}, \qquad \tau_{n+1} = \theta_n\tau_n, \qquad \sigma_{n+1} = \frac{\sigma_n}{\theta_n}
$$
keeps $\tau_n\sigma_n\|K\|^2$ invariant and yields $\|x^N - x^\star\|^2 = O(1/N^2)$, the optimal rate for this smoothness class [ref: TODO_REF Chambolle & Pock 2011]. The mechanism is that strong convexity contributes a $\tfrac\gamma2\|\cdot\|^2$ term that lets $1/\tau_n$ grow linearly, so $\tau_n \sim 1/(\gamma n)$ and the primal distance, scaled by $1/\tau_N$, decays quadratically — the saddle-point analogue of Nesterov acceleration for the smooth class.

**Theorem 4 (linear rate under bilateral strong convexity).** If both $g$ and $f^*$ are strongly convex (with moduli $\gamma_g, \gamma_{f^*}$ — equivalently $g$ strongly convex and $f$ smooth, *and* $f$ strongly convex and $g$ smooth, so both primal and dual problems are smooth strongly convex), fixed accelerated steps $\tau, \sigma$ give linear convergence $\|x^N - x^\star\|^2 + \|y^N - y^\star\|^2 = O(\omega^N)$ for some $\omega \in (0,1)$ determined by $\gamma_g, \gamma_{f^*}, \|K\|$ [ref: TODO_REF Chambolle & Pock 2011]. The contraction factor improves as the problem's two-sided conditioning improves, and the optimal fixed steps balance the primal and dual moduli against $\|K\|$ — the primal-dual counterpart of the linear rate of gradient descent on smooth strongly convex objectives.

**Theorem 5 (no linear solve; Condat-Vu extension).** Each Chambolle-Pock step evaluates $\operatorname{prox}_{\tau g}$, $\operatorname{prox}_{\sigma f^*}$, and the actions of $K, K^\top$ — never the inverse of $K$, $K^\top K$, or any quadratically-coupled prox; this is the decisive contrast with ADMM [44.06.04], whose $x$-update $\arg\min_x\{g(x) + \tfrac\rho2\|Kx - z + u\|^2\}$ is a *generalized* prox in the $K^\top K$-metric requiring a linear solve (or a linearizing inner approximation) when $K^\top K$ is not diagonal, exactly the case for the difference operator $K = \nabla$ of imaging [ref: TODO_REF Chambolle & Pock 2011]. The **Condat-Vu** generalization handles a third, smooth term: for $\min_x f(x_{\mathrm{sm}}) + g(x) + h(Kx)$ with $\nabla f_{\mathrm{sm}}$ $\beta$-Lipschitz, the iteration adds a forward gradient step $x^{n+1} = \operatorname{prox}_{\tau g}(x^n - \tau\nabla f_{\mathrm{sm}}(x^n) - \tau K^\top y^n)$, $y^{n+1} = \operatorname{prox}_{\sigma h^*}(y^n + \sigma K(2x^{n+1} - x^n))$, convergent under $\tau(\tfrac\beta2 + \sigma\|K\|^2) \le 1$ and recovering Chambolle-Pock when $f_{\mathrm{sm}} = 0$ [ref: TODO_REF Condat 2013].

**Synthesis.** Chambolle-Pock is the proximal point algorithm on the primal-dual monotone operator $T$ in the preconditioned $M$-metric, and this single identification is the foundational reason the method coheres: the alternating prox-ascent and prox-descent are the two block rows of one $M$-resolvent, the extrapolation $\bar x^n = 2x^n - x^{n-1}$ is the off-diagonal coupling $-K, -K^\top$ in $M$, and the step condition $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$ is exactly the positive-definiteness of $M$ that makes the resolvent firmly nonexpansive. This is exactly the saddle-point form of [44.02.03] solved by the two proximal primitives of [44.06.02], and it is dual to the Douglas-Rachford reading of ADMM in [44.06.04]: where ADMM splits the Fenchel dual through reflected resolvents and pays a linear solve when $K$ is a genuine coupling operator, Chambolle-Pock keeps $K$ in the metric and never inverts it, which is the central insight that makes it the method of choice for total-variation and imaging problems where $K$ is a difference operator with no usable factorization.

The base estimate generalises to the accelerated regimes by a uniform mechanism — strong convexity adds a damping term, $g$-strong-convexity alone buys $O(1/N^2)$ through the adaptive $(\tau_n, \sigma_n, \theta_n)$ schedule, and bilateral strong convexity buys a linear rate — so one gap-descent argument, sharpened by curvature, covers $O(1/N)$, $O(1/N^2)$, and linear convergence at once. Putting these together, the Condat-Vu extension folds a smooth term in by a forward gradient step, placing Chambolle-Pock inside the same three-operator splitting family as forward-backward [44.06.02] and ADMM [44.06.04], all of which are resolvent iterations on $0 \in \mathcal A + \mathcal B$ read through different reformulations of one saddle model.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (prox optimality as a subgradient inclusion).** *The Chambolle-Pock dual and primal updates satisfy $K\bar x^n - \tfrac1\sigma(y^{n+1} - y^n) \in \partial f^*(y^{n+1})$ and $-K^\top y^{n+1} - \tfrac1\tau(x^{n+1} - x^n) \in \partial g(x^{n+1})$.*

*Proof.* The update $y^{n+1} = \operatorname{prox}_{\sigma f^*}(\tilde y)$ with $\tilde y = y^n + \sigma K\bar x^n$ is, by definition, the minimizer of $f^*(y) + \tfrac1{2\sigma}\|y - \tilde y\|^2$. Fermat's rule [44.06.02] gives $0 \in \partial f^*(y^{n+1}) + \tfrac1\sigma(y^{n+1} - \tilde y)$, i.e. $\tfrac1\sigma(\tilde y - y^{n+1}) \in \partial f^*(y^{n+1})$. Substituting $\tilde y = y^n + \sigma K\bar x^n$ gives $K\bar x^n - \tfrac1\sigma(y^{n+1} - y^n) \in \partial f^*(y^{n+1})$. The primal update $x^{n+1} = \operatorname{prox}_{\tau g}(x^n - \tau K^\top y^{n+1})$ is handled identically: $0 \in \partial g(x^{n+1}) + \tfrac1\tau(x^{n+1} - (x^n - \tau K^\top y^{n+1}))$, i.e. $-K^\top y^{n+1} - \tfrac1\tau(x^{n+1} - x^n) \in \partial g(x^{n+1})$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (one-step primal-dual estimate).** *For every $(x, y)$,*
$$
\Phi(x^{n+1}, y) - \Phi(x, y^{n+1}) \le \frac{\|x - x^n\|^2 - \|x - x^{n+1}\|^2}{2\tau} + \frac{\|y - y^n\|^2 - \|y - y^{n+1}\|^2}{2\sigma} - \langle K(x^{n+1} - \bar x^n),\, y^{n+1} - y\rangle - \frac{\|x^{n+1}-x^n\|^2}{2\tau} - \frac{\|y^{n+1}-y^n\|^2}{2\sigma}.$$

*Proof.* The convex subgradient inequality for $f^*$ at $y^{n+1}$ with the inclusion of Proposition 1 gives $f^*(y) \ge f^*(y^{n+1}) + \langle K\bar x^n - \tfrac1\sigma(y^{n+1}-y^n), y - y^{n+1}\rangle$; for $g$ at $x^{n+1}$, $g(x) \ge g(x^{n+1}) + \langle -K^\top y^{n+1} - \tfrac1\tau(x^{n+1}-x^n), x - x^{n+1}\rangle$. Recall $\Phi(x^{n+1}, y) - \Phi(x, y^{n+1}) = [g(x^{n+1}) - g(x)] + [f^*(y^{n+1}) - f^*(y)] + \langle Kx^{n+1}, y\rangle - \langle Kx, y^{n+1}\rangle$. Insert the two inequalities (as upper bounds on $g(x^{n+1}) - g(x)$ and $f^*(y^{n+1}) - f^*(y)$), giving
$$
\Phi(x^{n+1},y) - \Phi(x,y^{n+1}) \le \tfrac1\tau\langle x^{n+1}-x^n, x - x^{n+1}\rangle + \tfrac1\sigma\langle y^{n+1}-y^n, y - y^{n+1}\rangle + \langle K(x^{n+1} - \bar x^n), y^{n+1} - y\rangle\cdot(-1) + B,
$$
where $B$ collects the bilinear terms $\langle Kx^{n+1}, y - y^{n+1}\rangle - \langle K(x^{n+1}-x), y^{n+1}\rangle + \langle K\bar x^n, y - y^{n+1}\rangle$ that combine to $-\langle K(x^{n+1}-\bar x^n), y^{n+1} - y\rangle$. Apply the polarization identity $\langle a - b, c - a\rangle = \tfrac12(\|c-b\|^2 - \|c-a\|^2 - \|a-b\|^2)$ to each inner product $\tfrac1\tau\langle x^{n+1}-x^n, x-x^{n+1}\rangle = \tfrac1{2\tau}(\|x-x^n\|^2 - \|x-x^{n+1}\|^2 - \|x^{n+1}-x^n\|^2)$ and likewise for the $\sigma$-term. Collecting yields the stated inequality. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (the extrapolation makes the coupling telescope).** *With $\theta = 1$, $\bar x^n = 2x^n - x^{n-1}$, so $x^{n+1} - \bar x^n = (x^{n+1} - x^n) - (x^n - x^{n-1})$, and the coupling term telescopes: summing $-\langle K(x^{n+1}-\bar x^n), y^{n+1}-y\rangle$ over $n$ leaves only boundary terms plus a quantity controlled by $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$.*

*Proof.* With $\bar x^n = x^n + (x^n - x^{n-1}) = 2x^n - x^{n-1}$, write $\delta x^n = x^n - x^{n-1}$, so $x^{n+1} - \bar x^n = \delta x^{n+1} - \delta x^n$. Then $-\langle K(\delta x^{n+1} - \delta x^n), y^{n+1} - y\rangle = -\langle K\delta x^{n+1}, y^{n+1}-y\rangle + \langle K\delta x^n, y^{n+1}-y\rangle$. Abel summation regroups $\sum_n[\langle K\delta x^n, y^{n+1}-y\rangle - \langle K\delta x^{n+1}, y^{n+1}-y\rangle]$ into $\sum_n\langle K\delta x^n, y^{n+1} - y^n\rangle$ plus boundary terms $\langle K\delta x^1, y^1 - y\rangle - \langle K\delta x^{N+1}, y^{N+1}-y\rangle$. Each interior term is bounded by Young's inequality, $|\langle K\delta x^n, y^{n+1}-y^n\rangle| \le \tfrac{\|K\|\alpha}{2}\|\delta x^n\|^2 + \tfrac{\|K\|}{2\alpha}\|y^{n+1}-y^n\|^2$; choosing $\alpha = \sqrt{\tau/\sigma}$ makes these absorbable into the $-\tfrac1{2\tau}\|x^{n+1}-x^n\|^2 - \tfrac1{2\sigma}\|y^{n+1}-y^n\|^2$ slack of Proposition 2 precisely when $\|K\|\sqrt{\tau\sigma} < 1$, i.e. $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$. The leftover is nonnegative and is dropped. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (ergodic gap bound).** *Under $\theta = 1$, $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$, summing Propositions 2-3 over $n = 0,\dots,N-1$ and averaging gives $\Phi(X_N, y) - \Phi(x, Y_N) \le \tfrac1N(\tfrac{\|x - x^0\|^2}{2\tau} + \tfrac{\|y - y^0\|^2}{2\sigma})$ for all $(x,y)$.*

*Proof.* Sum the inequality of Proposition 2 over $n = 0,\dots,N-1$. The squared-distance differences telescope to $\tfrac{\|x-x^0\|^2 - \|x-x^N\|^2}{2\tau} + \tfrac{\|y-y^0\|^2 - \|y-y^N\|^2}{2\sigma}$; drop the nonpositive $-\|x-x^N\|^2, -\|y-y^N\|^2$. The coupling sum is handled by Proposition 3, contributing only the absorbed nonnegative quantity (dropped) and boundary terms that vanish since $\delta x^0 = 0$ and the final term is again absorbed. Hence $\sum_{n=1}^N[\Phi(x^n, y) - \Phi(x, y^n)] \le \tfrac{\|x-x^0\|^2}{2\tau} + \tfrac{\|y-y^0\|^2}{2\sigma}$. By convexity of $\Phi(\cdot, y)$ and concavity of $\Phi(x,\cdot)$, Jensen gives $\Phi(X_N, y) - \Phi(x, Y_N) \le \tfrac1N\sum_{n=1}^N[\Phi(x^n, y)-\Phi(x,y^n)]$, and division by $N$ yields the claim. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (the partial gap is the right certificate).** *Taking the supremum of Proposition 4 over $(x,y) \in B_X\times B_Y$ with $(x^\star,y^\star) \in B_X\times B_Y$ gives $\mathcal G_{B_X\times B_Y}(X_N, Y_N) = O(1/N)$, and $\mathcal G = 0$ iff $(X_N, Y_N)$ is a saddle point of $\Phi$ on $B_X\times B_Y$.*

*Proof.* The left side of Proposition 4 is $\Phi(X_N, y) - \Phi(x, Y_N)$; its supremum over $(x,y)\in B_X\times B_Y$ is exactly $\max_{y\in B_Y}\Phi(X_N, y) - \min_{x\in B_X}\Phi(x, Y_N) = \mathcal G_{B_X\times B_Y}(X_N, Y_N)$ by the definition of the partial gap. The right side's supremum is $\tfrac1N\sup_{(x,y)}(\tfrac{\|x-x^0\|^2}{2\tau} + \tfrac{\|y-y^0\|^2}{2\sigma})$, finite since $B_X\times B_Y$ is bounded, so $\mathcal G = O(1/N)$. The gap is a difference of a max and a min of the same function, hence $\ge 0$; it is $0$ iff $\Phi(X_N, y) \le \Phi(X_N, Y_N) \le \Phi(x, Y_N)$ for all admissible $(x,y)$, the saddle-point inequalities. $\square$

**Proposition 6 ($M$-positive-definiteness equals the step condition).** *The block matrix $M = \begin{psmallmatrix} I/\tau & -K^\top \\ -K & I/\sigma\end{psmallmatrix}$ is positive definite iff $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$.*

*Proof.* $M \succ 0$ iff the leading block $I/\tau \succ 0$ (always, $\tau > 0$) and the Schur complement $I/\sigma - K(I/\tau)^{-1}K^\top = I/\sigma - \tau KK^\top \succ 0$. The latter holds iff $\tau\,\lambda_{\max}(KK^\top) < 1/\sigma$, i.e. $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$ since $\lambda_{\max}(KK^\top) = \|K\|^2$. Positive-definiteness of $M$ makes $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle_M$ an inner product, in which the proximal point step of Theorem 1 (Advanced results) is firmly nonexpansive, giving Fejér monotonicity of the iterates toward $\operatorname{zer}T$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Chambolle-Pock solves the Fenchel saddle model of [44.02.03]: the primal $\min_x f(Kx) + g(x)$ becomes $\min_x\max_y\{\langle Kx, y\rangle + g(x) - f^*(y)\}$ by conjugating $f$, and the dual variable $y$ ranges over $\operatorname{dom} f^*$. The conjugate calculus of that unit — that $f^*$ of a norm is a dual-ball indicator, that the prox of an indicator is a projection — is exactly what makes the dual prox-ascent step a closed-form clip in the imaging applications.

- The two updates are the proximal operators of [44.06.02]: $\operatorname{prox}_{\tau g} = (I + \tau\partial g)^{-1}$ and $\operatorname{prox}_{\sigma f^*} = (I + \sigma\partial f^*)^{-1}$, and the firm nonexpansiveness of the resolvent proved there is what makes the preconditioned proximal point operator of this unit contractive in the $M$-metric. Forward-backward of that unit is the single-operator special case; Chambolle-Pock is its primal-dual extension across the coupling operator $K$.

- Chambolle-Pock is the no-linear-solve companion of ADMM and Douglas-Rachford [44.06.04]: both attack the saddle model of [44.02.03] with proximal primitives, but ADMM is Douglas-Rachford on the Fenchel dual whose subproblems can carry a $K^\top K$-coupled quadratic (a linear solve when $K = \nabla$), while Chambolle-Pock keeps $K$ in the metric $M$ and uses only $K, K^\top$ actions — the distinction that decides which method suits total-variation imaging.

- Chambolle-Pock is the saddle-point solver that consumes the structured min-max model whose smoothing was studied in [44.06.08]: Nesterov smoothing of $f(Kx) = \max_y\{\langle Kx, y\rangle - f^*(y)\}$ regularizes the inner $y$ and runs an accelerated method on the smoothed primal, while Chambolle-Pock attacks the same saddle function directly without smoothing, trading the $O(1/\varepsilon)$ smoothed-primal rate for an $O(1/N)$ gap rate that needs no smoothing parameter, and matching the $O(1/N^2)$ accelerated rate when a block is strongly convex.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The primal-dual hybrid gradient method has roots in the **Arrow-Hurwicz** scheme of 1958 for finding saddle points of a Lagrangian by alternating gradient ascent in the dual and gradient descent in the primal, developed in the study of constrained optimization and economic equilibrium [ref: TODO_REF Arrow, Hurwicz & Uzawa 1958]. The modern proximal form for imaging was introduced by **Antonin Chambolle** and **Thomas Pock** in 2011 in *A first-order primal-dual algorithm for convex problems with applications to imaging* (Journal of Mathematical Imaging and Vision 40(1), 120-145), which gave the over-relaxed iteration, the step condition $\tau\sigma\|K\|^2 < 1$, the $O(1/N)$ ergodic-gap theorem, and the accelerated $O(1/N^2)$ and linear variants under strong convexity, together with the total-variation denoising and deconvolution applications that made the method standard in computational imaging [ref: TODO_REF Chambolle & Pock 2011]. The closely related primal-dual splitting that adds a smooth Lipschitz-gradient term was given independently by **Laurent Condat** in 2013 and by **B&#7857;ng C&#244;ng V&#361;** in the same period, yielding the three-operator scheme now called the Condat-Vu method [ref: TODO_REF Condat 2013]. The proximal-point and variational-inequality interpretation, which placed the method inside maximal-monotone-operator theory and explained its convergence as Fejér monotonicity in a preconditioned metric, was developed by **Bingsheng He** and **Xiaoming Yuan** in 2012 [ref: TODO_REF He & Yuan 2012]. The earlier PDHG iteration without the convergence-securing extrapolation was studied by **Mingqiang Zhu** and **Tony Chan** in 2008.
