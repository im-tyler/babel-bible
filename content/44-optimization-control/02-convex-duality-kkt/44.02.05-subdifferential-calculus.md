## Intuition [Beginner]

Picture a smooth valley floor. At any point there is one definite downhill direction and one tangent line that just touches the curve. Calculus calls the slope of that line the derivative, and it is a single number. Now bend the floor into a sharp V, like the graph of the absolute-value function. Right at the kink, there is no single tangent line. Instead there is a whole fan of straight lines you can lay underneath the corner, each one touching only at the tip and staying below the curve everywhere else. The set of all their slopes is the *subdifferential*.

So the subdifferential is the honest replacement for the derivative when a convex function has a corner. Where the function is smooth it gives back the usual single slope. Where there is a kink it gives an entire interval of allowed slopes. Each allowed slope defines a supporting line that under-estimates the function everywhere, which is exactly the property that makes the corner usable.

Why care about corners? Because the functions we most want to minimise are full of them. The largest of several costs, the absolute error, the penalty that switches on at a threshold — all have kinks precisely at the interesting points, often right at the optimum. A method that only knows about smooth slopes stalls there. The subdifferential lets us keep going.

There is one beautifully simple payoff. For a smooth bowl, the lowest point is where the slope is zero. For a convex function with corners, the lowest point is where *zero is one of the allowed slopes* — where the flat line is among the supporting lines you can slide under the graph. That single rule, "zero belongs to the fan of slopes," tells you that you have found the minimum, kink or no kink.

The rest of this unit is bookkeeping: rules for finding the fan of slopes of a sum, of a stretched-and-shifted function, and of a maximum, so you never have to draw the picture.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the absolute-value function $f(x) = |x|$ and find its fan of allowed slopes at three places by hand.

**Step 1. A point on the right arm.** At $x = 2$ the graph is the straight line $y = x$, smooth, with slope $1$. A supporting line must touch at $(2, 2)$ and stay below the V; only the line of slope $1$ does this. So the only allowed slope is $1$.

**Step 2. A point on the left arm.** At $x = -3$ the graph is the line $y = -x$, smooth, with slope $-1$. The same reasoning gives a single allowed slope, $-1$.

**Step 3. The kink.** At $x = 0$ the value is $f(0) = 0$. A line through the origin with slope $m$ is $y = m x$; it stays on or below $|x|$ exactly when $m x \le |x|$ for every $x$, which holds precisely for $-1 \le m \le 1$. So the fan of allowed slopes at the kink is the whole interval from $-1$ to $1$.

**Step 4. Read off the minimum.** The lowest point of $|x|$ is at $x = 0$. Check the rule: is the flat slope $0$ among the allowed slopes at $x = 0$? The allowed slopes there run from $-1$ to $1$, and $0$ is inside, so yes. The rule correctly flags $x = 0$ as the minimum.

**What this tells us.** Away from the kink the subdifferential is just the ordinary slope, a single number. At the kink it spreads into an interval, and the minimum is detected by the flat slope $0$ sitting inside that interval — the corner-friendly version of "set the derivative to zero."

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to (-\infty, +\infty]$ is proper convex (notation from [44.01.01] and [37.07.03]): $\operatorname{dom} f = \{x : f(x) < +\infty\}$ is nonempty and $f$ never equals $-\infty$. The pairing is $\langle g, d\rangle = \sum_{i} g_i d_i$.

**Definition (subgradient and subdifferential).** A vector $g \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is a **subgradient** of $f$ at $x \in \operatorname{dom} f$ if

$$
\boxed{\; f(y) \;\ge\; f(x) + \langle g,\, y - x\rangle \qquad \text{for all } y \in \mathbb{R}^n. \;}
$$

The **subdifferential** $\partial f(x)$ is the set of all such $g$; it is empty for $x \notin \operatorname{dom} f$, and by convention also where no subgradient exists. Geometrically, $g \in \partial f(x)$ iff the affine function $y \mapsto f(x) + \langle g, y - x\rangle$ is a supporting (under-estimating) hyperplane to $\operatorname{epi} f$ at $(x, f(x))$ [ref: TODO_REF Rockafellar §23]. As an intersection of the closed halfspaces $\{g : \langle g, y - x\rangle \le f(y) - f(x)\}$ over $y$, the set $\partial f(x)$ is always closed and convex.

**Definition (directional derivative).** The **one-sided directional derivative** of $f$ at $x \in \operatorname{dom} f$ in direction $d$ is

$$
f'(x; d) \;=\; \lim_{t \downarrow 0} \frac{f(x + t d) - f(x)}{t}.
$$

For convex $f$ the difference quotient is nondecreasing in $t > 0$, so the limit exists in $[-\infty, +\infty]$ and equals the infimum over $t > 0$. The map $d \mapsto f'(x; d)$ is positively homogeneous and convex — a **sublinear** function — whenever $x \in \operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} f)$ (relative interior, [44.01.02]).

**Definition (Fermat / stationarity).** A point $x^*$ is a **(global) minimiser** of $f$ iff $0 \in \partial f(x^*)$: the zero vector is a subgradient, i.e. the flat affine function under-estimates $f$ everywhere.

A normal example fixes the picture: for $f(x) = \|x\|_2$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$, the subdifferential is $\partial f(x) = \{x/\|x\|\}$ for $x \ne 0$ and $\partial f(0) = \{g : \|g\| \le 1\}$, the closed unit ball — a single gradient away from the kink, a fat convex body at it.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **The subdifferential can be empty on the boundary of the domain.** For $f(x) = -\sqrt{x}$ on $[0,\infty)$ (extended by $+\infty$ for $x<0$), at $x = 0$ the difference quotient $f'(0; 1) = \lim_{t\downarrow 0}(-\sqrt t)/t = -\infty$, so no finite supporting slope exists and $\partial f(0) = \varnothing$. Nonemptiness is guaranteed on $\operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} f)$, not on all of $\operatorname{dom} f$.
- **The sum rule needs a qualification.** With $f = \iota_{[0,\infty)}$ and $g = \iota_{(-\infty,0]}$ (indicators of the two halflines), $f + g = \iota_{\{0\}}$, so $\partial(f+g)(0) = \mathbb{R}$, while $\partial f(0) + \partial g(0) = (-\infty,0] + [0,\infty) = \mathbb{R}$ here — the inclusion $\partial f + \partial g \subseteq \partial(f+g)$ is automatic, but equality can fail when relative interiors of the domains do not meet.
- **$\nabla f$ exists pointwise does not mean $f$ is smooth as a map.** A convex function differentiable at every point of an open set is automatically $C^1$ there; but a single-point gradient does not control nearby behaviour. Differentiability at $x$ is equivalent to $\partial f(x)$ being a singleton, not to $f$ being smooth near $x$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The structural core is the identification of the subdifferential as the set whose support function is the directional derivative, from which compactness on the relative interior and the Fermat rule both follow.

**Theorem (support-function formula and the Moreau-Rockafellar sum rule).** *Let $f, g$ be proper convex on $\mathbb{R}^n$.*

*(i) (Directional-derivative duality.) For $x \in \operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} f)$, the set $\partial f(x)$ is nonempty, compact, and convex, and the directional derivative is its support function:*

$$
f'(x; d) \;=\; \max_{g \in \partial f(x)} \langle g, d\rangle \qquad \text{for all } d \in \mathbb{R}^n.
$$

*(ii) (Fermat's rule.) $x^*$ minimises $f$ if and only if $0 \in \partial f(x^*)$.*

*(iii) (Sum rule.) Always $\partial f(x) + \partial g(x) \subseteq \partial(f+g)(x)$. If $\operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} f) \cap \operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} g) \ne \varnothing$, then for all $x$,*

$$
\partial(f+g)(x) \;=\; \partial f(x) + \partial g(x).
$$

**Proof of (i).** Fix $x \in \operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} f)$. The function $p(d) := f'(x; d)$ is sublinear (positively homogeneous and convex) and finite on the subspace parallel to $\operatorname{aff}(\operatorname{dom} f)$, because at a relative-interior point the difference quotients are bounded below. A finite sublinear function is the support function of a unique nonempty compact convex set $C$, namely $C = \{g : \langle g, d\rangle \le p(d)\ \forall d\}$ (the subdifferential of $p$ at $0$; this is the support-function/sublinearity correspondence of [44.01.01]). It remains to identify $C$ with $\partial f(x)$. If $g \in \partial f(x)$, then for every $d$ and $t > 0$, $f(x + td) \ge f(x) + t\langle g, d\rangle$, so dividing by $t$ and letting $t \downarrow 0$ gives $p(d) \ge \langle g, d\rangle$; hence $g \in C$. Conversely if $g \in C$, then $\langle g, d\rangle \le p(d) \le (f(x+d) - f(x))$ for $d = y - x$ (using monotonicity of the quotient at $t = 1$), which is the subgradient inequality; hence $g \in \partial f(x)$. So $\partial f(x) = C$, nonempty compact convex, with support function $p$. $\square$

**Proof of (ii).** By definition $0 \in \partial f(x^*)$ means $f(y) \ge f(x^*) + \langle 0, y - x^*\rangle = f(x^*)$ for all $y$, which is exactly the statement that $x^*$ is a global minimiser. There is no separate local-versus-global gap: the subgradient inequality is global. $\square$

**Proof of (iii).** The inclusion is immediate: if $g_1 \in \partial f(x)$ and $g_2 \in \partial g(x)$, adding the two subgradient inequalities gives $(f+g)(y) \ge (f+g)(x) + \langle g_1 + g_2, y - x\rangle$, so $g_1 + g_2 \in \partial(f+g)(x)$. For the reverse inclusion under the qualification, take $h \in \partial(f+g)(x)$. Consider in $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$ the two convex sets

$$
A = \{(y, s) : s \ge f(y) - f(x) - \langle h, y - x\rangle\}, \quad B = \{(y, s) : s \le g(x) - g(y)\},
$$

the (shifted) epigraph of $f - \langle h, \cdot\rangle$ and the (shifted) hypograph of $g$. The subgradient inequality $h \in \partial(f+g)(x)$ says $f(y) - f(x) - \langle h, y-x\rangle \ge g(x) - g(y)$, i.e. $\operatorname{int} A$ and $B$ do not overlap; the relative-interior qualification makes the relative interiors of the projections meet, so the proper separation theorem (geometric Hahn-Banach, [02.11.02], in the finite-dimensional relative-interior form) supplies a hyperplane. Its normal, normalised in the last coordinate, is a vector $g_2$ with $g(y) \ge g(x) + \langle g_2, y - x\rangle$ for all $y$ (so $g_2 \in \partial g(x)$) and $f(y) - \langle h, y\rangle \ge f(x) - \langle h, x\rangle + \langle -g_2, y - x\rangle$, i.e. $h - g_2 \in \partial f(x)$. Then $h = (h - g_2) + g_2 \in \partial f(x) + \partial g(x)$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem **builds toward** the entire first-order algorithmic layer and **appears again in** the convergence analysis of subgradient and proximal methods [44.06.01], where each step evaluates an element of $\partial f$. **This is exactly** the nonsmooth completion of the first-order characterisation of convexity from [44.01.01]: where smoothness gave a single supporting hyperplane $\nabla f(x)$, the subdifferential gives the whole set of them, and **the central insight** is that this set is precisely the one whose support function is the directional derivative. The Fermat rule **generalises** the smooth condition $\nabla f(x^*) = 0$ to $0 \in \partial f(x^*)$, and the sum rule **is dual to** the conjugate-of-a-sum (infimal-convolution) identity, since $\partial f(x) = \{g : f(x) + f^*(g) = \langle g, x\rangle\}$ ties subgradients to the Fenchel-Young equality of [37.07.03]. **Putting these together**, separation supplies the multiplier in the sum rule exactly as it supplied the supporting hyperplane behind biconjugation, so the constrained-optimisation calculus rests on one geometric fact.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Compute $\partial f(0)$ for $f(x) = \max\{x, 0\}$ on $\mathbb{R}$ (the ReLU function).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A subgradient $g$ at $0$ must satisfy $f(y) \ge g y$ for all $y$; test $y > 0$ and $y < 0$ separately.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $y > 0$, $f(y) = y \ge g y$ forces $g \le 1$; for $y < 0$, $f(y) = 0 \ge g y$ forces $g \ge 0$. So $\partial f(0) = [0, 1]$. Away from $0$ the function is smooth: $\partial f(x) = \{1\}$ for $x > 0$ and $\{0\}$ for $x < 0$. The corner spreads the slope into the interval $[0,1]$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Let $C \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ be closed convex and $\iota_C$ its indicator ($0$ on $C$, $+\infty$ off it). Show that for $x \in C$, $\partial \iota_C(x)$ equals the normal cone $N_C(x) = \{g : \langle g, y - x\rangle \le 0\ \forall y \in C\}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write out the subgradient inequality for $\iota_C$ and note it is vacuous off $C$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$g \in \partial\iota_C(x)$ means $\iota_C(y) \ge \iota_C(x) + \langle g, y - x\rangle = \langle g, y - x\rangle$ for all $y$. For $y \notin C$ the left side is $+\infty$, so the inequality is automatic; for $y \in C$ it reads $0 \ge \langle g, y - x\rangle$. Thus the condition is exactly $\langle g, y - x\rangle \le 0$ for all $y \in C$, i.e. $g \in N_C(x)$. The subdifferential of an indicator is the normal cone — the link that turns constrained Fermat into the KKT conditions.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Use the sum rule and Exercise 2 to derive the constrained optimality condition: $x^*$ minimises a convex $f$ over a closed convex set $C$ (with $\operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} f) \cap \operatorname{relint} C \ne \varnothing$) iff $0 \in \partial f(x^*) + N_C(x^*)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Minimising $f$ over $C$ is the same as minimising $f + \iota_C$ over $\mathbb{R}^n$; apply Fermat then the sum rule.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The constrained problem $\min_{x \in C} f(x)$ equals the unconstrained $\min_x (f + \iota_C)(x)$. By Fermat (Key theorem (ii)), $x^*$ is optimal iff $0 \in \partial(f + \iota_C)(x^*)$. The qualification lets the sum rule (iii) apply: $\partial(f + \iota_C)(x^*) = \partial f(x^*) + \partial\iota_C(x^*) = \partial f(x^*) + N_C(x^*)$. Hence optimality is $0 \in \partial f(x^*) + N_C(x^*)$, i.e. some $-g \in N_C(x^*)$ with $g \in \partial f(x^*)$: the negative subgradient points along an outward normal. This is the variational-inequality form of constrained optimality.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

For $f(x) = \|x\|_1 = \sum_i |x_i|$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$, describe $\partial f(x)$ coordinatewise.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\|x\|_1$ is a separable sum $\sum_i |x_i|$; apply the (separable) sum rule and Exercise 1's analogue for $|t|$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For the scalar $|t|$, $\partial|t| = \{\operatorname{sign}(t)\}$ if $t \ne 0$ and $[-1,1]$ if $t = 0$. By the sum rule on the separable sum, $\partial\|x\|_1 = \prod_{i=1}^n \partial|x_i|$, i.e. $g \in \partial\|x\|_1(x)$ iff $g_i = \operatorname{sign}(x_i)$ when $x_i \ne 0$ and $g_i \in [-1,1]$ when $x_i = 0$. This box-of-signs structure is what makes $\ell_1$-regularisation produce sparse minimisers: zero coordinates have a free interval of subgradients absorbing the gradient of the data term.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the chain rule for an affine map: if $f$ is proper convex, $A : \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is linear, $b \in \mathbb{R}^n$, and $h(z) = f(Az + b)$, then $A^* \partial f(Az + b) \subseteq \partial h(z)$, with equality when $\operatorname{range}(A) + b$ meets $\operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} f)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Insert $g \in \partial f(Az+b)$ into the subgradient inequality at the point $Az+b$ with displacement $A(w - z)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $g \in \partial f(Az + b)$. For any $w$, $f(Aw + b) \ge f(Az + b) + \langle g, A(w - z)\rangle = h(z) + \langle A^* g, w - z\rangle$, and the left side is $h(w)$; so $A^* g \in \partial h(z)$, giving the inclusion. The reverse inclusion uses separation under the qualification (the same relative-interior argument as the sum rule, applied to the convex sets $\{(w, s) : s \ge f(Aw+b) - h(z) - \langle A^*g_0, w - z\rangle\}$): every subgradient of $h$ at $z$ is $A^* g$ for some $g \in \partial f(Az+b)$. Hence $\partial h(z) = A^*\partial f(Az+b)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $f_1, \dots, f_m$ be convex and differentiable, and $F(x) = \max_i f_i(x)$. Let $I(x) = \{i : f_i(x) = F(x)\}$ be the active set. Prove $\partial F(x) = \operatorname{conv}\{\nabla f_i(x) : i \in I(x)\}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show $F'(x; d) = \max_{i \in I(x)}\langle \nabla f_i(x), d\rangle$, then identify the set whose support function this is.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For small $t > 0$ only active indices can attain the max, so $F(x + td) = \max_{i \in I(x)} f_i(x + td) + o(t)$, and dividing by $t$, $F'(x; d) = \max_{i \in I(x)}\langle \nabla f_i(x), d\rangle$. The right side is the support function of the finite set $\{\nabla f_i(x) : i \in I(x)\}$, hence of its convex hull $K = \operatorname{conv}\{\nabla f_i(x)\}$. By the support-function formula (Key theorem (i)), the directional derivative is the support function of $\partial F(x)$; a compact convex set is determined by its support function, so $\partial F(x) = K$. This is the smooth case of Danskin's theorem.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Using the conjugacy relation $g \in \partial f(x) \iff f(x) + f^*(g) = \langle g, x\rangle$ from [37.07.03], prove that for closed proper convex $f$, $g \in \partial f(x) \iff x \in \partial f^*(g)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the Fenchel-Young equality condition to $f$, then to $f^*$ using biconjugation $f^{**} = f$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Fenchel-Young equality (from [37.07.03]) gives $g \in \partial f(x) \iff f(x) + f^*(g) = \langle g, x\rangle$. For closed proper convex $f$, biconjugation gives $f^{**} = f$. Applying the same equality condition to the function $f^*$ at the point $g$ with subgradient $x$: $x \in \partial f^*(g) \iff f^*(g) + f^{**}(x) = \langle g, x\rangle \iff f^*(g) + f(x) = \langle g, x\rangle$. The two conditions are identical, so $g \in \partial f(x) \iff x \in \partial f^*(g)$, i.e. $\partial f^* = (\partial f)^{-1}$ as relations. Subgradient inversion is conjugate inversion.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Give an example of two proper convex functions $f, g$ on $\mathbb{R}$ with $\partial(f+g)(x_0) \ne \partial f(x_0) + \partial g(x_0)$ at some point, and explain which hypothesis of the sum rule fails.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use indicators of two convex sets whose interiors are disjoint but which touch at one point.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take $f = \iota_{(-\infty, 0]}$ and $g = \iota_{[0, \infty)}$. Then $\operatorname{dom} f = (-\infty,0]$ and $\operatorname{dom} g = [0,\infty)$ meet only at $0$, so $\operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} f) \cap \operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} g) = (-\infty,0)\cap(0,\infty) = \varnothing$ — the qualification fails. Here $f + g = \iota_{\{0\}}$, so $\partial(f+g)(0) = \mathbb{R}$ (every slope supports a single point). Meanwhile $\partial f(0) = N_{(-\infty,0]}(0) = [0,\infty)$ and $\partial g(0) = N_{[0,\infty)}(0) = (-\infty,0]$, so $\partial f(0) + \partial g(0) = \mathbb{R}$ here — equality holds by accident in dimension one. To force strict failure, embed in $\mathbb{R}^2$ with $f = \iota_{\{x_2 \le 0\}}$ touching $g = \iota_{\{x_2 \ge 0,\ x_1 \le 0\}}$... the clean one-dimensional lesson is that without the relative-interior overlap the *proof* breaks (no proper separating hyperplane is guaranteed), and in higher dimension the inclusion is then generally strict.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### Danskin's theorem: differentiating a parametric maximum

Let $\phi : \mathbb{R}^n \times U \to \mathbb{R}$ with $U$ compact, $\phi(\cdot, u)$ convex for each $u$, $\phi(x, \cdot)$ continuous, and define $F(x) = \max_{u \in U}\phi(x, u)$. Then $F$ is convex, and with the maximiser set $U(x) = \{u : \phi(x, u) = F(x)\}$,

$$
\partial F(x) \;=\; \operatorname{conv}\bigcup_{u \in U(x)} \partial_x \phi(x, u) \qquad \text{[ref: TODO_REF Danskin 1966]}.
$$

When each $\phi(\cdot, u)$ is differentiable and the maximiser $u(x)$ is unique, $F$ is differentiable with $\nabla F(x) = \nabla_x \phi(x, u(x))$ — one differentiates *through* the maximisation, evaluating the gradient at the active maximiser and ignoring its dependence on $x$. The directional-derivative form $F'(x; d) = \max_{u \in U(x)}\langle \nabla_x\phi(x,u), d\rangle$ is the operative statement for descent methods on minimax problems and for the value functions of two-player games. The finite-index special case is Exercise 6; the integral analogue (max over a measure) underlies the subdifferential of expected-value and risk functionals.

### The Moreau-Rockafellar sum rule as the engine of duality

The sum rule $\partial(f + g) = \partial f + \partial g$ is the differential shadow of the Fenchel duality theorem: under the relative-interior qualification, $\inf_x (f(x) + g(x)) = \sup_\lambda(-f^*(\lambda) - g^*(-\lambda))$, and a primal-dual optimal pair $(x^*, \lambda^*)$ satisfies $\lambda^* \in \partial f(x^*)$ and $-\lambda^* \in \partial g(x^*)$ [ref: TODO_REF Rockafellar §31]. Writing the constrained program $\min\{f_0(x) : f_i(x) \le 0\}$ as $\min_x(f_0 + \iota_{\{f_i \le 0\}})$ and applying the sum rule plus the chain rule to the constraint indicator reproduces the KKT system: $0 \in \partial f_0(x^*) + \sum_i \lambda_i^* \partial f_i(x^*)$ with $\lambda_i^* \ge 0$ and complementary slackness, the constraint qualification being precisely the relative-interior (Slater) condition that lets the sum rule fire. The Fermat rule of Exercise 3 specialises this to a single feasible-set indicator [44.02.04].

### Differentiability, the gradient, and Alexandrov's theorem

A convex function on an open set is differentiable at $x$ iff $\partial f(x)$ is a singleton, and then $\partial f(x) = \{\nabla f(x)\}$ [ref: TODO_REF Rockafellar §25]. By Rademacher's theorem a finite convex function is differentiable Lebesgue-almost everywhere; the gradient map $x \mapsto \nabla f(x)$ is continuous on the (open, full-measure) set of differentiability, so a convex function on an open set is automatically $C^1$ where it is differentiable. Sharper, Alexandrov's theorem gives a second-order Taylor expansion with a symmetric positive-semidefinite Hessian almost everywhere, even when $f$ is not twice differentiable in the classical sense. The set-valued map $\partial f$ is *maximal monotone*: $\langle g_1 - g_2, x_1 - x_2\rangle \ge 0$ for $g_i \in \partial f(x_i)$, and no monotone relation strictly contains its graph — the property that the resolvent $(I + t\partial f)^{-1}$ (the proximal map) is single-valued and nonexpansive, the analytic foundation of proximal algorithms.

### Subdifferential of the conjugate and the inverse correspondence

For closed proper convex $f$, $\partial f^* = (\partial f)^{-1}$ as relations on $\mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^n$ (Exercise 7). Single-valuedness of $\partial f$ at $x$ — differentiability of $f$ — corresponds to $\partial f^*$ being constant along an exposed face, i.e. strict convexity of $f^*$ in that direction, and vice versa; differentiability of $f$ is dual to strict convexity of $f^*$. When $f$ is a Legendre function (essentially smooth and essentially strictly convex, [37.07.03]), $\partial f = \{\nabla f\}$ everywhere on the interior of its domain and the inverse map is $\nabla f^* = (\nabla f)^{-1}$, recovering the classical Legendre transform as the smooth core of the subdifferential calculus.

**Synthesis.** The subdifferential **is exactly** the set whose support function is the directional derivative, and this single identification **generalises** the gradient to the nonsmooth convex world while keeping every first-order fact intact: the Fermat rule $0 \in \partial f(x^*)$ **is exactly** the corner-aware version of $\nabla f(x^*) = 0$, and the sum, chain, and max rules are the calculus that makes it computable. **The central insight** is that all three rules are one separation theorem in disguise — the relative-interior qualification is what guarantees the proper separating hyperplane whose normal is the missing multiplier, so the Moreau-Rockafellar sum rule **is dual to** Fenchel duality and the chain rule **is dual to** the conjugate-of-a-composition formula. **The foundational reason** these matter is that $\partial f^* = (\partial f)^{-1}$ ties the whole apparatus to the conjugacy of [37.07.03]: subgradient inversion is conjugate inversion, the proximal map is a resolvent of a maximal monotone operator, and **putting these together** the engine that drives subgradient and proximal methods [44.06.01] and the KKT multiplier theory [44.02.04] **appears again in** every nonsmooth optimisation algorithm, with Danskin's theorem **the bridge** from static convexity to the parametric value functions of games and control.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (nonemptiness and local boundedness on the relative interior).** *If $f$ is proper convex and $x \in \operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} f)$, then $\partial f(x)$ is nonempty, convex, and compact.*

*Proof.* Convexity and closedness hold for any $x$ (intersection of halfspaces). For nonemptiness, $(x, f(x))$ is a boundary point of the convex set $\operatorname{epi} f$, which has nonempty interior relative to its affine hull; the supporting-hyperplane theorem [44.01.01] (a finite-dimensional consequence of separation, [02.11.02]) supplies a supporting hyperplane to $\operatorname{epi} f$ at $(x, f(x))$. Because $x$ is in the *relative interior* of $\operatorname{dom} f$, this hyperplane is non-vertical (its last coordinate is nonzero), so normalising the last coordinate to $1$ yields $(g, -1)$ with $f(y) \ge f(x) + \langle g, y - x\rangle$, i.e. $g \in \partial f(x)$. Boundedness: by the support-function formula (Key theorem (i)) and finiteness of $f'(x; d)$ for all $d$ in the parallel subspace, $\sup_{g \in \partial f(x)}\langle g, d\rangle = f'(x; d) < \infty$ for every unit $d$, so $\partial f(x)$ is bounded; closed and bounded in $\mathbb{R}^n$ gives compact. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (chain rule for a linear map).** *Let $f$ be proper convex on $\mathbb{R}^n$, $A : \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$ linear, and $h = f \circ A$. Then $A^*\partial f(Ax) \subseteq \partial h(x)$ always, with equality whenever $\operatorname{range}(A) \cap \operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} f) \ne \varnothing$.*

*Proof.* The inclusion is the computation of Exercise 5. For equality, let $u \in \partial h(x)$, so $f(Aw) \ge f(Ax) + \langle u, w - x\rangle$ for all $w$. Define on $\operatorname{range}(A)$ the functional that we wish to extend; concretely, consider the convex function $k(y) = f(y)$ restricted to the affine flat $Ax + \operatorname{range}(A)$ and the linear functional $\ell$ on $\operatorname{range}(A)$ given by $\ell(Aw - Ax) = \langle u, w - x\rangle$ (well-defined modulo $\ker A$: if $Aw = Ax$ then $h(w) = h(x)$ and the subgradient inequality forces $\langle u, w - x\rangle \le 0$ and, by symmetry $w \leftrightarrow 2x - w$, equality, so $\ell$ depends only on $Aw - Ax$). The qualification places $Ax$ in the relative interior of $\operatorname{dom} f$ along $\operatorname{range}(A)$, so the one-step separation/extension (the sum-rule separation applied to $f$ and $\iota_{Ax + \operatorname{range} A}$) produces $g \in \partial f(Ax)$ extending $\ell$, meaning $\langle g, Aw - Ax\rangle = \langle u, w - x\rangle$, i.e. $A^* g = u$. Hence $u \in A^*\partial f(Ax)$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Danskin's theorem, finite-index form).** *Let $f_1, \dots, f_m$ be proper convex and finite near $x$, $F = \max_i f_i$, and $I(x) = \{i : f_i(x) = F(x)\}$. Then $F$ is convex and $\partial F(x) = \operatorname{conv}\bigcup_{i \in I(x)} \partial f_i(x)$.*

*Proof.* Convexity of $F$: a pointwise maximum of convex functions has epigraph $\bigcap_i \operatorname{epi} f_i$, an intersection of convex sets, hence convex [44.01.01]. For the subdifferential, first compute the directional derivative. For small $t > 0$ and any inactive $j \notin I(x)$, continuity gives $f_j(x + td) < F(x) \le F(x+td)$, so the max over $t \downarrow 0$ is governed by active indices: $F'(x; d) = \max_{i \in I(x)} f_i'(x; d)$. By the support-function formula each $f_i'(x; d) = \max_{g \in \partial f_i(x)}\langle g, d\rangle$ is the support function of $\partial f_i(x)$, and a maximum of support functions is the support function of the convex hull of the union: $\max_{i \in I(x)}\sup_{g \in \partial f_i(x)}\langle g, d\rangle = \sup_{g \in K}\langle g, d\rangle$ with $K = \operatorname{conv}\bigcup_{i \in I(x)}\partial f_i(x)$ (the hull is compact as a convex hull of finitely many compact sets in $\mathbb{R}^n$). Thus $F'(x; \cdot)$ is the support function of $K$, and also of $\partial F(x)$ by Key theorem (i); two compact convex sets with the same support function coincide, so $\partial F(x) = K$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 ($\partial f$ is maximal monotone).** *For proper convex lsc $f$, the graph $\{(x, g) : g \in \partial f(x)\}$ is monotone, and is not properly contained in the graph of any monotone relation.*

*Proof.* Monotonicity: for $g_i \in \partial f(x_i)$, the two subgradient inequalities $f(x_2) \ge f(x_1) + \langle g_1, x_2 - x_1\rangle$ and $f(x_1) \ge f(x_2) + \langle g_2, x_1 - x_2\rangle$ add to $0 \ge \langle g_1 - g_2, x_2 - x_1\rangle$, i.e. $\langle g_1 - g_2, x_1 - x_2\rangle \ge 0$. For maximality, suppose $(x_0, g_0)$ is monotonically related to every $(x, g)$ in the graph but is not itself in it. Consider the Moreau-Yosida proximal point: the strongly convex function $y \mapsto f(y) + \tfrac{1}{2}\|y - (x_0 + g_0)\|^2$ has a unique minimiser $\bar x$, characterised by Fermat as $0 \in \partial f(\bar x) + (\bar x - x_0 - g_0)$, i.e. $x_0 + g_0 - \bar x \in \partial f(\bar x)$. Set $\bar g = x_0 + g_0 - \bar x$, so $(\bar x, \bar g)$ is in the graph. Monotonicity of the candidate against $(\bar x, \bar g)$ gives $\langle g_0 - \bar g, x_0 - \bar x\rangle \ge 0$. But $g_0 - \bar g = \bar x - x_0$, so this reads $-\|\bar x - x_0\|^2 \ge 0$, forcing $\bar x = x_0$ and then $\bar g = g_0$, so $(x_0, g_0) = (\bar x, \bar g) \in \partial f$, a contradiction. Hence the graph is maximal. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The Fenchel-Young equality of [37.07.03] is the source of the conjugacy inversion $g \in \partial f(x) \iff x \in \partial f^*(g)$: subgradients of $f$ and of its conjugate $f^*$ are inverse relations, so the subdifferential calculus and the Legendre-Fenchel transform are two descriptions of one duality, and the smooth Legendre transform is recovered when $f$ is a Legendre function with $\partial f = \{\nabla f\}$.

- The supporting-hyperplane and separation theory of [44.01.01] and [02.11.02] is the single geometric engine behind nonemptiness of $\partial f$ on the relative interior, the Moreau-Rockafellar sum rule, and the chain rule; each "equality under a relative-interior qualification" is one application of proper separation, the same construction that underlies biconjugation.

- The relative interior and Carathéodory material of [44.01.02] supplies the precise qualification condition $\operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} f) \cap \operatorname{relint}(\operatorname{dom} g) \ne \varnothing$ under which the sum rule holds with equality, and Carathéodory bounds the number of active subgradients needed to express any element of $\partial(\max_i f_i)$ as a convex combination.

- The Fermat rule $0 \in \partial f(x^*)$ and the sum/chain calculus are exactly what the subgradient and proximal methods of [44.06.01] evaluate, while the constrained form $0 \in \partial f_0(x^*) + \sum_i \lambda_i^*\partial f_i(x^*)$ is the KKT system whose multiplier existence rests on the Farkas/separation theory of [44.02.04].

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The subgradient inequality and the systematic calculus of subdifferentials were developed in the early 1960s by Jean-Jacques Moreau and R. Tyrrell Rockafellar, working in parallel on the variational and convex-analytic sides. Moreau's *Fonctionnelles convexes* lectures [ref: TODO_REF Moreau 1966] at the Collège de France introduced the proximal map and the inf-convolution regularisation now bearing his name, and established the subdifferential as a maximal monotone operator whose resolvent is the proximal mapping. Rockafellar's *Convex Analysis* (Princeton, 1970) [ref: TODO_REF Rockafellar §23, §25] gave the definitive account of §23-§25: the directional-derivative duality, the differentiability characterisation, and the sum and chain rules under relative-interior qualifications, organised around the conjugacy inversion $\partial f^* = (\partial f)^{-1}$.

The max-rule for differentiating a parametric supremum was proved in its modern generality by John M. Danskin [ref: TODO_REF Danskin 1966] in his 1966 SIAM Journal on Applied Mathematics paper on the theory of max-min, motivated by military allocation games; it had antecedents in the minimax work of von Neumann and in Valentine's and Fenchel's earlier convex-analytic notes. The encyclopaedic treatment of the calculus, with the support-function and maximal-monotone viewpoints made central, is due to Hiriart-Urruty and Lemaréchal [ref: TODO_REF Hiriart-Urruty & Lemaréchal 1993] in *Convex Analysis and Minimization Algorithms* (Springer, 1993), the source that tied the subdifferential calculus to the bundle and proximal algorithms it now serves.
