## Intuition [Beginner]

A set of points is *convex* when, for any two points you pick inside it, the entire straight line segment joining them also stays inside. A filled disk is convex; a crescent moon is not, because you can find two points in the crescent whose connecting segment pokes out into the empty bite. This one rule — no shortcuts leave the set — is the whole idea, and almost everything in optimization is built to keep it true.

A *convex function* is the matching idea for a curve or surface. Picture a bowl. If you mark two points on the bowl and stretch a straight string between them, the string never dips below the bowl's surface; it always rides on or above it. Functions whose graphs cup upward like a bowl are convex. The squared-distance function, the absolute value, and the exponential all cup this way.

Why does this matter so much? Because for a bowl-shaped landscape, every valley is *the* valley. There are no false bottoms, no side pockets where you could get stuck thinking you have found the lowest point. If you keep walking downhill, you reach the global minimum. That single guarantee is why convex problems are the ones we can actually solve, reliably and at scale, while general problems hide traps everywhere.

The catch is recognizing convexity in the wild. A real modeling problem arrives as a tangle of sums, maximums, and compositions, not as a tidy bowl. So the practical skill is a bookkeeping one: you learn a short list of building blocks that are convex, plus a list of operations — adding, taking maximums, certain rescalings — that turn convex pieces into convex wholes. Then you assemble your model from safe parts and certify the whole thing is convex without ever drawing it.

This unit is that toolkit. It defines the sets and functions precisely, gives the tests for spotting them, and catalogs the operations that preserve convexity, so the rest of optimization can stand on top.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the function $f(x) = x^2$ and check the bowl property at two specific points, then read off the consequence.

**Step 1. Pick two points.** Use $a = -1$ and $b = 3$. Their function values are $f(-1) = 1$ and $f(3) = 9$.

**Step 2. Find the midpoint of the segment.** The midpoint of $a$ and $b$ is $m = \tfrac{-1 + 3}{2} = 1$. The straight-line (chord) height above $m$ is the average of the endpoint heights: $\tfrac{1 + 9}{2} = 5$.

**Step 3. Compare with the actual curve.** The curve's own value at $m = 1$ is $f(1) = 1^2 = 1$. Since $1$ is below $5$, the curve dips beneath the chord exactly as a bowl should.

**Step 4. Try an off-center point too.** Take the point one quarter of the way from $a$ to $b$: $x = -1 + \tfrac{1}{4}(3 - (-1)) = 0$. The chord height there is $\tfrac{3}{4}\cdot 1 + \tfrac{1}{4}\cdot 9 = 0.75 + 2.25 = 3$. The curve value is $f(0) = 0$, again below the chord's $3$.

**What this tells us.** At every test point the parabola sits on or below the straight line connecting its endpoints. That is the defining inequality of a convex function, checked by hand. Because $x^2$ passes this test for *every* pair of points, its single lowest point at $x = 0$ is the one and only minimum — no other dip can exist.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, the ambient space is $\mathbb{R}^n$ with the standard inner product $\langle x, y\rangle = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i y_i$ and Euclidean norm $\|x\| = \langle x, x\rangle^{1/2}$.

**Definition (convex set).** A set $C \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is **convex** if for all $x, y \in C$ and all $\theta \in [0, 1]$ the point $\theta x + (1-\theta) y$ lies in $C$. The point $\theta x + (1-\theta) y$ is a **convex combination** of $x$ and $y$; more generally a convex combination of $x_1, \dots, x_k$ is $\sum_{i=1}^k \theta_i x_i$ with $\theta_i \geq 0$ and $\sum_i \theta_i = 1$. A set is convex if and only if it contains every convex combination of finitely many of its points [ref: TODO_REF Rockafellar §2].

**Definition (basic convex sets).** A **hyperplane** is a set $\{x : \langle a, x\rangle = b\}$ for $a \in \mathbb{R}^n \setminus \{0\}$, $b \in \mathbb{R}$; a **halfspace** is $\{x : \langle a, x\rangle \leq b\}$ (see [01.01.18]). Hyperplanes and halfspaces are convex. A **polyhedron** is a finite intersection of halfspaces. The **convex hull** $\operatorname{conv} S$ of a set $S$ is the smallest convex set containing $S$, equivalently the set of all convex combinations of points of $S$. A set $K$ is a **cone** if $x \in K$ and $\lambda \geq 0$ imply $\lambda x \in K$; it is a **convex cone** if additionally it is closed under addition, i.e. $x, y \in K \implies x + y \in K$.

**Definition (convex function).** A function $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to (-\infty, +\infty]$ is **convex** if its effective domain $\operatorname{dom} f = \{x : f(x) < +\infty\}$ is convex and, for all $x, y \in \operatorname{dom} f$ and $\theta \in [0, 1]$,

$$
\boxed{\; f(\theta x + (1-\theta) y) \;\leq\; \theta f(x) + (1-\theta) f(y). \;}
$$

It is **strictly convex** if the inequality is strict for $x \neq y$ and $\theta \in (0,1)$, and **concave** if $-f$ is convex. The **epigraph** is $\operatorname{epi} f = \{(x, t) \in \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R} : f(x) \leq t\}$, and the **$\alpha$-sublevel set** is $S_\alpha = \{x : f(x) \leq \alpha\}$.

**Definition (quasiconvex, log-convex).** A function $f$ is **quasiconvex** if every sublevel set $S_\alpha$ is convex; equivalently $f(\theta x + (1-\theta) y) \leq \max\{f(x), f(y)\}$. A positive function $f > 0$ is **log-convex** if $\log f$ is convex, and **log-concave** if $\log f$ is concave [ref: TODO_REF Boyd & Vandenberghe §3.4-§3.5].

The epigraph is the load-bearing object: $f$ is convex if and only if $\operatorname{epi} f$ is a convex subset of $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$. This converts every statement about convex functions into a statement about convex sets, and it is why the two halves of the theory are one theory.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **Convex sublevel sets do not force a convex function.** The function $f(x) = \sqrt{|x|}$ has sublevel sets $\{|x| \leq \alpha^2\}$, all convex intervals, so $f$ is quasiconvex; but $f$ is not convex, since its graph bulges *above* the chord near $0$. Quasiconvexity is strictly weaker than convexity.
- **A nonconvex domain breaks the definition before the inequality does.** A function defined only on $\{x : 1 \leq \|x\| \leq 2\}$ cannot be called convex regardless of its formula, because the annular domain is not convex; the convex-combination $\theta x + (1-\theta)y$ may leave the domain entirely.
- **Pointwise minimum is not convexity-preserving.** With $f_1(x) = (x-1)^2$ and $f_2(x) = (x+1)^2$, both convex, the pointwise minimum $\min\{f_1, f_2\}$ has a nonconvex double-well graph. Maxima preserve convexity; minima generally do not.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The central Intermediate result packages the three working characterizations of convexity for differentiable functions, then proves the second-order (Hessian) test that drives every modeling check downstream.

**Theorem (characterizations of convexity).** *Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ with convex open domain $\operatorname{dom} f$.*

*(i) (Zeroth order — epigraph.) $f$ is convex if and only if $\operatorname{epi} f$ is a convex set.*

*(ii) (First order.) If $f$ is differentiable, $f$ is convex if and only if for all $x, y \in \operatorname{dom} f$,*

$$
f(y) \;\geq\; f(x) + \langle \nabla f(x),\, y - x\rangle.
$$

*(iii) (Second order.) If $f$ is twice differentiable, $f$ is convex if and only if its Hessian $\nabla^2 f(x)$ is positive semidefinite (PSD) for every $x \in \operatorname{dom} f$.*

**Proof of (i).** Suppose $f$ is convex and take $(x, s), (y, t) \in \operatorname{epi} f$, so $f(x) \leq s$, $f(y) \leq t$. For $\theta \in [0,1]$, convexity gives $f(\theta x + (1-\theta)y) \leq \theta f(x) + (1-\theta)f(y) \leq \theta s + (1-\theta)t$, so $(\theta x + (1-\theta)y,\ \theta s + (1-\theta)t) \in \operatorname{epi} f$; the epigraph is convex. Conversely, if $\operatorname{epi} f$ is convex, apply convexity of the set to the points $(x, f(x))$ and $(y, f(y))$ to recover the defining inequality. $\square$

**Proof of (ii).** Assume convexity. Fix $x, y$ and $\theta \in (0,1]$. The convexity inequality rearranges to

$$
\frac{f(x + \theta(y - x)) - f(x)}{\theta} \;\leq\; f(y) - f(x).
$$

Letting $\theta \downarrow 0$, the left side tends to the directional derivative $\langle \nabla f(x), y - x\rangle$ (this is the definition of the gradient applied along $y-x$, valid because $\operatorname{dom} f$ is open; see [02.05.05]), giving $\langle \nabla f(x), y-x\rangle \leq f(y) - f(x)$, which is the claimed supporting-line inequality. Conversely, assume the first-order inequality holds everywhere. Fix $x, y$, set $z = \theta x + (1-\theta) y$, and apply the inequality twice, at $z$ toward $x$ and toward $y$:

$$
f(x) \geq f(z) + \langle \nabla f(z), x - z\rangle, \qquad f(y) \geq f(z) + \langle \nabla f(z), y - z\rangle.
$$

Multiply the first by $\theta$, the second by $1-\theta$, and add. The gradient terms combine to $\langle \nabla f(z),\ \theta x + (1-\theta)y - z\rangle = \langle \nabla f(z), 0\rangle = 0$, leaving $\theta f(x) + (1-\theta) f(y) \geq f(z)$, which is convexity. $\square$

**Proof of (iii).** Assume $\nabla^2 f(x) \succeq 0$ everywhere. By the second-order Taylor expansion with integral remainder (from [02.05.05]), for $x, y \in \operatorname{dom} f$,

$$
f(y) = f(x) + \langle \nabla f(x), y - x\rangle + \int_0^1 (1 - s)\, \langle y - x,\ \nabla^2 f(x + s(y-x))\,(y-x)\rangle \, ds.
$$

The integrand is nonnegative because each Hessian is PSD (the quadratic form $\langle v, Hv\rangle \geq 0$; see [01.01.15]), so $f(y) \geq f(x) + \langle \nabla f(x), y-x\rangle$, and by part (ii) $f$ is convex. Conversely, suppose $f$ is convex and fix $x \in \operatorname{dom} f$ and a direction $v$. The scalar function $g(s) = f(x + sv)$ is convex on an open interval about $0$, so $g''(0) = \langle v, \nabla^2 f(x)\, v\rangle \geq 0$ (a convex $C^2$ function of one variable has nonnegative second derivative). As $v$ was arbitrary, $\nabla^2 f(x)$ is PSD by the quadratic-form sign criterion of [01.01.15] and the spectral characterization of PSD matrices in [01.01.13]. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem **builds toward** the entire convexity-detection workflow of optimization and **appears again in** the optimality conditions of [44.02.01], where the first-order inequality $f(y) \geq f(x) + \langle \nabla f(x), y-x\rangle$ becomes the statement that a stationary point is a global minimizer. **This is exactly** the bridge between calculus and convex geometry: the analytic PSD-Hessian test of [02.05.05] **is dual to** the geometric epigraph condition, with the first-order inequality as the supporting hyperplane that links them. **The foundational reason** convex problems have no spurious local minima is part (ii): a zero gradient forces $f(y) \geq f(x)$ for all $y$, so a critical point is automatically global. **Putting these together**, the PSD criterion of [01.01.13] and [01.01.15] gives a mechanical certificate of convexity for any smooth model, which the operation calculus below extends to nonsmooth and composite functions, and which **generalises** to the subgradient inequality once differentiability is dropped.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the intersection of any family of convex sets $\{C_i\}_{i \in I}$ is convex.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take two points in the intersection and use the segment-containment definition in each $C_i$ separately.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $x, y \in \bigcap_i C_i$ and $\theta \in [0,1]$. For each $i$, both $x, y \in C_i$ and $C_i$ is convex, so $\theta x + (1-\theta)y \in C_i$. Since this holds for every $i$, the point lies in $\bigcap_i C_i$, which is therefore convex. This is the reason polyhedra (finite intersections of halfspaces) and the feasible sets of linear-matrix inequalities are convex.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Let $f$ be convex and $A : \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$ affine, $A(z) = Mz + c$. Show $g(z) = f(A(z))$ is convex.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the definition of convexity to $f$ at the points $A(z_1)$ and $A(z_2)$, using that $A$ maps convex combinations to convex combinations.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $\theta \in [0,1]$, affinity gives $A(\theta z_1 + (1-\theta)z_2) = \theta A(z_1) + (1-\theta)A(z_2)$. Then $g(\theta z_1 + (1-\theta)z_2) = f(\theta A(z_1) + (1-\theta)A(z_2)) \leq \theta f(A(z_1)) + (1-\theta)f(A(z_2)) = \theta g(z_1) + (1-\theta)g(z_2)$, using convexity of $f$. So composition with an affine map preserves convexity — the workhorse that lets $\|Mz - c\|$ and $f(Mz-c)$ inherit convexity from $\|\cdot\|$ and $f$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that the pointwise supremum $g(x) = \sup_{i \in I} f_i(x)$ of any family of convex functions $f_i$ is convex.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Work with epigraphs: $\operatorname{epi} g = \bigcap_i \operatorname{epi} f_i$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A point $(x, t)$ lies in $\operatorname{epi} g$ iff $g(x) \leq t$ iff $f_i(x) \leq t$ for every $i$ iff $(x,t) \in \operatorname{epi} f_i$ for every $i$. Hence $\operatorname{epi} g = \bigcap_i \operatorname{epi} f_i$, an intersection of convex sets, which is convex by Exercise 1. By the epigraph characterization, $g$ is convex. This is why the maximum eigenvalue $\lambda_{\max}(X) = \sup_{\|v\|=1}\langle v, Xv\rangle$ and the dual norm $\|x\|_* = \sup_{\|y\|\leq 1}\langle x, y\rangle$ are convex.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the quadratic $f(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}\langle x, Px\rangle + \langle q, x\rangle + r$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is convex if and only if $P$ is symmetric positive semidefinite.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\nabla^2 f$ and apply the second-order characterization.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Taking $P$ symmetric without loss (replace $P$ by its symmetric part $\tfrac{1}{2}(P + P^\top)$, which leaves the quadratic form unchanged), $\nabla f(x) = Px + q$ and $\nabla^2 f(x) = P$ for all $x$. By part (iii) of the Key theorem, $f$ is convex iff $\nabla^2 f \succeq 0$ everywhere, i.e. iff $P \succeq 0$. The sign of the quadratic form $\langle x, Px\rangle$ is read off the eigenvalues of $P$ via the spectral theorem [01.01.13]: $P \succeq 0$ iff all eigenvalues are nonnegative.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that the log-sum-exp function $f(x) = \log\!\big(\sum_{i=1}^n e^{x_i}\big)$ is convex on $\mathbb{R}^n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the Hessian and show $\langle v, \nabla^2 f(x)\, v\rangle \geq 0$ using the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality with weights $z_i = e^{x_i}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $z_i = e^{x_i}$, $Z = \sum_i z_i$. Then $\partial_i f = z_i / Z$ and $\partial_{ij}^2 f = \dfrac{\delta_{ij} z_i}{Z} - \dfrac{z_i z_j}{Z^2}$, so $\nabla^2 f = \dfrac{1}{Z}\operatorname{diag}(z) - \dfrac{1}{Z^2} z z^\top$. For any $v$,

$$
\langle v, \nabla^2 f\, v\rangle = \frac{\sum_i z_i v_i^2}{Z} - \frac{\big(\sum_i z_i v_i\big)^2}{Z^2} \geq 0
$$

by Cauchy-Schwarz applied to the vectors $(\sqrt{z_i})$ and $(\sqrt{z_i}\,v_i)$: $\big(\sum_i z_i v_i\big)^2 = \big(\sum_i \sqrt{z_i}\cdot \sqrt{z_i} v_i\big)^2 \leq \big(\sum_i z_i\big)\big(\sum_i z_i v_i^2\big)$. Dividing by $Z^2$ gives the inequality. So $\nabla^2 f \succeq 0$ and $f$ is convex. Log-sum-exp is the smooth surrogate for the maximum and the cumulant generating function of a categorical distribution.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be convex. Show that its **perspective** $g(x, t) = t\, f(x/t)$, defined on $\{(x, t) : t > 0,\ x/t \in \operatorname{dom} f\}$, is convex in $(x, t)$ jointly.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show that the epigraph of $g$ is the image of $\operatorname{epi} f$ under a linear map composed with scaling, or verify the convexity inequality directly using $\theta t_1 + (1-\theta) t_2$ as a new denominator.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take $(x_1, t_1), (x_2, t_2)$ in the domain and $\theta \in [0,1]$; set $\bar t = \theta t_1 + (1-\theta)t_2 > 0$ and $\mu = \theta t_1 / \bar t \in [0,1]$, so $1 - \mu = (1-\theta)t_2/\bar t$. Then

$$
g(\theta(x_1,t_1) + (1-\theta)(x_2,t_2)) = \bar t\, f\!\left(\frac{\theta x_1 + (1-\theta)x_2}{\bar t}\right) = \bar t\, f\!\left(\mu \frac{x_1}{t_1} + (1-\mu)\frac{x_2}{t_2}\right).
$$

By convexity of $f$ this is $\leq \bar t\big(\mu f(x_1/t_1) + (1-\mu)f(x_2/t_2)\big) = \theta t_1 f(x_1/t_1) + (1-\theta)t_2 f(x_2/t_2) = \theta g(x_1,t_1) + (1-\theta)g(x_2,t_2)$. The perspective is the operation behind the convexity of relative entropy $t\log(t/s)$ and of the Schur-complement reformulations used in conic programming.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $f(x, y)$ be convex jointly in $(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^m$ and let $C \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$ be convex. Prove the **partial minimization** $g(x) = \inf_{y \in C} f(x, y)$ is convex (assuming $g(x) > -\infty$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For an $\varepsilon$-argument: given $x_1, x_2$, choose near-minimizing $y_1, y_2$ and use $\theta y_1 + (1-\theta)y_2 \in C$ as a feasible point for the combined $x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix $x_1, x_2$, $\theta \in [0,1]$, and $\varepsilon > 0$. Pick $y_1, y_2 \in C$ with $f(x_i, y_i) \leq g(x_i) + \varepsilon$. Since $C$ is convex, $\bar y = \theta y_1 + (1-\theta)y_2 \in C$, so $\bar y$ is feasible for the infimum defining $g(\theta x_1 + (1-\theta)x_2)$. Joint convexity of $f$ then gives

$$
g(\theta x_1 + (1-\theta)x_2) \leq f(\theta x_1 + (1-\theta)x_2,\ \bar y) \leq \theta f(x_1, y_1) + (1-\theta)f(x_2, y_2) \leq \theta g(x_1) + (1-\theta)g(x_2) + \varepsilon.
$$

Letting $\varepsilon \downarrow 0$ yields convexity of $g$. Partial minimization is how distance-to-a-convex-set, the Schur complement, and marginalized value functions stay convex.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

State and prove the composition rule: if $h : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is convex and nondecreasing and $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is convex, then $h \circ f$ is convex. Give a counterexample showing "nondecreasing" cannot be dropped.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Chain the convexity inequality of $f$ with monotonicity then convexity of $h$. For the counterexample, take $h$ decreasing.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $\theta \in [0,1]$, convexity of $f$ gives $f(\theta x + (1-\theta)y) \leq \theta f(x) + (1-\theta)f(y)$. Applying $h$, which is nondecreasing, preserves the inequality: $h(f(\theta x + (1-\theta)y)) \leq h(\theta f(x) + (1-\theta)f(y))$. Then convexity of $h$ gives $h(\theta f(x) + (1-\theta)f(y)) \leq \theta h(f(x)) + (1-\theta)h(f(y))$. Composing, $(h\circ f)(\theta x + (1-\theta)y) \leq \theta (h\circ f)(x) + (1-\theta)(h\circ f)(y)$. **Counterexample:** $h(u) = -u$ (convex but decreasing) and $f(x) = x^2$ (convex); then $h(f(x)) = -x^2$ is concave, not convex. Monotonicity is what lets the outer convexity be applied to the already-ordered inner values.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

### Carathéodory, Minkowski, and the extreme-point structure of convex hulls

The convex hull in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is finitely generated pointwise. **Carathéodory's theorem** states that every point of $\operatorname{conv} S$ is a convex combination of at most $n+1$ points of $S$ [ref: TODO_REF Rockafellar §17]. For a compact convex set $C$, the **Krein-Milman theorem** in finite dimensions sharpens to the **Minkowski theorem**: $C$ is the convex hull of its extreme points, where $x$ is extreme if it is not the midpoint of any nondegenerate segment in $C$. The interplay of these two — a compact convex set is generated by finitely many extreme points per point, and equals the hull of all of them — underlies the vertex enumeration of polyhedra and the fundamental theorem of linear programming, that a linear objective attains its optimum at an extreme point.

### Supporting hyperplanes and the dual description of convex sets

A closed convex set has two equivalent descriptions: as the convex hull of its extreme points (inner, generative) and as the intersection of all halfspaces containing it (outer, dual). The bridge is the **supporting-hyperplane theorem**: at every boundary point $x_0$ of a convex set $C$ with nonempty interior there is $a \neq 0$ with $\langle a, x\rangle \leq \langle a, x_0\rangle$ for all $x \in C$. Equivalently, the **support function** $\sigma_C(a) = \sup_{x \in C}\langle a, x\rangle$ — convex, positively homogeneous, and the conjugate of the indicator $\iota_C$ — encodes $C$ completely, with $C = \{x : \langle a, x\rangle \leq \sigma_C(a)\ \text{for all } a\}$ when $C$ is closed convex. This dual description is the modeling content the conjugacy machinery of large-deviations rate functions [37.07.03] consumes, and the separation theorem it rests on is the geometric Hahn-Banach result of [02.11.02], here specialized to finite dimensions where it requires no choice principle.

### Cones, the recession cone, and the dual cone

For a convex set $C$, the **recession cone** $0^+C = \{d : x + td \in C\ \forall x \in C,\ t \geq 0\}$ records the directions of unbounded escape; $C$ is bounded iff $0^+C = \{0\}$, and a closed convex set is compact iff its recession cone reduces to the zero vector. For a convex cone $K$, the **dual cone** $K^* = \{y : \langle y, x\rangle \geq 0\ \forall x \in K\}$ is convex and closed, with $K^{**} = \operatorname{cl}\,K$. Self-dual cones — the nonnegative orthant $\mathbb{R}^n_+$, the second-order (Lorentz) cone $\{(x, t) : \|x\| \leq t\}$, and the positive-semidefinite cone $\mathbb{S}^n_+$ under the trace inner product — are the three pillars on which conic programming [44.05.01] is built, the PSD case resting directly on the spectral characterization of [01.01.13].

### Operation-preserving calculus as a disciplined modeling atom library

The operations established in the exercises — nonnegative weighted sums, pointwise suprema, affine precomposition, perspective, partial minimization, monotone-convex composition — form a closed calculus. The vector composition rule generalizes Exercise 8: for $f(x) = h(g_1(x), \dots, g_k(x))$ with $h$ convex, $f$ is convex provided that, for each coordinate $i$, either $h$ is nondecreasing in argument $i$ and $g_i$ is convex, or $h$ is nonincreasing in argument $i$ and $g_i$ is concave (with $g_i$ affine voiding the monotonicity requirement). This rule, applied recursively to an expression tree of certified atoms, is exactly the **disciplined convex programming** ruleset of modeling languages; a model is certified convex when its parse tree is built from convex/concave atoms combined by these sign-and-monotonicity-respecting operations, with no appeal to drawing the function or checking a Hessian.

**Synthesis.** The epigraph identity **is exactly** the device that **generalises** every set-level convexity statement to a function-level one and back: intersection of epigraphs is the pointwise supremum, projection of an epigraph is partial minimization, and the linear image of an epigraph is the perspective. **The central insight** of this unit is that convexity is *compositional* — a small library of convex atoms closed under a fixed list of operations certifies convexity of arbitrarily complex models, which **is dual to** the dual-cone and support-function descriptions that certify the same sets from the outside. **The foundational reason** this matters is that convexity is precisely the property making local optimality global, so the calculus is a correctness certificate for the solvability of the whole optimization-control section; **putting these together**, the PSD-Hessian test of [01.01.13] and [02.05.05] seeds the leaf atoms, the operation rules propagate convexity up the tree, and the supporting-hyperplane and dual-cone structure **appears again in** the duality and KKT theory of [44.02.01] and the conic programs of [44.05.01]. **The bridge is** the epigraph: it is the single object through which the geometry of convex sets and the analysis of convex functions are one subject.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (sublevel sets of a convex function are convex; the converse fails).** *If $f$ is convex then every sublevel set $S_\alpha = \{x : f(x) \leq \alpha\}$ is convex. The converse does not hold: convex sublevel sets characterize quasiconvexity, a strictly weaker property.*

*Proof.* Let $x, y \in S_\alpha$ and $\theta \in [0,1]$. Then $f(\theta x + (1-\theta)y) \leq \theta f(x) + (1-\theta)f(y) \leq \theta\alpha + (1-\theta)\alpha = \alpha$, so $\theta x + (1-\theta)y \in S_\alpha$. For the failure of the converse, $f(x) = \sqrt{|x|}$ has every sublevel set equal to the interval $[-\alpha^2, \alpha^2]$ (convex), yet $f$ is not convex since $f(\tfrac{1}{2}(-1) + \tfrac{1}{2}(1)) = f(0) = 0$ while $\tfrac{1}{2}f(-1) + \tfrac{1}{2}f(1) = 1$, and although here the inequality happens to hold, taking $x=0$, $y=4$ gives $f(2) = \sqrt 2 \approx 1.414 > \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 0 + \tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 2 = 1$, violating convexity. Convex sublevel sets are equivalent to the quasiconvexity inequality $f(\theta x + (1-\theta)y) \leq \max\{f(x), f(y)\}$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Jensen's inequality).** *Let $f$ be convex and $X$ an integrable $\mathbb{R}^n$-valued random vector with $\mathbb{E}\,X \in \operatorname{dom} f$. Then $f(\mathbb{E}\,X) \leq \mathbb{E}\,f(X)$.*

*Proof.* Assume first $f$ differentiable. The first-order condition at $x_0 = \mathbb{E}\,X$ gives $f(X) \geq f(x_0) + \langle \nabla f(x_0), X - x_0\rangle$ pointwise. Taking expectations, $\mathbb{E}\,f(X) \geq f(x_0) + \langle \nabla f(x_0), \mathbb{E}\,X - x_0\rangle = f(x_0)$, since $\mathbb{E}\,X - x_0 = 0$. For general convex $f$, replace the gradient by any subgradient $a \in \partial f(x_0)$, which exists at the interior point $x_0$ by the supporting-hyperplane theorem applied to $\operatorname{epi} f$ at $(x_0, f(x_0))$; the same expectation argument gives $\mathbb{E}\,f(X) \geq f(x_0)$. The finite-average case $X \in \{x_1, \dots, x_k\}$ with probabilities $\theta_i$ recovers $f(\sum_i \theta_i x_i) \leq \sum_i \theta_i f(x_i)$, the discrete form due to Jensen [ref: TODO_REF Jensen 1906]. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (the PSD cone is a self-dual convex cone).** *The set $\mathbb{S}^n_+$ of symmetric positive-semidefinite $n \times n$ matrices is a closed convex cone, and under the trace inner product $\langle A, B\rangle = \operatorname{tr}(AB)$ it is self-dual: $(\mathbb{S}^n_+)^* = \mathbb{S}^n_+$.*

*Proof.* Closure under nonnegative scaling is immediate, and for $A, B \succeq 0$ and $v \in \mathbb{R}^n$, $\langle v, (A+B)v\rangle = \langle v, Av\rangle + \langle v, Bv\rangle \geq 0$, so $A + B \succeq 0$; thus $\mathbb{S}^n_+$ is a convex cone. It is closed because $A \succeq 0$ iff $\langle v, Av\rangle \geq 0$ for all $v$, an intersection of closed halfspaces in matrix space. For self-duality, first let $A \succeq 0$ and $B \succeq 0$. By the spectral theorem [01.01.13] write $A = \sum_i \lambda_i u_i u_i^\top$ with $\lambda_i \geq 0$; then $\operatorname{tr}(AB) = \sum_i \lambda_i\, u_i^\top B\, u_i \geq 0$, so $\mathbb{S}^n_+ \subseteq (\mathbb{S}^n_+)^*$. Conversely, suppose $B$ satisfies $\operatorname{tr}(AB) \geq 0$ for all $A \succeq 0$. Testing against the rank-one matrices $A = vv^\top \succeq 0$ gives $\operatorname{tr}(vv^\top B) = v^\top B v \geq 0$ for all $v$, so $B \succeq 0$ (after symmetrizing $B$, since the trace form pairs symmetric matrices). Hence $(\mathbb{S}^n_+)^* \subseteq \mathbb{S}^n_+$, and the two coincide. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (log-convexity is closed under products and is stronger than convexity).** *If $f, g > 0$ are log-convex then $fg$ is log-convex, and any sum of log-convex functions is log-convex. Every log-convex function is convex.*

*Proof.* Log-convexity of $f$ means $\log f$ is convex. For the product, $\log(fg) = \log f + \log g$ is a sum of convex functions, hence convex, so $fg$ is log-convex. For the sum $f + g$: by the weighted arithmetic-geometric / Hölder inequality, for $\theta \in [0,1]$,

$$
f(\theta x + (1-\theta)y) + g(\theta x + (1-\theta)y) \leq f(x)^\theta f(y)^{1-\theta} + g(x)^\theta g(y)^{1-\theta} \leq (f(x)+g(x))^\theta (f(y)+g(y))^{1-\theta},
$$

the last step being Hölder's inequality applied to the two-term sums; taking logarithms gives convexity of $\log(f+g)$. Finally, if $\log f$ is convex then $f = \exp(\log f)$ is the composition of the convex nondecreasing $\exp$ with the convex $\log f$, hence convex by the composition rule (Exercise 8). The converse fails: $f(x) = x$ on $(0,\infty)$ is convex but $\log x$ is concave, so $f$ is not log-convex. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The Legendre-Fenchel transform and convex duality of [37.07.03] take the epigraph and support-function structure built here as their domain: the conjugate $f^*(\lambda) = \sup_x(\langle\lambda, x\rangle - f(x))$ is the support function of $\operatorname{epi} f$ read off slope-by-slope, and the biconjugation theorem $f^{**} = f$ is precisely the outer dual description (intersection of supporting halfspaces) of the convex epigraph specialized to functions.

- The geometric Hahn-Banach separation theorem of [02.11.02] is the infinite-dimensional source of the supporting-hyperplane and separating-hyperplane theorems used throughout this unit; in $\mathbb{R}^n$ those theorems need no choice principle, but the supporting-hyperplane existence that gives subgradients in Proposition 2 and the dual-cone identity in Proposition 3 are the finite-dimensional shadow of that result.

- The PSD-Hessian convexity test rests on the spectral theorem and the positive-semidefinite criterion of [01.01.13] and the quadratic-form sign analysis of [01.01.15]; a smooth model is certified convex exactly when its Hessian lies in the self-dual cone $\mathbb{S}^n_+$ at every point, tying convex modeling to the eigenvalue structure of symmetric matrices.

- The convex-duality and KKT optimality theory of [44.02.01] consumes the first-order characterization (a critical point of a convex function is a global minimizer) and the supporting-hyperplane structure as the foundation of Lagrangian duality, while the conic and semidefinite programs of [44.05.01] specialize the cone and dual-cone material to the orthant, Lorentz, and PSD cones.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Systematic convexity entered analysis through the work on inequalities at the turn of the twentieth century. Johan Jensen's 1906 paper in *Acta Mathematica* [ref: TODO_REF Jensen 1906] (volume 30, 175-193) isolated the midpoint inequality $f(\tfrac{x+y}{2}) \leq \tfrac{f(x)+f(y)}{2}$ as the defining feature of a class of functions and derived from it the chain of mean inequalities, giving convex functions their first dedicated treatment and the inequality that now bears his name. The geometry of convex bodies was developed in parallel by Hermann Minkowski [ref: TODO_REF Minkowski 1896] in *Geometrie der Zahlen*, where the support function, the gauge (Minkowski functional), and the supporting-hyperplane theorem were introduced as tools in the geometry of numbers, alongside the mixed-volume theory that later seeded the Brunn-Minkowski inequality.

The synthesis into a self-contained discipline is due to R. Tyrrell Rockafellar, whose *Convex Analysis* [ref: TODO_REF Rockafellar §1-§19] (Princeton, 1970) organized convex sets, convex functions, conjugacy, and subdifferentials around the epigraph and the duality of inner (generative) and outer (separating-halfspace) descriptions, building on Fenchel's 1949 conjugate-function duality and Moreau's infinite-dimensional extensions. The translation of this analytic theory into a computational and modeling discipline came with the interior-point revolution of the 1980s and 1990s and was codified for practitioners by Boyd and Vandenberghe [ref: TODO_REF Boyd & Vandenberghe §2-§3] (Cambridge, 2004), whose operation-preserving calculus is the disciplined-convex-programming ruleset that automated convexity verification in modern modeling languages.
