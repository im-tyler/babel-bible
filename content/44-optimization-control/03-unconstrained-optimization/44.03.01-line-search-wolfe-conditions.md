## Intuition [Beginner]

Most methods for minimising a function of many variables work the same way: from where you stand, pick a direction that heads downhill, then decide how far to walk along it before stopping to look around again. The direction is the easy part — the gradient tells you the steepest uphill way, so its opposite points downhill. The hard part is the second decision: how big a step to take.

Take too small a step and you barely move, so the method crawls and may need thousands of stops to get anywhere. Take too big a step and you can overshoot the bottom of the valley and end up higher than where you started, which is worse than not moving at all. The whole art of a *line search* is choosing a step length that makes real, lasting progress without overshooting.

You do not need the perfect step — finding the exact lowest point along the line is itself a hard one-dimensional problem you would rather not solve every iteration. You only need a step that is "good enough". Two plain requirements capture good enough. First, the function value should drop by a fair amount, not a token amount: this rules out steps that are too short relative to how steeply you set off. Second, the slope should flatten out: by the end of the step the ground should be much less steep downhill than it was at the start, which rules out stopping while you are still plunging steeply and could gain more.

These two requirements together are the Wolfe conditions, and almost every serious optimisation method leans on them.

## Visual [Beginner]

Imagine slicing the landscape with a vertical knife along your chosen downhill direction. What you see on the cut face is a one-dimensional curve: height versus how far you have walked, written $\phi(\alpha) = f(x + \alpha p)$, where $\alpha$ is the distance and $p$ is the direction. You start at $\alpha = 0$ heading downhill, so the curve slopes down to the right at first.

The first rule draws a straight reference line starting at $\phi(0)$ but tilted only a fraction as steeply as the true initial slope. Any step whose height lands on or below that gentle line counts as "enough decrease". The second rule looks at the slope of the curve where you stop: it must be noticeably less steep (less negative) than the slope you started with.

A second shaded band marks the alpha values where the curve's own slope is flatter than a fraction of the initial slope, the curvature condition. | step length | enough decrease? | slope flattened? | verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| far too small | yes, but tiny | no, still plunging | rejected (too short) |
| far too large | no, overshot upward | — | rejected (too long) |
| near the dip | yes | yes | accepted (Wolfe) |

The takeaway: the two rules carve out an interval of acceptable step lengths sitting around the bottom of the sliced curve. You do not have to hit the exact bottom; you only have to land inside that interval, and a short search always can.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let us minimise $f(x_1, x_2) = x_1^2 + 4 x_2^2$, a stretched bowl with its bottom at the origin. Start at the point $x = (1, 1)$. The downhill direction we use is the negative gradient. The gradient (the pair of slopes in the two coordinate directions) is $(2 x_1, 8 x_2)$, so at our point it is $(2, 8)$ and the search direction is its opposite, $p = -(2, 8) = (-2, -8)$.

Walking a distance $\alpha$ gives the point $x + \alpha p = (1 - 2\alpha,\ 1 - 8\alpha)$, and the sliced curve is
$$
\phi(\alpha) = (1 - 2\alpha)^2 + 4(1 - 8\alpha)^2.
$$
Its starting height is $\phi(0) = 1 + 4 = 5$, and its starting slope is $-(2^2 + 8^2) = -68$ (the negative of the gradient's squared length, because we walk straight down the gradient).

The first Wolfe rule, with the usual mild fraction $c_1 = 0.1$, accepts a step if $\phi(\alpha) \le 5 - 0.1 \cdot 68 \cdot \alpha = 5 - 6.8\,\alpha$.

Try $\alpha = 0.05$: the point is $(0.9, 0.6)$, and $\phi = 0.81 + 4(0.36) = 0.81 + 1.44 = 2.25$. The threshold is $5 - 6.8(0.05) = 5 - 0.34 = 4.66$. Since $2.25 \le 4.66$, the decrease rule passes comfortably.

Now check the slope at the stop. Differentiating, $\phi'(\alpha) = 2(1-2\alpha)(-2) + 8(1-8\alpha)(-8)$. At $\alpha = 0.05$: $\phi'(0.05) = -4(0.9) - 64(0.6) = -3.6 - 38.4 = -42$. With curvature fraction $c_2 = 0.9$, the slope must satisfy $\phi'(\alpha) \ge 0.9 \cdot (-68) = -61.2$. Since $-42 \ge -61.2$, the curvature rule also passes.

What this tells us: a single step of length $0.05$ cut the height from $5$ to $2.25$ and flattened the slope from $-68$ to $-42$, and it satisfies both Wolfe conditions. We made solid progress without solving the exact one-dimensional minimisation.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuously differentiable with gradient $g(x) = \nabla f(x)$. A **line-search method** generates iterates by
$$
x_{k+1} = x_k + \alpha_k p_k, \qquad \alpha_k > 0,
$$
where $p_k \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is a **search direction** and $\alpha_k$ is the **step length**. The direction $p_k$ is a **descent direction** at $x_k$ if
$$
g_k^\top p_k < 0, \qquad g_k := \nabla f(x_k),
$$
so that $f$ decreases along $p_k$ for small $\alpha$. The angle $\theta_k$ between $p_k$ and the steepest-descent direction $-g_k$ is given by
$$
\cos\theta_k = \frac{-g_k^\top p_k}{\|g_k\|\,\|p_k\|} \in (0, 1].
$$
The restriction of $f$ to the ray is the **one-dimensional merit function**
$$
\phi_k(\alpha) = f(x_k + \alpha p_k), \qquad \phi_k(0) = f(x_k), \quad \phi_k'(0) = g_k^\top p_k < 0.
$$

Fix constants $0 < c_1 < c_2 < 1$. The **Armijo (sufficient-decrease) condition** requires
$$
f(x_k + \alpha p_k) \le f(x_k) + c_1\,\alpha\, g_k^\top p_k, \qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad \phi_k(\alpha) \le \phi_k(0) + c_1\,\alpha\,\phi_k'(0).
$$
The **curvature condition** requires
$$
\nabla f(x_k + \alpha p_k)^\top p_k \ge c_2\, g_k^\top p_k, \qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad \phi_k'(\alpha) \ge c_2\,\phi_k'(0).
$$
Together these are the **(weak) Wolfe conditions**. The **strong Wolfe conditions** replace the curvature condition by the two-sided bound
$$
\big|\nabla f(x_k + \alpha p_k)^\top p_k\big| \le c_2\,\big|g_k^\top p_k\big|,
$$
which additionally rules out points where the slope has turned strongly positive (overshoot past a local minimiser of $\phi_k$). The **Goldstein conditions** bracket the decrease from both sides with a single constant $c \in (0, \tfrac12)$:
$$
f(x_k) + (1-c)\,\alpha\, g_k^\top p_k \le f(x_k + \alpha p_k) \le f(x_k) + c\,\alpha\, g_k^\top p_k,
$$
the right inequality being sufficient decrease and the left preventing too-short steps without using a gradient evaluation at the trial point.

The **backtracking line search** uses only the Armijo condition: starting from an initial $\bar\alpha > 0$ and a contraction $\rho \in (0,1)$, it repeatedly sets $\alpha \leftarrow \rho\alpha$ until the Armijo inequality holds. The new symbols $g_k$ (gradient), $\phi_k$ (one-dimensional merit function), $\theta_k$ (search-angle), and the constants $c_1, c_2$ are recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"Sufficient decrease alone guarantees progress to the minimum."* Armijo by itself admits arbitrarily short steps: for any descent direction the inequality holds for all small enough $\alpha$, so a method that always picks a minuscule $\alpha$ satisfies it while stalling. The curvature (or Goldstein lower) condition is what excludes too-short steps.

- *"The Wolfe conditions force $\alpha_k$ near the exact one-dimensional minimiser."* They only carve out an interval. A step can satisfy the weak Wolfe conditions while the slope there is positive (past the dip); the *strong* Wolfe conditions are needed to keep $\alpha_k$ close to a stationary point of $\phi_k$.

- *"Any direction with $g_k^\top p_k < 0$ gives global convergence."* Descent is necessary but not sufficient. If the directions become nearly orthogonal to $-g_k$ (so $\cos\theta_k \to 0$), Zoutendijk's sum can converge with $\|g_k\|\not\to 0$; the angle condition $\cos\theta_k \ge \delta > 0$ is the extra hypothesis that rescues convergence.

- *"Steepest descent converges quadratically like Newton."* On a strongly convex quadratic, steepest descent with exact line search converges only linearly, with factor $\big((\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1)\big)^2$ in the energy norm; an ill-conditioned Hessian ($\kappa$ large) makes this factor close to $1$ and the method crawls. The condition number, not the dimension, governs the rate.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result of line-search theory is Zoutendijk's theorem: under the Wolfe conditions and a Lipschitz gradient, the *gain* of each step, measured as $\cos^2\theta_k\,\|g_k\|^2$, is summable. Summability is the engine that converts a local acceptance rule into a global convergence statement, and it is the single inequality from which convergence to stationary points follows for every angle-bounded method.

**Theorem (Zoutendijk).** *Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuously differentiable and bounded below on the level set $\{x : f(x) \le f(x_0)\}$, with gradient $\nabla f$ Lipschitz continuous of constant $L$ on an open set containing that level set. Consider any line-search method $x_{k+1} = x_k + \alpha_k p_k$ in which each $p_k$ is a descent direction and each $\alpha_k$ satisfies the Wolfe conditions with fixed $0 < c_1 < c_2 < 1$. Then*
$$
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \cos^2\theta_k\,\|g_k\|^2 < \infty.
$$

**Proof.** From the curvature condition at the accepted step, $g_{k+1}^\top p_k \ge c_2\, g_k^\top p_k$, so subtracting $g_k^\top p_k$ from both sides gives
$$
(g_{k+1} - g_k)^\top p_k \ge (c_2 - 1)\, g_k^\top p_k.
$$
The right side is positive because $c_2 < 1$ and $g_k^\top p_k < 0$. On the other side, the Lipschitz bound gives $\|g_{k+1} - g_k\| \le L\,\|x_{k+1} - x_k\| = L\,\alpha_k\|p_k\|$, so by Cauchy-Schwarz $(g_{k+1} - g_k)^\top p_k \le L\,\alpha_k\|p_k\|^2$. Combining the two inequalities,
$$
(c_2 - 1)\, g_k^\top p_k \le L\,\alpha_k\|p_k\|^2 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \alpha_k \ge \frac{c_2 - 1}{L}\,\frac{g_k^\top p_k}{\|p_k\|^2} = \frac{1 - c_2}{L}\,\frac{-g_k^\top p_k}{\|p_k\|^2}.
$$
This is a lower bound on the step length forced by the curvature condition. Now insert it into the Armijo decrease. Sufficient decrease gives
$$
f(x_{k+1}) \le f(x_k) + c_1\,\alpha_k\, g_k^\top p_k = f(x_k) - c_1\,\alpha_k\,(-g_k^\top p_k),
$$
and using the step-length bound (and $-g_k^\top p_k > 0$),
$$
f(x_{k+1}) \le f(x_k) - c_1\,\frac{1 - c_2}{L}\,\frac{(g_k^\top p_k)^2}{\|p_k\|^2}.
$$
Write $c = c_1(1 - c_2)/L > 0$. By the definition of $\theta_k$, $(g_k^\top p_k)^2/\|p_k\|^2 = \cos^2\theta_k\,\|g_k\|^2$, hence
$$
f(x_{k+1}) \le f(x_k) - c\,\cos^2\theta_k\,\|g_k\|^2.
$$
Summing from $0$ to $N$ telescopes the left side: $f(x_{N+1}) \le f(x_0) - c\sum_{k=0}^{N}\cos^2\theta_k\|g_k\|^2$. Since $f$ is bounded below, $f(x_0) - f(x_{N+1})$ is bounded above uniformly in $N$, so the partial sums $\sum_{k=0}^{N}\cos^2\theta_k\|g_k\|^2$ are bounded; being nonnegative, the series converges. $\square$

**Bridge.** This summability is the foundational reason a purely local acceptance test produces a global guarantee, and it builds toward the angle-condition corollary that opens the Advanced results: if $\cos\theta_k \ge \delta > 0$ for all $k$, the converging series forces $\|g_k\|^2 \to 0$, so $\nabla f(x_k) \to 0$ and every limit point is stationary. The mechanism is exactly the descent lemma of multivariable Taylor expansion [02.05.05] married to the curvature-forced lower bound on $\alpha_k$ — the two Wolfe conditions are dual, sufficient decrease bounding progress from above and curvature bounding the step from below, and putting these together is what makes the per-step gain $\cos^2\theta_k\|g_k\|^2$ both real and summable. This is exactly the quantity the central insight of the chapter rests on: steepest descent takes $\cos\theta_k = 1$ and inherits convergence directly, while Newton [44.03.03] and quasi-Newton [44.03.04] methods must keep their directions angle-bounded to ride the same theorem, and the result appears again in the global-convergence analysis of nonlinear conjugate gradient [44.03.06] where maintaining $\cos\theta_k \ge \delta$ is the delicate point.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that backtracking from $\bar\alpha$ with contraction $\rho \in (0,1)$ terminates in finitely many steps and returns a step length $\alpha_k \ge \min\{\bar\alpha,\ \rho\,\alpha_{\mathrm{A}}\}$, where $\alpha_{\mathrm{A}}$ is the largest step for which Armijo holds.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Backtracking accepts the first $\rho^j\bar\alpha$ satisfying Armijo. Use that Armijo holds on $(0, \alpha_{\mathrm{A}}]$ for a quadratic-like decrease, and that the previous trial $\rho^{j-1}\bar\alpha$ failed.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Exercise 2, Armijo holds for all small enough $\alpha$, so some power $\rho^j\bar\alpha$ satisfies it; the loop halts at the first such $j$, finitely many steps since $\rho^j\bar\alpha \to 0$. If $\bar\alpha$ itself passes ($j = 0$) then $\alpha_k = \bar\alpha$. Otherwise the accepted value is $\alpha_k = \rho^j\bar\alpha$ where $\rho^{j-1}\bar\alpha$ was rejected, meaning $\rho^{j-1}\bar\alpha > \alpha_{\mathrm{A}}$ (Armijo fails above the acceptance threshold), so $\alpha_k = \rho\cdot\rho^{j-1}\bar\alpha > \rho\,\alpha_{\mathrm{A}}$. In either case $\alpha_k \ge \min\{\bar\alpha, \rho\,\alpha_{\mathrm{A}}\}$. This uniform lower bound is what lets backtracking-only methods (Armijo without an explicit curvature test) inherit a Zoutendijk-type guarantee. Full credit for finiteness plus the $\rho\,\alpha_{\mathrm{A}}$ bound.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the steepest-descent linear rate on a quadratic: for $f(x) = \tfrac12 x^\top A x - b^\top x$ with $A \succ 0$, exact line search gives $\|x_{k+1} - x_\star\|_A^2 \le \big((\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1)\big)^2\|x_k - x_\star\|_A^2$ with $\kappa = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Let $e_k = x_k - x_\star$, $g_k = Ae_k$. With exact step $\alpha_k = g_k^\top g_k/(g_k^\top A g_k)$, expand $\|e_{k+1}\|_A^2 = \|e_k\|_A^2(1 - (g_k^\top g_k)^2/((g_k^\top A g_k)(g_k^\top A^{-1}g_k)))$ and apply the Kantorovich inequality.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $e_k = x_k - x_\star$ so $g_k = \nabla f(x_k) = Ae_k$ and $\|e_k\|_A^2 = e_k^\top A e_k = g_k^\top A^{-1} g_k$. Exact line search along $-g_k$ uses $\alpha_k = (g_k^\top g_k)/(g_k^\top A g_k)$, and a direct expansion gives
$$
\|e_{k+1}\|_A^2 = \|e_k\|_A^2\left(1 - \frac{(g_k^\top g_k)^2}{(g_k^\top A g_k)(g_k^\top A^{-1} g_k)}\right).
$$
The Kantorovich inequality states that for $A \succ 0$ and any $v \ne 0$,
$$
\frac{(v^\top v)^2}{(v^\top A v)(v^\top A^{-1} v)} \ge \frac{4\lambda_{\max}\lambda_{\min}}{(\lambda_{\max} + \lambda_{\min})^2}.
$$
Substituting with $v = g_k$,
$$
\|e_{k+1}\|_A^2 \le \|e_k\|_A^2\left(1 - \frac{4\lambda_{\max}\lambda_{\min}}{(\lambda_{\max}+\lambda_{\min})^2}\right) = \|e_k\|_A^2\,\frac{(\lambda_{\max} - \lambda_{\min})^2}{(\lambda_{\max}+\lambda_{\min})^2} = \|e_k\|_A^2\left(\frac{\kappa - 1}{\kappa + 1}\right)^2,
$$
dividing numerator and denominator by $\lambda_{\min}$. The rate is linear with factor $((\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1))^2$; for large $\kappa$ this is $\approx 1 - 4/\kappa$, so the iteration count to fixed accuracy grows like $\kappa$. Full credit for the Kantorovich substitution and the final factor.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

The line-search framework is the iteration $x_{k+1} = x_k + \alpha_k p_k$ with a descent direction and a Wolfe-acceptable step, and the results below convert Zoutendijk's summability into convergence, exhibit the sharp rate of the canonical method, and locate the framework's limits — where the angle condition fails and where the local rate is governed by curvature rather than by the line search.

**Theorem 1 (global convergence under the angle condition).** Let the hypotheses of Zoutendijk's theorem hold, and suppose the search directions satisfy the **angle condition** $\cos\theta_k \ge \delta > 0$ for all $k$. Then $\lim_{k\to\infty}\|\nabla f(x_k)\| = 0$, so every limit point of $\{x_k\}$ is stationary. Indeed Zoutendijk gives $\sum_k \cos^2\theta_k\|g_k\|^2 < \infty$, and $\cos^2\theta_k \ge \delta^2$ forces $\sum_k \|g_k\|^2 < \infty$, whence $\|g_k\| \to 0$. For a convex $f$ a stationary point is a global minimiser, and for strongly convex $f$ the unique minimiser is reached; without convexity the guarantee is only stationarity, which is the honest ceiling of first-order line-search theory [ref: TODO_REF Nocedal, J. & Wright, S. J. — Numerical Optimization (2nd ed.)].

**Theorem 2 (steepest descent: sharp linear rate and its condition-number dependence).** For $f$ twice continuously differentiable with $\nabla^2 f(x_\star) = A \succ 0$ at a strict local minimiser, steepest descent with exact line search converges linearly with
$$
\frac{f(x_{k+1}) - f(x_\star)}{f(x_k) - f(x_\star)} \to \left(\frac{\kappa - 1}{\kappa + 1}\right)^2, \qquad \kappa = \frac{\lambda_{\max}(A)}{\lambda_{\min}(A)},
$$
and the bound is attained on the worst-case quadratic, where the iterates zig-zag between the two extreme eigendirections. The same factor governs steepest descent with Wolfe (inexact) steps up to constants. The rate degrades to $1$ as $\kappa \to \infty$: the per-iteration progress is $O(1/\kappa)$, the structural reason steepest descent is abandoned for the curvature-aware methods that follow. Kantorovich's inequality is the exact tool that makes the factor sharp [ref: TODO_REF Bertsekas, D. P. — Nonlinear Programming (3rd ed.)].

**Theorem 3 (Goldstein conditions and their relation to Wolfe).** The Goldstein conditions $f_k + (1-c)\alpha g_k^\top p_k \le f(x_k + \alpha p_k) \le f_k + c\alpha g_k^\top p_k$ with $c \in (0,\tfrac12)$ also admit a step-length interval and yield a Zoutendijk-type sum, using only function values — no gradient at the trial point — which suited early implementations. Their defect is that the lower Goldstein bound can exclude the exact one-dimensional minimiser, whereas the Wolfe curvature condition never does; for that reason Wolfe is preferred in quasi-Newton methods where the curvature condition additionally guarantees that the secant pair $(s_k, y_k) = (x_{k+1}-x_k,\ g_{k+1}-g_k)$ satisfies $s_k^\top y_k > 0$, the positivity that keeps the BFGS update positive-definite [ref: TODO_REF Dennis, J. E. & Schnabel, R. B. — Numerical Methods for Unconstrained Optimization and Nonlinear Equations].

**Theorem 4 (the curvature condition and quasi-Newton positivity).** Under the curvature condition $g_{k+1}^\top p_k \ge c_2\, g_k^\top p_k$, the curvature pair satisfies
$$
s_k^\top y_k = \alpha_k\, p_k^\top(g_{k+1} - g_k) = \alpha_k\big(p_k^\top g_{k+1} - p_k^\top g_k\big) \ge \alpha_k(c_2 - 1)\,p_k^\top g_k = \alpha_k(1 - c_2)\,(-p_k^\top g_k) > 0,
$$
since $\alpha_k > 0$, $c_2 < 1$, and $p_k^\top g_k < 0$. This single inequality is why the Wolfe conditions are the standard acceptance test for quasi-Newton methods: it certifies that the Broyden-Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shanno (BFGS) and Davidon-Fletcher-Powell (DFP) updates inherit positive-definiteness from one iterate to the next, so the generated directions stay descent directions automatically and the angle condition is maintained without an explicit safeguard. The local superlinear rate of these methods then takes over from the line search once the iterates are near the minimiser [ref: TODO_REF Nocedal, J. & Wright, S. J. — Numerical Optimization (2nd ed.)].

**Synthesis.** Line search is one framework — descent direction plus Wolfe-acceptable step — and Zoutendijk's summability $\sum_k \cos^2\theta_k\|g_k\|^2 < \infty$ is the single invariant from which its entire global theory descends, the foundational reason a local two-line acceptance test yields convergence to stationary points. The central insight is the division of labour the framework enforces: the line search owns *globalisation* — it drives $\|\nabla f_k\| \to 0$ from any start, provided the directions stay angle-bounded — while the *local rate* is owned by the direction, not the step. This is exactly why steepest descent, with the best possible $\cos\theta_k = 1$, still converges only linearly with the condition-number factor $((\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1))^2$: the line search cannot repair a poorly chosen direction, and Kantorovich's inequality makes the penalty sharp. The curvature condition is dual to sufficient decrease — one forbids too-short steps, the other too-long — and putting these together produces both the step-length lower bound that powers Zoutendijk and the secant positivity $s_k^\top y_k > 0$ that keeps quasi-Newton updates definite. The bridge is therefore explicit: the same Wolfe test that globalises steepest descent here generalises to certify the descent property of Newton [44.03.03], the positive-definiteness of BFGS [44.03.04], and the restart logic of nonlinear conjugate gradient [44.03.06], and trust-region methods [44.03.05] arise precisely by replacing the line search with a constrained model minimisation when even an angle-bounded direction is unreliable.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Zoutendijk summability).** *Under the hypotheses of the Key theorem — $f \in C^1$ bounded below, $\nabla f$ Lipschitz of constant $L$, descent directions, Wolfe steps with $0 < c_1 < c_2 < 1$ — one has $\sum_{k}\cos^2\theta_k\|g_k\|^2 < \infty$.*

*Proof.* The curvature condition gives $(g_{k+1}-g_k)^\top p_k \ge (c_2 - 1)g_k^\top p_k > 0$. The Lipschitz bound and Cauchy-Schwarz give $(g_{k+1}-g_k)^\top p_k \le \|g_{k+1}-g_k\|\|p_k\| \le L\alpha_k\|p_k\|^2$. Hence $\alpha_k \ge (1-c_2)(-g_k^\top p_k)/(L\|p_k\|^2)$. Substituting into the Armijo decrease $f(x_{k+1}) \le f(x_k) + c_1\alpha_k g_k^\top p_k = f(x_k) - c_1\alpha_k(-g_k^\top p_k)$ yields
$$
f(x_{k+1}) \le f(x_k) - \frac{c_1(1-c_2)}{L}\frac{(g_k^\top p_k)^2}{\|p_k\|^2} = f(x_k) - \frac{c_1(1-c_2)}{L}\cos^2\theta_k\|g_k\|^2.
$$
Telescoping and using the lower bound on $f$ bounds the nonnegative partial sums, so the series converges. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (angle-condition global convergence).** *If additionally $\cos\theta_k \ge \delta > 0$ for all $k$, then $\nabla f(x_k) \to 0$.*

*Proof.* From Proposition 1, $\sum_k \cos^2\theta_k\|g_k\|^2 < \infty$. The term bound $\cos^2\theta_k\|g_k\|^2 \ge \delta^2\|g_k\|^2$ gives $\delta^2\sum_k\|g_k\|^2 \le \sum_k\cos^2\theta_k\|g_k\|^2 < \infty$, so $\sum_k\|g_k\|^2 < \infty$ and the summand $\|g_k\|^2 \to 0$. Thus $\|\nabla f(x_k)\| \to 0$; any convergent subsequence $x_{k_j} \to \bar x$ has, by continuity of $\nabla f$, $\nabla f(\bar x) = 0$, so every limit point is stationary. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (existence of a Wolfe interval).** *If $\phi(\alpha) = f(x_k + \alpha p_k)$ is $C^1$, $\phi'(0) < 0$, and $\phi$ is bounded below on $\alpha \ge 0$, then for any $0 < c_1 < c_2 < 1$ the set of $\alpha > 0$ satisfying both the Armijo and curvature conditions contains a nonempty open interval; the strong Wolfe conditions hold there too.*

*Proof.* Let $\ell(\alpha) = \phi(0) + c_1\alpha\phi'(0)$. Since $\phi'(0) < 0$, $\ell$ is decreasing and unbounded below, while $\phi$ is bounded below, so the continuous map $\alpha \mapsto \phi(\alpha) - \ell(\alpha)$, zero at $\alpha = 0$ with negative one-sided derivative $(1-c_1)\phi'(0)$, is negative for small $\alpha > 0$ and becomes positive; let $\alpha'$ be the least positive root, so $\phi(\alpha') = \ell(\alpha')$ and Armijo holds on $(0,\alpha']$. The mean value theorem provides $\alpha'' \in (0,\alpha')$ with
$$
\phi'(\alpha'') = \frac{\phi(\alpha') - \phi(0)}{\alpha'} = c_1\phi'(0).
$$
Because $c_2 > c_1$ and $\phi'(0) < 0$, $\phi'(\alpha'') = c_1\phi'(0) > c_2\phi'(0)$, so curvature holds at $\alpha''$; since $\phi'(\alpha'') = c_1\phi'(0) < 0$, also $|\phi'(\alpha'')| = c_1|\phi'(0)| < c_2|\phi'(0)|$, the strong form. As $\alpha'' < \alpha'$, Armijo holds at $\alpha''$, and continuity of $\phi'$ and $\phi - \ell$ extends both conditions to an open interval about $\alpha''$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (backtracking terminates with a useful step).** *Backtracking from $\bar\alpha$ with contraction $\rho \in (0,1)$ and Armijo constant $c_1$ halts after finitely many reductions and returns $\alpha_k$ with $\alpha_k \ge \min\{\bar\alpha,\ \rho\,\alpha_{\mathrm A}\}$, where $\alpha_{\mathrm A} = \sup\{\alpha : \text{Armijo holds on } (0,\alpha]\}$.*

*Proof.* By the first-order expansion $\phi(\alpha) - \phi(0) - c_1\alpha\phi'(0) = (1-c_1)\phi'(0)\alpha + o(\alpha)$ with $(1-c_1)\phi'(0) < 0$, the Armijo inequality holds for all small $\alpha$, so some $\rho^j\bar\alpha$ satisfies it and the loop halts; finiteness follows from $\rho^j\bar\alpha \to 0$. If $j = 0$, $\alpha_k = \bar\alpha$. If $j \ge 1$, the prior trial $\rho^{j-1}\bar\alpha$ failed Armijo, so $\rho^{j-1}\bar\alpha > \alpha_{\mathrm A}$, giving $\alpha_k = \rho^j\bar\alpha = \rho\cdot\rho^{j-1}\bar\alpha > \rho\,\alpha_{\mathrm A}$. Hence $\alpha_k \ge \min\{\bar\alpha, \rho\,\alpha_{\mathrm A}\}$, a positive lower bound that, combined with a uniform lower bound on $\alpha_{\mathrm A}$ from the Lipschitz constant, yields a Zoutendijk sum for backtracking-only methods. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (steepest-descent linear rate via Kantorovich).** *For $f(x) = \tfrac12 x^\top A x - b^\top x$ with $A \succ 0$, steepest descent with exact line search satisfies $\|e_{k+1}\|_A^2 \le \big((\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1)\big)^2\|e_k\|_A^2$, $e_k = x_k - x_\star$, $\kappa = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$.*

*Proof.* With $g_k = Ae_k$ and exact step $\alpha_k = (g_k^\top g_k)/(g_k^\top A g_k)$, the energy-norm recursion expands to
$$
\|e_{k+1}\|_A^2 = \|e_k\|_A^2\left(1 - \frac{(g_k^\top g_k)^2}{(g_k^\top A g_k)(g_k^\top A^{-1} g_k)}\right),
$$
using $\|e_k\|_A^2 = g_k^\top A^{-1} g_k$. The Kantorovich inequality bounds the ratio below by $4\lambda_{\max}\lambda_{\min}/(\lambda_{\max}+\lambda_{\min})^2$, so the contraction factor is at most $1 - 4\lambda_{\max}\lambda_{\min}/(\lambda_{\max}+\lambda_{\min})^2 = (\lambda_{\max}-\lambda_{\min})^2/(\lambda_{\max}+\lambda_{\min})^2 = ((\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1))^2$. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (curvature condition forces $s_k^\top y_k > 0$).** *Under the curvature condition with $c_2 < 1$, the secant pair $s_k = x_{k+1}-x_k$, $y_k = g_{k+1}-g_k$ satisfies $s_k^\top y_k > 0$.*

*Proof.* Since $s_k = \alpha_k p_k$ with $\alpha_k > 0$, $s_k^\top y_k = \alpha_k p_k^\top(g_{k+1}-g_k)$. The curvature condition gives $p_k^\top g_{k+1} \ge c_2\,p_k^\top g_k$, so $p_k^\top(g_{k+1}-g_k) \ge (c_2-1)p_k^\top g_k = (1-c_2)(-p_k^\top g_k) > 0$ because $c_2 < 1$ and $p_k^\top g_k < 0$. Multiplying by $\alpha_k > 0$ gives $s_k^\top y_k > 0$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Convex functions and their second-order conditions [44.01.01] supply the structural guarantee that converts the line-search stationarity result into optimality: Zoutendijk's theorem alone delivers only $\nabla f(x_k) \to 0$, but when $f$ is convex a stationary point is a global minimiser, and when $f$ is strongly convex the unique minimiser is reached at the linear rate of Theorem 2. The strong-convexity modulus and the Hessian condition number $\kappa$ that govern the steepest-descent factor $((\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1))^2$ are exactly the curvature data that unit formalises, so the rate theory of line search is the quantitative shadow of the convexity theory.

- Multivariable Taylor expansion and the test for extrema [02.05.05] is the analytic engine of every estimate here: the descent lemma $f(x+\alpha p) \le f(x) + \alpha g^\top p + \tfrac12 L\alpha^2\|p\|^2$ that underlies sufficient decrease, the mean value theorem step in the Wolfe-existence proof, and the second-order expansion that produces the asymptotic steepest-descent rate all come from that unit's Taylor machinery. The first-order stationarity condition $\nabla f(x_\star) = 0$ that line search drives toward is the necessary condition for an extremum established there.

- Newton's method [44.03.03], quasi-Newton (BFGS/DFP) methods [44.03.04], trust-region methods [44.03.05], and nonlinear conjugate gradient [44.03.06] are the consumers of this framework: each chooses a search direction and then relies on the Wolfe line search of this unit to globalise it. The curvature condition's guarantee $s_k^\top y_k > 0$ is precisely what keeps the BFGS update positive-definite, the angle condition is the hypothesis Newton and quasi-Newton directions must verify to inherit Zoutendijk convergence, and the line-search-versus-trust-region split is the chapter's two answers to the same globalisation problem. The condition-number-dependent rate of steepest descent here is the baseline these curvature-aware methods improve upon.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The idea of minimising a function by repeatedly stepping along the negative gradient goes back to **Augustin-Louis Cauchy's 1847** note *Méthode générale pour la résolution des systèmes d'équations simultanées*, where he proposed following the steepest descent of a sum of squares to solve nonlinear systems [ref: TODO_REF Cauchy 1847]. The method was studied as a practical algorithm only in the twentieth century, and its slow, zig-zagging behaviour on ill-conditioned problems — quantified by the $((\kappa-1)/(\kappa+1))^2$ factor and Kantorovich's inequality — motivated the search for curvature-aware alternatives.

The modern theory of inexact line searches crystallised around three acceptance rules. **Larry Armijo's 1966** paper introduced the sufficient-decrease condition that bears his name, proving convergence of gradient methods under a step-reduction rule [ref: TODO_REF Armijo 1966]. **A. A. Goldstein** proposed his two-sided bound in the same period to keep steps from being too short without a trial gradient. **Philip Wolfe's 1969** paper *Convergence conditions for ascent methods* added the curvature condition, giving the pair now called the Wolfe conditions and isolating the property that makes them ideal for quasi-Newton methods [ref: TODO_REF Wolfe 1969]. The global-convergence analysis that ties these acceptance rules to stationarity through the summability of $\cos^2\theta_k\|g_k\|^2$ is due to **Guus Zoutendijk**, whose 1970 contribution made the angle condition the organising hypothesis of first-order convergence theory [ref: TODO_REF Zoutendijk 1970]. The synthesis presented in Nocedal and Wright's textbook is the form in which the framework is now taught and implemented.
