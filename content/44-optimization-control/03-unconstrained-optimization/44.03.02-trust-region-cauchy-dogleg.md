## Intuition [Beginner]

Suppose you are standing somewhere on a hilly landscape and want to walk to the lowest point. You cannot see the whole terrain, but you can feel the slope under your feet and how the slope is changing — enough to build a small bowl-shaped guess of what the ground looks like right around you. That guess is a model. The catch is that your model is only believable nearby. A few steps away the real terrain might curl up where your bowl curls down, so trusting the model far from your feet is reckless.

A trust-region method makes this caution explicit. It draws a circle around your current position — the *trust region* — and says: I will only step somewhere inside this circle, because that is the only place I believe my bowl. It then picks the lowest point of the bowl *within the circle* and walks there.

After the step, it checks honesty. It compares the drop the model promised with the drop that actually happened. If the model was accurate, it earned more trust, so the circle grows for next time. If the model overpromised, the circle shrinks. If the model lied badly, the step is thrown out and you try again from the same spot with a smaller circle.

This is a different bargain from the usual one. Most methods first pick a direction and then ask how far to go. A trust-region method fixes how far it is willing to go and then asks which direction is best within that reach.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the bowl-shaped model as a set of nested oval contour lines around a low point, and the trust region as a circle centred where you stand. The step you take is the lowest contour the circle can reach.

When the circle is small, the lowest reachable point sits on the circle's edge, and the best you can do is head roughly straight downhill. When the circle is large enough to contain the bowl's own bottom, the step lands at that interior bottom — the full model step — and the edge of the circle stops mattering.

The takeaway: the radius of the circle is a dial for how bold to be, and the honesty check turns that dial automatically — more accurate models earn bigger circles, and bad surprises shrink them.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take a model bowl in two variables, $m(p) = g \cdot p + \tfrac12 p^\top B p$, with downhill data
$$
g = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad B = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 4 \end{pmatrix},
$$
and a trust radius $\Delta = 1$ (so steps must satisfy $\|p\| \le 1$). The value at the centre is $m(0) = 0$.

First, the cheap downhill step. The steepest-descent direction is $-g = (2, 0)$, pointing along the first axis. The full model minimum is at $p^B = -B^{-1}g = (2, 0)$, which has length $2$ — outside the circle. So the model's own bottom is out of reach and the step will sit on the boundary.

Now find the best point along the downhill direction inside the circle. Moving a distance $t$ along $-g/\|g\| = (1,0)$ gives the point $(t, 0)$, with model value
$$
m(t,0) = g \cdot (t,0) + \tfrac12 (t,0)^\top B (t,0) = -2t + \tfrac12 (1)t^2 = \tfrac12 t^2 - 2t.
$$
The unconstrained minimum of $\tfrac12 t^2 - 2t$ is at $t = 2$, again outside the circle. Capped at $t = \Delta = 1$, the best allowed point is $(1, 0)$.

Read off the drop. The model promised $m(1,0) = \tfrac12 - 2 = -1.5$, a predicted reduction of $1.5$ from $m(0) = 0$. Suppose the real function dropped by $1.35$ when we stepped there. The honesty ratio is $1.35 / 1.5 = 0.9$.

What this tells us: the model predicted the drop well (ratio $0.9$, close to $1$), so we accept the step and grow the circle for the next iteration. The boundary cap is what kept us from overstepping toward the out-of-reach model minimum.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be twice continuously differentiable, and at the current iterate $x_k$ write $f_k = f(x_k)$, $g_k = \nabla f(x_k)$, and let $B_k$ be a symmetric matrix approximating $\nabla^2 f(x_k)$ (the exact Hessian, a quasi-Newton update, or any symmetric surrogate). The **quadratic model** of $f$ about $x_k$ is the second-order Taylor polynomial [02.05.05]
$$
m_k(p) = f_k + g_k^\top p + \tfrac12 p^\top B_k p, \qquad m_k(0) = f_k .
$$
The **trust region** is the ball $\{p : \|p\| \le \Delta_k\}$ of radius $\Delta_k > 0$ in a chosen norm (the Euclidean norm throughout, unless stated). The **trust-region subproblem** is
$$
p_k = \arg\min_{\|p\| \le \Delta_k} m_k(p) = \arg\min_{\|p\| \le \Delta_k}\ g_k^\top p + \tfrac12 p^\top B_k p .
$$
The **predicted reduction** is $\operatorname{pred}_k = m_k(0) - m_k(p_k) \ge 0$, and the **actual reduction** is $\operatorname{ared}_k = f(x_k) - f(x_k + p_k)$. The **ratio test** computes
$$
\rho_k = \frac{\operatorname{ared}_k}{\operatorname{pred}_k} = \frac{f(x_k) - f(x_k + p_k)}{m_k(0) - m_k(p_k)} .
$$
With constants $0 \le \eta < \tfrac14$, the step is accepted ($x_{k+1} = x_k + p_k$) when $\rho_k > \eta$ and rejected ($x_{k+1} = x_k$) otherwise, and the radius is updated by: shrink, $\Delta_{k+1} = \tfrac14\Delta_k$, when $\rho_k < \tfrac14$; expand, $\Delta_{k+1} = \min(2\Delta_k, \Delta_{\max})$, when $\rho_k > \tfrac34$ and $\|p_k\| = \Delta_k$; otherwise $\Delta_{k+1} = \Delta_k$. The symbols $g$ (gradient), $B$ (model Hessian), $\Delta$ (trust radius), $m$ (model), $\rho$ (ratio), and $\lambda$ (Lagrange multiplier) are recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

The **Cauchy point** $p_k^C$ is the minimiser of $m_k$ along the steepest-descent direction inside the region. Writing $p^S = -(\Delta_k/\|g_k\|)\,g_k$ for the boundary point in the $-g_k$ direction, the Cauchy point is $p_k^C = \tau_k\, p^S$ with
$$
\tau_k = \begin{cases} 1, & g_k^\top B_k g_k \le 0, \\ \min\!\big(1,\ \|g_k\|^3 / (\Delta_k\, g_k^\top B_k g_k)\big), & g_k^\top B_k g_k > 0. \end{cases}
$$
When the curvature $g_k^\top B_k g_k$ is nonpositive, the model decreases without bound along $-g_k$, so the step runs to the boundary; otherwise it stops at the line minimum if that lies inside the region.

The **dogleg method** applies when $B_k \succ 0$. It replaces the curved subproblem boundary with a piecewise-linear path from the origin through the **unconstrained Cauchy step** $p^U = -\dfrac{g_k^\top g_k}{g_k^\top B_k g_k}\,g_k$ to the **full Newton step** $p^B = -B_k^{-1}g_k$, and returns the point where this two-segment path first meets $\|p\| = \Delta_k$ (or $p^B$ itself if it lies inside). **Two-dimensional subspace minimisation** instead solves the subproblem exactly over the plane $\operatorname{span}\{g_k, B_k^{-1}g_k\}$, a strict generalisation of the dogleg path.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"The trust-region step is the Newton step capped to length $\Delta$."* Capping $p^B = -B^{-1}g$ to the boundary by scaling is not the subproblem solution: the true constrained minimiser bends toward the steepest-descent direction as $\Delta$ shrinks, which is exactly what the dogleg path and the $(B+\lambda I)$ characterisation capture and a naive rescaling misses.

- *"A trust-region method needs $B$ positive-definite."* The Cauchy point and the whole convergence theory work for any symmetric $B$, including indefinite ones; negative curvature $g^\top Bg \le 0$ simply sends the Cauchy step to the boundary. Only the dogleg method requires $B \succ 0$, because it needs the Newton step $p^B = -B^{-1}g$ to exist and to be a descent point.

- *"If $\rho_k$ is small the step direction was wrong."* A small ratio means the model mispredicted the *magnitude* of the drop at that range, not that the direction was bad. The response is to shrink $\Delta$ and re-solve, not to change methods; the same model is trusted over a shorter reach.

- *"The exact subproblem solution is always $-(B+\lambda I)^{-1}g$ for some $\lambda > 0$."* In the **hard case**, where $g$ is orthogonal to the eigenspace of the smallest eigenvalue of $B$ and that eigenvalue is negative, the solution at $\lambda = -\lambda_{\min}(B)$ requires adding a multiple of the corresponding eigenvector, since $B + \lambda I$ is singular and $-(B+\lambda I)^{-1}g$ alone does not reach the boundary.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is the Cauchy sufficient-reduction bound: the cheap steepest-descent step alone guarantees a model decrease proportional to the gradient and the radius. Because every practical step is required to do at least as well as the Cauchy point, this single inequality is what makes trust-region methods globally convergent.

**Theorem (Cauchy sufficient reduction).** *Let $m_k(p) = f_k + g_k^\top p + \tfrac12 p^\top B_k p$ with $g_k \ne 0$ and $B_k$ symmetric, and let $p_k^C$ be the Cauchy point for radius $\Delta_k$. Then*
$$
m_k(0) - m_k(p_k^C) \ \ge\ \tfrac12\,\|g_k\|\,\min\!\Big(\Delta_k,\ \frac{\|g_k\|}{\|B_k\|}\Big),
$$
*where $\|B_k\|$ is the spectral norm (read as $+\infty$, with the convention $\|g_k\|/\|B_k\| = 0$, only if $B_k = 0$, in which case the linear term gives $m_k(0)-m_k(p_k^C)=\|g_k\|\Delta_k$).*

**Proof.** Along the ray $p(t) = -t\,g_k/\|g_k\|$ for $t \ge 0$, the model is the scalar quadratic
$$
\psi(t) := m_k(p(t)) - f_k = -\|g_k\|\,t + \tfrac12\Big(\frac{g_k^\top B_k g_k}{\|g_k\|^2}\Big)t^2 ,
$$
since $g_k^\top p(t) = -\|g_k\| t$ and $p(t)^\top B_k p(t) = (g_k^\top B_k g_k/\|g_k\|^2)t^2$. Write $\gamma = g_k^\top B_k g_k/\|g_k\|^2$ for the curvature along $-g_k$; the Cauchy point is $p_k^C = p(t^*)$ with $t^*$ the minimiser of $\psi$ on $[0,\Delta_k]$.

*Case 1: $\gamma \le 0$.* Then $\psi$ is concave (or linear) and decreasing on $[0,\Delta_k]$, so $t^* = \Delta_k$ and
$$
m_k(0) - m_k(p_k^C) = -\psi(\Delta_k) = \|g_k\|\Delta_k - \tfrac12\gamma\Delta_k^2 \ge \|g_k\|\Delta_k \ge \tfrac12\|g_k\|\Delta_k \ge \tfrac12\|g_k\|\min\!\Big(\Delta_k, \tfrac{\|g_k\|}{\|B_k\|}\Big),
$$
using $-\tfrac12\gamma\Delta_k^2 \ge 0$ and that the minimum is at most $\Delta_k$.

*Case 2: $\gamma > 0$.* The unconstrained minimiser of $\psi$ is $t_u = \|g_k\|/\gamma$. If $t_u \le \Delta_k$, then $t^* = t_u$ and
$$
m_k(0) - m_k(p_k^C) = -\psi(t_u) = \frac{\|g_k\|^2}{2\gamma} = \frac{\|g_k\|^4}{2\,g_k^\top B_k g_k} \ge \frac{\|g_k\|^2}{2\|B_k\|},
$$
because $g_k^\top B_k g_k \le \|B_k\|\,\|g_k\|^2$. Since here $t_u \le \Delta_k$ means $\|g_k\|/\gamma \le \Delta_k$, and $\gamma \le \|B_k\|$ gives $\|g_k\|/\|B_k\| \le \|g_k\|/\gamma \le \Delta_k$, the minimum equals $\|g_k\|/\|B_k\|$ and the bound reads $\tfrac12\|g_k\|\cdot\|g_k\|/\|B_k\|$, matching. If instead $t_u > \Delta_k$, then $t^* = \Delta_k$ and
$$
m_k(0) - m_k(p_k^C) = -\psi(\Delta_k) = \|g_k\|\Delta_k - \tfrac12\gamma\Delta_k^2 .
$$
From $t_u = \|g_k\|/\gamma > \Delta_k$ we get $\gamma\Delta_k < \|g_k\|$, so $\tfrac12\gamma\Delta_k^2 < \tfrac12\|g_k\|\Delta_k$ and hence $\|g_k\|\Delta_k - \tfrac12\gamma\Delta_k^2 > \tfrac12\|g_k\|\Delta_k \ge \tfrac12\|g_k\|\min(\Delta_k, \|g_k\|/\|B_k\|)$. In every case the stated bound holds. $\square$

**Bridge.** This inequality is the foundational reason trust-region methods converge from any start: it certifies that even the crudest admissible step extracts a model decrease scaling like $\|g_k\|\min(\Delta_k, \|g_k\|/\|B_k\|)$, and any step required to match a fixed fraction of the Cauchy decrease inherits the same guarantee — this is exactly the role the sufficient-decrease (Armijo) inequality plays for line searches, with the radius $\Delta_k$ standing in for the step length. The central insight is that decoupling *how far* from *which way* lets the method handle indefinite $B_k$ that break the descent logic of Newton's method, because negative curvature merely drives the Cauchy step to the boundary rather than causing failure. The bound builds toward the global-convergence theorem of the Advanced results, where summing it across iterations forces $\|g_k\| \to 0$, and it appears again in the Steihaug-Toint method, whose first inner iterate is precisely the Cauchy point so the truncated solve dominates this decrease. Putting these together, the Cauchy point is to trust regions what steepest descent is to line search — the safe baseline that the Newton-like dogleg and exact steps improve upon without ever dropping below it; the bridge is that the same quantity $\|g_k\|/\|B_k\|$ measuring when curvature starts to bite reappears as the threshold separating the gradient-dominated regime from the Newton regime.

## Advanced results [Master]

The Cauchy bound certifies a baseline decrease; the results below build the exact theory of the subproblem on top of it — the global characterisation of the constrained minimiser through a single scalar multiplier, the hard case where that characterisation needs an eigenvector correction, the global-convergence theorem that turns the baseline decrease into $\|g_k\|\to0$, and the large-scale truncated-CG solver that realises the theory at the cost of one matrix-vector product per inner step.

**Theorem 1 (global characterisation of the subproblem solution).** A vector $p^*$ is a global solution of $\min_{\|p\|\le\Delta} m(p) = g^\top p + \tfrac12 p^\top B p$ if and only if $\|p^*\|\le\Delta$ and there exists $\lambda \ge 0$ such that
$$
(B + \lambda I)\,p^* = -g, \qquad \lambda(\Delta - \|p^*\|) = 0, \qquad B + \lambda I \succeq 0.
$$
The condition $B + \lambda I \succeq 0$ is what upgrades the first-order KKT stationarity of Exercise 5 to *global* optimality even when $B$ is indefinite: it forces $p^*$ to be the global minimiser of the *convex* shifted model $g^\top p + \tfrac12 p^\top(B+\lambda I)p$, whose constrained minimum over the ball coincides with that of $m$. When $B \succ 0$ and $\|B^{-1}g\| \le \Delta$ the solution is the interior Newton step with $\lambda = 0$; otherwise $\lambda > 0$ is the unique root of the secular equation on the boundary [ref: TODO_REF Conn, A. R., Gould, N. I. M. & Toint, P. L. — Trust-Region Methods].

**Theorem 2 (the secular equation and the hard case).** With $B = \sum_i \lambda_i q_iq_i^\top$ ($\lambda_1 \le \cdots \le \lambda_n$), the boundary solution solves the **secular equation**
$$
\frac{1}{\|p(\lambda)\|} - \frac{1}{\Delta} = 0, \qquad p(\lambda) = -(B+\lambda I)^{-1}g = -\sum_i \frac{q_i^\top g}{\lambda_i + \lambda}\,q_i,
$$
sought for $\lambda \in (\max(0, -\lambda_1), \infty)$, where $\|p(\lambda)\|$ is strictly decreasing (Exercise 6); the reciprocal form is used because $1/\|p(\lambda)\|$ is nearly linear in $\lambda$, so safeguarded Newton converges rapidly. The **hard case** occurs when $q_1^\top g = 0$ (more generally $g$ is orthogonal to the eigenspace of $\lambda_1$) and $\lambda_1 < 0$: then $\|p(\lambda)\| \to \|p(-\lambda_1)\| < \infty$ as $\lambda \downarrow -\lambda_1$, and if $\Delta$ exceeds this limit no $\lambda > -\lambda_1$ works. The resolution sets $\lambda = -\lambda_1$, takes $\hat p = -(B-\lambda_1 I)^\dagger g$ (the pseudoinverse, on the range of $B - \lambda_1 I$), and adds a multiple of the leftmost eigenvector, $p^* = \hat p + \sigma q_1$, with $\sigma$ chosen so $\|p^*\| = \Delta$; this $p^*$ satisfies $(B+\lambda I)p^* = -g$ with $B+\lambda I \succeq 0$ singular, meeting Theorem 1. The **Moré-Sorensen** algorithm implements this with a Cholesky factorisation of $B + \lambda I$ at each Newton step on the secular equation, safeguarding $\lambda$ to stay in the interval of definiteness [ref: TODO_REF Nocedal, J. & Wright, S. J. — Numerical Optimization (2nd ed.)].

**Theorem 3 (global convergence to stationary points).** Let $f$ be bounded below and $C^1$ with Lipschitz gradient, let $\{B_k\}$ be uniformly bounded ($\|B_k\| \le \beta$), and let each step $p_k$ satisfy the **fraction-of-Cauchy-decrease** condition $m_k(0) - m_k(p_k) \ge c_1\big(m_k(0) - m_k(p_k^C)\big)$ for a fixed $c_1 \in (0,1]$ and $\|p_k\| \le \Delta_k$. Then with acceptance threshold $\eta = 0$, $\liminf_{k\to\infty}\|g_k\| = 0$, and with $\eta > 0$, $\lim_{k\to\infty}\|g_k\| = 0$. The proof combines the Cauchy bound (Key theorem) with the ratio test: when $\|g_k\|$ stays bounded away from zero, the predicted reduction is bounded below by $\tfrac12 c_1\|g_k\|\min(\Delta_k, \|g_k\|/\beta)$, and a Taylor estimate of $\operatorname{ared} - \operatorname{pred} = O(\|p_k\|^2)$ forces $\rho_k \to 1$ for small $\Delta_k$, so the radius cannot shrink to zero, and an infinite sum of strictly positive reductions contradicts $f$ being bounded below. The Cauchy step is the only step quality the theorem needs, which is why the Cauchy point, dogleg, two-dimensional subspace, and exact solves all converge — each dominates the Cauchy decrease with $c_1 = 1$, the dogleg and exact solutions strictly improving on it [ref: TODO_REF Conn, A. R., Gould, N. I. M. & Toint, P. L. — Trust-Region Methods].

**Theorem 4 (Steihaug-Toint truncated CG).** For large $n$, factoring $B + \lambda I$ is prohibitive, so the subproblem is solved approximately by conjugate gradients [43.07.04] on $m(p)$ from $p_0 = 0$, with two extra stopping rules: terminate and return the boundary point if a CG iterate would leave the trust region, or if a search direction $d$ has nonpositive curvature $d^\top B d \le 0$ (Exercise 7). In exact arithmetic the CG iterates have strictly increasing norms $\|p_0\| < \|p_1\| < \cdots$, so the first boundary crossing is unambiguous, and the very first step is the Cauchy point — hence the truncated solution satisfies the fraction-of-Cauchy-decrease condition with $c_1 = 1$ and Theorem 3 applies. When $B \succ 0$ and the iteration converges inside the region, it returns the exact Newton step; when truncated at the boundary it returns a step bending from the Cauchy direction toward Newton, the iterative analogue of the dogleg. Each inner iteration costs one product $Bd$ and no factorisation, so the method scales to the Hessian-free regime where only matrix-vector products are available [ref: TODO_REF Steihaug, T. — The Conjugate Gradient Method and Trust Regions in Large Scale Optimization].

**Synthesis.** The trust-region step is one object — the constrained minimiser of the quadratic model over a ball — and the foundational reason the whole apparatus works is the Cauchy sufficient-reduction bound, which guarantees a model decrease scaling like $\|g_k\|\min(\Delta_k, \|g_k\|/\|B_k\|)$ from the cheapest admissible step, so that any step dominating it inherits global convergence. The central insight is that this decouples robustness from the Newton step: where line-search Newton must repair an indefinite Hessian before it can produce a descent direction, the trust-region subproblem absorbs indefiniteness through the multiplier $\lambda$, the characterisation $(B+\lambda I)p = -g$ with $B+\lambda I \succeq 0$ being exactly the convexification that makes the constrained problem well posed, and the hard case is the precise boundary where this convexification becomes singular and an eigenvector of the leftmost eigenvalue must be added. The dogleg and two-dimensional subspace methods are cheap approximations that interpolate between the Cauchy point and the Newton step $-B^{-1}g$, while Moré-Sorensen solves the secular equation exactly and Steihaug-Toint [43.07.04] solves it approximately by truncated conjugate gradients whose first iterate is the Cauchy point — so the same min-over-a-ball problem is dual to a one-parameter eigenvalue-shift problem in $\lambda$, and that duality is the bridge from the elementary steepest-descent baseline to the large-scale Hessian-free solvers. Putting these together, the Cauchy bound generalises the Armijo sufficient-decrease condition of line search, the secular monotonicity of $\|p(\lambda)\|$ generalises the one-dimensional line-minimisation, and the convergence theorem appears again wherever a globalised Newton method must converge from far away.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Cauchy sufficient reduction).** *For $m(p) = f + g^\top p + \tfrac12 p^\top Bp$ with $g \ne 0$, $B$ symmetric, and Cauchy point $p^C$ at radius $\Delta$, one has $m(0) - m(p^C) \ge \tfrac12\|g\|\min(\Delta, \|g\|/\|B\|)$.*

*Proof.* As in the Key theorem. Restricting $m$ to the ray $p(t) = -t g/\|g\|$ gives the scalar quadratic $\psi(t) = -\|g\|t + \tfrac12\gamma t^2$ with $\gamma = g^\top Bg/\|g\|^2 \le \|B\|$. If $\gamma \le 0$, $\psi$ is nonincreasing so $t^* = \Delta$ and $m(0)-m(p^C) = \|g\|\Delta - \tfrac12\gamma\Delta^2 \ge \|g\|\Delta \ge \tfrac12\|g\|\min(\Delta,\|g\|/\|B\|)$. If $\gamma > 0$ and $t_u = \|g\|/\gamma \le \Delta$, then $m(0)-m(p^C) = \|g\|^2/(2\gamma) \ge \|g\|^2/(2\|B\|) = \tfrac12\|g\|(\|g\|/\|B\|) \ge \tfrac12\|g\|\min(\Delta,\|g\|/\|B\|)$. If $\gamma > 0$ and $t_u > \Delta$, then $\gamma\Delta < \|g\|$ and $m(0)-m(p^C) = \|g\|\Delta - \tfrac12\gamma\Delta^2 > \tfrac12\|g\|\Delta \ge \tfrac12\|g\|\min(\Delta,\|g\|/\|B\|)$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (KKT characterisation with global optimality).** *$p^*$ solves $\min_{\|p\|\le\Delta} m(p)$ globally iff $\|p^*\|\le\Delta$ and there is $\lambda\ge0$ with $(B+\lambda I)p^* = -g$, $\lambda(\Delta-\|p^*\|)=0$, and $B+\lambda I\succeq0$.*

*Proof.* ($\Leftarrow$) Suppose such a $\lambda$ exists. Since $B+\lambda I\succeq0$, the function $\hat m(p) = g^\top p + \tfrac12 p^\top(B+\lambda I)p$ is convex, and $(B+\lambda I)p^* = -g$ makes $p^*$ its unconstrained global minimiser, so for all $p$, $\hat m(p) \ge \hat m(p^*)$, i.e.
$$
m(p) + \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\|p\|^2 \ge m(p^*) + \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\|p^*\|^2 .
$$
For any feasible $p$ with $\|p\|\le\Delta$, $\tfrac{\lambda}{2}\|p\|^2 \le \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\Delta^2 = \tfrac{\lambda}{2}\|p^*\|^2$ when $\lambda > 0$ (complementarity forces $\|p^*\| = \Delta$), and the inequality $\lambda(\Delta-\|p^*\|)=0$ also covers $\lambda = 0$ at once (the shift term vanishes); substituting gives $m(p) \ge m(p^*)$. So $p^*$ is a global minimiser. ($\Rightarrow$) Conversely, if $p^*$ is a global solution, the constraint qualification holds (a single smooth ball constraint), so KKT gives $\lambda \ge 0$ with $(B+\lambda I)p^* = -g$ and complementarity. For the curvature condition: if $\|p^*\| < \Delta$ then $\lambda = 0$ and second-order necessity at an interior minimiser gives $B\succeq0$; if $\|p^*\| = \Delta$, take any $w$ with $w^\top p^* = 0$ and consider feasible arcs on the sphere through $p^*$ tangent to $w$ — optimality along them yields $w^\top(B+\lambda I)w \ge 0$, and combined with $(B+\lambda I)p^* = -g$ along the radial direction, $B+\lambda I\succeq0$ on all of $\mathbb{R}^n$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (secular monotonicity).** *For symmetric $B$ with $\lambda_1 = \lambda_{\min}(B)$ and $g$ not orthogonal to the $\lambda_1$-eigenspace, $\|p(\lambda)\| = \|(B+\lambda I)^{-1}g\|$ is continuous and strictly decreasing on $(-\lambda_1, \infty)$ from $+\infty$ to $0$, so the secular equation $\|p(\lambda)\| = \Delta$ has a unique root for every $\Delta > 0$.*

*Proof.* As in Exercise 6: $\|p(\lambda)\|^2 = \sum_i (q_i^\top g)^2/(\lambda_i+\lambda)^2$ with each denominator positive on $(-\lambda_1,\infty)$, and $\tfrac{d}{d\lambda}\|p(\lambda)\|^2 = -2\sum_i (q_i^\top g)^2/(\lambda_i+\lambda)^3 < 0$ strictly, since some $q_i^\top g \ne 0$. As $\lambda\downarrow-\lambda_1$ the term with $q_1^\top g \ne 0$ blows up, giving $\|p(\lambda)\|\to+\infty$; as $\lambda\to\infty$ every term vanishes, giving $\|p(\lambda)\|\to0$. Strict monotone continuity gives the unique root. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (dogleg dominates the Cauchy decrease).** *For $B\succ0$, the dogleg step $p^{dl}$ at radius $\Delta$ satisfies $m(0) - m(p^{dl}) \ge m(0) - m(p^C)$, so it meets the fraction-of-Cauchy-decrease condition with $c_1 = 1$.*

*Proof.* By Exercise 4, $\|p(\tau)\|$ is nondecreasing along the dogleg path, and one shows $m(p(\tau))$ is nonincreasing along it: on the first segment $p(\tau) = \tau p^U$, $\tfrac{d}{d\tau}m(\tau p^U) = g^\top p^U + \tau (p^U)^\top B p^U$, and at $\tau\le1$ this is $\le g^\top p^U + (p^U)^\top Bp^U = 0$ at $\tau = 1$ by the definition of $p^U$ as the line minimiser, so $m$ decreases on $[0,1]$; on the second segment a direct computation using $p^B = -B^{-1}g$ shows $\tfrac{d}{d\tau}m(p(\tau)) \le 0$ for $\tau\in[1,2]$. Hence the dogleg point, taken at the boundary crossing or at $p^B$, has $m$-value no larger than the value at the unconstrained Cauchy step $p^U$, and the Cauchy point $p^C$ lies on the same initial ray at distance $\le\|p^U\|$ (when $p^U$ is interior, $p^C = p^U$; when capped, $p^C$ is the boundary point on the first segment, which the dogleg path passes through or beyond). In every case $m(0)-m(p^{dl}) \ge m(0)-m(p^C)$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (global convergence, $\liminf$ form).** *Under $f$ bounded below and $C^1$ with $L$-Lipschitz gradient, $\|B_k\|\le\beta$, the fraction-of-Cauchy-decrease condition with constant $c_1$, and $\eta = 0$, the iterates satisfy $\liminf_k\|g_k\| = 0$.*

*Proof sketch (full argument in Conn-Gould-Toint Ch. 6).* Suppose for contradiction $\|g_k\|\ge\epsilon > 0$ for all large $k$. By Proposition 1 and the fraction condition, $\operatorname{pred}_k \ge \tfrac12 c_1\epsilon\min(\Delta_k, \epsilon/\beta)$. A second-order Taylor bound with $L$-Lipschitz gradient gives $|\operatorname{ared}_k - \operatorname{pred}_k| \le c_2\Delta_k^2$ for a constant $c_2$ depending on $L,\beta$. Thus $|\rho_k - 1| \le c_2\Delta_k^2 / \operatorname{pred}_k \le 2c_2\Delta_k^2/(c_1\epsilon\min(\Delta_k,\epsilon/\beta))$, which tends to $0$ as $\Delta_k\to0$; hence there is $\bar\Delta > 0$ with $\rho_k > \tfrac14$ whenever $\Delta_k \le \bar\Delta$, so the radius-update rule never shrinks below $\tfrac14\bar\Delta$ and $\Delta_k \ge \delta > 0$ for all large $k$. Then every accepted step (and with $\eta = 0$ all steps with $\rho_k > 0$ are accepted, which holds for small enough $\Delta_k$) reduces $f$ by at least $\eta'\operatorname{pred}_k \ge \tfrac12 c_1\epsilon\min(\delta,\epsilon/\beta) > 0$, and infinitely many such reductions drive $f\to-\infty$, contradicting boundedness below. Therefore $\liminf_k\|g_k\| = 0$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Convex sets, functions, and operations [44.01.01] supplies the quadratic-form machinery the subproblem rests on: the model $m(p) = g^\top p + \tfrac12 p^\top Bp$ is convex exactly when $B \succeq 0$, and the global-optimality characterisation $(B+\lambda I)p^* = -g$ with $B+\lambda I \succeq 0$ is the convexification of an indefinite model — the multiplier $\lambda$ is chosen to push the shifted Hessian into the positive-semidefinite cone described in that unit, after which the constrained minimum over the ball is the minimum of a genuinely convex quadratic. The supporting-line and PSD-Hessian conditions of [44.01.01] are precisely the second-order facts the proof of Proposition 2 invokes.

- Multivariable Taylor and extrema [02.05.05] is the source of the quadratic model itself and of the convergence analysis: $m_k$ is the second-order Taylor polynomial of $f$ at $x_k$, the error $\operatorname{ared} - \operatorname{pred}$ is the Taylor remainder controlled by the Lipschitz constant of the gradient, and the ratio test measures how faithfully that truncated expansion predicts the true decrease. The global-convergence theorem is, at its core, a quantitative statement about when the Taylor remainder is small relative to the predicted reduction.

- The conjugate gradient method [43.07.04] is the engine of the large-scale solver: Steihaug-Toint truncated CG runs conjugate gradients on the model $m(p)$ from the origin, exploiting the monotone-increasing norm of the CG iterates to detect the trust-region boundary and the curvature test $d^\top Bd \le 0$ to detect indefiniteness, returning the boundary step in either case. The first CG iterate is the Cauchy point, so the truncated solve inherits the sufficient-reduction bound and hence global convergence, while costing only the matrix-vector products that make CG attractive for sparse or Hessian-free problems.

- Derivative computation by finite differences and automatic differentiation [44.03.08] supplies the gradients $g_k$ and the Hessian information $B_k$ (or Hessian-vector products $B_k v$) that every trust-region step consumes; the Hessian-free regime where only products $B_k v$ are available is exactly the setting where Steihaug-Toint truncated CG, rather than the factorisation-based Moré-Sorensen solver, is the method of choice, since reverse-mode automatic differentiation delivers $\nabla f$ cheaply and a second sweep or a finite difference of gradients delivers $B_k v$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The trust-region idea grew out of work on damped least squares and nonlinear regression. **Kenneth Levenberg** in 1944 and **Donald Marquardt** in 1963 proposed adding a multiple of the identity to the Gauss-Newton normal equations, solving $(J^\top J + \lambda I)p = -J^\top r$ — the same shift $(B + \lambda I)$ that appears in the exact trust-region characterisation, though Levenberg and Marquardt controlled $\lambda$ directly rather than through a radius [ref: TODO_REF Marquardt 1963]. **S. M. Goldfeldt, R. E. Quandt, and H. F. Trotter** in 1966 made the radius explicit, and the modern formulation with the ratio test and adaptive radius was consolidated by **M. J. D. Powell** in a sequence of papers from 1970 onward, who also analysed the dogleg method as a cheap approximate subproblem solver.

The exact subproblem theory — the global-optimality characterisation through $(B+\lambda I)\succeq0$ and the resolution of the hard case — was given its definitive treatment by **Jorge Moré and Danny Sorensen** in 1983, whose safeguarded-Newton algorithm on the secular equation remains the standard exact solver [ref: TODO_REF Moré-Sorensen 1983]. In the same year **Trond Steihaug** introduced the truncated-conjugate-gradient method that made trust regions practical for large-scale problems [ref: TODO_REF Steihaug 1983], independently obtained by **Toint**. The comprehensive synthesis is the monograph of **Andrew Conn, Nicholas Gould, and Philippe Toint** in 2000, which unified the convergence theory under the fraction-of-Cauchy-decrease condition and catalogued the subproblem solvers [ref: TODO_REF Conn-Gould-Toint 2000]. **Jorge Nocedal and Stephen Wright** present the method as the natural alternative to line search, organising it around the Cauchy point and the dogleg step [ref: TODO_REF Nocedal-Wright 2006].
