## Intuition [Beginner]

Full BFGS keeps a complete curvature table — a square array the size of the problem squared. For a problem in a million variables that array has a trillion entries, far too many to store. Limited-memory BFGS notices that the table was never used on its own: it only ever multiplied the gradient to produce a search direction. So instead of keeping the table, L-BFGS keeps just the raw measurements that built it — the last handful of step and slope-change pairs — and rebuilds the one multiplication it needs on the fly. Keeping the last five or ten pairs is usually enough, so the storage drops from a trillion numbers to a few million.

There is a second large-scale idea in this unit. Newton's method aims each step by solving a system of equations involving the curvature. Solving that system exactly is expensive, and early on it is wasted effort — the curvature you are solving against is only a local snapshot, so an exact answer to an approximate question buys you little. A truncated Newton method solves the system only roughly, using a cheap inner loop that you stop early, and tightens the accuracy as you close in on the answer. Far away you take quick sloppy steps; near the solution you solve carefully and recover Newton's full speed.

Picture packing for a trip with one small bag (L-BFGS): you cannot carry your whole closet, so you bring the few recent outfits that actually tell you what the weather has been like, and reconstruct a sensible choice from those. The truncated idea is like sketching a route: a rough sketch is fine while you are far away, and you fill in the fine detail only as you near your destination.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the curvature information as a stack of note cards. Full BFGS would keep one enormous card holding the entire table. L-BFGS instead keeps a short stack of the most recent cards, each recording one step and the slope change across it; when an older card is pushed off the bottom, its information is simply forgotten. To get a search direction, the method runs down the stack and back up it once — a quick two-pass sweep — which is exactly the two-loop recursion.

The takeaway: both methods refuse to store a full curvature table. L-BFGS keeps a short stack of recent measurements and sweeps it twice; truncated Newton keeps no table at all and instead solves the Newton equations approximately with an early-stopped inner loop.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let us run the L-BFGS direction sweep with a memory of just one pair, on a two-variable problem, so every number is concrete. Suppose the most recent step was $s = (1, 0)$ and the slope changed by $y = (2, 0)$ across it. The current gradient is $g = (3, 1)$, and we want the search direction.

First compute the helper number $\rho = 1 / (y \cdot s)$. Here $y \cdot s = 2 \cdot 1 + 0 \cdot 0 = 2$, so $\rho = 1/2 = 0.5$.

Backward pass. Set $q = g = (3, 1)$. Compute $a = \rho\,(s \cdot q) = 0.5 \cdot (1 \cdot 3 + 0 \cdot 1) = 0.5 \cdot 3 = 1.5$. Update $q \leftarrow q - a\,y = (3, 1) - 1.5\,(2, 0) = (3 - 3, 1) = (0, 1)$.

Scaling. The initial guess scales the identity by $\gamma = (s \cdot y)/(y \cdot y) = 2 / (2^2 + 0^2) = 2/4 = 0.5$. Apply it: $r = \gamma\, q = 0.5\,(0, 1) = (0, 0.5)$.

Forward pass. Compute $b = \rho\,(y \cdot r) = 0.5\,(2 \cdot 0 + 0 \cdot 0.5) = 0$. Update $r \leftarrow r + (a - b)\,s = (0, 0.5) + (1.5 - 0)\,(1, 0) = (1.5, 0.5)$.

The direction is $-r = (-1.5, -0.5)$.

What this tells us: from one stored pair and the current gradient, two short passes produce a search direction that already bends the plain downhill direction $-(3,1)$ toward the measured curvature — it stretched the first coordinate (where curvature was large) less than the second. We never built or stored a curvature matrix; we only swept the stored measurements.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be twice continuously differentiable with gradient $g(x) = \nabla f(x)$ and Hessian $\nabla^2 f(x)$. This unit treats two large-scale strategies that both refuse to store an $n \times n$ matrix.

**Limited-memory BFGS.** Recall from [44.03.04] the BFGS inverse update $H_{k+1} = V_k^\top H_k V_k + \rho_k s_k s_k^\top$ with $s_k = x_{k+1}-x_k$, $y_k = g_{k+1}-g_k$, $\rho_k = 1/(y_k^\top s_k)$, and $V_k = I - \rho_k y_k s_k^\top$. **L-BFGS** never forms $H_k$. It stores only the most recent $m$ correction pairs $\{(s_i, y_i)\}_{i=k-m}^{k-1}$ and defines the implicit inverse-Hessian approximation by applying $m$ BFGS updates to a scaled initial matrix $H_k^0 = \gamma_k I$:
$$
H_k = \big(V_{k-1}^\top \cdots V_{k-m}^\top\big)\,H_k^0\,\big(V_{k-m}\cdots V_{k-1}\big) + \sum_{i=k-m}^{k-1}\big(V_{k-1}^\top\cdots V_{i+1}^\top\big)\rho_i s_i s_i^\top\big(V_{i+1}\cdots V_{k-1}\big),
$$
with the standard scaling $\gamma_k = (s_{k-1}^\top y_{k-1})/(y_{k-1}^\top y_{k-1})$, which sizes the unit initial matrix to the most recent curvature. The product $H_k g_k$ is computed by the **two-loop recursion**: set $q \leftarrow g_k$; for $i = k-1, \dots, k-m$ set $\alpha_i \leftarrow \rho_i s_i^\top q$ and $q \leftarrow q - \alpha_i y_i$; set $r \leftarrow \gamma_k q$; for $i = k-m, \dots, k-1$ set $\beta \leftarrow \rho_i y_i^\top r$ and $r \leftarrow r + (\alpha_i - \beta) s_i$; return $H_k g_k = r$. The cost is $4mn$ multiply-adds and $O(mn)$ storage, with no matrix formed and no linear solve. The search direction is $p_k = -r$, and a line search satisfying the Wolfe conditions [44.03.01] supplies the step, guaranteeing $s_k^\top y_k > 0$ so each stored pair is curvature-admissible.

**Inexact (truncated) Newton.** An **inexact Newton method** computes a step $p_k$ that solves the Newton system only approximately: with residual $r_k = \nabla^2 f(x_k)\,p_k + g_k$, the step is accepted when
$$
\|r_k\| \le \eta_k\,\|g_k\|,
$$
where $\{\eta_k\} \subset [0,1)$ is the **forcing sequence**. The **truncated Newton-CG** method realises this by applying the linear conjugate gradient method [43.07.04] to $\nabla^2 f(x_k)\,p = -g_k$, terminating CG as soon as the residual test holds. Because CG accesses its matrix only through matrix-vector products, the Hessian is never assembled: each inner iteration needs one **Hessian-vector product** $\nabla^2 f(x_k)\,v$, supplied either by automatic differentiation or by the finite-difference approximation $\nabla^2 f(x_k)\,v \approx (g(x_k + hv) - g(x_k))/h$ of [44.03.08], making the method **Hessian-free**. When CG generates a direction $d_j$ with nonpositive curvature, $d_j^\top \nabla^2 f(x_k)\,d_j \le 0$, the iteration is **truncated**: on the first inner iteration it returns $-g_k$ (steepest descent), and thereafter it returns the current inner iterate, which is a descent direction. The new symbols $H_k^0$, $\gamma_k$, the two-loop scalars $\alpha_i, \beta$, the forcing sequence $\eta_k$, the inner residual $r_k$, and the Hessian-vector product are recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"L-BFGS is BFGS with a smaller matrix."* L-BFGS stores no matrix at all. It stores $2m$ vectors and reconstructs only the action $H_k g_k$. The implicit $H_k$ it represents changes its initial matrix $\gamma_k I$ every iteration, so it is not a truncation of a single evolving matrix but a fresh $m$-update product each step.

- *"A more accurate inner solve is always better."* Solving the Newton system to high accuracy far from the solution wastes inner iterations against a Hessian that is only a local model; the forcing sequence deliberately keeps $\eta_k$ large early. Over-solving raises cost with no gain in the outer rate, which is capped by how good the local quadratic model is.

- *"CG can be run to convergence on any Newton system."* Away from a minimiser the Hessian can be indefinite, and CG breaks down on indefinite systems. Truncated Newton must detect nonpositive curvature and stop, returning a guaranteed descent direction; ignoring this produces an ascent step or a CG division by a nonpositive curvature.

- *"Hessian-free means derivative-free."* The Hessian-vector product still needs first derivatives: automatic differentiation differentiates the gradient, and the finite-difference form evaluates the gradient at a perturbed point. The saving is the $n^2$ storage and assembly of the Hessian, not the use of derivatives.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is that the two-loop recursion is not an approximation of the BFGS direction but computes it exactly from the stored pairs: the $O(mn)$ sweep returns precisely $H_k g_k$ for the limited-memory $H_k$ defined by $m$ BFGS updates applied to $\gamma_k I$. This is what makes L-BFGS a genuine quasi-Newton method [44.03.04] rather than a heuristic, and it is the reason the line-search descent theory carries over unchanged.

**Theorem (two-loop recursion computes the L-BFGS direction).** *Let $H_k^0 = \gamma_k I \succ 0$ and let $\{(s_i, y_i)\}_{i=k-m}^{k-1}$ satisfy $s_i^\top y_i > 0$. Define $H_k$ by applying the BFGS inverse update successively to $H_k^0$ with the pairs $(s_{k-m}, y_{k-m}), \dots, (s_{k-1}, y_{k-1})$. Then the two-loop recursion returns $r = H_k g_k$, and $H_k \succ 0$, so $p_k = -H_k g_k$ is a descent direction.*

**Proof.** Write $V_i = I - \rho_i y_i s_i^\top$, $\rho_i = 1/(y_i^\top s_i)$. One BFGS inverse update is $H^{+} = V_i^\top H V_i + \rho_i s_i s_i^\top$. Applying $H^{+}$ to a vector $u$ gives
$$
H^{+} u = V_i^\top H (V_i u) + \rho_i s_i (s_i^\top u) = V_i^\top H\big(u - \rho_i y_i (s_i^\top u)\big) + \rho_i (s_i^\top u)\, s_i .
$$
Set $\alpha_i = \rho_i s_i^\top u$. The vector $u - \alpha_i y_i$ is precisely the backward-loop update $q \leftarrow q - \alpha_i y_i$, and $V_i^\top w = w - \rho_i s_i (y_i^\top w)$ acting on $w = H(u-\alpha_i y_i)$ produces $H(u-\alpha_i y_i) - \rho_i s_i\big(y_i^\top H(u-\alpha_i y_i)\big)$. Adding the term $\alpha_i s_i$ gives
$$
H^{+}u = H(u-\alpha_i y_i) + \big(\alpha_i - \rho_i y_i^\top H(u-\alpha_i y_i)\big)\,s_i .
$$
Reading this recursively: the innermost operation is $H_k^0$ applied to the fully-reduced $q$, and each layer outward restores one $s_i$ with coefficient $\alpha_i - \beta_i$ where $\beta_i = \rho_i y_i^\top r$ and $r$ is the partial result. The backward loop computes all $\alpha_i$ and reduces $g_k$ to $q = \big(V_{k-m}\cdots V_{k-1}\big)g_k$; the scaling computes $r = H_k^0 q$; the forward loop applies the $V_i^\top$ factors and adds the $\rho_i s_i s_i^\top$ terms in the correct order, yielding $r = H_k g_k$. Each $\alpha_i$ stored in the backward loop is exactly the $\alpha_i$ reused in the forward loop because $s_i^\top q$ at the backward visit equals $s_i^\top u$ at that layer.

For positive-definiteness, hereditary positive-definiteness of the BFGS inverse update [44.03.04] applies to each of the $m$ updates: $H_k^0 = \gamma_k I \succ 0$ since $\gamma_k = (s_{k-1}^\top y_{k-1})/(y_{k-1}^\top y_{k-1}) > 0$ by the curvature condition, and each update preserves positive-definiteness given $s_i^\top y_i > 0$. Hence $H_k \succ 0$, so $g_k^\top p_k = -g_k^\top H_k g_k < 0$ whenever $g_k \ne 0$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This identity is the foundational reason L-BFGS inherits the quasi-Newton theory of [44.03.04] wholesale: because the two-loop output is the exact BFGS direction for the implicit $H_k$, hereditary positive-definiteness makes $p_k$ a descent direction, and the Wolfe line search of [44.03.01] then supplies the next admissible pair $s_k^\top y_k > 0$, so the invariant propagates and the globalisation theory needs no change — this is exactly the same self-perpetuating curvature condition that drove full BFGS. The argument builds toward the compact-representation theorem of the Advanced results, where the same $m$-update product is rewritten as a single low-rank correction $\gamma_k I + UMU^\top$, and it appears again in the truncated-Newton analysis: there, positive curvature of the Hessian along the CG directions plays the role that $s_i^\top y_i > 0$ plays here, both being the condition under which the inner model stays a genuine bowl. The central insight is that limited memory changes only the *representation* of the quasi-Newton operator, not its mathematics; putting these together, L-BFGS generalises BFGS to large $n$ by storing the data that built the matrix instead of the matrix, and the bridge is the recursion identity that makes the two provably the same direction.

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the finite-difference Hessian-vector product $\nabla^2 f(x)v \approx (g(x+hv) - g(x))/h$ is first-order accurate: prove the error is $O(h)$ for smooth $f$, and state the trade-off in choosing $h$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Taylor-expand $g(x + hv) = g(x) + h\nabla^2 f(x)v + O(h^2)$ [02.05.05].

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Taylor's theorem [02.05.05] applied to the gradient, $g(x + hv) = g(x) + h\,\nabla^2 f(x)v + \tfrac{h^2}{2}D^3f(x)[v,v] + O(h^3)$. Subtracting $g(x)$ and dividing by $h$ gives $(g(x+hv)-g(x))/h = \nabla^2 f(x)v + \tfrac{h}{2}D^3f(x)[v,v] + O(h^2)$, so the truncation error is $O(h)$, first order. The trade-off: shrinking $h$ reduces truncation error but amplifies floating-point cancellation error in $g(x+hv) - g(x)$, which scales like $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}/h$; balancing the two gives an optimal $h \approx \sqrt{\varepsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}}$, where both errors are about $\sqrt{\varepsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}}$. (Automatic differentiation [44.03.08] avoids this trade-off, giving the product to working precision.) Full credit for the $O(h)$ expansion and the cancellation trade-off.

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

The Newton-CG inner iteration encounters a direction $d_j$ with $d_j^\top \nabla^2 f(x_k)\,d_j \le 0$ on iteration $j \ge 1$. Explain why returning the current inner iterate $z_j$ (rather than continuing CG) yields a descent direction, given that CG was started from $z_0 = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

With $z_0 = 0$, the first CG search direction is $-(-g_k) = $ the residual $g_k$ negated... track the sign: CG solves $\nabla^2 f\,p = -g_k$, so the initial residual is $-g_k$ and $g_k^\top z_j$ accumulates a definite sign.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

CG applied to $\nabla^2 f(x_k)\,p = -g_k$ from $z_0 = 0$ produces iterates $z_j$ that minimise the quadratic model $\tfrac12 z^\top \nabla^2 f(x_k)z + g_k^\top z$ over the expanding Krylov subspace, while the curvature along each accepted CG direction is positive (the iteration stops the moment nonpositive curvature appears). Each positive-curvature step strictly decreases the model below its value at $z_0 = 0$, where the model is $0$; hence the model at $z_j$ is negative, i.e. $\tfrac12 z_j^\top \nabla^2 f(x_k)z_j + g_k^\top z_j < 0$. The first term is nonnegative on the positive-curvature subspace explored, so $g_k^\top z_j < 0$: the returned $z_j$ is a descent direction. On the very first iteration ($j=1$) nonpositive curvature in the only available direction forces the fallback $p_k = -g_k$, also a descent direction. Full credit for the model-decrease argument giving $g_k^\top z_j < 0$.

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

State and prove the inexact-Newton rate dichotomy: assuming $x_k \to x^*$ with $\nabla^2 f(x^*) \succ 0$ and $\nabla^2 f$ Lipschitz near $x^*$, and unit steps $x_{k+1} = x_k + p_k$ with $\|\nabla^2 f(x_k)p_k + g_k\| \le \eta_k\|g_k\|$, show that $\eta_k \to 0$ gives q-superlinear convergence and $\eta_k = O(\|g_k\|)$ gives quadratic convergence.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $g_k = \nabla^2 f(x^*)e_k + O(\|e_k\|^2)$ with $e_k = x_k - x^*$, and write the exact Newton step $p_k^N = -\nabla^2 f(x_k)^{-1}g_k$. The inexact step is $p_k = p_k^N + \nabla^2 f(x_k)^{-1}r_k$ with $\|r_k\| \le \eta_k\|g_k\|$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $H^* = \nabla^2 f(x^*) \succ 0$, $H_k = \nabla^2 f(x_k)$, $e_k = x_k - x^*$, $r_k = H_k p_k + g_k$ with $\|r_k\| \le \eta_k\|g_k\|$. The exact Newton step satisfies $e_k + p_k^N = e_k - H_k^{-1}g_k$, and the standard exact-Newton estimate under a Lipschitz Hessian gives $\|e_k - H_k^{-1}g_k\| \le C\|e_k\|^2$. The inexact step is $p_k = -H_k^{-1}(g_k - r_k) = p_k^N + H_k^{-1}r_k$, so
$$
e_{k+1} = e_k + p_k = (e_k - H_k^{-1}g_k) + H_k^{-1}r_k,
$$
whence $\|e_{k+1}\| \le C\|e_k\|^2 + \|H_k^{-1}\|\,\|r_k\| \le C\|e_k\|^2 + \|H_k^{-1}\|\,\eta_k\|g_k\|$. Since $\|g_k\| = \|H^*e_k + O(\|e_k\|^2)\| \le L\|e_k\|$ near $x^*$ and $\|H_k^{-1}\| \le M$, this is $\|e_{k+1}\| \le C\|e_k\|^2 + ML\,\eta_k\|e_k\|$. If $\eta_k \to 0$ then $\|e_{k+1}\|/\|e_k\| \le C\|e_k\| + ML\eta_k \to 0$: q-superlinear. If $\eta_k \le K\|g_k\| \le KL\|e_k\|$ then $\|e_{k+1}\| \le C\|e_k\|^2 + ML\cdot KL\|e_k\|\cdot\|e_k\| = (C + MKL^2)\|e_k\|^2$: q-quadratic, recovering exact Newton. Full credit for the error split $e_{k+1} = (e_k - H_k^{-1}g_k) + H_k^{-1}r_k$ and both rate conclusions.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

Large-scale unconstrained optimization is organised here around two refusals to store an $n \times n$ matrix — the limited-memory secant representation and the matrix-free inexact Newton solve — and the theory below settles the compact form of the L-BFGS operator, the exact convergence dichotomy of inexact Newton, the negative-curvature handling that makes Newton-CG robust, and the practical comparison that governs which method a large problem wants.

**Theorem 1 (compact representation of L-BFGS).** With $S_k = [s_{k-m}\ \cdots\ s_{k-1}]$, $Y_k = [y_{k-m}\ \cdots\ y_{k-1}] \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times m}$, let $L_k$ be the strictly-lower-triangular part of $S_k^\top Y_k$ and $D_k = \operatorname{diag}(s_i^\top y_i)$. The limited-memory inverse-Hessian admits the compact form
$$
H_k = \gamma_k I + \begin{bmatrix}S_k & \gamma_k Y_k\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} R_k^{-\top}(D_k + \gamma_k Y_k^\top Y_k)R_k^{-1} & -R_k^{-\top} \\ -R_k^{-1} & 0 \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}S_k^\top \\ \gamma_k Y_k^\top\end{bmatrix},
$$
where $R_k$ is the upper-triangular part of $S_k^\top Y_k$. This expresses $H_k$ as a scaled identity plus a rank-$2m$ correction, so $H_k v$ costs $O(mn)$ via two tall-skinny matrix products and a small $2m \times 2m$ solve, the matrix-form equivalent of the two-loop recursion. The direct-Hessian approximation $B_k = H_k^{-1}$ has the dual compact form $B_k = \tfrac{1}{\gamma_k}I - [\,\tfrac{1}{\gamma_k}S_k\ \ Y_k\,]\,N_k^{-1}[\cdots]^\top$, which is what trust-region L-BFGS uses to apply $B_k$ and solve the subproblem [ref: TODO_REF Nocedal, J. & Wright, S. J. — Numerical Optimization (2nd ed.)].

**Theorem 2 (Dembo-Eisenstat-Steihaug local convergence of inexact Newton).** Let $F = \nabla f$ be continuously differentiable with $F'(x^*) = \nabla^2 f(x^*)$ nonsingular at a root $x^*$, and run the inexact Newton iteration accepting $p_k$ whenever $\|F(x_k) + F'(x_k)p_k\| \le \eta_k\|F(x_k)\|$. If $\eta_k \le \eta_{\max} < 1$ then for $x_0$ near $x^*$ the iterates converge to $x^*$, q-linearly in the norm $\|v\|_* = \|F'(x^*)v\|$ with asymptotic factor $\eta_{\max}$. Moreover $\eta_k \to 0$ forces q-superlinear convergence, and $\eta_k = O(\|F(x_k)\|^p)$ for $p \in (0,1]$ forces q-order $1 + p$; in particular $\eta_k = O(\|F(x_k)\|)$ recovers Newton's quadratic rate. The forcing sequence is thus a precise dial trading inner-solve accuracy against outer convergence order, and the practical choice $\eta_k = \min\{\tfrac12, \sqrt{\|F(x_k)\|}\}$ (Eisenstat-Walker) buys superlinear convergence at a controlled inner cost [ref: TODO_REF Dembo, R. S., Eisenstat, S. C. & Steihaug, T. — Inexact Newton methods].

**Theorem 3 (Newton-CG truncation and Steihaug-Toint trust-region CG).** Applying CG [43.07.04] to $\nabla^2 f(x_k)p = -g_k$ from $p^{(0)} = 0$: while the curvature $d_j^\top \nabla^2 f(x_k)d_j > 0$ along every generated direction, the iterates $p^{(j)}$ monotonically decrease the quadratic model and increase in norm, $\|p^{(j)}\|$ strictly increasing. If a nonpositive-curvature direction appears, the line-search Newton-CG returns $p^{(j)}$ (or $-g_k$ if $j = 0$), a descent direction; the trust-region variant (Steihaug-Toint) instead follows the CG path to the point where it crosses the trust-region boundary, exploiting the negative-curvature direction to reach the boundary. The monotone-norm-increase property is what makes the trust-region truncation well defined: the CG path leaves the trust region exactly once. This furnishes a globally convergent Hessian-free method whose inner cost adapts to the local difficulty of the Newton system [ref: TODO_REF Nocedal, J. & Wright, S. J. — Numerical Optimization (2nd ed.)].

**Theorem 4 (global convergence of L-BFGS on convex problems).** On a twice-differentiable $f$ with $\nabla^2 f$ bounded, $mI \preceq \nabla^2 f \preceq MI$ on the level set $\{f \le f(x_0)\}$ (strong convexity), L-BFGS with a Wolfe line search [44.03.01] and any fixed memory $m \ge 1$ converges to the unique minimiser at an R-linear rate, $f(x_k) - f^* \le c\,\rho^k$ for some $\rho \in (0,1)$. The proof bounds the eigenvalues of the implicit $H_k$ above and below uniformly in $k$ (the trace and determinant of $B_k = H_k^{-1}$ stay controlled because every stored pair satisfies $m \le y_i^\top y_i/(s_i^\top y_i)$ and $s_i^\top y_i/(s_i^\top s_i) \le M$), which keeps the search direction angle-bounded and feeds Zoutendijk's condition [44.03.01]. Unlike full BFGS the rate is only R-linear, not superlinear, because the finite memory cannot accumulate the full curvature; superlinearity is recovered only as $m \to \infty$ [ref: TODO_REF Liu, D. C. & Nocedal, J. — On the limited memory BFGS method for large scale optimization].

**Theorem 5 (L-BFGS versus truncated Newton: the cost dichotomy).** For a problem with $n$ variables, L-BFGS costs a fixed $O(mn)$ per outer iteration with $O(mn)$ storage and a single gradient evaluation, and needs no Hessian access; truncated Newton-CG costs $O(n)$ storage but a variable number $j_k$ of Hessian-vector products per outer iteration, total inner cost $O(j_k \cdot \mathrm{cost}(\nabla^2 f\, v))$, where $j_k$ grows as the forcing sequence tightens. The asymptotic comparison: truncated Newton attains superlinear or quadratic outer convergence (Theorem 2) and wins when Hessian-vector products are cheap and accurate (e.g. supplied by automatic differentiation [44.03.08]) and the Hessian is well-conditioned, so CG converges in few inner steps; L-BFGS attains only R-linear convergence (Theorem 4) but with a fixed, predictable, low per-iteration cost and no Hessian access, so it wins when Hessian-vector products are unavailable or expensive, when the problem is noisy, or when robustness and simplicity outweigh asymptotic rate. This is the standard division in large-scale smooth optimization and machine learning [ref: TODO_REF Nocedal, J. & Wright, S. J. — Numerical Optimization (2nd ed.)].

**Synthesis.** Large-scale unconstrained optimization is two answers to one constraint — do not store an $n \times n$ matrix — and the foundational reason both work is that a quasi-Newton or Newton step needs only the *action* of a curvature operator on a vector, never the operator itself. L-BFGS realises this by storing the last $m$ secant pairs and replaying them through the two-loop recursion, which computes the exact BFGS direction of [44.03.04] for an implicit $H_k = \gamma_k I + UMU^\top$; the central insight is that limited memory changes the representation, not the mathematics, so hereditary positive-definiteness and the Wolfe-line-search globalisation of [44.03.01] carry over unchanged, and this is exactly the same self-perpetuating curvature condition $s_i^\top y_i > 0$ that drove full BFGS. Truncated Newton realises the same matrix-free principle differently: it solves the Newton system of [44.03.03] approximately by CG [43.07.04] through Hessian-vector products of [44.03.08], and the Dembo-Eisenstat-Steihaug theorem reveals that the forcing sequence $\eta_k$ is dual to the convergence order, so inner accuracy and outer rate are two faces of one dial. Putting these together, L-BFGS is BFGS with a memory budget and truncated Newton is Newton with an accuracy budget, and the bridge between them is the conjugate-gradient method [43.07.04], at once the inner solver of truncated Newton and the limiting behaviour of BFGS, so the two families meet exactly where the Broyden-class conjugate-direction property of [44.03.04] said they would.

The bridge forward is explicit. The negative-curvature truncation that makes Newton-CG robust generalises to the trust-region setting [44.03.02], where the Steihaug-Toint inner CG follows its path to the trust-region boundary and the indefinite Hessian is handled by the subproblem rather than forbidden; the compact representation of L-BFGS is dual to the compact form of the direct approximation $B_k$ used inside limited-memory trust-region methods; and the cost dichotomy between fixed-budget L-BFGS and accuracy-budget truncated Newton is the same trade between memory and asymptotic rate that separates nonlinear conjugate gradient [44.03.06] from L-BFGS one rung lower. What full BFGS [44.03.04] does for medium problems, these two methods do for the largest — one by forgetting all but the recent curvature, the other by never forming the curvature at all.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (two-loop recursion computes $H_k g_k$).** *Let $H_k$ be defined by $m$ BFGS inverse updates with pairs $(s_i,y_i)$, $s_i^\top y_i > 0$, applied to $H_k^0 = \gamma_k I$. The two-loop recursion returns $r = H_k g_k$.*

*Proof.* As in the Key theorem. The single-update action $H^{+}u = H(u - \alpha_i y_i) + (\alpha_i - \rho_i y_i^\top H(u-\alpha_i y_i))s_i$ with $\alpha_i = \rho_i s_i^\top u$ is precisely one nesting of the backward reduction $q \leftarrow q - \alpha_i y_i$ and the forward restoration $r \leftarrow r + (\alpha_i - \beta_i)s_i$ with $\beta_i = \rho_i y_i^\top r$. The backward loop visits $i = k-1,\dots,k-m$ peeling off the $V_i$ factors from $g_k$; the scaling applies $H_k^0$; the forward loop visits $i = k-m,\dots,k-1$ restoring the $V_i^\top$ factors and the rank-one terms in the reverse order, so the composed operation is exactly $H_k g_k$. The stored $\alpha_i$ are reusable because $s_i^\top q$ at the backward visit to index $i$ equals $s_i^\top u_i$ where $u_i$ is the argument seen by the $i$-th nested update. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (positive-definiteness of the implicit $H_k$).** *If $\gamma_k > 0$ and $s_i^\top y_i > 0$ for all stored $i$, then $H_k \succ 0$ and $p_k = -H_k g_k$ is a descent direction.*

*Proof.* $H_k^0 = \gamma_k I \succ 0$. Each BFGS inverse update preserves positive-definiteness under $s_i^\top y_i > 0$ by hereditary positive-definiteness [44.03.04]: for the update $H^{+} = V_i^\top H V_i + \rho_i s_i s_i^\top$ and any $z \ne 0$, $z^\top H^{+}z = (V_i z)^\top H (V_i z) + \rho_i(s_i^\top z)^2 \ge 0$, vanishing only if $V_i z = 0$ and $s_i^\top z = 0$; but $V_i z = 0$ means $z = \rho_i y_i(s_i^\top z) = 0$, a contradiction. Inducting over the $m$ updates gives $H_k \succ 0$, so $g_k^\top p_k = -g_k^\top H_k g_k < 0$ for $g_k \ne 0$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (first-order accuracy of the finite-difference Hessian-vector product).** *For $f \in C^3$ near $x$, $\big\|(g(x+hv) - g(x))/h - \nabla^2 f(x)v\big\| = O(h)$.*

*Proof.* Taylor's theorem [02.05.05] applied to $g = \nabla f$ gives $g(x + hv) = g(x) + h\nabla^2 f(x)v + \tfrac{h^2}{2}D^3f(\xi)[v,v]$ for some $\xi$ on the segment. Subtract $g(x)$, divide by $h$: the difference quotient equals $\nabla^2 f(x)v + \tfrac{h}{2}D^3f(\xi)[v,v]$, and $\|D^3f\|$ bounded near $x$ makes the remainder $O(h)$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (inexact Newton local convergence).** *Under $x_k \to x^*$, $H^* = \nabla^2 f(x^*) \succ 0$, $\nabla^2 f$ Lipschitz near $x^*$, unit steps with $\|H_k p_k + g_k\| \le \eta_k\|g_k\|$: $\eta_k \le \eta_{\max} < 1$ gives q-linear convergence in $\|\cdot\|_* = \|H^*\cdot\|$ with factor $\eta_{\max}$; $\eta_k \to 0$ gives q-superlinear; $\eta_k = O(\|g_k\|)$ gives q-quadratic.*

*Proof.* Write $e_k = x_k - x^*$, $r_k = H_k p_k + g_k$. The exact Newton estimate under the Lipschitz Hessian gives $\|e_k - H_k^{-1}g_k\|_* \le C\|e_k\|_*^2$. The inexact step is $p_k = -H_k^{-1}(g_k - r_k)$, so $e_{k+1} = (e_k - H_k^{-1}g_k) + H_k^{-1}r_k$, whence
$$
\|e_{k+1}\|_* \le C\|e_k\|_*^2 + \|H^* H_k^{-1}r_k\| \le C\|e_k\|_*^2 + (1 + o(1))\|r_k\| .
$$
With $\|r_k\| \le \eta_k\|g_k\|$ and $\|g_k\| = \|H^*e_k\|(1 + o(1)) = \|e_k\|_*(1 + o(1))$, the linear term is $\eta_k\|e_k\|_*(1 + o(1))$. Thus $\|e_{k+1}\|_* \le (\eta_k + o(1))\|e_k\|_* + C\|e_k\|_*^2$. For $x_0$ close enough the quadratic term is dominated and $\|e_{k+1}\|_*/\|e_k\|_* \le \eta_{\max} + o(1) < 1$: q-linear with factor $\eta_{\max}$. If $\eta_k \to 0$ the ratio tends to $0$: q-superlinear. If $\eta_k \le K\|g_k\| = O(\|e_k\|_*)$, the linear term becomes $O(\|e_k\|_*^2)$ and $\|e_{k+1}\|_* = O(\|e_k\|_*^2)$: q-quadratic. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (Newton-CG returns a descent direction).** *CG on $\nabla^2 f(x_k)p = -g_k$ from $p^{(0)} = 0$, truncated at the first nonpositive-curvature direction (returning $-g_k$ if this is the first inner step), returns $p_k$ with $g_k^\top p_k < 0$.*

*Proof.* CG minimises $\phi(p) = \tfrac12 p^\top \nabla^2 f(x_k)p + g_k^\top p$ over the Krylov subspace generated, taking only positive-curvature steps before truncation. Since $\phi(0) = 0$ and each accepted step strictly decreases $\phi$, the returned $p^{(j)}$ has $\phi(p^{(j)}) < 0$, i.e. $\tfrac12 (p^{(j)})^\top \nabla^2 f(x_k)p^{(j)} + g_k^\top p^{(j)} < 0$. The quadratic term is $\ge 0$ on the explored positive-curvature subspace, so $g_k^\top p^{(j)} < 0$. If truncation occurs at $j = 0$ the fallback $p_k = -g_k$ gives $g_k^\top p_k = -\|g_k\|^2 < 0$. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (monotone norm increase of the CG path).** *While curvature stays positive, the CG iterates from $p^{(0)} = 0$ satisfy $\|p^{(j+1)}\| > \|p^{(j)}\|$.*

*Proof.* CG iterates expand as $p^{(j)} = \sum_{i=0}^{j-1}\xi_i d_i$ over $\nabla^2 f(x_k)$-conjugate directions $d_i$ with step coefficients $\xi_i = (r^{(i)\top}r^{(i)})/(d_i^\top \nabla^2 f(x_k)d_i) > 0$ (positive curvature). Conjugacy gives $d_i^\top d_l$ not necessarily zero, but the standard CG identity $\|p^{(j+1)}\|^2 = \|p^{(j)}\|^2 + 2\xi_j p^{(j)\top}d_j + \xi_j^2\|d_j\|^2$ with $p^{(j)\top}d_j > 0$ (each $p^{(j)}$ has nonnegative components along the running directions, provable by induction since $\xi_i > 0$ and $d_j = r^{(j)} + \tau_j d_{j-1}$ with $\tau_j \ge 0$) makes the increment strictly positive. Hence $\|p^{(j)}\|$ strictly increases, so the path crosses any trust-region boundary exactly once, justifying the Steihaug-Toint truncation. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Quasi-Newton methods (BFGS/DFP/SR1) [44.03.04] are the parent construction: L-BFGS *is* BFGS with the inverse-Hessian approximation stored as its last $m$ generating pairs rather than as a dense matrix, and the two-loop recursion computes the exact BFGS direction for the implicit $H_k = \gamma_k I + UMU^\top$. Hereditary positive-definiteness, the curvature condition $s_i^\top y_i > 0$, and the secant interpretation all transfer verbatim; what changes is only that finite memory degrades the asymptotic rate from superlinear to R-linear, recovered as $m \to \infty$. The Broyden-class conjugate-direction property of that unit is the structural reason L-BFGS behaves like a preconditioned nonlinear conjugate gradient on large problems.

- The linear conjugate gradient method [43.07.04] is the inner engine of truncated Newton: CG solves the symmetric positive-definite Newton system $\nabla^2 f(x_k)p = -g_k$ using only matrix-vector products, terminated early by the forcing-sequence residual test. The same CG, run on a quadratic with exact line search, coincides with full BFGS [44.03.04], so conjugate gradient is the common ancestor of both large-scale families — the inner solver of inexact Newton and the limiting behaviour of the limited-memory secant method. The negative-curvature breakdown of CG on indefinite systems is exactly what forces the Newton-CG truncation.

- Finite-difference and automatic differentiation [44.03.08] supply the Hessian-vector products $\nabla^2 f(x)v$ that make truncated Newton Hessian-free: automatic differentiation differentiates the gradient to give the product to working precision, while the finite difference $(g(x+hv)-g(x))/h$ gives it to $O(h)$ accuracy with a cancellation trade-off optimised at $h \approx \sqrt{\varepsilon_{\mathrm{mach}}}$. Without cheap Hessian-vector products truncated Newton loses its advantage, which is precisely the regime where L-BFGS — needing no Hessian access — is preferred.

- Newton's method [44.03.03], trust-region methods [44.03.02], and nonlinear conjugate gradient [44.03.06] complete the network: truncated Newton is the inexact, matrix-free realisation of Newton's method [44.03.03] with the forcing sequence dialing the convergence order; the negative-curvature truncation generalises to the Steihaug-Toint trust-region CG of [44.03.02], where the indefinite Hessian is absorbed by the subproblem; and L-BFGS sits one rung above nonlinear conjugate gradient [44.03.06] in the memory hierarchy, carrying $m$ directions of curvature memory against CG's single direction, the same memory-versus-rate trade made one level down.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The limited-memory idea is due to **Jorge Nocedal**, whose 1980 paper showed that storing only the last few BFGS correction pairs and replaying them through a recursion preserves most of the method's speed at a fraction of the storage [ref: TODO_REF Nocedal 1980]. The method was placed on a firm convergence footing by **Dong C. Liu and Jorge Nocedal** in 1989, who proved R-linear convergence on strongly convex problems and demonstrated that small memory ($m$ between 3 and 20) is competitive with full BFGS on large problems while scaling to dimensions where a dense matrix is impossible [ref: TODO_REF Liu-Nocedal 1989]. The two-loop recursion they popularised has become the standard direction computation in large-scale optimization libraries.

The inexact-Newton framework was formalised by **Ron Dembo, Stanley Eisenstat, and Trond Steihaug** in 1982, who introduced the forcing sequence $\eta_k$ and proved the local-convergence dichotomy relating it to linear, superlinear, and quadratic rates [ref: TODO_REF Dembo-Eisenstat-Steihaug 1982]. **Trond Steihaug** in 1983 and, independently, **Philippe Toint** combined conjugate gradient with trust regions, giving the negative-curvature-tolerant inner solver now called Steihaug-Toint CG [ref: TODO_REF Steihaug 1983]. The practical forcing-sequence choice $\eta_k = \min\{\tfrac12, \sqrt{\|\nabla f_k\|}\}$ that secures superlinear convergence at controlled cost is due to **Stanley Eisenstat and Homer Walker** in 1996 [ref: TODO_REF Eisenstat-Walker 1996]. The convergence theory of inexact Newton descends from the classical analysis of Newton's method for operator equations by **L. V. Kantorovich**, whose 1948 fixed-point estimates underlie the quadratic-rate baseline these methods relax.
