## Intuition [Beginner]

Almost every fast optimizer needs to know which way is downhill, and how the slope is bending. That information is the derivative: the gradient (the vector of slopes in each direction) and sometimes the second derivatives (how the slopes change). The trouble is that you often have a function only as a piece of code — a long recipe of additions, multiplications, square roots, and so on — with no tidy formula for its slope. So the practical question is: given the code for $f$, how do you get the code for its derivative?

The oldest trick is to nudge and compare. Move the input a tiny amount $h$, see how much the output changes, and divide: the change in output over the change in input is an estimate of the slope. This is the **finite-difference** idea, and it needs nothing but the ability to run $f$. Its weakness is the size of the nudge. Too big a nudge and the straight-line estimate is crude; too small a nudge and the two outputs are so close that rounding noise in the computer swamps the tiny difference. There is a sweet spot in the middle, and it is not as accurate as you might hope.

The sharper trick is to differentiate the recipe itself. Each basic step — add, multiply, take a sine — has a known derivative rule, and the chain rule stitches those rules together. A computer can apply the chain rule mechanically as it runs the code, producing the *exact* derivative (up to ordinary rounding), with no nudge and no guessing of $h$. This is **automatic differentiation**, and it comes in two directions of travel: forward, alongside the computation, and backward, retracing it.

The backward direction holds the prize that powers modern machine learning: it returns the slope in *every* input direction at once, for a cost close to a single run of $f$ — no matter whether there are ten inputs or ten million.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the finite-difference estimate first. On the curve $y = f(x)$, mark your point and a second point a little to the right at distance $h$. Draw the straight line through the two marks: its steepness is the finite-difference slope, a chord standing in for the true tangent. A symmetric version uses one mark on each side, left and right, and the chord through those two hugs the tangent more closely.

The step-size sweet spot is the heart of the method. Shrink $h$ and the chord lines up better with the tangent — until $h$ gets so small that the two output values agree in almost all their digits, and the subtraction throws away accuracy. The error first falls, bottoms out, then climbs again.

|---|---|---|---|
| forward difference | run $f$ only | about $n + 1$ runs of $f$ | modest (about half the digits) |
| central difference | run $f$ only | about $2n$ runs of $f$ | better (about two-thirds of the digits) |
| forward-mode AD | the code of $f$ | about $n$ runs' worth | full (exact rule, only rounding) |
| reverse-mode AD | the code of $f$ | a few runs' worth, any $n$ | full (exact rule, only rounding) |

The last row is the surprise: the cost of the whole gradient does not grow with the number of inputs. That single fact is why training a network with millions of parameters is even possible.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take $f(x) = x^3$ at the point $x = 2$, where the true slope is $f'(2) = 3 \cdot 2^2 = 12$. We estimate it three ways.

Forward difference with $h = 0.1$. Compute $f(2.1) = 9.261$ and $f(2) = 8$. The chord slope is
$$
\frac{f(2.1) - f(2)}{0.1} = \frac{9.261 - 8}{0.1} = \frac{1.261}{0.1} = 12.61.
$$
The error is $12.61 - 12 = 0.61$ — too high, because the chord leans up over the interval.

Central difference with $h = 0.1$. Compute $f(2.1) = 9.261$ and $f(1.9) = 6.859$. The symmetric chord slope is
$$
\frac{f(2.1) - f(1.9)}{2 \cdot 0.1} = \frac{9.261 - 6.859}{0.2} = \frac{2.402}{0.2} = 12.01.
$$
The error is only $0.01$ — the two-sided chord cancels most of the lean.

Automatic differentiation. The code for $f$ is "multiply $x$ by $x$ to get $x^2$, then multiply by $x$ again." Differentiating step by step with the product rule and feeding the slope through gives $3x^2 = 12$ at $x = 2$, exactly, with no $h$ at all.

What this tells us: halving the lean by going symmetric turned an error of $0.61$ into $0.01$, yet both finite-difference numbers are still approximations tied to the choice of $h$. The automatic-differentiation answer is the exact $12$, because it differentiates the recipe rather than sampling the output. The next sections make the step-size sweet spot precise and explain how the backward sweep gets every slope for nearly free.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be smooth, evaluated in floating-point arithmetic with unit roundoff $u$ (the floating-point model of [43.01.01]). Write $e_i$ for the $i$-th coordinate vector and $h > 0$ for a step.

**Finite differences.** The *forward-difference* approximation to a partial derivative is
$$
D_h^{+} f(x)_i = \frac{f(x + h e_i) - f(x)}{h},
$$
and the *central-difference* approximation is
$$
D_h^{0} f(x)_i = \frac{f(x + h e_i) - f(x - h e_i)}{2h}.
$$
By Taylor's theorem [02.05.05], $D_h^{+}f(x)_i = \partial_i f(x) + \tfrac12 h\,\partial_i^2 f(\xi) = \partial_i f(x) + O(h)$, while the odd-order terms cancel in the symmetric formula, giving $D_h^{0}f(x)_i = \partial_i f(x) + \tfrac16 h^2\,\partial_i^3 f(\xi) = \partial_i f(x) + O(h^2)$. A *Hessian-vector product* is obtained by differencing the gradient,
$$
\nabla^2 f(x)\, v \approx \frac{\nabla f(x + h v) - \nabla f(x)}{h},
$$
so a Newton-Krylov solver can apply $\nabla^2 f$ to a vector using only gradient evaluations, never forming the Hessian. A full forward-difference gradient costs $n+1$ evaluations of $f$; a central-difference gradient costs $2n$. The symbols $u$ (unit roundoff), $\partial_i$ (partial derivative), $\nabla$ (gradient), $\nabla^2$ (Hessian), $J$ (Jacobian), $\dot v$ (forward tangent), and $\bar v$ (reverse adjoint) are recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

**The evaluation procedure.** Automatic differentiation acts not on a formula but on an *evaluation procedure* (a Wengert list): the function is a finite straight-line program of intermediate variables $v_1, \dots, v_L$, each $v_k = \varphi_k(v_j : j \prec k)$ an elementary operation ($+, \times, \sin, \exp, \dots$) of earlier variables, with the inputs $v_1, \dots, v_n = x_1, \dots, x_n$ and the output $y = v_L$. The partial order $\prec$ is the *computational graph*: a directed acyclic graph with the inputs as sources and $y$ as the sink.

**Forward (tangent) mode.** Fix a direction $\dot x \in \mathbb{R}^n$. Carry with each $v_k$ a *tangent* $\dot v_k$ and propagate it forward by the chain rule alongside the primal values:
$$
\dot v_k = \sum_{j \prec k} \frac{\partial \varphi_k}{\partial v_j}\,\dot v_j .
$$
Equivalently, replace each value $v$ by the *dual number* $v + \dot v\,\varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon^2 = 0$; running the unchanged program on duals computes $y + (J\dot x)\,\varepsilon$, so one forward sweep yields the Jacobian-vector product $J\dot x$ (a single directional derivative). For a scalar $f$ this is one component of the gradient per sweep, hence $n$ sweeps for the full gradient.

**Reverse (adjoint) mode.** Run the primal forward once, recording the graph (the *tape*). Then propagate *adjoints* $\bar v_k = \partial y / \partial v_k$ backward from $\bar y = 1$:
$$
\bar v_j = \sum_{k : j \prec k} \bar v_k\,\frac{\partial \varphi_k}{\partial v_j},
$$
accumulating into each node from its successors. After the backward sweep, $\bar v_1, \dots, \bar v_n$ are exactly the components of $\nabla f(x)$. One forward run plus one backward run yields the *entire* gradient — the vector-Jacobian product $\bar y^\top J$ — independent of $n$. **Backpropagation** is precisely reverse-mode AD applied to a scalar loss.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"AD is just finite differences done carefully."* No nudge is taken and no $h$ is chosen; AD applies exact differentiation rules to each elementary operation, so its only error is ordinary floating-point rounding, not truncation. The step-size dilemma below does not apply to AD at all.

- *"AD is symbolic differentiation."* Symbolic differentiation manipulates expression trees and suffers *expression swell*: the derivative of a product of $m$ factors expands to $m$ terms, and nested compositions blow up combinatorially. AD instead evaluates derivatives numerically at the given point while reusing every shared subexpression through the graph, keeping the work proportional to the original program.

- *"Forward and reverse mode cost the same."* For $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$, forward mode costs $O(n)$ sweeps (one per input direction) and reverse mode costs $O(m)$ sweeps (one per output). For a scalar loss ($m = 1$ and $n$ large) reverse mode wins decisively; for $m \gg n$ (a tall Jacobian) forward mode is cheaper.

- *"Reverse mode is free."* It is cheap in *time* but pays in *memory*: the tape must store the intermediate values needed by the backward sweep, a cost proportional to the number of operations. Checkpointing trades some of this memory back for recomputation.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is the error decomposition that fixes the optimal finite-difference step, because it is the single statement that quantifies the truncation-versus-roundoff dilemma and pins the best attainable accuracy to the machine precision $u$ of [43.01.01].

**Theorem (optimal finite-difference step and accuracy floor).** *Let $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be $C^2$ near $x$ with $|f''| \le M_2$ on the relevant interval, and suppose each evaluation of $f$ is computed with a relative error at most $u$, so the stored value satisfies $|\mathrm{fl}(f(x)) - f(x)| \le u\,|f(x)| =: \delta$. Then the computed forward difference $\widetilde D_h^{+}f(x)$ satisfies*
$$
\big|\widetilde D_h^{+} f(x) - f'(x)\big| \;\le\; \underbrace{\tfrac12 M_2\, h}_{\text{truncation}} \;+\; \underbrace{\frac{2\delta}{h}}_{\text{roundoff}} ,
$$
*and the right-hand side is minimised at*
$$
h^\star = 2\sqrt{\delta / M_2} \;\sim\; \sqrt{u}, \qquad \text{with minimal bound } \; 2\sqrt{M_2\,\delta} \;\sim\; \sqrt{u}.
$$
*The central difference, with $|f'''| \le M_3$, obeys $|\widetilde D_h^{0}f(x) - f'(x)| \le \tfrac16 M_3 h^2 + \delta/h$, minimised at $h^\star \sim u^{1/3}$ with accuracy floor $\sim u^{2/3}$.*

*Proof.* Taylor's theorem [02.05.05] with Lagrange remainder gives $f(x+h) = f(x) + h f'(x) + \tfrac12 h^2 f''(\xi)$ for some $\xi$ between $x$ and $x+h$, so the exact forward difference satisfies
$$
\Big|\frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} - f'(x)\Big| = \tfrac12 h\,|f''(\xi)| \le \tfrac12 M_2 h,
$$
the truncation term. The computed numerator replaces $f(x+h), f(x)$ by stored values differing from the exact ones by at most $\delta$ each, so the computed quotient differs from the exact quotient by at most $(\delta + \delta)/h = 2\delta/h$, the roundoff term. The triangle inequality adds the two bounds. Let $g(h) = \tfrac12 M_2 h + 2\delta/h$; then $g'(h) = \tfrac12 M_2 - 2\delta/h^2$ vanishes at $h^\star = 2\sqrt{\delta/M_2}$, where $g''(h^\star) = 4\delta/(h^\star)^3 > 0$, so $h^\star$ is the minimiser. Substituting, $g(h^\star) = \tfrac12 M_2 \cdot 2\sqrt{\delta/M_2} + 2\delta/(2\sqrt{\delta/M_2}) = 2\sqrt{M_2\delta}$. Since $\delta = u|f(x)|$, both $h^\star$ and the floor scale as $\sqrt{u}$ up to the $O(1)$ factor $\sqrt{|f(x)|/M_2}$. For the central difference the truncation term is $\tfrac16 M_3 h^2$ (the odd-order Taylor terms cancel) and the roundoff term is $2\delta/(2h) = \delta/h$; minimising $\tfrac16 M_3 h^2 + \delta/h$ gives $h^\star = (3\delta/M_3)^{1/3} \sim u^{1/3}$ and floor $\sim u^{2/3}$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This decomposition is the foundational reason finite differences can never reach full machine accuracy: the subtraction of two nearby values forfeits about half the available digits for a forward difference and a third for a central difference, so the best a gradient-by-differencing can do is $\sqrt{u}$ or $u^{2/3}$, and this is exactly the cancellation phenomenon the floating-point model of [43.01.01] predicts. The $\sqrt{u}$ rule of thumb builds toward the contrast with automatic differentiation, where no subtraction of nearby quantities occurs and the only error is the ordinary rounding of [43.01.01], so AD returns derivatives to nearly full precision. The same Taylor cancellation that costs accuracy is dual to the cancellation that *buys* the higher order of the central formula: killing the $O(h)$ term is the central insight that improves the floor from $\sqrt{u}$ to $u^{2/3}$. Putting these together, the step-size analysis generalises directly to Hessian-vector products formed by differencing the gradient, where the accuracy floor degrades by another factor, and the dilemma appears again in [44.03.04] wherever a quasi-Newton or Newton-Krylov method must choose between differenced and automatically differentiated curvature information; the bridge is that AD removes the dilemma entirely by differentiating the program rather than sampling its output.

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that running an evaluation procedure on dual numbers $v + \dot v\,\varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon^2 = 0$ computes the directional derivative. Verify the product rule: if $a = u v$ with duals $u + \dot u\varepsilon$, $v + \dot v\varepsilon$, then the $\varepsilon$-coefficient of the product is $\dot u v + u\dot v$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Multiply $(u + \dot u\varepsilon)(v + \dot v\varepsilon)$ and discard the $\varepsilon^2$ term.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Expanding, $(u + \dot u\varepsilon)(v + \dot v\varepsilon) = uv + (\dot u v + u\dot v)\varepsilon + \dot u\dot v\,\varepsilon^2$. Since $\varepsilon^2 = 0$, this is $uv + (\dot u v + u\dot v)\varepsilon$, whose real part is the primal product and whose $\varepsilon$-coefficient is exactly the product-rule derivative $\dot u v + u\dot v$. By the same mechanism each elementary operation $\varphi$ maps $v + \dot v\varepsilon \mapsto \varphi(v) + \varphi'(v)\dot v\,\varepsilon$, so composing the whole program sends $x + \dot x\varepsilon$ to $f(x) + (J\dot x)\varepsilon$: the $\varepsilon$-coefficient is the Jacobian-vector product, the forward-mode directional derivative. Full credit for the expansion and the closure of the rule under composition.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

For $f(x_1, x_2) = x_1 x_2 + \sin x_1$, trace reverse mode. Forward: $v_1 = x_1$, $v_2 = x_2$, $v_3 = v_1 v_2$, $v_4 = \sin v_1$, $y = v_5 = v_3 + v_4$. Starting from $\bar y = 1$, compute the adjoints and confirm $\bar v_1 = \partial f/\partial x_1$, $\bar v_2 = \partial f/\partial x_2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\bar v_3 = \bar v_4 = \bar v_5 = 1$ from the sum. Then $v_1$ feeds both $v_3$ and $v_4$, so accumulate $\bar v_1 = \bar v_3 \cdot v_2 + \bar v_4 \cdot \cos v_1$.

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ be evaluated by a procedure of $\mathrm{OPS}$ elementary operations. Give the per-evaluation cost of computing the full Jacobian $J$ by (a) forward mode, (b) reverse mode, (c) forward differences, in terms of $n$, $m$, and $\mathrm{OPS}$, and state the regime in which each is preferred.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Forward mode gets one Jacobian *column* (one $J\dot x$) per sweep; reverse mode gets one Jacobian *row* (one $\bar y^\top J$) per sweep; a forward difference gets one column per extra function evaluation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) Forward mode propagates one tangent per input direction, so the full $J$ (its $n$ columns) costs $O(n \cdot \mathrm{OPS})$, with a small constant ($\le 2.5$) per sweep. (b) Reverse mode propagates one adjoint per output, so $J$ (its $m$ rows) costs $O(m \cdot \mathrm{OPS})$, with constant $\le 4$ per sweep but additional $O(\mathrm{OPS})$ memory for the tape. (c) Forward differences need one extra evaluation of $f$ per column, so $O(n\cdot\mathrm{OPS})$ in time like forward mode, but with truncation/roundoff error capped at $\sqrt{u}$ instead of full precision. Preference: reverse mode when $m \ll n$ (a scalar loss, $m=1$, is the extreme case — the cheap-gradient regime); forward mode when $n \ll m$ (a tall Jacobian); finite differences only when the source code is unavailable. Full credit for the three cost orders and the $m$ versus $n$ crossover.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the Hessian-vector product accuracy when $\nabla^2 f(x) v$ is formed by differencing an *automatically differentiated* gradient: $\nabla^2 f(x) v \approx (\nabla f(x + hv) - \nabla f(x))/h$, with $\nabla f$ computed to relative error $u$ by reverse mode. Find the optimal $h$ and the floor, and contrast with applying forward-over-reverse AD instead.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Treat $\nabla f$ as a vector-valued function known to accuracy $\delta \sim u\|\nabla f\|$; reuse the forward-difference balance $\tfrac12 M h + 2\delta/h$ componentwise.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Componentwise the differenced gradient has truncation error $\tfrac12 h\,\|\nabla^3 f\|\,\|v\|^2$ and roundoff error $\sim 2\delta/h$ with $\delta \sim u\|\nabla f\|$, so by the Key theorem's balance the optimum is $h^\star \sim \sqrt{u}$ and the accuracy floor is $\sim\sqrt{u}$ relative — only half the digits, even though $\nabla f$ itself was nearly exact, because the outer difference still subtracts nearby vectors. Applying forward-over-reverse AD instead — pushing a dual through the reverse sweep, i.e. evaluating the directional derivative of $\nabla f$ in direction $v$ — computes $\nabla^2 f(x) v$ to full precision with no step size, at cost a small constant times one gradient evaluation. ## Advanced results [Master]

The finite-difference floor is set by cancellation and the optimal step by a one-dimensional minimisation; the results below place the exact-derivative alternative on its complexity-theoretic footing — the cheap-gradient principle, the duality of the two AD modes, the memory cost of reversal and its checkpointing remedy, and the position of AD between the symbolic and numerical extremes.

**Theorem 1 (the cheap-gradient principle).** Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be computed by an evaluation procedure whose runtime, measured in a fixed cost model counting additions, multiplications, and library-call evaluations, is $\mathrm{TIME}(f)$. Reverse-mode AD computes the complete gradient $\nabla f(x)$ in time
$$
\mathrm{TIME}(\nabla f) \le \omega_{\mathrm{grad}}\cdot \mathrm{TIME}(f), \qquad \omega_{\mathrm{grad}} \in [3, 4],
$$
*independent of $n$*. The bound holds because each elementary operation $v_k = \varphi_k(v_j)$ contributes to the backward sweep only its local partials $\partial\varphi_k/\partial v_j$ times the incoming adjoint, a constant multiple of the work already done in the forward sweep; summing over all $L$ operations gives a constant overhead factor. The contrast with the $n+1$ evaluations of a forward-difference gradient is the entire reason gradient-based training of models with $n \sim 10^9$ parameters is feasible [ref: TODO_REF Griewank, A. & Walther, A. — Evaluating Derivatives].

**Theorem 2 (forward-reverse duality and the Jacobian).** For $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ with Jacobian $J \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$, forward mode evaluates the matrix-free product $v \mapsto Jv$ (a tangent) and reverse mode evaluates $w \mapsto J^\top w$ (an adjoint/cotangent), each at cost a small constant times $\mathrm{TIME}(f)$. The full Jacobian costs $\min(n, m)$ such sweeps up to the optimal-Jacobian-accumulation problem — finding the cheapest order in which to multiply the elementary local Jacobians along the graph — which is NP-hard in general, so practical AD uses the forward or reverse extreme or hand-tuned cross-country schedules. Forward and reverse are formal transposes: reverse mode on $f$ is forward mode on the adjoint program, the algorithmic shadow of $(J^\top)^\top = J$ [ref: TODO_REF Baydin, A. G., Pearlmutter, B. A., Radul, A. A. & Siskind, J. M. — Automatic Differentiation in Machine Learning: a Survey].

**Theorem 3 (memory cost of reversal and logarithmic checkpointing).** Reverse mode must make the intermediate values of the forward sweep available to the backward sweep. The naive tape stores all $L$ intermediates, $\mathrm{SPACE} = O(L)$. For an evaluation that decomposes into a chain of $L$ time steps (a deep network, a time-stepping PDE solver), the *Revolve* checkpointing schedule stores only $s$ checkpoints and recomputes intermediate segments on demand, achieving the optimal tradeoff: with $s$ checkpoints the chain of length $L$ is reversed using at most $t$ extra forward recomputations where $\binom{s+t}{s} \ge L$. Choosing $s = t = O(\log L)$ gives memory $O(\log L)$ at the price of an $O(\log L)$ factor in time — the binomial reversal schedule, optimal among all schedules [ref: TODO_REF Griewank, A. & Walther, A. — Evaluating Derivatives].

**Theorem 4 (AD versus symbolic and numerical differentiation).** Symbolic differentiation returns a closed-form expression but suffers expression swell: the symbolic derivative of a length-$L$ program can have size exponential in $L$ when common subexpressions are not shared, and evaluating it then costs far more than the cheap-gradient bound. Numerical (finite) differentiation is cheap to implement but caps accuracy at $\sqrt{u}$ (forward) or $u^{2/3}$ (central) by the Key theorem and costs $n+1$ evaluations for a gradient. Automatic differentiation occupies the remaining corner: it evaluates derivatives to full machine precision (no truncation), reuses every shared subexpression (no swell), and computes a scalar gradient at $O(1)$ function evaluations (no $n$-fold blowup). The three methods are distinguished not by the chain rule — all three obey it — but by *what they carry*: symbolic carries expressions, numerical carries perturbed values, AD carries derivative values attached to the live computation [ref: TODO_REF Baydin, A. G., Pearlmutter, B. A., Radul, A. A. & Siskind, J. M. — Automatic Differentiation in Machine Learning: a Survey].

**Synthesis.** Derivative computation splits into two regimes, and the foundational reason they differ is whether one *samples* the function or *differentiates its program*. Finite differences sample: the Key theorem shows this forfeits half the digits to the cancellation of [43.01.01], the $\sqrt{u}$ floor an irreducible tax on subtracting nearby values, and the cost scales with the input dimension $n$. Automatic differentiation differentiates the program: the cheap-gradient principle is exactly the statement that the reverse sweep reuses the forward computation operation for operation, so a scalar gradient costs a fixed multiple of the function regardless of $n$ — this is the central insight that makes large-scale optimization and the training of billion-parameter models possible. The two AD modes are dual, forward computing $Jv$ and reverse computing $J^\top w$, and the choice between them is governed by the shape of the Jacobian just as the choice of finite-difference order is governed by the balance of Taylor terms; backpropagation is simply reverse mode at a scalar loss.

The memory cost of reversal generalises the time-space tradeoff that pervades scientific computing, and logarithmic checkpointing is its optimal resolution, putting these together so that even an arbitrarily deep computation can be differentiated in bounded memory. The bridge from this unit to the rest of optimization is that every Newton, quasi-Newton, and stochastic-gradient method downstream consumes the gradients and Hessian-vector products produced here, and their efficiency is inherited from the cheap-gradient bound — AD is dual to the optimizer in the sense that the optimizer chooses the search direction and AD supplies, almost for free, the curvature information that direction needs.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (forward-difference error decomposition and optimal step).** *Under the Key theorem's hypotheses, the computed forward difference obeys $|\widetilde D_h^{+}f(x) - f'(x)| \le \tfrac12 M_2 h + 2\delta/h$ with $\delta = u|f(x)|$, minimised at $h^\star = 2\sqrt{\delta/M_2} \sim \sqrt u$ with floor $2\sqrt{M_2\delta}\sim\sqrt u$.*

*Proof.* This is the Key theorem. Taylor with Lagrange remainder [02.05.05] bounds the exact-quotient truncation by $\tfrac12 M_2 h$; the floating-point model of [43.01.01] bounds each stored value's error by $\delta$, contributing $2\delta/h$ after division; the triangle inequality sums them. Calculus on $g(h) = \tfrac12 M_2 h + 2\delta/h$ gives the unique positive critical point $h^\star = 2\sqrt{\delta/M_2}$ with $g''>0$, and $g(h^\star) = 2\sqrt{M_2\delta}$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (central-difference order and floor).** *For $f \in C^3$ with $|f'''|\le M_3$, $|\widetilde D_h^0 f(x) - f'(x)| \le \tfrac16 M_3 h^2 + \delta/h$, minimised at $h^\star = (3\delta/M_3)^{1/3}\sim u^{1/3}$ with floor $\sim u^{2/3}$.*

*Proof.* Taylor to third order at $x\pm h$ gives $f(x\pm h) = f(x) \pm hf'(x) + \tfrac12 h^2 f''(x) \pm \tfrac16 h^3 f'''(\xi_\pm)$; subtracting cancels the even-order terms, so $\tfrac{f(x+h)-f(x-h)}{2h} - f'(x) = \tfrac{1}{12}h^2(f'''(\xi_+)+f'''(\xi_-))$, bounded by $\tfrac16 M_3 h^2$. The two stored values each carry error $\delta$, contributing $2\delta/(2h)=\delta/h$. Minimising $\tfrac16 M_3 h^2 + \delta/h$: the derivative $\tfrac13 M_3 h - \delta/h^2 = 0$ gives $h^3 = 3\delta/M_3$, so $h^\star = (3\delta/M_3)^{1/3}\sim u^{1/3}$, and back-substitution gives a floor of order $\delta^{2/3}\sim u^{2/3}$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (forward mode computes $J\dot x$ via dual numbers).** *Running an evaluation procedure for $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^m$ on dual numbers over the ring $\mathbb{R}[\varepsilon]/(\varepsilon^2)$ with input $x + \dot x\,\varepsilon$ returns $f(x) + J(x)\dot x\,\varepsilon$.*

*Proof.* The dual numbers $\mathbb{R}[\varepsilon]/(\varepsilon^2)$ form a commutative ring in which $\varepsilon^2=0$. Each elementary smooth operation $\varphi$ extends to duals by its first-order Taylor jet, $\varphi(v + \dot v\varepsilon) = \varphi(v) + \varphi'(v)\dot v\,\varepsilon$, since higher terms carry $\varepsilon^2=0$; for a binary $\varphi(u,v)$ this gives $\varphi(u,v) + (\partial_u\varphi\,\dot u + \partial_v\varphi\,\dot v)\varepsilon$, the chain rule on the $\varepsilon$-coefficient. By induction on the length of the Wengert list, after processing $v_k$ its dual carries $(v_k, \dot v_k)$ with $\dot v_k = \sum_{j\prec k}(\partial\varphi_k/\partial v_j)\dot v_j$, which is the $k$-th component of the propagated directional derivative. At the outputs the $\varepsilon$-coefficients assemble into $J(x)\dot x$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (reverse mode computes $\nabla f$ in $O(1)$ function evaluations).** *For $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$ given by a procedure of $L$ elementary operations each of bounded arity, one forward sweep followed by one reverse adjoint sweep yields the exact $\nabla f(x)$ in total work $\le \omega_{\mathrm{grad}}\,\mathrm{TIME}(f)$ with $\omega_{\mathrm{grad}}$ an absolute constant.*

*Proof.* Initialise $\bar y = 1$ and $\bar v_k = 0$ for $k<L$. Process the operations in reverse order; when handling $v_k = \varphi_k(v_j:j\prec k)$, add $\bar v_k\,(\partial\varphi_k/\partial v_j)$ to each $\bar v_j$. By reverse induction this maintains the invariant $\bar v_k = \partial y/\partial v_k$ (total derivative through all paths from $v_k$ to $y$), because every path from $v_k$ to the sink passes through exactly one of $v_k$'s immediate successors and the partials multiply along edges and add over paths — the multivariate chain rule. At termination $\bar v_1,\dots,\bar v_n = \partial y/\partial x_1,\dots,\partial y/\partial x_n = \nabla f(x)$. Each operation is touched once in the forward sweep and once in the reverse sweep, and its reverse contribution is a fixed number of multiply-adds determined by its bounded arity, so the reverse work is at most a constant multiple of the forward work; with the forward sweep itself this gives total work $\le \omega_{\mathrm{grad}}\,\mathrm{TIME}(f)$, the constant absorbing the per-operation local-partial cost and independent of $n$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (binomial checkpointing bound).** *A computation structured as a chain of $L$ steps can be reversed using $s$ stored checkpoints and at most $t$ extra forward recomputations whenever $\binom{s+t}{s}\ge L$; choosing $s=t=O(\log L)$ reverses the chain in memory $O(\log L)$ and time $O(L\log L)$.*

*Proof.* Let $c(s,t)$ be the largest chain length reversible with $s$ checkpoints and $t$ recomputation passes. Place the first checkpoint after $c(s,t-1)$ steps: the suffix is reversed with $s$ checkpoints and $t-1$ passes, the prefix with the remaining $s-1$ checkpoints and $t$ passes once its end state is recomputed, giving the recurrence $c(s,t)=c(s-1,t)+c(s,t-1)$ with $c(s,0)=c(0,t)=1$, whose solution is the binomial coefficient $c(s,t)=\binom{s+t}{s}$. Hence any $L\le\binom{s+t}{s}$ is reversible within the stated resources. Taking $s=t=k$ gives $\binom{2k}{k}\sim 4^k/\sqrt{\pi k}\ge L$ for $k=O(\log L)$, so memory $s=O(\log L)$ and total recomputation work $O(L\log L)$. The schedule is optimal: no reversal of a length-$L$ chain with $s$ checkpoints and $t$ passes can exceed $\binom{s+t}{s}$ steps, by the same recurrence read as an upper bound. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Multivariable Taylor expansion and extrema [02.05.05] supplies the remainder estimates that make the finite-difference analysis quantitative: the $O(h)$ forward and $O(h^2)$ central truncation terms are the Lagrange remainders of the first- and second-order expansions, and the same second-order Taylor data — gradient and Hessian — is exactly what the optimizers downstream need and what AD delivers exactly. The vanishing-gradient condition for an extremum from that unit is the target every gradient-based method of this chapter drives toward, and the curvature (Hessian) it classifies critical points with is the object Hessian-vector products approximate or, through forward-over-reverse AD, compute exactly.

- Floating-point arithmetic and the IEEE model [43.01.01] is the source of the accuracy floor: the unit roundoff $u$ that bounds each evaluation's relative error is precisely what forces the $2\delta/h$ roundoff branch and caps differenced derivatives at $\sqrt u$. The catastrophic cancellation that the IEEE model predicts when subtracting nearby quantities is the exact mechanism that limits finite differences and that automatic differentiation sidesteps by never forming such a difference, so this unit is the applied payoff of that unit's error model.

- The conjugate gradient method [43.07.04] and Newton-Krylov optimization consume Hessian-vector products of the kind built here: a truncated-Newton method solves the Newton system $\nabla^2 f(x)\,p = -\nabla f(x)$ by CG, and CG needs only the action $v\mapsto \nabla^2 f(x) v$, supplied either by differencing the gradient (with the $\sqrt u$ floor of the Key theorem) or, to full precision, by forward-over-reverse AD. The cheap-gradient principle is what makes the per-CG-iteration cost a small multiple of one function evaluation, so the matrix-free curvature access of that unit and the matrix-free derivative access of this one compose into large-scale second-order optimization.

- The artificial-intelligence and machine-learning survey [25.09.01] is the large-scale consumer of reverse-mode AD: backpropagation is reverse-mode AD applied to a scalar training loss, and the cheap-gradient bound proved here is the reason a network with billions of parameters can be trained at all. This unit is the theorem-level cost-and-accuracy treatment that the survey references at a higher level; the survey covers the modelling and architectural context while this unit owns the derivative-computation cost theory, so the two are complementary rather than overlapping.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The finite-difference quotient is as old as the calculus, but its numerical-analysis treatment — the explicit balance of truncation against roundoff and the resulting optimal step near $\sqrt u$ — belongs to the mid-twentieth-century error-analysis tradition associated with **James H. Wilkinson**, whose backward-error framework of the 1960s made the $u/h$ cancellation term a quantitative object. The forward mode of automatic differentiation was given its modern form by **Robert E. Wengert** in 1964, whose "simple automatic derivative evaluation program" introduced the list of intermediate operations now called the Wengert list [ref: TODO_REF Wengert 1964].

The reverse mode has a more tangled lineage. **Seppo Linnainmaa**'s 1970 master's thesis and 1976 paper described the reverse accumulation of rounding errors over a computational graph — the algorithm now called backpropagation — predating its 1986 popularization in neural networks by **Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams** [ref: TODO_REF Linnainmaa 1976]. **Bert Speelpenning**'s 1980 dissertation proved that the reverse mode computes a gradient at a cost independent of the number of inputs, the cheap-gradient principle. The systematic theory — complexity bounds, optimal Jacobian accumulation, and the binomial checkpointing schedule — was consolidated by **Andreas Griewank** and **Andrea Walther**, the latter's *Revolve* algorithm of 1996 establishing the optimal logarithmic time-memory tradeoff for reversal [ref: TODO_REF Griewank, A. & Walther, A. — Evaluating Derivatives]. The 2018 survey of **Baydin, Pearlmutter, Radul, and Siskind** documented how reverse-mode AD, rediscovered repeatedly across numerical analysis and machine learning, became the computational substrate of deep learning [ref: TODO_REF Baydin, A. G., Pearlmutter, B. A., Radul, A. A. & Siskind, J. M. — Automatic Differentiation in Machine Learning: a Survey].
