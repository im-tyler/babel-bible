## Intuition [Beginner]

Some decision problems do not run forever and do not fade out by discounting; instead they aim at a destination. A robot wants to reach a charging dock, a board game wants to reach a finishing square, a project wants to reach completion. Once you arrive, the problem is over and nothing more is charged. Every step before arrival has a cost, and the chance of where you land next depends on what you do. The question is how to act so that the total cost of getting to the destination is as small as possible.

Because there is a real ending — the destination — you do not need to shrink future costs to keep the total finite. The total stays finite for another reason: a good plan actually reaches the destination, and once there, the bill stops. This is the **stochastic shortest path** idea. The destination is a special situation that costs nothing and that you never leave. A plan that is sure to reach it eventually pays a finite total; a plan that might wander away from the destination forever can pile up cost without end.

This splits all plans into two kinds. A **proper** plan is one that, no matter where you start, will reach the destination with full certainty given enough steps. An **improper** plan can get stuck circling among the other situations, never arriving. Proper plans are the well-behaved ones, and the whole method works when at least one proper plan exists and every improper plan is genuinely bad — costing an unbounded amount from somewhere.

The reward for setting things up this way is that the same repeat-until-settled method from earlier still finds the answer. The value of each situation is the cheapest expected total cost to reach the destination from there, and it satisfies one self-referential equation: value here equals best immediate cost plus the average value of where you land next.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a small map of situations with one marked as the destination. From each non-destination situation, each action is a fan of arrows to possible next situations, each arrow carrying a chance and the situation carrying a one-step cost. Arrows can point toward the destination or away from it. A plan is **proper** when, following its chosen arrows, every situation has a path of nonzero chance that eventually lands on the destination, so you are sure to arrive in the long run.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A delivery bot is at situation $A$ or $B$, and wants to reach the depot $D$, which costs nothing and is the end. From $A$ the only action is **drive**: cost $2$, and it reaches $D$ with chance $0.5$ or slips to $B$ with chance $0.5$. From $B$ the only action is **drive**: cost $3$, and it reaches $D$ with chance $1$. There is no discounting; we want the cheapest expected total cost to reach $D$. This plan is proper: from either start the bot is sure to arrive.

Write $J(A)$ and $J(B)$ for the expected total cost to reach $D$. At the depot $D$ the value is $0$. Let us find the values by repeated updates, starting both at $0$.

Sweep 1. From $A$: cost $2$ plus the average of next values, $0.5\,J(D)+0.5\,J(B)=0.5(0)+0.5(0)=0$, total $2$. From $B$: cost $3$ plus $J(D)=0$, total $3$. New table: $J(A)=2$, $J(B)=3$.

Sweep 2. From $A$: $2 + (0.5\cdot 0 + 0.5\cdot 3) = 2 + 1.5 = 3.5$. From $B$: $3 + 0 = 3$. New table: $J(A)=3.5$, $J(B)=3$.

Sweep 3. From $A$: $2 + (0.5\cdot 0 + 0.5\cdot 3) = 3.5$. From $B$: $3$. The table did not change, so we have the answer: $J(A)=3.5$, $J(B)=3$.

What this tells us: with no discounting, the values still settle to finite numbers because the plan is sure to reach the depot, where the bill stops. Starting at $B$ costs $3$; starting at $A$ costs $3.5$, the $2$ to drive plus the half-chance of a $3$ detour through $B$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **stochastic shortest path** (SSP) problem is a finite-state controlled Markov chain with states $\{1,\dots,n\}$ together with a **cost-free absorbing terminal state** $t$. For each non-terminal state $i$ there is a nonempty admissible-control set $U(i)$, transition probabilities $p_{ij}(u)$ (including $p_{it}(u)$ to the terminal state) summing to one over $j\in\{1,\dots,n,t\}$, and a bounded one-stage expected cost $g(i,u)$; the terminal state satisfies $p_{tt}=1$ and $g(t,\cdot)=0$. There is **no discounting**: $\alpha=1$. This is the undiscounted, terminating counterpart of the discounted MDP [44.08.03]. A **stationary policy** is a map $\mu$ with $\mu(i)\in U(i)$, and its **total expected cost** from $i$ is
$$
J_\mu(i)=\lim_{N\to\infty}\mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} g\big(x_k,\mu(x_k)\big)\,\Big|\,x_0=i\right],\qquad x_{k+1}\sim p_{x_k,\cdot}(\mu(x_k)),
$$
with $J_\mu(t)=0$; the limit may be $+\infty$.

Let $P_\mu=[p_{ij}(\mu(i))]_{i,j=1}^{n}$ be the **substochastic** matrix of transitions among the non-terminal states under $\mu$ (mass $1-\sum_j p_{ij}(\mu(i))$ leaks to $t$), and $g_\mu=(g(i,\mu(i)))_i$. A stationary policy $\mu$ is **proper** if, starting from every state, the terminal state is reached with probability one under $\mu$; equivalently there exist $m$ and $\rho<1$ with $\mathbb{P}(x_m\ne t\mid x_0=i,\mu)\le\rho$ for all $i$, equivalently the spectral radius of $P_\mu$ is strictly below one, so $I-P_\mu$ is invertible and $\sum_{k\ge0}P_\mu^k=(I-P_\mu)^{-1}$ converges. A policy that is not proper is **improper**. For proper $\mu$, $J_\mu=(I-P_\mu)^{-1}g_\mu$ is finite.

On the space $B(S)=\mathbb{R}^n$ of functions on the non-terminal states (with $J(t)=0$ fixed), define the **policy operator** $T_\mu$ and the **Bellman operator** $T$ by
$$
(T_\mu J)(i)=g(i,\mu(i))+\sum_{j=1}^{n}p_{ij}(\mu(i))\,J(j),\qquad
(TJ)(i)=\min_{u\in U(i)}\Big\{g(i,u)+\sum_{j=1}^{n}p_{ij}(u)\,J(j)\Big\},
$$
the terminal term $p_{it}(u)\,J(t)=0$ omitted. The **optimality (Bellman) equation** is $J^*=TJ^*$ and policy evaluation is $J_\mu=T_\mu J_\mu$. A policy $\mu$ is **greedy** for $J$ if $T_\mu J=TJ$. The two standing **SSP assumptions** are: (3.1) at least one proper policy exists; (3.2) every improper policy has $J_\mu(i)=+\infty$ for some $i$. The **weighted sup-norm** with weights $v=(v_1,\dots,v_n)>0$ is $\|J\|_v=\max_i |J(i)|/v_i$. The symbols $T$, $T_\mu$, $J^*$, $J_\mu$, $P_\mu$ (substochastic policy matrix), $\|\cdot\|_v$ (weighted sup-norm), and the terminal state $t$ are recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"With no discount factor the Bellman operator cannot be a contraction in any sup-norm."* In the plain sup-norm it generally is not — a proper policy's substochastic $P_\mu$ can have row sums equal to one for states far from the terminal. But the SSP assumptions deliver a positive weight vector $v$ and a modulus $\beta<1$ making $T$ a contraction in the **weighted** sup-norm $\|\cdot\|_v$. The weighting is the whole point; without it the contraction fails.

- *"Bellman's equation might have several solutions once $\alpha=1$."* Under the two assumptions $T$ has a unique fixed point among functions on the non-terminal states (with $J(t)=0$), and it is $J^*$. The terminal-state normalization $J(t)=0$ removes the additive-constant ambiguity that plagues the undiscounted average-cost problem; here the absorbing state pins the level.

- *"Improper policies are merely suboptimal, so they can be ignored."* Assumption (3.2) is what rules them out, and it is a genuine hypothesis: if some improper policy had finite cost everywhere, $J^*$ could fail to satisfy the contraction-based uniqueness, value iteration could converge to the wrong limit, and policy iteration could stall at an improper policy. The assumption forces every improper policy to be visibly infinite-cost somewhere.

- *"A greedy policy for the optimal cost could be improper."* Under the assumptions, any policy greedy for $J^*$ is proper and optimal. Were a greedy policy improper, assumption (3.2) would give it infinite cost from some state, contradicting $J_\mu=J^*<\infty$ obtained from greediness; properness of optimal policies is a consequence, not a separate hypothesis.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is that, under the two SSP assumptions, the undiscounted Bellman operator is a contraction in a suitable weighted sup-norm, recovering for the total-cost problem the entire fixed-point apparatus — existence, uniqueness, value-iteration convergence, greedy optimality — that the discount factor supplied automatically in the discounted case [44.08.02]. The discount modulus $\alpha$ is replaced by a constructed modulus $\beta<1$ and a constructed weight vector $v>0$.

**Theorem (SSP weighted-sup-norm contraction and the total-cost optimality equation).** *Let an SSP satisfy assumptions (3.1) and (3.2). Then there exist a vector $v=(v_1,\dots,v_n)$ with $v_i>0$ and a scalar $\beta\in[0,1)$ such that for every proper $\mu$ and for $T$,*
$$
\|T_\mu J-T_\mu J'\|_v\le\beta\,\|J-J'\|_v,\qquad \|TJ-TJ'\|_v\le\beta\,\|J-J'\|_v,\qquad J,J'\in\mathbb{R}^n.
$$
*Consequently $T$ has a unique fixed point $J^*\in\mathbb{R}^n$, which equals the optimal cost; value iteration $J_{k+1}=TJ_k$ from any $J_0$ converges to $J^*$ ($\|J_k-J^*\|_v\le\beta^k\|J_0-J^*\|_v$); each proper $T_\mu$ has the unique fixed point $J_\mu=(I-P_\mu)^{-1}g_\mu$; and a stationary policy $\mu^*$ is optimal if and only if it is greedy for $J^*$ ($T_{\mu^*}J^*=TJ^*$), in which case $\mu^*$ is proper.*

*Proof.* First construct $v$. Modify the SSP by charging a cost of $1$ at every non-terminal state under every control: the modified problem has the same proper policies, and for a proper $\mu$ the modified total cost is the expected number of steps to absorption, $v^\mu=(I-P_\mu)^{-1}\mathbf{1}>0$, finite by properness. Let $\hat J$ be the optimal cost of the modified problem (minimum expected steps to reach $t$); under the assumptions $\hat J$ is finite and satisfies $\hat J(i)=1+\min_u\sum_j p_{ij}(u)\hat J(j)$, so $\hat J(i)\ge 1+\sum_j p_{ij}(u)\hat J(j)$ for every admissible $u$. Set $v_i=\hat J(i)>0$. Then for each $i$ and each control $u$,
$$
\sum_j p_{ij}(u)\,v_j\le v_i-1\le\beta\,v_i,\qquad \beta:=\max_i\frac{v_i-1}{v_i}=1-\frac1{\max_i v_i}\in[0,1).
$$
Now bound $T$. For $J,J'$ and any $i$, pick $u$ attaining $(TJ)(i)$; then
$$
(TJ)(i)-(TJ')(i)\le \sum_j p_{ij}(u)\big(J(j)-J'(j)\big)\le \sum_j p_{ij}(u)\,v_j\,\|J-J'\|_v\le\beta\,v_i\,\|J-J'\|_v,
$$
using $|J(j)-J'(j)|\le v_j\|J-J'\|_v$ and the bound on $\sum_j p_{ij}(u)v_j$. The symmetric choice gives the reverse, so $|(TJ)(i)-(TJ')(i)|\le\beta v_i\|J-J'\|_v$, i.e. $|(TJ)(i)-(TJ')(i)|/v_i\le\beta\|J-J'\|_v$; the maximum over $i$ is the contraction bound for $T$. The single-control version gives it for each proper $T_\mu$ (whose own substochastic matrix obeys the same $\sum_j p_{ij}(\mu(i))v_j\le\beta v_i$).

Because $(\mathbb{R}^n,\|\cdot\|_v)$ is complete (all norms on $\mathbb{R}^n$ are equivalent, so completeness is the metric/Banach substrate of [02.01.05]) and $\beta<1$, the Banach fixed-point theorem gives a unique fixed point $J^*=TJ^*$ with the geometric estimate $\|J_k-J^*\|_v\le\beta^k\|J_0-J^*\|_v$, and a unique $J_\mu=T_\mu J_\mu$ for each proper $\mu$; solving the linear fixed point, $J_\mu=g_\mu+P_\mu J_\mu$ gives $J_\mu=(I-P_\mu)^{-1}g_\mu$.

That $J^*$ is the optimal cost and is attained by a greedy proper policy: let $\mu^*$ be greedy for $J^*$, so $T_{\mu^*}J^*=TJ^*=J^*$. The contraction bound applied to $T_{\mu^*}$ (its substochastic matrix obeys $\sum_j p_{ij}(\mu^*(i))v_j\le\beta v_i<v_i$, forcing strictly positive leakage to $t$ from the recurrent classes, hence $\mu^*$ proper) makes $J^*$ the unique fixed point of $T_{\mu^*}$, so $J^*=J_{\mu^*}<\infty$. For any proper $\mu$, monotonicity of $T_\mu$ and $T_\mu J^*\ge TJ^*=J^*$ iterated give $J_\mu=\lim_k T_\mu^k J^*\ge J^*$. For any improper $\mu$, assumption (3.2) gives $J_\mu(i)=+\infty\ge J^*(i)$ for some $i$, and $J_\mu\ge J^*$ pointwise holds because the per-state cost is nonneg-dominated by the proper optimum where finite. Hence $\inf_\mu J_\mu\ge J^*$, while $J_{\mu^*}=J^*$ shows the infimum is attained; thus $J^*=\inf_\mu J_\mu$. Finally $\mu$ optimal ($J_\mu=J^*$) gives, applying $T_\mu$ to its fixed point, $T_\mu J^*=T_\mu J_\mu=J_\mu=J^*=TJ^*$, so $\mu$ is greedy; the converse was shown. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem is the foundational reason undiscounted total-cost control is as tractable as the discounted case: where discounting handed over a contraction modulus for free, the SSP assumptions *manufacture* one — the constructed weight vector $v$ (minimum expected steps to the terminal state) and modulus $\beta=1-1/\max_i v_i$ play the role the discount $\alpha$ played in [44.08.02], and this is exactly the same Banach fixed-point machinery [02.01.05] read in a weighted norm. The central insight is that properness is a spectral condition — the substochastic $P_\mu$ has spectral radius below one — so $I-P_\mu$ inverts and the expected-step function is finite and positive, and that single positive vector simultaneously controls $T$ and every proper $T_\mu$. The construction generalises the discounted contraction (which is the SSP weighting with the uniform weight $v\equiv 1/(1-\alpha)$) and is dual, through the substochastic resolvent $(I-P_\mu)^{-1}$, to the discounted resolvent $(I-\alpha P_\mu)^{-1}$ of the Markov-chain evaluation step [44.08.03]. Putting these together, value iteration, policy iteration over proper policies, and the asynchronous variants are three algorithms for the one fixed point $J^*=TJ^*$, and the bridge is that adding a uniform termination probability $1-\alpha$ to any discounted MDP turns it into an SSP, so the discounted theory is a special case; this builds toward real-time and approximate dynamic programming, and appears again in the neuro-dynamic-programming treatment of reinforcement learning, where SSP is the default episodic model.

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).**

Two non-terminal states $\{1,2\}$, one forced action each. State $1$: cost $1$, goes to state $2$ with probability $1$. State $2$: cost $1$, goes to the terminal state with probability $0.5$ and back to state $1$ with probability $0.5$. No discounting. Write $J(1)=1+J(2)$ and $J(2)=1+0.5\,J(1)$ and solve for $J(1),J(2)$.

Substitute the first equation into the second to get one equation in $J(1)$.

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

Show that the discounted problem with a single state, self-loop cost $g$, and discount $\alpha\in(0,1)$ is the SSP with the same state, cost $g$, self-loop probability $\alpha$, and termination probability $1-\alpha$. Verify both give the same total/value cost for $g=3$, $\alpha=0.9$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The discounted value solves $J=g+\alpha J$; the SSP total cost solves $J=g+\alpha J$ as well, with the $\alpha$ now a survival probability.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Discounted: $J=g+\alpha J\Rightarrow J=g/(1-\alpha)=3/0.1=30$. SSP: the self-loop probability is $\alpha=0.9$ and termination $1-\alpha=0.1$, so $J=g+0.9J$, giving $J=3/0.1=30$. The two equations are identical: discounting by $\alpha$ each step is the same as surviving (not terminating) with probability $\alpha$ each step, which is why a discounted MDP is an SSP with an added uniform termination transition $1-\alpha$. Full credit for $J=30$ in both and the identification of $\alpha$ as survival probability.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $\mu$ be a proper stationary policy with substochastic matrix $P_\mu$ (spectral radius $<1$) and cost vector $g_\mu$. Show that the policy-evaluation equation $J_\mu=g_\mu+P_\mu J_\mu$ has the unique finite solution $J_\mu=(I-P_\mu)^{-1}g_\mu=\sum_{k\ge0}P_\mu^k g_\mu$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Spectral radius below one makes $I-P_\mu$ invertible and the Neumann series $\sum_k P_\mu^k$ converge to its inverse.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Because the spectral radius $\rho(P_\mu)<1$, the eigenvalues of $I-P_\mu$ are $1-\lambda$ with $|\lambda|<1$, none zero, so $I-P_\mu$ is invertible; equivalently the Neumann series $\sum_{k\ge0}P_\mu^k$ converges (its partial sums are Cauchy in any matrix norm with $\|P_\mu^k\|\to0$) and equals $(I-P_\mu)^{-1}$, since $(I-P_\mu)\sum_{k=0}^{m}P_\mu^k=I-P_\mu^{m+1}\to I$. Rearranging $J_\mu=g_\mu+P_\mu J_\mu$ gives $(I-P_\mu)J_\mu=g_\mu$, so $J_\mu=(I-P_\mu)^{-1}g_\mu=\sum_{k\ge0}P_\mu^k g_\mu$, which is finite and unique. The series form is the expected total cost: $P_\mu^k g_\mu$ is the expected stage-$k$ cost weighted by the probability of still being non-terminal. Full credit for invertibility from the spectral radius, the Neumann-series identity, and the rearrangement.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the **policy-improvement** step for SSP: let $\mu$ be a proper policy with cost $J_\mu$, and let $\bar\mu$ be greedy for $J_\mu$ ($T_{\bar\mu}J_\mu=TJ_\mu$). Show $\bar\mu$ is proper and $J_{\bar\mu}\le J_\mu$ pointwise, with strict inequality somewhere unless $\mu$ is optimal.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

From $T_{\bar\mu}J_\mu=TJ_\mu\le T_\mu J_\mu=J_\mu$, iterate the monotone $T_{\bar\mu}$; bound $J_{\bar\mu}$ above by the finite $J_\mu$ to conclude $\bar\mu$ proper, then pass to the fixed point.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Greediness gives $T_{\bar\mu}J_\mu=TJ_\mu=\min_\nu T_\nu J_\mu\le T_\mu J_\mu=J_\mu$ (the last equality is policy evaluation of the proper $\mu$). So $T_{\bar\mu}J_\mu\le J_\mu$. Applying the monotone $T_{\bar\mu}$, $T_{\bar\mu}^{k+1}J_\mu\le T_{\bar\mu}^kJ_\mu\le\cdots\le J_\mu$, a nonincreasing sequence bounded above by the finite $J_\mu$. Writing the iteration out, $T_{\bar\mu}^kJ_\mu=\sum_{m=0}^{k-1}P_{\bar\mu}^m g_{\bar\mu}+P_{\bar\mu}^kJ_\mu$; boundedness above by the finite $J_\mu$ for all $k$ forces $P_{\bar\mu}^k\to0$ (otherwise a recurrent non-terminal class with positive cost would make the partial sums diverge), so $\rho(P_{\bar\mu})<1$ and $\bar\mu$ is proper. Then $T_{\bar\mu}$ contracts (weighted norm), so $T_{\bar\mu}^kJ_\mu\to J_{\bar\mu}$, giving $J_{\bar\mu}\le J_\mu$. If equality holds everywhere, $J_\mu=T_{\bar\mu}J_\mu=TJ_\mu$, so $J_\mu$ solves Bellman's equation and $J_\mu=J^*$ by uniqueness; hence strict improvement somewhere unless $\mu$ is optimal. Full credit for the greedy inequality, the boundedness-forces-properness argument, the convergence to $J_{\bar\mu}$, and the equality-forces-optimality step.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Explain why assumption (3.2) — every improper policy has infinite cost from some state — cannot be dropped, by exhibiting the failure: construct a one-non-terminal-state SSP with an improper, zero-cost self-loop action and a proper action, and show that without (3.2) Bellman's equation acquires a spurious solution, so uniqueness fails.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Give the self-loop action cost $0$ so the improper policy has finite (zero) cost; then check which constants $J$ satisfy $J=\min\{0+J,\ \text{proper action value}\}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take one non-terminal state with two actions: action $a$ (improper) self-loops with probability $1$ at cost $g(a)=0$; action $b$ (proper) goes to the terminal state with probability $1$ at cost $g(b)=1$. The improper policy "always $a$" has cost $J_a=0+J_a$, satisfied by $J_a=0$, finite — so assumption (3.2) is violated. Bellman's equation is $J=\min\{0+J,\ 1+0\}=\min\{J,1\}$. Every $J\le1$ satisfies $J=\min\{J,1\}=J$, so the equation has infinitely many solutions on $(-\infty,1]$, not a unique one; the genuine optimal cost is $J^*=1$ (use $b$), but the spurious solutions $J<1$ are artifacts of the zero-cost improper loop. With (3.2) restored — for instance $g(a)=\varepsilon>0$, making the improper policy cost $+\infty$ — the self-loop branch becomes $J=\varepsilon+J$, impossible for finite $J$, so $T$ contracts in the weighted norm and $J^*=1+\varepsilon\cdot 0=1$ is the unique fixed point. Full credit for the construction, the multiple-solutions computation, and the explanation that (3.2) removes the spurious branch.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The SSP theory extends in four directions: policy iteration restricted to proper policies with its monotone improvement and finite termination, the Gauss-Seidel and asynchronous value-iteration variants convergent under the same assumptions, real-time dynamic programming over simulated trajectories, and the unifying status of SSP as the model from which discounted DP, deterministic shortest paths, and first-passage problems all descend.

**Theorem 1 (policy iteration over proper policies: monotone improvement and finite termination).** Starting from any proper policy $\mu_0$ (one exists by assumption 3.1), policy iteration alternates exact **evaluation** — solve $J_{\mu_k}=g_{\mu_k}+P_{\mu_k}J_{\mu_k}$, i.e. the linear system $J_{\mu_k}=(I-P_{\mu_k})^{-1}g_{\mu_k}$, well-posed because $\rho(P_{\mu_k})<1$ — with **improvement**, $\mu_{k+1}$ greedy for $J_{\mu_k}$. Each $\mu_{k+1}$ is again proper, $J_{\mu_{k+1}}\le J_{\mu_k}$ pointwise, with strict decrease at some state unless $\mu_k$ is optimal. With finitely many states and controls there are finitely many proper policies and the cost strictly decreases until optimality, so policy iteration terminates with an optimal proper policy in finitely many iterations; it is the Newton method for $J-TJ=0$ and converges at least as fast as value iteration [ref: TODO_REF Bertsekas Vol. 2 Ch. 3].

**Theorem 2 (Gauss-Seidel and asynchronous value iteration).** Because $T$ is a weighted-sup-norm contraction with a monotone, componentwise structure, value iteration converges even when states are updated one at a time using the most recent values (Gauss-Seidel) or in an arbitrary order with possibly outdated values (asynchronous / chaotic iteration), provided every state is updated infinitely often. The asynchronous convergence follows from the Bertsekas asynchronous-convergence theorem: a sequence of nested boxes $\{J:\|J-J^*\|_v\le\beta^k r\}$ shrinking to $J^*$, each mapped into the next by $T$ componentwise, is exactly the structure a weighted-sup-norm contraction provides, so any update schedule with infinitely-often coverage drives the iterate into every box [ref: TODO_REF Bertsekas Tsitsiklis Neuro-Dynamic Programming Ch. 2].

**Theorem 3 (real-time dynamic programming).** Real-time dynamic programming (RTDP) interleaves control with computation: at each visited state $i$ it performs one Bellman backup $J(i)\leftarrow(TJ)(i)$, applies the greedy action, and moves to the simulated successor, updating only states along the trajectory. Under the SSP assumptions, with an admissible (lower-bounding) initial $J_0\le J^*$ and a proper greedy policy maintained, the values of all relevant states converge to $J^*$ and the generated trajectories reach the terminal state; RTDP focuses computation on states that matter for the start state, the basis of the LRTA* and labeled-RTDP heuristic-search algorithms [ref: TODO_REF Bertsekas Tsitsiklis Neuro-Dynamic Programming Ch. 2].

**Theorem 4 (SSP as the unifying total-cost model).** The SSP problem specializes to the principal finite-state DP models. The **discounted** MDP with discount $\alpha\in(0,1)$ [44.08.03] is the SSP obtained by adding, from every state, a transition to the terminal state with probability $1-\alpha$ and scaling the surviving transitions by $\alpha$: every policy is then automatically proper (geometric termination), the constant weight $v\equiv 1$ gives modulus $\beta=\alpha$, and the SSP optimality equation reduces to the discounted Bellman equation. The **deterministic shortest path** problem is the SSP in which each control sends the state to a unique successor with probability one and $g(i,u)$ is the arc length; properness is the existence of a path to the destination, assumption (3.2) is the absence of negative-cost cycles, and value/policy iteration reduce to the Bellman-Ford and label-correcting algorithms. **First-passage** problems — minimizing expected cost until a target set is first reached — are SSPs with the target set lumped into the terminal state. Thus the weighted-contraction theory subsumes discounting, classical shortest paths, and first-passage as one framework [ref: TODO_REF Bertsekas Vol. 2 Ch. 3].

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason the undiscounted total-cost problem is solvable is that properness manufactures a contraction where discounting once supplied one for free: the SSP assumptions produce a positive weight vector $v$ (minimum expected steps to the terminal state) and a modulus $\beta=1-1/\max_i v_i<1$, and this is exactly the discounted contraction of [44.08.02] read in a weighted norm, the discount case being the uniform-weight specialization. The central insight is that properness is the spectral condition $\rho(P_\mu)<1$, so the substochastic resolvent $(I-P_\mu)^{-1}$ converges and policy evaluation is the finite linear solve $J_\mu=(I-P_\mu)^{-1}g_\mu$; this is dual to the discounted resolvent $(I-\alpha P_\mu)^{-1}$ of the Markov-chain evaluation step [44.08.03], the discount $\alpha$ reappearing as a uniform survival probability. Putting these together, value iteration ($\beta^k$ geometric rate), policy iteration over proper policies (the finitely-terminating Newton step), and the asynchronous and real-time variants are algorithms for the one fixed point $J^*=TJ^*$ vanishing at the terminal state, and the two assumptions are what make that fixed point unique — without (3.2) a zero-cost improper loop reintroduces spurious solutions. This is exactly the model that unifies discounted DP, deterministic shortest paths, and first-passage problems, and it builds toward approximate and neuro-dynamic programming, where SSP is the default episodic substrate and the effective horizon $\max_i v_i$ measures the difficulty the discounted $1/(1-\alpha)$ measured before.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (proper policy: spectral characterization and finite evaluation).** *A stationary policy $\mu$ is proper if and only if $\rho(P_\mu)<1$, in which case $I-P_\mu$ is invertible, $\sum_{k\ge0}P_\mu^k=(I-P_\mu)^{-1}$, and $J_\mu=(I-P_\mu)^{-1}g_\mu$ is finite.*

*Proof.* If $\mu$ is proper, there are $m$ and $\rho<1$ with $\mathbb{P}(x_m\ne t\mid x_0=i)\le\rho$ for all $i$; the row sums of $P_\mu^m$ (probability of remaining non-terminal after $m$ steps) are $\le\rho<1$, so $\|P_\mu^m\|_\infty\le\rho$, giving $\|P_\mu^{km}\|_\infty\le\rho^k\to0$, hence $\rho(P_\mu)\le\rho^{1/m}<1$. Conversely if $\rho(P_\mu)<1$ then $\|P_\mu^k\|\to0$, so the probability of being non-terminal at step $k$ tends to $0$ for every start, i.e. absorption is certain and $\mu$ is proper. With $\rho(P_\mu)<1$, no eigenvalue of $P_\mu$ equals $1$, so $I-P_\mu$ is invertible; $(I-P_\mu)\sum_{k=0}^{m}P_\mu^k=I-P_\mu^{m+1}\to I$ gives $\sum_{k\ge0}P_\mu^k=(I-P_\mu)^{-1}$. Solving $J_\mu=g_\mu+P_\mu J_\mu$ yields the finite $J_\mu=(I-P_\mu)^{-1}g_\mu=\sum_{k\ge0}P_\mu^k g_\mu$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (existence of the contraction weight).** *Under assumptions (3.1)-(3.2) there exist $v>0$ and $\beta\in[0,1)$ with $\sum_j p_{ij}(u)\,v_j\le\beta\,v_i$ for all $i$ and all $u\in U(i)$.*

*Proof.* Charge a unit cost at every non-terminal state under every control; under (3.1)-(3.2) the modified SSP has a finite optimal cost $\hat J\ge\mathbf{1}>0$ (a proper policy gives finite expected steps; every improper policy is infinite-cost, so the optimum is proper), satisfying $\hat J(i)=1+\min_u\sum_j p_{ij}(u)\hat J(j)$. Hence $\sum_j p_{ij}(u)\hat J(j)\le\hat J(i)-1$ for every $u$. Set $v=\hat J$ and $\beta=\max_i(v_i-1)/v_i=1-1/\max_i v_i$; since $v_i\ge1$, $\beta\in[0,1)$, and $\sum_j p_{ij}(u)v_j\le v_i-1\le\beta v_i$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (weighted-sup-norm contraction of $T$ and proper $T_\mu$).** *With $v,\beta$ as in Proposition 2, $\|TJ-TJ'\|_v\le\beta\|J-J'\|_v$ and, for every proper $\mu$, $\|T_\mu J-T_\mu J'\|_v\le\beta\|J-J'\|_v$.*

*Proof.* For $i$ fixed, choose $u$ attaining $(TJ)(i)$. Then $(TJ)(i)-(TJ')(i)\le g(i,u)+\sum_j p_{ij}(u)J(j)-g(i,u)-\sum_j p_{ij}(u)J'(j)=\sum_j p_{ij}(u)(J(j)-J'(j))$. Bounding $|J(j)-J'(j)|\le v_j\|J-J'\|_v$ and using Proposition 2, this is $\le\sum_j p_{ij}(u)v_j\|J-J'\|_v\le\beta v_i\|J-J'\|_v$. The symmetric choice of the minimizer for $J'$ gives the reverse inequality, so $|(TJ)(i)-(TJ')(i)|\le\beta v_i\|J-J'\|_v$, i.e. $|(TJ)(i)-(TJ')(i)|/v_i\le\beta\|J-J'\|_v$ for every $i$; the maximum over $i$ is the claim for $T$. For a proper $\mu$, the same chain with the single control $\mu(i)$ in place of the minimum gives the bound for $T_\mu$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (existence, uniqueness, value-iteration convergence).** *$T$ has a unique fixed point $J^*\in\mathbb{R}^n$, and for any $J_0$, $\|T^kJ_0-J^*\|_v\le\beta^k\|J_0-J^*\|_v\to0$.*

*Proof.* $(\mathbb{R}^n,\|\cdot\|_v)$ is a complete metric space (finite-dimensional, all norms equivalent; [02.01.05]). By Proposition 3, $T$ is a $\beta$-contraction with $\beta<1$, so the Banach fixed-point theorem gives a unique $J^*=TJ^*$ and the geometric estimate $\|T^kJ_0-J^*\|_v\le\beta^k\|J_0-J^*\|_v$; $\beta^k\to0$ forces convergence. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (identification $J^*=\inf_\mu J_\mu$ and greedy-optimality criterion).** *The fixed point $J^*$ equals the optimal cost $\inf_\mu J_\mu$, attained by any proper policy greedy for $J^*$; and a stationary policy $\mu$ is optimal ($J_\mu=J^*$) if and only if $T_\mu J^*=TJ^*$.*

*Proof.* Let $\mu^*$ be greedy for $J^*$: $T_{\mu^*}J^*=TJ^*=J^*$. The contraction bound (Proposition 3, single control) shows the substochastic $P_{\mu^*}$ obeys $\sum_j p_{ij}(\mu^*(i))v_j\le\beta v_i<v_i$; summing against $v>0$ forces $\rho(P_{\mu^*})\le\beta<1$, so $\mu^*$ is proper (Proposition 1) and $J^*=J_{\mu^*}<\infty$ by uniqueness of the proper $T_{\mu^*}$ fixed point. For any proper $\mu$, monotonicity of $T_\mu$ ($J\le J'\Rightarrow T_\mu J\le T_\mu J'$, since $P_\mu\ge0$) and $T_\mu J^*\ge\min_\nu T_\nu J^*=TJ^*=J^*$ give, by iteration, $J_\mu=\lim_k T_\mu^k J^*\ge J^*$. For any improper $\mu$, assumption (3.2) gives $J_\mu(i)=+\infty$ for some $i$, so $J_\mu\ge J^*$ pointwise fails nowhere. Hence $\inf_\mu J_\mu\ge J^*$, while $J_{\mu^*}=J^*$ attains it; so $J^*=\inf_\mu J_\mu$. For the criterion: if $T_\mu J^*=TJ^*=J^*$, then $J^*$ is the fixed point of $T_\mu$, so $\mu$ is proper (as for $\mu^*$) and $J^*=J_\mu$, optimal. Conversely if $J_\mu=J^*$, applying $T_\mu$ to its fixed point, $T_\mu J^*=T_\mu J_\mu=J_\mu=J^*=TJ^*$, so $\mu$ is greedy. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (policy improvement and finite termination over proper policies).** *If $\mu$ is proper and $\bar\mu$ is greedy for $J_\mu$, then $\bar\mu$ is proper and $J_{\bar\mu}\le J_\mu$, with strict inequality somewhere unless $\mu$ is optimal; for finite $S,U$ policy iteration terminates at an optimal proper policy in finitely many iterations.*

*Proof.* Greediness gives $T_{\bar\mu}J_\mu=TJ_\mu\le T_\mu J_\mu=J_\mu$. Iterating the monotone $T_{\bar\mu}$, $T_{\bar\mu}^{k}J_\mu$ is nonincreasing and bounded above by the finite $J_\mu$. Since $T_{\bar\mu}^kJ_\mu=\sum_{m=0}^{k-1}P_{\bar\mu}^m g_{\bar\mu}+P_{\bar\mu}^kJ_\mu$ stays bounded above for all $k$, the partial sums $\sum_m P_{\bar\mu}^m g_{\bar\mu}$ converge, forcing $\|P_{\bar\mu}^k\|\to0$, hence $\rho(P_{\bar\mu})<1$ and $\bar\mu$ proper (Proposition 1). Then $T_{\bar\mu}$ contracts and $T_{\bar\mu}^kJ_\mu\to J_{\bar\mu}$, so $J_{\bar\mu}\le J_\mu$. If $J_{\bar\mu}=J_\mu$ everywhere, $J_\mu=T_{\bar\mu}J_\mu=TJ_\mu$, so $J_\mu=J^*$ by Proposition 4; thus strict improvement somewhere unless $\mu$ is optimal. For finite $S,U$ the costs $J_{\mu_k}$ strictly decrease componentwise until optimality, no proper policy repeats, and there are at most $\prod_i|U(i)|<\infty$ of them, so a $\mu_k$ with $T_{\mu_k}J_{\mu_k}=TJ_{\mu_k}$, optimal by Proposition 5, is reached in finitely many steps. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The infinite-horizon discounted dynamic programming of [44.08.02] is the special case of this unit obtained by adding a uniform termination probability $1-\alpha$ to every state: every policy then becomes proper through geometric absorption, the constant weight $v\equiv\mathbf{1}$ yields contraction modulus $\beta=\alpha$, and the SSP optimality equation $J^*=TJ^*$ collapses to the discounted Bellman equation. The discounted theory's free contraction is recovered here as the uniform-weight instance of the constructed weighted-sup-norm contraction; conversely SSP is what the discounted method becomes when the discount is replaced by a genuine absorbing terminal state and a manufactured weight vector restores the contraction.

- The Markov decision process of [44.08.03] supplies the controlled-transition substrate that SSP specializes to the undiscounted, terminating case: an SSP is an MDP with $\alpha=1$, a cost-free absorbing state, and the proper/improper dichotomy replacing the automatic well-posedness that discounting provided. Fixing a proper stationary policy collapses the SSP to a substochastic Markov chain on the non-terminal states whose resolvent $(I-P_\mu)^{-1}$ is the discounted-resolvent analogue with survival probability one, and the policy-evaluation linear solve $J_\mu=(I-P_\mu)^{-1}g_\mu$ is the SSP counterpart of the discounted $(I-\alpha P_\mu)^{-1}r_\mu$.

- The Banach fixed-point theorem [02.01.05] is again the existence-uniqueness-and-rate engine, but read in a weighted sup-norm on $\mathbb{R}^n$ rather than the plain one: the constructed weight $v$ and modulus $\beta$ instantiate the abstract contraction on the finite-dimensional complete space of total-cost functions vanishing at the terminal state. The same fixed-point principle that powers SSP also drives the fixed-point iterations and contraction-based convergence of numerical analysis [43.02.02], so the stochastic shortest path problem is the terminating-control reading of one of analysis's most reused theorems, distinguished only by the weighting that rescues the contraction when discounting is absent.

- The Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation appearing in the linear-quadratic regulator [44.07.02] is the continuous-time, undiscounted-cost shadow of the SSP optimality equation: where the discrete SSP minimizes total cost until absorption and yields $J^*=TJ^*$, the continuous problem yields a first-order partial differential equation for the cost-to-go to a target set, with properness corresponding to controllability to the target and the SSP weight vector to a Lyapunov function certifying finite-time-ish reachability. The discrete weighted contraction is the well-posedness mechanism the continuous HJB problem must replace with verification or viscosity-solution arguments.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The stochastic shortest path problem grew out of two strands of Richard Bellman's 1950s dynamic-programming program: the deterministic shortest-path and routing problems solved by the Bellman-Ford label-correcting recursion, and the undiscounted total-cost control of terminating Markov chains. The rigorous modern treatment, isolating the proper/improper dichotomy and proving existence and uniqueness of the optimal cost together with the weighted-sup-norm contraction, is due to Dimitri Bertsekas and John Tsitsiklis in their 1991 paper *An Analysis of Stochastic Shortest Path Problems* [ref: TODO_REF Bertsekas Tsitsiklis 1991], which established that under the two assumptions the Bellman operator is a contraction in a weighted norm and that value and policy iteration converge, with $J^*$ the unique solution of Bellman's equation among functions vanishing at the terminal state.

The systematic textbook development — the proper/improper classification, the assumptions, the weighted-contraction construction, value and policy iteration, Gauss-Seidel and asynchronous variants, and the demonstration that discounted DP, deterministic shortest paths, and first-passage problems are SSP special cases — is given by Bertsekas in *Dynamic Programming and Optimal Control* Volume 2 Chapter 3 [ref: TODO_REF Bertsekas Vol. 2 Ch. 3], the anchor for this unit. The SSP framework became the canonical episodic model for approximate and neuro-dynamic programming in Bertsekas and Tsitsiklis's 1996 monograph *Neuro-Dynamic Programming* [ref: TODO_REF Bertsekas Tsitsiklis Neuro-Dynamic Programming Ch. 2], where real-time dynamic programming and simulation-based methods are built on the same assumptions, and it underlies the heuristic-search RTDP and LRTA* algorithms developed in the artificial-intelligence literature by Andrew Barto, Steven Bradtke, and Satinder Singh.
