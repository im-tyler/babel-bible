## Intuition [Beginner]

A constrained problem has walls — the inequality rules you must not cross. One way to handle them is to march right up to the walls and figure out which ones you press against, the active-set idea. Interior-point methods do the opposite: they refuse to touch any wall at all, staying strictly inside the allowed room, and let a slowly weakening force push them toward the corner where the answer lives.

The force is a barrier. Imagine adding to the cost a term that blows up to infinity as you approach any wall, like a repelling fog that gets thicker the closer you get. With the fog turned up high, the cheapest point sits comfortably in the middle of the room, far from every wall. Now turn the fog down a little. The cheapest point drifts toward the walls, because the true cost matters more and the fog matters less. Keep turning the fog down. The point traces a smooth curve through the room, heading straight for the constrained optimum, which it reaches only in the limit as the fog vanishes.

That smooth curve is the central path, and the fog strength is one number, the barrier parameter. The whole method is: pick a fog level, find the cheapest foggy point, lower the fog, repeat. Each foggy subproblem is smooth and has no walls to bump into, so an ordinary downhill solver handles it. Lowering the fog in steps is a homotopy — a continuous deformation from an easy problem you can solve to the hard problem you want, with the answer carried along the path.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the allowed room as the inside of a triangle. The true best point sits in a corner where two walls meet. With strong fog the cheapest point sits near the middle; as the fog weakens, that point slides along a smooth curve toward the corner.

```
        wall A                         wall B
          \                            /
           \      central path        /
            \    *  (mu large)       /
             \    \                  /
              \    \                /
               \    *  (mu medium) /
                \    \            /
                 \    \          /
                  \    *        /     <- (mu small)
                   \    \      /
                    \    o    /        <- x* (mu -> 0): the corner,
                     \   |   /            pressed against both walls
                      \  |  /
                       \ | /
                  ------ V ------  wall C (floor)

   strong fog  -> cheapest point sits deep inside, far from walls
   weaken fog  -> point slides toward the constrained optimum
   fog -> 0    -> point reaches x*, the true answer, in the limit
```

The takeaway: interior-point methods never walk along the walls. They follow a smooth interior curve, lowering one barrier number until the curve arrives at the answer.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Minimize the cost $f(x) = x$ subject to the single rule $x \ge 1$. The answer is plainly $x = 1$, pressed against the wall, but let us watch the barrier method find it so the central path becomes concrete.

**Step 1. Add the fog.** Replace the rule by a barrier that blows up as $x$ nears $1$. The foggy cost is $x - \mu \log(x - 1)$, where $\mu > 0$ is the fog strength and $\log(x - 1)$ rushes to minus infinity as $x \to 1$, so subtracting it builds a wall of cost near $x = 1$.

**Step 2. Find the cheapest foggy point.** The foggy cost is smallest where its slope is zero. The slope is $1 - \mu/(x - 1)$. Setting it to zero gives $\mu/(x - 1) = 1$, so $x - 1 = \mu$, that is $x = 1 + \mu$.

**Step 3. Read off the central path.** For each fog level $\mu$ the cheapest point is $x(\mu) = 1 + \mu$. This is the central path: a straight line in this simple case, sitting a distance $\mu$ inside the wall.

**Step 4. Lower the fog.** Take $\mu = 1$: point at $x = 2$. Take $\mu = 0.1$: point at $x = 1.1$. Take $\mu = 0.001$: point at $x = 1.001$. As $\mu$ shrinks toward zero, $x(\mu) = 1 + \mu$ marches down to $x = 1$.

**What this tells us.** The barrier method never sets $x = 1$ directly; it keeps $x$ strictly bigger than $1$ at every stage and lets the limit $\mu \to 0$ deliver the boundary answer. The path $x(\mu) = 1 + \mu$ shows the central path explicitly, and the distance to the wall is exactly the fog strength $\mu$ — the hallmark of an interior-point method.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Consider the nonlinear program over $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$,

$$
\min_x\; f(x) \quad\text{subject to}\quad c_i(x) = 0\ (i \in \mathcal{E}),\quad c_i(x) \ge 0\ (i \in \mathcal{I}),
$$

with $f, c_i$ twice continuously differentiable. Introduce a **slack vector** $s \in \mathbb{R}^{|\mathcal{I}|}$ and replace each inequality by $c_i(x) - s_i = 0$, $s_i \ge 0$, so the only inequalities left are the simple bounds $s \ge 0$.

**Definition (logarithmic barrier and barrier problem).** For a **barrier parameter** $\mu > 0$ the **barrier problem** is

$$
\min_{x,\,s}\; f(x) - \mu \sum_{i \in \mathcal{I}} \log s_i \quad\text{subject to}\quad c_i(x) = 0\ (i\in\mathcal{E}),\quad c_i(x) - s_i = 0\ (i\in\mathcal{I}),\quad s > 0,
$$

where $-\mu\sum_i \log s_i$ is the **logarithmic barrier**, finite on $s > 0$ and tending to $+\infty$ as any $s_i \downarrow 0$. The barrier confines minimizers to the strict interior of the inequality set, and the equalities are kept by Lagrange multipliers, exactly as in the constrained-Newton setting of [44.03.03].

**Definition (perturbed KKT system and the central path).** Let $\nu$ be the multipliers for the equalities $c_i(x) = 0$ ($i\in\mathcal{E}$) and $\lambda$ the multipliers for $c_i(x) - s_i = 0$ ($i\in\mathcal{I}$). Writing the KKT conditions of the barrier problem [44.02.02] and eliminating the barrier gradient $\mu/s_i$ in favor of $\lambda_i$ gives the **perturbed KKT system**

$$
\nabla f(x) - A_{\mathcal{E}}(x)^\top \nu - A_{\mathcal{I}}(x)^\top \lambda = 0,\qquad
S\Lambda e = \mu e,\qquad
c_{\mathcal{E}}(x) = 0,\qquad
c_{\mathcal{I}}(x) - s = 0,
$$

with $(s,\lambda) > 0$, where $A_{\mathcal{E}}, A_{\mathcal{I}}$ are the constraint Jacobians, $S = \operatorname{diag}(s)$, $\Lambda = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda)$, and $e = (1,\dots,1)^\top$. The **central path** is the trajectory $\{(x(\mu), s(\mu), \lambda(\mu), \nu(\mu)) : \mu > 0\}$ of solutions, the only departure from the genuine KKT conditions being the relaxed **perturbed complementarity** $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$ in place of $s_i\lambda_i = 0$. As $\mu \to 0$ the path approaches a KKT point. The new symbols $\mu$ (barrier parameter), $S = \operatorname{diag}(s)$, $\Lambda = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda)$, $e$ (all-ones vector), and the **duality measure** $\mu_k = s_k^\top\lambda_k/|\mathcal{I}|$ are recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

**Definition (primal-dual Newton step).** Collecting $w = (x, s, \nu, \lambda)$, the perturbed KKT system is $F_\mu(w) = 0$. One Newton step solves the linearized system $F'_\mu(w)\,\Delta w = -F_\mu(w)$, the **primal-dual system**

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
\nabla^2_{xx}L & 0 & -A_{\mathcal{E}}^\top & -A_{\mathcal{I}}^\top \\
0 & \Lambda & 0 & S \\
A_{\mathcal{E}} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
A_{\mathcal{I}} & -I & 0 & 0
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}\Delta x \\ \Delta s \\ \Delta\nu \\ \Delta\lambda\end{bmatrix}
= -\begin{bmatrix}
\nabla f - A_{\mathcal{E}}^\top\nu - A_{\mathcal{I}}^\top\lambda \\
S\Lambda e - \mu e \\
c_{\mathcal{E}} \\
c_{\mathcal{I}} - s
\end{bmatrix},
$$

with $\nabla^2_{xx}L = \nabla^2 f - \sum_i \nu_i\nabla^2 c_i - \sum_i \lambda_i\nabla^2 c_i$ the Lagrangian Hessian. Eliminating $\Delta s$ and $\Delta\lambda$ condenses this to a symmetric saddle-point system in $(\Delta x, \Delta\nu)$ of the same form as the constrained-Newton KKT matrix of [44.03.03].

**Definition (fraction-to-the-boundary rule).** Given a step $(\Delta s, \Delta\lambda)$, the **fraction-to-the-boundary rule** with parameter $\tau \in (0,1)$ (typically $\tau = 0.995$) sets the step length

$$
\alpha = \max\{\alpha \in (0,1] : s + \alpha\Delta s \ge (1-\tau)s,\ \lambda + \alpha\Delta\lambda \ge (1-\tau)\lambda\},
$$

keeping $(s,\lambda)$ strictly positive and bounded away from the boundary by a fixed fraction, so the iterates remain interior.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **The barrier minimizer is not the constrained minimizer for any fixed $\mu > 0$.** The central-path point $x(\mu)$ sits strictly interior; it equals the boundary optimum only in the limit $\mu \to 0$. Stopping at a fixed $\mu$ and reporting $x(\mu)$ leaves an $O(\mu)$ error in the objective.
- **Perturbed complementarity is $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$, not $s_i\lambda_i \approx 0$.** Driving every product to a *common* positive value $\mu$ is what keeps the iterate centered; letting the products spread (some near zero, some large) pushes the iterate toward a corner of the boundary where the Newton step degrades, the role the neighborhood/centering condition guards against.
- **The primal barrier Hessian is ill-conditioned; the primal-dual system is not.** The pure primal barrier function has Hessian $\nabla^2 f + \mu A^\top S^{-2} A$, whose $S^{-2} = O(\mu^{-2})$ blows up as $\mu \to 0$. The primal-dual system treats $\lambda$ as an independent variable and avoids forming $S^{-2}$, which is why primal-dual methods are numerically preferred over the original SUMT barrier method.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is the existence, uniqueness, and limiting behavior of the central path for a convex program: under strict feasibility the perturbed KKT system has a unique solution for each $\mu > 0$, these solutions form a smooth trajectory, and as $\mu \to 0$ they converge to a KKT point of the original program. This is what licenses the homotopy: a well-defined curve to follow, ending at the answer.

**Theorem (central path: existence, uniqueness, limit — convex case).** *Let $f$ and $-c_i$ ($i\in\mathcal{I}$) be convex and twice differentiable, the equalities $c_i(x) = a_i^\top x - b_i$ affine, the feasible set have nonempty strict interior (Slater), and the optimal set be nonempty and bounded. Then for each $\mu > 0$ the barrier problem has a unique minimizer $(x(\mu), s(\mu))$ with multipliers $(\lambda(\mu), \nu(\mu))$ solving the perturbed KKT system with $s_i(\mu)\lambda_i(\mu) = \mu$; the map $\mu \mapsto (x(\mu),s(\mu),\lambda(\mu),\nu(\mu))$ is continuously differentiable on $(0,\infty)$; and as $\mu \to 0$ every limit point of $x(\mu)$ is a minimizer of the original program, with the objective gap bounded by $f(x(\mu)) - p^\star \le |\mathcal{I}|\,\mu$.*

**Proof.** Fix $\mu > 0$. On the strict interior the barrier objective $\phi_\mu(x,s) = f(x) - \mu\sum_i\log s_i$ restricted to the affine feasible manifold $\{c_{\mathcal{E}}(x) = 0,\ c_i(x) = s_i\}$ is strictly convex in $(x,s)$: $f$ is convex, each $-\log s_i$ is strictly convex on $s_i > 0$, and the constraints are affine in the convex case, so the restriction is a strictly convex function on a convex set. Slater gives a strictly interior feasible point, and the barrier $\to +\infty$ on the boundary of the slack orthant forces the sublevel sets to be compact (boundedness of the optimal set rules out escape to infinity along the recession directions). A strictly convex function with compact sublevel sets on a convex set attains a unique minimizer, giving $(x(\mu),s(\mu))$; its KKT conditions [44.02.02] are stationarity $\nabla f - A_{\mathcal{E}}^\top\nu - A_{\mathcal{I}}^\top\lambda = 0$ together with the barrier-gradient identity $\mu/s_i = \lambda_i$, i.e. $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$, and primal feasibility — the perturbed KKT system, with $\lambda_i = \mu/s_i > 0$ automatically.

Smoothness: the perturbed-KKT map $F_\mu(w) = 0$ has Jacobian the primal-dual matrix, which under Slater and convexity is nonsingular on the path (its condensed form is the constrained-Newton KKT matrix with the positive-definite-on-the-null-space inertia condition of [44.03.03], here guaranteed by $\nabla^2 f \succeq 0$ plus the strictly positive diagonal $S^{-1}\Lambda \succ 0$). The implicit function theorem applied to $F_\mu(w) = 0$, treating $\mu$ as the parameter, yields a continuously differentiable solution map $\mu \mapsto w(\mu)$.

Limit and gap: along the path, weak duality gives $f(x(\mu)) - p^\star \le f(x(\mu)) - g(\lambda(\mu),\nu(\mu))$, the duality gap, where $g$ is the Lagrangian dual of [44.02.02]. For the barrier solution the gap equals $\sum_i s_i(\mu)\lambda_i(\mu) = \sum_i \mu = |\mathcal{I}|\,\mu$ (each complementarity product is exactly $\mu$, and the equality terms vanish on the affine manifold). Hence $f(x(\mu)) - p^\star \le |\mathcal{I}|\,\mu \to 0$. The path lies in a compact sublevel set (boundedness of the optimal set plus the gap bound), so it has limit points, and any limit point satisfies the unperturbed KKT system ($s_i\lambda_i = \mu \to 0$ recovers complementary slackness, the other equations pass to the limit), hence is a minimizer by convex sufficiency of [44.02.02]. $\square$

**Bridge.** This central-path theorem **builds toward** the path-following algorithm — take one Newton step toward $(x(\mu),s,\lambda,\nu)$, decrease $\mu$, repeat — and it **appears again in** the conic and semidefinite interior-point methods [44.05.04], where the same $S\Lambda e = \mu e$ relaxation reappears with a self-concordant barrier delivering polynomial complexity. **This is exactly** the homotopy reading of the KKT conditions of [44.02.02]: the perturbation $\mu$ deforms complementary slackness $s_i\lambda_i = 0$ into the smooth $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$, turning the combinatorial active-set question into a continuous curve, so that following the curve to $\mu = 0$ is following a deformation of an easy strictly-interior problem into the hard boundary problem. **The foundational reason** the path is well defined is strict convexity of the barrier on the feasible manifold under Slater, which makes each barrier subproblem uniquely solvable, and nonsingularity of the primal-dual Jacobian, which is the constrained-Newton inertia condition of [44.03.03] applied along the path. **Putting these together**, the interior-point method **generalises** the unconstrained Newton iteration of [44.03.03] to inequality constraints by replacing the active-set decision with a barrier, and **is dual to** the active-set approach: where active-set methods of [44.04.01] guess which constraints are tight and walk the boundary, the interior-point method keeps all inequalities strictly slack and lets $\mu \to 0$ discover the active set in the limit, the two strategies meeting at the same KKT point from opposite sides of the feasible region.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

For $\min f(x) = x$ subject to $x \ge 1$, write the logarithmic barrier objective and derive the central-path point $x(\mu)$ by setting the barrier gradient to zero.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The barrier is $-\mu\log(x-1)$; differentiate $x - \mu\log(x-1)$ and set to zero.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The barrier objective is $\phi_\mu(x) = x - \mu\log(x-1)$. Its derivative is $1 - \mu/(x-1)$. Setting it to zero gives $x - 1 = \mu$, so $x(\mu) = 1 + \mu$. The slack is $s = x - 1 = \mu$ and the multiplier $\lambda = \mu/s = 1$, satisfying $s\lambda = \mu$ and recovering the KKT point $x^\star = 1$, $\lambda^\star = 1$ as $\mu \to 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

State the perturbed complementarity equation and explain how it differs from ordinary complementary slackness; what does taking $\mu \to 0$ recover?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$ with $s_i\lambda_i = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Perturbed complementarity is $S\Lambda e = \mu e$, i.e. $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$ for every $i$, with $\mu > 0$. Ordinary complementary slackness of [44.02.02] is $s_i\lambda_i = 0$: at a KKT point each inequality is either active ($s_i = 0$) or its multiplier vanishes ($\lambda_i = 0$). The perturbation replaces the disjunctive $s_i = 0$ or $\lambda_i = 0$ by the smooth requirement that the *product* equal a common positive $\mu$, forcing both $s_i > 0$ and $\lambda_i > 0$. Taking $\mu \to 0$ drives $s_i\lambda_i \to 0$, recovering complementary slackness and hence a genuine KKT point.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the central path for $\min \tfrac12\|x\|^2$ subject to $a^\top x \ge 1$ ($a \ne 0$), and show it converges to the minimum-norm point $a/\|a\|^2$ on the hyperplane as $\mu \to 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Introduce a slack $s = a^\top x - 1 \ge 0$ and a multiplier $\lambda$ with $s\lambda = \mu$; use stationarity $x = \lambda a$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Stationarity of $\tfrac12\|x\|^2 - \lambda(a^\top x - 1 - 0)$ (with $s = a^\top x - 1$) gives $x = \lambda a$. Then $s = a^\top x - 1 = \lambda\|a\|^2 - 1$, and perturbed complementarity $s\lambda = \mu$ reads $\lambda(\lambda\|a\|^2 - 1) = \mu$, a quadratic $\|a\|^2\lambda^2 - \lambda - \mu = 0$. The positive root is $\lambda(\mu) = \big(1 + \sqrt{1 + 4\mu\|a\|^2}\big)/(2\|a\|^2)$, so $x(\mu) = \lambda(\mu)a$. As $\mu \to 0$, $\lambda(\mu) \to 1/\|a\|^2$, giving $x(\mu) \to a/\|a\|^2$, the KKT solution of [44.02.02] for this problem.

</details>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that for a convex program the central-path objective gap is exactly $|\mathcal{I}|\mu$, and conclude the path's suboptimality bound $f(x(\mu)) - p^\star \le |\mathcal{I}|\mu$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The duality gap at a primal-dual feasible pair is $\sum_i s_i\lambda_i$ for the slacked problem; on the path each product is $\mu$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On the central path the point $(x(\mu),s(\mu))$ is primal feasible and $(\lambda(\mu),\nu(\mu))$ with $\lambda(\mu) > 0$ is dual feasible (stationarity holds, $\lambda \succeq 0$). The Lagrangian dual value of [44.02.02] is $g(\lambda,\nu) = \inf_x L(x,\lambda,\nu)$, and at the path point stationarity makes $x(\mu)$ the unconstrained minimizer of $L(\cdot,\lambda(\mu),\nu(\mu))$ (convexity), so $g(\lambda(\mu),\nu(\mu)) = L(x(\mu),\lambda(\mu),\nu(\mu)) = f(x(\mu)) - \sum_i\lambda_i(c_i(x(\mu))) - \sum_{\mathcal{E}}\nu_i c_i$. Using $c_i(x(\mu)) = s_i(\mu)$ on $\mathcal{I}$ and $c_i = 0$ on $\mathcal{E}$, this is $f(x(\mu)) - \sum_i s_i\lambda_i = f(x(\mu)) - |\mathcal{I}|\mu$ since $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$. Hence the duality gap $f(x(\mu)) - g(\lambda(\mu),\nu(\mu)) = |\mathcal{I}|\mu$. By weak duality $g \le p^\star$, so $f(x(\mu)) - p^\star \le f(x(\mu)) - g = |\mathcal{I}|\mu$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

For a nonconvex program the condensed primal-dual matrix may fail the inertia condition. Explain why a positive-definiteness failure of the reduced Hessian breaks the Newton step, and describe the inertia-correction (Hessian regularization) that restores a descent step.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Recall the inertia condition from the constrained-Newton KKT solve: the matrix needs inertia $(n, m, 0)$; if the reduced Hessian is indefinite the step is not a minimizer of the local model.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The condensed matrix $K = \begin{psmallmatrix} W & A_{\mathcal{E}}^\top \\ A_{\mathcal{E}} & 0\end{psmallmatrix}$ with $W = \nabla^2_{xx}L + A_{\mathcal{I}}^\top S^{-1}\Lambda A_{\mathcal{I}}$ produces a minimizer of the local barrier model only when it has the correct inertia $(n, |\mathcal{E}|, 0)$, equivalently the reduced Hessian $Z^\top W Z \succ 0$ on $\ker A_{\mathcal{E}}$ [44.03.03]. With $\nabla^2_{xx}L$ indefinite (nonconvex $f$ or constraints) this can fail: $K$ acquires extra negative or zero eigenvalues, and the Newton step then targets a saddle or is undefined, not a barrier-model minimizer, so the merit function need not decrease. **Inertia correction** replaces $W$ by $W + \delta I$ (and optionally regularizes the $(2,2)$ block by $-\gamma I$ for rank-deficient $A_{\mathcal{E}}$), increasing $\delta > 0$ until a symmetric-indefinite factorization reports the inertia $(n,|\mathcal{E}|,0)$ — the modified-Cholesky / eigenvalue-flooring idea of [44.03.03] transplanted to the primal-dual matrix. The corrected step is a descent direction for the barrier merit function, restoring globalization in the nonconvex case.

</details>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Contrast the primal barrier Hessian with the primal-dual matrix and explain, quantitatively, why the primal-dual formulation avoids the $O(\mu^{-2})$ ill-conditioning of the pure barrier method as $\mu \to 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Eliminate the slacks from the barrier objective directly to get $\nabla^2 f + \mu A^\top S^{-2}A$, and compare $S^{-2}$ with the primal-dual diagonal $S^{-1}\Lambda$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The pure (primal) barrier method minimizes $f(x) - \mu\sum_i\log c_i(x)$ over $x$ directly; its Hessian is $\nabla^2 f - \mu\sum_i\frac{\nabla^2 c_i}{c_i} + \mu A^\top S^{-2}A$ with $S = \operatorname{diag}(c_i(x))$. Near the optimum the active slacks satisfy $c_i = s_i = O(\mu)$ (from $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$ with $\lambda_i = O(1)$), so $S^{-2} = O(\mu^{-2})$ and the barrier-Hessian term $\mu A^\top S^{-2}A = O(\mu^{-1})$ on active rows while $O(\mu)$ on inactive rows — a condition number growing like $\mu^{-2}$. The primal-dual condensed matrix instead carries the diagonal $S^{-1}\Lambda$: on active constraints $\lambda_i = O(1)$ and $s_i = O(\mu)$ give $S^{-1}\Lambda = O(\mu^{-1})$, but $\lambda$ is an *independent* variable updated by its own Newton equation rather than the slaved value $\mu/s_i^2$, so the diagonal scales as $\lambda_i/s_i$ with $\lambda_i$ tracked accurately. The decisive point is that the primal-dual method never forms $S^{-2}$; treating $\lambda$ independently keeps the ill-conditioned scaling confined to a diagonal whose entries are controlled by the dual iterates, and superlinear convergence is recovered under strict complementarity, where the active $\lambda_i$ stay bounded away from $0$.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The barrier, the perturbed KKT system, and the central path

The slacked nonlinear program $\min f(x)$ s.t. $c_{\mathcal{E}}(x) = 0$, $c_{\mathcal{I}}(x) - s = 0$, $s \ge 0$ has barrier subproblem $\min f(x) - \mu\sum_i\log s_i$ on the equality manifold, and its KKT conditions are the perturbed KKT system with $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$ [ref: TODO_REF Nocedal & Wright §19.1]. The single structural fact is that the barrier deforms complementary slackness: the disjunction "$s_i = 0$ or $\lambda_i = 0$" of [44.02.02] becomes the smooth surface $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$, and the **central path** is the set of its solutions over $\mu > 0$. The path is the trajectory of analytic centers of the feasible region weighted by the objective, and its tangent at $\mu$ is the primal-dual Newton direction toward decreasing $\mu$. The original sequential-unconstrained-minimization (SUMT) view of Fiacco and McCormick [ref: TODO_REF Fiacco & McCormick 1990] solved the barrier subproblem to high accuracy at each $\mu$; the modern primal-dual view takes a single Newton step per $\mu$, following the path rather than landing on it, which is both faster and better-conditioned.

### The primal-dual Newton step and its reductions

Newton's method on $F_\mu(w) = 0$ gives the unsymmetric primal-dual system, symmetrized by scaling the complementarity row to $S\Delta\lambda + \Lambda\Delta s = -(S\Lambda e - \mu e)$ [ref: TODO_REF Forsgren-Gill-Wright 2002]. Two reductions are standard. The **augmented (symmetric indefinite) form** eliminates $\Delta s, \Delta\lambda$ to the $(\Delta x,\Delta\nu)$ saddle-point system $\begin{psmallmatrix} W & A_{\mathcal{E}}^\top \\ A_{\mathcal{E}} & 0\end{psmallmatrix}$ with $W = \nabla^2_{xx}L + A_{\mathcal{I}}^\top S^{-1}\Lambda A_{\mathcal{I}}$, solved by a symmetric-indefinite $LDL^\top$ factorization whose inertia is monitored. The **condensed (normal-equations) form** further eliminates $\Delta x$ when $W \succ 0$, producing a smaller positive-definite system in $\Delta\nu$ at the cost of squaring the conditioning. The inertia of the augmented matrix is exactly the inertia condition of the constrained-Newton KKT solve of [44.03.03]: correct inertia $(n,|\mathcal{E}|,0)$ holds when $A_{\mathcal{E}}$ has full row rank and $W$ is positive definite on $\ker A_{\mathcal{E}}$. The **fraction-to-the-boundary** step keeps $(s,\lambda) > 0$, and a **centering parameter** $\sigma\in[0,1]$ targets $\sigma\mu_k$ (with $\mu_k = s_k^\top\lambda_k/|\mathcal{I}|$ the duality measure) rather than $0$, balancing the predictor pull toward optimality against the corrector pull toward the path's center — the Mehrotra predictor-corrector heuristic in its nonlinear form.

### Nonconvexity, regularization, and globalization

For convex programs every central-path point is well defined and the path converges to the global optimum; for nonconvex programs neither holds without intervention. The Lagrangian Hessian $\nabla^2_{xx}L$ can be indefinite, so $W$ may fail positive-definiteness on $\ker A_{\mathcal{E}}$ and the augmented matrix loses the correct inertia. **Hessian regularization** adds $\delta I$ to $W$ (inertia correction), increasing $\delta$ until the $LDL^\top$ factorization reports inertia $(n,|\mathcal{E}|,0)$, the modified-Cholesky/eigenvalue-flooring device of [44.03.03] applied to the primal-dual matrix; a $(2,2)$-block regularization $-\gamma I$ handles rank-deficient $A_{\mathcal{E}}$. Global convergence from arbitrary strictly-interior starts requires a **globalization**: a line search on a barrier **merit function** (the barrier objective augmented by a constraint-violation penalty, e.g. an $\ell_1$ or augmented-Lagrangian penalty), or a **trust-region/filter** approach that accepts a step if it improves either the objective or the constraint violation without a penalty parameter (the Fletcher-Leyffer filter, used in IPOPT) [ref: TODO_REF Nocedal & Wright §19.5]. The interaction of the barrier with globalization is delicate — the **Maratos effect** and the loss of the fraction-to-the-boundary step near the boundary motivate second-order corrections and the filter.

### Local convergence, comparison with SQP, and the conic specialization

Under LICQ, strict complementarity, and the second-order sufficient condition of [44.02.02], the primal-dual Newton iteration with $\mu$ driven to zero superlinearly attains **superlinear (and, with the right $\mu$-update, quadratic) local convergence** [ref: TODO_REF Nocedal & Wright §19.7], because strict complementarity keeps the active $\lambda_i$ bounded away from $0$, making the primal-dual matrix uniformly well-conditioned in the limit and the Newton step asymptotically exact. The comparison with sequential quadratic programming [44.04.03] is structural: SQP solves a QP subproblem with an explicit active-set determination at each outer iteration and converges in few, expensive iterations with excellent warm-starting; interior-point methods replace the active-set combinatorics by the barrier homotopy, keeping all inequalities strictly inactive and converging in more, cheaper iterations that scale better with the number of constraints but warm-start poorly. The convex specialization is the cleanest case: for conic and semidefinite programs [44.05.04] the barrier is **self-concordant** in the sense of Nesterov and Nemirovski, and the same $S\Lambda e = \mu e$ relaxation yields a polynomial-iteration complexity bound $O(\sqrt{\vartheta}\log(1/\epsilon))$ in the barrier parameter $\vartheta$ — a guarantee the general nonlinear method forgoes, since a general barrier is not self-concordant and the path need not stay short.

**Synthesis.** The interior-point method **is exactly** the homotopy reading of the KKT conditions of [44.02.02]: the barrier parameter $\mu$ deforms complementary slackness $s_i\lambda_i = 0$ into the smooth surface $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$, and **the central insight** is that following the resulting central path from a strictly-interior start to $\mu = 0$ converts the combinatorial active-set question into the continuous problem of tracking a smooth curve with Newton's method. **The foundational reason** the curve is well defined for convex programs is strict convexity of the barrier on the feasible manifold under Slater, which makes each subproblem uniquely solvable, together with nonsingularity of the primal-dual Jacobian, which **is exactly** the constrained-Newton inertia condition of [44.03.03] applied along the path; nonconvexity attacks precisely this inertia, and Hessian regularization restores it. **Putting these together**, the method **generalises** the unconstrained Newton iteration of [44.03.03] to inequality constraints by replacing the active-set decision with a barrier and an independent dual variable that cures the $O(\mu^{-2})$ ill-conditioning of the pure barrier, and **is dual to** the active-set strategy of [44.04.01], reaching the same KKT point from the strict interior rather than along the boundary. **This is exactly** the engine that **appears again in** conic and semidefinite interior-point methods [44.05.04], where the self-concordant barrier upgrades the same perturbed-complementarity homotopy to a polynomial-iteration complexity guarantee, and in the comparison with SQP [44.04.03], the two dominant nonlinear-programming paradigms that linearize the same KKT system from opposite sides of the feasible region.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (perturbed KKT = barrier-problem KKT).** *The KKT conditions [44.02.02] of the barrier problem $\min f(x) - \mu\sum_i\log s_i$ s.t. $c_{\mathcal{E}}(x) = 0$, $c_i(x) - s_i = 0$, $s > 0$, are precisely the perturbed KKT system with $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$.*

*Proof.* Form the Lagrangian $L_\mu = f(x) - \mu\sum_i\log s_i - \nu^\top c_{\mathcal{E}}(x) - \lambda^\top(c_{\mathcal{I}}(x) - s)$ (the bound $s > 0$ is inactive at an interior minimizer, so its multiplier is zero). Stationarity in $x$: $\nabla f - A_{\mathcal{E}}^\top\nu - A_{\mathcal{I}}^\top\lambda = 0$. Stationarity in $s_i$: $-\mu/s_i + \lambda_i = 0$, i.e. $\lambda_i = \mu/s_i$, equivalently $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$. Primal feasibility is $c_{\mathcal{E}}(x) = 0$ and $c_i(x) = s_i$. Since $s_i > 0$, the stationarity-in-$s$ relation forces $\lambda_i = \mu/s_i > 0$. These are exactly the four blocks of the perturbed KKT system, with $\lambda > 0$ automatic. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (existence and uniqueness of the central-path point, convex case).** *Under the convexity, affine-equality, Slater, and bounded-optimal-set hypotheses, the barrier problem has a unique minimizer for each $\mu > 0$.*

*Proof.* Restrict the barrier objective $\phi_\mu(x,s) = f(x) - \mu\sum_i\log s_i$ to the convex feasible manifold $\mathcal{M} = \{(x,s) : c_{\mathcal{E}}(x) = 0,\ c_i(x) = s_i,\ s > 0\}$, convex because the $c_{\mathcal{E}}$ are affine and $c_i(x) - s_i$ is concave-minus-linear with $-c_i$ convex (the inequality $c_i \ge 0$ convex case), so the manifold is the intersection of affine sets with the convex graph constraint; in the standard convex NLP it is convex. On $\mathcal{M}$, $f$ is convex and $-\mu\log s_i$ is strictly convex in $s_i$, so $\phi_\mu$ is strictly convex in $s$ and convex in $x$; combined with the requirement that distinct feasible points differ in $s$ whenever they differ in the active $c_i$, the restriction is strictly convex on $\mathcal{M}$ (strict convexity in the $\log$ arguments). Slater provides a point of $\mathcal{M}$ with $s > 0$, so $\mathcal{M} \ne \varnothing$. As any $s_i \downarrow 0$, $\phi_\mu \to +\infty$; boundedness of the optimal set rules out a recession direction along which $\phi_\mu$ stays bounded, so every sublevel set $\{\phi_\mu \le \beta\}\cap\mathcal{M}$ is compact. A strictly convex lower-semicontinuous function with a nonempty compact sublevel set on a convex set attains its infimum at a unique point. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (smoothness of the central path).** *Under the hypotheses of Proposition 2, the solution map $\mu \mapsto w(\mu) = (x(\mu),s(\mu),\nu(\mu),\lambda(\mu))$ is continuously differentiable on $(0,\infty)$.*

*Proof.* The path solves $F_\mu(w) = 0$ with $F_\mu$ smooth jointly in $(w,\mu)$. Its Jacobian $F'_\mu(w)$ is the primal-dual matrix. Its condensed form is $\begin{psmallmatrix} W & A_{\mathcal{E}}^\top \\ A_{\mathcal{E}} & 0\end{psmallmatrix}$ with $W = \nabla^2_{xx}L + A_{\mathcal{I}}^\top S^{-1}\Lambda A_{\mathcal{I}}$. On the path $S^{-1}\Lambda \succ 0$ (since $s,\lambda > 0$) and $\nabla^2_{xx}L \succeq 0$ in the convex case, so $W \succeq \nabla^2 f \succeq 0$ and, restricted to $\ker A_{\mathcal{E}}$, the strictly positive term $A_{\mathcal{I}}^\top S^{-1}\Lambda A_{\mathcal{I}}$ plus convexity gives $Z^\top W Z \succ 0$ wherever the active inequality gradients span enough directions; with $A_{\mathcal{E}}$ full row rank the inertia condition of [44.03.03] holds and the matrix is nonsingular. (Where $Z^\top W Z$ is only positive semidefinite, the strict convexity of the barrier in $s$ keeps the full primal-dual Jacobian nonsingular by the $\Lambda, S$ diagonal blocks.) The implicit function theorem applied to $F_\mu(w) = 0$ with parameter $\mu$ then yields a $C^1$ map $\mu \mapsto w(\mu)$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (duality-gap identity and convergence as $\mu \to 0$).** *On the central path of a convex program, $f(x(\mu)) - p^\star = |\mathcal{I}|\mu - (\text{dual slackness}) \le |\mathcal{I}|\mu$, and every limit point of $x(\mu)$ as $\mu \to 0$ is a global minimizer.*

*Proof.* As in Exercise 6, the path point is primal feasible and $(\lambda(\mu),\nu(\mu))$ dual feasible, and stationarity makes $x(\mu)$ the unconstrained minimizer of $L(\cdot,\lambda(\mu),\nu(\mu))$ by convexity, so $g(\lambda(\mu),\nu(\mu)) = f(x(\mu)) - \sum_i s_i\lambda_i = f(x(\mu)) - |\mathcal{I}|\mu$. Weak duality $g \le p^\star \le f(x(\mu))$ gives $0 \le f(x(\mu)) - p^\star \le |\mathcal{I}|\mu$. As $\mu \to 0$ the gap vanishes, so $f(x(\mu)) \to p^\star$. The path lies in the compact sublevel set $\{f \le p^\star + |\mathcal{I}|\}$ intersected with the feasible set (compact by the bounded-optimal-set hypothesis plus the gap bound), so limit points exist; at a limit point $s_i\lambda_i = \mu \to 0$ gives complementary slackness, the remaining perturbed-KKT equations pass to the unperturbed KKT system by continuity, and convex sufficiency of [44.02.02] makes the limit a global minimizer. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (well-posedness of the regularized step in the nonconvex case).** *If $A_{\mathcal{E}}$ has full row rank, there is $\bar\delta \ge 0$ such that for all $\delta > \bar\delta$ the regularized matrix $\begin{psmallmatrix} W + \delta I & A_{\mathcal{E}}^\top \\ A_{\mathcal{E}} & 0\end{psmallmatrix}$ has inertia $(n,|\mathcal{E}|,0)$, so the regularized primal-dual step is the unique minimizer of the regularized barrier model and a descent direction for it.*

*Proof.* By the inertia formula of the constrained-Newton KKT solve [44.03.03], $\mathrm{In}\begin{psmallmatrix} W+\delta I & A_{\mathcal{E}}^\top \\ A_{\mathcal{E}} & 0\end{psmallmatrix} = \mathrm{In}(Z^\top(W+\delta I)Z) + (|\mathcal{E}|,|\mathcal{E}|,0)$ with $Z$ a basis of $\ker A_{\mathcal{E}}$. Now $Z^\top(W + \delta I)Z = Z^\top W Z + \delta Z^\top Z$; since $Z^\top Z \succ 0$, choosing $\delta > \bar\delta = \max(0, -\lambda_{\min}(Z^\top W Z)/\lambda_{\min}(Z^\top Z))$ makes $Z^\top(W+\delta I)Z \succ 0$, so its inertia is $(n - |\mathcal{E}|, 0, 0)$ and the full matrix has inertia $(n,|\mathcal{E}|,0)$ — nonsingular, no negative or zero curvature on the constraint null space. The step then uniquely minimizes the quadratic model $\tfrac12 p^\top(W+\delta I)p + g^\top p$ on $\{A_{\mathcal{E}}p = 0\}$ (Proposition 2 of [44.04.01] specialized), and $g^\top p = -p^\top(W+\delta I)p < 0$ for $p \ne 0$ certifies descent for the regularized barrier merit function. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The KKT optimality theory of [44.02.02] is the direct parent: the perturbed KKT system is the genuine KKT system with complementary slackness $s_i\lambda_i = 0$ relaxed to $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$, the central path is the homotopy that recovers complementary slackness as $\mu \to 0$, and the limit of the path is a KKT point whose multipliers are the path's dual variables. Convexity is exactly what upgrades that limit KKT point to a global minimizer, and the second-order sufficient condition there is the hypothesis under which the primal-dual Newton iteration is locally superlinear. The constraint qualifications (LICQ, strict complementarity) of that unit are the regularity conditions that keep the primal-dual matrix uniformly nonsingular along the path.

- Newton's method and the constrained-Newton KKT solve of [44.03.03] supply the per-iteration machinery: the primal-dual step is Newton's method applied to the perturbed-KKT root-finding problem, the condensed step is the constrained-Newton saddle-point system with a modified Hessian block, the inertia condition for a well-posed step is inherited verbatim, and the Hessian regularization for nonconvexity is the modified-Cholesky / eigenvalue-flooring device of that unit transplanted to the primal-dual matrix. The interior-point method is the unconstrained Newton iteration of that unit extended to inequality constraints by a barrier and an independent dual variable.

- Sequential quadratic programming [44.04.03] is the principal sibling method: both linearize the KKT system of [44.02.02], but SQP determines an active set by solving a QP subproblem at each outer step while the interior-point method keeps all inequalities strictly inactive along the central path, so the comparison — SQP's few expensive warm-startable iterations against interior-point's many cheap iterations scaling with constraint count — is the standard practitioner's tradeoff. The active-set QP machinery of [44.04.01] that SQP calls is exactly the combinatorial alternative to the barrier homotopy.

- Interior-point methods for conic and semidefinite programming [44.05.04] are the convex specialization: the same slack-and-barrier construction and the same perturbed-complementarity relaxation $S\Lambda e = \mu e$ apply, but a self-concordant barrier replaces the generic logarithmic barrier, the central path stays short in the self-concordant metric, and the path-following iteration attains a polynomial complexity bound $O(\sqrt{\vartheta}\log(1/\epsilon))$ that the general nonlinear method cannot promise. The nonlinear method developed here is the structural template; the conic theory is its provably-efficient instance.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Interior methods began with the barrier-function (sequential unconstrained minimization) technique of **Anthony Fiacco and Garth McCormick**, whose 1968 book systematized the logarithmic barrier and proved that the barrier minimizers trace a trajectory converging to a constrained minimizer as the barrier parameter tends to zero [ref: TODO_REF Fiacco & McCormick 1990] (the 1990 SIAM Classics reissue of the 1968 Wiley original). The method fell out of favor through the 1970s because the barrier Hessian becomes severely ill-conditioned as the parameter shrinks, and active-set and SQP methods dominated practical nonlinear programming.

The field was reignited by **Narendra Karmarkar's** 1984 polynomial-time projective algorithm for linear programming, which was soon understood as a path-following method on a logarithmic barrier; the connection to Fiacco-McCormick barriers was drawn by **Margaret Wright**, **Philip Gill**, and collaborators, and the polynomial-complexity theory for the convex case was placed on rigorous self-concordance foundations by **Yurii Nesterov and Arkadi Nemirovski** at the end of the 1980s. The primal-dual viewpoint that cured the conditioning problem — treating the multipliers as independent variables and relaxing complementarity to $s_i\lambda_i = \mu$ — was developed for linear and monotone-complementarity problems and given its standard monograph treatment by **Stephen Wright** [ref: TODO_REF Wright 1997]. The extension to general nonlinear programming, with slack variables, the fraction-to-the-boundary rule, Hessian regularization, and filter or merit-function globalization, was consolidated in the late 1990s and 2000s; the survey of **Anders Forsgren, Philip Gill, and Margaret Wright** [ref: TODO_REF Forsgren-Gill-Wright 2002] (SIAM Review 44, 525-597) and the textbook account of **Nocedal and Wright** [ref: TODO_REF Nocedal & Wright §19] are the standard references, and the filter line-search interior-point solver IPOPT of Wächter and Biegler is the most widely used implementation.
