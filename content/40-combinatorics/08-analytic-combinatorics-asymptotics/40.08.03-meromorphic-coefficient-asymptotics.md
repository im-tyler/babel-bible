## Intuition [Beginner]

The previous unit handed you a way to write a generating function by recipe: describe what your objects are made of, and a fixed dictionary spells out the series. That series packages every count at once, but it does not yet tell you the one thing you most often want — *how fast do the counts grow?* This unit reads the growth rate straight off the formula, without expanding a single extra term.

The key change in viewpoint is to stop treating the variable as a bookkeeping marker and start treating it as a real (in fact complex) number you can plug in. A generating function is then a genuine function with a graph, and that graph has trouble spots: places where it blows up to infinity, or otherwise stops being smooth. These trouble spots are called *singularities*. For the composition series $\frac{1-z}{1-2z}$ from last unit, the trouble spot is at $z = \frac12$, where the bottom hits zero and the value shoots off.

Here is the headline fact. The trouble spot nearest the origin sets the growth rate of the counts. If the nearest one sits at distance $\rho$ from zero, then the count of size-$n$ objects grows like $(1/\rho)^n$ — one over that distance, raised to the power $n$. For compositions, $\rho = \frac12$, so the counts grow like $2^n$, matching the $2^{n-1}$ you found by hand.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the generating function as a landscape over a flat map. The flat map is the plane of input values, with the origin in the middle. The height of the landscape is how big the function is at each input. Most of the map is gently rolling, but at a singularity a thin spike rises to infinity — a pole, like a flagpole punched through the surface.

![A flat map (the complex plane) with the origin marked at the centre and a dashed circle around it. Two infinite spikes (poles) rise from the map where the generating function blows up: a near spike at distance rho from the origin sitting on the positive horizontal axis, and a farther spike beyond it. The dashed circle is drawn so its radius just touches the nearest spike. A label notes that the radius of this circle equals rho, the distance to the nearest spike, and that the growth rate of the counts is one over rho raised to the power n.](/img/meromorphic-coefficient-asymptotics-placeholder.svg)

Now grow a circle outward from the origin. As long as the circle stays in the rolling part of the map, the series behaves; the moment the circle's edge reaches the first spike, the series stops converging. So the radius of the largest safe circle is exactly the distance to the nearest spike. That radius is the number $\rho$, and the counts grow like $(1/\rho)^n$.

| where the nearest spike sits | how the counts grow |
|---|---|
| at distance $\rho = \frac12$ (compositions, $\frac{1-z}{1-2z}$) | like $(1/\rho)^n = 2^n$ |
| at distance $\rho$ on the positive axis | like $(1/\rho)^n$ |
| farther out (bigger $\rho$) | slower growth |
| closer in (smaller $\rho$) | faster growth |

A second spike farther away barely matters: its contribution is a smaller growth rate, swamped by the nearest one. So the *nearest* spike rules, and a useful bonus is that for honest counting series — whose counts are never negative — the nearest spike always sits on the positive axis, straight out to the right, where it is easy to find.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let us read the growth rate of **surjections** — the ways to split $n$ labelled tasks into an ordered list of nonempty groups (an ordered set partition). The number of these is called $R_n$, and the labelled symbolic method gives the generating function $R(z) = \frac{1}{2 - e^z}$, with the count attached to the peg $z^n/n!$. We will not need that derivation; we only need to find the nearest spike.

Step 1. Find where the function blows up. The value $\frac{1}{2 - e^z}$ shoots to infinity exactly when the bottom is zero, that is when $e^z = 2$. On the positive axis this happens at $z = \log 2$, which is about $0.693$. That is a spike.

Step 2. Check it is the nearest one. Any other solution of $e^z = 2$ needs the same size for $e^z$, so it sits at least as far out as $\log 2$ (the others are off the axis and farther from the origin). So the nearest spike is at distance $\rho = \log 2 \approx 0.693$.

Step 3. Read the growth rate. The counts (per the $z^n/n!$ peg) grow like $(1/\rho)^n = (1/\log 2)^n$. Since $1/\log 2 \approx 1.4427$, the growth base is about $1.4427$.

Step 4. Sanity-check the size. The first few surjection numbers are $R_0 = 1$, $R_1 = 1$, $R_2 = 3$, $R_3 = 13$, $R_4 = 75$, $R_5 = 541$. Dividing each by the one before gives ratios drifting up toward roughly $7$ — and indeed the full count per object is about $\frac{1}{2}(1/\log 2)^{n+1}\, n!$, whose successive ratios are $(1/\log 2)\cdot n$, growing because of the $n!$. The base $1/\log 2 \approx 1.4427$ is the part the nearest spike pinned down.

What this tells us: locating one spike — solving $e^z = 2$ — handed us the exponential growth base $1/\log 2$ of the surjection numbers with no recurrence and no table.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A generating function $f(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} f_n z^n$ with positive radius of convergence is treated as an **analytic function** of a complex variable on its disc of convergence, following the formal-power-series substrate of [40.08.01] now read analytically. Two analytic facts anchor everything below; both are imported, not reproved here.

**Cauchy's coefficient formula.** If $f$ is analytic in a disc containing $0$ and $\lambda$ is a positively oriented simple loop around $0$ inside the disc of analyticity, then
$$
f_n = [z^n]f = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\lambda \frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}}\,dz,
$$
the Cauchy integral formula of [06.01.02] applied to extract the $n$-th coefficient. The **radius of convergence** $\rho$ of the series equals the modulus of a singularity of $f$ nearest the origin: $f$ is analytic on $|z| < \rho$ and has at least one singularity on $|z| = \rho$.

A point $s$ on the circle $|z| = \rho$ is a **singularity** of $f$ if $f$ has no analytic continuation to any neighbourhood of $s$. The function $f$ is **meromorphic** on a domain (in the sense of [06.01.05]) if it is analytic there except at isolated **poles**: points $s$ near which $f(z) = \sum_{k=1}^{m} c_{-k}(z - s)^{-k} + g(z)$ with $g$ analytic at $s$, $c_{-m} \ne 0$; the integer $m \ge 1$ is the **order** of the pole, and $c_{-1} = \mathrm{Res}(f; s)$ is its **residue**. A pole of order $1$ is **simple**. A **rational** function $f = N/D$ ($N, D$ polynomials, $\gcd = 1$, $D(0) \ne 0$) is meromorphic on $\mathbb{C}$ with poles exactly at the zeros of $D$.

The **dominant singularities** of $f$ are those of smallest modulus, all lying on $|z| = \rho$; the **subdominant** singularities lie strictly farther out. The notation $[z^n]$, $\oint$, $\mathrm{Res}$, $\limsup$, and $\rho$ (radius / dominant-singularity modulus) is recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

**Exponential Growth Formula.** Writing $\rho$ for the radius of convergence, $\limsup_{n}|f_n|^{1/n} = 1/\rho$, so $f_n = \rho^{-n}\theta(n)$ with $\theta$ subexponential ($|\theta(n)|^{1/n} \to 1$). The **first principle of coefficient asymptotics** is this statement: the *location* of the dominant singularity fixes the exponential growth factor $\rho^{-n}$. The **second principle**, the subject of [40.08.04], is that the *nature* (type) of the dominant singularity fixes the subexponential factor $\theta(n)$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"The nearest singularity can always be read off the positive real axis."* Only for series with nonnegative coefficients — there Pringsheim's theorem guarantees a singularity at $z = \rho$ itself. A series such as $\frac{1}{1 + z^2}$ has coefficients $1, 0, -1, 0, 1, \dots$ of mixed sign, and its dominant singularities are the non-real points $z = \pm i$; nothing sits on the positive axis. Combinatorial OGFs, having nonnegative counts, are the favourable case.

- *"A pole of order $m$ gives growth $\rho^{-n}$."* The exponential factor is $\rho^{-n}$ regardless of order, but the *subexponential* factor depends on the order: a pole of order $m$ at $\rho$ contributes $\sim C\,\rho^{-n} n^{m-1}/(m-1)!$. A simple pole ($m=1$) gives a pure $C\rho^{-n}$; a double pole gives $C n \rho^{-n}$.

- *"All singularities contribute equally."* Only the dominant ones contribute to the leading asymptotic. A subdominant pole at $\rho' > \rho$ contributes $O((\rho')^{-n})$, exponentially smaller than $\rho^{-n}$, so it appears only in lower-order correction terms obtained by subtracting the dominant singular part.

- *"There is always a unique dominant singularity."* Several singularities can share the modulus $\rho$. When two simple poles sit at $\pm\rho$, their residues combine into an oscillating factor $(-1)^n$-style term; more generally $d$ poles equally spaced on $|z| = \rho$ produce coefficients that are nonzero only on a residue class mod $d$ (periodicity). Pringsheim guarantees one of these is the positive real point, but it need not be alone.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem is the **residue expansion of meromorphic coefficients**: it converts coefficient extraction into a finite sum of residues by deforming the Cauchy contour outward past the poles, and the dominant pole's residue is the leading asymptotic term [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick Ch. IV.5].

**Theorem (meromorphic coefficient asymptotics).** *Let $f$ be analytic at $0$ and meromorphic in the closed disc $|z| \le R$, with no pole on $|z| = R$, and let its poles in $|z| < R$ be $s_1, \dots, s_p$. Then*
$$
[z^n]f = -\sum_{k=1}^{p}\mathrm{Res}\Big(\frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}};\, z = s_k\Big) + \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z|=R}\frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}}\,dz,
$$
*and the remainder integral is $O(R^{-n})$. In particular, if the dominant pole $s_1 = \rho$ is the unique pole of smallest modulus and has order $m$ with singular part $\sum_{k=1}^{m}c_{-k}(z-\rho)^{-k}$, then*
$$
[z^n]f \sim \frac{c_{-m}}{(m-1)!}\,(-1)^m\,\rho^{-m}\binom{n+m-1}{m-1}\rho^{-n} \sim \frac{c_{-m}(-1)^m}{(m-1)!\,\rho^{m}}\,n^{m-1}\rho^{-n}.
$$

*Proof.* By Cauchy's coefficient formula, $[z^n]f = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z| = r}\frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}}\,dz$ for any $r$ with $0 < r < |s_1|$ (a circle inside the disc of analyticity at the origin). The integrand $f(z)z^{-n-1}$ is meromorphic in $|z| \le R$ with poles at $0$ (from $z^{-n-1}$, with residue $[z^n]f$ — but that pole is *inside* the small circle and already accounted as the integral itself) and at $s_1, \dots, s_p$. Deform the contour from $|z| = r$ outward to $|z| = R$. By the residue theorem of [06.01.03], the two circular integrals differ by $2\pi i$ times the sum of residues of $f(z)z^{-n-1}$ at the poles crossed, namely $s_1, \dots, s_p$:
$$
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z|=R}\frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}}\,dz - \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z|=r}\frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}}\,dz = \sum_{k=1}^{p}\mathrm{Res}\Big(\frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}};\, s_k\Big).
$$
Rearranging and using the inner integral $= [z^n]f$ gives the stated identity. For the remainder, $f$ is continuous hence bounded on the compact circle $|z| = R$, say $|f| \le M$; then $\big|\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z|=R}\frac{f}{z^{n+1}}\big| \le \frac{1}{2\pi}\cdot 2\pi R\cdot \frac{M}{R^{n+1}} = M R^{-n}$, which is $O(R^{-n})$.

For the asymptotic form, compute the dominant residue. Near $z = \rho$ write $f(z) = \frac{c_{-m}}{(z-\rho)^m} + \cdots$; then $\frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}}$ has the residue at $\rho$ equal to the coefficient of $(z-\rho)^{-1}$ in $\frac{c_{-m}}{(z-\rho)^m}z^{-n-1}$ plus lower-order pole contributions. Expanding $z^{-n-1} = \rho^{-n-1}\big(1 + \frac{z-\rho}{\rho}\big)^{-n-1}$ in powers of $(z-\rho)$ and extracting the $(z-\rho)^{m-1}$ coefficient gives
$$
\mathrm{Res}\Big(\frac{f}{z^{n+1}}; \rho\Big) = c_{-m}\,\rho^{-n-1}\binom{-n-1}{m-1}\rho^{-(m-1)} = c_{-m}\frac{(-1)^{m-1}}{\rho^{n+m}}\binom{n+m-1}{m-1},
$$
using $\binom{-n-1}{m-1} = (-1)^{m-1}\binom{n+m-1}{m-1}$. Since $[z^n]f = -\sum_k \mathrm{Res} + O(R^{-n})$ and the subdominant residues are each $O(|s_k|^{-n})$ with $|s_k| > \rho$, the dominant term is $-\mathrm{Res}(\cdot;\rho) = c_{-m}\frac{(-1)^{m}}{\rho^{n+m}}\binom{n+m-1}{m-1}$, and $\binom{n+m-1}{m-1} \sim \frac{n^{m-1}}{(m-1)!}$ gives the claimed $n^{m-1}\rho^{-n}$ form. $\square$

**Corollary (simple dominant pole).** If $\rho$ is a simple dominant pole with $f(z) \sim \frac{c}{1 - z/\rho}$ near $\rho$ (equivalently residue of $f$ at $\rho$ equal to $-c\rho$), then $[z^n]f \sim c\,\rho^{-n}$. For compositions $\frac{1-z}{1-2z}$ the pole is at $\rho = \frac12$, simple, with $f(z) = \frac{1-z}{1-2z}$ and local form $\frac{c}{1 - 2z}$, $c = \frac12$, recovering $[z^n]f = 2^{n-1}$ exactly for $n \ge 1$.

**Bridge.** This theorem is the foundational reason rational and meromorphic enumeration are governed by a finite amount of local data: the entire infinite sequence $[z^n]f$ is captured, up to an exponentially small error, by the residues of $f(z)z^{-n-1}$ at finitely many poles, and the dominant pole's order and residue alone fix the leading asymptotic. This is exactly the analytic completion of the partial-fraction reading of [40.01.04], where a rational $f = N/D$ was expanded as $\sum_j c_j (1 - \gamma_j z)^{-r_j}$ and its coefficients read as $\sum_j p_j(n)\gamma_j^n$; the residue at each reciprocal root $\gamma_j^{-1}$ is precisely the partial-fraction coefficient, so the residue sum *is* the partial-fraction sum, and the dominant root $\gamma$ with largest modulus is the reciprocal of the nearest pole, the central insight tying spectral radius to growth rate. The simple-pole corollary builds toward the supercritical-sequence schema previewed in [40.08.01], where $\frac{1}{1 - A(z)}$ acquires a simple pole at the root of $A(\sigma) = 1$, and it appears again in [40.08.04] as the rational/meromorphic base case of the singularity-analysis transfer, where algebraic and logarithmic singularities replace poles. Putting these together, the residue expansion generalises the dominant-eigenvalue asymptotics of the transfer matrix of [40.01.04]: the largest eigenvalue is the reciprocal of the nearest pole of $\det(I - zA)^{-1}$, so meromorphic coefficient asymptotics and Perron-Frobenius are the same statement read analytically.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove Pringsheim's theorem for the meromorphic case relevant here: if $f(z) = \sum_n f_n z^n$ has all $f_n \ge 0$ and radius of convergence $\rho \in (0, \infty)$, then $z = \rho$ is a singularity of $f$. Deduce that the dominant singularity of a combinatorial OGF can be sought on the positive real axis.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Suppose $f$ were analytic at $\rho$; then it has a Taylor expansion at the interior point $z_0 = \rho/2$ with radius $> \rho/2$. Bound the coefficients of that expansion using $f_n \ge 0$ to extend convergence past $|z| = \rho$, contradicting the definition of $\rho$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Assume for contradiction that $f$ is analytic at $z = \rho$. Then $f$ is analytic on a disc $|z - \rho| < \delta$ for some $\delta > 0$. Take the interior point $z_0 = \rho/2$; the Taylor series of $f$ at $z_0$, $f(z) = \sum_k \frac{f^{(k)}(z_0)}{k!}(z - z_0)^k$, has radius of convergence at least $\mathrm{dist}(z_0, \text{nearest singularity})$. The derivatives are $f^{(k)}(z_0) = \sum_{n \ge k} f_n \frac{n!}{(n-k)!}z_0^{n-k} \ge 0$ since every $f_n \ge 0$ and $z_0 > 0$.

Evaluate the Taylor series at a real point $z = \rho + \eta$ with small $\eta > 0$: every term $\frac{f^{(k)}(z_0)}{k!}(\rho + \eta - z_0)^k$ is nonnegative, and the partial sums are bounded by rearranging the double series $\sum_k \frac{1}{k!}(\rho/2 + \eta)^k \sum_{n\ge k} f_n \binom{n}{k}(\rho/2)^{n-k} = \sum_n f_n (\rho/2 + \eta + \rho/2)^n = \sum_n f_n(\rho + \eta)^n$ (binomial theorem, all terms nonnegative so the interchange is valid). If the Taylor series at $z_0$ converges at $\rho + \eta$ — which it does whenever $\rho/2 + \eta < \delta$, i.e. $\eta$ small, since the disc of analyticity at $z_0$ reaches past $\rho$ — then $\sum_n f_n(\rho + \eta)^n < \infty$, so the original series converges at $z = \rho + \eta > \rho$, contradicting that $\rho$ is the radius of convergence. Hence $\rho$ is a singularity. For a combinatorial OGF the counts $f_n \ge 0$, so its dominant singularity lies at the positive real $\rho$; one solves for the nearest positive zero of the denominator (rational/meromorphic case) to find it. Rubric: full credit for the nonnegativity of the $z_0$-derivatives, the binomial resummation to $\sum f_n(\rho+\eta)^n$, the contradiction with the radius, and the OGF corollary.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

Beyond the single dominant pole, the meromorphic theory extends through the systematic subtraction of singular parts, the treatment of several singularities on the circle of convergence, the supercritical-sequence schema as the canonical simple-pole source, the denumerant / multiple-pole-at-roots-of-unity case, and the analytic recasting of Perron-Frobenius.

**Theorem 1 (full singular expansion by subtraction of singularities).** Let $f$ be meromorphic in $|z| \le R$ with poles $s_1, \dots, s_p$ ordered by increasing modulus, $s_j$ of order $m_j$ and singular part $S_j(z) = \sum_{k=1}^{m_j}c^{(j)}_{-k}(z - s_j)^{-k}$. Then $f - \sum_j S_j$ is analytic in $|z| \le R$, so its coefficients are $O(R^{-n})$, and
$$
[z^n]f = \sum_{j=1}^{p}[z^n]S_j(z) + O(R^{-n}), \qquad [z^n]\frac{c_{-k}}{(s_j - z)^k} = c_{-k}\binom{n+k-1}{k-1}s_j^{-n-k},
$$
giving an asymptotic expansion to any prescribed exponential precision by taking $R$ past successively more poles. Each pole contributes a polynomial-in-$n$ times $s_j^{-n}$, and ordering by $|s_j|$ orders the terms by decreasing exponential weight [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick Ch. IV.5, Odlyzko 1995 §4].

**Theorem 2 (several dominant singularities and periodicity).** If $f$ has $d \ge 2$ poles of equal smallest modulus $\rho$, located at $\rho\,\omega_j$ with $\omega_j$ on the unit circle, the leading coefficient asymptotics is the sum of the $d$ dominant residues, $[z^n]f \sim \rho^{-n}\sum_j c_j \omega_j^{-n}$. When the $\omega_j$ are roots of unity (the typical case for combinatorial OGFs with a periodicity / gcd structure in the allowed sizes), the factor $\sum_j c_j\omega_j^{-n}$ is periodic in $n$, so $[z^n]f$ is a periodic modulation of $\rho^{-n}$; the period is the order of the group generated by the $\omega_j$. A function $f(z) = h(z^d)$ has all its dominant singularities at the $d$-th roots of $\rho^d$, the structural source of period-$d$ coefficients [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick Ch. IV.6].

**Theorem 3 (supercritical sequence, analytic form).** If $F = \mathrm{SEQ}(\mathcal{A})$ with $A(z)$ analytic at $0$, $A(0) = 0$, radius of convergence $r_A > 0$, and $A(z) \to A(r_A^-) > 1$, then $F(z) = \frac{1}{1 - A(z)}$ has a dominant singularity at the unique $\sigma \in (0, r_A)$ with $A(\sigma) = 1$, which is a *simple pole* because $A'(\sigma) > 0$ (positive coefficients). By the simple-pole corollary,
$$
[z^n]F(z) = [z^n]\frac{1}{1 - A(z)} \sim \frac{1}{\sigma A'(\sigma)}\,\sigma^{-n},
$$
the residue $-1/A'(\sigma)$ of $F$ at $\sigma$ producing the clean $\sigma^{-n}$ growth previewed combinatorially in [40.08.01]. The number of $\mathcal{A}$-components in a random size-$n$ object is then asymptotically Gaussian with mean and variance linear in $n$ [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick Ch. V.2].

**Theorem 4 (denumerants and poles at roots of unity).** The denumerant $d(n; a_1, \dots, a_k)$ counting representations $n = a_1 x_1 + \cdots + a_k x_k$ in nonnegative integers has OGF $\prod_{i=1}^{k}\frac{1}{1 - z^{a_i}}$, a rational function all of whose poles lie on the unit circle (at roots of unity), with the pole at $z = 1$ of order $k$ dominant. Hence $d(n; \mathbf{a}) \sim \frac{n^{k-1}}{(k-1)!\,a_1\cdots a_k}$, a quasi-polynomial in $n$ of degree $k - 1$: the order-$k$ pole at $1$ gives the polynomial growth $n^{k-1}$, while the lower-order poles at the other roots of unity contribute the periodic (quasi-polynomial) fluctuations. This is the Sylvester-Schur denumerant, the prototype of Ehrhart quasi-polynomials [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick Ch. IV.6].

**Theorem 5 (analytic Perron-Frobenius).** For a strongly connected weighted digraph with nonnegative transfer matrix $A$, the walk-generating function $[(I - zA)^{-1}]_{ij} = \frac{N_{ij}(z)}{\det(I - zA)}$ of [40.01.04] is rational with denominator $\det(I - zA) = \prod_i(1 - \lambda_i z)$, $\lambda_i$ the eigenvalues of $A$. Its dominant pole is $\rho = 1/\lambda_{\mathrm{PF}}$, the reciprocal of the Perron-Frobenius eigenvalue $\lambda_{\mathrm{PF}} = \max_i|\lambda_i|$, which is simple by Perron-Frobenius; therefore $(A^n)_{ij} \sim c_{ij}\lambda_{\mathrm{PF}}^n$ with $c_{ij}$ built from the left/right Perron eigenvectors. The meromorphic residue at $1/\lambda_{\mathrm{PF}}$ is exactly the Perron projector, so coefficient asymptotics and spectral dominance coincide [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick Ch. V.5].

**Synthesis.** Meromorphic coefficient asymptotics is the foundational reason a generating function's *finite* local data — the location, order, and residue of its dominant pole — determines the *infinite* asymptotic profile of its coefficients. The central insight is that Cauchy's coefficient formula turns coefficient extraction into a contour integral, and pushing that contour outward past the poles, by the residue theorem of [06.01.03], replaces the integral with a finite residue sum plus an exponentially small remainder; the dominant pole's residue is the leading term, and this is exactly the analytic completion of the partial-fraction reading of [40.01.04], where each residue at a reciprocal characteristic root is the partial-fraction coefficient. Pringsheim's theorem is the foundational reason combinatorics is the favourable case: nonnegative counts force the dominant singularity onto the positive real axis, so the growth base $\rho^{-1}$ is found by solving a real equation — $1 - 2z = 0$ for compositions, $A(\sigma) = 1$ for supercritical sequences, $2 - e^z = 0$ for surjections. This is dual to the spectral reading of the transfer matrix: the nearest pole is the reciprocal Perron-Frobenius eigenvalue, so meromorphic asymptotics and Perron-Frobenius dominance are one statement, and the residue at the dominant pole is the Perron projector — a reading that generalises the dominant-eigenvalue growth of [40.01.04].

Putting these together, the first principle (singularity *location* fixes the exponential factor $\rho^{-n}$) is complete for the rational and meromorphic world, where every singularity is a pole and the subexponential factor is the polynomial $n^{m-1}$ of the pole order; the bridge to [40.08.04] is that the *second* principle — singularity *type* fixes the subexponential factor — generalises this to the algebraic and logarithmic singularities ($\sqrt{1 - z/\rho}$, $\log\frac{1}{1-z/\rho}$) where the factor becomes $n^{-3/2}$, $n^{-1/2}$, or $1/\log n$, and the residue calculus is replaced by the Hankel-contour transfer theorems. The recipe-to-series dictionary of [40.08.01] and the series-to-growth reading of this unit are one analytic-combinatorics pipeline.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Cauchy's coefficient formula and the radius as nearest singularity).** *If $f = \sum_n f_n z^n$ is analytic on $|z| < \rho$, then for $0 < r < \rho$, $f_n = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z| = r}\frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}}\,dz$; and $\rho$ equals the distance from $0$ to the nearest singularity of $f$.*

*Proof.* On $|z| = r < \rho$ the series $\sum_m f_m z^m$ converges uniformly, so it may be integrated term by term against $\frac{1}{2\pi i}z^{-n-1}$: $\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint \frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}}dz = \sum_m f_m \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint z^{m-n-1}dz = \sum_m f_m [m = n] = f_n$, using $\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z|=r}z^{k}dz = [k = -1]$. This is the Cauchy integral formula for the $n$-th derivative, [06.01.02]. For the radius statement: $f$ is analytic on $|z| < \rho$ by hypothesis, so the nearest singularity is at distance $\ge \rho$; conversely if $f$ were analytic on an open disc of radius $\rho' > \rho$ about $0$, its Taylor series there would converge on $|z| < \rho'$ and agree with $\sum f_n z^n$, contradicting that $\rho$ is the radius of convergence (Cauchy-Hadamard). Hence a singularity sits on $|z| = \rho$ and none nearer. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (exponential growth formula).** *With $\rho$ the radius of convergence of $f = \sum f_n z^n$, $\limsup_n |f_n|^{1/n} = 1/\rho$, so $f_n = \rho^{-n}\theta(n)$ with $\limsup|\theta(n)|^{1/n} = 1$.*

*Proof.* This is Cauchy-Hadamard: the radius of convergence of a power series is $\rho = 1/\limsup_n|f_n|^{1/n}$. Indeed if $|z| < 1/\limsup|f_n|^{1/n}$ then $|f_n z^n|^{1/n} = |f_n|^{1/n}|z| < 1$ eventually, so $\sum f_n z^n$ converges by the root test; and if $|z| > 1/\limsup|f_n|^{1/n}$ the terms do not tend to $0$. Thus $\limsup|f_n|^{1/n} = 1/\rho$. Setting $\theta(n) = f_n \rho^n$ gives $\limsup|\theta(n)|^{1/n} = \rho\limsup|f_n|^{1/n} = \rho\cdot\rho^{-1} = 1$, the subexponential normalisation. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (residue expansion with remainder).** *Let $f$ be analytic at $0$, meromorphic in $|z| \le R$ with poles $s_1, \dots, s_p$ in $0 < |z| < R$ and none on $|z| = R$. Then $[z^n]f = -\sum_{k}\mathrm{Res}(f(z)z^{-n-1}; s_k) + \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z|=R}f(z)z^{-n-1}dz$, with the last term $O(R^{-n})$.*

*Proof.* The function $\phi(z) = f(z)z^{-n-1}$ is meromorphic in $|z| \le R$ with poles among $\{0, s_1, \dots, s_p\}$. By the residue theorem [06.01.03] applied to the circle $|z| = R$ (which encloses all of them and meets none), $\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z| = R}\phi = \mathrm{Res}(\phi; 0) + \sum_k \mathrm{Res}(\phi; s_k)$. The residue at $0$: near $0$, $f(z) = \sum_m f_m z^m$ is analytic, so $\phi(z) = \sum_m f_m z^{m - n - 1}$ and the coefficient of $z^{-1}$ is $f_n$, giving $\mathrm{Res}(\phi; 0) = f_n = [z^n]f$. Rearranging, $[z^n]f = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z|=R}\phi - \sum_k\mathrm{Res}(\phi; s_k)$. The bound on the boundary integral is $\big|\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z|=R}\phi\big| \le \frac{1}{2\pi}(2\pi R)\sup_{|z|=R}|f(z)|R^{-n-1} = R^{-n}\sup_{|z|=R}|f|$, and $\sup_{|z|=R}|f| < \infty$ by continuity on the compact circle, so the term is $O(R^{-n})$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (order-$m$ pole asymptotic).** *If the dominant pole $\rho > 0$ of $f$ is unique of smallest modulus and has order $m$ with leading Laurent coefficient $c_{-m}$, then $[z^n]f \sim \frac{(-1)^m c_{-m}}{(m-1)!\,\rho^m}\,n^{m-1}\rho^{-n}$.*

*Proof.* By Proposition 3 with $R$ chosen between $\rho$ and the next-smallest $|s_k|$, $[z^n]f = -\mathrm{Res}(f(z)z^{-n-1}; \rho) + O(R^{-n})$ with $R > \rho$ (no other pole is dominant). Near $\rho$, write $f(z) = \frac{c_{-m}}{(z - \rho)^m} + (\text{lower-order poles and analytic})$; the residue of $\frac{c_{-m}}{(z-\rho)^m}z^{-n-1}$ at $\rho$ is the coefficient of $(z - \rho)^{m-1}$ in the Taylor expansion of $c_{-m}z^{-n-1}$ about $\rho$, namely $c_{-m}\frac{1}{(m-1)!}\frac{d^{m-1}}{dz^{m-1}}z^{-n-1}\big|_{z=\rho} = c_{-m}\frac{(-1)^{m-1}(n+1)(n+2)\cdots(n+m-1)}{(m-1)!}\rho^{-n-m}$. The lower-order singular terms $(z-\rho)^{-j}$, $j < m$, contribute residues of order $n^{j-1}\rho^{-n}$, subleading. Thus $-\mathrm{Res} = c_{-m}\frac{(-1)^{m}}{(m-1)!}\binom{n+m-1}{m-1}(m-1)!\,\rho^{-n-m}/(m-1)!$; using $(n+1)\cdots(n+m-1) = \frac{(n+m-1)!}{n!} \sim n^{m-1}$, the leading term is $\frac{(-1)^m c_{-m}}{(m-1)!\,\rho^{m}}n^{m-1}\rho^{-n}$, and the remainder $O(R^{-n})$ with $R > \rho$ is exponentially smaller. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- This unit is the analytic completion of the rational-generating-function and transfer-matrix theory of [40.01.04]: there the closed form $a_n = \sum_i p_i(n)\gamma_i^n$ was obtained algebraically by partial fractions, and here each $\gamma_i = s_i^{-1}$ is the reciprocal of a pole $s_i$, the partial-fraction coefficient is the residue of $f(z)z^{-n-1}$ at that pole, and the dominant root $\gamma$ of largest modulus is the reciprocal of the nearest pole $\rho$. ## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The use of complex analysis to extract the growth of combinatorial sequences begins with the **circle method** and the saddle-point estimates of **Hardy and Ramanujan's 1918** asymptotic for the partition function $p(n)$, and with **Darboux's** late-nineteenth-century method of reading coefficient asymptotics from the singularities of a generating function on its circle of convergence. The clean statement that the radius of convergence is the distance to the nearest singularity, and that nonnegative coefficients force a singularity onto the positive axis, is **Alfred Pringsheim's** theorem (1894), foundational for the favourable combinatorial case [ref: TODO_REF Titchmarsh 1939].

The systematic residue / partial-fraction treatment of rational and meromorphic coefficient asymptotics, with the dominant pole giving $C\rho^{-n}n^{m-1}$ and the subtraction-of-singularities method exposing lower-order terms, was consolidated as the entry point to analytic combinatorics by **Philippe Flajolet and Robert Sedgewick**, whose *Analytic Combinatorics* (2009) [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick Ch. IV] organises it as the rational/meromorphic precursor to the algebraic-singularity transfer theory; **Andrew Odlyzko's 1995** *Handbook of Combinatorics* survey [ref: TODO_REF Odlyzko 1995] gives the parallel systematic account with explicit error bounds. The recognition that the dominant pole of a transfer-matrix generating function is the reciprocal Perron-Frobenius eigenvalue, tying meromorphic asymptotics to the spectral theory of nonnegative matrices, runs back to the statistical-mechanics transfer-matrix tradition of **Kramers and Wannier (1941)** and was made combinatorially explicit in Stanley's enumerative-combinatorics framework and in Flajolet-Sedgewick's Chapter V.
