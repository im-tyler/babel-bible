## Intuition [Beginner]

The chapter so far has counted objects and measured how fast the counts grow. This unit asks a finer question about a single random object. Pick a permutation of $n$ items at random; how many cycles does it have? Pick a set partition at random; how many blocks does it have? These numbers are not fixed — they change from one object to the next. So each one is a quantity that fluctuates, and the question is what its spread looks like across all objects of a given size.

You already know one shape that fluctuating quantities love to take. Add up many small, roughly independent contributions — many coin flips, many dice — and the total piles up into a bell curve, high in the middle and thin at the edges. That is the bell-shaped law that governs averages everywhere. The surprise of this unit is that a counted feature of a random combinatorial object very often follows the same bell-shaped law, even though the object was built by a counting recipe and not by tossing coins.

Why should counting and bell curves meet? Because many combinatorial parameters secretly are sums of many small almost-independent pieces. The number of cycles of a permutation is built up cycle by cycle; the number of blocks of a partition, block by block. Each small piece nudges the total a little, the pieces barely interfere, and the pile-up rule takes over. The bell curve appears for the same underlying reason it appears for coin flips.

The everyday picture is a gumball machine. Each gumball is one object; the number printed on it is the parameter. Most printed numbers cluster near a central value, fewer stray far, and a histogram of the numbers is a smooth hump. This unit explains when that hump is guaranteed to be the bell curve, how wide it is, and how fast it settles into shape.

Read top to bottom: a parameter fluctuates, a marking variable tracks it, a ratio of coefficients reads off its spread, and the pile-up rule makes the spread a bell curve. The worked example carries this through for the simplest case and shows the bell curve emerging by hand.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let us watch the bell curve appear for a quantity you can compute by hand: the number of heads in repeated tosses, which is the model every combinatorial bell-curve law imitates. Take $n = 4$ tosses of a fair coin and let the parameter be the number of heads.

Step 1. List the spread. Across all $16$ equally likely outcomes, the number of heads is $0$ once, $1$ four times, $2$ six times, $3$ four times, $4$ once. So the counts are $1, 4, 6, 4, 1$.

Step 2. Centre it. The average number of heads is $2$ (half of $4$). The counts are symmetric around $2$: the same on each side. The middle value is the tallest, and the bars fall away evenly.

Step 3. Read the width. The spread is measured by a width that for $n$ fair tosses is the square root of $n/4$, here the square root of $1$, which is $1$. So the bulk of the outcomes sits within about one step of the centre $2$, that is between $1$ and $3$.

Step 4. Match the hump. Plot the bars $1, 4, 6, 4, 1$: a clean hump, tallest in the middle, symmetric, thinning at both ends. This is the bell shape in miniature.

Step 5. Grow $n$. For $n = 100$ tosses the centre moves to $50$ and the width to the square root of $25$, which is $5$; the bars from $40$ to $60$ trace a far smoother bell. The shape sharpens into the ideal curve as $n$ grows.

Step 6. Transfer the lesson. A combinatorial parameter that is a pile-up of many small almost-independent contributions copies this behaviour: a centre that grows with size, a width that grows more slowly, and a re-scaled histogram converging to the same bell. The number of cycles of a random permutation does exactly this, with centre and width both growing like the logarithm of $n$.

What this tells us: the bell curve is the universal shape of a pile-up of many small contributions, and a counted feature of a random object inherits it whenever the feature is built that way — centre and width are all you need to locate and size the hump.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a combinatorial class with size function $|\cdot|$ and a **parameter** $\chi : \mathcal{F} \to \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$ (a marker such as the number of cycles, blocks, descents, or parts). The **bivariate generating function** marking $\chi$ is
$$
F(z, u) = \sum_{\omega \in \mathcal{F}} z^{|\omega|}\, u^{\chi(\omega)} = \sum_{n, k} f_{n,k}\, z^n u^k,
$$
where $f_{n,k} = \#\{\omega : |\omega| = n,\ \chi(\omega) = k\}$ (for labelled classes the OGF in $z$ is replaced by the EGF $\sum f_{n,k}\frac{z^n}{n!}u^k$, with $f_n = n!\,[z^n]F(z,1)$). Setting $u = 1$ recovers the univariate counting series $F(z,1) = \sum_n f_n z^n$, whose asymptotics are read by the methods of [40.08.03] and [40.08.06].

**Definition (parameter as a random variable).** Under the **uniform distribution** on the $f_n$ objects of size $n$, the parameter $\chi$ becomes a random variable $X_n$ with
$$
\mathbb{P}(X_n = k) = \frac{f_{n,k}}{f_n} = \frac{[z^n u^k]F(z,u)}{[z^n]F(z,1)}.
$$
Its **probability generating function** (PGF) is the ratio of coefficient extractions in $z$,
$$
p_n(u) = \mathbb{E}\big[u^{X_n}\big] = \sum_k \mathbb{P}(X_n = k)\, u^k = \frac{[z^n]F(z,u)}{[z^n]F(z,1)},
$$
a polynomial in $u$ with $p_n(1) = 1$. The mean and variance are read from $p_n$ by differentiation at $u = 1$:
$$
\mathbb{E}[X_n] = p_n'(1), \qquad \mathrm{Var}(X_n) = p_n''(1) + p_n'(1) - p_n'(1)^2.
$$
The notation $F(z,u)$, $[z^n u^k]$, $p_n(u)$, $X_n$, and the variability functional below are recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

**Definition (quasi-powers form).** A sequence of PGFs $p_n(u)$ has a **quasi-powers expansion** if there are functions $A, B$ analytic at $u = 1$ with $A(1) = B(1) = 1$, a sequence $\lambda_n \to \infty$, and a sequence $\kappa_n \to \infty$ such that
$$
p_n(u) = A(u)\, B(u)^{\lambda_n}\big(1 + O(\kappa_n^{-1})\big)
$$
holds uniformly for $u$ in a fixed complex neighbourhood of $1$. The name records that, to leading order, $p_n(u)$ is a large power $B(u)^{\lambda_n}$ — a "quasi-power" — modulated by the fixed factor $A(u)$. The **variability constant** of $B$ is
$$
\mathfrak{v}(B) = \frac{B''(1)}{B(1)} + \frac{B'(1)}{B(1)} - \left(\frac{B'(1)}{B(1)}\right)^2,
$$
the second derivative of $\log B(e^t)$ at $t = 0$; the **mean constant** is $\mathfrak{m}(B) = B'(1)/B(1) = \frac{d}{dt}\log B(e^t)\big|_{t=0}$.

A pure power $p_n(u) = B(u)^{\lambda_n}$ is the PGF of a sum of $\lambda_n$ independent identically distributed variables each with PGF $B(u)$ (when $\lambda_n$ is an integer); the quasi-powers hypothesis says that $X_n$ behaves, in the bulk, like such a sum of $\lambda_n$ small pieces, which is exactly the pile-up that produces a Gaussian.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"Knowing the mean and variance is the limit law."* The mean $p_n'(1) \sim \lambda_n\mathfrak{m}(B)$ and variance $p_n''(1)+p_n'(1)-p_n'(1)^2 \sim \lambda_n\mathfrak{v}(B)$ fix the centre and width of the hump but not its shape; a sequence can have linear-in-$n$ mean and variance and still converge to a non-Gaussian law. The Gaussian conclusion needs the full quasi-power form uniformly on a *complex* neighbourhood of $u = 1$, not just the first two moments at $u = 1$.

- *"$\lambda_n = n$ always."* The growth rate $\lambda_n$ is the number of small pieces, and it depends on the schema. For a movable pole (meromorphic schema) $\lambda_n = n$, giving mean and variance linear in $n$; for the number of cycles of a permutation the relevant rate is $\lambda_n = H_n \sim \ln n$, giving mean and variance of order $\ln n$. A bounded $\lambda_n$ does not give a Gaussian at all but a discrete (Poisson) limit.

- *"The bivariate singularity is just the univariate one with $u$ along for the ride."* The whole mechanism is that the dominant singularity $\rho(u)$ in $z$ *moves* with $u$. If $\rho(u) \equiv \rho(1)$ were constant in $u$, then $B(u) = \rho(1)/\rho(u) \equiv 1$, the variability would vanish, and there would be no spreading; the Gaussian comes precisely from the curvature of $\log\rho(u)$ at $u = 1$.

- *"A central limit theorem gives the individual probabilities."* The quasi-powers theorem in its basic form is a *central* limit theorem: it controls the distribution function $\mathbb{P}(X_n \le x)$. The *local* limit theorem — convergence of the single probabilities $\mathbb{P}(X_n = k)$ to the Gaussian density — needs an extra condition ruling out oscillation of $|p_n(u)|$ on $|u| = 1$ away from $u = 1$; a parameter supported on an arithmetic progression (e.g. always even) satisfies the central but not the local theorem without rescaling.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem is **Hwang's quasi-powers theorem**: a uniform quasi-power expansion of the PGFs forces a Gaussian limit with explicit mean, variance, and convergence speed. Every Gaussian law in this unit — cycles, descents, components — is one instance, obtained by exhibiting the quasi-power form for that parameter's bivariate generating function [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick §IX.5]. The proof runs the parameter's PGF through the characteristic-function machinery of the central limit theorem of [37.03.01], so this is the point where enumeration borrows the probabilist's continuity theorem.

**Theorem (quasi-powers; Hwang).** *Suppose the PGFs $p_n(u) = \mathbb{E}[u^{X_n}]$ admit a quasi-power expansion*
$$
p_n(u) = A(u)\, B(u)^{\lambda_n}\big(1 + O(\kappa_n^{-1})\big), \qquad \kappa_n \to \infty,
$$
*uniformly for $u$ in a fixed complex neighbourhood of $u = 1$, with $A, B$ analytic there, $A(1) = B(1) = 1$, $\lambda_n \to \infty$, and variability $\mathfrak{v}(B) \neq 0$. Then $\mathbb{E}[X_n] = \lambda_n\mathfrak{m}(B) + O(1)$, $\mathrm{Var}(X_n) = \lambda_n\mathfrak{v}(B) + O(1)$, and the standardised variable converges to the standard normal:*
$$
\frac{X_n - \lambda_n\mathfrak{m}(B)}{\sqrt{\lambda_n\,\mathfrak{v}(B)}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1),
$$
*with Berry-Esseen speed $\sup_x\big|\mathbb{P}(X_n \le \mathbb{E}[X_n] + x\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(X_n)}) - \Phi(x)\big| = O\!\big(\kappa_n^{-1} + \lambda_n^{-1/2}\big)$, where $\Phi$ is the standard normal distribution function.*

**Proof.** Write $\mu_n = \lambda_n\mathfrak{m}(B)$ and $\sigma_n^2 = \lambda_n\mathfrak{v}(B)$; the moment claims follow by differentiating $\log p_n(u) = \log A(u) + \lambda_n\log B(u) + O(\kappa_n^{-1})$ at $u = 1$ and using $\mathbb{E}[X_n] = (\log p_n)'(1) + \text{(lower)}$, $\mathrm{Var}(X_n) = (\log p_n)''(1) + (\log p_n)'(1)$, the cumulant reading of the PGF.

For the limit law, evaluate the PGF on the unit circle at $u = e^{it/\sigma_n}$, which produces the characteristic function of the standardised variable: with $Y_n = (X_n - \mu_n)/\sigma_n$,

$$
\mathbb{E}\big[e^{it Y_n}\big] = e^{-it\mu_n/\sigma_n}\, p_n\!\big(e^{it/\sigma_n}\big).
$$

Fix $t$. As $n \to \infty$, $e^{it/\sigma_n} \to 1$, so $u = e^{it/\sigma_n}$ lies in the neighbourhood where the expansion holds for all large $n$, and

$$
\log p_n\!\big(e^{it/\sigma_n}\big) = \log A\big(e^{it/\sigma_n}\big) + \lambda_n \log B\big(e^{it/\sigma_n}\big) + O(\kappa_n^{-1}).
$$

The first term is $O(1/\sigma_n) \to 0$ since $A(1) = 1$ and $A$ is analytic. Expand $\log B(e^s)$ at $s = 0$: with $g(s) := \log B(e^s)$ one has $g(0) = 0$, $g'(0) = \mathfrak{m}(B)$, $g''(0) = \mathfrak{v}(B)$, so $g(s) = \mathfrak{m}(B)\,s + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathfrak{v}(B)\,s^2 + O(s^3)$. Substituting $s = it/\sigma_n$,

$$
\lambda_n\log B\big(e^{it/\sigma_n}\big) = \lambda_n\Big(\mathfrak{m}(B)\frac{it}{\sigma_n} - \frac{1}{2}\mathfrak{v}(B)\frac{t^2}{\sigma_n^2} + O\big(\sigma_n^{-3}\big)\Big) = \frac{it\mu_n}{\sigma_n} - \frac{t^2}{2} + O\big(\lambda_n^{-1/2}\big),
$$

using $\lambda_n\mathfrak{m}(B) = \mu_n$ and $\lambda_n\mathfrak{v}(B) = \sigma_n^2$, and $\lambda_n/\sigma_n^3 = O(\lambda_n^{-1/2})$. Hence

$$
\log \mathbb{E}\big[e^{itY_n}\big] = -\frac{it\mu_n}{\sigma_n} + \frac{it\mu_n}{\sigma_n} - \frac{t^2}{2} + O\big(\kappa_n^{-1} + \lambda_n^{-1/2}\big) \longrightarrow -\frac{t^2}{2},
$$

the linear terms cancelling by the choice of centring. Thus $\mathbb{E}[e^{itY_n}] \to e^{-t^2/2}$ pointwise in $t$, the characteristic function of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$; by Lévy's continuity theorem [37.03.01], $Y_n \xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. The rate follows from the Berry-Esseen inequality: the explicit error $O(\kappa_n^{-1}+\lambda_n^{-1/2})$ in $\log\mathbb{E}[e^{itY_n}]$, uniform for $|t| \le c\sigma_n$, bounds the characteristic-function difference $|\mathbb{E}[e^{itY_n}] - e^{-t^2/2}|$, and Esseen's smoothing inequality converts this integrated difference into the stated sup-norm bound on the distribution functions. $\square$

**Corollary (mean and variance schema).** When $B(u) = \rho(1)/\rho(u)$ comes from a movable singularity $\rho(u)$ and $\lambda_n = n$, the mean is $-n\,\rho'(1)/\rho(1) + O(1)$ and the variance is $n\big(-\rho''(1)/\rho(1) - \rho'(1)/\rho(1) + (\rho'(1)/\rho(1))^2\big) + O(1)$, both linear in $n$, recovering Bender's central limit theorem [ref: TODO_REF Bender 1973].

**Bridge.** This theorem builds toward every named limit law in the chapter and appears again in [40.08.04], where the perturbed singularity analysis supplies the uniform expansion of $[z^n]F(z,u)$ that the hypothesis demands. The foundational reason a counting problem produces a probabilist's bell curve is that the quasi-power $B(u)^{\lambda_n}$ is exactly the probability generating function of a sum of $\lambda_n$ independent small pieces, so this is exactly the central limit theorem of [37.03.02] read through a generating function: the marking variable $u = e^{it/\sigma_n}$ turns the PGF into a characteristic function, and Lévy continuity does the rest. The mechanism generalises the saddle-point Gaussian of [40.08.06] — there the integrand collapsed to a Gaussian in the angle $\theta$; here a second Gaussian, in the marking direction $u$, rides on top — and putting these together, the central insight is that the same quadratic expansion of a logarithm that gave Stirling's $\sqrt{2\pi b_n}$ now gives the limiting $e^{-t^2/2}$, one collapse counting size and the other measuring a parameter.

## Advanced results [Master]

The quasi-powers theorem is the analytic kernel; its deployment rests on the two structural mechanisms that generate the quasi-power form from a bivariate generating function — the perturbed meromorphic schema and the perturbed singularity-analysis schema — together with the local-limit refinement, the discrete (Poisson) regime where the hypothesis fails, and the canonical permutation-cycle law as the worked frontier case.

**Theorem 1 (meromorphic schema: movable pole gives a Gaussian).** *Let $F(z,u)$ be analytic in a bidisc around $(0,1)$ such that, for each $u$ in a neighbourhood of $1$, the section $z \mapsto F(z,u)$ is meromorphic with a unique dominant simple pole at $z = \rho(u)$, with $\rho$ analytic, $\rho(1)$ the dominant pole of $F(z,1)$, and the residue analytic and nonzero. Then*
$$
p_n(u) \sim A(u)\,\Big(\frac{\rho(1)}{\rho(u)}\Big)^{n}, \qquad A(u) = \frac{\operatorname{Res}_{z=\rho(u)}F(z,u)/\rho(u)}{\operatorname{Res}_{z=\rho(1)}F(z,1)/\rho(1)},
$$
*a quasi-power with $B(u) = \rho(1)/\rho(u)$, $\lambda_n = n$, $\kappa_n = n$. Hence $X_n$ is asymptotically Gaussian with mean $\sim -n\rho'(1)/\rho(1)$ and variance $\sim n\,\mathfrak{v}(\rho(1)/\rho(\cdot))$, both linear in $n$, whenever the variability is nonzero* [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick §IX.6]. This is the law of the number of components in a supercritical sequence, the number of parts in a composition, and the number of $H$-patterns in random words; the movable pole of [40.08.03] perturbed by the marking variable is the whole content.

**Theorem 2 (singularity-analysis schema: movable algebraic singularity gives a Gaussian).** *Let $F(z,u) \sim c(u)\big(1 - z/\rho(u)\big)^{-\alpha(u)}$ near a movable algebraic singularity $z = \rho(u)$, with $c, \rho, \alpha$ analytic near $u = 1$ and $\rho$ non-constant. By the singularity-analysis transfer theorems (co-produced [40.08.04]), $[z^n]F(z,u) \sim \frac{c(u)}{\Gamma(\alpha(u))}\,n^{\alpha(u)-1}\,\rho(u)^{-n}$ uniformly for $u$ near $1$, so*
$$
p_n(u) \sim \frac{c(u)\,\Gamma(\alpha(1))}{c(1)\,\Gamma(\alpha(u))}\,n^{\alpha(u)-\alpha(1)}\Big(\frac{\rho(1)}{\rho(u)}\Big)^{n}.
$$
*When $\rho$ is non-constant the dominant quasi-power has $B(u) = \rho(1)/\rho(u)$, $\lambda_n = n$; when $\rho \equiv \rho(1)$ but $\alpha$ varies, $p_n(u) \sim \frac{c(u)\Gamma(\alpha(1))}{c(1)\Gamma(\alpha(u))}\big(e^{\alpha(u)}\big)^{\ln n}$ is a quasi-power with $\lambda_n = \ln n$, $B(u) = e^{\alpha(u)-\alpha(1)}$. Either way $X_n$ is asymptotically Gaussian* [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick §IX.7]. The non-constant-$\rho$ case governs the number of leaves and the number of nodes of given degree in simple varieties of random trees and the number of components of random mappings; the constant-$\rho$, varying-$\alpha$ case governs the number of cycles of a permutation ($\alpha(u) = u$, $\rho \equiv 1$, $\lambda_n = \ln n$) and the number of distinct prime factors $\omega(N)$ of a random integer (Erdős-Kac).

**Theorem 3 (central versus local limit law).** *Under the quasi-powers hypothesis, $X_n$ satisfies the central limit theorem (convergence of the distribution function $\mathbb{P}(X_n \le x)$ to $\Phi$). If in addition $|p_n(u)|$ is uniformly small on the arc $\{|u| = 1,\ |\arg u| \ge \delta\}$ for some $\delta > 0$ — equivalently $X_n$ is not concentrated on a proper arithmetic progression — then the local limit law holds:*
$$
\sup_k\left|\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(X_n)}\;\mathbb{P}(X_n = k) - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\exp\!\left(-\frac{(k - \mathbb{E}[X_n])^2}{2\,\mathrm{Var}(X_n)}\right)\right| \to 0.
$$
The local theorem is strictly stronger: it gives the individual probabilities, not merely the cumulative ones, and fails for a parameter supported on, say, the even integers until rescaled by the period [ref: TODO_REF Bender 1973]. The arc condition is the marking-variable analogue of the tail-negligibility hypothesis of the saddle-point method of [40.08.06].

**Theorem 4 (discrete/Poisson regime: when the hypothesis fails).** *If $\lambda_n \to \lambda < \infty$ stays bounded — the parameter is a count of rare events, each occurring with vanishing probability — the quasi-powers hypothesis $\lambda_n \to \infty$ fails and there is no Gaussian. Instead, when $p_n(u) \to e^{\lambda(u-1)}$ pointwise, $X_n$ converges to a Poisson law of mean $\lambda$:* $\mathbb{P}(X_n = k) \to e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k/k!$. The number of fixed points of a random permutation (mean $1$, Poisson$(1)$ in the limit), the number of singleton blocks of a random set partition, and the number of occurrences of a fixed pattern that appears $O(1)$ times are governed by this regime; the boundary between Poisson and Gaussian is exactly $\lambda_n$ bounded versus $\lambda_n \to \infty$ [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick §IX.11]. The Poisson convergence is the method-of-moments / Janson-inequality regime of the probabilistic combinatorics chapter [40.07.07], reached here analytically through the bivariate generating function.

**Theorem 5 (permutation cycles: the Goncharov / Erdős-Turán law).** *Let $X_n$ be the number of cycles of a uniformly random permutation of $[n]$, with PGF $p_n(u) = \binom{n+u-1}{n} = \frac{\Gamma(n+u)}{\Gamma(u)\,n!}$. Then $\mathbb{E}[X_n] = H_n$, $\mathrm{Var}(X_n) = H_n - H_n^{(2)}$, and*
$$
\frac{X_n - \ln n}{\sqrt{\ln n}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1),
$$
*with the local limit law $\mathbb{P}(X_n = k) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\ln n}}\exp(-\frac{(k-\ln n)^2}{2\ln n})$ for $k = \ln n + O(\sqrt{\ln n})$* [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick §IX.9]. The quasi-power is $p_n(u) \sim \frac{1}{\Gamma(u)}n^{u-1} = \frac{1}{\Gamma(u)}\big(e^{u-1}\big)^{\ln n}$, with $A(u) = 1/\Gamma(u)$, $B(u) = e^{u-1}$, $\lambda_n = \ln n$, $\kappa_n = \ln n$; the rate $\lambda_n = \ln n$ rather than $n$ is the signature of the logarithmic-structure (constant $\rho$, varying singular exponent) schema, and the same analysis with $\omega(N) = $ number of distinct primes of $N$ ($\lambda_n = \ln\ln N$) is the Erdős-Kac theorem.

**Synthesis.** The quasi-powers theorem is the foundational reason enumeration meets probability: a counted parameter of a random combinatorial object becomes a probabilist's Gaussian precisely when its probability generating function $p_n(u) = [z^n]F(z,u)/[z^n]F(z,1)$ is, to leading order, a large power $B(u)^{\lambda_n}$ of a fixed analytic function — the generating-function avatar of a sum of $\lambda_n$ independent small pieces. The central insight is that the marking substitution $u = e^{it/\sigma_n}$ turns this PGF into the characteristic function of the standardised variable, the quadratic expansion of $\log B(e^s)$ produces $e^{-t^2/2}$, and Lévy continuity [37.03.01] delivers the normal law with a Berry-Esseen rate; this is exactly the central limit theorem of [37.03.02] read through the bivariate generating function, and it is dual to the univariate saddle-point Gaussian of [40.08.06] — there a Gaussian in the contour angle counts size, here a second Gaussian in the marking direction measures a parameter, and the same logarithm-and-quadratic collapse drives both.

The two mechanisms producing the quasi-power are the two singularity readings of the chapter perturbed by $u$: the movable simple pole $\rho(u)$ of the meromorphic schema [40.08.03], giving $\lambda_n = n$ and laws linear in $n$ (components, descents), and the movable algebraic singularity $\rho(u)$ or movable exponent $\alpha(u)$ of the singularity-analysis schema [40.08.04], giving $\lambda_n = n$ or $\lambda_n = \ln n$ (trees, mappings, cycles). Putting these together, the whole asymptotic landscape of the chapter — meromorphic [40.08.03], singularity analysis [40.08.04], saddle point [40.08.06] — reappears here perturbed by a marking variable, and the same local analysis that read the *size* asymptotics now reads the *distribution* of every parameter the symbolic method can mark; this generalises the chapter's univariate growth analysis into a parametric limit-law machine, and the boundary where $\lambda_n$ stays bounded crosses out of the Gaussian world into the Poisson regime of [40.07.07], so the quasi-powers theorem is the capstone that classifies, by the growth of $\lambda_n$, when a combinatorial parameter is Gaussian, when it is Poisson, and how fast it converges.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (PGF extraction and moments).** *For a parameter $\chi$ with bivariate GF $F(z,u)$ and $f_n = [z^n]F(z,1) > 0$, the PGF of $X_n$ is $p_n(u) = [z^n]F(z,u)/f_n$, a polynomial with $p_n(1) = 1$, $p_n'(1) = \mathbb{E}[X_n]$, and $p_n''(1) + p_n'(1) - p_n'(1)^2 = \mathrm{Var}(X_n)$.*

*Proof.* By definition $[z^n]F(z,u) = \sum_k f_{n,k}u^k$, so $p_n(u) = \sum_k\frac{f_{n,k}}{f_n}u^k = \sum_k\mathbb{P}(X_n=k)u^k = \mathbb{E}[u^{X_n}]$. At $u = 1$, $\sum_k\mathbb{P}(X_n=k) = 1$. Differentiating, $p_n'(u) = \sum_k k\,\mathbb{P}(X_n=k)u^{k-1}$, so $p_n'(1) = \sum_k k\,\mathbb{P}(X_n=k) = \mathbb{E}[X_n]$. Next $p_n''(1) = \sum_k k(k-1)\mathbb{P}(X_n=k) = \mathbb{E}[X_n(X_n-1)] = \mathbb{E}[X_n^2] - \mathbb{E}[X_n]$, so $p_n''(1) + p_n'(1) - p_n'(1)^2 = \mathbb{E}[X_n^2] - \mathbb{E}[X_n] + \mathbb{E}[X_n] - \mathbb{E}[X_n]^2 = \mathrm{Var}(X_n)$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (cumulants of a quasi-power).** *If $p_n(u) = A(u)B(u)^{\lambda_n}(1 + O(\kappa_n^{-1}))$ uniformly near $u = 1$ with $A(1)=B(1)=1$, then $\mathbb{E}[X_n] = \lambda_n\mathfrak{m}(B) + \mathfrak{m}(A) + O(\kappa_n^{-1})$ and $\mathrm{Var}(X_n) = \lambda_n\mathfrak{v}(B) + \mathfrak{v}(A) + O(\kappa_n^{-1})$.*

*Proof.* Let $L_n(t) = \log p_n(e^t) = \log A(e^t) + \lambda_n\log B(e^t) + O(\kappa_n^{-1})$, the cumulant generating function of $X_n$ (the $O$-term analytic in $t$ near $0$, so differentiable term by term by uniform convergence on a complex neighbourhood and Cauchy's estimates). The first cumulant is $L_n'(0) = \mathbb{E}[X_n]$ and the second is $L_n''(0) = \mathrm{Var}(X_n)$. With $g_A(t) = \log A(e^t)$, $g_B(t) = \log B(e^t)$: $g_A(0) = g_B(0) = 0$, $g_B'(0) = \mathfrak{m}(B)$, $g_B''(0) = \mathfrak{v}(B)$ by the definitions, and likewise for $A$. Differentiating $L_n$ at $0$ gives $\mathbb{E}[X_n] = \mathfrak{m}(A) + \lambda_n\mathfrak{m}(B) + O(\kappa_n^{-1})$ and $\mathrm{Var}(X_n) = \mathfrak{v}(A) + \lambda_n\mathfrak{v}(B) + O(\kappa_n^{-1})$. Since $A$ is fixed, $\mathfrak{m}(A), \mathfrak{v}(A) = O(1)$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (quasi-powers central limit theorem).** *Under the hypotheses of Proposition 2 with $\lambda_n\to\infty$ and $\mathfrak{v}(B)\neq 0$, $Y_n := (X_n - \mathbb{E}[X_n])/\sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(X_n)} \xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.*

*Proof.* Set $\sigma_n = \sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(X_n)} \sim \sqrt{\lambda_n\mathfrak{v}(B)} \to\infty$. The characteristic function is $\phi_n(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{itY_n}] = e^{-it\mathbb{E}[X_n]/\sigma_n}p_n(e^{it/\sigma_n})$. Fix $t \in \mathbb{R}$. For $n$ large, $it/\sigma_n$ lies in the complex neighbourhood of $0$ where $L_n$ is analytic, so
$$
\log p_n(e^{it/\sigma_n}) = g_A(it/\sigma_n) + \lambda_n g_B(it/\sigma_n) + O(\kappa_n^{-1}).
$$
Taylor-expanding $g_B(s) = \mathfrak{m}(B)s + \tfrac12\mathfrak{v}(B)s^2 + O(s^3)$ at $s = it/\sigma_n$:
$$
\lambda_n g_B(it/\sigma_n) = \lambda_n\mathfrak{m}(B)\frac{it}{\sigma_n} - \lambda_n\mathfrak{v}(B)\frac{t^2}{2\sigma_n^2} + O\!\Big(\frac{\lambda_n}{\sigma_n^3}\Big).
$$
Now $\lambda_n\mathfrak{v}(B)/\sigma_n^2 \to 1$, $\lambda_n/\sigma_n^3 = O(\lambda_n^{-1/2}) \to 0$, and $g_A(it/\sigma_n) = O(1/\sigma_n)\to 0$. The term $\lambda_n\mathfrak{m}(B)(it/\sigma_n)$ combines with $-it\mathbb{E}[X_n]/\sigma_n = -it(\lambda_n\mathfrak{m}(B) + O(1))/\sigma_n$ to leave $O(t/\sigma_n)\to 0$. Hence $\log\phi_n(t) \to -t^2/2$, so $\phi_n(t)\to e^{-t^2/2}$ for every $t$. By Lévy's continuity theorem [37.03.01], pointwise convergence of characteristic functions to the continuous limit $e^{-t^2/2}$ implies $Y_n\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Berry-Esseen rate).** *Under the hypotheses of Proposition 3, $\sup_x|\mathbb{P}(Y_n\le x) - \Phi(x)| = O(\kappa_n^{-1} + \lambda_n^{-1/2})$.*

*Proof.* By Esseen's smoothing inequality, for any $T > 0$,
$$
\sup_x|\mathbb{P}(Y_n\le x) - \Phi(x)| \le \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-T}^{T}\frac{|\phi_n(t) - e^{-t^2/2}|}{|t|}\,dt + \frac{C}{T},
$$
with $C$ absolute. From Proposition 3, on $|t| \le T = c\sigma_n$ the expansion gives $\log\phi_n(t) = -\tfrac{t^2}{2} + R_n(t)$ with $|R_n(t)| \le C'(\kappa_n^{-1} + |t|^3\lambda_n/\sigma_n^3 + |t|/\sigma_n) = O\big((\kappa_n^{-1} + \lambda_n^{-1/2})(1 + |t|^3)\big)$, so $|\phi_n(t) - e^{-t^2/2}| \le e^{-t^2/2}|e^{R_n(t)} - 1| \le C''e^{-t^2/4}(\kappa_n^{-1}+\lambda_n^{-1/2})(1+|t|^3)$ for $|t|$ up to a fixed multiple of $\sigma_n$. Integrating against $1/|t|$ over $\mathbb{R}$ converges and is $O(\kappa_n^{-1}+\lambda_n^{-1/2})$; choosing $T \asymp \sigma_n \asymp \sqrt{\lambda_n}$ makes the $C/T = O(\lambda_n^{-1/2})$ tail subdominant. Summing the two contributions gives the stated rate. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (permutation-cycle Gaussian law).** *The number of cycles $X_n$ of a uniform random permutation satisfies $(X_n - \ln n)/\sqrt{\ln n}\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.*

*Proof.* The cycle-marked EGF is $F(z,u) = (1-z)^{-u}$ (a permutation is a set of cycles, each cycle marked by $u$, and the EGF of cycles is $\log\frac{1}{1-z}$, so the SET gives $\exp(u\log\frac1{1-z}) = (1-z)^{-u}$). Thus $[z^n]F(z,u) = \binom{n+u-1}{n}$ and, since $[z^n]F(z,1) = 1$, $p_n(u) = \binom{n+u-1}{n} = \frac{\Gamma(n+u)}{\Gamma(u)\Gamma(n+1)}$. The Gamma ratio asymptotic $\Gamma(n+u)/\Gamma(n+1)\sim n^{u-1}$ (uniform for $u$ in a neighbourhood of $1$, by Stirling for $\Gamma$) gives $p_n(u)\sim\frac{1}{\Gamma(u)}n^{u-1} = \frac{1}{\Gamma(u)}(e^{u-1})^{\ln n}$, a quasi-power with $A(u) = 1/\Gamma(u)$ ($A(1)=1$), $B(u) = e^{u-1}$ ($B(1)=1$), $\lambda_n = \ln n\to\infty$, $\kappa_n = \ln n$ (the relative error in the Gamma ratio is $O(1/n) = O(e^{-\lambda_n})$, hence $O(\kappa_n^{-1})$). Then $g_B(t) = \log B(e^t) = e^t - 1$, so $\mathfrak{m}(B) = 1$, $\mathfrak{v}(B) = 1 \neq 0$. By Proposition 3, $(X_n - \lambda_n)/\sqrt{\lambda_n} = (X_n - \ln n)/\sqrt{\ln n}\xrightarrow{d}\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, with the exact mean $H_n$ and variance $H_n - H_n^{(2)}$ from Proposition 1 differing from $\ln n$ by $O(1)$, absorbed in the standardisation. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- This unit is the bivariate completion of the meromorphic coefficient asymptotics of [40.08.03]: there a single dominant pole $\rho$ fixed the growth $\rho^{-n}$ of the counts; here the pole *moves* with the marking variable, $\rho = \rho(u)$, and the curvature of $\log\rho(u)$ at $u = 1$ is exactly the variance of the Gaussian limit law. The simple-pole coefficient asymptotic of that unit, applied uniformly in $u$, is the engine of the meromorphic schema (Theorem 1 and Exercise 8), so the supercritical-sequence component count promised at the end of [40.08.03] is delivered here as a central limit theorem.

- The companion unit [40.08.04] on singularity analysis supplies the other mechanism: when the dominant singularity is algebraic, $F(z,u)\sim c(u)(1-z/\rho(u))^{-\alpha(u)}$, the transfer theorems of [40.08.04] give the uniform-in-$u$ coefficient asymptotic that the singularity-analysis schema (Theorem 2) feeds to the quasi-powers theorem; the movable exponent $\alpha(u) = u$ with constant $\rho$ produces the logarithmic rate $\lambda_n = \ln n$ of the permutation-cycle law, and the movable $\rho(u)$ produces the linear-in-$n$ laws of random trees and mappings. This unit and [40.08.04] are co-produced; [40.08.04] is the univariate transfer theory and this unit is its parametric distribution-valued reading.

- The Gaussian conclusion is imported wholesale from the probability chapter: Lévy's continuity theorem [37.03.01] converts pointwise convergence of the PGF-derived characteristic functions into convergence in distribution, and the Lindeberg-Feller central limit theorem [37.03.02] is the prototype that the quasi-power $B(u)^{\lambda_n}$ imitates — a sum of $\lambda_n$ small almost-independent pieces. The Berry-Esseen rate of Proposition 4 is the combinatorial instance of the classical Berry-Esseen bound. The discrete regime of Theorem 4 connects forward to the Poisson-approximation and Janson-inequality methods of the probabilistic combinatorics chapter [40.07.07], reached here analytically through the bivariate generating function.

- The Eulerian-polynomial descent law (Exercise 7) ties this unit to the permutation-statistics unit [40.01.05], whose real-rooted Eulerian polynomials $A_n(u)$ furnish the cleanest quasi-power certificate (a product of independent-bit PGFs); the saddle-point Gaussian of the co-prerequisite [40.08.06] is the univariate shadow of the quasi-powers Gaussian, the angle-direction collapse there mirrored by the marking-direction collapse here, and the Stirling/Laplace quadratic of [06.01.15] underlies both. The chapter's whole asymptotic toolkit — meromorphic, singularity-analysis, saddle-point — reappears here perturbed by a marking variable to yield the limit law of every markable parameter.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The recognition that combinatorial parameters obey limit laws begins with **Goncharov's 1944** determination that the number of cycles of a random permutation is asymptotically normal with mean and variance $\sim\ln n$, and with the parallel number-theoretic discovery by **Erdős and Kac (1940)** that the number of distinct prime factors $\omega(N)$ of a random integer below $x$ is asymptotically normal with mean and variance $\sim\ln\ln x$ — the **Erdős-Kac theorem**, the founding instance of "probabilistic number theory" and a structural twin of the cycle law (both are sums of nearly independent indicators with logarithmic rate). **Erdős and Turán's** 1965-67 papers on the statistical theory of the symmetric group consolidated the cycle and order statistics of random permutations [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick §IX.9].

The analytic derivation of such laws directly from generating functions, rather than by the method of moments, was inaugurated by **Edward Bender's 1973** paper *Central and local limit theorems applied to asymptotic enumeration* [ref: TODO_REF Bender 1973], which showed that a bivariate generating function with a smoothly moving dominant singularity forces a Gaussian limit, and gave the local-limit refinement. **Bender and Richmond**, and later **Michael Drmota** and **Hsien-Kuei Hwang**, extended and quantified the framework. Hwang's 1994 thesis and his 1998 paper [ref: TODO_REF Hwang 1998] abstracted the common hypothesis into the **quasi-powers theorem**: a uniform expansion $p_n(u) = A(u)B(u)^{\lambda_n}(1+O(\kappa_n^{-1}))$ on a complex neighbourhood of $u = 1$ yields the Gaussian law together with a Berry-Esseen convergence rate $O(\kappa_n^{-1}+\lambda_n^{-1/2})$, unifying the meromorphic and singularity-analysis schemata under one analytic criterion. The codification within analytic combinatorics, organising the limit laws by the two singularity mechanisms and the growth of $\lambda_n$, is due to **Philippe Flajolet and Robert Sedgewick**, whose *Analytic Combinatorics* (2009) Chapter IX is the reference treatment [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick Ch. IX].
