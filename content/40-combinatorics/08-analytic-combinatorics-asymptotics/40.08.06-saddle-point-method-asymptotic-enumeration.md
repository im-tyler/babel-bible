## Intuition [Beginner]

Some counting sequences grow so fast that the usual asymptotic tools fail. The number of ways to pair people up, the number of ways to partition a set into groups, the factorial itself — these all outrun any fixed exponential rate. The generating functions that produce them, like the exponential of $z$ or the exponential of "the exponential of $z$ minus one", have no nearest bad point on the boundary to read the growth from. The previous chapters extracted growth rates from a singularity sitting at a fixed distance from the origin. Here there is no such fixed landmark, so a different idea is needed.

That idea starts from a way of pulling a single count back out of a generating function: a coefficient equals an average of the function around a circle, divided by the radius raised to a power. You get to choose the radius of that circle. Pick it too small and the radius-power in the denominator stays large, which inflates your estimate; pick it too large and the function itself blows up in the numerator. Somewhere in between is a best radius that balances these two opposing effects, and at that radius the estimate is sharpest.

The recipe is therefore: find the radius that balances the growing function against the shrinking radius-power, and read the count off there. The balance point is where a certain "tilt" of the function matches the size you are asking about. As the size grows, the best radius moves, tracking it. This balanced-radius method is the saddle-point method, and it is exactly what is needed for fast-growing, landmark-free sequences.

The everyday picture is a mountain pass. To cross a ridge you do not climb to a peak; you walk through the lowest gap, the pass, where the ground curves up on two sides and down on the other two. The best circle threads the function's integral through such a pass, and almost the entire value of the integral comes from the small stretch right at the gap.

|---|---|

Read top to bottom: a count is a loop average, the two effects fight, the best radius settles the fight, and the answer reads off in one term. The worked example below carries this through for the simplest fast-growing function and recovers a famous formula for the factorial.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let us estimate the number $1/n!$ that the function $e^z = 1 + z + \frac{z^2}{2!} + \cdots$ hangs on its size-$n$ peg, using the balance idea, and watch Stirling's formula for $n!$ fall out. We work the case $n = 5$ to keep numbers concrete, then state what the balance gives in general.

Step 1. The growing part is the function value $e^r$ on a circle of radius $r$; the shrinking part is $1$ divided by $r^5$ (the radius-power for size $5$). The estimate to balance is $e^r$ divided by $r^5$.

Step 2. Find the best radius. The function's "tilt" for $e^r$ is just $r$ itself (the rate at which $\ln e^r = r$ climbs, times $r$, is $r$). Setting this tilt equal to the size gives $r = 5$. So the best circle for the size-$5$ peg has radius $5$.

Step 3. Read off the estimate. Plug $r = 5$ into "function over radius-power": $e^5$ divided by $5^5$. That is about $148.4 \div 3125 \approx 0.0475$.

Step 4. Sharpen with the pass width. The gap at the saddle has a spread measured by the square root of $2\pi$ times the size, here $\sqrt{2\pi \cdot 5} \approx 5.60$. Dividing the Step 3 number by this width gives $0.0475 \div 5.60 \approx 0.00848$.

Step 5. Compare. The true value is $1/5! = 1/120 \approx 0.00833$. The estimate $0.00848$ is within about two percent.

Step 6. Read it as Stirling. Undoing the division, $n! \approx (n^n / e^n)\sqrt{2\pi n}$, which for $n = 5$ gives about $118$ against the true $120$. This is Stirling's formula, and the balance produced it.

What this tells us: choosing the radius to balance growth against the radius-power, then dividing by the pass width, turns one loop average into a sharp count — and on the simplest fast-growing function it reproduces Stirling.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $f(z) = \sum_{n \ge 0} a_n z^n$ be a power series with non-negative coefficients and radius of convergence $R \in (0, \infty]$, analytic in the open disc $|z| < R$. The starting point is **Cauchy's coefficient formula**: for any $r$ with $0 < r < R$,
$$
a_n = [z^n] f(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_{|z| = r} \frac{f(z)}{z^{n+1}}\, dz = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \frac{f(re^{i\theta})}{r^n e^{in\theta}}\, d\theta,
$$
the contour being the circle of radius $r$, traversed once counterclockwise [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick §VIII.1]. The integrand is $g(z) = f(z)\,z^{-n-1}$, whose logarithm is $\ln f(z) - (n+1)\ln z$. The **saddle-point method** chooses $r$ so that the circle passes through a saddle point of $g$ on the positive real axis.

**Definition (saddle point / saddle-point equation).** A **saddle point** of $g(z) = f(z)z^{-n-1}$ is a zero of $\frac{d}{dz}\ln g(z)$. On the positive real axis the **saddle-point equation** is
$$
\frac{d}{dr}\Big(\ln f(r) - (n+1)\ln r\Big) = 0 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \frac{r f'(r)}{f(r)} = n+1,
$$
and for asymptotic purposes one uses the simpler $r\,f'(r)/f(r) = n$, defining the **saddle radius** $r = r_n$. The function
$$
\alpha(r) := \frac{r f'(r)}{f(r)} = r\,\frac{d}{dr}\ln f(r)
$$
is the **first log-derivative on the circle** (a tilt or mean-value parameter); for $f$ with positive coefficients $\alpha$ increases from $0$ and the equation $\alpha(r_n) = n$ has a unique positive root for each large $n$.

**Definition (saddle-point variance).** The **second log-derivative on the circle** is
$$
b(r) := r\,\frac{d}{dr}\Big(r\,\frac{f'(r)}{f(r)}\Big) = r\,\alpha'(r) = \frac{d^2}{d\theta^2}\Big[\ln f(re^{i\theta})\Big]_{\theta = 0},
$$
the curvature of $\ln f(re^{i\theta}) - in\theta - n\ln r$ along the circle at $\theta = 0$. It is non-negative for the functions treated here and plays the role of a variance: it is the width of the Gaussian into which the integrand collapses near the saddle. The notation $[z^n]$, $\alpha(r)$, $b(r)$, $r_n$, and the contour integral $\oint_{|z|=r}$ are recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

**Definition (saddle-point estimate).** When the integrand concentrates at $\theta = 0$, the **saddle-point estimate** is
$$
a_n = [z^n] f(z) \sim \frac{f(r_n)}{r_n^{\,n}\,\sqrt{2\pi\, b(r_n)}}, \qquad n \to \infty.
$$
The numerator $f(r_n)/r_n^n = e^{\ln f(r_n) - n\ln r_n}$ is the value of the integrand at the saddle; the factor $(2\pi\, b(r_n))^{-1/2}$ is the Gaussian width of the pass. This is the same Laplace-method reduction that produces Stirling's formula in [06.01.15], here run on the circle rather than the real line.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"Singularity analysis and the saddle-point method are interchangeable."* They are complementary. Singularity analysis reads the growth of $a_n$ off a dominant singularity at a *fixed* finite radius $\rho$, giving $a_n \sim C\rho^{-n} n^{\alpha-1}$. The saddle-point method applies precisely when there is no usable finite singularity: $f$ entire ($R = \infty$, as $e^z$, $e^{e^z-1}$, $e^{z+z^2/2}$) or with a singularity so violent that the transfer theorems fail. The saddle radius $r_n$ then *moves with $n$* instead of sitting at a fixed $\rho$.

- *"The saddle radius is a constant determined by $f$."* It is a function of $n$: $r_n$ solves $\alpha(r_n) = n$, so as $n$ grows the best circle expands. For $e^z$, $r_n = n$; for $e^{z + z^2/2}$, $r_n \sim \sqrt{n}$; for $e^{e^z - 1}$, $r_n e^{r_n} = n$ gives $r_n \sim \ln n - \ln\ln n$. A fixed-radius reading would miss the growth entirely.

- *"The saddle-point bound $a_n \le f(r)/r^n$ already gives the asymptotic."* The bound holds for *every* $r$ and is one-sided; minimising it over $r$ (which lands at the saddle radius) gives the correct exponential order but overestimates by the Gaussian-width factor $\sqrt{2\pi b(r_n)}$. The bound captures $f(r_n)/r_n^n$; the *method* supplies the missing $(2\pi b(r_n))^{-1/2}$ and turns an inequality into an asymptotic equality.

- *"Any function admits the saddle-point estimate."* The Gaussian localisation requires that the contribution away from $\theta = 0$ be negligible and that the local expansion be genuinely quadratic. Hayman's admissibility conditions (capture, divergence, width) are a clean sufficient package; a function like $f(z) = e^z + e^{-z}\cdot(\text{small})$ with competing saddles on the circle, or one whose coefficients oscillate in sign, can violate the concentration hypothesis and fail the estimate.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem is the **basic saddle-point estimate for the Cauchy coefficient integral**: under concentration at the real saddle, the coefficient is the saddle value of the integrand divided by the Gaussian width. Every worked enumeration in this unit — Stirling, involutions, Bell numbers — is one instance, and the admissibility theory of the next section is the certification that the concentration hypothesis holds [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick §VIII.2].

**Theorem (basic saddle-point estimate).** *Let $f(z) = \sum_n a_n z^n$ have non-negative coefficients and radius of convergence $R \in (0,\infty]$. Let $r_n \in (0,R)$ solve the saddle-point equation $\alpha(r_n) = r_n f'(r_n)/f(r_n) = n$, with $b_n = b(r_n) = r_n \alpha'(r_n) \to \infty$. Suppose the integrand concentrates: for a suitable angular range $|\theta| \le \theta_0(n)$ with $\theta_0 \to 0$ and $b_n\theta_0^2 \to \infty$,*

1. *(central regime) $\ln f(r_n e^{i\theta}) = \ln f(r_n) + i n\theta - \tfrac{1}{2} b_n \theta^2 + o(1)$ uniformly for $|\theta| \le \theta_0$, and*
2. *(tail negligibility) $\int_{\theta_0 \le |\theta| \le \pi} |f(r_n e^{i\theta})|\, d\theta = o\!\big(f(r_n)\, \theta_0\big)$,*

*then*
$$
a_n \sim \frac{f(r_n)}{r_n^{\,n}\,\sqrt{2\pi\, b_n}}, \qquad n \to \infty.
$$

**Proof.** Apply Cauchy's formula on the saddle circle $|z| = r_n$:
$$
a_n = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \frac{f(r_n e^{i\theta})}{r_n^{\,n}\, e^{i n \theta}}\, d\theta = \frac{f(r_n)}{2\pi\, r_n^{\,n}}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \exp\!\Big(\ln f(r_n e^{i\theta}) - \ln f(r_n) - i n\theta\Big)\, d\theta.
$$
Split the integral at $\pm\theta_0$. On the central range $|\theta| \le \theta_0$, hypothesis (1) gives the exponent $-\tfrac{1}{2}b_n\theta^2 + o(1)$, the linear term $in\theta$ having cancelled against the saddle choice $\alpha(r_n) = n$ (which is exactly the vanishing of the first-order term in $\theta$: $\frac{d}{d\theta}[\ln f(r_n e^{i\theta})]_{\theta=0} = i\,\alpha(r_n) = in$). Hence the central contribution is
$$
\frac{f(r_n)}{2\pi\, r_n^{\,n}}\int_{-\theta_0}^{\theta_0} e^{-\frac{1}{2}b_n\theta^2}\,(1 + o(1))\, d\theta.
$$
Substitute $u = \theta\sqrt{b_n}$. Because $b_n\theta_0^2 \to \infty$, the limits $\pm\theta_0\sqrt{b_n}$ tend to $\pm\infty$, so $\int_{-\theta_0}^{\theta_0} e^{-\frac12 b_n\theta^2}\,d\theta = b_n^{-1/2}\int_{-\theta_0\sqrt{b_n}}^{\theta_0\sqrt{b_n}} e^{-u^2/2}\,du \to b_n^{-1/2}\sqrt{2\pi}$, the standard Gaussian integral [ref: TODO_REF de Bruijn Ch. 5]. The central contribution is therefore
$$
\frac{f(r_n)}{2\pi\, r_n^{\,n}}\cdot \frac{\sqrt{2\pi}}{\sqrt{b_n}}(1 + o(1)) = \frac{f(r_n)}{r_n^{\,n}\sqrt{2\pi\, b_n}}(1 + o(1)).
$$
By hypothesis (2) the tail $\theta_0 \le |\theta| \le \pi$ contributes $o\big(f(r_n)\,\theta_0/r_n^n\big)$, which is $o\big(f(r_n) (r_n^n\sqrt{2\pi b_n})^{-1}\big)$ since $\theta_0\sqrt{b_n} \to \infty$ forces $\theta_0 \gg b_n^{-1/2}$, making the tail subdominant to the central term. Summing the two ranges gives $a_n \sim f(r_n)/(r_n^n\sqrt{2\pi b_n})$. $\square$

**Corollary (Stirling from $e^z$).** For $f(z) = e^z$ one has $\alpha(r) = r$, so $r_n = n$, and $b(r) = r$, so $b_n = n$. The estimate reads $[z^n]e^z = 1/n! \sim e^n/(n^n\sqrt{2\pi n})$, i.e. $n! \sim n^n e^{-n}\sqrt{2\pi n}$, Stirling's formula. This is the same conclusion derived by the Laplace method on the real Gamma integral in [06.01.15]; here it is the circle-method shadow of that real-line computation.

**Bridge.** This estimate builds toward every fast-growth enumeration in the chapter and appears again in [40.08.07], where the same Gaussian localisation, now carried out with a secondary variable marking a parameter, produces quasi-power limit laws. The foundational reason the method works is that taking logarithms turns the product $f(z)z^{-n-1}$ into a sum whose real part has a strict maximum on the saddle circle exactly at $\theta = 0$, so the integral is a Laplace integral on the circle; this is exactly the steepest-descent picture of [06.01.15] transported from the real axis to the contour, and it generalises Stirling's single computation into a uniform machine. The saddle radius moving with $n$ is dual to the fixed dominant singularity of singularity analysis: putting these together, a fixed singularity gives $\rho^{-n}n^{\alpha-1}$ growth while a moving saddle gives the faster-than-exponential growth of entire generating functions, and the central insight is that both are one Cauchy integral read at the location where the integrand peaks.

## Advanced results [Master]

The basic estimate is the analytic kernel; its systematic deployment rests on the saddle-point bound as a one-sided estimate, Hayman's admissibility theorem certifying the concentration hypothesis once and for all, the closure calculus of H-admissible functions, and the canonical Moser-Wyman analysis of the Bell numbers as the worked frontier case.

**Theorem 1 (saddle-point bound).** *For $f(z) = \sum_n a_n z^n$ with non-negative coefficients and radius of convergence $R$, and every $0 < r < R$,*
$$
a_n \le \frac{f(r)}{r^{n}}, \qquad\text{minimised at } r = r_n \text{ where } \alpha(r_n) = n,
$$
*giving $a_n \le f(r_n)/r_n^n = \exp\!\big(\inf_{0<r<R}[\ln f(r) - n\ln r]\big)$.* The bound is immediate from $a_n r^n \le \sum_m a_m r^m = f(r)$ (all terms non-negative), and it is sharp on the exponential scale: the true coefficient is smaller only by the polynomial Gaussian-width factor $\sqrt{2\pi b_n}$ [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick §VIII.1]. The bound alone fixes the exponential growth rate $\limsup_n a_n^{1/n}$ and is the workhorse for upper estimates where the full asymptotic is not needed.

**Theorem 2 (Hayman's admissibility theorem).** *Call $f$ analytic in $|z| < R$ with positive Taylor coefficients **H-admissible** if, writing $a(r) = r f'(r)/f(r)$ and $b(r) = r a'(r)$, there is a function $\theta_0 : (0,R) \to (0,\pi)$ such that, as $r \to R^-$:*

- *(capture) $b(r) \to \infty$;*
- *(central / divergence) $f(re^{i\theta}) \sim f(r)\,e^{i\theta a(r) - \frac12 \theta^2 b(r)}$ uniformly for $|\theta| \le \theta_0(r)$;*
- *(width / negligibility) $f(re^{i\theta}) = o\!\big(f(r)/\sqrt{b(r)}\big)$ uniformly for $\theta_0(r) \le |\theta| \le \pi$.*

*Then for $n \to \infty$ the saddle-point estimate holds in its sharp form:*
$$
a_n = [z^n]f(z) \sim \frac{f(r_n)}{r_n^{\,n}\sqrt{2\pi\, b(r_n)}}, \qquad \alpha(r_n) = n.
$$
The three conditions are exactly the hypotheses of the Key theorem made into properties of $f$ alone, so that admissibility is checked once and the asymptotic follows for every $n$ [ref: TODO_REF Hayman 1956]. Hayman proved this as a generalisation of Stirling's formula, the case $f = e^z$ being the prototype.

**Theorem 3 (H-admissible function calculus).** *The class of H-admissible functions is closed under the following operations [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick §VIII.4]: if $f, g$ are H-admissible and $P$ is a polynomial with real coefficients and positive leading coefficient, then (i) $e^{f}$ is H-admissible; (ii) $f \cdot g$ is H-admissible; (iii) $f + P$ and $P\cdot f$ are H-admissible; (iv) $e^{P}$ is H-admissible whenever $\sum_{k} (\operatorname{Re} c_k) > 0$ ensures positive coefficients, in particular $e^{P(z)}$ for $P$ with non-negative coefficients and $\deg P \ge 1$; (v) if $f$ is H-admissible and $g$ has positive coefficients with $g = o(f)$ in the relevant sense, then $f + g$ is H-admissible.* The rules certify $e^z$, $e^{z + z^2/2}$ (involutions), $e^{e^z - 1}$ (Bell numbers), $e^{z/(1-z)}$ (fragmented permutations from [40.08.02]), and more, by composition rather than by re-checking the contour integral. The closure under $f \mapsto e^f$ is the engine: each application of $\exp$ to an admissible exponent stays admissible, which is why "exponential of a generating function" — the SET construction of [40.08.02] — is the natural saddle-point input.

**Theorem 4 (Moser-Wyman asymptotics for Bell numbers).** *The Bell numbers $B_n = n!\,[z^n]e^{e^z - 1}$ satisfy*
$$
B_n \sim \frac{n!\; e^{e^{r_n} - 1}}{r_n^{\,n}\sqrt{2\pi\, b_n}}, \qquad r_n e^{r_n} = n, \quad b_n = r_n(1 + r_n)e^{r_n} = n(1 + r_n),
$$
*with $r_n = W(n) = \ln n - \ln\ln n + o(1)$ the Lambert-W value; equivalently $B_n \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\,\lambda(n)^{n + 1/2}\,e^{\lambda(n) - n - 1}$ in the Moser-Wyman variable $\lambda(n) = e^{r_n} = n/r_n$* [ref: TODO_REF Moser-Wyman 1955]. The function $e^{e^z - 1}$ is entire, H-admissible by Theorem 3 (it is $e^{f}$ with $f = e^z - 1$ admissible), and the saddle is the doubly-exponential balance point $r e^r = n$ rather than the linear $r = n$ of $e^z$; the Lambert-W saddle is the signature of a doubly-exponential generating function. The same analysis with $e^z - 1$ replaced by $\frac{1}{2}(e^{2z} - 1) + \cdots$ handles the $r$-associated Bell and Stirling families.

**Theorem 5 (saddle-point asymptotics versus singularity analysis: the boundary).** *Let $f$ be entire of finite order $\rho$ and lower order equal to its order (regular growth). Then $[z^n]f$ admits a saddle-point asymptotic, with saddle radius $r_n$ determined by $\alpha(r_n) = n$, and $r_n \to \infty$. By contrast, if $f$ has a finite dominant singularity at $\rho < \infty$ of an integrable-singularity type, $[z^n]f$ is governed by singularity analysis with $r_n \to \rho$ fixed.* The two regimes meet at functions with a finite singularity of *exponential* type (an essential singularity on the boundary, as $e^{1/(1-z)}$ or the partition product near $z = 1$): there the relevant saddle approaches the boundary singularity, $r_n \to \rho^-$, and the analysis is a *boundary* saddle-point method, the analytic precursor of the circle method of [21.16.02] [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick §VIII.5]. The classification by where $r_n$ goes — to $\infty$, to a fixed $\rho$, or to a boundary singularity — organises the whole asymptotic landscape of the chapter.

**Synthesis.** The saddle-point method is the foundational reason that entire and fast-growing generating functions are not beyond asymptotic reach: where singularity analysis reads growth off a fixed singularity, the saddle-point method reads it off a moving circle whose radius $r_n$ is pinned by the balance $\alpha(r_n) = n$ between the function and the radius-power, and at that radius the Cauchy integral collapses to a Gaussian of width $\sqrt{2\pi b_n}$. The central insight is that taking the logarithm of the integrand $f(z)z^{-n-1}$ converts a product into a sum with a strict real-part maximum on the saddle circle, so the contour integral is a Laplace integral on the circle — exactly the steepest-descent computation that gives Stirling in [06.01.15], now run as a uniform machine. This is dual to singularity analysis construction by construction: a fixed singularity gives $\rho^{-n}n^{\alpha-1}$, a moving saddle gives faster-than-exponential growth, and putting these together with Theorem 5 shows the two are one Cauchy integral classified by where its saddle goes — to infinity (entire $f$), to a fixed $\rho$ (polar/algebraic $f$), or to a boundary essential singularity (the circle-method regime of [21.16.02]).

The bridge to enumeration is that Hayman's admissibility makes the concentration hypothesis a property of $f$ closed under $\exp$, sums, and products, so the SET-construction outputs of [40.08.02] — $e^{e^z-1}$ for Bell numbers, $e^{z+z^2/2}$ for involutions, $e^{z/(1-z)}$ for fragmented permutations — are admissible by the calculus and surrender their asymptotics to one formula. This appears again in the quasi-power limit laws of [40.08.07], where the same saddle, perturbed by a marking variable, yields Gaussian fluctuations of combinatorial parameters; the construction calculus, the singularity calculus, and the saddle-point calculus are one analytic combinatorics read at three resolutions.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Cauchy coefficient formula and saddle-point bound).** *For $f(z) = \sum_n a_n z^n$ analytic in $|z| < R$ with $a_n \ge 0$, and any $0 < r < R$: $a_n = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(re^{i\theta})r^{-n}e^{-in\theta}\,d\theta$, and $a_n \le f(r)\,r^{-n}$.*

*Proof.* Analyticity in $|z| < R$ gives the uniformly convergent series $f(re^{i\theta}) = \sum_m a_m r^m e^{im\theta}$ on the circle. Multiply by $r^{-n}e^{-in\theta}$ and integrate over $[-\pi,\pi]$; by orthogonality $\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi e^{i(m-n)\theta}\,d\theta = \delta_{mn}$, term-by-term integration (justified by uniform convergence) leaves only $m = n$, yielding $\frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^\pi f(re^{i\theta})r^{-n}e^{-in\theta}\,d\theta = a_n$. For the bound, $a_n r^n = |a_n r^n| \le \sum_{m} a_m r^m = f(r)$ since all $a_m r^m \ge 0$; divide by $r^n$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (uniqueness and monotonicity of the saddle radius).** *If $f$ has positive coefficients $a_n > 0$ for all $n$ in an unbounded set and $R \in (0,\infty]$, then $\alpha(r) = r f'(r)/f(r)$ is strictly increasing on $(0, R)$ with $\alpha(0^+) = \operatorname{ord}(f) \ge 0$ and $\alpha(R^-) = +\infty$; hence for each sufficiently large $n$ the equation $\alpha(r) = n$ has a unique root $r_n$, and $r_n$ increases to $R$ as $n \to \infty$.*

*Proof.* Write $\alpha(r) = \frac{\sum_m m\, a_m r^m}{\sum_m a_m r^m}$, the mean of $m$ under the probability weights $w_m(r) = a_m r^m/f(r)$. Its derivative is $\alpha'(r) = \frac{1}{r}\operatorname{Var}_{w(r)}(m) = \frac{b(r)}{r}$, the variance of $m$ under $w(r)$ divided by $r$; since $f$ has at least two distinct exponents with positive coefficients, the variance is strictly positive, so $\alpha' > 0$ and $\alpha$ is strictly increasing. As $r \to R^-$ the weights $w_m(r)$ shift mass to arbitrarily large $m$ (the radius of convergence being $R$), so $\alpha(r) = \mathbb{E}_{w(r)}[m] \to \infty$. As $r \to 0^+$, $\alpha(r) \to \operatorname{ord}(f)$, the least exponent. By the intermediate value theorem and strict monotonicity, $\alpha(r) = n$ has the unique root $r_n$ for $n > \operatorname{ord}(f)$, and monotonicity of $\alpha^{-1}$ gives $r_n \uparrow R$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Gaussian localisation; the basic estimate).** *Under the hypotheses of the Key theorem (central quadratic expansion and tail negligibility at the saddle radius $r_n$ with $b_n \to \infty$), $a_n \sim f(r_n)/(r_n^n\sqrt{2\pi b_n})$.*

*Proof.* This is the Key theorem; we record the localisation cleanly. By Proposition 1, $a_n = \frac{f(r_n)}{2\pi r_n^n}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \exp(h_n(\theta))\,d\theta$ with $h_n(\theta) = \ln f(r_n e^{i\theta}) - \ln f(r_n) - in\theta$. At the saddle, $h_n(0) = 0$ and $h_n'(0) = i(\alpha(r_n) - n) = 0$, while $h_n''(0) = -b_n$ (the second log-derivative on the circle, real and negative). Thus $\operatorname{Re} h_n(\theta) = -\tfrac12 b_n\theta^2 + O(b_n\theta^3)$ near $0$. Over $|\theta| \le \theta_0$ with $b_n\theta_0^3 \to 0$ and $b_n\theta_0^2 \to \infty$, $\exp(h_n(\theta)) = \exp(-\tfrac12 b_n\theta^2)(1 + o(1))$, and $\int_{|\theta|\le\theta_0}\exp(-\tfrac12 b_n\theta^2)\,d\theta \to \sqrt{2\pi/b_n}$ by the substitution $u = \theta\sqrt{b_n}$ and dominated convergence against $e^{-u^2/2}$. Over $\theta_0 \le |\theta| \le \pi$, tail negligibility gives a contribution $o(\sqrt{2\pi/b_n})$. Hence $\int_{-\pi}^\pi e^{h_n(\theta)}\,d\theta \sim \sqrt{2\pi/b_n}$ and $a_n \sim \frac{f(r_n)}{2\pi r_n^n}\sqrt{2\pi/b_n} = \frac{f(r_n)}{r_n^n\sqrt{2\pi b_n}}$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Stirling, involutions, Bell numbers from the estimate).** *The estimate yields $n! \sim n^n e^{-n}\sqrt{2\pi n}$; $I_n \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt 2}\,n^{n/2}e^{-n/2 + \sqrt n - 1/4}$ for involution numbers; and $B_n \sim n!\,e^{e^{r_n}-1}/(r_n^n\sqrt{2\pi n(1+r_n)})$ with $r_n e^{r_n} = n$ for Bell numbers.*

*Proof.* For $f = e^z$: $\alpha(r) = r$, $r_n = n$; $b(r) = r$, $b_n = n$. The estimate gives $[z^n]e^z = 1/n! \sim e^n/(n^n\sqrt{2\pi n})$, i.e. $n! \sim n^n e^{-n}\sqrt{2\pi n}$, agreeing with the Laplace-method derivation of [06.01.15]. For $f = e^{z + z^2/2}$: $\alpha(r) = r + r^2$, so $r_n = \sqrt n - \tfrac12 + O(n^{-1/2})$; $b(r) = r + 2r^2$, $b_n \sim 2n$. Then $f(r_n) = e^{r_n + r_n^2/2}$ and $r_n^{-n} = e^{-n\ln r_n}$; substituting $r_n^2 = n - r_n$ and $\ln r_n = \tfrac12\ln n + O(n^{-1/2})$ collects the exponent to $-\tfrac n2\ln n + \tfrac n2 + \sqrt n + O(1)$, and $I_n = n!\,[z^n]f \sim \frac{n!\,e^{r_n + r_n^2/2}}{r_n^n\sqrt{4\pi n}}$ reduces, via Stirling for $n!$, to $\frac{1}{\sqrt2}n^{n/2}e^{-n/2 + \sqrt n - 1/4}$. For $f = e^{e^z - 1}$: $\alpha(r) = re^r$, $r_n = W(n)$; $b(r) = re^r(1 + r) = n(1 + r_n)$; the estimate is the displayed $B_n$ asymptotic. Each is admissible by Theorem 3, so Proposition 3 applies. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (admissibility of $e^z$).** *The function $f(z) = e^z$ is H-admissible, with the saddle-point estimate giving Stirling's formula.*

*Proof.* Here $a(r) = r$, $b(r) = r \to \infty$ as $r \to \infty$, so capture holds. For the central condition take $\theta_0(r) = r^{-2/5}$: then $f(re^{i\theta}) = \exp(re^{i\theta}) = \exp(r\cos\theta + ir\sin\theta)$, and for $|\theta| \le \theta_0$, $r\cos\theta = r - \tfrac{r\theta^2}{2} + O(r\theta^4)$ with $r\theta^4 = O(r^{-3/5}) \to 0$, and $r\sin\theta = r\theta + O(r\theta^3)$ with $r\theta^3 = O(r^{-1/5}) \to 0$, so $f(re^{i\theta}) = f(r)e^{i\theta r - \frac12\theta^2 r}(1 + o(1))$, matching $a(r) = b(r) = r$. For width: on $\theta_0 \le |\theta| \le \pi$, $|f(re^{i\theta})| = e^{r\cos\theta} \le e^{r\cos\theta_0} = e^{r - \frac{r\theta_0^2}{2}(1 + o(1))} = f(r)e^{-\frac12 r^{1/5}(1+o(1))}$, which is $o(f(r)/\sqrt{r}) = o(f(r)/\sqrt{b(r)})$ since $r^{1/5} \gg \ln r$. All three conditions hold, so $e^z$ is H-admissible; the estimate then reads $1/n! \sim e^n/(n^n\sqrt{2\pi n})$, Stirling. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The generating functions this unit extracts are exactly the labelled SET-construction outputs of [40.08.02]: the Bell-number EGF $e^{e^z-1}$ (sets of nonempty blocks), the involution EGF $e^{z + z^2/2}$ (sets of $1$- and $2$-cycles), and the fragmented-permutation EGF $e^{z/(1-z)}$ (sets of nonempty sequences). That unit produces these functions exactly by the symbolic method; this unit reads their large-$n$ coefficient asymptotics off a moving saddle, and the H-admissibility closure under $\exp$ is precisely why "exponential of an admissible exponent" — the SET construction — is the canonical saddle-point input.

- The Laplace-method derivation of Stirling's formula in [06.01.15] is the real-line prototype of this unit's circle computation: there the integral $\Gamma(z+1) = \int_0^\infty e^{z\ln t - t}\,dt$ is localised at its real maximum $t = z$ and reduced to a Gaussian; here the Cauchy integral on the saddle circle is localised at $\theta = 0$ and reduced to the same Gaussian. The $e^z$ base case of the saddle-point method *is* Stirling, and that unit's Stirling asymptotic and Gamma function supply the $n!$ normalisation converting EGF coefficients to counts throughout this unit.

- The partition-function heuristic of Exercise 8 — a real saddle $r_n \to 1^-$ giving the exponential rate $e^{\pi\sqrt{2n/3}}$ — is made rigorous by the Hardy-Ramanujan-Rademacher circle method of [21.16.02]: where the saddle-point method uses the single dominant saddle near $z = 1$, the circle method partitions the unit circle into Farey arcs around every root of unity and sums the modular-transformed contributions, yielding the full convergent Rademacher series. The boundary saddle-point regime of Theorem 5 (where $r_n \to \rho$, an essential singularity on the circle of convergence) is the analytic bridge between the two methods.

- The perturbed saddle, where a secondary variable $u$ marks a combinatorial parameter and the saddle-point analysis is carried out for $f(z, u)$, produces the quasi-power limit laws and Gaussian fluctuations developed in the co-produced [40.08.07]; this unit fixes the one-variable saddle machinery and the H-admissibility calculus that [40.08.07] perturbs to obtain central limit theorems for parameters such as the number of blocks of a random set partition or the number of fixed points of a random involution.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The saddle-point method descends from the **method of steepest descent**, introduced by **Riemann** in unpublished work on hypergeometric functions and developed in print by **Debye (1909)** for the asymptotics of Bessel functions of large order [ref: TODO_REF Flajolet-Sedgewick §VIII]. The idea of deforming a contour through a zero of the derivative of the exponent, along the path where the imaginary part of the exponent is constant so that the modulus decreases as fast as possible, gives the real Laplace integral its complex-analytic generalisation; **Laplace's** own method for real integrals (eighteenth century, in the asymptotics of factorials and probability integrals) is the real-axis special case. The application to *coefficient* extraction — combining steepest descent with Cauchy's formula on a circle whose radius is tuned to the coefficient index — became the standard tool for entire and fast-growing generating functions.

The decisive enumerative results are **Moser and Wyman's 1955** asymptotic formula for the Bell numbers [ref: TODO_REF Moser-Wyman 1955], which fixed the saddle at the Lambert-W point $re^r = n$ and computed the full asymptotic of set-partition counts, and **Walter Hayman's 1956** *A generalisation of Stirling's formula* [ref: TODO_REF Hayman 1956], which isolated the three conditions — capture, central convergence, and width — under which the saddle-point estimate holds, and proved the closure of the admissible class under exponentiation, sums, and products. Hayman's admissibility turned the method from a case-by-case contour computation into a calculus: a function built from $z$ by $\exp$, polynomial multiplication, and addition is admissible without re-examination of the integral, and its coefficients obey $a_n \sim f(r_n)/(r_n^n\sqrt{2\pi b_n})$ at once. The codification within analytic combinatorics, with the saddle-point bound, the basic method, the admissibility theory, and the boundary-regime classification linking to the circle method, is due to **Flajolet and Sedgewick**, whose *Analytic Combinatorics* (2009) Chapter VIII is the reference treatment on which the asymptotic readings of [40.08.02] rest; **de Bruijn's** *Asymptotic Methods in Analysis* (1958) remains the canonical exposition of the underlying steepest-descent technique.
