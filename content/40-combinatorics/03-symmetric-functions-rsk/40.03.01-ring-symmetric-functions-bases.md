## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine a recipe that asks for some ingredients but does not care which jar each one came from. If you swap the salt jar with the sugar jar — relabelling the containers while keeping the contents — the dish is exactly the same. A **symmetric function** is an algebraic expression in several variables with this property: rename the variables in any order and the expression does not change. The expression $x + y$ is symmetric, because $y + x$ is the same thing. So is $xy$. But $x - y$ is not, because swapping $x$ and $y$ flips its sign.

Why care? Many natural quantities are blind to the order of their inputs. The total of a list, the product of a list, the sum of all pairwise products — none of these notice if you shuffle the list first. These order-blind quantities are exactly the symmetric functions, and they turn out to have a rich and tidy structure all their own. They are the right language for any problem where the objects are interchangeable, which in this curriculum means counting problems and the algebra of the symmetric group.

The central discovery of this unit is that there are a few **basic building blocks**, and every symmetric function is built from them in exactly one way. Think of how every whole number factors into primes: once you know the primes, you know all the numbers. Here the role of the primes is played by short families of simple symmetric functions. We will meet four such families in two or three variables, see that they say the same information in different dialects, and learn the recipe for translating between the dialects.

The pay-off is leverage. A messy-looking expression that happens to be symmetric can always be rewritten in terms of the building blocks, where it becomes short and computable. Newton found the first translation rules over three centuries ago, and they are still the workhorse of the subject.

## Visual [Beginner]

The four families of building blocks, shown in just three variables $x, y, z$. Each row is one family; the entries are the first few members. A **partition** — a way of writing a number as a sum of non-increasing pieces — is the address that labels each member.

| family | symbol | first member | second member | what it sums |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| monomial | $m$ | $x + y + z$ | $x^2 + y^2 + z^2$ | all rearrangements of one pattern |
| elementary | $e$ | $x + y + z$ | $xy + xz + yz$ | products of distinct variables |
| complete | $h$ | $x + y + z$ | $x^2{+}y^2{+}z^2{+}xy{+}xz{+}yz$ | all products of a given degree |
| power sum | $p$ | $x + y + z$ | $x^2 + y^2 + z^2$ | each variable raised to a power |

![A panel showing three variables x, y, z as three colored tokens, and four labelled shelves stacked vertically. The top shelf (monomial) shows the single pattern x squared spread over all three tokens as x squared plus y squared plus z squared. The second shelf (elementary) shows pairs of distinct tokens multiplied: xy, xz, yz, summed. The third shelf (complete) shows every degree-two product, the six terms, summed. Notice the first members all agree: $m$ for the pattern "one", $e$ for "one distinct factor", $h$ for "all degree-one products", and $p$ for "first power" are each just $x + y + z$. They part ways at degree two. Reading the table tells you the personality of each family: elementary uses *distinct* variables, complete uses *every* product, power sum keeps the variables *separate*. These different personalities are why translating between the families is the interesting part.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take two variables $x$ and $y$, and let us compute the first members of each family and watch one translation rule appear.

Step 1. Elementary functions. The first is $e_1 = x + y$. The second is $e_2 = xy$, the single product of distinct variables. There is no $e_3$ in two variables, since you cannot pick three distinct ones.

Step 2. Power sums. The first is $p_1 = x + y$, the same as $e_1$. The second is $p_2 = x^2 + y^2$.

Step 3. Complete functions. The first is $h_1 = x + y$. The second is $h_2 = x^2 + xy + y^2$, every product of total degree two.

Step 4. Translate $p_2$ into the elementary dialect. Start from the algebra identity $(x + y)^2 = x^2 + 2xy + y^2$. Rearranged, $x^2 + y^2 = (x + y)^2 - 2xy$. In our names this reads $p_2 = e_1^2 - 2e_2$. We have rewritten a power sum purely in terms of elementary functions, with whole-number coefficients.

Step 5. A second translation, complete from elementary. Note $h_2 = x^2 + xy + y^2 = (x + y)^2 - xy = e_1^2 - e_2$. And reading the complete and elementary first members back, $h_1 = e_1$.

What this tells us: the families are interchangeable. A quantity written in one dialect can always be rewritten in another, and the translation uses only addition, subtraction, and multiplication of the building blocks. The rule $p_2 = e_1^2 - 2e_2$ is the first case of Newton's identities, the systematic translation table this unit is built around.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a commutative base ring, by default $\mathbb{Z}$. For each $n$ let $\Lambda_n = \mathbb{Z}[x_1, \dots, x_n]^{S_n}$ be the ring of polynomials invariant under the $S_n$-action permuting the variables; it is graded by total degree, $\Lambda_n = \bigoplus_{d \ge 0} \Lambda_n^d$. The ring of **symmetric functions** is the inverse limit in the category of graded rings,
$$
\Lambda \;=\; \varprojlim_n \Lambda_n, \qquad \Lambda^d \;=\; \varprojlim_n \Lambda_n^d,
$$
taken degree by degree along the surjections $\rho_n : \Lambda_{n+1} \to \Lambda_n$ that set $x_{n+1} = 0$. An element of $\Lambda^d$ is a compatible family of symmetric polynomials of degree $d$, one in each number of variables, agreeing under the restrictions $\rho_n$; informally it is a "symmetric polynomial in infinitely many variables $x_1, x_2, \dots$" of bounded degree. The notation $\langle a, b\rangle$, $(a;q)_n$, $z_\lambda$, and the bases below are recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

Partitions index everything. A **partition** $\lambda = (\lambda_1 \ge \lambda_2 \ge \cdots \ge \lambda_\ell > 0)$ of $n = |\lambda| = \sum_i \lambda_i$ has length $\ell(\lambda) = \ell$; write $\lambda \vdash n$. For a partition $\lambda$ with $m_i$ parts equal to $i$, set $z_\lambda = \prod_{i \ge 1} i^{m_i}\, m_i!$, the order of the centralizer of a permutation of cycle type $\lambda$ in $S_n$.

**Definition (the four classical bases).** For each partition $\lambda$ define, as elements of $\Lambda$:

- **Monomial** $m_\lambda = \sum x^\alpha$, the sum over all distinct monomials $x^\alpha = x_1^{\alpha_1} x_2^{\alpha_2} \cdots$ whose exponent sequence $\alpha$ is a rearrangement of $\lambda$ (padded by zeros).
- **Elementary** $e_n = m_{(1^n)} = \sum_{i_1 < \cdots < i_n} x_{i_1}\cdots x_{i_n}$ for $n \ge 1$, $e_0 = 1$, and $e_\lambda = e_{\lambda_1} e_{\lambda_2}\cdots$.
- **Complete homogeneous** $h_n = \sum_{|\alpha| = n} x^\alpha = \sum_{\mu \vdash n} m_\mu$ for $n \ge 0$, and $h_\lambda = h_{\lambda_1} h_{\lambda_2}\cdots$.
- **Power sum** $p_n = \sum_i x_i^n = m_{(n)}$ for $n \ge 1$, and $p_\lambda = p_{\lambda_1} p_{\lambda_2}\cdots$.

The set $\{m_\lambda : \lambda \vdash d\}$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $\Lambda^d$ by construction: a symmetric function is determined by its coefficients on the orbit-sums $m_\lambda$. The Schur functions $s_\lambda$, the fifth and most important basis, are defined and developed in [40.03.02]; they will satisfy $s_\lambda = \sum_\mu K_{\lambda\mu} m_\mu$ with Kostka numbers $K_{\lambda\mu}$.

**Generating-function form.** Package the one-row functions into power series in a marker $t$:
$$
E(t) = \sum_{n \ge 0} e_n t^n = \prod_i (1 + x_i t), \qquad
H(t) = \sum_{n \ge 0} h_n t^n = \prod_i (1 - x_i t)^{-1}.
$$
These are the $q$-analogue-free companions of the products met in [40.01.06]: $E(t)$ chooses each variable at most once, $H(t)$ allows repetition. The power-sum series is $P(t) = \sum_{n \ge 1} p_n t^{n-1} = \sum_i \frac{x_i}{1 - x_i t}$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"$\Lambda$ is the ring of symmetric polynomials in some fixed number of variables."* It is not: $\Lambda$ has infinitely many variables, taken as an inverse limit. In $n$ variables the relation $e_{n+1} = 0$ holds, but in $\Lambda$ no $e_k$ vanishes. Working in $\Lambda$ avoids the spurious relations that finitely many variables impose.

- *"The $p_\lambda$ form a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis like the $m_\lambda$, $e_\lambda$, $h_\lambda$."* They form a basis only after tensoring with $\mathbb{Q}$. For instance $h_2 = \tfrac12 p_1^2 + \tfrac12 p_2$ needs the denominator $2$; the power sums generate $\Lambda_\mathbb{Q}$ but not $\Lambda$ over $\mathbb{Z}$.

- *"$\omega$ is some external symmetry one imposes."* It is the ring automorphism forced by $\omega(e_n) = h_n$ once one knows the $e_n$ are algebraically independent generators; its existence is a theorem, not a definition by fiat, and $\omega^2 = \mathrm{id}$ must be proved.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem is that the elementary functions are free polynomial generators of $\Lambda$, and dually so are the complete functions, with the two systems locked together by a single generating-function identity.

**Theorem (fundamental theorem of symmetric functions; the $H E$ relation).** *The ring of symmetric functions is a polynomial ring on the elementary functions,*
$$
\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}[e_1, e_2, e_3, \dots],
$$
*with the $e_n$ algebraically independent over $\mathbb{Z}$; equivalently $\{e_\lambda : \lambda \vdash d\}$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $\Lambda^d$. The complete functions satisfy*
$$
H(t)\,E(-t) = 1, \qquad \text{equivalently} \qquad \sum_{k=0}^{n} (-1)^k e_k\, h_{n-k} = 0 \ \ (n \ge 1),
$$
*and consequently $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}[h_1, h_2, \dots]$ as well, with the $h_n$ algebraically independent. The ring automorphism $\omega : \Lambda \to \Lambda$ determined by $\omega(e_n) = h_n$ is an involution, $\omega^2 = \mathrm{id}$.* [ref: TODO_REF Macdonald §2]

*Proof.* The product expansion gives, directly,
$$
H(t)E(-t) = \prod_i \frac{1}{1 - x_i t}\cdot \prod_i (1 - x_i t) = 1.
$$
Extracting the coefficient of $t^n$ for $n \ge 1$ yields $\sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^k e_k h_{n-k} = 0$. This is a triangular recursion: it expresses $h_n = e_1 h_{n-1} - e_2 h_{n-2} + \cdots + (-1)^{n-1} e_n$ in terms of $e_1, \dots, e_n$ and lower $h$'s, so by induction each $h_n$ lies in $\mathbb{Z}[e_1, \dots, e_n]$; symmetrically each $e_n$ lies in $\mathbb{Z}[h_1, \dots, h_n]$. Hence the two subrings $\mathbb{Z}[e_1, e_2, \dots]$ and $\mathbb{Z}[h_1, h_2, \dots]$ of $\Lambda$ coincide.

To see this common subring is all of $\Lambda$ and that the $e_n$ are free, count in each degree. In $\Lambda^d$ the monomial functions $\{m_\lambda : \lambda \vdash d\}$ are a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis, so $\mathrm{rk}_\mathbb{Z}\,\Lambda^d = p(d)$, the number of partitions of $d$. The products $e_\lambda = e_{\lambda_1}e_{\lambda_2}\cdots$ for $\lambda \vdash d$ also number $p(d)$. Order partitions by dominance and expand: a computation of leading terms gives $e_\lambda = m_{\lambda'} + \sum_{\mu \rhd \lambda'} a_{\lambda\mu} m_\mu$, where $\lambda'$ is the conjugate and the sum runs over partitions strictly dominating $\lambda'$. The transition matrix from $\{e_\lambda\}$ to $\{m_\mu\}$ is therefore unitriangular with respect to dominance (entries in $\mathbb{Z}$, ones on the diagonal after the reordering $\lambda \mapsto \lambda'$), hence invertible over $\mathbb{Z}$. So $\{e_\lambda\}$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $\Lambda^d$, the $e_n$ generate $\Lambda$, and any nonzero polynomial relation among them would make the $p(d)$ products $e_\lambda$ linearly dependent in some degree, contradicting the basis property. Thus the $e_n$ are algebraically independent and $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}[e_1, e_2, \dots]$.

Because $\{e_n\}$ are free generators, the assignment $e_n \mapsto h_n$ extends uniquely to a ring endomorphism $\omega$. Apply $\omega$ to the relation $\sum_k (-1)^k e_k h_{n-k} = 0$: it has the same shape with $e$ and $h$ swapped and the sign pattern preserved, and that swapped relation is the original read backwards, so $\omega(h_n) = e_n$. Therefore $\omega^2$ fixes every generator $e_n$, giving $\omega^2 = \mathrm{id}$, and $\omega$ is an involutive automorphism. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem is the foundational reason symmetric-function identities are *algebra* rather than infinite bookkeeping: once $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}[e_1, e_2, \dots]$ is a free polynomial ring, a symmetric function is a finite expression in the $e_n$ and any identity is a polynomial identity. It builds toward the Schur basis of [40.03.02], where the same unitriangular-against-dominance argument produces the Kostka matrix, and it appears again in the Hall inner product of [40.03.03], under which $\omega$ becomes an isometry. The $HE$ relation is exactly the $q = 1$, infinite-variable shadow of the elementary-versus-complete product identities of [40.01.06]; this is the central insight that the involution $\omega$ — swapping "distinct factors" with "all factors" — is the symmetric-function incarnation of conjugating Young diagrams. Putting these together, the fundamental theorem, the $HE$ relation, and $\omega$ are three views of one fact: $\Lambda$ is a free polynomial ring with a conjugation symmetry, and every later structure on $\Lambda$ is constrained to respect it.

## Advanced results [Master]

The four classical bases assemble into a single graded Hopf algebra, and the transition matrices among them encode the combinatorics that the rest of the chapter exploits.

**Theorem 1 (the five transition systems).** On $\Lambda^n$ the bases $\{m_\lambda\}, \{e_\lambda\}, \{h_\lambda\}, \{p_\lambda\}$, together with the Schur basis $\{s_\lambda\}$ of [40.03.02], are related by transition matrices that are all integral except those involving $\{p_\lambda\}$. Specifically $e_\lambda \to m_\mu$, $h_\lambda \to m_\mu$, and $s_\lambda \to m_\mu$ (the Kostka matrix) are unitriangular and integral with respect to dominance, while $h_n = \sum_{\lambda \vdash n} z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda$ and the inverse $p_n = \sum (\pm) \cdots$ require denominators: $\{p_\lambda\}$ is a $\mathbb{Q}$-basis of $\Lambda_\mathbb{Q}^n$ but not a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $\Lambda^n$ [ref: TODO_REF Macdonald §2]. The arithmetic obstruction is exactly the cycle-centralizer order $z_\lambda$, the same integer that controls conjugacy-class sizes in $S_n$.

**Theorem 2 ($\Lambda$ as a self-dual Hopf algebra).** With coproduct $\Delta(p_n) = p_n \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes p_n$ (the $p_n$ are primitive) and counit the degree-$0$ projection, $\Lambda$ is a graded, connected, commutative and cocommutative Hopf algebra over $\mathbb{Z}$, free as an algebra and cofree in the appropriate sense; it is the universal example among such "positive self-dual Hopf algebras" (Zelevinsky). The antipode is $S = (-1)^{\deg}\,\omega$ on the primitive generators. The involution $\omega$ is the Hopf automorphism exchanging the $e$ and $h$ generators; under the Hall inner product of [40.03.03] it is an isometry, and the self-duality identifies $\Lambda$ with its graded dual via $\langle h_\lambda, m_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$.

**Theorem 3 (Newton's identities, full form).** The power sums and elementary functions satisfy, for all $n \ge 1$,
$$
p_n - e_1 p_{n-1} + e_2 p_{n-2} - \cdots + (-1)^{n-1} e_{n-1} p_1 + (-1)^n n\, e_n = 0,
$$
equivalently the generating-function identity $P(-t) = E'(-t)/E(-t) = \frac{d}{dt}\log E(-t)$. These determine the $p_n$ from the $e_n$ over $\mathbb{Z}$, but inverting to get $e_n$ from $p_1, \dots, p_n$ introduces the denominator $n!$ in general (the Newton-Girard formulas), which is why $\Lambda_\mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}[p_1, p_2, \dots]$ but $\Lambda \ne \mathbb{Z}[p_1, p_2, \dots]$. The same identities, read in a splitting field, are the passage between the coefficients of a polynomial (its $e_k$, up to sign) and its power-sum symmetric functions of the roots — the computational heart of the resolvent method in Galois theory [40.03.06 context].

**Theorem 4 (specialization and principal evaluations).** Setting $x_1 = \cdots = x_n = 1$ and the rest $0$ (the *principal specialization in $n$ variables*) sends $e_k \mapsto \binom{n}{k}$, $h_k \mapsto \binom{n+k-1}{k}$, and $p_k \mapsto n$; the $HE$ relation becomes the Vandermonde-type identity $\sum_k (-1)^k \binom{n}{k}\binom{n + (m-k) - 1}{m-k} = 0$. Replacing the constant specialization by $x_i = q^{i-1}$ recovers the $q$-binomial generating functions of [40.01.06], so the Gaussian binomial $\binom{n}{k}_q$ is a principal specialization of $e_k$, and the partition generating function $\prod (1 - q^i)^{-1}$ is a specialization of $H$.

**Synthesis.** Putting these together, the foundational reason $\Lambda$ is the master ring of algebraic combinatorics is that it is simultaneously a free polynomial ring, a self-dual Hopf algebra, and an inverse limit of invariant rings, and these three faces force every basis to behave. The fundamental theorem makes $\{e_\lambda\}$ and $\{h_\lambda\}$ free bases; the Hopf self-duality of Theorem 2 is exactly the structure that the Hall inner product of [40.03.03] makes metric, and under it $\omega$ is an isometry exchanging the two free bases. The central insight is that conjugation of Young diagrams, the $e$–$h$ swap of $\omega$, and the antipode are one symmetry seen in combinatorics, algebra, and Hopf theory respectively; this is exactly why the Kostka unitriangularity that defines Schur functions in [40.03.02] and the cycle-centralizer denominators $z_\lambda$ that obstruct a $\mathbb{Z}$-structure on $\{p_\lambda\}$ are not accidents but consequences of the dominance order and the conjugacy combinatorics of $S_n$. The bridge is the principal specialization of Theorem 4, which generalises the $q$-binomial calculus of [40.01.06] into all of $\Lambda$ and appears again as the Frobenius characteristic of [40.03.06], where $p_\lambda \mapsto z_\lambda^{-1}$-weighted indicator functions translate the entire ring into the character theory of the symmetric groups. The $HE$ relation is the hinge: read in one variable it is conjugation, read across all degrees it is the antipode, and read on roots of a polynomial it is Newton-Girard.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (the $HE$ recursion and mutual generation).** *For all $n \ge 1$, $\sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^k e_k h_{n-k} = 0$, and consequently $\mathbb{Z}[e_1, e_2, \dots] = \mathbb{Z}[h_1, h_2, \dots]$ inside $\Lambda$.*

*Proof.* In $\Lambda[[t]]$ the products $E(t) = \prod_i(1 + x_i t)$ and $H(t) = \prod_i (1 - x_i t)^{-1}$ satisfy $H(t)E(-t) = \prod_i (1 - x_i t)^{-1}(1 - x_i t) = 1$. Equate coefficients of $t^n$: $\sum_{k=0}^n h_{n-k}(-1)^k e_k = 0$ for $n \ge 1$ (the $n = 0$ coefficient is $1$). Solving for the top complete function, $h_n = \sum_{k=1}^n (-1)^{k-1} e_k h_{n-k}$, expresses $h_n$ over $\mathbb{Z}$ in $e_1, \dots, e_n$ and $h_1, \dots, h_{n-1}$; induction on $n$ (base $h_0 = 1$) places every $h_n \in \mathbb{Z}[e_1, \dots, e_n]$. The relation is symmetric in $e \leftrightarrow h$ (it reads $\sum_k (-1)^k h_k e_{n-k} = 0$ after substituting $t \mapsto -t$ and using $E(t)H(-t) = 1$), so likewise every $e_n \in \mathbb{Z}[h_1, \dots, h_n]$. The two subrings therefore coincide. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (algebraic independence; $\{e_\lambda\}$ is a basis).** *The elementary functions $e_1, e_2, \dots$ are algebraically independent over $\mathbb{Z}$, and $\{e_\lambda : \lambda \vdash n\}$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $\Lambda^n$.*

*Proof.* Fix $n$. Order partitions of $n$ by dominance $\rhd$. Expanding the product $e_\lambda = e_{\lambda_1} e_{\lambda_2}\cdots$ and selecting, in each factor $e_{\lambda_j} = \sum_{i_1 < \cdots < i_{\lambda_j}} x_{i_1}\cdots x_{i_{\lambda_j}}$, the lowest-indexed monomial, the product's exponent vector assembles the columns of heights $\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \dots$ into the conjugate partition $\lambda'$; one checks every monomial of $e_\lambda$ has type $\mu \unrhd \lambda'$. Hence $e_\lambda = m_{\lambda'} + \sum_{\mu \rhd \lambda'} a_{\lambda\mu}\, m_\mu$, $a_{\lambda\mu} \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$. Since $\lambda \mapsto \lambda'$ is a bijection of partitions of $n$, the matrix $A = (a_{\lambda\mu})$ is unitriangular for the dominance order (after this relabelling), with $1$'s on the diagonal. A unitriangular integer matrix is invertible over $\mathbb{Z}$, so $\{e_\lambda\}$ and $\{m_\mu\}$ are related by an integer change of basis; as $\{m_\mu\}$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $\Lambda^n$ (by definition of the monomial functions), so is $\{e_\lambda\}$. Counting: there are $p(n)$ products $e_\lambda$ and $\mathrm{rk}_\mathbb{Z}\,\Lambda^n = p(n)$, so any algebraic relation among the $e_k$ would, in some degree, make the $p(n)$ functions $e_\lambda$ $\mathbb{Z}$-linearly dependent, contradicting the basis property. Therefore the $e_k$ are algebraically independent and $\Lambda = \mathbb{Z}[e_1, e_2, \dots]$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 ($\omega$ is a well-defined involution).** *There is a unique ring automorphism $\omega : \Lambda \to \Lambda$ with $\omega(e_n) = h_n$ for all $n$, and $\omega^2 = \mathrm{id}$; moreover $\omega(p_n) = (-1)^{n-1} p_n$.*

*Proof.* By Proposition 2 the $e_n$ are free polynomial generators, so $e_n \mapsto h_n$ extends uniquely to a ring endomorphism $\omega$. Applying $\omega$ to the $HE$ relation $\sum_k (-1)^k e_k h_{n-k} = 0$ gives $\sum_k (-1)^k h_k\, \omega(h_{n-k}) = 0$; comparing with the symmetric form $\sum_k (-1)^k h_k e_{n-k} = 0$ of Proposition 1 and solving the same triangular system forces $\omega(h_n) = e_n$. Thus $\omega^2(e_n) = \omega(h_n) = e_n$ on generators, so $\omega^2 = \mathrm{id}$ and $\omega$ is an involutive automorphism. For the power sums, recall $h_n = \sum_{\lambda \vdash n} z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda$ (Exercise 6) and the dual $e_n = \sum_{\lambda \vdash n} \varepsilon_\lambda z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda$ with $\varepsilon_\lambda = (-1)^{|\lambda| - \ell(\lambda)} = \prod_i (-1)^{\lambda_i - 1}$, obtained from $E(t) = \exp\big(\sum_n (-1)^{n-1} p_n t^n / n\big)$ (take $\log E(t) = \sum_i \log(1 + x_i t)$). Applying $\omega$ to $e_n$ and matching with $h_n$ shows $\omega$ acts on the $p$-expansion by $p_\lambda \mapsto \varepsilon_\lambda p_\lambda$; on a single $p_n$ this is $\omega(p_n) = (-1)^{n-1} p_n$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Newton's identities).** *For all $n \ge 1$, $\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} (-1)^k e_k\, p_{n-k} + (-1)^n n\, e_n = 0$.*

*Proof.* Differentiate $\log E(t) = \sum_i \log(1 + x_i t)$: $\frac{E'(t)}{E(t)} = \sum_i \frac{x_i}{1 + x_i t} = \sum_i \sum_{m \ge 0} x_i (-x_i t)^m = \sum_{m \ge 0} (-1)^m p_{m+1} t^m$. Hence $E'(t) = E(t)\sum_{m \ge 0}(-1)^m p_{m+1} t^m$. The left side has $t^{n-1}$-coefficient $n\, e_n$; the right side has $t^{n-1}$-coefficient $\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} e_k\,(-1)^{n-1-k} p_{n-k}$. Equate: $n e_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(-1)^{n-1-k} e_k\, p_{n-k}$, which rearranges to $\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}(-1)^k e_k p_{n-k} + (-1)^n n e_n = 0$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The indexing set of every basis in this unit is the partitions of $n$, the combinatorial object whose generating function, conjugation symmetry, and $q$-analogues are developed in [40.01.06]; the dominance order used to prove unitriangularity of the $e_\lambda \to m_\mu$ transition is the same partial order on partitions met there, and the principal specialization $x_i = q^{i-1}$ turns the elementary functions $e_k$ into the Gaussian binomials $\binom{n}{k}_q$ and the complete series $H$ into the partition generating function $\prod(1-q^i)^{-1}$. This unit is the algebraic ring built on top of the partition combinatorics of [40.01.06].

- The Schur basis $s_\lambda$, the fifth and central basis, is constructed in the co-produced unit [40.03.02] as the unitriangular completion $s_\lambda = m_\lambda + \sum_{\mu \lhd \lambda} K_{\lambda\mu} m_\mu$ against the dominance order introduced here, with the bialternant (Jacobi-Trudi) formulas expressing $s_\lambda$ as determinants in the $h$ and $e$ bases of this unit; the involution $\omega$ defined here acts as $\omega(s_\lambda) = s_{\lambda'}$ on Schur functions. The Hall inner product of the co-produced [40.03.03] makes $\{s_\lambda\}$ orthonormal and $\{p_\lambda\}$ orthogonal with $\langle p_\lambda, p_\lambda\rangle = z_\lambda$ — the same $z_\lambda$ that obstructs a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of power sums here — and turns $\omega$ into an isometry.

- The power-sum-to-character dictionary is the Frobenius characteristic of the co-produced [40.03.06]: the map sending the indicator of the conjugacy class of cycle type $\lambda$ in $S_n$ to $z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda$ is a ring isomorphism from the class functions of the symmetric groups onto $\Lambda$, carrying irreducible characters to Schur functions. The cycle-centralizer order $z_\lambda$ appearing in Newton's change of basis is exactly the centralizer order in $S_n$, so the arithmetic of $\{p_\lambda\}$ in this unit is the conjugacy-class arithmetic of the symmetric group; the Young-diagram and hook-length combinatorics that names the irreducibles is set up in the representation-theory units [07.05.02] and [07.05.10] (the Murnaghan-Nakayama rule, which is the power-sum expansion of Schur functions read as a character computation).

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The elementary-symmetric and power-sum functions, and the recursive identities linking them, are due to **Newton**, who recorded them in the *Arithmetica Universalis* (compiled from lectures of the 1670s-80s, published 1707) [ref: TODO_REF Newton 1707] as a method for computing the power sums of the roots of a polynomial from its coefficients without solving it; the formulas were given a determinantal form by **Girard** slightly earlier and are now called the Newton-Girard identities. The fundamental theorem of symmetric functions — that every symmetric polynomial is a unique polynomial in the elementary ones — was the technical engine of **Lagrange's** (1770) and later **Galois's** analysis of solvability, since the coefficients of a polynomial are, up to sign, the elementary symmetric functions of its roots, and Newton's identities convert between coefficient data and root data.

The Schur functions, foreshadowed here and built in [40.03.02], appear first as the antisymmetric-over-Vandermonde quotients in **Cauchy's** 1815 memoir on alternating functions [ref: TODO_REF Cauchy 1815], long before **Schur's** 1901 thesis identified them with the irreducible characters of the general linear group. The reorganization of the whole subject around the inverse-limit ring $\Lambda$, the four bases, the involution $\omega$, and the Hall inner product is largely **Philip Hall's** (1950s, in the context of the Hall algebra of finite abelian $p$-groups) and was codified in **Macdonald's** *Symmetric Functions and Hall Polynomials* (1979; 2nd ed. 1995). The Hopf-algebraic and self-duality structure was isolated by **Zelevinsky** (1981) and by **Geissinger**, placing $\Lambda$ as the universal positive self-dual Hopf algebra and tying its representation-theoretic ubiquity to that universality.
