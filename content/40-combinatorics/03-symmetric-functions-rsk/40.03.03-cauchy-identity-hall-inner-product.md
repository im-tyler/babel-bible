## Intuition [Beginner]

Suppose you have two different rulers for measuring the same family of objects. One ruler reports how many of each basic pattern an object contains; the other reports the patterns directly. If the two rulers are matched correctly, then measuring one ruler's marks against the other's gives a clean answer: a basic mark on one side lines up with exactly its partner on the other side, and with nothing else. Two families of building blocks that match this cleanly are called **dual** to each other. The whole point of this unit is one such matching, and a single product formula that detects when any matching is correct.

Why bother? In the previous units you met several families of symmetric expressions — the monomial family, the complete family, and the all-important Schur family. They all describe the same objects, but each emphasises something different. To compare them you need a way to "multiply two expressions and read off a number," much like measuring the angle between two arrows by a dot product. That measuring device is the **Hall inner product**. It is set up so the complete family and the monomial family are exactly dual: pairing one against the other returns one or zero, never anything messy.

The payoff is a single miracle. The Schur family turns out to be dual to *itself*: pairing two Schur expressions gives one when they are the same shape and zero otherwise. So the Schur functions are like a set of arrows all at right angles and all of unit length — an orthonormal system. That fact, proved here, is the keystone that makes Schur functions the natural alphabet of the subject. There is also a compact way to package the whole matching: one product formula, built by multiplying small fractions across every pairing of an $x$-variable with a $y$-variable, that detects exactly when two families fit together.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let us watch the matching work in the smallest interesting degree, degree two, using the two-variable building blocks from the previous units.

Step 1. List the degree-two pieces. There are two partitions of $2$, namely $(2)$ and $(1,1)$. The monomial pieces are $m_{(2)} = x_1^2 + x_2^2$ and $m_{(1,1)} = x_1 x_2$. The complete pieces are $h_{(2)} = h_2 = x_1^2 + x_1 x_2 + x_2^2$ and $h_{(1,1)} = h_1 h_1 = (x_1 + x_2)^2 = x_1^2 + 2 x_1 x_2 + x_2^2$.

Step 2. State the matching rule. The Hall pairing is built so that $h$ paired with $m$ gives $1$ when the labels match and $0$ otherwise. So we expect $\langle h_{(2)}, m_{(2)}\rangle = 1$, $\langle h_{(2)}, m_{(1,1)}\rangle = 0$, $\langle h_{(1,1)}, m_{(1,1)}\rangle = 1$, and $\langle h_{(1,1)}, m_{(2)}\rangle = 0$.

Step 3. Check it against the Schur pieces. The Schur functions in degree two are $s_{(2)} = h_2 = m_{(2)} + m_{(1,1)}$ and $s_{(1,1)} = e_2 = m_{(1,1)}$. Pair $s_{(2)}$ with $s_{(2)}$: write each Schur function in the complete-and-monomial language and use the matching rule from Step 2.

Step 4. Compute the keystone pairing. We have $s_{(2)} = h_2$, and $s_{(2)} = m_{(2)} + m_{(1,1)}$. Pairing the $h$ form against the $m$ form, $\langle h_2, m_{(2)} + m_{(1,1)}\rangle = \langle h_2, m_{(2)}\rangle + \langle h_2, m_{(1,1)}\rangle = 1 + 0 = 1$. So $\langle s_{(2)}, s_{(2)}\rangle = 1$.

Step 5. Confirm the off-diagonal. Now $s_{(1,1)} = e_2 = m_{(1,1)}$, and one can write $s_{(2)} = h_2$. Pair them: $\langle h_2, m_{(1,1)}\rangle = 0$. So $\langle s_{(2)}, s_{(1,1)}\rangle = 0$.

What this tells us: the Schur functions of the two different shapes are at right angles, and each has unit length. The matching rule for the complete and monomial families, applied carefully, forces the Schur family to be orthonormal. That self-matching is the heart of the unit.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Work in the ring $\Lambda$ of symmetric functions over $\mathbb{Z}$ (or $\mathbb{Q}$ where denominators occur), with the monomial $m_\lambda$, elementary $e_\lambda$, complete $h_\lambda$, power-sum $p_\lambda$, and Schur $s_\lambda$ bases of [40.03.01] and [40.03.02], the involution $\omega$ with $\omega(e_n) = h_n$, $\omega(s_\lambda) = s_{\lambda'}$, the conjugate $\lambda'$, and $z_\lambda = \prod_{i\ge 1} i^{m_i} m_i!$ where $m_i$ is the multiplicity of $i$ in $\lambda$. All notation is recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`. Use two disjoint variable alphabets $x = (x_1, x_2, \dots)$ and $y = (y_1, y_2, \dots)$.

**Definition (Hall inner product).** The **Hall inner product** is the $\mathbb{Z}$-bilinear symmetric form $\langle\, \cdot\, , \cdot\, \rangle : \Lambda \times \Lambda \to \mathbb{Z}$ characterised by declaring the complete and monomial bases dual,
$$
\langle h_\lambda, m_\mu\rangle \;=\; \delta_{\lambda\mu} \qquad (\lambda, \mu \vdash n,\ n \ge 0).
$$
The form is graded: $\langle f, g\rangle = 0$ when $f \in \Lambda^d$, $g \in \Lambda^e$ with $d \ne e$. Its existence and symmetry are part of the Key theorem.

**Definition (dual bases).** Two homogeneous bases $\{u_\lambda\}$ and $\{v_\lambda\}$ of $\Lambda$, indexed by partitions and graded so $u_\lambda, v_\lambda \in \Lambda^{|\lambda|}$, are **dual** (or **adjoint**) with respect to the Hall inner product when $\langle u_\lambda, v_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$. By definition $\{h_\lambda\}$ and $\{m_\mu\}$ are dual; the Key theorem shows $\{p_\lambda\}$ is self-dual up to the scalars $z_\lambda$ and $\{s_\lambda\}$ is exactly self-dual.

**Definition (the Cauchy kernel).** The **Cauchy kernel** in the two alphabets is the formal product
$$
\Omega(x, y) \;=\; \prod_{i, j} \frac{1}{1 - x_i y_j} \;=\; \sum_{\lambda} h_\lambda(x)\, m_\lambda(y) \;\in\; \widehat{\Lambda(x) \otimes \Lambda(y)},
$$
an element of the degree-completed tensor product, the last equality recorded below. The **dual Cauchy kernel** is $\Omega'(x,y) = \prod_{i,j}(1 + x_i y_j) = \sum_\lambda e_\lambda(x)\, m_\lambda(y)$.

**Definition (the skewing operator).** For $f \in \Lambda$, the **skewing operator** $f^\perp : \Lambda \to \Lambda$ is the adjoint of multiplication by $f$: the unique additive map with
$$
\langle f g,\, h\rangle \;=\; \langle g,\, f^\perp h\rangle \qquad (g, h \in \Lambda).
$$
On the Schur basis it acts by $s_\lambda^\perp s_\mu = s_{\mu/\lambda}$, the skew Schur function of [40.03.02] (with $s_{\mu/\lambda} = 0$ unless $\lambda \subseteq \mu$); this identity is established in the Full proof set.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"The $p_\lambda$ are orthonormal like the $s_\lambda$."* They are orthogonal but not normal: $\langle p_\lambda, p_\mu\rangle = z_\lambda\,\delta_{\lambda\mu}$, and $z_\lambda > 1$ except for $\lambda = (1^n)$. The normalised family $\{z_\lambda^{-1/2} p_\lambda\}$ is orthonormal only over a ring containing the square roots, so the $p_\lambda$ are a $\mathbb{Q}$-orthogonal basis, not a $\mathbb{Z}$-orthonormal one.

- *"Dual to $\{h_\lambda\}$ means $\{e_\lambda\}$."* The dual of $\{h_\lambda\}$ is $\{m_\mu\}$, not $\{e_\mu\}$. The elementary functions are dual to the *forgotten* basis $\{f_\mu\} = \{\omega m_\mu\}$, since $\langle e_\lambda, \omega m_\mu\rangle = \langle \omega h_\lambda, \omega m_\mu\rangle = \langle h_\lambda, m_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$ once $\omega$ is known to be an isometry.

- *"The Cauchy product is symmetric in $x$ and $y$ term by term in every expansion."* The kernel $\Omega(x,y)$ is symmetric under swapping the two alphabets, but its expansion $\sum_\lambda h_\lambda(x) m_\lambda(y)$ is not visibly so; the symmetry surfaces only in the Schur form $\sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y)$, where both alphabets carry the same basis.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem is the dual-bases characterisation: a single product formula detects duality, and applied to the Schur basis it yields the keystone orthonormality.

**Theorem (dual bases via the Cauchy kernel; Schur orthonormality).** *Let $\{u_\lambda\}, \{v_\lambda\}$ be homogeneous $\mathbb{Q}$-bases of $\Lambda_\mathbb{Q}$ indexed by partitions with $u_\lambda, v_\lambda \in \Lambda^{|\lambda|}$. Then*
$$
\langle u_\lambda, v_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu} \ \text{ for all } \lambda, \mu \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad \sum_{\lambda} u_\lambda(x)\, v_\lambda(y) = \prod_{i,j} \frac{1}{1 - x_i y_j}.
$$
*Consequently the Hall inner product is symmetric and well defined, $\langle p_\lambda, p_\mu\rangle = z_\lambda\,\delta_{\lambda\mu}$, the Cauchy identity holds in its three forms*
$$
\prod_{i,j}\frac{1}{1 - x_i y_j} = \sum_\lambda h_\lambda(x) m_\lambda(y) = \sum_\lambda z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda(x) p_\lambda(y) = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y),
$$
*and the Schur functions are orthonormal, $\langle s_\lambda, s_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$.* [ref: TODO_REF Stanley §7.12]

*Proof.* Fix the degree-$n$ graded pieces; everything is finite-dimensional there. Write $A = (\langle u_\lambda, v_\mu\rangle)_{\lambda,\mu \vdash n}$. Expand each basis against the dual pair $\{h_\lambda\}, \{m_\mu\}$: put $u_\lambda = \sum_\rho a_{\lambda\rho} h_\rho$ and $v_\mu = \sum_\sigma b_{\mu\sigma} m_\sigma$, so that $\langle u_\lambda, v_\mu\rangle = \sum_\rho a_{\lambda\rho} b_{\mu\rho}$, i.e. $A = a\, b^{\mathsf T}$ in matrix form. On the generating-function side,
$$
\sum_\lambda u_\lambda(x) v_\lambda(y) = \sum_\lambda \Big(\sum_\rho a_{\lambda\rho} h_\rho(x)\Big)\Big(\sum_\sigma b_{\lambda\sigma} m_\sigma(y)\Big) = \sum_{\rho,\sigma} \Big(\sum_\lambda a_{\lambda\rho} b_{\lambda\sigma}\Big) h_\rho(x) m_\sigma(y) = \sum_{\rho,\sigma} (a^{\mathsf T} b)_{\rho\sigma}\, h_\rho(x) m_\sigma(y).
$$
This equals the kernel expansion $\sum_\rho h_\rho(x) m_\rho(y)$ if and only if $a^{\mathsf T} b = I$, that is $b = (a^{\mathsf T})^{-1}$. Meanwhile $A = a b^{\mathsf T} = a\big((a^{\mathsf T})^{-1}\big)^{\mathsf T} = a (a^{-1}) = I$. Conversely $A = I$ forces $a b^{\mathsf T} = I$, hence $a^{\mathsf T} b = I$, giving the kernel. So duality and the product formula are equivalent, *provided* the kernel itself expands as $\sum_\rho h_\rho(x) m_\rho(y)$.

That base expansion is a direct computation: in a single $y$-variable, $\prod_i (1 - x_i y)^{-1} = \sum_{n} h_n(x) y^n$ is the definition of the complete functions; with the full alphabet $y$, expanding the product over $j$ and collecting the monomial $y^\alpha$ groups the $x$-coefficients into $h_{\alpha_1}(x) h_{\alpha_2}(x)\cdots$, and summing over the $S$-orbit of each exponent pattern $\lambda$ assembles $h_\lambda(x) m_\lambda(y)$. Hence $\Omega(x,y) = \sum_\lambda h_\lambda(x) m_\lambda(y)$, the $u = h$, $v = m$ instance, which records that $\{h_\lambda\}, \{m_\mu\}$ are dual and certifies the form is well defined.

Symmetry of the form: swapping the two alphabets fixes $\Omega(x,y)$, so $\sum_\lambda h_\lambda(x) m_\lambda(y) = \sum_\lambda m_\lambda(x) h_\lambda(y)$, whence $\{m_\lambda\}, \{h_\mu\}$ are also dual, i.e. $\langle m_\lambda, h_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu} = \langle h_\mu, m_\lambda\rangle$, and bilinear extension gives $\langle f, g\rangle = \langle g, f\rangle$ throughout. For the power sums, the identity $h_n = \sum_{\lambda\vdash n} z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda$ of [40.03.01] applied in both alphabets, together with $\prod_{i,j}(1-x_iy_j)^{-1} = \exp\sum_{k\ge 1} \tfrac1k p_k(x) p_k(y)$ (take $\log$ of the product), yields $\Omega = \sum_\lambda z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda(x) p_\lambda(y)$; matching this to the dual-bases criterion with $u_\lambda = z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda$, $v_\lambda = p_\lambda$ gives $\langle z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda, p_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$, i.e. $\langle p_\lambda, p_\mu\rangle = z_\lambda\,\delta_{\lambda\mu}$.

Finally the Schur form. The Schur functions satisfy the Cauchy identity $\sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y) = \Omega(x,y)$ — proved bijectively by the RSK correspondence of the co-produced [40.03.04], and independently by the Jacobi-Trudi / Lindström-Gessel-Viennot route in the Full proof set. Taking $u_\lambda = v_\lambda = s_\lambda$ in the established equivalence, the product formula on the right is exactly the criterion for $\langle s_\lambda, s_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem is the foundational reason the Hall inner product is forced rather than chosen: the single kernel $\prod(1-x_iy_j)^{-1}$ simultaneously defines the form (through $\{h_\lambda\}, \{m_\mu\}$), measures it (a basis is dual iff its Cauchy sum reproduces the kernel), and diagonalises it (the Schur form). It builds toward the Frobenius characteristic of the co-produced [40.03.06], where this very inner product becomes the character inner product on the symmetric groups and Schur orthonormality becomes the orthogonality of irreducible characters. The dual-bases criterion is exactly the statement that the kernel is the reproducing element of a self-pairing, and the three expansions of $\Omega$ are dual to one another through the change-of-basis matrices: the $h$-$m$ form exhibits the defining duality, the $p$-form exhibits the orthogonality with weights $z_\lambda$, and the $s$-form is the central insight that one basis is self-dual. Putting these together, the orthonormality $\langle s_\lambda, s_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$ generalises the Gram-matrix computation of any finite inner-product space to the graded ring $\Lambda$, and it appears again in the Pieri and Littlewood-Richardson adjointness of Advanced results, where $\langle s_\lambda s_\mu, s_\nu\rangle = c^\nu_{\lambda\mu}$.

## Advanced results [Master]

The Hall inner product turns every multiplicative identity of $\Lambda$ into an adjoint statement, and the Cauchy kernel is the reproducing element threading all of them.

**Theorem 1 (adjointness of multiplication and the Littlewood-Richardson pairing).** For every $f \in \Lambda$ the skewing operator $f^\perp$ is the adjoint of multiplication by $f$, $\langle fg, h\rangle = \langle g, f^\perp h\rangle$, and it is a coderivation-compatible map with $s_\lambda^\perp s_\mu = s_{\mu/\lambda}$. The structure constants of the Schur basis are read off as inner products: with $s_\lambda s_\mu = \sum_\nu c^\nu_{\lambda\mu} s_\nu$,
$$
c^\nu_{\lambda\mu} = \langle s_\lambda s_\mu,\, s_\nu\rangle = \langle s_\mu,\, s_\lambda^\perp s_\nu\rangle = \langle s_\mu,\, s_{\nu/\lambda}\rangle,
$$
so the Littlewood-Richardson coefficients of the co-produced [40.03.05] are the Schur-expansion coefficients of skew Schur functions, $s_{\nu/\lambda} = \sum_\mu c^\nu_{\lambda\mu} s_\mu$. The Pieri rules are the cases $\mu = (r)$ and $\mu = (1^r)$ [ref: TODO_REF Macdonald §5].

**Theorem 2 (the Cauchy kernel as reproducing element; coproduct duality).** Equip $\Lambda$ with the coproduct $\Delta f(x) = f(x', x'')$ (separating a single alphabet into two), making it the self-dual Hopf algebra of [40.03.01]. The Cauchy kernel $\Omega(x,y) = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y)$ is the canonical element of $\Lambda(x) \otimes \Lambda(y)$ dual to the inner product: for any $f$,
$$
\big\langle f(x),\, \Omega(x, y)\big\rangle_x = f(y),
$$
the pairing taken in the $x$-alphabet. Thus $\Omega$ reproduces every symmetric function, and the adjoint of multiplication is dual to the coproduct: $\langle fg, h\rangle = \langle f \otimes g, \Delta h\rangle$. The skewing operator $f^\perp$ is the image of $f$ under the convolution action, and $\omega$ is the Hopf isometry exchanging $\Omega \leftrightarrow \Omega'$.

**Theorem 3 (two proofs of Cauchy and their meeting point).** The Cauchy identity $\sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y) = \prod_{i,j}(1-x_iy_j)^{-1}$ admits a bijective proof and a determinantal proof. The **RSK proof** of the co-produced [40.03.04] is a content-preserving bijection between $\mathbb{N}$-matrices $(a_{ij})$ with $\sum a_{ij} < \infty$ — whose generating function is $\prod_{i,j}(1-x_iy_j)^{-1}$ — and pairs $(P, Q)$ of semistandard tableaux of the same shape, whose generating function is $\sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y)$. The **Jacobi-Trudi / Lindström-Gessel-Viennot proof** expands $s_\lambda(x) = \det(h_{\lambda_i - i + j}(x))$ and resums the determinantal series against the geometric kernel, the antisymmetrisation collapsing to the product. The two proofs meet at the same cancellation seen in [40.03.02]: the sign-reversing involution of the LGV lemma is the determinantal shadow of the RSK insertion, so the bijective and the determinantal routes compute one identity.

**Theorem 4 (the inner product is the character inner product).** Under the Frobenius characteristic $\mathrm{ch} : \bigoplus_n R(S_n) \to \Lambda$ of the co-produced [40.03.06], carrying the class function $f$ on $S_n$ to $\mathrm{ch}(f) = \sum_{\lambda\vdash n} z_\lambda^{-1} f(\lambda)\, p_\lambda$, the Hall inner product pulls back to the symmetric-group character inner product:
$$
\langle \mathrm{ch}(f), \mathrm{ch}(g)\rangle = \frac{1}{n!}\sum_{\sigma \in S_n} f(\sigma)\, g(\sigma^{-1}) = \frac{1}{|S_n|}\sum_\sigma f(\sigma)\overline{g(\sigma)}.
$$
Schur orthonormality $\langle s_\lambda, s_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$ becomes the first orthogonality relation $\langle \chi^\lambda, \chi^\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$ for the irreducible characters $\chi^\lambda = \mathrm{ch}^{-1}(s_\lambda)$, and $\langle p_\lambda, p_\mu\rangle = z_\lambda\delta_{\lambda\mu}$ is the column orthogonality weighted by centraliser orders. The Cauchy kernel becomes the regular-representation character, and the dual Cauchy identity the sign-twisted version.

**Synthesis.** Putting these together, the Hall inner product is the structure that makes $\Lambda$ a self-dual object, and the Cauchy kernel is the element that witnesses the self-duality at every level. The dual-bases theorem is exactly the statement that $\Omega(x,y)$ is the reproducing kernel of the pairing, so the foundational reason the Schur functions are orthonormal is that they are the basis in which the kernel is diagonal — $\Omega = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y)$ with a single shared index. The central insight is that three identifications coincide: the duality of $\{h_\lambda\}$ and $\{m_\mu\}$ that defines the form, the self-duality of $\{s_\lambda\}$ that diagonalises it, and the orthogonality of the irreducible $S_n$-characters under Frobenius — and the involution $\omega$, which is conjugation of diagrams in [40.03.01], is the isometry threading all three, sending $\Omega$ to the dual kernel $\Omega'$ and $s_\lambda$ to $s_{\lambda'}$. This is exactly why the Littlewood-Richardson coefficients of [40.03.05] are simultaneously multiplication constants $\langle s_\lambda s_\mu, s_\nu\rangle$ and comultiplication constants $\langle s_\mu, s_{\nu/\lambda}\rangle$: the adjointness of multiplication and skewing is dual to the Hopf coproduct of [40.03.01]. The bridge is the RSK correspondence of the co-produced [40.03.04], whose bijective proof of Cauchy is the combinatorial mechanism beneath the orthonormality, and which appears again as the Robinson-Schensted shadow of the character theory; the same kernel governs Pieri, the skewing operator, and the Frobenius characteristic of [40.03.06], so the entire metric geometry of $\Lambda$ generalises the orthogonality of characters from a single finite group to all symmetric groups at once.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (the two normalisations of the Hall inner product agree).** *The bilinear form fixed by $\langle h_\lambda, m_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$ satisfies $\langle p_\lambda, p_\mu\rangle = z_\lambda \delta_{\lambda\mu}$, and is symmetric.*

*Proof.* From $h_n = \sum_{\lambda\vdash n} z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda$ of [40.03.01], the generating identity $\sum_n h_n(x) t^n = \prod_i(1 - x_i t)^{-1}$ gives, with $t$ replaced by the alphabet $y$,
$$
\Omega(x,y) = \prod_{i,j}(1 - x_i y_j)^{-1} = \exp\Big(\sum_i \sum_{k\ge 1} \tfrac1k (x_i y_j)^k \text{ summed over } j\Big) = \exp\Big(\sum_{k\ge 1} \tfrac1k p_k(x) p_k(y)\Big).
$$
Expanding the exponential and collecting by cycle type as in the proof of $h_n = \sum z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda$ yields $\Omega = \sum_\lambda z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda(x) p_\lambda(y)$. The kernel also equals $\sum_\lambda h_\lambda(x) m_\lambda(y)$ (base expansion, Key theorem). Both are the canonical element of the pairing, so for the form with $\langle h_\lambda, m_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$ the dual-bases lemma applied to $u_\lambda = z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda$, $v_\mu = p_\mu$ gives $\langle z_\lambda^{-1} p_\lambda, p_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$, i.e. $\langle p_\lambda, p_\mu\rangle = z_\lambda \delta_{\lambda\mu}$. Symmetry: $\Omega(x,y) = \Omega(y,x)$ shows $\sum_\lambda h_\lambda(x)m_\lambda(y) = \sum_\lambda m_\lambda(x) h_\lambda(y)$, so $\langle m_\lambda, h_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu} = \langle h_\mu, m_\lambda\rangle$, and bilinearity propagates symmetry to all of $\Lambda$. The $p$-form, being diagonal with real entries $z_\lambda$, is manifestly symmetric, confirming consistency. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (dual-bases lemma).** *Homogeneous partition-indexed bases $\{u_\lambda\}, \{v_\lambda\}$ of $\Lambda_\mathbb{Q}$ satisfy $\langle u_\lambda, v_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$ iff $\sum_\lambda u_\lambda(x) v_\lambda(y) = \Omega(x,y)$.*

*Proof.* Restrict to degree $n$. Write $u_\lambda = \sum_\rho a_{\lambda\rho} h_\rho$, $v_\mu = \sum_\sigma b_{\mu\sigma} m_\sigma$ with invertible matrices $a, b$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ (both are bases). Then $\langle u_\lambda, v_\mu\rangle = \sum_\rho a_{\lambda\rho} b_{\mu\rho} = (a b^{\mathsf T})_{\lambda\mu}$, so $\langle u_\lambda, v_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$ iff $a b^{\mathsf T} = I$. Separately,
$$
\sum_\lambda u_\lambda(x) v_\lambda(y) = \sum_{\rho,\sigma}\Big(\sum_\lambda a_{\lambda\rho} b_{\lambda\sigma}\Big) h_\rho(x) m_\sigma(y) = \sum_{\rho,\sigma} (a^{\mathsf T} b)_{\rho\sigma} h_\rho(x) m_\sigma(y),
$$
which equals $\Omega = \sum_\rho h_\rho(x) m_\rho(y)$ iff $a^{\mathsf T} b = I$. The conditions $a b^{\mathsf T} = I$ and $a^{\mathsf T} b = I$ are equivalent for square matrices (each says $b^{\mathsf T} = a^{-1}$), so the two characterisations coincide. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Cauchy identity by RSK and by Jacobi-Trudi).** *$\displaystyle \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y) = \prod_{i,j}(1 - x_i y_j)^{-1}$.*

*Proof.* *(RSK route, cross-referenced to [40.03.04].)* The product $\prod_{i,j}(1-x_iy_j)^{-1} = \prod_{i,j}\sum_{a_{ij}\ge 0} (x_i y_j)^{a_{ij}}$ is the generating function $\sum_{A} x^{\mathrm{row}(A)} y^{\mathrm{col}(A)}$ over finitely-supported matrices $A = (a_{ij}) \in \mathbb{N}^{\infty\times\infty}$, where $\mathrm{row}(A)_i = \sum_j a_{ij}$ and $\mathrm{col}(A)_j = \sum_i a_{ij}$. The RSK correspondence is a bijection $A \leftrightarrow (P, Q)$ with $P, Q \in \mathrm{SSYT}$ of a common shape $\lambda$, content of $P$ equal to $\mathrm{col}(A)$ and content of $Q$ equal to $\mathrm{row}(A)$. Hence $x^{\mathrm{row}(A)} = x^{\mathrm{wt}(Q)}$ and $y^{\mathrm{col}(A)} = y^{\mathrm{wt}(P)}$, so summing over $A$ equals $\sum_\lambda\big(\sum_{Q} x^{\mathrm{wt}(Q)}\big)\big(\sum_{P} y^{\mathrm{wt}(P)}\big) = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y)$.

*(Jacobi-Trudi route.)* Work in $n$ $x$-variables and $n$ $y$-variables; the stable limit follows by letting $n\to\infty$. Using the bialternant $s_\lambda(x) = a_{\lambda+\delta}(x)/a_\delta(x)$ of [40.03.02], the sum $\sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y)$ multiplied by $a_\delta(x) a_\delta(y)$ becomes $\sum_\lambda a_{\lambda+\delta}(x) a_{\lambda+\delta}(y)$, a sum over strictly decreasing exponent vectors $\alpha = \lambda + \delta$ of $\det(x_i^{\alpha_j})\det(y_i^{\alpha_j})$. By the Cauchy-Binet expansion this equals $\det\big(\sum_{\alpha} x_i^\alpha y_k^\alpha\big)$ restricted appropriately, which resums to $\det\big((1 - x_i y_k)^{-1}\big)_{i,k}$. The latter determinant is evaluated by the Cauchy determinant formula $\det\big((1-x_iy_k)^{-1}\big) = \frac{\prod_{i<i'}(x_i - x_{i'})\prod_{k<k'}(y_k - y_{k'})}{\prod_{i,k}(1 - x_i y_k)} = \frac{a_\delta(x) a_\delta(y)}{\prod_{i,k}(1 - x_i y_k)}$. Dividing by $a_\delta(x) a_\delta(y)$ gives $\sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y) = \prod_{i,k}(1 - x_i y_k)^{-1}$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Schur orthonormality and the isometry of $\omega$).** *$\langle s_\lambda, s_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$, and $\langle \omega f, \omega g\rangle = \langle f, g\rangle$ with $\omega(s_\lambda) = s_{\lambda'}$.*

*Proof.* By Proposition 3, $\sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y) = \Omega(x,y)$; by Proposition 2 with $u_\lambda = v_\lambda = s_\lambda$ this is equivalent to $\langle s_\lambda, s_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$. For $\omega$: apply $\omega_x$ to $\Omega(x,y) = \sum_\lambda h_\lambda(x) m_\lambda(y)$, giving $\sum_\lambda e_\lambda(x) m_\lambda(y) = \prod_{i,j}(1 + x_i y_j) = \Omega'(x,y)$. The dual Cauchy identity $\Omega'(x,y) = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_{\lambda'}(y)$ (Proposition 5) and $\Omega = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y)$ then give $\omega_x\big(\sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y)\big) = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_{\lambda'}(y)$; comparing coefficients in the orthonormal $y$-Schur basis yields $\omega_x s_\lambda(x) = s_{\lambda'}(x)$. As conjugation $\lambda\mapsto\lambda'$ is an involution on partitions, $\omega$ permutes the orthonormal basis $\{s_\lambda\}$, so $\langle \omega s_\lambda, \omega s_\mu\rangle = \langle s_{\lambda'}, s_{\mu'}\rangle = \delta_{\lambda'\mu'} = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$; bilinear extension gives the isometry. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (dual Cauchy identity).** *$\displaystyle \prod_{i,j}(1 + x_i y_j) = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_{\lambda'}(y) = \sum_\lambda e_\lambda(x) m_\lambda(y)$.*

*Proof.* The $e$-$m$ form is the base expansion read for the elementary functions: $\prod_i(1 + x_i t) = \sum_n e_n(x) t^n$ defines the $e_n$, and expanding over the alphabet $y$ and collecting orbits assembles $\sum_\lambda e_\lambda(x) m_\lambda(y)$. For the Schur form, apply $\omega_x$ to the Cauchy identity $\sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y) = \prod_{i,j}(1-x_iy_j)^{-1}$. On the right, $\omega_x$ in the $h$-$m$ expansion sends $h_\lambda(x) \mapsto e_\lambda(x)$, turning $\prod(1-x_iy_j)^{-1} = \sum_\lambda h_\lambda(x)m_\lambda(y)$ into $\sum_\lambda e_\lambda(x) m_\lambda(y) = \prod_{i,j}(1+x_iy_j)$. On the left, $\omega_x s_\lambda(x) = s_{\lambda'}(x)$ (Proposition 4), so $\omega_x\big(\sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y)\big) = \sum_\lambda s_{\lambda'}(x) s_\lambda(y) = \sum_\mu s_\mu(x) s_{\mu'}(y)$ after reindexing $\mu = \lambda'$. Equating the two images gives the dual Cauchy identity. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (adjointness of $f^\perp$ and the LR pairing).** *For all $f$ there is a unique $f^\perp$ with $\langle fg, h\rangle = \langle g, f^\perp h\rangle$; it satisfies $s_\lambda^\perp s_\mu = s_{\mu/\lambda}$ and $c^\nu_{\lambda\mu} = \langle s_\mu, s_{\nu/\lambda}\rangle$.*

*Proof.* Orthonormality of $\{s_\lambda\}$ makes $\Lambda$ a (graded) inner-product space, so multiplication-by-$f$, a linear map, has a unique adjoint $f^\perp$ defined by $\langle s_\nu, f^\perp s_\mu\rangle := \langle f s_\nu, s_\mu\rangle$; bilinear extension gives $\langle fg, h\rangle = \langle g, f^\perp h\rangle$ for all $g, h$. For $f = s_\lambda$, the structure constants $s_\lambda s_\nu = \sum_\kappa c^\kappa_{\lambda\nu} s_\kappa$ give $\langle s_\lambda s_\nu, s_\mu\rangle = c^\mu_{\lambda\nu}$, so $\langle s_\nu, s_\lambda^\perp s_\mu\rangle = c^\mu_{\lambda\nu}$. The skew Schur function expands as $s_{\mu/\lambda} = \sum_\nu c^\mu_{\lambda\nu} s_\nu$ (the defining property of the LR coefficients in [40.03.05], equivalently the dual-basis expansion of the coproduct $\Delta s_\mu = \sum_{\lambda,\nu} c^\mu_{\lambda\nu} s_\lambda \otimes s_\nu$), so $\langle s_\nu, s_{\mu/\lambda}\rangle = c^\mu_{\lambda\nu} = \langle s_\nu, s_\lambda^\perp s_\mu\rangle$ for every $\nu$. By orthonormality $s_\lambda^\perp s_\mu = s_{\mu/\lambda}$, and reading $c^\nu_{\lambda\mu} = \langle s_\lambda s_\mu, s_\nu\rangle = \langle s_\mu, s_\lambda^\perp s_\nu\rangle = \langle s_\mu, s_{\nu/\lambda}\rangle$ gives the stated pairing. $\square$

- The Cauchy identity $\prod(1-x_iy_j)^{-1} = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y)$ is proved bijectively by the RSK correspondence of the co-produced [40.03.04], which is the combinatorial heart of the orthonormality; the Littlewood-Richardson coefficients $c^\nu_{\lambda\mu} = \langle s_\lambda s_\mu, s_\nu\rangle = \langle s_\mu, s_{\nu/\lambda}\rangle$ of the co-produced [40.03.05] are exactly the multiplication-versus-skewing constants made visible by the adjointness developed here. The Frobenius characteristic of the co-produced [40.03.06] carries the Hall inner product to the symmetric-group character inner product $\frac{1}{n!}\sum_\sigma \chi(\sigma)\psi(\sigma^{-1})$, so Schur orthonormality becomes the orthogonality of irreducible characters and the $z_\lambda$-weighting becomes column orthogonality.

- The same inner product is the bilinear form underlying the representation theory of [07.05.02] (Young diagrams and the irreducible $S_n$-modules) and [07.05.03] (Specht modules), where $\langle \chi^\lambda, \chi^\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$ is the first orthogonality relation; the power-sum expansion of $s_\lambda$ governed here by $\langle s_\lambda, p_\mu\rangle = \chi^\lambda(\mu)$ is the Murnaghan-Nakayama rule of [07.05.10]. The Cauchy kernel $\prod(1-x_iy_j)^{-1}$ is the symmetric-function avatar of the reproducing kernel in the theory of the resultant and of the Cauchy determinant, linking to the alternant computations of [40.03.02] and to the generating-function methods of [40.08.01]; the dual Cauchy identity $\prod(1+x_iy_j) = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_{\lambda'}(y)$ is the sign-twist that recurs in the Schur-Weyl duality of [07.05.04].

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The product $\prod_{i,j}(1 - x_i y_j)^{-1}$ and the antisymmetric quotients that become Schur functions both appear in **Cauchy's** 1815 memoir on alternating functions [ref: TODO_REF Cauchy 1815]; the Cauchy determinant $\det\big((x_i + y_j)^{-1}\big)$ and its relatives, from which the Schur-form Cauchy identity descends, are named for that work. The bilinear pairing that organises the modern theory — the inner product making the complete and monomial functions dual and the Schur functions orthonormal — is due to **Philip Hall**, who introduced it in the 1950s in connection with the Hall algebra of finite abelian $p$-groups, where the structure constants are the Hall polynomials; the name *Hall inner product* and the systematic development are codified in **Macdonald's** *Symmetric Functions and Hall Polynomials* (1979; 2nd ed. 1995) [ref: TODO_REF Macdonald §4].

The identification of the symmetric-function inner product with the orthogonality of symmetric-group characters runs through **Frobenius's** 1900 determination of the characters of $S_n$ and was given its symmetric-function form by **Littlewood** in *The Theory of Group Characters* [ref: TODO_REF Littlewood 1940], where the Cauchy identity is read as the decomposition of the tensor algebra and the orthonormality of the $s_\lambda$ as the orthonormality of the irreducible characters. The bijective proof of the Cauchy identity by the Robinson-Schensted-Knuth correspondence — **Robinson** (1938), **Schensted** (1961), and **Knuth** (1970) — supplied the combinatorial mechanism beneath the orthogonality, and the adjointness of multiplication and skewing was placed in its Hopf-algebraic setting by **Geissinger** and **Zelevinsky** (1981), who identified $\Lambda$ as the universal positive self-dual Hopf algebra in which the Cauchy kernel is the canonical pairing element.
