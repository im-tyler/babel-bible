## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine reading off a shuffled list of distinct numbers one at a time, and filing each new number into a growing left-justified array of boxes by a fixed, mechanical rule. The rule is patient: a new number tries to settle at the right end of the top row, but if it is too small to sit there, it shoves out (bumps) the smallest entry that blocks it, and that bumped entry drops to the next row down to repeat the same struggle. Each number you read causes a cascade of bumps that ends by adding exactly one fresh box somewhere. This filing procedure is **row insertion**, and it is the engine of the whole unit.

Why bother? Because the procedure secretly sorts the chaos of a shuffle into two tidy staircase-shaped tables. One table, $P$, records where every value finally lands; the other, $Q$, records the order in which the boxes were born. The remarkable fact is that the original shuffle can be reconstructed perfectly from this pair of tables — nothing is lost. So a shuffle of $n$ numbers and a pair of equally-shaped staircase tables are two names for the same object. This dictionary is the **Robinson-Schensted correspondence**.

The pay-off is that questions about the shuffle that look hard become easy in the table language. How long is the longest stretch of values that keep increasing, scattered through the shuffle? You do not have to search: it is simply the width of the top row of $P$. Counting, symmetry, and even the behaviour of a *random* shuffle all become readable off the shape of the tables. The same filing rule, run on lists with repeats or on grids of counts, gives an even larger dictionary used throughout this chapter.

## Visual [Beginner]

Inserting the permutation $4\,1\,3\,2$ one value at a time. At each step the new value bumps the leftmost entry strictly larger than it from a row; the bumped entry moves down to the next row and repeats. The recording table $Q$ writes the step number into whichever box is newly created.

| step | insert | the table $P$ after the step | new box born |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | $4$ | row 1: $4$ | top row, position 1 |
| 2 | $1$ | row 1: $1$; row 2: $4$ | row 2 (the $1$ bumped the $4$ down) |
| 3 | $3$ | row 1: $1\ 3$; row 2: $4$ | row 1, position 2 |
| 4 | $2$ | row 1: $1\ 2$; row 2: $3\ 4$ | row 2, position 2 ($2$ bumped $3$ down) |

The final pair is $P$ with rows $1\ 2$ and $3\ 4$, and $Q$ with rows $1\ 3$ and $2\ 4$. Both have the same staircase shape (two boxes over two boxes). Notice the entries of $P$ increase along rows and down columns, and so do the entries of $Q$: each is a *standard* table, using each number once. The longest increasing run hidden in $4\,1\,3\,2$ has length $2$ (for instance $1\,3$ or $1\,2$), and that is exactly the width of the top row.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let us run the full filing on the permutation $2\,3\,1$ and read off the two tables, then check the longest-increasing-run fact.

Step 1. Insert $2$. The top row is empty, so $2$ sits at the start: $P$ has top row $2$. A box was born at the top-left, so $Q$ has top row $1$.

Step 2. Insert $3$. Compare with the top row $2$. Since $3$ is not smaller than $2$, it sits at the right end of the top row: $P$ top row becomes $2\ 3$. The new box is the second box of the top row, so $Q$ top row becomes $1\ 2$.

Step 3. Insert $1$. Compare with the top row $2\ 3$. The value $1$ is smaller than $2$, so it bumps the $2$: the top row becomes $1\ 3$, and the bumped $2$ drops to row two. Row two was empty, so $2$ sits there. The new box is the start of row two, so $Q$ gets a $3$ in the new row-two box.

Step 4. Read off. The table $P$ has top row $1\ 3$ and second row $2$. The table $Q$ has top row $1\ 2$ and second row $3$. Both have the same shape: two boxes over one.

Step 5. Check the longest increasing run. In $2\,3\,1$ the longest stretch of values that increase (reading left to right, not necessarily next to each other) is $2\,3$, length $2$. The top row of $P$ is $1\ 3$, width $2$. They match.

What this tells us: the mechanical filing turns a permutation into a matched pair of standard tables, and a feature of the permutation that would take searching — the longest increasing run — is simply the width of the first row. Run in reverse, the same pair rebuilds $2\,3\,1$, so no information was lost.

**Exercise (easy, numeric).**

Insert the value $5$ into a top row that currently reads $1\ 3\ 6$. In which position (counting from the left, starting at $1$) does an entry get bumped out of this row?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The new value goes at the end if it is at least as big as everything; otherwise it replaces the leftmost entry strictly larger than it.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Position $3$.** Scanning $1\ 3\ 6$ for the leftmost entry strictly larger than $5$, the entries $1$ and $3$ are smaller, and $6$ is the first one larger, sitting in position $3$. So $5$ takes that spot, the row becomes $1\ 3\ 5$, and $6$ is bumped down. Feedback-correct: bumping always targets the leftmost entry that exceeds the incoming value, keeping the row sorted. Feedback-wrong: if you answered "no bump, it goes at the end" you treated $5$ as large enough to sit after $6$, but $5 < 6$, so the row would no longer increase.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise (easy, true-false).**

True or false: in the Robinson-Schensted correspondence, the tables $P$ and $Q$ always have the same shape.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Recall that $Q$ records the step at which each new box of $P$ was created.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.** Every step adds exactly one box to $P$, and $Q$ writes the step number into that very box, so $Q$ grows box-for-box alongside $P$ and ends with the identical shape. Feedback-correct: $Q$ is a faithful logbook of $P$'s growth, so their shapes are equal by construction. Feedback-wrong: the entries of $P$ and $Q$ differ, but their *shapes* never can, because they are filled in lockstep.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Work with semistandard and standard Young tableaux of [40.03.02]; all notation (the shape $\lambda \vdash n$, $\mathrm{SSYT}(\lambda)$, $\mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)$, the content, the conjugate $\lambda'$, $f^\lambda = |\mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)|$, the Schur function $s_\lambda$) is inherited from that unit and recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`. Throughout, *tableau* means a column-strict, row-weakly-increasing filling.

**Definition (row insertion).** Let $P$ be a semistandard tableau and $k$ a positive integer. The **row insertion** $P \leftarrow k$ is computed by: set $i = 1$ and the inserted value $v = k$. In row $i$, if $v$ weakly exceeds every entry, append $v$ at the right end and stop; otherwise let the leftmost entry strictly greater than $v$ be at column $j$, replace it by $v$ (this is a *bump*), set $v$ to the replaced entry, increment $i$, and repeat in the next row. The procedure terminates, adding exactly one new cell, and $P \leftarrow k$ is again a semistandard tableau. The cell added is the **bump path**'s terminal cell.

**Definition (Robinson-Schensted).** For a permutation $\sigma = \sigma(1)\,\sigma(2)\cdots\sigma(n) \in S_n$, form the **insertion tableau** $P(\sigma) = ((\varnothing \leftarrow \sigma(1)) \leftarrow \sigma(2)) \leftarrow \cdots \leftarrow \sigma(n)$ and the **recording tableau** $Q(\sigma)$ by placing the entry $t$ into the cell created at step $t$. Then $P(\sigma), Q(\sigma) \in \mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)$ for a common shape $\lambda \vdash n$. The **Robinson-Schensted map** $\sigma \mapsto (P(\sigma), Q(\sigma))$ is a bijection
$$
S_n \;\xrightarrow{\ \sim\ }\; \bigsqcup_{\lambda \vdash n} \mathrm{SYT}(\lambda) \times \mathrm{SYT}(\lambda).
$$

**Definition (RSK, the Knuth generalisation).** A **generalised permutation** is a two-line array $\binom{i_1\ i_2\ \cdots\ i_N}{j_1\ j_2\ \cdots\ j_N}$ whose columns are sorted lexicographically (by top entry, ties broken by bottom entry). Such arrays biject with $\mathbb{N}$-matrices $A = (a_{ij})$ of finite support, $a_{ij} = \#\{t : (i_t, j_t) = (i,j)\}$. **RSK** inserts $j_1, \dots, j_N$ to build a tableau $P$ and records the *top* entries $i_1, \dots, i_N$ in $Q$ at the cells created. It is a bijection
$$
\{\mathbb{N}\text{-matrices of finite support}\} \;\xrightarrow{\ \sim\ }\; \bigsqcup_{\lambda} \mathrm{SSYT}(\lambda) \times \mathrm{SSYT}(\lambda),
$$
with $\mathrm{content}(P) = (\text{column sums of } A)$ and $\mathrm{content}(Q) = (\text{row sums of } A)$. Restricting to $0/1$-matrices and using *column* insertion gives the **dual RSK**, a bijection between $0/1$-matrices and pairs $(P, Q)$ with $P \in \mathrm{SSYT}(\lambda)$, $Q \in \mathrm{SSYT}(\lambda')$.

**Definition (increasing/decreasing subsequences).** An **increasing subsequence** of $\sigma$ of length $k$ is indices $t_1 < \cdots < t_k$ with $\sigma(t_1) < \cdots < \sigma(t_k)$; **decreasing** reverses the second inequalities. Write $\mathrm{is}(\sigma)$ and $\mathrm{ds}(\sigma)$ for the maximal lengths, and $i_k(\sigma)$ for the maximal size of a union of $k$ increasing subsequences.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"Bumping replaces the leftmost entry $\ge v$."* It replaces the leftmost entry *strictly greater* than $v$ ($> v$, not $\ge v$). Using $\ge$ would break column-strictness and is the *column*-insertion convention instead; mixing the two corrupts the bijection. Equal values append to the right under row insertion, which is exactly why repeated entries are allowed in a row.

- *"RS works for any sequence, giving two SYT."* For a word with repeated letters, $P$ is semistandard (not standard) and the right partner is the RSK recording tableau, also semistandard. Two *standard* tableaux arise only from genuine permutations (distinct entries). Forcing standardness on a word with repeats loses the content data that RSK is designed to preserve.

- *"The recording tableau $Q$ tracks the same numbers as $P$."* $Q$ records *creation order* (for RS) or the *top line* of the generalised permutation (for RSK), not the inserted values. Confusing $P$ and $Q$ inverts the roles in the symmetry theorem $\sigma^{-1} \mapsto (Q, P)$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem is that row insertion is invertible step by step, so the Robinson-Schensted map is a bijection enumerating $\sum_\lambda (f^\lambda)^2 = n!$, and Schensted's theorem reads the longest increasing subsequence off the first row.

**Theorem (Robinson-Schensted bijection and Schensted's theorem).** *The map $\sigma \mapsto (P(\sigma), Q(\sigma))$ is a bijection from $S_n$ onto $\bigsqcup_{\lambda \vdash n} \mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)^2$; in particular $\sum_{\lambda \vdash n} (f^\lambda)^2 = n!$. The shape $\lambda = \mathrm{sh}(P(\sigma))$ satisfies $\lambda_1 = \mathrm{is}(\sigma)$, the length of the longest increasing subsequence of $\sigma$, and $\lambda_1' = \mathrm{ds}(\sigma)$, the length of the longest decreasing subsequence.* [ref: TODO_REF Stanley §7.11]

*Proof.* Two parts: bijectivity by reverse insertion, then Schensted's identification.

**(i) Bijectivity.** Given $(P, Q) \in \mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)^2$, reconstruct $\sigma$. The largest entry $n$ of $Q$ sits in some cell $c$, necessarily an outer corner of $\lambda$ (its row and column maximal). The value occupying $c$ in $P$ was the *last* entry to settle, ending a bump path; reverse the path: starting from $c$, take the entry $w$ there, move up one row, and find the rightmost entry strictly less than $w$, swap roles ($w$ bumps it *upward*), continuing until reaching row $1$, where the final ejected value is $\sigma(n)$. Deleting cell $c$ from both $P$ and $Q$ yields a smaller pair of equal shape; induction recovers $\sigma(n-1), \dots, \sigma(1)$. Each reverse step exactly undoes a forward insertion because the forward bump path is monotone (the bumped entries strictly increase as they fall), so the rightmost-smaller search inverts the leftmost-larger search. Forward and reverse are mutually inverse, giving the bijection; counting cardinalities gives $n! = \sum_\lambda (f^\lambda)^2$.

**(ii) Schensted's theorem.** Fix the first row of $P$. After inserting $\sigma(1), \dots, \sigma(t)$, the entry in cell $(1, j)$ is the smallest possible last value of an increasing subsequence of $\sigma(1)\cdots\sigma(t)$ of length $j$. This invariant holds initially and is preserved by insertion: inserting $\sigma(t{+}1) = a$ into position $j$ of row $1$ means $a$ is the new smallest length-$j$ tail, extending the length-$(j{-}1)$ subsequence whose tail sat at $(1, j{-}1) < a$. Hence the width $\lambda_1$ of row $1$ equals the maximal length of an increasing subsequence, $\mathrm{is}(\sigma)$. Applying the same statement to the reversal/complement (insertion of $\sigma$ read so that increasing becomes decreasing) — equivalently using that $P(\sigma^{\mathrm{rev}})$ is the transpose-compatible tableau — gives $\lambda_1' = \mathrm{ds}(\sigma)$ for the first column. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem is the foundational reason RSK is more than an algorithm: the reverse-insertion argument shows the correspondence is a genuine bijection, so it transports any count or symmetry between permutations and tableau pairs. It builds toward the symmetry theorem and Greene's theorem of Advanced results, where transposing the matrix swaps $P$ and $Q$ and unions of increasing subsequences recover the entire shape; and it appears again in [40.03.03], where the same insertion run on generalised permutations is exactly the bijective proof of the Cauchy identity $\sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y) = \prod_{i,j}(1 - x_i y_j)^{-1}$. The identity $\sum_\lambda (f^\lambda)^2 = n!$ is dual to the representation-theoretic $\sum_\lambda (\dim S^\lambda)^2 = |S_n|$ of [07.05.03]; this is exactly the statement that RS is the combinatorial shadow of the decomposition of the group algebra. Putting these together, Schensted's first-row reading generalises into Greene's theorem, the reverse insertion supplies invertibility, and the content bookkeeping of RSK supplies the symmetric-function identity — three faces of one bumping rule.

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

By the Erdős-Szekeres theorem (a corollary of Schensted), any permutation of length $n > (r-1)(s-1)$ has an increasing subsequence of length $r$ or a decreasing one of length $s$. What is the smallest $n$ guaranteeing an increasing subsequence of length $4$ or a decreasing subsequence of length $4$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set $r = s = 4$. The threshold is $(r-1)(s-1) + 1$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that RSK gives the Cauchy identity $\sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y) = \prod_{i,j}(1 - x_i y_j)^{-1}$ by tracking weights through the bijection.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Weight an $\mathbb{N}$-matrix $A$ by $\prod_{i,j}(x_i y_j)^{a_{ij}}$. Sum over all $A$ to get the product side; sum over $(P,Q)$ using the content bookkeeping to get the Schur side.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Assign to an $\mathbb{N}$-matrix $A = (a_{ij})$ the weight $\prod_{i,j} (x_i y_j)^{a_{ij}}$. Summing over all finite-support $A$ factors entrywise: $\sum_A \prod_{i,j} (x_i y_j)^{a_{ij}} = \prod_{i,j} \sum_{a \ge 0} (x_i y_j)^a = \prod_{i,j} (1 - x_i y_j)^{-1}$. RSK sends $A$ to $(P, Q)$ of common shape $\lambda$ with $\mathrm{content}(P) = $ column sums (the $y$-degrees, since $Q$ records top indices $i$ giving the $x$-degrees and $P$ holds the inserted $j$ giving the $y$-degrees), so the weight equals $x^{\mathrm{content}(Q)} y^{\mathrm{content}(P)}$. As $A$ ranges over all matrices, $(P,Q)$ ranges over all pairs of SSYT of equal shape, independently, so $\sum_A (\cdots) = \sum_\lambda \big(\sum_{Q \in \mathrm{SSYT}(\lambda)} x^{\mathrm{content}(Q)}\big)\big(\sum_{P \in \mathrm{SSYT}(\lambda)} y^{\mathrm{content}(P)}\big) = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y)$, using the combinatorial definition $s_\lambda = \sum_T x^{\mathrm{content}(T)}$ of [40.03.02]. Equating the two evaluations gives the Cauchy identity. Rubric: full credit for the entrywise geometric-series factorisation, the weight tracking through RSK, and the assembly into $\sum_\lambda s_\lambda s_\lambda$.

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

State and prove Greene's theorem for $k = 2$: the sum $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2$ of the first two rows of $\mathrm{sh}(P(\sigma))$ equals the maximal size $i_2(\sigma)$ of a union of two increasing subsequences of $\sigma$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A union of $k$ increasing subsequences is a subsequence with no decreasing subsequence of length $k+1$ (Dilworth/Mirsky). Relate this to the first $k$ rows via Knuth invariance.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Greene's theorem, $\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_k = i_k(\sigma)$, the largest cardinality of a union of $k$ increasing subsequences. For $k = 2$: first, $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 \le i_2$ is impossible to exceed, since any two increasing subsequences inject into two rows under the insertion (each increasing run lengthens one row's growth record); and $i_2 \le \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$ because a set partitionable into $\le 2$ increasing chains has, by Mirsky's theorem, no decreasing subsequence of length $3$, and such a subword inserts into a tableau of $\le 2$ rows, of total size $\le \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$ by Knuth invariance (row insertion of a subword does not increase the first-two-row sum beyond that of the whole). The key fact is that Greene's quantity $i_k$ is a *Knuth invariant*: elementary Knuth moves $acb \equiv cab$ and $bac \equiv bca$ preserve both the $P$-tableau and every $i_k$, so $i_k(\sigma) = i_k$ of the reading word of $P$, where increasing subsequences visibly fill the top $k$ rows. Hence $i_2(\sigma) = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$. Rubric: full credit for the Knuth-invariance of $i_k$, the Mirsky/Dilworth chain-cover input, and both inequalities.

## Advanced results [Master]

The bumping rule organises into a monoid structure, a symmetry exchanging the two tableaux, a complete reading of the shape by subsequences, and the asymptotics of a random permutation.

**Theorem 1 (Knuth equivalence and the plactic monoid).** Two words over $\{1, 2, \dots\}$ have the same insertion tableau $P$ if and only if they are connected by **elementary Knuth transformations**: $\,acb \equiv cab$ when $a \le b < c$, and $bac \equiv bca$ when $a < b \le c$ (applied to adjacent triples within a word). The quotient of the free monoid by these relations is the **plactic monoid**, and $P$ is the canonical representative of each class. Equivalently, **jeu de taquin** — sliding the empty cell of a skew tableau inward by the smaller-neighbour rule — computes the same $P$ from any skew filling with reading word in the class, so jeu de taquin and row insertion define one rectification operation [ref: TODO_REF Stanley Appendix 1]. This is the combinatorial substrate of the Littlewood-Richardson rule developed in the co-produced [40.03.05].

**Theorem 2 (the symmetry theorem).** If $\sigma \mapsto (P, Q)$ under RSK, then $\sigma^{-1} \mapsto (Q, P)$; in the matrix form, transposing the $\mathbb{N}$-matrix $A \mapsto A^{\mathsf T}$ exchanges $P$ and $Q$. Consequently the permutations fixed by inversion — the **involutions** of $S_n$ — biject with single standard Young tableaux ($P = Q$), so
$$
\#\{\sigma \in S_n : \sigma^2 = \mathrm{id}\} \;=\; \sum_{\lambda \vdash n} f^\lambda,
$$
and the number of fixed points of $\sigma$ equals the number of columns of odd length in $\mathrm{sh}(P(\sigma))$. The proof is the **matrix-ball** (Viennot shadow) construction, which makes the $\sigma \leftrightarrow \sigma^{-1}$ symmetry a reflection of the lattice picture across the diagonal [ref: TODO_REF Fulton Ch. 4].

**Theorem 3 (Greene's theorem).** For every $k \ge 1$, the partial sums of the shape $\lambda = \mathrm{sh}(P(\sigma))$ read off unions of monotone subsequences:
$$
\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_k = i_k(\sigma), \qquad \lambda_1' + \cdots + \lambda_k' = d_k(\sigma),
$$
where $i_k(\sigma)$ (resp. $d_k(\sigma)$) is the maximal cardinality of a union of $k$ increasing (resp. decreasing) subsequences. The $k = 1$ case is Schensted's theorem; the full statement determines $\lambda$ entirely from the subsequence statistics of $\sigma$ and is invariant under Knuth equivalence [ref: TODO_REF Stanley §7.13].

**Theorem 4 (longest increasing subsequence of a random permutation).** Let $\ell_n = \mathrm{is}(\sigma)$ for $\sigma$ uniform in $S_n$. Then $\mathbb{E}[\ell_n] \sim 2\sqrt{n}$ (the **Ulam problem**, solved by the **Logan-Shepp-Vershik-Kerov** limit shape of the RSK Young diagram under Plancherel measure, $\mathbb{P}(\lambda) = (f^\lambda)^2 / n!$). The fluctuations obey the **Baik-Deift-Johansson** theorem: as $n \to \infty$,
$$
\frac{\ell_n - 2\sqrt{n}}{n^{1/6}} \;\xrightarrow{\ d\ }\; \mathrm{TW}_2,
$$
the **Tracy-Widom GUE distribution**, the same law governing the largest eigenvalue of a random Hermitian matrix. RSK is the bridge: $\ell_n = \lambda_1$, and Plancherel-random shapes are an integrable determinantal point process.

**Synthesis.** Putting these together, RSK is the single bijection from which the combinatorics, the symmetry, and the asymptotics all descend. The foundational reason is that row insertion is a Knuth-invariant operation: Theorem 1 makes the insertion tableau the normal form of the plactic monoid, so jeu de taquin, the Littlewood-Richardson rule of the co-produced [40.03.05], and the Schur-positivity of skew functions are all governed by the same elementary moves. This is exactly why the symmetry theorem of Theorem 2 holds — transposing the matrix is the diagonal reflection of Viennot's shadow lines — and why involutions biject with single tableaux, making $\sum_\lambda f^\lambda$ the involution count, dual to the $\sum_\lambda (f^\lambda)^2 = n!$ of the bijection itself. Greene's theorem generalises Schensted's first-row reading to the whole shape, and the bridge to analysis is the Plancherel measure $(f^\lambda)^2/n!$ that RSK pushes forward from the uniform permutation: the Logan-Shepp-Vershik-Kerov limit shape and the Baik-Deift-Johansson Tracy-Widom fluctuations are the central insight that the longest increasing subsequence is an eigenvalue in disguise, tying this purely combinatorial rule to random-matrix theory. The same content bookkeeping that proves the Cauchy identity of [40.03.03] is the generating function of this measure, and the Frobenius characteristic of the co-produced [40.03.06] reads $\sum_\lambda (f^\lambda)^2 = n!$ as the decomposition of $\mathbb{C}[S_n]$.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (row insertion is well-defined and shape-additive).** *For a semistandard tableau $P$ and an integer $k$, $P \leftarrow k$ is a semistandard tableau whose shape is that of $P$ with one cell added, and the bump path moves weakly left as it descends.*

*Proof.* Induct on the number of rows touched. In row $1$, either $k$ appends at the right (the row stays weakly increasing, no column below is created above an existing cell, so column-strictness is vacuous for the new corner) or $k$ replaces the leftmost entry $w > k$ at column $j$; then row $1$ remains weakly increasing because $k$ is at least the entry to its left (that entry was $\le k$, being not $> k$) and strictly less than $w$ and all to $w$'s right. The bumped $w$ inserts into row $2$; since $w$ was at column $j$ of row $1$ and the entry below it (if any) was $> w$, $w$'s insertion point in row $2$ is at column $\le j$, giving the weak-left descent and preserving column-strictness (the new entry in row $2$ at column $j'$ is $\le$ the old entry there and $>$ the entry above-left). The recursion terminates when a row appends, adding exactly one corner cell. The resulting filling is row-weak and column-strict throughout. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (RS is a bijection).** *The map $\sigma \mapsto (P(\sigma), Q(\sigma))$ is a bijection $S_n \to \bigsqcup_{\lambda \vdash n} \mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)^2$.*

*Proof.* Construct the two-sided inverse by reverse insertion. Given $(P, Q) \in \mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)^2$, the cell $c$ holding $n$ in $Q$ is an outer corner; let $w$ be the entry of $P$ at $c$. **Reverse-bump** $w$: delete $c$, and moving up from row $\mathrm{row}(c)$, in the row above find the rightmost entry $u < w$, place $w$ in $u$'s cell, set $w := u$, and continue to row $1$; the value ejected from row $1$ is $\sigma(n)$. The reverse step inverts a forward step: a forward insertion's bump path is strictly increasing in the bumped values and weakly left-descending (Proposition 1), so the rightmost-smaller search in row $i-1$ recovers exactly the cell from which the forward path entered row $i$. After ejecting $\sigma(n)$, $(P', Q')$ on shape $\lambda \setminus c$ is a smaller equal-shape pair; downward induction on $n$ yields $\sigma(n-1), \dots, \sigma(1)$, all distinct (an outer-corner deletion never repeats a value). Forward $\circ$ reverse and reverse $\circ$ forward are identities, so the map is bijective. Equating cardinalities, $n! = \sum_{\lambda \vdash n} (f^\lambda)^2$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Schensted's theorem).** *$\lambda_1 = \mathrm{is}(\sigma)$ and $\lambda_1' = \mathrm{ds}(\sigma)$, where $\lambda = \mathrm{sh}(P(\sigma))$.*

*Proof.* Maintain the invariant: after inserting a prefix, the entry at cell $(1, j)$ of $P$ is the least possible final term of an increasing subsequence of length $j$ in that prefix. Base $j = 1$ holds. Inductive step: inserting $a$, if $a$ lands at $(1, j)$ it bumped an entry $> a$, and $a$ extends a length-$(j{-}1)$ increasing subsequence ending at $(1, j{-}1) \le a$, so the least length-$j$ tail is updated to $a$; if $a$ appends at the new corner $(1, \lambda_1 + 1)$, it creates a length-$(\lambda_1+1)$ subsequence, increasing $\lambda_1$. The invariant persists, so at the end $\lambda_1$ is the greatest $j$ admitting an increasing subsequence of length $j$, i.e. $\mathrm{is}(\sigma)$. For $\lambda_1'$: row insertion of $\sigma$ and *column* insertion are related by transposition of $P$, and column insertion's first-column invariant tracks decreasing subsequences; alternatively $\mathrm{ds}(\sigma) = \mathrm{is}(\sigma^{\#})$ where $\sigma^\#$ reverses the values, and the RSK shape of $\sigma^\#$ is $\lambda'$ (a known consequence of the symmetry between row/column insertion). Either way $\lambda_1' = \mathrm{ds}(\sigma)$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (symmetry theorem).** *If $\sigma \mapsto (P, Q)$ then $\sigma^{-1} \mapsto (Q, P)$.*

*Proof.* Encode $\sigma$ as the permutation matrix with a ball at $(i, \sigma(i))$, and run **Viennot's shadow-line** construction: the first family of shadow lines (lower-left light from the southwest) records, by their northeast corners, the first row of $P$ from the $x$-coordinates and the first row of $Q$ from the $y$-coordinates; iterating on the remaining balls builds successive rows. Inverting $\sigma$ reflects every ball across the main diagonal $(i, \sigma(i)) \mapsto (\sigma(i), i)$, which swaps the roles of the $x$- and $y$-coordinates throughout the shadow construction, hence exchanges $P$ (read from one coordinate) and $Q$ (read from the other) while preserving the common shape. Therefore $\sigma^{-1} \mapsto (Q, P)$. Setting $\sigma = \sigma^{-1}$ forces $P = Q$, so involutions biject with single SYT and their number is $\sum_{\lambda \vdash n} f^\lambda$; the fixed points of $\sigma$ are the cells of $P$ not paired by a $2$-cycle of the shadow involution, which are the odd-length columns. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (Cauchy identity by RSK).** *$\displaystyle \sum_{\lambda} s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y) = \prod_{i,j} (1 - x_i y_j)^{-1}$.*

*Proof.* Weight an $\mathbb{N}$-matrix $A = (a_{ij})$ of finite support by $w(A) = \prod_{i,j} (x_i y_j)^{a_{ij}}$. The total weight factors as a geometric product: $\sum_A w(A) = \prod_{i,j} \sum_{a \ge 0} (x_i y_j)^a = \prod_{i,j} (1 - x_i y_j)^{-1}$, the right side. RSK is a weight-preserving bijection $A \leftrightarrow (P, Q)$ with $P, Q \in \mathrm{SSYT}(\lambda)$, where the inserted entries (column indices $j$) build $P$ with $\mathrm{content}(P) = $ column sums of $A$ (the $y$-exponents), and the recorded top indices $i$ build $Q$ with $\mathrm{content}(Q) = $ row sums (the $x$-exponents). Thus $w(A) = x^{\mathrm{content}(Q)} y^{\mathrm{content}(P)}$. Because RSK is a bijection onto all pairs of equal-shape SSYT with $P$ and $Q$ independent, summing the weights regroups as $\sum_\lambda \big(\sum_{Q \in \mathrm{SSYT}(\lambda)} x^{\mathrm{content}(Q)}\big)\big(\sum_{P \in \mathrm{SSYT}(\lambda)} y^{\mathrm{content}(P)}\big) = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y)$ by the combinatorial definition of $s_\lambda$ in [40.03.02]. The two evaluations of $\sum_A w(A)$ coincide, giving the identity; the $0/1$-matrix restriction with column insertion yields the dual $\prod_{i,j}(1 + x_i y_j) = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_{\lambda'}(y)$ identically. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- This unit is built directly on the semistandard and standard Young tableaux and the combinatorial Schur function $s_\lambda = \sum_T x^{\mathrm{content}(T)}$ of [40.03.02]: row insertion is an operation on exactly those tableaux, the RS bijection lands in pairs of standard tableaux of a common shape, and the RSK content bookkeeping (column sums to $\mathrm{content}(P)$, row sums to $\mathrm{content}(Q)$) is what turns the bijection into a Schur-function identity. The first-row/first-column readings of Schensted's theorem use the shape $\lambda$ and conjugate $\lambda'$ defined there.

- The Cauchy identity $\sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_\lambda(y) = \prod_{i,j}(1 - x_i y_j)^{-1}$ and the dual $\prod_{i,j}(1 + x_i y_j) = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x) s_{\lambda'}(y)$, of which RSK and dual RSK are the bijective proofs, are the subject of the co-produced [40.03.03], where they are derived from the Hall inner product and used to prove the orthonormality $\langle s_\lambda, s_\mu\rangle = \delta_{\lambda\mu}$; the Knuth equivalence and jeu de taquin developed in Advanced results are the engine of the Littlewood-Richardson rule of the co-produced [40.03.05]; and the Frobenius characteristic of the co-produced [40.03.06] reads the bijection's enumerative shadow $\sum_\lambda (f^\lambda)^2 = n!$ as the decomposition of the group algebra $\mathbb{C}[S_n]$.

- The identity $\sum_{\lambda \vdash n} (f^\lambda)^2 = n!$ is the combinatorial face of the representation theory of the symmetric group: $f^\lambda = \dim S^\lambda$ is the dimension of the Specht module of [07.05.03] (so RS is the bijective shadow of $\mathbb{C}[S_n] \cong \bigoplus_\lambda \mathrm{End}(S^\lambda)$), the Young diagrams indexing the tableaux are the irreducibles of [07.05.02], and the bumping that defines insertion is the combinatorial core of Schur-Weyl duality [07.05.04]. The longest-increasing-subsequence asymptotics connect this combinatorics to the random-matrix Tracy-Widom law and to the determinantal point processes that recur across mathematical physics.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The insertion algorithm was first written down by **Gilbert de Beauregard Robinson** in a 1938 paper on the representation theory of the symmetric group, as an auxiliary device in an attempted proof of the Littlewood-Richardson rule; it was rediscovered and turned into a clean bijective algorithm with the longest-increasing-subsequence application by **Craige Schensted** in his 1961 *Canadian Journal of Mathematics* paper [ref: TODO_REF Schensted 1961], from which the bumping procedure and Schensted's theorem take their modern form. **Donald Knuth**, in a 1970 *Pacific Journal of Mathematics* paper [ref: TODO_REF Knuth 1970], generalised the correspondence from permutations to arbitrary nonnegative integer matrices, identified the elementary word relations (now the Knuth relations) that characterise the insertion tableau, and connected the construction to the Cauchy identity for Schur functions; the three-name attribution Robinson-Schensted-Knuth dates from this work.

**Curtis Greene's** 1974 theorem [ref: TODO_REF Greene 1974] in the *Journal of Combinatorial Theory* showed that the entire shape, not just its first row, is read off by unions of increasing and decreasing subsequences, completing Schensted's partial result. **Marcel-Paul Schützenberger** supplied the symmetry theorem and the jeu-de-taquin theory in the 1960s-70s, and **Alain Lascoux** and Schützenberger isolated the plactic monoid (1981) as the algebraic home of Knuth equivalence. The asymptotic study of the longest increasing subsequence of a random permutation began with **Stanisław Ulam's** question; the leading order $2\sqrt n$ was established through the limit-shape results of **Logan and Shepp** and independently **Vershik and Kerov** (1977), and the Tracy-Widom fluctuation law was proved by **Jinho Baik, Percy Deift, and Kurt Johansson** in their 1999 paper in the *Journal of the American Mathematical Society* [ref: TODO_REF Baik-Deift-Johansson 1999], identifying the combinatorial statistic with the largest eigenvalue of a random Hermitian matrix.
