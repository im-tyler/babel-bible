## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine a square grid of cells, each painted black or white. Read black as $+1$ and white as $-1$, so every row is a string of pluses and minuses. We want the rows to be as different from one another as possible. Two rows of length $n$ "agree" in some columns and "disagree" in the others; the most balanced relationship is when any two distinct rows agree in exactly half their columns and disagree in the other half. A square $\pm 1$ grid whose rows are pairwise balanced in this way is a **Hadamard matrix**.

Why care about maximal disagreement? If you broadcast each row as a coded message, two rows that agree only half the time are easy to tell apart even when a few entries get corrupted by noise. The same balance makes Hadamard matrices the backbone of error-correcting codes, of efficient ways to weigh objects or run experiments, and of the fast Walsh-Hadamard transform used in signal processing. Maximal balance is maximal usefulness.

There is a beautiful constraint hiding here. You can build such a grid of size $1$ and of size $2$, but after that the size is always a multiple of $4$: $4, 8, 12, 16, 20, \dots$. You cannot make a balanced grid of size $3$ or $6$. One of the oldest open questions in the subject asks whether a Hadamard matrix exists for *every* multiple of $4$ — believed yes, still unproven.

## Visual [Beginner]

A Hadamard matrix of order $4$ looks like the grid below, writing $+$ for $+1$ and $-$ for $-1$. Pick any two different rows and count: they agree in two columns and disagree in two. That fifty-fifty split is the whole idea.

![A 4 by 4 grid of plus and minus signs forming a Hadamard matrix, with the first row and first column all plus signs; arrows highlight two rows that match in exactly two columns and differ in the other two, illustrating the pairwise-balanced property.](/img/hadamard-matrices-paley-construction-placeholder.svg)

```
        col1  col2  col3  col4
 row1     +     +     +     +
 row2     +     -     +     -
 row3     +     +     -     -
 row4     +     -     -     +
```

Compare row 2 and row 3. Column by column the signs are $(+,+), (-,+), (+,-), (-,-)$: they match in columns 1 and 4, differ in columns 2 and 3 — two and two. Every pair of distinct rows behaves the same way. The doubling trick below grows this $4 \times 4$ block into an $8 \times 8$ one, then $16 \times 16$, and so on, doubling the size each time.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let us build a bigger Hadamard matrix from a smaller one by Sylvester's doubling rule. Start from the smallest interesting block,
$$
H_2 = \begin{pmatrix} + & + \\ + & - \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Step 1. Check $H_2$ is balanced. Its two rows are $(+,+)$ and $(+,-)$. They agree in column 1 and disagree in column 2 — one and one, a fifty-fifty split. Good.

Step 2. Apply the doubling rule. Make a $4 \times 4$ grid in four quarters: put a copy of $H_2$ in the top-left, top-right, and bottom-left quarters, and a copy with every sign flipped in the bottom-right quarter. Flipping $H_2$ gives $\begin{pmatrix} - & - \\ - & + \end{pmatrix}$.

Step 3. Write out the result.
$$
H_4 = \begin{pmatrix} + & + & + & + \\ + & - & + & - \\ + & + & - & - \\ + & - & - & + \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Step 4. Spot-check the balance. Compare rows 1 and 4: $(+,+,+,+)$ versus $(+,-,-,+)$. They agree in columns 1 and 4 and disagree in columns 2 and 3 — two and two. Try any other pair and you get the same two-versus-two split.

What this tells us: from one balanced block you manufacture a balanced block of twice the width. Repeating turns size $2$ into $4$, then $8$, then $16$, giving a Hadamard matrix of every size that is a power of $2$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $J$ denotes the all-ones matrix, $I$ the identity, and $\mathbf{1}$ the all-ones column vector, of whatever size context demands.

**Definition (Hadamard matrix).** A **Hadamard matrix** of order $n$ is a matrix $H \in \{+1, -1\}^{n \times n}$ satisfying
$$
H H^\top = n I_n.
$$
Equivalently, distinct rows are orthogonal as vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$; since each row has squared length $n$, two distinct rows agree in exactly $n/2$ coordinates. Because $H$ is square and $HH^\top = nI$, the relation $H^\top H = nI$ holds as well, so the columns are orthogonal too [ref: TODO_REF van Lint-Wilson Ch. 18].

**Definition (equivalence and normalisation).** Two Hadamard matrices are **equivalent** if one is obtained from the other by permuting rows, permuting columns, negating rows, and negating columns. Each such operation preserves $HH^\top = nI$. A Hadamard matrix is **normalised** (or in *standard form*) if its first row and first column consist entirely of $+1$; every Hadamard matrix is equivalent to a normalised one, obtained by negating each row and column whose leading entry is $-1$.

**Definition (conference matrix).** A **conference matrix** of order $n$ is a matrix $C \in \{0, \pm 1\}^{n \times n}$ with zero diagonal, $\pm 1$ off the diagonal, and
$$
C C^\top = (n-1) I_n.
$$
$C$ is **symmetric** when $C^\top = C$ and **skew** when $C^\top = -C$. Conference matrices are the bridge from quadratic residues to Hadamard matrices.

**Definition (quadratic-residue / Jacobsthal matrix).** Let $q$ be an odd prime power and $\chi : \mathbb{F}_q \to \{0, \pm 1\}$ the **quadratic-residue character**: $\chi(0) = 0$, $\chi(x) = +1$ if $x$ is a nonzero square, $\chi(x) = -1$ otherwise. The **Jacobsthal matrix** is $Q = \big(\chi(a - b)\big)_{a, b \in \mathbb{F}_q}$, a $q \times q$ matrix indexed by field elements. Its construction and properties draw on the structure of $\mathbb{F}_q$ and the Legendre symbol, applied here rather than re-derived.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"Orthogonal rows is enough; the column condition is a separate hypothesis."* For a *square* matrix, $HH^\top = nI$ already forces $H^\top H = nI$, because $H^\top = nH^{-1}$. Row-orthogonality and column-orthogonality are equivalent for Hadamard matrices, not independent assumptions.

- *"Every order divisible by $4$ is known to occur."* The divisibility $4 \mid n$ (for $n > 2$) is necessary, but sufficiency is the open **Hadamard conjecture**. Necessity is a theorem; sufficiency is not.

- *"The character $\chi$ is multiplicative on all of $\mathbb{F}_q$."* It is multiplicative, $\chi(xy) = \chi(x)\chi(y)$, but only the convention $\chi(0) = 0$ makes the key sum $\sum_{c} \chi(c)\chi(c + a) = -1$ (for $a \neq 0$) come out correctly; mishandling the zero term corrupts the conference-matrix identity.

- *"Type I and type II Paley matrices have the same order."* For $q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$ the construction yields order $q + 1$; for $q \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ the symmetric conference matrix must be doubled, giving order $2(q+1)$. The residue class of $q$ modulo $4$ controls whether $Q$ is skew or symmetric, and hence the order.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result fixes the possible orders. It rests on Hadamard's determinant inequality, whose equality case *characterises* Hadamard matrices among all $\pm 1$ matrices, and then on a short parity argument.

**Theorem (order constraint).** *If a Hadamard matrix of order $n$ exists, then $n \in \{1, 2\}$ or $4 \mid n$. Moreover, among all matrices $A \in \{\pm 1\}^{n \times n}$ the determinant satisfies $|\det A| \leq n^{n/2}$, with equality if and only if $A$ is a Hadamard matrix* [ref: TODO_REF Hadamard 1893].

*Proof.* For the determinant bound, let $A$ have rows $a_1, \dots, a_n \in \mathbb{R}^n$. Hadamard's inequality states $|\det A| \leq \prod_{i=1}^n \|a_i\|_2$, with equality iff the rows are pairwise orthogonal (or some row is zero). A $\pm 1$ row has $\|a_i\|_2 = \sqrt n$, so $|\det A| \leq n^{n/2}$, and equality holds iff the rows are pairwise orthogonal, i.e. $AA^\top = nI$ — exactly the Hadamard condition. This proves the characterisation.

Now suppose $H$ is Hadamard of order $n \geq 3$. Normalise so the first row is all $+1$. Orthogonality of the first row with any other row $r$ forces $\sum_j r_j = 0$, so each subsequent row has equally many $+1$ and $-1$ entries; in particular $n$ is even, $n = 2k$.

Take any two rows $r, s$ other than the first. Partition the columns by the sign pattern $(r_j, s_j) \in \{(+,+), (+,-), (-,+), (-,-)\}$ and let $a, b, c, d$ be the respective counts. Orthogonality of $r$ with the all-$+1$ first row gives $a + b = c + d = k$ (equal numbers of $+1$ and $-1$ in $r$); the same for $s$ gives $a + c = b + d = k$. Orthogonality of $r$ and $s$ gives $\langle r, s\rangle = a - b - c + d = 0$. Combining $a + b = k$, $a + c = k$, and $a - b - c + d = 0$ yields $b = c$ and then $a = d$ and $a = b$; hence $a = b = c = d = k/2$. Since $a$ is an integer, $k$ is even, so $n = 2k$ is divisible by $4$. The small cases $n = 1$ ($H = (1)$) and $n = 2$ (the matrix $H_2$) escape the three-row argument. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem builds toward every existence construction in the chapter: the determinant characterisation says a Hadamard matrix *is* the extremal point of the $\pm 1$ determinant problem, so each construction is a recipe for hitting that extremum. The foundational reason the order is divisible by $4$ is the three-row counting identity $a = b = c = d$, which is exactly the statement that any three rows of a normalised Hadamard matrix induce a balanced incidence pattern — this is the seed of the symmetric design appearing again in the Advanced results, where the rows-minus-first become the blocks of a $2\text{-}(4m-1, 2m-1, m-1)$ design. The argument generalises: replacing $\pm 1$ orthogonality by the quadratic-residue character gives the Paley construction, and the bridge is that orthogonality of $\pm 1$ vectors and the character sum $\sum_c \chi(c)\chi(c+a) = -1$ are the same balancing phenomenon read in two alphabets, $\{\pm 1\}$ and $\mathbb{F}_q$. Putting these together, the order constraint and the constructions are dual faces of one extremal problem.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

What is the determinant, in absolute value, of a Hadamard matrix of order $4$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A Hadamard matrix attains equality in $|\det H| = n^{n/2}$ with $n = 4$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$16$.** With $n = 4$, $|\det H| = 4^{4/2} = 4^2 = 16$. This is the maximum determinant achievable by any $4 \times 4$ matrix with entries in $\{\pm 1\}$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

Sylvester doubling starts from $H_1 = (1)$ and doubles the order at each step. After how many doublings do you first reach order $16$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Order goes $1, 2, 4, 8, 16$; count the steps from $1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$4$.** The orders are $2^0 = 1$, $2^1 = 2$, $2^2 = 4$, $2^3 = 8$, $2^4 = 16$; reaching $16 = 2^4$ takes $4$ doublings. The Sylvester family thus realises every order $2^k$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that if $H_1$ and $H_2$ are Hadamard matrices of orders $m$ and $n$, then the Kronecker product $H_1 \otimes H_2$ is a Hadamard matrix of order $mn$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $(A \otimes B)(C \otimes D) = (AC) \otimes (BD)$ and $(A \otimes B)^\top = A^\top \otimes B^\top$. Entries of a Kronecker product of $\pm 1$ matrices are products of $\pm 1$ entries.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Each entry of $H_1 \otimes H_2$ is a product $(H_1)_{ij}(H_2)_{kl} \in \{\pm 1\}$, so $H_1 \otimes H_2 \in \{\pm 1\}^{mn \times mn}$. Using the mixed-product rule and transpose rule,
$$
(H_1 \otimes H_2)(H_1 \otimes H_2)^\top = (H_1 \otimes H_2)(H_1^\top \otimes H_2^\top) = (H_1 H_1^\top) \otimes (H_2 H_2^\top) = (m I_m) \otimes (n I_n) = mn\, I_{mn}.
$$
Hence $H_1 \otimes H_2$ satisfies the Hadamard relation in order $mn$. Taking $H_1 = H_2 = H_2$ (the order-$2$ matrix) recovers Sylvester doubling. Rubric: full credit for the mixed-product identity and the conclusion $mn I$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $\chi$ be the quadratic-residue character of $\mathbb{F}_q$ ($q$ odd). Prove $\sum_{c \in \mathbb{F}_q} \chi(c) = 0$ and, for $a \neq 0$, $\sum_{c \in \mathbb{F}_q} \chi(c)\,\chi(c + a) = -1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the first sum, there are equally many nonzero squares and non-squares. For the second, factor $\chi(c)\chi(c+a) = \chi(c^2)\chi(1 + a c^{-1})$ on $c \neq 0$ using multiplicativity, then substitute $t = ac^{-1}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The nonzero elements of $\mathbb{F}_q$ split into $(q-1)/2$ squares ($\chi = +1$) and $(q-1)/2$ non-squares ($\chi = -1$), and $\chi(0) = 0$, so $\sum_c \chi(c) = 0$.

For the second sum, the $c = 0$ term vanishes. For $c \neq 0$, write $\chi(c)\chi(c+a) = \chi\big(c(c+a)\big) = \chi\big(c^2(1 + ac^{-1})\big) = \chi(c^2)\chi(1 + ac^{-1}) = \chi(1 + ac^{-1})$, since $\chi(c^2) = 1$. As $c$ ranges over $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$, the quantity $t = ac^{-1}$ ranges over $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ as well, and $1 + t$ ranges over $\mathbb{F}_q \setminus \{1\}$. Thus
$$
\sum_{c \neq 0} \chi(c)\chi(c+a) = \sum_{u \neq 1} \chi(u) = \Big(\sum_{u \in \mathbb{F}_q}\chi(u)\Big) - \chi(1) = 0 - 1 = -1.
$$
Rubric: full credit for the multiplicativity step, the change of variable, and isolating $\chi(1) = 1$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Using Exercise 4, show the Jacobsthal matrix $Q = (\chi(a-b))$ over $\mathbb{F}_q$ satisfies $Q Q^\top = q I - J$ and $Q J = J Q = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The $(a,b)$ entry of $QQ^\top$ is $\sum_c \chi(a-c)\chi(b-c)$. Substitute and split into the diagonal ($a = b$) and off-diagonal ($a \neq b$) cases. For $QJ$, use $\sum_c \chi(c) = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The $(a,b)$ entry of $QQ^\top$ is $\sum_{c} \chi(a-c)\chi(b-c)$. Put $u = a - c$; as $c$ runs over $\mathbb{F}_q$ so does $u$, and $b - c = u + (b - a)$. Hence the entry equals $\sum_u \chi(u)\chi(u + (b-a))$. If $a = b$ this is $\sum_u \chi(u)^2 = q - 1$ (every nonzero $u$ contributes $1$, and $\chi(0)^2 = 0$). If $a \neq b$, Exercise 4 gives $-1$. Therefore $QQ^\top = (q-1)I - (J - I) = qI - J$.

For $QJ$: the $(a, \cdot)$ row-sum is $\sum_b \chi(a-b) = \sum_u \chi(u) = 0$, so $QJ = 0$; symmetrically $JQ = 0$. Rubric: full credit for the substitution and both cases of the character sum.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, multiple choice).**

For which residue class of the odd prime power $q$ is the Jacobsthal matrix $Q$ skew-symmetric ($Q^\top = -Q$)?

A. $q \equiv 1 \pmod 4$  
B. $q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$  
C. $q \equiv 2 \pmod 4$  
D. all odd $q$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$Q^\top$ has $(a,b)$ entry $\chi(b - a) = \chi(-1)\chi(a-b)$. The sign of $\chi(-1)$ is $+1$ when $q \equiv 1$ and $-1$ when $q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. $q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$.** Since $\chi(-1) = (-1)^{(q-1)/2}$, one has $\chi(b-a) = \chi(-1)\chi(a-b)$, so $Q^\top = \chi(-1) Q$. When $q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$, $\chi(-1) = -1$ and $Q$ is skew; when $q \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, $\chi(-1) = +1$ and $Q$ is symmetric, forcing the type-II doubling. Feedback-wrong: $q \equiv 2 \pmod 4$ is impossible for an odd prime power, and the symmetric case is $q \equiv 1$, not all $q$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Paley type I: for $q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$, set $H = I_{q+1} + S$ where $S$ is the $(q+1) \times (q+1)$ matrix
$$
S = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \mathbf{1}^\top \\ -\mathbf{1} & Q \end{pmatrix},
$$
with $Q$ the Jacobsthal matrix. Prove $H$ is a Hadamard matrix of order $q+1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$, $Q$ is skew, so $S^\top = -S$. Compute $S S^\top = -S^2$ using $QQ^\top = qI - J$, $QJ = 0$, and $Q\mathbf{1} = 0$. Then $HH^\top = (I+S)(I-S) = I - S^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Entries of $H = I + S$ are $\pm 1$: the diagonal is $+1$, the first row/column off-diagonal entries are $\pm 1$, and the $Q$-block has $\pm 1$ off its diagonal with $+1$ on the diagonal from the added $I$. Since $q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$, $\chi(-1) = -1$ so $Q^\top = -Q$, hence $S^\top = -S$ (the corner is $0$, and the off-diagonal $\pm \mathbf 1$ blocks swap sign under transpose). Then
$$
HH^\top = (I + S)(I + S)^\top = (I+S)(I - S) = I - S^2 = I + S S^\top.
$$
Compute $SS^\top$ blockwise. The top-left entry is $\mathbf 1^\top \mathbf 1 = q$. The top-right is $\mathbf 1^\top Q^\top = (Q\mathbf 1)^\top = 0$ since each row of $Q$ sums to $0$. The bottom-left is likewise $0$. The bottom-right is $\mathbf 1\mathbf 1^\top + QQ^\top = J + (qI - J) = qI$. Hence $SS^\top = qI_{q+1}$, and $HH^\top = I + qI = (q+1)I$. So $H$ is Hadamard of order $q+1$. Rubric: full credit for the skew property, the block computation of $SS^\top$, and the conclusion.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $H$ be a normalised Hadamard matrix of order $4m$. Delete its first row and first column, replace $+1$ by $0$ and $-1$ by $1$ in the remaining $(4m-1) \times (4m-1)$ matrix, and call the result $N$. Prove that $N$ is the incidence matrix of a symmetric $2\text{-}(4m-1,\, 2m-1,\, m-1)$ design.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

After normalising, each non-first row has $2m$ entries $+1$ and $2m$ entries $-1$, one of which (the first column) is removed. Two non-first rows agree in $2m$ columns. Translate "agree/disagree" between $\pm 1$ rows into "common $1$'s" of the $0/1$ rows of $N$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Normalisation makes the first column all $+1$. A non-first row $r$ is orthogonal to the all-$+1$ first row, so it has exactly $2m$ entries $+1$ and $2m$ entries $-1$; removing the first-column $+1$ leaves $2m - 1$ entries $+1$ and $2m$ entries $-1$ among the remaining $4m-1$ columns. Under $+1 \mapsto 0$, $-1 \mapsto 1$, each row of $N$ has exactly $2m$ ones, but we instead read blocks as the $1$-positions: each of the $4m-1$ rows of $N$ has $2m - 1$ zeros becoming... more cleanly, set $b_r = \{j : H_{rj} = -1,\ j \neq 1\}$, so $|b_r| = 2m$; this is the standard convention giving block size $k = 2m-1$ after the symmetric complement. Two distinct non-first rows $r, s$ are orthogonal, so among the $4m$ columns they agree in $2m$ and disagree in $2m$; restricting to the $4m-1$ non-first columns and counting positions where both are $-1$ gives $m-1$ (from $a = b = c = d = m$ in the three-row identity, minus the first-column contribution). Hence any two blocks meet in $\lambda = m - 1$ points, every block has $k = 2m-1$ points, and there are $v = 4m-1$ points and blocks: a symmetric $2\text{-}(4m-1, 2m-1, m-1)$ design. Rubric: full credit for the row-balance count, the pairwise-intersection count $\lambda = m-1$, and identifying the symmetric design parameters.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

The order theorem and the two constructions situate Hadamard matrices inside design theory, coding theory, and an unresolved existence problem. The results below trace those threads.

**Theorem 1 (Sylvester-Kronecker family).** The map $H \mapsto H \otimes H_2$, equivalently $H \mapsto \begin{psmallmatrix} H & H \\ H & -H \end{psmallmatrix}$, sends a Hadamard matrix of order $n$ to one of order $2n$. Iterated from the seed $H_1 = (1)$ it produces a Hadamard matrix $H_{2^k}$ of every order $2^k$; the rows of $H_{2^k}$, indexed by $\{0,1\}^k$, are the Walsh functions $w_x(y) = (-1)^{\langle x, y\rangle}$, and the linear map they define is the Walsh-Hadamard transform [ref: TODO_REF Sylvester 1867].

**Theorem 2 (Paley constructions).** Let $q$ be an odd prime power and $Q$ the Jacobsthal matrix over $\mathbb{F}_q$, with $QQ^\top = qI - J$, $QJ = 0$, $Q^\top = \chi(-1)Q$ [ref: TODO_REF Paley 1933].

- *Type I* ($q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$, so $Q$ skew): $H = I_{q+1} + \big(\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & \mathbf 1^\top \\ -\mathbf 1 & Q\end{smallmatrix}\big)$ is a Hadamard matrix of order $q + 1$.
- *Type II* ($q \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, so $Q$ symmetric): the bordered matrix $C = \big(\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & \mathbf 1^\top \\ \mathbf 1 & Q\end{smallmatrix}\big)$ is a symmetric conference matrix of order $q+1$ with $CC^\top = qI$; substituting $\big(\begin{smallmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1\end{smallmatrix}\big)$ for each $+1$, $\big(\begin{smallmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1\end{smallmatrix}\big)$ for each $-1$, and $\big(\begin{smallmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1\end{smallmatrix}\big)$-type blocks for the zero diagonal yields a Hadamard matrix of order $2(q+1)$.

Together the Sylvester and Paley families realise every order $4m \leq 100$ except $4m = 92$ (first settled by Baumert-Golomb-Hall 1962 via a Williamson search), and infinitely many others.

**Theorem 3 (Hadamard matrices $\leftrightarrow$ symmetric designs).** Normalised Hadamard matrices of order $4m$ are in bijection (up to equivalence) with symmetric $2\text{-}(4m-1, 2m-1, m-1)$ designs, the **Hadamard designs**: deleting the first row and column and sending $+1 \mapsto 0$, $-1 \mapsto 1$ gives the incidence matrix of such a design, and the construction reverses. The complementary parameters $2\text{-}(4m-1, 2m, m)$ arise from the opposite sign convention [ref: TODO_REF Hall Ch. 14].

**Theorem 4 (Hadamard 3-design).** From a Hadamard matrix $H$ of order $n = 4m$, take the $2n$ vectors $\pm(\text{rows of } H)$; the supports of agreement partition the columns so that the $n$ columns, with the blocks $\{j : (\text{row } r)_j = (\text{row } s)_j\}$, form a $3\text{-}(4m, 2m, m-1)$ design. Hadamard matrices are thus a source of $3$-designs, rare among elementary constructions, and the only $3$-designs with $\lambda$ this small for general $m$.

**Theorem 5 (the Hadamard code).** The rows of an order-$n$ Hadamard matrix and their negatives form a binary code of length $n$ with $2n$ codewords, every two distinct codewords at Hamming distance $n/2$ (or $n$ for a word and its negative): a $[n, \log_2(2n), n/2]$ code meeting the Plotkin bound. The order-$32$ Paley-Hadamard code was used by the Mariner 9 spacecraft (1971) to transmit images of Mars, correcting up to $7$ bit errors per $32$-bit word. The first-order Reed-Muller code $\mathrm{RM}(1, k)$ is exactly the Sylvester-Hadamard code of length $2^k$ [ref: TODO_REF van Lint-Wilson Ch. 18].

**Theorem 6 (the Hadamard conjecture and asymptotics).** It is conjectured that a Hadamard matrix exists for every order $n \equiv 0 \pmod 4$. The smallest order in doubt has steadily fallen; as of the 2000s every multiple of $4$ below $668$ is realised. Asymptotically, de Launey and Gérard-Levavasseur-type results, and the Seberry construction, show a Hadamard matrix of order $n$ exists whenever $n$ is a multiple of $2^t$ with $t$ growing like $\log_2 n$; combined with Paley orders $q+1$ this gives density-$1$ realisation. The Bruck-Ryser-Chowla theorem supplies the only known nonexistence obstruction at the level of symmetric designs, and it never rules out a genuine multiple of $4$ — consistent with the conjecture.

**Synthesis.** Putting these threads together, the Hadamard matrix is the foundational reason three subjects share one combinatorial core: the orthogonality $HH^\top = nI$ is exactly the extremal condition of Hadamard's determinant problem, is exactly the balanced-incidence condition of a symmetric $2\text{-}(4m-1, 2m-1, m-1)$ design, and is exactly the equidistant condition of a code meeting the Plotkin bound. This is the central insight of the design-coding-graph triangle: a single $\pm 1$ array, depending on which structure one reads off its rows, becomes an extremal determinant, a symmetric design with its strongly regular point graph, or an optimal code. The Paley construction generalises the elementary Sylvester doubling by trading the alphabet $\{\pm 1\}$ for the field $\mathbb{F}_q$, where the character identity $\sum_c \chi(c)\chi(c+a) = -1$ plays the role orthogonality played for $\pm 1$ rows; the two are dual realisations of one balancing law. The bridge from the order theorem to existence is the Hadamard conjecture, which asserts that the necessary divisibility $4 \mid n$ is also sufficient — that the extremal determinant is always attained — and which the Sylvester, Paley, and Williamson families establish for an asymptotic density of orders without yet closing the gap.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Hadamard's determinant inequality and its equality case).** *For $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ with rows $a_1, \dots, a_n$, $|\det A| \leq \prod_{i=1}^n \|a_i\|_2$, with equality iff the rows are pairwise orthogonal or some $a_i = 0$. Consequently $|\det A| \leq n^{n/2}$ for $A \in \{\pm1\}^{n\times n}$, with equality iff $A$ is Hadamard.*

*Proof.* If some $a_i = 0$ both sides vanish. Otherwise apply Gram-Schmidt to $a_1, \dots, a_n$, obtaining orthogonal $b_1, \dots, b_n$ with $a_i = b_i + \sum_{j<i} c_{ij} b_j$; the change of basis is unipotent upper-triangular, so $\det A = \det B = \prod_i \|b_i\|$ (taking $B$ the matrix of rows $b_i$, orthogonal hence $|\det B| = \prod\|b_i\|$). Since $b_i$ is the component of $a_i$ orthogonal to $\mathrm{span}(a_1,\dots,a_{i-1})$, $\|b_i\| \leq \|a_i\|$, with equality iff $a_i \perp a_j$ for all $j < i$. Multiplying, $|\det A| = \prod\|b_i\| \leq \prod\|a_i\|$, equality iff all rows are pairwise orthogonal. For $\pm 1$ entries $\|a_i\| = \sqrt n$, giving $n^{n/2}$, attained iff $AA^\top = nI$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (order divisibility).** *A Hadamard matrix of order $n > 2$ has $4 \mid n$.*

*Proof.* Normalise the first row to all $+1$. Orthogonality with row $i \neq 1$ forces the row sum to vanish, so $n$ is even, $n = 2k$. Take rows $r, s \neq 1$ and let $a, b, c, d$ count columns with sign patterns $(+,+), (+,-), (-,+), (-,-)$. From row sums: $a+b = c+d$ and $a+c = b+d$, each equal to $k$. From $\langle r, s\rangle = 0$: $a - b - c + d = 0$. Solving, $b = c$, $a = d$, and $a + b = k$ together with $a - b - c + d = 2a - 2b = 0$ give $a = b$, hence $a = b = c = d = k/2$. As $a \in \mathbb{Z}$, $k$ is even and $4 \mid n$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Paley type I gives order $q+1$).** *For an odd prime power $q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$, the matrix $H = I_{q+1} + \big(\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & \mathbf 1^\top \\ -\mathbf 1 & Q\end{smallmatrix}\big)$ is Hadamard.*

*Proof.* By the character sums, $QQ^\top = qI - J$, $Q\mathbf 1 = 0$, $\mathbf 1^\top Q = 0$, and $Q^\top = \chi(-1)Q = -Q$ since $\chi(-1) = (-1)^{(q-1)/2} = -1$ for $q \equiv 3 \pmod 4$. Write $S = \big(\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & \mathbf 1^\top \\ -\mathbf 1 & Q\end{smallmatrix}\big)$; then $S^\top = \big(\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & -\mathbf 1^\top \\ \mathbf 1 & Q^\top\end{smallmatrix}\big) = \big(\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & -\mathbf 1^\top \\ \mathbf 1 & -Q\end{smallmatrix}\big) = -S$, so $S$ is skew and $H = I + S$ has $\pm 1$ entries (diagonal $+1$; off-diagonal $\pm 1$). Then
$$
SS^\top = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf 1^\top \mathbf 1 & \mathbf 1^\top Q^\top \\ Q^\top{}^\top\!\!\cdots \end{pmatrix}
$$
computed blockwise: top-left $\mathbf 1^\top\mathbf 1 = q$; top-right $\mathbf 1^\top Q^\top = (Q\mathbf 1)^\top = 0$; bottom-left $-\mathbf 1(0) + Q(-\mathbf 1)^\top$... directly, the bottom-right block is $(-\mathbf 1)(-\mathbf 1)^\top + QQ^\top = J + (qI - J) = qI$, and the cross blocks vanish because $Q\mathbf 1 = 0$. Hence $SS^\top = qI_{q+1}$. Finally $HH^\top = (I+S)(I - S) = I - S^2 = I + SS^\top = (q+1)I$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Paley conference matrix, type II).** *For $q \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, $C = \big(\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & \mathbf 1^\top \\ \mathbf 1 & Q\end{smallmatrix}\big)$ is a symmetric conference matrix: $C^\top = C$ and $CC^\top = qI_{q+1}$.*

*Proof.* Here $\chi(-1) = +1$, so $Q^\top = Q$ and $C^\top = C$. The diagonal of $C$ is $0$ (the corner, and $Q$'s diagonal $\chi(0) = 0$), off-diagonal entries are $\pm 1$. Blockwise $CC^\top = C^2 = \big(\begin{smallmatrix} \mathbf 1^\top\mathbf 1 & \mathbf 1^\top Q \\ Q\mathbf 1 & \mathbf 1\mathbf 1^\top + Q^2\end{smallmatrix}\big)$. Now $\mathbf 1^\top \mathbf 1 = q$, $\mathbf 1^\top Q = 0$ and $Q\mathbf 1 = 0$ by the row/column sums, and $\mathbf 1\mathbf 1^\top + Q^2 = J + QQ^\top = J + (qI - J) = qI$. Hence $CC^\top = qI_{q+1}$, a symmetric conference matrix. Replacing entries $0, +1, -1$ of $C$ by the $2\times 2$ blocks of Theorem 2 produces $H$ with $HH^\top = 2(q+1)I$, a Hadamard matrix of order $2(q+1)$; the block substitution is exactly the Kronecker tensoring of $C$ against $H_2$ adjusted on the diagonal, and the verification is the order-$2$ analogue of Proposition 3. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (Hadamard matrix yields a symmetric $2$-design).** *A normalised Hadamard matrix of order $4m$ produces a symmetric $2\text{-}(4m-1, 2m-1, m-1)$ design.*

*Proof.* Delete the first row and column; in the remaining $(4m-1)\times(4m-1)$ matrix $M$ each row has, by Proposition 2's count restricted to non-first columns, exactly $2m-1$ entries equal to $-1$ (taking $-1$ as incidence). Any two rows agree in $2m$ of the $4m$ columns; removing the first column (where both are $+1$) leaves agreement in $2m-1$ columns, of which the number where both equal $-1$ is, from $a = b = c = d = m$ in the three-row identity, exactly $m - 1$. Thus the $-1$-supports form blocks of size $k = 2m-1$ on $v = 4m-1$ points, any two blocks meeting in $\lambda = m-1$ points; dually any two points lie in $m-1$ common blocks. This is a symmetric $2\text{-}(4m-1, 2m-1, m-1)$ design. The reverse map sends such a design's incidence matrix $N$ to the bordered $\pm 1$ matrix $\big(\begin{smallmatrix} \mathbf 1^\top \\ \mathbf 1 \,\, J - 2N\end{smallmatrix}\big)$, which is Hadamard, establishing the bijection. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Finite fields $\mathbb{F}_q$ and their squares [21.02.01].** The Paley construction is an *application* of the structure of $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ as a cyclic group of even order: the quadratic-residue character $\chi$ is the unique order-$2$ multiplicative character, and the count of nonzero squares as $(q-1)/2$ is precisely the input that makes $\sum_c \chi(c) = 0$ and the Jacobsthal identity $QQ^\top = qI - J$ hold. The foundational reason Paley orders $q+1$ exist is that finite fields supply, for free, a set of size $q$ with a perfectly balanced quadratic character — the field's arithmetic is what manufactures the combinatorial balance.

- **Quadratic residues and the Legendre symbol [21.01.06].** The character $\chi$ is the Legendre symbol $\left(\tfrac{\cdot}{p}\right)$ when $q = p$ is prime, and its value $\chi(-1) = (-1)^{(q-1)/2}$ — a consequence of quadratic reciprocity's supplementary law — is exactly what decides whether the Jacobsthal matrix is skew ($q \equiv 3$) or symmetric ($q \equiv 1$), and hence whether the Paley construction is type I (order $q+1$) or type II (order $2(q+1)$). The residue class of $q$ modulo $4$, a number-theoretic datum, dictates the combinatorial output.

- **Balanced incomplete block designs and symmetric 2-designs [40.06.01].** Hadamard matrices of order $4m$ are the same data as symmetric $2\text{-}(4m-1, 2m-1, m-1)$ designs (Proposition 5), the densest family of symmetric BIBDs; the Bruck-Ryser-Chowla nonexistence test for symmetric designs is the design-theoretic shadow of the Hadamard order constraint, and the point graph of a Hadamard design is strongly regular, tying this unit to the design-coding-graph triangle developed alongside.

- **Linear codes and the Singleton/Plotkin bounds [40.06.06].** The Hadamard code — rows and their negatives — is the canonical code meeting the Plotkin bound, and the Sylvester-Hadamard code of length $2^k$ is literally the first-order Reed-Muller code $\mathrm{RM}(1,k)$; the orthogonality $HH^\top = nI$ is the equidistance that the coding-theoretic bound extremises, so the same matrix that solves the determinant problem also solves the optimal-distance problem.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The determinant inequality came first. In 1893 **Jacques Hadamard** [ref: TODO_REF Hadamard 1893] asked how large the determinant of a matrix with bounded entries can be and proved $|\det A| \leq \prod_i \|a_i\|$, observing that for $\pm 1$ entries the bound $n^{n/2}$ is met exactly when the rows are orthogonal — the matrices that now bear his name. The orthogonal $\pm 1$ arrays themselves were older: **James Joseph Sylvester** had introduced the doubling construction in 1867 [ref: TODO_REF Sylvester 1867], under the heading of "tessellated pavements" and "sign-successions," producing the order-$2^k$ family and noting their use in what would become signal analysis.

The decisive generalisation is due to **Raymond Paley**, whose 1933 paper *On orthogonal matrices* [ref: TODO_REF Paley 1933] built Hadamard matrices from the quadratic-residue character of a finite field, splitting into the type I and type II constructions according to $q \bmod 4$ and realising many orders beyond the powers of $2$. Paley conjectured that a Hadamard matrix exists for every order divisible by $4$; he died in an avalanche later that year at age $26$, and the conjecture remains open. The design-theoretic equivalence was systematised by **Marshall Hall** and others [ref: TODO_REF Hall Ch. 14], linking Hadamard matrices to symmetric $2$-designs and thereby to the Bruck-Ryser-Chowla obstruction, while the modern existence record rests on the Williamson construction and the Baumert-Golomb-Hall settlement of order $92$ in 1962. The textbook consolidation followed by van Lint and Wilson places the whole theory in the combinatorics of designs and codes [ref: TODO_REF van Lint-Wilson Ch. 18].
