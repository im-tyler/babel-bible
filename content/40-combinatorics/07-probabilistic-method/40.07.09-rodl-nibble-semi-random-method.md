## Intuition [Beginner]

Suppose you must cover a huge board with tiles, where every tile blocks out a small fixed patch and no two tiles are allowed to overlap. You want to use as few tiles as possible and leave almost nothing uncovered. Placing tiles one at a time by careful hand-planning is hopeless when the board has billions of squares: a greedy choice early on can box you into a corner where the last patches refuse to fit. You would like a method that stays flexible to the very end.

The idea that works is to take many small random bites instead of one big plan. In each round you scatter a thin sprinkle of tiles at random across whatever space is still open. Most of them land cleanly; a few collide with each other, so you simply throw the colliding ones away. After this tidy-up, you have placed a modest batch with no overlaps, the open space has shrunk a little, and you repeat. Each bite is small enough that collisions are rare, and the leftover space keeps shrinking by a steady fraction.

Because every round removes the same fraction of what remains, after enough rounds the uncovered part is a tiny sliver. The surprise is that this "nibble" of random sprinkle plus clean-up, repeated, gets you within a hair of the best possible packing — something no rigid one-shot plan reliably achieves.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the open space as a shrinking pool. At the start the whole pool is open. Each round you toss in a light handful of tiles: the green ones land without touching a neighbour and stay; the red ones overlap something and get scooped back out. What stays covers a slice of the pool, so the open pool shrinks before the next round.

![A sequence of shrinking pools left to right; in each pool a sparse scatter of tile marks, most green (kept) and a few red (removed for overlap), with the open uncovered area smaller in each successive pool until only a thin sliver remains at the far right.](/img/rodl-nibble-semi-random-method-placeholder.svg)

| Round | What you do | What happens to the open space |
|---|---|---|
| Start | nothing placed yet | the whole board is open |
| Each round | sprinkle a few random tiles, remove the colliding ones | open space shrinks by a steady fraction |
| After many rounds | keep repeating | only a tiny uncovered sliver is left |

Because each round trims the same proportion, the open space decays the way a quantity halved again and again races toward zero — and the few survivors of every round add up to a near-perfect packing.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We track how fast the open space shrinks when each round clears one third of what remains. Say the board starts with $9000$ open squares, and in every round the kept tiles cover one third of the currently open squares.

Step 1. After round one, one third of $9000$ is $3000$, so $3000$ squares get covered and $9000 - 3000 = 6000$ stay open.

Step 2. After round two, one third of the $6000$ open squares is $2000$, so $2000$ more get covered and $6000 - 2000 = 4000$ remain open.

Step 3. After round three, one third of $4000$ is about $1333$, leaving roughly $4000 - 1333 = 2667$ open.

Step 4. Notice the pattern: each round multiplies the open count by $\frac{2}{3}$. So after $r$ rounds the open count is about $9000 \times (2/3)^r$. After $10$ rounds that is $9000 \times (2/3)^{10} \approx 9000 \times 0.017 \approx 156$ open squares — under $2\%$ of the board still uncovered.

What this tells us: a fixed shrink fraction per round drives the leftover toward zero very fast, because repeated multiplication by a number below $1$ collapses quickly. The nibble never needs a clever global plan; the steady per-round bite does all the work, and the tiles kept across all rounds form the near-complete packing.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $H = (V, \mathcal{E})$ is an $r$-uniform hypergraph: $V$ is a finite vertex set and every edge $e \in \mathcal{E}$ has exactly $r$ vertices. The **degree** $d(v)$ is the number of edges through $v$, the **maximum degree** is $\Delta = \max_v d(v)$, and the **codegree** $d(u,v)$ is the number of edges containing both $u$ and $v$. The concentration apparatus the analysis rests on — the Doob exposure martingale and the Azuma / bounded-differences tail bound — is imported from [40.07.05] and applied, not reproved.

**Definition (covering and packing numbers).** Fix integers $n > k > \ell \ge 1$. A family $\mathcal{C}$ of $k$-subsets of $[n]$ is an **$(n,k,\ell)$-covering** if every $\ell$-subset of $[n]$ lies in at least one member of $\mathcal{C}$; the **covering number** $C(n,k,\ell)$ is the minimum size of such a family. A family $\mathcal{P}$ of $k$-subsets is an **$(n,k,\ell)$-packing** if every $\ell$-subset lies in at most one member; the **packing number** $D(n,k,\ell)$ is the maximum size. Counting incidences between $k$-sets and the $\binom{n}{\ell}$ target $\ell$-sets, with each $k$-set covering $\binom{k}{\ell}$ of them, gives the **fractional bounds**
$$
D(n,k,\ell) \le \frac{\binom{n}{\ell}}{\binom{k}{\ell}} \le C(n,k,\ell).
$$
A **Steiner system** $S(\ell, k, n)$ — when it exists — is simultaneously a packing and a covering meeting both bounds with equality (cross-ref [40.06.04]).

**Definition (semi-random / nibble process).** A **nibble** is one round of: (i) select each surviving candidate independently with a small probability $\varepsilon$ (the **bite size**); (ii) among the selected candidates, **activate** those that conflict with no other selected or previously chosen candidate; (iii) add the activated candidates to the output, mark the targets they now satisfy as **dead**, and pass the still-unsatisfied targets and unconflicted candidates as the **live** set to the next round. Iterating with a fixed bite size for $T = O(\varepsilon^{-1}\log(1/\delta))$ rounds drives the live target fraction below $\delta$.

**Definition (live-set trajectory).** Let $L_t$ be the number of live targets and $\Lambda_t$ a typical live candidate's residual degree after round $t$. The process is engineered so that, conditioned on the round-$t$ state, the expected one-step multiplicative change is $(1-\varepsilon)(1+o(1))$ for both, so the rescaled quantities follow the autonomous decay $x'(\tau) = -x(\tau)$ in continuous time $\tau = t\varepsilon$. Concentration of $L_t$, $\Lambda_t$ about this trajectory is the content of the analysis.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"One big random sample does the same job as many small nibbles."* A single sample of the right total size produces conflicts at a constant rate, killing a constant fraction of its own picks; the per-round bite must be $o(1)$ so that the conflict probability within a round is $o(1)$ and the activated fraction is $(1-o(1))$ of the selected.

- *"The nibble needs the hypergraph to be regular on the nose."* Near-regularity, $d(v) = (1\pm o(1))D$, suffices; the analysis only needs the live degrees to stay concentrated, not exactly equal.

- *"Small codegree is a convenience, not a hypothesis."* It is essential. If two vertices share $\Omega(D)$ edges, selecting one edge through the pair kills a constant fraction of the other's options in a single step, and the live degrees no longer decay smoothly — the trajectory description fails.

- *"The fractional bound is just a heuristic; the integral optimum could be far from it."* Erdős and Hanani conjectured, and Rödl proved, that for coverings and packings the integral optimum is asymptotically the fractional bound. The gap is $o(1)$ in ratio, not a constant factor.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is Rödl's theorem: the covering and packing numbers asymptotically meet the fractional bound, resolving the Erdős-Hanani conjecture. The proof is the prototype nibble, and the concentration estimates it needs are exactly the bounded-differences bounds of [40.07.05].

**Theorem (Rödl 1985; Erdős-Hanani conjecture).** *Fix integers $k > \ell \ge 1$. As $n \to \infty$,*
$$
C(n,k,\ell) = (1+o(1))\,\frac{\binom{n}{\ell}}{\binom{k}{\ell}}, \qquad D(n,k,\ell) = (1+o(1))\,\frac{\binom{n}{\ell}}{\binom{k}{\ell}}.
$$
[ref: TODO_REF Rödl 1985]

*Proof.* It suffices to prove the packing statement $D(n,k,\ell) \ge (1-o(1))\binom n\ell / \binom k\ell$; the covering statement follows by a symmetric argument that adds a thin layer of $k$-sets to cover the few leftover $\ell$-sets. Model the problem as a hypergraph $H$ whose vertices are the $\binom n\ell$ target $\ell$-sets and whose edges are the $k$-sets, each edge being the $\binom k\ell$-subset of $\ell$-sets it contains. Then $H$ is $\binom k\ell$-uniform, every vertex (an $\ell$-set) has degree $D = \binom{n-\ell}{k-\ell}$ (the $k$-sets through a fixed $\ell$-set), and the codegree of two $\ell$-sets is $O(\binom{n-\ell-1}{k-\ell-1}) = o(D)$ since extending two $\ell$-sets to a common $k$-set fixes more than $\ell$ points. A packing is a matching in $H$; a near-perfect matching covers $(1-o(1))\binom n\ell$ vertices and uses $(1-o(1))\binom n\ell/\binom k\ell$ edges.

Run the nibble on $H$ with bite size $\varepsilon$. In a round, select each live edge independently with probability $\varepsilon/D$, so a fixed vertex is covered by some selected edge with probability $\approx \varepsilon$. An edge is **activated** unless it shares a vertex with another selected edge; the expected number of competing selected edges through any of its $\binom k\ell$ vertices is $\binom k\ell \cdot D \cdot (\varepsilon/D) = \binom k\ell\,\varepsilon = O(\varepsilon)$, so an edge is activated with probability $1 - O(\varepsilon)$. Hence a vertex becomes dead in this round with probability $(1-O(\varepsilon))\varepsilon$, and the live-vertex count satisfies $\mathbb{E}[L_{t+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_t] = (1-\varepsilon+O(\varepsilon^2))L_t$. The residual degree of a surviving vertex contracts by the same factor in expectation, using small codegree to ensure that no single selected edge removes more than $o(D)$ of a vertex's options.

Both $L_t$ and the live degrees are functions of the independent edge-selection coins of round $t$, and altering one coin changes each by $O(1)$ relative to its scale; the bounded-differences inequality of [40.07.05] gives $L_{t+1} = (1-\varepsilon+O(\varepsilon^2))L_t (1+o(1))$ and degrees $= (1-\varepsilon+O(\varepsilon^2))^{} (1+o(1))D_t$ with probability $1 - \exp(-n^{\Omega(1)})$. Iterating $T = \lceil \varepsilon^{-1}\log(1/\delta)\rceil$ rounds and taking a union bound over rounds, with probability tending to $1$ the live-vertex fraction is at most $(1-\varepsilon)^T + o(1) \le \delta + o(1)$, while each round's activated edges form a partial matching and across all rounds they remain a single matching (an activated edge only touches live vertices, which are then killed). The matching covers $(1-\delta-o(1))\binom n\ell$ vertices, hence has $(1-\delta-o(1))\binom n\ell/\binom k\ell$ edges. Letting $\delta \to 0$ slowly with $n$ gives the claim. $\square$

**Bridge.** Rödl's theorem is the foundational reason the fractional relaxation of a packing problem is asymptotically achieved by an integral object: the nibble converts the greedy bound $\binom n\ell/\binom k\ell$ into a genuine matching by spending the slack as $o(1)$ losses spread thinly across many rounds, and this is exactly the move that the Pippenger-Spencer theorem of the Advanced results industrialises for arbitrary near-regular small-codegree hypergraphs. The construction builds toward the asymptotic existence of Steiner-system-like packings of [40.06.04], where the same matching-in-a-design-hypergraph reading appears again, and it generalises the deletion method of [40.07.02] from a single clean-up to an iterated one: where deletion removes defects once, the nibble removes a controlled fraction each round and re-bounds the survivors, so the bridge is that bounded-differences concentration from [40.07.05] is what makes the per-round bookkeeping survive iteration. Putting these together, the semi-random method is the first moment plus alteration run as a process rather than a single step, with concentration as the glue that keeps the expected trajectory honest at every round.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

In the hypergraph encoding of $D(n,k,\ell)$, show that the degree of every vertex (an $\ell$-set) is $D = \binom{n-\ell}{k-\ell}$ and that the codegree of two distinct $\ell$-sets is $o(D)$ as $n \to \infty$ with $k, \ell$ fixed.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A $k$-set through a fixed $\ell$-set is chosen by adding $k-\ell$ of the remaining $n-\ell$ points. For two $\ell$-sets, a common $k$-set must contain their union, of size $> \ell$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A vertex is an $\ell$-set $L$; an edge through it is a $k$-set $K \supseteq L$, determined by choosing the other $k-\ell$ points from $[n]\setminus L$, so $d(L) = \binom{n-\ell}{k-\ell} =: D \sim n^{k-\ell}/(k-\ell)!$. For two distinct $\ell$-sets $L_1, L_2$ with $|L_1 \cup L_2| = \ell + s$, $s \ge 1$, a common edge $K \supseteq L_1 \cup L_2$ is chosen by adding $k - \ell - s$ points from $[n] \setminus (L_1\cup L_2)$, so the codegree is $\binom{n-\ell-s}{k-\ell-s} \sim n^{k-\ell-s}/(k-\ell-s)! = O(n^{k-\ell-1})$. Hence codegree$/D = O(n^{-1}) = o(1)$, the small-codegree hypothesis of the nibble. Rubric: full credit for the degree count $\binom{n-\ell}{k-\ell}$, the union-size $\ge \ell+1$ observation, and the ratio $O(1/n) \to 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

In one nibble round on a $D$-regular $r$-uniform hypergraph, each edge is selected independently with probability $p = \varepsilon/D$. Show that a fixed edge $e$ is **activated** (no selected edge shares a vertex with it) with probability $1 - O(\varepsilon)$, and identify which hypothesis controls the error term.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Count the edges meeting $e$: at most $r$ vertices, each in $D-1$ other edges. Each is selected with probability $p$. Use a union bound and $rDp = r\varepsilon$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Edge $e$ has $r$ vertices, each lying in at most $D-1$ other edges, so at most $r(D-1)$ edges meet $e$ (codegree overlaps only reduce this count). Each such edge is selected with probability $p = \varepsilon/D$, so by a union bound the probability that some neighbour of $e$ is selected is at most $r(D-1)p \le rDp = r\varepsilon$. Hence, conditioned on $e$ itself being selected, it is activated with probability at least $1 - r\varepsilon = 1 - O(\varepsilon)$ for fixed $r$. The small-codegree hypothesis controls the second-order correction: without it, two vertices of $e$ could share many edges, making the union bound's overcount an undercount of the true correlation and spoiling the $1-O(\varepsilon)$ estimate's uniformity across edges. Rubric: full credit for the neighbour count $\le rD$, the union bound giving $1 - r\varepsilon$, and naming small codegree as the hypothesis behind uniformity.

</details>

</aside>

Let $L_t$ be the live-vertex count after round $t$, with $\mathbb{E}[L_{t+1}\mid\mathcal F_t] = (1-\varepsilon')L_t$ where $\varepsilon' = \varepsilon(1+o(1))$. Assuming each round's contribution concentrates, show that after $T = \lceil \varepsilon^{-1}\ln(1/\delta)\rceil$ rounds the expected live fraction is at most $\delta(1+o(1))$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Iterate the expectation to get $(1-\varepsilon')^T$, then use $1-\varepsilon' \le e^{-\varepsilon'}$ and $T\varepsilon' \ge \ln(1/\delta)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Taking expectations and iterating the one-step contraction, $\mathbb{E}[L_T]/L_0 = \prod_{t=0}^{T-1}(1-\varepsilon') = (1-\varepsilon')^T$. Using $1 - \varepsilon' \le e^{-\varepsilon'}$, $(1-\varepsilon')^T \le e^{-\varepsilon' T}$. With $T = \lceil \varepsilon^{-1}\ln(1/\delta)\rceil$ and $\varepsilon' = \varepsilon(1+o(1))$, we get $\varepsilon' T \ge (1+o(1))\ln(1/\delta)$, so $e^{-\varepsilon' T} \le \delta^{1+o(1)} = \delta(1+o(1))$ for fixed $\delta$. Hence the expected live fraction is at most $\delta(1+o(1))$. The bounded-differences inequality of [40.07.05] upgrades this expectation statement to a whp-statement, since $L_t$ changes by $O(1)$ per selection coin. Rubric: full credit for the product form $(1-\varepsilon')^T$, the exponential bound, and the substitution $T\varepsilon' \ge \ln(1/\delta)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

State the Pippenger-Spencer theorem and deduce the near-perfect-matching corollary: a nearly-regular $r$-uniform hypergraph with maximum codegree $o(D)$ has a matching covering $(1-o(1))|V|$ vertices.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Pippenger-Spencer bounds the chromatic index $\chi'(H) = (1+o(1))D$. A proper edge-colouring partitions the edges into matchings; the largest of the $(1+o(1))D$ colour classes must be big.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Theorem (Pippenger-Spencer).** If $H$ is $r$-uniform on $N$ vertices with every degree $(1\pm o(1))D$ and maximum codegree $o(D)$, then $\chi'(H) = (1+o(1))D$, where $\chi'(H)$ is the least number of matchings partitioning $\mathcal E(H)$. **Corollary.** The number of edges is $|\mathcal E| = (1\pm o(1))ND/r$ (sum of degrees over $r$), and they split into $(1+o(1))D$ matchings, so the average matching has $|\mathcal E|/\chi' = (1\pm o(1))N/r$ edges, covering $(1\pm o(1))N$ vertices; the largest class covers at least the average, giving a matching on $(1-o(1))N$ vertices. The proof of the theorem is the nibble run on edges-as-objects with $D$ available colours, each round colouring a random unconflicted slice and tracking the live (uncoloured) edges and available palette by [40.07.05] concentration. Rubric: full credit for the correct $\chi' = (1+o(1))D$ statement with both hypotheses, the edge count $ND/r$, and the averaging step extracting a near-perfect matching.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Explain in one paragraph why the bite size $\varepsilon$ must satisfy $\varepsilon \to 0$ (many rounds) rather than $\varepsilon = \Theta(1)$ (few rounds), and what role concentration plays in making the limit rigorous.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The activated fraction is $1 - O(\varepsilon)$ per round. If $\varepsilon$ is a constant, a constant fraction of picks self-conflict and are wasted, and the per-round loss no longer vanishes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If the bite size $\varepsilon$ is a constant, then within a single round a constant fraction $O(\varepsilon)$ of the selected candidates collide with one another and are discarded, so the round's loss relative to the ideal geometric contraction is a constant, not $o(1)$ — accumulated over the $O(1)$ rounds needed to finish, these constant losses leave a constant gap below the fractional bound, and the method achieves only $c\cdot\binom n\ell/\binom k\ell$ for some $c < 1$. Taking $\varepsilon \to 0$ makes the per-round self-conflict probability $O(\varepsilon) = o(1)$, so each round is $(1-o(1))$-efficient; the price is $T = \Theta(\varepsilon^{-1}\log(1/\delta))$ rounds, and the cumulative multiplicative error is $(1+o(1))^T$, which stays $1+o(1)$ only because the per-round error is $o(\varepsilon)$, i.e. summable. Concentration is what makes the limit rigorous: the expected trajectory $L_t \approx (1-\varepsilon)^t L_0$ is only an average, and over $T$ rounds the deviations could in principle compound; the bounded-differences inequality of [40.07.05] bounds each round's deviation by $\exp(-n^{\Omega(1)})$, so a union bound over all $T$ rounds keeps the entire trajectory within $(1+o(1))$ of its expectation with probability tending to $1$. Rubric: full credit for the constant-loss argument against $\varepsilon = \Theta(1)$, the $\varepsilon^{-1}$ round count, and the role of concentration plus union bound in controlling the whole trajectory.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The nibble matures into a general theory: the Pippenger-Spencer and Kahn theorems turn fractional relaxations into integral colourings for arbitrary near-regular small-codegree hypergraphs, the Ajtai-Komlós-Szemerédi process extracts large independent sets from sparse graphs, and Wormald's differential-equation method gives the systematic trajectory framing that makes "expected change per round" a rigorous description of the whole evolution.

**Theorem 1 (Pippenger-Spencer chromatic index).** Let $H$ be an $r$-uniform hypergraph on $N$ vertices, $r$ fixed, with all degrees $(1\pm o(1))D$ and maximum codegree $o(D)$ as $D \to \infty$. Then the chromatic index satisfies $\chi'(H) = (1+o(1))D$ [ref: TODO_REF Pippenger-Spencer 1989]. The lower bound $\chi' \ge \Delta = (1+o(1))D$ is immediate; the content is that the random-greedy semi-random colouring matches it, so the fractional edge-colouring bound is asymptotically integral. Reading one colour class at a time yields the near-perfect matching corollary that subsumes Rödl's theorem: the Erdős-Hanani hypergraph is near-regular with codegree $O(1/n)\cdot D$, so a matching covers $(1-o(1))$ of its vertices.

**Theorem 2 (Kahn's asymptotic list-edge-colouring).** Under the same near-regularity and small-codegree hypotheses, the *list* chromatic index is also $(1+o(1))D$: if each edge $e$ is given an arbitrary list $L(e)$ of $(1+o(1))D$ admissible colours, a proper colouring choosing each edge's colour from its own list still exists [ref: TODO_REF Kahn 1996]. The proof is a multi-round semi-random colouring in which each round assigns a random admissible colour to a slice of edges and retains the conflict-free assignments, with an entropy/concentration clean-up tracking the shrinking lists. This is the principle that *asymptotic goodness of the fractional relaxation forces integral goodness*, in its sharpest combinatorial form, and it implies the dense cases of the List-Edge-Colouring and Goldberg-Seymour conjectures.

**Theorem 3 (Ajtai-Komlós-Szemerédi; $R(3,k)$).** The semi-random method applied to the independent-set problem in triangle-free graphs of average degree $d$ produces an independent set of size $(1-o(1))\frac{n\ln d}{d}$, beating the greedy $n/d$ by the factor $\ln d$. Applied to the Ramsey graph, this yields $R(3,k) = O(k^2/\log k)$, which with Kim's matching lower bound (also semi-random, the *triangle-free process*) gives $R(3,k) = \Theta(k^2/\log k)$. The process reveals vertices in random order, greedily building an independent set while a concentration argument shows the surviving "uncoloured" neighbourhood shrinks along the predicted trajectory — the independent-set analogue of the live-degree decay in Rödl's proof.

**Theorem 4 (Wormald's differential-equation method).** Let $(\mathbf{Y}^{(m)})_{m\ge 0}$ be a random process on a state space, with bounded one-step changes $|Y_i^{(m+1)} - Y_i^{(m)}| \le \beta$ and conditional expected increment $\mathbb{E}[Y_i^{(m+1)} - Y_i^{(m)} \mid \mathcal F_m] = f_i(m/N, \mathbf{Y}^{(m)}/N) + o(1)$ for a Lipschitz $f$. Then, with the scaling $y_i(\tau) = Y_i^{(\tau N)}/N$, the process satisfies $y_i(\tau) = \hat y_i(\tau) + o(1)$ whp, uniformly until $\tau$ leaves the domain, where $\hat{\mathbf y}$ solves $\hat y_i'(\tau) = f_i(\tau, \hat{\mathbf y})$, $\hat{\mathbf y}(0) = \mathbf Y^{(0)}/N$ [ref: TODO_REF Wormald 1995]. The proof packages the bounded-differences inequality of [40.07.05]: the martingale of the rescaled trajectory has $O(\beta/N)$ increments, so over a window of $O(N)$ steps it stays within $o(1)$ of the integrated drift by Azuma plus a Grönwall comparison. The nibble's live-set sizes are precisely such bounded-step processes, and their decay $x'(\tau) = -x(\tau)$ is the autonomous system the method certifies.

**Theorem 5 (designs as the descendant; Keevash).** The asymptotic existence of designs — that for fixed $k, \ell$ and all large $n$ meeting the divisibility conditions there is an exact Steiner system $S(\ell, k, n)$ — was open for over a century and resolved by Keevash (2014) via *randomised algebraic constructions*, a far-reaching descendant of the nibble in which the semi-random matching is corrected to an exact one by an algebraic absorber [ref: TODO_REF Alon-Spencer 2016]. Rödl's nibble supplies the $(1-o(1))$-packing that Keevash's absorption upgrades to a perfect design; Glock-Kühn-Lo-Osthus gave an independent absorption proof. The lineage Erdős-Hanani conjecture $\to$ Rödl nibble $\to$ Pippenger-Spencer $\to$ Keevash is the through-line from "approximate packing" to "exact design."

**Synthesis.** The semi-random method is the deletion method of [40.07.02] iterated as a process, with bounded-differences concentration from [40.07.05] as the foundational reason the iteration is honest: each nibble removes a $(1-o(1))\varepsilon$-fraction of the live objects in expectation, and because the round's contribution is a bounded-difference function of independent selection coins, it concentrates around that expectation with $\exp(-n^{\Omega(1)})$ error, so a union bound over $O(\varepsilon^{-1}\log(1/\delta))$ rounds pins the whole trajectory $L_t \approx (1-\varepsilon)^t L_0$ to its mean. This is exactly the move that proves Rödl's resolution of the Erdős-Hanani conjecture, and the Pippenger-Spencer theorem generalises it from packings to the chromatic index of every near-regular small-codegree hypergraph, with Kahn's theorem sharpening it to the list setting — three faces of the central insight that asymptotic fractional optimality forces asymptotic integral optimality. The differential-equation method is dual to the round-by-round bookkeeping: where the nibble counts discrete rounds, Wormald reads the same data as the autonomous flow $x'(\tau) = -x(\tau)$ and certifies the discrete process tracks the continuous solution, which is the bridge from the heuristic "expected change per step" to a theorem. Putting these together, the lineage from the Ajtai-Komlós-Szemerédi $R(3,k)$ bound through Rödl and Pippenger-Spencer to Keevash's designs is one technique — build a near-optimal structure by many small random rounds with concentration-controlled clean-up — applied at successively higher resolution, and the absorption method that finishes the job is what converts the nibble's $(1-o(1))$-approximation into the long-sought exact object.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (one-round live-vertex contraction).** *Let $H$ be $D$-regular $r$-uniform with maximum codegree $C = o(D)$, and run one nibble round selecting each edge independently with probability $p = \varepsilon/D$. For a fixed live vertex $v$, the probability it becomes dead this round is $\varepsilon(1 - O(\varepsilon) - O(C/D))$.*

*Proof.* Vertex $v$ dies if at least one of its $D$ edges is activated. Let $A_v$ be the event that some edge through $v$ is selected: $\Pr(A_v) = 1 - (1-p)^D = 1 - (1-\varepsilon/D)^D = \varepsilon - \varepsilon^2/2 + O(\varepsilon^3) = \varepsilon(1-O(\varepsilon))$. Conditioned on a specific edge $e \ni v$ being selected, $e$ is activated unless a competing edge sharing one of $e$'s $r$ vertices is also selected; the number of such competitors is at most $r(D-1) - (\text{codegree overcount})$, each selected with probability $p$, so by inclusion-exclusion the activation probability is $1 - r(D-1)p + O((rDp)^2) = 1 - r\varepsilon + O(\varepsilon^2)$. The codegree enters because two vertices of $e$ may share up to $C$ edges, double-counted in $r(D-1)$; correcting this shifts the estimate by $O(rCp) = O(\varepsilon C/D)$. Combining, $v$ dies with probability $\varepsilon(1-O(\varepsilon))\cdot(1 - r\varepsilon + O(\varepsilon^2) + O(\varepsilon C/D)) = \varepsilon(1 - O(\varepsilon) - O(C/D))$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (concentration of the live count).** *With $H, p$ as above and $L_t$ the number of live vertices after round $t$, conditioned on $\mathcal F_t$, $L_{t+1}$ satisfies $\Pr(|L_{t+1} - \mathbb E[L_{t+1}\mid\mathcal F_t]| \ge \lambda) \le 2\exp(-2\lambda^2/(r^2 M_t))$, where $M_t$ is the number of edges selectable in round $t+1$.*

*Proof.* Condition on $\mathcal F_t$, fixing the live hypergraph. The randomness of round $t+1$ is the independent selection coin $\xi_e \in \{0,1\}$ for each of the $M_t$ live edges. Write $L_{t+1} = g(\xi)$, a function of these independent coins. Flipping one coin $\xi_e$ changes the activation status of $e$ and of the edges meeting $e$, altering the set of newly-dead vertices by at most the $\le r$ vertices of $e$ plus the vertices of edges sharing one of them; a careful accounting (an activated $e$ kills exactly its $r$ live vertices, and toggling $e$ can de-activate at most its immediate neighbours, each contributing $\le r$ vertices already counted) bounds the change in $L_{t+1}$ by $c_e = r$ uniformly. McDiarmid's bounded-differences inequality of [40.07.05] applied to $g$ with $\sum_e c_e^2 = r^2 M_t$ gives $\Pr(|L_{t+1} - \mathbb E[L_{t+1}\mid\mathcal F_t]| \ge \lambda) \le 2\exp(-2\lambda^2/(r^2 M_t))$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (trajectory bound over all rounds).** *For $\varepsilon \to 0$ slowly and $T = \lceil \varepsilon^{-1}\ln(1/\delta)\rceil$, with probability $1 - o(1)$ the live fraction after $T$ rounds is at most $\delta(1+o(1))$.*

*Proof.* By Proposition 1, $\mathbb E[L_{t+1}\mid\mathcal F_t] = (1 - \varepsilon(1-o(1)))L_t$ as long as $L_t = n^{\Omega(1)}$ (so degrees stay $n^{\Omega(1)}$ and the codegree correction $C/D$ remains $o(1)$). Set $\lambda_t = L_t^{2/3}$ in Proposition 2; since $M_t \le n L_t \le n^{O(1)}$ and $\lambda_t^2 = L_t^{4/3} \ge n^{\Omega(1)}\cdot M_t / n^{O(1)}$ in the regime $L_t \ge n^{c}$, the tail is $\exp(-n^{\Omega(1)})$. Thus, with failure probability $\exp(-n^{\Omega(1)})$ per round, $L_{t+1} = (1-\varepsilon(1-o(1)))L_t (1 \pm L_t^{-1/3})$. A union bound over the $T = n^{o(1)}$ rounds (for $\varepsilon \ge n^{-o(1)}$) keeps every round on track with probability $1 - T\exp(-n^{\Omega(1)}) = 1 - o(1)$. Multiplying the per-round factors, $L_T = L_0 \prod_{t<T}(1-\varepsilon(1-o(1)))(1\pm L_t^{-1/3}) = L_0 (1-\varepsilon)^T (1+o(1))$, and $(1-\varepsilon)^T \le e^{-\varepsilon T} \le \delta$ by the choice of $T$. The accumulated relative error $\prod (1\pm L_t^{-1/3})$ is $1+o(1)$ because $\sum_t L_t^{-1/3}$ is dominated by its smallest terms, which stay $n^{-\Omega(1)}$ until the process is stopped at live fraction $\delta$. Hence $L_T \le \delta(1+o(1))L_0$ whp. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Rödl packing lower bound).** *For fixed $k > \ell \ge 1$, $D(n,k,\ell) \ge (1-o(1))\binom n\ell/\binom k\ell$.*

*Proof.* Encode as the $\binom k\ell$-uniform hypergraph $H$ on the $\binom n\ell$ vertices (the $\ell$-sets) whose edges are the $k$-sets. By Exercise 3, $H$ is $D$-regular with $D = \binom{n-\ell}{k-\ell}$ and maximum codegree $O(D/n) = o(D)$. Run the nibble; the activated edges across all rounds form a matching $\mathcal M$ (an edge is activated only on currently-live vertices, which it then kills, so no two activated edges share a vertex). By Proposition 3, after $T = \lceil\varepsilon^{-1}\ln(1/\delta)\rceil$ rounds at most $\delta(1+o(1))\binom n\ell$ vertices remain live, so $\mathcal M$ covers $(1-\delta-o(1))\binom n\ell$ vertices and therefore contains $(1-\delta-o(1))\binom n\ell/\binom k\ell$ edges, each a $k$-set, no two sharing an $\ell$-set — an $(n,k,\ell)$-packing of that size. Sending $\delta \to 0$ slowly with $n$ gives $D(n,k,\ell) \ge (1-o(1))\binom n\ell/\binom k\ell$. The matching upper bound $D(n,k,\ell)\le \binom n\ell/\binom k\ell$ is the fractional count, so equality holds asymptotically. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (covering from packing).** *$C(n,k,\ell) \le (1+o(1))\binom n\ell/\binom k\ell$.*

*Proof.* Take the near-perfect packing $\mathcal P$ of Proposition 4, covering all but a set $U$ of at most $o(1)\binom n\ell$ $\ell$-sets. Cover each remaining $\ell$-set of $U$ by any single $k$-set containing it (one exists as $k > \ell$): this adds at most $|U| = o(1)\binom n\ell \le o(1)\binom n\ell/\binom k\ell \cdot \binom k\ell = o\!\big(\binom n\ell/\binom k\ell\big)$ extra $k$-sets once the bound $|U| = o(\binom n \ell)$ is divided through, since each added $k$-set still covers $\binom k\ell$ targets and the surplus is lower order. The union $\mathcal P \cup (\text{patch})$ is an $(n,k,\ell)$-covering of size $(1+o(1))\binom n\ell/\binom k\ell$, so $C(n,k,\ell)$ is at most this. With the fractional lower bound $C \ge \binom n\ell/\binom k\ell$, equality holds asymptotically. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The round-by-round control that makes the nibble rigorous is the bounded-differences (Azuma / McDiarmid) concentration of [40.07.05]: each round's newly-dead-vertex count is a bounded-difference function of that round's independent selection coins, so it concentrates around the contraction of Proposition 1 with $\exp(-n^{\Omega(1)})$ error, and the foundational reason the iterated process stays on its expected trajectory is the union bound over rounds that this exponential tail permits. Without that concentration the expected geometric decay would be only an average, and the cumulative deviation across $\Theta(\varepsilon^{-1}\log n)$ rounds could destroy the $(1+o(1))$ guarantee.

- The semi-random method is the deletion / alteration method of [40.07.02] run as a process rather than a single step: where deletion samples once and removes the rare defects, the nibble removes a controlled $(1-o(1))\varepsilon$-fraction each round and re-bounds the survivors, and the first-moment expectation calculations of [40.07.01] supply the per-round expected contraction that the alteration then realises. The Erdős-Hanani resolution is exactly this iterated alteration applied to the design hypergraph.

- The packings the nibble produces are the asymptotic version of the exact combinatorial designs of [40.06.04]: a Steiner system $S(\ell,k,n)$ is a perfect packing-and-covering meeting the fractional bound exactly, and the nibble shows that even when no exact system exists the optimum is within $(1+o(1))$ of the divisibility bound — the approximate statement that Keevash's absorption later upgraded to the exact existence of designs.

- The semi-random independent-set process behind the Ajtai-Komlós-Szemerédi bound $R(3,k) = \Theta(k^2/\log k)$ is the Ramsey-theoretic face of the same machine, complementing the discrepancy-theoretic control of structured deviations in [40.07.08]: where discrepancy measures how far a set system departs from its ideal balanced colouring, the nibble manufactures a near-ideal sparse structure whose deviations from the expected trajectory are held to $o(1)$ by concentration, and both are quantitative refinements of the probabilistic existence arguments of this chapter.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The covering-and-packing question was posed by **Paul Erdős and Haim Hanani in 1963**, who conjectured that the integral covering and packing numbers $C(n,k,\ell)$ and $D(n,k,\ell)$ approach the fractional (divisibility) bound $\binom n\ell/\binom k\ell$ in ratio as $n \to \infty$, and proved it for the boundary cases $\ell = k-1$ by elementary means. The general conjecture resisted for two decades until **Vojtěch Rödl's 1985 paper** in the *European Journal of Combinatorics* [ref: TODO_REF Rödl 1985], which introduced the iterated random-greedy "nibble" and a second-moment concentration argument to construct near-optimal packings directly; the technique was immediately recognised as a new general tool and the name *Rödl nibble* attached to it. **Frankl and Rödl** gave the near-perfect-matching formulation in the same period.

**Nicholas Pippenger and Joel Spencer's 1989 paper** [ref: TODO_REF Pippenger-Spencer 1989] in the *Journal of Combinatorial Theory* abstracted the method to the chromatic index of nearly-regular small-codegree uniform hypergraphs, and **Jeff Kahn's 1996 paper** [ref: TODO_REF Kahn 1996] extended it to the list setting, establishing that asymptotic fractional optimality forces integral optimality across a wide class. **Noga Wormald** [ref: TODO_REF Wormald 1995] systematised the trajectory analysis as the differential-equation method, identifying the autonomous Lipschitz system whose solution the bounded-step process tracks. The earlier **Ajtai-Komlós-Szemerédi** semi-random independent-set process (1980-81) had already used the same philosophy to prove $R(3,k) = O(k^2/\log k)$, later matched below by Kim's triangle-free process. The line culminates in **Keevash's 2014** randomised-algebraic resolution of the existence of designs, which combines the nibble with an absorption step to convert near-perfect packings into exact Steiner systems, settling a question open since the nineteenth century [ref: TODO_REF Alon-Spencer 2016].
