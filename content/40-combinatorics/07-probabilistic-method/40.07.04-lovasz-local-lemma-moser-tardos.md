## Intuition [Beginner]

The first-moment method proves a good object exists when the average number of flaws is below one. But often there are so many possible flaws that their average is enormous — thousands of bad spots on average — and the first moment says nothing useful. Yet you may still feel the object should exist, because each flaw involves only a few ingredients, and flaws far apart in the structure have nothing to do with one another. The Lovász Local Lemma turns that feeling into a proof.

Here is the idea. List all the bad events you want to avoid. Make each one rare on its own, with probability at most some small $p$. Now suppose each bad event is tangled up with only a few others — it shares ingredients with at most $d$ of them — and is otherwise completely independent of the rest. The lemma says: if rarity beats local crowding, by the clean rule that $p$ times about $d$ stays below a fixed budget, then with positive probability *every single* bad event is avoided at once. You do not need the average flaw count below one; you only need each flaw rare and each flaw lonely.

The second half of the story is that you can actually *find* the good object, not just know it exists. Start with a random guess. Whenever some bad event is currently happening, locally re-roll just the ingredients it touches, and repeat. This patient "fix the conflict in front of you" loop stops quickly, leaving a configuration with no bad events left.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture each bad event as a dot, and draw a line between two dots when those two events share an ingredient and so can influence each other. This picture is the dependency graph. A dot with few lines is a bad event that is nearly independent of everything; a dot with many lines is heavily entangled. The local lemma cares only about the *largest* number of lines at any dot — the local crowding $d$ — not about how many dots there are in total. A million lonely dots, each touching at most three others, are all avoidable together.

![A scatter of dots, each dot a bad event; thin lines connect dots that share an ingredient and can influence one another. Most dots have only two or three lines. A callout marks the busiest dot and labels its line count d. Beside it, a second small picture shows a random assignment with one bad event lit red, an arrow re-rolling just its ingredients, and the red event turning off.](/img/lovasz-local-lemma-moser-tardos-placeholder.svg)

| Quantity | Meaning | Local-lemma role |
|---|---|---|
| $p$ | how rare each bad event is | smaller is better |
| $d$ | most neighbours any event has | the local crowding |
| budget $e \times p \times (d+1)$ | rarity versus crowding | must stay at most $1$ |

The table is the whole criterion: keep the product $e \times p \times (d + 1)$ at or below $1$, and a flawless configuration is guaranteed to exist.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Suppose you have a long list of logical clauses, and you want one true/false setting of the variables that satisfies all of them. Each clause is a small demand on, say, $k = 3$ variables; a clause is "broken" by a random coin-flip setting exactly when all $3$ of its variables come out the one wrong way. We will see when a satisfying setting must exist.

Step 1. Find how rare one broken clause is. A clause of $3$ variables is broken only by one specific combination out of the $2 \times 2 \times 2 = 8$ equally likely settings of its three variables. So the chance a fixed clause is broken is $p = 1/8$.

Step 2. Count local crowding. Say each clause shares a variable with at most $d$ other clauses. Two clauses can conflict only when they share a variable, so $d$ is the largest number of clause-neighbours any clause has.

Step 3. Apply the budget rule $e \times p \times (d + 1) \le 1$. Using $e$ about $2.718$ and $p = 1/8$, the rule reads $2.718 \times \tfrac{1}{8} \times (d+1) \le 1$, that is $(d + 1) \le 8/2.718 \approx 2.94$. So $d + 1$ can be as large as $2$, giving $d = 1$.

Step 4. Read off the guarantee. If every clause shares variables with at most $d = 1$ other clause, the budget holds and a satisfying setting is guaranteed to exist — no matter how many clauses there are in total.

What this tells us: rarity ($p = 1/8$) and crowding ($d$) trade off through one clean inequality, and the total number of clauses never enters. For $3$-variable clauses the honest threshold grows like $2^3 = 8$ neighbours once the constant $e$ is handled sharply; the rough hand-count here keeps the arithmetic plain.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $A_1, \dots, A_n$ are events on a common probability space, thought of as the *bad* events one wishes to avoid simultaneously; the goal is to show $\Pr\big[\bigcap_{i} \overline{A_i}\big] > 0$. Basic probability — conditional probability, independence of families of events — is imported from the probability units $37.0\ast$ and applied here, not reproved.

**Definition (dependency graph).** A graph $D$ on vertex set $\{1, \dots, n\}$ is a **dependency graph** for the events $A_1, \dots, A_n$ if for each $i$ the event $A_i$ is *mutually independent* of the family $\{A_j : (i,j) \notin E(D)\}$ — that is, $A_i$ is independent of every event built from the $A_j$ with $j$ not adjacent to $i$. Write $\Gamma(i) = \{j : (i,j) \in E(D)\}$ for the neighbourhood; the events outside $\Gamma(i) \cup \{i\}$ exert no influence on $A_i$.

**The general (asymmetric) Lovász Local Lemma.** Suppose $D$ is a dependency graph for $A_1, \dots, A_n$ and there exist reals $x_1, \dots, x_n \in [0, 1)$ with
$$
\Pr[A_i] \;\le\; x_i \prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)} (1 - x_j) \qquad \text{for every } i.
$$
Then
$$
\Pr\Big[\bigcap_{i=1}^{n} \overline{A_i}\Big] \;\ge\; \prod_{i=1}^{n} (1 - x_i) \;>\; 0 ,
$$
so with positive probability none of the bad events occurs [ref: TODO_REF Alon-Spencer Ch. 5]. The weights $x_i$ are a free parameter chosen per application; the inequality says each event must be rarer than a budget discounted by the weights on its neighbours.

**The symmetric Lovász Local Lemma.** If each $A_i$ has $\Pr[A_i] \le p$ and the dependency graph has maximum degree at most $d$ (each event mutually independent of all but at most $d$ others), and
$$
e\,p\,(d + 1) \le 1 ,
$$
then $\Pr\big[\bigcap_i \overline{A_i}\big] > 0$. This follows from the general form by setting every $x_i = 1/(d+1)$ and using $(1 - 1/(d+1))^{d} > 1/e$.

The notation $\Gamma(i)$, $\overline{A_i}$, $\Pr[\cdot \mid \cdot]$, the dependency graph $D$, and the weights $x_i$ are registered in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"A dependency graph records all statistical correlation."* It records *enough* independence: $A_i$ must be independent of the joint behaviour of all non-neighbours, not merely pairwise uncorrelated. Pairwise independence does not suffice; mutual independence of the complement family is the requirement.

- *"The symmetric form needs $ep\,d \le 1$."* The correct factor is $d + 1$, not $d$: an event is counted among its own "forbidden" set, so the budget is $e\,p\,(d+1) \le 1$. Dropping the $+1$ overstates how much crowding is tolerated.

- *"$x_i$ must equal $\Pr[A_i]$."* The $x_i$ are slack variables, not the probabilities; one needs only $\Pr[A_i] \le x_i \prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)$, and choosing $x_i$ a little above $\Pr[A_i]$ to leave room is the standard move. The conclusion $\prod(1 - x_i)$ is then a valid lower bound on the survival probability.

- *"Positive probability means a large fraction."* The bound $\prod(1 - x_i)$ is typically exponentially small; the lemma is an *existence* statement, and a positive—however tiny—probability is exactly what existence requires.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is the general local lemma itself; its applications to $k$-SAT and hypergraph colouring follow as corollaries. The proof is the classical conditional-probability induction of Erdős and Lovász [ref: TODO_REF Erdős-Lovász 1975].

**Theorem (general Lovász Local Lemma).** *Let $A_1, \dots, A_n$ have dependency graph $D$, and let $x_1, \dots, x_n \in [0,1)$ satisfy $\Pr[A_i] \le x_i \prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)$ for all $i$. Then for every $i$ and every subset $S \subseteq \{1,\dots,n\} \setminus \{i\}$,*
$$
\Pr\Big[A_i \,\Big|\, \bigcap_{j \in S} \overline{A_j}\Big] \;\le\; x_i ,
$$
*and consequently $\Pr\big[\bigcap_{i} \overline{A_i}\big] \ge \prod_i (1 - x_i) > 0$.*

*Proof.* The conditional bound is proved by induction on $|S|$. For $S = \varnothing$, $\Pr[A_i] \le x_i \prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j) \le x_i$ since each factor is at most $1$. For the inductive step, split $S = S_1 \cup S_2$ where $S_1 = S \cap \Gamma(i)$ are the neighbours of $i$ in $S$ and $S_2 = S \setminus \Gamma(i)$ the non-neighbours. Write the conditional probability as a ratio,
$$
\Pr\Big[A_i \,\Big|\, \bigcap_{j \in S} \overline{A_j}\Big]
= \frac{\Pr\big[A_i \cap \bigcap_{j \in S_1}\overline{A_j} \,\big|\, \bigcap_{k \in S_2}\overline{A_k}\big]}
       {\Pr\big[\bigcap_{j \in S_1}\overline{A_j} \,\big|\, \bigcap_{k \in S_2}\overline{A_k}\big]} .
$$
Bound the numerator from above. Dropping the conditioning on $\bigcap_{j \in S_1}\overline{A_j}$ inside the numerator and using that $A_i$ is mutually independent of $\{A_k : k \in S_2\}$ (as $S_2 \cap \Gamma(i) = \varnothing$),
$$
\Pr\Big[A_i \cap \bigcap_{j \in S_1}\overline{A_j} \,\Big|\, \bigcap_{k \in S_2}\overline{A_k}\Big]
\le \Pr\Big[A_i \,\Big|\, \bigcap_{k \in S_2}\overline{A_k}\Big]
= \Pr[A_i] \le x_i \prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j).
$$

Now bound the denominator from below. Enumerate $S_1 = \{j_1, \dots, j_r\}$ and expand by the chain rule,
$$
\Pr\Big[\bigcap_{\ell=1}^{r}\overline{A_{j_\ell}} \,\Big|\, \bigcap_{k \in S_2}\overline{A_k}\Big]
= \prod_{\ell=1}^{r}\Pr\Big[\overline{A_{j_\ell}} \,\Big|\, \bigcap_{m < \ell}\overline{A_{j_m}} \cap \bigcap_{k \in S_2}\overline{A_k}\Big]
\ge \prod_{\ell=1}^{r}(1 - x_{j_\ell}),
$$
where each factor uses the induction hypothesis on a conditioning set of size $< |S|$ to give $\Pr[A_{j_\ell} \mid \cdots] \le x_{j_\ell}$, hence $\Pr[\overline{A_{j_\ell}} \mid \cdots] \ge 1 - x_{j_\ell}$. Since $S_1 \subseteq \Gamma(i)$, the product $\prod_{\ell}(1 - x_{j_\ell})$ is a sub-product of $\prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)$, and all factors lie in $(0,1]$, so the denominator is at least $\prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)$. Dividing,
$$
\Pr\Big[A_i \,\Big|\, \bigcap_{j \in S}\overline{A_j}\Big]
\le \frac{x_i \prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)}{\prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)} = x_i .
$$

Finally, by the chain rule over all $n$ events,
$$
\Pr\Big[\bigcap_{i=1}^{n}\overline{A_i}\Big]
= \prod_{i=1}^{n}\Pr\Big[\overline{A_i} \,\Big|\, \bigcap_{m < i}\overline{A_m}\Big]
\ge \prod_{i=1}^{n}(1 - x_i) > 0,
$$
each factor bounded below using the conditional estimate just proved. $\square$

**Corollary ($k$-SAT).** *Let $\Phi$ be a $k$-CNF formula in which every clause shares a variable with at most $d$ other clauses. If $e(d + 1) \le 2^k$, then $\Phi$ is satisfiable.* *Proof.* Assign each variable true/false by an independent fair coin. For clause $C$ let $A_C$ be the event that $C$ is unsatisfied; a $k$-clause is violated by exactly one of the $2^k$ settings of its variables, so $\Pr[A_C] = 2^{-k} =: p$. Two clause-events are dependent only when the clauses share a variable, so the dependency degree is at most $d$. The hypothesis $e(d+1) \le 2^k$ is $e\,p\,(d+1) \le 1$; the symmetric local lemma gives $\Pr[\bigcap_C \overline{A_C}] > 0$, so a satisfying assignment exists. $\square$

**Corollary (hypergraph 2-colouring / property B).** *A $k$-uniform hypergraph in which every edge meets at most $d$ others is 2-colourable (property B) whenever $e(d + 1) \le 2^{k-1}$.* *Proof.* Colour each vertex red/blue independently and uniformly. For edge $f$ let $A_f$ be the event that $f$ is monochromatic; $\Pr[A_f] = 2 \cdot 2^{-k} = 2^{1-k}$, and $A_f$ depends only on edges sharing a vertex with $f$, at most $d$ of them. Then $e\,2^{1-k}(d+1) \le 1$ is $e(d+1) \le 2^{k-1}$, and the symmetric form gives a proper 2-colouring. $\square$

**Bridge.** The local lemma builds toward every existence proof where the first moment is hopeless because there are too many bad events: it replaces the global demand "expected defects below one" with the local pair "each defect rare, each defect lonely", and the foundational reason this works is the conditional estimate $\Pr[A_i \mid \bigcap_{j \in S}\overline{A_j}] \le x_i$, which says that avoiding other bad events never inflates a given one beyond its private budget $x_i$. This is exactly the move that the deletion method of [40.07.02] could only approximate by removing defects after the fact; here the defects are forbidden *simultaneously*, and the bridge is that the same indicator-per-defect bookkeeping which gave $\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_i \Pr[A_i]$ there is now organised by the dependency graph rather than summed flat. The $k$-SAT and colouring corollaries appear again in the algorithmic story below, where the Moser-Tardos process does not merely certify existence but constructs the assignment; putting these together, the local lemma is the dependency-graph generalisation of the union bound, and its constructive counterpart is the central insight of the modern theory.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the symmetric local lemma from the general form: assuming $\Pr[A_i] \le p$ and degree $\le d$, set $x_i = 1/(d+1)$ and show $e\,p\,(d+1) \le 1$ implies $\Pr[A_i] \le x_i\prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

With $x_i = 1/(d+1)$, $\prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j) \ge (1 - 1/(d+1))^d$. Use the inequality $(1 - 1/(d+1))^d > 1/e$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Put $x_i = 1/(d+1)$ for all $i$. Since $|\Gamma(i)| \le d$ and each $1 - x_j = 1 - 1/(d+1) = d/(d+1) \in (0,1)$, the product over neighbours satisfies $\prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j) \ge \big(\tfrac{d}{d+1}\big)^d = \big(1 - \tfrac{1}{d+1}\big)^d$. The sequence $(1 - 1/(d+1))^d$ increases to $1/e$ and exceeds $1/e$ for all $d \ge 1$ (equivalently $(1 + 1/d)^{d} < e \le (1+1/d)^{d+1}$ gives $(d/(d+1))^d = (1+1/d)^{-d} > 1/e$). Hence $x_i \prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j) \ge \tfrac{1}{d+1}\cdot \tfrac{1}{e} = \tfrac{1}{e(d+1)}$. The hypothesis $e\,p\,(d+1) \le 1$ rearranges to $p \le \tfrac{1}{e(d+1)} \le x_i\prod_{j\in\Gamma(i)}(1-x_j)$, and since $\Pr[A_i] \le p$ the general-form inequality holds for every $i$. The general local lemma then gives $\Pr[\bigcap \overline{A_i}] \ge (1 - 1/(d+1))^n > 0$. Rubric: full credit for the choice $x_i = 1/(d+1)$, the bound $(d/(d+1))^d > 1/e$, and the rearrangement to $p \le 1/(e(d+1))$.

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

The Erdős-Lovász proof certifies $\Pr[\bigcap_i \overline{A_i}] \ge \prod_i (1 - x_i) > 0$, but that lower bound is typically exponentially small, so drawing fresh random assignments until all bad events vanish takes exponentially many tries — the existence proof gives no efficient search. The Moser-Tardos algorithm replaces global resampling with local repair: start from one random assignment of the underlying independent variables, and while any bad event $A_i$ currently holds, resample exactly the variables that $A_i$ depends on, repeating until none holds. The key theorem is that, under the same asymmetric condition $\Pr[A_i] \le x_i\prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)$, the expected total number of resamplings is at most $\sum_i x_i/(1 - x_i)$, a polynomial quantity in the relevant parameters — so the algorithm terminates fast and outputs a configuration avoiding every $A_i$. ## Advanced results [Master]

The existence lemma is the floor; its algorithmic, optimal, and lopsided refinements are the structure built on it. The Moser-Tardos algorithm makes the lemma constructive under the identical hypothesis, Shearer's bound identifies the exact boundary of avoidability for a fixed dependency graph, and the lopsided form trades independence for one-sided negative dependence.

**Theorem 1 (Moser-Tardos algorithmic local lemma; 2010).** Suppose each event $A_i$ is determined by a subset $\mathrm{vbl}(A_i)$ of independent random variables $\mathcal{P}$, with the dependency graph given by shared variables, and the asymmetric condition $\Pr[A_i] \le x_i \prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)$ holds. The sequential resampling process — repeatedly pick any currently-violated $A_i$ and resample all of $\mathrm{vbl}(A_i)$ — resamples event $A_i$ an expected number of times at most $x_i/(1 - x_i)$, so the expected total number of resampling steps is at most $\sum_i x_i/(1 - x_i)$, and the process terminates almost surely with an assignment avoiding all $A_i$ [ref: TODO_REF Moser-Tardos 2010]. In the symmetric regime $x_i = 1/(d+1)$ this is $O(n/d)$ expected resamplings per event in the relevant scaling, a polynomial-time constructive local lemma.

**Theorem 2 (witness-tree encoding).** Each resampling of $A_i$ during a run is assigned a *witness tree* $\tau$: a rooted tree labelled by events, recording the causal history of resamplings that forced this one, in which a child's event shares a variable with (or equals) its parent's event, and siblings carry distinct events. Two key facts drive the analysis. First, distinct resamplings of the same event produce distinct witness trees, so the number of resamplings is bounded by the number of *proper* witness trees that occur. Second, a fixed witness tree $\tau$ occurs in the execution with probability at most $\prod_{v \in \tau}\Pr[A_{[v]}]$, because the random choices that the tree certifies are independent and each must realise its event. Summing $\prod_v \Pr[A_{[v]}]$ over all witness trees rooted at $A_i$ — a sum controlled by a Galton-Watson branching process with offspring weights $x_j$ — gives the bound $x_i/(1 - x_i)$ of Theorem 1 [ref: TODO_REF Moser-Tardos 2010].

**Theorem 3 (entropy compression, Moser's earlier argument).** The witness-tree bound has an information-theoretic shadow: a run of $T$ resamplings consumes $T \cdot (\text{bits per resample})$ uniformly random bits, yet the entire run can be reconstructed from the final state plus an encoding of the witness forest whose length is $T \cdot (\text{bits}) - \Omega(T)$ when the local-lemma slack is strict. A run longer than a threshold would compress a random string below its entropy, which is impossible; hence the run is short with overwhelming probability. This *entropy-compression* method (Moser, STOC 2009) preceded the clean witness-tree probability bound and seeded a family of direct combinatorial arguments [ref: TODO_REF Moser-Tardos 2010].

**Theorem 4 (Shearer's optimal bound; 1985).** For a fixed dependency graph $D$, the set of probability vectors $(\Pr[A_i])_i$ for which *every* compatible family of events can be avoided is described exactly by the **independence polynomial** of $D$: avoidance is guaranteed iff for all induced subgraphs the alternating independent-set sums stay positive. For the $d$-regular dependency graph the optimal threshold is $p \le \frac{(d-1)^{d-1}}{d^d}$, asymptotic to $\frac{1}{e d}$ as $d \to \infty$ — improving the symmetric criterion's $\frac{1}{e(d+1)}$ by the missing additive unit and provably best possible: above Shearer's boundary there exist events meeting the probability constraints that cannot all be avoided [ref: TODO_REF Shearer 1985]. Shearer's condition is the local lemma's tight form; the Erdős-Lovász criterion is a convenient sufficient relaxation.

**Theorem 5 (lopsided / lopsidependency local lemma).** The mutual-independence hypothesis can be weakened to *one-sided negative dependence*: $D$ is a **lopsidependency graph** if $\Pr[A_i \mid \bigcap_{j \in S}\overline{A_j}] \le \Pr[A_i]$ for every $i$ and every $S \subseteq \{1, \dots, n\}\setminus(\Gamma(i)\cup\{i\})$. Under $\Pr[A_i] \le x_i\prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)$ the same conclusion $\Pr[\bigcap \overline{A_i}] \ge \prod(1 - x_i)$ holds. This covers random-permutation models — Latin transversals, the Erdős-Spencer pattern-avoidance bounds — where conditioning on avoiding some conflicts only *helps* avoid others, and the events are not independent but are negatively associated [ref: TODO_REF Alon-Spencer Ch. 5].

**Synthesis.** Putting these together, the local lemma is a single inequality read four ways: the existence form of Erdős-Lovász, the constructive form of Moser-Tardos, the optimal form of Shearer, and the negatively-dependent lopsided form, and the foundational reason all four cohere is the conditional bound $\Pr[A_i \mid \text{avoid a set of others}] \le x_i$ — once that holds, the chain rule delivers a positive survival probability, a branching-process sum delivers a polynomial resampling count, and the independence polynomial delivers the exact boundary. This is exactly the generalisation of the deletion method of [40.07.02] that the bridge there pointed to: where deletion tolerates a few defects and removes them, the local lemma forbids all defects at once by exploiting that they are locally sparse, and the Moser-Tardos process is the algorithmic deletion — it resamples defects out of existence. The central insight is that bounded dependence is as good as independence for existence, and the entropy-compression argument shows it is as good for *computation*: a long failing run would compress randomness past its entropy. This is dual to the second-moment method of [40.07.03], which controls the *variance* of a single global count, whereas the local lemma controls the *interaction structure* of many local events; both convert local information into a global existence verdict, and the modern theory of algorithmic sampling and approximate counting grows from Shearer's exact boundary.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (general local lemma, conditional estimate).** *Under the hypotheses $\Pr[A_i] \le x_i\prod_{j \in \Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)$ with $x_i \in [0,1)$, for every $i$ and every $S \subseteq \{1,\dots,n\}\setminus\{i\}$, $\Pr[A_i \mid \bigcap_{j \in S}\overline{A_j}] \le x_i$.*

*Proof.* Induct on $|S|$. If $S = \varnothing$, $\Pr[A_i] \le x_i\prod_{j\in\Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j) \le x_i$. Otherwise partition $S = S_1 \sqcup S_2$ with $S_1 = S \cap \Gamma(i)$, $S_2 = S \setminus \Gamma(i)$; assume the conditioning event has positive probability (else there is nothing to bound). Then
$$
\Pr\Big[A_i \,\Big|\, \bigcap_{j\in S}\overline{A_j}\Big] = \frac{\Pr[A_i \cap \bigcap_{j\in S_1}\overline{A_j}\mid \bigcap_{k\in S_2}\overline{A_k}]}{\Pr[\bigcap_{j\in S_1}\overline{A_j}\mid \bigcap_{k\in S_2}\overline{A_k}]}.
$$
For the numerator, $A_i \cap \bigcap_{j\in S_1}\overline{A_j} \subseteq A_i$, and since $S_2 \cap \Gamma(i) = \varnothing$ the event $A_i$ is independent of $\bigcap_{k\in S_2}\overline{A_k}$, so the numerator is $\le \Pr[A_i \mid \bigcap_{k\in S_2}\overline{A_k}] = \Pr[A_i] \le x_i\prod_{j\in\Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)$. For the denominator, write $S_1 = \{j_1,\dots,j_r\}$ and apply the chain rule; each factor $\Pr[\overline{A_{j_\ell}}\mid \bigcap_{m<\ell}\overline{A_{j_m}}\cap\bigcap_{k\in S_2}\overline{A_k}]$ has conditioning set of size $< |S|$, so the induction hypothesis gives $\Pr[A_{j_\ell}\mid\cdots]\le x_{j_\ell}$ and the factor is $\ge 1 - x_{j_\ell}$. Thus the denominator is $\ge \prod_{\ell=1}^r (1 - x_{j_\ell}) \ge \prod_{j\in\Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)$, since $S_1 \subseteq \Gamma(i)$ and the extra factors lie in $(0,1]$. Dividing, $\Pr[A_i \mid \bigcap_{j\in S}\overline{A_j}] \le x_i$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (positive survival probability).** *Under the same hypotheses, $\Pr[\bigcap_{i=1}^n \overline{A_i}] \ge \prod_{i=1}^n (1 - x_i) > 0$.*

*Proof.* By the chain rule, $\Pr[\bigcap_{i=1}^n \overline{A_i}] = \prod_{i=1}^n \Pr[\overline{A_i}\mid \bigcap_{m<i}\overline{A_m}]$. Each factor equals $1 - \Pr[A_i \mid \bigcap_{m<i}\overline{A_m}] \ge 1 - x_i$ by Proposition 1 applied to $S = \{1,\dots,i-1\}\setminus\{i\}$. Hence the product is $\ge \prod_i(1 - x_i)$, which is positive since each $x_i < 1$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (symmetric criterion).** *If $\Pr[A_i] \le p$ for all $i$, the dependency graph has maximum degree $\le d$, and $e\,p\,(d+1) \le 1$, then $\Pr[\bigcap_i \overline{A_i}] > 0$.*

*Proof.* Set $x_i = 1/(d+1)$. For $d \ge 1$, $\big(1 - \tfrac{1}{d+1}\big)^{d} = \big(\tfrac{d}{d+1}\big)^d = \big(1 + \tfrac 1d\big)^{-d} > e^{-1}$, since $(1 + 1/d)^d < e$. Therefore $x_i\prod_{j\in\Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j) \ge \tfrac{1}{d+1}\big(\tfrac{d}{d+1}\big)^d > \tfrac{1}{e(d+1)} \ge p \ge \Pr[A_i]$, the middle inequality from $e\,p\,(d+1)\le 1$. The general local lemma (Propositions 1-2) applies and gives $\Pr[\bigcap_i\overline{A_i}] \ge (1 - 1/(d+1))^n > 0$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 ($k$-SAT satisfiability).** *A $k$-CNF formula in which every clause shares a variable with at most $2^k/e - 1$ other clauses is satisfiable.*

*Proof.* Take an independent uniform assignment of the variables. For each clause $C$, $A_C = \{C\text{ unsatisfied}\}$ has $\Pr[A_C] = 2^{-k}$, the single falsifying assignment of $C$'s $k$ variables. The dependency graph joins clauses sharing a variable, of maximum degree $d \le 2^k/e - 1$. Then $e\,p\,(d+1) \le e\cdot 2^{-k}\cdot 2^k/e = 1$, so Proposition 3 gives $\Pr[\bigcap_C\overline{A_C}] > 0$; an assignment satisfying every clause exists. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (Moser-Tardos termination).** *In the variable model with $\Pr[A_i] \le x_i\prod_{j\in\Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)$, the resampling process resamples $A_i$ an expected $\le x_i/(1 - x_i)$ times; the expected total number of resamplings is $\le \sum_i x_i/(1 - x_i)$, finite, so the process halts almost surely at an assignment avoiding all $A_i$.*

*Proof sketch (witness trees).* Order the resamplings as they occur and attach to the $t$-th resampling (of some event $A_i$) a rooted **witness tree** $\tau_t$: the root is labelled $A_i$, and reading the resampling log backwards, each earlier resampling of an event $A_j$ sharing a variable with a node already in the tree is attached as a child of the deepest such node; siblings receive distinct labels. Distinct resamplings of $A_i$ yield distinct witness trees (the log reconstructs uniquely), so the number of times $A_i$ is resampled is at most the number of proper witness trees rooted at $A_i$ that *occur*. A fixed tree $\tau$ occurs only if, for each node $v$, the independent fresh variables drawn when $v$ was resampled realised $A_{[v]}$; these draws are independent across nodes, so $\Pr[\tau\text{ occurs}] \le \prod_{v\in\tau}\Pr[A_{[v]}]$. Summing over all witness trees rooted at $A_i$,
$$
\mathbb{E}[\#\text{resamplings of }A_i] \le \sum_{\tau\text{ rooted at }A_i}\prod_{v\in\tau}\Pr[A_{[v]}] \le \sum_{\tau}\prod_{v\in\tau}x_{[v]}\!\!\prod_{j\in\Gamma([v])}\!\!(1 - x_j).
$$
The right side is the total weight of a Galton-Watson branching process in which a node labelled $A_j$ has, for each $k \in \Gamma(j)\cup\{j\}$, an independent child labelled $A_k$ with "probability" $x_k$; the expected size of this process rooted at $A_i$ is exactly $x_i/(1 - x_i)$ (the standard generating-function identity for the sub-critical branching weights, valid since the $x_k$ satisfy the local-lemma inequality). Summing over $i$ gives $\mathbb{E}[\text{total resamplings}] \le \sum_i x_i/(1 - x_i) < \infty$. A process with finite expected length halts almost surely, and when it halts no $A_i$ is violated. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (lopsided local lemma).** *If $D$ is a lopsidependency graph — $\Pr[A_i \mid \bigcap_{j\in S}\overline{A_j}] \le \Pr[A_i]$ for all $i$ and $S \subseteq \{1,\dots,n\}\setminus(\Gamma(i)\cup\{i\})$ — and $\Pr[A_i] \le x_i\prod_{j\in\Gamma(i)}(1 - x_j)$, then $\Pr[\bigcap_i\overline{A_i}] \ge \prod_i(1 - x_i) > 0$.*

*Proof.* Repeat the induction of Proposition 1. The only place mutual independence was used is the numerator bound $\Pr[A_i \mid \bigcap_{k\in S_2}\overline{A_k}] = \Pr[A_i]$ with $S_2 \cap \Gamma(i) = \varnothing$. Under the lopsidependency hypothesis this equality is replaced by the inequality $\Pr[A_i \mid \bigcap_{k\in S_2}\overline{A_k}] \le \Pr[A_i]$, which is all the argument requires, since the numerator is bounded *above*. Every other step — the chain-rule lower bound on the denominator and the final telescoping — is unchanged, giving $\Pr[A_i\mid\bigcap_{j\in S}\overline{A_j}]\le x_i$ and hence $\Pr[\bigcap_i\overline{A_i}]\ge\prod_i(1 - x_i)$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The deletion method of [40.07.02] is the local lemma's predecessor on the same problems: deletion removes the rare defects from a random object after the fact, while the local lemma forbids them simultaneously by exploiting that each defect is mutually independent of all but a bounded number of others. The Ramsey and hypergraph-2-colouring applications recur in both, but the local-lemma constant is sharper — property B needs only the local edge-meeting degree below $2^{k-1}/e$, not the total edge count below $2^{k-1}$ — because bounded dependence replaces the global first-moment budget with a local one.

- The second-moment method of [40.07.03] is the dual tool: where the second moment controls the *variance of one global count* to force a single structure to appear, the local lemma controls the *dependency structure of many local events* to force them all to be avoided. Both convert local data — pairwise edge-sharing of subgraph copies there, the dependency neighbourhood here — into a global existence verdict, and the random-graph colouring thresholds of that unit are exactly where local-lemma list-colouring bounds (bounded-degree graphs are $(\Delta+1)$-list-colourable, sharpened toward $\Delta/\ln\Delta$ for triangle-free graphs by the local lemma) become the sharpest known.

- The entropy / compression analysis of the Moser-Tardos algorithm is the algorithmic face of the entropy method developed in the co-produced [40.07.06]: there entropy bounds the number of configurations of a constrained random structure, here the impossibility of compressing a random bit-string below its entropy bounds the length of a resampling run. The witness-tree counting and the Shannon-entropy lower bound are two encodings of the same information-theoretic obstruction, and the local lemma's constructive form is where the probabilistic method becomes an efficient algorithm rather than a pure existence proof.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The Lovász Local Lemma was introduced by **Paul Erdős and László Lovász** in their 1975 paper on $3$-chromatic hypergraphs [ref: TODO_REF Erdős-Lovász 1975], where the symmetric criterion $e\,p\,(d+1)\le 1$ first appeared as the tool that proved property B for hypergraphs far denser than the first moment could handle. The lemma answered a recurring difficulty: many natural existence questions place the expected number of bad events well above one, yet the bad events are locally sparse, and no prior method exploited that sparsity. The asymmetric weighted form and the conditional-probability induction are due to the same paper, refined by Spencer; the dependency-graph language became standard through Spencer's 1994 lectures [ref: TODO_REF Spencer 1994].

For three decades the lemma was non-constructive: the survival probability $\prod(1 - x_i)$ is exponentially small, so it certified existence without an efficient search. Partial algorithmic versions (Beck 1991, Alon, Molloy-Reed, Srinivasan) progressively reduced the slack required. The decisive resolution is **Robin Moser and Gábor Tardos's 2010 paper** [ref: TODO_REF Moser-Tardos 2010], which showed the naive "resample a violated event" process terminates in expected $\sum_i x_i/(1 - x_i)$ steps under the unmodified asymmetric condition; Moser's 2009 entropy-compression argument for the symmetric $k$-SAT case preceded it and is the conceptual seed. **James Shearer's 1985 paper** [ref: TODO_REF Shearer 1985] had earlier determined the exact boundary of avoidability for a fixed dependency graph through its independence polynomial, giving the optimal $d$-regular threshold $(d-1)^{d-1}/d^d \sim 1/(ed)$ against which the Erdős-Lovász criterion is a clean but lossy sufficient condition.
