## Intuition [Beginner]

A C*-algebra is a closed world of operators: you can add them, multiply them, star them, and the positive ones form a tidy cone. Sometimes you only care about a piece of that world that is closed under fewer operations. Keep the ability to add, to scale, to star, and to recognise which elements are positive, but give up multiplication. What remains is an operator system: a window onto the algebra that still respects positivity but no longer multiplies.

Why bother with a window instead of the whole house? Because the natural maps in this subject, the completely positive maps from the previous unit, often start life only on such a window. You measure part of a quantum apparatus and get a positivity-respecting rule defined on the part you can see. The first basic question is whether you can always extend that partial reading to the full apparatus without breaking positivity. Arveson's theorem says yes: any such partial reading finishes to a full one. This is the operator version of the rule that a sensible partial measurement never has to be thrown away.

The second question is the reverse. Suppose you are handed an abstract gadget with a notion of "positive at every matrix size" and a special element playing the role of the identity. Is it secretly a window onto some algebra of operators? The Choi-Effros theorem says yes again: the abstract positivity data is always realised concretely.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the algebra of two-by-two complex matrices. Inside it, keep only the diagonal matrices: the ones whose two off-diagonal corners are zero. This collection is closed under addition, under scaling, and under the star (the conjugate transpose of a diagonal matrix is again diagonal). It contains the identity matrix. And we can recognise its positive members: a diagonal matrix is positive exactly when both diagonal entries are at least zero. So the diagonal matrices form a window: an operator system sitting inside the full matrix algebra.

Now define a reading on this window. Send each diagonal matrix to the single number obtained by averaging its two diagonal entries, then place that number on the diagonal of a new one-by-one matrix. This sends positive to positive, so it is a sensible reading on the window. Arveson's promise is that we can extend it to all two-by-two matrices, not just the diagonal ones. One extension keeps the same recipe: average the two diagonal entries of any matrix, ignore the corners. It still sends positive matrices to non-negative numbers, because the diagonal of a positive matrix is non-negative.

What this tells us: the window carried just enough structure (addition, star, an identity, a positivity test) to make the reading meaningful, and the reading extended to the whole algebra without inventing anything. The corners we ignored were exactly the multiplication data the window had dropped.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $A$ be a unital C*-algebra [39.01.01]. An **operator system** is a closed subspace $S \subseteq A$ that is **self-adjoint** ($S = S^*$) and **unital** ($1 \in S$). Self-adjointness makes $S$ a $*$-vector space; together with $1 \in S$ it inherits not only the order on $S$ itself but the **matrix order**: for each $n \ge 1$ the matrix space $M_n(S) \subseteq M_n(A)$ carries the cone
$$
M_n(S)_+ = M_n(S) \cap M_n(A)_+ ,
$$
where $M_n(A)_+$ is the positive cone of the C*-algebra $M_n(A)$ [39.05.01]. The whole family $(M_n(S)_+)_{n \ge 1}$, together with the unit $1 \in S = M_1(S)$, is the data that makes $S$ an operator system; the element $1$ is an **order unit** in the sense that for every self-adjoint $s \in S$ there is $r > 0$ with $r1 + s \ge 0$ and $r1 - s \ge 0$.

A linear map $\varphi : S \to T$ between operator systems is **unital** if $\varphi(1) = 1$, and **completely positive** (CP) if every amplification $\varphi_n = \varphi \otimes \mathrm{id}_n : M_n(S) \to M_n(T)$ sends $M_n(S)_+$ into $M_n(T)_+$ [ref: TODO_REF Paulsen Ch. 6]. A **unital complete order isomorphism** is a unital bijection $\varphi$ with $\varphi$ and $\varphi^{-1}$ both CP; two operator systems are then identified.

An **abstract operator system** is a complex $*$-vector space $V$ with a distinguished family of cones $C_n \subseteq M_n(V)_{sa}$ (the self-adjoint part of $M_n(V)$) that is a **matrix ordering**: each $C_n$ is a proper cone, and **compatible**, meaning $\alpha^* C_n \alpha \subseteq C_m$ for every scalar matrix $\alpha \in M_{n,m}(\mathbb{C})$. A self-adjoint element $e \in V$ is a **matrix order unit** if for every self-adjoint $v \in M_n(V)$ there is $r > 0$ with $r(1_n \otimes e) + v \in C_n$; it is **Archimedean** if whenever $r(1_n \otimes e) + v \in C_n$ for all $r > 0$, then $v \in C_n$ [ref: TODO_REF Choi-Effros]. The Archimedean condition is the abstract trace of norm-closedness.

For operator **spaces** the analogous abstract data is a family of norms. An **operator space** is a subspace $X \subseteq B(H)$ with the inherited matrix norms $\|\cdot\|_n$ on $M_n(X) \subseteq M_n(B(H)) = B(H^{\oplus n})$. Abstractly, a matrix-normed space $(X, (\|\cdot\|_n))$ satisfies **Ruan's axioms** if
$$
\|\alpha x \beta\|_m \le \|\alpha\|\,\|x\|_n\,\|\beta\| \quad (\alpha \in M_{m,n}, \beta \in M_{n,m}), \qquad
\left\| \begin{smallmatrix} x & 0 \\ 0 & y \end{smallmatrix} \right\|_{n+m} = \max\{\|x\|_n, \|y\|_m\}
$$
for $x \in M_n(X)$, $y \in M_m(X)$ [ref: TODO_REF Ruan].

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **An operator system is not a subalgebra.** The diagonal-plus-antidiagonal subspace of $M_2(\mathbb{C})$, or the span of $1, u, u^*$ for a unitary $u$, is self-adjoint and unital but not closed under products. Operator systems drop multiplication; only the order and the unit survive.
- **The matrix order, not the order at level $1$, is the right datum.** Two operator systems can be order isomorphic at the scalar level $M_1$ yet fail to be completely order isomorphic. The transpose map on $M_2(\mathbb{C})$ is unital and positive (an order isomorphism at level $1$) but not $2$-positive [39.05.01], so it is not a complete order isomorphism. Positivity must be tested at every matrix level.
- **Archimedean is not automatic for an abstract order unit.** An order unit can fail the Archimedean condition; one must impose it (equivalently, pass to the Archimedeanisation) before the Choi-Effros realisation applies. Without it the abstract cone need not be the cone of a norm-closed concrete operator system.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Arveson's extension theorem).** *Let $A$ be a unital C*-algebra, $S \subseteq A$ an operator system, and $\varphi : S \to B(H)$ a completely positive map. Then there exists a completely positive map $\tilde\varphi : A \to B(H)$ with $\tilde\varphi|_S = \varphi$.* [ref: TODO_REF Arveson; Paulsen Ch. 6]

**Proof.** Reduce first to finite-dimensional $H$. If the theorem holds for every finite-dimensional compression, let $(P_\lambda)$ be the net of finite-rank projections on $H$ ordered by range inclusion, set $\varphi_\lambda = P_\lambda \varphi(\cdot) P_\lambda : S \to B(P_\lambda H)$, extend each to $\tilde\varphi_\lambda : A \to B(P_\lambda H) \subseteq B(H)$, and note $\|\tilde\varphi_\lambda(1)\| = \|\varphi_\lambda(1)\| \le \|\varphi(1)\|$, so the net $(\tilde\varphi_\lambda)$ is bounded. By Banach-Alaoglu in the point-weak-$*$ topology on the bounded set of maps $A \to B(H)$ it has a cluster point $\tilde\varphi$, which is CP (each $\tilde\varphi_\lambda$ is and the cones are weak-$*$ closed) and restricts to $\varphi$ on $S$.

So take $H = \mathbb{C}^m$, hence $B(H) = M_m(\mathbb{C})$. Completely positive maps $S \to M_m(\mathbb{C})$ correspond to positive linear functionals on $M_m(S) = M_m(\mathbb{C}) \otimes S$ by the canonical pairing: given $\varphi$, define $s_\varphi$ on $M_m(S)$ by
$$
s_\varphi\big([t_{ij}]\big) = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i,j=1}^m \big\langle \varphi(t_{ij}) e_j, e_i\big\rangle ,
$$
where $(e_i)$ is the standard basis of $\mathbb{C}^m$. Complete positivity of $\varphi$ is equivalent to positivity of $s_\varphi$ on the cone $M_m(S)_+$: the matrix $[t_{ij}] \ge 0$ is paired against the rank-one positive functional encoded by the vector $\sum_i e_i \otimes e_i$, and $\varphi_m([t_{ij}]) \ge 0$ unwinds to $s_\varphi([t_{ij}]) \ge 0$. The unital pairing identity $s_\varphi(1_{M_m(S)}) = \tfrac{1}{m}\operatorname{tr}\varphi(1)$ records the norm.

Now $M_m(S)$ is an operator system inside $M_m(A)$ with order unit $1_m = 1_{M_m} \otimes 1_A$, and $s_\varphi$ is a positive linear functional on it. A positive functional on an operator system is bounded with $\|s_\varphi\| = s_\varphi(1_m)$. By the Hahn-Banach theorem applied to the real ordered Banach space $M_m(A)_{sa}$ with order unit $1_m$ — extend the positive functional from the order-unit subspace $M_m(S)_{sa}$ to $M_m(A)_{sa}$ preserving the norm $\|\cdot\| = $ the order-unit norm, so that positivity is preserved — there is a positive functional $\tilde s$ on $M_m(A)$ with $\tilde s|_{M_m(S)} = s_\varphi$ and $\tilde s(1_m) = s_\varphi(1_m)$. Reverse the pairing: $\tilde s$ defines a completely positive $\tilde\varphi : A \to M_m(\mathbb{C})$ by $\langle \tilde\varphi(a) e_j, e_i\rangle = m\,\tilde s(e_{ij} \otimes a)$, and $\tilde\varphi|_S = \varphi$ because $\tilde s$ extends $s_\varphi$. $\square$

**Bridge.** Arveson's extension theorem builds toward the entire approximation theory of nuclear and exact C*-algebras, and it appears again in [39.05.03] where the identity map of a nuclear algebra is approximated by CP maps that factor through matrix algebras, each factoring map produced by extending off a finite-dimensional operator system. The foundational reason it holds is exactly the Stinespring dilation [39.05.01] reorganised as a positivity statement: a CP map into $B(H)$ is a matrix-level positive functional, and extending it is the order-theoretic Hahn-Banach theorem for the order unit $1$. This is exactly the completely positive lift of the scalar Hahn-Banach extension of states used in the Gelfand-Naimark theory, and it generalises that scalar fact in one direction while it is dual to the injectivity of $B(H)$ in another — $B(H)$ receives every extension precisely because it is injective in the category of operator systems. Putting these together, the central insight is that $B(H)$ is the operator-system analogue of an injective module, and the bridge is that injectivity is what makes the partial-to-full extension automatic, which is the structural fact the whole nuclearity chapter rests on.

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that the transpose $t : M_2(\mathbb{C}) \to M_2(\mathbb{C})$, viewed as a unital map of operator systems, is positive but not a complete order isomorphism.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Transposition preserves eigenvalues (so is positive at level $1$), but test $t_2$ on the positive matrix $[e_{ij}] \in M_2(M_2(\mathbb{C}))$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Transposition is unital and sends a self-adjoint matrix to one with the same eigenvalues, hence positive: it is an order isomorphism at level $1$. But the amplification $t_2 = t \otimes \mathrm{id}_2$ applied to the positive rank-one matrix $[e_{ij}] = (\sum_i e_{i1})(\sum_j e_{1j})^* \in M_2(M_2(\mathbb{C}))$ returns the swap operator, which has eigenvalue $-1$. So $t_2$ is not positive, $t$ is not $2$-positive, and therefore not completely positive; a complete order isomorphism requires $t$ and $t^{-1}$ both completely positive. Thus $t$ is a level-$1$ order isomorphism that fails completely, exhibiting the matrix order as strictly finer than the order at level $1$.

Rubric: full credit for the level-$1$ positivity, the $[e_{ij}]$ amplification, and the eigenvalue $-1$ certifying failure of $2$-positivity.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove that $B(H)$ is **injective** in the category of operator systems with completely positive maps: for any operator systems $S \subseteq T$ and any CP map $\varphi : S \to B(H)$, there is a CP extension $\tilde\varphi : T \to B(H)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Realise $T$ inside its containing C*-algebra and apply Arveson's extension theorem to the inclusion $S \subseteq T \subseteq A$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $T \subseteq A$ for a unital C*-algebra $A$ (every operator system sits in its generated C*-algebra), so $S \subseteq T \subseteq A$ is a chain of operator systems. Given CP $\varphi : S \to B(H)$, Arveson's extension theorem extends $\varphi$ to a CP map $\Phi : A \to B(H)$. Restrict: $\tilde\varphi = \Phi|_T : T \to B(H)$ is CP (restriction of a CP map to a sub-operator-system is CP, since each amplification restricts) and $\tilde\varphi|_S = \Phi|_S = \varphi$. Hence the CP extension across $S \subseteq T$ exists, which is the defining injectivity (lifting) property of $B(H)$.

Rubric: full credit for embedding $T$ in a C*-algebra, applying Arveson, and restricting to $T$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Let $\Phi : B(H) \to B(H)$ be a unital completely positive idempotent ($\Phi^2 = \Phi$) onto an operator system $S = \Phi(B(H))$. Define the **Choi-Effros product** $a \circ b = \Phi(ab)$ on $S$ and show $\Phi(a\,\Phi(b)) = \Phi(\Phi(a)\,b) = \Phi(\Phi(a)\Phi(b))$ for all $a, b$, so the product is well-defined on $S$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the multiplicative-domain identity from Stinespring: for a UCP idempotent, every $s \in S$ lies in the multiplicative domain of $\Phi$, so $\Phi(s a) = \Phi(s)\Phi(a) = s\Phi(a)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $\Phi$ is a UCP idempotent, $\Phi|_S = \mathrm{id}_S$. For $s = \Phi(b) \in S$ apply the multiplicative-domain analysis of $\Phi$ [39.05.01]: a UCP map satisfies $\Phi(sa) = \Phi(s)\Phi(a)$ and $\Phi(as) = \Phi(a)\Phi(s)$ whenever $s$ is in the multiplicative domain $\{x : \Phi(x^*x) = \Phi(x)^*\Phi(x),\ \Phi(xx^*) = \Phi(x)\Phi(x)^*\}$. Every $s \in S$ lies there because $\Phi$ fixes $S$ and Kadison-Schwarz becomes equality on the fixed-point set of a UCP idempotent: $\Phi(s^*s) \ge \Phi(s)^*\Phi(s) = s^*s$ while also $\Phi(s^*s) \le \Phi(\Phi(s^*s))$ forces $\Phi(s^*s) = s^*s = \Phi(s)^*\Phi(s)$. Therefore
$$
\Phi(a\,\Phi(b)) = \Phi(a)\,\Phi(b) \cdot \text{?}
$$
more precisely, with $s = \Phi(b)$ in the multiplicative domain, $\Phi(a\,\Phi(b)) = \Phi(a)\Phi(\Phi(b)) = \Phi(a)\Phi(b)$, and symmetrically $\Phi(\Phi(a)\,b) = \Phi(\Phi(a))\Phi(b) = \Phi(a)\Phi(b)$ and $\Phi(\Phi(a)\Phi(b)) = \Phi(a)\Phi(b)$. All three equal $\Phi(a)\Phi(b)$, so $a \circ b := \Phi(ab)$ depends only on $\Phi(a), \Phi(b)$, hence descends to a well-defined bilinear product on $S$. Associativity and the C*-identity for $\|\cdot\|$ on $S$ then make $(S, \circ)$ a C*-algebra with the inherited norm and involution.

Rubric: full credit for placing $S$ in the multiplicative domain (equality in Kadison-Schwarz for the idempotent) and deducing the three equalities.

</details>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

`lean_status: none` — Mathlib carries the C*-algebra positivity layer (`CStarAlgebra`, `cfc`, matrices over a C*-algebra and their order) but neither operator systems as a named structure (a self-adjoint unital subspace with the inherited family of matrix cones $M_n(S)_+$), the matrix-ordered $*$-vector space with an Archimedean order unit, completely positive maps between abstract ordered spaces, nor the extension and realisation theorems. The intended statement reads schematically:

```lean
import Mathlib.Analysis.CStarAlgebra.Basic
import Mathlib.Analysis.CStarAlgebra.Matrix

variable {A : Type*} [CStarAlgebra A] {H : Type*}
  [NormedAddCommGroup H] [InnerProductSpace ℂ H] [CompleteSpace H]

/-- Arveson extension: a completely positive map from an operator system
S ⊆ A into B(H) extends to a completely positive map on all of A. -/
theorem arveson_extension
    (S : Submodule ℂ A) (hS_unit : (1 : A) ∈ S) (hS_star : ∀ s ∈ S, star s ∈ S)
    (φ : S →ₗ[ℂ] (H →L[ℂ] H))
    (hcp : ∀ n (x : Matrix (Fin n) (Fin n) S), 0 ≤ x.map (Submodule.subtype S) →
            0 ≤ (x.map (fun s => φ s)))            -- complete positivity on S
    :
    ∃ ψ : A →ₗ[ℂ] (H →L[ℂ] H),
      (∀ s : S, ψ (s : A) = φ s) ∧
      (∀ n (x : Matrix (Fin n) (Fin n) A), 0 ≤ x → 0 ≤ (x.map ψ)) :=
  sorry  -- reduce to finite m, pair with positive functional on Mₘ(S),
         -- order-unit Hahn-Banach extension to Mₘ(A), pair back
```

## Advanced results [Master]

The extension theorem and the abstract characterisation organise the operator-system theory into a self-dual package.

**The Choi-Effros theorem.** Let $V$ be a complex $*$-vector space with a matrix ordering $(C_n)$ and an Archimedean matrix order unit $e$. Then there is a Hilbert space $K$ and a unital complete order isomorphism of $V$ onto a concrete operator system $S \subseteq B(K)$ [ref: TODO_REF Choi-Effros]. The realisation is built from the matrix states: the set of UCP maps $V \to M_n(\mathbb{C})$ separates points (Archimedean order unit) and assembling a faithful family $\bigoplus_\alpha \varphi_\alpha$ embeds $V$ completely order isomorphically. Thus abstract matrix-order data and concrete operator systems are the same theory; the matrix order, not the order at level $1$, is the complete invariant, and the transpose example shows level $1$ alone is insufficient.

**Injectivity and the Choi-Effros product.** An operator system $S$ is **injective** if it has the extension property of the previous exercise: every CP map into $S$ extends across any operator-system inclusion. Equivalently $S$ is the image of a UCP idempotent $\Phi : B(H) \to B(H)$ with $\Phi(B(H)) = S$. The Choi-Effros theorem on injective operator systems endows $S$ with the product $a \circ b = \Phi(ab)$, the involution and norm inherited from $B(H)$, making $(S, \circ)$ a C*-algebra — indeed a monotone-complete (AW$^*$, and for $H$ separable a von Neumann) C*-algebra — even though $S$ need not be a subalgebra of $B(H)$. Injective von Neumann algebras are precisely the ranges of normal conditional expectations, and the chain of equivalences injective $=$ semidiscrete $=$ hyperfinite $=$ amenable for factors is the Connes classification, of which this construction is the operator-system germ.

**Wittstock's completely bounded extension.** The order-theoretic Hahn-Banach of Arveson has a norm-theoretic twin. A linear map $\varphi : S \to B(H)$ from an operator space is **completely bounded** with $\|\varphi\|_{cb} = \sup_n \|\varphi_n\|$, and Wittstock's theorem extends a completely bounded map off a subspace of a C*-algebra to a completely bounded map on the whole algebra with the same cb-norm; for unital self-adjoint $\varphi$ on an operator system this specialises to Arveson via the identity $\|\varphi\|_{cb} = \|\varphi(1)\|$ for CP maps. Ruan's theorem closes the abstraction: a matrix-normed space satisfying Ruan's two axioms is completely isometric to a concrete operator space $X \subseteq B(H)$, the operator-space analogue of the Choi-Effros realisation [ref: TODO_REF Ruan].

**Paulsen's $2 \times 2$ trick.** Completely bounded maps and completely positive maps are linked by the operator system $\mathcal{S}_\varphi = \big\{ \begin{smallmatrix} \lambda 1 & \varphi(x) \\ \psi(y)^* & \mu 1 \end{smallmatrix} \big\}$: a map $\varphi$ on an operator space $X$ is completely contractive iff the associated map on the Paulsen system $S_X = \big(\begin{smallmatrix} \mathbb{C}1 & X \\ X^* & \mathbb{C}1\end{smallmatrix}\big)$ is completely positive. This reduces the completely bounded theory to the completely positive theory, so Wittstock's theorem follows from Arveson's by passing to the Paulsen system, and the two Hahn-Banach theorems are one theorem viewed through the $2 \times 2$ construction.

**Synthesis.** The operator-system framework is the foundational reason the maps of this subject extend: a CP map into $B(H)$ is a matrix-level positive functional, and this is exactly the order-theoretic Hahn-Banach datum, so Arveson's extension theorem generalises the scalar extension of states while it is dual to the injectivity of $B(H)$. Putting these together, the central insight is the self-duality of the package — the Choi-Effros theorem says abstract matrix order is always concretely realised, and the Choi-Effros product says an injective such system is secretly a C*-algebra, so the category of operator systems contains its own algebra objects as its injective objects. This is exactly the operator-space mirror of Ruan's axioms, and the bridge to the rest of the chapter is that nuclearity [39.05.03] is the statement that the identity map factors approximately through matrix algebras by UCP maps, each factor built by the Arveson extension whose existence rests on $B(H)$ being injective. The whole nuclearity-exactness theory is the study of which algebras the injective-operator-system machinery can finitely approximate.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (CP maps into $M_m$ are positive functionals on $M_m(S)$).** For an operator system $S$ and $m \ge 1$, the pairing $\varphi \mapsto s_\varphi$, $s_\varphi([t_{ij}]) = \tfrac{1}{m}\sum_{i,j}\langle \varphi(t_{ij})e_j, e_i\rangle$, is a bijection between completely positive maps $\varphi : S \to M_m(\mathbb{C})$ and positive linear functionals on the operator system $M_m(S)$. Indeed $M_m(S)_+ = \{[t_{ij}] : \varphi_m([t_{ij}]) \ge 0\text{-detected}\}$, and the matrix $[t_{ij}] \ge 0$ pairs against the vector $\zeta = \sum_i e_i \otimes e_i$ as $\langle \varphi_m([t_{ij}])\zeta, \zeta\rangle = \sum_{i,j}\langle \varphi(t_{ij})e_j, e_i\rangle = m\, s_\varphi([t_{ij}])$, so positivity of $\varphi_m$ on the cone is positivity of $s_\varphi$; the inverse $\langle\varphi(s)e_j, e_i\rangle = m\,s_\varphi(e_{ij}\otimes s)$ recovers $\varphi$, and complete positivity follows because every positive matrix in $M_n(M_m(S))$ rearranges to a positive element of $M_{nm}(S)$ paired against the corresponding $\zeta$.

**Proposition (order-unit Hahn-Banach).** Let $W$ be a real ordered vector space with Archimedean order unit $e$, $W_0 \subseteq W$ an order-unit subspace ($e \in W_0$), and $f : W_0 \to \mathbb{R}$ positive with $\|f\| = f(e)$. Then $f$ extends to a positive $\tilde f : W \to \mathbb{R}$ with $\tilde f(e) = f(e)$. The order-unit norm $\|w\| = \inf\{r > 0 : -re \le w \le re\}$ makes $f$ a bounded functional of norm $f(e)$ on $(W_0, \|\cdot\|)$; the classical Hahn-Banach theorem extends it to $\tilde f$ of the same norm; and $\|\tilde f\| = \tilde f(e)$ forces positivity, since $w \le re$ gives $\tilde f(re - w) = r f(e) - \tilde f(w) \ge 0$ by the norm bound $|\tilde f(w)| \le \|\tilde f\|\,\|w\|$. Applied to $W = M_m(A)_{sa}$, $W_0 = M_m(S)_{sa}$, $e = 1_m$, this is the engine of Arveson's theorem.

**Proposition (Arveson extension).** A CP map $\varphi : S \to B(H)$ on an operator system $S \subseteq A$ extends to a CP map $A \to B(H)$. Reduce to $H = \mathbb{C}^m$ by the point-weak-$*$ compactness argument on finite-rank compressions; pass to the positive functional $s_\varphi$ on $M_m(S)$; extend it by the order-unit Hahn-Banach proposition to a positive functional $\tilde s$ on $M_m(A)$ with $\tilde s(1_m) = s_\varphi(1_m)$; pair back to obtain the CP extension $\tilde\varphi$ with $\tilde\varphi|_S = \varphi$. The three propositions chain to give the theorem.

**Proposition (the matrix states separate points of an Archimedean operator system).** If $(V, (C_n), e)$ is a matrix-ordered $*$-vector space with Archimedean matrix order unit, then for every nonzero self-adjoint $v \in V$ there is $n$ and a UCP map $\varphi : V \to M_n(\mathbb{C})$ with $\varphi(v) \ne 0$. By the Archimedean property the state space $\{f : V \to \mathbb{C}\text{ positive}, f(e) = 1\}$ is weak-$*$ compact and separates self-adjoint elements through their order-unit norm; amplifying to matrix states ($V \to M_n$) only enriches the separating family. The direct sum $\bigoplus_\alpha \varphi_\alpha$ over a separating family of matrix states is a unital complete order injection of $V$ into $B(\bigoplus_\alpha \mathbb{C}^{n_\alpha})$, whose image is a concrete operator system — the Choi-Effros realisation.

**Proposition (Choi-Effros product makes an injective operator system a C*-algebra).** Let $\Phi : B(H) \to B(H)$ be a UCP idempotent with range $S$. The fixed-point set $S$ lies in the multiplicative domain of $\Phi$: for $s \in S$, Kadison-Schwarz [39.05.01] gives $\Phi(s^*s) \ge \Phi(s)^*\Phi(s) = s^*s$, while $\Phi$ unital idempotent and $\|\Phi\| = 1$ give $\Phi(s^*s) \le s^*s$ on the fixed set, so $\Phi(s^*s) = \Phi(s)^*\Phi(s)$ and likewise for $ss^*$. Hence $\Phi(sa) = \Phi(s)\Phi(a) = s\Phi(a)$ and $\Phi(as) = \Phi(a)s$ for $s \in S$. Define $a \circ b = \Phi(ab)$ on $S$; the multiplicative-domain identities show it depends only on $a, b \in S$ and is associative: $(a \circ b) \circ c = \Phi(\Phi(ab)c) = \Phi(abc) = \Phi(a\Phi(bc)) = a \circ (b \circ c)$. With the inherited involution and norm, $\|a \circ a^* \dots\|$ satisfies the C*-identity because the norm is the operator norm restricted to the fixed set, so $(S, \circ, *, \|\cdot\|)$ is a C*-algebra. When $\Phi$ is normal and $H$ separable, $S$ is a von Neumann algebra.

## Connections [Master]

- **Completely positive maps and the Stinespring dilation theorem [39.05.01]** — Stinespring is the engine of Arveson's theorem: a CP map into $B(H)$ is a compressed representation, and the extension recompresses an extended one. The multiplicative-domain analysis there is exactly what equips an injective operator system with the Choi-Effros product, so this unit is the operator-system completion of the dilation theory.

- **C*-algebras: axioms, spectrum, and the continuous functional calculus [39.01.01]** — the matrix order $M_n(S)_+ = M_n(S) \cap M_n(A)_+$ that defines an operator system is inherited from the C*-positivity of $M_n(A)$ built on the functional calculus, and the order-unit norm that drives the Hahn-Banach extension is the C*-norm restricted to $S$; the whole framework runs on that order.

- **Nuclear and exact C*-algebras [39.05.03]** — nuclearity is the approximate factorisation of the identity through matrix algebras by UCP maps $A \to M_n \to A$, each UCP map produced by an Arveson extension off a finite-dimensional operator system; the injectivity of $B(H)$ established here is precisely what makes those approximating maps exist.

- **The predual, normal states, and the $\sigma$-weak topology [39.03.02]** — the normal conditional expectations onto a von Neumann subalgebra are the normal UCP idempotents whose ranges carry the Choi-Effros product, linking injective operator systems to the predual and normality theory of von Neumann algebras.

- **Amenable groups, Følner sequences, and invariant means [39.05.06]** — injectivity of a group von Neumann algebra is equivalent to amenability of the group, and an invariant mean is the prototypical UCP idempotent (a conditional expectation onto the scalars); this unit supplies the operator-system meaning of that equivalence.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The extension theorem is due to William Arveson, who in his 1969 *Acta Mathematica* paper "Subalgebras of C*-algebras" proved that a completely positive map defined on an operator system extends to the ambient C*-algebra, and isolated the operator system and the completely bounded map as the basic objects of a noncommutative function theory [ref: TODO_REF Arveson 1969]. Arveson's motivation was the noncommutative analogue of the disc algebra and the boundary-representation theory generalising the Šilov boundary; the extension theorem played the role of the Hahn-Banach theorem in that program. The abstract characterisation came from Man-Duen Choi and Edward Effros, whose 1977 *Journal of Functional Analysis* paper "Injectivity and operator spaces" identified the matrix order with an Archimedean order unit as the exact abstract data of an operator system and constructed the multiplication making an injective operator system a C*-algebra [ref: TODO_REF Choi-Effros 1977]. Zhong-Jin Ruan's 1988 thesis work supplied the parallel matrix-norm axioms characterising operator spaces, and the Effros-Ruan monograph organised the resulting theory.

The conceptual shift is from algebras to ordered (or normed) matrix spaces as the primary objects: an operator system records positivity at every matrix level and forgets multiplication, yet the Choi-Effros theorem recovers multiplication exactly when the system is injective. The injective objects are the conditional-expectation ranges, and through Connes's work the injective factors are the amenable ones, so the operator-system extension theory is one source of the deep equivalence between approximation, amenability, and injectivity that governs the classification of von Neumann algebras and the nuclearity of C*-algebras.
