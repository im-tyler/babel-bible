## Intuition [Beginner]

Inside an algebra of operators there are special elements called projections: things that square to themselves, the operator version of "select this part, ignore the rest." Two projections should count as the same if one can be slid onto the other by a rotation inside the algebra. So a natural question is: how many genuinely different sizes of projection can you build? Adding projections that live in different corners just stacks their sizes, so the sizes form a system you can add. Allowing formal differences — the size of one projection minus another — turns that system into a group. This group is called $K_0$, and it is the first fingerprint of the algebra.

There is a second fingerprint. The reversible elements of the algebra, the unitaries, can sometimes be slid continuously back to the identity and sometimes cannot: they can be caught in a loop that will not untwist. Counting the genuinely different loops gives a second group, called $K_1$. Together $K_0$ and $K_1$ are the two simplest numerical shadows an operator algebra casts, and they are strong enough to tell many algebras apart even when nothing else will.

The surprise is that these same two groups, for an algebra of continuous functions on a shape, recover the count of vector bundles on that shape. So operator algebras carry their own topology.

## Visual [Beginner]

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the simplest interesting algebra: the $2 \times 2$ complex matrices, written $M_2$. What projections can you build, and what sizes do they come in?

A projection here is a matrix $p$ with $p^2 = p$ and $p = p^*$. The smallest nonzero one selects a single line, like

$$
p = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix},
$$

which has rank $1$. The identity matrix selects both lines and has rank $2$. The zero matrix has rank $0$. Two projections inside $M_2$ count as the same exactly when they have the same rank, because you can rotate one line onto any other line. So the available sizes are $0, 1, 2$ — just the rank.

When you stack projections from larger matrix blocks the rank keeps climbing through all the whole numbers, and allowing formal differences fills in the negatives. The reachable sizes become every integer.

What this tells us: for $M_2$ the group $K_0$ is the whole numbers under addition, $\mathbb{Z}$, with the size of a projection read off as its rank. Counting projections has become counting integers.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $A$ be a C*-algebra. Write $M_n(A)$ for the $n \times n$ matrices over $A$, a C*-algebra, and form the algebraic inductive limit $M_\infty(A) = \varinjlim M_n(A)$ under the corner embeddings $a \mapsto \operatorname{diag}(a,0)$ [39.01.01]. Two projections $p, q \in M_\infty(A)$ are **Murray-von Neumann equivalent**, $p \sim q$, when $p = v^*v$ and $q = vv^*$ for a partial isometry $v \in M_\infty(A)$; the equivalence classes form an abelian monoid $V(A)$ under $[p] + [q] = [p \oplus q]$, where $p \oplus q = \operatorname{diag}(p,q)$. For unital $A$ the **$K_0$ group** is the Grothendieck group $K_0(A) = G(V(A))$ [03.08.01]. Every element of $K_0(A)$ is a formal difference $[p] - [q]$, and $[p] = [q]$ in $K_0(A)$ iff $p \oplus r \sim q \oplus r$ for some projection $r$ (stable equivalence) [ref: TODO_REF Rørdam-Larsen-Laustsen Ch. 2].

For non-unital $A$ the naive Grothendieck construction is corrected through the **unitisation** $\widetilde A = A \oplus \mathbb{C}$, with the split exact sequence $0 \to A \to \widetilde A \xrightarrow{\pi} \mathbb{C} \to 0$. One sets $K_0(A) = \ker\big(\pi_* : K_0(\widetilde A) \to K_0(\mathbb{C})\big)$. This standard picture agrees with $G(V(A))$ when $A$ is unital and is functorial in $A$.

The **$K_1$ group** is built from unitaries. For unital $A$ let $U_n(A)$ be the unitary group of $M_n(A)$ and $U_\infty(A) = \varinjlim U_n(A)$ under $u \mapsto \operatorname{diag}(u,1)$. Then $K_1(A) = U_\infty(A)/U_\infty(A)_0$, the quotient by the connected component of the identity, equivalently $\pi_0(U_\infty(A))$. The group operation is induced by $\oplus$, and it agrees with the pointwise product because $\operatorname{diag}(u,v)$ and $\operatorname{diag}(uv,1)$ are connected through the rotation $\operatorname{diag}(u,1)\, R\, \operatorname{diag}(v,1)\, R^*$ with $R$ a path of unitaries swapping the two coordinates; hence $K_1(A)$ is abelian. For non-unital $A$ one again passes to $\widetilde A$. Both constructions are **half-exact, homotopy-invariant, stable, and continuous (commute with inductive limits)**; these four properties, established below, are what make operator K-theory computable.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- Murray-von Neumann equivalence is *not* unitary equivalence inside $A$ when $A$ is non-unital or the projections have different "complements." In $M_2$ the rank-one projections onto two lines are unitarily equivalent, but in general $p \sim q$ only forces a partial isometry, and $p \oplus 1 \sim q \oplus 1$ (stable equivalence) can hold without $p \sim q$ in $A$ itself. $K_0$ records the *stable* class.
- $K_0(A) = G(V(A))$ is the correct definition only for unital $A$. For non-unital $A$, using $G(V(A))$ directly can give the wrong answer (e.g., it fails to be half-exact); the unitisation-kernel picture is mandatory. A symptom: $V(A)$ may collapse to zero while $K_0(A)$ is not.
- $K_1(A)$ is the component group of *stabilised* unitaries $U_\infty(A)$, not of $U(A)$. For $A = C(\mathbb{T})$ the winding number makes $K_1 = \mathbb{Z}$, but the stabilisation is essential: $U_1$ already detects winding here, yet for general $A$ a unitary may only become non-null after enlarging the matrix size, and conversely a noncontractible loop in $U_1$ can die in $U_2$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (stability of $K$-theory).** *Let $A$ be a C*-algebra and $\mathbb{K} = \mathbb{K}(H)$ the algebra of compact operators on a separable infinite-dimensional Hilbert space. The corner embedding $\iota : A \to A \otimes \mathbb{K}$, $a \mapsto a \otimes e_{11}$, induces isomorphisms*
$$
\iota_* : K_0(A) \xrightarrow{\ \cong\ } K_0(A \otimes \mathbb{K}), \qquad \iota_* : K_1(A) \xrightarrow{\ \cong\ } K_1(A \otimes \mathbb{K}).
$$
*In particular K-theory is unchanged by tensoring with matrices: $K_*(M_n(A)) \cong K_*(A)$.* [ref: TODO_REF Blackadar Ch. 6]

**Proof.** Treat $K_0$; $K_1$ is parallel with unitaries replacing projections. The key structural fact is $A \otimes \mathbb{K} = \varinjlim M_n(A)$, the inductive limit of matrix amplifications under the upper-left corner embeddings $M_n(A) \hookrightarrow M_{n+1}(A)$. By the continuity property of $K_0$ (proved in the Full proof set), $K_0$ commutes with inductive limits, so
$$
K_0(A \otimes \mathbb{K}) = K_0\big(\varinjlim M_n(A)\big) \cong \varinjlim K_0(M_n(A)).
$$
It therefore suffices to show each corner inclusion $M_n(A) \hookrightarrow M_{n+1}(A)$ induces an isomorphism on $K_0$ compatible with the limit, equivalently that $K_0(M_n(A)) \cong K_0(A)$ canonically and the limit maps are identities under this identification.

For the matrix-stability isomorphism $K_0(M_n(A)) \cong K_0(A)$, observe that $M_m(M_n(A)) = M_{mn}(A)$, so the projection monoids satisfy $V(M_n(A)) = V(A)$ on the nose: a projection in $M_m(M_n(A))$ is a projection in $M_{mn}(A)$, and Murray-von Neumann equivalence is computed in the same ambient $M_\infty(A)$. The embedding $a \mapsto a \otimes e_{11}$ sends a projection $p \in M_m(A)$ to $\operatorname{diag}(p,0,\dots,0) \in M_{mn}(A)$, which is Murray-von Neumann equivalent to $p$ via the partial isometry collapsing the padding. Hence $\iota_*$ is the identity on $V$, an isomorphism after Grothendieck completion.

The corner maps $M_n(A) \hookrightarrow M_{n+1}(A)$ likewise act as the identity on $V(A)$ for the same reason: padding by a zero row and column does not change the Murray-von Neumann class. So the inductive system $K_0(M_n(A))$ is the constant system at $K_0(A)$ with identity bonding maps, whose limit is $K_0(A)$, and the composite identification with $K_0(A \otimes \mathbb{K})$ is exactly $\iota_*$. Therefore $\iota_* : K_0(A) \to K_0(A \otimes \mathbb{K})$ is an isomorphism. $\square$

**Bridge.** Stability builds toward the entire computational apparatus of the subject and appears again in [39.02.01], where it is precisely the reason the dimension group of an AF algebra is well-defined independent of the matrix size used to represent a projection: padding by zeros is invisible to $K_0$. The foundational reason the proof works is exactly that $A \otimes \mathbb{K}$ is the inductive limit of the matrix amplifications, so this is exactly the continuity property applied to the canonical stabilising system. Stability generalises the matrix-size independence already implicit in the rank invariant of the Beginner worked example, and putting these together with homotopy invariance gives the four pillars — half-exactness, homotopy invariance, stability, continuity — from which the index map and the six-term sequence are assembled; the bridge is that operator K-theory is a stable, homotopy-invariant, half-exact functor, the abstract shape shared with the topological K-theory of [03.08.01].

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that $K_0$ is a functor: a $*$-homomorphism $\phi : A \to B$ induces a group homomorphism $\phi_* : K_0(A) \to K_0(B)$, and $(\psi \circ \phi)_* = \psi_* \circ \phi_*$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply $\phi$ entrywise to projections in $M_\infty(A)$; check it respects $\sim$ and $\oplus$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Amplify $\phi$ to $\phi_\infty : M_\infty(A) \to M_\infty(B)$ entrywise. A projection $p$ maps to a projection $\phi_\infty(p)$ because $\phi$ preserves the relations $p^2 = p = p^*$. If $p = v^*v$, $q = vv^*$ then $\phi_\infty(v)$ is a partial isometry implementing $\phi_\infty(p) \sim \phi_\infty(q)$, so $\phi$ descends to a monoid map $V(A) \to V(B)$; it respects $\oplus$ since $\phi_\infty(\operatorname{diag}(p,q)) = \operatorname{diag}(\phi_\infty(p),\phi_\infty(q))$. Grothendieck completion is functorial, giving $\phi_* : K_0(A) \to K_0(B)$, and functoriality of completion together with $\phi_\infty$ being a homomorphism gives $(\psi\circ\phi)_* = \psi_*\circ\phi_*$ and $(\mathrm{id})_* = \mathrm{id}$.

Rubric: full credit for the amplified map preserving projections, $\sim$, $\oplus$, and the composition law via functoriality of Grothendieck completion.

</details>

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove that $K_0$ of an AF algebra $A = \varinjlim F_n$ equals its dimension group: $K_0(A) \cong \varinjlim K_0(F_n)$ as ordered abelian groups, with positive cone the image of $V(A)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use continuity of $K_0$ and the finite-dimensional computation $K_0(F_n) = \mathbb{Z}^{k_n}$; projections in an AF algebra perturb into a finite stage.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By continuity, $K_0(\varinjlim F_n) \cong \varinjlim K_0(F_n)$. For a finite-dimensional $F_n = \bigoplus_i M_{n_i}$ one has $K_0(F_n) = \mathbb{Z}^{k_n}$ with cone $\mathbb{Z}^{k_n}_{\ge 0}$ and the connecting maps given by the multiplicity matrices of the Bratteli diagram [39.02.01]. Order is preserved because each connecting map is positive. Thus $K_0(A) \cong \varinjlim K_0(F_n)$ as ordered groups, the dimension group, recovering the invariant of Elliott's theorem.

Rubric: full credit for continuity, the finite-dimensional cone, projection perturbation into a stage, and the ordered-limit identification.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Let $0 \to J \to A \xrightarrow{\pi} A/J \to 0$ be a short exact sequence of C*-algebras. Define the index map $\partial : K_1(A/J) \to K_0(J)$ on a unitary lift and prove it is well-defined as a group homomorphism on the class level.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Lift a unitary $u \in U_n(A/J)$ to a partial isometry or, via $\operatorname{diag}(u, u^*)$, to a unitary $w \in U_{2n}(A)$; then $w\, \operatorname{diag}(1,0)\, w^*$ and $\operatorname{diag}(1,0)$ differ by an element mapping to a projection over $J$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Given $[u] \in K_1(A/J)$ with $u \in U_n(A/J)$, the element $\operatorname{diag}(u,u^*) \in U_{2n}(A/J)$ is connected to $1$, so it lifts to a unitary $w \in U_{2n}(A)$ with $\pi_{2n}(w) = \operatorname{diag}(u,u^*)$ (homotopy lifting along the surjection of unitary groups, using that $U_0$ lifts). Set $p = w \operatorname{diag}(1_n,0_n) w^*$, a projection in $M_{2n}(A)$ with $\pi(p) = \operatorname{diag}(1_n,0_n)$, so $p - \operatorname{diag}(1_n,0_n) \in M_{2n}(J)$ (more precisely in $M_{2n}(\widetilde J)$ mapping to $0$ in $M_{2n}(\mathbb{C})$). Define
$$
\partial[u] = [p] - [\operatorname{diag}(1_n,0_n)] \in K_0(J).
$$
Well-definedness: a different unitary lift $w'$ satisfies $w' = wk$ with $k$ a unitary lift of $1$, hence $k$ homotopic to $1$ in $U_{2n}(A)$ keeping $\pi(k)=1$, so the resulting projections are homotopic over $J$ and define the same $K_0(J)$ class; independence of $n$ and of the representative $u$ in its $K_1$ class follows from stabilising and the homotopy invariance of $K_0$. Additivity $\partial([u]+[v]) = \partial[u]+\partial[v]$ comes from $\operatorname{diag}(u,v)$ lifting to $\operatorname{diag}(w_u,w_v)$ and $[p_u \oplus p_v] = [p_u]+[p_v]$. Hence $\partial$ is a well-defined homomorphism.

Rubric: full credit for the $\operatorname{diag}(u,u^*)$ unitary lift, the projection difference landing in $K_0(J)$, well-definedness via lift ambiguity being null-homotopic, and additivity.

## Advanced results [Master]

**The four pillars and the six-term exact sequence.** Operator K-theory is the universal functor on C*-algebras that is homotopy invariant, stable, and half-exact. From half-exactness alone, with the suspension $SA = C_0(\mathbb{R}) \otimes A$, one obtains a long exact sequence; Bott periodicity for C*-algebras, $K_0(A) \cong K_1(SA) \cong K_0(S^2 A)$, collapses it into the **six-term exact sequence**
$$
\begin{array}{ccccc}
K_0(J) & \to & K_0(A) & \to & K_0(A/J) \\
\uparrow{\scriptstyle\partial_1} & & & & \downarrow{\scriptstyle\partial_0} \\
K_1(A/J) & \leftarrow & K_1(A) & \leftarrow & K_1(J)
\end{array}
$$
attached to any short exact sequence $0 \to J \to A \to A/J \to 0$, with the exponential map $\partial_0 : K_0(A/J) \to K_1(J)$ and the index map $\partial_1 : K_1(A/J) \to K_0(J)$. This is the computational engine: the Pimsner-Voiculescu sequence used in [39.02.01] is the six-term sequence for the crossed-product mapping torus, and the Toeplitz extension $0 \to \mathbb{K} \to \mathcal{T} \to C(\mathbb{T}) \to 0$ has index map $\partial_1 : K_1(C(\mathbb{T})) = \mathbb{Z} \to K_0(\mathbb{K}) = \mathbb{Z}$ the Fredholm index, equal to minus the winding number [39.01.04].

**The standard computations.** The pillars reduce everything to small cases. For the scalars, $V(\mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{N}$ by rank, so $K_0(\mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{Z}$ and $K_1(\mathbb{C}) = 0$ since $U_\infty(\mathbb{C}) = \varinjlim U_n$ is connected. Matrix stability gives $K_0(M_n) = \mathbb{Z}$, $K_1(M_n) = 0$. For the compacts, $\mathbb{K} = \varinjlim M_n$ with the corner maps, so continuity gives $K_0(\mathbb{K}) = \mathbb{Z}$, $K_1(\mathbb{K}) = 0$ — the rank survives in the limit because each corner map is the identity on $K_0$. For $B(H)$ proper infiniteness ($1 \sim p < 1$, an Eilenberg swindle) forces $K_0(B(H)) = K_1(B(H)) = 0$; the Calkin algebra $Q(H) = B(H)/\mathbb{K}$ then has $K_0(Q(H)) = 0$ and $K_1(Q(H)) = \mathbb{Z}$, the index of essentially unitary operators, recovering Brown-Douglas-Fillmore. For an AF algebra, continuity gives $K_0 = $ the dimension group and $K_1 = 0$ (each $F_n$ has connected unitary group). For commutative $A = C(X)$, the Serre-Swan theorem identifies finitely generated projective $C(X)$-modules with vector bundles, so $K_0(C(X)) = K^0(X)$ and $K_1(C(X)) = K^1(X) = K^0(\Sigma X)$ topological K-theory [03.08.01].

**Order and the positive cone.** For a stably finite C*-algebra (one in which $1 \oplus p \sim 1$ forces $p = 0$, e.g. any AF algebra or any C*-algebra with a faithful trace) the image of $V(A)$ in $K_0(A)$ is a genuine cone making $(K_0(A), K_0(A)_+, [1_A])$ an ordered abelian group with order unit; this is the dimension group when $A$ is AF, and a faithful trace $\tau$ induces a state $\tau_* : K_0(A) \to \mathbb{R}$ on the ordered group [39.02.01]. Proper infiniteness destroys the order: when $[1] = 0$ the cone is everything and the order collapses, as for $B(H)$, $\mathbb{K}$ unitised wrongly, or the Cuntz algebras $\mathcal{O}_n$ with $K_0 = \mathbb{Z}/(n-1)$ [39.01.04]. The dichotomy — ordered $K_0$ for stably finite, torsion or vanishing $K_0$ for purely infinite — is the K-theoretic shadow of the type classification.

**Synthesis.** Operator K-theory is the central insight that organises the entire classification program: it is the foundational reason a noncommutative C*-algebra has a computable topology, because the two functors $K_0, K_1$ are stable, homotopy invariant, and half-exact, and putting these together with Bott periodicity yields the six-term exact sequence from which every concrete group is read off. This is exactly the noncommutative extension of the topological K-theory of [03.08.01]: the Serre-Swan theorem makes $K_0(C(X)) = K^0(X)$, so projections in matrix algebras over $C(X)$ are dual to vector bundles on $X$, and operator K-theory generalises this to algebras with no points at all. The dimension-group invariant of [39.02.01] is exactly $K_0$ specialised to AF algebras, where stability is the reason matrix size is invisible and continuity is the reason the limit of dimension groups is the dimension group of the limit; the bridge is that the index map $\partial : K_1(A/J) \to K_0(J)$ is dual to the connecting map of the extension, turning the Toeplitz, Cuntz, and crossed-product extensions of [39.01.04] into Fredholm-index computations, and the central insight that classifying C*-algebras has become computing an exact sequence of abelian groups is the launch of the Elliott program and of Kasparov's $KK$-theory.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (continuity: $K_0$ commutes with inductive limits).** Let $A = \varinjlim(A_n, \phi_n)$ be an inductive limit of C*-algebras with connecting $*$-homomorphisms $\phi_n : A_n \to A_{n+1}$. Then $K_0(A) \cong \varinjlim(K_0(A_n), (\phi_n)_*)$, and likewise for $K_1$.

*Proof.* A projection $p \in M_k(A)$ lies within distance $\varepsilon < 1$ of an entry-wise approximant $\phi_{\infty,n}(a)$ of a self-adjoint $a \in M_k(A_n)$ for some $n$, because $\bigcup_n \phi_{\infty,n}(A_n)$ is dense in $A$. For $\varepsilon$ small the spectrum of $a$ misses $[\varepsilon, 1-\varepsilon]$, so the spectral projection $q = \chi_{(1/2,\infty)}(a)$ is a projection in $M_k(A_n)$ (functional calculus, [39.01.01]) with $\|p - \phi_{\infty,n}(q)\| < 1$, hence $p \sim \phi_{\infty,n}(q)$ by the standard fact that close projections are unitarily equivalent. Thus every class in $V(A)$ comes from some $V(A_n)$, so the natural map $\varinjlim V(A_n) \to V(A)$ is surjective; injectivity follows because an equivalence $\phi_{\infty,n}(q) \sim \phi_{\infty,n}(q')$ in $A$ is implemented by a partial isometry approximable from a finite stage $A_m$, $m \ge n$, giving $\phi_{m,n}(q) \sim \phi_{m,n}(q')$ in $A_m$. Hence $V(A) = \varinjlim V(A_n)$, and Grothendieck completion commutes with inductive limits of monoids, giving $K_0(A) = \varinjlim K_0(A_n)$. The $K_1$ statement is identical with unitaries and the fact that a unitary near a unitary is homotopic to it. $\square$

**Proposition ($K_0(\mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{Z}$, $K_1(\mathbb{C}) = 0$).** A projection in $M_n(\mathbb{C})$ is classified up to Murray-von Neumann equivalence by its rank, so $V(\mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{N}$ with addition, whose Grothendieck group is $\mathbb{Z}$, generated by the class of a rank-one projection. For $K_1$, the unitary groups $U_n(\mathbb{C}) = U(n)$ are path connected (every unitary matrix is $\exp(iH)$ for self-adjoint $H$, and $t \mapsto \exp(itH)$ joins it to $1$), so $U_\infty(\mathbb{C})_0 = U_\infty(\mathbb{C})$ and $K_1(\mathbb{C}) = 0$. $\square$

**Proposition (Eilenberg swindle: $K_0(B(H)) = 0$).** Since $H \cong H \oplus H$, there is an isometry $s : H \to H$ with $ss^* = p$ a proper projection and $s^*s = 1$, so $1 \sim p$ with $1 - p \ne 0$; iterating, $B(H)$ contains a sequence of mutually orthogonal projections each $\sim 1$. For any projection $q$, choosing an infinite orthogonal family $q_1, q_2, \dots$ with each $q_i \sim q$ and $r = \sum q_i$ a projection (strong-operator convergence inside $B(H)$), one has $[q] + [r] = [r]$ in $V(B(H))$, so $[q] = 0$ in the Grothendieck group $K_0(B(H))$. As $q$ was arbitrary and projections generate, $K_0(B(H)) = 0$. The same swindle on unitaries (or homotopy invariance: $B(H)$ has a contractible unitary group by Kuiper's theorem) gives $K_1(B(H)) = 0$. $\square$

**Proposition (matrix stability $K_0(M_n(A)) \cong K_0(A)$).** Because $M_m(M_n(A)) = M_{mn}(A)$, the monoid $V(M_n(A))$, computed from projections in $\varinjlim_m M_m(M_n(A)) = \varinjlim_m M_{mn}(A) = M_\infty(A)$, equals $V(A)$. The corner inclusion $A \hookrightarrow M_n(A)$, $a \mapsto a \otimes e_{11}$, sends $[p]$ to the class of $\operatorname{diag}(p,0,\dots,0)$, equal to $[p]$ since padding by zeros is a Murray-von Neumann equivalence. Hence the induced map on $V$ is the identity, and $K_0(M_n(A)) = G(V(M_n(A))) = G(V(A)) = K_0(A)$. $\square$

**Proposition (the index map is exact at $K_1(A/J)$).** For $0 \to J \to A \xrightarrow{\pi} A/J \to 0$, the composite $K_1(A) \xrightarrow{\pi_*} K_1(A/J) \xrightarrow{\partial} K_0(J)$ is zero: a unitary $u = \pi(\tilde u)$ lifting from $U_n(A)$ has $\operatorname{diag}(u,u^*)$ lifting to the unitary $w = \operatorname{diag}(\tilde u, \tilde u^*) \in U_{2n}(A)$, whence $p = w\operatorname{diag}(1,0)w^* = \operatorname{diag}(\tilde u 1 \tilde u^*, 0) = \operatorname{diag}(1,0)$, so $\partial[u] = [p] - [\operatorname{diag}(1,0)] = 0$. Conversely if $\partial[u] = 0$ then the projection $p$ is connected over $J$ to $\operatorname{diag}(1,0)$, and the unitary $w$ implementing this can be adjusted by a unitary over $\widetilde J$ to lie in the image of $U(A)$, exhibiting $[u] \in \operatorname{im}\pi_*$. This is the exactness used to splice the six-term sequence; the remaining exactness statements are the half-exactness of $K_0, K_1$ together with the Bott isomorphism $K_0(SA) \cong K_1(A)$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **AF Algebras, Bratteli Diagrams, and the Irrational Rotation Algebra [39.02.01]** — the dimension group $(K_0(A), K_0(A)_+, \Sigma(A))$ classifying AF algebras is exactly $K_0$ of this unit equipped with its order and scale; continuity of $K_0$ is the reason the dimension group is the limit of the finite-stage $\mathbb{Z}^{k_n}$, and the Pimsner-Voiculescu computation of $K_0(A_\theta) \cong \mathbb{Z}^2$ is the six-term sequence applied to a crossed product.

- **C*-algebras: axioms, spectrum, and the continuous functional calculus [39.01.01]** — projections are produced from self-adjoint elements by the functional calculus, and the perturbation argument that close projections are unitarily equivalent (used throughout the continuity and stability proofs) is functional calculus applied to spectral gaps; the matrix amplifications $M_n(A)$ are the C*-algebraic substrate of $M_\infty(A)$.

- **Topological K-theory [03.08.01]** — operator K-theory is the noncommutative extension of the vector-bundle K-theory there: the Serre-Swan theorem makes $K_0(C(X)) = K^0(X)$ and $K_1(C(X)) = K^1(X)$, so projections over $C(X)$ correspond to bundles on $X$, and the abstract stable/homotopy-invariant/half-exact axioms are shared between the two theories.

- **Toeplitz and Cuntz Algebras and Extensions [39.01.04]** — the six-term exact sequence turns the Toeplitz extension $0 \to \mathbb{K} \to \mathcal{T} \to C(\mathbb{T}) \to 0$ into the Fredholm-index map $\partial : K_1(C(\mathbb{T})) = \mathbb{Z} \to K_0(\mathbb{K}) = \mathbb{Z}$, and computes $K_0(\mathcal{O}_n) = \mathbb{Z}/(n-1)$ for the Cuntz algebras, the purely-infinite counterpart to the stably finite dimension groups.

- **Comparison of Projections and the Murray-von Neumann Type Classification [39.03.04]** — Murray-von Neumann equivalence of projections is the same relation refined in the type classification of von Neumann algebras; stable finiteness (ordered $K_0$) versus proper infiniteness ($K_0$ with $[1]=0$) is the K-theoretic image of the finite/infinite dichotomy of factors.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The transport of Grothendieck's group completion from algebraic geometry into topology was carried out by Atiyah and Hirzebruch in 1959-1961, defining $K^0(X)$ from vector bundles and proving the Riemann-Roch theorem in K-theoretic form [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah-Hirzebruch 1961]. The bridge to operator algebras runs through the Serre-Swan theorem: Swan showed in 1962 that finitely generated projective modules over $C(X)$ are exactly the sections of vector bundles, so the algebraic $K_0$ of the ring $C(X)$ coincides with the topological $K^0(X)$ [ref: TODO_REF Swan 1962]. This made it natural to define $K_0$ of an arbitrary C*-algebra through projections in matrix amplifications.

The $K_1$ group as components of the unitary group, the stability and homotopy-invariance properties, and the six-term exact sequence were developed in the 1970s, with the index map identified with the Fredholm index through the Toeplitz extension and Brown-Douglas-Fillmore theory. Blackadar's monograph consolidated the subject, and Cuntz's picture of $K$-theory via quasihomomorphisms and Kasparov's $KK$-theory placed $K_0$ and $K_1$ inside a bivariant framework in which the index map and Bott periodicity become formal consequences [ref: TODO_REF Blackadar 1998]. Connes's program of noncommutative geometry took operator K-theory as the homology theory of noncommutative spaces, pairing it with cyclic cohomology through the Chern character.
