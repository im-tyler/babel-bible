## Intuition [Beginner]

A cell's life revolves around one question: to divide or not to divide. The **cell cycle** is the sequence of events a cell goes through to grow and divide into two daughter cells.

The cycle has four main phases. **G1 (Gap 1):** the cell grows and prepares for DNA replication. **S (Synthesis):** the cell copies its entire genome. **G2 (Gap 2):** the cell checks that DNA is fully and correctly copied, and prepares for division. **M (Mitosis):** the cell divides — the nucleus splits (mitosis) and then the cell pinches in two (cytokinesis).

Not all cells cycle continuously. Many cells exit the cycle into **G0**, a resting state where they neither divide nor prepare to divide. Nerve cells and muscle cells are permanently in G0. Liver cells can re-enter the cycle if needed.

The cycle is controlled by **checkpoints** — quality-control stations that ensure each phase is completed correctly before the next begins. The three major checkpoints are: G1/S (is the cell ready to replicate DNA?), G2/M (is DNA fully copied and undamaged?), and the spindle checkpoint (are all chromosomes properly attached to the spindle before segregation?).

## Visual [Beginner]

Imagine the cell cycle as a circular track with four stations. The cell starts at G1, moves to S, then G2, then M, and back to G1. At each station, a checkpoint inspector checks that everything is in order.

During M phase (mitosis), the cell goes through a choreographed sequence: the chromosomes condense, line up in the middle, are pulled to opposite ends, and then two new nuclei form around each set.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A typical mammalian cell divides every 24 hours. S phase takes about 8 hours, G2 takes about 4 hours, and M phase takes about 1 hour. G1 takes the remaining 11 hours.

If a drug arrests cells at the G2/M checkpoint (preventing entry into mitosis), what fraction of an asynchronous population (cells randomly distributed throughout the cycle) would be arrested?

Cells in G1, S, and G2 are all upstream of the block and will accumulate at G2/M. Cells in M phase will complete division and re-enter G1, then progress through S and G2 before also being arrested. The fraction arrested at any instant is the fraction of cells in G1 + S + G2:

$(11 + 8 + 4) / 24 = 23/24 \approx 96\%$.

Nearly the entire population accumulates at G2/M, because only cells currently in M phase (1/24 $\approx$ 4%) can complete the cycle. This is the principle behind **cell synchronization** by cell cycle blockers.

</details>

This is why G1/S blockers like hydroxyurea or mimosine can synchronize an entire population.

</details>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The cell cycle is the ordered series of events by which a growing cell duplicates its contents and divides into two daughter cells. In eukaryotes, the cycle consists of four phases: G1, S, G2, and M.

### Cyclins and CDKs

The engine of the cell cycle is the **cyclin-dependent kinase (CDK)** system. CDKs are serine/threonine kinases that are activated by binding to **cyclin** partners. Cyclins are so named because their concentrations cycle — they are synthesized and degraded at specific points in the cell cycle.

**Key cyclin-CDK complexes (mammalian):**

| Phase | Cyclin | CDK | Primary function |
|-------|--------|-----|------------------|
| Late G1 | Cyclin D | CDK4/6 | G1 progression, Rb phosphorylation |
| G1/S | Cyclin E | CDK2 | Initiation of S phase |
| S | Cyclin A | CDK2 | DNA replication, S phase progression |
| G2/M | Cyclin A | CDK1 | Preparation for mitosis |
| M | Cyclin B | CDK1 | Mitosis (MPF activity) |

**CDK regulation:**
- **Cyclin binding:** CDKs are inactive without cyclin binding. Cyclin binding opens the active site.
- **Phosphorylation:** T-loop phosphorylation (at Thr160 in CDK2 by CAK, CDK-activating kinase) is required for full activity. Inhibitory phosphorylation (Tyr15, Thr14 in CDK1 by Wee1/Myt1) keeps CDK1-cyclin B inactive until the G2/M transition.
- **CDK inhibitors (CKIs):** p21 (Cip1), p27 (Kip1), and p16 (INK4a) bind and inhibit specific cyclin-CDK complexes.
- **Ubiquitin-mediated degradation:** Cyclins are degraded by the proteasome after ubiquitination by APC/C (anaphase-promoting complex, active in M and G1) or SCF (Skp1-Cul1-F-box, active in G1/S).

### Checkpoints

**G1/S checkpoint (Restriction point in mammalian cells, Start in yeast):**
- Checks: cell size, nutrient availability, growth factor signals, DNA damage.
- Mechanism: Growth factor signaling (RTK/MAPK) induces cyclin D expression. Cyclin D-CDK4/6 phosphorylates Rb (retinoblastoma protein). Hypophosphorylated Rb binds and inhibits E2F transcription factors. Phosphorylation of Rb releases E2F, which activates cyclin E, cyclin A, and DNA replication genes. This creates a **positive feedback loop**: E2F activates its own expression through cyclin E-CDK2 further phosphorylating Rb.
- DNA damage response: ATM/ATR kinases activate p53, which induces p21 (a CKI), inhibiting cyclin-CDK complexes and arresting the cell at G1/S.

**G2/M checkpoint:**
- Checks: DNA replication completion, DNA damage.
- Mechanism: Inactive CDK1-cyclin B (phosphorylated at Tyr15 by Wee1) is held in check. At G2/M, Cdc25 phosphatase removes the inhibitory phosphate, activating CDK1. Active CDK1 phosphorylates Cdc25 (positive feedback) and inhibits Wee1 (double-negative feedback), creating a **bistable switch** that drives an irreversible transition into mitosis.
- DNA damage: ATM/ATR maintain Wee1 activity and inhibit Cdc25, preventing CDK1 activation.

**Spindle assembly checkpoint (SAC):**
- Checks: All kinetochores are properly attached to spindle microtubules.
- Mechanism: Unattached kinetochores recruit Mad2, which binds and inhibits Cdc20 (the APC/C activator). Without active APC/C-Cdc20, securin and cyclin B are not degraded. Securin inhibits separase, the protease that cleaves cohesin (the protein holding sister chromatids together). When all kinetochores are properly attached, Mad2 inhibition is released, APC/C-Cdc20 degrades securin and cyclin B, separase cleaves cohesin, and sister chromatids segregate.

### Mitosis stages

1. **Prophase:** Chromosomes condense. Centrosomes duplicate and migrate to opposite poles. Spindle microtubules form.

2. **Prometaphase:** Nuclear envelope breaks down. Kinetochores (protein complexes at centromeres) capture spindle microtubules.

3. **Metaphase:** Chromosomes align at the metaphase plate (equatorial plane). The spindle assembly checkpoint operates here.

4. **Anaphase:** Cohesin is cleaved by separase. Sister chromatids separate and are pulled to opposite poles by microtubule depolymerization (anaphase A) and spindle elongation (anaphase B).

5. **Telophase:** Nuclear envelopes reform around each set of chromosomes. Chromosomes decondense.

**Cytokinesis:** A contractile ring of actin and myosin pinches the cell in two at the midbody. In animal cells, this creates two daughter cells. In plant cells, a cell plate forms instead.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**The cell cycle is controlled by a bistable switch driven by positive feedback in CDK1 regulation.**

The G2/M transition exhibits **bistability**: the cell is either in interphase (low CDK1 activity) or mitosis (high CDK1 activity), with no stable intermediate. This is because of two interlocking feedback loops:

1. CDK1 activates its activator Cdc25 (positive feedback: more CDK1 activity leads to more Cdc25 activity, which further activates CDK1).

2. CDK1 inhibits its inhibitor Wee1 (double-negative feedback: more CDK1 activity leads to less Wee1 activity, which further activates CDK1).

The combined effect creates a system with two stable states and a threshold. Cyclin B accumulation raises CDK1 levels until the threshold is crossed, at which point the system snaps irreversibly to the high-CDK1 (mitotic) state. The reverse transition (mitotic exit) is triggered by APC/C-mediated cyclin B degradation, which lowers CDK1 below the lower threshold.

### Worked example: drug arrest fraction

A drug arrests cells at G2/M. The cell cycle is 24 hours: G1 = 8h, S = 8h, G2 = 4h, M = 4h (note the atypically long M phase for this example). In an asynchronous population, cells are uniformly distributed throughout the cycle.

The fraction of cells arrested at G2/M at any given instant is the fraction of cells that have reached G2/M but cannot pass through it. Since the drug blocks at G2/M, cells accumulate at the block. In steady state, nearly 100% accumulate (as shown above). At the initial moment, the fraction *at or past G2* is: $(4 + 4) / 24 = 8/24 \approx 33\%$ are in G2 or M. The remaining 67% are in G1 or S and will eventually reach the block.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Name the four phases of the cell cycle in order and briefly state what happens in each.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

G1, S, G2, M. Growth, DNA synthesis, preparation, division.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

1. **G1 (Gap 1):** Cell growth, metabolic activity, preparation for DNA replication. The G1/S checkpoint (restriction point) controls entry into S phase.
2. **S (Synthesis):** DNA replication. The entire genome is duplicated.
3. **G2 (Gap 2):** Preparation for mitosis. DNA damage is checked. The G2/M checkpoint controls entry into M phase.
4. **M (Mitosis):** Nuclear division (prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase) followed by cytokinesis (cell splitting).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

What is the role of the retinoblastoma protein (Rb) in cell cycle control?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Rb binds a transcription factor. When Rb is phosphorylated, what happens?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Rb is a **tumor suppressor** that controls the G1/S transition. In its hypophosphorylated (active) form, Rb binds and inhibits E2F transcription factors. When growth factor signaling activates cyclin D-CDK4/6, Rb is progressively phosphorylated. Hyperphosphorylated Rb releases E2F, which activates genes required for S phase (including cyclin E and DNA replication enzymes). Loss of Rb function (by mutation or viral oncoprotein binding) removes this brake, allowing uncontrolled cell cycle entry — this is why Rb was the first tumor suppressor gene discovered.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Explain how the positive feedback loop between CDK1 and Cdc25 creates an irreversible switch at the G2/M transition.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

CDK1 activates Cdc25. Cdc25 activates CDK1. What does this loop do?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The loop works as follows: Cyclin B accumulates during G2, forming inactive CDK1-cyclin B complexes (inhibited by Wee1-mediated phosphorylation). As cyclin B levels rise, a small amount of CDK1 is activated (by basal Cdc25 activity). This active CDK1 phosphorylates and activates more Cdc25, which dephosphorylates and activates more CDK1. This is **positive feedback**.

Additionally, CDK1 phosphorylates and inactivates Wee1 (its inhibitor), creating a **double-negative feedback** that further reinforces CDK1 activation.

The combined feedback creates a bistable switch with two stable states: low CDK1 (interphase) and high CDK1 (mitosis). Once CDK1 activity crosses the activation threshold, the feedback loop drives it rapidly to the maximum (mitotic) state. The transition is **irreversible** because returning to the low state requires degradation of cyclin B by APC/C — a separate mechanism that is activated only after chromosomes have properly segregated.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

The cancer drug paclitaxel (Taxol) stabilizes microtubules, preventing their depolymerization. Explain why this arrests cells in mitosis.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

During anaphase, kinetochore microtubules must shorten to pull chromosomes apart. What happens if microtubules cannot depolymerize?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

During mitosis, kinetochore microtubules must be dynamic — they grow and shrink to allow chromosomes to congress to the metaphase plate and then shorten (depolymerize) during anaphase to pull sister chromatids toward the poles.

Paclitaxel stabilizes microtubules by binding the beta-tubulin subunit and preventing depolymerization. This freezes the spindle in a static state: chromosomes cannot properly align, the spindle checkpoint remains active (unattached or improperly attached kinetochores persist), and the cell is arrested in metaphase. Prolonged mitotic arrest eventually triggers **mitotic catastrophe** — the cell either undergoes apoptosis or exits mitosis without proper chromosome segregation (mitotic slippage), leading to aneuploidy and cell death. This selective toxicity to dividing cells is the basis of paclitaxel's anti-cancer activity.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

p53 is mutated in ~50% of all human cancers. Explain the multiple roles of p53 in cell cycle control and why its loss promotes cancer.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

p53 is activated by DNA damage. It induces cell cycle arrest and apoptosis. What happens when it is lost?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

p53 is a transcription factor activated by DNA damage (via ATM/ATR kinases and the stabilizing effect of phosphorylation, which prevents MDM2-mediated degradation). It has three major tumor-suppressive functions:

1. **Cell cycle arrest:** p53 induces p21 transcription. p21 inhibits cyclin-CDK complexes, arresting the cell at G1/S or G2/M. This gives the cell time to repair DNA damage before replicating or dividing.

2. **Apoptosis:** If DNA damage is too severe to repair, p53 activates pro-apoptotic genes (Bax, Puma, Noxa) and represses anti-apoptotic genes (Bcl-2). This eliminates potentially cancerous cells.

3. **Senescence:** p53 can trigger permanent cell cycle exit (senescence), irreversibly preventing damaged cells from proliferating.

When p53 is mutated:
- DNA damage does not trigger arrest — cells replicate damaged DNA, accumulating mutations.
- Apoptosis is not induced — damaged cells survive instead of being eliminated.
- Genomic instability increases — chromosome abnormalities and aneuploidy accumulate.
- The DNA damage checkpoint is effectively disabled — the cell loses its most important quality-control mechanism.

This is why p53 mutation is so common in cancer: it removes the "guardian of the genome," allowing the genomic chaos that drives tumor evolution.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

The anaphase-promoting complex/cyclosome (APC/C) targets both securin and cyclin B for degradation. Securin degradation activates separase (which cleaves cohesin), while cyclin B degradation inactivates CDK1. Explain why the order of these events matters: what would go wrong if cyclin B were degraded *before* securin?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

CDK1 activity is needed to maintain the mitotic state. If CDK1 is turned off early, what happens to the mitotic apparatus?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If cyclin B were degraded before securin, CDK1 would be inactivated prematurely. The consequences:

1. **Premature mitotic exit:** CDK1 activity maintains the mitotic state — condensed chromosomes, broken-down nuclear envelope, assembled spindle. Inactivating CDK1 before chromosome segregation is complete would cause the cell to begin exiting mitosis while chromosomes are still attached to the metaphase plate.

2. **Chromosome segregation failure:** Without CDK1 activity, the spindle would begin to disassemble, chromosomes would start to decondense, and the nuclear envelope would reform — all before separase has had a chance to cleave cohesin and allow sister chromatid separation.

3. **Nuclear envelope reformation around unsegregated chromosomes:** This would trap both sets of chromosomes in a single nucleus, causing severe aneuploidy or a polyploid cell.

In reality, APC/C-Cdc20 targets securin first (or simultaneously), allowing separase activation while CDK1 activity is still high enough to maintain the mitotic apparatus. CDK1 is then inactivated by cyclin B degradation after chromosome segregation is well underway. This ordering is enforced by the differential sensitivity of securin and cyclin B to APC/C, and by the fact that separase also promotes cyclin B degradation (positive feedback for mitotic exit).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The MPF (Maturation/Mitosis Promoting Factor) assay by Yoshio Masui (1971) showed that cytoplasm from a mitotic frog egg could induce mitosis when injected into an interphase egg. Describe how this experiment demonstrated that MPF is a cytoplasmic factor and discuss its molecular identity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If mitosis-inducing activity can be transferred by cytoplasm alone, the factor is not nuclear. What is MPF?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Masui and Markert (1971) extracted cytoplasm from frog oocytes that had been hormonally induced to undergo maturation (enter M phase) and injected it into immature oocytes. The recipient oocytes immediately underwent maturation — they entered M phase. The key observations:

1. **Cytoplasmic transfer:** The active factor was in the cytoplasm, not the nucleus (nuclear transfer did not work).
2. **Activity cycles:** MPF activity was high in M phase and low in interphase. Injection of interphase cytoplasm did not induce maturation.
3. **Self-amplification:** A small amount of MPF-containing cytoplasm could activate MPF in a much larger volume of recipient cytoplasm, suggesting a positive feedback loop.

The molecular identity of MPF was discovered in 1988 by Maller and colleagues: MPF = **CDK1-cyclin B complex**. The self-amplification observed by Masui corresponds to the CDK1-Cdc25 positive feedback loop described in this unit. This experiment was pivotal because it established that the trigger for mitosis is a *biochemical* signal (a kinase activity) rather than a structural or nuclear event.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Cancer cells often have mutations that inactivate the G1/S checkpoint (e.g., Rb loss, p16 mutation, cyclin D amplification). Explain why loss of this specific checkpoint is more commonly selected for in cancer than loss of the G2/M or spindle checkpoints.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider which checkpoint is the primary gatekeeper for cell cycle entry. What happens if it is removed?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The G1/S checkpoint (restriction point) is the primary decision point for whether a cell *enters the cell cycle at all*. It integrates growth factor signals, nutrient status, cell size, and DNA damage. Once a cell passes the restriction point, it is committed to completing S, G2, and M.

In cancer, the selective pressure is for **proliferation** — bypassing the signals that normally restrain cell division. Mutations that disable the G1/S checkpoint (Rb loss, p16 loss, cyclin D amplification, p53 mutation) allow cells to enter S phase without the normal growth factor requirements or DNA quality checks. This gives cancer cells a growth advantage.

The G2/M and spindle checkpoints are less frequently inactivated in cancer because:
1. Loss of the G2/M checkpoint would cause cells to enter mitosis with damaged or unreplicated DNA, leading to mitotic catastrophe and cell death — *counter-selective* for the tumor.
2. Loss of the spindle checkpoint would cause massive aneuploidy, which is often lethal to the cell. Some aneuploidy is tolerated (and even selected for in cancer), but complete loss of the spindle checkpoint is usually fatal.

The G1/S checkpoint is the "weakest link" that cancer cells most benefit from breaking: it unlocks uncontrolled proliferation without immediately killing the cell. The G2/M and spindle checkpoints are retained because they protect against lethal genomic damage, even in cancer cells.

</details>

</aside>

## Connections [Master]

- **DNA replication [17.05.01]** occurs during S phase and must be completed before mitosis begins. The G2/M checkpoint enforces this dependency.

- **Cell signaling [17.07.01]** provides the external signals (growth factors) that drive the G1/S transition through RTK/MAPK pathways activating cyclin D expression.

- **Mutation and repair [17.06.01]** connects through the DNA damage checkpoints: ATM/ATR kinases sense DNA damage and halt the cell cycle via p53 and CHK1/CHK2 to allow repair before replication or division.

- **Cancer biology** is fundamentally the disease of cell cycle deregulation. Oncogenes (Ras, Myc, cyclin D) drive cycle progression; tumor suppressors (Rb, p53, p16) restrain it. The balance determines whether cells divide or not.

- **Dynamical systems theory [02.12.01]** provides the mathematical framework for understanding the cell cycle as a limit-cycle oscillator, with the CDK-cyclin system producing sustained oscillations in kinase activity.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The genetic dissection of the cell cycle began with Leland Hartwell's isolation of **cdc (cell division cycle) mutants** in budding yeast (1970s), which identified genes required at specific cycle stages. Paul Nurse found the homologous gene (cdc2) in fission yeast and showed that the human version (CDK1) could substitute for it — demonstrating the universal conservation of the cell cycle machinery. Tim Hunt discovered cyclins in sea urchin embryos as proteins that accumulated and then abruptly disappeared at each cell division. All three shared the 2001 Nobel Prize.

The dynamical-systems perspective was developed by Albert Novak, John Tyson, and Bela Novak, who showed that the CDK-cyclin regulatory network could be modeled as a system of ODEs that produces limit-cycle oscillations. Their models reproduce the major features of the cell cycle (bistable G2/M switch, irreversible transitions) and make testable predictions about how perturbations affect cycle timing.

The philosophical significance is that the cell cycle demonstrates how a complex, ordered process can emerge from a relatively simple set of interacting components (a handful of cyclins, CDKs, and regulators). The irreversibility of cell cycle transitions — once you commit to S phase, you finish replication; once you enter mitosis, you finish division — is not enforced by an external controller but by the intrinsic dynamics of the feedback loops. The cell cycle is a self-organizing oscillator, not a clockwork machine.
