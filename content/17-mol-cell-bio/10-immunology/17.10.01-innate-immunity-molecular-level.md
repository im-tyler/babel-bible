## Intuition [Beginner]

Your body is under constant assault. Bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites try to get in every time you breathe, eat, or get a cut. Your first line of defense is the **innate immune system** — a rapid, non-specific response that attacks anything that looks foreign.

The innate system does not need to "learn" like the adaptive immune system (antibodies and T cells). It is ready from birth. It recognizes broad patterns common to pathogens but absent from healthy human cells: bacterial cell-wall components, viral RNA, fungal cell walls. These patterns are detected by **pattern recognition receptors (PRRs)**.

When a PRR detects a pathogen, it triggers an alarm. The cell releases **cytokines** — chemical messengers that recruit and activate other immune cells. **Phagocytes** (neutrophils and macrophages) literally eat the invaders. **Complement proteins** in the blood coat pathogens and punch holes in their membranes. **Natural killer (NK) cells** destroy cells that have been infected by viruses or have turned cancerous.

The innate response is fast (minutes to hours) but imprecise. It buys time for the slower but more precise adaptive immune system (days) to mount a targeted response.

## Visual [Beginner]

Imagine a castle under siege. The castle walls are your skin and mucous membranes. Outside the walls, guards (PRRs) patrol and look for anything wearing enemy uniforms (pathogen patterns like LPS on bacteria or double-stranded RNA from viruses).

When a guard spots an intruder, it sounds an alarm (releases cytokines like TNF-alpha and IL-6). Soldiers (neutrophils) rush to the scene and engulf the enemy (phagocytosis). Siege weapons (complement proteins) attack the enemy directly. Special agents (NK cells) identify and eliminate traitors inside the castle walls (virus-infected cells).

## Worked example [Beginner]

Lipopolysaccharide (LPS) from Gram-negative bacteria is detected by Toll-like receptor 4 (TLR4) on macrophages. Trace the signaling pathway:

1. **LPS binds LBP** (LPS-binding protein) in the blood, which transfers LPS to **CD14** on the macrophage surface.

2. CD14 presents LPS to the **TLR4/MD-2 complex**. TLR4 dimerizes.

3. TLR4's intracellular TIR domain recruits the adaptor **MyD88**.

4. MyD88 recruits **IRAK4** (IL-1 receptor-associated kinase 4), which phosphorylates **IRAK1**.

5. IRAK1 activates **TRAF6** (TNF receptor-associated factor 6), a ubiquitin ligase.

6. TRAF6-mediated ubiquitination activates the **TAK1** kinase complex.

7. TAK1 activates two pathways:
   - **IKK complex** $\to$ phosphorylation and degradation of **IkB** $\to$ release of **NF-kB** $\to$ NF-kB enters nucleus and activates inflammatory genes (TNF-a, IL-6, IL-1b, COX-2).
   - **MAPK cascade** (JNK, p38, ERK) $\to$ activation of AP-1 transcription factor $\to$ additional inflammatory gene expression.

8. The macrophage secretes **TNF-alpha** and **IL-6**, causing local inflammation, fever, and recruitment of neutrophils.

**Exercise (easy, true/false).**

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise (medium, symbolic).**

Natural killer (NK) cells kill virus-infected cells but spare healthy cells. They use a "missing self" recognition strategy. Explain how this works in terms of MHC class I molecules.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Healthy cells display MHC class I on their surface. Viruses often block MHC I expression. NK cells have inhibitory receptors for MHC I. What happens when MHC I is absent?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

NK cells have two types of receptors: **activating receptors** (which recognize stress-induced molecules on target cells) and **inhibitory receptors** (which bind MHC class I molecules on healthy cells).

In a **healthy cell**, MHC class I is displayed on the surface. NK cell inhibitory receptors bind MHC I, sending a "do not kill" signal that overrides the activating signal. The NK cell spares the cell.

In a **virus-infected cell**, many viruses have evolved mechanisms to downregulate MHC class I (to avoid detection by cytotoxic T cells of the adaptive immune system). Without MHC I on the surface, the NK cell's inhibitory receptors are not engaged. The activating signal (from stress ligands that are upregulated on infected cells) dominates, and the NK cell kills the target by releasing perforin (which forms pores) and granzymes (which enter through the pores and activate apoptosis).

This is the "missing self" hypothesis (Karre, 1985): NK cells detect the *absence* of self markers (MHC I) rather than the presence of foreign markers.

</details>

</aside>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Innate immunity is the first-line, non-specific defense system that provides immediate protection against pathogens through physical barriers, pattern recognition, phagocytosis, inflammation, complement activation, and cytotoxic killing.

### Pattern recognition receptors (PRRs)

PRRs are germline-encoded receptors that recognize **pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs)** — conserved molecular structures shared by broad classes of microbes — and **damage-associated molecular patterns (DAMPs)** — host molecules released during tissue damage.

**Toll-like receptors (TLRs):** The best-characterized PRR family. 10 functional TLRs in humans.

| TLR | Ligand (PAMP) | Source |
|-----|--------------|--------|
| TLR1/2 | Triacyl lipopeptides | Bacteria |
| TLR2 | Peptidoglycan, lipoteichoic acid | Gram+ bacteria |
| TLR3 | Double-stranded RNA | Viruses |
| TLR4 | LPS (lipopolysaccharide) | Gram- bacteria |
| TLR5 | Flagellin | Motile bacteria |
| TLR7/8 | Single-stranded RNA | Viruses |
| TLR9 | Unmethylated CpG DNA | Bacteria, viruses |
| TLR10 | Unknown | (inhibitory?) |

**NOD-like receptors (NLRs):** Cytoplasmic sensors that detect intracellular PAMPs and DAMPs. Key members:
- **NOD1/NOD2:** Detect bacterial peptidoglycan fragments in the cytosol. NOD2 mutations are associated with Crohn's disease.
- **NLRP3:** Forms the **inflammasome** — a multi-protein complex that activates caspase-1, which cleaves pro-IL-1beta and pro-IL-18 into active cytokines. Triggered by diverse stimuli: uric acid crystals (gout), amyloid-beta (Alzheimer's), ATP, pore-forming toxins.

**RIG-I-like receptors (RLRs):** Detect viral RNA in the cytoplasm. RIG-I senses 5'-triphosphate RNA (a hallmark of viral replication); MDA5 detects long dsRNA.

**C-type lectin receptors (CLRs):** Detect fungal carbohydrate patterns (mannans, beta-glucans).

### Complement system

The complement system is a cascade of ~30 plasma proteins that opsonize pathogens, recruit phagocytes, and directly kill bacteria by membrane attack complex (MAC) formation.

**Three activation pathways:**

1. **Classical pathway:** Antibody-antigen complexes bind C1q, initiating the cascade. Links innate and adaptive immunity.

2. **Lectin pathway:** Mannose-binding lectin (MBL) binds mannose on pathogen surfaces, initiating the cascade via MBL-associated serine proteases (MASPs).

3. **Alternative pathway:** C3 spontaneously hydrolyzes at low rate. On pathogen surfaces (lacking regulatory proteins), this initiates an amplification loop. This is the most ancient pathway.

All three converge on **C3 convertase**, which cleaves C3 into:
- **C3a:** Anaphylatoxin (triggers mast cell degranulation, inflammation)
- **C3b:** Opsonin (coats pathogen surface, marking it for phagocytosis via complement receptors on macrophages)

The **membrane attack complex (MAC):** C5 convertase cleaves C5 into C5a (anaphylatoxin) and C5b. C5b recruits C6, C7, C8, and multiple C9 molecules to form a pore (MAC) in the pathogen membrane, causing lysis.

### Cytokines

Key innate immune cytokines:

- **TNF-alpha:** Induces inflammation, fever, endothelial activation (upregulates adhesion molecules for leukocyte recruitment). Excess TNF causes septic shock.
- **IL-1beta:** Produced upon inflammasome activation. Induces fever, T cell activation. Targeted by anakinra (IL-1 receptor antagonist).
- **IL-6:** Acute-phase response (stimulates liver to produce CRP, fibrinogen), B cell differentiation, fever.
- **Type I interferons (IFN-alpha, IFN-beta):** Antiviral cytokines produced by virus-infected cells. Induce an antiviral state in neighboring cells (upregulate Mx proteins, PKR, OAS). Activated by TLR3, TLR7/8, RIG-I/MDA5 signaling.
- **IL-12:** Produced by dendritic cells and macrophages. Drives Th1 differentiation and NK cell activation.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**The pattern recognition theory (Janeway, 1989): The innate immune system detects infection by recognizing conserved molecular patterns that are shared by large classes of pathogens but are not present in the host.**

The theory makes three predictions:

1. **Conserved patterns exist.** PAMPs are molecular structures essential for pathogen survival (and therefore difficult for the pathogen to alter). LPS in Gram-negative outer membranes, peptidoglycan in bacterial cell walls, double-stranded RNA during viral replication, unmethylated CpG DNA in bacterial genomes. These cannot be easily mutated away without killing the pathogen.

2. **Specific receptors detect these patterns.** The host evolves germline-encoded receptors (TLRs, NLRs, RLRs) that bind these conserved patterns. This is a form of **innate immune recognition** — genetically hardwired, not learned.

3. **Pattern recognition activates adaptive immunity.** The innate system does not just fight infection directly — it also *activates* the adaptive immune system by providing co-stimulatory signals and cytokines that direct T and B cell responses. This was Janeway's key insight: innate immunity provides the context that determines whether adaptive immunity is activated.

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Explain the difference between PAMPs and DAMPs. Give an example of each.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

PAMPs come from pathogens. DAMPs come from damaged host cells.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**PAMPs (Pathogen-Associated Molecular Patterns):** Conserved molecular structures on pathogens that are recognized by PRRs. Example: LPS (lipopolysaccharide) in the outer membrane of Gram-negative bacteria.

**DAMPs (Damage-Associated Molecular Patterns):** Host molecules that are normally intracellular but become extracellular during tissue damage, where they activate innate immunity. <aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Explain how the NLRP3 inflammasome is activated and why its dysregulation contributes to both autoimmune and metabolic diseases.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

NLRP3 integrates multiple danger signals. What happens when it is overactive?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

NLRP3 inflammasome activation requires two signals:
1. **Priming:** TLR signaling (e.g., LPS via TLR4) upregulates NLRP3 and pro-IL-1beta expression (via NF-kB).
2. **Activation:** A second stimulus triggers NLRP3 oligomerization and inflammasome assembly. Activators include: extracellular ATP (via P2X7 receptor and K+ efflux), uric acid crystals (gout), amyloid-beta plaques (Alzheimer's), cholesterol crystals (atherosclerosis), mitochondrial ROS, and pore-forming toxins.

The assembled inflammasome recruits and activates caspase-1, which cleaves pro-IL-1beta and pro-IL-18 into active cytokines and also triggers pyroptosis (inflammatory cell death).

Dysregulation contributes to:
- **Gout:** Uric acid crystals chronically activate NLRP3, causing persistent IL-1beta production and joint inflammation.
- **Alzheimer's disease:** Amyloid-beta plaques activate NLRP3 in microglia, contributing to neuroinflammation.
- **Atherosclerosis:** Cholesterol crystals in arterial plaques activate NLRP3 in macrophages.
- **Type 2 diabetes:** NLRP3 activation by glucose and free fatty acids in pancreatic islets impairs insulin secretion.

NLRP3 gain-of-function mutations cause cryopyrin-associated periodic syndromes (CAPS), treated with IL-1 blockers (anakinra, canakinumab).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Trace the complement alternative pathway from spontaneous C3 hydrolysis to MAC formation. Why is the alternative pathway called "amplification" loop?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

C3b deposited on pathogen surfaces forms more C3 convertase, which makes more C3b — a positive feedback loop.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

1. C3 spontaneously hydrolyzes to C3(H2O) at a low rate (tickover).
2. C3(H2O) binds Factor B, which is cleaved by Factor D to form C3(H2O)Bb — a fluid-phase C3 convertase.
3. This convertase cleaves C3 into C3a + C3b.
4. On host cells, Factor I (with cofactor Factor H) quickly degrades C3b — the pathway is inactivated.
5. On pathogen surfaces (which lack Factor H and other regulators), C3b binds and forms C3bB (with Factor B), which is cleaved to **C3bBb** — the alternative pathway C3 convertase.
6. C3bBb is stabilized by **properdin** and cleaves more C3 into C3a + C3b.
7. New C3b deposits nearby, forming more C3bBb — **positive feedback amplification**.
8. Some C3b joins the existing C3 convertase to form **C3bBbC3b** (C5 convertase), which cleaves C5 into C5a + C5b.
9. C5b initiates MAC formation: C5b-C6-C7-C8-C9(n).

It is called the amplification loop because each C3b deposited creates a new C3 convertase that produces more C3b, which creates more convertases. A single initial event can be amplified into millions of C3b deposits on the pathogen surface.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

Sepsis is a life-threatening condition caused by dysregulated innate immune response to infection. LPS from Gram-negative bacteria is the most common trigger. Trace how LPS can lead to septic shock through TNF-alpha overproduction, and explain why anti-TNF therapy has been disappointing in sepsis despite TNF being a central mediator.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

TNF causes vasodilation and vascular leak. Why would blocking it after sepsis onset not help?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Path to septic shock:**
1. Bacteria release LPS into the bloodstream.
2. LPS binds TLR4 on macrophages throughout the body (especially liver, spleen, lungs).
3. Massive NF-kB activation produces huge amounts of TNF-alpha, IL-1beta, and IL-6.
4. TNF-alpha causes: (a) systemic vasodilation (hypotension), (b) increased vascular permeability (edema, fluid loss from circulation), (c) endothelial activation (pro-coagulant state, DIC), (d) myocardial depression.
5. The combination of hypotension, edema, and microvascular thrombosis causes organ failure and death.

**Why anti-TNF therapy fails:**
TNF-alpha peaks within 1-2 hours of LPS exposure and triggers downstream cascades (IL-1, IL-6, coagulation, complement) that continue independently. By the time sepsis is clinically recognized (typically hours after the TNF peak), blocking TNF does not stop the already-activated cascades. Additionally, TNF has beneficial roles in early infection control — blocking it may worsen the underlying infection. The timing problem (too late to block the initial trigger) and the redundancy of inflammatory mediators (blocking one does not stop the others) explain why dozens of anti-inflammatory sepsis trials have failed.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

TLRs use the same basic signaling architecture (TIR domain adaptors, IRAK, TRAF6, NF-kB) but activate different gene programs depending on which TLR is stimulated and in which cell type. Explain how signaling specificity is achieved from a shared pathway.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Different TLRs use different adaptor combinations. The cellular context matters.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Specificity arises from multiple layers:

1. **Adaptor selection:** All TLRs signal through TIR domains, but they recruit different adaptor proteins:
   - **MyD88:** Used by all TLRs except TLR3. Activates NF-kB and MAPKs.
   - **TRIF:** Used by TLR3 and TLR4 (MyD88-independent pathway). Activates IRF3 (interferon regulatory factor 3) in addition to NF-kB, inducing type I interferon production.
   - **TRAM:** Bridging adaptor for TRIF recruitment to TLR4.
   - **TIRAP/Mal:** Bridging adaptor for MyD88 recruitment to TLR2 and TLR4.

2. **Cell type context:** The same TLR signaling in different cell types produces different outcomes because of cell-type-specific transcription factor repertoires, chromatin states, and effector functions. TLR4 in macrophages produces TNF-alpha and IL-6; TLR4 in dendritic cells induces co-stimulatory molecule expression (CD80/86) for T cell activation; TLR4 in endothelial cells induces adhesion molecules (E-selectin, ICAM-1).

3. **Temporal dynamics:** Early vs. late gene induction is controlled by transcription factor kinetics. NF-kB target genes include both immediate-early genes (TNF-alpha) and secondary response genes (inducible by autocrine cytokine signaling).

4. **Subcellular localization:** TLR4 signals from both the plasma membrane (MyD88-dependent, early NF-kB) and endosomes (TRIF-dependent, late NF-kB + IRF3), producing temporally distinct outputs.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The hygiene hypothesis proposes that reduced exposure to microbes in early childhood (due to antibiotics, cesarean delivery, small family size, clean water) increases the risk of allergic and autoimmune diseases. Explain the immunological mechanism linking reduced innate immune stimulation to increased atopy (allergy).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Think about T helper cell differentiation. Th1 vs Th2 balance. What drives Th1 differentiation?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The mechanistic basis involves the **Th1/Th2 balance**:

Naive CD4+ T cells differentiate into:
- **Th1 cells:** Produce IFN-gamma, fight intracellular bacteria and viruses. Induced by IL-12 from dendritic cells/macrophages activated via TLR signaling.
- **Th2 cells:** Produce IL-4, IL-5, IL-13. Mediate allergic responses and anti-helminth immunity.
- **Treg cells:** Suppress immune responses. Induced by TGF-beta and retinoic acid.

In the absence of adequate microbial stimulation (fewer infections, less environmental bacteria, fewer TLR ligands encountered), innate immune cells produce less IL-12. Without IL-12 to drive Th1 differentiation, the immune system defaults toward **Th2** responses. Th2 cytokines (IL-4, IL-5) promote IgE production by B cells, eosinophil recruitment, and mast cell sensitization — the hallmarks of allergy.

Additionally, reduced TLR stimulation leads to fewer Treg cells (which require chronic low-level innate stimulation for maintenance), reducing immune tolerance.

This explains the epidemiological observation that children raised on farms (high microbial exposure) have lower rates of asthma, hay fever, and eczema, and that mice raised in germ-free environments develop exaggerated Th2/allergic responses. The innate immune system's TLR signaling provides the "training" that keeps the adaptive immune system balanced.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

HIV depletes CD4+ T cells, which are central to adaptive immunity. However, HIV+ patients also suffer from increased susceptibility to bacterial infections (innate immune targets) long before CD4 counts drop significantly. Explain three mechanisms by which HIV impairs innate immunity independent of CD4+ T cell depletion.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

HIV infects macrophages and dendritic cells (which express CD4). It also causes immune activation and gut barrier disruption.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

1. **Macrophage infection and dysfunction:** HIV infects CD4+ macrophages (one of the few cell types, along with T cells, that HIV directly infects). Infected macrophages have impaired phagocytosis, reduced cytokine production, and diminished antigen presentation. They also serve as long-lived viral reservoirs.

2. **Gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) destruction:** HIV rapidly depletes CD4+ T cells in the gut mucosa (which contains ~70% of the body's immune cells), causing loss of mucosal integrity. Bacterial products (LPS) translocate from the gut lumen into the bloodstream, causing chronic innate immune activation. This persistent activation paradoxically *exhausts* innate immune cells and impairs their ability to respond to new infections.

3. **Neutrophil dysfunction:** Chronic immune activation in HIV infection leads to neutrophil exhaustion and impaired chemotaxis, phagocytosis, and oxidative burst. Neutropenia (low neutrophil count) also occurs as a side effect of antiretroviral therapy and opportunistic infections.

4. **Dendritic cell impairment:** HIV infects and alters dendritic cell function, reducing their ability to present antigens and produce IL-12, which impairs both innate and adaptive immune responses.

5. **NK cell dysfunction:** Chronic immune activation leads to NK cell exhaustion and reduced cytotoxic capacity, impairing viral and tumor surveillance.

These innate immune defects explain why HIV+ patients are susceptible to bacterial pneumonia, tuberculosis, and other bacterial infections even when their adaptive immune function (measured by CD4 count) has not yet severely declined.

</details>

## Connections [Master]

- **Cell signaling [17.07.01]** is the molecular mechanism underlying innate immune activation. TLR signaling uses the same NF-kB and MAPK pathways described in the signaling unit.

- **Cell structure [17.01.01]** provides the context for phagocytosis: membrane remodeling, lysosomal fusion, and the cytoskeletal machinery needed for engulfment.

- **Cell cycle [17.08.01]** connects through immune cell proliferation: activated lymphocytes undergo clonal expansion, driven by cytokine signaling through cyclin-CDK activation.

- **Transcription [17.05.02]** is activated by innate signaling: NF-kB, IRF3, and AP-1 are transcription factors that induce inflammatory gene expression.

- **Evolution [19.04.01]** explains the conservation of PAMPs (pathogens cannot easily change LPS or peptidoglycan structure) and the ancient origins of innate immunity (TLRs are found in insects, plants, and even single-celled organisms).

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The concept of innate immunity dates to Ilya Metchnikoff's observation in 1882 that starfish larval cells surrounded and engulfed thorns — the discovery of phagocytosis (Nobel Prize 1908, shared with Ehrlich for adaptive immunity). Metchnikoff proposed that cellular immunity (phagocytes) was the primary defense, while Ehrlich championed humoral immunity (antibodies). Both were right; the two systems cooperate.

The modern understanding of innate immunity was revolutionized by Charles Janeway's 1989 proposition that the innate immune system provides the "instructions" for adaptive immunity through pattern recognition. This was confirmed by the discovery of human Toll-like receptors by Janeway and Medzhitov (1997) and the identification of TLR4 as the LPS receptor by Beutler (1998, Nobel Prize 2011). Hoffmann's work on Drosophila Toll (Nobel Prize 2011, shared with Beutler) showed that innate immunity is ancient and conserved.

The philosophical significance is that innate immunity solves the "self/nonself discrimination" problem not by recognizing every possible foreign molecule (an impossible task) but by recognizing a limited set of conserved patterns that reliably indicate microbial presence. This is a *computational* solution to a recognition problem: rather than cataloging all possible pathogens, the innate system detects a small number of molecular signatures that are highly correlated with infection. It is an elegant example of how biological systems solve complex problems with simple rules.
