## Intuition [Beginner]

Cells need energy to survive, grow, and divide. The primary energy currency is ATP. Cellular respiration is the process by which cells extract energy from glucose (and other fuel molecules) to regenerate ATP from ADP and inorganic phosphate. This process occurs in three main stages: glycolysis (in the cytoplasm), the citric acid cycle (in the mitochondrial matrix), and oxidative phosphorylation (on the inner mitochondrial membrane). This unit covers the first two.

**Glycolysis** (Greek: glykys = sweet, lysis = splitting) breaks one molecule of glucose (6 carbons) into two molecules of pyruvate (3 carbons each). It happens in ten enzyme-catalysed steps in the cytoplasm. The net yield per glucose is 2 ATP (used directly by the cell) and 2 NADH (electron carriers that feed into oxidative phosphorylation). Glycolysis does not require oxygen; it is an ancient pathway that evolved before atmospheric oxygen existed.

Glycolysis has two phases. The **investment phase** (steps 1-5) uses 2 ATP to phosphorylate glucose and split it into two three-carbon molecules. The **payoff phase** (steps 6-10) produces 4 ATP (for a net of 2) and 2 NADH. The key regulated enzyme is phosphofructokinase (PFK), which commits glucose to glycolysis and is inhibited by ATP and citrate (signals of abundant energy).

After glycolysis, pyruvate enters the mitochondrion (in eukaryotes) and is oxidised to acetyl-CoA by the **pyruvate dehydrogenase complex** (PDC). This reaction produces one NADH per pyruvate (2 per glucose) and releases one CO2 per pyruvate. Acetyl-CoA is a two-carbon molecule that feeds into the citric acid cycle.

The **citric acid cycle** (also called the Krebs cycle or TCA cycle) takes place in the mitochondrial matrix. Each turn of the cycle oxidises one acetyl-CoA (2 carbons) to 2 CO2, producing 3 NADH, 1 FADH2, and 1 GTP (equivalent to 1 ATP). Because each glucose produces 2 acetyl-CoA, the cycle turns twice per glucose, yielding 6 NADH, 2 FADH2, and 2 GTP.

The NADH and FADH2 from glycolysis, pyruvate oxidation, and the citric acid cycle carry high-energy electrons to the electron transport chain (unit 17.04.02), where the majority of ATP is produced.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Calculate the total ATP yield from one glucose molecule through glycolysis and the citric acid cycle (before oxidative phosphorylation).

Step 1. **Glycolysis:** 1 glucose yields 2 pyruvate. Net: 2 ATP + 2 NADH.

Step 2. **Pyruvate oxidation:** 2 pyruvate yield 2 acetyl-CoA. Net: 2 NADH + 2 CO2.

Step 3. **Citric acid cycle (2 turns):** 2 acetyl-CoA yield 6 NADH + 2 FADH2 + 2 GTP + 4 CO2.

Step 4. **Total electron carriers:**

| Stage | NADH | FADH2 | Substrate-level ATP/GTP |
|---|---|---|---|
| Glycolysis | 2 | 0 | 2 ATP |
| Pyruvate oxidation | 2 | 0 | 0 |
| Citric acid cycle | 6 | 2 | 2 GTP |
| **Total** | **10** | **2** | **4** |

Step 5. **ATP equivalent estimate.** Using the approximate ATP yield per electron carrier (from established P/O ratios): each NADH produces ~2.5 ATP, each FADH2 produces ~1.5 ATP through oxidative phosphorylation. Therefore:

- NADH: $10 \times 2.5 = 25$ ATP
- FADH2: $2 \times 1.5 = 3$ ATP
- Substrate-level: 4 ATP (2 from glycolysis + 2 GTP)
- **Total: ~32 ATP per glucose**

However, the cytoplasmic NADH from glycolysis (2 NADH) must be shuttled into the mitochondrion, and the shuttle mechanism affects the yield. The malate-aspartate shuttle preserves the full 2.5 ATP/NADH; the glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle transfers electrons to FAD, yielding only ~1.5 ATP per NADH. With the glycerol-3-phosphate shuttle: $8 \times 2.5 + (2+2) \times 1.5 + 4 = 20 + 6 + 4 = 30$ ATP. This unit focuses on the substrate-level yield (4 ATP equivalent); the full accounting is completed in unit 17.04.02.

**Exercise (easy, true/false).**

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

**False.** No enzyme in the citric acid cycle uses oxygen directly as a substrate. The cycle itself only produces NADH, FADH2, GTP, and CO2. <aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise (medium, short answer).**

Explain why phosphofructokinase (PFK-1) is called the "committed step" of glycolysis, and describe how it is regulated by ATP and AMP. Why does this make metabolic sense?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider what happens to fructose-6-phosphate if it is not phosphorylated by PFK-1. Also consider the cell's energy state: when is glycolysis needed most?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

PFK-1 catalyses the phosphorylation of fructose-6-phosphate to fructose-1,6-bisphosphate. Before this step, the intermediates (glucose-6-phosphate and fructose-6-phosphate) can be diverted to other pathways: the pentose phosphate pathway or glycogen synthesis. After PFK-1 acts, the product is committed to glycolysis -- there is no alternative metabolic fate for fructose-1,6-bisphosphate.

PFK-1 is allosterically inhibited by ATP (binding to a regulatory site reduces activity) and activated by AMP (which competes with ATP at the regulatory site). When ATP levels are high, the cell has ample energy and glycolysis is slowed. When AMP levels are high (indicating low ATP), PFK-1 is activated, accelerating glycolysis to regenerate ATP. This feedback regulation matches glycolytic flux to the cell's energy demand.

</details>

</aside>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Glycolysis: ten-step pathway

The ten reactions of glycolysis, catalysed by soluble cytoplasmic enzymes:

**Investment phase (steps 1-5, consume 2 ATP):**

| Step | Enzyme | Reaction | ATP/NADH |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Hexokinase | Glucose + ATP -> Glucose-6-phosphate + ADP | -1 ATP |
| 2 | Phosphoglucose isomerase | Glucose-6-P <-> Fructose-6-P | - |
| 3 | Phosphofructokinase-1 (PFK-1) | Fructose-6-P + ATP -> Fructose-1,6-bisP + ADP | -1 ATP |
| 4 | Aldolase | Fructose-1,6-bisP -> DHAP + G3P | - |
| 5 | Triose phosphate isomerase | DHAP <-> G3P | - |

**Payoff phase (steps 6-10, produce 4 ATP + 2 NADH per glucose):**

| Step | Enzyme | Reaction | ATP/NADH |
|---|---|---|---|
| 6 | G3P dehydrogenase | 2 G3P + 2 NAD+ + 2 Pi -> 2 1,3-BPG + 2 NADH | +2 NADH |
| 7 | Phosphoglycerate kinase | 2 1,3-BPG + 2 ADP -> 2 3-PG + 2 ATP | +2 ATP |
| 8 | Phosphoglycerate mutase | 2 3-PG -> 2 2-PG | - |
| 9 | Enolase | 2 2-PG -> 2 PEP + 2 H2O | - |
| 10 | Pyruvate kinase | 2 PEP + 2 ADP -> 2 Pyruvate + 2 ATP | +2 ATP |

Net per glucose: 2 ATP + 2 NADH + 2 pyruvate.

The three irreversible steps (1, 3, 10) are sites of regulation. Steps 1 and 3 are regulated by feedback inhibition; step 10 (pyruvate kinase) is regulated by allosteric effectors (activated by fructose-1,6-bisphosphate via feedforward, inhibited by ATP and alanine).

### Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex (PDC)

The PDC is a multi-enzyme complex that converts pyruvate to acetyl-CoA:

$$
\text{Pyruvate} + \text{CoA} + \text{NAD}^+ \rightarrow \text{Acetyl-CoA} + \text{CO}_2 + \text{NADH}
$$

The complex contains three enzymes (E1: pyruvate dehydrogenase, E2: dihydrolipoyl transacetylase, E3: dihydrolipoyl dehydrogenase) and five cofactors (TPP, lipoamide, CoA, FAD, NAD+). The reaction is irreversible and commits pyruvate to acetyl-CoA rather than other fates (lactate, oxaloacetate, alanine).

PDC regulation: phosphorylation of E1 by PDK (pyruvate dehydrogenase kinase, activated by ATP, NADH, acetyl-CoA) inactivates the complex. Dephosphorylation by PDP (pyruvate dehydrogenase phosphatase, activated by Ca2+) reactivates it.

### Citric acid cycle: eight reactions

The eight steps of the cycle (per acetyl-CoA):

| Step | Enzyme | Reaction | Product |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Citrate synthase | Acetyl-CoA + Oxaloacetate -> Citrate | Citrate |
| 2 | Aconitase | Citrate <-> Isocitrate | Isocitrate |
| 3 | Isocitrate dehydrogenase | Isocitrate + NAD+ -> alpha-KG + CO2 + NADH | alpha-KG + NADH |
| 4 | alpha-KG dehydrogenase | alpha-KG + NAD+ + CoA -> Succinyl-CoA + CO2 + NADH | Succinyl-CoA + NADH |
| 5 | Succinyl-CoA synthetase | Succinyl-CoA + GDP + Pi -> Succinate + GTP + CoA | GTP |
| 6 | Succinate dehydrogenase | Succinate + FAD -> Fumarate + FADH2 | FADH2 |
| 7 | Fumarase | Fumarate + H2O -> Malate | Malate |
| 8 | Malate dehydrogenase | Malate + NAD+ -> Oxaloacetate + NADH | NADH |

Net per acetyl-CoA: 3 NADH + 1 FADH2 + 1 GTP + 2 CO2.

Per glucose (2 acetyl-CoA): 6 NADH + 2 FADH2 + 2 GTP + 4 CO2.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Complete carbon and energy accounting for glucose oxidation through glycolysis and the citric acid cycle).** *One molecule of glucose (C6H12O6) oxidised through glycolysis, pyruvate oxidation, and two turns of the citric acid cycle yields 6 CO2, 10 NADH, 2 FADH2, and 4 ATP equivalents (2 ATP + 2 GTP). All 6 carbons are accounted for as CO2. The 24 electrons removed from glucose are carried by NADH and FADH2 to the electron transport chain.*

**Proof (carbon and electron bookkeeping).**

*Carbon accounting:*

- Glycolysis: C6 (glucose) -> 2 x C3 (pyruvate). No carbon lost.
- Pyruvate oxidation: 2 x C3 (pyruvate) -> 2 x C2 (acetyl-CoA) + 2 x CO2. Lost: 2 CO2.
- CAC turn 1: C2 (acetyl-CoA) + C4 (oxaloacetate) -> C6 -> ... -> C4 (oxaloacetate) + 2 CO2.
- CAC turn 2: C2 (acetyl-CoA) + C4 (oxaloacetate) -> C6 -> ... -> C4 (oxaloacetate) + 2 CO2.
- Total CO2: 2 + 2 + 2 = 6 CO2. Matches the 6 carbons in glucose.

*Electron accounting:*

Glucose: $\text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6$. Fully oxidised: $6\text{CO}_2$.

Oxidation state of C in glucose: each C is bonded to H, O, and other C. Average oxidation state: 0 (since $\text{C}_6\text{H}_{12}\text{O}_6$: $6x + 12(+1) + 6(-2) = 0$, so $6x - 0 = 0$, $x = 0$).

Oxidation state of C in CO2: +4. Change per C: +4 electrons removed. For 6 carbons: 24 electrons removed.

Each NADH carries 2 electrons. Each FADH2 carries 2 electrons.

Electrons carried: $10 \times 2 + 2 \times 2 = 24$ electrons. Matches.

*Energy accounting:*

10 NADH x ~2.5 ATP = 25 ATP
2 FADH2 x ~1.5 ATP = 3 ATP
4 substrate-level ATP/GTP
**Total: ~32 ATP per glucose** (using modern P/O ratios and the malate-aspartate shuttle). $\square$

### Worked example: energetic efficiency

The standard free energy change for complete glucose oxidation is $\Delta G^{\circ'} = -2840\,\text{kJ/mol}$. The free energy of ATP hydrolysis under cellular conditions is approximately $\Delta G_{\text{ATP}} \approx -50\,\text{kJ/mol}$.

If 32 ATP are produced: total energy captured = $32 \times 50 = 1600\,\text{kJ/mol}$.

Efficiency = $1600 / 2840 \times 100\% = 56\%$.

This is remarkably efficient compared to human-made engines (typically 25-40%). The remaining 44% is released as heat, which maintains body temperature in endothermic animals.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).**

The standard free energy change for the reaction catalysed by aldolase (fructose-1,6-bisphosphate -> DHAP + G3P) is $\Delta G^{\circ'} = +23.8\,\text{kJ/mol}$, making it thermodynamically unfavourable in isolation. Yet in the cell, the reaction proceeds forward. Explain how, using the actual intracellular concentrations: [fructose-1,6-bisP] = 0.031 mM, [DHAP] = 0.14 mM, [G3P] = 0.019 mM at $T = 310\,\text{K}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $\Delta G = \Delta G^{\circ'} + RT\ln(Q)$ where $Q = [\text{DHAP}][\text{G3P}]/[\text{F-1,6-bisP}]$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$Q = (0.14 \times 0.019) / 0.031 = 0.00266 / 0.031 = 0.0859$

$RT\ln(Q) = 8.315 \times 310 \times \ln(0.0859) = 2578 \times (-2.453) = -6324\,\text{J/mol} = -6.32\,\text{kJ/mol}$

$\Delta G = +23.8 + (-6.32) = +17.5\,\text{kJ/mol}$

The reaction is still unfavourable under these conditions. The cell drives it forward by rapid removal of G3P by the next enzyme (G3P dehydrogenase), which keeps [G3P] very low. This coupling of an unfavourable reaction to a favourable downstream step (near-equilibrium displacement by product removal) is a common theme in metabolism. The effective $\Delta G$ approaches zero (equilibrium) under actual cellular conditions because the downstream step pulls the reaction.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short answer).**

Arsenate (AsO4 3-) is a structural analogue of phosphate and can substitute for Pi in the G3P dehydrogenase reaction, producing 1-arseno-3-phosphoglycerate instead of 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate. This compound is unstable and hydrolyses spontaneously. Explain why arsenate poisoning causes glycolysis to produce zero net ATP.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The unstable arseno intermediate hydrolyses before it can participate in the substrate-level phosphorylation step. Which step produces ATP?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In normal glycolysis, step 6 (G3P dehydrogenase) produces 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate (1,3-BPG), which is then used in step 7 (phosphoglycerate kinase) to produce ATP by substrate-level phosphorylation. When arsenate replaces phosphate, the 1-arseno-3-phosphoglycerate intermediate hydrolyses spontaneously to 3-phosphoglycerate, bypassing the ATP-producing step 7.

The result: the payoff phase still produces 2 ATP from step 10 (pyruvate kinase), but the 2 ATP normally produced at step 7 are lost. Combined with the 2 ATP invested in steps 1 and 3, the net yield becomes: $2 + 2 - 2 - 2 = 0$ ATP. Glycolysis consumes as much ATP as it produces, making it energetically useless. This is why arsenate is acutely toxic: it uncouples substrate-level phosphorylation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short answer).**

During intense exercise, muscle cells consume ATP faster than oxygen can be delivered. Predict the metabolic consequence for pyruvate and explain how lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) solves the resulting problem.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

When oxygen is limiting, the electron transport chain stops. What happens to NADH/NAD+ ratio? How does the cell regenerate NAD+ for glycolysis to continue?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

When oxygen is limited, the electron transport chain cannot oxidise NADH to NAD+. NADH accumulates and NAD+ is depleted. Without NAD+, glycolysis halts at step 6 (G3P dehydrogenase requires NAD+), and the cell cannot produce ATP even by the anaerobic glycolytic pathway.

Lactate dehydrogenase solves this by reducing pyruvate to lactate, oxidising NADH to NAD+ in the process:

$$
\text{Pyruvate} + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^+ \rightarrow \text{Lactate} + \text{NAD}^+
$$

This regenerates NAD+, allowing glycolysis to continue producing ATP anaerobically (2 ATP per glucose). The lactate is exported to the blood, taken up by the liver, and reconverted to glucose via the Cori cycle (at a cost of 6 ATP per glucose in the liver). The oxygen debt incurred during intense exercise is repaid when lactate is metabolised after exercise ends.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, numeric).**

The enzyme succinate dehydrogenase (Complex II of the electron transport chain) catalyses the oxidation of succinate to fumarate, reducing FAD to FADH2. Malonate is a competitive inhibitor of succinate dehydrogenase with $K_i = 10\,\mu\text{M}$. If the $K_m$ for succinate is $500\,\mu\text{M}$ and [succinate] = 2 mM, calculate the concentration of malonate needed to inhibit the reaction by 50%.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For competitive inhibition: $v_i/v_0 = 1 / (1 + [I]/K_i \times K_m/([S] + K_m))$. At 50% inhibition, $v_i/v_0 = 0.5$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For competitive inhibition, the apparent $K_m$ is: $K_{m,\text{app}} = K_m(1 + [I]/K_i)$.

At 50% inhibition, $v_i = 0.5 v_0$:

$$
\frac{v_0}{2} = \frac{V_{\max}[S]}{K_{m,\text{app}} + [S]}
$$

And $v_0 = V_{\max}[S]/(K_m + [S])$. Setting $v_i = 0.5 v_0$:

$$
\frac{[S]}{K_m(1 + [I]/K_i) + [S]} = 0.5 \times \frac{[S]}{K_m + [S]}
$$

Simplifying: $K_m + [S] = 0.5 \times K_m(1 + [I]/K_i) + 0.5[S]$

$0.5K_m + 0.5[S] = 0.5 K_m \times [I]/K_i$

$[I] = K_i \times (K_m + [S])/K_m = 10 \times (500 + 2000)/500 = 10 \times 5 = 50\,\mu\text{M}$

A malonate concentration of 50 $\mu$M reduces succinate dehydrogenase activity by 50% under these conditions. Adding malonate to isolated mitochondria blocks the citric acid cycle at step 6, causing succinate to accumulate and preventing FADH2 production.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

Thiamine (vitamin B1) deficiency causes beriberi, characterised by neurological and cardiovascular symptoms. Explain the biochemical basis: which enzymes are affected and why the symptoms occur.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Thiamine pyrophosphate (TPP) is a cofactor for enzymes that catalyse decarboxylation of alpha-keto acids. Which steps in glycolysis and the CAC involve alpha-keto acids?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

TPP is an essential cofactor for three enzymes in glucose metabolism:

1. **Pyruvate dehydrogenase complex** (PDC, E1 subunit): requires TPP to decarboxylate pyruvate to the hydroxyethyl-TPP intermediate.
2. **Alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase** (CAC step 4): requires TPP to decarboxylate alpha-ketoglutarate to succinyl-CoA.
3. **Transketolase** (pentose phosphate pathway): requires TPP for transfer of two-carbon units.

In thiamine deficiency, PDC activity is impaired, reducing the conversion of pyruvate to acetyl-CoA. Pyruvate and lactate accumulate, causing lactic acidosis. Alpha-ketoglutarate accumulation impairs the citric acid cycle, reducing ATP production.

The neurological symptoms (peripheral neuropathy, ataxia, confusion) occur because neurons depend almost entirely on glucose oxidation for ATP and are highly sensitive to energy depletion. Cardiovascular symptoms (tachycardia, oedema, heart failure) result from impaired ATP production in cardiac muscle, which has continuous energy demands. The heart dilates and cardiac output initially increases (wet beriberi) or decreases (dry beriberi) depending on the clinical presentation.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, short answer).**

The citric acid cycle is described as both a catabolic and an anabolic pathway (amphibolic). Provide three examples of how cycle intermediates are drawn off for biosynthesis, and explain how the cell replenishes the cycle when intermediates are removed (anaplerotic reactions).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Biosynthesis uses cycle intermediates as carbon skeletons. If an intermediate is removed, the cycle cannot complete unless it is replenished. The most important anaplerotic enzyme uses pyruvate and CO2.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Three anabolic withdrawals:

1. **Oxaloacetate -> phosphoenolpyruvate** via PEP carboxykinase for gluconeogenesis (the synthesis of glucose from non-carbohydrate precursors).
2. **Alpha-ketoglutarate -> glutamate** via glutamate dehydrogenase (reductive amination), providing the amino acid for protein synthesis and as a nitrogen donor for other amino acids and nucleotides.
3. **Citrate -> acetyl-CoA** in the cytoplasm via ATP-citrate lyase, providing acetyl-CoA for fatty acid synthesis. (Citrate exported from mitochondria is cleaved to oxaloacetate + acetyl-CoA in the cytosol.)

The primary anaplerotic reaction is catalysed by **pyruvate carboxylase**:

$$
\text{Pyruvate} + \text{CO}_2 + \text{ATP} \rightarrow \text{Oxaloacetate} + \text{ADP} + \text{P}_i
$$

This reaction replenishes oxaloacetate when it is drawn off for biosynthesis. Pyruvate carboxylase is activated by acetyl-CoA (a signal that the citric acid cycle is running low on oxaloacetate relative to acetyl-CoA supply). Other anaplerotic reactions include: glutamate -> alpha-ketoglutarate (via transamination or glutamate dehydrogenase), and malic enzyme (pyruvate + CO2 + NADPH -> malate + NADP+).

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 9 (hard, short answer).**

Fluoroacetate is a potent poison found in some toxic plants (e.g., *Dichapetalum cymosum*). After ingestion, fluoroacetate is converted to fluoroacetyl-CoA, which enters the citric acid cycle to form fluorocitrate. Fluorocitrate is not a substrate for aconitase but instead binds to it tightly, inhibiting the enzyme. This is called "lethal synthesis." Explain the metabolic consequences of aconitase inhibition and why this mechanism is more effective than direct enzyme inhibition.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Fluoroacetate itself is not toxic; it must first be metabolised into the actual poison. Why is this conversion advantageous for the toxic plant?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Aconitase inhibition blocks the conversion of citrate to isocitrate (step 2). Citrate accumulates, and the rest of the cycle is starved of substrates. No NADH, FADH2, or GTP is produced from steps 3-8. The cell cannot generate ATP through oxidative phosphorylation, leading to energy depletion and death.

The "lethal synthesis" mechanism is particularly insidious because fluoroacetate itself is not recognised as a toxin by the cell. It is a structural analogue of acetate and is metabolised by the same enzymes (acetyl-CoA synthetase) that process normal acetate. The cell itself synthesises the actual poison (fluorocitrate) from the harmless precursor. This makes fluoroacetate difficult for cells to detect and exclude. Herbivores that eat the plant cannot selectively avoid metabolising fluoroacetate because it enters the same metabolic pathways as a normal nutrient.

</details>

## Advanced topics: thermodynamic driving forces and metabolic control analysis [Master]

The citric acid cycle operates far from equilibrium. Three reactions have large negative $\Delta G$ values that make them essentially irreversible: citrate synthase ($\Delta G \approx -32\,\text{kJ/mol}$), isocitrate dehydrogenase ($\Delta G \approx -21\,\text{kJ/mol}$), and alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase ($\Delta G \approx -34\,\text{kJ/mol}$). These three steps determine the direction of flux through the cycle and are the primary regulatory points.

**Metabolic control analysis (MCA)** provides a quantitative framework for understanding how control is distributed among enzymes in a pathway. The flux control coefficient $C_J^{E_i}$ quantifies the fractional change in pathway flux $J$ resulting from a fractional change in enzyme $E_i$ activity:

$$
C_J^{E_i} = \frac{dJ/J}{dE_i/E_i} = \frac{d\ln J}{d\ln E_i}
$$

The summation theorem states that the flux control coefficients of all enzymes in a pathway sum to 1:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{n} C_J^{E_i} = 1
$$

For glycolysis, experimental measurements show that PFK-1 has the highest flux control coefficient ($C_J \approx 0.3$-$0.5$ depending on conditions), hexokinase has $C_J \approx 0.1$-$0.2$, and pyruvate kinase has $C_J \approx 0.1$-$0.3$. The remaining control is distributed among the near-equilibrium enzymes, each contributing a small fraction. This result contradicts the intuitive notion of a single "rate-limiting step"; control is shared among multiple enzymes.

The **crossover theorem** (Chance and Williams, 1955) predicts that when an inhibitor acts on a specific step in a pathway, metabolites before the block accumulate and metabolites after the block are depleted. This theorem provides a diagnostic tool: by measuring the concentration profile of all intermediates, the site of inhibition can be identified. For example, fluorocitrate inhibition of aconitase causes citrate accumulation and depletion of all downstream intermediates (isocitrate, alpha-KG, succinyl-CoA, succinate, fumarate, malate, oxaloacetate).

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Glycolysis was the first metabolic pathway to be fully elucidated. The key work was done by Gustav Embden, Otto Meyerhof, and Jakub Karol Parnas in the 1930s, building on earlier observations by Eduard Buchner (who showed that cell-free yeast extracts could ferment sugar, disproving the vitalist claim that fermentation required living cells) and by Harden and Young (who discovered the role of phosphate and coenzymes in fermentation).

The citric acid cycle was proposed by Hans Krebs in 1937, based on experiments using pigeon breast muscle mince. Krebs observed that adding citrate, isocitrate, alpha-ketoglutarate, succinate, fumarate, malate, or oxaloacetate catalytically increased oxygen consumption, while other organic acids did not. This suggested a cyclic pathway. The key insight was that a two-carbon unit (later identified as acetyl-CoA) combined with a four-carbon acceptor (oxaloacetate) to form a six-carbon intermediate (citrate), which was then sequentially degraded back to the four-carbon acceptor.

The identification of acetyl-CoA as the two-carbon donor was made by Fritz Lipmann in 1945 (Nobel Prize 1953, shared with Krebs). The role of CoA (coenzyme A) as an acyl-group carrier was a major advance in understanding the chemistry of acyl-transfer reactions.

The pyruvate dehydrogenase complex, characterised by Lester Reed in the 1950s-60s, revealed a remarkable piece of molecular architecture: the three enzymes are physically associated in a large multienzyme complex, with the lipoyl swinging arm of E2 shuttling reaction intermediates between active sites without releasing them to solution. This substrate channelling increases catalytic efficiency by reducing transit times and protecting reactive intermediates.
