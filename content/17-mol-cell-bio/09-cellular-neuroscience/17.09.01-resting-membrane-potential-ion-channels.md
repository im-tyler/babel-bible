## Intuition [Beginner]

Every cell has an electrical charge difference across its membrane — the **membrane potential**. The inside of the cell is negative relative to the outside. In neurons, this resting potential is about **-70 millivolts (mV)**.

This voltage exists because of three things. First, ion concentrations are unequal inside and out. Potassium (K+) is high inside; sodium (Na+) is high outside. Second, the membrane is selectively permeable — at rest, it lets K+ pass much more than Na+. Third, the **sodium-potassium pump** constantly exports 3 Na+ and imports 2 K+, using ATP, to maintain the concentration gradients.

Because the membrane at rest is mostly permeable to K+, potassium leaks out through **potassium leak channels**, driven by its concentration gradient. Each K+ that leaves carries one positive charge, making the inside more negative. This builds up an electrical force that pulls K+ back in. At the **Nernst equilibrium**, the concentration gradient pushing K+ out exactly balances the electrical gradient pulling it in. For K+, this equilibrium is about -89 mV.

The actual resting potential (-70 mV) is less negative because the membrane is not perfectly selective — some Na+ leaks in, partially offsetting the K+ outward current. The **Goldman equation** accounts for all permeable ions.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the cell membrane as a wall. On the outside: high Na+ (145 mM), low K+ (5 mM). On the inside: low Na+ (12 mM), high K+ (140 mM). The wall has small doors (ion channels). At rest, the K+ doors are open (leak channels), the Na+ doors are mostly closed.

K+ ions stream out through the open doors, carrying positive charge. The inside becomes negative. The negativity pulls K+ back. When outward chemical push equals inward electrical pull, equilibrium is reached.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Calculate the Nernst potential for K+ at body temperature (37 C = 310 K):

$E_K = \frac{RT}{zF} \ln \frac{[\text{K}^+]_{\text{out}}}{[\text{K}^+]_{\text{in}}}$

With $R = 8.314$ J/(mol K), $T = 310$ K, $z = +1$ for K+, $F = 96,485$ C/mol:

$E_K = \frac{8.314 \times 310}{1 \times 96485} \ln \frac{5}{140} = 0.0267 \times \ln(0.0357)$

$= 0.0267 \times (-3.33) = -0.089$ V = **-89 mV**.

At -89 mV, the electrical force pulling K+ in exactly balances the concentration force pushing K+ out. The resting potential is -70 mV (less negative), meaning the membrane is not at K+ equilibrium — there is a small net K+ efflux at rest, balanced by the Na+/K+ pump.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The resting membrane potential ($V_m$) is the electrical potential difference across the plasma membrane of a cell at steady state, resulting from the unequal distribution of ions and the selective permeability of the membrane.

### The Nernst equation

For a single ion species X with charge z at electrochemical equilibrium:

$$E_X = \frac{RT}{zF} \ln \frac{[X]_{\text{out}}}{[X]_{\text{in}}}$$

At 37 C, $RT/F \approx 26.7$ mV. For monovalent ions (z = 1), converting to log base 10:

$$E_X = 61.5 \log_{10} \frac{[X]_{\text{out}}}{[X]_{\text{in}}} \text{ mV}$$

Typical equilibrium potentials (mammalian neuron, 37 C):

| Ion | [out] (mM) | [in] (mM) | $E_X$ (mV) |
|-----|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| K+  | 5         | 140       | -89       |
| Na+ | 145       | 12        | +67       |
| Cl- | 110       | 4         | -89       |
| Ca2+| 2         | 0.0001    | +126      |

### The Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz (GHK) equation

When the membrane is permeable to multiple ions, the resting potential is a weighted average of their equilibrium potentials:

$$V_m = \frac{RT}{F} \ln \frac{P_K[K^+]_{\text{out}} + P_{Na}[Na^+]_{\text{out}} + P_{Cl}[Cl^-]_{\text{in}}}{P_K[K^+]_{\text{in}} + P_{Na}[Na^+]_{\text{in}} + P_{Cl}[Cl^-]_{\text{out}}}$$

where $P_K$, $P_{Na}$, $P_{Cl}$ are the membrane permeability coefficients for each ion. At rest, $P_K \gg P_{Na} \gg P_{Cl}$, with the ratio $P_K : P_{Na} : P_{Cl} \approx 100 : 1 : 10$ in a typical neuron. This is why $V_m$ is close to $E_K$.

For the typical resting neuron: $V_m \approx -70$ mV, between $E_K = -89$ mV and $E_{Na} = +67$ mV, pulled toward $E_K$ by the high K+ permeability.

### Ion channels

Ion channels are transmembrane proteins that form aqueous pores through which specific ions pass. They are classified by:

**Gating mechanism:**
- **Leak channels:** Constitutively open (or weakly voltage-sensitive). K+ leak channels (KCNK/TASK/TREK families) set the resting potential.
- **Voltage-gated channels:** Open or close in response to changes in membrane potential. Key families: NaV (voltage-gated Na+), KV (voltage-gated K+), CaV (voltage-gated Ca2+). Contain voltage-sensor domains (S4 helix with positively charged arginine residues that move in response to voltage).
- **Ligand-gated channels:** Open when a specific molecule binds. Nicotinic acetylcholine receptor (nAChR), GABA-A receptor, glutamate receptors (AMPA, NMDA).
- **Mechanically-gated channels:** Respond to membrane stretch or tension.

**Selectivity:**
- K+ channels are ~10,000-fold selective for K+ over Na+. The selectivity filter contains a signature sequence (TVGYG) with backbone carbonyl oxygens that coordinate dehydrated K+ ions. Na+ is too small to interact optimally with these coordination sites.
- Na+ channels select for Na+ over K+ using a different mechanism involving a narrow selectivity filter with conserved aspartate, glutamate, lysine, and alanine (DEKA motif).
- Ca2+ channels use a ring of glutamate residues (EEEE motif) that preferentially binds Ca2+ over Na+ by its higher charge density.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**The resting membrane potential is determined by the weighted average of the equilibrium potentials of all permeable ions, with weights proportional to their permeabilities (Goldman equation).**

*Derivation sketch.* Under the constant-field assumption (the electric field is uniform across the membrane of thickness $\delta$), the Nernst-Planck flux equation for each ion $j$ gives a current:

$$I_j = P_j z_j^2 \frac{F^2 V_m}{RT} \frac{[j]_{\text{out}} - [j]_{\text{in}} e^{-z_j FV_m/RT}}{1 - e^{-z_j FV_m/RT}}$$

At steady state, the net current is zero: $I_K + I_{Na} + I_{Cl} = 0$. Solving for $V_m$ yields the GHK equation above.

### Worked example: Nernst potential for K+

At 37 C with $[K^+]_{\text{out}} = 5$ mM and $[K^+]_{\text{in}} = 140$ mM:

$$E_K = \frac{8.314 \times 310}{1 \times 96485} \ln \frac{5}{140} = 0.0267 \times (-3.33) = -0.089 \text{ V} = -89 \text{ mV}$$

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

If extracellular K+ increases from 5 mM to 10 mM (hyperkalemia), calculate the new K+ equilibrium potential. Does the resting potential become more negative or less negative?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Recompute $E_K$ with $[K^+]_{\text{out}} = 10$ mM instead of 5 mM.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$E_K = 61.5 \times \log_{10}(10/140) = 61.5 \times (-1.146) = -70.5$ mV.

The new $E_K$ is **-70.5 mV** (compared to -89 mV normally). The resting potential becomes **less negative** (depolarized), moving from ~-70 mV toward -60 to -65 mV. Hyperkalemia is dangerous because it depolarizes cells, potentially triggering arrhythmias in the heart (partially depolarized cells have inactivated Na+ channels and become inexcitable).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Explain why the Na+/K+ pump is *electrogenic*. How much does it contribute to the resting potential?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The pump moves 3 Na+ out for every 2 K+ in. That is not charge-balanced.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Na+/K+ pump exports 3 positive charges and imports 2 positive charges per cycle, creating a net outward current of one positive charge per cycle. This directly contributes to the inside-negative potential. The electrogenic contribution is approximately **-4 to -5 mV** of the total resting potential of -70 mV. The remaining ~-65 mV comes from the passive diffusion of K+ through leak channels (the K+ equilibrium potential, weighted by permeability).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Ouabain is a drug that inhibits the Na+/K+ pump. Predict what happens to the resting membrane potential over time after ouabain is applied. Consider both the immediate and long-term effects.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The pump maintains concentration gradients. Without it, what happens to those gradients?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Immediate effect:** Very little change in membrane potential. The resting potential is primarily set by K+ leak channels and the K+ gradient, and the gradient changes slowly. The -4 mV electrogenic contribution of the pump is lost, causing a slight depolarization (~4 mV).

**Long-term effect (minutes to hours):** Without the pump, the Na+ and K+ gradients gradually run down. Na+ leaks in (driven by both concentration and electrical gradients), and K+ leaks out. Intracellular K+ decreases, extracellular K+ increases, and $E_K$ becomes less negative. The resting potential slowly depolarizes toward 0 mV as the gradients collapse. Eventually, the cell cannot maintain its resting potential, ion gradients, or volume regulation, leading to cell death.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Use the GHK equation to calculate the resting potential of a neuron with $P_K : P_{Na} : P_{Cl} = 100 : 1 : 10$. Ion concentrations: $[K^+]_{\text{out}} = 5$, $[K^+]_{\text{in}} = 140$; $[Na^+]_{\text{out}} = 145$, $[Na^+]_{\text{in}} = 12$; $[Cl^-]_{\text{out}} = 110$, $[Cl^-]_{\text{in}} = 4$ (all mM). At 37 C.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute into the GHK equation. Note that Cl- is inverted (inside in numerator, outside in denominator) because it is negatively charged.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$$V_m = 61.5 \times \log_{10} \frac{100(5) + 1(145) + 10(4)}{100(140) + 1(12) + 10(110)}$$

Numerator: $500 + 145 + 40 = 685$.

Denominator: $14000 + 12 + 1100 = 15112$.

$V_m = 61.5 \times \log_{10}(685/15112) = 61.5 \times \log_{10}(0.0453) = 61.5 \times (-1.344) = -82.7$ mV.

The calculated resting potential is approximately **-83 mV**, close to but less negative than $E_K$ (-89 mV), reflecting the small Na+ and Cl- contributions.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

K+ channels are 10,000-fold selective for K+ over Na+, despite Na+ being *smaller* than K+. Explain the structural basis for this selectivity (the "snug-fit" mechanism of the K+ channel selectivity filter).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Both ions must shed their hydration shell to pass through. What coordinates the ion in the filter?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

K+ and Na+ both travel through ion channels as dehydrated ions — they must shed their water shell to enter the narrow selectivity filter. In K+ channels, the selectivity filter contains four binding sites, each formed by a ring of backbone carbonyl oxygen atoms from the signature TVGYG sequence. These oxygens are positioned at exactly the right distance (~2.8 A) to coordinate a dehydrated K+ ion (radius 1.33 A) with optimal geometry, replacing its water shell.

Na+ (radius 0.95 A) is too small to contact all the carbonyl oxygens simultaneously. The "snug fit" that makes K+ coordination energetically favorable does not work for Na+: Na+ would rattle loosely in the K+-sized binding site, with fewer favorable interactions than it had with water. The energetic cost of dehydration exceeds the energetic gain from the partial coordination, so Na+ does not enter the filter.

This is an elegant example of *size selectivity*: the channel is built to fit K+ precisely, and the energetic penalty for Na+ to enter the wrong-sized cavity prevents it from permeating. Roderick MacKinnon determined the K+ channel structure in 1998 (Nobel Prize 2003).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Tetrodotoxin (TTX, from puffer fish) blocks voltage-gated Na+ channels with very high affinity (Kd ~ 1-10 nM). Explain why TTX causes paralysis and why puffer fish are immune to their own toxin.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Na+ channels are essential for action potentials. What happens if they are blocked? And why do puffer fish neurons still fire?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Paralysis mechanism:** Voltage-gated Na+ channels carry the inward Na+ current that drives the depolarizing upstroke of the action potential. TTX binds to the outer mouth of the Na+ channel pore with high affinity, physically occluding it. Without functional Na+ channels, neurons cannot fire action potentials. Motor neurons cannot signal muscles, causing flaccid paralysis. Sensory neurons cannot transmit pain or touch. Diaphragm paralysis leads to respiratory failure — the usual cause of death from TTX poisoning.

**Puffer fish immunity:** Puffer fish have Na+ channels with specific amino acid substitutions in the TTX-binding site that reduce TTX affinity by ~1,000-fold. A single mutation (e.g., a phenylalanine to cysteine change in the pore loop of domain I) is sufficient to confer TTX resistance while preserving normal Na+ channel function. This is an example of *evolutionary arms race* dynamics: garter snakes that eat TTX-bearing newts have also evolved TTX-resistant Na+ channels through convergent amino acid substitutions.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the Nernst equation from thermodynamic first principles. Start from the condition of electrochemical equilibrium and show that the electrical work of moving an ion across the membrane must equal the chemical work against the concentration gradient.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

At equilibrium, the electrochemical potential $\mu = \mu^0 + RT\ln[X] + zFV$ is equal on both sides of the membrane.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The electrochemical potential of ion X on each side of the membrane is:

Inside: $\mu_{\text{in}} = \mu^0 + RT\ln[X]_{\text{in}} + zFV_{\text{in}}$

Outside: $\mu_{\text{out}} = \mu^0 + RT\ln[X]_{\text{out}} + zFV_{\text{out}}$

At equilibrium, $\mu_{\text{in}} = \mu_{\text{out}}$:

$$RT\ln[X]_{\text{in}} + zFV_{\text{in}} = RT\ln[X]_{\text{out}} + zFV_{\text{out}}$$

$$zF(V_{\text{in}} - V_{\text{out}}) = RT\ln\frac{[X]_{\text{out}}}{[X]_{\text{in}}}$$

$$V_m = V_{\text{in}} - V_{\text{out}} = \frac{RT}{zF}\ln\frac{[X]_{\text{out}}}{[X]_{\text{in}}}$$

This is the Nernst equation. The physical meaning: at equilibrium, the electrical energy cost of moving a charge across the potential ($zFV_m$) exactly equals the chemical free energy gained by moving down the concentration gradient ($RT\ln([X]_{\text{out}}/[X]_{\text{in}})$).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

A cell has a Cl- equilibrium potential of -89 mV and a resting potential of -70 mV. Is Cl- at equilibrium? If not, in which direction is Cl- driven, and what mechanism maintains the Cl- gradient?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$E_{Cl} = -89$ mV, $V_m = -70$ mV. The driving force is $V_m - E_{Cl}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Cl- is **not at equilibrium**. The driving force on Cl- is: $V_m - E_{Cl} = -70 - (-89) = +19$ mV.

For a *negative* ion, a positive driving force means Cl- is driven **inward** (toward the more positive electrical side, which is outside — but wait, the convention needs care). The electrochemical gradient on Cl- at $V_m = -70$ mV is: the concentration gradient pushes Cl- out (high outside), but the electrical gradient (inside negative) pulls Cl- in. At $E_{Cl} = -89$ mV, these balance. Since $V_m = -70$ mV is *above* $E_{Cl}$ (less negative), the membrane is not negative enough to hold Cl- inside — Cl- has a net tendency to **enter** the cell.

In most neurons, Cl- is kept near equilibrium by **KCC2** (a K+-Cl- cotransporter that extrudes Cl-) and **NKCC1** (a Na+-K+-2Cl- cotransporter that imports Cl-). In mature neurons, KCC2 dominates, keeping $[Cl^-]_{\text{in}}$ low and $E_{Cl}$ close to $V_m$. In immature neurons, NKCC1 dominates, raising $[Cl^-]_{\text{in}}$ and making $E_{Cl}$ more positive than $V_m$, which means GABA-A receptor activation (Cl- influx) is *depolarizing* rather than hyperpolarizing in developing neurons.

</details>

</aside>

## Connections [Master]

- **Membrane structure [17.02.02]** sets the biophysical context: the lipid bilayer is impermeable to ions, which is why ion channels and pumps are necessary for any ion movement.

- **Thermodynamics [14.11.01]** provides the Nernst equation and the GHK equation through electrochemical equilibrium and the constant-field approximation.

- **Action potential [17.09.02]** builds directly on the resting potential: voltage-gated Na+ and K+ channels transiently change the permeability ratios, causing the membrane potential to swing from resting (-70 mV) toward $E_{Na}$ (+67 mV) and back.

- **Cell signaling [17.07.01]** uses ligand-gated ion channels as receptors. The nicotinic acetylcholine receptor and GABA-A receptor are both ion channels opened by neurotransmitter binding.

- **Oxidative phosphorylation [17.04.02]** also depends on membrane potential (the proton motive force), though across the mitochondrial inner membrane rather than the plasma membrane.

- **Cytoskeleton and contractile proteins [17.03.02].** Axonal transport of vesicles containing ion channels and neurotransmitters depends on microtubule motor proteins (kinesin and dynein). The distal localisation of voltage-gated sodium channels at the axon initial segment requires ankyrin-G-mediated tethering to the actin-microtubule cytoskeleton, directly linking cytoskeletal organisation to neuronal excitability.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The existence of the resting potential was first demonstrated by du Bois-Reymond in 1848 using galvanometers. The Nernst equation was derived by Walther Nernst in 1888 from thermodynamic principles, and David Goldman derived the constant-field equation in 1943. Hodgkin and Katz (1949) applied the Goldman equation to the squid giant axon, showing that the resting potential is primarily a K+ diffusion potential with a small Na+ contribution.

The ion channel concept replaced the "pore" and "carrier" models of the 1950s-60s. Patch-clamp recording by Neher and Sakmann (1976, Nobel Prize 1991) allowed measurement of currents through single ion channels, revealing that channels open and close in discrete stochastic events with conductances of 1-300 pS. The crystal structure of the KcsA K+ channel by MacKinnon (1998, Nobel Prize 2003) revealed the atomic basis of K+ selectivity.
