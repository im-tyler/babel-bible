## Intuition [Beginner]

DNA is remarkably stable, but it is not invincible. Every day, every cell in your body suffers tens of thousands of DNA lesions — breaks, chemical modifications, mispaired bases. Most are fixed. When they are not, the result is a **mutation**: a permanent change in the DNA sequence.

Mutations come in several types. A **point mutation** changes a single base pair. If it changes the amino acid encoded, it is a **missense** mutation (like sickle-cell: Glu to Val). If it creates a premature stop codon, it is a **nonsense** mutation. If it changes a base but does not change the amino acid (due to genetic code redundancy), it is a **silent** mutation. If bases are inserted or deleted (not in multiples of three), the reading frame shifts — a **frameshift** — garbling everything downstream.

Mutations can arise from replication errors, from chemical damage (UV light, cigarette smoke, reactive oxygen species), or from mobile genetic elements that jump around the genome.

Cells have multiple repair systems. Some correct mismatched bases right after replication. Others detect and remove damaged bases. Some patch broken DNA strands. When repair fails, mutations accumulate. A few mutations in the right (or wrong) genes can lead to cancer.

## Visual [Beginner]

Think of DNA as a long text. A point mutation is a typo — one wrong letter. A missense mutation changes a word's meaning (the $\to$ tea). A nonsense mutation ends the sentence prematurely (the big cat $\to$ the big [stop]). A frameshift shifts all the reading boundaries (t|he|big|cat $\to$ th|eb|ig|ca|t...).

## Worked example [Beginner]

The sickle-cell mutation is a single base change in the HBB gene (beta-globin). The normal codon at position 6 is GAG (glutamic acid). The mutation changes it to GTG (valine):

Normal DNA: ...GAG... $\to$ mRNA: ...GAG... $\to$ Amino acid: Glu
Mutant DNA: ...GTG... $\to$ mRNA: ...GUG... $\to$ Amino acid: Val

This is an A$\to$T transversion (a purine is replaced by a pyrimidine). A single atom-level change — replacing a negatively charged, hydrophilic side chain with a non-polar, hydrophobic one — creates a sticky patch on the hemoglobin surface that causes polymerization, distorting red blood cells into sickle shapes. The consequences: pain crises, organ damage, anemia. Yet the mutation persists because carriers (heterozygotes) have resistance to malaria.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A mutation is any heritable change in the nucleotide sequence of a genome. DNA repair encompasses the enzymatic pathways that detect and correct DNA damage and replication errors.

### Types of mutations

**By scale:**
- **Point mutations:** Single base-pair substitutions. Classified as transitions (purine$\leftrightarrow$purine: A$\leftrightarrow$G; pyrimidine$\leftrightarrow$pyrimidine: C$\leftrightarrow$T) or transversions (purine$\leftrightarrow$pyrimidine).
- **Insertions/deletions (indels):** Addition or removal of one or more base pairs.
- **Copy number variations (CNVs):** Duplications or deletions of larger chromosomal segments.
- **Chromosomal rearrangements:** Translocations, inversions, fusions.

**By effect on the protein:**
- **Silent (synonymous):** Codon changes but amino acid does not (due to code degeneracy).
- **Missense:** One amino acid replaced by another. Conservative (similar properties) or non-conservative (different properties).
- **Nonsense:** Codon becomes a stop codon, truncating the protein.
- **Readthrough:** Stop codon mutated to a sense codon, extending the protein.
- **Frameshift:** Indel not divisible by 3 shifts the reading frame, altering all downstream amino acids.
- **Splice-site mutations:** Disrupt intron-exon boundaries, causing aberrant splicing.

### DNA damage types

**Spontaneous damage** (~10,000-100,000 lesions per cell per day):
- **Depurination:** Loss of purine bases (A or G), leaving an apurinic (AP) site. ~10,000 per cell per day.
- **Deamination:** Loss of an amino group from a base. Cytosine deaminates to uracil (~100-500 per day). 5-methylcytosine deaminates to thymine (irreparable by normal BER — this is a major source of C$\to$T transitions at CpG dinucleotides).
- **Oxidation:** Reactive oxygen species (ROS) modify bases. 8-oxo-guanine (8-oxoG) is the most common oxidative lesion; it pairs with A instead of C, causing G$\to$T transversions.

**Induced damage:**
- **UV radiation:** Causes cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers (CPDs) and 6-4 photoproducts between adjacent pyrimidines. These distort the helix and block replication.
- **Ionizing radiation:** Causes single-strand breaks (SSBs), double-strand breaks (DSBs), and base modifications.
- **Chemical mutagens:** Alkylating agents (add methyl/ethyl groups to bases), deaminating agents (nitrous acid), base analogs (5-bromouracil), intercalating agents (ethidium bromide, causing frameshifts).

### Repair mechanisms

**1. Mismatch repair (MMR).** Corrects base-pairing errors (mismatches, small insertions/deletions) that escape DNA polymerase proofreading. In E. coli: MutS recognizes the mismatch, MutL recruits MutH, which nicks the newly synthesized strand at a hemimethylated GATC site. The nicked strand is excised (by exonucleases) past the mismatch and resynthesized. In eukaryotes: MSH2-MSH6 (MutS homolog) recognizes mismatches; MLH1-PMS2 (MutL homolog) recruits the excision machinery. Strand discrimination in eukaryotes is not fully understood but may involve nicks in the lagging strand. MMR improves fidelity ~100-fold, from ~$10^{-7}$ to ~$10^{-9}$ errors per base. Loss of MMR (e.g., MLH1 or MSH2 mutations) causes Lynch syndrome (hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer).

**2. Base excision repair (BER).** Repairs small base modifications (deamination, oxidation, alkylation). A DNA glycosylase recognizes and removes the damaged base, creating an AP site. AP endonuclease (APE1) nicks the backbone. DNA polymerase beta fills the gap, and DNA ligase seals it. Different glycosylases target different lesions: UNG (uracil), OGG1 (8-oxoG), MBD4 (T from 5-methyl-C deamination).

**3. Nucleotide excision repair (NER).** Repairs bulky lesions that distort the helix (UV dimers, bulky chemical adducts). Two sub-pathways: global genome NER (GG-NER, scans entire genome; uses XPC for damage recognition) and transcription-coupled NER (TC-NER, repairs lesions in actively transcribed strands; uses CSB/CSA). In both, TFIIH unwinds the DNA, XPA verifies the lesion, and endonucleases XPF-ERCC1 (5' cut) and XPG (3' cut) excise a 24-32 nt oligonucleotide. DNA polymerase fills the gap and ligase seals it. Defects in NER cause xeroderma pigmentosum (extreme UV sensitivity and skin cancer).

**4. Double-strand break repair.** Two pathways:
- **Non-homologous end joining (NHEJ):** Directly ligates broken ends. Fast but error-prone (can lose or add nucleotides). Uses Ku70/80 (binds ends), DNA-PKcs, Artemis (processes ends), XRCC4-Ligase IV. Active throughout the cell cycle.
- **Homologous recombination (HR):** Uses the sister chromatid as a template for accurate repair. The 5' ends are resected to create 3' overhangs. Rad51 (with BRCA2 loading) invades the homologous template, DNA synthesis copies the missing information, and the junctions are resolved. Accurate but restricted to S/G2 phase (when sister chromatids are available). BRCA1/BRCA2 mutations impair HR and cause hereditary breast/ovarian cancer.

**5. Direct reversal.** A few lesions are repaired directly without excision:
- Photolyase (in bacteria, plants, and many animals but NOT placental mammals) uses blue light to split UV dimers.
- O6-methylguanine methyltransferase (MGMT) transfers a methyl group from O6-methylG to itself (suicide enzyme).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**The mutation rate sets the boundary between evolutionary change and genomic stasis.**

The per-base mutation rate in humans is approximately $1.2 \times 10^{-8}$ per base per generation. For a diploid genome of $6.4 \times 10^9$ bp, this gives:

$$6.4 \times 10^9 \times 1.2 \times 10^{-8} \approx 77 \text{ de novo mutations per genome per generation.}$$

Of these, approximately 1-2 affect coding sequences, and perhaps 0.01-0.1 are deleterious. This rate is low enough that most offspring are functional but high enough to generate the variation needed for evolution.

### Worked example: the sickle-cell mutation

The HBB gene's sixth codon is GAG (Glu) in the normal allele (HbA) and GTG (Val) in the sickle-cell allele (HbS).

The DNA change on the coding strand: A $\to$ T at the second position of the codon. This is a **transversion** (purine to pyrimidine).

Why does this single change cause disease? Glutamic acid at position 6 sits on the surface of the hemoglobin molecule, exposed to solvent. Its negative charge (from the carboxylate group) repels other hemoglobin molecules. Replacing it with valine removes the charge and exposes a hydrophobic side chain. Under deoxygenated conditions, this hydrophobic patch fits into a complementary pocket on a neighboring deoxyhemoglobin molecule. The interaction is cooperative: once a few molecules link up, the polymer grows rapidly, forming long fibers that distort the red blood cell.

The maintenance of the HbS allele at high frequency in malaria-endemic regions (~10-20% in parts of Africa) is explained by **balancing selection**: heterozygotes (HbA/HbS, "sickle-cell trait") have partial resistance to *P. falciparum* malaria, because the infected red blood cells sickle under low oxygen and are cleared by the spleen. The fitness advantage in malarial environments outweighs the severe cost of homozygosity (HbS/HbS, sickle-cell disease).

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

The normal codon for tryptophan is UGG. A mutation changes it to UGA. What type of mutation is this, and what is the effect?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

UGA is one of the three stop codons.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

This is a **nonsense mutation**. UGG (Trp) changes to UGA (Stop). Translation terminates prematurely at this position. Since Trp is encoded by only one codon (UGG), any substitution at this codon will change the amino acid or create a stop codon — Trp has zero degeneracy, making it vulnerable to mutations.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Name the four major DNA repair pathways and the type of damage each primarily addresses.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

One for mismatches, one for small base modifications, one for bulky lesions, one for double-strand breaks.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

1. **Mismatch repair (MMR):** Replication errors — base mismatches and small indels.
2. **Base excision repair (BER):** Small chemical modifications — deaminated, oxidized, or alkylated bases.
3. **Nucleotide excision repair (NER):** Bulky helix-distorting lesions — UV dimers, chemical adducts.
4. **Double-strand break repair (NHEJ or HR):** Both-strand breaks from ionizing radiation or replication fork collapse.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

CpG dinucleotides are mutation hotspots in the human genome. Explain why, tracing the molecular mechanism from 5-methylcytosine to a permanent C$\to$T transition.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

5-methylcytosine deaminates. What does it become? Can the cell distinguish this from a normal base?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In vertebrate genomes, cytosines in CpG dinucleotides are often methylated at the 5 position (5-methylcytosine, 5mC). Spontaneous deamination of 5mC removes the amino group and produces **thymine** (T). Unlike deamination of unmethylated C (which produces uracil, recognized as foreign in DNA and removed by BER), deamination of 5mC produces **thymine**, a normal DNA base. The resulting G-T mismatch is difficult for repair systems to resolve because both bases are "legitimate." If replication occurs before repair, the T directs incorporation of A, and the original C-G pair becomes a T-A pair — a permanent C$\to$T transition. This mechanism accounts for ~30% of all point mutations in the human genome and explains why CpG dinucleotides are severely underrepresented (mutated away) in vertebrate genomes.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

BRCA1 and BRCA2 mutations dramatically increase breast and ovarian cancer risk. Explain the connection between these genes' function in DNA repair and cancer susceptibility.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

BRCA1 and BRCA2 are involved in homologous recombination. What happens when this repair pathway is broken?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

BRCA1 helps initiate homologous recombination (HR) by promoting DNA end resection at double-strand breaks. BRCA2 loads the Rad51 recombinase onto single-stranded DNA at the break site. Without functional BRCA1 or BRCA2, cells cannot perform accurate HR repair.

Instead, DSBs are repaired by the error-prone NHEJ pathway, which introduces small insertions and deletions. Over time, this genomic instability leads to accumulation of mutations, chromosomal rearrangements, and eventually the activation of oncogenes or loss of tumor suppressors.

Individuals with one defective copy of BRCA1 or BRCA2 are heterozygous in all cells. When the second copy is somatically lost (loss of heterozygosity) in breast or ovarian epithelial cells, the resulting cells have no functional HR. These cells accumulate mutations at an accelerated rate, leading to cancer. The lifetime risk is ~60-85% for breast cancer and ~15-40% for ovarian cancer in BRCA1 carriers.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, numeric).**

Lindahl (1993) estimated that a mammalian cell experiences ~10,000 depurination events, ~3,000 single-strand breaks, ~1,000 oxidative lesions, and ~500 cytosine deaminations per day. If repair efficiency is 99.9%, calculate how many lesions per day become permanent mutations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

0.1% of total lesions escape repair.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Total lesions per day: $10,000 + 3,000 + 1,000 + 500 = 14,500$.

At 99.9% repair efficiency, 0.1% escape: $14,500 \times 0.001 = 14.5$ lesions become permanent per cell per day.

Over a 70-year human lifespan (~25,500 days): $14.5 \times 25,500 \approx 370,000$ mutations per cell. This is why cancer is primarily a disease of aging — the cumulative mutation burden eventually hits the wrong genes. Tissues with high turnover (epithelial lining of gut, skin, lung) have the highest cancer rates because each cell division is an opportunity for replication errors to add to the damage-derived mutations.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Xeroderma pigmentosum (XP) is caused by defects in nucleotide excision repair. Patients have ~2,000-fold increased risk of skin cancer. Explain why NER deficiency specifically predisposes to UV-induced skin cancer rather than, say, lung or colon cancer.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What type of DNA damage does NER repair? What is the primary source of that damage? Which tissues are most exposed?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

NER specifically repairs bulky, helix-distorting lesions — primarily **cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers (CPDs) and 6-4 photoproducts** caused by UV-B radiation. Skin is the organ most exposed to UV radiation (sunlight). In XP patients, UV-induced DNA lesions in skin cells are not repaired, leading to a very high mutation rate in sun-exposed skin. The mutations accumulate in oncogenes and tumor suppressors (especially in keratinocytes and melanocytes), causing basal cell carcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and melanoma.

Internal organs (lung, colon) are not directly exposed to UV, so the NER defect has a much smaller effect there. Lung and colon cancers in XP patients occur at roughly normal rates. The tissue-specific cancer risk pattern directly reflects the tissue-specific exposure to the relevant mutagen (UV light), confirming the role of NER in protecting against UV damage specifically.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The Ames test uses Salmonella bacteria that cannot synthesize histidine (his- mutants) to screen for mutagens. A compound is applied to his- bacteria on plates lacking histidine. If colonies grow, the compound caused a reversion mutation restoring his+ function. Explain: (a) Why does colony growth indicate mutagenicity? (b) Why is a liver extract (S9 fraction) included in the test? (c) What are the limitations of this test?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

his- bacteria cannot grow without histidine. Only a mutation back to his+ allows growth. Liver enzymes metabolize many compounds into active forms.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) his- bacteria have a mutation in a histidine biosynthesis gene. On medium lacking histidine, they cannot grow. If a test compound is mutagenic, it may cause a **reversion** mutation (back-mutation) that restores the his+ phenotype. Each colony on the plate represents one bacterium that acquired a his+ reversion — the more colonies, the more mutagenic the compound.

(b) Many chemicals are not mutagenic themselves but become mutagenic after **metabolic activation** in the liver. The S9 fraction (supernatant from liver homogenate centrifuged at 9,000 x g) contains cytochrome P450 enzymes and other Phase I/II metabolizing enzymes. Including S9 simulates the metabolic processing that would occur in a whole animal, detecting pro-mutagens that require activation (e.g., aflatoxin, benzopyrene).

(c) Limitations: (1) Bacteria differ from humans in metabolism, DNA repair, and chromosome structure — some human carcinogens are missed. (2) The test uses specific reversion events, so it may not detect all mutation types. (3) It does not detect non-genotoxic carcinogens (which promote cancer without causing DNA damage). (4) It gives no information about dose-response in whole organisms. The Ames test is a rapid screen, not a definitive carcinogenicity test.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Tumor cells with defective mismatch repair show **microsatellite instability (MSI)** — variation in the length of short repeated sequences (e.g., AAAAAAA). Explain why MMR deficiency specifically causes instability at microsatellite sequences.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

During replication of a repetitive sequence, the nascent strand can "slip" relative to the template, forming a loop. What normally fixes this?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

During DNA replication of microsatellite repeats (e.g., A$_n$ or CA$_n$), the nascent strand can transiently **slip** (disengage and reanneal) relative to the template strand. This "strand slippage" creates a small loop of unpaired bases — an insertion loop on the nascent strand (if it reanneals out of register, looping out an extra repeat) or on the template strand (deletion loop).

In cells with functional **MMR**, these slipped-strand intermediates are recognized by MutS-alpha (MSH2-MSH6) as insertion/deletion loops and are repaired. The newly synthesized strand is identified (by nicks or other signals), the loop is excised, and the DNA is resynthesized correctly.

In MMR-deficient cells (e.g., loss of MLH1 or MSH2), these slipped-strand intermediates are **not repaired**. After the next round of replication, the loop becomes a permanent insertion or deletion. Over many cell divisions, the microsatellite repeat number drifts up and down stochastically — this is microsatellite instability.

MSI is used as a clinical diagnostic marker: tumors with MSI-high status indicate defective MMR, which has implications for prognosis and treatment (MSI-high tumors respond better to immune checkpoint inhibitors because of their high mutation burden and neoantigen load).

</details>

</aside>

## Connections [Master]

- **DNA replication [17.05.01]** is the primary source of mutations through replication errors; the proofreading and MMR systems described here are the corrective layers.

- **Translation [17.05.03]** is affected by mutations: missense mutations change one amino acid, nonsense mutations truncate the protein, and frameshift mutations scramble the sequence.

- **Evolution [19.04.01]** depends on mutations as the ultimate source of genetic variation. The mutation rate determines the pace at which natural selection can act.

- **Cancer biology** is fundamentally a disease of mutation accumulation. Most cancers require 3-7 driver mutations in oncogenes and tumor suppressors, plus hundreds to thousands of passenger mutations.

- **Cell cycle checkpoints [17.08.01]** halt the cell cycle in response to DNA damage, giving repair systems time to work before replication or mitosis proceeds.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Tomas Lindahl's 1993 Nature paper, "Instability and decay of the primary structure of DNA," systematically cataloged the spontaneous damage that DNA suffers and calculated that without repair, DNA would degrade to useless fragments within hours. This paper made the quantitative case that DNA repair is not a luxury but a necessity, and laid the groundwork for Lindahl's own work on base excision repair (Nobel Prize 2015, shared with Modrich and Sancar).

Aziz Sancar discovered nucleotide excision repair in bacteria and then characterized the analogous pathway in humans, connecting it to xeroderma pigmentosum. Paul Modrich elucidated the mismatch repair pathway and showed how it uses methylation patterns in bacteria to distinguish parental from daughter strands.

The concept of mutation has philosophical weight. Mutations are random with respect to fitness — the environment does not direct which mutations occur. This randomness was established by the Luria-Delbruck fluctuation test (1943), which showed that bacterial resistance to phage arises spontaneously before exposure, not in response to it. The implication is that evolution has no foresight: it works with the random variation that happens to arise, filtering it through natural selection after the fact.
