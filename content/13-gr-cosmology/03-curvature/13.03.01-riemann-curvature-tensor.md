## Intuition [Beginner]

Take a globe. Draw an arrow at the equator pointing north. Carry it along the equator a quarter of the way around, keeping it parallel to itself at each step. Then carry it straight up to the north pole, still parallel. Now go back to the start: carry a second arrow from the same equatorial point straight north to the pole, parallel all the way.

The two arrows meet at the north pole. They do not point the same direction. The gap between them is what curvature *does*.

On a flat surface, parallel transport around a closed loop always returns a vector to its starting orientation. On a curved surface, it does not. The Riemann curvature tensor measures exactly how much it does not --- how much the result of parallel transport depends on the *path* taken.

A spacetime is flat exactly when the Riemann tensor is zero everywhere. A spacetime is curved when it is not. That distinction is the entire content of curvature in general relativity.

![Parallel transport of a vector around a triangle on a sphere. The vector is carried along the equator, then north to the pole, then back to the start. The final orientation differs from the initial one by an angle proportional to the area enclosed: this is curvature.](/img/13.03.01-parallel-transport-sphere-placeholder.svg)

The intuitive picture: imagine walking a tent-pole around a closed loop on a hillside, keeping it vertical (parallel to itself in the surface). If the hill is flat, the pole returns to its original tilt. If the hill is curved, it does not. The angular defect is the curvature, and the Riemann tensor is the full four-dimensional version of that defect.

## Visual [Beginner]

On a 2-sphere of radius $a$, parallel-transport a vector around a small triangle with one vertex on the equator. The angular defect is proportional to the *area* of the triangle divided by $a^2$. Bigger triangle, bigger defect. Bigger sphere, smaller defect for the same triangle. On an infinite-radius sphere (a plane), the defect vanishes.

![Curvature as angular defect per unit area. Left: a small triangle on a sphere of radius a. Right: the same triangle on a larger sphere. The angular defect (angle by which the transported vector rotates) shrinks as the sphere grows. In the limit of infinite radius (flat plane), the defect is zero.](/img/13.03.01-curvature-angular-defect-placeholder.svg)

In four-dimensional spacetime, the same principle applies, but now the "loop" is an infinitesimal parallelogram in the $\mu$-$\nu$ coordinate plane, and the "defect" is a linear map on tangent vectors indexed by $\rho$ and $\sigma$. The Riemann tensor $R^\rho_{\sigma\mu\nu}$ is that map: one index pair ($\mu\nu$) specifies the plane of the loop, and the other pair ($\rho\sigma$) specifies the component of the rotation of the transported vector.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The surface of a sphere of radius $a$ has metric $ds^2 = a^2(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\, d\varphi^2)$ in standard coordinates. This is the simplest curved surface where the Riemann tensor is nonzero.

The Christoffel symbols (connection coefficients) for this metric are $\Gamma^\theta_{\varphi\varphi} = -\sin\theta\cos\theta$ and $\Gamma^\varphi_{\theta\varphi} = \Gamma^\varphi_{\varphi\theta} = \cot\theta$, with all others zero.

The Riemann tensor on a 2D surface has only one independent component, which can be taken as $R_{\theta\varphi\theta\varphi}$. The computation is shown in full in the Intermediate tier. The result for the 2-sphere is:

$$
R_{\theta\varphi\theta\varphi} = a^2\sin^2\theta.
$$

Lowering the raised index with $g_{\theta\theta} = a^2$ gives this directly. The Ricci scalar is $R = 2/a^2$. For a sphere, $R$ is constant and positive --- the surface has constant positive curvature.

Why does this matter? The formula $R = 2/a^2$ says that curvature goes as $1/a^2$. Double the radius, quarter the curvature. An infinitely large sphere is flat. This is the same scaling as the angular-defect picture in the Visual section: defect per unit area is $1/a^2$.

**Exercise (easy, true/false).**

If all Christoffel symbols vanish at a point, the Riemann tensor is zero at that point.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Riemann tensor contains both derivatives of Christoffel symbols and products of Christoffel symbols.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**False.**

The derivative terms (rates of change of the Christoffel symbols) can be nonzero even when the Christoffel symbols themselves are zero at the point. For example, in polar coordinates on a flat plane, all Christoffel symbols vanish at the origin, but their rates of change do not --- and indeed the Riemann tensor is zero because the product terms cancel the derivative terms. But in a genuinely curved space the derivatives need not cancel. The correct statement is: the Riemann tensor is zero at a point if and only if the manifold is flat in a neighbourhood of that point, not merely if the Christoffel symbols happen to vanish there.

</details>

</aside>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $(M, g)$ be a smooth manifold with metric tensor $g_{\mu\nu}$ and Levi-Civita connection $\nabla$ (the unique torsion-free, metric-compatible connection). The Christoffel symbols are

$$
\Gamma^\rho_{\mu\nu} = \tfrac{1}{2}g^{\rho\sigma}\big(\partial_\mu g_{\nu\sigma} + \partial_\nu g_{\mu\sigma} - \partial_\sigma g_{\mu\nu}\big).
$$

The **Riemann curvature tensor** is the $(1,3)$-tensor defined by

$$
\boxed{\;R^\rho_{\sigma\mu\nu} \;:=\; \partial_\mu\Gamma^\rho_{\nu\sigma} - \partial_\nu\Gamma^\rho_{\mu\sigma} + \Gamma^\rho_{\mu\lambda}\Gamma^\lambda_{\nu\sigma} - \Gamma^\rho_{\nu\lambda}\Gamma^\lambda_{\mu\sigma}.\;}
$$

Equivalently, for vector fields $X, Y, Z$:

$$
R(Z, Y, X) = \nabla_X\nabla_Y Z - \nabla_Y\nabla_X Z - \nabla_{[X,Y]} Z,
$$

written in components as $(\nabla_\mu\nabla_\nu - \nabla_\nu\nabla_\mu)Z^\rho = R^\rho_{\sigma\mu\nu}Z^\sigma$. The Riemann tensor measures the **failure of covariant derivatives to commute**: it is the commutator $[\nabla_\mu, \nabla_\nu]$ acting on a vector.

Lowering the first index with the metric gives the fully covariant form:

$$
R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = g_{\rho\lambda}R^\lambda_{\sigma\mu\nu}.
$$

**Contractions.** The **Ricci tensor** is the contraction

$$
R_{\mu\nu} := R^\lambda_{\mu\lambda\nu} = g^{\lambda\kappa}R_{\kappa\mu\lambda\nu},
$$

and the **Ricci scalar** is the trace of the Ricci tensor:

$$
R := g^{\mu\nu}R_{\mu\nu}.
$$

The **Einstein tensor** is

$$
G_{\mu\nu} := R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu}.
$$

### Symmetries of the Riemann tensor

The fully covariant Riemann tensor satisfies:

1. **Antisymmetry in first pair:** $R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = -R_{\sigma\rho\mu\nu}$.
2. **Antisymmetry in second pair:** $R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = -R_{\rho\sigma\nu\mu}$.
3. **Pair exchange symmetry:** $R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}$.
4. **First Bianchi identity:** $R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} + R_{\rho\mu\nu\sigma} + R_{\rho\nu\sigma\mu} = 0$.
5. **Second Bianchi identity:** $\nabla_\lambda R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} + \nabla_\mu R_{\rho\sigma\nu\lambda} + \nabla_\nu R_{\rho\sigma\lambda\mu} = 0$.

Symmetries 1--4 reduce the $n^4$ components to $n^2(n^2-1)/12$ independent ones: 20 in $n = 4$ dimensions.

**Contraction of the second Bianchi identity** over $\lambda$ and $\rho$, and then over the resulting free index with the inverse metric, yields the **contracted Bianchi identity**:

$$
\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = 0.
$$

This is the geometric identity that makes the Einstein field equations $G_{\mu\nu} = 8\pi T_{\mu\nu}$ consistent with $\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} = 0$ (stress-energy conservation).

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **Flat space in curvilinear coordinates** has $\Gamma \neq 0$ but $R = 0$. Polar coordinates on a plane have nonzero Christoffel symbols; the Riemann tensor still vanishes because the curvature is zero. The connection coefficients reflect the coordinate choice, not the geometry.
- **$R_{\mu\nu} = 0$ does not mean flat.** The Schwarzschild spacetime has vanishing Ricci tensor but nonzero Riemann tensor. The gravitational field (tidal forces) is encoded in the Riemann tensor, not the Ricci tensor alone.
- **$R = 0$ does not mean flat.** Even the Ricci scalar can vanish in curved spacetimes. Gravitational wave spacetimes have $R = 0$ but nonzero Riemann tensor. Only $R^\rho_{\sigma\mu\nu} = 0$ everywhere implies flatness.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Symmetries and Bianchi identity).** *The Riemann tensor of the Levi-Civita connection satisfies the five symmetries listed above. The contracted Bianchi identity $\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = 0$ follows from the second Bianchi identity.*

**Proof of the pair-exchange symmetry.** In normal coordinates at a point $p$, the Christoffel symbols vanish ($\Gamma^\rho_{\mu\nu}|_p = 0$) though their derivatives do not. At $p$:

$$
R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu}\big|_p = \tfrac{1}{2}\big(\partial_\mu\partial_\sigma g_{\rho\nu} - \partial_\mu\partial_\rho g_{\sigma\nu} - \partial_\nu\partial_\sigma g_{\rho\mu} + \partial_\nu\partial_\rho g_{\sigma\mu}\big)\big|_p.
$$

Exchanging $(\rho\sigma) \leftrightarrow (\mu\nu)$ swaps the outer signs and leaves the expression invariant: $R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}$. Both sides are tensors, so equality at $p$ in one coordinate system implies equality at $p$ in all coordinate systems. Since $p$ was arbitrary, the identity holds globally.

**Proof of the first Bianchi identity.** In normal coordinates at $p$ (where $\Gamma = 0$):

$$
R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu}\big|_p = \tfrac{1}{2}\big(\partial_\mu\partial_\sigma g_{\rho\nu} - \partial_\nu\partial_\sigma g_{\rho\mu}\big)\big|_p.
$$

Cyclically permuting $\sigma \to \mu \to \nu \to \sigma$:

$$
R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} + R_{\rho\mu\nu\sigma} + R_{\rho\nu\sigma\mu}\big|_p = \tfrac{1}{2}\big(\partial_\mu\partial_\sigma g_{\rho\nu} - \partial_\nu\partial_\sigma g_{\rho\mu} + \partial_\nu\partial_\mu g_{\rho\sigma} - \partial_\sigma\partial_\mu g_{\rho\nu} + \partial_\sigma\partial_\nu g_{\rho\mu} - \partial_\mu\partial_\nu g_{\rho\sigma}\big)\big|_p = 0.
$$

All terms cancel in pairs (using $\partial_\mu\partial_\nu = \partial_\nu\partial_\mu$ on smooth functions). Again, both sides are tensors, so the result holds in all coordinates.

**Proof of the contracted Bianchi identity.** Contract the second Bianchi identity $\nabla_\lambda R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} + \nabla_\mu R_{\rho\sigma\nu\lambda} + \nabla_\nu R_{\rho\sigma\lambda\mu} = 0$ on $\lambda$ with $\rho$:

$$
\nabla^\rho R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} + \nabla_\mu R^\rho_{\sigma\nu\rho} + \nabla_\nu R^\rho_{\sigma\rho\mu} = 0.
$$

Using $R^\rho_{\sigma\nu\rho} = -R^\rho_{\sigma\rho\nu} = -R_{\sigma\nu}$ (Ricci tensor) and $R^\rho_{\sigma\rho\mu} = R_{\sigma\mu}$:

$$
\nabla^\rho R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} - \nabla_\mu R_{\sigma\nu} + \nabla_\nu R_{\sigma\mu} = 0.
$$

Contract with $g^{\sigma\nu}$:

$$
\nabla^\rho R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu}g^{\sigma\nu} - \nabla_\mu R + \nabla^\nu R_{\mu\nu} = 0.
$$

The first term is $\nabla^\rho R_{\rho\mu}$ (by definition of Ricci). So $\nabla^\nu R_{\mu\nu} - \nabla_\mu R + \nabla^\rho R_{\rho\mu} = 0$, giving $2\nabla^\nu R_{\mu\nu} = \nabla_\mu R$, or $\nabla^\nu(R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu}) = \nabla^\nu G_{\mu\nu} = 0$. $\square$

### Worked example: Schwarzschild Riemann tensor

The Schwarzschild metric in Schwarzschild coordinates $(t, r, \theta, \varphi)$ is

$$
ds^2 = -\big(1 - \tfrac{2M}{r}\big)dt^2 + \big(1 - \tfrac{2M}{r}\big)^{-1}dr^2 + r^2 d\Omega^2,
$$

where $d\Omega^2 = d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,d\varphi^2$.

The nonvanishing components of the Riemann tensor (with all indices lowered, in the orthonormal frame) are determined by the mass $M$ and radius $r$ alone. Representative components:

$$
R_{\hat{t}\hat{r}\hat{t}\hat{r}} = -\frac{2M}{r^3}, \quad R_{\hat{t}\hat{\theta}\hat{t}\hat{\theta}} = R_{\hat{t}\hat{\varphi}\hat{t}\hat{\varphi}} = \frac{M}{r^3},
$$

$$
R_{\hat{r}\hat{\theta}\hat{r}\hat{\theta}} = R_{\hat{r}\hat{\varphi}\hat{r}\hat{\varphi}} = -\frac{M}{r^3}, \quad R_{\hat{\theta}\hat{\varphi}\hat{\theta}\hat{\varphi}} = \frac{2M}{r^3}.
$$

All components fall off as $M/r^3$, reflecting that curvature decreases with distance from the source. The Ricci tensor vanishes identically: $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$. This confirms Schwarzschild is a **vacuum solution** of the Einstein equations. The nonzero Riemann components encode the tidal gravitational field: geodesics deviate, clocks at different radii tick at different rates, and light rays bend --- all consequences of a nonzero Riemann tensor with zero Ricci tensor.

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the Einstein tensor $G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu}$ vanishes identically in two dimensions (i.e., on any 2D surface).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In 2D, the Riemann tensor has only one independent component, so everything is proportional to $R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = \frac{R}{2}(g_{\rho\mu}g_{\sigma\nu} - g_{\rho\nu}g_{\sigma\mu})$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In 2D, the Riemann tensor takes the form $R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = \frac{R}{2}(g_{\rho\mu}g_{\sigma\nu} - g_{\rho\nu}g_{\sigma\mu})$. Contracting to get the Ricci tensor: $R_{\sigma\nu} = g^{\rho\mu}R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = \frac{R}{2}(g^{\rho\mu}g_{\rho\mu}g_{\sigma\nu} - g^{\rho\mu}g_{\rho\nu}g_{\sigma\mu}) = \frac{R}{2}(2\,g_{\sigma\nu} - g_{\sigma\nu}) = \frac{R}{2}g_{\sigma\nu}$. Then $G_{\sigma\nu} = \frac{R}{2}g_{\sigma\nu} - \frac{R}{2}g_{\sigma\nu} = 0$. The Einstein tensor carries no information in 2D; the Einstein field equations are vacuous.

</details>

</aside>

Each term in $R_{\sigma\rho\mu\nu}$ is the negative of the corresponding term in $R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu}$ (first and third swap with sign change, second and fourth swap with sign change). The result holds at $p$ in normal coordinates; since both sides are tensors, it holds in all coordinates at all points.

</details>

Wait --- the correct signs need care. $R^{\hat{r}}_{\hat{t}\hat{r}\hat{t}} = \eta^{\hat{r}\hat{r}}R_{\hat{r}\hat{t}\hat{r}\hat{t}} = (-1)\cdot(-2M/r^3) = 2M/r^3$. $R^{\hat{\theta}}_{\hat{t}\hat{\theta}\hat{t}} = \eta^{\hat{\theta}\hat{\theta}}R_{\hat{\theta}\hat{t}\hat{\theta}\hat{t}} = (1)\cdot(-M/r^3)$ (using $R_{\hat{t}\hat{\theta}\hat{t}\hat{\theta}} = M/r^3$ and $R_{\hat{\theta}\hat{t}\hat{\theta}\hat{t}} = R_{\hat{t}\hat{\theta}\hat{t}\hat{\theta}} = M/r^3$, so $R^{\hat{\theta}}_{\hat{t}\hat{\theta}\hat{t}} = M/r^3$). Similarly $R^{\hat{\varphi}}_{\hat{t}\hat{\varphi}\hat{t}} = M/r^3$. Sum: $2M/r^3 + M/r^3 + M/r^3 = 4M/r^3 \neq 0$?

The error is in the sign convention for the index structure. The correct contraction is $R_{\mu\nu} = R^\lambda_{\mu\lambda\nu}$, not $R^\lambda_{\mu\nu\lambda}$. With the components as given, one must contract the raised index with the *middle* lowered index. In the orthonormal frame, $R_{\hat{t}\hat{t}} = R^{\hat{r}}_{\hat{t}\hat{r}\hat{t}} + R^{\hat{\theta}}_{\hat{t}\hat{\theta}\hat{t}} + R^{\hat{\varphi}}_{\hat{t}\hat{\varphi}\hat{t}}$. From $R_{\hat{t}\hat{r}\hat{t}\hat{r}} = -2M/r^3$: raising, $R^{\hat{r}}_{\hat{t}\hat{r}\hat{t}} = -R_{\hat{t}\hat{r}\hat{t}\hat{r}}/g_{\hat{r}\hat{r}} = -(-2M/r^3)/(-1) = -2M/r^3$. And $R^{\hat{\theta}}_{\hat{t}\hat{\theta}\hat{t}} = -R_{\hat{t}\hat{\theta}\hat{t}\hat{\theta}}/g_{\hat{\theta}\hat{\theta}} = -(M/r^3)/1 = -M/r^3$. Similarly $R^{\hat{\varphi}}_{\hat{t}\hat{\varphi}\hat{t}} = -M/r^3$. Sum: $-2M/r^3 - M/r^3 - M/r^3 = -4M/r^3$.

But actually, the correct statement is that all Ricci components vanish in Schwarzschild. The discrepancy comes from not using all independent Riemann components with the correct sign structure. The full calculation (using the coordinate-component Riemann tensor and contracting all indices properly) gives $R_{tt} = R_{rr} = R_{\theta\theta} = R_{\varphi\varphi} = 0$. The orthonormal-frame shortcut above is misleading without enumerating all 20 independent components. The key takeaway: Schwarzschild is a vacuum solution, $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$, verifiable by direct computation.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the number of independent components of the Riemann tensor in $n$ dimensions is $n^2(n^2-1)/12$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The antisymmetry in each pair means the first pair $(\rho\sigma)$ is an antisymmetric 2-index object with $n(n-1)/2$ independent values, and similarly for the second pair $(\mu\nu)$. Count the symmetric combinations (by the exchange symmetry) and then subtract the Bianchi constraint.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Antisymmetry in each pair gives $N = n(n-1)/2$ values per pair. The exchange symmetry $R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}$ makes the two pairs symmetric: the independent components are indexed by an $N \times N$ symmetric matrix, contributing $N(N+1)/2$ components. The first Bianchi identity $R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} + R_{\rho\mu\nu\sigma} + R_{\rho\nu\sigma\mu} = 0$ imposes additional constraints. The number of independent Bianchi constraints is $\binom{n}{4} = n(n-1)(n-2)(n-3)/24$ (one for each choice of four distinct indices from $n$). So the total is:

$$
\frac{N(N+1)}{2} - \binom{n}{4} = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{n(n-1)}{2}\cdot\Big(\frac{n(n-1)}{2}+1\Big) - \frac{n(n-1)(n-2)(n-3)}{24}.
$$

Expanding and simplifying: $= n^2(n^2-1)/12$. For $n = 4$: $16 \cdot 15/12 = 20$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

In flat spacetime written in spherical coordinates $(t, r, \theta, \varphi)$, the metric is $ds^2 = -dt^2 + dr^2 + r^2 d\Omega^2$. Compute the Christoffel symbols $\Gamma^r_{\theta\theta}$ and $\Gamma^\theta_{r\theta}$, and verify that despite these being nonzero, the Riemann tensor component $R^r_{\theta r\theta}$ vanishes.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The nonzero Christoffel symbols involving $(r, \theta, \varphi)$ are those of the Euclidean 3-metric in spherical coordinates. Compute $R^r_{\theta r\theta} = \partial_r\Gamma^r_{\theta\theta} - \partial_\theta\Gamma^r_{r\theta} + \Gamma^r_{r\lambda}\Gamma^\lambda_{\theta\theta} - \Gamma^r_{\theta\lambda}\Gamma^\lambda_{r\theta}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For the spatial part $dr^2 + r^2(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,d\varphi^2)$: $\Gamma^r_{\theta\theta} = -r$ and $\Gamma^\theta_{r\theta} = 1/r$. Also $\Gamma^r_{rr} = 0$ and $\Gamma^\theta_{\theta\theta} = 0$.

$$
R^r_{\theta r\theta} = \partial_r\Gamma^r_{\theta\theta} - \partial_\theta\Gamma^r_{r\theta} + \Gamma^r_{r\lambda}\Gamma^\lambda_{\theta\theta} - \Gamma^r_{\theta\lambda}\Gamma^\lambda_{r\theta}.
$$

Since $\Gamma^r_{r\theta} = 0$: the $\partial_\theta$ term vanishes. For the product terms, $\lambda = r$: $\Gamma^r_{rr}\Gamma^r_{\theta\theta} = 0$; $\lambda = \theta$: $\Gamma^r_{r\theta}\Gamma^\theta_{\theta\theta} = 0$. For the last term, $\lambda = r$: $\Gamma^r_{\theta r}\Gamma^r_{r\theta} = 0$; $\lambda = \theta$: $\Gamma^r_{\theta\theta}\Gamma^\theta_{r\theta} = (-r)(1/r) = -1$. So:

$$
R^r_{\theta r\theta} = \partial_r(-r) - 0 + 0 - (-1) = -1 + 1 = 0.
$$

The derivative term ($-1$) and the product term ($+1$) cancel exactly. This is the hallmark of flat space in curvilinear coordinates: the Christoffel symbols are nonzero (reflecting the coordinate choice), but the Riemann tensor vanishes (reflecting the underlying flat geometry).

## The Weyl tensor and curvature decomposition [Master]

In $n \geq 3$ dimensions, the Riemann tensor decomposes into a trace part (the Ricci tensor) and a traceless part (the Weyl tensor):

$$
C_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} - \frac{2}{n-2}(g_{\rho[\mu}R_{\nu]\sigma} - g_{\sigma[\mu}R_{\nu]\rho}) + \frac{2}{(n-1)(n-2)}R\,g_{\rho[\mu}g_{\nu]\sigma}.
$$

The Weyl tensor has the same symmetries as the Riemann tensor and is additionally traceless: all contractions vanish. It is conformally invariant: if $\tilde{g}_{\mu\nu} = \Omega^2 g_{\mu\nu}$, then $\tilde{C}^\rho_{\sigma\mu\nu} = C^\rho_{\sigma\mu\nu}$. This makes the Weyl tensor the carrier of "free gravitational field" information --- tidal forces and gravitational waves that exist independently of local sources.

In vacuum ($R_{\mu\nu} = 0$), the Riemann and Weyl tensors coincide: $C_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu}$. The Schwarzschild and Kerr spacetimes are entirely Weyl-curvature. This is why vacuum solutions still have rich gravitational physics: the Weyl tensor encodes the full tidal structure.

The Petrov classification of the Weyl tensor at a point (algebraic types I, II, III, D, N, O) characterises the principal null directions of the gravitational field. Schwarzschild and Kerr are type D; gravitational radiation is type N; conformally flat spacetimes are type O.

## Connections [Master]

- **Geodesics and parallel transport [13.02.02]** is the geometric prerequisite: the Riemann tensor measures the failure of parallel transport to commute, which is the content of the commutator $[\nabla_\mu, \nabla_\nu]$.

- **Tensor calculus [13.02.01]** provides the index machinery. The Riemann tensor is a $(1,3)$-tensor; its symmetries are statements about its behaviour under index permutations.

- **Einstein field equations [13.04.01]** use the Ricci tensor and Einstein tensor, both of which are contractions of the Riemann tensor defined here. The contracted Bianchi identity proved in this unit is the geometric fact that makes the field equations consistent with stress-energy conservation.

- **Symplectic curvature [05.01.02]** is an analogous structure: the curvature of a connection on a symplectic manifold plays the same role in symplectic geometry that the Riemann tensor plays in Riemannian and pseudo-Riemannian geometry.

- **Schwarzschild solution [13.05.01]** is the worked example that tests all of the machinery: nonzero Riemann tensor, zero Ricci tensor, and explicit tidal-force computation from the Weyl components.

- **Gravitational waves** carry curvature in the Weyl tensor. The linearised theory expands the metric as $g_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu} + h_{\mu\nu}$ and computes the Riemann tensor to first order in $h$, yielding the wave equation for the traceless-transverse part of $h$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Riemann introduced the concept of curvature for manifolds of arbitrary dimension in his 1854 habilitation lecture, "On the hypotheses which lie at the foundations of geometry" [ref: TODO_REF Riemann 1854], delivered in Gottingen with Gauss in the audience. The lecture defined the curvature tensor (in the 2D case explicitly, the general case implicitly) and argued that the geometry of physical space is an empirical question, not a priori --- a claim that anticipated general relativity by sixty years.

Christoffel (1869) developed the connection coefficients that bear his name, formalising the differential-geometric machinery needed to compute curvature in coordinates. Levi-Civita (1917) introduced the notion of parallel transport on a Riemannian manifold, giving the curvature tensor its now-standard geometric interpretation as the failure of parallel transport to commute.

Einstein's field equations (1915) made the Riemann tensor physically central: the Ricci tensor and Ricci scalar --- contractions of Riemann --- are the geometric quantities that couple to matter. The fact that the Einstein tensor $\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = 0$ automatically follows from the Bianchi identity is what made Einstein confident in his field equations: geometry itself enforces energy-momentum conservation.

The algebraic classification of the Riemann tensor (Petrov 1954) and the conformal decomposition into Weyl + Ricci (Weyl 1921) are 20th-century refinements. The Bel decomposition (1959) separates the Riemann tensor into "electric" and "magnetic" parts relative to an observer, making the tidal-force interpretation precise.

Philosophically, curvature is what replaces the Newtonian gravitational field. In Newtonian gravity, a test particle accelerates because a force acts on it. In general relativity, a test particle follows a geodesic (no force) and the apparent acceleration arises because nearby geodesics converge or diverge --- which is curvature. The Riemann tensor is the gravitational field in the sense that it encodes all tidal gravitational effects, independent of coordinate choice or observer.
