## Intuition [Beginner]

Gravity is not a force. It is the shape of space and time. A heavy object like the Sun bends spacetime around itself, and everything nearby -- planets, light rays, clocks -- responds to that bend. The Einstein field equations are the rule that tells you exactly how much bending a given amount of matter produces.

Think of a stretched rubber sheet. Place a bowling ball on it. The sheet sags. Roll a marble nearby and it curves toward the bowling ball, not because the ball pulls the marble, but because the marble is following the curve of the sheet. In general relativity the "sheet" is spacetime itself, and the "bowling ball" is any concentration of mass and energy.

The equation comes in two halves. On the left: the Einstein tensor $G_{\mu\nu}$, which describes how spacetime is curved. On the right: the stress-energy tensor $T_{\mu\nu}$, which describes how matter and energy are distributed. The proportionality constant $8\pi G/c^4$ sets the scale -- it says that you need an enormous amount of energy to produce even a tiny amount of curvature, which is why gravity is so weak compared to electromagnetism.

In words: matter tells spacetime how to curve; curved spacetime tells matter how to move. The first half is the field equation itself. The second half is the geodesic equation, which follows from the curvature the field equation produces.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A vacuum is a region with no matter or energy -- so $T_{\mu\nu} = 0$ everywhere. What do the Einstein equations say about spacetime in a vacuum?

The Einstein field equations are $G_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} T_{\mu\nu}$. If $T_{\mu\nu} = 0$, then $G_{\mu\nu} = 0$. The Einstein tensor is defined as $G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu}$, where $R_{\mu\nu}$ is the Ricci tensor and $R$ is the scalar curvature.

Taking the trace of both sides by contracting with $g^{\mu\nu}$ gives $G = R - \frac{1}{2}(4)R = -R$. So $G_{\mu\nu} = 0$ means $R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu} = 0$. Taking the trace: $R - 2R = -R = 0$, so $R = 0$. Substituting back: $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$.

In a vacuum the Einstein equations reduce to $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$. This does not mean spacetime is flat -- the full Riemann curvature tensor can still be nonzero. It means only the contracted part vanishes. The Schwarzschild solution (describing spacetime outside a spherical mass) satisfies $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$ while having genuine curvature, including the gravitational field that bends light and slows clocks.

Minkowski space (flat spacetime with metric $\eta_{\mu\nu}$) is also a vacuum solution: all Christoffel symbols vanish, so $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$ and $G_{\mu\nu} = 0$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The **Einstein field equations** (EFE) are

$$
\boxed{G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda\,g_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}\,T_{\mu\nu},}
$$

where:
- $G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu}$ is the **Einstein tensor** [13.03.01],
- $R_{\mu\nu}$ is the Ricci tensor of the Levi-Civita connection of the metric $g_{\mu\nu}$,
- $R = g^{\mu\nu}R_{\mu\nu}$ is the Ricci scalar (scalar curvature),
- $\Lambda$ is the **cosmological constant** (discussed below),
- $T_{\mu\nu}$ is the **stress-energy tensor** [11.01.01],
- $G$ is Newton's gravitational constant,
- $c$ is the speed of light.

The Einstein tensor is symmetric ($G_{\mu\nu} = G_{\nu\mu}$) because $R_{\mu\nu}$ is symmetric and $g_{\mu\nu}$ is symmetric. In four dimensions the equation represents 10 independent component equations (a symmetric $4 \times 4$ tensor has 10 independent components after accounting for symmetry).

### The trace-reversed form

Contracting the EFE with $g^{\mu\nu}$ and using $g^{\mu\nu}G_{\mu\nu} = R - 2R = -R$ in four dimensions gives $-R + 4\Lambda = 8\pi G T/c^4$, where $T = g^{\mu\nu}T_{\mu\nu}$ is the trace of the stress-energy tensor. Substituting back:

$$
R_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}\Big(T_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2}T\,g_{\mu\nu}\Big) + \Lambda\,g_{\mu\nu}.
$$

In vacuum ($T_{\mu\nu} = 0$, $\Lambda = 0$) this reduces to $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$, as derived in the worked example.

### The cosmological constant

The term $\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}$ can be moved to the right side and absorbed into the stress-energy tensor as a contribution $T_{\mu\nu}^{(\Lambda)} = -\frac{c^4 \Lambda}{8\pi G}\,g_{\mu\nu}$. This is the stress-energy of the vacuum: a uniform energy density $\rho_\Lambda = \frac{c^2 \Lambda}{8\pi G}$ with pressure $p_\Lambda = -\rho_\Lambda c^2$. The cosmological constant is not a fudge or an afterthought -- it is the vacuum energy of spacetime, and observational cosmology (supernova data, CMB, baryon acoustic oscillations) has established $\Lambda > 0$ with $\rho_\Lambda \approx 5.96 \times 10^{-27}\;\text{kg/m}^3$, driving the accelerated expansion of the universe.

The observed value of $\Lambda$ corresponds to an energy density of about $5.4 \times 10^{-10}\;\text{J/m}^3$. The discrepancy between this value and the naive quantum-field-theory estimate of $\sim 10^{113}\;\text{J/m}^3$ is the cosmological constant problem -- one of the deepest unsolved problems in theoretical physics.

### The Bianchi identity and conservation of energy

The contracted second Bianchi identity [13.03.01] states

$$
\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} \;=\; 0.
$$

This is a geometric identity, not a physical postulate -- it follows from the definition of $G_{\mu\nu}$ in terms of the Riemann tensor. Taking the covariant divergence of the EFE then gives

$$
\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} \;=\; 0,
$$

which is the local conservation of energy and momentum. This is not an independent physical law in GR -- it is a *consequence* of the field equations, enforced by the geometry. Any matter model that fails to satisfy $\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} = 0$ is inconsistent with the Einstein equations.

### The Newtonian limit

In the weak-field, slow-motion limit, the metric is approximately $g_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu} + h_{\mu\nu}$ with $|h_{\mu\nu}| \ll 1$. For a static, pressureless source ($T_{tt} = \rho c^2$, all other components negligible), the $tt$ component of the EFE reduces to

$$
\nabla^2 \Phi \;=\; 4\pi G \rho,
$$

where $\Phi$ is the Newtonian gravitational potential related to $h_{tt}$ by $h_{tt} = -2\Phi/c^2$. This is Poisson's equation for Newtonian gravity. The factor $8\pi G/c^4$ in the EFE was chosen by Einstein to reproduce exactly this limit, and this fixes the coupling constant with no remaining freedom.

### Counterexamples and common slips

- The EFE is a set of 10 coupled nonlinear partial differential equations for the 10 components of $g_{\mu\nu}$. Because it is nonlinear, you cannot superpose solutions. Two Schwarzschild spacetimes placed side by side do not produce a valid two-body solution -- the field of each one changes the spacetime in which the other lives.
- The vacuum equations $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$ do not mean "no gravity." They mean "no matter." Gravitational fields persist in vacuum -- this is the content of the Schwarzschild solution and of gravitational waves.
- The cosmological constant $\Lambda$ is not a correction to the field equations. It can be moved to either side of the equation. On the left, it is a property of the geometry. On the right, it is the vacuum energy. The two interpretations are mathematically equivalent; the difference is interpretive.
- The EFE determines the metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ given the matter distribution $T_{\mu\nu}$, but $T_{\mu\nu}$ itself depends on $g_{\mu\nu}$ (for instance, the fluid stress-energy involves $g_{\mu\nu}$ in the normalisation of the four-velocity). The equations are therefore coupled: geometry determines the motion of matter, and matter determines the geometry.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Uniqueness of the Einstein equations).** *The Einstein tensor equation $G_{\mu\nu} = \kappa T_{\mu\nu}$ (with $G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu}$) is the unique symmetric $(0,2)$-tensor equation for the metric that satisfies all three of the following:*

*(a) **Newtonian limit.** In the weak-field, slow-motion limit, the equation reproduces Poisson's equation $\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G\rho$.*

*(b) **Divergence-free.** The left side satisfies $\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = 0$ identically (the contracted Bianchi identity), ensuring energy-momentum conservation $\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} = 0$.*

*(c) **Linear in second derivatives.** The equation involves at most second derivatives of the metric, and these appear linearly (the Riemann tensor is linear in second derivatives of $g_{\mu\nu}$).*

**Proof.** We establish uniqueness by exhausting the possibilities.

*Step 1: Available tensors.* Any tensor constructed from the metric and its derivatives that is symmetric and of type $(0,2)$ is, to lowest meaningful order, a linear combination of $g_{\mu\nu}$, $R_{\mu\nu}$, and $R\,g_{\mu\nu}$. Higher-order terms (products of curvature tensors, derivatives of curvature) involve more than second derivatives of $g_{\mu\nu}$ or are nonlinear in second derivatives.

*Step 2: Linearity in second derivatives.* Condition (c) restricts the left side to the most general form linear in second derivatives of $g_{\mu\nu}$:

$$
A_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \alpha\,R_{\mu\nu} + \beta\,R\,g_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda\,g_{\mu\nu},
$$

where $\alpha$, $\beta$, and $\Lambda$ are constants. The term $\Lambda g_{\mu\nu}$ involves zero derivatives of the metric.

*Step 3: Divergence-free condition.* Computing $\nabla^\mu A_{\mu\nu} = 0$ and using the contracted Bianchi identity $\nabla^\mu(R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu}) = 0$:

$$
\nabla^\mu A_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \alpha\,\nabla^\mu R_{\mu\nu} + \beta\,\nabla_\nu R.
$$

From the contracted Bianchi identity, $\nabla^\mu R_{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{2}\nabla_\nu R$. Substituting:

$$
\nabla^\mu A_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \big(\tfrac{\alpha}{2} + \beta\big)\nabla_\nu R.
$$

For this to vanish for all metrics, we need $\beta = -\alpha/2$. Setting $\alpha = 1$ by convention gives $\beta = -1/2$ and

$$
A_{\mu\nu} \;=\; R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda\,g_{\mu\nu} \;=\; G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda\,g_{\mu\nu}.
$$

*Step 4: Newtonian limit.* For $\Lambda = 0$, matching to Poisson's equation in the weak-field limit fixes the proportionality constant to $\kappa = 8\pi G/c^4$. A nonzero $\Lambda$ contributes at order $\Lambda r^2$ to the potential and is negligible at solar-system scales for the observed value of $\Lambda$.

Therefore the Einstein field equations $G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = (8\pi G/c^4)T_{\mu\nu}$ are the unique tensor equation satisfying (a), (b), and (c). $\blacksquare$

**Remark.** Dropping condition (c) permits higher-derivative theories (e.g., $f(R)$ gravity, Lovelock gravity in dimensions greater than four). In four dimensions, Lovelock's theorem states that the Einstein tensor is the unique symmetric, divergence-free $(0,2)$-tensor that is linear in second derivatives and involves at most the metric and its first two derivatives. This is a stronger version of the uniqueness result.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the Einstein tensor $G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu}$ vanishes identically in flat Minkowski spacetime (metric $\eta_{\mu\nu}$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In flat spacetime the Christoffel symbols vanish in Cartesian coordinates, so the Riemann tensor and all its contractions vanish.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In Minkowski spacetime with Cartesian coordinates, the metric components $\eta_{\mu\nu}$ are constant. All Christoffel symbols $\Gamma^\rho_{\mu\nu} = \frac{1}{2}\eta^{\rho\sigma}(\partial_\mu\eta_{\nu\sigma} + \partial_\nu\eta_{\mu\sigma} - \partial_\sigma\eta_{\mu\nu}) = 0$ since every derivative of $\eta_{\mu\nu}$ vanishes. The Riemann tensor is built from derivatives of Christoffel symbols and products of Christoffel symbols, so $R^\rho{}_{\sigma\mu\nu} = 0$. Consequently $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$, $R = 0$, and $G_{\mu\nu} = 0 - 0 = 0$. This confirms that Minkowski space is a vacuum solution of the Einstein equations.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Take the trace of the Einstein tensor $G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu}$ by contracting with $g^{\mu\nu}$. Show that $G = -R$ in four dimensions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$g^{\mu\nu}g_{\mu\nu} = 4$ in four dimensions. Contract both terms separately.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$G = g^{\mu\nu}G_{\mu\nu} = g^{\mu\nu}R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}R\,g^{\mu\nu}g_{\mu\nu} = R - \frac{1}{2}R \cdot 4 = R - 2R = -R$.

So $G = -R$ in four spacetime dimensions. In $n$ dimensions the same calculation gives $G = R - (n/2)R = (1 - n/2)R$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Starting from the Einstein field equations $G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = (8\pi G/c^4)T_{\mu\nu}$, derive the trace-reversed form $R_{\mu\nu} = (8\pi G/c^4)(T_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}T\,g_{\mu\nu}) + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take the trace of the field equations to find $R$ in terms of $T$ and $\Lambda$. Then substitute back.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The EFE gives $R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}T_{\mu\nu}$. Contract with $g^{\mu\nu}$:

$$
-R + 4\Lambda = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}T,
$$

using $G = -R$ and $g^{\mu\nu}g_{\mu\nu} = 4$. So $R = 4\Lambda - \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}T$. Substituting into the EFE:

$$
R_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}T_{\mu\nu} - \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} + \frac{1}{2}\Big(4\Lambda - \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}T\Big)g_{\mu\nu}
\;=\; \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}\Big(T_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}T\,g_{\mu\nu}\Big) + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu}.
$$

For vacuum ($T_{\mu\nu} = 0$) with $\Lambda = 0$, this gives $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

The stress-energy tensor for a perfect fluid with density $\rho$, pressure $p$, and four-velocity $u^\mu$ (normalised so $g_{\mu\nu}u^\mu u^\nu = -c^2$) is $T_{\mu\nu} = (\rho + p/c^2)u_\mu u_\nu + p\,g_{\mu\nu}$. Show that for dust (pressureless matter, $p = 0$) the trace is $T = \rho c^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Contract $T_{\mu\nu}$ with $g^{\mu\nu}$. Use the normalisation of $u^\mu$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For dust ($p = 0$): $T_{\mu\nu} = \rho u_\mu u_\nu$. The trace is

$$
T = g^{\mu\nu}T_{\mu\nu} = \rho\,g^{\mu\nu}u_\mu u_\nu = \rho(-c^2) = -\rho c^2.
$$

(Note: the sign depends on the metric signature convention. In the mostly-plus convention $(-,+,+,+)$, $g^{\mu\nu}u_\mu u_\nu = -c^2$.) The trace-reversed field equations for dust involve $T_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}Tg_{\mu\nu} = \rho u_\mu u_\nu + \frac{1}{2}\rho c^2 g_{\mu\nu}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

The Einstein-Hilbert action is $S_{\mathrm{EH}} = \frac{c^4}{16\pi G}\int R\,\sqrt{-g}\,d^4x$, where $g = \det(g_{\mu\nu})$. Show that varying $S_{\mathrm{EH}}$ with respect to the metric yields the vacuum Einstein equations $G_{\mu\nu} = 0$ (ignoring boundary terms).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The variational derivative of $\int R\sqrt{-g}\,d^4x$ with respect to $g^{\mu\nu}$ is $(R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu})\sqrt{-g}$, up to a total divergence. You need not derive this from scratch; state the key intermediate results.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The variation of the scalar curvature is $\delta R = (R_{\mu\nu} - \nabla_\rho\nabla^\rho g_{\mu\nu} + \nabla_\mu\nabla_\nu) \delta g^{\mu\nu}$. The variation of the volume element is $\delta\sqrt{-g} = -\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{-g}\,g_{\mu\nu}\,\delta g^{\mu\nu}$. Combining:

$$
\delta(R\sqrt{-g}) = \sqrt{-g}\Big(R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2}R\,g_{\mu\nu}\Big)\delta g^{\mu\nu} + \sqrt{-g}(\nabla_\rho\nabla^\rho g_{\mu\nu} - \nabla_\mu\nabla_\nu)\delta g^{\mu\nu}.
$$

The second group of terms is a total divergence (it integrates to a boundary term, which vanishes for appropriate boundary conditions). Setting the remaining coefficient to zero:

$$
\frac{c^4}{16\pi G}\sqrt{-g}\,G_{\mu\nu} = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad G_{\mu\nu} = 0.
$$

This is the vacuum Einstein equation. Adding a matter action $S_m = \int \mathcal{L}_m\sqrt{-g}\,d^4x$ and defining $T_{\mu\nu} = -\frac{2}{\sqrt{-g}}\frac{\delta S_m}{\delta g^{\mu\nu}}$ recovers the full EFE.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Starting from the definition of the Riemann tensor in terms of the Christoffel symbols and the definition of the Ricci tensor as its contraction, show that $\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = 0$ follows from the (second) Bianchi identity $\nabla_\lambda R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} + \nabla_\rho R_{\sigma\lambda\mu\nu} + \nabla_\sigma R_{\lambda\rho\mu\nu} = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Contract the Bianchi identity on $\lambda$ and $\mu$ to get an identity involving $\nabla^\mu R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu}$. Then contract again on $\rho$ and $\nu$ to relate to the divergence of the Ricci tensor and the gradient of the scalar curvature.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Contract $\nabla_\lambda R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} + \nabla_\rho R_{\sigma\lambda\mu\nu} + \nabla_\sigma R_{\lambda\rho\mu\nu} = 0$ on indices $\lambda$ and $\mu$:

$$
\nabla^\mu R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} + \nabla_\rho R_{\sigma\mu}{}^\mu{}_\nu - \nabla_\sigma R_{\mu\rho}{}^\mu{}_\nu = 0.
$$

Renaming and using $R_{\sigma\mu}{}^\mu{}_\nu = R_{\sigma\nu}$:

$$
\nabla^\mu R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} + \nabla_\rho R_{\sigma\nu} - \nabla_\sigma R_{\rho\nu} = 0.
$$

Now contract on $\rho$ and $\nu$ using $g^{\rho\nu}$:

$$
g^{\rho\nu}\nabla^\mu R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} + \nabla^\nu R_{\sigma\nu} - \nabla_\sigma R = 0.
$$

The first term: $g^{\rho\nu}\nabla^\mu R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu} = \nabla^\mu(g^{\rho\nu}R_{\rho\sigma\mu\nu}) = \nabla^\mu R^\nu{}_{\sigma\mu\nu} = -\nabla^\mu R_{\sigma\mu} = -\nabla^\mu R_{\mu\sigma}$ (by antisymmetry and symmetry of $R_{\mu\nu}$). So:

$$
-\nabla^\mu R_{\mu\sigma} + \nabla^\nu R_{\sigma\nu} - \nabla_\sigma R = 0.
$$

The first two terms cancel, leaving $\nabla_\sigma R = 2\nabla^\mu R_{\mu\sigma}$. Now compute:

$$
\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = \nabla^\mu R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2}(\nabla_\nu R)\,g_{\mu\nu}\,g^{\mu\nu}\cdot\tfrac{1}{4}\cdot 4 = \nabla^\mu R_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2}\nabla_\nu R.
$$

Wait -- more carefully: $\nabla^\mu(R\,g_{\mu\nu}) = (\nabla^\mu R)g_{\mu\nu} = \nabla_\nu R$. So $\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = \nabla^\mu R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}\nabla_\nu R$. Using $\nabla_\nu R = 2\nabla^\mu R_{\mu\nu}$ from the contracted Bianchi identity, $\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = \nabla^\mu R_{\mu\nu} - \nabla^\mu R_{\mu\nu} = 0$. $\blacksquare$

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

In the weak-field limit, write $g_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu} + h_{\mu\nu}$ with $|h_{\mu\nu}| \ll 1$. Show that for a static source ($T_{tt} = \rho c^2$, all other components negligible) the $tt$ component of the linearised Einstein equations gives Poisson's equation $\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G\rho$, where $h_{tt} = -2\Phi/c^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

To first order in $h$, the Ricci tensor is $R_{\mu\nu} \approx \frac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu\partial^\alpha h_{\alpha\nu} + \partial_\nu\partial^\alpha h_{\alpha\mu} - \Box h_{\mu\nu} - \partial_\mu\partial_\nu h)$ where $h = \eta^{\mu\nu}h_{\mu\nu}$ and $\Box = \eta^{\alpha\beta}\partial_\alpha\partial_\beta$. For a static field, drop all time derivatives.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a static perturbation, $\partial_t h_{\mu\nu} = 0$, so $\Box h_{tt} \to -\nabla^2 h_{tt}$. The trace-reversed field equation for the $tt$ component gives (to linear order, with $\Lambda = 0$):

$$
\bar{R}_{tt} \;=\; \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}\Big(T_{tt} - \tfrac{1}{2}T\,\eta_{tt}\Big).
$$

For dust ($p = 0$, $T = -\rho c^2$ in the mostly-plus convention), $T_{tt} - \frac{1}{2}T\eta_{tt} = \rho c^2 - \frac{1}{2}(-\rho c^2)(-1) = \rho c^2 - \frac{1}{2}\rho c^2 = \frac{1}{2}\rho c^2$.

For a static, spatially-localised perturbation with $h_{\mu\nu}$ diagonal and $h_{ij} = 0$ for $i,j = 1,2,3$ (the Newtonian gauge), the linearised Ricci tensor gives $R_{tt} \approx -\frac{1}{2}\nabla^2 h_{tt}$. So:

$$
-\tfrac{1}{2}\nabla^2 h_{tt} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4} \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}\rho c^2 = \frac{4\pi G}{c^2}\rho.
$$

Setting $h_{tt} = -2\Phi/c^2$:

$$
-\tfrac{1}{2}\nabla^2(-2\Phi/c^2) = \frac{4\pi G}{c^2}\rho \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \frac{1}{c^2}\nabla^2\Phi = \frac{4\pi G}{c^2}\rho \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G\rho.
$$

This is Poisson's equation for the Newtonian gravitational potential. The constant $8\pi G/c^4$ in the EFE was fixed by requiring exactly this match.

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

State Lovelock's theorem and explain why it implies that in four spacetime dimensions, the Einstein tensor is the unique symmetric divergence-free $(0,2)$-tensor derivable from the metric and its first two derivatives, without higher derivatives or nonlinear-in-second-derivative terms.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Lovelock's theorem (1971) constrains the form of the Euler-Lagrange equations derived from a Lagrangian that is a function of the metric and its derivatives, in arbitrary dimension. In dimension $n = 4$, the result is very restrictive.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Lovelock's theorem.** In a four-dimensional spacetime, the only tensor $A_{\mu\nu}$ that is (i) symmetric, (ii) a function of the metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ and its first and second derivatives (but not higher), (iii) linear in second derivatives of $g_{\mu\nu}$, and (iv) divergence-free ($\nabla^\mu A_{\mu\nu} = 0$), is

$$
A_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \alpha\,G_{\mu\nu} + \beta\,g_{\mu\nu}
$$

for constants $\alpha$ and $\beta$.

The proof proceeds by constructing the most general such tensor from the metric and its derivatives. In $n$ dimensions, the $k$-th Lovelock tensor is built from the $k$-fold antisymmetrised product of the Riemann tensor. For $n = 4$, the zeroth Lovelock tensor is $g_{\mu\nu}$ and the first Lovelock tensor is $G_{\mu\nu}$. The second Lovelock tensor (the Gauss-Bonnet tensor) vanishes identically in four dimensions because the relevant $5$-index antisymmetrisation $R_{[\rho\sigma\mu\nu}\delta^{\alpha}_\lambda]$ has no independent components when $n \leq 4$. Higher Lovelock tensors also vanish. So the most general divergence-free symmetric tensor linear in second derivatives is $\alpha G_{\mu\nu} + \beta g_{\mu\nu}$.

The constant $\beta$ plays the role of the cosmological constant $\Lambda$. Fixing $\alpha = 1$ (a normalisation choice) and matching to the Newtonian limit determines the coupling to $8\pi G/c^4$. This is why the Einstein equations have the form they do: not because Einstein guessed correctly, but because no other form is possible given the requirements of symmetry, covariance, and the order of derivatives.

</details>

</aside>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The Einstein field equations sit atop a chain of formalised prerequisites that Mathlib does not yet provide. The chain is:

1. **Pseudo-Riemannian (Lorentzian) metrics.** Mathlib has positive-definite Riemannian metrics but not indefinite-signature pseudo-Riemannian metrics as a distinct structure. The signature constraint $(-,+,+,+)$ is essential for GR.

2. **Levi-Civita connection.** The unique torsion-free metric-compatible connection on a pseudo-Riemannian manifold. Mathlib has affine connections on vector bundles but not the Levi-Civita connection of a metric.

3. **Curvature tensors.** The Riemann tensor $R^\rho{}_{\sigma\mu\nu}$, its contractions (Ricci tensor $R_{\mu\nu}$, scalar curvature $R$), and the Einstein tensor $G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}Rg_{\mu\nu}$. None of these exist in Mathlib.

4. **Contracted Bianchi identity.** The theorem $\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = 0$ is a formal statement about the curvature tensors that would need to be proved from the definitions.

5. **Einstein-Hilbert action.** The variational principle $\delta \int R\sqrt{-g}\,d^4x = \int G_{\mu\nu}\sqrt{-g}\,\delta g^{\mu\nu}\,d^4x$ connects the Lagrangian formulation to the tensor equation.

A natural formalisation target is: given a pseudo-Riemannian manifold $(M, g)$, define the Einstein tensor and prove it is divergence-free. The Einstein field equations themselves would then be a definition (a structure parametrised by $T_{\mu\nu}$ and $\Lambda$) rather than a theorem.

## Advanced results [Master]

### The Einstein-Hilbert action and the Palatini formalism

The Einstein field equations can be derived from a variational principle. The **Einstein-Hilbert action** is

$$
S_{\mathrm{EH}} \;=\; \frac{c^4}{16\pi G}\int_M R\,\sqrt{-g}\,d^4x,
$$

where $R$ is the scalar curvature and $\sqrt{-g}$ is the square root of the negative of the metric determinant (the volume element). Varying $S_{\mathrm{EH}}$ with respect to $g^{\mu\nu}$ and discarding boundary terms (or adding the Gibbons-Hawking-York boundary term) yields $G_{\mu\nu} = 0$.

The **Palatini formalism** treats the connection $\Gamma^\rho_{\mu\nu}$ and the metric $g_{\mu\nu}$ as independent variables. Varying the action $\int g^{\mu\nu}R_{\mu\nu}(\Gamma)\sqrt{-g}\,d^4x$ with respect to $\Gamma$ yields the metric-compatibility condition $\nabla_\rho g_{\mu\nu} = 0$ (which identifies $\Gamma$ as the Levi-Civita connection), and varying with respect to $g^{\mu\nu}$ then yields the Einstein equations. The Palatini approach is used in $f(R)$ theories of gravity and in the first-order formulation of GR used in loop quantum gravity.

Adding the cosmological constant modifies the action by a term $\frac{c^4\Lambda}{8\pi G}\int\sqrt{-g}\,d^4x$, which is a constant multiple of the four-volume. Adding matter via $S_m = \int\mathcal{L}_m\sqrt{-g}\,d^4x$ defines the stress-energy tensor as $T_{\mu\nu} = -\frac{2}{\sqrt{-g}}\frac{\delta S_m}{\delta g^{\mu\nu}}$.

### Lovelock gravity and the dimensionality constraint

Lovelock's 1971 theorem generalises the Einstein tensor to arbitrary dimension. The $k$-th Lovelock tensor is

$$
L^{(k)}_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \frac{1}{2^k}\delta^{\alpha_1\beta_1\cdots\alpha_k\beta_k}_{\mu\nu\rho_1\sigma_1\cdots\rho_{k-1}\sigma_{k-1}} \prod_{i=1}^k R^{\rho_i\sigma_i}{}_{\alpha_i\beta_i},
$$

where $\delta^{\cdots}_{\cdots}$ is the generalised Kronecker delta. For $k = 0$, $L^{(0)}_{\mu\nu} = g_{\mu\nu}$. For $k = 1$, $L^{(1)}_{\mu\nu} = G_{\mu\nu}$. For $k \geq 2$, the Lovelock tensor vanishes in $n < 2k + 2$ dimensions because the antisymmetrisation over more indices than the dimension has no independent components.

In four dimensions, only $k = 0$ and $k = 1$ survive, giving $A_{\mu\nu} = \alpha G_{\mu\nu} + \beta g_{\mu\nu}$. In five dimensions, $k = 2$ (the Gauss-Bonnet term) is nonvanishing and contributes to the field equations. The Gauss-Bonnet Lagrangian $R^2 - 4R_{\mu\nu}R^{\mu\nu} + R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}R^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}$ is a topological invariant in four dimensions (contributing a total divergence to the action) but is dynamical in five and higher dimensions.

### Energy conditions

The EFE alone does not determine $T_{\mu\nu}$ -- that depends on the matter model. **Energy conditions** are constraints on $T_{\mu\nu}$ that codify physically reasonable matter:

- **Weak energy condition (WEC):** $T_{\mu\nu}t^\mu t^\nu \geq 0$ for all timelike $t^\mu$. Every observer measures non-negative energy density.
- **Null energy condition (NEC):** $T_{\mu\nu}k^\mu k^\nu \geq 0$ for all null $k^\mu$. The weakest standard condition.
- **Strong energy condition (SEC):** $(T_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}Tg_{\mu\nu})t^\mu t^\nu \geq 0$ for all timelike $t^\mu$. Through the EFE, this requires $R_{\mu\nu}t^\mu t^\nu \geq 0$ -- gravity is always attractive.
- **Dominant energy condition (DEC):** $T_{\mu\nu}t^\mu$ is a non-spacelike, future-directed vector for every future-directed timelike $t^\mu$. Energy does not flow faster than light.

The singularity theorems of Hawking and Penrose assume the SEC or NEC. The observed acceleration of the cosmic expansion (which requires $\ddot{a} > 0$ in the Friedmann equations) violates the SEC, indicating that either the cosmological constant or dark energy provides a form of stress-energy that does not satisfy this condition. The NEC is the one energy condition not observationally violated.

### The initial-value formulation

The EFE constitute 10 coupled nonlinear PDEs for $g_{\mu\nu}$. The initial-value formulation (due to Lichnerowicz, Choquet-Bruhat, and others) casts them as a Cauchy problem. On a spacelike hypersurface $\Sigma$, specify the induced metric $\gamma_{ij}$ and the extrinsic curvature $K_{ij}$ (which encodes how $\Sigma$ is embedded in spacetime). The Einstein equations split into:

- **Constraint equations:** four equations ($G_{\mu\nu}n^\mu n^\nu = 8\pi G\,T_{\mu\nu}n^\mu n^\nu$ and three analogous equations) that the initial data $(\gamma_{ij}, K_{ij})$ must satisfy on $\Sigma$.
- **Evolution equations:** six second-order PDEs that determine how $(\gamma_{ij}, K_{ij})$ evolve off $\Sigma$.

The remaining four degrees of freedom correspond to coordinate choices (gauge freedom). Choquet-Bruhat (1952) proved local existence and uniqueness of solutions for suitably smooth initial data. Choquet-Bruhat and Geroch (1969) proved the existence of a unique maximal globally hyperbolic development for any admissible initial data.

## Connections [Master]

- **Riemann curvature tensor [13.03.01]:** The Einstein tensor $G_{\mu\nu} = R_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}Rg_{\mu\nu}$ is constructed from contractions of the Riemann tensor. The field equations are a statement about curvature, and every specific solution (Schwarzschild, Kerr, FLRW) is found by computing the Riemann tensor for a metric ansatz and imposing $G_{\mu\nu} = (8\pi G/c^4)T_{\mu\nu}$.

- **Tensors on manifolds [13.02.01]:** The field equation $G_{\mu\nu} = (8\pi G/c^4)T_{\mu\nu}$ is a tensor equation between two $(0,2)$-tensor fields. Its coordinate independence follows from the tensoriality of both sides.

- **Thermodynamics and the stress-energy tensor [11.01.01]:** $T_{\mu\nu}$ encodes the density, flux, and pressure of matter and energy. Its form depends on the matter model (perfect fluid, electromagnetic field, scalar field, vacuum energy). The divergence-free condition $\nabla^\mu T_{\mu\nu} = 0$ is the general-relativistic statement of energy-momentum conservation.

- **Schwarzschild solution [13.05.01]:** The simplest non-flat solution of the vacuum Einstein equations $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$. The Schwarzschild metric describes spacetime outside any static, spherically symmetric mass distribution.

- **Cosmology [13.08.01]:** Feeding the FLRW metric ansatz (spatially homogeneous and isotropic) into the Einstein equations yields the Friedmann equations governing the scale factor $a(t)$ of the universe.

- **Newton's laws [09.01.02]:** Newton's law of gravitation $F = GMm/r^2$ and Poisson's equation $\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G\rho$ are the non-relativistic limits of the Einstein field equations. The EFE reduce to Poisson's equation in the weak-field, slow-motion regime.

- **Special relativity [10.05.01]:** Minkowski spacetime ($\eta_{\mu\nu}$) is a vacuum solution of the Einstein equations with $\Lambda = 0$. SR is the local physics in each tangent space of a GR spacetime.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Einstein presented the field equations to the Prussian Academy of Sciences in Berlin on 25 November 1915, in the paper "Die Feldgleichungen der Gravitation." He had been pursuing a theory of gravitation based on the curvature of spacetime since 1907, when the equivalence principle first suggested that gravity is a feature of geometry. The path from the EP to the field equations took eight years.

The period from 1912 to 1915 was one of intense struggle. Einstein and Grossmann published the "Entwurf" theory in 1913, which had the correct geometric picture (gravity = curvature of a four-dimensional manifold) but the wrong field equations. Einstein spent two years trying to fix the Entwurf equations before realising, in the autumn of 1915, that the correct equations were obtained from the Ricci tensor and the Ricci scalar. He published several versions in November 1915 before arriving at the final form on 25 November.

David Hilbert, working independently in Gottingen, derived the field equations from a variational principle (the Einstein-Hilbert action) and submitted his paper five days before Einstein's presentation. The priority question has been debated extensively; the consensus, established by Corry, Renn, and Stachel (1997), is that Hilbert's first submission contained a different (incorrect) form of the equations and was revised after seeing Einstein's final paper. Einstein and Hilbert corresponded during November 1915 and each acknowledged the other's contribution. There is no real priority dispute: Einstein had the physical insight and the correct equations first; Hilbert had the elegant variational derivation.

The Newtonian limit was the decisive check. On 18 November 1915, Einstein used his (nearly final) field equations to compute the perihelion precession of Mercury and obtained $43$ arcseconds per century -- exactly the observed anomaly. He later recounted that this calculation gave him heart palpitations. The correct prediction of Mercury's orbit was the first empirical triumph of the theory, decades before the solar-eclipse light-bending test and the gravitational-redshift measurement.

The cosmological constant $\Lambda$ was introduced by Einstein in 1917 to allow a static universe (the Einstein static universe). After Hubble's 1929 discovery of cosmic expansion, Einstein abandoned $\Lambda$, reportedly calling it his "greatest blunder." The discovery of cosmic acceleration in 1998 (Perlmutter, Riess, Schmidt) revived $\Lambda$ as the simplest explanation, and $\Lambda > 0$ is now a central feature of the standard cosmological model. The story illustrates that what Einstein considered a mistake was in fact a correct and necessary term -- the cosmological constant is the vacuum energy, and its value is determined by observation, not by theoretical prejudice.

The initial-value formulation was developed by Lichnerowicz (1944) and Choquet-Bruhat (1952), establishing that the Einstein equations constitute a well-posed hyperbolic system. The global existence theory (Choquet-Bruhat and Geroch 1969) confirms that admissible initial data determine a unique maximal development -- a remarkable result for a system of nonlinear equations with gauge freedom.
