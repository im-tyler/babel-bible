## Intuition [Beginner]

When two black holes spiral into each other, the collision sends ripples through spacetime itself. These ripples are gravitational waves. They are not waves travelling *through* spacetime the way sound travels through air. They are changes *of* spacetime curvature that propagate outward at the speed of light.

Einstein predicted gravitational waves in 1916, just one year after publishing his field equations. But the effect is so small that it took a century to detect one. On 14 September 2015, the LIGO observatory measured a distortion in spacetime caused by two black holes, each about 30 times the mass of the Sun, merging roughly 400 million light-years away. The measured strain -- the fractional change in distance between two points -- was about $10^{-21}$. That means a one-metre ruler changed length by one billionth of a billionth of a millimetre.

Gravitational waves have two polarizations, called plus ($h_+$) and cross ($h_\times$), named for the shape they impose on a ring of freely falling particles. A plus-polarized wave stretches the ring along one axis and compresses it along the perpendicular axis, then reverses. A cross-polarized wave does the same thing rotated by 45 degrees. This is analogous to electromagnetic waves having two transverse polarizations.

The power source for gravitational waves is the acceleration of mass. But not just any acceleration produces them. A perfectly spherical explosion radiates zero gravitational waves. What matters is the *asymmetry* of the mass distribution, captured by the quadrupole moment. Binary systems -- two stars or two black holes orbiting each other -- are efficient gravitational-wave emitters because their mass distribution changes dramatically and asymmetrically with each orbit.

## Visual [Beginner]

The key point: no force pushes the particles. Each particle moves inertially along its own geodesic. The wave changes the geometry of spacetime between them, which changes the *measured* distance between freely moving objects. This is why LIGO uses laser interferometry -- it measures the change in distance between two mirrors, each freely suspended, as the wave passes.

## Worked example [Beginner]

On 14 September 2015, LIGO detected gravitational waves from two black holes merging. This event is called GW150914. The two black holes had masses of approximately 36 and 29 times the mass of the Sun. They merged at a distance of roughly 400 Mpc (about 1.3 billion light-years) from Earth.

The peak strain measured at Earth was $h \approx 10^{-21}$. What does this number mean in physical terms?

LIGO's arms are 4 km long. A strain of $10^{-21}$ means the arm length changed by $\Delta L = h \times L = 10^{-21} \times 4000\text{ m} = 4 \times 10^{-18}\text{ m}$. This is less than one thousandth of the diameter of a proton.

The signal swept upward in frequency from about 35 Hz to 250 Hz over roughly 0.2 seconds, then rang down. This "chirp" pattern -- rising frequency and amplitude followed by a quick decay -- matches the prediction for two black holes spiralling together, merging, and ringing down to a single black hole. The final black hole had a mass of about 62 solar masses. The missing 3 solar masses ($36 + 29 - 62 = 3$) were radiated as gravitational-wave energy in a fraction of a second, briefly outshining the entire visible universe in gravitational-wave luminosity.

The peak luminosity can be estimated. Three solar masses of energy ($3 \times 2 \times 10^{30}\text{ kg} \times (3 \times 10^8)^2 \approx 5.4 \times 10^{47}\text{ J}$) radiated in about 0.01 seconds gives a power of order $5 \times 10^{49}\text{ W}$. For comparison, the Sun's electromagnetic luminosity is $3.8 \times 10^{26}\text{ W}$. The merger outshone all the stars in the observable universe combined by a factor of roughly $10^{23}$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Consider a spacetime whose metric deviates only slightly from the flat Minkowski metric $\eta_{\mu\nu}$. Write

$$
g_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu} + h_{\mu\nu}, \qquad |h_{\mu\nu}| \ll 1.
$$

The symmetric tensor field $h_{\mu\nu}$ is the **metric perturbation**. All computations are carried out to first order in $h$ (the **linearized theory**); products $h \cdot h$ and higher powers are discarded.

### The trace-reversed perturbation

Define the **trace-reversed perturbation**

$$
\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} \;:=\; h_{\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{2}\eta_{\mu\nu}\,h, \qquad h := \eta^{\alpha\beta}h_{\alpha\beta}.
$$

This satisfies $h_{\mu\nu} = \bar{h}_{\mu\nu} - \frac{1}{2}\eta_{\mu\nu}\bar{h}$, where $\bar{h} = \eta^{\mu\nu}\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = -h$. The trace-reversal simplifies the linearized Einstein equations.

### The linearized Einstein equations

To first order in $h$, the Ricci tensor is

$$
R_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\big(\partial_\mu\partial^\alpha h_{\alpha\nu} + \partial_\nu\partial^\alpha h_{\alpha\mu} - \Box h_{\mu\nu} - \partial_\mu\partial_\nu h\big),
$$

where $\Box := \eta^{\alpha\beta}\partial_\alpha\partial_\beta$ is the flat-space d'Alembertian and indices are raised and lowered with $\eta$. The linearized Einstein tensor is $G_{\mu\nu}^{(1)} = R_{\mu\nu}^{(1)} - \frac{1}{2}\eta_{\mu\nu}R^{(1)}$, and the linearized field equations are

$$
G_{\mu\nu}^{(1)} \;=\; \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}T_{\mu\nu}.
$$

### The Lorenz gauge

The linearized equations simplify in the **Lorenz gauge** (also called the de Donder gauge), defined by

$$
\partial^\mu\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} \;=\; 0.
$$

This condition can always be achieved by an infinitesimal coordinate transformation $x^\mu \to x^\mu + \xi^\mu$ with $|\xi| \ll 1$, which changes $h_{\mu\nu}$ by $h_{\mu\nu} \to h_{\mu\nu} - \partial_\mu\xi_\nu - \partial_\nu\xi_\mu$. In Lorenz gauge the linearized Einstein equations become

$$
\boxed{\;\;\Box\,\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} \;=\; -\frac{16\pi G}{c^4}\,T_{\mu\nu}.\;\;}
$$

This is an inhomogeneous wave equation for each component of $\bar{h}_{\mu\nu}$, with source $-16\pi G\,T_{\mu\nu}/c^4$.

### The transverse-traceless gauge

In vacuum ($T_{\mu\nu} = 0$), the equation becomes $\Box\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = 0$: each component satisfies the free wave equation. Further gauge freedom (residual after imposing the Lorenz condition) can be used to impose the **transverse-traceless** (TT) conditions:

$$
h^{TT}_{0\mu} = 0, \qquad \partial^j h^{TT}_{ij} = 0, \qquad \delta^{ij}h^{TT}_{ij} = 0.
$$

In this gauge the perturbation has only two independent spatial components, corresponding to the two gravitational-wave polarizations $h_+$ and $h_\times$:

$$
h^{TT}_{ij}(t, z) \;=\; \begin{pmatrix} h_+ & h_\times & 0 \\ h_\times & -h_+ & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\cos\!\big(\omega(t - z/c)\big),
$$

for a plane wave propagating in the $z$-direction with angular frequency $\omega$.

### Counterexamples and common slips

- Gravitational waves are not "ripples in space." They are changes in spacetime curvature -- changes in the proper distance between freely falling test particles, not displacements of particles through a fixed background.
- The strain $h \sim 10^{-21}$ does not mean objects physically move by that amount. It means the spacetime metric changes by one part in $10^{21}$. A solid ruler resists this change; only freely suspended test masses (like LIGO's mirrors) reflect the full strain.
- The linearized theory is an approximation valid when $|h_{\mu\nu}| \ll 1$. Near a black hole merger, $|h_{\mu\nu}| \sim 1$ and the full nonlinear equations are needed. Linearized theory describes wave propagation far from the source and the emission from weakly gravitating systems.
- The Lorenz gauge does not fully fix the gauge. It imposes four conditions ($\partial^\mu\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = 0$) on ten components, leaving residual freedom that is used to reach the TT gauge.
- Gravitational waves carry energy, momentum, and angular momentum. This was debated for decades (the "pseudotensor" problem) but is established rigorously by the Isaacson effective stress-energy tensor and confirmed by the Hulse-Taylor pulsar orbital decay.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Quadrupole formula).** *A slowly-moving, weakly-self-gravitating source with mass density $\rho$ and mass quadrupole moment*

$$
Q_{ij}(t) \;:=\; \int \rho(t, \mathbf{x})\big(x_i x_j - \tfrac{1}{3}\delta_{ij}r^2\big)\,d^3x
$$

*radiates gravitational waves. In the transverse-traceless gauge, the perturbation in the **radiation zone** (distance $r$ much larger than the source size and the reduced wavelength) is*

$$
h^{TT}_{ij}(t, \mathbf{x}) \;=\; \frac{2G}{c^4\,r}\,\ddot{Q}^{TT}_{ij}\!\big(t - r/c\big),
$$

*where the double dot denotes the second time derivative and the superscript $TT$ denotes projection onto the transverse-traceless components. The total power (luminosity) radiated is*

$$
\boxed{\;\;P \;=\; \frac{G}{5c^5}\,\Big\langle \dddot{Q}_{ij}\,\dddot{Q}^{ij} \Big\rangle,\;\;}
$$

*where angle brackets denote a time average over several wave periods and triple dots denote the third time derivative.*

**Proof (sketch).** The strategy has three steps.

*Step 1: Solve the wave equation with a Green's function.* The linearized equation $\Box\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = -16\pi G\,T_{\mu\nu}/c^4$ is solved by the retarded potential:

$$
\bar{h}_{\mu\nu}(t, \mathbf{x}) \;=\; \frac{4G}{c^4}\int \frac{T_{\mu\nu}(t - |\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}'|/c,\;\mathbf{x}')}{|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}'|}\,d^3x'.
$$

*Step 2: Expand in the radiation zone.* At distance $r = |\mathbf{x}|$ much larger than the source size $d$, approximate $|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}'| \approx r - \hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \mathbf{x}'$ where $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \mathbf{x}/r$. For a slowly-moving source ($v \ll c$), the relevant components of $T_{\mu\nu}$ are dominated by $T_{tt} = \rho c^2$, and the spatial components of $\bar{h}_{\mu\nu}$ involve the second time derivative of the mass quadrupole moment:

$$
\bar{h}_{ij}(t, \mathbf{x}) \;=\; \frac{2G}{c^4\,r}\,\ddot{Q}_{ij}(t_{\rm ret}),
$$

where $t_{\rm ret} = t - r/c$ is the retarded time. The factor $1/c^4$ (not $1/c^2$ as in electromagnetism) reflects that gravity is a spin-2 field: the source is the quadrupole moment, not the dipole moment.

*Step 3: Compute the radiated power via the Isaacson tensor.* The effective stress-energy tensor for gravitational waves, averaged over several wavelengths, is

$$
t_{\mu\nu}^{\rm GW} \;=\; \frac{c^4}{32\pi G}\big\langle \partial_\mu h^{TT}_{ij}\,\partial_\nu h^{ij}_{TT}\big\rangle.
$$

The energy flux in the radial direction is $c\,t_{0r}^{\rm GW}$. Integrating over a sphere of radius $r$ and substituting the quadrupole expression for $h^{TT}_{ij}$ gives

$$
P \;=\; \frac{G}{5c^5}\,\Big\langle \dddot{Q}_{ij}\,\dddot{Q}^{ij} \Big\rangle.
$$

The factor $1/c^5$ (not $1/c^3$ as in the electromagnetic dipole formula) means gravitational radiation is extraordinarily weak for solar-system sources. For binary systems, $\dddot{Q} \sim M R^2 \omega^3$ where $M$ is the total mass, $R$ the separation, and $\omega$ the orbital frequency. Substituting Kepler's law $\omega^2 = GM/R^3$ gives $P \propto (M/R)^5$, which diverges as the binary shrinks -- producing the dramatic chirp observed by LIGO. $\blacksquare$

### Application to a circular binary

For a binary with masses $m_1, m_2$, total mass $M = m_1 + m_2$, symmetric mass ratio $\eta = m_1 m_2 / M^2$, and orbital separation $R$, the quadrupole formula gives the power

$$
P \;=\; \frac{32}{5}\,\frac{G^4}{c^5}\,\frac{m_1^2 m_2^2\,M}{R^5}.
$$

Energy loss causes the orbit to shrink: $dE/dt = -P$. The orbital energy is $E = -Gm_1 m_2 / (2R)$, so $dR/dt = -64G^3 m_1 m_2 M / (5c^5 R^3)$. Integrating gives the **chirp time** -- the time until merger from a given separation:

$$
\tau \;=\; \frac{5}{256}\,\frac{c^5\,R^4}{G^3\,m_1\,m_2\,M}.
$$

For GW150914, the binary entered LIGO's sensitive band ($\sim 35$ Hz, corresponding to $R \approx 350$ km) approximately 0.2 seconds before merger. The predicted chirp time at this separation, for $m_1 = 36 M_\odot$, $m_2 = 29 M_\odot$, is consistent with the observed 0.2-second inspiral signal.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that in vacuum ($T_{\mu\nu} = 0$), the linearized Einstein equations in Lorenz gauge reduce to the wave equation $\Box\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In Lorenz gauge, $\partial^\mu\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = 0$. Write out the linearized Ricci tensor in terms of $\bar{h}_{\mu\nu}$ and use the gauge condition to simplify.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The linearized Ricci tensor in terms of $\bar{h}$ is $R_{\mu\nu}^{(1)} = -\frac{1}{2}\Box\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} + \frac{1}{2}\partial_\mu\partial^\alpha\bar{h}_{\alpha\nu} + \frac{1}{2}\partial_\nu\partial^\alpha\bar{h}_{\alpha\mu}$. The Lorenz gauge condition $\partial^\alpha\bar{h}_{\alpha\nu} = 0$ kills the last two terms, leaving $R_{\mu\nu}^{(1)} = -\frac{1}{2}\Box\bar{h}_{\mu\nu}$. The linearized field equations in vacuum give $-\frac{1}{2}\Box\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = 0$, hence $\Box\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Verify that an infinitesimal coordinate transformation $x^\mu \to x^\mu + \xi^\mu$ changes the perturbation by $h_{\mu\nu} \to h_{\mu\nu} - \partial_\mu\xi_\nu - \partial_\nu\xi_\mu$ to first order.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Under $x^\mu \to x^\mu + \xi^\mu$, the metric transforms as a rank-$(0,2)$ tensor. Write $g'_{\mu\nu}(x') = \partial x^\alpha/\partial x'^\mu \cdot \partial x^\beta/\partial x'^\nu \cdot g_{\alpha\beta}(x)$ and expand to first order in $\xi$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Jacobian is $\partial x^\alpha / \partial x'^\mu = \delta^\alpha_\mu - \partial_\mu\xi^\alpha$ to first order. So

$$
g'_{\mu\nu}(x') = (\delta^\alpha_\mu - \partial_\mu\xi^\alpha)(\delta^\beta_\nu - \partial_\nu\xi^\beta)\,g_{\alpha\beta}(x) = g_{\mu\nu}(x) - \partial_\mu\xi_\nu - \partial_\nu\xi_\mu + O(\xi^2).
$$

Since $g_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu} + h_{\mu\nu}$ and $g'_{\mu\nu} = \eta_{\mu\nu} + h'_{\mu\nu}$, the new perturbation is $h'_{\mu\nu} = h_{\mu\nu} - \partial_\mu\xi_\nu - \partial_\nu\xi_\mu$. The Minkowski metric is unchanged because $\eta_{\mu\nu}$ is constant. This is the **gauge transformation** in linearized gravity, analogous to the gauge transformation $A_\mu \to A_\mu + \partial_\mu\lambda$ in electromagnetism.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the Lorenz gauge condition $\partial^\mu\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = 0$ can always be imposed. Specifically, given an arbitrary $h_{\mu\nu}$ with $\partial^\mu\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = f_\nu \neq 0$, find a gauge vector $\xi^\mu$ such that the transformed $\bar{h}'_{\mu\nu}$ satisfies $\partial^\mu\bar{h}'_{\mu\nu} = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute how $\partial^\mu\bar{h}_{\mu\nu}$ changes under a gauge transformation. The trace-reversed perturbation transforms as $\bar{h}'_{\mu\nu} = \bar{h}_{\mu\nu} - \partial_\mu\xi_\nu - \partial_\nu\xi_\mu + \eta_{\mu\nu}\partial^\alpha\xi_\alpha$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The trace-reversed perturbation transforms as $\bar{h}'_{\mu\nu} = \bar{h}_{\mu\nu} - \partial_\mu\xi_\nu - \partial_\nu\xi_\mu + \eta_{\mu\nu}\partial^\alpha\xi_\alpha$. Taking the divergence:

$$
\partial^\mu\bar{h}'_{\mu\nu} = \partial^\mu\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} - \Box\xi_\nu - \partial_\nu(\partial^\mu\xi_\mu) + \partial_\nu(\partial^\alpha\xi_\alpha) = f_\nu - \Box\xi_\nu.
$$

Setting $\partial^\mu\bar{h}'_{\mu\nu} = 0$ requires $\Box\xi_\nu = f_\nu$. This is a wave equation for $\xi_\nu$ with source $f_\nu$. A solution always exists (by the existence theory for the inhomogeneous wave equation). So the Lorenz gauge can always be reached.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

The Hulse-Taylor binary pulsar PSR B1913+16 has orbital period $P_{\rm orb} \approx 7.75$ hours, eccentricity $e \approx 0.617$, and component masses $m_1 \approx 1.44 M_\odot$, $m_2 \approx 1.39 M_\odot$. The observed orbital period derivative is $\dot{P}_{\rm orb} \approx -2.4 \times 10^{-12}\text{ s/s}$. The prediction of general relativity (using the quadrupole formula, with eccentricity corrections from Peters and Mathews) is $\dot{P}_{\rm orb}^{\rm GR} \approx -2.4 \times 10^{-12}\text{ s/s}$. What is the ratio of the observed rate to the GR prediction?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The ratio is simply the observed value divided by the predicted value. The significance is in the agreement to high precision.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Ratio $= \dot{P}_{\rm orb}^{\rm obs} / \dot{P}_{\rm orb}^{\rm GR} = (-2.4 \times 10^{-12}) / (-2.4 \times 10^{-12}) = 1.00$.

The agreement is better than $0.2\%$ over four decades of observation. This was the first indirect detection of gravitational waves, earning Hulse and Taylor the 1993 Nobel Prize. The orbital decay is caused by energy loss to gravitational radiation, exactly as the quadrupole formula predicts. The agreement provides a precision test of GR in the radiative regime, decades before direct detection by LIGO.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

For a circular binary with masses $m_1, m_2$ and separation $R$, compute the quadrupole moment tensor $Q_{ij}$ and its third time derivative $\dddot{Q}_{ij}$. Use the result to verify the quadrupole-formula luminosity $P = (32/5)(G^4/c^5)(m_1^2 m_2^2 M / R^5)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Place the binary in the $x$-$y$ plane with masses at angles $\omega t$ and $\omega t + \pi$. The center of mass is at the origin. Compute the mass density as two delta functions, then evaluate $Q_{ij}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\mu = m_1 m_2 / M$ be the reduced mass. Place $m_1$ at position $(R_1\cos\omega t, R_1\sin\omega t, 0)$ and $m_2$ at $(-R_2\cos\omega t, -R_2\sin\omega t, 0)$, where $R_1 = m_2 R/M$ and $R_2 = m_1 R/M$. The quadrupole moment has nonzero components:

$$
Q_{xx} = \mu R^2(\cos^2\omega t - \tfrac{1}{3}), \quad Q_{yy} = \mu R^2(\sin^2\omega t - \tfrac{1}{3}), \quad Q_{xy} = \mu R^2\sin\omega t\cos\omega t.
$$

The third time derivatives are $\dddot{Q}_{xx} = 4\mu R^2\omega^3\sin 2\omega t$, $\dddot{Q}_{yy} = -4\mu R^2\omega^3\sin 2\omega t$, $\dddot{Q}_{xy} = -4\mu R^2\omega^3\cos 2\omega t$.

Computing $\dddot{Q}_{ij}\dddot{Q}^{ij} = 32\mu^2 R^4\omega^6$. Using Kepler's law $\omega^2 = GM/R^3$:

$$
P = \frac{G}{5c^5}\cdot 32\mu^2 R^4\omega^6 = \frac{32G}{5c^5}\cdot\frac{G^3 m_1^2 m_2^2}{M^2 R^5}\cdot M^2 = \frac{32G^4 m_1^2 m_2^2 M}{5c^5 R^5}.
$$

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the TT projection of a symmetric traceless tensor $A_{ij}$ in the direction $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ can be written as $A_{ij}^{TT} = P_{ik}P_{jl}A_{kl} - \frac{1}{2}P_{ij}P_{kl}A_{kl}$ where $P_{ij} = \delta_{ij} - \hat{n}_i\hat{n}_j$ is the projector onto the plane transverse to $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$. Verify that this satisfies the TT conditions: transverse ($\hat{n}^i A_{ij}^{TT} = 0$) and traceless ($\delta^{ij}A_{ij}^{TT} = 0$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$P_{ij}\hat{n}^j = 0$ and $P_{ij}\delta^{jk}P_{kl} = P_{il}$ (idempotent). Check transversality by contracting with $\hat{n}$, and check tracelessness by contracting with $\delta^{ij}$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the symmetry of $A^{TT}$, also $\hat{n}^j A_{ij}^{TT} = 0$.

**Tracelessness:** $\delta^{ij}A_{ij}^{TT} = \delta^{ij}P_{ik}P_{jl}A_{kl} - \frac{1}{2}\delta^{ij}P_{ij}P_{kl}A_{kl}$. The first term: $\delta^{ij}P_{ik}P_{jl} = P_{jk}P_{jl} = P_{kl}$ (since $P$ is idempotent in 3D: $P_{jk}P_{kl} = P_{jl}$). The trace of $P$ in 3D is $\delta^{ij}P_{ij} = \delta^{ij}(\delta_{ij} - \hat{n}_i\hat{n}_j) = 3 - 1 = 2$. So $\delta^{ij}A_{ij}^{TT} = P_{kl}A_{kl} - \frac{1}{2}\cdot 2 \cdot P_{kl}A_{kl} = P_{kl}A_{kl} - P_{kl}A_{kl} = 0$.

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The linearized Einstein equations and gravitational wave theory sit atop the same prerequisite chain as the full Einstein equations [13.04.01], with additional gaps:

1. **Wave operator on Minkowski space.** The d'Alembertian $\Box = \eta^{\mu\nu}\partial_\mu\partial_\nu$ acting on tensor fields would need to be defined once pseudo-Riemannian geometry is available.

2. **Lorenz gauge and gauge fixing.** The condition $\partial^\mu\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = 0$ and the residual gauge freedom leading to the TT gauge are statements about solutions of a PDE system. Formalising gauge existence would require PDE existence results not currently in Mathlib.

3. **Retarded Green's function.** The solution of the inhomogeneous wave equation by the retarded potential is a standard result in analysis, but Mathlib does not yet have the infrastructure for distributional solutions of the wave equation.

4. **Quadrupole formula.** Deriving the formula from the linearized equations requires: defining the mass quadrupole moment $Q_{ij}$, computing its time derivatives for a given source, performing the far-field expansion of the retarded potential, and integrating the Isaacson effective stress-energy tensor over a sphere. Each step is within the scope of a formalised analysis but requires layers that do not yet exist.

A minimal formalisation target would be: given a perturbation $h_{\mu\nu}$ in Lorenz gauge satisfying $\Box\bar{h}_{\mu\nu} = 0$, prove that the TT-projected perturbation $h_{ij}^{TT}$ has exactly two independent components. This is a linear-algebraic result about the projector $P_{ij}$.

## The Isaacson effective stress-energy tensor [Master]

Gravitational waves carry energy. This was disputed for decades because the gravitational stress-energy "pseudotensor" is coordinate-dependent. Isaacson (1968) resolved this by averaging over several wavelengths, producing a gauge-invariant effective stress-energy tensor.

### Derivation via the shortwave approximation

Write the metric as a slowly varying background $\bar{g}_{\mu\nu}$ plus a rapidly oscillating perturbation $h_{\mu\nu}$ with characteristic wavelength $\lambda$ much shorter than the background curvature scale $L$:

$$
g_{\mu\nu} \;=\; \bar{g}_{\mu\nu} + h_{\mu\nu}, \qquad \lambda \ll L.
$$

Substitute into the vacuum Einstein equations $R_{\mu\nu} = 0$ and expand to second order in $h$. The zeroth-order equation constrains the background: $R^{(0)}_{\mu\nu}[\bar{g}] = 0$. The first-order equation gives the wave equation for $h_{\mu\nu}$ on the background. The second-order equation, averaged over a region of size $\gg\lambda$ but $\ll L$, produces

$$
\bar{G}_{\mu\nu}[\bar{g}] \;=\; 8\pi G\,t_{\mu\nu}^{\rm GW}/c^4,
$$

where the **Isaacson tensor** is

$$
t_{\mu\nu}^{\rm GW} \;=\; \frac{c^4}{32\pi G}\,\Big\langle \nabla_\mu h^{TT}_{ij}\,\nabla_\nu h^{ij}_{TT}\Big\rangle.
$$

The angle brackets denote an average over several wavelengths. This quantity is gauge-invariant (under infinitesimal diffeomorphisms) because the averaging kills terms that are total derivatives. The background curvature "feels" the gravitational waves as an effective matter source, exactly as the electromagnetic field carries energy that curves spacetime.

### Physical consequences

The Isaacson tensor gives the energy flux of a plane gravitational wave propagating in direction $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ as

$$
\frac{dE}{dt\,dA} \;=\; \frac{c^3}{16\pi G}\,\omega^2\big(h_+^2 + h_\times^2\big).
$$

Integrating over a sphere of radius $r$ around the source and using $h \sim (2G/c^4 r)\ddot{Q}$ reproduces the quadrupole luminosity formula. The Isaacson tensor also gives the momentum flux, which produces a gravitational-wave "rocket effect" on asymmetric sources and contributes to the recoil of merged black holes.

## Connections [Master]

- **Einstein field equations [13.04.01]:** Gravitational waves are solutions of the linearized Einstein equations, which are the first-order perturbative expansion of the full nonlinear equations around Minkowski space. The linearized theory is valid where $|h_{\mu\nu}| \ll 1$; the full equations are needed near the source.

- **Riemann curvature tensor [13.03.01]:** A gravitational wave is a propagating perturbation of the Riemann tensor. The linearized Riemann tensor $R^{(1)}_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}$ is nonzero even though the background (Minkowski) Riemann tensor vanishes. The wave carries curvature information.

- **Electromagnetic waves [10.04.02]:** The analogy is instructive and the differences are important. Both satisfy wave equations and have two transverse polarizations. But gravitational waves are spin-2 (source: quadrupole moment) while electromagnetic waves are spin-1 (source: dipole moment). Gravitational waves couple to the metric, not to a vector potential. The gravitational power formula carries $1/c^5$ versus $1/c^3$ for electromagnetism, making gravitational radiation far weaker.

- **Orbits in Schwarzschild geometry [13.05.02]:** The Hulse-Taylor binary pulsar provides a precision test of the quadrupole formula. The observed orbital decay matches the GR prediction to better than $0.2\%$, confirming that orbiting bodies radiate gravitational waves and that the quadrupole formula gives the correct power.

- **Energy conservation in GR [11.01.01]:** Gravitational waves carry energy, which is encoded in the Isaacson effective stress-energy tensor. The energy removed from the binary orbit equals the energy carried away by the waves, consistent with the Bianchi identity $\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = 0$ acting as the conservation law.

- **Cosmology [13.08.01]:** A stochastic background of gravitational waves from the early universe (inflation, phase transitions, cosmic strings) would imprint a signature on the cosmic microwave background polarization (B-modes) and in the low-frequency gravitational-wave spectrum targeted by pulsar timing arrays and the space-based detector LISA.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Einstein predicted gravitational waves in 1916, within a year of publishing the field equations. His paper "Naherungsweise Integration der Feldgleichungen der Gravitation" linearized the field equations and found wave solutions. He initially made an error in the coordinate conditions (using a non-gauge-invariant argument to claim only one polarization), which was corrected by Eddington in 1922.

The physical reality of gravitational waves was debated for decades. The skepticism had two roots. First, coordinate artefacts in the linearized theory can mimic wave behaviour -- what if gravitational waves were merely a feature of the coordinate system, not a physical effect? Second, the gravitational energy-momentum pseudotensor is not a tensor, so defining "energy carried by a gravitational wave" appeared coordinate-dependent. The resolution came from Bondi's 1962 "news function" analysis (proving that gravitational waves carry mass away from an isolated system) and from Isaacson's 1968 shortwave averaging (producing a gauge-invariant effective stress-energy tensor).

The first indirect detection came in 1974. Russell Hulse and Joseph Taylor discovered the binary pulsar PSR B1913+16 -- two neutron stars in a tight orbit. Timing the pulsar's radio pulses over subsequent years revealed that the orbital period was decreasing at exactly the rate predicted by the quadrupole formula. This was gravitational-wave emission stealing orbital energy, observed through its cumulative effect on the orbit. Hulse and Taylor received the 1993 Nobel Prize.

Direct detection required measuring strains of order $10^{-21}$. Joseph Weber built the first gravitational-wave detectors (resonant bars) in the 1960s. His controversial claims of detection were not replicated. The LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory) project, proposed by Rainer Weiss in 1972 and developed by Weiss, Kip Thorne, and Ronald Drever over four decades, used laser interferometry with 4-kilometre arms to achieve the required sensitivity. Advanced LIGO began its first observing run in September 2015.

GW150914 was detected on 14 September 2015. The signal arrived at the Livingston, Louisiana detector, then 6.9 milliseconds later at the Hanford, Washington detector, consistent with a wave propagating at the speed of light. The waveform matched the GR prediction for a binary black hole merger with component masses $36^{+5}_{-4}\,M_\odot$ and $29^{+4}_{-4}\,M_\odot$, at a luminosity distance of $410^{+160}_{-180}$ Mpc. Weiss, Barish, and Thorne received the 2017 Nobel Prize.

The philosophical significance: gravitational waves provide a new channel for observing the universe. Unlike electromagnetic radiation, gravitational waves interact weakly with matter and carry information about the bulk motion of mass, not the thermodynamics of surfaces. Binary black hole mergers are invisible electromagnetically but are among the brightest gravitational-wave sources in the universe. Multi-messenger astronomy -- combining gravitational-wave and electromagnetic observations -- began with GW170817 (a binary neutron star merger detected in both gravitational waves and gamma rays), which constrained the equation of state of neutron-star matter and provided an independent measurement of the Hubble constant.
