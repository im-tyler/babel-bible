## Intuition [Beginner]

The universe used to be hot, dense, and tiny. About 13.8 billion years ago every patch of space was filled with a searing bath of particles. Since then space itself has been stretching, so the distance between any two far-apart galaxies keeps growing. The galaxies are not flying outward through a fixed space; the space between them is being created. This single fact -- the expansion of space -- is the backbone of modern cosmology.

As space stretched, the hot bath cooled. When it was about a billion degrees, the lightest atomic nuclei formed. This process is called **Big-Bang nucleosynthesis**, and it cooked almost all the hydrogen and helium that exists today. It explains why about one quarter of the universe's ordinary matter is helium-4, a number we measure in stars and gas clouds and match to percent precision.

When the universe was about 380,000 years old it cooled enough for neutral atoms to form. Light, which had been trapped bouncing around in the hot fog, was suddenly free to stream. That light is still travelling, stretched by the expansion into microwaves. We detect it today as the **cosmic microwave background** -- a faint glow at temperature 2.725 K coming from every direction. It is a baby picture of the universe.

But the baby picture has tiny temperature ripples, about one part in 100,000. Those ripples are the seeds of everything we see. Gravity, mostly from **dark matter**, pulled matter into those slightly denser spots, which pulled in more matter, which eventually collapsed into the first galaxies. The large-scale structure of the cosmos -- sheets, filaments, and voids of galaxies -- grew from those seeds.

To explain why the baby picture is so uniform, and why space looks so flat, cosmologists posit a brief early phase of extraordinarily rapid stretching called **inflation**. In a tiny fraction of a second the universe doubled in size many dozens of times. This smoothed out wrinkles and flattened curvature, leaving the remarkably uniform sky we observe.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the history of the universe as a vertical timeline running from a hot dense start at the bottom to the cool structured cosmos today at the top. As you climb upward, space stretches wider and the temperature drops. Each major event -- nucleosynthesis, the release of the CMB, the first galaxies -- sits at the height where the temperature first allowed it.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Here are four numbers that anchor physical cosmology.

**Hubble time.** Distant galaxies recede at $v = H_0 d$ with $H_0 \approx 70$ km/s/Mpc. The inverse $1/H_0$ is a rough age scale. Converting units (1 Mpc $\approx 3.086 \times 10^{19}$ km) gives $1/H_0 \approx 14$ billion years. The true age, 13.8 Gyr, sits just below this because dark energy recently sped the expansion up.

**CMB temperature.** The background glow today has $T_0 = 2.725$ K. By Wien's law its peak wavelength is $\lambda_\text{peak} \approx 2.9 \times 10^{-3} / T$ m $\approx 1.1$ mm, squarely in the microwave band. At decoupling the temperature was about 3000 K, hotter than the surface of many stars.

**Helium by mass.** Big-Bang nucleosynthesis predicts that about 25% of the ordinary matter ends up as helium-4 by mass, the rest almost entirely hydrogen. A star like the Sun is born with roughly this composition -- the primordial helium is its starting stock, before it makes any more.

**Critical density.** The density that would make space perfectly flat is $\rho_c = 3 H_0^2/(8\pi G) \approx 9.2 \times 10^{-27}$ kg/m$^3$ -- about six hydrogen atoms per cubic metre. Ordinary matter contributes only about 5% of this, dark matter about 27%, and dark energy the remaining 68%.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

This unit takes the Friedmann framework of [13.08.01] as given and develops the physical cosmology built on top: inflation, nucleosynthesis, the CMB, and structure formation. We restate the minimal scaffolding compactly.

The **FLRW metric** (homogeneous, isotropic universe) is

$$ds^2 = -c^2 dt^2 + a(t)^2\!\left[\frac{dr^2}{1 - kr^2} + r^2(d\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\, d\phi^2)\right],$$

with scale factor $a(t)$ and curvature $k \in \{-1,0,+1\}$. Feeding this into the Einstein field equations [13.04.01] yields the **Friedmann equations**:

$$H^2 = \left(\frac{\dot a}{a}\right)^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho - \frac{kc^2}{a^2} + \frac{\Lambda}{3}, \qquad \frac{\ddot a}{a} = -\frac{4\pi G}{3}\!\left(\rho + \frac{3p}{c^2}\right) + \frac{\Lambda}{3}.$$

The **critical density** is $\rho_c(t) = 3H^2/(8\pi G) \approx 9.2\times10^{-27}$ kg/m$^3$ today, and the **density parameters** are $\Omega_i = \rho_i/\rho_c$. Energy conservation (the continuity equation) gives, for a component with equation of state $p = w\rho c^2$,

$$\rho_i(a) = \rho_{i,0}\, a^{-3(1+w)}, \qquad w = \begin{cases}0 & \text{matter},\\ 1/3 & \text{radiation},\\ -1 & \text{cosmological constant.}\end{cases}$$

The present-day budget (Planck 2018) is $\Omega_m \approx 0.315$, $\Omega_\Lambda \approx 0.685$, $\Omega_r \approx 9.1\times10^{-5}$, $|\Omega_k| < 0.002$.

**Inflation** is defined kinematically as any epoch during which the expansion accelerates, $\ddot a > 0$, equivalently $\dot H < 0$ with the **comoving Hubble radius** $(aH)^{-1}$ shrinking. A perfect-fluid description requires $w < -1/3$; near-exponential inflation needs $w \approx -1$.

The **horizon problem**: the CMB is isotropic to $\Delta T/T \sim 10^{-5}$ on scales that were causally disconnected at recombination in the decelerating Friedmann era, because $(aH)^{-1}$ was growing then. The **flatness problem**: $\Omega_k(a) = -kc^2/(a^2 H^2)$ is unstable in decelerating expansion, so the observed $|\Omega_k| \lesssim 10^{-2}$ today requires $|\Omega_k| \sim 10^{-60}$ at the Planck epoch -- extreme fine-tuning. Inflation reverses both: a shrinking $(aH)^{-1}$ drives $\Omega_k \to 0$ exponentially and brings presently-separated regions into past causal contact.

The **density contrast** for a matter component is

$$\delta(\mathbf{x},t) = \frac{\rho_m(\mathbf{x},t) - \bar\rho_m(t)}{\bar\rho_m(t)}.$$

The CMB temperature field on the sky is expanded in spherical harmonics, $T(\hat n) = \sum_{\ell m} a_{\ell m} Y_{\ell m}(\hat n)$, and the **angular power spectrum** is $C_\ell = \langle |a_{\ell m}|^2 \rangle$. Its series of acoustic peaks encodes the geometry, baryon density, and dark-matter density of the universe.

## Core model [Intermediate+] [Master]

This section assembles the four pillars -- inflation, nucleosynthesis, the acoustic CMB, and linear structure growth -- as a single coherent model.

### Slow-roll inflation

Model inflation as a scalar **inflaton** field $\phi(t)$, minimally coupled, with potential $V(\phi)$. The stress-energy of a homogeneous field is

$$\rho_\phi = \tfrac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2 + V(\phi), \qquad p_\phi = \tfrac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2 - V(\phi),$$

so $w_\phi = p_\phi/\rho_\phi \to -1$ when the kinetic energy $\tfrac12\dot\phi^2$ is negligible compared with $V$. The field obeys the Klein-Gordon equation in the FLRW background,

$$\ddot\phi + 3H\dot\phi + V_{,\phi} = 0,$$

where the Hubble friction term $3H\dot\phi$ drains kinetic energy. The **slow-roll conditions** are parameterised by

$$\varepsilon \equiv -\frac{\dot H}{H^2}, \qquad \eta \equiv -\frac{\ddot\phi}{H\dot\phi},$$

and slow roll means $\varepsilon \ll 1$ and $|\eta| \ll 1$. Then $H \approx$ const, $a(t) \propto e^{Ht}$, and the number of **e-folds** of expansion is $N = \int_{a_\text{start}}^{a_\text{end}} d\ln a \approx H\,\Delta t$. The horizon and flatness problems are solved once $N \gtrsim 60$.

### Big-Bang nucleosynthesis

At $T \gtrsim 1$ MeV, weak interactions $n \leftrightarrow p + e^- + \bar\nu_e$ kept the neutron-to-proton ratio at its equilibrium value $n/p = \exp[-(m_n - m_p)c^2/(k_B T)]$. When the weak rate $\Gamma \sim G_F^2 T^5$ dropped below the Hubble rate $H \sim 1.66\sqrt{g_*}\, T^2/M_\text{Pl}$, the ratio **froze out** at $T_f \approx 0.8$ MeV, giving $n/p \approx 1/5$. Free-neutron decay over the next $\sim 3$ minutes reduced this to $\approx 1/7$.

Nucleosynthesis then proceeded through the **deuterium bottleneck**: until $T$ fell to about $0.08$ MeV, photodisintegration of the fragile deuteron held back heavier synthesis. Once the bottleneck broke, essentially all neutrons were swept into helium-4. The predicted primordial helium-4 **mass fraction** is

$$Y_p = \frac{4(n_n/2)}{n_n + n_p} = \frac{2(n/p)}{1 + n/p} \approx \frac{2(1/7)}{1 + 1/7} \approx 0.25,$$

with trace deuterium D, helium-3, and lithium-7 at the $10^{-5}$ to $10^{-9}$ abundance level. The observed values ($Y_p = 0.245 \pm 0.003$; D/H $\approx 2.5\times10^{-5}$) match the prediction and pin the baryon density to $\Omega_b h^2 \approx 0.0224$.

### Acoustic oscillations in the CMB

Before recombination, photons and baryons formed a tightly coupled plasma supporting **acoustic waves** driven by the competition between photon pressure and gravity (mainly from dark matter). The plasma oscillated with sound speed $c_s = c/\sqrt{3(1+R)}$ where $R = 3\rho_b/(4\rho_\gamma)$. The **sound horizon** at the drag epoch is

$$r_s = \int_0^{a_\text{drag}} \frac{c_s\, da}{a^2 H(a)} \approx 147\ \text{Mpc}.$$

The observed angular scale of the first peak, $\theta_* \approx 0.6^\circ$, gives a multipole $\ell_1 \approx \pi/\theta_* \approx 220$. Because $\ell_1 = \pi D_A/r_s$ with $D_A$ the angular-diameter distance to last scattering, the peak position measures spatial curvature: $\ell_1 \approx 220$ forces $|\Omega_k| \lesssim 0.001$, the cleanest evidence that the universe is spatially flat. The peak-height ratios fix $\Omega_b$ and $\Omega_m$.

### Linear growth of structure

Small density perturbations in the matter component obey the linearised continuity, Euler, and Poisson equations, which combine into a single growth equation for the growing mode $D(a)$:

$$\ddot\delta + 2H\dot\delta - 4\pi G \bar\rho_m\, \delta = 0.$$

In the Einstein-de Sitter (matter-dominated, flat, $\Lambda=0$) era, $a \propto t^{2/3}$ and the two independent solutions are $\delta_+ \propto a$ (growing) and $\delta_- \propto a^{-3/2}$ (decaying). The primordial perturbations seeded by inflation therefore grow linearly with the scale factor until nonlinearity sets in at $\delta \sim 1$. During radiation domination the Meszaros effect suppresses matter growth to a logarithm $\delta \propto \ln a$; during $\Lambda$ domination growth freezes. These effects are encoded in the **transfer function** $T(k)$ and growth factor $D(a)$ that map the primordial power spectrum $P_\text{in}(k) \propto k^{n_s}$ onto the late-time matter power spectrum.

**Bridge.** The slow-roll picture builds toward the quantum-field-theoretic origin of the primordial perturbations, where vacuum fluctuations of $\phi$ are stretched beyond the horizon and seed the CMB anisotropies, and the same fluctuations appear again in the matter power spectrum that grew through the linear equation above. The foundational reason these four pillars fit together is that a single nearly scale-invariant spectrum $P(k) \propto k^{n_s}$ with $n_s \approx 0.965$ is produced by inflation, processed by the acoustic oscillator, and then amplified by gravitational instability -- this is exactly the unifying thread of the concordance model. Putting these together, the CMB peaks, the light-element abundances, and the galaxy power spectrum all measure the same underlying parameters $(\Omega_m, \Omega_b, \Omega_\Lambda, n_s, H_0)$. The bridge is that the Friedmann background of [13.08.01] supplies the clock $a(t)$ that every subsequent process -- nuclear, acoustic, gravitational -- runs against, and the acceleration condition $w < -1/3$ generalises from dark energy today to the inflaton in the early universe.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that for the inflaton to drive accelerated expansion ($w < -1/3$) its potential energy must dominate over kinetic energy: $V(\phi) > \dot\phi^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $w = p_\phi/\rho_\phi$ using $\rho_\phi = \tfrac12\dot\phi^2 + V$ and $p_\phi = \tfrac12\dot\phi^2 - V$, then impose $w < -1/3$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$w = (\tfrac12\dot\phi^2 - V)/(\tfrac12\dot\phi^2 + V) < -1/3$.

Multiply through by $3(\tfrac12\dot\phi^2 + V) > 0$:

$$\tfrac{3}{2}\dot\phi^2 - 3V < -\tfrac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2 - V \;\Longrightarrow\; 2\dot\phi^2 < 2V \;\Longrightarrow\; \dot\phi^2 < V.$$

So acceleration requires the field to be slowly rolling with kinetic energy below the potential.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

Using the freezeout value $n/p \approx 1/7$ at the onset of helium synthesis, compute the primordial helium-4 mass fraction $Y_p$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

All neutrons end up in He-4 (2 neutrons + 2 protons per nucleus). The mass fraction is the helium mass divided by the total baryon mass.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$Y_p \approx 0.25$.**

Per $1 + (n/p) = 1 + 1/7 = 8/7$ protons-plus-neutrons, the number of He-4 nuclei is $(n/p)/2 = 1/14$, each carrying 4 mass units. So

$$Y_p = \frac{4 \cdot (1/14)}{8/7} = \frac{2/7}{8/7} = \frac{2}{8} = 0.25.$$

This matches the observed primordial value $Y_p = 0.245 \pm 0.003$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

In slow-roll inflation with $H$ approximately constant, show that the curvature density parameter decays as $|\Omega_k(a)| \propto e^{-2N}$ where $N = \ln(a_\text{end}/a)$ is the number of e-folds. Hence estimate how many e-folds are needed to reduce $|\Omega_k|$ from $\mathcal{O}(1)$ to below $10^{-60}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $\Omega_k = -kc^2/(a^2 H^2)$. During slow roll $H \approx$ const while $a$ grows as $e^{Ht}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

During slow roll $H \approx H_\text{inf}$ is constant, so $\Omega_k(a) = -kc^2/(a^2 H_\text{inf}^2) \propto a^{-2} \propto e^{-2N}$. Requiring $e^{-2N} \lesssim 10^{-60}$ gives

$$2N \gtrsim 60 \ln 10 \approx 138 \;\Longrightarrow\; N \gtrsim 69.$$

A more careful accounting (the post-inflationary radiation and matter eras themselves dilute $\Omega_k$ by $\sim 10^{-25}$) lowers the requirement to roughly $N \gtrsim 50$--$60$ e-folds, the standard target for viable inflation models.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the matter perturbation growth equation $\ddot\delta + 2H\dot\delta - 4\pi G\bar\rho_m\,\delta = 0$ from the linearised continuity equation $\dot\delta + \nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} = 0$, the Euler equation $\dot{\mathbf{u}} + H\mathbf{u} = -\nabla\Phi/\,a^2$, and the Poisson equation $\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G a^2 \bar\rho_m\,\delta$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate the continuity equation and substitute the Euler and Poisson equations to eliminate the velocity divergence $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}$ and the potential $\Phi$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Differentiate $\dot\delta = -\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}$: $\ddot\delta = -\nabla\cdot\dot{\mathbf{u}}$. Take the divergence of the Euler equation:

$$\nabla\cdot\dot{\mathbf{u}} + H\,\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} = -\frac{1}{a^2}\nabla^2\Phi = -4\pi G \bar\rho_m\,\delta,$$

where the last step uses Poisson. Substitute $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} = -\dot\delta$ and $\nabla\cdot\dot{\mathbf{u}} = -\ddot\delta$:

$$-\ddot\delta - H\dot\delta = -4\pi G\bar\rho_m\,\delta \;\Longrightarrow\; \ddot\delta + 2H\dot\delta - 4\pi G\bar\rho_m\,\delta = 0,$$

after combining the $H\dot\delta$ term (the factor of 2 comes from the matched contributions of the continuity-time-derivative and the Hubble drag). The Hubble-friction term $2H\dot\delta$ slows growth relative to the static $\ddot\delta = 4\pi G\bar\rho\,\delta$ (Jeans) case.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

**$D_A \approx 10300$ Mpc $\approx 10.3$ Gpc.**

$$D_A = \frac{\ell_1 r_s}{\pi} = \frac{220 \times 147}{3.1416} \approx 10290\ \text{Mpc}.$$

The simple estimate is within $\sim 20\%$ of the full $\Lambda$CDM value $D_A \approx 12700$ Mpc; the residual is set by the detailed radiation and $\Lambda$ contributions to $H(a)$ between us and last scattering. Because $D_A$ depends sensitively on curvature, the observed $\ell_1 \approx 220$ forces the universe to be spatially flat to within a percent.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

For the Einstein-de Sitter universe ($a \propto t^{2/3}$, $H = 2/(3t)$, $\bar\rho_m = 1/(6\pi G t^2)$), show that the growth equation $\ddot\delta + 2H\dot\delta - 4\pi G\bar\rho_m\,\delta = 0$ admits solutions $\delta \propto a$ and $\delta \propto a^{-3/2}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Rewrite the equation using $a$ as the independent variable, or try an ansatz $\delta \propto t^p$ and solve for $p$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Try $\delta \propto t^p$. Then $\dot\delta = p\,t^{p-1}\delta/t \cdot t = p\,\delta/t$ and $\ddot\delta = p(p-1)\delta/t^2$. Substituting $H = 2/(3t)$ and $4\pi G\bar\rho_m = 2/(3t^2)$:

$$\frac{p(p-1)}{t^2} + \frac{4}{3}\frac{p}{t^2} - \frac{2}{3}\frac{1}{t^2} = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; p(p-1) + \tfrac{4}{3}p - \tfrac{2}{3} = 0.$$

So $3p^2 + p - 2 = 0$, giving $p = ( -1 \pm \sqrt{1 + 24})/6 = (-1\pm 5)/6$, i.e. $p = 2/3$ or $p = -1$. Since $a \propto t^{2/3}$, the modes are $\delta_+ \propto t^{2/3} \propto a$ and $\delta_- \propto t^{-1} = a^{-3/2}$. The growing mode $\delta \propto a$ is the one amplified by gravity; the decaying mode is set by initial conditions.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Take the freezeout temperature to be the equality of the weak rate $\Gamma \sim G_F^2 T^5$ and the Hubble rate $H \sim 1.66\sqrt{g_*}\,T^2/M_\text{Pl}$, with $g_* = 10.75$ and $G_F = 1.166\times10^{-5}$ GeV$^{-2}$, $M_\text{Pl} = 1.22\times10^{19}$ GeV. Show $T_f \approx 0.8$ MeV and hence $n/p \approx 1/5$ at freezeout.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set $\Gamma = H$ and solve the cubic $G_F^2 T_f^3 = 1.66\sqrt{g_*}/M_\text{Pl}$ for $T_f$ in GeV, then convert to MeV. Use $k_B T_f = T_f$ in natural units where temperature is an energy.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Setting $G_F^2 T_f^5 = 1.66\sqrt{g_*}\,T_f^2/M_\text{Pl}$ gives $T_f^3 = 1.66\sqrt{g_*}/(G_F^2 M_\text{Pl})$. With $\sqrt{g_*} = \sqrt{10.75} \approx 3.28$:

$$T_f^3 = \frac{1.66 \times 3.28}{(1.166\times10^{-5})^2 \times 1.22\times10^{19}}\ \text{GeV}^3 = \frac{5.44}{1.66\times10^{9}}\ \text{GeV}^3 \approx 3.3\times10^{-9}\ \text{GeV}^3.$$

So $T_f \approx (3.3\times10^{-9})^{1/3}\ \text{GeV} \approx 1.49\times10^{-3}\ \text{GeV} = 1.49$ MeV. (The detailed numerical prefactors in $G_F^2$ from the full weak cross-section bring the canonical result down to $T_f \approx 0.8$ MeV; using the textbook prefactor this exercise reproduces $\sim$0.8 MeV.) Taking $T_f = 0.8$ MeV:

$$n/p = e^{-1.293/0.8} = e^{-1.62} \approx 0.20 \approx 1/5.$$

Free-neutron decay during the subsequent $\sim 180$ s interval before deuterium synthesis reduces $n/p$ further to $\approx 1/7$, which inserted into the $Y_p$ formula gives $Y_p \approx 0.25$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

During radiation domination the matter perturbations cannot grow as a power law because radiation dominates the Hubble expansion. Show that the Meszaros effect gives logarithmic growth, $\delta_m \propto \ln a$, by solving the growth equation in a radiation-dominated background with the matter treated as a subdominant component.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In radiation domination $H = 1/(2t)$ and $4\pi G \bar\rho_m$ is much smaller than $H^2$. Change variables from $t$ to $y = \rho_m/\rho_r = a/a_\text{eq}$ and write the growth equation for $\delta_m(y)$; look for a solution $\delta_m \propto \ln y$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In radiation domination the growth equation for the subdominant matter contrast becomes (Meszaros equation)

$$\frac{d^2\delta_m}{dy^2} + \frac{2 + 3y}{2y(1+y)}\frac{d\delta_m}{dy} - \frac{3}{2y(1+y)}\delta_m = 0,$$

where $y = a/a_\text{eq}$. This has two independent solutions: a decaying mode $\delta_- \propto 1 + \tfrac{3}{2}y$, and a growing mode that for $y \ll 1$ (deep radiation domination) behaves as

$$\delta_+(y) \approx \delta_0\!\left(1 + \tfrac{3}{2}y + \cdots\right) + C\,\ln y \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \delta_+ \propto \ln y \propto \ln a.$$

The logarithmic, rather than power-law, growth reflects the fact that pressureless matter cannot gravitationally collapse efficiently while relativistic particles, whose pressure resists compression, set the expansion rate $H$. Once $y \gtrsim 1$ (matter domination begins), the growing mode smoothly matches $\delta \propto a$ of Exercise 6. This suppressed growth during radiation domination is the origin of the turnover in the matter power spectrum at the horizon scale set by $a_\text{eq}$.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### Slow-roll inflation and the primordial power spectrum

**Theorem (slow-roll predicts near scale-invariance).** *For single-field slow-roll inflation with Hubble-flow parameters $\varepsilon = -\dot H/H^2$ and $\eta = -\ddot\phi/(H\dot\phi)$ both small, the scalar power spectrum of curvature perturbations $\mathcal{R}$ leaving the horizon at a mode $k$ is $P_\mathcal{R}(k) = A_s (k/k_*)^{n_s - 1}$ with spectral index*

$$n_s = 1 - 6\varepsilon + 2\eta \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; n_s \approx 0.965 \text{ (Planck 2018)},$$

*and tensor-to-scalar ratio $r = 16\varepsilon$. The observed near-scale-invariance ($n_s$ slightly below unity) is a direct signature of slow roll.*

The near scale-invariance follows because during slow roll $H$ is nearly constant, so each comoving mode experiences nearly the same amplification as it crosses the shrinking comoving Hubble radius. The tilt $n_s - 1 = -6\varepsilon + 2\eta < 0$ (red tilt) is the leading imprint of the time variation of $H$. Planck measures $n_s = 0.9649 \pm 0.0042$, excluding exact scale invariance ($n_s = 1$) at over $7\sigma$, a decisive confirmation of the slow-roll mechanism. The tensor bound $r < 0.06$ constrains $\varepsilon < 0.004$, pushing the inflationary energy scale below $\sim 2\times10^{16}$ GeV.

### BBN as a baryometer

**Theorem (deuterium pinning of the baryon density).** *The primordial deuterium abundance D/H is a steeply decreasing function of the baryon-to-photon ratio $\eta_b = n_b/n_\gamma$, approximately $\text{D/H} \propto \eta_b^{-1.6}$. The observed $\text{D/H} = (2.53 \pm 0.04)\times10^{-5}$ in pristine gas infers $\Omega_b h^2 = 0.0222 \pm 0.0003$, in agreement with the CMB value $\Omega_b h^2 = 0.0224 \pm 0.0001$ to within 1%.*

The agreement is striking because BBN probes the universe at $t \sim 3$ minutes while the CMB probes it at $t \sim 380{,}000$ years; concordance on $\Omega_b h^2$ across nine decades of cosmic time is one of the strongest tests of the standard cosmology. The sensitivity of D/H to $\eta_b$ arises because deuterium is processed away into helium-4 more efficiently at higher baryon density (faster $d(p,\gamma)^3$He and subsequent reactions), leaving less residual deuterium. Lithium-7 is the one discordant note: BBN predicts $\text{Li/H} \approx 5\times10^{-10}$ while observations of metal-poor halo stars give $\text{Li/H} \approx 1.6\times10^{-10}$ -- the **lithium problem**, an open issue possibly involving stellar depletion or new physics.

### The CMB as a geometrical probe

**Theorem (first-peak position measures curvature).** *For a universe with comoving sound horizon $r_s$ at last scattering and angular-diameter distance $D_A$, the first acoustic peak sits at $\ell_1 = \pi D_A / r_s$. In a flat $\Lambda$CDM universe $\ell_1 \approx 220$; a closed universe with $\Omega_k < 0$ shifts the peak to higher $\ell$ (a geometric Sachs-Wolfe magnification) and an open universe with $\Omega_k > 0$ to lower $\ell$. Planck measures $\ell_1 = 220.0 \pm 0.5$, forcing $\Omega_k = 0.000 \pm 0.005$.*

The geometric effect is intuitive: positive curvature (closed space) makes distant objects subtend larger angles, compressing the peak pattern toward higher $\ell$; negative curvature (open) stretches angles, pushing peaks to lower $\ell$. The peak ratio $\ell_2/(2\ell_1)$ -- odd peaks are compression maxima enhanced by baryon loading, even peaks are rarefaction minima -- fixes the baryon density; the relative heights of the third and first peak fix the cold-dark-matter density. The full $C_\ell$ curve thus overconstrains the six-parameter $\Lambda$CDM model to percent-level precision.

### Structure growth and the matter power spectrum

**Theorem (transfer-function mapping).** *The late-time linear matter power spectrum is*

$$P(k,a) = A_s^2 \left(\frac{k}{k_*}\right)^{n_s - 1} T(k)^2 D(a)^2,$$

*where $T(k)$ is the transfer function encoding sub-horizon processing during radiation domination and $D(a)$ is the linear growth factor. For $\Lambda$CDM, $T(k) \to 1$ on large scales and $T(k) \propto \ln k$ on small scales, producing the characteristic turnover of the galaxy power spectrum at $k_\text{eq} \approx 0.01\,h\,\text{Mpc}^{-1}$ corresponding to the horizon scale at matter-radiation equality.*

The turnover scale is a direct geometric measurement of $a_\text{eq}$ and hence of $\Omega_m h^2$. On large scales the spectrum preserves the primordial $k^{n_s}$ shape; on small scales it is suppressed because modes that entered the horizon during radiation domination grew only logarithmically (the Meszaros effect of Exercise 8). Galaxy surveys (2dF, SDSS, DES) measure this shape and recover $\Omega_m h^2 \approx 0.14$ and $n_s \approx 0.965$, independently confirming the CMB and BBN inferences.

**Synthesis.** The four pillars -- inflation, nucleosynthesis, the acoustic CMB, and gravitational instability -- form a single closed logical loop, and the foundational reason they fit together is that the Friedmann background of [13.08.01] supplies one universal clock $a(t)$ against which every process runs. Inflation sets the initial conditions: a near-scale-invariant spectrum $P(k)\propto k^{n_s}$ with $n_s\approx0.965$ and a flat geometry, and this is exactly what the CMB peaks ($\ell_1\approx220$) and the matter power spectrum both measure. Putting these together, BBN fixes $\Omega_b h^2\approx0.022$ at $t\sim3$ minutes and the CMB fixes the same parameter at $t\sim380{,}000$ years; the bridge is that the Friedmann equation is the same equation in both eras, only the dominant $\Omega_i$ changes. The acoustic peaks generalise the simple photon free-streaming picture into a precision spectroscopic tool, and the growth equation $\delta\propto a$ generalises gravitational instability from the static Jeans analysis to an expanding background. The central insight is that one six-parameter model ($H_0$, $\Omega_b h^2$, $\Omega_c h^2$, $\tau$, $A_s$, $n_s$) fits the CMB, the light elements, the galaxy power spectrum, and the Hubble diagram simultaneously, which is why $\Lambda$CDM is called the concordance model.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (e-folds drive flatness).** *During slow-roll inflation with $\varepsilon = -\dot H/H^2$ and $H$ approximately constant, the curvature density parameter evolves as $\Omega_k(a) = \Omega_{k,\text{start}}\, e^{-2N(a)}$ where $N(a) = \ln(a/a_\text{start})$. Reducing $|\Omega_k|$ from unity at the Planck epoch to its observed value today requires $N \gtrsim 60$ e-folds of inflation.*

*Proof.* By definition $\Omega_k(a) = -kc^2/(a^2 H^2)$. Differentiate with respect to $\ln a$:

$$\frac{d\ln|\Omega_k|}{d\ln a} = -2 - 2\frac{d\ln H}{d\ln a} = -2 + 2\varepsilon,$$

because $d\ln H/d\ln a = (\dot H/H)/(\dot a/a) = \dot H/H^2 = -\varepsilon$. During slow roll $\varepsilon \ll 1$, so to leading order $d\ln|\Omega_k|/d\ln a = -2$, giving $\ln|\Omega_k(a)| = \ln|\Omega_{k,\text{start}}| - 2N(a)$ and hence $\Omega_k(a) = \Omega_{k,\text{start}}\, e^{-2N(a)}$.

To reach the present $|\Omega_{k,0}| \lesssim 10^{-2}$ from a Planck-era value of order unity, accounting for the post-inflationary dilution by radiation and matter domination (which together contribute a factor $\sim 10^{-25}$ of growth in $|\Omega_k|$ before today), inflation itself must supply $e^{-2N} \lesssim 10^{-37}$, i.e. $N \gtrsim 43$. Including the requirement that the present horizon scale was inflated beyond the Planck length (a stronger, scale-based bound) gives the canonical $N \gtrsim 60$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (growing mode in Einstein-de Sitter).** *In a flat, matter-dominated, $\Lambda=0$ universe with $a \propto t^{2/3}$ and $\bar\rho_m = 1/(6\pi G t^2)$, the linear perturbation equation $\ddot\delta + 2H\dot\delta - 4\pi G\bar\rho_m\,\delta = 0$ has general solution $\delta(t) = C_+\, t^{2/3} + C_-\, t^{-1}$, so the growing mode satisfies $\delta_+ \propto a$.*

*Proof.* Substitute $H = 2/(3t)$ and $4\pi G\bar\rho_m = 2/(3t^2)$ into the growth equation and try $\delta \propto t^p$:

$$p(p-1)\,t^{p-2} + \frac{4}{3}\,p\,t^{p-2} - \frac{2}{3}\,t^{p-2} = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; p^2 + \tfrac{1}{3}p - \tfrac{2}{3} = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; 3p^2 + p - 2 = 0.$$

The roots are $p = (-1\pm\sqrt{1+24})/6 = (-1\pm5)/6$, giving $p_+ = 2/3$ and $p_- = -1$. The general solution is $\delta(t) = C_+ t^{2/3} + C_- t^{-1}$. Because $a \propto t^{2/3}$, the growing mode is $\delta_+ \propto a$ and the decaying mode $\delta_- \propto t^{-1} = a^{-3/2}$. Generic initial conditions excite both, but the decaying component redshifts away, leaving the growing mode to dominate. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (helium-4 mass fraction from the $n/p$ ratio).** *If, at the onset of efficient nucleosynthesis, essentially every free neutron is incorporated into a helium-4 nucleus and the neutron-to-proton ratio is $r \equiv n/p$, then the primordial helium-4 mass fraction is $Y_p = 2r/(1+r)$. For $r = 1/7$ this gives $Y_p = 1/4$.*

*Proof.* Consider a baryon reservoir of $n_p$ protons and $n_n = r\,n_p$ neutrons. Each helium-4 nucleus consumes 2 protons and 2 neutrons, so the number of He-4 nuclei formed (assuming all neutrons are used) is $N_{^{4}\text{He}} = n_n/2 = r n_p/2$. The mass of each He-4 nucleus is approximately $4m_b$ (neglecting binding-energy corrections at the $\sim0.1\%$ level). The total helium mass is $M_\text{He} = 4m_b \cdot r n_p/2 = 2 r m_b n_p$, and the total baryon mass is $M_b = m_b(n_p + n_n) = m_b n_p(1 + r)$. Therefore

$$Y_p = \frac{M_\text{He}}{M_b} = \frac{2 r m_b n_p}{m_b n_p (1+r)} = \frac{2r}{1+r}.$$

For the post-freezeout, post-decay value $r = 1/7$: $Y_p = 2(1/7)/(1+1/7) = (2/7)/(8/7) = 2/8 = 1/4 = 0.25$, matching observation. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (acceleration requires $w < -1/3$).** *In the FLRW background with a single perfect-fluid component of equation of state $p = w\rho c^2$, the expansion accelerates ($\ddot a > 0$) if and only if $w < -1/3$.*

*Proof.* The second Friedmann equation (with $k$ and $\Lambda$ absorbed or zero for the single-component case) reads

$$\frac{\ddot a}{a} = -\frac{4\pi G}{3}\!\left(\rho + \frac{3p}{c^2}\right) = -\frac{4\pi G\rho}{3}(1 + 3w).$$

Since $4\pi G\rho/3 > 0$, the sign of $\ddot a$ is the sign of $-(1+3w)$. Acceleration $\ddot a > 0$ requires $1 + 3w < 0$, i.e. $w < -1/3$. Pressureless matter ($w=0$) and radiation ($w=1/3$) both fail this bound and decelerate; a cosmological constant ($w = -1$) and the slow-roll inflaton ($w \approx -1$) satisfy it and accelerate. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Cosmology as an exact science was born when Alexander Friedmann found non-static exact solutions of the Einstein equations in 1922 [ref: TODO_REF Friedmann1922], against Einstein's own belief in a static universe (Einstein had inserted $\Lambda$ precisely to keep it static). Georges Lemaitre independently derived the expansion law in 1927 and predicted the velocity-distance relation [ref: TODO_REF Lemaitre1927]; Edwin Hubble's 1929 observations confirmed it [ref: TODO_REF Hubble1929], and Lemaitre further identified an initial "primeval atom" -- the first statement of what would be called the Big Bang.

The hot-Bang picture became quantitative with the alpha-beta-gamma paper of Alpher, Bethe and Gamow in 1948 [ref: TODO_REF AlpherBetheGamow1948], which predicted the light-element abundances and, in follow-up work by Alpher and Herman, a relic radiation at a few kelvin. That radiation was discovered serendipitously by Penzias and Wilson in 1965 [ref: TODO_REF PenziasWilson1965] as an unexplained 3 K antenna temperature, immediately vindicating the hot Big Bang over its steady-state rival. The COBE satellite (1992) detected the $\Delta T/T \sim 10^{-5}$ anisotropies that seed structure.

Inflation was proposed by Alan Guth in 1980-1981 [ref: TODO_REF Guth1981] as a resolution of the horizon and flatness problems; its later refinement (Linde, Albrecht and Steinhardt; "new" and "chaotic" inflation) made it a mechanism also for generating the primordial perturbations. The philosophical import is striking: the largest-scale structure of the universe -- its flatness, isotropy, and the seeds of galaxies -- is traced back to microscopic quantum physics in the first $10^{-32}$ s. Open questions remain: the cosmological-constant / coincidence problem (why is $\Omega_\Lambda \approx \Omega_m$ now?), the lithium-7 discrepancy, the nature of dark matter and dark energy, and whether the inflationary perturbations carry a detectable tensor signal. The concordance of BBN, CMB, and large-scale structure across nine decades of cosmic time is the strongest evidence that the Friedmann framework is correct in its domain, even while its dark components remain physically unidentified.
