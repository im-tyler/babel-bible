## Intuition [Beginner]

A great many constructions take a thing and wrap it in extra structure of a fixed kind. Take a set and form all finite lists you can build from its elements. Take a set and form all the formal weighted blends of its elements. Take a set and form all its subsets. Each of these is a way of packaging the original thing inside a roomier container, always the same kind of container. A monad is the bookkeeping that says how such a packaging behaves: how a bare element slips into the container as the simplest possible package, and how a container full of containers collapses back down into one.

Two small habits make a packaging a monad. First, any plain element can be put into the container in the most economical way, as a one-item package with nothing extra added. Second, whenever you end up with a package of packages, there is one honest way to flatten it into a single package. A list of lists flattens by concatenation. A blend of blends flattens by multiplying the weights through. Once these two habits agree with each other in the obvious bookkeeping, you have a monad.

Why bother naming this? Because the same two habits show up everywhere, and pinning them down lets you reason about lists, probabilities, subsets, and computations that might fail, all with one vocabulary instead of a separate story each time.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a machine that wraps things. Feed it a set of plain items on the left and it returns the set of all packages built from those items on the right. The list machine returns all finite lists; the subset machine returns all subsets; the blend machine returns all weighted blends.

Two arrows control the machine. The "wrap" arrow takes a bare item and returns its simplest package: the one-item list, the singleton subset, the blend that puts all its weight on that single item. The "flatten" arrow takes a package whose contents are themselves packages and merges them into one package at a single level.

| feature | the wrap arrow (unit) | the flatten arrow (multiplication) |
|---|---|---|
| takes in | a bare item | a package of packages |
| returns | the simplest one-item package | one merged package |
| list machine | the one-element list $[a]$ | concatenate all the inner lists |
| subset machine | the singleton $\{a\}$ | take the union of all inner subsets |
| blend machine | all weight on $a$ | multiply outer and inner weights |

![A wrapping machine with sets on the left and packaged sets on the right; a wrap arrow carries a bare item to its simplest one-item package, and a flatten arrow carries a package-of-packages down to a single merged package, with list concatenation, subset union, and weighted-blend multiplication shown as three instances of the same flatten step](/img/monads-eilenberg-moore-kleisli-placeholder.svg)

The picture's point is that wrap and flatten are not arbitrary. Flattening twice from the outside must agree with flattening twice from the inside, and wrapping then flattening must give back what you started with. Those agreements are the laws of a monad.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Work with the list machine on a small set, $\{a, b\}$. A package here is a finite list, such as $[a]$, $[b, a]$, or the empty list $[\ ]$.

Step 1. Wrap. The wrap arrow sends the bare item $a$ to the one-item list $[a]$, and sends $b$ to $[b]$. Nothing extra is added; the package holds exactly one item.

Step 2. Build a package of packages. Consider the list of lists $\big[\,[a, b],\ [a]\,\big]$. Its two entries are themselves lists. This is what the flatten arrow is built to handle.

Step 3. Flatten. Concatenating the inner lists in order glues $[a, b]$ to $[a]$, giving the single list $[a, b, a]$. The two-level package has become one level.

Step 4. Check the wrap-then-flatten habit. Start from the plain list $[a, b]$. Wrap each entry to get the list of one-item lists $\big[\,[a],\ [b]\,\big]$, then flatten by concatenation: $[a]$ glued to $[b]$ is $[a, b]$. You are back where you began.

What this tells us: wrapping puts an item in as a length-one package, flattening glues nested packages into one, and the two are tuned so that introducing a layer and then removing it changes nothing. Those exact habits, holding for every list, are what make the list machine a monad.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\mathcal{C}$ be a category. A *monad* on $\mathcal{C}$ is a triple $(T, \eta, \mu)$ where $T\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{C}$ is an endofunctor and $\eta\colon\mathrm{Id}_{\mathcal{C}}\Rightarrow T$ (the *unit*) and $\mu\colon T^2\Rightarrow T$ (the *multiplication*) are natural transformations making the *associativity* and *unit* squares commute [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998]:

$$
\mu\circ T\mu = \mu\circ \mu T \colon T^3\Rightarrow T,
\qquad
\mu\circ T\eta = \mathrm{id}_T = \mu\circ \eta T \colon T\Rightarrow T.
$$

Here $T\mu$ and $\mu T$ are the whiskered composites of [41.01.02]. A monad is exactly a *monoid in the endofunctor category* $([\mathcal{C},\mathcal{C}],\circ,\mathrm{Id})$: the monoidal product is functor composition, the multiplication is $\mu$, and the unit is $\eta$, with the two diagrams the monoid associativity and unit laws transcribed. Notation introduced here ($T$ for the monad endofunctor, $\eta$ for the unit, $\mu$ for the multiplication, $\mathcal{C}^T$ and $\mathcal{C}_T$ below) is recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

**Definition (the monad of an adjunction).** Let $F\dashv G$ with $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$, $G\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}$, unit $\eta\colon\mathrm{Id}_{\mathcal{C}}\Rightarrow GF$ and counit $\varepsilon\colon FG\Rightarrow\mathrm{Id}_{\mathcal{D}}$ from [41.03.01]. Then $T = GF$ carries a monad structure with this same $\eta$ as unit and $\mu = G\varepsilon F\colon GFGF\Rightarrow GF$ as multiplication. Dually $FG$ carries a *comonad* structure on $\mathcal{D}$.

**Definition (Eilenberg-Moore algebra).** A *$T$-algebra* (or *Eilenberg-Moore algebra*) for a monad $(T,\eta,\mu)$ is a pair $(A, a)$ with $A$ an object of $\mathcal{C}$ and a *structure map* $a\colon TA\to A$ satisfying the *unit law* $a\circ\eta_A = \mathrm{id}_A$ and the *associativity law* $a\circ \mu_A = a\circ Ta$. A *morphism of $T$-algebras* $(A,a)\to(B,b)$ is a map $h\colon A\to B$ with $h\circ a = b\circ Th$. These form the *Eilenberg-Moore category* $\mathcal{C}^T$. There is a *forgetful functor* $G^T\colon\mathcal{C}^T\to\mathcal{C}$, $(A,a)\mapsto A$, and a *free functor* $F^T\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{C}^T$ sending $C$ to the *free algebra* $(TC,\mu_C)$.

**Definition (Kleisli category).** The *Kleisli category* $\mathcal{C}_T$ has the same objects as $\mathcal{C}$; a morphism $C\rightsquigarrow D$ in $\mathcal{C}_T$ is a $\mathcal{C}$-morphism $C\to TD$. The identity on $C$ is $\eta_C\colon C\to TC$, and the *Kleisli composite* of $f\colon C\to TD$ and $g\colon D\to TE$ is $\mu_E\circ Tg\circ f\colon C\to TE$. There is a free functor $F_T\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{C}_T$ (identity on objects, $f\mapsto\eta\circ f$) left adjoint to $G_T\colon\mathcal{C}_T\to\mathcal{C}$, $C\mapsto TC$.

The corpus of monads mirrors the free-forgetful gallery of [41.03.01]. The free-monoid monad on $\mathbf{Set}$ has algebras the *monoids*; the free-group monad has algebras the *groups*; the free $R$-module monad has algebras the $R$-modules; the *powerset monad* $\mathcal{P}$ (with $\eta$ the singleton and $\mu$ the union) has algebras the *complete sup-semilattices* (equivalently complete lattices); the *distribution monad* $\mathcal{D}$ of finitely-supported probability measures has algebras the *convex sets*. In functional programming the *list*, *Maybe*, and *state* monads package nondeterminism, partiality, and mutable state, and a program with effects is a Kleisli arrow.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *A structure map is more than a left inverse to the unit.* The unit law $a\circ\eta_A=\mathrm{id}_A$ alone does not make $(A,a)$ an algebra; the associativity law $a\circ\mu_A=a\circ Ta$ is an independent constraint and is what forces $a$ to respect nesting. For the list monad it is exactly the law that a monoid multiplication is associative.

- *Not every $TA\to A$ underlies an algebra.* For the powerset monad an algebra structure on $A$ is a join operation $\bigvee\colon\mathcal{P}A\to A$ that is associative and unital in the monad sense, which forces $A$ to be a complete sup-semilattice; an arbitrary set map $\mathcal{P}A\to A$ choosing one element of each subset is not an algebra unless it is a genuine supremum.

- *The Kleisli and Eilenberg-Moore categories are different in general.* $\mathcal{C}_T$ is equivalent to the full subcategory of *free* algebras inside $\mathcal{C}^T$; the inclusion is an equivalence onto all of $\mathcal{C}^T$ only when every algebra is free, which fails already for the list monad, where most monoids are not free.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is that monads and adjunctions are tied at the source: every adjunction produces a monad, and the construction is the engine behind every later resolution.

**Theorem (the monad of an adjunction).** Let $F\dashv G$ with unit $\eta$ and counit $\varepsilon$. Then $(T,\eta,\mu)$ with $T = GF$ and $\mu = G\varepsilon F$ is a monad on $\mathcal{C}$ [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998].

*Proof.* The unit $\eta\colon\mathrm{Id}\Rightarrow GF$ and the natural transformation $\mu = G\varepsilon F\colon GFGF\Rightarrow GF$ are natural as composites and whiskerings of natural transformations. For the right unit law, $\mu\circ \eta T = G\varepsilon F\circ\eta_{GF}$. Evaluating the second triangle identity $(G\varepsilon)\circ(\eta G)=\mathrm{id}_G$ of [41.03.01] and whiskering on the right by $F$ gives $G\varepsilon F\circ\eta_{GF}=\mathrm{id}_{GF}$, so $\mu\circ\eta T=\mathrm{id}_T$. For the left unit law, $\mu\circ T\eta = G\varepsilon F\circ GF\eta = G(\varepsilon F\circ F\eta)$; the first triangle identity $(\varepsilon F)\circ(F\eta)=\mathrm{id}_F$ gives $\varepsilon F\circ F\eta=\mathrm{id}_F$, so applying $G$ yields $\mathrm{id}_{GF}$. For associativity, both $\mu\circ T\mu$ and $\mu\circ\mu T$ unfold to $G\varepsilon F\circ G F G\varepsilon F$ versus $G\varepsilon F\circ G\varepsilon FGF$. Naturality of $\varepsilon$ at the morphism $\varepsilon F\colon FGF\Rightarrow F$ reads $\varepsilon\circ FG\varepsilon F=\varepsilon\circ\varepsilon FGF$ (both equal $\varepsilon F\circ\varepsilon$ as maps $FGFGF\Rightarrow F$ after applying $\varepsilon$); applying $G$ and whiskering supplies $G\varepsilon F\circ GFG\varepsilon F=G\varepsilon F\circ G\varepsilon FGF$, which is $\mu\circ T\mu=\mu\circ\mu T$. Hence $(GF,\eta,G\varepsilon F)$ satisfies the monad laws. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem builds toward the converse — that *every* monad arises from an adjunction — and appears again in the Master tier, where the Eilenberg-Moore and Kleisli categories furnish two canonical such adjunctions. The foundational reason the monad laws fall out of the triangle identities is that $\mu = G\varepsilon F$ inherits associativity from the single naturality square of the counit at $\varepsilon F$, and the unit laws are the two triangle identities of [41.03.01] read through $G$ and $F$; this is exactly the calculation that turns the data of an adjunction into a monoid in $[\mathcal{C},\mathcal{C}]$. The construction generalises the free-monoid example of the Beginner tier, where $T$ is the list endofunctor and $\mu$ is concatenation, and it is dual to the comonad $FG$ on $\mathcal{D}$ built from the same data with $\eta$ and $\varepsilon$ trading roles. Putting these together, the central insight is that an adjunction carries strictly more information than its monad — many adjunctions can induce the same $T$ — and the bridge is that the surplus is organised by a category of resolutions whose initial and terminal objects are the Kleisli and Eilenberg-Moore constructions, the comparison theorem cross-referenced to the monadicity unit [41.05.02].

For the free algebra $(TC,\mu_C)$, the structure map is the multiplication $\mu_C\colon T^2C\to TC$.

The free functor sends $C$ to $(TC,\mu_C)$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify that an Eilenberg-Moore algebra $(A,a)$ for the free-monoid (list) monad $T$ on $\mathbf{Set}$ is the same as a monoid structure on $A$, where $a$ sends a list to its product.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The unit law fixes the empty and singleton lists; the associativity law forces the binary product to associate.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define the binary product $m(x,y)=a([x,y])$ and the unit $e=a([\ ])$. The unit law $a\circ\eta_A=\mathrm{id}$ gives $a([x])=x$, so singletons evaluate to themselves. The associativity law $a\circ\mu_A=a\circ Ta$ applied to $\big[[x],[y,z]\big]$ versus $\big[[x,y],[z]\big]$ forces $m(x,m(y,z))=m(m(x,y),z)$, and applied to $\big[[\ ],[x]\big]$ forces $m(e,x)=x$ (similarly on the right). Thus $(A,m,e)$ is a monoid. Conversely any monoid gives $a$ by iterated product, recovering an algebra. The two descriptions coincide.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

For the powerset monad $\mathcal{P}$ on $\mathbf{Set}$ ($\eta_A$ the singleton, $\mu_A$ the union), show that an algebra structure $a\colon\mathcal{P}A\to A$ makes $A$ a complete sup-semilattice with $\bigvee S=a(S)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Read the unit law on singletons and the associativity law on a set of subsets.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Set $\bigvee S=a(S)$. The unit law $a(\{x\})=x$ says the join of a singleton is its element. The associativity law $a\circ\mu_A=a\circ\mathcal{P}a$ applied to a family $\{S_i\}_i$ reads $a\big(\bigcup_i S_i\big)=a\big(\{a(S_i)\}_i\big)$, the statement that $\bigvee\big(\bigcup_i S_i\big)=\bigvee_i\big(\bigvee S_i\big)$. Defining $x\le y$ by $a(\{x,y\})=y$ makes $\le$ a partial order for which $a(S)$ is the least upper bound of $S$. Completeness follows because $a$ is defined on every subset. Hence algebras are exactly complete sup-semilattices, equivalently complete lattices.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Write out Kleisli composition explicitly for the Maybe monad $T A=A+\{\bot\}$ on $\mathbf{Set}$, and identify the Kleisli identity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A Kleisli arrow is a partial function; composition propagates $\bot$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A Kleisli arrow $C\rightsquigarrow D$ is a map $f\colon C\to D+\{\bot\}$, that is, a partial function. The unit $\eta_C(c)=c$ (the left injection) is the Kleisli identity: the everywhere-defined inclusion. The Kleisli composite of $f\colon C\to D+\{\bot\}$ and $g\colon D\to E+\{\bot\}$ is $\mu_E\circ Tg\circ f$, which sends $c$ to $g(f(c))$ when $f(c)\in D$ and to $\bot$ when $f(c)=\bot$. This is precisely composition of partial functions, with $\bot$ absorbing. The Kleisli category of Maybe is the category of sets and partial functions.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the free functor $F^T\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{C}^T$ is left adjoint to the forgetful functor $G^T\colon\mathcal{C}^T\to\mathcal{C}$, and that the monad it induces is the original $(T,\eta,\mu)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A map $(TC,\mu_C)\to(A,a)$ is determined by where it sends $\eta_C$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Given $C\in\mathcal{C}$ and an algebra $(A,a)$, a $T$-algebra morphism $h\colon(TC,\mu_C)\to(A,a)$ satisfies $h\circ\mu_C=a\circ Th$. Precomposing the bijection candidate with $\eta_C$ gives a map $\bar h=h\circ\eta_C\colon C\to A$; conversely a map $f\colon C\to A$ extends to $h=a\circ Tf\colon TC\to A$, which is an algebra morphism by the algebra laws and is the unique one with $h\circ\eta_C=f$ (using the unit law $a\circ\eta_A=\mathrm{id}$ and naturality of $\eta$). These constructions are mutually inverse and natural, giving $\mathcal{C}^T\big(F^TC,(A,a)\big)\cong\mathcal{C}\big(C,G^T(A,a)\big)$. The unit of this adjunction is $\eta$, and the counit at $(A,a)$ is $a$ itself, so the induced multiplication $G^T\varepsilon^T F^T$ has component $\mu_C$ — the original monad.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Kleisli category $\mathcal{C}_T$ is isomorphic to the full subcategory of $\mathcal{C}^T$ spanned by the free algebras.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Send the object $C$ of $\mathcal{C}_T$ to the free algebra $(TC,\mu_C)$ and a Kleisli arrow $f\colon C\to TD$ to its algebra-morphism extension.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define $K\colon\mathcal{C}_T\to\mathcal{C}^T$ on objects by $C\mapsto(TC,\mu_C)$ and on a Kleisli arrow $f\colon C\to TD$ by $K(f)=\mu_D\circ Tf\colon TC\to TD$. Then $K(f)$ is an algebra morphism $(TC,\mu_C)\to(TD,\mu_D)$ by the associativity law, and $K$ preserves Kleisli identities ($K(\eta_C)=\mu_C\circ T\eta_C=\mathrm{id}_{TC}$ by the unit law) and Kleisli composites (by associativity and naturality). The assignment $f\mapsto K(f)$ is a bijection onto $\mathcal{C}^T\big((TC,\mu_C),(TD,\mu_D)\big)$ because every algebra morphism out of a free algebra is determined by its restriction along $\eta_C$, as in Exercise 6. Hence $K$ is full, faithful, and bijective onto the free algebras, an isomorphism onto that full subcategory.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (Eilenberg-Moore and Kleisli resolve every monad).** Let $(T,\eta,\mu)$ be a monad on $\mathcal{C}$. The free-forgetful adjunction $F^T\dashv G^T$ on $\mathcal{C}^T$ and the Kleisli adjunction $F_T\dashv G_T$ on $\mathcal{C}_T$ each induce exactly $(T,\eta,\mu)$ [ref: TODO_REF Eilenberg-Moore 1965]. Thus a monad that looks like the shadow of an adjunction is one: every monad is $GF$ for at least these two adjoint pairs.

**Theorem (the category of resolutions; Kleisli initial, Eilenberg-Moore terminal).** Fix a monad $T$ on $\mathcal{C}$ and let $\mathbf{Adj}(T)$ be the category whose objects are adjunctions $F\dashv G$, $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$, inducing $T$, and whose morphisms $(\mathcal{D},F,G)\to(\mathcal{D}',F',G')$ are functors $H\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{D}'$ with $HF=F'$ and $G'H=G$. Then the Kleisli resolution is *initial* and the Eilenberg-Moore resolution is *terminal* in $\mathbf{Adj}(T)$ [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998]. The terminal mediating functor is the *comparison functor* $K^T\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}^T$, $d\mapsto(Gd,\ G\varepsilon_d)$, the unique functor over $\mathcal{C}$ with $K^TF=F^T$; the initial mediating functor $K_T\colon\mathcal{C}_T\to\mathcal{D}$ is the comparison out of the Kleisli category.

**Theorem (monadicity; statement).** An adjunction $F\dashv G$ is *monadic* when its comparison functor $K^T\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}^T$ is an equivalence; Beck's theorem characterises monadicity by $G$ creating coequalisers of $G$-split pairs. The free-monoid, free-group, free-module, powerset, and compact-Hausdorff adjunctions are all monadic, so $\mathbf{Mon}$, $\mathbf{Grp}$, $R\text{-}\mathbf{Mod}$, complete sup-semilattices, and $\mathbf{CHaus}$ are recovered as Eilenberg-Moore categories. The full statement and proof, together with the Lawvere-theory presentation of finitary monads, are developed in [41.05.02].

**Theorem (comonads, dually).** A *comonad* $(D,\varepsilon,\delta)$ on $\mathcal{C}$ is an endofunctor with $\varepsilon\colon D\Rightarrow\mathrm{Id}$ (counit) and $\delta\colon D\Rightarrow D^2$ (comultiplication) satisfying the dual laws; it is a comonoid in $[\mathcal{C},\mathcal{C}]$. Every adjunction $F\dashv G$ gives the comonad $FG$ on $\mathcal{D}$, with *coalgebras* dual to algebras. The *store* (costate) comonad and the *stream* comonad model context-dependent computation as the dual of effectful computation, and the coalgebras of a comonad on a presheaf topos model state machines and transition systems.

**Synthesis.** Putting these together, a monad is the algebraic residue of an adjunction, and the two canonical adjunctions that resolve it sit at the extreme ends of the category of all resolutions: the Kleisli category is the initial resolution, built from the free algebras alone, and the Eilenberg-Moore category is the terminal resolution, built from all algebras. The foundational reason both exist for every monad is that the monad axioms are exactly the structure needed to define algebra morphisms and Kleisli composition, so the data of $(T,\eta,\mu)$ already carries its own free-forgetful adjunction. This generalises the free-forgetful gallery of [41.03.01]: monoids, groups, modules, complete lattices, and convex sets are the Eilenberg-Moore categories of the free-monoid, free-group, free-module, powerset, and distribution monads, and monadicity [41.05.02] is the statement that an algebraic category is its own category of algebras. This is exactly the perspective that makes the codensity monad $\mathrm{Ran}_G G$ — the right Kan extension formed when $G$ has no left adjoint — the correct repair of a missing left adjoint, the bridge to the Kan theory of [41.06.02], where the comparison functors of this unit become the universal arrows into a Kan extension. The central insight is that "monad", "monoid in $[\mathcal{C},\mathcal{C}]$", "algebraic theory presented by operations and equations", and "shadow of an adjunction" name one structure from four directions.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (the Eilenberg-Moore adjunction induces $T$).** *For a monad $(T,\eta,\mu)$, the free functor $F^T\colon C\mapsto(TC,\mu_C)$ is left adjoint to the forgetful $G^T$, with unit $\eta$ and counit $\varepsilon^T_{(A,a)}=a$, and the induced monad is $(T,\eta,\mu)$.*

*Proof.* That $(TC,\mu_C)$ is an algebra is the associativity and left-unit laws of $T$. For $C\in\mathcal{C}$ and $(A,a)\in\mathcal{C}^T$ define $\Phi\colon\mathcal{C}^T(F^TC,(A,a))\to\mathcal{C}(C,A)$ by $\Phi(h)=h\circ\eta_C$ and $\Psi\colon\mathcal{C}(C,A)\to\mathcal{C}^T(F^TC,(A,a))$ by $\Psi(f)=a\circ Tf$. The map $\Psi(f)$ is an algebra morphism: $\Psi(f)\circ\mu_C = a\circ Tf\circ\mu_C = a\circ\mu_A\circ T^2f = a\circ Ta\circ T^2f = a\circ T(\Psi(f))$, using naturality of $\mu$ and the algebra associativity law. Then $\Phi\Psi(f)=a\circ Tf\circ\eta_C=a\circ\eta_A\circ f=f$ by naturality of $\eta$ and the algebra unit law; and $\Psi\Phi(h)=a\circ T(h\circ\eta_C)=a\circ Th\circ T\eta_C = h\circ\mu_C\circ T\eta_C = h$, using that $h$ is an algebra morphism, $h\circ\mu_C = a\circ Th$ rearranged, together with the monad left-unit law $\mu_C\circ T\eta_C=\mathrm{id}$. The bijection is natural, so $F^T\dashv G^T$. The counit at $(A,a)$ is $\Psi(\mathrm{id}_A)=a$, and the induced multiplication is $G^T\varepsilon^T F^T$ with component at $C$ equal to $\varepsilon^T_{(TC,\mu_C)}=\mu_C$, while the unit is $\eta$. Hence the induced monad is $(T,\eta,\mu)$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (the comparison functor is the unique mediating functor; Eilenberg-Moore is terminal).** *Let $F\dashv G$ with counit $\varepsilon$ induce $T$. The assignment $K^T(d)=(Gd,\ G\varepsilon_d)$, $K^T(g)=Gg$, is a functor $\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}^T$ with $G^T K^T=G$ and $K^T F=F^T$, and it is the unique such functor over $\mathcal{C}$.*

*Proof.* That $(Gd,G\varepsilon_d)$ is an algebra: the unit law is $G\varepsilon_d\circ\eta_{Gd}=\mathrm{id}_{Gd}$, the second triangle identity of [41.03.01]; the associativity law $G\varepsilon_d\circ\mu_{Gd}=G\varepsilon_d\circ T(G\varepsilon_d)$ reduces, with $\mu=G\varepsilon F$, to naturality of $\varepsilon$ at $\varepsilon_d$ after applying $G$. For $g\colon d\to d'$, $Gg$ is an algebra morphism by naturality of $\varepsilon$. Then $G^TK^T=G$ on the nose, and $K^TF(C)=(GFC,G\varepsilon_{FC})=(TC,\mu_C)=F^TC$, so $K^TF=F^T$. For uniqueness, any functor $H\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}^T$ with $G^TH=G$ has $H(d)=(Gd,a_d)$ for some structure map $a_d$; the requirement that $H$ send the counit component to the algebra structure (forced by $HF=F^T$ together with the adjunction triangle for $\varepsilon$) pins $a_d=G\varepsilon_d$. So $H=K^T$. This exhibits $(\mathcal{C}^T,F^T,G^T)$ as terminal in $\mathbf{Adj}(T)$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (the Kleisli category is the free algebras; Kleisli is initial).** *The functor $K_T\colon\mathcal{C}_T\to\mathcal{C}^T$, $C\mapsto(TC,\mu_C)$, $f\mapsto\mu_{D}\circ Tf$ for $f\colon C\to TD$, is full, faithful, identity-on-the-free-algebras, and exhibits $\mathcal{C}_T$ as initial in $\mathbf{Adj}(T)$.*

*Proof.* Functoriality: $K_T(\eta_C)=\mu_C\circ T\eta_C=\mathrm{id}_{TC}$ by the right-unit law, and for $f\colon C\to TD$, $g\colon D\to TE$ the Kleisli composite $\mu_E\circ Tg\circ f$ maps under $K_T$ to $\mu_E\circ T(\mu_E\circ Tg\circ f)$, which equals $(\mu_E\circ Tg)\circ(\mu_D\circ Tf)$ by the associativity law and naturality of $\mu$ — the composite of the images. Fullness and faithfulness: algebra morphisms $(TC,\mu_C)\to(TD,\mu_D)$ are in bijection with maps $C\to TD$ by restriction along $\eta_C$ and extension by $\Psi$ as in Proposition 1, and this bijection is exactly $K_T$ on hom-sets. For initiality, given any resolution $F\dashv G$ inducing $T$, the unique functor $L\colon\mathcal{C}_T\to\mathcal{D}$ with $LF_T=F$ and $GL=G_T$ is forced on objects by $L(C)=FC$ and on a Kleisli arrow $f\colon C\to TD=GFD$ by $L(f)=\varepsilon_{FD}\circ Ff$, the transpose of $f$; functoriality and the two equations follow from the triangle identities. Uniqueness is forced because every object of $\mathcal{C}_T$ is in the image of $F_T$ and every arrow factors through units. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (the two comparisons compose to the inclusion of free algebras).** *The composite $K^T\circ K_T\colon\mathcal{C}_T\to\mathcal{C}^T$ of the Eilenberg-Moore comparison of the Kleisli adjunction with the Kleisli initial map equals the canonical full embedding $\mathcal{C}_T\hookrightarrow\mathcal{C}^T$ onto the free algebras.*

*Proof.* The Kleisli adjunction $F_T\dashv G_T$ has counit whose component at $C$ (an object of $\mathcal{C}_T$) is the Kleisli arrow $\mathrm{id}_{TC}\colon TC\to TC$ viewed as $TC\rightsquigarrow C$. Its Eilenberg-Moore comparison $K^T$ sends the object $C$ to $(G_T C, G_T\varepsilon^{\mathrm{Kl}}_C)=(TC,\mu_C)$, the free algebra, and sends a Kleisli arrow to the $G_T$-image of its transpose, which is $\mu_D\circ Tf$. This is exactly $K_T$ as defined in Proposition 3. Both routes therefore land on the free algebra $(TC,\mu_C)$ and agree on morphisms, so the composite is the full embedding onto the free algebras, recovering the isomorphism of the Intermediate Exercise 7. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Adjunctions [41.03.01].** A monad is by construction the data $GF$, $\eta$, $G\varepsilon F$ extracted from an adjunction $F\dashv G$, and the monad axioms are the triangle identities and the counit naturality square read through $G$ and $F$. Conversely the free-forgetful adjunctions of [41.03.01] — free monoid, free group, free module, free vector space — are precisely the adjunctions whose Eilenberg-Moore categories are $\mathbf{Mon}$, $\mathbf{Grp}$, $R\text{-}\mathbf{Mod}$, and $\mathbf{Vect}_k$, so this unit explains why the entire free-forgetful gallery is a single phenomenon.

- **Natural transformations and the endofunctor category [41.01.02].** A monad is a monoid in the strict monoidal category $([\mathcal{C},\mathcal{C}],\circ,\mathrm{Id})$ of [41.01.02], and the whiskered composites $T\mu$, $\mu T$, $T\eta$, $\eta T$ appearing in the monad laws are the horizontal composites defined there. The associativity and unit squares are the monoid axioms transcribed into the $2$-categorical language of $\mathbf{Cat}$.

- **Monadicity and Lawvere theories [41.05.02].** The comparison functor $K^T$ introduced here is an equivalence exactly when the adjunction is monadic, and Beck's monadicity theorem — characterising this by the creation of $G$-split coequalisers — is proved in [41.05.02], where finitary monads on $\mathbf{Set}$ are identified with Lawvere algebraic theories, making "algebras for a monad" and "models of an equational theory" the same notion.

- **Kan extensions and the codensity monad [41.06.02].** When a functor $G\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}$ has no left adjoint, the right Kan extension $\mathrm{Ran}_G G$ still carries a monad structure, the *codensity monad*, which is the universal monad through which $G$ factors; this is the Kan-theoretic generalisation of the monad of an adjunction developed in [41.06.02], where the comparison functors of this unit reappear as universal arrows into a Kan extension.

- **Limits and colimits [41.02.01].** The forgetful functor $G^T\colon\mathcal{C}^T\to\mathcal{C}$ creates all limits and those colimits preserved by $T$ and $T^2$, so the limit theory of [41.02.01] computes limits of algebras objectwise in $\mathcal{C}$; Beck's monadicity criterion is itself a statement about the creation of a specific class of coequalisers, tying the algebra category's structure to the colimit theory of [41.02.01].

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The monad concept emerged in the early 1960s under several names. Roger Godement had used the iterated comonad of a sheaf-cohomology resolution — the "standard construction" — and the abstract pattern was isolated as a *triple* (the term long preferred by the Eilenberg school) and as a *monad* (Mac Lane's later coinage, by analogy with the monoid it generalises). The decisive papers appeared together: Samuel Eilenberg and John Moore's *Adjoint functors and triples* (1965) [ref: TODO_REF Eilenberg-Moore 1965] constructed the category of algebras now bearing their names and proved that it resolves the monad, while Heinrich Kleisli's *Every standard construction is induced by a pair of adjoint functors* (1965) [ref: TODO_REF Kleisli 1965] constructed the other canonical resolution from the free algebras alone. Peter Huber had observed shortly before that every adjunction yields a monad, posing the converse that these two papers answered.

The systematisation into the two-sided universal property — Kleisli initial, Eilenberg-Moore terminal among resolutions — and the comparison functors belongs to Mac Lane's *Categories for the Working Mathematician* (1971; 2nd ed. 1998) [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998], Chapter VI, alongside Jonathan Beck's monadicity theorem from his 1967 Columbia thesis. The recognition that the algebras of the free-monoid, free-group, and powerset monads are monoids, groups, and complete lattices connected monad theory to William Lawvere's 1963 functorial semantics of algebraic theories, and Eugenio Moggi's 1989 *Notions of computation and monads* transplanted the Kleisli category into programming-language semantics, where a monad models a class of computational effects and a Kleisli arrow is an effectful program.
