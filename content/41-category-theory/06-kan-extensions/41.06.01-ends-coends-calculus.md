## Intuition [Beginner]

Some constructions in mathematics depend on a label in two opposite ways at once: a quantity grows when the label moves forward and shrinks when the same label moves backward. The cleanest example is a comparison between two things tagged by the same name. To compare a measurement at a point with another measurement at that same point, you need the point in both roles: as the source you read from and as the target you write to. A rule that uses one label in both roles is the kind of thing this unit learns to package.

When a family of pieces is glued together compatibly across every label, there are two natural single objects to extract. One is the part that all the pieces agree on no matter how you shuffle the labels: the common core, the most you can say that survives every comparison. The other is the whole formed by piling all the pieces together and then declaring two contributions equal whenever a label-shuffle relates them: the merged total, the least you can collapse to without losing anything.

Think of an average over every way a structure can map to itself. The first object, the common core, is the universal place that maps consistently into all the self-comparisons. The second, the merged total, is the universal place that all the self-comparisons map consistently out of. Both are averages of a family indexed by labels that pull in two directions. The end is the common core; the coend is the merged total.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a long row of boxes, one box for each label. Each box holds the value of a rule that reads the label as a source and writes it as a target at the same time. Arrows between labels act on the boxes in two directions: a forward arrow pushes one box ahead, and the same arrow pulls another box back. A compatible family is a choice that respects both pushes and pulls everywhere.

| object | what it collects | universal direction |
|---|---|---|
| the end (common core) | the part every box agrees on | maps consistently *into* all boxes |
| the coend (merged total) | all boxes piled up, glued by label-shuffles | all boxes map consistently *out* |
| a natural comparison | one number per box, respecting both directions | named by the end of a hom-rule |

![A horizontal row of boxes indexed by labels, each box fed by a forward arrow from the left and a backward arrow from the right; above the row a single common-core node sends one consistent arrow down into every box, and below the row a single merged-total node receives one consistent arrow up from every box, the two nodes labelled end and coend](/img/ends-coends-calculus-placeholder.svg)

The two nodes outside the row are the answers. The node on top is the most economical source feeding every box compatibly; the node on the bottom is the most economical target collecting every box compatibly. Reading the same row two ways gives the two constructions, and most calculations in this unit move between the top reading and the bottom reading.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Work in a small world with two labels, $a$ and $b$, and one forward arrow $r$ from $a$ to $b$ besides the do-nothing arrows. A rule reads a label twice, once forward and once backward, and returns a set. Set the rule to return, at the matched pair $(a,a)$, the two-element set $\{0,1\}$; at $(b,b)$, the one-element set $\{*\}$; and across the arrow $r$ it sends $0$ and $1$ both to $*$ when pushing forward, while pulling backward sends $*$ to $0$.

Step 1. Find the common core, the choices agreeing across $r$. A choice picks one element at $a$ and one at $b$. Compatibility across $r$ demands that pushing the $a$-choice forward and pulling the $b$-choice backward land on matching answers. Pulling $*$ backward gives $0$, so the $a$-choice must be $0$.

Step 2. Read off the common core. Only the pair (choose $0$ at $a$, choose $*$ at $b$) survives. The common core is a one-element set: a single compatible choice.

Step 3. Find the merged total. Pile the boxes: $\{0,1\}$ from $(a,a)$ and $\{*\}$ from $(b,b)$, three elements before gluing. The arrow $r$ glues $0$ to $*$, since pushing $0$ forward gives $*$. After gluing, $0$ and $*$ are one element, and $1$ is left alone.

What this tells us: the common core had one element, while the merged total had two. These are the two averages of the same family. The end shrank the family to what everything shares; the coend collapsed the family to its merged whole.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\mathcal{C}$ and $\mathcal{D}$ be categories and $F\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\times\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ a bifunctor, contravariant in its first variable and covariant in its second. Such an $F$ is a *profunctor-shaped* datum: it reads an object as both a source and a target. The matched diagonal values $F(c,c)$ are the objects this unit averages over [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998].

**Definition (dinatural transformation; wedge; cowedge).** A *wedge* from an object $d\in\mathcal{D}$ to $F$ is a family of arrows $\omega_c\colon d\to F(c,c)$, one for each object $c$, such that for every arrow $f\colon c\to c'$ the *dinaturality* square
$$
F(c,f)\circ\omega_c \;=\; F(f,c')\circ\omega_{c'}
$$
commutes, where $F(c,f)=F(\mathrm{id}_c,f)$ acts on the covariant slot and $F(f,c')=F(f,\mathrm{id}_{c'})$ on the contravariant slot. Dually a *cowedge* from $F$ to $d$ is a family $\pi_c\colon F(c,c)\to d$ with $\pi_c\circ F(f,c')=\pi_{c'}\circ F(c,f)$ for all $f\colon c\to c'$. A *dinatural transformation* between two such bifunctors is the evident family making the two-sided hexagon commute; a wedge is the dinatural transformation from a constant bifunctor.

**Definition (end; coend).** The *end* $\int_{c\in\mathcal{C}}F(c,c)$ is the universal wedge: an object $\int_{c}F(c,c)$ with a wedge $(\lambda_c\colon\int_{c}F(c,c)\to F(c,c))$ such that every wedge $(\omega_c\colon d\to F(c,c))$ factors as $\omega_c=\lambda_c\circ\bar{\omega}$ through a unique $\bar{\omega}\colon d\to\int_{c}F(c,c)$. The *coend* $\int^{c\in\mathcal{C}}F(c,c)$ is the universal cowedge: an object with a cowedge $(\iota_c\colon F(c,c)\to\int^{c}F(c,c))$ through which every cowedge factors uniquely. The integral-sign notation is due to Yoneda [ref: TODO_REF Yoneda 1960]; the subscript marks the end (a limit) and the superscript the coend (a colimit). The notation introduced here — $\int_{c}$, $\int^{c}$, the copower $S\cdot d$ and power $d^{S}$ of $d\in\mathcal{D}$ by a set $S$, the cotensor $\pitchfork$ and tensor $\otimes$ of an enriched setting, and $\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{C})$ for the twisted-arrow category — is recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

**Definition (twisted-arrow category).** The *twisted-arrow category* $\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{C})$ has objects the arrows $f\colon c\to c'$ of $\mathcal{C}$ and morphisms $f\to g$ the pairs $(u,v)$ with $g=v\circ f\circ u$ (an inward factorisation). A bifunctor $F\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\times\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ restricts along the projection $\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{C})\to\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\times\mathcal{C}$, $f\mapsto(\mathrm{dom}\,f,\mathrm{cod}\,f)$, to a diagram on $\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{C})$, and the end and coend are computed as an ordinary limit and colimit of that diagram.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *An end is not a limit over $\mathcal{C}$.* The end ranges over the twisted-arrow category, not over $\mathcal{C}$; reading $\int_{c}F(c,c)$ as $\lim_{c}F(c,c)$ for some functor of one variable discards the contravariant slot and the dinaturality coherence that ties the two slots together.

- *Dinaturality is weaker than naturality and does not compose.* Dinatural transformations between bifunctors need not compose to a dinatural transformation in general; the wedge case (one side constant) is the safe special case the (co)end packages, which is why ends are defined as universal wedges rather than as limits of a dinaturality 2-category.

- *The copower $S\cdot d$ is a coproduct, not a product.* In the coend $\int^{c}W(c)\cdot F(c)$ the symbol $W(c)\cdot F(c)$ is the $W(c)$-fold coproduct of $F(c)$; confusing it with the power $F(c)^{W(c)}$ (a product) swaps a weighted colimit for a weighted limit and reverses every universal arrow.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The keystone of the calculus expresses the set of natural transformations between two functors as an end. It is the bridge from the Yoneda viewpoint of [41.04.02] into the integral notation.

**Theorem (natural transformations as an end).** Let $F,G\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ be functors into a locally small category, and define the bifunctor $\mathcal{D}(F-,G-)\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\times\mathcal{C}\to\mathbf{Set}$ by $(c,c')\mapsto\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gc')$. Then there is a bijection, natural in $F$ and $G$,
$$
\mathrm{Nat}(F,G)\;\cong\;\int_{c\in\mathcal{C}}\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gc),
$$
identifying a natural transformation with a universal wedge over the hom-bifunctor [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998].

*Proof.* A wedge from the one-point set $1$ to $\mathcal{D}(F-,G-)$ is a family of elements $\alpha_c\in\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gc)$, i.e. a family of arrows $\alpha_c\colon Fc\to Gc$, one per object $c$. The dinaturality condition for the wedge at an arrow $f\colon c\to c'$ reads
$$
\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gf)(\alpha_c)\;=\;\mathcal{D}(Ff,Gc')(\alpha_{c'}),
$$
where $\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gf)$ is post-composition by $Gf$ and $\mathcal{D}(Ff,Gc')$ is pre-composition by $Ff$. Spelled out, the left side is $Gf\circ\alpha_c$ and the right side is $\alpha_{c'}\circ Ff$, so the condition is $Gf\circ\alpha_c=\alpha_{c'}\circ Ff$ — exactly the naturality square of $\alpha\colon F\Rightarrow G$. Hence wedges from $1$ to $\mathcal{D}(F-,G-)$ are precisely natural transformations $F\Rightarrow G$.

By the universal property of the end, wedges from $1$ to the hom-bifunctor are in bijection with maps $1\to\int_{c}\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gc)$, that is, with elements of the end. Composing the two bijections gives $\mathrm{Nat}(F,G)\cong\int_{c}\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gc)$, sending $\alpha$ to the element it names and back. Naturality in $F$ and $G$ holds because both sides are computed from the same hom-bifunctor and the bijection is induced by the identity wedge, which is preserved by whiskering. $\square$

**Bridge.** This end formula builds toward the entire (co)end calculus and appears again in [41.06.02], where it is the engine of the Kan-extension formulas. The foundational reason a single wedge encodes a natural transformation is that dinaturality over the hom-bifunctor unwinds to the naturality square, so the integral $\int_{c}\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gc)$ *is* the hom-set of the functor category read as a universal cone; this is exactly the end-shaped repackaging of the Yoneda viewpoint of [41.04.02], where natural transformations out of a representable were computed by evaluation at the identity. Putting these together, the ninja-Yoneda density formula $F\cong\int^{c}\mathcal{C}(-,c)\cdot Fc$ and the end form proved here are dual halves of one principle, and the bridge is from "compatible family of arrows" to "single universal object", which generalises the limit-and-colimit machinery of [41.02.01] from one-variable diagrams to bifunctors varying in two opposite directions at once.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the ninja-Yoneda (co-Yoneda) identity $\int^{c}\mathcal{C}(c,d)\cdot Fc\cong Fd$ for a covariant functor $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathbf{Set}$, where $\mathcal{C}(c,d)\cdot Fc$ is the copower.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Map the cowedge out of the coend to $Fd$ using the action of $F$ on arrows, and check the universal property against an arbitrary target set.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

This is a cowedge: for $h\colon c\to c'$, both routes send $(h^*g',x)$ and $(g',F(h)x)$ to $F(g')(F(h)x)=F(g'\circ h)(x)$, so the coend relation is respected. Given any cowedge $(\mu_c\colon\mathcal{C}(c,d)\cdot Fc\to S)$, the dinaturality at $\mathrm{id}_d$ forces every $(g,x)$ with $g\colon c\to d$ to be identified with $(\mathrm{id}_d,F(g)x)$ in the coend, so a map out of the coend is determined by its value on $(\mathrm{id}_d,y)$, $y\in Fd$. Hence $\theta$ is a bijection on cowedges, identifying the coend with $Fd$. This is the density theorem of [41.04.02] in coend form.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the Fubini theorem for coends: for $H\colon(\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{B})^{\mathrm{op}}\times(\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{B})\to\mathcal{D}$ that one packages as a bifunctor separately in each pair of slots, $\int^{(c,b)}H\cong\int^{c}\int^{b}H\cong\int^{b}\int^{c}H$, whenever the inner coends exist.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Both iterated coends and the joint coend are colimits over the relevant twisted-arrow categories; exhibit a comparison of cowedges and use that colimits commute with colimits.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The joint coend is the colimit of the restriction of $H$ to $\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{B})$, and $\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{B})\cong\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{C})\times\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{B})$ since an arrow of a product is a pair of arrows and inward factorisations split coordinatewise. A colimit over a product category is computed as an iterated colimit in either order — colimits commute with colimits — so the colimit over $\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{C})\times\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{B})$ equals $\int^{c}(\int^{b}H)$ and equals $\int^{b}(\int^{c}H)$. A cowedge out of the joint coend restricts to a cowedge in each variable separately and conversely, and the universal such object is unique up to canonical isomorphism, giving the three-way isomorphism. The end version is dual, with limits commuting with limits. The named consequence is that ends in independent variables commute, the manipulation that makes the calculus computational.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (ends and coends as twisted-arrow (co)limits).** For $F\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\times\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ with $\mathcal{C}$ small and $\mathcal{D}$ complete, the end exists and is computed as the limit
$$
\int_{c}F(c,c)\;\cong\;\lim\Bigl(\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{C})^{\mathrm{op}}\xrightarrow{\ }\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\times\mathcal{C}\xrightarrow{\ F\ }\mathcal{D}\Bigr),
$$
and, when $\mathcal{D}$ is cocomplete, the coend is the colimit of the same composite over $\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{C})$. Equivalently the end is the equalizer of the two maps $\prod_{c}F(c,c)\rightrightarrows\prod_{f\colon c\to c'}F(c,c')$ induced by the two slot actions, and the coend is the coequalizer of the dual maps between coproducts. The equalizer presentation is the working computation in $\mathbf{Set}$ and in any category where the relevant products and coproducts are explicit.

**Theorem (the (co)Yoneda / ninja-Yoneda lemma).** For $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathbf{Set}$ and $G\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathbf{Set}$,
$$
Fc\;\cong\;\int^{d}\mathcal{C}(d,c)\cdot Fd,\qquad
Fc\;\cong\;\int_{d}\bigl(Fd\bigr)^{\mathcal{C}(c,d)},\qquad
G\;\cong\;\int^{d}Gd\cdot\mathcal{C}(-,d),
$$
the coend and end forms reducing any (co)representable-weighted (co)limit to evaluation. These identities are the density theorem of [41.04.02] written as rewrite rules: the coend "integrates away" a hom-functor in a slot by setting the bound variable to the free one. The same reduction proves $\mathrm{Nat}(F,G)\cong\int_{c}\mathbf{Set}(Fc,Gc)$ from the Key theorem and extends, with no change of argument, to the enriched setting where $\mathbf{Set}$ is replaced by a closed symmetric monoidal $\mathcal{V}$ and the copower $S\cdot d$ by the tensor $S\otimes d$ and the power by the cotensor $S\pitchfork d$.

**Theorem (weighted limits and colimits as (co)ends).** Let $W\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathbf{Set}$ be a weight and $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ a diagram in a category with the needed (co)powers and (co)limits. The *weighted limit* and *weighted colimit* are
$$
\lim{}^{W}F\;=\;\int_{c}\bigl(F c\bigr)^{W c}\;=\;\int_{c}W c\pitchfork Fc,\qquad
\mathrm{colim}^{W}F\;=\;\int^{c}W c\cdot Fc,
$$
the end of the cotensor and the coend of the tensor. The conical case is the constant weight $W=\Delta 1$: then $\lim^{\Delta 1}F=\lim_{\mathcal{C}}F$ and $\mathrm{colim}^{\Delta 1}F=\mathrm{colim}_{\mathcal{C}}F$, recovering ordinary limits and colimits. The defining adjunction $\mathcal{D}(\mathrm{colim}^{W}F,d)\cong\int_{c}\mathbf{Set}(Wc,\mathcal{D}(Fc,d))=[\mathcal{C},\mathbf{Set}](W,\mathcal{D}(F-,d))$ exhibits the weighted colimit as the object representing weighted cocones, the form Kelly takes as primitive in the enriched theory [ref: TODO_REF Kelly 1982].

**Theorem (the functor tensor product and the bar construction).** For functors $G\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathcal{D}$ and $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathbf{Set}$ in a cocomplete $\mathcal{D}$, the *functor tensor product* is the coend $G\otimes_{\mathcal{C}}F=\int^{c}Gc\cdot Fc$, a weighted colimit of $G$ by $F$. When $\mathcal{C}$ carries a simplicial or monoidal structure the *two-sided bar construction* $B(G,\mathcal{C},F)$ is the simplicial object whose geometric realization is this coend, with $n$-simplices $\coprod Gc_0\cdot\mathcal{C}(c_0,c_1)\cdots\mathcal{C}(c_{n-1},c_n)\cdot Fc_n$ and the coend arising as the realization $\int^{[n]\in\Delta}\Delta^{n}\cdot B_n$. The *nerve* of a small category $\mathcal{C}$ is the simplicial set $N\mathcal{C}$ with $N\mathcal{C}_n=\mathcal{C}([n],\mathcal{C})$ the chains of $n$ composable arrows, and its geometric realization is the coend $|N\mathcal{C}|=\int^{[n]}\Delta^{n}\times N\mathcal{C}_n$, the canonical example in which a coend over the simplex category glues standard simplices along face and degeneracy maps.

**Theorem (geometric realization as a coend).** The geometric realization of a simplicial set $X\colon\Delta^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathbf{Set}$ is the coend
$$
|X|\;=\;\int^{[n]\in\Delta}X_n\cdot\Delta^{n}\;=\;\int^{[n]}X_n\times\Delta^{n},
$$
the tensor of the discrete set $X_n$ with the topological standard simplex $\Delta^{n}$, glued by the face and degeneracy identifications encoded in the dinaturality over $\Delta$. By the (co)Yoneda lemma this is the left Kan extension along the Yoneda embedding $\Delta\hookrightarrow[\Delta^{\mathrm{op}},\mathbf{Set}]$ of the cosimplicial space $[n]\mapsto\Delta^{n}$, so realization is the unique colimit-preserving extension sending the representable $\Delta[n]$ to $\Delta^{n}$. Singular homology, the singular-set functor, and the realization-nerve adjunction all read off this one coend.

**Synthesis.** Putting these together, the (co)end calculus is the manipulable face of the density theorem of [41.04.02], and every formula here is the universal cone of [41.02.01] read for a bifunctor varying in two opposite directions. The foundational reason a coend computes geometric realization, the bar construction, and the functor tensor product alike is that each is a weighted colimit $\int^{c}Wc\cdot Fc$, so they differ only in the weight; the (co)Yoneda lemma is exactly the rule that integrates away a representable weight, and this is dual to the end form $\mathrm{Nat}(F,G)\cong\int_{c}\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gc)$ that names natural transformations. The central insight is that the Fubini theorem makes these integrals associative and commutative in independent variables, so the Kan-extension formula $\mathrm{Lan}_{K}F\cong\int^{c}\mathcal{D}(Kc,-)\cdot Fc$ of [41.06.02] is computed by the same moves, which generalises the conical limits and colimits of [41.02.01] to the weighted setting and carries over verbatim to enriched categories with $\mathbf{Set}$ replaced by a monoidal $\mathcal{V}$. The bridge is that the entire toolkit — Fubini, (co)Yoneda, weighted (co)limits — exists so that the Kan-extension formulas of the next unit can be written and evaluated as integrals.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (the end is the equalizer of the two slot actions).** *For $F\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\times\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ with $\mathcal{C}$ small and $\mathcal{D}$ complete, $\int_{c}F(c,c)$ is the equalizer of the parallel pair*
$$
\textstyle\prod_{c}F(c,c)\;\underset{t}{\overset{s}{\rightrightarrows}}\;\prod_{f\colon c\to c'}F(c,c'),
$$
*where $s$ has $(c\to c')$-component $F(c,f)\circ\mathrm{pr}_c$ and $t$ has component $F(f,c')\circ\mathrm{pr}_{c'}$.*

*Proof.* A map $d\to\prod_{c}F(c,c)$ is a family $(\omega_c\colon d\to F(c,c))$. Composing with $s$ gives, in the $f\colon c\to c'$ slot, $F(c,f)\circ\omega_c$; composing with $t$ gives $F(f,c')\circ\omega_{c'}$. So $s\circ\omega=t\circ\omega$ holds if and only if $F(c,f)\circ\omega_c=F(f,c')\circ\omega_{c'}$ for every $f$, which is the dinaturality condition defining a wedge. The equalizer is therefore the universal object through which every wedge factors, i.e. the end. Completeness of $\mathcal{D}$ provides the two products and the equalizer, so the end exists. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (natural transformations form an end).** *For $F,G\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ locally small, $\mathrm{Nat}(F,G)\cong\int_{c}\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gc)$, naturally in $F$ and $G$.*

*Proof.* By Proposition 1 the end $\int_{c}\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gc)$ is the equalizer in $\mathbf{Set}$ of $s,t\colon\prod_{c}\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gc)\rightrightarrows\prod_{f\colon c\to c'}\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gc')$ with $s(\alpha)_f=Gf\circ\alpha_c$ and $t(\alpha)_f=\alpha_{c'}\circ Ff$. An element of the equalizer is a family $(\alpha_c)$ with $Gf\circ\alpha_c=\alpha_{c'}\circ Ff$ for all $f$, which is precisely a natural transformation $\alpha\colon F\Rightarrow G$. The assignment $\alpha\mapsto(\alpha_c)$ is a bijection onto the equalizer, inverse to reading the components back off. Naturality in $F$ along $\beta\colon F'\Rightarrow F$ acts by whiskering $\alpha\mapsto\alpha\circ\beta$ and matches the induced map on equalizers; naturality in $G$ is dual. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (the co-Yoneda lemma).** *For $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathbf{Set}$, the map $\theta\colon\int^{d}\mathcal{C}(d,c)\cdot Fd\to Fc$, $\theta[g,x]=F(g)(x)$, is a bijection.*

*Proof.* As a $\mathbf{Set}$-coend, $\int^{d}\mathcal{C}(d,c)\cdot Fd=\big(\coprod_{d}\mathcal{C}(d,c)\times Fd\big)/\!\sim$, where $\sim$ identifies, for $h\colon d\to d'$, the element $(g'\circ h,\,x)$ at $d$ with $(g',\,F(h)x)$ at $d'$ for $g'\colon d'\to c$ and $x\in Fd$. The map $\theta[g,x]=F(g)(x)$ is well defined: $F(g'\circ h)(x)=F(g')(F(h)x)$ by functoriality, so it respects $\sim$. It is surjective: $y\in Fc$ is $\theta[\mathrm{id}_c,y]$. It is injective: every class has a representative with $g=\mathrm{id}_c$, since $[g,x]=[\mathrm{id}_c\circ g,x]\sim[\mathrm{id}_c,F(g)x]$, and $[\mathrm{id}_c,y]=[\mathrm{id}_c,y']$ forces $y=y'$ because no further relation touches the $\mathrm{id}_c$-fibre. Hence $\theta$ is a bijection, natural in $c$. The dual end form $\int_{d}(Fd)^{\mathcal{C}(c,d)}\cong Fc$ follows by applying this in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$ together with Proposition 2. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Fubini for coends).** *For $H\colon(\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{B})^{\mathrm{op}}\times(\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{B})\to\mathcal{D}$ with $\mathcal{D}$ cocomplete, $\int^{(c,b)}H\cong\int^{c}\!\int^{b}H\cong\int^{b}\!\int^{c}H$.*

*Proof.* The coend $\int^{(c,b)}H$ is the colimit of $H$ restricted along $\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{B})\to(\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{B})^{\mathrm{op}}\times(\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{B})$. An arrow of $\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{B}$ is a pair of arrows, and an inward factorisation $(u,v)$ splits coordinatewise, so $\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{B})\cong\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{C})\times\mathrm{TwArr}(\mathcal{B})$ as categories. The colimit of a diagram over a product category is computed as an iterated colimit, $\mathrm{colim}_{X\times Y}\cong\mathrm{colim}_{X}\mathrm{colim}_{Y}\cong\mathrm{colim}_{Y}\mathrm{colim}_{X}$, because colimits commute with colimits and both compute the left adjoint to the constant-diagram functor $\mathcal{D}\to[X\times Y,\mathcal{D}]$. Reading the inner colimit as the coend in $b$ and the outer as the coend in $c$ (and the reverse) gives the two iterated coends, each canonically isomorphic to the joint one. The end statement is dual: $\mathrm{TwArr}$ splits identically and limits commute with limits. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (weighted colimits as coends, conical case).** *For $W\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathbf{Set}$ and $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ cocomplete, the object $\int^{c}Wc\cdot Fc$ represents weighted cocones: $\mathcal{D}\bigl(\int^{c}Wc\cdot Fc,\;d\bigr)\cong\int_{c}\mathbf{Set}\bigl(Wc,\mathcal{D}(Fc,d)\bigr)$, and for $W=\Delta 1$ this is $\mathrm{colim}_{\mathcal{C}}F$.*

*Proof.* Using the universal property of the coend and of the copower, $\mathcal{D}(\int^{c}Wc\cdot Fc,d)\cong\int_{c}\mathcal{D}(Wc\cdot Fc,d)\cong\int_{c}\mathbf{Set}(Wc,\mathcal{D}(Fc,d))$, where the first isomorphism is the continuity of $\mathcal{D}(-,d)$ carrying a colimit to a limit (a coend to an end), and the second is the copower-power adjunction $\mathcal{D}(S\cdot a,d)\cong\mathbf{Set}(S,\mathcal{D}(a,d))$ applied inside the end. By Proposition 2 the right-hand end is $\mathrm{Nat}(W,\mathcal{D}(F-,d))=[\mathcal{C},\mathbf{Set}](W,\mathcal{D}(F-,d))$, the set of weighted cocones, so the coend represents them. For $W=\Delta 1$ the copower $1\cdot Fc=Fc$ and the weighted-cocone set becomes the ordinary cocone set, whence $\int^{c}Fc\cong\mathrm{colim}_{\mathcal{C}}F$ by Exercise 5. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **The Yoneda lemma, the Yoneda embedding, and density [41.04.02].** The prerequisite unit proves the density theorem and previews the co-Yoneda coend $F\cong\int^{c}Fc\cdot\mathcal{C}(-,c)$ without the calculus to manipulate it. This unit supplies that calculus: the ninja-Yoneda identities of Proposition 3 are density written as rewrite rules, and the end form $\mathrm{Nat}(F,G)\cong\int_{c}\mathcal{D}(Fc,Gc)$ of Proposition 2 is the Yoneda viewpoint — natural transformations computed by evaluation — repackaged as a universal wedge.

- **Limits and colimits as universal cones [41.02.01].** Ends are limits and coends are colimits over the twisted-arrow category, so Proposition 1's equalizer presentation specialises the cone machinery of [41.02.01] to bifunctors. Weighted limits and colimits generalise the conical case proved there: $\lim^{\Delta 1}F=\lim_{\mathcal{C}}F$ recovers the universal cone, and the weighted form $\int^{c}Wc\cdot Fc$ is the colimit re-weighted by a presheaf.

- **Kan extensions [41.06.02].** The co-produced unit [41.06.02] defines the pointwise left and right Kan extensions by the coend and end formulas $\mathrm{Lan}_{K}F\cong\int^{c}\mathcal{D}(Kc,-)\cdot Fc$ and $\mathrm{Ran}_{K}F\cong\int_{c}(Fc)^{\mathcal{D}(-,Kc)}$, which are exactly the weighted colimit and weighted limit of this unit with the hom-functor as weight. The Fubini theorem and the (co)Yoneda lemma proved here are the moves that evaluate those formulas, so [41.06.02] is the calculus of this unit applied to extension problems.

- **Monads and their algebras [41.05.01].** The two-sided bar construction $B(G,\mathcal{C},F)=\int^{c}Gc\cdot Fc$ specialises, for a monad regarded as a one-object enriched category, to the simplicial bar resolution computing monadic (co)limits and the Eilenberg-Moore and Kleisli comparisons; the coend over the simplex category is the resolution that the monadicity machinery of [41.05.01] uses implicitly.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The integral-sign notation for ends and coends is due to Nobuo Yoneda, who introduced it in his 1960 study of $\mathrm{Ext}$ and exact sequences, extending the bijection that bears his name to a calculus of bifunctor-valued limits [ref: TODO_REF Yoneda 1960]. Mac Lane systematised dinatural transformations, ends, coends, and the Fubini theorem in Chapter IX of *Categories for the Working Mathematician*, where the end is defined as a universal wedge and the coend computation of Kan extensions appears in Chapter X [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998]. Kelly's *Basic Concepts of Enriched Category Theory* (1982) recast weighted limits and colimits as ends and coends over an enriching category $\mathcal{V}$, taking the weighted form rather than the conical form as primitive, the choice that made the enriched theory uniform [ref: TODO_REF Kelly 1982].

The coend model of geometric realization traces to the simplicial methods of Milnor and Kan and was given its modern form in the two-sided bar construction, with Riehl's *Category Theory in Context* and Loregian's *(Co)end Calculus* presenting the integral notation as a working tool for homotopy theory and enriched category theory [ref: TODO_REF Loregian 2021]. The reading of realization as a left Kan extension along the Yoneda embedding, sending each representable simplex to its topological model, is the form that unifies the nerve, the singular-set functor, and the realization-nerve adjunction as instances of one coend.
