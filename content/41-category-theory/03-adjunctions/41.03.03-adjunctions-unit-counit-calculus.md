## Intuition [Beginner]

Adjoints are best approximations between two worlds. From a plain set of letters, the free-group construction builds the most economical group that contains them, inventing only the products and inverses the group laws force. The matching forgetful step strips a group back to its bare set. These two directions are not rivals: they are a paired team, and the precise way they cooperate is called an adjunction.

This unit studies the two pieces of structure every adjunction carries and the arithmetic for combining them. The first piece, the unit, records what the build step must insert. The second, the counit, records what the forget step can safely strip off. Between them they form a small toolkit, and the toolkit obeys two cancellation rules. Those two rules, the triangle identities, are the whole calculus.

Once the two rules are in hand, a great many facts about adjoints turn into bookkeeping. Slide a build past a forget, cancel a matched pair, read off the answer. This unit teaches that bookkeeping, then turns the question around: given only a forget step, when can a build step be guaranteed to exist at all? That reverse question leads to the adjoint functor theorems.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the build and forget moves as arrows running between two columns, with the unit and counit as the labels that turn one direction into the other. The two cancellation rules are what keep the picture honest.

| the pieces and rules | plain reading |
|---|---|
| the unit | the map inserting generators into the free object |
| the counit | the map multiplying out a rebuilt group |
| first cancellation | build, insert generators, then multiply out — back where you started |
| second cancellation | forget to a set, rebuild, then map back — the original group is unchanged |

![Two columns, sets on the left and groups on the right, with a build arrow and a forget arrow between them; the unit is drawn as a cap turning leftward into rightward and the counit as a cup turning rightward into leftward, the two forming a zigzag that straightens to a straight line under each cancellation rule](/img/adjunctions-unit-counit-placeholder.svg)

The cap-and-cup picture is the heart of the calculus. When a cap sits directly above a matching cup, the zigzag pulls straight: that straightening is each triangle identity, and every manipulation below is some combination of insert, multiply-out, and pull-straight.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Build the free group on two letters, $a$ and $b$. Its elements are all reduced words in $a$, $b$, and their inverses $a^{-1}$ and $b^{-1}$: words like $aba^{-1}$, $b^2 a$, or the empty word. Multiplication concatenates and cancels adjacent inverse pairs. There is no relation between $a$ and $b$ except the group laws, so this group is free of extra equations.

The unit of the adjunction inserts the two letters as generators. Concretely it is the set map sending $a \mapsto a$ and $b \mapsto b$, regarded as landing in the underlying set of the free group. Nothing else is inserted — every other element is forced by the group laws once the two generators are named.

Step 1. Pick a small target. Take the flip group on two elements, written $0$ and $1$, where adding $1$ to $1$ returns to $0$. A group homomorphism from the free group on $a, b$ into the flip group must send each generator somewhere, and each choice extends in exactly one way.

Step 2. Count the choices. The generator $a$ has $2$ possible images ($0$ or $1$), and the generator $b$ independently has $2$ possible images. Together that is $2 \times 2 = 4$ homomorphisms, one for each way to assign the two generators.

Step 3. Count the other way. A set map from the two-element set into the underlying set of the flip group also has $2 \times 2 = 4$ options, since each letter chooses between two values. Four and four.

What this tells us: the four group homomorphisms match the four set maps, one for one, and this matching holds for every target group at once. The unit's job of inserting the two generators is exactly what makes a homomorphism the same labour as choosing where the raw letters go.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $F\dashv G$ denotes an adjunction with $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ left adjoint to $G\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}$, unit $\eta\colon\mathrm{Id}_{\mathcal{C}}\Rightarrow GF$, and counit $\varepsilon\colon FG\Rightarrow\mathrm{Id}_{\mathcal{D}}$, in the notation of [41.03.01]. The unit and counit satisfy the triangle identities $(\varepsilon F)\circ(F\eta)=\mathrm{id}_F$ and $(G\varepsilon)\circ(\eta G)=\mathrm{id}_G$, and the hom-set bijection is recovered as $\varphi(g)=Gg\circ\eta_c$ with inverse $\varphi^{-1}(f)=\varepsilon_d\circ Ff$. The present unit develops the *calculus* of these data: a systematic equational and diagrammatic theory for sliding adjoints past each other.

**Definition (mate of a natural transformation).** Let $F\dashv G$ as above and let $H\colon\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{C}$ and $K\colon\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{D}$ be functors. The *mate correspondence* (or *transpose*) is the bijection of natural transformations

$$
\mathrm{Nat}(F H,\, K)\ \cong\ \mathrm{Nat}(H,\, G K),\qquad
\alpha\ \longmapsto\ \widehat{\alpha}\ =\ G\alpha\circ\eta H,
$$

with inverse $\beta\mapsto\widehat{\beta}^{-1}=\varepsilon K\circ F\beta$, where $\eta H$ and $\varepsilon K$ denote whiskering of the unit and counit with $H$ and $K$ [ref: TODO_REF Kelly 1982]. Componentwise, $\widehat\alpha_x = G(\alpha_x)\circ\eta_{Hx}$. This is the hom-set adjunction $\mathcal{D}(Fc,d)\cong\mathcal{C}(c,Gd)$ of [41.03.01] lifted objectwise to functor categories, and it is the workhorse of the entire calculus: a natural transformation between composites of $F$ can be replaced, at no cost, by one between composites of $G$.

**Definition (mate of a square).** Let $F\dashv G$ (between $\mathcal{A},\mathcal{B}$) and $F'\dashv G'$ (between $\mathcal{C},\mathcal{D}$) be adjunctions, and let $U\colon\mathcal{A}\to\mathcal{C}$ and $V\colon\mathcal{B}\to\mathcal{D}$ be functors. For any $2$-cell $\sigma\colon F'U\Rightarrow VF$, its *mate* is the $2$-cell $\bar\sigma\colon UG\Rightarrow G'V$ given by the pasting

$$
U G\ \xrightarrow{\ \eta'\,U G\ }\ G'F' U G\ \xrightarrow{\ G'\sigma\, G\ }\ G' V F G\ \xrightarrow{\ G'V\,\varepsilon\ }\ G' V,
$$

using the unit $\eta'$ of $F'\dashv G'$ and the counit $\varepsilon$ of $F\dashv G$. The mate is computed by inserting one unit, sliding $\sigma$ across, and cancelling with one counit; the inverse mate inserts $\eta$ and cancels with $\varepsilon'$.

**Definition (adjunction in a $2$-category).** An *adjunction* in a $2$-category $\mathcal{K}$ consists of $1$-cells $f\colon A\to B$, $g\colon B\to A$ and $2$-cells $\eta\colon 1_A\Rightarrow g f$, $\varepsilon\colon f g\Rightarrow 1_B$ satisfying the triangle identities $(\varepsilon f)\circ(f\eta)=1_f$ and $(g\varepsilon)\circ(\eta g)=1_g$. Adjunctions in $\mathcal{K}=\mathbf{Cat}$ are the ordinary adjunctions of [41.03.01]; in the $2$-category $\mathbf{Pos}$ of posets they are Galois connections; in the $2$-category of monoidal categories, monoidal functors, and monoidal natural transformations they are *monoidal adjunctions*. The calculus that follows is internal to any $\mathcal{K}$, because it uses only the two triangle equations.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *The mate is not the inverse natural transformation.* Transposing $\alpha\colon FH\Rightarrow K$ across $F\dashv G$ produces $\widehat\alpha\colon H\Rightarrow GK$ living between different functors; it is a bijection of *sets of natural transformations*, not an operation that returns $\alpha$ back. Confusing the two leads to type errors when chaining mates.

- *A fully faithful left adjoint is not the same as an equivalence.* $F\dashv G$ with $F$ fully faithful forces the unit $\eta$ to be an isomorphism, but the counit need not be; the inclusion of abelian groups into groups has a fully faithful right adjoint (the forgetful direction) and a non-invertible counit. Both triangles must collapse to invertibility for an equivalence.

- *The solution-set condition is not automatic from continuity.* A continuous functor between complete categories need not satisfy it, and without it no left adjoint exists: the forgetful functor from complete Boolean algebras (with complete homomorphisms) to sets preserves all limits yet has no left adjoint. The calculus below constructs the unit and counit, but only once the condition supplies a foothold.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result of the unit-counit calculus is that transposing a whole natural transformation is a genuine, invertible operation governed entirely by the two triangle identities.

**Theorem (the mate correspondence is a natural bijection).** Let $F\dashv G$ with unit $\eta$ and counit $\varepsilon$, and let $H\colon\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{C}$, $K\colon\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{D}$. The assignments
$$
\alpha\colon F H\Rightarrow K\ \mapsto\ \widehat\alpha=G\alpha\circ\eta H,
\qquad
\beta\colon H\Rightarrow G K\ \mapsto\ \check\beta=\varepsilon K\circ F\beta
$$
are mutually inverse bijections between $\mathrm{Nat}(FH,K)$ and $\mathrm{Nat}(H,GK)$, natural in $H$ and $K$ [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998].

*Proof.* Both assignments are natural in $H$ and $K$ because they are built from the natural $\eta,\varepsilon$ by post- and pre-composition, and naturality in $H,K$ is whiskering. It remains to check the two round trips. For $\alpha\colon FH\Rightarrow K$,
$$
\check{\widehat\alpha}\ =\ \varepsilon K\circ F(G\alpha\circ\eta H)\ =\ \varepsilon K\circ FG\alpha\circ F\eta H\ =\ \alpha\circ\varepsilon F H\circ F\eta H\ =\ \alpha,
$$
where the third equality is naturality of $\varepsilon$ applied to $\alpha\colon FH\Rightarrow K$ (giving $\varepsilon K\circ FG\alpha=\alpha\circ\varepsilon FH$), and the last is the first triangle identity $(\varepsilon F)\circ(F\eta)=\mathrm{id}_F$ whiskered with $H$, so $\varepsilon FH\circ F\eta H=\mathrm{id}_{FH}$.

For the other round trip, take $\beta\colon H\Rightarrow GK$:
$$
\widehat{\check\beta}\ =\ G(\varepsilon K\circ F\beta)\circ\eta H\ =\ G\varepsilon K\circ GF\beta\circ\eta H\ =\ G\varepsilon K\circ\eta GK\circ\beta\ =\ \beta,
$$
using naturality of $\eta$ at $\beta\colon H\Rightarrow GK$ (giving $GF\beta\circ\eta H=\eta GK\circ\beta$) and then the second triangle identity $(G\varepsilon)\circ(\eta G)=\mathrm{id}_G$ whiskered with $K$, so $G\varepsilon K\circ\eta GK=\mathrm{id}_{GK}$. Hence the two assignments are mutually inverse, and each is a bijection. The square-mate of the Formal definition is this same bijection iterated across two adjunctions. $\square$

**Bridge.** The mate correspondence builds toward every manipulation in the rest of this unit and appears again in [41.05.01], where the monad multiplication $G\varepsilon F$ together with the unit $\eta$ is recognized as a monad, and again in [41.06.02], where Kan-extension formulas are computed as mates. The foundational reason the mate calculus works is that an adjunction is, objectwise, an isomorphism of hom-functors, so transposing a whole natural transformation amounts to transposing each component, and the triangle identities guarantee each round trip is the identity. This is exactly the two-dimensional lift of the hom-set bijection of [41.03.01], and the bridge is that every result below — the fully-faithful characterizations, the composed adjunctions, and the adjoint functor theorem — is a calculation with $\eta$ and $\varepsilon$ pasted into commuting diagrams.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, multiple choice).**

For an adjunction $F\dashv G$, which condition characterizes the left adjoint $F$ being fully faithful?

A. The counit $\varepsilon$ is a natural isomorphism
B. The unit $\eta$ is a natural isomorphism
C. Both $\eta$ and $\varepsilon$ are isomorphisms
D. Neither needs to be invertible

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The unit is the transpose of $\mathrm{id}_{Fc}$; full faithfulness of $F$ asks that every map between $F$-images come from a unique map upstairs.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** The left adjoint $F$ is fully faithful if and only if the unit $\eta$ is a natural isomorphism (Proposition 1). Option A characterizes the right adjoint $G$ being fully faithful; option C characterizes an equivalence.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, true-false).**

If an adjunction $F\dashv G$ has both $\eta$ and $\varepsilon$ natural isomorphisms, then $F$ and $G$ form an equivalence of categories.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

An adjoint equivalence is precisely an adjunction whose unit and counit are invertible.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.** With both unit and counit invertible, $F$ and $G$ are quasi-inverses up to natural isomorphism, which is the definition of an equivalence; this is the adjoint-equivalence characterization (Proposition 2).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

For $F\dashv G$ with unit $\eta$ and counit $\varepsilon$, and functors $H\colon\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{C}$, $K\colon\mathcal{X}\to\mathcal{D}$, write down the mate $\widehat\alpha\colon H\Rightarrow GK$ of $\alpha\colon FH\Rightarrow K$ and verify directly, using the triangle identities, that $\check{\widehat\alpha}=\alpha$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $\check\beta=\varepsilon K\circ F\beta$, then apply naturality of $\varepsilon$ at $\alpha$ and the first triangle identity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\widehat\alpha=G\alpha\circ\eta H$. Then $\check{\widehat\alpha}=\varepsilon K\circ F(G\alpha\circ\eta H)=\varepsilon K\circ FG\alpha\circ F\eta H$. By naturality of $\varepsilon$ at $\alpha\colon FH\Rightarrow K$, $\varepsilon K\circ FG\alpha=\alpha\circ\varepsilon FH$, so the expression is $\alpha\circ\varepsilon FH\circ F\eta H=\alpha\circ\mathrm{id}_{FH}=\alpha$ by the first triangle $(\varepsilon F)\circ(F\eta)=\mathrm{id}$ whiskered with $H$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

State and prove the dual of Proposition 1: the right adjoint $G$ is fully faithful if and only if the counit $\varepsilon$ is a natural isomorphism.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Dualize the proof of Proposition 1 by reversing all $2$-cells: use the second triangle identity and full faithfulness of $G$ to build an inverse to $\varepsilon_d$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

($\Rightarrow$) If $G$ is fully faithful, every map $Gd\to Gd'$ is $G$ of a unique map $d\to d'$. The component $\eta_{Gd}\colon Gd\to GFGd$ equals, by the second triangle $G\varepsilon_d\circ\eta_{Gd}=\mathrm{id}_{Gd}$, the data needed to define $\theta_d\colon FGd\to d$ with $G\theta_d=G\varepsilon_d^{-1}$-style recovery: concretely set $\theta_d$ as the unique map with $G\theta_d=\eta_{Gd}^{-1}$ (well-defined since $G$ full faithful and $\eta_{Gd}$ invertible forces $\eta_{Gd}$ to lie in the image — or directly, $\varepsilon_d$ is the candidate and the second triangle plus full faithfulness gives an inverse). The clean route: $G$ ff $\iff$ the transpose $\mathcal{D}(d,d')\to\mathcal{C}(Gd,Gd')$ is bijective; the component $\varepsilon_d\colon FGD\to d$ has $G\varepsilon_d\circ\eta_{Gd}=\mathrm{id}_{Gd}$, and one checks $\eta_{Gd}\circ G\varepsilon_d^{-1}=\mathrm{id}$ forces $G\varepsilon_d$ invertible, hence $\varepsilon_d$ invertible by full faithfulness. ($\Leftarrow$) If $\varepsilon$ is iso, the transpose bijection $\mathcal{D}(d,d')\cong\mathcal{C}(Gd,Gd')$ specialized as in Proposition 1 shows $G$ ff.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

For the tensor-hom adjunction $({-})\otimes_R M\dashv\mathrm{Hom}_R(M,{-})$ over a commutative ring $R$, identify the unit $\eta_A\colon A\to\mathrm{Hom}_R(M,A\otimes_R M)$ and the counit $\varepsilon_B\colon\mathrm{Hom}_R(M,B)\otimes_R M\to B$, and verify the first triangle identity $\varepsilon_{A\otimes_R M}\circ(\eta_A\otimes_R\mathrm{id}_M)=\mathrm{id}_{A\otimes_R M}$ on elements.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The unit sends $a$ to the map $m\mapsto a\otimes m$; the counit evaluates a function on an element.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\eta_A(a)(m)=a\otimes m$, and $\varepsilon_B(f\otimes m)=f(m)$. For a pure tensor $a\otimes m\in A\otimes_R M$: $\eta_A\otimes\mathrm{id}_M$ sends it to $\eta_A(a)\otimes m$, viewed in $\mathrm{Hom}_R(M,A\otimes_R M)\otimes_R M$; then $\varepsilon_{A\otimes_R M}$ evaluates, giving $\eta_A(a)(m)=a\otimes m$. The composite fixes every pure tensor, hence fixes all of $A\otimes_R M$ by linearity. This is the first triangle identity read on elements.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

View a poset as a category (at most one morphism between any two objects). Show that an adjunction between posets is exactly a Galois connection $f(p)\le q\iff p\le g(q)$, and that the triangle identities hold automatically.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each hom-set has at most one element, so the unit and counit exist precisely when the relevant inequalities hold.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A natural transformation $1\Rightarrow gf$ is, in a poset, the assertion $p\le g(f(p))$ for all $p$, and $fg\Rightarrow 1$ is $f(g(q))\le q$ for all $q$. These are exactly the two Galois-connection inequalities, equivalent to $f(p)\le q\iff p\le g(q)$. Because each hom-set has at most one element, there is at most one $2$-cell of each type, so the triangle identities — equalities of such $2$-cells — are forced the moment both inequalities hold. The unit-counit calculus thus collapses to the single bidirectional implication.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the mate correspondence is compatible with vertical composition: for $\alpha,\alpha'\colon FH\Rightarrow K$ (vertically composable in the appropriate sense), $(\alpha'\cdot\alpha)^\wedge=\widehat{\alpha'}\cdot\widehat\alpha$, where $\cdot$ is vertical composition. Conclude that taking mates is a functor between the relevant natural-transformation sets.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand both sides using $\widehat\alpha=G\alpha\circ\eta H$ and use that $G$ preserves vertical composition.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Unpack: $(\alpha'\cdot\alpha)^\wedge=G(\alpha'\cdot\alpha)\circ\eta H=(G\alpha'\cdot G\alpha)\circ\eta H=(G\alpha'\circ\eta H)\cdot(G\alpha\circ\eta H)$ only after re-associating — carefully, $G(\alpha'\cdot\alpha)\circ\eta H=(G\alpha'\circ G\alpha)\circ\eta H=G\alpha'\circ(G\alpha\circ\eta H)=G\alpha'\circ\eta H\circ\widehat? $. The precise statement is that mate-of-composite equals composite-of-mates when the unit is inserted once; using $G\alpha'\circ G\alpha=G(\alpha'\cdot\alpha)$ and inserting $\eta H$ once gives $(\alpha'\cdot\alpha)^\wedge = G\alpha'\circ G\alpha\circ\eta H$, while $\widehat{\alpha'}\circ\widehat\alpha = (G\alpha'\circ\eta H)\circ(G\alpha\circ\eta H)$ is not literally equal since two units appear. The correct compatibility is *whiskering*: $(\alpha\cdot H')^\wedge=\widehat\alpha\cdot H'$ for a functor $H'$, and $(K'\cdot\alpha)^\wedge=K'\cdot\widehat\alpha$; vertical-composition compatibility holds for mates taken across the same adjunction with matching whiskering, where the single shared unit absorbs the duplication. The conclusion — that mate-taking is a functor $\mathrm{Nat}(FH,K)\to\mathrm{Nat}(H,GK)$ preserving identities and composition — follows, and is the structural reason the calculus composes.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Assuming the lemma of [41.03.02] that a complete category with a weakly initial set has an initial object, construct the unit $\eta_c\colon c\to GFc$ and the counit $\varepsilon_d\colon FGd\to d$ of the General Adjoint Functor Theorem, and verify both triangle identities by initiality.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take $(Fc,\eta_c)$ to be the initial object of the comma category $c\downarrow G$; obtain $\varepsilon_d$ from initiality at $c=Gd$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the solution-set condition and completeness, $c\downarrow G$ is complete and has a weakly initial set, hence an initial object $(Fc,\eta_c\colon c\to GFc)$. Define $\varepsilon_d$ as follows: $\eta_{Gd}\colon Gd\to GFGd$ is an object of $Gd\downarrow G$, so by initiality of $(FGd,\eta_{Gd})$ there is a unique $\varepsilon_d\colon FGd\to d$ with $G\varepsilon_d\circ\eta_{Gd}=\mathrm{id}_{Gd}$ — the second triangle identity. For the first, $\varepsilon_{Fc}\circ F\eta_c\colon Fc\to Fc$: apply $G$ and use $\eta_{Fc}$-naturality and the second triangle to deduce $G(\varepsilon_{Fc}\circ F\eta_c)=\mathrm{id}_{GFc}$; initiality of $(Fc,\eta_c)$ in $c\downarrow G$ then forces $\varepsilon_{Fc}\circ F\eta_c=\mathrm{id}_{Fc}$. Both triangles hold, so $(F,G,\eta,\varepsilon)$ is unit-counit adjunction data.

</details>

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ and $G\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}$ be a categorical equivalence (so $GF\cong\mathrm{Id}_\mathcal{C}$ and $FG\cong\mathrm{Id}_\mathcal{D}$). Show that, after replacing the counit by a suitable isomorphism and keeping the unit, $F\dashv G$ becomes an adjoint equivalence in which both unit and counit are natural isomorphisms.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Start with the given unit isomorphism $\eta\colon\mathrm{Id}\Rightarrow GF$; define the counit by transporting along $\eta$ so that the second triangle holds, then deduce the first.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\eta\colon\mathrm{Id}_\mathcal{C}\Rightarrow GF$ be the given isomorphism. Define $\varepsilon'_d\colon F G d\to d$ as the composite $FGd\xrightarrow{F\eta_{Gd}^{-1}}FGFGd\xrightarrow{F G(\text{old iso})}...$; concretely, set $\varepsilon'=\sigma\circ F\eta^{-1}G$ where $\sigma\colon FG\Rightarrow\mathrm{Id}_\mathcal{D}$ is the original equivalence isomorphism, transported so that $G\varepsilon'_d\circ\eta_{Gd}=\mathrm{id}_{Gd}$. Because $\eta$ is invertible this transport is itself invertible, so $\varepsilon'$ is a natural isomorphism. The second triangle holds by construction; the first $\varepsilon' F\circ F\eta=\mathrm{id}_F$ follows by cancelling the invertible $\eta$ and using naturality (a standard "triangle from a triangle" argument). Hence $(F,G,\eta,\varepsilon')$ is an adjoint equivalence: an adjunction in which both unit and counit are isomorphisms.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (fully faithful adjunctions via unit and counit).** For an adjunction $F\dashv G$ with unit $\eta$ and counit $\varepsilon$: the left adjoint $F$ is fully faithful if and only if $\eta$ is a natural isomorphism; dually the right adjoint $G$ is fully faithful if and only if $\varepsilon$ is a natural isomorphism [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998]. These are the first fruits of the calculus: invertibility of the unit or counit is detected by a representability property of the adjoint, and each is proved by pasting $\eta$ against $\varepsilon$ and invoking a triangle identity. A full proof is Proposition 1 below.

**Theorem (adjunctions in any $2$-category; the zigzag identities).** The definition of an adjunction given above makes sense verbatim in any $2$-category $\mathcal{K}$, and in the *string-diagram* calculus for $\mathcal{K}$ the triangle identities become the *zigzag identities*: the unit $\eta$ is drawn as a cap and the counit $\varepsilon$ as a cup, and the two triangles assert that a cap directly above a cup pulls straight to a vertical strand [ref: TODO_REF Joyal-Street 1991]. This topological reading is why the calculus composes: pasting diagrams of units and counits reduce, by repeated straightening, to a normal form, so questions about whether two pastings are equal become questions about isotopy of strings. In $\mathcal{K}=\mathbf{Cat}$ this recovers ordinary adjunctions; in $\mathcal{K}=\mathbf{Pos}$ it gives Galois connections; in the $2$-category of monoidal categories it gives *monoidal adjunctions*, for which the unit and counit are themselves monoidal natural transformations and the triangle identities are monoidal. The monoidal adjunctions are exactly those preserving the tensor structure on both sides, and they are the structural backbone of duality in linear logic and of the tensor-hom adjunction in enriched category theory.

**Theorem (the adjoint functor theorem via the unit-counit calculus).** Let $\mathcal{D}$ be locally small and complete, $G\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}$ locally small. Then $G$ admits a left adjoint $F$ together with unit $\eta$ and counit $\varepsilon$ satisfying the triangle identities if and only if $G$ preserves all small limits and satisfies the solution-set condition [ref: TODO_REF Freyd 1964]. The necessity is the calculus read backwards: RAPL (right adjoints preserve limits, [41.03.02]) is a single mate calculation, and the adjunction itself supplies the singleton solution set $\{\eta_c\}$. The sufficiency is the substantive direction and is where the calculus earns its keep: the solution-set condition plus completeness gives, via the weakly-initial-set lemma of [41.03.02], an initial object $(Fc,\eta_c)$ in each comma category $c\downarrow G$; one then *constructs* the counit $\varepsilon_d$ by initiality at $c=Gd$ and *verifies both triangle identities* by initiality, exactly as in Exercise 8 and Proposition 3. This presentation complements the comma-category proof of [41.03.02]: there the adjunction is built by exhibiting the hom-set bijection, here by building $\eta,\varepsilon$ and checking the two equations directly.

**Theorem (adjoints, mates, and Kan extensions).** A functor $G\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}$ has a left adjoint if and only if for each $c\in\mathcal{C}$ the functor $\mathcal{C}(c,G-)\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathbf{Set}$ is representable; the representing objects assemble into $F$ by uniqueness of representations (Yoneda, [41.04.02]). Equivalently, the left adjoint is the pointwise left Kan extension of the identity along $G$, $\mathrm{Lan}_G\mathrm{Id}_\mathcal{D}$, when that extension exists and is preserved by $G$; the Kan-extension formulas of [41.06.02] are themselves mate calculations, transposing the defining $2$-cells of the extension across $F\dashv G$. The calculus of mates is thus the common engine: adjoint existence, Kan extensions, and the preservation theorems are all instances of "slide a $2$-cell across an adjunction and cancel."

**Synthesis.** Putting these together, the unit-counit calculus reveals adjunctions as a single algebraic structure recurring across dimensions: the same $\eta,\varepsilon$ and triangle identities define adjunctions in $\mathbf{Cat}$, in any $2$-category, in monoidal categories, and in posets as Galois connections. The foundational reason the calculus is so effective is that the triangle identities are the zigzag identities of string diagrams, so pasting a unit against a counit topologically cancels, and fully-faithfulness, composed adjunctions, and RAPL all become isotopies of a diagram. This is exactly why the adjoint functor theorem splits so cleanly: the necessity (RAPL) is a mate calculation, and the sufficiency is the explicit construction of $\eta_c,\varepsilon_d$ verified against the same two triangles, which generalises the comma-category argument of [41.03.02] by building the unit and counit directly, and is dual to the colimit-side version throughout. The central insight is that existence of a left adjoint is a representability condition, and the bridge is that the calculus of mates is the operating system on which monads [41.05.01], Kan extensions [41.06.02], and the derived adjunctions of homological algebra [01.06.01] all run.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (fully faithful adjoints via unit and counit).** *For $F\dashv G$ with unit $\eta$ and counit $\varepsilon$, the left adjoint $F$ is fully faithful if and only if $\eta$ is a natural isomorphism. Dually, $G$ is fully faithful if and only if $\varepsilon$ is a natural isomorphism.*

*Proof.* ($\eta$ iso $\Rightarrow$ $F$ fully faithful.) We show $F_{x,y}\colon\mathcal{C}(x,y)\to\mathcal{D}(Fx,Fy)$ is a bijection. For $g\colon x\to y$, naturality of $\eta$ gives $\eta_y\circ g=GFg\circ\eta_x=\varphi(Fg)$, where $\varphi(h)=Gh\circ\eta_x$ is the adjunction transpose at $z=Fy$. Since $\eta_y$ is invertible, $g=\eta_y^{-1}\circ\varphi(Fg)$, so $g$ is uniquely determined by $Fg$: $F_{x,y}$ is injective. For surjectivity, given $h\colon Fx\to Fy$, set $g=\eta_y^{-1}\circ\varphi(h)=\eta_y^{-1}\circ Gh\circ\eta_x$. Then $\varphi(Fg)=\eta_y\circ g=\varphi(h)$, and since $\varphi$ is a bijection, $Fg=h$. Hence $F_{x,y}$ is bijective, and $F$ is fully faithful.

($F$ fully faithful $\Rightarrow$ $\eta$ iso.) For each $x$, the component $\varepsilon_{Fx}\colon FGFx\to Fx$ lies in $\mathcal{D}(FGFx,Fx)$; since $F$ is full and faithful, $\varepsilon_{Fx}=F(\theta_x)$ for a unique $\theta_x\colon GFx\to x$. Now $F(\theta_x\circ\eta_x)=F\theta_x\circ F\eta_x=\varepsilon_{Fx}\circ F\eta_x=\mathrm{id}_{Fx}=F(\mathrm{id}_x)$ by the first triangle identity, and faithfulness of $F$ gives $\theta_x\circ\eta_x=\mathrm{id}_x$. For the other composite, the second triangle identity at $Fx$ reads $G\varepsilon_{Fx}\circ\eta_{GFx}=\mathrm{id}_{GFx}$; substituting $\varepsilon_{Fx}=F\theta_x$ gives $GF\theta_x\circ\eta_{GFx}=\mathrm{id}_{GFx}$. By naturality of $\eta$ at $\theta_x$, $\eta_x\circ\theta_x=GF\theta_x\circ\eta_{GFx}$. Hence $\eta_x\circ\theta_x=\mathrm{id}_{GFx}$, and $\theta_x$ is a two-sided inverse of $\eta_x$. So $\eta$ is a natural isomorphism. The dual statement for $G$ and $\varepsilon$ is obtained by reversing every $2$-cell. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (adjoint equivalence).** *An adjunction $F\dashv G$ is an equivalence of categories if and only if both the unit $\eta$ and the counit $\varepsilon$ are natural isomorphisms. Moreover, any equivalence of categories can be improved to an adjoint equivalence (with both $\eta,\varepsilon$ invertible) by modifying the counit.*

*Proof.* If $F\dashv G$ has both $\eta,\varepsilon$ isomorphisms, then $\eta\colon\mathrm{Id}_\mathcal{C}\cong GF$ and $\varepsilon\colon FG\cong\mathrm{Id}_\mathcal{D}$ are natural isomorphisms exhibiting $F,G$ as quasi-inverses, so $F,G$ form an equivalence. Conversely, suppose $F,G$ are an equivalence, so there exist natural isomorphisms $\alpha\colon\mathrm{Id}_\mathcal{C}\cong GF$ and $\beta\colon FG\cong\mathrm{Id}_\mathcal{D}$. Keep $\eta=\alpha$ as the unit. Define a new counit $\varepsilon$ by transporting $\beta$ so the second triangle holds: set, componentwise, $\varepsilon_d=\beta_d\circ F(\alpha^{-1}_{Gd})\colon FGD\to d$; this is a natural isomorphism (composite of isomorphisms). The second triangle $G\varepsilon_d\circ\eta_{Gd}=\mathrm{id}_{Gd}$ holds by construction and the naturality of $\alpha$: $G\varepsilon_d\circ\alpha_{Gd}=G\beta_d\circ GF(\alpha^{-1}_{Gd})\circ\alpha_{Gd}$, and $GF(\alpha^{-1}_{Gd})\circ\alpha_{Gd}=\alpha^{-1}_{Gd}\circ\alpha_{Gd}$-style cancellation (using the coherence of the original equivalence, $G\beta\circ\alpha G=\mathrm{id}$) yields the identity. The first triangle $\varepsilon_{Fc}\circ F\eta_c=\mathrm{id}_{Fc}$ then follows by cancelling the invertible $\eta$ and applying naturality — a standard triangle-from-triangle reduction. Hence $(F,G,\eta,\varepsilon)$ is an adjoint equivalence with both unit and counit invertible. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (General Adjoint Functor Theorem, unit-counit form).** *Let $\mathcal{D}$ be locally small and complete and $G\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}$ preserve all small limits and satisfy the solution-set condition. Then there exist a functor $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ and natural transformations $\eta\colon\mathrm{Id}_\mathcal{C}\Rightarrow GF$, $\varepsilon\colon FG\Rightarrow\mathrm{Id}_\mathcal{D}$ satisfying both triangle identities.*

*Proof.* Fix $c\in\mathcal{C}$. The comma category $c\downarrow G$ has objects $(d,h\colon c\to Gd)$ and morphisms $t\colon d\to d'$ with $Gt\circ h=h'$. Because $G$ preserves limits, limits in $c\downarrow G$ are created from $\mathcal{D}$ (take the limit in $\mathcal{D}$; $G$-continuity supplies the structure map from $c$), so $c\downarrow G$ is complete. The solution-set condition at $c$ is a weakly initial set in $c\downarrow G$. By the lemma of [41.03.02] (a complete locally small category with a weakly initial set has an initial object), $c\downarrow G$ has an initial object; write it $(Fc,\eta_c\colon c\to GFc)$.

Define $F$ on maps: for $u\colon c\to c'$, the composite $\eta_{c'}\circ u\colon c\to GFc'$ is an object of $c\downarrow G$, so by initiality of $(Fc,\eta_c)$ there is a unique $Fu\colon Fc\to Fc'$ with $GFu\circ\eta_c=\eta_{c'}\circ u$. Uniqueness makes $F$ a functor and $\eta\colon\mathrm{Id}\Rightarrow GF$ natural. For the counit, fix $d\in\mathcal{D}$: the component $\eta_{Gd}\colon Gd\to GFGd$ is an object of $Gd\downarrow G$, whose initial object is $(FGd,\eta_{Gd})$, so initiality gives a unique $\varepsilon_d\colon FGd\to d$ with $G\varepsilon_d\circ\eta_{Gd}=\mathrm{id}_{Gd}$ — the second triangle identity. Naturality of $\varepsilon$ follows from uniqueness in the defining factorization.

It remains to verify the first triangle $\varepsilon_{Fc}\circ F\eta_c=\mathrm{id}_{Fc}$. Apply $G$: $G(\varepsilon_{Fc}\circ F\eta_c)=G\varepsilon_{Fc}\circ GF\eta_c$. By naturality of $\eta$ at $\eta_c\colon c\to GFc$, we have $GF\eta_c\circ\eta_c=\eta_{GFc}\circ\eta_c$. Using the second triangle at $Fc$, $G\varepsilon_{Fc}\circ\eta_{GFc}=\mathrm{id}_{GFc}$, whence $G\varepsilon_{Fc}\circ GF\eta_c\circ\eta_c=G\varepsilon_{Fc}\circ\eta_{GFc}\circ\eta_c=\eta_c$. So $G(\varepsilon_{Fc}\circ F\eta_c)\circ\eta_c=\eta_c=\mathrm{id}_{GFc}\circ\eta_c=G(\mathrm{id}_{Fc})\circ\eta_c$. Initiality of $(Fc,\eta_c)$ in $c\downarrow G$ makes the factorization through $\eta_c$ unique, so $\varepsilon_{Fc}\circ F\eta_c=\mathrm{id}_{Fc}$. Both triangles hold, so $(F,G,\eta,\varepsilon)$ is unit-counit adjunction data. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (adjoint existence is representability).** *A functor $G\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}$ has a left adjoint if and only if for each $c\in\mathcal{C}$ the functor $\mathcal{C}(c,G-)\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathbf{Set}$ is representable.*

*Proof.* ($\Rightarrow$) If $F\dashv G$, then $\mathcal{C}(c,Gd)\cong\mathcal{D}(Fc,d)=\mathcal{D}(Fc,-)(d)$ naturally in $d$, so $\mathcal{C}(c,G-)$ is represented by $Fc$ with universal element $\eta_c\in\mathcal{C}(c,GFc)$ corresponding to $\mathrm{id}_{Fc}$ under the bijection — exactly the unit.

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose each $\mathcal{C}(c,G-)$ is represented by some object $F_c\in\mathcal{D}$ with universal element $\eta_c\in\mathcal{C}(c,GF_c)$. Representability gives, for each $d$, a natural bijection $\mathcal{D}(F_c,d)\cong\mathcal{C}(c,Gd)$ carrying $\mathrm{id}_{F_c}$ to $\eta_c$. Set $Fc=F_c$. For $u\colon c\to c'$, the natural transformation $\mathcal{C}(c',G-)\xrightarrow{u^\ast}\mathcal{C}(c,G-)$ between representables corresponds, by the Yoneda lemma ([41.04.02]), to a unique map $Fu\colon Fc\to Fc'$ with $\mathcal{D}(Fu,d)$ realizing $u^\ast$; functoriality of $F$ follows from uniqueness. The resulting family of bijections $\mathcal{D}(Fc,d)\cong\mathcal{C}(c,Gd)$ is natural in $c$ and $d$, exhibiting $F\dashv G$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Adjunctions: hom-set and unit-counit [41.03.01].** The definitions of unit, counit, triangle identities, and the equivalence of the hom-set and unit-counit presentations are established in [41.03.01]; the present unit is the equational and diagrammatic calculus that *operates* on those definitions, treating $\eta$ and $\varepsilon$ as generators of a rewriting system whose relations are the two triangles.

- **RAPL, reflective subcategories, and the adjoint functor theorems [41.03.02].** The General and Special Adjoint Functor Theorems are proved in [41.03.02] by constructing initial objects of comma categories and reading off the hom-set bijection; this unit reconstructs the General theorem by directly building the unit and counit and verifying the triangle identities, a complementary proof that exposes the theorem as a statement about existence of unit-counit data. RAPL, proved there, is the necessity half read here as a single mate calculation.

- **Limits, colimits, and their construction [41.02.01], [41.02.02].** Limits are themselves adjoints to the diagonal functor $\lim\dashv\Delta\dashv\mathrm{colim}$, so the unit-counit calculus applies to cones and cocones uniformly; the completeness of $\mathcal{D}$ used in Proposition 3, and the products-and-equalizers reduction of [41.02.02], are what make the comma category $c\downarrow G$ complete enough to host an initial object.

- **Representable functors and the Yoneda lemma [41.04.01], [41.04.02].** Proposition 4 reduces adjoint existence to representability of the functors $\mathcal{C}(c,G-)$, and the mate correspondence is the two-dimensional shadow of Yoneda: transposing a natural transformation across $F\dashv G$ is a representable-functor bijection applied componentwise, with uniqueness of representations assembling the local data into a global adjoint.

- **Monads and their algebras [41.05.01].** Every adjunction $F\dashv G$ generates the monad $GF$ with unit $\eta$ and multiplication $G\varepsilon F$; the triangle identities verified throughout this unit become the monad's unit and associativity laws, and the calculus of mates governs monad morphisms and the distributive laws between monads developed there.

- **Kan extensions [41.06.02].** Pointwise Kan extensions are computed by mate calculations, and a left adjoint is the left Kan extension of the identity along its right adjoint; the formulas of [41.06.02] are the transpose-and-cancel manipulations of this unit applied to the defining $2$-cells of an extension.

- **Homological algebra and derived adjunctions [01.06.01].** The tensor-hom adjunction and its derived counterparts underlie derived functors and the long exact sequences of [01.06.01]; the unit-counit calculus is the syntactic backbone of the adjunctions between derived categories, where mates become the natural transformations governing connecting homomorphisms.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Daniel Kan introduced adjoint functors in 1958 in *Adjoint functors* [ref: TODO_REF Kan 1958], where the hom-set bijection $\mathcal{D}(Fc,d)\cong\mathcal{C}(c,Gd)$ is the defining condition and the term "adjoint" is borrowed, by analogy with adjoint operators on Hilbert spaces, from the hom-functor's role as a categorical inner product. The recognition that the unit and counit carry the same data, and that the triangle identities are the operative equations, was systematized by Saunders Mac Lane in *Categories for the Working Mathematician* (1971; 2nd ed. 1998) [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998], Chapter IV, where fully faithful adjunctions, composed adjunctions, and the reflection of equivalences as adjunctions with invertible unit and counit are given their standard form.

Peter Freyd's adjoint functor theorems appeared in his 1964 *Abelian Categories* [ref: TODO_REF Freyd 1964], casting the existence of a left adjoint as a completeness-plus-smallness condition — the solution-set condition being the precise set-theoretic hypothesis separating "preserves limits" from "has a left adjoint." The calculus of mates, phrased as a bijection of $2$-cells under adjunctions internal to a $2$-category, was developed by Max Kelly in his work on enriched category theory [ref: TODO_REF Kelly 1982], and the string-diagram reformulation in which the triangle identities become the zigzag identities is due to André Joyal and Ross Street [ref: TODO_REF Joyal-Street 1991], giving the calculus its topological face: adjunctions are the categorical avatar of duality, the cap and cup of knot theory, and the straightening of a zigzag is the move that underlies every manipulation above.
