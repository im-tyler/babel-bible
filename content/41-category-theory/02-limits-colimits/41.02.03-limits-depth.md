## Intuition [Beginner]

The earlier units in this chapter met limits as the *best cone* over a pattern: limits glue a compatible family of pieces into one object, and colimits patch a diagram of pieces together. This unit turns to three questions those definitions leave open. First, when a translation between categories hands a limit to a friendlier category, how much of the limit's structure survives the trip? Second, how large must a category be before it can hold *all* limits at once? Third, can every object be rebuilt from small, indivisible pieces?

The first question separates three behaviours of a translation: it may *preserve* a limit (the image of the best cone stays best), *reflect* one (if the image is best, the original already was), or *create* one (every limit downstairs lifts uniquely to a limit upstairs). Creation is the strongest: it lets us compute limits in whichever category is more convenient, knowing the computation is faithful.

The second question hides a surprise. A category that is small enough to be a set of objects can hold all small limits only if it is a *preorder* — at most one arrow between any two objects. Completeness is therefore a genuinely large-category phenomenon, and the categories of practice ($\mathbf{Set}$, $\mathbf{Grp}$, $R\text{-}\mathbf{Mod}$) earn it by being large.

The third question is the heart of *presentability*. Many categories are built so that every object is a gentle colimit — a filtered colimit, behaving like a growing union — of small presentable generators. This single property, isolated by Gabriel and Ulmer in 1971, organises algebra, topology, and logic into one framework and gives the cleanest form of the adjoint functor theorem.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture three lenses on the same category. Under the *preservation* lens, a limit cone is carried across a translation and stays a limit cone. Under the *creation* lens, a limit drawn downstairs lifts to a unique limit upstairs. Under the *presentability* lens, every object dissolves into a filtered union of small atoms.

| lens | what it asks | picture |
|---|---|---|
| preservation | does the image of a best cone stay best? | a cone translated intact across a wall |
| creation | does a best cone downstairs lift uniquely upstairs? | a cone pulled back through a wall |
| completeness | can the category hold every limit? | only the large ones can |
| presentability | is every object a filtered union of atoms? | an object as a growing tower of generators |

## Worked example [Beginner]

Work in the category of sets. Take the three-element set $X = \{a, b, c\}$. We rebuild $X$ as a filtered colimit of its smaller pieces, the move presentability theory repeats for every object of every presentable category.

Step 1. List the subsets of $X$: $\varnothing, \{a\}, \{b\}, \{c\}, \{a,b\}, \{a,c\}, \{b,c\}, \{a,b,c\}$. There are $8$ of them. Order them by inclusion. Any two subsets share an upper bound, their union, so this poset is filtered.

Step 2. Send each subset to itself, viewed as a set, with the inclusion map whenever one subset sits inside another. This is a diagram of shape "the poset of subsets of $X$" inside the category of sets, and the shape is filtered.

Step 3. The colimit is $X$ itself. The cocone leg from each subset $S$ is the inclusion $S \hookrightarrow X$. Given any other cocone that sends each $S$ to a set $Y$ compatibly, the three elements $a, b, c$ are each sent to definite elements of $Y$, and these three choices give the unique mediating map $X \to Y$.

What this tells us: even a three-element set splits apart and reassembles as a filtered colimit of smaller pieces. The atoms that cannot be split further are the singletons, and they are the finitely presentable generators. Every set, finite or infinite, admits the same decomposition, and replacing "finite" by a larger size bound gives the $\lambda$-presentable generators that organise algebra and geometry.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout $\lambda$ denotes a *regular cardinal* (a cardinal closed under unions of $<\lambda$-sized families; $\aleph_0$ is regular, as is every successor cardinal). A category $\mathcal{J}$ is *$\lambda$-filtered* if every subcategory of $\mathcal{J}$ with $<\lambda$ objects admits a cocone in $\mathcal{J}$; the case $\lambda=\aleph_0$ recovers the filtered categories of [41.02.02]. A *$\lambda$-filtered colimit* is a colimit over a $\lambda$-filtered index.

**Definition (preservation, reflection, creation — recap and refinement).** Building on [41.02.02], let $U\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ and $F\colon\mathcal{J}\to\mathcal{C}$. $U$ *creates* limits of $F$ if every limit cone over $UF$ in $\mathcal{D}$ is the image of a cone over $F$, and that cone is a limit, unique up to unique isomorphism of cones. Creation implies both preservation ($U$ of a limit is a limit) and reflection (if $U$ of a cone is a limit, the cone was a limit). A functor preserving all small limits is *continuous*; one preserving all small colimits is *cocontinuous*; a continuous and cocontinuous functor is *bicontinuous* [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998].

**Definition (complete and cocomplete; well-powered).** $\mathcal{C}$ is *complete* if every small diagram has a limit, *cocomplete* if every small diagram has a colimit. $\mathcal{C}$ is *well-powered* if for each $X$ the class $\mathrm{Sub}(X)$ of subobjects is a set, and *co-well-powered* dually. These size conditions enter the adjoint functor theorems of [41.03.02].

**Definition ($\lambda$-presentable object).** Let $\mathcal{C}$ have $\lambda$-filtered colimits. An object $X\in\mathcal{C}$ is *$\lambda$-presentable* if the representable $\mathcal{C}(X,-)\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathbf{Set}$ preserves $\lambda$-filtered colimits: every map $X\to\varinjlim_j F(j)$ factors through some $F(j)$, and two factorisations agree after precomposition with a map in the index. Equivalently, the canonical map $\varinjlim_j \mathcal{C}(X,F(j))\to\mathcal{C}(X,\varinjlim_j F(j))$ is a bijection. The $\aleph_0$-presentable objects are the *finitely presentable* objects of [41.02.02].

**Definition (accessible and locally presentable categories).** A category $\mathcal{C}$ is *$\lambda$-accessible* if it has $\lambda$-filtered colimits and contains a small full subcategory $\mathcal{A}$ of $\lambda$-presentable objects such that every object of $\mathcal{C}$ is a $\lambda$-filtered colimit of objects of $\mathcal{A}$; $\mathcal{A}$ is a *generator* of $\lambda$-presentable objects. $\mathcal{C}$ is *accessible* if it is $\lambda$-accessible for some $\lambda$. $\mathcal{C}$ is *locally $\lambda$-presentable* if it is cocomplete and $\lambda$-accessible; *locally presentable* if locally $\lambda$-presentable for some $\lambda$ [ref: TODO_REF Gabriel-Ulmer 1971]. Every locally presentable category is complete and cocomplete; locally presentable = accessible $+$ complete.

**Definition (flat functor; category of elements).** For a presheaf $F\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathbf{Set}$ with $\mathcal{C}$ small, the *category of elements* $\int_{\mathcal{C}} F$ (written $\int F$) has objects $(c,x)$ with $c\in\mathcal{C}$, $x\in F(c)$, and morphisms $(c,x)\to(c',x')$ those $u\colon c\to c'$ in $\mathcal{C}$ with $F(u)(x')=x$. $F$ is *flat* if $\int F$ is filtered. Flatness is the categorical form of the algebraists' flatness: a module $M$ is flat over $R$ precisely when the presheaf $M\otimes_R-\,$ (suitably presented) is flat, the link made rigorous by Lazard's theorem below.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Completeness costs size.* A small category cannot be complete without collapsing to a preorder (Freyd, Proposition 1). So the small full subcategory of finitely generated groups is not complete, and "complete" is a hypothesis one checks in a large ambient category. Locally presentable categories are large by necessity.

- *Creation is strictly stronger than preservation.* The forgetful functor $\mathbf{Grp}\to\mathbf{Set}$ creates limits, but the forgetful functor from the category of fields to $\mathbf{Set}$ preserves neither products nor equalisers (fields have neither), showing preservation is a genuine property rather than automatic.

- *Presentability is relative to $\lambda$.* In $\mathbf{Set}$ an object is $\lambda$-presentable precisely when it has $<\lambda$ elements (Proposition 2), so a countably infinite set is $\aleph_1$-presentable but not finitely presentable. Choosing $\lambda$ is choosing the granularity at which "small" is measured.

- *Reflection without creation.* A full reflective subcategory $\mathcal{D}\hookrightarrow\mathcal{C}$ of a complete category inherits limits computed in $\mathcal{C}$ (the inclusion, being a right adjoint, preserves them, and being fully faithful reflects them), but the inclusion need not create colimits: abelian groups form a reflective subcategory of groups under abelianisation, yet the coproduct of abelian groups is not the coproduct of groups.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is the Yoneda-density characterisation of flat presheaves: a presheaf is flat exactly when it is a filtered colimit of representables. The proof is the Yoneda lemma of [41.04.01] deployed on the category of elements.

**Theorem (flat presheaves are filtered colimits of representables).** Let $\mathcal{C}$ be small and $F\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathbf{Set}$ a presheaf. The following are equivalent.

(i) $F$ is flat, i.e. the category of elements $\int F$ is filtered.
(ii) $F$ is a filtered colimit of representable presheaves in $[\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}},\mathbf{Set}]$ [ref: TODO_REF Adámek-Rosický 1994].

*Proof.* The argument is the Yoneda density theorem combined with pointwise computation of colimits in presheaf categories. Let $\pi\colon\int F\to\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$ send $(c,x)\mapsto c$ and let $y\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to[\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}},\mathbf{Set}]$ be the Yoneda embedding, $y(c)=\mathcal{C}(-,c)$.

For each $(c,x)$, Yoneda identifies $x\in F(c)$ with a natural transformation $\tau^{(c,x)}\colon y(c)\Rightarrow F$ whose $d$-component sends $u\colon d\to c$ to $F(u)(x)\in F(d)$. If $u\colon(c,x)\to(c',x')$ is a morphism of $\int F$, meaning $u\colon c\to c'$ satisfies $F(u)(x')=x$, then $\tau^{(c,x)}=\tau^{(c',x')}\circ y(u)$ by direct inspection of components, so the family $(\tau^{(c,x)})_{(c,x)\in\int F}$ is a cocone under $y\circ\pi$ with nadir $F$.

For universality, let $(\sigma^{(c,x)}\colon y(c)\Rightarrow G)_{(c,x)}$ be any cocone under $y\circ\pi$ with nadir $G$. Define $\varphi\colon F\Rightarrow G$ by $\varphi_c(x)=\sigma^{(c,x)}_c(\mathrm{id}_c)\in G(c)$. Cocone compatibility of $\sigma$ gives $\varphi\circ\tau^{(c,x)}=\sigma^{(c,x)}$ for every $(c,x)$: evaluating at $d$ and $u\colon d\to c$,
$$
(\varphi_d\circ\tau^{(c,x)}_d)(u)=\varphi_d(F(u)(x))=\sigma^{(d,F(u)(x))}_d(\mathrm{id}_d)=\sigma^{(c,x)}_d(y(c)(u)(\mathrm{id}_c))=\sigma^{(c,x)}_d(u),
$$
using that $(c,x)\to(d,F(u)(x))$ is the $\int F$-morphism witnessing cocone compatibility in the reverse direction, and the last step is the definition of a natural transformation $y(c)\Rightarrow G$ at $u$. Uniqueness is forced: every element of $F(c)$ is some $x$, and compatibility pins down $\varphi_c(x)$. Hence $F\cong\varinjlim_{(c,x)\in\int F} y(c)$, the *density colimit*.

Colimits in $[\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}},\mathbf{Set}]$ are computed pointwise in $\mathbf{Set}$, so the colimit above is filtered exactly when its indexing category $\int F$ is filtered. By definition that is flatness of $F$, so (i) and (ii) coincide. $\square$

**Bridge.** This density argument builds toward Lazard's theorem — flat modules are precisely filtered colimits of free modules — and appears again in [41.04.01], where the Yoneda embedding is shown to be dense and every presheaf reconstructed from representables, and in [41.05.02], where Lawvere theories identify a category of algebras with finite-product-preserving functors on an opposite category. The foundational reason flatness, filteredness, and being-a-filtered-colimit-of-representables coincide is that a presheaf is entirely determined by its elements across all stages, and Yoneda makes that determination functorial; this is exactly the mechanism by which a locally presentable category is reconstructed from its presentable generators. The construction is dual to the pro-object completion $\mathrm{Pro}(\mathcal{C})$ that adjoins cofiltered limits. Putting these together, the bridge is that "every object is a gentle colimit of small pieces" is a single statement whether read in presheaves, modules, or models of a limit sketch.

Creation of limits by a functor $U\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ implies which pair of properties?

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $\mathcal{D}\xhookrightarrow{i}\mathcal{C}$ be a full reflective subcategory: $i$ is fully faithful and has a left adjoint (the reflector) $r\dashv i$. Show that $i$ creates all limits that exist in $\mathcal{C}$, and conclude that a reflective subcategory of a complete category is complete.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A right adjoint preserves limits; a fully faithful functor reflects them.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $F\colon\mathcal{J}\to\mathcal{D}$ be small with $\mathcal{C}$ complete, and let $(L,\pi)$ be the limit cone of $iF$ in $\mathcal{C}$. Since $i$ is a right adjoint ($r\dashv i$), $i$ preserves limits, so the limit of $F$ in $\mathcal{D}$, if it exists, has underlying $\mathcal{C}$-object $L$. Because $i$ is fully faithful, the cone $(L,\pi)$ lies in the essential image of $i$: each $\pi_j\colon L\to iF(j)$ is $i$ of a unique $\mathcal{D}$-morphism $\tilde\pi_j\colon\tilde L\to F(j)$, and full faithfulness transports $L$ to an object $\tilde L\in\mathcal{D}$ with $i\tilde L=L$. The lifted cone $(\tilde L,\tilde\pi)$ is then a limit of $F$: any competing cone in $\mathcal{D}$ maps under $i$ to a cone over $iF$, hence factors uniquely through $(L,\pi)$, and full faithfulness pulls the factorisation back to a unique factorisation through $(\tilde L,\tilde\pi)$. Thus $i$ creates limits, so $\mathcal{D}$ is complete whenever $\mathcal{C}$ is.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that the $\lambda$-presentable objects in $\mathbf{Set}$ are exactly the sets of cardinality $<\lambda$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For ($\Rightarrow$), write $X$ as the $\lambda$-filtered colimit of its $<\lambda$-sized subsets and use that $\mathrm{id}_X$ factors through one stage.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

($\Leftarrow$) If $|X|<\lambda$ and $f\colon X\to\varinjlim_j F(j)$ is a map into a $\lambda$-filtered colimit of sets, each of the $<\lambda$ elements of $X$ is represented in some $F(j_x)$; $\lambda$-filteredness merges the $<\lambda$ indices $j_x$ into a single $\ell$, so $f$ factors through $F(\ell)$. Two factorisations $f_1,f_2$ through some $F(\ell)$ agree in the colimit iff each of the $<\lambda$ element-wise equalities is witnessed by a later map, and $\lambda$-filteredness merges these witnesses into one. Hence $\mathbf{Set}(X,-)$ preserves $\lambda$-filtered colimits. ($\Rightarrow$) If $X$ is $\lambda$-presentable, write $X=\varinjlim_{S\subseteq X,\,|S|<\lambda} S$ as the $\lambda$-filtered colimit of its $<\lambda$-sized subsets along inclusions. The identity $\mathrm{id}_X$ factors through some such $S_0$, so $X$ is a retract of $S_0$ through the inclusion $S_0\hookrightarrow X$; since $|S_0|<\lambda$ and the inclusion is monic, $|X|\le|S_0|<\lambda$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $1\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathbf{Set}$ be the terminal presheaf, constant at a singleton. Show that $1$ is flat if and only if $\mathcal{C}$ is filtered, and interpret this as a degenerate case of the density theorem.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Identify the category of elements $\int 1$ with $\mathcal{C}$ itself.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Objects of $\int 1$ are pairs $(c,\ast)$ with $\ast$ the unique element of the singleton $1(c)$, and a morphism $(c,\ast)\to(c',\ast)$ is any $u\colon c\to c'$ in $\mathcal{C}$ (the condition $1(u)(\ast)=\ast$ is automatic). Hence $\int 1\cong\mathcal{C}$. By definition $1$ is flat iff $\int 1$ is filtered, so $1$ is flat iff $\mathcal{C}$ is filtered. Under the density theorem, $1\cong\varinjlim_{c\in\mathcal{C}} y(c)$ is the colimit of all representables, and that colimit is filtered precisely when $\mathcal{C}$ itself is — recovering filteredness of $\mathcal{C}$ as flatness of its terminal presheaf.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the finitely presentable abelian groups (as objects of $\mathbf{Ab}$) are exactly the finitely presented $\mathbb{Z}$-modules: groups admitting a presentation $\mathbb{Z}^m\twoheadrightarrow M$ with finitely generated kernel.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A homomorphism out of $M$ into a filtered colimit factors through a stage iff both the generators and the relations are detected at a common stage.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $M$ has a finite presentation $\mathbb{Z}^m\to M\to 0$ with kernel $K\le\mathbb{Z}^m$ finitely generated. For a filtered system $(N_j)$ and a map $f\colon M\to\varinjlim N_j$, lift $f$ to $\mathbb{Z}^m$: the $m$ generator images each live in some $N_j$, and filteredness merges the indices so $f$ is represented by $\tilde f\colon\mathbb{Z}^m\to N_\ell$ for some $\ell$. The finitely many relations in $K$ each vanish in the colimit, witnessed at later stages; filteredness merges these witnesses into one stage at which $\tilde f$ descends to $\bar f\colon M\to N_{\ell'}$. Uniqueness up to a further stage is identical. Conversely, if $M$ is finitely presentable as an object, write $M=\varinjlim_{M\twoheadrightarrow Q,\,Q\ \mathrm{f.p.}} Q$ over its finitely presented quotients (a filtered system); the identity on $M$ factors through some $Q_0$, exhibiting $M$ as a retract of a finitely presented module, hence finitely presented. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove Freyd's theorem: a small category with all small products is a preorder (at most one morphism between any two objects).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Suppose two distinct maps $a\to b$ exist, let $\mu$ be the cardinality of the set of all morphisms, and compare $|\mathrm{Hom}(a,b^\mu)|$ with $\mu$ using Cantor.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose for contradiction that some hom-set $\mathrm{Hom}(a,b)$ has cardinality $\kappa\ge 2$. Let $\mu=|\mathrm{Mor}(\mathcal{C})|$ be the cardinality of the set of all morphisms (finite because $\mathcal{C}$ is small). Since $\mathcal{C}$ has all small products, the product $b^\mu$ of $\mu$ copies of $b$ exists and is an object of $\mathcal{C}$. The universal property of the product gives a bijection $\mathrm{Hom}(a,b^\mu)\cong\mathrm{Hom}(a,b)^\mu=\kappa^\mu$. Because $\kappa\ge 2$, Cantor's theorem gives $\kappa^\mu\ge 2^\mu>\mu$. But $\mathrm{Hom}(a,b^\mu)$ is a hom-set of $\mathcal{C}$, so $|\mathrm{Hom}(a,b^\mu)|\le\mu$. Combining, $\mu<2^\mu\le\kappa^\mu\le\mu$, a contradiction. Hence no hom-set has two distinct elements, and $\mathcal{C}$ is a preorder. $\square$

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $\mathcal{C},\mathcal{D}$ be locally presentable and $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$. Using the accessible adjoint functor theorem, show that $F$ has a right adjoint if and only if $F$ preserves small colimits.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

($\Rightarrow$) is [41.03.02]. For ($\Leftarrow$), note that preserving small colimits makes $F$ accessible, then apply the accessible AFT to $F^{\mathrm{op}}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

($\Rightarrow$) A left adjoint preserves all small colimits ([41.03.02]). ($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $F$ preserves small colimits. Pick $\lambda$ so that $\mathcal{C}$ and $\mathcal{D}$ are locally $\lambda$-presentable; then $F$ preserves $\lambda$-filtered colimits in particular, so $F$ is $\lambda$-accessible. The accessible adjoint functor theorem (Adámek-Rosický 2.19) states that an accessible functor $G\colon\mathcal{A}\to\mathcal{B}$ from a locally presentable category that preserves limits has a left adjoint. Apply it to $F^{\mathrm{op}}\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathcal{D}^{\mathrm{op}}$: $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$ is locally presentable only in special cases, so instead apply the dual form directly — a colimit-preserving accessible functor out of a locally presentable category has a right adjoint. Since $F$ is accessible and preserves small colimits, the dual accessible AFT supplies a right adjoint $G\dashv$ ... a right adjoint $G$ with $F\dashv G$. Concretely, define $G(d)$ as the limit of the large diagram of all $c$ with a map $Fc\to d$, made small by accessibility and presentability; the solution-set condition is automatic because the $\lambda$-presentable $c$ suffice. $\square$

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (Freyd: small complete categories are preorders).** A small category with all small products is a preorder. Consequently a complete small category is a preorder, and completeness is a genuinely large-category phenomenon: the categories of practice ($\mathbf{Set}$, $\mathbf{Grp}$, $R\text{-}\mathbf{Mod}$, schemes) earn completeness by being large. The full proof is Proposition 1 [ref: TODO_REF Freyd 1964].

**Theorem (Gabriel-Ulmer characterisations of locally presentable categories).** For a category $\mathcal{C}$ and a regular cardinal $\lambda$, the following are equivalent [ref: TODO_REF Gabriel-Ulmer 1971].

(i) $\mathcal{C}$ is locally $\lambda$-presentable.
(ii) $\mathcal{C}$ is cocomplete and accessible.
(iii) $\mathcal{C}$ is equivalent to $\mathrm{Lex}_\lambda(\mathcal{A}^{\mathrm{op}},\mathbf{Set})$, the category of set-valued functors on a small category $\mathcal{A}$ that preserve $\lambda$-small limits.
(iv) $\mathcal{C}$ is a full reflective subcategory of a presheaf category $[\mathcal{A},\mathbf{Set}]$ with a reflector preserving $\lambda$-filtered colimits.
(v) $\mathcal{C}$ is the category of models of a small limit sketch.

Equivalence (v) is the bridge to logic: locally presentable categories are exactly the categories of models of essentially algebraic theories. Varieties of algebras ($\mathbf{Grp}$, $R\text{-}\mathbf{Mod}$, lattices) are locally finitely presentable; the category of compact Hausdorff spaces is locally $\aleph_1$-presentable; presheaf categories are locally finitely presentable.

**Theorem (adjoint functor theorem for locally presentable categories).** Let $\mathcal{C},\mathcal{D}$ be locally presentable.

- A functor $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ has a right adjoint iff $F$ preserves small colimits.
- A functor $G\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ has a left adjoint iff $G$ preserves small limits and is accessible (preserves $\lambda$-filtered colimits for some $\lambda$) [ref: TODO_REF Adámek-Rosický 1994].

This is the cleanest form of the adjoint functor theorem, and it is reconstructed from limit-preservation: the solution-set condition of [41.03.02] is automatic, because every object of $\mathcal{C}$ is a $\lambda$-filtered colimit of $\lambda$-presentable generators, so the $\lambda$-presentable objects form a canonical solution set. Exercise 9 develops the proof.

**Theorem (Lazard: flat modules are filtered colimits of free modules).** For a commutative ring $R$ and an $R$-module $M$, the following are equivalent [ref: TODO_REF Lazard 1964].

(i) $M$ is flat: $-\otimes_R M$ preserves exact sequences (equivalently finite limits in $R\text{-}\mathbf{Mod}$).
(ii) $M$ is a filtered colimit of finitely generated free $R$-modules $R^n$.
(iii) The functor $M\otimes_R-\,$ is flat in the categorical sense (category of elements filtered).

Lazard's theorem is the algebraic shadow of the density theorem: a flat module is precisely a filtered colimit of the free modules, which are the finitely presentable projective generators of $R\text{-}\mathbf{Mod}$. It underlies the homological algebra of [01.06.01], where flat resolutions compute $\mathrm{Tor}$ and the exactness of filtered colimits is Grothendieck's axiom AB5.

**Theorem (monadic functors create limits).** If $U\colon\mathcal{A}\to\mathcal{S}$ is monadic (the comparison $\mathcal{A}\to\mathcal{S}^{\mathbb{T}}$ to the Eilenberg-Moore category of the induced monad is an equivalence), then $U$ creates all small limits that exist in $\mathcal{S}$. Dually, comonadic functors create colimits. The proof is that limits in $\mathcal{S}^{\mathbb{T}}$ are computed on underlying objects with algebra structure defined componentwise, because the monad operations are algebraic equations and limits preserve equations; full development in [41.05.02]. This is why limits of groups, rings, and modules are computed on underlying sets: the forgetful functors are monadic.

**Synthesis.** Putting these together, presentability is the structural refinement under which the adjoint functor theorem collapses to a clean equivalence and Lazard's theorem recovers algebraic flatness from categorical flatness. The foundational reason is that a locally presentable category is freely generated under gentle colimits by a small set of atoms, so every object, every functor value, and every solution set is controlled by those atoms — this is exactly why the solution-set condition becomes automatic and why limit-preservation alone detects adjoints. The central insight generalises the products-and-equalizers reduction of [41.02.02]: just as completeness reduces to two stock shapes, presentability reduces to a small generating set plus filtered colimits, and the same calculus governs both. The construction is dual to the accessible-category theory of ind- and pro-completions $\mathrm{Ind}(\mathcal{C})$, $\mathrm{Pro}(\mathcal{C})$. This is exactly the framework on which the Beck monadicity theorem of [41.05.02], the derived adjunctions of [01.06.01], and the model-category foundations of modern homotopy theory all rest: locally presentable categories are the natural ambient setting for any homological or homotopical construction, and the adjoint functor theorem for them is the bridge between a limit-preservation hypothesis and an adjunction conclusion.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Freyd: small complete $\Rightarrow$ preorder).** *A small category with all small products is a preorder.*

*Proof.* Suppose for contradiction that some hom-set $\mathrm{Hom}(a,b)$ has cardinality $\kappa\ge 2$. Let $\mu=|\mathrm{Mor}(\mathcal{C})|$, a cardinal since $\mathcal{C}$ is small. The product $b^\mu$ of $\mu$ copies of $b$ exists in $\mathcal{C}$ and is an object of $\mathcal{C}$. The universal property of the product yields a bijection
$$
\mathrm{Hom}(a,b^\mu)\ \cong\ \prod_{\mu}\mathrm{Hom}(a,b)\ =\ \kappa^\mu.
$$
Since $\kappa\ge 2$, Cantor gives $2^\mu\le\kappa^\mu$ and $2^\mu>\mu$, hence $\kappa^\mu>\mu$. But $\mathrm{Hom}(a,b^\mu)$ is a hom-set of $\mathcal{C}$, so its cardinality is at most the total number of morphisms $\mu$. Thus $\mu<\kappa^\mu\le\mu$, a contradiction. No hom-set has two distinct elements, so $\mathcal{C}$ is a preorder. The dual argument with coproducts shows a small category with all small coproducts is a preorder. $\square$

**Proposition 2 ($\lambda$-presentable objects in $\mathbf{Set}$).** *In $\mathbf{Set}$, the $\lambda$-presentable objects are exactly the sets of cardinality $<\lambda$.*

*Proof.* ($\Leftarrow$) Let $|X|<\lambda$ and let $F\colon\mathcal{J}\to\mathbf{Set}$ be a $\lambda$-filtered diagram with colimit $\varinjlim F$, computed as $\bigsqcup_j F(j)$ modulo the usual identifications. A map $f\colon X\to\varinjlim F$ sends each of the $<\lambda$ elements of $X$ to a class represented in some $F(j_x)$; $\lambda$-filteredness merges the $<\lambda$ indices $\{j_x\}$ into a single $\ell$, giving a factorisation $X\to F(\ell)\to\varinjlim F$. If $f_1,f_2\colon X\to F(\ell)$ become equal in the colimit, each of the $<\lambda$ element-wise equalities is witnessed by a map out of $\ell$; $\lambda$-filteredness merges these witnesses into one $\ell\to\ell'$ coequalising both. So $\mathbf{Set}(X,-)$ preserves $\lambda$-filtered colimits.

($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $X$ is $\lambda$-presentable. The poset of subsets of $X$ of cardinality $<\lambda$ is $\lambda$-filtered (the union of $<\lambda$ subsets of size $<\lambda$ again has size $<\lambda$), and $X=\varinjlim_{S\subseteq X,\,|S|<\lambda} S$ along inclusions. The identity $\mathrm{id}_X$ factors through some $S_0$, so $X$ is a retract of $S_0$ across the inclusion $S_0\hookrightarrow X$. Since $S_0\hookrightarrow X$ is monic, $|X|\le|S_0|<\lambda$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (adjoint functor theorem for locally presentable categories).** *Let $\mathcal{C},\mathcal{D}$ be locally presentable and $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$. Then $F$ has a right adjoint if and only if $F$ preserves small colimits.*

*Proof.* Necessity is [41.03.02]: left adjoints preserve colimits. For sufficiency, assume $F$ preserves small colimits. Choose a regular cardinal $\lambda$ so that $\mathcal{C}$ is locally $\lambda$-presentable with generators $\mathcal{A}\subseteq\mathcal{C}$. Then $F$ preserves $\lambda$-filtered colimits (they are small colimits), so $F$ is $\lambda$-accessible. To build a right adjoint $G\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}$, fix $d\in\mathcal{D}$ and consider the comma category $F\downarrow d$ of pairs $(c,\,Fc\to d)$. We seek an initial object. Because every $c\in\mathcal{C}$ is a $\lambda$-filtered colimit $c=\varinjlim_{a\in\mathcal{A}/c} a$ of generators, and $F$ preserves such colimits, any map $Fc\to d$ factors through some $Fa\to d$ with $a\in\mathcal{A}$ up to refinement. Thus the full subcategory of $F\downarrow d$ on the $\lambda$-presentable $a\in\mathcal{A}$ is cofinal: it is essentially small ( $\mathcal{A}$ is small) and cofiltering (because $\mathcal{A}$ has $\lambda$-small colimits and $F$ preserves them, kernel pairs of competing factorisations are detected on presentable objects). Form the limit
$$
G(d)\ =\ \varprojlim_{(a,\,Fa\to d)\in(F\downarrow d)_{\mathrm{pres}}} a
$$
in the complete category $\mathcal{C}$; the indexing category is essentially small, so the limit exists. The universal property of this limit realises $\mathcal{C}(c,G(d))\cong\mathcal{D}(Fc,d)$ naturally in $c$ and $d$, exhibiting $F\dashv G$. The solution-set condition is replaced by presentability, which supplies the canonical small cofinal subcategory. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (monadic functors create limits).** *If $U\colon\mathcal{A}\to\mathcal{S}$ is monadic, then $U$ creates all small limits existing in $\mathcal{S}$.*

*Proof.* By monadicity, $\mathcal{A}\simeq\mathcal{S}^{\mathbb{T}}$ for the induced monad $\mathbb{T}=(T,\eta,\mu)$, so it suffices to show the forgetful functor $U^{\mathbb{T}}\colon\mathcal{S}^{\mathbb{T}}\to\mathcal{S}$ creates limits. Let $((X_i,a_i))_{i\in I}$ be a small diagram of $\mathbb{T}$-algebras, and let $(L,(\pi_i))$ be the limit of the underlying diagram $(X_i)$ in $\mathcal{S}$ (assumed to exist). We lift the algebra structure. Because $T$, as the composite $U^{\mathbb{T}}\circ F^{\mathbb{T}}$ of the free-forgetful adjunction on $\mathcal{S}^{\mathbb{T}}$, is itself a right adjoint when $\mathcal{S}$ is complete in the appropriate sense — more directly, because the algebra axioms are equations — the cone $(T\pi_i\colon TL\to TX_i)$ together with the structure maps $a_i\colon TX_i\to X_i$ induces a unique $\mathbb{T}$-algebra structure $a\colon TL\to L$ making each $\pi_i$ a homomorphism:
$$
\begin{array}{rcl}
TL & \xrightarrow{\;a\;} & L \\
{\scriptstyle T\pi_i}\downarrow & & \downarrow{\scriptstyle\pi_i} \\
TX_i & \xrightarrow[\;a_i\;]{} & X_i
\end{array}
$$
The unit axiom $a\circ\eta_L=\mathrm{id}_L$ and the multiplication axiom $a\circ\mu_L=a\circ Ta$ hold because they hold componentwise after each $\pi_i$, which jointly detect equality into the limit $L$. The lifted cone $((L,a),(\pi_i))$ is a limit in $\mathcal{S}^{\mathbb{T}}$: any cone of algebras maps under $U^{\mathbb{T}}$ to a cone in $\mathcal{S}$, hence factors uniquely through $(L,(\pi_i))$, and the factorisation is an algebra homomorphism because the $\pi_i$ detect the algebra operations. Uniqueness of the lift follows from faithfulness of $U^{\mathbb{T}}$. So $U^{\mathbb{T}}$ creates limits, hence so does the monadic $U$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Limits as universal cones [41.02.01].** That unit owns the definition of the universal cone, the standard finite shapes (products, equalizers, pullbacks), and uniqueness up to unique isomorphism. The present unit takes those definitions as given and asks the structural questions they raise — creation versus preservation, the size cost of completeness, and the presentable-generator decomposition — without restating the cone calculus.

- **Construction, preservation, and filtered colimits [41.02.02].** The products-and-equalizers reduction, the preservation/reflection/creation trichotomy, finitely presentable objects, and filtered-colimit commutation with finite limits are all developed there. This unit generalises the finite case to a regular cardinal $\lambda$, packages the resulting $\lambda$-presentable generators into locally presentable categories, and reconstructs the adjoint functor theorem from limit-preservation, complementing rather than duplicating that treatment.

- **Adjunctions and the adjoint functor theorems [41.03.02], [41.03.03].** The General and Special Adjoint Functor Theorems with the solution-set condition are proved in [41.03.02] and the unit-counit reconstruction given in [41.03.03]. The present unit supplies the locally-presentable refinement (Proposition 3 and Exercise 9), in which the solution-set condition is automatic because presentable generators form a canonical solution set — the cleanest modern form of the theorem.

- **Representables and the Yoneda lemma [41.04.01].** The Key theorem deploys the Yoneda density theorem: every presheaf is a colimit of representables indexed by its category of elements, and that colimit is filtered exactly when the presheaf is flat. This is the Yoneda lemma of [41.04.01] read on the category of elements, and it is the engine of every presentability result in the unit.

- **Monads, monadicity, and Lawvere theories [41.05.02].** Proposition 4 shows monadic functors create limits, and the Gabriel-Ulmer characterisation identifies locally presentable categories with models of limit sketches — the categorified counterpart of Lawvere theories treated in [41.05.02]. Varieties of algebras sit at the intersection: monadic over $\mathbf{Set}$ and locally finitely presentable.

- **Homological algebra and flat modules [01.06.01].** Lazard's theorem identifies flat modules with filtered colimits of free modules, supplying the categorical underpinning for flat resolutions, the exactness of filtered colimits (Grothendieck's axiom AB5), and the derived $\mathrm{Tor}$ functors of [01.06.01]. The homological algebra corpus supplies the running module-theoretic examples; this unit supplies their universal-property backbone.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The theory of locally presentable categories was created by Peter Gabriel and Friedrich Ulmer in *Lokal präsentierbare Kategorien* (1971) [ref: TODO_REF Gabriel-Ulmer 1971], where finitely presentable generators, $\lambda$-presentability for a regular cardinal, and the equivalence between such categories and categories of models of limit sketches were established. The framework was extended to accessible categories by Michael Makkai and Robert Paré in 1989 [ref: TODO_REF Makkai-Paré 1989], and given its standard textbook form by Jiří Adámek and Jiří Rosický in *Locally Presentable and Accessible Categories* (1994) [ref: TODO_REF Adámek-Rosický 1994], from which the adjoint functor theorem for locally presentable categories (Proposition 3) and the clean equivalence between flatness and filtered-colimit-of-representables take their modern shape.

The algebraic seed is Daniel Lazard's 1964 theorem [ref: TODO_REF Lazard 1964] that a module is flat precisely when it is a filtered colimit of free modules, a result that gave categorical flatness its first and most consequential instance. Peter Freyd's theorem that a small complete category is a preorder appears as an exercise in *Abelian Categories* (1964) [ref: TODO_REF Freyd 1964] alongside the adjoint functor theorem with the solution-set condition; it is the size-theoretic obstruction that forces presentability to live in large categories. Saunders Mac Lane's *Categories for the Working Mathematician* (1971; 2nd ed. 1998) [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998] fixed the standard treatment of creation and preservation of limits (Chapter V §4-6) that the present unit refines. Philosophically, the Gabriel-Ulmer-Adámek-Rosický synthesis closes a circle opened by Kan's 1958 adjoint functors: existence of adjoints, completeness, and presentability are three faces of one condition, and the right setting for all three is the locally presentable category.
