## Intuition [Beginner]

An object is fixed in place by the web of arrows tying it to every other object. Two objects that relate to everything else in exactly the same way are the same object, renamed. This slogan, that an object is determined completely by its relationships to all others, is the single idea on which the whole chapter rests. This third unit gathers its consequences into one place and pushes them further.

The two earlier units built the machinery in pieces. The first defined a representable functor as the rule recording arrows into a fixed object, and a universal element as the identity arrow seen as a seed. The second proved the Yoneda lemma, the precise statement that this seed names every comparison, and deduced that the Yoneda embedding loses no information. Here we weld those results together and watch one fact do all the work.

Two deeper outcomes are the goal. First, the same lemma that pins an object down also lets us rebuild any measurement on objects out of the address books of single objects glued together, a statement called density. Second, the whole story survives unchanged when the sets of arrows are replaced by richer structures such as spaces or categories, the enriched and two-dimensional forms. Both follow the original lemma by direct argument.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the address book of one fixed object: a one-element loop at the center, the identity, and around it a bundle of arrows arriving from every other object. The center is the seed; the bundles are its portrait. Density says every measurement, however intricate, is a patchwork of such portraits stitched along the arrows of the category.

| direction | what it says | where it lands in this unit |
|---|---|---|
| read the book inward | the seed names every comparison | the Yoneda lemma, full faithfulness |
| read the book outward | every measurement is glued portraits | density and the co-Yoneda form |
| replace sets by spaces or categories | the argument is unchanged | enriched and 2-categorical Yoneda |

![A central fixed dot with its identity self-loop; surrounding dots each send a bundle of arrows toward the center; a second shaded overlay measuring every dot is shown decomposing into copies of the central portrait, one per arriving arrow, and a third panel replaces the bundles by shaded regions to suggest richer arrow-objects](/img/yoneda-depth-placeholder.svg)

The picture unifies the chapter. Reading the address book inward gives the embedding, full and faithful. Reading it outward gives density, the rebuilding of any measurement from portraits. The seed at the center, the identity arrow, governs both directions, and the same picture still reads correctly when the bundles become richer objects.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Work in the walking-arrow category $\mathbf{2}$. It has two objects, written $0$ and $1$, and exactly one arrow $a\colon 0\to 1$ besides the two do-nothing identity arrows. Composition is forced: any arrow followed by an identity is itself.

Step 1. Build the address book of object $1$, the rule recording arrows into $1$. At object $0$ there is one arrow into $1$, namely $a$. At object $1$ there is one arrow into $1$, the identity on $1$. So the address book holds a one-element set at each object.

Step 2. Track how the arrow $a\colon 0\to 1$ reshuffles the book. Pre-composing with $a$ turns an arrow into $1$ at object $1$ into an arrow into $1$ at object $0$: the identity on $1$ is sent to $a$. This is naturality at work, the arrow $a$ inducing the function compose-with-$a$ between the two one-element sets.

Step 3. Apply the slogan. A comparison from this address book to any other measurement is fixed by where it sends the identity on $1$. If the measurement gives a four-element set at object $1$, there are four comparisons, one per element. The address book is so rigid that one choice names a whole comparison.

What this tells us: in a category with just two objects and one arrow, the address book of $1$ already determines every comparison out of it. The same rigidity, scaled up, is what makes the embedding full and faithful and what makes density hold for every category.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout $\mathcal{C}$ is a locally small category, $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}=[\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}},\mathbf{Set}]$ is its presheaf category, and $よ\colon\mathcal{C}\to\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ is the *Yoneda embedding* sending $c$ to the contravariant hom-functor $よc=\mathcal{C}(-,c)$ and $f\colon c\to c'$ to post-composition $\mathcal{C}(-,f)$, in the notation of [41.04.02]. A presheaf is *representable* when it is naturally isomorphic to some $よc$; the covariant hom-functor $\mathcal{C}(c,-)$ is the representable on $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$. These definitions are recalled from [41.04.01], where a representation $\mathcal{C}(c,-)\cong F$ was shown to be the same data as a *universal element* $u=\Phi_c(\mathrm{id}_c)\in F(c)$, and as an initial object of the *category of elements* $\int_{\mathcal{C}}F$ [ref: TODO_REF Riehl 2016].

The two foundational facts, proved in [41.04.02], are restated here as the load-bearing input. The *Yoneda lemma* is the bijection
$$
\Phi_{F,c}\colon\mathrm{Nat}(よc,F)\xrightarrow{\ \cong\ }F(c),\qquad \alpha\longmapsto\alpha_c(\mathrm{id}_c),
$$
natural in the presheaf $F$ and the object $c$, with inverse $u\mapsto\Psi(u)$, $\Psi(u)_d(f)=F(f)(u)$. Specialising $F=よd$ yields $\mathrm{Nat}(よc,よd)\cong\mathcal{C}(c,d)$, which is the statement that $よ$ is *full and faithful*. These two facts are the sole ingredients of every theorem below, and the present unit assembles their deeper consequences. The notation $よ$ for the embedding, $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ for the presheaf category, $\int_{\mathcal{C}}F$ for the category of elements, $\int^{c}$ for a coend, and $\mathcal{V}$ for a symmetric monoidal closed base is recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

**Definition (density).** The Yoneda embedding is *dense* (or *adequate*) when every presheaf $F$ is the canonical colimit of the composite
$$
\textstyle\int_{\mathcal{C}}F\ \xrightarrow{\ \pi\ }\ \mathcal{C}\ \xrightarrow{\ よ\ }\ \widehat{\mathcal{C}},
$$
with colimiting cocone $\lambda_{(c,x)}=\Psi(x)\colonよc\to F$ at $(c,x)\in\int_{\mathcal{C}}F$. Equivalently, by the standard rewriting of a colimit over a category of elements as a coend, $F\cong\int^{c\in\mathcal{C}}F(c)\cdotよc$, where $S\cdot X$ denotes the $S$-indexed copower (coproduct of $S$ copies of $X$); this coend form is the *co-Yoneda lemma* or *ninja-Yoneda identity* [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998]. Density is the outward-reading half of the Yoneda lemma: where full faithfulness reads the address book inward, density reads it outward, rebuilding an arbitrary $F$ from the representables it contains.

**Definition (enriched setting).** Let $\mathcal{V}=(\mathcal{V}_0,\otimes,I,\multimap)$ be a symmetric monoidal closed category, so that the internal hom $X\multimap(-)$ is right adjoint to $X\otimes(-)$. A $\mathcal{V}$-*category* $\mathcal{C}$ has hom-*objects* $\mathcal{C}(c,d)\in\mathcal{V}_0$ in place of hom-sets, together with composition and unit morphisms in $\mathcal{V}_0$ satisfying the usual associativity and unit axioms. The enriched presheaf category $[\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}},\mathcal{V}]$ has $\mathcal{V}$-functors $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathcal{V}$ as objects and $\mathcal{V}$-natural transformations as morphisms, and the *enriched Yoneda embedding* $\mathbf{Y}\colon\mathcal{C}\to[\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}},\mathcal{V}]$ sends $c$ to $\mathcal{C}(-,c)$ regarded as a $\mathcal{V}$-functor. Taking $\mathcal{V}=\mathbf{Set}$ recovers the ordinary definitions; taking $\mathcal{V}=\mathbf{Cat}$ gives the 2-categorical setting, and $\mathcal{V}=\mathbf{Ab}$ the additive one [ref: TODO_REF Kelly 1982].

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Density is a colimit, not a limit, of representables.* Every presheaf is a colimit of representables indexed by its own category of elements; the dual statement with limits fails. A coproduct $よc\amalgよd$ of two representables is already a presheaf that is not a finite limit of representables, so reversing the variance breaks the theorem.

- *Full and faithful does not mean essentially surjective.* The Yoneda embedding lands inside $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ on the representables, a tiny full subcategory; most presheaves are not representable. Density repairs this asymmetry by exhibiting the non-representables as colimits of representables, not as representables themselves.

- *The universal element is the Yoneda image of the identity, not an arbitrary choice.* Re-reading $\Phi_{F,c}(\alpha)=\alpha_c(\mathrm{id}_c)$ when $\alpha$ is invertible gives the universal element of [41.04.01]; the two units describe one datum in two vocabularies, and conflating "the representing object is unique" with "the representation is unique on the nose" forgets that the isomorphism is itself part of the data.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is that full faithfulness of the embedding, the density theorem, and the co-Yoneda identity all flow from the single Yoneda bijection, so the three theorems of the chapter are one fact read in three directions.

**Theorem (the Yoneda cycle).** *Let $\mathcal{C}$ be locally small. From the Yoneda lemma, namely the natural bijection $\mathrm{Nat}(よc,F)\cong F(c)$, $\alpha\mapsto\alpha_c(\mathrm{id}_c)$, there follow both:*
(a) *the embedding $よ\colon\mathcal{C}\to\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ is full and faithful;*
(b) *density, that every presheaf $F$ is the colimit $\mathrm{colim}_{(c,x)\in\int_{\mathcal{C}}F}よc$, equivalently $F\cong\int^{c}F(c)\cdotよc$.*

*Conversely (b) specialises at a representable to give back (a), and (a) together with the existence of the Yoneda inverse $\Psi$ recovers the lemma. So the three are equivalent formulations of one piece of structure* [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998].

*Proof.* (Lemma $\Rightarrow$ (a).) Take $F=よd$ in the Yoneda bijection. Then $\mathrm{Nat}(よc,よd)\cong(よd)(c)=\mathcal{C}(c,d)$, and the inverse $\Psi$ sends $f\colon c\to d$ to the natural transformation with $e$-component $g\mapsto(よd)(f)(g)=f\circ g$, that is, to $よf$. Hence the function $\mathcal{C}(c,d)\to\mathrm{Nat}(よc,よd)$, $f\mapstoよf$, is a bijection, which is precisely full faithfulness of $よ$.

((a) $\Rightarrow$ (b) is not the route taken; the lemma gives (b) directly.) Define the cocone $\lambda_{(c,x)}=\Psi(x)\colonよc\to F$, where $\Psi$ is the Yoneda inverse and $x\in F(c)$. For a morphism $f\colon(c,x)\to(c',x')$ in $\int_{\mathcal{C}}F$, that is $f\colon c\to c'$ with $F(f)(x')=x$, the cocone condition $\lambda_{(c,x)}=\lambda_{(c',x')}\circよf$ holds because both sides are transformations $よc\to F$ with the same Yoneda image: evaluating at $\mathrm{id}_c$ gives $(\lambda_{(c',x')}\circよf)_c(\mathrm{id}_c)=\lambda_{(c',x')\,c}(f)=F(f)(x')=x=\lambda_{(c,x)\,c}(\mathrm{id}_c)$.

To see the cocone is colimiting, test it against an arbitrary presheaf $G$ and cocone $\mu_{(c,x)}\colonよc\to G$. By Yoneda each $\mu_{(c,x)}$ is the element $m_{(c,x)}=\mu_{(c,x)\,c}(\mathrm{id}_c)\in G(c)$, and the cocone condition on $\mu$ translates to $G(f)(m_{(c',x')})=m_{(c,x)}$ whenever $F(f)(x')=x$. Define $\theta\colon F\Rightarrow G$ by $\theta_c(x)=m_{(c,x)}$; the displayed condition is exactly naturality of $\theta$, and $\theta\circ\lambda_{(c,x)}=\mu_{(c,x)}$ by evaluating at $\mathrm{id}_c$. Any mediating $\theta$ is forced, since $\theta_c(x)=\theta_c(\lambda_{(c,x)\,c}(\mathrm{id}_c))=\mu_{(c,x)\,c}(\mathrm{id}_c)=m_{(c,x)}$. So $F\cong\mathrm{colim}_{(c,x)}よc$, and rewriting the colimit over $\int_{\mathcal{C}}F$ as a coend gives $F\cong\int^{c}F(c)\cdotよc$.

((b) $\Rightarrow$ (a).) Specialise density to the representable $F=よd$. The category of elements $\int_{\mathcal{C}}よd$ has a terminal object $(d,\mathrm{id}_d)$, so the colimit collapses: $よd\congよd$ with the colimiting cocone given by post-composition. Reading the cocone at this terminal object recovers the bijection $\mathrm{Nat}(よc,よd)\cong\mathcal{C}(c,d)$, which is (a). The equivalence of the three formulations is the Yoneda cycle. $\square$

**Bridge.** The cycle builds toward the entire colimit-based viewpoint of category theory and appears again in [41.06.02], where the free-cocompletion universal property $F\mapsto\int^{c}F(c)\cdot H(c)$ is recognised as the pointwise left Kan extension $\mathrm{Lan}_{よ}H$, and in [41.05.02], where Lawvere theories and monadic adjunctions are built from presheaf-style free constructions. The foundational reason one lemma does all this work is that the Yoneda inverse $\Psi(u)_d(f)=F(f)(u)$ reconstructs every component from a single value $u$, so a comparison and a value are the same thing; this is exactly the mechanism that makes the address book rigid enough to be full and faithful and rich enough to be dense, and putting these together, the bridge is that full faithfulness and density are the inward and outward readings of one bijection, so representability in [41.04.01], the lemma in [41.04.02], and the constructions of [41.03.01] all reduce to evaluating a natural transformation at an identity arrow.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, multiple choice).**

Which statement is equivalent to the Yoneda embedding $よ\colon\mathcal{C}\to\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ being full and faithful?

A. Every presheaf is representable
B. For all $c,d$, the map $\mathcal{C}(c,d)\to\mathrm{Nat}(よc,よd)$, $f\mapstoよf$, is a bijection
C. The embedding is essentially surjective
D. Every presheaf is a limit of representables

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Full and faithful is a statement about hom-sets bijecting with natural transformations between the embedded images.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** Full faithfulness says exactly that $f\mapstoよf$ is a bijection on hom-sets, which is the Yoneda lemma specialised to $F=よd$. Option A is false (most presheaves are not representable); option C is false (the representables are a small subcategory); option D reverses density, which is a colimit, not a limit.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

For a discrete category $\mathcal{C}$ with three objects and only identity arrows, how many elements does the category of elements $\int_{\mathcal{C}}F$ have when $F$ sends each of the three objects to a set of sizes $2,3,5$ respectively?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Objects of $\int_{\mathcal{C}}F$ are pairs $(c,x)$ with $x\in F(c)$; with no non-identity arrows there are no extra morphisms.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$10$.** The category of elements has one object per element of each $F(c)$, so $2+3+5=10$ objects, and only identity morphisms since $\mathcal{C}$ is discrete. The density colimit then has ten indexing objects. Adding the sizes reflects that $F\cong\int^{c}F(c)\cdotよc$ is a coproduct of ten representables in this discrete case.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Using the co-Yoneda identity $F\cong\int^{c}F(c)\cdotよc$, show that the Yoneda embedding preserves any limit that exists in $\mathcal{C}$, and explain in one sentence why it need not preserve colimits.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Evaluate both sides at an object $d$: $(よL)(d)=\mathcal{C}(d,L)$, and use that $\mathcal{C}(d,-)$ preserves limits.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $L=\lim_j D_j$ in $\mathcal{C}$. Limits in $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ are pointwise, so it suffices to show $(よL)(d)=\mathcal{C}(d,L)$ is $\lim_j\mathcal{C}(d,D_j)$ for each $d$. This is the statement that the covariant hom-functor $\mathcal{C}(d,-)$ preserves limits, which holds because a cone over $D$ with apex $d$ is exactly a compatible family of arrows $d\to D_j$, uniquely factoring through $L$. Hence $よL\cong\lim_jよD_j$. Colimits are different: $\mathcal{C}(d,-)$ does not preserve colimits in general (for instance $\mathcal{C}(d,c\amalg c')$ is not $\mathcal{C}(d,c)\amalg\mathcal{C}(d,c')$ in general), so $よ$ need not preserve them; density is the theorem that repairs this by adjoining formal colimits of representables.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Rewrite the density colimit $F\cong\mathrm{colim}_{(c,x)\in\int_{\mathcal{C}}F}よc$ as the coend $F\cong\int^{c}F(c)\cdotよc$, and verify the coend's two identification rules match the morphisms of $\int_{\mathcal{C}}F$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A coend $\int^{c}G(c,c)$ quotients $\coprod_c G(c,c)$ by the relation generated by $G(f,\mathrm{id})$ and $G(\mathrm{id},f)$; identify $G(c,c)=F(c)\cdotよc$ and trace a morphism $f\colon c\to c'$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The coproduct $\coprod_{c}F(c)\cdotよc$ has summands indexed by pairs $(c,x)$ with $x\in F(c)$, matching the objects of $\int_{\mathcal{C}}F$. The coend identifies, for each $f\colon c\to c'$, the copy of $よc'$ in the $F(c')$-summand indexed by $x'$ with the copy of $よc$ in the $F(c)$-summand indexed by $x=F(f)(x')$, via precomposition with $f$. This is exactly the morphism $(c,x)\to(c',x')$ of $\int_{\mathcal{C}}F$ run in the indexing category, so the colimit over $\int_{\mathcal{C}}F$ and the coend compute the same quotient, giving $F\cong\int^{c}F(c)\cdotよc$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

A *universal property* states that $A\times B$ represents the functor $C\mapsto\mathcal{C}(C,A)\times\mathcal{C}(C,B)$. Rephrase this as a natural isomorphism of hom-functors and identify the universal element, linking the statement to [41.04.01].

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A natural isomorphism of hom-functors is an isomorphism $よ(A\times B)\congよA\timesよB$ in $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$; the universal element is the pair of projections.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The universal property gives $\mathcal{C}(C,A\times B)\cong\mathcal{C}(C,A)\times\mathcal{C}(C,B)$ naturally in $C$, which is the componentwise statement $よ(A\times B)\congよA\timesよB$ in the presheaf category (products there are pointwise). The universal element is $(\pi_A,\pi_B)\in\mathcal{C}(A\times B,A)\times\mathcal{C}(A\times B,B)$, the image of $\mathrm{id}_{A\times B}$, and every pair $(g,h)$ is recovered as $(\pi_A,\pi_B)$ whiskered by the unique $C\to A\times B$. This is the representability statement of [41.04.01] read through the Yoneda embedding: the product exists precisely when $よA\timesよB$ lies in the essential image of $よ$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove directly from the co-Yoneda identity that a functor $H\colon\widehat{\mathcal{C}}\to\mathcal{E}$ into a cocomplete $\mathcal{E}$ preserves all small colimits if and only if it is the left Kan extension $\mathrm{Lan}_{よ}(H\circよ)$ of its restriction to the representables.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use density to write any $F$ as a colimit of representables, then use that colimits commute with colimits in $\mathcal{E}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define $\widetilde H=\mathrm{Lan}_{よ}(H\circよ)$ by $\widetilde H(F)=\mathrm{colim}_{(c,x)\in\int_{\mathcal{C}}F}H(よc)=\int^{c}F(c)\cdot H(よc)$, which exists since $\mathcal{E}$ is cocomplete. On a representable, $\int_{\mathcal{C}}よd$ has terminal object $(d,\mathrm{id}_d)$, so $\widetilde H(よd)\cong H(よd)$, giving $\widetilde H\circよ\cong H\circよ$. If $H$ preserves colimits, then by density $H(F)\cong H(\mathrm{colim}_{(c,x)}よc)\cong\mathrm{colim}_{(c,x)}H(よc)\cong\widetilde H(F)$, naturally in $F$, so $H\cong\widetilde H=\mathrm{Lan}_{よ}(H\circよ)$. Conversely, if $H\cong\mathrm{Lan}_{よ}(H\circよ)$, then since colimits in $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ are computed objectwise and the density colimit commutes with any further colimit, $H$ preserves colimits. Hence cocontinuous functors out of $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ are exactly left Kan extensions of their representable-restrictions, the free-cocompletion property.

</details>

</aside>

Let $\mathcal{V}=\mathbf{Ab}$. State the enriched Yoneda lemma for a $\mathbf{Ab}$-functor $F\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathbf{Ab}$ and verify it specialises, on underlying sets, to the ordinary Yoneda lemma for the underlying ordinary category.

Elements of an abelian group $A$ are maps $\mathbb{Z}\to A$; the $\mathbf{Ab}$-enriched bijection evaluated at the unit recovers the set-level bijection.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $\mathcal{V}=\mathbf{Ab}$ the enriched Yoneda lemma gives an isomorphism of abelian groups $[\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}},\mathbf{Ab}](\mathcal{C}(-,c),F)\cong F(c)$ in $\mathbf{Ab}$, natural in $c$ and $F$ in the enriched sense. Taking underlying sets, the left side is the set of $\mathbf{Ab}$-natural transformations, which refines the set of ordinary natural transformations by requiring the naturality squares to be group homomorphisms, and the right side is the underlying set of $F(c)$. Elements of $F(c)$ correspond to homomorphisms $\mathbb{Z}\to F(c)$, and chasing the unit $1\in\mathbb{Z}=よc(c)$ through the enriched bijection recovers the ordinary Yoneda image $\alpha\mapsto\alpha_c(\mathrm{id}_c)$, so the enriched lemma specialises on underlying sets to the ordinary one of [41.04.02]. ## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (the free cocompletion).** For any locally small $\mathcal{C}$ the presheaf category $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}=[\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}},\mathbf{Set}]$ is the *free colimit-completion* of $\mathcal{C}$: it is cocomplete, and for every cocomplete category $\mathcal{E}$, restriction along $よ$ is an equivalence between cocontinuous functors $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}\to\mathcal{E}$ and arbitrary functors $\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{E}$ [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane-Moerdijk 1992]. The equivalence sends $H\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{E}$ to $\widetilde H=\mathrm{Lan}_{よ}H$ with $\widetilde H(F)=\int^{c}F(c)\cdot H(c)$, the density coend with $よc$ replaced by $H(c)$; on a representable the category of elements has a terminal object, so $\widetilde H(よc)\cong H(c)$ and the unit of the universal property holds. Density is thus the local statement of which free cocompletion is the global one: every presheaf is rebuilt from representables, so a colimit-preserving functor is fixed by where it sends the representables, and conversely any choice on representables extends uniquely. A full proof is Proposition 2 below.

**Theorem (the enriched Yoneda lemma).** Let $\mathcal{V}$ be a symmetric monoidal closed category and $\mathcal{C}$ a $\mathcal{V}$-category. For every $\mathcal{V}$-functor $F\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathcal{V}$ and every object $c$ there is a $\mathcal{V}$-natural isomorphism
$$
[\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}},\mathcal{V}]\bigl(\mathcal{C}(-,c),F\bigr)\ \cong\ F(c)
$$
in $\mathcal{V}_0$, the *enriched Yoneda lemma* [ref: TODO_REF Kelly 1982]. The bijection on underlying elements (maps $I\to F(c)$) is the ordinary one: a $\mathcal{V}$-natural transformation is determined by the image of $\mathrm{id}_c\colon I\to\mathcal{C}(c,c)$. The enriched embedding $\mathbf{Y}\colon\mathcal{C}\to[\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}},\mathcal{V}]$ is full and faithful in the enriched sense, and enriched density holds: every $\mathcal{V}$-presheaf is a weighted colimit of enriched representables. Taking $\mathcal{V}=\mathbf{Set}$ recovers the entire chapter verbatim, so the ordinary theory is the $\mathbf{Set}$-enriched case of a uniform statement; taking $\mathcal{V}=\mathbf{Cat}$ yields the 2-categorical Yoneda below.

**Theorem (the 2-categorical and bicategorical Yoneda).** For a 2-category $\mathcal{K}$ and a 2-functor $F\colon\mathcal{K}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathbf{Cat}$, the category of 2-natural transformations $\mathcal{K}(-,A)\Rightarrow F$ is isomorphic to $F(A)$: this is the *2-Yoneda lemma*, the $\mathcal{V}=\mathbf{Cat}$ instance of the enriched lemma. At the level of bicategories, Street's bicategorical Yoneda states that the bicategory of pseudofunctors $\mathcal{B}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathbf{Cat}$, pseudonatural transformations, and modifications is locally equivalent to $\mathcal{B}$ on the representables, and every pseudofunctor is a bicategorical colimit of representables [ref: TODO_REF Street 1980]. This bicategorical form is what identifies fibrations with pseudofunctors through the Grothendieck construction and underlies the indexed reading of the category of elements; the ordinary Yoneda lemma is its truncation to the 1-categorical case.

**Theorem (universal properties as natural isomorphisms of hom-functors).** A universal property in the sense of [41.04.01] is equivalently an isomorphism in $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ between a presheaf and a representable. Concretely, a limit of $D\colon\mathcal{J}\to\mathcal{C}$ is a representing object for the cone functor $C\mapsto\mathrm{Cone}(C,D)$, that is, an isomorphism $よ(\lim D)\cong\mathrm{Cone}(-,D)$; dually a colimit is a representation of the covariant cocone functor on $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$. Free constructions are the covariant side: the free group on a set $S$ is a representing object for $G\mapsto\mathbf{Set}(S,U(G))$, equivalently a natural isomorphism $\mathbf{Grp}(F(S),-)\cong\mathbf{Set}(S,U(-))$, which is the hom-set adjunction of [41.03.01]. Thus every universal property is the assertion that some presheaf is representable, and the Yoneda lemma is what turns that assertion into a computable bijection.

**Theorem (free constructions via left Kan extensions).** Density makes every free construction a left Kan extension. The free cocompletion is $\mathrm{Lan}_{よ}\mathrm{Id}_{\mathcal{C}}$; the free finite-limit completion is the same construction on $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$ followed by finite-limit closure; the free monoid on an object of a monoidal category, the free $R$-module on a set, and the free group are each $\mathrm{Lan}$ of an inclusion along a Yoneda-type embedding, computed by the coend $\int^{c}F(c)\cdot H(c)$ of [41.06.02]. This is why the left Kan extension formulas of [41.06.02] subsume the constructions of [41.05.02]: a left adjoint is a left Kan extension of the identity along its right adjoint, and a free algebra functor is a left Kan extension of the generators along the forgetful functor. The co-Yoneda identity is the special case $H=よ$ in which the extension reproduces its input.

**Synthesis.** Putting these together, the Yoneda lemma is the single fact from which full faithfulness, density, the free cocompletion, the enriched lemma, and the bicategorical lemma all descend. The foundational reason one bijection does all of this is that the inverse $\Psi(u)_d(f)=F(f)(u)$ reconstructs an entire transformation from one value, so the address book is simultaneously rigid enough to be full and faithful and rich enough to be dense, and this is exactly the content of the Yoneda cycle proved above. Reading the bijection in $\mathcal{V}$-enriched form generalises it to spaces, categories, and abelian groups without change of argument, so the $\mathbf{Set}$-enriched theory of [41.04.01] and [41.04.02] is the base case of a uniform statement; reading it two-dimensionally gives Street's bicategorical Yoneda, which is dual throughout to the covariant, co-Yoneda side. The central insight is that density makes $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ the free cocompletion, so every cocontinuous functor is a left Kan extension of its restriction to representables and the bridge is that adjunctions [41.03.01], monads [41.05.01], and Kan extensions [41.06.02] are each an instance of extending along $よ$, which is why all of category theory reduces to evaluating a natural transformation at an identity arrow.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (co-Yoneda identity, full proof).** *For every presheaf $F\in\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ the canonical map $\int^{c}F(c)\cdotよc\to F$ with $e$-component sending $(x,f\colon e\to c)\mapsto F(f)(x)$ is a natural isomorphism.*

*Proof.* The candidate $\theta\colon\int^{c}F(c)\cdotよc\to F$ is well-defined on the coend quotient because the two identifications agree: for $g\colon c\to c'$, an element $(x\in F(c),\, f\colon e\to c)$ identified with $(F(g)(x),\, g\circ f)$ on one side and $(x,\, f)$ traced through $よ$ on the other both map to $F(f)(x)=F(g\circ f)(F(g^{-1}\text{-style}))$; concretely $F(f)(x)$ is invariant since $F(g\circ f)(\,{-}\,)$ agrees with $F(f)$ after the $F(g)$-quotient, which is the coend relation. Naturality in $e$ holds because $F$ is a functor. Define the inverse $\iota_e\colon F(e)\to\int^{c}F(c)\cdotよc$ by $\iota_e(x)=(x,\mathrm{id}_e)\in F(e)\cdotよe$. Then $\theta_e\circ\iota_e(x)=F(\mathrm{id}_e)(x)=x$, so $\theta\circ\iota=\mathrm{id}$. For the other composite, an element $(x,f\colon e\to c)$ in the $F(c)\cdotよc$-summand is equal in the coend to $(F(f)(x),\mathrm{id}_e)\in F(e)\cdotよe$ via the morphism $f\colon(e,F(f)(x))\to(c,x)$ in $\int_{\mathcal{C}}F$, so $\iota_e\circ\theta$ fixes every representative, hence $\iota\circ\theta=\mathrm{id}$. Therefore $\theta$ is a natural isomorphism and $F\cong\int^{c}F(c)\cdotよc$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (free cocompletion universal property, full proof).** *Let $\mathcal{E}$ be cocomplete and $H\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{E}$ a functor. Then $\widetilde H(F)=\mathrm{colim}_{(c,x)\in\int_{\mathcal{C}}F}H(c)$ defines a cocontinuous functor $\widetilde H=\mathrm{Lan}_{よ}H\colon\widehat{\mathcal{C}}\to\mathcal{E}$ with $\widetilde H\circよ\cong H$, and any cocontinuous $K\colon\widehat{\mathcal{C}}\to\mathcal{E}$ with $K\circよ\cong H$ satisfies $K\cong\widetilde H$.*

*Proof.* The assignment is functorial: a natural transformation $\beta\colon F\Rightarrow G$ induces a map $\int_{\mathcal{C}}F\to\int_{\mathcal{C}}G$ sending $(c,x)\mapsto(c,\beta_c(x))$, hence a map between the colimits defining $\widetilde H(F)$ and $\widetilde H(G)$, and this preserves identities and composition by the functoriality of colimits. For a representable, $\int_{\mathcal{C}}よc$ has terminal object $(c,\mathrm{id}_c)$, so the colimit collapses: $\widetilde H(よc)\cong H(c)$, giving the unit isomorphism $\widetilde H\circよ\cong H$. Cocontinuity: a colimit of presheaves is computed pointwise, and re-indexing the categories of elements shows $\int_{\mathcal{C}}(\mathrm{colim}_i F_i)$ is the appropriate colimit of the $\int_{\mathcal{C}}F_i$; since colimits commute with colimits in $\mathcal{E}$, $\widetilde H(\mathrm{colim}_i F_i)\cong\mathrm{colim}_i\widetilde H(F_i)$. For uniqueness, if $K$ is cocontinuous with $K\circよ\cong H$, then by density $K(F)\cong K(\mathrm{colim}_{(c,x)}よc)\cong\mathrm{colim}_{(c,x)}K(よc)\cong\mathrm{colim}_{(c,x)}H(c)\cong\widetilde H(F)$, naturally in $F$, so $K\cong\widetilde H$. Hence restriction along $よ$ is an equivalence between cocontinuous functors $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}\to\mathcal{E}$ and functors $\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{E}$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (the Yoneda embedding preserves limits and reflects isomorphisms).** *The functor $よ\colon\mathcal{C}\to\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ preserves every limit that exists in $\mathcal{C}$, and reflects isomorphisms: if $よf$ is an isomorphism of presheaves then $f$ is an isomorphism in $\mathcal{C}$.*

*Proof.* For limits, let $L=\lim_j D_j$. Since limits in $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ are pointwise, $(\lim_jよD_j)(d)=\lim_j\mathcal{C}(d,D_j)$, and $\mathcal{C}(d,-)$ preserves limits because a cone over $D$ with apex $d$ is a compatible family $d\to D_j$ uniquely factoring through $L$, giving $\mathcal{C}(d,L)\cong\lim_j\mathcal{C}(d,D_j)=(\lim_jよD_j)(d)=(よL)(d)$ naturally in $d$. So $よL\cong\lim_jよD_j$. For reflection, suppose $よf\colonよc\toよc'$ is an isomorphism. Since $よ$ is full, the inverse $(よf)^{-1}=よg$ for some $g\colon c'\to c$. Then $よ(g\circ f)=よg\circよf=\mathrm{id}_{よc}=よ(\mathrm{id}_c)$, and faithfulness gives $g\circ f=\mathrm{id}_c$; symmetrically $f\circ g=\mathrm{id}_{c'}$. Hence $f$ is an isomorphism with inverse $g$. A full and faithful functor always reflects isomorphisms by this argument. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (the enriched Yoneda bijection).** *For a symmetric monoidal closed $\mathcal{V}$, a $\mathcal{V}$-category $\mathcal{C}$, a $\mathcal{V}$-functor $F\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathcal{V}$, and $c\in\mathcal{C}$, the map on underlying sets $\mathcal{V}_0\bigl(I,[\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}},\mathcal{V}](\mathcal{C}(-,c),F)\bigr)\to\mathcal{V}_0(I,F(c))$ sending a $\mathcal{V}$-natural transformation $\alpha$ to $I\xrightarrow{\mathrm{id}_c}\mathcal{C}(c,c)\xrightarrow{\alpha_c}F(c)$ is a bijection, natural in $c$ and $F$.*

*Proof.* A $\mathcal{V}$-natural transformation $\alpha\colon\mathcal{C}(-,c)\Rightarrow F$ is, in particular, a family of $\mathcal{V}_0$-morphisms $\alpha_d\colon\mathcal{C}(d,c)\to F(d)$ satisfying the $\mathcal{V}$-naturality condition that for all $d,e$ a certain square built from composition and $F$ commutes. The composite $u_\alpha=\alpha_c\circ\mathrm{id}_c\colon I\to F(c)$ records the image of the identity. Conversely, given $u\colon I\to F(c)$, define $\alpha^u_d\colon\mathcal{C}(d,c)\to F(d)$ as the composite $\mathcal{C}(d,c)\cong I\otimes\mathcal{C}(d,c)\xrightarrow{u\otimes\mathrm{id}}F(c)\otimes\mathcal{C}(d,c)\xrightarrow{\mathrm{comp}}F(d)$, where the last map is the $F$-action on the hom-object, the enriched analogue of $f\mapsto F(f)(u)$. The $\mathcal{V}$-naturality of $\alpha^u$ follows from the associativity of the $F$-action and the composition coherence of $\mathcal{C}$. The two constructions are mutually inverse: $\alpha^{u_\alpha}_d$ evaluated via the unit recovers $\alpha_d$ by the enriched naturality of $\alpha$ at the identity, and $u_{\alpha^u}=\alpha^u_c\circ\mathrm{id}_c=u$ by the unit coherence. Hence the map on underlying elements is a bijection, and the $\mathcal{V}$-naturality in $c$ and $F$ is the enriched analogue of the ordinary naturality square, transported along the closed structure of $\mathcal{V}$. For $\mathcal{V}=\mathbf{Set}$ with $I=1$ this reduces verbatim to the ordinary Yoneda lemma. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **The Yoneda lemma and the embedding [41.04.02].** The sibling unit states and proves the Yoneda lemma and the full faithfulness of $よ$ as its central results; the present unit takes those as input and establishes the Yoneda cycle — that the lemma, full faithfulness, density, and the co-Yoneda identity are one fact in four directions — and pushes into the enriched, 2-categorical, and free-cocompletion consequences. Propositions 1-2 below are the deeper forms of the density and free-cocompletion statements sketched in [41.04.02].

- **Representable functors and universal elements [41.04.01].** The representability theorem of [41.04.01] identifies a representation, a universal element, and a terminal object of the category of elements. The present unit reads that theorem through the Yoneda embedding: a universal property is the assertion that a presheaf is representable, and the cycle proved here is what turns that assertion into the computable bijection $\mathrm{Nat}(よc,F)\cong F(c)$.

- **Limits and universal cones [41.02.01].** A limit is a representing object for the cone functor, equivalently an isomorphism $よ(\lim D)\cong\mathrm{Cone}(-,D)$ in $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$, and Proposition 3 shows $よ$ preserves the limits of [41.02.01] because each $\mathcal{C}(d,-)$ does. Density is the complementary theorem that $よ$ does not preserve colimits but instead freely adjoins them, making $\widehat{\mathcal{C}}$ the universal home of formal colimits over the limits of [41.02.01].

- **Adjunctions as representability [41.03.01], [41.03.03].** A left adjoint to $U$ is a choice of representation of $\mathcal{C}(c,U(-))$ for every $c$, with the universal elements assembling into the unit of the adjunction; the unit-counit calculus of [41.03.03] is the two-dimensional shadow of the Yoneda bijection applied componentwise. The free-construction theorem above restates this as: a left adjoint is a left Kan extension of the identity along its right adjoint, computed by the density coend.

- **Monads and algebraic free constructions [41.05.01], [41.05.02].** Every adjunction $F\dashv G$ generates the monad $GF$ of [41.05.01], and the free-algebra functor of [41.05.02] is the left Kan extension of the generators along the forgetful functor, the same coend formula $\int^{c}F(c)\cdot H(c)$ that gives free cocompletion. Lawvere theories are presheaf-style free constructions on a skeleton, so the density theorem governs their completion to categories of algebras.

- **Ends, coends, and Kan extensions [41.06.01], [41.06.02].** The co-Yoneda identity $F\cong\int^{c}F(c)\cdotよc$ is given its full calculus in [41.06.01], where the Fubini and continuity rules make the formula computational, and the free-cocompletion extension $\mathrm{Lan}_{よ}H$ is the leading example of the pointwise Kan extensions of [41.06.02]. The Yoneda cycle proved here is the lemma from which those general formulas descend by replacing $よc$ with an arbitrary weight.

- **The functor of points in algebraic geometry [04.02.01].** The geometric unit [04.02.01] develops the reading under which a scheme is its representable presheaf $\mathbf{Sch}(-,S)$ and moduli problems are functors to be proved representable; the density theorem here is what allows arbitrary presheaves to be handled as colimits of representables even when not themselves representable, the entry point to algebraic stacks where representability fails.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The bijection at the heart of this unit is attributed to Nobuo Yoneda from a 1954 conversation at the Gare du Nord in Paris, reported by Saunders Mac Lane, and entered the literature through Yoneda's paper on the homology theory of modules [ref: TODO_REF Yoneda 1954]. Mac Lane's *Categories for the Working Mathematician* (1971; 2nd ed. 1998) fixed the lemma, the embedding, and the observation that an object is determined up to isomorphism by the functor it represents as the central results of Chapter III [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998]; the coend form, often called the co-Yoneda or ninja-Yoneda identity, is developed there in Chapter X alongside the ends-and-coends calculus that makes density computational.

The density theorem and the free-cocompletion reading of presheaves were systematised in topos theory: Mac Lane and Moerdijk's *Sheaves in Geometry and Logic* (1992) presents every presheaf as a colimit of representables in its first chapter and uses it to build the sheafification adjunction [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane-Moerdijk 1992]. The enriched and weighted-colimit forms are due to Max Kelly, whose *Basic Concepts of Enriched Category Theory* (1982) gives the $\mathcal{V}$-enriched Yoneda lemma and the density theorem for $\mathcal{V}$-presheaves as the uniform backbone of enriched theory [ref: TODO_REF Kelly 1982]. The bicategorical refinement — the Yoneda lemma for bicategories, under which every pseudofunctor is a bicategorical colimit of representables — is due to Ross Street, appearing in his work on fibrations in bicategories [ref: TODO_REF Street 1980], and is the formal setting that identifies fibrations with pseudofunctors. Grothendieck's functor-of-points philosophy, in which a geometric object is the functor it represents on all test objects [ref: TODO_REF Grothendieck 1960], is the geometric avatar of the same slogan: the address book of a scheme is its complete portrait, and density is what licenses the passage from representable functors to arbitrary moduli functors and on to stacks.
