## Intuition [Beginner]

Across mathematics the same shape of idea keeps reappearing. Sets come with functions between them. Groups come with homomorphisms. Shapes in geometry come with continuous deformations. Each time there is a kind of object and a kind of arrow that carries one object to another. A category is the simple device that records both at once: a collection of objects together with the arrows allowed between them, and a rule for following one arrow after another.

Only two things are required for the bookkeeping to work. First, arrows compose: an arrow from $A$ to $B$ followed by an arrow from $B$ to $C$ yields an arrow from $A$ to $C$. Second, every object carries an identity arrow, a do-nothing arrow that leaves any arrow it meets unchanged, the way the function fixing every point leaves a set alone.

The striking part is how much you can say while looking only at the arrows and never opening up the objects. Whether two objects deserve to be called "the same" can be settled by the arrows around them. This arrows-first habit turns loose analogies between different fields into exact statements you can prove once and reuse everywhere.

A functor is the next idea. It is a translation between two whole categories: a rule that sends objects to objects and arrows to arrows while respecting both composition and identities. Functors are how a fact established in one area gets carried faithfully into another.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture two boards of pins. The left board is one category, the right board another. On each board, curved strings connect the pins; these strings are the arrows of that category. A functor is a careful tracing: it pins every left dot to a chosen right dot and every left string to a right string, so that "string then string" on the left always lands on "string then string" on the right.

| feature | a category | a functor between two |
|---|---|---|
| has | objects (pins) and arrows (strings) | a target pin for each source pin |
| respects | composition and identities | composition and identities |
| typical example | sets with functions | sending a set to its number of elements |
| reversing arrows gives | the opposite category | the same functor, arrows flipped |

![Two boards of pins connected by internal strings; a functor traces the left board onto the right board, sending each pin to a pin and each string to a string, so a triangle of composable strings on the left maps to a triangle on the right](/img/categories-functors-duality-placeholder.svg)

The point of the picture is that a functor preserves the shape of the diagram, not merely the separate pins. A triangle of composable strings on the left becomes a triangle on the right. That shape-keeping is the whole meaning of the word functor, and it is why a functor carries structure honestly from one category to another.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Work inside the category of sets: the objects are sets, the arrows are ordinary functions. Take a fixed set $A$ with two elements, $A = \{0, 1\}$.

Step 1. Build a rule that sends each set $X$ to the set of all functions from $A$ to $X$. Name this collection $\mathrm{Hom}(A, X)$. A function from a two-element set to $X$ is just a choice of two elements of $X$ in order, so $\mathrm{Hom}(A, X)$ behaves like the set of ordered pairs drawn from $X$.

Step 2. Check that the rule also acts on arrows. Given a function $g$ from $X$ to $Y$, turn any function $f$ from $A$ to $X$ into the function "$g$ following $f$", which runs from $A$ to $Y$. So the rule sends an arrow $X \to Y$ to an arrow $\mathrm{Hom}(A,X) \to \mathrm{Hom}(A,Y)$, keeping the direction.

Step 3. Confirm identities are respected. If $g$ is the do-nothing function on $X$, then "$g$ following $f$" returns $f$ unchanged, so the do-nothing arrow maps to the do-nothing arrow.

What this tells us: the construction "functions out of $A$" is itself a functor on the category of sets. Now run the same recipe with the arrows flipped, sending $X$ to "functions into $A$": following an arrow $X \to Y$ now goes backward, $\mathrm{Hom}(Y,A) \to \mathrm{Hom}(X,A)$. The two recipes are mirror images. That mirror is the duality this unit is built around.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A *category* $\mathcal{C}$ consists of a class of *objects* $\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$; for each ordered pair $(A,B)$ of objects a collection $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,B)$ of *morphisms* (also written $\mathcal{C}(A,B)$); a *composition* operation $\circ\colon \mathrm{Hom}(B,C)\times\mathrm{Hom}(A,B)\to\mathrm{Hom}(A,C)$; and for each object $A$ an *identity* $\mathrm{id}_A\in\mathrm{Hom}(A,A)$ [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998]. These satisfy *associativity*, $h\circ(g\circ f)=(h\circ g)\circ f$ whenever the composites are defined, and the *identity law*, $\mathrm{id}_B\circ f = f = f\circ\mathrm{id}_A$ for every $f\colon A\to B$.

The *size hierarchy* matters once Hom-collections are themselves objects of study. A category is *small* if $\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$ and the totality of morphisms are sets; *locally small* if each $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(A,B)$ is a set (the standard ambient assumption); and *large* otherwise. The standard examples $\mathbf{Set}$ (sets and functions), $\mathbf{Grp}$ (groups and homomorphisms), $\mathbf{Top}$ (topological spaces and continuous maps), and $\mathbf{Vect}_k$ ($k$-vector spaces and linear maps) are locally small but not small; collecting all their objects into a single set is barred by the set-theoretic paradoxes, so one fixes a Grothendieck universe or works with classes.

Two examples encode familiar structures as one-object or arrows-only categories. A *monoid* $M$ is a category $\mathbf{B}M$ with a single object $\ast$ whose morphisms $\ast\to\ast$ are the elements of $M$, composition the monoid product, identity the unit. A *preordered set* $(P,\le)$ is a category $\mathcal{C}_P$ whose objects are the elements of $P$, with exactly one morphism $a\to b$ when $a\le b$ and none otherwise; associativity and identities encode transitivity and reflexivity. Such *thin* categories — at most one morphism between any two objects — are exactly the preorders.

**Definition (iso, mono, epi).** A morphism $f\colon A\to B$ is an *isomorphism* if there is $g\colon B\to A$ with $g\circ f=\mathrm{id}_A$ and $f\circ g=\mathrm{id}_B$. It is a *monomorphism* (left-cancellable) if $f\circ u=f\circ v$ forces $u=v$, and an *epimorphism* (right-cancellable) if $u\circ f=v\circ f$ forces $u=v$. In $\mathbf{Set}$ these recover injective and surjective functions, but the match is not universal: the inclusion $\mathbb{Z}\hookrightarrow\mathbb{Q}$ of rings is both monic and epic yet not an isomorphism, and in $\mathbf{Top}$ a continuous bijection that is monic and epic need not be a homeomorphism.

**Definition (opposite category).** The *opposite* $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$ has the same objects, with $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}}(A,B)=\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(B,A)$ and composition $g\,\circ^{\mathrm{op}}\,f := f\circ g$. Identities are unchanged, and $(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}})^{\mathrm{op}}=\mathcal{C}$ on the nose. Under this reversal a monomorphism in $\mathcal{C}$ is exactly an epimorphism in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$, and an initial object becomes a terminal one.

**Definition (product and slice categories).** The *product category* $\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{D}$ has objects the pairs $(C,D)$ and morphisms the pairs $(f,g)$, composed coordinatewise. For a fixed object $A$ of $\mathcal{C}$, the *slice* (or *over*) category $\mathcal{C}/A$ has as objects the morphisms $f\colon X\to A$ into $A$, and as morphisms $f\to f'$ the morphisms $h\colon X\to X'$ with $f'\circ h=f$. The dual *coslice* $A/\mathcal{C}$ is $(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}/A)^{\mathrm{op}}$, with objects the morphisms out of $A$.

**Definition (functor).** A *covariant functor* $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ assigns to each object $A$ an object $F(A)$ and to each morphism $f\colon A\to B$ a morphism $F(f)\colon F(A)\to F(B)$, with $F(\mathrm{id}_A)=\mathrm{id}_{F(A)}$ and $F(g\circ f)=F(g)\circ F(f)$. A *contravariant* functor reverses arrows, $F(f)\colon F(B)\to F(A)$ with $F(g\circ f)=F(f)\circ F(g)$; equivalently it is a covariant functor on $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$. The *covariant Hom-functor* $\mathrm{Hom}(A,-)\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathbf{Set}$ sends $f\colon X\to Y$ to postcomposition $g\mapsto f\circ g$; the *contravariant* $\mathrm{Hom}(-,B)\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathbf{Set}$ sends $f$ to precomposition $g\mapsto g\circ f$. Notation introduced here ($\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}$, $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$, $\mathcal{C}/A$, $\mathrm{id}_A$) is recorded in `_meta/NOTATION.md`.

**Definition (kinds of functor; subcategories).** $F$ is *faithful* if each map $\mathrm{Hom}(A,B)\to\mathrm{Hom}(FA,FB)$ is injective, *full* if each is surjective, and *essentially surjective* if every object of $\mathcal{D}$ is isomorphic to some $F(A)$. A *subcategory* $\mathcal{S}\subseteq\mathcal{C}$ is a category whose objects and morphisms lie in $\mathcal{C}$ with the inherited composition; it is *full* when the inclusion is a full functor. Small categories and functors between them form a category $\mathbf{Cat}$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Mono and epi are not injective and surjective.* The cancellation properties are defined purely by arrows; in concrete categories they often but not always coincide with injectivity and surjectivity. The dense inclusion $\mathbb{Q}\hookrightarrow\mathbb{R}$ in the category of Hausdorff spaces, or $\mathbb{Z}\hookrightarrow\mathbb{Q}$ in commutative rings, is epic without being surjective: an arrow agreeing on a dense or generating part already agrees everywhere.

- *A bijective morphism need not be an isomorphism.* In $\mathbf{Top}$ the identity from a set with the discrete topology to the same set with a coarser topology is a continuous bijection — monic and epic — but its inverse is not continuous. Being iso requires a two-sided inverse *inside the category*, not merely a bijection of underlying sets.

- *A functor is not a function on Hom-sets in isolation.* The assignment must respect composition: sending each $f$ to a same-shaped arrow while failing $F(g\circ f)=F(g)\circ F(f)$ gives only a graph map. The power-set assignment $X\mapsto\mathcal{P}(X)$ becomes a functor only after fixing a direction — direct image makes it covariant, preimage makes it contravariant.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result of the unit is the duality principle: every theorem about categories has a dual, obtained by reversing arrows, and a single proof establishes both.

**Theorem (duality principle).** Let $\Phi$ be a statement in the first-order language of categories — built from objects, morphisms, the relations "is the source of", "is the target of", "is the composite of", and "is an identity". Let $\Phi^{\mathrm{op}}$ be the statement obtained by reversing every morphism (swapping source and target, and reversing every composite $g\circ f\rightsquigarrow f\circ g$). Then $\Phi$ holds in a category $\mathcal{C}$ if and only if $\Phi^{\mathrm{op}}$ holds in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$. Consequently, if $\Phi$ is a theorem (true in every category), then $\Phi^{\mathrm{op}}$ is a theorem as well [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998].

*Proof.* The opposite construction is an exact, structure-reversing relabelling. By definition $\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}})=\mathrm{Ob}(\mathcal{C})$ and $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}}(A,B)=\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(B,A)$, with composition $g\,\circ^{\mathrm{op}}\,f=f\circ g$ and the same identities. So a morphism $f$ has source $A$ and target $B$ in $\mathcal{C}$ if and only if it has source $B$ and target $A$ in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$, and $w=g\circ f$ holds in $\mathcal{C}$ if and only if $w=f\circ^{\mathrm{op}}g$ holds in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$; the identity predicate is preserved verbatim.

Interpret the language of categories in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$. Reading any basic relation of $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$ back through these identifications produces precisely the arrow-reversed relation of $\mathcal{C}$. By induction on the construction of $\Phi$ from atomic relations using the logical connectives and quantifiers (which range over the same objects and morphisms in both readings, since the underlying data are equal), the truth value of $\Phi$ evaluated in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$ equals the truth value of $\Phi^{\mathrm{op}}$ evaluated in $\mathcal{C}$. This is the first claim.

For the second, suppose $\Phi$ holds in every category. Given any category $\mathcal{D}$, apply the hypothesis to $\mathcal{D}^{\mathrm{op}}$: then $\Phi$ holds in $\mathcal{D}^{\mathrm{op}}$, so by the first claim $\Phi^{\mathrm{op}}$ holds in $(\mathcal{D}^{\mathrm{op}})^{\mathrm{op}}=\mathcal{D}$. Since $\mathcal{D}$ was arbitrary, $\Phi^{\mathrm{op}}$ is a theorem. The involution $(-)^{\mathrm{op}}$ on statements satisfies $(\Phi^{\mathrm{op}})^{\mathrm{op}}=\Phi$, so the correspondence between theorems and their duals is a bijection. $\square$

**Bridge.** The duality principle builds toward every later "co-" notion in the corpus — coproduct, colimit, cokernel, comonad — and appears again in [41.02.01], where limits and colimits are shown to be dual under exactly this reversal, so one existence proof discharges both. The foundational reason it works is that $(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}})^{\mathrm{op}}=\mathcal{C}$ makes arrow-reversal an involution; the central insight is that a categorical statement carries no information beyond the pattern of its arrows, so reversing them is a symmetry of the whole language. This is exactly the mechanism by which a monomorphism is dual to an epimorphism and an initial object is dual to a terminal one: each pair is a single statement read in $\mathcal{C}$ and in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$. Putting these together, duality halves the labour of the subject — the bridge is that proving a fact about products silently proves the matching fact about coproducts, and this discipline of prove-once recurs whenever a universal property and its dual govern a construction.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that in the category $\mathbf{CRing}$ of commutative rings the inclusion $\iota\colon\mathbb{Z}\to\mathbb{Q}$ is an epimorphism, though it is not surjective.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Two ring homomorphisms out of $\mathbb{Q}$ agreeing on $\mathbb{Z}$ must agree on every $1/n$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $u,v\colon\mathbb{Q}\to R$ satisfy $u\circ\iota=v\circ\iota$, so $u(n)=v(n)$ for all $n\in\mathbb{Z}$. For nonzero $n$, $u(1/n)$ is the inverse of $u(n)$ in $R$ and likewise $v(1/n)$ is the inverse of $v(n)=u(n)$; inverses are unique, so $u(1/n)=v(1/n)$, hence $u(m/n)=u(m)u(1/n)=v(m/n)$ for all rationals. Thus $u=v$, proving $\iota$ is epic. It is plainly not surjective. So "epimorphism" and "surjective" diverge in $\mathbf{CRing}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

State the dual of the statement "$A$ is an initial object" and prove, using the duality principle, that initial objects are unique up to isomorphism given that terminal objects are.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reverse the unique arrow: initial in $\mathcal{C}$ is terminal in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

"$A$ is initial" reads: for every $X$ there is exactly one morphism $A\to X$. Its dual reverses the arrow: "for every $X$ there is exactly one morphism $X\to A$", which is "$A$ is terminal". An object is initial in $\mathcal{C}$ iff it is terminal in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$. The fact "any two terminal objects are uniquely isomorphic" is a theorem; applying it in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$ and reading the result back in $\mathcal{C}$ (where isomorphism is self-dual) yields "any two initial objects are uniquely isomorphic". One proof, two theorems.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $U\colon\mathbf{Grp}\to\mathbf{Set}$ be the forgetful functor. Show $U$ is faithful but not full.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A group homomorphism is determined by its underlying function (faithfulness); but not every function between underlying sets respects the operation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Faithful: two homomorphisms $G\to H$ with the same underlying function are equal as homomorphisms, so $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Grp}}(G,H)\to\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{Set}}(UG,UH)$ is injective. Not full: take $G=H=\mathbb{Z}/3$. There are $9$ functions between the underlying $3$-element sets, but only homomorphisms fixing $0$ and respecting addition, of which there are $3$ (the maps $x\mapsto kx$). So the map on Hom-sets is not surjective, and $U$ is not full.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that a fully faithful functor $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ *reflects* isomorphisms: if $F(f)$ is an isomorphism in $\mathcal{D}$, then $f$ is an isomorphism in $\mathcal{C}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Fullness produces a candidate inverse; faithfulness forces the inverse equations back in $\mathcal{C}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $f\colon A\to B$ with $F(f)$ invertible, inverse $k\colon FB\to FA$. By fullness $k=F(g)$ for some $g\colon B\to A$. Then $F(g\circ f)=F(g)\circ F(f)=k\circ F(f)=\mathrm{id}_{FA}=F(\mathrm{id}_A)$, and faithfulness gives $g\circ f=\mathrm{id}_A$; symmetrically $F(f\circ g)=F(f)\circ k=\mathrm{id}_{FB}=F(\mathrm{id}_B)$ forces $f\circ g=\mathrm{id}_B$. So $g$ is a two-sided inverse and $f$ is an isomorphism. (A fully faithful $F$ need not be injective on objects, but it reflects isos.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

A category $\mathcal{C}$ is a *groupoid* if every morphism is an isomorphism. Show that the one-object groupoids are exactly the groups, and describe what a functor between one-object groupoids is.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reuse the monoid-as-one-object-category dictionary and add invertibility.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A one-object category $\mathbf{B}M$ has its morphisms $\ast\to\ast$ forming a monoid $M$ under composition. Requiring every morphism to be an isomorphism means every element of $M$ has a two-sided inverse, i.e. $M$ is a group. Conversely any group $G$ gives a one-object groupoid $\mathbf{B}G$. A functor $\mathbf{B}G\to\mathbf{B}H$ sends $\ast$ to $\ast$ and is a map $G\to H$ preserving composition and identity — exactly a group homomorphism. So $\mathbf{B}(-)$ is a fully faithful embedding of $\mathbf{Grp}$ into one-object groupoids.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Proposition (size and Hom-functor representability constraints).** A locally small category $\mathcal{C}$ admits, for each object $A$, the Hom-functors $\mathrm{Hom}(A,-)\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathbf{Set}$ and $\mathrm{Hom}(-,A)\colon\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathbf{Set}$, valued in genuine sets precisely because each Hom-collection is a set. Local smallness is what makes $\mathbf{Set}$-valued functors the right probes; in a merely large category the Hom-functors land in proper classes and the representable-functor machinery of [41.04.02] does not get off the ground. The product $\mathcal{C}\times\mathcal{D}$ and slice $\mathcal{C}/A$ inherit local smallness, and $(\mathcal{C}/A)^{\mathrm{op}}=\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}/A$ is the coslice — duality acting on the comma constructions.

**Theorem (functoriality of geometric and algebraic invariants).** The fundamental group is a functor $\pi_1\colon\mathbf{Top}_\ast\to\mathbf{Grp}$ from pointed spaces: a based map $f\colon(X,x_0)\to(Y,y_0)$ induces $f_*\colon\pi_1(X,x_0)\to\pi_1(Y,y_0)$ by $[\gamma]\mapsto[f\circ\gamma]$, with $(\mathrm{id})_*=\mathrm{id}$ and $(g\circ f)_*=g_*\circ f_*$. Singular homology is a sequence of functors $H_n\colon\mathbf{Top}\to\mathbf{Ab}$, and singular cohomology $H^n\colon\mathbf{Top}^{\mathrm{op}}\to\mathbf{Ab}$ is contravariant. Functoriality is precisely what makes these invariants useful: a property visible in $\mathbf{Grp}$ or $\mathbf{Ab}$ obstructs the existence of a map upstream in $\mathbf{Top}$.

**Theorem (faithful $\ne$ injective on objects; full + faithful + essentially surjective $=$ equivalence).** A functor that is full, faithful, and essentially surjective is an *equivalence of categories*: there is a functor $G\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{C}$ and natural isomorphisms $GF\cong\mathrm{Id}_{\mathcal{C}}$, $FG\cong\mathrm{Id}_{\mathcal{D}}$ (the natural-isomorphism language is developed in [41.01.02]). Equivalence is the correct notion of sameness for categories — coarser than isomorphism, since it ignores the difference between isomorphic objects, yet preserving every property expressible in the categorical language. The skeleton of $\mathcal{C}$, a full subcategory with one object per isomorphism class, is equivalent to $\mathcal{C}$.

**Theorem ($\mathbf{Cat}$ as a category, and the $2$-dimensional refinement).** Small categories and functors form a category $\mathbf{Cat}$, with composition of functors associative and identities the identity functors. But $\mathbf{Cat}$ carries more: between two parallel functors live natural transformations, making $\mathbf{Cat}$ a $2$-category whose $2$-cells are the subject of [41.01.02]. The opposite construction $(-)^{\mathrm{op}}\colon\mathbf{Cat}\to\mathbf{Cat}$ is itself a functor, an involution on objects with $((-)^{\mathrm{op}})^2=\mathrm{Id}$, which is the structural home of the duality principle: every theorem about $\mathbf{Cat}$ that is stable under $(-)^{\mathrm{op}}$ comes in dual pairs.

**Synthesis.** Putting these together, a category is a self-contained universe of arrows, a functor is a translation between such universes, and duality is the symmetry that halves the subject. The foundational reason the whole apparatus coheres is that $(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}})^{\mathrm{op}}=\mathcal{C}$: arrow-reversal is an involution, so every construction acquires a dual for free and every theorem stable under reversal comes in a matched pair. This is exactly the pattern that generalises from mono/epi and initial/terminal to the limit/colimit duality of [41.02.01] and to the representable/corepresentable split that the Yoneda lemma of [41.04.02] exploits; the central insight is that an object is determined by the arrows around it, so reversing those arrows is a genuine symmetry of meaning rather than a notational trick. The bridge is functoriality itself: $\pi_1$, homology, and the Hom-functors all transport categorical structure faithfully, which is exactly why an obstruction proved in one category propagates to another, and the same transport, applied to natural transformations in [41.01.02], upgrades $\mathbf{Cat}$ from a category to a $2$-category where equivalence — not isomorphism — is the right notion of sameness.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (the Hom-functor is a functor).** For a fixed object $A$ in a locally small category $\mathcal{C}$, the assignment $\mathrm{Hom}(A,-)\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathbf{Set}$ is a covariant functor.

*Proof.* On objects $X\mapsto\mathrm{Hom}(A,X)$, a set by local smallness. On a morphism $f\colon X\to Y$ define $f_*(g)=f\circ g$ for $g\colon A\to X$; since $f\circ g\colon A\to Y$, this is a function $\mathrm{Hom}(A,X)\to\mathrm{Hom}(A,Y)$. Identities: $(\mathrm{id}_X)_*(g)=\mathrm{id}_X\circ g=g$, so $(\mathrm{id}_X)_*=\mathrm{id}_{\mathrm{Hom}(A,X)}$. Composition: for $f\colon X\to Y$ and $h\colon Y\to Z$, $(h\circ f)_*(g)=(h\circ f)\circ g=h\circ(f\circ g)=h_*\bigl(f_*(g)\bigr)$ by associativity, so $(h\circ f)_*=h_*\circ f_*$. Both functor axioms hold. The contravariant statement for $\mathrm{Hom}(-,A)$ is its dual, obtained by reading this proof in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (composition of functors is a functor; $\mathbf{Cat}$ is a category).** *If $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ and $G\colon\mathcal{D}\to\mathcal{E}$ are functors then $G\circ F$ is a functor, composition is associative, and the identity functors are neutral.*

*Proof.* Define $(G\circ F)(A)=G(F(A))$ and $(G\circ F)(f)=G(F(f))$. Identities: $(G\circ F)(\mathrm{id}_A)=G(F(\mathrm{id}_A))=G(\mathrm{id}_{FA})=\mathrm{id}_{GFA}$. Composites: $(G\circ F)(g\circ f)=G\bigl(F(g)\circ F(f)\bigr)=G(F(g))\circ G(F(f))$, using functoriality of $F$ then of $G$. So $G\circ F$ is a functor. Associativity $(H\circ G)\circ F=H\circ(G\circ F)$ and the identity laws hold because they hold object- and morphism-wise in the codomains, where composition of the underlying assignments is ordinary function composition. Hence small categories and functors form a category $\mathbf{Cat}$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (mono is dual to epi under $(-)^{\mathrm{op}}$).** *A morphism $f$ is a monomorphism in $\mathcal{C}$ if and only if $f$ is an epimorphism in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$.*

*Proof.* $f\colon A\to B$ is monic in $\mathcal{C}$ when $f\circ u=f\circ v\Rightarrow u=v$ for all $u,v\colon T\to A$. Read these data in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$: the morphism $f$ now runs $B\to A$, and $u,v$ run $A\to T$, with the $\mathcal{C}$-composite $f\circ u$ equal to the $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$-composite $u\circ^{\mathrm{op}}f$. The monic condition becomes $u\circ^{\mathrm{op}}f=v\circ^{\mathrm{op}}f\Rightarrow u=v$, which is exactly right-cancellability of $f$ in $\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}}$, i.e. $f$ is epic there. The converse is the same equivalence read backwards, using $(\mathcal{C}^{\mathrm{op}})^{\mathrm{op}}=\mathcal{C}$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (equivalences preserve mono, epi, and iso).** *If $F\colon\mathcal{C}\to\mathcal{D}$ is full and faithful, then $f$ is monic (resp. epic, resp. iso) in $\mathcal{C}$ iff $F(f)$ is monic (resp. epic, resp. iso) in $\mathcal{D}$, restricted to test maps in the image.*

*Proof.* For iso this is Exercise 6 together with its converse: a functor sends isomorphisms to isomorphisms because it preserves the inverse equations, and a fully faithful functor reflects them. For monos: faithfulness gives, for $u,v$ with $F(f)\circ F(u)=F(f)\circ F(v)$, that $F(f\circ u)=F(f\circ v)$ hence $f\circ u=f\circ v$, and if $f$ is monic then $u=v$, so $F(f)$ is monic against maps from the image; fullness lets every test map into $FA$ be realised as some $F(u)$, completing the reflection. The epi statement is the dual, obtained by reading the mono argument in the opposite categories via Proposition 3. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Natural transformations and the $2$-category $\mathbf{Cat}$ [41.01.02].** The morphisms *between* functors are natural transformations; they upgrade $\mathbf{Cat}$ from the plain category built here into a $2$-category and supply the natural isomorphisms that define equivalence of categories. The faithful/full/essentially-surjective trichotomy introduced in this unit is exactly the data that, with naturality, characterises an equivalence, so [41.01.02] is the immediate continuation of the functor theory developed here.

- **Limits and colimits [41.02.01].** Products and slice categories here are the smallest cases of the general limit/colimit calculus, and the duality principle proved in this unit is what makes colimits the formal mirror of limits: one establishes the limit theory and reads off the colimit theory in the opposite category. Initial and terminal objects, shown dual here, are the empty-diagram limit and colimit.

- **The Yoneda lemma and representability [41.04.02].** The covariant and contravariant Hom-functors constructed in this unit are the representable functors whose natural transformations the Yoneda lemma computes; the slogan "an object is determined by the arrows around it", used here to motivate the duality principle, becomes the precise Yoneda embedding in [41.04.02], with the contravariant Hom-functor giving the presheaf incarnation.

- **The categorical survey [01.02.09].** This unit deepens the opening pages of the foundations-level survey [01.02.09], which treats categories, functors, natural transformations, Yoneda, and adjunction in a single sweep; the present unit owns the careful theory of categories, the size hierarchy, mono/epi/iso across concrete categories, and the duality principle, which [01.02.09] states more briefly. The groups, rings, modules, and spaces that the rest of the corpus studies are the running examples of the categories named here, so this unit is the language in which their structural theory is phrased.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Category theory was created by Samuel Eilenberg and Saunders Mac Lane in their 1945 paper *General theory of natural equivalences* [ref: TODO_REF Eilenberg-Mac Lane 1945], whose stated aim was to make precise the word "natural" in statements such as the natural isomorphism between a finite-dimensional vector space and its double dual. Defining "natural transformation" forced the prior definitions of functor and category, so the three notions were introduced in descending order of abstraction from the phenomenon they were meant to explain. The duality principle and the systematic use of the opposite category were already present in that first paper, where reversing arrows was recognised as a symmetry of the new language.

The maturation of the framework belongs to the 1950s and 1960s. Mac Lane's *Categories for the Working Mathematician* (1971; 2nd ed. 1998) [ref: TODO_REF Mac Lane 1998] codified the size discipline of small, locally small, and large categories, the comma and slice constructions, and the duality principle as a working tool. Peter Freyd's *Abelian Categories* (1964) pushed duality to the point where each theorem of homological algebra automatically yields its dual, and the contravariant Hom-functors developed here became the foundation of sheaf and presheaf theory in Grothendieck's reworking of algebraic geometry. The structuralist reading — that a mathematical object has no content beyond its position in a web of morphisms, so that arrow-reversal is a genuine symmetry of meaning — descends directly from Eilenberg and Mac Lane's original concern with naturality.
