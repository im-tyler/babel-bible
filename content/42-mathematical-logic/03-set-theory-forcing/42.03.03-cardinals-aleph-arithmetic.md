## Intuition [Beginner]

Two herders, neither of whom can count past three, want to know who owns more sheep. They line their flocks up side by side and pair them off — one of yours next to one of mine — and keep going. If every sheep finds a partner with none left over, the flocks are the same size. If one side runs out first, that side is smaller. This pairing trick decides "same size" without ever naming a number. It is the whole idea behind measuring how big a collection is.

The surprise is that this trick keeps working past every finite number, and when it does it tells us something strange: not all infinities are the same. The counting numbers $0, 1, 2, 3, \dots$ can be paired off perfectly with the even numbers, so "infinitely many counting numbers" and "infinitely many even numbers" are the same size. A part can be as big as the whole. That never happens with finite collections, and it is the first sign that infinite sizes obey their own rules.

But here is the twist that started a whole subject: the points on a line cannot be paired off with the counting numbers, no matter how cleverly you try. There are strictly more of them. So there is a smallest infinity, the size of the counting numbers, and then bigger infinities above it, climbing forever. A **cardinal number** is a name for one of these sizes, finite or infinite, and the arithmetic of these names is what this unit is about.

## Visual [Beginner]

To compare two collections, draw arrows pairing each item on the left with exactly one item on the right. A pairing that uses up both sides with nothing left over means "same size."

![Two columns of dots connected by arrows showing a one-to-one pairing between the counting numbers and the even numbers, with the counting number n matched to the even number two times n, illustrating that an infinite set can match a part of itself.](/img/cardinals-aleph-arithmetic-placeholder.svg)

```
   counting numbers        even numbers
        0  ------------------>  0
        1  ------------------>  2
        2  ------------------>  4
        3  ------------------>  6
        4  ------------------>  8
       ...                     ...
       (n maps to 2n; every even number is hit exactly once)
```

The pairing above sends each counting number $n$ to the even number $2n$. Every even number is the partner of exactly one counting number, and nothing is left over on either side. So these two infinite collections have the same size, even though one looks like only "half" of the other.

The next table records the first few sizes. The finite sizes are the ordinary counts. Then comes the smallest infinite size, written $\aleph_0$ ("aleph-zero"), the size of the counting numbers. Above it sits a strictly larger infinite size, the number of points on a line.

| collection | size |
|---|---|
| $\{\ \}$ (nothing) | $0$ |
| $\{a, b, c\}$ | $3$ |
| all counting numbers | $\aleph_0$ (smallest infinity) |
| all even numbers | $\aleph_0$ (same as above) |
| all points on a line | bigger than $\aleph_0$ |

The key idea: "same size" means "can be paired off exactly," and once you allow infinite collections, this gives a ladder of infinite sizes, each strictly bigger than the last.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let us show by hand that there are exactly as many counting numbers as there are *pairs* of counting numbers — a result that feels impossible, since pairs look far more plentiful.

We want to pair off each spot in an endless grid with a single counting number, missing none. Picture the pairs $(a, b)$ laid out as a grid: row $a$, column $b$. Instead of reading across a row (which never ends, so we would never reach the next row), we walk along the diagonals.

Step 1. The first diagonal holds just $(0,0)$. Give it the number $0$.

Step 2. The next diagonal holds $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$. Give them $1$ and $2$.

Step 3. The next diagonal holds $(2,0), (1,1), (0,2)$. Give them $3, 4, 5$.

Step 4. The next diagonal holds four pairs. Give them $6, 7, 8, 9$.

Because each diagonal is finite, we finish it and move on, and every pair sits on exactly one diagonal, so every pair eventually gets a number. No pair is skipped and no number is used twice.

Step 5. Count the labels handed out before reaching diagonal number $d$: the diagonals before it hold $1 + 2 + 3 + \dots + d$ pairs. For $d = 4$ that is $1 + 2 + 3 + 4 = 10$ pairs already labelled $0$ through $9$, so the pair $(4, 0)$ at the start of diagonal $4$ gets the number $10$.

What this tells us: the grid of all pairs, which looks two-dimensional and far bigger, is still only $\aleph_0$ in size. Bundling counting numbers into pairs does not escape the smallest infinity. This is the seed of the rule that for infinite sizes, multiplying a size by itself gives back the same size.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

All of the following takes place inside ZFC (cross-ref [42.03.01]), and the ordinals, transfinite recursion, and the well-ordering of $\mathrm{On}$ are as developed in [42.03.02]; the Axiom of Choice is assumed throughout except where its omission is flagged.

Two sets $A, B$ are **equinumerous**, written $A \approx B$, when there is a bijection $A \to B$. Write $A \preceq B$ when there is an injection $A \to B$, and $A \prec B$ when $A \preceq B$ but $A \not\approx B$. The relation $\approx$ is an equivalence relation on sets, and $\preceq$ is a preorder; the Cantor-Schröder-Bernstein theorem (next section) shows $\preceq$ is antisymmetric up to $\approx$.

A **cardinal** is an **initial ordinal**: an ordinal $\kappa$ such that no $\alpha < \kappa$ satisfies $\alpha \approx \kappa$ [ref: TODO_REF Kunen Ch. I §11]. Every natural number is a cardinal, $\omega$ is the least infinite cardinal, and under Choice every set $A$ is equinumerous with a unique cardinal $|A|$, its **cardinality** — for $A$ can be well-ordered (cross-ref [42.03.02]), hence is equinumerous with some ordinal, and $|A|$ is the least such ordinal. The infinite cardinals are enumerated by the **aleph function**, defined by transfinite recursion: $\aleph_0 = \omega$; $\aleph_{\alpha+1} = \aleph_\alpha^+$, the least cardinal strictly greater than $\aleph_\alpha$; and $\aleph_\lambda = \sup_{\beta < \lambda} \aleph_\beta$ at limits. One also writes $\omega_\alpha$ for $\aleph_\alpha$ when emphasizing its role as an ordinal. The alephs are strictly increasing, and every infinite cardinal is some $\aleph_\alpha$; the cardinals, like the ordinals, are well-ordered.

For cardinals $\kappa, \lambda$, **cardinal addition** and **multiplication** are defined by
$$
\kappa \oplus \lambda = |\kappa \sqcup \lambda|, \qquad \kappa \otimes \lambda = |\kappa \times \lambda|,
$$
where $\sqcup$ is disjoint union; these are commutative and associative and agree with ordinary arithmetic on finite cardinals. **Cardinal exponentiation** is $\kappa^\lambda = |{}^{\lambda}\kappa|$, the cardinality of the set of all functions $\lambda \to \kappa$. The **continuum** is $\mathfrak{c} = 2^{\aleph_0} = |\mathcal{P}(\omega)| = |\mathbb{R}|$. The **beth function** is $\beth_0 = \aleph_0$, $\beth_{\alpha+1} = 2^{\beth_\alpha}$, $\beth_\lambda = \sup_{\beta < \lambda}\beth_\beta$.

A set is **finite** if equinumerous with some $n < \omega$, **countable** if $\preceq \omega$, **countably infinite** if $\approx \omega$, and **uncountable** otherwise. A set is **Dedekind-infinite** if it is equinumerous with a proper subset of itself; under Choice this coincides with being infinite.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"Cardinal arithmetic is just ordinal arithmetic."* It is not: cardinal operations record only size, ordinal operations record order type. Thus $\aleph_0 \oplus \aleph_0 = \aleph_0$ while $\omega + \omega \neq \omega$, and $\aleph_0 \otimes \aleph_0 = \aleph_0$ while $\omega \cdot \omega \neq \omega$. The same symbol $\omega = \aleph_0$ names one object but carries two arithmetics.

- *"$2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_1$ is a theorem."* It is the Continuum Hypothesis, independent of ZFC (cross-ref [42.03.06], [42.03.08]). ZFC proves $2^{\aleph_0} > \aleph_0$ (Cantor) and $\operatorname{cf}(2^{\aleph_0}) > \aleph_0$ (König), so $2^{\aleph_0} \neq \aleph_\omega$, but it does not pin the value.

- *"If $A$ is infinite then $A \approx \omega$."* Only countable infinite sets satisfy this. $\mathbb{R}$ is infinite and uncountable; $\omega \prec \mathbb{R}$ strictly, by Cantor's theorem.

- *"$\preceq$ might fail to be antisymmetric, so cardinalities could be incomparable."* Cantor-Schröder-Bernstein gives antisymmetry without Choice; linearity (trichotomy of cardinals) needs the well-ordering theorem (cross-ref [42.03.02], [42.03.05]) and is equivalent to Choice.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The organising theorem of infinite cardinal arithmetic is that addition and multiplication of infinite cardinals collapse entirely: they are governed by the single operation $\max$. Everything interesting about cardinal arithmetic is thereby pushed into exponentiation, which is where independence lives.

**Theorem (Hessenberg; absorption of infinite sums and products).** *For an infinite cardinal $\kappa$, $\kappa \otimes \kappa = \kappa$. Consequently, for infinite cardinals $\kappa, \lambda$ with $\lambda \le \kappa$ and $\kappa$ infinite,*
$$
\kappa \oplus \lambda = \kappa \otimes \lambda = \kappa = \max(\kappa, \lambda)
$$
*(the product requiring $\lambda \neq 0$)* [ref: TODO_REF Kunen Ch. I §12].

*Proof.* The core is $\kappa \otimes \kappa = \kappa$, proved by transfinite induction on the infinite cardinal $\kappa$. Define the **canonical (Gödel) well-ordering** $\lhd$ of $\mathrm{On} \times \mathrm{On}$ by
$$
(\alpha_1, \beta_1) \lhd (\alpha_2, \beta_2) \iff
\begin{cases}
\max(\alpha_1,\beta_1) < \max(\alpha_2,\beta_2), \text{ or}\\
\max(\alpha_1,\beta_1) = \max(\alpha_2,\beta_2) \text{ and } (\alpha_1,\beta_1) <_{\text{lex}} (\alpha_2,\beta_2),
\end{cases}
$$
comparing first by the larger coordinate, then lexicographically. This is a well-ordering: any nonempty class has an element minimising $\max$, among which one minimises $\alpha$, among which one minimises $\beta$.

Let $\Gamma(\alpha,\beta)$ be the order type of the initial segment of $\lhd$ below $(\alpha,\beta)$. The claim is that $\Gamma$ restricts to a bijection $\kappa \times \kappa \to \kappa$ for every infinite cardinal $\kappa$, which gives $\kappa \otimes \kappa = \kappa$.

Induct on $\kappa$. Suppose $\mu \otimes \mu = \mu$ for every infinite cardinal $\mu < \kappa$. Let $(\alpha, \beta) \in \kappa \times \kappa$ and set $\gamma = \max(\alpha, \beta) + 1$, so $\gamma < \kappa$ (as $\kappa$ is a limit ordinal, being an infinite cardinal). Every pair $\lhd (\alpha,\beta)$ has both coordinates $< \gamma$, so the segment below $(\alpha,\beta)$ embeds into $\gamma \times \gamma$, giving $\Gamma(\alpha,\beta) \le |\gamma \times \gamma| = |\gamma| \otimes |\gamma|$. Now $|\gamma| < \kappa$. If $\gamma$ is finite the product is finite $< \kappa$; if $\gamma$ is infinite then by the induction hypothesis $|\gamma| \otimes |\gamma| = |\gamma| < \kappa$. Either way $\Gamma(\alpha,\beta) < \kappa$. Hence $\Gamma$ maps $\kappa \times \kappa$ into $\kappa$; it is injective (order-preserving for a well-order) and its image is an initial segment of $\kappa$, in fact all of $\kappa$ since $\kappa \times \kappa$ surjects onto $\kappa$ via the first coordinate (so the image has size $\ge \kappa$). Thus $\Gamma : \kappa \times \kappa \to \kappa$ is a bijection and $\kappa \otimes \kappa = \kappa$.

The absorption laws follow. For $0 < \lambda \le \kappa$ with $\kappa$ infinite,
$$
\kappa \le \kappa \oplus \lambda \le \kappa \oplus \kappa = 2 \otimes \kappa \le \kappa \otimes \kappa = \kappa,
$$
and $\kappa \le \kappa \otimes \lambda \le \kappa \otimes \kappa = \kappa$, so by Cantor-Schröder-Bernstein all the displayed quantities equal $\kappa = \max(\kappa,\lambda)$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The collapse $\kappa \oplus \lambda = \kappa \otimes \lambda = \max(\kappa,\lambda)$ is the foundational reason cardinal arithmetic looks deceptively simple at the additive and multiplicative level: the two operations carry no information beyond which cardinal is larger, so this is exactly where ordinal arithmetic and cardinal arithmetic part ways — the order-type memory of [42.03.02] is discarded and only size remains. It builds toward exponentiation, the one operation that survives the collapse: $2^\kappa > \kappa$ always holds (Cantor's theorem, proved below), and the value of $2^{\aleph_0}$ is not decided by ZFC, which is where the Continuum Hypothesis and the independence results of [42.03.06] and [42.03.08] enter. The canonical well-ordering $\lhd$ generalises the order-type machinery of [42.03.02] and is dual to the cofinality analysis of [42.03.04], where König's theorem sharpens "exponentiation is large" into "$\operatorname{cf}(2^\kappa) > \kappa$." Putting these together, the central insight is that the entire content of cardinal arithmetic migrates into the exponential, and the alephs constructed here are exactly the cardinals consumed by Morley's categoricity theorem [42.02.06], where "categorical in some uncountable power" means categorical in every $\aleph_\alpha$ with $\alpha \ge 1$. This appears again whenever a transfinite construction must count its stages by a cardinal rather than an ordinal.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the Cantor-Schröder-Bernstein theorem: if $A \preceq B$ and $B \preceq A$ then $A \approx B$. (Do not assume Choice.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Let $f : A \to B$ and $g : B \to A$ be injections. Partition $A$ by the chains generated by $g^{-1}, f^{-1}$ according to where the chain originates, and define a bijection piecewise.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $f : A \to B$, $g : B \to A$ be injections. Call $a \in A$ a *B-origin* point if iterating $g^{-1}, f^{-1}, g^{-1}, \dots$ from $a$ terminates at a point of $B \setminus f[A]$, an *A-origin* point if it terminates in $A \setminus g[B]$, and a *no-origin* point if the iteration never terminates. These three classes partition $A$. Define $h : A \to B$ by $h(a) = f(a)$ on the $A$-origin and no-origin points and $h(a) = g^{-1}(a)$ on the $B$-origin points (where $g^{-1}$ is defined since such $a \in g[B]$). Then $h$ is a bijection: on each class the maps are injective and their images partition $B$ by the symmetric classification of $B$. Concretely, $f$ restricted to the $A$-origin/no-origin part lands exactly on the $A$-origin/no-origin part of $B$, and $g^{-1}$ on the $B$-origin part of $A$ lands on the $B$-origin part of $B$. Rubric: full credit for the chain/origin partition, the piecewise definition, and verifying bijectivity by the matched classification on $B$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove Cantor's theorem: for every set $A$, $A \prec \mathcal{P}(A)$; that is, $|A| < |\mathcal{P}(A)|$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The map $a \mapsto \{a\}$ injects $A$ into $\mathcal{P}(A)$. For non-surjectivity of any $F : A \to \mathcal{P}(A)$, consider the diagonal set $D = \{a : a \notin F(a)\}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The map $a \mapsto \{a\}$ is an injection $A \to \mathcal{P}(A)$, so $A \preceq \mathcal{P}(A)$. Suppose toward contradiction some $F : A \to \mathcal{P}(A)$ were surjective. Let $D = \{a \in A : a \notin F(a)\} \in \mathcal{P}(A)$. By surjectivity $D = F(d)$ for some $d \in A$. Then $d \in D \iff d \notin F(d) = D$, a contradiction. So no surjection exists, $A \not\approx \mathcal{P}(A)$, and with the injection above $A \prec \mathcal{P}(A)$. Rubric: full credit for the singleton injection, the diagonal set $D$, and the contradiction $d \in D \iff d \notin D$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that $|\mathbb{R}| = 2^{\aleph_0}$, i.e. the reals are equinumerous with $\mathcal{P}(\omega)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Inject $\mathcal{P}(\omega) \to \mathbb{R}$ by binary expansions and $\mathbb{R} \to \mathcal{P}(\omega)$ via Dedekind cuts of rationals, then apply Cantor-Schröder-Bernstein.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Inject $\mathcal{P}(\omega) \to \mathbb{R}$ by sending $S \subseteq \omega$ to the number $\sum_{n \in S} 3^{-(n+1)}$ (base-$3$ digits in $\{0,1\}$ avoids the double-counting of binary $0.0111\dots = 0.1000\dots$, so the map is injective). Hence $2^{\aleph_0} \preceq |\mathbb{R}|$. Conversely inject $\mathbb{R} \to \mathcal{P}(\mathbb{Q})$ by $r \mapsto \{q \in \mathbb{Q} : q < r\}$, the Dedekind cut, which is injective since the rationals are dense; and $|\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{Q})| = 2^{|\mathbb{Q}|} = 2^{\aleph_0}$ as $|\mathbb{Q}| = \aleph_0$. So $|\mathbb{R}| \preceq 2^{\aleph_0}$. Cantor-Schröder-Bernstein gives $|\mathbb{R}| = 2^{\aleph_0}$. Rubric: full credit for both injections (with the base-$3$ or equivalent fix for binary ambiguity) and the appeal to Cantor-Schröder-Bernstein.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove König's theorem in the form $\kappa < \kappa^{\operatorname{cf}(\kappa)}$ for every infinite cardinal $\kappa$, and deduce $\operatorname{cf}(2^{\aleph_0}) > \aleph_0$, hence $2^{\aleph_0} \neq \aleph_\omega$. (You may use the general König inequality $\sum_{i} \kappa_i < \prod_i \lambda_i$ when $\kappa_i < \lambda_i$ for all $i$; cofinality is developed in [42.03.04].)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $\kappa = \sum_{i < \operatorname{cf}(\kappa)} \kappa_i$ with each $\kappa_i < \kappa$, and apply the König inequality with $\lambda_i = \kappa$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\mu = \operatorname{cf}(\kappa)$ and write $\kappa = \sup_{i < \mu} \kappa_i$ as an increasing union with each $\kappa_i < \kappa$, so $\kappa = \sum_{i < \mu} \kappa_i$ (the sum is $\ge \kappa$ by the sup, $\le \mu \otimes \kappa = \kappa$ by absorption). Apply the König inequality with $\lambda_i = \kappa$ for all $i$: since $\kappa_i < \kappa = \lambda_i$,
$$
\kappa = \sum_{i<\mu} \kappa_i < \prod_{i<\mu} \kappa = \kappa^\mu = \kappa^{\operatorname{cf}(\kappa)}.
$$
For the corollary, set $\theta = 2^{\aleph_0}$ and suppose $\operatorname{cf}(\theta) = \aleph_0$. Then König gives $\theta < \theta^{\operatorname{cf}(\theta)} = \theta^{\aleph_0} = (2^{\aleph_0})^{\aleph_0} = 2^{\aleph_0 \otimes \aleph_0} = 2^{\aleph_0} = \theta$, a contradiction. So $\operatorname{cf}(2^{\aleph_0}) > \aleph_0$. Since $\operatorname{cf}(\aleph_\omega) = \aleph_0$, the continuum cannot be $\aleph_\omega$. Rubric: full credit for the sum-representation along cofinality, the König-inequality application, and the cofinality contradiction with the exponent law $(2^{\aleph_0})^{\aleph_0} = 2^{\aleph_0}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the exponent laws $\kappa^{\lambda \oplus \mu} = \kappa^\lambda \otimes \kappa^\mu$ and $(\kappa^\lambda)^\mu = \kappa^{\lambda \otimes \mu}$, and use them with Cantor's theorem to show that for $2 \le \kappa \le 2^{\aleph_0}$ one has $\kappa^{\aleph_0} = 2^{\aleph_0}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The first two are bijections of function sets: ${}^{\lambda \sqcup \mu}\kappa \approx {}^\lambda\kappa \times {}^\mu\kappa$ and ${}^\mu({}^\lambda\kappa) \approx {}^{\lambda\times\mu}\kappa$ (currying). For the last, sandwich with monotonicity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A function $\lambda \sqcup \mu \to \kappa$ is the same datum as a pair of functions $\lambda \to \kappa$ and $\mu \to \kappa$ (restrict to each part), giving a bijection ${}^{\lambda\sqcup\mu}\kappa \approx {}^\lambda\kappa \times {}^\mu\kappa$, hence $\kappa^{\lambda\oplus\mu} = \kappa^\lambda \otimes \kappa^\mu$. Currying gives a bijection ${}^\mu({}^\lambda\kappa) \approx {}^{\lambda\times\mu}\kappa$ (a function of $\mu$ returning a function of $\lambda$ is a function of $\lambda \times \mu$), hence $(\kappa^\lambda)^\mu = \kappa^{\lambda\otimes\mu}$. Now for $2 \le \kappa \le 2^{\aleph_0}$, monotonicity of exponentiation gives
$$
2^{\aleph_0} \le \kappa^{\aleph_0} \le (2^{\aleph_0})^{\aleph_0} = 2^{\aleph_0 \otimes \aleph_0} = 2^{\aleph_0},
$$
so $\kappa^{\aleph_0} = 2^{\aleph_0}$ by Cantor-Schröder-Bernstein. Rubric: full credit for the two bijections (disjoint-union and currying), the monotone sandwich, and the use of $\aleph_0 \otimes \aleph_0 = \aleph_0$.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The collapse of $\oplus$ and $\otimes$ leaves exponentiation as the sole carrier of arithmetic content, and the structure of $\kappa \mapsto 2^\kappa$ is the deep question. The alephs and beths frame it; König's theorem and cofinality constrain it; the Continuum Hypothesis names the one value ZFC refuses to decide.

**Theorem 1 (Cantor's theorem and the strict tower).** For every cardinal $\kappa$, $\kappa < 2^\kappa$ [ref: TODO_REF Cantor 1895]. The injection $\kappa \to \mathcal{P}(\kappa)$, $\alpha \mapsto \{\alpha\}$, and the diagonal non-surjectivity of any $F : \kappa \to \mathcal{P}(\kappa)$ via $D = \{\alpha : \alpha \notin F(\alpha)\}$ give the strict inequality. Iterating produces the beth tower $\beth_0 = \aleph_0 < \beth_1 = 2^{\aleph_0} < \beth_2 = 2^{2^{\aleph_0}} < \cdots$, and $\beth_\alpha \ge \aleph_\alpha$ for all $\alpha$ with equality for all $\alpha$ exactly under the Generalized Continuum Hypothesis.

**Theorem 2 (absorption and the arithmetic of alephs).** For infinite cardinals, $\aleph_\alpha \oplus \aleph_\beta = \aleph_\alpha \otimes \aleph_\beta = \aleph_{\max(\alpha,\beta)}$ [ref: TODO_REF Kunen Ch. I §12], a restatement of Hessenberg's theorem. Infinite sums and products also collapse: if $\kappa_i \le \kappa$ for $i < \lambda$ with $\lambda \le \kappa$ infinite and some $\kappa_i$ infinite, then $\sum_{i<\lambda}\kappa_i = \sup_i \kappa_i \cdot \lambda = \max(\sup_i \kappa_i, \lambda)$. The whole additive-multiplicative theory of infinite cardinals is thereby decidable in ZFC; only exponentiation escapes.

**Theorem 3 (König's theorem).** If $\kappa_i < \lambda_i$ for all $i \in I$, then $\sum_{i\in I}\kappa_i < \prod_{i\in I}\lambda_i$ [ref: TODO_REF Jech Ch. 5]. The corollary $\kappa < \kappa^{\operatorname{cf}(\kappa)}$ yields $\operatorname{cf}(2^\kappa) > \kappa$, the one substantive ZFC constraint on the continuum function: $2^{\aleph_0}$ can consistently be $\aleph_1, \aleph_2, \aleph_{17}, \aleph_{\omega+1}, \dots$ but never $\aleph_\omega$ or any cardinal of countable cofinality. The full refinement — regular versus singular cardinals, the Singular Cardinals Hypothesis, and Easton's freedom theorem for regular cardinals — is the subject of [42.03.04].

**Theorem 4 (the Continuum Hypothesis, stated).** CH asserts $2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_1$, equivalently $\beth_1 = \aleph_1$, equivalently every uncountable set of reals is equinumerous with $\mathbb{R}$. GCH asserts $2^{\aleph_\alpha} = \aleph_{\alpha+1}$ for all $\alpha$, equivalently $\beth_\alpha = \aleph_\alpha$ for all $\alpha$. Both are independent of ZFC: Gödel's constructible universe $L$ [42.03.06] models GCH (so they are consistent), and Cohen's forcing [42.03.08] produces models of $\neg$CH (so they are not provable). Under GCH, exponentiation is completely determined: $\kappa^\lambda$ equals $\kappa^+$, $\kappa$, or $\lambda^+$ according to the relation of $\lambda$ to $\kappa$ and $\operatorname{cf}(\kappa)$.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason cardinal arithmetic splits into a decidable part and an undecidable part is the absorption theorem: $\oplus$ and $\otimes$ on infinite cardinals are $\max$, so they retain nothing of the well-ordering data of [42.03.02], and this is exactly what concentrates all arithmetic content into exponentiation. The central insight is that ZFC pins down $2^\kappa$ only through two facts — Cantor's $2^\kappa > \kappa$ and König's $\operatorname{cf}(2^\kappa) > \kappa$ — and these are dual: Cantor's diagonal gives strict increase, König's diagonal-across-a-cofinal-sequence gives the cofinality bar, the same argument run once versus run along a cofinal family. Putting these together, the continuum function $\kappa \mapsto 2^\kappa$ on regular cardinals is free up to monotonicity and the König bound (Easton), and this is the bridge to [42.03.04] (where cofinality and the singular-cardinal refinement live) and to the independence units [42.03.06] and [42.03.08] (where $L$ forces GCH and Cohen forcing breaks CH). The alephs assembled here are dual to the model-theoretic spectrum: Morley's theorem [42.02.06] says a countable theory categorical in one uncountable $\aleph_\alpha$ is categorical in all of them, so the well-ordering of cardinals proved here is precisely what makes "uncountably categorical" a single property rather than a family indexed by $\alpha$. The whole edifice generalises the counting theorem of [42.03.02]: there a well-order has a unique ordinal type; here a set has a unique cardinal, the least ordinal of its size, and the arithmetic of these least-ordinals is what the unit computes.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (every infinite cardinal is a limit ordinal).** *If $\kappa$ is an infinite cardinal then $\kappa$ is a limit ordinal.*

*Proof.* Suppose $\kappa = \alpha + 1$ were a successor. The map $\alpha + 1 \to \alpha$ sending $0 \mapsto $ (some fixed point of $\alpha$, possible as $\alpha$ is infinite) and shifting is a bijection; concretely, since $\alpha$ is infinite there is a bijection $\alpha \approx \alpha + 1$ (Dedekind-infiniteness: pick an injection $\omega \hookrightarrow \alpha$ and use the shift $n \mapsto n+1$ on the copy of $\omega$, identity elsewhere, to absorb one extra point). Then $\kappa = \alpha + 1 \approx \alpha < \kappa$, contradicting that $\kappa$ is initial. Hence $\kappa$ is not a successor, and being infinite it is not $0$, so it is a limit ordinal. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Cantor-Schröder-Bernstein).** *If $A \preceq B$ and $B \preceq A$ then $A \approx B$, provably in ZF (no Choice).*

*Proof.* Let $f : A \to B$, $g : B \to A$ be injections. Set $C_0 = A \setminus g[B]$ and $C_{n+1} = g[f[C_n]]$, and $C = \bigcup_{n<\omega} C_n$. Define $h : A \to B$ by $h(x) = f(x)$ if $x \in C$ and $h(x) = g^{-1}(x)$ if $x \notin C$ (for $x \notin C \supseteq C_0$ we have $x \in g[B]$, so $g^{-1}(x)$ is defined). Injectivity: $f$ and $g^{-1}$ are each injective, and their images are disjoint — if $f(x) = g^{-1}(y)$ with $x \in C$, $y \notin C$, then $y = g(f(x)) \in g[f[C_n]] = C_{n+1} \subseteq C$, contradiction. Surjectivity: given $b \in B$, if $g(b) \in C$ then $g(b) \in C_{n+1}$ for some $n$ (it is not in $C_0 = A\setminus g[B]$), so $g(b) = g(f(x))$ with $x \in C_n$, whence $b = f(x) = h(x)$; if $g(b) \notin C$ then $b = g^{-1}(g(b)) = h(g(b))$. So $h$ is a bijection. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Cantor's theorem).** *For every set $A$, there is no surjection $A \to \mathcal{P}(A)$; hence $A \prec \mathcal{P}(A)$.*

*Proof.* Let $F : A \to \mathcal{P}(A)$ be any function and put $D = \{a \in A : a \notin F(a)\}$, a subset of $A$ by Separation. If $D = F(d)$ for some $d$, then $d \in D \iff d \notin F(d) = D$, impossible. So $D$ is not in the range of $F$ and $F$ is not surjective. The singleton map $a \mapsto \{a\}$ injects $A$ into $\mathcal{P}(A)$, so $A \preceq \mathcal{P}(A)$ and, no surjection existing, $A \not\approx \mathcal{P}(A)$, giving $A \prec \mathcal{P}(A)$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (uncountability of $\mathbb{R}$ by diagonalisation).** *The set $\{0,1\}^\omega$ of infinite binary sequences is uncountable, and $|\mathbb{R}| = 2^{\aleph_0} > \aleph_0$.*

*Proof.* Suppose $s : \omega \to \{0,1\}^\omega$ enumerated all binary sequences. Define $d \in \{0,1\}^\omega$ by $d(n) = 1 - s(n)(n)$, flipping the $n$-th bit of the $n$-th listed sequence. Then $d$ differs from $s(n)$ at coordinate $n$ for every $n$, so $d$ is not in the range of $s$, contradicting that $s$ enumerates all sequences. Hence $\{0,1\}^\omega$ is uncountable; since $|\{0,1\}^\omega| = 2^{\aleph_0}$ and (Exercise 5) $|\mathbb{R}| = 2^{\aleph_0}$, the reals are uncountable. This is the special case of Proposition 3 at $A = \omega$, the diagonal set $D$ being read as the flipped sequence $d$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (Hessenberg, $\kappa \otimes \kappa = \kappa$).** *For every infinite cardinal $\kappa$, the canonical well-ordering $\lhd$ of $\kappa \times \kappa$ has order type $\kappa$.*

*Proof.* This is the core of the Key theorem; restated as a standalone proposition for the proof set. By transfinite induction on infinite cardinals $\kappa$, assuming $\mu \otimes \mu = \mu$ for infinite $\mu < \kappa$. For $(\alpha,\beta) \in \kappa\times\kappa$ let $\gamma = \max(\alpha,\beta)+1 < \kappa$ (Proposition 1 gives $\kappa$ a limit). The $\lhd$-segment below $(\alpha,\beta)$ injects into $\gamma\times\gamma$, so its order type is $\le |\gamma|\otimes|\gamma|$, which is $<\kappa$ (finite if $\gamma$ finite; equal to $|\gamma| < \kappa$ by the induction hypothesis if $\gamma$ infinite). Thus the order type $\Gamma$ maps $\kappa\times\kappa$ into $\kappa$; it is an order isomorphism onto an initial segment, and since the first-coordinate projection shows $|\kappa\times\kappa| \ge \kappa$, the image is all of $\kappa$. Hence $\kappa\otimes\kappa = |\kappa\times\kappa| = \kappa$. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (König's inequality).** *If $\kappa_i < \lambda_i$ for all $i \in I$ then $\sum_{i\in I}\kappa_i < \prod_{i\in I}\lambda_i$.*

*Proof.* That $\le$ holds is routine. For strictness, suppose $G : \bigsqcup_i \kappa_i \to \prod_i \lambda_i$ is given; it suffices to show $G$ is not surjective. For each $i$, the $i$-th coordinates $\{G(x)(i) : x \in \kappa_i\text{-block}\}$ form a subset of $\lambda_i$ of size $\le |\kappa_i| = \kappa_i < \lambda_i$, so there is $f(i) \in \lambda_i$ omitted by every $G(x)$ with $x$ in the $i$-th block. (Choosing such $f(i)$ uses the Axiom of Choice across $i \in I$.) The function $f = (f(i))_i \in \prod_i \lambda_i$ then differs from every $G(x)$: if $x$ lies in the $i$-th block, $G(x)(i) \neq f(i)$ by construction. So $f \notin \operatorname{ran}(G)$ and $G$ is not surjective. This is the diagonal argument of Proposition 3 run simultaneously in every coordinate. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Cofinality and the Singular Cardinals Hypothesis [42.03.04] are the direct continuation of this unit's exponentiation theory: König's theorem proved here gives the bound $\operatorname{cf}(2^\kappa) > \kappa$, and [42.03.04] develops the machinery (regular versus singular cardinals, $\operatorname{cf}(\kappa)$, Easton's theorem freeing the continuum function on regular cardinals, the much harder Singular Cardinals problem) that says exactly how free the map $\kappa \mapsto 2^\kappa$ is. This unit owns the absorption laws and the statement of König's theorem; [42.03.04] owns the cofinality refinement and the singular-cardinal arithmetic that the absorption laws cannot reach.

- The constructible universe $L$ [42.03.06] is where the Generalized Continuum Hypothesis stated here becomes a theorem: Gödel's inner model satisfies $2^{\aleph_\alpha} = \aleph_{\alpha+1}$ for all $\alpha$ (equivalently $\beth_\alpha = \aleph_\alpha$), via a condensation argument that bounds the number of subsets of $\aleph_\alpha$ appearing in $L$ by $\aleph_{\alpha+1}$. The alephs and the beth function defined in this unit are exactly the objects whose coincidence GCH asserts, and the independence of CH from ZFC (forcing, [42.03.08]) is the complementary half — $L$ shows CH is consistent, Cohen's models show $\neg$CH is consistent.

- Morley's categoricity theorem [42.02.06] consumes the well-ordering of the uncountable cardinals established here: its statement is that a countable first-order theory categorical in some uncountable cardinal $\aleph_\alpha$ ($\alpha \ge 1$) is categorical in every uncountable cardinal. That this is a dichotomy — countable versus all-uncountable — rather than a per-cardinal phenomenon depends on the alephs being well-ordered with $\aleph_1$ the least uncountable one, so the transfinite induction up the cardinal ladder that drives the proof (counting types over models of each successive size) has the order type supplied by this unit.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The cardinal numbers originate with Georg Cantor, who isolated *Mächtigkeit* (power, cardinality) as the invariant of a set under bijection and proved in an 1874 *Crelle* paper that the algebraic numbers are countable while the reals are not — the first theorem distinguishing two infinite sizes. The diagonal argument in its clean form appeared in his 1891 note in the *Jahresbericht der Deutschen Mathematiker-Vereinigung*, yielding both the uncountability of the reals and the general theorem $|A| < |\mathcal{P}(A)|$. Cantor's 1895 *Beiträge zur Begründung der transfiniten Mengenlehre I* [ref: TODO_REF Cantor 1895] fixed the aleph notation, defined cardinal addition, multiplication, and exponentiation through disjoint unions, products, and function sets, and conjectured the Continuum Hypothesis $2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_1$; he was unable to prove the comparability of cardinals, which Zermelo's 1904 well-ordering theorem later supplied from the Axiom of Choice.

The absorption theorem $\kappa \otimes \kappa = \kappa$ is due to Gerhard Hessenberg in his 1906 *Grundbegriffe der Mengenlehre*, with the canonical well-ordering of $\mathrm{On}\times\mathrm{On}$ later codified by Gödel in his 1940 monograph on $L$. Julius König's 1905 inequality $\sum \kappa_i < \prod \lambda_i$ corrected an earlier flawed attempt by König to refute the Continuum Hypothesis and remains the only substantive ZFC constraint on $2^\kappa$. Hilbert placed the Continuum Hypothesis first among his 1900 problems; Gödel proved in 1938-1940 that CH and GCH hold in the constructible universe, hence are consistent with ZFC [ref: TODO_REF Kunen Ch. I §13], and Paul Cohen proved in 1963 that $\neg$CH is also consistent, inventing forcing for the purpose. The standard modern developments are Kunen's [ref: TODO_REF Kunen Ch. I §10-13] and Jech's [ref: TODO_REF Jech Ch. 3], from which the present treatment of cardinality, the absorption laws, and exponentiation is drawn.
