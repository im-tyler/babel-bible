## Intuition [Beginner]

The previous units showed that the size of the real line is not decided by the usual rules: you can grow the universe by adding new reals and make the continuum hypothesis fail, or trim it and make it hold. That freedom is unsettling. If the rules leave the most basic question about infinity open, which extra rules should we adopt? This unit is about the two great answers set theory has developed, and how they organize an otherwise chaotic landscape.

The first answer is a new axiom that says, in effect, "stop forcing — the universe already contains every small generic object you could build." If a gentle construction of a certain controlled kind could produce some object meeting a modest list of demands, then that object is already here. This single principle, Martin's axiom, decides a long list of questions that the bare rules leave open, and it works by imagining many forcing steps stacked one after another rather than just one.

The second answer reaches upward instead of sideways. It posits infinities so vast that their mere existence cannot be proved from below, each one stronger than the last. These large infinities form a ruler. Any new assumption you might add can be measured against the ruler by asking which large infinity it is as risky as. The remarkable empirical fact is that almost everything lines up neatly along this single scale, turning a wilderness of independent statements into an ordered tower.

The key idea: forcing revealed the rules do not pin down the universe, and the response is a pair of programs — Martin's axiom organizing the real line sideways, large infinities organizing strength upward — that together hunt for the right extra rules.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let us see Martin's axiom do one concrete job: rule out a strange object called a gap. Picture two growing towers of sets of whole numbers. Tower $A$ climbs upward, each set containing the previous one; tower $B$ descends, each set inside the previous one; and every $A$-set sits inside every $B$-set with room to spare. The question: is there a single set $C$ slotted neatly between the two towers, above all of $A$ and below all of $B$? Sometimes the towers are short and a $C$ is plain; the worry is towers as long as the next size of infinity, where a $C$ might fail to exist — a gap.

Step 1. Set up a gentle search for $C$. A promise is a finite snapshot: finitely many numbers declared "in $C$" and finitely many declared "out", consistent with sitting between the towers so far.

Step 2. For each level of tower $A$, the demand "$C$ contains this whole $A$-level" can always be met by extending a finite promise; likewise "$C$ avoids enough of this $B$-level". Each demand is a reachable region.

Step 3. The number of demands is one per tower level — fewer than the continuum if the towers are shorter than the continuum.

Step 4. The gentle search is of the controlled kind Martin's axiom governs, and there are fewer demands than the continuum, so the axiom hands us a finished filter meeting them all. Reading off that filter gives the set $C$.

What this tells us: under Martin's axiom plus the failure of the continuum hypothesis, no gap of length below the continuum exists — a question the plain rules leave open is settled by the single principle "small gentle searches already succeed".

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Work in ZFC with the forcing apparatus of [42.03.07] and the ccc machinery of [42.03.08]: posets $(P, \le, 1_P)$, compatibility, antichains, dense sets, $M$-generic filters, $P$-names, the generic extension $M[G]$, and the countable chain condition (every antichain is countable).

For a cardinal $\kappa$, **Martin's axiom at $\kappa$**, written $\mathrm{MA}_\kappa$, is the statement: for every ccc poset $P$ and every family $\mathcal{D}$ of at most $\kappa$ dense subsets of $P$, there is a filter $G \subseteq P$ such that $G \cap D \ne \varnothing$ for all $D \in \mathcal{D}$. Such a $G$ is called **$\mathcal{D}$-generic**. **Martin's axiom** is $\mathrm{MA} = \mathrm{MA}_{<2^{\aleph_0}}$, that is, $\mathrm{MA}_\kappa$ holds for every $\kappa < 2^{\aleph_0}$. By the Rasiowa-Sikorski lemma $\mathrm{MA}_{\aleph_0}$ is a theorem of ZFC, and $\mathrm{MA}_{2^{\aleph_0}}$ is false (it fails already for $P = \operatorname{Add}(\omega,1)$), so $\mathrm{MA}$ is informative precisely when $2^{\aleph_0} > \aleph_1$; under CH, $\mathrm{MA}$ holds vacuously.

A **two-step iteration** $P * \dot Q$ has as conditions pairs $(p, \dot q)$ with $p \in P$ and $\dot q$ a $P$-name forced by $p$ to be a condition of $\dot Q$, ordered by $(p', \dot q') \le (p, \dot q)$ iff $p' \le p$ and $p' \Vdash \dot q' \le \dot q$. A **forcing iteration of length $\alpha$ with support type $s$** is a sequence $(P_\xi : \xi \le \alpha)$ with $P_{\xi+1} = P_\xi * \dot Q_\xi$ and, at limits $\eta$, $P_\eta$ the inverse limit restricted to conditions whose support lies in the chosen ideal — **finite support** ($|\operatorname{supp}(p)| < \aleph_0$) or **countable support** ($|\operatorname{supp}(p)| \le \aleph_0$).

A cardinal $\kappa$ is **inaccessible** if it is uncountable, regular, and strong-limit ($\lambda < \kappa \Rightarrow 2^\lambda < \kappa$); **measurable** if it carries a $\kappa$-complete non-principal ultrafilter $U$ on $\kappa$. The **critical point** of a non-identity elementary embedding $j : V \to M$ into a transitive class $M$ is the least ordinal moved.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The organising fact is that finite-support iteration keeps the chain condition, which is what lets a single iteration force Martin's axiom. The chain condition survives because supports are finite and antichains are controlled by the $\Delta$-system lemma of [42.03.08], exactly as for the one-step Cohen poset.

**Theorem (finite-support ccc iteration is ccc).** *Let $(P_\xi : \xi \le \alpha)$ be a finite-support iteration such that for every $\xi < \alpha$ one has $\Vdash_{P_\xi} \dot Q_\xi$ is ccc. Then $P_\alpha$ is ccc.* [ref: TODO_REF Kunen Ch. VIII]

*Proof.* By induction on $\alpha$. Successor steps reduce to the two-step case: if $P_\xi$ is ccc and $\Vdash_{P_\xi} \dot Q_\xi$ is ccc, then $P_\xi * \dot Q_\xi$ is ccc, because an antichain of $P_\xi * \dot Q_\xi$ of size $\aleph_1$ projects (after refining the second coordinates with a name for a maximal antichain of $\dot Q_\xi$, countable by the assumed ccc) to an uncountable antichain of $P_\xi$ or to an uncountable antichain of $\dot Q_\xi$ in some extension, each excluded. For the limit $\alpha$, suppose $A = \{(p_\beta) : \beta < \omega_1\}$ is an antichain of $P_\alpha$. Each $p_\beta$ has finite support $s_\beta \subseteq \alpha$. Apply the $\Delta$-system lemma to $\{s_\beta : \beta < \omega_1\}$: an uncountable $W$ with $\{s_\beta : \beta \in W\}$ a $\Delta$-system of root $r$, where $r \subseteq \alpha$ is finite. The root $r$ is contained in some $P_\gamma$ with $\gamma < \alpha$ (as $\operatorname{cf}(\alpha)$ may be uncountable, finiteness of $r$ places it below $\alpha$). Restricting the conditions to $r$ gives $\{p_\beta \restriction r : \beta \in W\}$, $\aleph_1$-many conditions of the ccc poset $P_\gamma$ (induction hypothesis), so two of them, $p_\beta \restriction r$ and $p_{\beta'} \restriction r$, are compatible in $P_\gamma$ via some $q$. Off the root the supports $s_\beta, s_{\beta'}$ are disjoint, so $q$ together with the disjoint parts amalgamates to a common extension of $p_\beta$ and $p_{\beta'}$ in $P_\alpha$. That contradicts antichain-hood; hence $|A| \le \aleph_0$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The finite-support theorem is the foundational reason a single forcing can install Martin's axiom: the chain condition that [42.03.08] secured for one Cohen step now survives stacking $\aleph_2$ steps, so a bookkeeping iteration can meet every ccc forcing and every dense family without ever collapsing $\aleph_1$. This is exactly the mechanism that builds toward the Solovay-Tennenbaum theorem in the Advanced results — once $P_{\aleph_2}$ is ccc and anticipates all small ccc data, $M[G_{\aleph_2}] \models \mathrm{MA} + 2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_2$. The construction generalises the single Cohen poset of [42.03.08] from one step to a transfinite stack: $\operatorname{Add}(\omega, \aleph_2)$ is the special case where every $\dot Q_\xi$ is Cohen forcing and nothing is anticipated. The central insight is that genericity is *iterable* — a long enough controlled iteration makes the universe generic for an entire class of forcings at once, which is precisely what Martin's axiom asserts about the ground model after the fact. This appears again in the countable-support iteration of proper forcings that yields PFA, where the preservation theorem upgrades from ccc to properness and the supports from finite to countable, and the same amalgamation-over-a-root argument is replaced by the elementary-submodel genericity of Shelah's properness. Putting these together, the modern theory of the continuum is the theory of which forcings can be iterated while preserving $\aleph_1$, and Martin's axiom is the first and gentlest such iteration program.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the Rasiowa-Sikorski lemma, i.e. $\mathrm{MA}_{\aleph_0}$: for any poset $P$ and countably many dense sets $D_0, D_1, \dots$, there is a filter meeting every $D_n$. (No ccc needed.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Build a descending sequence of conditions, one entering each $D_n$ in turn.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Start with any $p_0 \in P$. Given $p_n$, since $D_n$ is dense there is $p_{n+1} \le p_n$ with $p_{n+1} \in D_n$. The sequence $p_0 \ge p_1 \ge \cdots$ is a descending chain; let $G = \{q \in P : \exists n\ (p_n \le q)\}$, the upward closure of the chain. Then $G$ is a filter: it is upward closed by construction, and any two $p_m, p_n$ have the common lower bound $p_{\max(m,n)} \in G$, so any two elements of $G$ are compatible within $G$. And $p_{n+1} \in G \cap D_n$ for each $n$, so $G$ meets every $D_n$. Rubric: full credit for the descending construction, the verification that the upward closure is a filter, and the meeting of each $D_n$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that $\mathrm{MA}_{\aleph_1}$ implies the product of two ccc posets is ccc. (Hence under $\mathrm{MA}+\neg\mathrm{CH}$, ccc is productive.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If $P \times Q$ had an uncountable antichain, use $\mathrm{MA}_{\aleph_1}$ on $P$ to find a filter meeting $\aleph_1$ dense sets coding the antichain's first coordinates, and derive an uncountable antichain in $Q$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $P, Q$ are ccc but $P \times Q$ has an antichain $\{(p_\xi, q_\xi) : \xi < \omega_1\}$. The sets $E_\xi = \{p \in P : p \le p_\xi$ or $p \perp p_\xi\}$ are dense in $P$. By $\mathrm{MA}_{\aleph_1}$ applied to the ccc poset $P$ and the $\aleph_1$ dense sets $E_\xi$, there is a filter $G$ meeting each $E_\xi$; let $S = \{\xi : G \ni$ some $p \le p_\xi\}$, so $G$ "selects" the $p_\xi$ with $\xi \in S$ and these are pairwise compatible (members of a filter), hence $\{q_\xi : \xi \in S\}$ must be pairwise incompatible in $Q$ (else some $(p_\xi,q_\xi),(p_\eta,q_\eta)$ would be compatible in the product). If $S$ were uncountable this is an uncountable antichain in the ccc $Q$, impossible; if $S$ is countable, the complementary $\aleph_1$-many $\xi \notin S$ have $G$ meeting $E_\xi$ through a $p \perp p_\xi$, and a symmetric count via a generic filter on $Q$ forces an uncountable antichain in $P$. Either way a ccc factor has an uncountable antichain, a contradiction; so $P \times Q$ is ccc. Rubric: credit for the dense sets $E_\xi$, the $\mathrm{MA}_{\aleph_1}$ filter, and the transfer of the uncountable antichain into one ccc factor.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $j : V \to M$ be a nontrivial elementary embedding into a transitive class $M$ with critical point $\kappa$. Show $\kappa$ is inaccessible in $V$: regular and strong-limit.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use elementarity and $V_\kappa \subseteq M$ with $V_\kappa = (V_\kappa)^M$; transfer regularity and the strong-limit property between $V$ and $M$ via $j$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $\kappa = \operatorname{crit}(j)$, $j$ fixes every $\alpha < \kappa$ and $j(\kappa) > \kappa$. Regular: if $\kappa = \sup_{i<\lambda} \beta_i$ with $\lambda < \kappa$ and each $\beta_i < \kappa$, then $j(\beta_i) = \beta_i$ and $j(\lambda) = \lambda$, so $j(\kappa) = j(\sup \beta_i) = \sup_{i<\lambda} j(\beta_i) = \sup_{i<\lambda}\beta_i = \kappa$, contradicting $j(\kappa) > \kappa$; hence $\kappa$ has no short cofinal sequence and is regular. Strong-limit: for $\lambda < \kappa$, $j(2^\lambda) = 2^{j(\lambda)} = 2^\lambda$ by elementarity and $j \restriction \lambda = \mathrm{id}$, and since $j$ fixes ordinals below $\kappa$ but moves $\kappa$, the cardinal $2^\lambda$ cannot equal or exceed $\kappa$ (else it would be moved or pin $\kappa$ below the critical point); so $2^\lambda < \kappa$. Thus $\kappa$ is regular and strong-limit, i.e. inaccessible (it is uncountable since the embedding is nontrivial). Rubric: credit for using $\operatorname{crit}(j)=\kappa$ to fix small ordinals, the cofinality argument for regularity, and the elementarity argument for strong-limit.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Sketch why a measurable cardinal $\kappa$ gives a nontrivial elementary embedding $j : V \to M$ with $\operatorname{crit}(j) = \kappa$, via the ultrapower by a $\kappa$-complete non-principal ultrafilter $U$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Form $\mathrm{Ult}(V,U)$ with Łoś's theorem; $\kappa$-completeness gives well-foundedness; take the transitive collapse and let $j$ be the canonical embedding $[c_x] \mapsto$ the constant function.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Form the ultrapower $\mathrm{Ult}(V,U)$ of functions $f : \kappa \to V$ modulo $=_U$ (agreement on a set in $U$), with membership $[f] \in_U [g]$ iff $\{\alpha : f(\alpha) \in g(\alpha)\} \in U$. Łoś's theorem gives $\mathrm{Ult}(V,U) \models \varphi([f_1],\dots)$ iff $\{\alpha : \varphi(f_1(\alpha),\dots)\} \in U$, so the diagonal map $j(x) = [c_x]$ (constant function $c_x$) is elementary: $\varphi(x)$ holds in $V$ iff $\{\alpha : \varphi(x)\} = \kappa \in U$ iff $\mathrm{Ult} \models \varphi(j(x))$. $\kappa$-completeness of $U$ (closure under intersections of $<\kappa$ members) makes $\in_U$ well-founded — any descending $\in_U$-sequence would, by $\kappa$-completeness, induce a genuinely descending $\in$-sequence on a set in $U$, impossible — so $\mathrm{Ult}$ is isomorphic to a transitive class $M$; let $j : V \to M$ be the composite. The critical point is $\kappa$: each $\alpha < \kappa$ is fixed because $U$ is $\kappa$-complete and non-principal (the function $\mathrm{id}$ witnesses $j(\alpha) = \alpha$ for $\alpha < \kappa$ but $[\mathrm{id}] $ is a new ordinal with $\kappa \le [\mathrm{id}] < j(\kappa)$, so $j(\kappa) > \kappa$). Hence $j$ is nontrivial with $\operatorname{crit}(j) = \kappa$. Rubric: credit for Łoś giving elementarity, $\kappa$-completeness giving well-foundedness and transitive collapse, and the identity function witnessing $\operatorname{crit}(j) = \kappa$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Explain why no large-cardinal axiom of the standard hierarchy decides CH, by sketching the Levy-Solovay theorem: small forcing preserves measurability.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If $|P| < \kappa$ and $\kappa$ is measurable via $U$, lift $U$ to a $\kappa$-complete ultrafilter in $V[G]$; then force CH or $\neg$CH by Cohen-type forcing of size $<\kappa$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\kappa$ be measurable via a $\kappa$-complete non-principal ultrafilter $U$, and let $P$ be a forcing with $|P| < \kappa$ ("small"). In $V[G]$ define $\bar U = \{X \subseteq \kappa : \exists Y \in U,\ Y \subseteq X\}$, the upward closure of $U$. Because $|P| < \kappa$ and $\kappa$ is regular, every new subset of $\kappa$ in $V[G]$ is decided by an antichain of size $<\kappa$, so $\kappa$-completeness of $U$ transfers: $\bar U$ is a $\kappa$-complete non-principal ultrafilter on $\kappa$ in $V[G]$, witnessing that $\kappa$ stays measurable. Now take $P$ to be a Cohen forcing $\operatorname{Add}(\omega, \aleph_2)$ of size $\aleph_2 < \kappa$ (forcing $\neg$CH) or a countably-closed collapse of size $<\kappa$ (forcing CH); both are small relative to $\kappa$, so $\kappa$ remains measurable in $V[G]$ while CH takes either truth value. Hence measurability is compatible with CH and with $\neg$CH, so a measurable cardinal cannot decide CH; the same small-forcing argument applies to every large cardinal whose defining embedding/ultrafilter is $\kappa$-complete, i.e. the entire standard hierarchy. Rubric: credit for the lifted ultrafilter $\bar U$, the small-forcing transfer of $\kappa$-completeness, and the conclusion that CH is forceable either way above the large cardinal.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The independence of CH from [42.03.08] left set theory with a question of method: which extra axioms should organize the universe? Two answers, forcing axioms and large cardinals, structure everything below, and they meet in the theory of consistency strength and determinacy.

**Theorem 1 (Solovay-Tennenbaum; $\operatorname{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC}) \Rightarrow \operatorname{Con}(\mathrm{ZFC} + \mathrm{MA} + \neg\mathrm{CH})$).** Over $M \models \mathrm{ZFC} + \mathrm{GCH}$, perform a finite-support iteration $(P_\xi : \xi \le \aleph_2)$ of length $\aleph_2$ in which a bookkeeping function $h : \aleph_2 \to \aleph_2 \times \aleph_2$ guarantees that every ccc forcing $\dot Q$ of hereditary size $<\aleph_2$ appearing in any intermediate model, together with every family of $<\aleph_2$ dense sets, is enumerated and forced at some stage $\dot Q_\xi$ [ref: TODO_REF Kunen Ch. VIII]. Each $\dot Q_\xi$ is ccc, so by the Key theorem $P_{\aleph_2}$ is ccc; cardinals are preserved (the ccc preservation of [42.03.08]). A nice-name count gives $2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_2$ in $M[G]$, and the bookkeeping ensures every ccc poset of size $<2^{\aleph_0}$ with $<2^{\aleph_0}$ dense sets was met along the way, with the relevant filter surviving into the final model. Hence $M[G] \models \mathrm{MA} + 2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_2$, so $\mathrm{MA} + \neg\mathrm{CH}$ is consistent relative to ZFC.

**Theorem 2 (consequences of MA + ¬CH).** Under $\mathrm{MA} + \neg\mathrm{CH}$: the union of fewer than $2^{\aleph_0}$ Lebesgue-null sets is null and of fewer than $2^{\aleph_0}$ meagre sets is meagre, so the additivity of measure and of category both equal $2^{\aleph_0}$ [ref: TODO_REF Kunen Ch. III]; every ccc poset has Knaster's property K (every uncountable subset has an uncountable centered subset), whence ccc is productive; there are no Suslin lines, so the Suslin hypothesis holds; and $\mathfrak{p} = 2^{\aleph_0}$, collapsing the small cardinal invariants of the continuum to the continuum and trivialising the lower part of the Cichoń diagram of [42.03.08]. The single principle "the universe is already generic for small ccc forcings" thus settles a long list of questions the bare axioms leave open, each by exhibiting a ccc forcing whose generic produces the desired object and invoking $\mathrm{MA}$ to place that object in the ground universe.

**Theorem 3 (proper forcing and PFA).** Shelah's class of **proper** forcings — those preserving stationary subsets of $[\lambda]^{\aleph_0}$, equivalently those for which club-many countable $N \prec H_\theta$ admit $(N,P)$-generic conditions — is closed under countable-support iteration and preserves $\aleph_1$ [ref: TODO_REF Jech Ch. 16-17]. The **Proper Forcing Axiom** PFA is $\mathrm{MA}$-style genericity for all proper posets and $\aleph_1$-many dense sets; it implies $2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_2$, the Suslin hypothesis, that all $\aleph_1$-Aronszajn trees are special, and Baumgartner's theorem that all $\aleph_1$-dense sets of reals are order-isomorphic. PFA's consistency is obtained from a supercompact cardinal, the first appearance of the upper hierarchy controlling a statement about the reals; it sharpens $\mathrm{MA}$ by replacing ccc (finite-support) with proper (countable-support), at the cost of large-cardinal strength.

**Theorem 4 (the large-cardinal hierarchy and consistency strength).** The cardinals inaccessible $<$ Mahlo $<$ weakly compact $<$ Ramsey $<$ measurable $<$ Woodin $<$ supercompact form a strictly increasing scale of consistency strength: each proves $\operatorname{Con}$ of the theory asserting any smaller one exists [ref: TODO_REF Kanamori Ch. 1-6]. A measurable cardinal is characterised by a nontrivial elementary embedding $j : V \to M = \mathrm{Ult}(V,U)$ with $\operatorname{crit}(j) = \kappa$ (Exercise 6), and by Scott's theorem a measurable refutes $V = L$; stronger cardinals demand $M$ to be closed under longer sequences ($\kappa$ supercompact iff for all $\lambda$ there is $j$ with $\operatorname{crit} \kappa$, $j(\kappa) > \lambda$, $M^\lambda \subseteq M$). The central empirical fact: every known natural extension of ZFC is equiconsistent with some level of this hierarchy, so large cardinals serve as the canonical yardstick of consistency strength, and the apparent linearity of that yardstick is one of the deepest unexplained regularities in foundations.

**Theorem 5 (large cardinals and determinacy).** The Axiom of Determinacy AD contradicts the axiom of choice [42.03.05] but holds in $L(\mathbb{R})$ under sufficient large cardinals [ref: TODO_REF Kanamori Ch. 32]. The Martin-Steel theorem: $n$ Woodin cardinals with a measurable above yield $\Pi^1_{n+1}$-determinacy, so infinitely many Woodin cardinals give full projective determinacy PD, whence every projective set of reals is Lebesgue measurable, has the Baire property, and has the perfect-set property — the regularity properties that $V = L$ falsifies and that ZFC alone cannot settle. Solovay's model, built from a single inaccessible, makes *every* set of reals Lebesgue measurable (dropping choice). These results realise the program of large cardinals as new axioms: by reaching upward in consistency strength one settles downward questions about the definable reals that forcing showed ZFC cannot decide.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason set theory did not end with the independence of CH is that forcing is not only a destabiliser but a constructor: the same finite-support ccc iteration that [42.03.08] used for one Cohen step, iterated $\aleph_2$ times under bookkeeping, builds a universe satisfying Martin's axiom, and the central insight is that genericity is iterable — a long enough chain-condition-preserving iteration makes the ground universe generic for an entire class of forcings, which is exactly what a forcing axiom asserts after the fact. This is exactly the seam between the two great programs: $\mathrm{MA}$, PFA, and the proper-forcing axioms organize the universe sideways at the level of the continuum, fixing $2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_2$ and the cardinal invariants, while the large-cardinal hierarchy organizes it upward by consistency strength, and putting these together the two meet precisely at PFA, whose consistency needs a supercompact, and at determinacy, where Woodin cardinals settle the regularity of the projective reals.

The large-cardinal route is dual to the forcing route of [42.03.08]: forcing changes the model to alter a statement's truth, an elementary embedding $j : V \to M$ holds the universe fixed and reflects truth between $V$ and an inner model, so the constructible universe of [42.03.06] caps the continuum from below while a measurable refutes $V = L$ from above, and the bridge is consistency strength — Levy-Solovay shows small forcing is absorbed, so large cardinals cannot decide CH, and the modern search (Woodin's $\Omega$-logic, the $\mathbb{P}_{\max}$ axiom, the multiverse) is the attempt to find the right axioms that the cofinality and cardinal-arithmetic constraints of [42.03.04] leave open. This generalises the single forcing step of [42.03.08] into set theory's permanent program: to organize the universe by the two perpendicular rulers of forcing-iterability and consistency strength, and to ask which extra axioms those rulers recommend.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (two-step ccc iteration is ccc).** *If $P$ is ccc and $\Vdash_P \dot Q$ is ccc, then $P * \dot Q$ is ccc.*

*Proof.* Suppose $\{(p_\xi, \dot q_\xi) : \xi < \omega_1\}$ is an antichain of $P * \dot Q$. For each pair $\xi \ne \eta$, $(p_\xi,\dot q_\xi) \perp (p_\eta, \dot q_\eta)$ means no $r \le p_\xi, p_\eta$ forces $\dot q_\xi, \dot q_\eta$ compatible. Work in $V^P$: let $G$ be $P$-generic and set $S = \{\xi : p_\xi \in G\}$. The names $\dot q_\xi$ for $\xi \in S$ interpret to conditions $q_\xi^G$ of $\dot Q[G]$, pairwise incompatible (if $q_\xi^G, q_\eta^G$ had a common extension, a condition in $G$ below $p_\xi, p_\eta$ would force compatibility). Since $\dot Q$ is ccc in $V[G]$, $\{q_\xi^G : \xi \in S\}$ is countable, so $S$ is countable in $V[G]$ for every generic $G$. Thus $\Vdash_P |\{\xi : p_\xi \in \dot G\}| \le \aleph_0$. But $P$ is ccc, so by the maximal-antichain/countable-cover bound of [42.03.08] the set $\{\xi : p_\xi \in \dot G\}$ ranges, across generics, over a ground-model-countable union; were $\{p_\xi : \xi<\omega_1\}$ to have $\aleph_1$-many distinct conditions each in some generic, an uncountable antichain of $P$ would result, contradicting ccc unless uncountably many $p_\xi$ coincide — and equal $p_\xi$ force their $\dot q_\xi$ pairwise incompatible, an uncountable antichain of the ccc $\dot Q$. Either way a contradiction; so $|{\{(p_\xi,\dot q_\xi)\}}| \le \aleph_0$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 ($\mathrm{MA}_{\aleph_0}$ is a theorem of ZFC).** *For any poset $P$ and countably many dense $D_n \subseteq P$, a filter meets every $D_n$.*

*Proof.* Build $p_0 \ge p_1 \ge \cdots$ with $p_{n+1} \in D_n$ by density (Exercise 3). The upward closure $G$ of the chain is a filter and $p_{n+1} \in G \cap D_n$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 ($\mathrm{MA}_{\aleph_1}$ implies the Suslin hypothesis).** *Under $\mathrm{MA}_{\aleph_1}$ there is no Suslin line; equivalently every ccc dense complete linear order without endpoints is separable.*

*Proof.* A Suslin line yields a Suslin tree $T$: a tree of height $\omega_1$ with no uncountable chain or antichain. View $T$ as a forcing poset (ordered by reverse tree-order, so extensions are stronger). $T$ is ccc precisely because it has no uncountable antichain. For each $\alpha < \omega_1$ the set $D_\alpha = \{t \in T : \operatorname{ht}(t) > \alpha\}$ is dense (every node has extensions at arbitrarily high levels, as $T$ has height $\omega_1$ and no maximal nodes once pruned). By $\mathrm{MA}_{\aleph_1}$ applied to the ccc poset $T$ and the $\aleph_1$ dense sets $\{D_\alpha\}$, there is a filter $G$ meeting each $D_\alpha$. But a filter in a tree is a chain (any two elements are compatible, hence tree-comparable), and meeting every $D_\alpha$ makes it cofinal in the $\omega_1$ levels, so $G$ is an uncountable chain in $T$ — contradicting Suslinity. Hence no Suslin tree, and no Suslin line, exists. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Scott: a measurable refutes $V = L$).** *If there is a measurable cardinal, then $V \ne L$.*

*Proof.* Let $\kappa$ be measurable via $U$ and $j : V \to M = \mathrm{Ult}(V,U)$ with $\operatorname{crit}(j) = \kappa$ (Exercise 6). Suppose $V = L$. Then $M \subseteq L = V$, and since $M$ is a transitive class model of ZFC containing all ordinals, $M = L = V$ as well (the constructible universe is the minimal such; $L^M = L$ by absoluteness of the $L$-hierarchy [42.03.06]). Elementarity gives $M \models V = L$, so $j : L \to L$ is a nontrivial elementary embedding of $L$ into itself with critical point $\kappa$. By Kunen's argument (or directly: the least ordinal moved cannot exist for an embedding of $L$ definable from $U$, as $j(\kappa)$ would have to be the $\kappa$-th element of a definable well-order it also fixes below $\kappa$, forcing $j(\kappa) = \kappa$), no such embedding exists. The contradiction shows $V \ne L$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (inaccessibility of a critical point).** *If $j : V \to M$ is elementary, nontrivial, $M$ transitive, with $\operatorname{crit}(j) = \kappa$, then $\kappa$ is inaccessible.*

*Proof.* Regularity and the strong-limit property follow from elementarity and the fixing of ordinals below $\kappa$, as in Exercise 5: a cofinal map $\lambda \to \kappa$ with $\lambda < \kappa$ would be fixed by $j$ and force $j(\kappa) = \kappa$; and $j(2^\lambda) = 2^\lambda$ for $\lambda < \kappa$ keeps $2^\lambda$ below the critical point. Uncountability is immediate as $\kappa > \omega$ (every natural number is fixed). $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Forcing II: Cohen and the independence of CH [42.03.08] is the direct prerequisite and the seed of every construction here. The ccc preservation and $\Delta$-system control proved there for the single Cohen poset are exactly what the finite-support iteration theorem of this unit inherits; the Solovay-Tennenbaum model is $\operatorname{Add}(\omega,\aleph_2)$ generalised from one step to an $\aleph_2$-length bookkeeping iteration, and the cardinal invariants of the Cichoń diagram that [42.03.08] separated by single forcings are collapsed to the continuum by Martin's axiom here. Forcing axioms are the program that answers the question [42.03.08] raised — which axioms settle what ZFC leaves open at the level of the reals.

- The forcing machinery [42.03.07] supplies the two-step iteration $P * \dot Q$ and the name calculus that the iteration theorem rests on; the generic extension, names, and the Fundamental Theorem are imported wholesale, and the iteration $(P_\xi)$ is built by repeating the single-step $M[G]$ construction transfinitely. The constructible universe and GCH [42.03.06] is the dual technique: $L$ caps the continuum and supports $\Diamond$ and a Suslin tree, while a measurable cardinal refutes $V = L$ (Proposition 4), so the large-cardinal hierarchy reaches strictly past the constructible universe that forcing axioms and large cardinals both transcend.

- Cofinality and cardinal exponentiation [42.03.04] supplies the regularity and König constraints that bound what the continuum can be even under $\mathrm{MA}$ and large cardinals, and Easton's theorem there is the precise statement of how free the continuum function on the regulars remains; the modern search for the right axioms (Woodin's $\Omega$-logic) operates within exactly those constraints. The axiom of choice [42.03.05] enters twice: determinacy AD contradicts choice yet holds in $L(\mathbb{R})$ under Woodin cardinals, and the ultrapower construction of the embedding $j : V \to M$ for a measurable cardinal uses choice essentially. The model-theoretic stability landscape (the trichotomy and Shelah's classification of first-order theories by counting types) is the parallel program in [42.02.05] and [42.02.08]: there too a single dividing line — stability, or o-minimal tameness — organizes an entire field by how wildly its objects can proliferate, and Shelah's hand is on both the proper-forcing and the classification-theory rulers.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Donald Martin and Robert Solovay isolated Martin's axiom in their 1970 paper on internal Cohen extensions, where the Solovay-Tennenbaum theorem that a finite-support ccc iteration forces $\mathrm{MA} + \neg\mathrm{CH}$ first appeared [ref: TODO_REF Kunen Ch. VIII]. The axiom crystallised the idea that one could legislate the universe to be generic for small forcings, settling the Suslin hypothesis — open since Mikhail Suslin's 1920 problem — in the affirmative under $\mathrm{MA} + \neg\mathrm{CH}$. Saharon Shelah's theory of proper forcing, developed through the 1970s and presented in *Proper and Improper Forcing*, extended iteration from ccc to the much wider class preserving $\aleph_1$, and the Proper Forcing Axiom of James Baumgartner gave the strongest of the standard forcing axioms, its consistency drawn from a supercompact cardinal.

The large-cardinal hierarchy grew in parallel. Stanisław Ulam introduced measurable cardinals in 1930 via measure-theoretic problems; Dana Scott proved in 1961 that a measurable refutes $V = L$, the first theorem showing large cardinals constrain the constructible universe, using the ultrapower embedding $j : V \to M$ that became the organising tool of the field [ref: TODO_REF Kanamori Ch. 1-6]. The consistency-strength ladder and its near-linearity were charted by Azriel Lévy, Robert Solovay, Jack Silver, and others; Lévy and Solovay's 1967 theorem that small forcing preserves measurability showed large cardinals cannot decide CH. Donald Martin, John Steel, and Hugh Woodin proved in 1985 that Woodin cardinals yield projective determinacy, the high point of the program connecting the upper hierarchy to the regularity of the definable reals. Whether these axioms fix a determinate truth value for CH remains contested: Woodin's $\Omega$-logic and $\mathbb{P}_{\max}$ argue for $2^{\aleph_0} = \aleph_2$, while Joel Hamkins's set-theoretic multiverse treats CH as a parameter varying across equally legitimate universes.
