## Intuition [Beginner]

The previous unit taught you to compare two worlds: when one sits inside another, and when no statement can tell two worlds apart. This unit answers the next question. How do you *build* a world that contains exactly the features you want? The tool is compactness, and the trick that aims it is the method of diagrams.

Compactness is a promise about demands. Suppose you have a long, possibly endless list of demands you want a single world to satisfy. The promise says: if every finite handful of those demands can be met by some world, then the whole list can be met by one world at once. You only ever have to check finite handfuls, which you can often do by hand. The full theorem was proved earlier; here it becomes a construction machine.

The method of diagrams is how you load the machine. To force a new world to contain a faithful copy of a world you already have, you give every object in the old world its own private name, then write down every basic fact about those named objects ŌĆö who is below whom, what adds to what. Any world that believes all of those labelled facts is forced to contain a copy of the old world.

Now you stack on extra demands ŌĆö "there is a number bigger than all of these" ŌĆö check that each finite handful is met inside the old world, and let compactness deliver a new world holding both the copy and the intruder. Listing facts, naming objects, checking finite handfuls: that is the whole craft this unit teaches.

```
   add one new name  c  and the endless demands:
        c > name(0) ,  c > name(1) ,  c > name(2) , ...

     0   1   2   3   ...  ........  ...  c   c+1  ...
     |---|---|---|----        ----|----|---|---
      planted by the diagram        forced past every name
```

| move | what you write down | what it forces |
|---|---|---|
| atomic diagram | basic facts, each object named | a copy of the old world is embedded |
| elementary diagram | every fact, not just basic ones | an exact, statement-respecting copy |
| extra demands | "bigger than every name" | an intruder past the whole copy |
| compactness | check each finite handful | one world meeting all demands at once |

Read top to bottom: name everything, list the facts, pile on what you want, check finite handfuls, collect the world.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We build a world that contains a faithful copy of the whole numbers and also a single number sitting past all of them. We do it with the two moves: write the diagram, then add demands.

Step 1. Name every whole number. Give $0$ the name $n_0$, give $1$ the name $n_1$, give $2$ the name $n_2$, and so on, one private name per number.

Step 2. Write down the basic facts ŌĆö the diagram. For every pair, record who is below whom: $n_0 < n_1$, $n_1 < n_2$, $n_2 < n_3$, and so on; also record the addition facts like $n_1 + n_1 = n_2$. This is a long list, but every item on it is a plain basic fact true of the whole numbers.

Step 3. Add a new name $c$ and the extra demands $c > n_0$, $c > n_1$, $c > n_2$, continuing without end. These say $c$ beats every named whole number.

Step 4. Check finite handfuls. Take any finite piece of the whole list. It mentions finitely many named numbers, with a largest name, say $n_N$. Inside the ordinary whole numbers, let $c$ stand for $N + 1$. Then every basic fact in the handful is true (it was true of the whole numbers) and every demand $c > n_k$ in the handful holds because $N + 1$ beats $0, 1, \dots, N$. So the handful is met.

Step 5. Apply compactness. Every finite handful is met, so one world meets the entire list. In that world the names $n_0, n_1, n_2, \dots$ pick out a faithful copy of the whole numbers (all the basic facts hold), and $c$ names an object bigger than every one of them.

What this tells us: the diagram forced the copy, the extra demands forced the intruder, and finite-handful checking ŌĆö each handful met by a big enough ordinary number ŌĆö let compactness hand us a single world with both. The recipe is general: name, list, demand, check, collect.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a first-order language $\mathcal{L}$, the satisfaction relation $\models$, and the embedding/elementary-embedding hierarchy of [42.02.01]. The **compactness theorem** ŌĆö a set of $\mathcal{L}$-sentences has a model iff every finite subset does ŌĆö is established in [42.01.07]; this unit takes it as given and deploys it. The deployment device is the method of diagrams.

Let $\mathfrak{M}$ be an $\mathcal{L}$-structure with universe $M$. Expand $\mathcal{L}$ to $\mathcal{L}_M = \mathcal{L} \cup \{\, c_a : a \in M \,\}$ by a fresh constant for each element. The **atomic diagram** is
$$
\mathrm{Diag}(\mathfrak{M}) = \{\, \varphi(c_{a_1}, \dots, c_{a_n}) : \varphi \text{ atomic or negated-atomic}, \ \mathfrak{M} \models \varphi[a_1, \dots, a_n] \,\},
$$
and the **elementary diagram** $\mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{M})$ is the set of *all* $\mathcal{L}_M$-sentences true in $(\mathfrak{M}, a)_{a \in M}$ (the expansion interpreting $c_a$ as $a$) [ref: TODO_REF Marker Ch. 2].

**Diagram lemma.** For an $\mathcal{L}_M$-structure $\mathfrak{N}$, write $h : a \mapsto c_a^{\mathfrak{N}}$. Then $\mathfrak{N} \models \mathrm{Diag}(\mathfrak{M})$ iff $h$ is an $\mathcal{L}$-embedding of $\mathfrak{M}$ into the reduct $\mathfrak{N}\!\restriction\!\mathcal{L}$, and $\mathfrak{N} \models \mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{M})$ iff $h$ is an *elementary* embedding. The slogan: *models of the diagram are the structures admitting an embedding of $\mathfrak{M}$; models of the elementary diagram are those admitting an elementary embedding.* Composing the lemma with compactness gives the workhorse: an $\mathcal{L}$-theory $T$ has a model into which $\mathfrak{M}$ embeds iff $T \cup \mathrm{Diag}(\mathfrak{M})$ is satisfiable, and an elementary extension of $\mathfrak{M}$ satisfying $T$ iff $T \cup \mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{M})$ is satisfiable. Consistency becomes "there is a model embedding what I want."

Two named structural facts deployed below. A theory $T$ is **complete** when for every $\mathcal{L}$-sentence $\sigma$, either $T \models \sigma$ or $T \models \neg\sigma$. $T$ is **$\kappa$-categorical** when all its models of cardinality $\kappa$ are isomorphic. A class $\mathcal{K}$ of structures has the **joint embedding property** when any two members embed into a common member, and a theory admits **elementary amalgamation** when any two elementary extensions $\mathfrak{N}_1, \mathfrak{N}_2$ of a common $\mathfrak{M}$ admit elementary embeddings into a single $\mathfrak{P}$ agreeing on $\mathfrak{M}$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"$\mathrm{Diag}(\mathfrak{M})$ already pins down $\mathfrak{M}$ up to isomorphism."* It pins down only an embedded copy. The structure $(\mathbb{Z}; <)$ models $\mathrm{Diag}(\mathbb{N}; <)$ via the inclusion, yet is not isomorphic to $(\mathbb{N}; <)$. The atomic diagram controls the quantifier-free fragment; quantified facts are the surplus carried only by $\mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}$.

- *"A $\kappa$-categorical theory is complete with no further hypothesis."* The ┼üo┼ø-Vaught test needs *no finite models*. The theory of a single equivalence relation with exactly two classes, each infinite, fails categoricity in the relevant sense once finite models intrude; the cleanest statements require ruling finite models out so that all $\kappa$-sized models are infinite and L├Čwenheim-Skolem applies uniformly.

- *"Compactness builds the model, so the diagram is optional."* Compactness only certifies satisfiability of a sentence set. Without the diagram there is no sentence set whose models are forced to *contain* a prescribed structure; the diagram is exactly the translation of "embeds $\mathfrak{M}$" into first-order sentences that compactness can act on.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature result is the **┼üo┼ø-Vaught test**, the most-used completeness criterion in model theory: categoricity at a single cardinal, plus the absence of finite models, forces completeness. It is the diagram-and-compactness method turned into a decision procedure for "does this theory settle every sentence?"

**Theorem (┼üo┼ø-Vaught test).** *Let $T$ be a satisfiable $\mathcal{L}$-theory with no finite models. If $T$ is $\kappa$-categorical for some $\kappa \ge |\mathcal{L}| + \aleph_0$, then $T$ is complete* [ref: TODO_REF Marker Ch. 2].

*Proof.* Suppose, toward a contradiction, that $T$ is not complete: some sentence $\sigma$ has $T \not\models \sigma$ and $T \not\models \neg\sigma$. Then both $T \cup \{\neg\sigma\}$ and $T \cup \{\sigma\}$ are satisfiable, by models $\mathfrak{A}$ and $\mathfrak{B}$ respectively, with $\mathfrak{A} \models \neg\sigma$ and $\mathfrak{B} \models \sigma$. Since $T$ has no finite models, $\mathfrak{A}$ and $\mathfrak{B}$ are infinite. By the L├Čwenheim-Skolem theorems of [42.01.07] ŌĆö downward to shrink and upward (adjoin $\kappa$ distinct constants to $\mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}$ and apply compactness) to grow ŌĆö each of $\mathfrak{A}, \mathfrak{B}$ has a model elementarily equivalent to it of cardinality exactly $\kappa$. Call these $\mathfrak{A}_\kappa \equiv \mathfrak{A}$ and $\mathfrak{B}_\kappa \equiv \mathfrak{B}$; both model $T$, both have cardinality $\kappa$. Now $\kappa$-categoricity gives $\mathfrak{A}_\kappa \cong \mathfrak{B}_\kappa$, hence $\mathfrak{A}_\kappa \equiv \mathfrak{B}_\kappa$. But $\mathfrak{A}_\kappa \models \neg\sigma$ (it agrees with $\mathfrak{A}$) while $\mathfrak{B}_\kappa \models \sigma$ (it agrees with $\mathfrak{B}$), so $\mathfrak{A}_\kappa \not\equiv \mathfrak{B}_\kappa$. The contradiction shows no such $\sigma$ exists, so $T$ is complete. $\square$

**Corollary (worked completeness results).** The theory $\mathrm{DLO}$ of dense linear orders without endpoints is $\aleph_0$-categorical (Cantor's back-and-forth theorem, [42.02.01]) with no finite models, hence complete; the theory $\mathrm{ACF}_p$ of algebraically closed fields of fixed characteristic $p$ is $\kappa$-categorical in every uncountable $\kappa$ (a model is determined up to isomorphism by its transcendence degree, which equals $\kappa$ when $\kappa > \aleph_0$) with no finite models, hence complete ŌĆö and decidable, since a complete recursively axiomatized theory is decidable. The same test certifies the completeness of the theory of the random graph and of infinite-dimensional vector spaces over a fixed field. The deeper structure of $\kappa$-categoricity ŌĆö Morley's theorem that categoricity in one uncountable cardinal forces it in all ŌĆö is the subject of [42.02.06].

**Bridge.** The ┼üo┼ø-Vaught test is the foundational reason a single categoricity datum settles every sentence: completeness, an unbounded condition over all sentences, is decided by one isomorphism at one cardinal, and this is exactly the diagram-and-compactness method run as a completeness criterion. It builds toward the elementary amalgamation and Robinson consistency results below, whose proofs splice models along shared elementary diagrams, and it appears again in the categoricity theory of [42.02.06], where Morley's theorem upgrades the single-cardinal hypothesis to a transfer across all uncountable cardinals. The argument generalises the non-standard-model construction of [42.01.07] from "force one intruder" to "force a model of prescribed size elementarily equivalent to a given one," and putting these together, the upward and downward L├Čwenheim-Skolem moves that resize a model are precisely the steps that make two arbitrary models of $T$ comparable at the categoricity cardinal. The central insight is that completeness equals "$\equiv$-uniqueness," and at the categoricity cardinal $\equiv$-uniqueness collapses into $\cong$-uniqueness, which the hypothesis hands over for free.

## Advanced results [Master]

The compactness theorem and the diagram lemma organize five developments: the diagram dictionary turning consistency into embeddings, the ┼üo┼ø-Vaught completeness test with its amalgamation companions, the Robinson-Craig-Beth circle on consistency and definability, the preservation theorems reading the quantifier prefix off the map hierarchy, and the ultraproduct construction supplying the calculus-free proof and the Keisler-Shelah characterization of $\equiv$.

**Theorem 1 (the diagram dictionary).** For an $\mathcal{L}$-structure $\mathfrak{M}$ and an $\mathcal{L}$-theory $T$: $T$ has a model embedding $\mathfrak{M}$ iff $T \cup \mathrm{Diag}(\mathfrak{M})$ is satisfiable, and $T$ has a model into which $\mathfrak{M}$ embeds elementarily iff $T \cup \mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{M})$ is satisfiable [ref: TODO_REF Marker Ch. 2]. Fed to compactness, this manufactures structures with prescribed substructures: the non-standard models of arithmetic ($T = \mathrm{Th}(\mathfrak{N})$, $\mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{N})$, plus $c > S^n0$); the proper elementary extensions of any infinite $\mathfrak{M}$ (add $d \ne c_a$ for all $a$); the models realizing a prescribed set of formulas (a type) by adding those formulas in a fresh variable-constant and checking finite satisfiability against $\mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{M})$ ŌĆö the construction [42.02.03] develops in full. The diagram is the precise translation under which "embeds $\mathfrak{M}$" becomes a first-order constraint that compactness can satisfy.

**Theorem 2 (┼üo┼ø-Vaught test, amalgamation, joint embedding).** A satisfiable theory with no finite models that is $\kappa$-categorical for some $\kappa \ge |\mathcal{L}| + \aleph_0$ is complete [ref: TODO_REF Marker Ch. 2]; the worked instances are $\mathrm{DLO}$, $\mathrm{ACF}_p$, the random graph, and infinite vector spaces over a fixed field, all thereby complete and (being recursively axiomatized) decidable. The proof is the resize-to-$\kappa$-and-compare argument, and the same diagram amalgamation yields more: any two elementary extensions of a common $\mathfrak{M}$ amalgamate elementarily over $\mathfrak{M}$ (satisfiability of $\mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{N}_1) \cup \mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{N}_2)$ with shared $M$-constants), and a complete theory has the joint embedding property (its models elementarily embed into a common elementary extension). These amalgamation facts are the existence proofs behind monster models and the homogeneous-universal models on which stability theory operates.

**Theorem 3 (Robinson consistency, Craig interpolation, Beth definability).** Robinson's joint consistency theorem: for theories $T_1 \supseteq T$ in $L_1$ and $T_2 \supseteq T$ in $L_2$ with $T$ a complete theory in $L_0 = L_1 \cap L_2$, the union $T_1 \cup T_2$ is consistent; in general $T_1 \cup T_2$ is consistent iff no $L_0$-sentence is proved by $T_1$ and refuted by $T_2$ [ref: TODO_REF Robinson 1956]. The proof is the alternating elementary-chain amalgamation: build $\mathfrak{M}_0 \preceq \mathfrak{M}_1 \preceq \cdots$ flipping between models of $T_1$ and $T_2$, at each stage realizing the elementary diagram of the previous model over the shared $L_0$-reduct by compactness and the $L_0$-completeness hypothesis, and taking the union, whose $L_1$- and $L_2$-reducts model $T_1$ and $T_2$. Craig's interpolation theorem ŌĆö $\models \varphi \to \psi$ yields an interpolant $\theta$ in the common vocabulary with $\models \varphi \to \theta \to \psi$ ŌĆö follows from Robinson consistency by the no-interpolant-implies-joint-consistency contrapositive [ref: TODO_REF Craig 1957]. Beth's definability theorem ŌĆö implicit definability of a predicate by a theory equals explicit definability over the old vocabulary ŌĆö follows from interpolation applied to the two-copy theory $T(R) \cup T(R')$ asserting that two predicates implicitly defined the same way must coincide; the interpolant in the shared old vocabulary is the explicit definition.

**Theorem 4 (preservation theorems via diagrams).** The ┼üo┼ø-Tarski theorem: a first-order theory is preserved under substructures iff it is axiomatizable by universal ($\Pi_1$) sentences, and preserved under extensions iff by existential ($\Sigma_1$) sentences [ref: TODO_REF Chang and Keisler Ch. 3]. The forward direction (syntax $\to$ preservation) is the quantifier-free restriction of [42.02.01]; the converse (preservation $\to$ syntax) is the diagram argument: if $T$ is preserved under substructures, the set of $\Pi_1$-consequences $T_\forall$ has the same models as $T$, proved by embedding a model of $T_\forall$ into a model of $T$ via a compactness argument on $\mathrm{Diag}$ plus the universal theory of the target. The Chang-┼üo┼ø-Suszko theorem extends this: a theory is preserved under unions of chains iff it is $\Pi_2$-axiomatizable, the diagram amalgamation now run along an $\omega$-chain. These read the quantifier prefix as the exact semantic invariant a map respects, the preservation counterpart of the prenex hierarchy.

**Theorem 5 (ultraproducts, ┼üo┼ø's theorem, Keisler-Shelah).** The ultraproduct $\prod_U \mathfrak{M}_i$ over an ultrafilter $U$ on the index set satisfies a formula of a tuple of $U$-classes iff $U$-almost-all factors satisfy it of the representatives ŌĆö **┼üo┼ø's theorem** [ref: TODO_REF Chang and Keisler Ch. 4]. This yields the calculus-free compactness proof (the ultraproduct of models of each finite subset, over an ultrafilter concentrating on the cones, models the whole theory), and it is a construction tool: the diagonal embedding into the **ultrapower** $\mathfrak{M}^I/U$ is elementary, so $\mathfrak{M}^I/U$ is an elementary extension; $\mathbb{R}^* = \mathbb{R}^{\mathbb{N}}/U$ over a non-principal $U$ is the hyperreals, with the transfer principle as ┼üo┼ø applied to $\mathbb{R}$. The **Keisler-Shelah isomorphism theorem** closes the circle: $\mathfrak{M} \equiv \mathfrak{N}$ iff there is an index set $I$ and ultrafilter $U$ with $\mathfrak{M}^I/U \cong \mathfrak{N}^I/U$ ŌĆö elementary equivalence is exactly isomorphism of suitable ultrapowers, a purely algebraic rendering of the logical relation $\equiv$ of [42.02.01].

**Synthesis.** The diagram lemma is the foundational reason model theory is constructive rather than merely comparative, and putting these together it drives all five developments through one translation: "$\mathfrak{N}$ embeds $\mathfrak{M}$" becomes "$\mathfrak{N} \models \mathrm{Diag}(\mathfrak{M})$," so compactness, which only ever certifies satisfiability, becomes a machine for building structures with prescribed parts. This is exactly what Theorem 1 makes precise and what the ┼üo┼ø-Vaught test of Theorem 2 exploits ŌĆö completeness is $\equiv$-uniqueness, and the resize-to-$\kappa$ moves of [42.01.07] collapse $\equiv$-uniqueness into the $\cong$-uniqueness that categoricity supplies. The Robinson-Craig-Beth circle of Theorem 3 is the same amalgamation read for definability: the alternating elementary chain that splices two theories over a shared complete $L_0$-theory is dual to the interpolant that separates them, and Beth definability is the central insight that implicit and explicit definition coincide because the splicing model and the interpolating sentence are two faces of one consistency fact. The preservation theorems of Theorem 4 generalise the map hierarchy of [42.02.01] from "which formulas a fixed map respects" to "which theories a class of maps respects," reading the quantifier prefix as the semantic invariant; the converse directions are diagram-and-compactness arguments, the bridge being that $T_\forall$ and $T$ share models exactly when every model of the universal theory embeds into a model of $T$.

Ultraproducts of Theorem 5 supply the alternative proof of the compactness that started the chapter and, via Keisler-Shelah, identify the logical relation $\equiv$ with an algebraic isomorphism of ultrapowers ŌĆö the same equivalence approached from compactness, from games [42.02.01], and now from ultrafilters. This is the workhorse promised at the Beginner tier: name, list, demand, check, collect, assembled into the engine that builds the types [42.02.03], the quantifier-eliminating theories [42.02.05], and the categorical theories [42.02.06] of the rest of model theory.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (diagram lemma).** *Let $\mathfrak{M}$ be an $\mathcal{L}$-structure and $\mathfrak{N}$ an $\mathcal{L}_M$-structure, $h : a \mapsto c_a^{\mathfrak{N}}$. Then $\mathfrak{N} \models \mathrm{Diag}(\mathfrak{M})$ iff $h$ is an $\mathcal{L}$-embedding $\mathfrak{M} \to \mathfrak{N}\!\restriction\!\mathcal{L}$, and $\mathfrak{N} \models \mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{M})$ iff $h$ is elementary.*

*Proof.* For the atomic case, $\mathfrak{N} \models \mathrm{Diag}(\mathfrak{M})$ means that for every atomic or negated-atomic $\varphi$ and tuple $\bar a$ from $M$, $\mathfrak{M} \models \varphi[\bar a]$ iff $\mathfrak{N} \models \varphi(c_{\bar a})$, and $\mathfrak{N} \models \varphi(c_{\bar a})$ iff $\mathfrak{N}\!\restriction\!\mathcal{L} \models \varphi[h\bar a]$ by the definition of $h$. So membership in $\mathrm{Diag}(\mathfrak{M})$ records exactly $\mathfrak{M} \models \varphi[\bar a] \Leftrightarrow \mathfrak{N}\!\restriction\!\mathcal{L} \models \varphi[h\bar a]$ for all atomic and negated-atomic $\varphi$. This biconditional for all such $\varphi$ is precisely the condition that $h$ preserves and reflects atomic relations and term values; the negated equalities $c_a \ne c_b$ ($a \ne b$) force $h$ injective. By the embedding criterion of [42.02.01], $h$ is an embedding. The elementary case repeats the argument with $\varphi$ ranging over *all* $\mathcal{L}$-formulas: $\mathfrak{N} \models \mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{M})$ iff $\mathfrak{M} \models \varphi[\bar a] \Leftrightarrow \mathfrak{N}\!\restriction\!\mathcal{L} \models \varphi[h\bar a]$ for every $\varphi$, which is the definition of $h$ being elementary. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (consistency-to-embedding).** *An $\mathcal{L}$-theory $T$ has a model admitting an embedding (resp. elementary embedding) of $\mathfrak{M}$ iff $T \cup \mathrm{Diag}(\mathfrak{M})$ (resp. $T \cup \mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{M})$) is satisfiable.*

*Proof.* ($\Rightarrow$) If $\mathfrak{N} \models T$ with an embedding $g : \mathfrak{M} \to \mathfrak{N}$, expand $\mathfrak{N}$ to $\mathcal{L}_M$ by $c_a^{\mathfrak{N}^*} = g(a)$; then $\mathfrak{N}^* \models T$ (the sentences of $T$ are $\mathcal{L}$-sentences, unaffected) and $\mathfrak{N}^* \models \mathrm{Diag}(\mathfrak{M})$ by Proposition 1, so the union is satisfiable. ($\Leftarrow$) If $\mathfrak{N}^* \models T \cup \mathrm{Diag}(\mathfrak{M})$, its reduct $\mathfrak{N} = \mathfrak{N}^*\!\restriction\!\mathcal{L}$ models $T$ and, by Proposition 1, receives the embedding $a \mapsto c_a^{\mathfrak{N}^*}$. The elementary case is identical with $\mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (┼üo┼ø-Vaught test).** *A satisfiable $\mathcal{L}$-theory $T$ with no finite models that is $\kappa$-categorical for some $\kappa \ge |\mathcal{L}| + \aleph_0$ is complete.*

*Proof.* If not, fix $\sigma$ with $T \cup \{\sigma\}$ and $T \cup \{\neg\sigma\}$ both satisfiable, by infinite models $\mathfrak{B} \models T \cup \{\sigma\}$, $\mathfrak{A} \models T \cup \{\neg\sigma\}$ (infinite because $T$ has no finite models). By the downward and upward L├Čwenheim-Skolem theorems [42.01.07] ŌĆö the upward direction via adjoining $\kappa$ distinct constants to the elementary diagram and applying compactness, then trimming by the downward theorem ŌĆö there are $\mathfrak{A}_\kappa \equiv \mathfrak{A}$ and $\mathfrak{B}_\kappa \equiv \mathfrak{B}$ of cardinality exactly $\kappa$, both modelling $T$. By $\kappa$-categoricity $\mathfrak{A}_\kappa \cong \mathfrak{B}_\kappa$, so $\mathfrak{A}_\kappa \equiv \mathfrak{B}_\kappa$. But $\mathfrak{A}_\kappa \models \neg\sigma$ and $\mathfrak{B}_\kappa \models \sigma$, contradicting $\equiv$. So $T$ decides every $\sigma$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (proper elementary extensions exist).** *Every infinite $\mathcal{L}$-structure $\mathfrak{M}$ has a proper elementary extension.*

*Proof.* Let $\Gamma = \mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{M}) \cup \{\, d \ne c_a : a \in M\,\}$ in $\mathcal{L}_M \cup \{d\}$. A finite $\Gamma_0$ constrains $d \ne c_a$ for $a$ in a finite $F \subseteq M$; interpreting the base as $(\mathfrak{M}, a)_{a \in M}$ and $d$ as any element of the infinite set $M \setminus F$ models $\Gamma_0$. By compactness $\Gamma$ has a model $\mathfrak{N}^*$; by Proposition 2 (elementary case) its reduct $\mathfrak{N}$ is an elementary extension of $\mathfrak{M}$ via $a \mapsto c_a^{\mathfrak{N}^*}$, and $d^{\mathfrak{N}^*}$ differs from every $c_a^{\mathfrak{N}^*}$, so the embedding is not surjective: $\mathfrak{M} \prec \mathfrak{N}$ properly. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (elementary amalgamation).** *If $\mathfrak{M} \preceq \mathfrak{N}_1$ and $\mathfrak{M} \preceq \mathfrak{N}_2$, there are a structure $\mathfrak{P}$ and elementary embeddings $f_i : \mathfrak{N}_i \to \mathfrak{P}$ agreeing on $\mathfrak{M}$.*

*Proof.* In a language with disjoint constants $\{c_b\}_{b \in N_1}$, $\{d_e\}_{e \in N_2}$ identified on $M$ ($c_a = d_a$ for $a \in M$), let $\Gamma = \mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{N}_1) \cup \mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}(\mathfrak{N}_2)$. A finite $\Gamma_0$ mentions finitely many $d_e$, say $e \in E \subseteq N_2 \setminus M$, with a conjunction $\chi(\bar c, \bar d_E)$ of their elementary-diagram sentences over $M$-constants $\bar c$. Since $\mathfrak{N}_2 \succeq \mathfrak{M}$, the tuple $\bar e$ realizes some $\mathcal{L}(M)$-type, and as $\mathfrak{N}_1 \succeq \mathfrak{M}$ as well, $\mathfrak{N}_1 \equiv_M \mathfrak{N}_2$, so $\mathfrak{N}_1 \models \exists \bar x\, \chi(\bar c, \bar x)$ (the existential closure over the $M$-parameters holds in $\mathfrak{N}_2$, hence in $\mathfrak{N}_1$). Interpreting the $\mathcal{N}_1$-constants in $\mathfrak{N}_1$ and the finitely many $d_e$ as such witnesses models $\Gamma_0$. By compactness $\Gamma$ is satisfiable in some $\mathfrak{P}^*$; Proposition 2 extracts elementary embeddings $f_1, f_2$ of $\mathfrak{N}_1, \mathfrak{N}_2$ into $\mathfrak{P} = \mathfrak{P}^*\!\restriction\!\mathcal{L}$, and the identified $M$-constants force $f_1\!\restriction\! M = f_2\!\restriction\! M$. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (┼üo┼ø's theorem and the elementary ultrapower).** *For an ultrafilter $U$ on $I$ and structures $(\mathfrak{M}_i)_{i \in I}$, $\prod_U \mathfrak{M}_i \models \varphi[[\,f_1\,]_U, \dots] \Leftrightarrow \{\, i : \mathfrak{M}_i \models \varphi[f_1(i), \dots]\,\} \in U$; consequently the diagonal map $\mathfrak{M} \to \mathfrak{M}^I/U$ is elementary.*

*Proof.* Induct on $\varphi$. Atomic formulas hold by the definition of the ultraproduct operations on $U$-classes and the ultrafilter's closure under supersets and finite intersections. Negation uses that $U$ is an ultrafilter (a set is in $U$ iff its complement is not): $\prod_U \mathfrak{M}_i \models \neg\varphi$ iff $\{i : \mathfrak{M}_i \models \varphi\} \notin U$ iff its complement $\{i : \mathfrak{M}_i \models \neg\varphi\} \in U$. Conjunction uses closure under intersection. For $\exists y\, \psi$: if the index set $J = \{i : \mathfrak{M}_i \models \exists y\, \psi\} \in U$, choose for each $i \in J$ a witness $g(i)$ and set $g(i)$ arbitrarily off $J$; then $\{i : \mathfrak{M}_i \models \psi[g(i), \dots]\} \supseteq J \in U$, so by the inductive hypothesis $\prod_U \mathfrak{M}_i \models \psi[[\,g\,]_U, \dots]$, whence $\models \exists y\,\psi$. Conversely a witness class $[\,g\,]_U$ pushes its defining index set into $U$ and hence into $J$. Taking all $\mathfrak{M}_i = \mathfrak{M}$ and $f$ constant gives, for the diagonal $d(a) = [i \mapsto a]_U$, the index set $\varnothing$ or $I$; since $I \in U$, $\prod_U \mathfrak{M} \models \varphi[d\bar a] \Leftrightarrow \mathfrak{M} \models \varphi[\bar a]$, so $d$ is elementary. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Structures, embeddings, and elementary equivalence [42.02.01] supplies the map hierarchy and the diagram definitions this unit deploys. That unit owns the static comparison of two structures; this unit turns the comparison into a generator of new ones.

- Compactness and the L├Čwenheim-Skolem theorems for first-order logic [42.01.07] is the metatheorem this unit takes as its engine. Where that unit proves compactness, this unit aims it: every construction here is a finite-satisfiability check feeding the theorem of that unit.

- Types and the omitting types theorem, co-produced as [42.02.03], extends the diagram method from realizing a single structure to realizing and omitting prescribed sets of formulas. The diagram-plus-new-constant construction that builds elementary extensions here is the realizing half; the omitting types theorem is its dual, constructing models that avoid a non-isolated type, and both run on the consistency-to-model translation this unit establishes. Saturated and homogeneous models are the amalgamation closure of the extensions built here.

- Quantifier elimination and model completeness, co-produced as [42.02.05], specialize the preservation theorems and the diagram method to theories whose embeddings are all elementary. Model completeness is precisely that every embedding between models is elementary ŌĆö the diagram lemma then makes $\mathrm{Diag}$ and $\mathrm{Diag}_{\mathrm{el}}$ interchangeable over models of the theory ŌĆö and Robinson's test for model completeness is a direct diagram-and-compactness criterion. The completeness results the ┼üo┼ø-Vaught test yields here ($\mathrm{ACF}_p$, $\mathrm{DLO}$) are exactly the theories that unit shows eliminate quantifiers.

- Categoricity and Morley's theorem [42.02.06] upgrades the single-cardinal hypothesis of the ┼üo┼ø-Vaught test. Where the test here takes $\kappa$-categoricity at one cardinal as input to completeness, Morley's theorem proves that uncountable categoricity at one cardinal transfers to all uncountable cardinals, refining the crude completeness criterion into the stability-theoretic classification. The amalgamation and elementary-extension constructions of this unit are the existence lemmas that the categoricity analysis presupposes.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The method of diagrams is due to Abraham Robinson, who introduced it in the early 1950s as the systematic device for converting algebraic and model-theoretic existence questions into questions of consistency, to be settled by the compactness theorem. Robinson's 1956 paper "A result on consistency and its application to the theory of definition" proved the joint consistency theorem and applied it to the theory of definition, recovering Evert Beth's 1953 theorem that implicit definability coincides with explicit definability [ref: TODO_REF Robinson 1956]. William Craig's 1957 "Three uses of the Herbrand-Gentzen theorem" established the interpolation theorem, originally by proof-theoretic means via cut elimination, and observed its bearing on definability; the model-theoretic derivation of interpolation from Robinson consistency, and of Beth definability from interpolation, organized these results into the single circle presented here [ref: TODO_REF Craig 1957].

The ┼üo┼ø-Vaught test was isolated independently by Jerzy ┼üo┼ø and Robert Vaught around 1954 as the first general criterion deriving completeness from categoricity, and it remains the standard route to the completeness of $\mathrm{ACF}_p$, $\mathrm{DLO}$, and the random graph. ┼üo┼ø's fundamental theorem on ultraproducts, also from 1955, supplied the calculus-free proof of compactness and the ultrapower construction; H. Jerome Keisler and later Saharon Shelah established that elementary equivalence is exactly isomorphism of suitable ultrapowers, the Keisler-Shelah theorem, codified with the preservation theorems of ┼üo┼ø, Tarski, Chang, and Suszko in the textbooks of Chang and Keisler, Hodges, and Marker [ref: TODO_REF Chang and Keisler Ch. 4].
