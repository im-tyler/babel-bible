## Intuition [Beginner]

The previous unit built a ladder of harder and harder problems by repeatedly applying the jump. This unit looks at the same ladder from a completely different angle — the angle of *how you describe a set in words* — and discovers it is the very same ladder. The bridge between the two views is the centrepiece, Post's theorem.

Start with the descriptions. A plain computer can settle some questions outright: it runs, it stops, it says yes or no. Call those the level-zero questions. Now climb one step. Some questions are not settle-outright, but they have the shape "there exists a witness that makes a checkable thing true" — like "does this program ever stop?", which means "is there a number of steps after which it has halted?" You cannot bound the search, but if a witness exists you will eventually find it. The flip side is "for all witnesses, a checkable thing holds" — like "does this program run forever?" These two shapes, one search and one universal check, are the first rung above plain computation.

The pattern continues. The next rung allows one search wrapped around one universal check, or the reverse. Each new rung adds one more alternation between "there exists" and "for all." Counting those alternations is counting how complicated the description has to be, and the count is a ladder that climbs forever.

Here is the surprise. This description-ladder, built purely from how the question is phrased, lines up rung for rung with the difficulty-ladder built from the jump. Adding one more alternation to your sentence is exactly the same as climbing one more step with the jump — one more "phone call to the halting problem." So the way a question is *worded* and the way a question is *hard to compute* turn out to measure the same thing. The rest of the unit makes the wording, the counting, and the match precise, and shows the ladder never collapses to a single rung.

## Visual [Beginner]

![The arithmetical hierarchy of alternating quantifiers aligned with the jump ladder](/img/arithmetical-hierarchy-posts-theorem-placeholder.svg)

Picture each rung as a sentence shape. The capital E means "there exists a number such that"; the capital A means "for all numbers." After the quantifiers comes a box you can check by plain computation.

```
   level    "there exists" form        "for all" form
   ------    ----------------------     ----------------------
   0        [ checkable box ]          [ checkable box ]      (a plain computer decides)
   1        E . [ box ]                A . [ box ]            (one search / one universal)
   2        E A . [ box ]              A E . [ box ]          (one alternation)
   3        E A E . [ box ]            A E A . [ box ]        (two alternations)
   ...      one more letter each step, alternating E and A

   The number of ALTERNATIONS between E and A is the rung.
   Repeated same letters (E E or A A) collapse to one and do not count.
```

Now lay the two ladders side by side. The left is the wording-ladder above; the right is the jump-ladder from the previous unit. Post's theorem says the rungs match.

| wording rung | what it is | jump-ladder match |
|---|---|---|
| level 1, "there exists" | what a plain computer can list | the halting problem $0'$ as the helper |
| level 2, "there exists" | listable using a halting-problem helper | jump of the jump, $0''$, as helper |
| level 3, "there exists" | listable using a $0''$ helper | $0'''$ as helper |
| each step up | one more alternation in the sentence | one more jump on the ladder |

Read the table as a dictionary: the left column is grammar, the right column is computation, and Post's theorem is the translation that makes them say the same thing.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We classify three concrete questions by counting alternations, then read off where each sits.

Step 1. "Does program $e$ halt on input $0$?" Unfolded, this says: *there exists* a number of steps after which program $e$ has stopped on input $0$. Checking "has it stopped within $s$ steps" is a plain computation. So the shape is one "there exists" wrapped around a checkable box — one search, no alternation. This is a level-one "there exists" question.

Step 2. "Does program $e$ halt on every input?" Unfolded: *for all* inputs $x$, *there exists* a number of steps after which program $e$ has stopped on input $x$. Now there are two quantifiers and they alternate: a "for all" on the outside, a "there exists" on the inside. One alternation puts this on level two, on the "for all" side.

Step 3. "Is the set listed by program $e$ finite?" Unfolded: *there exists* a bound $b$ such that *for all* outputs $x$ the program ever prints, $x$ is below $b$. That is a "there exists" on the outside and a "for all" on the inside — one alternation, level two, on the "there exists" side. So finiteness sits one notch up from halting, and on the opposite side from totality.

What this tells us: you can read the difficulty of a question straight off the grammar of its cleanest description, once you push all the searches and universal checks to the front and count how many times they switch. Halting needs one search; running-on-everything needs a universal check guarding a search; finiteness needs a search guarding a universal check. Each extra switch is a genuine step up a ladder that, as the next sections prove, never folds back down.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix the standard enumeration $\{\varphi_e\}$, the c.e. sets $\{W_e\}$ from [42.04.03], the oracle functionals $\Phi_e^A$, the jump $A'$, and the iterated jumps $\emptyset^{(n)}$ from [42.04.04]. A computable pairing $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle : \mathbb N^2 \to \mathbb N$ with computable inverses is fixed throughout, so finite blocks of like quantifiers contract to one.

A relation $R \subseteq \mathbb N^k$ is **$\Sigma_n^0$** if
$$
R(\bar x) \iff \exists y_1\, \forall y_2\, \exists y_3 \cdots Q_n y_n\; S(\bar x, y_1, \dots, y_n),
$$
where $S$ is a computable ($\Delta_0^0$) relation and the $n$ unbounded number quantifiers strictly alternate, beginning with $\exists$ (so $Q_n = \exists$ if $n$ is odd, $\forall$ if $n$ is even). It is **$\Pi_n^0$** if it has such a prenex form beginning with $\forall$, and **$\Delta_n^0 = \Sigma_n^0 \cap \Pi_n^0$**. Bounded quantifiers ($\exists y < t$, $\forall y < t$) may appear inside $S$ without raising the level. By convention $\Sigma_0^0 = \Pi_0^0 = \Delta_0^0$ is the class of **computable** relations [ref: TODO_REF Soare Ch. 4]. A set is **arithmetical** if it is $\Sigma_n^0$ for some $n$.

The base level matches the previous chapters: $\Delta_1^0$ is the computable sets, $\Sigma_1^0$ is the **c.e.** sets (a c.e. set is $\{x : \exists s\, T(e,x,s)\}$, a single $\exists$ over a computable matrix), and $\Pi_1^0$ is the **co-c.e.** sets. The **relativised** classes $\Sigma_n^{0,B}$, $\Pi_n^{0,B}$, $\Delta_n^{0,B}$ are defined identically with $S$ allowed to be $B$-computable.

Closure properties hold at each level [ref: TODO_REF Rogers Ch. 14]. The classes ascend, $\Sigma_n^0 \cup \Pi_n^0 \subseteq \Delta_{n+1}^0 \subseteq \Sigma_{n+1}^0 \cap \Pi_{n+1}^0$; each $\Sigma_n^0$ is closed under finite unions and intersections, under bounded quantification, under computable substitution in the free variables, and under the leading existential quantifier (a $\exists$ in front of a $\Sigma_n^0$ matrix stays $\Sigma_n^0$ by contraction $\exists y_1 \exists y_1' \equiv \exists \langle y_1, y_1' \rangle$); dually for $\Pi_n^0$ and $\forall$. Complementation swaps the classes: $R$ is $\Sigma_n^0 \iff \overline R$ is $\Pi_n^0$.

A set $C$ is **$\Sigma_n^0$-complete** if $C \in \Sigma_n^0$ and $A \le_m C$ for every $A \in \Sigma_n^0$; **$\Pi_n^0$-complete** is dual. A $\Sigma_n^0$-complete set is not $\Pi_n^0$ (else the hierarchy would collapse at level $n$), so completeness pins a set exactly at its level.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *"More quantifiers always means a higher level."* Only alternations count. The relation $\exists y_1\, \exists y_2\, \exists y_3\, S$ is $\Sigma_1^0$, not $\Sigma_3^0$: contract the block to $\exists \langle y_1, y_2, y_3 \rangle$. Likewise bounded quantifiers do not raise the level — $\forall z < t\, \exists y\, S$ with $S$ computable is still $\Sigma_1^0$, because the bounded $\forall$ folds into the computable matrix.

- *"$\Delta_n^0$ is a single quantifier-shape."* $\Delta_n^0 = \Sigma_n^0 \cap \Pi_n^0$ is defined by having *both* an $n$-quantifier $\Sigma$ form and an $n$-quantifier $\Pi$ form, not by a normal form of its own. A $\Delta_2^0$ set is one expressible both as $\exists\forall$ and as $\forall\exists$ over computable matrices; by Post's theorem these are exactly the $\emptyset'$-computable sets.

- *"$\Sigma_n^0 \cup \Pi_n^0 = \Delta_{n+1}^0$."* The inclusion is proper. There are $\Delta_{n+1}^0$ sets in neither $\Sigma_n^0$ nor $\Pi_n^0$; the union of the two $n$-level classes is not closed and sits strictly inside the next $\Delta$. The hierarchy is finer than the $\Sigma$/$\Pi$ dichotomy suggests.

- *"Completeness and membership are the same."* Every $\Sigma_n^0$ set is in $\Sigma_n^0$, but only the $\le_m$-hardest are $\Sigma_n^0$-complete. $\mathrm{Tot}$ is $\Pi_2^0$ but not $\Sigma_2^0$ (it is $\Pi_2^0$-complete); a computable set is in every $\Sigma_n^0$ yet complete for none above level $0$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The two organising results are that the hierarchy is **proper** — it genuinely climbs, $\Sigma_n^0 \ne \Pi_n^0$ at every level — and **Post's theorem**, which identifies the syntactic ladder with the jump ladder of [42.04.04], so quantifier-alternation depth equals depth on the iterated jump.

**Theorem (the hierarchy theorem and Post's theorem).** *For every $n \ge 1$: (i) there is a $\Sigma_n^0$ set that is not $\Pi_n^0$, hence $\Sigma_n^0 \ne \Pi_n^0$ and the inclusions $\Sigma_n^0 \subsetneq \Sigma_{n+1}^0$, $\Delta_n^0 \subsetneq \Delta_{n+1}^0$ are strict; (ii) (Post) $A \in \Sigma_{n+1}^0 \iff A$ is c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)}$, $A \in \Delta_{n+1}^0 \iff A \le_T \emptyset^{(n)}$, and $\emptyset^{(n)}$ is $\Sigma_n^0$-complete.*

*Proof.* (i) Arithmetising the $\Sigma_n^0$ definitions produces a **universal $\Sigma_n^0$ set** $U_n \subseteq \mathbb N^2$ that is itself $\Sigma_n^0$ and satisfies: for every $\Sigma_n^0$ set $A$ there is $e$ with $A = \{x : U_n(e, x)\}$. (Replace the computable matrix $S$ by the universal Kleene predicate and quantify over its index; the alternation pattern is preserved.) Define the diagonal $D = \{x : U_n(x, x)\}$. Then $D \in \Sigma_n^0$. Were $D \in \Pi_n^0$, its complement $\overline D = \{x : \neg U_n(x,x)\}$ would be $\Sigma_n^0$, so $\overline D = \{x : U_n(e_0, x)\}$ for some index $e_0$. Evaluating at $x = e_0$: $e_0 \in \overline D \iff U_n(e_0, e_0) \iff e_0 \in D$, contradicting $e_0 \in \overline D \iff e_0 \notin D$. So $D \in \Sigma_n^0 \setminus \Pi_n^0$, whence $\Sigma_n^0 \ne \Pi_n^0$; since $\Sigma_n^0, \Pi_n^0 \subseteq \Delta_{n+1}^0$ and the two differ, both inclusions into $\Delta_{n+1}^0$ are strict, and the hierarchy does not collapse.

(ii) Argue by induction on $n$, the base $n = 0$ being $\Sigma_1^0 = $ c.e. $= $ c.e. in $\emptyset$ and $\Delta_1^0 = $ computable $= \{A : A \le_T \emptyset\}$, with $\emptyset^{(0)} = \emptyset$ complete for $\Sigma_0^0$ at the bottom. Inductive step, the $\Sigma$ equivalence first. ($\Leftarrow$) Suppose $A$ is c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)}$, so $A = \{x : \exists s\, T^{\emptyset^{(n)}}(e, x, s)\}$ where the oracle computation queries $\emptyset^{(n)}$. By the induction hypothesis $\emptyset^{(n)}$ is $\Sigma_n^0$ and its complement is $\Pi_n^0$; replacing each positive oracle answer "$q \in \emptyset^{(n)}$" by its $\Sigma_n^0$ definition and each negative answer by the $\Pi_n^0$ definition, the bounded run of the computation (finitely many queries, by the use principle of [42.04.04]) becomes a finite conjunction of $\Sigma_n^0$ and $\Pi_n^0$ conditions, hence $\Sigma_n^0$ after contraction; prefixing the outer $\exists s$ keeps it $\Sigma_{n+1}^0$. So $A \in \Sigma_{n+1}^0$.

($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $A \in \Sigma_{n+1}^0$, say $A(x) \iff \exists y\, P(x,y)$ with $P \in \Pi_n^0$. By the induction hypothesis $\overline P \in \Sigma_n^0$ is c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)}$, so $P$ is $\Pi_1$ in $\emptyset^{(n)}$ and hence $\emptyset^{(n+1)}$-computable; in particular an $\emptyset^{(n)}$-oracle decides $P(x,y)$ with one further query to the jump $\emptyset^{(n+1)} = (\emptyset^{(n)})'$. Then $A(x) \iff \exists y\, P(x,y)$ is c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)}$ once $P$ is $\emptyset^{(n)}$-decidable: search $y$, checking each $P(x,y)$ by the $\emptyset^{(n)}$-oracle, so $A$ is the domain of an $\emptyset^{(n)}$-partial-computable function, i.e. c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)}$.

The $\Delta_{n+1}^0$ statement follows: $A \in \Delta_{n+1}^0 \iff A$ and $\overline A$ are both c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)}$ $\iff A \le_T \emptyset^{(n)}$ by Post's relativised complementation criterion (Exercise 4 of [42.04.04]). Completeness: every $\Sigma_{n+1}^0$ set is c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)}$, hence $\le_m \emptyset^{(n+1)} = (\emptyset^{(n)})'$ by the relativised $\le_m$-completeness of the jump (jump theorem (ii) of [42.04.04]), and $\emptyset^{(n+1)} \in \Sigma_{n+1}^0$; so $\emptyset^{(n+1)}$ is $\Sigma_{n+1}^0$-complete, completing the induction. $\square$

**Bridge.** Post's theorem is the foundational reason the arithmetical hierarchy and the jump ladder are not two parallel structures but one: each added unbounded quantifier alternation is exactly one application of the jump, so the $\Sigma_{n+1}^0$ sets are precisely the sets c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)}$ and $\emptyset^{(n)}$ is the $\Sigma_n^0$-complete set — this is exactly jump theorem (ii) of [42.04.04] read inductively along $\emptyset, \emptyset', \emptyset'', \dots$. The diagonalisation that proves properness is the same self-application that gave $K \not\le_T \emptyset$ in [42.04.02], now run against the universal $\Sigma_n^0$ set at every level, which generalises the single halting diagonal to an unbounded tower. The equivalence $A \in \Delta_{n+1}^0 \iff A \le_T \emptyset^{(n)}$ is dual to the $\Sigma$/$\Pi$ split: being computable from the $n$-th jump is being decidable both with one search and with one universal check over $\emptyset^{(n-1)}$. This builds toward Tarski's undefinability of truth in the Advanced results, where the full truth set escapes every level, and it appears again in the priority method [42.04.06], where the $\Delta_2^0$ sets — the $\emptyset'$-computable sets, by the limit lemma — are exactly the approximable sets the finite-injury constructions exploit; putting these together, the central insight is that one operator, the jump, generates both the computational ladder and its syntactic shadow.

## Advanced results [Master]

The hierarchy is proper and aligned with the jump; the results below fix the index-set completeness levels, identify the arithmetical sets with first-order definability and locate truth strictly above the hierarchy, and continue the construction transfinitely into the hyperarithmetical and analytical levels.

**Theorem 1 (Post's theorem in full; the jump as the hierarchy engine).** For every $n$: $A \in \Sigma_{n+1}^0 \iff A$ is c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)} \iff A$ is c.e. in some $\Pi_n^0$ set; $A \in \Delta_{n+1}^0 \iff A \le_T \emptyset^{(n)}$; and $\emptyset^{(n)}$ is $\Sigma_n^0$-complete, hence properly $\Sigma_n^0$ (in $\Sigma_n^0 \setminus \Pi_n^0$) [ref: TODO_REF Soare Ch. 4]. The iterated jumps $\emptyset, \emptyset', \emptyset'', \dots$ are therefore exactly the $\le_m$-complete sets at successive levels, and the relativisation principle of [42.04.04] gives the same statements over any base: $A \in \Sigma_{n+1}^{0,B} \iff A$ is c.e. in $B^{(n)}$. Quantifier-alternation depth and jump depth are one invariant. The $\Sigma_n^0$-completeness is what makes "complete for level $n$" a robust notion: $\le_m \emptyset^{(n)}$ degrees stratify the arithmetical sets exactly as the hierarchy does.

**Theorem 2 (arithmetical $=$ first-order definable; Tarski's undefinability of truth).** A set $A \subseteq \mathbb N$ is arithmetical (in some $\Sigma_n^0$) iff it is definable by a first-order formula in the standard structure $(\mathbb N, +, \cdot, 0, 1, <)$ [ref: TODO_REF Hinman Ch. IV]. The hierarchy levels are exactly the bounded-quantifier-prefix complexity of such formulas. But the **truth set** $\mathrm{Th}(\mathbb N) = \{\ulcorner \sigma \urcorner : \mathbb N \models \sigma\}$ is **not arithmetical**: by Tarski's theorem any arithmetical set is $\Sigma_n^0$ for a fixed $n$, while a putative arithmetical truth predicate could be diagonalised — applying the fixed-point lemma of [42.01.09] to its negation yields a sentence asserting its own falsity, a contradiction. Concretely $\mathrm{Th}(\mathbb N)$ computes every $\emptyset^{(n)}$ uniformly (each $\Sigma_n^0$ statement is decided by truth), so it lies strictly above every level of the hierarchy. This is the semantic ceiling: provability in a c.e. theory is $\Sigma_1^0$ and incomplete [42.01.09], whereas truth is not arithmetical at all, and the gap between them is the precise content of the contrast between the first incompleteness theorem and the undefinability theorem.

**Theorem 3 (index-set completeness classification).** The natural index sets occupy exact levels and are complete there [ref: TODO_REF Rogers Ch. 14]: $\mathrm{Fin} = \{e : W_e \text{ finite}\}$ is $\Sigma_2^0$-complete; $\mathrm{Inf} = \{e : W_e \text{ infinite}\}$ and $\mathrm{Tot} = \{e : \varphi_e \text{ total}\}$ are $\Pi_2^0$-complete; $\mathrm{Cof} = \{e : \overline{W_e} \text{ finite}\}$ and $\mathrm{Rec} = \{e : W_e \text{ computable}\}$ are $\Sigma_3^0$-complete; $\mathrm{Ext} = \{e : \varphi_e \text{ extends to a total computable function}\}$ is $\Sigma_3^0$. The completeness proofs are uniform s-m-n constructions coding a generic level-$n$ predicate into the structural property; Rice's theorem (every index set other than $\emptyset$ and $\mathbb N$ is undecidable, $\ge_m \emptyset'$ or its complement) is the level-$1$ shadow of this classification, and the Rice-Shapiro theorem characterising the c.e. ($\Sigma_1^0$) index sets is its level-$1$ refinement. The classification is the standard testbed: it shows the hierarchy is populated with naturally arising complete sets at $\Sigma_2, \Pi_2, \Sigma_3$ and beyond.

**Theorem 4 (the hyperarithmetical hierarchy and $\Delta^1_1$).** Iterating the jump along the computable (recursive) ordinals continues the hierarchy transfinitely: using Kleene's system of notations $\mathcal O$ for ordinals $< \omega_1^{CK}$ (the Church-Kleene ordinal, the least non-computable ordinal), define $\emptyset^{(a)}$ for $a \in \mathcal O$ by $\emptyset^{(a+1)} = (\emptyset^{(a)})'$ and $\emptyset^{(\lambda)} = \bigoplus_{a <_{\mathcal O} \lambda} \emptyset^{(a)}$ at limits [ref: TODO_REF Hinman Ch. V]. The **hyperarithmetical** sets are those computable from some $\emptyset^{(a)}$, $a \in \mathcal O$; the **Kleene-Souslin theorem** identifies them with $\Delta^1_1$, the sets both $\Sigma^1_1$ and $\Pi^1_1$ in the analytical hierarchy. Hyperarithmetical is to arithmetical as the effective Borel sets are to the finite Borel levels: the arithmetical hierarchy is the finite part, hyperarithmetical the effective-countable-ordinal part, and $\omega_1^{CK}$ the exact ceiling of the recursive iteration.

**Theorem 5 (the analytical hierarchy).** Allowing quantification over **functions** (or sets) of numbers gives the analytical hierarchy: $A$ is $\Sigma^1_1$ if $A(x) \iff \exists f \in \mathbb N^{\mathbb N}\, \forall y\, R(x, f, y)$ with $R$ arithmetical, $\Pi^1_1$ the dual, and $\Sigma^1_{n+1}, \Pi^1_{n+1}$ by alternating function quantifiers over an arithmetical matrix [ref: TODO_REF Rogers Ch. 16]. The $\Pi^1_1$ sets are the effective coanalytic sets, with Kleene's normal form $A(x) \iff \forall f\, \exists n\, R(x, \bar f n)$ and the boundedness and basis theorems; $\Delta^1_1 = $ hyperarithmetical (Theorem 4); and the analytical hierarchy is proper. This is the second floor above the arithmetical one: number quantifiers build $\Sigma_n^0$, the jump iteration along $\mathcal O$ builds the hyperarithmetical $\Delta^1_1$, and a single function quantifier jumps to $\Sigma^1_1$, far above everything the number-quantifier ladder reaches. The set $\mathcal O$ itself is $\Pi^1_1$-complete, the canonical object at the base of the analytical hierarchy.

**Synthesis.** The arithmetical hierarchy is the foundational reason definability and computability are the same measurement taken twice: a set's quantifier-alternation depth in arithmetic is its depth on the jump ladder, and this is exactly Post's theorem — $\Sigma_{n+1}^0$ is c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)}$, $\Delta_{n+1}^0$ is $\le_T \emptyset^{(n)}$, $\emptyset^{(n)}$ is $\Sigma_n^0$-complete — so the iterated jump of [42.04.04] is the engine that climbs both ladders at once. Properness is the relativised halting diagonal of [42.04.02] run against the universal $\Sigma_n^0$ set at every level, which generalises the single $K \not\le_T \emptyset$ into an unbounded non-collapsing tower; putting these together, the hierarchy theorem and Post's theorem are one phenomenon seen syntactically and computationally. The arithmetical sets are precisely the first-order-definable subsets of $(\mathbb N, +, \cdot)$, and truth is dual to this in the sharpest way — not arithmetical at all, lying above every $\emptyset^{(n)}$ — which is the central insight separating Gödel incompleteness (provability is $\Sigma_1^0$, [42.01.09]) from Tarski undefinability (truth is non-arithmetical), and the bridge by which the $\Delta_2^0 = $ limit-computable sets feed the finite-injury approximations of the priority method [42.04.06]. The continuation past $\omega$ is the same construction transfinite: the jump iterated along Kleene's $\mathcal O$ up to $\omega_1^{CK}$ gives the hyperarithmetical $\Delta^1_1$ sets, and a function quantifier lifts to the analytical hierarchy where $\mathcal O$ is $\Pi^1_1$-complete — the arithmetical hierarchy is the first finite stretch of a ladder that, by the relativisation principle, repeats its own structure at every height.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (closure and the ascending inclusions).** *For each $n$: $\Sigma_n^0$ is closed under $\cup$, $\cap$, leading $\exists$, bounded quantification, and computable substitution; $R \in \Sigma_n^0 \iff \overline R \in \Pi_n^0$; and $\Sigma_n^0 \cup \Pi_n^0 \subseteq \Delta_{n+1}^0$.*

*Proof.* Complementation is by pushing negation through the prefix, flipping each quantifier and negating the computable matrix (which stays computable), turning the $\Sigma_n^0$ form into a $\Pi_n^0$ form. Leading $\exists$ closure is quantifier contraction (Exercise 3). Closure under $\cup, \cap$: prenex the two forms with disjoint bound variables and merge — for $\cap$ interleave matching quantifiers, for $\cup$ likewise, using that $\exists y\, P \vee \exists y'\, P' \equiv \exists \langle y,y'\rangle\,(P \vee P')$ and the matrix combination stays at the matrix level. Bounded quantification folds into the computable matrix since $\forall z < t$ and $\exists z < t$ of a computable relation are computable. Computable substitution $\bar x \mapsto h(\bar x)$ replaces the matrix $S(\bar x, \bar y)$ by the computable $S(h(\bar x), \bar y)$. For the inclusion, a $\Sigma_n^0$ relation $\exists y_1 \cdots S$ is also $\Delta_{n+1}^0$: it is $\Sigma_{n+1}^0$ (prefix a vacuous quantifier or absorb), and its complement is $\Pi_n^0 \subseteq \Pi_{n+1}^0 \subseteq \Sigma_{n+1}^0 \cap \Pi_{n+1}^0$... more directly $\Sigma_n^0 \subseteq \Sigma_{n+1}^0$ and $\Sigma_n^0 \subseteq \Pi_{n+1}^0$ by prefixing a dummy $\forall$, so $\Sigma_n^0 \subseteq \Delta_{n+1}^0$; symmetrically $\Pi_n^0 \subseteq \Delta_{n+1}^0$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (universal $\Sigma_n^0$ set and the hierarchy theorem).** *There is a $\Sigma_n^0$ relation $U_n(e,x)$ universal for $\Sigma_n^0$ sets, and the diagonal $D = \{x : U_n(x,x)\} \in \Sigma_n^0 \setminus \Pi_n^0$.*

*Proof.* Every $\Sigma_n^0$ set is $\{x : \exists y_1 \forall y_2 \cdots S_e(x, \bar y)\}$ where $S_e$ ranges over computable matrices, uniformly indexed by $e$ via the Kleene predicate: take $S_e(x, \bar y) \iff T(e, \langle x, \bar y\rangle, (\bar y)_{\text{last}})$ or any fixed universal computable relation. Set $U_n(e,x) \iff \exists y_1 \forall y_2 \cdots Q y_n\, S_e(x, \bar y)$; this is $\Sigma_n^0$ as a relation in $(e,x)$ and enumerates all $\Sigma_n^0$ sets as $e$ varies. If $D = \{x : U_n(x,x)\}$ were $\Pi_n^0$ then $\overline D \in \Sigma_n^0$, so $\overline D = \{x : U_n(e_0,x)\}$ for some $e_0$; then $e_0 \in \overline D \iff U_n(e_0,e_0) \iff e_0 \in D$, contradicting $\overline D = \mathbb N \setminus D$. So $D \notin \Pi_n^0$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Post's theorem, successor step).** *$A \in \Sigma_{n+1}^0 \iff A$ is c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)}$.*

*Proof.* ($\Rightarrow$) $A(x) \iff \exists y\, P(x,y)$ with $P \in \Pi_n^0$. By induction $\emptyset^{(n)}$ is $\Sigma_n^0$-complete, so $\overline P \le_m \emptyset^{(n)}$ (as $\overline P \in \Sigma_n^0$); thus $P$ is $\emptyset^{(n)}$-decidable (one oracle query to decide $\overline P$, then negate). Then $A = \mathrm{dom}$ of the $\emptyset^{(n)}$-partial-computable function searching $y$ until $P(x,y)$ holds, so $A$ is c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)}$. ($\Leftarrow$) $A = \{x : \exists s\, \Phi_{e,s}^{\emptyset^{(n)}}(x)\!\downarrow\}$. A converging oracle computation uses $\emptyset^{(n)}$ at finitely many points (use principle, [42.04.04]); replacing each positive query "$q \in \emptyset^{(n)}$" by its $\Sigma_n^0$ definition and each negative query by its $\Pi_n^0$ definition expresses "$\Phi_{e,s}^{\emptyset^{(n)}}(x)\!\downarrow$ via a length-$s$ run with this query pattern" as a finite Boolean combination of $\Sigma_n^0$ and $\Pi_n^0$ matrices, itself $\Sigma_n^0$ after contraction; the outer $\exists s$ keeps it $\Sigma_{n+1}^0$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 ($\emptyset^{(n)}$ is $\Sigma_n^0$-complete).** *$\emptyset^{(n)} \in \Sigma_n^0$ and every $A \in \Sigma_n^0$ satisfies $A \le_m \emptyset^{(n)}$.*

*Proof.* By induction. $n = 1$: $\emptyset' = K$ is $\Sigma_1^0$ (c.e.) and $\le_m$-complete for $\Sigma_1^0$ by the $m$-completeness of $K$ [42.04.03]. Step: assume $\emptyset^{(n)}$ is $\Sigma_n^0$-complete. By Proposition 3 the $\Sigma_{n+1}^0$ sets are exactly the c.e.-in-$\emptyset^{(n)}$ sets, and by jump theorem (ii) of [42.04.04] every set c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)}$ is $\le_m (\emptyset^{(n)})' = \emptyset^{(n+1)}$. Also $\emptyset^{(n+1)}$ is c.e. in $\emptyset^{(n)}$, hence $\Sigma_{n+1}^0$ by Proposition 3. So $\emptyset^{(n+1)}$ is $\Sigma_{n+1}^0$-complete. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (the limit lemma).** *$A \le_T \emptyset'$ iff $A(x) = \lim_s g(x,s)$ for a computable $g$ with $\{0,1\}$ values and the limit existing at each $x$.*

*Proof.* ($\Rightarrow$) With $A = \Phi_e^{\emptyset'}$ total and the canonical approximation $\emptyset'_s$, set $g(x,s)$ to the value of the length-$s$ run $\Phi_{e,s}^{\emptyset'_s}(x)$ if it converges with use $\le s$, else $0$. The true computation has finite use $u$ and converges by some stage; from the stage where $\emptyset'_s \restriction u = \emptyset' \restriction u$ and the run has converged, the use lemma [42.04.04] fixes $g(x,s) = A(x)$ permanently. ($\Leftarrow$) $A(x) = 1 \iff \exists s\, \forall t \ge s\, g(x,t) = 1$ is $\Sigma_2^0$, and the same for $A(x) = 0$, so $A \in \Delta_2^0$, hence $A \le_T \emptyset'$ by Post (Proposition 3 plus complementation). $\square$

**Proposition 6 ($\mathrm{Fin}$ is $\Sigma_2^0$-complete).** *$\mathrm{Fin} \in \Sigma_2^0$ and every $\Sigma_2^0$ set $\le_m \mathrm{Fin}$.*

*Proof.* Membership: $\mathrm{Fin}(e) \iff \exists n\, \forall x\, \forall s\, (T(e,x,s) \to x \le n)$, prefix $\exists\forall$, so $\Sigma_2^0$. Hardness: let $A \in \Sigma_2^0$, $A(x) \iff \exists n\, \forall y\, R(x,n,y)$ with $R$ computable; equivalently $A(x) \iff \neg\big(\forall n\, \exists y\, \neg R(x,n,y)\big)$. By s-m-n build $f$ with $\varphi_{f(x)}$ enumerating $\{n : \exists y\, \neg R(x,n,y)\}$: on a dovetailed search, put $n$ into $W_{f(x)}$ when a $y$ with $\neg R(x,n,y)$ appears. Then $W_{f(x)}$ is finite iff only finitely many $n$ have such a $y$ iff some tail of $n$ has $\forall y\, R(x,n,y)$ — but to align with $A$ exactly, arrange instead $\varphi_{f(x)}$ to enumerate a marker for each $n$ failing $\forall y\, R(x,n,y)$ up to the least witnessing $n^*$, so $W_{f(x)}$ is finite iff $\exists n\, \forall y\, R(x,n,y)$, i.e. iff $A(x)$. Thus $x \in A \iff f(x) \in \mathrm{Fin}$, giving $A \le_m \mathrm{Fin}$. Hence $\mathrm{Fin}$ is $\Sigma_2^0$-complete. $\square$

**Proposition 7 (Tarski: truth is not arithmetical).** *$\mathrm{Th}(\mathbb N) = \{\ulcorner\sigma\urcorner : \mathbb N \models \sigma\}$ is not $\Sigma_n^0$ for any $n$.*

*Proof.* Suppose $\mathrm{Th}(\mathbb N)$ were arithmetical, so definable by an arithmetical formula $\mathrm{Tr}(v)$ with $\mathbb N \models \mathrm{Tr}(\ulcorner\sigma\urcorner) \iff \mathbb N \models \sigma$. By the fixed-point lemma [42.01.09] applied to $\neg\mathrm{Tr}(v)$ there is a sentence $\lambda$ with $\mathbb N \models \lambda \iff \mathbb N \models \neg\mathrm{Tr}(\ulcorner\lambda\urcorner)$. Then $\mathbb N \models \lambda \iff \mathbb N \models \neg\mathrm{Tr}(\ulcorner\lambda\urcorner) \iff \mathbb N \not\models \mathrm{Tr}(\ulcorner\lambda\urcorner) \iff \mathbb N \not\models \lambda$, a contradiction. So no arithmetical truth predicate exists, and since every arithmetical set is $\Sigma_n^0$ for some fixed $n$, $\mathrm{Th}(\mathbb N)$ is in no $\Sigma_n^0$. (That it computes every $\emptyset^{(n)}$ — decide a $\Sigma_n^0$ sentence by truth — places it strictly above each level.) $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The arithmetical hierarchy was isolated by Stephen Kleene in the early 1940s, in his work on the predicates definable in arithmetic by alternating number quantifiers over a recursive matrix, and independently approached by Andrzej Mostowski, after whom the classification is sometimes called the Kleene-Mostowski hierarchy [ref: TODO_REF Rogers Ch. 14]. Kleene proved the enumeration and hierarchy theorems showing the levels are distinct and introduced the iterated jump; the identification of the syntactic hierarchy with the jump ladder is **Post's theorem**, announced in Emil Post's 1948 abstract "Degrees of recursive unsolvability" and developed in the Post-Kleene circle, tying definability complexity to the degrees of unsolvability of [42.04.04]. Alfred Tarski's 1933 theorem on the undefinability of truth — that the set of true arithmetic sentences is not arithmetically definable — predates the hierarchy but receives its sharpest recursion-theoretic form here, as the statement that $\mathrm{Th}(\mathbb N)$ lies above every $\emptyset^{(n)}$.

The continuation past the finite levels is Kleene's again: his 1955 papers on the hyperarithmetical hierarchy and the system of notations $\mathcal O$ for the recursive ordinals extended the jump iteration transfinitely to $\omega_1^{CK}$, and the Kleene-Souslin theorem identified the hyperarithmetical sets with $\Delta^1_1$, the effective analogue of the Borel sets. The analytical hierarchy, with quantifiers over functions, was developed by Kleene and Mostowski and refined through the 1960s by the work of Spector, Gandy, and Addison connecting effective descriptive set theory to the classical Borel and projective hierarchies; $\mathcal O$ itself is $\Pi^1_1$-complete. The arithmetical hierarchy thus stands at the junction of three traditions: the recursion theory of Post and Kleene, the definability theory of Tarski and Mostowski, and the descriptive set theory of Souslin and Luzin, with Post's theorem the hinge that makes quantifier complexity and computational difficulty a single graded invariant.
