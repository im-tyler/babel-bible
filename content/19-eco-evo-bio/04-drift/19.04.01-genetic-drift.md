## Intuition [Beginner]

Genetic drift is random change in allele frequencies due to chance events in reproduction. In any population, not every individual passes on their genes equally — some have more offspring by luck, not because their genes are better. This randomness is **genetic drift**.

Drift is strongest in small populations. Imagine a population of just 10 individuals, where allele A is at 50% frequency (5 copies). By chance, the individuals carrying A might have fewer offspring than those carrying a — not because A is worse, but simply because of random variation in reproductive success. In the next generation, A might drop to 30% or rise to 60%. Over many generations, this random fluctuation can drive an allele to fixation (100% frequency) or loss (0%), even without any selective advantage or disadvantage.

In a large population (say, 1 million individuals), the same random fluctuations occur, but their effect on allele frequency is much smaller. The law of large numbers ensures that the allele frequency stays close to its expected value. Drift is therefore inversely proportional to population size.

The key insight: drift is evolution by chance, not by adaptation. A neutral allele (one with no fitness effect) can spread to fixation purely by drift, and a slightly beneficial allele can be lost by drift if the population is small enough. The relative strength of selection versus drift is captured by the product $Ns$ (population size times selection coefficient): when $Ns$ is much greater than 1, selection dominates; when $Ns$ is much less than 1, drift dominates.

Some replicates fix the A allele quickly; others lose it. The average time to fixation or loss depends on population size: in a population of $2N$ gene copies, the expected time to fixation of a neutral allele starting at frequency $p$ is approximately $-4N[p \ln p + (1-p) \ln(1-p)]$ generations.

## Worked example [Beginner]

In a population of $N = 50$ diploid individuals, a new neutral mutation arises as a single copy. What is the probability that it eventually fixes?

The fixation probability of a single new neutral allele in a diploid population of size $N$ is:

$$
P_{\text{fix}} = \frac{1}{2N}.
$$

For $N = 50$: $P_{\text{fix}} = 1/(2 \times 50) = 1/100 = 1\%$.

There is a 99% chance the new allele is lost by drift. But if it does fix, it takes on average about $4N = 200$ generations to do so. This result applies to every new neutral mutation that arises — most are lost, but the occasional one drifts to fixation purely by chance.

**Exercise (medium, short answer).**

Explain the difference between a population bottleneck and a founder effect. How do both relate to genetic drift?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Both reduce population size, but for different reasons. What triggers each?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A **population bottleneck** occurs when a large population is drastically reduced in size (e.g., by a natural disaster, epidemic, or habitat destruction). The survivors carry only a sample of the original genetic diversity, and drift is strong in the small surviving population. Alleles that were rare in the original population may be lost or may drift to higher frequency by chance.

A **founder effect** occurs when a small number of individuals colonise a new habitat (found a new population). The founders carry only a subset of the source population's genetic diversity. The new population starts with allele frequencies that may differ from the source population purely by sampling error. Both bottleneck and founder effects are special cases of drift: they are instances where population size is reduced, amplifying the effect of random sampling.

</details>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### The Wright-Fisher model

The **Wright-Fisher model** is the standard mathematical model of genetic drift. Consider a diploid population of constant size $N$ (so $2N$ gene copies at a locus). Let $X_t$ be the number of copies of allele A in generation $t$. The model assumes:

1. Generations are non-overlapping (discrete time).
2. Each gene copy in generation $t+1$ is drawn independently and uniformly at random from the $2N$ copies in generation $t$ (with replacement, multinomial sampling).

Under these assumptions, $X_{t+1}$ given $X_t = i$ follows a **binomial distribution**:

$$
X_{t+1} \sim \text{Binomial}(2N, p_t),
$$

where $p_t = i/(2N)$ is the allele frequency in generation $t$. The allele frequency in the next generation is:

$$
p_{t+1} = X_{t+1}/(2N).
$$

The **expected value** is $E[p_{t+1}] = p_t$ (drift has no directional bias). The **variance** is $\text{Var}(p_{t+1}) = p_t(1-p_t)/(2N)$. This variance — the magnitude of random fluctuation per generation — is inversely proportional to population size.

### Fixation probability

For a neutral allele at initial frequency $p_0$ in a Wright-Fisher population of size $N$:

$$
P_{\text{fix}} = p_0.
$$

For a new mutation (initial frequency $p_0 = 1/(2N)$):

$$
P_{\text{fix}} = \frac{1}{2N}.
$$

This result follows from the **martingale property** of neutral drift: the expected allele frequency at any future time equals the current frequency.

### Effective population size

The **effective population size** $N_e$ is the size of an ideal Wright-Fisher population that would produce the same rate of genetic drift as the actual population. Deviations from the ideal (unequal sex ratio, variation in offspring number, population size fluctuations) reduce $N_e$ below the census size $N$:

$$
N_e \leq N.
$$

For example, in a population with unequal sex ratio ($N_m$ males, $N_f$ females):

$$
N_e = \frac{4 N_m N_f}{N_m + N_f}.
$$

If there are 10 males and 990 females ($N = 1000$), $N_e = 4 \times 10 \times 990 / 1000 = 39.6$. The effective population size is only 40 despite 1000 individuals, because the few males are the genetic bottleneck.

### Time to fixation

The expected time to fixation (conditional on fixation occurring) for a neutral allele at initial frequency $p_0$ in a Wright-Fisher population is:

$$
T_{\text{fix}}(p_0) = -4N_e \left[\frac{(1-p_0)}{p_0} \ln(1-p_0)\right].
$$

For a new mutation ($p_0 = 1/(2N)$), $T_{\text{fix}} \approx 4N_e$ generations.

### The coalescent

The **coalescent** (Kingman, 1982) traces the genealogy of a sample of genes backward in time. Two gene copies **coalesce** (find their most recent common ancestor) at rate $1/(2N_e)$ per generation. For a sample of $k$ lineages, the time until the next coalescent event is exponentially distributed with rate $\binom{k}{2}/(2N_e)$. The total time to the most recent common ancestor (TMRCA) of the entire sample is:

$$
E[T_{\text{MRCA}}] = 2N_e \sum_{i=1}^{n-1} \frac{1}{i} \approx 2N_e \ln(n),
$$

where $n$ is the sample size.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Fixation probability of a new neutral allele).** *In the Wright-Fisher model with population size $N$, the probability that a newly arisen neutral allele present in a single copy eventually reaches fixation is $1/(2N)$.*

**Proof.** Let $p_0 = 1/(2N)$ be the initial frequency of the new allele. The Wright-Fisher model for a neutral allele is a martingale: $E[p_{t+1} | p_t] = p_t$. By the martingale stopping theorem, the expected frequency at the time of absorption (fixation or loss) equals the initial frequency:

$$
E[p_{\infty}] = p_0.
$$

At absorption, $p_{\infty}$ is either 1 (fixation) or 0 (loss). Let $P_{\text{fix}}$ be the probability of fixation. Then:

$$
E[p_{\infty}] = P_{\text{fix}} \times 1 + (1 - P_{\text{fix}}) \times 0 = P_{\text{fix}}.
$$

Therefore $P_{\text{fix}} = p_0 = 1/(2N)$. $\square$

This result is remarkable for its generality: it holds regardless of the specific path the allele takes, and it makes no assumptions about the selective environment (beyond neutrality). Every new neutral mutation in a population of $2N$ gene copies has exactly one chance in $2N$ of ultimately fixing.

**Exercise 4 (medium, short answer).**

The cheetah (*Acinonyx jubatus*) has extremely low genetic diversity — far less than other large cats. Explain how a population bottleneck could produce this pattern.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

During a bottleneck, many alleles are lost. After the bottleneck, does the population recover its lost diversity?

Many alleles — especially rare ones — are lost because they happened not to be present in the survivors. </details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short answer).**

The neutral theory of molecular evolution (Kimura, 1968) proposes that most molecular variation within and between species is due to drift acting on neutral mutations rather than selection. What two predictions of the neutral theory distinguish it from selectionist alternatives?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The neutral theory makes quantitative predictions about the rate of molecular evolution and the relationship between polymorphism and divergence. What are they?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(1) **Rate of molecular evolution = mutation rate.** The neutral theory predicts that the rate of substitution (fixation of new alleles) at neutral sites equals the mutation rate $\mu$, independent of population size. This is because: substitution rate = (number of new mutations per generation) times (fixation probability) = $2N\mu \times 1/(2N) = \mu$. If most substitutions are driven by positive selection, the rate would depend on the selective advantage and the rate of environmental change, not just the mutation rate.

(2) **Polymorphism is proportional to $N_e \mu$.** Under the neutral theory, the expected heterozygosity at a neutral locus is $\theta = 4N_e\mu$. This predicts that species with larger effective population sizes should have more genetic diversity. Data broadly support this (e.g., marine species with large $N_e$ have higher diversity than terrestrial species with small $N_e$), though there are notable exceptions that require selectionist explanations.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

For the Wright-Fisher model with population size $N$ and initial frequency $p_0 = 0.5$, derive the expected heterozygosity after $t$ generations. The heterozygosity at time $t$ is $H_t = 2p_t(1-p_t)$. Show that $E[H_t] = H_0(1 - 1/(2N))^t$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $E[p_{t+1}(1-p_{t+1}) | p_t]$ using the variance of the binomial sampling.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$p_{t+1} \sim p_t + \epsilon$ where $E[\epsilon | p_t] = 0$ and $\text{Var}(\epsilon | p_t) = p_t(1-p_t)/(2N)$. Then:

$$
E[p_{t+1}(1-p_{t+1}) | p_t] = E[(p_t + \epsilon)(1 - p_t - \epsilon) | p_t] = p_t(1-p_t) - E[\epsilon^2 | p_t].
$$

Since $E[\epsilon^2 | p_t] = \text{Var}(\epsilon | p_t) = p_t(1-p_t)/(2N)$:

$$
E[H_{t+1} | p_t] = H_t - \frac{H_t}{2N} = H_t\left(1 - \frac{1}{2N}\right).
$$

Taking expectations and iterating: $E[H_t] = H_0(1 - 1/(2N))^t$. Heterozygosity decays exponentially at rate $1/(2N)$ per generation. In the limit $t \to \infty$, heterozygosity goes to zero — the population eventually becomes monomorphic (fixed for one allele or the other). The rate of decay is faster in smaller populations.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, short answer).**

Explain why the coalescent is a more powerful framework for analysing DNA sequence data than the classical Wright-Fisher forward-time model.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Wright-Fisher model simulates the entire population forward in time. The coalescent traces only the sampled lineages backward. Which is more efficient?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The coalescent is more efficient for two reasons. (1) **Computational efficiency**: the Wright-Fisher model tracks all $2N$ gene copies in the population for every generation, even though a DNA sequence sample typically contains only $n = 10-100$ individuals. The coalescent traces only the $n$ sampled lineages backward in time, ignoring the vast majority of the population. (2) **Analytical tractability**: the coalescent produces exact probability distributions for genealogical quantities (branch lengths, tree shape, time to MRCA) that can be used to compute likelihoods for statistical inference. Modern population genetics uses coalescent-based methods to estimate population sizes, migration rates, and divergence times from sequence data — analyses that would be impractical with the forward-time model.

</details>

</aside>

## The coalescent and modern population genetics [Master]

The coalescent has become the central tool of modern population genetics. Extensions include:

- **Structured coalescent**: models migration between subpopulations, where lineages can move between demes as they are traced backward. Coalescence occurs only when two lineages are in the same deme.
- **Coalescent with recombination**: traces the ancestry of multiple linked loci, producing an **ancestral recombination graph (ARG)** rather than a simple tree. This is the foundation for haplotype-based inference methods.
- **Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC)**: uses coalescent simulations to approximate the likelihood of observed data under complex demographic models, bypassing the need for exact likelihood calculations.
- **Multiple-merger coalescents**: models with large variance in offspring number (e.g., sweepstakes reproduction in marine species), where a single individual can contribute a large fraction of the next generation, producing multiple simultaneous coalescences.

The **neutrality test** framework uses coalescent predictions to detect selection. Key tests include: Tajima's D (comparing two estimators of $\theta$ that should be equal under neutrality), Fu and Li's test (comparing total mutations to external branch mutations), and the HKA test (comparing polymorphism to divergence at multiple loci). Significant departures from neutral expectations suggest selection.

## Connections [Master]

- **Mendelian genetics [19.01.01]** provides the locus-allele framework. Drift changes allele frequencies through random sampling of Mendelian inheritance events.

- **Natural selection [19.03.01]** and drift are the two main forces changing allele frequencies. The product $Ns$ determines which dominates. This unit provides the drift side of that balance.

- **Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium [19.02.01]** assumes infinite population size. Drift is the deviation from Hardy-Weinberg expectations in finite populations.

- **Molecular biology [17.05.01]** provides the DNA sequence data that the neutral theory and coalescent analysis interpret. Without molecular data, the neutral theory could not be tested.

- **Probability theory [02.13.01]** underpins the entire mathematical framework: the Wright-Fisher model is a Markov chain, the coalescent is a continuous-time stochastic process, and fixation probabilities are derived from martingale theory.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Sewall Wright introduced genetic drift (which he called "random drift" or the "Sewall Wright effect") in his 1931 paper *Evolution in Mendelian Populations*. Wright saw drift as one component of his **shifting balance theory** of evolution: populations explore an adaptive landscape through drift (which allows them to cross fitness valleys that selection alone cannot cross), then selection drives them up new fitness peaks, and interdemic selection spreads the new peak adaptation to other populations. This theory was influential but remains controversial.

Motoo Kimura's **neutral theory of molecular evolution** (1968) was the most consequential application of drift. Kimura argued that the observed rate of molecular evolution (roughly one substitution per generation per genome) was too high to be explained by positive selection, because the selective advantage required would impose an unrealistic genetic load. The neutral theory resolved this: if most substitutions are neutral, the rate equals the mutation rate, and no genetic load is incurred. The neutral theory does not claim that selection is unimportant — only that most molecular variation is neutral. Selection is still responsible for adaptive evolution.

John Kingman's coalescent (1982) unified the retrospective (backward-in-time) approach to population genetics. The coalescent provided a mathematically rigorous framework for analysing genealogies, which became the basis for modern statistical methods in population genetics (Bayesian skyline plots, demographic inference, species delimitation).

Philosophically, drift raises the question of **contingency in evolution**: if evolution is partly driven by random events, then replaying the tape of life would produce different outcomes. Gould's *Wonderful Life* (1989) argued that contingency is a dominant theme; Conway Morris's *Life's Solution* (2003) argued that convergence dominates. The truth likely involves both: drift creates contingency at the molecular level, while selection drives convergence at the phenotypic level when the same adaptive problems are encountered independently.
