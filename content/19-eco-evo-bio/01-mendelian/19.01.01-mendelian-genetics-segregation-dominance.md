## Intuition [Beginner]

Gregor Mendel, an Augustinian friar working in Brno (now the Czech Republic), discovered the fundamental rules of inheritance by breeding pea plants in the 1850s and 1860s. His experiments revealed that inheritance is **particulate** — traits are passed on by discrete units (now called **genes**) that do not blend together. This was a revolutionary idea, because most biologists at the time believed in blending inheritance.

Mendel's first key insight was the **law of segregation**. Every organism carries two copies of each gene (one from each parent). These two copies (called **alleles**) may be the same or different. When the organism produces gametes (sperm or eggs), the two alleles separate — each gamete receives exactly one. At fertilisation, the offspring receives one allele from each parent, restoring the pair.

His second key insight was the concept of **dominance**. When an individual carries two different alleles (e.g., Aa), one allele (A, the **dominant** allele) may mask the effect of the other (a, the **recessive** allele). The individual's **phenotype** (observable trait) shows the dominant form, even though the **genotype** (genetic makeup) contains both alleles.

Mendel's law of **independent assortment** states that alleles of different genes are distributed to gametes independently of one another. This holds when the genes are on different chromosomes (or far apart on the same chromosome), so the inheritance of one trait does not influence the inheritance of another.

## Visual [Beginner]

A **Punnett square** is a grid that predicts the genotypes of offspring from a given cross. For a monohybrid cross between two heterozygotes (Aa x Aa), the Punnett square is:

![Punnett square for an Aa x Aa monohybrid cross. The four cells show genotypes: AA, Aa, aA, aa in a 1:2:1 ratio. Phenotypes show a 3:1 ratio of dominant to recessive.](/img/19.01.01-punnett-square-placeholder.svg)

The four equally likely outcomes are: AA (1/4), Aa (1/4), aA (1/4), aa (1/4). Since Aa and aA are genetically identical, the genotype ratio is 1 AA : 2 Aa : 1 aa. If A is dominant, the phenotype ratio is 3 dominant : 1 recessive.

## Worked example [Beginner]

In a dihybrid cross (AaBb x AaBb), both parents are heterozygous for two independently assorting genes. Predict the phenotype ratio and calculate expected numbers from 160 offspring.

By Mendel's law of independent assortment, each gene segregates independently. For gene A alone (Aa x Aa): the phenotype ratio is 3 dominant (A_) : 1 recessive (aa). For gene B alone (Bb x Bb): the ratio is 3 dominant (B_) : 1 recessive (bb).

Combining the two genes: $3/4 \times 3/4 = 9/16$ show both dominant phenotypes (A_B_). $3/4 \times 1/4 = 3/16$ show A dominant, b recessive (A_bb). $1/4 \times 3/4 = 3/16$ show a recessive, B dominant (aaB_). $1/4 \times 1/4 = 1/16$ show both recessive phenotypes (aabb).

The **9:3:3:1 ratio**. From 160 offspring: 90, 30, 30, and 10 expected respectively.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Mendel's laws

**First law (segregation):** A diploid individual carries two alleles at each autosomal locus. During gamete formation, the two alleles segregate so that each gamete carries exactly one. Formally, for a parent with genotype $A_1A_2$, the gametes are $A_1$ and $A_2$ each with probability 1/2.

**Second law (independent assortment):** Alleles at different loci segregate independently into gametes. For a dihybrid $A_1A_2B_1B_2$ with the two loci on different chromosomes, the four gamete types ($A_1B_1$, $A_1B_2$, $A_2B_1$, $A_2B_2$) are produced in equal frequency (1/4 each). Independent assortment fails for linked loci on the same chromosome; recombination between them produces a proportion of non-parental gamete types.

### Extensions to Mendel

**Incomplete dominance:** The heterozygote shows an intermediate phenotype. Example: snapdragons — RR (red) x rr (white) produces Rr (pink) in the F1, and the F2 ratio is 1 red : 2 pink : 1 white.

**Codominance:** Both alleles are fully expressed in the heterozygote. Example: human ABO blood groups — IA and IB alleles are codominant (IAIB genotype produces AB blood type), while both are dominant to i (which produces type O).

**Epistasis:** One gene masks or modifies the effect of another gene. In **recessive epistasis**, a homozygous recessive genotype at one locus (e.g., cc) prevents the expression of alleles at a second locus (e.g., B/b), regardless of the genotype at that second locus. The dihybrid ratio is modified from 9:3:3:1 to 9:3:4 (if cc masks B/b).

**Pleiotropy:** One gene affects multiple phenotypic traits. Example: the gene causing Marfan syndrome affects connective tissue throughout the body, producing tall stature, long limbs, lens dislocation, and aortic aneurysms from a single mutation.

**Polygenic inheritance:** Multiple genes contribute additively to a single quantitative trait (e.g., height, skin colour). The distribution of phenotypes in the population approaches a normal (Gaussian) distribution as the number of contributing loci increases.

### Chi-square test

The chi-square ($\chi^2$) test evaluates whether observed data are consistent with predicted Mendelian ratios:

$$
\chi^2 = \sum \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i},
$$

where $O_i$ are observed counts and $E_i$ are expected counts for each class $i$. The degrees of freedom are the number of classes minus one. A significant $\chi^2$ value (exceeding the critical value for the given degrees of freedom at $p = 0.05$) suggests the observed data deviate from the predicted ratio, indicating either non-Mendelian inheritance or experimental sampling effects.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Mendel's 9:3:3:1 ratio for a dihybrid cross with independent assortment).** *For a cross between two double heterozygotes AaBb x AaBb, where A is dominant to a, B is dominant to b, and the two loci assort independently, the phenotype ratio among offspring is 9 A_B_ : 3 A_bb : 3 aaB_ : 1 aabb.*

**Proof.** By the law of independent assortment, the outcome at each locus is independent. For locus A alone: Aa x Aa gives AA (1/4), Aa (1/2), aa (1/4). The dominant phenotype (A_) has probability 3/4; the recessive phenotype (aa) has probability 1/4. Similarly for locus B: B_ has probability 3/4; bb has probability 1/4.

By independence: $P(A\_B\_) = (3/4)(3/4) = 9/16$. $P(A\_bb) = (3/4)(1/4) = 3/16$. $P(aaB\_) = (1/4)(3/4) = 3/16$. $P(aabb) = (1/4)(1/4) = 1/16$. $\square$

The ratio 9:3:3:1 is a diagnostic signature of two independently assorting loci with complete dominance at each. Deviations from this ratio are informative: a 9:7 ratio suggests **complementary gene interaction** (recessive epistasis where both genes are needed); a 9:3:4 ratio suggests a **recessive epistatic** interaction at one locus; a 12:3:1 ratio suggests **dominant epistasis**.

In a cross Aa x Aa producing 480 offspring, how many are expected to have each genotype (AA, Aa, aa)?

The expected genotype ratio is 1:2:1.

**Exercise 2 (medium, numeric).**

A test cross of a plant with purple flowers (dominant) with a white-flowered plant (recessive, aa) produces 52 purple and 48 white offspring. Use a chi-square test to determine whether the unknown parent is likely AA or Aa. Use 1 degree of freedom, critical value 3.84 at $p = 0.05$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If the parent is Aa, the expected ratio is 1:1. If AA, all offspring should be purple.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If the parent is AA: all 100 offspring should be purple. Observed (52 purple, 48 white) strongly contradicts this. If the parent is Aa: expected 50 purple, 50 white.

$\chi^2 = (52-50)^2/50 + (48-50)^2/50 = 4/50 + 4/50 = 0.16$. This is well below the critical value of 3.84, so the data are consistent with the Aa hypothesis. The parent is heterozygous.

</details>

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).**

In a cross between two plants heterozygous for incompletely dominant flower colour (Rr x Rr, where RR = red, Rr = pink, rr = white), 200 offspring are produced. How many are expected to be pink?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

With incomplete dominance, the genotype ratio equals the phenotype ratio.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**100.** The genotype ratio is 1 RR : 2 Rr : 1 rr. Rr (pink) constitutes 1/2 of the offspring. $200 \times 1/2 = 100$ pink offspring.

**Exercise 4 (medium, short answer).**

In Labrador retrievers, coat colour is determined by two genes: B (black pigment) is dominant to b (brown), and E (pigment deposition) is dominant to e (no deposition, producing yellow). A yellow Labrador with the genotype Bbee is crossed with a brown Labrador (bbEe). What phenotypes and proportions are expected in the offspring?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

This is an example of epistasis. The ee genotype at the E locus masks the B/b genotype. Set up the cross Bbee x bbEe.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Gametes from Bbee: Be, bE, be, bE (wait — Bbee gametes: Be, be, Be, be — no, let me be careful). Bbee can produce: Be, be. bbEe can produce: bE, be. Cross: Be x bE = BbEe (black), Be x be = Bbee (yellow), be x bE = bbEe (brown), be x be = bbee (yellow). Ratio: 1 black : 1 brown : 2 yellow. The ee genotype is epistatic to B/b, producing yellow regardless of the pigment gene.

</details>

**Exercise 5 (hard, numeric).**

In a trihybrid cross AaBbCc x AaBbCc with complete dominance at all three loci and independent assortment, what fraction of offspring display the dominant phenotype at all three loci?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The probability of the dominant phenotype at each locus is 3/4. Multiply across three independent loci.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**27/64.** At each locus, the probability of the dominant phenotype (A_, B_, C_) is 3/4. By independence: $(3/4)^3 = 27/64$. This generalises: for $n$ independently assorting loci with complete dominance, the fraction showing all dominant phenotypes is $(3/4)^n$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short answer).**

In sweet peas, two genes (C and P) interact to produce purple flowers through complementary gene interaction. Both C_ and P_ must be present for purple flowers; cc or pp produces white flowers. A dihybrid cross CcPp x CcPp produces what phenotype ratio? Explain why this differs from 9:3:3:1.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Count how many of the 16 genotype classes in a dihybrid cross have at least one dominant allele at both loci.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The ratio is **9 purple : 7 white**. Of the 16 equally likely genotypes from a CcPp x CcPp cross, 9 have at least one C and at least one P allele (C_P_): 9/16 are purple. The remaining 7 are: C_pp (3/16), ccP_ (3/16), and ccpp (1/16) — all white because they lack at least one functional gene. The 9:7 ratio results from **complementary gene interaction**: both genes are needed for the trait, so loss of either one produces the same (white) phenotype. This collapses two of the usual four classes into one, converting 9:3:3:1 into 9:(3+3+1) = 9:7.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Two loci are linked on the same chromosome with recombination frequency $r = 0.2$ (20 map units). A dihybrid in coupling (AB/ab) is test-crossed with ab/ab. Derive the expected genotype frequencies among offspring.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Parental gametes (AB, ab) are produced at frequency $(1-r)/2$ each. Recombinant gametes (Ab, aB) are produced at frequency $r/2$ each.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The dihybrid AB/ab produces gametes: AB at frequency $(1-0.2)/2 = 0.4$, ab at frequency 0.4, Ab at frequency $0.2/2 = 0.1$, aB at frequency 0.1. The test-cross parent ab/ab produces only ab gametes. Offspring genotypes (and their frequencies): AB/ab = 0.4, ab/ab = 0.4, Ab/ab = 0.1, aB/ab = 0.1. The parental types (AB/ab and ab/ab) total 80%; the recombinant types (Ab/ab and aB/ab) total 20%, matching the recombination frequency. This is the basis for genetic mapping: recombination frequency directly estimates map distance between loci.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, short answer).**

In humans, ABO blood type is determined by three alleles: $I^A$ and $I^B$ are codominant, and $i$ is recessive to both. A person with blood type A marries a person with blood type B. They have a child with blood type O. What are the parents' genotypes?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Blood type O requires genotype $ii$. The child must have received an $i$ allele from each parent.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Both parents must be heterozygous: $I^Ai$ (type A parent) and $I^Bi$ (type B parent). Only then can both pass the $i$ allele to produce an $ii$ child (type O). If the type A parent were $I^AI^A$, no $i$ allele could be transmitted. If the type B parent were $I^BI^B$, the same. The presence of a type O child proves both parents carry the $i$ allele.

</details>

## Mendelian genetics in modern context [Master]

The rediscovery of Mendel's laws in 1900 (by de Vries, Correns, and Tschermak independently) is one of the most dramatic episodes in the history of science. Mendel's 1866 paper had been cited only a handful of times before 1900. The simultaneous rediscovery triggered a revolution in biology, leading to the chromosome theory of inheritance (Sutton and Boveri, 1902-1903), the concept of genetic linkage (Morgan, 1910), and eventually the molecular identification of DNA as the genetic material.

Modern quantitative genetics builds on Mendel's insight that inheritance is particulate. The **infinitesimal model** (Fisher, 1918) assumes that quantitative traits are controlled by a very large number of Mendelian loci, each with a small additive effect. Under this model, the phenotype distribution in the offspring generation is a normal distribution whose mean and variance can be predicted from the parental generation — a result that bridges Mendelian genetics with the biometrical approach of Galton and Pearson.

## Connections [Master]

- **DNA and gene expression [17.05.01]** provides the molecular basis for Mendel's "factors." Genes are segments of DNA, and alleles are variant forms of those segments. Dominance arises when one allele produces a functional protein and the other does not (or produces a non-functional version).

- **Gene regulation [17.06.01]** explains many extensions to Mendelian ratios. Epistasis occurs when one gene's product regulates another gene's expression. Pleiotropy arises when a gene is expressed in multiple tissues.

- **Cell division and meiosis [17.08.01]** is the cellular mechanism that enforces Mendel's laws. Segregation occurs because homologous chromosomes separate at meiosis I. Independent assortment occurs because different chromosome pairs orient independently on the meiotic spindle.

- **Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium [19.02.01]** extends Mendelian genotype ratios to entire populations, providing the null model against which evolutionary forces (selection, drift, migration, mutation) are detected.

- **Natural selection [19.03.01]** acts on the phenotypic variation produced by Mendelian inheritance, changing allele frequencies in populations over generations.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Mendel's work is a landmark of scientific methodology. He chose pea plants because they had distinct, easy-to-classify traits (round vs wrinkled seeds, yellow vs green pods, tall vs short stems), could be self-fertilised or cross-fertilised under controlled conditions, and produced large numbers of offspring. His use of **quantitative counting** — recording the exact numbers of each phenotype in each generation — was unprecedented in biology and allowed him to recognise the mathematical ratios that led to his laws.

Mendel's results were not immediately accepted. When he presented his work to the Natural History Society of Brunn in 1865, the audience is reported to have responded with polite silence. The paper was published in the society's proceedings in 1866 but went largely unnoticed until 1900. The reasons for this neglect are debated: perhaps the mathematical approach was unfamiliar to biologists, or perhaps the prevailing emphasis on Darwinian continuous variation made discrete inheritance seem irrelevant.

Fisher (1936) famously analysed Mendel's data and argued that the fit to expected ratios was suspiciously close — too good to be true, suggesting that Mendel (or an assistant) may have stopped counting when the results looked right, or selectively reported the best-fitting experiments. This controversy remains unresolved but does not diminish the correctness of Mendel's conclusions.

Philosophically, Mendel's laws embody **reductionism in biology**: the behaviour of whole organisms (trait inheritance) is explained by the properties of underlying units (genes/alleles). The success of this reductionist programme — from Mendel's factors to Watson and Crick's double helix to modern genomics — is one of the great achievements of science.
