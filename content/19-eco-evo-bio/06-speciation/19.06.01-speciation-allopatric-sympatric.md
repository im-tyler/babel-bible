## Intuition [Beginner]

A **species** is a group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring. This is the **biological species concept**. Members of the same species share a gene pool — they can exchange genes through mating. Members of different species cannot (or do not) interbreed successfully.

**Speciation** is the process by which one species splits into two. The key requirement is **reproductive isolation** — something must prevent gene flow between two groups so they can evolve independently. Once isolated, the groups accumulate genetic differences through natural selection, genetic drift, and mutation. Eventually, they become so different that they can no longer interbreed even if they come back into contact.

The most common route is **allopatric speciation** ("other homeland"): a geographic barrier splits a population into two physically separated groups. A mountain range, a river, or an ocean island can divide a species. With no gene flow between them, each group evolves independently. Given enough time, they become distinct species.

**Sympatric speciation** ("same homeland") is rarer but possible. Here, a new species arises within the same geographic area as the parent species, without a physical barrier. This can happen through ecological specialisation (different food sources or habitats) or through polyploidy (a doubling of chromosome number that immediately creates reproductive isolation, common in plants).

## Visual [Beginner]

Imagine a population of beetles spread across a valley. A new river forms, splitting the population into east and west groups.

![Allopatric speciation diagram: a continuous population split by a geographic barrier (river). The two isolated populations diverge over time through different selection pressures and drift. When the barrier is removed, they can no longer interbreed — they are separate species.](/img/19.06.01-allopatric-speciation-placeholder.svg)

On the east side, the environment favours dark-coloured beetles (dark soil). On the west side, light-coloured beetles are favoured (sandy soil). Over thousands of generations, the two populations diverge in colour, size, and mating behaviour. Even if the river dries up and the populations meet again, they may not interbreed — they have become separate species.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Darwin's finches on the Galapagos Islands are a textbook case of speciation. The 13 species of finches all descend from a single ancestral species that colonised the islands from mainland South America.

The process worked as follows. A small group of finches reached one island. Some individuals dispersed to other islands. Because each island had different food sources (large hard seeds, small soft seeds, insects, cactus flowers), natural selection favoured different beak shapes on each island.

On islands with large hard seeds, birds with larger, stronger beaks survived better. On islands with insects, birds with narrow, pointed beaks were favoured. Over many generations, the isolated populations on each island diverged in beak size and shape.

When individuals from different islands occasionally came into contact, they often did not interbreed because their beak differences were associated with different mating songs. The result: 13 species, each specialised for a different food source, all from one common ancestor. This is **adaptive radiation** — rapid speciation driven by ecological opportunity.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### The biological species concept

Under the biological species concept (Mayr, 1942), species are groups of actually or potentially interbreeding natural populations that are **reproductively isolated** from other such groups. Reproductive isolation (RI) is measured as the proportion of gene flow prevented relative to random mating:

$$
RI = 1 - \frac{H}{C},
$$

where $H$ is the observed number of hybrids and $C$ is the expected number under random mating. $RI = 1$ means complete isolation; $RI = 0$ means no isolation.

### Pre-zygotic barriers

These prevent hybrid formation:

- **Temporal isolation**: breeding at different times
- **Ecological (habitat) isolation**: occupying different habitats
- **Behavioural isolation**: different courtship signals
- **Mechanical isolation**: incompatible reproductive structures
- **Gametic isolation**: sperm-egg incompatibility

### Post-zygotic barriers

These reduce hybrid fitness:

- **Hybrid inviability**: $F_1$ zygotes fail to develop or survive poorly
- **Hybrid sterility**: $F_1$ adults are sterile (Haldane's rule: the heterogametic sex is affected first)
- **Hybrid breakdown**: $F_2$ or backcross generations have reduced fitness

### The Dobzhansky-Muller model

The **Dobzhansky-Muller incompatibility model** explains how reproductive isolation can evolve without either population ever passing through a fitness valley. Consider an ancestral population fixed for alleles $A_1$ and $B_1$. Population 1 fixes $A_2$ (substituting for $A_1$) while population 2 fixes $B_2$ (substituting for $B_1$). Neither substitution is deleterious in its own genetic background. But when the two populations hybridise, the combination $A_2 B_2$ has never been "tested" by selection and may be incompatible, causing hybrid dysfunction.

Mathematically, let the fitness of genotype $A_i B_j$ be $w_{ij}$. The ancestral genotype $A_1 B_1$ has fitness 1. Population 1 evolves $A_2 B_1$ with fitness $w_{21} = 1$ (neutral substitution). Population 2 evolves $A_1 B_2$ with fitness $w_{12} = 1$. But the hybrid genotype $A_2 B_2$ has fitness $w_{22} < 1$ — a negative epistatic interaction that was never exposed to selection in either pure population.

### Gene flow and divergence

The balance between divergent selection and gene flow is captured by:

$$
\Delta p = \frac{V_m + V_s}{1 + V_m + V_s},
$$

where $V_m$ represents the homogenising effect of migration and $V_s$ the divergent effect of selection. Speciation occurs when divergent selection ($V_s$) overwhelms gene flow ($V_m$), or when gene flow is eliminated by a geographic barrier.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Time to speciation under neutrality).** *In the allopatric model with no gene flow, two populations of effective size $N_e$ will accumulate incompatible substitutions at a rate that grows approximately quadratically with time. The expected number of Dobzhansky-Muller incompatibilities after $t$ generations is proportional to $t^2$.*

**Proof sketch.** Consider two loci, each with two possible substitutions. Population 1 can substitute allele $A_2$ at locus 1, and Population 2 can substitute allele $B_2$ at locus 2. The probability that Population 1 fixes a substitution at a particular locus in $t$ generations is approximately $1 - e^{-\mu t}$, where $\mu$ is the mutation rate to alleles that could participate in incompatibilities.

After $t$ generations, Population 1 has made roughly $\alpha t$ substitutions (where $\alpha$ is the substitution rate), and Population 2 has made roughly $\alpha t$ substitutions. The number of pairwise interactions among these substitutions in a hybrid is $(\alpha t)(\alpha t) = \alpha^2 t^2$. If a fraction $c$ of these pairwise combinations are incompatible, the expected number of incompatibilities is $c \alpha^2 t^2$.

The quadratic accumulation rate means that reproductive isolation builds slowly at first (few incompatibilities when $t$ is small) but accelerates. This "snowball" effect predicts that the strength of reproductive isolation between two species should increase faster than linearly with genetic distance. $\square$

This **snowball theory** of Orr (1995) makes a testable prediction: the number of genetic incompatibilities should be proportional to $D^2$ (where $D$ is genetic distance), not to $D$. Data from Drosophila species pairs broadly support this pattern.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, short answer).**

Why is sympatric speciation generally considered more difficult to achieve than allopatric speciation?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In sympatry, the two incipient species share the same geographic area. What prevents gene flow between them?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In sympatry, the two populations are not physically separated, so random mating continually mixes their gene pools. For speciation to occur, some form of assortative mating or strong disruptive selection must overcome this gene flow. Natural selection must directly favour non-random mating, or ecological divergence must be strong enough to reduce interbreeding. In allopatry, geographic isolation eliminates gene flow entirely, making divergence much easier.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

Two species of frog breed in the same pond. Species A breeds in March and Species B breeds in June. What type of reproductive barrier is this? Is it pre-zygotic or post-zygotic?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Does mating or fertilisation occur between the two species?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Temporal isolation, a pre-zygotic barrier.** The species breed at different times of year, so mating between them never occurs. Since no zygote is formed, this is pre-zygotic. Temporal isolation is one of the most common reproductive barriers in plants and animals with seasonal reproduction.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, short answer).**

Explain how polyploidy can cause instantaneous speciation in plants.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Polyploidy doubles the chromosome number. What happens when a polyploid individual mates with a normal (diploid) individual?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A polyploid individual (e.g., tetraploid with 4n chromosomes) arises when a mitotic or meiotic error doubles the chromosome set. When this tetraploid tries to mate with a normal diploid (2n), the hybrid is triploid (3n). Triploids are typically sterile because meiosis cannot properly segregate the unpaired chromosomes — the chromosomes cannot form balanced pairs at metaphase I. The tetraploid is therefore reproductively isolated from the diploid parent population in a single generation. This is the only mechanism that can produce a new species instantaneously. Polyploidy is estimated to be responsible for 2-4% of speciation events in flowering plants, and many crop species (wheat, cotton, oats) are polyploids.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short answer).**

Explain the Dobzhansky-Muller model using a concrete example with two loci and two populations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Start with an ancestral population fixed for alleles $A_1$ and $B_1$. What happens when the two isolated populations each fix a different new allele?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

An ancestral population is fixed for alleles $A_1$ at locus A and $B_1$ at locus B. The population splits geographically. In Population 1, allele $A_2$ arises and goes to fixation (substituting for $A_1$). The genotype $A_2 B_1$ has normal fitness. In Population 2, allele $B_2$ arises and goes to fixation. The genotype $A_1 B_2$ has normal fitness. If the two populations come back into contact, hybrids carry both new alleles: $A_2 B_2$. This combination has never existed before, and if there is a negative epistatic interaction between $A_2$ and $B_2$, the hybrid has reduced fitness. Neither population ever passed through a fitness valley — each substitution was neutral or beneficial in its own genetic background. But the untested combination in hybrids creates post-zygotic isolation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short answer).**

**Reinforcement** is the process by which natural selection strengthens pre-zygotic isolation when two partially diverged species come into secondary contact. Explain the logic.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If hybrids have low fitness, what happens to individuals that mate with the other species versus those that mate with their own?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

When two species come into secondary contact, individuals that mate with their own species produce viable offspring, while those that mate with the other species produce low-fitness hybrids. Natural selection therefore favours traits that prevent hybridisation — such as more divergent mating signals, greater mate discrimination, or different breeding times. Over generations, pre-zygotic isolation strengthens as a direct result of selection against hybridisation. This process is called **reinforcement** (also known as the Wallace effect). It predicts that sympatric populations of two species should show stronger pre-zygotic isolation than allopatric populations of the same species pair — a pattern confirmed in many taxa, including Drosophila and tree frogs.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short answer).**

Explain the **snowball theory** of speciation and its key prediction about how reproductive isolation should accumulate with time.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each new substitution can be incompatible with any substitution in the other population. How does the number of pairwise combinations grow?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The snowball theory (Orr, 1995) predicts that the number of Dobzhansky-Muller incompatibilities should accumulate quadratically with time (or genetic distance). If Population 1 has made $k$ substitutions and Population 2 has made $k$ substitutions, there are $k^2$ possible pairwise interactions between them. Only a fraction are incompatible, but the total grows as $k^2$, not $k$. The key prediction: reproductive isolation should increase faster than linearly with genetic distance. Data from Drosophila support this — the fraction of inviable or sterile $F_1$ hybrids increases more steeply between more distantly related species pairs than between closely related pairs.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

**Haldane's rule** states that in hybrids between two species, if one sex is absent, rare, or sterile, it is the heterogametic sex (XY in mammals, ZW in birds). Explain how the Dobzhansky-Muller model accounts for this pattern.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider that recessive alleles on the X (or Z) chromosome are exposed in the heterogametic sex but not in the homogametic sex.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Under the Dobzhansky-Muller model, incompatible substitutions are often recessive. In the homogametic sex (XX females in mammals, ZZ males in birds), a recessive incompatible allele on the X/Z is masked by the dominant compatible allele from the other species. But in the heterogametic sex (XY males, ZW females), the single X (or Z) chromosome is expressed without a homologous partner, so any incompatible recessive allele is fully exposed. The result: the heterogametic sex suffers disproportionate hybrid dysfunction. This explanation, called the **dominance theory**, was proposed by Turelli and Orr (1995) and accounts for Haldane's rule across a wide range of taxa.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, short answer).**

Describe what a **hybrid zone** is and explain the difference between a tension zone and a bounded hybrid superiority zone.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What maintains the hybrid zone? Is it a balance between dispersal and selection against hybrids, or do hybrids have higher fitness in a particular area?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A **hybrid zone** is a narrow geographic region where two species meet and interbreed, producing hybrids. In a **tension zone**, hybrids have reduced fitness everywhere, and the zone is maintained by a balance between dispersal of parental individuals into the zone (which creates hybrids) and selection against hybrids (which removes them). The zone width is determined by the ratio of dispersal distance to selection strength. In a **bounded hybrid superiority zone**, hybrids have higher fitness than either parent species in the zone itself (perhaps due to an environmental gradient), but lower fitness outside it. The zone is maintained by environmental selection favouring hybrids in the intermediate habitat.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, short answer).**

Cichlid fish in the African Great Lakes (Victoria, Malawi, Tanganyika) have undergone explosive adaptive radiation, producing hundreds of species within each lake — with no geographic barriers within the lake. Explain how sympatric speciation might have operated in this system.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider sexual selection, ecological specialisation, and depth-related habitat partitioning within the lake.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Several mechanisms facilitate sympatric speciation in cichlids. (1) **Sexual selection on male colour**: females prefer males of specific colours, and colour is genetically linked to mate choice. Divergent female preferences can create assortative mating without geographic barriers. (2) **Ecological specialisation**: different species specialise on different food sources (algae scraping, snail crushing, fish hunting, plankton filtering), and jaw morphology co-evolves with habitat depth. (3) **Depth-related light environment**: at different depths, water filters different wavelengths of light, altering the appearance of male colours and the visual sensitivity of female eyes. This creates "sensory drive" — depth-correlated divergence in both male colour and female visual systems that promotes reproductive isolation along a depth gradient. (4) **Micro-allopatry**: fine-scale habitat structure (rocky vs sandy substrates at different points along the shoreline) creates quasi-geographic barriers within the lake. These mechanisms interact, creating many ecological niches and strong assortative mating that together generate hundreds of species.

</details>

</aside>

## Speciation genomics [Master]

Modern genomic data reveal that speciation is rarely an all-or-nothing process. **Genomic islands of divergence** — regions of the genome with elevated differentiation ($F_{ST}$) between incipient species — are interspersed with regions of low differentiation where gene flow continues. This heterogeneous pattern suggests that speciation occurs via divergence at a subset of loci under strong selection (those affecting ecological traits or reproductive isolation), while the rest of the genome homogenises through gene flow.

The **speciation-with-gene-flow** model, parameterised by the migration rate $m$ and the strength of divergent selection $s$, predicts that loci under strong selection ($s \gg m$) can diverge even while neutral loci remain undifferentiated. Whole-genome sequencing of incipient species pairs (e.g., *Heliconius* butterflies, *Anopheles* mosquitoes) confirms this pattern: regions containing colour-pattern genes or host-preference genes show high divergence embedded in a low-divergence genomic background.

## Connections [Master]

- **Natural selection [19.03.01]** drives the divergent adaptation that initiates speciation. Different selection pressures in different environments (or on different ecological niches) are the primary cause of phenotypic divergence between incipient species.

- **Genetic drift [19.04.01]** contributes to divergence, especially in small isolated populations. Drift can fix different alleles in allopatric populations, creating the raw material for Dobzhansky-Muller incompatibilities.

- **Quantitative genetics [19.05.01]** provides the framework for understanding how heritable traits diverge under selection during speciation. The breeder's equation predicts how quickly ecological traits diverge between isolated populations.

- **Phylogenetics [19.07.01]** reconstructs the tree of speciation events. The timing and pattern of speciation inferred from phylogenies can be compared with biogeographic and fossil data.

- **Mendelian genetics [19.01.01]** underpins the genetic basis of reproductive isolation. Incompatibilities arise from interactions between specific alleles at different loci, following Mendelian inheritance.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Charles Darwin titled his most famous work *On the Origin of Species* (1859), yet he struggled to explain how one species splits into two. He focused on gradual divergence through natural selection but did not articulate the role of geographic isolation. It was Ernst Mayr who championed allopatric speciation in *Systematics and the Origin of Species* (1942), arguing that geographic isolation is the dominant mode of speciation in animals. Mayr's insistence on the primacy of allopatry was influential but also dogmatic — it delayed recognition of sympatric speciation as a legitimate process.

Theodosius Dobzhansky's *Genetics and the Origin of Species* (1937) provided the genetic framework. His concept of reproductive isolation as the defining feature of species, combined with the Dobzhansky-Muller model for how incompatibilities arise, remains the theoretical foundation. The model shows that speciation does not require either population to pass through a fitness valley — a key theoretical insight.

The **species problem** — the difficulty of defining what a species is — remains unresolved. The biological species concept fails for asexual organisms, for fossils, and for partially interbreeding species complexes. Alternative concepts include the phylogenetic species concept (smallest monophyletic group on a phylogenetic tree), the ecological species concept (group occupying a unique adaptive zone), and the genotypic cluster concept (group forming a distinct cluster in multivariate genetic space). Each concept works better for some taxa than others, suggesting that "species" may not be a single kind of entity.
