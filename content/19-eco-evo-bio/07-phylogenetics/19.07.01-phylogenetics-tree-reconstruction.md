## Intuition [Beginner]

A **phylogenetic tree** is a diagram showing the evolutionary relationships among species. Each branch represents a lineage, and each branching point (node) represents a common ancestor. Species that share a more recent common ancestor are more closely related.

Think of it like a family tree, but for species instead of individuals. Humans and chimpanzees share a common ancestor that lived about 6-7 million years ago. Humans and mice share a more distant common ancestor (about 75 million years ago). Humans and fruit flies share an even more distant one (about 600 million years ago). The tree captures these relationships.

The tips of the tree represent living (or extinct) species. The root represents the most recent common ancestor of all the species in the tree. Moving from the tips toward the root means going backward in time.

**Cladistics** is the method of building phylogenetic trees based on **shared derived characters** — traits that evolved in a common ancestor and are inherited by all its descendants. A derived character (also called an **apomorphy**) is one that is new, not ancestral. A shared derived character (**synapomorphy**) is evidence that the species sharing it form a clade — a group consisting of an ancestor and all its descendants.

It is important to distinguish **homology** (similarity due to common ancestry) from **homoplasy** (similarity due to convergent evolution). Bat wings and bird wings look similar and serve the same function, but they evolved independently from different ancestral structures. This is homoplasy, not homology.

## Visual [Beginner]

Consider four species: Human, Mouse, Chicken, and Frog. A phylogenetic tree groups them by relatedness.

Human and Mouse are closest relatives — they share a recent common ancestor. Chicken is more distantly related. Frog is the outgroup (most distantly related). The branch lengths can represent either the amount of genetic change or time (if the tree is ultrametric).

## Worked example [Beginner]

Construct a simple phylogeny for four taxa using five binary characters:

| Character | Taxon A | Taxon B | Taxon C | Taxon D (outgroup) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | 1 | 1 | 0 | 0 |
| 2 | 1 | 1 | 0 | 0 |
| 3 | 1 | 0 | 0 | 0 |
| 4 | 0 | 1 | 1 | 0 |
| 5 | 0 | 0 | 1 | 0 |

The outgroup (Taxon D) has state 0 for all characters, so state 1 is derived.

Characters 1 and 2 are shared by A and B — this is a synapomorphy grouping A and B together. Character 4 is shared by B and C — but this conflicts with characters 1 and 2. Character 5 is unique to C (an autapomorphy). Character 3 is unique to A.

Under **parsimony** (prefer the tree requiring fewest evolutionary changes), the tree that groups A with B requires: characters 1, 2 change once on the AB ancestor (2 steps), character 3 changes once in A (1 step), character 4 changes once in C (1 step, since we infer it evolved in C independently — homoplasy), and character 5 changes once in C (1 step). Total: 5 steps.

Any tree that groups B with C instead would require more changes (characters 1, 2 would each need to evolve twice). So parsimony supports the grouping (A, B).

**Exercise (medium, short answer).**

Explain the difference between a **monophyletic**, **paraphyletic**, and **polyphyletic** group. Which one is the only valid taxonomic grouping in cladistics?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider whether the group includes all descendants of its common ancestor. A monophyletic group is a clade — what about the others?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A **monophyletic group** (clade) consists of an ancestor and all of its descendants. Example: Mammals (all descendants of the last common ancestor of all mammals). A **paraphyletic group** consists of an ancestor and some, but not all, of its descendants. Example: "Reptiles" (excluding birds, which are descended from reptilian ancestors) is paraphyletic. A **polyphyletic group** consists of descendants of different ancestors, grouped by convergent features. Example: "Flying vertebrates" (bats and birds) is polyphyletic. Only monophyletic groups are valid in cladistics because only they reflect actual evolutionary history.

</details>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Tree structure

A **phylogenetic tree** $T$ is a connected acyclic graph. A **rooted** tree has a designated root node representing the most recent common ancestor of all taxa. An **unrooted** tree specifies branching order but not the direction of time. The number of distinct unrooted binary trees for $n$ taxa is:

$$
N_u(n) = (2n - 5)!! = (2n - 5)(2n - 7) \cdots 3 \cdot 1.
$$

For $n = 4$: $N_u = 3$ unrooted trees. For $n = 10$: $N_u = 2{,}027{,}025$. The super-exponential growth makes exhaustive search impractical for large datasets.

### Substitution models

Sequence evolution along a branch is modelled as a continuous-time Markov chain with rate matrix $Q$. The simplest model, **Jukes-Cantor** (JC69), assumes equal base frequencies ($\pi_A = \pi_C = \pi_G = \pi_T = 0.25$) and equal substitution rates:

$$
Q = \mu \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1/3 & 1/3 & 1/3 \\ 1/3 & -1 & 1/3 & 1/3 \\ 1/3 & 1/3 & -1 & 1/3 \\ 1/3 & 1/3 & 1/3 & -1 \end{pmatrix},
$$

where $\mu$ is the overall substitution rate. The probability of change over branch length $t$ (in expected substitutions per site) is:

$$
P(\text{different}) = \frac{3}{4}(1 - e^{-4\mu t/3}).
$$

The **Kimura 2-parameter** (K80) model allows different rates for transitions ($A \leftrightarrow G$, $C \leftrightarrow T$) and transversions, with transition/transversion ratio $\kappa$.

### Parsimony

**Maximum parsimony** seeks the tree requiring the minimum number of character-state changes. The **Fitch algorithm** computes the parsimony score for a given tree in $O(nk)$ time ($n$ taxa, $k$ characters) by a single post-order traversal. For each node, compute the set of possible ancestral states; the parsimony score increments when the sets of child nodes are disjoint.

### Maximum likelihood

**Maximum likelihood** finds the tree $T$ and branch lengths $\mathbf{t}$ that maximise:

$$
L(T, \mathbf{t}) = P(\text{data} \mid T, \mathbf{t}, Q) = \prod_{\text{sites } i} P(D_i \mid T, \mathbf{t}, Q),
$$

where $D_i$ is the observed nucleotide pattern at site $i$. The likelihood for each site is computed by Felsenstein's **pruning algorithm** — a dynamic programming algorithm that computes the conditional likelihood at each node in $O(4^2 n)$ per site by post-order traversal.

### Bayesian inference

**Bayesian phylogenetics** computes the posterior distribution:

$$
P(T, \mathbf{t} \mid \text{data}) \propto P(\text{data} \mid T, \mathbf{t}, Q) \cdot P(T) \cdot P(\mathbf{t}),
$$

using Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) to sample from the posterior. The output is a distribution of trees, summarised by a **majority-rule consensus tree** where each clade is annotated with its posterior probability.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Felsenstein's pruning algorithm computes site likelihoods in linear time).** *For a binary phylogenetic tree with $n$ tips and a $k$-state substitution model, the likelihood at a single site can be computed in $O(nk^2)$ time.*

**Proof.** Define the **conditional likelihood** $L_i(s)$ at node $i$ as the probability of the observed data at all tips descending from node $i$, given that node $i$ has state $s$. For a tip with observed state $s_0$:

$$
L_{\text{tip}}(s) = \begin{cases} 1 & s = s_0 \\ 0 & s \neq s_0 \end{cases}.
$$

For an internal node $i$ with children $j$ and $k$ connected by branches of lengths $t_j$ and $t_k$:

$$
L_i(s) = \left[\sum_{s'} P_{ss'}(t_j) L_j(s')\right] \left[\sum_{s'} P_{ss'}(t_k) L_k(s')\right],
$$

where $P_{ss'}(t)$ is the transition probability from state $s$ to state $s'$ over branch length $t$, computed as $P(t) = e^{Qt}$.

The computation proceeds by post-order traversal (children before parent). At each of the $n - 1$ internal nodes, we compute $k$ conditional likelihoods, each involving two sums of $k$ terms: $O(k^2)$ per node. Total: $O(nk^2)$.

At the root, the full site likelihood is:

$$
L_{\text{site}} = \sum_{s} \pi_s L_{\text{root}}(s),
$$

where $\pi_s$ is the stationary frequency of state $s$. $\square$

This algorithm makes maximum likelihood phylogenetics computationally tractable. For a dataset with $S$ sites, the total likelihood computation is $O(nk^2 S)$, which is linear in the number of taxa and sites.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

How many distinct unrooted binary trees are there for 5 taxa?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the formula $N_u(n) = (2n - 5)!!$.

**15.** $N_u(5) = (2 \times 5 - 5)!! = 5!! = 5 \times 3 \times 1 = 15$. For just 5 taxa, there are 15 possible tree topologies. For 10 taxa, the number grows to over 2 million — illustrating why heuristic search algorithms are essential for large datasets.

</details>

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

Under the Jukes-Cantor model, two sequences differ at 20% of sites. Calculate the estimated number of substitutions per site (the branch length).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Jukes-Cantor correction is $d = -\frac{3}{4}\ln(1 - \frac{4}{3}p)$ where $p$ is the observed proportion of differences.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**0.217 substitutions per site.** $p = 0.20$. $d = -\frac{3}{4}\ln(1 - \frac{4}{3} \times 0.20) = -\frac{3}{4}\ln(1 - 0.267) = -\frac{3}{4}\ln(0.733) = -\frac{3}{4}(-0.310) = 0.233$. Actually: $d = -0.75 \times \ln(0.7333) = -0.75 \times (-0.3102) = 0.2327$. The corrected distance (0.233) is larger than the observed proportion (0.20) because Jukes-Cantor accounts for multiple substitutions at the same site that obscure the true distance.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, short answer).**

Explain why **long branch attraction** is a problem for maximum parsimony. Under what conditions does it occur?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

When two long branches (fast-evolving lineages) are separated by short branches, what can parsimony incorrectly infer?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Long branch attraction (LBA) occurs when two unrelated lineages have each accumulated many substitutions independently (long branches). Under parsimony, the most parsimonious explanation for the shared derived states is that the two lineages are closely related — because placing them together requires fewer total changes than placing them separately. But the shared states are actually homoplasies (convergent substitutions), not synapomorphies. LBA is a particular problem when the true tree has long branches separated by short branches (a topology sometimes called the "Felsenstein zone"). Maximum likelihood and Bayesian methods are less susceptible to LBA because they model the substitution process explicitly and account for the higher probability of homoplasy on long branches.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short answer).**

Describe the **bootstrap** method for assessing clade support in phylogenetics.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The bootstrap creates pseudo-replicate datasets by resampling sites with replacement. What does a high bootstrap proportion mean?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Bootstrap analysis (Felsenstein, 1985) proceeds as follows: (1) Generate many (typically 100-1000) pseudo-replicate datasets by resampling alignment columns with replacement, each replicate having the same number of sites as the original. (2) Reconstruct a tree for each replicate using the same method. (3) For each clade in the original tree, count the fraction of bootstrap replicates in which that clade appears. This **bootstrap proportion** (BP) measures the robustness of the clade to sampling variation in the data. A BP of 95% means the clade appeared in 95% of bootstrap trees. Conventionally, BP $\geq$ 70% is considered moderate support and BP $\geq$ 95% strong support, though the precise statistical interpretation has been debated.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, numeric).**

Under the Jukes-Cantor model with substitution rate $\mu = 0.01$ substitutions per site per million years, two sequences differ at 40% of sites. Estimate the time since their common ancestor.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

First compute the corrected distance $d$, then divide by $2\mu$ (since substitutions accumulate on both branches since divergence).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**~34.5 million years.** $p = 0.40$. $d = -\frac{3}{4}\ln(1 - \frac{4}{3} \times 0.40) = -0.75\ln(1 - 0.533) = -0.75\ln(0.467) = -0.75(-0.762) = 0.571$ substitutions per site. This is the total branch length between the two sequences. Time $= d / (2\mu) = 0.571 / (2 \times 0.01) = 0.571/0.02 = 28.6$ million years. Note the large correction: the raw p-distance (0.40) substantially underestimates the true evolutionary distance (0.571) because of multiple hits.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short answer).**

Explain the concept of a **molecular clock** and discuss why it is often violated in practice.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The molecular clock hypothesis assumes that the rate of molecular evolution is approximately constant across lineages. What biological factors could cause rate variation?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The molecular clock hypothesis (Zuckerkandl and Pauling, 1962) proposes that molecular substitutions accumulate at a roughly constant rate over time, allowing branch lengths to be converted to divergence times. In practice, the clock is often violated for several reasons. (1) **Generation time**: organisms with shorter generations have more replication cycles per unit time, potentially accumulating more substitutions. (2) **Mutation rate variation**: different DNA repair efficiencies, metabolic rates (free radical damage), and UV exposure create lineage-specific rate variation. (3) **Selection**: purifying selection slows evolution at constrained sites, while positive selection accelerates it. (4) **Population size**: the neutral theory predicts that the substitution rate equals the mutation rate regardless of population size, but slightly deleterious mutations behave differently in populations of different sizes. **Relaxed clock models** (e.g., uncorrelated lognormal relaxed clock) allow rates to vary across branches and are now standard in Bayesian dating analyses.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

For the Jukes-Cantor model, derive the transition probability $P_{ii}(t)$ (probability that a site in state $i$ is still in state $i$ after time $t$) and $P_{ij}(t)$ for $i \neq j$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The transition probability matrix is $P(t) = e^{Qt}$. Diagonalise $Q$ or solve the system of differential equations.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For JC69, all off-diagonal rates are equal ($\alpha = \mu/3$) and all states are symmetric. The transition probabilities are:

$$
P_{ii}(t) = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{3}{4}e^{-4\alpha t},
$$

$$
P_{ij}(t) = \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{4}e^{-4\alpha t}, \quad i \neq j.
$$

Verification: at $t = 0$, $P_{ii} = 1$ and $P_{ij} = 0$ (no change). As $t \to \infty$, $P_{ii} = P_{ij} = 1/4$ (the stationary distribution). The probability of change is $1 - P_{ii}(t) = \frac{3}{4}(1 - e^{-4\alpha t})$, which is the formula used in the Jukes-Cantor distance correction.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, short answer).**

Explain why maximum likelihood and Bayesian methods are generally preferred over maximum parsimony for modern phylogenetic analysis.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What assumptions does parsimony make about the evolutionary process? How do model-based methods differ?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Parsimony makes no explicit assumptions about the substitution process — it simply minimises the number of changes. This is a strength (model-free) but also a weakness: it cannot account for varying rates of evolution across lineages or sites, leading to systematic errors like long branch attraction. Maximum likelihood and Bayesian methods use an explicit substitution model (JC69, K80, GTR, etc.) that can accommodate rate variation, base frequency biases, and different transition/transversion ratios. By modelling the process that generated the data, they produce more accurate trees, especially when branch lengths are heterogeneous. The cost is computational: ML and Bayesian analyses require optimisation or MCMC over tree space and are substantially slower than parsimony.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced phylogenetic methods [Master]

### Coalescent-based species trees

Gene trees (phylogenies inferred from individual loci) can differ from the species tree due to **incomplete lineage sorting** (ILS) — the persistence of ancestral polymorphism through speciation events. Under the **multispecies coalescent**, gene tree discordance is expected and quantifiable: the probability that a gene tree differs from the species tree for a three-taxon case is $P(\text{discordance}) = \frac{2}{3}e^{-T/\theta}$, where $T$ is the internal branch length in coalescent units and $\theta = 4N_e\mu$.

Methods like **ASTRAL** (summary method) and **StarBEAST2** (full multispecies coalescent) infer species trees from sets of gene trees, correctly accounting for ILS rather than treating gene tree conflict as noise.

### Phylogenomics and data partitioning

Modern phylogenetic analyses use hundreds to thousands of loci from genome-scale data. **Partitioning** assigns different substitution models to different subsets of the data (e.g., codon positions, genes, introns vs exons). Over-partitioning inflates the number of parameters and can overfit; under-partitioning pools heterogeneous data. Model selection criteria (AIC, BIC) guide partition selection. Recent approaches like **PartitionFinder** and **model selection across partition schemes** automate this process.

### Phylogenetic networks

When reticulate evolution (hybridisation, horizontal gene transfer) is pervasive, a bifurcating tree is an inadequate representation. **Phylogenetic networks** generalise trees by allowing nodes with more than two parents, representing reticulation events. Split networks (NeighbourNet) and explicit hybridisation networks (PhyloNet) visualise conflicting phylogenetic signals that trees cannot capture.

## Connections [Master]

- **Speciation [19.06.01]** produces the branching events that phylogenetic trees represent. The timing and order of speciation events determine the tree topology.

- **Genetic drift [19.04.01]** drives the fixation of neutral substitutions along branches, providing the molecular variation that phylogenetic methods analyse. The coalescent connects drift to gene tree topology.

- **Mendelian genetics [19.01.01]** provides the basis for heritable transmission of DNA sequences, which is the prerequisite for building genealogies from molecular data.

- **Sequence comparison [12.06.01]** provides the alignments from which phylogenies are inferred. Alignment quality directly determines phylogenetic accuracy.

- **Probability theory [02.13.01]** underpins all model-based phylogenetic methods: likelihood computation, Bayesian inference, and bootstrapping are all probabilistic procedures.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Phylogenetic systematics began with Willi Hennig's *Phylogenetic Systematics* (1966), which argued that biological classification should reflect evolutionary history, with only monophyletic groups accepted as valid taxa. Hennig's framework — cladistics — transformed taxonomy from a subjective exercise into a testable scientific endeavour.

The molecular revolution transformed phylogenetics. Before DNA sequencing, trees were built from morphological characters, which are limited in number and often homoplastic. Zuckerkandl and Pauling (1962) proposed the molecular clock, and by the 1980s, DNA sequences were being used to reconstruct evolutionary relationships. Felsenstein's maximum likelihood method (1981) and bootstrap (1985) established the statistical foundation that modern phylogenetics builds upon.

The discovery of widespread gene tree discordance (beginning in the mid-2000s with multilocus sequencing) challenged the assumption that a single tree describes the history of a group of species. Incomplete lineage sorting, hybridisation, and horizontal gene transfer mean that different parts of the genome can have different evolutionary histories. This realisation led to the **multispecies coalescent** framework and a shift from seeking "the tree" to estimating a species tree with quantified uncertainty.

Philosophically, phylogenetics raises questions about the nature of biological classification. Should taxonomy be purely phylogenetic (reflecting evolutionary history), or should it also incorporate ecological and phenotypic information? The debate between phylogenetic nomenclature (the PhyloCode) and traditional rank-based nomenclature (the International Code) reflects deeper disagreements about what taxonomy is for.
