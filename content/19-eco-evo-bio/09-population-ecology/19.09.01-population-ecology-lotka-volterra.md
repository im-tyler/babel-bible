## Intuition [Beginner]

How fast can a population grow? If every individual produces more than one surviving offspring each generation, the population grows exponentially. A single bacterium dividing every 20 minutes produces over 1 billion descendants in 10 hours. This is **exponential growth**: the population multiplies by a constant factor each time step.

But exponential growth cannot continue forever. Eventually, resources run out — food, space, light, water. The population hits a ceiling called the **carrying capacity** ($K$), the maximum number of individuals the environment can sustain. Growth slows as the population approaches $K$, producing an S-shaped curve called **logistic growth**.

What happens when two species interact? The simplest case is **predator and prey**. When prey are abundant, predators have plenty of food and their population grows. More predators eat more prey, reducing the prey population. With fewer prey, predators starve and their population declines. With fewer predators, prey recover. This creates a cycle — populations of both species oscillate over time.

The **Lotka-Volterra equations** model this cycle mathematically. Alfred Lotka (1925) and Vito Volterra (1926) independently derived these equations, which predict regular oscillations in predator and prey populations. The Canadian lynx and snowshoe hare show this pattern in real data, with roughly 10-year cycles documented over 200 years of fur-trapping records.

In the phase plane (prey on x-axis, predator on y-axis), the populations trace a closed loop around the equilibrium point, going counterclockwise: more prey leads to more predators, which reduces prey, which reduces predators, allowing prey to recover.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider logistic growth with $r = 0.5$ per year, $K = 1000$, and initial population $N_0 = 10$.

The logistic equation is:

$$
\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\left(1 - \frac{N}{K}\right).
$$

The solution is:

$$
N(t) = \frac{K}{1 + \left(\frac{K - N_0}{N_0}\right)e^{-rt}}.
$$

Substituting $K = 1000$, $N_0 = 10$, $r = 0.5$:

$$
N(t) = \frac{1000}{1 + 99 \cdot e^{-0.5t}}.
$$

At $t = 10$: $N(10) = 1000/(1 + 99 \cdot e^{-5}) = 1000/(1 + 99 \times 0.00674) = 1000/(1 + 0.667) = 1000/1.667 = 600$ (approximately).

The population grows from 10 to about 600 in 10 time units, following the S-shaped logistic curve. At $t = 20$: $N(20) = 1000/(1 + 99 \cdot e^{-10}) = 1000/(1 + 0.0045) = 996$. The population is near carrying capacity.

Explain the difference between **r-selected** and **K-selected** species. Give one example of each.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Exponential growth

For a population with instantaneous growth rate $r$ (births minus deaths per individual per unit time):

$$
\frac{dN}{dt} = rN.
$$

Solution: $N(t) = N_0 e^{rt}$. Doubling time: $t_d = \ln 2 / r$.

### Logistic growth

The logistic equation adds density-dependent regulation:

$$
\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\left(1 - \frac{N}{K}\right).
$$

Solution:

$$
N(t) = \frac{K}{1 + \left(\frac{K - N_0}{N_0}\right)e^{-rt}}.
$$

Equilibria: $N^* = 0$ (unstable for $r > 0$) and $N^* = K$ (globally stable). The **maximum growth rate** occurs at $N = K/2$ (the inflection point), where $dN/dt = rK/4$.

### Lotka-Volterra predator-prey

Let $x$ = prey density, $y$ = predator density:

$$
\frac{dx}{dt} = \alpha x - \beta xy = x(\alpha - \beta y),
$$

$$
\frac{dy}{dt} = \delta xy - \gamma y = y(\delta x - \gamma),
$$

where $\alpha$ is the prey growth rate, $\beta$ is the predation rate, $\delta$ is the predator conversion efficiency, and $\gamma$ is the predator death rate. Equilibrium at $x^* = \gamma/\delta$, $y^* = \alpha/\beta$.

The system has a **conserved quantity** (first integral):

$$
H(x, y) = \delta x - \gamma \ln x + \beta y - \alpha \ln y,
$$

so trajectories lie on level curves of $H$, forming closed orbits around the equilibrium. The populations oscillate indefinitely with amplitude determined by initial conditions.

### Lotka-Volterra competition

For two competing species:

$$
\frac{dN_1}{dt} = r_1 N_1\left(\frac{K_1 - N_1 - \alpha_{12}N_2}{K_1}\right),
$$

$$
\frac{dN_2}{dt} = r_2 N_2\left(\frac{K_2 - N_2 - \alpha_{21}N_1}{K_2}\right),
$$

where $\alpha_{12}$ is the competitive effect of species 2 on species 1, and $\alpha_{21}$ is the reverse. **Coexistence** requires:

$$
\alpha_{12} < \frac{K_1}{K_2} \quad \text{and} \quad \alpha_{21} < \frac{K_2}{K_1}.
$$

For equal carrying capacities ($K_1 = K_2$), coexistence requires $\alpha_{12}\alpha_{21} < 1$ — each species inhibits itself more than it inhibits the other.

### Life tables and survivorship

A **life table** tabulates age-specific survival and reproduction:

- $l_x$ = proportion surviving to age $x$
- $m_x$ = average number of offspring produced at age $x$

The **net reproductive rate** $R_0 = \sum l_x m_x$ gives the expected lifetime reproductive output. A population grows when $R_0 > 1$, is stable when $R_0 = 1$, and declines when $R_0 < 1$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Lotka-Volterra cycles are neutrally stable).** *The Lotka-Volterra predator-prey system has a centre (neutrally stable equilibrium) at $(x^*, y^*) = (\gamma/\delta, \alpha/\beta)$. All non-equilibrium trajectories are closed orbits, and the amplitude of oscillation depends on initial conditions.*

**Proof.** The equilibrium satisfies $dx/dt = 0$ and $dy/dt = 0$. From $x(\alpha - \beta y) = 0$: either $x = 0$ or $y = \alpha/\beta$. From $y(\delta x - \gamma) = 0$: either $y = 0$ or $x = \gamma/\delta$. The interior equilibrium is $(\gamma/\delta, \alpha/\beta)$.

To show closed orbits, we demonstrate the conserved quantity. Rearranging the equations:

$$
\frac{dx}{x(\alpha - \beta y)} = dt = \frac{dy}{y(\delta x - \gamma)}.
$$

Separating:

$$
\frac{(\delta x - \gamma)}{x} dx = \frac{(\alpha - \beta y)}{y} dy.
$$

Integrating both sides:

$$
\delta x - \gamma \ln x = \alpha \ln y - \beta y + C,
$$

which gives $H(x,y) = \delta x - \gamma \ln x + \beta y - \alpha \ln y = C$ (constant).

Since $H$ is strictly convex with a unique minimum at the equilibrium, the level curves $H = C$ are closed curves surrounding the equilibrium. Each trajectory lies on one such curve, producing a periodic orbit. $\square$

Neutral stability means the system is **structurally unstable**: any small perturbation to the equations (e.g., adding prey self-limitation $-dx^2/K$) changes the dynamics qualitatively, typically producing a stable limit cycle or a stable equilibrium instead of neutral cycles.

In the Lotka-Volterra competition model with $K_1 = 100$, $K_2 = 80$, $\alpha_{12} = 0.6$, $\alpha_{21} = 0.5$, determine whether the two species can coexist.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the maximum growth rate of the logistic equation and show that it occurs at $N = K/2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The growth rate is $f(N) = rN(1 - N/K)$. Take the derivative with respect to $N$ and set it to zero.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The population growth rate is $f(N) = rN - rN^2/K$. Taking the derivative: $f'(N) = r - 2rN/K$. Setting $f'(N) = 0$: $r = 2rN/K$, so $N = K/2$. The second derivative $f''(N) = -2r/K < 0$, confirming this is a maximum. The maximum growth rate is $f(K/2) = r(K/2)(1 - 1/2) = rK/4$. This is the maximum sustainable yield (MSY) in fisheries science — the largest harvest that can be sustained indefinitely, achieved by maintaining the population at half its carrying capacity.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

The basic Lotka-Volterra predator-prey model assumes that prey grow exponentially in the absence of predators. What happens if prey growth is logistic instead? How does this change the dynamics?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Adding $-rN^2/K$ to the prey equation introduces density-dependent self-limitation. Does this stabilise or destabilise the cycles?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Replacing exponential prey growth with logistic growth gives:

$$
\frac{dx}{dt} = rx\left(1 - \frac{x}{K}\right) - \beta xy.
$$

This breaks the conserved quantity $H(x,y)$, so trajectories are no longer closed orbits. The equilibrium becomes **asymptotically stable** (a stable spiral) for moderate carrying capacities $K$. Prey self-limitation acts as a restoring force that damps the oscillations. For very large $K$, the dynamics approach the original Lotka-Volterra neutral cycles. For very small $K$, predators may not have enough prey to persist, and the system collapses to prey-only logistic growth. The logistic prey model is more realistic than the original because real prey populations do experience intraspecific competition.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, short answer).**

Describe the three types of **survivorship curves** (Type I, II, III) and give an example organism for each.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Type I: most mortality at old age. Type II: constant mortality rate. Type III: most mortality at young age.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Type I**: Low mortality early in life, with most deaths occurring at old age. Typical of K-selected species with high parental care. Example: humans, elephants. The survivorship curve ($l_x$ vs age) is convex.

**Type II**: Constant mortality rate throughout life. The same probability of dying at any age. Example: some birds (robins), small mammals. The survivorship curve is a straight line on a semi-log plot.

**Type III**: Very high mortality early in life, with few individuals surviving to adulthood. Typical of r-selected species that produce many offspring with no parental care. Example: oysters, sea turtles, most fish. The survivorship curve is concave — a sharp initial decline followed by a plateau.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, short answer).**

The **Ricker model** is a discrete-time population model: $N_{t+1} = N_t e^{r(1 - N_t/K)}$. For what values of $r$ does the population exhibit (a) stable equilibrium, (b) damped oscillations, (c) stable limit cycles, and (d) chaos?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Linearise around the equilibrium $N^* = K$ and examine the eigenvalue of the Jacobian. The stability depends on the magnitude of the eigenvalue.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Linearising around $N^* = K$, the Jacobian eigenvalue is $\lambda = 1 - r$. Stability requires $|\lambda| < 1$, i.e., $|1 - r| < 1$, so $0 < r < 2$.

(a) **Monotonic approach to equilibrium**: $0 < r < 1$ ($0 < \lambda < 1$).

(b) **Damped oscillations**: $1 < r < 2$ ($-1 < \lambda < 0$). The population overshoots and oscillates back to equilibrium.

(c) **Stable limit cycles (period-doubling)**: at $r = 2$, the equilibrium loses stability ($\lambda = -1$), and a period-2 cycle appears. Further increases in $r$ produce period-4, period-8, etc.

(d) **Chaos**: beyond $r \approx 2.692$, the period-doubling cascade converges and the system exhibits chaotic dynamics — sensitive dependence on initial conditions, aperiodic behaviour, and a strange attractor. This was one of the first biological examples of chaos, studied by Robert May (1976).

</details>

</aside>

## Nonlinear dynamics and ecological complexity [Master]

### Stability analysis of communities

Robert May (1972) showed that randomly assembled ecological communities become less stable as they become more complex. For a community of $n$ species with random interaction strengths of mean 0 and standard deviation $\sigma$, the probability of local stability decreases as:

$$
P(\text{stable}) \to 0 \quad \text{as} \quad \sigma\sqrt{nC} > 1,
$$

where $C$ is the connectance (fraction of non-zero interactions). This **complexity-stability paradox** challenged the intuition that diverse communities are more stable, suggesting instead that stability requires either weak interactions, special network structure (e.g., trophic pyramids), or specific sign patterns (e.g., competition $+$ mutualism mixtures).

### Spatial dynamics and metapopulations

The **Levins metapopulation model** describes a population of populations inhabiting discrete patches:

$$
\frac{dp}{dt} = cp(1 - p) - ep,
$$

where $p$ is the fraction of occupied patches, $c$ is the colonisation rate, and $e$ is the extinction rate. The equilibrium $p^* = 1 - e/c$ exists only if $c > e$ (colonisation exceeds extinction). Metapopulation theory explains how species persist in fragmented landscapes despite frequent local extinctions, and has direct applications to conservation biology and reserve design.

## Connections [Master]

- **Natural selection [19.03.01]** shapes life history traits (age at first reproduction, clutch size, longevity) that determine $r$ and $K$. r/K selection theory connects evolutionary biology to population dynamics.

- **ODEs [02.08.01]** provide the mathematical framework for all continuous-time population models. Phase-plane analysis, linearisation, and stability theory are the analytical tools used throughout this unit.

- **Genetic drift [19.04.01]** is stronger in small populations, and population ecology predicts which populations will be small. The interaction of ecological dynamics and drift affects genetic diversity and extinction risk.

- **Community ecology [19.10.01]** extends the two-species Lotka-Volterra models to multi-species food webs. The competition and predator-prey models developed here are the building blocks for community theory.

- **Nonlinear dynamics [18.02.01]** provides the mathematical tools for analysing the Ricker model, logistic map, and other discrete-time population models that exhibit chaos and bifurcations.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Alfred Lotka, a physical chemist, derived the predator-prey equations in *Elements of Physical Biology* (1925) as part of his ambitious programme to apply physical principles to biology. Vito Volterra, a mathematician, independently derived the same equations in 1926 at the request of his son-in-law Umberto D'Ancona, who had noticed that predator fish increased in proportion to prey fish during World War I when fishing was reduced in the Adriatic. The independent discovery of the same equations by two researchers in different fields is a striking example of convergent intellectual discovery.

Robert May's 1972 Nature paper "Will a large complex system be stable?" fundamentally changed community ecology. Before May, ecologists generally believed that complex, species-rich communities were more stable. May showed mathematically that the opposite is true for randomly assembled communities: complexity destabilises. This triggered decades of research into what special structures (food web architecture, interaction strength distributions, spatial heterogeneity) allow real diverse communities to persist.

The discovery of chaos in the logistic map (May, 1976) was influential beyond ecology. It showed that simple deterministic equations can produce apparently random behaviour — a foundational insight of chaos theory. The implication for ecology is that apparently erratic population fluctuations need not be driven by environmental stochasticity; they can arise from deterministic nonlinearity alone.
