## Intuition [Beginner]

Sexual selection is a special form of natural selection that acts on traits related to mating success rather than survival. Darwin proposed it in 1871 to explain features that seem to reduce survival, such as the peacock's extravagant tail. A long, heavy tail makes the peacock more visible to predators and harder to escape — yet peahens prefer males with larger, more elaborate tails. The mating advantage must outweigh the survival cost.

Sexual selection operates through two main mechanisms. **Intersexual selection** (mate choice) occurs when members of one sex (usually females) preferentially choose mates with particular traits. The peacock's tail is the paradigmatic example. **Intrasexual selection** (competition) occurs when members of the same sex (usually males) compete directly for access to mates. Deer antlers, elephant seals fighting on beaches, and male frogs calling to outcompete rivals are examples.

Why is it usually males that compete and females that choose? The answer lies in **anisogamy** — the difference in gamete size. Eggs are large and expensive (full of nutrients); sperm are small and cheap. A female produces relatively few eggs and invests heavily in each one. A male can produce vast numbers of sperm at little cost. This asymmetry means female reproductive success is limited by egg production, while male reproductive success is limited by access to mates. The result: females are selective, males compete.

## Visual [Beginner]

A peacock's tail illustrates the tension between natural selection and sexual selection. Natural selection favours a shorter, less conspicuous tail (easier to escape predators, lower energy cost). Sexual selection favours a longer, more elaborate tail (more attractive to females).

![Peacock tail as an example of a sexually selected trait. The tail is costly to produce and maintain (natural selection disadvantage) but increases mating success (sexual selection advantage). The balance of these two forces determines the equilibrium tail size.](/img/19.03.02-peacock-tail-placeholder.svg)

When sexual selection is stronger than the survival cost, the trait evolves to extreme sizes. The equilibrium point is where the marginal mating benefit equals the marginal survival cost. Past this point, the survival cost exceeds the mating benefit, and selection halts.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Explain the peacock's tail via two competing hypotheses:

**Fisherian runaway selection:** Females initially have a slight preference for longer tails (perhaps because longer tails indicate better health). Males with longer tails attract more mates and have more offspring. Their sons inherit longer tails; their daughters inherit the preference. The preference and the trait become genetically correlated and co-evolve in a positive feedback loop, each driving the other to greater extremes until the survival cost halts the process.

**Handicap principle (Zahavi):** Only genuinely high-quality males can afford the cost of producing and maintaining a large tail. The tail is a reliable signal of male quality because low-quality males cannot fake it — attempting to produce a large tail would leave them too weakened to survive. Females who choose males with large tails are choosing males with good genes, and their offspring inherit those good genes.

Both hypotheses can explain the peacock's tail. They are not mutually exclusive, and both probably contribute in most real cases.

Explain why sexual dimorphism (differences between males and females) is more pronounced in polygynous species (one male mates with multiple females) than in monogamous species.

In polygyny, how many males get to mate? What does this mean for the intensity of selection on male traits?

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Bateman's principle

A. J. Bateman (1948) demonstrated in fruit flies that: (1) males show greater variance in reproductive success than females; (2) male reproductive success increases with number of mates, while female reproductive success plateaus after one mating. The **Bateman gradient** ($\beta_{ss}$) quantifies the regression of reproductive success on mating success for each sex. A steeper gradient in males indicates stronger sexual selection.

### Fisherian runaway

Fisher (1930) proposed that a female preference and the preferred male trait can co-evolve through a **self-reinforcing** process:

1. A female preference gene (e.g., preferring longer tails) arises and spreads by drift or direct selection.
2. Females with the preference mate with males that have longer tails.
3. Male offspring inherit both the trait gene (from father) and the preference gene (from mother), establishing a **genetic correlation** between trait and preference.
4. Because the genes are correlated, selection on one drags the other along. The trait and preference escalate together ("runaway") until natural selection (survival costs) halts the process.

Lande (1981) formalised this as a pair of coupled equations:

$$
\Delta \bar{z} = \frac{1}{2} G_z (\beta_z + \alpha \beta_p)
$$
$$
\Delta \bar{p} = \frac{1}{2} G_p (\beta_p + \alpha \beta_z)
$$

where $\bar{z}$ is the mean male trait, $\bar{p}$ is the mean female preference, $G_z$ and $G_p$ are additive genetic variances, $\alpha$ is the genetic correlation between trait and preference, and $\beta_z$, $\beta_p$ are the selection gradients. When $\alpha$ is large enough, the system is **unstable**: small deviations from the equilibrium trigger runaway escalation.

### The handicap principle

Zahavi (1975) proposed that costly traits are **reliable signals** of quality precisely because they are costly. A low-quality individual cannot afford the trait without suffering unacceptable survival costs. Therefore, only high-quality individuals can display the trait at full intensity, making the trait an honest indicator of genetic quality. The female benefits by choosing a high-quality mate whose genes improve offspring fitness.

The handicap principle was initially controversial because it seemed paradoxical that a costly trait could be favoured by selection. Grafen (1990) provided the formal proof that a signalling equilibrium exists where the trait cost is condition-dependent, the signal is honest, and females benefit from choosing honest signalers.

### Good genes hypotheses

Under **good genes** models, female preference for a male trait is adaptive because the trait indicates heritable genetic quality. The parasite-mediated sexual selection hypothesis (Hamilton and Zuk, 1982) proposes that males with the most elaborate secondary sexual traits are those most resistant to parasites, and females choosing these males produce offspring with better parasite resistance.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Fisherian runaway instability condition).** *In Lande's model of sexual selection, the male trait and female preference co-evolve unstably (runaway occurs) when the genetic correlation $\alpha$ between trait and preference exceeds a critical threshold determined by the balance of natural and sexual selection. Below this threshold, the system is stable and the trait and preference remain at their natural-selection equilibrium.*

**Proof (sketch).** The Lande equations describe the joint evolution of $\bar{z}$ (male trait) and $\bar{p}$ (female preference). At equilibrium, both selection gradients are zero. Linearising around the equilibrium and examining the eigenvalues of the Jacobian matrix:

$$
\mathbf{J} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix} G_z \beta_z' & G_z \alpha \\ G_p \alpha & G_p \beta_p' \end{pmatrix},
$$

where $\beta_z'$ and $\beta_p'$ are the curvatures of the fitness surfaces. The system is unstable (runaway occurs) when at least one eigenvalue has positive real part. This happens when:

$$
\alpha^2 > \frac{\beta_z' \beta_p'}{G_z G_p}.
$$

When this inequality holds, a small perturbation in either $\bar{z}$ or $\bar{p}$ triggers a self-reinforcing escalation. The equilibrium is an unstable saddle point, and the trait and preference diverge until the survival costs become large enough to restore stability at a new (possibly very different) equilibrium. $\square$

The runaway condition depends on both the genetic correlation $\alpha$ (which must be large) and the strength of stabilizing selection ($\beta_z'$, $\beta_p'$, which must be weak). Large populations with many loci contributing to the genetic correlation are most susceptible to runaway.

**Exercise 1 (easy, multiple choice).**

In seahorses, males incubate the young in a pouch and provide all parental care. Which sex would you expect to be more selective about mates?

A. Females
B. Males
C. Both equally
D. Neither

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

When one sex invests more in offspring, that sex becomes the limiting resource. The other sex competes for access.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Males.** In seahorses, the male's parental investment (carrying the young) is greater than the female's (producing eggs). Males are therefore the limiting sex, and females compete for access to males. The roles are reversed: females are larger, more aggressive, and compete for mates, while males are selective. This "sex-role reversal" is a powerful test of Bateman's principle — wherever investment is higher, that sex is choosy.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (medium, short answer).**

The genetic correlation between a male trait and a female preference is essential for Fisherian runaway. Explain how this correlation arises and how it is maintained.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Females with the preference mate with males with the trait. What do their offspring inherit?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The correlation arises through **non-random mating**: females carrying the preference gene mate with males carrying the trait gene. Their sons inherit the trait from their father and the preference from their mother (but the preference is not expressed in males, or is expressed at a different locus). Their daughters inherit the trait (which may be expressed weakly or not at all in females) and the preference from their mother. Over generations, the preference gene and the trait gene become physically linked on the same chromosomes through statistical association (linkage disequilibrium), even if they are not physically close. This **linkage disequilibrium** is the genetic correlation that drives runaway. Recombination breaks down the correlation slowly; runaway is faster than recombination breakdown when the genetic variance is large.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, short answer).**

Explain the difference between "good genes" and "good taste" (Fisherian) models of sexual selection. Do females benefit directly in each case?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In one model, the offspring inherit something useful. In the other, the offspring inherit only a preference for an arbitrary trait.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**"Good genes" models:** The male trait indicates heritable genetic quality (e.g., disease resistance). Females who choose males with the trait produce offspring that inherit the good genes, increasing their survival. The female benefits **indirectly** through offspring quality.

**"Good taste" (Fisherian) models:** The male trait is arbitrary — it has no intrinsic connection to quality. Females who prefer the trait produce sons that inherit both the attractive trait and the daughter-preference, making their sons attractive to the next generation of females. The female benefits **indirectly** through the mating success of her sons ("sexy sons"), not through offspring survival. The preference is self-reinforcing.

In both cases the benefit is indirect (through offspring), but the mechanism differs: good genes = offspring survival, good taste = offspring mating success.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (hard, short answer).**

The lek paradox: in lek-breeding species (where males gather to display and females choose), sexual selection should rapidly deplete genetic variation in male display traits, because females choose the same best males each generation. Yet variation in male traits persists. Propose three resolutions to this paradox.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What processes can maintain or restore genetic variation despite strong selection?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(1) **Mutation-selection balance:** New mutations continuously introduce genetic variation. Although selection removes variation, mutation replenishes it. If the mutation rate is high enough (especially at many loci contributing to the trait), variation is maintained.

(2) **Fluctuating selection:** If the "best" trait value changes across generations (because the environment, parasite community, or female preference distribution shifts), the direction of selection fluctuates. Different alleles are favoured at different times, maintaining variation.

(3) **Condition-dependence:** Male display traits depend on overall condition (health, nutrition, parasite load), which is influenced by many genes across the genome. Even if display-trait genes are fixed, the many condition-related genes remain variable. Selection on condition-dependent traits does not deplete variation because it acts on the entire genome, not a few loci.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

Suppose a male trait $z$ has survival fitness $w_s = 1 - c \cdot z^2$ (cost increasing with trait size) and mating fitness $w_m = 1 + m \cdot z$ (mating advantage increasing linearly with trait size). Total fitness is $w = w_s \times w_m$. Derive the equilibrium trait size $z^*$ where fitness is maximised.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate $w = (1 - cz^2)(1 + mz)$ with respect to $z$ and set the derivative to zero.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$w(z) = (1 - cz^2)(1 + mz) = 1 + mz - cz^2 - cmz^3$. Differentiating: $dw/dz = m - 2cz - 3cmz^2$. Setting to zero: $3cmz^2 + 2cz - m = 0$. Using the quadratic formula:

$$
z = \frac{-2c \pm \sqrt{4c^2 + 12c^2 m^2}}{6cm} = \frac{-2c \pm 2c\sqrt{1 + 3m^2}}{6cm}.
$$

Taking the positive root (trait size must be positive):

$$
z^* = \frac{-1 + \sqrt{1 + 3m^2}}{3m}.
$$

For small $m$ (weak sexual selection): $z^* \approx m/2$ (trait is small, proportional to sexual selection strength). For large $m$: $z^* \approx 1/\sqrt{3}$ (trait approaches a constant determined by the cost parameter, not the mating benefit — the cost dominates). The equilibrium reflects the balance between survival cost and mating benefit.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, short answer).**

In many bird species, males are brightly coloured while females are camouflaged. Explain this pattern in terms of the trade-off between sexual selection and natural selection on each sex.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider the costs and benefits of bright coloration for each sex.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For males: sexual selection favours bright colours (attracts females, signals quality). The survival cost (predation risk) is offset by the mating benefit. For females: there is no mating benefit to bright colour (because males do not choose mates based on plumage in these species), but there is a survival cost (predation during nesting). Natural selection favours camouflage. The result is sexual dichromatism: bright males, drab females. This pattern is strongest in polygynous species and weakest (or absent) in monogamous species where both sexes invest equally in offspring and both are choosy.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

Female songbirds in some species prefer males with larger song repertoires. How would you distinguish whether this preference is adaptive (good genes) vs. a Fisherian arbitrary preference?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If the preference is adaptive, the offspring of males with larger repertoires should have higher survival or condition. If it is arbitrary, only the sons' mating success should be higher.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Test the offspring. If the preference is adaptive (good genes), then controlling for maternal effects: (1) offspring of males with larger repertoires should have higher survival, better immune function, or lower parasite loads; (2) the correlation between repertoire size and offspring quality should persist even when the father provides no parental care (ruling out direct benefits). If the preference is arbitrary (Fisherian), then: (1) offspring survival and condition should NOT correlate with father's repertoire size (after controlling for maternal effects); (2) SONS of males with larger repertoires should themselves have larger repertoires and higher mating success (inheriting the "sexy" trait). In practice, the two hypotheses are difficult to distinguish cleanly, and both mechanisms likely contribute simultaneously.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (medium, short answer).**

Male elephant seals compete violently for control of beach territories. A single dominant male may mate with over 100 females, while most males fail to mate at all. What type of sexual selection is this, and what traits would you expect to evolve in males?

## Sexual conflict and the evolution of mating systems [Master]

**Sexual conflict** occurs when traits that increase the fitness of one sex reduce the fitness of the other. Male seed beetles, for example, have spiny genitalia that damage the female reproductive tract, reducing her lifespan but increasing the probability that she will use his sperm. This is an **antagonistic coevolutionary arms race**: males evolve traits that improve their fertilisation success, and females evolve counter-adaptations to reduce the damage.

The **operational sex ratio** (OSR) — the ratio of sexually active males to fertilisable females at any given time — is a key predictor of the intensity of sexual selection. In species where females are synchronous (all receptive at once), the OSR approaches 1:1 and sexual selection is weak. Where females are asynchronous (receptive one at a time), the OSR is male-biased and sexual selection is intense.

## Connections [Master]

- **Natural selection [19.03.01]** is the broader framework within which sexual selection operates. Both change allele frequencies based on fitness differences, but sexual selection focuses on the mating-success component of fitness.

- **Kin selection [19.03.03]** modifies the fitness concept in a different direction from sexual selection — extending it to include effects on relatives rather than restricting it to mating success.

- **Mendelian genetics [19.01.01]** provides the genetic basis for the heritable traits and preferences that sexual selection acts upon.

- **Molecular biology [17.05.01]** underlies the genetic mechanisms of trait-preference correlations, sex-linked inheritance, and condition-dependent expression.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Darwin introduced sexual selection in *The Descent of Man* (1871), recognising that some traits could not be explained by survival advantage alone. The idea was initially resisted — Wallace, Darwin's co-discoverer of natural selection, rejected it, arguing that all ornamental traits could be explained by natural selection for vigour. The concept languished until Fisher's formal treatment in 1930 and the empirical work of the 1970s-80s (Trivers 1972 on parental investment, Zahavi 1975 on the handicap principle, Hamilton and Zuk 1982 on parasites).

Anders Moller's long-term study of barn swallows (1988-present) and Malte Andersson's widowbird experiments (1982) provided some of the most compelling experimental evidence for sexual selection. These studies demonstrated that manipulating a single sexually selected trait (tail length) produced measurable changes in mating success.

Philosophically, sexual selection raises the question of **aesthetic evolution**: do animals have "tastes" or "preferences" in a meaningful sense, or are preferences mere mechanistic responses? The question connects to the broader debate about animal cognition and the nature of choice. Darwin himself was comfortable attributing a "sense of beauty" to female birds; modern researchers are more cautious but recognise that cognitive processes (learning, comparison, assessment) play a role in mate choice.
