## Intuition [Beginner]

Altruism — helping others at a cost to yourself — seems to contradict natural selection. If natural selection favours individuals that maximise their own reproductive success, why would any organism sacrifice its own fitness to help another?

The answer, discovered by William Hamilton in 1964, is that natural selection acts not on individuals but on **genes**. An altruistic act reduces the actor's direct fitness, but if the beneficiary is a relative who shares the same genes, those shared genes may increase in frequency even though the actor pays a cost.

**Hamilton's rule** formalises this: an altruistic act evolves when $rB > C$, where $r$ is the **coefficient of relatedness** between actor and beneficiary, $B$ is the fitness benefit to the beneficiary, and $C$ is the fitness cost to the actor.

The coefficient of relatedness measures the probability that two individuals share a particular gene **identical by descent** (inherited from a recent common ancestor). Between full siblings, $r = 0.5$ (they share half their genes on average). Between parent and offspring, $r = 0.5$. Between cousins, $r = 0.125$.

Hamilton's rule explains many behaviours that seem selfless. A ground squirrel that gives an alarm call when it spots a predator puts itself at risk (the predator may locate the caller) but warns nearby relatives. If the call saves two siblings ($r = 0.5$ each), the genetic payoff is $0.5 \times 2 = 1.0$, which exceeds the personal risk. The genes for alarm calling increase in frequency because they are present in the saved relatives.

## Visual [Beginner]

The coefficient of relatedness forms a hierarchy. You share 100% of your genes with yourself ($r = 1$), 50% with each parent and full sibling ($r = 0.5$), 25% with each grandparent, grandchild, aunt, uncle, niece, and nephew ($r = 0.25$), and 12.5% with each cousin ($r = 0.125$).

![Coefficient of relatedness diagram showing r values for different family relationships: self (1.0), parent/offspring/sibling (0.5), grandparent/aunt/nephew (0.25), cousin (0.125).](/img/19.03.03-relatedness-placeholder.svg)

Hamilton's rule predicts that altruism is more likely between close relatives (high $r$), when the benefit $B$ is large, and when the cost $C$ is small. This is why altruism is most common in social insects, where colony members are exceptionally closely related.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A ground squirrel gives an alarm call when it spots a hawk. The call costs the caller $C = 0.1$ units of fitness (increased predation risk). The call saves 2 siblings from being eaten ($B = 1.0$ per sibling, $r = 0.5$ per sibling).

Apply Hamilton's rule: $rB = 0.5 \times 2 \times 1.0 = 1.0$. Compare to $C = 0.1$. Since $1.0 > 0.1$, Hamilton's rule is satisfied. The alarm call is favoured by selection.

The calculation shows that saving two siblings more than compensates for the personal risk. The genes responsible for alarm calling are present in the caller and (on average) in each sibling. Saving two siblings preserves more copies of the gene than the caller risks losing.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Coefficient of relatedness

The **coefficient of relatedness** $r$ between two individuals is the probability that a gene in one individual is identical by descent to a gene at the same locus in the other. For diploid organisms:

| Relationship | $r$ |
|---|---|
| Self | 1.0 |
| Parent-offspring | 0.5 |
| Full sibling | 0.5 |
| Half sibling | 0.25 |
| Grandparent-grandchild | 0.25 |
| Aunt/uncle-niece/nephew | 0.25 |
| First cousin | 0.125 |

For haplodiploid species (e.g., Hymenoptera: ants, bees, wasps), relatedness is asymmetric. Females are diploid (from fertilised eggs); males are haploid (from unfertilised eggs). A female is related to her full sister by $r = 0.75$ (they share their father's entire genome, since he is haploid) but to her brother by $r = 0.25$. This asymmetry may help explain **eusociality** in Hymenoptera: females are more closely related to their sisters than to their own potential offspring ($r = 0.75$ vs $0.5$), making it genetically advantageous to help their mother raise sisters rather than reproduce themselves.

### Inclusive fitness

**Inclusive fitness** = direct fitness + indirect fitness:

$$
w_{\text{inclusive}} = w_{\text{direct}} + \sum_i r_i \cdot \Delta w_i,
$$

where $w_{\text{direct}}$ is the individual's own offspring production and $\sum_i r_i \cdot \Delta w_i$ is the sum of relatedness-weighted fitness effects on all relatives $i$. Selection maximises inclusive fitness, not direct fitness alone.

### Eusociality

**Eusociality** is the extreme form of social organisation characterised by: (1) reproductive division of labour (queens reproduce, workers do not), (2) cooperative care of young, and (3) overlapping generations. Eusocial species include all ants, some bees and wasps, termites, naked mole rats, and some shrimp.

Hamilton's rule provides one explanation for eusociality: workers gain more inclusive fitness by helping raise siblings (to which they are highly related) than by producing their own offspring. In haplodiploid species, the high sister-sister relatedness ($r = 0.75$) amplifies this effect.

### Reciprocal altruism

**Reciprocal altruism** (Trivers, 1971) extends altruism to non-relatives. An individual helps another at a cost, expecting the favour to be returned in the future. The condition for evolution is that the benefit exceeds the cost ($B > C$), that individuals interact repeatedly, and that there is a mechanism to detect and punish cheaters (those who receive help but do not reciprocate).

The **iterated Prisoner's Dilemma** models this interaction. In computer tournaments (Axelrod, 1984), the strategy **"Tit for Tat"** (cooperate on the first move, then copy the opponent's last move) performed best, demonstrating that simple reciprocity can sustain cooperation.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Hamilton's rule).** *An allele for altruistic behaviour increases in frequency when $rB > C$, where $r$ is the coefficient of relatedness between actor and beneficiary, $B$ is the fitness benefit to the beneficiary, and $C$ is the fitness cost to the actor.*

**Proof.** Consider a single locus with two alleles: $A$ (altruistic) and $a$ (non-altruistic). An $A$-carrier performs the altruistic act with fitness cost $C$ and confers fitness benefit $B$ on the recipient.

Let $p$ be the frequency of allele $A$. The change in allele frequency depends on whether the beneficiaries of altruism also carry allele $A$ more often than the population average. The **conditional probability** that the recipient carries allele $A$ given that the actor does is $P(A_{\text{recipient}} | A_{\text{actor}}) = r + (1-r)p$, where $r$ is the relatedness coefficient. This exceeds the population frequency $p$ by an amount $r(1-p)$.

The net fitness effect on allele $A$ is:
- Cost to the actor: $-C$ (reduces direct transmission).
- Benefit to the recipient, weighted by probability of shared $A$ allele: $+B \times r$ (the excess probability of finding $A$ in the recipient, times the benefit).

The inclusive fitness effect is $-C + rB$. Selection favours allele $A$ when $-C + rB > 0$, i.e., $rB > C$. $\square$

This elegant result shows that the condition for altruism depends on three quantities: the cost, the benefit, and the relatedness. None alone is sufficient; all three must be considered together.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

In honeybees (haplodiploid), a worker bee is related to her full sister by $r = 0.75$ and to her own potential offspring by $r = 0.5$. If helping the queen produce one more sister provides 1 unit of fitness benefit, and the worker could alternatively produce one offspring of her own providing 1 unit of direct fitness, which strategy has higher inclusive fitness?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare $r \times B$ for helping (sister) vs. direct fitness for reproducing (own offspring).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Helping: $r \times B = 0.75 \times 1 = 0.75$ inclusive fitness units. Reproducing: direct fitness = $1 \times 0.5$ (relatedness to own offspring) = 0.5, but this is direct fitness counted fully. Wait — inclusive fitness counts direct fitness at full value. The comparison is: helping costs the worker $C$ (not stated) but gains $0.75 \times B = 0.75$ through the sister. Reproducing gains $0.5$ through the offspring (relatedness to offspring is 0.5). Since $0.75 > 0.5$, **helping is favoured** when the benefit per offspring is equal. This is the haplodiploidy hypothesis for eusociality in Hymenoptera.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (medium, numeric).**

A soldier in a termite colony defends the nest against an invading ant, sacrificing its own life ($C = 1.0$, the soldier dies) but saving the queen (its mother, $r = 0.5$) who will produce 10 more offspring. Is this sacrifice favoured by kin selection?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$rB = 0.5 \times 10 = 5.0$. Compare to $C = 1.0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Yes.** $rB = 0.5 \times 10 = 5.0 > 1.0 = C$. The genetic payoff of saving the queen (who will produce 10 more siblings) far exceeds the cost of the soldier's death. The genes for defensive behaviour are present in the saved queen and will be passed to the next generation of siblings.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, short answer).**

Naked mole rats are eusocial mammals. A colony contains one queen, 1-3 breeding males, and many non-reproductive workers. The workers are full siblings. Explain why workers forgo reproduction in terms of Hamilton's rule.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What is the relatedness between a worker and her siblings vs. her own potential offspring? What ecological constraints limit the worker's ability to reproduce independently?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Workers are full siblings of each other and of the queen's future offspring ($r = 0.5$ to siblings). They are also related to their own potential offspring at $r = 0.5$. The relatedness is equal, so inclusive fitness alone does not strongly favour helping over reproducing. The critical additional factor is **ecological constraint**: in the arid environments where naked mole rats live, dispersing to found a new colony has very low probability of success (most dispersers die). The expected direct fitness from reproducing independently is therefore very low. Meanwhile, helping the queen produce siblings has a high probability of success. When the effective cost of personal reproduction is high (due to ecological constraints), even a modest inclusive fitness benefit from helping can satisfy Hamilton's rule.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short answer).**

In the iterated Prisoner's Dilemma, "Tit for Tat" (cooperate first, then copy the opponent's last move) is a successful strategy. Explain why it works and why it fails if the number of future interactions is very small.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Tit for Tat rewards cooperation and punishes defection. But in the last interaction, there is no future to punish defection.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Tit for Tat succeeds because it: (1) is **nice** (starts with cooperation), (2) is **retaliatory** (punishes defection on the next move), (3) is **forgiving** (returns to cooperation after the opponent cooperates), and (4) is **simple** (easy for opponents to understand and adapt to). These properties allow mutual cooperation to be sustained. However, in the **last interaction** (or when the total number of interactions is known and small), there is no future consequence for defection. The rational strategy in the final round is to defect (no punishment possible). By backward induction, cooperation unravels from the last round to the first. Tit for Tat therefore requires an **indefinite or unknown number of future interactions** to maintain cooperation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, numeric).**

In a haplodiploid species, a female's relatedness to her full sister is 0.75 (they share the father's entire haploid genome). However, if the queen mates with multiple males, the average relatedness between sisters decreases. If the queen mates with 2 males, what is the average sister-sister relatedness? Under what condition does Hamilton's rule still favour helping?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

With 2 mates, half the sisters share a father (relatedness 0.75) and half have different fathers (relatedness 0.25). Average these.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With 2 equally represented fathers: half the sister pairs are full sisters ($r = 0.75$) and half are half-sisters ($r = 0.25$). Average relatedness = $(0.75 + 0.25)/2 = 0.5$. Hamilton's rule for helping vs. reproducing: $0.5 \times B > 0.5 \times B_{\text{own}}$. The advantage of helping over own reproduction disappears when sister relatedness drops to 0.5 (same as relatedness to own offspring). This explains why **polyandry** (queen mating with multiple males) reduces worker altruism and can lead to worker reproduction in social insects. Many eusocial species have evolved mechanisms to maintain high relatedness (monogamy) because it is the foundation of eusociality.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short answer).**

Greenbeard effects occur when a single gene causes both a visible marker (the "green beard") and altruistic behaviour toward others with the same marker. Explain why greenbeard genes are rare in nature despite being theoretically favoured by Hamilton's rule with $r = 1$ at that locus.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What happens if the greenbeard gene mutates so that the marker is displayed but the altruistic behaviour is absent? Would cheaters prosper?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Greenbeard genes are vulnerable to **cheaters**: mutant alleles that display the marker (green beard) but do not perform the altruistic behaviour. Such cheaters receive the benefits of others' altruism without paying the cost. Because the cheater saves the cost $C$, it has higher fitness than the altruistic greenbeard carriers, and the cheating allele spreads. The greenbeard system is evolutionarily unstable because the three necessary features (marker, recognition, altruism) can be decoupled by mutation. Stable greenbeards exist only when the marker and the altruistic behaviour are mechanistically inseparable — the same molecular feature that is recognised also causes the behaviour. This is rare. The general principle is that recognition systems must be **unfakeable** to be evolutionarily stable.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

The "superorganism" concept treats an ant colony as a single organism rather than a group of individuals. Evaluate this analogy in terms of Hamilton's rule and the conditions under which it is valid.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

When relatedness is very high and there is strong reproductive division of labour, the interests of colony members are aligned. When do interests diverge?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The superorganism analogy is valid when: (1) relatedness among colony members is very high (ideally $r \to 1$), so there is no conflict of genetic interest; (2) there is complete reproductive division of labour (only the queen reproduces); and (3) colony members cannot increase their fitness by behaving selfishly (ecological constraints prevent independent reproduction). Under these conditions, selection acts on the colony as a unit, and the analogy to a multicellular organism (where cells are genetically identical and only germ cells reproduce) is strong. The analogy breaks down when: (1) relatedness is less than 1 (e.g., multiple queens, polyandry), creating potential for conflict; (2) workers retain the ability to reproduce and may lay their own eggs (worker policing and egg-eating demonstrate this conflict); (3) different genetic lineages within the colony have divergent interests. The superorganism concept is therefore a useful approximation under some conditions but not a universal truth.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, short answer).**

Vampire bats regurgitate blood meals to feed roost-mates who failed to feed. The donors pay a cost (sharing their meal) and the recipients gain a benefit (avoiding starvation). Many roost-mates are not close relatives. How does this behaviour persist?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If not kin selection, what other mechanism could maintain cooperation?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Reciprocal altruism.** Vampire bats live in stable roost groups and interact with the same individuals repeatedly over long periods. A bat that donates blood today expects the recipient to reciprocate in the future when the donor is unsuccessful. Bats remember past interactions and preferentially feed individuals who have previously shared with them (a Tit-for-Tat-like strategy). Individuals that fail to reciprocate are excluded from future sharing. Wilkinson's (1984) field study demonstrated that regurgitation was directed preferentially toward past donors and close associates, confirming the reciprocal altruism mechanism.

</details>

</aside>

## Inclusive fitness in the age of genomics [Master]

Modern genomic methods allow direct measurement of relatedness using genetic markers. Genome-wide SNP data provide actual pairwise relatedness estimates that can differ from pedigree-based expectations due to Mendelian sampling variance. This has led to the development of **relatedness estimators** (Queller-Goodnight, Wang, Lynch-Ritland) that enable empirical tests of Hamilton's rule in wild populations.

The **inclusive fitness** approach has been challenged by Nowak, Tarnita, and Wilson (2010), who argued that Hamilton's rule is rarely quantitatively accurate and that standard natural selection models suffice. This critique was rebutted by over 100 evolutionary biologists in a signed letter (Abbot et al. 2011), defending inclusive fitness as the correct and general framework for social evolution. The debate is partly semantic (what counts as "inclusive fitness" vs. "standard selection") and partly substantive (whether the simplifying assumptions of Hamilton's rule hold in complex social systems).

- **Population genetics [19.04.01]** provides the framework for modelling allele frequency change under kin selection. The inclusive fitness approach can be derived from standard population-genetic models.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Hamilton's 1964 papers are among the most cited in evolutionary biology. Working largely in isolation at the London School of Economics and later at the University of Michigan, Hamilton developed the inclusive fitness framework as a mathematical extension of Fisher's fundamental theorem. His insight was to recognise that fitness must be measured not just by an individual's own reproduction but by the total effect of its behaviour on the propagation of its genes, whether those genes are in its own body or in relatives.

The concept was initially called "kin selection" by Maynard Smith (1964), a term that has persisted even though Hamilton's framework is more general (it applies to any situation where actors and recipients are genetically correlated, not only kin). Trivers (1971) extended the framework to non-kin with reciprocal altruism.

The application of kin selection to human behaviour in Wilson's *Sociobiology* (1975) sparked the "sociobiology debate," with critics (Gould, Lewontin) arguing that genetic explanations of human social behaviour were reductionist and politically dangerous. The debate continues in modified form around evolutionary psychology.

Philosophically, Hamilton's rule resolves the apparent paradox of altruism by shifting the unit of analysis from the individual organism to the gene. This "gene's-eye view" was popularised by Dawkins's *The Selfish Gene* (1976) and remains the dominant framework for understanding the evolution of social behaviour.
