## Intuition [Beginner]

Islands are natural experiments in ecology. Every island has a definite boundary, a known area, and a known distance from the mainland. These facts make islands ideal for studying what determines how many species live in a given place.

Large islands near the mainland tend to have many species; small islands far from the mainland tend to have few. This pattern is so reliable it can be described mathematically. The **theory of island biogeography**, developed by Robert MacArthur and Edward Osborne Wilson in 1967, explains why: the number of species on an island represents a dynamic balance between new species arriving (**immigration**) and existing species disappearing (**extinction**).

Think of a bathtub with the tap running and the drain open. If the inflow rate equals the outflow rate, the water level stays constant — even though individual water molecules are constantly entering and leaving. Island species work the same way: the total count stays roughly steady while individual species come and go.

This idea also applies to habitat fragments. A patch of forest surrounded by farmland is effectively an island in a sea of agriculture. The same principles — area, isolation, immigration, extinction — govern how many species the fragment can support.

The equilibrium species count depends on two factors. **Area**: larger islands support bigger populations, so extinction rates are lower and the equilibrium is higher. **Distance**: nearer islands receive more immigrants, so immigration rates are higher and the equilibrium is higher. The intersection of the two curves gives the predicted species number.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The Galapagos Islands, about 1000 km off the coast of Ecuador, provide a classic illustration. Among these islands, plant species richness closely follows the species-area relationship. Isabela, the largest island at about 4600 km$^2$, supports roughly 400 plant species. Genovesa, a small island of about 14 km$^2$, supports about 60 plant species.

The distance effect is also visible. Cocos Island, roughly the same size as some Galapagos islands but located 500 km from the mainland (versus 1000 km for Galapagos), has different species counts because its isolation differs. In the West Indies, species richness on islands decreases systematically with distance from the South American mainland, even after accounting for island area.

The species-area relationship follows a power law: doubling the area does not double the number of species — it increases by a smaller factor. This means that a single large reserve typically holds more species total than several small reserves of the same combined area, a result with direct consequences for conservation planning.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### The species-area relationship

The **species-area relationship** (SAR) describes how species richness $S$ increases with area $A$. The most widely used form is the power-law model (Arrhenius 1921):

$$
S = cA^z,
$$

where $c$ is a constant that depends on the taxonomic group and biogeographic region, and $z$ is the slope parameter, typically in the range $0.20$–$0.35$ for true islands and habitat fragments. Taking logarithms gives a linear relationship:

$$
\log S = \log c + z \log A.
$$

Values of $z$ near $0.25$ are typical for oceanic islands; values near $0.15$–$0.20$ are typical for samples within continuous habitats (the "mainland" SAR). The difference arises because islands sample from a fixed pool with isolation effects, while mainland samples census nested areas within a contiguous community.

### The MacArthur-Wilson equilibrium model

MacArthur and Wilson (1967) modelled species richness on an island as the outcome of two opposing processes. Let $P$ be the size of the mainland species pool, $S$ the number of species currently on the island, and $S^*$ the equilibrium species number.

**Immigration rate** $\lambda(S)$: the rate at which new species colonise the island. This is a decreasing function of $S$, because as more species from the pool are already present, fewer new species remain to arrive. At $S = 0$, $\lambda = \lambda_{\max}$ (maximum immigration rate). At $S = P$, $\lambda = 0$ (all mainland species are already present).

**Extinction rate** $\mu(S)$: the rate at which resident species go extinct on the island. This is an increasing function of $S$, because more species means more populations at risk. At $S = 0$, $\mu = 0$ (nothing to go extinct). $\mu$ also depends on island area: for a given $S$, smaller islands have higher extinction rates because populations are smaller.

**Equilibrium** $S^*$ is the value of $S$ where $\lambda(S^*) = \mu(S^*)$. At this point, the rate of new arrivals equals the rate of disappearances. The equilibrium is dynamic: individual species continue to arrive and go extinct, but the total number oscillates around $S^*$.

The simplest linear form sets $\lambda(S) = \lambda_{\max}(1 - S/P)$ and $\mu(S) = \mu_{\max} \cdot S/P$. Then:

$$
S^* = \frac{\lambda_{\max}}{\lambda_{\max} + \mu_{\max}} \cdot P.
$$

The **distance effect**: islands farther from the mainland have lower $\lambda_{\max}$ (harder to reach), shifting the immigration curve downward and reducing $S^*$.

The **area effect**: smaller islands have higher $\mu_{\max}$ (smaller populations, higher extinction risk), shifting the extinction curve upward and reducing $S^*$.

### Species turnover

At equilibrium, the **turnover rate** $T$ is the number of species replaced per unit time:

$$
T = \lambda(S^*) = \mu(S^*).
$$

Turnover is higher on small islands (high extinction and immigration rates at equilibrium) than on large islands. This prediction has been confirmed empirically: Diamond (1969) documented high turnover on small California Channel Islands using bird census data over several decades.

### Target effect, rescue effect, and nestedness

The **target effect** modifies the distance effect: larger islands are easier to "hit" by dispersing organisms, so they receive more immigrants than small islands at the same distance. This means area affects both extinction (through population size) and immigration (through target size).

The **rescue effect** (Brown and Kodric-Brown 1977): immigration can prevent extinction. A population on the brink of local extinction may be "rescued" by incoming individuals from the mainland or other islands. The rescue effect makes the effective extinction rate on near islands lower than on far islands, because near islands receive more immigrants. This introduces a coupling between distance and extinction, modifying the basic MacArthur-Wilson model.

**Nestedness**: in an archipelago, the species on species-poor islands tend to form subsets of the species on species-rich islands. A small island does not contain a random sample from the pool — it contains the most widespread, resilient species that also occur on the larger islands. Nestedness arises when extinction risk is non-random: species with small populations or narrow habitat requirements disappear first from small islands, leaving a core set of generalists.

### Relaxation

When a continuous habitat is fragmented into isolated patches, species are initially present at densities appropriate for the original large area. Over time, species that cannot sustain populations in the smaller fragments gradually disappear. This process is called **relaxation**, and the time lag between fragmentation and the new, lower equilibrium species count can be decades to centuries. The current species richness of a recently created fragment overestimates its long-term equilibrium — a phenomenon called **extinction debt**.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **The species-area relationship is not a sampling artefact.** The power-law SAR holds for true isolates (islands, habitat fragments) where extinction and immigration dynamics are at work, not just for nested census plots within continuous habitat. The "island" SAR (z ~ 0.25-0.35) is steeper than the "mainland" SAR (z ~ 0.12-0.18), and the two reflect different processes: the former reflects genuine loss of species from isolates, while the latter reflects sampling more individuals in larger areas.

- **Equilibrium does not mean static.** The MacArthur-Wilson equilibrium is a dynamic equilibrium. Species constantly arrive and disappear. The number of species is approximately constant, but the identities of those species change. Confusing dynamic equilibrium with a static species list is a common error.

- **The SAR does not prove causation from area alone.** Larger islands often have greater habitat diversity (more elevation zones, more habitat types), which contributes to higher species richness independently of area per se. Disentangling pure area effects from habitat diversity effects requires comparing islands of similar area but different habitat heterogeneity, or statistically controlling for habitat diversity.

- **Island biogeography does not apply only to oceanic islands.** Mountain tops, lakes, forest fragments, caves, host plants for herbivorous insects, and even individual trees in a forest can function as "islands" for the organisms that inhabit them. The theory applies whenever a habitat patch is surrounded by inhospitable matrix that limits dispersal.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (MacArthur-Wilson equilibrium species number).** *Consider an island at distance $d$ from a mainland species pool of size $P$. Let the immigration rate be $\lambda(S, d) = \lambda_0 e^{-kd}(1 - S/P)$, where $\lambda_0$ is the maximum immigration rate at zero distance and $k$ is a distance-decay constant. Let the extinction rate be $\mu(S, A) = (e_0 / A^x) \cdot S$, where $A$ is island area, $e_0$ is a baseline extinction rate per species, and $x > 0$ captures the area-dependent reduction in extinction. Then the equilibrium species number is:*

$$
S^* = \frac{\lambda_0 e^{-kd}}{\lambda_0 e^{-kd} + e_0/A^x} \cdot P.
$$

*Furthermore, $S^*$ is an increasing function of $A$ and a decreasing function of $d$.*

*Proof.* Set $\lambda(S^*, d) = \mu(S^*, A)$:

$$
\lambda_0 e^{-kd}\left(1 - \frac{S^*}{P}\right) = \frac{e_0}{A^x} S^*.
$$

Expanding: $\lambda_0 e^{-kd} - \frac{\lambda_0 e^{-kd}}{P} S^* = \frac{e_0}{A^x} S^*$. Collecting $S^*$ terms on one side:

$$
\lambda_0 e^{-kd} = S^*\left(\frac{\lambda_0 e^{-kd}}{P} + \frac{e_0}{A^x}\right).
$$

Solving for $S^*$:

$$
S^* = \frac{\lambda_0 e^{-kd}}{\frac{\lambda_0 e^{-kd}}{P} + \frac{e_0}{A^x}} = \frac{\lambda_0 e^{-kd}}{\frac{\lambda_0 e^{-kd} A^x + e_0 P}{PA^x}} = \frac{\lambda_0 e^{-kd} P A^x}{\lambda_0 e^{-kd} A^x + e_0 P} = \frac{\lambda_0 e^{-kd}}{\lambda_0 e^{-kd} + e_0/A^x} \cdot P.
$$

To show $S^*$ increases with $A$, compute $\partial S^*/\partial A$. Write $S^* = P \cdot f(A)$ where $f(A) = \lambda_0 e^{-kd}/(\lambda_0 e^{-kd} + e_0/A^x)$. Since $e_0/A^x$ is a decreasing function of $A$, the denominator decreases as $A$ increases, so $f(A)$ increases, and $S^*$ increases with $A$.

To show $S^*$ decreases with $d$, observe that $\lambda_0 e^{-kd}$ is a decreasing function of $d$. Write $S^* = P \cdot g(d)$ where $g(d) = \lambda_0 e^{-kd}/(\lambda_0 e^{-kd} + e_0/A^x)$. Computing:

$$
\frac{\partial g}{\partial d} = \frac{-k\lambda_0 e^{-kd}(\lambda_0 e^{-kd} + e_0/A^x) - \lambda_0 e^{-kd}(-k\lambda_0 e^{-kd})}{(\lambda_0 e^{-kd} + e_0/A^x)^2} = \frac{-k\lambda_0 e^{-kd} \cdot e_0/A^x}{(\lambda_0 e^{-kd} + e_0/A^x)^2} < 0.
$$

So $S^*$ decreases with distance $d$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The MacArthur-Wilson equilibrium connects to the community ecology framework of [19.10.01]: immigration and extinction are population-level processes, but their balance determines community-level species richness. The competitive exclusion principle from [19.10.01] feeds into the extinction rate — species that lose competitive interactions on the island face higher extinction risk. The equilibrium model also connects to metapopulation dynamics [19.09.02]: each species on the island can be viewed as a metapopulation occupying the "island patch," with colonisation corresponding to immigration and local extinction corresponding to species loss.

Explain the difference between the distance effect and the area effect in the MacArthur-Wilson model.

Distance affects immigration. Area affects extinction. How?

**Exercise 3 (medium, short answer).**

Two islands have the same area but differ in distance from the mainland: island A is 10 km away, island B is 200 km away. Both are at equilibrium. Which island has higher species turnover? Explain.

Island B (distant) likely has higher turnover per species at equilibrium, but the total turnover rate depends on $S^*$. Island B has a lower $S^*$ because its immigration curve is depressed. The key point: island B has fewer species at equilibrium, and each species on island B is more "precarious" (closer to extinction) because the rescue effect is weaker — fewer immigrants arrive to bolster populations. The ratio $T/S^*$ (turnover per species) is higher on island B, meaning the community composition changes faster relative to its size.

A forest is divided into two 100-hectare fragments separated by 2 km of agricultural land. Using island biogeography theory, evaluate whether a single 200-hectare reserve would support more species than the two fragments combined. What role does the slope parameter $z$ play in this comparison?

## The Simberloff-Wilson experiment and equilibrium challenges [Master]

### The Simberloff-Wilson mangrove island experiment

The most direct test of the MacArthur-Wilson equilibrium theory was conducted by Daniel Simberloff and Edward O. Wilson (1969, 1970) in the Florida Keys. They selected eight small mangrove islands, censused all terrestrial arthropod species on each island, and then enclosed each island in a tent and fumigated with methyl bromide to defaunate it completely — killing all arthropods while leaving the mangrove trees intact.

After defaunation, they monitored recolonisation at regular intervals. The results strongly supported the equilibrium model:

1. **Species number returned to approximately pre-defaunation levels.** Within about 200–300 days, each island had regained roughly the same number of arthropod species it had before fumigation. This is the predicted equilibrium: the same island, with the same area and isolation, should support the same $S^*$.

2. **Species composition differed.** Although the *number* of species returned to pre-defaunation levels, the *identities* of those species were substantially different. Many original species failed to recolonise, and many new species colonised that had not been present before. This turnover is exactly what the equilibrium model predicts — $S^*$ is stable, but the roster of species is dynamic.

3. **Area and distance effects were confirmed.** Larger islands recovered more species; islands closer to the mainland recovered faster. The distance effect was particularly strong for the most isolated island (E1), which had the slowest recolonisation rate.

4. **The rate of approach to equilibrium was consistent with the model.** Initially, species accumulated rapidly (high immigration rate, low extinction rate). As species accumulated, the colonisation rate decreased and the extinction rate increased, producing the predicted decelerating approach to $S^*$.

The experiment was not without limitations. The islands were very small (11–18 m in diameter), the species pool was limited to arthropods that could aerially disperse to mangroves, and the timescale was short (the experiment ran for about one year). Critics noted that some "extinctions" might have been sampling artefacts (failing to detect rare species), and that the equilibrium might be transient rather than long-term. Nevertheless, the experiment remains one of the most elegant tests of a predictive ecological theory and is a landmark in experimental ecology.

### Equilibrium challenges and nonequilibrium hypotheses

The MacArthur-Wilson model assumes that islands reach a stable equilibrium species number. Several challenges to this assumption have been raised:

**Chronic disturbance** can prevent islands from reaching equilibrium. If disturbances (hurricanes, volcanic eruptions, human exploitation) occur frequently enough, species richness may fluctuate around a mean that is lower than the theoretical $S^*$, or may drift without settling. Whittaker (1998) argued that many volcanic islands are chronically disturbed and never reach equilibrium, making the model's predictions unreliable for these systems.

**Nonequilibrium hypotheses** propose that species richness is determined by factors other than the immigration-extinction balance. Hubbell's (2001) neutral theory, for instance, models island species richness as the outcome of stochastic birth-death-immigration processes without requiring an equilibrium in the MacArthur-Wilson sense. The species richness distribution emerges from the fundamental biodiversity parameter $\theta$ and the dispersal limitation parameter $m$, and may fluctuate substantially without converging.

**Transient dynamics** may dominate on geological timescales. Islands formed recently (volcanic islands like Surtsey, which emerged from the sea in 1963) are still accumulating species and have not reached equilibrium. Conversely, islands that are eroding or subsiding may be losing species faster than new ones arrive. The equilibrium model applies most cleanly to islands that are geologically stable and have existed long enough for colonisation and extinction processes to balance.

**Multiple equilibria** are possible when species interactions create feedbacks. An island colonised by a keystone predator may reach a different equilibrium species number than an identical island without the predator. Priority effects and historical contingency can produce alternative stable states, as discussed in [19.10.02] for succession.

### Species-area relationships in fragments versus true islands

The SAR in habitat fragments often differs from that on true oceanic islands. On true islands, the matrix (ocean) is uniformly hostile — no terrestrial species can persist in it. Habitat fragments, by contrast, are surrounded by a matrix that may be partially permeable: some species can cross agricultural land, use hedgerows as corridors, or even persist in the matrix itself.

This difference has several consequences. First, the effective isolation of a fragment depends on the matrix quality — a forest fragment surrounded by mature secondary growth is less isolated than one surrounded by intensive agriculture, even at the same distance. Second, the effective area of a fragment may be larger than its geometric area if species can use edge habitats and the surrounding matrix. Third, edge effects (microclimatic changes at the fragment boundary) can increase extinction rates in small fragments beyond what the area effect alone predicts, because a larger proportion of the fragment is affected by edge conditions.

The SAR slope $z$ for habitat fragments is often higher than for true islands of comparable area, because fragmentation introduces edge effects, reduced connectivity, and human disturbance that compound the area effect. Laurance et al. (2011) documented $z$ values of 0.3–0.5 for forest fragments in the Brazilian Amazon, compared to typical oceanic island values of 0.25–0.35.

### Nested subset analysis

**Nested subset analysis** formalises the assessment of nestedness in species-by-site matrices. The most widely used metric is the **matrix temperature** (Atmar and Patterson 1993), which measures the degree to which the presence-absence matrix deviates from perfect nestedness. A "cold" matrix is perfectly nested (all species-poor sites are subsets of species-rich sites); a "hot" matrix has many unexpected presences and absences.

Patterson and Atmar (1986) analysed land-bridge islands (mountain tops isolated by rising sea levels or valley flooding) and found strong nestedness, supporting the selective extinction hypothesis: species went extinct from small habitat islands in a predictable order determined by population size and habitat specificity. Wright et al. (1998) extended nested subset analysis to a wide range of island systems and found that area and habitat diversity were the best predictors of nestedness, with isolation playing a secondary role.

**Incidence functions** (Diamond 1975) provide a related approach. For each species, the incidence $J_i$ is the probability that species $i$ is present on an island of a given area (or other feature). Incidence functions are typically sigmoidal: species with low area requirements have $J_i \approx 1$ even on small islands, while species requiring large areas have $J_i \approx 0$ on small islands and $J_i \approx 1$ only on the largest islands. The suite of incidence functions across all species in the pool predicts the species-area relationship and nestedness pattern.

### Island-mainland versus island-island models

The classical MacArthur-Wilson model assumes a one-way flow: species immigrate from a mainland source to the island. This **island-mainland model** is appropriate for oceanic islands near a continental source.

The **island-island model** (sometimes called the archipelago model) treats all islands as both sources and recipients. Species can move between islands in either direction, and the species pool is not fixed but is the union of all species present across the archipelago. This model is more realistic for archipelagos like the Galapagos or the West Indies, where inter-island dispersal is significant.

In the island-island model, the equilibrium species number on each island depends on the configuration of the entire archipelago. An island near many other species-rich islands may have a higher $S^*$ than predicted by its area and distance from the mainland alone, because it receives immigrants from neighbouring islands. This produces a spatial coupling that the classical model ignores.

## Metacommunity theory, SLOSS, and conservation applications [Master]

### Metacommunity paradigms applied to islands

The four metacommunity paradigms of Leibold et al. (2004) — patch dynamics, species sorting, mass effects, and neutral — can be applied to island systems.

**Patch dynamics**: islands are patches that undergo local extinction and recolonisation. The competition-colonisation trade-off predicts that inferior competitors can persist on islands from which superior competitors are absent, producing a checkerboard distribution pattern. This paradigm is most applicable to archipelagos with many small islands of similar habitat.

**Species sorting**: different islands provide different environmental conditions, and species track these conditions. An island with a freshwater marsh supports wetland species regardless of its area or isolation. This paradigm emphasises habitat diversity over area per se, and predicts that environmental heterogeneity among islands contributes to regional diversity.

**Mass effects**: dispersal from nearby islands or the mainland maintains species on islands where they would otherwise go extinct. The rescue effect is a mass effect — immigration subsidises sink populations. This paradigm predicts that islands in clusters (near other islands) will have higher species richness than isolated islands of the same area, because they receive more immigrants from multiple sources.

**Neutral**: all species are ecologically equivalent, and island species richness reflects stochastic colonisation and extinction. Hubbell's (2001) unified neutral theory predicts the species-area relationship and species-abundance distributions on islands from just two parameters ($\theta$ and $m$). The neutral prediction for $z$ is approximately $\log(2)/\log(\text{area ratio})$, which matches empirical values of 0.25–0.35 surprisingly well.

### The SLOSS debate

**SLOSS** — Single Large Or Several Small reserves — is the central conservation design question derived from island biogeography theory. If a fixed total area $A_{\text{total}}$ is available for protection, should it be designated as one large reserve of area $A_{\text{total}}$, or as $n$ smaller reserves each of area $A_{\text{total}}/n$?

The MacArthur-Wilson model predicts that a single large reserve has a higher equilibrium species number than any one small reserve, but the *total* species across all small reserves depends on whether they sample different species pools. The SAR analysis gives:

- Single large: $S_1 = c(A_{\text{total}})^z$.
- Several small (assuming independent pools): $S_n = n \cdot c(A_{\text{total}}/n)^z = c \cdot A_{\text{total}}^z \cdot n^{1-z}$.

Since $z < 1$, $n^{1-z} > 1$ for $n > 1$, so the total species across $n$ independent reserves exceeds the species in one large reserve. This calculation favours several small reserves, *provided* the small reserves sample independently from the species pool.

However, several factors reverse this conclusion in practice. First, small reserves are subject to **edge effects** that increase effective extinction rates beyond the pure area prediction. Second, small reserves have higher turnover and are more vulnerable to stochastic extinction. Third, small populations in small reserves may fall below minimum viable population sizes. Fourth, the assumption of independent sampling is often violated — nestedness means small reserves share most species with large ones.

Diamond (1975) argued from incidence functions that a single large reserve is generally superior, because many species require large areas and will be absent from all small reserves regardless of number. Simberloff and Abele (1976) countered that several small reserves can capture more total species if they sample different habitat types or biogeographic sub-regions. The debate remains unresolved in general — the answer depends on the taxonomic group, the landscape configuration, and whether the goal is to maximise total species, protect area-sensitive species, or maintain viable populations of targeted taxa.

### Extinction debt

**Extinction debt** (Tilman et al. 1994) is the number of species committed to extinction following habitat destruction but not yet extinct. When habitat is destroyed, species lose habitat area. The MacArthur-Wilson model predicts that the equilibrium species number for the remaining habitat is lower. However, species do not go extinct instantly — they decline gradually toward the new equilibrium. The difference between the current species number and the new, lower equilibrium is the extinction debt.

Extinction debt means that the full biodiversity impact of habitat destruction is not immediately apparent. A landscape that has lost 50% of its forest may still contain most of the original forest species, but many of those species are "living dead" — populations too small to persist indefinitely. The relaxation time (the time to reach the new equilibrium) can be decades to centuries for long-lived organisms like trees.

Kuussaari et al. (2009) reviewed empirical evidence for extinction debt across taxa and ecosystems. They found that extinction debt is widespread in fragmented landscapes, with relaxation times of 20–500 years depending on taxon and landscape context. Tropical forest fragments showed particularly large extinction debts, because many tropical tree species have very low population densities and long generation times, delaying the extinction response.

### Conservation corridors

Conservation corridors are strips of habitat that connect otherwise isolated fragments, increasing connectivity and reducing the effective isolation of each patch. From the perspective of island biogeography, corridors reduce the effective distance between fragments, increasing immigration rates and enhancing the rescue effect.

The efficacy of corridors has been tested experimentally. The Corridor Experiment at the Savannah River Site in South Carolina (Nick M. Haddad and colleagues) established landscape plots with connected and isolated patches of open habitat within a pine plantation. Over two decades, connected patches maintained higher species richness, higher population densities, and lower extinction rates than isolated patches of the same area. Plant, insect, and bird species all showed positive corridor effects, with the strongest effects for species with limited dispersal ability.

Corridors are not a universal solution. They can facilitate the spread of diseases, invasive species, and fire between patches. Beier and Noss (1998) reviewed corridor studies and concluded that corridors are most effective when they maintain or restore historical connectivity (reconnecting fragments that were once part of a continuous habitat) and are less effective when they create novel connections between historically isolated habitats.

### Ecosystem collapse and regime shifts

Island biogeography provides a framework for understanding ecosystem collapse on habitat fragments. As habitat area decreases, extinction rates increase. Below a critical area threshold, extinction rates may exceed immigration rates permanently, and the community collapses to a much lower species number — a regime shift analogous to the alternative stable states discussed in [19.10.02].

The concept of **minimum viable ecosystem** area extends the minimum viable population concept from population biology to community ecology. Below a critical area, not only do individual species go extinct, but the ecosystem functions they support (pollination, seed dispersal, nutrient cycling) may collapse, producing cascading extinctions that further reduce species richness. This positive feedback between species loss and ecosystem degradation can produce abrupt, nonlinear declines in biodiversity — a ecological tipping point.

Hansen et al. (2013) documented regime shifts in tropical forest fragments in Brazil, where fragments below about 30 hectares experienced cascading tree mortality, canopy degradation, and conversion to vine-dominated scrubland within 10–20 years of isolation. The fragmented forest crossed a threshold below which internal feedbacks (edge-induced drying, wind damage, fire invasion) drove the system to an alternative state.

## Connections [Master]

- **Community ecology [19.10.01]** provides the interaction types and competitive exclusion principle that underlie island species dynamics. Extinction on islands is often driven by competitive exclusion (superior competitors displace inferior ones) and predation (introduced predators cause island extinctions). The keystone species concept from [19.10.01] is relevant because removing a keystone predator from an island can trigger cascading extinctions.

- **Succession [19.10.02]** and island biogeography are complementary. Primary succession on a new volcanic island is the initial phase of the colonisation process that MacArthur and Wilson modelled. The successional trajectory of the island's vegetation affects habitat diversity, which in turn affects the equilibrium species number. The relaxation process after habitat fragmentation is a form of reverse succession — species are lost as the community moves to a new, less diverse equilibrium.

- **Population ecology [19.09.01]** provides the demographic models that determine extinction risk. Small island populations are vulnerable to demographic stochasticity, environmental stochasticity, and genetic drift (the Allee effect). The population models from [19.09.01] feed directly into the extinction rate function $\mu(S)$ of the MacArthur-Wilson model.

- **Metapopulation dynamics [19.09.02]** are the population-level analogue of island biogeography. Each species on an island occupies a "patch" (the island) within a metapopulation whose other patches are on the mainland and other islands. The colonisation-extinction dynamics of metapopulations mirror the immigration-extinction dynamics of island biogeography, but at the population rather than community level.

- **Conservation biology [19.14.01]** applies island biogeography directly to reserve design, habitat fragmentation, and corridor planning. The species-area relationship predicts species loss from habitat destruction, the equilibrium model predicts the long-term species carrying capacity of reserves, and the concept of extinction debt warns that current species counts may overestimate future diversity in fragmented landscapes.

- **Biogeography [19.12.01]** provides the geological and historical context. Island age, geological origin (volcanic, continental, land-bridge), and historical connection to the mainland all modify the predictions of the equilibrium model. Ancient continental islands (like New Zealand or Madagascar) may carry relict species that persist from before isolation, inflating species richness above the MacArthur-Wilson equilibrium.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The theory of island biogeography was published in 1967 by Robert H. MacArthur and Edward O. Wilson in their monograph *The Theory of Island Biogeography* (Princeton University Press, Princeton). MacArthur, a mathematical ecologist at Princeton, and Wilson, a taxonomist and entomologist at Harvard, combined mathematical modelling with extensive natural history knowledge of island faunas — particularly Wilson's expertise on ants of the Pacific and Melanesian islands [ref: TODO_REF MacArthur & Wilson 1967].

The book was revolutionary in several respects. First, it proposed that ecological patterns could be predicted from a small number of measurable parameters (island area, isolation, and the size of the species pool) using a simple dynamical model. This was a departure from the descriptive, natural-history tradition that dominated biogeography. Second, it treated species richness as a dynamic equilibrium rather than a static property, emphasising process over pattern. Third, it made testable predictions that could be evaluated experimentally — a rarity in ecology at the time.

The empirical foundation for the theory was built on decades of biogeographic observation. Philip Darlington's work on carabid beetles on islands in the Southwest Pacific and the West Indies had documented the species-area relationship and distance effect in the 1940s and 1950s [ref: TODO_REF Darlington]. MacArthur and Wilson synthesised these observations into their mathematical framework.

The experimental test came quickly. Daniel Simberloff, a graduate student of Wilson at Harvard, designed the mangrove island defaunation experiment in the Florida Keys. The experiment, published in *Ecology* in 1969 (Simberloff and Wilson) and 1970 (Wilson and Simberloff), became one of the most famous experiments in ecology. Simberloff went on to become a leading critic of some extensions of the theory, arguing that habitat diversity and historical factors were often more important than area and isolation per se [ref: TODO_REF Simberloff & Wilson 1969].

Jared Diamond's (1975) paper "Assembly of species communities" in *Ecology and Evolution of Communities* (ed. Cody and Diamond, Harvard UP) applied island biogeography to land-bridge islands and introduced the concept of incidence functions and assembly rules. Diamond's analysis of bird communities on the islands of New Guinea and the Bismarck Archipelago suggested that competition structured island communities, producing "forbidden combinations" of species that never co-occurred. This claim was challenged by Connor and Simberloff (1979), who argued that the patterns Diamond attributed to competition could be produced by random colonisation and extinction — a debate that continues in various forms [ref: TODO_REF Diamond 1975; Connor & Simberloff 1979].

The SLOSS debate originated with Diamond (1975) and was joined by Simberloff and Abele (1976, *Science* **191**, 285-286). Simberloff and Abele argued that several small reserves could contain more total species than one large reserve, challenging the prevailing conservation assumption that bigger was always better. The debate consumed considerable attention in the 1970s and 1980s and was never fully resolved, though most ecologists now agree that the answer depends on the specific taxa, landscape, and conservation goals [ref: TODO_REF Simberloff & Abele 1976].

The concept of **extinction debt** was formalised by Tilman, May, Lehman, and Nowak (1994, *Nature* **371**, 65-66), who used a simple model to show that habitat destruction commits species to extinction long after the destruction occurs. This concept has become central to conservation biology, because it implies that current biodiversity in fragmented landscapes may be inflated relative to the long-term equilibrium [ref: TODO_REF Tilman et al. 1994].

Stephen Hubbell's *The Unified Neutral Theory of Biodiversity and Biogeography* (Princeton University Press, 2001) challenged the niche-based foundations of island biogeography by showing that many of its predictions — including the species-area relationship — could be derived from a neutral model in which all species are ecologically equivalent. Hubbell's monograph sparked intense debate and stimulated a new generation of research comparing neutral and niche-based explanations for biodiversity patterns [ref: TODO_REF Hubbell 2001].

Mathew Leibold, Jonathan Chase, Jonathan Shurin, and colleagues (2004, *Ecology Letters* **7**, 601-613) organised metacommunity theory into the four paradigms discussed in this unit, providing a synthetic framework that incorporates island biogeography as a special case within a broader theory of spatial community dynamics [ref: TODO_REF Leibold et al. 2004].

The application of island biogeography to conservation was championed by William Newmark (1987, *Nature* **335**, 328), who documented that mammal extinctions in North American national parks followed the patterns predicted by the MacArthur-Wilson model: smaller and more isolated parks had lost more species. Newmark's work was among the first to show that protected areas function as islands and lose species according to island biogeography predictions [ref: TODO_REF Newmark 1987].
