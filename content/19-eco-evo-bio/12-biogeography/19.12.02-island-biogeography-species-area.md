## Intuition [Beginner]

Picture a bathtub with the tap running and the plug partly out. The water climbs until the outflow matches the inflow, then holds level. An island's species count behaves the same way. Fresh species arrive like water from a tap — that is **immigration**. Resident species die out like water down the drain — that is **extinction**. The level where the two balance is the **equilibrium species number**.

Two knobs move that level. A **bigger island shrinks the drain**: larger populations persist longer, so extinction runs slower. A **nearer island opens the tap wider**: colonizers cross a short sea gap more easily, so immigration runs faster. A large island close to a mainland sits high on the curve; a small, remote island sits low. The number $S^*$ stays steady, but the identities holding each slot keep swapping — this is **species turnover**.

The same logic now rules parks and forest fragments ringed by farmland. When continuous habitat is chopped into pieces, each piece "relaxes" toward its own smaller $S^*$, shedding species for decades. That raises the central question of reserve design: is one large reserve better than several small ones of equal total area? This debate — **SLOSS**, Single Large or Several Small — is island biogeography turned into policy.

## Visual [Beginner]

The equilibrium model as two crossing rate curves. Immigration falls as the island fills toward the mainland pool $P$; extinction rises as crowding grows. Where they cross sits $S^*$.

```
Rate
 ^
 | I_0  \                         immigration  I(S) = I_0 (1 - S/P)
 |       \                          (high on empty island -> 0 at full pool)
 |        \
 |         \                      extinction  E(S) = (e/A) * S
 |          \                       (rises with crowding; flatter on big islands)
 |           \      /
 |            \    /
 |             \  /
 |              \/  <-  equilibrium S*   (I = E here)
 |              /\
 |             /  \
 |            /    \   turnover T* = I(S*) = E(S*)
 |           /      \   (rate at which identities swap while S* holds)
 |          /        \
 |_________/__________\____________________>  S  (species on island)
 0        S*          P                     (mainland species pool)
```

How area and isolation move the crossing point:

| Island type | Immigration curve | Extinction curve | Equilibrium $S^*$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Large and near | high (starts at large $I_0$) | flat (small slope $e/A$) | highest |
| Small and near | high | steep (large $e/A$) | intermediate |
| Large and far | low (small $I_0$) | flat | intermediate |
| Small and far | low | steep | lowest |

The SLOSS choice, drawn at equal total area:

```

area A, one block               n blocks each of area A/n

+------------------------+      +------+ +------+ +------+
|                        |      |      | |      | |      |
|   S = c * A^z          |      |c(A/n)^z| |...  | |c(A/n)^z|
|                        |      |      | |      | |      |
+------------------------+      +------+ +------+ +------+

because z < 1, species            each block holds fewer species;
richness rises sublinearly        the UNION can exceed option A
with area, so concentrating      only if blocks differ in species

```

## Worked example [Beginner]

A conservation agency can buy land for forest birds in one of two configurations with the same total area. For this region the species-area rule is $S = 10\,A^{0.30}$, with $A$ in km$^2$.

**Option A — one large reserve of 100 km$^2$:**

$$S_A = 10 \times 100^{0.30} = 10 \times 3.98 \approx 40 \text{ species.}$$

**Option B — four small reserves of 25 km$^2$ each:**

$$S_{\text{each}} = 10 \times 25^{0.30} = 10 \times 2.63 \approx 26 \text{ species per block.}$$

If the four blocks sit in the same forest and share most of their birds, the union is close to 26 species — far below the 40 of the single large block. If the blocks are spread across different habitats with little overlap, their union could approach $4 \times 26 = 104$, but real reserves always share species, so the truth lies between.

**What this tells us.** Because $z$ is less than 1, doubling area does not double species: richness rises sublinearly with area, so concentrating habitat usually packs in more species than splitting it. That is the engine behind the Single Large side of SLOSS. The exception — when several small blocks actually win — is the subject of the deeper analysis below.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let an island lie at distance $D$ from a species-source mainland whose pool contains $P$ species. Define two rate functions of the number of species $S$ currently on the island [ref: TODO_REF MacArthur & Wilson 1967]:

- the **immigration rate** $I(S)$, the number of new species arriving per unit time when $S$ species are already present;
- the **extinction rate** $E(S)$, the number of resident species lost per unit time.

In the linear model the two rates are straight lines:

$$I(S) = I_0\!\left(1 - \frac{S}{P}\right), \qquad E(S) = \frac{e}{A}\,S,$$

where $I_0 = I_0(D)$ is the maximum immigration rate (a decreasing function of isolation $D$), $e$ is a per-species extinction coefficient, and $A$ is island area. Two derived quantities carry the model's predictive content:

- the **equilibrium species number** $S^*$, defined by $I(S^*) = E(S^*)$;
- the **turnover rate at equilibrium** $T^* = I(S^*) = E(S^*)$, the rate at which species identities are exchanged while $S^*$ holds.

The **species-area relationship** (SAR) is the power law

$$S = c\,A^{z} \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad \log S = \log c + z\log A,$$

where $c$ is a taxon- and region-dependent constant and $z$ is the slope of the line on a log-log plot. For isolated islands and other true isolates, $z \in [0.25, 0.35]$; for nested subareas censused within a single continuous mainland, $z \in [0.10, 0.20]$ [ref: TODO_REF Rosenzweig 1995]. The two ranges reflect different processes, examined in the Advanced results.

## Core model [Intermediate+] {#core-model}

Setting $I(S^*) = E(S^*)$ and solving gives the equilibrium count and the turnover rate [ref: TODO_REF MacArthur & Wilson 1963]:

$$S^* = \frac{P\,I_0\,A}{I_0 A + e P}, \qquad T^* = \frac{e\,P\,I_0}{I_0 A + e P}.$$

The formula encodes four qualitative claims, each readable as a statement about which way the two rate lines move. Increasing area $A$ flattens the extinction line (its slope $e/A$ shrinks); the crossing point slides right and down, so $S^*$ rises while $T^*$ falls. Large islands therefore hold more species *and* turn over more slowly. Raising isolation depresses $I_0$; the immigration line pivots downward, the crossing slides left, and both $S^*$ and $T^*$ fall. Remote islands are species-poor and sluggish. Combining the two, the four island types rank as $S^*_{\text{large, near}} > S^*_{\text{large, far}} \approx S^*_{\text{small, near}} > S^*_{\text{small, far}}$, with the middle ordering parameter-dependent but the extremes robust.

### The rescue effect: a correction to the basic model

Brown and Kodric-Brown (1977) observed that immigration does more than add new species — incoming individuals also prop up sinking populations of species already present, depressing their extinction rate [ref: TODO_REF Brown & Kodric-Brown 1977]. The corrected extinction rate acquires an isolation dependence:

$$E(S, D) = \frac{e}{A}\bigl(1 - \mu\,I_0(D)\bigr) S, \qquad 0 \le \mu < 1,$$

so near islands (large $I_0$) suffer lower extinction than far islands of identical size. The rescue effect amplifies the area-by-isolation interaction: it steepens the contrast between near and far islands beyond what the original model allows, and it explains why metapopulations of poor dispersers persist only within well-connected archipelagos.

### Evidence: fumigation, volcanoes, and turnover

The most direct test is the Simberloff-Wilson mangrove experiment. Small mangrove islands in Florida Bay were censused for arthropods, encased in tents, and fumigated with methyl bromide to eliminate the fauna; recolonization was then tracked [ref: TODO_REF Simberloff & Wilson 1969]. Three predictions were confirmed. Species numbers returned to approximately pre-defaunation levels, supporting an equilibrium. The particular species differed from the original community, confirming turnover. Larger and nearer islands regained species faster and supported higher equilibria, matching the area and isolation gradients. Natural experiments agree: Surtsey, which emerged from the sea south of Iceland in 1963, accumulated vascular plants along a decelerating curve toward an equilibrium consistent with its area and isolation; Krakatau, sterilized by eruption in 1883, recolonized along the same predicted trajectory.

The model's limitations are the entry to its modern descendants. It treats the species pool $P$ as fixed and external, ignoring in-situ evolution; yet oceanic islands are evolutionary engines whose endemic radiations (Hawaiian honeycreepers, Galapagos finches, anoline lizards) change the pool itself over time. It is silent on habitat heterogeneity, trophic structure, and environmental stochasticity. These gaps motivate the incidence-function, neutral, and general-dynamic generalizations taken up at Master tier.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

An island lies 2 km from a mainland pool of $P = 200$ species. Its maximum immigration rate is $I_0 = 10$ species/year, its per-species extinction coefficient is $e = 2$/year, and its area is $A = 5$ km$^2$. Compute the equilibrium species number $S^*$ and the turnover rate $T^*$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $S^* = \dfrac{P I_0 A}{I_0 A + e P}$ and $T^* = \dfrac{e P I_0}{I_0 A + e P}$. As a check, confirm $T^* = (e/A)\,S^*$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$$S^* = \frac{200 \cdot 10 \cdot 5}{10 \cdot 5 + 2 \cdot 200} = \frac{10000}{450} \approx 22.2 \text{ species.}$$

$$T^* = \frac{2 \cdot 200 \cdot 10}{450} = \frac{4000}{450} \approx 8.9 \text{ species/year.}$$

Check: $(e/A)\,S^* = (2/5)(22.2) = 8.9$. ✓ The island holds about 22 species at equilibrium, with roughly 9 arrivals and 9 losses each year — the roster turns over nearly half its slots annually even though the count is stable.

</details>

**Exercise 2 (medium, numeric).**

Two islands in the same archipelago give the following bird counts: a 10 km$^2$ island holds 30 species; a 1000 km$^2$ island holds 120 species. Estimate the species-area exponent $z$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On the log-log form $\log S = \log c + z\log A$, the exponent is the slope between the two data points: $z = \dfrac{\log S_2 - \log S_1}{\log A_2 - \log A_1}$.

$$z = \frac{\log 120 - \log 30}{\log 1000 - \log 10} = \frac{\log(120/30)}{\log(1000/10)} = \frac{\log 4}{\log 100} = \frac{0.602}{2.00} \approx 0.30.$$

The exponent $z \approx 0.30$ falls squarely in the isolate range $[0.25, 0.35]$, as expected for true islands. The constant is $c = 30 / 10^{0.30} \approx 15$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, short answer).**

Near islands hold more species than far islands, yet they also have *higher* equilibrium turnover $T^*$. Explain why having more species does not imply more stability, using the rate-curve picture.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Turnover is the *height* of the crossing point on the rate axis, not its horizontal position. Consider what a large $I_0$ does to where the two lines meet vertically.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$S^*$ is the horizontal coordinate of the crossing of $I(S)$ and $E(S)$; $T^*$ is its vertical coordinate. Raising $I_0$ lifts the immigration line, which moves the crossing both rightward (more species) and upward (a higher shared rate). A near island therefore has a large $S^*$ *and* a large $T^*$: its faucet runs fast, so even though it holds more species, individuals and species are constantly cycling through. By contrast, increasing area $A$ flattens the extinction line, sliding the crossing rightward but *downward*, so large islands have both more species and lower turnover. The two "is good for diversity" knobs — area and nearness — act on different axes and have opposite effects on stability.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short answer).**

State the rescue effect and predict its observable signature in a comparison of near and far islands of equal area.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Recall that arriving immigrants can also rescue extant populations, not only found new ones. Which rate, $I$ or $E$, does this modify, and on which type of island?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Summary</summary>

The rescue effect is the depression of a resident species' extinction probability by continued immigration: immigrants replenish sinking populations and so reduce the effective extinction rate below the value the basic model assigns. It introduces an isolation dependence into $E(S)$ itself, since near islands receive more immigrants. The observable signature is that near islands of a given area show lower extinction rates and longer persistence of any given species than equally sized far islands, *beyond* what the extra species count alone would predict. Operationally, per-species incidence (the probability of finding a given species on the island) declines more slowly with isolation than the unrescued model allows, and population sizes of shared species are larger and less variable on near islands.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (hard, numeric).**

A region can be protected either as one 400 km$^2$ block or as eight 50 km$^2$ blocks. Use the species-area rule with $z = 0.25$ (and absorb $c$ into the comparison). Suppose each small block shares a common core of species with the others but has a fraction $f$ unique to it, so the union over eight blocks is $S_{\text{union}} = S_{\text{small}}\bigl[(1-f) + 8f\bigr]$. For what value of $f$ do the two options protect equal species, and which option wins above that threshold?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $S_{\text{large}} = c\cdot 400^{0.25}$ and $S_{\text{small}} = c\cdot 50^{0.25}$, then solve $S_{\text{union}} = S_{\text{large}}$ for $f$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$S_{\text{large}} = c\cdot 400^{0.25} = c\cdot 4.472$. $S_{\text{small}} = c\cdot 50^{0.25} = c\cdot 2.659$. Setting the union equal to the single large block:

$$2.659\,c\bigl[(1-f) + 8f\bigr] = 4.472\,c \;\Longrightarrow\; (1 + 7f) = \frac{4.472}{2.659} \approx 1.682.$$

So $7f \approx 0.682$, giving $f \approx 0.097$. If less than about 10% of each small block's species are unique to it (low beta diversity), the single large reserve wins; if more than 10% are unique (high beta diversity), the eight small reserves collectively protect more species. This formalizes the modern SLOSS resolution: the answer turns on between-site complementarity, not on area alone.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, analysis).**

A continuous forest covering 1000 km$^2$ is reduced by logging to 100 km$^2$ over two years. Using $z = 0.30$, predict the *eventual* species loss relative to the original community, and explain why the observed loss in the decade immediately following fragmentation is typically much smaller. Define **relaxation** and **extinction debt**.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $S_{\text{new}}/S_{\text{old}} = (A_{\text{new}}/A_{\text{old}})^{z}$. Then consider how quickly populations actually reach their new, smaller equilibrium.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The eventual species ratio is $(100/1000)^{0.30} = 0.10^{0.30} \approx 0.50$, so about half the original species are predicted to be lost once the new equilibrium is reached. The immediate post-fragmentation community, however, still holds most of the original species: the long-lived trees, slow-breeding vertebrates, and large-population invertebrates do not vanish instantly. **Relaxation** is the time-lagged approach of the community from its initial (over-full) state down to the new equilibrium $S^*$ appropriate to the reduced area. The species "committed to extinction" by the area loss but not yet gone are the **extinction debt**. The debt is paid over decades to centuries, with the time scale set by generation length and population dynamics: slow-breeding species linger longest. This lag is why fragmented landscapes often look healthier than they are, and why the species-area model must be read as a long-term prediction, not a snapshot.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master] {#advanced-results}

Three threads extend the equilibrium model: a theory of the exponent $z$, a per-species stochastic reformulation, and the resolution of SLOSS in terms of beta diversity. Each dissolves a limitation of the original theory without abandoning its core.

### Why $z \approx 0.25$ to $0.35$: Preston's canonical lognormal

Preston (1962) observed that the abundances of species in a large community typically follow a lognormal distribution. If that lognormal has its "veil line" (the abundance below which species go unsampled) fixed by the sample size in the canonical position, the species-area slope predicted for an independent sample is $z \approx \log_{10} 2 / \log_{10} e \approx 0.301$ — Preston's canonical value, close to the empirical isolate mean [ref: TODO_REF Rosenzweig 1995]. May (1975) recovered the same result from the sampling statistics of a broken-stick abundance model. The deeper point is Rosenzweig's typology: the species-area relationship is not one phenomenon but four. A *within-area* curve (nested sub-transects of one census) gives shallow $z \approx 0.13$ because the sub-samples share species by construction. A *continental* curve (independent points within one biome) gives $z \approx 0.15$ to $0.20$. An *inter-provincial* curve (comparing separate biogeographic provinces) gives steeper $z \approx 0.6$ to $1.0$, and the *global* curve steeper still. Only the first two are "ecological"; the last two mix ecological with evolutionary time and confound ecological turnover with phylogenetic divergence. Treating all species-area data as interchangeable is the most common error in applied uses of the model.

### Incidence functions: from $S^*$ to per-species probabilities

The deterministic $S^*$ is the mean of an underlying stochastic process. For each species $i$, model presence as a birth-death process with colonization rate $c_i$ and local extinction rate $e_i$ [ref: TODO_REF Whittaker & Fernandez-Palacios 2007]. At stochastic equilibrium the probability that species $i$ occupies the island is

$$p_i^{\text{eq}} = \frac{c_i}{c_i + e_i},$$

and the expected richness is $S^* = \sum_i p_i^{\text{eq}}$, recovering the aggregate model as a sum over species-specific incidences. This reformulation does three things at once. It makes the rescue effect automatic, since $e_i$ depends on the immigration it receives. It connects island biogeography to metapopulation theory, of which it is the multi-species limit. And it admits heterogeneity: area-sensitive and isolation-sensitive species acquire different $c_i, e_i$, so the community is no longer a homogeneous aggregate. The price is parameter explosion: a community of $P$ species carries $2P$ rates, which must be estimated or constrained by traits.

### The SLOSS resolution

The worked example and Exercise 5 encode the formal content of the SLOSS debate. For one block of area $A$ against $n$ blocks each of area $A/n$, the single-large count is $c A^z$ and the each-small count is $c(A/n)^z$. If each small block contributes a fraction $f$ of unique species, the union is $c(A/n)^z[(1-f) + nf]$. Single Large wins when $f < f^*$, Several Small when $f > f^*$, where $f^* = \dfrac{(A/(A/n))^z - 1}{n - 1} = \dfrac{n^z - 1}{n-1}$. The threshold is set entirely by $z$ and $n$, and the data question is whether real beta diversity clears it. Diamond (1975) argued from incidence functions that large, connected reserves minimize extinction for area-sensitive species; Simberloff and Abele (1976) countered that high beta diversity could make a network of small reserves superior. The synthesis, due to Haila, Margules, Higgs, and others, is that no universal ranking exists: Single Large minimizes extinction risk for populations requiring large contiguous habitat, Several Small maximizes sampled diversity when habitats differ, and the practical answer requires knowing both $z$ and the beta-diversity structure of the region [ref: TODO_REF Losos & Ricklefs 2009].

**Synthesis.** Putting these together, the foundational reason the equilibrium model survives is that its two-rate structure appears again in every descendant: incidence functions are per-species rate equations, metapopulation theory is the rescue effect generalized, neutral theory is the same pool-colonization-extinction balance with demographic equivalence imposed, and the general dynamic model adds only an ontogenetic time axis to the same immigration-extinction bookkeeping. The central insight — that richness is a balance of input and loss rates, not a static property of a place — generalises from oceanic islands to forest fragments, mountaintops, lakes, and host-parasite systems alike, and this is exactly why habitat fragmentation behaves as a second-generation island biogeography. The bridge is that every patch in a fragmented landscape is an island whose $S^*$ has just been moved downward, and the debt it owes is the integral of the difference.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (equilibrium species number and turnover).** In the linear MacArthur-Wilson model with $I(S)=I_0(1-S/P)$ and $E(S)=(e/A)S$, the equilibrium count and turnover rate are $S^* = \dfrac{P I_0 A}{I_0 A + e P}$ and $T^* = \dfrac{e P I_0}{I_0 A + e P}$.

*Proof.* Equilibrium requires $I(S^*) = E(S^*)$, that is $I_0(1 - S^*/P) = (e/A)S^*$. Expanding the left side gives $I_0 - (I_0/P)S^* = (e/A)S^*$. Collecting the terms in $S^*$ onto the right yields $I_0 = S^*\bigl(\tfrac{I_0}{P} + \tfrac{e}{A}\bigr)$, and dividing through by the bracketed factor produces $S^* = I_0\big/\bigl(\tfrac{I_0}{P}+\tfrac{e}{A}\bigr)$. Multiplying numerator and denominator by $PA$ gives $S^* = \dfrac{P I_0 A}{I_0 A + e P}$, the first claim. For the turnover, evaluate either rate at $S^*$: $E(S^*) = (e/A)\cdot\dfrac{P I_0 A}{I_0 A + e P} = \dfrac{e P I_0}{I_0 A + e P}$, where the factor $A$ cancels, and the same value is obtained from $I(S^*)$ by direct substitution. This is $T^*$, the second claim. $\square$

**Proposition (area loss and species loss).** If habitat area is reduced to a fraction $\phi \in (0,1]$ of its original value, the species-area law $S=cA^z$ predicts a retained fraction $S_{\text{new}}/S_{\text{old}} = \phi^{\,z}$ of species and a lost fraction $1 - \phi^{\,z}$.

*Proof.* Write $S_{\text{old}} = c A^z$ for the original area $A$ and $S_{\text{new}} = c(\phi A)^z$ for the reduced area. Forming the ratio, the constant $c$ and the original area cancel: $S_{\text{new}}/S_{\text{old}} = c(\phi A)^z/(c A^z) = \phi^z A^z/A^z = \phi^z$. Because $0 < \phi < 1$ and $z>0$, the power $\phi^z$ lies strictly between $\phi$ and $1$, so the proportional species loss $1 - \phi^z$ is always smaller than the proportional area loss $1-\phi$: halving habitat loses fewer than half the species, which is the asymmetry that makes the species-area curve a predictive tool for conservation. $\square$

**Proposition (SLOSS threshold).** Comparing one reserve of area $A$ to $n$ reserves each of area $A/n$ with per-block uniqueness fraction $f$, the two configurations protect equal species when $f = f^* = \dfrac{n^z - 1}{n - 1}$, with Several Small favored for $f > f^*$ and Single Large for $f < f^*$.

*Proof.* The single large block holds $S_{\text{L}} = c A^z$. Each small block holds $S_{\text{s}} = c(A/n)^z = c A^z n^{-z} = S_{\text{L}}\,n^{-z}$. Under the overlap model the union over $n$ small blocks is $S_{\text{union}} = S_{\text{s}}[(1-f)+nf] = S_{\text{L}}\,n^{-z}[1 + (n-1)f]$. Setting $S_{\text{union}} = S_{\text{L}}$ and cancelling $S_{\text{L}}$ gives $n^{-z}[1+(n-1)f] = 1$, that is $1 + (n-1)f = n^z$. Solving for $f$ yields $f = (n^z - 1)/(n-1) = f^*$. Because $S_{\text{union}}$ is strictly increasing in $f$ while $S_{\text{L}}$ is independent of it, the inequality reverses across the threshold: Several Small wins above $f^*$ and Single Large wins below it. Since $0 < z < 1$ implies $1 < n^z < n$, the threshold $f^*$ lies in $(0,1)$, so a decisive answer always exists once beta diversity is measured. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Biogeography (general) [19.12.01].** This unit deepens the island subtheory sketched in the parent unit, which named the equilibrium idea but left its rate-curve mechanics, turnover algebra, rescue effect, and SLOS consequences for the present treatment. The species-area exponent $z$ studied here is the same quantity the parent used heuristically; here it receives its Prestonian and Rosenzweigian interpretation, and the four-type typology distinguishes the isolate $z$ from the mainland $z$ that the parent conflated.

- **Community ecology [19.10.01].** Island biogeography is at base a community-assembly model: it predicts how many species coexist given a pool and a set of arrival and loss rates. The parent community-ecology unit's treatment of interactions (competition, predation, mutualism) supplies the mechanism by which the extinction rate $E(S)$ steepens as the island fills, since biotic resistance and competitive displacement raise local extinction. Rescue-effect and incidence-function models are most realistic when $E(S)$ embeds those interaction terms rather than treating them as a single slope.

- **Conservation biology [19.14.01].** The SLOSS threshold, the extinction-debt calculation, and the species-area prediction of biodiversity loss from habitat reduction are direct inputs to reserve design and red-list forecasting. The Full proof set's area-loss proposition is the equation behind estimates of future species commitment under deforestation scenarios, and the SLOSS proposition converts directly into the decision rule a reserve planner applies when allocating a fixed conservation budget across one large or several small parcels.

- **Metapopulation dynamics [19.09.02].** The incidence-function reformulation $p_i^{\text{eq}} = c_i/(c_i+e_i)$ is, species-by-species, the Levins metapopulation model. The rescue effect is the colonization-rescue term of metapopulation persistence, and an archipelago of habitat fragments is precisely the spatial setting in which the single-island $S^*$ must be replaced by a coupled patch network whose colonization rates depend on inter-patch distances.

- **Natural selection and speciation [19.03.01].** The equilibrium model is ecological: it takes the mainland pool $P$ as fixed. Over evolutionary time the pool itself is not fixed, and adaptive radiation on isolated archipelagos generates the endemic species that constitute an island's deviation above its immigration-only equilibrium. The selective pressures and founder-event speciation treated in the evolution units are what turn an island from a passive sampler of the mainland into a generator of novelties, which is the engine MacArthur and Wilson's purely ecological model does not capture.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Island biogeography before 1963 was a descriptive enterprise. Wallace, Darlington, and Mayr had catalogued the regularities — more species on larger islands, fewer on remote ones, impoverished but endemic biotas on oceanic islands — without a predictive mechanism. Robert MacArthur and E. O. Wilson changed this with a 1963 paper in *Evolution* and the 1967 monograph *The Theory of Island Biogeography* [ref: TODO_REF MacArthur & Wilson 1963], casting species number as the equilibrium of two measurable rate processes. The move was programmatic: ecology, they argued, could aspire to the predictive, model-based form of physics, with immigration and extinction playing the roles of birth and death rates in a demographic balance.

The 1969 Simberloff-Wilson mangrove fumigation experiment was the field's first manipulative test of a population-level theory and remains its most cited [ref: TODO_REF Simberloff & Wilson 1969]. It also seeded the discipline's longest-running policy argument. Jared Diamond's 1975 design principles for nature reserves, derived from incidence functions, asserted that large, connected reserves invariably outperform small, isolated ones. Daniel Simberloff and Lawrence Abele's 1976 rebuttal in *Science* argued from the same theory that a network of small reserves could hold more species when beta diversity was high. The SLOSS debate, sharpened through the 1980s by Higgs, Usher, Margules, and Haila, was eventually resolved not by victory for either side but by the recognition that the answer is conditional on measurable quantities — the area exponent $z$ and the between-site complementarity $f$ — rather than on a universal rule [ref: TODO_REF Losos & Ricklefs 2009].

The 1977 rescue effect of Brown and Kodric-Brown and the 2001 neutral theory of Hubbell each challenged a simplifying assumption of the original model. The rescue effect showed that immigration and extinction are not independent, since the former depresses the latter. Neutral theory showed that the species-area exponent $z \approx 0.25$ could be recovered from a model with no niches and no equilibrium at all, raising the question of whether the regularities MacArthur and Wilson explained were evidence for their mechanism or merely compatible with it. The current view holds both: equilibrium and neutral processes operate at different scales and time horizons, and their signatures are distinguishable in turnover and abundance-distribution data.

The most recent extension is Whittaker, Triantis, and Ladle's general dynamic model, which adds an ontogenetic axis: real islands are not static targets but pass through youth, maturity, and old age, with area, elevation, and habitat diversity changing over geological time. The immigration-extinction equilibrium still holds, but its parameters become functions of island age. This recovers the original model as the snapshot at a fixed ontogenetic stage and reconciles the equilibrium view with the evolutionary reality that MacArthur and Wilson bracketed away — that islands are, over sufficient time, generators of species, not merely collectors of them.
