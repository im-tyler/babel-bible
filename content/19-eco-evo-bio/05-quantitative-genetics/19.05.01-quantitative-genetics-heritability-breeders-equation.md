## Intuition [Beginner]

Most traits in biology are not simple Mendelian either/or characteristics like pea colour. Instead, they vary continuously along a spectrum. Height in humans, milk yield in cattle, seed weight in plants — these are **quantitative traits**. They are measured on a scale, not sorted into discrete boxes.

Quantitative traits are typically influenced by many genes, each with a small effect, plus the environment. Your height depends on hundreds of genetic variants and on nutrition, health, and other environmental factors. The result is a bell-curve distribution in the population.

**Heritability** measures how much of the variation in a trait is due to genetic differences between individuals. A heritability of 1.0 would mean all variation is genetic; 0.0 would mean all variation is environmental. Real traits fall in between. Human height has a heritability of about 0.8, meaning roughly 80% of the variation in height among individuals is due to genetic differences.

The **breeder's equation** predicts how much a trait will change in one generation in response to selection. If breeders select the tallest plants as parents, how much taller will the next generation be? The answer depends on both the strength of selection and the heritability of the trait.

## Visual [Beginner]

Imagine a population of plants with a bell-curve distribution of heights. A breeder selects only the tallest 20% to reproduce.

![Quantitative trait distribution showing selection differential. A bell curve of plant heights with a shaded region representing the selected parents (top 20%). The selection differential S is the difference between the mean of selected parents and the population mean. The response R is the difference between the offspring mean and the original population mean, with R = h^2 S.](/img/19.05.01-heritability-breeders-equation-placeholder.svg)

The **selection differential** $S$ is the gap between the mean of the selected parents and the overall population mean. The **response** $R$ is the shift in the next generation's mean. The breeder's equation $R = h^2 S$ connects them: the response equals heritability times the selection differential.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A plant breeder measures seed weight in a population of wheat. The population mean is 40 mg. The breeder selects plants with an average seed weight of 42 mg as parents. The heritability of seed weight in this population is $h^2 = 0.6$.

The selection differential is $S = 42 - 40 = 2$ mg.

The predicted response in the next generation is:

$$
R = h^2 \times S = 0.6 \times 2 = 1.2 \text{ mg}.
$$

The next generation is predicted to have a mean seed weight of $40 + 1.2 = 41.2$ mg.

If the breeder continues selecting for a mean of 42 mg each generation, the response per generation stays at 1.2 mg — until genetic variation is depleted and heritability drops.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise (medium, short answer).**

Explain the difference between narrow-sense heritability ($h^2$) and broad-sense heritability ($H^2$). Why is narrow-sense heritability more useful for predicting response to selection?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Think about which components of genetic variance are passed predictably from parents to offspring and which are not.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Broad-sense heritability** $H^2 = V_G / V_P$ is the fraction of phenotypic variance due to all genetic effects (additive, dominance, and epistatic). **Narrow-sense heritability** $h^2 = V_A / V_P$ includes only the additive component. Narrow-sense heritability predicts response to selection because additive effects are transmitted predictably from parents to offspring — each offspring inherits exactly half of each parent's additive genetic value. Dominance and epistatic effects are not transmitted faithfully because genotypes are broken up and reassembled each generation by segregation and recombination. The breeder's equation $R = h^2 S$ works because only additive variance fuels a sustained selection response.

</details>

</aside>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Variance components

For a quantitative trait with phenotypic value $z$, the total phenotypic variance $V_P$ decomposes as:

$$
V_P = V_G + V_E + 2\text{Cov}(G, E),
$$

where $V_G$ is genetic variance, $V_E$ is environmental variance, and $\text{Cov}(G, E)$ captures gene-environment covariance (often assumed zero). The genetic variance further decomposes:

$$
V_G = V_A + V_D + V_I,
$$

where $V_A$ is **additive variance** (the variance due to the average effects of individual alleles), $V_D$ is **dominance variance** (variance from interactions between alleles at the same locus), and $V_I$ is **epistatic variance** (variance from interactions between alleles at different loci).

### Heritability

**Broad-sense heritability**:

$$
H^2 = \frac{V_G}{V_P}.
$$

**Narrow-sense heritability**:

$$
h^2 = \frac{V_A}{V_P}.
$$

Since $V_A \leq V_G \leq V_P$, we have $h^2 \leq H^2 \leq 1$.

### The breeder's equation

The **breeder's equation** (Lush, 1937) predicts the response to selection:

$$
R = h^2 S,
$$

where $R$ is the change in the population mean across one generation and $S$ is the **selection differential** (the difference between the mean of selected parents and the population mean). The selection differential can be expressed as:

$$
S = i \sigma_P,
$$

where $i$ is the **selection intensity** (a function of the fraction selected) and $\sigma_P = \sqrt{V_P}$ is the phenotypic standard deviation. Substituting:

$$
R = h^2 i \sigma_P = i \sigma_A h,
$$

where $\sigma_A = \sqrt{V_A}$ and $h = \sqrt{h^2} = \sigma_A / \sigma_P$.

### Estimating heritability

Three principal methods estimate $h^2$:

1. **Parent-offspring regression**: regress offspring phenotype on midparent value (average of both parents). The regression slope equals $h^2$.

2. **Twin studies**: compare trait correlation between monozygotic (MZ) twins, who share 100% of genes, and dizygotic (DZ) twins, who share 50% on average. Falconer's formula gives $h^2 = 2(r_{MZ} - r_{DZ})$, where $r$ is the correlation coefficient.

3. **GWAS (genome-wide association studies)**: estimate the variance explained by all measured SNP markers, giving **SNP heritability** $h^2_{SNP}$. This is a lower bound on $h^2$ because SNPs tag only common variants.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Breeder's equation from the Robertson-Price identity).** *The response to selection equals the narrow-sense heritability times the selection differential: $R = h^2 S$.*

**Proof.** The **Robertson-Price identity** states that the selection differential equals the covariance between the trait and relative fitness:

$$
S = \text{Cov}(z, w),
$$

where $z$ is the trait value and $w$ is relative fitness (absolute fitness divided by mean fitness). The response in the next generation is:

$$
R = \text{Cov}(A, w),
$$

where $A$ is the **breeding value** (additive genetic value) of the trait. This is because only the additive component of the trait is transmitted faithfully to offspring.

Write $z = A + D + E$, where $D$ is the dominance-plus-epistatic deviation and $E$ is the environmental deviation. Assuming $\text{Cov}(D, w) = 0$ and $\text{Cov}(E, w) = 0$ (no selection on dominance or environmental components), we get:

$$
S = \text{Cov}(A + D + E, w) = \text{Cov}(A, w) = R.
$$

Wait — this gives $S = R$, which is wrong. The correct argument uses the regression of breeding value on phenotype. The breeding value $A$ regressed on phenotype $z$ gives:

$$
A = h^2(z - \mu) + \epsilon,
$$

where the regression slope is $b_{A,z} = V_A / V_P = h^2$ and $\epsilon$ is residual (non-additive plus environmental). Then:

$$
R = E[A_{\text{offspring}}] - \mu = E[h^2(z - \mu)] = h^2 E[z_{\text{selected}} - \mu] = h^2 S. \square
$$

The key insight is that the regression of breeding value on phenotype has slope $h^2$. Selected parents have above-average phenotypes, and their breeding values are above average by a factor of $h^2$ times their phenotypic excess.

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

A GWAS of human height identifies 3,000 SNPs that together explain 25% of phenotypic variance ($h^2_{SNP} = 0.25$), but twin and family studies estimate $h^2 = 0.80$. Explain the "missing heritability" gap and list three possible causes.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What sources of genetic variation might GWAS fail to capture?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The missing heritability gap ($0.80 - 0.25 = 0.55$) represents genetic variance that family studies detect but GWAS-tagged SNPs do not. Three causes: (1) **Rare variants** with large effects that GWAS arrays do not tag (MAF too low for statistical detection). (2) **Many common variants with very small effects** that are individually undetectable at genome-wide significance thresholds but collectively explain substantial variance. </details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Consider a single locus with two alleles, A and a, at frequencies $p$ and $q = 1 - p$. The genotypic values (average phenotypes) are: $AA \to +a$, $Aa \to d$, $aa \to -a$. Show that the additive genetic variance is $V_A = 2pqa^2$ when $d = 0$ (no dominance).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The average effect of allele A is $\alpha_A = q[a + d(q - p)]$ and of allele a is $\alpha_a = -p[a + d(q - p)]$. With $d = 0$, these simplify. Then $V_A = 2pq(\alpha_A - \alpha_a)^2$... but there is a simpler form. Actually use $V_A = 2pq\alpha^2$ where $\alpha = a + d(q-p)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $d = 0$: the average effect of an allele substitution is $\alpha = a + 0 \times (q - p) = a$. The additive variance is:

$$
V_A = 2pq\alpha^2 = 2pqa^2.
$$

This is maximised at $p = q = 0.5$ where $V_A = a^2/2$. As one allele approaches fixation ($p \to 0$ or $p \to 1$), $V_A \to 0$ because there is no genetic variation left. This is the simplest possible quantitative-genetics model — one additive locus — and it already shows the fundamental relationship: additive variance depends on both the effect size ($a$) and the allele frequency ($p$).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (medium, short answer).**

Artificial selection on a trait increases the mean for many generations, then plateaus. Explain why selection response eventually stops even though the breeder keeps selecting the same fraction of parents.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What happens to $V_A$ and $h^2$ as favourable alleles fix?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Each generation of selection increases the frequency of favourable alleles. As these alleles approach fixation, the additive genetic variance $V_A$ declines (because $V_A$ depends on allele frequencies being intermediate). Since $h^2 = V_A/V_P$, heritability drops. The breeder's equation $R = h^2 S$ predicts that response shrinks even if $S$ stays constant. Eventually, all favourable alleles fix, $V_A = 0$, $h^2 = 0$, and $R = 0$. The selection plateau is caused by depletion of genetic variation. New mutation can slowly restore variation, producing a very slow continued response called the "mutation-limited" phase.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced topics in quantitative genetics [Master]

### The multivariate breeder's equation

When multiple traits are under selection simultaneously, the univariate breeder's equation generalises to the **Lande equation** (1979):

$$
\Delta \bar{\mathbf{z}} = \mathbf{G}\boldsymbol{\beta},
$$

where $\mathbf{G}$ is the additive genetic variance-covariance matrix and $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ is the vector of directional selection gradients (partial regression coefficients of fitness on each trait, holding other traits constant). A trait that has zero direct selection ($\beta_j = 0$) can still evolve if it is genetically correlated with a trait under selection. This **correlated response** explains many seemingly maladaptive evolutionary outcomes.

### The infinitesimal model

Fisher's infinitesimal model (1918) assumes that a quantitative trait is controlled by an infinite number of loci, each with infinitesimally small effect. Under this model, selection changes the mean but does not change the variance — each offspring's breeding value is drawn from a normal distribution with mean equal to the midparent value and constant variance $V_A/2$. The infinitesimal model is the basis for BLUP (Best Linear Unbiased Prediction) and animal model methodologies used in modern breeding programs.

### GWAS and polygenic scores

A **genome-wide association study** tests each of millions of SNPs for association with a quantitative trait. The effect sizes $\hat{\beta}_j$ from GWAS are combined into a **polygenic score**: $\text{PGS}_i = \sum_j \hat{\beta}_j x_{ij}$, where $x_{ij}$ is the genotype of individual $i$ at SNP $j$. The variance explained by the PGS estimates $h^2_{SNP}$. For height, current PGS explain roughly 25% of variance using ~3,000 genome-wide significant SNPs, and over 40% using all SNPs in a jointly-estimated model.

## Connections [Master]

- **Mendelian genetics [19.01.01]** provides the allele-segregation basis upon which the variance-component decomposition is built. Additive effects are average effects of individual alleles inherited in Mendelian fashion.

- **Natural selection [19.03.01]** creates the selection differential $S$. The breeder's equation quantifies how much phenotypic change selection produces per generation for a given genetic architecture.

- **Genetic drift [19.04.01]** erodes additive genetic variance in small populations, reducing $h^2$ and therefore the response to selection. Drift also creates variation in $S$ across replicate populations.

- **Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium [19.02.01]** is the baseline from which genotype frequencies are computed. Variance components depend on allele frequencies that are themselves governed by Hardy-Weinberg expectations (in the absence of selection and drift).

- **Probability and statistics [02.13.01]** provide the regression and variance-decomposition machinery. Parent-offspring regression and ANOVA are the primary estimation methods for heritability.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Fisher's 1918 paper "The correlation between relatives on the supposition of Mendelian inheritance" resolved the conflict between biometricians (who studied continuous variation statistically) and Mendelians (who studied discrete traits). Fisher showed that continuous variation is consistent with Mendelian inheritance when many loci contribute small effects. This was one of the most consequential papers in 20th-century biology.

Jay Lush (1937) formalised heritability and the breeder's equation in the context of animal breeding, creating the discipline of quantitative genetics as applied to artificial selection. His framework enabled the dramatic increases in agricultural productivity during the 20th century — modern dairy cattle produce over four times as much milk as their 1940 counterparts, largely through systematic application of the breeder's equation.

The **nature-nurture debate** has been one of the most contentious in psychology and biology. Heritability estimates are frequently misinterpreted as immutable. In fact, heritability is a population-level, environment-specific statistic. A trait can have high heritability and still be responsive to environmental intervention. The classic example: height has $h^2 \approx 0.8$ in modern developed nations, but average height has increased by 10-15 cm over the past 150 years due to improved nutrition — an environmental change, not genetic evolution.
