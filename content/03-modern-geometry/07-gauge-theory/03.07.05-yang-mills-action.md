## Intuition [Beginner]

A gauge field is a rule for comparing internal directions at nearby points. Curvature measures the failure of those comparisons to close up after a tiny loop. When the failure is large, the field stores more energy.

Yang-Mills theory turns that idea into a number. It looks at the curvature everywhere on the space and adds up its squared size. Low action means the field bends gently or cancels itself well. High action means strong curvature is spread through the space.

Electromagnetism is the abelian example. Yang-Mills keeps the same field-energy idea but allows the internal rotations to fail to commute, which is the source of non-abelian gauge theory.

## Visual [Beginner]

The surface carries a grid of local comparisons. The colored loops mark places where transport around a small loop does not return the internal arrow to its starting direction.

![A curved surface with connection lines, field loops, and an action meter measuring curvature energy.](/img/yang-mills-action-placeholder.svg)

The action meter is a mnemonic: Yang-Mills does not count loops. It measures the total squared size of curvature.

## Worked introduction [Beginner]

Imagine walking around a small square while carrying an arrow that can rotate in an internal color space. If the arrow returns unchanged, the field is flat along that square. If it returns rotated, the square has curvature.

Now compare two fields. Field A rotates the arrow a little on most squares. Field B rotates it strongly on a few squares and gently elsewhere. Yang-Mills assigns a cost by squaring the size of each rotation before adding the costs. Strong curvature is punished more than weak curvature.

The preferred fields are the stationary ones: small allowed changes do not lower the action to first order.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $P\to M$ be a principal $G$-bundle over a compact oriented Riemannian manifold, let $\mathfrak g$ be the Lie algebra of $G$, and fix an $\operatorname{Ad}$-invariant inner product $\langle-,-\rangle$ on $\mathfrak g$. A connection $A$ has curvature

$$
F_A\in \Omega^2(M;\operatorname{ad}P).
$$

The **Yang-Mills action** is

$$
\operatorname{YM}(A)=\frac12\int_M |F_A|^2\,d\operatorname{vol}
=\frac12\int_M \langle F_A\wedge *F_A\rangle .
$$

The first expression uses the Riemannian metric and the inner product on $\mathfrak g$; the second is the differential-form expression with the Hodge star. This is the standard non-abelian generalization of Maxwell field energy [ref: tong §2].

A connection is a **Yang-Mills connection** if it is a critical point of $\operatorname{YM}$ on the affine space of connections. Its Euler-Lagrange equation is

$$
d_A^*F_A=0,
$$

together with the Bianchi identity

$$
d_AF_A=0.
$$

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (gauge invariance of the Yang-Mills action).** Let $u$ be a gauge transformation of $P$. If $A^u$ is the transformed connection, then

$$
\operatorname{YM}(A^u)=\operatorname{YM}(A).
$$

**Proof.** Under a gauge transformation, curvature transforms by the adjoint action:

$$
F_{A^u}=\operatorname{Ad}_{u^{-1}}F_A.
$$

The chosen inner product on $\mathfrak g$ is $\operatorname{Ad}$-invariant, so

$$
|\operatorname{Ad}_{u^{-1}}F_A|^2=|F_A|^2
$$

pointwise. The gauge transformation acts only in the internal Lie-algebra direction; it does not change the Riemannian volume form on $M$. Therefore the integrands in $\operatorname{YM}(A^u)$ and $\operatorname{YM}(A)$ agree at every point. Integrating gives the stated equality. $\square$

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

On a two-dimensional region of area $3$, a $U(1)$ curvature form has constant pointwise norm $2$. With the normalization $\operatorname{YM}(A)=\frac12\int_M |F_A|^2\,d\operatorname{vol}$, compute $\operatorname{YM}(A)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The squared norm is constant.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The squared norm is $4$, so

$$
\operatorname{YM}(A)=\frac12\cdot 4\cdot 3=6.
$$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, conceptual).**

Why is an $\operatorname{Ad}$-invariant inner product on $\mathfrak g$ part of the definition?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Look at the proof of gauge invariance.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

It makes the pointwise norm of curvature independent of the chosen gauge. Without this invariance, replacing $F_A$ by $\operatorname{Ad}_{u^{-1}}F_A$ could change the integrand.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="derivation" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, derivation).**

Let $A_t=A+ta$ be a path of connections with $a\in\Omega^1(M;\operatorname{ad}P)$. Use

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\bigg|_{t=0}F_{A_t}=d_Aa
$$

to derive the weak Euler-Lagrange equation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate $\frac12\int_M\langle F_{A_t}\wedge *F_{A_t}\rangle$ and integrate by parts.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Differentiating gives

$$
\delta\operatorname{YM}_A(a)=\int_M\langle d_Aa\wedge *F_A\rangle .
$$

Using the formal adjoint of $d_A$ gives

$$
\delta\operatorname{YM}_A(a)=\int_M\langle a\wedge *d_A^*F_A\rangle .
$$

This vanishes for every $a$ precisely when $d_A^*F_A=0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, conceptual).**

Explain why the Bianchi identity is not the Euler-Lagrange equation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

One identity holds for every connection; the other selects critical points.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Bianchi identity $d_AF_A=0$ is a structural identity following from the definition of curvature. The equation $d_A^*F_A=0$ is variational: it is the condition that the first variation of the Yang-Mills action vanish.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Show that a flat connection is Yang-Mills.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute $F_A=0$ into the Euler-Lagrange equation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $F_A=0$, then $d_A^*F_A=d_A^*0=0$. Therefore the Yang-Mills equation holds.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

In dimension four, decompose $F_A=F_A^++F_A^-$ into self-dual and anti-self-dual parts. Show that a self-dual or anti-self-dual connection is Yang-Mills.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $*F_A=\pm F_A$ and the Bianchi identity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $*F_A=F_A$, then

$$
d_A^*F_A=-*d_A*F_A=-*d_AF_A=0
$$

by the Bianchi identity. The anti-self-dual case has $*F_A=-F_A$ and gives the same conclusion with the opposite sign.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Why does Chern-Weil theory produce topological quantities from curvature while Yang-Mills produces a metric-dependent action?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare invariant polynomial evaluation with the Hodge star.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Chern-Weil evaluates invariant polynomials on curvature and takes de Rham cohomology classes; the resulting classes are independent of the chosen connection [03.06.06]. Yang-Mills uses the Riemannian norm, equivalently the Hodge star, so the value of the functional depends on the metric.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

On a compact oriented four-manifold, the decomposition

$$
\Omega^2(M;\operatorname{ad}P)=\Omega^2_+(M;\operatorname{ad}P)\oplus\Omega^2_-(M;\operatorname{ad}P)
$$

gives the identity

$$
\operatorname{YM}(A)=\|F_A^+\|^2+\|F_A^-\|^2.
$$

The Chern-Weil term

$$
\int_M\langle F_A\wedge F_A\rangle
$$

is topological after normalization, while $\operatorname{YM}(A)$ depends on the metric [03.06.06]. This yields lower bounds for the action in fixed topological sectors, attained by self-dual or anti-self-dual connections.

Atiyah and Bott described Yang-Mills theory over a Riemann surface as an infinite-dimensional symplectic quotient problem. The curvature map is a moment map for the gauge-group action on the space of connections, and the Yang-Mills functional is the squared norm of that moment map [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah-Bott 1983].

In four dimensions the same functional underlies Donaldson theory. Anti-self-dual connections satisfy first-order equations whose moduli spaces carry invariants of smooth four-manifolds [ref: TODO_REF Donaldson-Kronheimer §2].

## Full proof set [Master]

**First variation.** For a variation $A_t=A+ta$, curvature satisfies

$$
F_{A_t}=F_A+t\,d_Aa+\frac{t^2}{2}[a\wedge a].
$$

Thus

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\bigg|_{t=0}\operatorname{YM}(A_t)
=\int_M\langle d_Aa\wedge *F_A\rangle.
$$

On a closed manifold, integration by parts gives

$$
\int_M\langle d_Aa\wedge *F_A\rangle
=\int_M\langle a\wedge *d_A^*F_A\rangle.
$$

The first variation vanishes for all $a$ exactly when $d_A^*F_A=0$.

**Four-dimensional instantons.** If $F_A^+=0$ or $F_A^-=0$, then the Yang-Mills equation follows from the Bianchi identity:

$$
d_A^*F_A=-*d_A*F_A=\mp *d_AF_A=0.
$$

These first-order equations are stronger than the second-order Yang-Mills equation and are elliptic modulo gauge after imposing a slice condition.

**Relation to Chern-Weil.** The expression $\langle F_A\wedge F_A\rangle$ represents a characteristic class after applying the appropriate invariant polynomial [03.06.06]. Yang-Mills uses $\langle F_A\wedge *F_A\rangle$ instead. Replacing the second $F_A$ by $*F_A$ introduces the metric and turns a topological representative into an energy density.

## Connections [Master]

- **Chern-Weil homomorphism [03.06.06]** — both theories evaluate curvature through invariant inner products or polynomials.
- **Pontryagin and Chern classes [03.06.04]** — topological charge terms in four-dimensional gauge theory are characteristic classes.
- **Dirac operator [03.09.08]** — coupled Dirac operators use the same gauge connections, and curvature appears in Weitzenbock formulas.
- **Atiyah-Singer index theorem [03.09.10]** — instanton moduli-space dimensions are computed by elliptic deformation complexes.
- **CFT basics [03.10.02]** — two-dimensional gauge and conformal theories share stress-energy and symmetry methods, though Yang-Mills itself is not conformal in every dimension.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Yang and Mills introduced non-abelian gauge fields in 1954 as a symmetry principle for isospin. The modern geometric formulation identifies their field strength with curvature of a connection, and the action with the $L^2$ norm of curvature [ref: tong §2].

Atiyah and Bott's 1983 paper placed Yang-Mills equations over Riemann surfaces in symplectic and Morse-theoretic form [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah-Bott 1983]. Donaldson's four-dimensional theory used anti-self-dual Yang-Mills connections to distinguish smooth structures on topological four-manifolds [ref: TODO_REF Donaldson-Kronheimer §2].
