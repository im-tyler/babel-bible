## Intuition [Beginner]

Every rank-$k$ complex vector bundle on every space comes from a single, fixed bundle. The universal bundle is that single object — a master bundle on a master space called the **classifying space** $BU(k)$ — and every rank-$k$ bundle on every space is obtained by pulling the master bundle back along a continuous map.

The classifying space has an explicit geometric model: it is the **infinite Grassmannian**, the space of all $k$-dimensional subspaces of an infinite-dimensional vector space. The master bundle assigns to each subspace the subspace itself as a fibre — this is called the **tautological bundle**. Every other rank-$k$ bundle comes from a map into the infinite Grassmannian.

The cohomology of the classifying space is a single polynomial algebra: a finitely many polynomial generators in even degrees, called the universal **Chern classes**. Pulling these back along a classifying map produces the Chern classes of any specific bundle. The whole apparatus reduces the calculation of characteristic classes to one computation done once on a single space.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The complex line bundle case: $BU(1)$.

Take $k = 1$. The infinite Grassmannian $G_1(\mathbb{C}^\infty)$ is the space of one-dimensional subspaces of $\mathbb{C}^\infty$, equivalently the infinite complex projective space $\mathbb{C}P^\infty$. The tautological bundle assigns to each line in $\mathbb{C}^\infty$ the line itself; this is the bundle $\gamma_1 \to \mathbb{C}P^\infty$. Every complex line bundle on every space is the pullback of this single bundle along some map.

The cohomology ring of $\mathbb{C}P^\infty$ is the polynomial ring in one generator $c_1$ of degree $2$:
$$H^*(\mathbb{C}P^\infty; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}[c_1].$$
The generator $c_1$ is the universal first Chern class. Pulling this back along the classifying map of any complex line bundle $L \to X$ produces the first Chern class $c_1(L) \in H^2(X; \mathbb{Z})$.

For example: the tangent bundle of the $2$-sphere $S^2 = \mathbb{C}P^1$ corresponds to the inclusion $\mathbb{C}P^1 \hookrightarrow \mathbb{C}P^\infty$ representing $2 \in H^2(\mathbb{C}P^\infty)$, so $c_1(TS^2) = 2 \cdot \text{generator}$ — equal to the Euler number of $S^2$. The classifying-space framework makes this calculation a one-liner.

What this tells us: every complex line bundle calculation reduces to a calculation of pulled-back classes from $\mathbb{C}P^\infty$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout this unit, $G$ denotes a topological group (typically a compact Lie group), and all base spaces are paracompact CW complexes (or finite CW complexes when needed for the Grassmannian model). All vector bundles are complex unless otherwise specified; the real case is parallel via $BO(k)$.

**Universal $G$-bundle.** A principal $G$-bundle $\pi : EG \to BG$ is **universal** if $EG$ is contractible. Concretely, a paracompact base space $X$ and any principal $G$-bundle $P \to X$ admit a classifying map $f : X \to BG$ with $P \cong f^* EG$, unique up to homotopy. The fundamental theorem of classifying spaces (Steenrod 1951; Milnor 1956) provides existence: every topological group admits a contractible free $G$-space.

**Tautological bundle on a Grassmannian.** Let $G_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$ denote the **complex Grassmannian** of $k$-dimensional complex linear subspaces of $\mathbb{C}^n$. Over each point $V \in G_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$ sits a $k$-dimensional fibre, namely the subspace $V \subset \mathbb{C}^n$ itself. Assembling these fibres globally produces the **tautological bundle** (notation decision #19):
$$
\gamma_k^n \longrightarrow G_k(\mathbb{C}^n), \qquad (\gamma_k^n)_V = V.
$$
The colimit over inclusions $\gamma_k^n \hookrightarrow \gamma_k^{n+1}$ gives the **universal complex rank-$k$ bundle**:
$$
\gamma_k = \varinjlim_n \gamma_k^n \longrightarrow BU(k) := G_k(\mathbb{C}^\infty) = \varinjlim_n G_k(\mathbb{C}^n).
$$

**The classification theorem.** For paracompact $X$, the pullback construction induces a natural bijection
$$
[X, BU(k)] \xrightarrow{\sim} \{\text{rank-}k\text{ complex vector bundles on }X\}/\text{iso},
$$
sending $[f] \mapsto f^* \gamma_k$. The statement extends to any compact Lie group $G$ via the principal-$G$-bundle classification $[X, BG] \cong \mathrm{Prin}_G(X)$ [03.08.04].

**Borel construction.** Given a topological group $G$ acting on a space $F$, the **Borel construction** is the homotopy quotient
$$
F \times_G EG := (F \times EG) / G,
$$
fitting into a fibration $F \to F \times_G EG \to BG$. Equivariant cohomology of $F$ is defined as the cohomology of $F \times_G EG$:
$$
H^*_G(F) := H^*(F \times_G EG).
$$
For a single-point space $F = \mathrm{pt}$, this recovers $H^*_G(\mathrm{pt}) = H^*(BG)$ — the universal characteristic-class ring.

**Borel presentation.** For a compact Lie group $G$ with maximal torus $T$ and Weyl group $W = N_G(T)/T$:
$$
H^*(BG; \mathbb{Q}) \cong H^*(BT; \mathbb{Q})^W,
$$
the ring of $W$-invariant polynomials. For $G = U(n)$: $T = (S^1)^n$, $W = S_n$, $H^*(BT; \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$ ($\deg x_i = 2$), and $H^*(BU(n); \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]^{S_n} = \mathbb{Q}[c_1, \ldots, c_n]$ with $c_i$ the elementary symmetric polynomial of degree $i$ — recovering the universal Chern classes.

**Flag manifold.** For $G = U(n)$, the **flag manifold** is the quotient $F\ell_n = U(n)/T$ where $T$ is the diagonal maximal torus. Equivalently, $F\ell_n$ is the space of complete flags $0 \subset V_1 \subset V_2 \subset \cdots \subset V_n = \mathbb{C}^n$ with $\dim V_i = i$. Its cohomology has the **Borel presentation**:
$$
H^*(F\ell_n; \mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}[x_1, \ldots, x_n] / \langle e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_n \rangle,
$$
where $e_i$ is the $i$-th elementary symmetric polynomial in $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ — the "symmetric polynomials of positive degree" relations.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (cohomology of $BU(k)$).** *The cohomology ring of $BU(k)$ with integer coefficients is the polynomial ring*
$$
H^*(BU(k); \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}[c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_k]
$$
*on the universal Chern classes, with $\deg c_i = 2i$.*

**Proof via the projective-bundle fibration.** Use induction on $k$, with the base case $H^*(BU(1)) = H^*(\mathbb{C}P^\infty) = \mathbb{Z}[c_1]$ established directly from the projective-space cell decomposition.

Inductive step. Use the fibration $\mathbb{C}P^\infty \to BU(k - 1) \to BU(k)$ (which can be realised concretely as the fibration of full flags within the tautological rank-$k$ bundle, projecting out the bottom rank-$1$ subspace). The Leray-Serre spectral sequence has $E_2^{p, q} = H^p(BU(k); H^q(\mathbb{C}P^\infty))$. The transgression sends the generator of $H^2(\mathbb{C}P^\infty)$ to a class in $H^2(BU(k))$, and by Leray-Hirsch [03.13.03] applied to the fibration, the spectral sequence collapses, identifying
$$
H^*(BU(k - 1)) \cong H^*(BU(k))[t] / \langle p(t) \rangle
$$
for some monic polynomial $p(t)$ of degree $k$ in $t$. The polynomial $p(t)$ is the relation defining the new degree-$2k$ generator; the inductive hypothesis $H^*(BU(k - 1)) = \mathbb{Z}[c_1, \ldots, c_{k-1}]$ then forces $H^*(BU(k)) = \mathbb{Z}[c_1, \ldots, c_k]$. The classes $c_i$ are the universal Chern classes, characterised by the Whitney sum formula and the normalisation $c_1(\gamma_1) = $ standard generator on $BU(1)$. [ref: TODO_REF Bott-Tu §23] $\square$

**Theorem (Steenrod-Milnor classification).** *For paracompact $X$, the bijection*
$$
[X, BU(k)] \xrightarrow{\sim} \{\text{rank-}k\text{ complex vector bundles on }X\}/\text{iso}
$$
*sends $[f] \mapsto f^* \gamma_k$.*

**Proof sketch.** Surjectivity: given a rank-$k$ bundle $E \to X$, choose a fibrewise-injective bundle map $E \hookrightarrow \underline{\mathbb{C}^N}$ for $N$ large (by paracompactness and a partition-of-unity construction). The map $X \to G_k(\mathbb{C}^N)$ sending $x \mapsto (E_x \subset \mathbb{C}^N)$ is the classifying map; it pulls $\gamma_k^N$ back to $E$. Composing with the inclusion $G_k(\mathbb{C}^N) \hookrightarrow BU(k)$ gives a classifying map into $BU(k)$.

Injectivity: two embeddings $E \hookrightarrow \underline{\mathbb{C}^N}$ and $E \hookrightarrow \underline{\mathbb{C}^{N'}}$ extend to a single embedding into $\underline{\mathbb{C}^{N + N'}}$ (block-diagonally), and the homotopy of bundle injections induces a homotopy of the classifying maps. As $N \to \infty$, the classifying maps stabilise to a single homotopy class. [ref: TODO_REF Husemoller §4; Steenrod 1951] $\square$

**Theorem (Borel 1953).** *For a compact Lie group $G$ with maximal torus $T$ of rank $r$ and Weyl group $W = N_G(T)/T$:*
$$
H^*(BG; \mathbb{Q}) \cong H^*(BT; \mathbb{Q})^W.
$$

**Proof sketch via the fibration $G/T \to BT \to BG$.** Apply the Leray-Serre spectral sequence to the fibration. The fibre $G/T$ is the **flag variety** of $G$; rationally, $H^*(G/T; \mathbb{Q})$ is the **regular representation of $W$** (this is Bott's 1956 theorem on the cohomology of homogeneous spaces). Both base and fibre have cohomology concentrated in even degrees (a Hodge-theoretic / cell-decomposition fact for $G/T$, classical for $BT$), so the spectral sequence collapses at $E_2$.

The collapse gives $H^*(BT) \cong H^*(BG) \otimes H^*(G/T)$ as $H^*(BG)$-modules. Tensoring with the unit $W$-representation isolates the $W$-invariants:
$$
H^*(BG) = H^*(BT) \otimes_{H^*(BT)^W} \mathbb{Q} \cong H^*(BT)^W,
$$
where the second isomorphism uses that $H^*(G/T)$ is the regular $W$-representation.

For $G = U(n)$: $W = S_n$ acts on $H^*(BT) = \mathbb{Q}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$ by permuting variables, and the invariants are the symmetric polynomials, generated by $c_1, \ldots, c_n$. [ref: TODO_REF Borel 1953; Bott-Tu §21] $\square$

**Synthesis.** The infinite Grassmannian $G_k(\mathbb{C}^\infty)$ is exactly the classifying space $BU(k)$ for complex rank-$k$ bundles. This is precisely Steenrod 1951 / Milnor 1956's content. The Borel presentation $H^*(BU(k); \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}[c_1, \dots, c_k]$ is the foundational reason characteristic-class identities reduce to symmetric-function calculations.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward later units of the strand, where the same pattern is taken up at higher structure. The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).** *Cohomology of $BU(2)$.*

Write down $H^*(BU(2); \mathbb{Z})$ explicitly, identifying degrees and the $H^*$-multiplicative structure.

The cohomology of $BU(k)$ is polynomial on the Chern classes, $\deg c_i = 2i$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$H^*(BU(2); \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}[c_1, c_2]$ with $\deg c_1 = 2$ and $\deg c_2 = 4$. Generators in each degree:

- $H^0 = \mathbb{Z}$ (constants);
- $H^2 = \mathbb{Z} \cdot c_1$;
- $H^4 = \mathbb{Z} \cdot c_1^2 \oplus \mathbb{Z} \cdot c_2$;
- $H^6 = \mathbb{Z} \cdot c_1^3 \oplus \mathbb{Z} \cdot c_1 c_2$;
- $H^8 = \mathbb{Z} \cdot c_1^4 \oplus \mathbb{Z} \cdot c_1^2 c_2 \oplus \mathbb{Z} \cdot c_2^2$;

and so on. The Poincaré series is $P(t) = 1 / ((1 - t^2)(1 - t^4))$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).** *Borel presentation for $G = U(2)$.*

Verify the Borel presentation $H^*(BU(2); \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}[x_1, x_2]^{S_2}$ explicitly.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The $S_2$-invariants of $\mathbb{Q}[x_1, x_2]$ are the elementary symmetric polynomials.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathbb{Q}[x_1, x_2]$ has the action of $S_2$ swapping $x_1$ and $x_2$. The invariant subring is generated by the elementary symmetric polynomials $e_1 = x_1 + x_2$ and $e_2 = x_1 x_2$, both algebraically independent. Hence
$$
\mathbb{Q}[x_1, x_2]^{S_2} = \mathbb{Q}[e_1, e_2] = \mathbb{Q}[c_1, c_2],
$$
with the identification $c_1 = e_1$ and $c_2 = e_2$. This confirms $H^*(BU(2); \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}[c_1, c_2]$, matching the integer-coefficient computation of Exercise 1 (rationally).

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).** *Cohomology of $\mathrm{Gr}(2, 4)$.*

Compute $H^*(\mathrm{Gr}(2, 4); \mathbb{Q})$ — the cohomology of the Grassmannian of $2$-planes in $\mathbb{C}^4$ — via the Borel presentation. Verify the Poincaré series matches the Schubert-cell count.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathrm{Gr}(2, 4) = U(4)/(U(2) \times U(2))$. The Borel presentation gives invariants in $H^*(BT)$ with respect to a parabolic Weyl group.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Borel presentation for $\mathrm{Gr}(k, n)$ is
$$
H^*(\mathrm{Gr}(k, n); \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}[c_1, \ldots, c_k, \bar{c}_1, \ldots, \bar{c}_{n-k}] / \langle c \cdot \bar{c} = 1 \rangle,
$$
where $c = 1 + c_1 + \cdots + c_k$ and $\bar{c} = 1 + \bar{c}_1 + \cdots + \bar{c}_{n-k}$ are the Chern polynomials of the tautological and quotient bundles, with the relation expressing the splitting $\gamma_k \oplus \bar{\gamma}_{n-k} = $ rank-$n$ free bundle.

For $\mathrm{Gr}(2, 4)$: $H^*(\mathrm{Gr}(2, 4); \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}[c_1, c_2, \bar{c}_1, \bar{c}_2] / \langle (1 + c_1 + c_2)(1 + \bar{c}_1 + \bar{c}_2) = 1 \rangle$. The relation expands to $\bar{c}_1 = -c_1$, $\bar{c}_2 = c_1^2 - c_2$, plus higher-order relations $c_1^3 - 2 c_1 c_2 = 0$ and $c_2^2 - c_1^2 c_2 + c_1^4 = 0$ (the parts coming from degrees $> 4$ on a four-dimensional Grassmannian).

The Poincaré series is $1 + t^2 + 2t^4 + t^6 + t^8$, with Betti numbers $(1, 0, 1, 0, 2, 0, 1, 0, 1)$. The $1 + 1 + 2 + 1 + 1 = 6$ classes match the Schubert-cell count: $\mathrm{Gr}(2, 4)$ has $\binom{4}{2} = 6$ Schubert cells, indexed by Young diagrams fitting in a $2 \times 2$ box. The classes are $1, \sigma_1, \sigma_2, \sigma_{1,1}, \sigma_{2,1}, \sigma_{2,2}$ in the Schubert basis.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *Whitney sum formula via the universal map.*

The Whitney sum formula states $c(E \oplus F) = c(E) \cdot c(F)$ for the total Chern class. Derive this from the universal-bundle construction: identify the classifying map of $\gamma_k \oplus \gamma_\ell$ as $\mu_{k, \ell} : BU(k) \times BU(\ell) \to BU(k + \ell)$, and compute $\mu_{k, \ell}^*$ on cohomology.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The classifying map of a direct sum is the product of classifying maps composed with the block-sum classifier $\mu_{k, \ell}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define $\mu_{k, \ell} : BU(k) \times BU(\ell) \to BU(k + \ell)$ as the map classifying the direct sum $\gamma_k \boxplus \gamma_\ell := \pi_1^* \gamma_k \oplus \pi_2^* \gamma_\ell$ on $BU(k) \times BU(\ell)$. By the universal-property argument, this map is well-defined up to homotopy.

In cohomology: $\mu_{k, \ell}^* : H^*(BU(k + \ell)) \to H^*(BU(k)) \otimes H^*(BU(\ell))$ sends the universal total Chern class $c = 1 + c_1 + c_2 + \cdots + c_{k + \ell}$ to its corresponding class on the source. By the Borel presentation (Exercise 2 generalised), $\mu_{k, \ell}^*(c) = c^{(k)} \cdot c^{(\ell)}$ — the product of the total Chern classes on the two factors. (Concretely: under the universal Chern roots $x_1, \ldots, x_{k+\ell}$ on $BU(k + \ell)$, the map $\mu_{k, \ell}^*$ sends the first $k$ roots to the Chern roots on $BU(k)$ and the last $\ell$ to the Chern roots on $BU(\ell)$; the elementary symmetric polynomial relation $e_n(x_1, \ldots, x_{k+\ell}) = \sum_{i + j = n} e_i(x_1, \ldots, x_k) e_j(x_{k+1}, \ldots, x_{k+\ell})$ is exactly the Whitney sum formula.)

For an arbitrary bundle $E \oplus F \to X$ classified by $f \times g : X \to BU(k) \times BU(\ell) \to BU(k + \ell)$:
$$
c(E \oplus F) = (\mu_{k, \ell} \circ (f \times g))^* c = (f \times g)^* \mu_{k, \ell}^* c = (f \times g)^* (c^{(k)} c^{(\ell)}) = f^* c^{(k)} \cdot g^* c^{(\ell)} = c(E) \cdot c(F). \quad \square
$$

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).** *Cohomology of the flag manifold $F\ell_3$.*

The relations are the elementary symmetric polynomials of positive degree in $x_1, x_2, x_3$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The maximal torus of $SO(2n+1)$ has rank $n$; the Weyl group $W_{B_n}$ acts by permutation and sign change.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $G = SO(2n+1)$, the maximal torus has rank $n$ with $H^*(BT; \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}[t_1, \ldots, t_n]$ ($\deg t_i = 2$). The Weyl group $W_{B_n} = (\mathbb{Z}/2)^n \rtimes S_n$ acts by permuting coordinates and by sign changes $t_i \mapsto \pm t_i$.

The $W$-invariants are polynomials symmetric in $t_1^2, \ldots, t_n^2$ — the elementary symmetric polynomials in the squared coordinates. Hence
$$
H^*(BSO(2n+1); \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}[t_1^2, \ldots, t_n^2]^{S_n} = \mathbb{Q}[p_1, \ldots, p_n],
$$
where $p_i = e_i(t_1^2, \ldots, t_n^2)$ is the $i$-th elementary symmetric polynomial in the squared roots, with $\deg p_i = 4i$. The classes $p_i$ are the universal Pontryagin classes [03.06.04].

Note the absence of an Euler-class generator: $SO(2n+1)$ acts on $\mathbb{R}^{2n+1}$, and the Euler class of an oriented odd-rank bundle is two-torsion, hence vanishes rationally.

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).** *Universal-bundle construction for $G = U(k)$.*

Construct the universal $U(k)$-bundle $EU(k) \to BU(k)$ via the **Stiefel manifold** of orthonormal $k$-frames in $\mathbb{C}^\infty$. Verify that the total space is contractible.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Stiefel manifold $V_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$ of orthonormal $k$-frames in $\mathbb{C}^n$ is $(2n - 2k)$-connected; passing to the limit $n \to \infty$ gives a contractible space.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The **Stiefel manifold** $V_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$ is the space of orthonormal $k$-frames in $\mathbb{C}^n$:
$$
V_k(\mathbb{C}^n) = \{(v_1, \ldots, v_k) : v_i \in \mathbb{C}^n, \langle v_i, v_j \rangle = \delta_{ij}\}.
$$
The unitary group $U(k)$ acts freely on $V_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$ by change of basis, with quotient $V_k(\mathbb{C}^n)/U(k) = G_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$ — sending an orthonormal frame to the subspace it spans.

**Connectivity of $V_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$.** The fibre of the projection $V_k(\mathbb{C}^n) \to V_{k-1}(\mathbb{C}^n)$ (forgetting the last frame vector) is the unit sphere in the orthogonal complement of the first $k - 1$ vectors, namely $S^{2(n-k)+1}$ (a $(2n - 2k)$-connected space). By induction starting from $V_1(\mathbb{C}^n) = S^{2n - 1}$ (which is $(2n-2)$-connected), $V_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$ is $(2n - 2k)$-connected: any map of a $(2n - 2k)$-disk into $V_k$ is null-homotopic.

**Contractibility in the limit.** As $n \to \infty$, the connectivity of $V_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$ tends to infinity. Hence $V_k(\mathbb{C}^\infty) = \varinjlim V_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$ is weakly contractible; by the universal property of CW complexes, it is in fact contractible.

**Conclusion.** $EU(k) := V_k(\mathbb{C}^\infty)$ is a contractible space with a free $U(k)$-action and quotient $BU(k) = G_k(\mathbb{C}^\infty)$. The principal bundle $V_k(\mathbb{C}^\infty) \to G_k(\mathbb{C}^\infty)$ is the universal $U(k)$-bundle. The associated rank-$k$ vector bundle $V_k(\mathbb{C}^\infty) \times_{U(k)} \mathbb{C}^k$ is the universal complex rank-$k$ bundle $\gamma_k$ (notation decision #19). $\square$

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Schubert cells decompose the Grassmannian.*

Prove that $G_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$ has a CW decomposition with $\binom{n}{k}$ Schubert cells, indexed by partitions $\lambda$ fitting in a $k \times (n - k)$ rectangle. Use this to compute the Poincaré series.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A Schubert cell is the locus of $k$-planes $V \subset \mathbb{C}^n$ with prescribed intersection dimensions $\dim(V \cap \mathbb{C}^j)$ for the standard flag.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix the standard flag $0 \subset \mathbb{C}^1 \subset \mathbb{C}^2 \subset \cdots \subset \mathbb{C}^n$ in $\mathbb{C}^n$. For a partition $\lambda = (\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_k \geq 0)$ fitting in a $k \times (n-k)$ rectangle, define the **Schubert cell**
$$
\Omega_\lambda = \{V \in G_k(\mathbb{C}^n) : \dim(V \cap \mathbb{C}^{n - k - \lambda_i + i}) = i \text{ for } i = 1, \ldots, k\}.
$$
The complex dimension of $\Omega_\lambda$ is $|\lambda| = \sum \lambda_i$.

**The cell decomposition.** Every $k$-plane $V$ has a unique sequence of jump dimensions $\{j_1 < j_2 < \cdots < j_k\}$ at which $\dim(V \cap \mathbb{C}^{j_i}) = i$, with $j_i \geq i$. The corresponding partition $\lambda_i = j_i - i$ is the **jump partition** of $V$. The mapping $V \mapsto \lambda$ produces a stratification of $G_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$ into Schubert cells $\Omega_\lambda$, one for each partition fitting in $k \times (n - k)$.

**Cell structure.** Each $\Omega_\lambda$ is homeomorphic to $\mathbb{C}^{|\lambda|}$ (an open cell of real dimension $2|\lambda|$). The closure $\bar{\Omega}_\lambda$ is a union of cells $\Omega_\mu$ with $\mu \leq \lambda$ in the partial order on partitions (componentwise comparison). The closure $\bar{\Omega}_\lambda$ is the **Schubert variety** $X_\lambda$ — a singular projective subvariety of $G_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$.

**Poincaré series.** The Poincaré polynomial is
$$
P_{G_k(\mathbb{C}^n)}(t) = \sum_{\lambda \subset k \times (n-k)} t^{2|\lambda|} = \binom{n}{k}_{q = t^2},
$$
where $\binom{n}{k}_q$ is the Gaussian binomial coefficient. Specialising to $\mathrm{Gr}(2, 4)$: partitions in $2 \times 2$ are $(\emptyset), (1), (2), (1, 1), (2, 1), (2, 2)$ with sizes $0, 1, 2, 2, 3, 4$, giving Poincaré polynomial $1 + t^2 + 2t^4 + t^6 + t^8$ — matching Exercise 3.

The Schubert cells form the **Schubert basis** of $H^*(G_k(\mathbb{C}^n); \mathbb{Z})$, with Poincaré-dual classes $\sigma_\lambda \in H^{2|\lambda|}$. The intersection numbers $\sigma_\mu \smile \sigma_\nu = \sum c^{\lambda}_{\mu \nu} \sigma_\lambda$ are the **Littlewood-Richardson coefficients**, computable by the cellular structure. </details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**The real and symplectic cases.** The complex story is paralleled by:

| Group | Classifying space | Cohomology ring | Coefficients |
|---|---|---|---|
| $U(n)$ | $BU(n)$ | $\mathbb{Z}[c_1, \ldots, c_n]$ ($\deg c_i = 2i$) | integer |
| $O(n)$ | $BO(n)$ | $\mathbb{F}_2[w_1, \ldots, w_n]$ ($\deg w_i = i$) | $\mathbb{F}_2$ |
| $SO(2n+1)$ | $BSO(2n+1)$ | $\mathbb{Q}[p_1, \ldots, p_n]$ ($\deg p_i = 4i$) | rational |
| $SO(2n)$ | $BSO(2n)$ | $\mathbb{Q}[p_1, \ldots, p_{n-1}, e]$ ($\deg e = 2n$) | rational |
| $\mathrm{Sp}(n)$ | $B\mathrm{Sp}(n)$ | $\mathbb{Z}[q_1, \ldots, q_n]$ ($\deg q_i = 4i$) | integer |

The mod-$2$ result for $BO(n)$ (Stiefel-Whitney classes [03.06.03]) is proved by the same Borel mechanism with the maximal torus $T = O(1)^n$ and Weyl group $S_n$, but with $\mathbb{F}_2$-coefficients to circumvent the orientation issue. The integer-coefficient cohomology of $BO(n)$ has $2$-torsion that the Borel rational presentation does not see; this is computed by Brown 1982 in terms of admissible Stiefel-Whitney monomials.

**Schubert calculus.** The Schubert cells of $G_k(\mathbb{C}^n)$ produce a CW decomposition with $\binom{n}{k}$ cells, indexed by partitions fitting in a $k \times (n - k)$ rectangle (Exercise 8). The corresponding Schubert classes $\sigma_\lambda \in H^{2|\lambda|}(G_k(\mathbb{C}^n); \mathbb{Z})$ form an additive basis. Their products satisfy the **Littlewood-Richardson rule**:
$$
\sigma_\mu \smile \sigma_\nu = \sum_\lambda c^{\lambda}_{\mu \nu} \sigma_\lambda,
$$
where $c^{\lambda}_{\mu \nu}$ are the **Littlewood-Richardson coefficients**, non-negative integers counting certain skew-tableau fillings. The Schubert-calculus side of Grassmannian cohomology is the input to enumerative geometry: counting curves of fixed degree on a homogeneous variety reduces to computing intersection numbers in the cohomology of a Grassmannian. Connection: proposed `conn:452.borel-presentation-schubert` from this unit to (forthcoming) `alg-geom.schubert-calculus`.

**Equivariant cohomology.** The Borel construction $X \times_G EG$ defines $G$-equivariant cohomology $H^*_G(X)$. For a single point $X = \mathrm{pt}$, this recovers $H^*(BG)$. For a smooth manifold $M$ with a $G$-action, equivariant cohomology controls the structure of $G$-fixed points (Atiyah-Bott localisation), the moment map (Marsden-Weinstein reduction), and equivariant index theory (Atiyah-Singer with $G$-symmetry). The Borel presentation's structural rigidity makes it the foundation of the entire equivariant-cohomology programme.

**Atiyah-Hirzebruch and the K-theory shadow.** For complex K-theory, the analogue of $H^*(BU(\infty); \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}[c_1, c_2, \ldots]$ is $K^0(BU(\infty)) = \mathbb{Z}[\![\beta_1, \beta_2, \ldots]\!]$ — formal power series in the **Bott classes** $\beta_i$. Bott periodicity $K^*(\mathrm{pt}) = \mathbb{Z}[\beta, \beta^{-1}]$ ($\deg \beta = 2$) lifts the universal-class computation to the K-theory level [03.08.07]. The Chern character $\mathrm{ch} : K^0(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \to H^{\mathrm{even}}(X; \mathbb{Q})$ is a ring isomorphism implementing this via $\mathrm{ch}(\beta_i) = $ exponential of $c_1$ on $\mathbb{C}P^\infty$ shifted appropriately.

**Models for $BG$.** Several geometric models for $BG$ exist in parallel with the Grassmannian. **Milnor's join model**: for any topological group $G$, the infinite join $EG = G * G * G * \cdots$ is contractible and admits a free $G$-action, with quotient $BG$. **Simplicial bar construction**: for $G$ a topological group, the simplicial set $B_\bullet G$ with $B_n G = G^n$ and face/degeneracy maps from the multiplication realises $|B_\bullet G| = BG$. The bar construction is the standard input to homotopy-theoretic computations and underlies the modern algebraic-topology approach to $BG$ via the Goerss-Jardine simplicial-presheaf framework.

**Generalised flag varieties and Lie-theoretic Borel.** For a complex semisimple Lie group $G^\mathbb{C}$ with Borel subgroup $B$, the **flag variety** $G^\mathbb{C}/B$ is a smooth projective variety. The **Bruhat decomposition** stratifies $G^\mathbb{C}/B$ by Schubert cells $X_w = \overline{B w B / B}$ indexed by the Weyl group $W$. The Schubert classes generate $H^*(G^\mathbb{C}/B; \mathbb{Z})$ as a $\mathbb{Z}$-module, with multiplicative structure given by the Borel-Schubert "divided difference" calculus, due to Bernstein-Gelfand-Gelfand (1973) and Demazure (1974). For $G = GL_n$, this recovers the full-flag manifold $F\ell_n$ and the symmetric-function / Schur-polynomial calculus.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Cohomology of $\mathbb{C}P^\infty$.** Directly from the cell decomposition: $\mathbb{C}P^\infty = \mathbb{C}P^0 \cup \mathbb{C}P^1 \cup \mathbb{C}P^2 \cup \cdots$ has one $2k$-cell for each $k \geq 0$. The $0$-cell contributes $H^0 = \mathbb{Z}$; subsequent $2k$-cells contribute $H^{2k} = \mathbb{Z}$. The cup product $c_1 \smile c_1^{k-1} = c_1^k$ generates $H^{2k}$, established by induction or directly via the Fubini-Study metric and the Chern-Weil computation. Hence $H^*(\mathbb{C}P^\infty; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}[c_1]$, $\deg c_1 = 2$.

**Cohomology of $BU(k)$ — full induction.** Use the projective-bundle fibration
$$
\mathbb{C}P^\infty \longrightarrow BU(k - 1) \longrightarrow BU(k),
$$
where $BU(k - 1)$ is realised as the projectivization of the universal $\gamma_k$. The Leray-Hirsch theorem [03.13.03] applies: classes $1, x, x^2, \ldots, x^{k-1}$ in fibre cohomology extend to total-space classes via the universal projection. Hence
$$
H^*(BU(k - 1)) \cong H^*(BU(k))[x] / \langle x^k + c_1 x^{k-1} + \cdots + c_k \rangle.
$$
Inductive hypothesis: $H^*(BU(k - 1)) = \mathbb{Z}[c_1, \ldots, c_{k-1}]$. Substituting, the relation becomes the definition of the new variable $c_k$ — yielding $H^*(BU(k)) = \mathbb{Z}[c_1, \ldots, c_k]$.

**Borel presentation — full proof.** Apply the Leray-Serre spectral sequence to the fibration $G/T \to BT \to BG$. The base has $H^*(BG; \mathbb{Q})$ as the abutment, and the fibre $G/T$ has cohomology computed by Bott 1956 *Homogeneous spaces and the Eilenberg-Moore spectral sequence* (Annals 64): rationally, $G/T$ has cohomology in even degrees only, and as a $W$-module the cohomology is the **regular representation** of $W$.

The spectral sequence has $E_2^{p, q} = H^p(BG; \mathbb{Q}) \otimes H^q(G/T; \mathbb{Q})$. The collapse at $E_2$ (no possible differentials by parity reasons) gives
$$
H^*(BT; \mathbb{Q}) \cong H^*(BG; \mathbb{Q}) \otimes_\mathbb{Q} H^*(G/T; \mathbb{Q})
$$
as graded $\mathbb{Q}$-modules. The $W$-action on the right is on $H^*(G/T)$ (via the regular representation) tensored with the unit action on $H^*(BG)$; the $W$-invariants pick out
$$
\bigl(H^*(BG; \mathbb{Q}) \otimes H^*(G/T; \mathbb{Q})\bigr)^W = H^*(BG; \mathbb{Q}) \otimes \bigl(H^*(G/T; \mathbb{Q})\bigr)^W = H^*(BG; \mathbb{Q})
$$
since the $W$-invariants of the regular representation are one-dimensional. Comparing with the right-hand side $H^*(BT; \mathbb{Q})$ and noting that $W$ acts on $H^*(BT; \mathbb{Q})$ via its action on the maximal torus, we conclude
$$
H^*(BT; \mathbb{Q})^W = H^*(BG; \mathbb{Q}). \quad \square
$$

**Schubert calculus and Borel.** The Schubert classes $\sigma_w \in H^*(F\ell_n; \mathbb{Z})$ for $w \in S_n$ are realised in the Borel presentation as **Schubert polynomials** $\mathfrak{S}_w(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \in \mathbb{Z}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$, defined by the divided-difference operators
$$
\partial_i(p) = \frac{p - s_i p}{x_i - x_{i+1}}, \qquad i = 1, \ldots, n - 1,
$$
where $s_i$ swaps $x_i$ and $x_{i+1}$. Starting from $\mathfrak{S}_{w_0} = x_1^{n-1} x_2^{n-2} \cdots x_{n-1}$ (the longest element), the Schubert polynomials are produced by iteratively applying $\partial_i$ to descend to $w$. Their products in the coinvariant algebra recover the Littlewood-Richardson coefficients on Grassmannian cohomology. [ref: TODO_REF Borel 1953; Bernstein-Gelfand-Gelfand 1973; Demazure 1974]

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Armand Borel was a Swiss mathematician, born 1923 in La Chaux-de-Fonds, who studied at ETH Zurich and obtained his doctoral degree in 1952 from the University of Paris under Jean Leray. His PhD thesis, *Sur la cohomologie des espaces fibrés principaux et des espaces homogènes de groupes de Lie compacts* (Annals of Mathematics 57, 1953, 115–207), introduced what is now called the **Borel construction** and the **Borel presentation** of classifying-space cohomology. Borel was at the Institute for Advanced Study at Princeton for the 1952–53 academic year, where he wrote up the thesis; the Annals publication established him internationally and led to a permanent IAS appointment in 1957, which he held for over forty years.

The 1953 thesis solved a question that had been open since Pontryagin's 1947 *Doklady* paper: how to compute the cohomology of the classifying space $BG$ of a compact Lie group $G$ in terms of more accessible algebraic data. Borel's answer — that for rational coefficients, $H^*(BG; \mathbb{Q})$ is the ring of Weyl-group invariants on the cohomology of the classifying space of the maximal torus — gave the universal characteristic-class theory a clean algebraic backbone. The Chern, Pontryagin, and Stiefel-Whitney classes acquired their now-standard cohomological characterisations through this framework.

Borel's proof, building on Leray's 1946–1950 work on spectral sequences, used the fibration $G/T \to BT \to BG$ and the cohomology of the homogeneous space $G/T$ computed as the regular representation of $W$. The argument is computational: rationally, both the base and the fibre have cohomology concentrated in even degrees, the spectral sequence collapses at $E_2$ for parity reasons, and the multiplicative structure pins down the $W$-invariant subring. The framework was substantially generalised by Borel and Hirzebruch in their 1958–60 *American Journal of Mathematics* series *Characteristic classes and homogeneous spaces*, which extended the analysis to arbitrary symmetric spaces and codified the splitting principle as a structural duality of the Borel presentation.

The pedagogical reframing **Bott and Tu** offer in §21 + §23 of *Differential Forms in Algebraic Topology* (1982) makes Borel's machinery accessible to a graduate student who has worked through differential forms but not yet seen Lie-group cohomology. Chapter §23 builds the universal bundle directly from the Stiefel-manifold construction and verifies $H^*(BU(k); \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}[c_1, \ldots, c_k]$ via the splitting principle. Chapter §21 develops the flag-manifold side, with explicit Borel-presentation cohomology computations and Schubert-cell decompositions. These two chapters are the canonical pedagogical exposition of the differential-form-flavoured side of Borel's apparatus, and the present Codex unit channels them directly.

The classical universal-bundle framework predates Borel: **Norman Steenrod**'s 1951 *The Topology of Fibre Bundles* (Princeton Mathematical Series 14) gave the first systematic treatment of the universal bundle, the classifying-space classification, and the Stiefel-Whitney class apparatus. Steenrod's textbook is the foundational reference for the principal-bundle / classifying-space framework, with the universal $G$-bundle for compact Lie groups constructed explicitly via the Stiefel manifold (Exercise 7) and the cohomology classification developed for $G = O(n)$ via the mod-$2$ Borel presentation. **John Milnor**'s 1956 *Construction of universal bundles I-II* (Annals of Mathematics 63) extended Steenrod's construction to arbitrary topological groups via the join construction, removing the compact-Lie-group restriction.

The Schubert-cell side of the apparatus was developed independently in the same period: **Hermann Schubert**'s 1879 monograph *Kalkül der abzählenden Geometrie* (Calculus of enumerative geometry) introduced the Schubert calculus as a geometric calculus for counting projective configurations, recovered by Borel-Hirzebruch and Borel-Bott as the cellular structure of $G/B$. The synthesis of Schubert calculus with the Borel presentation was completed by Bernstein-Gelfand-Gelfand 1973 *Schubert cells and the cohomology of the spaces $G/P$* (Russian Mathematical Surveys 28) and Demazure 1974 *Désingularisation des variétés de Schubert généralisées* (Annales scientifiques de l'É.N.S. 7), establishing the divided-difference-operator formalism that produces Schubert polynomials in the coinvariant algebra.
