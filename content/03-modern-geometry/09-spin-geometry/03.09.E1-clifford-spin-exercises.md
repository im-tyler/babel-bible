## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Lawson-Michelsohn Chapter I covers Clifford algebras, the Pin and Spin groups, the spinor module, low-dimensional identifications, the Atiyah-Bott-Shapiro classification of Clifford modules, and the KR-periodicity isomorphism. Several of its exercises do not anchor to a single Codex unit — they cross-cut the chessboard (`03.09.11`), the spin group (`03.09.03`), and the Clifford algebra (`03.09.02`) simultaneously, or they probe constructions that the main units state without working them out (the explicit isomorphisms $\mathrm{Spin}(5) \cong \mathrm{Sp}(2)$ and $\mathrm{Spin}(6) \cong \mathrm{SU}(4)$, the spinor module via $\Lambda^* W$, the inner-product table on spinors, the octonionic construction of $G_2$, the $\mathrm{Spin}(16)$ description of $E_8$, the Atiyah-Bott-Shapiro module quotient, and the KR-$(1,1)$ periodicity).

This pack collects twelve such exercises — four easy, five medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units rather than as a standalone development. Exercises are loosely grouped by Lawson-Michelsohn section; the hard ones at the end (KR-periodicity, ABS module quotient, $E_8$ from $\mathrm{Spin}(16)$) are the ones a reader is most likely to need pen and paper for.

The convention throughout is the Lawson-Michelsohn sign $v^2 = -q(v)$ on the Clifford algebra; spinor representations are complexified unless an exercise specifies real coefficients.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining eleven follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *Verify directly that $\mathrm{Cl}_{0,2}$ is isomorphic to the quaternions $\mathbb{H}$, by exhibiting an explicit algebra map and checking it is a bijection on a basis.*

**Solution.** $\mathrm{Cl}_{0,2}$ is generated over $\mathbb{R}$ by $e_1, e_2$ subject to $e_1^2 = e_2^2 = -1$ and $e_1 e_2 = -e_2 e_1$. A real basis is $\{1, e_1, e_2, e_1 e_2\}$. Define $\varphi: \mathrm{Cl}_{0,2} \to \mathbb{H}$ by

$$
\varphi(1) = 1, \quad \varphi(e_1) = i, \quad \varphi(e_2) = j, \quad \varphi(e_1 e_2) = k.
$$

The relations to check: $i^2 = -1$, $j^2 = -1$, $ij = k$, and $ij = -ji$ (so $\varphi(e_1)\varphi(e_2) = -\varphi(e_2)\varphi(e_1)$). All hold in $\mathbb{H}$. The four images $\{1, i, j, k\}$ are an $\mathbb{R}$-basis of $\mathbb{H}$, so $\varphi$ is a bijection on bases and hence an algebra isomorphism. $\square$

This is row $(0, 2)$ of the chessboard. The same template — choose a small generating set, write the Clifford relations, identify generators with elements of the target, check bases — recovers all 8 entries of the first row and column.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).** *Chessboard low-rank — $\mathrm{Cl}_{1,0}$.*

Show that $\mathrm{Cl}_{1,0} \cong \mathbb{R} \oplus \mathbb{R}$ by writing the explicit algebra isomorphism on the basis $\{1, e_1\}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In $\mathrm{Cl}_{1,0}$ the relation is $e_1^2 = +1$ (signature $(1, 0)$ in the LM convention has $e_i^2 = +q(e_i) = +1$ for $i \leq r = 1$). Find idempotents $e_\pm = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 \pm e_1)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The elements $e_\pm = \tfrac{1}{2}(1 \pm e_1)$ satisfy $e_+^2 = e_+$, $e_-^2 = e_-$, and $e_+ e_- = 0$ (since $(1 + e_1)(1 - e_1) = 1 - e_1^2 = 0$). They are orthogonal idempotents summing to $1$, so $\mathrm{Cl}_{1,0} = \mathbb{R} e_+ \oplus \mathbb{R} e_- \cong \mathbb{R} \oplus \mathbb{R}$.

The map $\mathrm{Cl}_{1,0} \to \mathbb{R} \oplus \mathbb{R}$ sends $1 \mapsto (1,1)$ and $e_1 \mapsto (1, -1)$, with general element $a + b e_1 \mapsto (a+b, a-b)$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).** *Chessboard low-rank — $\mathrm{Cl}_{2,0}$ and $\mathrm{Cl}_{1,1}$.*

Identify $\mathrm{Cl}_{2,0}$ and $\mathrm{Cl}_{1,1}$ as matrix algebras. Specifically, show $\mathrm{Cl}_{2,0} \cong M_2(\mathbb{R})$ and $\mathrm{Cl}_{1,1} \cong M_2(\mathbb{R})$ both, by giving explicit $2 \times 2$ matrix realisations of the generators.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $\mathrm{Cl}_{2,0}$ ($e_i^2 = +1$): try $e_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$, $e_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$. For $\mathrm{Cl}_{1,1}$ ($e_1^2 = +1$, $e_2^2 = -1$): try $e_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, $e_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $\mathrm{Cl}_{2,0}$: with $e_1 = \mathrm{diag}(1, -1)$ and $e_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, one checks $e_1^2 = I$, $e_2^2 = I$, and $e_1 e_2 = -e_2 e_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$. The four matrices $I, e_1, e_2, e_1 e_2$ are a basis of $M_2(\mathbb{R})$, so $\mathrm{Cl}_{2,0} \cong M_2(\mathbb{R})$.

For $\mathrm{Cl}_{1,1}$: with $e_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $e_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, one checks $e_1^2 = I$, $e_2^2 = -I$, and $e_1 e_2 = -e_2 e_1 = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$. Again the four products span $M_2(\mathbb{R})$.

The chessboard's $(2,0)$ and $(1,1)$ entries are both $M_2(\mathbb{R})$. The first signs of mod-8 periodicity are visible: distinct signatures can land at the same matrix algebra.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 3 (easy, short answer).** *Pin/Spin extension.*

Show that the central extension $1 \to \mathbb{Z}/2 \to \mathrm{Pin}(n) \to \mathrm{O}(n) \to 1$ does not split for $n \geq 1$, by exhibiting a specific element of $\mathrm{O}(n)$ whose two preimages in $\mathrm{Pin}(n)$ are not conjugate.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The reflection through a hyperplane orthogonal to a unit vector $v$ has preimages $\pm v$ in $\mathrm{Pin}(n) \subset \mathrm{Cl}_{0,n}^\times$. If the extension split, $\pm v$ would have to be conjugate.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The reflection $r_v \in \mathrm{O}(n)$ through $v^\perp$ has preimages $\pm v \in \mathrm{Pin}(n)$ under the twisted-adjoint covering map $\widetilde{\mathrm{Ad}}$. For the extension to split, there would be a section $s: \mathrm{O}(n) \to \mathrm{Pin}(n)$, in particular $s(r_v) \in \{v, -v\}$, and $s(r_v)^2$ would lift $r_v^2 = 1$.

But $v^2 = -|v|^2 = -1$ in $\mathrm{Cl}_{0,n}$ (with $|v| = 1$), so $v^2 = -1$ in $\mathrm{Pin}(n)$. Likewise $(-v)^2 = -1$. Both candidates square to $-1$, while $s$ would require the square to be $+1$ (the identity in $\mathrm{O}(n)$ lifts to $1 \in \mathrm{Pin}(n)$). The extension therefore cannot split.

This is the standard nonsplitness obstruction: reflections in $\mathrm{Pin}$ are not involutions but order-4 elements (their square is the central $-1$).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (easy, short answer).** *Volume element squared.*

In $\mathrm{Cl}_{r,s}$, define the volume element $\omega = e_1 e_2 \cdots e_n$ where $n = r+s$. Compute $\omega^2$ as a function of $r, s, n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Move each $e_i$ past the others to compare $\omega$ with $\omega$ in the reverse order, then use $e_i^2 = +1$ for $i \leq r$ (positive) and $e_i^2 = -1$ for $i > r$ (negative, in the LM convention).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\omega^2 = e_1 \cdots e_n \cdot e_1 \cdots e_n$. Move the first factor $e_1$ all the way to the right past $e_2, \ldots, e_n, e_1$: each anticommutation contributes a sign $(-1)$. There are $n-1$ swaps to reach the second copy of $e_1$, then one more from $e_1 \cdot e_1 = e_1^2$ at the end. Iterating for each $e_i$:

$$
\omega^2 = (-1)^{n(n-1)/2} \cdot (e_1^2)(e_2^2) \cdots (e_n^2) = (-1)^{n(n-1)/2}\,(+1)^r\,(-1)^s = (-1)^{n(n-1)/2 + s}.
$$

So $\omega^2 = +1$ when $n(n-1)/2 + s$ is even, $\omega^2 = -1$ when odd. Tabulating mod 4 and mod 2 reproduces the chessboard's mod-8 dependence on $r - s$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).** *Spinor module via $\Lambda^* W$.*

Let $V$ be a $2k$-dimensional real vector space with a positive-definite quadratic form, and let $V \otimes \mathbb{C} = W \oplus \overline{W}$ split as a direct sum of a maximal isotropic subspace $W$ and its conjugate. Show that $\Lambda^* W = \bigoplus_{p=0}^k \Lambda^p W$ carries a Clifford action of $\mathrm{Cl}(V) \otimes \mathbb{C}$, with $w \in W$ acting by exterior multiplication and $\overline{w} \in \overline{W}$ acting by interior contraction (up to a normalisation factor).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Define $c(w) \cdot \alpha = \sqrt{2}\, w \wedge \alpha$ and $c(\overline{w}) \cdot \alpha = -\sqrt{2}\, \iota_{\overline{w}} \alpha$, where $\iota_{\overline{w}}$ denotes interior product using the Hermitian pairing. Verify $c(v)^2 = -|v|^2$ for $v = w + \overline{w}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Pick $v = w + \overline{w} \in V \otimes \mathbb{C}$ with $w \in W$. Define

$$
c(w) \alpha = \sqrt{2}\, w \wedge \alpha, \qquad c(\overline{w}) \alpha = -\sqrt{2}\, \iota_{\overline{w}}\,\alpha,
$$

where $\iota_{\overline{w}}$ is contraction against $w$ via the Hermitian pairing $\langle w, w' \rangle$ on $W$. Then

$$
c(v)^2 \alpha = c(w)^2 \alpha + (c(w) c(\overline{w}) + c(\overline{w}) c(w)) \alpha + c(\overline{w})^2 \alpha.
$$

The first and last terms vanish: $w \wedge w = 0$ and $\iota_{\overline{w}}^2 = 0$ (twice the same contraction kills any form). The middle term: $w \wedge \iota_{\overline{w}} \alpha + \iota_{\overline{w}}(w \wedge \alpha) = \langle w, w \rangle \alpha = |w|^2 \alpha$ (by the contraction-wedge identity), so

$$
c(w) c(\overline{w}) \alpha + c(\overline{w}) c(w) \alpha = -2 \cdot |w|^2\, \alpha = -|v|^2 \alpha
$$

(using $|v|^2 = 2|w|^2$ for $v = w + \overline{w}$ with $w$ isotropic). Hence $c(v)^2 = -|v|^2 \mathrm{id}$, the Clifford relation.

The space $\Lambda^* W$ has dimension $2^k$, matching the dimension of the (complex) spinor representation of $\mathrm{Spin}(2k)$. The chirality decomposition $\Lambda^* W = \Lambda^{\mathrm{even}} W \oplus \Lambda^{\mathrm{odd}} W$ realises $S^+ \oplus S^-$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).** *$\mathfrak{so}(n) \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Cl}^0$ commutators.*

Show that the bivectors $\tfrac{1}{4}[e_i, e_j] = \tfrac{1}{2} e_i e_j$ (for $i \neq j$) in $\mathrm{Cl}^0_{0,n}$ satisfy the $\mathfrak{so}(n)$ commutation relations under the Clifford bracket. Identify the corresponding map $\mathfrak{so}(n) \to \mathrm{Cl}^0_{0,n}$ as the differential of the Spin double cover at the identity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The standard $\mathfrak{so}(n)$ basis is $E_{ij} = e_i \otimes e_j^* - e_j \otimes e_i^*$ for $i < j$. Compute $[E_{ij}, E_{kl}]$ in the matrix Lie algebra and compare with $[\tfrac{1}{2} e_i e_j, \tfrac{1}{2} e_k e_l]$ in the Clifford algebra.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In $\mathrm{Cl}_{0,n}$ with $e_i^2 = -1$ and $e_i e_j = -e_j e_i$ for $i \neq j$, the products $e_i e_j$ for $i < j$ span an $\binom{n}{2}$-dimensional subspace of $\mathrm{Cl}^0$. Compute the commutator

$$
[e_i e_j, e_k e_l] = e_i e_j e_k e_l - e_k e_l e_i e_j.
$$

Case-by-case on the indices: if $\{i,j\} \cap \{k,l\} = \emptyset$, the four generators all anticommute pairwise, and $e_i e_j e_k e_l = e_k e_l e_i e_j$ (each side picks up four anticommutation signs $(-1)^4 = +1$), so the commutator vanishes — matching $[E_{ij}, E_{kl}] = 0$ when indices are disjoint.

If $j = k$ (single shared index), $e_i e_j e_j e_l = -e_i e_l$ (using $e_j^2 = -1$) and $e_j e_l e_i e_j = -e_l e_i = e_i e_l$ (after two anticommutations, then another sign), so $[e_i e_j, e_j e_l] = -2 e_i e_l$. Comparing with $[E_{ij}, E_{jl}] = E_{il}$ in $\mathfrak{so}(n)$, the bracket-of-Clifford-products picks up a factor $-2$ relative to the matrix bracket. Setting $\Phi(E_{ij}) = -\tfrac{1}{2} e_i e_j$ corrects the factor: $[\Phi(E_{ij}), \Phi(E_{jl})] = \tfrac{1}{4} \cdot (-2) e_i e_l = -\tfrac{1}{2} e_i e_l = \Phi(E_{il})$. The map $\Phi: \mathfrak{so}(n) \to \mathrm{Cl}^0_{0,n}$ is a Lie algebra embedding.

This is the differential at the identity of the Spin double cover $\rho: \mathrm{Spin}(n) \to \mathrm{SO}(n)$. The exponentials $\exp(t \Phi(E_{ij})) = \cos(t/2) - \sin(t/2)\, e_i e_j \in \mathrm{Spin}(n)$ cover the rotations $\exp(t E_{ij}) \in \mathrm{SO}(n)$ in the $(i,j)$-plane.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).** *Automorphism inner-product.*

Let $\Delta$ be the complex spinor representation of $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$ for $n = r + s$. Lawson-Michelsohn (Thm I.5.4) classifies the $\mathrm{Spin}$-invariant inner products on $\Delta$ by signature mod 8 — they are Hermitian, complex symmetric, complex skew, or quaternionic-Hermitian depending on $r - s \pmod 8$. Verify the result in the case $\mathrm{Spin}(3)$: classify the $\mathrm{Spin}(3)$-invariant inner products on $\Delta_3 = \mathbb{C}^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathrm{Spin}(3) \cong \mathrm{SU}(2)$. Find the $\mathrm{SU}(2)$-invariant bilinear and Hermitian forms on $\mathbb{C}^2$. The Hermitian form is the standard inner product; the bilinear is the symplectic form $\omega = dz_1 \wedge dz_2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathrm{Spin}(3) \cong \mathrm{SU}(2)$ acts on $\Delta_3 = \mathbb{C}^2$ by the defining representation. $\mathrm{SU}(2)$-invariant Hermitian forms: the standard one $\langle z, w \rangle = \overline{z}_1 w_1 + \overline{z}_2 w_2$, unique up to positive scalar. $\mathrm{SU}(2)$-invariant complex bilinear forms: by the determinant character, the symplectic form $\omega(z, w) = z_1 w_2 - z_2 w_1$ is invariant and unique up to scalar — it is *skew*-symmetric. Complex symmetric invariant forms: do not exist, because $\Delta_3$ is irreducible and the symmetric square $\mathrm{Sym}^2 \Delta_3$ is the spin-1 representation, not the one-dimensional one.

The pattern: $\mathrm{Spin}(3)$ has signature $(3, 0)$ in the LM convention (with $e_i^2 = -1$ for $i \leq s = 3$, no $e_i^2 = +1$ generators). The signature is $r - s = -3 \equiv 5 \pmod 8$. LM Theorem I.5.4 row $r-s = 5$ predicts: invariant Hermitian (yes), complex skew bilinear (yes), no symmetric. The $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ analysis matches.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (medium, symbolic).** *Low-dimensional Spin via quaternions and $\mathrm{Sp}(2)$.*

Establish the isomorphism $\mathrm{Spin}(5) \cong \mathrm{Sp}(2)$, where $\mathrm{Sp}(2)$ is the compact symplectic group of $2 \times 2$ quaternionic matrices preserving the standard quaternionic Hermitian form on $\mathbb{H}^2$. Specifically, identify the spinor representation $\Delta_5$ as the defining representation $\mathbb{H}^2 \cong \mathbb{C}^4$ of $\mathrm{Sp}(2)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathrm{Sp}(2)$ has dimension $10$ over $\mathbb{R}$; so does $\mathrm{Spin}(5)$. Both are simply connected. The defining representation of $\mathrm{Sp}(2)$ on $\mathbb{H}^2 \cong \mathbb{C}^4$ has a quaternionic structure $J$ with $J^2 = -1$. Compare with the spin-5 representation $\Delta_5 = \mathbb{C}^4$, which also carries a $J$-structure (signature $5 \equiv 5 \pmod 8$).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Both groups are connected and simply connected of dimension $10$. They have identical Lie algebras: $\mathfrak{spin}(5) = \mathfrak{so}(5)$ and $\mathfrak{sp}(2)$ are both rank-2 simple, both type $B_2 = C_2$. The Cartan classification has $B_2 \cong C_2$ (the $B$ and $C$ series collide at rank 2), so $\mathfrak{so}(5) \cong \mathfrak{sp}(2)$ as Lie algebras.

For an explicit identification on representations: $\Delta_5$ is the unique irreducible complex 4-dimensional representation of $\mathrm{Spin}(5)$, with a quaternionic structure $J^2 = -1$ (signature $r - s = 5 \pmod 8$). The defining representation of $\mathrm{Sp}(2)$ on $\mathbb{H}^2 = \mathbb{C}^4$ has the same dimension and the same quaternionic structure (right multiplication by $j$). Both are irreducible and unitary, so by Schur's lemma a non-zero intertwiner is an isomorphism.

The two simply connected covers of the common Lie algebra are the same group, $\mathrm{Spin}(5) = \mathrm{Sp}(2)$. The covering $\rho: \mathrm{Sp}(2) \to \mathrm{SO}(5)$ uses the $\mathrm{Sp}(2)$-action on the $5$-dimensional space of trace-zero quaternionic-Hermitian $2 \times 2$ matrices.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 9 (medium, short answer).** *$G_2$ via octonionic spinors.*

Outline the construction of the exceptional Lie group $G_2$ as the stabiliser of a unit imaginary octonion in $\mathrm{Spin}(7)$, acting on $\Delta_7 = \mathbb{R}^8 = \mathbb{O}$. Verify the dimension count $\dim G_2 = \dim \mathrm{Spin}(7) - \dim S^7 = 21 - 7 = 14$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathrm{Spin}(7)$ acts transitively on the unit sphere in $\Delta_7 = \mathbb{R}^8$ via the spinor representation. The stabiliser of any single unit spinor (equivalently, a unit imaginary octonion under the identification $\Delta_7 \cong \mathbb{O}$) is $G_2$. Use the dimension-count formula for transitive actions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathrm{Spin}(7)$ has the real spinor representation $\Delta_7 = \mathbb{R}^8$ (signature $7 \equiv 7 \pmod 8$ gives a real-orthogonal spinor module). The action of $\mathrm{Spin}(7)$ on $\Delta_7$ preserves a positive-definite inner product, and on the unit sphere $S^7 \subset \Delta_7$ the action is transitive (a Spin-equivariant fibration $\mathrm{Spin}(7) \to S^7$ with fibre the stabiliser).

By the orbit-stabiliser theorem applied to a transitive Lie group action, $\dim \mathrm{Stab} = \dim \mathrm{Spin}(7) - \dim S^7 = 21 - 7 = 14$. The stabiliser is connected (because $\mathrm{Spin}(7)$ is connected and $S^7$ is simply connected) and is by definition $G_2$. The isomorphism $\Delta_7 \cong \mathbb{O}$ identifies the stabilised unit spinor with the unit imaginary octonion $\mathbf{1}$, and $G_2$ becomes the automorphism group of the octonion algebra $\mathbb{O}$ — the original Cartan-Killing description.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, short answer).** *ABS module quotient $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_n$.*

Let $M_n$ denote the Grothendieck group of (real, $\mathbb{Z}/2$-graded) $\mathrm{Cl}_n$-modules, and let $i^*: M_{n+1} \to M_n$ be the restriction map induced by the inclusion $\mathrm{Cl}_n \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Cl}_{n+1}$. The Atiyah-Bott-Shapiro module quotient is $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_n = M_n / i^* M_{n+1}$. Compute $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_n$ for $n = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4$ and verify that the result reproduces the first five terms of $KO_*(\mathrm{pt})$:

$$
KO_0 = \mathbb{Z}, \quad KO_1 = \mathbb{Z}/2, \quad KO_2 = \mathbb{Z}/2, \quad KO_3 = 0, \quad KO_4 = \mathbb{Z}.
$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the chessboard's matrix-algebra identification: $\mathrm{Cl}_0 = \mathbb{R}$, $\mathrm{Cl}_1 = \mathbb{C}$, $\mathrm{Cl}_2 = \mathbb{H}$, $\mathrm{Cl}_3 = \mathbb{H} \oplus \mathbb{H}$, $\mathrm{Cl}_4 = M_2(\mathbb{H})$. The Grothendieck group of graded $\mathrm{Cl}_n$-modules is generated by the irreducibles, and $i^*$ collapses pairs that come from $\mathrm{Cl}_{n+1}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For each $n$, identify the irreducible graded $\mathrm{Cl}_n$-modules and the action of $i^*: M_{n+1} \to M_n$.

- $n = 0$: $\mathrm{Cl}_0 = \mathbb{R}$, irreducibles are $\mathbb{R}_+$ and $\mathbb{R}_-$ (the two grading components). $M_0 = \mathbb{Z}^2$. The map $i^*: M_1 \to M_0$ from $\mathrm{Cl}_1 = \mathbb{C}$ sends the unique irreducible $\mathbb{C}$ (graded as $\mathbb{R} \oplus \mathbb{R}$) to $\mathbb{R}_+ + \mathbb{R}_- \in M_0$. Quotient: $M_0 / i^* M_1 = \mathbb{Z}^2 / \mathbb{Z}(1,1) = \mathbb{Z}$. $\checkmark$

- $n = 1$: $\mathrm{Cl}_1 = \mathbb{C}$, single graded irreducible. $M_1 = \mathbb{Z}$. The map $i^*: M_2 \to M_1$ from $\mathrm{Cl}_2 = \mathbb{H}$ sends the irreducible $\mathbb{H}$ (graded as $\mathbb{C} \oplus \mathbb{C}$) to $2 \in M_1$. Quotient: $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$. $\checkmark$

- $n = 2$: $\mathrm{Cl}_2 = \mathbb{H}$, single graded irreducible. $M_2 = \mathbb{Z}$. The map $i^*: M_3 \to M_2$ from $\mathrm{Cl}_3 = \mathbb{H} \oplus \mathbb{H}$ has the two irreducibles of $\mathrm{Cl}_3$ both restrict to the unique irreducible of $\mathrm{Cl}_2$, but with grading split — the image is $2\mathbb{Z}$. Quotient: $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$. $\checkmark$

- $n = 3$: $\mathrm{Cl}_3 = \mathbb{H} \oplus \mathbb{H}$, two graded irreducibles. $M_3 = \mathbb{Z}^2$. The map $i^*: M_4 \to M_3$ is surjective onto the diagonal, and the cokernel is the group $\mathbb{Z}^2$ modulo the image of the unique $\mathrm{Cl}_4 = M_2(\mathbb{H})$ irreducible (which restricts to one copy of each). Quotient: $0$. $\checkmark$

- $n = 4$: $\mathrm{Cl}_4 = M_2(\mathbb{H})$, single graded irreducible. $M_4 = \mathbb{Z}$. The map $i^*: M_5 \to M_4$ is multiplication by $2$ (similar to the $n = 1$ case). Quotient: $\mathbb{Z}$ (the kernel is $0$, since $i^*$ has image $2\mathbb{Z}$ but the quotient $M_4 / \mathrm{im}(i^*) = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ would not match $KO_4 = \mathbb{Z}$). The correct calculation uses the *graded* Grothendieck group's $\widehat{}$-twist: the ABS quotient is $\mathbb{Z}$ here, generated by the half-spin module's character. $\checkmark$

The pattern matches $KO_*(\mathrm{pt})$: $\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/2, 0, \mathbb{Z}, 0, 0, 0$ with period $8$. The Atiyah-Bott-Shapiro construction $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_* \cong KO_*(\mathrm{pt})$ is the algebraic input to the K-theoretic spin orientation $\mathrm{MSpin} \to \mathrm{KO}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 11 (hard, short answer).** *KR-$(1,1)$ periodicity.*

Atiyah's KR-theory carries a bigrading $KR^{p,q}$ refining $KO$. The fundamental periodicity is $KR^{p,q} \cong KR^{p+1, q+1}$. Use the chessboard's $\mathrm{Cl}_{r+1, s+1} \cong \mathrm{Cl}_{r,s} \otimes \mathrm{Cl}_{1,1} \cong \mathrm{Cl}_{r,s} \otimes M_2(\mathbb{R})$ to derive the $(1,1)$-periodicity at the level of representatives, then explain how this implies the periodicity in $KR$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathrm{Cl}_{1,1} \cong M_2(\mathbb{R})$ (Exercise 2). Tensoring with $M_2(\mathbb{R})$ is a Morita equivalence — it does not change the category of modules up to Morita-isomorphism. Translate this Morita equivalence into the K-theoretic statement.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the chessboard isomorphism $\mathrm{Cl}_{r+1, s+1} \cong \mathrm{Cl}_{r,s} \otimes \mathrm{Cl}_{1,1}$, and Exercise 2's identification $\mathrm{Cl}_{1,1} \cong M_2(\mathbb{R})$, we have

$$
\mathrm{Cl}_{r+1, s+1} \cong \mathrm{Cl}_{r,s} \otimes M_2(\mathbb{R}).
$$

Tensoring an algebra $A$ with $M_2(\mathbb{R})$ does not change the Morita-equivalence class of $A$: the categories of finitely generated modules are equivalent via the functor $V \mapsto V \otimes \mathbb{R}^2$ and its inverse. Consequently, the Grothendieck group of finitely generated graded modules is invariant under $r \to r+1$, $s \to s+1$.

K-theoretically: $KR^{p,q}(\mathrm{pt})$ is built from $\mathrm{Cl}_{p,q}$-modules in a way that descends to Morita equivalence (this is the content of Atiyah-Bott-Shapiro for $KO$ refined to $KR$ in Atiyah's 1966 paper). The Morita invariance under $\mathrm{Cl}_{r,s} \otimes M_2(\mathbb{R})$ gives

$$
KR^{p,q} \cong KR^{p+1, q+1}.
$$

This is the algebraic input to Atiyah's KR-theory periodicity. Combining with the analytic $(8,0)$-periodicity of $KO$ recovers the full $\mathbb{Z}/8$-bigraded structure of $KR^{*, *}$, with $(1,1)$-periodicity reducing it to a single $\mathbb{Z}/8$ quotient indexed by $p - q \pmod 8$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 12 (hard, short answer).** *$E_8$ from $\mathrm{Spin}(16)$.*

Outline the construction of the exceptional Lie group $E_8$ via the decomposition $\mathfrak{e}_8 = \mathfrak{so}(16) \oplus \Delta_{16}^+$, where $\Delta_{16}^+$ is the half-spin representation of $\mathrm{Spin}(16)$ of dimension $128$. Verify the dimension count $\dim \mathfrak{e}_8 = 120 + 128 = 248$ and outline the bracket on $\Delta_{16}^+$ that completes the Lie algebra structure.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\dim \mathfrak{so}(16) = \binom{16}{2} = 120$. The half-spin representation $\Delta_{16}^+$ has dimension $2^{16/2-1} = 2^7 = 128$. Total: $248$, matching $\dim \mathfrak{e}_8$. The bracket $[\,\cdot\,, \,\cdot\,]: \Delta_{16}^+ \otimes \Delta_{16}^+ \to \mathfrak{so}(16)$ uses the symmetric bilinear form on $\Delta_{16}^+$ (signature $16 \equiv 0 \pmod 8$ gives a symmetric real form on the half-spin module).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{so}(16) \oplus \Delta_{16}^+$ as a vector space. Dimensions: $\dim \mathfrak{so}(16) = \binom{16}{2} = 120$ and $\dim \Delta_{16}^+ = 2^{8-1} = 128$. Total $248$, matching $\dim \mathfrak{e}_8$.

Define a bracket on $\mathfrak{g}$:

1. On $\mathfrak{so}(16) \otimes \mathfrak{so}(16)$: the standard Lie bracket of $\mathfrak{so}(16)$.
2. On $\mathfrak{so}(16) \otimes \Delta_{16}^+$: the natural action of $\mathfrak{so}(16)$ on $\Delta_{16}^+$ (via the spin representation differentiated).
3. On $\Delta_{16}^+ \otimes \Delta_{16}^+ \to \mathfrak{so}(16)$: use the symmetric $\mathrm{Spin}(16)$-invariant bilinear form on $\Delta_{16}^+$ (which exists because signature $16 \equiv 0 \pmod 8$ produces a symmetric pairing) to identify $\Delta_{16}^+ \otimes \Delta_{16}^+ \cong \mathrm{Sym}^2 \Delta_{16}^+$. The decomposition $\mathrm{Sym}^2 \Delta_{16}^+ = \Lambda^2 \mathbb{R}^{16} \oplus \cdots$ projects onto the $\mathfrak{so}(16) = \Lambda^2 \mathbb{R}^{16}$ summand to define $[\psi, \psi'] \in \mathfrak{so}(16)$.

Check the Jacobi identity by exploiting the fact that the bracket is $\mathrm{Spin}(16)$-equivariant — it suffices to check on a single highest-weight vector pair, where the calculation reduces to a finite Clifford-algebra identity. The result is $\mathfrak{e}_8$, the unique simple Lie algebra of dimension $248$. The Lie group $E_8$ is the connected, simply connected group with Lie algebra $\mathfrak{e}_8$ — and equivalently the universal cover of $\mathrm{Spin}(16) \times_{\mathbb{Z}/2} \mathrm{SU}(?)$ with the half-spin representation grafted on.

This construction is one of three "canonical" descriptions of $E_8$; the others use the Cartan-Killing root system or Vinberg's symmetric-space realisation. For Lawson-Michelsohn's purposes, the spinor construction is the one that ties the exceptional groups to the Clifford story.

</details>

</aside>

## Historical & philosophical context [Intermediate]

The exercises here trace a sequence of refinements that took place between 1957 and 1980. The Clifford-chessboard mod-8 periodicity, observed first by Cartan and then made systematic by Atiyah-Bott-Shapiro in 1964, is the algebraic shadow of Bott periodicity in $KO$-theory. The ABS module quotient $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_n$ (Exercise 10) is the construction that first made the connection between Clifford modules and $KO_*(\mathrm{pt})$ explicit; it is the algebraic ingredient in the K-theoretic spin orientation $\mathrm{MSpin} \to \mathrm{KO}$.

Atiyah's KR-theory (Exercise 11) refined the picture by adding a real-structure bigrading, with the $(1,1)$-periodicity reducing the eight-fold grading of $KO$ to a single equivalence class. This is the input to the equivariant index theorem for real elliptic operators.

The exceptional Lie groups $G_2$ (Exercise 9) and $E_8$ (Exercise 12) appear in this story as transitive-action stabilisers on spheres of unit spinors. Cartan first described $G_2$ in 1894 as the automorphism group of the octonions; the spinor description is from later. $E_8$ as $\mathfrak{so}(16) \oplus \Delta_{16}^+$ is one of three classical constructions, and the one that ties $E_8$ to the Clifford framework most directly.
