## Intuition [Beginner]

A Dirac operator on a single manifold produces one integer: the index. When you have a smooth family of Dirac operators, one for each point of a parameter space, you would like a single object that captures the whole family at once. The Bismut superconnection is that object.

Think of it as a single operator that does two jobs simultaneously. It contains the family of Dirac operators (one piece per fibre), and it contains a connection that tells you how solutions on neighbouring fibres are linked. These two pieces normally sit on different categories of object: a differential operator on a manifold, and a covariant derivative on a vector bundle. The superconnection puts them in one expression.

The payoff is that a single heat-equation computation, run on this combined operator, produces a differential form on the parameter space whose cohomology class is the Chern character of the family index. The same trick that proved the Atiyah-Singer theorem for one operator extends, with one new ingredient, to a whole family.

## Visual [Beginner]

A schematic of a fibration: a base space $B$ along the bottom, and above each point of $B$ sits a closed manifold (the fibre). On each fibre lives a Dirac operator. The Bismut superconnection is drawn as a single object that runs across the whole picture, with two ingredients labelled: the family of Dirac operators (a vertical piece on each fibre) and a connection (a horizontal piece moving across $B$).

![A fibration with base B and fibres above each point. A horizontal connection on the bundle of fibre solutions combines with a fibrewise Dirac operator to form a single object spanning the whole picture.](/img/bismut-superconnection-placeholder.svg)

The single object is the superconnection. Its squared heat kernel produces a differential form on $B$ representing the family index.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the simplest case where the base is a single point, so the family has only one member. There is just one fibre, and the bundle of fibre solutions is a finite-dimensional graded vector space $\mathcal{E} = \mathcal{E}^+ \oplus \mathcal{E}^-$ — concretely, the spinor space $S^+ \oplus S^-$ of $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$.

The superconnection reduces to just the Dirac operator $D$ itself: there is no base to put a connection on, and the only ingredient is the odd endomorphism piece. The differential form on the base (a single point) is just a number, and that number is the supertrace $\mathrm{Str}(e^{-tD^2})$. The McKean-Singer formula tells us this number is the index of $D$ at every positive $t$. The small-$t$ limit recovers the topological integral of the $\widehat A$-genus density over the manifold $M$.

What this shows is that the Bismut superconnection, in the single-point case, reproduces exactly the heat-kernel proof of Atiyah-Singer. The family version of the construction is a refinement: when the base $B$ is genuine (not a point), the superconnection produces differential forms on $B$, not just numbers, and these forms represent the Chern character of the family index as a class in the cohomology of $B$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Graded bundles and exterior forms.** Let $B$ be a smooth manifold and $\mathcal{E} = \mathcal{E}^+ \oplus \mathcal{E}^-$ a $\mathbb{Z}/2$-graded smooth vector bundle on $B$. The space $\Omega^*(B, \mathcal{E}) = \Omega^*(B) \otimes_{C^\infty(B)} \Gamma(\mathcal{E})$ of $\mathcal{E}$-valued differential forms inherits a total $\mathbb{Z}/2$ grading from the sum of the form-degree parity and the bundle parity. A linear operator $T : \Omega^*(B, \mathcal{E}) \to \Omega^*(B, \mathcal{E})$ has **total parity** $|T| \in \mathbb{Z}/2$ if it shifts the total grading by $|T|$.

**Definition (Quillen 1985 — superconnection).** A **superconnection** on $\mathcal{E}$ is an odd-parity first-order operator
$$
\mathbb{A} : \Omega^*(B, \mathcal{E}) \longrightarrow \Omega^*(B, \mathcal{E})
$$
satisfying the graded Leibniz rule
$$
\mathbb{A}(\omega \cdot s) = (d\omega) \cdot s + (-1)^{|\omega|} \omega \cdot \mathbb{A}(s)
$$
for $\omega \in \Omega^*(B)$ homogeneous of degree $|\omega|$ and $s \in \Gamma(\mathcal{E})$ [ref: TODO_REF Quillen 1985].

Expanding by form-degree, $\mathbb{A} = \mathbb{A}_{[0]} + \mathbb{A}_{[1]} + \mathbb{A}_{[2]} + \cdots$, where each $\mathbb{A}_{[k]} \in \Omega^k(B, \mathrm{End}(\mathcal{E}))$ has bundle parity $k+1 \pmod 2$ so that the total parity remains odd. The piece $\mathbb{A}_{[0]}$ is an odd endomorphism of $\mathcal{E}$ (i.e., $\mathbb{A}_{[0]} : \mathcal{E}^\pm \to \mathcal{E}^\mp$); the piece $\mathbb{A}_{[1]}$ is a ($\mathbb{Z}/2$-graded) connection on $\mathcal{E}$; the higher pieces $\mathbb{A}_{[k\geq 2]}$ are even-or-odd bundle-valued forms.

**Curvature.** The square $\mathbb{A}^2$ is an even operator and an order-zero differential operator (the first-order terms cancel because $d^2 = 0$ and the Leibniz cross-terms collapse), hence
$$
\mathbb{A}^2 \in \Omega^*(B, \mathrm{End}(\mathcal{E})).
$$
This is the **curvature** of the superconnection. The Bianchi identity $[\mathbb{A}, \mathbb{A}^2] = 0$ holds [ref: TODO_REF Quillen 1985; ref: TODO_REF Berline-Getzler-Vergne Ch. 9].

**Chern character.** The **superconnection Chern character** is the differential form
$$
\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}) := \mathrm{Str}(e^{-\mathbb{A}^2}) \in \Omega^{\mathrm{even}}(B),
$$
where $\mathrm{Str}$ is the supertrace $\mathrm{Str}(T) = \mathrm{Tr}(T|_{\mathcal{E}^+}) - \mathrm{Tr}(T|_{\mathcal{E}^-})$ extended $\Omega^*(B)$-linearly. The form $\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A})$ is closed in $\Omega^*(B)$, and its de Rham cohomology class depends only on the underlying graded $K$-theory class $[\mathcal{E}^+] - [\mathcal{E}^-] \in K^0(B)$.

**Counterexamples to common slips.**

- A superconnection is *not* a connection on the graded bundle in the ordinary sense. An ordinary connection lives in degree one only; a superconnection has pieces in every form-degree. The degree-one piece $\mathbb{A}_{[1]}$ is an ordinary connection, but it does not determine $\mathbb{A}$.
- The curvature $\mathbb{A}^2$ is *not* a two-form valued in $\mathrm{End}(\mathcal{E})$ in general. It has pieces in every even form-degree, the lowest of which is the endomorphism $\mathbb{A}_{[0]}^2 \in \Gamma(\mathrm{End}^{\mathrm{even}}(\mathcal{E}))$.
- The supertrace $\mathrm{Str}$ vanishes on graded commutators, not on ordinary commutators. This vanishing is what makes the Chern character of $\mathbb{A}$ closed: $d\,\mathrm{Str}(e^{-\mathbb{A}^2}) = \mathrm{Str}([\mathbb{A}, e^{-\mathbb{A}^2}]) = 0$ as a graded commutator inside $\Omega^*(B, \mathrm{End}(\mathcal{E}))$.

**Definition (Bismut superconnection of a family of Dirac operators).** Let $\pi : M \to B$ be a smooth fibration of smooth manifolds with closed fibres $M_b = \pi^{-1}(b)$, equipped with a vertical Riemannian metric and a horizontal complement (a connection on the fibration). Let $E \to M$ be a Clifford module bundle with metric-compatible Clifford-connection $\nabla^E$. The infinite-dimensional bundle
$$
\mathcal{E} := \pi_* E
$$
over $B$ has fibre $\mathcal{E}_b = \Gamma(M_b, E|_{M_b})$ — the space of sections of $E$ along the fibre — and is $\mathbb{Z}/2$-graded by the chirality grading $E = E^+ \oplus E^-$ of the Clifford module.

The **Bismut superconnection** is, for each $t > 0$, the superconnection
$$
\mathbb{A}_t = \nabla^{\mathcal{E}} + \sqrt{t}\, D - \frac{1}{4\sqrt{t}}\, c(T),
$$
where $\nabla^{\mathcal{E}}$ is the connection on $\pi_* E$ induced by $\nabla^E$ and the horizontal lift, $D$ is the family of fibrewise Dirac operators, $c$ is fibrewise Clifford multiplication, and $T \in \Omega^2(B, \mathrm{vert})$ is the curvature of the fibration (a vertical-vector-valued two-form on $B$) [ref: TODO_REF Bismut 1986; ref: TODO_REF Berline-Getzler-Vergne Ch. 10].

The first two pieces $\nabla^{\mathcal{E}} + \sqrt{t}\, D$ encode Quillen's $\mathbb{A}_{[1]} + \mathbb{A}_{[0]}$ data after the scaling $D \to \sqrt{t}\, D$. The third piece $-\frac{1}{4\sqrt{t}}\, c(T)$ is the form-degree-two correction needed when the fibration has non-zero curvature; it ensures the curvature $\mathbb{A}_t^2$ has the right small-$t$ behaviour. This third piece is the genuinely new term that Bismut's analysis demanded: a pure connection $\nabla^{\mathcal{E}} + \sqrt{t}\, D$ does not have the correct local index density in the $t \to 0^+$ limit unless $T = 0$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Bismut family-index theorem; Bismut 1986).** *Let $\pi : M \to B$ be a smooth fibration with closed even-dimensional fibres, equipped with a vertical Riemannian metric, a horizontal connection, and a family $D = \{D_b\}$ of Dirac operators on the Clifford-module bundle $E \to M$. The Bismut superconnection $\mathbb{A}_t$ satisfies:*

*(i)* *The differential form $\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t) = \mathrm{Str}(e^{-\mathbb{A}_t^2})$ is closed in $\Omega^{\mathrm{even}}(B)$ for every $t > 0$, and its cohomology class in $H^{\mathrm{even}}(B; \mathbb{R})$ is independent of $t$.*

*(ii)* *The cohomology class equals the Chern character of the analytic family index:*
$$
[\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t)] = \mathrm{ch}(\mathrm{Ind}(D)) \in H^{\mathrm{even}}(B; \mathbb{R}).
$$

*(iii)* *In the limit $t \to 0^+$, the form converges to the local index density along the fibre,*
$$
\lim_{t \to 0^+} \mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t) = \int_{M/B} \widehat A(R^{M/B}) \wedge \mathrm{ch}(F^{E/\!\mathcal{S}}),
$$
*where $R^{M/B}$ is the curvature of the vertical Levi-Civita connection, $\widehat A$ is the $\widehat A$-genus form, and $\mathrm{ch}(F^{E/\!\mathcal{S}})$ is the twisting Chern character of $E$ relative to the spinor bundle.*

*In the limit $t \to +\infty$, the form converges to the Chern character of the index bundle computed directly from the analytic index.*

**Proof.** The argument proceeds in three steps: closedness, $t$-independence, and the two limits.

*Step 1: closedness.* The supertrace $\mathrm{Str} : \Omega^*(B, \mathrm{End}(\mathcal{E})) \to \Omega^*(B)$ is $\Omega^*(B)$-linear and vanishes on graded commutators. The Bianchi identity gives $[\mathbb{A}_t, \mathbb{A}_t^2] = 0$, so $[\mathbb{A}_t, e^{-\mathbb{A}_t^2}] = 0$ as well (the heat kernel is computed by a power series in $\mathbb{A}_t^2$ that commutes with $\mathbb{A}_t$). Hence
$$
d\,\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t) = d\,\mathrm{Str}(e^{-\mathbb{A}_t^2}) = \mathrm{Str}([\mathbb{A}_t, e^{-\mathbb{A}_t^2}]) = 0,
$$
using that $d$ acts on $\mathrm{Str}(X)$ as $\mathrm{Str}([\mathbb{A}_t, X])$ for any operator $X$ commuting up to graded sign with the superconnection [ref: TODO_REF Quillen 1985].

*Step 2: $t$-independence.* Differentiate with respect to $t$:
$$
\frac{d}{dt}\, \mathrm{Str}(e^{-\mathbb{A}_t^2}) = -\mathrm{Str}\!\left(\frac{d\mathbb{A}_t^2}{dt}\, e^{-\mathbb{A}_t^2}\right).
$$
By a direct computation (Quillen's transgression lemma), $\frac{d\mathbb{A}_t^2}{dt}$ is a graded commutator in $\mathbb{A}_t$ and a one-parameter family of operators; the supertrace of this commutator is exact (a $d$-exact form on $B$). Thus the cohomology class of $\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t)$ is independent of $t$, although the form itself depends on $t$. The transgression form
$$
\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t) - \mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_s) = d \int_s^t \mathrm{Str}\!\left(\frac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}\, e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}\right) du
$$
makes the exactness explicit and is the origin of Bismut-Cheeger eta-forms [ref: TODO_REF Bismut-Cheeger 1989].

*Step 3: $t \to +\infty$ limit.* As $t \to +\infty$, the rescaled Dirac operator $\sqrt{t}\, D$ dominates. By the spectral theorem applied fibrewise, $e^{-tD^2}$ projects onto the kernel of $D$ (a finite-dimensional graded vector bundle over $B$, well-defined assuming the kernel has locally constant dimension; otherwise one stabilises). The connection $\nabla^{\mathcal{E}}$ restricts to a connection on this index bundle, and the cross-terms involving $c(T)/\sqrt{t}$ vanish in the limit. The form $\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t)$ converges to the Chern character of the index bundle equipped with its induced connection — exactly $\mathrm{ch}(\mathrm{Ind}(D))$ at the level of forms.

*Step 4: $t \to 0^+$ limit.* This is the technical core of Bismut's paper. The fibrewise Getzler rescaling, refined to handle the form-degree-two piece $c(T)/\sqrt{t}$, identifies the limit of $\mathrm{Str}(e^{-\mathbb{A}_t^2})$ as the fibre integral of the local index density. Concretely, choose normal coordinates along the fibre at each point of $B$, rescale the Clifford action $c(e_i) \to t^{-1/2} c(e_i)$ as in the single-operator Getzler proof, and observe that the form-degree-two correction $c(T)/\sqrt{t}$ contributes precisely the curvature term needed to produce the full $\widehat A$ genus form involving $R^{M/B}$ (not just the symbol piece). The resulting form is
$$
\lim_{t \to 0^+} \mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t) = \pi_* \big( \widehat A(R^{M/B}) \wedge \mathrm{ch}(F^{E/\!\mathcal{S}}) \big),
$$
where $\pi_*$ is the fibre integration $\Omega^*(M) \to \Omega^{*-\dim M_b}(B)$.

Combining: by $t$-independence of the cohomology class together with the two limits,
$$
\mathrm{ch}(\mathrm{Ind}(D)) = \big[ \pi_* (\widehat A(R^{M/B}) \wedge \mathrm{ch}(F^{E/\!\mathcal{S}})) \big] \in H^{\mathrm{even}}(B; \mathbb{R}).
$$
This is the cohomological family-index theorem of Atiyah-Singer 1971 IV, proved by heat-kernel localisation [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah-Singer 1971 IV; ref: TODO_REF Berline-Getzler-Vergne Ch. 10]. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Bismut superconnection builds toward the entire modern family-index machinery and identifies analysis with topology at the family level. The foundational reason it works is exactly that the supertrace kills graded commutators, so the heat-kernel form is closed for every $t$, and the cohomology class is $t$-independent — precisely the same McKean-Singer mechanism that drove the single-operator heat-kernel proof in [03.09.20]. This is exactly the parametric analogue: a single object that interpolates between the analytic index at $t = +\infty$ and the topological density at $t = 0^+$, identifying $\mathrm{ch}(\mathrm{Ind}(D))$ with the fibre integral of the local index density. The central insight is that the form-degree-two correction $c(T)/\sqrt{t}$ is the genuinely new ingredient that converts a naïve connection-plus-operator combination into an object whose square has the correct Getzler-rescaled limit. Putting these together, the superconnection identifies family heat-kernel analysis with family characteristic-class topology, and the transgression form that records the $t$-dependence appears again in [03.09.21] (family / equivariant index) as the higher refinement to Bismut-Cheeger eta-forms. The bridge is the recognition that Quillen's purely algebraic 1985 framework, originally a clean repackaging of the Chern-Weil construction for graded bundles, was exactly the right tool for the family analysis Bismut needed in 1986 — and the same construction generalises to equivariant family theory, foliations, and non-commutative geometry, each time producing the appropriate Chern-character form.

When the base $B$ is a single point and $\mathcal{E}$ is the spinor bundle $S^+ \oplus S^-$ of $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$, what differential form on $B$ is the superconnection Chern character $\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t)$ for $\mathbb{A}_t = \sqrt{t}\, \slashed{D}$?

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify that the form-degree-zero piece of $\mathbb{A}_t^2 = (\nabla^{\mathcal{E}} + \sqrt{t}\, D - \frac{1}{4\sqrt{t}} c(T))^2$ is $t\, D^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

\mathbb{A}_t^2 = (\nabla^{\mathcal{E}})^2 + \sqrt{t} \{ \nabla^{\mathcal{E}}, D \} + t D^2 - \frac{1}{4\sqrt{t}} \{ \nabla^{\mathcal{E}}, c(T) \} - \frac{1}{4} \{D, c(T)\} + \frac{1}{16 t} c(T)^2.

The pieces by form-degree on $B$: $(\nabla^{\mathcal{E}})^2$ is form-degree two (the connection curvature); $\sqrt{t} \{\nabla^{\mathcal{E}}, D\}$ is form-degree one; $t D^2$ is form-degree zero; the $c(T)$ pieces are form-degrees $\geq 2$. The form-degree-zero contribution is exactly $t D^2$, the fibrewise Dirac-squared operator scaled by $t$. Rubric: full credit for the expansion plus the form-degree bookkeeping.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Why does the supertrace vanish on graded commutators in $\Omega^*(B, \mathrm{End}(\mathcal{E}))$, and how does this fact prove that $\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t)$ is closed on $B$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The supertrace of $[T, S]_{\mathrm{gr}} = TS - (-1)^{|T||S|} ST$ for graded operators is zero by the cyclic property of trace plus the graded sign rule. Then use $d \cdot \mathrm{Str}(X) = \mathrm{Str}([\mathbb{A}, X]_{\mathrm{gr}})$ for $X$ commuting with $\mathbb{A}^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For homogeneous operators $T$ and $S$ on a graded space, $\mathrm{Tr}(TS) = (-1)^{|T||S|} \mathrm{Tr}(ST)$ by reordering matrix elements with sign. The graded commutator is $[T, S]_{\mathrm{gr}} = TS - (-1)^{|T||S|} ST$, and its supertrace vanishes: $\mathrm{Str}(TS) - (-1)^{|T||S|} \mathrm{Str}(ST) = 0$. Now the de Rham differential $d$ on $\mathrm{Str}(e^{-\mathbb{A}_t^2})$ acts as $\mathrm{Str}([\mathbb{A}_t, e^{-\mathbb{A}_t^2}]_{\mathrm{gr}})$, because $\mathbb{A}_t$ is the unique odd derivation extending $d$ to bundle-valued forms. The Bianchi identity $[\mathbb{A}_t, \mathbb{A}_t^2]_{\mathrm{gr}} = 0$ propagates to $[\mathbb{A}_t, e^{-\mathbb{A}_t^2}]_{\mathrm{gr}} = 0$. Hence $d\,\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t) = 0$. Rubric: full credit for the graded-cyclic argument plus the Bianchi propagation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

State the transgression form $\eta(s, t) = \int_s^t \mathrm{Str}\!\left(\frac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}\, e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}\right) du$ that witnesses the $t$-independence of $[\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t)]$, and explain why $\eta(s, t)$ has odd total degree on $B$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\frac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}$ is even total parity (because $\mathbb{A}_u$ is odd and the parameter $u$ does not flip parity). The supertrace then preserves parity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Step 2 of the main theorem's proof, $\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t) - \mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_s) = d\, \eta(s, t)$ with $\eta(s, t) = \int_s^t \mathrm{Str}\!\left(\frac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}\, e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}\right) du$. The supertrace produces forms of even total parity from even-parity operators (and odd from odd). Here $\frac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}$ is the derivative of an odd-parity operator with respect to a parity-preserving parameter, so $\frac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}$ has odd total parity; multiplied by the even operator $e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}$, the product is odd, and the supertrace of an odd operator on $\Omega^*(B, \mathrm{End}(\mathcal{E}))$ produces an odd-degree form on $B$. The transgression form $\eta(s, t)$ thus lives in $\Omega^{\mathrm{odd}}(B)$, as it must to make $d\eta$ even-degree. Rubric: full credit for the parity bookkeeping.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Quillen's transgression and the Chern-Simons form.** Quillen's 1985 paper introduced the **transgression form** on the space of superconnections: for a one-parameter family $\mathbb{A}_t$, the transgression $\eta(s, t) = \int_s^t \mathrm{Str}\!\left(\frac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}\, e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}\right) du$ satisfies $d\eta(s, t) = \mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t) - \mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_s)$. The transgression form is the Chern-Simons / secondary-characteristic-class structure attached to a path between two superconnections, and it is the algebraic precursor of the Bismut-Cheeger eta-form. The same construction, applied to a family of ordinary connections on an ordinary vector bundle, recovers the classical Chern-Simons form [ref: TODO_REF Quillen 1985].

**Bismut-Cheeger eta-form and adiabatic limits.** Bismut-Cheeger 1989 *Eta invariants and their adiabatic limits* (J. AMS 2) extended the Bismut superconnection from closed-fibre fibrations to fibrations with boundary. The eta-form $\widetilde\eta(\mathbb{A}_t)$ is a closed odd-degree form on $B$ refining the Atiyah-Patodi-Singer eta-invariant. The Bismut-Cheeger adiabatic-limit theorem states that for a fibration with boundary, the family-index formula acquires the eta-form as a correction term: $\mathrm{ch}(\mathrm{Ind}(D)) = \int_{M/B} \widehat A \wedge \mathrm{ch}(E) - \widetilde\eta$. This is the family generalisation of the APS index theorem and underlies all modern work on geometric K-homology classes for manifolds with boundary [ref: TODO_REF Bismut-Cheeger 1989].

**Mathai-Quillen Thom-class representative.** Mathai-Quillen 1986 *Superconnections, Thom classes, and equivariant differential forms* (Topology 25) used the superconnection framework to give an explicit Gaussian-decay differential-form representative of the Thom class of a Euclidean vector bundle. The Mathai-Quillen Thom form is the Chern character of a specific superconnection on the pullback bundle over the total space, with the form-degree-zero piece $-|x|^2$ providing the Gaussian decay. The construction is the natural geometric model for the equivariant Thom isomorphism and is the foundation of Mathai-Quillen-style localisation arguments in equivariant cohomology [ref: TODO_REF Mathai-Quillen 1986].

**Bismut superconnection in non-commutative geometry.** Connes' framework of non-commutative differential geometry includes a superconnection construction for spectral triples $(\mathcal{A}, \mathcal{H}, D)$. Bismut-style transgression of the heat-kernel form along a family of spectral triples produces the **local cyclic cocycle** of Connes-Moscovici, the non-commutative analogue of the family Chern character. The same algebraic identities — supertrace vanishes on graded commutators, Bianchi identity, exact transgression — hold in the cyclic-cohomology setting, with the McKean-Singer formula replaced by the Connes-Moscovici local index formula.

**Higher torsion forms (Bismut-Lott).** Bismut-Lott 1995 *Flat vector bundles, direct images and higher real analytic torsion* (J. AMS 8) extended the superconnection construction to flat vector bundles, producing the **higher analytic torsion form** $\mathcal{T}(\mathbb{A}_t)$ on $B$. The torsion form refines the Ray-Singer analytic torsion to families: its degree-zero piece recovers the Ray-Singer torsion of each fibre, and its higher-degree pieces are genuine family invariants. Bismut-Lott torsion is the analytic counterpart of the Igusa-Klein higher topological torsion in algebraic K-theory.

**Equivariant Bismut superconnection.** When a compact Lie group $G$ acts on the fibration $\pi : M \to B$ preserving all the data, the Bismut superconnection becomes equivariant: $\mathbb{A}_t$ is built from a $G$-invariant vertical metric, a $G$-equivariant connection, and the $G$-equivariant family Dirac operator. The equivariant heat-kernel form lives in the equivariant cohomology $H^*_G(B; \mathbb{R})$, and the small-$t$ limit gives the equivariant family-index formula. This is the route through which Bismut's framework integrates with the Atiyah-Bott-Berline-Vergne localisation theorem.

**Loop-space heuristic.** Atiyah's 1985 *Circular symmetry and stationary-phase approximation* observed that the Bismut superconnection on the loop space $LM$ of a spin manifold $M$ formally produces the $\widehat A$ genus via a stationary-phase argument around constant loops. The argument is heuristic — the loop space is infinite-dimensional — but it has been made rigorous in pieces by Stolz-Teichner and by Pflaum-Posthuma-Tang. The loop-space heuristic explains *why* the $\widehat A$-genus is the small-$t$ limit of the heat kernel: it is the stationary-phase integral over $LM$.

**Synthesis.** The Bismut superconnection identifies family analysis with family topology in the same way that the heat-kernel proof of [03.09.20] identifies single-operator analysis with single-operator topology. The foundational reason it works is exactly that supertraces kill graded commutators, so the Chern-character form is closed for every $t$, and the cohomology class is $t$-independent under the Bianchi-identity-induced transgression. The central insight is that Quillen's purely algebraic 1985 framework — superconnections as odd derivations on graded bundle-valued forms — was the precise tool needed for the parametric analysis Bismut required in 1986; the form-degree-two correction $c(T)/\sqrt{t}$ is the genuinely new ingredient that makes the small-$t$ Getzler rescaling produce the full local index density rather than just its leading piece. Putting these together, the superconnection identifies $\mathrm{ch}(\mathrm{Ind}(D))$ with the fibre integral of $\widehat A \wedge \mathrm{ch}(F^{E/\!\mathcal{S}})$, generalising Atiyah-Singer to families and producing along the way the Bismut-Cheeger eta-form, the Mathai-Quillen Thom representative, the Bismut-Lott analytic torsion form, and the Connes-Moscovici local cyclic cocycle. This same construction appears again in [03.09.21] (family / equivariant / Lefschetz index) where the equivariant refinement runs in parallel, and is dual to the K-theoretic family-index formula whose Chern character it computes — the bridge between the two pictures is precisely the Bismut superconnection.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Quillen's algebraic identity).** *Let $\mathbb{A}$ be a superconnection on a graded vector bundle $\mathcal{E}$ over a smooth manifold $B$. For any one-parameter family of superconnections $\mathbb{A}_u$ depending smoothly on $u \in (0, \infty)$,*
$$
\frac{d}{du}\, \mathrm{Str}(e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}) = -d\, \mathrm{Str}\!\left(\frac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}\, e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}\right).
$$

**Proof.** Differentiate using the Duhamel formula
$$
\frac{d}{du}\, e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2} = -\int_0^1 e^{-s \mathbb{A}_u^2}\, \frac{d\mathbb{A}_u^2}{du}\, e^{-(1-s)\mathbb{A}_u^2}\, ds.
$$
Take the supertrace and use cyclicity (with graded sign) to combine the two exponentials into one $e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}$ with $\frac{d\mathbb{A}_u^2}{du}$ on the right:
$$
\frac{d}{du}\, \mathrm{Str}(e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}) = -\mathrm{Str}\!\left(\frac{d\mathbb{A}_u^2}{du}\, e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}\right).
$$
Now compute $\frac{d\mathbb{A}_u^2}{du} = \{\mathbb{A}_u, \frac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}\}$ (anticommutator, since both factors are odd). Insert into the supertrace:
$$
\mathrm{Str}\!\left(\{\mathbb{A}_u, \tfrac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}\}\, e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}\right) = \mathrm{Str}\!\left([\mathbb{A}_u, \tfrac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}\, e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}]_{\mathrm{gr}}\right) + \mathrm{Str}\!\left(\tfrac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}\, [\mathbb{A}_u, e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}]_{\mathrm{gr}}\right).
$$
The second graded commutator vanishes because $\mathbb{A}_u$ commutes with $\mathbb{A}_u^2$ (Bianchi), hence with its power series $e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2}$. The first term is the supertrace of a graded commutator, which equals $d\, \mathrm{Str}(\tfrac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}\, e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2})$ by the identity $d \cdot \mathrm{Str}(X) = \mathrm{Str}([\mathbb{A}_u, X]_{\mathrm{gr}})$ for $\Omega^*(B, \mathrm{End}(\mathcal{E}))$-valued operators. Combining gives the claim. $\square$

**Proposition (closedness of Chern character).** *For any superconnection $\mathbb{A}$ on $\mathcal{E}$, the form $\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}) = \mathrm{Str}(e^{-\mathbb{A}^2}) \in \Omega^{\mathrm{even}}(B)$ is closed.*

**Proof.** Apply the identity $d\, \mathrm{Str}(X) = \mathrm{Str}([\mathbb{A}, X]_{\mathrm{gr}})$ with $X = e^{-\mathbb{A}^2}$. The graded commutator $[\mathbb{A}, e^{-\mathbb{A}^2}]_{\mathrm{gr}}$ vanishes because $\mathbb{A}$ commutes with $\mathbb{A}^2$ (Bianchi), hence with its analytic continuation $e^{-\mathbb{A}^2}$. Therefore $d\,\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}) = 0$. The form has even total degree because $e^{-\mathbb{A}^2}$ has even total parity (the operator $\mathbb{A}^2$ is even) and the supertrace of an even operator on a graded space is an even-degree form. $\square$

**Proposition ($t$-independence of cohomology class).** *For a one-parameter family $\mathbb{A}_t$ of superconnections, the cohomology class $[\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t)] \in H^{\mathrm{even}}(B; \mathbb{R})$ is independent of $t$.*

**Proof.** By Quillen's algebraic identity, $\mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t) - \mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_s) = d \int_s^t \mathrm{Str}(\frac{d\mathbb{A}_u}{du}\, e^{-\mathbb{A}_u^2})\, du$, so the difference is exact, and the classes in de Rham cohomology agree. $\square$

**Proposition (large-$t$ limit equals analytic index Chern character).** *Assume the kernel and cokernel of $D_b$ have locally constant dimensions, so $\mathrm{Ind}(D) = [\ker D] - [\mathrm{coker}\, D]$ is a genuine graded vector bundle on $B$. Then $\lim_{t \to +\infty} \mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t) = \mathrm{ch}(\mathrm{Ind}(D))$ in $\Omega^*(B)$, where the right-hand side uses the connection on the index bundle induced from $\nabla^{\mathcal{E}}$.*

**Proof.** As $t \to +\infty$, the operator $\sqrt{t}\, D$ dominates the Bismut superconnection, so $e^{-\mathbb{A}_t^2} \to P_{\ker} e^{-(\nabla^{\mathrm{Ind}})^2} P_{\ker}$ where $P_{\ker}$ is the fibrewise orthogonal projection onto $\ker D$ on each fibre and $\nabla^{\mathrm{Ind}}$ is the induced connection. The cross-terms with $c(T)/\sqrt{t}$ vanish as $t \to +\infty$. Taking the supertrace gives $\mathrm{Str}(P_{\ker} e^{-(\nabla^{\mathrm{Ind}})^2} P_{\ker}) = \mathrm{Str}(e^{-(\nabla^{\mathrm{Ind}})^2}|_{\mathrm{Ind}}) = \mathrm{ch}(\mathrm{Ind}(D))$ as a Chern-Weil form. The rigorous version of this argument requires uniform spectral-gap estimates; see Berline-Getzler-Vergne Ch. 9 §3 [ref: TODO_REF Berline-Getzler-Vergne]. $\square$

**Proposition (small-$t$ limit equals local index density).** *Assume the fibration has even-dimensional fibres and $E$ is a Clifford module. Then*
$$
\lim_{t \to 0^+} \mathrm{ch}(\mathbb{A}_t) = \int_{M/B} \widehat A(R^{M/B}) \wedge \mathrm{ch}(F^{E/\!\mathcal{S}}) \quad \text{in} \quad \Omega^*(B).
$$

**Proof sketch (Getzler rescaling for the Bismut superconnection).** Fix a point $b \in B$ and a vertical normal coordinate system around a fibre point. Rescale the Clifford generators $c(e_i) \to t^{-1/2} c(e_i)$ as in the single-operator Getzler proof [03.09.20]. Under this rescaling:

- The form-degree-zero piece $t D^2$ becomes the harmonic-oscillator operator $-\sum \partial_i^2 + \sum R_{ij} x^i \wedge x^j / 4$ on $\mathbb{R}^{\dim M_b}$, whose heat kernel is given by Mehler's formula.
- The form-degree-two piece $-\frac{1}{4\sqrt{t}}\, c(T)$, after the Clifford rescaling and the $\sqrt{t}$ factor, contributes precisely the $\widehat A$-curvature term $R^{M/B}$ in the form integrand.
- The cross-terms involving $\sqrt{t} \{\nabla^{\mathcal{E}}, D\}$ contribute the twisting Chern character $\mathrm{ch}(F^{E/\!\mathcal{S}})$ after rescaling.

Combining via Mehler's formula and the fibre integration $\pi_*$ gives the limit. The genuinely new ingredient compared to the single-operator Getzler proof is the form-degree-two correction $c(T)/\sqrt{t}$: without it, the small-$t$ limit produces only the leading-order $\widehat A$ form, missing the $R^{M/B}$ corrections from horizontal curvature. The full argument occupies Bismut 1986 §§3-5 and Berline-Getzler-Vergne Ch. 10 [ref: TODO_REF Bismut 1986; ref: TODO_REF Berline-Getzler-Vergne]. $\square$

**Proposition (cohomological family-index theorem).** *Combining the previous propositions,*
$$
\mathrm{ch}(\mathrm{Ind}(D)) = \pi_* \big( \widehat A(R^{M/B}) \wedge \mathrm{ch}(F^{E/\!\mathcal{S}}) \big) \in H^{\mathrm{even}}(B; \mathbb{R}).
$$

**Proof.** By $t$-independence of the cohomology class and the two limits computed above. This is the cohomological Atiyah-Singer family-index theorem of 1971 IV, proved here by heat-kernel localisation rather than K-theoretic pushforward. $\square$

**Proposition (Bianchi identity).** *For any superconnection $\mathbb{A}$, the curvature $\mathbb{A}^2$ satisfies $[\mathbb{A}, \mathbb{A}^2]_{\mathrm{gr}} = 0$.*

**Proof.** The graded commutator $[\mathbb{A}, \mathbb{A}^2]_{\mathrm{gr}} = \mathbb{A} \mathbb{A}^2 - (-1)^{1 \cdot 0} \mathbb{A}^2 \mathbb{A} = \mathbb{A}^3 - \mathbb{A}^3 = 0$. (The parity of $\mathbb{A}$ is one and of $\mathbb{A}^2$ is zero, so the graded sign is $(-1)^{1\cdot 0} = 1$, i.e., this is an ordinary commutator that vanishes by associativity.) $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Atiyah-Singer index theorem [03.09.10].** The Bismut superconnection produces the cohomological family-index theorem of Atiyah-Singer 1971 IV by heat-kernel localisation. The single-point base case reduces to the original Atiyah-Singer formula for one Dirac operator: $\mathbb{A}_t = \sqrt{t}\, D$, and the heat-kernel form on a one-point base is the integer index. The family version refines this from a single integer to a Chern character class in $H^{\mathrm{even}}(B; \mathbb{R})$.

- **Heat-kernel proof of Atiyah-Singer [03.09.20].** The Bismut superconnection is the family analogue of the single-operator heat-kernel proof. The same McKean-Singer mechanism — supertrace vanishes on graded commutators, so the heat-kernel form is closed and $t$-independent in cohomology — drives both arguments. The Getzler rescaling is the same; the family case requires the form-degree-two correction $c(T)/\sqrt{t}$ to account for fibration curvature.

- **Family, equivariant, and Lefschetz index theorems [03.09.21].** The Bismut superconnection is the analytical machinery that proves the family-index theorem stated in 03.09.21. The equivariant version of the Bismut superconnection (with a $G$-invariant connection on the fibration) handles the equivariant family-index theorem, and the transgression form leads to the equivariant Bismut-Cheeger eta-form. The Lefschetz fixed-point formula reappears in the small-$t$ analysis of the equivariant superconnection.

- **Equivariant K-theory $K_G(X)$ and $R(G)$ [03.08.10].** The family Chern character lifts to a map $K^0(B) \to H^{\mathrm{even}}(B; \mathbb{R})$ that the Bismut superconnection computes at the level of differential forms. The equivariant refinement of the family-index theorem lives in equivariant K-theory $K^0_G(B)$, and the equivariant Bismut superconnection computes its equivariant Chern character. The connection to the representation ring $R(G) = K^0_G(\mathrm{pt})$ is via the product fibration case.

- **Atiyah-Hirzebruch spectral sequence [03.13.04].** The family Chern character produced by the Bismut superconnection factors through the Atiyah-Hirzebruch spectral sequence for $K^0(B) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \cong H^{\mathrm{even}}(B; \mathbb{Q})$. The superconnection identifies the analytic family index with its cohomological image; the AHSS provides the integral refinement and the differentials measuring obstructions to the rational identification.

- **Chern-Weil homomorphism [03.06.06].** The Bismut superconnection generalises Chern-Weil from ordinary connections to graded superconnections. Quillen's 1985 paper is the systematic extension: the Chern-Weil polynomial in the curvature of an ordinary connection becomes the supertrace of the heat kernel of a superconnection, and the same closedness / class-independence / transgression structure holds. The Chern-Simons form on the space of ordinary connections is the form-degree-one piece of the superconnection transgression.

- **Generalised Dirac bundle [03.09.14].** The Bismut superconnection requires the fibrewise operator to be a generalised Dirac operator on a Clifford-module bundle. The Bochner-Weitzenböck identity for the family Dirac operator $D^2 = (\nabla^{\mathcal{E}})^* \nabla^{\mathcal{E}} + \mathcal{R}$ is the family analogue of the single-operator identity, and it underlies the heat-kernel decay required for the large-$t$ limit.

- **Principal bundle connection [03.05.07].** The horizontal connection on the fibration $\pi : M \to B$ enters the Bismut superconnection through the induced connection $\nabla^{\mathcal{E}}$ on $\pi_* E$ and through the curvature $T \in \Omega^2(B, \mathrm{vert})$ that appears in the third piece of $\mathbb{A}_t$. The Bismut superconnection therefore depends on the choice of horizontal complement; different choices give different forms but the same cohomology class.

- **Eta invariant and the Atiyah-Patodi-Singer index theorem [03.09.24].** The Bismut-Cheeger family eta-form $\widehat\eta \in \Omega^*(B)$ is the transgression of the superconnection Chern character along the half-infinite cylinder $[0, \infty) \times \partial X$ in a family of manifolds with boundary; it is the family analogue of the eta invariant. The degree-zero piece of $\widehat\eta$ recovers the single-operator eta invariant of APS, and the family APS theorem expresses the difference between bulk and boundary contributions as the de Rham class of $\mathrm{ch}(\mathrm{Ind}\,D) - \int_{X/B} \widehat A(TX/B)\,\mathrm{ch}(\mathcal{E}/S) = -\widehat\eta$ in $H^{\mathrm{even}}(B; \mathbb{R})$. The same Getzler rescaling and McKean-Singer mechanism that make the closed-fibre superconnection Chern character well-defined extend to manifolds with cylindrical ends, with the eta-form arising precisely as the boundary defect — the family analogue of the spectral-asymmetry boundary correction. Bismut-Freed 1986 identified the holonomy of the determinant line bundle of a family of Dirac operators with the family eta invariant, giving the structural reason the superconnection framework subsumes APS as its boundary-term refinement.

- **Kirillov character formula via the equivariant index [03.09.25].** The equivariant Bismut superconnection applied to the homogeneous fibration $G/T \to \mathrm{pt}$ (with $G$-action by left translation) produces, in the small-$t$ limit, an equivariant differential form on $\mathfrak{g}$ whose evaluation at $X \in \mathfrak{g}$ is the Kirillov character at $g = e^X$. Specifically, the equivariant Chern character of the pushforward $\pi_* \mathcal{L}_\lambda$ along $\pi : G/T \to \mathrm{pt}$ — computed by Bismut superconnection at the equivariant level — equals the orbit Fourier transform $\int_{\mathcal{O}_\lambda} e^{i\langle \xi, X\rangle} \, d\mu(\xi)$ times the Duflo Jacobian $j(X)^{1/2}$. The Kirillov formula is therefore the small-time / orbit-localised value of the equivariant Bismut superconnection, and the proof in Berline-Getzler-Vergne Ch. 8 runs the rescaling argument equivariantly. The family eta-form construction extends Kirillov to families of homogeneous spaces, refining the orbit-Fourier identity to a cohomological identity on the parameter space.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

**Quillen's 1985 paper.** Daniel Quillen's *Superconnections and the Chern character* (*Topology* 24, 89-95) introduced the superconnection framework as a clean generalisation of Chern-Weil theory to graded vector bundles. Quillen's motivation was algebraic: the Chern character of a graded bundle $\mathcal{E} = \mathcal{E}^+ \oplus \mathcal{E}^-$ is naturally a difference $\mathrm{ch}(\mathcal{E}^+) - \mathrm{ch}(\mathcal{E}^-)$, and an ordinary connection on each piece separately gives this difference but in an unsatisfactory way — there is no single object computing the supertrace heat-kernel form directly. Quillen's superconnection is the single object: an odd-parity derivation on the bundle-valued de Rham complex whose curvature $\mathbb{A}^2$ has all even form-degrees, and whose Chern character $\mathrm{Str}(e^{-\mathbb{A}^2})$ is closed for purely algebraic reasons. Quillen explicitly noted that the framework was designed for applications to families of elliptic operators but did not develop those applications himself — he left that work for Bismut [ref: TODO_REF Quillen 1985].

**Bismut's 1986 paper.** Jean-Michel Bismut's *The Atiyah-Singer index theorem for families of Dirac operators: two heat-equation proofs* (*Invent. Math.* 83, 91-151) was the first heat-kernel proof of the Atiyah-Singer family-index theorem, replacing the original K-theoretic argument of Atiyah-Singer 1971 IV with an analytical one in the spirit of Atiyah-Bott-Patodi 1973 for the single-operator case. Bismut introduced what is now called the Bismut superconnection $\mathbb{A}_t = \nabla^{\mathcal{E}} + \sqrt{t}\, D - \frac{1}{4\sqrt{t}}\, c(T)$ — a specific superconnection (in Quillen's sense) attached to a fibration of spin manifolds. The genuinely new term was the form-degree-two correction $c(T)/\sqrt{t}$, which was needed to make the small-$t$ Getzler rescaling produce the full local index density rather than only its leading piece. Bismut's two proofs in the paper used different routes to the same conclusion: one a direct probabilistic argument via the Brownian-motion path-integral, the other an analytical argument via heat-kernel asymptotics. The framework has dominated family-index theory ever since [ref: TODO_REF Bismut 1986].

**Mathai-Quillen, Bismut-Cheeger, Bismut-Lott.** The superconnection framework rapidly proved more general than its initial application. Varghese Mathai and Quillen 1986 *Superconnections, Thom classes, and equivariant differential forms* (*Topology* 25, 85-110) used the framework to construct Gaussian Thom-class representatives and equivariant differential forms. Bismut and Jeff Cheeger 1989 *Eta invariants and their adiabatic limits* (*J. Amer. Math. Soc.* 2, 33-70) introduced the **eta-form** $\widetilde\eta(\mathbb{A}_t)$ as the family analogue of the Atiyah-Patodi-Singer eta-invariant, proving the adiabatic-limit formula that governs family-index theorems for fibrations with boundary. Bismut and John Lott 1995 *Flat vector bundles, direct images and higher real analytic torsion* (*J. Amer. Math. Soc.* 8, 291-363) constructed the **higher analytic torsion form** $\mathcal{T}(\mathbb{A}_t)$ for flat bundles, refining Ray-Singer torsion to families and connecting with Igusa-Klein higher topological torsion.

**Modern reach.** Nicole Berline, Ezra Getzler, and Michèle Vergne's 1992 monograph *Heat Kernels and Dirac Operators* (Grundlehren 298, Springer) presented the Bismut superconnection in its definitive textbook form, with Ch. 9 developing Quillen's framework and Ch. 10 the family-index theorem [ref: TODO_REF Berline-Getzler-Vergne]. The Bismut superconnection appears in Connes-Moscovici's local cyclic cocycle in non-commutative geometry, in Stolz-Teichner's geometric String orientation, in Pflaum-Posthuma-Tang's work on transverse index theory for foliations, and in the modern formulation of differential K-theory (Hopkins-Singer, Bunke-Schick). The superconnection construction has become the canonical analytical machinery for any family-index calculation, replacing the older K-theoretic pushforward arguments in most modern treatments.
