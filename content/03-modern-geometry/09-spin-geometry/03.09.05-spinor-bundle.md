## Intuition [Beginner]

A *spinor bundle* is the geometric object whose sections are spinor fields — the wave functions of fermions in physics, and the natural objects on which the Dirac operator acts in geometry.

Constructing it requires both a *spin structure* on the underlying manifold (which lifts the rotation group of frames to its double cover, the Spin group) and the *complex spin representation* of the Spin group on a vector space of spinors. The associated bundle is then built fibre-by-fibre by replacing each $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$-frame with the corresponding spinor space.

Why bother? Because spinors are physically real but cannot be defined directly from the manifold's tangent or cotangent bundle — a topological obstruction (the second Stiefel-Whitney class) forces the spinor bundle to live one level *above* the frame bundle, on a double cover. The Dirac equation, the Atiyah-Singer index theorem, and most of modern mathematical physics depend on this construction.

## Visual [Beginner]

A curved manifold with a spin structure (the double cover of its frame bundle) and, attached at every point, the complex spinor space — the fibre of the spinor bundle.

![A curved 2-surface with spin frames at each point, and complex spinor fibres attached above them.](/img/spinor-bundle-placeholder.svg)

A spinor field is a smooth choice of one spinor at each point — a section of the spinor bundle.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider a sphere $S^2$. It is orientable and has a unique spin structure (since $H^1(S^2; \mathbb{Z}/2) = 0$). The complex spin representation of $\mathrm{Spin}(2) \cong U(1)$ is two-dimensional (the Pauli spinor space). The spinor bundle is therefore a complex rank-2 vector bundle over $S^2$.

In this special low-dimensional case, the spinor bundle (denoted $\mathfrak{S}$ throughout this chapter, following Lawson-Michelsohn's Fraktur S) splits as $\mathfrak{S}^+ \oplus \mathfrak{S}^-$ — the *positive* and *negative* chirality components — with $\mathfrak{S}^\pm$ each a complex line bundle. By a calculation involving the spin structure on $S^2$, one finds $\mathfrak{S}^+ \cong \mathcal{O}(-1)$ and $\mathfrak{S}^- \cong \mathcal{O}(1)$ (the tautological and hyperplane line bundles).

Sections of $\mathfrak{S}^+$ are positive-chirality spinors; sections of $\mathfrak{S}^-$ are negative-chirality. The Dirac operator [03.09.08] sends $\mathfrak{S}^+$ to $\mathfrak{S}^-$ and vice versa, mixing chirality. On $S^2$, harmonic spinors (kernel of the Dirac operator) form a finite-dimensional space whose dimension equals the index from Atiyah-Singer.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $(M, g)$ be an oriented Riemannian manifold of dimension $n$ with a spin structure $\pi : P_{\mathrm{Spin}(n)} \to P_{\mathrm{SO}(n)}(M)$ [03.09.04]. Let $\sigma : \mathrm{Spin}(n) \to \mathrm{GL}(\Delta_n)$ be the complex spin representation, where $\Delta_n$ is the complex spinor space (of complex dimension $2^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}$).

The **spinor bundle** $\mathfrak{S} \to M$ is the associated complex vector bundle

$$
\mathfrak{S} := P_{\mathrm{Spin}(n)} \times_\sigma \Delta_n,
$$

with fibre $\mathfrak{S}_p = (P_{\mathrm{Spin}(n)})_p \times_\sigma \Delta_n$ at each point $p \in M$. As a complex vector bundle [03.05.08], it has rank $2^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}$. (Notation: throughout the spin-geometry chapter we adopt Lawson-Michelsohn's Fraktur S — $\mathfrak{S}$ — for the spinor bundle, reserving the italic $S$ for spheres and other ambient symbols.)

**Sections of $\mathfrak{S}$** are *spinor fields*: smooth maps $\psi : M \to \mathfrak{S}$ with $\pi_{\mathfrak{S}} \circ \psi = \mathrm{id}_M$.

**The Clifford bundle.** The fibrewise Clifford-bundle perspective of Lawson-Michelsohn (Definition II.3.1) packages Clifford multiplication into a globally defined bundle. Form the **Clifford bundle**
$$
\mathrm{Cl}(TM) := P_{\mathrm{SO}(n)}(M) \times_{O(n)} \mathrm{Cl}_n,
$$
a bundle of associative algebras whose fibre at $p$ is $\mathrm{Cl}(T_p M, g_p)$. The spinor bundle is then a $\mathrm{Cl}(TM)$-module bundle: for each $p$, the fibre $\mathfrak{S}_p$ is a left $\mathrm{Cl}(T_p M)$-module via the spin representation. The Clifford bundle perspective is what makes the Clifford action of differential forms on spinors coordinate-free at the bundle level.

**Clifford multiplication** is a bundle map

$$
c : T^\ast M \otimes \mathfrak{S} \to \mathfrak{S}, \qquad c(\xi \otimes \psi) := \xi \cdot \psi,
$$

induced fibrewise from the Clifford action $T_p^\ast M \otimes \Delta_n \to \Delta_n$ that comes with the spin representation [03.09.02]. It satisfies $c(\xi)^2 = -|\xi|^2_g \cdot \mathrm{id}_{\mathfrak{S}}$ — the Clifford relation lifted to the spinor bundle.

**Spinor connection.** The Levi-Civita connection on $TM$ induces a connection on $P_{\mathrm{SO}(n)}(M)$ [03.05.07], which lifts uniquely to a connection on $P_{\mathrm{Spin}(n)}$ (since $\mathrm{Spin}(n) \to \mathrm{SO}(n)$ is a local diffeomorphism). This induces a covariant derivative on the spinor bundle

$$
\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}} : \Gamma(\mathfrak{S}) \to \Gamma(T^\ast M \otimes \mathfrak{S}),
$$

the **spinor connection**.

**Even-dimensional chirality decomposition.** When $n = 2k$, the complex Clifford algebra has a *complex volume element* $\omega_\mathbb{C} := i^k e_1 e_2 \cdots e_n$ which acts on $\Delta_n$ with eigenvalues $\pm 1$. The spin representation splits as $\Delta_n = \Delta_n^+ \oplus \Delta_n^-$ into the $+1$ and $-1$ eigenspaces (the half-spin representations), each of complex dimension $2^{k-1}$. Correspondingly, the spinor bundle splits:

$$
\mathfrak{S} = \mathfrak{S}^+ \oplus \mathfrak{S}^-, \qquad \mathfrak{S}^\pm := P_{\mathrm{Spin}(n)} \times_{\sigma} \Delta_n^\pm.
$$

Clifford multiplication by a one-form anti-commutes with the chirality grading and exchanges $\mathfrak{S}^+$ with $\mathfrak{S}^-$.

**Hermitian structure.** The spin representation $\sigma$ on $\Delta_n$ admits a $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$-invariant Hermitian inner product (in fact, an essentially unique one up to scaling), inducing a Hermitian metric on $\mathfrak{S}$ for which $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$ is metric-compatible.

**Spinor inner products by signature mod 8.** On a real spin manifold of signature $(r,s)$ with $r-s \!\!\mod 8 = m$, the spin representation $\Delta_n$ carries a Clifford-invariant bilinear form whose type — Hermitian, symmetric, skew-Hermitian, or skew-symmetric — depends only on $m$. Lawson-Michelsohn's Theorem I.5.4 records the eight cases:

| $r-s \!\!\mod 8$ | Form on $\Delta_n$ | Charge conjugation $J$ | $J^2$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| $0$ | symmetric, real | real | $+1$ |
| $1$ | symmetric, real | real | $+1$ |
| $2$ | Hermitian | complex anti-linear | $-1$ |
| $3$ | skew-Hermitian | complex anti-linear | $-1$ |
| $4$ | skew-symmetric, quaternionic | quaternionic | $-1$ |
| $5$ | skew-symmetric, quaternionic | quaternionic | $-1$ |
| $6$ | Hermitian | complex anti-linear | $+1$ |
| $7$ | symmetric, real | real | $+1$ |

The **charge-conjugation operator** $J : \Delta_n \to \Delta_n$ is the complex anti-linear Spin-equivariant intertwiner that realises the inner product as $\langle \psi, \phi\rangle = (J\psi, \phi)$ (with $(\cdot,\cdot)$ Hermitian) or its real / quaternionic analogues. The classification $J^2 = \pm 1$ — given by the table — is what determines whether spinors come in real, complex, or quaternionic species, and underwrites every reality condition in physics (Majorana spinors in $r-s \!\!\mod 8 \in \{0,1,7\}$, symplectic-Majorana in $\{4,5\}$, no Majorana possible in $\{2,3,6\}$). This classification is Proposition I.5.5 in Lawson-Michelsohn [ref: TODO_REF Lawson-Michelsohn §I.5]. The spinor bundle is exactly the bundle associated to the principal $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$-bundle by the spin representation; this identifies the global field theory with a structure-group reduction, which is the bridge between local Clifford algebra and global geometry.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (existence and uniqueness of the spinor connection).** *Let $(M, g)$ be an oriented Riemannian spin manifold with spin structure $P_{\mathrm{Spin}(n)} \to P_{\mathrm{SO}(n)}(M)$. The Levi-Civita connection on $TM$ induces a unique connection on $P_{\mathrm{Spin}(n)}$ that descends, via the associated-bundle construction with the spin representation $\sigma$, to a covariant derivative $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$ on $\mathfrak{S}$. This $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$ is the unique connection on $\mathfrak{S}$ that:*

*(i) is compatible with the Hermitian metric on $\mathfrak{S}$, and*

*(ii) satisfies the Clifford-product Leibniz rule:*

$$
\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}_X(c(\xi) \psi) = c(\nabla^{LC}_X \xi) \psi + c(\xi) \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}_X \psi
$$

*for $X \in TM$, $\xi \in T^\ast M$, $\psi \in \Gamma(\mathfrak{S})$, where $\nabla^{LC}$ is the Levi-Civita connection on the cotangent bundle.*

**Proof (existence).** The Levi-Civita connection is a connection on $P_{\mathrm{SO}(n)}(M)$, given by an $\mathfrak{so}(n)$-valued 1-form $\omega^{LC}$ on $P_{\mathrm{SO}(n)}$. Since the cover $\rho : \mathrm{Spin}(n) \to \mathrm{SO}(n)$ is a local diffeomorphism, its differential $d\rho_e : \mathfrak{spin}(n) \to \mathfrak{so}(n)$ is an isomorphism of Lie algebras. Pull $\omega^{LC}$ back to $P_{\mathrm{Spin}(n)}$ via the spin-cover map $\pi$ and compose with $(d\rho_e)^{-1}$:

$$
\widetilde{\omega} := (d\rho_e)^{-1} \circ \pi^\ast \omega^{LC}.
$$

Verify $\widetilde\omega$ is a connection 1-form on $P_{\mathrm{Spin}(n)}$: it is equivariant under $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$-right-translation (since $\rho$ intertwines adjoint actions) and reproduces the fundamental vertical vector field of $\mathfrak{spin}(n)$ on the spin double cover. Both properties follow from $\rho$ being a Lie-group homomorphism with kernel $\mathbb{Z}/2$.

Descend $\widetilde\omega$ to a covariant derivative on the associated bundle $\mathfrak{S} = P_{\mathrm{Spin}(n)} \times_\sigma \Delta_n$ via the standard associated-bundle construction. The result is $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$.

**(uniqueness).** Suppose $\nabla'$ is another connection on $\mathfrak{S}$ satisfying (i) and (ii). Then $\nabla' - \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$ is a 1-form on $M$ with values in $\mathrm{End}(\mathfrak{S})$. Compatibility with the Hermitian metric forces it to be skew-Hermitian-valued. The Leibniz rule (ii) then forces $\nabla' - \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$ to commute with all Clifford multiplications $c(\xi)$ (since the same identity holds for both connections). On an irreducible Clifford module $\Delta_n$, the only endomorphisms commuting with all Clifford multiplications are scalars. Combined with skew-Hermitian-ness, the difference is zero. So $\nabla' = \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$. $\square$

The two characterising properties (i) and (ii) are the working definition of the spinor connection — they are how one *recognises* it in computations without going through the spin double cover.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [03.09.08] (dirac operator), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [03.09.10] (atiyah-singer index theorem). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

What is the complex rank of the spinor bundle on a 4-manifold?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In dimension $n$, complex rank is $2^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $n = 4$, $2^{\lfloor 4/2 \rfloor} = 2^2 = 4$. The spinor bundle on a 4-dimensional spin manifold has complex rank 4. It splits as $\mathfrak{S}^+ \oplus \mathfrak{S}^-$ with each half-spinor subbundle of complex rank 2.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).**

Show that the Hermitian inner product on $\Delta_n$ is unique up to a positive scalar.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply Schur's lemma to the Hermitian-form-valued $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$-equivariant intertwiner.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$-invariant Hermitian inner product is the same as a $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$-equivariant linear map $\Delta_n \to \overline{\Delta_n}$ that is positive-definite. The space of such intertwiners is one-dimensional by Schur's lemma (the spin representation $\Delta_n$ is irreducible for odd $n$ and a sum of two irreducibles for even $n$, but the Hermitian structure couples the two halves to the same form). Up to a positive scalar, the form is unique; this is the canonical Hermitian inner product on the spinor space.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that Clifford multiplication $c(\xi) : \mathfrak{S}_p \to \mathfrak{S}_p$ by a unit-norm covector $\xi \in T_p^\ast M$ is skew-Hermitian (i.e., $c(\xi)^\ast = -c(\xi)$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the Clifford relation $c(\xi)^2 = -|\xi|^2 = -1$ and the fact that the Hermitian inner product is $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$-invariant.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Clifford action of $T^\ast M$ on $\Delta_n$ extends, on the level of even-degree elements, to a $\mathrm{Spin}(n) \subset \mathrm{Cl}^0$ representation. The Hermitian inner product is invariant under $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$, hence under conjugation by spin elements. Together with the Clifford relation $c(\xi)^2 = -|\xi|^2 = -1$ for unit $\xi$, this forces $c(\xi)^\ast = -c(\xi)$ — i.e., $c(\xi)$ is skew-Hermitian. The full proof uses the unitary structure on the spin representation, which is induced by the Hermitian form on $\Delta_n$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

In a local orthonormal frame $e_1, \ldots, e_n$ with dual coframe $e^1, \ldots, e^n$, write the local expression for $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}_X$ in terms of the Levi-Civita connection coefficients $\omega^j_k$ on $TM$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The $\mathfrak{so}(n)$ part of the Levi-Civita connection lifts to $\mathfrak{spin}(n)$ via the explicit isomorphism $\mathfrak{so}(n) \cong \mathfrak{spin}(n) \subseteq \mathrm{Cl}^0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The local connection 1-form on the spin double cover is

$$
\widetilde{\omega} = \tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{j < k} \omega^j_k \, e_j e_k \in \mathfrak{spin}(n) \subseteq \mathrm{Cl}^0(\mathbb{R}^n),
$$

where $e_j e_k$ is the Clifford product. Acting on a spinor field $\psi$,

$$
\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}_X \psi = X(\psi) + \tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{j < k} \omega^j_k(X) \, c(e^j) c(e^k) \psi.
$$

The factor $\tfrac{1}{2}$ comes from the standard isomorphism $\mathfrak{so}(n) \cong \mathfrak{spin}(n)$ via $E_{jk} \mapsto \tfrac{1}{2} e_j e_k$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why does the spinor bundle exist only when $M$ has a spin structure?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Without a spin structure, there is no globally consistent way to lift orthonormal frames to Spin frames.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The spinor bundle is the associated bundle to the spin double cover $P_{\mathrm{Spin}(n)} \to P_{\mathrm{SO}(n)}(M)$ via the spin representation. Without a spin structure, there is no $P_{\mathrm{Spin}(n)}$ to associate from. Locally one can always choose Spin lifts of frames, but globally the lifts may fail to glue smoothly — exactly when the obstruction $w_2(M) \in H^2(M; \mathbb{Z}/2)$ is non-zero. Manifolds with $w_2(M) \neq 0$ admit no global spinor bundle; manifolds with $w_2(M) = 0$ admit one for each spin structure (parametrised by $H^1(M; \mathbb{Z}/2)$).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that on an even-dimensional spin manifold, the chirality grading $\mathfrak{S} = \mathfrak{S}^+ \oplus \mathfrak{S}^-$ is preserved by $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$ but reversed by $c(\xi)$ for any $\xi \in T^\ast M$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The complex volume element $\omega_\mathbb{C}$ commutes with even Clifford elements and anticommutes with odd ones; $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$ acts via $\mathfrak{spin}(n) \subseteq \mathrm{Cl}^0$, and Clifford multiplication by a covector is in $\mathrm{Cl}^1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The complex volume element $\omega_\mathbb{C} = i^k e_1 \cdots e_n$ commutes with $\mathrm{Cl}^0$ (the even part) and anticommutes with $\mathrm{Cl}^1$ (the odd part). The spinor connection $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$ is induced from the spin representation, which factors through $\mathrm{Cl}^0$ (since $\mathfrak{spin}(n) \subseteq \mathrm{Cl}^0$). So $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$ commutes with $\omega_\mathbb{C}$, and therefore preserves the $\pm 1$ eigenspaces $\Delta_n^\pm$ — equivalently, $S^\pm$. Clifford multiplication $c(\xi)$ for $\xi \in T^\ast M$ corresponds to an element of $\mathrm{Cl}^1$ (the cotangent space sits in the odd part), so it anticommutes with $\omega_\mathbb{C}$ and exchanges the chirality eigenspaces $S^+$ and $S^-$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Why is the spinor bundle on $\mathbb{R}^n$ globally a product but on a non-simply-connected spin manifold it can have multiple non-isomorphic versions?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Multiple non-isomorphic spinor bundles correspond to multiple non-isomorphic spin structures.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On $\mathbb{R}^n$ (which is simply-connected and contractible), $H^1(\mathbb{R}^n; \mathbb{Z}/2) = 0$, so the spin structure is unique, and the associated spinor bundle is the product $\mathbb{R}^n \times \Delta_n$. On a non-simply-connected spin manifold $M$, $H^1(M; \mathbb{Z}/2)$ classifies spin structures: each spin structure gives a (potentially) different spinor bundle. For example, $S^1$ has two spin structures (the "periodic" Ramond and "antiperiodic" Neveu-Schwarz spinors), and they yield non-isomorphic spinor bundles. Tori $T^n$ have $2^n$ spin structures, giving $2^n$ different spinor bundles. The choice matters for computing the Dirac index and matters physically (Ramond/Neveu-Schwarz boundary conditions in 2D fermionic CFT).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).**

Construct the complex spin representation $\Delta_n$ in even dimension $n = 2k$ as the exterior algebra $\Lambda^\ast W$ of a maximal isotropic subspace $W \subset \mathbb{C}^n$ for the standard symmetric form. Verify that the Clifford action of $\mathbb{C}^n$ on $\Lambda^\ast W$ — by wedge for vectors in $W$ and contraction for vectors in $W^\ast$ — satisfies the Clifford relation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take $\mathbb{C}^n = W \oplus W^\ast$ as a polarisation of the standard symmetric form, and identify $W^\ast$ with $W^\vee$ via the form. Each $v = w + \alpha$ with $w \in W$, $\alpha \in W^\ast$ acts on $\Lambda^\ast W$ as $w\wedge - \iota_\alpha$. Compute $v^2$ on $\Lambda^\ast W$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Polarise $\mathbb{C}^n = W \oplus W^\ast$ with $W$ a maximal isotropic subspace under the standard symmetric form $q$, and $W^\ast$ a complementary isotropic subspace dually paired with $W$. Define the action of $v = w + \alpha \in \mathbb{C}^n$ on $\omega \in \Lambda^\ast W$ by
$$
c(v)\omega = w\wedge\omega - \iota_\alpha\omega
$$
(wedge by the $W$-part minus interior product by the $W^\ast$-part). Compute
$$
c(v)^2\omega = (w\wedge - \iota_\alpha)(w\wedge\omega - \iota_\alpha\omega) = -w\wedge\iota_\alpha\omega - \iota_\alpha(w\wedge\omega),
$$
the wedge-wedge and interior-interior terms vanishing by antisymmetry. The cross-terms simplify via the standard identity $\iota_\alpha(w\wedge\omega) + w\wedge\iota_\alpha\omega = \alpha(w)\,\omega$. Therefore $c(v)^2\omega = -\alpha(w)\,\omega = -q(v)\,\omega$ (using $q(w + \alpha) = \alpha(w)$ from the polarisation), so $c(v)^2 = -q(v)\cdot\mathrm{id}$ — the Clifford relation. $\square$

This is Lawson-Michelsohn's §I.5 construction of the spin module from a maximal isotropic subspace, the foundational example of the spin representation. The total complex dimension of $\Lambda^\ast W$ is $2^k = 2^{n/2}$, matching the dimension of $\Delta_n$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (medium, symbolic).**

On a spin 4-manifold, verify the isomorphism $\mathfrak{S}^+ \otimes \mathfrak{S}^- \cong \Lambda^1 T^\ast M \otimes \mathbb{C}$ as complex bundles.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the dimensions: $\mathfrak{S}^\pm$ each rank $2$, so $\mathfrak{S}^+\otimes\mathfrak{S}^-$ has rank $4$, matching $\Lambda^1 T^\ast M\otimes\mathbb{C}$. Then identify the $\mathrm{Spin}(4) = \mathrm{SU}(2)\times\mathrm{SU}(2)$-equivariant structure.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In dimension 4, $\mathrm{Spin}(4) \cong \mathrm{SU}(2)_+ \times \mathrm{SU}(2)_-$. The half-spin representations $\Delta_4^\pm$ are the standard 2-dimensional representations of $\mathrm{SU}(2)_\pm$ respectively. Their tensor product $\Delta_4^+ \otimes \Delta_4^-$ is the standard $(\mathrm{SU}(2)_+,\mathrm{SU}(2)_-)$-bi-representation on $\mathbb{C}^2 \otimes \mathbb{C}^2 \cong \mathbb{C}^4$, which under the $\mathrm{Spin}(4) \to \mathrm{SO}(4)$ cover descends to the standard representation of $\mathrm{SO}(4)$ on $\mathbb{R}^4 \otimes \mathbb{C} = \mathbb{C}^4$.

Globally, the spin double cover transports this representation-level identification to a bundle isomorphism $\mathfrak{S}^+ \otimes \mathfrak{S}^- \cong TM \otimes \mathbb{C} \cong \Lambda^1 T^\ast M \otimes \mathbb{C}$ (using the metric to identify $TM$ with $T^\ast M$). $\square$

This isomorphism is load-bearing for the Atiyah-Singer index theorem on 4-manifolds, where $\mathfrak{S}^+\otimes\mathfrak{S}^-$-valued sections become differential 1-forms — the bridge between Dirac and Hodge theory in dimension 4.

</details>

**Exercise 10 (hard, symbolic).**

Compute the volume-element square $\omega_{\mathbb{R}}^2 \in \mathrm{Cl}_{r,s}$ for the real Clifford volume element $\omega_{\mathbb{R}} = e_1 e_2 \cdots e_n$ in each signature mod 8.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reorder the product, count sign flips: $\omega^2 = (-1)^{n(n-1)/2}\prod e_i^2$. With $n = r + s$ generators of which $r$ have $e_i^2 = +1$ and $s$ have $e_i^2 = -1$, $\prod e_i^2 = (-1)^s$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Reorder $\omega^2 = (e_1 \cdots e_n)(e_1\cdots e_n)$. Bringing the second copy past the first generates $n(n-1)/2$ sign flips from the Clifford anti-commutativity (each $e_i$ moves past $n-1$ others, divided by $2$ for double counting). After ordering $\omega^2 = (-1)^{n(n-1)/2}\prod_{i=1}^n e_i^2$.

In signature $(r,s)$ with $e_i^2 = +1$ for $i \leq r$ and $e_i^2 = -1$ for $r < i \leq n$, the product $\prod e_i^2 = (-1)^s$. Combining:
$$
\omega_{\mathbb{R}}^2 = (-1)^{n(n-1)/2 + s}.
$$
With $n = r + s$ this becomes $(-1)^{(r+s)(r+s-1)/2 + s}$. Plugging in mod 8 (writing $m = r - s\!\!\mod 8$):

| $r-s \!\!\mod 8$ | $\omega^2$ |
|---|---|
| $0$ | $+1$ |
| $1$ | $-1$ |
| $2$ | $-1$ |
| $3$ | $+1$ |
| $4$ | $+1$ |
| $5$ | $-1$ |
| $6$ | $-1$ |
| $7$ | $+1$ |

The pattern $+,-,-,+,+,-,-,+$ has period 4 in this presentation; the full mod-8 dependence enters via the sign of $\omega^2$ combined with the reality / quaternionicity of $\Delta_n$. The four cases $\omega^2 = +1$ are exactly those where the chirality decomposition $\Delta_n = \Delta_n^+ \oplus \Delta_n^-$ exists over $\mathbb{R}$ (real-line eigenvalues), and the four with $\omega^2 = -1$ require complexification. $\square$

This calculation is the foundation of the eight-fold periodic charge-conjugation classification (Lawson-Michelsohn Theorem I.5.4 and Proposition I.5.5).

## Advanced results [Master]

**The Clifford bundle as a bundle of algebras.** The fibrewise definition of $\mathrm{Cl}(TM)$ given in the formal-definition section assembles into a globally defined bundle of associative algebras. Its sections form the *Clifford algebra of $M$*, $\Gamma(\mathrm{Cl}(TM))$, into which differential forms embed via the Clifford map $\Lambda^\ast T^\ast M \to \mathrm{Cl}(TM)$ that sends $\xi_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \xi_k \mapsto \xi_1\cdot \xi_2 \cdots \xi_k$ (Clifford-symmetrised over permutations). The spinor bundle is then a $\Gamma(\mathrm{Cl}(TM))$-module, recovering the fibrewise Clifford action as a global module structure [ref: TODO_REF Lawson-Michelsohn §II.3 Definition II.3.1]. This is the bundle-of-Clifford-modules perspective that underlies the abstract Dirac-bundle construction of [03.09.14].

**Charge-conjugation classification.** Specialising the inner-product table of the formal-definition section, the charge-conjugation operator $J$ on the spinor bundle satisfies $J^2 = +1$ in signatures $(r-s) \!\!\mod 8 \in \{0, 1, 6, 7\}$ and $J^2 = -1$ in $\{2, 3, 4, 5\}$. The $+1$ cases admit *Majorana spinors* (real eigenvectors of $J$); the $-1$ cases admit only *symplectic-Majorana spinors* (eigenvectors after pairing two species). This eight-fold split is the geometric origin of the species-multiplicity rules in supersymmetric and string-theoretic physics, and matches the $KO_\ast$ periodicity of [03.09.15] via the ABS isomorphism: charge-conjugation $J$ on $\mathfrak{S}$ is precisely the right Cl_n-action structure that makes the spinor bundle a Cl_n-linear Dirac bundle.

**Twisted spinor bundles.** For an auxiliary Hermitian vector bundle $E \to M$ with connection $\nabla^E$, the *twisted spinor bundle* $\mathfrak{S} \otimes E$ inherits a tensor connection $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S} \otimes E} = \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}} \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes \nabla^E$ and a Clifford action that ignores the $E$-tensor factor. The corresponding twisted Dirac operator $\not{\!D}_E$ is the basic object of the Atiyah-Singer index theorem in its index-twisted form.

**Spin-c structures.** Even when $M$ does not admit a spin structure (i.e., $w_2(M) \neq 0$), a weaker condition — *spin-c* — may hold. A spin-c structure is a lift of $TM$'s frame bundle through $\mathrm{Spin}^c(n) \to \mathrm{SO}(n)$, where $\mathrm{Spin}^c(n) := \mathrm{Spin}(n) \times_{\mathbb{Z}/2} U(1)$. Spin-c manifolds carry spin-c spinor bundles whose Dirac operators are central to Seiberg-Witten theory.

**Theorem (LM D.1, $\mathrm{Spin}^c$ obstruction).** *An oriented manifold $M$ admits a $\mathrm{Spin}^c$ structure if and only if the third integral Stiefel-Whitney class $W_3(M)\in H^3(M;\mathbb{Z})$ vanishes; equivalently, if and only if $w_2(M)$ is the mod-$2$ reduction of an integral class.* The proof tracks the Bockstein. Consider the short exact sequence of structure groups

$$
1\to U(1)\to \mathrm{Spin}^c(n)\to SO(n)\to 1.
$$

The associated long exact sequence in non-abelian cohomology shows that the obstruction to lifting an $SO(n)$-bundle to $\mathrm{Spin}^c(n)$ lies in $H^2(M;U(1))\cong H^3(M;\mathbb{Z})$ (using $U(1)\simeq K(\mathbb{Z},1)$). Tracing the obstruction map identifies it with the Bockstein $\beta\colon H^2(M;\mathbb{F}_2)\to H^3(M;\mathbb{Z})$ applied to $w_2(M)$, that is $W_3(M)=\beta w_2(M)$. The lift exists iff $W_3=0$, equivalently iff $w_2$ is in the image of mod-$2$ reduction $H^2(M;\mathbb{Z})\to H^2(M;\mathbb{F}_2)$. The set of $\mathrm{Spin}^c$ structures on $M$, when non-empty, is a torsor over $H^2(M;\mathbb{Z})$, the cohomology classifying line bundles. In dimension $4$, every closed orientable manifold satisfies $W_3=0$ because $H^3(M;\mathbb{Z})$ injects into $H^3(M;\mathbb{Q})$ (no mod-$2$ Bockstein image survives) for orientable closed 4-manifolds, recovering the standard fact that every such manifold is $\mathrm{Spin}^c$.

**Theorem (LM D.2, almost-complex implies $\mathrm{Spin}^c$ canonically).** *Every almost-complex manifold carries a canonical $\mathrm{Spin}^c$ structure determined by the almost-complex structure $J$.* Let $J\colon TM\to TM$ be an almost-complex structure with associated unitary frame reduction $U(n)\hookrightarrow SO(2n)$. The map $U(n)\to\mathrm{Spin}^c(2n)$ defined by $A\mapsto (\widetilde{A},\det A)$ — where $\widetilde A$ lifts $A\in U(n)\subset SO(2n)$ to $\mathrm{Spin}(2n)$ ambiguously by sign and $\det A\in U(1)$ resolves the sign — produces a homomorphism $U(n)\to\mathrm{Spin}^c(2n)$ commuting with the projection to $SO(2n)$. (The construction uses that the obstruction to lifting $U(n)\to \mathrm{Spin}(2n)$ is exactly $\det\colon U(n)\to U(1)$, which is absorbed into the $U(1)$ factor of $\mathrm{Spin}^c$.) The unitary frame bundle $P_{U(n)}\to M$ pushed forward along this homomorphism yields the canonical $\mathrm{Spin}^c$ structure; its determinant line bundle is the *anticanonical bundle* $K_M^{-1}=\det T^{1,0}M$, and the associated $\mathrm{Spin}^c$ Dirac operator is the Dolbeault operator $\sqrt{2}(\bar\partial+\bar\partial^*)$ on $\Omega^{0,*}(M)$ [03.09.08]. Combined with the previous theorem, this gives the characteristic-class identity $w_2(M)\equiv c_1(M)\pmod 2$ for every almost-complex manifold, and explains why the standard examples — $\mathbb{CP}^n$, complex projective varieties, Kähler manifolds — admit $\mathrm{Spin}^c$ Dirac operators even when they fail to be spin.

**Lichnerowicz formula.** The square of the Dirac operator on a spin manifold is

$$
\not{\!D}^2 = \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}\ast} \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}} + \tfrac{1}{4}\,\mathrm{Scal},
$$

where $\mathrm{Scal}$ is scalar curvature [03.09.04]. (Throughout the spin-geometry chapter we write $\mathrm{Scal}$ for scalar curvature and reserve $R$ for the Riemann tensor; Lawson-Michelsohn overload $R$ for both, and the disambiguation pre-empts the most common reading error.) The curvature term arises directly from the curvature of the spinor connection $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$, computed via the spin lift of the Riemann curvature tensor. The Lichnerowicz formula is the source of the obstruction to positive scalar curvature on closed spin manifolds with non-zero Â-genus.

**Atiyah-Singer with spinors.** For a closed even-dimensional spin manifold $M$ and Hermitian bundle $E$ with connection,

$$
\mathrm{ind}(\not{\!D}_E^+) = \langle \widehat{A}(M) \, \mathrm{ch}(E), [M] \rangle,
$$

where $\not{\!D}^+_E$ is the chiral Dirac operator on $\mathfrak{S}^+ \otimes E$ [03.09.10]. The spinor bundle is the geometric carrier of this most-celebrated index theorem.

**Spinor bundles on Lorentzian manifolds.** In Lorentzian signature $(1, n-1)$, the relevant spin structure is for the proper-orthochronous Lorentz group, and the spinor bundle is associated to the spin representation $\Delta_{1, n-1}$ of $\mathrm{Spin}^+(1, n-1)$. Causality and time-orientation enter as additional structure. The Dirac equation in general relativity lives on this Lorentzian spinor bundle.

**Synthesis.** The spinor bundle $S \to M$ is the vector bundle associated to the Spin(n)-principal bundle via the irreducible spin representation, and its existence is the geometric realisation of the spin structure atop the oriented Riemannian manifold. Clifford multiplication $TM \otimes S \to S$ equips spinors with a pointwise algebraic action by tangent vectors, while the spin connection lifts the Levi-Civita connection and yields the Dirac operator $D : \Gamma(S) \to \Gamma(S)$ as the composition of Clifford multiplication with the covariant derivative. The spinor bundle is the arena where the Lichnerowicz formula, index theory, and parallel spinor classification all take place, and its complex/real/quaternionic structure is governed by the Clifford chessboard, linking the geometry of M to representation theory and K-theory.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Spinor connection existence and uniqueness.** Proved in §"Key theorem".

**Hermitian metric on the spinor bundle.** The complex spin representation $\Delta_n$ admits a $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$-invariant positive-definite Hermitian inner product, unique up to a positive scalar (Exercise 2). Patching this fibrewise via the associated-bundle construction gives a global Hermitian metric on $\mathfrak{S}$, automatically $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$-parallel by construction (since the Levi-Civita connection on the frame bundle preserves the metric, and the spin lift inherits the property).

**Clifford-multiplication compatibility.** $c : T^\ast M \otimes \mathfrak{S} \to \mathfrak{S}$ is a $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$-parallel bundle map: $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}(c(\xi) \psi) = c(\nabla^{LC} \xi) \psi + c(\xi) \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}} \psi$. The proof is by direct computation in a local frame, using the explicit formula for $\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$ in terms of $\mathfrak{spin}(n) \subseteq \mathrm{Cl}^0$ (Exercise 4) and the commutation relations between $\mathfrak{spin}(n)$ and $\mathrm{Cl}^1$ inside the Clifford algebra.

**Chirality decomposition (even dimension).** Proved in Exercise 6.

**Lichnerowicz formula.** In normal coordinates with synchronous frame at a point, $\not{\!D}^2 = \sum_{i,j} c(e^i) c(e^j) \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}_{e_i} \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}_{e_j}$. Splitting into symmetric (= connection Laplacian) and antisymmetric (= curvature) parts: the symmetric part is $-\sum_i (\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}_{e_i})^2 = \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}\ast} \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}$ in normal coordinates. The antisymmetric part is $\tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{i<j} c(e^i) c(e^j) [\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}_{e_i}, \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}_{e_j}]$, and the spin-bundle curvature $[\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}_{e_i}, \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}}_{e_j}] = R^{\mathfrak{S}}_{ij}$ is computed from the Riemann curvature via the spin lift. After Clifford-contracting and using the first Bianchi identity, the result is $\tfrac{1}{4} \mathrm{Scal} \cdot \mathrm{id}_{\mathfrak{S}}$, where $\mathrm{Scal} = R^k_{kij} \delta^{ij}$ is the scalar curvature. So $\not{\!D}^2 = \nabla^{\mathfrak{S}\ast}\nabla^{\mathfrak{S}} + \mathrm{Scal}/4$ on a closed spin manifold.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The notion of a spinor field on a curved manifold was introduced informally by Weyl (1929) and Fock (1929) in the context of Dirac equations on general relativistic backgrounds. The bundle-theoretic formulation crystallised in the 1950s through the work of Lichnerowicz, who gave the eponymous formula and the obstruction to positive scalar curvature, and through Atiyah and Singer's 1963 reformulation that made the spinor bundle the natural setting for index theory.

Lawson and Michelsohn's 1989 monograph systematised the entire theory: spin structures, spinor bundles, Dirac operators, and the Atiyah-Singer apparatus on spin manifolds [ref: TODO_REF Lawson-Michelsohn §II–§III]. Friedrich's later text emphasised the Riemannian-geometric perspective with computational examples [ref: TODO_REF Friedrich Ch. 1].

In physics, the spinor bundle is the geometric setting for fermion fields: the Dirac equation, the Standard Model fermion content, and string-theoretic worldsheet fermions are all sections of appropriate spinor bundles. The chirality decomposition $\mathfrak{S} = \mathfrak{S}^+ \oplus \mathfrak{S}^-$ is the geometric origin of the parity asymmetry in the weak interaction. Anomalies in chiral gauge theories are computed via index theorems on spinor bundles.
