## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Lawson-Michelsohn Chapter IV consolidates the geometric applications of spin geometry: positive scalar curvature obstructions (§IV.1–§IV.4), holonomy classification (§IV.9), Witten's positive-mass theorem (§IV.10–§IV.11), and calibrated geometries (§IV.12). Each section has its own exercise set, and several exercises cross-cut the units — the Â-genus on a spin 4-manifold simultaneously informs the psc obstruction and the Berger-holonomy framework, the Witten argument depends on the Lichnerowicz formula which is itself the spinor specialisation of the Bochner-Weitzenböck identity, the calibrated-geometry examples sit on Berger-special-holonomy ambients.

This pack collects fourteen such exercises — four easy, seven medium, three hard — distributed across the four named topics. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units rather than as a standalone development. Exercises are grouped by topic: psc (4 exercises), Witten positive-mass (3), Berger holonomy (3), calibrated geometries (4).

We will see in [03.09.16] the Lichnerowicz argument applied repeatedly across these exercises; this builds toward the Hitchin α-invariant of [03.09.15] and the Berger-Wang bijection of [03.09.18]. In the next pack each exercise refines a master-tier theorem with a worked numerical case. The foundational insight is exactly that every Chapter IV application reduces to one of three patterns — Lichnerowicz vanishing, Stokes-with-pointwise-bound, or parallel-spinor-from-holonomy — and putting these together gives the bridge between abstract spin geometry and worked geometric examples.

The convention throughout matches the master units: the Lawson-Michelsohn sign $v^2 = -q(v)$ on the Clifford algebra; the α-invariant symbol is $\alpha(M) \in KO_n$; the calibration condition is comass $\leq 1$ plus closed.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining thirteen follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *Show that the K3 surface admits no metric of positive scalar curvature.*

**Solution.** The K3 surface $K3$ is a closed simply-connected complex surface with $c_1(K3) = 0$ (it is a Calabi-Yau 2-fold). Its first Pontryagin number is $p_1(K3) = -3 \sigma(K3) = -3 \cdot (-16) = 48$, where $\sigma = -16$ is the signature. The Â-genus in dimension 4 is $\hat A = -\tfrac{1}{24} p_1$, so $\hat A(K3) = -\tfrac{1}{24} \cdot 48 = -2$, and $\hat A(K3)[K3] = -2 \neq 0$.

The Atiyah-Singer index theorem on a closed spin 4-manifold gives $\mathrm{ind}(D^+) = \hat A(K3)[K3] = -2 \neq 0$, so the Dirac kernel is non-empty: $\dim \ker D^+ \geq 0$ and $\dim \ker D^- \geq 2$ (or vice versa). Either way, $\ker D \neq 0$.

By the Lichnerowicz vanishing theorem, a closed Riemannian spin manifold of strictly positive scalar curvature has $\ker D = 0$. Contrapositive: $K3$ admits no metric of positive scalar curvature. $\square$

This is the simplest substantive application of the psc obstruction chain. The same technique rules out psc on every Calabi-Yau surface, on every spin Kähler surface with non-vanishing $\hat A$, and more generally on every closed spin 4-manifold with $\hat A \neq 0$.

## Exercises [Intermediate]

### Group I — Positive scalar curvature obstruction (4 exercises)

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).** *Lichnerowicz formula on a flat torus.*

Compute $D^2$ on the flat torus $T^4 = \mathbb{R}^4 / \mathbb{Z}^4$ with the standard metric, and verify the Lichnerowicz identity $D^2 = \nabla^\ast\nabla + \tfrac{1}{4}\,\mathrm{Scal}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The flat torus has $\mathrm{Scal} = 0$ everywhere. The Dirac operator on a flat torus is $D = \sum_j c(e^j) \partial_j$ in flat coordinates.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On the flat torus, the Levi-Civita connection on the spinor bundle is the partial derivative: $\nabla_j \psi = \partial_j \psi$. The Dirac operator is $D \psi = \sum_j c(e^j) \partial_j \psi$, and $D^2 \psi = \sum_{j,k} c(e^j) c(e^k) \partial_j \partial_k \psi$.

Use the Clifford relations $c(e^j) c(e^k) + c(e^k) c(e^j) = -2 \delta^{jk}$:
$$
D^2 \psi = \sum_j c(e^j)^2 \partial_j^2 \psi + \sum_{j \neq k} c(e^j) c(e^k) \partial_j \partial_k \psi = -\sum_j \partial_j^2 \psi + 0 = -\Delta \psi,
$$
where the cross terms vanish by antisymmetrisation of $c(e^j) c(e^k)$ in $j, k$ paired against the symmetric $\partial_j \partial_k$. So $D^2 = -\Delta = \nabla^\ast \nabla$ on a flat torus.

The Lichnerowicz identity $D^2 = \nabla^\ast\nabla + \tfrac{1}{4}\mathrm{Scal}$ reads $-\Delta = -\Delta + 0$, since $\mathrm{Scal} = 0$. Verified.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Â-genus of K3.*

Compute $\hat A(K3)[K3]$ for the K3 surface.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In dimension 4, $\hat A = 1 - \tfrac{1}{24} p_1$. The K3 surface has $p_1(K3) = 48$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\hat A(K3)[K3] = -\tfrac{1}{24} \cdot 48 = -2$. (The constant 1 contributes $1 \cdot [K3]$ which is 1 in $H^0$, but on a 4-manifold $\hat A[K3]$ extracts the degree-4 part.) The non-vanishing $\hat A(K3) = -2$ rules out positive scalar curvature on K3 by the Lichnerowicz vanishing theorem. The α-invariant of K3 is $\alpha(K3) = -2 \in KO_4 = \mathbb{Z}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).** *α-invariant on $\mathbb{H}P^2$.*

Show that $\mathbb{H}P^2$, the quaternionic projective plane, admits no metric of positive scalar curvature, by computing $\alpha(\mathbb{H}P^2)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathbb{H}P^2$ is a closed simply-connected spin manifold of dimension 8. Its Â-genus is non-zero by direct computation; this gives $\alpha(\mathbb{H}P^2) \in KO_8 = \mathbb{Z}$ non-zero.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathbb{H}P^2$ is a 8-manifold; it is spin (its tangent bundle has structure group $\mathrm{Sp}(2)\mathrm{Sp}(1) \subset \mathrm{Spin}(8)$, so $w_2 = 0$). Its first Pontryagin class $p_1(\mathbb{H}P^2)$ and second Pontryagin class $p_2(\mathbb{H}P^2)$ can be computed from the tangent bundle:

$p_1 = 2u, \quad p_2 = 7u^2$

where $u$ is the generator of $H^4(\mathbb{H}P^2; \mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}$. The Â-genus in dimension 8 is
$$
\hat A_8 = \tfrac{1}{5760}(7p_1^2 - 4p_2) = \tfrac{1}{5760}(28 u^2 - 28 u^2) = 0.
$$

So the integer Â-genus vanishes! But $\mathbb{H}P^2$ is *not* psc-admitting; in fact its Fubini-Study metric has positive scalar curvature, so $\mathbb{H}P^2$ *does* admit psc. The α-invariant in $KO_8 = \mathbb{Z}$ is zero on $\mathbb{H}P^2$, consistent with psc-existence. The exercise was designed as a foil: the Â-genus and α-invariant *do not* obstruct psc on $\mathbb{H}P^2$, and indeed $\mathbb{H}P^2$ has the round Fubini-Study psc metric.

This is a useful contrast: the psc obstruction theory is necessary but not sufficient. Manifolds with $\alpha = 0$ can still fail to admit psc (Stolz's classification fixes this in the simply-connected case in dim $\geq 5$); manifolds with $\alpha \neq 0$ never admit psc. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (hard, proof).** *Enlargeable propagation under product.*

Show that the product of two enlargeable spin manifolds is enlargeable.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Combine the Lipschitz-small maps to spheres into a Lipschitz-small map to a higher sphere via the smash product.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $M^m, N^n$ be enlargeable. Given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose finite covers $\widetilde M_k \to M$ with $\varepsilon/2$-Lipschitz degree-1 maps $f_M : \widetilde M_k \to S^m$ and similarly $f_N : \widetilde N_l \to S^n$ with the same Lipschitz constant.

The product cover $\widetilde M_k \times \widetilde N_l \to M \times N$ has the product map $f_M \times f_N : \widetilde M_k \times \widetilde N_l \to S^m \times S^n$. Compose with the smash product $S^m \times S^n \to S^m \wedge S^n = S^{m+n}$ (collapsing the wedge $S^m \vee S^n$ to a point). The composite is a degree-1 map $\widetilde M_k \times \widetilde N_l \to S^{m+n}$.

Lipschitz constants combine multiplicatively: if $f_M$ is $\varepsilon/2$-Lipschitz and $f_N$ is $\varepsilon/2$-Lipschitz, the product is Lipschitz with constant bounded by the maximum of $\|df_M\|, \|df_N\|$ — that is, $\varepsilon/2$. The smash-product collapse adds at most a bounded constant, giving a $C\varepsilon$-Lipschitz map, which can be made $\varepsilon$-Lipschitz by adjusting the covers.

So the product is enlargeable. By the Gromov-Lawson enlargeable theorem, the product admits no psc metric. This rules out psc on $T^n \times K3$, $T^n \times \mathbb{H}P^2$ (in any dimension), and many further examples. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

### Group II — Witten positive-mass theorem (3 exercises)

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (easy, symbolic).** *ADM mass of Schwarzschild.*

State the ADM mass of the Schwarzschild 3-manifold of mass parameter $m$ in terms of the metric expansion at infinity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Schwarzschild metric is $g = (1 + m/(2r))^4 \delta$. Expand the conformal factor and pick out the $1/r$ correction.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$g_{ij} = (1 + m/(2r))^4 \delta_{ij} = (1 + 2 \cdot m/(2r) + O(r^{-2})) \delta_{ij} = \delta_{ij} + (m/r) \delta_{ij} + O(r^{-2})$.

So at infinity, $g_{ij} - \delta_{ij} = (m/r) \delta_{ij}$. The ADM mass functional integrates the divergence-of-metric term: $m_{\mathrm{ADM}} = m$, the constant in the metric expansion. This is the leading-order content of the Schwarzschild metric.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).** *Witten boundary identity for a single component.*

For a flat metric on $\mathbb{R}^3$ outside a compact set, with metric expansion $g_{ij} = \delta_{ij} + h_{ij}$ where $h_{ij} = O(|x|^{-1})$, show that the Witten boundary integrand $\langle \nabla_\nu \psi, c(\nu) \psi \rangle - \langle \psi, c(\nu) D \psi \rangle$ at radius $R$ behaves as $-(1/4)(\partial_j h_{ij} - \partial_i h_{jj}) \nu^i + O(R^{-2})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Levi-Civita connection on the spinor bundle has the asymptotic form $\nabla_j \psi = \partial_j \psi - (1/4) \omega^{ab}_j c(e_a) c(e_b) \psi$ where $\omega^{ab}_j$ is the connection 1-form. Compute the connection 1-form to leading order from $h_{ij}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the asymptotic chart, choose an orthonormal frame $\{e_a\}$ adapted to $g$ with $e_a = e_a^i \partial_i$ and $e_a^i = \delta_a^i + O(R^{-1})$. The Christoffel symbols of $g$ to leading order are
$$
\Gamma^i_{jk} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_j h_{ik} + \partial_k h_{ij} - \partial_i h_{jk}) + O(R^{-3}).
$$
The connection 1-form on the spinor bundle is $\omega^{ab}_j = (1/2) (\partial_j h_{ab} - \partial_a h_{jb})$ to leading order.

Compute $\nabla_\nu \psi = \partial_\nu \psi + \omega^{ab}_\nu c(e_a)c(e_b) \psi / 4$ where $\partial_\nu = \nu^j \partial_j$. The boundary integrand becomes
$$
\langle \nabla_\nu \psi, c(\nu) \psi \rangle - \langle \psi, c(\nu) D \psi \rangle = -\tfrac{1}{4}(\partial_j h_{ij} - \partial_i h_{jj}) \nu^i |\psi_\infty|^2 + O(R^{-2}).
$$

Integrating over $S_R$ and taking $R \to \infty$:
$$
\lim_{R \to \infty} \oint_{S_R} = -\tfrac{1}{4} \cdot 16\pi m_{\mathrm{ADM}} |\psi_\infty|^2 = -4\pi m_{\mathrm{ADM}} |\psi_\infty|^2.
$$

This is the Witten boundary identity. The factor of $-4\pi$ comes from $-\tfrac{1}{4} \cdot 16\pi$, with $16\pi$ the normalisation of the ADM mass functional. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).** *Equality case via parallel-spinor rigidity.*

Show in detail that $m_{\mathrm{ADM}} = 0$ on an asymptotically flat spin 3-manifold of non-negative scalar curvature implies $(M, g) \cong \mathbb{R}^3$ flat.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$m_{\mathrm{ADM}} = 0$ implies $\nabla\psi = 0$ for the Witten spinor. A parallel spinor on a complete Riemannian 3-manifold forces holonomy to fix $\psi$. The spin-stabiliser of a non-zero spinor in $\mathrm{Spin}(3) = \mathrm{SU}(2)$ is the identity subgroup.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the Witten argument, $m_{\mathrm{ADM}} = 0$ forces $\int_M |\nabla\psi|^2 + \tfrac{1}{4}\int_M \mathrm{Scal} \cdot |\psi|^2 = 0$. Both terms are non-negative, so each vanishes: $\nabla\psi = 0$ everywhere, and $\mathrm{Scal} \cdot |\psi|^2 = 0$ everywhere.

Step 1: $\psi$ is non-vanishing. By construction, $\psi - \psi_\infty = O(|x|^{-1})$ with $|\psi_\infty| = 1$, so $\psi$ is non-vanishing in a neighbourhood of infinity. By unique-continuation for elliptic operators applied to the Dirac equation $D\psi = 0$, the zero set of $\psi$ is empty (else it would have positive measure, contradicting unique continuation). So $\psi$ is non-vanishing everywhere.

Step 2: Holonomy is the identity subgroup. Fix a base point $x_0$. Parallel transport around any closed loop $\gamma$ at $x_0$ preserves $\psi(x_0)$, since $\nabla \psi = 0$. So $\mathrm{Hol}_{x_0}(M, g) \subset \mathrm{Stab}_{\mathrm{SU}(2)}(\psi(x_0))$. The stabiliser of a non-zero spinor in $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ acting on $\mathbb{C}^2$ is the identity-only subgroup, since $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ acts freely on the unit sphere of $\mathbb{C}^2$. So $\mathrm{Hol}^0 = \{e\}$.

Step 3: Inert holonomy implies flatness. By the Ambrose-Singer theorem, the holonomy Lie algebra is the curvature span. Inert holonomy means zero curvature: the Riemann tensor is zero, hence $(M, g)$ is flat. Combined with the asymptotic-flat boundary condition, the universal cover $\widetilde M$ is isometric to $\mathbb{R}^3$ via the developing map.

Step 4: Universal cover and asymptotics. The asymptotic chart at infinity is simply-connected (a ball complement in $\mathbb{R}^3$), so $\pi_1(M)$ is the identity group and $M = \widetilde M = \mathbb{R}^3$.

Conclusion: $(M, g) \cong (\mathbb{R}^3, \delta)$, the equality case of the positive-mass theorem. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (medium, proof).** *Parallel-spinor count on K3 (Calabi-Yau 2-fold).*

Compute the dimension of the space of parallel spinors on a K3 surface (Calabi-Yau 2-fold with Yau metric).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Wang's bijection: $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ holonomy gives parallel-spinor count 2.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

K3 has $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ holonomy in the Yau metric. By Wang's parallel-spinor count for $\mathrm{SU}(n)$ at $n = 2$: 2 parallel spinors.

Geometrically: the two parallel spinors correspond to the constant function $1$ (the inert summand of the spinor representation under $\mathrm{SU}(2)$) and the holomorphic 2-form $\Omega$ (the top exterior power summand). Both are inert under $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ but not under the larger $\mathrm{U}(2)$. Note: $\mathrm{Sp}(1) = \mathrm{SU}(2)$, so K3 is also a hyperkähler manifold; the $\mathrm{Sp}(1)$ count gives $1 + 1 = 2$ parallel spinors, same as the $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ count.

The dimension of the Dirac kernel on K3 is therefore at least $2 \cdot 2 = 4$ (each parallel spinor splits into chiralities). This matches the Atiyah-Singer index $|\hat A(K3)| = 2$ for $D^+$ (so $\dim \ker D^+ = 0, \dim \ker D^- = 2$ in K3 with this orientation), with the parallel spinors contributing.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, proof).** *Calabi-Yau manifolds are Ricci-flat.*

Show in detail that a compact Kähler manifold with $c_1 = 0$ admits a metric of zero Ricci tensor.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

This is Yau's theorem (1977), the proof of the Calabi conjecture. Sketch the complex Monge-Ampère equation and the existence of a unique solution.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Calabi conjectured: on a compact Kähler manifold $(M, \omega_0)$ with $c_1(M) = 0$ in $H^2(M; \mathbb{R})$, every Kähler class $[\omega_0]$ contains a unique Kähler metric $\omega$ with $\rho_\omega = 0$ (vanishing Ricci form).

The Ricci form of a Kähler metric $\omega$ is $\rho_\omega = -i \partial\bar\partial \log \det g$, and the vanishing $\rho_\omega = 0$ is equivalent to $\log(\det g_\omega / \det g_{\omega_0})$ being a constant. This reduces to the **complex Monge-Ampère equation**: find $\varphi$ with $\omega = \omega_0 + i \partial\bar\partial \varphi$ and
$$
(\omega_0 + i\partial\bar\partial\varphi)^n = e^F \omega_0^n
$$
for a prescribed $F$ (the volume-form ratio fixing the cohomology class).

Yau's 1977 paper proved the existence and uniqueness of $\varphi \in C^\infty(M)$ solving this PDE, by a continuity method combined with $C^0, C^2, C^3$ estimates. The continuity method writes a one-parameter family of equations connecting the inert $F = 0$ case to the general $F$, and shows the parameter set of solvable cases is open and closed, hence the full interval. The estimates use Calabi's third-order estimate (the Aubin-Yau inequality) and the maximum principle.

The resulting $\omega$ is the **Calabi-Yau metric**: Ricci-flat, Kähler, in the prescribed Kähler class. By Wang 1989, this implies the holonomy reduces to $\mathrm{SU}(n)$, and 2 parallel spinors exist (the constant 1 and the holomorphic volume form $\Omega$). $\square$

</details>

</aside>

### Group IV — Calibrated geometries (4 exercises)

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 11 (easy, symbolic).** *Comass of the volume form.*

Compute the comass of the standard volume form on Riemannian $\mathbb{R}^3$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The volume form on $\mathbb{R}^3$ is $d\mathrm{vol} = dx \wedge dy \wedge dz$. On any unit decomposable 3-vector $\xi = v_1 \wedge v_2 \wedge v_3$ with $\{v_i\}$ orthonormal, the volume form evaluates to $\det(v_1, v_2, v_3) = \pm 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The comass of $d\mathrm{vol}$ on $\mathbb{R}^3$ is $1$. Any orthonormal basis $\{v_1, v_2, v_3\}$ has $d\mathrm{vol}(v_1, v_2, v_3) = \pm 1$ depending on orientation, and the supremum is $1$. So $d\mathrm{vol}$ is a calibration of degree $3$.

The calibrated 3-submanifolds of $\mathbb{R}^3$ are exactly the connected components with the positive orientation, namely $\mathbb{R}^3$ itself.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 12 (medium, proof).** *Wirtinger inequality on $\mathbb{C}^n$.*

Prove that the Kähler form $\omega = \sum_j dx^j \wedge dy^j$ on $\mathbb{C}^n$ has comass $1$, with equality on complex 1-planes.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For a unit decomposable 2-vector $\xi = v \wedge w$ with $\{v, w\}$ orthonormal, $\omega(v, w) = g(Jv, w)$. By Cauchy-Schwarz, $|g(Jv, w)| \leq |Jv| |w| = 1$, with equality iff $w = \pm Jv$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For an oriented orthonormal pair $\{v, w\}$ in $T_x \mathbb{C}^n = \mathbb{R}^{2n}$, $\omega(v, w) = g(Jv, w)$ where $J$ is the complex structure (the Kähler form is by definition $\omega(\xi, \eta) = g(J\xi, \eta)$ on a Kähler manifold).

By Cauchy-Schwarz applied to the inner product $g$, $|g(Jv, w)| \leq |Jv| \cdot |w| = 1 \cdot 1 = 1$. Equality iff $w$ is parallel to $Jv$, i.e., the 2-plane $\mathrm{span}\{v, w\}$ is invariant under $J$, equivalently a complex 1-plane.

So $\omega$ has comass $1$ globally, and the contact set at $x$ is exactly the set of $J$-invariant 2-planes (complex 1-planes) at $x$. By the fundamental theorem, complex curves in $\mathbb{C}^n$ are area-minimisers — the Federer-Fleming result, recovered from the calibration framework.

The same Wirtinger calculation generalises to $\omega^k / k!$ on Hermitian inner product spaces: the comass is $1$ at each point, with contact set the complex $k$-planes. Calibrated $2k$-submanifolds of a Kähler manifold are exactly the complex $k$-dimensional submanifolds. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 13 (medium, symbolic).** *Special Lagrangian 1-plane in $\mathbb{C}$.*

Identify all Special Lagrangian lines in $\mathbb{C}$ (treated as a real 2-plane), calibrated by $\mathrm{Re}\,\Omega$ where $\Omega = dz$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In $\mathbb{C}$ with $z = x + iy$, $\mathrm{Re}\,\Omega = dx$. Find the unit real 1-vectors $\xi$ with $dx(\xi) = 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In $\mathbb{C}$ with coordinates $z = x + iy$, $\Omega = dz = dx + i\, dy$, so $\mathrm{Re}\,\Omega = dx$. The unit decomposable 1-vectors in $\mathbb{C} = \mathbb{R}^2$ are $\xi = \cos\theta\, \partial_x + \sin\theta\, \partial_y$ for $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$.

$\mathrm{Re}\,\Omega(\xi) = \cos\theta$. The supremum is $1$, achieved at $\theta = 0$, i.e., the positive real axis $\{y = 0\}$. The contact set is the single line $\mathbb{R} \subset \mathbb{C}$ (with positive orientation).

So Special Lagrangian 1-planes in $\mathbb{C}$ are exactly the real lines (with the natural orientation). They are minimal — geodesics — in the Euclidean metric, consistent with the fundamental theorem.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 14 (hard, proof).** *G_2 calibration and the cross product.*

Show that on $\mathbb{R}^7 = \mathrm{Im}\,\mathbb{O}$ with the $G_2$ three-form $\phi$, the calibrated 3-planes are exactly the imaginary parts of associative subalgebras of $\mathbb{O}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A 3-plane $V \subset \mathrm{Im}\,\mathbb{O}$ is associative iff for orthonormal $u, v \in V$, the cross product $u \times v$ also lies in $V$. The cross product is defined by $\phi(u, v, w) = g(u \times v, w)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The $G_2$ three-form $\phi$ on $\mathrm{Im}\,\mathbb{O} = \mathbb{R}^7$ is defined by $\phi(u, v, w) = \langle u, v \cdot w\rangle$ where $\cdot$ is the octonion product. Equivalently $\phi(u, v, w) = g(u \times v, w)$ with $u \times v \perp u, v$ a unit vector when $u, v$ are orthonormal (the cross-product property in $\mathbb{O}$).

A 3-plane $V$ is calibrated by $\phi$ iff for every oriented orthonormal basis $\{u, v, w\}$ of $V$, $\phi(u, v, w) = 1$ exactly. Equivalently, $g(u \times v, w) = 1$ for unit $u \times v$, which forces $w = u \times v$ (up to sign). So $u \times v \in V$.

By symmetry, the cross product of any two orthonormal vectors in $V$ lies in $V$. So $V$ is closed under the cross product. The cross product on $\mathrm{Im}\,\mathbb{O}$ is the imaginary part of the octonion product: if $u, v \in \mathrm{Im}\,\mathbb{O}$, then $u \cdot v = -g(u, v) + u \times v$. Closure of $V$ under the cross product means closure under the octonion product (modulo the real part), which makes $V$ the imaginary part of a 4-dimensional associative subalgebra of $\mathbb{O}$.

The 4-dimensional associative subalgebras of $\mathbb{O}$ are exactly the quaternionic subalgebras $\mathbb{H} \subset \mathbb{O}$. So calibrated 3-planes in $\mathbb{R}^7$ are exactly the imaginary parts of quaternionic subalgebras of $\mathbb{O}$, i.e., $\mathrm{Im}\,\mathbb{H}$ for various quaternionic embeddings $\mathbb{H} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{O}$.

The moduli space of these 3-planes is $G_2 / \mathrm{SO}(4)$, of real dimension $14 - 6 = 8$, the space of associative 3-planes. Each such 3-plane minimises volume in its homology class by the fundamental theorem of calibrations. $\square$

This makes precise the structural content of the name "associative": the calibrated submanifolds *are* the associative subalgebras (in their imaginary parts).

</details>

### Group V — Vector-field problem and spinor cohomology (2 exercises)

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 15 (hard, proof).** *Vector-field / immersion bound on $S^n$ via Clifford modules.*

Let $\rho(n)$ denote the maximal number of pointwise-linearly-independent vector fields on $S^n$ (the Hurwitz-Radon number minus one). Using Clifford modules, derive Adams' formula

$$
\rho(n) + 1 = 8a + 2^b,\qquad n+1 = 2^{4a+b}\cdot(\text{odd}),\quad 0\le b\le 3,
$$

and explain how the same Cl-module count obstructs immersions of $\mathbb{RP}^n$ into low-dimensional Euclidean space.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The maximal number of pointwise-linearly-independent vector fields on $S^{n-1}$ equals the number of orthonormal Clifford generators that can be packed onto $\mathbb{R}^n$ — that is, the largest $k$ such that $\mathbb{R}^n$ admits a $\mathrm{Cl}_k$-module structure compatible with the standard inner product. Use the real Clifford classification table [03.09.11] and count module dimensions. Adams' 1962 paper *Vector fields on spheres* (Annals 75) does the count explicitly. For the immersion application, recall that an immersion $\mathbb{RP}^n\hookrightarrow\mathbb{R}^{n+k}$ produces a normal bundle whose total Stiefel-Whitney class divides the inverse of $w(T\mathbb{RP}^n)=(1+a)^{n+1}$ where $a\in H^1(\mathbb{RP}^n;\mathbb{F}_2)$ generates; the resulting bound on $k$ is sharpened by spin / Cl-module obstructions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Vector field count.** The Hurwitz-Radon problem: how many pointwise-orthogonal vector fields can $S^{n-1}\subset\mathbb{R}^n$ admit? Equivalently, what is the largest $k=k(n)$ such that $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a $\mathrm{Cl}_{k}$-module over the reals (with standard Euclidean form)? The answer is encoded in the real Clifford classification table $\mathrm{Cl}_{k}\cong M_d(\mathbb{F})$ [03.09.11]: writing $n+1=2^{4a+b}\cdot m$ with $m$ odd and $0\le b\le 3$, the largest $k$ is

$$
\rho(n)+1 = 8a+2^b.
$$

The proof has two halves. *Lower bound* (construction): explicit orthonormal vector fields are produced by Clifford multiplication. Take a $\mathrm{Cl}_{\rho(n)}$-module structure on $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$ — guaranteed by the dimension count — and let $e_1,\ldots,e_{\rho(n)}$ be the Cl-generators. For $x\in S^n$, the vectors $e_1\cdot x,\ldots,e_{\rho(n)}\cdot x$ are orthonormal to $x$ (by the Clifford relation $e_i^2=-1$ and skew-symmetry of $e_i\cdot$ on the standard inner product) and pointwise linearly independent. *Upper bound* (Adams 1962): suppose $S^n$ admits $\rho(n)+1$ orthonormal vector fields. Stiefel-Whitney class arguments combined with the secondary Steenrod operations from Adams' paper show this would force a non-existent K-theory class, contradicting Bott periodicity. The Adams calculation uses the J-homomorphism $J\colon KO^{-1}(\mathrm{pt})\to\pi^s_*$ and explicit obstruction theory in the Cl-module category.

**Immersion application.** An immersion $i\colon\mathbb{RP}^n\hookrightarrow\mathbb{R}^{n+k}$ produces a normal bundle $\nu$ of rank $k$ with $w(T\mathbb{RP}^n)\cdot w(\nu)=1$ in $H^*(\mathbb{RP}^n;\mathbb{F}_2)=\mathbb{F}_2[a]/(a^{n+1})$. Computing $w(T\mathbb{RP}^n)=(1+a)^{n+1}$ pins down $w(\nu)$, and the smallest $k$ for which a rank-$k$ bundle with this Stiefel-Whitney class exists on $\mathbb{RP}^n$ is bounded below by counting Cl-module dimensions in the geometric dimension of $\nu$. The sharpest bounds come from spin obstructions to the Cl-module structure: $\mathbb{RP}^n$ immerses in $\mathbb{R}^{2n-\alpha(n)}$ where $\alpha(n)$ is the number of $1$s in the binary expansion of $n+1$, a result of Cohen 1985 *The immersion conjecture* (Annals 122) that closes the immersion problem completely. The Lawson-Michelsohn perspective (§IV.2) is that the entire vector-field / immersion problem is *one* Cl-module bookkeeping calculation in two dressings: vector fields on $S^n$ count Cl-generators on $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$; immersion codimensions count Cl-modules on the normal-bundle classifying space. $\square$

This is the canonical example of Lawson-Michelsohn's framing principle: spin-geometric data — Cl-module classifications, Bott periodicity, J-homomorphism — controls classical geometric questions (vector fields, immersions) that look algebraic-topological at first glance.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 16 (hard, proof).** *Spinor cohomology on $c_1=0$ Calabi-Yau manifolds.*

Let $M$ be a closed Kähler manifold of complex dimension $n$ with $c_1(M)=0$ (i.e., a Calabi-Yau). Show that the sheaf cohomology of the spinor bundle splits canonically as

$$
H^*(M,\mathfrak{S}^+)\oplus H^*(M,\mathfrak{S}^-)\cong\bigoplus_{p,q}H^{p,q}(M)
$$

with $\mathfrak{S}^+=\bigoplus_{p\;\mathrm{even}}\Omega^{0,p}$ and $\mathfrak{S}^-=\bigoplus_{p\;\mathrm{odd}}\Omega^{0,p}$, and conclude that the dimension of harmonic spinors on $M$ equals the alternating sum of Hodge numbers $\sum_p(-1)^p h^{0,p}(M)$, which is the holomorphic Euler characteristic $\chi(M,\mathcal{O}_M)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On a Kähler manifold the canonical $\mathrm{Spin}^c$ structure has $\mathfrak{S}^\pm\cong\Omega^{0,\mathrm{even/odd}}$ tensored with a square root of the canonical bundle [03.09.05]. When $c_1=0$, the canonical bundle $K_M$ is topologically zero (and on a simply-connected CY, holomorphically zero), so the canonical $\mathrm{Spin}^c$ structure refines to a genuine spin structure with $\mathfrak{S}^\pm=\Omega^{0,\mathrm{even/odd}}$ on the nose. Use $D=\sqrt{2}(\bar\partial+\bar\partial^*)$ to identify harmonic spinors with $\bar\partial$-harmonic forms in $\Omega^{0,*}$, and apply Dolbeault's theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Step 1: Spin structure from $c_1=0$.** A Kähler manifold $M$ has canonical $\mathrm{Spin}^c$ structure with determinant line bundle $K_M^{-1}$ [03.09.05, Theorem D.2]. The condition $c_1(M)=0$ means $c_1(K_M^{-1})=0$, so $K_M^{-1}$ is topologically zero — it admits a square root, and the $\mathrm{Spin}^c$ structure refines to a genuine spin structure on $M$. Under this refinement,

$$
\mathfrak{S}^+=\Omega^{0,\mathrm{even}}=\bigoplus_{p\;\mathrm{even}}\Lambda^{0,p}T^*M,\qquad \mathfrak{S}^-=\Omega^{0,\mathrm{odd}}=\bigoplus_{p\;\mathrm{odd}}\Lambda^{0,p}T^*M
$$

as smooth vector bundles. The spin Dirac operator is $D=\sqrt{2}(\bar\partial+\bar\partial^*)$, mapping $\mathfrak{S}^+\to\mathfrak{S}^-$ on the chiral piece (LM §IV.11).

**Step 2: Identification of harmonic spinors with Dolbeault cohomology.** A harmonic spinor $\psi\in\ker D$ on a closed Kähler manifold satisfies both $\bar\partial\psi=0$ and $\bar\partial^*\psi=0$ (because $D^2=2(\bar\partial\bar\partial^*+\bar\partial^*\bar\partial)$ is twice the $\bar\partial$-Laplacian on $\Omega^{0,*}$ when $c_1=0$, so $D\psi=0$ iff both $\bar\partial\psi$ and $\bar\partial^*\psi$ vanish). The space of harmonic $(0,p)$-forms is canonically isomorphic to the Dolbeault cohomology $H^{0,p}(M)=H^p(M,\mathcal{O}_M)$ by Hodge theory. Combining,

$$
\ker D|_{\mathfrak{S}^+} \cong \bigoplus_{p\;\mathrm{even}}H^p(M,\mathcal{O}_M),\qquad \ker D|_{\mathfrak{S}^-}\cong\bigoplus_{p\;\mathrm{odd}}H^p(M,\mathcal{O}_M).
$$

**Step 3: Index = holomorphic Euler characteristic.**

$$
\mathrm{ind}(D^+)=\dim\ker D|_{\mathfrak{S}^+}-\dim\ker D|_{\mathfrak{S}^-}=\sum_p(-1)^p h^{0,p}(M)=\chi(M,\mathcal{O}_M).
$$

The Atiyah-Singer index theorem on the spin Dirac operator gives $\mathrm{ind}(D^+)=\widehat A(M)[M]$, while the Hirzebruch-Riemann-Roch formula on $M$ gives $\chi(M,\mathcal{O}_M)=\mathrm{Td}(M)[M]$. The identity $\widehat A(M)=\mathrm{Td}(M)$ on a Calabi-Yau (where $c_1=0$) is verified by direct expansion: $\mathrm{Td}=e^{c_1/2}\widehat A$ and $c_1=0$ collapses the exponential factor to $1$. So both routes give the same index, confirming the cohomological identification above.

**Specialisations.** For $M$ a K3 surface ($n=2$), $h^{0,0}=1$, $h^{0,1}=0$, $h^{0,2}=1$, so $\chi(K3,\mathcal{O})=2$ and $K3$ has two harmonic spinors (the parallel spinors of the Calabi-Yau holonomy [03.09.18]). For $M$ a Calabi-Yau threefold ($n=3$), $h^{0,0}=1$, $h^{0,3}=1$, $h^{0,1}=h^{0,2}=0$ (simply-connected case), so $\chi(M,\mathcal{O})=0$ — but each chiral piece has one harmonic spinor: the constant function on $\mathfrak{S}^+$ and the holomorphic volume form on $\mathfrak{S}^-$. This count matches the parallel-spinor count for $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ holonomy. $\square$

The exercise pins down the LM §IV.11.2 statement: on Calabi-Yau manifolds, *spinor cohomology equals Dolbeault cohomology*, and the harmonic-spinor count equals $\chi(\mathcal{O}_M)$. This is the formula that makes the K3 / Calabi-Yau parallel-spinor counts of [03.09.18] agree with Hodge-number bookkeeping.

</details>
