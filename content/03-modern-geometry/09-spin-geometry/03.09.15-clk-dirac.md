## Intuition [Beginner]

The Dirac operator on a spin manifold has an integer index — the analytic count of harmonic spinors of one chirality minus the other. That integer is what the Atiyah-Singer theorem computes via the Â-genus. But it is a coarse invariant: in many dimensions, the Â-genus is forced to vanish for parity reasons, and yet the Dirac operator still carries information about whether the manifold admits a metric of positive scalar curvature.

The $\mathrm{Cl}_k$-linear Dirac operator is the refinement that captures this missing information. The idea is to enrich the spinor bundle with an extra Clifford-module structure — a right action of the real Clifford algebra $\mathrm{Cl}_k$ that commutes with the Dirac operator — and to take the Clifford-equivariant index. The result is no longer an integer; it lives in the abelian group $KO_k$, the $k$-th coefficient of real K-theory, which equals $\mathbb{Z}$, $\mathbb{Z}/2$, $\mathbb{Z}/2$, or $0$ depending on $k\!\!\mod 8$.

In dimensions where $KO_k = \mathbb{Z}/2$ (notably $k\equiv 1, 2 \!\!\mod 8$), the Cl_k-linear index detects spin manifolds that admit no harmonic spinor and yet still obstruct positive scalar curvature. This is what makes Hitchin's α-invariant possible — the original 1974 application that found exotic spheres without psc metrics.

The Dirac operator now respects this extra symmetry, and its kernel decomposes into Cl_k-modules. The index, in place of an integer, is a class in the eight-fold-periodic group $KO_k$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider a closed spin manifold of dimension $1$ — a circle $S^1$ with the non-bounding (Ramond) spin structure. The ordinary Dirac operator on $S^1$ has a one-dimensional kernel of constant spinors, and the integer index is zero (positive- and negative-chirality kernels match in odd dimensions). The Â-genus vanishes too. So at the integer level, $S^1$ is invisible.

Enrich by $\mathrm{Cl}_1$: the spinor bundle of $S^1$ already carries a natural right Cl_1-action because the ordinary spinor bundle in dimension 1 is itself a Cl_1-module. The kernel of the Cl_1-linear Dirac operator on $S^1$ — one constant spinor with a graded Cl_1-action — represents a non-zero class in $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_1 = KO_1 = \mathbb{Z}/2$. So $\widehat\alpha(S^1) = 1 \in \mathbb{Z}/2$ for the non-bounding circle, and $0$ for the bounding (Neveu-Schwarz) circle.

What this tells us: the integer index sees nothing on $S^1$, but the Cl_k-linear refinement separates the two spin structures. This is the simplest example of a spin manifold whose psc-obstruction lives entirely in the mod-2 stratum $KO_1$. Hitchin's exotic-sphere examples generalise this to dimensions $8k+1$ and $8k+2$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $(M^n, g)$ be a closed Riemannian spin manifold. A **$\mathrm{Cl}_k$-linear Dirac bundle** on $M$ is a Dirac bundle $(\mathfrak{E}, c, \nabla^{\mathfrak{E}})$ in the sense of [03.09.14], together with a right action $\mathfrak{E} \otimes \mathrm{Cl}_k \to \mathfrak{E}$ that

(i) is parallel: $\nabla^{\mathfrak{E}}_X(\psi \cdot a) = (\nabla^{\mathfrak{E}}_X\psi) \cdot a$ for all $a \in \mathrm{Cl}_k$;

(ii) commutes with the Clifford action of $T^\ast M$: $c(\xi)(\psi \cdot a) = (c(\xi)\psi)\cdot a$;

(iii) is Hermitian: the right Cl_k-action preserves the inner product up to the standard involution $a \mapsto a^\ast$ on $\mathrm{Cl}_k$.

The $\mathbb{Z}/2$-grading on $\mathfrak{E}$ combines with the standard $\mathbb{Z}/2$-grading on $\mathrm{Cl}_k$ (induced by $\alpha : v \mapsto -v$) to give a graded-tensor structure. Throughout, $\mathrm{Cl}_k$ denotes the real Clifford algebra of $\mathbb{R}^{0,k}$ — the negative-definite signature in the convention $v^2 = -|v|^2$.

Notation: $\mathrm{Cl}_k$ in this unit is the *standard real Clifford algebra in $k$ generators with $v^2 = -|v|^2$*, the same algebra denoted $\mathrm{Cl}_{0,k}$ in the chessboard classification of [03.09.11]. The right-action structure here is independent of the chessboard's $(r,s)$ signature framework; only the negative-definite $\mathrm{Cl}_k$ enters [ref: TODO_REF Lawson-Michelsohn §II.7].

The **$\mathrm{Cl}_k$-linear Dirac operator** $D : \Gamma(\mathfrak{E}) \to \Gamma(\mathfrak{E})$ is the Dirac operator of the underlying Dirac bundle, automatically Cl_k-linear by assumption (ii). Its kernel $\ker D$ inherits a graded right Cl_k-module structure, and the **Clifford-index** is the class
$$
\widehat{\alpha}(D) := [\ker D] \in \widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_k := M_k / i^\ast M_{k+1},
$$
where $M_k$ is the Grothendieck group of $\mathbb{Z}/2$-graded right $\mathrm{Cl}_k$-modules and $i^\ast M_{k+1} \to M_k$ is the restriction map along $\mathrm{Cl}_k \hookrightarrow \mathrm{Cl}_{k+1}$. This is the **ABS module quotient**.

**Theorem (Atiyah-Bott-Shapiro 1964).** *There is a natural isomorphism $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_k \cong KO_k$ identifying the eight-fold periodic Clifford-module classification with the eight-fold periodic real K-theory* [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah-Bott-Shapiro 1964].

The values are
$$
KO_k \cong \mathbb{Z},\, \mathbb{Z}/2,\, \mathbb{Z}/2,\, 0,\, \mathbb{Z},\, 0,\, 0,\, 0
\qquad (k = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7 \!\!\mod 8).
$$

**The α-invariant.** When $\mathfrak{E} = \mathfrak{S}$ is the spinor bundle of an $n$-dimensional spin manifold, the Cl_n-action arises naturally from the Cl_n-module structure on the fibre $\Delta_n$ (treating the underlying vector space as a real Cl_n-module via the spin representation). The resulting Cl_n-linear Dirac operator has Clifford-index
$$
\widehat\alpha(M) := \widehat\alpha(D_{\mathfrak{S}}) \in KO_n,
$$
the **α-invariant** of the spin manifold $M$ — the foundational psc obstruction for spin manifolds whose integer Â-genus vanishes.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Cl_k-linear Atiyah-Singer index theorem; Atiyah-Singer 1971).** *Let $M^n$ be a closed Riemannian spin manifold and $\mathfrak{E}$ a $\mathrm{Cl}_k$-linear Dirac bundle on $M$. The Clifford-index $\widehat\alpha(D_{\mathfrak{E}}) \in KO_k$ equals the topological pushforward*
$$
\widehat\alpha(D_{\mathfrak{E}}) = \pi_!^{KO}\big([\sigma(D_{\mathfrak{E}})]\big),
$$
*where $[\sigma(D_{\mathfrak{E}})] \in KO^0_{\mathrm{Cl}_k}(T^\ast M)$ is the Cl_k-equivariant symbol class and $\pi_!^{KO} : KO^0_{\mathrm{Cl}_k}(T^\ast M) \to KO_k$ is the K-theoretic pushforward to a point.*

In the special case of the spin Dirac operator $D_{\mathfrak{S}}$, the formula reads
$$
\widehat\alpha(M) = \pi_!^{KO}\big([\mathfrak{S}]\big) \in KO_n,
$$
the KO-pushforward of the spinor bundle's KO-class, and is the universal psc obstruction.

**Proof sketch.** The proof has three steps, each requiring substantial input from elsewhere in the spin-geometry chapter.

*Step 1: Cl_k-equivariant analytic index.* On a closed spin manifold, the Cl_k-linear Dirac operator $D_{\mathfrak{E}}$ is elliptic and Cl_k-linear. Standard elliptic theory (cf. [03.09.06]) makes its kernel and cokernel finite-dimensional. The graded difference $[\ker D^+] - [\ker D^-] \in M_k$ is the analytic Clifford-index. Modding out by the image of $i^\ast : M_{k+1} \to M_k$ (the Cl_{k+1}-modules viewed as Cl_k-modules) yields the well-defined class $\widehat\alpha(D_{\mathfrak{E}}) \in \widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_k$. The quotient is needed because Cl_{k+1}-modules contribute zero to the Cl_k-equivariant index.

*Step 2: ABS isomorphism.* The Atiyah-Bott-Shapiro construction of 1964 identifies $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_k$ with $KO_k$ via the *ABS map* sending a graded Cl_k-module $V$ to the Thom class $[V] \in KO^0_c(\mathbb{R}^k)$ of the standard $\mathbb{R}^k$-bundle, via the Clifford-multiplication isomorphism on the Thom space. The isomorphism is natural in $k$ and respects the eight-fold Bott periodicity on both sides.

*Step 3: Topological pushforward equals analytic index.* The Cl_k-linear Atiyah-Singer theorem proves the analytic side equals the topological side via the same K-theoretic embedding-and-difference-bundle argument as in the original Atiyah-Singer index theorem [03.09.10], adapted to the Cl_k-equivariant setting. The key technical input is the Cl_k-equivariant Bott periodicity, which lets the symbol class on $T^\ast M$ be reduced to a class on a point via successive embeddings $M \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^N$ and tubular-neighbourhood Thom isomorphisms.

For the spinor-bundle case $\mathfrak{E} = \mathfrak{S}$, the symbol class simplifies by the spin Thom isomorphism $KO^0_{\mathrm{Cl}_n}(T^\ast M) \cong KO^0(M)$, and the pushforward is the canonical $\pi_!^{KO} : KO^0(M) \to KO_n$ [ref: TODO_REF Lawson-Michelsohn §III.16 + Atiyah-Singer 1971]. $\square$

**Corollary (Hitchin's α-invariant theorem, 1974).** *On a closed spin manifold $M^n$, if $M$ admits a metric of positive scalar curvature, then $\widehat\alpha(M) = 0$ in $KO_n$.* The proof is the Cl_k-linear refinement of the Lichnerowicz argument: positive scalar curvature forces $\ker D = 0$ as a Cl_k-module, so the Clifford-index vanishes.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward later units of the strand, where the same pattern is taken up at higher structure. The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

What is $KO_4$?

Real Bott periodicity: $KO_\ast = \mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/2, 0, \mathbb{Z}, 0, 0, 0$ in degrees $0$ through $7$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$KO_4 = \mathbb{Z}$. In dimension $4$ (and more generally $4 \!\!\mod 8$), the Cl_k-linear index reduces to a $\mathbb{Z}$-valued invariant. On a closed spin 4-manifold this $\mathbb{Z}$ is divisible by $2$ (Rokhlin's theorem), reflecting the deeper $\mathbb{Z}/2$-symmetry of the Cl_3-action that survives one stratum.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

State the ABS isomorphism $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_k \cong KO_k$ for $k = 0, 1, 2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_0$ is graded $\mathbb{R}$-vector spaces modulo $\mathrm{Cl}_1$-modules; $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_1$ is graded Cl_1-modules modulo Cl_2-modules; etc.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_0 = K$-theory of graded $\mathbb{R}$-vector spaces modulo those extending to graded Cl_1-modules $\cong \mathbb{Z}$, generated by $[\mathbb{R}_+] - [\mathbb{R}_-]$ (the difference of the rank-one $\pm$-graded line). This is $KO_0 = \mathbb{Z}$.

$\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_1 = \mathbb{Z}/2$, generated by the irreducible graded Cl_1-module $\mathbb{R}^{1,0} \oplus \mathbb{R}^{0,1}$ — non-zero in $M_1$, but two copies of it extend to a Cl_2-module (since Cl_2 has a single irreducible $\mathbb{Z}/2$-graded module of total real dimension 4). This is $KO_1 = \mathbb{Z}/2$.

$\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_2 = \mathbb{Z}/2$, generated by a similar argument involving the irreducible Cl_2-module not extending to a Cl_3-module. This is $KO_2 = \mathbb{Z}/2$. The pattern continues with eight-fold periodicity.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the Cl_k-linear Dirac operator $D_{\mathfrak{E}}$ is Cl_k-linear, i.e., $D_{\mathfrak{E}}(\psi \cdot a) = (D_{\mathfrak{E}}\psi)\cdot a$ for $a \in \mathrm{Cl}_k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the parallel-Cl_k axiom and the commutation of Cl_k with the Clifford action of $T^\ast M$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In a local orthonormal frame, $D_{\mathfrak{E}}\psi = \sum_j c(e^j) \nabla^{\mathfrak{E}}_{e_j}\psi$. Compute
$$
D_{\mathfrak{E}}(\psi \cdot a) = \sum_j c(e^j) \nabla^{\mathfrak{E}}_{e_j}(\psi \cdot a) = \sum_j c(e^j)\big((\nabla^{\mathfrak{E}}_{e_j}\psi)\cdot a\big),
$$
using axiom (i) (parallel Cl_k-action). The Clifford action of $T^\ast M$ commutes with the right Cl_k-action by axiom (ii), so $c(e^j)((\nabla\psi)\cdot a) = (c(e^j)\nabla\psi)\cdot a$. Summing,
$$
D_{\mathfrak{E}}(\psi\cdot a) = \Big(\sum_j c(e^j)\nabla^{\mathfrak{E}}_{e_j}\psi\Big)\cdot a = (D_{\mathfrak{E}}\psi)\cdot a. \quad \square
$$

The kernel and cokernel of $D_{\mathfrak{E}}$ are therefore graded right Cl_k-modules, and the analytic Cl_k-linear index is well-defined as a class in $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_k$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

For $\mathfrak{E} = \mathfrak{S}$, the spinor bundle of a closed spin manifold $M^n$ in dimension $n \equiv 4 \!\!\mod 8$, write the Clifford-index $\widehat\alpha(M) \in KO_n = \mathbb{Z}$ in terms of the Â-genus.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

When $KO_n = \mathbb{Z}$, the Cl_k-linear index reduces to twice the integer Dirac index by the Cl_3-extension obstruction.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In dimensions $n \equiv 4 \!\!\mod 8$, the $KO_n = \mathbb{Z}$-valued Clifford-index of the spin Dirac operator equals $2\cdot \mathrm{ind}(D^+) = 2\hat A(M)\cdot[M]$ — the factor of $2$ coming from the quaternionic structure of the spinor module (which is a Cl_3-module, contributing a factor-of-2 multiplicity in the Cl_3-quotient). So $\widehat\alpha(M) = 2\hat A(M)[M] \in \mathbb{Z}$.

This is one form of the *generalised integrality theorem*: $\hat A(M)[M]$ is even on a closed spin manifold of dimension $4 \!\!\mod 8$, the simplest non-zero output of the Cl_k-linear refinement.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Show that on a closed spin manifold of dimension $n \equiv 1, 2 \!\!\mod 8$, every metric of positive scalar curvature has $\widehat\alpha(M) = 0 \in \mathbb{Z}/2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply Lichnerowicz to the Cl_k-linear Dirac operator: positive scalar curvature forces $\ker D = 0$ as a Cl_k-module.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Bochner-Weitzenböck identity on the Cl_n-linear spinor bundle reads $D^2 = \nabla^\ast\nabla + \tfrac{1}{4}\,\mathrm{Scal}$ (the Lichnerowicz specialisation, [03.09.14]). The right Cl_n-action commutes with both terms — with the connection by parallel-Cl_n axiom, and with scalar multiplication automatically — so the equation is Cl_n-linear.

Suppose $D\psi = 0$. Then $0 = \langle D^2\psi, \psi\rangle = \|\nabla\psi\|^2 + \int_M \tfrac{1}{4}\mathrm{Scal}\,|\psi|^2\,d\mathrm{vol}$. With $\mathrm{Scal} > 0$, both terms are non-negative and the second forces $\psi = 0$. So $\ker D = 0$ as an ungraded vector space, hence as a graded Cl_n-module.

Therefore $[\ker D^+] - [\ker D^-] = 0$ in $M_n$, hence $\widehat\alpha(M) = 0$ in $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_n = KO_n = \mathbb{Z}/2$. By the contrapositive, if $\widehat\alpha(M) \neq 0$ in $\mathbb{Z}/2$, $M$ admits no metric of positive scalar curvature. $\square$

This is Hitchin's α-invariant obstruction in its mod-2 form. Hitchin 1974 found exotic spheres in dimensions $9$ and $10$ with $\widehat\alpha \neq 0$, ruling out psc metrics.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that the ABS module quotient $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_k$ has a natural ring structure under tensor product, and that the ABS isomorphism $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_k \cong KO_k$ is a ring isomorphism.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The graded tensor product of a Cl_k-module with a Cl_l-module is a Cl_{k+l}-module via $\mathrm{Cl}_k \widehat\otimes \mathrm{Cl}_l \cong \mathrm{Cl}_{k+l}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The graded Clifford-algebra isomorphism $\mathrm{Cl}_k \widehat\otimes \mathrm{Cl}_l \cong \mathrm{Cl}_{k+l}$ ([03.09.11]) makes $\bigoplus_k \widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_k$ into a graded ring under tensor product, with unit $1 \in \widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_0 = \mathbb{Z}$. The relation $i^\ast M_{k+1} \subset M_k$ is preserved under tensor product (a Cl_{k+1}-module tensored with anything is still extendable from the Cl_k-side), so the quotient inherits the ring structure.

The ABS map sends a graded Cl_k-module $V$ to the Thom class $[V] \in KO^0_c(\mathbb{R}^k)$, which under the Bott map becomes a class in $KO_k$. Tensor product of modules corresponds to external product of Thom classes, which corresponds to the K-theoretic ring structure on $\bigoplus_k KO_k = KO_\ast$. So the ABS map is a ring map.

Finally, the ABS map is an isomorphism degree-by-degree by direct calculation in degrees $0$–$7$ (eight-fold Bott periodicity matches eight-fold Clifford-module periodicity), and the ring structures match. So $\bigoplus_k \widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_k \cong KO_\ast$ as rings. $\square$

This ring-isomorphism perspective is what makes the Cl_k-linear theory genuinely K-theoretic, not just a degree-by-degree coincidence: the multiplicative structure on $KO_\ast$ (Bott class, $\eta$-class with $\eta^2 \neq 0$, etc.) translates directly into Clifford-module algebra.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

Show that the α-invariant is multiplicative under product of spin manifolds: $\widehat\alpha(M \times N) = \widehat\alpha(M) \cdot \widehat\alpha(N)$ in $KO_{m+n}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the spinor-bundle splitting $\mathfrak{S}_{M\times N} \cong \mathfrak{S}_M \widehat\otimes \mathfrak{S}_N$ and the ring-isomorphism property of ABS.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The spinor bundle of a Riemannian spin product splits as a graded tensor product $\mathfrak{S}_{M\times N} \cong \mathfrak{S}_M \,\widehat\otimes\, \mathfrak{S}_N$, with the Cl_{m+n}-action factoring as $\mathrm{Cl}_m \widehat\otimes \mathrm{Cl}_n \cong \mathrm{Cl}_{m+n}$. Under this identification, $D_{M\times N} = D_M \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes D_N$ in graded form, and the kernel splits:
$$
\ker D_{M\times N} \cong \ker D_M \,\widehat\otimes\, \ker D_N
$$
as graded Cl_{m+n}-modules. The Clifford-index of a graded tensor product equals the product in the ABS ring (Exercise 6),
$$
\widehat\alpha(M\times N) = \widehat\alpha(M) \cdot \widehat\alpha(N) \in KO_{m+n}. \quad\square
$$

This multiplicativity is what makes the α-invariant computable on examples: it is determined by primitive generators in low degrees, propagated via product. Hitchin's exotic-sphere examples in dimension $9$ are obtained by taking a Bott-manifold-like generator in $KO_8$ and multiplying by a $KO_1 = \mathbb{Z}/2$ class in dimension $1$.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Atiyah-Singer Index IV.** The original 1971 *Index of elliptic operators IV* extends the index theorem to Cl_k-linear elliptic operators on closed manifolds. The analytic-index side is the Clifford-index in $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_k$; the topological-index side is the KO-theoretic pushforward of the Cl_k-equivariant symbol class. Atiyah-Singer's argument adapts the original embedding-and-Thom-isomorphism proof to handle the Cl_k-equivariance throughout. The result subsumes the integer index theorem (the $k = 0$ case) and the mod-2 Adams-d-invariant index in dimension $4n+1, 4n+2$.

**Generalised integrality.** Specialising to the spin Dirac operator on a closed spin manifold $M^n$:

- $n \equiv 0 \!\!\mod 8$: $\widehat\alpha(M) = \hat A(M)[M] \in KO_0 = \mathbb{Z}$ (no enhancement over the integer index).
- $n \equiv 4 \!\!\mod 8$: $\widehat\alpha(M) = \tfrac{1}{2}\hat A(M)[M]\cdot 2 \in KO_4 = \mathbb{Z}$ — the *quaternionic* spinor module gives a factor-of-two divisibility, so $\hat A[M]$ is automatically even on closed spin 4k-manifolds with $k$ odd. This is the *Atiyah-Hirzebruch generalised integrality theorem*.
- $n \equiv 1, 2 \!\!\mod 8$: $\widehat\alpha(M) \in \mathbb{Z}/2$. This is the Hitchin α-invariant in its original form.
- Other dimensions: $KO_n = 0$ and $\widehat\alpha(M) = 0$ for the structural reason that the target group is zero.

**Hitchin's exotic-sphere examples.** Hitchin (1974, *Harmonic spinors*) showed that the α-invariant detects exotic differentiable structures: there exist spin manifolds in dimensions $8k+1$ and $8k+2$ that are homeomorphic but not diffeomorphic, with the α-invariant distinguishing them. As a corollary, Hitchin produced exotic spheres in these dimensions admitting no metric of positive scalar curvature — in stark contrast to the standard sphere, which carries the round metric of positive scalar curvature.

**Foundation for the psc obstruction chain.** The full psc-obstruction theory (Hitchin → Gromov-Lawson → Stolz) is built on the Cl_k-linear Dirac index. The unit [03.09.16] (forthcoming, Batch 2) develops this. **Lateral connection** to the α-invariant obstruction: the Cl_k-linear index is the foundational input from which the α-invariant, the Gromov-Lawson enlargeability obstruction, and Stolz's classification all follow [`conn:417.clk-dirac-alpha-invariant`, anchor: *α-invariant built on Cl_k-linear Dirac index in KO*].

**Cl_k-equivariant K-theory.** The full Cl_k-linear AS theorem can be stated as a natural transformation between functors $KO^0_{\mathrm{Cl}_k}(T^\ast M) \to KO_k$ and $\widehat\alpha : (\text{Cl}_k\text{-Dirac bundles on } M) \to KO_k$. This functorial perspective is necessary for the family Cl_k-linear index (Lawson-Michelsohn §III.16), where the index lives in $KO^\ast(B)$ over a parameter base $B$.

**Real vs. complex.** The Cl_k-linear theory is intrinsically real: $\mathrm{Cl}_k$ are real Clifford algebras, the index lives in real KO-theory, and the eight-fold periodicity is the real Bott periodicity. The complex analogue uses complex Clifford algebras $\mathbb{C}\ell_k$ and complex KU-theory; the periodicity collapses to two-fold ($\mathrm{Cl}^{\mathbb{C}}_{k+2} \cong \mathrm{Cl}^{\mathbb{C}}_k \otimes M_2(\mathbb{C})$, $KU_\ast = \mathbb{Z},0,\mathbb{Z},0,\ldots$), and the refinement collapses too — the complex Cl_k-linear index is just the integer index in even degrees and zero in odd.

**Synthesis.** The Cl_k-linear Dirac operator refines the complex-valued index to an element of KO-theory by exploiting the real Clifford module structure on spinor bundles. The periodicity of Clifford algebras $\mathrm{Cl}_{k+8} \cong \mathrm{Cl}_k \otimes \mathbb{R}(16)$ induces the 8-fold periodicity of real K-theory, and the Atiyah-Bott-Shapiro construction shows that the index in $KO^{-n}(\mathrm{pt})$ is the ABS map applied to the symbol class. This KO-valued index detects real-analytic phenomena invisible to the complex index—the distinction between real and quaternionic structures, the Â-genus integrality conditions, and the Ochanine genus in bordism. The resulting framework unifies the complex index theorem with real algebraic topology, showing that the index theorem is inherently a statement about Clifford module categories over smooth manifolds.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Atiyah and Singer's 1971 *The index of elliptic operators IV* (Annals of Mathematics 93, 119–138) extended their original index theorem to a Clifford-module-equivariant setting. The motivation, in the introduction to Index IV, was double: first, to refine the integer index in dimensions where it was known to be even (the Atiyah-Hirzebruch generalised integrality), and second, to recover by elliptic-operator methods the classical $\mathbb{Z}/2$-valued obstructions of Atiyah-Hirzebruch and others that had previously been accessible only through cobordism. The framing in the paper is K-theoretic throughout: "we generalise the index theorem to elliptic operators which are Clifford-linear, with the analytic index taking values in real K-theory." The Clifford-module quotient $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_k$ is named after Atiyah-Bott-Shapiro 1964 (*Clifford modules*, Topology 3 Suppl. 1) — the foundational paper that identified $\widehat{\mathfrak{M}}_k$ with $KO_k$ and made the K-theoretic interpretation possible.

The application that gave the theory teeth came three years later: Nigel Hitchin's 1974 *Harmonic spinors* (Advances in Mathematics 14, 1–55) used the Cl_k-linear refinement to produce, for the first time, spin manifolds in dimensions $1$ and $2 \!\!\mod 8$ admitting no psc metric — including exotic spheres. Hitchin's calculation specialised the abstract Cl_k-linear AS theorem to the spinor bundle and gave concrete generators of $KO_n$ for the relevant dimensions. The resulting α-invariant became the standard obstruction tool in the subsequent psc-classification programme of Gromov-Lawson, Stolz, and Schick.

The conceptual lesson is the Atiyah-Singer one: *every shadow on the integer-index side, every parity of the integer Â-genus, every cobordism torsion class — all of these admit a unified description as elements of one K-theoretic invariant, computed by one elliptic-operator construction.* What had been a decade-long sequence of separate ad-hoc integrality theorems (Rokhlin, Atiyah-Hirzebruch, Lichnerowicz) reduces to a single calculation in $KO_\ast$. Hitchin's α-invariant, the Mishchenko-Fomenko higher signature, the Stolz-Teichner programme — all live downstream of the Cl_k-linear refinement.
