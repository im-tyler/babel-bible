## Intuition [Beginner]

Think of a smooth map as a way to project or squash a surface onto a lower-dimensional target. Most input points are *regular*: the map keeps enough independent directions there. But at some points the map collapses directions — these are the *critical* points. Sard's theorem says the images of all critical points, the *critical values*, occupy zero area on the target. Throw a dart at the target and you would never hit one.

Transversality is the precise way two objects "meet nicely." A map is transverse to a shape inside the target when the map's own directions together with that shape's directions fill up all of the target. When that fails, a tiny nudge almost always fixes it. The deep content of differential topology is that meeting nicely is the *generic* case: failing to is fragile, a coincidence that disappears under an arbitrarily small perturbation.

Why bother? Because genericity turns existence into a counting argument. You can always tilt a height function so every level is clean (a Morse function). You can always nudge a curve so it crosses another rather than grazing. Sard and transversality are the engine behind every "just perturb it" theorem in geometry.

## Visual [Beginner]

A smooth map folds a sheet onto a line. The fold line (critical points) is large, but its image on the target is a single point — zero length.

![A two-dimensional sheet mapped to a one-dimensional target; the critical set is a whole curve, but its image on the target collapses to a single critical value of measure zero.](/img/sard-theorem-transversality-genericity-placeholder.svg)

The picture shows the load-bearing slogan: the critical set in the source can be large, even a whole curve, while the set of critical values in the target is vanishingly small.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the map $f(x, y) = x^2$ from the plane $\mathbb{R}^2$ to the line $\mathbb{R}$. The rate of change in the $x$-direction is $2x$, and in the $y$-direction it is $0$.

This rate vector $(2x, 0)$ vanishes exactly on the vertical line $x = 0$, the whole $y$-axis. So the critical set is the entire $y$-axis $\{(0, y)\}$ — a large, one-dimensional object.

Now look at the values. Every critical point $(0, y)$ maps to $f(0, y) = 0$. So the whole $y$-axis collapses to the single value $0$. The set of critical values is $\{0\}$, one point, which has zero length on the line.

Every other value is regular. For instance $9$ is regular: its preimage is the pair of vertical lines $x = 3$ and $x = -3$, a clean one-dimensional shape. What this tells us: critical points can be plentiful, but critical values are always negligible — and regular values give preimages that are smooth shapes of the expected size.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $f : M \to N$ be a smooth map between smooth manifolds without boundary, $\dim M = m$, $\dim N = n$. Write $df_p : T_p M \to T_{f(p)} N$ for the differential at $p$.

A point $p \in M$ is a **regular point** of $f$ when $df_p$ is surjective ($\operatorname{rank} df_p = n$); otherwise $p$ is a **critical point**. The **critical set** is
$$C_f = \{p \in M : \operatorname{rank} df_p < n\}.$$
A value $q \in N$ is a **regular value** when *every* $p \in f^{-1}(q)$ is a regular point (this is vacuous when $f^{-1}(q) = \emptyset$, so points outside the image count as regular). A value that is not regular is a **critical value**, i.e. an element of $f(C_f)$.

A subset $E \subseteq N$ has **measure zero** when, in every coordinate chart, the corresponding subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ has $n$-dimensional Lebesgue measure zero; the property is chart-independent for smooth manifolds [02.07.01]. With this language Sard's theorem reads: *for $f$ of sufficient differentiability, $f(C_f)$ has measure zero in $N$*.

Let $Z \subseteq N$ be a smooth submanifold. The map $f$ is **transverse to $Z$ at $p$**, written $f \pitchfork_p Z$, when either $f(p) \notin Z$ or
$$df_p(T_p M) + T_{f(p)} Z = T_{f(p)} N.$$
The map $f$ is **transverse to $Z$**, $f \pitchfork Z$, when $f \pitchfork_p Z$ for all $p \in M$. Two submanifolds $X, Y \subseteq M$ are **transverse**, $X \pitchfork Y$, when $T_p X + T_p Y = T_p M$ for every $p \in X \cap Y$. The expected dimension of a transverse intersection is $\dim X + \dim Y - \dim M$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Critical value, yet nice preimage.* For $f(x) = x^3$ from $\mathbb{R}$ to $\mathbb{R}$ the origin is critical ($f'(0) = 0$) and $0$ is a critical value, but $f^{-1}(0) = \{0\}$ is still a $0$-manifold. Transversality is sufficient for the preimage theorem, not necessary.
- *Failure of the preimage theorem without transversality.* For $F(x,y) = x^2 - y^3$ from $\mathbb{R}^2$ to $\mathbb{R}$ the value $0$ is critical ($dF_{(0,0)} = 0$) and $F^{-1}(0)$ is the cuspidal cubic $x^2 = y^3$, which is not a smooth submanifold at the origin.
- *Sard needs differentiability.* Whitney constructed a $C^1$ map $\mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ whose critical values contain a whole interval. The conclusion of Sard's theorem fails for $C^k$ maps when $k < \max(n - m, 0) + 1$; the $C^\infty$ hypothesis is not pedantry.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Preimage theorem).** *Let $f : M \to N$ be smooth and let $Z \subseteq N$ be a smooth submanifold without boundary. If $f \pitchfork Z$, then $f^{-1}(Z)$ is a smooth submanifold of $M$ and*
$$\operatorname{codim}_M f^{-1}(Z) = \operatorname{codim}_N Z.$$
*In particular, the preimage of a regular value $q \in N$ is either empty or a smooth submanifold of dimension $\dim M - \dim N$.*

*Proof.* Transversality, the differential, and submanifold structure are all local and preserved by diffeomorphism, so reduce to the model case. Pick a chart on $N$ at $f(p)$ in which $Z$ becomes the coordinate slice $\mathbb{R}^k \times \{0\}^{n-k} \subset \mathbb{R}^n$, and a chart on $M$ at $p$ identifying a neighbourhood with an open set in $\mathbb{R}^m$. Write $f = (f_1, \ldots, f_k, f_{k+1}, \ldots, f_n)$ and set
$$F := (f_{k+1}, \ldots, f_n) : M \to \mathbb{R}^{n-k}.$$
Then $f^{-1}(Z) = F^{-1}(0)$. The transversality condition $df_p(T_p M) + T_{f(p)} Z = T_{f(p)} N$ is, after quotienting $T_{f(p)} N$ by $T_{f(p)} Z \cong \mathbb{R}^k$, exactly the surjectivity of the composite
$$T_p M \xrightarrow{df_p} T_{f(p)} N \twoheadrightarrow T_{f(p)} N / T_{f(p)} Z \cong \mathbb{R}^{n-k},$$
which is $dF_p$. Hence $0$ is a regular value of $F$.

By the regular-value theorem (the implicit function theorem), $F^{-1}(0)$ is locally, after reordering the source coordinates, the graph of a smooth function $\mathbb{R}^{m-(n-k)} \to \mathbb{R}^{n-k}$, and is therefore a smooth submanifold of codimension $n - k = \operatorname{codim}_N Z$. The local submanifold charts glue across the atlas of $M$ because the transitions are smooth, giving $f^{-1}(Z)$ the structure of a smooth submanifold of $M$ of the claimed codimension. $\square$

**Bridge.** The preimage theorem builds toward the Thom transversality theorem of the Master tier, where transversality graduates from a property of a single map to a generic property of the whole mapping space, and appears again in [03.15.02] as the Fredholm transversality condition that makes the trajectory spaces of a Morse–Smale flow smooth manifolds. This is exactly the local model that all of differential topology reads off, and the codimension count $\operatorname{codim} Z$ is the foundational reason transversality is stable under perturbation; putting these together, every general-position argument in the subject is the preimage theorem paired with Sard.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Let $Z \subseteq N$ be a closed submanifold and $f \pitchfork Z$. Show that there is an open neighbourhood of $f$ in the strong $C^1$ topology consisting of maps also transverse to $Z$ (transversality is an open condition).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On the compact part of $f^{-1}$ of a tubular neighbourhood of $Z$, the surjectivity of $df_p + T_{f(p)}Z \to T_{f(p)}N$ has a positive margin that survives small $C^1$ perturbations.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take a tubular neighbourhood $U$ of $Z$ in $N$ with retraction $r : U \to Z$; points outside $U$ are already at positive distance from $Z$. On $K := f^{-1}(\overline{U})$, transversality says the bundle map $\varphi : TM|_K \to \nu(Z)|_{f(K)}$ to the normal bundle of $Z$, $\varphi(p,v) = df_p(v) \bmod T_{f(p)}Z$, is fibrewise surjective. Fibrewise surjectivity over a set is an open condition on the defining minors of $df$; after exhausting $K$ by compact sets, a uniform positive lower bound on the relevant minors persists for every $g$ with $\|g - f\|_{C^1}$ and $\|dg - df\|$ sufficiently small on each compact piece. Outside $\overline{U}$ the perturbed map still avoids $Z$ by the distance margin. Hence a $C^1$-neighbourhood of $f$ lies in $\pitchfork(Z)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, short-answer).**

Using Sard's theorem, explain why the set of regular values of a smooth map $f : M \to N$ is dense in $N$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The complement of the regular values is the set of critical values. What does Sard say about a measure-zero subset of a manifold?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Sard's theorem says the set of critical values $f(C_f)$ has measure zero in $N$. A measure-zero subset of a manifold has empty interior (otherwise it would contain a chart ball of positive measure). Its complement, the set of regular values, is therefore dense: every open set in $N$ contains a regular value. This density is what makes "pick a regular value" a legitimate move in proofs.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

Let $M \subset \mathbb{R}^K$ be a compact smooth submanifold. Sketch why Morse functions are dense in the space of smooth real-valued functions on $M$, using Sard.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Linear height functions $h_a(x) = \langle a, x \rangle$ parameterised by $a \in S^{K-1}$ form a family $H(x,a) = h_a(x)$; apply Sard to the projection of the relevant critical locus to the parameter sphere.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Consider $H : M \times S^{K-1} \to \mathbb{R}$, $H(x,a) = \langle a, x \rangle$. A point $(x,a)$ is critical for the map $\Phi : M \times S^{K-1} \to \mathbb{R} \times S^{K-1}$, $\Phi(x,a) = (H(x,a), a)$, precisely when $a$ is normal to $M$ at $x$ (the $M$-component of $dH$ vanishes iff $a \in (T_x M)^\perp$). For fixed $x$ the set of such normals is a submanifold of $S^{K-1}$ of dimension $K - 1 - \dim M$, so the set of "bad" parameters $a$ — those for which $h_a$ has a degenerate critical point — is the image of a smooth map from a manifold of dimension $K - 1 - \dim M + \dim M - 1 = K - 2 < K - 1$, hence has measure zero by Sard applied to the projection. For almost every $a$, the height function $h_a$ is Morse. Linear combinations then approximate any smooth function, giving density.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, conceptual).**

State the sharp differentiability hypothesis under which Sard's theorem holds for a $C^k$ map $f : \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$, and explain why the theorem fails below it.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

</details>

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Sard's theorem (smooth finite-dimensional case).** Let $f : M \to N$ be $C^\infty$. Then the set $f(C_f)$ of critical values has measure zero in $N$. The argument is by induction on the domain dimension with a rank stratification of $C_f$ and a Fubini reduction; the only analytic input is that a $C^1$ map contracts the measure of a small cube by the operator norm of its differential [ref: TODO_REF Sard 1942]. The sharp $C^k$ version, due to Sard and refined by Morse, requires $k \ge \max(m - n, 0) + 1$ derivatives and is best possible.

**Thom (first) transversality theorem.** Let $Z \subseteq N$ be a smooth submanifold. The set of smooth maps $f : M \to N$ transverse to $Z$ is residual (a countable intersection of open dense sets) in the strong $C^\infty$ topology on $C^\infty(M, N)$. Combined with openness of transversality for closed $Z$, transverse maps form a dense open set: a generic smooth map is transverse to any prescribed countable family of submanifolds. The proof reduces, via charts and partitions of unity, to the parametric transversality theorem, which is itself Sard applied to the projection of the transverse preimage of $Z$ under a universal evaluation map [ref: TODO_REF Thom 1956].

**Parametric transversality.** If $F : M \times S \to N$ is transverse to $Z$, then for almost every $s \in S$ the slice $f_s(\cdot) = F(\cdot, s)$ is transverse to $Z$. Concretely, $W := F^{-1}(Z)$ is a submanifold of $M \times S$ by the preimage theorem, and $f_s \pitchfork Z$ exactly when $s$ is a regular value of the projection $\pi_S : W \to S$; the bad parameters are the critical values of $\pi_S$, which have measure zero by Sard. This is the mechanism that converts "exists a good perturbation" into "almost every perturbation is good."

**Density of Morse functions.** On a compact manifold every smooth function is uniformly approximated by a Morse function. The proof applies Sard to the family of height functions on an embedding $M \hookrightarrow \mathbb{R}^K$: the parameters $a \in S^{K-1}$ for which the height $h_a(x) = \langle a, x \rangle$ has a degenerate critical point form the critical values of a smooth map whose domain has smaller dimension than $S^{K-1}$, hence have measure zero. The output is a dense supply of functions with non-degenerate critical points — the input hypothesis for all of Morse theory [03.15.08].

**Whitney embedding theorems.** Every smooth $n$-manifold embeds into $\mathbb{R}^{2n+1}$ (weak) and into $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ (strong). The proof embeds $M$ into some $\mathbb{R}^K$ by partitions of unity, then projects generically: the set of linear projections $\mathbb{R}^K \to \mathbb{R}^{2n+1}$ that fail to be immersions or injective is the image of a smooth map from a lower-dimensional manifold (jets of pairs, tangent directions), hence has measure zero by Sard. Iterating with transversality to secant-tangent varieties brings the target down to $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ [ref: TODO_REF Whitney 1944]. The abstract manifold definition and the submanifold-of-Euclidean-space definition coincide.

**Multi-jet transversality (Thom).** Let $J^r(M, N)$ be the space of $r$-jets of smooth maps and $J^r_k(M,N)$ the $k$-fold multi-jet space. For any submanifold $W \subseteq J^r_k(M,N)$, the set of maps whose $k$-fold $r$-jet extension is transverse to $W$ is residual. This is the engine of singularity theory: it says that prescribed local singularity types either occur stably and generically or not at all, and underlies the classification of stable maps and Thom's transversality-based foundations for catastrophe theory.

**Baire genericity.** Because $C^\infty(M, N)$ with the strong topology is a Baire space, residual equals dense, and every countable list of transversality requirements (transverse to $Z_1$, to $Z_2$, Morse, self-transverse, ...) is met simultaneously by a residual set of maps. This is the precise sense in which "general position" is the default state of affairs in differential topology, and the reason existence-by-genericity proofs are legitimate.

**Synthesis.** Sard's theorem is the foundational reason transversality is generic rather than exceptional: the critical values of an evaluation map have measure zero, so the transverse parameters form a dense open complement, and this is exactly the Baire statement that transverse maps are residual. The central insight is that every failure of good behaviour — a tangency, a rank drop, a degenerate critical point — is detected as the image of a critical locus, hence carries codimension and is avoidable by an arbitrarily small perturbation. Putting these together, Thom's transversality theorem generalises from submanifolds of the target to multi-jet data, identifying the transverse condition with openness plus density in the Whitney topology. The bridge is that each existence-by-genericity theorem in geometry — Morse functions [03.15.08], Whitney embeddings, Smale transversality, Fredholm-regular moduli spaces [03.15.02] — is a single instance of the Sard-plus-transversality pattern, and the construction appears again in [03.04.06], where transversal intersection realises the de Rham cup product under Poincaré duality.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Sard's theorem, smooth case).** *Let $f : U \to \mathbb{R}^n$ be a $C^\infty$ map, $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$ open. Then $f(C_f)$ has measure zero in $\mathbb{R}^n$.*

*Proof.* Stratify the critical set by rank: $C_f = \bigcup_{0 \le r < n} C_r$ where $C_r = \{x \in U : \operatorname{rank} df_x = r\}$. We prove $f(C_r)$ has measure zero for every $r < n$.

*Claim (rank-$r$ stratum).* For every compact $K \subseteq C_r$, $f(K)$ has measure zero. Cover $K$ by finitely many boxes on each of which, after a $C^1$ change of source and target coordinates, $f$ takes the form $f(x_1, \ldots, x_m) = (x_1, \ldots, x_r, g_{r+1}(x), \ldots, g_n(x))$ with $\partial g_i / \partial x_j|_K = 0$ for $j \le r$ (constant-rank normal form). On a box of side $\delta$, the last $n - r$ coordinates of $f(K)$ vary, in the transverse directions $x_{r+1}, \ldots, x_m$, by at most $\varepsilon(\delta) \cdot \delta$ with $\varepsilon(\delta) \to 0$ as $\delta \to 0$ (uniform continuity of $\partial g_i / \partial x_j$); the first $r$ coordinates fill an $r$-dimensional face.

The image box has volume $\le C \cdot \delta^r \cdot (\varepsilon(\delta)\delta)^{n-r}$. Summing over the $O(\delta^{-(m-r)})$ transverse boxes covering the transverse slice gives total volume $\le C' \cdot \varepsilon(\delta)^{n-r} \cdot \delta^{m - r - (m-r)(n-r)} \cdot \delta^{(m-r)} \to 0$, so $f(K)$ has measure zero for $r < n$ when $n - r \ge 1$ and the rank-$r$ slice has transverse dimension $m - r$; the induction on $m$ (Fubini across one source coordinate, base case $m = 1$ the mean-value estimate) closes the argument for $r \le n - 2$ where the rank normal form alone does not suffice. The details of the covering estimate are in [ref: TODO_REF Guillemin & Pollack Ch. 2 §3]. Since $\mathbb{R}^m$ is a countable union of compact boxes, $f(C_f)$ is a countable union of measure-zero sets, hence measure zero. The manifold version follows by taking a countable atlas on $N$. $\square$

**Proposition (transversality is open and dense).** *Let $Z \subseteq N$ be a closed submanifold. The set $\pitchfork(Z) \subseteq C^\infty(M, N)$ is open in the strong $C^\infty$ topology and dense.*

*Proof.* Openness is Exercise 5 globalised to the strong topology. For density, fix $f \in C^\infty(M,N)$, a locally finite cover $\{U_i\}$ of $M$ with $f(U_i)$ in a chart of $N$, and a family of perturbation parameters $s_i \in \mathbb{R}^q$ carried in disjoint small balls $V_i \subset U_i$. Build $F : M \times S \to N$ (with $S = \prod_i \mathbb{R}^q$ restricted to a small parameter set) so that on $V_i$ the map $F(\cdot, s_i)$ ranges through a $q$-dimensional normal spread to $Z$, and $F(\cdot, 0) = f$; choose $q = \operatorname{codim} Z$ so that $F \pitchfork Z$ on each $V_i$ and everywhere outside by construction. By the parametric transversality theorem, the set of parameters $s$ with $F_s \pitchfork Z$ has full measure in $S$, in particular is dense; picking such a parameter with $\|s\|$ small makes $F_s$ an arbitrarily small (strong-$C^\infty$) perturbation of $f$ that is transverse to $Z$. Hence $\pitchfork(Z)$ is dense. $\square$

**Proposition (regular values are dense).** *For $f : M \to N$ smooth, the set of regular values is dense in $N$.*

*Proof.* By Sard, $f(C_f)$ has measure zero. A measure-zero subset of an $n$-manifold has empty interior: were some chart ball $B \subseteq f(C_f)$, pulling back to $\mathbb{R}^n$ would give a set of positive Lebesgue measure contained in a measure-zero set, a contradiction. Therefore $N \setminus f(C_f)$, the set of regular values, is dense. $\square$

**Proposition (preimage theorem — intersection form).** *If $X, Y \subseteq M$ are transverse submanifolds, then $X \cap Y$ is a submanifold with $\operatorname{codim}(X \cap Y) = \operatorname{codim} X + \operatorname{codim} Y$, or is empty.*

*Proof.* Apply the preimage theorem to the inclusion $\iota : X \hookrightarrow M$ and the submanifold $Y \subseteq M$: transversality $X \pitchfork Y$ is exactly $\iota \pitchfork Y$, so $\iota^{-1}(Y) = X \cap Y$ is a submanifold of $X$ of codimension $\operatorname{codim}_M Y$, equivalently a submanifold of $M$ of codimension $\operatorname{codim} X + \operatorname{codim} Y$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Smooth manifold [03.02.01].** This unit is the differential-topology layer on top of the smooth-manifold foundation: every statement here is a theorem about smooth maps between the manifolds defined there, and the differential $df_p$ is read off from the tangent space constructed in the sibling unit.

- **Morse homology [03.15.08].** The density of Morse functions proved above is the existence input for the Morse complex; without Sard there is no guarantee that a generic real-valued function has only non-degenerate critical points, and the Morse homology theorem has no chain group to build on.

- **Fredholm transversality and trajectory spaces [03.15.02].** The moduli spaces of Morse–Smale flow lines are smooth manifolds of the expected dimension precisely because a Fredholm map is made transverse to zero by a generic perturbation — the infinite-dimensional echo of the finite-dimensional transversality genericity established here.

- **De Rham cohomology [03.04.06].** Transversal intersection of cycles realises the cup product at the level of differential forms and underlies the de Rham pairing made concrete by Poincaré duality; Sard guarantees that representatives can always be moved into general position so the intersections are genuine submanifolds.

- **Whitney immersion obstruction [03.06.16].** The existence side of Whitney's theorems (immersion and embedding into Euclidean space of controlled dimension) is a Sard argument on the space of projections, while the obstruction side refines the dimension count via characteristic classes; this unit supplies the genericity half of that pairing.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Arthur Sard proved the eponymous theorem in 1939 (published 1942) in *The measurement of critical values of differentiable maps* [ref: TODO_REF Sard 1942], sharpening earlier observations of Morse on the negligibility of critical values for generic real-valued functions. Sard's formulation gave the optimal differentiability threshold $k \ge \max(m - n, 0) + 1$ and the rank-stratified proof that remains the standard route.

René Thom's 1956 paper *Quelques propriétés globales des variétés différentiables* [ref: TODO_REF Thom 1956] promoted Sard's measure-zero statement into a genericity theorem for the space of smooth maps: transversality to any prescribed submanifold, and to any prescribed singularity type via multi-jets, holds on a residual set. This converted "general position" from an ad hoc geometric trick into a theorem, and founded the modern theory of singularities of differentiable mappings developed by Thom, Mather, and Arnold.

Hassler Whitney's embedding and immersion theorems of 1936 and 1944 [ref: TODO_REF Whitney 1944] were both among the first applications of the new differential-topological machinery and a driving force behind it; his $C^1$ counterexample showed simultaneously that the smoothness hypothesis in Sard is sharp and that low-differentiability geometry harbours pathologies absent from the $C^\infty$ category. The synthesis of Sard, transversality, and genericity into the working toolkit of differential topology was completed in the expositions of Milnor (1965) and Guillemin–Pollack (1974), and systematised in Hirsch's 1976 treatise.
