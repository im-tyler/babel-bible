## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

This pack drills the core machinery of the exterior calculus: the algebra of alternating forms and the wedge product; pullback $f^*$ and its commutation with $d$; the exterior derivative; and the generalized Stokes theorem $\int_M d\omega=\int_{\partial M}\omega$ together with its three classical specializations — Green's theorem, the Kelvin-Stokes curl theorem, and the divergence theorem. The latter half exercises de Rham cohomology computations (closed-versus-exact, the angle form, punctured spaces) and integration on oriented manifolds.

The problems — three easy, four medium, three hard — follow Shifrin's Chapter 8 in spirit, with one cohomology problem aligned to Bott-Tu and one polynomial-form computation aligned to the Arnold-Falk-Winther de Rham complex (the bridge to finite element exterior calculus). Each problem has a hint and a full solution. The pack is read alongside its prerequisite units.

Conventions: forms live on an oriented manifold; $d$ is the unique antiderivation with $d^2=0$ that restricts to the differential on functions; pullback satisfies $f^*(d\omega)=d(f^*\omega)$ and $f^*(\alpha\wedge\beta)=f^*\alpha\wedge f^*\beta$; and $\partial M$ carries the induced (outward-normal-first / Stokes) orientation, so that the generalized Stokes theorem holds with no sign correction.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

We work one problem in full as an exemplar. The remaining problems use the same problem/hint/answer structure.

**Lead exercise.** *Derive the classical divergence theorem from the generalized Stokes theorem.*

**Solution.** Let $\Omega\subset\mathbb{R}^3$ be a compact region with smooth boundary surface $S=\partial\Omega$, and let $\mathbf{F}=(P,Q,R)$ be a smooth vector field. Associate to $\mathbf{F}$ the 2-form

$$
\omega_{\mathbf{F}}=P\,dy\wedge dz+Q\,dz\wedge dx+R\,dx\wedge dy.
$$

Its exterior derivative is

$$
d\omega_{\mathbf{F}}=\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}+\frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}+\frac{\partial R}{\partial z}\right)dx\wedge dy\wedge dz=(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F})\,dV,
$$

since, for instance, $d(P\,dy\wedge dz)=\frac{\partial P}{\partial x}\,dx\wedge dy\wedge dz$ (the $dy$- and $dz$-derivatives wedge to zero against the repeated factors).

The generalized Stokes theorem gives $\int_\Omega d\omega_{\mathbf{F}}=\int_{\partial\Omega}\omega_{\mathbf{F}}$. The left side is $\int_\Omega(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F})\,dV$. On the right, restricting $\omega_{\mathbf{F}}$ to $S$ with its outward orientation reproduces the flux integral: $\omega_{\mathbf{F}}|_S=\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dA$, where $\mathbf{n}$ is the outward unit normal and $dA$ the surface area form. Hence

$$
\int_\Omega(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F})\,dV=\int_S\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dA,
$$

the divergence theorem. $\square$

The same pattern produces the other classical theorems: Green's theorem is generalized Stokes for a 1-form on a planar region, and the Kelvin-Stokes curl theorem is generalized Stokes for a 1-form on a surface (Exercise 6). The single statement $\int_M d\omega=\int_{\partial M}\omega$ unifies the three vector-calculus integral theorems, each recovered by choosing the dimension and the form-degree.

## Exercises [Intermediate]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).** *Wedge of two 1-forms.*

Let $\alpha=a_1\,dx+a_2\,dy+a_3\,dz$ and $\beta=b_1\,dx+b_2\,dy+b_3\,dz$. Compute $\alpha\wedge\beta$ and identify its coefficients with a cross product.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use antisymmetry $dx\wedge dy=-dy\wedge dx$ and $dx\wedge dx=0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Expanding bilinearly and using $dx_i\wedge dx_i=0$ and antisymmetry,

$$
\alpha\wedge\beta=(a_1b_2-a_2b_1)\,dx\wedge dy+(a_2b_3-a_3b_2)\,dy\wedge dz+(a_3b_1-a_1b_3)\,dz\wedge dx.
$$

The three coefficients are exactly the components of the cross product $\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}$, with $(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b})_z=a_1b_2-a_2b_1$ paired to $dx\wedge dy$, and cyclically. Under the identification of 2-forms in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with vector fields, the wedge of 1-forms *is* the cross product. This is why $d$ of a 1-form recovers the curl (Exercise 6).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).** *Pullback of a 1-form.*

Let $f\colon\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}^2$, $f(u,v)=(u^2-v^2,\,2uv)$, with target coordinates $(x,y)$. Compute $f^*(dx)$, $f^*(dy)$, and $f^*(dx\wedge dy)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$f^*(dx)=d(x\circ f)$, and $f^*(dx\wedge dy)=(\det Df)\,du\wedge dv$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $x\circ f=u^2-v^2$ and $y\circ f=2uv$,

$$
f^*(dx)=d(u^2-v^2)=2u\,du-2v\,dv,\qquad f^*(dy)=d(2uv)=2v\,du+2u\,dv.
$$

Wedging,

$$
f^*(dx\wedge dy)=(2u\,du-2v\,dv)\wedge(2v\,du+2u\,dv)=(4u^2+4v^2)\,du\wedge dv,
$$

so $f^*(dx\wedge dy)=(\det Df)\,du\wedge dv$ with $\det Df=4(u^2+v^2)$. This is the Jacobian factor of the change-of-variables formula, here arising as the pullback of the area form by the complex squaring map $w\mapsto w^2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, symbolic).** *$d^2=0$ on a 1-form.*

Verify directly that $d(d\alpha)=0$ for the 1-form $\alpha=f\,dx+g\,dy$ on $\mathbb{R}^2$, given $f,g\in C^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $d\alpha$, then $d$ of that. Equality of mixed partials forces the cancellation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

First $d\alpha=df\wedge dx+dg\wedge dy=\big(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\,dy\big)\wedge dx+\big(\frac{\partial g}{\partial x}\,dx\big)\wedge dy=\big(\frac{\partial g}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\big)\,dx\wedge dy$ (the $dx\wedge dx$ and $dy\wedge dy$ terms vanish).

Then $d(d\alpha)=d\big(\frac{\partial g}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\big)\wedge dx\wedge dy$. Any 1-form wedged with $dx\wedge dy$ on $\mathbb{R}^2$ is a 3-form, which is zero in two variables. So $d(d\alpha)=0$ automatically here; more structurally, the coefficient is a function and $dh\wedge dx\wedge dy=0$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

To see $d^2=0$ where it is not automatic, note the engine is equality of mixed partials: $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x\,\partial y}=\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y\,\partial x}$, which is exactly what makes $d^2=0$ hold in every dimension for $C^2$ forms.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *Pullback commutes with $d$.*

Let $f\colon M\to N$ be smooth and $\omega$ a $k$-form on $N$. Prove $f^*(d\omega)=d(f^*\omega)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reduce to coordinates. It suffices to check on $\omega=h\,dy_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dy_{i_k}$ and on functions, where it is the chain rule.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Both sides are linear and local, so check on a monomial $\omega=h\,dy_{I}$ with $dy_I=dy_{i_1}\wedge\cdots\wedge dy_{i_k}$ in local coordinates $y$ on $N$.

**On functions ($k=0$):** $f^*(dh)=f^*\big(\sum_j\frac{\partial h}{\partial y_j}\,dy_j\big)=\sum_j(\frac{\partial h}{\partial y_j}\circ f)\,d(y_j\circ f)=d(h\circ f)=d(f^*h)$, which is precisely the chain rule.

**General $k$:** Using $f^*(\alpha\wedge\beta)=f^*\alpha\wedge f^*\beta$ and $f^*(dy_I)=d(y_{i_1}\circ f)\wedge\cdots$, note $f^*(dy_I)$ is itself exact, hence closed: $d(f^*dy_I)=0$ since $f^*dy_{i_j}=d(y_{i_j}\circ f)$ and $d^2=0$. Then
$$
d(f^*\omega)=d\big((h\circ f)\,f^*dy_I\big)=d(h\circ f)\wedge f^*dy_I=f^*(dh)\wedge f^*(dy_I)=f^*(dh\wedge dy_I)=f^*(d\omega),
$$
using the function case in the middle step. So $f^*$ and $d$ commute, which is what makes $f^*$ a chain map on the de Rham complex and induces $f^*\colon H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(N)\to H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).** *Closed but not exact: the angle form.*

On $\mathbb{R}^2\setminus\{0\}$ let $\omega=\frac{-y\,dx+x\,dy}{x^2+y^2}$. Show $d\omega=0$, compute $\oint_{S^1}\omega$, and conclude $\omega$ is not exact.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In polar coordinates $\omega=d\theta$ locally. An exact form integrates to zero around any loop.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $P=\frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}$, $Q=\frac{x}{x^2+y^2}$. A direct computation gives $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}=\frac{y^2-x^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2}=\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$, so $d\omega=\big(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\big)dx\wedge dy=0$. Thus $\omega$ is closed.

In polar coordinates $x=r\cos\theta$, $y=r\sin\theta$, one checks $\omega=d\theta$ (locally). Integrating around the unit circle, parametrise $\theta\in[0,2\pi)$:

$$
\oint_{S^1}\omega=\int_0^{2\pi}d\theta=2\pi\neq 0.
$$

If $\omega=df$ were exact, Stokes (or the fundamental theorem on the closed loop) would force $\oint_{S^1}\omega=0$. Since the integral is $2\pi$, $\omega$ is closed but not exact: $[\omega]$ is a nonzero class generating $H^1_{\mathrm{dR}}(\mathbb{R}^2\setminus\{0\})\cong\mathbb{R}$. The obstruction is the puncture; $\theta$ is not a single-valued function on the punctured plane.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).** *Kelvin-Stokes from generalized Stokes.*

Let $S\subset\mathbb{R}^3$ be a compact oriented surface with boundary curve $\partial S$, and $\mathbf{F}=(P,Q,R)$ a vector field. Derive the curl theorem $\oint_{\partial S}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}=\iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dA$ from $\int_S d\omega=\int_{\partial S}\omega$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take the work 1-form $\omega=P\,dx+Q\,dy+R\,dz$. Show $d\omega$ is the flux 2-form of $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\omega=P\,dx+Q\,dy+R\,dz$, the 1-form dual to $\mathbf{F}$, so $\int_{\partial S}\omega=\oint_{\partial S}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$.

Compute $d\omega$. Each term contributes, e.g. $d(P\,dx)=\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\,dy\wedge dx+\frac{\partial P}{\partial z}\,dz\wedge dx$. Collecting all terms and using antisymmetry,

$$
d\omega=\Big(\tfrac{\partial R}{\partial y}-\tfrac{\partial Q}{\partial z}\Big)dy\wedge dz+\Big(\tfrac{\partial P}{\partial z}-\tfrac{\partial R}{\partial x}\Big)dz\wedge dx+\Big(\tfrac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\tfrac{\partial P}{\partial y}\Big)dx\wedge dy.
$$

The three coefficients are exactly the components of $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$, so $d\omega=\omega_{\nabla\times\mathbf{F}}$, the flux 2-form of the curl. Restricting to $S$ with its orientation gives $\int_S d\omega=\iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dA$.

Generalized Stokes $\int_S d\omega=\int_{\partial S}\omega$ then reads

$$
\iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dA=\oint_{\partial S}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r},
$$

the Kelvin-Stokes curl theorem. Green's theorem is the planar case $R=0$, $S\subset\{z=0\}$, where the curl reduces to $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}-\frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, numeric).** *De Rham cohomology of the 2-torus.*

Compute $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(T^2)$ and exhibit generators as explicit forms.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$T^2=S^1\times S^1$. Use the Künneth formula, or write down $d\theta_1$, $d\theta_2$, $d\theta_1\wedge d\theta_2$ and check they are closed and non-exact.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Use angular coordinates $\theta_1,\theta_2$ on $T^2=S^1\times S^1$ (each $\theta_i$ defined mod $2\pi$; the 1-forms $d\theta_i$ are globally defined and closed, though $\theta_i$ is not single-valued).

By the Künneth formula $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(T^2)=H^*(S^1)\otimes H^*(S^1)$:

$$
H^0=\mathbb{R}\ \langle 1\rangle,\quad H^1=\mathbb{R}^2\ \langle[d\theta_1],[d\theta_2]\rangle,\quad H^2=\mathbb{R}\ \langle[d\theta_1\wedge d\theta_2]\rangle.
$$

Each generator is closed (every $d\theta_i$ is closed and their wedge is too). None is exact: $\oint_{\gamma_i}d\theta_j=2\pi\delta_{ij}$ over the two generating loops shows $[d\theta_1],[d\theta_2]$ are independent and nonzero, and $\int_{T^2}d\theta_1\wedge d\theta_2=(2\pi)^2\neq 0$ shows $[d\theta_1\wedge d\theta_2]\neq 0$. The Betti numbers $(1,2,1)$ match the singular homology of the torus and have alternating sum $\chi(T^2)=0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Poincaré lemma via the homotopy operator.*

Prove the Poincaré lemma: every closed $k$-form ($k\geq 1$) on a star-shaped open set $U\subset\mathbb{R}^n$ is exact.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Build a homotopy operator $K$ with $dK+Kd=\mathrm{id}$ on positive-degree forms, by integrating along the radial contraction $H(t,x)=tx$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Assume $U$ is star-shaped about the origin, so $H\colon[0,1]\times U\to U$, $H(t,x)=tx$, is a smooth homotopy from the constant map (at $t=0$) to $\mathrm{id}_U$ (at $t=1$). For a $k$-form $\omega$, define the homotopy operator $K\omega$ by pulling back $\omega$ under $H$ and integrating out the $dt$ direction:

$$
(K\omega)=\int_0^1\iota_{\partial_t}\,(H^*\omega)\,dt,
$$

a $(k-1)$-form on $U$, where $\iota_{\partial_t}$ contracts the $dt$ factor.

The standard computation (Cartan's formula $\mathcal{L}_{\partial_t}=d\iota_{\partial_t}+\iota_{\partial_t}d$ applied to $H^*\omega$, integrated over $t\in[0,1]$) yields the **homotopy identity**

$$
dK\omega+K\,d\omega=H_1^*\omega-H_0^*\omega=\omega-0=\omega\qquad(k\geq 1),
$$

since $H_1=\mathrm{id}$ and $H_0$ is constant (so $H_0^*\omega=0$ for $k\geq 1$).

Now if $\omega$ is closed, $d\omega=0$, so the identity collapses to $\omega=dK\omega$. Thus $\omega$ is exact, with explicit primitive $K\omega$. Hence $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(U)=0$ for $k\geq 1$ on any star-shaped (in particular convex) $U$. This is the local vanishing that makes de Rham cohomology a global invariant: on any manifold, a good cover is one all of whose intersections are contractible, hence have vanishing positive-degree cohomology by this lemma.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, numeric).** *Solid angle and the degree of the Gauss map.*

Let $\sigma=\frac{x\,dy\wedge dz+y\,dz\wedge dx+z\,dx\wedge dy}{(x^2+y^2+z^2)^{3/2}}$ on $\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\{0\}$. Show $d\sigma=0$ and compute $\int_{S^2}\sigma$ over the unit sphere.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\sigma$ restricts to the area form on the unit sphere. Closedness is a direct (if tedious) computation; the integral is the total solid angle.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On the unit sphere $r=1$ the denominator is $1$ and $\sigma|_{S^2}=x\,dy\wedge dz+y\,dz\wedge dx+z\,dx\wedge dy$, which is exactly the standard area 2-form of $S^2$ (the contraction $\iota_{\mathbf{r}}(dx\wedge dy\wedge dz)$ restricted to the sphere). Hence

$$
\int_{S^2}\sigma=\mathrm{Area}(S^2)=4\pi.
$$

Closedness: $\sigma$ is the pullback of the area form under the Gauss map $x\mapsto x/|x|$, and away from the origin $d\sigma=0$ by a direct computation (each term's $r^{-3}$ scaling cancels the polynomial derivatives; equivalently $\sigma$ is the flux form of the inverse-square field $\mathbf{x}/|\mathbf{x}|^3$, which is divergence-free off the origin).

Because $\int_{S^2}\sigma=4\pi\neq 0$, $\sigma$ is closed but not exact on $\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\{0\}$, generating $H^2_{\mathrm{dR}}(\mathbb{R}^3\setminus\{0\})\cong\mathbb{R}$. Normalising by $4\pi$ gives the generator measuring the **degree** of a map into $S^2$ via $\frac{1}{4\pi}\int f^*\sigma$ — the three-dimensional analogue of the winding number from Exercise 5.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, symbolic).** *Polynomial de Rham complex on the line: the Koszul bridge to FEEC.*

On $\mathbb{R}^1$ the polynomial spaces are $\mathcal{P}_r\Lambda^0=\mathcal{P}_r$ (polynomials of degree $\leq r$) and $\mathcal{P}_r\Lambda^1=\mathcal{P}_r\,dx$. Show $d\colon\mathcal{P}_r\Lambda^0\to\mathcal{P}_{r-1}\Lambda^1$ together with the Koszul map $\kappa\colon\mathcal{P}_{r-1}\Lambda^1\to\mathcal{P}_r\Lambda^0$, $\kappa(p\,dx)=\int$-style contraction, realise the Poincaré lemma at polynomial level, and verify the dimension count.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On $\mathbb{R}^1$ the Koszul operator is $\kappa(p(x)\,dx)=x\,p(x)$ (contraction with the Euler/radial vector field). Use $d\kappa+\kappa d=(r+k)\cdot\mathrm{id}$ on homogeneous pieces.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In one variable $d(p(x))=p'(x)\,dx$, and the Koszul (contraction-with-$x\,\partial_x$) operator is $\kappa(q(x)\,dx)=x\,q(x)$, a $0$-form. On a homogeneous polynomial $x^m$ (degree $m$, form-degree $0$): $\kappa\,d(x^m)=\kappa(m\,x^{m-1}\,dx)=m\,x^m$, and $d\kappa(x^m)=0$ since $\kappa$ of a $0$-form is $0$. So $(d\kappa+\kappa d)(x^m)=m\,x^m=(m+k)\,x^m$ with $k=0$. On a 1-form $x^m\,dx$: $\kappa(x^m\,dx)=x^{m+1}$, $d\kappa(x^m\,dx)=(m+1)x^m\,dx$, and $\kappa\,d(x^m\,dx)=0$, giving $(d\kappa+\kappa d)=(m+1)\,\mathrm{id}=(m+k)\,\mathrm{id}$ with $k=1$. This is the **Koszul homotopy identity** $d\kappa+\kappa d=(\deg+\text{form-degree})\,\mathrm{id}$, the polynomial Poincaré lemma: on homogeneous pieces of positive total degree it is invertible, so the polynomial de Rham complex is exact there.

Dimension check via the AFW formula $\dim\mathcal{P}_r\Lambda^k(\mathbb{R}^n)=\binom{r+n}{r+k}\binom{r+k}{k}$ with $n=1$: $\dim\mathcal{P}_r\Lambda^0=\binom{r+1}{r}\binom{r}{0}=r+1$ (the polynomials $1,x,\dots,x^r$) and $\dim\mathcal{P}_r\Lambda^1=\binom{r+1}{r+1}\binom{r+1}{1}=r+1$ ($dx,x\,dx,\dots,x^r\,dx$). The map $d\colon\mathcal{P}_r\Lambda^0\to\mathcal{P}_{r-1}\Lambda^1$ has rank $r$ (kernel the constants), and the reduced space $\mathcal{P}_r^-\Lambda^1=\mathcal{P}_{r-1}\Lambda^1$ has dimension $r$, matching $\dim\mathcal{P}_r^-\Lambda^k=\binom{r+n-1}{r+k-1}\binom{r+k-1}{k}$ at $n=1,k=1$. This homogeneous Koszul splitting is the algebraic engine behind the finite element exterior calculus spaces.

</details>

</aside>
