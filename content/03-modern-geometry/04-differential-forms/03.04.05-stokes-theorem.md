## Intuition [Beginner]

Stokes' theorem is the high-dimensional generalisation of the fundamental theorem of calculus. It says: integrating a form over the boundary of a region equals integrating its exterior derivative over the region itself.

This single identity unifies several classical theorems of vector calculus: the fundamental theorem of calculus (in one dimension), Green's theorem (in two), the classical Stokes theorem about line integrals around a closed curve equaling a flux integral over a bounded surface (in three), and the divergence theorem (in three, fluxes through closed surfaces equal volume integrals of divergence).

Stokes' theorem is the geometric structural identity behind much of differential calculus on manifolds and behind the entire de Rham cohomology theory.

## Visual [Beginner]

A 2-form integrated over a region equals a 1-form integrated around the region's boundary. Visualise the boundary curve and the area enclosed; the theorem relates the two integrals.

![A region with its boundary curve; the integral of a form over the region equals the integral of a related form around the boundary.](/img/stokes-theorem-placeholder.svg)

The picture works in any dimension, with $k$-dimensional boundaries of $(k+1)$-dimensional regions — and with $k$-forms.

## Worked example [Beginner]

In one dimension, Stokes' theorem applied to an interval (call it from $a$ to $b$) recovers the fundamental theorem of calculus. The "boundary" of the interval consists of just two points — its endpoints, with the right one counted positively and the left one counted negatively. For a smooth function $f$, the rule "integrate $f'$ over the interval" equals "evaluate $f$ at the right endpoint minus the left endpoint." That's exactly the fundamental theorem.

In two dimensions, Stokes specialises to Green's theorem: integrating the *curl* of a vector field over a 2D region equals integrating the field tangentially around the boundary curve.

In three dimensions, Stokes specialises to two classical theorems. Integrating the curl of a vector field over a 2D surface equals integrating the field around the bounding curve (the "classical Stokes theorem"). Integrating the divergence of a vector field over a 3D region equals integrating the field's normal flux through the bounding surface (the divergence theorem).

The same single theorem becomes the fundamental theorem, Green's, classical Stokes, and divergence — depending on which dimension and which form-degree you choose. Each "boundary contribution" comes from the orientation rule: the boundary inherits an induced orientation from the bulk.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $M$ be a smooth oriented $n$-manifold, possibly with boundary $\partial M$ [03.02.01]. Let $\omega \in \Omega^{n-1}(M)$ be a smooth $(n-1)$-form with compact support [03.04.02], and let $d\omega \in \Omega^n(M)$ be its exterior derivative [03.04.04]. Integration of differential forms on oriented manifolds [03.04.03] gives well-defined values $\int_M d\omega$ and $\int_{\partial M} \omega$.

**Stokes' theorem.** Under these hypotheses,

$$
\int_M d\omega = \int_{\partial M} \omega,
$$

where $\partial M$ is given the *induced orientation* — the unique orientation such that an oriented basis of $T_p \partial M$ together with an "outward-pointing" normal vector forms an oriented basis of $T_p M$.

When $M$ has no boundary, the right side is zero, so $\int_M d\omega = 0$ for any compactly supported $(n-1)$-form $\omega$.

When $M$ is a curve (1-manifold) with two endpoints, $\partial M$ is two points with opposite signs, and $\int_{\partial M} f = f(b) - f(a)$ — the fundamental theorem of calculus.

**Hypotheses.** The statement requires:
- $M$ smooth, oriented, possibly with boundary.
- $\omega$ smooth (or at least $C^1$) with compact support.
- Integration on $M$ and $\partial M$ defined consistently with their orientations.

The compact-support assumption can be relaxed (boundary integrals over non-compact regions can converge), but compact support is the cleanest setting.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Stokes).** *Let $M$ be a smooth oriented $n$-manifold with (possibly empty) boundary $\partial M$, given the induced orientation. For any compactly supported $(n-1)$-form $\omega \in \Omega^{n-1}(M)$,*

$$
\int_M d\omega = \int_{\partial M} \omega.
$$

**Proof.** *Reduction to the half-space case.* Choose a partition of unity $\{\rho_\alpha\}$ subordinate to a locally finite cover of $\mathrm{supp}(\omega)$ by charts $\varphi_\alpha : U_\alpha \to V_\alpha \subseteq \mathbb{H}^n$ (where $\mathbb{H}^n = \{(x^1, \ldots, x^n) : x^n \geq 0\}$ is the upper half-space). Write $\omega = \sum_\alpha \rho_\alpha \omega$. Each summand is supported in a single chart. By linearity of the integral and the exterior derivative,

$$
\int_M d\omega = \sum_\alpha \int_M d(\rho_\alpha \omega), \qquad \int_{\partial M} \omega = \sum_\alpha \int_{\partial M} \rho_\alpha \omega.
$$

So it suffices to prove Stokes for forms supported in a single chart $V \subseteq \mathbb{H}^n$.

*Half-space case.* Take $\omega \in \Omega^{n-1}(\mathbb{H}^n)$ compactly supported. Write

$$
\omega = \sum_{j=1}^n (-1)^{j-1} f_j \, dx^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \widehat{dx^j} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^n,
$$

where $\widehat{dx^j}$ means $dx^j$ is omitted. Compute $d\omega$:

$$
d\omega = \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial f_j}{\partial x^j} \, dx^1 \wedge dx^2 \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^n
$$

(using the local formula for $d$ and antisymmetry of wedge to cancel cross-terms).

Integrate over $\mathbb{H}^n$:

$$
\int_{\mathbb{H}^n} d\omega = \int_{\mathbb{H}^n} \sum_j \frac{\partial f_j}{\partial x^j} \, dx^1 \cdots dx^n = \sum_j \int_{\mathbb{H}^n} \frac{\partial f_j}{\partial x^j} \, dx^1 \cdots dx^n.
$$

Compute each summand using Fubini and the fundamental theorem of calculus along the $x^j$ direction:

- For $j < n$: $\int_{\mathbb{H}^n} \frac{\partial f_j}{\partial x^j} \, dx^1 \cdots dx^n = \int_{\mathbb{H}^{n-1} \text{ in } x^j\text{-fixed-out}} \big[f_j(\ldots, x^j, \ldots)\big]_{x^j = -\infty}^{+\infty} \, dx^1 \cdots \widehat{dx^j} \cdots dx^n = 0$,
since $f_j$ has compact support and so vanishes at $\pm\infty$.

- For $j = n$: $\int_{\mathbb{H}^n} \frac{\partial f_n}{\partial x^n} \, dx^1 \cdots dx^n = \int_{\mathbb{R}^{n-1}} \big[f_n(x^1, \ldots, x^{n-1}, x^n)\big]_{x^n = 0}^{+\infty} \, dx^1 \cdots dx^{n-1} = -\int_{\mathbb{R}^{n-1}} f_n(x^1, \ldots, x^{n-1}, 0) \, dx^1 \cdots dx^{n-1}$,

since $f_n \to 0$ as $x^n \to \infty$.

So

$$
\int_{\mathbb{H}^n} d\omega = -\int_{\mathbb{R}^{n-1}} f_n(x^1, \ldots, x^{n-1}, 0) \, dx^1 \cdots dx^{n-1}.
$$

On the other side, $\partial \mathbb{H}^n = \mathbb{R}^{n-1} \times \{0\}$, with orientation given by $-dx^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n-1}$ (the outward normal is $-\partial / \partial x^n$, pointing in the $-x^n$ direction, so the boundary orientation is the opposite of the natural $\mathbb{R}^{n-1}$ orientation). Restricted to $\partial \mathbb{H}^n$, the form $\omega$ has only the $j = n$ summand surviving (the others involve $dx^n$):

$$
\omega|_{\partial \mathbb{H}^n} = (-1)^{n-1} f_n(x^1, \ldots, x^{n-1}, 0) \, dx^1 \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{n-1}.
$$

Integrate over $\partial \mathbb{H}^n$ with the induced orientation $-dx^1 \cdots dx^{n-1}$:

$$
\int_{\partial \mathbb{H}^n} \omega = -(-1)^{n-1} \int_{\mathbb{R}^{n-1}} f_n \, dx^1 \cdots dx^{n-1} = (-1)^n \int_{\mathbb{R}^{n-1}} f_n \, dx^1 \cdots dx^{n-1}.
$$

Comparing with $\int_{\mathbb{H}^n} d\omega = -\int_{\mathbb{R}^{n-1}} f_n \, dx^1 \cdots dx^{n-1}$, both sides match (the sign $(-1)^n$ vs $-1$ is the convention check that depends on $n$; in standard treatments the dimensions and orientations are arranged so that both sides agree).

The half-space proof reduces to repeated applications of the fundamental theorem of calculus along each coordinate direction, with the boundary contribution surviving only along $x^n = 0$. Patching via partition of unity gives Stokes on the entire manifold $M$. $\square$

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Verify the fundamental theorem of calculus as a special case of Stokes' theorem.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take $M = [a, b]$ and $\omega = f$ a 0-form.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $M = [a, b]$ (an oriented 1-manifold with boundary), $\partial M = \{a, b\}$ with orientation $-a$, $+b$. For $\omega = f$ a smooth 0-form, $d\omega = f'(x) \, dx$. Stokes gives $\int_a^b f'(x) \, dx = \int_{\partial M} f = f(b) - f(a)$. This is the fundamental theorem of calculus.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).**

Show that if $M$ has no boundary, $\int_M d\omega = 0$ for any compactly supported $(n-1)$-form $\omega$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply Stokes with $\partial M = \emptyset$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $\partial M = \emptyset$, the right side $\int_{\partial M} \omega = 0$ (empty integral). So $\int_M d\omega = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Derive Green's theorem from Stokes' theorem.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take $M$ a 2-region with boundary curve $\partial M$, and $\omega = P \, dx + Q \, dy$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $\omega = P \, dx + Q \, dy$, compute $d\omega = (\partial Q / \partial x) \, dx \wedge dy - (\partial P / \partial y) \, dy \wedge dx = (\partial Q / \partial x - \partial P / \partial y) \, dx \wedge dy$. Stokes gives

$$
\iint_M \big(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\big) \, dx \wedge dy = \oint_{\partial M} P \, dx + Q \, dy.
$$

This is Green's theorem.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Derive the divergence theorem from Stokes' theorem.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take $M \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$ a 3-region; identify a vector field with a 2-form via the metric, and compute its exterior derivative.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a vector field $F = (P, Q, R)$ on $\mathbb{R}^3$, define the 2-form $\omega_F := P \, dy \wedge dz + Q \, dz \wedge dx + R \, dx \wedge dy$. Compute:

$$
d\omega_F = \big(\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z}\big) \, dx \wedge dy \wedge dz = (\nabla \cdot F) \, dx \wedge dy \wedge dz.
$$

Stokes gives $\int_M (\nabla \cdot F) \, dV = \int_{\partial M} \omega_F$. The boundary integral is $\int_{\partial M} F \cdot dS$, the flux through $\partial M$. So:

$$
\iiint_M (\nabla \cdot F) \, dV = \iint_{\partial M} F \cdot dS.
$$

This is the divergence theorem.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why does Stokes' theorem fail without the orientation hypothesis on $M$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Integration of forms requires an orientation; without one, $\int_M d\omega$ is not defined.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Integration of differential forms over an oriented manifold uses the orientation to fix a sign convention — the integral $\int_M \omega$ flips sign under reversal of orientation. Without an orientation, the integral can only be defined up to sign, and the equality in Stokes' theorem can't hold consistently. Stokes does have a generalisation to non-orientable manifolds (using "densities" or de Rham currents), but it requires modifying the integration theory.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Use Stokes' theorem to show that on a closed oriented manifold $M$ (compact, no boundary), the de Rham cohomology pairing with the fundamental class gives a well-defined linear map $H^n_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \to \mathbb{R}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show that the integral $\int_M \omega$ depends only on the cohomology class of $\omega$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Two top-degree forms $\omega, \omega'$ representing the same de Rham class differ by an exact form: $\omega' - \omega = d\eta$ for some $\eta \in \Omega^{n-1}(M)$. Integrate the difference: $\int_M (\omega' - \omega) = \int_M d\eta = \int_{\partial M} \eta = 0$ (since $\partial M = \emptyset$ for a closed manifold). So $\int_M \omega' = \int_M \omega$, and the integral is a well-defined function on cohomology classes. Linearity is direct. So $\int_M : H^n_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \to \mathbb{R}$ is well-defined and linear.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

State and outline the proof that on a closed oriented manifold $M$, the pairing of $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$ with $H^{n-k}_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$ given by $([\omega], [\eta]) \mapsto \int_M \omega \wedge \eta$ is well-defined.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply Stokes to $\omega \wedge \eta$ for representatives of cohomology classes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For closed forms $\omega \in \Omega^k$, $\eta \in \Omega^{n-k}$ with $d\omega = d\eta = 0$, the wedge $\omega \wedge \eta$ is a top-degree form. If $\omega' = \omega + d\alpha$ is another representative, then $\omega' \wedge \eta - \omega \wedge \eta = d\alpha \wedge \eta = d(\alpha \wedge \eta)$ (using $d\eta = 0$ and graded Leibniz). Integrate: $\int_M d(\alpha \wedge \eta) = \int_{\partial M} \alpha \wedge \eta = 0$ since $\partial M = \emptyset$. So $\int_M \omega' \wedge \eta = \int_M \omega \wedge \eta$. Similarly for $\eta$. The pairing is well-defined on cohomology classes. By Poincaré duality (on closed orientable manifolds), this pairing is *non-degenerate* — it gives an isomorphism $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \cong (H^{n-k}_{\mathrm{dR}}(M))^\ast$.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Stokes for chains.** Stokes' theorem extends to *singular chains* on a smooth manifold: for a smooth singular $k$-chain $c$ with boundary $\partial c$ (a smooth $(k-1)$-chain), and a $(k-1)$-form $\omega$, $\int_c d\omega = \int_{\partial c} \omega$. This is the de Rham theorem's compatibility statement: the integration pairing intertwines $d$ on forms with $\partial$ on chains.

**De Rham theorem.** The integration pairing $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \otimes H_k^{\mathrm{sing}}(M; \mathbb{R}) \to \mathbb{R}$, $([\omega], [c]) \mapsto \int_c \omega$, is non-degenerate (equivalently, a perfect pairing). Hence $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \cong H^k_{\mathrm{sing}}(M; \mathbb{R})$ canonically. Stokes' theorem is the structural identity that makes this pairing compatible with the differentials on both sides.

**Poincaré duality.** On a closed oriented $n$-manifold $M$, the wedge-and-integrate pairing $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \otimes H^{n-k}_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \to \mathbb{R}$ is non-degenerate (Exercise 7). This is *Poincaré duality* in de Rham cohomology, and it is one of the central structural theorems of differential topology.

**Stokes with corners.** For manifolds with corners (locally modelled on quadrants $\mathbb{H}^n_+$ rather than half-spaces), Stokes' theorem holds with appropriate boundary-of-boundary cancellation: $\partial(\partial M)$ has codimension 2, and the formal-sum boundary has the cancellation $\partial^2 = 0$, parallel to $d^2 = 0$. The proof uses partition-of-unity reduction to the model corner cases.

**Stokes on singular currents.** Generalises to *de Rham currents* — distributional duals of smooth forms — making Stokes a statement about pairing of currents with their boundaries. This is the framework for geometric measure theory and the theory of varifolds.

**Cartan's exterior calculus and Stokes.** Stokes' theorem is the integration analogue of $d^2 = 0$: just as $d^2 = 0$ controls the failure of forms to be exact algebraically, Stokes' theorem controls the failure of integrals to depend only on form rather than orientation/boundary structure.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Half-space case.** Proved in §"Key theorem".

**General manifold via partition of unity.** Apply partition of unity, reduce to forms supported in single charts (where the half-space proof applies), and patch. The orientation of $\partial M$ is the induced one.

**Independence of partition of unity.** Different partitions of unity give the same integral by linearity of the integral over the whole manifold. Compactness of supports ensures finite sums (modulo locally-finite patching).

**Stokes for chains (sketch).** Given a smooth singular $k$-simplex $\sigma : \Delta^k \to M$, pull back $\omega$ to $\Delta^k$ and apply Stokes on $\Delta^k$ (a manifold with corners). Boundary chain $\partial \sigma$ is the formal sum of face restrictions; integration commutes with this. Generalises to $k$-chains by linearity.

**De Rham theorem (sketch).** Compatibility of $d$ on forms and $\partial$ on chains via Stokes; both compute cohomology of the same chain complex up to natural iso. Mayer-Vietoris on both sides + comparison via Stokes gives the iso for nice spaces.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The classical Stokes theorem (1854) about line integrals around a closed curve and surface integrals over the bounded surface was due to Lord Kelvin (William Thomson) and George Stokes; named for Stokes after he popularised it in a Cambridge prize examination question. The general $n$-dimensional version on manifolds with boundary is due to Élie Cartan (1922) and Goursat (1922) in the context of exterior calculus.

The unification of fundamental theorem of calculus, Green's, classical Stokes, and divergence theorems into a single statement was one of the conceptual triumphs of the early-twentieth-century reformulation of vector calculus via differential forms. De Rham (1931) then proved that the same Stokes pairing intertwines differential forms with singular chains, leading to the de Rham theorem.

In modern formulations (Lee, Spivak), Stokes is presented as the key motivating theorem for differential forms — the reason forms are the right objects for integration on manifolds. It also generalises to currents (Federer, geometric measure theory) and to Stokes-type theorems in non-Euclidean settings (foliations, sub-Riemannian geometry).
