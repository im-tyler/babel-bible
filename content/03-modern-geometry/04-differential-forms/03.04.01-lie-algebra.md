## Intuition [Beginner]

A Lie algebra is the *infinitesimal version* of a continuous group of symmetries. Take the rotations of three-dimensional space: they form a smooth surface of moves you can chain together, but the chain order matters — rotating about $x$ then about $y$ is not the same as rotating about $y$ then about $x$. The *amount by which the order matters*, examined infinitesimally near the identity rotation, is the Lie bracket.

Two infinitesimal rotations don't add and undo neatly: their commutator records what fails to cancel. The Lie algebra is the vector space of infinitesimal moves equipped with this bracket, and the bracket records all the rotation group's geometry up to first order around the identity.

The same picture works whenever a smooth space of transformations is built around an identity — rotations, translations on a curved manifold, gauge transformations in physics. The Lie algebra is the linear ledger of how those transformations refuse to commute.

## Visual [Beginner]

Two arrows meeting at a point, each representing an infinitesimal flow. Following one then the other doesn't return to the same place as following them in the reverse order; the small gap between the two end-points is the bracket.

![Two infinitesimal flow arrows whose composition in opposite orders disagrees by a third arrow — the bracket.](/img/lie-algebra-placeholder.svg)

That third arrow is also an infinitesimal flow. The bracket lives inside the same linear space the original arrows do; that's what makes a Lie algebra a *closed* algebraic object.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the three coordinate-axis rotations of three-dimensional space. Pick infinitesimal generators $L_x$, $L_y$, $L_z$ — small rotations about each axis. They live in a three-dimensional vector space.

Compute the bracket $[L_x, L_y]$. Operationally: rotate slightly about $x$, then slightly about $y$, then back about $x$, then back about $y$. To first order in the angles, the result is a tiny rotation. *About which axis?* About $z$.

The same computation gives

$$
[L_x, L_y] = L_z, \qquad [L_y, L_z] = L_x, \qquad [L_z, L_x] = L_y.
$$

These are the structure constants of the Lie algebra of rotations, $\mathfrak{so}(3)$. They tell you everything about how 3D rotations chain together infinitesimally — and through the exponential map, everything about the global rotation group too.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $K$ be a field. A **Lie algebra** over $K$ is a vector space $\mathfrak{g}$ over $K$ [01.01.03] equipped with a bilinear map

$$
[\,\cdot\,, \,\cdot\,] : \mathfrak{g} \times \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{g}
$$

called the **Lie bracket**, satisfying:

1. **Antisymmetry**: $[X, X] = 0$ for all $X \in \mathfrak{g}$.
2. **Jacobi identity**: $[X, [Y, Z]] + [Y, [Z, X]] + [Z, [X, Y]] = 0$ for all $X, Y, Z \in \mathfrak{g}$.

When $\mathrm{char}\, K \neq 2$, antisymmetry is equivalent to the more familiar relation $[X, Y] = -[Y, X]$: substituting $X + Y$ for $X$ in $[X, X] = 0$ and expanding gives $[X, Y] + [Y, X] = 0$.

A **Lie subalgebra** of $\mathfrak{g}$ is a vector subspace $\mathfrak{h} \subseteq \mathfrak{g}$ closed under the bracket. An **ideal** is a subspace $\mathfrak{i} \subseteq \mathfrak{g}$ with $[\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{i}] \subseteq \mathfrak{i}$. A **homomorphism** $\varphi : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{h}$ of Lie algebras is a $K$-linear map preserving the bracket: $\varphi([X, Y]) = [\varphi(X), \varphi(Y)]$.

The **adjoint representation** sends each $X \in \mathfrak{g}$ to the linear map

$$
\mathrm{ad}_X : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{g}, \qquad \mathrm{ad}_X(Y) = [X, Y].
$$

The Jacobi identity is precisely the statement that $\mathrm{ad}$ is a Lie-algebra homomorphism from $\mathfrak{g}$ to $\mathfrak{gl}(\mathfrak{g})$ — the bracket on the latter being the commutator of linear maps [ref: quantum-well Lie algebras.md].

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (commutator on an associative algebra).** *Let $A$ be an associative $K$-algebra. Define $[a, b] := ab - ba$. Then $A$, viewed as a vector space with this bracket, is a Lie algebra.*

**Proof.** Bilinearity and antisymmetry are immediate from distributivity and the definition. We verify the Jacobi identity. Expand each cyclic term:

$$
[a, [b, c]] = a(bc - cb) - (bc - cb)a = abc - acb - bca + cba.
$$

By cyclic permutation $a \to b \to c \to a$,

$$
[b, [c, a]] = bca - bac - cab + acb,
$$

$$
[c, [a, b]] = cab - cba - abc + bac.
$$

Add the three expressions. Each term on the right cancels with another:

- $abc$ in the first cancels $-abc$ in the third.
- $-acb$ in the first cancels $acb$ in the second.
- $-bca$ in the first cancels $bca$ in the second.
- $cba$ in the first cancels $-cba$ in the third.
- $-bac$ in the second cancels $bac$ in the third.
- $-cab$ in the second cancels $cab$ in the third.

So the sum is zero, which is the Jacobi identity. $\square$

**Corollary.** $\mathfrak{gl}_n(K) := M_n(K)$ with $[A, B] = AB - BA$ is a Lie algebra. Every Lie subalgebra of $\mathfrak{gl}_n(K)$ — for example $\mathfrak{sl}_n$ (traceless matrices), $\mathfrak{so}_n$ (skew-symmetric matrices), $\mathfrak{u}_n$ (skew-Hermitian matrices) — inherits the structure.

The corollary is the source of every Lie algebra arising in classical geometry. Ado's theorem (deeper, not proved here) says the converse: every finite-dimensional Lie algebra over a field of characteristic zero embeds into some $\mathfrak{gl}_n(K)$.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [03.06.05] (invariant polynomial on a lie algebra), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [03.06.06] (chern-weil homomorphism). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).**

Show that the bracket $[X, Y] = -[Y, X]$ follows from $[X, X] = 0$ when $\mathrm{char}\, K \neq 2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute $X + Y$ into the antisymmetry condition.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$0 = [X + Y, X + Y] = [X, X] + [X, Y] + [Y, X] + [Y, Y] = [X, Y] + [Y, X]$, so $[X, Y] = -[Y, X]$. The argument requires only that $2 \neq 0$ in $K$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the trace-zero matrices $\mathfrak{sl}_n(K) := \{A \in M_n(K) : \mathrm{tr}\, A = 0\}$ form a Lie subalgebra of $\mathfrak{gl}_n(K)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Trace is linear and satisfies $\mathrm{tr}(AB) = \mathrm{tr}(BA)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The set is a vector subspace because trace is linear. It is closed under the commutator because $\mathrm{tr}(AB - BA) = \mathrm{tr}(AB) - \mathrm{tr}(BA) = 0$ for all $A, B$, regardless of whether $A$ or $B$ has trace zero. So $[A, B] \in \mathfrak{sl}_n$ whenever $A, B \in \mathfrak{sl}_n$ (in fact for *any* $A, B \in \mathfrak{gl}_n$).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Show that the kernel of a Lie-algebra homomorphism $\varphi : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{h}$ is an ideal in $\mathfrak{g}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $\varphi([X, Y]) = [\varphi(X), \varphi(Y)]$ with one factor in $\ker\varphi$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The kernel is a subspace because $\varphi$ is linear. For $Y \in \ker\varphi$ and any $X \in \mathfrak{g}$, $\varphi([X, Y]) = [\varphi(X), \varphi(Y)] = [\varphi(X), 0] = 0$, so $[X, Y] \in \ker\varphi$. This is the ideal condition $[\mathfrak{g}, \ker\varphi] \subseteq \ker\varphi$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify the $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ relations $[L_x, L_y] = L_z$ etc. using the matrix realization

$$
L_x = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0\end{pmatrix}, \quad L_y = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}, \quad L_z = \begin{pmatrix}0 & -1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}.
$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $L_x L_y - L_y L_x$ as a matrix and compare to $L_z$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$L_x L_y$ has entry $(i, j)$ equal to $\sum_k (L_x)_{ik}(L_y)_{kj}$. A direct computation gives

$$
L_x L_y = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad L_y L_x = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0\end{pmatrix},
$$

so $[L_x, L_y] = L_x L_y - L_y L_x = L_z$. The other two relations follow by cyclic permutation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that $\mathrm{ad} : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{gl}(\mathfrak{g})$, $X \mapsto \mathrm{ad}_X$, is a Lie-algebra homomorphism.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

You need to verify $\mathrm{ad}_{[X,Y]} = [\mathrm{ad}_X, \mathrm{ad}_Y]$. Use the Jacobi identity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $Z \in \mathfrak{g}$,

$$
[\mathrm{ad}_X, \mathrm{ad}_Y](Z) = \mathrm{ad}_X(\mathrm{ad}_Y Z) - \mathrm{ad}_Y(\mathrm{ad}_X Z) = [X, [Y, Z]] - [Y, [X, Z]].
$$

The Jacobi identity rearranges to $[X, [Y, Z]] - [Y, [X, Z]] = [[X, Y], Z] = \mathrm{ad}_{[X, Y]}(Z)$. So $[\mathrm{ad}_X, \mathrm{ad}_Y] = \mathrm{ad}_{[X, Y]}$ as maps $\mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{g}$. Linearity of $\mathrm{ad}$ is immediate from bilinearity of the bracket.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Why is the Jacobi identity automatically satisfied when the bracket comes from an associative product, but not automatic in general?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Look at the proof of the key theorem above and ask which step needed associativity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The cancellation in the proof rearranges products of three associative-algebra elements. Without associativity, the rearrangements would not be equalities and the cancellation would fail. The Jacobi identity is therefore the *minimum surviving fragment of associativity* under the commutator construction. A bilinear bracket on a vector space need not arise from any associative product, and there is no a priori reason for cyclic sums of double brackets to vanish — the Jacobi identity is an extra axiom that must be assumed (or verified case by case).

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

A Lie algebra is **simple** if it is nonabelian and has no proper nonzero ideals. **Semisimple** means the radical (largest solvable ideal) is zero; equivalently, $\mathfrak{g}$ is a direct sum of simple Lie algebras.

The finite-dimensional simple Lie algebras over $\mathbb{C}$ are classified by Killing and Cartan into four infinite families (the classical types $A_n, B_n, C_n, D_n$ corresponding to $\mathfrak{sl}_{n+1}, \mathfrak{so}_{2n+1}, \mathfrak{sp}_{2n}, \mathfrak{so}_{2n}$) plus five exceptional cases ($G_2, F_4, E_6, E_7, E_8$). The classification proceeds via the Killing form

$$
B(X, Y) := \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}_X \, \mathrm{ad}_Y),
$$

a symmetric invariant bilinear form whose nondegeneracy characterises semisimplicity (Cartan's criterion).

Every finite-dimensional Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ over a field of characteristic zero has a unique maximal solvable ideal (the **radical**), and Levi's theorem gives a canonical decomposition $\mathfrak{g} = \mathrm{rad}(\mathfrak{g}) \oplus \mathfrak{s}$ with $\mathfrak{s}$ semisimple — the Levi subalgebra. Solvable Lie algebras over $\mathbb{C}$ are upper-triangularisable in any finite-dimensional representation (Lie's theorem), generalising the classical result that a single nilpotent matrix can be put in Jordan form.

The exponential map $\exp : \mathfrak{g} \to G$ for a Lie group $G$ realises the Lie algebra as the tangent space at the identity, and the Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff formula expresses $\exp(X) \exp(Y)$ as $\exp$ of a series in iterated brackets. The Lie bracket is therefore the *first nontrivial term* in the failure of the group product to be commutative.

Infinite-dimensional Lie algebras play a role too. The Witt algebra $\mathfrak{W}$ of holomorphic vector fields on the punctured disk, and its central extension the Virasoro algebra [03.11.03], are infinite-dimensional Lie algebras whose representation theory underlies two-dimensional conformal field theory [03.10.02]. Affine Lie algebras (Kac-Moody algebras) extend the classical classification into the infinite-dimensional setting.

**Synthesis.** This construction generalises the pattern fixed in [01.01.03] (vector space), with the symmetric data replaced by its skew or twisted analogue. Read in the opposite direction, the construction is dual to the metric story: complements and orthogonality are taken with respect to the bilinear datum of this unit, not a metric, and the resulting category of subobjects is the one the rest of the strand classifies. The central insight is that this datum identifies algebra with geometry: functions become vector fields, subspaces become quotients, and invariants become cohomology classes — and that identification is the engine driving every theorem downstream.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Antisymmetry from $[X, X] = 0$.** Expand $0 = [X + Y, X + Y]$ using bilinearity:

$$
0 = [X, X] + [X, Y] + [Y, X] + [Y, Y] = [X, Y] + [Y, X].
$$

So $[X, Y] = -[Y, X]$. The argument needs $2 \neq 0$ in $K$ to invert no factor; the implication holds in any characteristic for this direction.

**Adjoint is a homomorphism.** Compute, for $Z \in \mathfrak{g}$,

$$
\mathrm{ad}_{[X, Y]}(Z) = [[X, Y], Z].
$$

The Jacobi identity says

$$
[[X, Y], Z] + [[Y, Z], X] + [[Z, X], Y] = 0,
$$

equivalently

$$
[[X, Y], Z] = [X, [Y, Z]] - [Y, [X, Z]].
$$

The right-hand side is $\mathrm{ad}_X(\mathrm{ad}_Y Z) - \mathrm{ad}_Y(\mathrm{ad}_X Z) = [\mathrm{ad}_X, \mathrm{ad}_Y](Z)$. Both sides agree for all $Z$, so $\mathrm{ad}_{[X, Y]} = [\mathrm{ad}_X, \mathrm{ad}_Y]$.

**Killing form is invariant.** For $X, Y, Z \in \mathfrak{g}$,

$$
B([X, Y], Z) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}_{[X, Y]} \mathrm{ad}_Z) = \mathrm{tr}([\mathrm{ad}_X, \mathrm{ad}_Y] \mathrm{ad}_Z).
$$

Using the trace identity $\mathrm{tr}([A, B] C) = \mathrm{tr}(A [B, C])$,

$$
\mathrm{tr}([\mathrm{ad}_X, \mathrm{ad}_Y] \mathrm{ad}_Z) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}_X [\mathrm{ad}_Y, \mathrm{ad}_Z]) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_{[Y, Z]}) = B(X, [Y, Z]).
$$

So $B([X, Y], Z) = B(X, [Y, Z])$, the *invariance* of the Killing form under the adjoint action.

**Subalgebra of $\mathfrak{gl}_n$ generated by skew-symmetric matrices is closed.** If $A^T = -A$ and $B^T = -B$, then $(AB)^T = B^T A^T = (-B)(-A) = BA$, so $[A, B]^T = (AB - BA)^T = BA - AB = -[A, B]$. The commutator of two skew-symmetric matrices is skew-symmetric, so $\mathfrak{so}_n$ is a Lie subalgebra of $\mathfrak{gl}_n$.

## Connections [Master]

- **Vector space [01.01.03]** — the underlying linear structure on which the bracket lives.

- **Invariant polynomial on a Lie algebra [03.06.05]** — adjoint-invariant polynomials on $\mathfrak{g}$ are the input to Chern-Weil theory.

- **Chern-Weil homomorphism [03.06.06]** — turns invariant polynomials into characteristic classes via curvature.

- **Yang-Mills action [03.07.05]** — gauge potentials are $\mathrm{ad}\, P$-valued forms; field strength is the curvature 2-form valued in $\mathfrak{g}$.

- **Spin group [03.09.03]** — the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{spin}(n)$ is isomorphic to $\mathfrak{so}(n)$, and rotations live as exponentials of skew-symmetric matrices.

- **Central extension of a Lie algebra [03.11.01]** — modifies the bracket by a 2-cocycle, producing the Heisenberg, Virasoro, and affine Kac-Moody algebras.

- **Virasoro algebra [03.11.03]** — the central extension of the Witt algebra; the symmetry algebra of two-dimensional CFT.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Sophus Lie introduced what we now call Lie groups in the 1870s as continuous symmetry groups of differential equations. His infinitesimal method linearised these groups at the identity and studied the resulting bracket structure. Killing classified the simple complex Lie algebras in 1888–1890; Cartan corrected and completed the classification in his 1894 thesis, fixing the structural mistakes Killing had made and identifying the five exceptional algebras [ref: TODO_REF Humphreys §1].

The modern axiomatic definition — vector space with a bilinear, antisymmetric, Jacobi-respecting bracket — was crystallised by Hermann Weyl and others in the 1920s, separating the algebraic content from the original analytic motivation. Bourbaki's treatment (1960s) made the abstraction definitive [ref: TODO_REF Serre Part I].

In physics, Lie algebras describe infinitesimal symmetries of every kind: gauge symmetries (Yang-Mills theory uses $\mathfrak{su}(2), \mathfrak{su}(3), \mathfrak{u}(1)$), spacetime symmetries (Poincaré algebra), and conformal symmetries (Virasoro algebra in two dimensions). Noether's theorem translates each Lie-algebra generator of a symmetry into a conserved current of the corresponding field theory.
