## Intuition [Beginner]

Variational calculus studies quantities that depend on a whole path, surface, or field. Instead of changing one number, you change an entire object a tiny amount and ask how the total score changes.

The shortest path is the basic picture. Try nearby paths with the same endpoints. The shortest one has no first-order improvement: tiny allowed changes do not decrease its length to first order.

Physics uses the same idea. An action assigns a number to a motion or field. The physical equation says that the first-order change of the action vanishes.

## Visual [Beginner]

The dashed paths are variations of the blue path. The critical path is the one whose action has zero first-order change under all allowed variations.

![Nearby paths connecting the same endpoints, with one critical path.](/img/variational-calculus-placeholder.svg)

Variational calculus turns "best among nearby objects" into a differential equation.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider paths from point A to point B in a flat plane. The action is the path length.

If a path bends outward, you can usually shorten it by nudging the bend inward while keeping the endpoints fixed. A straight line has no such first-order shortening.

What this tells us: the equation for a critical path is a local condition that comes from testing every allowed tiny change.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\mathcal C$ be a space of smooth fields or paths on a manifold, and let

$$
S:\mathcal C\to\mathbb R
$$

be an action functional, typically defined by integrating a Lagrangian density over a manifold [03.04.03]. A **variation** of $\phi\in\mathcal C$ is a smooth one-parameter family $\phi_t$ with $\phi_0=\phi$ and prescribed boundary behavior.

The **first variation** of $S$ at $\phi$ in the direction $\dot\phi$ is

$$
\delta S_\phi(\dot\phi)=\left.\frac{d}{dt}\right|_{t=0}S(\phi_t).
$$

The field $\phi$ is **critical** if $\delta S_\phi(\dot\phi)=0$ for every allowed variation [ref: TODO_REF Arnold §III.12].

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Euler-Lagrange equation in one dimension).** Let

$$
S[q]=\int_a^b L(t,q(t),q'(t))\,dt
$$

for smooth paths $q$ with fixed endpoints. If $q$ is critical, then

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial q}-\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial q'}=0.
$$

**Proof.** Let $q_t=q+t\eta$, where $\eta(a)=\eta(b)=0$. Differentiating under the integral gives

$$
\delta S_q(\eta)=\int_a^b
\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial q}\eta+
\frac{\partial L}{\partial q'}\eta'\right)\,dt.
$$

Integrate the second term by parts:

$$
\int_a^b\frac{\partial L}{\partial q'}\eta'\,dt
=\left[\frac{\partial L}{\partial q'}\eta\right]_a^b
-\int_a^b\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial q'}\,\eta\,dt.
$$

The boundary term vanishes because $\eta(a)=\eta(b)=0$. Therefore

$$
\delta S_q(\eta)=\int_a^b
\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial q}-\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial q'}\right)\eta\,dt.
$$

If this is zero for every compactly supported variation $\eta$, the fundamental lemma of variational calculus gives the Euler-Lagrange equation. $\square$

## Advanced results [Master]

For fields given by sections of a bundle $E\to M$, a first-order Lagrangian density depends on the first jet $j^1\phi$. The first variation decomposes into an Euler-Lagrange term plus a boundary term:

$$
\delta S_\phi(\eta)=\int_M \langle \operatorname{EL}(\phi),\eta\rangle+\int_{\partial M}\Theta(\phi,\eta).
$$

With compactly supported variations or fixed boundary data, the boundary term vanishes and critical points satisfy $\operatorname{EL}(\phi)=0$ [ref: TODO_REF Spivak §10].

For a principal connection $A$, the Yang-Mills action has first variation

$$
\delta YM_A(a)=2\int_M\langle d_A a,*F_A\rangle
=2\int_M\langle a,d_A^\ast F_A\rangle,
$$

so the Euler-Lagrange equation is $d_A^\ast F_A=0$ [03.07.05].

**Synthesis.** Variational calculus on manifolds reformulates the Euler-Lagrange equations as geometric conditions on the action functional $S[\phi] = \int_M L(\phi, d\phi) \, \mathrm{vol}$, where L is a function on the jet bundle of the field configuration space. The first variation $\delta S = 0$ produces the Euler-Lagrange equations as a condition on sections, while the second variation governs stability and connects to the spectral theory of the Jacobi operator—an elliptic operator whose index counts conjugate points along geodesics. The Palais-Smale condition extends the finite-dimensional critical-point theory (Morse theory) to the infinite-dimensional setting of function spaces, and Noether's theorem identifies each continuous symmetry of L with a conserved current, linking Lie group actions, differential forms, and the Hamiltonian formalism into the variational framework underlying all of classical and quantum field theory.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Fundamental lemma.** If a smooth function $f$ on an interval satisfies

$$
\int_a^b f(t)\eta(t)\,dt=0
$$

for every compactly supported smooth $\eta$, then $f=0$. If $f(t_0)\ne0$, continuity gives an interval where $f$ has a fixed sign. Choosing $\eta$ nonnegative and supported there gives a nonzero integral, a contradiction.

**Yang-Mills first variation.** The curvature variation is $\delta F_A=d_Aa$. Differentiating $\int_M\langle F_A,*F_A\rangle$ gives $2\int_M\langle d_Aa,*F_A\rangle$. The covariant integration-by-parts formula moves $d_A$ to its formal adjoint, yielding $2\int_M\langle a,d_A^\ast F_A\rangle$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The calculus of variations grew from the brachistochrone problem, geodesics, and classical mechanics. Euler and Lagrange developed the differential equations for extrema of integral functionals, and Hamilton's principle made the action central to mechanics.

Arnold's formulation places variational principles inside symplectic geometry and mechanics, while gauge theory applies the same structure to spaces of connections [ref: TODO_REF Arnold §III.12].
