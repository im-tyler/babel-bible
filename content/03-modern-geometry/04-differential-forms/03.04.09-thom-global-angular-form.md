## Intuition [Beginner]

A **vector bundle** is a smoothly varying family of vector spaces over a base space. Imagine a curved surface and, at every point, a separate copy of a fixed vector space attached — a "fiber" — varying smoothly as the point moves. The **Thom isomorphism** is the statement that the cohomology of the *whole bundle* (with a suitable support condition) is the cohomology of the *base*, shifted up in degree by the rank of the fiber.

The trick that makes the isomorphism explicit is **integration along the fiber**. Take a form on the bundle with appropriate support; integrate over the fiber direction, leaving a form on the base. Going the other direction: take a form on the base, pull it back to the bundle, and wedge with a special form $\Phi$ — the **Thom class** — that integrates to one on each fiber. The two operations are mutually inverse, up to homotopy.

The Thom class $\Phi$ is constructed concretely from the **global angular form** $\psi$. On a sphere bundle (one obtained by trimming each vector-space fiber of a vector bundle to the unit sphere of that fiber), the angular form is the bundle-version of $d\theta$ on a circle: a one-form whose fiber-integral is one. The Bott-Tu sign convention pins this: $d\psi = -\pi^* e(E)$, where $e(E)$ is the **Euler class** of the original vector bundle. The Euler class is the obstruction to a global non-zero section, and it appears here as the failure of $\psi$ to be closed.

The whole story is the differential-form face of one of the most basic identifications in topology: the cohomology of a vector bundle equals the cohomology of its base, shifted by the rank.

## Visual [Beginner]

A circle bundle: a thin tube wrapping a base circle, with each point of the base supporting its own tiny circle as a fiber. On every fiber, an angular form measures the fraction of the circle traversed. As the base point moves around the loop, the angular forms on adjacent fibers fit together into a global one-form $\psi$ on the tube. The differential of $\psi$ is no longer zero on the tube — it equals (the negative of) a base form pulled back by the bundle projection.

![A base circle with a circle attached at every point, forming a torus. An arrow on each fiber circle shows the angular form pointing in the fiber direction. A separate base form is shown on the base circle, related to the differential of the global angular form by a sign and a pullback.](/img/cv-cohomology-thom-isomorphism-de-rham-placeholder.svg)

The picture is a recipe: build a Thom class from the angular form, and the cohomology of the bundle is the cohomology of the base, shifted by the rank.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the simplest oriented rank-two real vector bundle: the product bundle on a point, with fiber $\mathbb{R}^2$. The Thom class lives in degree two compactly-supported cohomology of $\mathbb{R}^2$. Concretely, build a smooth bump form on $\mathbb{R}^2$ whose fiber-integral is one — for instance, a smooth approximation to $\frac{1}{2\pi r} \, dr \wedge d\theta$ on the unit disc, smoothed and bump-cut so that it has compact support. Call it $\Phi$. Its fiber integral over $\mathbb{R}^2$ is, by construction, one.

The Thom-isomorphism map sends a constant on the base point to $\Phi$ on the bundle. It sends the constant five to the form $5\Phi$, which integrates to five on the fiber. The inverse map — fiber integration — sends $5\Phi$ back to the constant five.

Now twist: replace the base point by a circle, and take the Möbius bundle (rank-one, non-orientable). The Thom class no longer exists in real cohomology — the Möbius bundle is non-orientable, and the orientation obstruction is precisely the failure of a Thom class to exist with $\mathbb{R}$-coefficients. (With $\mathbb{Z}/2$-coefficients, a $\mathbb{Z}/2$-valued Thom class still exists; with twisted coefficients, an $\mathbb{R}$-valued Thom class exists in the orientation local system.)

What this tells us: the Thom class is the differential-form realisation of the orientation of the bundle, and its existence as a form is exactly the statement that the bundle is orientable.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\pi \colon E \to M$ be a rank-$r$ smooth vector bundle over a smooth manifold $M$.

**Compactly-supported forms.** A differential form $\omega \in \Omega^*(M)$ has **compact support** if there is a compact set $K \subset M$ with $\mathrm{supp}\,\omega \subset K$. The complex of compactly-supported forms is $\Omega^*_c(M)$, with differential $d$ inherited from $\Omega^*(M)$. Its cohomology is $H^*_c(M)$, the **compactly-supported de Rham cohomology** [ref: TODO_REF Bott-Tu §6].

**Compactly-vertical forms.** A form $\omega \in \Omega^*(E)$ has **compact vertical support** if for every compact $K \subset M$, the support $\mathrm{supp}(\omega) \cap \pi^{-1}(K)$ is compact. The complex of such forms is $\Omega^*_{cv}(E)$ — Bott-Tu's coinage — with $d$ inherited from $\Omega^*(E)$. Its cohomology is $H^*_{cv}(E)$, the **compactly-vertical cohomology** [ref: TODO_REF Bott-Tu §6 — Bott-Tu coinage].

**Integration along the fiber.** For an oriented rank-$r$ vector bundle $\pi \colon E \to M$, define

$$
\pi_* \colon \Omega^{p+r}_{cv}(E) \;\to\; \Omega^p(M)
$$

by the formula

$$
(\pi_* \omega)_x(v_1, \ldots, v_p) \;=\; \int_{E_x} \omega\bigl(\widetilde{v}_1, \ldots, \widetilde{v}_p, \cdot, \ldots, \cdot\bigr),
$$

where $\widetilde{v}_i$ is any tangent lift of $v_i$ along $\pi$, and the remaining slots are integrated over the fiber $E_x \cong \mathbb{R}^r$ with its given orientation. The integral is independent of the choice of lift because the form has compact vertical support [ref: TODO_REF Bott-Tu §6].

**Commutation with $d$.** For an oriented bundle,

$$
d \circ \pi_* \;=\; \pi_* \circ d,
$$

so $\pi_*$ descends to a map on cohomology

$$
\pi_* \colon H^{p+r}_{cv}(E) \;\to\; H^p(M).
$$

(For unoriented bundles, the formula picks up the orientation local system as a twist.)

**Thom class.** The **Thom class** $\Phi \in H^r_{cv}(E)$ is the cohomology class characterised by

$$
\int_{\mathrm{fiber}} \Phi \;=\; 1
$$

— i.e., $\pi_* \Phi = 1 \in H^0(M)$. Existence and uniqueness of the Thom class for an oriented vector bundle is the content of the Thom isomorphism theorem (proved in §Key theorem). Bott-Tu fix the symbol $\Phi$ for this class throughout [ref: TODO_REF Bott-Tu §6].

**Global angular form.** Let $\pi \colon S(E) \to M$ be the unit-sphere bundle of an oriented rank-$r$ Euclidean vector bundle. The **global angular form** $\psi \in \Omega^{r-1}(S(E))$ is a globally defined $(r-1)$-form on $S(E)$ that restricts to the standard volume form (normalised so that the fiber integral equals one) on each fiber $S(E)_x \cong S^{r-1}$. The Bott-Tu construction (per §Full proof set) gives a $\psi$ satisfying

$$
d\psi \;=\; -\pi^* e(E),
$$

where $e(E) \in H^r(M)$ is the **Euler class** of the bundle. This is the **Bott-Tu sign convention**; the alternative $d\psi = +\pi^* e$ is used by Milnor-Stasheff (with all subsequent signs flipped consistently) [ref: TODO_REF Bott-Tu §6, with sign comparison from Milnor-Stasheff §11].

**Euler class — de Rham model.** $e(E) \in H^r(M)$ is defined by either:

(i) $e(E) = -\pi_* (d\psi)$ for $\psi$ the global angular form on $S(E)$ (Bott-Tu sign);

(ii) $e(E) = i^* \Phi$ for $i \colon M \hookrightarrow E$ the zero section, $\Phi$ the Thom class.

The two definitions agree (per §Full proof set).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (de Rham Thom isomorphism).** *Let $\pi \colon E \to M$ be an oriented rank-$r$ smooth vector bundle. The map*

$$
T \colon H^p(M) \;\to\; H^{p+r}_{cv}(E), \qquad [\omega] \;\mapsto\; [\pi^* \omega \wedge \Phi]
$$

*is an isomorphism, with inverse $\pi_*$.*

**Proof.** We prove the theorem in three steps: (a) Poincaré lemma for $H^*_{cv}(\mathbb{R}^r) \to H^*$ on a point; (b) Mayer-Vietoris induction over a finite good cover (per [03.04.10]) trivializing the bundle on each cover element; (c) the same compatibility argument that proves Künneth.

*Step (a) — Local case: $E = M \times \mathbb{R}^r \to M = \{*\}$.* Here $H^*_{cv}(\mathbb{R}^r) = H^*_c(\mathbb{R}^r)$, which equals $\mathbb{R}$ in degree $r$ and zero elsewhere (the compactly-supported Poincaré lemma — proved in [03.04.06]). The map $T$ in this case is $\mathbb{R} \to H^r_c(\mathbb{R}^r) = \mathbb{R}$, sending $1 \mapsto [\Phi]$. Since $\Phi$ has fiber integral one, $\pi_* \Phi = 1$, so $T$ is an isomorphism in this case with inverse $\pi_*$.

*Step (b) — Product bundle over arbitrary base: $E = M \times \mathbb{R}^r$.* By the Künneth theorem (which we prove independently — see [03.04.12] forthcoming, or Bott-Tu §5 directly): $H^*_{cv}(M \times \mathbb{R}^r) \cong H^*(M) \otimes H^*_c(\mathbb{R}^r) = H^*(M) \otimes \mathbb{R}[r]$, where $\mathbb{R}[r]$ is $\mathbb{R}$ in degree $r$. Hence $H^{p+r}_{cv}(M \times \mathbb{R}^r) \cong H^p(M) \otimes \mathbb{R}$, and the isomorphism is given by $\omega \otimes 1 \mapsto \pi^* \omega \wedge \Phi$. So $T$ is an isomorphism for product bundles.

*Step (c) — General bundle: Mayer-Vietoris induction.* Choose a finite good cover $\{U_1, \ldots, U_n\}$ of $M$ trivializing $E$ on each $U_i$ (refine the good cover if needed; the trivialization-and-good-cover refinement is finite by compactness when $M$ is compact, otherwise countable). Set $E_i = E|_{U_i}$. Each $E_i$ is a product bundle over $U_i$, so $T$ is an isomorphism on $E_i$ by step (b).

Run Mayer-Vietoris induction on the size of the cover (per [03.04.10]). At each step, the cover $\{V_{k-1}, U_k\}$ of $V_k = U_1 \cup \cdots \cup U_k$ produces an MV sequence both for $H^*$ on $V_k$ and for $H^*_{cv}$ on $E|_{V_k}$. The natural transformation $T$ provides a map between the two MV sequences, commuting with restrictions and (after a routine sign check) with the connecting maps. By the inductive hypothesis, $T$ is an isomorphism on $V_{k-1}$, on $U_k$, and on $V_{k-1} \cap U_k$ (each a smaller-cover instance). The five lemma then gives that $T$ is an isomorphism on $V_k$.

After $n$ steps, $V_n = M$, so $T \colon H^p(M) \to H^{p+r}_{cv}(E)$ is an isomorphism. The inverse is $\pi_*$ by direct check on representatives. $\square$

The proof is the canonical Bott-Tu §1.7 dual-proof template: Mayer-Vietoris induction over a good cover to globalise a local result. The same Thom isomorphism is **re-proved** in the forthcoming Čech-de Rham double-complex unit [03.04.11] via tic-tac-toe, with the second proof shorter — the dual-proof discipline.

**Synthesis.** The Thom isomorphism is the foundational reason characteristic-class theory is computable in differential-form language. This is exactly the equivalence between $H^*(M)$ and $H^*_{cv}(E)$, which generalises to the K-theoretic Thom isomorphism. The bridge between bundle topology and base cohomology is exactly the Thom class.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Compute $H^*_c(\mathbb{R}^n)$ for general $n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The compactly-supported Poincaré lemma: $H^*_c(\mathbb{R}^n) = \mathbb{R}$ in degree $n$, zero elsewhere.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$H^q_c(\mathbb{R}^n) = \mathbb{R}$ for $q = n$, and $H^q_c(\mathbb{R}^n) = 0$ for $q \neq n$. The non-zero class is represented by any compactly-supported volume form $\omega$ with $\int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \omega = 1$ (such forms exist — take a smooth bump in the unit ball normalised to integrate to one). The pairing with constants gives the integration isomorphism $H^n_c(\mathbb{R}^n) \cong \mathbb{R}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (medium, proof).**

Verify that $d \circ \pi_* = \pi_* \circ d$ on $\Omega^{p+r}_{cv}(E)$ for an oriented rank-$r$ bundle.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiation in the base direction commutes with integration in the fiber direction, by Stokes on the fiber and the compact-vertical support hypothesis.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $\omega \in \Omega^{p+r}_{cv}(E)$, write in local trivialization $\omega = \sum_I \omega_I (x, y)\, dx^I + \text{terms with fewer fiber-direction differentials}$. Integration along the fiber kills terms without enough fiber differentials. Differentiation $d = d_x + d_y$ splits into base and fiber pieces. The fiber piece $d_y$, integrated over the fiber by Stokes, gives a boundary term — which vanishes because $\omega$ has compact vertical support. The base piece $d_x$ commutes with the fiber integral by linearity. Hence $\pi_*(d\omega) = d(\pi_* \omega)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the global angular form on the product circle bundle $S^1 \times M \to M$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The product bundle has a globally defined fiber coordinate $\theta$; the standard one-form $\frac{d\theta}{2\pi}$ is the global angular form, with fiber integral one.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On $S^1 \times M$, set $\psi = \frac{d\theta}{2\pi}$ where $\theta$ is the standard angle coordinate on $S^1$. Then $\int_{\mathrm{fiber}} \psi = \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{d\theta}{2\pi} = 1$, and $d\psi = 0$ (since $d\theta$ is closed). The Euler class is $e = 0$ (the product bundle has a global non-zero section, so its Euler class vanishes). Bott-Tu sign convention $d\psi = -\pi^* e = 0$ — consistent.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute $\int_{S^2} e(TS^2)$ using the de Rham model.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Euler class of $TS^2$ is the Euler-characteristic class. The integral equals $\chi(S^2) = 2$ by the Gauss-Bonnet theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\int_{S^2} e(TS^2) = \chi(S^2) = 2$. Concretely, choose any Riemannian metric on $S^2$ (e.g., the round metric of curvature $1$). The Chern-Weil representative of $e(TS^2)$ is the Pfaffian of the curvature divided by $2\pi$: for the round metric, this is $\frac{K}{2\pi} \, dA$ where $K = 1$ is the Gaussian curvature. Then $\int_{S^2} \frac{1}{2\pi} \, dA = \frac{4\pi}{2\pi} = 2 = \chi(S^2)$. This is the Gauss-Bonnet theorem.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, proof).**

Prove the Thom isomorphism for the product rank-$r$ bundle on $\mathbb{R}^n$ directly, without invoking Künneth.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the compactly-supported Poincaré lemma in $r$ fiber directions and the contractibility of $\mathbb{R}^n$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$E = \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^r$. $H^*_{cv}(E) = H^*_{cv}(\mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^r)$. Iterated application of the compactly-supported Poincaré lemma in the fiber directions gives $H^q_{cv}(\mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^r) \cong H^{q-r}(\mathbb{R}^n)$ — i.e., shift down by $r$, then drop the compact-support condition (the base is now contractible, with the integration-along-fiber map providing the inverse). Hence $H^q_{cv}(E) \cong H^{q-r}(\mathbb{R}^n) = \mathbb{R}$ for $q = r$, zero elsewhere. Both sides $H^p(\mathbb{R}^n)$ and $H^{p+r}_{cv}(E)$ are $\mathbb{R}$ for $p = 0$ (resp. $p+r = r$) and zero elsewhere; the map $T$ is an isomorphism by direct check on representatives.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Prove the **projection formula** $\pi_*(\pi^* \alpha \wedge \omega) = \alpha \wedge \pi_*(\omega)$ for $\alpha \in \Omega^*(M)$ and $\omega \in \Omega^*_{cv}(E)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Pulled-back base forms behave as constants when integrated over the fiber.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take $\alpha \in \Omega^p(M)$, $\omega \in \Omega^{q}_{cv}(E)$. In local trivialization $E|_U \cong U \times \mathbb{R}^r$, write $\pi^* \alpha = \alpha(x)$ (constant in fiber direction $y$) and $\omega = \omega(x, y)$. Then $\pi^* \alpha \wedge \omega = \alpha(x) \wedge \omega(x, y)$. Fiber integration: $\pi_*(\alpha \wedge \omega)(x) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^r} \alpha(x) \wedge \omega(x, y)\, dy = \alpha(x) \wedge \int_{\mathbb{R}^r} \omega(x, y)\, dy = \alpha(x) \wedge \pi_*\omega(x) = (\alpha \wedge \pi_*\omega)(x)$, using that $\alpha(x)$ is independent of $y$ and so factors out of the fiber integral. Hence $\pi_*(\pi^* \alpha \wedge \omega) = \alpha \wedge \pi_* \omega$. Globally this follows by patching.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Compare the Bott-Tu sign convention $d\psi = -\pi^* e(E)$ with the Milnor-Stasheff sign convention $d\psi = +\pi^* e(E)$. Which downstream identities flip sign?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The two conventions differ by an overall sign on $\psi$ (or equivalently on $e$). Track which formulas pick up a relative sign.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Bott-Tu $d\psi = -\pi^* e$ corresponds to the convention $\int_{\mathrm{fiber}} \psi = +1$ on the unit-sphere bundle and the Hopf-index identity $\sum \mathrm{ind}(p_i) = \chi(M)$ in the form where the sum is over zeros of a generic section weighted by the local Euler-class evaluation (no extra sign). Milnor-Stasheff $d\psi = +\pi^* e$ corresponds to $\int_{\mathrm{fiber}} \psi = -1$, with all fiber-integral identities flipped. The Codex Pass-4 convention is Bott-Tu; this is consistent with the Lawson-Michelsohn shipped strand (per LM §I.4), with Berline-Getzler-Vergne (per BGV Ch. 1), and with the Hopf-index proof in [03.05.10] forthcoming. Switching conventions globally is a sign-by-sign exercise; the choice is what fixes the relative phase in the Atiyah-Singer index density and downstream characteristic-class computations.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Thom class as relative cohomology.** $H^*_{cv}(E) \cong H^*(D(E), S(E))$ where $D(E)$ is the unit-disc bundle and $S(E)$ is the unit-sphere bundle of an oriented Euclidean rank-$r$ bundle. The Thom class $\Phi \in H^r_{cv}(E)$ corresponds to the **relative fundamental class** $[D(E), S(E)]$. By `conn:435.thom-iso-de-rham-relative`, the Thom isomorphism is the relative de Rham long exact sequence on a tubular neighborhood of the zero section. This identifies the Thom isomorphism with a structural fact about the cohomology of pairs.

**Gysin sequence.** The unit-sphere bundle $\pi \colon S(E) \to M$ of an oriented rank-$r$ Euclidean bundle has the **Gysin sequence**

$$
\cdots \to H^{q-r}(M) \xrightarrow{\cup e(E)} H^q(M) \xrightarrow{\pi^*} H^q(S(E)) \xrightarrow{\pi_*} H^{q-r+1}(M) \to \cdots
$$

derived from the Thom isomorphism applied to the disc-sphere pair. The cup-with-Euler-class map is the connecting homomorphism. The Gysin sequence will appear in the spectral-sequence chapter and powers many computations of $H^*$ on flag manifolds and Stiefel manifolds.

**Hopf index theorem.** A generic smooth section $s \colon M \to E$ of an oriented rank-$r$ bundle on a closed oriented $n$-manifold ($n = r$) has finitely many zeros, each with a local index $\pm 1$. The **Hopf index theorem** states $\sum_{p \in s^{-1}(0)} \mathrm{ind}(s, p) = \int_M e(E) = \langle e(E), [M] \rangle$. The proof uses the Thom class to localise the count of zeros to a Euler-class evaluation. This is the entire content of the forthcoming sphere-bundle unit [03.05.10], and it is the Bott-Tu de Rham version of the Poincaré-Hopf theorem.

**Connection to Chern-Weil theory.** The Euler form is the simplest of the Chern-Weil characteristic classes (per [03.06.06]). For a rank-$r$ oriented Euclidean bundle with metric connection, the Euler form is the **Pfaffian of the curvature** $\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{r/2}} \mathrm{Pf}(\Omega)$ (when $r$ even). By `conn:433.thom-de-rham-chern-weil`, *de Rham Thom class equivalent to Chern-Weil Euler form* on the same bundle (equivalence). The two routes — Thom class on the total space, Chern-Weil on the base — give the same cohomology class on $M$.

**Connection to spin geometry.** On a spin manifold, the global angular form on the spinor unit-sphere bundle is the structural input for the $\widehat A$-genus density in the local Atiyah-Singer index theorem (per [03.09.20]). By `conn:434.global-angular-form-spin`, *spin-geometry Â-genus machinery built on the global angular form* (foundation-of). Both the Bismut superconnection formalism and the Getzler rescaling use the angular-form sign convention as a structural input.

**Connection to twisted cohomology.** On a non-orientable manifold, the orientation local system $\mathcal{o}$ replaces the constant sheaf in the Thom apparatus. By `conn:439.local-system-twisted-de-rham`, *twisted de Rham complex built on orientation local system* (foundation-of). This is the structural foundation for twisted Poincaré duality on non-orientable manifolds, treated downstream in [04.03.02].

**Relative de Rham complex.** The pair $(E, E \setminus M)$ — total space minus zero section — has relative de Rham cohomology $H^*(E, E \setminus M) \cong H^*_{cv}(E)$ (forms supported in a tubular neighborhood of the zero section). The Thom class is the relative orientation class. This is the structural foundation for the **Gysin pushforward** in algebraic topology and the entire excision-style apparatus.

**Madsen-Tornehave variant.** Madsen-Tornehave §11 gives an alternative construction of the Thom class via the Atiyah-Bott parametrix: a smoothing of the formal $\delta$-function on the zero section, supported in a tubular neighborhood. The Madsen-Tornehave construction is computationally less explicit than the Bott-Tu global-angular-form construction but generalises more readily to non-orientable bundles and to families.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Existence of a global angular form.** Let $\pi \colon S(E) \to M$ be the unit-sphere bundle of an oriented rank-$r$ Euclidean bundle $E$. Choose a finite good cover $\{U_\alpha\}$ of $M$ trivializing $E$. On each $S(E)|_{U_\alpha} \cong U_\alpha \times S^{r-1}$, the standard volume form $\eta_\alpha$ on $S^{r-1}$ pulls back to a closed $(r-1)$-form $\widetilde \eta_\alpha$ on $S(E)|_{U_\alpha}$ with fiber integral one. On overlaps $U_\alpha \cap U_\beta$, the difference $\widetilde \eta_\alpha - \widetilde \eta_\beta$ is a closed $(r-1)$-form whose fiber integral vanishes; by the Mayer-Vietoris connecting homomorphism (per [03.04.07]), this difference produces a closed $r$-form on $M$ with cohomology class $e_\alpha - e_\beta$ in $H^r(M)$. Patching the local angular forms by a partition of unity gives a global $(r-1)$-form $\psi$ on $S(E)$. By the construction, $d\psi$ is fiberwise zero — so $d\psi$ is the pullback of an $r$-form on $M$. The cohomology class $[d\psi] \in H^r(M)$ is by definition $-e(E)$ (the Bott-Tu sign), giving $d\psi = -\pi^* e(E) + \text{exact}$. Up to an exact correction in $\Omega^{r-1}(S(E))$, we may arrange $d\psi = -\pi^* e(E)$ on the nose.

**Thom class via the angular form.** The unit-disc bundle $D(E)$ has boundary $S(E)$. Fix a smooth bump function $\rho \colon [0, \infty) \to \mathbb{R}$ with $\rho = -1$ near $0$ and $\rho = 0$ near $1$. Define on $E$ (using the radial coordinate $r$ and the angular form $\psi$):

$$
\Phi \;=\; d(\rho(r) \psi) \;=\; \rho'(r)\, dr \wedge \psi + \rho(r)\, d\psi.
$$

This is a closed compactly-vertical $r$-form on $E$ (the support is in the unit-disc bundle, where $\rho$ is non-zero; closedness is direct from $d\rho \wedge d\psi = -\rho \cdot d(\pi^* e(E)) = 0$ and $d^2 = 0$). The fiber integral is

$$
\int_{\mathrm{fiber}} \Phi \;=\; \int_0^\infty \rho'(r)\, dr \cdot \int_{S^{r-1}} \psi_{\mathrm{fiber}} \;+\; \int_0^\infty \rho(r)\, dr \cdot \int_{S^{r-1}} d\psi_{\mathrm{fiber}}.
$$

The second integral has $d\psi_{\mathrm{fiber}}$ on a sphere, integrating to zero by Stokes. The first integral is $[\rho]_0^\infty \cdot 1 = (0 - (-1)) \cdot 1 = 1$. Hence $\pi_* \Phi = 1$, so $\Phi$ represents the Thom class.

**Euler class via zero section.** The zero section $i \colon M \hookrightarrow E$ pulls $\Phi$ back to $i^* \Phi$. Computing in local coordinates: $i^* \Phi = i^*(\rho'(r) \, dr \wedge \psi + \rho(r) d\psi)$. At $r = 0$ in the fiber, $\rho(0) = -1$ and the term $dr$ vanishes when restricted to the zero section. Hence $i^* \Phi = -d\psi|_{r=0} = \pi^* e(E) |_{r=0} = e(E)$ on the base. So $e(E) = i^* \Phi$ as cohomology classes — the second definition of the Euler class.

**Both Euler-class definitions agree.** Definition (i): $e = -\pi_*(d\psi) / \int_{\mathrm{fiber}} \psi = -\pi_*(d\psi)$. Definition (ii): $e = i^* \Phi$. By the construction above, $i^* \Phi = -d\psi|_{r=0} \cdot 1 = $ pullback of $-\pi^* e(E)$ by the zero section $= e(E)$. Both give the same class in $H^r(M)$.

**Mayer-Vietoris compatibility.** The Thom-isomorphism map $T \colon \alpha \mapsto \pi^* \alpha \wedge \Phi$ commutes with restriction: $T(\alpha|_U) = T(\alpha)|_{E|_U}$. It also commutes with the MV connecting homomorphism, up to a sign that depends on the rank parity. The five-lemma argument in step (c) of the main theorem uses this compatibility plus the inductive hypothesis to globalise the product-bundle case.

**Naturality.** For a smooth $f \colon M' \to M$, the pullback bundle $f^* E \to M'$ has $f^* \Phi$ as its Thom class. Hence $T_{M'} \circ f^* = f^* \circ T_M$ on cohomology — the Thom isomorphism is natural in the base.

**Multiplicativity.** For a Whitney sum $E \oplus F$ of oriented bundles, $\Phi_{E \oplus F} = \pi_E^* \Phi_E \wedge \pi_F^* \Phi_F$, where $\pi_E, \pi_F$ are the projections to the two factor bundles. This implies $e(E \oplus F) = e(E) \cup e(F)$ on the base (multiplicativity of the Euler class).

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

**René Thom**'s 1952 *Espaces fibrés en sphères et carrés de Steenrod* (Annales scientifiques de l'École Normale Supérieure 69) introduced the topological Thom isomorphism in singular cohomology, and used it to prove what is now called the **cobordism-flavour interpretation** of characteristic numbers: the Stiefel-Whitney numbers of a closed manifold are determined by its bordism class, and the Steenrod squares act on the Thom space of a bundle by a transparent rule. Thom's original 1952 framework was simplicial-cohomological, with the Thom class a relative cohomology class of the disc-sphere pair $(D(E), S(E))$ in singular $\mathbb{Z}$-cohomology. The constructive content was the **Thom space** $E^+$ — the one-point compactification of the disc bundle — and the identification $H^{*+r}(E^+) \cong H^*(M)$ as a graded module over $H^*(M)$.

The differential-form version of the Thom isomorphism — via integration along the fiber, the global angular form, and the de Rham apparatus — is what we channel here directly from §6 of **Bott** and **Tu**'s 1982 *Differential Forms in Algebraic Topology*. Bott-Tu's exposition takes the originator-text role: their treatment is canonical for the de Rham face of the theory. The symbol $\Omega^*_{cv}$ for compactly-vertical forms is Bott-Tu's coinage; the symbol $\Phi$ for the Thom class as a fixed name (not just an abstract cohomology class) is theirs; the explicit construction $\Phi = d(\rho(r) \psi)$ via the global angular form $\psi$ is theirs; the sign convention $d\psi = -\pi^* e(E)$ is theirs.

The contrast between Thom 1952 and Bott-Tu 1982 is structurally instructive. Thom's original argument carries a **cobordism flavour**: the Thom isomorphism is a structural fact about the action of cohomology operations on Thom spaces, with applications to bordism rings and Steenrod-squares classification. Bott-Tu's argument carries a **differential-form flavour**: the Thom isomorphism is a clean cohomological identity between $H^*(M)$ and $H^*_{cv}(E)$, derived via Mayer-Vietoris induction over a finite good cover and made explicit via the global angular form. The first is structurally illuminating but computationally heavy; the second is computationally direct and makes the Euler class transparent as the obstruction to a global non-zero section of the bundle.

Bott and Tu's pedagogical move is to introduce the Thom class **before** the spectral-sequence machinery (which appears in their Chapter III). This inversion is what gives the de Rham Thom isomorphism its punch as a teaching tool: the reader has, by the time they reach §6, only the Mayer-Vietoris sequence and the good-cover induction in their toolkit, yet they can compute the Euler class, prove the Hopf index theorem, and derive the projection formula. The §1.7 *no-obstacle path* directive is what motivates putting the Thom apparatus before the abstract spectral-sequence framework.

The deeper structural lesson — visible from the spectral-sequence chapter and made explicit in the Čech-de Rham double-complex unit [03.04.11] forthcoming — is that the Thom isomorphism is one of two **dual proofs** of the same identity, the other being the tic-tac-toe collapse of the double complex. Bott and Tu walk the reader through both: the §6 Mayer-Vietoris-induction proof of the Thom isomorphism is the first telling; the Čech-de Rham re-proof is the second telling. The second telling is shorter, because the first installed the right machinery — and this is the dual-proof discipline that organises the central chapters of *Differential Forms in Algebraic Topology*. The reader emerges, by the end of Chapter II, with the Thom isomorphism, the Euler class, the Hopf index theorem, and the global angular form in hand, and an instinct for when a result admits two proofs and what each proof reveals.
