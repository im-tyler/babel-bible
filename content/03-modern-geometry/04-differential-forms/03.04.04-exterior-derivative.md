## Intuition [Beginner]

The *exterior derivative* $d$ takes a $k$-form to a $(k+1)$-form. It is the unique generalisation of "take the derivative" that makes sense on differential forms of any degree, on any smooth manifold, in a coordinate-free way.

For a function $f$ (a 0-form), $df$ is the gradient — the 1-form whose value on a tangent vector $v$ is the directional derivative of $f$ along $v$. For higher-degree forms, $d$ extends this by the Leibniz rule and the structural identity $d^2 = 0$.

The identity $d \circ d = 0$ is the geometric heart of de Rham cohomology: it says that exact forms (those equal to $d\eta$ for some $\eta$) are automatically closed (those satisfying $d\omega = 0$). The reverse — when is a closed form exact? — is the global question that de Rham cohomology answers.

## Visual [Beginner]

A 1-form on a surface, with arrows indicating its values. Apply $d$ and you get a 2-form, a signed-area density that detects how much the original 1-form "rotates" or fails to be conservative.

![A 1-form on a surface and its exterior derivative — a 2-form measuring the rotation/non-conservativeness of the original.](/img/exterior-derivative-placeholder.svg)

The pattern recurs in higher degrees: $d$ on a $k$-form produces a $(k+1)$-form measuring the "twisting" of the original.

## Worked example [Beginner]

In two dimensions with coordinates $(x, y)$: take the 1-form $\omega = -y \, dx + x \, dy$. Apply $d$:

$$
d\omega = -dy \wedge dx + dx \wedge dy = dx \wedge dy + dx \wedge dy = 2 \, dx \wedge dy.
$$

The derivative is a 2-form — twice the standard area form. This 1-form is "rotational": it generates rotation, and its exterior derivative captures that rotation as twice the area density.

By contrast, $\eta = x \, dx + y \, dy$ has

$$
d\eta = dx \wedge dx + dy \wedge dy = 0,
$$

since each summand has a wedge of a 1-form with itself, which vanishes by antisymmetry. So $\eta$ is closed. In fact $\eta = d(\tfrac{1}{2}(x^2 + y^2))$, so $\eta$ is exact — the gradient of the squared-distance function.

The exterior derivative tells you, in one calculation, whether a 1-form on a 2D region is potentially the gradient of some function (closed forms are candidates) and computationally extracts when it isn't (nonzero $d\omega$).

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $M$ be a smooth manifold [03.02.01] and $\Omega^k(M)$ the space of differential $k$-forms on $M$ [03.04.02].

The **exterior derivative** is the unique $\mathbb{R}$-linear operator

$$
d : \Omega^k(M) \to \Omega^{k+1}(M)
$$

(for each $k \geq 0$) characterised by the following four axioms:

1. **On 0-forms (functions)**: for $f \in C^\infty(M) = \Omega^0(M)$ and tangent vector $v \in T_p M$,

$$
df_p(v) = v(f) := \text{directional derivative of } f \text{ at } p \text{ along } v.
$$

2. **$\mathbb{R}$-linearity**: $d(\alpha + \beta) = d\alpha + d\beta$ and $d(c \cdot \alpha) = c \cdot d\alpha$ for $c \in \mathbb{R}$.

3. **Graded Leibniz rule**: for $\alpha \in \Omega^k(M)$ and $\beta \in \Omega^l(M)$,

$$
d(\alpha \wedge \beta) = d\alpha \wedge \beta + (-1)^k \alpha \wedge d\beta.
$$

4. **$d^2 = 0$**: $d(d\omega) = 0$ for every form $\omega$.

These four properties uniquely determine $d$, and $d$ exists. (Existence and uniqueness are proved in §"Key theorem".)

In a local chart with coordinates $(x^1, \ldots, x^n)$, the formula is:

$$
d\big(f \, dx^{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_k}\big) = \sum_j \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^j} \, dx^j \wedge dx^{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_k}.
$$

The exterior derivative *commutes with pullback*: for a smooth map $\varphi : M \to N$ and $\omega \in \Omega^k(N)$,

$$
\varphi^\ast(d\omega) = d(\varphi^\ast \omega).
$$

This makes $d$ natural with respect to smooth maps.

A form $\omega$ with $d\omega = 0$ is **closed**; a form $\omega = d\eta$ for some $\eta$ is **exact**. By $d^2 = 0$, every exact form is closed; the converse fails in general, and the failure is captured by the de Rham cohomology groups [03.04.06].

The pair $(\Omega^\bullet(M), d)$ is the **de Rham complex** of $M$:

$$
0 \to \Omega^0(M) \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^1(M) \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^2(M) \xrightarrow{d} \cdots \xrightarrow{d} \Omega^n(M) \to 0.
$$

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (existence and uniqueness of $d$).** *On any smooth manifold $M$, there exists a unique linear operator $d : \Omega^\bullet(M) \to \Omega^{\bullet+1}(M)$ satisfying the four axioms above.*

**Proof.** *Uniqueness.* Suppose $d$ and $d'$ both satisfy the axioms. We show they agree on every $\omega \in \Omega^k(M)$.

In a local chart with coordinates $(x^1, \ldots, x^n)$, write $\omega = \sum_I f_I \, dx^I$ where $I$ ranges over multi-indices $(i_1 < \cdots < i_k)$ and $dx^I = dx^{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_k}$. By linearity, it suffices to check $d(f \, dx^I) = d'(f \, dx^I)$.

Apply the Leibniz rule to $f \cdot dx^I$:

$$
d(f \, dx^I) = df \wedge dx^I + f \, d(dx^I).
$$

By axiom 1, $df = \sum_j (\partial f / \partial x^j) \, dx^j$, which is determined.

For $d(dx^I)$: write $dx^I = dx^{i_1} \wedge dx^{i_2} \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_k}$ and apply Leibniz iteratively:

$$
d(dx^I) = \sum_a (-1)^{a-1} dx^{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge d(dx^{i_a}) \wedge \cdots \wedge dx^{i_k}.
$$

But $d(dx^{i_a}) = d(d(x^{i_a})) = 0$ by axiom 4. So $d(dx^I) = 0$.

Therefore $d(f \, dx^I) = df \wedge dx^I$, the same formula for both $d$ and $d'$. Hence $d = d'$ in every chart, and globally.

*Existence.* Define $d$ in each chart by the formula

$$
d\big(f \, dx^I\big) := \sum_j \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^j} \, dx^j \wedge dx^I.
$$

Verify the four axioms hold (linearity is direct; Leibniz follows by computation; $d^2 = 0$ from symmetry of mixed partials and antisymmetry of wedge — see below).

To show this chart-defined $d$ glues consistently across overlapping charts: the formula in different coordinate systems must agree on overlaps. Compute $d$ in a different coordinate system $(\tilde{x}^1, \ldots, \tilde{x}^n)$ on the overlap. The chain rule plus the local-formula expansion shows the two expressions agree (this is essentially the chain rule for partial derivatives plus antisymmetry of wedge). Hence $d$ is globally defined. $\square$

**$d^2 = 0$ proof.** Compute $d^2(f \, dx^I)$:

$$
d^2(f \, dx^I) = d\Big(\sum_j \frac{\partial f}{\partial x^j} \, dx^j \wedge dx^I\Big) = \sum_{l, j} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^l \partial x^j} \, dx^l \wedge dx^j \wedge dx^I.
$$

For each unordered pair $\{l, j\}$ with $l \neq j$, the contributions cancel by symmetry of mixed partials ($\partial^2 f / \partial x^l \partial x^j = \partial^2 f / \partial x^j \partial x^l$) and antisymmetry of wedge ($dx^l \wedge dx^j = -dx^j \wedge dx^l$). Diagonal contributions vanish since $dx^j \wedge dx^j = 0$. So $d^2 = 0$.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [03.04.05] (stokes' theorem), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [03.04.06] (de rham cohomology). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Compute $d(xy \, dx + x^2 \, dy)$ on $\mathbb{R}^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the local formula $d(f \, dx^j) = \sum_l (\partial f / \partial x^l) \, dx^l \wedge dx^j$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$d(xy \, dx) = (\partial(xy)/\partial y) \, dy \wedge dx = x \, dy \wedge dx = -x \, dx \wedge dy$.
$d(x^2 \, dy) = 2x \, dx \wedge dy$.
Sum: $(2x - x) \, dx \wedge dy = x \, dx \wedge dy$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).**

Show that the exterior derivative of any 0-form $f$ in a single coordinate system is the gradient 1-form $\sum_j (\partial f / \partial x^j) \, dx^j$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the local formula $d(f \cdot 1) = \sum_j (\partial f / \partial x^j) \, dx^j$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$df = d(f \, 1) = \sum_j (\partial f / \partial x^j) \, dx^j \wedge 1 = \sum_j (\partial f / \partial x^j) \, dx^j$. The empty multi-index $I$ corresponds to the 0-form $f \cdot 1$, and the formula reduces to the gradient.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the graded Leibniz rule, applied iteratively, recovers the local formula $d(f \, dx^I) = df \wedge dx^I$ on basic forms.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use induction on the length of the multi-index $I$, plus $d(dx^j) = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$d(f \, dx^I) = df \wedge dx^I + f \, d(dx^I)$ by Leibniz on the product $f \cdot dx^I$. Then $d(dx^I) = 0$ follows by induction on $|I|$ using Leibniz: each factor $dx^{i_a}$ satisfies $d(dx^{i_a}) = d^2(x^{i_a}) = 0$. So $d(f \, dx^I) = df \wedge dx^I$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute $d\omega$ for $\omega = x \, dy \wedge dz + y \, dz \wedge dx + z \, dx \wedge dy$ on $\mathbb{R}^3$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply $d$ to each term using the local formula.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$d(x \, dy \wedge dz) = dx \wedge dy \wedge dz$.
$d(y \, dz \wedge dx) = dy \wedge dz \wedge dx = dx \wedge dy \wedge dz$ (cyclic).
$d(z \, dx \wedge dy) = dz \wedge dx \wedge dy = dx \wedge dy \wedge dz$ (cyclic).
Sum: $3 \, dx \wedge dy \wedge dz$. (This $\omega$ is the standard "radial" 2-form on $\mathbb{R}^3 \setminus \{0\}$ that, normalised, is the angular 2-form on $S^2$.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Show that the exterior derivative commutes with pullback: $\varphi^\ast(d\omega) = d(\varphi^\ast \omega)$ for a smooth map $\varphi : M \to N$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Verify on 0-forms (chain rule), then extend by Leibniz and uniqueness.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For 0-forms $f \in C^\infty(N)$, $\varphi^\ast(df) = d(f \circ \varphi) = d(\varphi^\ast f)$ by the chain rule. For higher-degree forms, both $\varphi^\ast \circ d$ and $d \circ \varphi^\ast$ are linear operators satisfying the four axioms of $d$ on $\Omega^\bullet(M)$ (linearity, action on 0-forms, Leibniz, $d^2 = 0$). By the uniqueness theorem, they agree on all of $\Omega^\bullet(M)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, conceptual).**

Why is the closed-but-not-exact 1-form $d\theta = (-y \, dx + x \, dy)/(x^2 + y^2)$ on $\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{0\}$ the prototypical example of de Rham cohomology?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $d(d\theta)$ and check whether $\theta$ exists as a global function.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$d(d\theta) = 0$ by direct calculation, so $d\theta$ is closed. But $\theta$ (the angle function) is multi-valued — it picks up $2\pi$ each time you go around the origin — so there is no globally-defined function $\theta$ on $\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{0\}$ with $d\theta$ as written. Hence $d\theta$ is closed but not exact, and represents a nonzero class in $H^1(\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{0\}; \mathbb{R}) \cong \mathbb{R}$ — the first de Rham cohomology of the punctured plane.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

Prove the Poincaré lemma in dimension 1: every closed 1-form on a star-shaped open subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ is exact.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $\omega = \sum_j a_j(x) \, dx^j$ closed (i.e., $\partial a_j / \partial x^k = \partial a_k / \partial x^j$), define $f(x) := \int_0^1 \sum_j a_j(tx) x^j \, dt$ and check $df = \omega$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Star-shaped means there exists $x_0$ (we take $x_0 = 0$ WLOG) such that for every $x$ in the domain, the line segment from $0$ to $x$ stays in the domain. Define $f(x) := \int_0^1 \sum_j a_j(tx) x^j \, dt$. Compute $\partial f / \partial x^k$:

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x^k} = \int_0^1 \Big( a_k(tx) + \sum_j t \frac{\partial a_j}{\partial x^k}(tx) x^j \Big) \, dt.
$$

Use closedness $\partial a_j / \partial x^k = \partial a_k / \partial x^j$:

$$
= \int_0^1 \Big( a_k(tx) + \sum_j t \frac{\partial a_k}{\partial x^j}(tx) x^j \Big) \, dt = \int_0^1 \frac{d}{dt}(t \, a_k(tx)) \, dt = a_k(x) - 0 = a_k(x).
$$

So $df = \omega$, and $\omega$ is exact.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Naturality and pullback.** The exterior derivative commutes with pullback (Exercise 5). Combined with the de Rham complex being a functor, this gives a contravariant functor from smooth manifolds to chain complexes of $\mathbb{R}$-vector spaces. Cohomology of this functor is de Rham cohomology [03.04.06], which is a homotopy invariant of smooth manifolds.

**Cartan magic formula.** $\mathcal{L}_X = d \, \iota_X + \iota_X \, d$, where $\mathcal{L}_X$ is the Lie derivative along a vector field $X$ and $\iota_X$ is the interior product (contraction with $X$). This relates the three fundamental operations on differential forms — $d$, $\iota$, $\mathcal{L}$ — and is the key identity for working with vector fields and forms together.

**Poincaré lemma.** On a star-shaped (or more generally, contractible) open subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$, every closed form is exact. The proof uses a *cone construction* (homotopy operator). The lemma fails on non-contractible domains — its failure is exactly de Rham cohomology.

**De Rham complex and cohomology.** The complex $(\Omega^\bullet(M), d)$ has cohomology $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) := \ker(d : \Omega^k \to \Omega^{k+1}) / \mathrm{im}(d : \Omega^{k-1} \to \Omega^k)$. By de Rham's theorem [03.04.06], this is canonically isomorphic to the singular cohomology with real coefficients $H^k(M; \mathbb{R})$.

**Exterior derivative on Lie groups.** On a Lie group $G$, the *Maurer-Cartan form* $\theta$ is the canonical $\mathfrak{g}$-valued 1-form satisfying $\theta_g(v) = (L_{g^{-1}})_\ast v$. It satisfies the *Maurer-Cartan equation* $d\theta + \tfrac{1}{2}[\theta \wedge \theta] = 0$, the structural identity that encodes the bracket.

**Generalisation: covariant exterior derivative.** For bundle-valued forms (forms with values in a vector bundle $E$ with connection $\nabla$), the covariant exterior derivative $d_\nabla : \Omega^k(M; E) \to \Omega^{k+1}(M; E)$ generalises $d$. It satisfies $d_\nabla^2 = R^\nabla \wedge$, where $R^\nabla$ is the curvature of $\nabla$ — the failure of $d_\nabla$ to square to zero measures curvature [03.05.09].

**Synthesis.** The exterior derivative d:Ω^k(M)→Ω^{k+1}(M) is the unique graded-derivation extending the differential of a function to all degrees while satisfying d²=0, and its nilpotence is the analytic engine of cohomology. On 0-forms d recovers the gradient, on 1-forms d recovers the curl, and on 2-forms d recovers the divergence—the classical vector calculus operations unified into a single operator. The coordinate-free formula d(∑f_I dx^I)=∑df_I∧dx^I conceals a deep property: d commutes with pullback along any smooth map, making de Rham cohomology functorial. This functoriality, combined with the Poincaré lemma (local exactness), drives the Mayer-Vietoris sequence and the computation of cohomology from local-to-global gluing data.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Existence and uniqueness.** Proved in §"Key theorem".

**$d^2 = 0$.** Proved in §"Key theorem" supplement. Symmetry of mixed partials + antisymmetry of wedge cancellation.

**Naturality (commutation with pullback).** Proved in Exercise 5 — uniqueness of $d$ + verification on 0-forms.

**Cartan magic formula.** Compute $\mathcal{L}_X(f) = X(f) = df(X) = \iota_X(df)$, so $\mathcal{L}_X = \iota_X d$ on 0-forms. For higher forms, both sides are derivations of degree 0 satisfying the same recursive structure under Leibniz; equality on 0-forms + matching derivation property forces equality on all forms.

**Poincaré lemma.** Proved in Exercise 7 in dimension 1; the higher-degree version uses the same cone-construction homotopy operator $K$ with $dK + Kd = \mathrm{id}$.

**Maurer-Cartan equation.** Direct computation on a Lie group: $\theta$ is the $\mathfrak{g}$-valued left-invariant 1-form with $\theta_e = \mathrm{id}_{\mathfrak{g}}$. Its exterior derivative satisfies $d\theta(X^\sharp, Y^\sharp) = -[X, Y]$ for left-invariant vector fields $X^\sharp, Y^\sharp$ generated by $X, Y \in \mathfrak{g}$. Combining with $\theta(X^\sharp) = X$: $d\theta = -\tfrac{1}{2}[\theta \wedge \theta]$, i.e., $d\theta + \tfrac{1}{2}[\theta \wedge \theta] = 0$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The exterior derivative crystallised in Élie Cartan's 1899 paper *Sur certaines expressions différentielles*, which introduced what we now call differential forms and the exterior derivative as the unifying language for line, surface, and volume integrals. Cartan's *exterior calculus* unified the gradient, curl, divergence, and higher analogues into a single operator $d$ acting on forms of all degrees.

Goursat (1922) and Élie Cartan (1934) developed Stokes's theorem in its modern form, and de Rham (1931) proved the cohomological version that connects analytic data (closed forms modulo exact) to topological data (singular cohomology with real coefficients). Bott and Tu's monograph (1982) systematised the theory for modern algebraic-topology audiences.

In modern physics, $d$ is the universal "field-strength" operator: for a gauge potential $A$ (a connection 1-form), the field strength $F = dA + \tfrac{1}{2}[A \wedge A]$ uses the covariant exterior derivative. Conservation laws are statements about closed forms; the lack of magnetic monopoles in Maxwell's theory is the closedness of the magnetic-flux 2-form.
