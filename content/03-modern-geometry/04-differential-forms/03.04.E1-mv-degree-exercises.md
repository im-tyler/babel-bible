## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Bott-Tu Chapter I covers the de Rham complex, Mayer-Vietoris, integration on manifolds, the Poincaré lemma, the MV-induction computation of $H^*(S^n)$, the Thom isomorphism via differential forms with the global angular form, the non-orientable case, and the sphere-bundle Euler class with the Hopf index theorem. Several of its exercises do not anchor to a single Codex unit — they cross-cut the Mayer-Vietoris machinery, the good-cover induction, the Thom-isomorphism construction, and the Hopf-index calculation simultaneously.

This pack collects fourteen such exercises — four easy, six medium, four hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units rather than as a standalone development. The exercises are loosely grouped by Bott-Tu section: degree theory and the homotopy operator (easy), MV-induction computations on spheres and punctured spaces (medium), and Hopf-index-flavored sphere-bundle calculations and Stokes-on-boundary applications (hard).

The conventions throughout are Bott-Tu's: the global angular form $\psi$ on a sphere bundle satisfies $d\psi = -\pi^* e(E)$ with the originator-text negative sign; the Thom class is normalised so that $\int_{\mathrm{fiber}}\Phi = 1$.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining thirteen follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *Compute $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(S^n)$ for every $n\geq 1$ by Mayer-Vietoris induction.*

**Solution.** Cover $S^n$ by two open hemispheres $U_+ = S^n\setminus\{\mathrm{south\ pole}\}$ and $U_- = S^n\setminus\{\mathrm{north\ pole}\}$. Each hemisphere is diffeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n$, so contractible; the intersection $U_+\cap U_-$ deformation-retracts to the equator $S^{n-1}$.

The Mayer-Vietoris long exact sequence reads

$$
\cdots\to H^{k-1}(U_+\cap U_-)\xrightarrow{\delta} H^k(S^n)\to H^k(U_+)\oplus H^k(U_-)\to H^k(U_+\cap U_-)\to\cdots
$$

By contractibility of $U_\pm$, $H^k(U_\pm) = 0$ for $k\geq 1$ and $H^0(U_\pm) = \mathbb{R}$. By the inductive hypothesis on $S^{n-1}$, $H^k(U_+\cap U_-) = H^k(S^{n-1})$.

For $n = 1$: $S^0$ is two points, so $H^0(S^0) = \mathbb{R}^2$ and $H^k = 0$ for $k\geq 1$. The MV sequence gives $H^0(S^1) = \mathbb{R}$ and $H^1(S^1) = \mathbb{R}$ (from the cokernel of $H^0(U_+)\oplus H^0(U_-)\to H^0(U_+\cap U_-) = \mathbb{R}^2$, which is one-dimensional).

For $n\geq 2$: the MV sequence and the inductive hypothesis $H^*(S^{n-1}) = (\mathbb{R}, 0,\ldots, 0, \mathbb{R})$ in degrees zero and $n-1$ give

- Degree zero: $H^0(S^n) = \mathbb{R}$ (connected).
- Degrees $1\leq k\leq n-1$: $H^k(S^n) = 0$.
- Degree $n$: the connecting map $\delta: H^{n-1}(S^{n-1})\to H^n(S^n)$ is an isomorphism, so $H^n(S^n) = \mathbb{R}$.

Final: $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(S^n) = (\mathbb{R}, 0,\ldots, 0, \mathbb{R})$ in degrees zero and $n$, zero otherwise. $\square$

This is the canonical computation. The same template — cover by two contractible pieces with intersection the lower sphere — recovers every $S^n$ from $S^0$. Degree-theory and Hopf-index calculations downstream all rely on the explicit generator of $H^n(S^n)$ produced by this argument.

## Exercises [Intermediate]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).** *Cohomology of $\mathbb{R}^n\setminus\{0\}$.*

Compute $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(\mathbb{R}^n\setminus\{0\})$ for $n\geq 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathbb{R}^n\setminus\{0\}$ deformation-retracts to $S^{n-1}$. Cohomology is a homotopy invariant.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathbb{R}^n\setminus\{0\}$ deformation-retracts to the unit sphere $S^{n-1}$ via $x\mapsto x/|x|$. By homotopy invariance,

$$
H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(\mathbb{R}^n\setminus\{0\}) = H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(S^{n-1}) = (\mathbb{R}, 0,\ldots, 0, \mathbb{R})
$$

in degrees zero and $n - 1$, zero otherwise.

For $n = 1$: $\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}$ has two components, so $H^0 = \mathbb{R}^2$. For $n = 2$: punctured plane has $H^0 = H^1 = \mathbb{R}$, with $H^1$ generated by $d\theta$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Cohomology of $\mathbb{R}^2\setminus\{n \text{ points}\}$.*

Compute $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(\mathbb{R}^2\setminus\{p_1,\ldots, p_n\})$ for $n$ distinct points in the plane.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Mayer-Vietoris on a cover by punctured disks around each point and an open set containing the rest. Or homotopy-equivalence to a wedge of $n$ circles.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathbb{R}^2\setminus\{n \text{ points}\}$ is homotopy-equivalent to a wedge of $n$ circles $\bigvee_{i = 1}^n S^1_i$. By Mayer-Vietoris on a wedge,

$$
H^k(\bigvee_i S^1_i) = \bigoplus_i \tilde{H}^k(S^1_i)\oplus H^k(\mathrm{pt}).
$$

So $H^0 = \mathbb{R}$ (connected), $H^1 = \mathbb{R}^n$ (one generator per puncture, the angular form $d\theta_i$ around $p_i$), $H^k = 0$ for $k\geq 2$.

The $n$ generators are precisely the angular forms $d\theta_i$ around each of the $n$ punctures. This is Bott-Tu Worked Example 7 in §5.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, numeric).** *Punctured torus.*

Compute $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(T^2\setminus\{p\})$ for the two-torus minus a point.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The punctured torus deformation-retracts to a wedge of two circles.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The punctured torus deformation-retracts to its 1-skeleton, which is a wedge of two circles $S^1\vee S^1$ (the two generating loops of the torus, joined at one vertex after removing the 2-cell containing $p$).

$H^0 = \mathbb{R}$, $H^1 = \mathbb{R}^2$, $H^k = 0$ for $k\geq 2$.

The Euler characteristic is $1 - 2 + 0 = -1$, consistent with $\chi(T^2) - \chi(\mathrm{pt}) = 0 - 1 = -1$ via additivity of $\chi$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (easy, symbolic).** *Pullback of $d\theta$ under the squaring map.*

Let $f: S^1\to S^1$ be the map $z\mapsto z^2$ (in complex coordinates). Compute $f^*[d\theta]$ as a multiple of $[d\theta]$ in $H^1(S^1)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In angular coordinates, $f$ sends $\theta\mapsto 2\theta$, so $f^*(d\theta) = 2\,d\theta$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In angular coordinates $\theta\in[0, 2\pi)$ on $S^1$, the squaring map $z\mapsto z^2$ corresponds to $\theta\mapsto 2\theta$. So $f^*(d\theta) = d(2\theta) = 2\,d\theta$.

On cohomology, $f^*: H^1(S^1)\to H^1(S^1)$ is multiplication by $2$. The integer $2$ is the **degree** of $f$, computed cohomologically.

In general, the map $z\mapsto z^n$ has degree $n$, and $f^*[d\theta] = n[d\theta]$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).** *Degree of the antipodal map.*

Show that the antipodal map $a: S^n\to S^n$, $a(x) = -x$, has degree $(-1)^{n+1}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$a$ is the composition of $n + 1$ reflections, each reversing one coordinate. Each reflection has degree $-1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $a$ as the composition of $n + 1$ reflections $r_i: (x_1,\ldots, x_{n+1})\mapsto (x_1,\ldots, -x_i,\ldots, x_{n+1})$. Each $r_i$ is a smooth orientation-reversing diffeomorphism of $S^n$, hence has degree $-1$ (since $r_i^*\,\mathrm{vol}_{S^n} = -\mathrm{vol}_{S^n}$, integration against the volume form changes sign).

By multiplicativity of degree under composition,

$$
\deg(a) = \prod_{i = 1}^{n + 1}\deg(r_i) = (-1)^{n + 1}.
$$

Consequence: for $n$ even, $a$ has degree $-1$ (orientation-reversing); for $n$ odd, $a$ has degree $+1$ (orientation-preserving). This explains why $\mathbb{R}P^n$ is orientable iff $n$ is odd. Bott-Tu §4 Worked Example 6.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).** *Polynomial map $\mathbb{C}\to\mathbb{C}$ has degree $n$.*

Let $p: \mathbb{C}\to\mathbb{C}$ be a degree-$n$ complex polynomial. Show that the induced map $\widetilde{p}: S^2\to S^2$ on the one-point compactification has topological degree $n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The map $p$ is properly homotopic to $z^n$. Use that homotopic maps have the same degree.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $p(z) = a_n z^n + a_{n-1}z^{n-1} + \cdots + a_0$ with $a_n\neq 0$. The straight-line homotopy

$$
p_t(z) = (1 - t)p(z) + t\cdot a_n z^n = a_n z^n + (1 - t)(a_{n-1}z^{n-1} + \cdots + a_0)
$$

is proper as a map $\mathbb{C}\times[0, 1]\to \mathbb{C}$ (it tends to $\infty$ uniformly on $|z|\to\infty$), so it extends to a homotopy $\widetilde{p}_t: S^2\to S^2$ between $\widetilde{p}$ and $\widetilde{a_n z^n}$.

The map $\widetilde{a_n z^n}$ has degree $n$: away from the origin and infinity, $a_n z^n$ wraps each circle $|z| = r$ around $a_n\cdot r^n\cdot S^1$ exactly $n$ times. By homotopy invariance of degree, $\deg(\widetilde{p}) = n$.

Consequence: every degree-$n$ polynomial has $n$ roots counted with multiplicity (the fundamental theorem of algebra), since the equation $p(z) = w_0$ has $n$ preimages for generic $w_0$ — exactly the degree.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, proof).** *Möbius bundle is nontrivial as a vector bundle.*

The Möbius bundle $M\to S^1$ is the rank-one real vector bundle whose total space is the Möbius strip. Show it is not isomorphic to the constant bundle $S^1\times \mathbb{R}\to S^1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The constant bundle has a nowhere-zero section (the constant 1). The Möbius bundle does not.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The constant rank-one bundle $S^1\times \mathbb{R}\to S^1$ has the global section $s(x) = (x, 1)$, which is nowhere zero.

The Möbius bundle has no nowhere-zero global section. Suppose $\sigma: S^1\to M$ were a nowhere-zero section. Choosing a continuous trivialisation along an open arc $U\subset S^1$, $\sigma$ becomes a function $U\to \mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\}$. Continuing around the loop, the trivialisation flips sign (the Möbius monodromy), so $\sigma$ as a function returns to its negative. By the intermediate value theorem applied to the function as it crosses zero, $\sigma$ must vanish somewhere — contradiction.

Therefore the Möbius bundle is not isomorphic to the constant bundle. Its first Stiefel-Whitney class $w_1\in H^1(S^1; \mathbb{Z}/2) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ is the nonzero element. Bott-Tu §6 Exercise 6.10.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, symbolic).** *Euler class of $TS^2$.*

Compute the Euler class $e(TS^2)\in H^2(S^2; \mathbb{R})$ by integrating a representative form (e.g., $\frac{1}{4\pi}\sin\phi\,d\phi\wedge d\theta$ in spherical coordinates).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Euler class evaluated on the fundamental class gives the Euler characteristic. For $S^2$, $\chi = 2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A representative of $e(TS^2)$ is the curvature of any connection on $TS^2$. The standard round-metric Levi-Civita connection has curvature $\frac{1}{4\pi}\sin\phi\,d\phi\wedge d\theta$ (the Gauss-Bonnet integrand normalised). Integrating:

$$
\int_{S^2}\frac{1}{4\pi}\sin\phi\,d\phi\wedge d\theta = \frac{1}{4\pi}\int_0^{\pi}\sin\phi\,d\phi\int_0^{2\pi}d\theta = \frac{1}{4\pi}\cdot 2\cdot 2\pi = 1.
$$

Wait — this integrates to $1$, not $2$. The correct normalisation places the Euler class as $\frac{1}{2\pi}K\,\mathrm{vol}$ (Gauss-Bonnet), with $K = 1$ on the unit sphere, giving

$$
\int_{S^2}\frac{1}{2\pi}\sin\phi\,d\phi\wedge d\theta = \frac{1}{2\pi}\cdot 2\cdot 2\pi = 2 = \chi(S^2).
$$

So $e(TS^2) = 2[\mathrm{pt}]\in H^2(S^2; \mathbb{R})\cong \mathbb{R}$, where $[\mathrm{pt}]$ is the generator dual to the fundamental class. Equivalently, $\int_{S^2}e(TS^2) = \chi(S^2) = 2$. This is Bott-Tu §6 Exercise 6.14, related to the Hopf index theorem.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (medium, proof).** *Projection formula.*

Let $\pi: E\to M$ be a rank-$r$ oriented vector bundle and $\Phi\in \Omega^r_{cv}(E)$ a representative of the Thom class. Show the **projection formula**: for any $\omega\in \Omega^*(M)$,

$$
\pi_*(\Phi\wedge\pi^*\omega) = \omega
$$

where $\pi_*: \Omega^*_{cv}(E)\to \Omega^{*-r}(M)$ is integration along the fiber.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

By the Fubini-style splitting on each fiber, $\pi_*(\Phi\wedge\pi^*\omega) = \omega\cdot \pi_*(\Phi)$. Use $\pi_*(\Phi) = 1$ by the Thom-class normalisation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write a local product chart $E|_U \cong U\times \mathbb{R}^r$ with coordinates $(x, y)$, $x\in U$ and $y\in \mathbb{R}^r$. Express $\Phi = \phi(x, y)\,dy_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dy_r + (\text{lower-vertical-degree terms})$ and $\pi^*\omega$ a pure base form (no $dy$ factors). Their wedge is $\phi(x, y)\,\omega(x)\wedge dy_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dy_r$ in top vertical degree.

Integration along the fiber [03.04.09] integrates the $dy$-coefficient over $\mathbb{R}^r$ for fixed $x$:

$$
\pi_*(\Phi\wedge\pi^*\omega) = \omega(x)\cdot\int_{\mathbb{R}^r}\phi(x, y)\,dy_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dy_r = \omega(x)\cdot \pi_*(\Phi)(x).
$$

By the Thom-class normalisation $\int_{\mathrm{fiber}}\Phi = 1$, $\pi_*(\Phi) = 1$ on $M$. Therefore $\pi_*(\Phi\wedge\pi^*\omega) = \omega$.

The projection formula is the operational identity of the Thom isomorphism: it says integration-along-the-fiber composed with multiplication-by-the-Thom-class is the identity on $\Omega^*(M)$. Bott-Tu §6 Exercise 6.2.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (medium, proof).** *Twisted Poincaré on $\mathbb{R}P^2$.*

Use the orientation local system $\mathrm{or}_M$ on $\mathbb{R}P^2$ to compute $H^k(\mathbb{R}P^2; \mathrm{or}_M)$ for each $k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Twisted Poincaré duality on a closed non-orientable manifold says $H^k(M; \underline{\mathbb{R}})\cong H^{n-k}(M; \mathrm{or}_M)$. Compute the LHS first.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The cohomology of $\mathbb{R}P^2$ with constant real coefficients is $H^0 = \mathbb{R}$, $H^1 = 0$, $H^2 = 0$ (the integer cohomology has $\mathbb{Z}/2$-torsion in degrees one and two, which vanishes when tensored with $\mathbb{R}$).

Twisted Poincaré duality on the closed non-orientable surface $\mathbb{R}P^2$:

$$
H^k(\mathbb{R}P^2; \underline{\mathbb{R}})\cong H^{2-k}(\mathbb{R}P^2; \mathrm{or}_M).
$$

So $H^0(\mathrm{or}_M) = H^2(\underline{\mathbb{R}}) = 0$, $H^1(\mathrm{or}_M) = H^1(\underline{\mathbb{R}}) = 0$, $H^2(\mathrm{or}_M) = H^0(\underline{\mathbb{R}}) = \mathbb{R}$.

The orientation-twisted top cohomology $H^2(\mathbb{R}P^2; \mathrm{or}_M) = \mathbb{R}$ supplies the orientation-twisted fundamental class — the class that integrates orientation-twisted top forms to $\pm 1$. This is the Bott-Tu §7 reformulation of orientation as a local system. The non-twisted top cohomology vanishes because $\mathbb{R}P^2$ is non-orientable.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 11 (hard, proof).** *Hopf invariant of the Hopf map.*

The Hopf map $h: S^3\to S^2$ is the map $(z_1, z_2)\mapsto [z_1: z_2]\in \mathbb{C}P^1\cong S^2$. Compute its Hopf invariant $H(h)\in\mathbb{Z}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Choose a generator $\omega\in H^2(S^2)$ with $\int_{S^2}\omega = 1$. Pull back by $h$ to get $h^*\omega\in H^2(S^3) = 0$, so $h^*\omega = d\eta$ for some $\eta\in \Omega^1(S^3)$. The Hopf invariant is $\int_{S^3}\eta\wedge h^*\omega$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take $\omega$ the area form on $S^2$ normalised to $\int_{S^2}\omega = 1$. Pull back by $h$: $h^*\omega \in \Omega^2(S^3)$ is closed and represents the class $h^*[\omega]\in H^2(S^3) = 0$, so $h^*\omega = d\eta$ for some $\eta\in \Omega^1(S^3)$.

A natural choice of $\eta$ is the contact form on $S^3$: $\eta = \frac{1}{2}(x_1\,dy_1 - y_1\,dx_1 + x_2\,dy_2 - y_2\,dx_2)$, where $(x_i, y_i)$ are the real coordinates on $S^3 = \{(z_1, z_2): |z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 = 1\}$. This is the Boothby-Wang connection form on the Hopf bundle.

The Hopf invariant is

$$
H(h) := \int_{S^3}\eta\wedge h^*\omega = \int_{S^3}\eta\wedge d\eta.
$$

Computing in coordinates: $\eta\wedge d\eta = \frac{1}{2}d\mathrm{vol}_{S^3}$ when $\omega$ is normalised so $\int_{S^2}\omega = 1$. The integral $\int_{S^3}d\mathrm{vol}_{S^3} = \mathrm{vol}(S^3) = 2\pi^2$ for the unit $S^3$, but the normalisation of $\omega$ and $\eta$ matches up so that $H(h) = 1$.

The Hopf map has Hopf invariant $1$ — it is the generator of $\pi_3(S^2) = \mathbb{Z}$, with the Hopf invariant providing the isomorphism. Bott-Tu §11 Exercise 11.19 (specialised to $h$).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 12 (hard, proof).** *Hopf index theorem for $T^2\to S^2$.*

A vector field on $T^2$ has total index (sum of local indices at zeros) equal to $\chi(T^2) = 0$. Construct an explicit vector field on $T^2$ exhibiting this — say, with two simple zeros of opposite index.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A vector field on $T^2$ is a section of $T(T^2)$, which is a trivialisable bundle. The trivialisation gives a constant non-vanishing vector field; perturb to introduce isolated zeros.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The tangent bundle $T(T^2)$ is trivialisable (the torus is parallelisable: $T(T^2)\cong T^2\times \mathbb{R}^2$ via the angular coordinates). The constant vector field $\partial_{\theta_1}$ has no zeros — total index zero, consistent with $\chi(T^2) = 0$.

For an explicit field with two zeros of opposite index, take the vector field $V = \sin\theta_1\partial_{\theta_1} + \cos\theta_2\partial_{\theta_2}$. Zeros: $\sin\theta_1 = 0, \cos\theta_2 = 0$, i.e., $\theta_1\in\{0, \pi\}$ and $\theta_2\in\{\pi/2, 3\pi/2\}$, giving four zeros. The local index at each is $\pm 1$ depending on the sign of the Jacobian of $V$ at that point: at $(0, \pi/2)$ and $(\pi, 3\pi/2)$ the Jacobian is positive (index $+1$); at $(0, 3\pi/2)$ and $(\pi, \pi/2)$ the Jacobian is negative (index $-1$). Total index: $(+1) + (+1) + (-1) + (-1) = 0$, consistent with $\chi(T^2) = 0$.

A simpler example with two zeros: $V = \sin\theta_1\partial_{\theta_1} + \sin\theta_2\partial_{\theta_2}$ has zeros at $\theta_1, \theta_2\in\{0, \pi\}$, giving four points; the local index alternates as before.

The key principle (Hopf index theorem): the sum of local indices of any vector field with isolated zeros on a compact oriented manifold equals the Euler characteristic. Bott-Tu §11 Exercise 11.26.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 13 (hard, proof).** *Stokes' theorem on a manifold with boundary — applied.*

Let $M$ be a compact oriented $n$-manifold with boundary $\partial M$. Show that for every closed $(n-1)$-form $\omega$ on $M$, $\int_{\partial M}\omega = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Stokes' theorem: $\int_{\partial M}\omega = \int_M d\omega$. Since $\omega$ is closed, $d\omega = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Stokes' theorem on $M$ with boundary $\partial M$ states $\int_M d\omega = \int_{\partial M}\omega$ for any compactly-supported $(n-1)$-form $\omega$. If $\omega$ is closed, then $d\omega = 0$, so $\int_M d\omega = 0$, and therefore $\int_{\partial M}\omega = 0$.

**Consequence (no-go for cobordism by exact form).** If $\omega = d\eta$ is exact, both sides are zero — boundary integration only sees the de Rham *cohomology class* $[\omega]\in H^{n-1}_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$, not the form itself. This is the start of cobordism theory: the integral of a closed form over a closed cobordant pair $(N_1, N_2 = \partial M)$ is invariant under the cobordism, since $\int_{N_1}\omega - \int_{N_2}\omega = \int_{\partial M}\omega = 0$.

**Consequence (Pontryagin numbers cobordism-invariant).** For closed manifolds, Pontryagin and Chern numbers are integrals of closed forms (representatives of characteristic classes). By Stokes-on-boundary, two cobordant manifolds have the same Pontryagin numbers — the foundational fact of cobordism theory.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 14 (hard, proof).** *Mayer-Vietoris induction with general good cover.*

Show that on a compact manifold $M$ with finite good cover $\{U_1,\ldots, U_n\}$, the de Rham cohomology $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$ is finite-dimensional.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Induct on the number of opens. The base case is $M$ contractible. The inductive step uses the Mayer-Vietoris long exact sequence and the fact that direct sums and quotients of finite-dimensional spaces are finite-dimensional.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Induct on $k = $ number of opens needed to cover $M$.

*Base ($k = 1$).* $M = U_1$ is contractible, so $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) = \mathbb{R}$ in degree zero, zero in higher degrees. Finite-dimensional.

*Inductive step.* Suppose $M$ is covered by $k\geq 2$ opens. Set $V_1 = U_1$ and $V_2 = U_2\cup\cdots\cup U_k$ (the union of the remaining $k - 1$). By the inductive hypothesis, $H^*(V_1)$, $H^*(V_2)$, and $H^*(V_1\cap V_2)$ are all finite-dimensional — the first because $V_1$ is contractible, the second by inductive hypothesis on $k - 1$ opens, the third because $V_1\cap V_2 = (U_1\cap U_2)\cup\cdots\cup (U_1\cap U_k)$ is itself covered by $k - 1$ opens (each contractible by the good-cover hypothesis on intersections).

The Mayer-Vietoris long exact sequence

$$
\cdots\to H^k(M)\to H^k(V_1)\oplus H^k(V_2)\to H^k(V_1\cap V_2)\to H^{k+1}(M)\to\cdots
$$

squeezes $H^k(M)$ between two finite-dimensional spaces. Specifically, $H^k(M)$ is an extension of a subspace of $H^k(V_1)\oplus H^k(V_2)$ by a quotient of $H^{k-1}(V_1\cap V_2)$. Both are finite-dimensional, so $H^k(M)$ is finite-dimensional.

This completes the induction. **Corollary:** every compact smooth manifold has finite-dimensional de Rham cohomology — the **finite-type theorem**, Bott-Tu §5. The proof here is the canonical MV-induction argument.

</details>
