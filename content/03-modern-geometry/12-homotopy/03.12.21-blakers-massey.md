## Intuition [Beginner]

The Blakers-Massey theorem answers a question that homology answers easily but homotopy refuses to answer. If a space splits as the union of two overlapping pieces, can the homotopy groups of the union be reconstructed from the homotopy groups of the pieces and their overlap? Homology gives a yes through the Mayer-Vietoris sequence, but homotopy gives a no in general. The Blakers-Massey theorem says the no becomes a yes inside a controlled range of dimensions, with the range determined by how connected each piece is.

The intuition is that homotopy and homology agree in low dimensions when the pieces are highly connected, and they agree because the geometry has no room to disagree. A piece that is $m$-connected has no interesting maps from spheres of dimension below $m$, so anything happening in the union below dimension $m + n$ comes from a single piece, just as in homology. Above this range, the union starts seeing genuinely new homotopy classes that were invisible to either piece on its own.

The reason this matters is the Freudenthal suspension theorem. Suspending a space stretches it into a pair of cones glued along a copy of the original, and the Blakers-Massey theorem applied to that pair is what controls how suspension affects homotopy groups. Stable homotopy theory is born from this control.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute the suspension stabilisation range for the $2$-sphere $S^2$ using the Blakers-Massey idea.

Step 1. The suspension of $S^2$ is the $3$-sphere $S^3$. Write $S^3$ as the union of its upper cone $C_+ S^2$ and lower cone $C_- S^2$ glued along the equatorial $S^2$.

Step 2. Each cone is contractible, and the equator $S^2$ is $1$-connected. The pair $(C_+ S^2, S^2)$ being formed by attaching a single $3$-cell to $S^2$ is $2$-connected, since the cone provides a null-homotopy for every map from a sphere of dimension up to $2$. The same holds for $(C_- S^2, S^2)$.

Step 3. The Blakers-Massey theorem with $m = n = 2$ says the inclusion of pairs $(C_+ S^2, S^2) \hookrightarrow (S^3, C_- S^2)$ induces an isomorphism on relative homotopy groups in dimensions below $m + n = 4$, and a surjection in dimension $4$.

Step 4. Combining with the long exact sequence of the pair $(C_+ S^2, S^2)$ and the contractibility of the cones, the suspension map $\pi_k(S^2) \to \pi_{k+1}(S^3)$ is an isomorphism for $k < 3$ and a surjection for $k = 3$. So $\pi_2(S^2) = \pi_3(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}$ confirms an isomorphism, and $\pi_3(S^2) \to \pi_4(S^3)$ is a surjection. Both groups are $\mathbb{Z}/2$ generated by the suspension of the Hopf map, matching the prediction.

What this tells us: the suspension map is well-behaved up to a sharply specified dimension, and the Blakers-Massey theorem is what specifies the range. Above the range, the suspension can fail to be surjective and stable homotopy theory takes over.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a topological space and $A, B \subseteq X$ subspaces with $X = A \cup B$ and $C = A \cap B$ path-connected. The triple $(X; A, B)$ is called a **CW triad** when $X$ is a CW complex and $A, B$ are subcomplexes with $C$ a subcomplex of both. The pair $(A, C)$ is **$m$-connected** when $\pi_k(A, C) = 0$ for $1 \leq k \leq m$; equivalently, every map of pairs $(D^k, S^{k-1}) \to (A, C)$ for $k \leq m$ is homotopic relative to $S^{k-1}$ to a map landing in $C$.

The inclusion of pairs $(A, C) \hookrightarrow (X, B)$ induces a homomorphism on relative homotopy groups
$$
i_* : \pi_k(A, C) \to \pi_k(X, B)
$$
for each $k \geq 1$. The Blakers-Massey theorem quantifies when $i_*$ is an isomorphism.

A pair $(X, A)$ is **$m$-connected** when $\pi_k(X, A) = 0$ for $1 \leq k \leq m$. The constants $m$ and $n$ in the theorem refer to the connectivities of the two inclusion pairs $(A, C)$ and $(B, C)$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The hypothesis is on the *pairs* $(A, C)$ and $(B, C)$, not on the connectivity of $A$, $B$, or $C$ separately. A path-connected $A$ with no further structure does not give any $m$-connectedness for the pair.
- The conclusion is sharp at $k = m + n$: the map $i_*$ is surjective there but need not be injective, and the surjectivity can fail for $k = m + n + 1$.
- The intersection $C$ must be path-connected for the relative homotopy groups to be uniformly well-defined; without this hypothesis, the theorem's statement requires basepoint adjustments per component.
- Excision in homotopy fails outside the Blakers-Massey range. The Hopf map provides the canonical witness: $\pi_3(S^2) = \mathbb{Z}$ is non-zero while the corresponding ordinary excision in homology returns $H_3(S^2) = 0$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Blakers-Massey, homotopy excision; Hatcher Theorem 4.23).** *Let $(X; A, B)$ be a CW triad with $C = A \cap B$ path-connected. Suppose the inclusion $(A, C) \hookrightarrow (X, B)$ induces a pair $(X, B)$ that is $m$-connected, in the sense that the relative homotopy groups $\pi_k(A, C) = 0$ for $k \leq m$, and likewise the inclusion $(B, C) \hookrightarrow (X, A)$ has $\pi_k(B, C) = 0$ for $k \leq n$. Then the inclusion-induced map*
$$
i_* : \pi_k(A, C) \to \pi_k(X, B)
$$
*is an isomorphism for $1 \leq k < m + n$ and a surjection for $k = m + n$.*

**Proof.** The argument proceeds by induction on the cell decomposition of the triad, ultimately reducing the general statement to a single explicit model space $X = D^p \cup_{S^{p-1}} D^q$ glued along a common boundary sphere.

Step one — reduction by cellular approximation. By cellular approximation applied to a representing map $f : (D^k, S^{k-1}) \to (X, B)$, the image of $f$ may be assumed to lie in a finite subcomplex of $X$. Since the connectivities of $(A, C)$ and $(B, C)$ are controlled by the cells of $A \setminus C$ and $B \setminus C$, the argument reduces to the case where $A$ has a single relative cell of some dimension $p \geq m + 1$ above $C$ and $B$ has a single relative cell of some dimension $q \geq n + 1$ above $C$. The induction on the number of cells of each type is straightforward: each new cell either preserves the connectivity range or extends it.

Step two — the model space. After the reduction, write $A = C \cup_{S^{p-1}} D^p$ and $B = C \cup_{S^{q-1}} D^q$ with the attaching maps lying in $C$. The triad becomes
$$
X = C \cup_{S^{p-1}} D^p \cup_{S^{q-1}} D^q,
$$
with $D^p$ attached along its boundary to $C$ via one map and $D^q$ attached along its boundary to $C$ via another. The relative homotopy groups $\pi_k(A, C)$ and $\pi_k(X, B)$ each become the homotopy groups of an explicit pushout that can be analysed by direct geometric methods.

Step three — explicit computation in the model. The pair $(A, C)$ is the cofibre of an attached $p$-cell, so its relative homotopy groups vanish below dimension $p$ and equal $\mathbb{Z}$ in dimension $p$ (by excision in homology and the relative Hurewicz theorem [03.12.19] applied to a wedge of spheres). The pair $(X, B)$ is similarly described: $X / B$ is homotopy equivalent to the smash $D^p / S^{p-1} = S^p$ joined with $D^q / S^{q-1} = S^q$, modulo cells of dimension exceeding $p + q$. Concretely, a representative of an element of $\pi_k(X, B)$ is a map $(D^k, S^{k-1}) \to (X, B)$ which by cellular approximation is homotopic to a map intersecting the interior of $D^p$ transversely; the inverse image is then a $(k - p)$-dimensional submanifold mapped to a single point, contributing a non-zero class only when $k \geq p + q$.

Step four — comparison of the inclusion. The map $i_* : \pi_k(A, C) \to \pi_k(X, B)$ in the model is surjective by construction (every $(D^k, S^{k-1})$ representative in $(X, B)$ deforms into $(A, C)$ once the codimension condition $k < p + q$ is met) and injective for $k < p + q$ (any two representatives in $(A, C)$ that become equivalent in $(X, B)$ admit a homotopy in $(X, B)$ which by the same codimension condition can be deformed to lie in $(A, C)$). With $p \geq m + 1$ and $q \geq n + 1$, the bound $p + q \geq m + n + 2$ gives the desired range $k < m + n + 1$ for isomorphism, and surjectivity at $k = m + n$ follows from the codimension count being borderline. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Blakers-Massey theorem builds toward the entire infrastructure of stable homotopy theory and the modern $\infty$-categorical reformulation of algebraic topology. The foundational reason it holds is that excision in homology is a universal property of the long exact sequence of a pair, while excision in homotopy is a *connectivity-bounded* property of pushouts: in a CW pushout square, the comparison map of relative homotopy groups becomes an isomorphism in a range determined by the connectivities of the two arms. This is exactly the same range that controls the Freudenthal suspension theorem, since the suspension of a space presents itself as a pushout $\Sigma X = C_+ X \cup_X C_- X$ of two contractible cones glued along $X$. The central insight is that homotopy excision is a quantitative statement: where ordinary excision is exact, homotopy excision is approximately exact, with the approximation governed by the bottom-dimensional connectivity of the pieces.

Putting these together, the theorem reduces to a calculation on a single explicit model space $X = D^p \cup_{S^{p-1}} D^q$, and the calculation yields the same range $k < p + q$ that drives the Freudenthal suspension theorem. The bridge is the recognition that the suspension map is the special case of the Blakers-Massey comparison applied to the cone decomposition, and the Blakers-Massey range appears again in [03.12.03] (suspension) where the Freudenthal range $k < 2n - 1$ for an $(n-1)$-connected space is exactly the Blakers-Massey range for $m = n$ applied to the cones. The bridge is also the recognition that the failure of excision above the Blakers-Massey range is what forces stable homotopy theory into existence — every higher-dimensional measurement that fails homotopy excision is a witness to a non-stable phenomenon, and the stable range is exactly the Blakers-Massey range.

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Compare excision in homology and Blakers-Massey in homotopy on the same data: a CW triad $(X; A, B)$ with $X = A \cup B$ and $C = A \cap B$. State precisely where the two theorems agree and where they diverge.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

State the Mayer-Vietoris-style long exact sequence for homotopy that holds *inside* the Blakers-Massey range, and explain why it fails outside the range.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Inside the Blakers-Massey range, the relative homotopy groups of $(X, B)$ and $(A, C)$ coincide, so the long exact sequence of the pair $(X, B)$ feeds into a Mayer-Vietoris-type exact sequence for the triad. Outside the range, the identification of relative groups breaks, and the sequence loses exactness at the relevant degree.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Inside the Blakers-Massey range $k < m + n$, the long exact sequence of the triad reads
$$
\cdots \to \pi_{k+1}(X, B) \to \pi_k(C) \to \pi_k(A) \oplus \pi_k(B) \to \pi_k(X) \to \pi_{k-1}(C) \to \cdots
$$
matching the Mayer-Vietoris sequence in homology under the Hurewicz comparison [03.12.19]. Outside the range, the identification $\pi_k(A, C) \cong \pi_k(X, B)$ fails, and the sequence ceases to be exact at the third term. The failure is detected by classes that survive the suspension stabilisation but that ordinary excision destroys; the Hopf map and its higher suspension stabilisations are the canonical examples. Rubric: full credit for the inside-range exact sequence and a worked failure example outside.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove the Freudenthal suspension theorem from the Blakers-Massey theorem in detail. Specifically: given an $(n-1)$-connected based CW complex $X$ with $n \geq 1$, show that the suspension map $\Sigma : \pi_k(X) \to \pi_{k+1}(\Sigma X)$ is an isomorphism for $k < 2n - 1$ and a surjection for $k = 2n - 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Decompose $\Sigma X = C_+ X \cup C_- X$ glued along $X$, with both cones contractible. Apply Blakers-Massey to the triad $(\Sigma X; C_+ X, C_- X)$ and compose with the long exact sequence of the pair $(C_+ X, X)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Set $A = C_+ X$ and $B = C_- X$, so $X = A \cup B$ is $\Sigma X$ and $C = A \cap B$ is the equatorial copy of $X$. Both cones are contractible, so the pairs $(A, C)$ and $(B, C)$ each have the homotopy type of $(C X, X)$. The pair $(C X, X)$ is $n$-connected, since $X$ is $(n-1)$-connected and the cone provides a null-homotopy of any map of a $k$-disk into $C X$ relative to its boundary in $X$ for $k \leq n$. By Blakers-Massey with $m = n$, the inclusion $i_* : \pi_k(C_+ X, X) \to \pi_k(\Sigma X, C_- X)$ is an isomorphism for $k < 2n$ and a surjection for $k = 2n$. The long exact sequence of $(C_+ X, X)$ gives $\pi_k(C_+ X, X) \cong \pi_{k-1}(X)$ via the connecting boundary, since $\pi_k(C_+ X) = 0$. Similarly the long exact sequence of $(\Sigma X, C_- X)$ gives $\pi_k(\Sigma X, C_- X) \cong \pi_k(\Sigma X)$ (after a degree shift, since the cone is contractible). Combining, the suspension map $\pi_{k-1}(X) \to \pi_k(\Sigma X)$ is an isomorphism for $k < 2n$ and a surjection for $k = 2n$. Re-indexing $k - 1$ as $k$ gives the Freudenthal range $k < 2n - 1$ for isomorphism and $k = 2n - 1$ for surjectivity. Rubric: full credit for the cone identification, the Blakers-Massey application, and the long-exact-sequence chase.

</details>

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Use Blakers-Massey to identify the lowest non-zero stable homotopy group $\pi_n^{\mathrm{st}}(S^0) = \mathrm{colim}_k \pi_{n+k}(S^k)$ for $n = 1$, namely $\pi_1^{\mathrm{st}} = \mathbb{Z}/2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Hopf map $\eta : S^3 \to S^2$ generates $\pi_3(S^2) = \mathbb{Z}$. By Freudenthal/Blakers-Massey, the suspensions $\Sigma^j \eta : S^{3+j} \to S^{2+j}$ stabilise from $j = 1$ onwards. Compute the stable target.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Hopf map $\eta : S^3 \to S^2$ generates $\pi_3(S^2) = \mathbb{Z}$. Suspending once gives $\Sigma \eta : S^4 \to S^3$ in $\pi_4(S^3)$. By Freudenthal applied to $X = S^2$ (which is $1$-connected, so $n = 2$), the suspension map $\pi_3(S^2) \to \pi_4(S^3)$ is a *surjection* but not an isomorphism (we are at the boundary $k = 2n - 1 = 3$). Computation gives $\pi_4(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}/2$, so the suspension kills the even multiples of $\eta$ and identifies $\Sigma \eta$ with the generator of $\mathbb{Z}/2$. Suspending again gives $\Sigma^2 \eta \in \pi_5(S^4)$, and by Freudenthal applied to $S^3$ (now $2$-connected, $n = 3$, range $k < 5$), the suspension $\pi_4(S^3) \to \pi_5(S^4)$ is an *isomorphism*, so $\pi_5(S^4) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ generated by $\Sigma^2 \eta$. Further suspensions remain isomorphisms by Freudenthal applied to higher-connected spheres, so $\pi_1^{\mathrm{st}} = \mathbb{Z}/2$ generated by $\eta$ as a stable class. Rubric: full credit for the iterated Freudenthal application and the identification of the stable group.

</details>

theorem blakersMassey_excision

The proof gap is substantive. Mathlib needs the relative homotopy groups as a uniformly developed family, the cellular approximation theorem for triads, the homotopy pushout structure of $\Sigma X = C_+ X \cup_X C_- X$, the explicit model space $D^p \cup_{S^{p-1}} D^q$ as a pushout in `TopCat`, and the inductive cellular reduction. ## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (Blakers-Massey, sharp form).** *Let $(X; A, B)$ be a CW triad with $C = A \cap B$ path-connected, the pair $(A, C)$ being $m$-connected and the pair $(B, C)$ being $n$-connected, with $m, n \geq 1$. The inclusion-induced map $i_* : \pi_k(A, C) \to \pi_k(X, B)$ is an isomorphism for $1 \leq k < m + n$, a surjection for $k = m + n$, and the kernel of $i_*$ at $k = m + n$ is generated by Whitehead products of classes from $\pi_*(A, C)$ and $\pi_*(B, C)$ in suitable bidegrees.*

The Whitehead product description of the kernel at the boundary degree is the content of the original Blakers-Massey four-paper series in *Annals of Math.* The kernel at $k = m + n$ is the image of the Whitehead-product map $\pi_{m+1}(A, C) \otimes \pi_{n+1}(B, C) \to \pi_{m+n+1}(X)$, restricted to elements that bound in the relative groups. The general Whitehead-product theorem for triads is the structural reason that the boundary degree is a surjection but not an isomorphism.

**Theorem (homotopy pushout reformulation).** *Let $\square$ be a homotopy pushout square of based spaces*
$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
C & \to & A \\
\downarrow & & \downarrow \\
B & \to & X
\end{array}
$$
*with $(A, C)$ $m$-connected and $(B, C)$ $n$-connected. The comparison map of homotopy fibres of the right and bottom arrows*
$$
\mathrm{hofib}(B \to X) \to \mathrm{hofib}(C \to A)
$$
*is $(m + n - 1)$-connected.*

This is the homotopy-pushout / homotopy-pullback reformulation due to Mather, Goodwillie, and others. The connectivity bound $m + n - 1$ is what later gets categorified to the higher-topoi statement of Anel-Biedermann-Finster-Joyal.

**Theorem (Freudenthal suspension as corollary).** *Let $X$ be an $(n-1)$-connected based CW complex with $n \geq 1$. The suspension map $\Sigma : \pi_k(X) \to \pi_{k+1}(\Sigma X)$ is an isomorphism for $k < 2n - 1$ and a surjection for $k = 2n - 1$.*

The Freudenthal range is exactly the Blakers-Massey range applied to the cone decomposition $\Sigma X = C_+ X \cup_X C_- X$, with both cone-pairs being $n$-connected because $X$ is $(n-1)$-connected and the cone provides a null-homotopy in the cone of any map of a $k$-disk relative to its boundary in $X$ for $k \leq n$.

**Theorem (homotopy Mayer-Vietoris in the stable range).** *Inside the Blakers-Massey range $k < m + n$, the triad $(X; A, B)$ has a long exact sequence in homotopy*
$$
\cdots \to \pi_{k+1}(X) \to \pi_k(C) \to \pi_k(A) \oplus \pi_k(B) \to \pi_k(X) \to \pi_{k-1}(C) \to \cdots
$$
*matching the homology Mayer-Vietoris sequence under the Hurewicz comparison.*

The homotopy Mayer-Vietoris sequence is the bridge from homotopy to homology in the stable range and the foundation of the Adams spectral sequence at $E_2$, where the Mayer-Vietoris-type splitting of the sphere spectrum into a wedge with cofibre is what controls the differentials in low degrees.

**Theorem (higher-categorical generalisation; Anel-Biedermann-Finster-Joyal 2017).** *Let $\mathcal{T}$ be an $\infty$-topos and $f : A \to X$, $g : B \to X$ morphisms with $A, B \to X$ being respectively $m$- and $n$-connected in the $\infty$-topos sense. The pullback comparison map of fibre objects in the homotopy pullback square is $(m + n - 1)$-connected.*

The Anel-Biedermann-Finster-Joyal theorem reformulates Blakers-Massey as an intrinsic statement about $\infty$-topos localisations: connectivity is a modal property, and the Blakers-Massey connectivity bound is the universal statement of how connectivity propagates through pullback-pushout squares. The same statement holds in modal homotopy type theory as proved internally by Finster-Rijke; the connectivity statement is then a theorem about the interaction of two left-exact modalities.

**Theorem (Goodwillie calculus, first derivative).** *In Goodwillie's calculus of functors on based spaces, the linearisation $P_1 F$ of a homotopy functor $F$ satisfies a Blakers-Massey-type connectivity statement: the comparison from $F$ to $P_1 F$ is highly connected on highly-connected spaces, with the connectivity bound governed by the cross-effects of $F$.*

The Goodwillie tower of a functor is a sequence of polynomial approximations whose convergence is controlled by Blakers-Massey-type connectivity bounds, and the first stage of the tower is exactly the suspension stabilisation governed by Freudenthal — itself the corollary of Blakers-Massey applied to cone decompositions.

**Synthesis.** The Blakers-Massey theorem is the foundational reason that excision in homotopy holds approximately, with the approximation governed by the connectivities of the pieces of a CW triad. The central insight is that homotopy excision is a quantitative reformulation of homology excision: where homology exact-sequences are unconditional, homotopy exact-sequences are exact in a range determined by the bottom-dimensional connectivity of the pieces. Putting these together, the cellular induction reduces the general theorem to a single explicit model space $X = D^p \cup_{S^{p-1}} D^q$ where the comparison map is computed by a transversality argument in codimension $p + q$; the corollaries are immediate. Freudenthal's suspension theorem is the special case applied to cone decompositions; the homotopy Mayer-Vietoris sequence is the unrolled exact form of the comparison; the higher-categorical generalisation of Anel-Biedermann-Finster-Joyal is the intrinsic statement in $\infty$-topoi; the Goodwillie tower is the iterated polynomial approximation whose convergence is Blakers-Massey at every stage.

This is exactly the same connectivity-controlled comparison that drives stable homotopy theory as a separate discipline from unstable homotopy theory: the stable range is the Blakers-Massey range, and the stable category is the $\infty$-categorical localisation at maps that are isomorphisms in the Blakers-Massey range. The bridge to suspension theory [03.12.03] is the Freudenthal corollary, which identifies the suspension map's iso range with the Blakers-Massey range applied to cone decompositions; the bridge to Hurewicz theory [03.12.19] is the comparison of the relative homotopy and homology groups inside the stable range, where the two are isomorphic by the Hurewicz theorem applied skeleton-by-skeleton; the bridge to spectra [03.12.04] is the iterated stabilisation of the suspension map, whose convergence is Blakers-Massey at every stage. Putting these together, the Blakers-Massey theorem is the dimensional anchor of stable homotopy theory: where excision in homotopy holds, the homotopy and homology of a triad agree; where excision fails, the failure is the content of unstable phenomena. The bridge specialises the homology Mayer-Vietoris sequence to a homotopy Mayer-Vietoris sequence inside the stable range, generalises to the connectivity statement in $\infty$-topoi, and is dual to the Goodwillie tower viewed as iterated polynomial approximation.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Theorem (Blakers-Massey, homotopy excision), proof.** Let $(X; A, B)$ be a CW triad with $C = A \cap B$ path-connected, $(A, C)$ being $m$-connected, and $(B, C)$ being $n$-connected, with $m, n \geq 1$. The argument reduces by induction on cell decomposition to a single explicit model.

By cellular approximation applied to a representing pair $(D^k, S^{k-1}) \to (X, B)$, the image lies in a finite subcomplex of $X$. The connectivity of $(A, C)$ is controlled by the cells of $A \setminus C$: these have dimension at least $m + 1$, since attaching a cell of dimension $d \leq m$ to $C$ would create a non-zero element of $\pi_d(A, C)$ via the characteristic map of the cell. Similarly the cells of $B \setminus C$ have dimension at least $n + 1$. The induction on the number of cells of each type proceeds as follows: each new cell either preserves the connectivity range (because its boundary lies in the previous skeleton, which already satisfies the connectivity condition) or extends it (in the boundary case, by precisely one dimension).

Reduce to the model: $A = C \cup_{S^{p-1}} D^p$ and $B = C \cup_{S^{q-1}} D^q$ with $p \geq m + 1$ and $q \geq n + 1$, and the attaching maps $\varphi_A : S^{p-1} \to C$ and $\varphi_B : S^{q-1} \to C$ landing in $C$. The triad is
$$
X = C \cup_{S^{p-1}} D^p \cup_{S^{q-1}} D^q,
$$
a pushout in CW complexes.

Compute $\pi_k(A, C)$. The pair $(A, C)$ is the cofibre of an attached $p$-cell, so $\pi_k(A, C) = 0$ for $k < p$, $\pi_p(A, C) = \mathbb{Z}$ generated by the characteristic map of the $p$-cell, and $\pi_{p+1}(A, C) = $ the kernel of the boundary map $\pi_p(A, C) \to \pi_{p-1}(C)$. The relative Hurewicz theorem [03.12.19] applied to the cofibre identifies these groups with the cellular homology of the relative complex.

Compute $\pi_k(X, B)$. The pair $(X, B)$ is the cofibre of attaching the $p$-cell to $B = C \cup D^q$; equivalently, $X / B \simeq A / C \wedge \cdot$ in a sense made precise by the Whitehead-product structure. Concretely, a representative of an element of $\pi_k(X, B)$ is a map $f : (D^k, S^{k-1}) \to (X, B)$, which by cellular approximation may be assumed transverse to the interior of the $p$-cell. The inverse image $f^{-1}(\mathrm{int}(D^p))$ is a $(k - p)$-dimensional submanifold of $D^k$, and its closure projects to the $p$-cell with degree counted by the local intersection number. Similarly the $q$-cell of $B$ contributes a $(k - q)$-dimensional submanifold. When $k < p + q$, the codimension count $k - p - q < 0$ forces these submanifolds to be empty after a small perturbation (by a transversality argument in codimension $p + q$), so the map $f$ deforms into a map missing the interior of the $q$-cell, i.e., into a map landing in $A = X \setminus \mathrm{int}(D^q)$, i.e., into a representative of an element of $\pi_k(A, C)$.

Compare. The map $i_* : \pi_k(A, C) \to \pi_k(X, B)$ is surjective for $k \leq p + q$ by the deformation argument (every $(D^k, S^{k-1}) \to (X, B)$ deforms into $(A, C)$ for $k \leq p + q$, with strict inequality at the surjectivity boundary). Injectivity holds for $k < p + q$: a homotopy in $(X, B)$ between two maps in $(A, C)$ is itself a $(D^{k+1}, S^k) \to (X, B)$ representative, which by the deformation argument with $k + 1 \leq p + q$ deforms into $(A, C)$, providing a homotopy in $(A, C)$.

With $p \geq m + 1$ and $q \geq n + 1$, the bound $k < p + q$ becomes $k \leq p + q - 1 \leq (m + 1) + (n + 1) - 1 = m + n + 1$, giving the desired range $k < m + n + 1$ for isomorphism (i.e., $k \leq m + n$ for the boundary case being surjective and $k < m + n$ for isomorphism). Re-indexing matches the theorem statement. $\square$

**Theorem (homotopy pushout reformulation), proof.** Given the homotopy pushout square as in the statement, the homotopy fibres of the right and bottom arrows are computed via the path-loop fibration. The comparison map $\mathrm{hofib}(B \to X) \to \mathrm{hofib}(C \to A)$ at degree $k$ is identified with the inclusion $\pi_{k+1}(X, B) \to \pi_{k+1}(A, C)$ (with sign and loop-suspension convention), so the connectivity of the comparison map is one less than the Blakers-Massey range. With Blakers-Massey isomorphism for $k < m + n$, the comparison of fibres is $(m + n - 1)$-connected. $\square$

**Theorem (Freudenthal suspension as corollary), proof.** Set $A = C_+ X$, $B = C_- X$, $C = X$ embedded as the equator of $\Sigma X$. Both cones are contractible, and the pair $(C_+ X, X)$ is $n$-connected: any map $(D^k, S^{k-1}) \to (C_+ X, X)$ for $k \leq n$ deforms into $X$ by null-homotopy of the disk in the cone using the cone parameter, since $X$ is $(n-1)$-connected and the boundary $S^{k-1}$ already lies in $X$. By symmetry $(C_- X, X)$ is also $n$-connected. Apply Blakers-Massey with $m = n$: the inclusion $i_* : \pi_k(C_+ X, X) \to \pi_k(\Sigma X, C_- X)$ is an iso for $k < 2n$ and a surjection for $k = 2n$. The long exact sequence of the pair $(C_+ X, X)$ gives $\pi_k(C_+ X, X) \cong \pi_{k-1}(X)$ via the boundary, since $\pi_k(C_+ X) = 0$ for the contractible cone. Similarly the long exact sequence of $(\Sigma X, C_- X)$ gives $\pi_k(\Sigma X, C_- X) \cong \pi_k(\Sigma X)$ since the cone is contractible. Composing, the suspension map $\pi_{k-1}(X) \to \pi_k(\Sigma X)$ is an iso for $k < 2n$ and a surjection for $k = 2n$. Re-indexing: $\Sigma : \pi_k(X) \to \pi_{k+1}(\Sigma X)$ is an iso for $k < 2n - 1$ and a surjection for $k = 2n - 1$. $\square$

**Theorem (homotopy Mayer-Vietoris in the stable range), proof.** Inside the Blakers-Massey range $k < m + n$, the inclusion $i_* : \pi_k(A, C) \to \pi_k(X, B)$ is an isomorphism. The long exact sequence of the pair $(X, B)$ then becomes
$$
\cdots \to \pi_{k+1}(X, B) \to \pi_k(B) \to \pi_k(X) \to \pi_k(X, B) \to \cdots
$$
with $\pi_k(X, B) \cong \pi_k(A, C)$. The long exact sequence of the pair $(A, C)$ gives a parallel sequence
$$
\cdots \to \pi_{k+1}(A, C) \to \pi_k(C) \to \pi_k(A) \to \pi_k(A, C) \to \cdots
$$
Splicing the two via the Blakers-Massey isomorphism produces the homotopy Mayer-Vietoris sequence
$$
\cdots \to \pi_{k+1}(X) \to \pi_k(C) \to \pi_k(A) \oplus \pi_k(B) \to \pi_k(X) \to \pi_{k-1}(C) \to \cdots
$$
exact in the stable range. The matching with the homology Mayer-Vietoris sequence under the Hurewicz comparison [03.12.19] follows from the naturality of the Hurewicz map applied to each term. $\square$

**Theorem (higher-categorical generalisation), proof.** Stated; full proof in Anel-Biedermann-Finster-Joyal 2017. The proof in $\infty$-topoi uses the modal-operator framework: connectivity in an $\infty$-topos is a modality, and the Blakers-Massey statement is the universal statement that the join of two $\infty$-topos-connected modalities behaves additively. The proof is a direct manipulation of universal properties in the $\infty$-categorical setting, with no cellular induction. $\square$

**Theorem (Goodwillie calculus, first derivative), proof.** Stated; full proof in Goodwillie's *Calculus II* paper (1992). The first derivative $\partial F$ of a homotopy functor $F$ on based spaces is defined as the linearisation, and the comparison $F \to P_1 F$ to its first polynomial approximation is governed by a Blakers-Massey-type connectivity statement on the cross-effects of $F$. The convergence of the full Goodwillie tower is then Blakers-Massey applied at every stage. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Suspension and Freudenthal [03.12.03].** The Blakers-Massey theorem is the immediate engine of the Freudenthal suspension theorem: applied to the cone decomposition $\Sigma X = C_+ X \cup_X C_- X$ with both pairs $n$-connected, it yields the suspension iso range $k < 2n - 1$. The suspension unit's Freudenthal proof invokes Blakers-Massey explicitly; this unit makes that invocation concrete by stating and proving the underlying excision theorem.

- **Hurewicz theorem [03.12.19].** The relative Hurewicz theorem identifies $\pi_n(X, A)$ with $H_n(X, A)$ for an $(n-1)$-connected pair $(X, A)$ with $A$ simply-connected. Inside the Blakers-Massey range, the homotopy Mayer-Vietoris sequence matches the homology Mayer-Vietoris sequence via the Hurewicz comparison, giving an explicit dictionary between homotopy and homology of triads. The Hurewicz isomorphism is what powers the cellular reduction in the Blakers-Massey proof.

- **CW complex [03.12.10].** The Blakers-Massey theorem's modern proof is by induction on cell decomposition of the triad: cellular approximation reduces the general statement to the model space $X = D^p \cup_{S^{p-1}} D^q$, where the comparison map is computed by direct transversality. The CW hypothesis is essential for cellular approximation, and the model space is itself a $2$-cell CW complex with explicit attaching maps.

- **Spectrum and stable homotopy [03.12.04].** Stable homotopy theory studies the iterated suspension of a space, $\Sigma^\infty X$. The stable range is the dimensional region in which suspension stabilises, and the Blakers-Massey theorem identifies this range explicitly: a class in $\pi_k(X)$ stabilises after finitely many suspensions, with the number of suspensions needed governed by the Blakers-Massey-Freudenthal range. Spectra are the formal objects on which these stable maps live, and the Adams spectral sequence has its $E_2$ term controlled by the Mayer-Vietoris-type splittings that Blakers-Massey makes possible.

- **Whitehead's theorem [03.12.20].** Whitehead's theorem states that a map between simply-connected CW complexes inducing iso on all $\pi_n$ is a homotopy equivalence. Combined with Blakers-Massey, the corollary is that a map between simply-connected CW complexes inducing iso in homotopy in the stable range is a homotopy equivalence in the stable range, which is the foundational statement of stable homotopy theory.

- **Eilenberg-MacLane space [03.12.05].** The construction of $K(\pi, n)$ as a CW complex by attaching cells to kill unwanted homotopy groups uses cellular approximation and the long exact sequences of pairs in a way that overlaps the Blakers-Massey arguments. The Postnikov-tower description of a space identifies the $k$-invariants as cohomology classes whose detection is governed by the Blakers-Massey-Freudenthal stabilisation range.

- **Higher-categorical reformulations.** The Anel-Biedermann-Finster-Joyal reformulation of Blakers-Massey in $\infty$-topoi is the foundational statement of how connectivity propagates through pullback-pushout squares in any $\infty$-categorical localisation. The same statement holds in modal homotopy type theory and forms the basis of the Finster-Rijke proof internal to type theory, identifying Blakers-Massey as a universal property of left-exact modalities rather than a cellular phenomenon.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The Blakers-Massey theorem is due to Albert Blakers and William Massey, in a four-paper series titled *The homotopy groups of a triad* published in *Annals of Mathematics* during 1949-1953 [ref: TODO_REF]. The first paper (Annals 53, 1951) introduced the triad homotopy groups $\pi_k(X; A, B)$ as a relative analogue of the relative homotopy groups, with the long exact sequence of a triad. The second paper (Annals 55, 1952) contained the connectivity theorem in its sharp form, identifying the range in which the inclusion of pairs induces an iso on relative homotopy groups. The third paper (Annals 58, 1953) established the structural identification of the kernel at the boundary degree as a Whitehead product, and gave applications including Freudenthal's suspension theorem and the computation of certain Hopf invariants.

Blakers and Massey's original proof used the simplicial-set technology of the late 1940s, with intricate combinatorial arguments specific to triads. The modern textbook proof in Hatcher §4.2 [ref: TODO_REF] uses the cellular reduction to the model space $D^p \cup_{S^{p-1}} D^q$ and a transversality argument; this approach is due to a series of simplifications by Whitehead, James, and Spanier in the 1950s and 1960s, with the explicit model-space reduction crystallising in tom Dieck's textbook treatment. The categorical proof via the action of the loop space on the homotopy fibre is due to Mather and Goodwillie in the 1970s and 1980s.

The higher-categorical generalisation of Blakers-Massey to $\infty$-topoi was established by Anel, Biedermann, Finster, and Joyal in 2017 [ref: TODO_REF], reformulating the connectivity statement as an intrinsic property of left-exact modalities. The theorem in this form is internal to homotopy type theory and was given a synthetic proof by Finster and Rijke in the same period, identifying Blakers-Massey as a universal statement about the join of two $\infty$-topos-connected modalities. The Goodwillie tower of a homotopy functor, developed by Tom Goodwillie in a series of papers from 1990 onwards, identifies the convergence of the polynomial approximations to a functor as a Blakers-Massey-type statement at every stage, embedding the original theorem in a much larger framework of functor calculus.

The conceptual significance of the theorem is the quantification of homotopy excision: where ordinary homology excision is unconditional, homotopy excision holds approximately, with the approximation governed by connectivity. This quantification is what makes stable homotopy theory possible as a separate discipline, and what makes the higher-categorical generalisation of Anel-Biedermann-Finster-Joyal an intrinsic universal statement in any $\infty$-topos.
