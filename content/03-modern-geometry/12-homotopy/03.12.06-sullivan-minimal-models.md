## Intuition [Beginner]

The cohomology ring of a space records the holes; the Sullivan minimal model records the holes *and* the relations among them, in a single algebraic gadget made out of polynomial differential forms with rational coefficients. From this gadget you can read off the *rational* homotopy groups of the space — that is, the homotopy groups computed up to torsion, with rational-number coefficients — by a recipe simpler than any direct construction.

The starting point is a striking observation. Two simply-connected spaces with the same rational cohomology ring can have different rational homotopy groups, but the *minimal model* — a small graded-commutative differential algebra built recursively from the cohomology and its higher Massey products — captures everything. Two such spaces with the same minimal model have the same rational homotopy type, and conversely.

The framework is named for Dennis Sullivan, who built it in the 1970s by enriching the de Rham complex of a space with combinatorial polynomial-form data. Bott-Tu §19 is the canonical pedagogical exposition of the differential-form-flavoured side of the theory.

## Visual [Beginner]

A simply-connected space's rational invariants — the rational cohomology ring and the rational homotopy groups — are bundled into one small algebra, the minimal model.

The picture is the schematic. Reading the algebra recovers both the rational cohomology ring (in low generators) and the rational homotopy groups (as the dual of the indecomposable elements).

## Worked example [Beginner]

The minimal model of $S^2$. Take a single generator $x$ in degree $2$ with $dx = 0$ (so $x$ is closed). The rational cohomology of the resulting algebra has $1$ in degree $0$ and $x$ in degree $2$, but it also has $x^2$ in degree $4$, $x^3$ in degree $6$, and so on — too much to match $H^*(S^2; \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}$ in degrees $0$ and $2$ only.

The fix: introduce a second generator $y$ in degree $3$ with $dy = x^2$. Now $x^2$ is exact, so the cohomology in degree $4$ vanishes; and $xy$ in degree $5$ is closed but $d(xy) = x \cdot x^2 = x^3$ exact, so we keep cancelling powers of $x$ at every odd shift. The resulting cohomology is $\mathbb{Q}$ in degrees $0$ and $2$ only — matching $S^2$.

The two generators $x$ (degree $2$) and $y$ (degree $3$) tell us the rational homotopy groups: rationally, $\pi_2(S^2) = \mathbb{Q}$ and $\pi_3(S^2) = \mathbb{Q}$, with all higher rational homotopy groups vanishing. The Whitehead product $[\iota, \iota]$ in $\pi_3(S^2)$ — non-zero rationally — is exactly the nonzero differential $dy = x^2$ in the minimal model.

What this tells us: the minimal model is a small algebra whose generators encode rational homotopy and whose differential encodes Whitehead products and Massey products, and whose cohomology equals the cohomology of the space rationally.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout this unit, all spaces are simply-connected and of finite rational type — that is, $H^n(X; \mathbb{Q})$ is finite-dimensional for every $n$. All differential graded algebras (DGAs) are over $\mathbb{Q}$, graded-commutative, and concentrated in non-negative degrees.

**The polynomial-form functor $A_{PL}$.** Sullivan's construction begins with the **piecewise-polynomial de Rham functor** $A_{PL}$, a contravariant functor from simplicial sets (and hence topological spaces, via the singular complex) to commutative DGAs over $\mathbb{Q}$. For a simplicial set $K$, $A_{PL}^n(K)$ consists of compatible polynomial $n$-forms on each simplex with rational coefficients [ref: TODO_REF Bott-Tu §19; Félix-Halperin-Thomas §10]. The cohomology of $A_{PL}(X)$ equals the rational singular cohomology:
$$
H^*(A_{PL}(X)) \cong H^*(X; \mathbb{Q}).
$$

The functor $A_{PL}$ is the rational analogue of the smooth de Rham complex $\Omega^*(M)$, with the smooth real-coefficient theory replaced by a piecewise polynomial $\mathbb{Q}$-coefficient theory that descends to topological (not just smooth) spaces. On a smooth manifold, the inclusion $A_{PL}(X) \hookrightarrow \Omega^*(X)$ tensored with $\mathbb{R}$ is a quasi-isomorphism — the rational refinement of the de Rham theorem [03.04.13].

**Sullivan algebra.** A commutative DGA $(A, d)$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ is **Sullivan** if its underlying graded algebra is a free graded-commutative algebra $\Lambda V$ on a graded vector space $V = V^{\geq 1}$, and $V$ admits an increasing filtration $V(0) \subseteq V(1) \subseteq \cdots$ with $V = \bigcup V(k)$ and $d V(k) \subseteq \Lambda V(k - 1)$ for each $k \geq 1$. The filtration is the **Sullivan condition**; it is equivalent to requiring that $A$ be obtained from $\mathbb{Q}$ by iterated Hirsch extensions (free DGA extensions with prescribed differential).

**Minimal Sullivan algebra.** A Sullivan algebra $(\Lambda V, d)$ is **minimal** if $d V \subseteq \Lambda^{\geq 2} V$ — that is, the differential of every generator lies in the squared augmentation ideal (no "linear" terms). Equivalently, the generators $V$ are precisely the *indecomposables* of $\Lambda V$.

**Sullivan minimal model.** A **minimal model** of a commutative DGA $(A, d)$ is a quasi-isomorphism
$$
\varphi : (\Lambda V, d) \xrightarrow{\sim} (A, d)
$$
where $(\Lambda V, d)$ is a minimal Sullivan algebra. The **minimal model of a space** $X$ is the minimal model of $A_{PL}(X)$, written $M_X = (\Lambda V_X, d)$.

**Rational homotopy type.** A **rational homotopy equivalence** between simply-connected spaces is a continuous map $f : X \to Y$ inducing isomorphisms $\pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \xrightarrow{\sim} \pi_n(Y) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ for all $n$. The **rational homotopy type** of $X$ is the equivalence class of $X$ under the relation generated by rational equivalences.

The notation $PX$ for the path space, used throughout this unit and the Whitehead-tower unit [03.12.07], follows decision #32: $PX = \mathrm{Map}_*(I, X)$ is the based path space, a contractible space fitting into the path-loop fibration $\Omega X \to PX \to X$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Sullivan 1977, existence and uniqueness of the minimal model).** *Every simply-connected commutative DGA $(A, d)$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ with $H^0(A) = \mathbb{Q}$ and $H^*(A)$ of finite type admits a minimal model $\varphi : (\Lambda V, d) \xrightarrow{\sim} (A, d)$, unique up to isomorphism of DGAs covering the identity on $A$.*

**Proof.** *Existence by induction.* Construct $V$ degree by degree.

In degree $1$: $V^1 = 0$ since $A$ is simply-connected, so $H^1(A) = 0$, and the simply-connected hypothesis forces no degree-$1$ generators.

In degree $2$: pick a basis $\{x_i\}$ of $H^2(A)$ and lift each $x_i$ to a closed element $\tilde{x}_i \in A^2$. Set $V^2 = \mathbb{Q}\{x_i\}$ with $d x_i = 0$, and define $\varphi(x_i) = \tilde{x}_i$. The induced map $\Lambda V^2 \to A$ is a quasi-isomorphism in degrees $\leq 2$.

Inductive step. Suppose $\varphi : \Lambda V^{<n} \to A$ is built and is a quasi-isomorphism in degrees $< n$ and surjective in degree $n$ on cohomology. Two corrections are needed in degree $n$:

1. **Add cohomology generators.** Pick representatives in $A^n$ of the cokernel of $H^n(\Lambda V^{<n}) \to H^n(A)$ — that is, classes in $H^n(A)$ not yet hit. For each, add a generator in $V^n$ with zero differential, mapping under $\varphi$ to the chosen representative.

2. **Kill spurious cohomology.** Identify the kernel of $H^{n+1}(\Lambda V^{<n}) \to H^{n+1}(A)$ — closed elements of degree $n+1$ in $\Lambda V^{<n}$ whose image in $A$ is exact. For each generator $\zeta$ of this kernel, choose $\tilde{\zeta} \in A^n$ with $d \tilde{\zeta} = \varphi(\zeta)$, and add a generator $y_\zeta \in V^n$ with $d y_\zeta = \zeta$ and $\varphi(y_\zeta) = \tilde{\zeta}$.

After both additions, the extended map is a quasi-isomorphism in degrees $\leq n$, and the induction proceeds. The minimality condition $d V \subseteq \Lambda^{\geq 2} V$ is automatic in step 1 (zero differential) and arranged in step 2 by the inductive choice that $\zeta$ is a polynomial of degree $\geq 2$ in the existing generators (only $\zeta$ outside the augmentation ideal is hit, but those have already been used in step 1). [ref: TODO_REF Bott-Tu §19; Félix-Halperin-Thomas §12]

*Uniqueness.* Given two minimal models $\varphi_1 : (\Lambda V_1, d) \to A$ and $\varphi_2 : (\Lambda V_2, d) \to A$, an isomorphism $\Lambda V_1 \xrightarrow{\sim} \Lambda V_2$ covering the identity is constructed by the **lifting lemma** for minimal Sullivan algebras: any quasi-isomorphism $A \xrightarrow{\sim} B$ admits a section through any minimal Sullivan algebra mapping to $A$. Applying this lemma to the identity on $A$ produces a chain map $\Lambda V_1 \to \Lambda V_2$ covering the identity; reversing gives the inverse, and the composition is homotopic (over $A$) to the identity by minimality. $\square$

**Theorem (Sullivan's main theorem).** *For a simply-connected space $X$ of finite rational type, the minimal model $(\Lambda V_X, d)$ of $A_{PL}(X)$ determines the rational homotopy type of $X$. Specifically:*
$$
V_X^n \cong \mathrm{Hom}_\mathbb{Z}(\pi_n(X), \mathbb{Q})
$$
*as graded $\mathbb{Q}$-vector spaces. The differential $d : V_X \to \Lambda^{\geq 2} V_X$ encodes the rational Whitehead products and higher Massey products in $\pi_*(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$.*

**Proof sketch.** The proof has two parts.

The cohomological part: by construction, $\varphi : \Lambda V_X \to A_{PL}(X)$ is a quasi-isomorphism, so $H^*(\Lambda V_X) = H^*(X; \mathbb{Q})$.

The homotopical part: the indecomposable space $V_X^n = (V_X / V_X \cdot V_X)^n$ is dual to $\pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ via the **rational Hurewicz pairing** for minimal models. The argument runs through the bar construction: $\mathrm{Hom}(\pi_n(X), \mathbb{Q})$ is computed from the rational homotopy of the rationalisation $X_\mathbb{Q}$, which by Sullivan's spatial realisation $|\Lambda V_X|$ is built precisely so that the indecomposables of $\Lambda V_X$ in degree $n$ enumerate the rational homotopy in degree $n$. The differential captures higher Whitehead products: a nonzero $d y = \sum c_{ij} x_i x_j + \cdots$ encodes the bracket $[\iota_{x_i}, \iota_{x_j}]$ in $\pi_*(X)$ as a polynomial relation in the rationalisation. [ref: TODO_REF Sullivan 1977; Félix-Halperin-Thomas §15] $\square$

**Synthesis.** The Sullivan minimal model is exactly the rational refinement of the de Rham complex. This is precisely Sullivan 1977's main theorem: the minimal model encodes both the rational cohomology ring and the rational homotopy groups. The minimal model is an instance of the broader principle that rational homotopy is a finite-dimensional linear-algebra problem.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [03.12.07] (whitehead tower, rational hurewicz theorem, and serre's finiteness), where the same data is developed in the next layer of the strand. The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

Write down the minimal model of $S^{2k}$ for $k \geq 1$. Identify the generators and the differential.

A single even-degree generator gives too much cohomology (a polynomial algebra). A second odd-degree generator with appropriate differential cuts back to the right answer.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The minimal model is $\Lambda(x, y)$ with $\deg x = 2k$, $\deg y = 4k - 1$, $dx = 0$, $dy = x^2$. The cohomology is $\mathbb{Q}$ in degrees $0$ and $2k$, zero elsewhere — matching $H^*(S^{2k}; \mathbb{Q})$. Generators: $x \leftrightarrow \pi_{2k}(S^{2k}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (degree of the fundamental class), $y \leftrightarrow \pi_{4k-1}(S^{2k}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (the Whitehead product $[\iota, \iota]$, which is rationally non-zero exactly for even-dimensional spheres).

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).** *Minimal model of $S^{2k+1}$ (free case).*

Write down the minimal model of $S^{2k+1}$ for $k \geq 0$. Why does it have a single generator?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In odd degree, the square of the generator vanishes by graded commutativity, so the polynomial-algebra issue does not arise.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The minimal model is $\Lambda(x)$ with $\deg x = 2k+1$, $dx = 0$. Since $x$ is in odd degree, $x^2 = 0$ by graded commutativity, so the cohomology of the algebra is $\mathbb{Q} \oplus \mathbb{Q} \cdot x$ in degrees $0$ and $2k+1$ — matching $S^{2k+1}$. The single generator $x \leftrightarrow \pi_{2k+1}(S^{2k+1}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$, and all higher rational homotopy of $S^{2k+1}$ vanishes.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).** *Minimal model of $\mathbb{C}P^n$.*

Write down the minimal model of $\mathbb{C}P^n$ for $n \geq 1$. Confirm it recovers $H^*(\mathbb{C}P^n; \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}[x]/(x^{n+1})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A single degree-$2$ generator gives $\mathbb{Q}[x]$, infinitely many cohomology classes. A second generator in odd degree, with differential the top class $x^{n+1}$, truncates correctly.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The minimal model is $\Lambda(x, y)$ with $\deg x = 2$, $\deg y = 2n+1$, $dx = 0$, $dy = x^{n+1}$.

Cohomology: in degrees $\leq 2n$, the relations contributed by $y$ are not yet active (since $\deg y = 2n+1$), so cohomology equals $\mathbb{Q}[x]$ truncated at $x^{n+1}$ (which becomes exact via $d y$). In degrees $> 2n+1$: every closed element involves $y$ or $x^{n+1}$, both of which are exact or hit by $d$. Final cohomology: $\mathbb{Q}[x]/(x^{n+1})$ — matching $\mathbb{C}P^n$.

Rational homotopy: $\pi_2(\mathbb{C}P^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (from $x$) and $\pi_{2n+1}(\mathbb{C}P^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (from $y$); all other rational homotopy vanishes. (Via the Hopf fibration $S^1 \to S^{2n+1} \to \mathbb{C}P^n$, the $\pi_{2n+1}$ generator descends from the rational generator of $\pi_{2n+1}(S^{2n+1})$.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *Quasi-isomorphism of minimal models.*

Show that two minimal Sullivan algebras quasi-isomorphic to a common DGA are isomorphic.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the lifting lemma: a minimal Sullivan algebra has the lifting property against any quasi-isomorphism.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\varphi_1 : M_1 \to A$ and $\varphi_2 : M_2 \to A$ be two minimal-model quasi-isomorphisms. By the lifting lemma applied to $\varphi_2$ (viewed as a quasi-isomorphism), there exists a chain map $\psi : M_1 \to M_2$ with $\varphi_2 \psi \simeq \varphi_1$ (homotopic over $A$). Symmetrically there is $\chi : M_2 \to M_1$. The composites $\psi \chi$ and $\chi \psi$ both cover the identity quasi-isomorphism on $A$, so by minimality of $M_1$ and $M_2$ they are isomorphisms (a self-quasi-isomorphism of a minimal Sullivan algebra is automatically an isomorphism, since the differential is decomposable so quasi-iso of indecomposable spaces forces iso). Hence $\psi$ and $\chi$ are mutual inverses up to homotopy, and on indecomposables they are inverse isomorphisms. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).** *Minimal model of a product.*

Show that the minimal model of $X \times Y$ is the tensor product $M_X \otimes M_Y$ of the minimal models of $X$ and $Y$, provided $X$ and $Y$ are simply-connected of finite rational type.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Künneth theorem rationally: $H^*(X \times Y; \mathbb{Q}) = H^*(X; \mathbb{Q}) \otimes H^*(Y; \mathbb{Q})$. Apply at the cochain level via $A_{PL}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The functor $A_{PL}$ is monoidal: $A_{PL}(X \times Y) \xrightarrow{\sim} A_{PL}(X) \otimes A_{PL}(Y)$ as DGAs (a rational analogue of the de Rham product theorem). The tensor product $M_X \otimes M_Y$ of two minimal Sullivan algebras is again a Sullivan algebra (free over $V_X \oplus V_Y$ with differential $d_X \otimes 1 + 1 \otimes d_Y$ on generators). Since both factors are minimal — $d V_X \subseteq \Lambda^{\geq 2} V_X$ and similarly for $V_Y$ — the tensor differential is decomposable, hence $M_X \otimes M_Y$ is minimal. The composite quasi-isomorphism $M_X \otimes M_Y \to A_{PL}(X) \otimes A_{PL}(Y) \xrightarrow{\sim} A_{PL}(X \times Y)$ is a minimal model. By uniqueness, $M_{X \times Y} \cong M_X \otimes M_Y$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).** *Halperin's algorithm for a fibration.*

Suppose $F \to E \to B$ is a fibration of simply-connected spaces of finite rational type, with $F$ admitting a minimal model $M_F = (\Lambda V_F, d_F)$ and $B$ admitting $M_B = (\Lambda V_B, d_B)$. Construct a minimal model of $E$ as a "twisted" tensor product, with the twisting given by the spectral sequence.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Halperin's algorithm: form the underlying graded algebra $M_B \otimes M_F$ and modify the differential to encode the fibration's monodromy.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Halperin's twisted-tensor model.** Form the underlying graded algebra $M_E = M_B \otimes \Lambda V_F$ as a free graded-commutative $M_B$-algebra. Define a differential $d_E$ on $M_E$ by:

- $d_E(b \otimes 1) = d_B(b) \otimes 1$ for $b \in M_B$;
- $d_E(1 \otimes v) = 1 \otimes d_F(v) + \tau(v)$ for $v \in V_F$, where $\tau : V_F \to M_B \otimes \Lambda V_F$ is a *perturbation* of degree $+1$ encoding the Leray-Serre transgression.

The condition $d_E^2 = 0$ forces $\tau$ to satisfy a Maurer-Cartan-type equation. The construction proceeds inductively in the $V_F$-filtration: at each step, $\tau$ is chosen on new generators of $V_F$ to lift the differential of $M_F$ to a chain map compatible with the spectral sequence on $E$. Sullivan and Halperin proved (1979, *Trans. AMS* 244) that such a $\tau$ exists, that the resulting $(M_B \otimes \Lambda V_F, d_E)$ is a Sullivan model of $E$, and that minimality is preserved if both $M_B$ and $M_F$ are minimal.

The transgression $\tau$ corresponds to the differentials $d_r$ on the Leray-Serre spectral sequence [03.13.02] in cohomology; the model thus carries the spectral-sequence data exactly. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).** *Sullivan's solution to Serre's question.*

Use the minimal model to compute $\pi_*(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ for all $n \geq 2$, recovering Serre's theorem (1953) that $\pi_k(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ is non-zero only in two cases: $\pi_n(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ for all $n$, and $\pi_{4k-1}(S^{2k}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ for all $k \geq 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Read off the indecomposables of the minimal models from Exercises 1 and 2.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Exercise 2, the minimal model of $S^{2k+1}$ is $\Lambda(x)$ with $\deg x = 2k+1$ and $dx = 0$. The indecomposables are concentrated in degree $2k+1$, so $\pi_{2k+1}(S^{2k+1}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ and all other rational homotopy of $S^{2k+1}$ vanishes.

By Exercise 1, the minimal model of $S^{2k}$ is $\Lambda(x, y)$ with $\deg x = 2k$, $\deg y = 4k-1$, $d y = x^2$. The indecomposables are in degrees $2k$ and $4k-1$, so $\pi_{2k}(S^{2k}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (from $x$) and $\pi_{4k-1}(S^{2k}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (from $y$); all other rational homotopy vanishes.

Combining: $\pi_k(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \neq 0$ only when $k = n$ (for any $n$) or $n = 2j$ even and $k = 4j - 1$. This is Serre's 1953 finiteness statement at the rational level. The integer-coefficient finiteness — that the kernel of $\pi_k(S^n) \to \pi_k(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ is finite — requires Serre's spectral-sequence argument, which the minimal-model approach completes by giving the rational answer outright. [ref: TODO_REF Sullivan 1977; Serre 1953] $\square$

The computation is the simplest illustration of Sullivan's slogan: rational homotopy is "calculus on graded vector spaces," and the minimal model is the calculus engine.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Formality of compact Kähler manifolds.*

A simply-connected DGA $A$ is **formal** if its minimal model coincides with the minimal model of its cohomology ring (viewed as a DGA with zero differential). Sketch the proof of Deligne-Griffiths-Morgan-Sullivan 1975 that every simply-connected compact Kähler manifold is formal.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the $dd^c$-lemma: on a compact Kähler manifold, the $\partial \bar{\partial}$-lemma forces $A_{PL}(X)$ to be quasi-isomorphic to $H^*(X; \mathbb{R})$ as a DGA. Tensor with $\mathbb{C}$ then descend.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $X$ be a simply-connected compact Kähler manifold. Equip the de Rham complex $\Omega^*_\mathbb{C}(X) = \Omega^*(X) \otimes \mathbb{C}$ with the Kähler structure: holomorphic / antiholomorphic decomposition $d = \partial + \bar{\partial}$, the Hodge $\star$, the $dd^c$-operator $d^c = i(\bar{\partial} - \partial)/2$.

**Step 1: $dd^c$-lemma.** The Hodge theory of compact Kähler manifolds (Weil 1958, Andreotti-Vesentini 1965) gives the $\partial \bar{\partial}$-lemma: for any $d$-closed and $d^c$-closed form $\alpha$ which is either $d$-exact or $d^c$-exact, there exists $\beta$ with $\alpha = d d^c \beta$.

**Step 2: zigzag of quasi-isomorphisms.** Define two subcomplexes of $\Omega^*_\mathbb{C}(X)$:
$$
\ker d^c \xleftarrow{\text{incl}} (\ker d \cap \ker d^c) \xrightarrow{\text{quotient}} H^*_{d^c}(\Omega).
$$
The $dd^c$-lemma implies both arrows are quasi-isomorphisms (with respect to $d$). The right end is the cohomology with respect to $d^c$, which by Hodge equals the cohomology of the de Rham complex (the harmonic forms are bi-closed). Hence $\Omega^*_\mathbb{C}(X)$ is quasi-isomorphic, as a DGA, to $H^*(X; \mathbb{C})$ with zero differential.

**Step 3: descend to $\mathbb{Q}$.** The quasi-isomorphism above is over $\mathbb{C}$. The Sullivan-Quillen rational machinery descends: by uniqueness of the minimal model, $M_X \otimes \mathbb{C} \cong M_{H^*(X; \mathbb{C})}$, and since both sides are defined rationally and the isomorphism is intrinsic, $M_X \cong M_{H^*(X; \mathbb{Q})}$.

**Conclusion.** The minimal model of $X$ is determined by its cohomology ring with no Massey-product corrections. The rational homotopy type of $X$ is a function only of $H^*(X; \mathbb{Q})$ — a statement of remarkable rigidity. [ref: TODO_REF Deligne-Griffiths-Morgan-Sullivan 1975] $\square$

The corollary: simply-connected compact Kähler manifolds with isomorphic rational cohomology rings are rationally homotopy equivalent. This was the Hodge-theoretic payoff of the Sullivan apparatus and remains one of its principal applications.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Bigraded models and the Quillen-Sullivan equivalence.** Quillen 1969 *Rational homotopy theory* (Annals 90) gave a parallel construction in DG Lie algebras: the **Quillen model** of a simply-connected space is a DG Lie algebra over $\mathbb{Q}$ generated by the desuspended rational homology. The Quillen and Sullivan theories are related by a duality: Sullivan models live in the category of commutative DGAs (cochain side), Quillen models in DG Lie algebras (chain side), and the categories are connected by a Koszul duality of $A_\infty$-type. For finite-type simply-connected spaces, both produce the same rational homotopy type; the differential-form side (Sullivan) is more amenable to explicit computation, while the Lie-algebraic side (Quillen) is better suited to the universal-property-driven proofs.

**Formality and the rational dichotomy.** A space is **formal** if its minimal model is determined by the cohomology ring alone — equivalently, all Massey products vanish rationally. The 1975 Deligne-Griffiths-Morgan-Sullivan theorem establishes formality for simply-connected compact Kähler manifolds. Counterexamples to formality exist outside the Kähler class: nilmanifolds (Heisenberg-style), four-manifolds with nonzero Massey products, generic symplectic manifolds. The rational dichotomy "formal vs. non-formal" stratifies a great deal of geometric topology.

**Rational Hurewicz and the rationalisation functor.** Sullivan's spatial realisation functor $|\cdot|$ takes a minimal Sullivan algebra to a simplicial set whose geometric realisation is the *rationalisation* $X_\mathbb{Q}$ of the original space. The rationalisation is a CW complex with $\pi_n(X_\mathbb{Q}) = \pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ and $H_n(X_\mathbb{Q}; \mathbb{Z}) = H_n(X; \mathbb{Q})$. The rational Hurewicz theorem [03.12.07] states that if $X$ is simply-connected and $\pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = 0$ for $1 \leq i \leq n - 1$, then the rational Hurewicz map $\pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \to H_n(X; \mathbb{Q})$ is an isomorphism. The minimal-model proof reads off both sides directly from the indecomposable layer.

**Loop space cohomology.** For a simply-connected space $X$ with minimal model $(\Lambda V, d)$, the cohomology of the loop space $\Omega X$ is (rationally) the cohomology of the **shifted-suspension** $(\Lambda s^{-1} V, 0)$ — a free graded-commutative algebra on the desuspended generators with zero differential. This is the rational version of the Bott-Samelson theorem on $\Omega S^n$ [from EP3 problem set]. The Eilenberg-Moore spectral sequence computes $H^*(\Omega X; \mathbb{Q})$ and degenerates rationally, producing this expression.

**Formal homotopy types and rational rigidity.** A consequence of formality is that the rational homotopy type of a formal space is rigid under perturbations of the cohomology ring: small deformations of $H^*(X; \mathbb{Q})$ produce small deformations of $X_\mathbb{Q}$. Non-formality manifests as moduli of rational homotopy types refining a fixed cohomology ring — exactly the moduli that Massey products and higher operations parametrise.

**The minimal model of a Lie group.** For a compact connected Lie group $G$ with rational cohomology ring $H^*(G; \mathbb{Q}) = \Lambda(x_1, \ldots, x_r)$ on odd-degree primitive generators, the minimal model of $G$ is exactly this exterior algebra with zero differential. Lie groups are formal. Borel's 1953 computation of $H^*(BG; \mathbb{Q})$ [03.08.05] sits at the next layer: the minimal model of $BG$ is $\mathbb{Q}[y_1, \ldots, y_r]$ with $\deg y_i = \deg x_i + 1$, the polynomial algebra dual to the loop space cohomology of $G$.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Construction of the $A_{PL}$ functor.** For a simplicial set $K$, define $A_{PL}^n(K)$ to be the set of compatible families $\{\omega_\sigma\}_{\sigma \in K_p}$ where $\omega_\sigma \in \mathbb{Q}[t_0, \ldots, t_p, dt_0, \ldots, dt_p] / (\sum t_i - 1, \sum dt_i)$ is a polynomial $n$-form on the $p$-simplex with the relations enforcing barycentric coordinates, and the compatibility means that face and degeneracy operators induce the expected restriction maps. Multiplication is wedge product of polynomial forms; differential is the de Rham differential applied piecewise.

**Theorem ($A_{PL}$ computes rational cohomology).** *For any simplicial set $K$ — in particular, the singular complex of a topological space — the cohomology of $A_{PL}(K)$ is naturally isomorphic to the singular cohomology of $|K|$ with $\mathbb{Q}$ coefficients.*

**Proof sketch.** This is the rational analogue of the de Rham theorem. The integration map
$$
\int : A_{PL}(K) \to C^*(K; \mathbb{Q})
$$
sends a polynomial form to its values on the simplices integrated against the standard volume. Integration is a quasi-isomorphism by a Mayer-Vietoris-type induction over a good cover (or directly by the simplicial-form analogue of the de Rham theorem). The rational refinement comes from the explicit polynomial structure: integrals of polynomial forms over standard simplices are rational numbers. [ref: TODO_REF Bott-Tu §19; Félix-Halperin-Thomas §10] $\square$

**Construction of the minimal model — full induction.** Given a simply-connected DGA $(A, d)$ with $H^0(A) = \mathbb{Q}$ and $H^*(A)$ of finite type, build $\Lambda V \to A$ by induction on cohomological degree.

Base case ($n = 1$): $V^1 = 0$. Define $\varphi_0 : \mathbb{Q} \to A$ as the unit map, a quasi-isomorphism in degree $0$.

Inductive step. Suppose $\varphi_{n-1} : (\Lambda V^{<n}, d) \to A$ is a quasi-isomorphism in degrees $< n$ and surjective in cohomology in degree $n$. Define $V^n$ in two pieces:

(a) **Surjectivity in degree $n$.** Pick a basis $\{[a_i]\}$ of $\mathrm{coker}(H^n(\Lambda V^{<n}) \to H^n(A))$, lift each to $a_i \in A^n$ closed, and add generators $x_i \in V^n$ with $d x_i = 0$ and $\varphi_n(x_i) = a_i$. After this step, $H^n(\Lambda V^{\leq n}) \to H^n(A)$ is surjective.

(b) **Injectivity in degree $n+1$.** Pick a basis $\{[\zeta_j]\}$ of $\ker(H^{n+1}(\Lambda V^{\leq n}) \to H^{n+1}(A))$ — closed elements of degree $n+1$ in $\Lambda V^{\leq n}$ whose image in $A$ is exact. Each $\zeta_j$ is decomposable in $\Lambda V^{\leq n}$ (since $\zeta_j$ is in cohomological degree $n+1$ and $\zeta_j$ being a cocycle in $\Lambda V^{\leq n}$ means it is built from existing generators, which sit in degrees $\leq n$, and degree-$n+1$ elements of $\Lambda V^{\leq n}$ that are not generators are decomposable). Pick $b_j \in A^n$ with $d b_j = \varphi_n(\zeta_j)$, and add a generator $y_j \in V^n$ with $d y_j = \zeta_j \in \Lambda V^{\leq n}$ (decomposable, so the minimality condition is preserved) and $\varphi_n(y_j) = b_j$.

After (b), the kernel of $H^{n+1}(\Lambda V^{\leq n}) \to H^{n+1}(A)$ is killed, restoring quasi-isomorphism in degree $n+1$ on the cohomological-injectivity side.

The induction proceeds. Minimality is preserved: in (a), $d x_i = 0 \in \Lambda^{\geq 2} V$ holds vacuously; in (b), $d y_j = \zeta_j$ is decomposable by construction.

**Theorem (lifting lemma for minimal Sullivan algebras).** *Let $\varphi : (M, d_M) \to (A, d_A)$ be a chain map from a minimal Sullivan algebra $M$ to a CDGA $A$, and let $\eta : (B, d_B) \xrightarrow{\sim} (A, d_A)$ be a quasi-isomorphism. Then $\varphi$ admits a lift $\tilde{\varphi} : M \to B$ with $\eta \tilde{\varphi} = \varphi$, unique up to chain homotopy.*

**Proof sketch.** Build $\tilde{\varphi}$ by induction on the Sullivan filtration $V(0) \subseteq V(1) \subseteq \cdots$ of $M$. At each stage, the obstruction to lifting on the next level is a class in a relative cohomology group $H^*(\mathrm{Cone}(\eta))$, which vanishes because $\eta$ is a quasi-isomorphism. The minimality condition $d V \subseteq \Lambda^{\geq 2} V$ ensures the obstruction class lives in a degree where the cone-cohomology argument applies. [ref: TODO_REF Félix-Halperin-Thomas §14] $\square$

**Theorem (Sullivan main theorem — full statement).** *For a simply-connected space $X$ of finite rational type with minimal model $(\Lambda V, d)$:*

1. *$H^*(\Lambda V) \cong H^*(X; \mathbb{Q})$ as graded rings.*
2. *$V^n \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbb{Z}}(\pi_n(X), \mathbb{Q})$ as $\mathbb{Q}$-vector spaces.*
3. *The differential $d$ on $V$ is dual to the rational Whitehead-product structure on $\pi_*(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$. Specifically, the linear part of $d : V^n \to \Lambda^2 V$ encodes Whitehead products $[\,,\,] : \pi_p(X) \otimes \pi_q(X) \to \pi_{p+q-1}(X)$ via duality.*
4. *Minimal models are functorial: a continuous map $f : X \to Y$ induces a chain map $f^* : M_Y \to M_X$, well-defined up to homotopy, and rational equivalences correspond to isomorphisms of minimal models.*

**Proof references.** (1) is by construction; (2) and (3) are the rational-homotopy-pairing theorem of [ref: TODO_REF Sullivan 1977 §3]; (4) follows from (1)-(3) by the lifting lemma. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Dennis Sullivan's 1977 paper *Infinitesimal computations in topology* (Publications mathématiques de l'I.H.É.S. 47, 269–331) is one of the great mathematical works of the second half of the twentieth century. Sullivan was at the Institut des Hautes Études Scientifiques in Bures-sur-Yvette, the institution Cartan had directed and where Grothendieck had reformulated algebraic geometry a decade earlier. Daniel Quillen had just published the model-categorical version of rational homotopy theory in his 1969 *Rational homotopy theory* (Annals of Mathematics 90, 205–295), constructing for each simply-connected space a DG Lie algebra encoding the rational homotopy type. Sullivan's *differential* approach was the parallel story, working through commutative differential graded algebras of polynomial forms — a category richer in computational structure and tied directly to differential geometry.

Sullivan's 1977 paper opens with an extended programmatic essay sketching what rational homotopy theory should be: a calculus on the rational invariants of a space, with the polynomial-form complex playing the role of the de Rham complex on smooth manifolds. The minimal model, Sullivan wrote, makes "rational homotopy theory perfectly computable" — every simply-connected finite-type space has a small graded-commutative differential algebra over $\mathbb{Q}$ that recovers both the cohomology ring and the rational homotopy groups, with all higher Massey products encoded in the differential. The empirical achievement: rational homotopy type, previously an inaccessible invariant, became a finite-dimensional linear-algebra problem.

The pedagogical reframing **Bott and Tu** offer in §19 of *Differential Forms in Algebraic Topology* (1982) makes Sullivan's machinery accessible to a graduate student who has worked through differential forms but not yet seen homotopy theory. They construct the minimal model directly as a small differential-form algebra, build the minimal models of $S^n$ and $\mathbb{C}P^n$ explicitly, and trace Sullivan's main theorem through the Hopf fibration. The chapter is one of the canonical entry points into rational homotopy from a differential-geometric direction; Bott-Tu §19 is the originator-text for the differential-form-flavoured exposition that this Babel Bible unit channels.

Two further developments anchor the modern theory. The 1975 paper of Deligne, Griffiths, Morgan, and Sullivan, *Real homotopy theory of Kähler manifolds* (Inventiones 29, 245–274), proved that simply-connected compact Kähler manifolds are formal — their minimal models depend only on the cohomology ring with no Massey-product corrections (Exercise 8). This pulled Hodge theory through the rational-homotopy lens and remains a load-bearing result of Hodge-theoretic geometry. Halperin and Stasheff's 1979 *Trans. AMS* paper extended the framework to fibrations, codifying the perturbation algorithm (Exercise 6) for computing minimal models of total spaces from minimal models of base and fibre. By the early 1980s the apparatus had become the standard computational language for rational invariants of Lie groups, fibre bundles, and homogeneous spaces.

The Quillen-Sullivan equivalence — the duality between the DG Lie algebraic and the commutative DGA presentations — was made precise in Quillen's original 1969 paper and refined by Tanré, Hess, and others through the 1980s. The two sides of the equivalence are connected by Koszul duality, with the commutative side better suited to differential-geometric computation and the Lie side better suited to abstract universal-property arguments.
