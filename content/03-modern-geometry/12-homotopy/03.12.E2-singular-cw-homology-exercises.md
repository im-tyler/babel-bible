## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Hatcher's Chapter 2 builds ordinary homology three ways — simplicial, singular, and cellular — and proves they agree on CW complexes. The chapter's engine is the trio of Eilenberg-Steenrod axioms made operational: the long exact sequence of a pair, excision (packaged as Mayer-Vietoris), and the dimension axiom. With these in hand the chapter computes $H_*(S^n)$ by induction, defines and computes the degree of a self-map of $S^n$, and grinds out the cellular chain complexes of projective spaces, lens spaces, and Moore spaces.

This pack collects ten exercises drawn from §2.1, §2.2, and §2.C — three easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. The exercises are grouped by technique: $\Delta$-complex and degree warm-ups (easy), Mayer-Vietoris and cellular-chain computations of standard spaces (medium), and the structural results that pin down homology up to isomorphism — the universal coefficient theorem in action and the homology of an infinite product of cells (hard).

The conventions are Hatcher's: $H_n$ denotes reduced or unreduced singular homology with $\mathbb{Z}$ coefficients unless stated, and the cellular boundary map is computed by the degree formula $d_n([e^n_\alpha]) = \sum_\beta d_{\alpha\beta}\,[e^{n-1}_\beta]$, where $d_{\alpha\beta}$ is the degree of the attaching-then-collapse map $S^{n-1}_\alpha \to X^{n-1} \to S^{n-1}_\beta$.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as the model solution. The remaining nine follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *Compute $H_*(\mathbb{R}P^n; \mathbb{Z})$ from the cellular chain complex.*

**Solution.** Real projective $n$-space has a CW structure with exactly one cell $e^k$ in each dimension $0 \le k \le n$, where $\mathbb{R}P^k = \mathbb{R}P^{k-1} \cup e^k$ and the attaching map $S^{k-1} \to \mathbb{R}P^{k-1}$ is the standard double cover. The cellular chain complex is therefore

$$
0 \to \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{d_n} \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{d_{n-1}} \cdots \xrightarrow{d_1} \mathbb{Z} \to 0,
$$

one $\mathbb{Z}$ in each degree $0$ through $n$. The cellular boundary $d_k$ is multiplication by the degree of the composite $S^{k-1} \xrightarrow{\text{2-fold cover}} \mathbb{R}P^{k-1} \to \mathbb{R}P^{k-1}/\mathbb{R}P^{k-2} = S^{k-1}$. That composite is $1 + (-1)^k$ (the antipodal identification contributes the antipodal-map degree $(-1)^k$ to the identity): so $d_k = 0$ for $k$ odd and $d_k = \pm 2$ for $k$ even.

The complex thus alternates $\cdots \xrightarrow{0} \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{2} \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{0} \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{2} \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{0} \mathbb{Z}$. Reading homology degree by degree:

- $H_0 = \mathbb{Z}$ (path-connected).
- For $0 < k < n$ odd: $H_k = \ker(d_k)/\mathrm{im}(d_{k+1}) = \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ (kernel of $0$ is $\mathbb{Z}$, image of multiplication-by-$2$ is $2\mathbb{Z}$).
- For $0 < k < n$ even: $H_k = 0$ (kernel of multiplication-by-$2$ is $0$).
- $H_n = \mathbb{Z}$ if $n$ is odd (the top map into degree $n$ is $0$, and $d_n$ out of degree $n$ does not exist), and $H_n = 0$ if $n$ is even.

Summary: $H_k(\mathbb{R}P^n) = \mathbb{Z}$ for $k = 0$; $\mathbb{Z}/2$ for $k$ odd, $0 < k < n$; $0$ for $k$ even, $0 < k < n$; and $\mathbb{Z}$ in degree $n$ when $n$ is odd, $0$ when $n$ is even. $\square$

This computation is the template for every cellular calculation downstream: write the cells, compute each boundary by a degree, read off homology. The lens-space and $\mathbb{C}P^n$ exercises differ only in the cell dimensions and the boundary degrees.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).** *Homology of $\mathbb{C}P^n$.*

Give $\mathbb{C}P^n$ its standard CW structure with one cell in each even dimension $0, 2, 4, \ldots, 2n$, and compute $H_*(\mathbb{C}P^n; \mathbb{Z})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

All cells are in even dimensions, so the cellular chain groups in odd degrees are zero. What does that force every boundary map to be?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The cellular chain complex has $C_k = \mathbb{Z}$ for $k$ even, $0 \le k \le 2n$, and $C_k = 0$ for $k$ odd. Every boundary map $d_k: C_k \to C_{k-1}$ therefore has either zero domain or zero codomain, so $d_k = 0$ for all $k$.

Hence homology equals the chain groups:

$$
H_k(\mathbb{C}P^n) = \begin{cases} \mathbb{Z} & k = 0, 2, 4, \ldots, 2n \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}
$$

The Euler characteristic is $n + 1$, consistent with the cell count. This is Hatcher §2.2 Example 2.42.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Degree of a self-map of $S^n$.*

Let $f: S^n \to S^n$ be a map and $f_*: H_n(S^n) \to H_n(S^n)$ the induced map on top homology, identified with multiplication by an integer $\deg f$. Compute $\deg f$ for (a) the identity, (b) a constant map, (c) a reflection through a hyperplane, and (d) the antipodal map.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The identity induces the identity; a constant map factors through a point. A reflection is one coordinate sign-change; the antipodal map is $n+1$ of them.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) $\deg(\mathrm{id}) = 1$, since $\mathrm{id}_* = \mathrm{id}$ on $H_n(S^n) = \mathbb{Z}$.

(b) A constant map factors as $S^n \to \mathrm{pt} \to S^n$. Since $H_n(\mathrm{pt}) = 0$ for $n \ge 1$, $f_* = 0$, so $\deg f = 0$.

(c) A reflection $r$ swaps the two hemispheres and reverses orientation; on $H_n(S^n)$ it acts by $-1$, so $\deg r = -1$.

(d) The antipodal map $a$ is the composite of $n+1$ coordinate reflections, so by multiplicativity of degree $\deg a = (-1)^{n+1}$. This is Hatcher §2.2.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, proof).** *Simplicial equals singular for a $\Delta$-complex.*

State the theorem that simplicial homology of a $\Delta$-complex agrees with its singular homology, and use it to compute $H_*(T^2)$ from the standard two-triangle $\Delta$-complex structure on the torus.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The torus is one vertex, three edges $a, b, c$, two 2-simplices $U, L$ with $\partial U = \partial L = a + b - c$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Theorem (Hatcher 2.27).** For a $\Delta$-complex $X$, the inclusion of simplicial into singular chains induces an isomorphism $H_n^{\Delta}(X) \cong H_n(X)$ for all $n$.

The standard torus $\Delta$-structure has one vertex $v$, three edges $a, b, c$ (all loops), and two triangles $U, L$ with $\partial U = \partial L = a + b - c$. Chain groups: $C_0 = \mathbb{Z}$, $C_1 = \mathbb{Z}^3$, $C_2 = \mathbb{Z}^2$.

$\partial_1 = 0$ (every edge is a loop, $\partial(\text{edge}) = v - v = 0$). $\partial_2(U) = \partial_2(L) = a + b - c$, so $\mathrm{im}\,\partial_2 = \mathbb{Z}\langle a+b-c\rangle$ and $\ker \partial_2 = \mathbb{Z}\langle U - L\rangle$.

- $H_0 = \mathbb{Z}$.
- $H_1 = \ker\partial_1 / \mathrm{im}\,\partial_2 = \mathbb{Z}^3 / \langle a+b-c\rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}^2$.
- $H_2 = \ker\partial_2 = \mathbb{Z}$ (generated by $U - L$, the fundamental class).

This recovers $H_*(T^2) = (\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Z}^2, \mathbb{Z})$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).** *Homology of the lens space $L(p; q)$.*

For coprime integers $p \ge 1$ and $q$, the three-dimensional lens space $L(p;q) = S^3/\mathbb{Z}_p$ has a CW structure with one cell in each dimension $0, 1, 2, 3$. The cellular boundary maps are $d_1 = 0$, $d_2 = \times p$, $d_3 = 0$. Compute $H_*(L(p;q); \mathbb{Z})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write the chain complex $\mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{0} \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{p} \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{0} \mathbb{Z}$ and read homology degree by degree.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The cellular chain complex is

$$
0 \to \underset{C_3}{\mathbb{Z}} \xrightarrow{\;0\;} \underset{C_2}{\mathbb{Z}} \xrightarrow{\;p\;} \underset{C_1}{\mathbb{Z}} \xrightarrow{\;0\;} \underset{C_0}{\mathbb{Z}} \to 0.
$$

- $H_0 = \mathbb{Z}$ (connected).
- $H_1 = \ker(0)/\mathrm{im}(p) = \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$.
- $H_2 = \ker(p)/\mathrm{im}(0) = 0$ (multiplication by $p$ is injective on $\mathbb{Z}$).
- $H_3 = \ker(0) = \mathbb{Z}$ (the fundamental class; $L(p;q)$ is a closed orientable 3-manifold).

So $H_*(L(p;q)) = (\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Z}/p, 0, \mathbb{Z})$. Note the homology is independent of $q$ — the integers $q$ and $q'$ give homotopy-distinct but homology-equivalent lens spaces, which is why lens spaces are Hatcher's flagship example that homology does not determine homotopy type. Hatcher §2.2 Example 2.43.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).** *Mayer-Vietoris for the wedge.*

Use the Mayer-Vietoris sequence to prove that for well-pointed spaces, $\tilde{H}_n(X \vee Y) \cong \tilde{H}_n(X) \oplus \tilde{H}_n(Y)$ for all $n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Fatten the wedge point: take $A$ a neighborhood of $X$ deformation-retracting to $X$, $B$ a neighborhood of $Y$, with $A \cap B$ contractible.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Choose open sets $A \supset X$ and $B \supset Y$ in $X \vee Y$ such that $A$ deformation-retracts to $X$, $B$ deformation-retracts to $Y$, and $A \cap B$ is contractible (a neighborhood of the basepoint). Well-pointedness guarantees such neighborhoods exist. Then $A \cup B = X \vee Y$ and the reduced Mayer-Vietoris sequence reads

$$
\tilde{H}_n(A\cap B) \to \tilde{H}_n(A)\oplus\tilde{H}_n(B) \to \tilde{H}_n(X\vee Y) \to \tilde{H}_{n-1}(A\cap B).
$$

Since $A \cap B$ is contractible, $\tilde{H}_*(A\cap B) = 0$, so the outer terms vanish and the middle map is an isomorphism:

$$
\tilde{H}_n(X)\oplus\tilde{H}_n(Y) = \tilde{H}_n(A)\oplus\tilde{H}_n(B) \xrightarrow{\;\cong\;} \tilde{H}_n(X\vee Y).
$$

The deformation retractions identify $\tilde{H}_*(A) = \tilde{H}_*(X)$ and $\tilde{H}_*(B) = \tilde{H}_*(Y)$. This is the homology analogue of the van Kampen splitting and underlies the computation of homology of any wedge of spheres. Hatcher §2.2.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, numeric).** *Homology of a Moore space.*

The Moore space $M(\mathbb{Z}/m, n)$ is built from $S^n$ by attaching an $(n+1)$-cell via a degree-$m$ map $S^n \to S^n$. Compute its reduced homology $\tilde{H}_*(M(\mathbb{Z}/m, n); \mathbb{Z})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The cellular chain complex has $C_{n+1} = \mathbb{Z}$, $C_n = \mathbb{Z}$, with boundary the degree-$m$ map, i.e. multiplication by $m$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The CW structure has one $0$-cell, one $n$-cell, and one $(n+1)$-cell (for $n \ge 1$). The reduced cellular chain complex has $\tilde{C}_{n+1} = \mathbb{Z}$, $\tilde{C}_n = \mathbb{Z}$, and the boundary $d_{n+1}: C_{n+1} \to C_n$ is multiplication by the degree $m$. All other chain groups vanish.

- $\tilde{H}_{n+1} = \ker(\times m) = 0$.
- $\tilde{H}_n = \mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z}$ (cokernel of multiplication by $m$).
- $\tilde{H}_k = 0$ for all other $k$.

So $M(\mathbb{Z}/m, n)$ has a single nonzero reduced homology group $\mathbb{Z}/m$ concentrated in degree $n$ — exactly the defining property of a Moore space. This is the homology dual of the Eilenberg-MacLane space and the building block for arbitrary homology via wedges and telescopes. Hatcher §2.2.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, proof).** *Euler characteristic from homology.*

Prove that for a finite CW complex $X$, the alternating sum of cell counts equals the alternating sum of Betti numbers: $\sum_n (-1)^n c_n = \sum_n (-1)^n \mathrm{rank}\, H_n(X)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply rank-nullity to each cellular boundary map. The cellular chain group $C_n$ has rank $c_n$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The cellular chain complex $(C_*, d_*)$ has $\mathrm{rank}\, C_n = c_n$ (the number of $n$-cells). Let $Z_n = \ker d_n$ and $B_n = \mathrm{im}\, d_{n+1}$, with ranks $z_n$ and $b_n$. Rank-nullity for $d_n: C_n \to C_{n-1}$ gives

$$
c_n = z_n + b_{n-1}.
$$

The rank of $H_n = Z_n / B_n$ is $z_n - b_n$ (rank is additive on the short exact sequence $0 \to B_n \to Z_n \to H_n \to 0$, modulo torsion). Now compute the alternating sum:

$$
\sum_n (-1)^n c_n = \sum_n (-1)^n (z_n + b_{n-1}) = \sum_n (-1)^n z_n + \sum_n (-1)^n b_{n-1}.
$$

Reindex the second sum: $\sum_n (-1)^n b_{n-1} = -\sum_n (-1)^n b_n$. Hence

$$
\sum_n (-1)^n c_n = \sum_n (-1)^n (z_n - b_n) = \sum_n (-1)^n \mathrm{rank}\, H_n(X).
$$

The middle sum telescopes the boundary ranks away, leaving the homological Euler characteristic. This is the homological invariance of $\chi$ — the alternating cell count is a homotopy invariant. Hatcher §2.2 Theorem 2.44.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Universal coefficients in action.*

State the universal coefficient theorem for homology and use it to compute $H_*(\mathbb{R}P^3; \mathbb{Z}/2)$ from the integral homology $H_*(\mathbb{R}P^3; \mathbb{Z}) = (\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Z}/2, 0, \mathbb{Z})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The UCT gives $H_n(X; G) \cong (H_n(X) \otimes G) \oplus \mathrm{Tor}(H_{n-1}(X), G)$. Compute each tensor and each Tor against $G = \mathbb{Z}/2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Theorem (UCT for homology).** For any space $X$ and abelian group $G$, there is a natural split short exact sequence

$$
0 \to H_n(X) \otimes G \to H_n(X; G) \to \mathrm{Tor}(H_{n-1}(X), G) \to 0,
$$

so $H_n(X; G) \cong (H_n(X)\otimes G) \oplus \mathrm{Tor}(H_{n-1}(X), G)$.

Take $G = \mathbb{Z}/2$ and $H_*(\mathbb{R}P^3) = (\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Z}/2, 0, \mathbb{Z})$. Recall $\mathbb{Z}\otimes\mathbb{Z}/2 = \mathbb{Z}/2$, $(\mathbb{Z}/2)\otimes\mathbb{Z}/2 = \mathbb{Z}/2$, $\mathrm{Tor}(\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Z}/2) = 0$, $\mathrm{Tor}(\mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/2) = \mathbb{Z}/2$.

- $H_0 = (\mathbb{Z}\otimes\mathbb{Z}/2) \oplus \mathrm{Tor}(0, -) = \mathbb{Z}/2$.
- $H_1 = (\mathbb{Z}/2 \otimes \mathbb{Z}/2) \oplus \mathrm{Tor}(\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Z}/2) = \mathbb{Z}/2 \oplus 0 = \mathbb{Z}/2$.
- $H_2 = (0 \otimes \mathbb{Z}/2) \oplus \mathrm{Tor}(\mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/2) = 0 \oplus \mathbb{Z}/2 = \mathbb{Z}/2$.
- $H_3 = (\mathbb{Z}\otimes\mathbb{Z}/2) \oplus \mathrm{Tor}(0, -) = \mathbb{Z}/2$.

So $H_*(\mathbb{R}P^3; \mathbb{Z}/2) = (\mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/2, \mathbb{Z}/2)$ — one $\mathbb{Z}/2$ in each degree, matching $\mathbb{R}P^3$'s mod-2 homology as a closed 3-manifold. The Tor term in degree 2 is the homological shadow of the torsion in $H_1$. Hatcher §3.A.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *Hurewicz isomorphism for a wedge of spheres.*

Let $X = \bigvee_{i=1}^k S^n$ with $n \ge 2$. Use the Hurewicz theorem to compute $\pi_n(X)$, and explain why the theorem does not directly compute $\pi_{n+1}(X)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$X$ is $(n-1)$-connected. The Hurewicz map $\pi_n \to H_n$ is an isomorphism in the first nonvanishing degree.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The wedge $X = \bigvee_{i=1}^k S^n$ is $(n-1)$-connected: it is simply connected (for $n \ge 2$) and has $H_i(X) = 0$ for $1 \le i \le n-1$, with $H_n(X) = \mathbb{Z}^k$ (by the wedge formula, one generator per sphere).

**Hurewicz theorem.** If $X$ is $(n-1)$-connected with $n \ge 2$, then $\pi_i(X) = 0$ for $i < n$ and the Hurewicz map $h: \pi_n(X) \to H_n(X)$ is an isomorphism.

Applying it in degree $n$: $\pi_n(X) \cong H_n(X) = \mathbb{Z}^k$, with generators the inclusions of the $k$ spheres.

The theorem does **not** compute $\pi_{n+1}(X)$: Hurewicz only identifies the *first* nonvanishing homotopy group with the corresponding homology group. In degree $n+1$ the map $h: \pi_{n+1}(X) \to H_{n+1}(X) = 0$ is generally neither injective nor surjective onto something interesting — $\pi_{n+1}(X)$ can be nonzero even though $H_{n+1}(X) = 0$. For example $\pi_{n+1}(S^n) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ for $n \ge 3$ (the suspended Hopf map), invisible to homology. Computing $\pi_{n+1}$ requires the Whitehead product and the relative/stable refinements beyond plain Hurewicz. Hatcher §4.2.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, proof).** *Homology of $S^n$ by the long exact sequence of a pair.*

Compute $H_*(S^n)$ for all $n \ge 1$ using the long exact sequence of the pair $(D^n, S^{n-1})$ together with excision, by induction on $n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\tilde{H}_k(S^n) \cong H_k(D^n, S^{n-1})$ via the quotient $D^n/S^{n-1} = S^n$, and the LES of $(D^n, S^{n-1})$ relates this to $\tilde{H}_{k-1}(S^{n-1})$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The pair $(D^n, S^{n-1})$ is a good pair, so $H_k(D^n, S^{n-1}) \cong \tilde{H}_k(D^n/S^{n-1}) = \tilde{H}_k(S^n)$ (the quotient collapses the boundary of the disk to give a sphere).

The long exact sequence of the pair reads

$$
\cdots \to \tilde{H}_k(D^n) \to H_k(D^n, S^{n-1}) \xrightarrow{\partial} \tilde{H}_{k-1}(S^{n-1}) \to \tilde{H}_{k-1}(D^n) \to \cdots
$$

Since $D^n$ is contractible, $\tilde{H}_*(D^n) = 0$, so the connecting map is an isomorphism:

$$
\tilde{H}_k(S^n) \cong H_k(D^n, S^{n-1}) \xrightarrow[\;\cong\;]{\partial} \tilde{H}_{k-1}(S^{n-1}).
$$

This is the suspension isomorphism in homology: $\tilde{H}_k(S^n) \cong \tilde{H}_{k-1}(S^{n-1})$.

**Induction.** Base case $S^0$ (two points): $\tilde{H}_0(S^0) = \mathbb{Z}$, all higher groups zero. Inductively, $\tilde{H}_k(S^n) = \tilde{H}_{k-1}(S^{n-1}) = \cdots = \tilde{H}_{k-n}(S^0)$, which is $\mathbb{Z}$ exactly when $k = n$ and $0$ otherwise.

Result: $\tilde{H}_k(S^n) = \mathbb{Z}$ for $k = n$ and $0$ otherwise; unreduced, $H_0(S^n) = H_n(S^n) = \mathbb{Z}$ (with $H_0 = H_n = \mathbb{Z}^2$ collapsing appropriately only at $n=0$). This is the canonical inductive computation, Hatcher §2.1.

</details>

</aside>
