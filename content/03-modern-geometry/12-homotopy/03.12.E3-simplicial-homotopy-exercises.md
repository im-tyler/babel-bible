## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Goerss-Jardine develops homotopy theory inside the category $\mathbf{sSet}$ of simplicial sets, replacing topological spaces by functors $\Delta^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathbf{Set}$. The central objects are **Kan complexes** — simplicial sets in which every horn $\Lambda^n_k \to X$ extends to a simplex $\Delta^n \to X$ — because these are exactly the objects whose combinatorial homotopy groups behave like the homotopy groups of a space. The book's spine is the Kan-Quillen model structure on $\mathbf{sSet}$, whose weak equivalences are the maps inducing isomorphisms on homotopy groups and whose fibrant objects are the Kan complexes.

This pack collects nine exercises drawn from Chapters I and II — two easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. The exercises are grouped: horn-filling and nerve warm-ups (easy), the combinatorial homotopy groups and the long exact sequence of a Kan fibration (medium), and the model-structure axioms and the identification $\pi_n(X, x) = [(\Delta^n, \partial\Delta^n), (X, x)]$ as honest group structures (hard).

The conventions are Goerss-Jardine's: $\Delta^n$ is the standard $n$-simplex (the representable functor on $[n]$), $\partial\Delta^n$ its boundary, $\Lambda^n_k$ the $k$-th horn (the boundary with the $k$-th face deleted). A map is a **Kan fibration** if it has the right lifting property against all horn inclusions $\Lambda^n_k \hookrightarrow \Delta^n$; a Kan complex is one for which $X \to *$ is a Kan fibration.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as the model solution. The remaining eight follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *Show that the nerve of a category is a Kan complex if and only if the category is a groupoid.*

**Solution.** Let $\mathcal{C}$ be a small category and $N\mathcal{C}$ its nerve, with $n$-simplices the strings of $n$ composable morphisms. A horn $\Lambda^n_k \to N\mathcal{C}$ is the data of all faces of an $n$-simplex except the $k$-th, compatibly; a filler is an $n$-simplex restricting to them.

**Inner horns ($0 < k < n$) always fill.** For $n = 2$, an inner horn $\Lambda^2_1 \to N\mathcal{C}$ supplies morphisms $f: x \to y$ and $g: y \to z$ (the two edges meeting at the middle vertex), and the unique filler is the composite $g \circ f$, with the missing face $x \to z$ being exactly $g \circ f$. Higher inner horns fill by the same associativity bookkeeping. So the nerve of any category is always an inner Kan complex (a quasi-category).

**Outer horns force invertibility.** Consider the outer horn $\Lambda^2_0 \to N\mathcal{C}$: it supplies $f: x \to y$ (edge $01$) and $h: x \to z$ (edge $02$), and a filler must produce a morphism $g: y \to z$ with $g \circ f = h$. For arbitrary $f, h$ this requires solving $g = h \circ f^{-1}$, possible for all inputs precisely when $f$ is invertible. Likewise the horn $\Lambda^2_2$ forces right-invertibility. So all outer horns fill if and only if every morphism of $\mathcal{C}$ is an isomorphism, i.e. $\mathcal{C}$ is a groupoid.

**Conclusion.** $N\mathcal{C}$ is a Kan complex iff every $2$-dimensional outer horn fills iff every morphism is invertible iff $\mathcal{C}$ is a groupoid. The higher horns then fill automatically since the nerve is $2$-coskeletal. $\square$

This is the structural fact separating quasi-categories (inner horns only, nerves of categories) from Kan complexes (all horns, nerves of groupoids). It is the entry point to the entire $\infty$-categorical dictionary.

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).** *Geometric realization preserves products.*

State the theorem that geometric realization $|{-}|: \mathbf{sSet} \to \mathbf{Top}$ preserves finite products (landing in compactly generated spaces), and explain why this fails in the naive product topology.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$|X \times Y| \cong |X| \times |Y|$ holds in compactly generated spaces. The issue is the product topology versus the $k$-ification.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Theorem (Goerss-Jardine I.2).** The geometric realization functor $|{-}|$ preserves finite limits; in particular the natural map $|X \times Y| \to |X| \times |Y|$ is a homeomorphism when the target carries the compactly generated (k-space) topology.

The realization $|X|$ is a colimit of simplices glued by the face and degeneracy maps. The natural map $|X \times Y| \to |X| \times |Y|$ is always a continuous bijection, but in the *ordinary* product topology it need not be a homeomorphism: a product of two CW complexes is CW only after $k$-ification when one factor is not locally compact (Dowker's counterexample). Working in $\mathbf{CGHaus}$, products are computed with the compactly generated topology, and the map becomes a homeomorphism. This is why simplicial homotopy theory is set in compactly generated spaces — realization is then a strong monoidal left adjoint, matching products on the nose. Goerss-Jardine §I.2.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Horns of the 2-simplex.*

List the horns $\Lambda^2_k$ of $\Delta^2$, identify which are inner and which are outer, and state which horn inclusions a Kan fibration must lift against.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\Lambda^2_k$ is $\partial\Delta^2$ with the face opposite vertex $k$ removed. Inner means $0 < k < n$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The triangle $\Delta^2$ has three horns, each obtained from the boundary $\partial\Delta^2$ (three edges) by deleting one edge:

- $\Lambda^2_0$: keep edges $01$ and $02$ (the two edges at vertex $0$), delete edge $12$. **Outer.**
- $\Lambda^2_1$: keep edges $01$ and $12$ (the two edges at vertex $1$), delete edge $02$. **Inner.**
- $\Lambda^2_2$: keep edges $02$ and $12$ (the two edges at vertex $2$), delete edge $01$. **Outer.**

A **Kan fibration** $p: X \to Y$ must have the right lifting property against *all* horn inclusions $\Lambda^n_k \hookrightarrow \Delta^n$, $0 \le k \le n$, $n \ge 1$ — inner and outer alike. A **left/inner fibration** (quasi-categorical) lifts only against inner horns. Goerss-Jardine §I.3.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).** *$\pi_0$ of a Kan complex is well-defined.*

For a Kan complex $X$, define $\pi_0(X)$ as the set of $0$-simplices modulo the relation $x \sim y$ iff there is a $1$-simplex with faces $x, y$. Prove this relation is an equivalence relation, using the Kan condition.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reflexivity uses a degenerate $1$-simplex. Symmetry and transitivity use horn-filling on $\Lambda^2_0$ and $\Lambda^2_1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $x \sim y$ if there exists $\omega \in X_1$ with $d_1\omega = x$, $d_0\omega = y$.

**Reflexive.** The degeneracy $s_0 x \in X_1$ has $d_0 s_0 x = d_1 s_0 x = x$, so $x \sim x$.

**Symmetric.** Suppose $\omega: x \to y$. Build the horn $\Lambda^2_0 \to X$ with edge $01$ equal to $\omega$ and edge $02$ equal to $s_0 x$ (the constant path at $x$). The Kan condition fills it to a $2$-simplex $\sigma$ whose remaining face $d_0\sigma$ is a $1$-simplex from $y$ to $x$. Hence $y \sim x$.

**Transitive.** Suppose $\omega: x \to y$ and $\tau: y \to z$. Form the horn $\Lambda^2_1 \to X$ with edge $01 = \omega$ and edge $12 = \tau$. The Kan condition fills it to a $2$-simplex $\sigma$, whose face $d_1\sigma$ is a $1$-simplex from $x$ to $z$. Hence $x \sim z$.

So $\sim$ is an equivalence relation and $\pi_0(X) = X_0/\!\sim$ is well-defined. Each step used the horn-filling property; in a non-Kan simplicial set the relation need not even be symmetric. Goerss-Jardine §I.7.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *The simplicial homotopy groups carry a group structure.*

For a pointed Kan complex $(X, x)$ and $n \ge 1$, define $\pi_n(X, x)$ as homotopy classes of maps $(\Delta^n, \partial\Delta^n) \to (X, x)$, equivalently $n$-simplices $\alpha$ with all faces degenerate at $x$, modulo homotopy. Sketch how the Kan condition produces the group multiplication.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Given representatives $\alpha, \beta$, build a horn $\Lambda^{n+1}_k$ whose relevant faces are $\alpha$, $\beta$, and basepoint-degenerate simplices; the missing face is the product.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Represent classes by $n$-simplices $\alpha, \beta \in X_n$ all of whose faces are the constant $s_0^{n-1}x$ (the basepoint). To multiply, construct a horn $\Lambda^{n+1}_{n} \to X$: assign to the faces $d_{n-1}, d_{n+1}$ the simplices $\alpha$ and $\beta$, and to every other face the basepoint-degenerate $n$-simplex. These faces agree on overlaps because all their own faces are basepoint, so they assemble into a well-defined horn. The Kan condition fills it to an $(n+1)$-simplex $\sigma$; the missing face $d_n\sigma$ is again an $n$-simplex with all faces at the basepoint, and we define $[\alpha]\cdot[\beta] := [d_n\sigma]$.

The Kan condition also supplies the homotopies showing this is independent of the chosen fillers, that the basepoint-degenerate simplex is a two-sided unit, that inverses exist (fill a horn missing a different face), and — for $n \ge 2$ — that the operation is abelian (the Eckmann-Hilton argument runs because two independent "directions" of multiplication exist). So $\pi_n(X, x)$ is a group, abelian for $n \ge 2$, isomorphic to $\pi_n(|X|, x)$. Goerss-Jardine §I.7.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).** *Long exact sequence of a Kan fibration.*

Let $p: E \to B$ be a Kan fibration of pointed Kan complexes with fibre $F = p^{-1}(b)$. State the long exact sequence relating $\pi_*(F)$, $\pi_*(E)$, $\pi_*(B)$, and indicate where the Kan condition enters in defining the connecting map.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The sequence has the same shape as for a Serre fibration of spaces. The connecting map $\partial: \pi_n(B) \to \pi_{n-1}(F)$ lifts a representing simplex through $p$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Theorem.** A Kan fibration $p: E \to B$ with fibre $F$ over the basepoint yields a long exact sequence of homotopy groups

$$
\cdots \to \pi_n(F) \to \pi_n(E) \xrightarrow{p_*} \pi_n(B) \xrightarrow{\partial} \pi_{n-1}(F) \to \cdots \to \pi_0(E) \to \pi_0(B).
$$

The maps $\pi_n(F) \to \pi_n(E)$ and $p_*$ are induced by the inclusion $F \hookrightarrow E$ and by $p$.

The connecting homomorphism $\partial$ uses the Kan condition directly. Given a class $[\beta] \in \pi_n(B)$ represented by $\beta: \Delta^n \to B$ with $\partial\Delta^n$ at the basepoint, the basepoint of $E$ supplies a lift of $\Lambda^n_0 \to E$ (all faces map to the basepoint of $E$, which lies over the basepoint of $B$). Filling against $p$ — possible because $p$ is a Kan fibration, so the lifting problem $\Lambda^n_0 \to E$, $\Delta^n \to B$ has a solution $\tilde\beta: \Delta^n \to E$ — produces a simplex whose last face $d_n\tilde\beta$ lands in $F$ and represents $\partial[\beta] \in \pi_{n-1}(F)$.

Exactness at each spot is verified by further horn-fillings; the argument is the combinatorial mirror of the topological fibration sequence. Goerss-Jardine §I.7. This is the workhorse computational tool of simplicial homotopy theory.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, numeric).** *Homotopy groups of a minimal $K(\mathbb{Z}, 1)$.*

The simplicial abelian group $K(\mathbb{Z}, 1) = N\mathbb{Z}$, the nerve of $\mathbb{Z}$ regarded as a one-object groupoid, is a Kan complex. Compute its simplicial homotopy groups $\pi_n(K(\mathbb{Z},1))$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The nerve of a group $G$ realizes to $BG$, the classifying space, which is a $K(G, 1)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The simplicial set $N\mathbb{Z}$ (nerve of $\mathbb{Z}$ as a one-object groupoid) has $n$-simplices the $n$-tuples $(a_1, \ldots, a_n) \in \mathbb{Z}^n$ (composable strings), face maps multiplying adjacent entries, degeneracies inserting identities. It is a Kan complex because $\mathbb{Z}$ is a group (a groupoid), as established in the lead exercise.

Its realization $|N\mathbb{Z}| = B\mathbb{Z} \simeq S^1$, the circle. So

$$
\pi_n(K(\mathbb{Z},1)) = \pi_n(S^1) = \begin{cases} \mathbb{Z} & n = 1 \\ 0 & n \neq 1. \end{cases}
$$

This is the defining property of an Eilenberg-MacLane space $K(\mathbb{Z}, 1)$: a single homotopy group $\mathbb{Z}$ concentrated in degree $1$. The simplicial model $N\mathbb{Z}$ is the standard combinatorial $K(\mathbb{Z}, 1)$, and the Dold-Kan construction generalizes it to $K(A, n)$ for any abelian group $A$ and degree $n$. Goerss-Jardine §I.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).** *$\mathrm{Ex}^\infty$ is a fibrant replacement.*

Kan's functor $\mathrm{Ex}$ has a natural transformation $X \to \mathrm{Ex}\, X$, and the colimit $\mathrm{Ex}^\infty X = \mathrm{colim}(X \to \mathrm{Ex}\,X \to \mathrm{Ex}^2 X \to \cdots)$ is always a Kan complex. State the two key properties of $\mathrm{Ex}^\infty$ and explain why it serves as a fibrant replacement in the Kan-Quillen model structure.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathrm{Ex}$ is right adjoint to subdivision $\mathrm{sd}$. The map $X \to \mathrm{Ex}^\infty X$ is a weak equivalence and the target is Kan.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Kan's extension functor $\mathrm{Ex}$ is defined by $(\mathrm{Ex}\, X)_n = \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbf{sSet}}(\mathrm{sd}\,\Delta^n, X)$, where $\mathrm{sd}$ is barycentric subdivision; it is right adjoint to $\mathrm{sd}$. The unit gives a natural map $\eta: X \to \mathrm{Ex}\,X$, and iterating yields $\mathrm{Ex}^\infty X = \mathrm{colim}_k \mathrm{Ex}^k X$.

**Two key properties (Goerss-Jardine III.4).**
1. $\mathrm{Ex}^\infty X$ is a Kan complex for every simplicial set $X$. (Any horn $\Lambda^n_k \to \mathrm{Ex}^\infty X$ factors through some finite stage $\mathrm{Ex}^k X$ since horns are finite; subdivision-then-adjunction provides a filler one stage up, which lands in the colimit.)
2. The natural map $X \to \mathrm{Ex}^\infty X$ is a weak equivalence (it induces an isomorphism on all homotopy groups; subdivision does not change the homotopy type of the realization).

Together these say: $X \to \mathrm{Ex}^\infty X$ is a weak equivalence into a Kan (fibrant) object. Since one checks that the map is also a cofibration (a monomorphism), it is an **acyclic cofibration into a fibrant object** — precisely a fibrant replacement of $X$ in the Kan-Quillen model structure. Unlike the abstract small-object-argument replacement, $\mathrm{Ex}^\infty$ is explicit and functorial, which is why it appears throughout the theory. Goerss-Jardine §III.4.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *The Kan-Quillen model structure: verify the lifting axiom in one case.*

In the Kan-Quillen model structure on $\mathbf{sSet}$, cofibrations are monomorphisms, fibrations are Kan fibrations, and weak equivalences are maps inducing $\pi_*$-isomorphisms on fibrant replacements. Prove that every acyclic cofibration has the left lifting property against every fibration in the case of horn inclusions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Horn inclusions $\Lambda^n_k \hookrightarrow \Delta^n$ are the generating acyclic cofibrations. The lifting property against Kan fibrations is the *definition* of a Kan fibration.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the Kan-Quillen model structure, the **generating acyclic cofibrations** are exactly the horn inclusions $\{\Lambda^n_k \hookrightarrow \Delta^n : n \ge 1,\, 0 \le k \le n\}$, and the **generating cofibrations** are the boundary inclusions $\{\partial\Delta^n \hookrightarrow \Delta^n\}$.

**Claim.** A map $p: X \to Y$ has the right lifting property against all horn inclusions iff $p$ is a Kan fibration.

This is immediate from the definitions: the right lifting property of $p$ against $\Lambda^n_k \hookrightarrow \Delta^n$ says exactly that every commuting square

$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
\Lambda^n_k & \to & X \\
\downarrow & & \downarrow{\scriptstyle p} \\
\Delta^n & \to & Y
\end{array}
$$

admits a diagonal lift $\Delta^n \to X$ — which is the definition of $p$ being a Kan fibration. So a Kan fibration lifts against every generating acyclic cofibration.

The general acyclic cofibrations are retracts of transfinite compositions of pushouts of these horn inclusions (the small-object argument). Lifting properties are closed under retracts, pushouts, and transfinite composition, so a Kan fibration lifts against *every* acyclic cofibration, not just the generators. This verifies one half of the lifting axiom CM4; the other half (cofibrations lift against acyclic fibrations) is dual, using the boundary inclusions. Goerss-Jardine §I.11, §II.3.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *Quasi-categories versus Kan complexes.*

A quasi-category is a simplicial set with fillers for all *inner* horns. Prove that a quasi-category in which every $1$-simplex is invertible (in the homotopy category) is a Kan complex.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The only horns a quasi-category might fail to fill are the outer ones $\Lambda^n_0$ and $\Lambda^n_n$. Reduce outer-horn filling for $n \ge 2$ to invertibility of the relevant edge.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $X$ be a quasi-category — inner horns $\Lambda^n_k$ ($0 < k < n$) all fill. The homotopy category $\mathrm{ho}(X)$ has the $0$-simplices as objects and homotopy classes of $1$-simplices as morphisms, with composition supplied by inner-horn fillers (the lead exercise's $\Lambda^2_1$ argument). Suppose every morphism of $\mathrm{ho}(X)$ is invertible, i.e. every $1$-simplex of $X$ is an equivalence.

We must fill the outer horns. Consider $\Lambda^n_0 \to X$ for $n \ge 2$. Joyal's theorem (the "special outer horn" criterion) states: in a quasi-category, an outer horn $\Lambda^n_0 \to X$ fills whenever the initial edge (the $1$-simplex spanning vertices $0,1$) is an equivalence. By hypothesis every edge is an equivalence, so this initial edge is, and the horn fills. Symmetrically $\Lambda^n_n$ fills when its terminal edge is an equivalence, which again holds. The case $n = 1$ is vacuous.

Therefore every horn — inner and outer — fills, so $X$ is a Kan complex.

This is the simplicial form of the slogan "a Kan complex is an $\infty$-groupoid": Kan complexes are exactly the quasi-categories whose every morphism is invertible. The general filling of special outer horns is Joyal's lifting theorem, proved by induction on dimension using inner-horn fillers. Goerss-Jardine, and Joyal/Lurie for the outer-horn criterion.

</details>

</aside>
