## Intuition [Beginner]

The *suspension* of a topological space $X$ is the space you get by stretching $X$ between two cone-points: take two cones over $X$ and glue them along their common base. Geometrically: $X$ is "lifted" into one higher dimension by stretching it through two opposite poles.

The suspension of an $n$-sphere is the $(n+1)$-sphere: $\Sigma S^n \cong S^{n+1}$. Iterating, $n$-fold suspension of a point is the $n$-sphere. So suspension is the homotopical operation that increases dimension by one.

Why care? Because suspension is the right way to compare homotopy in different dimensions. The Freudenthal suspension theorem says that for highly connected spaces, suspension induces an isomorphism on homotopy groups. In the *limit* as you keep suspending, the homotopy groups stabilise — these are the *stable homotopy groups* — and the resulting "stable" world is much simpler than the unstable one.

## Visual [Beginner]

A space $X$ stretched between two cone-points (north and south "pole") to form its suspension $\Sigma X$. The original $X$ sits in the middle as the equator.

![A space X between two cone points, with the original X visible as an equatorial slice in the suspension.](/img/suspension-placeholder.svg)

Iterating: $\Sigma^k X$ adds $k$ pairs of cone-points, raising the "dimension" of $X$ by $k$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The suspension of the 0-sphere $S^0$ (two points) is the 1-sphere $S^1$ (a circle). Two cones each have one point at the apex; gluing the bases together produces a circle.

The suspension of the 1-sphere $S^1$ (a circle) is the 2-sphere $S^2$. Two cones over the circle produce two disks; gluing along their boundaries gives the 2-sphere.

Iterating: $\Sigma^n S^0 = S^n$, generalising the suspension-of-a-circle pattern.

The Freudenthal suspension theorem: for a highly-connected space $X$ (more precisely, an $n$-connected space), the suspension map $\pi_k(X) \to \pi_{k+1}(\Sigma X)$ is an isomorphism for $k \leq 2n$ and surjective for $k = 2n + 1$. Applied to spheres, this says $\pi_k(S^n) \to \pi_{k+1}(S^{n+1})$ is an iso for small enough $k$ — the source of stable homotopy.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a topological space [02.01.01]. The **(unreduced) suspension** of $X$ is the quotient

$$
SX := (X \times [0, 1]) / \big( (x, 0) \sim (x', 0) \text{ and } (x, 1) \sim (x', 1) \big).
$$

The two equivalence classes corresponding to the collapsed slices $\{0\}$ and $\{1\}$ are the **cone-points** (or "apex" and "co-apex") of $SX$.

For pointed spaces $(X, x_0)$ — topological spaces with a chosen basepoint — the **reduced suspension** $\Sigma X$ is the further quotient

$$
\Sigma X := SX / \big( \{x_0\} \times [0, 1] \big),
$$

collapsing the *meridian* through the basepoint to a single point. The result is naturally pointed by the image of this collapsed meridian.

For "nice" spaces (e.g., CW complexes), the unreduced suspension and the reduced suspension are homotopy equivalent — only the basepoint convention differs.

The **iterated suspension** $\Sigma^k X$ is defined recursively as $\Sigma(\Sigma^{k-1} X)$, with $\Sigma^0 X = X$.

**Adjunction with loop space.** The reduced suspension $\Sigma$ is left-adjoint to the loop-space functor $\Omega$:

$$
[\Sigma X, Y] \cong [X, \Omega Y]
$$

for pointed CW complexes $X, Y$. This is the *fundamental adjunction* of pointed homotopy theory and the source of much computational power.

**Suspension on homotopy groups.** The suspension map $\Sigma : \pi_n(X, x_0) \to \pi_{n+1}(\Sigma X, *)$ sends a based map $f : (S^n, *) \to (X, x_0)$ to $\Sigma f : (\Sigma S^n, *) = (S^{n+1}, *) \to (\Sigma X, *)$.

**Key examples.**

- $\Sigma S^n \cong S^{n+1}$ (homotopy equivalence).
- $\Sigma X$ is always simply connected if $X$ is path-connected.
- $\Sigma$ commutes with smash products: $\Sigma(X \wedge Y) \cong \Sigma X \wedge Y \cong X \wedge \Sigma Y$ (for pointed spaces).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Freudenthal suspension).** *Let $X$ be an $n$-connected pointed CW complex (i.e., $\pi_k(X) = 0$ for $k \leq n$). Then the suspension map*

$$
\Sigma : \pi_k(X) \to \pi_{k+1}(\Sigma X)
$$

*is an isomorphism for $k \leq 2n$, and a surjection for $k = 2n + 1$.*

**Proof sketch.** Use the *homotopy excision theorem*. The suspension $\Sigma X$ is the union of two cones $C_+ X$ and $C_- X$ glued along $X$. Both cones are contractible. The pair $(\Sigma X, C_+ X)$ has the property that $C_+ X \cup C_- X = \Sigma X$ and $C_+ X \cap C_- X = X$. Excision in homotopy (modified for the connectivity hypothesis) compares the relative homotopy groups $\pi_k(\Sigma X, C_+ X) \to \pi_k(\Sigma X / C_+ X)$.

For $X$ which is $n$-connected, both cones are contractible and the relative groups simplify. The connecting map in the long exact sequence of the pair $(C_+ X, X)$ — together with the homotopy equivalence $\Sigma X / C_+ X \simeq \Sigma X / *$ — yields the suspension iso in the claimed range. The detailed proof (in Hatcher Ch. 4, or May Ch. 8) uses the *Blakers-Massey theorem* in the form: for sufficiently connected pieces, excision in homotopy holds in a range of degrees. $\square$

The *suspension* gives a map between unstable homotopy groups that becomes more and more an isomorphism as you increase suspension count, eventually stabilising — this is the entry into stable homotopy theory [03.08.06], where suspension is invertible (formally) and the homotopy structure is much more tractable.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Compute $\Sigma^2 S^0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\Sigma S^0 = S^1$, $\Sigma S^1 = S^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\Sigma^2 S^0 = \Sigma(\Sigma S^0) = \Sigma S^1 = S^2$. More generally, $\Sigma^n S^0 = S^n$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).**

Show that the suspension of any path-connected space is simply connected.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Loops in $\Sigma X$ can be deformed up through one of the cones to the corresponding cone-point.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A loop in $\Sigma X$ based at the basepoint can be split into segments in the upper cone $C_+ X$ and the lower cone $C_- X$, joined at points of $X$. Each segment in a cone is null-homotopic (cones are contractible). For path-connected $X$, the points of attachment can be slid to the basepoint via a path in $X$ (which has a continuous parametrisation since $X$ is path-connected). Combining: any loop in $\Sigma X$ is null-homotopic. So $\pi_1(\Sigma X) = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

State and outline the suspension-loop adjunction $[\Sigma X, Y] \cong [X, \Omega Y]$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A based map $\Sigma X \to Y$ is the same as a based homotopy of based maps $X \to Y$ — equivalently a based map from $X$ to the loop space.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A based map $f : \Sigma X \to Y$ corresponds to a based map $X \times [0, 1] \to Y$ that sends $X \times \{0\}, X \times \{1\}$, and $\{x_0\} \times [0, 1]$ to the basepoint $y_0 \in Y$. By currying, this is the same as a continuous map $X \to \mathrm{Map}_*(I, Y)$ where the target is loops in $Y$ — i.e., a map $X \to \Omega Y$. Homotopy classes of such maps biject. So $[\Sigma X, Y]_* \cong [X, \Omega Y]_*$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Show that suspension induces a homomorphism on homotopy groups: $\Sigma : \pi_n(X) \to \pi_{n+1}(\Sigma X)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use functoriality of suspension and the fact that $\Sigma S^n = S^{n+1}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a based map $f : (S^n, *) \to (X, x_0)$, define $\Sigma f : (\Sigma S^n, *) = (S^{n+1}, *) \to (\Sigma X, *)$ by $\Sigma f([s, t]) := [f(s), t]$. Continuity follows from continuity of $f$ and the quotient topology. Functoriality and naturality give $\Sigma(f \cdot g) = (\Sigma f) \cdot (\Sigma g)$ for the group operation in homotopy groups (concatenation in the suspension coordinate). So $\Sigma$ is a homomorphism.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why is the suspension of a sphere always a higher-dimensional sphere?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The cone over $S^n$ is the $(n+1)$-disk; two disks glued along their boundary form $S^{n+1}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The cone $C S^n = (S^n \times [0, 1]) / (S^n \times \{0\})$ is homeomorphic to the $(n+1)$-dimensional disk $D^{n+1}$ (the cone-point is the centre of the disk, and the equator $S^n \times \{1\}$ is the boundary $\partial D^{n+1} = S^n$). Suspension glues two such cones along their common $S^n$ equator: this produces $D^{n+1}_+ \cup_{S^n} D^{n+1}_- = S^{n+1}$, a sphere of one higher dimension.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that suspension increases the connectivity of a space by one: if $X$ is $n$-connected, then $\Sigma X$ is $(n+1)$-connected.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use Freudenthal in a range of degrees and the contractibility of the cones.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $X$ $n$-connected: $\pi_k(X) = 0$ for $k \leq n$. By Freudenthal, $\pi_{k+1}(\Sigma X) \cong \pi_k(X) = 0$ for $k \leq 2n$. Specialising to $k \leq n + 1 \leq 2n$ (which holds for $n \geq 1$), $\pi_{k+1}(\Sigma X) = 0$ for $k + 1 \leq n + 2$. So $\pi_j(\Sigma X) = 0$ for $j \leq n + 1$, which means $\Sigma X$ is $(n+1)$-connected.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Why does the suspension functor on the homotopy category fail to be invertible (in the unstable setting)?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Suspension increases connectivity but doesn't reverse it.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suspension always produces a simply-connected space (Exercise 2). So the suspension functor $\Sigma$ is not surjective onto the homotopy category — for example, $S^1$ (not simply connected) cannot be in the image of $\Sigma$. Hence $\Sigma$ has no inverse functor in the unstable homotopy category.

In the *stable* homotopy category, this is fixed by formally inverting suspension: spectra are constructed precisely to make $\Sigma$ an equivalence. In the stable category, $\Sigma X$ and $\Sigma^{-1} X$ both exist for every spectrum $X$, and the homotopy structure is much simpler than the unstable one.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Stable homotopy groups of spheres.** The *stable* homotopy groups $\pi_k^s := \lim_{n \to \infty} \pi_{n+k}(S^n)$ are well-defined by the Freudenthal theorem (the limit stabilises). They form a deeply studied invariant: $\pi_0^s = \mathbb{Z}$, $\pi_1^s = \mathbb{Z}/2$, $\pi_2^s = \mathbb{Z}/2$, $\pi_3^s = \mathbb{Z}/24$, etc. These are the homotopy groups of the sphere spectrum $\mathbb{S}$.

**Suspension and homology.** Reduced homology and cohomology shift under suspension:

$$
\widetilde{H}_n(\Sigma X) \cong \widetilde{H}_{n-1}(X), \qquad \widetilde{H}^n(\Sigma X) \cong \widetilde{H}^{n-1}(X).
$$

This is the *suspension isomorphism* in (co)homology, a special case of the long exact sequence of the cone-pair.

**Smash product and suspension.** The reduced suspension $\Sigma X = S^1 \wedge X$, where $\wedge$ is the smash product of pointed spaces $X \wedge Y := (X \times Y)/(X \vee Y)$. Iterated: $\Sigma^n X = S^n \wedge X$. So suspension is "smashing with the sphere $S^1$" — a perspective that extends naturally to spectra.

**Loop spaces and $\Omega$-spectra.** The right adjoint to suspension is the loop-space functor $\Omega Y := \mathrm{Map}_*(S^1, Y)$. An $\Omega$-spectrum is a sequence $(E_n)$ with structure maps $E_n \xrightarrow{\sim} \Omega E_{n+1}$ — a "stable" version of a topological space. Each cohomology theory $E^*$ is represented by an $\Omega$-spectrum.

**Eilenberg-MacLane spaces and suspension.** $K(A, n) = \Omega K(A, n+1)$ — the loop space of $K(A, n+1)$ is $K(A, n)$. Equivalently, $\Sigma K(A, n)$ is "almost" $K(A, n+1)$ in low degrees, but has additional homotopy in higher degrees that the loop-space adjunction strips out.

**Spanier-Whitehead duality and stable category.** In the stable homotopy category (objects: spectra), suspension is invertible. Spanier-Whitehead duality assigns to each finite spectrum $X$ a dual $DX$ with $DX \wedge X \to S^0$ giving a duality. This is the stable analogue of Poincaré duality on closed manifolds.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Suspension of a sphere is a sphere.** Proved in Exercise 5: cones over $S^n$ are $(n+1)$-disks, glued along $S^n$ form $S^{n+1}$.

**Suspension-loop adjunction.** Sketched in Exercise 3. The full categorical statement: $\Sigma : \mathbf{Ho}(\mathbf{Top}_*) \to \mathbf{Ho}(\mathbf{Top}_*)$ is left-adjoint to $\Omega$. The unit and counit of the adjunction are natural maps $X \to \Omega \Sigma X$ and $\Sigma \Omega Y \to Y$.

**Freudenthal suspension theorem.** Sketched in §"Key theorem". Full proof uses the Blakers-Massey theorem applied to the cone-cofibration sequence $X \to C_+ X \to \Sigma X$.

**Suspension isomorphism in (co)homology.** Apply the Mayer-Vietoris sequence to the cover $\Sigma X = C_+ X \cup C_- X$ with intersection $X$. Cones are contractible, so their (co)homology vanishes in positive degrees. The connecting homomorphism in the resulting long exact sequence gives the suspension iso $\widetilde{H}_n(X) \cong \widetilde{H}_{n+1}(\Sigma X)$ (with cohomology by duality).

**Connectivity raises by one.** Proved in Exercise 6.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The suspension construction goes back to Freudenthal's 1937 paper, where he introduced what is now called the Freudenthal suspension theorem and used it to begin the study of stable homotopy. The terminology "suspension" comes from the geometric picture of stretching a space between two pole-points.

The shift from unstable to stable homotopy theory — formalised by Boardman (1965) and others through the construction of the *stable homotopy category* — was one of the most significant conceptual developments of mid-twentieth-century algebraic topology. It made suspension into an invertible operation, simplified many calculations, and produced the *category of spectra* as the natural home for generalised cohomology theories.

In modern language, suspension is the *shift* operator in the stable category: $\Sigma$ corresponds to multiplication by the unit object $S^1$ (the circle, viewed as a one-dimensional sphere). The stable category is generated by the sphere spectrum $\mathbb{S}$ under suspension.
