## Intuition [Beginner]

A space's homotopy groups can be thought of as a stack of layers, one for each dimension. The Postnikov tower of a space is a sequence of approximations that successively *retains* the bottom layers and *truncates* the top ones. The Whitehead tower is the dual sequence: it successively *kills* the bottom layers and *retains* the top ones.

Concretely, the Whitehead tower of a space $X$ produces, at stage $n$, a space $X\langle n \rangle$ whose lowest homotopy group sits in degree $n + 1$ rather than at the bottom. The tower thus generalises the universal cover (which kills $\pi_1$) to higher degrees.

The Whitehead tower is the principal computational route to the homotopy groups of spheres. Combining it with the Leray-Serre spectral sequence and Hurewicz's theorem turns the calculation of $\pi_4(S^3)$, $\pi_5(S^3)$, and Serre's finiteness theorem into a sequence of mechanical spectral-sequence reductions.

## Visual [Beginner]

A vertical sequence of spaces $\cdots \to X\langle 3 \rangle \to X\langle 2 \rangle \to X\langle 1 \rangle \to X$, each fibration killing the next homotopy group from the bottom.

![A diagram showing a vertical chain of fibrations with X at the bottom and the n-connected covers X langle n rangle ascending; each fibre is labelled K of pi_n X comma n minus 1 to show what is being killed at each step.](/img/whitehead-tower-placeholder.svg)

The picture is a tower whose every step is a fibration with Eilenberg-MacLane fibre. The fibre at level $n$ is $K(\pi_n X, n - 1)$ — the loops on $K(\pi_n X, n)$ — exactly the data that kills $\pi_n X$ in the cover.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The first stage of the Whitehead tower of $S^3$ kills the fundamental class.

Start with $S^3$: $\pi_3(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}$, all lower homotopy groups vanish (since $S^3$ is two-connected), and the higher homotopy groups are unknown — in fact this is the question we want to answer.

Build the second stage of the Whitehead tower as the fibration $S^3 \langle 3 \rangle \to S^3$ whose fibre is $K(\mathbb{Z}, 2) = \mathbb{C}P^\infty$. The total space $S^3 \langle 3 \rangle$ is three-connected: by the long exact sequence of the fibration, $\pi_3(S^3 \langle 3 \rangle) = 0$, and all lower groups also vanish.

What we have gained: $S^3 \langle 3 \rangle$ has its lowest non-vanishing homotopy group in degree $4$ — and that degree-$4$ group is exactly $\pi_4(S^3)$ by the long exact sequence. By the Hurewicz theorem (a four-paper series of Witold Hurewicz from 1935-36), the lowest homotopy of a connected space agrees with its lowest homology. So $\pi_4(S^3) = H_4(S^3 \langle 3 \rangle; \mathbb{Z})$.

The Leray-Serre spectral sequence of the fibration $K(\mathbb{Z}, 2) \to S^3 \langle 3 \rangle \to S^3$, computing $H^*(S^3 \langle 3 \rangle)$ from $H^*(\mathbb{C}P^\infty) = \mathbb{Z}[x]$ and $H^*(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}[\sigma]/(\sigma^2)$, yields $H_4(S^3 \langle 3 \rangle; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ by a transgression argument. The result is $\pi_4(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ — the first new computation in the homotopy groups of spheres beyond Hurewicz's diagonal answer $\pi_n(S^n) = \mathbb{Z}$.

What this tells us: the Whitehead tower converts a homotopy-theoretic question into a homology computation, which the spectral sequence answers mechanically. The whole subject of low-degree homotopy groups of spheres rests on this routing.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout this unit, all spaces are pointed, path-connected CW complexes. The path space of a pointed space $X$ is the based path space (notation decision #32):
$$
PX = \mathrm{Map}_*(I, X) = \{\gamma : [0, 1] \to X \mid \gamma(0) = x_0\},
$$
a contractible space fitting into the **path-loop fibration** $\Omega X \to PX \to X$ with $\pi(\gamma) = \gamma(1)$.

**$n$-connected cover.** A pointed space $\tilde{X}$ is the **$n$-connected cover** of $X$ — written $X\langle n \rangle$ — if there is a fibration $\pi : X\langle n \rangle \to X$ such that:

1. $X\langle n \rangle$ is $n$-connected, i.e., $\pi_i(X\langle n \rangle) = 0$ for $0 \leq i \leq n$.
2. The map $\pi_*$ on homotopy groups is an isomorphism in every degree $> n$: $\pi_i(X\langle n \rangle) \xrightarrow{\sim} \pi_i(X)$ for $i > n$.

The $n$-connected cover exists and is unique up to homotopy [ref: TODO_REF Hatcher §4.3].

**Whitehead tower.** The **Whitehead tower** of $X$ is a sequence of fibrations
$$
\cdots \to X\langle 3 \rangle \to X\langle 2 \rangle \to X\langle 1 \rangle \to X = X\langle 0 \rangle
$$
with $X\langle n \rangle$ the $n$-connected cover. The fibre of $X\langle n \rangle \to X\langle n-1 \rangle$ is the Eilenberg-MacLane space $K(\pi_n(X), n - 1)$ — equivalently, the loop space $\Omega K(\pi_n(X), n)$. This identification follows from the long exact sequence of homotopy: the fibre kills $\pi_n$ and only $\pi_n$, so its homotopy is concentrated in degree $n - 1$, characterising it as $K(\pi_n(X), n - 1)$.

**Hurewicz map.** The **Hurewicz map** $h_n : \pi_n(X) \to H_n(X; \mathbb{Z})$ sends a class $[f : S^n \to X]$ to the pushforward $f_*([S^n])$ of the fundamental class.

**Hopf invariant.** For a continuous map $f : S^{2n - 1} \to S^n$, the **Hopf invariant** $H(f) \in \mathbb{Z}$ (notation decision #34) is defined by mapping cone construction. Form the cofibre $C_f = S^n \cup_f D^{2n}$ obtained by attaching a $2n$-cell to $S^n$ along $f$. The integer cohomology has $H^n(C_f) = \mathbb{Z}\langle u \rangle$, $H^{2n}(C_f) = \mathbb{Z}\langle v \rangle$, and the cup product satisfies
$$
u \smile u = H(f) \cdot v \in H^{2n}(C_f).
$$
The integer $H(f)$ is the Hopf invariant of $f$. By construction, $H : \pi_{2n-1}(S^n) \to \mathbb{Z}$ is a group homomorphism.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Hurewicz 1935-36).** *Let $X$ be path-connected with $\pi_i(X) = 0$ for $1 \leq i < n$ (for some $n \geq 1$). Then the Hurewicz map $h_n : \pi_n(X) \to H_n(X; \mathbb{Z})$ is:*

- *the abelianisation map for $n = 1$;*
- *an isomorphism for $n \geq 2$.*

**Proof sketch.** Approximate $X$ by a CW complex with a single 0-cell, no cells in degrees $1, \ldots, n-1$ (this requires the connectivity hypothesis), and with cells in degree $n$ generating the relations on $\pi_n$. The cellular chain complex of $X$ in degrees $0$ through $n+1$ is then visibly the same as the abelianisation of the $\pi_n$ relations data, giving the isomorphism in degree $n$. The general case reduces to this CW-complex form via a CW-approximation. [ref: TODO_REF Hatcher §4.2; Bott-Tu §17] $\square$

**Theorem (rational Hurewicz).** *Let $X$ be simply-connected with $\pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = 0$ for $1 \leq i < n$ (for some $n \geq 2$). Then:*

1. *The rational Hurewicz map $\pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \to H_n(X; \mathbb{Q})$ is an isomorphism.*
2. *More generally, $\pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \to H_i(X; \mathbb{Q})$ is an isomorphism for $1 \leq i \leq 2n - 2$ and surjective for $i = 2n - 1$.*

**Proof sketch.** The integer Hurewicz theorem gives the result mod torsion; rationally, the obstructions vanish in the higher range by a spectral-sequence argument on the Whitehead tower. The Sullivan minimal model [03.12.06] provides an alternative direct route: the indecomposable space $V^n$ of the minimal model is dual to $\pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, and $V^n$ injects into the cohomology by construction. $\square$

**Theorem (Serre 1953, finiteness of homotopy groups of spheres).** *For $k > n \geq 2$, the homotopy group $\pi_k(S^n)$ is finite, except in the case $n = 2j$ even and $k = 4j - 1$, where $\pi_k(S^n)$ has rank one — i.e., $\pi_{4j - 1}(S^{2j}) = \mathbb{Z} \oplus \text{(finite)}$.*

**Proof sketch.** Apply rational Hurewicz to the Whitehead tower. For $n$ odd, the rational cohomology of $S^n$ is concentrated in degrees $0$ and $n$, and the Whitehead tower modifies this only through cancellations that preserve rational rank, yielding $\pi_k(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = 0$ for $k \neq n$. For $n$ even, the Whitehead-tower stage $S^n \langle n \rangle$ retains a rational class from the Whitehead bracket $[\iota_n, \iota_n] \in \pi_{2n - 1}(S^n)$, giving $\pi_{2n - 1}(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$. The integer-coefficient finiteness follows by Serre's prime-by-prime argument on the spectral sequence, using that the kernel of $\pi_k \to \pi_k \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ is finite for $k > n$. [ref: TODO_REF Serre 1953; Bott-Tu §18] $\square$

The argument is the canonical illustration of the Whitehead-tower-plus-spectral-sequence machinery. The case $n = 2$, $k = 3$ recovers Hopf's classical $\pi_3(S^2) = \mathbb{Z}$ from the Hopf fibration; the case $n = 3$, $k = 4$ gives $\pi_4(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}/2$.

**Synthesis.** The Whitehead tower is exactly the dual of the Postnikov tower: truncation from below versus truncation from above. This is the foundational reason rational Hurewicz holds, because every fibre is a rational Eilenberg-MacLane space. Serre 1953's finiteness theorem $\pi_k(S^n)$ finite for $k > n$ is exactly an instance of this machine.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward later units of the strand, where the same pattern is taken up at higher structure. The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).** *Hurewicz at the bottom of the universal cover.*

Let $X$ be a connected space with universal cover $\tilde{X} = X\langle 1 \rangle$. State what Hurewicz's theorem gives in degree $2$ for $\tilde{X}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The universal cover is simply-connected, so Hurewicz applies in degree $2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $\tilde{X}$ is simply-connected, $\pi_2(\tilde{X}) \xrightarrow{\sim} H_2(\tilde{X}; \mathbb{Z})$ is an isomorphism (Hurewicz with $n = 2$). By the long exact sequence of the universal-cover fibration $\pi_1(X) \to \tilde{X} \to X$, $\pi_2(X) = \pi_2(\tilde{X})$, so $\pi_2(X) = H_2(\tilde{X}; \mathbb{Z})$. This is the standard tool for computing $\pi_2$ of any space with a nonzero fundamental group.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).** *Fibre of the second Whitehead-tower stage.*

Compute the fibre of the projection $S^3\langle 3 \rangle \to S^3$ in the Whitehead tower of $S^3$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The fibre kills the lowest non-vanishing homotopy group, $\pi_3(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}$.

The fibre is $K(\pi_3(S^3), 2) = K(\mathbb{Z}, 2) = \mathbb{C}P^\infty$. The fibration is $\mathbb{C}P^\infty \to S^3\langle 3 \rangle \to S^3$ with $S^3\langle 3 \rangle$ three-connected. The fundamental class $\sigma \in H^3(S^3)$ classifies the fibration via the map $S^3 \to K(\mathbb{Z}, 3)$ representing $\sigma$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).** *Computation of $\pi_4(S^3)$.*

Use the Whitehead tower fibration $\mathbb{C}P^\infty \to S^3\langle 3 \rangle \to S^3$ and the Leray-Serre spectral sequence to compute $\pi_4(S^3)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

By Hurewicz applied to $S^3\langle 3 \rangle$, $\pi_4(S^3) = \pi_4(S^3\langle 3 \rangle) = H_4(S^3\langle 3 \rangle; \mathbb{Z})$. Compute the right-hand side via the Serre spectral sequence.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$E_2^{p, q} = H^p(S^3; H^q(\mathbb{C}P^\infty))$ with $H^*(\mathbb{C}P^\infty) = \mathbb{Z}[x]$ ($\deg x = 2$) and $H^*(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}[\sigma]/(\sigma^2)$ ($\deg \sigma = 3$). The non-zero entries are at columns $0$ and $3$, rows $0, 2, 4, \ldots$.

The transgression $d_3 : H^q(\mathbb{C}P^\infty) \to H^{q+1}(S^3)$ acts on $x \in H^2(\mathbb{C}P^\infty)$ as $d_3(x) = \sigma \in H^3(S^3)$ (the fundamental class), since this fibration is classified by $\sigma$.

By multiplicativity, $d_3(x^k) = k x^{k-1} \sigma$ in $E_3^{3, 2k-2}$. Working through the differentials with $\mathbb{Z}$ coefficients: $d_3(x) = \sigma$ kills $x$ and $\sigma$; $d_3(x^2) = 2 x \sigma$ has image $2 \cdot \mathbb{Z}$ in $E_3^{3, 2}$, so the kernel of $d_3$ on $x^2$ is zero and the cokernel on $x \sigma$ is $\mathbb{Z}/2$, surviving to $E_4^{3, 2} = \mathbb{Z}/2$.

By the convergence theorem, $H^5(S^3\langle 3 \rangle; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}/2$. By universal coefficients, $H_4(S^3\langle 3 \rangle; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ (no Ext correction since cohomology has just torsion of $\mathbb{Z}/2$). By the rational Hurewicz applied to the three-connected space $S^3\langle 3 \rangle$, $\pi_4(S^3\langle 3 \rangle) = H_4(S^3\langle 3 \rangle; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}/2$. Combined with $\pi_4(S^3) = \pi_4(S^3\langle 3 \rangle)$ from the Whitehead-tower long exact sequence:
$$
\pi_4(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}/2. \quad \square
$$
This is the canonical first computation of homotopy groups of spheres beyond the Hurewicz diagonal.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).** *Hopf invariant of the Hopf map.*

Compute the Hopf invariant of the Hopf map $\eta : S^3 \to S^2$ defined by $\eta(z, w) = [z : w]$ on $S^3 \subset \mathbb{C}^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Form the cofibre $C_\eta = S^2 \cup_\eta D^4$. By the Hopf-fibration construction, $C_\eta = \mathbb{C}P^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The cofibre is $C_\eta = S^2 \cup_\eta D^4 = \mathbb{C}P^2$ (the standard cell decomposition of complex projective space attaches a $4$-cell to $\mathbb{C}P^1 = S^2$ via the Hopf map). The cohomology is $H^*(\mathbb{C}P^2; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}[x]/(x^3)$, $\deg x = 2$. Identifying $u = x \in H^2(C_\eta)$ and $v = x^2 \in H^4(C_\eta)$, the cup product is $u \smile u = x \smile x = x^2 = v$.

Hence $H(\eta) = 1$. The Hopf map has Hopf invariant one. By Adams 1960, the only spheres on which a Hopf-invariant-one map exists are $S^1, S^3, S^7, S^{15}$, corresponding to the real, complex, quaternion, and octonion division algebras. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).** *Whitehead tower as a tower of principal $K(\pi, n - 1)$-fibrations.*

Show that each stage $X\langle n \rangle \to X\langle n - 1 \rangle$ of the Whitehead tower is a principal $K(\pi_n(X), n - 1)$-fibration, equivalently the homotopy fibre of a map $X\langle n - 1 \rangle \to K(\pi_n(X), n)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The classifying map of the fibration is the lift of the fundamental class of $X\langle n - 1 \rangle$ in degree $n$ to $K(\pi_n(X), n)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $X\langle n - 1 \rangle$ is $(n-1)$-connected with $\pi_n(X\langle n - 1 \rangle) = \pi_n(X)$, the Hurewicz theorem gives $H_n(X\langle n - 1 \rangle; \mathbb{Z}) = \pi_n(X)$. By Brown representability, $H^n(X\langle n - 1 \rangle; \pi_n(X)) = [X\langle n - 1 \rangle, K(\pi_n(X), n)]$. The identity in $\mathrm{Hom}(\pi_n, \pi_n)$ corresponds to a class $u \in H^n(X\langle n - 1 \rangle; \pi_n(X))$, hence to a map $u : X\langle n - 1 \rangle \to K(\pi_n(X), n)$.

The homotopy fibre of $u$ has homotopy groups: $\pi_i = \pi_i(X\langle n - 1 \rangle)$ for $i \neq n$, and $\pi_n = \ker(u_* : \pi_n(X\langle n - 1 \rangle) \to \pi_n(K(\pi_n(X), n))) = 0$ (since $u_*$ is the identity on $\pi_n(X)$). Hence the homotopy fibre is $n$-connected with the correct higher homotopy, and equals $X\langle n \rangle$ up to homotopy.

The fibre of the path-loop fibration $K(\pi_n(X), n - 1) \to PK(\pi_n(X), n) \to K(\pi_n(X), n)$ is exactly $\Omega K(\pi_n(X), n) = K(\pi_n(X), n - 1)$. Pulling back the path-loop fibration along $u$ gives the desired principal $K(\pi_n(X), n - 1)$-fibration on $X\langle n \rangle \to X\langle n - 1 \rangle$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

Apply the Whitehead tower one more level beyond Exercise 3 to compute $\pi_5(S^3)$.

The next Whitehead-tower stage kills $\pi_4(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}/2$. The fibre is $K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 3)$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The next stage of the Whitehead tower is $S^3\langle 4 \rangle \to S^3\langle 3 \rangle$ with fibre $K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 3)$. The Leray-Serre spectral sequence has $E_2^{p, q} = H^p(S^3\langle 3 \rangle; H^q(K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 3); \mathbb{Z}))$. We need $H^5(K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 3); \mathbb{Z})$ and the transgression structure.

Tracking the transgression and applying Hurewicz to $S^3\langle 4 \rangle$, the resulting computation gives $\pi_5(S^3) = H_5(S^3\langle 4 \rangle; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}/2$. (The detailed computation uses Cartan's table for $H^*(K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 3))$ and is most efficiently done in §18 of Bott-Tu.) [ref: TODO_REF Bott-Tu §18; Hatcher §4.3] $\square$

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).** *Serre's finiteness for $\pi_k(S^n)$, $k > n$.*

Sketch the proof of Serre's 1953 theorem that $\pi_k(S^n)$ is finite for $k > n$, except for $\pi_{4j - 1}(S^{2j})$ which has a free $\mathbb{Z}$-summand. Use the Whitehead tower and rational Hurewicz.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Whitehead-tower spectral sequence has rational $E_2$-page that collapses or is nearly so; the integer-coefficient version is finite-rank by tensor-product analysis.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Step 1: rationally.** Apply rational Hurewicz to the Whitehead tower of $S^n$. For $n$ odd ($n = 2j + 1$): the minimal model of $S^{2j+1}$ is $\Lambda(x)$ with $\deg x = 2j + 1$, so $\pi_*(S^{2j+1}) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ is concentrated in degree $2j + 1$ with value $\mathbb{Q}$, zero elsewhere [03.12.06].

For $n$ even ($n = 2j$): the minimal model of $S^{2j}$ is $\Lambda(x, y)$ with $\deg x = 2j$, $\deg y = 4j - 1$, $dy = x^2$. Hence $\pi_{2j}(S^{2j}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (from $x$) and $\pi_{4j - 1}(S^{2j}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (from $y$, the rational Whitehead-bracket class). All other rational homotopy of $S^{2j}$ vanishes.

**Step 2: integer finiteness.** For $k > n$ and not in the rational-non-zero case above, we have $\pi_k(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = 0$, so $\pi_k(S^n)$ has rank zero. Serre proved that homotopy groups of finite-dimensional simply-connected CW complexes are finitely generated (1951 thesis); combined with rank zero, this gives $\pi_k(S^n)$ finite.

**Step 3: the exception.** For $n = 2j$ and $k = 4j - 1$, $\pi_k(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ has rank one, so $\pi_k(S^n) = \mathbb{Z} \oplus T$ where $T$ is finite torsion. Equivalently, $\pi_{4j-1}(S^{2j})$ has a free summand of rank one, generated by the Whitehead product $[\iota_{2j}, \iota_{2j}]$ — and on $S^2, S^4, S^8$ specifically by the Hopf invariant generator (Hopf maps $\eta : S^3 \to S^2$, $\nu : S^7 \to S^4$, $\sigma : S^{15} \to S^8$). $\square$

The argument illustrates how Sullivan's minimal-model machinery and Whitehead-tower spectral sequences combine: the rational answer is read off the minimal model, and the Hurewicz theorem promotes this to an integer-finiteness statement.

</details>

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Whitehead's theorem on weak equivalences.*

Prove the **Whitehead theorem**: a map $f : X \to Y$ between simply-connected CW complexes inducing isomorphisms on all homotopy groups is a homotopy equivalence.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the Whitehead tower to build a homotopy inverse cell by cell: first kill homotopy in degree $n$ on both sides, then proceed.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the Hurewicz theorem applied to the mapping cone $C_f = Y \cup_f \mathrm{Cone}(X)$: since $f$ induces isomorphisms on all $\pi_n$, the long exact sequence shows $\pi_n(C_f) = 0$ for all $n$. Hence $C_f$ is weakly contractible, and by Whitehead's theorem applied to the inclusion $Y \hookrightarrow C_f$ (which is a homotopy equivalence iff $C_f$ is contractible relative to $Y$), the mapping cone admits a deformation retraction onto $Y$.

This deformation retraction provides a homotopy inverse: pulling the deformation back along $X \to C_f$ gives a map $g : Y \to X$ with $g f \simeq \mathrm{id}_X$ and $f g \simeq \mathrm{id}_Y$.

Alternatively, build $g$ inductively over the Whitehead tower: at stage $n$, the lifting problem $X^{(n)} \to Y^{(n)}$ has obstruction in $\pi_n$, which vanishes by hypothesis, so the lift exists. Iterating gives the homotopy inverse. [ref: TODO_REF Hatcher §4.1; Bott-Tu §17] $\square$

This theorem is the foundational reason why CW-approximation works: weakly equivalent simply-connected CW complexes are homotopy equivalent. The simply-connected hypothesis is essential; without it, equivalence on homotopy groups is not enough (the lens spaces $L(p, q)$ provide counterexamples).

## Advanced results [Master]

**Postnikov tower as the dual.** The Postnikov tower $X \to \cdots \to X_2 \to X_1 \to X_0$ truncates a space from above by killing high-degree homotopy. Each stage $X_n \to X_{n-1}$ is a principal fibration with fibre $K(\pi_n(X), n)$, classified by a $k$-invariant $k_n \in H^{n+1}(X_{n-1}; \pi_n(X))$. The Whitehead tower truncates from below by the dual construction. Together, the two towers bracket the homotopy structure: at any chosen pair $(m, n)$ with $m \leq n$, the homotopy of $X$ in the strip $m \leq i \leq n$ is recovered by the truncated tower segment. The dual relationship is captured by the proposed connection `conn:449.whitehead-postnikov-dual`.

**Adams's solution to the Hopf-invariant-one problem.** Adams 1960 *On the non-existence of elements of Hopf invariant one* (Annals of Math. 72, 20–104) proved that maps $f : S^{2n-1} \to S^n$ with $H(f) = \pm 1$ exist only for $n \in \{1, 2, 4, 8\}$, recovering the four real division algebras (real, complex, quaternion, octonion). The proof uses secondary cohomology operations on the cofibre $C_f$ to detect the obstruction; specifically, the Steenrod squares $\mathrm{Sq}^{2^i}$ on $H^*(C_f; \mathbb{F}_2)$ acting via the Adem relations. Adams's theorem closes the question of higher Hopf invariants left open by Hurewicz; the integer Hopf invariant is constrained to even values for $n \notin \{1, 2, 4, 8\}$.

**Stable homotopy and the EHP sequence.** Whitehead's 1953 *Annals* paper *On the homology suspension* introduced the **EHP sequence** — a long exact sequence relating $\pi_*(S^n)$, $\pi_*(S^{n+1})$, and $\pi_*(S^{2n+1})$ via James's reduction $\Sigma S^n \to \Omega S^{n+1}$. The EHP sequence is the principal computational tool for the stable range of homotopy groups of spheres and connects naturally to the Whitehead tower at each level.

**Serre's $\mathcal{C}$-theory.** Serre's 1953 paper introduced the framework of **classes of abelian groups** $\mathcal{C}$ (e.g., the class of finite abelian groups, the class of $p$-torsion abelian groups). Computations modulo $\mathcal{C}$ identify two homomorphisms whose kernel and cokernel both belong to $\mathcal{C}$. Serre's finiteness theorem is proved by showing that the rational Hurewicz isomorphism extends modulo the class of finite abelian groups to the integer Hurewicz, with all spectral-sequence differentials respected. The $\mathcal{C}$-theory framework remains the standard language for "homotopy modulo torsion" arguments.

**Connection to Sullivan's rational homotopy.** The Whitehead tower of a simply-connected space $X$ has a *rational* analogue: the Whitehead tower of the rationalisation $X_\mathbb{Q}$ has fibres $K(\pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}, n - 1)$, computable directly from the Sullivan minimal model [03.12.06]. Halperin's algorithm produces minimal models compatible with the Whitehead-tower fibre structure, and the rational Hurewicz theorem becomes transparent at the minimal-model level: indecomposables of degree $n$ correspond to $\pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ classes that survive in homology.

**Higher homotopy groups of spheres — modern state.** Beyond Hurewicz's diagonal $\pi_n(S^n) = \mathbb{Z}$, Hopf's $\pi_3(S^2) = \mathbb{Z}$, and Serre's finiteness, the table of $\pi_k(S^n)$ remains an active computational subject. Toda's 1962 monograph *Composition Methods in Homotopy Groups of Spheres* tabulates groups up to degree $30$. Adams 1962 introduced the Adams spectral sequence as an alternative to the Whitehead-tower route, more efficient for large degrees. Mahowald's 1980s computations and the Hopkins-Mahowald-Hill 2009 *Kervaire invariant* result resolved one of the last open structural questions in low-dimensional homotopy.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Construction of the Whitehead tower — full account.** Let $X$ be a pointed connected CW complex. We build the Whitehead tower $\cdots \to X\langle 2 \rangle \to X\langle 1 \rangle \to X$ inductively.

*Stage 0:* $X\langle 0 \rangle = X$.

*Stage $n$, given $X\langle n-1 \rangle$:* The space $X\langle n-1 \rangle$ is $(n-1)$-connected with $\pi_n(X\langle n-1 \rangle) \cong \pi_n(X)$ (by induction). By the Hurewicz theorem, $H_n(X\langle n-1 \rangle; \mathbb{Z}) = \pi_n(X)$, and Brown representability identifies a canonical map
$$
u_n : X\langle n - 1 \rangle \to K(\pi_n(X), n)
$$
representing the identity class in $H^n(X\langle n-1 \rangle; \pi_n(X)) = \mathrm{Hom}(\pi_n(X), \pi_n(X))$. Define $X\langle n \rangle$ as the homotopy fibre of $u_n$. The fibre is $\Omega K(\pi_n(X), n) = K(\pi_n(X), n - 1)$, and the long exact sequence of the fibration
$$
K(\pi_n(X), n - 1) \to X\langle n \rangle \to X\langle n - 1 \rangle \xrightarrow{u_n} K(\pi_n(X), n)
$$
gives:

- $\pi_i(X\langle n \rangle) = \pi_i(X\langle n-1 \rangle)$ for $i \neq n - 1, n$;
- $\pi_n(X\langle n \rangle) = \ker(u_{n,*}) = 0$ (since $u_{n,*}$ is the identity on $\pi_n(X)$);
- $\pi_{n-1}(X\langle n \rangle) = 0$ as well (since $\pi_{n-1}(X\langle n-1 \rangle) = 0$ by induction).

Hence $X\langle n \rangle$ is $n$-connected with $\pi_i(X\langle n \rangle) = \pi_i(X)$ for $i > n$, completing the induction.

**Theorem (universal property).** *The map $X\langle n \rangle \to X$ is universal among $n$-connected covers: for any $n$-connected pointed space $Y$ with a map $Y \to X$, the map factors uniquely up to homotopy through $X\langle n \rangle$.*

**Proof.** Lifting in the principal fibration $X\langle n \rangle \to X\langle n - 1 \rangle$ proceeds via the obstruction class in $H^*(Y; \pi_n(X))$, which vanishes since $Y$ is $n$-connected. Iterating from stage $0$ up to stage $n$ produces the unique factorisation. [ref: TODO_REF Hatcher §4.3] $\square$

**Theorem (Hurewicz, full proof).** *Let $X$ be a path-connected space with $\pi_i(X) = 0$ for $1 \leq i < n$ where $n \geq 2$. Then the Hurewicz map $h_n : \pi_n(X) \to H_n(X; \mathbb{Z})$ is an isomorphism.*

**Proof.** By CW approximation, replace $X$ by a CW complex with one $0$-cell, no cells in degrees $1, \ldots, n - 1$, and cells in degrees $n$ and higher. The cellular chain complex in degrees $\leq n + 1$ reads:
$$
\mathbb{Z}^{(\text{$n+1$-cells})} \xrightarrow{\partial} \mathbb{Z}^{(\text{$n$-cells})} \xrightarrow{0} \mathbb{Z}.
$$
Hence $H_n(X; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}^{(\text{$n$-cells})} / \mathrm{image}(\partial)$.

The homotopy group $\pi_n(X)$ is computed from the same data: each $n$-cell contributes a generator (its attaching map represents an element of $\pi_n$ of the $n$-skeleton, which is a wedge of $n$-spheres), and each $(n+1)$-cell contributes a relation (its attaching map represents a relation in $\pi_n$ of the $n$-skeleton). For $n \geq 2$, $\pi_n(\bigvee S^n) = \bigoplus \mathbb{Z}$ (no abelianisation issue), so $\pi_n(X)$ matches the cellular cokernel exactly: $\pi_n(X) = \mathbb{Z}^{(\text{$n$-cells})} / \mathrm{image}(\partial)$.

Hence $\pi_n(X) = H_n(X; \mathbb{Z})$, and the Hurewicz map is the identity on these representatives. The argument extends to give the natural Hurewicz map for any space. [ref: TODO_REF Hatcher §4.2] $\square$

**Theorem (rational Hurewicz, full proof).** *Let $X$ be simply-connected with $\pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = 0$ for $1 \leq i < n$, $n \geq 2$. Then $h_i \otimes \mathbb{Q} : \pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \to H_i(X; \mathbb{Q})$ is an isomorphism for $1 \leq i \leq 2n - 2$ and surjective for $i = 2n - 1$.*

**Proof sketch.** The Sullivan minimal model $(\Lambda V_X, d)$ has $V_X^i = 0$ for $1 \leq i < n$ by hypothesis (the indecomposables in degree $i$ are dual to $\pi_i \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, which vanishes). The cohomology of $\Lambda V_X$ in degrees $i \leq 2n - 2$ is generated linearly by single generators in $V_X^i$ (no products of $V_X^{\geq n}$ elements yet appear in cohomological degree $\leq 2n - 2$). Hence $H^i(\Lambda V_X) = V_X^i = (\pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q})^*$ for $i \leq 2n - 2$, and dualising gives $H_i(X; \mathbb{Q}) = \pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$.

In degree $2n - 1$, the cohomology $H^{2n-1}(\Lambda V_X)$ contains $V_X^{2n-1}$ and possibly relations from products of two $V_X^{\geq n}$ elements; on dualisation, the homology surjects onto $\pi_{2n-1}(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, but may have a kernel coming from those products. [ref: TODO_REF Sullivan 1977 §3; Bott-Tu §17] $\square$

**Theorem ($\pi_4(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}/2$, full proof).** Apply the construction above. The Whitehead-tower stage $S^3 \langle 3 \rangle$ is the homotopy fibre of $u_3 : S^3 \to K(\mathbb{Z}, 3)$, classified by the fundamental class $\sigma \in H^3(S^3)$. The fibration is $K(\mathbb{Z}, 2) \to S^3 \langle 3 \rangle \to S^3$. By Hurewicz applied to $S^3 \langle 3 \rangle$ (three-connected), $\pi_4(S^3) = \pi_4(S^3 \langle 3 \rangle) = H_4(S^3 \langle 3 \rangle; \mathbb{Z})$.

The Leray-Serre spectral sequence has $E_2^{p, q} = H^p(S^3) \otimes H^q(\mathbb{C}P^\infty)$ since the base is simply-connected and the coefficients are constant. Concretely $H^*(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}[\sigma]/(\sigma^2)$ with $\deg \sigma = 3$, $H^*(\mathbb{C}P^\infty) = \mathbb{Z}[x]$ with $\deg x = 2$. The transgression $d_3 : E_3^{0, 2} \to E_3^{3, 0}$ sends $x$ to $\sigma$ (this is the classifying-map data: the Whitehead-tower fibre is classified by $\sigma$, so $d_3(x) = \sigma$).

By multiplicativity, $d_3(x^k) = k x^{k-1} \sigma$ in $E_3^{3, 2k - 2}$. With integer coefficients:

- $d_3(x^2) = 2 x \sigma \in E_3^{3, 2}$. The kernel of $d_3$ on $E_3^{0, 4}$ is $\{a x^2 : a \cdot 2 = 0\} = 0$.
- The image $d_3(E_3^{0, 4}) \to E_3^{3, 2}$ is the subgroup $2\mathbb{Z} \cdot x \sigma \subset \mathbb{Z} \cdot x \sigma$, so the cokernel is $\mathbb{Z}/2$.

Hence $E_4^{3, 2} = \mathbb{Z}/2$ in total degree $5$, while $E_4^{0, 4} = 0$. All higher differentials vanish for degree reasons, so $H^5(S^3 \langle 3 \rangle; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}/2$. Universal coefficients gives $H_4(S^3 \langle 3 \rangle; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ (the extension and Ext terms work out since $\mathbb{Z}/2$ is the only relevant torsion). Hence:
$$
\pi_4(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}/2. \quad \square
$$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

**Witold Hurewicz** introduced the higher homotopy groups in a four-paper series *Beiträge zur Topologie der Deformationen I-IV* in the *Proceedings of the Royal Academy of Sciences in Amsterdam* (vol. 38, 1935: 112–119, 521–528; vol. 39, 1936: 117–126, 215–224). The first paper defined $\pi_n(X)$ for $n \geq 2$ as homotopy classes of based maps $S^n \to X$ and proved that these groups are abelian for $n \geq 2$ (an observation that surprised Hurewicz himself; the corresponding fact for $n = 1$ — that $\pi_1$ is generally non-abelian — had set the expectation otherwise). The third and fourth papers established the **Hurewicz theorem**, identifying the lowest non-vanishing homotopy group with the corresponding homology group. Hurewicz was at Princeton, working in the orbit of Lefschetz; the four papers laid the foundations of the modern subject of homotopy theory and remain cited verbatim in every textbook treatment.

**George W. Whitehead** (not to be confused with John Henry Constantine Whitehead, the British topologist whose name is often confused with his) developed the dual to the Postnikov tower in his 1953 *Annals of Mathematics* paper *On the homology suspension* (vol. 57, 386–394). Whitehead was at MIT, building on Postnikov's 1951 *Doklady* construction of the truncation tower. The Whitehead tower's clean formulation as iterated principal $K(\pi, n - 1)$-fibrations made it the operational tool for low-degree homotopy computations: when one wants to find $\pi_{n+1}(X)$ from $\pi_n(X)$, the Whitehead-tower stage $X\langle n \rangle$ realises the appropriate connectivity, and the Serre spectral sequence does the work.

The companion **Whitehead theorem** on weak equivalences — that a map between simply-connected CW complexes inducing isomorphisms on all $\pi_n$ is a homotopy equivalence — was proved by J.H.C. Whitehead in his 1949 paper *Combinatorial homotopy I* (Bull. Amer. Math. Soc. 55, 213–245). The proof uses an inductive lifting along the cellular structure, a technique that remains central to modern homotopy theory.

**Jean-Pierre Serre**'s 1953 paper *Groupes d'homotopie et classes de groupes abéliens* (*Annals of Mathematics* 58, 258–294) used the spectral sequence developed in his 1951 PhD thesis to prove the **finiteness of $\pi_k(S^n)$ for $k > n$**, except for $\pi_{4j-1}(S^{2j})$ which has a free rank-one summand from the Hopf invariant. The proof introduced the framework of **classes of abelian groups** $\mathcal{C}$ — a calculus modulo a class of "negligible" abelian groups — which became the standard language for "homotopy modulo torsion" computations. Serre's argument runs through the rational vanishing on the Whitehead tower (the rational Hurewicz argument of the present unit) combined with finite-generation of homotopy groups of finite-dimensional simply-connected CW complexes (an earlier result of his thesis). At 27, Serre was Cartan's student and a Bourbaki regular; the finiteness theorem cemented his international reputation and contributed to his 1954 Fields Medal — the youngest awardee in the history of the prize at that point.

The combination of the Whitehead tower, Hurewicz's theorem, and the Serre spectral sequence is the single most powerful machine in mid-twentieth-century algebraic topology. **Bott and Tu** §17 + §18 expose this machinery from the differential-form-flavoured side, with explicit computations of $\pi_4(S^3)$ and $\pi_5(S^3)$ via the Postnikov tower of $K(\mathbb{Z}, 3)$ — the chapter is a canonical entry point to the subject from a geometric direction. The further development — Adams's Hopf-invariant-one theorem, Toda's tables, the Adams spectral sequence — built outward from this foundation.
