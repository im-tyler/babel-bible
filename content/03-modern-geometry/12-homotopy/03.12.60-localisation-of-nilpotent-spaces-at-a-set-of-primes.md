## Intuition [Beginner]

Picture the integer $12$. If you only care about the prime $3$, the factors of $2$ are a nuisance. You would like to be allowed to divide by $2$ freely, so that $12$ and $3$ become the same up to a unit. Doing this for every prime except $3$ produces a new number system, the ring you get by inverting $2, 5, 7, 11, \ldots$ but keeping $3$. In that system $12 = 4 \times 3$ is just $3$ times an invertible factor, so all the information left is the power of $3$.

Localising a space at a set of primes $T$ does the same thing one dimension higher. You start with a space $X$ and build a new space $X_T$ in which every prime outside $T$ has been made invertible. Concretely, the homotopy groups change from ordinary abelian groups to groups where you are allowed to divide by every prime that is not in $T$. The part of $X$ that lived at the unwanted primes is washed out, and what survives is exactly the $T$-primary information.

The two extreme choices are worth naming. If $T$ is the single prime $\{p\}$, you keep only the $p$-part: this is $p$-localisation, written $X_{(p)}$. If $T$ is empty, you invert every prime, keeping nothing but the rational skeleton: this is rationalisation $X_\mathbb{Q}$. Every other $T$ sits between these.

## Visual [Beginner]

Imagine a dial with a slot for every prime: $2, 3, 5, 7, 11, \ldots$. Choosing a set $T$ is choosing which slots stay switched on. The localisation map $X \to X_T$ keeps the switched-on primes and silences the rest. Rationalisation switches every prime off; $p$-localisation leaves a single switch on.

![A schematic placeholder showing a row of prime-labelled switches, with the subset T highlighted, and an arrow from a space X to its localisation X_T that silences the primes outside T while keeping the primes inside T intact.](/img/localisation-of-nilpotent-spaces-at-a-set-of-primes-placeholder.svg)

The picture captures the central feature: localisation does not add anything new, it only forgets the primes outside $T$. The map $X \to X_T$ is always going in one direction, from the full space toward a simpler space that remembers fewer primes.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the simplest interesting target group and watch localisation act on it.

Step 1. Start with the abelian group $\mathbb{Z}/12$. Its prime content is one factor of $4$ at the prime $2$ and one factor of $3$ at the prime $3$, since $12 = 4 \times 3$. By the structure of finite groups, $\mathbb{Z}/12 = \mathbb{Z}/4 \times \mathbb{Z}/3$.

Step 2. Localise at $T = \{3\}$. The rule is: invert every prime except $3$. Inverting $2$ kills the factor $\mathbb{Z}/4$ entirely, because once $2$ is a unit, the equation $4x = 0$ forces $x = 0$. The factor $\mathbb{Z}/3$ is untouched, since $3$ is the prime we kept. So the localisation of $\mathbb{Z}/12$ at $\{3\}$ is $\mathbb{Z}/3$.

Step 3. Localise the same group at $T = \{2\}$ instead. Now $3$ is inverted, which deletes the $\mathbb{Z}/3$ factor, and the $\mathbb{Z}/4$ survives. The localisation is $\mathbb{Z}/4$.

Step 4. Localise at $T = \varnothing$ (rationalise). Every prime is inverted, so both finite factors vanish. The result is the zero group, matching the fact that a finite group has no rational content.

What this tells us: a space whose only homotopy is $\mathbb{Z}/12$ in some degree splits, after localisation, into a clean $3$-part and a clean $2$-part, with nothing left over rationally. Localising at $T$ is the space-level version of selecting the $T$-primary block of every homotopy group.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a set $T$ of primes. Let $\mathbb{Z}_T \subseteq \mathbb{Q}$ denote the subring of rationals whose denominators are products of primes outside $T$; equivalently $\mathbb{Z}_T = \mathbb{Z}[\,p^{-1} : p \notin T\,]$, the localisation of $\mathbb{Z}$ away from the multiplicative set generated by the primes not in $T$. An abelian group $A$ is $T$-local if it is a $\mathbb{Z}_T$-module, equivalently if multiplication by every prime $q \notin T$ is an isomorphism of $A$. The $T$-localisation of $A$ is $A_T := A \otimes_\mathbb{Z} \mathbb{Z}_T$ with the evident map $A \to A_T$.

**Definition (nilpotent space).** A connected based space $X$ is nilpotent if $\pi_1(X)$ is a nilpotent group and acts nilpotently on each higher homotopy group $\pi_n(X)$, $n \geq 2$; this is the standing class, recalled from [03.12.45], on which the constructions below are well behaved. Simply connected spaces are members of this class.

**Definition ($T$-local space).** A nilpotent space $Z$ is $T$-local if every homotopy group $\pi_n(Z)$ is a $\mathbb{Z}_T$-module (for $n = 1$, a $T$-local nilpotent group in the sense that each lower-central subquotient is a $\mathbb{Z}_T$-module). Equivalently, $Z$ is $T$-local exactly when its reduced integral homology groups $\widetilde{H}_n(Z; \mathbb{Z})$ are $\mathbb{Z}_T$-modules.

**Definition ($T$-equivalence).** A map $f : X \to Y$ of nilpotent spaces is a $T$-equivalence if it induces an isomorphism $\widetilde{H}_*(X; \mathbb{Z}_T) \to \widetilde{H}_*(Y; \mathbb{Z}_T)$ on homology with $\mathbb{Z}_T$-coefficients; for nilpotent spaces this is equivalent to inducing an isomorphism $\pi_*(X) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T \to \pi_*(Y) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$ after localising the homotopy groups.

**Definition ($T$-localisation of a space).** A $T$-localisation of a nilpotent space $X$ is a map $\eta : X \to X_T$ such that $X_T$ is $T$-local and $\eta$ is a $T$-equivalence. As a homological localisation $X_T = L_{H\mathbb{Z}_T} X$ this is the Bousfield localisation at the Moore-spectrum / Eilenberg-MacLane spectrum $H\mathbb{Z}_T$, in the framework of [03.12.48]; it exists, is functorial, and is determined up to homotopy under $X$ by the universal property below. The named endpoints are $T = \{p\}$, giving $p$-localisation $X_{(p)}$, and $T = \varnothing$, giving rationalisation $X_\mathbb{Q} = X_{(0)}$ of [03.12.06].

**Definition (universal property).** The map $\eta : X \to X_T$ is initial among maps from $X$ to $T$-local spaces: for every $T$-local nilpotent space $Z$, the induced map $\eta^* : [X_T, Z] \to [X, Z]$ on based homotopy classes is a bijection.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Localisation is not completion.* The $p$-localisation $X_{(p)}$ has homotopy groups $\pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$, where $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)} \subset \mathbb{Q}$ is the integers localised at $p$ (a subring of $\mathbb{Q}$). The $p$-completion $X^\wedge_p$ of [03.12.45] has homotopy groups $\pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_p$, the $p$-adic integers (a profinite ring containing $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$). They agree on torsion at $p$ but differ on the free part: $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$ is countable, $\mathbb{Z}_p$ is uncountable.
- *Non-nilpotent fundamental groups break the homotopy description.* When $\pi_1(X)$ is not nilpotent, $\pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$ need not compute the homotopy of any localisation; the homological localisation $L_{H\mathbb{Z}_T}$ still exists but loses the simple group-by-group description.
- *Inverting primes is not deleting torsion at those primes only when $T \neq \varnothing$.* Rationalisation ($T = \varnothing$) deletes all torsion; for nonempty $T$, the $T$-primary torsion is retained, and only the primes outside $T$ are cleared.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Characterisation of $T$-localisation; Sullivan 1970/1974, Hilton-Mislin-Roitberg 1975).** *Let $X$ be a nilpotent space and $\eta : X \to Y$ a map of nilpotent spaces. The following are equivalent.*

*(1) $\eta$ is a $T$-localisation: $Y$ is $T$-local and $\eta$ is a $T$-equivalence.*

*(2) For every $n \geq 1$, the induced map $\pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T \to \pi_n(Y)$ is an isomorphism, where the source carries the $\mathbb{Z}_T$-localised homotopy group and the target $\pi_n(Y)$ is already $T$-local.*

*(3) For every $n$, the induced map $H_n(X; \mathbb{Z}_T) \to H_n(Y; \mathbb{Z}_T)$ is an isomorphism and $\widetilde{H}_n(Y; \mathbb{Z})$ is a $\mathbb{Z}_T$-module.*

*When these hold, $\eta$ is the universal map from $X$ to a $T$-local space, and $Y$ is determined up to homotopy under $X$.*

*Proof.* We induct up a principal refinement of the Postnikov tower of $X$, available because $X$ is nilpotent: there is a tower of principal fibrations $X_n \to X_{n-1}$ with fibre an Eilenberg-MacLane space $K(A_n, m_n)$, classified by a $k$-invariant in $H^{m_n+1}(X_{n-1}; A_n)$, whose inverse limit is $X$ (this is the structural content recalled from [03.12.45]; the nilpotent action is exactly what allows the Postnikov stages to be refined into principal ones).

**Step 1.** *The case of an Eilenberg-MacLane space.* For $X = K(A, m)$ with $A$ abelian, localisation of the homotopy group gives the map $K(A, m) \to K(A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T, m)$ induced by $A \to A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$. The target is $T$-local since its only nonzero homotopy group $A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$ is a $\mathbb{Z}_T$-module. By the universal-coefficient computation, $H_*(K(A,m); \mathbb{Z}_T)$ depends on $A$ only through $A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$ together with the Tor-term $\mathrm{Tor}(A, \mathbb{Z}_T) = 0$ (because $\mathbb{Z}_T$ is flat over $\mathbb{Z}$), so the map is an isomorphism on $\mathbb{Z}_T$-homology, hence a $T$-equivalence. This establishes (1) $\Leftrightarrow$ (2) $\Leftrightarrow$ (3) for a single Eilenberg-MacLane stage.

**Step 2.** *Localising a principal fibration.* Suppose $X_{n-1} \to (X_{n-1})_T$ is a $T$-localisation and $X_n \to X_{n-1}$ is a principal $K(A_n, m_n)$-fibration with $k$-invariant $k \in H^{m_n+1}(X_{n-1}; A_n)$. The image of $k$ under $A_n \to A_n \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$ defines a $k$-invariant over $(X_{n-1})_T$, hence a principal $K(A_n \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T, m_n)$-fibration $(X_n)_T \to (X_{n-1})_T$ and a map $X_n \to (X_n)_T$ extending the localisation downstairs. Both the fibre map $K(A_n, m_n) \to K(A_n \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T, m_n)$ and the base map $X_{n-1} \to (X_{n-1})_T$ are $\mathbb{Z}_T$-homology isomorphisms by the inductive hypothesis and Step 1. The Zeeman comparison theorem for the Serre spectral sequence with $\mathbb{Z}_T$-coefficients then forces the total-space map $X_n \to (X_n)_T$ to be a $\mathbb{Z}_T$-homology isomorphism, so it is a $T$-equivalence onto a $T$-local space.

**Step 3.** *Passage to the limit.* The tower of localised stages assembles to a $T$-localisation $X \to X_T = \varprojlim (X_n)_T$. On homotopy groups, the inverse limit produces $\pi_n(X_T) = \pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$ with no $\lim^1$ obstruction, because at each finite stage the homotopy groups are already $\mathbb{Z}_T$-modules and the maps are surjective on them; this gives (2). The homological statement (3) follows from (2) for nilpotent spaces by the relative Hurewicz theorem applied stage by stage with $\mathbb{Z}_T$-coefficients.

**Step 4.** *Universality.* Given any $T$-local nilpotent space $Z$ and a map $g : X \to Z$, induct up the tower: at each stage $g$ extends uniquely (up to homotopy) over $(X_n)_T$ because the obstruction and indeterminacy groups $H^*(X_n; \pi_*(Z))$ are already $\mathbb{Z}_T$-modules, so precomposition with the $T$-equivalence $X_n \to (X_n)_T$ is a bijection on the relevant cohomology. Passing to the limit yields a unique factorisation $X \to X_T \to Z$, which is the universal property. $\square$

**Bridge.** This characterisation builds toward the localisation fracture theorem below and appears again in the completion story of [03.12.45], where the same Postnikov-refinement induction runs with $p$-adic coefficients instead of $\mathbb{Z}_T$-coefficients. The foundational reason the argument works is that $\mathbb{Z}_T$ is a flat subring of $\mathbb{Q}$, so localising homotopy groups is exact and the Tor-terms vanish; this is exactly the homotopical lift of the algebraic statement that $A \mapsto A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$ is an exact functor on abelian groups. The central insight is that nilpotence buys a principal Postnikov refinement, which reduces every space-level statement to a sequence of statements about $K(A, n)$'s, where localisation is visibly the algebraic localisation of the single coefficient group. Putting these together, the homotopy category of $T$-local nilpotent spaces is a reflective subcategory of all nilpotent spaces, with reflector $L_T = (-)_T$, and this reflection generalises the rationalisation reflector of [03.12.06] from $T = \varnothing$ to arbitrary $T$. This pattern recurs throughout the subject: every well-behaved localisation of spaces is a reflection onto a local subcategory, and the prime-set case is the arithmetic prototype.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the homotopy groups of the $p$-localisation $S^3_{(p)}$ of the $3$-sphere in low degrees, and contrast $\pi_3$ and $\pi_6$ at the prime $p = 2$ against $p = 5$. Use that $\pi_3(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}$ and $\pi_6(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}/12$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\pi_n(S^3_{(p)}) = \pi_n(S^3) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$. Localise each finite group at the chosen prime and the free group by tensoring with $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the characterisation theorem, $\pi_n(S^3_{(p)}) = \pi_n(S^3) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$.

In degree $3$: $\pi_3(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}$, so $\pi_3(S^3_{(p)}) = \mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$ for every prime $p$ (the free part survives at every prime).

In degree $6$: $\pi_6(S^3) = \mathbb{Z}/12 = \mathbb{Z}/4 \times \mathbb{Z}/3$. At $p = 2$, tensoring with $\mathbb{Z}_{(2)}$ inverts $3$ and kills the $\mathbb{Z}/3$, leaving $\pi_6(S^3_{(2)}) = \mathbb{Z}/4$. At $p = 5$, tensoring with $\mathbb{Z}_{(5)}$ inverts both $2$ and $3$, killing the entire group, so $\pi_6(S^3_{(5)}) = 0$.

This is the geometric content of "inverting primes outside $T$": at $p = 5$ the sphere $S^3$ has no $5$-primary torsion in this degree, so the localised homotopy group vanishes. Rubric: full credit for $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$ in degree $3$, $\mathbb{Z}/4$ at $p=2$ and $0$ at $p=5$ in degree $6$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

State the difference between $S^3_{(p)}$ ($p$-localisation) and $S^{3\wedge}_p$ ($p$-completion) at the level of $\pi_3$, and explain why they nevertheless induce the same map on mod-$p$ homology.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\pi_3 = \mathbb{Z}$. Localisation tensors with $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)} \subset \mathbb{Q}$; completion tensors with $\mathbb{Z}_p$. Compare both with $\mathbb{Z}/p$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On $\pi_3 = \mathbb{Z}$, localisation gives $\pi_3(S^3_{(p)}) = \mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$, the subring of $\mathbb{Q}$ with denominators prime to $p$ — a countable subring of $\mathbb{Q}$. Completion gives $\pi_3(S^{3\wedge}_p) = \mathbb{Z}_p$, the $p$-adic integers — an uncountable profinite ring. The natural map $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)} \to \mathbb{Z}_p$ is the completion, injective but far from surjective.

They induce the same map on mod-$p$ homology because $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)} \otimes \mathbb{Z}/p = \mathbb{Z}/p = \mathbb{Z}_p \otimes \mathbb{Z}/p$: both rings have the same residue field, so after reducing mod $p$ the difference between the subring of $\mathbb{Q}$ and the $p$-adic integers disappears. This is precisely why localisation and completion agree on torsion and mod-$p$ data but differ on the free part. Rubric: full credit for the $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$ versus $\mathbb{Z}_p$ contrast plus the shared residue field $\mathbb{Z}/p$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Prove that the $T$-localisation map $\eta : X \to X_T$ is itself a $T$-local space's identity in the sense that $(X_T)_T \simeq X_T$, i.e. that $L_T$ is idempotent.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use that $X_T$ is already $T$-local and apply the universal property to the identity map $X_T \to X_T$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$X_T$ is $T$-local by construction. Applying the universal property of $T$-localisation to $X_T$, the map $\eta_{X_T} : X_T \to (X_T)_T$ is initial among maps from $X_T$ to $T$-local spaces. But the identity $\mathrm{id} : X_T \to X_T$ is a map to a $T$-local space, so it factors uniquely as $X_T \xrightarrow{\eta_{X_T}} (X_T)_T \xrightarrow{r} X_T$ with $r \circ \eta_{X_T} = \mathrm{id}$.

Conversely $\eta_{X_T}$ is a $T$-equivalence between $T$-local spaces; a $T$-equivalence of $T$-local spaces is a homotopy equivalence, because on each homotopy group it is an isomorphism of $\mathbb{Z}_T$-modules (the localisation $(-) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$ acts as the identity on a $\mathbb{Z}_T$-module). Hence $\eta_{X_T}$ is an equivalence and $(X_T)_T \simeq X_T$. This is the idempotence of the reflector $L_T$. Rubric: full credit for the universal-property factorisation plus the observation that a $T$-equivalence of $T$-local spaces is an equivalence.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $T = \{2, 3\}$. Compute the localisation of the abelian group $A = \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}/5 \oplus \mathbb{Z}/9$ at $T$, and identify which summands survive.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Tensor with $\mathbb{Z}_T$, the integers with $5, 7, 11, \ldots$ inverted but $2$ and $3$ kept. A finite cyclic $\mathbb{Z}/q^k$ survives iff $q \in T$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$ with $T = \{2,3\}$ inverts every prime except $2$ and $3$. The free summand becomes $\mathbb{Z} \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T = \mathbb{Z}_T$. The summand $\mathbb{Z}/5$ is killed, since $5 \notin T$ is inverted and $5 x = 0$ then forces $x = 0$. The summand $\mathbb{Z}/9 = \mathbb{Z}/3^2$ survives unchanged, since $3 \in T$.

So $A_T = \mathbb{Z}_T \oplus \mathbb{Z}/9$. The free part is retained at every prime in $T$; the $5$-torsion is cleared because $5$ is outside $T$; the $3$-primary torsion is kept because $3$ is inside $T$. Rubric: full credit for $\mathbb{Z}_T \oplus \mathbb{Z}/9$ with the deletion of $\mathbb{Z}/5$ explained.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

State and prove the localisation fracture (pullback) theorem in the two-set form: for disjoint sets of primes $T_1, T_2$ with $T = T_1 \cup T_2$, the space $X_T$ is the homotopy pullback of $X_{T_1}$ and $X_{T_2}$ over $X_{T_1 \cap T_2} = X_{T_1} {}_{\mathbb{Q}\text{-glued}}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reduce to homotopy groups via the principal Postnikov refinement, and use the algebraic pullback square for $A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$ in terms of $A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_{T_1}$, $A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_{T_2}$, and $A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_{T_1 \cap T_2}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Statement.* For disjoint $T_1, T_2$ and $T = T_1 \cup T_2$, the commutative square
$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
X_T & \to & X_{T_1} \\
\downarrow & & \downarrow \\
X_{T_2} & \to & X_{T_1 \cap T_2}
\end{array}
$$
(where the right and bottom maps further localise away the extra primes, and $T_1 \cap T_2 = \varnothing$ gives the rational corner $X_\mathbb{Q}$) is a homotopy pullback of nilpotent spaces.

*Proof.* By the principal Postnikov refinement it suffices to prove the statement on a single Eilenberg-MacLane stage $K(A, n)$, i.e. to prove that for a finitely generated abelian group $A$ the square of localised groups
$$
A_T \to A_{T_1}, \qquad A_{T_2} \to A_{T_1 \cap T_2}
$$
is a pullback of abelian groups. Concretely $A_T = A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$, and the claim is
$$
A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T \;\cong\; (A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_{T_1}) \times_{A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_{T_1 \cap T_2}} (A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_{T_2}).
$$
This is the tensor product of $A$ with the ring-level pullback $\mathbb{Z}_T = \mathbb{Z}_{T_1} \times_{\mathbb{Z}_{T_1 \cap T_2}} \mathbb{Z}_{T_2}$, which holds because a fraction with denominator a product of primes outside $T$ is precisely one that lies in $\mathbb{Z}_{T_1}$ and in $\mathbb{Z}_{T_2}$ simultaneously, with the two views agreeing in the common further-localisation $\mathbb{Z}_{T_1 \cap T_2}$. Flatness of each $\mathbb{Z}_{T_i}$ over $\mathbb{Z}$ preserves the pullback under $A \otimes (-)$, and since $\mathbb{Z}_{T_1 \cap T_2}$ is flat the higher derived terms vanish, so no $\lim^1$ obstruction enters. The homotopy-group pullback then lifts to a homotopy-pullback of spaces by the Postnikov induction, exactly as in the Zeeman comparison argument of the main proof. Rubric: full credit for the ring-level pullback $\mathbb{Z}_T = \mathbb{Z}_{T_1} \times_{\mathbb{Z}_{T_1 \cap T_2}} \mathbb{Z}_{T_2}$, the flatness remark, and the lift to spaces.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Explain how the localisation fracture square of Exercise 7, taken to the extreme one-prime-at-a-time decomposition, relates to the arithmetic-square completion fracture of [03.12.45]. Identify precisely where the two diagrams differ.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Both reassemble $X$ from $p$-by-$p$ pieces and a rational corner. One uses $X_{(p)}$ (localisation, $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)} \subset \mathbb{Q}$); the other uses $X^\wedge_p$ (completion, $\mathbb{Z}_p$). Compare the gluing corner.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Iterating the localisation fracture (Exercise 7) down to single primes assembles $X = X_{(\text{all primes})}$ as a homotopy limit of its $p$-localisations $X_{(p)}$ over the rationalisation $X_\mathbb{Q}$: the homotopy groups satisfy $\pi_n(X) = \bigcap_p \pi_n(X_{(p)})$ inside $\pi_n(X_\mathbb{Q}) = \pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, the algebraic statement that $\mathbb{Z} = \bigcap_p \mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$ inside $\mathbb{Q}$.

The arithmetic-square completion fracture of [03.12.45] reassembles $X$ instead from its $p$-completions $X^\wedge_p$ and rationalisation, with gluing corner the rationalised product $X_\mathbb{Q}(\prod_p X^\wedge_p)$, governed by the adelic short exact sequence $0 \to \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Q} \oplus \hat{\mathbb{Z}} \to \mathbb{A}_f \to 0$.

The precise difference: localisation fracture uses $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)} \subset \mathbb{Q}$ and glues over the rationals directly, with $\bigcap_p \mathbb{Z}_{(p)} = \mathbb{Z}$ and no finite-adele correction; completion fracture uses the $p$-adic $\mathbb{Z}_p$ and must glue over the finite adeles $\mathbb{A}_f = \hat{\mathbb{Z}} \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, which carries a genuine $\lim^1$ bookkeeping requirement and a finite-type hypothesis. The localisation square needs no profinite gluing corner; the completion square does. Rubric: full credit for the $\bigcap_p \mathbb{Z}_{(p)} = \mathbb{Z}$ localisation statement, the adelic completion statement, and the identification of the gluing corner as the precise point of difference.

## Advanced results [Master]

### The localisation functor and its homological description

**Theorem (Bousfield 1975; existence of $T$-localisation).** *For every set of primes $T$, the assignment $X \mapsto X_T = L_{H\mathbb{Z}_T} X$ is an idempotent functor $\mathrm{Ho}(\mathbf{Top}_*)_{\mathrm{nil}} \to \mathrm{Ho}(\mathbf{Top}_*)_{\mathrm{nil}}$ on the homotopy category of nilpotent spaces, with natural transformation $\eta : \mathrm{id} \to L_T$ exhibiting $X_T$ as the reflection of $X$ into the full subcategory of $T$-local spaces.*

The functor is the Bousfield localisation at the homology theory $H\mathbb{Z}_T$, constructed by the small-object argument inside a localised model structure on $\mathbf{sSet}_*$ whose weak equivalences are the $\mathbb{Z}_T$-homology isomorphisms, in the sense of [03.12.48]. On the nilpotent class the abstract localisation coincides with the explicit Postnikov-induction construction of the Key theorem, and the homotopy groups are computed by $\pi_n(X_T) = \pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$ with the $\pi_1$-action localised correspondingly. The flatness of $\mathbb{Z}_T$ over $\mathbb{Z}$ is what makes the homology theory $H\mathbb{Z}_T$ behave like a localisation rather than a completion: $\mathbb{Z}_T$-homology is ordinary homology with coefficients in a subring of $\mathbb{Q}$, so all Tor-terms vanish and the localisation map is a homology isomorphism in $\mathbb{Z}_T$-coefficients by construction.

### Localisation of nilpotent groups

**Theorem (Hilton-Mislin-Roitberg 1975; localisation of nilpotent groups).** *Let $G$ be a nilpotent group with lower central series $G = \Gamma_1 \supseteq \Gamma_2 \supseteq \cdots \supseteq \Gamma_{c+1} = 1$. There is a $T$-localisation $G \to G_T$, a homomorphism into a $T$-local nilpotent group, universal among maps from $G$ to $T$-local nilpotent groups, and it is computed by localising each subquotient: $\Gamma_i(G_T)/\Gamma_{i+1}(G_T) = (\Gamma_i(G)/\Gamma_{i+1}(G)) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$. The localisation $G \to G_T$ is a $T$-isomorphism: its kernel and cokernel (as a map of nilpotent groups) are $T'$-torsion for $T' $ the complementary set of primes.*

The non-abelian content is that localisation respects the nilpotent filtration but does not in general distribute over a presentation; the construction proceeds by induction on the nilpotency class $c$, localising the central extension $1 \to Z(G) \to G \to G/Z(G) \to 1$ one stage at a time. This is the group-theoretic input that drives the $\pi_1$-level of the space-level localisation: a nilpotent space localises because its fundamental group is a nilpotent group, for which the localisation above exists, and its higher homotopy groups are modules over $\pi_1$ on which the action localises compatibly.

### The localisation fracture theorem

**Theorem (Sullivan 1974; Hilton-Mislin-Roitberg 1975, fracture for localisation).** *Let $X$ be a nilpotent space and let $\{T_i\}$ be a family of sets of primes whose union is the set of all primes and whose pairwise intersections all equal a common subset $S$. Then the natural map*
$$
X_{\bigcup_i T_i} \;\longrightarrow\; \operatorname{holim}_i \, X_{T_i}
$$
*is a homotopy equivalence, where the homotopy limit is taken over the diagram of further-localisation maps $X_{T_i} \to X_S$. In the basic two-set case with $S = \varnothing$, this is the pullback square $X = X_{(p)} \times^h_{X_\mathbb{Q}} X_{(p')}$ reassembling $X$ from a prime and its complement over the rationalisation.*

This is the Part 2 fracture theorem of May-Ponto, distinct from the Part 3 arithmetic-square completion fracture of [03.12.45]: here the corners are localisations $X_{T_i}$ (homotopy groups $\otimes \mathbb{Z}_{T_i}$, subrings of $\mathbb{Q}$) and the gluing corner is a further localisation, with no profinite completion and no finite-adele correction. The full prime-by-prime form recovers $X$ as the homotopy limit of all its $p$-localisations over the rationalisation, the homotopical lift of $\mathbb{Z} = \bigcap_p \mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$ inside $\mathbb{Q}$.

**Synthesis.** The localisation $X \to X_T$ is the foundational reason the homotopy theory of nilpotent spaces splits into independent prime-local pieces, and this is exactly the homotopical lift of the arithmetic fact that an abelian group is assembled from its localisations $A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$ glued over $A \otimes \mathbb{Q}$. The construction builds toward the completion theory of [03.12.45] and appears again in [03.12.06], where rationalisation is the endpoint $T = \varnothing$ of the present family. The central insight is that nilpotence provides a principal Postnikov refinement, which reduces every localisation statement to the algebraic localisation of a single coefficient group; putting these together, the localisation fracture theorem is dual to the completion fracture in the precise sense that one glues over the rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ while the other glues over the finite adeles $\mathbb{A}_f$, and the comparison map $X_{(p)} \to X^\wedge_p$ from localisation to completion generalises the inclusion $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{Z}_p$ of a subring of $\mathbb{Q}$ into a profinite ring. The bridge is that both fracture squares are instances of a single principle, that a nilpotent finite-type space is the homotopy limit of its arithmetic localisations over their common rational shadow, and the two theorems differ only in whether the local data is localised (subring of $\mathbb{Q}$) or completed (profinite).

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (homotopy groups of a $T$-localisation).** *Let $X$ be a nilpotent space and $X \to X_T$ its $T$-localisation. Then for every $n \geq 1$ the map $\pi_n(X) \to \pi_n(X_T)$ induces an isomorphism $\pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T \xrightarrow{\;\cong\;} \pi_n(X_T)$, where for $n = 1$ the tensor is the localisation of the nilpotent group $\pi_1(X)$ on its lower-central subquotients.*

*Proof.* Run the principal Postnikov refinement of $X$, a tower $\cdots \to X_n \to X_{n-1} \to \cdots$ with principal fibres $K(A_n, m_n)$ and $\varprojlim X_n \simeq X$, available by nilpotence (recalled from [03.12.45]). The base case $K(A, m) \to K(A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T, m)$ has the asserted homotopy-group statement by definition, since $A \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$ is the localised coefficient group and $\mathbb{Z}_T$ is flat, so $\mathrm{Tor}(A, \mathbb{Z}_T) = 0$ and no correction term appears.

For the inductive step, the fibration sequence $K(A_n \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T, m_n) \to (X_n)_T \to (X_{n-1})_T$ produces a long exact sequence of homotopy groups mapping from that of $K(A_n, m_n) \to X_n \to X_{n-1}$. By the inductive hypothesis the base and fibre maps are localisations on homotopy groups; since $(-) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$ is exact (flatness of $\mathbb{Z}_T$), the five-lemma applied to the localised long exact sequence gives that $\pi_k(X_n) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T \to \pi_k((X_n)_T)$ is an isomorphism for all $k$.

Passing to the inverse limit, $\pi_n(X_T) = \pi_n(\varprojlim (X_n)_T)$. The relevant $\lim^1$ term vanishes: at each finite stage the homotopy groups are finitely generated nilpotent (so finitely generated $\mathbb{Z}_T$-modules after localisation) and the tower maps are surjective on $\pi_n$ in the eventual range, satisfying the Mittag-Leffler criterion. Hence $\pi_n(X_T) = \varprojlim \pi_n((X_n)_T) = \varprojlim (\pi_n(X_n) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T) = (\varprojlim \pi_n(X_n)) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T = \pi_n(X) \otimes \mathbb{Z}_T$, where the third equality uses that localisation commutes with the eventually-constant inverse system. For $n = 1$ the same argument runs on the lower-central subquotients of the nilpotent group $\pi_1(X)$, using the localisation of nilpotent groups of the Advanced-results theorem. $\square$

**Proposition (homology characterisation of $T$-local spaces).** *A nilpotent space $Z$ has $\mathbb{Z}_T$-module homotopy groups if and only if its reduced integral homology groups $\widetilde{H}_n(Z; \mathbb{Z})$ are $\mathbb{Z}_T$-modules for all $n$.*

*Proof.* Suppose the homotopy groups are $\mathbb{Z}_T$-modules. Induct up the Postnikov tower: $\widetilde{H}_*(K(A, m); \mathbb{Z})$ for a $\mathbb{Z}_T$-module $A$ consists of $\mathbb{Z}_T$-modules, because the integral homology of $K(A, m)$ is built from $A$ by iterated Tor and tensor operations (the homology of Eilenberg-MacLane spaces), all of which preserve the property of being a $\mathbb{Z}_T$-module when $A$ is one and $\mathbb{Z}_T$ is a localisation of $\mathbb{Z}$. The Serre spectral sequence of each principal fibration has $E^2$-page a bigraded $\mathbb{Z}_T$-module by the inductive hypothesis on base and the just-established statement on fibre; the property passes to $E^\infty$ and hence to the homology of the total space, since an extension of $\mathbb{Z}_T$-modules is a $\mathbb{Z}_T$-module.

Conversely, suppose the reduced homology groups are $\mathbb{Z}_T$-modules. For a simply connected $Z$ the Hurewicz theorem gives $\pi_2(Z) = H_2(Z; \mathbb{Z})$, a $\mathbb{Z}_T$-module; inducting with the relative Hurewicz theorem up the Postnikov tower shows each $\pi_n(Z)$ is a $\mathbb{Z}_T$-module. For the nilpotent non-simply-connected case the same induction runs on the principal refinement, with the action of $\pi_1$ on the higher groups being $\mathbb{Z}_T$-linear because $\pi_1$ itself is $T$-local on its subquotients. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The completion counterpart of this unit is the arithmetic square and integral fracture theorem [03.12.45], which reassembles a nilpotent finite-type space from its $p$-completions $X^\wedge_p$ and rationalisation. The present unit builds directly atop it: it inherits the nilpotent-space definition, the rationalisation endpoint, and the homotopy-pullback fracture pattern, and replaces the profinite $p$-adic completion $\mathbb{Z}_p$ by the subring-of-$\mathbb{Q}$ localisation $\mathbb{Z}_T$, gluing over $\mathbb{Q}$ rather than over the finite adeles.

- The rationalisation $X_\mathbb{Q}$ studied through Sullivan minimal models [03.12.06] is the endpoint $T = \varnothing$ of the localisation family developed here. Every statement about $X_T$ specialises, when all primes are inverted, to a statement about the rational homotopy type, and the present unit shows rationalisation is one member of a one-parameter family indexed by sets of primes.

- The construction realises $X_T$ as a Bousfield localisation of a model category [03.12.48], with localising homology theory $H\mathbb{Z}_T$; the abstract reflector $L_{H\mathbb{Z}_T}$ of that framework is identified, on nilpotent spaces, with the explicit Postnikov-induction localisation of this unit, giving a worked instance of the general localisation machinery.

- The nilpotent-group and nilpotent-space theory underlying the construction — the lower central series, the principal Postnikov refinement, and the recognition criteria — is the natural prerequisite developed in the co-produced unit on nilpotent groups and spaces [03.12.61], which supplies the structural input that the localisation here consumes group-by-group along the central series.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The localisation of spaces at a set of primes originates with Dennis Sullivan's 1970 MIT notes and his 1974 *Annals of Mathematics* paper [ref: TODO_REF Sullivan 1974], which introduced the prime-by-prime study of homotopy types and the fracture squares assembling a space from its localisations and rationalisation; the same paper used the profinite completion and its Galois action to prove the Adams conjecture. Aldridge Bousfield's 1975 *Topology* paper [ref: TODO_REF Bousfield 1975] recast localisation as an idempotent functor on the homotopy category determined by a homology theory, making the existence and functoriality of $X_T$ a special case of homological localisation and freeing it from the case-by-case Postnikov constructions. The systematic group-theoretic and space-level development, including the careful treatment of nilpotent fundamental groups and the characterisation theorems, was carried out by Peter Hilton, Guido Mislin, and Joseph Roitberg in their 1975 monograph [ref: TODO_REF Hilton-Mislin-Roitberg 1975], which remains the canonical reference for the nilpotent case and fixes the convention, retained here, that $\mathbb{Z}_T$ denotes the integers with the primes outside $T$ inverted.

The conceptual debt to number theory is explicit in Sullivan's own framing: the assembly of a space from its $p$-local pieces over the rationals mirrors the assembly of an integer from its images in the local rings $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)}$, and the distinction between localisation and completion at a prime is the topological shadow of the inclusion $\mathbb{Z}_{(p)} \hookrightarrow \mathbb{Z}_p$ of a subring of $\mathbb{Q}$ into a profinite ring. May and Ponto [ref: TODO_REF May-Ponto 2012] organise the modern textbook account by separating the localisation theory (their Part 2) from the completion theory (their Part 3), the division this unit and [03.12.45] respect.
