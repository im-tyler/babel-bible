## Intuition [Beginner]

An *Eilenberg-MacLane space* $K(A, n)$ is a topological space whose homotopy is concentrated in one single degree: $\pi_n(K(A, n)) = A$ and all other homotopy groups vanish.

These spaces are the *minimal* spaces with given homotopy. They serve as the universal home for *ordinary cohomology*: cohomology classes $H^n(X; A)$ are exactly homotopy classes of maps $X \to K(A, n)$.

The simplest examples: $K(\mathbb{Z}, 1) = S^1$ (the circle, with $\pi_1 = \mathbb{Z}$), $K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 1) = \mathbb{RP}^\infty$ (infinite real projective space, with $\pi_1 = \mathbb{Z}/2$), and $K(\mathbb{Z}, 2) = \mathbb{CP}^\infty$ (infinite complex projective space, with $\pi_2 = \mathbb{Z}$). For higher $n$, the spaces become more complicated and don't have such clean geometric descriptions.

## Visual [Beginner]

A space with all homotopy concentrated in a single degree — pictured as a "pure" homotopy object whose only nonvanishing $\pi$-group is at one designated level.

![A space with all homotopy zero except $\pi_n = A$, the defining property of $K(A, n)$.](/img/eilenberg-maclane-placeholder.svg)

These spaces are the building blocks of homotopy theory: every space can be "decomposed" via Postnikov towers into Eilenberg-MacLane pieces, one per homotopy degree.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The space $K(\mathbb{Z}, 1) = S^1$. By covering-space theory, the universal cover of $S^1$ is the contractible real line $\mathbb{R}$, with deck transformation group $\mathbb{Z}$. So $\pi_1(S^1) = \mathbb{Z}$ and $\pi_k(S^1) = 0$ for $k \geq 2$ (the higher homotopy groups vanish because the universal cover is contractible). This makes $S^1$ exactly the $K(\mathbb{Z}, 1)$ space.

The space $K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 1) = \mathbb{RP}^\infty$. Its universal cover is $S^\infty$ (the infinite-dimensional sphere), which is contractible. The deck transformation group is $\mathbb{Z}/2$ (the antipodal action), so $\pi_1(\mathbb{RP}^\infty) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ with all higher homotopy zero.

The space $K(\mathbb{Z}, 2) = \mathbb{CP}^\infty$. By the Hopf fibration $S^1 \to S^\infty \to \mathbb{CP}^\infty$ (after taking infinite-dimensional limits), the long exact sequence gives $\pi_2(\mathbb{CP}^\infty) = \mathbb{Z}$ and all other homotopy zero.

The cohomology of $K(\mathbb{Z}, n)$ is computed by Cartan-Serre and underlies the Steenrod algebra of cohomology operations.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $A$ be an abelian group and $n \geq 1$ an integer. An **Eilenberg-MacLane space of type $(A, n)$**, denoted $K(A, n)$, is a topological space (more precisely, a homotopy type — usually realised as a CW complex) with:

$$
\pi_n(K(A, n), *) = A, \qquad \pi_k(K(A, n), *) = 0 \text{ for } k \neq n.
$$

For $n = 0$ the convention is $K(A, 0) = A$ as a discrete topological space (so $\pi_0 = A$ and higher homotopy vanishes).

For $n \geq 1$, $K(A, n)$ is unique up to weak homotopy equivalence (this is *not* a unique space, but a unique homotopy type). For $n \geq 2$, $A$ must be abelian (because $\pi_n$ for $n \geq 2$ is automatically abelian by Eckmann-Hilton).

**Loop-space characterisation.** $K(A, n) \simeq \Omega K(A, n+1)$, where $\Omega Y$ is the loop space. Equivalently, $K(A, n)$ is the $n$-th space in the *Eilenberg-MacLane spectrum* $HA = \{K(A, 0), K(A, 1), K(A, 2), \ldots\}$ [03.12.04].

**Representability of cohomology.** For any pointed CW complex $X$, ordinary cohomology with coefficients in $A$ is represented by mapping into $K(A, n)$:

$$
H^n(X; A) \cong [X, K(A, n)]_*.
$$

This is the Brown representability theorem [03.12.04] applied to ordinary singular cohomology, with representing spectrum the Eilenberg-MacLane spectrum.

**Examples and computations.**

- $K(\mathbb{Z}, 1) = S^1$.
- $K(\mathbb{Z}/n, 1) = $ "lens space at infinity" — the infinite lens space $L^\infty(n) = S^\infty / (\mathbb{Z}/n)$.
- $K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 1) = \mathbb{RP}^\infty$.
- $K(\mathbb{Z}, 2) = \mathbb{CP}^\infty$ (or $BU(1) = BS^1$).
- $K(\mathbb{Z}, n)$ for $n \geq 3$: no clean geometric description; can be constructed by attaching cells to kill higher homotopy beyond degree $n$.

**Cohomology of Eilenberg-MacLane spaces.** $H^*(K(\mathbb{Z}/2, n); \mathbb{Z}/2)$ is the polynomial algebra on Steenrod operations applied to the fundamental class, by Serre's computation. This is the prototype for the *Steenrod algebra* $\mathcal{A}_2$ of cohomology operations.

**Postnikov towers.** Every connected CW complex $X$ has a *Postnikov tower*: a sequence of spaces $X^{(n)}$ for $n \geq 0$ with $X^{(n)} \to X^{(n-1)}$ a principal fibration with fibre $K(\pi_n(X), n)$, and $X \to X^{(n)}$ inducing iso on $\pi_k$ for $k \leq n$. The decomposition into Eilenberg-MacLane fibres is the homotopy-theoretic analogue of the chain-complex decomposition into degree pieces.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (existence and uniqueness of Eilenberg-MacLane spaces).** *For any abelian group $A$ and any $n \geq 1$, there exists a CW complex $K(A, n)$ with $\pi_n(K(A, n)) = A$ and $\pi_k(K(A, n)) = 0$ for $k \neq n$. Moreover, $K(A, n)$ is unique up to weak homotopy equivalence.*

**Proof sketch (existence).** Build $K(A, n)$ by attaching cells to kill homotopy beyond degree $n$ and to realise $A$ at degree $n$.

*Step 1: Realise $\pi_n = A$.* Choose a free resolution $0 \to F_1 \to F_0 \to A \to 0$ of $A$ as a $\mathbb{Z}$-module. Let $F_0$ have basis indexed by some set $S_0$ and $F_1$ by $S_1$ with attaching map $\partial : F_1 \to F_0$. Construct a Moore space $M(A, n)$ by taking a wedge of $n$-spheres indexed by $S_0$ (giving $\pi_n = F_0$ on $n$-skeleton) and attaching $(n+1)$-cells indexed by $S_1$ along maps representing $\partial$ (collapsing the $\pi_n$ to $A$).

*Step 2: Kill higher homotopy.* For each $k > n$ and each generator of $\pi_k$ at this stage, attach a $(k+1)$-cell along the generating map to make the generator null-homotopic. Repeat for each generator at each degree. The result is a CW complex with $\pi_n = A$ and $\pi_k = 0$ for $k > n$ (higher homotopy is killed by the cell attachments).

*Step 3: Verify $\pi_k = 0$ for $k < n$.* The construction starts with cells of dimension $\geq n$, so $K(A, n)$ has only cells of dimension $\geq n$ (after Step 2). Hence $\pi_k = 0$ for $k < n$ by cellular approximation.

**Proof sketch (uniqueness).** Suppose $K, K'$ are two CW complexes with the homotopy of $K(A, n)$. The identity map of $\pi_n$ gives a map $K \to K'$ on the $n$-skeletons. Obstructions to extending to higher cells lie in $H^{k+1}(K; \pi_k(K')) = H^{k+1}(K; 0) = 0$ for $k > n$. So the map extends, and similarly its inverse. The obtained map is a weak equivalence by construction (induces iso on all $\pi_k$). $\square$

This existence-and-uniqueness theorem makes $K(A, n)$ a *well-defined homotopy type* — unique up to weak homotopy equivalence, even though no specific topological space is privileged.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that $\Omega K(A, n+1) \simeq K(A, n)$ for $n \geq 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $\pi_k(\Omega Y) = \pi_{k+1}(Y)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $Y = K(A, n+1)$, the loop space $\Omega Y$ has $\pi_k(\Omega Y) = \pi_{k+1}(Y) = \pi_{k+1}(K(A, n+1))$. By definition of $K(A, n+1)$:
- $\pi_{k+1}(K(A, n+1)) = A$ if $k + 1 = n + 1$, i.e., $k = n$;
- $\pi_{k+1}(K(A, n+1)) = 0$ if $k + 1 \neq n + 1$, i.e., $k \neq n$.

So $\Omega K(A, n+1)$ has $\pi_k = A$ at $k = n$ and zero elsewhere — exactly the homotopy of $K(A, n)$. By uniqueness of Eilenberg-MacLane homotopy type, $\Omega K(A, n+1) \simeq K(A, n)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, conceptual).**

Why is $H^n(X; A) \cong [X, K(A, n)]_*$ a representability statement?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Cohomology is a functor; representability means it is a representable functor on the homotopy category.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The functor $X \mapsto H^n(X; A)$ from pointed CW complexes to abelian groups is representable iff there exists a *single* space $K$ such that $H^n(X; A) \cong [X, K]_*$ naturally in $X$. This is the Yoneda-theoretic content of "representability." Brown's theorem says cohomology theories are representable; the Eilenberg-MacLane theorem says specifically that ordinary cohomology with coefficients in $A$ at degree $n$ is represented by $K(A, n)$. The space $K(A, n)$ is the *universal* example: every cohomology class on every space is "pulled back" from a class on $K(A, n)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Show that $\mathbb{RP}^\infty = K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 1)$ using the universal cover.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The universal cover of $\mathbb{RP}^\infty$ is $S^\infty$, which is contractible.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathbb{RP}^\infty := \mathrm{colim}_n \mathbb{RP}^n$ is the infinite real projective space. Its universal cover is $S^\infty := \mathrm{colim}_n S^n$ — the infinite-dimensional sphere — with deck transformation group $\mathbb{Z}/2$ (the antipodal action).

$S^\infty$ is contractible: any compact set in $S^\infty$ lies in some $S^N$, which is itself contractible inside $S^{N+1}$ via a sliding-to-the-pole homotopy. So $S^\infty$ is contractible.

By covering space theory, $\pi_k(\mathbb{RP}^\infty) = \pi_k(S^\infty)$ for $k \geq 2$ (covering doesn't change higher homotopy), so $\pi_k(\mathbb{RP}^\infty) = 0$ for $k \geq 2$. And $\pi_1(\mathbb{RP}^\infty) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ (the deck group).

So $\mathbb{RP}^\infty$ has $\pi_1 = \mathbb{Z}/2$ and all higher $\pi_k = 0$ — exactly $K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 1)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

State Serre's calculation of $H^\ast(K(\mathbb{Z}/2, n); \mathbb{Z}/2)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The cohomology is generated by the fundamental class and Steenrod squares applied to it.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\iota_n \in H^n(K(\mathbb{Z}/2, n); \mathbb{Z}/2)$ be the fundamental class (corresponding to the identity in the representability iso $H^n(K(\mathbb{Z}/2, n); \mathbb{Z}/2) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2$).

**Serre's theorem (1951).** $H^\ast(K(\mathbb{Z}/2, n); \mathbb{Z}/2)$ is a polynomial algebra over $\mathbb{Z}/2$ on the *admissible monomials* $\mathrm{Sq}^I(\iota_n)$ where $I = (i_1, i_2, \ldots, i_k)$ ranges over admissible (excess-bounded) sequences of Steenrod squares.

This computation makes $K(\mathbb{Z}/2, n)$'s cohomology the *free Steenrod-algebra module* on a single generator. It is the basis for the modern theory of the Steenrod algebra and many spectral-sequence computations in stable homotopy.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Sketch the construction of the Postnikov tower of a connected CW complex $X$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Iteratively kill homotopy beyond a target degree, producing a sequence of approximations $X^{(n)}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Build a tower $\cdots \to X^{(n)} \to X^{(n-1)} \to \cdots \to X^{(1)} \to X^{(0)}$ with maps $X \to X^{(n)}$ such that:

- $X \to X^{(n)}$ induces iso on $\pi_k$ for $k \leq n$.
- $X^{(n)}$ has $\pi_k = 0$ for $k > n$.

To construct $X^{(n)}$: start from $X$, attach cells of dimension $> n + 1$ to kill $\pi_k$ for $k > n$. The resulting space has homotopy concentrated in degrees $\leq n$.

The fibration $X^{(n)} \to X^{(n-1)}$ has fibre $K(\pi_n(X), n)$ — the "homotopy at degree $n$" extracted as a single Eilenberg-MacLane piece.

The Postnikov tower is the homotopy-theoretic analogue of a *filtration of a chain complex by degree* — it decomposes $X$ into one Eilenberg-MacLane piece per homotopy degree, glued together by *k-invariants* in $H^{n+2}(X^{(n-1)}; \pi_n(X))$.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Cohomology operations.** A *cohomology operation* of type $(A, n) \to (B, m)$ is a natural transformation $H^n(-; A) \to H^m(-; B)$. By representability, this is the same as a homotopy class of maps $K(A, n) \to K(B, m)$, equivalently an element of $H^m(K(A, n); B)$. The Steenrod algebra $\mathcal{A}_p$ at prime $p$ is the algebra of cohomology operations on mod-$p$ cohomology, with multiplication = composition.

**Steenrod squares.** The basic mod-2 cohomology operations are *Steenrod squares* $\mathrm{Sq}^i : H^n(-; \mathbb{Z}/2) \to H^{n+i}(-; \mathbb{Z}/2)$, satisfying the Cartan formula and Adem relations. The Steenrod algebra $\mathcal{A}_2$ is generated by $\{\mathrm{Sq}^i\}_{i \geq 1}$ subject to these relations.

**Eilenberg-MacLane spectrum.** The collection $HA = \{K(A, 0), K(A, 1), K(A, 2), \ldots\}$ with structure maps $\Sigma K(A, n) \to K(A, n+1)$ forms a spectrum [03.12.04] representing ordinary cohomology with coefficients in $A$. $H \mathbb{Z}$ is one of the most important spectra in algebraic topology.

**Brown representability and Eilenberg-MacLane.** Eilenberg-MacLane spaces are the special case of Brown representability for ordinary cohomology. The spectrum $HA$ has $\pi_n(HA) = A$ at $n = 0$ and zero elsewhere — making it the "minimal" spectrum representing $H^*(-; A)$.

**Postnikov towers.** Every space $X$ has a tower of approximations $X^{(0)} \leftarrow X^{(1)} \leftarrow \cdots$ where $X^{(n)}$ has homotopy of $X$ truncated at degree $n$. The fibres of consecutive towers are Eilenberg-MacLane spaces. Postnikov towers are the homotopy-theoretic analogue of filtration by degree.

**Cohomology with twisted coefficients.** For $\pi_1(X)$-modules, generalised Eilenberg-MacLane constructions $K(A, n)$ with twisted coefficients give *equivariant cohomology* and underlie modern equivariant homotopy theory.

**Applications to characteristic-class theory.** Stiefel-Whitney classes $w_i$ correspond to maps $BO(n) \to K(\mathbb{Z}/2, i)$, hence to elements of $H^i(BO(n); \mathbb{Z}/2)$. Chern classes $c_i$ correspond to $BU(n) \to K(\mathbb{Z}, 2i)$. The classifying-space cohomology rings of $BO(n)$ and $BU(n)$ are exactly the Eilenberg-MacLane representability of these characteristic classes.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Existence.** Sketched in §"Key theorem". The cell-attachment construction produces $K(A, n)$ as a CW complex.

**Uniqueness.** Proved via obstruction theory in §"Key theorem".

**Loop-space relation.** Proved in Exercise 3.

**Cohomology representability (sketch).** Brown's theorem applied to $H^n(-; A)$ gives a representing spectrum, which by uniqueness and the homotopy-group profile is the Eilenberg-MacLane spectrum $HA$. The level-$n$ space $K(A, n)$ then represents $H^n$ specifically.

**Postnikov tower (sketch).** Proved in Exercise 7. The tower exists by repeated application of the cell-attachment killing of homotopy.

**Steenrod algebra structure.** Cohomology operations form an algebra under composition; the algebra is generated by Steenrod squares (mod 2) or reduced powers (mod $p$) subject to the Adem relations. The algebra structure follows from the multiplicative structure of cohomology operations and the Yoneda-style composition of natural transformations.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Samuel Eilenberg and Saunders Mac Lane introduced the spaces $K(\Pi, n)$ in their 1945 paper *Relations between homology and homotopy groups of spaces*, motivated by the classification of group extensions. Serre's 1951 thesis computed the cohomology of these spaces and established their role in stable homotopy theory.

The Postnikov tower (Postnikov, 1951) made Eilenberg-MacLane spaces the building blocks of homotopy theory: every space decomposes into Eilenberg-MacLane fibres glued by $k$-invariants. This structural decomposition underlies obstruction theory, cohomology operations, and the modern theory of homotopy types via $\infty$-groupoids.

In the modern $\infty$-categorical formalism, $K(A, n)$ is the *$n$-th truncation* of the abelian group $A$ viewed as a homotopical object — a "fully Postnikov tower" perspective. Eilenberg-MacLane spaces sit at the heart of the connection between stable homotopy theory, derived algebra, and arithmetic geometry.
