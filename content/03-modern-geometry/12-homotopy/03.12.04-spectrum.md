## Intuition [Beginner]

A *spectrum* is the homotopy-theoretic object that "stabilises" the homotopy of spaces with respect to suspension. Where a topological space has homotopy groups in finitely many connected directions, a spectrum has homotopy groups in *all* integer degrees — positive *and* negative.

The motivating idea: take a sequence of pointed spaces $E_0, E_1, E_2, \ldots$ and structure maps connecting them via suspension. The simplest example is the *sphere spectrum* $\mathbb{S} = (S^0, S^1, S^2, \ldots)$ with suspension structure maps $\Sigma S^n \to S^{n+1}$ being the identity (homotopy-equivalence).

Spectra are the natural objects of *generalised cohomology*: every cohomology theory (singular, K-theory, bordism, …) is represented by a spectrum. They live in the *stable homotopy category*, where suspension is invertible — a much more algebraically tractable place than ordinary spaces.

## Visual [Beginner]

A sequence of spaces stacked vertically, with suspension arrows connecting each to the next. Together they form one structured object — a spectrum.

Stable homotopy theory is the study of these stacked sequences and their morphisms.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The **sphere spectrum** $\mathbb{S}$ is the simplest example: $\mathbb{S}_n := S^n$ for $n \geq 0$, with structure maps $\Sigma S^n = S^{n+1}$. Its stable homotopy groups $\pi_k^s(\mathbb{S}) := \pi_k^s$ — the *stable homotopy groups of spheres* — form a deep computational and conceptual frontier.

The first few:
- $\pi_0^s = \mathbb{Z}$ (the degree)
- $\pi_1^s = \mathbb{Z}/2$ (Hopf)
- $\pi_2^s = \mathbb{Z}/2$
- $\pi_3^s = \mathbb{Z}/24$
- $\pi_4^s = 0$
- $\pi_5^s = 0$
- $\pi_6^s = \mathbb{Z}/2$
- $\pi_7^s = \mathbb{Z}/240$

These are highly nontrivial finite abelian groups. Computing them is one of the central challenges of algebraic topology.

Other spectra: **Eilenberg-MacLane spectra** $H A$ for an abelian group $A$ (representing ordinary cohomology with coefficients in $A$), **K-theory spectra** $\mathbf{KU}, \mathbf{KO}$ (representing complex / real K-theory), **bordism spectra** $\mathbf{MO}, \mathbf{MU}, \mathbf{MSO}$ (representing unoriented / complex / oriented bordism).

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **spectrum** $E$ (in the simplest, sequential model) is a sequence of pointed topological spaces

$$
E_0, E_1, E_2, \ldots
$$

together with **structure maps**

$$
\sigma_n : \Sigma E_n \to E_{n+1}
$$

(or equivalently, by the suspension-loop adjunction, $\widetilde{\sigma}_n : E_n \to \Omega E_{n+1}$).

A spectrum is an **$\Omega$-spectrum** if each $\widetilde{\sigma}_n : E_n \to \Omega E_{n+1}$ is a (weak) homotopy equivalence. $\Omega$-spectra are the "fibrant" or "good" representatives of the stable homotopy type.

A **morphism of spectra** $f : E \to F$ is a sequence of based maps $f_n : E_n \to F_n$ commuting with the structure maps (up to homotopy in the appropriate sense).

The **stable homotopy groups** of a spectrum $E$ are

$$
\pi_n(E) := \mathrm{colim}_k \, \pi_{n+k}(E_k),
$$

where the colimit is taken along the suspension maps. By the Freudenthal suspension theorem [03.12.03], for sufficiently connected spaces, the colimit stabilises, and the result is well-defined for all integers $n$ (positive *and* negative, depending on the spectrum's connectivity).

The **stable homotopy category** $\mathrm{Ho}(\mathrm{Sp})$ is the homotopy category of spectra under weak equivalence (maps inducing isomorphisms on stable homotopy groups). It is *triangulated*, *symmetric monoidal* (with smash product $E \wedge F$ as the tensor), and *generated* (under colimits and suspension) by the sphere spectrum $\mathbb{S}$.

**Key examples.**

- **Sphere spectrum** $\mathbb{S}$: $\mathbb{S}_n = S^n$ with identity structure maps. The unit object of the smash-product symmetric-monoidal structure.
- **Eilenberg-MacLane spectrum** $H A$ for abelian $A$: $(HA)_n = K(A, n)$, the Eilenberg-MacLane space [03.12.05]. Represents ordinary cohomology: $H^n(X; A) = [X, K(A, n)] = \pi_{-n}(F(X, HA))$ where $F$ is the function spectrum.
- **K-theory spectrum** $\mathbf{KU}$: $\mathbf{KU}_n = \mathbb{Z} \times BU$ for even $n$ and $U$ for odd $n$, with structure maps coming from Bott periodicity. Represents complex topological K-theory.
- **Bordism spectrum** $\mathbf{MO}$: built from Thom spaces of universal bundles over $BO(n)$. Represents unoriented bordism.

**Generalised cohomology.** Every spectrum $E$ defines a *generalised cohomology theory*

$$
E^n(X) := [X, E_n]_{\mathrm{stable}}
$$

(homotopy classes of stable maps), satisfying the Eilenberg-Steenrod axioms except possibly the *dimension axiom* (which is replaced by the homotopy groups of $E$).

Conversely, by the **Brown representability theorem**, every generalised cohomology theory is represented by some spectrum, unique up to homotopy. So the *stable homotopy category* and the *category of generalised cohomology theories* are equivalent (this is sometimes called *Brown representability*).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Brown representability).** *Every additive cohomology theory $h^\ast$ on the homotopy category of pointed CW complexes that satisfies the Eilenberg-Steenrod axioms (except possibly dimension) is representable: there exists an $\Omega$-spectrum $E$ such that*

$$
h^n(X) \cong [X, E_n]_*.
$$

*Conversely, every $\Omega$-spectrum determines such a cohomology theory by the same formula.*

**Proof sketch (representability direction).** Given a cohomology theory $h^\ast$, construct $E_n$ to represent $h^n$ on each finite CW complex via the *Yoneda lemma* applied to the functor $X \mapsto h^n(X)$. The technical work is to verify the construction:

- $h^n$ is *representable* on finite CW: use the small-object argument plus the *additivity* and *Mayer-Vietoris* axioms to build a representing space $E_n$ as a colimit.
- The structure maps $\sigma : \Sigma E_n \to E_{n+1}$ come from the *suspension isomorphism* $h^n(X) \cong h^{n+1}(\Sigma X)$ (an axiom of additive cohomology theories).
- The $\Omega$-spectrum condition follows from the suspension iso, by the suspension-loop adjunction $[\Sigma X, E_{n+1}] = [X, \Omega E_{n+1}]$.

The uniqueness of the representing spectrum is up to weak equivalence in the stable category. The *Yoneda-style* formal construction makes this universal property precise.

**Proof sketch (converse direction).** Given an $\Omega$-spectrum $E$, define $E^n(X) := [X, E_n]_*$. The Eilenberg-Steenrod axioms for additive cohomology — homotopy invariance, suspension isomorphism, Mayer-Vietoris, additivity over wedges — all follow from the spectrum structure of $E$ and the $\Omega$-spectrum condition. $\square$

This duality between spectra and cohomology theories is one of the central structural results of algebraic topology and is the conceptual reason spectra are the "right" objects for stable homotopy.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Verify that the sphere spectrum is an $\Omega$-spectrum after appropriate fibrant replacement (i.e., the structure maps $\widetilde{\sigma}_n : S^n \to \Omega S^{n+1}$ become weak equivalences in the stable category).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The map $S^n \to \Omega S^{n+1}$ corresponds via adjunction to the identity $\Sigma S^n = S^{n+1}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The structure map $\sigma_n : \Sigma S^n \to S^{n+1}$ is the identity (or a chosen homotopy equivalence). The adjoint $\widetilde{\sigma}_n : S^n \to \Omega S^{n+1}$ may not literally be a weak equivalence at the space level, but in the stable category the sphere spectrum *is* equivalent to its $\Omega$-spectrum replacement. Mathematical phrase: the sphere spectrum is "fibrant up to weak equivalence." Detailed verification uses the homotopy excision / Freudenthal theorem to compare $\pi_k(S^n)$ with $\pi_k(\Omega S^{n+1}) = \pi_{k+1}(S^{n+1})$ in the stable range.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

What is $\pi_3^s$, the third stable homotopy group of spheres?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

This is one of the famous "nontrivial" stable groups.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\pi_3^s = \mathbb{Z}/24$. The generator is the *Hopf invariant* class, related to the quaternionic Hopf fibration $S^7 \to S^4$. The 24 reflects the order of the binary tetrahedral group.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the homotopy groups of an $\Omega$-spectrum $E$ are well-defined integer-indexed groups: $\pi_k(E) = \pi_n(E_{n+k})$ for all sufficiently large $n$, with $k$ allowed to be negative.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For an $\Omega$-spectrum, $E_n \simeq \Omega E_{n+1}$, so $\pi_n(E_{n+k}) \cong \pi_{n-1}(\Omega E_{n+k}) = \pi_{n-1}(E_{n+k-1})$ ... shift indices.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For an $\Omega$-spectrum, the homotopy equivalence $E_m \simeq \Omega E_{m+1}$ gives $\pi_j(E_m) \cong \pi_{j+1}(E_{m+1})$ for every $j \geq 0$. Iterate: $\pi_j(E_m) \cong \pi_{j+r}(E_{m+r})$ for any $r \geq 0$. Setting $j + r$ and shifting indices, the homotopy groups depend only on $j - m = k$, the "stable degree." So $\pi_k(E) := \pi_{n+k}(E_n)$ is well-defined for any $n$ large enough that $n + k \geq 0$ — and crucially, for negative $k$ too, as long as $n + k \geq 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, conceptual).**

What is the smash product $E \wedge F$ of two spectra, and why is it the "right" notion of tensor?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For spaces, smash product is $X \wedge Y = (X \times Y)/(X \vee Y)$. For spectra, the formula must respect suspension structure.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For spectra $E, F$, the smash product $E \wedge F$ is defined level-wise (with appropriate categorical bookkeeping) so that $\pi_*(E \wedge F)$ recovers the stable homotopy of the smash product. In *symmetric* spectrum models or modern $\infty$-categorical models, $\wedge$ is the symmetric-monoidal tensor: $\mathbb{S} \wedge E \simeq E$ (sphere is unit), $E \wedge F \simeq F \wedge E$ (commutative), and associativity holds. This makes the stable category a closed symmetric monoidal category — a "ring-like" environment for spectra.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why is the stable homotopy category "easier" than the unstable one?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Stably, suspension is invertible; many obstruction-theoretic arguments simplify.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the stable category:

- Suspension is invertible: every spectrum has a "desuspension."
- Maps between objects form abelian groups (not just sets).
- The category is *triangulated*: long exact sequences in homotopy come from cofibre sequences automatically.
- Generalised cohomology theories are equivalent to spectra (Brown representability).

Many computations that are intractable unstably (homotopy groups of spheres, classification of bundles, Adams spectral sequence input) become structured stably. The price: the stable category loses some unstable information (e.g., $\pi_1$ is always abelian stably, but the unstable $\pi_1$ can be nonabelian).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

State the Eilenberg-MacLane representability of ordinary cohomology: for an abelian group $A$ and a CW complex $X$,

$$
H^n(X; A) \cong [X, K(A, n)]_*.
$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Brown representability applied to ordinary singular cohomology, with representing spectrum $HA$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Brown representability, ordinary cohomology with coefficients in $A$ is representable by an $\Omega$-spectrum. The representing spaces $K(A, n)$ for $n \geq 0$ have $\pi_n(K(A, n)) = A$ and $\pi_k(K(A, n)) = 0$ for $k \neq n$. The structure maps $K(A, n) \xrightarrow{\sim} \Omega K(A, n+1)$ make this an $\Omega$-spectrum, the Eilenberg-MacLane spectrum $HA$.

The representability iso $H^n(X; A) \cong [X, K(A, n)]_*$ is then by definition (or by Brown representability), assuming $X$ is a CW complex. This connects ordinary cohomology — defined via singular chains — to the homotopical theory of mapping into Eilenberg-MacLane spaces.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

State the chromatic filtration of the stable homotopy category and its connection to formal group laws.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The stable category is filtered by Morava K-theories $K(n)$ for $n \geq 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The *chromatic filtration*: the stable homotopy category is filtered by *height* $n$ (for $n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$), where height-$n$ spectra are detected by *Morava K-theory* $K(n)$ — a generalised cohomology theory whose coefficient ring is $\mathbb{F}_p[v_n^{\pm 1}]$ at prime $p$. Heights correspond to *formal group laws* of height $n$:

- Height 0: rational stable homotopy / $H \mathbb{Q}$.
- Height 1: complex K-theory $\mathbf{KU}$ at $p$, with formal group law the multiplicative one.
- Height 2: elliptic cohomology, Morava E-theory $E_2$.
- Higher heights: less geometric, defined formally.

The chromatic structure (Ravenel-Hopkins-Smith, 1980s-90s) organises stable homotopy by the "chromatic level" of the formal group law. This is the source of much modern stable homotopy theory and connects to algebraic geometry via the moduli of formal groups.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Brown representability.** Stated and sketched in §"Key theorem".

**Stable homotopy category as triangulated.** $\mathrm{Ho}(\mathrm{Sp})$ is triangulated: every cofibre sequence $A \to B \to C \to \Sigma A$ produces a long exact sequence in homotopy. This is the homotopy-theoretic analogue of derived categories in homological algebra.

**Smash product and ring spectra.** A *ring spectrum* is a spectrum $R$ with a multiplication map $R \wedge R \to R$ and unit $\mathbb{S} \to R$ satisfying associativity and unit axioms up to homotopy. Every classical cohomology ring (singular, K-theory, bordism) lifts to a ring spectrum. *Highly-structured* ring spectra ($A_\infty$, $E_\infty$) are the modern stable analogues of associative / commutative rings.

**Adams spectral sequence.** Computes stable homotopy groups via $\mathrm{Ext}$-groups in modules over the Steenrod algebra $\mathcal{A}_p$. The $E_2$-page is purely algebraic; differentials encode the analytic / topological refinement. The Adams spectral sequence converges to (a localisation of) $\pi_*^s$.

**Bousfield localisation.** A spectrum $E$ defines an *$E$-localisation functor* $L_E$ on the stable category. $E$-local objects are spectra with $E_*$-isomorphisms inverted. Examples: rationalisation $L_{\mathbb{H}\mathbb{Q}}$, $K(n)$-localisation (chromatic).

**Stable category is the universal example of a stable $\infty$-category.** In Lurie's $\infty$-categorical formalism, the stable category $\mathrm{Sp}$ is the free stable $\infty$-category on a single generator (the sphere spectrum). All stable $\infty$-categories receive maps from $\mathrm{Sp}$, making it the "ground field" of higher algebra.

**Formal group laws and chromatic homotopy.** As in Exercise 7. The chromatic filtration is the source of much recent activity (topological modular forms, derived algebraic geometry, equivariant elliptic cohomology).

**Synthesis.** Spectra are the stabilised objects of homotopy theory: a spectrum $E$ encodes a sequence of spaces with structure maps $\Sigma E_n \to E_{n+1}$ (or equivalently $E_n \to \Omega E_{n+1}$), the resulting stable homotopy category admits a symmetric monoidal smash product making cohomology theories representable, Brown representability identifies every generalized cohomology theory with maps into a spectrum, and the suspension-loop adjunction descends to an equivalence $\Sigma \rightleftarrows \Omega$ in the stable category that has no analogue unstably. Eilenberg-MacLane spectra $HA$ represent ordinary cohomology with coefficients in $A$, the sphere spectrum represents stable cohomotopy, and the Bott-periodic K-theory spectrum $KU$ represents complex topological K-theory — connecting this abstract machinery back to the concrete vector-bundle invariants of [03.08.01]. Modern structured spectra (symmetric, orthogonal) resolve the technical difficulties with the smash product and enable a full $\infty$-categorical treatment.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Stable homotopy is well-defined.** Proved in Exercise 3.

**Brown representability.** Sketched in §"Key theorem". Full proof: Hatcher Ch. 4.E or Switzer §9.

**Triangulated structure.** Proved by checking the axioms (TR1–TR4) for the cofibre-sequence functor on spectra. The shift functor is suspension; cofibre sequences are stable cofibrations.

**Symmetric monoidal structure.** Modern construction (e.g., symmetric spectra) gives a strict symmetric monoidal model category. The smash product on objects produces a homotopy-symmetric-monoidal structure on $\mathrm{Ho}(\mathrm{Sp})$.

**$E_\infty$-rings as commutative monoids.** A commutative monoid in the symmetric-monoidal category of spectra (modulo homotopy, with all higher coherences) is an $E_\infty$-ring spectrum. Examples include $H\mathbb{Z}$, $\mathbf{KU}$, $\mathbf{MO}$, the sphere $\mathbb{S}$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Spectra were introduced by Lima (1959) and Whitehead (1962) as a formal device to make suspension invertible. Adams (1964) gave the first systematic exposition, including the Adams spectral sequence. Boardman (1965) constructed the stable homotopy category as the homotopy category of "naive" spectra, and Frank Adams's *Stable Homotopy and Generalised Homology* (1974) became the standard reference.

Modern strictifications — *symmetric spectra* (Hovey-Shipley-Smith, 2000), *S-modules* (Elmendorf-Kriz-Mandell-May, 1997), *orthogonal spectra* — turn the stable homotopy category into a strict symmetric monoidal category. Lurie's *Higher Topos Theory* (2009) and *Higher Algebra* (2011) reconstruct the stable category from $\infty$-categorical foundations, integrating it with derived algebraic geometry and the theory of factorisation algebras.

The chromatic perspective (Quillen, Morava, Hopkins, Ravenel) connects stable homotopy theory to formal group laws and arithmetic geometry, opening connections to number theory, mathematical physics (string theory's elliptic cohomology), and the modern theory of moduli spaces.
