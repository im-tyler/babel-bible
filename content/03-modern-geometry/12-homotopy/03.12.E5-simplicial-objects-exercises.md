## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

May's *Simplicial Objects in Algebraic Topology* develops the algebra of functors $\Delta^{\mathrm{op}} \to \mathcal{A}$ into a target category $\mathcal{A}$. When $\mathcal{A} = \mathbf{Ab}$, the resulting **simplicial abelian groups** are equivalent to non-negatively graded chain complexes via the **Dold-Kan correspondence** — the structural theorem that homological algebra and simplicial algebra are the same subject in degree $\ge 0$. The book also builds the **bar construction**, which turns a group (or monoid, or algebra) into a simplicial object whose realization is a classifying space, and proves the **Eilenberg-Zilber theorem** comparing the chains on a product with the tensor product of chains.

This pack collects nine exercises drawn from §17-§31 — two easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. The exercises are grouped: the normalized/Moore complex and degeneracies (easy), the Dold-Kan functors and the bar construction (medium), and the Eilenberg-Zilber theorem with its shuffle and Alexander-Whitney maps, plus the homotopy of a simplicial abelian group (hard).

The conventions are May's: for a simplicial abelian group $A$, the **Moore complex** $(C_*A, \partial)$ has $C_n A = A_n$ with $\partial = \sum_i (-1)^i d_i$; the **normalized complex** $N_n A = \bigcap_{i>0} \ker d_i$ with differential $d_0$; and the **degenerate subcomplex** $D_n A$ is generated by the images of the degeneracies $s_i$. Homotopy of a simplicial abelian group means homology of its associated chain complex: $\pi_n A = H_n(C_* A)$.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as the model solution. The remaining eight follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *State the Dold-Kan correspondence and prove the normalization theorem $C_* A = N_* A \oplus D_* A$ with $D_* A$ acyclic.*

**Solution.** **Dold-Kan correspondence.** The normalized-chains functor $N: \mathbf{sAb} \to \mathrm{Ch}_{\ge 0}(\mathbf{Ab})$ and the functor $\Gamma: \mathrm{Ch}_{\ge 0} \to \mathbf{sAb}$ are mutually inverse equivalences of categories. Under it, $\pi_n A = H_n(N A)$, and the equivalence is natural.

**Normalization theorem.** For a simplicial abelian group $A$, the Moore complex splits as $C_* A = N_* A \oplus D_* A$, where $N_n A = \bigcap_{i \ge 1}\ker d_i$ is the normalized subcomplex and $D_n A = \sum_i \mathrm{im}\, s_i$ is the degenerate subcomplex, and the inclusion $N_* A \hookrightarrow C_* A$ is a chain-homotopy equivalence because $D_* A$ is acyclic.

**Proof.** First, $C_n A = N_n A \oplus D_n A$ as abelian groups: the simplicial identities let one write any element uniquely as a sum of a normalized element and a sum of degenerate ones, by an explicit idempotent built from the $s_i d_i$. Concretely, the operators $P^{(k)} = \prod (1 - s_i d_i)$ assembled in order give a projection of $C_n A$ onto $N_n A$ along $D_n A$. The differential $\partial = \sum_i (-1)^i d_i$ restricts to $N_* A$ (since on the normalized part all $d_i$ with $i \ge 1$ vanish, leaving $d_0$ up to sign) and preserves $D_* A$.

The degenerate complex $D_* A$ is acyclic: an explicit contracting homotopy is built from the extra degeneracy / the simplicial identities $d_i s_i = \mathrm{id}$, which shows every cycle in $D_*$ is a boundary. Hence the projection $C_* A \to N_* A$ is a quasi-isomorphism — in fact a chain-homotopy equivalence — and $H_*(C_* A) = H_*(N_* A)$. $\square$

This is the engine of the whole subject: it lets one compute the homotopy of a simplicial abelian group with the small normalized complex, and it is the technical core of the Dold-Kan equivalence.

## Exercises [Intermediate]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).** *Simplicial identities for faces and degeneracies.*

Write down the simplicial identities relating the face maps $d_i$ and degeneracy maps $s_j$, and verify the identity $d_i s_i = \mathrm{id} = d_{i+1} s_i$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

There are five families. The ones you need say "a degeneracy followed by its adjacent face is the identity."

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The **simplicial identities** are:

$$
\begin{aligned}
d_i d_j &= d_{j-1} d_i && (i < j), \\
s_i s_j &= s_{j+1} s_i && (i \le j), \\
d_i s_j &= s_{j-1} d_i && (i < j), \\
d_i s_j &= \mathrm{id} && (i = j \text{ or } i = j+1), \\
d_i s_j &= s_j d_{i-1} && (i > j+1).
\end{aligned}
$$

The fourth family gives directly $d_j s_j = \mathrm{id}$ and $d_{j+1} s_j = \mathrm{id}$; setting $i = j$ in the first case and $i = j+1$ in the second. Geometrically, $s_j$ inserts a degenerate (repeated) vertex, and the two adjacent faces $d_j, d_{j+1}$ delete that repeated vertex, returning the original simplex. These two identities are what make the degenerate subcomplex acyclic and power the normalization theorem. May §1, §17.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Dold-Kan on a chain complex concentrated in one degree.*

Apply the Dold-Kan functor $\Gamma$ to the chain complex with $A$ in degree $n$ and $0$ elsewhere. Identify the resulting simplicial abelian group.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\Gamma$ of $A[n]$ is the simplicial abelian group $K(A, n)$ — the Eilenberg-MacLane object.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The chain complex $A[n]$ (the group $A$ in degree $n$, zero elsewhere) corresponds under $\Gamma$ to the **Eilenberg-MacLane simplicial abelian group** $K(A, n)$.

By the Dold-Kan dictionary, its homotopy groups equal the homology of the chain complex:

$$
\pi_k(\Gamma A[n]) = H_k(A[n]) = \begin{cases} A & k = n \\ 0 & k \neq n. \end{cases}
$$

So $\Gamma A[n] = K(A, n)$, the simplicial model of the Eilenberg-MacLane space whose realization is the topological $K(A, n)$. Explicitly, $\Gamma A[n]$ in simplicial degree $m$ is a sum of copies of $A$ indexed by the surjections $[m] \twoheadrightarrow [n]$ in $\Delta$ — the degeneracy operators of the simplex category. This is the canonical example showing Dold-Kan converts the one-term chain complex into the building block of all homology. May §22, §23.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).** *$\pi_*$ of a simplicial abelian group is its normalized homology.*

Prove that for a simplicial abelian group $A$, the homotopy groups $\pi_n(A)$ (defined simplicially via the Kan condition) coincide with the homology of the normalized chain complex $H_n(N A)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Every simplicial abelian group is a Kan complex. Identify the cycles and boundaries of $N A$ with simplicial homotopy classes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Step 1: $A$ is a Kan complex.** A simplicial abelian group satisfies the Kan condition: any horn $\Lambda^n_k \to A$ extends, because one can solve the horn-filling equations linearly using the group structure and the simplicial identities (Moore's theorem). So the simplicial homotopy groups $\pi_n(A, 0)$ are defined.

**Step 2: identify cycles.** An element of $\pi_n(A)$ is represented by an $n$-simplex $\alpha$ with all faces zero, i.e. $d_i \alpha = 0$ for all $i$. Such $\alpha$ lies in $N_n A = \bigcap_{i \ge 1}\ker d_i$ and additionally has $d_0 \alpha = 0$ — exactly a cycle of the normalized complex (whose differential is $d_0$ on $N_*$).

**Step 3: identify the relation.** Two such representatives $\alpha, \alpha'$ are simplicially homotopic iff their difference $\alpha - \alpha'$ is $d_0$ of a normalized $(n+1)$-chain — exactly a boundary in $N_* A$. The homotopy is built from an $(n+1)$-simplex whose faces encode the homotopy, which the linearity again lets one normalize.

Hence $\pi_n(A) = \ker(d_0: N_n A \to N_{n-1} A)/\mathrm{im}(d_0: N_{n+1} A \to N_n A) = H_n(N A)$. The group structure on $\pi_n$ matches the additive structure on homology. This is the bridge theorem: simplicial homotopy of an abelian object = chain homology. May §17, §22.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *The bar construction of a group.*

Describe the bar construction $B_\bullet G$ of a (discrete) group $G$ as a simplicial set, and show its realization $BG = |B_\bullet G|$ is a $K(G, 1)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$B_n G = G^n$, with face maps multiplying or dropping adjacent entries. This is the nerve of $G$ as a one-object groupoid.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The **bar construction** $B_\bullet G$ has $n$-simplices $B_n G = G^n = \{[g_1 | g_2 | \cdots | g_n]\}$ (tuples of group elements, "bars" separating them). The face maps are

$$
d_i [g_1|\cdots|g_n] = \begin{cases}
[g_2|\cdots|g_n] & i = 0 \\
[g_1|\cdots|g_i g_{i+1}|\cdots|g_n] & 0 < i < n \\
[g_1|\cdots|g_{n-1}] & i = n,
\end{cases}
$$

and degeneracies insert an identity element. This is exactly the nerve $N G$ of $G$ regarded as a one-object groupoid.

**$BG$ is a $K(G,1)$.** Since $G$ is a groupoid (every element invertible), $N G = B_\bullet G$ is a Kan complex. Its realization is connected with $\pi_1(BG) = G$ (loops are words in the generators, relations are the $2$-simplices recording multiplication). The higher homotopy vanishes: the universal cover of $BG$ is the realization of the contractible simplicial set $E_\bullet G$ (with $E_n G = G^{n+1}$), so $\pi_n(BG) = 0$ for $n \ge 2$. Hence $BG \simeq K(G, 1)$, the classifying space.

The bar construction generalizes to a two-sided bar $B(M, G, N)$ for a monoid $G$ acting on $M, N$, the universal device for homotopy quotients and classifying spaces of group actions. May §29-§31.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).** *Homology of the bar construction is group homology.*

Show that the homology of the chain complex of the bar construction $B_\bullet G$ computes the group homology $H_*(G; \mathbb{Z})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The normalized chains on $B_\bullet G$ form the standard bar resolution computing $\mathrm{Tor}^{\mathbb{Z}G}_*(\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Z})$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Apply the free-abelian-group functor $\mathbb{Z}[-]$ to the bar construction to get a simplicial abelian group $\mathbb{Z}[B_\bullet G]$, then take its associated (normalized) chain complex $C_*$. By definition $H_*(BG; \mathbb{Z}) = H_*(C_*)$.

The normalized complex of $\mathbb{Z}[B_\bullet G]$ is precisely the **standard (inhomogeneous) bar resolution**: $C_n = \mathbb{Z}[G^n]$ with differential $\partial = \sum_i (-1)^i d_i$, where the $d_i$ are the bar face maps of Exercise 4. This is the canonical free resolution of $\mathbb{Z}$ as a $\mathbb{Z}G$-module with the identity $G$-action, after tensoring down with $\mathbb{Z}$ over $\mathbb{Z}G$. Therefore

$$
H_n(BG; \mathbb{Z}) = H_n(C_*) = \mathrm{Tor}^{\mathbb{Z}G}_n(\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Z}) = H_n(G; \mathbb{Z}),
$$

the group homology of $G$. Dually, cohomology of $BG$ is group cohomology $H^*(G; \mathbb{Z})$.

This is the topological incarnation of group (co)homology: the classifying space $BG = K(G,1)$ has (co)homology equal to the algebraically defined group (co)homology, and the bar construction is the explicit chain-level bridge. May §29; Weibel §8.4.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, numeric).** *Shuffle map on low-degree simplices.*

The Eilenberg-Zilber shuffle map $\nabla: C_p(X) \otimes C_q(Y) \to C_{p+q}(X \times Y)$ sends a tensor of simplices to a signed sum of $(p,q)$-shuffles. Write $\nabla$ explicitly for $p = q = 1$, the product of two $1$-simplices.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A $(1,1)$-shuffle of a square is its two triangles. The two $(1,1)$-shuffles of $[01]$ are $(0)(1)$ orderings of the two degeneracy directions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $p = q = 1$, the shuffle map sends $x \otimes y$ (with $x \in C_1 X$, $y \in C_1 Y$) to a sum over the two $(1,1)$-shuffles, which triangulate the square $\Delta^1 \times \Delta^1$ into two $2$-simplices:

$$
\nabla(x \otimes y) = (s_1 x \times s_0 y) - (s_0 x \times s_1 y),
$$

where $s_0, s_1$ are the degeneracies producing the two prisms over the edges, and the sign is the shuffle sign $\mathrm{sgn}(\mu)$. Geometrically: the square with corners $(x_0,y_0), (x_1,y_0), (x_0,y_1), (x_1,y_1)$ splits along a diagonal into two triangles, and $\nabla$ records this triangulation with the standard orientation signs.

In general $\nabla(x \otimes y) = \sum_{(\mu,\nu)} \mathrm{sgn}(\mu,\nu)\, (s_\nu x \times s_\mu y)$ summed over $(p,q)$-shuffles $(\mu,\nu)$ of $\{0,\ldots,p+q-1\}$. The shuffle map is a chain map and, paired with the Alexander-Whitney map, gives the Eilenberg-Zilber equivalence. May §29.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).** *The Eilenberg-Zilber theorem.*

State the Eilenberg-Zilber theorem and prove it via acyclic models, identifying the two natural chain-homotopy-inverse maps.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The shuffle map $\nabla$ and the Alexander-Whitney map $AW$ are chain-homotopy inverse. Acyclic models supplies the homotopies because both sides agree on representables and are acyclic in positive degrees.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Theorem (Eilenberg-Zilber).** For simplicial sets (or spaces) $X, Y$, the chain complexes $C_*(X \times Y)$ and $C_*(X) \otimes C_*(Y)$ are naturally chain-homotopy equivalent, via the **Alexander-Whitney map** $AW: C_*(X\times Y) \to C_*(X) \otimes C_*(Y)$ and the **shuffle map** $\nabla: C_*(X) \otimes C_*(Y) \to C_*(X \times Y)$, which are mutually chain-homotopy inverse: $AW \circ \nabla = \mathrm{id}$ and $\nabla \circ AW \simeq \mathrm{id}$.

The Alexander-Whitney map is the "front face $\otimes$ back face" formula

$$
AW(\sigma) = \sum_{p+q=n} (d_{p+1}\cdots d_n\,\sigma) \otimes (d_0^p\, \sigma),
$$

and $\nabla$ is the shuffle map (Exercise 6).

**Proof by acyclic models.** Both functors $F = C_*(- \times -)$ and $G = C_*(-) \otimes C_*(-)$ from $\mathbf{sSet} \times \mathbf{sSet}$ to chain complexes are **free on the models** $\{(\Delta^p, \Delta^q)\}$ (their chains are generated by the images of the universal simplices) and **acyclic on models** (the chains of $\Delta^p \times \Delta^q$ and of $C_*\Delta^p \otimes C_*\Delta^q$ are both contractible above degree $0$, since products and tensors of simplices are contractible). The acyclic-models theorem then guarantees:
1. natural chain maps $F \to G$ and $G \to F$ extending the identity in degree $0$ exist and are unique up to natural chain homotopy;
2. any two such composites are naturally chain-homotopic to the identity.

So $AW$ and $\nabla$, being two such natural maps that are the identity in degree $0$, are forced to be chain-homotopy inverse. This is the prototypical acyclic-models argument and the technical foundation of the Künneth theorem and the cup product. May §29; the acyclic-models machinery of unit 03.12.34.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Dold-Kan is an equivalence: the unit and counit.*

Sketch the proof that $N: \mathbf{sAb} \to \mathrm{Ch}_{\ge 0}$ and $\Gamma$ are inverse equivalences, by describing the natural isomorphisms $N\Gamma \cong \mathrm{id}$ and $\Gamma N \cong \mathrm{id}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\Gamma C$ in degree $n$ is $\bigoplus_{[n]\twoheadrightarrow[k]} C_k$. Show $N\Gamma C \cong C$ by isolating the non-degenerate summand, and $\Gamma N A \cong A$ via the normalization splitting.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**The functor $\Gamma$.** For a chain complex $C$, define $(\Gamma C)_n = \bigoplus_{[n] \twoheadrightarrow [k]} C_k$, the sum over surjections in $\Delta$ (equivalently, over iterated degeneracies). Face and degeneracy maps are defined by the simplicial-identity bookkeeping: degeneracies act by reindexing the surjections, and faces use the differential of $C$ on the "non-degenerate" summand.

**$N\Gamma C \cong C$.** The normalized complex picks out exactly the summand indexed by the identity surjection $[n] = [n]$, namely $C_n$, killing all properly-degenerate summands. The induced differential on this summand is the differential of $C$ (the $d_0$ on the normalized part reproduces $\partial^C$). So $N\Gamma C = C$ naturally.

**$\Gamma N A \cong A$.** By the normalization theorem (lead exercise), $A_n = \bigoplus_{[n]\twoheadrightarrow[k]} N_k A$: every element of $A_n$ decomposes uniquely as a sum over its degeneracies of normalized elements (the splitting $C_* A = N_* A \oplus D_* A$ iterated). This decomposition is exactly the formula defining $(\Gamma N A)_n$, and it is compatible with faces and degeneracies. So $\Gamma N A \cong A$ naturally.

Both composites are the identity up to natural isomorphism, so $N$ and $\Gamma$ are inverse equivalences $\mathbf{sAb} \simeq \mathrm{Ch}_{\ge 0}(\mathbf{Ab})$. The same argument works with $\mathbf{Ab}$ replaced by any abelian category. May §22; Goerss-Jardine III.2.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *Künneth via Eilenberg-Zilber.*

Use the Eilenberg-Zilber theorem to derive the Künneth short exact sequence for $H_*(X \times Y)$ in terms of $H_*(X)$ and $H_*(Y)$ over a PID.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Eilenberg-Zilber gives $C_*(X\times Y) \simeq C_*(X)\otimes C_*(Y)$; apply the algebraic Künneth theorem for the tensor product of chain complexes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the Eilenberg-Zilber theorem (Exercise 7), there is a natural chain-homotopy equivalence $C_*(X \times Y) \simeq C_*(X) \otimes C_*(Y)$, hence an isomorphism on homology:

$$
H_n(X \times Y) \cong H_n\big(C_*(X) \otimes C_*(Y)\big).
$$

Now apply the **algebraic Künneth theorem** for a tensor product of chain complexes of flat (e.g. free) modules over a PID $R$: there is a natural short exact sequence

$$
0 \to \bigoplus_{i+j=n} H_i(X) \otimes_R H_j(Y) \to H_n(X \times Y) \to \bigoplus_{i+j=n-1} \mathrm{Tor}^R_1(H_i(X), H_j(Y)) \to 0,
$$

and the sequence splits (non-naturally). The first term is the "naive" tensor product of homologies; the Tor term measures the failure of the tensor product of homology to compute the homology of the tensor product, exactly the torsion-interaction correction.

So the homological Künneth formula is the composite of two facts: Eilenberg-Zilber (a geometric/simplicial statement, proved by acyclic models) and the algebraic Künneth theorem (pure homological algebra over a PID). Over a field $R = k$, Tor vanishes and one gets the clean $H_n(X\times Y) = \bigoplus_{i+j=n} H_i(X)\otimes H_j(Y)$. May §29; Weibel §3.6.

</details>

</aside>
