## Intuition [Beginner]

A *covering space* of a space $X$ is a "stack of pancakes" sitting above $X$ — another space $\widetilde{X}$ together with a continuous projection $\widetilde{X} \to X$ that, locally over every point of $X$, looks like a stack of disjoint copies of a small neighbourhood.

The simplest example: the real line $\mathbb{R}$ is a covering space of the circle $S^1$, with the projection $\mathbb{R} \to S^1$, $t \mapsto e^{2\pi i t}$. Each point of the circle has a neighbourhood whose preimage in $\mathbb{R}$ is a disjoint union of intervals, one per integer "winding number." The line covers the circle infinitely many times.

Covering spaces are the geometric realisation of $\pi_1$: connected covers of $X$ correspond bijectively (the *Galois correspondence*) to subgroups of $\pi_1(X, x_0)$. The identity subgroup corresponds to the *universal cover* — the largest, simply connected cover. Every other connected cover sits between $X$ and its universal cover.

## Visual [Beginner]

A blob $X$ at the bottom; multiple sheets above it, each a copy of $X$, with a projection arrow downward. Locally each point of $X$ has a "stack" of preimages.

![A topological space X at the bottom with multiple covering sheets above it, projecting down via a covering map. The preimage of every point is a discrete set of points, one per sheet.](/img/covering-space-placeholder.svg)

The number of sheets is the *degree* of the cover. For $\mathbb{R} \to S^1$ this is countably infinite; for the standard double cover $S^n \to \mathbb{RP}^n$ it is two; the universal cover may have any cardinality up to $|\pi_1(X)|$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The standard exponential map $\exp : \mathbb{R} \to S^1$, $t \mapsto e^{2\pi i t}$, is a covering. For each point $z \in S^1$, the preimage $\exp^{-1}(z) = \{t \in \mathbb{R} : e^{2\pi i t} = z\}$ is a discrete set $\{t_0, t_0 + 1, t_0 + 2, \ldots\} \cup \{t_0 - 1, t_0 - 2, \ldots\}$ — an arithmetic progression of step 1, indexed by $\mathbb{Z}$.

Locally, a small arc around $z \in S^1$ has preimage equal to a disjoint union of small intervals around each $t_0 + n$. The covering projection restricted to each interval is a homeomorphism onto the arc.

This makes $\mathbb{R}$ the universal cover of $S^1$: it is simply connected (contractible, in fact), and the deck transformations — the homeomorphisms of $\mathbb{R}$ commuting with $\exp$ — are translations by integers, forming a group isomorphic to $\pi_1(S^1) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.

For the sphere $S^n$ ($n \geq 2$), the antipodal map $S^n \to \mathbb{RP}^n$ is a 2-fold cover. The deck transformation group is $\mathbb{Z}/2$, matching $\pi_1(\mathbb{RP}^n) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2$ for $n \geq 2$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a topological space [02.01.01]. A **covering map** is a continuous surjection $p : \widetilde{X} \to X$ such that every point $x \in X$ has an open neighbourhood $U$ with the property:

$$
p^{-1}(U) = \bigsqcup_{\alpha \in A} V_\alpha,
$$

a disjoint union of open subsets $V_\alpha \subseteq \widetilde{X}$ such that the restriction $p|_{V_\alpha} : V_\alpha \to U$ is a homeomorphism for every $\alpha$. Such a $U$ is **evenly covered**, and the $V_\alpha$ are the *sheets* over $U$. The cardinality $|A|$ is the **degree** or *number of sheets* (locally constant on $X$ if $X$ is connected).

The space $\widetilde{X}$ is a **covering space** of $X$ via the covering map $p$.

A covering map is **path-connected** when $\widetilde X$ is path-connected. A path-connected covering map $p : \widetilde X \to X$ is a **universal cover** when $\widetilde X$ is simply connected.

The **path-lifting property**: for any continuous path $\gamma : [0, 1] \to X$ and any lift $\widetilde{x_0} \in p^{-1}(\gamma(0))$ of the starting point, there is a unique continuous lift $\widetilde{\gamma} : [0, 1] \to \widetilde{X}$ with $p \circ \widetilde{\gamma} = \gamma$ and $\widetilde{\gamma}(0) = \widetilde{x_0}$.

The **homotopy-lifting property** generalises this: for any continuous family of paths (a homotopy) $H : [0, 1] \times [0, 1] \to X$ and any lift of an initial path, the homotopy lifts uniquely to $\widetilde{X}$.

A **deck transformation** of a covering $p : \widetilde{X} \to X$ is a homeomorphism $\varphi : \widetilde{X} \to \widetilde{X}$ with $p \circ \varphi = p$. The deck transformations form a group $\mathrm{Deck}(p)$ under composition, acting on $\widetilde{X}$ by permuting the sheets.

For the universal cover $\widetilde{X} \to X$ of a path-connected space (with reasonable local conditions), $\mathrm{Deck}(\widetilde{X} / X) \cong \pi_1(X, x_0)$ canonically.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (path lifting).** *Let $p : \widetilde{X} \to X$ be a covering map, $\gamma : [0, 1] \to X$ a continuous path, and $\widetilde{x_0} \in p^{-1}(\gamma(0))$. Then there exists a unique continuous lift $\widetilde{\gamma} : [0, 1] \to \widetilde{X}$ with $\widetilde{\gamma}(0) = \widetilde{x_0}$ and $p \circ \widetilde{\gamma} = \gamma$.*

**Proof.** *Existence.* The image $\gamma([0, 1])$ is compact. Cover $X$ by evenly-covered open sets; pull back to an open cover of $[0, 1]$ via $\gamma$. By Lebesgue's number lemma, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that any subinterval of $[0, 1]$ of length $< \delta$ is mapped by $\gamma$ entirely into a single evenly-covered open set. Choose a partition $0 = t_0 < t_1 < \ldots < t_n = 1$ with $t_{i+1} - t_i < \delta$.

Inductively define the lift on $[t_i, t_{i+1}]$: at $t_0 = 0$, $\widetilde{\gamma}(0) := \widetilde{x_0}$. Suppose $\widetilde{\gamma}$ is defined on $[0, t_i]$ with $\widetilde{\gamma}(t_i) \in \widetilde{X}$. Let $U_i \ni \gamma([t_i, t_{i+1}])$ be evenly covered, and let $V_i$ be the unique sheet over $U_i$ containing $\widetilde{\gamma}(t_i)$. Define $\widetilde{\gamma}|_{[t_i, t_{i+1}]} := (p|_{V_i})^{-1} \circ \gamma|_{[t_i, t_{i+1}]}$. This is continuous (composition of continuous maps).

The pieces glue continuously at each $t_i$ because the formula at $t_i$ from both sides agrees. So $\widetilde{\gamma} : [0, 1] \to \widetilde{X}$ is the desired lift.

*Uniqueness.* If $\widetilde{\gamma}_1, \widetilde{\gamma}_2$ are two lifts with $\widetilde{\gamma}_1(0) = \widetilde{\gamma}_2(0) = \widetilde{x_0}$, the set $A := \{t \in [0, 1] : \widetilde{\gamma}_1(t) = \widetilde{\gamma}_2(t)\}$ is non-empty (contains $0$), open (if $\widetilde{\gamma}_1(t) = \widetilde{\gamma}_2(t) =: \widetilde{x}$, the unique sheet over a neighbourhood of $\gamma(t)$ containing $\widetilde{x}$ contains both lifts on a neighbourhood of $t$), and closed (limit points of agreement). So $A = [0, 1]$. $\square$

The same argument with two parameters proves homotopy lifting; the lifted homotopy is unique given a chosen lift of one initial path.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [03.05.05] (double cover), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [03.09.04] (spin structure on an oriented riemannian manifold). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

What is the degree of the antipodal cover $S^n \to \mathbb{RP}^n$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each point of projective space corresponds to a pair of antipodal points on the sphere.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**2.** Each $[v] \in \mathbb{RP}^n$ has preimage $\{v, -v\} \subset S^n$ — exactly two points. The cover is 2-fold.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).**

Show that a covering map is open.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the local-homeomorphism property on each sheet.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $p : \widetilde{X} \to X$ be a covering and $W \subseteq \widetilde{X}$ open. For each $\widetilde{x} \in W$, the point $p(\widetilde{x})$ has an evenly-covered open neighbourhood $U$. Let $V$ be the sheet over $U$ containing $\widetilde{x}$. Then $W \cap V$ is open in $V$, and $p|_V$ is a homeomorphism, so $p(W \cap V)$ is open in $U$, hence open in $X$. The image $p(W) = \bigcup_{\widetilde{x} \in W} p(W \cap V_{\widetilde{x}})$ is a union of opens, hence open.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the deck transformation group of the covering $\mathbb{R} \to S^1$, $t \mapsto e^{2\pi i t}$, is $\mathbb{Z}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A deck transformation $\varphi$ satisfies $\exp \circ \varphi = \exp$, so $\varphi(t) - t \in \mathbb{Z}$ for all $t$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A deck transformation $\varphi : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfies $e^{2\pi i \varphi(t)} = e^{2\pi i t}$, equivalently $\varphi(t) - t \in \mathbb{Z}$ for every $t$. Continuity of $\varphi - \mathrm{id}_{\mathbb{R}}$ valued in $\mathbb{Z}$ (a discrete space) forces this difference to be locally constant, hence constant since $\mathbb{R}$ is connected. So $\varphi(t) = t + n$ for some integer $n$. Conversely, every translation by an integer is a deck transformation. So $\mathrm{Deck}(\mathbb{R} \to S^1) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, conceptual).**

State the Galois correspondence between subgroups of $\pi_1(X, x_0)$ and connected covers of $X$ (with mild local conditions on $X$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Subgroups of $\pi_1$ index connected covers, with the identity subgroup giving the universal cover.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a path-connected, locally path-connected, semi-locally simply connected space $X$ with basepoint $x_0$, there is a bijection between:

- Conjugacy classes of subgroups $H \leq \pi_1(X, x_0)$, and
- Isomorphism classes of based connected covers $p : (\widetilde{X}, \widetilde{x_0}) \to (X, x_0)$.

The bijection sends a subgroup $H$ to the cover whose induced map $p_\ast : \pi_1(\widetilde{X}, \widetilde{x_0}) \to \pi_1(X, x_0)$ has image $H$. The identity subgroup $H = 0$ corresponds to the universal cover; the full group $H = \pi_1(X)$ corresponds to $X$ itself.

This is a topological analogue of Galois correspondence between subgroups of a Galois group and intermediate field extensions.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Show that the lift of a loop in $X$ under a covering need not be a loop in $\widetilde{X}$, and exhibit this explicitly for $\mathbb{R} \to S^1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Lift the loop $\gamma(t) = e^{2\pi i t}$ around the circle.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The loop $\gamma : [0, 1] \to S^1$ given by $\gamma(t) = e^{2\pi i t}$ has lift $\widetilde{\gamma}(t) = t$ starting at $0 \in \mathbb{R}$. The endpoint $\widetilde{\gamma}(1) = 1 \neq 0 = \widetilde{\gamma}(0)$. So the lift is not a loop. The endpoint difference $1 - 0 = 1$ is the *winding number* of the original loop, recording $[\gamma] = 1 \in \pi_1(S^1) \cong \mathbb{Z}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Prove that a path-connected covering space $\widetilde{X} \to X$ has $\pi_1(\widetilde{X})$ injecting into $\pi_1(X)$ as a subgroup.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use homotopy lifting + uniqueness of lifts to show injectivity of $p_\ast$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $\widetilde{\gamma} : (S^1, *) \to (\widetilde{X}, \widetilde{x_0})$ is a loop with $p_\ast([\widetilde{\gamma}]) = 0$ in $\pi_1(X, x_0)$. Then $p \circ \widetilde{\gamma}$ is null-homotopic in $X$ via some $H : [0, 1]^2 \to X$ with $H(\cdot, 0) = p \circ \widetilde{\gamma}$ and $H(\cdot, 1) = $ constant. By the homotopy-lifting property, $H$ lifts uniquely to $\widetilde{H} : [0, 1]^2 \to \widetilde{X}$ with $\widetilde{H}(\cdot, 0) = \widetilde{\gamma}$. Continuity of $\widetilde{H}$ on $[0, 1] \times \{1\}$ valued in the discrete fibre forces this slice to be constant. So $\widetilde{\gamma}$ is null-homotopic in $\widetilde{X}$, and $[\widetilde{\gamma}] = 0$ in $\pi_1(\widetilde{X})$. Hence $p_\ast$ is injective.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Why does a simply connected space have only the identity covering (up to isomorphism)?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the Galois correspondence and the fact that the identity subgroup gives the universal cover.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $X$ is simply connected, $\pi_1(X) = 0$. By the Galois correspondence, the only subgroup of the identity group is itself, corresponding to a unique connected cover — namely $X \to X$, the identity. So $X$ admits no nontrivial connected covers.

(Disconnected covers of $X$ are still possible, but they decompose as disjoint unions of copies of $X$, each mapping by the identity. So even those are uninteresting.)

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Existence of universal covers.** A path-connected, locally path-connected, semi-locally simply connected space admits a universal cover, unique up to isomorphism. The construction: take $\widetilde{X} := \{[\gamma] : \gamma$ is a path in $X$ starting at $x_0\}$, where $[\gamma]$ is the homotopy class with fixed endpoints. The projection sends $[\gamma]$ to its endpoint. The topology is generated by basic opens determined by the local-simple-connectedness condition.

**Galois correspondence.** Stated in Exercise 4. Conjugacy classes of subgroups $H \leq \pi_1(X, x_0)$ ↔ isomorphism classes of connected covers. Normal subgroups correspond to *normal covers*, where the deck transformation group acts transitively on each fibre. For normal covers, $\mathrm{Deck}(\widetilde{X}/X) \cong \pi_1(X)/H$.

**Quotient by free actions.** A free properly discontinuous action of a group $G$ on a simply connected space $\widetilde{X}$ yields a covering $\widetilde{X} \to \widetilde{X}/G$ with deck group $G$ and base fundamental group $G$. Many examples: $\mathbb{R}^n \to T^n$ via $\mathbb{Z}^n$-action, $S^n \to \mathbb{RP}^n$ via $\mathbb{Z}/2$-action, $S^3 \to \mathrm{SO}(3)$ via the $\mathbb{Z}/2$ centre of $\mathrm{Spin}(3)$.

**Lifting criterion.** Given a covering $p : \widetilde{X} \to X$ and a continuous map $f : Y \to X$ from a path-connected, locally path-connected $Y$, there is a continuous lift $\widetilde{f} : Y \to \widetilde{X}$ iff $f_\ast(\pi_1(Y, y_0)) \subseteq p_\ast(\pi_1(\widetilde{X}, \widetilde{x_0}))$. The lift, when it exists, is unique once a lift of one point is fixed.

**Spin double covers.** The cover $\mathrm{Spin}(n) \to \mathrm{SO}(n)$ for $n \geq 3$ is a connected double cover with deck group $\mathbb{Z}/2$. The corresponding subgroup of $\pi_1(\mathrm{SO}(n)) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2$ is the identity subgroup, making $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$ the universal cover of $\mathrm{SO}(n)$. This is the topological foundation of spin structures [03.09.04].

**Connection to algebraic topology.** Covering spaces are the topological avatars of Galois extensions in number theory; the Galois correspondence is precisely analogous. In the language of $\infty$-categories, covers of $X$ are objects of the "étale topos" of $X$, and the Galois correspondence is a special case of étale-fundamental-groupoid recovery.

**Synthesis.** Covering spaces are the topological embodiment of the fundamental group: connected covers of a reasonably nice space $X$ correspond bijectively to subgroups of $\pi_1(X)$ via the Galois correspondence, the universal cover $\widetilde{X}$ is the largest simply connected cover from which all others are quotiented, the deck transformation group of $\widetilde{X} \to X$ is isomorphic to $\pi_1(X)$, and the unique path-lifting property makes covering spaces the geometric tool for converting homotopy information into topological constructions. In the smooth setting, the spin group [03.09.03] arises as the universal cover of $\mathrm{SO}(n)$ for $n \geq 3$, and spin structures [03.09.04] on a manifold are lifts of the frame bundle's $\mathrm{SO}(n)$-structure to a $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$-structure — a covering-space problem at the level of principal bundles. The classification of covering spaces by $\pi_1$ is the prototype for the classifying-space correspondence [03.08.04] between bundles and homotopy classes of maps.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Path-lifting.** Proved in §"Key theorem".

**Homotopy-lifting.** Apply the Lebesgue-number argument in $[0, 1] \times [0, 1]$ with the partition refinement done in both coordinates. The two-dimensional cells are evenly-covered, and a uniqueness argument with the "agree on boundary" condition forces the lift to be unique once an initial path's lift is chosen.

**Existence of universal cover (sketch).** Define $\widetilde{X}$ as homotopy classes of paths starting at $x_0$; topology by basic opens. Verify that the natural projection $\widetilde{X} \to X$ is a covering, that $\widetilde{X}$ is simply connected (homotopy classes of paths starting at $x_0$ form a tree-like structure), and that the deck group acts faithfully and transitively on fibres.

**Galois correspondence (sketch).** Given a subgroup $H \leq \pi_1(X, x_0)$, construct the cover $X_H := \widetilde{X}/H$ where $H$ acts via deck transformations. Verify that $\pi_1(X_H, [\widetilde{x_0}]) \cong H$ under the natural map. Conversely, given a connected cover $\widetilde{X} \to X$, take $H := p_\ast(\pi_1(\widetilde{X}, \widetilde{x_0}))$. The two assignments are inverse up to base-point conjugacy.

**$\mathrm{Spin}(n)$ as universal cover for $n \geq 3$.** $\pi_1(\mathrm{SO}(n)) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2$ for $n \geq 3$ (a classical computation using the long exact sequence of the fibration $\mathrm{SO}(n-1) \to \mathrm{SO}(n) \to S^{n-1}$). The connected double cover is $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$, with the identity subgroup of $\pi_1$. By the Galois correspondence, $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$ is the universal cover.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Riemann's notion of "Riemann surface" (1851) was the first systematic encounter with covering spaces — multi-valued holomorphic functions like $\sqrt{z}$ and $\log z$ resolve to single-valued functions on appropriate covers of the punctured plane. Poincaré's *Analysis Situs* (1895) introduced the universal cover of an arbitrary topological space and recognised the deck group as the fundamental group.

The Galois correspondence between covers and subgroups of $\pi_1$ — a topological analogue of Galois's theory of field extensions — was developed in the early twentieth century through the work of Schreier (1927) and others. It made covering-space theory the prototype for fundamental groupoids, étale fundamental groups in algebraic geometry (Grothendieck's SGA), and the modern theory of $\infty$-groupoids.

In physics, double covers play a role wherever spinor representations appear: $\mathrm{Spin}(n) \to \mathrm{SO}(n)$ is the source of spin-1/2 fermions, and the covering-space topology underlies why spin structures exist in some manifolds and not others. The Aharonov-Bohm effect realises in physics the holonomy of the universal cover.
