## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Bott-Tu Chapter III §19 introduces Sullivan's minimal-model machinery as the differential-form approach to rational homotopy theory. Several Bott-Tu exercises and named computations are not anchored in a single Babel Bible unit but instead cross-cut the minimal-model construction, the rational Hurewicz theorem, the Whitehead-tower spectral sequence, and the Sullivan-Halperin algorithm for fibrations. This pack collects eight such exercises — two easy, four medium, two hard — each with a hint and full solution.

The pack is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units N12 (`03.12.06` Sullivan minimal models) and N14 (`03.12.07` Whitehead tower) rather than as a standalone development. The exercises are loosely grouped: the easy ones drill the minimal-model algorithm on canonical spaces ($S^{2k}$, $\mathbb{C}P^n$); the medium ones run computations of fibration models and rational Hurewicz applications; the hard ones recover Sullivan's solution to Serre's question on $\pi_*(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ and the formality theorem of Deligne-Griffiths-Morgan-Sullivan for compact Kähler manifolds.

The conventions follow Bott-Tu and Félix-Halperin-Thomas: all spaces are simply-connected of finite rational type; minimal models are over $\mathbb{Q}$, with the differential satisfying $d V \subseteq \Lambda^{\geq 2} V$; the path-space symbol is $PX$ (notation decision #32). Coefficients are rational unless otherwise specified.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one canonical exercise in full as an exemplar of the format.

**Lead exercise.** *Compute the minimal model of $S^4$ and read off $\pi_*(S^4) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$.*

**Solution.** Apply the inductive construction of N12 to the polynomial-form algebra of $S^4$.

Step 1. In degree $4$: $H^4(S^4; \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}$, so add a generator $x \in V^4$ with $dx = 0$ and $\varphi(x) = $ volume class. This gives $\Lambda(x)$ surjecting on cohomology in degree $4$.

Step 2. Spurious cohomology in degree $8$: $\Lambda(x)$ has $x^2 \in $ degree $8$ closed but not represented in $H^8(S^4) = 0$. Add a generator $y \in V^7$ with $dy = x^2$ and $\varphi(y) \in A_{PL}(S^4)^7$ chosen so that $d \varphi(y) = \varphi(x)^2$.

Step 3. Verify cohomology of $\Lambda(x, y)$ matches $H^*(S^4; \mathbb{Q})$. Closed elements:
- degree $0$: $\mathbb{Q}$ (constants);
- degree $4$: $\mathbb{Q} \cdot x$;
- degree $7$: $\mathbb{Q} \cdot y$ (closed by $dy = x^2 \neq 0$, but $y$ itself is *not* closed; so this row is empty);
- degree $8$: $\mathbb{Q} \cdot x^2$, but $x^2 = dy$ is exact, so cohomology vanishes.

Continuing: degree $11$ has $xy$ closed (since $d(xy) = -x \cdot x^2 = -x^3$ exact iff $x^3$ is hit, which it is by $d(x y) = -x^3$ if $x y$ is closed... actually $d(xy) = dx \cdot y - x \cdot dy = 0 - x \cdot x^2 = -x^3$, so $xy$ is *not* closed). Cohomology in degrees $\geq 4$: only $\mathbb{Q}$ in degree $4$ survives, all higher classes cancel.

Final cohomology: $\mathbb{Q}$ in degrees $0$ and $4$, zero elsewhere — matching $S^4$.

Read off rational homotopy: $V^4 = \mathbb{Q} \cdot x$ gives $\pi_4(S^4) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (the fundamental class); $V^7 = \mathbb{Q} \cdot y$ gives $\pi_7(S^4) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (the rational Whitehead bracket $[\iota_4, \iota_4]$); all other $V^n = 0$, so $\pi_n(S^4) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = 0$ for $n \neq 4, 7$.

Sanity check via Serre's finiteness: $\pi_k(S^4)$ is finite for $k > 4$, except $\pi_7(S^4) = \mathbb{Z} \oplus \pi_7(S^4)_{\mathrm{tor}}$ where $\pi_7(S^4)_{\mathrm{tor}}$ is finite. The rank-one summand corresponds to the Hopf map $\nu : S^7 \to S^4$ with Hopf invariant one. $\square$

This computation is the canonical illustration of the Sullivan apparatus: the minimal-model algorithm is mechanical, and once one knows $S^{2k}$ has minimal model $\Lambda(x, y)$ with $dy = x^2$, the rational homotopy is read directly off $V$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).** *Minimal model of $S^{2k}$.*

Generalise the lead exercise. Compute the minimal model of $S^{2k}$ for $k \geq 1$ and identify $\pi_*(S^{2k}) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Replace $\deg x = 4$ with $\deg x = 2k$ and $\deg y = 7$ with $\deg y = 4k - 1$ in the lead exercise.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The minimal model is $\Lambda(x, y)$ with $\deg x = 2k$, $\deg y = 4k - 1$, $dx = 0$, $dy = x^2$. Cohomology computation parallel to the lead exercise: closed-and-exact bookkeeping cancels everything in degrees $> 2k$, leaving cohomology $\mathbb{Q}$ in degrees $0$ and $2k$ only — matching $S^{2k}$.

Rational homotopy: $\pi_{2k}(S^{2k}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (from $x$) and $\pi_{4k - 1}(S^{2k}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (from $y$, the rational Whitehead bracket), with all other rational homotopy zero. This reproduces Serre's 1953 rational answer for even spheres. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).** *Minimal model of $\mathbb{C}P^n$.*

Compute the minimal model of $\mathbb{C}P^n$ and read off $\pi_*(\mathbb{C}P^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A degree-$2$ generator $x$ with zero differential gives the polynomial $\mathbb{Q}[x]$ — too much. A second generator $y$ in odd degree truncates correctly.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The minimal model is $\Lambda(x, y)$ with $\deg x = 2$, $\deg y = 2n + 1$, $dx = 0$, $dy = x^{n+1}$. Cohomology: in degrees $\leq 2n$, the algebra computes $\mathbb{Q}[x] / (x^{n+1})$ since $x^{n+1} = dy$ is exact, and beyond that all classes are killed by $d$. Final cohomology: $\mathbb{Q}[x] / (x^{n+1})$ — matching $\mathbb{C}P^n$.

Rational homotopy: $\pi_2(\mathbb{C}P^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (from $x$), $\pi_{2n+1}(\mathbb{C}P^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ (from $y$), all other rational homotopy zero. The $\pi_{2n+1}$ generator descends from the rational generator of $\pi_{2n+1}(S^{2n+1})$ via the Hopf fibration $S^1 \to S^{2n+1} \to \mathbb{C}P^n$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).** *Minimal model of $\Omega S^n$ (Bott-Samelson).*

Compute the minimal model of the based loop space $\Omega S^n$ and recover the Bott-Samelson computation of $H^*(\Omega S^n; \mathbb{Q})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For a simply-connected $X$ with minimal model $(\Lambda V, d)$, the loop-space cohomology $H^*(\Omega X; \mathbb{Q})$ is computed from the *desuspended* algebra $(\Lambda s^{-1} V, 0)$ — free graded-commutative on the desuspended generators with zero differential.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $X = S^n$ with $n \geq 2$:

**Case $n$ odd ($n = 2k + 1$).** Minimal model of $S^{2k+1}$ is $\Lambda(x)$ with $\deg x = 2k + 1$. Desuspension shifts degree by $-1$: $s^{-1} V = \mathbb{Q} \cdot s^{-1} x$ with $\deg s^{-1} x = 2k$. The algebra $\Lambda s^{-1} V = \mathbb{Q}[s^{-1} x]$ is polynomial in one even-degree generator. Hence
$$
H^*(\Omega S^{2k+1}; \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}[\sigma], \qquad \deg \sigma = 2k.
$$
Concretely, $H^*(\Omega S^3) = \mathbb{Q}[\sigma]$ with $\deg \sigma = 2$, matching the classical Bott-Samelson computation.

**Case $n$ even ($n = 2k$).** Minimal model of $S^{2k}$ is $\Lambda(x, y)$ with $\deg x = 2k$, $\deg y = 4k - 1$, $dy = x^2$. The desuspended algebra has generators $s^{-1} x$ (degree $2k - 1$, odd) and $s^{-1} y$ (degree $4k - 2$, even). The differential vanishes (loop-space cohomology). Hence
$$
H^*(\Omega S^{2k}; \mathbb{Q}) = \Lambda(s^{-1} x) \otimes \mathbb{Q}[s^{-1} y] = \Lambda(\tau) \otimes \mathbb{Q}[\sigma],
$$
with $\deg \tau = 2k - 1$, $\deg \sigma = 4k - 2$. The exterior factor on $\tau$ comes from odd-degree graded-commutativity ($\tau^2 = 0$), and the polynomial factor on $\sigma$ is unconstrained even-degree.

Concretely $H^*(\Omega S^2; \mathbb{Q}) = \Lambda(\tau) \otimes \mathbb{Q}[\sigma]$ with $\deg \tau = 1$, $\deg \sigma = 2$ — the Bott-Samelson decomposition of $\Omega S^2$ as $\mathbb{Z} \times S^1 \times \mathbb{C}P^\infty$ rationally. [ref: TODO_REF Bott-Samelson 1958] $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *Rational Hurewicz on a simply-connected space.*

Use Sullivan's main theorem to prove the rational Hurewicz theorem: if $X$ is simply-connected with $\pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = 0$ for $1 \leq i < n$, then $\pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \xrightarrow{\sim} H_i(X; \mathbb{Q})$ is an isomorphism for $1 \leq i \leq 2n - 2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Read off the indecomposable layer of the minimal model. In the relevant degree range, no products of indecomposables can interfere.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Sullivan's main theorem, the indecomposable graded vector space $V$ of the minimal model of $X$ satisfies $V^i \cong (\pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q})^*$ as graded $\mathbb{Q}$-vector spaces. The hypothesis $\pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = 0$ for $1 \leq i < n$ gives $V^i = 0$ for $1 \leq i < n$.

In the minimal model $(\Lambda V, d)$, an arbitrary cohomology class in degree $\leq 2n - 2$ is represented by an element of $\Lambda V$ in that degree. Since the lowest-degree generators of $V$ sit in degree $n$, products of two or more indecomposables sit in degree $\geq 2n$. Hence in degree $\leq 2n - 2$, the only contributions to $\Lambda V$ are linear combinations of single indecomposables — i.e., $V$ itself.

The differential $d$ on $V$ lands in $\Lambda^{\geq 2} V$ by minimality, hence in degree $\geq 2n$. So $d$ vanishes on $V^i$ for $i \leq 2n - 2$, making each generator a closed element. Cohomology in degrees $\leq 2n - 2$:
$$
H^i(\Lambda V) = V^i = (\pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q})^* \quad \text{for } 1 \leq i \leq 2n - 2.
$$

By the quasi-isomorphism $\Lambda V \xrightarrow{\sim} A_{PL}(X)$ and $H^*(A_{PL}(X)) = H^*(X; \mathbb{Q})$, the cohomological side is $H^i(X; \mathbb{Q}) = (\pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q})^*$ in this range. Dualising gives $H_i(X; \mathbb{Q}) = \pi_i(X) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, and the natural Hurewicz map is the isomorphism. $\square$

In degree $2n - 1$, products $V^a \otimes V^b$ with $a + b = 2n - 1$ require $a = n$ and $b = n - 1$ or vice versa — but $V^{n-1} = 0$, so no products appear. Hence the isomorphism extends to degree $2n - 1$ as well; in degree $2n$, products $V^n \otimes V^n$ contribute and the Hurewicz map is only surjective.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).** *Halperin's algorithm for the Hopf fibration.*

Apply Halperin's twisted-tensor model to the Hopf fibration $S^1 \to S^3 \to S^2$ and verify that the resulting minimal model of $S^3$ matches the direct computation $\Lambda(z)$ with $\deg z = 3$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Minimal model of $S^2$ is $\Lambda(x_2, y_3)$ with $dy_3 = x_2^2$; minimal model of $S^1$ is — well, $S^1$ is not simply-connected, so handle the fibration via the universal cover or directly via Halperin's perturbation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Hopf fibration $S^1 \to S^3 \to S^2$ has $S^1$ not simply-connected, so the standard Sullivan-Halperin algorithm does not apply directly. The remedy: pass to the universal cover, which makes the fibration $\mathbb{R} \to S^3 \to S^2$ — but $\mathbb{R}$ is contractible, and then the Halperin model of $S^3$ is just the model of $S^2$ extended by the contractible fibre, which collapses.

A cleaner approach: think of the Hopf fibration as the principal $U(1) = S^1$-bundle on $S^2 = \mathbb{C}P^1$ classified by the universal element $1 \in H^2(S^2)$. The Borel construction $S^3 \times_{U(1)} EU(1)$ is homotopy equivalent to $S^2$ (since $S^3 / U(1) = S^2$), and the universal classifying map $S^2 \to BU(1) = \mathbb{C}P^\infty$ has Halperin model the inclusion $\Lambda(c) \hookrightarrow \Lambda(c, y)/(dy = c)$ where $c \in H^2$ on both sides.

Instead, work directly. Define the perturbed tensor product
$$
M_{\mathrm{Hopf}} = M_{S^2} \otimes \Lambda(z),
$$
with $\deg z = 1$ (the fibre $S^1$ generator) and $dz = -x_2$ (the perturbation $\tau(z) = -x_2$, encoding the universal Euler class). Compute cohomology:

- Closed elements: those with $d = 0$. The differential is $d(z) = -x_2$, $d(x_2) = 0$, $d(y_3) = x_2^2$.
- Closed combinations: $1, z y_3 + ? \cdot ?, \ldots$ — careful bookkeeping.

A direct check: in degree $3$, the closed elements include $x_2 z + y_3$ (since $d(x_2 z) = -x_2^2$ and $d(y_3) = x_2^2$, so $d(x_2 z + y_3) = 0$). This represents the fundamental class $[S^3] \in H^3(S^3; \mathbb{Q})$.

The minimal model produced has only one indecomposable in degree $3$ (after canceling redundant generators by quasi-isomorphism), giving $\Lambda(z')$ with $\deg z' = 3$, $dz' = 0$ — the minimal model of $S^3$, matching Exercise 2 of N12. The Halperin perturbation $\tau$ has correctly computed the model of the total space from the model of the base and the Euler-class data. $\square$

This is the cleanest illustration of Halperin's algorithm: the perturbation $\tau$ encodes the spectral-sequence transgression at the cochain level.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).** *Minimal model of a compact Lie group.*

Compute the minimal model of $SU(3)$ and verify it is the exterior algebra on the primitive cohomology generators.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The cohomology ring of $SU(n)$ is the exterior algebra $H^*(SU(n); \mathbb{Q}) = \Lambda(\alpha_3, \alpha_5, \ldots, \alpha_{2n-1})$ with $\deg \alpha_{2i+1} = 2i + 1$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n - 1$. Compact Lie groups are formal.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $SU(3)$, the rational cohomology ring is $H^*(SU(3); \mathbb{Q}) = \Lambda(\alpha_3, \alpha_5)$ with $\deg \alpha_3 = 3$ and $\deg \alpha_5 = 5$ (the primitive generators). The cohomology is concentrated in degrees $0, 3, 5, 8$, with each degree carrying $\mathbb{Q}$.

**Formality of $SU(3)$.** Compact connected Lie groups are formal: their minimal models are determined by the cohomology ring with no Massey-product corrections. The proof: the Hopf-algebra structure on $H^*(G; \mathbb{Q})$ for a compact Lie group $G$ implies that $H^*(G; \mathbb{Q})$ is generated by primitive elements in odd degrees, which form an exterior algebra. Since odd-degree generators square to zero by graded-commutativity, the algebra has no relations, and the minimal model is just the cohomology ring with zero differential.

Hence $M_{SU(3)} = \Lambda(\alpha_3, \alpha_5)$ with $d \alpha_3 = d \alpha_5 = 0$. Rational homotopy: $\pi_3(SU(3)) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$, $\pi_5(SU(3)) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$, all other rational homotopy zero. This matches Bott periodicity for the unitary group at the level of low rational homotopy. $\square$

The structure generalises: $SU(n)$ has minimal model $\Lambda(\alpha_3, \alpha_5, \ldots, \alpha_{2n-1})$, and rational homotopy concentrated in odd degrees. The associated classifying space $BSU(n)$ has minimal model $\mathbb{Q}[c_2, c_3, \ldots, c_n]$ (polynomial on the loop-space-suspended generators), recovering Borel's presentation $H^*(BSU(n); \mathbb{Q}) = \mathbb{Q}[c_2, \ldots, c_n]$ from the present unit.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).** *Sullivan's solution to Serre's question on $\pi_*(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$.*

Using the minimal models computed in Exercises 1 and 2 of N12 plus Exercise 1 above, recover Serre's 1953 finiteness theorem: $\pi_k(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q} \neq 0$ only when $k = n$ for any $n$, or $n = 2j$ even and $k = 4j - 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Read the indecomposable layer $V^k$ of the minimal model directly. Sullivan's main theorem gives $\pi_k(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = (V^k)^*$ for the minimal model $(\Lambda V, d)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Step 1: minimal models.** For $n$ odd ($n = 2j + 1$), the minimal model of $S^{2j+1}$ is $\Lambda(x)$ with $\deg x = 2j + 1$ and $dx = 0$. The indecomposable space is $V = \mathbb{Q} \cdot x$, concentrated in degree $2j + 1$. By Sullivan's main theorem, $\pi_k(S^{2j+1}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = (V^k)^* = \mathbb{Q}$ for $k = 2j + 1$, and $0$ for $k \neq 2j + 1$.

For $n$ even ($n = 2j$), the minimal model is $\Lambda(x, y)$ with $\deg x = 2j$, $\deg y = 4j - 1$, $dy = x^2$. The indecomposable space is $V = \mathbb{Q} \cdot x \oplus \mathbb{Q} \cdot y$, supported in degrees $2j$ and $4j - 1$. Hence $\pi_k(S^{2j}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$ for $k = 2j$ or $k = 4j - 1$, and $0$ otherwise.

**Step 2: combine.** $\pi_k(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ is non-zero precisely when:
- $k = n$ (the Hurewicz diagonal, $\pi_n(S^n) = \mathbb{Z}$, rationally $\mathbb{Q}$);
- or $n = 2j$ even and $k = 4j - 1$ (the rational Whitehead bracket, $\pi_{4j - 1}(S^{2j}) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = \mathbb{Q}$).

In all other cases $\pi_k(S^n) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = 0$, equivalently $\pi_k(S^n)$ is torsion.

**Step 3: integer finiteness.** For $k > n$ and not in the rational-non-zero case, $\pi_k(S^n)$ has rank zero. Combined with the finite-generation of homotopy groups of finite-dimensional simply-connected CW complexes (Serre 1951), $\pi_k(S^n)$ is finite. For $n$ even and $k = 4j - 1$, $\pi_k(S^n) = \mathbb{Z} \oplus \pi_k(S^n)_{\mathrm{tor}}$ where the torsion part is finite. $\square$

This is the cleanest statement of Sullivan's solution: the rational answer is read directly off the minimal model, and the integer answer follows from finite-generation. The proof reduces Serre's 1953 spectral-sequence argument (which is intricate) to the elementary observation that the minimal model has only finitely many indecomposable generators in each degree. [ref: TODO_REF Sullivan 1977; Serre 1953]

The exception $n = 2j, k = 4j - 1$ corresponds to the **Hopf-invariant generators**: $\eta : S^3 \to S^2$ ($j = 1$), $\nu : S^7 \to S^4$ ($j = 2$), $\sigma : S^{15} \to S^8$ ($j = 4$), and (for $j \neq 1, 2, 4$) Whitehead-bracket generators $[\iota_{2j}, \iota_{2j}]$. By Adams 1960, only $j = 1, 2, 4$ have Hopf-invariant-one generators; the higher cases are realised by Whitehead brackets with non-zero rational image.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Formality of compact Kähler manifolds (Deligne-Griffiths-Morgan-Sullivan 1975).*

Sketch the proof that every simply-connected compact Kähler manifold is formal: its minimal model is determined by its cohomology ring with zero differential, no Massey-product corrections.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the $\partial \bar{\partial}$-lemma on a compact Kähler manifold: if $\alpha$ is $d$-closed and $d^c$-closed, and is $d$-exact or $d^c$-exact, then $\alpha = d d^c \beta$ for some $\beta$. This makes the de Rham complex rigid as a DGA.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $X$ be a simply-connected compact Kähler manifold. Equip the complex de Rham complex $\Omega^*_\mathbb{C}(X) = \Omega^*(X) \otimes \mathbb{C}$ with the Kähler structure: holomorphic / antiholomorphic decomposition $d = \partial + \bar{\partial}$, the Hodge $\star$, and $d^c = i(\bar{\partial} - \partial)/2$.

**Step 1: $\partial \bar{\partial}$-lemma.** The Hodge theory of compact Kähler manifolds (Andreotti-Vesentini, Weil) gives the **$\partial \bar{\partial}$-lemma**: if $\alpha$ is both $d$-closed and $d^c$-closed, and $\alpha$ is either $d$-exact or $d^c$-exact, then $\alpha = d d^c \beta = -2 i \partial \bar{\partial} \beta$ for some $\beta$. This is the key Kähler-geometric input.

**Step 2: zigzag of quasi-isomorphisms over $\mathbb{C}$.** Define two intermediate DGAs:

$$
A_\bullet := \ker d^c \cap \Omega^*_\mathbb{C}(X), \qquad B_\bullet := H^*_{d^c}(\Omega^*_\mathbb{C}(X), d^c).
$$

Two natural maps:

- **Inclusion** $i : A_\bullet \hookrightarrow \Omega^*_\mathbb{C}(X)$ — closed-under-$d^c$ forms include into all forms.
- **Projection** $\pi : A_\bullet \to B_\bullet$ — $d$-cocycles in $\ker d^c$ project to $d^c$-cohomology classes; $\pi$ kills $d$-coboundaries that are $d^c$-exact, which by $\partial \bar{\partial}$-lemma is equivalent to $\partial \bar{\partial}$-exact, hence to $d d^c$-exact, hence in the image of $A_\bullet$ by the chain-homotopy from the $\partial \bar{\partial}$-equation.

Both $i$ and $\pi$ are quasi-isomorphisms with respect to $d$:
- $i$ quasi-iso: by $\partial \bar{\partial}$-lemma, every $d$-closed form is $d$-cohomologous to one in $\ker d^c$.
- $\pi$ quasi-iso: $H^*(A, d) \cong H^*(B, d^c)$ via the Hodge structure, with $H^*(B, d^c)$ tautologically equal to $B$ as a graded vector space (since $d^c$ vanishes on $B$).

Hence $\Omega^*_\mathbb{C}(X) \xleftarrow{i} A_\bullet \xrightarrow{\pi} B_\bullet$ is a zigzag of DGA quasi-isomorphisms, and the right end $B_\bullet$ is a graded $\mathbb{C}$-algebra with zero differential. Comparing cohomology, $B_\bullet \cong H^*(X; \mathbb{C})$ as graded algebras.

**Step 3: descend to $\mathbb{Q}$.** The zigzag is over $\mathbb{C}$, but the de Rham complex is the complexification of a $\mathbb{Q}$-form of itself ($A_{PL}$ at the rational level). By uniqueness of the minimal model:
$$
M_X \otimes \mathbb{C} \cong M_{H^*(X; \mathbb{C})}.
$$
The right-hand side is the minimal model of the cohomology ring as a DGA with zero differential. Since both sides are intrinsically defined as $\mathbb{Q}$-algebras and the isomorphism is compatible with the cohomology structures, descent gives
$$
M_X \cong M_{H^*(X; \mathbb{Q})}.
$$

**Conclusion.** The minimal model of $X$ is determined by its cohomology ring; $X$ is formal. $\square$

**Corollary.** Two simply-connected compact Kähler manifolds with isomorphic rational cohomology rings are rationally homotopy equivalent. The Massey-product / formality dichotomy is therefore *invisible* on the Kähler side — a striking rigidity theorem.

The result is one of the principal applications of the Sullivan apparatus and is a foundational theorem of Hodge-theoretic geometry. [ref: TODO_REF Deligne-Griffiths-Morgan-Sullivan 1975]
