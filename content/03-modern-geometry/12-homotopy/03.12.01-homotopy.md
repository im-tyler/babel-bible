## Intuition [Beginner]

Two paths in a space are *homotopic* if you can continuously deform one into the other without breaking it. Two spaces are *homotopy equivalent* if you can continuously deform one to the other and back without tearing.

Homotopy is the equivalence relation that captures "essentially the same shape" when stretching, bending, or shrinking is allowed but cutting and gluing is not. Under homotopy, a coffee cup becomes a doughnut (one hole), a sphere is different from a doughnut (different number of holes), and a disk shrinks to a point.

The **fundamental group** $\pi_1(X, x_0)$ records which loops in $X$ based at a point $x_0$ can be deformed into one another. It is the simplest of an entire family of *homotopy groups* $\pi_n(X, x_0)$ that capture higher-dimensional "holes" in $X$. The fundamental group is nontrivial for the circle ($\pi_1(S^1) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, recording how many times a loop winds around) and zero for the sphere ($\pi_1(S^2) = 0$, since every loop on the sphere shrinks).

## Visual [Beginner]

Two paths in a space, with a continuous family of intermediate paths interpolating them. Homotopy is the existence of such a family.

![Two paths from one point to another, with a continuous family of intermediate paths interpolating between them.](/img/homotopy-placeholder.svg)

For loops based at a fixed point, composition (concatenate one loop after another) gives a group structure on equivalence classes — the fundamental group.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the circle $S^1$ embedded in the plane. Loops based at a fixed point $x_0 \in S^1$ can wind around the circle any integer number of times — clockwise (negative) or counterclockwise (positive). A loop winding $n$ times can be continuously deformed into any other loop winding $n$ times, but not into one winding $m \neq n$ times.

So the fundamental group of the circle is the integers: $\pi_1(S^1, x_0) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, with the integer recording the *winding number*.

For the sphere $S^2$, every loop can be continuously slid until it shrinks to a point — there are no "obstructions" because $S^2$ is simply connected. So $\pi_1(S^2) = 0$.

For the torus $T^2 = S^1 \times S^1$, loops can independently wind around the "longitude" or the "meridian" circles, giving $\pi_1(T^2) = \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z}$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X, Y$ be topological spaces [02.01.01]. Two continuous maps $f, g : X \to Y$ [02.01.02] are **homotopic**, written $f \simeq g$, if there exists a continuous map

$$
H : X \times [0, 1] \to Y
$$

with $H(x, 0) = f(x)$ and $H(x, 1) = g(x)$ for all $x \in X$. The map $H$ is a **homotopy** from $f$ to $g$.

Homotopy is an equivalence relation on continuous maps $X \to Y$. The set of equivalence classes is denoted $[X, Y]$.

A continuous map $f : X \to Y$ is a **homotopy equivalence** if there exists $g : Y \to X$ with $g \circ f \simeq \mathrm{id}_X$ and $f \circ g \simeq \mathrm{id}_Y$. Two spaces $X, Y$ are **homotopy equivalent** ($X \simeq Y$) if such an $f$ exists.

A space is **contractible** if it is homotopy equivalent to a point — equivalently, the identity $\mathrm{id}_X$ is homotopic to a constant map.

For pointed spaces $(X, x_0)$ and $(Y, y_0)$ — topological spaces with a chosen basepoint — a **based map** $f : (X, x_0) \to (Y, y_0)$ sends $x_0$ to $y_0$. Based homotopies fix the basepoint throughout.

The **fundamental group** $\pi_1(X, x_0)$ is the set of homotopy classes of based loops $\gamma : ([0, 1], \{0, 1\}) \to (X, x_0)$ — loops sending both endpoints to $x_0$ — under the operation of *concatenation*:

$$
(\gamma_1 \cdot \gamma_2)(t) := \begin{cases}
\gamma_1(2t) & 0 \leq t \leq 1/2 \\
\gamma_2(2t - 1) & 1/2 \leq t \leq 1
\end{cases}
$$

This makes $\pi_1(X, x_0)$ a group: associativity holds up to homotopy, the constant loop at $x_0$ is the identity, and the inverse of $\gamma$ is $\gamma$ traversed backwards.

For $n \geq 2$, the **$n$-th homotopy group** $\pi_n(X, x_0)$ is the set of based homotopy classes of maps $(S^n, *) \to (X, x_0)$, where $S^n$ has a chosen basepoint. The group operation comes from the pinch map $S^n \to S^n \vee S^n$. For $n \geq 2$, all $\pi_n$ are abelian (Eckmann-Hilton).

A space $X$ is **simply connected** if it is path-connected and $\pi_1(X, x_0) = 0$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (functoriality of $\pi_1$).** *A based continuous map $f : (X, x_0) \to (Y, y_0)$ induces a group homomorphism*

$$
f_\ast : \pi_1(X, x_0) \to \pi_1(Y, y_0), \qquad f_\ast([\gamma]) := [f \circ \gamma].
$$

*This assignment satisfies $(g \circ f)_\ast = g_\ast \circ f_\ast$ and $(\mathrm{id}_X)_\ast = \mathrm{id}_{\pi_1(X, x_0)}$. Hence $\pi_1$ is a functor from the category of pointed topological spaces to the category of groups.*

*Moreover, if $f \simeq g$ are based homotopic, then $f_\ast = g_\ast$.*

**Proof.** *Well-defined.* If $\gamma \simeq \gamma'$ via a based homotopy $H : [0, 1] \times [0, 1] \to X$, then $f \circ H : [0, 1] \times [0, 1] \to Y$ is a based homotopy from $f \circ \gamma$ to $f \circ \gamma'$ in $Y$. So $[f \circ \gamma] = [f \circ \gamma']$ in $\pi_1(Y, y_0)$, and $f_\ast$ is well-defined on equivalence classes.

*Homomorphism.* For loops $\gamma_1, \gamma_2$, the concatenation satisfies $f \circ (\gamma_1 \cdot \gamma_2) = (f \circ \gamma_1) \cdot (f \circ \gamma_2)$ (point-by-point, by the definition of concatenation). So $f_\ast([\gamma_1 \cdot \gamma_2]) = f_\ast([\gamma_1]) \cdot f_\ast([\gamma_2])$.

*Functoriality.* For a composition $g \circ f$,

$$
(g \circ f)_\ast([\gamma]) = [(g \circ f) \circ \gamma] = [g \circ (f \circ \gamma)] = g_\ast([f \circ \gamma]) = g_\ast(f_\ast([\gamma])).
$$

The identity case is direct: $(\mathrm{id}_X) \circ \gamma = \gamma$.

*Homotopy invariance.* If $f \simeq g$ via a based homotopy $H : X \times [0, 1] \to Y$, then for any loop $\gamma : [0, 1] \to X$ at $x_0$, the composite $H \circ (\gamma \times \mathrm{id}) : [0, 1] \times [0, 1] \to Y$ is a based homotopy from $f \circ \gamma$ to $g \circ \gamma$. So $f_\ast([\gamma]) = g_\ast([\gamma])$, hence $f_\ast = g_\ast$. $\square$

The theorem says $\pi_1$ is a *homotopy invariant*: homotopy equivalent spaces have isomorphic fundamental groups. This is the fundamental computational tool — to compute $\pi_1(X)$, replace $X$ by something homotopy equivalent and easier to handle.

The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. ## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Show that homotopy is an equivalence relation on continuous maps.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Verify reflexivity, symmetry, transitivity by constructing explicit homotopies.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Reflexive*: $f \simeq f$ via $H(x, t) := f(x)$.

*Symmetric*: if $H : f \simeq g$, then $H'(x, t) := H(x, 1-t)$ gives $g \simeq f$.

*Transitive*: if $H_1 : f \simeq g$ and $H_2 : g \simeq h$, define

$$
H(x, t) := \begin{cases} H_1(x, 2t) & 0 \leq t \leq 1/2 \\ H_2(x, 2t-1) & 1/2 \leq t \leq 1 \end{cases}
$$

Continuity at $t = 1/2$ follows from $H_1(x, 1) = g(x) = H_2(x, 0)$. Then $H : f \simeq h$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

Compute $\pi_1(S^1 \times S^1)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The fundamental group of a product is the product of fundamental groups.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\pi_1(S^1 \times S^1) = \pi_1(S^1) \times \pi_1(S^1) = \mathbb{Z} \times \mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z}^2$. The two integer factors record winding around each circle factor.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that any two continuous maps $f, g : X \to \mathbb{R}^n$ are homotopic.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the straight-line homotopy.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define $H(x, t) := (1 - t) f(x) + t g(x)$. This is continuous (as a composition of continuous functions), satisfies $H(x, 0) = f(x)$ and $H(x, 1) = g(x)$, and lands in $\mathbb{R}^n$ (which is convex). So $H$ is a homotopy from $f$ to $g$. The same argument shows $\mathbb{R}^n$ is contractible.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Show that the fundamental group is independent of basepoint, up to isomorphism, for path-connected spaces.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Conjugate by a path from one basepoint to another.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For path-connected $X$ and basepoints $x_0, x_1$, choose a path $\alpha$ from $x_0$ to $x_1$. Define $\beta_\alpha : \pi_1(X, x_0) \to \pi_1(X, x_1)$ by $\beta_\alpha([\gamma]) := [\bar\alpha \cdot \gamma \cdot \alpha]$, where $\bar\alpha$ is $\alpha$ traversed backwards. Verify $\beta_\alpha$ is a homomorphism (concatenation is preserved up to homotopy) with inverse $\beta_{\bar\alpha}$. So $\pi_1(X, x_0) \cong \pi_1(X, x_1)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why are higher homotopy groups $\pi_n(X)$ for $n \geq 2$ automatically abelian?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Eckmann-Hilton argument: two compatible group structures on the same set must agree and be commutative.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $n \geq 2$, there are at least two ways to "compose" maps $S^n \to X$ corresponding to two different coordinates of $S^n$ on which to perform the pinch construction. The Eckmann-Hilton argument: if a set carries two group operations $\cdot_1, \cdot_2$ that satisfy the interchange law $(a \cdot_1 b) \cdot_2 (c \cdot_1 d) = (a \cdot_2 c) \cdot_1 (b \cdot_2 d)$, then both operations agree and are commutative. Higher homotopy groups inherit such a pair of compatible operations from the multiple "directions" available on $S^n$, making them abelian.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that contractible spaces have all homotopy groups zero: $\pi_n(X, x_0) = 0$ for all $n \geq 0$ if $X$ is contractible.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Any map $S^n \to X$ extends to a map $D^{n+1} \to X$, hence is null-homotopic.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $X$ is contractible via a homotopy $H : X \times [0, 1] \to X$ with $H(x, 0) = x$ and $H(x, 1) = x_0$ (constant). For any based map $f : (S^n, *) \to (X, x_0)$, define $G : S^n \times [0, 1] \to X$ by $G(s, t) := H(f(s), t)$. Then $G(s, 0) = f(s)$ and $G(s, 1) = x_0$ (the constant map at $x_0$). So $f$ is based-homotopic to the constant map. Hence $[f] = 0 \in \pi_n(X, x_0)$, and $\pi_n(X, x_0) = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

State the relationship between $\pi_1$ and the first singular homology group $H_1$ for a path-connected space.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Hurewicz's theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Hurewicz's theorem.** For a path-connected space $X$, the natural map $\pi_1(X, x_0) \to H_1(X; \mathbb{Z})$ — sending a loop class to the homology class of its image as a 1-cycle — is the *abelianisation* $\pi_1 \to \pi_1/[\pi_1, \pi_1]$. So $H_1(X; \mathbb{Z})$ is the abelianisation of $\pi_1(X, x_0)$. For higher dimensions ($n \geq 2$ and $X$ simply connected up to dimension $n - 1$), Hurewicz gives an isomorphism $\pi_n(X) \cong H_n(X; \mathbb{Z})$.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Seifert-Van Kampen theorem.** For an open cover $X = U \cup V$ with $U \cap V$ path-connected and $U, V, U \cap V$ all containing the basepoint, the fundamental group decomposes as

$$
\pi_1(X) \cong \pi_1(U) \ast_{\pi_1(U \cap V)} \pi_1(V),
$$

an *amalgamated free product*. This is the central computational tool for fundamental groups: it reduces $\pi_1(X)$ to fundamental groups of simpler pieces.

**Universal cover.** For a path-connected space $X$ with reasonable local conditions (semi-locally simply connected), there exists a universal cover $\widetilde{X} \to X$ — a simply connected covering space with deck transformation group $\pi_1(X, x_0)$. Covering spaces of $X$ correspond bijectively (via the Galois correspondence) to subgroups of $\pi_1(X, x_0)$.

**Long exact sequence of a fibration.** A Serre fibration $F \to E \to B$ gives a long exact sequence of homotopy groups

$$
\cdots \to \pi_n(F) \to \pi_n(E) \to \pi_n(B) \to \pi_{n-1}(F) \to \cdots
$$

This computes homotopy groups of total spaces from base and fibre.

**Homotopy groups of spheres.** $\pi_n(S^m)$ is a deeply studied object. For $n < m$, $\pi_n(S^m) = 0$; for $n = m$, $\pi_m(S^m) = \mathbb{Z}$; for $n > m$, the groups are largely unknown and are the subject of stable homotopy theory (the homotopy groups stabilise as $m \to \infty$, giving the *stable homotopy groups of spheres* $\pi_k^s$).

**Whitehead's theorem.** A continuous map $f : X \to Y$ between CW complexes that induces isomorphisms on all homotopy groups $\pi_n$ is a homotopy equivalence. This makes homotopy groups a *complete* invariant of homotopy type for CW complexes.

**Eilenberg-MacLane spaces.** For each abelian group $A$ and integer $n \geq 1$, there is a space $K(A, n)$ with $\pi_n(K(A, n)) = A$ and $\pi_k(K(A, n)) = 0$ for $k \neq n$. These are unique up to homotopy and represent ordinary cohomology: $H^n(X; A) = [X, K(A, n)]$.

**Synthesis.** Homotopy is the equivalence relation that identifies continuous deformations: the fundamental group $\pi_1$ records the distinct ways loops can wrap around holes in a space, higher homotopy groups $\pi_n$ capture higher-dimensional sphere-valued maps up to deformation, covering spaces [03.12.02] realize the subgroup lattice of $\pi_1$ as a geometric classification of spaces lying over $X$, and the suspension operation [03.12.03] shifts homotopy groups by one degree and eventually stabilizes into the stable stems. The homotopy extension property makes homotopy equivalence the right notion of "same shape" for continuous maps (as opposed to homeomorphism, which is too rigid), and the Whitehead theorem shows that a map between CW complexes inducing isomorphisms on all homotopy groups is a homotopy equivalence — making homotopy groups the complete algebraic invariant for this class of spaces. The entire homotopy-theoretic strand builds on these patterns: classifying spaces [03.08.04], spectra [03.12.04], and Eilenberg-MacLane spaces [03.12.05] all exist to organize homotopy classes of maps into computable algebraic structures.

## Full proof set [Master]

**$\pi_1$ is a group.** The associativity of concatenation up to homotopy follows from explicit reparametrisations of $[0, 1]$. The identity element is the constant loop at $x_0$. The inverse of $[\gamma]$ is $[\bar\gamma]$ where $\bar\gamma(t) := \gamma(1 - t)$; the composition $\gamma \cdot \bar\gamma$ is null-homotopic via the homotopy that "retracts" the loop to a point.

**Functoriality of $\pi_1$.** Proved in §"Key theorem".

**Homotopy invariance.** If $X \simeq Y$ are homotopy equivalent via $f : X \to Y$ and $g : Y \to X$ with $g \circ f \simeq \mathrm{id}_X$, $f \circ g \simeq \mathrm{id}_Y$, then $g_\ast \circ f_\ast = (g \circ f)_\ast = (\mathrm{id}_X)_\ast = \mathrm{id}_{\pi_1(X)}$ (using the homotopy-invariance lemma from §"Key theorem"). Similarly $f_\ast \circ g_\ast = \mathrm{id}_{\pi_1(Y)}$. So $f_\ast$ is an isomorphism with inverse $g_\ast$.

**$\pi_1(S^1) = \mathbb{Z}$.** The standard proof uses the universal cover $\mathbb{R} \to S^1$ given by $t \mapsto e^{2\pi i t}$. Loops in $S^1$ lift uniquely to paths in $\mathbb{R}$ starting at the chosen lift of the basepoint; the *lift's endpoint* — necessarily an integer because the cover map is $2\pi$-periodic — gives the winding number, defining a homomorphism $\pi_1(S^1) \to \mathbb{Z}$. Surjectivity is immediate (the loop $t \mapsto e^{2\pi i n t}$ has winding number $n$). Injectivity follows from path-lifting: if a loop has winding zero, its lift to $\mathbb{R}$ is a loop in $\mathbb{R}$ (a contractible space), hence null-homotopic, and the homotopy descends.

**Eckmann-Hilton (for $\pi_n$ abelianness).** Proved in Exercise 5 sketch; the formal argument uses the interchange law and the existence of an identity element to show that the two compatible group operations agree and are commutative.

## Connections [Master]

- **Topological space [02.01.01]** — the underlying setting.

- **Continuous map [02.01.02]** — homotopy is an equivalence relation on continuous maps.

- **Covering space [03.12.02]** — covering spaces are classified by subgroups of $\pi_1$.

- **Classifying space [03.08.04]** — $BG = K(G, 1)$ for a discrete group $G$; classifying spaces represent principal-bundle theory.

- **Stable homotopy [03.08.06]** — the limit $\pi_k^s = \lim_m \pi_{m+k}(S^m)$ of homotopy groups under suspension.

- **Bott periodicity [03.08.07]** — periodicity of stable homotopy of classical groups.

- **Spin structure [03.09.04]** — the obstruction $w_2$ classifies spin structures via $H^2(M; \mathbb{Z}/2) = [M, K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 2)]$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Henri Poincaré introduced the fundamental group in his 1895 *Analysis Situs* as a tool for distinguishing topological spaces — his motivating example was distinguishing the sphere $S^2$ (simply connected) from the torus $T^2$ (with infinite fundamental group). The systematic theory of higher homotopy groups was developed by Hurewicz in the 1930s, with the long exact sequence of a fibration following from the work of Serre in the 1950s.

The relationship between homotopy and homology — Hurewicz's theorem and its extensions — and the deep computational difficulty of homotopy groups of spheres are among the central themes of twentieth-century algebraic topology. Adams' spectral sequence (1958) and the chromatic decomposition (Ravenel and others, 1980s) organised stable homotopy into a layered structure indexed by formal-group laws.

In modern category theory, homotopy theory is the prototype of a *higher category* — composition is associative only up to higher homotopies, which themselves compose up to even higher homotopies. The $\infty$-category formalism (Lurie, 2000s) makes this precise and underlies modern derived algebraic geometry and topological field theory.
