## Intuition [Beginner]

A connection lets you carry vectors along paths on a curved space. Carry a vector around a small loop and bring it back. On a flat space, it returns unchanged. On a curved space, it comes back rotated.

That rotation — the *failure of parallel transport to close up around a small loop* — is curvature. It's a local quantity (one number per pair of directions) that records how much the space twists.

For a sphere, transport a vector along the equator, then up to the north pole, then back down on a different meridian — the vector has rotated. The rotation per unit area is the Gaussian curvature. The same idea, generalised, gives the curvature of any connection on any bundle.

## Visual [Beginner]

A small parallelogram on a curved surface. A vector traverses the boundary; on return, it's rotated. The rotation angle, divided by the parallelogram's area, is the curvature in those directions.

![A vector parallel-transported around a small loop on a curved surface, returning rotated by an angle proportional to the loop's enclosed area.](/img/curvature-placeholder.svg)

The smaller the loop, the better this approximation. In the limit, curvature is the *infinitesimal failure of parallel transport to commute*.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider the unit sphere $S^2$ with the Levi-Civita connection. Take a small geodesic triangle near the north pole with two right-angle corners on the equator and a vertex angle $\alpha$ at the pole.

Parallel-transport a tangent vector around the boundary. By the *Gauss-Bonnet theorem*, the total rotation equals the *angular excess* of the triangle:

$$
\text{rotation} = \alpha + \tfrac{\pi}{2} + \tfrac{\pi}{2} - \pi = \alpha.
$$

Equivalently, the rotation equals "curvature times enclosed area." For the round sphere of radius $1$, the curvature is constant ($K = 1$), and the area of a triangle with angles summing to a half-turn plus $\alpha$ is exactly $\alpha$. The two computations agree: rotation $=$ curvature $\times$ area $=$ angular excess. For higher-dimensional bundles (e.g., a vector bundle of rank $r$ over a base manifold), curvature is a 2-form valued in *endomorphisms of the fibre* — at each point and pair of tangent directions, it gives a linear map of the fibre.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\pi : P \to M$ be a smooth principal $G$-bundle [03.05.01] and $\omega$ a connection 1-form on $P$ [03.05.07], i.e., a $\mathfrak{g}$-valued 1-form $\omega \in \Omega^1(P; \mathfrak{g})$ satisfying:

- $\omega(\xi^\#) = \xi$ for every $\xi \in \mathfrak{g}$, where $\xi^\#$ is the fundamental vertical vector field generated by $\xi$;
- $R_g^\ast \omega = \mathrm{Ad}_{g^{-1}} \omega$ for every $g \in G$.

The **curvature 2-form** of $\omega$ is the $\mathfrak{g}$-valued 2-form $\Omega \in \Omega^2(P; \mathfrak{g})$ defined by the **Cartan structure equation**:

$$
\Omega = d\omega + \tfrac{1}{2} [\omega \wedge \omega],
$$

where $[\omega \wedge \omega]$ is the $\mathfrak{g}$-valued 2-form whose value on tangent vectors $X, Y$ is

$$
[\omega \wedge \omega](X, Y) = [\omega(X), \omega(Y)] - [\omega(Y), \omega(X)] = 2 [\omega(X), \omega(Y)],
$$

so equivalently $\Omega(X, Y) = d\omega(X, Y) + [\omega(X), \omega(Y)]$.

The curvature is **horizontal** (vanishes when one input is vertical) and **equivariant** ($R_g^\ast \Omega = \mathrm{Ad}_{g^{-1}} \Omega$), so it descends from a form on $P$ to a *base-manifold form* valued in the adjoint bundle:

$$
F \in \Omega^2(M; \mathrm{ad}\, P), \qquad \mathrm{ad}\, P = P \times_{\mathrm{Ad}} \mathfrak{g}.
$$

In gauge-theory notation, with $A$ the local pullback of $\omega$ along a section $s : U \to P$, the local curvature is

$$
F_A = dA + \tfrac{1}{2} [A \wedge A].
$$

This is the *field strength* in physics. For abelian groups, $[A \wedge A] = 0$, and $F = dA$ is just the exterior derivative — recovering the familiar electromagnetic field strength $F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu$ from the gauge potential $A_\mu$.

For a vector bundle $E \to M$ with connection $\nabla : \Gamma(E) \to \Gamma(T^\ast M \otimes E)$ [03.05.04], the curvature is

$$
R^\nabla(X, Y) s = \nabla_X \nabla_Y s - \nabla_Y \nabla_X s - \nabla_{[X, Y]} s,
$$

which gives a $C^\infty(M)$-bilinear map $TM \times TM \to \mathrm{End}(E)$, equivalently $R^\nabla \in \Omega^2(M; \mathrm{End}(E))$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Bianchi identity).** *Let $\Omega$ be the curvature 2-form of a connection $\omega$ on a principal $G$-bundle. Then*

$$
D_\omega \Omega = 0,
$$

*where $D_\omega = d + [\omega, \,\cdot\,]$ is the covariant exterior derivative.*

**Proof.** Apply the exterior derivative to the Cartan structure equation $\Omega = d\omega + \tfrac{1}{2}[\omega \wedge \omega]$:

$$
d\Omega = d(d\omega) + \tfrac{1}{2} d[\omega \wedge \omega] = \tfrac{1}{2} d[\omega \wedge \omega],
$$

since $d^2 = 0$. The graded Leibniz rule for the bracket of forms gives

$$
d[\omega \wedge \omega] = [d\omega \wedge \omega] - [\omega \wedge d\omega] = 2[d\omega \wedge \omega],
$$

using the symmetry $[\omega \wedge \alpha] = -[\alpha \wedge \omega]$ for 1-forms $\omega, \alpha$ valued in $\mathfrak{g}$ (the sign comes from both the form-degree swap and the bracket antisymmetry, which compose to a positive sign for two $\mathfrak{g}$-valued 1-forms). So

$$
d\Omega = [d\omega \wedge \omega].
$$

On the other hand, $[\omega, \Omega] = [\omega, d\omega + \tfrac{1}{2}[\omega \wedge \omega]] = [\omega \wedge d\omega] + \tfrac{1}{2}[\omega \wedge [\omega \wedge \omega]]$. The Jacobi identity for $\mathfrak{g}$, applied to a triple wedge of $\omega$'s, gives $[\omega \wedge [\omega \wedge \omega]] = 0$. So

$$
[\omega, \Omega] = [\omega \wedge d\omega] = -[d\omega \wedge \omega].
$$

Therefore

$$
D_\omega \Omega = d\Omega + [\omega, \Omega] = [d\omega \wedge \omega] - [d\omega \wedge \omega] = 0. \quad\square
$$

The Bianchi identity is the structural identity behind every closedness statement in characteristic-class theory: it is the input that makes Chern-Weil forms automatically closed [03.06.06]. In gauge theory it underwrites the topological-charge identities like $d \mathrm{tr}(F \wedge F) = 0$.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Show that for an abelian Lie group $G$, the curvature reduces to $\Omega = d\omega$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What is $[\omega \wedge \omega]$ when the bracket on $\mathfrak{g}$ is zero?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For abelian $G$, the bracket on $\mathfrak{g}$ is identically zero, so $[\omega \wedge \omega] = 0$. The Cartan equation reduces to $\Omega = d\omega$. This is the case for $G = U(1)$ (electromagnetism) and $G = (\mathbb{R}, +)^n$ (translation gauge fields).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

For a $U(1)$-connection with local 1-form $A = A_\mu \, dx^\mu$, write the components of $F = dA$ in coordinates.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the exterior derivative coordinate by coordinate.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$F = \tfrac{1}{2} F_{\mu\nu} \, dx^\mu \wedge dx^\nu$ with $F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu$. This is the electromagnetic field strength: the components of the magnetic field and electric field assembled into one antisymmetric tensor.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that a connection has zero curvature if and only if its parallel transport is *path-independent* (locally; globally, in simply-connected regions).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Equate path-independence with the local exactness of the connection — equivalent to the Frobenius integrability of the horizontal distribution.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The horizontal distribution on $P$ is integrable (in the Frobenius sense) iff the curvature 2-form vanishes. Frobenius integrability is the condition that the bracket of two horizontal vector fields is again horizontal; the curvature 2-form is precisely the obstruction to that. When $\Omega = 0$, the horizontal distribution foliates $P$ into horizontal leaves, and parallel transport along any path within a simply-connected region depends only on endpoints. Conversely, if parallel transport is path-independent in some neighbourhood, transport around an infinitesimal closed loop is the identity, so $\Omega = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

For a connection with local 1-form $A$ valued in a matrix Lie algebra, write $F$ in components as $F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu + [A_\mu, A_\nu]$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $F = dA + \tfrac{1}{2}[A \wedge A]$ in coordinates.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$dA = \partial_\mu A_\nu \, dx^\mu \wedge dx^\nu = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu) \, dx^\mu \wedge dx^\nu$.
$\tfrac{1}{2}[A \wedge A] = \tfrac{1}{2}[A_\mu, A_\nu] \, dx^\mu \wedge dx^\nu$ (where $\mu < \nu$, with the antisymmetric pairing).
Combining: $F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu + [A_\mu, A_\nu]$. The $[A_\mu, A_\nu]$ term is the *non-abelian correction* that vanishes for $U(1)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Show that under a gauge transformation $g : U \to G$, the local curvature transforms by $F^g = \mathrm{Ad}_{g^{-1}} F$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The connection transforms by $A^g = g^{-1} A g + g^{-1} dg$. Substitute into the Cartan equation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$F^g = dA^g + \tfrac{1}{2}[A^g \wedge A^g]$. Substituting $A^g = g^{-1} A g + g^{-1} dg$ and expanding (using the Maurer-Cartan equation $d(g^{-1}dg) = -(g^{-1}dg) \wedge (g^{-1}dg)$ for the inhomogeneous term), the cross-terms cancel and the result simplifies to $F^g = g^{-1} F g = \mathrm{Ad}_{g^{-1}} F$. This is the fundamental gauge-covariance of curvature: it transforms tensorially under the adjoint action, while the connection $A$ does not.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Prove the second Bianchi identity for a vector-bundle connection: $\nabla_X R(Y, Z) + \nabla_Y R(Z, X) + \nabla_Z R(X, Y) = 0$ (modulo terms involving brackets of $X, Y, Z$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the definition $R(X, Y) = \nabla_X \nabla_Y - \nabla_Y \nabla_X - \nabla_{[X, Y]}$ and apply $\nabla_Z$. Cyclic permutation cancellations parallel the algebraic Jacobi identity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Bianchi identity for the principal-bundle curvature ($D_\omega \Omega = 0$) translates to the second Bianchi identity for the associated vector-bundle curvature. Computationally: applying $\nabla_Z$ to $R(X, Y) = \nabla_X \nabla_Y - \nabla_Y \nabla_X - \nabla_{[X, Y]}$ and summing cyclically over $(X, Y, Z)$, the third-order terms cancel by the Jacobi identity for $\mathrm{End}(E)$-valued operators, and the lower-order terms arrange into the bracket combinations indicated. The clean statement (in coordinate-free form) is $D R = 0$ for the induced connection $D$ on $\mathrm{End}(E)$-valued forms.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Why does the Bianchi identity make $\mathrm{tr}(F \wedge F)$ a closed 4-form?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply $d$ and use the Bianchi identity $D F = 0$ along with the cyclic property of trace.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$d \mathrm{tr}(F \wedge F) = \mathrm{tr}(dF \wedge F) - \mathrm{tr}(F \wedge dF) = 2\mathrm{tr}(dF \wedge F)$ (sign from form-degree). The Bianchi identity gives $dF = -[A, F]$ in local trivialisation, so $\mathrm{tr}(dF \wedge F) = -\mathrm{tr}([A, F] \wedge F) = -\mathrm{tr}(A F^2 - F A F) = 0$ by the cyclic property of trace (and the fact that the result is a 4-form, which is automatically symmetric in its $\mathfrak{g}$-valued slots through the trace). So $\mathrm{tr}(F \wedge F)$ is closed and represents a de Rham cohomology class — the topological charge $\int_M \mathrm{tr}(F \wedge F)$ in 4-manifold gauge theory.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

Curvature is the obstruction to flatness, but it has finer structural roles depending on the geometric setting.

**Yang-Mills equations.** The variational equations of the Yang-Mills action [03.07.05] are $D_\omega \ast \Omega = 0$ — the *covariant codifferential* of curvature vanishes. Combined with the Bianchi identity $D_\omega \Omega = 0$, this gives a coupled first-order system. In four dimensions the special solutions where $\ast \Omega = \pm \Omega$ (instantons) automatically satisfy both.

**Riemannian curvature tensor.** For the Levi-Civita connection on $TM$, the curvature 2-form $R^{\mathrm{LC}} \in \Omega^2(M; \mathrm{End}(TM))$ has the Riemann tensor as its components. Under the splitting $\mathrm{End}(TM) \cong TM \otimes T^\ast M$, the resulting tensor $R_{ijkl}$ has the well-known symmetries: $R_{ijkl} = -R_{jikl} = -R_{ijlk} = R_{klij}$ (the last by the algebraic Bianchi identity). Contracting once gives the Ricci curvature, contracting again gives the scalar curvature, and removing the trace gives the Weyl conformal curvature.

**Lichnerowicz formula.** The square of the Dirac operator on a spin manifold [03.09.08] decomposes as

$$
\not{\!D}^2 = \nabla^\ast \nabla + \tfrac{1}{4} R,
$$

where $R$ is the scalar curvature of the Levi-Civita connection — a curvature term arising directly from the spin connection's Cartan structure equation.

**Chern-Weil and characteristic classes.** Invariant polynomials evaluated on curvature [03.06.06] produce closed forms whose cohomology classes are independent of the chosen connection. This is the geometric source of every characteristic class with a differential-form representative — Chern, Pontryagin, Euler, Â — all live in the image of this construction [03.06.04].

**Holonomy.** Parallel transport around a closed loop based at $p \in M$ defines an element of the structure group $G$. The holonomy group $\mathrm{Hol}(p) \subseteq G$ is the subgroup generated by all such loop transports. Curvature determines the *Lie algebra* of $\mathrm{Hol}(p)$ via the Ambrose-Singer theorem: $\mathrm{Lie}(\mathrm{Hol}(p))$ is spanned by all values of $\Omega$ at points reachable by parallel transport from $p$.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Cartan structure equation as an identity.** The structure equation $\Omega = d\omega + \tfrac{1}{2}[\omega \wedge \omega]$ is *defined* this way; its content is that the right-hand side is horizontal and equivariant. *Horizontality*: for a vertical vector $\xi^\#$ generating $\xi \in \mathfrak{g}$, $\omega(\xi^\#) = \xi$ and $\xi$ is constant along $P$, so $d\omega(\xi^\#, X) = -\omega([\xi^\#, X])$ for any $X$. The bracket term gives the compensating contribution: a calculation shows $\Omega(\xi^\#, X) = 0$ for any $X$. *Equivariance* follows from $R_g^\ast \omega = \mathrm{Ad}_{g^{-1}} \omega$ and the corresponding equivariance of the bracket-of-forms.

**Bianchi identity.** Proved in §"Key theorem".

**Vector-bundle curvature is $C^\infty$-bilinear.** For $f \in C^\infty(M)$,

$$
R(fX, Y)s = \nabla_{fX}\nabla_Y s - \nabla_Y \nabla_{fX} s - \nabla_{[fX, Y]} s.
$$

Using $\nabla_{fX} = f \nabla_X$ and $[fX, Y] = f[X, Y] - (Yf) X$,

$$
R(fX, Y)s = f \nabla_X \nabla_Y s - \nabla_Y(f \nabla_X s) - f \nabla_{[X, Y]} s + (Yf) \nabla_X s
$$
$$
= f \nabla_X \nabla_Y s - (Yf) \nabla_X s - f \nabla_Y \nabla_X s - f \nabla_{[X, Y]} s + (Yf) \nabla_X s = f R(X, Y) s.
$$

So $R$ is $C^\infty(M)$-linear in $X$ (and similarly in $Y$ and $s$), making it a tensor field — a section of $\Lambda^2 T^\ast M \otimes \mathrm{End}(E)$, equivalently $\Omega^2(M; \mathrm{End}(E))$.

**Gauge covariance.** Under $A \mapsto A^g = g^{-1}Ag + g^{-1}dg$, the Maurer-Cartan equation $d(g^{-1}dg) = -(g^{-1}dg) \wedge (g^{-1}dg)$ produces cancellations leaving $F^g = g^{-1} F g$. This is the algebraic content of the Cartan structure equation under gauge transformations.

**Curvature is the Frobenius obstruction.** The horizontal distribution $H \subseteq TP$ is the $\ker \omega$. By Frobenius's theorem, $H$ is integrable iff $[H, H] \subseteq H$. For two horizontal $X, Y$, $\omega([X, Y]) = -d\omega(X, Y)$ (by Cartan's magic formula, since $\omega$ vanishes on $H$). On horizontal vectors, $\Omega(X, Y) = d\omega(X, Y)$, so $\omega([X, Y]) = 0$ iff $\Omega = 0$. Therefore $\Omega \equiv 0$ iff $H$ is integrable iff parallel transport is path-independent (locally).

## Connections [Master]

- **Principal bundle [03.05.01]** — the structural setting.
- **Connection on a principal bundle [03.05.07]** — supplies the 1-form $\omega$.
- **Connection on a vector bundle [03.05.04]** — the associated-bundle version yields $R^\nabla$.
- **Lie algebra [03.04.01]** — curvature takes values in $\mathfrak{g}$ (or $\mathrm{End}(E)$ on the associated side).
- **De Rham cohomology [03.04.06]** — the cohomology class of an invariant polynomial in $\Omega$ is the Chern-Weil image.
- **Chern-Weil homomorphism [03.06.06]** — the downstream construction that turns $\Omega$ into characteristic classes.
- **Yang-Mills action [03.07.05]** — the squared $L^2$ norm of curvature, treated variationally.
- **Pontryagin and Chern classes [03.06.04]** — specific invariant polynomials in $\Omega$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Curvature in its modern bundle-theoretic form was crystallised by Élie Cartan in the early twentieth century, building on Riemann's notion of sectional curvature and the development of the absolute differential calculus by Ricci and Levi-Civita. Cartan's structure equations were the geometric heart of his moving-frame method, and they predate the abstract bundle-theoretic formulation that Ehresmann developed in the late 1940s [ref: TODO_REF Kobayashi-Nomizu §III.5].

The simultaneous development of gauge theory in physics (Yang-Mills 1954) and Chern-Weil theory in mathematics (Chern, Weil, late 1940s) made curvature the central object connecting two previously separate streams. By the 1980s, with Atiyah-Bott on Yang-Mills over Riemann surfaces and Donaldson's four-manifold theory, curvature of a connection had become the geometric input that links analytic invariants (Yang-Mills functional, Donaldson invariants) to topological invariants (Pontryagin classes, instanton charges). The Bianchi identity is the structural identity that makes this entire programme coherent.
