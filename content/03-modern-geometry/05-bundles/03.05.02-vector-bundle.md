## Intuition [Beginner]

A vector bundle is a space made by attaching a vector space to every point of another space. The base tells you where you are. The attached vector space tells you what vectors are available at that point.

The simplest example is a product: every point carries the same copy of a vector space in the same way. More interesting examples twist, so nearby fibers can be compared locally but not always aligned globally.

This idea lets geometry turn pointwise linear algebra into global structure. Tangent vectors, spinors, and gauge fields all live in vector bundles.

## Visual [Beginner]

The green shape is the base. Above each point is a small vector space. In the red window, the bundle looks like a product.

![A curved base space with vector planes attached as fibers and a local product chart.](/img/vector-bundle-placeholder.svg)

The fibers can be added and scaled one point at a time. The bundle records how those vector spaces vary across the base.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take a circle as the base. Attach a copy of the number line above every point.

If the lines are aligned the same way all around the circle, the result is a cylinder-like product bundle. If the line flips once before returning to the starting point, the result is the line-bundle version of a Mobius strip.

In both cases, each individual fiber is a line. The difference lies in how the lines are glued as the base point moves.

What this tells us: a vector bundle can have simple fibers while still carrying global twisting.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $B$ be a topological space and let $K$ be $\mathbb R$ or $\mathbb C$. A **rank-$n$ vector bundle over $B$** is a continuous surjection

$$
\pi:E\to B
$$

such that each fiber $E_b=\pi^{-1}(b)$ is an $n$-dimensional vector space over $K$, and every $b\in B$ has an open neighborhood $U$ with a homeomorphism

$$
\Phi_U:\pi^{-1}(U)\longrightarrow U\times K^n
$$

over $U$ whose restriction to each fiber is a linear isomorphism

$$
E_b\cong \{b\}\times K^n.
$$

For smooth vector bundles, $B$ is a smooth manifold, $E$ is a smooth manifold, $\pi$ is smooth, and local product charts are smooth with fiberwise-linear transition maps [ref: TODO_REF Milnor-Stasheff §2].

A **section** of $E$ is a map $s:B\to E$ with $\pi\circ s=\operatorname{id}_B$. The set of continuous or smooth sections is denoted $\Gamma(E)$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (sections form a vector space).** Let $\pi:E\to B$ be a vector bundle over $K$. The set $\Gamma(E)$ of continuous sections is a vector space over $K$ under pointwise addition and scalar multiplication.

**Proof.** If $s,t\in\Gamma(E)$, then $s(b)$ and $t(b)$ lie in the same fiber $E_b$ for every $b\in B$. Since $E_b$ is a vector space, define

$$
(s+t)(b)=s(b)+t(b),\qquad (as)(b)=a\,s(b).
$$

These maps still land in $E_b$, so $\pi((s+t)(b))=b$ and $\pi((as)(b))=b$. Thus $s+t$ and $as$ are sections.

Continuity is local. On a product chart $\pi^{-1}(U)\cong U\times K^n$, sections are represented by maps $U\to K^n$. Pointwise addition and scalar multiplication of such coordinate maps are continuous, so the resulting sections are continuous. The vector-space axioms hold fiber by fiber because each $E_b$ is a vector space [01.01.03]. $\square$

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [03.05.04] (connection on a vector bundle), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [03.06.04] (pontryagin and chern classes). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the product bundle $B\times K^n\to B$ is a vector bundle.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the identity map as the product chart over every open set.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The projection $(b,v)\mapsto b$ is continuous. The fiber over $b$ is $\{b\}\times K^n$, identified linearly with $K^n$. The identity map gives a local product chart over every open subset of $B$, and the fiberwise maps are linear isomorphisms.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, conceptual).**

Why must transition maps for a rank-$n$ vector bundle take values in $\operatorname{GL}_n(K)$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Changing from one product chart to another must preserve fiberwise linear structure.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On an overlap, two product charts identify the same fiber with $K^n$. Their comparison is a linear isomorphism of $K^n$, hence an element of $\operatorname{GL}_n(K)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Let $E\to B$ and $F\to B$ be vector bundles. Define $(E\oplus F)_b=E_b\oplus F_b$. Explain why this is locally a vector bundle.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use product charts for $E$ and $F$ over a common open set.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Over an open set where $E\cong U\times K^m$ and $F\cong U\times K^n$, the fiberwise direct sum is identified with $U\times (K^m\oplus K^n)\cong U\times K^{m+n}$. The transition maps are block sums of linear isomorphisms, so they are linear isomorphisms.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that a rank-$n$ vector bundle determines a principal $\operatorname{GL}_n(K)$-bundle of frames.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A frame of $E_b$ is an ordered basis of the fiber.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\operatorname{Fr}(E)_b$ be the set of ordered bases of $E_b$. The group $\operatorname{GL}_n(K)$ acts on the right by change of basis. This action is free and transitive on each frame set. Local vector-bundle charts identify frames with $U\times\operatorname{GL}_n(K)$, giving the local product structure of a principal bundle [03.05.01].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Vector bundles can be added by direct sum, and isomorphism classes form a commutative monoid. Topological K-theory group-completes this monoid, turning formal differences of vector bundles into K-theory classes [03.08.07].

## Advanced results [Master]

A rank-$n$ vector bundle is equivalently a principal $\operatorname{GL}_n(K)$-bundle together with the standard associated-bundle construction. Given the frame bundle $\operatorname{Fr}(E)$, one recovers

$$
E\cong \operatorname{Fr}(E)\times_{\operatorname{GL}_n(K)} K^n.
$$

This equivalence is the bridge between vector-bundle characteristic classes and principal-bundle Chern-Weil theory [03.05.01] [ref: TODO_REF Husemoller §3].

Vector bundles admit natural operations: direct sum, tensor product, dual, exterior powers, pullback, and Hom bundles. Characteristic classes are designed to behave predictably under these operations, especially direct sum and pullback [03.06.04].

Over a compact Hausdorff space, complex vector bundles form a commutative monoid under direct sum. The Grothendieck group completion is $K^0(B)$, the starting point of topological K-theory [03.08.07] [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah K-Theory §1.1].

**Synthesis.** This construction generalises the pattern fixed in [01.01.03] (vector space), with the symmetric data replaced by its skew or twisted analogue. Read in the opposite direction, the construction is dual to the metric story: complements and orthogonality are taken with respect to the bilinear datum of this unit, not a metric, and the resulting category of subobjects is the one the rest of the strand classifies. The central insight is that this datum identifies algebra with geometry: functions become vector fields, subspaces become quotients, and invariants become cohomology classes — and that identification is the engine driving every theorem downstream.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Transition functions.** Choose local vector-bundle charts $\Phi_i:\pi^{-1}(U_i)\to U_i\times K^n$. On overlaps, the comparison $\Phi_i\Phi_j^{-1}$ fixes the base coordinate and is fiberwise linear. It has the form

$$
(x,v)\mapsto (x,g_{ij}(x)v)
$$

with $g_{ij}:U_i\cap U_j\to \operatorname{GL}_n(K)$. Associativity of chart comparison gives $g_{ik}=g_{ij}g_{jk}$ on triple overlaps.

**Frame bundle.** For each $b\in B$, let $\operatorname{Fr}(E)_b$ be the set of linear isomorphisms $K^n\to E_b$. Right composition by $\operatorname{GL}_n(K)$ gives a free transitive action. A local vector-bundle chart identifies $\operatorname{Fr}(E)|_U$ with $U\times\operatorname{GL}_n(K)$, proving that $\operatorname{Fr}(E)\to B$ is a principal bundle.

**Associated-bundle recovery.** Map $[\varphi,v]\in \operatorname{Fr}(E)\times_{\operatorname{GL}_n(K)}K^n$ to $\varphi(v)\in E$. The quotient relation identifies $(\varphi A,v)$ with $(\varphi,Av)$, and both map to the same vector. Fiberwise, every vector in $E_b$ is $\varphi(v)$ for some frame $\varphi$, and freeness gives uniqueness modulo the quotient relation.

## Connections [Master]

- **Vector space [01.01.03]** — every fiber is a vector space and all local charts are fiberwise linear.

- **Principal bundle [03.05.01]** — frame bundles are principal bundles, and vector bundles are associated bundles.

- **Pontryagin and Chern classes [03.06.04]** — characteristic classes are invariants of real and complex vector bundles.

- **Bott periodicity [03.08.07]** — topological K-theory is built from vector bundles.

- **Dirac operator [03.09.08]** — spinor bundles and twisting bundles are vector bundles carrying differential operators.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Vector bundles grew from the study of tangent spaces, normal data, and transition functions in topology and differential geometry. Steenrod and later Husemoller organized bundles through local product data and structure groups; Milnor and Stasheff made vector bundles the central objects behind characteristic classes [ref: TODO_REF Milnor-Stasheff §2].

Atiyah's formulation of K-theory placed vector bundles into a stable algebraic setting: direct sum becomes addition, tensor product becomes multiplication, and formal differences become cohomological invariants of spaces [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah K-Theory §1.1].
