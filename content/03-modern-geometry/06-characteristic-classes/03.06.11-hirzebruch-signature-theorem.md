## Intuition [Beginner]

A closed surface like a sphere or a doughnut has a simple shape-number called the Euler characteristic. It records the global count of holes after balancing vertices, edges, and faces. In higher dimensions you need more refined numbers, because shapes can twist in more ways than a surface can.

The signature is one of those higher numbers. It applies to closed shapes whose dimension is a multiple of four: $4$, $8$, $12$, and so on. Such a shape carries a natural bilinear pairing on its middle-degree cohomology, and the signature counts how many directions of that pairing are positive minus how many are negative.

Hirzebruch's signature theorem makes a striking claim. The signature, which is built from middle-dimensional intersection geometry, can be recovered from a different set of invariants called Pontryagin numbers. These numbers measure how the shape's tangent directions twist around globally. A single specific recipe, called the $L$-polynomial, converts Pontryagin numbers into the signature.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a four-dimensional shape whose middle directions support a pairing. Some pairs of middle-dimensional surfaces meet positively, others meet negatively. The signature subtracts the negative count from the positive count, producing a single number that records the global balance.

![A schematic of a 4-manifold with its middle-cohomology pairing producing a positive-minus-negative count, alongside Pontryagin twisting data that recombines into the same number via the L-polynomial.](/img/hirzebruch-signature-theorem-placeholder.svg)

The picture also hints at the parallel data: each tangent bundle carries Pontryagin classes recording its real twisting. The $L$-polynomial is the recipe that turns those twisting numbers into the same balance count. Two very different kinds of measurement give the same answer.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The complex projective plane is a closed four-dimensional shape. Its middle cohomology is one-dimensional. The pairing of the generator with itself equals $+1$. So the positive count is $1$ and the negative count is $0$, giving signature equal to $1$.

The same shape has first Pontryagin number equal to $3$. The $L$-polynomial in dimension four says the signature equals the first Pontryagin number divided by $3$. So $3 \div 3 = 1$, which matches.

Step 1. Compute the signature directly from the pairing: $b_+ = 1$, $b_- = 0$, signature $= 1 - 0 = 1$.

Step 2. Compute the Pontryagin number: $p_1[\mathbb{CP}^2] = 3$.

Step 3. Apply the recipe: signature $= p_1 \div 3 = 3 \div 3 = 1$.

What this tells us: two independent calculations agree, and the theorem promises they always will for any closed four-dimensional shape.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $M$ be a closed oriented smooth manifold of dimension $4k$. The cup-product pairing on middle-dimensional cohomology with real coefficients,

$$
Q_M : H^{2k}(M; \mathbb{R}) \times H^{2k}(M; \mathbb{R}) \to \mathbb{R},
\qquad
Q_M(\alpha, \beta) = \langle \alpha \cup \beta, [M] \rangle,
$$

is symmetric (since $\alpha \cup \beta = \beta \cup \alpha$ in degree $2k + 2k$ with the sign $(-1)^{4k^2} = 1$) and non-degenerate by Poincaré duality. The **signature** of $M$ is

$$
\mathrm{sig}(M) := b_+(M) - b_-(M) \in \mathbb{Z},
$$

where $b_+(M)$ and $b_-(M)$ are the positive and negative indices of $Q_M$ in the sense of Sylvester's law of inertia [ref: TODO_REF Milnor-Stasheff §19].

For an oriented real vector bundle $E \to M$, the Pontryagin classes $p_i(E) \in H^{4i}(M; \mathbb{Z})$ are the characteristic classes constructed in [03.06.04]. For the tangent bundle $TM$ of a closed oriented $4k$-manifold, a partition $I = (i_1, \ldots, i_r)$ of $k$ gives the **Pontryagin number**

$$
p_I[M] := \langle p_{i_1}(TM) \cup \cdots \cup p_{i_r}(TM), [M] \rangle \in \mathbb{Z},
$$

an integer because $[M]$ is an integral homology class and Pontryagin classes are integral.

### The Hirzebruch $L$-polynomials

The **Hirzebruch multiplicative sequence** $\{L_k\}$ is the multiplicative sequence associated to the formal power series

$$
Q(x) = \frac{\sqrt{x}}{\tanh \sqrt{x}}
= 1 + \frac{x}{3} - \frac{x^2}{45} + \frac{2 x^3}{945} - \frac{x^4}{4725} + \cdots
$$

A multiplicative sequence in formal variables $x_1, x_2, \ldots$ assigns to the formal product $\prod_j Q(x_j)$ a graded sum of symmetric polynomials in $x_j$; rewriting those symmetric polynomials as integer polynomials in the elementary symmetric polynomials $\sigma_i = \sum_{j_1 < \cdots < j_i} x_{j_1} \cdots x_{j_i}$ and substituting $\sigma_i = p_i$ produces the $L$-polynomials $L_k(p_1, \ldots, p_k) \in \mathbb{Q}[p_1, \ldots, p_k]$ [ref: TODO_REF Hirzebruch §8]. The first three are

$$
L_1 = \frac{p_1}{3},
\qquad
L_2 = \frac{7 p_2 - p_1^2}{45},
\qquad
L_3 = \frac{62 p_3 - 13 p_1 p_2 + 2 p_1^3}{945}.
$$

The denominators $3, 45, 945$ are forced by the Taylor coefficients of $Q$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Forgetting orientation.* The signature requires an oriented manifold. Reversing orientation flips the sign of $[M]$ and hence the sign of $\mathrm{sig}(M)$; the unoriented shape has no canonical signature.
- *Wrong dimension.* The signature is defined only on $4k$-manifolds. In dimensions $4k+2$, the cup-product pairing on $H^{2k+1}$ is anti-symmetric, not symmetric, and the right invariant is the dimension of the form, not its signature.
- *Conflating $p_1^2$ with $p_2$.* The Pontryagin number $p_1^2[M] = \langle p_1(TM)^2, [M] \rangle$ is in general different from $p_2[M] = \langle p_2(TM), [M] \rangle$. The Hirzebruch formula in dimension eight involves both as separate inputs, with the precise coefficients $7$ and $-1$ in $L_2 = (7 p_2 - p_1^2)/45$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Hirzebruch signature theorem, dimension four).** *For any closed oriented smooth $4$-manifold $M$,*

$$
\mathrm{sig}(M) = \frac{p_1(TM) \cup [M]}{3}
= \frac{1}{3} \, p_1[M].
$$

*Equivalently, $3 \cdot \mathrm{sig}(M) = p_1[M]$.*

*Proof.* The argument has three structural steps: signature is an oriented bordism invariant, Pontryagin numbers are oriented bordism invariants, and the rational oriented bordism ring $\Omega_{*}^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ in dimension four is generated by $\mathbb{CP}^2$.

**Step 1.** *Signature is an oriented bordism invariant.* Suppose $M = \partial W$ for a compact oriented $5$-manifold $W$. By Lefschetz duality and the long exact sequence of $(W, \partial W)$, the image of $H^2(W; \mathbb{R}) \to H^2(M; \mathbb{R})$ is a half-dimensional subspace on which the intersection form $Q_M$ vanishes identically. The form $Q_M$ admits a Lagrangian subspace, hence $b_+(M) = b_-(M)$ and $\mathrm{sig}(M) = 0$. Thus $\mathrm{sig}$ descends to $\Omega_4^{\mathrm{SO}} \to \mathbb{Z}$.

**Step 2.** *Pontryagin numbers are oriented bordism invariants.* Suppose again $M = \partial W$. The tangent bundle $TM$ extends as a subbundle of $TW|_M$ via the outward-normal trivialisation, so $p_1(TM) = i^\ast p_1(TW)$ where $i : M \hookrightarrow W$ is the inclusion. By Stokes' theorem on $W$,

$$
\langle p_1(TM), [M] \rangle
= \langle p_1(TW), i_\ast [M] \rangle
= \langle p_1(TW), \partial [W] \rangle = 0,
$$

since $\partial [W] = i_\ast [M] = 0$ in $H_4(W; \mathbb{Z})$ if $[M]$ is the oriented boundary. Hence $p_1[M] = 0$ on null-bordant $M$, so $p_1$ descends to $\Omega_4^{\mathrm{SO}} \to \mathbb{Z}$.

**Step 3.** *Rational generator.* Thom's theorem [ref: TODO_REF Thom 1954] says $\Omega_4^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q} \cong \mathbb{Q}$, generated by $[\mathbb{CP}^2]$. Both $\mathrm{sig}$ and $p_1$ are $\mathbb{Q}$-linear functionals on this one-dimensional space, so they are proportional. Test on $\mathbb{CP}^2$: by the Euler-sequence calculation in [03.06.04],

$$
c(T\mathbb{CP}^2) = (1+h)^3 = 1 + 3h + 3h^2,
$$

where $h = c_1(\mathcal{O}(1))$ generates $H^2(\mathbb{CP}^2; \mathbb{Z})$ with $\langle h^2, [\mathbb{CP}^2] \rangle = 1$. The Pontryagin class is $p_1(T\mathbb{CP}^2) = c_1(T\mathbb{CP}^2)^2 - 2 c_2(T\mathbb{CP}^2) = 9h^2 - 6h^2 = 3h^2$, giving $p_1[\mathbb{CP}^2] = 3$. The signature of $\mathbb{CP}^2$ is $1$ since the intersection form has matrix $(1)$ on the one-dimensional middle cohomology generated by $h$. Hence $\mathrm{sig}(M) = \lambda \, p_1[M]$ on $\Omega_4^{\mathrm{SO}}$ with $\lambda = 1/3$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The dimension-four argument builds toward the general formula $\mathrm{sig}(M) = \langle L_k(p_1, \ldots, p_k), [M] \rangle$ in dimension $4k$, which appears again in [03.06.04] (Pontryagin classes) and [03.09.10] (Atiyah-Singer index theorem). The foundational reason the formula works is exactly that signature and Pontryagin numbers both factor through the rational oriented bordism ring $\Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, and the multiplicative-sequence structure of the $L$-polynomial generalises the proportionality in dimension four to all dimensions. The central insight is that signature is multiplicative on products, and there is a unique multiplicative sequence in Pontryagin classes giving signature $1$ on $\mathbb{CP}^{2k}$ for every $k$ — this is exactly the $L$-genus. Putting these together, the signature is dual to a specific polynomial in Pontryagin numbers; the bridge to index theory is Atiyah-Singer, which identifies the signature with the index of the signature operator and the $L$-genus with its topological index.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

## Advanced results [Master]

### Signature as a bordism invariant of $4k$-manifolds

The structural fact that the signature factors through oriented bordism is itself an instance of a general principle: any homomorphism from the cobordism ring $\Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}}$ to a commutative ring is a **genus**. Thom 1954 [ref: TODO_REF Thom 1954] established the rational structure $\Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q} \cong \mathbb{Q}[\mathbb{CP}^2, \mathbb{CP}^4, \mathbb{CP}^6, \ldots]$ as a polynomial ring generated by the even complex projective spaces.

**Theorem (Thom 1954, oriented bordism rationally).** *The signature $\mathrm{sig} : \Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}} \to \mathbb{Z}$ is a well-defined ring homomorphism: additive on disjoint union, multiplicative on Cartesian product, and zero on null-bordant manifolds.*

The bordism invariance follows from the half-dimensional-Lagrangian argument in dimension four, extended to dimension $4k$ by the standard Mayer-Vietoris / Lefschetz-duality sequence on the bounding $(4k+1)$-manifold $W$: the image of $H^{2k}(W; \mathbb{R}) \to H^{2k}(\partial W; \mathbb{R})$ is half-dimensional and isotropic for the intersection form on $\partial W$, forcing $\mathrm{sig}(\partial W) = 0$. The multiplicativity $\mathrm{sig}(M \times N) = \mathrm{sig}(M) \cdot \mathrm{sig}(N)$ follows from the Künneth decomposition: $H^*(M \times N) = H^*(M) \otimes H^*(N)$ as graded rings, and the intersection form of $M \times N$ in middle degree decomposes as a tensor product of the intersection forms of $M$ and $N$ together with off-diagonal hyperbolic pieces with signature zero [ref: TODO_REF Milnor-Stasheff §19].

**Theorem (Borel-Hirzebruch).** *Any ring homomorphism $\varphi : \Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q} \to \mathbb{Q}$ is uniquely determined by its values $\varphi[\mathbb{CP}^{2k}]$ for $k \geq 1$.*

The polynomial-ring structure of $\Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ means a genus is a tuple of rationals $(\varphi[\mathbb{CP}^2], \varphi[\mathbb{CP}^4], \varphi[\mathbb{CP}^6], \ldots)$, one number per even complex projective space. The signature corresponds to the tuple $(1, 1, 1, \ldots)$. This is the structural reason Hirzebruch's formula is determined uniquely: there is exactly one multiplicative sequence in Pontryagin classes whose evaluation on $\mathbb{CP}^{2k}$ equals $1$ for every $k$, and that sequence is $\{L_k\}$.

### Multiplicative sequences and the $L$-polynomial

Hirzebruch's deepest organisational insight was the systematic packaging of $\Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}}$-genera via formal power series. Given a formal power series

$$
Q(x) = 1 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + a_3 x^3 + \cdots \in \mathbb{Q}[[x]],
$$

the associated **multiplicative sequence** $\{K_n\}$ is defined as follows. Consider the formal product

$$
\prod_{j=1}^{\infty} Q(x_j) = 1 + K_1(x_1, x_2, \ldots) + K_2(x_1, x_2, \ldots) + K_3(x_1, x_2, \ldots) + \cdots
$$

where $K_n$ is the homogeneous degree-$n$ component. Each $K_n$ is symmetric in the $x_j$; rewriting $K_n$ as a polynomial in the elementary symmetric functions $\sigma_i = \sum_{j_1 < \cdots < j_i} x_{j_1} \cdots x_{j_i}$ and substituting $\sigma_i \mapsto p_i$ produces a polynomial $K_n(p_1, \ldots, p_n) \in \mathbb{Q}[p_1, \ldots, p_n]$ [ref: TODO_REF Hirzebruch §8].

**Theorem (Hirzebruch, classification of multiplicative sequences).** *The assignment $Q \mapsto \{K_n\}$ is a bijection between formal power series $Q(x) \in \mathbb{Q}[[x]]$ with constant term $1$ and multiplicative sequences in Pontryagin classes. A multiplicative sequence $K = \{K_n\}$ produces a genus $\varphi_K : \Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q} \to \mathbb{Q}$ via $\varphi_K[M] = \langle K_k(p_1, \ldots, p_k), [M] \rangle$ for $M^{4k}$.*

The signature is the genus $\varphi_L$ for the $L$-sequence with $Q_L(x) = \sqrt{x}/\tanh \sqrt{x}$. The $\hat{A}$-genus is the genus for $Q_{\hat{A}}(x) = (\sqrt{x}/2) / \sinh(\sqrt{x}/2)$. The Todd genus is the genus for $Q_{\mathrm{Td}}(x) = x/(1 - e^{-x})$, but expressed in Chern roots rather than Pontryagin roots; converting Chern data to Pontryagin data via the splitting principle of [03.06.04] aligns the conventions.

The defining property — what makes $Q_L(x) = \sqrt{x}/\tanh \sqrt{x}$ specifically the right power series for the signature — is the **normalisation** $\varphi_L[\mathbb{CP}^{2k}] = 1$ for every $k \geq 1$. Direct computation on $\mathbb{CP}^{2k}$ using the Euler sequence pulls back to the requirement that the coefficient of $h^{2k}$ in $((1+h)^{2k+1})$ matched with the formal $L$-series produces $1$. Working backwards from this constraint uniquely identifies the Taylor series of $Q_L$: the coefficients $1, 1/3, -1/45, 2/945, -1/4725, \ldots$ are exactly the values needed.

### Hirzebruch's signature formula and the $K3$ example

**Theorem (Hirzebruch signature formula, general dimension).** *For every closed oriented smooth $4k$-manifold $M$,*

$$
\mathrm{sig}(M) = \langle L_k(p_1(TM), \ldots, p_k(TM)), [M] \rangle.
$$

*Proof.* By Thom's theorem the rational oriented bordism ring is a polynomial ring on $[\mathbb{CP}^2], [\mathbb{CP}^4], [\mathbb{CP}^6], \ldots$. Both sides are $\mathbb{Q}$-linear functionals on $\Omega_{4k}^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ that are multiplicative on products. By the Borel-Hirzebruch determination of genera, both sides are uniquely determined by their values on $\mathbb{CP}^{2k}$ for $k \geq 1$. On $\mathbb{CP}^{2k}$, the signature is $1$ by direct intersection-form computation, and the value of $\langle L_k, [\mathbb{CP}^{2k}] \rangle$ is $1$ by the defining normalisation of the $L$-series. Hence both sides agree on the generators of the rational oriented bordism ring, and by multiplicativity and additivity they agree on all of $\Omega_{4k}^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, hence on $\Omega_{4k}^{\mathrm{SO}}$ since both are integer-valued. $\square$

**Worked computation on $K3$.** The $K3$ surface is the most-studied non-projective-space example. Its intersection form, computed from the Mukai lattice, is

$$
Q_{K3} = 2 (-E_8) \oplus 3 H,
$$

where $E_8$ is the $8 \times 8$ even unimodular positive-definite lattice and $H = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ has signature $0$. Therefore

$$
\mathrm{sig}(K3) = 2 \cdot (-8) + 3 \cdot 0 = -16.
$$

Hirzebruch's formula in dimension four predicts $p_1[K3] = 3 \cdot (-16) = -48$. This matches the direct Chern-class calculation: $K3$ is a Calabi-Yau surface with $c_1(K3) = 0$ and $c_2(K3) = \chi(K3) = 24$, so $p_1(TK3) = c_1^2 - 2 c_2 = 0 - 48 = -48$ as a real-cohomology class, and pairing with the fundamental class gives $-48$. The dimension-four Hirzebruch formula thus recovers a deep arithmetic fact (the $E_8$-structure of the $K3$ lattice) from purely characteristic-class data.

**Worked computation on $\mathbb{HP}^2$.** The quaternionic projective plane is an 8-manifold with $\mathrm{sig}(\mathbb{HP}^2) = 1$ and total Pontryagin class $p(T\mathbb{HP}^2) = (1+u)^6 / (1+4u)$ where $u$ generates $H^4(\mathbb{HP}^2; \mathbb{Z})$. Expanding modulo $u^3$ gives $p_1 = 2u$, $p_2 = 7 u^2$, hence $p_1^2[\mathbb{HP}^2] = 4$ and $p_2[\mathbb{HP}^2] = 7$. The $L_2$-formula evaluates as $(7 \cdot 7 - 4)/45 = 45/45 = 1$, confirming $\mathrm{sig}(\mathbb{HP}^2) = 1$. This was historically Hirzebruch's first substantive verification of the formula in dimension eight [ref: TODO_REF Hirzebruch 1953].

### Atiyah-Singer reformulation: signature as the index of the signature operator

Atiyah-Singer 1968 [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah-Singer 1968] reformulated Hirzebruch's signature theorem as a special case of their general index theorem. On a closed oriented Riemannian $4k$-manifold $M$, the **Hodge star operator** $\ast$ satisfies $\ast^2 = (-1)^{p(n-p)} = (-1)^{p(4k-p)}$ on $p$-forms; the involution $\tau$ on the de Rham complex defined by

$$
\tau(\omega) = i^{p(p-1) + 2k} \ast \omega
\quad \text{for } \omega \in \Omega^p(M)
$$

satisfies $\tau^2 = +1$, splitting $\Omega^\bullet(M) = \Omega^+ \oplus \Omega^-$ into $\pm 1$-eigenspaces. The **signature operator** is the first-order elliptic differential operator

$$
D^+ = d + d^\ast : \Omega^+(M) \to \Omega^-(M),
$$

whose adjoint is $D^- = d + d^\ast : \Omega^- \to \Omega^+$.

**Theorem (Atiyah-Singer, Index of Elliptic Operators III).** *The analytic index of $D^+$ equals the signature:*

$$
\mathrm{index}(D^+) = \dim \ker(D^+) - \dim \ker(D^-) = \mathrm{sig}(M).
$$

*The topological index of $D^+$ equals $\langle L_k(p_1, \ldots, p_k), [M] \rangle$, recovering Hirzebruch's formula as the analytic-equals-topological index identity.*

The analytic step identifies $\ker(D^+)$ and $\ker(D^-)$ with the $\pm 1$-eigenspaces of $\tau$ acting on harmonic middle-degree forms $\mathcal{H}^{2k}(M)$. By Hodge theory $\mathcal{H}^{2k}(M) \cong H^{2k}(M; \mathbb{R})$, and the involution $\tau$ on $\mathcal{H}^{2k}$ coincides (up to a normalisation constant) with the involution $\alpha \mapsto \ast \alpha$, whose $\pm 1$-eigenspaces are precisely the self-dual and anti-self-dual harmonic $2k$-forms; the intersection form $Q_M(\alpha, \beta) = \int_M \alpha \wedge \beta$ is positive on self-dual forms and negative on anti-self-dual forms (a real-elementary computation), giving

$$
\mathrm{index}(D^+) = b_+(M) - b_-(M) = \mathrm{sig}(M).
$$

The topological step expresses the topological index of $D^+$ via Atiyah-Singer's universal formula $\mathrm{index}(D) = \int_M \mathrm{ch}(\sigma(D)) \cdot \mathrm{Td}(TM_\mathbb{C}) / e(TM_\mathbb{R})$, and a direct computation on the symbol $\sigma(D^+)$ (which factors through the Thom class of $TM$) reduces this to $\langle L_k(p_1, \ldots, p_k), [M] \rangle$ [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah-Singer 1968]. The Atiyah-Singer reformulation places the Hirzebruch theorem inside a vastly larger framework: the signature operator is one of four canonical elliptic complexes (Dolbeault, Dirac, signature, Euler) whose indices recover the four classical genera (Todd, $\hat{A}$, signature/$L$, Euler).

**Synthesis.** The Hirzebruch signature theorem identifies signature with the $L$-genus, and this is exactly the structural content that organises four-dimensional topology. The foundational reason is the bordism invariance of both signature and Pontryagin numbers combined with the polynomial-ring structure of $\Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ on the complex projective space generators. The central insight is multiplicativity: a multiplicative sequence in Pontryagin classes is determined by its generating power series, and the $L$-series $Q_L(x) = \sqrt{x}/\tanh \sqrt{x}$ is pinned down uniquely by the normalisation $\varphi_L[\mathbb{CP}^{2k}] = 1$.

Putting these together with the Atiyah-Singer index theorem, the signature identifies the analytic index of the signature operator $D^+ = d + d^\ast$ on the de Rham complex with a specific cohomological pairing — this is exactly the analytic-topological duality that makes the signature both computable in middle-cohomology and recoverable from tangent-bundle data. The bridge to gauge theory is the Donaldson invariants of [03.07.05], where the signature pairing enters the dimension formula for the moduli space of self-dual connections; the bridge to four-manifold topology is the Rokhlin theorem, where the divisibility $\mathrm{sig}(M) \equiv 0 \pmod{16}$ for spin $4$-manifolds is the foundational integrality result. The pattern generalises in two directions simultaneously: the Atiyah-Singer index theorem [03.09.10] identifies every classical genus as the index of a canonical elliptic operator, and the multiplicative-sequence machinery of [03.06.04] identifies every characteristic-class polynomial as a genus on the rational oriented bordism ring.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Bordism invariance of signature).** *Let $M^{4k}$ be a closed oriented smooth manifold. If $M = \partial W$ for a compact oriented $(4k+1)$-manifold $W$, then $\mathrm{sig}(M) = 0$.*

*Proof.* Consider the long exact sequence of the pair $(W, M)$ in cohomology with real coefficients:

$$
\cdots \to H^{2k-1}(M) \to H^{2k}(W, M) \xrightarrow{j^\ast} H^{2k}(W) \xrightarrow{i^\ast} H^{2k}(M) \xrightarrow{\delta} H^{2k+1}(W, M) \to \cdots
$$

Let $V = i^\ast(H^{2k}(W; \mathbb{R})) \subset H^{2k}(M; \mathbb{R})$. We claim two facts: (i) $V$ is isotropic for $Q_M$, and (ii) $V$ is half-dimensional.

For (i), if $\alpha, \beta \in H^{2k}(W; \mathbb{R})$, then $\alpha \cup \beta \in H^{4k}(W; \mathbb{R})$ and the boundary pairing gives

$$
Q_M(i^\ast \alpha, i^\ast \beta) = \langle i^\ast(\alpha \cup \beta), [M] \rangle = \langle \alpha \cup \beta, i_\ast [M] \rangle = \langle \alpha \cup \beta, \partial [W] \rangle.
$$

By Stokes' theorem (in its Poincaré-duality formulation), $\langle \alpha \cup \beta, \partial [W] \rangle = \langle d(\alpha \cup \beta), [W] \rangle = 0$ since closed forms have zero differential. Hence $Q_M$ vanishes on $V \times V$.

For (ii), Lefschetz duality gives $H^{2k}(W; \mathbb{R}) \cong H_{2k+1}(W, M; \mathbb{R})$ and $H^{2k+1}(W, M; \mathbb{R}) \cong H_{2k}(W; \mathbb{R})$. Combining with the long exact sequence,

$$
\dim i^\ast(H^{2k}(W)) = \dim H^{2k}(W) - \dim \ker(i^\ast) = \dim H^{2k}(W) - \dim j^\ast(H^{2k}(W, M)).
$$

By Lefschetz duality $\dim H^{2k}(W, M) = \dim H^{2k+1}(W)$, and the half-sum relation $\dim H^{2k}(M) = 2 \dim V$ follows from a careful counting in the long exact sequence (Milnor-Stasheff §19, Lemma 19.1) [ref: TODO_REF Milnor-Stasheff §19]. Therefore $V$ is a Lagrangian subspace of $H^{2k}(M; \mathbb{R})$ for $Q_M$.

A non-degenerate symmetric bilinear form on $\mathbb{R}^n$ that admits a Lagrangian subspace has equal positive and negative indices, so $\mathrm{sig}(M) = b_+(M) - b_-(M) = 0$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Multiplicativity of signature on products).** *Let $M^{4k}$ and $N^{4l}$ be closed oriented smooth manifolds. Then*

$$
\mathrm{sig}(M \times N) = \mathrm{sig}(M) \cdot \mathrm{sig}(N).
$$

*Proof.* The middle cohomology of $M \times N$ in degree $2(k+l)$ decomposes via the Künneth formula as

$$
H^{2(k+l)}(M \times N; \mathbb{R}) = \bigoplus_{p+q = 2(k+l)} H^p(M; \mathbb{R}) \otimes H^q(N; \mathbb{R}).
$$

Partition the summands into three groups: the *diagonal block* $H^{2k}(M) \otimes H^{2l}(N)$, the *anti-diagonal block* $\bigoplus_{p + q = 2(k+l), p \neq 2k} H^p(M) \otimes H^q(N)$ pairs $(p,q)$ with $(4k-p, 4l-q)$, and the off-diagonal blocks.

The intersection form $Q_{M \times N}$ restricted to the diagonal block is exactly $Q_M \otimes Q_N$: for $\alpha_1 \otimes \beta_1, \alpha_2 \otimes \beta_2 \in H^{2k}(M) \otimes H^{2l}(N)$,

$$
Q_{M \times N}(\alpha_1 \otimes \beta_1, \alpha_2 \otimes \beta_2) = \langle (\alpha_1 \cup \alpha_2) \otimes (\beta_1 \cup \beta_2), [M] \times [N] \rangle = Q_M(\alpha_1, \alpha_2) \cdot Q_N(\beta_1, \beta_2).
$$

By Sylvester's law of inertia applied to a tensor product of forms, the signature of $Q_M \otimes Q_N$ is $\mathrm{sig}(M) \cdot \mathrm{sig}(N)$.

The intersection form on off-diagonal blocks $H^p(M) \otimes H^q(N) \oplus H^{4k-p}(M) \otimes H^{4l-q}(N)$ (for $p \neq 2k$) is a hyperbolic form: it pairs the two summands non-degenerately while vanishing on each summand individually. A hyperbolic form has equal positive and negative indices, hence contributes $0$ to the signature.

Adding contributions: $\mathrm{sig}(M \times N) = \mathrm{sig}(M) \cdot \mathrm{sig}(N) + 0 = \mathrm{sig}(M) \cdot \mathrm{sig}(N)$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Hirzebruch signature formula).** *For every closed oriented smooth manifold $M^{4k}$,*

$$
\mathrm{sig}(M) = \langle L_k(p_1(TM), \ldots, p_k(TM)), [M] \rangle,
$$

*where $L_k$ is the degree-$k$ component of the multiplicative sequence associated to $Q_L(x) = \sqrt{x} / \tanh \sqrt{x}$.*

*Proof.* Define two functionals $\sigma, \lambda : \Omega_{4k}^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q} \to \mathbb{Q}$ by $\sigma[M] := \mathrm{sig}(M)$ and $\lambda[M] := \langle L_k(p_1, \ldots, p_k), [M] \rangle$. We show $\sigma = \lambda$ on $\Omega_{4k}^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q}$.

Both $\sigma$ and $\lambda$ are well-defined (bordism invariance for $\sigma$ is Proposition 1; for $\lambda$ it follows from $p_i[M] = 0$ on null-bordant $M$, which is Stokes' theorem applied to $p_i(TM)$ restricted from $W$). Both are multiplicative on products: $\sigma$ by Proposition 2, $\lambda$ because $L_k$ is a multiplicative sequence ($L_{k+l}(p_1, \ldots, p_{k+l})_{M \times N} = L_k(p_1(TM), \ldots) \cdot L_l(p_1(TN), \ldots)$ by the formal-product definition of multiplicative sequence and the Künneth formula on $p_i(T(M \times N)) = p_i(TM) + p_i(TN)$ under the splitting).

By Thom's theorem [ref: TODO_REF Thom 1954], $\Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ is a polynomial ring generated by the classes $[\mathbb{CP}^{2k}]$, $k \geq 1$. The multiplicativity of $\sigma$ and $\lambda$ reduces the identity $\sigma = \lambda$ to verification on the generators $[\mathbb{CP}^{2k}]$.

On $\mathbb{CP}^{2k}$: the cohomology ring is $\mathbb{Z}[h]/(h^{2k+1})$ with $\langle h^{2k}, [\mathbb{CP}^{2k}] \rangle = 1$. The Euler sequence gives $c(T\mathbb{CP}^{2k}) = (1+h)^{2k+1}$, hence $c_1(T\mathbb{CP}^{2k}) = (2k+1) h$ and the Pontryagin classes satisfy

$$
p(T\mathbb{CP}^{2k}) = (1 + h^2)^{2k+1}
$$

after passing through the complexification convention. The signature is computed directly: $H^{2k}(\mathbb{CP}^{2k}; \mathbb{R}) = \mathbb{R}\langle h^k \rangle$ is one-dimensional, and $Q(h^k, h^k) = \langle h^{2k}, [\mathbb{CP}^{2k}] \rangle = 1 > 0$, so $b_+ = 1$, $b_- = 0$, $\sigma[\mathbb{CP}^{2k}] = 1$.

The $\lambda$-value on $\mathbb{CP}^{2k}$ is the coefficient of $h^{2k}$ in $\prod_{j=1}^{2k+1} Q_L(h^2) = (Q_L(h^2))^{2k+1}$, where each factor of $h^2$ corresponds to one Chern root squared. By residue calculus or direct expansion, this coefficient equals $1$ for every $k \geq 1$ — this is the defining property of $Q_L$ that pins down the Taylor series $Q_L(x) = 1 + x/3 - x^2/45 + \cdots$ uniquely [ref: TODO_REF Hirzebruch §8].

Since $\sigma[\mathbb{CP}^{2k}] = \lambda[\mathbb{CP}^{2k}] = 1$ for every $k \geq 1$ and both are multiplicative on $\Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, they agree on all of $\Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q}$. Both functionals are $\mathbb{Z}$-valued on $\Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}}$ (signature is integral by definition; Pontryagin numbers are integers and $L_k$ has rational coefficients, but the integer combinations cancel the denominators when paired against the fundamental class — this last fact is the integrality content of the theorem, established by the fact that both sides are integer-valued on every closed oriented $4k$-manifold). Therefore the identity holds in $\mathbb{Z}$ on $\Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}}$, completing the proof. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (Atiyah-Singer reformulation).** *Let $M^{4k}$ be a closed oriented Riemannian manifold. The signature operator $D^+ = d + d^\ast : \Omega^+(M) \to \Omega^-(M)$ has analytic index*

$$
\mathrm{index}(D^+) = \dim \ker D^+ - \dim \ker D^- = \mathrm{sig}(M),
$$

*and the topological index, computed by the Atiyah-Singer formula, equals $\langle L_k(p_1, \ldots, p_k), [M] \rangle$.*

*Proof sketch.* The analytic step: by Hodge theory, $\ker D^+ \subset \Omega^+$ consists of harmonic self-dual forms, and similarly $\ker D^- \subset \Omega^-$ consists of harmonic anti-self-dual forms. In middle degree $2k$, the involution $\tau = i^{p(p-1) + 2k} \ast$ specialises to a multiple of the Hodge star $\ast$ on $\mathcal{H}^{2k}(M)$, and the splitting $\mathcal{H}^{2k} = \mathcal{H}^+_{2k} \oplus \mathcal{H}^-_{2k}$ into $\pm 1$-eigenspaces of $\ast$ identifies with the splitting of $H^{2k}(M; \mathbb{R})$ by sign of the intersection form: $\dim \mathcal{H}^+_{2k} = b_+$ and $\dim \mathcal{H}^-_{2k} = b_-$. Off middle degree, $\tau$ swaps $\Omega^p$ with $\Omega^{4k-p}$ via the Hodge star up to a sign, and contributions of $\ker D^+$ and $\ker D^-$ from these complementary degrees cancel. Hence $\mathrm{index}(D^+) = b_+ - b_- = \mathrm{sig}(M)$.

The topological step: Atiyah-Singer's universal formula gives

$$
\mathrm{index}(D^+) = \int_M \frac{\mathrm{ch}(\sigma(D^+))}{e(TM_\mathbb{R})} \cdot \mathrm{Td}(TM \otimes \mathbb{C}),
$$

where $\sigma(D^+)$ is the symbol class in $K(T^\ast M)$. A direct computation (Atiyah-Singer 1968 III §6 [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah-Singer 1968]) on the symbol of the signature operator simplifies this integrand to $L_k(p_1, \ldots, p_k)$ after substituting the formal identities $\mathrm{ch}(\sigma(D^+)) / e = \prod_j (e^{x_j} + e^{-x_j})$ and $\mathrm{Td}(TM_\mathbb{C}) = \prod_j x_j / (1 - e^{-x_j})$, where $x_j$ are the Chern roots of $TM \otimes \mathbb{C}$ (equivalently, $y_j^2 = x_j x_{-j}$ are the formal Pontryagin roots). After algebraic simplification, the product collapses to $\prod_j y_j / \tanh y_j$, whose degree-$k$ component in $p_i = e_i(y_j^2)$ is precisely $L_k(p_1, \ldots, p_k)$. Pairing against $[M]$ recovers Hirzebruch's expression. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Hirzebruch 1953 [ref: TODO_REF Hirzebruch 1953] first proved the signature formula in dimension eight as part of his work on the Riemann-Roch theorem for complex manifolds, introducing the multiplicative-sequence formalism that would unify the Todd genus, the $\hat{A}$-genus, and the signature in a single framework. The full general-dimension statement appeared in Hirzebruch's *Topological Methods in Algebraic Geometry* (1956, third edition 1966) [ref: TODO_REF Hirzebruch §8], which became the canonical reference for the entire characteristic-class apparatus of complex manifold theory.

The bordism-theoretic proof rests on Thom's earlier work [ref: TODO_REF Thom 1954], establishing the polynomial-ring structure of $\Omega_*^{\mathrm{SO}} \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ on the projective-space generators — a result Hirzebruch himself acknowledged as the structural foundation of his theorem. Thom-Pontryagin theory in turn rests on Pontryagin's earlier work on characteristic classes of real bundles and on the cobordism construction of associated framed manifolds.

The decisive twentieth-century reframing came in Atiyah-Singer 1968 [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah-Singer 1968], identifying Hirzebruch's formula as a special case of their general index theorem applied to the signature operator $D^+ = d + d^\ast$. The signature operator joins the Dolbeault operator (whose index is the holomorphic Euler characteristic, recovering the Hirzebruch-Riemann-Roch formula), the Dirac operator (whose index is the $\hat{A}$-genus), and the Euler operator (whose index is the Euler characteristic) as the four canonical elliptic complexes whose analytic indices match Hirzebruch's four canonical multiplicative sequences. The signature theorem is no longer just a topological identity; it is the analytic-topological duality that organises four-manifold topology, gauge theory, and the global analysis of elliptic operators into a single framework.
