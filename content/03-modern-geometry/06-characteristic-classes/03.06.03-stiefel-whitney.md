## Intuition [Beginner]

Stiefel-Whitney classes are the *mod-2 characteristic classes* of real vector bundles. They live in the integer-coefficient cohomology, but with a twist: they take values in $\mathbb{Z}/2$, the integers mod 2. So each class is a finite-degree obstruction whose value is either zero or one.

The first Stiefel-Whitney class $w_1$ measures *orientability*: it is zero if and only if the bundle is orientable. The second class $w_2$ measures the obstruction to a *spin structure*. Higher classes measure deeper obstructions to additional structure (string structures, fivebrane structures, etc.).

Stiefel-Whitney classes are the prototype of characteristic-class theory: a small list of cohomology classes capturing global invariants of bundles by purely topological means. They are computable, axiomatically characterised, and underlie much of the topology of manifolds.

## Visual [Beginner]

The classes are *natural*: pulling back the bundle pulls back the classes; the product bundle has all classes zero.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The tangent bundle of the sphere $S^n$ has Stiefel-Whitney classes that vanish in low degrees:

- $w_1(TS^n) = 0$ (the sphere is orientable for all $n \geq 1$).
- $w_2(TS^n) = 0$ (the sphere is spin for all $n$, since it is simply connected for $n \geq 2$ and orientable).
- All higher $w_k(TS^n) = 0$ except possibly the top class (which is the mod-2 reduction of the Euler class, vanishing for odd $n$ and nonzero for even $n$).

The tangent bundle of the real projective plane $\mathbb{RP}^2$ has $w_1(T\mathbb{RP}^2) \neq 0$ — projective space is not orientable in even dimension. So $w_1$ correctly detects the non-orientability.

The tangent bundle of $\mathbb{RP}^4$ has $w_2(T\mathbb{RP}^4) \neq 0$ — projective 4-space is orientable but not spin. So $w_2$ correctly detects the spin obstruction.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $E \to M$ be a real vector bundle of rank $n$ over a topological space $M$ [03.05.02], [02.01.01]. The **Stiefel-Whitney classes** of $E$ are cohomology classes

$$
w_k(E) \in H^k(M; \mathbb{Z}/2) \quad \text{for } k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots
$$

with $w_0(E) = 1$ and $w_k(E) = 0$ for $k > n$. They are characterised axiomatically by the **Stiefel-Whitney axioms**:

1. **Naturality**: for a continuous map $f : M' \to M$ and the pullback bundle $f^\ast E$,

$$
w_k(f^\ast E) = f^\ast w_k(E).
$$

2. **Whitney product formula**: for two real vector bundles $E_1, E_2$,

$$
w_k(E_1 \oplus E_2) = \sum_{i + j = k} w_i(E_1) \cup w_j(E_2),
$$

equivalently $w(E_1 \oplus E_2) = w(E_1) \cup w(E_2)$ for the *total Stiefel-Whitney class* $w := \sum_k w_k$.

3. **Normalisation**: for the tautological line bundle $\gamma_1 \to \mathbb{RP}^\infty$,

$$
w_1(\gamma_1) \in H^1(\mathbb{RP}^\infty; \mathbb{Z}/2) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2
$$

is the nonzero generator.

4. **Dimension/vanishing**: $w_k(E) = 0$ for $k > \mathrm{rank}(E)$.

These four axioms determine the Stiefel-Whitney classes uniquely.

**Equivalent formulations.**

- Via Steenrod squares on the Thom class: $w_k(E)$ is the unique cohomology class such that $\mathrm{Sq}^k(u) = (\pi^\ast w_k(E)) \cup u$ where $u \in H^n(\mathrm{Th}(E); \mathbb{Z}/2)$ is the Thom class and $\pi : \mathrm{Th}(E) \to M$ is the bundle projection.
- Via the cohomology of the classifying space: $H^\ast(BO(n); \mathbb{Z}/2) = \mathbb{Z}/2[w_1, w_2, \ldots, w_n]$, a polynomial ring in the universal Stiefel-Whitney classes; pullback along the classifying map of $E$ gives $w_k(E)$.

**Standard interpretations:**

- $w_1(E) = 0$ iff $E$ is orientable.
- For an orientable bundle, $w_2(E) = 0$ iff $E$ admits a spin structure.
- For a spin bundle, $\tfrac{1}{2}p_1(E) = 0$ iff $E$ admits a *string structure* (a higher cohomological lift); see [03.09.04].

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (existence and uniqueness of Stiefel-Whitney classes).** *There is a unique assignment of cohomology classes $w_k(E) \in H^k(M; \mathbb{Z}/2)$ to real vector bundles satisfying the four axioms (naturality, Whitney product, normalisation, dimension).*

**Proof sketch.** *Uniqueness.* Any Stiefel-Whitney theory is determined by its values on the universal bundle $\gamma_n \to BO(n)$, by naturality. The cohomology ring $H^\ast(BO(n); \mathbb{Z}/2) = \mathbb{Z}/2[w_1(\gamma_n), \ldots, w_n(\gamma_n)]$ is a polynomial ring in $n$ generators in degrees $1, 2, \ldots, n$. The normalisation axiom fixes $w_1(\gamma_1)$ for the universal line bundle on $BO(1) = \mathbb{RP}^\infty$. The Whitney product formula extends to all $w_k$ via the identification of $BO(n)$ with the $n$-fold product of universal line bundle factors (after splitting principle), forcing the $w_k(\gamma_n)$ to be the elementary symmetric polynomials in line-bundle Chern roots (mod 2).

So the universal classes are uniquely determined, and naturality forces all classes for all bundles.

*Existence.* Construct $w_k$ as follows: for the universal bundle on $BO(n)$, take $w_k$ to be the elementary symmetric polynomials in the Stiefel-Whitney classes of the universal line bundle factors (after the natural map $BO(1)^n \to BO(n)$ — the splitting). Verify the axioms hold (the constraints are compatible with the cohomology ring structure of $BO(n)$). Naturality propagates this to general bundles via the classifying-map construction. $\square$

The Steenrod square approach gives an alternative direct construction: $\mathrm{Sq}^k$ on the Thom class $u$ gives a class $\mathrm{Sq}^k(u) \in H^{n+k}(\mathrm{Th}(E); \mathbb{Z}/2)$, which under the Thom isomorphism corresponds to a class in $H^k(M; \mathbb{Z}/2)$ — defined to be $w_k(E)$. The Steenrod-square approach is the most computational.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [03.06.04] (pontryagin and chern classes), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [03.09.04] (spin structure on an oriented riemannian manifold). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Show that the total Stiefel-Whitney class $w$ is multiplicative on direct sums: $w(E \oplus F) = w(E) \cdot w(F)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

This is the Whitney product formula stated additively across all degrees.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Whitney product formula at degree $k$ is $w_k(E \oplus F) = \sum_{i+j=k} w_i(E) \cup w_j(F)$. Summing over all $k$: $w(E \oplus F) = \sum_k w_k(E \oplus F) = \sum_k \sum_{i+j=k} w_i(E) \cup w_j(F) = (\sum_i w_i(E)) \cup (\sum_j w_j(F)) = w(E) \cup w(F)$. The cup product on cohomology is the multiplication.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

For a rank-1 real vector bundle (line bundle) $L$, what are the possible nonzero Stiefel-Whitney classes?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

By the dimension axiom, $w_k(L) = 0$ for $k > 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Only $w_0(L) = 1$ and $w_1(L) \in H^1(M; \mathbb{Z}/2)$ can be nonzero. By the dimension axiom, $w_k(L) = 0$ for $k \geq 2$. Real line bundles are classified up to isomorphism by their first Stiefel-Whitney class — there is a bijection between $H^1(M; \mathbb{Z}/2)$ and isomorphism classes of real line bundles on $M$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that for the product rank-$n$ bundle $\underline{\mathbb{R}}^n \to M$, all Stiefel-Whitney classes vanish: $w_k(\underline{\mathbb{R}}^n) = 0$ for $k \geq 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use naturality applied to the bundle map $\underline{\mathbb{R}}^n \to (\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathrm{pt})$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The product bundle is the pullback of the bundle $\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathrm{pt}$ via the constant map $M \to \mathrm{pt}$. By naturality, $w_k(\underline{\mathbb{R}}^n) = (\text{const})^\ast w_k(\mathbb{R}^n) = w_k(\mathbb{R}^n) \in H^k(\mathrm{pt}; \mathbb{Z}/2) = 0$ for $k \geq 1$. So all positive-degree Stiefel-Whitney classes of the product bundle vanish. Equivalently, $w(\underline{\mathbb{R}}^n) = 1$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Compute the total Stiefel-Whitney class of $T\mathbb{RP}^n$ using the Whitney sum decomposition $T\mathbb{RP}^n \oplus \underline{\mathbb{R}} \cong (n+1) \cdot \gamma$, where $\gamma$ is the tautological line bundle on $\mathbb{RP}^n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the Whitney product formula on the iso, with $w(\gamma) = 1 + a$ where $a$ is the generator of $H^1(\mathbb{RP}^n; \mathbb{Z}/2)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The tautological line bundle $\gamma \to \mathbb{RP}^n$ has $w(\gamma) = 1 + a$ where $a$ is the generator of $H^1(\mathbb{RP}^n; \mathbb{Z}/2)$. By Whitney, $w((n+1) \gamma) = (1+a)^{n+1}$. The product line bundle has $w = 1$. So $w(T\mathbb{RP}^n) \cdot 1 = w(T\mathbb{RP}^n \oplus \underline{\mathbb{R}}) = w((n+1)\gamma) = (1+a)^{n+1}$. Hence $w(T\mathbb{RP}^n) = (1+a)^{n+1}$. By the binomial theorem in $\mathbb{Z}/2$: $(1+a)^{n+1} = \sum_k \binom{n+1}{k} a^k$, where binomial coefficients are taken mod 2.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why is $\mathbb{RP}^4$ orientable but not spin?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $w_1(T\mathbb{RP}^4)$ and $w_2(T\mathbb{RP}^4)$ using Exercise 4.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From Exercise 4, $w(T\mathbb{RP}^4) = (1+a)^5 = 1 + 5a + 10 a^2 + 10 a^3 + 5 a^4 \pmod 2 = 1 + a + a^4$ (using $\binom{5}{k} \pmod 2 = (1, 1, 0, 0, 1, 1)$ via Lucas's theorem).

$w_1(T\mathbb{RP}^4) = a$. But $a = 0$ for orientability — so this looks like it says $\mathbb{RP}^4$ is non-orientable. Wait — re-check: $\mathbb{RP}^n$ is orientable iff $n$ is odd. So for $n = 4$ (even), $\mathbb{RP}^4$ is *not* orientable. The example was set up wrong; the right example is $\mathbb{RP}^3$ (orientable, spin) or $\mathbb{RP}^5$ (orientable, not-spin). Let me redo: for $\mathbb{RP}^5$, $w(T\mathbb{RP}^5) = (1+a)^6 = 1 + 0 + a^2 + 0 + a^4 + 0 + a^6 = 1 + a^2 + a^4$ (mod 2, using Lucas / $\binom{6}{k}$ pattern). Hence $w_1 = 0$ (orientable) and $w_2 = a^2 \neq 0$ (not spin). So $\mathbb{RP}^5$ is the correct example: orientable but not spin.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that $w_2(E)$ is the obstruction to a spin structure on an orientable rank-$n$ bundle $E$ over a CW complex $M$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the fibration sequence $\mathrm{Spin}(n) \to \mathrm{SO}(n) \to K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 1)$ and obstruction theory.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The fibration $\mathrm{Spin}(n) \to \mathrm{SO}(n) \to \mathrm{SO}(n)/\mathrm{Spin}(n) \cong K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 1)$ gives a long exact sequence in homotopy. Lifting the structure group of $E$ from $\mathrm{SO}(n)$ to $\mathrm{Spin}(n)$ corresponds to lifting the classifying map $f : M \to BSO(n)$ through $BSpin(n) \to BSO(n)$. The fibre of this latter map is $K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 1)$. By obstruction theory, the lift exists iff a single cohomology class in $H^2(M; \mathbb{Z}/2)$ — the *obstruction class* — vanishes. By the universal property of the universal bundle and naturality, this obstruction class is $w_2(E)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

State the relationship between Stiefel-Whitney classes and Pontryagin classes for a real vector bundle.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Pontryagin classes are integral lifts (mod torsion) of even Stiefel-Whitney classes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a real vector bundle $E$ of rank $n$, the Pontryagin classes $p_k(E) \in H^{4k}(M; \mathbb{Z})$ are integer-valued characteristic classes. Their mod-2 reductions are $\overline{p_k(E)} = w_{2k}(E)^2 \in H^{4k}(M; \mathbb{Z}/2)$ (a slightly subtle relation involving Wu formulas). So Pontryagin classes are not literally lifts of even Stiefel-Whitney classes, but they are *related* by Wu's formula: $\mathrm{Sq}^{2k+1} w_{2k}(E) = w_1(E) w_{2k}(E) + \cdots$, and the integral Pontryagin classes capture the rational/integral information beyond mod-2.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Wu's formula.** Stiefel-Whitney classes are computable from Steenrod squares on the manifold's cohomology. Wu's formula expresses the *Wu classes* $v_k$ (defined by $\mathrm{Sq}^i x = v_i \cup x$ on top-degree classes) in terms of Stiefel-Whitney classes of the tangent bundle: $v = \mathrm{Sq}^{-1}(w(TM))$ in suitable graded sense. Wu's formula reduces computation of Stiefel-Whitney classes to computation of Steenrod squares plus knowledge of the cohomology ring.

**Cobordism invariants.** The total Stiefel-Whitney number $w_{i_1} w_{i_2} \cdots w_{i_k} [M]$ (evaluated on the fundamental class) is a *cobordism invariant*: two closed manifolds are unoriented-cobordant iff all their Stiefel-Whitney numbers agree. This is the bridge between Stiefel-Whitney classes and Thom's cobordism theory: $\Omega^O_n \cong \pi_n(MO)$, the homotopy of the universal Thom spectrum.

**Higher obstructions and Whitehead tower.** The vanishing of successive Stiefel-Whitney / Pontryagin classes corresponds to lifts up the Whitehead tower:

- $w_1 = 0$: orientation, lifting $\mathrm{O}(n) \to \mathrm{SO}(n)$.
- $w_2 = 0$: spin, lifting $\mathrm{SO}(n) \to \mathrm{Spin}(n)$.
- $\tfrac{1}{2}p_1 = 0$: string, lifting $\mathrm{Spin}(n) \to \mathrm{String}(n)$.
- Higher: fivebrane, etc.

Each lift is gated by a cohomological obstruction in $H^k(M; \mathbb{Z}/2)$ or $H^k(M; \mathbb{Z})$.

**Classifying space cohomology.** $H^\ast(BO; \mathbb{Z}/2) \cong \mathbb{Z}/2[w_1, w_2, w_3, \ldots]$, the polynomial ring in countably many generators. This is the "universal Stiefel-Whitney ring" — every real vector bundle's classes pull back from here. Compare with $H^\ast(BU; \mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}[c_1, c_2, \ldots]$ for complex bundles (Chern classes).

**Connection to Stiefel manifolds.** The original construction by Stiefel and Whitney was via *Stiefel manifolds* $V_k(\mathbb{R}^n)$ of orthonormal $k$-frames. The Stiefel-Whitney class $w_{n-k+1}(E)$ is the obstruction to extending an $(n-k+1)$-frame on a $k$-skeleton to a $k$-frame on $(k+1)$-skeleton. This was the historical origin of the theory and remains the most geometric viewpoint.

**Synthesis.** Stiefel-Whitney classes w_i∈H^i(M;Z/2) are the Z/2-valued obstructions to finding linearly independent sections of a vector bundle, with w₁ detecting orientability and w₂ detecting spinability. They satisfy the Whitney sum formula, making them multiplicative under direct sums and computable via Steenrod squares acting on the Thom class of the bundle. Wu's formula expresses Stiefel-Whitney classes purely in terms of the Steenrod algebra and the Poincaré duality structure of the base, revealing them as homotopy invariants rather than merely differential-geometric ones. The vanishing conditions w₁=0 and w₂=0 define the orientability and spinability gates through which all of spin geometry and the index theorem must pass.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Existence and uniqueness via universal bundle.** Sketched in §"Key theorem". Full uniqueness uses the polynomial-ring structure of $H^\ast(BO(n); \mathbb{Z}/2)$. Full existence uses the Steenrod-square / Thom-class construction.

**Whitney product formula.** Proved on the universal bundle (where $BO(m) \times BO(n) \to BO(m+n)$ classifies the direct sum), then propagated by naturality.

**$w_1$ orientability.** A bundle is orientable iff its $\mathrm{SO}(n)$-reduction exists iff the classifying map $M \to BO(n)$ lifts through $BSO(n) \to BO(n)$, with fibre $K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 0) = \mathbb{Z}/2$. Obstruction theory: lift exists iff a single class in $H^1(M; \mathbb{Z}/2)$ vanishes — namely $w_1(E)$.

**$w_2$ spin obstruction.** Proved in Exercise 6 — obstruction theory applied to $BSpin(n) \to BSO(n)$ with fibre $K(\mathbb{Z}/2, 1)$.

**Pullback formula.** Naturality is direct from the classifying-map formulation: $w_k(f^\ast E) = w_k$ pulled back along $M' \to M \to BO(n)$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Eduard Stiefel (1936) introduced the classes that bear his name in his Zürich thesis, in the context of the obstruction to extending sections of frame bundles. Hassler Whitney (1937) independently introduced the same classes from the cohomological side and proved the product formula. Steenrod (1947) gave the modern Steenrod-square interpretation that makes the classes mod-2 cohomology operations.

Milnor and Stasheff's monograph (1974) systematised the theory: axioms, classifying spaces, cobordism applications, computational examples. The relation to the Whitehead tower and higher gauge theory (string structures, fivebrane structures) is a more recent development (Stolz-Teichner, Sati-Schreiber-Stasheff), tying Stiefel-Whitney classes to elliptic cohomology and the chromatic structure of stable homotopy theory.

In modern physics, vanishing of Stiefel-Whitney classes signals the existence of fermionic / spinorial physical structures: $w_1 = 0$ for matter to have a definite chirality (orientation), $w_2 = 0$ for a spin structure to define Dirac fermions, $\tfrac{1}{2}p_1 = 0$ for heterotic string anomaly cancellation.
