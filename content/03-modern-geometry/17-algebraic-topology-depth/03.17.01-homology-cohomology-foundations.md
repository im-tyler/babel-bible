## Intuition [Beginner]

Homology counts the holes of a space, one count for each dimension. Where the fundamental group records the loops you cannot shrink, homology records, in each dimension $n$, how many independent "$n$-dimensional holes" the space has. The counts are packaged as abelian groups $H_0$, $H_1$, $H_2$, and so on.

The zeroth group $H_0$ counts connected pieces: a space with three disjoint chunks has $H_0$ equal to three copies of $\mathbb{Z}$. The first group $H_1$ counts loops that are not filled in by a surface. The second group $H_2$ counts enclosed voids, like the hollow inside of a sphere $S^2$.

The difference from the fundamental group is that homology is abelian and computable. Where $\pi_1$ can be a wildly non-abelian group, $H_1$ is always abelian — it is the abelian shadow of $\pi_1$. And higher homology $H_2, H_3, \dots$ captures higher-dimensional holes that the fundamental group never sees.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture three spaces side by side: the sphere $S^2$, the torus $T^2$, and the double torus (a figure-eight shaped doughnut with two holes). Each has one connected piece, so $H_0$ is $\mathbb{Z}$ for all three. But the higher groups differ, and the differences measure the holes.

![Three spaces with their homology groups labelled: the sphere has one two-dimensional void, the torus has two independent one-dimensional loops and one two-dimensional void, the double torus has four one-dimensional loops and one two-dimensional void. A table beneath lists the group H_n for n = 0, 1, 2 next to each shape.](/img/homology-cohomology-foundations-placeholder.svg)

| Space | $H_0$ | $H_1$ | $H_2$ | What the counts mean |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sphere $S^2$ | $\mathbb{Z}$ | $0$ | $\mathbb{Z}$ | one void, no un-filled loops |
| Torus $T^2$ | $\mathbb{Z}$ | $\mathbb{Z}^2$ | $\mathbb{Z}$ | two loops (longitude, meridian), one void |
| Double torus | $\mathbb{Z}$ | $\mathbb{Z}^4$ | $\mathbb{Z}$ | four loops, one void |

The number of copies of $\mathbb{Z}$ in $H_1$ is the number of independent one-dimensional holes; the number in $H_2$ is the number of independent enclosed voids.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute the homology of the torus $T^2$ using its cell structure. Build the torus as a square whose edges are glued in pairs: the two horizontal edges glue to one loop $a$, the two vertical edges glue to a second loop $b$, and the corners all meet at a single point.

This cell structure has one $0$-cell (the corner point), two $1$-cells (the loops $a$ and $b$), and one $2$-cell (the square itself). So the chain groups are $C_0 = \mathbb{Z}$, $C_1 = \mathbb{Z}^2$, and $C_2 = \mathbb{Z}$.

Now read off the boundary maps. Each $1$-cell starts and ends at the single $0$-cell, and the two endpoint signs cancel. So the boundary map from $C_1$ down to $C_0$ sends every $1$-cell to zero. The boundary of the $2$-cell, read around the square, is $a + b - a - b$, which equals zero. So both boundary maps vanish.

Reading off the homology: $H_0 = \mathbb{Z}$ (one connected piece), $H_1 = \mathbb{Z}^2$ (the two free loops $a$ and $b$), and $H_2 = \mathbb{Z}$ (the whole torus surface, with zero boundary, is a genuine two-dimensional hole). The same recipe on the sphere $S^2$ — built from one $0$-cell and one $2$-cell with nothing in between — gives $H_0 = \mathbb{Z}$, $H_1 = 0$, $H_2 = \mathbb{Z}$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **chain complex** $(C_*, \partial)$ over a commutative ring $R$ is a sequence of $R$-modules and $R$-linear maps

$$
\cdots \xrightarrow{\partial_{n+2}} C_{n+1} \xrightarrow{\partial_{n+1}} C_n \xrightarrow{\partial_n} C_{n-1} \xrightarrow{\partial_{n-1}} \cdots
$$

with $\partial_n \circ \partial_{n+1} = 0$ for every $n$. Elements of $C_n$ are **$n$-chains**; elements of $\ker \partial_n$ are **$n$-cycles**; elements of $\operatorname{im} \partial_{n+1}$ are **$n$-boundaries**. The condition $\partial^2 = 0$ says every boundary is a cycle. The **$n$-th homology** is

$$
H_n(C_*) := \ker \partial_n \,/\, \operatorname{im} \partial_{n+1}.
$$

**Singular homology.** For a topological space $X$ [02.01.01], the **standard $n$-simplex** is $\Delta^n = \{ (t_0, \dots, t_n) \in \mathbb{R}^{n+1} : t_i \geq 0,\ \sum t_i = 1 \}$. A **singular $n$-simplex** is any continuous map $\sigma : \Delta^n \to X$. The **singular chain group** $C_n(X; R)$ is the free $R$-module on the set of singular $n$-simplices. The **boundary operator** is the alternating restriction to faces:

$$
\partial_n(\sigma) = \sum_{i=0}^{n} (-1)^i \, \sigma|_{[v_0, \dots, \hat v_i, \dots, v_n]},
$$

where the hat denotes omission of the $i$-th vertex. A signed-pair cancellation argument gives $\partial_{n-1} \circ \partial_n = 0$, so $(C_*(X; R), \partial)$ is a chain complex. Its homology is the **singular homology** $H_n(X; R)$.

**Simplicial and cellular homology.** If $X$ is a $\Delta$-complex or a simplicial complex, the chain complex is the free module on the combinatorial simplices, with boundary given by the same signed face formula; this is **simplicial homology** [03.12.12]. If $X$ is a CW complex [03.12.10] with $n$-skeleton $X^n$, the **cellular chain complex** has $C_n^{\mathrm{CW}}(X) = H_n(X^n, X^{n-1}; R)$, a free $R$-module on the $n$-cells, with boundary the connecting map of the triple $(X^n, X^{n-1}, X^{n-2})$. The **cellular boundary formula** computes this degree by degree: the coefficient of a cell $e^n_\beta$ in $\partial(e^n_\alpha)$ is the degree of the composite $S^{n-1}_\alpha \to X^{n-1} \to S^{n-1}_\beta$.

**Relative homology and pairs.** For a pair $(X, A)$ with $A \subset X$, the relative chain complex is the quotient $C_*(X, A) = C_*(X) / C_*(A)$, and $H_n(X, A; R)$ is its homology. The short exact sequence of chain complexes $0 \to C_*(A) \to C_*(X) \to C_*(X, A) \to 0$ induces a **long exact sequence of a pair**.

**Cohomology.** Applying the contravariant functor $\operatorname{Hom}_R(-, M)$ to a chain complex reverses the arrows and produces a **cochain complex** $(C^*, d)$ with $d^n : C^n \to C^{n+1}$ and $d \circ d = 0$. The **$n$-th cohomology** is $H^n(C^*) = \ker d^n / \operatorname{im} d^{n-1}$. For singular cochains $C^n(X; M) = \operatorname{Hom}(C_n(X), M)$ this gives **singular cohomology** $H^n(X; M)$.

**The cup product.** The diagonal map $\Delta : X \to X \times X$ and the cochain cross product combine to give a product on cohomology. For $[\varphi] \in H^p(X; R)$ and $[\psi] \in H^q(X; R)$, the **cup product** is

$$
[\varphi] \smile [\psi] := [\Delta^*(\varphi \times \psi)] \in H^{p+q}(X; R).
$$

Under $\smile$, the direct sum $H^*(X; R) = \bigoplus_n H^n(X; R)$ becomes a graded-commutative ring, the **cohomology ring**. Cohomology, not homology, carries this multiplicative structure: this is the decisive reason cohomology is the more powerful invariant for applications.

**de Rham cohomology.** On a smooth manifold $M$ [02.01.01, 48.01.01], the exterior derivative $d : \Omega^p(M) \to \Omega^{p+1}(M)$ on differential $p$-forms satisfies $d \circ d = 0$, giving the **de Rham complex** $(\Omega^*(M), d)$. Its cohomology is the **de Rham cohomology** $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)$, a real vector space. **de Rham's theorem** identifies it with singular cohomology with real coefficients: $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \cong H^*(M; \mathbb{R})$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (long exact sequence of a pair).** *Let $(X, A)$ be a pair of topological spaces with $A \subset X$, and fix a coefficient ring $R$. There is a long exact sequence*

$$
\cdots \longrightarrow H_n(A; R) \xrightarrow{\ i_*\ } H_n(X; R) \xrightarrow{\ j_*\ } H_n(X, A; R) \xrightarrow{\ \partial \ } H_{n-1}(A; R) \longrightarrow \cdots
$$

*where $i_*$ is induced by the inclusion $i : A \hookrightarrow X$, $j_*$ by the quotient $j : (X, \varnothing) \to (X, A)$, and $\partial$ is the connecting homomorphism. The sequence is natural in the pair.*

This is the content of the **exactness axiom** of Eilenberg-Steenrod: the proof derives it from a purely algebraic fact about short exact sequences of chain complexes.

**Proof.** The inclusion $i : A \hookrightarrow X$ induces an injective chain map $i_\# : C_*(A) \to C_*(X)$ (a singular simplex of $A$ is, by inclusion, a singular simplex of $X$). The quotient map $j : C_*(X) \to C_*(X)/C_*(A) = C_*(X, A)$ is surjective. These fit into a short exact sequence of chain complexes

$$
0 \longrightarrow C_*(A) \xrightarrow{i_\#} C_*(X) \xrightarrow{j_\#} C_*(X, A) \longrightarrow 0
$$

that commutes with the boundary operators (a cycle relation is preserved by both maps).

The long exact sequence is then the standard **zig-zag lemma** for a short exact sequence of chain complexes. Define the connecting homomorphism $\partial : H_n(X, A) \to H_{n-1}(A)$ by the recipe: lift a relative cycle $c \in C_n(X)$ with $j_\#(c)$ a relative cycle (so $\partial c \in C_{n-1}(A)$); since $c$ is a relative cycle, $\partial c$ lies in $\ker j_\# = \operatorname{im} i_\#$, i.e. $\partial c = i_\#(a)$ for some $a \in C_{n-1}(A)$. Because $\partial(\partial c) = 0$ and $i_\#$ is injective, $a$ is a cycle in $C_{n-1}(A)$. Set $\partial([j_\# c]) := [a] \in H_{n-1}(A)$.

*Well-definedness.* If $c$ is replaced by $c + \partial b$ for $b \in C_n(X)$, then $\partial(c + \partial b) = \partial c$, so $a$ is unchanged. If $j_\#(c)$ is replaced by $j_\#(c) + j_\#(\partial b') = j_\#(c + \partial b')$ with $b' \in C_n(X)$, again $\partial$ is unaffected. If a representative differs by a relative boundary $j_\#(\partial \tilde c)$ with $\tilde c \in C_{n+1}(X)$, then $a$ changes by the boundary $\partial (i_\#^{-1} \partial \tilde c)$, which is zero in homology. So $\partial$ is well-defined.

*Exactness at $H_n(X)$.* $\operatorname{im} i_* = \ker j_*$. A class $[c] \in H_n(X)$ maps to zero in $H_n(X, A)$ iff $j_\#(c) = \partial d$ for some relative chain $d \in C_{n+1}(X, A)$; lifting $d$ to $\tilde d \in C_{n+1}(X)$ gives $c - \partial \tilde d \in \ker j_\# = \operatorname{im} i_\#$, so $[c] = i_*[a]$ for some $[a] \in H_n(A)$. The reverse inclusion $\operatorname{im} i_* \subset \ker j_*$ holds because $j_\# \circ i_\# = 0$.

*Exactness at $H_n(X, A)$.* $\operatorname{im} j_* = \ker \partial$. A class $[j_\# c]$ has $\partial([j_\# c]) = 0$ iff the corresponding cycle $a \in C_{n-1}(A)$ is a boundary, $a = \partial a'$; adjusting $c$ by the chain that $a'$ bounds gives a cycle of $X$, hence lies in $\operatorname{im} j_*$. Again $\operatorname{im} j_* \subset \ker \partial$ is direct from $i_\# \circ \partial = 0$ on chains.

*Exactness at $H_{n-1}(A)$.* $\operatorname{im} \partial = \ker i_*$. A class $[a] \in H_{n-1}(A)$ lies in $\operatorname{im} \partial$ iff some $c \in C_n(X)$ has $\partial c = i_\#(a)$; this is exactly the condition that $i_*[a] = [i_\# a] = [\partial c] = 0$ in $H_n(X)$. Hence $\operatorname{im} \partial = \ker i_*$.

Naturality in the pair follows because every step — the lift, the boundary, the quotient — is functorial in chain maps. $\square$

The other four Eilenberg-Steenrod axioms are: **homotopy** (homotopic maps induce equal maps on homology), **excision** (if $Z \subset A$ with $\overline Z \subset \operatorname{int} A$, the inclusion $(X - Z, A - Z) \to (X, A)$ induces an isomorphism on homology), **dimension** ($H_0(\mathrm{pt}) = R$, $H_n(\mathrm{pt}) = 0$ for $n > 0$), and **additivity** (homology of a disjoint union is the direct sum). The five axioms together characterise ordinary homology on CW-pairs up to natural isomorphism [ref: TODO_REF EilenbergSteenrod1952].

**Bridge.** The long exact sequence of a pair builds toward the Mayer–Vietoris sequence and the cellular boundary formula, and it appears again in the construction of the Serre and Leray–Serre spectral sequences [03.13.01, 03.13.02], where it is iterated through the exact-couple machinery. The foundational reason this unit's machinery organises all of algebraic topology is that exactness, excision, and homotopy invariance are precisely the three properties any "homology-like" theory must satisfy; this is exactly the content of the Eilenberg-Steenrod axioms, and the bridge is that every generalised cohomology theory — K-theory, cobordism, sheaf cohomology — is obtained by varying these axioms.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).** *Homology of the $n$-sphere.*

Give the singular homology groups $H_k(S^n; \mathbb{Z})$ for every $k \geq 0$, where $S^n$ is the $n$-sphere with its standard CW structure (one $0$-cell and one $n$-cell).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The cellular chain complex has $C_0 = \mathbb{Z}$ and $C_n = \mathbb{Z}$, with every other $C_k = 0$. All boundary maps vanish (their source or target is zero).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$H_0(S^n; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}$, $H_n(S^n; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}$, and $H_k(S^n; \mathbb{Z}) = 0$ for $k \neq 0, n$. The two copies of $\mathbb{Z}$ correspond to: the single path component (degree $0$) and the single $n$-dimensional void (degree $n$). For $n = 1$ this recovers $H_0(S^1) = H_1(S^1) = \mathbb{Z}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, multiple choice).** *Euler characteristic.*

The Euler characteristic of a finite CW complex is $\chi(X) = \sum_n (-1)^n c_n$, where $c_n$ is the number of $n$-cells. For the torus $T^2$ (one $0$-cell, two $1$-cells, one $2$-cell) this gives:

A. $0$
B. $1$
C. $2$
D. $-1$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $1 - 2 + 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**A.** $0$. The Euler–Poincaré theorem says this also equals $\sum_n (-1)^n \operatorname{rank} H_n(X)$, which for the torus is $1 - 2 + 1 = 0$ as well. The sphere $S^2$ has $\chi = 1 - 0 + 1 = 2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).** *$H_0$ is path components.*

Let $X$ be a topological space. Prove that $H_0(X; R)$ is the free $R$-module on the set of path components of $X$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A $0$-cycle is a formal $R$-linear combination of points. Two points lie in the same $0$-homology class iff they are joined by a path. Show a path $\gamma : [0, 1] \to X$ is a singular $1$-simplex with $\partial \gamma = \gamma(1) - \gamma(0)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$C_0(X; R)$ is the free $R$-module on the points of $X$. Since $C_{-1} = 0$, every $0$-chain is a cycle, so $H_0 = C_0 / \operatorname{im} \partial_1$. A singular $1$-simplex is a path $\gamma : \Delta^1 \to X$, identified with a path $[0,1] \to X$ after reparametrisation; its boundary is $\partial_1(\gamma) = \gamma(1) - \gamma(0)$. So in the quotient, the endpoints of any path are identified.

Pick a representative point $x_\alpha$ in each path component $\alpha$. For any point $x$ in component $\alpha$, a path from $x_\alpha$ to $x$ gives $[x] = [x_\alpha]$ in $H_0$. Different components cannot be identified (there is no path between them). So $H_0(X; R) \cong \bigoplus_\alpha R$, one summand per path component. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).** *Cup product on the torus.*

The cohomology ring $H^*(T^2; \mathbb{Z})$ has generators $\alpha, \beta \in H^1(T^2; \mathbb{Z})$ dual to the two $1$-cells. State the cup products $\alpha \smile \alpha$, $\alpha \smile \beta$, $\beta \smile \alpha$, and $\beta \smile \beta$, and identify the top class.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Cohomology is graded-commutative: $\alpha \smile \beta = -\, \beta \smile \alpha$. The square of any degree-one class on a torus vanishes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\alpha \smile \alpha = 0$ and $\beta \smile \beta = 0$ (squares of odd-degree classes vanish over $\mathbb{Z}$ up to the graded-commutativity sign; on the torus the cellular computation gives zero directly). $\alpha \smile \beta = -\, \beta \smile \alpha$, and this product is a generator of $H^2(T^2; \mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}$. So the cohomology ring is the exterior algebra $\Lambda_{\mathbb{Z}}(\alpha, \beta)$ on two degree-one generators, and $\alpha \smile \beta$ is the top (orientation) class.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).** *Mayer–Vietoris by excision.*

Let $X = A \cup B$ with $A, B$ open. Derive the **Mayer–Vietoris long exact sequence**:

$$
\cdots \to H_n(A \cap B) \to H_n(A) \oplus H_n(B) \to H_n(X) \to H_{n-1}(A \cap B) \to \cdots
$$

from the long exact sequence of the pair $(A \cup B, A)$ together with excision.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Excision of $B$ from $(X, A)$ — equivalently the hypothesis $\overline B \subset \operatorname{int} A$ replaced by the open-cover form — gives $H_n(X, A) \cong H_n(B, A \cap B)$. Then splice the long exact sequence of $(X, A)$ with that of $(B, A \cap B)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The open-cover form of excision gives $H_n(X, A) \cong H_n(B, A \cap B)$ because $X - B \subset A$ and the cover hypothesis is satisfied. Write the long exact sequence of the pair $(B, A \cap B)$:

$$
\cdots \to H_n(A \cap B) \to H_n(B) \to H_n(B, A \cap B) \to H_{n-1}(A \cap B) \to \cdots
$$

Substituting $H_n(B, A \cap B) \cong H_n(X, A)$ and splicing with the long exact sequence of $(X, A)$, which contains $H_n(A) \to H_n(X) \to H_n(X, A)$, replaces the relative term by a map out of $H_n(X)$. The diagonal map $H_n(A \cap B) \to H_n(A) \oplus H_n(B)$ (inclusion of each piece) and the subtraction map $H_n(A) \oplus H_n(B) \to H_n(X)$ emerge from the splicing. The result is the displayed Mayer–Vietoris sequence. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).** *de Rham via Mayer–Vietoris.*

Using the Mayer–Vietoris sequence and induction on the number of sets in a good cover, prove de Rham's theorem for a smooth manifold $M$ admitting a finite good cover: $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \cong H^*(M; \mathbb{R})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Both theories satisfy the homotopy axiom (homotopic maps agree) and both give the same answer on a contractible open set (namely $\mathbb{R}$ in degree $0$ and $0$ elsewhere). Both have a Mayer–Vietoris sequence for an open cover. Induct on the number of good-cover sets, using the five-lemma.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $f : H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(-) \to H^*(-; \mathbb{R})$ be the de Rham map (integration of forms over cycles descends to a map on cohomology). Both sides are *homotopy functors*: a homotopy equivalence induces an isomorphism on each, and both send a point to $\mathbb{R}$ in degree $0$ and zero elsewhere. Both satisfy the *Mayer–Vietoris property* for a two-set open cover: de Rham by the partition-of-unity construction on forms, singular by Exercise 5. The de Rham map is a chain map between the two Mayer–Vietoris sequences.

Induct on the number $k$ of sets in a finite good cover. For $k = 1$, $M$ is diffeomorphic to $\mathbb{R}^n$ (contractible), so both sides are $\mathbb{R}$ in degree $0$ and zero elsewhere, and $f$ is an isomorphism. For the inductive step, write $M = U \cup V$ with $U$ a union of $k - 1$ good-cover sets and $V$ a single good-cover set, so $U$, $V$, and $U \cap V$ each admit good covers with fewer than $k$ sets. The de Rham map gives a morphism of Mayer–Vietoris sequences relating the de Rham and singular theories of $(U, V, U \cap V, M)$. By the inductive hypothesis it is an isomorphism on $U$, $V$, and $U \cap V$; by the five-lemma it is an isomorphism on $M$. $\square$

This is the standard Bott–Tu proof.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).** *Homology of real projective space.*

Using the CW structure on $\mathbb{RP}^n$ with one cell in each dimension $0, 1, \dots, n$, compute the cellular chain complex with $\mathbb{Z}$ and $\mathbb{Z}/2$ coefficients, and read off $H_k(\mathbb{RP}^n; \mathbb{Z})$ and $H_k(\mathbb{RP}^n; \mathbb{Z}/2)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The cellular boundary $C_k \to C_{k-1}$ is multiplication by $1 + (-1)^k$. So it is zero for $k$ odd and multiplication by $2$ for $k$ even.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $\mathbb{Z}$ coefficients, $C_k = \mathbb{Z}$ for $0 \leq k \leq n$, and $\partial_k$ is multiplication by $1 + (-1)^k$: zero for $k$ odd, $2$ for $k$ even. Reading off:

- $H_0(\mathbb{RP}^n; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}$;
- $H_k(\mathbb{RP}^n; \mathbb{Z}) = 0$ for $k$ odd, $0 < k < n$;
- $H_k(\mathbb{RP}^n; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ for $k$ even, $0 < k < n$;
- $H_n(\mathbb{RP}^n; \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}$ if $n$ is odd, and $0$ if $n$ is even.

With $\mathbb{Z}/2$ coefficients, every boundary map is zero (since $2 = 0$), so $H_k(\mathbb{RP}^n; \mathbb{Z}/2) = \mathbb{Z}/2$ for $0 \leq k \leq n$. This uniform answer is the shadow of Steenrod-squaring structure on the mod-$2$ cohomology ring.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Cellular homology computes singular homology.** For any CW complex $X$, the cellular chain complex $C_*^{\mathrm{CW}}(X)$ has homology naturally isomorphic to the singular homology of $X$. The proof uses the long exact sequences of the triples $(X^n, X^{n-1}, X^{n-2})$ to identify $H_n(X^n, X^{n-1}) \cong H_n(X)$ after quotienting by the adjacent skeleton, and then reads off the cellular boundary formula. This is what makes CW complexes the workhorse setting for homology computation: a finite CW structure reduces $H_*(X)$ to linear algebra over a finitely generated free module.

**The Hurewicz theorem.** For a path-connected space $X$, the natural map $h : \pi_1(X, x_0) \to H_1(X; \mathbb{Z})$ — send a loop to the homology class of the singular $1$-cycle it traces — is the **abelianisation**: $H_1(X; \mathbb{Z}) \cong \pi_1(X, x_0) / [\pi_1, \pi_1]$. More generally, if $X$ is $(n-1)$-connected for $n \geq 2$ (so $\pi_k(X) = 0$ for $k < n$), the **Hurewicz homomorphism** $\pi_n(X) \to H_n(X; \mathbb{Z})$ is an isomorphism, and $\pi_{n+1}(X) \to H_{n+1}(X; \mathbb{Z})$ is surjective. The Hurewicz theorem is the precise sense in which homology is the first homotopy-theoretic shadow of a space [ref: TODO_REF Hurewicz1935].

**The universal coefficient theorems.** Homology and cohomology with arbitrary coefficient groups are recovered from integral homology by algebraic operations. The **universal coefficient theorem for homology** gives a short exact sequence $0 \to H_n(X; \mathbb{Z}) \otimes G \to H_n(X; G) \to \operatorname{Tor}(H_{n-1}(X; \mathbb{Z}), G) \to 0$ that splits (unnaturally). The **universal coefficient theorem for cohomology** gives $0 \to \operatorname{Ext}(H_{n-1}(X; \mathbb{Z}), G) \to H^n(X; G) \to \operatorname{Hom}(H_n(X; \mathbb{Z}), G) \to 0$, also splitting. The $\operatorname{Ext}$ term is the algebraic signature of cohomology being the linear dual of homology with a one-degree shift.

**Poincaré duality.** For a closed orientable $n$-manifold $M$, cap product with a fundamental class $[M] \in H_n(M; \mathbb{Z})$ gives an isomorphism $H^k(M; \mathbb{Z}) \xrightarrow{\ \cap [M]\ } H_{n-k}(M; \mathbb{Z})$ for every $k$. In de Rham language, the wedge product paired with integration gives a perfect pairing $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \times H^{n-k}_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \to \mathbb{R}$. Poincaré duality is the statement that, on a closed orientable manifold, homology and cohomology are reflections of each other across the middle dimension. It underlies the intersection form on $H^{n/2}$ of even-dimensional manifolds, and hence the classification of simply connected $4$-manifolds (Freedman, Donaldson) [ref: TODO_REF Poincare1895].

**The Künneth formula.** For spaces $X, Y$ over a field $k$, $H^n(X \times Y; k) \cong \bigoplus_{p + q = n} H^p(X; k) \otimes H^q(Y; k)$, and the cohomology ring is the graded tensor product. Over $\mathbb{Z}$ the formula acquires a $\operatorname{Tor}$ correction. The Künneth formula is the homological shadow of the product of spaces, and the Leray–Hirsch / splitting-principle theorems are its fibrewise refinements.

**de Rham's theorem and Hodge theory.** de Rham's theorem identifies $H^*_{\mathrm{dR}}(M) \cong H^*(M; \mathbb{R})$. When $M$ carries a Riemannian metric [48.01.01], the Hodge theorem identifies each de Rham class with a unique harmonic representative. Combined with Poincaré duality, this gives the Hodge decomposition on a Kähler manifold, the gateway to modern complex and algebraic geometry.

**Synthesis.** The Eilenberg-Steenrod axioms are the foundational reason every "homology-like" theory shares the same formal behaviour, and the long exact sequence of a pair is the central insight that propagates local data into global invariants; this is exactly the structure that iterates into the spectral sequences of [03.13.01], generalises to generalised cohomology theories and sheaf cohomology [04.03.01], and is dual to the cup-product ring structure that makes cohomology the richer invariant. Putting these together, the bridge is that the five axioms — homotopy, exactness, excision, dimension, additivity — pin down ordinary homology up to natural isomorphism on CW-pairs, and every departure from "ordinary" (K-theory, cobordism, sheaf cohomology, de Rham) is a controlled variation of one axiom while preserving the others. The Hurewicz theorem ties homology back to homotopy [03.12.01, 03.12.19], and Poincaré duality ties cohomology to the geometry of manifolds [48.01.01]; both are instances of the same axiom-driven machinery.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition ($\partial^2 = 0$ for singular chains).** *For every $n \geq 1$ and every singular $n$-simplex $\sigma : \Delta^n \to X$, $\partial_{n-1}(\partial_n \sigma) = 0$. Hence $\partial \circ \partial = 0$ on all of $C_*(X)$.*

*Proof.* Write $F_i : \Delta^{n-1} \to \Delta^n$ for the $i$-th face inclusion (omitting the $i$-th vertex), so $\partial_n \sigma = \sum_{i=0}^{n} (-1)^i \, \sigma \circ F_i$. Then

$$
\partial_{n-1}(\partial_n \sigma) = \sum_{i=0}^{n} \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} (-1)^{i+j} \, \sigma \circ F_i \circ F_j^{(i)},
$$

where $F_j^{(i)}$ is the $j$-th face of $\Delta^{n-1}$ embedded into $\Delta^n$ through $F_i$. The standard identity on face inclusions is $F_i \circ F_{j}^{(i)} = F_{j+1} \circ F_j^{(j+1?)}$ for $i \leq j$, equivalently the two composites $F_i \circ F_{j+1}$ (omitting $i$ then $j+1$) and $F_{j+1} \circ F_i$ (omitting $j+1$ then $i$, relabelled) both omit the same pair of vertices. Concretely $F_i \circ F_{j-1} = F_{j} \circ F_i$ for $i < j$: both maps omit the vertices $v_i$ and $v_j$, in either order. The two terms in the double sum corresponding to the pair $(i, j)$ with $i < j$ thus have signs $(-1)^{i + (j-1)}$ and $(-1)^{j + i}$, which are negatives of each other. They cancel. Every term is paired with a unique cancelling partner, so the sum is zero. $\square$

**Proposition (naturality of the connecting homomorphism).** *A map of pairs $f : (X, A) \to (Y, B)$ induces a commutative square*

$$
\begin{array}{ccc}
H_n(X, A) & \xrightarrow{\ \partial \ } & H_{n-1}(A) \\
{\scriptstyle f_*}\downarrow & & \downarrow {\scriptstyle (f|_A)_*} \\
H_n(Y, B) & \xrightarrow{\ \partial \ } & H_{n-1}(B)
\end{array}
$$

*Proof.* Chase a class $[c] \in H_n(X, A)$ represented by a relative cycle $c \in C_n(X)$ with $\partial c \in C_{n-1}(A)$. Its boundary under $\partial$ is $[a]$ where $a \in C_{n-1}(A)$ is the unique chain with $i_\# a = \partial c$. Apply $f_*$: $f_\#(c) \in C_n(Y)$ is a relative cycle, and $\partial(f_\# c) = f_\#(\partial c) = f_\#(i_\# a) = i_\#((f|_A)_\# a)$. So the boundary of $f_\#(c)$ in $C_{n-1}(B)$ is $(f|_A)_\#(a)$, giving $\partial(f_*[c]) = (f|_A)_*[a] = (f|_A)_*(\partial[c])$. $\square$

**Proposition (Hurewicz map is a homomorphism).** *For a path-connected space $X$ with basepoint $x_0$, the map $h : \pi_1(X, x_0) \to H_1(X; \mathbb{Z})$ sending $[\gamma]$ to the homology class of the singular $1$-cycle $\gamma$ is a group homomorphism, and its kernel is the commutator subgroup $[\pi_1, \pi_1]$.*

*Sketch.* That $h$ is a homomorphism: the concatenation $\gamma_1 \cdot \gamma_2$ is, as a singular $1$-chain, homologous to $\gamma_1 + \gamma_2$ (a reparametrisation of $[0,1]$ realises the difference as a boundary of a singular $2$-simplex). So $h([\gamma_1][\gamma_2]) = h([\gamma_1]) + h([\gamma_2])$.

The kernel: $H_1$ is abelian, so the commutator subgroup is contained in $\ker h$. For the reverse, the singular chain complex in degree one, after quotienting by boundaries, is generated by loops modulo the relation that two loops homotopic through a "filled-in disk" are identified. Identifying each generator with its image in the abelianisation of $\pi_1$ gives a well-defined isomorphism $H_1(X; \mathbb{Z}) \xrightarrow{\sim} \pi_1(X, x_0)_{\mathrm{ab}}$, inverse to $h$ post-composed with abelianisation. So $\ker h = [\pi_1, \pi_1]$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Homotopy and the fundamental group** [03.12.01] — the prerequisite invariant: the Hurewicz theorem identifies $H_1$ with the abelianisation of $\pi_1$, and identifies $\pi_n$ with $H_n$ for the first non-vanishing dimension. Homology is the abelian, computable shadow of homotopy. By `conn:hurewicz.bridge`, *$H_1$ is the abelianisation of $\pi_1$* (the central bridge from homotopy to homology, expanded in [03.12.19]).

- **Singular, simplicial, and cellular homology** [03.12.11, 03.12.12, 03.12.13] — the three concrete avatars of the single axiomatic theory. The Eilenberg-Steenrod axioms guarantee these agree on CW-pairs; cellular homology is the one used for hand computation. The unit here is the axiomatic unification; the per-theory units carry the chain-level details.

- **Eilenberg-Steenrod axioms (dedicated unit)** [03.12.15] — the full axiomatic treatment, including the uniqueness theorem and the extension to generalised cohomology theories. The present unit states the axioms and proves the exactness axiom in detail; [03.12.15] covers the remaining four and the uniqueness proof.

- **Spectral sequences** [03.13.01] — the long exact sequence of a pair, iterated through the exact-couple machinery of Massey, becomes the spectral sequence of a filtered complex. The Leray–Serre spectral sequence [03.13.02] computes the homology of a total space from base and fibre, exactly the propagation pattern of the exactness axiom at higher bookkeeping resolution. By `conn:les.iterates`, *every spectral sequence is an iterated long exact sequence* (specialisation).

- **Poincaré duality and the cap product** [03.12.16, 03.12.17] — the duality between homology and cohomology on a closed orientable manifold, implemented by cap product with the fundamental class. Stated here as a theorem; the dedicated unit carries the proof via the dual cell decomposition.

- **De Rham cohomology and the Čech–de Rham complex** [03.04.06, 03.04.11] — de Rham's theorem identifies the analytic theory of differential forms with the topological theory of singular cochains. The Čech–de Rham double complex of [03.04.11] is the prototype filtered complex whose spectral sequence gives the de Rham isomorphism; the hypercohomology framework of [03.04.14] is the sheaf-theoretic generalisation.

- **Sheaf cohomology** [04.03.01] — the cohomology of a space with coefficients in a sheaf is the natural generalisation of singular cohomology beyond constant coefficients. The Leray spectral sequence computes it from the higher direct image sheaves, unifying the topological and analytic theories.

- **Riemannian geometry and Hodge theory** [48.01.01] — a Riemannian metric selects a harmonic representative in each de Rham class (Hodge theorem), refining the bare de Rham isomorphism into the Hodge decomposition on Kähler manifolds. This is the gateway from algebraic topology to complex and algebraic geometry.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Homology begins with **Henri Poincaré's 1895 memoir *Analysis Situs*** [ref: TODO_REF Poincare1895], where he introduced the **Betti numbers** (named after Enrico Betti) and the incidence matrices that encoded how $n$-dimensional cells meet $(n-1)$-dimensional cells — the precursor of the boundary operator. Poincaré's motivating problem was the classification of manifolds up to homeomorphism, and his central observation was the **Euler–Poincaré formula** $\chi = \sum_n (-1)^n \beta_n$, identifying the alternating sum of the Betti numbers with the classical Euler characteristic. The memoir did not have the modern chain-complex formalism; that emerged only in the 1920s through the work of Emmy Noether and her school, who recognised that "homology" should take values in abelian groups rather than merely in integer Betti numbers. The shift from numbers to groups is the decisive abstraction that made modern algebraic topology possible.

The axiomatic foundation came with **Samuel Eilenberg and Norman Steenrod's 1952 book *Foundations of Algebraic Topology*** [ref: TODO_REF EilenbergSteenrod1952], which proposed that a homology theory is anything satisfying the five axioms — homotopy, exactness, excision, dimension, additivity. The axioms isolate the properties that make the theory computable, and the **uniqueness theorem** shows that on CW-pairs there is only one such theory with the prescribed coefficient group. The axiomatic viewpoint made it possible to define *generalised* homology theories — K-theory, cobordism, stable homotopy — by relaxing the dimension axiom. Every cohomology theory in modern topology is, by design, a variation on the Eilenberg-Steenrod axioms.

**Georges de Rham's 1931 theorem** [ref: TODO_REF DeRham1931] settled a question going back to Poincaré and Élie Cartan: the topological obstructions measured by singular cohomology with real coefficients are exactly the obstructions to solving $d\omega = \eta$ on a smooth manifold, i.e. the closed differential forms modulo the exact ones. De Rham's proof used a double-complex argument that anticipates the sheaf-theoretic machinery later codified by Leray, Cartan, and Serre. The theorem is the cornerstone of differential topology: it is what licenses the replacement of topological data (singular cochains) with analytic data (differential forms) whenever a smooth structure is present.

The **Hurewicz theorem** — published by **Witold Hurewicz in 1935–1936** in his four-part *Beiträge zur Topologie der Deformationen* — established the precise bridge from homotopy to homology: the first non-vanishing homotopy and homology groups of a simply connected space agree. This result, together with the higher homotopy groups Hurewicz introduced in the same papers, completed the conceptual triangle linking homotopy, homology, and the geometry of manifolds that has organised algebraic topology ever since.
