## Intuition [Beginner]

A tensor product is a way to combine two independent vector inputs into one new kind of vector.

Think of choosing a color and a direction. "Red pointing east" is not just a color and not just a direction. It is a paired object that remembers both choices. If colors can be blended and directions can be added, the paired objects should respect both kinds of linear mixing.

Tensor products matter because geometry often carries several linear ingredients at once: directions, forms, spinors, matrices, and bundle fibers.

## Visual [Beginner]

The tensor product stores paired vector data in a single combined space.

![Two vector spaces feeding paired inputs into a combined tensor-product space.](/img/tensor-product-placeholder.svg)

The combined space is built so pairing behaves linearly in each input.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take one space with basis vectors red and blue. Take another space with basis vectors left and right.

The combined space has four basic pairs:

red-left, red-right, blue-left, blue-right.

If a color is half red plus half blue, pairing it with right gives half of red-right plus half of blue-right.

What this tells us: tensor product turns paired linear choices into ordinary vectors in a larger space.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $V$ and $W$ be vector spaces over a field $K$ [01.01.01], [01.01.03]. A **tensor product** of $V$ and $W$ is a vector space $V\otimes_K W$ together with a bilinear map

$$
\tau:V\times W\to V\otimes_K W
$$

such that every bilinear map $B:V\times W\to U$ into a vector space $U$ factors uniquely through a linear map $\widetilde B:V\otimes_K W\to U$:

$$
B=\widetilde B\circ \tau.
$$

The element $\tau(v,w)$ is written $v\otimes w$ and is called a **pure tensor**. The defining relations are

$$
(v+v')\otimes w=v\otimes w+v'\otimes w,\quad
v\otimes(w+w')=v\otimes w+v\otimes w',
$$

and

$$
(av)\otimes w=a(v\otimes w)=v\otimes(aw).
$$

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (basis of a tensor product).** Let $V$ have basis $e_1,\ldots,e_m$ and let $W$ have basis $f_1,\ldots,f_n$. Then the elements $e_i\otimes f_j$ form a basis of $V\otimes_K W$.

**Proof.** First show spanning. Write

$$
v=\sum_i a_i e_i,\qquad w=\sum_j b_j f_j.
$$

Bilinearity gives

$$
v\otimes w=\sum_{i,j}a_i b_j(e_i\otimes f_j).
$$

Since pure tensors generate the tensor product, the displayed tensors span.

For linear independence, define for each pair $(r,s)$ a bilinear map $B_{rs}:V\times W\to K$ by

$$
B_{rs}(e_i,f_j)=
\begin{cases}
1,&(i,j)=(r,s),\\
0,&\text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
$$

By the universal property, $B_{rs}$ induces a linear map $\widetilde B_{rs}:V\otimes_K W\to K$. If

$$
\sum_{i,j}c_{ij}(e_i\otimes f_j)=0,
$$

applying $\widetilde B_{rs}$ gives $c_{rs}=0$. This holds for every pair $(r,s)$, so all coefficients vanish. $\square$

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Why is the assignment $(v,w)\mapsto v\otimes w$ not usually linear as a map from $V\times W$ with componentwise addition?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare $(v+v')\otimes(w+w')$ with $v\otimes w+v'\otimes w'$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The expansion contains cross terms: $(v+v')\otimes(w+w')=v\otimes w+v\otimes w'+v'\otimes w+v'\otimes w'$. A linear map on the product would produce only the first and last terms.

Rubric: full credit for identifying the cross terms.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Let $\beta:V\times W\to U$ be bilinear. State the map whose existence is guaranteed by the tensor-product universal property.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

It is a linear map out of $V\otimes W$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

There is a unique linear map $\widetilde\beta:V\otimes W\to U$ such that $\widetilde\beta(v\otimes w)=\beta(v,w)$.

Rubric: full credit for domain, codomain, linearity, and agreement on pure tensors.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove that $0\otimes w=0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $(0+0)\otimes w=0\otimes w$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By bilinearity, $0\otimes w=(0+0)\otimes w=0\otimes w+0\otimes w$. Add the additive inverse of $0\otimes w$ to both sides to obtain $0=0\otimes w$.

Rubric: full credit for using additivity in the first variable and cancellation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Let $V$ and $W$ be one-dimensional with bases $e$ and $f$. Prove that every element of $V\otimes W$ is a pure tensor.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the basis theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Every element is $c(e\otimes f)$ for some scalar $c$. If $c=0$, it is $0\otimes f$. If $c\ne0$, it is $(ce)\otimes f$. Thus every element is pure.

Rubric: full credit for reducing to the single basis tensor.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The tensor product is characterized up to unique isomorphism by its universal property. If $(T,\tau)$ and $(T',\tau')$ both represent bilinear maps out of $V\times W$, the universal property gives unique linear maps $T\to T'$ and $T'\to T$ carrying one universal bilinear map to the other. The two composites preserve the universal maps and therefore equal the identity maps.

The construction is functorial: linear maps $f:V\to V'$ and $g:W\to W'$ induce a linear map

$$
f\otimes g:V\otimes W\to V'\otimes W'
$$

with $(f\otimes g)(v\otimes w)=f(v)\otimes g(w)$. Associativity and symmetry constraints make tensor product the monoidal structure on vector spaces [ref: TODO_REF Lang §XVI §1-§2].

For modules over a commutative ring, the same universal property defines $M\otimes_R N$. Exactness properties become subtler: tensoring is right exact in general and exact when the module is flat. The present unit uses vector spaces, where all modules are free and tensoring is exact.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (uniqueness).** Tensor products satisfying the universal property are uniquely isomorphic.

Let $(T,\tau)$ and $(T',\tau')$ be two tensor products of $V$ and $W$. Since $\tau'$ is bilinear, the universal property of $T$ gives a unique linear map $\alpha:T\to T'$ with $\alpha\circ\tau=\tau'$. Since $\tau$ is bilinear, the universal property of $T'$ gives a unique linear map $\beta:T'\to T$ with $\beta\circ\tau'=\tau$.

Then $\beta\alpha\circ\tau=\beta\circ\tau'=\tau$. The identity map on $T$ also has this property, so uniqueness gives $\beta\alpha=\operatorname{id}_T$. Similarly, $\alpha\beta=\operatorname{id}_{T'}$. Thus $\alpha$ is an isomorphism.

## Connections [Master]

Tensor algebra [03.01.04] is the direct sum of all tensor powers of a vector space.

Quotient algebra [03.01.05] uses tensor algebras and ideals to impose relations.

Vector bundles [03.05.02] have tensor products fiberwise, producing new bundles from old ones.

Clifford algebra [03.09.02] starts from tensor algebra and then quotients by quadratic relations.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Tensor products grew out of multilinear algebra and invariant theory, where one needed a linear object representing bilinear and multilinear operations. Bourbaki and Cartan-Eilenberg helped fix the universal-property formulation in modern algebraic language [ref: TODO_REF Bourbaki Algebra I Ch. III].

Lang's treatment emphasizes tensor products as representing objects for bilinear maps, the formulation used in algebra, geometry, and topology [ref: TODO_REF Lang §XVI §1-§2].
