## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine a pile of sand on a beach and a hole next to it. You want to move every grain of sand into the hole, paying for each grain a cost proportional to the square of the distance you carry it. The cheapest plan for moving the entire pile is the *optimal transport plan*, and the minimum total cost (the square root of it, to be precise) is the **Wasserstein distance** between the initial mass distribution on the pile and the final distribution in the hole. Gaspard Monge wrote down this problem in 1781 to plan military earthworks; it is the original problem in optimal transport.

What is striking is that Wasserstein distance is more than a number. Felix Otto noticed in 2001 that you can put a Riemannian-geometry structure on the space of probability distributions in such a way that Wasserstein distance becomes the *geodesic distance*. The straightest path from one distribution to another is a continuous interpolation that shifts mass at constant speed along straight lines — the visual is mass flowing without pressure. And the geodesic equation, viewed in this geometry, turns out to be the equation describing dust particles moving freely under inertia (the pressureless Euler equation).

The remarkable consequence is that the heat equation, which describes how a hot spot of mass diffuses outward, becomes in Otto's picture the *gradient descent* of the entropy functional in Wasserstein geometry. Diffusion is descent down the entropy landscape, with the descent direction set by the Wasserstein metric. This single observation has reshaped twentieth-century mathematics around the unifying idea that many evolution equations are gradient flows on the space of probability distributions, and that the geometry of these flows controls their stability, regularity, and long-time behaviour.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture three things stacked vertically. At the bottom, two mass distributions on the real line — say a Gaussian bell curve centred at $x = -2$ and another centred at $x = +2$, both the same width. In the middle, a continuous slow-motion movie of one bell sliding rigidly along the $x$-axis until it lands on the other — this is the Wasserstein geodesic. At the top, the abstract space $\mathcal{P}_2$ of all probability measures with finite second moment, drawn as a curved surface, with the two bells appearing as two points and the geodesic as a smooth arc joining them.

![Wasserstein geodesic: a sliding Gaussian, drawn as a path in the infinite-dimensional manifold of probability measures with the Otto Riemannian metric.](/img/wasserstein-metric-and-otto-calculus-placeholder.svg)

The visual captures Otto's insight: optimal transport gives the space of probability distributions a geometry. Geodesics are interpolations by mass-preserving sliding. The heat equation runs downhill on the entropy potential in this geometry.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The simplest optimal transport is between two point masses. Place a single grain of sand at $x = 0$ and ask how to move it to $x = 5$.

Step 1. The initial distribution is the Dirac mass $\mu_0 = \delta_0$, supported entirely at the point $0$. The final distribution is $\mu_1 = \delta_5$.

Step 2. The only transport plan is to carry the single grain from $0$ to $5$. The cost is the squared distance $|5 - 0|^2 = 25$.

Step 3. The Wasserstein-2 distance is $W_2(\mu_0, \mu_1) = \sqrt{25} = 5$. In this simplest case the distance between Dirac masses is the ordinary distance between their supports.

Step 4. The geodesic interpolation is the Dirac mass at the point $(1 - t) \cdot 0 + t \cdot 5 = 5t$, written $\mu_t = \delta_{5t}$ for $t \in [0, 1]$. The grain moves at constant speed $5$ from $0$ to $5$, just as a free particle would.

What this tells us: Wasserstein distance generalises ordinary Euclidean distance from points to whole distributions, and Wasserstein geodesics generalise straight lines. When the distributions are spread out (not single points), the geodesic does not move mass uniformly: each tiny piece of mass takes its own straight-line route to its image under the optimal transport map.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $(\mathcal{X}, d)$ denote a Polish metric space (the canonical case is $\mathcal{X} = \mathbb{R}^n$ with Euclidean distance). Write $\mathcal{P}(\mathcal{X})$ for the space of Borel probability measures on $\mathcal{X}$.

**Definition (second moment).** For $\mu \in \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{X})$ and a chosen base point $x_0 \in \mathcal{X}$, the second moment is $M_2(\mu) = \int d(x, x_0)^2 \, d\mu(x)$. Let
$$
\mathcal{P}_2(\mathcal{X}) = \{\mu \in \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{X}) : M_2(\mu) < \infty\}.
$$
The space $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathcal{X})$ is independent of the choice of $x_0$.

**Definition (coupling).** For $\mu, \nu \in \mathcal{P}_2(\mathcal{X})$, a **coupling** of $\mu$ and $\nu$ is a probability measure $\pi$ on $\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{X}$ with marginals $(\mathrm{pr}_1)_* \pi = \mu$ and $(\mathrm{pr}_2)_* \pi = \nu$. The set of couplings is denoted $\Pi(\mu, \nu)$.

**Definition (Wasserstein-2 distance).** The **Wasserstein-2 distance** between $\mu, \nu \in \mathcal{P}_2(\mathcal{X})$ is
$$
W_2(\mu, \nu) = \left(\inf_{\pi \in \Pi(\mu, \nu)} \int_{\mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{X}} d(x, y)^2 \, d\pi(x, y)\right)^{1/2}.
$$
The infimum is attained (existence via the direct method on the weak-* compact set $\Pi(\mu, \nu)$ combined with lower-semicontinuity of the cost functional); an attaining coupling is called an **optimal coupling**.

**Theorem (Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality).** For $\mu, \nu \in \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$,
$$
W_2^2(\mu, \nu) = \sup_{(\phi, \psi)} \left\{\int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \phi \, d\mu + \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \psi \, d\nu : \phi(x) + \psi(y) \leq |x-y|^2 \text{ for all } x, y\right\}.
$$
The supremum is attained at a pair of $c$-conjugate functions, called the **Kantorovich potentials**.

**Definition (Monge map).** A **Monge map** for the transport problem from $\mu$ to $\nu$ is a Borel map $T : \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{X}$ with $T_* \mu = \nu$ minimising the Monge cost $\int d(x, T(x))^2 \, d\mu(x)$. Equivalently, the coupling $(\mathrm{id} \times T)_* \mu$ is optimal in the Kantorovich sense.

**Theorem (Brenier 1991).** Let $\mu, \nu \in \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ with $\mu$ absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue measure. Then there exists a unique (up to $\mu$-null modifications) Monge map $T : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$, and $T = \nabla \varphi$ for a convex function $\varphi : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$. Conversely, the gradient of any convex $\varphi$ with $(\nabla \varphi)_* \mu = \nu$ is optimal.

**Definition (Otto Riemannian structure on $\mathcal{P}_2$).** Otto 2001 introduced the formal Riemannian structure on $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$:
- *Tangent space at $\mu$*: $T_\mu \mathcal{P}_2 = \overline{\{\nabla \phi : \phi \in C_c^\infty(\mathbb{R}^n)\}}^{L^2(\mu)}$, identifying tangent vectors with gradient vector fields modulo the equivalence under $L^2(\mu)$.
- *Metric*: $\langle \nabla \phi, \nabla \psi \rangle_\mu = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \nabla\phi(x) \cdot \nabla\psi(x) \, d\mu(x)$.
- *Continuity equation*: a curve $t \mapsto \mu_t$ has tangent vector $\nabla \phi_t$ iff $\partial_t \mu_t + \nabla \cdot (\mu_t \nabla \phi_t) = 0$ in the weak sense.

**Definition (Hirota gradient flow).** A curve $t \mapsto \mu_t$ in $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ is the **$W_2$-gradient flow** of a functional $F : \mathcal{P}_2 \to \mathbb{R}$ if
$$
\partial_t \mu_t + \nabla \cdot (\mu_t \nabla \phi_t) = 0, \qquad \nabla \phi_t = -\nabla_{\mu_t} F,
$$
where $\nabla_\mu F$ denotes the Otto gradient computed from the first variation $\delta F / \delta \mu$ by $\nabla_\mu F = \nabla (\delta F / \delta \mu)$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Brenier's theorem needs absolute continuity of $\mu$.* If $\mu$ has atoms, the optimal coupling need not be supported on a graph: splitting mass is generically required, so no Monge map exists.
- *The Otto tangent space is not all of $L^2(\mu, \mathbb{R}^n)$.* Vector fields that are not (closures of) gradients lie in the orthogonal complement and do not represent infinitesimal changes of probability measure; they generate volume-preserving rearrangements of mass.
- *Wasserstein-2 is not Hilbertian on $\mathcal{P}_2$.* The cone-of-measures structure makes Wasserstein space into a positively curved metric space in the Alexandrov sense when $n \geq 2$, and tangent cones (rather than tangent spaces) are the rigorous replacement of Otto's heuristic.
- *Geodesic interpolation is not linear interpolation.* The interpolation $(1-t)\mu_0 + t\mu_1$ is a curve in $\mathcal{P}_2$ but it is not the Wasserstein geodesic; the geodesic is $\mu_t = ((1-t)\mathrm{id} + tT)_* \mu_0$, supported on a sliding deformation of $\mu_0$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Otto 2001 — gradient flow of entropy is the heat equation).** *Let $H : \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n) \to \mathbb{R} \cup \{+\infty\}$ denote the Boltzmann entropy*
$$
H(\rho) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \rho(x) \log \rho(x) \, dx
$$
*for measures absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue measure, with $H(\mu) = +\infty$ for singular $\mu$. The Otto-Wasserstein gradient flow of $H$ on $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ is the heat equation $\partial_t \rho_t = \Delta \rho_t$.*

*Proof.* The first variation of $H$ at $\rho$ in the direction $h$ (a signed measure with $\int h \, dx = 0$) is
$$
\delta H(\rho)[h] = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} (\log \rho(x) + 1) h(x) \, dx,
$$
so the *Euclidean* gradient is $\delta H / \delta \rho = \log \rho + 1$.

By the Otto recipe, the Wasserstein gradient is the gradient of the first variation:
$$
\nabla_\rho H = \nabla \left(\frac{\delta H}{\delta \rho}\right) = \nabla \log \rho = \frac{\nabla \rho}{\rho}.
$$

The Wasserstein gradient flow equation $\partial_t \rho_t = -\nabla_{\rho_t} H$ in the Otto sense reads (via the continuity equation linking tangent vector $-\nabla \log \rho$ to $\partial_t \rho$ through $\partial_t \rho_t + \nabla \cdot (\rho_t v_t) = 0$ with $v_t = -\nabla \log \rho_t$):
$$
\partial_t \rho_t + \nabla \cdot \left(\rho_t \cdot \left(-\frac{\nabla \rho_t}{\rho_t}\right)\right) = 0.
$$

Simplifying $\rho_t \cdot (\nabla \rho_t / \rho_t) = \nabla \rho_t$ gives
$$
\partial_t \rho_t - \nabla \cdot \nabla \rho_t = \partial_t \rho_t - \Delta \rho_t = 0,
$$
i.e. the heat equation. $\square$

**Theorem (Otto 2001 — the geodesic equation is pressureless Euler).** *A curve $t \mapsto \mu_t = \rho_t \, dx$ in $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ is a Wasserstein geodesic iff there is a velocity field $v_t = \nabla \phi_t$ such that*
$$
\partial_t \rho_t + \nabla \cdot (\rho_t v_t) = 0, \qquad \partial_t v_t + (v_t \cdot \nabla) v_t = 0,
$$
*i.e. iff $(\rho_t, v_t)$ solves the pressureless Euler system with potential velocity field.*

*Proof.* The Riemannian geodesic equation for an abstract Riemannian manifold $(M, g)$ is $\nabla_{\dot\gamma} \dot\gamma = 0$, where $\nabla$ is the Levi-Civita connection. On $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ with the Otto metric, the tangent vector $\dot\mu_t$ corresponds to a gradient field $\nabla\phi_t$ via the continuity equation $\partial_t \rho_t + \nabla \cdot (\rho_t \nabla \phi_t) = 0$.

The Otto Levi-Civita connection computes the covariant time-derivative of $\nabla\phi_t$ along $\dot\mu_t$ as
$$
(\nabla_{\dot\mu_t} \dot\mu_t)_x = \partial_t \nabla\phi_t(x) + (\nabla\phi_t \cdot \nabla) \nabla\phi_t(x),
$$
which is the material derivative of the velocity field along the flow. Setting this to zero yields
$$
\partial_t \nabla\phi_t + (\nabla\phi_t \cdot \nabla) \nabla\phi_t = 0.
$$
Writing $v_t = \nabla\phi_t$ and using that $(v \cdot \nabla) \nabla\phi = \nabla(|v|^2/2)$ for irrotational $v$, this is equivalent to
$$
\partial_t v_t + (v_t \cdot \nabla) v_t = 0,
$$
the pressureless Euler equation. The continuity equation gives the mass-transport component. $\square$

**Bridge.** Otto's calculus identifies $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ with the dual orbit-space picture of the Euler-Arnold programme [05.09.05]. The diffeomorphism group $\mathrm{Diff}_+(\mathbb{R}^n)$ acts on a fixed reference measure $\mu_0$ by push-forward, and the quotient $\mathrm{Diff}_+ / \mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}(\mu_0)$ is identified with $\mathcal{P}_2$. The right-invariant $L^2$ metric on $\mathrm{Diff}_+$ descends to the Otto-Wasserstein metric on the quotient. The pressureless Euler equation is the geodesic equation on the quotient, and the JKO scheme discretises gradient flow in this geometry.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Compute the Wasserstein-2 distance squared between $\mu_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}\delta_0 + \tfrac{1}{2}\delta_2$ (mass split evenly between $0$ and $2$) and $\nu = \tfrac{1}{2}\delta_3 + \tfrac{1}{2}\delta_5$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

There are two natural couplings, mapping $\{0, 2\} \to \{3, 5\}$: one increasing and one decreasing. Compute both costs and take the smaller.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The increasing coupling sends $0 \to 3$ and $2 \to 5$, with cost $\tfrac{1}{2}|0-3|^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}|2-5|^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(9) + \tfrac{1}{2}(9) = 9$. The decreasing coupling sends $0 \to 5$ and $2 \to 3$, with cost $\tfrac{1}{2}|0-5|^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}|2-3|^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(25) + \tfrac{1}{2}(1) = 13$. The optimal cost is $9$, hence $W_2^2(\mu_0, \nu) = 9$ and $W_2 = 3$. Rubric: full credit for both calculations and recognising that the increasing (monotone) coupling is optimal in dimension one — this is a special case of the Hoeffding-Fréchet rearrangement.

</details>

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that for $\mu_0, \mu_1 \in \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R})$ with continuous distribution functions $F_0, F_1$, the Wasserstein-2 distance is

W_2^2(\mu_0, \mu_1) = \int_0^1 |F_0^{-1}(u) - F_1^{-1}(u)|^2 \, du.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The monotone rearrangement $T = F_1^{-1} \circ F_0$ is the Monge optimal map in one dimension.

In dimension one, the Brenier optimal map is $T = F_1^{-1} \circ F_0$ (the monotone rearrangement), and $T$ is increasing because both $F_0$ and $F_1^{-1}$ are. Substituting $u = F_0(x)$ in the Monge cost gives $\int_\mathbb{R} |x - T(x)|^2 d\mu_0(x) = \int_0^1 |F_0^{-1}(u) - F_1^{-1}(u)|^2 \, du$. This is the **Hoeffding-Fréchet identity**, exhibiting $W_2^2$ as the $L^2(0, 1)$ distance between quantile functions. Rubric: full credit for the change-of-variables derivation and the identification of $T$ as monotone rearrangement.

</details>

Set $F_i^{-1}(u) = m_i + \sigma_i \Phi^{-1}(u)$. Then $F_0^{-1}(u) - F_1^{-1}(u) = (m_0 - m_1) + (\sigma_0 - \sigma_1)\Phi^{-1}(u)$. By Exercise 2,
$$
W_2^2 = \int_0^1 [(m_0 - m_1) + (\sigma_0 - \sigma_1)\Phi^{-1}(u)]^2 du.
$$
Expanding the square: $(m_0 - m_1)^2 + 2(m_0 - m_1)(\sigma_0 - \sigma_1)\int \Phi^{-1}(u) du + (\sigma_0 - \sigma_1)^2 \int (\Phi^{-1}(u))^2 du$. The first integral is the mean of a standard normal, equal to $0$; the second is the variance, equal to $1$. So $W_2^2 = (m_1 - m_0)^2 + (\sigma_1 - \sigma_0)^2$. The optimal map is $T(x) = m_1 + (\sigma_1/\sigma_0)(x - m_0)$, an affine scaling-and-translation. Rubric: full credit for the quantile substitution, the integral evaluation, and the affine optimal-map identification.

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Define **displacement convexity** of a functional $F : \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n) \to \mathbb{R}$. Show that the second moment $M(\rho) = \int |x|^2 \, d\rho(x)$ is displacement convex.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Displacement convexity means convexity along Wasserstein geodesics $\rho_t = ((1-t)\mathrm{id} + tT)_* \rho_0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$F$ is **displacement convex** if for every Wasserstein geodesic $\rho_t = ((1-t)\mathrm{id} + tT)_* \rho_0$, the function $t \mapsto F(\rho_t)$ is convex on $[0, 1]$. For the second moment,
$$

**Exercise 6 (medium, short-answer).**

State the JKO scheme for approximating the heat equation as a Wasserstein gradient flow of entropy. Outline the convergence argument.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The JKO scheme defines $\rho^{n+1}$ as the minimiser of $\frac{1}{2\tau} W_2^2(\rho, \rho^n) + H(\rho)$, where $\tau$ is the time step.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**JKO 1998 scheme** (Jordan-Kinderlehrer-Otto, *SIAM J. Math. Anal.* 29). Given a time step $\tau > 0$ and initial measure $\rho^0 \in \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ with $H(\rho^0) < \infty$, define iteratively
$$
\rho^{n+1} = \mathop{\arg\min}_{\rho \in \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)} \left\{\frac{1}{2\tau} W_2^2(\rho, \rho^n) + H(\rho)\right\}.
$$
Each minimiser exists by lower semicontinuity of $W_2^2 + H$ and weak-* compactness of sublevel sets in $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$. Define the piecewise-constant interpolation $\rho^\tau_t = \rho^n$ for $t \in [n\tau, (n+1)\tau)$. **Convergence**: as $\tau \to 0$, $\rho^\tau_t \rightharpoonup \rho_t$ weak-* uniformly on compact time intervals, where $\rho_t$ solves the heat equation $\partial_t \rho_t = \Delta \rho_t$. The convergence is proven by showing (1) the discrete Euler-Lagrange equation for the minimiser $\rho^{n+1}$ reads $\nabla\phi^{n+1}/\tau = -\nabla \log \rho^{n+1}$ where $T^{n+1} = \mathrm{id} - \nabla\phi^{n+1}$ is the Brenier map from $\rho^{n+1}$ to $\rho^n$; (2) the discrete continuity equation $(\rho^{n+1} - \rho^n)/\tau + \nabla \cdot (\rho^{n+1} \nabla\phi^{n+1}/\tau) = 0$ at first order; (3) compactness of the interpolation in $C([0, T]; \mathcal{P}_2)$ via the energy bound $H(\rho^n) \leq H(\rho^0)$; (4) passing to the limit in the weak formulation. The JKO scheme is now the standard variational discretisation underlying numerical methods for the Fokker-Planck equation, McKean-Vlasov equations, and many degenerate parabolic PDEs (Otto 2001 *porous medium equation*; Agueh 2005 *q-Laplacian*; Carrillo-McCann-Villani 2003 *granular media*). Rubric: full credit for the variational scheme, the first-order Euler-Lagrange identification, and the convergence outline.

**Exercise 7 (medium, short-answer).**

State Brenier's polar factorisation theorem and explain its relationship to the optimal transport map.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Brenier's polar factorisation: every vector field $F : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ with $F_* \mathrm{Leb}|_\Omega \ll \mathrm{Leb}$ factors as $F = \nabla \varphi \circ s$ for $\varphi$ convex and $s$ measure-preserving.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Brenier 1991 polar factorisation theorem** (*Comm. Pure Appl. Math.* 44). Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ be open bounded with $|\Omega| < \infty$, and let $F : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}^n$ be a Borel map such that $F_* (\mathrm{Leb}|_\Omega)$ is absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue measure on $\mathbb{R}^n$. Then $F$ admits a unique (up to null sets) factorisation
$$
F = \nabla \varphi \circ s
$$
where $\varphi : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R} \cup \{+\infty\}$ is lower-semicontinuous convex and $s : \Omega \to \Omega$ is measure-preserving with respect to Lebesgue measure on $\Omega$. The map $\nabla \varphi$ is the Brenier optimal-transport map from $\mathrm{Leb}|_\Omega$ to $F_* \mathrm{Leb}|_\Omega$, and $s$ is the volume-preserving "remixing" part. **Relationship to optimal transport**: when $F = T$ is the optimal-transport map from $\mu = \mathrm{Leb}|_\Omega$ to $\nu = F_* \mathrm{Leb}|_\Omega$, the measure-preserving factor reduces to the identity ($s = \mathrm{id}$) and $T = \nabla\varphi$. The polar factorisation generalises the polar decomposition of an invertible matrix $M = O \cdot P$ (orthogonal $\times$ positive symmetric) to the nonlinear setting: $\nabla\varphi$ is the analogue of the symmetric-positive factor, and the measure-preserving $s$ is the analogue of the orthogonal factor. This was Brenier's original motivation, generalising Helmholtz decomposition for ideal-fluid Cauchy problems. Rubric: full credit for the theorem statement, the convex-potential identification, and the polar-decomposition analogy.

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

The Bakry-Émery $\Gamma_2$-criterion and the Cordero-Erausquin-McCann-Schmuckenschläger 2001 displacement-convexity theorem give an equivalence between Ricci curvature bounds and properties of entropy along Wasserstein geodesics. State the equivalence and explain how it leads to the Lott-Sturm-Villani definition of synthetic Ricci-curvature lower bounds on metric measure spaces.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The key is: $\mathrm{Ric}_g \geq K \cdot g$ on a Riemannian manifold $(M, g)$ iff the entropy is $K$-convex along Wasserstein geodesics.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Theorem (CEMS 2001 — Ricci-convexity equivalence).** Let $(M, g)$ be a smooth complete Riemannian manifold equipped with the volume measure $\mathrm{vol}_g$. The following are equivalent:
(1) $\mathrm{Ric}_g \geq K \cdot g$ pointwise;
(2) for every Wasserstein-2 geodesic $\mu_t \in \mathcal{P}_2(M)$, the entropy $H(\rho_t) = \int \rho_t \log \rho_t \, d\mathrm{vol}_g$ satisfies $H(\rho_t) \leq (1-t) H(\rho_0) + t H(\rho_1) - \tfrac{K}{2} t(1-t) W_2^2(\rho_0, \rho_1)$ for all $t \in [0, 1]$ (i.e. $H$ is $K$-displacement convex);
(3) the Bakry-Émery curvature-dimension condition $\Gamma_2 \geq K \cdot \Gamma$ holds for the Laplace-Beltrami operator.

The equivalence is proven via Otto's calculus: the Otto Hessian of $H$ at $\rho$ in the direction $\nabla\phi$ is $\int |\nabla^2 \phi|^2 \rho \, d\mathrm{vol} + \int \mathrm{Ric}(\nabla\phi, \nabla\phi) \rho \, d\mathrm{vol}$ (Otto-Villani 2000, Otto 2001), and $K$-convexity along geodesics reduces to a pointwise bound on this Hessian, which is a Ricci-curvature bound. **Lott-Sturm-Villani synthetic theory** (Lott-Villani 2009 *Ann. Math.* 169; Sturm 2006 *Acta Math.* 196). On a general metric measure space $(X, d, m)$ — which need not be smooth — *define* $(X, d, m)$ to have **Ricci curvature $\geq K$ and dimension $\leq N$** (the $\mathrm{CD}(K, N)$ condition) iff suitable entropy functionals are $(K, N)$-displacement convex along Wasserstein geodesics in $\mathcal{P}_2(X)$. The synthetic theory subsumes the smooth case (where it agrees with classical Ricci bounds), gives a notion of curvature on Alexandrov spaces and on collapsing limits, satisfies stability under measured Gromov-Hausdorff convergence (Sturm 2006), and gives an axiomatic framework for the Cheeger-Colding theory of Ricci-limit spaces. This is widely viewed as one of the most striking applications of the Otto calculus and the deepest reason why optimal transport became central to modern geometric analysis. Rubric: full credit for the CEMS equivalence statement, the Otto-Hessian connection, and the LSV synthetic-Ricci definition.

</details>

**Exercise 9 (hard, short-answer).**

State the Otto-Villani 2000 HWI inequality and explain its role in deriving log-Sobolev and Talagrand transportation inequalities.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

HWI stands for entropy ($H$) - Wasserstein ($W$) - Fisher information ($I$). The inequality interpolates between log-Sobolev and Talagrand.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Theorem (Otto-Villani 2000 — HWI inequality).** Let $d\mu = e^{-V} \, dx$ be a probability measure on $\mathbb{R}^n$ with $\nabla^2 V \geq K \cdot I_n$ for some $K \in \mathbb{R}$. For every $\nu \in \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ with $\nu \ll \mu$ and finite Fisher information $I(\nu | \mu) = \int |\nabla \log (d\nu/d\mu)|^2 \, d\nu$,
$$
H(\nu | \mu) \leq W_2(\nu, \mu) \sqrt{I(\nu | \mu)} - \tfrac{K}{2} W_2^2(\nu, \mu),
$$
where $H(\nu | \mu) = \int \log(d\nu/d\mu) \, d\nu$ is the relative entropy.

**Consequences**: (i) when $K > 0$, applying $K W_2^2 \leq 2 H$ (Talagrand's inequality, a special case below) and AM-GM gives the **log-Sobolev inequality** $H(\nu | \mu) \leq (1/2K) I(\nu | \mu)$, recovering the Bakry-Émery 1985 result. (ii) Setting $\nu = \mu$ in the limit gives the **Talagrand $\mathbf{T}_2$ inequality** $W_2^2(\nu, \mu) \leq (2/K) H(\nu | \mu)$, originally proven by Talagrand 1996 for the Gaussian. (iii) The HWI inequality is the Wasserstein gradient-flow expression of the dissipation identity $\frac{d}{dt} H(\rho_t | \mu) = -I(\rho_t | \mu)$ for the Fokker-Planck flow toward $\mu$, integrated against the geodesic from $\mu$ to $\nu$.

The proof uses Otto's calculus directly: along the Wasserstein geodesic from $\mu$ to $\nu$, the entropy $H(\cdot | \mu)$ is $K$-convex by CEMS 2001 (Exercise 8), the gradient at $\mu$ is zero (since $\mu$ is the minimiser), and Cauchy-Schwarz in Otto's tangent space links the gradient norm at $\nu$ to the Fisher information. This is one of the cleanest demonstrations of Otto's heuristic delivering sharp functional inequalities. Rubric: full credit for the HWI statement, the log-Sobolev / Talagrand consequences, and the Otto-calculus proof sketch.

**Exercise 10 (hard, short-answer).**

The Arnold-Khesin 2nd-edition account places the pressureless Euler equation on $\mathcal{P}_2(M)$ in the Euler-Arnold framework. State the identification $\mathcal{P}_2(M) \cong \mathrm{Diff}_+(M) / \mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}(M)$ and explain how the Wasserstein metric arises as a quotient of an $L^2$ metric.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The action of $\mathrm{Diff}_+$ on a fixed reference measure $\mu_0$ has stabiliser $\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}(\mu_0)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $M$ be a smooth manifold with a fixed background volume form (or, in the $\mathbb{R}^n$ case, Lebesgue measure), and fix a reference probability measure $\mu_0 \in \mathcal{P}_2(M)$ with smooth positive density. The group $\mathrm{Diff}_+(M)$ of orientation-preserving diffeomorphisms acts on $\mathcal{P}_2(M)$ by push-forward $\eta \cdot \mu = \eta_* \mu$. The stabiliser of $\mu_0$ is precisely the group $\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}(\mu_0) = \{\eta : \eta_* \mu_0 = \mu_0\}$ of $\mu_0$-preserving diffeomorphisms. By Moser's theorem (1965, *Trans. AMS* 120), any two smooth positive probability densities are $\mathrm{Diff}_+$-conjugate, so the orbit through $\mu_0$ is all of $\mathcal{P}_2(M)^{\mathrm{ac}, +}$ (smooth positive measures). The orbit map gives the **identification**
$$
\mathrm{Diff}_+(M) / \mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}(\mu_0) \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathcal{P}_2(M)^{\mathrm{ac}, +}, \qquad [\eta] \mapsto \eta_* \mu_0.
$$

The right-invariant $L^2$ metric on $\mathrm{Diff}_+(M)$, $\langle X, Y\rangle_\eta = \int_M \langle X(x), Y(x)\rangle_g \, d\mu_0(x)$ (with $X, Y$ tangent vectors at $\eta$, i.e. vector fields composed with $\eta$), is right-$\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}$-invariant, hence descends to a (formal) Riemannian metric on the quotient $\mathcal{P}_2$. Otto's identification is that this quotient metric is exactly the **Wasserstein-2 metric**: $W_2^2(\mu_0, \mu_1) = \inf_\eta \int_M |X|^2 d\mu_0$ over horizontal lifts $X$ of curves from $\mu_0$ to $\mu_1$, and the infimum is achieved on the Brenier map $T = \nabla\varphi$. The Euler-Arnold geodesic equation on the quotient is the pressureless Euler system [05.09.05], placing optimal transport in the same right-invariant-metric framework as Arnold's 1966 ideal-fluid hydrodynamics.

The **distinction** from Arnold's original setup is that Arnold considered the *volume-preserving* subgroup $\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}$ with its own right-invariant $L^2$ metric (giving the incompressible Euler equation), whereas Otto considers the *full* $\mathrm{Diff}_+$ modulo $\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}$ (giving pressureless / compressible Euler). The two pictures are linked by the moment-map / coadjoint-orbit description: $\mathcal{P}_2(M)$ is the dual of the abelian Lie algebra of smooth functions on $M$ modulo constants, and the Wasserstein metric is the Otto-Khesin-Misiolek 2003 [ref: TODO_REF Arnold-Khesin] right-invariant metric on this dual. Rubric: full credit for the quotient identification, the descent of the $L^2$ metric, and the relationship to Arnold's incompressible Euler.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Brenier's theorem and Monge-Ampère regularity (1991, Caffarelli 1992-2000).** Brenier 1991 [ref: TODO_REF Brenier 1991] (*Comm. Pure Appl. Math.* 44) proved the existence and uniqueness of optimal transport maps for quadratic cost on $\mathbb{R}^n$ when the source $\mu$ is absolutely continuous. The map is the gradient of a convex function $T = \nabla \varphi$, and $\varphi$ solves the Monge-Ampère equation $\det(\nabla^2 \varphi(x)) = \rho(x) / \sigma(\nabla\varphi(x))$ where $d\mu = \rho \, dx$, $d\nu = \sigma \, dy$. Caffarelli 1992 (*Comm. Pure Appl. Math.* 45) proved $C^{1, \alpha}$-regularity of $\varphi$ on the interior of convex source domains; under stronger assumptions (uniform convexity, smooth densities) the regularity bootstraps to $C^\infty$. The Monge-Ampère viewpoint underlies the entire regularity theory of optimal transport and has driven significant developments in fully nonlinear elliptic PDE.

**McCann interpolation and displacement convexity (1995-1997).** McCann 1995 [ref: TODO_REF McCann 1995] (*Duke Math. J.* 80) and McCann 1997 [ref: TODO_REF McCann 1997] (*Adv. Math.* 128) introduced **displacement interpolation**: the curve $\mu_t = ((1-t)\mathrm{id} + tT)_* \mu_0$ is the Wasserstein geodesic from $\mu_0$ to $\mu_1 = T_* \mu_0$. The notion of **displacement convexity** — convexity along these geodesics — gave a new perspective on classical functional inequalities (sharp Brunn-Minkowski, Prékopa-Leindler, sharp Sobolev) by linearising the relevant quantities along the geodesic. McCann's PhD thesis (Princeton 1994, advised by Lieb) is widely viewed as the bridge from the static Monge-Kantorovich theory to the modern geometric optimal-transport programme.

**Otto's Riemannian calculus (1998-2001).** Otto's *Comm. PDE* 26 paper [ref: TODO_REF Otto 2001] is the canonical reference; the 1998 Bonn preprint (with Westdickenberg and others) developed the calculus piecewise. The formal structure: $T_\mu \mathcal{P}_2 = \overline{\{\nabla\phi\}}^{L^2(\mu)}$, the Levi-Civita connection is $\nabla_{\nabla\phi}\nabla\psi = \nabla(\nabla\phi \cdot \nabla\psi) - \text{(symmetric correction)}$, and the Otto Hessian of a functional $F$ at $\rho$ in the direction $\nabla\phi$ is $\mathrm{Hess}_\rho F[\nabla\phi] = \int [|\nabla^2 \phi|^2_{\mathrm{HS}} + (\nabla^2 V)(\nabla\phi, \nabla\phi)] \rho \, dx$ when $F(\rho) = \int (V\rho + \rho\log\rho) dx$, with $V$ the potential. This Hessian formula, combined with the Bakry-Émery curvature-dimension condition $\nabla^2 V \geq K I$, yields the $K$-displacement convexity of free energy and hence functional inequalities. The Otto calculus made rigorous in metric-measure-space generality by Ambrosio-Gigli-Savaré 2005 [ref: TODO_REF Ambrosio-Gigli-Savaré 2005] *Gradient Flows in Metric Spaces and in the Space of Probability Measures* (Birkhäuser).

**JKO scheme and Wasserstein gradient flows (1998).** Jordan-Kinderlehrer-Otto 1998 [ref: TODO_REF JKO 1998] (*SIAM J. Math. Anal.* 29) discovered that the heat equation arises as the gradient flow of Boltzmann entropy in Wasserstein geometry, via the iterative variational scheme $\rho^{n+1} = \arg\min \{W_2^2(\rho, \rho^n)/2\tau + H(\rho)\}$. The convergence as $\tau \to 0$ to the heat equation is the prototype JKO theorem, and the scheme generalises: Fokker-Planck $\partial_t \rho = \Delta \rho + \nabla \cdot (\rho \nabla V)$ is the $W_2$-gradient flow of $\int (\rho\log\rho + V\rho) dx$; porous medium $\partial_t \rho = \Delta(\rho^m)$ is the gradient flow of $\int \rho^m/(m-1) dx$ (Otto 2001); $q$-Laplacian flows, granular media (Carrillo-McCann-Villani 2003 *Rev. Mat. Iberoam.* 19), McKean-Vlasov flows with nonlocal interaction, Patlak-Keller-Segel chemotaxis (Blanchet-Calvez-Carrillo 2008). The JKO scheme is now the standard tool for proving existence, uniqueness, and long-time behaviour of degenerate parabolic PDEs.

**Lott-Sturm-Villani synthetic Ricci theory (2005-2009).** Lott-Villani 2009 [ref: TODO_REF Lott-Villani 2009] (*Ann. Math.* 169) and Sturm 2006 [ref: TODO_REF Sturm 2006] (*Acta Math.* 196) used displacement convexity of entropy along Wasserstein geodesics to define **synthetic Ricci-curvature lower bounds** on metric measure spaces $(X, d, m)$. The $\mathrm{CD}(K, N)$ condition reads: for every Wasserstein-2 geodesic in $\mathcal{P}_2(X, m)$, suitable entropy functionals satisfy displacement convexity with parameter $K$ and dimension $N$. The theory recovers smooth Ricci-curvature bounds on Riemannian manifolds (Cordero-Erausquin-McCann-Schmuckenschläger 2001), is stable under measured Gromov-Hausdorff convergence, gives a self-contained framework for Ricci-limit spaces (Cheeger-Colding 1997 ff.), and has been refined by Ambrosio-Gigli-Savaré 2014 ($\mathrm{RCD}$ Riemannian-curvature-dimension condition), Bacher-Sturm 2010 (reduced $\mathrm{CD}^*$), and Cavalletti-Mondino 2017 ($\mathrm{CD}_{\mathrm{loc}}$ on essentially non-branching spaces).

**Dynamic formulation and connection to fluid mechanics (Benamou-Brenier 2000).** Benamou-Brenier 2000 [ref: TODO_REF Benamou-Brenier 2000] (*Numer. Math.* 84) recast the Wasserstein-2 distance as
$$
W_2^2(\mu_0, \mu_1) = \inf_{\rho_t, v_t} \int_0^1 \int |v_t|^2 \rho_t \, dx \, dt,
$$
subject to $\partial_t \rho_t + \nabla \cdot (\rho_t v_t) = 0$, $\rho_0 = \mu_0$, $\rho_1 = \mu_1$. The minimisers have $v_t = \nabla\phi_t$ irrotational, $\rho_t$ the Wasserstein geodesic, and $\phi_t$ the Eulerian potential. This **dynamic** or **fluid** formulation makes the connection to pressureless Euler explicit and underlies efficient numerical algorithms (Benamou-Brenier alternating-minimisation, Sinkhorn iterations via entropy regularisation due to Cuturi 2013 *NeurIPS*).

**Optimal transport on Riemannian manifolds and beyond.** McCann 2001 (*Geom. Funct. Anal.* 11) extended Brenier's theorem to compact Riemannian manifolds with cost $d^2/2$, $d$ the geodesic distance; the optimal map is $T(x) = \exp_x(-\nabla \varphi(x))$ for $\varphi$ $c$-concave. Ma-Trudinger-Wang 2005 (*Arch. Rational Mech. Anal.* 177) identified the curvature-of-cost condition controlling regularity. Loeper 2009 (*Acta Math.* 202) proved the equivalence of MTW non-negativity with $C^1$-regularity of optimal maps on the round sphere, opening the regularity theory in non-flat geometries. Figalli-Kim-McCann 2013 (*Comm. PDE* 38) extended this to general MTW manifolds.

**Connections to PDE, probability, and machine learning.** Wasserstein gradient flow has reshaped the analysis of dissipative PDE: porous medium (Otto 2001), thin-film equations (Bertozzi-Pugh 1996, recast in Wasserstein by Matthes-McCann-Savaré 2009), Cahn-Hilliard (Lisini-Matthes-Savaré 2012), Patlak-Keller-Segel (Blanchet 2013). In probability: Talagrand's $T_2$ inequality (1996), the Otto-Villani 2000 HWI inequality, Bakry-Émery hypercontractivity. In machine learning: Wasserstein GANs (Arjovsky-Chintala-Bottou 2017 *ICML*), sliced Wasserstein and entropy-regularised transport (Sinkhorn algorithm: Cuturi 2013), Wasserstein gradient descent for training neural networks (Chizat-Bach 2018 *NeurIPS*), and optimal-transport-based domain adaptation (Courty-Flamary-Tuia-Rakotomamonjy 2017 *IEEE TPAMI*).

**Arnold-Khesin synthesis (2021).** The 2nd edition of Arnold-Khesin [ref: TODO_REF Arnold-Khesin] adds a new chapter integrating Otto's calculus with the Euler-Arnold programme. The identification $\mathcal{P}_2(M) \cong \mathrm{Diff}_+(M)/\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}(M)$ places Wasserstein geometry in the same right-invariant-metric framework as ideal fluid hydrodynamics, with the *quotient* $L^2$ metric on $\mathrm{Diff}_+ / \mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}$ producing the Otto-Wasserstein metric and the geodesic equation producing the pressureless Euler system. The full Euler equation with pressure is recovered on the volume-preserving subgroup $\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}$ via Arnold's 1966 reduction. Khesin-Misiolek 2003 (*Adv. Math.* 176) developed the moment-map description: $\mathcal{P}_2(M)$ is the cotangent space at $\mu_0$ of the abelian Lie algebra $C^\infty(M)/\mathbb{R}$, and the symplectic structure is the canonical one. The Madelung-Brenier framework (Brenier 1989, Carlen-Gangbo 2003) realises the quantum-mechanical wavefunction equation as a Wasserstein gradient flow of the Fisher information, completing the unification of optimal transport, fluid mechanics, and quantum hydrodynamics under a single geometric heading.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Lemma (existence of optimal couplings).** *For $\mu, \nu \in \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$, the infimum $W_2^2(\mu, \nu) = \inf_{\pi \in \Pi(\mu, \nu)} \int |x-y|^2 d\pi$ is attained.*

*Proof.* The set $\Pi(\mu, \nu)$ is convex and weakly-* compact in $\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^n)$ (tightness follows from the marginal constraints and Prokhorov's theorem applied to $\mu$ and $\nu$ separately). The cost functional $\pi \mapsto \int |x-y|^2 d\pi$ is weak-* lower semicontinuous (by Fatou applied to the non-negative continuous integrand $|x-y|^2$, with the uniform bound from finite second moments giving uniform integrability on compact sets). A lower-semicontinuous functional on a weak-* compact convex set attains its infimum. $\square$

**Theorem (Brenier 1991 — existence and uniqueness of the Monge map).** *Let $\mu, \nu \in \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ with $\mu \ll \mathrm{Leb}$. There exists a unique (up to $\mu$-null modifications) map $T = \nabla\varphi$ with $\varphi$ convex and $T_* \mu = \nu$, minimising the Monge cost. The induced coupling $(\mathrm{id} \times T)_* \mu$ is the unique optimal Kantorovich coupling.*

*Proof.* *Step 1 (duality).* Apply Kantorovich-Rubinstein duality: $W_2^2(\mu, \nu) = \sup_{(\phi, \psi)} \int \phi d\mu + \int \psi d\nu$ over pairs with $\phi(x) + \psi(y) \leq |x-y|^2$ everywhere. By a standard argument (replacing $\phi$ with its *$c$-conjugate* $\psi^c(x) = \inf_y \{|x-y|^2 - \psi(y)\}$ and iterating), we may assume the dual is achieved at a pair with $\phi = \psi^c$ and $\psi = \phi^c$, both **$c$-concave** functions.

*Step 2 (envelope characterisation).* In the quadratic-cost case, $\psi^c(x) = \inf_y \{|x-y|^2 - \psi(y)\} = |x|^2 - \sup_y \{2 x \cdot y - |y|^2 + \psi(y)\}$. Defining $\varphi(x) = (|x|^2 - \psi^c(x))/2 = \sup_y \{x \cdot y - (|y|^2 - \psi(y))/2\}$ exhibits $\varphi$ as a supremum of affine functions, hence **convex**.

*Step 3 (optimality at the graph of $\nabla\varphi$).* The complementary-slackness condition $\phi(x) + \psi(y) = |x-y|^2$ holds on $\mathrm{supp}(\pi^*)$ for any optimal coupling $\pi^*$. Substituting $\phi = \psi^c$ and rewriting in terms of $\varphi$ gives the condition $y \in \partial\varphi(x)$ ($\partial\varphi$ the subdifferential) on $\mathrm{supp}(\pi^*)$. Since $\mu \ll \mathrm{Leb}$ and convex functions are Lebesgue-a.e. differentiable (Alexandrov 1939, sharpened by Rademacher), $\partial\varphi(x) = \{\nabla\varphi(x)\}$ for $\mu$-a.e. $x$. Hence $\pi^*$ is supported on the graph of $\nabla\varphi$, and $T = \nabla\varphi$ is the optimal Monge map.

*Step 4 (uniqueness).* Suppose two convex potentials $\varphi_1, \varphi_2$ both yield optimal maps. Their average $\varphi = (\varphi_1 + \varphi_2)/2$ is convex and $\nabla\varphi = (\nabla\varphi_1 + \nabla\varphi_2)/2$ pushes $\mu$ to a measure $\nu'$. Strict convexity of the cost in the coupling forces $\nu' = \nu$ and $\nabla\varphi_1 = \nabla\varphi_2$ $\mu$-a.e. Uniqueness up to additive constant on the potential and $\mu$-null modifications of the map. $\square$

**Theorem (Otto 2001 — heat equation is the entropy gradient flow, full version).** *Let $H(\rho) = \int \rho \log \rho \, dx$ on $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$. A curve $t \mapsto \rho_t \in \mathcal{P}_2 \cap L^1(\mathbb{R}^n)$ is the Wasserstein gradient flow of $H$ iff $\partial_t \rho_t = \Delta \rho_t$ in the sense of distributions.*

*Proof.* *Necessity.* Otto's recipe identifies the Wasserstein gradient as $\nabla_\rho H = \nabla(\delta H/\delta\rho) = \nabla(\log\rho + 1) = \nabla\log\rho = \nabla\rho/\rho$. The gradient flow equation $\partial_t \rho_t = -\nabla_{\rho_t} H$ in the Otto sense reads (via the continuity equation linking tangent vector $-\nabla\log\rho$ to $\partial_t \rho$ through $\partial_t \rho_t + \nabla \cdot (\rho_t v_t) = 0$ with $v_t = -\nabla\log\rho_t$):
$$
\partial_t \rho_t + \nabla \cdot (\rho_t \cdot (-\nabla\rho_t/\rho_t)) = 0 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \partial_t \rho_t = \nabla \cdot \nabla \rho_t = \Delta\rho_t.
$$

*Sufficiency.* For the converse, work with the JKO scheme. Define $\rho^{n+1, \tau} = \arg\min\{W_2^2(\rho, \rho^{n, \tau})/(2\tau) + H(\rho)\}$. The Euler-Lagrange equation at the minimiser gives (using a first-order perturbation argument and the Brenier-map characterisation of $W_2^2$): there exists $\phi^{n+1}$ convex with $\nabla\phi^{n+1}$ being the optimal map from $\rho^{n+1}$ to $\rho^n$, and $\nabla\phi^{n+1}/\tau = -\nabla\log\rho^{n+1}$ in the $L^2(\rho^{n+1})$ sense. Passing to the limit $\tau \to 0$ (justified by AGS gradient-flow compactness machinery) recovers the continuous equation $\partial_t \rho_t = \Delta\rho_t$. The argument generalises to Fokker-Planck and to gradient flows of any displacement-convex functional. Full detail: JKO 1998 [ref: TODO_REF JKO 1998] and Ambrosio-Gigli-Savaré 2005 [ref: TODO_REF Ambrosio-Gigli-Savaré 2005] Ch. 11. $\square$

**Theorem (geodesic equation = pressureless Euler, formal proof).** *On $(\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n), W_2)$ with Otto's formal Riemannian structure, the geodesic equation in the variables $(\rho_t, v_t = \nabla\phi_t)$ is the pressureless Euler system $\partial_t \rho_t + \nabla \cdot (\rho_t v_t) = 0$, $\partial_t v_t + (v_t \cdot \nabla) v_t = 0$.*

*Proof.* The energy functional along a curve $t \mapsto \rho_t$ with tangent $\nabla\phi_t$ is
$$
E[\rho, \phi] = \int_0^1 \int |\nabla\phi_t|^2 \rho_t \, dx \, dt.
$$
The constraint is $\partial_t \rho_t + \nabla \cdot (\rho_t \nabla\phi_t) = 0$. Introducing a Lagrange multiplier $\lambda_t$ and computing first variations: $\delta E / \delta \rho_t$ gives $|\nabla\phi_t|^2 = -2 \partial_t \lambda_t - 2 \nabla\phi_t \cdot \nabla\lambda_t$, and $\delta E / \delta \phi_t$ (after integration by parts) gives $2 \nabla \cdot (\rho_t \nabla\phi_t) = -2 \nabla \cdot (\rho_t \nabla\lambda_t)$ so $\nabla\lambda_t = -\nabla\phi_t \pmod{\rho_t}$. Substituting back: $-2\partial_t \nabla\phi_t - 2 (\nabla\phi_t \cdot \nabla)\nabla\phi_t = \nabla |\nabla\phi_t|^2 = 2(\nabla^2 \phi_t) \nabla\phi_t = 2(\nabla\phi_t \cdot \nabla)\nabla\phi_t$ (using symmetry of $\nabla^2\phi_t$ and the identity $\nabla|v|^2/2 = (v \cdot \nabla)v$ for $v = \nabla\phi$ irrotational), so
$$
\partial_t \nabla\phi_t + (\nabla\phi_t \cdot \nabla)\nabla\phi_t = 0,
$$
which is the pressureless Euler equation. The continuity equation $\partial_t \rho_t + \nabla \cdot (\rho_t \nabla\phi_t) = 0$ is the constraint. Geodesics are precisely the solutions $(\rho_t, \phi_t)$ of this coupled system with endpoint conditions $\rho_0 = \mu_0$, $\rho_1 = \mu_1$, and $\nabla\phi_0 = \nabla\varphi - \mathrm{id}$ where $\varphi$ is the Brenier potential from $\mu_0$ to $\mu_1$. $\square$

**Corollary (closed-form Gaussian Wasserstein).** *For Gaussians $\mu_i = \mathcal{N}(m_i, \Sigma_i)$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$,*
$$
W_2^2(\mu_0, \mu_1) = |m_1 - m_0|^2 + \mathrm{tr}(\Sigma_0 + \Sigma_1 - 2(\Sigma_0^{1/2}\Sigma_1\Sigma_0^{1/2})^{1/2}).
$$
*The optimal map is affine: $T(x) = m_1 + A(x - m_0)$ with $A = \Sigma_0^{-1/2}(\Sigma_0^{1/2}\Sigma_1\Sigma_0^{1/2})^{1/2}\Sigma_0^{-1/2}$, the unique positive-symmetric solution of $A \Sigma_0 A = \Sigma_1$.*

*Proof.* The convex potential $\varphi(x) = (x - m_0) \cdot m_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}(x - m_0)^T A (x - m_0)$ has gradient $\nabla\varphi(x) = m_1 + A(x - m_0) = T(x)$. Push-forward: $T_* \mathcal{N}(m_0, \Sigma_0) = \mathcal{N}(m_1, A \Sigma_0 A) = \mathcal{N}(m_1, \Sigma_1)$. By Brenier's theorem $T$ is optimal. The cost is computed directly: $W_2^2 = \int |x - T(x)|^2 d\mu_0(x) = |m_1 - m_0|^2 + \mathrm{tr}((I - A)\Sigma_0 (I - A))$, which simplifies using $A \Sigma_0 A = \Sigma_1$ to the stated formula. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Euler-Arnold equations [05.09.05].** The pressureless Euler equation is the geodesic equation on $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ with the Otto metric, and the identification $\mathcal{P}_2(M) \cong \mathrm{Diff}_+(M)/\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}(M)$ places optimal transport inside Arnold's 1966 framework of geodesics on Lie groups. The volume-preserving subgroup gives the incompressible Euler equation; the full diffeomorphism group modulo volume-preservation gives the optimal-transport / pressureless picture. Khesin-Misiolek 2003 (*Adv. Math.* 176) developed the moment-map description connecting the two.

- **Hamiltonian vector field [05.02.05].** Otto's calculus realises $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$ as a (formal) Riemannian manifold whose Levi-Civita connection has a Hamiltonian interpretation: the Hamilton-Jacobi equation $\partial_t \phi_t + |\nabla\phi_t|^2/2 = 0$ generates Wasserstein geodesics. The Wasserstein geodesic flow is a Hamiltonian flow on $T^* \mathcal{P}_2$ for the kinetic-energy Hamiltonian.

- **Liouville volume [05.02.07].** The connection between optimal transport and Liouville's theorem is via the dual / Eulerian description: along a Wasserstein geodesic, the volume element $\rho_t \, dx$ is conserved by the continuity equation, just as the symplectic volume is conserved by Hamiltonian flow. The Madelung-Bohm transformation $\psi = \sqrt{\rho} e^{i S/\hbar}$ connects Wasserstein geometry to the Madelung quantum-hydrodynamic equations and to Liouville volume on phase space.

- **Probability theory rules and distributions [26.02.01].** The Wasserstein metric metrizes weak convergence on $\mathcal{P}_2$ supplemented by second-moment convergence — a refinement of the weak topology. It dominates the total variation in a precise sense (Kantorovich-Rubinstein for $W_1$; for $W_2$ the comparison goes through Pinsker's inequality and entropy). Convergence rates in central limit theorems, large deviations, and concentration inequalities are most naturally expressed in Wasserstein distance.

- **Ideal-fluid hydrodynamics (Arnold-Khesin Ch. I).** Arnold-Khesin 2nd-edition new chapter on optimal-transport geometry of $\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}$ integrates the Wasserstein-Otto framework with the topological-fluid-mechanics programme. The pressureless Euler equation on $\mathcal{P}_2$ and the incompressible Euler equation on $\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}$ are two faces of a single geodesic-flow picture, with the link supplied by the moment-map / coadjoint-orbit identifications.

- **Information geometry and statistical inference.** The Fisher-Rao metric on $\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{R}^n)$ is a different Riemannian structure on probability distributions, equivalent to the Otto metric only at the level of *infinitesimal* second-order analysis (both have the same Hessian of Boltzmann entropy at any reference measure). The Otto metric is the unique structure for which the heat equation is gradient flow of entropy; the Fisher-Rao metric is the unique invariant structure under sufficient-statistic transformations. The Madelung-Brenier picture (Carlen-Gangbo 2003) realises the quantum-mechanical Schrödinger equation as Wasserstein gradient flow of the Fisher information $\int |\nabla\sqrt{\rho}|^2 dx = \tfrac{1}{4}\int |\nabla\log\rho|^2 \rho dx$, unifying the two metrics within a single PDE.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Gaspard Monge 1781 [ref: TODO_REF Monge 1781] (*Histoire de l'Académie Royale des Sciences*) posed the original transport problem in the context of military earthworks: given a pile of earth at a construction site and a hole to be filled, find the cheapest assignment of mass from source to destination. Monge proved that the optimal map has the geometric property that transport rays do not cross — anticipating, by 200 years, the convex-potential characterisation of Brenier. The problem was studied sporadically through the nineteenth century by Appell, Hadamard, and others, but did not attract sustained attention until Leonid Kantorovich's 1942 paper [ref: TODO_REF Kantorovich 1942] (*Dokl. Akad. Nauk SSSR* 37) introduced the linear-programming relaxation: instead of insisting on a deterministic transport map, allow mass to *split* and look for the optimal coupling. Kantorovich's reformulation made the problem mathematically tractable and earned him the 1975 Nobel Prize in Economics (jointly with T. Koopmans) for applications to resource allocation.

Yann Brenier 1987 (*C. R. Acad. Sci. Paris* 305) and 1991 [ref: TODO_REF Brenier 1991] (*Comm. Pure Appl. Math.* 44) revolutionised the theory by proving that the optimal map with quadratic cost is the gradient of a convex function. This polar-factorisation theorem connected optimal transport to the Monge-Ampère equation and to the theory of convex duality, opening the door to regularity theory (Caffarelli 1992, *Comm. Pure Appl. Math.* 45). Robert McCann's 1994 PhD thesis (Princeton, advised by Lieb) and his 1995 [ref: TODO_REF McCann 1995] *Duke Math. J.* and 1997 [ref: TODO_REF McCann 1997] *Adv. Math.* papers introduced **displacement interpolation** and **displacement convexity**, providing the bridge from static Monge-Kantorovich to dynamic Wasserstein geometry.

Felix Otto's 1998-2001 work [ref: TODO_REF Otto 2001] (*Comm. Partial Differential Equations* 26) introduced the formal Riemannian calculus on $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbb{R}^n)$, recognising that the Wasserstein-2 distance defines a geodesic Riemannian structure whose tangent spaces are $L^2(\mu)$-gradients and whose geodesic equation is the pressureless Euler system. Jordan-Kinderlehrer-Otto 1998 [ref: TODO_REF JKO 1998] (*SIAM J. Math. Anal.* 29) had already shown the heat equation is gradient flow of entropy in this geometry. Cédric Villani's 2003 [ref: TODO_REF Villani 2003] *Topics in Optimal Transportation* (AMS) and 2008 *Optimal Transport: Old and New* (Springer) made the modern theory accessible to a broad mathematical audience; Villani received the 2010 Fields Medal partly for this work.

Ambrosio-Gigli-Savaré 2005 [ref: TODO_REF Ambrosio-Gigli-Savaré 2005] (Birkhäuser) provided rigorous analytic foundations for Otto's formal calculus, developing the theory of gradient flows on metric spaces in full generality. Lott-Villani 2009 [ref: TODO_REF Lott-Villani 2009] (*Ann. Math.* 169) and Sturm 2006 [ref: TODO_REF Sturm 2006] (*Acta Math.* 196) used Wasserstein geometry to define synthetic Ricci-curvature lower bounds on metric measure spaces, extending Cheeger-Colding theory to a fully axiomatic setting. Otto-Villani 2000 (*J. Funct. Anal.* 173) and Bakry-Émery 1985 (*Sém. Probab.* XIX) linked Wasserstein gradient flow to log-Sobolev inequalities and to the Bakry-Émery $\Gamma_2$-criterion. By the 2010s optimal transport had become a unifying language across PDE, probability, geometric analysis, computer science, and machine learning, and the 2nd edition of Arnold-Khesin [ref: TODO_REF Arnold-Khesin] integrated it explicitly into the Euler-Arnold programme.
