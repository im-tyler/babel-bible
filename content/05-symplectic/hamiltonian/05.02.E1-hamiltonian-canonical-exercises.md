## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Arnold's Part III recasts mechanics on the cotangent bundle $T^*M$ with its canonical symplectic form $\omega = dp\wedge dq = -d\theta$, $\theta = p\,dq$. A Hamiltonian $H : T^*M \to \mathbb{R}$ defines the vector field $X_H$ by $\iota_{X_H}\omega = dH$, whose flow is Hamilton's equations $\dot q = \partial_p H$, $\dot p = -\partial_q H$. The Poisson bracket $\{f, g\} = \omega(X_f, X_g)$ controls the time evolution of observables; a transformation is canonical exactly when it preserves $\omega$, and generating functions parametrise such transformations. The Hamilton-Jacobi equation $\partial_t S + H(q, \partial_q S, t) = 0$ uses a single generating function to integrate the whole flow.

This pack collects nine exercises — two easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units [05.02.01], [05.02.02], [05.02.05], [05.05.03], [05.05.04], and [05.02.07], not as a standalone development. The problems are grouped by Arnold section: Hamilton's equations and Poisson-bracket computations (easy/medium), canonicity tests and the four generating-function types (medium), and Hamilton-Jacobi separation of variables (hard).

The conventions throughout are Arnold's: $\omega = dp\wedge dq$ (so $\dot q = \partial_p H$, $\dot p = -\partial_q H$), $\{f, g\} = \partial_p f\,\partial_q g - \partial_q f\,\partial_p g$ in one degree of freedom with the sign giving $\dot f = \{H, f\}$, and the four generating functions $S_1(q, Q)$, $S_2(q, P)$, $S_3(p, Q)$, $S_4(p, P)$ in Goldstein's labelling.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining eight follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *Show that a transformation $(q, p)\mapsto (Q, P)$ generated by $S_2(q, P)$ via $p = \partial_q S_2$, $Q = \partial_P S_2$ is canonical, and verify on $S_2 = qP$ (the identity) and $S_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(q^2 + P^2)\cot$ ... — take $S_2 = f(q)P$.*

**Solution.** A type-2 generating function $S_2(q, P)$ defines the transformation implicitly by

$$
p = \frac{\partial S_2}{\partial q}, \qquad Q = \frac{\partial S_2}{\partial P}.
$$

To see canonicity, recall the defining identity of generating functions: $p\,dq - P\,dQ = dS_2 - d(PQ)$, equivalently $p\,dq + Q\,dP = dS_2$. Taking the exterior derivative of $p\,dq + Q\,dP = dS_2$ and using $d^2 = 0$:

$$
dp\wedge dq + dQ\wedge dP = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad dp\wedge dq = dP\wedge dQ.
$$

That is exactly $\omega = dp\wedge dq = dP\wedge dQ$ — the transformation pulls the canonical 2-form back to itself, so it is a symplectomorphism, i.e. canonical.

Now $S_2 = f(q)\,P$ for a diffeomorphism $f$. Then $p = \partial_q S_2 = f'(q)P$ and $Q = \partial_P S_2 = f(q)$. So the point transformation is $Q = f(q)$ on the base, with momentum $P = p / f'(q)$ (solving $p = f'(q)P$). Check directly:

$$
dQ = f'(q)\,dq, \qquad dP = d\!\left(\frac{p}{f'(q)}\right) = \frac{dp}{f'(q)} - \frac{p f''(q)}{f'(q)^2}dq.
$$

Then $dP\wedge dQ = \left(\frac{dp}{f'} - \frac{pf''}{(f')^2}dq\right)\wedge f'\,dq = dp\wedge dq$ (the $dq\wedge dq$ term vanishes). So $S_2 = qP$ gives the identity ($f = \mathrm{id}$), and any base diffeomorphism $f$ lifts canonically to $T^*M$ — the cotangent lift. $\square$

This is the operational meaning of "generating function": it produces a canonical transformation for free, with canonicity guaranteed by $d^2 = 0$. Every generating-function exercise downstream runs this argument with a different $S$.

## Exercises [Intermediate]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).** *Hamilton's equations from the Legendre transform.*

Given $H(q, p) = \tfrac{p^2}{2m} + V(q)$, write Hamilton's equations and show they reproduce Newton's equation $m\ddot q = -V'(q)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\dot q = \partial_p H$, $\dot p = -\partial_q H$. Differentiate the first and substitute the second.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Hamilton's equations are

$$
\dot q = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p} = \frac{p}{m}, \qquad \dot p = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q} = -V'(q).
$$

From the first, $p = m\dot q$, so $\dot p = m\ddot q$. Substituting into the second:

$$
m\ddot q = -V'(q),
$$

which is Newton's second law. The two first-order Hamilton equations are equivalent to the single second-order Newton equation; the cost is doubling the dimension (phase space $T^*M$ instead of configuration space $M$), the gain is the symplectic geometry. Arnold §37, unit [05.02.01].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Fundamental Poisson brackets.*

Compute the brackets $\{q, p\}$, $\{q, q\}$, $\{p, p\}$, and $\{q, H\}$ for $H = \tfrac{p^2}{2m} + V(q)$ in one degree of freedom.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $\{f, g\} = \partial_q f\,\partial_p g - \partial_p f\,\partial_q g$. Note the time-evolution identity $\dot f = \{f, H\}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $\{f, g\} = \partial_q f\,\partial_p g - \partial_p f\,\partial_q g$:

$$
\{q, p\} = 1\cdot 1 - 0\cdot 0 = 1, \quad \{q, q\} = 0, \quad \{p, p\} = 0.
$$

These are the fundamental brackets. For $\{q, H\}$:

$$
\{q, H\} = \partial_q q\,\partial_p H - \partial_p q\,\partial_q H = 1\cdot\frac{p}{m} - 0 = \frac{p}{m} = \dot q,
$$

recovering Hamilton's first equation through the evolution identity $\dot f = \{f, H\}$. (Sign convention: Arnold's $\dot f = \{f, H\}$ matches $\{q, p\} = 1$.) Arnold §40, unit [05.02.02].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).** *Jacobi identity gives a conserved bracket.*

Suppose $f$ and $g$ are both conserved along the Hamiltonian flow of $H$ (so $\{f, H\} = \{g, H\} = 0$). Use the Jacobi identity to show $\{f, g\}$ is also conserved. This is Poisson's theorem.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Jacobi identity is $\{\{f, g\}, H\} + \{\{g, H\}, f\} + \{\{H, f\}, g\} = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A function $u$ is conserved iff $\{u, H\} = 0$ (since $\dot u = \{u, H\}$). Apply the Jacobi identity to $f, g, H$:

$$
\{\{f, g\}, H\} + \{\{g, H\}, f\} + \{\{H, f\}, g\} = 0.
$$

By hypothesis $\{g, H\} = 0$ and $\{H, f\} = -\{f, H\} = 0$, so the last two terms vanish, leaving

$$
\{\{f, g\}, H\} = 0.
$$

Therefore $\{f, g\}$ is conserved. This is Poisson's theorem: the Poisson bracket of two integrals of motion is again an integral of motion. It is the mechanism by which a symmetry algebra (e.g. the $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ angular-momentum brackets $\{L_i, L_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk}L_k$) closes, and it sometimes manufactures new conserved quantities. Arnold §40.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).** *Test a transformation for canonicity.*

Decide whether $(q, p)\mapsto (Q, P) = (q\cos\alpha - p\sin\alpha,\; q\sin\alpha + p\cos\alpha)$ — rotation by a fixed angle $\alpha$ in phase space — is canonical.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $dP\wedge dQ$ and compare to $dp\wedge dq$. Equivalently check $\{Q, P\} = 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Compute the Poisson bracket $\{Q, P\}$ in the original coordinates:

$$
\{Q, P\} = \partial_q Q\,\partial_p P - \partial_p Q\,\partial_q P.
$$

Here $\partial_q Q = \cos\alpha$, $\partial_p Q = -\sin\alpha$, $\partial_q P = \sin\alpha$, $\partial_p P = \cos\alpha$. So

$$
\{Q, P\} = \cos\alpha\cdot\cos\alpha - (-\sin\alpha)\cdot\sin\alpha = \cos^2\alpha + \sin^2\alpha = 1.
$$

Since $\{Q, P\} = 1 = \{q, p\}$, the transformation preserves the fundamental bracket, hence is canonical. Equivalently $dP\wedge dQ = dp\wedge dq$ because the Jacobian is a rotation, with determinant $1$. Phase-space rotations are canonical — they are the time-$\alpha$ flow of the harmonic-oscillator Hamiltonian $\tfrac{1}{2}(q^2 + p^2)$. Arnold §45.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).** *The $S_1$ generating function $qQ$ swaps coordinate and momentum.*

For the type-1 generating function $S_1(q, Q) = qQ$, with relations $p = \partial_q S_1$, $P = -\partial_Q S_1$, find the resulting canonical transformation and interpret it.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $p$ and $P$ from $S_1 = qQ$, then solve for $(Q, P)$ in terms of $(q, p)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $S_1(q, Q) = qQ$:

$$
p = \frac{\partial S_1}{\partial q} = Q, \qquad P = -\frac{\partial S_1}{\partial Q} = -q.
$$

So $Q = p$ and $P = -q$. The transformation is

$$
(q, p)\longmapsto (Q, P) = (p, -q).
$$

It interchanges coordinate and momentum (up to a sign), demonstrating that in Hamiltonian mechanics positions and momenta are on equal footing — there is no intrinsic distinction, only a symplectic pairing. Canonicity check: $dP\wedge dQ = d(-q)\wedge d(p) = -dq\wedge dp = dp\wedge dq = \omega$. This $S_1 = qQ$ is the standard example showing that type-1 generators can produce transformations no point (configuration-space) transformation could. Arnold §45-§46.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, numeric).** *Liouville volume preservation for a linear flow.*

The Hamiltonian flow of $H = \tfrac{1}{2}(p^2 + \omega_0^2 q^2)$ is linear in $(q, p)$. Without integrating, argue that it preserves phase-space area, and confirm by computing the trace of the linearised generator.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Liouville's theorem: every Hamiltonian flow preserves $\omega^n/n!$. For a linear flow $\dot z = Az$ the volume is preserved iff $\mathrm{tr}\,A = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Hamilton's equations give $\dot q = p$, $\dot p = -\omega_0^2 q$, i.e. $\dot z = Az$ with $z = (q, p)^\top$ and

$$
A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -\omega_0^2 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

A linear flow $e^{tA}$ has Jacobian determinant $\det e^{tA} = e^{t\,\mathrm{tr}\,A}$. Here $\mathrm{tr}\,A = 0 + 0 = 0$, so $\det e^{tA} = 1$ for all $t$: the flow preserves area. This is the special case of Liouville's theorem [05.02.07] — every Hamiltonian generator has trace zero because $A = J\,\mathrm{Hess}(H)$ with $J$ the symplectic matrix, and $\mathrm{tr}(J\,\mathrm{Hess}(H)) = 0$ by antisymmetry of $J$ against the symmetric Hessian. Hamiltonian flows are incompressible. Arnold §38.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).** *Harmonic oscillator by Hamilton-Jacobi.*

Solve the harmonic oscillator $H = \tfrac{1}{2}(p^2 + \omega_0^2 q^2)$ by the time-independent Hamilton-Jacobi equation: find a complete integral $W(q; \alpha)$ of $H(q, \partial_q W) = \alpha$, and identify the resulting action variable.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set $p = \partial_q W$ and solve $\tfrac{1}{2}((\partial_q W)^2 + \omega_0^2 q^2) = \alpha$ for $\partial_q W$, then integrate. The action is $I = \tfrac{1}{2\pi}\oint p\,dq$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The time-independent Hamilton-Jacobi equation is $H(q, \partial_q W) = \alpha$, i.e.

$$
\tfrac{1}{2}\big((\partial_q W)^2 + \omega_0^2 q^2\big) = \alpha \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \partial_q W = \sqrt{2\alpha - \omega_0^2 q^2}.
$$

Integrating gives the complete integral $W(q; \alpha) = \int\sqrt{2\alpha - \omega_0^2 q^2}\,dq$, depending on the single parameter $\alpha$ (the energy). The full action is $S = W - \alpha t$; the new coordinate $\beta = \partial_\alpha W - t$ is constant along the motion, which integrates the flow by quadrature.

The action variable is the loop integral over one period, where $q$ runs between the turning points $\pm\sqrt{2\alpha}/\omega_0$:

$$
I = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint p\,dq = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint\sqrt{2\alpha - \omega_0^2 q^2}\,dq = \frac{1}{2\pi}\cdot\frac{2\pi\alpha}{\omega_0} = \frac{\alpha}{\omega_0}.
$$

(The loop integral is the area $\pi a b$ of the ellipse with semi-axes $a = \sqrt{2\alpha}/\omega_0$, $b = \sqrt{2\alpha}$, giving $\pi\cdot 2\alpha/\omega_0$.) So $\alpha = \omega_0 I$, that is $H = \omega_0 I$ in action-angle variables: the oscillator energy is linear in the action with frequency $\omega_0 = \partial H/\partial I$. Arnold §47-§50, units [05.05.04], [05.02.04].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).** *Separation of variables for the Kepler problem.*

For the planar Kepler Hamiltonian $H = \tfrac{1}{2}(p_r^2 + p_\varphi^2/r^2) - k/r$, show that the Hamilton-Jacobi equation separates as $W = W_r(r) + p_\varphi\varphi$, and write the radial quadrature.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\varphi$ is cyclic, so $\partial_\varphi W$ is a constant $= p_\varphi$. Substitute the ansatz $W = W_r(r) + p_\varphi\varphi$ into $H(q, \partial_q W) = E$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Hamilton-Jacobi equation $H(q, \partial_q W) = E$ is

$$
\tfrac{1}{2}\left((\partial_r W)^2 + \frac{(\partial_\varphi W)^2}{r^2}\right) - \frac{k}{r} = E.
$$

Since $\varphi$ is cyclic ($H$ has no explicit $\varphi$), try the additively separated ansatz $W = W_r(r) + W_\varphi(\varphi)$. The $\varphi$-dependence enters only through $(\partial_\varphi W)^2/r^2$, so $\partial_\varphi W$ must be a constant; call it $p_\varphi$ (the conserved angular momentum). Then $W_\varphi = p_\varphi\varphi$ and the radial equation is

$$
\tfrac{1}{2}\left((\partial_r W_r)^2 + \frac{p_\varphi^2}{r^2}\right) - \frac{k}{r} = E.
$$

Solving for $\partial_r W_r$ and integrating gives the radial quadrature:

$$
W_r(r) = \int\sqrt{2E + \frac{2k}{r} - \frac{p_\varphi^2}{r^2}}\,\;dr.
$$

So $W(r, \varphi; E, p_\varphi) = W_r(r) + p_\varphi\varphi$ is a complete integral depending on the two parameters $(E, p_\varphi)$ — exactly $n = 2$ for two degrees of freedom, as Jacobi's theorem requires. The Kepler problem is integrated by quadratures; the integrals are the action variables yielding the orbit equation and Kepler's third law. Arnold §47-§48.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *Time evolution is a canonical transformation.*

Let $g^t$ be the time-$t$ flow of a Hamiltonian $H$ on $(T^*M, \omega)$. Prove that each $g^t$ is a symplectomorphism, $(g^t)^*\omega = \omega$, using Cartan's magic formula.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show $\frac{d}{dt}(g^t)^*\omega = (g^t)^*\mathcal{L}_{X_H}\omega$, then compute $\mathcal{L}_{X_H}\omega$ with Cartan's formula $\mathcal{L}_X = d\iota_X + \iota_X d$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The flow of a vector field satisfies $\frac{d}{dt}(g^t)^*\alpha = (g^t)^*\mathcal{L}_{X_H}\alpha$ for any form $\alpha$. Apply this to $\omega$:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}(g^t)^*\omega = (g^t)^*\,\mathcal{L}_{X_H}\omega.
$$

Compute $\mathcal{L}_{X_H}\omega$ by Cartan's magic formula $\mathcal{L}_X = d\,\iota_X + \iota_X\,d$:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{X_H}\omega = d\,\iota_{X_H}\omega + \iota_{X_H}\,d\omega.
$$

Now $d\omega = 0$ ($\omega$ is closed), so the second term vanishes. The defining property of the Hamiltonian vector field is $\iota_{X_H}\omega = dH$, so the first term is $d(dH) = d^2 H = 0$. Therefore $\mathcal{L}_{X_H}\omega = 0$.

Hence $\frac{d}{dt}(g^t)^*\omega = 0$, so $(g^t)^*\omega$ is constant in $t$; at $t = 0$ it equals $\omega$, giving $(g^t)^*\omega = \omega$ for all $t$. The Hamiltonian flow is a one-parameter family of canonical transformations. Wedging $n$ times, it also preserves $\omega^n/n!$ — that is Liouville's theorem [05.02.07] as a corollary. Arnold §38, §45.

</details>

</aside>
