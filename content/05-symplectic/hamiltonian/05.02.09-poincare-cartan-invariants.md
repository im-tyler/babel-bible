## Intuition [Beginner]

Hamiltonian mechanics on a phase space of positions and momenta has a clock running alongside it. Stack the clock on top of the phase space, and you get **extended phase space**: a single big space where every point records a position, a momentum, and a moment in time. A trajectory of the mechanical system becomes a curve through this extended space, drawn out as time advances.

On extended phase space there is one privileged one-form: the **Poincaré-Cartan one-form** $\theta = p\, dq - H\, dt$. It packages the symplectic data and the energy together. What Poincaré discovered in 1890 is that integrating $\theta$ around any closed loop in extended phase space gives a number that the Hamiltonian flow cannot change. Slide the loop along by the flow and the integral stays the same.

This is a generalisation of conservation of energy and conservation of phase volume rolled into one statement. It produces a whole family of integral invariants, one for each even degree from 2 up to the dimension of phase space. The top one is the Liouville volume; the bottom one is the loop integral itself.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the one-dimensional harmonic oscillator: position $q$, momentum $p$, energy $H = \tfrac{1}{2}(q^2 + p^2)$. A periodic orbit at energy $E$ is a circle of radius $\sqrt{2E}$ in the $(q, p)$ plane, traversed in time $2\pi$.

Lift the orbit into extended phase space by also marking time. The orbit becomes a helix wrapping around the time axis, one full turn per unit of time scaled by the period. Pick the closed loop $\gamma$ at time $t = 0$ — the original circle.

The Poincaré-Cartan integral around the time-zero loop is

$$
\oint_\gamma p\, dq - H\, dt = \oint_\gamma p\, dq = \pi \cdot 2E = 2\pi E.
$$

The $H\, dt$ term drops out because the loop sits at a single time and $dt = 0$ along it. The $\oint p\, dq$ part is the area enclosed in the $(q, p)$ plane, which is $\pi \cdot (\text{radius})^2 = 2\pi E$. After one period the orbit returns to itself, so the same loop appears at time $t = 2\pi$ with the same $2\pi E$. What this tells us: the loop integral is the **action** of the orbit, $2\pi E$ for the oscillator at energy $E$, and the Hamiltonian flow preserves this action exactly.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $Q$ be a smooth manifold and $T^*Q$ its cotangent bundle, with canonical one-form $\theta_Q = p\, dq$ and canonical symplectic form $\omega_Q = -d\theta_Q = dq \wedge dp$ (sign convention as in [05.02.05]; for the Poincaré-Cartan setup we use the opposite-sign canonical one-form $p\, dq$, matching Arnold §44). The **extended phase space** is the product $\widetilde{M} = T^*Q \times \mathbb{R}$ with coordinate $t$ on the second factor and pull-backs of $(q, p)$ from the first.

For a smooth time-dependent Hamiltonian $H : T^*Q \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$, the **Poincaré-Cartan one-form** is

$$
\theta = p\, dq - H\, dt
$$

on $\widetilde{M}$. Its exterior derivative is the closed two-form

$$
\omega = d\theta = dp \wedge dq - dH \wedge dt = dp \wedge dq - \frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i}\, dq^i \wedge dt - \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}\, dp_i \wedge dt
$$

on $\widetilde{M}$. The form $\omega$ has rank $2n$ and a one-dimensional kernel at every point; the unique (up to scaling) direction in $\ker \omega$ that projects to $\partial_t$ on the time factor is the **suspended Hamiltonian vector field**

$$
\widetilde{X}_H = X_H + \partial_t,
$$

where $X_H$ is the (time-dependent) Hamiltonian vector field of $H$ on $T^*Q$ defined by $\iota_{X_H} \omega_Q = dH|_{T^*Q}$ [05.02.01]. Trajectories of the original Hamiltonian system are integral curves of $\widetilde{X}_H$.

For each $k = 1, \ldots, n$, the closed two-form $\omega = d\theta$ has a $k$-th wedge power $\omega^k = \omega \wedge \cdots \wedge \omega$ ($k$ factors), a closed $2k$-form on $\widetilde{M}$. The **Poincaré-Cartan integral invariants** are the integrals

$$
\oint_\gamma \theta, \qquad \int_{\Sigma_k} \omega^k \quad (k = 1, \ldots, n),
$$

over closed one-cycles $\gamma$ and closed $2k$-cycles $\Sigma_k$ in $\widetilde{M}$. The case $k = n$ recovers the symplectic volume on each time slice, the Liouville form of [05.02.07].

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Poincaré-Cartan integral invariants).** *Let $H$ be a smooth Hamiltonian on $T^*Q \times \mathbb{R}$, $\widetilde{X}_H = X_H + \partial_t$ the suspended vector field, and $\widetilde\varphi_s$ its flow on $\widetilde{M}$ for parameter $s \in \mathbb{R}$. Then for every closed one-cycle $\gamma \subset \widetilde{M}$ and every closed $2k$-cycle $\Sigma_k \subset \widetilde{M}$ ($k = 1, \ldots, n$) on which the flow is defined,*

$$
\oint_{\widetilde\varphi_s(\gamma)} \theta = \oint_\gamma \theta, \qquad \int_{\widetilde\varphi_s(\Sigma_k)} \omega^k = \int_{\Sigma_k} \omega^k.
$$

**Proof.** Differentiate the loop integral in $s$. The standard flow identity gives

$$
\frac{d}{ds} \oint_{\widetilde\varphi_s(\gamma)} \theta = \oint_{\widetilde\varphi_s(\gamma)} \mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H} \theta.
$$

Cartan's formula expands the Lie derivative:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H} \theta = d \iota_{\widetilde{X}_H} \theta + \iota_{\widetilde{X}_H} d\theta = d \iota_{\widetilde{X}_H} \theta + \iota_{\widetilde{X}_H} \omega.
$$

The second term vanishes by construction: $\widetilde{X}_H \in \ker \omega$, so $\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H} \omega = 0$. The first term is exact, and the integral of an exact one-form around a closed cycle is zero by Stokes:

$$
\oint_{\widetilde\varphi_s(\gamma)} d\big(\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H} \theta\big) = 0.
$$

Hence $\frac{d}{ds} \oint_{\widetilde\varphi_s(\gamma)} \theta = 0$, so the loop integral is constant in $s$.

For the higher invariants, the same Cartan calculation applied to $\omega^k$ uses closedness of $\omega$ and $\widetilde{X}_H \in \ker \omega$. The graded Leibniz rule gives $\mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H} \omega^k = k\, \omega^{k-1} \wedge \mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H} \omega$. Then $\mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega = d \iota_{\widetilde{X}_H} \omega + \iota_{\widetilde{X}_H} d\omega = 0 + 0 = 0$ (the first term because $\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega = 0$, the second because $d\omega = d^2\theta = 0$). So $\mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H} \omega^k = 0$, and integrating over a closed $2k$-cycle gives $\frac{d}{ds} \int_{\widetilde\varphi_s(\Sigma_k)} \omega^k = 0$. $\square$

**Geometric reformulation.** Equivalently, given two closed loops $\gamma_1, \gamma_2$ that bound a tube $T$ of trajectories of $\widetilde{X}_H$ in $\widetilde{M}$, Stokes' theorem yields

$$
\oint_{\gamma_1} \theta - \oint_{\gamma_2} \theta = \int_T d\theta = \int_T \omega = 0,
$$

because the tangent space to $T$ at every point contains the kernel direction $\widetilde{X}_H$, so $\omega$ restricted to $T$ vanishes pointwise. The loop integrals on the two rims of any tube of trajectories are equal.

**Bridge.** The Poincaré-Cartan calculation here builds toward [05.02.04] (action-angle coordinates), where the action variables $I_k$ are defined as $I_k = \frac{1}{2\pi} \oint_{\gamma_k} p\, dq$ along basis loops $\gamma_k$ on a Liouville torus, and the conservation of the actions under the Hamiltonian flow is exactly the first Poincaré-Cartan invariant applied to $n$ Poisson-commuting integrals; the construction appears again in the Liouville-Arnold integrability theorem, where the same loop integrals on a compact connected level set produce the $n$ angle-conjugate coordinates. Putting these together, the foundational reason integrable systems have global action-angle coordinates is exactly the Poincaré-Cartan invariance: the loop integrals identify cohomology classes on level sets with conserved quantities, and the bridge between the analytic identity $\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega = 0$ and the geometric conclusion $\oint_\gamma \theta = \mathrm{const}$ is the foundational reason action variables are coordinate-free conserved quantities of every integrable system.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **Energy is not constant along $\theta$ unless the loop sits at fixed time.** The full integrand $p\, dq - H\, dt$ has a non-zero $H\, dt$ contribution along any loop with non-zero time displacement. The conserved quantity is the **integral**, not the integrand.
- **The kernel direction is the suspended vector field, not the bare $X_H$.** On extended phase space, $X_H$ alone does not lie in $\ker \omega$; only $X_H + \partial_t$ does. The $\partial_t$ component is what cancels the $dH \wedge dt$ piece in $\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega$.
- **The first integral invariant is not the Liouville volume.** The first invariant is a one-cycle integral; the Liouville volume is the top wedge power $\omega^n / n!$ integrated over a $2n$-cycle. They live at opposite ends of the graded family $\omega^k$.
- **Closedness alone is insufficient.** The proof uses both $d\omega = 0$ and the $\ker \omega$ characterisation of the Hamiltonian direction; without the kernel input, $\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega$ would not vanish and the Lie derivative would not be exact.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Compute $d\theta$ for $\theta = p\, dq - H\, dt$ on $T^*Q \times \mathbb{R}$, expressed in canonical coordinates $(q^i, p_i, t)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply $d$ to each summand and use $d^2 = 0$ to drop higher-order terms.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$d\theta = d(p_i\, dq^i) - d(H\, dt) = dp_i \wedge dq^i - dH \wedge dt$. Expanding $dH = (\partial H/\partial q^i)\, dq^i + (\partial H/\partial p_i)\, dp_i + (\partial H/\partial t)\, dt$ and noting $dt \wedge dt = 0$,

$$
d\theta = dp_i \wedge dq^i - \frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i}\, dq^i \wedge dt - \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}\, dp_i \wedge dt.
$$

The form is closed automatically as $d(d\theta) = 0$. Rubric: full credit for the explicit two-form $\omega = d\theta$ in coordinates.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Verify directly that the suspended vector field $\widetilde{X}_H = X_H + \partial_t$ lies in the kernel of $\omega = d\theta$, where $X_H = (\partial H/\partial p_i)\, \partial_{q^i} - (\partial H/\partial q^i)\, \partial_{p_i}$ on $T^*Q$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H} \omega$ term by term and use Hamilton's equations.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}(dp_i \wedge dq^i) = (\widetilde{X}_H \mathbin{\lrcorner} dp_i)\, dq^i - dp_i\, (\widetilde{X}_H \mathbin{\lrcorner} dq^i) = -(\partial H/\partial q^i)\, dq^i - (\partial H/\partial p_i)\, dp_i$.

$\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}(dH \wedge dt) = (\widetilde{X}_H(H))\, dt - dH = \big[(\partial H/\partial p_i)(\partial H/\partial q^i) - (\partial H/\partial q^i)(\partial H/\partial p_i) + \partial H/\partial t\big]\, dt - dH = (\partial H/\partial t)\, dt - dH$.

Subtracting,

$$
\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega = -(\partial H/\partial q^i)\, dq^i - (\partial H/\partial p_i)\, dp_i - (\partial H/\partial t)\, dt + dH = -dH + dH = 0.
$$

Rubric: full credit for the term-by-term cancellation and the identification $\widetilde{X}_H \in \ker \omega$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 3 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that for a closed loop $\gamma$ sitting at one fixed time $t_0$ in extended phase space, the Poincaré-Cartan invariant reduces to the second invariant $\int_\Sigma dp \wedge dq$ over any spanning surface $\Sigma$ at time $t_0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On a fixed-time loop, $dt = 0$. Use Stokes on the spanning surface in the slice.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

At fixed $t_0$, $dt = 0$ along $\gamma$, so $\theta|_\gamma = p\, dq$ and $\oint_\gamma \theta = \oint_\gamma p\, dq$. Pick a spanning surface $\Sigma \subset T^*Q \times \{t_0\}$ with $\partial \Sigma = \gamma$. By Stokes, $\oint_\gamma p\, dq = \int_\Sigma d(p\, dq) = \int_\Sigma dp \wedge dq$. Hence the first invariant on a fixed-time loop equals the second invariant over a spanning surface in the same time slice. The second invariant is the **Cartan form** of the symplectic-form integral; it is a relative integral invariant of the Hamiltonian flow. Rubric: full credit for the Stokes step and the identification of the two invariants on a fixed-time loop.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Derive the Maupertuis principle from the Poincaré-Cartan invariant: on a fixed energy level $H = E$, the variational principle $\delta \int p\, dq = 0$ governs the spatial trajectories.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On the level set $\{H = E\}$, $H\, dt = E\, dt$ along trajectories, and $\int E\, dt$ has fixed extremum on level sets.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Hamilton's principle on extended phase space says trajectories extremise $\int (p\, dq - H\, dt)$ over varied paths joining fixed endpoints in $\widetilde{M}$. Restrict to varied paths lying on $\{H = E\}$: along any such path, $H\, dt = E\, dt$, and $\int E\, dt = E \cdot (t_1 - t_0)$ depends only on the total elapsed time. Fixing the elapsed time across the variation makes the $H\, dt$ contribution constant, so the variational principle reduces to $\delta \int p\, dq = 0$ over paths on $\{H = E\}$ with fixed endpoints and fixed elapsed time. This is the Maupertuis principle: among all paths on the energy shell joining two configurations, the physical trajectory extremises the spatial action $\int p\, dq$. The principle uses just the spatial one-form because the temporal correction $H\, dt$ has fixed extremum on the level set. Rubric: full credit for the restriction-to-level-set argument and the reduction to $\delta \int p\, dq = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Show that the top integral invariant $\omega^n$ on extended phase space restricts to the symplectic volume $\omega_Q^n$ on each time slice $T^*Q \times \{t\}$, recovering Liouville's theorem [05.02.07] as the top-degree case.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\omega^n$ in coordinates and pull back along the inclusion $T^*Q \times \{t_0\} \hookrightarrow \widetilde{M}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $\omega = \omega_Q + \alpha \wedge dt$ where $\omega_Q = dp \wedge dq$ on $T^*Q$ and $\alpha = -dH = -(\partial H/\partial q^i)\, dq^i - (\partial H/\partial p_i)\, dp_i - (\partial H/\partial t)\, dt$ (the time-component of $-dH$ contributes to $dH \wedge dt$ but $dt \wedge dt = 0$). Expanding $\omega^n$ by the binomial-type identity for forms, the only term surviving on a $2n$-cycle inside a fixed time slice is $\omega_Q^n$, since any term containing $dt$ has a factor $\iota^* dt = 0$ when pulled back along the inclusion $\iota : T^*Q \times \{t_0\} \hookrightarrow \widetilde{M}$. Hence $\iota^* \omega^n = \omega_Q^n$, and integrating over a $2n$-cycle in the time slice gives the Liouville integral. The top Poincaré-Cartan invariant is the Liouville volume; volume preservation under the suspended flow restricts on each time slice to the volume preservation of [05.02.07]. Rubric: full credit for the pullback calculation and the identification with Liouville volume.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Show that for a time-independent Hamiltonian, the loop integral $\oint_\gamma p\, dq - H\, dt$ on a closed orbit equals the action $\oint p\, dq$ minus $E \cdot T$, where $E$ is the energy and $T$ is the period. Specialise to the harmonic oscillator and confirm the result $2\pi I$ stated in the unit's worked example.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On a closed orbit at energy $E$, the helix in extended phase space closes up after one period; the time displacement around the loop is $T$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a time-independent $H$, a closed orbit at energy $E$ traces a circle in $T^*Q$ in time $T = T(E)$. Lifting into extended phase space gives a helix; closing the loop by adding the time-displaced segment closes a one-cycle whose time projection is the interval of length $T$. Along the orbit, $H = E$ is constant, so $\oint H\, dt = E \cdot T$. Hence $\oint_\gamma (p\, dq - H\, dt) = \oint p\, dq - E T$. For the harmonic oscillator at energy $E$, the action is $\oint p\, dq = 2\pi E$ (Exercise on the worked example) and the period is $T = 2\pi$, so the Poincaré-Cartan integral on the closed helical loop is $2\pi E - 2\pi E = 0$ — the helical loop is exact in the closed-orbit cohomology. Rubric: full credit for the $E T$ identification, the helical-versus-fixed-time distinction, and the harmonic-oscillator specialisation.

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

```lean
-- Statement target: the loop integral of the Poincaré-Cartan one-form
-- θ = p dq - H dt on extended phase space T*Q × ℝ is invariant under
-- the suspended Hamiltonian flow. Mathlib has differential forms,
-- exterior derivative, Lie derivative, and Cartan's formula in
-- skeletal form, but no packaged Poincaré-Cartan invariant theorem.

variable {Q : Type*} [SmoothManifold Q] (H : (TStar Q) × ℝ → ℝ)

/-- The Poincaré-Cartan one-form on extended phase space. -/
noncomputable def poincareCartanOneForm
    (H : (TStar Q) × ℝ → ℝ) : DifferentialForm ((TStar Q) × ℝ) 1 :=
  canonicalOneForm Q - H • (dt : DifferentialForm ((TStar Q) × ℝ) 1)

/-- The suspended Hamiltonian vector field on extended phase space. -/
noncomputable def suspendedHamiltonianVF
    (H : (TStar Q) × ℝ → ℝ) : VectorField ((TStar Q) × ℝ) :=
  hamiltonianVF (H ∘ Prod.fst) + ∂t

/-- The first Poincaré-Cartan integral invariant. -/
theorem poincare_cartan_loop_invariance
    (H : (TStar Q) × ℝ → ℝ) (γ : Loop ((TStar Q) × ℝ)) (s : ℝ) :
    integralOver (suspendedFlow H s ∘ γ) (poincareCartanOneForm H)
      = integralOver γ (poincareCartanOneForm H) := by
  -- Reduce to L_{X_H + ∂_t} θ exact:
  -- ι_{X_H + ∂_t} dθ = 0 by ker-ω characterisation.
  -- L_{X_H + ∂_t} θ = d (ι_{X_H + ∂_t} θ) by Cartan.
  -- Loop integral of an exact one-form is zero.
  sorry  -- Mathlib lacks the packaged Poincaré-Cartan theorem
```

The proof would proceed by establishing the kernel characterisation $\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H} \omega = 0$ via Hamilton's equations, applying Cartan's formula to get $\mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H} \theta = d\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\theta$, and concluding by Stokes that the loop integral of an exact one-form on a closed cycle vanishes. ## Advanced results [Master]

The Poincaré-Cartan invariants are the canonical hierarchy of conserved quantities of a Hamiltonian flow on extended phase space. Stating the family in its full graded form, and tracing the invariants back to their cohomological origin, makes the position of the Liouville volume and the action variables in classical mechanics structurally precise.

**The graded family.** On extended phase space $\widetilde{M} = T^*Q \times \mathbb{R}$ with Poincaré-Cartan one-form $\theta = p\, dq - H\, dt$ and $\omega = d\theta$, the wedge powers $\omega^k$ for $k = 1, \ldots, n$ form a graded family of closed forms each preserved by the suspended Hamiltonian flow $\widetilde\varphi_s$. The corresponding integral invariants are:

- $k = 0$ (degree 1, integrand $\theta$): the **first integral invariant**, $\oint_\gamma \theta$ over a closed one-cycle. Poincaré 1890.
- $k = 1$ (degree 2, integrand $\omega$): the **second integral invariant**, $\int_\Sigma \omega$ over a closed two-cycle. Cartan 1922.
- $k$ (degree $2k$, integrand $\omega^k$): the **$k$-th integral invariant**, a relative invariant in Cartan's terminology.
- $k = n$ (degree $2n$, integrand $\omega^n / n!$): the **Liouville volume**, the top-degree case. Recovers [05.02.07].

The Cartan-formula proof is uniform across the family: closedness of $\omega$ plus the kernel characterisation $\widetilde{X}_H \in \ker \omega$ kill the Lie derivative on each wedge power.

**Relative versus absolute invariants.** Cartan distinguished **absolute** integral invariants (preserved by every flow that preserves the form) from **relative** ones (preserved only by Hamiltonian flows on closed cycles, with boundary corrections in general). The Liouville volume is absolute on $\widetilde{M}$; the Poincaré loop integral is relative — its invariance requires the closed-loop hypothesis, since for an open one-chain $c$ with boundary $\partial c$, $\oint_{\widetilde\varphi_s(c)}\theta - \oint_c \theta = \int_c d\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\theta + \big[\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\theta\big]_{\partial c}$ has a non-vanishing boundary contribution. The relative-invariant terminology is exactly this: invariance modulo exact one-forms or boundary corrections.

**Maupertuis principle.** On the energy level $\{H = E\}$ for a time-independent $H$, the spatial one-form $p\, dq$ alone gives the Maupertuis variational principle $\delta \int p\, dq = 0$. The temporal correction $H\, dt = E\, dt$ has fixed extremum on level sets when the elapsed time is held constant in the variation, so the Hamilton principle on extended phase space reduces to the spatial principle on the energy shell. This identifies trajectories with critical points of the spatial action and is the geometric-optics limit of Hamilton-Jacobi: $p\, dq$ on the energy shell is the eikonal one-form.

**Action-angle and Liouville-Arnold.** For an integrable system with $n$ Poisson-commuting integrals $f_1, \ldots, f_n$ (with $f_1 = H$) and a compact connected regular level set $M_c$, the Liouville-Arnold theorem gives $M_c \cong T^n$ and constructs action-angle coordinates $(I_k, \theta^k)$ on a tubular neighbourhood. The action coordinates are exactly the first Poincaré-Cartan invariants on basis loops:

$$
I_k = \frac{1}{2\pi} \oint_{\gamma_k} p\, dq.
$$

The angles $\theta^k$ are the canonical conjugates determined by $dI_k \wedge d\theta^k = \omega_Q$ on the neighbourhood. Putting these together, action-angle coordinates are the geometric realisation of the Poincaré-Cartan invariants as $n$-tuples of conserved quantities and their conjugates. The bridge between the analytic identity $\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega = 0$ and the geometric conclusion $I_k = \mathrm{const}$ is the foundational reason every integrable system carries action-angle coordinates: the loop integrals identify cohomology classes on the Liouville torus with action variables, and the central insight is that the $H^1(T^n; \mathbb{R}) \cong \mathbb{R}^n$ structure of the torus is the cohomological shadow of the Poincaré-Cartan invariance.

**Cohomological framing.** The first Poincaré-Cartan invariant is a pairing between $H_1(\widetilde{M}; \mathbb{R})$ and the de Rham class of $\theta$ (where $\theta$ has a class only when restricted to closed-orbit cycles). On a Liouville torus, $\theta = p\, dq$ is a closed one-form (after restriction; though not exact on $T^n$), and the $n$ cohomology classes $[p\, dq|_{\gamma_k}] \in H^1(T^n; \mathbb{R})$ are exactly the actions $I_k$. The higher invariants pair with $H_{2k}(\widetilde{M}; \mathbb{R})$ via $[\omega^k]$. The graded family of Poincaré-Cartan invariants is the de Rham pairing of the closed forms $\omega^k$ with the homology of cycles preserved by the suspended flow.

**Generalisations to general one-forms.** Cartan's 1922 *Leçons sur les invariants intégraux* developed the path-method machinery for general one-forms $\theta$ on a manifold $M$ with $\omega = d\theta$ rank-$2n$ and a one-dimensional kernel. The corresponding flow preserves loop integrals of $\theta$ and the wedge powers of $\omega$. The Hamiltonian case is the special case $\theta = p\, dq - H\, dt$. The contact-geometry case is the special case where $\theta$ is a contact one-form on a $(2n+1)$-manifold and the kernel direction is the Reeb vector field; the contact form $\alpha$ and Reeb $R$ satisfy $\iota_R d\alpha = 0$, the contact analogue of the kernel characterisation. Putting these together, the Poincaré-Cartan invariants are the Hamiltonian instance of a wider Cartan-style invariant theory of one-forms with one-dimensional kernel.

**Synthesis.** The Poincaré-Cartan integral invariants are the canonical hierarchy of conserved quantities of a Hamiltonian flow, derived from the Poincaré-Cartan one-form $\theta = p\, dq - H\, dt$ by exterior calculus and the kernel characterisation $\widetilde{X}_H \in \ker d\theta$. The proof reduces in three lines to closedness of $\omega$ and the kernel input — no specific input from the Hamiltonian enters. Read in the opposite direction, every conserved loop integral on extended phase space is a Poincaré-Cartan invariant, and the bridge between the analytic identity $\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega = 0$ and the geometric conclusion $\oint_\gamma \theta = \mathrm{const}$ is the foundational reason that action variables, the Liouville volume, and the higher invariants are coordinate-free conserved quantities of every Hamiltonian system. The central insight is that the graded family $\theta, \omega, \omega^2, \ldots, \omega^n$ produces all integral invariants by the same Cartan-formula calculation. Putting these together, the entire structural apparatus of conserved quantities in Hamiltonian mechanics — action variables, Liouville volume, Maupertuis principle, action-angle coordinates — recurs as a special case of the single identity $\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega = 0$.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Theorem (first Poincaré-Cartan invariant).** *On extended phase space $\widetilde{M} = T^*Q \times \mathbb{R}$, the loop integral $\oint_\gamma \theta$ is invariant under the suspended Hamiltonian flow $\widetilde\varphi_s$ for every closed one-cycle $\gamma$.*

*Proof.* See the Intermediate Key theorem section above. The chain of identities is $\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega = 0$ (kernel characterisation, verified in Exercise 2), $\mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H}\theta = d\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\theta + \iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega = d\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\theta$ (Cartan's formula plus the kernel input), then $\frac{d}{ds}\oint_{\widetilde\varphi_s(\gamma)}\theta = \oint_{\widetilde\varphi_s(\gamma)} \mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H}\theta = \oint_{\widetilde\varphi_s(\gamma)} d\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\theta = 0$ by Stokes on the closed cycle. $\square$

**Theorem ($k$-th Poincaré-Cartan invariant).** *For each $k = 1, \ldots, n$, the integral $\int_{\Sigma_k} \omega^k$ is invariant under $\widetilde\varphi_s$ for every closed $2k$-cycle $\Sigma_k \subset \widetilde{M}$.*

*Proof.* The graded Leibniz rule gives $\mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega^k = k\, \omega^{k-1} \wedge \mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega$. Cartan's formula gives $\mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega = d\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega + \iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}d\omega = 0 + 0 = 0$ (first term: $\widetilde{X}_H \in \ker\omega$; second term: $d\omega = d^2\theta = 0$). Hence $\mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega^k = 0$, and by the standard flow identity $\frac{d}{ds}\int_{\widetilde\varphi_s(\Sigma_k)}\omega^k = \int_{\Sigma_k}\mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega^k = 0$. $\square$

**Lemma (kernel characterisation of $\widetilde{X}_H$).** *The two-form $\omega = d\theta$ has rank $2n$ at every point of $\widetilde{M}$, hence a one-dimensional kernel; the unique direction in $\ker\omega$ that projects to $\partial_t$ on the time factor is $\widetilde{X}_H = X_H + \partial_t$.*

*Proof.* In coordinates $(q^i, p_i, t)$, $\omega = dp_i \wedge dq^i - (\partial H/\partial q^i)\, dq^i \wedge dt - (\partial H/\partial p_i)\, dp_i \wedge dt$. The matrix of $\omega$ in the ordered basis $(\partial_{q^i}, \partial_{p_i}, \partial_t)$ has the block-form

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
0 & I_n & -\partial H/\partial q \\
-I_n & 0 & -\partial H/\partial p \\
\partial H/\partial q & \partial H/\partial p & 0
\end{pmatrix}
$$

with rank $2n$. The kernel is spanned by $\widetilde{X}_H = (\partial H/\partial p_i)\, \partial_{q^i} - (\partial H/\partial q^i)\, \partial_{p_i} + \partial_t$, verified by direct contraction: $\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega = 0$ (computed in Exercise 2). The projection of $\widetilde{X}_H$ onto $\partial_t$ has coefficient one. $\square$

**Theorem (tube identity).** *Let $\gamma_1, \gamma_2 \subset \widetilde{M}$ be two closed one-cycles bounding a compact tube $T$ of trajectories of $\widetilde{X}_H$. Then $\oint_{\gamma_1}\theta = \oint_{\gamma_2}\theta$.*

*Proof.* By Stokes, $\oint_{\gamma_1}\theta - \oint_{\gamma_2}\theta = \int_T d\theta = \int_T \omega$. The tangent space $T_x T$ at every point $x \in T$ contains the kernel direction $\widetilde{X}_H(x) \in \ker \omega$, so $\omega|_T$ has rank at most one — but $\omega|_T$ is a two-form, so $\omega|_T = 0$ pointwise. Hence $\int_T \omega = 0$. $\square$

**Theorem (Maupertuis principle).** *On a fixed energy level $\{H = E\}$ for a time-independent Hamiltonian, varied paths joining two configurations $q_0, q_1$ in time intervals of fixed length $T$ extremise $\int p\, dq - E\, dt$, equivalently $\int p\, dq$, over the level set.*

*Proof.* Hamilton's principle on extended phase space gives stationarity of $\int(p\, dq - H\, dt) = \int(p\, dq - E\, dt)$ on the level set. Fixing the elapsed time $T$ across the variation makes $\int E\, dt = E T$ constant, so the variation reduces to $\delta \int p\, dq = 0$. Trajectories on $\{H = E\}$ are the critical points. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Hamiltonian vector field [05.02.01].** The kernel characterisation $\widetilde{X}_H \in \ker \omega$ on extended phase space is the natural reformulation of the defining equation $\iota_{X_H}\omega_Q = dH$ on phase space; the one extra dimension $\partial_t$ is the suspension that makes the kernel one-dimensional.

- **Liouville's volume theorem [05.02.07].** The top integral invariant $\omega^n$ on extended phase space restricts on each time slice to the symplectic volume $\omega_Q^n$ on $T^*Q$; the Cartan-formula calculation on $\widetilde{M}$ recovers the Liouville theorem on each fixed-time slice as the top-degree case.

- **Action-angle coordinates [05.02.04].** Action variables $I_k = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_{\gamma_k} p\, dq$ on a Liouville torus are the first Poincaré-Cartan invariants applied to basis loops; angle coordinates are the canonical conjugates. The Liouville-Arnold theorem packages the action variables and angles into global coordinates on the tubular neighbourhood of a regular compact level set.

- **Symplectic manifold [05.01.02].** Closedness $d\omega = 0$ — the half of the symplectic-form definition that does not use non-degeneracy — is what makes the Poincaré-Cartan calculation work; without it, $\mathcal{L}_{\widetilde{X}_H}\omega^k$ acquires a non-vanishing $\iota_{\widetilde{X}_H} d\omega$ term.

- **Cotangent bundle [05.02.05].** The canonical one-form $\theta_Q = p\, dq$ on $T^*Q$ is the spatial half of the Poincaré-Cartan one-form; $T^*Q$ is the configuration setting and $T^*Q \times \mathbb{R}$ is the extended setting. The naturality of $\theta_Q$ propagates to the naturality of $\theta = \theta_Q - H\, dt$ on $\widetilde{M}$.

- **Hamilton-Jacobi equation [05.05.04].** The Hamilton-Jacobi PDE $\partial_t S + H(q, \partial_q S, t) = 0$ is the eikonal equation for the action $S(q, t)$ defined via the line integral $S = \int \theta$ over a Lagrangian section of $\widetilde{M}$. The Poincaré-Cartan invariant is what makes $S$ well-defined on the section up to global cohomology.

- **Generating functions [05.05.03].** The first Poincaré-Cartan invariant is the obstruction to a generating function being globally well-defined: a canonical transformation has a generating function iff it preserves $\theta$ up to exact corrections, which is the cocycle condition for the loop-integral pairing.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Henri Poincaré published the first integral invariant in *Les Méthodes Nouvelles de la Mécanique Céleste* Vol. III §245-§249 in 1899 (the volume bearing the date 1899 in print, with the integral-invariant material drawn from his earlier 1890 Acta Mathematica paper *Sur le problème des trois corps et les équations de la dynamique* [ref: TODO_REF Poincaré]). The motivation was the three-body problem: Poincaré sought conserved quantities of the planetary Hamiltonian to control the long-time behaviour of perturbed orbits. The loop integral $\oint p\, dq - H\, dt$ on extended phase space gave the first non-energy conserved quantity on a generic three-body orbit. Poincaré called it the *invariant intégral relatif*: relative because the invariance hypothesis is the closed-loop condition, not absolute over open chains.

Élie Cartan extended Poincaré's invariants to the general theory in *Leçons sur les invariants intégraux* (Hermann, Paris, 1922) [ref: TODO_REF Cartan]. Cartan's framing replaced Poincaré's coordinate computations with the modern exterior-calculus formalism: a one-form $\theta$ on a manifold with $d\theta$ rank-$2n$ and one-dimensional kernel produces a hierarchy of integral invariants by wedging $d\theta$. The Hamiltonian case is the special case $\theta = p\, dq - H\, dt$. Cartan's *Leçons* also introduced the absolute / relative distinction and the path-method that later became Moser's trick [05.01.05]. The phrase "Poincaré-Cartan integral invariants" attributes the first invariant to Poincaré and the systematic theory to Cartan.

Arnold *Mathematical Methods of Classical Mechanics* §44 [ref: TODO_REF Arnold] gives the modern symplectic-manifold treatment: extended phase space $T^*Q \times \mathbb{R}$ with the suspended Hamiltonian vector field $X_H + \partial_t$, kernel characterisation, and the graded family of invariants. Arnold's framing emphasises the Liouville volume as the top-degree case and the action variables as the loop integrals on integrable level sets, packaging the Liouville-Arnold theorem [05.02.04] as a global statement about Poincaré-Cartan invariants on a Liouville torus. Abraham-Marsden *Foundations of Mechanics* §3.4 [ref: TODO_REF Abraham-Marsden] gives the canonical mathematical-physics treatment, deriving the invariants from the Cartan-formula calculation on extended phase space and showing the cohomological framing of action variables.

The Poincaré-Cartan invariants are the structural ancestor of several twentieth-century developments: symplectic capacities and Gromov's non-squeezing theorem (which place the Poincaré loop integral inside a wider rigidity theory); contact geometry (where the contact one-form $\alpha$ and Reeb vector field $R$ satisfy $\iota_R d\alpha = 0$, the contact analogue of the Hamiltonian kernel); and KAM theory (where the action variables on Liouville tori serve as the unperturbed coordinates against which Diophantine tori are measured).
