## Intuition [Beginner]

The Lagrangian method takes a single function — the Lagrangian $L$ — and turns it into equations of motion. The examples in this unit show how this works in practice for four physical systems that every physicist and engineer meets repeatedly.

The harmonic oscillator is a mass on a spring: it bounces back and forth at a fixed frequency. The pendulum is a mass swinging under gravity: it oscillates at small angles and goes over the top at large ones. The two-body problem is a planet orbiting a star: gravity pulls them into ellipses. A charged particle in a magnetic field spirals along helical paths.

Each of these systems has a Lagrangian, and in each case the Euler-Lagrange equation reproduces the correct equation of motion. The four examples together show that the Lagrangian formalism handles constraints, external fields, and multi-particle systems with the same mechanical procedure.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the harmonic oscillator: a particle of mass $m = 2$ on a spring with spring constant $k = 8$. The kinetic energy is $T = \dot q^2$ (one-half of $m$ times velocity-squared) and the potential energy is $V = 4 q^2$ (one-half of $k$ times displacement-squared). The Lagrangian is

$$
L = \dot q^2 - 4 q^2.
$$

Step 1. Compute the derivative of $L$ with respect to velocity: $2 \dot q$.

Step 2. Compute its rate of change: $2 \ddot q$.

Step 3. Compute the derivative of $L$ with respect to position: $-8 q$.

Setting rate-of-change of step 1 equal to step 3 gives $2 \ddot q = -8 q$, or $\ddot q = -4 q$. The angular frequency is $\omega = 2$ and the solutions are $q(t) = A \cos(2t) + B \sin(2t)$.

What this tells us: from a single function $L$ we recovered the entire harmonic motion by a mechanical procedure — differentiate twice and read off the equation.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

This unit collects four canonical worked examples of the Euler-Lagrange equations applied to specific Lagrangians. For each example, the procedure is:

1. Identify the configuration manifold $Q$ and the Lagrangian $L : TQ \to \mathbb{R}$.
2. Write the Euler-Lagrange equations $\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i}$.
3. Solve the resulting ODE (or identify its qualitative features).
4. Extract physical consequences.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **The pendulum Lagrangian uses $\cos\theta$, not $\sin\theta$, for the potential.** With the zero of gravitational potential at the pivot, $V = -m g \ell \cos\theta$, and $L = T - V = \tfrac{1}{2} m \ell^2 \dot\theta^2 + m g \ell \cos\theta$. The equation of motion is $\ddot\theta = -(g/\ell)\sin\theta$, not $\cos\theta$ — the sine comes from differentiating $\cos\theta$ with respect to $\theta$.
- **The two-body reduction requires the centre-of-mass coordinate, not just the relative coordinate.** The full six-degree-of-freedom problem splits into a free-particle motion of the centre of mass (3 DOF) and a one-body Kepler problem for the relative coordinate (3 DOF). Dropping the centre-of-mass piece loses conservation of total momentum.
- **The magnetic force does no work, yet the vector potential enters the Lagrangian.** The term $q \mathbf{A} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}}$ in the Lagrangian is velocity-dependent, so the Euler-Lagrange equation produces the velocity-dependent Lorentz force $q \dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \mathbf{B}$ without violating energy conservation (the magnetic force is perpendicular to velocity, hence does zero work).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Euler-Lagrange equations for the four canonical examples).** *Each of the following four systems admits a Lagrangian whose Euler-Lagrange equations reproduce the correct equation of motion:*

*(i) Harmonic oscillator: $Q = \mathbb{R}$, $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}k q^2$, giving $\ddot q + (k/m) q = 0$.*

*(ii) Simple pendulum: $Q = S^1$ with coordinate $\theta$, $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2 \dot\theta^2 + m g \ell \cos\theta$, giving $\ddot\theta + (g/\ell)\sin\theta = 0$.*

*(iii) Two-body Kepler problem: $Q = \mathbb{R}^3$ (relative coordinate $r$), $L = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu \|\dot r\|^2 + G M \mu / \|r\|$, giving $\mu \ddot r = -G M \mu \, r / \|r\|^3$.*

*(iv) Charged particle in an EM field: $Q = \mathbb{R}^3$, $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\|\dot r\|^2 + q \Phi(r,t) - q \mathbf{A}(r,t) \cdot \dot r$, giving $m\ddot r = q(E + \dot r \times B)$ where $E = -\nabla \Phi - \partial \mathbf{A} / \partial t$ and $B = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$.*

*Proof.* Each case is a direct computation.

**(i) Harmonic oscillator.** $\partial L / \partial \dot q = m \dot q$, $\partial L / \partial q = -k q$. The Euler-Lagrange equation is $\frac{d}{dt}(m \dot q) = -k q$, i.e. $m \ddot q = -k q$, or $\ddot q + (k/m) q = 0$. Solutions: $q(t) = A \cos \omega t + B \sin \omega t$ with $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$.

**(ii) Pendulum.** $\partial L / \partial \dot\theta = m \ell^2 \dot\theta$, $\partial L / \partial \theta = -m g \ell \sin\theta$. The Euler-Lagrange equation is $m \ell^2 \ddot\theta = -m g \ell \sin\theta$, i.e. $\ddot\theta + (g/\ell)\sin\theta = 0$. For small angles, $\sin\theta \approx \theta$ and the equation reduces to the harmonic oscillator with $\omega = \sqrt{g/\ell}$.

**(iii) Two-body Kepler.** Let $m_1, m_2$ be the two masses, $r_1, r_2 \in \mathbb{R}^3$ their positions. The full Lagrangian on $\mathbb{R}^6$ is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m_1\|\dot r_1\|^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}m_2\|\dot r_2\|^2 + G m_1 m_2 / \|r_1 - r_2\|$. Introduce centre-of-mass $R = (m_1 r_1 + m_2 r_2)/(m_1 + m_2)$ and relative coordinate $r = r_1 - r_2$. The Jacobian of the change of variables has unit determinant, and $L$ separates as $L = \tfrac{1}{2}(m_1 + m_2)\|\dot R\|^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\mu \|\dot r\|^2 + G m_1 m_2 / \|r\|$ where $\mu = m_1 m_2 / (m_1 + m_2)$ is the reduced mass. The centre-of-mass coordinate $R$ is cyclic, giving free-particle motion. The relative coordinate satisfies $\mu \ddot r = -G m_1 m_2 r / \|r\|^3$. Setting $M = m_1 + m_2$ and dividing by $\mu$ gives $\ddot r = -G M r / \|r\|^3$ — the one-body Kepler problem.

**(iv) Charged particle.** The Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\|\dot r\|^2 + q\Phi - q \mathbf{A} \cdot \dot r$. Compute $\partial L / \partial \dot r^i = m \dot r^i - q A_i$. Then

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\!\left(m \dot r^i - q A_i\right) = m \ddot r^i - q \frac{\partial A_i}{\partial t} - q \frac{\partial A_i}{\partial r^j} \dot r^j.
$$

The position derivative is $\partial L / \partial r^i = q \partial_i \Phi - q (\partial_i A_j) \dot r^j$. Equating:

$$
m \ddot r^i = q\!\left(-\frac{\partial A_i}{\partial t} - \partial_i \Phi\right) + q\!\left(\frac{\partial A_j}{\partial r^i} - \frac{\partial A_i}{\partial r^j}\right)\!\dot r^j.
$$

The first bracket is $E_i$ (the $i$-th component of the electric field). The second bracket is $\varepsilon_{ijk} B_k \dot r^j = (\dot r \times B)_i$ (the $i$-th component of $\dot r \times B$). So $m \ddot r = q(E + \dot r \times B)$ — the Lorentz force law. $\square$

**Bridge.** The four examples in this theorem build toward the general theory of Lagrangian mechanics on $TQ$ [05.00.01] by showing that the abstract Euler-Lagrange machinery produces correct equations of motion in every concrete case. The bridge between the abstract and the concrete is the coordinate computation: each example picks a chart on $Q$, writes $L$ in local coordinates, and the Euler-Lagrange computation is a mechanical algebraic procedure. This is exactly the pattern that appears again in [05.00.10] (scattering, where the Kepler orbit enters the cross-section calculation) and in [05.00.11] (normal modes, where the quadratic Lagrangian near equilibrium generates the stiffness matrix). The foundational reason these four examples are canonical is that they exhibit the four qualitatively distinct types of Lagrangian system: natural ($L = T - V$ with $V$ position-dependent), constrained (the pendulum on $S^1$), reduced (the two-body problem after centre-of-mass separation), and velocity-dependent (the charged particle with the magnetic term $A \cdot \dot r$). Putting these together one sees that the Lagrangian formalism unifies conservative forces, geometric constraints, and electromagnetic interactions under a single variational principle.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

For the harmonic oscillator $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}k q^2$, compute the energy $E_L = \dot q \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q} - L$ and verify that it is conserved along solutions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$E_L = m \dot q^2 - L = m \dot q^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}k q^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}k q^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$E_L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}k q^2 = T + V$. Along solutions $q(t) = A\cos\omega t + B\sin\omega t$, $\dot q = -A\omega\sin\omega t + B\omega\cos\omega t$, and $E_L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(A^2 + B^2)\omega^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}k(A^2 + B^2)$, a constant.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

A pendulum has length $\ell = 10$ m. What is the small-angle oscillation period? Use $g = 9.8$ m/s$^2$ and round to one decimal place.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For small angles, $\omega = \sqrt{g/\ell}$ and $T = 2\pi/\omega$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$6.3$ seconds.**

$\omega = \sqrt{9.8/10} = \sqrt{0.98} \approx 0.9899$ rad/s. Period $T = 2\pi / 0.9899 \approx 6.35$ s, rounding to $6.3$ s.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

For the two-body Kepler problem, carry out the reduction to centre-of-mass and relative coordinates. Show that the Lagrangian separates and identify the effective one-body equation for the relative coordinate.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $R = (m_1 r_1 + m_2 r_2)/(m_1 + m_2)$ and $r = r_1 - r_2$. Express $\dot r_1$ and $\dot r_2$ in terms of $\dot R$ and $\dot r$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Invert: $r_1 = R + (m_2/M) r$ and $r_2 = R - (m_1/M) r$ where $M = m_1 + m_2$. Then $\dot r_1 = \dot R + (m_2/M)\dot r$ and $\dot r_2 = \dot R - (m_1/M)\dot r$. Substitute into $T$:

$$
T = \tfrac{1}{2}m_1|\dot R + (m_2/M)\dot r|^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}m_2|\dot R - (m_1/M)\dot r|^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}M|\dot R|^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\mu|\dot r|^2
$$

where $\mu = m_1 m_2 / M$ and the cross terms cancel. The Lagrangian becomes

$$
L = \tfrac{1}{2}M|\dot R|^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\mu|\dot r|^2 + \frac{G m_1 m_2}{|r|}.
$$

$R$ is cyclic (no dependence on $R$), giving constant total momentum $P = M \dot R$. The relative coordinate satisfies $\mu \ddot r = -G m_1 m_2 r / |r|^3$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the pendulum equation $\ddot\theta = -(g/\ell)\sin\theta$ has a first integral (conserved energy) and express it.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Multiply both sides by $\dot\theta$ and recognise the left side as a total time derivative.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Multiply by $\dot\theta$: $\dot\theta \ddot\theta = -(g/\ell) \sin\theta \, \dot\theta$. The left side is $\frac{d}{dt}(\tfrac{1}{2}\dot\theta^2)$ and the right side is $\frac{d}{dt}((g/\ell)\cos\theta)$. Integrating: $\tfrac{1}{2}\dot\theta^2 - (g/\ell)\cos\theta = \text{const}$. In terms of the Lagrangian energy: $E = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2\dot\theta^2 - mg\ell\cos\theta$ is conserved. The level sets of $E$ in the $(\theta, \dot\theta)$ phase plane are the pendulum phase portrait: closed loops for $|E| < mg\ell$ (libration) and open curves for $E > mg\ell$ (rotation), separated by the separatrix at $E = mg\ell$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

For a charged particle in a uniform magnetic field $B = B_0 \hat{z}$ with vector potential $\mathbf{A} = \tfrac{1}{2} B_0 (-y, x, 0)$, write the Lagrangian and show that the motion in the $xy$-plane is circular with angular frequency $\omega_c = q B_0 / m$ (the cyclotron frequency).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write the Euler-Lagrange equations for $x$ and $y$. Identify the Lorentz force components and solve for circular motion.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot x^2 + \dot y^2 + \dot z^2) - \tfrac{1}{2}q B_0(-y \dot x + x \dot y)$. The $z$-equation gives $\ddot z = 0$ (free motion along $B$). The $x$-equation: $m\ddot x - \tfrac{1}{2}q B_0 \dot y = \tfrac{1}{2}q B_0 \dot y$, i.e. $m\ddot x = q B_0 \dot y$. The $y$-equation: $m\ddot y + \tfrac{1}{2}q B_0 \dot x = -\tfrac{1}{2}q B_0 \dot x$, i.e. $m\ddot y = -q B_0 \dot x$. Combine: set $w = x + iy$, then $m\ddot w = q B_0 (i \dot y - i \dot x \cdot i) = -i q B_0 \dot w$... more directly: $m\ddot x = q B_0 \dot y$ and $m\ddot y = -q B_0 \dot x$ give $\ddot x = \omega_c \dot y$ and $\ddot y = -\omega_c \dot x$ with $\omega_c = q B_0 / m$. Circular solution: $x(t) = x_0 + R\cos(\omega_c t + \phi)$, $y(t) = y_0 + R\sin(\omega_c t + \phi)$ satisfies both. The particle gyrates at the cyclotron frequency.

</details>

</aside>

An electron (charge $q = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C, mass $m = 9.1 \times 10^{-31}$ kg) moves in a magnetic field $B_0 = 0.01$ Tesla. Compute the cyclotron frequency $\omega_c$ in radians per second. Use scientific notation with one decimal place.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\omega_c = q B_0 / m$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$1.8 \times 10^9$ rad/s.**

$\omega_c = (1.6 \times 10^{-19})(0.01) / (9.1 \times 10^{-31}) = 1.6 \times 10^{-21} / 9.1 \times 10^{-31} \approx 1.76 \times 10^9$ rad/s.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Show that the Kepler orbit in polar coordinates $(r, \phi)$ satisfies the Binet equation $d^2 u / d\phi^2 + u = G M / \ell^2$ where $u = 1/r$ and $\ell = \mu r^2 \dot\phi$ is the specific angular momentum. Solve it and identify the conic sections.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use conservation of angular momentum to eliminate $t$ in favour of $\phi$: $d/dt = (\ell/\mu r^2) d/d\phi$. Write the radial equation $\mu(\ddot r - r\dot\phi^2) = -G M \mu / r^2$ in terms of $u(\phi)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From $\ell = \mu r^2 \dot\phi$, $\dot\phi = \ell / (\mu r^2)$. Compute $\dot r = dr/dt = (dr/d\phi)(d\phi/dt) = (-1/u^2)(du/d\phi)(\ell u^2 / \mu) = -(\ell/\mu)(du/d\phi)$. Then $\ddot r = d/dt[-(\ell/\mu)(du/d\phi)] = -(\ell/\mu)(d^2 u / d\phi^2)\dot\phi = -(\ell^2 u^2 / \mu^2)(d^2 u / d\phi^2)$. Substituting into the radial equation $\ddot r - r\dot\phi^2 = -GM/r^2$:

$$
-\frac{\ell^2 u^2}{\mu^2}\frac{d^2u}{d\phi^2} - \frac{1}{u}\frac{\ell^2 u^4}{\mu^2} = -GM u^2.
$$

Dividing by $-\ell^2 u^2/\mu^2$:

$$
\frac{d^2u}{d\phi^2} + u = \frac{GM\mu^2}{\ell^2}.
$$

This is the Binet equation. The general solution is $u(\phi) = GM\mu^2/\ell^2 + A\cos(\phi - \phi_0)$. In terms of $r$: $r(\phi) = \ell^2/(GM\mu^2) / [1 + e\cos(\phi - \phi_0)]$ where $e = A\ell^2/(GM\mu^2)$ is the eccentricity. This is the polar equation of a conic: ellipse for $e < 1$, parabola for $e = 1$, hyperbola for $e > 1$. Rubric: full credit for the time-elimination, the Binet equation, and the conic identification.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

For a charged particle in a uniform magnetic field $B_0 \hat{z}$, show that the Lagrangian is invariant under translations along $z$ and rotations about the $z$-axis. Identify the two conserved Noether charges and give their physical meaning.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Check invariance of $L$ under $z \mapsto z + a$ and $(x, y) \mapsto R(\theta)(x, y)$ where $R$ is a rotation. The Noether charges are $p_z = m\dot z$ (linear momentum along $B$) and $p_\theta = m(x\dot y - y\dot x) + \tfrac{1}{2}q B_0(x^2 + y^2)$ (canonical angular momentum about $B$).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Translation along $z$: $L$ depends on $\dot z$ but not on $z$, so $z$ is cyclic and $p_z = \partial L / \partial \dot z = m\dot z$ is conserved — the linear momentum along the field direction (free motion).

Rotation about $z$: under $(x, y) \mapsto (x\cos\epsilon - y\sin\epsilon, x\sin\epsilon + y\cos\epsilon)$, the kinetic energy is rotation-invariant ($\dot x^2 + \dot y^2$ is unchanged) and the magnetic term transforms as $-y\dot x + x\dot y \mapsto (-y\cos\epsilon - x\sin\epsilon)(\dot x\cos\epsilon - \dot y\sin\epsilon) + (x\cos\epsilon - y\sin\epsilon)(\dot x\sin\epsilon + \dot y\cos\epsilon)$. Expanding and simplifying to first order in $\epsilon$ recovers the same expression: $-y\dot x + x\dot y$ is rotation-invariant. So $L$ is invariant under rotations about $z$.

The conserved angular momentum is $p_\phi = x \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot y} - y \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x} = m(x\dot y - y\dot x) + \tfrac{1}{2}q B_0(x^2 + y^2)$. The first term is the mechanical angular momentum; the second is the additional canonical contribution from the vector potential. The conservation of this combined quantity is a consequence of the axial symmetry of the magnetic field. Rubric: full credit for verifying both symmetries, computing the Noether charges, and identifying the mechanical versus canonical angular momentum.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Harmonic oscillator — complete solution.** The general solution $q(t) = A\cos\omega t + B\sin\omega t$ with $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$ is the projection of uniform circular motion in the $(q, p/m\omega)$ plane. The complex notation $z(t) = q(t) + i p(t)/(m\omega) = C e^{-i\omega t}$ makes the oscillator into a one-dimensional unitary representation of the circle group $U(1)$. The energy levels $E_n = \hbar\omega(n + 1/2)$ of the quantum harmonic oscillator are the eigenvalues of the number operator on this representation.

**Pendulum — phase portrait and elliptic functions.** The energy integral $E = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2\dot\theta^2 - mg\ell\cos\theta$ defines the phase portrait in $(\theta, \dot\theta)$. Three regimes: (a) $E < mg\ell$: libration (closed loops around the stable equilibrium $\theta = 0$), with period $T = 4\sqrt{\ell/g}\, K(\sin(\theta_0/2))$ where $K$ is the complete elliptic integral of the first kind and $\theta_0$ is the amplitude; (b) $E = mg\ell$: the separatrix, connecting the unstable equilibria $\theta = \pm\pi$; (c) $E > mg\ell$: rotation (open curves with $\dot\theta$ never vanishing). The solution $\theta(t)$ for case (a) is a Jacobi elliptic function $\theta(t) = 2\arcsin(k \, \mathrm{sn}(\sqrt{g/\ell}\, t, k))$ where $k = \sin(\theta_0/2)$.

**Two-body Kepler problem — conservation laws and orbit closure.** The Kepler Lagrangian in polar coordinates separates into radial and angular parts. Angular momentum conservation $\ell = \mu r^2\dot\phi$ is a consequence of the $\mathrm{SO}(2)$ rotational symmetry. The Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector $\mathbf{A} = \dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \mathbf{L} - GM\mu \hat{\mathbf{r}}$ is an additional conserved quantity specific to the $1/r$ potential (a consequence of the $\mathrm{SO}(4)$ symmetry of the Kepler problem at negative energy, which enlarges the manifest $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ rotational symmetry). The existence of $\mathbf{A}$ explains the closure of Kepler orbits: it fixes the orientation of the ellipse in the orbital plane.

**Charged particle — adiabatic invariants.** In a non-uniform magnetic field that varies slowly compared to the cyclotron period, the magnetic moment $\mu_B = mv_\perp^2 / (2B)$ is an adiabatic invariant — it is preserved to exponential accuracy along the guiding-centre trajectory. The charged particle spirals along the field line with conserved $v_\parallel$ and adiabatically conserved $v_\perp$, leading to the mirror effect: as the particle enters a region of stronger $B$, $v_\perp$ grows (to keep $\mu_B$ constant) at the expense of $v_\parallel$, and the particle reflects if $v_\parallel$ reaches zero. This is the principle behind magnetic confinement fusion devices.

**Theorem (Runge-Lenz conservation in Kepler).** *For the Kepler problem $\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = -GM \mathbf{r}/r^3$ with angular momentum $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \dot{\mathbf{r}}$, the vector $\mathbf{A} = \dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \mathbf{L} - GM \hat{\mathbf{r}}$ is conserved: $d\mathbf{A}/dt = 0$. The direction of $\mathbf{A}$ is the perihelion direction and $|\mathbf{A}| = GM e$ where $e$ is the eccentricity.*

**Synthesis.** The four canonical examples of this unit are the foundational instances of Lagrangian mechanics, and the reason they are canonical is that each exhibits a distinct qualitative feature that recurs throughout the subject. The harmonic oscillator is the linear case — every smooth Lagrangian near a stable equilibrium reduces to a harmonic oscillator at leading order, which appears again in [05.00.11] (normal modes) as the eigenvalue problem for small oscillations. The pendulum is the nonlinear case — the phase-portrait topology changes at the separatrix, and this is exactly the pattern that generalises to Hamiltonian systems on cotangent bundles of non-simply-connected manifolds.

The bridge between the pendulum and the Kepler problem is the central-force reduction: both separate in polar coordinates by conservation of angular momentum, and the Binet equation is the universal tool for central-force orbit calculations. The charged-particle example is the bridge between conservative mechanics and gauge theory: the velocity-dependent term $q\mathbf{A}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ in the Lagrangian is the simplest instance of a minimal coupling prescription, and this pattern recurs in [05.00.01] (Lagrangian on $TQ$) where the Poincare-Cartan one-form acquires an electromagnetic contribution. Putting these together identifies the four examples as the four corners of the Lagrangian landscape: linear, nonlinear, reduced (central-force), and gauge-coupled. The foundational reason the Euler-Lagrange equations handle all four uniformly is that the variational principle makes no assumption about the form of $L$ — it works for any smooth function on $TQ$ — and the central insight is that the four qualitatively different physical behaviours arise from four qualitatively different Lagrangians, all governed by the same Euler-Lagrange machinery.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (harmonic oscillator energy conservation).** *The energy $E = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}k q^2$ is constant along solutions of $\ddot q = -(k/m)q$.*

*Proof.* $\frac{dE}{dt} = m\dot q\ddot q + k q \dot q = \dot q(m\ddot q + kq) = \dot q \cdot 0 = 0$. $\square$

**Proposition (pendulum energy conservation).** *The energy $E = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2\dot\theta^2 - mg\ell\cos\theta$ is constant along solutions of $\ddot\theta = -(g/\ell)\sin\theta$.*

*Proof.* $\frac{dE}{dt} = m\ell^2\dot\theta\ddot\theta + mg\ell\sin\theta\,\dot\theta = \dot\theta(m\ell^2\ddot\theta + mg\ell\sin\theta) = \dot\theta \cdot 0 = 0$ after substituting $\ddot\theta = -(g/\ell)\sin\theta$. $\square$

**Proposition (Runge-Lenz conservation).** *For $\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = -GM\mathbf{r}/r^3$ with $r = |\mathbf{r}|$, define $\mathbf{A} = \dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \mathbf{L} - GM\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ where $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \dot{\mathbf{r}}$ and $\hat{\mathbf{r}} = \mathbf{r}/r$. Then $d\mathbf{A}/dt = 0$.*

*Proof.* Compute $d\mathbf{A}/dt = \ddot{\mathbf{r}} \times \mathbf{L} + \dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \dot{\mathbf{L}} - GM\frac{d}{dt}(\mathbf{r}/r)$. Since $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \dot{\mathbf{r}}$ is conserved ($d\mathbf{L}/dt = \dot{\mathbf{r}} \times \dot{\mathbf{r}} + \mathbf{r} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}} = 0 + \mathbf{r} \times (-GM\mathbf{r}/r^3) = 0$), $\dot{\mathbf{L}} = 0$. Also $\ddot{\mathbf{r}} \times \mathbf{L} = (-GM\mathbf{r}/r^3) \times (\mathbf{r} \times \dot{\mathbf{r}})$. Using the BAC-CAB rule: $(-GM/r^3)[\mathbf{r}(\mathbf{r}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}}) - \dot{\mathbf{r}}(\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{r})] = (-GM/r^3)[\mathbf{r}(\mathbf{r}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}}) - r^2\dot{\mathbf{r}}]$. Meanwhile $-GM\frac{d}{dt}(\mathbf{r}/r) = -GM[\dot{\mathbf{r}}/r - \mathbf{r}(\mathbf{r}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}})/r^3] = -GM\dot{\mathbf{r}}/r + GM\mathbf{r}(\mathbf{r}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}})/r^3$. Adding: the $\mathbf{r}$-terms cancel and the $\dot{\mathbf{r}}$-terms give $(-GM)(-r^2\dot{\mathbf{r}}/r^3) + (-GM\dot{\mathbf{r}}/r) = GM\dot{\mathbf{r}}/r - GM\dot{\mathbf{r}}/r = 0$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Lagrangian on $TQ$ [05.00.01].** Each example in this unit is a concrete instance of the abstract Lagrangian $L : TQ \to \mathbb{R}$ developed in [05.00.01]. The harmonic oscillator uses $Q = \mathbb{R}$, the pendulum uses $Q = S^1$, the Kepler problem uses the reduced $Q = \mathbb{R}^3$, and the charged particle uses $Q = \mathbb{R}^3$ with a velocity-dependent Lagrangian.

- **Noether's theorem [05.00.04].** The conservation laws exhibited in each example — energy for the oscillator and pendulum, angular momentum for the Kepler problem, linear momentum along $B$ and canonical angular momentum for the charged particle — are all instances of Noether's theorem. The Runge-Lenz vector is the non-manifest symmetry (the $\mathrm{SO}(4)$ of the Kepler problem) that goes beyond the standard Noether catalogue of [05.00.04].

- **Hamilton's principle [05.00.02].** Each worked example can be derived from the variational principle: the physical trajectory makes the action $S = \int L\, dt$ critical among nearby paths with the same endpoints. The Euler-Lagrange equations computed here are the stationarity conditions for $S$.

- **Scattering and Rutherford formula [05.00.10].** The Kepler problem at positive energy ($E > 0$, hyperbolic orbits) is the prototype scattering problem. The Rutherford cross-section is computed from the Kepler hyperbolic trajectory in the Coulomb potential.

- **Small oscillations and normal modes [05.00.11].** The harmonic oscillator of this unit is the one-degree-of-freedom version of the normal-mode analysis. Near any stable equilibrium, the Lagrangian reduces to a sum of harmonic-oscillator Lagrangians in the normal coordinates.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Joseph-Louis Lagrange's *Mecanique analytique* (1788) [ref: TODO_REF Lagrange 1788] collected the first systematic set of worked examples in generalised coordinates: the harmonic oscillator, the compound pendulum, the two-body problem, and the rigid body. Lagrange's contribution was not any single example but the realisation that the same procedure — write $L = T - V$ and apply the Euler-Lagrange equation — handles every case.

The two-body problem was solved by Isaac Newton in the *Principia* (1687) [ref: TODO_REF Newton 1687] using geometric methods; the algebraic solution via the Binet equation and conic sections is due to Johann Bernoulli and Jakob Hermann in the early 1700s. The Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector was known to Laplace and Lagrange; its interpretation as a conserved quantity reflecting the hidden $\mathrm{SO}(4)$ symmetry was made by Vladimir Fock (1935) and Valentin Bargmann (1936) in the quantum context.

The formulation of the charged-particle Lagrangian with the vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ is due to Hendrik Lorentz and was placed in the variational framework by Arnold Sommerfeld and Karl Schwarzschild around 1903-1916 [ref: TODO_REF]. The gauge-invariant form $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot r|^2 + q\Phi - q\mathbf{A}\cdot\dot r$ became standard with the development of classical electrodynamics in the early 20th century.
