## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Arnold's Part II builds classical mechanics on the configuration manifold $M$: a Lagrangian is a smooth function $L : TM \to \mathbb{R}$, a motion is a critical point of the action $S[\gamma] = \int_a^b L(\gamma, \dot\gamma)\,dt$, and the Euler-Lagrange equation $\frac{d}{dt}\partial_v L - \partial_q L = 0$ is the condition for criticality. The Legendre transform (fibre derivative) $\mathbb{F}L : (q, v) \mapsto (q, \partial_v L)$ carries this to the Hamiltonian side, and Noether's theorem ties each one-parameter symmetry group of $L$ to a conserved quantity. Several of Arnold's Part II problems cut across these results at once — a single computation will set up the action, derive the equations of motion, and read off a conservation law from an invariance.

This pack collects nine such exercises — three easy, four medium, two hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units [05.00.01], [05.00.02], [05.00.03], [05.00.04], and the worked-examples unit [05.00.09], not as a standalone development. The problems are grouped by Arnold section: the Euler-Lagrange machinery and elementary variational problems (easy), the Legendre transform and worked mechanical systems (medium), and Noether-theorem applications (hard).

The conventions throughout are Arnold's: $q$ denotes generalised coordinates on $M$, $v = \dot q$ the fibre coordinate on $TM$, $p = \partial_v L$ the conjugate momentum, $\omega = dp \wedge dq = -d\theta$ with $\theta = p\,dq$, and the energy is $E = p\cdot v - L$.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining eight follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *Derive the Euler-Lagrange equation from Hamilton's principle, and verify that $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot q|^2 - V(q)$ recovers Newton's equation.*

**Solution.** Let $\gamma : [a, b] \to M$ be a motion and consider a variation $\gamma_s$ with $\gamma_0 = \gamma$, fixed endpoints $\gamma_s(a) = \gamma(a)$, $\gamma_s(b) = \gamma(b)$, and variation field $\delta q = \partial_s\gamma_s\big|_{s=0}$, which vanishes at $a$ and $b$. The action is $S[\gamma_s] = \int_a^b L(\gamma_s, \dot\gamma_s)\,dt$. Differentiate under the integral sign at $s = 0$ in a chart:

$$
\frac{d}{ds}S[\gamma_s]\Big|_{s=0} = \int_a^b \left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial q}\,\delta q + \frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\,\delta\dot q\right) dt.
$$

Since $\delta\dot q = \frac{d}{dt}\delta q$, integrate the second term by parts:

$$
\int_a^b \frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\,\frac{d}{dt}\delta q\,dt = \left[\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\,\delta q\right]_a^b - \int_a^b \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\right)\delta q\,dt.
$$

The boundary term vanishes because $\delta q(a) = \delta q(b) = 0$. Collecting,

$$
\delta S = \int_a^b \left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial q} - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\right)\delta q\,dt.
$$

A motion is a critical point, so $\delta S = 0$ for every admissible $\delta q$. By the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations, the integrand's coefficient vanishes identically:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial v} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q} = 0.
$$

For $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot q|^2 - V(q)$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$: $\partial_v L = m\dot q$ and $\partial_q L = -\nabla V$. The Euler-Lagrange equation becomes $\frac{d}{dt}(m\dot q) + \nabla V = 0$, that is $m\ddot q = -\nabla V(q)$ — Newton's second law with force $-\nabla V$. $\square$

This is the canonical bridge from the variational principle to the equations of motion. Every problem downstream that "derives the equations of motion" runs this same first-variation argument, then specialises the Lagrangian.

## Exercises [Intermediate]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).** *Euler-Lagrange for the shortest path.*

Let $L(y, y') = \sqrt{1 + (y')^2}$ on $\mathbb{R}^2$, so $S[y] = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + (y')^2}\,dx$ is arc length. Show that the Euler-Lagrange equation forces $y$ to be a straight line.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$L$ has no explicit $y$-dependence, so $\partial_y L = 0$. The Euler-Lagrange equation then says $\partial_{y'}L$ is constant in $x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $\partial_y L = 0$, the Euler-Lagrange equation $\frac{d}{dx}\partial_{y'}L - \partial_y L = 0$ reduces to $\frac{d}{dx}\partial_{y'}L = 0$, so $\partial_{y'}L$ is a constant $c$:

$$
\frac{y'}{\sqrt{1 + (y')^2}} = c.
$$

Solving for $y'$: $(y')^2 = c^2(1 + (y')^2)$, so $(y')^2(1 - c^2) = c^2$ and $y' = c/\sqrt{1 - c^2}$, a constant. Therefore $y(x) = mx + b$ is a straight line, with slope $m = c/\sqrt{1 - c^2}$.

This is the variational proof that straight lines minimise length in the plane — the prototype Euler-Lagrange problem (Arnold §12).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).** *Conserved energy when $L$ is time-independent.*

Suppose $L = L(q, v)$ has no explicit time dependence. Show that the energy $E = v\cdot\partial_v L - L$ is conserved along motions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate $E$ along a solution and use the Euler-Lagrange equation $\frac{d}{dt}\partial_v L = \partial_q L$ to cancel terms.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Compute $\frac{dE}{dt}$ along a motion, with $v = \dot q$:

$$
\frac{dE}{dt} = \dot v\cdot\partial_v L + v\cdot\frac{d}{dt}\partial_v L - \frac{dL}{dt}.
$$

By the chain rule (no explicit $t$), $\frac{dL}{dt} = \partial_q L\cdot\dot q + \partial_v L\cdot\dot v = \partial_q L\cdot v + \partial_v L\cdot\dot v$. Substituting and cancelling $\partial_v L\cdot\dot v$:

$$
\frac{dE}{dt} = v\cdot\frac{d}{dt}\partial_v L - \partial_q L\cdot v = v\cdot\left(\frac{d}{dt}\partial_v L - \partial_q L\right) = 0
$$

by the Euler-Lagrange equation. So $E$ is constant along motions. This is the energy integral; it is the Noether conserved quantity for time-translation symmetry (Arnold §16).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, numeric).** *Momentum conjugate to a cyclic coordinate.*

A particle moves in the plane under a central potential, with Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot r^2 + r^2\dot\varphi^2) - V(r)$ in polar coordinates. Compute the momentum $p_\varphi$ conjugate to $\varphi$ and show it is conserved.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\varphi$ is cyclic — it does not appear in $L$. The conjugate momentum $p_\varphi = \partial L/\partial\dot\varphi$ is then constant.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The conjugate momentum is

$$
p_\varphi = \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot\varphi} = m r^2\dot\varphi.
$$

Because $L$ does not depend on $\varphi$ explicitly ($\partial_\varphi L = 0$), the Euler-Lagrange equation for $\varphi$ gives $\frac{d}{dt}p_\varphi = \partial_\varphi L = 0$, so $p_\varphi = m r^2\dot\varphi$ is conserved.

This is the angular momentum, and its conservation is Kepler's second law: $r^2\dot\varphi = $ const means the radius vector sweeps equal areas in equal times (the areal velocity is $\tfrac{1}{2}r^2\dot\varphi$). Arnold §16, central-force example.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).** *Legendre transform of a hyper-regular Lagrangian.*

Let $L = \tfrac{1}{2}\,v^\top A\,v - V(q)$ on $T\mathbb{R}^n$, with $A$ a constant symmetric positive-definite matrix. Compute the Hamiltonian $H(q, p)$ via the Legendre transform $H = p\cdot v - L$ with $p = \partial_v L$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Invert $p = Av$ to get $v = A^{-1}p$, then substitute into $H = p\cdot v - L$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The fibre derivative is $p = \partial_v L = A v$. Since $A$ is positive-definite it is invertible, so $L$ is hyper-regular and $v = A^{-1}p$. Substitute into $H = p\cdot v - L$:

$$
H = p\cdot A^{-1}p - \left(\tfrac{1}{2}(A^{-1}p)^\top A (A^{-1}p) - V(q)\right).
$$

Using $A^\top = A$, the kinetic term is $\tfrac{1}{2}(A^{-1}p)^\top A (A^{-1}p) = \tfrac{1}{2}p^\top A^{-1}p$. Therefore

$$
H(q, p) = p^\top A^{-1}p - \tfrac{1}{2}p^\top A^{-1}p + V(q) = \tfrac{1}{2}\,p^\top A^{-1}p + V(q).
$$

The Legendre transform inverts the mass matrix: $\tfrac{1}{2}v^\top A v$ on the Lagrangian side becomes $\tfrac{1}{2}p^\top A^{-1}p$ on the Hamiltonian side, and $-V$ flips sign to $+V$. For $A = mI$ this is $H = |p|^2/(2m) + V$. Arnold §15 (Legendre transform), unit [05.00.03].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).** *Legendre transform is an involution.*

Let $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be smooth and strictly convex ($f'' > 0$). Its Legendre transform is $g(p) = \sup_v\,(pv - f(v))$. Show that the Legendre transform of $g$ is $f$ again.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The supremum is attained where $p = f'(v)$. Differentiate $g(p) = pv(p) - f(v(p))$ to find $g'(p) = v(p)$, then transform again.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For fixed $p$, the function $v\mapsto pv - f(v)$ has derivative $p - f'(v)$, vanishing where $p = f'(v)$. Strict convexity makes $f'$ strictly increasing, hence invertible; write $v = v(p)$ for the solution. The supremum is attained there (the second derivative is $-f'' < 0$), so $g(p) = p\,v(p) - f(v(p))$.

Differentiate, using $p = f'(v(p))$:

$$
g'(p) = v(p) + p\,v'(p) - f'(v(p))\,v'(p) = v(p) + \big(p - f'(v(p))\big)v'(p) = v(p).
$$

Now form the Legendre transform of $g$: $h(w) = \sup_p\,(wp - g(p))$, attained where $w = g'(p) = v(p)$, i.e. $p = f'(w)$. Then

$$
h(w) = w\,p - g(p) = w\,f'(w) - \big(f'(w)\,w - f(w)\big) = f(w).
$$

So $h = f$: the Legendre transform is an involution on strictly convex functions. This is why the Lagrangian-to-Hamiltonian and Hamiltonian-to-Lagrangian passages are mutually inverse (Arnold §14).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).** *Noether: rotational symmetry gives angular momentum.*

Let $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2 - V(|\mathbf{r}|)$ for $\mathbf{r}\in\mathbb{R}^3$, invariant under the rotation group $SO(3)$. Using Noether's theorem, show that angular momentum $\mathbf{L} = m\,\mathbf{r}\times\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ is conserved.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take the one-parameter rotation $g_s$ about a fixed axis $\mathbf{n}$. The Noether quantity is $I = \partial_v L\cdot\frac{d}{ds}g_s(\mathbf{r})\big|_{s=0}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $g_s$ be rotation by angle $s$ about the unit axis $\mathbf{n}$, so $\frac{d}{ds}g_s(\mathbf{r})\big|_{s=0} = \mathbf{n}\times\mathbf{r}$. Since $V$ depends only on $|\mathbf{r}|$ and rotations preserve length and speed, $L(g_s\mathbf{r}, g_s\dot{\mathbf{r}}) = L(\mathbf{r}, \dot{\mathbf{r}})$: the action is invariant.

Noether's theorem states that the quantity

$$
I = \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot{\mathbf{r}}}\cdot\frac{d}{ds}g_s(\mathbf{r})\Big|_{s=0}
$$

is conserved. Here $\partial_{\dot{\mathbf{r}}}L = m\dot{\mathbf{r}}$, so

$$
I = m\dot{\mathbf{r}}\cdot(\mathbf{n}\times\mathbf{r}) = \mathbf{n}\cdot(\mathbf{r}\times m\dot{\mathbf{r}}) = \mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{L},
$$

using the cyclic property of the scalar triple product. Since $\mathbf{n}$ is an arbitrary axis, every component $\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{L}$ is conserved, hence the full vector $\mathbf{L} = m\,\mathbf{r}\times\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ is conserved. Rotational symmetry yields angular-momentum conservation — Arnold §20, unit [05.00.04].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, numeric).** *Brachistochrone reduces to a cycloid.*

A bead slides without friction under gravity from $(0,0)$ to $(x_1, y_1)$ along a curve $y(x)$ (with $y$ measured downward). The descent time is $T[y] = \int_0^{x_1}\sqrt{\frac{1 + (y')^2}{2gy}}\,dx$. Show the minimising curve satisfies $y(1 + (y')^2) = $ const, the cycloid equation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The integrand $F = \sqrt{(1+(y')^2)/(2gy)}$ has no explicit $x$-dependence. Use the Beltrami identity $F - y'\,\partial_{y'}F = $ const.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Because $F$ does not depend explicitly on $x$, the Euler-Lagrange equation has a first integral, the Beltrami identity: $F - y'\,\partial_{y'}F = C$. Compute

$$
\partial_{y'}F = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2gy}}\cdot\frac{y'}{\sqrt{1 + (y')^2}}.
$$

Then

$$
F - y'\partial_{y'}F = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2gy}}\left(\sqrt{1 + (y')^2} - \frac{(y')^2}{\sqrt{1 + (y')^2}}\right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2gy}}\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + (y')^2}} = C.
$$

Squaring and rearranging: $\frac{1}{2gy\,(1 + (y')^2)} = C^2$, that is

$$
y\,(1 + (y')^2) = \frac{1}{2gC^2} =: k,
$$

a constant. This is the differential equation of a cycloid; parametrising $x = \tfrac{k}{2}(\theta - \sin\theta)$, $y = \tfrac{k}{2}(1 - \cos\theta)$ solves it. The brachistochrone is a cycloid — the classical Bernoulli problem, Arnold §12.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Noether's theorem in full, with a divergence term.*

Suppose a one-parameter family of maps $q\mapsto Q(q, s)$ with $Q(q, 0) = q$ changes the Lagrangian only by a total time derivative: $L(Q, \dot Q) = L(q, \dot q) + s\,\frac{dF}{dt} + O(s^2)$ for some function $F(q, t)$. Show that $I = \partial_v L\cdot\frac{\partial Q}{\partial s}\big|_{s=0} - F$ is conserved along motions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate the invariance relation in $s$ at $s = 0$, write the result as $\partial_q L\cdot\xi + \partial_v L\cdot\dot\xi = \frac{dF}{dt}$ with $\xi = \partial_s Q|_{s=0}$, and use the Euler-Lagrange equation to turn $\partial_q L$ into $\frac{d}{dt}\partial_v L$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\xi = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial s}\big|_{s=0}$, the infinitesimal generator. Differentiating the quasi-invariance relation $L(Q, \dot Q) = L + s\frac{dF}{dt} + O(s^2)$ at $s = 0$:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial q}\cdot\xi + \frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\cdot\dot\xi = \frac{dF}{dt},
$$

where $\dot\xi = \frac{d}{dt}\xi$. Along a motion, the Euler-Lagrange equation gives $\partial_q L = \frac{d}{dt}\partial_v L$. Substitute:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\right)\cdot\xi + \frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\cdot\dot\xi = \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\cdot\xi\right) = \frac{dF}{dt}.
$$

Therefore $\frac{d}{dt}\big(\partial_v L\cdot\xi - F\big) = 0$, so

$$
I = \frac{\partial L}{\partial v}\cdot\xi - F
$$

is conserved. The divergence term $F$ matters precisely for quasi-symmetries that change $L$ by a total derivative — the most important case being Galilean boosts, where $F = m\,\mathbf{v}_{\text{boost}}\cdot\mathbf{r}$ and the conserved quantity $I$ is the initial position of the centre of mass. When $F = 0$ this reduces to the strict-symmetry Noether theorem. Arnold §20; the boost case anchors [05.00.07].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).** *Geodesic flow as a Lagrangian system.*

On a Riemannian manifold $(M, g)$ take $L(q, v) = \tfrac{1}{2}\,g_{ij}(q)\,v^i v^j$. Show the Euler-Lagrange equations are the geodesic equations $\ddot q^k + \Gamma^k_{ij}\dot q^i\dot q^j = 0$, with $\Gamma^k_{ij}$ the Christoffel symbols of $g$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\partial_v L$, $\partial_q L$, and $\frac{d}{dt}\partial_v L$ in coordinates. Use the symmetry $g_{ij} = g_{ji}$ and the definition $\Gamma^k_{ij} = \tfrac{1}{2}g^{kl}(\partial_i g_{lj} + \partial_j g_{li} - \partial_l g_{ij})$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $L = \tfrac{1}{2}g_{ij}(q)v^i v^j$:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial v^k} = g_{kj}v^j, \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^k} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\partial_k g_{ij}\,v^i v^j.
$$

Differentiate the momentum in time along a curve ($v = \dot q$):

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial v^k} = g_{kj}\ddot q^j + \partial_i g_{kj}\,\dot q^i\dot q^j.
$$

The Euler-Lagrange equation $\frac{d}{dt}\partial_v L - \partial_q L = 0$ reads

$$
g_{kj}\ddot q^j + \partial_i g_{kj}\,\dot q^i\dot q^j - \tfrac{1}{2}\partial_k g_{ij}\,\dot q^i\dot q^j = 0.
$$

Symmetrise the middle term in $i\leftrightarrow j$ (legal since $\dot q^i\dot q^j$ is symmetric): $\partial_i g_{kj}\dot q^i\dot q^j = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_i g_{kj} + \partial_j g_{ki})\dot q^i\dot q^j$. Then

$$
g_{kj}\ddot q^j + \tfrac{1}{2}\big(\partial_i g_{kj} + \partial_j g_{ki} - \partial_k g_{ij}\big)\dot q^i\dot q^j = 0.
$$

Contract with the inverse metric $g^{lk}$ and use $g^{lk}g_{kj} = \delta^l_j$:

$$
\ddot q^l + \tfrac{1}{2}g^{lk}\big(\partial_i g_{kj} + \partial_j g_{ki} - \partial_k g_{ij}\big)\dot q^i\dot q^j = \ddot q^l + \Gamma^l_{ij}\dot q^i\dot q^j = 0.
$$

These are the geodesic equations. So free motion on $(M, g)$ — the Lagrangian with kinetic energy alone — traces geodesics; this is the Lagrangian shadow of the geodesic Hamiltonian flow [05.02.06]. Arnold §17.

</details>

</aside>
