## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

A symplectic manifold $(M^{2n}, \omega)$ carries a closed nondegenerate 2-form. Its distinguished submanifolds are the Lagrangians — maximal isotropics of dimension $n$, on which $\omega$ restricts to zero. A Hamiltonian action of a Lie group $G$ has a moment map $\mu : M \to \mathfrak{g}^*$ packaging the conserved quantities, the geometric form of Noether's theorem. The Liouville-Arnold theorem closes the circle: if $n$ functions Poisson-commute and are independent on a compact connected level set, that level set is an $n$-torus, and a neighbourhood carries action-angle coordinates $(I, \theta)$ in which the flow is linear. Arnold's Part III appendices treat these as a single package — symplectic structure, moment map, integrability.

This pack collects nine exercises — two easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units [05.01.02], [05.02.03], [05.02.04], [05.04.01], and [05.05.01], not as a standalone development. The problems are grouped by topic: checking symplectic forms and Lagrangian submanifolds (easy/medium), constructing moment maps (medium), and applying Liouville-Arnold to produce action-angle variables (hard).

The conventions throughout are Arnold's and Cannas's: $(M, \omega)$ symplectic with $\omega^n \neq 0$; a Lagrangian $L$ satisfies $\dim L = n$ and $\omega|_L = 0$; the moment map obeys $\iota_{X_\xi}\omega = d\langle\mu, \xi\rangle$ for each $\xi\in\mathfrak{g}$; action variables are $I_j = \tfrac{1}{2\pi}\oint_{\gamma_j}\theta$ over the basis cycles $\gamma_j$ of the Liouville torus, with $\theta = p\,dq$.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining eight follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *State and prove the torus half of the Liouville-Arnold theorem: a compact connected regular level set of $n$ independent Poisson-commuting integrals on $(M^{2n}, \omega)$ is diffeomorphic to the $n$-torus $T^n$.*

**Solution.** Let $f_1, \ldots, f_n : M \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfy $\{f_i, f_j\} = 0$ for all $i, j$, with $df_1, \ldots, df_n$ linearly independent on the level set $M_c = \{f = c\}$, which we assume compact and connected. Set $X_i = X_{f_i}$, the Hamiltonian vector fields.

*The $X_i$ are tangent to $M_c$.* Since $X_i(f_j) = \{f_j, f_i\} = 0$, each $X_i$ preserves every $f_j$, so it is tangent to $M_c$.

*The $X_i$ commute.* The Lie bracket of Hamiltonian fields is $[X_i, X_j] = -X_{\{f_i, f_j\}} = X_0 = 0$ because $\{f_i, f_j\} = 0$. So the flows commute.

*The $X_i$ are independent and span $TM_c$.* Nondegeneracy of $\omega$ and independence of the $df_i$ force $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ to be pointwise linearly independent; since $\dim M_c = 2n - n = n$, they frame $TM_c$.

So $M_c$ carries $n$ commuting, complete (by compactness), pointwise-independent vector fields. Their joint flow defines an action of $\mathbb{R}^n$ on $M_c$:

$$
\Phi : \mathbb{R}^n \times M_c \to M_c, \qquad \Phi(t, x) = g^{t_1}_{X_1}\cdots g^{t_n}_{X_n}(x).
$$

This action is transitive (the orbit of any point is open and closed in connected $M_c$) and locally free. The stabiliser of a point is a discrete subgroup — a lattice $\Lambda \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ — and is the same lattice everywhere by transitivity. Compactness forces $\Lambda$ to have full rank $n$. Therefore

$$
M_c \cong \mathbb{R}^n / \Lambda \cong T^n,
$$

the $n$-torus. $\square$

This is Arnold's signature theorem: integrability forces the regular compact level sets to be tori, foliating phase space, with the flow becoming linear (quasi-periodic) on each torus. The action-angle coordinates of the next exercises are the global completion of this picture.

## Exercises [Intermediate]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).** *The standard form on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ is symplectic.*

Verify that $\omega = \sum_{i=1}^n dp_i\wedge dq_i$ on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ is closed and nondegenerate.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Closedness: $\omega$ has constant coefficients. Nondegeneracy: compute $\omega^n$ and check it is a nonzero multiple of the volume form.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Closed.* $\omega = \sum_i dp_i\wedge dq_i$ has constant coefficients, so $d\omega = \sum_i d(dp_i\wedge dq_i) = 0$ since $d(dp_i) = d(dq_i) = 0$.

*Nondegenerate.* Compute the top power. Because the $dp_i\wedge dq_i$ for distinct $i$ commute as 2-forms and each squares to zero,

$$
\omega^n = \left(\sum_i dp_i\wedge dq_i\right)^n = n!\;dp_1\wedge dq_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dp_n\wedge dq_n,
$$

which is $n!$ times the standard volume form, hence nowhere zero. A 2-form with $\omega^n \neq 0$ is nondegenerate. So $(\mathbb{R}^{2n}, \omega)$ is symplectic — the Darboux model every symplectic manifold looks like locally. Unit [05.01.02].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).** *The graph of $df$ is Lagrangian.*

Let $f : M \to \mathbb{R}$ be smooth and $T^*M$ its cotangent bundle with $\omega = -d\theta$. Show the graph $\{(q, df_q) : q\in M\}$ is a Lagrangian submanifold of $T^*M$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The graph is the image of the section $s = df : M \to T^*M$. Pull back $\theta$ by this section: $s^*\theta = df$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The graph $\Gamma_{df}$ is the image of the 1-form $df$ viewed as a section $s : M \to T^*M$, $s(q) = (q, df_q)$. Its dimension is $\dim M = n$, half of $\dim T^*M = 2n$ — the right dimension for a Lagrangian.

The tautological 1-form $\theta$ has the reproducing property $s^*\theta = \alpha$ for any 1-form $\alpha = s$. Here $s = df$, so $s^*\theta = df$. Therefore

$$
s^*\omega = s^*(-d\theta) = -d(s^*\theta) = -d(df) = -d^2 f = 0.
$$

So $\omega$ restricts to zero on $\Gamma_{df}$, and with the half-dimension count this makes $\Gamma_{df}$ Lagrangian. (More generally, the graph of a closed 1-form is Lagrangian, and a section is Lagrangian iff the 1-form is closed.) Unit [05.05.01].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).** *Moment map for the rotation $S^1$-action on $\mathbb{R}^2$.*

Let $S^1$ act on $(\mathbb{R}^2, \omega = dp\wedge dq)$ by rotation. Find a moment map $\mu : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R} \cong \mathfrak{u}(1)^*$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The generating vector field of rotation is $X = q\,\partial_p - p\,\partial_q$. Solve $\iota_X\omega = d\mu$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The $S^1$-rotation by angle $\theta$ generates the vector field $X = -p\,\partial_q + q\,\partial_p$ (infinitesimal rotation). Contract with $\omega = dp\wedge dq$:

$$
\iota_X\omega = \iota_X(dp\wedge dq) = (\iota_X dp)\,dq - (\iota_X dq)\,dp = q\,dq - (-p)\,dp = q\,dq + p\,dp.
$$

We need $\mu$ with $\iota_X\omega = d\mu$:

$$
d\mu = q\,dq + p\,dp = d\!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}(q^2 + p^2)\right).
$$

So $\mu = \tfrac{1}{2}(p^2 + q^2)$ is a moment map (up to an additive constant). Geometrically it is the harmonic-oscillator Hamiltonian — the conserved quantity (here the energy / the radial action) for rotational symmetry, exactly the Noether quantity the moment map packages. Arnold Appendix 5; unit [05.04.01].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *Equivariance of the moment map for a $G$-action.*

For a Hamiltonian action of $G$ with moment map $\mu : M \to \mathfrak{g}^*$, suppose $\mu$ is $G$-equivariant for the coadjoint action. Show that the components $\mu^\xi := \langle\mu, \xi\rangle$ satisfy $\{\mu^\xi, \mu^\eta\} = \mu^{[\xi, \eta]}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate the equivariance relation, or compute $\{\mu^\xi, \mu^\eta\} = \omega(X_\xi, X_\eta)$ and use that the assignment $\xi\mapsto X_\xi$ is a Lie-algebra antihomomorphism (or homomorphism, per convention).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By definition of the moment map, $X_\xi = X_{\mu^\xi}$ is the Hamiltonian vector field of $\mu^\xi$. The Poisson bracket of two Hamiltonians equals $\omega$ applied to their fields:

$$
\{\mu^\xi, \mu^\eta\} = \omega(X_{\mu^\xi}, X_{\mu^\eta}) = \omega(X_\xi, X_\eta).
$$

The infinitesimal action is a Lie-algebra homomorphism $\xi\mapsto X_\xi$ with $[X_\xi, X_\eta] = X_{[\xi, \eta]}$. The bracket of Hamiltonian fields satisfies $[X_f, X_g] = X_{\{f, g\}}$, so comparing,

$$
X_{\{\mu^\xi, \mu^\eta\}} = [X_{\mu^\xi}, X_{\mu^\eta}] = [X_\xi, X_\eta] = X_{[\xi, \eta]} = X_{\mu^{[\xi, \eta]}}.
$$

Two Hamiltonians with the same Hamiltonian vector field differ by a locally constant function; equivariance pins this constant to zero, giving

$$
\{\mu^\xi, \mu^\eta\} = \mu^{[\xi, \eta]}.
$$

So the moment map is a Poisson map intertwining the Lie bracket on $\mathfrak{g}$ with the Poisson bracket on $M$ — the bracket-preserving property that makes equivariant moment maps the geometric incarnation of Noether's theorem. Arnold Appendix 5; unit [05.04.01].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).** *Action variable as enclosed area for the oscillator.*

For $H = \tfrac{1}{2}(p^2 + \omega_0^2 q^2)$, compute the action variable $I = \tfrac{1}{2\pi}\oint p\,dq$ on the energy level $H = E$ and confirm $H = \omega_0 I$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The level set is an ellipse. The loop integral $\oint p\,dq$ equals its enclosed area.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The level set $H = E$ is the ellipse $\tfrac{1}{2}p^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\omega_0^2 q^2 = E$, i.e. $\frac{p^2}{2E} + \frac{q^2}{2E/\omega_0^2} = 1$, with semi-axes $a = \sqrt{2E}$ (in $p$) and $b = \sqrt{2E}/\omega_0$ (in $q$). By Stokes/Green's theorem, $\oint p\,dq$ equals the enclosed area:

$$
\oint p\,dq = \pi\,a\,b = \pi\cdot\sqrt{2E}\cdot\frac{\sqrt{2E}}{\omega_0} = \frac{2\pi E}{\omega_0}.
$$

Therefore

$$
I = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint p\,dq = \frac{1}{2\pi}\cdot\frac{2\pi E}{\omega_0} = \frac{E}{\omega_0}.
$$

Inverting, $E = \omega_0 I$, so $H = \omega_0 I$ in action variables. The angular frequency is $\partial H/\partial I = \omega_0$, independent of amplitude — the isochronism of the harmonic oscillator. This is the simplest action-angle computation, Arnold §50; unit [05.02.04].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).** *Poisson-commuting integrals for a separable system.*

For $H = \tfrac{1}{2}(p_1^2 + p_2^2) + V_1(q_1) + V_2(q_2)$ on $\mathbb{R}^4$, exhibit two independent Poisson-commuting integrals and conclude the system is Liouville-integrable.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The energy in each separate degree of freedom is conserved. Check the bracket of $f_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}p_1^2 + V_1(q_1)$ and $f_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}p_2^2 + V_2(q_2)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take

$$
f_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}p_1^2 + V_1(q_1), \qquad f_2 = \tfrac{1}{2}p_2^2 + V_2(q_2),
$$

so $H = f_1 + f_2$. Each $f_i$ depends only on the $i$-th pair $(q_i, p_i)$. Their Poisson bracket in the four-dimensional phase space is

$$
\{f_1, f_2\} = \sum_{i=1}^2\left(\partial_{q_i}f_1\,\partial_{p_i}f_2 - \partial_{p_i}f_1\,\partial_{q_i}f_2\right) = 0,
$$

because $f_1$ has no $(q_2, p_2)$-dependence and $f_2$ has no $(q_1, p_1)$-dependence: every term contains a factor that vanishes. They are independent wherever $p_1, p_2$ are not both forced equal by the level constraints (generically $df_1, df_2$ are linearly independent). With $n = 2$ Poisson-commuting independent integrals on a $4$-dimensional symplectic manifold, the system is Liouville-integrable, and on compact connected level sets the Liouville-Arnold theorem gives $T^2$ tori with action-angle coordinates. Units [05.02.03], [05.02.04].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).** *Liouville tori are Lagrangian.*

In the setting of the Liouville-Arnold theorem, show that each regular level set $M_c = \{f_1 = c_1, \ldots, f_n = c_n\}$ is a Lagrangian submanifold of $(M^{2n}, \omega)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$M_c$ has dimension $n$. It remains to show $\omega|_{M_c} = 0$. The tangent space is spanned by the Hamiltonian fields $X_{f_i}$; compute $\omega(X_{f_i}, X_{f_j})$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The level set $M_c$ is cut out by $n$ functions with independent differentials, so $\dim M_c = 2n - n = n$ — half-dimensional, the dimension count for a Lagrangian.

As shown in the lead exercise, the Hamiltonian vector fields $X_{f_1}, \ldots, X_{f_n}$ are tangent to $M_c$ and pointwise independent, so they frame the tangent space $T_x M_c$ at each point. Evaluate $\omega$ on this frame:

$$
\omega(X_{f_i}, X_{f_j}) = \{f_i, f_j\} = 0
$$

for all $i, j$, by the defining Poisson-commutativity. Since the $X_{f_i}$ span $T_x M_c$, this says $\omega|_{T_x M_c} = 0$: the form restricts to zero on the level set.

A half-dimensional submanifold on which $\omega$ vanishes is Lagrangian. So each compact regular Liouville torus is a Lagrangian $T^n \subset M$. The invariant tori of an integrable system foliate phase space by Lagrangians — this is the geometric content tying [05.05.01], [05.02.03], and [05.02.04] together, and the configuration KAM perturbs. Arnold §49.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).** *Action-angle variables linearise the flow.*

Assume action-angle coordinates $(I, \theta)$ exist on a Liouville torus with $\omega = \sum_j dI_j\wedge d\theta_j$ and $H = H(I)$ alone. Solve Hamilton's equations and show the motion is quasi-periodic on the torus.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write Hamilton's equations for $H = H(I)$ in the $(I, \theta)$ chart. Since $H$ is independent of $\theta$, the actions are constant.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In action-angle coordinates with $\omega = \sum_j dI_j\wedge d\theta_j$, Hamilton's equations are

$$
\dot I_j = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial\theta_j}, \qquad \dot\theta_j = \frac{\partial H}{\partial I_j}.
$$

Because $H = H(I)$ does not depend on the angles, $\partial H/\partial\theta_j = 0$, so

$$
\dot I_j = 0 \;\Rightarrow\; I_j = \text{const}, \qquad \dot\theta_j = \frac{\partial H}{\partial I_j}(I) =: \nu_j(I) = \text{const}.
$$

The frequencies $\nu_j$ are constant because they depend only on the (constant) actions. Integrating,

$$
I_j(t) = I_j(0), \qquad \theta_j(t) = \theta_j(0) + \nu_j\,t \pmod{2\pi}.
$$

The motion stays on the torus $\{I = \text{const}\}$ and winds around it at constant angular frequencies $\nu_j(I)$ — quasi-periodic (conditionally periodic) motion. It is periodic when the $\nu_j$ are commensurate and densely fills the torus when they are rationally independent. This linear-flow-on-a-torus is the canonical form of every integrable system near a regular compact level set, and the unperturbed picture KAM theory studies [05.09.01]. Arnold §50; unit [05.02.04].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *Darboux coordinates exist locally — uniqueness of the local model.*

Argue that every point of a symplectic manifold $(M^{2n}, \omega)$ has a chart in which $\omega = \sum_i dp_i\wedge dq_i$, so all symplectic manifolds of a given dimension are locally indistinguishable. Sketch the Moser-trick proof.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Linear algebra gives a basis at one point where $\omega$ is standard; the Moser trick (Moser's homotopy method) upgrades this to a neighbourhood by absorbing the difference $\omega_t = \omega_0 + t(\omega_1 - \omega_0)$ into a flow.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Pointwise normal form.* By the linear theory, the antisymmetric nondegenerate form $\omega_x$ on $T_x M$ admits a symplectic basis, so in suitable linear coordinates $\omega$ agrees with the standard form $\omega_0 = \sum_i dp_i\wedge dq_i$ at the single point $x$.

*Moser trick to a neighbourhood.* On a ball around $x$, interpolate $\omega_t = \omega_0 + t(\omega - \omega_0)$ for $t\in[0,1]$. Each $\omega_t$ is closed, and (shrinking the ball) nondegenerate since the family starts and stays near $\omega_0$. By the Poincaré lemma the closed form $\omega - \omega_0$, which vanishes at $x$, is exact: $\omega - \omega_0 = d\sigma$ with $\sigma$ vanishing to first order at $x$. Define the time-dependent vector field $X_t$ by $\iota_{X_t}\omega_t = -\sigma$ (solvable by nondegeneracy). Its flow $\psi_t$ satisfies, via Cartan's formula,

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\psi_t^*\omega_t = \psi_t^*\big(\mathcal{L}_{X_t}\omega_t + \tfrac{d}{dt}\omega_t\big) = \psi_t^*\big(d\iota_{X_t}\omega_t + (\omega - \omega_0)\big) = \psi_t^*\big(-d\sigma + d\sigma\big) = 0.
$$

So $\psi_1^*\omega = \psi_1^*\omega_1 = \psi_0^*\omega_0 = \omega_0$. The diffeomorphism $\psi_1$ is the Darboux chart: it pulls $\omega$ back to the standard form. Hence symplectic manifolds have no local invariants beyond dimension — the contrast with Riemannian geometry, where curvature obstructs local flatness. Units [05.01.02], and the Moser trick of [05.01.05].

</details>

</aside>
