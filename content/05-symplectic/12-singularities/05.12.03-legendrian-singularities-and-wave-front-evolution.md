## Intuition [Beginner]

A wavefront is the moving silhouette of a disturbance spreading through space. Think of the bright crescent on the bottom of a swimming pool when sunlight crosses a rippled surface, or the leading edge of a shock wave radiating out from a stone dropped in water. The full physical content of the wave is encoded in a higher-dimensional object that remembers position and direction at once; the wavefront is its shadow on space.

Most of the time this shadow is a smooth surface. But at special points it folds onto itself, develops a sharp cusp, or pinches into a corner. These singular points are not failures of the picture. They are forced — generic wavefronts must develop a small list of standard singularities, and the list is universal across optics, acoustics, and Hamiltonian mechanics.

Arnold worked out the list in the 1970s. In codimension up to three the only stable shapes are cusps and swallowtails, with two umbilical patterns thrown in. This unit explains why the list looks the way it does and how a wavefront evolves through its singularities under Hamiltonian flow.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the line of positions to be the horizontal axis with coordinate $q$, and the vertical axis to be the wavefront height $z$. Build the simplest cuspidal Legendrian as a parametrised curve

$$
t \mapsto (q, p, z) = (t^2,\ t,\ \tfrac{2}{3} t^3)
$$

inside the three-dimensional $1$-jet space with coordinates $(q, p, z)$. Drop the slope coordinate $p$ to project onto the $(q, z)$ plane. The image traces out the curve $z = \tfrac{2}{3} t^3$ with $q = t^2$, which can be rewritten by eliminating $t$ as $z^2 = \tfrac{4}{9} q^3$, or in cleaner form $27 z^2 = 4 q^3$ after rescaling.

At $t = 0$ the projection lands at the origin and the moving point reverses direction in $q$ — the curve doubles back on itself, producing a sharp inward-pointing cusp pointed along the positive $q$ axis. To the right of the origin the front consists of two smooth sheets; to the left there is no front at all.

What this tells us: the cusp is the universal signature of an $A_2$ singularity, and the front cannot be smoothed away by any small perturbation. It is the simplest stable feature of a propagating wavefront.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $(N^{2n+1}, \alpha)$ be a contact manifold in the sense of [05.10.01]: a smooth manifold of dimension $2n + 1$ carrying a $1$-form $\alpha$ with $\alpha \wedge (d\alpha)^n \neq 0$ pointwise. The contact distribution $\xi = \ker\alpha$ is a hyperplane field of rank $2n$ on which $d\alpha$ restricts to a symplectic form. A submanifold $\Lambda \subset N$ is **Legendrian** when $\dim \Lambda = n$ and $\alpha|_\Lambda = 0$. The dimension $n$ is the maximal integer compatible with $\alpha|_\Lambda = 0$, by non-integrability of $\xi$.

The model contact manifold is the **1-jet bundle** $J^1(M, \mathbb{R}) = T^*M \times \mathbb{R}$ of a smooth base $M^n$, with coordinates $(q, p, z)$ and contact form $\alpha = dz - p \, dq$ (writing $p \, dq = \sum_{i=1}^n p_i \, dq_i$). The contact condition is immediate: $d\alpha = -dp \wedge dq$, so $\alpha \wedge (d\alpha)^n = dz \wedge (-dp \wedge dq)^n \neq 0$. Two natural projections sit alongside:

$$
\pi_{LP} : J^1(M, \mathbb{R}) \to T^*M, \qquad (q, p, z) \mapsto (q, p),
$$

the **Lagrangian projection**, and

$$
\pi_F : J^1(M, \mathbb{R}) \to M \times \mathbb{R}, \qquad (q, p, z) \mapsto (q, z),
$$

the **front projection**. The image $W(\Lambda) = \pi_F(\Lambda) \subset M \times \mathbb{R}$ is the **wave front** of the Legendrian $\Lambda$.

**Generating-function description.** Given a smooth function $F : M \times \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}$, the set
$$
\Lambda_F = \{ (q, \partial_q F, F)\ :\ \partial_u F(q, u) = 0 \} \subset J^1(M, \mathbb{R})
$$
is a Legendrian submanifold of $J^1(M, \mathbb{R})$ provided $F$ is **non-degenerate** in the sense that $0$ is a regular value of $\partial_u F : M \times \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}^k$. Every germ of a Legendrian in $J^1(M)$ arises locally from such a generating family; the parameter dimension $k$ may be reduced to zero (a true function $F : M \to \mathbb{R}$) iff the Lagrangian projection $\pi_{LP}|_\Lambda$ is a local diffeomorphism. The front $W(\Lambda_F)$ is the **discriminant** of $F$ along $u$:
$$
W(\Lambda_F) = \{ (q, z) \in M \times \mathbb{R}\ :\ \exists u,\ \partial_u F(q, u) = 0,\ F(q, u) = z \}.
$$
This realises wavefronts as discriminants of families of functions — the bridge to singularity theory.

**Equivalence of Legendrians.** Two Legendrians $\Lambda, \Lambda' \subset (N, \alpha)$ are **contactomorphic** if there exists a contactomorphism $\Phi : N \to N$ (a diffeomorphism with $\Phi^* \alpha = \lambda \alpha$ for some nowhere-zero function $\lambda$) mapping one to the other. Two generating families $F$ and $F'$ define contactomorphic Legendrians iff they are equivalent under the **stable right-equivalence** group: $F'(q, u) = F(\psi(q, u)) + Q(u')$ for some diffeomorphism $\psi$ and some nondegenerate quadratic form $Q$ in extra parameters $u'$. Singularity theory of front projections is the right-equivalence theory of generating families [ref: TODO_REF Arnold-Givental EMS Vol 4 Ch 5].

The sign convention follows [05.12.01]: $\omega_0 = \sum dq_i \wedge dp_i$ on $T^*M$ and $\alpha = dz - p \, dq$ on $J^1(M)$, so $-d\alpha = dp \wedge dq = -\omega_0$ on $\ker dz$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Arnold's ADE classification of stable Legendrian-front singularities).** Let $\Lambda \subset J^1(M, \mathbb{R})$ be a generic Legendrian germ. Up to contactomorphism, every stable singularity of the front projection $\pi_F|_\Lambda$ at codimension $\leq 3$ in $\dim M = n$ is right-equivalent to one of the following generating-family normal forms:

| Type | Generating family $F(q, u)$ | Front singularity |
|---|---|---|
| $A_2$ | $u^3 + q_1 u$ | semicubic cusp |
| $A_3$ | $u^4 + q_1 u^2 + q_2 u$ | swallowtail |
| $A_4$ | $u^5 + q_1 u^3 + q_2 u^2 + q_3 u$ | butterfly |
| $D_4^+$ | $u_1^2 u_2 - u_2^3 + q_1 u_1^2 + q_2 u_1 + q_3 u_2$ | hyperbolic umbilic (purse) |
| $D_4^-$ | $u_1^2 u_2 + u_2^3 + q_1 u_1^2 + q_2 u_1 + q_3 u_2$ | elliptic umbilic (pyramid) |

In particular, in codimension exactly $1$ the only stable Legendrian-front singularity is $A_2$, the semicubic cusp.

**Proof sketch.** The reduction proceeds in three steps: (i) Legendrian germs in $J^1(M)$ are classified by germs of generating families $F : M \times \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}$ modulo stable right-equivalence; (ii) stable right-equivalence of function germs is the equivalence classified by Arnold's normal-form theorem; (iii) the front projection $\pi_F$ corresponds to the discriminant map of the family, so the wave-front singularities are exactly the discriminant singularities of the generating family.

(i) follows from the local Legendrian-equivalence theorem of Arnold-Hörmander: any Legendrian germ in $J^1(M)$ at a point where $\pi_{LP}|_\Lambda$ has rank deficit $k$ has a generating family with exactly $k$ extra parameters $u$, and Legendrian germs are contactomorphic iff their generating families are stably right-equivalent.

(ii) Arnold's normal-form theorem [ref: TODO_REF Arnold-Goryunov-Lyashko-Vasiliev] states that simple function germs $f : (\mathbb{R}^k, 0) \to (\mathbb{R}, 0)$ — those with finitely many right-equivalence classes in any neighbourhood — fall into the ADE list $A_k$ (one variable, $f = u^{k+1}$), $D_k$ (two variables, $f = u_1^2 u_2 \pm u_2^{k-1}$), $E_6, E_7, E_8$. The codimension of a class in the space of germs equals the Milnor number $\mu(f) = \dim_{\mathbb{R}} \mathbb{R}[[u]] / (\partial f / \partial u)$ minus one, plus the dimension of the moduli of $\mathbb{R}$-versal unfoldings minus their parameter count. In codimension at most $3$ the survivors are $A_2, A_3, A_4, D_4^\pm$.

(iii) The discriminant of an $\mathbb{R}$-versal unfolding $F(q, u) = f(u) + q_1 \phi_1(u) + \cdots + q_n \phi_n(u)$ of a simple germ $f$, where $\{\phi_i\}$ projects to a basis of the local algebra $\mathbb{R}[[u]] / (\partial f / \partial u)$, is the locus
$$
\Delta(F) = \{ q \in \mathbb{R}^n\ :\ \exists u_0 \text{ with } \partial_u F(q, u_0) = 0 \text{ and } \det \partial^2_{uu} F(q, u_0) = 0 \}.
$$
The semicubic cusp profile $4 p^3 - 27 q^2 = 0$ is the discriminant of $F(q, u) = u^3 + qu$; the swallowtail is the discriminant of the quartic versal family. This computation is direct.

Combining the three steps: a generic Legendrian-front singularity in codimension $\leq 3$ is contactomorphic to the discriminant of one of the five simple-singularity versal families above, and these are pairwise distinct because they are pairwise distinct as discriminants in $\mathbb{R}^n$. $\square$

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

The Legendrian curve $\Lambda \subset J^1(\mathbb{R})$ is parametrised by $t \mapsto (q, p, z) = (t^2, t, \tfrac{2}{3} t^3)$. Verify that $\alpha|_\Lambda = 0$ for $\alpha = dz - p \, dq$, and write down the front $W(\Lambda) \subset \mathbb{R}^2_{q,z}$ as an implicit equation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $dz - p \, dq$ along the curve; eliminate the parameter $t$ between $q = t^2$ and $z = (2/3) t^3$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Along the curve $\dot{q} = 2t$, $\dot{z} = 2 t^2$, and $p = t$, so $\dot{z} - p \dot{q} = 2 t^2 - t \cdot 2 t = 0$. Hence $\alpha|_\Lambda = 0$ and $\Lambda$ is Legendrian. From $q = t^2$ and $z = \tfrac{2}{3} t^3$ we obtain $t = z / (\tfrac{2}{3} t^2) = (3z) / (2 q)$ when $q > 0$, and substituting $t^2 = q$ gives $z^2 = (4/9) q^3$, i.e. $9 z^2 = 4 q^3$. Multiplying through by $3$ to match Arnold's standard form: $27 z^2 = 12 q^3$, equivalently the semicubic cusp profile.

Rubric: full credit for verifying $\alpha|_\Lambda = 0$ and producing the implicit equation $9 z^2 = 4 q^3$ (or any scalar-multiple equivalent).

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Explain why the singularities of the front projection $\pi_F : \Lambda \to M \times \mathbb{R}$ live at points where the Lagrangian projection $\pi_{LP} : \Lambda \to T^*M$ has rank deficit.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the fact that $J^1(M) = T^*M \times \mathbb{R}$ is fibered over both $T^*M$ and $M \times \mathbb{R}$; analyse the kernel of $d\pi_F|_\Lambda$ in terms of $d\pi_{LP}|_\Lambda$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

At a point $x \in \Lambda$ the tangent space $T_x \Lambda$ is $n$-dimensional and Legendrian. The front projection has differential $d\pi_F(v_q, v_p, v_z) = (v_q, v_z)$, so $\ker d\pi_F|_{T_x \Lambda} = \{v \in T_x \Lambda : v_q = 0, v_z = 0\}$. Since $\alpha|_\Lambda = 0$ gives $v_z = p \, v_q$ on $T_x \Lambda$, the condition $v_q = 0$ already forces $v_z = 0$; hence $\ker d\pi_F|_{T_x \Lambda} = \{v \in T_x \Lambda : v_q = 0\}$. The Lagrangian projection has differential $d\pi_{LP}(v_q, v_p, v_z) = (v_q, v_p)$, with kernel on $T_x \Lambda$ given by $\{v : v_q = 0, v_p = 0\}$. The two kernels agree on $T_x \Lambda$: if $v_q = 0$ then $\alpha|_\Lambda = 0$ gives no constraint on $v_p$, but a Legendrian $\Lambda$ is by construction not vertical in the $z$-direction, so $v_p = 0$ holds whenever $v_q = 0$ for any $v \in T_x\Lambda$ off the singular locus. Therefore $\pi_F$ has rank deficit exactly when $\pi_{LP}$ has rank deficit, both controlled by the dimension of $T_x\Lambda \cap \ker d\pi_{LP}$.

Rubric: full credit for identifying $\ker d\pi_F|_{T_x\Lambda}$ with the kernel of $\pi_{LP}|_\Lambda$ via the Legendrian condition; partial credit for stating the conclusion without the kernel computation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that the generating family $F(q, u) = u^4 + q_1 u^2 + q_2 u$ produces the swallowtail front in $\mathbb{R}^2_{q_1, q_2}$, and describe the swallowtail's qualitative features (number of sheets, self-intersection).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The discriminant is the locus where $\partial_u F = 4u^3 + 2 q_1 u + q_2 = 0$ has a double root in $u$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The critical-point equation $\partial_u F = 4u^3 + 2 q_1 u + q_2 = 0$ is a cubic in $u$ with discriminant (in $u$) equal to $-4 (2 q_1)^3 - 27 q_2^2 = -32 q_1^3 - 27 q_2^2$. The locus where the cubic has a repeated root is therefore $\{32 q_1^3 + 27 q_2^2 = 0\}$ — a semicubic cusp in the $(q_1, q_2)$ plane along the negative-$q_1$ axis. To get the swallowtail itself we plot the full discriminant in three dimensions $(q_1, q_2, z = F)$, parametrised by the parameter $u$. As $u$ varies, the front has three sheets in the region with three real critical points, meeting along a cusp curve where two sheets are tangent, with the cusp curves themselves meeting at a self-intersection point at the origin where two of the three real roots coalesce. The classical picture is a folded-over surface whose silhouette in the $(q_1, q_2)$ plane has both a cuspidal edge and a transverse self-intersection — exactly the shape of a swallow's tail.

Rubric: full credit for the cubic-discriminant computation and naming the cuspidal edge plus self-intersection; partial credit for either alone.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

State and justify the Maslov-index increment rule across a transverse $A_2$ crossing of the front projection. That is, if a path $\gamma : [0, 1] \to \Lambda$ in a Legendrian crosses the singular locus of $\pi_F|_\Lambda$ transversally at an $A_2$ point, by how much does the integer Maslov index (defined as the pull-back of the universal class $\mu$ along the Gauss map $\gamma \to \Lambda(n)$) jump?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use [05.12.01]: the Maslov index is the signed intersection number of $\gamma$ with the Maslov cycle $\Sigma_1$. At an $A_2$ crossing, the dimension of the kernel of $\pi_{LP}|_\Lambda$ jumps by one.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

At a transverse $A_2$ crossing the Lagrangian projection $\pi_{LP}|_\Lambda$ passes from a local diffeomorphism on one side of the crossing to having a one-dimensional kernel at the crossing point and back to a local diffeomorphism on the other side. Via the Gauss-type map $\Lambda \to \Lambda(n)$, this corresponds to the tangent Lagrangian $T_x \Lambda$ becoming momentarily non-transverse to the fixed reference vertical Lagrangian $L_0 = T_q^* M$ at the crossing point. The Maslov cycle $\Sigma$ relative to $L_0$ has codimension-one top stratum $\Sigma_1 = \{L : \dim(L \cap L_0) = 1\}$; the path $\gamma$ crosses $\Sigma_1$ transversally once. By the intersection-theoretic formula from [05.12.01] (Maslov cycle as Poincaré dual of $\mu$), the Maslov index increment equals the sign of the crossing form, which at a generic $A_2$ crossing is $\pm 1$. The sign is fixed by the orientation of the path relative to the co-orientation of $\Sigma_1$, and is determined by the sign of the leading coefficient in the generating family normal form $u^3 + q u$: positive (passing from one side to the other in the direction of increasing $q$) gives $+1$; the reverse traversal gives $-1$.

Rubric: full credit for naming the $\pm 1$ increment and grounding it in the intersection-number formula for the Maslov index; bonus for the sign-from-the-generating-family observation.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The lift of $X_H$ to $J^1(M)$ is the contact Hamiltonian vector field of the Hamiltonian $H(q, p) + 0$; transversality to the Maslov cycle is a generic codimension-one phenomenon.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A Hamiltonian vector field $X_H$ on $T^*M$ lifts to a contact vector field $\tilde X_H$ on $J^1(M)$ via $\tilde X_H = X_H + (p \cdot \partial_p H - H) \partial_z$, preserving the contact form $\alpha = dz - p \, dq$ up to a Lie-derivative term proportional to $\alpha$. The contact flow $\tilde \Phi_t$ preserves the Legendrian condition, so $\Phi_t \Lambda := \tilde\Phi_t(\Lambda)$ is a one-parameter family of Legendrians. The Gauss map $\Phi_t \Lambda \to \Lambda(n)$ is a one-parameter family of Lagrangian-valued maps; transversality to the codimension-one Maslov cycle $\Sigma_1 \subset \Lambda(n)$ is a generic condition by Thom transversality. At generic times $t$ the map misses $\Sigma_1$ entirely, the Lagrangian projection $\pi_{LP}|_{\Phi_t \Lambda}$ is a local diffeomorphism, and the front $W(\Phi_t \Lambda)$ is locally smooth. At isolated times $t_*$ the Gauss map hits $\Sigma_1$ transversally at an isolated point, the Lagrangian projection acquires a one-dimensional kernel there, and by the ADE classification (codimension $1$ stable case = $A_2$ only) the front develops a semicubic cusp at the corresponding base point. Generic flows produce isolated cusp instants in time, with each instant flanked by smooth-front intervals on either side.

## Advanced results [Master]

Fix a smooth base $M^n$ and the $1$-jet bundle $J^1(M, \mathbb{R}) = T^*M \times \mathbb{R}$ with contact form $\alpha = dz - p \, dq$. The front-projection map $\pi_F : J^1(M, \mathbb{R}) \to M \times \mathbb{R}$ is the canonical evaluation surjection; its restriction to a Legendrian $\Lambda$ has its critical locus controlled by the rank deficit of the Lagrangian projection $\pi_{LP} : J^1(M, \mathbb{R}) \to T^*M$.

**Theorem (Arnold's stability theorem for Legendrian singularities).** Let $\Lambda \subset J^1(M, \mathbb{R})$ be a Legendrian germ at a point $x_0$. The germ of the front $W(\Lambda) = \pi_F(\Lambda)$ at $\pi_F(x_0)$ is **stable** under small Legendrian perturbations of $\Lambda$ iff $\Lambda$ admits a generating family $F(q, u)$ at $x_0$ whose function germ $f(u) = F(q_0, u)$ at the corresponding parameter value is a simple right-equivalence-class representative — that is, $A_k$ for some $k \geq 1$, or $D_k$ for some $k \geq 4$, or $E_6, E_7, E_8$. The codimension of the stratum in the space of Legendrian germs equals the Tjurina number $\tau(f) = \dim_\mathbb{R} \mathbb{R}[[u]] / (f, \partial_u f)$ of the underlying germ [ref: TODO_REF Arnold-Givental EMS Vol 4 Ch 5].

In codimension $\leq 3$ the simple-germ list reduces to $A_2, A_3, A_4, D_4^\pm$ — the five entries of the Key-theorem table — and the front-singularity nomenclature (semicubic cusp, swallowtail, butterfly, hyperbolic and elliptic umbilic) is in standard usage across geometric optics and Hamiltonian mechanics.

**The Maslov index along front evolution.** Under a smooth one-parameter family of Legendrians $\Lambda_t = \tilde\Phi_t(\Lambda_0)$, $t \in [0, 1]$, generated by the lift of a Hamiltonian flow $X_H$, the integer Maslov index of a fixed loop $\gamma : S^1 \to \Lambda_0$ — pulled forward to a loop $\gamma_t : S^1 \to \Lambda_t$ — is constant in $t$ (homotopy invariance of the Maslov index, [05.12.01]). What changes is the **front intersection pattern**: the Lagrangian projection $\pi_{LP}|_{\Lambda_t}$ acquires and loses fold and cusp singularities as $t$ varies, and the family of fronts $W(\Lambda_t)$ traces out a Legendrian variety in $M \times \mathbb{R} \times [0, 1]$ whose own singular locus is again an ADE configuration in one higher codimension — the **bifurcation diagram** of the family.

For a generic homotopy of Legendrians passing through an isolated $A_2$ event at time $t_*$, the loop $\gamma_t$ picks up a $\pm 1$ contribution to its Maslov index at each transverse $A_2$ crossing in the $(t, t_*)$ unfolding. The signed sum is the Maslov index $\mathrm{Maslov}(\gamma_0) - \mathrm{Maslov}(\gamma_1) = 0$ when $\gamma_t$ is genuinely the homotopic image of a single loop — but the **front Maslov increment**, summing $\pm 1$ over $A_2$-cusp crossings of the front projection of a fixed Legendrian path $\gamma \times [0, 1]$, recovers the integer Maslov index of the path-with-endpoints by the Robbin-Salamon formula [ref: TODO_REF Arnold-Givental EMS Vol 4 Ch 5].

**Generating-family invariance and the parametric viewpoint.** Stable right-equivalence of generating families $F(q, u)$ and $F'(q, u')$ is generated by three moves: (i) right-action by a diffeomorphism $(q, u) \mapsto (q, \psi(q, u))$; (ii) addition of a function of $q$ alone, $F'(q, u) = F(q, u) + g(q)$; (iii) stabilisation, $F'(q, u, u') = F(q, u) + Q(u')$ for a nondegenerate quadratic form $Q$. Move (i) implements diffeomorphism reparametrisations of the parameter space; (ii) implements vertical translation of the front in the $z$-direction; (iii) is the stabilisation of the right-equivalence class to its minimal versal unfolding.

A Legendrian germ at a point with $k$-dimensional kernel of $\pi_{LP}$ has a generating family with exactly $k$ extra parameters $u$ (after stable reduction); the corresponding germ on the $u$-space is the unique-up-to-right-equivalence object whose ADE class names the singularity. The Milnor number $\mu(f)$ of the germ equals the dimension of the local algebra $\mathbb{R}[[u]] / (\partial_u f)$, and Arnold's series-classification organises simple germs by $\mu$: $\mu(A_k) = k$, $\mu(D_k) = k$, $\mu(E_k) = k$.

**Geometric optics and caustics.** In wave optics the wavefront at time $t$ of an instantaneous point source at $x_0 \in M$ is the front projection of the Legendrian
$$
\Lambda_t = \{ (q, p, z)\ :\ |p|_g = 1,\ z = t,\ q \in \mathrm{geodesic\ at\ distance\ } t \text{ from } x_0 \}
$$
in $J^1(M)$ with $g$ a Riemannian metric. The caustic of the family $\{\Lambda_t\}$ — the locus in $M$ where the wavefront has a singularity — is exactly the set of focal points of the geodesic flow from $x_0$, and its ADE-type at each focal point is controlled by the rank deficit of the exponential map. The classical caustic catalogue (Cayley 1857 reflection caustics; Tait 1865 catacaustics) reorganises as Arnold's ADE list, with the rainbow caustic in atmospheric optics being an $A_2$ cusp and the optical-disk caustic of a sphere a $D_4^+$ umbilic [ref: TODO_REF Arnold *Singularities of Caustics and Wave Fronts*].

**Hörmander-Maslov phase in oscillatory integrals.** The asymptotic expansion of an oscillatory integral
$$
I(\hbar) = \int e^{i S(q, u) / \hbar} a(q, u) \, du \qquad (\hbar \to 0^+)
$$
on the critical set $\{\partial_u S = 0\}$ picks up a phase factor $\exp(-i \pi \nu / 2)$ where $\nu$ is the **Maslov index** of the underlying Lagrangian, computed as the signed count of $A_2$ crossings of the projected Lagrangian along the integration contour. This is the Hörmander 1971 generalisation of the WKB ansatz to Fourier integral operators; the integer correction encodes precisely the topological data of the Legendrian-front singularities the contour traverses [ref: TODO_REF Hörmander 1971].

**Wall-crossing and Donaldson-Thomas theory.** In Kontsevich-Soibelman's 2008 framework for refined DT invariants, the BPS spectrum of a Calabi-Yau category jumps across walls in stability-condition space whose local geometry is modelled on the Legendrian-singularity ADE list: an $A_2$ wall produces a primitive wall-crossing formula linking adjacent chambers by a conjugation in a torus, and the same five-singularity codimension-$\leq 3$ list of Arnold reappears as the codimension-$\leq 3$ wall types in the stability manifold. This is the most recent instance of Arnold's ADE classification surfacing in apparently unrelated mathematics.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition.** Let $F : M \times \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}$ be smooth with $0$ a regular value of $\partial_u F$. The set $\Lambda_F = \{(q, \partial_q F(q, u), F(q, u)) : \partial_u F(q, u) = 0\}$ is a smooth $n$-dimensional Legendrian submanifold of $J^1(M, \mathbb{R})$.

Smoothness: the regular-value hypothesis means the constraint $\partial_u F = 0$ cuts out a smooth $n$-dimensional submanifold $\Sigma \subset M \times \mathbb{R}^k$. The map $\Sigma \to J^1(M, \mathbb{R})$, $(q, u) \mapsto (q, \partial_q F(q, u), F(q, u))$, has injective differential at every point (the $q$-component projects to a chart on $M$, and the $z$-component is determined by $F$), so its image $\Lambda_F$ is a smooth $n$-dimensional embedded submanifold.

Legendrian: on $\Lambda_F$, parametrised by $(q, u) \in \Sigma$, the canonical $1$-form $\alpha = dz - p \, dq$ pulls back to
$$
dF(q, u) - \partial_q F(q, u) \, dq = \partial_q F \, dq + \partial_u F \, du - \partial_q F \, dq = \partial_u F \, du.
$$
On $\Sigma = \{\partial_u F = 0\}$, this vanishes identically. Hence $\alpha|_{\Lambda_F} = 0$ and $\Lambda_F$ is Legendrian. $\square$

**Proposition (front of $A_2$).** For $F(q, u) = u^3 + q u$ the front $W(\Lambda_F) \subset \mathbb{R}^2_{q, z}$ is the semicubic cusp $\{27 z^2 + 4 q^3 = 0\}$.

The critical equation is $\partial_u F = 3 u^2 + q = 0$, so $q = -3 u^2$. On the critical set, $z = F(q, u) = u^3 + q u = u^3 - 3 u^3 = -2 u^3$. From $q = -3 u^2$ we get $u^2 = -q / 3$, so $u^6 = -q^3 / 27$; from $z = -2 u^3$ we get $z^2 = 4 u^6 = -4 q^3 / 27$. Rearranging: $27 z^2 + 4 q^3 = 0$.

The curve is parametrised by $u \in \mathbb{R}$ as $(q, z) = (-3 u^2, -2 u^3)$, tracing the standard semicubic cusp $z^2 = -4 q^3 / 27$ for $q \leq 0$. The point $u = 0$ maps to the origin, where $dq/du = -6 u = 0$ and $dz/du = -6 u^2 = 0$ both vanish to first order — the cusp singularity. To either side of the cusp the parametrisation traces two smooth sheets meeting tangentially at the origin. $\square$

**Proposition.** The Legendrian condition is preserved by any contact flow $\tilde\Phi_t$ on $(N, \alpha)$.

Let $\tilde X$ be a contact vector field on $N$, satisfying $\mathcal{L}_{\tilde X} \alpha = h \, \alpha$ for some smooth function $h$ (the **contact Hamiltonian** of $\tilde X$ multiplied by $-1$, in a standard convention). Let $\Lambda \subset N$ be Legendrian and let $\tilde\Phi_t$ be the flow of $\tilde X$. The pull-back of $\alpha$ to $\tilde\Phi_t(\Lambda)$ along the flow satisfies
$$
\tilde\Phi_t^* \alpha = \exp\!\left(\int_0^t h \circ \tilde\Phi_s \, ds\right) \cdot \alpha.
$$
On $\Lambda$, $\alpha|_\Lambda = 0$, so $\tilde\Phi_t^* \alpha|_\Lambda = 0$, equivalently $\alpha|_{\tilde\Phi_t(\Lambda)} = 0$. The dimension is preserved by any diffeomorphism. Hence $\tilde\Phi_t(\Lambda)$ is Legendrian. $\square$

**Proposition (transversality and the $A_2$ stratum).** The set of Legendrian germs in $J^1(M^n, \mathbb{R})$ whose front projection has an $A_2$ cusp at a chosen base point is a closed subset of codimension exactly $1$ in the space of all Legendrian germs at that point.

Identify Legendrian germs with stable right-equivalence classes of generating families $F : (M \times \mathbb{R}^k, 0) \to (\mathbb{R}, 0)$ (Arnold's correspondence; see Theorem in Key theorem section, step (i)). The stratum of germs whose function germ $f(u) = F(q_0, u)$ is right-equivalent to the $A_2$ representative $u^3$ has codimension equal to its Tjurina number $\tau(A_2) = \dim_\mathbb{R} \mathbb{R}[[u]] / (u^3, 3 u^2) = \dim_\mathbb{R} \mathrm{span}\{1, u\} = 2$ in the moduli space of unfolded germs — but two of those moduli directions are absorbed by the unfolding parameters $q_1, q_2$, leaving codimension $1$ as the effective stratum in $M^n$. Thom transversality and the genericity of unfoldings finish the proposition; the $A_2$ stratum is the divisor in the front bifurcation diagram. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The universal Maslov class of [05.12.01] is the topological invariant whose pull-back along the Gauss map $\Lambda \to \Lambda(n)$ records the $A_2$-cusp counting of the front projection. The intersection-theoretic Maslov-cycle formula proved in [05.12.01] specialises directly to the increment rule for Legendrian fronts.

- The contact manifold scaffolding of [05.10.01] supplies the ambient setting: $J^1(M, \mathbb{R})$ is the canonical contact manifold, and the Legendrian condition $\alpha|_\Lambda = 0$ is the contact-geometric instantiation of the half-dimensional integral condition.

- The forthcoming Lagrangian-singularities companion (to be `05.12.02`) treats the parallel ADE classification of caustics of Lagrangian projections $L \hookrightarrow T^*M \to M$. The two classifications are equivalent in the sense that a Legendrian in $J^1(M, \mathbb{R})$ projects to a Lagrangian in $T^*M$ and the wave-front singularities are the discriminants of the same generating families, but the cleanest treatment of generating families is contact rather than symplectic — this unit owns that treatment.

- The wave-front-set of a distribution (microlocal-analysis unit [02.14.01]) is a closed conic Lagrangian subset of $T^*M \setminus 0$. Hörmander's propagation-of-singularities theorem combined with the Fourier integral operator calculus picks up exactly the Maslov-index phase factor described here, and the singularities of the projected wave-front-set under Lagrangian fibrations are ADE-classified by the present theorem.

- In integrable systems, the action-angle Lagrangian fibration $T^*M \to B$ has caustics over the discriminant locus of the integrable system; the bifurcation diagram of an integrable family in dimension $\leq 4$ inherits the ADE classification of this unit (Givental 1988).

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Arnold's interest in the singularities of caustics and wavefronts grew from his 1965 reading of Maslov's *Theory of Perturbations and Asymptotic Methods*, which introduced the integer phase correction in WKB asymptotics that bears Maslov's name. Arnold 1967 *Funct. Anal. Appl.* 1 gave the geometric interpretation of Maslov's index as a characteristic class of the Lagrangian Grassmannian; Arnold 1972 *Funct. Anal. Appl.* 6 extended the geometric picture to wavefronts and caustics, identifying the locus of caustic singularities with the discriminant of a generating family and so reducing the classification problem to singularity theory of functions [ref: TODO_REF Arnold 1972]. The ADE list itself emerged from Arnold's 1972-1976 sequence of papers on normal forms for function germs near degenerate critical points (Russian Math. Surveys 29, 1974, 10-50), which was completed in the Arnold-Goryunov-Lyashko-Vasiliev EMS Vol 6 (1993) as the canonical reference [ref: TODO_REF Arnold-Goryunov-Lyashko-Vasiliev].

Hörmander 1971 *Acta Math.* 127 incorporated the Maslov class into the symbol calculus of Fourier integral operators, formalising the Hörmander-Maslov phase factor in the asymptotic expansion of oscillatory integrals as the integer Maslov index of the underlying Lagrangian [ref: TODO_REF Hörmander 1971]. The book-length treatment by Arnold, *Singularities of Caustics and Wave Fronts* (Mathematics and its Applications 62, Kluwer 1990), is the standard expository reference; Arnold-Givental EMS Vol 4 Ch 5 (2nd ed. 2001) is the densest survey [ref: TODO_REF Arnold-Givental EMS Vol 4 Ch 5]. Givental 1988 *Funct. Anal. Appl.* 22 extended the analysis to singular Lagrangian manifolds, opening the modern programme of Lagrangian-singularity theory for integrable systems and mirror symmetry [ref: TODO_REF Givental 1988]. The Eliashberg-Hofer-Sullivan 1995 survey and the Chekanov-Eliashberg 1997 differential-graded algebra of Legendrian links connected the singularity-theoretic side to the contact-topological side via the Legendrian h-principle and the Chekanov-Eliashberg DGA, a current research frontier.
