## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Lang's *Basic Mathematics* Part I treats precalculus as a foundations course: the arithmetic of integers, rationals, and reals is built from the field axioms and the order axioms rather than assumed as a collection of procedures. This pack exercises that foundational layer. The problems test the field and order axioms for $\mathbb{R}$ [00.01.01], the absolute value and the triangle inequality [00.01.02], polynomial and rational manipulation [00.01.03], the quadratic formula and discriminant [00.03.02], and the sign-chart solution of inequalities [00.04.01]. A handful of elementary number-theory problems — divisibility, the Euclidean algorithm, irrationality proofs — round out the set, matching the proof-flavoured exercises Lang scatters through Part I.

The pack collects ten problems: three easy, four medium, three hard. Each carries a hint and a full solution. The conventions follow the prerequisite units: $\mathbb{N} = \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$ as in the Babel Bible number-systems unit (Lang's own text uses $\mathbb{N}$ from $1$, a difference flagged in the Lang notation crosswalk); $|x|$ denotes absolute value; intervals are written with parentheses for open and brackets for closed.

The problems are meant to be worked alongside the prerequisite concept units rather than as a standalone development. The recurring theme is that every algebraic manipulation a reader will later perform mechanically — clearing a denominator, completing a square, flipping an inequality when multiplying by a negative — is a consequence of a named axiom, and the exercises make that dependence explicit.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

We work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining nine follow the same structure: problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks.

**Lead exercise.** *Prove that $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational, then show $\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}$ is irrational.*

**Solution.** First, $\sqrt{2}$. Suppose for contradiction that $\sqrt{2} = p/q$ with $p, q$ integers, $q \neq 0$, and the fraction in lowest terms, so $\gcd(p, q) = 1$. Squaring gives $2q^2 = p^2$, so $p^2$ is even. The square of an odd integer is odd (since $(2k+1)^2 = 4k^2 + 4k + 1$ is odd), so $p$ must be even: write $p = 2m$. Then $2q^2 = 4m^2$, so $q^2 = 2m^2$, meaning $q^2$ is even and hence $q$ is even. But then $2 \mid p$ and $2 \mid q$ contradicts $\gcd(p, q) = 1$. So no such fraction exists, and $\sqrt{2}$ is irrational.

Now $\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}$. Suppose it equals a rational $r$. Then $\sqrt{3} = r - \sqrt{2}$. Square both sides:

$$
3 = r^2 - 2r\sqrt{2} + 2, \qquad \text{so} \qquad 2r\sqrt{2} = r^2 - 1.
$$

If $r \neq 0$, this gives $\sqrt{2} = (r^2 - 1)/(2r)$, a ratio of rationals, hence rational — contradicting the first part. If $r = 0$ then $\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3} = 0$, impossible since both summands are positive. Either way we reach a contradiction, so $\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}$ is irrational. $\square$

The two-step pattern — assume rational, square to isolate a known irrational, derive a contradiction — is the workhorse of every elementary irrationality proof. It rests only on the field axioms (to manipulate the equation) and the closure of $\mathbb{Q}$ under arithmetic (to conclude the isolated expression is rational).

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).** *Distributive-law expansions.*

Using only the field axioms (commutativity, associativity, distributivity), expand and simplify $(a + b)(a - b)$ and $(a + b)^2$, naming the axiom at each step where distributivity is invoked.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Distributivity is $x(y + z) = xy + xz$. Apply it once to split off the first factor, then again to each resulting product.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $(a + b)(a - b)$: by distributivity over the second factor, $(a + b)(a - b) = (a + b)a - (a + b)b$. Distribute again: $(a+b)a = a^2 + ba$ and $(a+b)b = ab + b^2$. So

$$
(a + b)(a - b) = a^2 + ba - ab - b^2 = a^2 - b^2,
$$

using commutativity $ba = ab$ to cancel the middle terms.

For $(a + b)^2 = (a + b)(a + b)$: distributing gives $a^2 + ba + ab + b^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$, again using commutativity to combine $ba + ab = 2ab$.

Both identities are consequences of distributivity plus commutativity of multiplication — not separate rules to memorise. This is Lang's central pedagogical point in Ch. 1.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Absolute value as distance.*

Solve $|x - 3| < 5$ and $|2x + 1| \geq 7$, expressing each solution set as an interval or union of intervals.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$|u| < a$ means $-a < u < a$; $|u| \geq a$ means $u \geq a$ or $u \leq -a$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $|x - 3| < 5$: this is $-5 < x - 3 < 5$, i.e. $-2 < x < 8$. Solution set $(-2, 8)$. Read as distance: the points within $5$ of $3$.

For $|2x + 1| \geq 7$: either $2x + 1 \geq 7$ or $2x + 1 \leq -7$. The first gives $2x \geq 6$, so $x \geq 3$. The second gives $2x \leq -8$, so $x \leq -4$. Solution set $(-\infty, -4] \cup [3, \infty)$.

The equivalence $|u| < a \iff -a < u < a$ [00.01.02] turns every absolute-value inequality into a pair of linear inequalities.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, numeric).** *Euclidean algorithm and Bezout.*

Compute $\gcd(252, 198)$ by the Euclidean algorithm, then find integers $x, y$ with $252x + 198y = \gcd(252, 198)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Run repeated division with remainder until the remainder is zero; the last nonzero remainder is the gcd. Then back-substitute to express it as a combination.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Euclidean algorithm:

$$
252 = 1\cdot 198 + 54, \quad 198 = 3\cdot 54 + 36, \quad 54 = 1\cdot 36 + 18, \quad 36 = 2\cdot 18 + 0.
$$

The last nonzero remainder is $18$, so $\gcd(252, 198) = 18$.

Back-substitute. From the third line, $18 = 54 - 36$. Substitute $36 = 198 - 3\cdot 54$:

$$
18 = 54 - (198 - 3\cdot 54) = 4\cdot 54 - 198.
$$

Substitute $54 = 252 - 198$:

$$
18 = 4(252 - 198) - 198 = 4\cdot 252 - 5\cdot 198.
$$

So $x = 4$, $y = -5$: $252\cdot 4 + 198\cdot(-5) = 1008 - 990 = 18$. ✓

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *The triangle inequality and its reverse.*

Prove the triangle inequality $|x + y| \leq |x| + |y|$ for all real $x, y$, then deduce the reverse triangle inequality $\bigl||x| - |y|\bigr| \leq |x - y|$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the forward inequality, square both sides (both are nonnegative) and use $xy \leq |xy| = |x||y|$. For the reverse, apply the forward inequality to $x = (x - y) + y$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Forward.** Both sides of $|x + y| \leq |x| + |y|$ are nonnegative, so it suffices to compare squares. We have $|x+y|^2 = (x+y)^2 = x^2 + 2xy + y^2$ and $(|x| + |y|)^2 = x^2 + 2|x||y| + y^2$. The difference is $2(|x||y| - xy) \geq 0$ because $xy \leq |xy| = |x||y|$. So $|x+y|^2 \leq (|x|+|y|)^2$, and taking nonnegative square roots gives the inequality.

**Reverse.** Write $x = (x - y) + y$ and apply the forward inequality: $|x| \leq |x - y| + |y|$, so $|x| - |y| \leq |x - y|$. By symmetry (swap $x, y$ and use $|y - x| = |x - y|$), $|y| - |x| \leq |x - y|$. Together, $\bigl||x| - |y|\bigr| \leq |x - y|$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).** *Discriminant and the nature of the roots.*

For the quadratic $2x^2 - 4x + c = 0$, find the values of $c$ for which the equation has (a) two distinct real roots, (b) one repeated real root, (c) a complex-conjugate pair. For the repeated-root case give the root.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The discriminant is $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$. Its sign classifies the roots; $\Delta = 0$ gives the repeated root $x = -b/(2a)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Here $a = 2$, $b = -4$, so $\Delta = (-4)^2 - 4\cdot 2\cdot c = 16 - 8c$.

(a) Two distinct real roots: $\Delta > 0 \iff 16 - 8c > 0 \iff c < 2$.

(b) One repeated real root: $\Delta = 0 \iff c = 2$. The root is $x = -b/(2a) = 4/4 = 1$.

(c) Complex-conjugate pair: $\Delta < 0 \iff c > 2$.

The discriminant classification [00.03.02] partitions $c$-values cleanly at the single threshold $c = 2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).** *Order axioms: multiplying an inequality by a negative.*

From the order axioms — (O1) for any $x$, exactly one of $x > 0$, $x = 0$, $x < 0$ holds; (O2) $x, y > 0 \Rightarrow x + y > 0$; (O3) $x, y > 0 \Rightarrow xy > 0$ — prove that if $a < b$ and $c < 0$, then $ac > bc$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$a < b$ means $b - a > 0$, and $c < 0$ means $-c > 0$. Multiply these two positives using (O3).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By definition of the ordering, $a < b$ means $b - a > 0$, and $c < 0$ means $-c > 0$. By (O3), the product of two positive numbers is positive:

$$
(b - a)(-c) > 0.
$$

Expand using distributivity: $(b - a)(-c) = -bc + ac = ac - bc$. So $ac - bc > 0$, which means $ac > bc$. $\square$

This is the precise reason an inequality reverses when both sides are multiplied by a negative number: the rule is not a convention but a theorem from the three order axioms. It underwrites every sign-chart manipulation in [00.04.01].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).** *Quadratic inequality by sign chart.*

Solve $x^2 - x - 6 \geq 0$, giving the solution as a union of intervals, and verify the answer by testing one point in each region.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Factor the quadratic to find the roots, then determine the sign of the product on each interval the roots cut the line into.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Factor: $x^2 - x - 6 = (x - 3)(x + 2)$, with roots $x = -2$ and $x = 3$. These cut the line into three regions.

- On $(-\infty, -2)$: take $x = -3$, giving $(-6)(-1) = 6 > 0$. ✓
- On $(-2, 3)$: take $x = 0$, giving $(-3)(2) = -6 < 0$.
- On $(3, \infty)$: take $x = 4$, giving $(1)(6) = 6 > 0$. ✓

The product is $\geq 0$ on the outer two regions, including the endpoints where it equals zero. Solution set:

$$
(-\infty, -2] \cup [3, \infty).
$$

The sign-chart method [00.04.01] turns a quadratic inequality into a sign count of the linear factors.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *AM-GM for two terms, with equality.*

Prove that for nonnegative reals $a, b$, the arithmetic-geometric-mean inequality $\sqrt{ab} \leq \dfrac{a + b}{2}$ holds, and that equality holds if and only if $a = b$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Start from the fact that a real square is nonnegative: $(\sqrt{a} - \sqrt{b})^2 \geq 0$. Expand and rearrange.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $a, b \geq 0$, the real numbers $\sqrt{a}$ and $\sqrt{b}$ exist, and any real square is nonnegative:

$$
(\sqrt{a} - \sqrt{b})^2 \geq 0.
$$

Expand: $a - 2\sqrt{a}\sqrt{b} + b \geq 0$, i.e. $a + b \geq 2\sqrt{ab}$. Dividing by $2$ (positive, so the inequality direction is preserved) gives

$$
\frac{a + b}{2} \geq \sqrt{ab}.
$$

**Equality.** The single inequality used was $(\sqrt{a} - \sqrt{b})^2 \geq 0$, which is an equality exactly when $\sqrt{a} - \sqrt{b} = 0$, i.e. $\sqrt{a} = \sqrt{b}$, i.e. $a = b$. So equality in AM-GM holds iff $a = b$. $\square$

The proof needs only the order axiom that squares are nonnegative — itself a consequence of (O3) applied to $x$ and $x$, or to $-x$ and $-x$. Lang presents AM-GM as a worked example precisely to show how much follows from the ordering alone.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *Density of the rationals.*

Prove that between any two distinct reals $a < b$ there is a rational number. You may use the Archimedean property: for every real $t > 0$ there is a positive integer $n$ with $1/n < t$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Choose $n$ so large that $1/n < b - a$, so steps of size $1/n$ cannot jump over the interval. Then take the smallest integer $m$ with $m/n > a$ and show $m/n < b$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $b - a > 0$, the Archimedean property gives a positive integer $n$ with $1/n < b - a$, equivalently $n(b - a) > 1$.

Consider the integers $m$ with $m/n > a$, i.e. $m > na$. Such integers exist (Archimedean property again, applied to bound $na$ above), and they are bounded below, so by well-ordering there is a least one; call it $m$. Then $m/n > a$, while minimality gives $(m - 1)/n \leq a$, i.e. $m \leq na + 1$.

Now estimate $m/n$ from above:

$$
\frac{m}{n} \leq \frac{na + 1}{n} = a + \frac{1}{n} < a + (b - a) = b.
$$

So $a < m/n < b$, and $m/n$ is rational. $\square$

The argument is the precalculus seed of the density statement in [00.01.01]; it is the reason the rationals can approximate any real to arbitrary precision, which Apostol's epsilon-delta limits later exploit.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, numeric).** *Rational-roots test.*

Find all rational roots of $p(x) = 2x^3 - 3x^2 - 11x + 6$, then factor $p$ completely over $\mathbb{R}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The rational-root theorem says any rational root $p/q$ in lowest terms has $p$ dividing the constant term and $q$ dividing the leading coefficient. Test the finite list of candidates, then divide out and solve the remaining quadratic.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Constant term $6$, leading coefficient $2$. Candidate rational roots $p/q$ have $p \in \{\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 6\}$ and $q \in \{1, 2\}$, giving candidates $\pm 1, \pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 6, \pm \tfrac12, \pm \tfrac32$.

Test $x = 3$: $2\cdot 27 - 3\cdot 9 - 11\cdot 3 + 6 = 54 - 27 - 33 + 6 = 0$. So $x = 3$ is a root and $(x - 3)$ divides $p$.

Divide: $2x^3 - 3x^2 - 11x + 6 = (x - 3)(2x^2 + 3x - 2)$. Factor the quadratic: $2x^2 + 3x - 2 = (2x - 1)(x + 2)$, with roots $x = \tfrac12$ and $x = -2$.

So the rational roots are $x = 3,\ \tfrac12,\ -2$, and the complete real factorisation is

$$
p(x) = (x - 3)(2x - 1)(x + 2) = 2(x - 3)\bigl(x - \tfrac12\bigr)(x + 2).
$$

The rational-root test plus polynomial division [00.01.03] reduces a cubic to a quadratic, which the quadratic formula [00.03.02] finishes.

</details>

</aside>
