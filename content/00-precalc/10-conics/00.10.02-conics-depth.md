## Intuition [Beginner]

Picture a double cone — two ice-cream cones glued tip to tip. Now slice it with a flat plane. Four things can happen depending on the angle of the cut. Cut straight across and you get a **circle**. Tilt the blade a little and the circle stretches into an oval, the **ellipse**. Tilt until the blade runs parallel to the side of the cone and the curve never closes back up — it is a **parabola**. Tilt even steeper so the blade cuts both cones and you get two outward-facing curves, the **hyperbola**.

Here is the deeper surprise. All four curves follow the very same rule. Pick a fixed point called the **focus** and a fixed line called the **directrix**. For every point on the curve, divide its distance to the focus by its distance to the directrix. That ratio is a single constant number, the **eccentricity** $e$. A ratio of $1$ gives the parabola; below $1$ gives the ellipse (and the circle is the case $e = 0$); above $1$ gives the hyperbola.

One rule, three curves, and the number $e$ alone decides which one you draw. This unit builds the parabola, the ellipse, and the hyperbola in full detail by starting from that one ratio rule and turning it into an equation you can graph.

## Visual [Beginner]

The table collects the four slices, the curve each one produces, and the eccentricity that labels it. Reading down the table, the eccentricity grows from $0$ (a perfect circle) upward through the ellipses, hits $1$ at the parabola, and keeps climbing through the hyperbolas.

| Cut angle | Curve | Eccentricity $e$ |
|---|---|---|
| Flat, across the axis | Circle | $0$ |
| Tilted, shallow | Ellipse | $0 < e < 1$ |
| Parallel to the side | Parabola | $1$ |
| Steeper than the side | Hyperbola | $e > 1$ |

The same four curves, drawn from the focus-directrix rule, land in the same order. As $e$ slides from $0$ upward the shape stretches, opens, and finally splits into two branches.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Example 1.** Identify and sketch $y^2 = 8x$.

Step 1. The equation has a $y^2$ term and a single $x$ term, but no $x^2$. That signature is a **parabola**.

Step 2. Match it against the standard parabola form $y^2 = 4px$. The coefficient of $x$ gives $4p = 8$, so $p = 2$.

Step 3. Because $x$ is positive on the right side and the squared variable is $y$, the parabola opens to the right. Its vertex sits at the origin and its focus is the point $(2, 0)$. The directrix is the vertical line $x = -2$.

**Example 2.** Identify and sketch $x^2/25 + y^2/9 = 1$.

Step 1. Both $x^2$ and $y^2$ appear and they share the same sign. That signature is an **ellipse**.

Step 2. Read off the two semi-axes straight from the denominators: $a = 5$ and $b = 3$.

Step 3. The focal distance obeys $c^2 = a^2 - b^2 = 25 - 9 = 16$, so $c = 4$. The foci are the two points $(\pm 4, 0)$ on the long axis. The ellipse is a stretched circle, $10$ wide and $6$ tall, centred at the origin.

Both answers fall out the same way: read the signs and the squared terms to name the curve, then read off the parameters to place its key points.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **conic section** is the locus of a point $P$ that keeps a fixed ratio between its distance to a fixed point $F$ (the **focus**) and its distance to a fixed line $\ell$ (the **directrix**), with $F \notin \ell$. The ratio is the **eccentricity** $e \geq 0$:

$$
\frac{d(P, F)}{d(P, \ell)} = e.
$$

The value of $e$ alone names the curve. When $e = 1$ the locus is a **parabola**; when $0 \leq e < 1$ it is an **ellipse** (and $e = 0$ collapses it to a **circle**); when $e > 1$ it is a **hyperbola**. Each curve also admits a second, two-foci characterisation that is often easier to use.

**Parabola.** Place the focus at $F = (p, 0)$ and the directrix at $x = -p$, with $p \neq 0$. The locus $d(P, F) = d(P, \ell)$ gives the standard form

$$
y^2 = 4px,
$$

with vertex at the origin and the line through $F$ perpendicular to $\ell$ (here the $x$-axis) as the **axis of symmetry**. Translating the vertex to $(h, k)$ gives the two shifted forms

$$
y = a(x - h)^2 + k \quad (\text{vertical axis}), \qquad x = a(y - k)^2 + h \quad (\text{horizontal axis}),
$$

where $a = 1/(4p)$. The sign of $a$ fixes the opening direction: $a > 0$ opens the vertical form upward and the horizontal form to the right.

**Ellipse.** The ellipse with foci $F_1, F_2$ is the locus of points $P$ with

$$
d(P, F_1) + d(P, F_2) = 2a,
$$

a constant larger than the distance between the foci. With foci at $(\pm c, 0)$ and $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$, its standard form is

$$
\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1, \qquad a > b > 0,
$$

with semi-major axis $a$, semi-minor axis $b$, and eccentricity $e = c/a \in [0, 1)$. The two descriptions are equivalent: the focus-directrix ellipse and the constant-sum ellipse are the same curve [ref: TODO_REF Stewart — Precalculus: Mathematics for Calculus].

**Hyperbola.** The hyperbola with foci $F_1, F_2$ is the locus of points $P$ for which the absolute difference is constant:

$$
\lvert\, d(P, F_1) - d(P, F_2)\, \rvert = 2a.
$$

With foci at $(\pm c, 0)$ and $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$, the standard form is

$$
\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1,
$$

with transverse semi-axis $a$, conjugate semi-axis $b$, eccentricity $e = c/a > 1$, and asymptotes $y = \pm (b/a)\, x$. The curve has two branches opening left and right, each hugging its asymptotes at infinity.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The standard form $y^2 = 4px$ opens to the right only when $p > 0$; with $p < 0$ it opens to the left. The squared variable, not the sign alone, fixes which axis the parabola spans, and the sign of $p$ (or $a$) fixes the direction along that axis.
- In the ellipse $x^2/a^2 + y^2/b^2 = 1$ the larger denominator labels the major axis. If $b > a$ the major axis is vertical and the foci are at $(0, \pm c)$ with $c^2 = b^2 - a^2$, not $(\pm c, 0)$.
- The hyperbola $x^2/a^2 - y^2/b^2 = 1$ opens left and right because the $x^2$ term is positive. Swapping the signs to $y^2/b^2 - x^2/a^2 = 1$ opens it up and down, and the foci move to the $y$-axis. The two equations are **conjugate** hyperbolas, not the same curve.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

This section derives every standard form above from a single starting point: a distance condition. The first derivation handles the parabola; the next two build the ellipse and hyperbola from their two-foci definitions, which themselves follow from the focus-directrix rule.

**Derivation 1 (parabola, vertex at the origin).** Take the focus $F = (p, 0)$ and the directrix $\ell: x = -p$. For $P = (x, y)$ the definition $d(P, F) = d(P, \ell)$ reads

$$
\sqrt{(x - p)^2 + y^2} \;=\; \lvert x + p \rvert.
$$

Square both sides and expand:

$$
(x - p)^2 + y^2 = (x + p)^2 \;\Longrightarrow\; x^2 - 2px + p^2 + y^2 = x^2 + 2px + p^2.
$$

Cancel $x^2$ and $p^2$ and collect: $y^2 = 4px$. Translating the vertex to $(h, k)$ by the substitution $x \mapsto x - h$, $y \mapsto y - k$ gives $(y - k)^2 = 4p(x - h)$, i.e. $y = \tfrac{1}{4p}(x - h)^2 + k$. Setting $a = 1/(4p)$ recovers the vertical-axis form $y = a(x - h)^2 + k$. Swapping the roles of $x$ and $y$ (focus on the $y$-axis, horizontal directrix) gives the horizontal-axis form $x = a(y - k)^2 + h$ with the same relation $a = 1/(4p)$.

**Derivation 2 (ellipse, constant sum $2a$).** Take foci $F_1 = (c, 0)$, $F_2 = (-c, 0)$ with $0 \leq c < a$ and impose $d(P, F_1) + d(P, F_2) = 2a$. Write $d_1 = d(P, F_1)$, $d_2 = d(P, F_2)$. The defining relation and its rearrangement give

$$
d_1 = 2a - d_2 \;\Longrightarrow\; d_1^2 = 4a^2 - 4a\, d_2 + d_2^2.
$$

Substitute the squared distance formulas $d_1^2 = (x - c)^2 + y^2$ and $d_2^2 = (x + c)^2 + y^2$ and simplify:

$$
(x - c)^2 + y^2 = 4a^2 - 4a\, d_2 + (x + c)^2 + y^2.
$$

Cancel $y^2$, expand the squares, and collect to isolate the surviving square root:

$$
-4cx = 4a^2 - 4a\, d_2 \;\Longrightarrow\; d_2 = a + \frac{cx}{a}.
$$

Square once more, $d_2^2 = (a + cx/a)^2$, and equate to $(x + c)^2 + y^2$:

$$
a^2 + 2cx + \frac{c^2 x^2}{a^2} = x^2 + 2cx + c^2 + y^2.
$$

Cancel $2cx$, collect the $x^2$ terms, and use $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$:

$$
\frac{a^2 - c^2}{a^2}\, x^2 + y^2 = a^2 - c^2 \;\Longrightarrow\; \frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1,
$$

where $b^2 = a^2 - c^2$. The relation $e = c/a$ then reads $e^2 = 1 - b^2/a^2$, confirming $0 \leq e < 1$. Reversing the algebra shows that every point on this curve satisfies the constant-sum condition, so the two characterisations coincide.

**Derivation 3 (hyperbola, constant difference $2a$).** With the same foci $F_1 = (c, 0)$, $F_2 = (-c, 0)$ but now $c > a > 0$, impose $\lvert d_1 - d_2 \rvert = 2a$. On the right branch $d_2 - d_1 = 2a$. The same squaring dance as above, with $-2a$ in place of $2a$, yields

$$
d_2 = -a + \frac{cx}{a} \quad \text{(right branch)},
$$

and after squaring and collecting with $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$,

$$
\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1.
$$

The asymptotes fall out by division: rewrite as $y^2 = b^2(x^2/a^2 - 1)$, so $y = \pm (b/a)\sqrt{x^2 - a^2}$. As $\lvert x \rvert \to \infty$ the term $-a^2$ under the root becomes negligible and $y \to \pm (b/a)\lvert x \rvert$, giving the asymptotes $y = \pm (b/a)\, x$.

**Bridge.** The focus-directrix derivation builds toward [00.11.01], where the single polar equation $r = \ell/(1 + e\cos\theta)$ reproduces all three standard forms at once, and appears again in [00.11.02] as the rational parametrisation of each conic. The foundational reason one distance-ratio produces three distinct curves is that the algebraic sign of $1 - e^2$ selects which branch of the quadratic the locus occupies — this is exactly the content of the three derivations above, where $b^2 = a^2 - c^2$ (ellipse) versus $b^2 = c^2 - a^2$ (hyperbola) is the central insight, and the parabola sits between them as the case $c^2 = a^2$ that cancels the constant term. Putting these together, the bridge is between the geometric definition (a ratio or sum of distances) and the algebraic equation (a degree-two curve in $x$ and $y$), and the pattern generalises to the discriminant test of [00.10.01], which classifies any quadratic by a single number $\Delta = B^2 - 4AC$.

Find the vertex, focus, and directrix of the parabola $x = -\tfrac{1}{12}\, y^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare with $x = \tfrac{1}{4p}\, y^2$ and read off $4p$.

Vertex $(0, 0)$; focus $(-3, 0)$; directrix $x = 3$.

Writing $x = \tfrac{1}{4p} y^2$ gives $\tfrac{1}{4p} = -\tfrac{1}{12}$, so $4p = -12$ and $p = -3$. The negative sign opens the parabola to the left, the vertex stays at the origin, the focus is at $(p, 0) = (-3, 0)$, and the directrix is $x = -p = 3$.

Rubric: full credit for the correct sign of $p$ together with focus and directrix.

Identify the conic $16x^2 + 25y^2 = 400$, then state its centre, the semi-axes, and the foci.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Divide through by $400$ to reach the standard form $x^2/a^2 + y^2/b^2 = 1$, then use $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Reduce $y^2 - 6y - 4x + 1 = 0$ to standard form by completing the square, then state the vertex, focus, and directrix.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Isolate the $y$-terms, complete the square in $y$, and match the result to $(y - k)^2 = 4p(x - h)$.

Standard form $(y - 3)^2 = 4(x + 2)$; vertex $(-2, 3)$; focus $(-1, 3)$; directrix $x = -3$.

Thus $4p = 4$, giving $p = 1$, and the parabola opens to the right with vertex $(-2, 3)$. The focus is one unit right of the vertex at $(-1, 3)$, and the directrix is one unit left at $x = -3$.

Rubric: full credit for completing the square correctly and locating the vertex, focus, and directrix.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Reduce $4x^2 + 9y^2 - 8x + 36y + 4 = 0$ to standard form, then state the centre, semi-axes, and foci.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Group the $x$-terms and the $y$-terms, complete the square in each, and divide by the constant that appears.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Standard form $(x - 1)^2/9 + (y + 2)^2/4 = 1$; centre $(1, -2)$; $a = 3$, $b = 2$; foci $(1 \pm \sqrt{5}, -2)$.

Group: $4(x^2 - 2x) + 9(y^2 + 4y) = -4$. Complete both squares: $4(x - 1)^2 - 4 + 9(y + 2)^2 - 36 = -4$, so $4(x - 1)^2 + 9(y + 2)^2 = 36$. Dividing by $36$ gives $(x - 1)^2/9 + (y + 2)^2/4 = 1$. Then $a = 3$, $b = 2$, and $c = \sqrt{9 - 4} = \sqrt{5}$, so the foci are $(1 - \sqrt{5}, -2)$ and $(1 + \sqrt{5}, -2)$.

Rubric: full credit for both squares, the centre, and both foci.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Reduce $x^2 - 4y^2 + 6x + 8y - 3 = 0$ to standard form, classify the conic, and give the centre and asymptotes.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The opposite signs of the $x^2$ and $y^2$ coefficients signal a hyperbola. Complete the square in both variables.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Hyperbola; standard form $(x + 3)^2/8 - (y - 1)^2/2 = 1$; centre $(-3, 1)$; asymptotes $y = 1 \pm \tfrac{1}{2}(x + 3)$.

Group: $(x^2 + 6x) - 4(y^2 - 2y) = 3$. Complete both squares: $(x + 3)^2 - 9 - 4[(y - 1)^2 - 1] = 3$, so $(x + 3)^2 - 4(y - 1)^2 = 8$. Dividing by $8$ gives $(x + 3)^2/8 - (y - 1)^2/2 = 1$. Thus $a = 2\sqrt{2}$, $b = \sqrt{2}$, and the asymptote slopes are $\pm b/a = \pm 1/2$, centred at $(-3, 1)$.

Rubric: full credit for classification, centre, and both asymptote equations.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (medium, numeric).**

A whispering gallery is a room whose horizontal cross-section is an ellipse. The room is $100$ feet long and $40$ feet wide. Two people standing at the foci can hear each other with perfect clarity while listeners elsewhere cannot. Where should the two people stand? Give the distance of each from the centre of the room, to the nearest foot.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The length is the major axis $2a$ and the width is the minor axis $2b$. Use $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Each person stands about $46$ feet from the centre, along the long axis.

So the two people stand at $(\pm 46, 0)$ relative to the centre. The reflection property of the ellipse gathers every sound ray leaving one focus onto the other, which is why the whisper carries.

Rubric: full credit for computing $c$ and rounding sensibly.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the standard form of the hyperbola from the difference-of-distances definition. Take foci $F_1 = (c, 0)$, $F_2 = (-c, 0)$ with $c > a > 0$, impose $\lvert d_1 - d_2 \rvert = 2a$ on the right branch, and show the locus is $x^2/a^2 - y^2/b^2 = 1$ with $b^2 = c^2 - a^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On the right branch $d_2 - d_1 = 2a$. Isolate one square root, square, simplify, and use $b^2 = c^2 - a^2$ at the end.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On the right branch $d_2 - d_1 = 2a$, so $d_1 = d_2 - 2a$ and squaring gives $d_1^2 = d_2^2 - 4a\, d_2 + 4a^2$. Substitute the squared distance formulas:

$$
(x - c)^2 + y^2 = (x + c)^2 + y^2 - 4a\, d_2 + 4a^2.
$$

Cancel $y^2$, expand, and collect: $-4cx = -4a\, d_2 + 4a^2$, so $d_2 = a + cx/a$. Squaring again, $d_2^2 = (a + cx/a)^2 = a^2 + 2cx + c^2 x^2/a^2$. Equate to $(x + c)^2 + y^2 = x^2 + 2cx + c^2 + y^2$ and cancel $2cx$:

$$
a^2 + \frac{c^2 x^2}{a^2} = x^2 + c^2 + y^2.
$$

Multiply through by $a^2$ and collect the $x^2$ term: $(c^2 - a^2) x^2 - a^2 y^2 = a^2(c^2 - a^2)$. Setting $b^2 = c^2 - a^2 > 0$ and dividing by $a^2 b^2$ yields $x^2/a^2 - y^2/b^2 = 1$.

Rubric: full credit for the two squarings, the simplification, and the identification $b^2 = c^2 - a^2$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the hyperbola $x^2/a^2 - y^2/b^2 = 1$ has asymptotes $y = \pm (b/a)\, x$. Specifically, prove that for each choice of sign the vertical distance from a point on the curve to the line $y = (b/a)\, x$ tends to $0$ as $x \to +\infty$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Solve the standard form for $y$ on the upper branch, subtract the line, and bound the result after rationalising.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On the upper branch $y = +(b/a)\sqrt{x^2 - a^2}$. The vertical gap to the line $y = (b/a)\, x$ is

$$
\delta(x) = \frac{b}{a}\Bigl( x - \sqrt{x^2 - a^2} \Bigr) = \frac{b}{a} \cdot \frac{a^2}{x + \sqrt{x^2 - a^2}},
$$

where the second equality rationalises by multiplying by $(x + \sqrt{x^2 - a^2})/(x + \sqrt{x^2 - a^2})$. As $x \to +\infty$ the denominator grows without bound, so $\delta(x) \to 0$. The lower branch $y = -(b/a)\sqrt{x^2 - a^2}$ is the mirror image and approaches $y = -(b/a)\, x$ by the same argument. Hence both asymptotes are $y = \pm (b/a)\, x$.

Rubric: full credit for solving for $y$, rationalising the gap, and arguing the limit.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (focal-sum and focal-difference characterisations).** The ellipse is the unique locus with $d(P, F_1) + d(P, F_2) = 2a > d(F_1, F_2)$; the hyperbola is the unique locus with $\lvert d(P, F_1) - d(P, F_2) \rvert = 2a < d(F_1, F_2)$. These two-foci definitions are equivalent to the single-focus-directrix definition with $e = c/a$: every focus-directrix ellipse has a second focus (the reflection of the first through the centre), and every focus-directrix hyperbola likewise.

**Theorem 2 (reflection properties).** A ray leaving one focus of an ellipse reflects off the curve to pass through the other focus; a ray leaving the focus of a parabola reflects parallel to the axis; and a ray aimed at one focus of a hyperbola reflects as though coming from the other. All three follow from the equal-angles law of reflection combined with the tangent line to the curve, and they underlie telescope, antenna, and lithotripsy design.

**Theorem 3 (tangent lines).** The tangent to the ellipse $x^2/a^2 + y^2/b^2 = 1$ at $(x_0, y_0)$ is $x_0 x/a^2 + y_0 y/b^2 = 1$. The tangent to the parabola $y^2 = 4px$ at $(x_0, y_0)$ is $y_0 y = 2p(x + x_0)$. The tangent to the hyperbola $x^2/a^2 - y^2/b^2 = 1$ at $(x_0, y_0)$ is $x_0 x/a^2 - y_0 y/b^2 = 1$. Each formula is the **polar line** of the point of tangency and doubles as the equation of the chord of contact from an external point.

**Theorem 4 (polar unification).** With the focus at the origin and the directrix at $x = \ell/e$, every conic of eccentricity $e$ satisfies

$$
r = \frac{\ell}{1 + e\cos\theta},
$$

where $\ell$ is the semi-latus rectum. Substituting $e < 1$, $e = 1$, $e > 1$ recovers the ellipse, parabola, and hyperbola standard forms, so the polar equation packages the entire eccentricity classification into a single expression [ref: TODO_REF Stewart — Precalculus: Mathematics for Calculus].

**Theorem 5 (Dandelin spheres).** Inside each nappe of the cone one can inscribe a sphere tangent to both the cone and the cutting plane. The tangent point of such a sphere with the cutting plane is a focus of the conic, and the circle of tangency with the cone lies in a plane whose intersection with the cutting plane is a directrix. The Dandelin construction gives a purely geometric proof that the cone-slicing definition and the focus-directrix definition describe the same curves.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason the three conics form one family is the unified focus-directrix definition, parametrised by the single continuous value $e$. The central insight is that each conic's signature property — the parabola's equal distances, the ellipse's constant sum $2a$, the hyperbola's constant difference $2a$ — is exactly the geometric shadow of one algebraic identity, and this generalises immediately to the polar form $r = \ell/(1 + e\cos\theta)$ that reproduces all three standard forms from one equation. The reflection properties and tangent-line formulas follow from the same focus-directrix seed via the equal-angles law, and putting these together, the bridge is between Apollonius's synthetic slicing picture and Descartes's algebraic picture of degree-two curves. The pattern generalises once more in projective geometry, where the distinction between ellipse, parabola, and hyperbola dissolves: a projective transformation carries any non-degenerate conic to any other, and the three types record only how the curve meets the line at infinity.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (ellipse focal sum).** The sum of the distances from any point $P$ on the ellipse $x^2/a^2 + y^2/b^2 = 1$ ($a > b$) to the foci $F_1 = (c, 0)$, $F_2 = (-c, 0)$, where $c^2 = a^2 - b^2$, is the constant $2a$.

*Proof.* Take $P = (x, y)$ on the ellipse, so $y^2 = b^2(1 - x^2/a^2)$ and $\lvert x \rvert \leq a$. Define

$$
d_1^2 = (x - c)^2 + y^2, \qquad d_2^2 = (x + c)^2 + y^2.
$$

Substitute $y^2 = b^2 - (b^2/a^2) x^2$ and use $b^2 = a^2 - c^2$:

$$
d_1^2 = x^2 - 2cx + c^2 + b^2 - \frac{b^2 x^2}{a^2} = x^2\!\left(1 - \frac{b^2}{a^2}\right) - 2cx + (c^2 + b^2).
$$

Since $1 - b^2/a^2 = c^2/a^2$ and $c^2 + b^2 = a^2$, this becomes

$$
d_1^2 = \frac{c^2 x^2}{a^2} - 2cx + a^2 = \left(\frac{cx}{a} - a\right)^{\!2}.
$$

Because $c < a$ and $\lvert x \rvert \leq a$, the quantity $a - cx/a$ is non-negative, so $d_1 = a - cx/a$. A symmetric computation gives $d_2 = a + cx/a$. Adding,

$$
d_1 + d_2 = \left(a - \frac{cx}{a}\right) + \left(a + \frac{cx}{a}\right) = 2a. \qquad \square
$$

**Proposition 2 (hyperbola asymptotes).** The hyperbola $x^2/a^2 - y^2/b^2 = 1$ has asymptotes $y = \pm (b/a)\, x$.

*Proof.* Solve the standard form for $y$ on the upper branch: $y = (b/a)\sqrt{x^2 - a^2}$, defined for $\lvert x \rvert \geq a$. Compare with the line $L(x) = (b/a)\, x$. Their difference, rationalised, is

$$
L(x) - y = \frac{b}{a}\!\left(x - \sqrt{x^2 - a^2}\right) = \frac{b}{a} \cdot \frac{a^2}{x + \sqrt{x^2 - a^2}}.
$$

As $x \to +\infty$ the denominator $x + \sqrt{x^2 - a^2}$ grows without bound, so the difference tends to $0$. The upper branch therefore approaches the line $y = (b/a)\, x$. The lower branch $y = -(b/a)\sqrt{x^2 - a^2}$ is the reflection through the $x$-axis and approaches $y = -(b/a)\, x$ by the same argument. Both asymptotes are thus $y = \pm (b/a)\, x$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (horizontal parabola from focus and directrix).** The locus of points equidistant from the focus $F = (h + p, k)$ and the directrix $\ell: x = h - p$ (a vertical line, perpendicular to the horizontal axis of symmetry) is the parabola $x = \dfrac{1}{4p}(y - k)^2 + h$, i.e. the horizontal-axis standard form $x = a(y - k)^2 + h$ with $a = 1/(4p)$.

*Proof.* For $P = (x, y)$ the defining condition $d(P, F) = d(P, \ell)$ reads

$$
\sqrt{(x - h - p)^2 + (y - k)^2} = \lvert x - h + p \rvert.
$$

Square both sides and expand each quadratic in $x - h$:

$$
(x - h)^2 - 2p(x - h) + p^2 + (y - k)^2 = (x - h)^2 + 2p(x - h) + p^2.
$$

Cancel $(x - h)^2$ and $p^2$ from both sides:

$$
-2p(x - h) + (y - k)^2 = 2p(x - h).
$$

Collect the $x - h$ terms: $(y - k)^2 = 4p(x - h)$. Solve for $x$:

$$
x = \frac{1}{4p}(y - k)^2 + h.
$$

Setting $a = 1/(4p)$ recovers the horizontal-axis standard form $x = a(y - k)^2 + h$. The sign of $p$ (equivalently of $a$) fixes the opening direction: $p > 0$ opens the parabola to the right, $p < 0$ to the left. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **The conic classification and discriminant [00.10.01].** This unit is the deep companion to [00.10.01]: the sibling unit classifies any quadratic $Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2 + \cdots = 0$ by the single number $\Delta = B^2 - 4AC$, while the present unit derives each conic's standard form by hand from a distance condition. Read together, the two units show that the geometric focus-directrix picture and the algebraic discriminant picture describe one and the same family of curves.

- **Cartesian coordinates and the distance formula [00.09.01].** Every derivation in this unit runs on the distance formula $\sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$ from [00.09.01]. The focus-directrix condition $d(P, F)/d(P, \ell) = e$ becomes an equation only once Cartesian coordinates translate "distance" into square roots of sums of squares, and completing the square is exactly the coordinate-algebra move that recentres the curve at its vertex or centre.

- **Polar coordinates and the unified conic [00.11.01].** The polar equation $r = \ell/(1 + e\cos\theta)$ derived in [00.11.01] packages the three standard forms of this unit into a single expression indexed by $e$. The focus placed at the pole in that unit is the same focus that appears in the focus-directrix definition here, so the polar form is the natural coordinate change that turns the distance-ratio rule directly into a graph.

- **Parametric and rational curves [00.11.02].** Each standard form derived here admits a rational parametrisation developed in [00.11.02] (for instance the parabola $y^2 = 4px$ parametrises as $(pt^2, 2pt)$, and the ellipse $x^2/a^2 + y^2/b^2 = 1$ as $(a\cos t, b\sin t)$). Those parametrisations convert the implicit equations of this unit into the rational maps that underpin projective geometry and Bezier-style curve design.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Menaechmus of Proconnesus, around 350 BCE, is credited with the discovery of the conic sections while working on the problem of doubling the cube [ref: TODO_REF Coolidge — A History of the Conic Sections and Geometrical Topics]. He obtained the three curves by slicing a cone at three different fixed angles, and the resulting "symptom" relations — the precursor of our standard forms — gave the first geometric solution to the Delian problem of constructing $\sqrt[3]{2}$. Menaechmus's account survives only through later commentators; the direct textual tradition begins with Apollonius.

Apollonius of Perga, around 200 BCE, codified the theory in the eight-book treatise *On Conic Sections* [ref: TODO_REF Apollonius of Perga — On Conic Sections]. Apollonius proved that all three curves can be cut from a single cone by varying the angle of the plane, derived the focus-directrix characterisation, developed the theory of conjugate diameters, and named the curves *ellipse*, *parabola*, and *hyperbola* from the Greek verbs for "falling short", "application", and "exceeding" — describing how the cutting plane compares to a generator of the cone. The Apollonian names survive unchanged in the modern standard forms $x^2/a^2 + y^2/b^2 = 1$, $y^2 = 4px$, and $x^2/a^2 - y^2/b^2 = 1$.

Johannes Kepler, in the *Astronomia Nova* of 1609, established that the orbit of Mars is an ellipse with the sun at one focus, displacing two millennia of circular-orbit astronomy and turning the conic sections from a pure geometry into the language of celestial mechanics. Kepler's first law binds the focus-directrix theory of this unit to the physical world: the eccentricity $e = c/a$ that labels a conic on paper is the same $e$ that measures how far a planet's orbit departs from a circle, with Earth at $e \approx 0.017$ and Mercury at $e \approx 0.206$. René Descartes's 1637 coordinate reformulation recast the Apollonian curves as the degree-two equations $Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2 + \cdots = 0$ studied in [00.10.01], and the nineteenth-century projective geometers — Poncelet, Steiner, Chasles — finally unified the three types by showing that any non-degenerate conic can be carried to any other by a projective transformation.
