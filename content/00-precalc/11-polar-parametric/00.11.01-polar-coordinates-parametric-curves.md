## Intuition [Beginner]

A point in the plane can be located in two ways. The familiar way uses horizontal and vertical distances: $(x, y)$. The second way uses a distance and a direction: walk $r$ steps from the origin at angle $\theta$, and you arrive at the same point. The pair $(r, \theta)$ is called a **polar coordinate** of the point.

Think of a radar screen. The sweep arm rotates around the centre, and a blip appears at a distance and a direction from the centre. That is polar coordinates: the distance $r$ is how far out the blip is, and the angle $\theta$ is which direction the arm was pointing when the blip appeared. The radar does not need an $x$-axis and a $y$-axis — it uses a single angle and a single distance.

Polar coordinates exist because some curves are much easier to describe with a distance and an angle than with two separate distances. A circle centred at the origin is just $r = 5$ in polar form, a single number, whereas in Cartesian form it is $x^2 + y^2 = 25$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Convert the polar point $(r, \theta) = (4, 30^\circ)$ to Cartesian coordinates $(x, y)$.

Step 1. The conversion formulas are $x = r \cos\theta$ and $y = r \sin\theta$.

Step 2. Using the special-angle values from [00.07.01]: $\cos 30^\circ = \sqrt{3}/2$ and $\sin 30^\circ = 1/2$.

Step 3. Compute: $x = 4 \cdot \sqrt{3}/2 = 2\sqrt{3}$ and $y = 4 \cdot 1/2 = 2$.

What this tells us: polar and Cartesian coordinates name the same point differently. The conversion is a direct application of sine and cosine from [00.07.01].

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The **polar coordinates** of a point $P$ in the plane are an ordered pair $(r, \theta)$ where $r \geq 0$ is the distance from the origin $O$ to $P$, and $\theta$ is the angle measured counter-clockwise from the positive $x$-axis to the ray $OP$ [ref: TODO_REF Lang — Basic Mathematics Ch. 9].

**Conversion formulas.** The polar pair $(r, \theta)$ and the Cartesian pair $(x, y)$ are related by

$$
x = r\cos\theta, \qquad y = r\sin\theta,
$$

and inversely

$$
r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}, \qquad \tan\theta = \frac{y}{x} \quad (x \neq 0).
$$

The angle $\theta$ is determined by $\theta = \mathrm{atan2}(y, x)$ (the two-argument arctangent), which accounts for the quadrant.

**Parametric curves.** A **parametric curve** in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is a map $t \mapsto (x(t), y(t))$ from an interval of real numbers to the plane. The parameter $t$ traces the curve as it varies.

**Polar curves.** A **polar curve** is the set of points $(r, \theta)$ satisfying an equation $r = f(\theta)$ for $\theta$ in some interval.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- Polar coordinates are not unique. The point $(r, \theta)$ is the same as $(r, \theta + 2\pi)$ and, when $r > 0$, also the same as $(-r, \theta + \pi)$. The origin has $r = 0$ with $\theta$ arbitrary.
- The formula $\theta = \arctan(y/x)$ fails when $x = 0$ (vertical line through the origin). The two-argument arctangent $\mathrm{atan2}(y, x)$ handles all cases by checking the signs of $x$ and $y$.
- A parametric curve can cross itself. The curve $t \mapsto (\cos t, \sin 2t)$ passes through the same point at different $t$-values, but a function $y = f(x)$ cannot.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (polar-rectangular equivalence).** The map $(r, \theta) \mapsto (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$ is a surjection from $[0, \infty) \times [0, 2\pi)$ onto $\mathbb{R}^2$, and is injective on $(0, \infty) \times [0, 2\pi)$. Every point $(x, y) \neq (0, 0)$ has a unique polar representation with $r > 0$ and $0 \leq \theta < 2\pi$.

*Proof.* Surjectivity: for any $(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2$, set $r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ and $\theta = \mathrm{atan2}(y, x)$ adjusted to $[0, 2\pi)$. Then $r\cos\theta = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} \cdot x/\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = x$ and $r\sin\theta = y$, verifying $(r, \theta) \mapsto (x, y)$.

Injectivity on $(0, \infty) \times [0, 2\pi)$: suppose $(r_1, \theta_1)$ and $(r_2, \theta_2)$ in this domain map to the same $(x, y)$. Then $r_1 = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = r_2$. If $r_1 = r_2 > 0$, then $\cos\theta_1 = x/r_1 = \cos\theta_2$ and $\sin\theta_1 = y/r_1 = \sin\theta_2$. Two angles in $[0, 2\pi)$ with the same sine and cosine are equal. The origin $(r = 0)$ has all $\theta$ mapping to $(0, 0)$, so injectivity fails only at $r = 0$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The polar-rectangular conversion builds toward [00.10.01] where the polar form $r = l/(1 + e\cos\theta)$ unifies the conic sections, and appears again in [00.11.02] where parametric descriptions of conics use the angular parameter. The foundational reason polar coordinates work is that the pair $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ is a unit vector, and this is exactly the content from [00.07.01] that identifies the trigonometric functions with the coordinates of a point on the unit circle. The central insight is that $(r, \theta)$ encodes the same information as $(x, y)$, and putting these together, the conversion formulas are simply the multiplication of a unit direction vector by a scalar length. The pattern generalises to cylindrical and spherical coordinates in three dimensions, and the bridge is between the rectangular and angular viewpoints on the plane.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, numeric).**

Compute the arc length of the cardioid $r = 1 + \cos\theta$ for $0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi$. Give the exact answer.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Arc length in polar: $L = \int_0^{2\pi}\sqrt{r^2 + (dr/d\theta)^2}\,d\theta$. Compute $dr/d\theta = -\sin\theta$, then simplify the integrand.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$8$. $r = 1 + \cos\theta$, $dr/d\theta = -\sin\theta$. The integrand is $\sqrt{(1 + \cos\theta)^2 + \sin^2\theta} = \sqrt{2 + 2\cos\theta} = \sqrt{4\cos^2(\theta/2)} = 2|\cos(\theta/2)|$. Over $[0, 2\pi]$: $\cos(\theta/2) \geq 0$ for $\theta \in [0, \pi]$ and $\leq 0$ for $\theta \in [\pi, 2\pi]$, so $|\cos(\theta/2)| = \cos(\theta/2)$ on $[0, \pi]$ and $-\cos(\theta/2)$ on $[\pi, 2\pi]$. ## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (polar form of conics).** Every conic section with eccentricity $e$, one focus at the origin, and directrix $x = l/e$ to the right of the origin has polar equation

$$
r = \frac{l}{1 + e\cos\theta},
$$

where $l$ is the semi-latus rectum [ref: TODO_REF Apostol — Calculus Vol. 1]. The three types correspond to $e < 1$ (ellipse), $e = 1$ (parabola), $e > 1$ (hyperbola).

**Theorem 2 (area in polar coordinates).** The area enclosed by the polar curve $r = f(\theta)$ for $\alpha \leq \theta \leq \beta$ is

$$
A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\alpha}^{\beta} [f(\theta)]^2 \, d\theta.
$$

**Theorem 3 (arc length in polar coordinates).** The arc length of the polar curve $r = f(\theta)$ for $\alpha \leq \theta \leq \beta$ is

$$
L = \int_{\alpha}^{\beta} \sqrt{r^2 + \left(\frac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2} \, d\theta.
$$

**Theorem 4 (the cycloid).** The curve traced by a point on the rim of a circle of radius $a$ rolling along the $x$-axis has parametric equations $x = a(\theta - \sin\theta)$, $y = a(1 - \cos\theta)$. The cycloid is the solution to the brachistochrone problem (Johann Bernoulli 1696): the curve of fastest descent between two points under gravity.

**Theorem 5 (rose curves).** The polar curve $r = a\cos(n\theta)$ or $r = a\sin(n\theta)$ has $n$ petals if $n$ is odd and $2n$ petals if $n$ is even. Each petal has length $a$.

**Theorem 6 (lemniscate of Bernoulli).** The polar curve $r^2 = a^2\cos(2\theta)$ is a figure-eight curve, the first curve described by its polar equation (Bernoulli 1694). Its total area is $a^2$.

**Theorem 7 (polar-rectangular equivalence as a coordinate chart).** The map $\Phi: (0, \infty) \times (0, 2\pi) \to \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{(x, 0) : x \geq 0\}$ given by $\Phi(r, \theta) = (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$ is a diffeomorphism onto its image, with Jacobian determinant $r$. The factor $r$ in the Jacobian is the foundational reason the area formula picks up a factor $r \, dr \, d\theta$ in double integrals.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason polar coordinates are effective is that the angular coordinate $\theta$ is the natural parameter for curves with radial symmetry. The central insight is that the conversion $(r, \theta) \mapsto (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$ is exactly the polar decomposition of a vector into magnitude and direction, and the bridge is between the algebraic operations on coordinates and the geometric operations of rotation and scaling. This is exactly the content from [00.07.01] where the point $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ on the unit circle represents a direction. Putting these together, the area formula $A = \frac{1}{2}\int r^2\,d\theta$ identifies the area element in polar coordinates with the Jacobian factor $r$, the arc-length formula generalises the Pythagorean theorem from [00.09.01] to curved paths, and the polar form of conics $r = l/(1 + e\cos\theta)$ unifies the three types from [00.10.01] into a single equation. The pattern recurs throughout calculus: the cycloid, the lemniscate, and the rose curves are the canonical polar curves that motivate multivariable integration and the change-of-variables theorem.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (area in polar coordinates).** The area enclosed by $r = f(\theta)$, $\alpha \leq \theta \leq \beta$, is $A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta f(\theta)^2\,d\theta$.

*Proof.* Subdivide $[\alpha, \beta]$ into $n$ subintervals of width $\Delta\theta$. The sector of the circle of radius $r = f(\theta_i^*)$ between angles $\theta_i$ and $\theta_{i+1}$ has area $\frac{1}{2}f(\theta_i^*)^2 \Delta\theta$. Summing and taking the limit gives the Riemann integral $\frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta f(\theta)^2\,d\theta$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (arc length in polar coordinates).** The arc length of $r = f(\theta)$ is $L = \int_\alpha^\beta\sqrt{f(\theta)^2 + f'(\theta)^2}\,d\theta$.

*Proof.* The parametric form is $x(\theta) = f(\theta)\cos\theta$, $y(\theta) = f(\theta)\sin\theta$. Compute $dx/d\theta = f'\cos\theta - f\sin\theta$ and $dy/d\theta = f'\sin\theta + f\cos\theta$. Then

$$
\left(\frac{dx}{d\theta}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{d\theta}\right)^2 = f'^2\cos^2\theta - 2ff'\cos\theta\sin\theta + f^2\sin^2\theta + f'^2\sin^2\theta + 2ff'\sin\theta\cos\theta + f^2\cos^2\theta = f'^2 + f^2.
$$

Taking the square root and integrating gives $L = \int_\alpha^\beta\sqrt{f^2 + f'^2}\,d\theta$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (polar form of conics).** A conic with focus at the origin, eccentricity $e$, and directrix $x = d$ (with $d > 0$) has polar equation $r = ed/(1 + e\cos\theta)$.

*Proof.* The focus-directrix condition is $d(P, F) = e \cdot d(P, \ell)$. With $F$ at the origin and the directrix the line $x = d$, a point $P = (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$ satisfies $r = e|d - r\cos\theta|$. For points with $r\cos\theta < d$: $r = e(d - r\cos\theta)$, so $r(1 + e\cos\theta) = ed$, giving $r = ed/(1 + e\cos\theta)$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Conic sections [00.10.01].** The polar form $r = l/(1 + e\cos\theta)$ unifies the three conic types from that unit into a single equation parametrised by eccentricity $e$. The polar-rectangular conversion is the tool that transforms the Cartesian standard forms (ellipse, parabola, hyperbola) into their polar counterparts. The classification by eccentricity is the same classification that governs the focus-directrix definition.

- **Unit-circle trigonometry [00.07.01].** The polar-rectangular conversion $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$ is the content from that unit scaled by $r$: the point $(\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ on the unit circle is the direction, and $r$ is the magnitude. The periodicity and parity of $\sin$ and $\cos$ govern the symmetry of polar curves.

- **Cartesian coordinates and distance [00.09.01].** The arc-length formula in polar coordinates generalises the distance formula from that unit: for a straight line $r = c\sec\theta$ the arc-length integral recovers the linear distance, and the area formula $A = \frac{1}{2}\int r^2\,d\theta$ is the polar counterpart of the Cartesian area formulas that build on the Cartesian grid.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Isaac Newton 1671 *Method of Fluxions* [ref: TODO_REF Newton 1671] introduced polar coordinates as a systematic tool for describing curves, though the idea of specifying points by distance and angle predates him in the work of Ptolemy on the stereographic projection. Jakob Bernoulli 1691, in a series of papers in *Acta Eruditorum*, developed the systematic use of polar coordinates for studying curves, including the lemniscate ($r^2 = a^2\cos(2\theta)$) and the logarithmic spiral ($r = e^{a\theta}$) [ref: TODO_REF Bernoulli 1691]. Johann Bernoulli 1696 posed the brachistochrone problem, whose solution — the cycloid — demonstrated the power of parametric curve descriptions. The area and arc-length formulas in polar coordinates were developed by Euler in his 1748 *Introductio in analysin infinitorum*, which gave the first comprehensive treatment of polar curves in the modern analytical framework.
