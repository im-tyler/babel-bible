## Intuition [Beginner]

Some equations have no real solution. The equation $x^2 + 1 = 0$ asks for a number whose square is $-1$. No real number works, because every real square is zero or positive. The number line has no answer.

The solution is to invent a new number, called $i$, with the property $i^2 = -1$. This single addition, plus the rule that arithmetic works as usual, produces the **complex numbers**. Every complex number has the form $a + bi$ where $a$ and $b$ are real. The letter $i$ is not a variable — it is a fixed symbol meaning "the number whose square is $-1$."

Complex numbers live in a plane, not on a line. The real part $a$ gives the horizontal coordinate, and the imaginary part $b$ gives the vertical coordinate. The number $3 + 2i$ sits three units right and two units up from the origin. This picture turns every algebraic operation into geometry: adding shifts the point, and multiplying rotates and stretches it.

## Visual [Beginner]

The complex plane has a horizontal axis for the real part and a vertical axis for the imaginary part. Each complex number $a + bi$ corresponds to exactly one point $(a, b)$ in this plane.

![The complex plane with axes labelled Re and Im, showing the points 3+2i, -1+4i, 2-3i, and -2-i plotted, with the unit circle drawn and the point i marked at (0,1). An arrow from the origin to 3+2i is labelled with its modulus r and angle theta.](/img/complex-numbers-introductory-placeholder.svg)

The horizontal axis holds the real numbers (where $b = 0$). The vertical axis holds the purely imaginary numbers (where $a = 0$). The distance from the origin to the point $a + bi$ is $\sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$, called the **modulus**, measured by the same distance formula used in coordinate geometry.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Add and multiply $(2 + 3i)$ and $(1 - 4i)$.

**Addition.** $(2 + 3i) + (1 - 4i) = (2 + 1) + (3 - 4)i = 3 - i$. Add real parts together, add imaginary parts together.

**Multiplication.** $(2 + 3i)(1 - 4i) = 2(1) + 2(-4i) + 3i(1) + 3i(-4i) = 2 - 8i + 3i - 12i^2$. Since $i^2 = -1$, the last term becomes $-12(-1) = 12$. So the result is $(2 + 12) + (-8 + 3)i = 14 - 5i$.

What this tells us: complex arithmetic follows the same distributive rules as real arithmetic, with the single extra rule $i^2 = -1$ handling the new symbol.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The **complex numbers** $\mathbb{C}$ are the set of ordered pairs $(a, b)$ of real numbers, equipped with addition and multiplication:

$$(a, b) + (c, d) = (a + c,\ b + d),$$

$$(a, b) \cdot (c, d) = (ac - bd,\ ad + bc).$$

The notation $a + bi$ abbreviates $(a, b)$, where $i$ denotes the pair $(0, 1)$. The multiplication rule is engineered so that $i^2 = (0, 1) \cdot (0, 1) = (-1, 0) = -1$.

**Definition.** For $z = a + bi \in \mathbb{C}$: the **real part** $\operatorname{Re}(z) = a$, the **imaginary part** $\operatorname{Im}(z) = b$, the **modulus** $|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$, and the **complex conjugate** $\bar{z} = a - bi$.

The set $\mathbb{C}$ with these operations is a field. Additive identity is $0 = 0 + 0i$; multiplicative identity is $1 = 1 + 0i$. The multiplicative inverse of $z = a + bi \neq 0$ is:

$$z^{-1} = \frac{\bar{z}}{|z|^2} = \frac{a - bi}{a^2 + b^2} = \frac{a}{a^2 + b^2} - \frac{b}{a^2 + b^2}i.$$

The key identities are: $z \bar{z} = |z|^2$, $\overline{z + w} = \bar{z} + \bar{w}$, and $\overline{zw} = \bar{z}\bar{w}$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *$i$ is not "imaginary" in the colloquial sense.* It is a well-defined algebraic object: the ordered pair $(0, 1)$ satisfying $(0,1)^2 = (-1, 0)$.
- *The imaginary part of $a + bi$ is $b$, not $bi$.* The imaginary part is a real number.
- *Complex numbers are not ordered.* There is no relation $<$ on $\mathbb{C}$ compatible with the field operations, because $i^2 = -1$ contradicts the requirement that squares be non-negative.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (fundamental theorem of algebra, statement).** Every non-constant polynomial with complex coefficients has at least one root in $\mathbb{C}$. Equivalently, every polynomial of degree $n \geq 1$ with complex coefficients factors as $a_n(z - r_1)(z - r_2) \cdots (z - r_n)$ for some $r_1, \ldots, r_n \in \mathbb{C}$.

The proof (due to Gauss, who gave four different proofs between 1799 and 1849) uses topological properties of $\mathbb{C}$ that are beyond this unit's scope. The algebraic content is that $\mathbb{C}$ is **algebraically closed**: the process of solving polynomial equations terminates inside $\mathbb{C}$.

**Theorem (de Moivre's formula).** If $z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$, then for any integer $n$:

$$z^n = r^n(\cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta).$$

*Proof.* For $n = 0$: $z^0 = 1 = r^0(\cos 0 + i\sin 0)$. For the inductive step, assume $z^n = r^n(\cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta)$. Then:

$$z^{n+1} = z^n \cdot z = r^n(\cos n\theta + i\sin n\theta) \cdot r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta).$$

Multiplying out:

$$= r^{n+1}[(\cos n\theta \cos\theta - \sin n\theta \sin\theta) + i(\cos n\theta \sin\theta + \sin n\theta \cos\theta)]$$

$$= r^{n+1}[\cos(n\theta + \theta) + i\sin(n\theta + \theta)] = r^{n+1}(\cos(n+1)\theta + i\sin(n+1)\theta)$$

by the angle-addition formulas. The case $n < 0$ follows from $z^{-n} = 1/z^n$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The foundational reason complex numbers are indispensable in mathematics is that they close the algebraic gap left by the reals: where $x^2 + 1 = 0$ has no solution in $\mathbb{R}$, the number $i$ provides one, and the fundamental theorem of algebra guarantees that no further extension is needed for polynomial equations. This is exactly the content that identifies $\mathbb{C}$ with the algebraic closure of $\mathbb{R}$. The central insight is that the two-dimensional geometry of the complex plane — modulus, argument, rotation — is the same structure as the algebra of $\mathbb{C}$, and de Moivre's formula puts these together by showing that raising to the $n$-th power is rotation by $n\theta$ and scaling by $r^n$. The bridge is between algebra and geometry: multiplication of complex numbers is geometrically a rotation and scaling, and this pattern builds toward [00.05.01] where the exponential function $e^x$ is reinterpreted as $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ (the Euler identity), and appears again in [00.03.02] where the quadratic formula produces complex roots when the discriminant is negative.

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove the **triangle inequality** for complex numbers: $|z + w| \leq |z| + |w|$ for all $z, w \in \mathbb{C}$.

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Show that the field $\mathbb{C}$ cannot be ordered. That is, there is no subset $P \subset \mathbb{C}$ satisfying: (i) for any $z \in \mathbb{C}$, exactly one of $z \in P$, $z = 0$, $-z \in P$ holds; (ii) $P$ is closed under addition and multiplication.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider $i$. If $i \in P$ or $-i \in P$, what does the multiplicative closure of $P$ force?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $i \in P$, then $i^2 = -1 \in P$ by multiplicative closure, so $(-1)(-1) = 1 \in P$ again. But also $(-1) \cdot i = -i$ must be checked. If $-i \in P$, then $(-i)^2 = (-1)^2 i^2 = i^2 = -1 \in P$ as well. In either case $-1 \in P$. But then $(-1) \in P$ and $1 = (-1)(-1) \in P$ by closure, while also $1 \in P$ from $i^2 = -1$ and $(-1)^2 = 1$. The contradiction is that $i$ and $-i$ cannot both be excluded from $P$ (by trichotomy one must be in $P$), but either choice forces $-1 \in P$, which then forces $1 \in P$ and $-1 \in P$ simultaneously, violating trichotomy.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (Euler identity).** For any real $\theta$:

$$e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta.$$

This is proved by substituting $z = i\theta$ into the power series $e^z = 1 + z + z^2/2! + z^3/3! + \cdots$ and separating real and imaginary parts: $e^{i\theta} = (1 - \theta^2/2! + \theta^4/4! - \cdots) + i(\theta - \theta^3/3! + \theta^5/5! - \cdots) = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$. The special case $\theta = \pi$ gives $e^{i\pi} = -1$, the celebrated equation linking $e$, $i$, $\pi$, $1$, and $0$.

**Theorem 2 (polar form).** Every nonzero complex number $z$ has a unique representation $z = r(\cos\theta + i\sin\theta)$ with $r = |z| > 0$ and $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$. Equivalently, $z = re^{i\theta}$.

The angle $\theta$ is the **argument** $\arg(z)$. Multiplication in polar form is $z_1 z_2 = r_1 r_2 e^{i(\theta_1 + \theta_2)}$: moduli multiply, arguments add. This is the geometric content of complex multiplication — rotation by the sum of angles and scaling by the product of lengths.

**Theorem 3 (roots of unity).** The $n$-th roots of unity are $e^{2\pi ik/n}$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$. They form a regular $n$-gon inscribed in the unit circle.

The $n$-th roots of any nonzero complex number $w = \rho e^{i\phi}$ are $\rho^{1/n} e^{i(\phi + 2\pi k)/n}$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$, evenly spaced on a circle of radius $\rho^{1/n}$.

**Theorem 4 (conjugate root theorem).** If $p(z) = a_n z^n + \cdots + a_0$ has real coefficients and $p(r) = 0$, then $p(\bar{r}) = 0$. Non-real roots of real polynomials occur in conjugate pairs.

The proof uses $\overline{p(r)} = \overline{a_n r^n + \cdots + a_0} = a_n \bar{r}^n + \cdots + a_0 = p(\bar{r})$ because the coefficients are real, so conjugation passes through.

**Theorem 5 (geometric interpretation of inversion).** The map $z \mapsto 1/z$ on $\mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}$ is the composition of reflection in the unit circle ($z \mapsto z/|z|^2$) and complex conjugation ($z \mapsto \bar{z}$). Geometrically, inversion takes a point at distance $r$ from the origin to a point at distance $1/r$ along the same ray, then reflects across the real axis.

**Theorem 6 (the complex numbers as a real algebra).** The field $\mathbb{C}$ is a two-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{R}$ with basis $\{1, i\}$. Multiplication by a fixed complex number $a + bi$ is a linear map $\mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ represented by the matrix $\begin{pmatrix} a & -b \\ b & a \end{pmatrix}$, which is a rotation through angle $\theta$ and scaling by $r = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason complex numbers unify algebra and geometry is that the algebraic structure of $\mathbb{C}$ as a field coincides with the geometric structure of the plane as a space of rotations and scalings. The central insight is that multiplication by $a + bi$ is the linear map $\begin{pmatrix} a & -b \\ b & a \end{pmatrix}$, and this is exactly the rotation-by-$\theta$-and-scale-by-$r$ transformation where $r$ and $\theta$ are the modulus and argument. Putting these together with the Euler identity, the bridge is between the exponential function and the rotation group: $e^{i\theta}$ parametrises the unit circle, de Moivre's formula generalises to $n$-th powers as $n$-fold rotations, and the $n$-th roots of unity generalise into the cyclic group of order $n$. This pattern identifies $\mathbb{C}$ with the algebraic closure of $\mathbb{R}$ and identifies the unit circle with the group of rotations in the plane. The complex plane appears again in [00.05.01] through the exponential function, in [00.03.02] through the quadratic formula's discriminant, and throughout the complex-analysis strand beginning with [06.01.01].

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 ($\mathbb{C}$ is a field).** With the addition $(a,b) + (c,d) = (a+c, b+d)$ and multiplication $(a,b)(c,d) = (ac-bd, ad+bc)$, the set $\mathbb{C} = \{(a,b) : a, b \in \mathbb{R}\}$ satisfies all field axioms.

*Proof.* Addition is commutative and associative because it is component-wise real addition. The additive identity is $(0,0)$ and additive inverses are $-(a,b) = (-a,-b)$. Multiplication is commutative because $(a,b)(c,d) = (ac-bd,ad+bc) = (ca-db,da+cb) = (c,d)(a,b)$. Associativity: $[(a,b)(c,d)](e,f) = (ac-bd,ad+bc)(e,f) = ((ac-bd)e-(ad+bc)f,(ac-bd)f+(ad+bc)e) = (ace-bde-adf-bcf,acf-bdf+ade+bce)$. Direct computation of $(a,b)[(c,d)(e,f)]$ yields $(a,b)(ce-df,cf+de) = (a(ce-df)-b(cf+de),a(cf+de)+b(ce-df)) = (ace-adf-bcf-bde,acf+ade+bce-bdf)$, which agrees. The multiplicative identity is $(1,0)$. For $(a,b) \neq (0,0)$, the inverse is $(a/(a^2+b^2), -b/(a^2+b^2))$ because $(a,b) \cdot (a/(a^2+b^2), -b/(a^2+b^2)) = ((a^2+b^2)/(a^2+b^2), (-ab+ba)/(a^2+b^2)) = (1,0)$. Distributivity is verified by expanding both sides and comparing. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (conjugate root theorem).** If $p(z) = a_n z^n + a_{n-1} z^{n-1} + \cdots + a_0$ with $a_k \in \mathbb{R}$ for all $k$, and if $p(r) = 0$ for some $r \in \mathbb{C}$, then $p(\bar{r}) = 0$.

*Proof.* For real $a$ and complex $z$: $\overline{az} = a\bar{z}$ because $a = \bar{a}$. By induction: $\overline{z^n} = \bar{z}^n$. So $\overline{p(r)} = \overline{a_n r^n + \cdots + a_0} = \bar{a}_n \overline{r^n} + \cdots + \bar{a}_0 = a_n \bar{r}^n + \cdots + a_0 = p(\bar{r})$. Since $p(r) = 0$, $\overline{p(r)} = \bar{0} = 0$, giving $p(\bar{r}) = 0$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Euler identity from power series).** The power series $e^z = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} z^k / k!$ converges for all $z \in \mathbb{C}$ and satisfies $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$.

*Proof.* For $z = i\theta$ the series is $1 + i\theta + (i\theta)^2/2! + (i\theta)^3/3! + \cdots = 1 + i\theta - \theta^2/2! - i\theta^3/3! + \theta^4/4! + i\theta^5/5! - \cdots$. Separating real and imaginary parts: $\operatorname{Re}(e^{i\theta}) = 1 - \theta^2/2! + \theta^4/4! - \cdots = \cos\theta$ and $\operatorname{Im}(e^{i\theta}) = \theta - \theta^3/3! + \theta^5/5! - \cdots = \sin\theta$. Both series converge absolutely for all $\theta$, so the rearrangement is valid. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Quadratic equations and the discriminant [00.03.02].** When the discriminant $b^2 - 4ac$ of a quadratic equation $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$ is negative, the quadratic formula $x = (-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac})/(2a)$ involves the square root of a negative number. Complex numbers make sense of this: $\sqrt{-16} = 4i$, and the two roots $-1 + 2i$ and $-1 - 2i$ are complex conjugates (by Proposition 2). The quadratic formula's failure over the reals is the historical motivation for complex numbers.

- **Real numbers and the completeness of $\mathbb{R}$ [00.01.01].** The complex numbers $\mathbb{C}$ are built from ordered pairs of real numbers, inheriting the completeness of $\mathbb{R}$ through the identification $\mathbb{C} \cong \mathbb{R}^2$. The modulus $|z| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$ uses the real square root whose existence depends on completeness, and the convergence of the power series $e^z = \sum z^k/k!$ depends on completeness of $\mathbb{R}$ to guarantee absolute convergence.

- **Real exponents and the exponential function [00.05.01].** The Euler identity $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ extends the real exponential function $e^x$ to complex arguments. Where the real exponential maps $\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}_{>0}$ along the positive real axis, the complex exponential maps $\mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}$, and its restriction to the imaginary axis traces out the unit circle. The exponential law $e^{z+w} = e^z e^w$ holds for complex $z, w$ exactly as it does for real exponents.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Bombelli 1572 *L'Algebra* [ref: TODO_REF Bombelli1572] was the first text to treat square roots of negative numbers as legitimate arithmetic objects. Bombelli encountered expressions like $\sqrt{-1}$ while applying Cardano's cubic formula to the equation $x^3 = 15x + 4$, and showed that the imaginary parts cancel to produce the real root $x = 4$. Wessel 1797 *Om directionens analytiske betegning* (read before the Royal Danish Academy) introduced the geometric representation of complex numbers as points in the plane, defining addition and multiplication geometrically. Argand 1806 *Essai sur une maniere de representer les quantites imaginaires dans les constructions geometriques* independently rediscovered the same plane representation. Hamilton 1837 *Theory of Conjugate Functions or Algebraic Couples* (Trans. Royal Irish Academy 17) gave the formal definition of $\mathbb{C}$ as ordered pairs $(a, b)$ with the multiplication rule $(a,b)(c,d) = (ac-bd, ad+bc)$, removing all mystery by grounding complex arithmetic in real-pair arithmetic with no reference to "imaginary" quantities. Gauss 1831 *Theoria residuorum biquadraticorum, Commentatio secunda* (Comment. Soc. Regiae Sci. Gottingensis 7) popularised the complex plane and coined the term "complex number," establishing the geometric viewpoint as standard.
