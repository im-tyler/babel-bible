## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Lang's *Basic Mathematics* Parts III-V build coordinate geometry, functions, and trigonometry as a single derived structure: lines and conics come from their geometric loci, the trigonometric functions from the unit circle, and the addition formulas from the rotation matrix acting on $\mathbb{R}^2$. This pack exercises that block. The problems test function composition and inverses [00.02.05], the equation of a line and slope conditions [00.03.01], right-triangle trigonometry and the Pythagorean identity [00.06.01], the unit-circle extension to all angles [00.07.01], the addition and double-angle formulas [00.08.01], the distance formula in the plane [00.09.01], and the standard-form equations of the conics [00.10.01].

The pack collects ten problems: three easy, four medium, three hard. Each carries a hint and a full solution. Angles are measured in radians by default, matching Lang's Part V convention and the unit-circle unit; where a degree value is more natural for a special triangle it is given explicitly. Lines are written in slope-intercept or point-slope form, conics in the centre-translated standard form, and functions in Lang's $f : A \to B$ notation.

The problems are meant to be worked alongside the prerequisite concept units. The recurring theme is Lang's reverse order: rather than declaring an equation and graphing it, the reader derives the equation from a geometric or analytic definition â€” a line from two points, an identity from a rotation, a conic from a focus condition â€” and then verifies the algebra. The trigonometry problems in particular lean on deriving identities rather than recalling them.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

We work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining nine follow the same structure: problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks.

**Lead exercise.** *Derive the addition formula $\cos(x + y) = \cos x\cos y - \sin x\sin y$ from the rotation matrix, then deduce the double-angle formula for $\cos 2x$.*

**Solution.** A rotation of the plane by angle $\theta$ is the linear map with matrix

$$
R_\theta = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix},
$$

read off by tracking where the basis vectors $(1, 0)$ and $(0, 1)$ land on the unit circle [00.07.01]. Rotating by $x$ and then by $y$ is the same as rotating by $x + y$, so $R_y R_x = R_{x+y}$. Multiply the matrices on the left:

$$
R_y R_x = \begin{pmatrix} \cos y & -\sin y \\ \sin y & \cos y \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} \cos x & -\sin x \\ \sin x & \cos x \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos y\cos x - \sin y\sin x & \;\cdots \\ \cdots & \cdots \end{pmatrix}.
$$

The top-left entry of $R_{x+y}$ is $\cos(x + y)$ by definition. Equating the top-left entries of the two sides gives

$$
\cos(x + y) = \cos x\cos y - \sin x\sin y.
$$

The same comparison on the bottom-left entry yields $\sin(x + y) = \sin x\cos y + \cos x\sin y$.

**Double angle.** Set $y = x$ in the cosine formula:

$$
\cos 2x = \cos x\cos x - \sin x\sin x = \cos^2 x - \sin^2 x.
$$

Using the Pythagorean identity $\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1$ [00.06.01], this also reads $\cos 2x = 2\cos^2 x - 1 = 1 - 2\sin^2 x$. $\square$

The rotation-matrix derivation is Lang's signature move: the addition formulas feel inevitable because they are just the statement that composing two rotations adds the angles. Every other identity in the pack â€” half-angle, product-to-sum, $\cos 3x$ â€” is a corollary.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).** *Line through two points.*

Find the equation of the line through $(-1, 2)$ and $(3, -4)$ in slope-intercept form, and determine where it crosses the $x$-axis.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Slope is rise over run, $m = (y_2 - y_1)/(x_2 - x_1)$. Then use point-slope form with either point.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Slope: $m = \dfrac{-4 - 2}{3 - (-1)} = \dfrac{-6}{4} = -\dfrac{3}{2}$.

Point-slope through $(-1, 2)$: $y - 2 = -\tfrac32(x + 1)$, so $y = -\tfrac32 x - \tfrac32 + 2 = -\tfrac32 x + \tfrac12$.

$x$-intercept: set $y = 0$, giving $\tfrac32 x = \tfrac12$, so $x = \tfrac13$. The line crosses the $x$-axis at $(\tfrac13, 0)$.

The two-point and point-slope forms [00.03.01] determine the line uniquely from any two of its points.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Special-angle values from a triangle.*

Without a calculator, evaluate $\sin(\pi/6)$, $\cos(\pi/6)$, and $\tan(\pi/3)$ using the $30$-$60$-$90$ triangle.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The $30$-$60$-$90$ triangle has side ratios $1 : \sqrt{3} : 2$ opposite the $30Â°, 60Â°, 90Â°$ angles. Recall $\pi/6 = 30Â°$ and $\pi/3 = 60Â°$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In a $30$-$60$-$90$ triangle with hypotenuse $2$, the side opposite $30Â°$ has length $1$ and the side opposite $60Â°$ has length $\sqrt{3}$.

- $\sin(\pi/6) = \dfrac{\text{opp}}{\text{hyp}} = \dfrac{1}{2}$.
- $\cos(\pi/6) = \dfrac{\text{adj}}{\text{hyp}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$.
- $\tan(\pi/3) = \dfrac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}} = \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{1} = \sqrt{3}$.

These are the SOH-CAH-TOA ratios [00.06.01] read directly off the special triangle, not memorised in isolation.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 3 (easy, numeric).** *Distance and midpoint.*

Find the distance between $(2, -1)$ and $(7, 11)$, and the midpoint of the segment joining them.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The distance formula is $\sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}$; the midpoint is the coordinatewise average.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Distance:

$$
\sqrt{(7 - 2)^2 + (11 - (-1))^2} = \sqrt{5^2 + 12^2} = \sqrt{25 + 144} = \sqrt{169} = 13.
$$

Midpoint: $\left(\dfrac{2 + 7}{2}, \dfrac{-1 + 11}{2}\right) = \left(\dfrac{9}{2}, 5\right)$.

The distance formula [00.09.01] is the Pythagorean theorem applied to the right triangle whose legs are the coordinate differences.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *Composition and inverse of an affine function.*

Let $f(x) = 3x - 5$ and $g(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}x + 1$. Compute $f \circ g$ and $g \circ f$, show they differ, then find $f^{-1}$ and verify $f \circ f^{-1} = \mathrm{id}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$(f \circ g)(x) = f(g(x))$ â€” substitute $g$ into $f$. To invert $f$, solve $y = 3x - 5$ for $x$ in terms of $y$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$(f \circ g)(x) = f\bigl(\tfrac12 x + 1\bigr) = 3\bigl(\tfrac12 x + 1\bigr) - 5 = \tfrac32 x - 2$.

$(g \circ f)(x) = g(3x - 5) = \tfrac12(3x - 5) + 1 = \tfrac32 x - \tfrac32$.

The two differ in their constant term ($-2$ versus $-\tfrac32$), so composition is not commutative.

**Inverse.** Solve $y = 3x - 5$: $x = (y + 5)/3$, so $f^{-1}(x) = \dfrac{x + 5}{3}$.

**Check.** $f(f^{-1}(x)) = 3\cdot\dfrac{x + 5}{3} - 5 = (x + 5) - 5 = x$. So $f \circ f^{-1} = \mathrm{id}$. âś“

Composition is associative with the identity as a two-sided unit, and an affine map with nonzero slope is a bijection, hence invertible [00.02.05].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).** *Circle from centre and radius; line intersection.*

The circle $C$ has centre $(1, -2)$ and radius $5$. Write its equation, then find the points where the line $y = x + 1$ meets $C$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The circle is $(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2$. Substitute the line into the circle equation and solve the resulting quadratic in $x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Circle: $(x - 1)^2 + (y + 2)^2 = 25$.

Substitute $y = x + 1$, so $y + 2 = x + 3$:

$$
(x - 1)^2 + (x + 3)^2 = 25.
$$

Expand: $(x^2 - 2x + 1) + (x^2 + 6x + 9) = 25$, i.e. $2x^2 + 4x + 10 = 25$, so $2x^2 + 4x - 15 = 0$. The quadratic formula gives

$$
x = \frac{-4 \pm \sqrt{16 + 120}}{4} = \frac{-4 \pm \sqrt{136}}{4} = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{34}}{2}.
$$

The two intersection points are $\bigl(\tfrac{-2 + \sqrt{34}}{2},\, \tfrac{\sqrt{34}}{2}\bigr)$ and $\bigl(\tfrac{-2 - \sqrt{34}}{2},\, -\tfrac{\sqrt{34}}{2}\bigr)$, taking $y = x + 1$. The positive discriminant confirms the line is a secant, cutting $C$ in two points [00.10.01].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).** *Derive $\cos 3x$.*

Express $\cos 3x$ as a polynomial in $\cos x$, using the addition and double-angle formulas.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $3x = 2x + x$, apply the cosine addition formula, then substitute the double-angle expressions for $\cos 2x$ and $\sin 2x$, and clear $\sin^2 x = 1 - \cos^2 x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the addition formula, $\cos 3x = \cos(2x + x) = \cos 2x\cos x - \sin 2x\sin x$.

Substitute $\cos 2x = 2\cos^2 x - 1$ and $\sin 2x = 2\sin x\cos x$:

$$
\cos 3x = (2\cos^2 x - 1)\cos x - (2\sin x\cos x)\sin x = 2\cos^3 x - \cos x - 2\sin^2 x\cos x.
$$

Replace $\sin^2 x = 1 - \cos^2 x$:

$$
\cos 3x = 2\cos^3 x - \cos x - 2(1 - \cos^2 x)\cos x = 2\cos^3 x - \cos x - 2\cos x + 2\cos^3 x.
$$

Collect: $\cos 3x = 4\cos^3 x - 3\cos x$.

This is the cubic Chebyshev relation, derived purely from the addition formulas [00.08.01] and the Pythagorean identity.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, numeric).** *Solving a trigonometric equation on the circle.*

Find all $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$ with $2\sin^2\theta - \sin\theta - 1 = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Treat it as a quadratic in $u = \sin\theta$. Solve for $u$, then find the angles on the unit circle giving each value.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $u = \sin\theta$. Then $2u^2 - u - 1 = 0$ factors as $(2u + 1)(u - 1) = 0$, so $u = 1$ or $u = -\tfrac12$.

- $\sin\theta = 1$: on $[0, 2\pi)$ this is $\theta = \pi/2$.
- $\sin\theta = -\tfrac12$: the reference angle is $\pi/6$, and sine is negative in the third and fourth quadrants, giving $\theta = \pi + \pi/6 = 7\pi/6$ and $\theta = 2\pi - \pi/6 = 11\pi/6$.

Solutions: $\theta \in \{\pi/2,\ 7\pi/6,\ 11\pi/6\}$.

The unit-circle reading of $\sin\theta = -\tfrac12$ [00.07.01] supplies both quadrant solutions, which a right-triangle-only view would miss.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).** *Ellipse from the focus-sum definition.*

Derive the standard equation of the ellipse defined as the locus of points whose distances to the two foci $(\pm c, 0)$ sum to the constant $2a$ (with $a > c > 0$). Identify the semi-minor axis $b$ and the eccentricity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write the distance-sum condition, isolate one radical, square, simplify, isolate the remaining radical, and square again. The combination $a^2 - c^2$ will appear; name it $b^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A point $(x, y)$ on the ellipse satisfies $\sqrt{(x + c)^2 + y^2} + \sqrt{(x - c)^2 + y^2} = 2a$.

Isolate and square the first radical: $\sqrt{(x + c)^2 + y^2} = 2a - \sqrt{(x - c)^2 + y^2}$. Squaring both sides,

$$
(x + c)^2 + y^2 = 4a^2 - 4a\sqrt{(x - c)^2 + y^2} + (x - c)^2 + y^2.
$$

The $(x \pm c)^2$ terms expand to $x^2 \pm 2cx + c^2$; subtracting, the left minus the corresponding right gives $4cx$. So $4cx = 4a^2 - 4a\sqrt{(x - c)^2 + y^2}$, i.e. $a\sqrt{(x - c)^2 + y^2} = a^2 - cx$. Square again:

$$
a^2\bigl[(x - c)^2 + y^2\bigr] = a^4 - 2a^2 cx + c^2 x^2.
$$

Expand the left side and cancel the $-2a^2 cx$ terms that appear on both sides:

$$
a^2 x^2 - a^2 c^2 + a^2 c^2 \;+\; a^2 y^2 = a^4 + c^2 x^2 - a^2 c^2 \;\;\Rightarrow\;\; (a^2 - c^2)x^2 + a^2 y^2 = a^2(a^2 - c^2).
$$

Set $b^2 = a^2 - c^2 > 0$ and divide by $a^2 b^2$:

$$
\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1.
$$

The eccentricity is $e = c/a \in (0, 1)$, and $b = \sqrt{a^2 - c^2}$ is the semi-minor axis. The derive-then-verify order is Lang's [00.10.01]: the geometric locus comes first, the algebra confirms it.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *Perpendicular slopes multiply to $-1$.*

Prove that two non-vertical lines with slopes $m_1$ and $m_2$ are perpendicular if and only if $m_1 m_2 = -1$. Use the distance formula and the converse of the Pythagorean theorem.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Translate so the lines cross at the origin. Take the points $(1, m_1)$ and $(1, m_2)$ on them; the lines are perpendicular iff the triangle with vertices $O, (1, m_1), (1, m_2)$ has its right angle at $O$, i.e. iff the Pythagorean relation holds on the three squared distances.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Place the intersection at the origin, so the lines are $y = m_1 x$ and $y = m_2 x$. Pick $P_1 = (1, m_1)$ and $P_2 = (1, m_2)$, one on each line. The lines are perpendicular exactly when the angle at $O$ in triangle $OP_1P_2$ is a right angle, which by the converse of the Pythagorean theorem holds iff

$$
|OP_1|^2 + |OP_2|^2 = |P_1 P_2|^2.
$$

Compute the squared distances [00.09.01]: $|OP_1|^2 = 1 + m_1^2$, $|OP_2|^2 = 1 + m_2^2$, and $|P_1 P_2|^2 = (1 - 1)^2 + (m_1 - m_2)^2 = m_1^2 - 2m_1 m_2 + m_2^2$.

The Pythagorean condition becomes

$$
(1 + m_1^2) + (1 + m_2^2) = m_1^2 - 2m_1 m_2 + m_2^2,
$$

i.e. $2 = -2 m_1 m_2$, i.e. $m_1 m_2 = -1$. Each step is an equivalence, so perpendicularity holds iff $m_1 m_2 = -1$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 10 (hard, symbolic).** *Sum-to-product from the addition formulas.*

Derive the identity $\sin A + \sin B = 2\sin\!\left(\dfrac{A + B}{2}\right)\cos\!\left(\dfrac{A - B}{2}\right)$, and use it to express $\sin 75Â° + \sin 15Â°$ in closed form.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set $A = s + t$ and $B = s - t$, so $s = (A+B)/2$ and $t = (A-B)/2$. Add the sine addition formulas for $\sin(s + t)$ and $\sin(s - t)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $s = (A + B)/2$ and $t = (A - B)/2$, so $A = s + t$ and $B = s - t$. By the sine addition formula [00.08.01],

$$
\sin(s + t) = \sin s\cos t + \cos s\sin t, \qquad \sin(s - t) = \sin s\cos t - \cos s\sin t.
$$

Adding, the $\cos s\sin t$ terms cancel:

$$
\sin A + \sin B = \sin(s+t) + \sin(s-t) = 2\sin s\cos t = 2\sin\!\left(\frac{A+B}{2}\right)\cos\!\left(\frac{A-B}{2}\right).
$$

**Application.** With $A = 75Â°$, $B = 15Â°$: $(A + B)/2 = 45Â°$ and $(A - B)/2 = 30Â°$, so

$$
\sin 75Â° + \sin 15Â° = 2\sin 45Â°\cos 30Â° = 2\cdot\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\cdot\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} = \frac{\sqrt{6}}{2}.
$$

The sum-to-product identities are corollaries of the addition formulas, obtained by adding or subtracting the $\pm t$ versions â€” Lang's derived-not-memorised treatment.

</details>

</aside>
