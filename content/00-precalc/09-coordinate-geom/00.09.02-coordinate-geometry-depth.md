## Intuition [Beginner]

The coordinate plane turns geometry into algebra. Every point becomes a pair of numbers $(x, y)$. A straight line becomes an equation you can write down and solve. A circle becomes an equation too. Shape questions — how far apart, do they meet, are they parallel — become number questions you handle with arithmetic.

Distance comes from a right triangle. The horizontal gap between two points is one leg and the vertical gap is the other. The straight-line distance is the hypotenuse, so Pythagoras gives it. A line's steepness is its slope, the ratio of rise to run. Two lines are parallel when their slopes match and perpendicular when their slopes multiply to $-1$.

A circle is every point at a fixed distance (the radius) from a center. Writing that condition as an equation produces the circle form. Once lines and circles both have equations, you can answer geometric questions by algebra: solving the equations together tells you whether and where the shapes meet.

## Visual [Beginner]

| Object | Geometric description | Equation |
|---|---|---|
| Distance | hypotenuse of the gap right triangle | $d = \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2}$ |
| Line | constant slope between any two of its points | $y = mx + b$ |
| Perpendicular lines | slopes multiply to $-1$ | $m_1 m_2 = -1$ |
| Circle | all points at distance $r$ from $(h, k)$ | $(x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2$ |

![A coordinate grid showing two points joined by a right-triangle hypotenuse, a line with slope marked as rise over run, and a circle centered at (2,-1) with radius 3.](/img/coordinate-geometry-depth-placeholder.svg)

The pattern is the same throughout: a geometric condition (fixed distance, fixed slope, fixed direction) becomes an algebraic equation. Reading the equation tells you the shape and reading the shape tells you the equation.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Example 1 — distance.** Find the distance between $A = (1, 2)$ and $B = (4, 6)$.

Horizontal gap: $4 - 1 = 3$. Vertical gap: $6 - 2 = 4$. These are the legs. The distance is the hypotenuse:

$$\sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5.$$

**Example 2 — a line.** Find the equation of the line through $(1, 2)$ and $(3, 8)$.

Slope is rise over run: $m = (8 - 2)/(3 - 1) = 6/2 = 3$. Using point-slope through $(1, 2)$: $y - 2 = 3(x - 1)$. Solving for $y$ gives $y = 3x - 1$. The slope is $3$ and the $y$-intercept is $-1$.

**Example 3 — a circle.** Write the equation of the circle centered at $(2, -1)$ with radius $3$.

Every point $(x, y)$ on the circle is distance $3$ from $(2, -1)$. Squaring the distance condition gives $(x - 2)^2 + (y + 1)^2 = 9$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $P_1 = (x_1, y_1)$ and $P_2 = (x_2, y_2)$ are points in $\mathbb{R}^2$ [ref: TODO_REF Stewart — Precalculus Ch. 1].

**Distance and midpoint.** The straight-line distance is

$$
d(P_1, P_2) = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2},
$$

and the midpoint of the segment $P_1 P_2$ is

$$
M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2},\; \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right).
$$

**Slope.** For $x_1 \neq x_2$ the slope of the line through $P_1$ and $P_2$ is $m = (y_2 - y_1)/(x_2 - x_1)$. A vertical line has undefined slope.

**Line equations — four interchangeable forms.**

- *Slope-intercept:* $y = mx + b$, with slope $m$ and $y$-intercept $b$.
- *Point-slope:* $y - y_0 = m(x - x_0)$, through $(x_0, y_0)$ with slope $m$.
- *Two-point:* $y - y_1 = \dfrac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}(x - x_1)$ for $x_1 \neq x_2$.
- *General:* $Ax + By + C = 0$ with $(A, B) \neq (0, 0)$. When $B \neq 0$ this is $y = (-A/B)x + (-C/B)$.

Converting between forms is mechanical: expand point-slope to read off slope and intercept, or collect terms of $y = mx + b$ into $Ax + By + C = 0$. The general form uniquely handles vertical lines ($B = 0$), which the slope-intercept form cannot write.

**Parallel and perpendicular.** Two non-vertical lines with slopes $m_1, m_2$ are parallel iff $m_1 = m_2$ and perpendicular iff $m_1 m_2 = -1$. A vertical line is perpendicular to a horizontal line.

**Circle as a locus.** The circle with center $C = (h, k)$ and radius $r > 0$ is the set of points at distance $r$ from $C$:

$$
(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2.
$$

Expanding gives the *general second-degree circle equation* $x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$ (the coefficients of $x^2$ and $y^2$ are equal and there is no $xy$ term). Completing the square recovers the center and radius:

$$
x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \left(x + \tfrac{D}{2}\right)^2 + \left(y + \tfrac{E}{2}\right)^2 = \tfrac{D^2 + E^2}{4} - F.
$$

The equation represents a genuine circle iff $\tfrac{D^2 + E^2}{4} - F > 0$, a single point iff it equals $0$, and the empty set iff it is negative.

### Converting between descriptions and equations

- *Three points $\to$ circle:* substituting each point into $x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$ yields three linear equations in $D, E, F$.
- *Center and tangent line $\to$ circle:* if a line is tangent to the circle, the distance from the center to the line equals the radius.
- *Diameter $\to$ circle:* for endpoints $A, B$, the center is the midpoint of $AB$ and the radius is $d(A, B)/2$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+] {#key-theorem}

**Theorem (distance formula, Pythagorean origin).** For $P_1 = (x_1, y_1)$ and $P_2 = (x_2, y_2)$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$,

$$
d(P_1, P_2) = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}.
$$

*Proof.* Form the right triangle with vertices $P_1$, $P_2$, and the corner $Q = (x_2, y_1)$ whose sides run parallel to the axes. The leg $P_1 Q$ is horizontal with length $|x_2 - x_1|$ and the leg $Q P_2$ is vertical with length $|y_2 - y_1|$. The segment $P_1 P_2$ is the hypotenuse. By the Pythagorean theorem,

$$
d(P_1, P_2)^2 = |x_2 - x_1|^2 + |y_2 - y_1|^2 = (x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2,
$$

since $|a|^2 = a^2$ for every real $a$. Taking the non-negative square root gives the distance formula. $\square$

**Corollary (circle equation).** The locus of points at distance $r$ from $(h, k)$ is $(x - h)^2 + (y - k)^2 = r^2$, because substituting the distance formula into $d((x, y), (h, k)) = r$ and squaring produces exactly this equation.

**Bridge.** The distance formula builds toward the whole circle-and-line machinery of this unit, and the same Pythagorean sum-of-squares appears again in [00.06.01] as the identity underlying right-triangle trigonometry and in [00.10.01] as the metric that defines conic sections by focus-directrix conditions. This is exactly the construction that promotes one-dimensional absolute-value distance [00.03.02] into the Euclidean metric on the plane, and the central insight is that squaring the distance kills the absolute values and turns a geometric locus into a polynomial equation. The bridge is between measurement (geometry) and equations (algebra): every planar locus becomes an algebraic condition on coordinate pairs, and the pattern generalises to the distance on $\mathbb{R}^n$ and to the complex modulus on $\mathbb{C}$ in [00.14.01]. Putting these together, the Pythagorean theorem is the foundational reason coordinate geometry works at all.

Prove that the perpendicular bisectors of the three sides of a triangle meet at a single point (the circumcenter), and that this point is the center of the circle through the three vertices.

## Advanced results [Master]

The intermediate results specialise a coordinate framework in which lines and circles are solution sets of polynomial equations of degree one and two. The master-tier results handle the general second-degree circle, the distance from a point to a line, tangency, and the radical axis of two circles.

**Theorem 1 (general second-degree circle).** An equation $x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$ represents a circle with center $(-D/2, -E/2)$ and radius $\sqrt{(D^2 + E^2)/4 - F}$ when $(D^2 + E^2)/4 - F > 0$; a single point when it is $0$; and the empty set when it is negative. The proof is completing the square in $x$ and in $y$.

**Theorem 2 (distance from a point to a line).** The distance from $(x_0, y_0)$ to the line $Ax + By + C = 0$ is

$$
d = \frac{|A x_0 + B y_0 + C|}{\sqrt{A^2 + B^2}}.
$$

The formula is invariant under scaling $(A, B, C)$ by a nonzero constant, so it depends only on the line.

**Theorem 3 (tangency criterion).** A line $L$ is tangent to the circle with center $C$ and radius $r$ iff $d(C, L) = r$. Equivalently the system of the line and circle equations has exactly one solution. The proof inserts the parameterisation of $L$ into the circle equation and observes that the resulting quadratic has a repeated root exactly when its discriminant vanishes, which is the same condition as $d(C, L) = r$.

**Theorem 4 (radical axis).** Given two non-concentric circles $S_1: x^2 + y^2 + D_1 x + E_1 y + F_1 = 0$ and $S_2: x^2 + y^2 + D_2 x + E_2 y + F_2 = 0$, the set of points with equal power with respect to both circles is the line $(D_1 - D_2)x + (E_1 - E_2)y + (F_1 - F_2) = 0$. Subtracting the equations cancels the quadratic terms, leaving a linear locus.

**Theorem 5 (reflection across a line).** The reflection of $(x_0, y_0)$ across $Ax + By + C = 0$ is

$$
(x_0, y_0) - \frac{2(A x_0 + B y_0 + C)}{A^2 + B^2}\,(A, B).
$$

Reflections preserve distance (they are orthogonal transformations), so the circle and its reflected image have equal radii.

**Synthesis.** The distance formula is the foundational reason every geometric locus in this unit admits an algebraic equation, and this is exactly the identification Descartes made between curves and equations. The bridge is between the metric structure of the plane and the algebra of polynomials: lines are degree-one loci, circles are degree-two loci, and tangency is the algebraic condition that a line and a circle share a single point. The general-circle formula generalises to arbitrary conic sections in [00.10.01], where completing the square becomes classifying a general second-degree curve, and the point-to-line distance generalises to the projection onto an affine subspace. Putting these together, the perpendicular-slope, tangency, and radical-axis results are all instances of one pattern: a geometric relation among points becomes the vanishing or equality of an algebraic expression, and the central insight is that the coordinates make the translation automatic.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (perpendicular slope condition).** Two non-vertical lines with slopes $m_1$ and $m_2$ are perpendicular iff $m_1 m_2 = -1$.

*Proof.* A line of slope $m$ has direction vector $(1, m)$. For two lines through a common point with slopes $m_1, m_2$ the direction vectors are $v_1 = (1, m_1)$ and $v_2 = (1, m_2)$, with dot product

$$
v_1 \cdot v_2 = 1 + m_1 m_2.
$$

Two vectors are perpendicular iff their dot product vanishes, giving $1 + m_1 m_2 = 0$, i.e. $m_1 m_2 = -1$. Translation does not change slope, so the same condition holds for lines not through a common point. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (general circle via completing the square).** For real $D, E, F$,

$$
x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0 \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \left(x + \tfrac{D}{2}\right)^2 + \left(y + \tfrac{E}{2}\right)^2 = \tfrac{D^2 + E^2}{4} - F.
$$

*Proof.* Add $D^2/4$ and $E^2/4$ to both sides of $x^2 + Dx + y^2 + Ey = -F$. The left becomes a sum of two perfect squares:

$$
\left(x^2 + Dx + \tfrac{D^2}{4}\right) + \left(y^2 + Ey + \tfrac{E^2}{4}\right) = \tfrac{D^2}{4} + \tfrac{E^2}{4} - F,
$$

i.e. $(x + D/2)^2 + (y + E/2)^2 = (D^2 + E^2)/4 - F$. Reading off the radius requires the right-hand side to be positive; it is $0$ for a point-circle and negative for the empty set. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (distance from a point to a line).** The distance from $(x_0, y_0)$ to $Ax + By + C = 0$ is $|A x_0 + B y_0 + C|/\sqrt{A^2 + B^2}$.

*Proof.* The vector $n = (A, B)$ is normal to the line. Parametrise the perpendicular through $(x_0, y_0)$ as $(x, y) = (x_0 + At, y_0 + Bt)$. Substituting into $Ax + By + C = 0$ gives

$$
A(x_0 + At) + B(y_0 + Bt) + C = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; (A^2 + B^2) t = -(A x_0 + B y_0 + C),
$$

so $t = -(A x_0 + B y_0 + C)/(A^2 + B^2)$. The distance from $(x_0, y_0)$ to the foot of the perpendicular is $|t| \cdot \|(A, B)\|$, hence

$$
d = \frac{|A x_0 + B y_0 + C|}{A^2 + B^2} \cdot \sqrt{A^2 + B^2} = \frac{|A x_0 + B y_0 + C|}{\sqrt{A^2 + B^2}}. \qquad \square
$$

## Connections [Master]

- **Cartesian coordinates and distance [00.09.01].** This unit deepens the coordinate-and-distance foundation of [00.09.01] by adding the full line-equation family (four interchangeable forms with conversions), the perpendicular-slope relation, and the circle as a locus. The distance formula proved there becomes the load-bearing tool here: the circle equation is the distance formula squared, and the midpoint becomes the center of a diameter-defined circle.

- **Linear equations and the line [00.03.01].** The general line $Ax + By + C = 0$ studied abstractly in [00.03.01] acquires geometric content here through slope, intercepts, and the parallel-perpendicular classification. Solving a $2 \times 2$ linear system — the core skill of that unit — is exactly the tool used to find where a line meets a circle or where two perpendicular bisectors cross.

- **Quadratic equations and completing the square [00.03.02].** Recovering the center and radius from $x^2 + y^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$ runs the completing-the-square technique of [00.03.02] twice, once per variable. The sign of the constant that remains decides whether the locus is a circle, a point, or empty, paralleling how the discriminant there decides the number of real roots.

- **Conic sections [00.10.01].** The circle is the special case of an ellipse with coincident foci, and the focus-directrix definition of conics in [00.10.01] uses the same distance formula developed here. Completing the square in two variables, the central technique of this unit, is the entry point to classifying the general second-degree curve as parabola, ellipse, or hyperbola.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

René Descartes published *La Géométrie* in 1637 as an appendix to the *Discours de la méthode* [ref: TODO_REF Descartes 1637], introducing the systematic correspondence between algebraic equations and geometric curves. By assigning numerical coordinates to points, Descartes showed that a curve could be studied through its equation: the distance and locus problems of classical Euclidean geometry became algebraic manipulations. Pierre de Fermat, working independently in the 1636 manuscript *Ad Locos Planos et Solidos Isagoge* (published posthumously in 1679) [ref: TODO_REF Fermat 1636], arrived at the same correspondence from the opposite direction, starting from geometric locus conditions and writing the equations they imply.

The two inventions differ in emphasis and complement each other. Descartes proceeds from equations to curves, using algebraic operations (including what we now call completing the square) to construct and classify curves. Fermat proceeds from geometric conditions — fixed distance from a point (a circle), fixed ratio of distances (a conic) — to the equations encoding them, which is precisely the locus viewpoint that defines the circle in this unit. The modern formulation of $\mathbb{R}^2$ as a metric space, in which the distance formula is the canonical example of a metric and the circle is a metric sphere, crystallised with Fréchet's 1906 *Sur quelques points du calcul fonctionnel* and the twentieth-century development of normed vector spaces.
