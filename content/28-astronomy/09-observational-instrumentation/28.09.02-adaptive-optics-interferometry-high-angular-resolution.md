## Intuition [Beginner]

A 10-metre telescope ought to see fine detail. Its theoretical sharpness at visible light is about a hundredth of an arcsecond, fine enough to resolve a coin seen from across a city. But point it at a star from the ground and the image smears into a blob about half an arcsecond across. The atmosphere, not the mirror, is the bottleneck. Moving pockets of air bend the incoming light thousands of times a second, scrambling the wavefront long before it reaches the detector. Astronomers call this scrambling *seeing*, and for three centuries it capped every ground-based telescope at roughly the same fuzzy resolution as Galileo's instrument.

Adaptive optics fixes this by measuring the distortion and undoing it in real time. A thin flexible mirror, driven by hundreds of tiny pistons, is reshaped about a thousand times a second into precisely the form that cancels the atmospheric warp. The wavefront arrives at the science camera straightened, and the telescope performs near its true diffraction limit. The mirror becomes an active optical element rather than a passive piece of glass.

There is a second, deeper trick. To resolve ever finer angles you need a wider aperture, but a single mirror can only be built so large. Interferometry gets around this by combining the light from two or more separated telescopes. The resolving power is then set not by the size of each dish but by the distance between them, the *baseline*. Two telescopes on opposite sides of a mountain, or on opposite sides of the Earth, behave like a single dish as wide as their separation. That is how astronomers photographed the shadow of a black hole.

## Worked example [Beginner]

### Example 1: The diffraction limit a 10-metre telescope ought to reach

The diffraction limit of a circular aperture is $\theta = 1.22\,\lambda/D$. Take a 10-metre mirror observing at $\lambda = 500\,\text{nm} = 5.0\times 10^{-7}\,\text{m}$.

Then $\theta = 1.22 \times 5.0\times 10^{-7} / 10 = 6.1\times 10^{-8}$ radians. There are 206,265 arcseconds in a radian, so $\theta \approx 6.1\times 10^{-8} \times 206{,}265 \approx 0.013$ arcseconds. That is the theoretical sharpness.

Typical ground-based seeing is about 0.5 arcseconds, roughly forty times worse. The atmosphere, not the mirror, throws away the detail. Adaptive optics exists to give that factor of forty back.

### Example 2: The Event Horizon Telescope at the size of the Earth

In interferometry the resolution is set by the baseline, $\theta \approx \lambda/B$, without the 1.22 factor. The Event Horizon Telescope observes at $\lambda = 1.3\,\text{mm} = 1.3\times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}$ and uses baselines comparable to the diameter of the Earth, $B \approx 12{,}700\,\text{km} = 1.27\times 10^{7}\,\text{m}$.

Then $\theta = 1.3\times 10^{-3}/1.27\times 10^{7} \approx 1.0\times 10^{-10}$ radians. Converting to arcseconds gives $1.0\times 10^{-10} \times 206{,}265 \approx 2.1\times 10^{-5}$ arcseconds, or about 21 microarcseconds.

That is sharp enough to resolve the shadow of the supermassive black hole in the galaxy M87 from 54 million light-years away. No single dish that size could ever be built; only an array spanning the planet reaches it.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### The Fried parameter and the seeing limit

The atmosphere is modelled as a Kolmogorov turbulence field whose refractive-index fluctuations accumulate along the line of sight. The *Fried parameter* $r_0$ is the aperture diameter over which the root-mean-square wavefront error accumulates to approximately one radian [ref: TODO_REF Fried 1966]. It scales with wavelength as

$$r_0(\lambda) \;\propto\; \lambda^{6/5},$$

so $r_0 \approx 10$-20 cm at $\lambda = 500\,\text{nm}$ at a good site, growing to roughly 1 m in the near-infrared $K$ band. A telescope of diameter $D \gg r_0$ is *seeing-limited* rather than diffraction-limited: its long-exposure angular resolution is

$$\theta_{\text{seeing}} \;\approx\; 0.98\,\frac{\lambda}{r_0} \;\approx\; \frac{\lambda}{r_0},$$

independent of $D$. A 10-metre telescope in 0.5 arcsecond seeing delivers the same angular resolution as a 1-metre telescope in the same air. Closing the gap between $\lambda/r_0$ and $\lambda/D$ is the entire purpose of adaptive optics.

### Adaptive-optics correction, Strehl ratio, and isoplanatic angle

An adaptive-optics system estimates the incoming wavefront $\varphi(\mathbf{x},t)$ and imprints its conjugate on a deformable mirror with $N$ actuators updated at loop frequency $f_{\text{loop}}$ (typically $\sim$1 kHz). The *Strehl ratio* $S$ is the ratio of the peak intensity of the delivered point-spread function to the peak of the ideal diffraction-limited pattern. For residual rms wavefront error $\sigma$ the *Marechal approximation* gives

$$S \;\approx\; \exp\!\left[-(2\pi\sigma/\lambda)^2\right].$$

A Strehl ratio $S > 0.8$ is regarded as essentially diffraction-limited. The *isoplanatic angle* $\theta_0$ is the angular radius over which the AO correction remains valid; it scales as $\theta_0 \approx 0.314\, r_0/h$ where $h$ is a characteristic turbulence altitude of a few kilometres, giving $\theta_0$ of a few arcseconds in the near-infrared.

### Mutual coherence and the complex visibility

Let $E_1(t)$ and $E_2(t)$ be the complex analytic electric fields at two telescopes separated by the baseline vector $\mathbf{B}$. The *mutual coherence* is the time-averaged correlation

$$\Gamma_{12}(\mathbf{B}) \;=\; \langle E_1(t)\,E_2^{*}(t)\rangle.$$

Normalising by the product of the single-station intensities yields the *complex visibility* $V = \Gamma_{12}/\sqrt{\Gamma_{11}\Gamma_{22}}$, with modulus $|V|\in[0,1]$ and phase $\arg V$. The baseline in dimensionless spatial-frequency units is $\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{B}/\lambda$, measured in wavelengths.

### Closure phase and very-long-baseline interferometry

For a triangle of telescopes $(1,2,3)$, each baseline $(i,j)$ measures a visibility phase that is the sum of the true object phase $\phi_{ij}$ and an atmospheric *piston* error $\alpha_i-\alpha_j$ at the two stations. The *closure phase*

$$\Psi_{123} \;=\; \arg V_{12} + \arg V_{23} + \arg V_{31}$$

is invariant under the per-station pistons $\alpha_i$ [ref: TODO_REF Jennison 1958]. In very-long-baseline interferometry (VLBI) the stations are too far apart to combine light in real time; each records its signal against a hydrogen-maser local clock onto disk, and the baselines are cross-correlated later at a central processor.

## Key result [Intermediate+]

Two results organise high-angular-resolution astronomy, one for each technique. Both reduce the problem of resolving power to a single length scale.

**Seeing limit and its removal.** Over an aperture of diameter $D$ the turbulence-induced phase error accumulates coherently only up to the scale $r_0$. The long-exposure seeing disk therefore has angular radius $\theta_{\text{seeing}} \approx \lambda/r_0$, independent of how large the mirror is. The diffraction limit $\theta_{\text{diff}} = 1.22\lambda/D$ is recovered only when the residual phase error after correction satisfies $\sigma \ll \lambda/(2\pi)$, which by the Marechal relation corresponds to $S \to 1$. Adaptive optics drives $\sigma$ down by measuring the wavefront with a Shack-Hartmann sensor (a lenslet array that images the guide star onto many sub-apertures, each giving a local wavefront tilt) and commanding a deformable mirror to subtract the measured phase. With $N$ actuators across $D$ the fitting-limited residual is $\sigma_{\text{fit}}\sim (r_0/D)^{5/3}\lambda/(2\pi)$, so for $D/r_0\sim 50$ (an 8-metre telescope in $r_0=15$ cm seeing) one needs $N$ of several hundred actuators and a loop bandwidth of a few hundred hertz to track the atmospheric coherence time $\tau_0\sim r_0/v_{\text{wind}}$. Laser guide stars, exciting sodium in the mesosphere at 90 km altitude with 589 nm light, supply a reference beacon anywhere on the sky and lift the natural-guide-star sky-coverage limit from a few percent to nearly all of it.

**Interferometric resolution.** When the light from two telescopes separated by $\mathbf{B}$ is combined, the measured complex visibility is the two-dimensional Fourier transform of the source's brightness distribution evaluated at spatial frequency $\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{B}/\lambda$. A source of angular extent $\theta_s$ produces a visibility that first falls to zero at $|\mathbf{u}|\sim 1/\theta_s$, hence at baseline $B\sim \lambda/\theta_s$. The smallest angle the array resolves is therefore

$$\theta_{\text{int}} \;\sim\; \frac{\lambda}{B}.$$

This is the Van Cittert-Zernike theorem in its observational form: resolution is set by the longest baseline, not by the individual dish. VLTI and CHARA reach milliarcsecond resolution at optical and near-infrared wavelengths, resolving stellar surfaces and the orbits of hot stars about the Galactic Centre; ALMA reaches a few milliarcseconds in the submillimetre; the Event Horizon Telescope reaches $\sim 20\,\mu\text{as}$ at 1.3 mm with Earth-diameter baselines [ref: TODO_REF EHT 2019].

**Bridge.** The result that resolution is set by the longest dimension of the aperture builds toward every high-resolution observation in modern astronomy, and the diffraction-limited scaling $\theta\sim\lambda/D$ appears again in the radio interferometers that mapped the cosmic microwave background in [28.04.03] and the X-ray and gamma-ray instruments of [28.08.01]. The foundational reason a single mirror and an interferometric array share the same scaling law is that both measure Fourier components of the source brightness: replacing the diameter $D$ with the baseline $B$ generalises the Rayleigh criterion to synthesised apertures spanning a mountain, a continent, or the Earth, and this is exactly the duality that lets a planet-spanning array resolve a black-hole shadow twenty microarcseconds across. Putting these together, the bridge is that adaptive optics recovers the single-dish diffraction limit beneath the atmosphere while interferometry extends that limit without bound.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Error budgets, speckle, and curvature sensing

The residual wavefront error of an adaptive-optics system decomposes in quadrature into four terms [ref: TODO_REF Roddier 1999]. The *fitting error* falls as $(D/r_0)^{5/3}/N^{5/6}$ in the phase variance, setting the actuator count needed for a given Strehl. The *temporal error* scales as $(f_{\text{turb}}/f_{\text{loop}})^{5/3}$, where $f_{\text{turb}}\sim v_{\text{wind}}/r_0$ is the Greenwood frequency, so loop bandwidths of several hundred hertz are required at optical wavelengths and relax in the infrared. The *measurement error* is set by the guide-star photon rate per sub-aperture per frame and grows as the square root of the source brightness. The *anisoplanatism error* grows as $(\theta/\theta_0)^{5/3}$ with angular distance from the guide star, the fundamental limit on the corrected field of view that motivates multi-conjugate and ground-layer AO systems deploying several deformable mirrors optically conjugate to different turbulence altitudes.

Before real-time AO was practical, Labeyrie's stellar speckle interferometry recovered diffraction-limited information by a statistical route [ref: TODO_REF Labeyrie 1970]. A short exposure, shorter than the atmospheric coherence time, freezes the turbulence into a pattern of fine speckles, each speckle as small as the diffraction limit of the full aperture. Averaging the squared modulus of the Fourier transform of many such exposures yields the modulus of the source's visibility function up to the diffraction cutoff $D/\lambda$. Speckle interferometry gave the first diffraction-limited measurements from large telescopes and remains the conceptual bridge between the long-exposure seeing limit and full wavefront correction.

Roddier's curvature-sensing architecture measures the wavefront's Laplacian, the local curvature, by imaging the pupil at equal distances on either side of focus and taking the intensity difference [ref: TODO_REF Roddier 1988]. A bimorph deformable mirror, whose bending matches a Laplacian input directly, then closes a loop that is computationally lighter than a Shack-Hartmann zonal reconstructor. Extreme-AO systems such as SCExAO at Subaru and SPHERE at the VLT push Strehl ratios above 0.9 in the near-infrared, the regime in which a faint exoplanet can be distinguished from the diffraction rings of its host star by coronagraphic suppression of the stellar halo.

### Aperture synthesis, VLBI correlation, and the Event Horizon Telescope

The Van Cittert-Zernike theorem states that a single baseline measures one Fourier component of the source; an image requires sampling many spatial frequencies. Earth-rotation synthesis exploits the Earth's spin to rotate the baseline through a range of orientations, sweeping each $(u,v)$ point along an elliptical track and so filling the Fourier plane over hours of observation. Arrays such as the VLA, ALMA, and VLTI combine many simultaneous baselines with Earth rotation to synthesise a well-filled aperture.

In VLBI the stations are continents apart and the signal-to-noise of a single baseline integration is small. Each station heterodynes the incoming radio wave against a maser-referenced local oscillator, digitises it, and writes it to disk with precise time stamps. A central correlator then multiplies the recorded streams pairwise over a grid of trial delays and fringe rates, searching for the interference peak; the location of that peak gives the geometric delay, and its complex amplitude is the visibility. Because no phase reference is shared between stations, the raw phases are dominated by maser and atmospheric drift. Closure quantities survive: the closure phase on a triangle is piston-invariant, and the closure amplitude on a quadrilateral is invariant to per-station gain errors. Imaging proceeds by fitting the measured closure quantities and any absolute amplitude information to a brightness model.

The Event Horizon Telescope operates at $\lambda=1.3\,\text{mm}$ with a network of stations from Hawaii to the South Pole and France, reaching baselines of up to $10^7$ metres and a resolution of about 20 microarcseconds [ref: TODO_REF EHT 2019]. At that scale the photon-ring shadow of a supermassive black hole is directly resolvable, and the 2019 image of M87 and the 2022 image of Sgr A* confirmed the predictions of Kerr geometry at the event-horizon scale. Optical and infrared interferometry, exemplified by GRAVITY at the VLTI, reaches comparable angular scales for bright sources and has monitored the relativistic orbital motion of the star S2 about the Galactic Centre black hole, detecting both the gravitational redshift and the Schwarzschild precession.

**Synthesis.** Adaptive optics and interferometry are two answers to the same question, and the foundational reason they fit together is that angular resolution is fundamentally a measurement of spatial frequency, $u=B/\lambda$. This is exactly the content of the Van Cittert-Zernike theorem: every baseline measures one Fourier component of the source, and sampling many baselines reconstructs the image. The single-dish diffraction limit $\theta\sim\lambda/D$ generalises to $\theta\sim\lambda/B$ once the aperture is allowed to be virtual, and closure phase is dual to the gauge freedom that lets per-station atmospheric piston cancel out of a triangle, leaving a robust observable. Adaptive optics recovers the missing phase in real time by feeding the wavefront error back to a deformable mirror; putting these together, the bridge is that both techniques convert atmospheric corruption, the astronomer's oldest enemy, into a solvable inverse problem, and the pattern recurs from Labeyrie's speckle interferometry through VLBI to the Event Horizon Telescope.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Interferometric resolution from the Van Cittert-Zernike theorem).** Let an incoherent, spatially extended source subtend a small solid angle $\Omega$ on the sky, with specific brightness $I(\mathbf{s})$ in direction $\mathbf{s}$. Then the complex visibility measured by two telescopes separated by baseline $\mathbf{B}$ is

$$V(\mathbf{u}) \;=\; \frac{\displaystyle\int_\Omega I(\mathbf{s})\,e^{-2\pi i\,\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{s}}\,d\Omega}{\displaystyle\int_\Omega I(\mathbf{s})\,d\Omega}, \qquad \mathbf{u}=\mathbf{B}/\lambda.$$

Consequently a source of characteristic angular extent $\theta_s$ has its visibility fall to zero first at $|\mathbf{u}|\sim 1/\theta_s$, and the array resolves angles $\theta\sim\lambda/B$.

*Proof.* Write the analytic signal at telescope $i$ as the superposition of the fields radiated incoherently by each source element, $E_i(t)=\int_\Omega A(\mathbf{s})\,e^{2\pi i\nu t - i k\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}_i\cdot\mathbf{s}}\,d\Omega$ in the scalar paraxial approximation, where $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_i$ is the line-of-sight unit vector at telescope $i$ and $A(\mathbf{s})$ collects the brightness and path attenuation in direction $\mathbf{s}$. The mutual coherence is the time average

$$\Gamma_{12} \;=\; \langle E_1 E_2^{*}\rangle \;=\; \int_\Omega\!\int_\Omega \langle A(\mathbf{s})A^{*}(\mathbf{s}')\rangle\, e^{-ik(\hat{\mathbf{n}}_1-\hat{\mathbf{n}}_2)\cdot\mathbf{s}}\,d\Omega\,d\Omega'.$$

For a spatially incoherent source the brightnesses at distinct directions are uncorrelated, so $\langle A(\mathbf{s})A^{*}(\mathbf{s}')\rangle = I(\mathbf{s})\,\delta(\mathbf{s}-\mathbf{s}')$, and the double integral collapses to the single integral $\Gamma_{12}\propto\int_\Omega I(\mathbf{s})\,e^{-ik(\hat{\mathbf{n}}_1-\hat{\mathbf{n}}_2)\cdot\mathbf{s}}\,d\Omega$. Setting the baseline vector $\mathbf{B}$ in wavelengths, $\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{B}/\lambda=(\hat{\mathbf{n}}_1-\hat{\mathbf{n}}_2)/\lambda$ in the small-angle sky frame, gives $k(\hat{\mathbf{n}}_1-\hat{\mathbf{n}}_2)\cdot\mathbf{s}=2\pi\,\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{s}$, hence $\Gamma_{12}\propto\int_\Omega I(\mathbf{s})\,e^{-2\pi i\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{s}}\,d\Omega$. Normalising by $\Gamma_{11}=\int_\Omega I\,d\Omega$ yields the stated $V(\mathbf{u})$. The visibility is the Fourier transform of $I$ at frequency $\mathbf{u}$, so by the scaling of the Fourier transform a source of angular extent $\theta_s$ has spectral content out to $|\mathbf{u}|\sim 1/\theta_s$ and negligible power beyond; the first zero lies at $|\mathbf{u}|\sim 1/\theta_s$, i.e. at baseline $B\sim\lambda/\theta_s$. Re-reading this as the smallest angle resolved at given baseline yields $\theta\sim\lambda/B$. $\square$

**Proposition (Closure phase cancels atmospheric piston).** Let three telescopes $1,2,3$ measure complex visibilities $V_{12},V_{23},V_{31}$ on baselines $(1,2),(2,3),(3,1)$. Suppose the measured phase on each baseline is the true object phase $\phi_{ij}$ plus a per-station atmospheric piston $\alpha_i$, so $\arg V_{ij}^{\text{meas}}=\phi_{ij}+\alpha_i-\alpha_j$. Then the closure phase $\Psi_{123}=\arg V_{12}+\arg V_{23}+\arg V_{31}$ equals $\phi_{12}+\phi_{23}+\phi_{31}$ and is independent of $\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\alpha_3$.

*Proof.* Expand the closure sum using the measured phases:

$$\Psi_{123} \;=\; (\phi_{12}+\alpha_1-\alpha_2) + (\phi_{23}+\alpha_2-\alpha_3) + (\phi_{31}+\alpha_3-\alpha_1).$$

Collecting the piston terms gives $(\alpha_1-\alpha_2)+(\alpha_2-\alpha_3)+(\alpha_3-\alpha_1)$, in which each $\alpha_i$ enters once positively and once negatively and therefore telescopes to zero. The closure phase reduces to

$$\Psi_{123} \;=\; \phi_{12}+\phi_{23}+\phi_{31},$$

which depends only on the object phases and is invariant under any additive per-station piston. With $N$ telescopes one obtains $N(N-1)/2$ measured phases but only $N$ unknown pistons, leaving $(N-1)(N-2)/2$ independent closure phases; for $N=3$ this is a single robust observable, which is why Jennison's technique unlocked imaging with phase-unstable arrays. $\square$

**Proposition (Airy first zero and the single-aperture limit).** For a uniformly illuminated circular aperture of diameter $D$ the far-field intensity pattern has its first zero at angular radius $\theta_1=1.22\,\lambda/D$.

*Proof.* In the Fraunhofer regime the field at angle $\theta$ is the two-dimensional Fourier transform of the aperture transmission. In polar aperture coordinates $(\rho,\phi)$, $\rho\in[0,D/2]$, rotational symmetry reduces the transform to a Hankel integral over the radial spatial frequency $q=\sin\theta/\lambda$,

$$U(q) \;=\; 2\pi\int_0^{D/2}\rho\,J_0(2\pi q\rho)\,d\rho,$$

where the azimuthal integral has produced the order-zero Bessel function $J_0$. The radial Bessel integral satisfies $\int_0^a\rho\,J_0(\kappa\rho)\,d\rho = a\,J_1(\kappa a)/\kappa$, giving $U(q)\propto J_1(\pi D q)/(\pi D q)$, the Airy amplitude. The intensity is $I(q)\propto[J_1(\pi Dq)/(\pi Dq)]^2$, whose first zero coincides with the first zero $j_{1,1}=3.8317$ of $J_1$. Setting $\pi D q_1=j_{1,1}$ and using the small-angle relation $q=\sin\theta/\lambda\approx\theta/\lambda$ yields $\theta_1=(j_{1,1}/\pi)\lambda/D=1.22\,\lambda/D$, since $3.8317/\pi=1.2197$. $\square$

The $\sim 20\,\mu\text{as}$ resolution achieved by Earth-sized VLBI baselines is what makes the photon-ring shadow of the supermassive black holes in M87 and Sgr A* directly resolvable, converting the Kerr geometry of high-energy astrophysics from a theoretical object into an imaged one, and the radio and submillimetre instrumentation surveyed there is exactly what the correlator architecture here stitches into a global array.

CHARA and VLTI measurements of stellar diameters, the oblateness of rapidly rotating stars, and the hot-spot structure of supergiant surfaces operate at milliarcsecond resolution, the scale at which the Hertzsprung-Russell diagram's radius axis becomes directly measurable rather than inferred from luminosity and temperature. The uniform-disk visibility criterion derived here is the working tool of those observations.

Aperture synthesis underlies the angular resolution of the cosmic microwave background anisotropy maps treated in [28.04.03] and the milliarcsecond structures of active galactic nuclei in [28.03.03]. The interferometric arrays that first resolved the CMB acoustic peaks and the VLBI arrays that resolve relativistic jets emerging from supermassive black holes are direct applications of the Van Cittert-Zernike theorem and closure-phase imaging developed here, with the wavelength scaled from optical to microwave and the baselines grown from hundreds of metres to the diameter of the Earth.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The possibility of correcting atmospheric seeing in real time was first stated by Horace Babcock in 1953 [ref: TODO_REF Babcock 1953]. Babcock, then at Mount Wilson and Palomar, proposed feeding a wavefront measurement to a deformable optical element coated on an oil film, so that the element would imprint the conjugate of the measured distortion and straighten the wavefront before detection. The proposal preceded every technology needed to realise it: the wavefront sensors, fast electronics, and controllable mirrors it required did not exist in 1953, and Babcock's paper sat largely unread for two decades. It was the arrival of solid-state detectors, fast digital control, and the military's interest in laser propagation through the atmosphere in the 1970s that turned the concept into working systems, first at sub-aperture scales and then on full telescopes.

Antoine Labeyrie's 1970 demonstration that short-exposure stellar images preserve diffraction-limited information in their speckle pattern [ref: TODO_REF Labeyrie 1970] was the first crack in the seeing wall from the ground. By Fourier-analysing many speckle exposures he recovered the modulus of the visibility function up to the diffraction cutoff of a large telescope, a statistical recovery that preceded real-time correction and motivated the subsequent development of full wavefront sensing. Labeyrie went on to build the Grand Interferometre a 2 Telescopes and to advocate optical aperture synthesis, the programme the VLTI eventually realised.

The closure-phase technique, published by Roger Jennison in 1958 [ref: TODO_REF Jennison 1958], solved the problem that had defeated earlier radio interferometers: the phase on each baseline was corrupted by station-specific atmospheric and instrumental delays, making direct Fourier inversion impossible. Jennison showed that summing the three phases around a triangle of telescopes cancels the per-station terms and leaves a quantity depending only on the source. The technique, developed for the Jodrell Bank radio-linked arrays at metre wavelengths, was carried into optical interferometry by Readhead and others in the 1980s and remains the backbone of imaging at both wavelength regimes.

The conceptual arc culminates in the Event Horizon Telescope. Linking radio observatories from Hawaii to the South Pole and France at 1.3 mm wavelength, the collaboration released in 2019 the first image of the shadow of a black hole, the $6.5\times 10^9\,M_\odot$ object at the centre of M87 [ref: TODO_REF EHT 2019], and followed it in 2022 with the image of the $4\times 10^6\,M_\odot$ black hole Sgr A* at the centre of the Milky Way. The $\sim 20\,\mu\text{as}$ resolution was achieved by hydrogen-maser-referenced recording and offline correlation on baselines the size of the Earth, the longest separations ever synthesised. The result crowned a line of thought that runs from Babcock's deformable oil film and Jennison's closure phase through Ryle's aperture synthesis to a virtual telescope the diameter of a planet.
