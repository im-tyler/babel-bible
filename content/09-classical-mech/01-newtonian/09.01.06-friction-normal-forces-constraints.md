## Intuition [Beginner]

Push a book across a table. It slows down. Something opposes its motion. That something is **friction** — a force between two surfaces in contact, always directed opposite to the relative motion (or the tendency toward motion).

There are two kinds. **Static friction** holds the book still when you push gently but not hard enough. It adjusts itself up to a maximum value, which is proportional to how hard the book presses against the table. **Kinetic friction** acts once the book is sliding. It has a fixed magnitude (again proportional to the pressing force) and is generally slightly less than the maximum static friction.

The "pressing force" is the **normal force**. It is the force the table exerts upward on the book, perpendicular to the surface. The name comes from the mathematical word for perpendicular. If the book weighs 10 newtons and sits on a flat table, the normal force is 10 newtons upward — exactly balancing gravity. On a tilted surface the normal force is smaller, only the component of gravity perpendicular to the surface.

Normal forces are **constraint forces**. They enforce a geometric restriction: the book cannot pass through the table. The table pushes back just hard enough to prevent penetration. The book's motion is **constrained** to remain on or above the table surface.

Constraints appear everywhere. A bead on a wire is constrained to follow the wire's shape. A pendulum bob is constrained to remain at a fixed string length from the pivot. A door on hinges is constrained to rotate about the hinge axis. In every case, the constraint generates a force — normal, tension, hinge reaction — that does no work on the system (the force is always perpendicular to the allowed motion, or at least to the virtual displacement).

## Visual [Beginner]

![Block on an inclined plane. The weight mg acts straight down, the normal force N acts perpendicular to the surface, and kinetic friction f_k acts up the slope opposite to the direction of motion. The angle of the incline is labelled theta.](/img/09.01.06-inclined-plane-forces-placeholder.svg)

The three forces on the block — weight, normal force, and friction — form a right triangle when the block slides at constant speed. The normal force balances the perpendicular component of gravity ($N = mg\cos\theta$) and friction balances the parallel component ($f_k = mg\sin\theta$).

## Worked example [Beginner]

A block of mass $m = 2$ kg sits on a ramp inclined at 30 degrees. The coefficient of static friction is 0.4 and the coefficient of kinetic friction is 0.3. Does the block slide? If so, what is its acceleration?

**Step 1: Compare static friction with the downhill pull.**

The downhill component of gravity is $mg\sin(30^\circ) = 2 \times 9.8 \times 0.5 = 9.8$ N. The normal force is $N = mg\cos(30^\circ) = 2 \times 9.8 \times 0.866 = 17.0$ N. The maximum static friction is $\mu_s N = 0.4 \times 17.0 = 6.8$ N.

Since 9.8 N exceeds 6.8 N, the block slides.

**Step 2: Find the acceleration.**

Once sliding, kinetic friction applies: $f_k = \mu_k N = 0.3 \times 17.0 = 5.1$ N uphill. The net downhill force is $9.8 - 5.1 = 4.7$ N. By Newton's second law: $a = F/m = 4.7/2 = 2.35$ m/s squared downhill.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Static and kinetic friction (Coulomb model).** For two surfaces in contact with a normal force $N$:

- **Static friction:** $|f_s| \leq \mu_s N$, where $\mu_s$ is the coefficient of static friction. The friction force takes whatever value (up to the maximum) is needed to prevent relative motion. Motion initiates when the applied force exceeds $\mu_s N$.
- **Kinetic friction:** $f_k = \mu_k N$ opposing the direction of relative sliding, where $\mu_k$ is the coefficient of kinetic friction and typically $\mu_k < \mu_s$.

The coefficients $\mu_s$ and $\mu_k$ are dimensionless and depend on the materials and surface conditions.

**Normal force and constraint forces.** The normal force $\mathbf{N}$ is a reaction force perpendicular to a surface that prevents interpenetration. More generally, **constraint forces** are forces that enforce geometric restrictions on the motion. They are determined not by a force law (like gravity) but by the requirement that the constraint be satisfied at all times.

**Holonomic constraints.** A constraint is **holonomic** if it can be written as an equation relating the coordinates (and possibly time) but not the velocities:

$$
f(q^1, q^2, \ldots, q^n, t) \;=\; 0.
$$

Examples: a particle constrained to a surface ($f(\mathbf{r}) = 0$); a rigid rod of length $\ell$ connecting two particles ($|\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2|^2 = \ell^2$); a bead constrained to a curve. Each holonomic constraint reduces the number of independent coordinates by one.

Holonomic constraints are classified further as **scleronomic** (time-independent: $f(q) = 0$) or **rheonomic** (time-dependent: $f(q, t) = 0$). A bead on a fixed wire is scleronomic; a bead on a moving wire is rheonomic.

**Non-holonomic constraints.** A constraint that cannot be integrated to the form $f(q, t) = 0$ is **non-holonomic**. The standard example is a disk rolling without slipping on a plane: the rolling condition $dx - R\,d\phi\cos\theta = 0$, $dy - R\,d\phi\sin\theta = 0$ relates differentials of position to the rotation angle $\phi$ and the heading $\theta$, but cannot be integrated to a relation among coordinates alone. Non-holonomic constraints restrict velocities without reducing the accessible configuration space.

**Virtual displacement and virtual work.** A **virtual displacement** $\delta\mathbf{r}_i$ is an infinitesimal change in the coordinates consistent with the constraints at a fixed time (the frozen-time displacement). For a holonomic constraint $f(q, t) = 0$, virtual displacements satisfy $\sum_i (\partial f/\partial q^i)\delta q^i = 0$. The **virtual work** of a force $\mathbf{F}_i$ on the $i$-th particle is $\delta W = \sum_i \mathbf{F}_i \cdot \delta\mathbf{r}_i$.

**D'Alembert's principle.** The total force on particle $i$ decomposes as $\mathbf{F}_i = \mathbf{F}_i^{(a)} + \mathbf{F}_i^{(c)}$ where $\mathbf{F}_i^{(a)}$ are applied forces (gravity, springs, etc.) and $\mathbf{F}_i^{(c)}$ are constraint forces. D'Alembert's principle states:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^N \left(\mathbf{F}_i^{(a)} - m_i \mathbf{a}_i\right) \cdot \delta\mathbf{r}_i \;=\; 0
$$

for all virtual displacements $\delta\mathbf{r}_i$ consistent with the constraints. The underlying assumption is that constraint forces do zero virtual work: $\sum_i \mathbf{F}_i^{(c)} \cdot \delta\mathbf{r}_i = 0$. This is the content of the **principle of virtual work** for constraint forces: constraint forces are perpendicular to the constraint surface and therefore perpendicular to virtual displacements along it.

### Classification summary

| Constraint type | Equation form | Examples | Degrees of freedom |
|---|---|---|---|
| Holonomic scleronomic | $f(q) = 0$ | Bead on fixed wire, pendulum | Reduced by 1 per constraint |
| Holonomic rheonomic | $f(q, t) = 0$ | Bead on moving wire | Reduced by 1 per constraint |
| Non-holonomic | $g(q, \dot{q}, t) = 0$ (non-integrable) | Rolling disk, skate constraint | Not reduced |

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Key result (Constraint forces from D'Alembert's principle).** *For a system of $N$ particles subject to holonomic constraints $f_\alpha(q^1, \ldots, q^{3N}, t) = 0$ for $\alpha = 1, \ldots, k$, the constraint forces are given by*

$$
\mathbf{F}_i^{(c)} \;=\; \sum_{\alpha=1}^k \lambda_\alpha \frac{\partial f_\alpha}{\partial \mathbf{r}_i},
$$

*where the $\lambda_\alpha$ are Lagrange multipliers determined by the requirement that the constraints be satisfied.*

**Proof.** The constraint $f_\alpha = 0$ implies $\delta f_\alpha = 0$ for virtual displacements: $\sum_i (\partial f_\alpha/\partial \mathbf{r}_i) \cdot \delta\mathbf{r}_i = 0$. These are $k$ linear constraints on the $\delta\mathbf{r}_i$. Introduce $k$ multipliers $\lambda_\alpha$ and add $\sum_\alpha \lambda_\alpha \delta f_\alpha = 0$ to D'Alembert's principle:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^N \left(\mathbf{F}_i^{(a)} - m_i\mathbf{a}_i + \sum_{\alpha=1}^k \lambda_\alpha \frac{\partial f_\alpha}{\partial \mathbf{r}_i}\right) \cdot \delta\mathbf{r}_i \;=\; 0.
$$

Choose the $\lambda_\alpha$ so that the coefficient of each constrained $\delta\mathbf{r}_i$ component vanishes. The remaining $3N - k$ free components of $\delta\mathbf{r}_i$ are independently variable, so their coefficients must also vanish. This gives $3N$ equations:

$$
m_i \mathbf{a}_i \;=\; \mathbf{F}_i^{(a)} + \sum_{\alpha=1}^k \lambda_\alpha \frac{\partial f_\alpha}{\partial \mathbf{r}_i},
$$

which are Newton's equations with the constraint forces identified as $\sum_\alpha \lambda_\alpha (\partial f_\alpha/\partial \mathbf{r}_i)$. $\square$

**Worked example: the simple pendulum.** A mass $m$ hangs from a string of length $\ell$ fixed at the origin. The constraint is $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2 - \ell^2 = 0$. The constraint force (tension) is $\mathbf{F}^{(c)} = \lambda\nabla f = 2\lambda(x, y) = 2\lambda\mathbf{r}$, directed radially. Using the polar angle $\theta$ as the single generalised coordinate, D'Alembert's principle gives $m\ell\ddot\theta = -mg\sin\theta$ with no tension appearing — the tension has been eliminated by the coordinate choice.

**Bridge.** D'Alembert's principle builds toward the Lagrangian formulation [09.02.03], where constraint forces are handled via Lagrange multipliers or eliminated entirely by choosing generalised coordinates adapted to the constraints; the foundational reason is that constraint forces do zero virtual work, which is exactly the property that makes the Euler-Lagrange equations independent of the constraint forces when the right coordinates are chosen. This result generalises to field-theoretic constraints in continuum mechanics [09.07.01], and the central insight — that constraints reduce the configuration space to a submanifold — is dual to the geometric treatment of constrained motion on manifolds in the Hamiltonian framework.

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Two blocks of masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ are connected by a light inextensible string over a frictionless pulley (an Atwood machine). Write the constraint equation relating the positions and the constraint force (string tension).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If one block goes up by $x$, the other goes down by $x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Constraint: $y_1 + y_2 = \text{const}$, or equivalently $\dot{y}_1 = -\dot{y}_2$. The constraint force on each block is the string tension $T$, directed upward on both. The equations of motion are $m_1\ddot{y}_1 = m_1 g - T$ and $m_2\ddot{y}_2 = m_2 g - T$. Using the constraint: $m_1\ddot{y}_1 - m_2\ddot{y}_2 = (m_1 - m_2)g$, giving $\ddot{y}_1 = (m_1 - m_2)g/(m_1 + m_2)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

A particle of mass $m$ slides on the inside of a smooth hemispherical bowl of radius $R$. Write the constraint equation, identify the constraint force, and determine the condition for circular motion at height $h$ above the bottom.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The constraint is $r = R$ (particle on the inner surface). The constraint force is the normal reaction, directed radially inward.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Constraint: $f(r) = r - R = 0$ (in spherical coordinates centred at the bowl centre). The constraint force is $N = \lambda\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ directed inward. For circular motion at angle $\theta$ from the bottom (so $h = R(1 - \cos\theta)$), the radial equation is $N - mg\cos\theta = mv^2/R$ and the tangential equation is $mg\sin\theta = 0$ (circular orbit requires $\theta = 0$). At arbitrary $\theta$, circular motion requires an additional tangential force. For motion along the horizontal circle at height $h$: $N\cos\theta - mg = 0$ and $N\sin\theta = m\omega^2 R\sin\theta$, giving $\omega = \sqrt{g/(R\cos\theta)}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A uniform disk of radius $r$ rolls without slipping down an inclined plane at angle $\theta$. Write the non-holonomic constraint (rolling condition), and find the linear acceleration of the disk's centre.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Rolling without slipping means $v = r\omega$. The moment of inertia of a uniform disk is $I = mr^2/2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The rolling constraint is $v_{\rm cm} = r\dot\phi$ where $\phi$ is the rotation angle (non-holonomic: relates velocity to angular velocity). Newton's second law along the plane: $mg\sin\theta - f = ma$, where $f$ is friction. Torque equation about the centre: $fr = I\dot\omega = (mr^2/2)(a/r)$, giving $f = ma/2$. Substituting: $mg\sin\theta - ma/2 = ma$, so $a = (2/3)g\sin\theta$. Friction does no work (the contact point is instantaneously at rest), so the rolling constraint is workless even though friction is present.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

State D'Alembert's principle for a single particle constrained to move on a smooth surface $f(\mathbf{r}) = 0$, and show that it reduces to motion under the tangential component of the applied force.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Virtual displacements lie in the tangent plane: $\nabla f \cdot \delta\mathbf{r} = 0$. The constraint force is $\lambda\nabla f$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

D'Alembert's principle: $(\mathbf{F}^{(a)} - m\mathbf{a})\cdot\delta\mathbf{r} = 0$ for all $\delta\mathbf{r}$ with $\nabla f\cdot\delta\mathbf{r} = 0$. Write $\mathbf{F}^{(a)} - m\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{F}_\parallel + \mathbf{F}_\perp$ decomposed into components tangential and normal to the surface. The normal component is balanced by the constraint force. The equation of motion on the surface involves only the tangential applied force.

</details>

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Classify each constraint as holonomic or non-holonomic, and as scleronomic or rheonomic: (a) a particle on the surface of a sphere $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = R^2$; (b) a particle constrained so that $z = \dot{x}$; (c) a bead on a rotating hoop $x^2 + y^2 = R^2$ in the plane $z = \omega t$; (d) a car whose front wheels can only roll without slipping.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that for a system of $N$ particles subject only to holonomic constraints with smooth constraint surfaces, the total virtual work of the constraint forces vanishes.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each constraint force is proportional to the gradient of the constraint function. Virtual displacements satisfy $\nabla f_\alpha \cdot \delta\mathbf{r}_i = 0$ summed over $i$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The constraint force on particle $i$ is $\mathbf{F}_i^{(c)} = \sum_\alpha \lambda_\alpha \partial f_\alpha/\partial\mathbf{r}_i$. The virtual work is $\delta W^{(c)} = \sum_i \mathbf{F}_i^{(c)}\cdot\delta\mathbf{r}_i = \sum_\alpha \lambda_\alpha \sum_i (\partial f_\alpha/\partial\mathbf{r}_i)\cdot\delta\mathbf{r}_i = \sum_\alpha \lambda_\alpha \delta f_\alpha$. By definition of virtual displacement (consistent with the constraints), $\delta f_\alpha = 0$ for each $\alpha$. Therefore $\delta W^{(c)} = 0$. This is the principle of virtual work for ideal constraints.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

A ladder of length $L$ and mass $m$ leans against a smooth (frictionless) wall at angle $\theta$ with the floor. The floor has coefficient of static friction $\mu_s$. Find the minimum $\theta$ for which the ladder does not slip.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Three forces act: weight at the centre, normal force from the wall (horizontal, at the top), and the floor reaction (normal plus friction at the bottom). Use force balance and torque balance.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Forces: weight $mg$ downward at the midpoint, wall normal force $N_w$ horizontal at the top, floor normal force $N_f$ upward, and floor friction $f$ horizontal at the bottom. Force balance: $N_f = mg$ and $N_w = f$. Torque about the bottom contact point: $mg(L/2)\cos\theta = N_w L\sin\theta$, giving $N_w = mg\cos\theta/(2\sin\theta) = mg/(2\tan\theta)$. The friction condition is $f = N_w \leq \mu_s N_f = \mu_s mg$, so $mg/(2\tan\theta) \leq \mu_s mg$, giving $\tan\theta \geq 1/(2\mu_s)$, or $\theta_{\min} = \arctan(1/(2\mu_s))$. For $\mu_s = 0.4$: $\theta_{\min} = \arctan(1.25) \approx 51^\circ$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that a non-holonomic constraint of the form $g(q, \dot{q}) = 0$ that can be integrated to $f(q, t) = C$ (a constant) is actually holonomic. Give an explicit example and a non-integrable counterexample.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Frobenius integrability theorem provides conditions. For a single constraint in two variables, any constraint is integrable. For constraints in three or more variables, integrability is not guaranteed.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $g(q, \dot{q}) = A_i(q)\dot{q}^i = 0$ can be written as $A_i dq^i = 0$ being an exact differential $df = 0$, then $f = C$ and the constraint is holonomic. This requires $\partial A_i/\partial q^j = \partial A_j/\partial q^i$ for all $i, j$ (the Frobenius condition for a 1-form to be exact). Example: $y\,dx - x\,dy = 0$ is not exact ($\partial(y)/\partial y = 1 \neq -1 = \partial(-x)/\partial x$) but $xdx + ydy = 0$ is exact ($df = 0$ with $f = (x^2+y^2)/2$), giving the holonomic constraint $x^2 + y^2 = \text{const}$. The rolling disk constraint $dx - R\cos\theta\,d\phi = 0$, $dy - R\sin\theta\,d\phi = 0$ in variables $(x, y, \theta, \phi)$ is not integrable: the Frobenius condition fails, and no function $f(x,y,\theta,\phi) = C$ captures the rolling constraint.

</details>

</aside>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The Coulomb friction model — with its discontinuous dependence on velocity sign and its inequality constraints — is not represented in Mathlib. The classification of constraints (holonomic, non-holonomic, scleronomic, rheonomic) would require building a type-class hierarchy on top of Mathlib's smooth-manifold machinery. D'Alembert's principle, as a statement about virtual work vanishing, requires integrating the tangent-space geometry of constraint submanifolds with force-balance equations, which is a meaningful formalisation project but has not been undertaken.

## Advanced results: D'Alembert's principle as the bridge to Lagrangian mechanics [Master]

### From D'Alembert to the Euler-Lagrange equations

D'Alembert's principle is not merely a computational tool — it is the conceptual bridge from Newtonian mechanics to the Lagrangian formulation. When the constraints are holonomic, the virtual displacements can be parameterised by independent generalised coordinates $q^1, \ldots, q^n$ (where $n = 3N - k$ is the number of degrees of freedom). Expressing the positions as $\mathbf{r}_i = \mathbf{r}_i(q^1, \ldots, q^n, t)$ and substituting into D'Alembert's principle:

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n \left[\sum_{i=1}^N \left(\mathbf{F}_i^{(a)} \cdot \frac{\partial\mathbf{r}_i}{\partial q^j}\right) - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial T}{\partial\dot q^j} + \frac{\partial T}{\partial q^j}\right] \delta q^j \;=\; 0.
$$

Since the $\delta q^j$ are independent, each coefficient must vanish. Defining the generalised force $Q_j = \sum_i \mathbf{F}_i^{(a)}\cdot\partial\mathbf{r}_i/\partial q^j$ and the kinetic energy $T$:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial T}{\partial\dot q^j} - \frac{\partial T}{\partial q^j} \;=\; Q_j.
$$

If the applied forces are conservative ($\mathbf{F}_i^{(a)} = -\nabla_i V$), then $Q_j = -\partial V/\partial q^j$ and the equations become

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q^j} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^j} \;=\; 0,
$$

where $L = T - V$ is the Lagrangian. These are the **Euler-Lagrange equations** [09.02.02]. The entire derivation rests on D'Alembert's principle and the workless nature of constraint forces.

### Non-holonomic constraints and the Lagrange-d'Alembert equations

For non-holonomic constraints written as $A_{\alpha j}\dot{q}^j = 0$ ($\alpha = 1, \ldots, m$), the Euler-Lagrange equations are modified:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q^j} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^j} \;=\; \sum_{\alpha=1}^m \mu_\alpha A_{\alpha j},
$$

where the $\mu_\alpha$ are Lagrange multipliers for the non-holonomic constraints. These are the **Lagrange-d'Alembert equations**. They differ from the holonomic case: the non-holonomic multipliers cannot be absorbed into a redefined Lagrangian because the constraints do not reduce the configuration space.

### The geometry of constraint forces

On a configuration manifold $Q$, a holonomic constraint $f(q) = 0$ defines a submanifold $S = f^{-1}(0) \subset Q$. The constraint force at each point is proportional to $\nabla f$, which lies in the normal bundle of $S$. Virtual displacements lie in the tangent bundle $TS$. The vanishing of virtual work is precisely the orthogonality $\mathbf{F}^{(c)} \perp T_qS$ at each point.

For non-holonomic constraints, the constraint is a distribution $D \subset TQ$ (a sub-bundle of the tangent bundle). The constraint forces annihilate $D$, and the dynamics is restricted to $D$ without reducing the configuration space. The Frobenius theorem states that $D$ is integrable (comes from a submanifold) if and only if it is closed under the Lie bracket — this is the geometric content of the holonomic/non-holonomic distinction.

**Synthesis.** The classification of constraints into holonomic and non-holonomic is the foundational reason the Lagrangian formalism can eliminate constraint forces entirely in the holonomic case and must retain Lagrange multipliers in the non-holonomic case. The central insight is that holonomic constraints define submanifolds of configuration space, and D'Alembert's principle — the vanishing of virtual work — is exactly the statement that constraint forces are normal to these submanifolds. Putting these together, the passage from Newtonian force-balance to the Euler-Lagrange equations is a single conceptual step enabled by the geometry of constraints. This result generalises to infinite-dimensional systems in continuum mechanics [09.07.01] and appears again in the Hamiltonian treatment of constrained systems [09.02.03].

## Connections [Master]

- **[09.02.02]** The Euler-Lagrange equations are derived from D'Alembert's principle by choosing generalised coordinates adapted to the constraints; this unit provides the Newtonian starting point that the Lagrangian formalism generalises.
- **[09.02.03]** Constrained Lagrangian mechanics treats holonomic constraints via Lagrange multipliers, extending this unit's treatment to compute the constraint forces explicitly rather than eliminating them.
- **[09.07.01]** Continuum mechanics imposes field-theoretic constraints (incompressibility, rigidity) that are the infinite-dimensional analogues of the holonomic constraints studied here; D'Alembert's principle extends to field theories as the principle of virtual work.
- **[09.03.01]** Noether's theorem identifies continuous symmetries of the Lagrangian with conserved quantities; the constraint forces studied here break symmetries in a controlled way that Noether's theorem can accommodate via gauge degrees of freedom.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

D'Alembert's principle appears in his *Traite de dynamique* (1743), where he proposed replacing the Newtonian force balance with a principle of virtual work. The key idea — that equilibrium plus the "lost force" $-m\mathbf{a}$ gives a virtual-work equation — allowed the newly developed calculus of variations to be applied to dynamics. This was the direct precursor to Lagrange's *Mechanique analytique* (1788), which built the entire Lagrangian formalism on D'Alembert's foundation.

The Coulomb friction model (static and kinetic friction with constant coefficients) was proposed by Charles-Augustin de Coulomb in 1781 based on extensive experiments. It remains the standard engineering model despite its simplifications: real friction involves microscopic surface interactions, temperature dependence, and velocity dependence that the Coulomb model ignores.

The distinction between holonomic and non-holonomic constraints was clarified by Hertz in *The Principles of Mechanics* (1894), who recognised that non-holonomic constraints represent a fundamentally different type of restriction on motion — one that constrains velocities without constraining configurations. The Frobenius theorem (1877) provides the mathematical criterion for integrability, connecting the physics of constraints to the geometry of distributions on manifolds.

A philosophical point: constraint forces illustrate that not all forces in mechanics arise from force laws. The normal force and friction are determined by the requirement that constraints be satisfied, not by independent laws. This distinction — between forces given by laws (gravity, electromagnetism) and forces determined by constraints — is a structural feature of Newtonian mechanics that persists in the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian frameworks, where it appears as the distinction between the Lagrangian (which encodes the force laws) and the constraint equations (which restrict the configuration space).
