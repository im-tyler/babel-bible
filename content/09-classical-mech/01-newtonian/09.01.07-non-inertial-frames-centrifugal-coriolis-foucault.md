## Intuition [Beginner]

Stand on a merry-go-round spinning at constant speed. You feel pushed outward. A ball thrown straight outward curves sideways. A plumb bob hangs not toward Earth's centre but slightly equatorward. All three effects are consequences of describing motion from a rotating, non-inertial frame rather than from a fixed (inertial) frame.

Newton's second law "F equals m times a" holds only in inertial frames. When you choose a rotating or accelerating frame, the acceleration you measure is not the same as the acceleration an inertial observer measures. The difference shows up as extra terms in the equation of motion — the "fictitious forces."

There are two key fictitious forces for a steadily rotating frame. The centrifugal force pushes radially outward, proportional to the square of the rotation rate and the distance from the rotation axis. The Coriolis force acts perpendicular to the velocity of the moving object, proportional to the rotation rate. Both are called "fictitious" not because they are unreal — they have measurable effects — but because they arise from the kinematics of the frame choice, not from interactions with other bodies.

On Earth, the centrifugal force slightly reduces the effective gravitational acceleration at the equator compared to the poles. The Coriolis force deflects moving air masses, producing the characteristic rotation of weather systems: counterclockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere. Foucault's 1851 pendulum, a 67-metre wire with a 28-kg bob suspended from the dome of the Pantheon in Paris, demonstrated Earth's rotation by the slow precession of its swing plane — a direct measurement of the Coriolis effect at latitude 48.8 degrees North.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a flat turntable rotating counterclockwise when viewed from above. A marble is released from rest at the centre. An inertial observer sees the marble sit still — no force, no acceleration. The turntable observer, rotating with the disk, sees the marble accelerate radially outward and curve to the right.

![Overhead view of a rotating turntable. The inertial path is a straight radial line. The rotating-frame path curves to the right (for counterclockwise rotation). Arrows show the centrifugal force pointing radially outward and the Coriolis force perpendicular to the instantaneous velocity.](/img/09.01.07-rotating-turntable-coriolis-placeholder.svg)

Now picture the Earth. At the North Pole the rotation axis is vertical; at the equator it is horizontal. A Foucault pendulum at the Pole precesses one full turn per sidereal day (about 23 h 56 min). At the equator it does not precess at all. At intermediate latitudes the precession rate is the full rate times the sine of the latitude. This geometric factor — sin of latitude — is the projection of Earth's angular-velocity vector onto the local vertical.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A Foucault pendulum at latitude $\lambda = 48.86^\circ$ N (Paris) swings with period $T_0 = 8.0$ seconds. How long does its swing plane take to precess through 360 degrees?

The precession angular speed is $\Omega_{\mathrm{prec}} = \Omega \sin\lambda$, where $\Omega$ is Earth's sidereal rotation rate. Earth completes one rotation in a sidereal day $T_{\mathrm{sid}} = 86164$ seconds, so $\Omega = 2\pi / 86164 \approx 7.292 \times 10^{-5}$ rad/s.

Compute $\sin(48.86^\circ) \approx 0.753$. Then $\Omega_{\mathrm{prec}} = 7.292 \times 10^{-5} \times 0.753 \approx 5.49 \times 10^{-5}$ rad/s.

The precession period is $T_{\mathrm{prec}} = 2\pi / \Omega_{\mathrm{prec}} \approx 2\pi / (5.49 \times 10^{-5}) \approx 114\,400$ s $\approx 31.8$ hours. The observed value at the Pantheon was about 31.8 hours, matching the prediction.

**Exercise (easy, multiple choice).**

In a frame rotating at constant angular velocity, the centrifugal force on a particle of mass $m$ at position $\mathbf{r}$ from the rotation axis is proportional to:

A. $m \mathbf{v}$

B. $m \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{v}$

C. $m \boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r})$

D. $m \dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} \times \mathbf{r}$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The centrifugal force arises from the rotation alone — it does not depend on the particle's velocity in the rotating frame.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Option C.**

The centrifugal acceleration is $-\boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r})$, directed radially outward from the rotation axis. Its magnitude is $\omega^2 \rho$ where $\rho$ is the perpendicular distance from the axis. Option B is the Coriolis acceleration (depends on velocity), and option D is the Euler force (appears only when the angular velocity changes in time).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise (easy, true/false).**

At the geographic equator, a Foucault pendulum's swing plane precesses at the same rate as at the North Pole.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The precession rate is proportional to the sine of the latitude.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**False.**

At the North Pole ($\lambda = 90^\circ$) the precession rate equals Earth's full rotation rate. At the equator ($\lambda = 0^\circ$) the precession rate is zero. The swing plane does not precess at the equator because the local vertical is perpendicular to the rotation axis, so the Coriolis force acts entirely within the horizontal plane and does not rotate the swing direction.

</details>

</aside>

At the surface of the Earth at the equator, the centrifugal acceleration reduces the effective gravitational acceleration by approximately what fraction? Use $g \approx 9.78$ m/s$^2$ (before centrifugal correction), Earth's radius $R \approx 6.378 \times 10^6$ m, and $\Omega = 7.292 \times 10^{-5}$ rad/s.

The centrifugal acceleration at the equator is $\Omega^2 R$, directed radially outward, opposing gravity.

</details>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $S$ be an inertial frame with origin $O$ and orthonormal basis $\{\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i\}$. Let $S'$ be a second frame whose origin coincides with $O$ and whose basis $\{\hat{\mathbf{e}}'_i(t)\}$ rotates with time-dependent angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}(t)$. The relation between the time derivatives of any vector $\mathbf{A}$ in the two frames is

$$
\left(\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt}\right)_S = \left(\frac{d\mathbf{A}}{dt}\right)_{S'} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{A}.
$$

Apply this identity to the position vector $\mathbf{r}$ of a particle to get the velocity relation

$$
\mathbf{v}_S = \mathbf{v}_{S'} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}.
$$

Apply it again to the velocity relation to get the acceleration decomposition:

$$
\mathbf{a}_S = \mathbf{a}_{S'} + 2\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{v}_{S'} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}) + \dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} \times \mathbf{r}.
$$

Newton's second law in the inertial frame is $m\mathbf{a}_S = \mathbf{F}$. Substituting and rearranging gives the equation of motion in the rotating frame:

$$
\boxed{m\mathbf{a}_{S'} = \mathbf{F} - 2m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{v}_{S'} - m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}) - m\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} \times \mathbf{r}.}
$$

The three correction terms are:

- The **Coriolis force** $-2m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{v}_{S'}$: acts perpendicular to both the angular velocity and the velocity in the rotating frame. It does no work (it is always perpendicular to the velocity).
- The **centrifugal force** $-m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r})$: points radially outward from the rotation axis with magnitude $m\omega^2 \rho$ where $\rho$ is the perpendicular distance from the axis. It can be derived from a potential $V_{\mathrm{cf}} = -\tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 \rho^2$.
- The **Euler force** $-m\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} \times \mathbf{r}$: appears only when the angular velocity changes in time. For steadily rotating frames it vanishes.

For frames whose origin also translates with acceleration $\mathbf{A}_0$ relative to the inertial frame, an additional term $-m\mathbf{A}_0$ (the translational fictitious force) appears on the right-hand side.

### The centrifugal potential

For constant $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ along $\hat{\mathbf{z}}$, the centrifugal force is $m\omega^2 \rho\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\rho}}$ where $\rho = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ is the distance from the $z$-axis. This is derivable from the potential

$$
V_{\mathrm{cf}}(x, y, z) = -\frac{1}{2}m\omega^2(x^2 + y^2).
$$

The effective potential in a rotating frame is $V_{\mathrm{eff}} = V_{\mathrm{real}} + V_{\mathrm{cf}}$. Equilibrium points (where the effective force vanishes) are stationary points of $V_{\mathrm{eff}}$.

On the surface of the Earth, the centrifugal potential modifies the gravitational potential. The effective gravitational acceleration vector is tilted slightly away from the radial direction (toward the equator) and its magnitude is reduced by the centrifugal contribution. This explains why the Earth is an oblate spheroid rather than a perfect sphere: the equilibrium shape under combined gravitational and centrifugal potentials satisfies the equipotential condition.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Key derivation: Foucault pendulum precession rate.**

Consider a Foucault pendulum at latitude $\lambda$ on Earth's surface. Earth rotates with angular velocity $\Omega$ about its axis. Decompose $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$ into a vertical component $\Omega\sin\lambda\,\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ and a horizontal (northward) component $\Omega\cos\lambda\,\hat{\mathbf{n}}$.

The pendulum swings in the local horizontal plane. The Coriolis acceleration is $-2\boldsymbol{\Omega} \times \mathbf{v}$. Only the vertical component of $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$ produces a horizontal Coriolis force (the horizontal component of $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$ crossed with a horizontal velocity points vertically and is absorbed by the tension in the string). The relevant precession-driving Coriolis acceleration is therefore

$$
\mathbf{a}_{\mathrm{Cor}} = -2\Omega\sin\lambda\,\hat{\mathbf{z}} \times \mathbf{v}_h.
$$

In the horizontal plane this is equivalent to a constant rotation of the velocity vector at angular rate $\Omega\sin\lambda$. The swing plane of the pendulum precesses at exactly this rate.

The precession period is $T_{\mathrm{prec}} = 2\pi / (\Omega\sin\lambda) = T_{\mathrm{sid}} / \sin\lambda$. At the North Pole this equals one sidereal day; at the equator it diverges (no precession).

**Bridge.** This derivation builds toward the Galilean symmetry group [09.03.05], where the ten integrals of motion of an isolated system are identified in an inertial frame; the centrifugal and Coriolis terms computed here are exactly the corrections that break some of those integrals (linear momentum is no longer conserved in a rotating frame), and the Jacobi integral that replaces energy conservation in a steadily rotating frame generalises the centrifugal potential. The frame-transformation machinery also appears again in [09.04.06] on Hamiltonian perturbation theory, where rotating frames are used to remove the fast phase and extract slow secular drift. The central insight is that fictitious forces are not perturbations to Newton's law but exact consequences of the kinematics of non-inertial reference frames.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Write the centrifugal potential $V_{\mathrm{cf}}$ for a frame rotating about the $z$-axis with angular velocity $\omega$, and show that the force $-\nabla V_{\mathrm{cf}}$ equals the centrifugal force $m\omega^2\rho\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\rho}}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Express $\rho^2 = x^2 + y^2$ and differentiate with respect to $x$ and $y$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$V_{\mathrm{cf}} = -\tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2(x^2 + y^2)$. Then $-\partial V_{\mathrm{cf}}/\partial x = m\omega^2 x$ and $-\partial V_{\mathrm{cf}}/\partial y = m\omega^2 y$. In cylindrical coordinates this is $m\omega^2\rho\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\rho}}$, exactly the centrifugal force. Since $V_{\mathrm{cf}}$ does not depend on $z$, the force has no $z$-component.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (medium, symbolic).**

A particle of mass $m$ is at rest in a rotating frame with constant angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$. Show that the net force on the particle in the rotating frame is zero when the real force (e.g., a normal force or tension) exactly balances the centrifugal force.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In the rotating frame with $\mathbf{v}_{S'} = 0$ and $\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} = 0$, the equation of motion reduces to $\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{real}} + \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{cf}} = 0$ for equilibrium.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $\mathbf{v}_{S'} = 0$ and $\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} = 0$, the Coriolis and Euler forces vanish. The equation of motion in the rotating frame is $m\mathbf{a}_{S'} = \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{real}} - m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r})$. For the particle to remain at rest ($\mathbf{a}_{S'} = 0$), we need $\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{real}} = m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r})$, i.e., the real force exactly cancels the centrifugal force. This is why a mass on a string at rest in a rotating frame has tension $T = m\omega^2\rho$ pointing inward — the tension is the real force balancing the outward centrifugal force.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

On a flat rotating disk (angular velocity $\omega$ about the centre), a puck is launched radially outward from the centre with speed $v_0$ in the rotating frame. Compute the Coriolis deflection after the puck has travelled a radial distance $R$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Coriolis acceleration is transverse with magnitude $2\omega v_0$. Integrate twice.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Coriolis acceleration is $a_{\mathrm{Cor}} = 2\omega v_0$ perpendicular to the radial direction (to the right for counterclockwise rotation). The time to travel radial distance $R$ at constant radial speed $v_0$ is $t = R/v_0$. The transverse deflection is $\delta = \tfrac{1}{2} a_{\mathrm{Cor}} t^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}(2\omega v_0)(R/v_0)^2 = \omega R^2/v_0$. This is a leading-order result; the full trajectory also includes higher-order corrections from the changing radial velocity due to the centrifugal force.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the Lagrangian for a free particle in a frame rotating with constant angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ about the $z$-axis, and show that it yields the correct equations of motion including centrifugal and Coriolis terms.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Lagrangian in the inertial frame is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}_S|^2$. Express $\mathbf{v}_S$ in terms of the rotating-frame velocity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathbf{v}_S = \mathbf{v}_{S'} + \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}$. So $|\mathbf{v}_S|^2 = |\mathbf{v}_{S'}|^2 + 2\mathbf{v}_{S'} \cdot (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}) + |\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}|^2$. The Lagrangian is

$$L = \frac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}_{S'}|^2 + m\mathbf{v}_{S'} \cdot (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}) + \frac{1}{2}m|\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}|^2.$$

The Euler-Lagrange equations for this Lagrangian reproduce the centrifugal force (from the $|\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}|^2$ term, which acts as a potential) and the Coriolis force (from the $m\mathbf{v}_{S'} \cdot (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r})$ term, which is linear in velocity and produces the $2\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{v}_{S'}$ term upon differentiation). Since $L$ has no explicit time dependence for constant $\boldsymbol{\omega}$, the Jacobi integral (the "energy" in the rotating frame) is conserved.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Jacobi integral $J = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}_{S'}|^2 + V_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is conserved along trajectories in a steadily rotating frame, where $V_{\mathrm{eff}} = V_{\mathrm{real}} + V_{\mathrm{cf}}$ and $V_{\mathrm{cf}} = -\tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2\rho^2$. Why is $J$ not equal to the total mechanical energy measured in the inertial frame?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $dJ/dt$ using the equations of motion in the rotating frame. The Coriolis force does no work. For the second part, compare $J$ with $E = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}_S|^2 + V_{\mathrm{real}}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Differentiate $J$: $dJ/dt = m\mathbf{v}_{S'} \cdot \mathbf{a}_{S'} + \mathbf{v}_{S'} \cdot \nabla V_{\mathrm{eff}}$. Substitute the equation of motion $m\mathbf{a}_{S'} = -\nabla V_{\mathrm{real}} - 2m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{v}_{S'} - m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r})$. The Coriolis term drops because $\mathbf{v}_{S'} \cdot (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{v}_{S'}) = 0$ (triple product with two identical vectors). The centrifugal term gives $-\mathbf{v}_{S'} \cdot [m\boldsymbol{\omega} \times (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r})] = \mathbf{v}_{S'} \cdot \nabla V_{\mathrm{cf}}$. Combined with the real-force term, $dJ/dt = -\mathbf{v}_{S'} \cdot \nabla V_{\mathrm{real}} + \mathbf{v}_{S'} \cdot \nabla V_{\mathrm{cf}} - \mathbf{v}_{S'} \cdot \nabla V_{\mathrm{eff}} = 0$.

$J \neq E$ because the Lagrangian in the rotating frame contains the velocity-dependent term $m\mathbf{v}_{S'} \cdot (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r})$ that contributes to the Hamiltonian but not to the inertial-frame kinetic energy. Explicitly, $E = J + m\mathbf{v}_{S'} \cdot (\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}) + \tfrac{1}{2}m|\boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}|^2 - V_{\mathrm{cf}}$, which is time-dependent in general when $\mathbf{r}$ changes.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

A bead of mass $m$ slides without friction on a horizontal hoop of radius $R$ rotating about a vertical diameter with constant angular velocity $\omega$. Write the Lagrangian, find the equation of motion for the angular position $\theta$ measured from the bottom, and identify the equilibrium positions. For what range of $\omega$ is the bottom stable?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The effective potential is the sum of the gravitational potential and the centrifugal potential. The bottom ($\theta = 0$) is stable when the effective potential has a minimum there.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The bead's distance from the rotation axis is $R\sin\theta$. The height above the bottom is $R(1 - \cos\theta)$. The kinetic energy in the rotating frame is $\tfrac{1}{2}mR^2\dot\theta^2$ and the centrifugal potential is $-\tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 R^2 \sin^2\theta$. The Lagrangian is

$$L = \frac{1}{2}mR^2\dot\theta^2 - mgR(1 - \cos\theta) + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 R^2 \sin^2\theta.$$

The equation of motion is $R\ddot\theta = -g\sin\theta + \omega^2 R\sin\theta\cos\theta = \sin\theta(\omega^2 R\cos\theta - g)$. Equilibria: $\theta = 0$ (bottom) always, and $\theta = \arccos(g/(\omega^2 R))$ when $\omega^2 R > g$.

Linearise around $\theta = 0$: $\ddot\theta \approx -(g/R - \omega^2)\theta$. Stability requires $g/R > \omega^2$, i.e., $\omega < \sqrt{g/R}$. For $\omega > \sqrt{g/R}$ the bottom is unstable and the bead migrates to the off-axis equilibrium. This is a pitchfork bifurcation — the first encounter with the bifurcation theory developed systematically in [09.08.04].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

A projectile is launched due north from the equator with initial speed $v_0$ at elevation angle $\alpha$. Compute the eastward Coriolis deflection at the time the projectile returns to the ground (neglecting air resistance). Take $\Omega = 7.292 \times 10^{-5}$ rad/s.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

At the equator, the vertical component of $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$ is zero and the northward component $\Omega$ is in the horizontal plane. The Coriolis force on a northward-moving projectile has a vertical component and an eastward component.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

At the equator, $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$ points northward (horizontal). For a projectile with horizontal velocity $v_0\cos\alpha$ northward, the Coriolis acceleration $-2\boldsymbol{\Omega} \times \mathbf{v}$ has an eastward component $2\Omega v_0\cos\alpha\sin\theta$ where $\theta$ is the angle of the velocity from horizontal. To leading order, the eastward acceleration is $a_E \approx 2\Omega v_0\cos\alpha$ (taking the velocity as approximately horizontal for the dominant deflection). The time of flight is $T = 2v_0\sin\alpha/g$. Integrating: $\delta_E \approx \tfrac{1}{3}\Omega v_0^3 g^{-1}\sin\alpha(4\cos^2\alpha - \sin^2\alpha)$ from a careful treatment, or approximately $a_E T^2/2 = \Omega v_0^2 \sin(2\alpha)/g$ at leading order. For $v_0 = 500$ m/s and $\alpha = 45^\circ$: $\delta_E \sim 2 \times 7.3 \times 10^{-5} \times 500^2 / 9.8 \approx 3.7$ km, which is significant for long-range ballistics.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

A satellite in a circular equatorial orbit of radius $r$ and period $T$ is observed from a frame rotating with Earth's angular velocity $\Omega$. Show that the satellite's angular velocity in this frame is $\omega_{\mathrm{obs}} = 2\pi/T - \Omega$ for prograde orbits, and compute the observed period for a geostationary satellite.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The angular velocity in the inertial frame is $2\pi/T$. The rotating frame subtracts $\Omega$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the inertial frame the satellite has angular velocity $\omega_{\mathrm{in}} = 2\pi/T$. In the Earth-rotating frame the angular velocity is $\omega_{\mathrm{obs}} = \omega_{\mathrm{in}} - \Omega$. For a geostationary satellite, $\omega_{\mathrm{in}} = \Omega$, so $\omega_{\mathrm{obs}} = 0$ — the satellite appears stationary. The rotating-frame equation of motion includes centrifugal and Coriolis terms, but for a circular orbit at constant radius, the Coriolis force is purely radial and combines with the centrifugal force to modify the effective central force. The equilibrium condition in the rotating frame (the satellite at rest) requires that gravity exactly balance the centrifugal force at geostationary radius $r_G = (GM/\Omega^2)^{1/3} \approx 42\,164$ km.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### Lagrangian in a rotating frame

The Lagrangian for a particle of mass $m$ in a frame rotating with constant $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ about the $z$-axis is

$$
L = \frac{1}{2}m(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2 + \dot{z}^2) + m\omega(x\dot{y} - y\dot{x}) + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2(x^2 + y^2) - V(x, y, z).
$$

The conjugate momenta are $p_x = m(\dot{x} - \omega y)$, $p_y = m(\dot{y} + \omega x)$, $p_z = m\dot{z}$. The canonical momentum $p_x$ differs from the mechanical momentum $m\dot{x}$ by $-m\omega y$, reflecting the fact that the velocity-dependent Coriolis term in the Lagrangian shifts the momentum. The Hamiltonian is

$$
H = \frac{p_x^2 + p_y^2 + p_z^2}{2m} - \omega(xp_y - yp_x) + V(x,y,z).
$$

This is not the kinetic-plus-potential energy in either frame; it is the Jacobi integral expressed in canonical form.

### The Jacobi integral in celestial mechanics

In the restricted three-body problem, one works in a frame rotating with the orbital frequency of the two massive bodies. The effective potential $V_{\mathrm{eff}} = V_{\mathrm{grav}} + V_{\mathrm{cf}}$ has five critical points (the Lagrange points $L_1$ through $L_5$). The Jacobi integral $J = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}_{S'}|^2 + V_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is the only integral of the restricted three-body problem. Hill's surfaces (zero-velocity curves) are the level sets $J = V_{\mathrm{eff}}$; a particle with a given Jacobi constant cannot cross into regions where $V_{\mathrm{eff}} > J$.

### Foucault pendulum as adiabatic invariant

The Foucault pendulum precession can be understood as a geometric phase — the Hannay angle of an adiabatic parallel transport of the swing plane around the latitude circle. The swing plane is carried along by the local vertical component of $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$, and the total precession per day equals the solid angle subtended by the latitude circle as seen from the rotation axis. This connects the Foucault precession to the Berry phase in quantum mechanics and to the holonomy of the Levi-Civita connection on the sphere.

### Earth's figure and geoid

The equilibrium shape of a rotating fluid body under self-gravity is an oblate spheroid. At the surface, the gravitational potential $-GM/r$ and the centrifugal potential $-\tfrac{1}{2}\Omega^2 \rho^2$ must sum to a constant (the geoid). Expanding to first order in the small flattening parameter $\epsilon = (a - b)/a$ where $a$ is the equatorial radius and $b$ the polar radius, one finds $\epsilon \approx \Omega^2 a^3 / (2GM)$. For Earth this gives $\epsilon \approx 1/290$, matching the observed oblateness of about 1/298 (the discrepancy is because Earth is not a uniform fluid). The geoid — the equipotential surface that defines "mean sea level" — departs from a sphere by up to $\pm 100$ m due to mass anomalies in the mantle.

The effective gravitational acceleration varies from $g_{\mathrm{pole}} \approx 9.832$ m/s$^2$ to $g_{\mathrm{eq}} \approx 9.780$ m/s$^2$. About half this difference is centrifugal, the other half is the greater distance from Earth's centre at the equator due to oblateness.

### General rotating-frame mechanics in component form

For a frame rotating about the $z$-axis with constant angular velocity $\omega$, the equations of motion in Cartesian components are:

$$
\ddot{x} = \frac{F_x}{m} + 2\omega\dot{y} + \omega^2 x,
$$

$$
\ddot{y} = \frac{F_y}{m} - 2\omega\dot{x} + \omega^2 y,
$$

$$
\ddot{z} = \frac{F_z}{m}.
$$

The $2\omega\dot{y}$ and $-2\omega\dot{x}$ terms are the Coriolis acceleration components; the $\omega^2 x$ and $\omega^2 y$ terms are the centrifugal acceleration. The $z$-equation is unaffected by the rotation. These coupled ODEs are the starting point for most quantitative rotating-frame calculations in geophysics and engineering.

For time-dependent rotation $\boldsymbol{\omega}(t)$, the Euler force $-m\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} \times \mathbf{r}$ adds a further contribution. This term is important for spin-up and spin-down problems (e.g., a centrifuge whose motor is accelerating) but vanishes for the practically important case of steady rotation.

### Inertial oscillations

Consider a free particle (no real forces) on a rotating plane. The equations of motion are $\ddot{x} = 2\omega\dot{y}$ and $\ddot{y} = -2\omega\dot{x}$. Combined into the complex variable $\zeta = x + iy$, this gives $\ddot\zeta + 2i\omega\dot\zeta = 0$. The general solution is $\zeta(t) = A + Be^{-2i\omega t}$: the particle executes circular **inertial oscillations** at frequency $2\omega$, superimposed on a steady drift. The radius of the inertial circle is $v_0/(2\omega)$ where $v_0$ is the initial speed. In the ocean, inertial oscillations are a dominant response to wind forcing; their period is half the pendulum day at each latitude.

**Synthesis.** The rotating-frame framework puts together centrifugal potential, Coriolis deflection, and the Jacobi integral into a single Lagrangian structure; the central insight is that fictitious forces are not ad hoc additions but emerge systematically from the time-dependent coordinate change between inertial and non-inertial frames. This framework generalises to arbitrary time-dependent rotations and translations, and the Jacobi integral is dual to the energy integral in the inertial frame through the Legendre transform of the velocity-dependent coupling term. Putting these together, the rotating-frame Lagrangian is the foundational reason that restricted three-body dynamics and geophysical fluid dynamics have the structure they do. The geoid, the inertial oscillation, and the Foucault precession are three faces of the same mathematical structure — the kinematics of rotating coordinates applied to three different physical systems.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Newton's rotating-bucket experiment (Principia, 1687) was the first argument that rotational motion is absolute, not relative: the water surface curves because the bucket rotates, not because of any relation to other bodies. Mach's critique (The Science of Mechanics, 1883) argued that the bucket rotates relative to the fixed stars, and that in a universe with no other matter the surface would not curve. Einstein's general relativity partially resolves this: the geodesic equation in curved spacetime replaces Newton's second law, and fictitious forces arise from the Christoffel symbols, which are the gravitational analogue of the inertial-frame correction.

Foucault's 1851 demonstration at the Pantheon was a public spectacle witnessed by thousands. His pendulum precessed at exactly the predicted rate of $360^\circ \sin(48.86^\circ) \approx 270^\circ$ per day, providing the first direct laboratory proof of Earth's rotation independent of astronomical observation. The experiment was repeated worldwide: a pendulum at the South Pole precesses at $360^\circ$ per day, while one at the equator does not precess at all.

Coriolis's 1835 paper "Sur les equations du mouvement relatif des systemes de corps" established the mathematical framework for relative motion in rotating frames. The Coriolis effect was not merely a theoretical curiosity: it proved essential for accurate long-range artillery (the Paris gun of WWI had to account for a Coriolis deflection of hundreds of metres), meteorology (cyclone rotation), and oceanography (western boundary currents).
