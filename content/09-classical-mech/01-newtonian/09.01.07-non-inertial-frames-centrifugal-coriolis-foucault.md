## Intuition [Beginner]

Stand on a merry-go-round and spin. You feel a force pushing you outward, away from the centre. If you let go, you fly off. But what pushes you? There is no physical object exerting an outward force on your body. The rope or railing you grip pulls you inward. The "outward push" is not a real force at all. It is a **fictitious force** — an artefact of describing motion from a rotating, and therefore non-inertial, frame of reference [ref: TODO_REF].

Newton's laws hold exactly only in **inertial frames**: frames that are not accelerating and not rotating. In an inertial frame, a body with no forces on it moves in a straight line at constant speed. A rotating frame violates this condition. When you describe motion from a rotating platform, Newton's second law $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$ no longer holds unless you add extra terms to account for the frame's rotation. These extra terms are the fictitious forces.

The most familiar fictitious force is the **centrifugal force**. On the merry-go-round, you are at rest in the rotating frame. In the inertial frame (the ground), you are accelerating inward — the railing exerts a centripetal (centre-seeking) force. The centrifugal force is the fictitious force that "explains" why you remain at rest in the rotating frame despite the real inward pull of the railing. It balances the centripetal force so that the net force in the rotating frame is zero [ref: TODO_REF].

A second fictitious force appears when something moves within the rotating frame. Throw a ball straight outward from the centre of the merry-go-round and it curves. In the rotating frame, the ball's path bends to one side. The fictitious force responsible for this deflection is the **Coriolis force**. It acts perpendicular to the velocity of the moving object, deflecting it sideways without speeding it up or slowing it down [ref: TODO_REF].

The Coriolis force shapes weather on Earth. The planet rotates, so the atmosphere is a rotating frame. Air flowing from high pressure to low pressure does not travel in a straight line. In the northern hemisphere, the Coriolis force deflects moving air to the right, creating the counter-clockwise circulation around low-pressure systems (cyclones) and clockwise circulation around high-pressure systems (anticyclones). Without the Coriolis force, air would rush directly from high to low pressure and weather systems would dissipate in hours instead of persisting for days [ref: TODO_REF].

The most dramatic demonstration of Earth's rotation is **Foucault's pendulum**. In 1851, Leon Foucault hung a 67-metre pendulum from the dome of the Pantheon in Paris. As it swung, the plane of oscillation slowly rotated, completing a full circuit in about 32 hours. In the inertial frame, the pendulum plane is fixed and the Earth rotates beneath it. In the Earth's rotating frame, the Coriolis force causes the slow precession. At the north pole, the pendulum plane rotates once per sidereal day (about 23 hours 56 minutes). At the equator, there is no rotation at all. At latitude $\lambda$, the rotation period is $24/\sin\lambda$ hours — longer at lower latitudes, diverging at the equator [ref: TODO_REF].

The key insight: fictitious forces are not forces at all. They are corrections you must add to Newton's second law when you choose to work in a non-inertial frame. In the inertial frame, they vanish. But working in the rotating frame is often far more convenient — nobody wants to describe weather patterns from a frame fixed to distant stars.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A child sits 1.5 m from the centre of a merry-go-round rotating at 10 revolutions per minute. What is the centrifugal force on the child if their mass is 30 kg?

**Step 1: Convert angular velocity to radians per second.**

10 revolutions per minute equals $10 \times 2\pi / 60 = \pi/3 \approx 1.047$ rad/s.

**Step 2: Compute the centrifugal force.**

The centrifugal force magnitude is $m\omega^2 r = 30 \times (1.047)^2 \times 1.5 = 30 \times 1.096 \times 1.5 = 49.3$ N.

**Step 3: Interpret.**

In the rotating frame, the child experiences an outward force of about 49 N (roughly 5 kg equivalent). To remain at rest in the rotating frame, the child must grip something that provides an equal and opposite inward (centripetal) force. In the inertial frame, there is no outward force. The grip provides the centripetal force $mv^2/r = m\omega^2 r$ that keeps the child moving in a circle [ref: TODO_REF].

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Time derivative in a rotating frame

Let $\mathbf{Q}$ be any vector. In an inertial frame with basis vectors $\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i$, the time derivative is $\left(d\mathbf{Q}/dt\right)_{\rm inertial} = \dot{Q}_i\,\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i$. In a frame rotating with angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ relative to the inertial frame, the basis vectors are time-dependent. The relation between the two derivatives is

$$
\boxed{\left(\frac{d\mathbf{Q}}{dt}\right)_{\!\!\text{inertial}} \;=\; \left(\frac{d\mathbf{Q}}{dt}\right)_{\!\!\text{rot}} \;+\; \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{Q}.}
$$

The term $\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{Q}$ accounts for the rotation of the basis vectors. This formula is the foundation of all fictitious-force derivations [ref: TODO_REF].

### Equation of motion in a rotating frame

Apply the derivative formula to the position vector $\mathbf{r}$ of a particle:

$$
\mathbf{v}_{\rm inertial} \;=\; \mathbf{v}_{\rm rot} \;+\; \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}.
$$

Apply it again to the velocity:

$$
\mathbf{a}_{\rm inertial} \;=\; \mathbf{a}_{\rm rot} \;+\; 2\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\rm rot} \;+\; \boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}) \;+\; \dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}\times\mathbf{r}.
$$

Newton's second law in the inertial frame is $m\mathbf{a}_{\rm inertial} = \mathbf{F}$, where $\mathbf{F}$ is the sum of all real forces. Substituting and rearranging:

$$
m\mathbf{a}_{\rm rot} \;=\; \mathbf{F} \;-\; 2m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\rm rot} \;-\; m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}) \;-\; m\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}\times\mathbf{r}.
$$

The three correction terms are the fictitious forces:

$$
\boxed{m\mathbf{a}_{\rm rot} \;=\; \mathbf{F} \;+\; \mathbf{F}_{\rm centrifugal} \;+\; \mathbf{F}_{\rm Coriolis} \;+\; \mathbf{F}_{\rm Euler},}
$$

where [ref: TODO_REF]:

- **Centrifugal force:** $\mathbf{F}_{\rm cf} = -m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r})$. Always points away from the rotation axis. Its magnitude is $m\omega^2\rho$, where $\rho$ is the perpendicular distance from the rotation axis.

- **Coriolis force:** $\mathbf{F}_{\rm Cor} = -2m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\rm rot}$. Perpendicular to the velocity in the rotating frame. It does no work ($\mathbf{F}_{\rm Cor}\cdot\mathbf{v}_{\rm rot} = 0$).

- **Euler force:** $\mathbf{F}_{\rm Euler} = -m\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}\times\mathbf{r}$. Present only when the angular velocity is changing in magnitude or direction. For uniform rotation ($\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} = 0$), the Euler force vanishes.

### Properties of the fictitious forces

The centrifugal force depends only on position, not on velocity. It is radial (perpendicular to the rotation axis, pointing outward). Its magnitude at distance $\rho$ from the rotation axis is $m\omega^2\rho$. It can be derived from a potential:

$$
V_{\rm cf} \;=\; -\tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2\rho^2.
$$

The Coriolis force depends on velocity in the rotating frame. It is perpendicular to $\mathbf{v}_{\rm rot}$ and therefore does no work. It cannot be derived from a potential (it is velocity-dependent), but it can be derived from the velocity-dependent generalized potential $U = m\mathbf{v}_{\rm rot}\cdot(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r})$ in the Lagrangian framework [ref: TODO_REF].

The Euler force depends on the rate of change of angular velocity. For a frame rotating at constant angular speed (such as the Earth on short timescales), $\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}} = 0$ and the Euler force is absent. It matters for accelerating machinery and for frames with precessing rotation axes.

### General non-inertial frames: accelerating and rotating

If the origin of the rotating frame also accelerates with acceleration $\mathbf{A}_0$ relative to the inertial frame, an additional fictitious force appears:

$$
\mathbf{F}_{\rm trans} \;=\; -m\mathbf{A}_0.
$$

The full equation of motion becomes

$$
\boxed{m\mathbf{a}_{\rm rot} \;=\; \mathbf{F} \;-\; m\mathbf{A}_0 \;-\; 2m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}_{\rm rot} \;-\; m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}) \;-\; m\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}\times\mathbf{r}.}
$$

The translational fictitious force $-m\mathbf{A}_0$ is the simplest non-inertial correction. In an elevator accelerating upward at rate $a$, a passenger feels heavier: the effective gravitational acceleration is $g + a$. In an elevator accelerating downward, the passenger feels lighter. If the elevator cable snaps and the car free-falls ($\mathbf{A}_0 = \mathbf{g}$), the translational force exactly cancels gravity and the passenger experiences weightlessness [ref: TODO_REF].

### Foucault pendulum

A simple pendulum of length $\ell$ and bob mass $m$ swings at latitude $\lambda$ on the Earth. In the rotating frame of the Earth, the equation of motion includes the Coriolis force. For small oscillations ($x$, $y$ small compared to $\ell$), the linearised equations are

$$
\ddot{x} \;=\; -\frac{g}{\ell}x \;+\; 2\omega_z\,\dot{y}, \qquad \ddot{y} \;=\; -\frac{g}{\ell}y \;-\; 2\omega_z\,\dot{x},
$$

where $\omega_z = \omega\sin\lambda$ is the vertical component of the Earth's angular velocity at latitude $\lambda$. The centrifugal force is absorbed into the effective $g$ (which is the measured value) and does not affect the pendulum's precession [ref: TODO_REF].

The precession rate of the swing plane is

$$
\boxed{\Omega_{\rm prec} \;=\; -\omega\sin\lambda,}
$$

negative because the plane rotates clockwise (when viewed from above) in the northern hemisphere. The precession period is $T_{\rm prec} = 2\pi/(\omega\sin\lambda) = T_{\rm sidereal}/\sin\lambda$. At the poles ($\lambda = \pm 90^\circ$), $T_{\rm prec} = T_{\rm sidereal} \approx 23.93$ h. At the equator ($\lambda = 0$), the precession rate is zero.

The precession is purely a Coriolis effect. The component of $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ along the local vertical ($\omega\sin\lambda$) produces the sideways deflection that rotates the swing plane. The horizontal component of $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ does not contribute to the precession (it produces a Coriolis force in the vertical direction, which merely modifies the effective tension in the string).

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Key result (Fictitious forces from the rotating-frame transform).** *The equation of motion in a frame rotating with angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ relative to an inertial frame, with the origin of the rotating frame accelerating at $\mathbf{A}_0$, is*

$$
\boxed{m\mathbf{a}' \;=\; \mathbf{F} \;-\; m\mathbf{A}_0 \;-\; 2m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}' \;-\; m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}') \;-\; m\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}\times\mathbf{r}',}
$$

*where primed quantities are measured in the rotating frame.*

**Derivation.** Let the position of a particle in the inertial frame be $\mathbf{r}$ and in the rotating frame be $\mathbf{r}'$. The origins are related by $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{R}_0 + \mathbf{r}'$, where $\mathbf{R}_0(t)$ is the position of the rotating-frame origin in the inertial frame. The derivative formula gives

$$
\dot{\mathbf{r}} \;=\; \dot{\mathbf{R}}_0 + \dot{\mathbf{r}}' + \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}'.
$$

Differentiating once more:

$$
\ddot{\mathbf{r}} \;=\; \ddot{\mathbf{R}}_0 + \ddot{\mathbf{r}}' + 2\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\dot{\mathbf{r}}' + \boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}') + \dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}\times\mathbf{r}'.
$$

Now apply Newton's second law in the inertial frame: $m\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = \mathbf{F}$. Solve for $\ddot{\mathbf{r}}'$:

$$
m\ddot{\mathbf{r}}' \;=\; \mathbf{F} - m\ddot{\mathbf{R}}_0 - 2m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\dot{\mathbf{r}}' - m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}') - m\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}\times\mathbf{r}'.
$$

Each correction term is identified as a fictitious force: the translational force $-m\ddot{\mathbf{R}}_0$, the Coriolis force $-2m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\dot{\mathbf{r}}'$, the centrifugal force $-m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}')$, and the Euler force $-m\dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}\times\mathbf{r}'$ [ref: TODO_REF].

### Worked example: effective gravity on Earth

The Earth rotates with angular velocity $\omega \approx 7.292 \times 10^{-5}$ rad/s. At the equator ($\lambda = 0$, distance from axis $\rho = R_E \approx 6.378 \times 10^6$ m), the centrifugal acceleration is

$$
a_{\rm cf} \;=\; \omega^2 R_E \;=\; (7.292\times 10^{-5})^2 \times 6.378\times 10^6 \;\approx\; 0.0339 \;\text{m/s}^2.
$$

The measured gravitational acceleration at the equator ($g \approx 9.780$ m/s$^2$) is less than the gravitational acceleration at the poles ($g \approx 9.832$ m/s$^2$) by about 0.052 m/s$^2$. The difference is partly due to the centrifugal force (0.034 m/s$^2$) and partly due to the Earth's equatorial bulge (the extra 0.018 m/s$^2$ comes from being farther from the centre at the equator). The effective gravity $\mathbf{g}_{\rm eff}$ is the vector sum of the true gravitational attraction and the centrifugal acceleration [ref: TODO_REF].

### Worked example: projectile deflection due to Coriolis force

A projectile is fired due north from latitude $\lambda = 45^\circ$ with speed $v_0 = 500$ m/s. Find the eastward deflection due to the Coriolis force.

The Coriolis acceleration in the eastward direction is $a_E = 2\omega_z v_N = 2\omega\sin\lambda\, v_N$, where $v_N \approx v_0$ (the northward velocity). Using $\omega = 7.292\times 10^{-5}$ rad/s:

$$
a_E \;=\; 2 \times 7.292\times 10^{-5} \times \sin(45^\circ) \times 500 \;\approx\; 0.0516 \;\text{m/s}^2.
$$

The time of flight depends on the range. For a range of 10 km, the flight time is roughly $t \approx 10^4/500 = 20$ s. The eastward deflection is

$$
\delta_E \;\approx\; \tfrac{1}{2}a_E t^2 \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2} \times 0.0516 \times 400 \;\approx\; 10.3 \;\text{m}.
$$

The projectile lands about 10 m to the east of where it would land on a non-rotating Earth. This is a substantial deflection for long-range ballistics and must be accounted for in artillery fire [ref: TODO_REF].

### Foucault pendulum derivation

Write the pendulum's horizontal position as $\mathbf{u} = (x, y)$ in the local tangent plane at latitude $\lambda$. For small oscillations, the restoring force is $-(mg/\ell)\mathbf{u}$. Including the Coriolis force, the horizontal equations of motion are:

$$
\ddot{x} + \frac{g}{\ell}x \;=\; 2\omega\sin\lambda\,\dot{y}, \qquad \ddot{y} + \frac{g}{\ell}y \;=\; -2\omega\sin\lambda\,\dot{x}.
$$

Define $\omega_z = \omega\sin\lambda$ and the natural frequency $\omega_0 = \sqrt{g/\ell}$. Combine into a complex variable $\eta = x + iy$:

$$
\ddot{\eta} + 2i\omega_z\dot{\eta} + \omega_0^2\eta \;=\; 0.
$$

The ansatz $\eta = e^{-i\omega_z t}\zeta(t)$ transforms this to

$$
\ddot{\zeta} + (\omega_0^2 + \omega_z^2)\zeta \;=\; 0.
$$

Since $\omega_z \ll \omega_0$ (the Earth rotates much more slowly than a pendulum swings), the correction $\omega_z^2$ is negligible and $\zeta$ oscillates at frequency $\omega_0$. The solution is

$$
\eta(t) \;=\; e^{-i\omega_z t}\left(A\cos\omega_0 t + B\sin\omega_0 t\right).
$$

The pendulum swings at frequency $\omega_0$ while the plane of oscillation rotates at angular rate $-\omega_z = -\omega\sin\lambda$. This is the Foucault precession [ref: TODO_REF].

**Bridge.** The rotating-frame formalism developed here connects directly to rigid-body dynamics [09.03.01], where the angular-velocity vector $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and the rotating-frame derivative formula become the language of Euler's equations for tumbling bodies. The centrifugal potential $V_{\rm cf} = -\tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2\rho^2$ appears in the Lagrangian formulation [09.02.01] as a velocity-dependent term when the Lagrangian is expressed in non-inertial coordinates. The general non-inertial frame equation, with both translation and rotation, anticipates the equivalence principle of general relativity [09.06.01], where gravitational and fictitious forces are identified as manifestations of the same geometric structure.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

A satellite orbits Earth in a circular orbit of radius $r$. In the rotating frame that co-rotates with the satellite (so the satellite is at rest), write down the centrifugal force and compare it with the gravitational force. What is the net force on the satellite in this frame?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The angular velocity of the co-rotating frame equals the satellite's orbital angular velocity: $\omega = \sqrt{GM/r^3}$. The centrifugal force is $m\omega^2 r$ outward, and gravity is $GMm/r^2$ inward.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the co-rotating frame, the centrifugal force is $m\omega^2 r$ radially outward and gravity is $GMm/r^2$ radially inward. Since $\omega^2 = GM/r^3$, the centrifugal force equals $GMm/r^2$, exactly balancing gravity. The net force is zero, consistent with the satellite being at rest in the co-rotating frame. This is the basis of "weightlessness" in orbit: the gravitational and centrifugal forces cancel in the rotating frame.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

A bug crawls radially outward with speed $v$ on a disk rotating at angular velocity $\omega$. Find the magnitude and direction of the Coriolis force on the bug (as seen in the rotating frame).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Coriolis force is $\mathbf{F}_{\rm Cor} = -2m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}'$. The velocity in the rotating frame is radially outward. $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ is perpendicular to the disk.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\boldsymbol{\omega} = \omega\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ (perpendicular to the disk) and $\mathbf{v}' = v\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ (radially outward). Then $\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}' = \omega v(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\times\hat{\mathbf{r}}) = \omega v\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$, so $\mathbf{F}_{\rm Cor} = -2m\omega v\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$. The Coriolis force has magnitude $2m\omega v$ and points opposite to the direction of rotation (tangentially, against the rotation direction if $\omega > 0$). In the inertial frame, the bug needs extra tangential velocity as it moves outward to conserve angular momentum; in the rotating frame, this appears as the Coriolis deflection.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 3 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the Coriolis force does no work. Prove $\mathbf{F}_{\rm Cor}\cdot\mathbf{v}' = 0$ for arbitrary $\mathbf{v}'$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Coriolis force is proportional to $\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}'$. Use the property of the cross product: $\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}) = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathbf{F}_{\rm Cor}\cdot\mathbf{v}' = -2m(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}')\cdot\mathbf{v}'$. The scalar triple product with two identical vectors vanishes: $(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}')\cdot\mathbf{v}' = \boldsymbol{\omega}\cdot(\mathbf{v}'\times\mathbf{v}') = 0$. Therefore $\mathbf{F}_{\rm Cor}\cdot\mathbf{v}' = 0$ for all $\mathbf{v}'$. The Coriolis force is always perpendicular to the velocity, so it changes direction but not speed.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A mass $m$ is free to slide without friction on a horizontal rod that rotates about one end at constant angular velocity $\omega$. Find the radial equation of motion and solve it for the position $r(t)$ given initial conditions $r(0) = r_0$, $\dot{r}(0) = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In the rotating frame, the centrifugal force is $m\omega^2 r$ outward and the Coriolis force is perpendicular to the rod (absorbed by the rod's normal force). Only the centrifugal force drives the radial motion.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the rotating frame, radial forces: centrifugal $m\omega^2 r$ outward, Coriolis $-2m\omega\dot{r}$ perpendicular to rod (provided by the rod's constraint, does no radial work). Radial equation: $m\ddot{r} = m\omega^2 r$, or $\ddot{r} = \omega^2 r$. Solution: $r(t) = A e^{\omega t} + B e^{-\omega t}$. Initial conditions: $r(0) = r_0$ gives $A + B = r_0$; $\dot{r}(0) = 0$ gives $\omega(A - B) = 0$, so $A = B = r_0/2$. Result: $r(t) = r_0\cosh(\omega t)$. The mass accelerates exponentially outward. The Coriolis force acts sideways, and the rod provides the normal force to keep the mass on the rod.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the centrifugal potential $V_{\rm cf} = -\tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2\rho^2$ and verify that $\mathbf{F}_{\rm cf} = -\nabla V_{\rm cf}$ gives the centrifugal force $-m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Decompose $\mathbf{r}$ into a component $\boldsymbol{\rho}$ perpendicular to $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and a component $\mathbf{z}$ parallel to $\boldsymbol{\omega}$. The centrifugal force depends only on $\boldsymbol{\rho}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $\mathbf{r} = \boldsymbol{\rho} + \mathbf{z}$ where $\boldsymbol{\rho}$ is perpendicular to $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and $\mathbf{z}$ is parallel. Then $\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r} = \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\boldsymbol{\rho}$ (the parallel component gives zero in the cross product). Then $\boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}) = \boldsymbol{\omega}\times(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\boldsymbol{\rho}) = -\omega^2\boldsymbol{\rho}$ (using $\hat{\mathbf{z}}\times(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\times\boldsymbol{\rho}) = -\boldsymbol{\rho}$ for perpendicular $\boldsymbol{\rho}$). So $\mathbf{F}_{\rm cf} = m\omega^2\boldsymbol{\rho}$. The centrifugal potential is $V_{\rm cf} = -\tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2\rho^2$. Then $-\nabla V_{\rm cf} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial\boldsymbol{\rho}}(-\tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2\rho^2) = m\omega^2\boldsymbol{\rho}$, which matches the centrifugal force.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

A train of mass $m$ travels due south at speed $v = 200$ km/h at latitude $\lambda = 60^\circ$N. Find the magnitude and direction of the Coriolis force on the train. Compare it to the train's weight.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Coriolis acceleration is $2\omega\sin\lambda\, v$ directed eastward for southward motion in the northern hemisphere. Use $\omega = 7.292\times 10^{-5}$ rad/s.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$v = 200\times 10^3/3600 \approx 55.6$ m/s. Coriolis acceleration: $a_{\rm Cor} = 2\omega\sin\lambda\, v = 2 \times 7.292\times 10^{-5} \times \sin(60^\circ) \times 55.6 \approx 7.02\times 10^{-3}$ m/s$^2$. The force is directed eastward (southward velocity in the northern hemisphere is deflected to the right, which is west -- correction: actually, $-\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v}'$ for $\mathbf{v}'$ pointing south gives eastward deflection). The ratio to gravity: $a_{\rm Cor}/g \approx 7.02\times 10^{-3}/9.8 \approx 7.2\times 10^{-4}$. The Coriolis force is about 0.07% of the train's weight. It is tiny but measurable with sensitive instruments and produces a measurable asymmetry in the rail wear on east vs. west rails.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the Foucault precession rate $\Omega_{\rm prec} = -\omega\sin\lambda$ by solving the complex equation $\ddot{\eta} + 2i\omega_z\dot{\eta} + \omega_0^2\eta = 0$ for $\eta = x + iy$, treating $\omega_z/\omega_0$ as a small parameter. Show that the secular terms arise from the $2i\omega_z\dot{\eta}$ coupling.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the substitution $\eta(t) = e^{-i\omega_z t}\zeta(t)$ to remove the first-derivative term, then solve for $\zeta$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Substitute $\eta = e^{-i\omega_z t}\zeta$: $\dot{\eta} = e^{-i\omega_z t}(\dot{\zeta} - i\omega_z\zeta)$, $\ddot{\eta} = e^{-i\omega_z t}(\ddot{\zeta} - 2i\omega_z\dot{\zeta} - \omega_z^2\zeta)$. Inserting: $e^{-i\omega_z t}[\ddot{\zeta} - 2i\omega_z\dot{\zeta} - \omega_z^2\zeta + 2i\omega_z(\dot{\zeta} - i\omega_z\zeta) + \omega_0^2\zeta] = 0$. The $-2i\omega_z\dot{\zeta}$ and $+2i\omega_z\dot{\zeta}$ cancel. Remaining: $\ddot{\zeta} + (\omega_0^2 + \omega_z^2)\zeta = 0$. Since $\omega_z \ll \omega_0$, approximate $\omega_0^2 + \omega_z^2 \approx \omega_0^2$. Solution: $\zeta(t) = A\cos\omega_0 t + B\sin\omega_0 t$. Full solution: $\eta(t) = e^{-i\omega_z t}(A\cos\omega_0 t + B\sin\omega_0 t)$. The $e^{-i\omega_z t}$ factor rotates the oscillation plane at rate $-\omega_z = -\omega\sin\lambda$, which is the Foucault precession.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

A thin spherical shell of radius $R$ rotates slowly with angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ in an otherwise empty universe. A test particle inside the shell experiences a force proportional to its velocity relative to the shell. This is the **Lense-Thirring effect** (frame-dragging). Estimate the precession rate for a Foucault pendulum inside such a shell and compare it with the precession rate from the Earth's rotation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Lense-Thirring precession rate for a thin shell of mass $M$ and radius $R$ is $\Omega_{\rm LT} = 2GJ/(c^2 R^3)$ where $J = \tfrac{2}{3}MR^2\omega$ is the angular momentum. This is a general-relativistic effect.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a thin spherical shell: $J = \tfrac{2}{3}MR^2\omega$. Lense-Thirring precession rate: $\Omega_{\rm LT} = 2GJ/(c^2R^3) = 4GM\omega/(3c^2R)$. For the Earth: $M \approx 5.97\times 10^{24}$ kg, $R \approx 6.37\times 10^6$ m, $\omega \approx 7.29\times 10^{-5}$ rad/s. Then $\Omega_{\rm LT} = 4 \times 6.67\times 10^{-11} \times 5.97\times 10^{24} \times 7.29\times 10^{-5} / (3 \times (3\times 10^8)^2 \times 6.37\times 10^6) \approx 7\times 10^{-14}$ rad/s. Compare with the Foucault precession at $45^\circ$: $\omega\sin 45^\circ \approx 5.2\times 10^{-5}$ rad/s. The ratio is $\sim 10^{-9}$. Frame-dragging is utterly negligible for Foucault pendulum experiments on Earth, though it has been measured by the Gravity Probe B satellite to about 20% accuracy.

</details>

</aside>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The rotating-frame derivative formula and the resulting fictitious-force equations are not represented in Mathlib. Formalising them would require: (1) a construction of SO(3) rotations parameterised by angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$, (2) the time-derivative transfer formula $(d/dt)_{\rm inertial} = (d/dt)_{\rm rot} + \boldsymbol{\omega}\times$, proved as a theorem about tangent-vector transport along a curve in the rotation group, and (3) the identification of the centrifugal, Coriolis, and Euler terms as forces in Newton's second law. The Foucault pendulum analysis would further require the small-oscillation linearisation and the complex-variable trick $\eta = x + iy$. None of this machinery exists in Mathlib currently.

## Advanced results [Master]

### The Lagrangian derivation of fictitious forces

The fictitious forces can be derived from the Lagrangian by writing $L = T - V$ in non-inertial coordinates. For a particle of mass $m$ in a frame rotating with constant $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and with origin coincident with the inertial origin, the velocity relation is $\mathbf{v}_{\rm inertial} = \mathbf{v}_{\rm rot} + \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}$. The kinetic energy is

$$
T \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}_{\rm rot} + \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}|^2 \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}mv_{\rm rot}^2 + m\mathbf{v}_{\rm rot}\cdot(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}) + \tfrac{1}{2}m|\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}|^2.
$$

The Lagrangian in the rotating frame is

$$
L \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}mv_{\rm rot}^2 + m\mathbf{v}_{\rm rot}\cdot(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}) - V + \tfrac{1}{2}m|\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}|^2.
$$

The term $m\mathbf{v}_{\rm rot}\cdot(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r})$ is a velocity-dependent generalized potential that generates the Coriolis force. The term $\tfrac{1}{2}m|\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r}|^2 = -V_{\rm cf}$ is the centrifugal potential. The Euler-Lagrange equations reproduce the rotating-frame equation of motion exactly [ref: TODO_REF].

### Coriolis force and atmospheric circulation

The large-scale circulation of the atmosphere is governed by the geostrophic balance between the Coriolis force and the horizontal pressure gradient:

$$
-2m\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{v} \;\approx\; -\nabla p\,dV.
$$

In the northern hemisphere, the Coriolis force deflects air to the right. Around a low-pressure centre, the deflection produces counter-clockwise circulation (cyclonic rotation). Around a high-pressure centre, it produces clockwise circulation (anticyclonic rotation). The **Rossby number** $Ro = U/(fL)$, where $U$ is the characteristic velocity, $f = 2\omega\sin\lambda$ is the Coriolis parameter, and $L$ is the characteristic length, measures the relative importance of inertial to Coriolis forces. For large-scale weather ($L \sim 1000$ km, $U \sim 10$ m/s), $Ro \sim 0.1$, indicating the Coriolis force dominates. For a bathtub drain ($L \sim 0.1$ m, $U \sim 0.1$ m/s), $Ro \sim 10^4$, and the Coriolis force is negligible -- the direction of bathtub drainage is determined by residual angular momentum from filling, not by the Earth's rotation [ref: TODO_REF].

### General relativity: frame-dragging and the Lense-Thirring effect

The identification of fictitious forces with aspects of spacetime geometry reaches its culmination in general relativity. In the weak-field, slow-rotation limit, a rotating mass drags the local inertial frames in the direction of rotation. This **Lense-Thirring effect** (or frame-dragging) causes a gyroscope near a rotating mass to precess at rate

$$
\boldsymbol{\Omega}_{\rm LT} \;=\; \frac{G}{c^2 r^3}\left[3(\mathbf{J}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{r}})\hat{\mathbf{r}} - \mathbf{J}\right],
$$

where $\mathbf{J}$ is the angular momentum of the central body. The effect is analogous to the Coriolis force from a rotating frame, but it is a genuine gravitational effect -- a distortion of spacetime itself, not a coordinate artefact [ref: TODO_REF].

The Gravity Probe B experiment (2004--2011) measured the geodetic precession (6606 mas/yr) and the Lense-Thirring precession (39 mas/yr) of gyroscopes in a polar orbit around Earth, confirming the frame-dragging prediction to about 20% accuracy. The much more precise LARES satellite (launched 2012) has improved this to about 1% accuracy.

### The equivalence principle and non-inertial frames

The translational fictitious force $-m\mathbf{A}_0$ is proportional to mass, just like gravity. This is not a coincidence -- it is the **weak equivalence principle**: the inertial mass (which multiplies $\mathbf{A}_0$) equals the gravitational mass (which multiplies $\mathbf{g}$). In a freely falling elevator, the gravitational force $m\mathbf{g}$ and the fictitious force $-m\mathbf{A}_0$ (with $\mathbf{A}_0 = \mathbf{g}$) exactly cancel, producing a local inertial frame [ref: TODO_REF].

Einstein's insight was to elevate this from an observation about Newtonian mechanics to a fundamental principle: gravity is not a force but a manifestation of curved spacetime. In general relativity, the fictitious forces of non-inertial frames and the gravitational "force" are both encoded in the metric tensor $g_{\mu\nu}$. The centrifugal and Coriolis forces of this unit are the flat-spacetime precursors of the gravitational effects that arise when spacetime is curved.

### The rotating-frame approach in computational mechanics

In numerical simulations of rotating machinery (turbine blades, helicopter rotors, planetary atmospheres), the rotating-frame formulation is essential. Computing in the inertial frame would require resolving the rapid rotation at every timestep; in the rotating frame, the rotation is removed and only the slower relative motion needs to be tracked. The centrifugal and Coriolis forces are added as source terms to the equations of motion.

The approach generalises to multiple rotating frames (e.g., a planet orbiting a star while rotating about its own axis) through the composition of rotations: successive applications of the rotating-frame derivative formula. The total fictitious force is the sum of contributions from each rotating frame, with cross-terms coupling the different rotations [ref: TODO_REF].

**Synthesis.** The fictitious forces of non-inertial frames are corrections to Newton's second law that appear when the reference frame accelerates or rotates. The centrifugal force is radial and derivable from a potential. The Coriolis force is velocity-dependent, perpendicular to motion, and does no work. The Euler force arises from changing angular velocity. Together with the translational fictitious force, they account for all non-inertial effects in Newtonian mechanics. The Foucault pendulum demonstrates that even a small rotation rate ($\omega \approx 7.3\times 10^{-5}$ rad/s for Earth) produces measurable effects when integrated over time. The generalisation to curved spacetime -- where gravity and fictitious forces are unified in the metric -- is the conceptual heart of general relativity. The practical importance of rotating-frame analysis in engineering, meteorology, and navigation ensures that these fictitious forces, despite their name, are indispensable tools.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Gaspard-Gustave de Coriolis published his analysis of the forces in rotating frames in 1835, in a paper titled "Sur les equations du mouvement relatif des systemes de corps" (*Journal de l'Ecole Polytechnique*, Cahier 24). Coriolis was building on the earlier work of Euler and Lagrange on rigid-body rotation. His contribution was the precise identification of the two terms -- the centrifugal acceleration and the compound centrifugal acceleration (now called the Coriolis acceleration) -- that appear when Newton's equations are transformed to a rotating frame [ref: TODO_REF].

Coriolis was not thinking of meteorology. His original motivation was the theory of waterwheels and rotating machinery. The application to atmospheric science came decades later. William Ferrel in 1856 identified the role of the Coriolis deflection in shaping atmospheric circulation, and the full geostrophic balance was developed by the Bergen school of meteorology in the early twentieth century.

Leon Foucault's pendulum experiment of 1851 was designed as a public demonstration of the Earth's rotation. He first tested a 2-metre pendulum in his cellar, then an 11-metre pendulum at the Observatoire de Paris, and finally the 67-metre pendulum in the Pantheon. The demonstration was an immediate sensation. Napoleon III requested a special showing. The pendulum's slow precession -- visible to any observer who waited a few minutes -- provided the first direct, laboratory-scale proof of the Earth's rotation that did not depend on astronomical observations [ref: TODO_REF].

Prior to Foucault, the Earth's rotation was inferred from stellar observations (the apparent motion of stars across the sky) and from the shape of the Earth (the equatorial bulge, measured by geodetic surveys). Foucault's pendulum made the rotation visible indoors, without reference to the sky. It remains one of the most elegant experiments in the history of physics.

The philosophical dimension: fictitious forces illustrate that the form of physical laws depends on the choice of reference frame. Newton's first law ("a body with no force on it moves in a straight line") is not a universal truth -- it holds only in inertial frames. In a rotating frame, a body with no real forces on it curves. This raises the question: what distinguishes inertial frames from non-inertial frames? In Newtonian mechanics, the answer is Machian: inertial frames are those fixed relative to the distant stars. In general relativity, the answer is local: inertial frames are the freely falling frames determined by the local spacetime geometry. The fictitious forces of this unit are the Newtonian precursors to the geometric understanding of gravity.

A further philosophical point: the Coriolis force is velocity-dependent and cannot be derived from a potential in the ordinary sense. Yet it does no work and is perfectly conservative. This makes it an unusual type of force -- one that deflects without energising or dissipating. The generalised potential $U = m\mathbf{v}\cdot(\boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r})$ in the Lagrangian framework captures this behaviour, but the velocity dependence means the Coriolis force does not fit the Newtonian paradigm of forces determined by position alone. The velocity-dependent force law is a portent of the magnetic Lorentz force $\mathbf{F} = q\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}$, which shares the same mathematical structure and the same property of doing no work.
