## Intuition [Beginner]

Drop an apple. It accelerates downward at about 10 metres per second squared. Newton's insight was that this same acceleration — the same force, scaled by mass — also pulls the Moon toward the Earth. The only difference is distance: the Moon is about 60 Earth-radii away, and the force weakens as the square of that distance.

This is Newton's law of gravitation. Every pair of masses attracts. The force between two point masses is proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. Double the distance, quarter the force.

Now imagine one very large mass, like the Earth, and one small test mass. The large mass produces a **gravitational field** at every point in space. The field tells you the acceleration any test mass would experience if placed there. Near the Earth's surface the field points straight down and has magnitude about 9.8 m/s squared. Far from Earth it points toward Earth's centre and weakens.

There is also a **gravitational potential**, a single number at each point in space. The field is the slope of this potential — it points from high potential toward low potential, and its strength is how fast the potential changes. For a point mass, the potential at distance r is negative, equal to minus a constant divided by r. The minus sign reflects that gravity is attractive: masses "roll downhill" toward lower (more negative) potential.

One beautiful result ties all this together: the **shell theorem**. Take a hollow spherical shell of uniform density. Outside the shell, the gravitational field is exactly as though all the shell's mass were concentrated at its centre. Inside the shell, the gravitational field is exactly zero — the contributions from all parts of the shell cancel perfectly.

This theorem explains why you can treat planets as point masses when computing orbits, and why the gravitational field inside a uniform solid sphere grows linearly with distance from the centre rather than following the inverse-square law.

## Visual [Beginner]

![Gravitational field lines of a point mass. Radial arrows point inward toward the centre, with spacing increasing as 1/r^2 to represent the weakening field. Superimposed: a spherical shell with field lines passing through it outside and no field lines inside, illustrating the shell theorem.](/img/09.01.05-gravitational-field-shell-theorem-placeholder.svg)

Outside a point mass M the field arrows grow shorter with distance, following $g = GM/r^2$. Inside a uniform spherical shell the arrows vanish — every piece of the shell pulls in a different direction and the net force is zero.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A uniform solid sphere has mass M and radius R. What is the gravitational field at a point a distance r from the centre, both inside (r less than R) and outside (r greater than R)?

**Outside (r greater than R):** By the shell theorem, the entire sphere acts as a point mass at its centre. The field points inward with magnitude $g = GM/r^2$. This is the familiar inverse-square result.

**Inside (r less than R):** Split the sphere into an inner solid sphere of radius r and an outer spherical shell from r to R. The outer shell contributes zero field at the point (shell theorem — you are inside it). The inner sphere has mass equal to M times the volume fraction, which is M times r-cubed divided by R-cubed. The field from this inner sphere is:

$g = G(M r^3/R^3)/r^2 = GMr/R^3$

So inside a uniform sphere the field grows linearly with distance from the centre. At the centre itself the field is zero. At the surface (r equals R) the inside formula gives $GM/R^2$, matching the outside formula — the field is continuous across the surface.

A numerical check: for Earth (M about 6 times 10 to the 24 kg, R about 6.4 times 10 to the 6 m), the surface field is $g = GM/R^2$ which equals about 9.8 m/s squared. Halfway to the centre (r = R/2), the field is half the surface value, about 4.9 m/s squared.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Newton's law of gravitation.** Two point masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ separated by a distance $r$ attract each other with force of magnitude

$$
F \;=\; \frac{G m_1 m_2}{r^2},
$$

directed along the line joining them. The gravitational constant $G \approx 6.674 \times 10^{-11}\;\mathrm{N\,m^2\,kg^{-2}}$. In vector form, the force on $m_2$ due to $m_1$ is

$$
\mathbf{F}_{12} \;=\; -\frac{G m_1 m_2}{|\mathbf{r}_2 - \mathbf{r}_1|^3}(\mathbf{r}_2 - \mathbf{r}_1).
$$

**Gravitational field.** The gravitational field produced by a mass distribution is the force per unit mass on a test particle:

$$
\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{r}) \;=\; -G \int \frac{\rho(\mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|^3}(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}')\, d^3\mathbf{r}',
$$

where $\rho(\mathbf{r}')$ is the mass density. For a single point mass $M$ at the origin, $\mathbf{g} = -GM\hat{\mathbf{r}}/r^2$.

**Gravitational potential.** Because the gravitational force is conservative (it does zero work around any closed path), there exists a scalar potential $\Phi$ such that $\mathbf{g} = -\nabla\Phi$. For a point mass $M$:

$$
\Phi(r) \;=\; -\frac{GM}{r},
$$

with the convention $\Phi \to 0$ as $r \to \infty$. For a continuous distribution, $\Phi(\mathbf{r}) = -G\int \rho(\mathbf{r}')/|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|\, d^3\mathbf{r}'$.

**Gauss's law for gravity.** Taking the divergence of $\mathbf{g}$ and using $\nabla^2(1/r) = -4\pi\delta(\mathbf{r})$ yields the differential form:

$$
\nabla \cdot \mathbf{g} \;=\; -4\pi G\rho,
$$

or equivalently $\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G\rho$ (Poisson's equation). The integral form is

$$
\oint_S \mathbf{g} \cdot d\mathbf{A} \;=\; -4\pi G M_{\mathrm{enc}},
$$

where $M_{\mathrm{enc}}$ is the mass enclosed by the surface $S$. In vacuum ($\rho = 0$), $\nabla^2\Phi = 0$ (Laplace's equation).

### Counterexamples and cautions

- The shell theorem applies to **spherically symmetric** distributions. An ellipsoidal mass distribution does not produce a pure inverse-square field outside it; the external potential contains multipole terms.
- The potential $\Phi = -GM/r$ is defined up to an additive constant. The convention $\Phi(\infty) = 0$ is standard in astrophysics but not universal; in geophysics one sometimes sets $\Phi = 0$ at the surface.
- Gauss's law for gravity has the opposite sign to Gauss's law in electrostatics because mass is always positive (gravity is always attractive), whereas electric charge comes in both signs.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Shell theorem — Newton's proposition).** *A uniform spherical shell of mass $M$ and radius $R$ produces: (i) outside the shell, a gravitational field identical to that of a point mass $M$ at the centre; (ii) inside the shell, a gravitational field that vanishes identically.*

**Proof.** Consider a thin uniform spherical shell of surface mass density $\sigma$ and radius $R$. Place a test mass at distance $r$ from the centre along the $z$-axis. Partition the shell into thin rings of angular half-width $d\theta$ centred on the axis. A ring at polar angle $\theta$ has radius $R\sin\theta$, circumference $2\pi R\sin\theta$, area $2\pi R^2 \sin\theta\, d\theta$, and mass $dm = \sigma \cdot 2\pi R^2 \sin\theta\, d\theta$.

Every point on the ring is at distance $s = \sqrt{r^2 + R^2 - 2rR\cos\theta}$ from the test mass. By symmetry the net force points along the $z$-axis. The $z$-component of force from the ring is

$$
dF_z \;=\; -\frac{Gm\,dm}{s^2}\cos\alpha,
$$

where $\alpha$ is the angle between the line from the ring to the test mass and the $z$-axis, satisfying $s\cos\alpha = r - R\cos\theta$.

Substituting and simplifying using $s^2 = r^2 + R^2 - 2rR\cos\theta$, so $s\,ds = rR\sin\theta\, d\theta$:

$$
dF_z \;=\; -\frac{Gm\sigma \cdot 2\pi R}{2r^2}\left(1 - \frac{R^2 - r^2}{s^2}\right) ds.
$$

**Case (i): $r > R$ (outside).** The variable $s$ ranges from $r - R$ to $r + R$. Integrating:

$$
F_z \;=\; -\frac{Gm\sigma \cdot 2\pi R}{2r^2}\left[s + \frac{R^2 - r^2}{s}\right]_{s=r-R}^{s=r+R}.
$$

At the upper limit: $(r+R) + (R^2 - r^2)/(r+R) = (r+R) + (R-r) = 2R$. At the lower limit: $(r-R) + (R^2 - r^2)/(r-R) = (r-R) + (-(r+R)) = -2R$. The difference is $2R - (-2R) = 4R$, giving

$$
F_z \;=\; -\frac{Gm\sigma \cdot 2\pi R \cdot 4R}{2r^2} \;=\; -\frac{Gm \cdot 4\pi R^2 \sigma}{r^2} \;=\; -\frac{GMm}{r^2}.
$$

This is exactly the force of a point mass $M = 4\pi R^2\sigma$ at the origin.

**Case (ii): $r < R$ (inside).** Now $s$ ranges from $R - r$ to $R + r$. At the upper limit: $(R+r) + (R^2 - r^2)/(R+r) = (R+r) + (R-r) = 2R$. At the lower limit: $(R-r) + (R^2 - r^2)/(R-r) = (R-r) + (R+r) = 2R$. The difference is $2R - 2R = 0$, so

$$
F_z \;=\; 0.
$$

The field vanishes identically inside a uniform shell. $\square$

**Worked example: field inside and outside a uniform solid sphere.** For a solid sphere of uniform density $\rho$ and radius $R$, split the sphere at radius $r$. The mass enclosed is $M(r) = (4/3)\pi\rho\, r^3 = M(r/R)^3$. By Gauss's law:

$$
4\pi r^2 g(r) \;=\; -4\pi G M(r).
$$

Outside ($r > R$): $g = -GM/r^2$. Inside ($r < R$): $g = -GMr/R^3$. The field is continuous at $r = R$ and linear inside.

**Bridge.** The shell theorem builds toward the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian treatments of orbital mechanics [09.02.01], where the potential $\Phi = -GM/r$ appears in the action integral; this is exactly the reason the central-force problem reduces to an equivalent one-body problem, and the result generalises to arbitrary spherically symmetric mass distributions via Gauss's law. The potential-theoretic perspective — Poisson's equation for the gravitational field — appears again in the continuum mechanics treatment of self-gravitating bodies [09.07.01], and the cancellation inside a shell is dual to the divergence-free property of the field in vacuum regions.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Compute the gravitational potential energy of two point masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ separated by distance $r$, and verify that the negative gradient of this energy gives Newton's gravitational force law.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The potential energy is $U(r) = -Gm_1 m_2/r$. Compute $-dU/dr$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$U(r) = -Gm_1 m_2/r$. Then $F_r = -dU/dr = -Gm_1 m_2/r^2$, directed inward (attractive). The negative sign in $U$ ensures that the force derived from the potential gradient matches the attractive direction of gravity.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

A uniform solid sphere of mass $M$ and radius $R$ has gravitational potential $\Phi(r)$ defined by $\Phi(\infty) = 0$. Find $\Phi(r)$ for $r > R$ and for $r < R$, and verify that $\Phi$ is continuous at $r = R$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Outside: $\Phi = -GM/r$. Inside: integrate $g = -d\Phi/dr = GMr/R^3$ from $R$ inward.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Outside: $\Phi(r) = -GM/r$. Inside: $d\Phi/dr = GMr/R^3$, so $\Phi(r) = GMr^2/(2R^3) + C$. Matching at $r = R$: $-GM/R = GM/(2R) + C$, giving $C = -3GM/(2R)$. Therefore $\Phi(r) = -GM(3R^2 - r^2)/(2R^3)$ for $r < R$. At $r = R$, both expressions give $-GM/R$. The potential is parabolic inside and hyperbolic outside.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Using Gauss's law for gravity, compute the gravitational field inside an infinite slab of uniform density $\rho$ and thickness $2d$, at height $z$ above the midplane with $|z| < d$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Choose a Gaussian pillbox straddling the midplane with faces at $\pm z$. The enclosed mass is $\rho \cdot A \cdot 2z$ where $A$ is the face area. By symmetry the field is perpendicular to the slab.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By symmetry $\mathbf{g}$ is vertical and uniform across the pillbox faces. Gauss's law: $2g(z) A = -4\pi G \rho A \cdot 2|z|$, where the factor 2 accounts for two faces (above and below). So $g(z) = -4\pi G\rho|z| \cdot \mathrm{sgn}(z)$, or $g(z) = -4\pi G\rho\, z$ for $z > 0$. The field is linear in $z$, directed toward the midplane, and vanishes at $z = 0$. This is the gravitational analogue of the electric field inside a uniformly charged slab.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify directly that the gravitational potential $\Phi(\mathbf{r}) = -GM/|\mathbf{r}|$ satisfies Laplace's equation $\nabla^2\Phi = 0$ everywhere except at the origin, and compute $\nabla^2\Phi$ in the distributional sense.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the identity $\nabla^2(1/r) = -4\pi\delta(\mathbf{r})$ in three dimensions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $r \neq 0$, $\nabla^2(1/r) = 0$ by direct computation in spherical coordinates: $\nabla^2 f(r) = (1/r^2)d/dr(r^2 df/dr)$. With $f = 1/r$: $df/dr = -1/r^2$, so $r^2 df/dr = -1$, and $d(-1)/dr = 0$. In the distributional sense, $\nabla^2(1/r) = -4\pi\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{r})$, so $\nabla^2\Phi = GM \cdot 4\pi\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{r}) = 4\pi GM\,\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{r})$. Comparing with Poisson's equation $\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G\rho$, this gives $\rho(\mathbf{r}) = M\,\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{r})$ — the mass is a point source at the origin.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

A planet of mass $M$ and radius $R$ has a spherical cavity of radius $R/2$ centred at distance $R/2$ from the planet's centre. Find the gravitational field inside the cavity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use superposition. The planet with a cavity equals a solid planet minus a solid sphere filling the cavity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Model the body as a uniform solid sphere of density $\rho$ minus a smaller sphere of the same density at the cavity position. The field at a point $\mathbf{r}$ inside the cavity from the big sphere would be $\mathbf{g}_1 = -(4\pi G\rho/3)\mathbf{r}$. The field from the small sphere (centred at $\mathbf{a}$ where $|\mathbf{a}| = R/2$) is $\mathbf{g}_2 = -(4\pi G\rho/3)(\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{a})$. The net field is $\mathbf{g}_1 - \mathbf{g}_2 = (4\pi G\rho/3)\mathbf{a}$, which is a uniform field pointing from the cavity centre toward the planet centre. The field inside the cavity is constant — independent of position within the cavity. This result is sometimes called the "gravity tunnel" theorem.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the gravitational potential energy of a uniform solid sphere of mass $M$ and radius $R$ is $U = -3GM^2/(5R)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Build the sphere shell by shell. The energy to bring a thin shell of mass $dm$ from infinity to radius $r$, where mass $m(r)$ is already assembled, is $dU = -Gm(r)\,dm/r$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a sphere of density $\rho$, $m(r) = (4/3)\pi\rho r^3$ and $dm = 4\pi\rho r^2 dr$. The energy to add the shell at radius $r$ is $dU = -Gm(r)\,dm/r = -G(4\pi\rho/3)r^2 \cdot 4\pi\rho r^2\, dr = -(16\pi^2 G\rho^2/3) r^4\, dr$. Integrating from 0 to $R$:

$$
U = -\frac{16\pi^2 G\rho^2}{3}\int_0^R r^4\, dr = -\frac{16\pi^2 G\rho^2 R^5}{15}.
$$

Substituting $\rho = M/(4\pi R^3/3) = 3M/(4\pi R^3)$:

$$
U = -\frac{16\pi^2 G}{15}\cdot\frac{9M^2}{16\pi^2 R^6}\cdot R^5 = -\frac{3GM^2}{5R}.
$$

This self-energy is important in astrophysics: a star's gravitational binding energy is of this order.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-diculty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the multipole expansion of the gravitational potential outside a bounded mass distribution $\rho(\mathbf{r}')$ confined to $|\mathbf{r}'| < a$. Show that the first correction to the monopole term $-GM/r$ is the quadrupole $-\Phi_2 = -GQ_{ij}x^ix^j/(2r^5)$ where $Q_{ij} = \int \rho(\mathbf{r}')(3x_i'x_j' - r'^2\delta_{ij})\, d^3\mathbf{r}'$ is the quadrupole moment tensor.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $1/|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|$ in Legendre polynomials or use the Taylor expansion of $|\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}'|^{-1}$ for $r > r'$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $r > r'$, $|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|^{-1} = r^{-1}(1 - 2\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{r}'/r^2 + r'^2/r^2)^{-1/2}$. Expanding to second order: $|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|^{-1} \approx 1/r + \mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{r}'/r^3 + (3(\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{r}')^2 - r^2 r'^2)/(2r^5)$. Multiplying by $-G\rho(\mathbf{r}')$ and integrating: the monopole term is $-GM/r$. The dipole term $-G\int \rho\mathbf{r}'d^3\mathbf{r}' \cdot \hat{\mathbf{r}}/r^2$ vanishes when the origin is the centre of mass. The quadrupole term is $-G/(2r^5)\int\rho(3(\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{r}')^2 - r^2 r'^2)\, d^3\mathbf{r}'$, which equals $-GQ_{ij}x^ix^j/(2r^5)$ in index notation. For an axially symmetric body $Q_{ij} = Q(\delta_{ij} - 3\hat{z}_i\hat{z}_j)$ where $Q = \int\rho(3z'^2 - r'^2)/2\, d^3\mathbf{r}'$ is the scalar quadrupole moment. The Earth's quadrupole (oblate shape) produces measurable corrections to satellite orbits.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

A particle tunnels straight through a hole drilled through the centre of a uniform non-rotating Earth (radius $R$, mass $M$). Show that the motion is simple harmonic and find its period. Ignore all dissipative effects.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Inside the sphere, $g(r) = -GMr/R^3$. Write Newton's second law for the radial displacement.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The equation of motion is $m\ddot{r} = mg(r) = -mGMr/R^3$, i.e., $\ddot{r} = -(GM/R^3)r$. This is simple harmonic with angular frequency $\omega = \sqrt{GM/R^3}$. The period is $T = 2\pi\sqrt{R^3/(GM)}$. For Earth, $T \approx 2\pi\sqrt{(6.4\times10^6)^3/(6.67\times10^{-11}\times6\times10^{24})} \approx 5060\;\mathrm{s} \approx 84\;\mathrm{min}$. This equals the orbital period of a satellite skimming the surface — a consequence of the shell theorem and the harmonic-oscillator potential inside a uniform sphere.

</details>

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that Gauss's law for gravity follows from the inverse-square nature of the gravitational force. Then argue that the shell theorem is a consequence of Gauss's law plus spherical symmetry.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the first part, compute the divergence of $\mathbf{g} = -GM\hat{\mathbf{r}}/r^2$ and use $\nabla\cdot(\hat{\mathbf{r}}/r^2) = 4\pi\delta(\mathbf{r})$. For the second part, apply Gauss's law to a concentric spherical surface inside a uniform shell.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The field of a point mass is $\mathbf{g} = -GM\hat{\mathbf{r}}/r^2$. The divergence of $\hat{\mathbf{r}}/r^2$ in 3D is $\nabla\cdot(\hat{\mathbf{r}}/r^2) = 4\pi\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{r})$, so $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{g} = -4\pi GM\delta^{(3)}(\mathbf{r})$. For a continuous distribution, integrating over the source gives $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{g} = -4\pi G\rho$. By the divergence theorem, $\oint\mathbf{g}\cdot d\mathbf{A} = -4\pi G M_{\mathrm{enc}}$.

For the shell theorem: take a spherical Gaussian surface of radius $r < R$ concentric with a uniform shell. By symmetry, $\mathbf{g}$ is radial and constant on the surface, so $\oint\mathbf{g}\cdot d\mathbf{A} = 4\pi r^2 g(r)$. The enclosed mass is zero (no mass inside $r$), so $g(r) = 0$. For $r > R$, the enclosed mass is the total shell mass $M$, giving $g(r) = -GM/r^2$. This derivation shows the shell theorem is really a consequence of inverse-square symmetry.

The main ingredients needed are: (1) a definition of the Newtonian gravitational field as a vector-valued integral over a mass distribution; (2) the computation of $\nabla\cdot(\hat{\mathbf{r}}/r^2) = 4\pi\delta(\mathbf{r})$ in the distributional sense; (3) integration over the sphere $S^2$ with the inverse-square kernel to prove the cancellation inside a shell. Each of these requires assembling existing Mathlib components (spherical integration, the Dirac delta as a distribution, divergence theorem for vector fields) into a coherent gravitational-mechanics framework.

## Advanced results: potential theory and the shell theorem in generality [Master]

### Newton's potential and harmonic functions

The gravitational potential $\Phi$ satisfies Laplace's equation $\nabla^2\Phi = 0$ in vacuum. The solutions — **harmonic functions** — have powerful regularity properties. A function harmonic on a ball agrees with its average over any smaller concentric sphere (the mean-value property). This property underlies the uniqueness of the external gravitational field: given the mass distribution inside, the external potential is uniquely determined.

**Proposition.** *If $\Phi$ is harmonic on the annulus $a < r < b$ and continuous on the closed annulus, then the values of $\Phi$ on the inner sphere $r = a$ and the outer sphere $r = b$ uniquely determine $\Phi$ throughout the annulus.*

*Proof sketch.* The difference of two solutions is harmonic and vanishes on both boundaries. By the maximum principle for harmonic functions (a harmonic function on a bounded domain attains its maximum and minimum on the boundary), the difference vanishes everywhere. $\square$

This uniqueness result guarantees that the inverse-square field is the only spherically symmetric solution of Laplace's equation in three dimensions. In $n$ dimensions, the corresponding field goes as $r^{1-n}$ and the shell theorem generalises accordingly.

### The shell theorem via Gauss's law: symmetry arguments

The shell theorem is a special case of a general principle: for any force law $F \propto r^{-\alpha}$ in $n$ dimensions, the field inside a spherically symmetric shell vanishes if and only if $\alpha = n - 1$. In three dimensions ($n = 3$), the inverse-square law $\alpha = 2$ is precisely the critical exponent. This is why Newton's law is the unique power law compatible with the shell theorem, and why Gauss's law works as a practical tool.

**Proposition.** *In $n$-dimensional Euclidean space, a spherically symmetric mass shell produces zero interior gravitational field if and only if the force law is $F \propto r^{1-n}$.*

*Proof.* The Gauss-law argument: $\oint_S \mathbf{g}\cdot d\mathbf{A} \propto M_{\mathrm{enc}}$. For a surface inside the shell, $M_{\mathrm{enc}} = 0$. By symmetry $\mathbf{g}$ is radial and constant on $S$, so $g \cdot A_n(r) = 0$ where $A_n(r) = C_n r^{n-1}$ is the surface area of an $(n-1)$-sphere. This gives $g = 0$ provided the surface area $A_n(r) \neq 0$ (which holds for all $r > 0$). The converse — that the field outside is $g \propto M/r^{n-1}$ — follows from the same argument with $M_{\mathrm{enc}} = M$. $\square$

### Self-gravitating polytropes and the Lane-Emden equation

The gravitational potential inside a self-gravitating gaseous body satisfies Poisson's equation $\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G\rho$ coupled to an equation of state. For a polytropic gas $P = K\rho^{1+1/n}$, the hydrostatic equilibrium condition $dP/dr = -\rho\, d\Phi/dr$ combined with Poisson's equation gives the **Lane-Emden equation**:

$$
\frac{1}{\xi^2}\frac{d}{d\xi}\left(\xi^2\frac{d\theta}{d\xi}\right) + \theta^n \;=\; 0,
$$

where $\xi$ is a dimensionless radius and $\theta$ is a dimensionless density. The boundary conditions are $\theta(0) = 1$ and $\theta'(0) = 0$. The first zero of $\theta(\xi)$ gives the stellar radius. Analytic solutions exist only for $n = 0$ (uniform density), $n = 1$, and $n = 5$ (infinite-radius isothermal sphere). The case $n = 3/2$ (fully convective star) and $n = 3$ (radiative star) require numerical integration and are central to stellar structure theory.

**Synthesis.** The shell theorem is the foundational reason that Newtonian gravity outside a spherical body reduces to a one-body central-force problem; the gravitational potential and Gauss's law generalise this insight to arbitrary mass distributions via Poisson's equation. The central insight is that the inverse-square law is uniquely compatible with the vanishing of the interior field, and this is exactly the property that makes Newtonian gravity linear and superposable — the field of a composite body is the sum of the fields of its parts. Putting these together, the potential-theoretic framework unifies the point-mass field, the shell theorem, Gauss's law, and the multipole expansion into a single coherent structure governed by Laplace's and Poisson's equations. The generalisation to continuum mechanics and self-gravitating systems completes the bridge from the two-body Kepler problem [09.01.04] to the physics of stars.

## Connections [Master]

- **[09.02.01]** The action principle introduces the gravitational potential energy $-GMm/r$ as a term in the Lagrangian; the Euler-Lagrange equations for this Lagrangian reproduce the Kepler orbits studied in [09.01.04], and the shell theorem justifies treating planets as point masses in the Lagrangian framework.
- **[09.07.01]** Continuum mechanics generalises the gravitational field and potential to mass distributions described by density fields; Gauss's law in its differential form becomes the Poisson equation for the potential, which is the starting point for the theory of self-gravitating fluids and elastic media under gravity.
- **[09.01.04]** The two-body Kepler problem exploits the shell theorem to reduce the orbital dynamics to motion in a central $-GM/r$ potential; the conservation laws (energy, angular momentum) that follow are consequences of the conservative nature of the gravitational force established in this unit.
- **[02.01.01]** Topological spaces provide the mathematical framework in which continuity of the gravitational potential is formulated; the harmonic-function regularity theory used in the uniqueness proof relies on the open-set topology of Euclidean space.
- **[28.03.01]** Stellar astrophysics applies the gravitational self-energy formula $-3GM^2/(5R)$ and the Lane-Emden equation derived here to model the internal structure of main-sequence stars, white dwarfs, and neutron stars.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Newton's Principia (1687) contains the first proof of the shell theorem in Proposition LXXI of Book I. The proof is entirely geometric, using Euclidean constructions rather than calculus — which Newton had developed but chose not to publish in this form. The shell theorem was critical for Newton's programme: it justified treating the Earth and Moon as point masses in the law of gravitation, which was essential for comparing the acceleration of the falling apple with the centripetal acceleration of the Moon.

Henry Cavendish (1798) performed the first laboratory measurement of $G$ using a torsion balance, confirming the quantitative prediction of the inverse-square law. Before Cavendish, $G$ was unknown — only the product $GM_{\oplus}$ could be determined from orbital mechanics (via Kepler's third law).

Poisson (1813) formulated the differential equation $\nabla^2\Phi = 4\pi G\rho$, placing Newtonian gravity on the same mathematical footing as electrostatics. This potential-theoretic framework was later essential for the development of general relativity, where the Newtonian potential is replaced by the metric tensor and Poisson's equation becomes the Einstein field equations.

A philosophical point: the shell theorem illustrates a general pattern in physics where symmetries of the source produce simplifications in the field. The spherical symmetry of the shell leads to the complete cancellation of the interior field. This pattern repeats in electrostatics (Gauss's law), magnetostatics (Ampere's law), and general relativity (Birkhoff's theorem — the exterior spacetime of a spherically symmetric mass is exactly the Schwarzschild solution).
