## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine sitting in a rowboat on a still lake, holding a pile of bricks. Throw a brick backward. The boat moves forward. Throw another. The boat moves faster. Each brick carries away momentum, and the boat recoils to conserve the total momentum of boat-plus-brick system.

A rocket does the same thing, except it throws hot gas instead of bricks. The gas exits the nozzle at high speed, carrying momentum away from the vehicle. By Newton's third law, the vehicle receives an equal and opposite push — the thrust.

The crucial insight is that the rocket's mass decreases as it burns fuel. A 100-tonne rocket that is 90 percent fuel starts heavy and ends light. The same thrust produces more acceleration on the light vehicle than on the heavy one. The result is a logarithmic relationship: to double the final velocity you need roughly four times the fuel, not twice. This is the Tsiolkovsky rocket equation, derived in 1903 by the Russian schoolteacher Konstantin Tsiolkovsky, and it governs every space mission ever flown.

Three numbers determine a rocket's performance: the exhaust velocity (how fast the gas leaves), the initial mass (fuel plus structure plus payload), and the final mass (structure plus payload). The velocity the rocket gains depends only on the ratio of these masses and the exhaust velocity — not on the burn rate, the thrust level, or how the fuel is distributed over time. This counterintuitive result is a direct consequence of momentum conservation applied in the instantaneously comoving inertial frame of the rocket.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a cylindrical rocket in space, far from any gravitational field. At time $t$ it has mass $m$ and velocity $v$. In a small time interval $dt$ it ejects a small mass $dm_{\mathrm{fuel}}$ at exhaust velocity $v_{\mathrm{ex}}$ relative to the rocket. The rocket's mass decreases to $m - |dm_{\mathrm{fuel}}|$ and its velocity increases to $v + dv$.

![Diagram of a rocket at time t (mass m, velocity v) and at time t+dt (mass m-dm, velocity v+dv). A small exhaust element of mass dm moves at velocity v - v_ex relative to the ground frame. Momentum balance: the total momentum at t equals the total momentum at t+dt.](/img/09.01.08-rocket-momentum-balance-placeholder.svg)

The key picture is a momentum balance: before the burn, all the mass moves at velocity $v$. After the burn, most of the mass moves faster (at $v + dv$) while the ejected fuel moves slower (at $v - v_{\mathrm{ex}}$). The total momentum is the same.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A single-stage rocket has exhaust velocity $v_{\mathrm{ex}} = 3000$ m/s. Its initial mass is $m_0 = 100\,000$ kg, of which $m_{\mathrm{fuel}} = 80\,000$ kg is fuel. What velocity does it reach in space (no gravity, no drag)?

The Tsiolkovsky equation gives $\Delta v = v_{\mathrm{ex}} \ln(m_0/m_f)$ where $m_f = m_0 - m_{\mathrm{fuel}} = 20\,000$ kg.

$$
\Delta v = 3000 \times \ln(100\,000 / 20\,000) = 3000 \times \ln 5 \approx 3000 \times 1.609 = 4829 \text{ m/s}.
$$

To reach low Earth orbit ($\Delta v \approx 9400$ m/s including gravity and drag losses), a single stage would need $m_0/m_f = e^{9400/3000} \approx e^{3.13} \approx 22.9$, meaning only about 4.4 percent of the launch mass could be structure and payload — extremely demanding. This is why multi-stage rockets are used: each stage discards empty structure before the next stage ignites, keeping the mass ratio of each individual stage achievable.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Consider a rocket of mass $m(t)$ moving along a straight line with velocity $v(t)$ in an inertial frame. The rocket ejects exhaust at velocity $v_{\mathrm{ex}}$ relative to the rocket (the exhaust speed in the rocket's instantaneous rest frame). In the absence of external forces, momentum conservation for the system "rocket plus exhaust ejected in the interval $[t, t+dt]$" gives

$$
m(t)\,v(t) = [m(t) + dm] \cdot [v(t) + dv] + (-dm)(v(t) - v_{\mathrm{ex}}),
$$

where $dm < 0$ is the change in the rocket's mass (a negative number as fuel is expelled). Expanding to first order:

$$
mv = mv + m\,dv + v\,dm + dm\,dv - v\,dm + v_{\mathrm{ex}}\,dm.
$$

The $dm\,dv$ term is second order and vanishes. Simplifying:

$$
m\,dv = -v_{\mathrm{ex}}\,dm.
$$

This is the **Tsiolkovsky rocket equation** in differential form. Separating variables and integrating from the initial state $(m_0, v_0)$ to the final state $(m_f, v_f)$:

$$
\boxed{\Delta v = v_f - v_0 = v_{\mathrm{ex}} \ln\frac{m_0}{m_f}.}
$$

### Generalisation: external forces and gravity

When an external force $F_{\mathrm{ext}}$ acts on the rocket (gravity, drag), the equation of motion generalises to

$$
m\frac{dv}{dt} = -v_{\mathrm{ex}}\frac{dm}{dt} + F_{\mathrm{ext}}.
$$

For vertical launch against gravity with $F_{\mathrm{ext}} = -mg$ (neglecting the variation of $g$ with altitude and air resistance), the velocity at burnout is

$$
v_f = v_{\mathrm{ex}} \ln\frac{m_0}{m_f} - g\,t_{\mathrm{burn}},
$$

where $t_{\mathrm{burn}}$ is the burn duration. The gravity-loss term $g\,t_{\mathrm{burn}}$ penalises slow burns — one reason why high-thrust launch vehicles are preferred despite the Tsiolkovsky equation's independence from burn rate in the free-space case.

### Thrust and specific impulse

The **thrust** of a rocket engine is the force it exerts on the vehicle:

$$
F_{\mathrm{thrust}} = v_{\mathrm{ex}} \left|\frac{dm}{dt}\right| = v_{\mathrm{ex}} \dot{m}_{\mathrm{fuel}}.
$$

The **specific impulse** $I_{\mathrm{sp}}$ is the exhaust velocity expressed in units of $g_0 = 9.80665$ m/s$^2$:

$$
I_{\mathrm{sp}} = \frac{v_{\mathrm{ex}}}{g_0}.
$$

Typical values: solid propellant $I_{\mathrm{sp}} \approx 250$ s ($v_{\mathrm{ex}} \approx 2450$ m/s), liquid hydrogen/liquid oxygen $I_{\mathrm{sp}} \approx 450$ s ($v_{\mathrm{ex}} \approx 4400$ m/s), ion thruster $I_{\mathrm{sp}} \approx 3000$ s ($v_{\mathrm{ex}} \approx 29\,400$ m/s). The ion thruster has far higher $v_{\mathrm{ex}}$ but far lower thrust than chemical engines, making it suitable for deep-space manoeuvres but not for launch.

### Mass fraction and the tyranny of the rocket equation

The **propellant mass fraction** $\zeta = m_p/m_0$ determines the achievable $\Delta v$. From Tsiolkovsky: $\Delta v/v_{\mathrm{ex}} = \ln(1/(1-\zeta))$, so $\zeta = 1 - e^{-\Delta v/v_{\mathrm{ex}}}$. For $\Delta v/v_{\mathrm{ex}} = 1$, $\zeta \approx 0.632$. For $\Delta v/v_{\mathrm{ex}} = 2$, $\zeta \approx 0.865$. For $\Delta v/v_{\mathrm{ex}} = 3$, $\zeta \approx 0.950$. The relationship is deeply unfavourable: each additional unit of $\Delta v/v_{\mathrm{ex}}$ requires an exponentially larger fraction of the vehicle to be fuel. This exponential "tyranny" is the fundamental reason space travel is expensive and why staging, high-energy propellants, and alternative propulsion concepts (nuclear thermal, electric, solar sail) are actively pursued. The structural mass fraction — the fraction of the non-payload mass that is structure rather than fuel — must be below about $0.1$ for a useful single-stage vehicle, a target that remains at the edge of materials technology.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Key derivation: optimal staging.**

Consider an $N$-stage rocket. Stage $i$ has structural mass $m_{s,i}$, fuel mass $m_{p,i}$, and exhaust velocity $v_{\mathrm{ex},i}$. The payload mass is $m_L$. Define the **payload fraction** of stage $i$ as the ratio of everything above stage $i$ to the total mass at stage $i$ ignition. The $\Delta v$ of stage $i$ is

$$
\Delta v_i = v_{\mathrm{ex},i} \ln\frac{m_{0,i}}{m_{0,i} - m_{p,i}},
$$

where $m_{0,i}$ is the total mass at stage-$i$ ignition. The total $\Delta v$ is $\Delta v = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \Delta v_i$.

For a two-stage rocket with equal exhaust velocities $v_{\mathrm{ex}}$ and equal mass ratios $m_{0,i}/(m_{0,i} - m_{p,i}) = R$ per stage, the total $\Delta v$ is

$$
\Delta v = 2 v_{\mathrm{ex}} \ln R.
$$

A single-stage rocket with the same total mass and the same exhaust velocity achieves $\Delta v = v_{\mathrm{ex}} \ln R_{\mathrm{total}}$ where $R_{\mathrm{total}}$ is the overall mass ratio. For the same structural technology, $R_{\mathrm{total}} < R^2$ because the single stage must carry all structure to the end. Staging wins because it discards empty structure en route.

**Bridge.** This derivation builds toward the continuum mechanics of thrust generation in [09.07.03] where the ideal-fluid Euler equations describe the flow through the nozzle that produces the exhaust velocity appearing in Tsiolkovsky's formula; the variable-mass framework also appears again in the Hamiltonian perturbation theory of [09.04.06], where the slow loss of energy from a nearly integrable system is modelled as a secular drift analogous to the gravity-loss term in the rocket equation. The central insight is that momentum conservation for an open system (one with mass flux across its boundary) requires tracking the momentum carried by the leaving mass; the rocket equation is the one-dimensional, discrete-mass version of the Reynolds transport theorem that underlies all of continuum mechanics.

Derive the Tsiolkovsky equation from the differential form $m\,dv = -v_{\mathrm{ex}}\,dm$ by separation of variables.

**Exercise 2 (medium, symbolic).**

A two-stage rocket has exhaust velocity $v_{\mathrm{ex}} = 3000$ m/s for both stages. Stage 1 has initial mass $m_{0,1} = 100\,000$ kg and fuel mass $m_{p,1} = 70\,000$ kg. Stage 2 has initial mass $m_{0,2} = 20\,000$ kg and fuel mass $m_{p,2} = 15\,000$ kg. The payload is $m_L = 1000$ kg. Compute the total $\Delta v$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the mass ratio for each stage separately. Stage 1 fires first; its final mass includes all of stage 2 plus the payload.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Stage 1: $m_{0,1} = 100\,000$ kg, $m_{f,1} = m_{0,1} - m_{p,1} = 30\,000$ kg. $\Delta v_1 = 3000 \times \ln(100/30) = 3000 \times 1.204 = 3611$ m/s.

Stage 2: $m_{0,2} = 20\,000$ kg, $m_{f,2} = m_{0,2} - m_{p,2} = 5000$ kg. $\Delta v_2 = 3000 \times \ln(20/5) = 3000 \times 1.386 = 4159$ m/s.

Total: $\Delta v = 3611 + 4159 = 7770$ m/s. A single-stage rocket with the same total fuel (85,000 kg), same total structure (14,000 kg), and same payload (1000 kg) would give $\Delta v = 3000 \times \ln(100/15) = 3000 \times 1.897 = 5691$ m/s. Staging gains over 2000 m/s.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that for a rocket launched vertically against constant gravity $g$, with constant exhaust velocity $v_{\mathrm{ex}}$ and constant mass flow rate $\dot{m}$, the height at burnout is $h = v_{\mathrm{ex}} t_b - \tfrac{1}{2}g t_b^2 - v_{\mathrm{ex}} \frac{m_0}{\dot{m}}(1 - e^{-\dot{m}t_b/m_0}) + \tfrac{1}{2}g t_b^2 \cdot \frac{1}{something}$... Wait — derive the exact burnout velocity and state why the gravity loss is proportional to burn time.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Integrate $dv/dt = -v_{\mathrm{ex}} \dot{m}/m - g$ with $m(t) = m_0 - \dot{m}t$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $m(t) = m_0 - \dot{m}t$ and constant $\dot{m} > 0$: $dv = (-v_{\mathrm{ex}} \dot{m}/(m_0 - \dot{m}t) - g)\,dt$. Integrating from 0 to $t_b$:

$$v(t_b) = v_{\mathrm{ex}} \ln\frac{m_0}{m_0 - \dot{m}t_b} - g\,t_b = v_{\mathrm{ex}} \ln\frac{m_0}{m_f} - g\,t_b.$$

The gravity-loss term $g\,t_b$ reduces the burnout velocity by an amount proportional to the burn time. This is why high-thrust (short-burn) trajectories are more efficient for launch: the gravity loss is minimised. A low-thrust ion engine, despite its high $v_{\mathrm{ex}}$, would spend so long fighting gravity near the surface that it could not launch at all — it must be deployed after reaching orbit by chemical means.

</details>

**Exercise 4 (hard, symbolic).**

A spacecraft of initial mass $m_0$ must achieve $\Delta v$ in free space. The exhaust velocity is $v_{\mathrm{ex}}$. Show that the fuel mass required is $m_{\mathrm{fuel}} = m_{\mathrm{payload}}(e^{\Delta v / v_{\mathrm{ex}}} - 1)$ when the structural mass is negligible. For $\Delta v = 10$ km/s and $v_{\mathrm{ex}} = 3$ km/s, what fraction of the launch mass is fuel?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

From Tsiolkovsky: $m_0/m_f = e^{\Delta v/v_{\mathrm{ex}}}$. The fuel is $m_0 - m_f = m_f(e^{\Delta v/v_{\mathrm{ex}}} - 1)$.

</details>

$m_0 = m_f e^{\Delta v/v_{\mathrm{ex}}}$. With negligible structural mass, $m_f = m_{\mathrm{payload}}$, so $m_{\mathrm{fuel}} = m_{\mathrm{payload}}(e^{\Delta v/v_{\mathrm{ex}}} - 1)$. For $\Delta v = 10$ km/s, $v_{\mathrm{ex}} = 3$ km/s: $\Delta v/v_{\mathrm{ex}} = 10/3 \approx 3.33$, so $e^{-3.33} \approx 0.0357$. The fuel fraction is $1 - 0.0357 = 0.964$, meaning 96.4 percent of the launch mass must be fuel. Only 3.6 percent is available for structure and payload. This extreme mass ratio is why single-stage-to-orbit vehicles are so challenging to build.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the rocket equation from the general momentum-conservation principle for a system of particles. At time $t$ the system consists of the rocket (mass $m(t)$, velocity $v(t)$) and the exhaust already emitted. At time $t + dt$ the rocket has mass $m + dm$ and velocity $v + dv$, and a small element $|dm|$ of exhaust has been added at velocity $v - v_{\mathrm{ex}}$. Show that total momentum conservation yields the Tsiolkovsky equation, and explain why the momentum of the previously emitted exhaust does not enter.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider only the increment in momentum during $[t, t+dt]$. The previously emitted exhaust carries constant momentum and its time derivative is zero.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

At time $t$, the rocket has momentum $p(t) = m(t)v(t) + p_{\mathrm{exhaust\,previously\,emitted}}$. At time $t+dt$, $p(t+dt) = (m + dm)(v + dv) + p_{\mathrm{exhaust\,previously\,emitted}} + |dm|(v - v_{\mathrm{ex}})$. Conservation: $p(t+dt) = p(t)$ (no external forces). The previously emitted exhaust cancels:

$$mv = (m + dm)(v + dv) + (-dm)(v - v_{\mathrm{ex}}).$$

Expanding and dropping the second-order term $dm\,dv$:

$$mv = mv + m\,dv + v\,dm + dm\,dv - v\,dm + v_{\mathrm{ex}}\,dm = mv + m\,dv + v_{\mathrm{ex}}\,dm.$$

So $m\,dv = -v_{\mathrm{ex}}\,dm$, giving the differential form of the rocket equation. The key point is that the previously emitted exhaust carries constant momentum (no force acts on it), so its contribution drops out when we equate momenta at $t$ and $t+dt$. Only the newly emitted element matters.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

An ion thruster has $I_{\mathrm{sp}} = 3000$ s and produces thrust of 0.2 N. If the spacecraft has initial mass 1000 kg, how long does it take to achieve $\Delta v = 5$ km/s? How much propellant is consumed?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$v_{\mathrm{ex}} = I_{\mathrm{sp}} \times g_0$. Use Tsiolkovsky for the mass ratio, then compute propellant mass and burn time.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$v_{\mathrm{ex}} = 3000 \times 9.807 = 29\,421$ m/s. From Tsiolkovsky: $m_f = m_0 e^{-\Delta v/v_{\mathrm{ex}}} = 1000 \times e^{-5000/29421} = 1000 \times e^{-0.1700} = 1000 \times 0.8437 = 843.7$ kg. Propellant consumed: $1000 - 843.7 = 156.3$ kg.

Mass flow rate: $\dot{m} = F/v_{\mathrm{ex}} = 0.2/29421 = 6.80 \times 10^{-6}$ kg/s. Burn time: $t = 156.3 / (6.80 \times 10^{-6}) = 2.30 \times 10^7$ s $\approx 266$ days. This long burn time illustrates why ion thrusters are used only in space: gravity loss during a 266-day burn near Earth would be catastrophic.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

A rocket ejects mass at a constant rate $\dot{m}$ and constant exhaust velocity $v_{\mathrm{ex}}$ in a uniform gravitational field $g$. Derive the condition for the rocket to lift off from rest, and express the acceleration at liftoff as a function of $m_0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

At $t = 0$ the acceleration is $a = F_{\mathrm{thrust}}/m_0 - g$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The thrust is $F = v_{\mathrm{ex}} \dot{m}$. The net upward force is $F - m_0 g$. Liftoff requires $F > m_0 g$, i.e., $v_{\mathrm{ex}} \dot{m} > m_0 g$, or equivalently the **thrust-to-weight ratio** $F/(m_0 g) > 1$. The initial acceleration is $a_0 = v_{\mathrm{ex}} \dot{m}/m_0 - g$. For a typical launch vehicle with thrust-to-weight ratio of 1.2–1.5, $a_0 = (0.2$ to $0.5)g$ — the rocket barely accelerates at first, then accelerates faster as fuel burns off and the mass decreases.

</details>

With structural fraction $\epsilon$, $m_{s,i} = \epsilon(m_{s,i} + m_{p,i})$, so $m_{0,i} - m_{p,i} = m_{s,i} + (\text{everything above}) = \epsilon(m_{s,i} + m_{p,i}) + (\text{above})$.

For the optimisation: the total $\Delta v = v_{\mathrm{ex}}[\ln(m_{0,1}/m_{f,1}) + \ln(m_{0,2}/m_{f,2})]$. Writing $m_{0,1} = m_{\mathrm{total}}$, $m_{f,1} = m_{\mathrm{total}} - m_{p,1}$, $m_{0,2} = m_{\mathrm{total}} - m_{p,1} - m_{s,1}$, and $m_{f,2} = m_{0,2} - m_{p,2}$. The optimisation over $m_{p,1}$ and $m_{p,2}$ subject to total-mass constraint shows that equal mass ratios maximise $\Delta v$ — this is the "equal partition of velocity increment" principle.

For the numerical case: total available for propellant and structure = $100 - 5 = 95$ tonnes. Each stage gets 47.5 tonnes. With $\epsilon = 0.1$: $m_{s,i} = 4.75$ tonnes, $m_{p,i} = 42.75$ tonnes. Stage 1 mass ratio: $100/(100 - 42.75) = 100/57.25 = 1.747$. Stage 2 mass ratio: $57.25 - 4.75 = 52.5$ initial; $52.5/(52.5 - 42.75) = 52.5/9.75 = 5.385$. The stages are not equal-ratio because the structural mass is counted differently. The exact optimum requires solving a Lagrange-multiplier problem.

## Advanced results [Master]

### The Meschersky equation and the Reynolds transport theorem

The general equation of motion for a variable-mass particle was written by Ivan Meshchersky in 1897:

$$
m\frac{d\mathbf{v}}{dt} = \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{ext}} + \dot{m}_{\mathrm{in}}(\mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{in}} - \mathbf{v}) - \dot{m}_{\mathrm{out}}(\mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{out}} - \mathbf{v}),
$$

where $\dot{m}_{\mathrm{in}}$ and $\dot{m}_{\mathrm{out}}$ are the incoming and outgoing mass flow rates, and $\mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{in}}$, $\mathbf{u}_{\mathrm{out}}$ are the absolute velocities of the mass entering and leaving the control volume. For a rocket with only outgoing mass at velocity $\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{v}_{\mathrm{ex}}$ relative to the ground, this reduces to $m\,d\mathbf{v}/dt = \mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{ext}} + v_{\mathrm{ex}} \dot{m}$.

The continuum generalisation is the **Reynolds transport theorem**. For any extensive property $B$ (mass, momentum, energy) of a fluid in a control volume $V$ with boundary $S$:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\int_V \rho b\,dV = \int_V \frac{\partial(\rho b)}{\partial t}\,dV + \oint_S \rho b\,\mathbf{v}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS,
$$

where $b = B/m$ is the intensive property and $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is the outward normal. Setting $B = m\mathbf{v}$ (momentum) and applying Newton's second law to the control volume gives the momentum equation of fluid mechanics. The rocket equation is the one-dimensional, single-particle limit of this continuum theorem.

### Relativistic rocket equation

For velocities approaching the speed of light, the Tsiolkovsky equation generalises. The relativistic rocket equation is

$$
\Delta v = c\,\tanh\left(\frac{v_{\mathrm{ex}}}{c}\ln\frac{m_0}{m_f}\right),
$$

where $c$ is the speed of light. For $v_{\mathrm{ex}} \ll c$ this reduces to the classical result. The photon rocket ($v_{\mathrm{ex}} = c$) gives $\Delta v = c\,\tanh(\ln(m_0/m_f))$. To reach $0.9c$ with a photon rocket requires $m_0/m_f = e^{\mathrm{arctanh}(0.9)} = e^{1.472} \approx 4.4$, far more favourable than chemical propulsion but requiring complete matter-antimatter annihilation.

### The water rocket and the impulse approximation

A toy water rocket demonstrates the variable-mass principle without combustion. A plastic bottle partially filled with water is pressurised with air. When released, the air pressure expels the water through the nozzle. The exhaust velocity is determined by Bernoulli's equation: $v_{\mathrm{ex}} \approx \sqrt{2(P_{\mathrm{air}} - P_{\mathrm{atm}})/\rho_{\mathrm{water}}}$. For a typical 2-litre bottle pressurised to 5 atm, $v_{\mathrm{ex}} \approx \sqrt{2 \times 4 \times 10^5 / 1000} \approx 28$ m/s. The mass ratio is approximately $(m_{\mathrm{bottle}} + m_{\mathrm{water}} + m_{\mathrm{air}}) / (m_{\mathrm{bottle}} + m_{\mathrm{air}}) \approx 2.1/0.2 \approx 10.5$, giving $\Delta v \approx 28 \times \ln 10.5 \approx 66$ m/s. Actual performance is lower due to air drag, non-instantaneous burn, and the two-phase (water then air) exhaust, but the order of magnitude is correct.

### Vertical flight with air resistance

Adding aerodynamic drag $F_D = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho C_D A v^2$ to the vertical rocket equation gives

$$
m\frac{dv}{dt} = v_{\mathrm{ex}} \dot{m} - mg - \tfrac{1}{2}\rho C_D A v^2.
$$

This ODE has no closed-form solution in general. Numerical integration shows that drag losses for a typical launch vehicle amount to $100$–$300$ m/s, compared to gravity losses of $1000$–$2000$ m/s. The total velocity budget for low Earth orbit is approximately:

$$
\Delta v_{\mathrm{orbital}} \approx 7800 + 1500 + 200 = 9500 \text{ m/s},
$$

where 7800 m/s is the circular orbital velocity, 1500 m/s is the gravity loss, and 200 m/s is the drag loss. The rocket must provide all of this from its Tsiolkovsky budget plus the contribution from Earth's rotation (about 460 m/s at the equator for eastward launches).

### Mass-flow continuity and the nozzle

The exhaust velocity is not an independent parameter — it depends on the nozzle geometry through the de Laval nozzle relation. For an isentropic expansion of an ideal gas from chamber pressure $P_c$ and temperature $T_c$ to exit pressure $P_e$:

$$
v_{\mathrm{ex}} = \sqrt{\frac{2\gamma}{\gamma - 1}\frac{R T_c}{M}\left[1 - \left(\frac{P_e}{P_c}\right)^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}\right]},
$$

where $\gamma$ is the ratio of specific heats, $R$ is the gas constant, and $M$ is the molecular weight of the exhaust gas. This relation explains why hydrogen-rich exhaust (low $M$) gives high $v_{\mathrm{ex}}$: the factor $RT_c/M$ scales inversely with molecular weight. Liquid hydrogen/liquid oxygen combustion produces water vapour ($M = 18$ g/mol) at $T_c \approx 3500$ K, giving $v_{\mathrm{ex}} \approx 4400$ m/s — the highest achievable with chemical propellants.

### The Oberth effect

A rocket burning its engine at high velocity gains more orbital energy per unit of fuel than one burning at low velocity. This is the **Oberth effect**, first described by Hermann Oberth in 1927. The reason: the kinetic energy gained is $\Delta E_k = \tfrac{1}{2}m_f(v + \Delta v)^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}m_f v^2 = m_f v\,\Delta v + \tfrac{1}{2}m_f (\Delta v)^2$. For a given $\Delta v$ (fixed by the mass ratio and exhaust velocity), the energy gain is proportional to $v$. Burning at periapsis of an elliptical orbit (where $v$ is largest) is therefore more efficient than burning at apoapsis.

The Oberth effect is not a violation of energy conservation — the exhaust also carries away kinetic energy, and at high velocity the exhaust is left with less kinetic energy in the inertial frame (more of the chemical energy goes to the vehicle). The effect is a direct consequence of the nonlinearity of the kinetic-energy formula $E_k = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$ and is one of the key tactical principles of astrodynamics.

### Historical note: Tsiolkovsky, Goddard, and Oberth

Tsiolkovsky's 1903 paper was a theoretical treatment; he never built a rocket. Robert Goddard launched the first liquid-fuelled rocket in 1926 (a 2.5-second, 12-metre flight). Hermann Oberth's 1929 book "Wege zur Raumschiffahrt" provided detailed engineering calculations and inspired the Verein fur Raumschiffahrt (VfR), the German rocket society that eventually led to the V-2 and, through Operation Paperclip, to the American space programme. The Tsiolkovsky equation remains the foundational calculation in every mission-planning document to this day.

**Synthesis.** The variable-mass framework puts together momentum conservation, the control-volume perspective, and the logarithmic Tsiolkovsky result into a single coherent structure; the central insight is that Newton's second law applies to a fixed set of particles, and when mass crosses the system boundary the momentum flux must be accounted for explicitly. This generalises to the Reynolds transport theorem of continuum mechanics [09.07.03] and to the relativistic rocket equation. Putting these together, the rocket equation is the discrete, one-dimensional limit of the general momentum-balance equation for open systems, and its logarithmic dependence on mass ratio is the foundational reason that space access is energetically expensive and that staging is an engineering necessity rather than a convenience.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The Tsiolkovsky equation (1903) predates practical rocketry by decades. Tsiolkovsky, a deaf Russian schoolteacher in Kaluga, derived the logarithmic result in "Exploration of the Universe with Reaction Machines" and proposed liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen as propellants. He also introduced the concept of multi-stage rockets, writing: "The Earth is the cradle of humanity, but one cannot remain in the cradle forever."

Goddard's 1919 Smithsonian paper "A Method of Reaching Extreme Altitudes" was ridiculed by the New York Times (which editorially doubted that rockets could work in vacuum), prompting Goddard to work in secrecy. The Times issued a retraction on July 17, 1969, the day after Apollo 11 launched. The Goddard-vs-press episode remains a cautionary tale about the relationship between expert knowledge and public understanding of physics.
