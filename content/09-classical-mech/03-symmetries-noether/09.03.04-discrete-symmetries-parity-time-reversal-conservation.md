## Intuition [Beginner]

Film a collision between two billiard balls. Now play the film backwards. The reversed motion looks perfectly physical -- the balls approach, collide, and separate, following the same rules of mechanics. This is **time-reversal symmetry**: the laws of Newtonian mechanics work equally well whether time runs forward or backward.

Now look at the collision in a mirror. The mirror-image motion is also physically valid -- forces, accelerations, and trajectories all behave correctly. This is **parity symmetry** (space inversion): reflecting all positions through a mirror plane produces another valid motion.

These are **discrete** symmetries. Unlike the continuous symmetries studied with Noether's theorem -- translations, rotations -- you cannot continuously deform a system into its mirror image or its time-reversed version. You have to flip all at once. Discrete symmetries do not produce conserved quantities in the Noether sense, but they impose powerful constraints on what kinds of motion are physically possible.

Not all forces respect these symmetries. Friction breaks time-reversal symmetry: a sliding block decelerates and stops, but the time-reversed motion -- a block spontaneously accelerating from rest -- never happens. The weak nuclear force breaks parity symmetry: certain particle decays prefer one handedness over the other. Identifying which symmetries hold and which are broken is one of the most powerful diagnostic techniques in physics.

The key point is that symmetries constrain the form of physical laws. If you demand that a law be unchanged by a mirror reflection, you rule out entire classes of possible equations. If you demand time-reversal invariance, you eliminate all terms that are odd in velocity. These constraints are as powerful as conservation laws, and in some ways more fundamental: they tell you what kinds of forces *can* exist, not just what quantities are conserved.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A ball is thrown upward from height $h$ with speed $v_0$. The trajectory is $y(t) = h + v_0 t - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2$.

**Time reversal.** Under $t \to -t$: $y(-t) = h - v_0 t - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2$. This is the trajectory of a ball thrown downward with speed $v_0$ from height $h$ -- also a valid solution. The peak occurs at $t^* = v_0/g$. The motion from $t = 0$ to $t = 2t^*$ is symmetric about $t^*$: $y(t^* + s) = y(t^* - s)$. The ascending and descending halves are time-reversed copies of each other [ref: TODO_REF].

**Parity.** Under $\mathbf{r} \to -\mathbf{r}$: the ball at height $y$ moves to height $-y$ in a gravitational field that now points upward. Parity alone is not a symmetry of the gravitational system -- gravity has a preferred direction (downward). But if you also reflect the source of the gravitational field (the Earth), the physics is restored. This illustrates an important principle: a symmetry of the *equations of motion* may be broken by the *boundary conditions* or *external fields*.

**Friction breaks time reversal.** A block sliding to a stop on a table: $v(t) = v_0 - \mu_k g t$. Under $t \to -t$: $v(-t) = v_0 + \mu_k g t$. This describes a block that starts slowly and accelerates -- a motion that violates the friction law (friction opposes motion). The time-reversed motion is not a solution. Friction is thermodynamically irreversible [ref: TODO_REF].

**Exercise (easy, multiple choice).**

Under time reversal ($t \to -t$), the velocity $\mathbf{v}$ transforms as:

A. $\mathbf{v} \to \mathbf{v}$ (unchanged)

B. $\mathbf{v} \to -\mathbf{v}$ (reversed)

C. $\mathbf{v} \to \mathbf{v}/2$

D. $\mathbf{v} \to 2\mathbf{v}$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Velocity is the rate of change of position with respect to time. If time reverses direction, what happens to the rate?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$\mathbf{v} \to -\mathbf{v}$** -- option B.

Since $\mathbf{v} = d\mathbf{r}/dt$, replacing $t$ with $-t$ gives $d\mathbf{r}/d(-t) = -\mathbf{v}$. All velocities reverse direction under time reversal. Acceleration is unchanged: $d(-\mathbf{v})/d(-t) = d\mathbf{v}/dt = \mathbf{a}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise (easy, true/false).**

Angular momentum $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}$ changes sign under parity ($\mathbf{r} \to -\mathbf{r}$, $\mathbf{p} \to -\mathbf{p}$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $(-\mathbf{r}) \times (-\mathbf{p})$. How does the cross product of two reversed vectors behave?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**False.** Angular momentum is a pseudovector (axial vector).

$(-\mathbf{r}) \times (-\mathbf{p}) = (-1)(-1)(\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}) = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p} = \mathbf{L}$. The two sign changes from parity cancel in the cross product. This is why angular momentum is classified as an axial vector rather than a polar vector. Under parity, axial vectors do not flip; under time reversal, they do ($\mathbf{r} \to \mathbf{r}$, $\mathbf{p} \to -\mathbf{p}$, so $\mathbf{L} \to -\mathbf{L}$).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise (easy, symbolic).**

A ball bounces elastically off a wall. Describe what happens under (a) time reversal and (b) parity (mirror reflection through the wall). Is the reflected motion physically valid in each case?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Under time reversal, the ball retraces its path: it approaches the wall, bounces, and moves away. Under parity, the wall is the mirror plane.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) Time reversal: the ball approaches the wall from the left, collides, and rebounds to the left. This is identical to the forward-time motion with the initial and final velocities swapped. Since the collision is elastic, the reversed motion is physically valid -- same speed before and after.

(b) Parity through the wall: the ball's approach from the left becomes an approach from the right (mirror image). The reflected motion is also physically valid -- the wall exerts the same force regardless of which side the ball approaches from.

</details>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Parity (space inversion)

The **parity transformation** $\mathcal{P}$ inverts all spatial coordinates:

$$
\boxed{\mathcal{P}: \mathbf{r} \;\longmapsto\; -\mathbf{r}, \qquad \mathcal{P}^2 = \mathrm{id}.}
$$

On phase space, $\mathcal{P}$ acts as $(q^i, p_i) \mapsto (-q^i, -p_i)$. A Lagrangian system has parity symmetry if $L(-q, -\dot{q}, t) = L(q, \dot{q}, t)$. This holds when the potential is an even function of position: $V(-\mathbf{r}) = V(\mathbf{r})$, as in the harmonic oscillator $V = \tfrac{1}{2}kr^2$ or the gravitational potential $V = -GM/r$ [ref: TODO_REF].

Under parity, physical quantities classify into two types:

| Quantity | Type | Transformation under $\mathcal{P}$ |
|---|---|---|
| Position $\mathbf{r}$ | Polar vector | $\mathbf{r} \to -\mathbf{r}$ |
| Velocity $\mathbf{v}$ | Polar vector | $\mathbf{v} \to -\mathbf{v}$ |
| Momentum $\mathbf{p}$ | Polar vector | $\mathbf{p} \to -\mathbf{p}$ |
| Force $\mathbf{F}$ | Polar vector | $\mathbf{F} \to -\mathbf{F}$ |
| Angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ | Axial (pseudo)vector | $\mathbf{L} \to +\mathbf{L}$ |
| Torque $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ | Axial (pseudo)vector | $\boldsymbol{\tau} \to +\boldsymbol{\tau}$ |
| Energy $E$ | Scalar | $E \to E$ |
| $\mathbf{r} \cdot (\mathbf{p} \times \mathbf{q})$ | Pseudoscalar | Changes sign |

The classification follows from counting sign changes: a dot product of two polar vectors is a scalar (two sign changes cancel), a cross product of two polar vectors is an axial vector (two sign changes cancel), and a dot product of a polar vector with an axial vector is a pseudoscalar (one sign change) [ref: TODO_REF].

### Time reversal

The **time-reversal transformation** $\mathcal{T}$ reverses the direction of time:

$$
\boxed{\mathcal{T}: t \;\longmapsto\; -t, \qquad \mathcal{T}: (q^i, p_i) \;\longmapsto\; (q^i, -p_i).}
$$

Positions are unchanged, momenta reverse (because $\mathbf{v} = d\mathbf{r}/dt$ reverses when $t \to -t$), and time flips. A Lagrangian system is time-reversal invariant if $L(q, -\dot{q}, -t) = L(q, \dot{q}, t)$. This holds when $L = T(\dot{q}) - V(q)$ with $T$ quadratic in $\dot{q}$ and $V$ independent of $\dot{q}$ -- the standard form for conservative mechanical systems [ref: TODO_REF].

The conditions are precise: $L$ must contain only even powers of $\dot{q}$ (after removing total time derivatives). Velocity-dependent forces break time-reversal invariance because they introduce odd powers of $\dot{q}$.

### The magnetic field and time-reversal breaking

A charged particle in a magnetic field has Lagrangian

$$
L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{\mathbf{r}}^2 + e\mathbf{A} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}} - e\Phi.
$$

The term $e\mathbf{A} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}}$ is linear (odd) in $\dot{\mathbf{r}}$. Under time reversal: $L \to \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{\mathbf{r}}^2 - e\mathbf{A} \cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}} - e\Phi \neq L$. Time-reversal invariance is broken.

The resolution is that the magnetic field itself reverses under time reversal: $\mathbf{B} \to -\mathbf{B}$. The Lorentz force $\mathbf{F} = e\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{B}$ is time-reversal invariant if both $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ reverse -- but if the magnetic field is produced by external currents (which are themselves composed of moving charges), then $\mathbf{B}$ does reverse under $\mathcal{T}$, and the full system (particle plus field source) is time-reversal invariant. The apparent breaking occurs when the field source is treated as fixed [ref: TODO_REF].

This is a deep pattern: many apparent symmetry violations in physics arise from treating part of the system as external. The symmetry is restored when the full closed system is considered.

### CPT theorem preview

In relativistic quantum field theory, the **CPT theorem** (Luders, Pauli, 1950s) states that the combined operation of charge conjugation $\mathcal{C}$ (particle to antiparticle), parity $\mathcal{P}$ (space inversion), and time reversal $\mathcal{T}$ is always an exact symmetry of any Lorentz-invariant local quantum field theory. Individual symmetries can be violated -- parity is violated by the weak interaction, CP is violated in neutral kaon decays -- but CPT as a whole cannot be violated without abandoning either Lorentz invariance or locality [ref: TODO_REF].

In classical mechanics, the CPT theorem has no direct analogue (there are no antiparticles), but the pattern is instructive: the combination of all three discrete operations is more fundamental than any individual one. The classical precursor is the observation that combining parity and time reversal ($\mathcal{PT}$) can restore a symmetry that neither $\mathcal{P}$ nor $\mathcal{T}$ possesses individually.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

### Reversibility theorem

**Key result.** *If the Lagrangian $L = T(\dot{q}) - V(q)$ with $T$ quadratic in $\dot{q}$, then for every solution $q(t)$ of the Euler-Lagrange equations, the time-reversed trajectory $q(-t)$ is also a solution.*

**Proof.** The Euler-Lagrange equations are $\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}^i} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i}$. Since $T = \tfrac{1}{2}a_{ij}\dot{q}^i\dot{q}^j$ is quadratic in $\dot{q}$, we have $\partial T / \partial \dot{q}^i = a_{ij}\dot{q}^j$ (linear in $\dot{q}$) and $\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot{q}^i} = a_{ij}\ddot{q}^j$ (involves $\ddot{q}$). Under $t \to -t$: $\dot{q} \to -\dot{q}$ and $\ddot{q} \to \ddot{q}$ (two minus signs from the double derivative). The left side $a_{ij}\ddot{q}^j$ is unchanged. The right side $\partial V / \partial q^i$ depends only on $q$, not on $t$, so it is also unchanged. Therefore $q(-t)$ satisfies the same equation. $\square$

**Corollary.** *In a time-reversal invariant system, if a trajectory passes through configuration $\mathbf{q}_0$ with velocity $\mathbf{v}_0$ at time $t_0$, then there exists a trajectory passing through $\mathbf{q}_0$ with velocity $-\mathbf{v}_0$ [ref: TODO_REF].*

### Parity and the double-well potential

Consider $V(x) = \lambda(x^2 - a^2)^2$, a symmetric double well with minima at $x = \pm a$. Under parity $x \to -x$: $V(-x) = V(x)$, so parity is a symmetry. The orbits in phase space $(x, p)$ come in parity-conjugate pairs: if $(x(t), p(t))$ is a solution, so is $(-x(t), -p(t))$. The separatrix (the orbit with $E = 0$ connecting the unstable equilibrium at $x = 0$ to itself) is parity-invariant as a set.

### Symplectic classification

On the symplectic manifold $(M, \omega)$:

- **Parity** $\mathcal{P}$ is a **symplectic involution**: $\mathcal{P}^*\omega = \omega$ and $\mathcal{P}^2 = \mathrm{id}$.
- **Time reversal** $\mathcal{T}$ is an **anti-symplectic involution**: $\mathcal{T}^*\omega = -\omega$ and $\mathcal{T}^2 = \mathrm{id}$.

This distinction has no analogue in Riemannian geometry. The symplectic structure of classical mechanics treats parity and time reversal differently at a fundamental geometric level. The fixed-point set of $\mathcal{T}$ is $\{(q, 0) : q \in Q\}$, the zero section of the cotangent bundle -- the set of all rest configurations [ref: TODO_REF].

**Bridge.** The discrete symmetries of parity and time reversal build toward the classification of dynamical systems by their symmetry groups; the foundational reason is that each discrete symmetry halves the effective phase space (parity relates $\mathbf{r}$ to $-\mathbf{r}$, time reversal relates $\mathbf{v}$ to $-\mathbf{v}$), and the central insight is that time-reversal invariance constrains the structure of periodic orbits and equilibrium points. The combination $\mathcal{PT}$ acts as a symplectic map and can restore symmetries broken individually. The generalisation to anti-symplectic involutions appears in the symplectic geometry treatment of Hamiltonian mechanics, and putting these together, discrete symmetries provide the simplest examples of symmetry reduction.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the kinetic energy $T = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{\mathbf{r}}^2$ is invariant under both parity ($\mathbf{r} \to -\mathbf{r}$) and time reversal ($t \to -t$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Under parity, $\dot{\mathbf{r}} \to -\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ but the square removes the sign. Under time reversal, $\dot{\mathbf{r}} \to -\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ likewise.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$T = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2$. Under parity: $|-\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2 = |\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2$, so $T \to T$. Under time reversal: $|d\mathbf{r}/d(-t)|^2 = |-\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2 = |\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2$, so $T \to T$. The kinetic energy is a true scalar under both transformations.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

The Lagrangian for a charged particle in a magnetic field is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{\mathbf{r}}^2 + e\mathbf{A}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}}$. Show that time reversal is broken by the magnetic term.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Under time reversal, $\dot{\mathbf{r}} \to -\dot{\mathbf{r}}$. Check the second term.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Under time reversal: $\tfrac{1}{2}m|-\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2 + e\mathbf{A}\cdot(-\dot{\mathbf{r}}) = T - e\mathbf{A}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}}$. This equals $L$ only if $\mathbf{A} = 0$. The magnetic term $e\mathbf{A}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ is odd in $\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ and breaks time-reversal invariance. Equivalently, $\mathbf{B} = \nabla \times \mathbf{A}$ changes sign under time reversal.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Classify each quantity as scalar, pseudoscalar, polar vector, or axial vector under parity: (a) $\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{p}$; (b) $\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{p}$; (c) $\mathbf{r}\cdot(\mathbf{p}\times\mathbf{q})$; (d) $\nabla V$; (e) $\nabla\times\mathbf{A}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Dot products of two polar vectors are scalars. Cross products of two polar vectors are axial vectors. Triple products with an odd number of cross products are pseudoscalars.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) $\mathbf{r}\cdot\mathbf{p}$: scalar (dot product of two polar vectors, both flip, product of signs is $+1$).
(b) $\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{L}$: axial vector (cross product of two polar vectors; two flips cancel).
(c) $\mathbf{r}\cdot(\mathbf{p}\times\mathbf{q})$: pseudoscalar (dot product of a polar vector with an axial vector; changes sign under parity).
(d) $\nabla V$: polar vector (gradient of a scalar field transforms like position).
(e) $\nabla\times\mathbf{A}$: axial vector (curl of a polar vector gives an axial vector -- this is why the magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ is an axial vector).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A damped harmonic oscillator has equation $m\ddot{x} + b\dot{x} + kx = 0$. Show that this equation is not invariant under time reversal $t \to -t$ and identify which term breaks the symmetry.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Under $t \to -t$: $\dot{x} \to -\dot{x}$ and $\ddot{x} \to \ddot{x}$. Substitute and compare.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Substituting: $m\ddot{x} + b(-\dot{x}) + kx = m\ddot{x} - b\dot{x} + kx \neq 0$. The damping term $b\dot{x}$ changes sign under time reversal, breaking the symmetry. Physically, the time-reversed motion would be a self-amplifying oscillation (negative damping), which does not satisfy the original equation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the Hamiltonian $H(q, p)$ of a time-reversal invariant system satisfies $H(q, -p) = H(q, p)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $L = T(\dot{q}) - V(q)$ with $T$ quadratic, $H = T(p) + V(q)$ where $T(p)$ is quadratic in $p$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $L = T(\dot{q}) - V(q)$ with $T = \tfrac{1}{2}a_{ij}\dot{q}^i\dot{q}^j$, the conjugate momentum is $p_i = a_{ij}\dot{q}^j$ (linear in $\dot{q}$). The Hamiltonian is $H = \tfrac{1}{2}a^{ij}p_ip_j + V(q)$ where $a^{ij}$ is the inverse mass matrix. Since $H$ depends on $p$ only through $p^2$ (quadratic), $H(q, -p) = H(q, p)$. The energy surface in phase space is symmetric under $p \to -p$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

A rigid body rotates freely in space (no external torques). The angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ is conserved. Determine whether $\mathbf{L}$ is even or odd under (a) parity and (b) time reversal. Explain why angular momentum has different parity behaviour under the two transformations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Angular momentum is $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{p} = I\boldsymbol{\omega}$. Track what happens to each factor under each transformation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) Under parity: $\mathbf{r} \to -\mathbf{r}$, $\mathbf{p} \to -\mathbf{p}$, so $\mathbf{L} = (-\mathbf{r})\times(-\mathbf{p}) = \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{p} = \mathbf{L}$. Angular momentum is even under parity (axial vector).

(b) Under time reversal: $\mathbf{r} \to \mathbf{r}$, $\mathbf{p} \to -\mathbf{p}$, so $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r}\times(-\mathbf{p}) = -\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{p} = -\mathbf{L}$. Angular momentum is odd under time reversal.

The difference arises because parity flips both factors in the cross product (two minus signs cancel), while time reversal flips only one (the momentum, not the position). This reflects the geometric fact that angular momentum involves an oriented plane: parity does not reverse the orientation of a plane (the two edges both flip), but time reversal does (it reverses the sense of rotation).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the time-reversal map $\mathcal{T}: (q, p) \mapsto (q, -p)$ is an anti-symplectic involution on $T^*Q$, i.e., $\mathcal{T}^*\omega = -\omega$ and $\mathcal{T}^2 = \mathrm{id}$, where $\omega = dq^i \wedge dp_i$ is the canonical symplectic form. Contrast this with parity $\mathcal{P}: (q, p) \mapsto (-q, -p)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Pull back the 2-form: $\mathcal{T}^*(dq^i \wedge dp_i) = d(\mathcal{T}^*q^i) \wedge d(\mathcal{T}^*p_i)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $\mathcal{T}$: $\mathcal{T}^*q^i = q^i$ and $\mathcal{T}^*p_i = -p_i$. So $\mathcal{T}^*\omega = dq^i \wedge d(-p_i) = -(dq^i \wedge dp_i) = -\omega$. Also $\mathcal{T}^2(q, p) = \mathcal{T}(q, -p) = (q, p)$, so $\mathcal{T}^2 = \mathrm{id}$.

For $\mathcal{P}$: $\mathcal{P}^*q^i = -q^i$ and $\mathcal{P}^*p_i = -p_i$. So $\mathcal{P}^*\omega = d(-q^i) \wedge d(-p_i) = (-dq^i) \wedge (-dp_i) = dq^i \wedge dp_i = +\omega$.

Parity is symplectic ($\mathcal{P}^*\omega = +\omega$); time reversal is anti-symplectic ($\mathcal{T}^*\omega = -\omega$). This geometric distinction has no Riemannian analogue and encodes the physical difference: parity preserves the phase-space orientation, while time reversal reverses it.

</details>

</aside>

Consider the combined PT transformation: $(q, p, t) \mapsto (-q, p, -t)$. Show that if $V(-q) = V(q)$ and $T$ is quadratic in $p$, then PT is a symmetry of the Hamiltonian system. Compute $\mathcal{PT}^*\omega$ and determine whether $\mathcal{PT}$ is symplectic or anti-symplectic.

Under $\mathcal{PT}$: $q \to -q$ and $p \to p$ (the sign changes from $\mathcal{P}$ and $\mathcal{T}$ on $p$ cancel: $-(-p) = p$). The Hamiltonian: $H(-q, p) = \tfrac{1}{2}a^{ij}p_ip_j + V(-q) = H(q, p)$ when $V(-q) = V(q)$.

Although $\mathcal{PT}$ is a symmetry of $H$, it is anti-symplectic (not symplectic). In quantum mechanics, PT-symmetric Hamiltonians with real spectra form an active research area initiated by Bender and Boettcher (1998). The classical result here is the simplest instance: combining two symmetries, one symplectic and one anti-symplectic, yields an anti-symplectic symmetry that constrains the dynamics.

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The formalisation of discrete symmetries requires: (1) the parity involution $\mathcal{P}: T^*Q \to T^*Q$ sending $(q, p) \to (-q, -p)$; (2) the time-reversal anti-symplectic involution $\mathcal{T}: T^*Q \to T^*Q$ sending $(q, p) \to (q, -p)$; (3) the statement that $\mathcal{P}$ is symplectic ($\mathcal{P}^*\omega = \omega$) and $\mathcal{T}$ is anti-symplectic ($\mathcal{T}^*\omega = -\omega$); (4) the reversibility theorem; and (5) the classification of scalars, pseudoscalars, polar vectors, and axial vectors under parity. Items (1)-(3) require extending Mathlib's symplectic machinery with anti-symplectic maps. Item (4) is a theorem about solutions of the Euler-Lagrange equations. Item (5) requires a representation-theoretic treatment of the group $\mathbb{Z}_2$ acting on tangent and cotangent spaces.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Anti-symplectic involutions and reversible systems

On a symplectic manifold $(M, \omega)$, an anti-symplectic involution is a diffeomorphism $\mathcal{T}: M \to M$ with $\mathcal{T}^*\omega = -\omega$ and $\mathcal{T}^2 = \mathrm{id}$. The fixed-point set $\mathrm{Fix}(\mathcal{T}) = \{x \in M : \mathcal{T}(x) = x\}$ is a **Lagrangian submanifold** -- a submanifold of half the dimension of $M$ on which $\omega$ vanishes identically [ref: TODO_REF].

For the canonical time reversal on $T^*Q$: $\mathrm{Fix}(\mathcal{T}) = \{(q, 0) : q \in Q\}$, the zero section of the cotangent bundle. This is the set of all rest configurations -- states with zero momentum.

**Proposition.** *If $\mathcal{T}$ is an anti-symplectic involution commuting with the Hamiltonian flow $\phi_t$ (i.e., $\mathcal{T}\circ\phi_t = \phi_{-t}\circ\mathcal{T}$), then $\mathcal{T}$ maps each trajectory to its time reversal.*

*Proof.* Differentiate $\mathcal{T}\circ\phi_t$ in $t$ at $t = 0$: $T\mathcal{T}\cdot X_H = -X_H\circ\mathcal{T}$ (the pushforward of the Hamiltonian vector field reverses sign). So $\mathcal{T}\circ\phi_t$ satisfies $d/dt(\mathcal{T}\circ\phi_t) = -X_H\circ(\mathcal{T}\circ\phi_t)$, which means $\mathcal{T}\circ\phi_t = \phi_{-t}\circ\mathcal{T}$. $\square$

### Reversible periodic orbits

A periodic orbit $\gamma$ is **symmetric** if $\mathcal{T}(\gamma) = \gamma$. Symmetric periodic orbits are guaranteed to exist under mild hypotheses: a time-reversal invariant Hamiltonian system on $T^*S^n$ always has at least one symmetric periodic orbit on each energy surface (the Seifert conjecture, proved in various cases).

**Proposition.** *A symmetric periodic orbit of a time-reversal invariant system intersects the fixed-point set $\mathrm{Fix}(\mathcal{T})$ in exactly two points.*

*Proof.* The orbit is invariant under $\mathcal{T}$, which reverses its parametrisation. A periodic orbit traversed forward and backward must agree at exactly the "turning points" -- where the momentum vanishes. For the zero-section fixed-point set, these are the points where the trajectory crosses zero momentum. $\square$

### Antiunitary operators in quantum mechanics

In quantum mechanics, discrete symmetries acquire a new representation-theoretic character. Wigner's theorem states that any symmetry of quantum mechanics is represented by either a **unitary** or **antiunitary** operator on Hilbert space. An antiunitary operator $\Theta$ satisfies $\langle \Theta\psi | \Theta\phi \rangle = \overline{\langle \psi | \phi \rangle}$ (complex conjugation of the inner product).

Parity $\mathcal{P}$ is represented by a **unitary** operator: $\hat{P}\psi(\mathbf{r}) = \psi(-\mathbf{r})$. This preserves the inner product.

Time reversal $\mathcal{T}$ is represented by an **antiunitary** operator: $\hat{T}\psi(\mathbf{r}) = \psi(\mathbf{r})^*$ (complex conjugation, plus spin reversal for spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ particles). For spinless particles, $\hat{T}^2 = +1$; for spin-$\tfrac{1}{2}$ particles, $\hat{T}^2 = -1$ (Kramer's theorem: all energy levels of a time-reversal invariant system with half-integer spin are at least doubly degenerate) [ref: TODO_REF].

The reason time reversal must be antiunitary is fundamental: time reversal must reverse the direction of motion. In quantum mechanics, the time evolution is $e^{-iHt/\hbar}$. Under $t \to -t$, this becomes $e^{iHt/\hbar} = (e^{-iHt/\hbar})^{-1}$. The complex conjugation in the antiunitary operator accomplishes this inversion of the time-evolution operator.

### CP violation preview

The combined symmetry CP (charge conjugation combined with parity) was once thought to be exact. Cronin and Fitch (1964) showed that CP is violated in neutral kaon ($K^0$) decays: the long-lived neutral kaon $K_L$, which should be a CP eigenstate decaying only into three-pion states, was observed to decay into two-pion states at a rate of about 0.2%. This small CP-violating amplitude has profound consequences: it is one of the three Sakharov conditions (1967) necessary for baryogenesis -- the generation of the matter-antimatter asymmetry of the universe.

CP violation implies, via the CPT theorem, that time-reversal symmetry T is also violated in the weak interaction. Direct observation of T violation in neutral kaon oscillations was achieved by the CPLEAR experiment at CERN (1998). In classical mechanics, all these effects are invisible -- the weak interaction has no classical limit. But the conceptual framework of discrete symmetry breaking was developed within classical mechanics first, and the classical intuition (symmetries constrain dynamics; symmetry violation requires explanation) carries over directly [ref: TODO_REF].

### Detailed balance

Time-reversal invariance has a direct consequence for transition rates in statistical mechanics. **Detailed balance** states that at thermal equilibrium, the rate of every forward transition equals the rate of the corresponding reverse transition:

$$
W(i \to j)\, P_i^{\rm eq} \;=\; W(j \to i)\, P_j^{\rm eq},
$$

where $W(i \to j)$ is the transition rate from state $i$ to state $j$ and $P_i^{\rm eq}$ is the equilibrium probability of state $i$ [ref: TODO_REF].

The physical origin is time-reversal invariance: for every microscopic trajectory from state $i$ to state $j$, there is a time-reversed trajectory from $j$ to $i$ with the same probability weight. This is the microscopic reversibility established by the reversibility theorem, applied to the ensemble of trajectories in phase space. Detailed balance is the condition that guarantees approach to thermal equilibrium and underlies the Boltzmann H-theorem.

When detailed balance is violated (in driven systems, active matter, systems with magnetic fields), the system does not relax to thermal equilibrium but instead reaches a nonequilibrium steady state with nonvanishing probability currents. The magnitude of detailed-balance violation is a quantitative measure of how far a system is from equilibrium.

### Onsager reciprocity relations

Lars Onsager (1931) showed that time-reversal invariance imposes symmetry conditions on the transport coefficients relating forces to fluxes. If $J_i = \sum_j L_{ij} X_j$ (fluxes linear in forces), then

$$
\boxed{L_{ij} \;=\; L_{ji}.}
$$

The proof uses microscopic reversibility (time-reversal invariance of the underlying dynamics). The physical content: the cross-coupling between force $i$ and flux $j$ equals the cross-coupling between force $j$ and flux $i$. For example, the coefficient relating temperature gradients to mass flow equals the coefficient relating concentration gradients to heat flow (the thermoelectric effects: the Seebeck and Peltier coefficients are related by $L_{12} = L_{21}$) [ref: TODO_REF].

Onsager received the 1968 Nobel Prize for this result. The reciprocity relations are a macroscopic consequence of the time-reversal invariance of microscopic dynamics. They apply only near equilibrium and only when the system is time-reversal invariant (magnetic fields break the relations unless the field is also reversed).

**Synthesis.** The discrete symmetries of parity and time reversal are the foundational reason that many classical systems possess paired or symmetric orbits; the central insight is that parity acts symplectically and time reversal acts anti-symplectically, and each halves the effective phase space. This is exactly the geometric content of the reversibility theorem and its corollaries for periodic orbits. The consequences extend far beyond mechanics: detailed balance in statistical mechanics, Onsager reciprocity in nonequilibrium thermodynamics, and the CPT theorem in quantum field theory are all descendants of the same discrete symmetry analysis. Putting these together, the anti-symplectic involution framework unifies parity, time reversal, and their combinations under a single geometric description that constrains dynamics at every scale from classical orbits to particle physics.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

The history of discrete symmetries in physics is a story of progressive violation: each symmetry, once assumed to be fundamental, was eventually found to be broken by specific interactions.

Parity was assumed to be an exact symmetry of nature until 1956. Lee and Yang (1956), then at Columbia University, pointed out that while parity had been verified for the strong and electromagnetic interactions, it had never been tested for the weak interaction. They proposed several experimental tests. Chien-Shiung Wu (Madame Wu) and collaborators at the National Bureau of Standards carried out the definitive experiment in December 1956: they aligned cobalt-60 nuclei at cryogenic temperatures and measured the angular distribution of beta-decay electrons. The electrons were emitted preferentially opposite to the nuclear spin direction -- a result impossible if parity were conserved. The paper (Wu et al., *Physical Review* **105**, 1413, 1957) was published in January 1957. Lee and Yang received the 1957 Nobel Prize in Physics [ref: TODO_REF].

The result was deeply unsettling. It meant the universe is not mirror-symmetric at the fundamental level: a mirror-reflected universe would behave differently from ours. Heisenberg reportedly said it felt as if "God had shown a left-handed preference." Landau (1957) immediately proposed that CP symmetry -- the combined operation of charge conjugation and parity -- was the true symmetry. For seven years, CP appeared to be exact.

Then Cronin and Fitch (1964), studying neutral kaon decays at Brookhaven, observed that the long-lived kaon $K_L$ occasionally decayed into two pions -- a CP-forbidden channel. The violation was tiny (about 0.2%) but unmistakable. Cronin and Fitch received the 1980 Nobel Prize. CP violation is now understood within the Standard Model as arising from a complex phase in the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa (CKM) quark mixing matrix. Kobayashi and Maskawa received the 2008 Nobel Prize [ref: TODO_REF].

The CPT theorem (Luders, 1954; Pauli, 1955) provides the final chapter: in any Lorentz-invariant local quantum field theory, the combined operation CPT is always an exact symmetry. Individual violations of C, P, and T are possible, and CP violation implies T violation (since CPT must hold). The CPT theorem is arguably the most general symmetry statement in fundamental physics. Experimental tests of CPT invariance -- comparing the masses, charges, and magnetic moments of particles and antiparticles with ever-increasing precision -- continue to be a major programme in particle physics.

In classical mechanics, the philosophical significance of discrete symmetries is different but equally profound. Time-reversal invariance of Newtonian mechanics means that the fundamental laws do not distinguish past from future -- yet macroscopic irreversibility is ubiquitous. This tension, first articulated by Boltzmann (1870s), is resolved by statistical mechanics: the arrow of time is an emergent property of systems with many degrees of freedom, not a feature of the underlying dynamics. The reversibility theorem is the mathematical statement of this tension: every microscopic trajectory is reversible, but macroscopic processes are not.

The Onsager reciprocity relations (1931) represent a direct application of time-reversal invariance to macroscopic physics. Onsager's insight was that the time-reversal symmetry of microscopic dynamics imposes constraints on macroscopic transport coefficients. The Nobel committee cited this as "the discovery of the reciprocal relations, fundamental for the thermodynamics of irreversible processes." It remains one of the deepest connections between microscopic reversibility and macroscopic phenomenology.
