## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine filming a collision between two billiard balls and then playing the film backwards. The reversed motion looks perfectly physical — the balls approach, collide, and separate, following the same rules of mechanics. This is **time-reversal symmetry**: the laws of Newtonian mechanics look the same whether time runs forward or backward.

Now imagine looking at the collision in a mirror. The mirror-image motion is also physically valid — forces, accelerations, and trajectories all behave correctly. This is **parity symmetry** (space inversion): reflecting all positions through a point (the mirror) produces another valid motion.

These are **discrete** symmetries, unlike the continuous symmetries (translations, rotations) that give conservation laws via Noether's theorem. You cannot continuously deform a system into its mirror image or its time-reversed version — you have to flip all at once. Discrete symmetries do not produce conserved quantities in the Noether sense, but they do impose powerful constraints on what kinds of motion are possible.

Not all forces respect these symmetries. Friction breaks time-reversal symmetry (a sliding block decelerates, but the time-reversed motion — a spontaneously accelerating block — never happens). The weak nuclear force breaks parity symmetry (certain particle decays prefer one handedness). Identifying which symmetries hold and which are broken is a central technique in physics.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A ball is thrown upward from height $h$ with speed $v_0$. The trajectory is $y(t) = h + v_0 t - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2$. Under time reversal $t \to -t$: $y(-t) = h - v_0 t - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2$. This is the trajectory of a ball thrown downward with speed $v_0$ from height $h$, which is also a valid solution.

The peak of the trajectory occurs at $t^* = v_0/g$. The motion from $t = 0$ to $t = 2t^*$ is symmetric about $t^*$: $y(t^* + s) = y(t^* - s)$. Time-reversal symmetry about the peak says the ascending and descending halves of the trajectory are mirror images.

Under parity $\mathbf{r} \to -\mathbf{r}$: the ball at height $y$ moves to height $-y$ in a gravitational field that now points upward. This is not the same physical situation (gravity reversed), so parity alone is not a symmetry of the gravitational system. But parity combined with changing $g \to -g$ (reflecting the source) restores the symmetry.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Parity (space inversion).** The parity transformation $\mathcal{P}$ acts on phase space by

$$
\mathcal{P}: (q^i, p_i) \;\longmapsto\; (-q^i, -p_i).
$$

For a Lagrangian system, $\mathcal{P}$ is a symmetry if $L(-q, -\dot{q}, t) = L(q, \dot{q}, t)$. This holds when the potential is an even function: $V(-\mathbf{r}) = V(\mathbf{r})$ (e.g., the harmonic oscillator $V = \tfrac{1}{2}k r^2$, the gravitational potential $V = -GM/r$).

Under parity, physical quantities transform as:

| Quantity | Type | Transformation |
|---|---|---|
| Position $\mathbf{r}$ | Polar vector | $\mathbf{r} \to -\mathbf{r}$ |
| Velocity $\mathbf{v}$ | Polar vector | $\mathbf{v} \to -\mathbf{v}$ |
| Momentum $\mathbf{p}$ | Polar vector | $\mathbf{p} \to -\mathbf{p}$ |
| Angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ | Axial (pseudo)vector | $\mathbf{L} \to \mathbf{L}$ |
| Force $\mathbf{F}$ | Polar vector | $\mathbf{F} \to -\mathbf{F}$ |
| Torque $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ | Axial (pseudo)vector | $\boldsymbol{\tau} \to \boldsymbol{\tau}$ |
| Energy $E$ | Scalar | $E \to E$ |

**Time reversal.** The time-reversal transformation $\mathcal{T}$ acts by

$$
\mathcal{T}: (q^i, p_i) \;\longmapsto\; (q^i, -p_i), \qquad t \;\longmapsto\; -t.
$$

Note: positions are unchanged, momenta reverse (because velocity reverses), and time flips. A Lagrangian system is time-reversal invariant if $L(q, -\dot{q}, t) = L(q, \dot{q}, -t)$. This holds when $L = T(\dot{q}) - V(q)$ with $T$ quadratic in $\dot{q}$ and $V$ independent of $\dot{q}$.

On the symplectic manifold, $\mathcal{T}$ is an **anti-symplectic involution**: it satisfies $\mathcal{T}^*\omega = -\omega$ (it reverses the symplectic form) and $\mathcal{T}^2 = \mathrm{id}$. This distinguishes it from parity, which is a **symplectic involution** ($\mathcal{P}^*\omega = \omega$).

**Conditions for time-reversal invariance.** A system with Lagrangian $L(q, \dot{q}, t)$ is time-reversal invariant if and only if $L$ contains only even powers of $\dot{q}$ (after removing any total time derivatives). Velocity-dependent forces (friction, magnetic Lorentz force) break time-reversal invariance because they introduce odd powers of $\dot{q}$.

### Symmetry breaking examples

- **Friction**: $L$ gains a term linear in $\dot{q}$ (or the Rayleigh dissipation function is nonzero). Time reversal is broken.
- **Magnetic force**: $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{\mathbf{r}}^2 + e\mathbf{A}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}} - eV$. The term $e\mathbf{A}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ is odd under $\dot{\mathbf{r}} \to -\dot{\mathbf{r}}$, breaking time-reversal invariance. Under $\mathcal{T}$, $\mathbf{B} \to -\mathbf{B}$ (the magnetic field reverses).
- **Weak interaction**: The $V-A$ coupling structure of the weak interaction is not invariant under parity, as demonstrated by Wu et al. (1957) in the cobalt-60 experiment.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Key result (Reversibility theorem).** *If the Lagrangian $L = T(\dot{q}) - V(q)$ with $T$ quadratic in $\dot{q}$, then for every solution $q(t)$ of the Euler-Lagrange equations, the time-reversed trajectory $q(-t)$ is also a solution.*

**Proof.** The Euler-Lagrange equations are $d/dt(\partial L/\partial\dot{q}^i) = \partial L/\partial q^i$. Since $T$ is quadratic in $\dot{q}$, $\partial T/\partial\dot{q}^i$ is linear in $\dot{q}$, so $d/dt(\partial T/\partial\dot{q}^i)$ is a second time derivative of $q^i$ and is unchanged under $t \to -t$ (two minus signs cancel). The right side $\partial V/\partial q^i$ depends only on $q$, not on $t$, so it is also unchanged. Therefore $q(-t)$ satisfies the same equation. $\square$

**Corollary.** *In a time-reversal invariant system, if a trajectory passes through a configuration $\mathbf{q}_0$ with velocity $\mathbf{v}_0$ at time $t_0$, then there exists a trajectory passing through $\mathbf{q}_0$ with velocity $-\mathbf{v}_0$ at some later time.*

**Worked example: parity and the double-well potential.** Consider $V(x) = \lambda(x^2 - a^2)^2$, a symmetric double well with minima at $x = \pm a$. Under parity $x \to -x$: $V(-x) = V(x)$, so parity is a symmetry. The orbits in phase space $(x, p)$ come in parity-conjugate pairs: if $(x(t), p(t))$ is a solution, so is $(-x(t), -p(t))$. The separatrix (the orbit with $E = 0$ connecting the unstable equilibrium at $x = 0$ to itself) is parity-invariant as a set.

**Bridge.** The discrete symmetries of parity and time reversal build toward the classification of dynamical systems by their symmetry groups; the foundational reason is that each discrete symmetry halves the effective phase space (parity relates $\mathbf{r}$ to $-\mathbf{r}$, time reversal relates $\mathbf{v}$ to $-\mathbf{v}$), and the central insight is that time-reversal invariance constrains the structure of periodic orbits and equilibrium points. This is exactly the content of the Poincar\'e recurrence theorem and the reversibility arguments underlying the microcanonical ensemble in statistical mechanics. The generalisation to anti-symplectic involutions appears again in the symplectic geometry chapter [09.04.05], and putting these together, discrete symmetries provide the simplest examples of symmetry reduction in Hamiltonian mechanics.

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the Hamiltonian $H(q, p)$ of a time-reversal invariant system satisfies $H(q, -p) = H(q, p)$.

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The formalisation of discrete symmetries requires: (1) the parity involution $\mathcal{P}: T^*Q \to T^*Q$ sending $(q, p) \to (-q, -p)$; (2) the time-reversal anti-symplectic involution $\mathcal{T}: T^*Q \to T^*Q$ sending $(q, p) \to (q, -p)$; (3) the statement that $\mathcal{P}$ is symplectic ($\mathcal{P}^*\omega = \omega$) and $\mathcal{T}$ is anti-symplectic ($\mathcal{T}^*\omega = -\omega$); and (4) the reversibility theorem. Items (1)-(3) require extending Mathlib's symplectic machinery with anti-symplectic maps.

## Advanced results: anti-symplectic involutions and reversible systems [Master]

### The geometry of time reversal

On a symplectic manifold $(M, \omega)$, an anti-symplectic involution is a diffeomorphism $\mathcal{T}: M \to M$ with $\mathcal{T}^*\omega = -\omega$ and $\mathcal{T}^2 = \mathrm{id}$. The fixed-point set $\mathrm{Fix}(\mathcal{T}) = \{x \in M : \mathcal{T}(x) = x\}$ is a Lagrangian submanifold (half-dimensional, on which $\omega$ vanishes).

For the canonical time reversal on $T^*Q$: $\mathrm{Fix}(\mathcal{T}) = \{(q, 0) : q \in Q\}$, the zero section of the cotangent bundle. This is the set of states with zero momentum — the "rest configurations."

**Proposition.** *If $\mathcal{T}$ is an anti-symplectic involution commuting with the Hamiltonian flow $\phi_t$ (i.e., $\mathcal{T}\circ\phi_t = \phi_{-t}\circ\mathcal{T}$), then $\mathcal{T}$ maps each trajectory to its time reversal.*

*Proof.* Differentiate $\mathcal{T}\circ\phi_t$ in $t$ at $t = 0$: $T\mathcal{T}\cdot X_H = -X_H\circ\mathcal{T}$ (the pushforward of the Hamiltonian vector field reverses sign). So $\mathcal{T}\circ\phi_t$ satisfies $d/dt(\mathcal{T}\circ\phi_t) = -X_H\circ(\mathcal{T}\circ\phi_t)$, which means $\mathcal{T}\circ\phi_t = \phi_{-t}\circ\mathcal{T}$. $\square$

### Reversible periodic orbits

A periodic orbit $\gamma$ is **symmetric** if $\mathcal{T}(\gamma) = \gamma$. Symmetric periodic orbits are guaranteed to exist under mild hypotheses by the Seifert conjecture (proved in various cases): a time-reversal invariant Hamiltonian system on $T^*S^n$ always has at least one symmetric periodic orbit on each energy surface.

**Proposition.** *A symmetric periodic orbit of a time-reversal invariant system intersects the fixed-point set $\mathrm{Fix}(\mathcal{T})$ in exactly two points.*

*Proof.* The orbit is invariant under $\mathcal{T}$, which reverses its parametrisation. A periodic orbit traversed forward and traversed backward must agree at exactly the "turning points" — where the momentum vanishes. For the zero-section fixed-point set, these are the points where the trajectory crosses zero momentum. $\square$

**Synthesis.** The discrete symmetries of parity and time reversal are the foundational reason that many classical systems possess paired or symmetric orbits; the central insight is that parity acts symplectically and time reversal acts anti-symplectically, and each halves the effective phase space. This is exactly the geometric content of the reversibility theorem and its corollaries for periodic orbits. Putting these together, the anti-symplectic involution framework unifies parity, time reversal, and their combinations (PT symmetry) under a single geometric description. The generalisation to quantum mechanics — where parity becomes unitary and time reversal becomes anti-unitary — appears again in the quantum chapters [12.03.01].

## Connections [Master]

- **[09.03.01]** Noether's theorem handles continuous symmetries; this unit extends the symmetry analysis to discrete symmetries, which do not generate conserved quantities but constrain the orbit structure.
- **[09.04.02]** Hamilton's equations inherit parity and time-reversal symmetries from the Lagrangian formulation; the Hamiltonian framework makes the anti-symplectic nature of time reversal explicit.
- **[09.04.05]** Symplectic structure provides the natural setting for anti-symplectic involutions; Darboux's theorem guarantees that local coordinates can be chosen to make time reversal look like $(q, p) \to (q, -p)$.
- **[12.03.01]** Quantum mechanics promotes parity to a unitary operator and time reversal to an anti-unitary operator (Wigner's theorem), preserving the discrete symmetry structure but modifying its representation.
- **[38.07.01]** Ergodic theory uses time-reversal invariance to prove the equality of time averages and phase-space averages (Birkhoff's theorem); the reversibility established here is the dynamical input.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The discovery that nature breaks parity was one of the most dramatic events in 20th-century physics. Lee and Yang (1956) proposed that the weak interaction might violate parity, and Wu et al. (1957) confirmed this experimentally using beta decay of polarised cobalt-60 nuclei. The electrons were emitted preferentially in one direction relative to the nuclear spin, proving that the weak interaction distinguishes left from right. This was deeply unsettling: it meant the universe is not mirror-symmetric at the fundamental level.

Landau (1957) proposed CP symmetry (combined charge conjugation and parity) as the true symmetry, but Cronin and Fitch (1964) showed that CP is also violated in neutral kaon decays. The CPT theorem (Luders, Pauli, 1950s) states that the combination of charge conjugation, parity, and time reversal is always a symmetry of any Lorentz-invariant quantum field theory. This makes CPT violation a signal of physics beyond the Standard Model.

In classical mechanics, time-reversal invariance is intimately connected to the arrow of time problem. Newtonian mechanics is time-reversal invariant, yet macroscopic phenomena are irreversible. The resolution lies in statistical mechanics: the second law of thermodynamics arises from the statistical behaviour of large numbers of particles, not from a fundamental asymmetry in the equations of motion.
