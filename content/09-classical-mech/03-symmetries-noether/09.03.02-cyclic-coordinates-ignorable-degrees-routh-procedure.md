## Intuition [Beginner]

Some coordinates in a mechanical system are "invisible" to the energy. The Lagrangian depends on them only through their velocities, not on their actual values. These are called **cyclic coordinates** (also **ignorable coordinates**) because the Lagrangian cycles past them without noticing where they are.

Think of a puck sliding on a perfectly smooth, infinite, flat air table. The puck's $x$-coordinate does not affect its energy. The kinetic energy depends on how fast it moves in the $x$-direction ($\tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2$), but not on *where* it is along $x$. The coordinate $x$ is cyclic because $L$ contains $\dot{x}$ but not $x$ itself [ref: TODO_REF].

The practical payoff is immediate: the momentum conjugate to a cyclic coordinate is automatically conserved. For the puck, the $x$-momentum $p_x = m\dot{x}$ is constant. No force in the $x$-direction means the momentum does not change. This is Newton's first law, but it now falls out of the Lagrangian structure without any extra input.

Now consider a planet orbiting a star. In polar coordinates $(r, \phi)$, the Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot{r}^2 + r^2\dot\phi^2) + k/r$. The angle $\phi$ does not appear anywhere in $L$; only $\dot\phi$ does. The angle is cyclic. Its conjugate momentum is $p_\phi = mr^2\dot\phi$ -- the angular momentum. Because $\phi$ is cyclic, angular momentum is conserved. This is not a coincidence: azimuthal symmetry (no preferred direction in the plane) is exactly the statement that the physics does not depend on the angle, and the conserved quantity is the angular momentum [ref: TODO_REF].

The word "ignorable" is apt. Once you know a coordinate is cyclic, you can ignore it as an independent variable and fold its conserved momentum into the remaining equations as a constant parameter. The system effectively has one fewer degree of freedom. You solve the reduced problem for the non-cyclic coordinates, then recover the cyclic coordinate by a single integration if needed.

When a coordinate is cyclic, you can eliminate it from the problem entirely. You solve for the conserved momentum once, plug the constant value back in, and reduce the system to fewer equations. This is the content of **Routh's procedure**: a partial Legendre transform that replaces cyclic velocities with their conserved momenta, yielding a reduced function (the Routhian) that governs only the non-cyclic coordinates [ref: TODO_REF].

A key subtlety: whether a coordinate is cyclic depends on the coordinate system. A free particle has all three Cartesian coordinates cyclic, but in spherical coordinates only $\phi$ is cyclic (the azimuthal angle). The physical symmetry (translational invariance of empty space) is the same regardless of coordinates, but the cyclic-coordinate structure of the Lagrangian depends on how you parametrise the configuration space.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A particle of mass $m$ moves in a central-force potential $V(r)$ in the plane. The Lagrangian in polar coordinates is

$$
L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot{r}^2 + r^2\dot\phi^2) - V(r).
$$

**Step 1: Identify the cyclic coordinate.**

The coordinate $\phi$ does not appear in $L$ (only $\dot\phi$ does). So $\phi$ is cyclic.

**Step 2: Find the conserved momentum.**

The conjugate momentum is $p_\phi = mr^2\dot\phi = \ell$ (a constant). This is the angular momentum.

**Step 3: Reduce the radial equation.**

The full radial Euler-Lagrange equation is $m\ddot{r} = mr\dot\phi^2 - V'(r)$. Substituting $\dot\phi = \ell/(mr^2)$:

$$
m\ddot{r} = \frac{\ell^2}{mr^3} - V'(r).
$$

This is a one-dimensional problem in $r$ with an effective potential $V_{\rm eff}(r) = \ell^2/(2mr^2) + V(r)$. The centrifugal barrier $\ell^2/(2mr^2)$ acts like a repulsive potential that prevents the particle from reaching $r = 0$ (for nonzero $\ell$).

**Step 4: Kepler problem.**

For $V(r) = -k/r$ (Kepler/Coulomb problem), $V_{\rm eff} = \ell^2/(2mr^2) - k/r$ has a minimum at $r_0 = \ell^2/(mk)$ with energy $E_{\rm min} = -mk^2/(2\ell^2)$. Circular orbits sit at this minimum. For $E_{\rm min} < E < 0$, the radial motion oscillates between a perihelion $r_{\min}$ and aphelion $r_{\max}$ while the angle $\phi$ advances [ref: TODO_REF].

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Cyclic (ignorable) coordinates

A coordinate $q^i$ is **cyclic** (or **ignorable**) in the Lagrangian $L(q, \dot{q}, t)$ if

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} \;=\; 0.
$$

The Euler-Lagrange equation for $q^i$ then reads

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot{q}^i} \;=\; 0,
$$

so the conjugate momentum $p_i = \partial L/\partial\dot{q}^i$ is a constant of motion. The terminology reflects the physical content: a cyclic coordinate corresponds to a translational symmetry of the Lagrangian (a shift $q^i \to q^i + \epsilon$ leaves $L$ unchanged), and by Noether's theorem [09.03.01], the associated Noether charge -- the conjugate momentum -- is conserved [ref: TODO_REF].

### Generalised momenta

For any coordinate $q^i$ (cyclic or not), the **generalised momentum** (or **canonical momentum**) is defined as

$$
p_i \;:=\; \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot{q}^i}.
$$

This quantity need not have dimensions of momentum. For a Cartesian coordinate, $p_x = m\dot{x}$ is the ordinary linear momentum. For an angle, $p_\phi = mr^2\dot\phi$ is the angular momentum. For a charged particle in a magnetic field, $p_i$ includes a contribution from the vector potential and need not equal $m\dot{x}^i$ at all. The generalised momentum is the quantity conserved when its conjugate coordinate is cyclic [ref: TODO_REF].

### Reduction by elimination of cyclic coordinates

Suppose the coordinates are split into cyclic $q^1, \ldots, q^k$ and non-cyclic $q^{k+1}, \ldots, q^n$. The Lagrangian can be written $L(q^{k+1}, \ldots, q^n,\, \dot{q}^1, \ldots, \dot{q}^n,\, t)$. The conserved momenta are $p_\alpha = \partial L/\partial\dot{q}^\alpha = c_\alpha$ (constants) for $\alpha = 1, \ldots, k$.

Solve these $k$ equations for $\dot{q}^\alpha = \dot{q}^\alpha(q^{k+1}, \ldots, q^n,\, \dot{q}^{k+1}, \ldots, \dot{q}^n,\, c_1, \ldots, c_k,\, t)$ and substitute back into the remaining Euler-Lagrange equations. This reduces the problem from $n$ to $n - k$ second-order equations.

### The Routhian

A more systematic approach uses the **Routhian**, defined by performing a partial Legendre transform over the cyclic coordinates only:

$$
R(q^{k+1}, \ldots, q^n,\, \dot{q}^{k+1}, \ldots, \dot{q}^n,\, p_1, \ldots, p_k,\, t) \;:=\; L - \sum_{\alpha=1}^k p_\alpha\, \dot{q}^\alpha.
$$

In this expression, the cyclic velocities $\dot{q}^\alpha$ are understood to have been eliminated using the relations $p_\alpha = \partial L/\partial\dot{q}^\alpha = c_\alpha$ [ref: TODO_REF].

The Routhian satisfies:

- Euler-Lagrange equations in the non-cyclic coordinates (Lagrangian side):
$$\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial R}{\partial\dot{q}^j} - \frac{\partial R}{\partial q^j} \;=\; 0, \qquad j = k+1, \ldots, n.$$

- Hamilton's equations in the cyclic coordinates (Hamiltonian side):
$$\dot{q}^\alpha \;=\; -\frac{\partial R}{\partial p_\alpha}, \qquad \alpha = 1, \ldots, k.$$

The Routhian is a hybrid object: Lagrangian in the non-cyclic coordinates and Hamiltonian in the cyclic ones. It provides a systematic computational procedure: solve the reduced Lagrangian equations for the non-cyclic coordinates, then recover the cyclic coordinates by quadrature using Hamilton's equations.

### Application: the Kepler problem

The Lagrangian for the Kepler problem in polar coordinates is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot{r}^2 + r^2\dot\phi^2) + k/r$. The angle $\phi$ is cyclic with conserved $p_\phi = mr^2\dot\phi = \ell$. The Routhian is

$$
R \;=\; L - \ell\dot\phi \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{r}^2 - \frac{\ell^2}{2mr^2} - V(r),
$$

where $V(r) = -k/r$. The Euler-Lagrange equation for $r$ gives $m\ddot{r} = -dV_{\rm eff}/dr$ with $V_{\rm eff} = \ell^2/(2mr^2) - k/r$. The two-dimensional orbital problem has been reduced to a one-dimensional radial problem parameterised by the conserved angular momentum $\ell$ [ref: TODO_REF].

### Application: the symmetric top

For a symmetric top (moments of inertia $I_1 = I_2 \neq I_3$) in a gravitational field, the Euler angles are $(\theta, \phi, \psi)$. The Lagrangian is

$$
L \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}I_1(\dot\theta^2 + \dot\phi^2\sin^2\theta) + \tfrac{1}{2}I_3(\dot\psi + \dot\phi\cos\theta)^2 - Mgl\cos\theta.
$$

Both $\phi$ and $\psi$ are cyclic. The conserved quantities are $p_\phi = I_1\dot\phi\sin^2\theta + I_3(\dot\psi + \dot\phi\cos\theta)\cos\theta$ (angular momentum about the vertical) and $p_\psi = I_3(\dot\psi + \dot\phi\cos\theta)$ (angular momentum about the symmetry axis). The Routhian eliminates both cyclic coordinates, yielding an effective one-dimensional equation for the nutation angle $\theta$ with $p_\phi$ and $p_\psi$ as parameters [ref: TODO_REF].

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Key result (Reduction by cyclic coordinates).** *For a Lagrangian system with $k$ cyclic coordinates, the dynamics of the remaining $n - k$ coordinates is governed by a reduced Lagrangian depending on the conserved momenta as parameters. The full dynamics is recovered by quadrature on the cyclic coordinates.*

**Proof.** Write the Lagrangian as $L = L(q^a,\, \dot{q}^\alpha,\, \dot{q}^a,\, t)$ where $\alpha = 1, \ldots, k$ are cyclic indices and $a = k+1, \ldots, n$ are non-cyclic. The conserved momenta are $p_\alpha = \partial L/\partial\dot{q}^\alpha = c_\alpha$. Define the Routhian $R = L - \sum_\alpha p_\alpha \dot{q}^\alpha$.

For the non-cyclic coordinates, compute $\partial R/\partial\dot{q}^a = \partial L/\partial\dot{q}^a$ and $\partial R/\partial q^a = \partial L/\partial q^a$. The Euler-Lagrange equations for $q^a$ are unchanged: $d/dt(\partial R/\partial\dot{q}^a) - \partial R/\partial q^a = 0$.

For the cyclic coordinates: $\dot{q}^\alpha = -\partial R/\partial p_\alpha$ and $\dot{p}_\alpha = 0$ (conservation). After solving the reduced equations for $q^a(t)$, the cyclic coordinates are obtained by integrating $dq^\alpha/dt = -\partial R/\partial p_\alpha\big|_{q^a(t),\, c_\alpha}$. This is a quadrature: the right-hand side is a known function of $t$ once the non-cyclic dynamics has been solved. $\square$

### Worked example: particle on a cone

A particle of mass $m$ moves on a cone $z = r\cot\alpha$ in a gravitational field $V = mgz$. The Lagrangian, constrained to the cone surface, is

$$
L \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot{r}^2\csc^2\alpha + r^2\dot\phi^2) - mgr\cot\alpha.
$$

The coordinate $\phi$ is cyclic with conserved $p_\phi = mr^2\dot\phi = \ell$. The effective radial equation is

$$
m\csc^2\alpha\,\ddot{r} \;=\; \frac{\ell^2}{mr^3} - mg\cot\alpha.
$$

The effective potential is $V_{\rm eff}(r) = \ell^2/(2mr^2) + mgr\cot\alpha$. The centrifugal barrier prevents the particle from reaching the apex, and the linear gravitational potential drives it outward. The particle oscillates radially while circulating around the cone axis.

### Worked example: particle in a uniform magnetic field

A charged particle of mass $m$ and charge $e$ moves in a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = B\hat{z}$ described by the symmetric-gauge vector potential $\mathbf{A} = \tfrac{1}{2}B(-y, x, 0)$. The Lagrangian is

$$
L \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2 + \dot{z}^2) + \tfrac{1}{2}eB(x\dot{y} - y\dot{x}).
$$

In polar coordinates: $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot{r}^2 + r^2\dot\phi^2) + \tfrac{1}{2}eBr^2\dot\phi$. The coordinate $\phi$ is cyclic. The conserved canonical momentum is $p_\phi = mr^2\dot\phi + \tfrac{1}{2}eBr^2 = mr^2(\dot\phi + \omega_c/2)$ where $\omega_c = eB/m$ is the cyclotron frequency. Note that the conserved quantity is the canonical angular momentum, not the kinetic angular momentum $mr^2\dot\phi$. The coordinate $z$ is also cyclic with $p_z = m\dot{z}$ conserved (free motion along the field). The particle traces helical orbits around the field lines with the Larmor radius $r_L = mv_\perp/(eB)$ [ref: TODO_REF].

**Bridge.** The reduction by cyclic coordinates builds toward the full Hamiltonian formalism [09.04.01] and the action-angle variable construction [09.06.01]; the foundational reason is that cyclic coordinates identify symmetries of the Lagrangian, and the central insight is that each symmetry removes one degree of freedom from the effective dynamics. This is exactly the content of Noether's theorem [09.03.01] applied to the specific case where the symmetry is translation in a coordinate, and the Routhian generalises this idea by providing a systematic procedure for the elimination. The reduction appears again in the Liouville-Arnold theorem [09.06.02], where $n$ independent conserved quantities in involution reduce a $2n$-dimensional phase space to motion on an $n$-torus.

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

A particle of mass $m$ moves in the potential $V = -k/r + \beta/(2r^2)$ (Coulomb plus inverse-square correction) in the plane. Show that $\phi$ is cyclic, find the effective radial potential, and determine the condition for closed orbits.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Closed orbits require that the radial period equals a rational multiple of the angular period. Use the orbit equation $d^2u/d\phi^2 + u = mk/\ell^2_{\rm eff}$ where $\ell_{\rm eff}$ absorbs the correction.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot{r}^2 + r^2\dot\phi^2) + k/r - \beta/(2r^2)$. $\phi$ is cyclic, $p_\phi = \ell$. Effective potential: $V_{\rm eff}(r) = (\ell^2 - m\beta)/(2mr^2) - k/r$. The orbit equation (using $u = 1/r$): $d^2u/d\phi^2 + (1 - m\beta/\ell^2)u = mk/\ell^2$. This gives $u = mk/[\ell^2(1 - m\beta/\ell^2)](1 + e\cos(\nu\phi))$ where $\nu = \sqrt{1 - m\beta/\ell^2}$. The orbit closes after $2\pi/\nu$ radians. Closed orbits require $\nu$ to be rational -- generically the orbit is an open rosette. For $\beta = 0$ (pure Coulomb), $\nu = 1$ and all bounded orbits close (the accidental degeneracy of the Kepler problem).

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that if the Lagrangian $L(q^1, \ldots, q^n, \dot{q}^1, \ldots, \dot{q}^n, t)$ has $k$ cyclic coordinates, then the Routhian $R$ satisfies the Euler-Lagrange equations in the non-cyclic coordinates and Hamilton's equations in the cyclic coordinates.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\partial R/\partial q^a$ and $d/dt(\partial R/\partial\dot{q}^a)$ for non-cyclic $q^a$, and $\partial R/\partial p_\alpha$ for cyclic $\alpha$. Use the definition $R = L - \sum_\alpha p_\alpha\dot{q}^\alpha$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For non-cyclic $q^a$: $\partial R/\partial q^a = \partial L/\partial q^a - \sum_\alpha p_\alpha\, \partial\dot{q}^\alpha/\partial q^a$. But $R$ is expressed with $\dot{q}^\alpha$ eliminated using $p_\alpha = c_\alpha$, so $R$ depends on $q^a$ and $\dot{q}^a$ (and the constants $c_\alpha$). The key identity is that when $\dot{q}^\alpha$ is eliminated in favour of $p_\alpha$, the partial derivatives $\partial R/\partial q^a$ and $\partial R/\partial\dot{q}^a$ match $\partial L/\partial q^a$ and $\partial L/\partial\dot{q}^a$ respectively (the extra terms from the Legendre transform cancel because they involve only cyclic quantities). Therefore $d/dt(\partial R/\partial\dot{q}^a) - \partial R/\partial q^a = d/dt(\partial L/\partial\dot{q}^a) - \partial L/\partial q^a = 0$. For cyclic $q^\alpha$: $\dot{q}^\alpha = -\partial R/\partial p_\alpha$ follows from the Legendre transform relation $\partial L/\partial\dot{q}^\alpha = p_\alpha$ and the definition of $R$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Jacobi integral (energy in the rotating frame) for the restricted three-body problem arises as the conserved quantity associated with a cyclic coordinate in the rotating frame Lagrangian. Explicitly construct the rotating-frame Lagrangian and identify the cyclic coordinate.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In the co-rotating frame with angular velocity $\omega$, the Lagrangian picks up a Coriolis term and a centrifugal term. The angle about the rotation axis becomes cyclic if the gravitational potential is time-independent in the rotating frame.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the rotating frame with coordinates $(x, y)$ in the plane of rotation, $L = \tfrac{1}{2}(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2) + \omega(x\dot{y} - y\dot{x}) + \tfrac{1}{2}\omega^2(x^2 + y^2) + V(x, y)$ where the last two terms are the centrifugal potential and $V$ is the gravitational potential of the two primaries. Transform to polar coordinates in the rotating frame: $L = \tfrac{1}{2}(\dot{r}^2 + r^2\dot\theta^2) + \omega r^2\dot\theta + \tfrac{1}{2}\omega^2 r^2 + V(r, \theta)$. If $V$ is independent of $\theta$ in the rotating frame (which happens at the Lagrange points L4 and L5), then $\theta$ is cyclic. In the general case, the Jacobi constant $C_J = -2H_{\rm rot} = -(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2) - 2U(x,y)$ where $U = V + \tfrac{1}{2}\omega^2(x^2+y^2)$ is the effective potential, arises because the Hamiltonian in the rotating frame is time-independent (the rotating-frame Lagrangian does not depend explicitly on time).

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The Routhian as a partial Legendre transform

The full Legendre transform converts the Lagrangian $L(q, \dot{q}, t)$ into the Hamiltonian $H(q, p, t)$ by replacing *all* velocities $\dot{q}^i$ with momenta $p_i = \partial L/\partial\dot{q}^i$. The Routhian performs the Legendre transform only over the cyclic coordinates, leaving the non-cyclic coordinates in Lagrangian form. This partial transform is well-defined precisely because the cyclic coordinates decouple: the Hessian $\partial^2 L/\partial\dot{q}^\alpha\partial\dot{q}^\beta$ (for cyclic indices $\alpha, \beta$) is non-degenerate, guaranteeing the invertibility needed to solve for $\dot{q}^\alpha$ in terms of $p_\alpha$.

On the cotangent bundle $T^*Q$, the Routhian corresponds to a fibred structure: the momenta $p_\alpha$ of cyclic coordinates parametrise fibres of a projection $T^*Q \to T^*Q_{\rm reduced}$, where $Q_{\rm reduced}$ is the configuration space with the cyclic coordinates quotiented out. The reduced dynamics lives on $T^*Q_{\rm reduced}$ and is governed by the Routhian [ref: TODO_REF].

### Symplectic reduction (Marsden-Weinstein)

The elimination of cyclic coordinates has a geometric interpretation. If $q^\alpha$ is cyclic with conserved momentum $p_\alpha = c_\alpha$, the level set $\{p_\alpha = c_\alpha\}$ is a coisotropic submanifold of phase space. The Marsden-Weinstein reduction theorem states that the quotient of this level set by the flow of the cyclic coordinate (translations in $q^\alpha$) is itself a symplectic manifold of dimension $2(n-1)$, on which the reduced dynamics lives.

For $k$ cyclic coordinates, the reduction removes $2k$ dimensions from phase space: $k$ from fixing the momenta and $k$ from quotienting by the cyclic flows. The reduced space has dimension $2(n-k)$ and carries a natural symplectic structure inherited from the original phase space [ref: TODO_REF].

The Routhian procedure is the computational manifestation of Marsden-Weinstein reduction in coordinates. The conserved momenta $c_\alpha$ select the level set; the Routhian $R$ is the Lagrangian description of the reduced dynamics on the quotient; and the Hamilton's equations $\dot{q}^\alpha = -\partial R/\partial p_\alpha$ encode the reconstruction of the cyclic coordinates from the reduced flow.

### Hamilton-Jacobi connection

The cyclic-coordinate reduction connects directly to the Hamilton-Jacobi theory [09.06.01]. In the Hamilton-Jacobi framework, the goal is to find a generating function $S(q, t)$ that makes *all* coordinates cyclic in the new canonical variables. The Hamilton-Jacobi equation is $\partial S/\partial t + H(q, \partial S/\partial q, t) = 0$. When some coordinates are already cyclic, the solution separates: $S = \sum_\alpha c_\alpha q^\alpha + \tilde{S}(q^{k+1}, \ldots, q^n, t)$, where the cyclic coordinates contribute only linear terms with the conserved momenta as coefficients. The remaining function $\tilde{S}$ satisfies a reduced Hamilton-Jacobi equation with fewer independent variables. The Routhian procedure is the Lagrangian-side counterpart of this separation [ref: TODO_REF].

### Integrable systems preview

A system with $n$ degrees of freedom is **Liouville integrable** if it possesses $n$ independent conserved quantities in involution (mutually Poisson-commuting). Cyclic coordinates provide the simplest mechanism for integrability: each cyclic coordinate yields one conserved momentum, and if all $n$ coordinates are cyclic, the system is immediately integrable (all momenta are constant).

The Kepler problem has two degrees of freedom $(r, \phi)$. The angle $\phi$ is cyclic, giving one conserved quantity ($\ell$). Energy conservation provides a second, independent, involutive quantity ($E$). The Kepler problem is therefore Liouville integrable. Moreover, it is **superintegrable**: the Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector provides additional conserved quantities beyond the minimum needed for integrability. This superintegrability is not visible as additional cyclic coordinates; it arises from a hidden $\mathrm{SO}(4)$ symmetry that becomes visible only after Moser regularisation [ref: TODO_REF].

The two-body central-force problem, the symmetric top, and the anisotropic harmonic oscillator with rational frequency ratios are all examples where cyclic-coordinate reduction is the first step in proving integrability. The Liouville-Arnold theorem [09.06.02] extends this to the general case: $n$ conserved quantities in involution confine the motion to an $n$-torus in phase space, and action-angle variables provide a global coordinate system adapted to this torus structure.

### Cyclic coordinates and hidden symmetries

A coordinate may be cyclic in one parametrisation but not in another. The physical content is the symmetry: if the Lagrangian is invariant under translations in some direction on the configuration manifold, the corresponding momentum is conserved regardless of the coordinates used to express the Lagrangian. The choice of coordinates reveals or obscures the symmetry.

The deepest example is the Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector of the Kepler problem: it is conserved (making the Kepler problem superintegrable) but does not correspond to any cyclic coordinate in the standard polar or Cartesian parametrisations. It arises from a hidden $\mathrm{SO}(4)$ symmetry that becomes visible only after the Moser regularisation, which maps the Kepler flow to geodesic flow on $S^3$ [ref: TODO_REF].

### The Routhian as a generating function

The Routhian $R$ can be interpreted as a type-2 generating function for the canonical transformation that separates cyclic from non-cyclic coordinates. In the full Hamiltonian framework, this canonical transformation block-diagonalises the Hamiltonian into a sum of a "free" part (in the cyclic coordinates) and an "interacting" part (in the non-cyclic coordinates).

**Synthesis.** The elimination of cyclic coordinates is the foundational reason that symmetries reduce the effective dimensionality of a mechanical problem; the central insight is that each conserved quantity removes one degree of freedom, and the Routhian provides the systematic procedure for carrying out this reduction. This is exactly the structure that the Marsden-Weinstein theorem elevates to a geometric statement about symplectic quotients. Putting these together, the passage from cyclic coordinates to reduced dynamics is the simplest instance of the general principle that conserved quantities generate symplectic reductions, and this principle appears again in the Hamilton-Jacobi separation of variables [09.06.01] and the Liouville-Arnold theorem [09.06.02].

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Edward John Routh introduced the partial Legendre transform that bears his name in his *Treatise on the Dynamics of a System of Rigid Bodies* (first edition 1860, with major expansions through the sixth edition of 1905). Routh was a Cambridge mathematician and coach whose students included J. J. Thomson. His procedure was developed as a practical computational tool: rather than going all the way to the Hamiltonian (which requires Legendre-transforming every coordinate), Routh's approach transforms only the cyclic coordinates, leaving the remaining coordinates in the more familiar Lagrangian form. This hybrid strategy was particularly suited to the rigid-body problems that dominated Cambridge mathematical physics in the nineteenth century, where problems like the spinning top have two cyclic Euler angles ($\phi$ and $\psi$) and one non-cyclic angle ($\theta$) [ref: TODO_REF].

The term "ignorable coordinate" is due to the English applied-mathematics tradition, particularly the textbook treatments by Whittaker (*Analytical Dynamics*, 1904) and Lamont (*The Dynamics of Particles*, 1923). The term reflects the practical orientation: once a coordinate has been identified as cyclic, the working physicist ignores it as an independent variable and absorbs its conserved momentum into the remaining equations as a parameter. The synonym "cyclic coordinate" is older, appearing already in the works of Jacobi, and reflects the geometric picture that the corresponding coordinate enters the Lagrangian only through its velocity -- the Lagrangian "cycles" through the coordinate without registering its value.

The connection between cyclic coordinates and Noether's theorem was implicit in the nineteenth-century variational calculus but was made fully explicit by Emmy Noether in 1918. The Lagrangian being independent of a coordinate $q^i$ is precisely the condition for invariance under the one-parameter group of transformations $q^i \to q^i + \epsilon$, and the conserved Noether charge is the conjugate momentum $p_i$. Routh's procedure is therefore the computational implementation of Noether's theorem for the simplest class of symmetries: translations in individual coordinates.

The geometric interpretation -- that cyclic coordinates generate symplectic reductions -- was developed by Marsden and Weinstein in the 1970s as part of the symplectic geometry programme in mechanics. Their reduction theorem (*Reduction of symplectic manifolds with symmetry*, 1974) shows that Routh's procedure is not merely a computational trick but a geometric operation on the phase space: quotienting by the symmetry group and restricting to a level set of the conserved momentum produces a reduced symplectic manifold on which the simplified dynamics lives.

A philosophical point: cyclic coordinates illustrate that the choice of coordinates matters for the simplicity of the analysis, even though the physics is coordinate-independent. The same system may have no cyclic coordinates in one parametrisation and several in another. The art of classical mechanics is partly the art of choosing coordinates adapted to the symmetries -- a theme that recurs throughout the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formalisms and reaches its fullest expression in the Hamilton-Jacobi theory, where the goal is to find coordinates that make *all* coordinates cyclic.

The textbook tradition treats cyclic coordinates early and centrally. Taylor (*Classical Mechanics*, 2005) introduces them in Chapter 7 as the simplest conservation law derivable from the Euler-Lagrange equations. Goldstein (*Classical Mechanics*, 3rd ed., 2002) treats them in Chapter 8 as the motivation for the Hamiltonian formalism and the Legendre transform. Landau and Lifshitz (*Mechanics*, 3rd ed., 1976) derive conservation of momentum and angular momentum from the homogeneity and isotropy of space in Section 6, treating cyclic coordinates as the coordinate expression of these fundamental symmetries. Arnold (*Mathematical Methods of Classical Mechanics*, 1989) places the Routhian in the context of Legendre transforms and symplectic geometry, connecting it to the general theory of generating functions for canonical transformations.
