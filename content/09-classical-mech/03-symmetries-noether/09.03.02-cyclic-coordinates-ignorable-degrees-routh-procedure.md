## Intuition [Beginner]

Some coordinates in a mechanical system are "invisible" to the energy. The Lagrangian depends on them only through their velocities, not on their actual values.

Think of a puck sliding on a perfectly smooth, infinite, flat air table. The puck's $x$-coordinate does not affect its energy — the kinetic energy depends on how fast it moves in the $x$-direction, but not on *where* it is along $x$. The coordinate $x$ is called **cyclic** (or **ignorable**) because the Lagrangian "cycles past" it without noticing it.

The practical payoff: the momentum conjugate to a cyclic coordinate is automatically conserved. For the puck, the $x$-momentum is constant — no force in the $x$-direction means the momentum does not change. This is Newton's first law, but now it falls out of the Lagrangian structure without any extra input.

When a coordinate is cyclic, you can eliminate it from the problem entirely. You solve for the conserved momentum once, plug the constant value back in, and reduce the system to fewer equations. This is the content of **Routh's procedure**: a partial Legendre transform that replaces cyclic velocities with their conserved momenta, yielding a reduced function (the Routhian) that governs only the non-cyclic coordinates.

The angular coordinate $\phi$ is cyclic. Its conservation law ($L_z = mr^2\dot\phi = \text{const}$) lets you reduce the 2D orbital problem to a 1D radial problem in the effective potential.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A particle of mass $m$ moves in a central-force potential $V(r)$ in the plane. The Lagrangian in polar coordinates is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot{r}^2 + r^2\dot\phi^2) - V(r)$.

The coordinate $\phi$ does not appear in $L$ (only $\dot\phi$ does). So $\phi$ is cyclic, and the conjugate momentum $p_\phi = mr^2\dot\phi = \ell$ is conserved (angular momentum).

The radial equation is $m\ddot{r} = mr\dot\phi^2 - V'(r)$. Substituting $\dot\phi = \ell/(mr^2)$:

$m\ddot{r} = \ell^2/(mr^3) - V'(r)$.

This is a one-dimensional problem in $r$ with an effective potential $V_{\rm eff}(r) = \ell^2/(2mr^2) + V(r)$. The centrifugal barrier $\ell^2/(2mr^2)$ acts like a repulsive potential that prevents the particle from reaching $r = 0$.

For $V(r) = -k/r$ (Kepler problem), $V_{\rm eff} = \ell^2/(2mr^2) - k/r$ has a minimum at $r_0 = \ell^2/(mk)$ with energy $E_{\rm min} = -mk^2/(2\ell^2)$. Circular orbits sit at this minimum.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Cyclic (ignorable) coordinates.** A coordinate $q^i$ is **cyclic** in the Lagrangian $L(q, \dot{q}, t)$ if $\partial L/\partial q^i = 0$. The Euler-Lagrange equation for $q^i$ then reads

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot{q}^i} \;=\; 0,
$$

so the conjugate momentum $p_i = \partial L/\partial\dot{q}^i$ is a constant of motion.

**Reduction procedure.** Suppose the coordinates are split into cyclic $q^1, \ldots, q^k$ and non-cyclic $q^{k+1}, \ldots, q^n$. The Lagrangian can be written $L(q^{k+1}, \ldots, q^n, \dot{q}^1, \ldots, \dot{q}^n, t)$. The conserved momenta are $p_\alpha = \partial L/\partial\dot{q}^\alpha = c_\alpha$ (constants) for $\alpha = 1, \ldots, k$.

Solve these $k$ equations for $\dot{q}^\alpha = \dot{q}^\alpha(q^{k+1}, \ldots, \dot{q}^{k+1}, \ldots, c_1, \ldots, c_k, t)$ and substitute back into the remaining Euler-Lagrange equations. This reduces the problem from $n$ to $n - k$ second-order equations.

**The Routhian.** A more systematic approach uses the **Routhian**, defined by performing a partial Legendre transform over the cyclic coordinates only:

$$
R(q^{k+1}, \ldots, q^n, \dot{q}^{k+1}, \ldots, \dot{q}^n, p_1, \ldots, p_k, t) \;:=\; L - \sum_{\alpha=1}^k p_\alpha \dot{q}^\alpha.
$$

The Routhian satisfies:

- Euler-Lagrange equations in the non-cyclic coordinates (Lagrangian side):
$$\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial R}{\partial\dot{q}^j} - \frac{\partial R}{\partial q^j} \;=\; 0, \qquad j = k+1, \ldots, n.$$

- Hamilton's equations in the cyclic coordinates (Hamiltonian side):
$$\dot{q}^\alpha \;=\; -\frac{\partial R}{\partial p_\alpha}, \qquad \alpha = 1, \ldots, k.$$

The Routhian is a hybrid object: Lagrangian in the non-cyclic coordinates and Hamiltonian in the cyclic ones.

### Example: the symmetric top

For a symmetric top (moments of inertia $I_1 = I_2 \neq I_3$) in a gravitational field, the Euler angles are $(\theta, \phi, \psi)$. The Lagrangian is

$$
L \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}I_1(\dot\theta^2 + \dot\phi^2\sin^2\theta) + \tfrac{1}{2}I_3(\dot\psi + \dot\phi\cos\theta)^2 - Mgl\cos\theta.
$$

Both $\phi$ and $\psi$ are cyclic. The conserved quantities are $p_\phi = I_1\dot\phi\sin^2\theta + I_3(\dot\psi + \dot\phi\cos\theta)\cos\theta$ and $p_\psi = I_3(\dot\psi + \dot\phi\cos\theta)$. The effective one-dimensional equation for $\theta$ involves $p_\phi$ and $p_\psi$ as parameters.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Key result (Reduction by cyclic coordinates).** *For a Lagrangian system with $k$ cyclic coordinates, the dynamics of the remaining $n - k$ coordinates is governed by a reduced Lagrangian depending on the conserved momenta as parameters. The full dynamics is recovered by quadrature on the cyclic coordinates.*

**Proof.** Write the Lagrangian as $L = L(q^a, \dot{q}^\alpha, \dot{q}^a, t)$ where $\alpha = 1, \ldots, k$ are cyclic indices and $a = k+1, \ldots, n$ are non-cyclic. The conserved momenta are $p_\alpha = \partial L/\partial\dot{q}^\alpha = c_\alpha$. Define the Routhian $R = L - \sum_\alpha p_\alpha \dot{q}^\alpha$.

For the non-cyclic coordinates, compute $\partial R/\partial\dot{q}^a = \partial L/\partial\dot{q}^a$ and $\partial R/\partial q^a = \partial L/\partial q^a$. The Euler-Lagrange equations for $q^a$ are unchanged: $d/dt(\partial R/\partial\dot{q}^a) - \partial R/\partial q^a = 0$.

For the cyclic coordinates: $\dot{q}^\alpha = -\partial R/\partial p_\alpha$ and $\dot{p}_\alpha = 0$ (conservation). After solving the reduced equations for $q^a(t)$, the cyclic coordinates are obtained by integrating $dq^\alpha/dt = -\partial R/\partial p_\alpha|_{q^a(t), c_\alpha}$. $\square$

**Worked example: the planar double pendulum.** Two pendulums of lengths $\ell_1, \ell_2$ and masses $m_1, m_2$ in a uniform gravitational field. In the absence of a preferred horizontal direction, only the *relative* angle $\theta_2 - \theta_1$ appears in the potential. If the system is free to rotate about the vertical (no preferred direction), the angle $\phi = (m_1\ell_1^2\theta_1 + m_2\ell_2^2\theta_2)/(m_1\ell_1^2 + m_2\ell_2^2)$ is cyclic and the total angular momentum is conserved, reducing the problem by one degree of freedom.

**Bridge.** The reduction by cyclic coordinates builds toward the full Hamiltonian formalism [09.04.01] and the action-angle variable construction [09.06.01]; the foundational reason is that cyclic coordinates identify symmetries of the Lagrangian, and the central insight is that each symmetry removes one degree of freedom from the effective dynamics. This is exactly the content of Noether's theorem [09.03.01] applied to the specific case where the symmetry is translation in a coordinate, and the Routhian generalises this idea by providing a systematic procedure for the elimination. The reduction appears again in the Liouville-Arnold theorem [09.06.02], where $n$ independent conserved quantities in involution reduce a $2n$-dimensional phase space to motion on an $n$-torus.

Prove that if the Lagrangian $L(q^1, \ldots, q^n, \dot{q}^1, \ldots, \dot{q}^n, t)$ has $k$ cyclic coordinates, then the Routhian $R$ satisfies the Euler-Lagrange equations in the non-cyclic coordinates and Hamilton's equations in the cyclic coordinates.

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The identification of cyclic coordinates and the construction of the Routhian would require extending Mathlib's Euler-Lagrange machinery with: (1) a formal definition of "coordinate absent from the Lagrangian"; (2) the theorem that conjugate momenta of cyclic coordinates are conserved; (3) the partial Legendre transform defining the Routhian; and (4) the proof that the Routhian satisfies Lagrangian equations in non-cyclic and Hamiltonian equations in cyclic coordinates. Items (1) and (2) are within reach of existing Mathlib machinery; items (3) and (4) require the Legendre transform infrastructure.

## Advanced results: Marsden-Weinstein reduction and the geometry of cyclic coordinates [Master]

### Symplectic reduction

The elimination of cyclic coordinates has a geometric interpretation. If $q^\alpha$ is cyclic with conserved momentum $p_\alpha = c_\alpha$, the level set $\{p_\alpha = c_\alpha\}$ is a coisotropic submanifold of phase space. The Marsden-Weinstein reduction theorem states that the quotient of this level set by the flow of the cyclic coordinate (translations in $q^\alpha$) is itself a symplectic manifold of dimension $2(n-1)$, on which the reduced dynamics lives.

For $k$ cyclic coordinates, the reduction removes $2k$ dimensions from phase space: $k$ from fixing the momenta and $k$ from quotienting by the cyclic flows. The reduced space has dimension $2(n-k)$ and carries a natural symplectic structure inherited from the original phase space.

### The Routhian as a generating function

The Routhian $R$ can be interpreted as a type-2 generating function for the canonical transformation that separates cyclic from non-cyclic coordinates. In the full Hamiltonian framework, this canonical transformation block-diagonalises the Hamiltonian into a sum of a "free" part (in the cyclic coordinates) and an "interacting" part (in the non-cyclic coordinates).

### Cyclic coordinates and hidden symmetries

A coordinate may be cyclic in one parametrisation but not in another. The physical content is the symmetry: if the Lagrangian is invariant under translations in some direction on the configuration manifold, the corresponding momentum is conserved regardless of the coordinates used to express the Lagrangian. The deepest example is the Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector of the Kepler problem: it is conserved (making the Kepler problem superintegrable) but does not correspond to any cyclic coordinate in the standard parametrisations. It arises from a hidden $\mathrm{SO}(4)$ symmetry that becomes visible only after the Moser regularisation.

**Synthesis.** The elimination of cyclic coordinates is the foundational reason that symmetries reduce the effective dimensionality of a mechanical problem; the central insight is that each conserved quantity removes one degree of freedom, and the Routhian provides the systematic procedure for carrying out this reduction. This is exactly the structure that the Marsden-Weinstein theorem elevates to a geometric statement about symplectic quotients. Putting these together, the passage from cyclic coordinates to reduced dynamics is the simplest instance of the general principle that conserved quantities generate symplectic reductions, and this principle appears again in the action-angle variable construction [09.06.01] and the Liouville-Arnold theorem [09.06.02].

## Connections [Master]

- **[09.03.01]** Noether's theorem provides the general framework: cyclic coordinates correspond to translational symmetries, and the conserved momentum is the Noether charge.
- **[09.04.01]** The Legendre transform extends the partial Legendre transform of Routh's procedure to all coordinates simultaneously, producing the Hamiltonian from the Lagrangian.
- **[09.06.01]** Action-angle variables are constructed when all coordinates can be made cyclic by a suitable canonical transformation; this unit's reduction procedure is the first step.
- **[09.06.02]** The Liouville-Arnold theorem generalises the reduction by cyclic coordinates to the case of $n$ independent conserved quantities in involution.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Routh's procedure appears in Routh's *Treatise on the Dynamics of a System of Rigid Bodies* (1877), where he introduced the partial Legendre transform as a practical tool for eliminating cyclic coordinates without going all the way to the Hamiltonian. The idea that symmetries reduce degrees of freedom was implicit in the work of Euler, Lagrange, and Jacobi, but Routh gave it a systematic computational form.

The geometric interpretation — that cyclic coordinates generate symplectic reductions — was developed by Marsden and Weinstein in the 1970s as part of the symplectic geometry programme in mechanics. Their reduction theorem shows that Routh's procedure is not merely a computational trick but a geometric operation on the phase space.

A philosophical point: cyclic coordinates illustrate that the choice of coordinates matters for the simplicity of the analysis, even though the physics is coordinate-independent. The same system may have no cyclic coordinates in one parametrisation and several in another. The art of classical mechanics is partly the art of choosing coordinates adapted to the symmetries — a theme that recurs throughout the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formalisms.
