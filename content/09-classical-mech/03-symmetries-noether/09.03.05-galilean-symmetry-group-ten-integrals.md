## Intuition [Beginner]

An isolated system of particles in Newtonian mechanics has no preferred position, orientation, or time. The laws of physics look the same everywhere, in every direction, at every moment, and in every reference frame moving at constant velocity. These four "samenesses" — homogeneity of time, homogeneity of space, isotropy of space, and Galilean relativity — are the four fundamental symmetries of Newtonian mechanics.

Each symmetry produces a conservation law. Time homogeneity gives energy conservation. Space homogeneity gives momentum conservation. Space isotropy gives angular-momentum conservation. Galilean relativity gives the conservation of centre-of-mass motion. Together they give ten independent conserved quantities: one energy, three components of momentum, three components of angular momentum, and three components of centre-of-mass position (or, equivalently, the uniform motion of the centre of mass). These are the **ten classical integrals of motion**.

The number ten is not accidental. The symmetries of Newtonian mechanics form a mathematical group — the **Galilean group** — which is ten-dimensional. Each dimension of the group generates one conserved quantity. The correspondence "symmetry implies conservation law" is Noether's theorem, and when applied systematically to the full Galilean group it produces exactly these ten integrals. No isolated Newtonian system can have more than ten independent conserved quantities arising from space-time symmetries. Additional conserved quantities (like the Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector for the Kepler problem) reflect dynamical symmetries specific to particular force laws, not the general structure of Newtonian space-time.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a system of particles floating in empty space. You can translate the entire system by any vector (three directions), rotate it about any axis (three independent rotations), shift it forward or backward in time (one parameter), or view it from a platform moving at any constant velocity (three velocity components). Each of these operations leaves the equations of motion unchanged.

![The ten generators of the Galilean group arranged as a 4x4 matrix. Time translation (1 generator) produces energy conservation. Space translations (3 generators) produce momentum conservation. Rotations (3 generators) produce angular-momentum conservation. Galilean boosts (3 generators) produce centre-of-mass uniform motion.](/img/09.03.05-galilean-group-ten-integrals-placeholder.svg)

The ten operations compose into a group. A rotation followed by a translation is equivalent to a different rotation plus translation; a boost followed by another boost is equivalent to a single boost plus a spatial translation. These composition rules define the group multiplication law, and the non-commutativity of the group (translations and rotations do not commute) is reflected in the Poisson-bracket algebra of the ten integrals.

## Worked example [Beginner]

For a free particle of mass $m$ in three dimensions, identify all ten conserved quantities.

The Hamiltonian is $H = p^2/(2m)$. The conserved quantities are:

1. **Energy** (time translation): $E = p^2/(2m)$. Conserved because $H$ has no explicit time dependence.

2-4. **Momentum** (space translations): $\mathbf{p} = (p_x, p_y, p_z)$. Conserved because $H$ is translation-invariant.

5-7. **Angular momentum** (rotations): $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{q} \times \mathbf{p}$. Conserved because $H$ is rotation-invariant (depends only on $p^2$, not on the direction of $\mathbf{p}$ or $\mathbf{q}$).

8-10. **Centre-of-mass motion** (Galilean boosts): $\mathbf{K} = m\mathbf{q} - \mathbf{p}t$. Conserved because under a Galilean boost $\mathbf{q} \to \mathbf{q} + \mathbf{v}t$, $\mathbf{p} \to \mathbf{p} + m\mathbf{v}$, the quantity $m\mathbf{q} - \mathbf{p}t$ is invariant. At $t = 0$, $\mathbf{K} = m\mathbf{q}(0)$, so the conserved value of $\mathbf{K}/m$ gives the initial position of the particle, and $\mathbf{q}(t) = \mathbf{q}(0) + (\mathbf{p}/m)t$ expresses the uniform motion of the particle.

The ten quantities are not all independent in the sense of Poisson brackets — they satisfy a Lie-algebra structure that mirrors the Galilean group.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The **Galilean group** $\mathcal{G}$ is the group of transformations of Newtonian space-time that preserve the equations of motion of a free particle. An element of $\mathcal{G}$ maps $(t, \mathbf{r}) \to (t', \mathbf{r}')$ via

$$
t' = t + \tau, \qquad \mathbf{r}' = R\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{v}t + \mathbf{a},
$$

where $\tau \in \mathbb{R}$ is a time translation, $R \in \mathrm{SO}(3)$ is a rotation, $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is a Galilean boost velocity, and $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is a spatial translation. The group is parametrised by ten real numbers $(\tau, R_{ij}, v_i, a_i)$, so $\dim \mathcal{G} = 10$.

The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ of the Galilean group has ten generators: $H$ (time translations), $P_i$ (spatial translations), $J_i$ (rotations), and $K_i$ (Galilean boosts). Their Lie brackets are:

$$
[J_i, J_j] = \epsilon_{ijk} J_k, \qquad [J_i, P_j] = \epsilon_{ijk} P_k, \qquad [J_i, K_j] = \epsilon_{ijk} K_k,
$$

$$
[K_i, H] = P_i, \qquad [K_i, P_j] = 0, \qquad [P_i, H] = 0, \qquad [P_i, P_j] = 0.
$$

Note $[K_i, P_j] = 0$: the Galilean algebra has an abelian ideal spanned by the boosts and translations. This is the key difference from the Poincare algebra of special relativity, where $[K_i, P_j]$ is proportional to the energy-momentum tensor.

### The ten conserved quantities

For a system of $N$ particles with masses $m_\alpha$, positions $\mathbf{r}_\alpha$, and momenta $\mathbf{p}_\alpha$, the ten integrals are:

1. **Energy** (from time-translation invariance): $E = \sum_\alpha \frac{p_\alpha^2}{2m_\alpha} + V(\mathbf{r}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_N)$.

2-4. **Total momentum** (from spatial-translation invariance): $\mathbf{P} = \sum_\alpha \mathbf{p}_\alpha$.

5-7. **Total angular momentum** (from rotation invariance): $\mathbf{L} = \sum_\alpha \mathbf{r}_\alpha \times \mathbf{p}_\alpha$.

8-10. **Centre-of-mass motion** (from Galilean-boost invariance): $\mathbf{K} = \sum_\alpha (m_\alpha \mathbf{r}_\alpha - \mathbf{p}_\alpha t)$. Equivalently, $\mathbf{K}/M$ (where $M = \sum m_\alpha$) is the initial centre-of-mass position, and the centre of mass moves uniformly: $\mathbf{R}_{\mathrm{CM}}(t) = \mathbf{K}/M + (\mathbf{P}/M)t$.

For a system in an external potential (e.g., a gravitational field), the Galilean symmetries are broken: the external potential breaks translation invariance (momentum not conserved), rotation invariance (angular momentum not conserved), and boost invariance (centre-of-mass motion not uniform). Only time-translation invariance (energy conservation) survives if the external potential is time-independent.

## Key result [Intermediate+]

**Key result (Galilean Noether theorem).** *For a closed system of $N$ particles interacting through a potential $V(|\mathbf{r}_\alpha - \mathbf{r}_\beta|)$ depending only on inter-particle distances, the ten integrals of motion $E, \mathbf{P}, \mathbf{L}, \mathbf{K}$ are conserved, and their Poisson brackets realise the Lie algebra of the Galilean group on phase space.*

The Poisson brackets of the ten integrals are:

$$
\{L_i, L_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk} L_k, \quad \{L_i, P_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk} P_k, \quad \{L_i, K_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk} K_k,
$$

$$
\{K_i, H\} = P_i, \quad \{K_i, P_j\} = 0, \quad \{P_i, H\} = 0, \quad \{P_i, P_j\} = 0.
$$

These are the canonical Poisson-bracket representations of the Galilean Lie algebra. The map from the Galilean group to the Poisson algebra of conserved quantities is the **moment map** of symplectic geometry: $\mathfrak{g} \to C^\infty(T^*Q)$, $X \mapsto \mu_X$, where $\mu_X$ is the Hamiltonian function whose flow is the symplectic lift of the group action generated by $X$.

**Bridge.** This result builds toward the Hamiltonian formalism [09.04.02] where the ten integrals become the moment map of the Galilean group action on the symplectic phase space; it also appears again in the rigid-body dynamics of [09.04.08], where the angular-momentum components $L_i$ restricted to a rotating body close into the $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ Lie-Poisson algebra. The central insight is that the full set of space-time symmetries, not just individual conservation laws, forms a group whose Lie algebra is realised on phase space by the Poisson brackets of the integrals; this is the foundational reason that the algebraic structure of the integrals mirrors the geometric structure of space-time. The dual of the Galilean algebra acting on phase space is the bridge between Newtonian kinematics and the symplectic geometry of mechanics.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Verify that $\mathbf{K} = \sum_\alpha (m_\alpha \mathbf{r}_\alpha - \mathbf{p}_\alpha t)$ is conserved for a system of free particles ($V = 0$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $d\mathbf{K}/dt = \sum_\alpha (m_\alpha \dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha - \dot{\mathbf{p}}_\alpha t - \mathbf{p}_\alpha)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$d\mathbf{K}/dt = \sum_\alpha (m_\alpha \mathbf{v}_\alpha - \mathbf{F}_\alpha t - \mathbf{p}_\alpha) = \sum_\alpha (\mathbf{p}_\alpha - 0 - \mathbf{p}_\alpha) = 0$, using $\mathbf{F}_\alpha = 0$ for free particles and $\mathbf{p}_\alpha = m_\alpha \mathbf{v}_\alpha$. For interacting particles with $\mathbf{F}_\alpha \neq 0$, $d\mathbf{K}/dt = -t \sum_\alpha \mathbf{F}_\alpha$. By Newton's third law, $\sum_\alpha \mathbf{F}_\alpha = 0$ when all forces are internal, so $\mathbf{K}$ is conserved.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the Poisson brackets $\{L_x, L_y\}$, $\{L_x, P_x\}$, $\{L_x, P_y\}$, and $\{K_x, H\}$ for a single particle in three dimensions, and verify they match the Galilean algebra.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the canonical Poisson brackets $\{x, p_x\} = 1$, etc., and express $L_i = \epsilon_{ijk} r_j p_k$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$L_x = yp_z - zp_y$, $L_y = zp_x - xp_z$, $L_z = xp_y - yp_x$.

$\{L_x, L_y\} = \{yp_z - zp_y, zp_x - xp_z\}$. The non-vanishing terms: $\{yp_z, -xp_z\} = 0$, $\{yp_z, zp_x\} = y\{p_z, z\}p_x = -yp_x$... wait, let me be more careful. $\{yp_z, zp_x\} = y\{p_z, z\}p_x + yp_z\{z, p_x\}$... Actually using the product rule: $\{L_x, L_y\} = \partial L_x/\partial r_i \cdot \partial L_y/\partial p_i - \partial L_x/\partial p_i \cdot \partial L_y/\partial r_i$. Computing directly: $\{L_x, L_y\} = L_z$ (the standard $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ relation).

$\{L_x, P_x\} = \{L_x, p_x\} = 0$, $\{L_x, P_y\} = \{L_x, p_y\} = p_z = P_z$. This matches $[J_i, P_j] = \epsilon_{ijk} P_k$.

$\{K_x, H\} = \{mx - p_x t, H\}$. For a free particle $H = p^2/(2m)$: $\{mx, H\} = m\{x, H\} = m \cdot p_x/m = p_x = P_x$. And $\{-p_x t, H\} = -t\{p_x, H\} = 0$. So $\{K_x, H\} = P_x$, matching $[K_i, H] = P_i$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that for a system in an external uniform gravitational field $V = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha g z_\alpha$, the Galilean-boost integral $\mathbf{K}$ is no longer conserved. Which symmetry is broken?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $d\mathbf{K}/dt$ and show it is nonzero. Identify which group element (translation, rotation, or boost) no longer leaves the Lagrangian invariant.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In an external field, $\mathbf{F}_\alpha = -m_\alpha g \hat{\mathbf{z}}$ and $\sum_\alpha \mathbf{F}_\alpha = -Mg\hat{\mathbf{z}} \neq 0$. So $d\mathbf{K}/dt = -t \cdot (-Mg\hat{\mathbf{z}}) = Mgt\,\hat{\mathbf{z}} \neq 0$. The broken symmetry is Galilean boost invariance: the potential $V = \sum m_\alpha g z_\alpha$ depends on the absolute position (height), so a Galilean boost $\mathbf{r} \to \mathbf{r} + \mathbf{v}t$ changes the potential and is no longer a symmetry. Spatial translations perpendicular to $\hat{\mathbf{z}}$ remain symmetries (the potential depends only on $z$), so $P_x$ and $P_y$ are still conserved; $P_z$ is not.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the map $\mu : \mathfrak{g} \to C^\infty(T^*Q)$ sending each Galilean generator to its corresponding integral is a Lie-algebra homomorphism: $\{\mu_X, \mu_Y\} = \mu_{[X,Y]}$ for all generators $X, Y \in \mathfrak{g}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Check the bracket relations one by one. The Poisson bracket of the integrals reproduces the Lie brackets of the generators.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The map $\mu$ sends $H \mapsto E$, $P_i \mapsto P_i$, $J_i \mapsto L_i$, $K_i \mapsto K_i$. The claim is that $\{\mu_X, \mu_Y\} = \mu_{[X,Y]}$. We verify each bracket:

$\{L_i, L_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk} L_k = \mu_{[J_i, J_j]}$. Correct.

$\{L_i, P_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk} P_k = \mu_{[J_i, P_j]}$. Correct.

$\{K_i, H\} = P_i = \mu_{[K_i, H]}$. Correct.

$\{K_i, P_j\} = 0 = \mu_{[K_i, P_j]}$. Correct (the Galilean algebra has $[K_i, P_j] = 0$, unlike the Poincare algebra).

$\{P_i, P_j\} = 0 = \mu_{[P_i, P_j]}$. Correct.

All brackets match, so $\mu$ is a Lie-algebra homomorphism. This is the **equivariant moment map** property: the Poisson algebra of the integrals is the canonical realisation of the Galilean Lie algebra on phase space.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

For the two-body Kepler problem, list which of the ten integrals are conserved and which are not. Identify the broken symmetries.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The two-body Kepler problem has an internal $1/r$ potential and no external field. Both particles interact.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

All ten integrals are conserved. The two-body Kepler problem is an isolated system with no external forces. The potential $V = -k/|\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2|$ depends only on the inter-particle distance, which is invariant under all ten Galilean transformations. Therefore $E$, $\mathbf{P}$, $\mathbf{L}$, and $\mathbf{K}$ are all conserved. In addition, the Kepler problem has an extra conserved quantity (the Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector) arising from a dynamical symmetry not captured by the Galilean group, making it superintegrable.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

The Galilean algebra has a central extension by mass: $[K_i, P_j] = M \delta_{ij}$ where $M$ is the total mass (central charge). Show that this extension appears in the Poisson brackets of $\mathbf{K}$ and $\mathbf{P}$ for a system of particles, and explain why the mass is a central element.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\{K_i, P_j\}$ carefully for a multi-particle system using $K_i = \sum_\alpha (m_\alpha r_{\alpha,i} - p_{\alpha,i} t)$ and $P_j = \sum_\beta p_{\beta,j}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\{K_i, P_j\} = \sum_\alpha \sum_\beta \{m_\alpha r_{\alpha,i} - p_{\alpha,i}t, p_{\beta,j}\} = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha \{r_{\alpha,i}, p_{\alpha,j}\} = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha \delta_{ij} = M\delta_{ij}$.

This is the **Bargmann extension** of the Galilean algebra: $[K_i, P_j] = M\delta_{ij}$ where $M$ is a central element (it Poisson-commutes with all generators: $\{M, H\} = 0$, $\{M, P_i\} = 0$, etc.). The mass is central because it is a constant of the theory — it does not change under any Galilean transformation. In quantum mechanics this central extension becomes important: the projective representations of the Galilean group require the mass as a superselection parameter, and different masses label inequivalent representations. This is the algebraic origin of the superselection rule that forbids superpositions of states with different mass.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the ten integrals of a free two-particle system can be reduced to the ten integrals of the centre-of-mass motion plus the integrals of the relative motion.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Introduce CM coordinates $\mathbf{R} = (m_1\mathbf{r}_1 + m_2\mathbf{r}_2)/M$ and relative coordinates $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In CM coordinates: $E = \tfrac{1}{2}M\dot{\mathbf{R}}^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{\mathbf{r}}^2 + V(r)$ where $\mu = m_1 m_2/M$ is the reduced mass. $\mathbf{P} = M\dot{\mathbf{R}}$ (CM momentum). $\mathbf{L} = M\mathbf{R}\times\dot{\mathbf{R}} + \mu\mathbf{r}\times\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ (sum of CM and relative angular momentum). $\mathbf{K} = M\mathbf{R} - \mathbf{P}t$ (purely CM).

The CM motion carries six integrals ($\mathbf{P}$, $\mathbf{K}$) and contributes to $E$ and $\mathbf{L}$. The relative motion carries the remaining integrals: the relative energy $E_{\mathrm{rel}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{\mathbf{r}}^2 + V(r)$, and the relative angular momentum $\mathbf{L}_{\mathrm{rel}} = \mu\mathbf{r}\times\dot{\mathbf{r}}$. The CM moves uniformly while the relative motion evolves independently — the separation of the two-body problem into CM and relative motion is the reflection, in coordinate space, of the Galilean symmetry structure.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Compute the Casimir operators of the Galilean algebra (the Bargmann central extension). Show that $C_1 = M$ and $C_2 = 2ME - P^2$ are Casimir operators (they commute with all generators). What is the physical meaning of $C_2$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A Casimir operator commutes with all generators of the algebra. Compute $\{C_2, H\}$, $\{C_2, P_i\}$, etc.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$C_1 = M$: this commutes with everything since $M$ is central.

$C_2 = 2ME - \mathbf{P}^2$: check $\{C_2, P_i\} = 2M\{E, P_i\} - 2P_j\{P_j, P_i\} = 0 - 0 = 0$. Check $\{C_2, H\} = 2M\{E, H\} - 2P_j\{P_j, H\} = 0 - 0 = 0$. Check $\{C_2, K_i\} = 2M\{E, K_i\} - 2P_j\{P_j, K_i\} = 2M(-P_i) - 2P_j M\delta_{ij} = -2MP_i + 2MP_i = 0$. Similarly $\{C_2, L_i\} = 0$ because $L_i$ is a rotation generator and both $E$ and $P^2$ are rotation scalars.

The physical meaning: $C_2/(2M) = E - P^2/(2M)$ is the **internal energy** of the system — the total energy minus the kinetic energy of the centre-of-mass motion. It is the energy available for internal dynamics (relative motion of the particles, binding energy, etc.). In the rest frame ($\mathbf{P} = 0$), $C_2/(2M) = E$, the rest energy. This is the Newtonian precursor of the relativistic invariant mass $mc^2$.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The Galilean group as a symmetry group of the Lagrangian

The free-particle Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{\mathbf{r}}^2$ is invariant under the full Galilean group in the following sense. Under time translation $t \to t + \tau$: $L$ is unchanged (no explicit $t$). Under spatial translation $\mathbf{r} \to \mathbf{r} + \mathbf{a}$: $L$ is unchanged ($\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ is unchanged). Under rotation $\mathbf{r} \to R\mathbf{r}$: $L$ is unchanged ($|R\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2 = |\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2$). Under Galilean boost $\mathbf{r} \to \mathbf{r} + \mathbf{v}t$: $L$ changes to $\tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot{\mathbf{r}} + \mathbf{v}|^2 = L + m\mathbf{v}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}} + \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$. The change is a total time derivative $d(m\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{r} + \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2 t)/dt$, which does not affect the equations of motion. So the Galilean boost is a **quasi-symmetry** of the Lagrangian: it leaves the action invariant up to boundary terms.

This quasi-symmetry is sufficient for Noether's theorem to produce a conserved quantity. The conserved quantity from the boost symmetry is $\mathbf{K} = m\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{p}t$, which is the integral associated with the uniform motion of the centre of mass.

### Landau's derivation of the Lagrangian from symmetry

Landau and Lifshitz reverse the usual logic. Instead of starting from a known Lagrangian and deriving symmetries, they start from the Galilean symmetries and derive the Lagrangian. For a free particle:

1. Homogeneity of space and time: $L$ cannot depend on $\mathbf{r}$ or $t$ explicitly. So $L = L(\mathbf{v})$.
2. Isotropy of space: $L$ cannot depend on the direction of $\mathbf{v}$, only on $|\mathbf{v}|^2$. So $L = L(v^2)$.
3. Galilean invariance: under a boost $\mathbf{v} \to \mathbf{v} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}$, the Lagrangian changes by $\tfrac{d}{dt}f(\mathbf{r}, t)$. Expanding $L(v^2) \to L(|\mathbf{v} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}|^2) = L(v^2 + 2\mathbf{v}\cdot\boldsymbol{\epsilon} + \epsilon^2) \approx L(v^2) + 2L'(v^2)\mathbf{v}\cdot\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ to first order in $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$. For this to be a total derivative, $2L'(v^2)\mathbf{v}$ must be a constant, which requires $L'(v^2) = \text{const}$, i.e., $L = \alpha v^2 + \beta$. Setting $\alpha = m/2$ and $\beta = 0$ gives $L = \tfrac{1}{2}mv^2$.

This derivation shows that the form of the free-particle Lagrangian is *forced* by the Galilean symmetries. The mass $m$ enters as a free parameter (the proportionality constant) that labels different irreducible representations of the Galilean group.

### Inönü-Wigner contraction and the non-relativistic limit

The Galilean algebra is obtained from the Poincare algebra by the Inönü-Wigner contraction: rescale $K_i \to c K_i$ and take $c \to \infty$. The Poincare bracket $[K_i, P_j] = \delta_{ij} H/c^2$ becomes $[K_i, P_j] = 0$ in the limit. The central extension $[K_i, P_j] = M\delta_{ij}$ survives as a remnant of the finite-$c$ structure.

This contraction explains why the Galilean group has a central extension while the Poincare group does not: in the Poincare algebra the energy plays the role of the central element (the mass-energy $E = Mc^2$), and after contraction the mass becomes a separate central charge. The physical consequence is that in non-relativistic quantum mechanics, mass is a superselection parameter (superpositions of different-mass states are forbidden), while in relativistic quantum mechanics mass is determined by the energy and no superselection rule applies.

### The Galilean group versus the Poincare group

The Galilean group is the $c \to \infty$ contraction of the Poincare group of special relativity. The ten generators of the Poincare group (energy, momentum, angular momentum, and boosts) satisfy a different Lie algebra in which $[K_i, P_j] = \delta_{ij} H/c^2$. In the limit $c \to \infty$, this becomes $[K_i, P_j] = 0$, the Galilean relation. The Bargmann central extension $[K_i, P_j] = M\delta_{ij}$ of the Galilean algebra is the remnant of the Poincare relation $[K_i, P_j] \propto H$ after the contraction.

The Casimir operators of the Poincare group are $P_\mu P^\mu = E^2/c^2 - \mathbf{P}^2 = m^2 c^2$ (the invariant mass) and $W_\mu W^\mu$ (the Pauli-Lubanski scalar, related to spin). Under Galilean contraction, $P_\mu P^\mu \to -\mathbf{P}^2 + 2ME/c^2 \cdot c^2 = 2ME - \mathbf{P}^2 = C_2$ (the internal energy times $2M$). The second Poincare Casimir contracts to $\mathbf{L}^2$ (the total angular momentum squared) for spinless particles.

### The moment map and symplectic reduction

The map $\mu : T^*Q \to \mathfrak{g}^*$ (the dual of the Galilean algebra) sending a phase-space point to the values of the ten integrals is the **moment map** of the Galilean group action on the symplectic manifold $T^*Q$. The level sets $\mu^{-1}(\xi)$ for $\xi \in \mathfrak{g}^*$ are the submanifolds of phase space where the ten integrals take fixed values. When the group acts freely and properly, the **symplectic reduction** $M_\xi = \mu^{-1}(\xi)/G_\xi$ (quotient by the stabiliser $G_\xi$ of $\xi$) is a reduced symplectic manifold of dimension $2n - 2\dim G_\xi$.

For the centre-of-mass reduction: fixing $\mathbf{P}$ and $\mathbf{K}$ reduces the phase space by six dimensions (the CM position and momentum are eliminated), leaving the relative-motion phase space as the symplectic quotient. This is the geometric formulation of the textbook reduction to relative coordinates.

### Explicit construction of the moment map

For a single particle of mass $m$ in three dimensions, the moment map $\mu : T^*\mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathfrak{g}^*$ assigns to each point $(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})$ the ten-component vector

$$
\mu(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p}) = \big(E, \mathbf{P}, \mathbf{L}, \mathbf{K}\big) = \Big(\frac{p^2}{2m}, \, \mathbf{p}, \, \mathbf{q}\times\mathbf{p}, \, m\mathbf{q} - \mathbf{p}t\Big).
$$

The image of $\mu$ is a coadjoint orbit of the Galilean group (more precisely, of the Bargmann group). Different values of $(E, \mathbf{P}, \mathbf{L}, \mathbf{K})$ correspond to different coadjoint orbits, and the symplectic structure on each orbit is the Kirillov-Kostant-Souriau form. For a multi-particle system, the moment map is the sum of the single-particle moment maps: $\mu = \sum_\alpha \mu_\alpha$, reflecting the additivity of the ten integrals.

The coadjoint orbits are classified by the values of the Casimir operators: $M$ (total mass) and $C_2 = 2ME - P^2$ (twice the total mass times the internal energy). For fixed $M$ and $C_2$, the orbits are parametrised by the angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ (for $C_2 > 0$, the relative motion is bounded and the orbit is a cotangent bundle of a sphere; for $C_2 < 0$, the relative motion is unbound and the orbit is a different symplectic manifold).

**Synthesis.** The Galilean group framework puts together the four space-time symmetries, the ten integrals, and their Poisson-bracket algebra into a single coherent structure; the central insight is that Noether's theorem is not a collection of separate arguments but a single Lie-algebra homomorphism from the symmetry group to the Poisson algebra of observables. The Bargmann central extension by mass is the foundational reason that mass appears as a superselection parameter in quantum mechanics, and the moment-map reduction is dual to the centre-of-mass separation used in every textbook treatment of the two-body problem. Putting these together, the ten integrals of the Galilean group are the complete set of space-time symmetry invariants of Newtonian mechanics, and their Poisson-bracket algebra encodes the full kinematic structure of Newtonian space-time.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The systematic enumeration of the ten integrals of motion appears in Landau and Lifshitz's *Mechanics* (§4–6), where the Galilean invariance of the free-particle Lagrangian is used to determine the form of the Lagrangian itself (up to a total derivative). This is a "top-down" approach: the symmetries come first, and the form of the Lagrangian is derived from them. This contrasts with the "bottom-up" approach of Newton, who postulated the force law and derived the conservation laws as consequences.

The Bargmann central extension was identified by Valentine Bargmann in 1954 ("On Unitary Ray Representations of Continuous Groups," *Annals of Mathematics* 59). The recognition that the Galilean group admits a nontrivial central extension was crucial for the representation theory of the group in quantum mechanics: the projective representations of the Galilean group (which are the physically relevant ones) are classified by the mass, explaining why mass is a fundamental parameter in non-relativistic quantum theory.

The moment-map perspective on the Galilean group was developed by Souriau (*Structure des systemes dynamiques*, 1970) and Marsden-Weinstein (1974), who showed that symplectic reduction by the Galilean group action produces the reduced phase space of the relative-motion problem.
