## Intuition [Beginner]

Watch a game of pool from the side of the table. Now walk to the other side and watch the same game. The physics is identical -- the balls collide the same way, follow the same rules, conserve the same quantities -- even though your viewpoint has shifted. This is the simplest instance of Galilean relativity: the laws of mechanics are the same in every inertial frame.

Now imagine a much bigger shift. Get in a train moving at constant velocity and play pool on the train. The game looks exactly the same as it did on the ground. The train is an inertial frame, and no mechanical experiment performed inside it can tell you whether the train is moving or standing still. Galileo articulated this principle in 1632 using the example of a ship: "Shut yourself up with some friend in the main cabin below decks... have the ship proceed with any speed you like, so long as the motion is uniform... you will not be able to tell whether the ship is moving or standing still."

The principle of Galilean relativity says that the laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames. An isolated mechanical system -- one with no external forces -- has ten fundamental symmetries, and each symmetry produces a conserved quantity through Noether's theorem. These ten conserved quantities are the **ten integrals of motion**:

1. **Energy** (1 quantity): the system looks the same at all times -- time-translation symmetry.
2. **Linear momentum** (3 quantities): the system looks the same from every position -- spatial-translation symmetry in three independent directions.
3. **Angular momentum** (3 quantities): the system looks the same from every angle -- rotational symmetry about three independent axes.
4. **Center-of-mass motion** (3 quantities): the system looks the same in every inertial frame -- Galilean boost symmetry in three independent directions.

The count is $1 + 3 + 3 + 3 = 10$. Every isolated Newtonian system has exactly these ten conserved quantities. They are the "integrals of motion" in the sense that they are constants along every valid trajectory -- they integrate to the same value at all times. No other conserved quantities exist for generic isolated systems; the ten integrals are complete.

The deep point is that the symmetries of space and time *are* the conservation laws. If space is the same everywhere (no preferred origin), momentum is conserved. If space is the same in every direction (no preferred axis), angular momentum is conserved. If time is the same at every moment (no preferred instant), energy is conserved. If physics is the same in every inertial frame (no preferred velocity), the center of mass moves at constant velocity. These are not independent assumptions -- they all follow from a single structure: the **Galilean group**, the group of all transformations that preserve the form of Newton's laws [ref: TODO_REF].

## Worked example [Beginner]

A single free particle of mass $m$ moves with constant velocity $\mathbf{v}_0$. Its position at time $t$ is $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}_0 t$.

**Energy.** The kinetic energy is $E = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}_0|^2$, constant for all time. This is the conserved quantity from time-translation symmetry: the system does not change if you shift the origin of time.

**Linear momentum.** $\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}_0$, constant for all time. This is the conserved quantity from spatial-translation symmetry: the system does not change if you shift the origin of space.

**Angular momentum.** $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p} = (\mathbf{r}_0 + \mathbf{v}_0 t) \times m\mathbf{v}_0 = \mathbf{r}_0 \times m\mathbf{v}_0 + t(\mathbf{v}_0 \times m\mathbf{v}_0) = \mathbf{r}_0 \times m\mathbf{v}_0$. The second term vanishes because $\mathbf{v}_0 \times \mathbf{v}_0 = 0$. So $\mathbf{L}$ is constant. This is the conserved quantity from rotational symmetry [ref: TODO_REF].

**Center-of-mass motion.** Define $\mathbf{K} = m\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{p}t = m\mathbf{r}_0 + m\mathbf{v}_0 t - m\mathbf{v}_0 t = m\mathbf{r}_0$, constant. This is the conserved quantity from boost symmetry: the system does not change if you change to a new inertial frame.

For a single free particle, all four conserved quantities are all constant as a direct consequence of the free-particle equations. For a multi-particle system, they become powerful: the total energy, total momentum, total angular momentum, and center-of-mass motion of an isolated system never change, regardless of how complicated the internal interactions are.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### The Galilean group

The **Galilean group** $\mathcal{G}$ is the group of transformations of Galilean spacetime that preserve the form of Newton's equations. An element $g \in \mathcal{G}$ acts on a spacetime event $(t, \mathbf{r})$ as:

$$
g: (t, \mathbf{r}) \;\longmapsto\; (t', \mathbf{r}'), \qquad t' = t + \tau, \quad \mathbf{r}' = R\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{v}t + \mathbf{a},
$$

where $\tau \in \mathbb{R}$ is a time translation, $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is a spatial translation, $R \in SO(3)$ is a rotation, and $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is a Galilean boost (change of inertial frame). The group has dimension $1 + 3 + 3 + 3 = 10$ [ref: TODO_REF].

The composition law for two elements $g_1 = (\tau_1, \mathbf{a}_1, \mathbf{v}_1, R_1)$ and $g_2 = (\tau_2, \mathbf{a}_2, \mathbf{v}_2, R_2)$ is:

$$
g_1 \cdot g_2 = \bigl(\tau_1 + \tau_2,\; \mathbf{a}_1 + R_1\mathbf{a}_2 + \mathbf{v}_1\tau_2,\; \mathbf{v}_1 + R_1\mathbf{v}_2,\; R_1 R_2\bigr).
$$

The non-abelian structure comes from the rotation subgroup $SO(3)$ and from the semi-direct product between boosts and translations: the order of translation followed by boost differs from boost followed by translation.

### The ten integrals of motion

For a system of $N$ particles with masses $m_\alpha$ at positions $\mathbf{r}_\alpha$, interacting through a translation-invariant, rotation-invariant, time-independent internal potential $V(\mathbf{r}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_N)$, the ten conserved quantities are [ref: TODO_REF]:

**Energy** (from time-translation symmetry):

$$
E = \sum_{\alpha=1}^{N} \frac{|\mathbf{p}_\alpha|^2}{2m_\alpha} + V(\mathbf{r}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_N).
$$

**Linear momentum** (from spatial-translation symmetry, 3 components):

$$
\mathbf{P} = \sum_{\alpha=1}^{N} \mathbf{p}_\alpha.
$$

**Angular momentum** (from rotational symmetry, 3 components):

$$
\mathbf{L} = \sum_{\alpha=1}^{N} \mathbf{r}_\alpha \times \mathbf{p}_\alpha.
$$

**Center-of-mass integral** (from boost symmetry, 3 components):

$$
\mathbf{K} = \sum_{\alpha=1}^{N} \bigl(m_\alpha \mathbf{r}_\alpha - \mathbf{p}_\alpha t\bigr) = M\mathbf{R}_{\rm cm} - \mathbf{P}t,
$$

where $M = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha$ is the total mass and $\mathbf{R}_{\rm cm} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_\alpha m_\alpha \mathbf{r}_\alpha$ is the center-of-mass position.

The constancy of $\mathbf{K}$ says that the center of mass moves at constant velocity: $\mathbf{R}_{\rm cm}(t) = \mathbf{R}_{\rm cm}(0) + (\mathbf{P}/M)t$, regardless of the internal dynamics. This is the content of Newton's third law for an isolated system.

### Galilean Lie algebra

The generators of the Galilean group satisfy the following commutation relations (Lie algebra), written using Poisson brackets on phase space [ref: TODO_REF]:

| $\{,\}$ | $H$ | $P_i$ | $L_i$ | $K_i$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| $H$ | $0$ | $0$ | $0$ | $P_i$ |
| $P_i$ | $0$ | $0$ | $\epsilon_{ijk}P_k$ | $m\delta_{ij}$ |
| $L_i$ | $0$ | $-\epsilon_{ijk}P_k$ | $\epsilon_{ijk}L_k$ | $\epsilon_{ijk}K_k$ |
| $K_i$ | $-P_i$ | $-m\delta_{ij}$ | $-\epsilon_{ijk}K_k$ | $0$ |

Here $m$ is the total mass of the system, $H$ is the Hamiltonian (generator of time translations), $P_i$ are the momentum components, $L_i$ are the angular momentum components, and $K_i$ are the boost generators.

Key features of this algebra:
- The momentum $\mathbf{P}$ is the Noether charge of spatial translations; it commutes with $H$ (energy is conserved).
- The angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ generates rotations; it rotates both $\mathbf{P}$ and $\mathbf{K}$ as vectors.
- The boost generators $\mathbf{K}$ do **not** commute with $H$: $\{K_i, H\} = P_i$. This reflects the fact that boosts change velocities (and hence kinetic energy), but the change is compensated by the shift in the reference frame.
- The boosts have a non-zero bracket with translations: $\{K_i, P_j\} = m\delta_{ij}$. The total mass $m$ appears as a central element -- it commutes with everything.

The appearance of $m$ in the algebra is the signal that the Galilean group admits a **central extension**: the mass is a central charge. This has profound consequences in quantum mechanics, where it leads to a superselection rule for mass [ref: TODO_REF].

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

### Noether derivation for spatial translations

**Theorem.** *If the Lagrangian $L(\mathbf{r}_\alpha, \dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha, t)$ is invariant under the infinitesimal translation $\mathbf{r}_\alpha \to \mathbf{r}_\alpha + \epsilon\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i$ (for each $i = 1, 2, 3$ independently), then the total linear momentum $\mathbf{P} = \sum_\alpha \mathbf{p}_\alpha$ is conserved.*

**Proof.** Under the infinitesimal transformation $\mathbf{r}_\alpha \to \mathbf{r}_\alpha + \epsilon\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i$, the Lagrangian changes by $\delta L = \epsilon \sum_\alpha \frac{\partial L}{\partial r_\alpha^i}$. Invariance requires $\delta L = 0$, hence $\sum_\alpha \frac{\partial L}{\partial r_\alpha^i} = 0$. By the Euler-Lagrange equations, $\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{r}_\alpha^i} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial r_\alpha^i}$. Summing over $\alpha$: $\frac{d}{dt}\sum_\alpha \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{r}_\alpha^i} = \sum_\alpha \frac{\partial L}{\partial r_\alpha^i} = 0$. Since $p_{\alpha,i} = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{r}_\alpha^i}$, this gives $\frac{dP_i}{dt} = 0$. $\square$

### Noether derivation for rotations

**Theorem.** *If $L$ is invariant under infinitesimal rotations about axis $\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i$, then the component $L_i$ of total angular momentum is conserved.*

**Proof.** An infinitesimal rotation about the $z$-axis sends $\mathbf{r}_\alpha \to \mathbf{r}_\alpha + \epsilon(\hat{\mathbf{z}} \times \mathbf{r}_\alpha)$ and $\dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha \to \dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha + \epsilon(\hat{\mathbf{z}} \times \dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha)$. The change in $L$ is $\delta L = \epsilon \sum_\alpha \left[\frac{\partial L}{\partial \mathbf{r}_\alpha}\cdot(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\times\mathbf{r}_\alpha) + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha}\cdot(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\times\dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha)\right]$. Invariance requires $\delta L = 0$. Using $\mathbf{a}\cdot(\hat{\mathbf{z}}\times\mathbf{b}) = \hat{\mathbf{z}}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{a})$ and the Euler-Lagrange equations: $\frac{d}{dt}\sum_\alpha \mathbf{r}_\alpha \times \mathbf{p}_\alpha = 0$, so $L_z$ is constant. The same argument works for the other two axes. $\square$

### Noether derivation for Galilean boosts

**Theorem.** *If the Lagrangian of an isolated N-particle system is invariant under Galilean boosts up to a total time derivative, then the quantity $\mathbf{K} = \sum_\alpha(m_\alpha\mathbf{r}_\alpha - \mathbf{p}_\alpha t)$ is conserved.*

**Proof.** An infinitesimal Galilean boost along $\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i$ sends $\mathbf{r}_\alpha \to \mathbf{r}_\alpha$ and $\dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha \to \dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha + \epsilon\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i$ (velocities shift by a constant). For the standard Lagrangian $L = \sum_\alpha \tfrac{1}{2}m_\alpha|\dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha|^2 - V(\mathbf{r}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{r}_N)$:

$$
L \to \sum_\alpha \tfrac{1}{2}m_\alpha|\dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha + \epsilon\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i|^2 - V = L + \epsilon\sum_\alpha m_\alpha \dot{r}_\alpha^i + O(\epsilon^2).
$$

The change is $\delta L = \epsilon \sum_\alpha m_\alpha \dot{r}_\alpha^i = \epsilon \frac{d}{dt}\sum_\alpha m_\alpha r_\alpha^i = \frac{d}{dt}(\epsilon \sum_\alpha m_\alpha r_\alpha^i)$. This is a total time derivative, so by the extended Noether theorem, the quantity

$$
Q = \sum_\alpha \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{r}_\alpha^i} \cdot 0 - \left(-\sum_\alpha m_\alpha r_\alpha^i\right) = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha r_\alpha^i - t\sum_\alpha p_{\alpha,i}
$$

is conserved. The Noether charge is $\mathbf{K} = \sum_\alpha(m_\alpha\mathbf{r}_\alpha - \mathbf{p}_\alpha t)$. $\square$

The key difference from translations and rotations is that boosts change $L$ by a total time derivative, not by zero. This is a **quasi-symmetry**: the action changes by a boundary term, which is sufficient for Noether's theorem to apply. The requirement that $V$ depends only on relative positions $\mathbf{r}_\alpha - \mathbf{r}_\beta$ (not on absolute positions) is essential [ref: TODO_REF].

### Poisson bracket realization

The ten conserved quantities $(H, P_i, L_i, K_i)$ are functions on the $6N$-dimensional phase space. Their Poisson brackets close to form the Galilean Lie algebra:

$$
\{P_i, L_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk}P_k, \qquad \{L_i, L_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk}L_k, \qquad \{K_i, H\} = P_i,
$$

$$
\{K_i, P_j\} = M\delta_{ij}, \qquad \{K_i, L_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk}K_k,
$$

where $M = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha$ is the total mass. The map from Galilean group generators to phase-space functions is a **Lie algebra homomorphism** (it preserves the bracket structure). This is the Poisson-bracket realization of the Galilean algebra [ref: TODO_REF].

**Bridge.** The Galilean group organizes the symmetries of Newtonian spacetime into a single algebraic structure; the foundational reason is that the homogeneity and isotropy of space and time, together with the equivalence of inertial frames, generate a 10-dimensional Lie group. The central insight is that the ten conserved quantities are not independent facts but are unified as the coadjoint orbit of the Galilean group acting on phase space. The Poisson bracket algebra of the integrals mirrors the group composition law. The generalisation to non-inertial frames and constrained systems appears in the Hamiltonian formalism via canonical transformations, and putting these together, the Galilean group provides the complete symmetry classification for isolated Newtonian systems.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Verify directly that the center-of-mass integral $\mathbf{K} = M\mathbf{R}_{\rm cm} - \mathbf{P}t$ is constant for an isolated two-particle system with $m_1 = m_2 = m$, $\mathbf{r}_1 = (x_1, 0, 0)$, $\mathbf{r}_2 = (x_2, 0, 0)$, interacting through $V = k|x_1 - x_2|$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $d\mathbf{K}/dt = M\dot{\mathbf{R}}_{\rm cm} - \mathbf{P}$. Show this vanishes using Newton's third law.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathbf{P} = m\dot{\mathbf{r}}_1 + m\dot{\mathbf{r}}_2$. The forces are $\mathbf{F}_1 = -\partial V/\partial x_1 = -k\,\mathrm{sgn}(x_1 - x_2)$ and $\mathbf{F}_2 = +k\,\mathrm{sgn}(x_1 - x_2)$. By Newton's second law: $d\mathbf{P}/dt = \mathbf{F}_1 + \mathbf{F}_2 = 0$, so $\mathbf{P}$ is constant.

$\dot{\mathbf{R}}_{\rm cm} = \mathbf{P}/M = \mathbf{P}/(2m)$, also constant. Therefore $d\mathbf{K}/dt = M \cdot \mathbf{P}/M - \mathbf{P} = \mathbf{P} - \mathbf{P} = 0$. The center of mass moves at constant velocity.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the Poisson bracket $\{K_x, P_x\} = M$ for a single particle of mass $m$, where $K_x = mx - p_x t$ and $P_x = p_x$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the canonical Poisson brackets $\{x, p_x\} = 1$ and $\{t, p_x\} = 0$ (time is not a phase-space variable).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\{K_x, P_x\} = \{mx - p_x t, p_x\} = m\{x, p_x\} - t\{p_x, p_x\} = m(1) - t(0) = m = M$ for a single particle. For an $N$-particle system, $\{K_x, P_x\} = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha\{x_\alpha, p_{\alpha,x}\} = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha = M$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the Poisson bracket $\{K_i, H\}$ for an $N$-particle system with $H = \sum_\alpha \frac{|\mathbf{p}_\alpha|^2}{2m_\alpha} + V(\mathbf{r}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_N)$ where $V$ depends only on relative positions. Show that the result is $P_i$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$K_i = \sum_\alpha(m_\alpha r_\alpha^i - p_{\alpha,i}t)$. Use $\{r_\alpha^i, p_{\beta,j}\} = \delta_{\alpha\beta}\delta_{ij}$ and $\{p_{\alpha,i}, V\} = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial r_\alpha^i}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\{K_i, H\} = \sum_\alpha\left[m_\alpha\{r_\alpha^i, H\} - t\{p_{\alpha,i}, H\}\right]$.

$\{r_\alpha^i, H\} = \{r_\alpha^i, \frac{p_{\alpha,i}^2}{2m_\alpha}\} = \frac{p_{\alpha,i}}{m_\alpha}$ (using canonical brackets).

$\{p_{\alpha,i}, H\} = -\frac{\partial V}{\partial r_\alpha^i}$.

So $\{K_i, H\} = \sum_\alpha\left[p_{\alpha,i} + t\frac{\partial V}{\partial r_\alpha^i}\right]$. Since $V$ depends only on relative positions $\mathbf{r}_\alpha - \mathbf{r}_\beta$, we have $\sum_\alpha \frac{\partial V}{\partial r_\alpha^i} = 0$ (each term cancels with its partner). Therefore $\{K_i, H\} = \sum_\alpha p_{\alpha,i} = P_i$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the rotation generators $L_i = \epsilon_{ijk}\sum_\alpha r_\alpha^j p_{\alpha,k}$ satisfy the angular momentum algebra $\{L_i, L_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk}L_k$ under Poisson brackets.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the identity $\epsilon_{iab}\epsilon_{jcd} = \delta_{ij}\delta_{ac}\delta_{bd} - \ldots$ or work component by component. Start with $\{L_x, L_y\}$ and show it equals $L_z$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$L_x = \sum_\alpha(y_\alpha p_{\alpha,z} - z_\alpha p_{\alpha,y})$, $L_y = \sum_\alpha(z_\alpha p_{\alpha,x} - x_\alpha p_{\alpha,z})$.

$\{L_x, L_y\} = \sum_\alpha\left[\{y_\alpha p_{\alpha,z}, z_\alpha p_{\alpha,x}\} - \{y_\alpha p_{\alpha,z}, x_\alpha p_{\alpha,z}\} - \{z_\alpha p_{\alpha,y}, z_\alpha p_{\alpha,x}\} + \{z_\alpha p_{\alpha,y}, x_\alpha p_{\alpha,z}\}\right]$.

The key terms: $\{y_\alpha p_{\alpha,z}, z_\alpha p_{\alpha,x}\}$ -- expand using the Leibniz rule and $\{y_\alpha, p_{\alpha,x}\} = 0$, $\{y_\alpha, z_\alpha\} = 0$, $\{p_{\alpha,z}, z_\alpha\} = -1$, $\{p_{\alpha,z}, p_{\alpha,x}\} = 0$: this gives $-y_\alpha p_{\alpha,x}$.

Similarly: $\{z_\alpha p_{\alpha,y}, x_\alpha p_{\alpha,z}\}$ gives $x_\alpha p_{\alpha,y}$.

The cross terms vanish. So $\{L_x, L_y\} = \sum_\alpha(x_\alpha p_{\alpha,y} - y_\alpha p_{\alpha,x}) = L_z$. The same pattern gives the full algebra $\{L_i, L_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk}L_k$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Galilean boost transformation $g_v: (t, \mathbf{r}_\alpha) \mapsto (t, \mathbf{r}_\alpha + \mathbf{v}t)$ changes the Lagrangian by a total time derivative for an isolated $N$-particle system: $L' = L + \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{1}{2}Mv^2 t + M\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{R}_{\rm cm}\right)$, where $M$ is the total mass and $\mathbf{R}_{\rm cm}$ is the center of mass. Conclude that the equations of motion are invariant.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Under the boost, $\dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha \to \dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha + \mathbf{v}$. Expand the new kinetic energy and use the fact that $V$ depends only on relative positions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Under the boost: $\mathbf{r}_\alpha \to \mathbf{r}_\alpha + \mathbf{v}t$, $\dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha \to \dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha + \mathbf{v}$.

New kinetic energy: $T' = \sum_\alpha \tfrac{1}{2}m_\alpha|\dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha + \mathbf{v}|^2 = T + \mathbf{v}\cdot\sum_\alpha m_\alpha\dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha + \tfrac{1}{2}Mv^2 = T + \mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{P} + \tfrac{1}{2}Mv^2$.

Since $V$ depends only on relative positions and these are unchanged ($(\mathbf{r}_\alpha + \mathbf{v}t) - (\mathbf{r}_\beta + \mathbf{v}t) = \mathbf{r}_\alpha - \mathbf{r}_\beta$), $V' = V$.

$L' = T' - V' = L + \mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{P} + \tfrac{1}{2}Mv^2 = L + \frac{d}{dt}\left(\mathbf{v}\cdot M\mathbf{R}_{\rm cm} + \tfrac{1}{2}Mv^2 t\right)$.

The extra term is a total time derivative $\frac{dF}{dt}$ with $F = \mathbf{v}\cdot M\mathbf{R}_{\rm cm} + \tfrac{1}{2}Mv^2 t$. Total time derivatives do not affect the Euler-Lagrange equations, so the equations of motion are invariant.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Construct the $5\times 5$ matrix representation of a Galilean transformation: $g = (\tau, \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{v}, R)$ acts on the column vector $(1, t, x, y, z)^T$. Show that the composition of two boosts $g_{v_1}$ and $g_{v_2}$ gives a boost by $\mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_2$ plus a spatial translation $\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{v}_1\tau_2$. Explain why this extra translation appears and how it relates to the non-commutativity of boosts with time translations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write boosts as matrices acting on $(1, t, x, y, z)^T$ and multiply. The key is that boosts change positions by $\mathbf{v}t$, so composing two boosts introduces a term proportional to $t$ that gets absorbed into a translation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A Galilean boost by velocity $\mathbf{v}$ can be written as:

$$
g_v = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ \mathbf{0} & 1 & \mathbf{v}^T \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{0} & I_3 \end{pmatrix}
$$

acting on the augmented vector $(1, t, \mathbf{r})^T$. (The first component 1 is included to handle the affine term.)

A boost $\mathbf{v}_1$ followed by boost $\mathbf{v}_2$ gives velocity $\mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_2$ (Galilean velocity addition). In the matrix representation, the composition is straightforward because the boost matrices are abelian: $g_{v_2} \cdot g_{v_1}$ gives $\mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_2$.

The non-obvious composition is boost with time translation: $g_v \cdot g_\tau$ produces not just a boost and a time shift but also a spatial translation $\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{v}\tau$. This is because boosting by $\mathbf{v}$ and then shifting time by $\tau$ moves the particle by $\mathbf{v}\tau$ relative to the boosted frame. The semi-direct product structure $\mathbf{a}_1 + R_1\mathbf{a}_2 + \mathbf{v}_1\tau_2$ in the composition law encodes precisely this coupling.

For the free particle ($V = 0$), verify all ten independent Poisson brackets among the generators $(H, P_i, L_i, K_i)$ listed in the Galilean Lie algebra table. In particular, show that $\{K_i, K_j\} = 0$ and $\{K_i, L_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk}K_k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The formalisation of the Galilean group and the ten integrals requires: (1) the Galilean group $\mathcal{G}$ as a 10-dimensional Lie group with its matrix representation and composition law; (2) the Galilean Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ with generators $H$, $P_i$, $L_i$, $K_i$ and the bracket relations; (3) the Poisson-bracket realisation on $T^*(\mathbb{R}^{3N})$ mapping Lie algebra generators to phase-space functions; (4) the ten integrals of motion as Noether charges and the proof that their Poisson brackets reproduce the Lie algebra; (5) the Bargmann central extension. Items (1)-(2) can be built on Mathlib's Lie group infrastructure. Items (3)-(4) require the momentum map (co-moment map) from the Lie algebra to smooth functions on the symplectic manifold. Item (5) requires the theory of central extensions of Lie algebras.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Galilean group vs Lorentz group

The Galilean group is the $c \to \infty$ limit (or, more precisely, the $v/c \to 0$ limit) of the **Poincare group**, the symmetry group of special relativity. Both groups share the subgroups of rotations $SO(3)$ and spatial translations $\mathbb{R}^3$, but they differ in how boosts act:

- **Galilean boosts**: $\mathbf{r}' = \mathbf{r} - \mathbf{v}t$, $t' = t$. Velocities add: $\mathbf{u}' = \mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}$.
- **Lorentz boosts**: $(ct', x') = \gamma(ct - \beta x, x - \beta ct)$ with $\beta = v/c$, $\gamma = 1/\sqrt{1-\beta^2}$. Velocities add relativistically: $u' = (u - v)/(1 - uv/c^2)$.

The Galilean group has 10 parameters; the Poincare group also has 10 parameters (time translation, 3 spatial translations, 3 rotations, 3 Lorentz boosts). The number of conserved quantities is the same, but their structure differs [ref: TODO_REF]:

| Conserved quantity | Galilean group | Poincare group |
|---|---|---|
| Time translation | Energy $E$ | Energy $E = \gamma mc^2$ |
| Spatial translations | Momentum $\mathbf{P}$ | Momentum $\mathbf{P} = \gamma m\mathbf{v}$ |
| Rotations | Angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ | Angular momentum $\mathbf{L}$ |
| Boosts | Center-of-mass $\mathbf{K} = M\mathbf{R} - \mathbf{P}t$ | Center-of-energy $\mathbf{K} = t\mathbf{P} - E\mathbf{R}/c^2$ |

In the Galilean limit $c \to \infty$: $\gamma \to 1$, $E/c^2 \to m$, and the Poincare center-of-energy integral $\mathbf{K} = t\mathbf{P} - (E/c^2)\mathbf{R}$ becomes $t\mathbf{P} - m\mathbf{R} = -\mathbf{K}_{\rm Galilean}$, recovering the Galilean center-of-mass integral.

The key structural difference is in the Lie algebra. For the Poincare group, $\{K_i^{\rm Poincare}, H\} = P_i$ and $\{K_i^{\rm Poincare}, P_j\} = (H/c^2)\delta_{ij}$. In the limit $c \to \infty$, the second bracket becomes $\{K_i, P_j\} = 0$ -- but this is wrong for the Galilean algebra, where $\{K_i, P_j\} = M\delta_{ij}$. The mass $M$ appears as a remnant of the relativistic energy term $H/c^2$. This signals that the Galilean group is not simply the Poincare group with $c = \infty$; it is a **contraction** of the Poincare group (Inonu-Wigner contraction, 1953), and the contraction procedure generates the central extension.

### Bargmann central extension

The Galilean group as defined above has the property that $\{K_i, P_j\} = M\delta_{ij}$, where $M$ is a central element (it commutes with all generators). This means the algebra of observables is not the Galilean Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ itself but a **central extension** of $\mathfrak{g}$ by a one-dimensional center spanned by $M$. This extended algebra is called the **Bargmann algebra** $\mathfrak{g}_B$ [ref: TODO_REF].

The Bargmann algebra $\mathfrak{g}_B$ is an 11-dimensional Lie algebra with generators $(H, P_i, L_i, K_i, M)$ and the additional relation $[M, \cdot] = 0$ (M commutes with everything). The bracket $\{K_i, P_j\} = M\delta_{ij}$ is the only place where $M$ appears in a non-vanishing bracket..

In classical mechanics, the central extension is a curiosity: you can always work with $\mathfrak{g}_B$ or quotient out $M$ and work with $\mathfrak{g}$. In quantum mechanics, the situation is fundamentally different. The representation theory of the Galilean group requires **projective representations** (representations up to a phase), and the projective representations of $\mathcal{G}$ are in one-to-one correspondence with the ordinary representations of the centrally extended group $\mathcal{G}_B$ (the Bargmann group). The mass $M$ labels the irreducible projective representations and gives rise to the **mass superselection rule**: you cannot form coherent superpositions of states with different total mass [ref: TODO_REF].

This is one of the deepest connections between classical and quantum mechanics. The Bargmann central extension, which is an algebraic curiosity in the classical theory, becomes a physical necessity in the quantum theory. It is the reason that mass is a fundamental parameter in non-relativistic quantum mechanics and cannot be changed by any Galilean-invariant operation.

### Projective representations

A **projective representation** of a group $G$ on a vector space $V$ is a map $\rho: G \to GL(V)$ satisfying $\rho(g_1)\rho(g_2) = \omega(g_1, g_2)\rho(g_1 g_2)$, where $\omega(g_1, g_2) \in U(1)$ is a phase factor. For the Galilean group, the phase factor is $\omega(g_1, g_2) = e^{im\theta(g_1, g_2)/\hbar}$, where $m$ is the total mass and $\theta$ is a 2-cocycle determined by the group composition law.

Bargmann (1954) proved that every continuous projective representation of the Galilean group arises from an ordinary representation of the central extension $\mathcal{G}_B$. The 2-cocycle $\theta$ measures the failure of the representation to be genuine; it is classified by the second cohomology group $H^2(\mathcal{G}, U(1)) \cong \mathbb{R}$, and the real parameter labelling the cohomology class is the mass $m$.

The contrast with the Poincare group is sharp. The Poincare group has $H^2(\mathcal{G}_{\rm Poincare}, U(1)) = 0$: all projective representations are genuine representations (can have the phase absorbed). There is no mass superselection rule in relativistic quantum mechanics -- mass is a Casimir operator of the Poincare algebra, not a central charge. This difference is a direct consequence of the Inonu-Wigner contraction: the central charge of the Galilean algebra descends from the $H/c^2$ term in the Poincare algebra [ref: TODO_REF].

### Historical development

Galileo introduced the principle of relativity in 1632 in his *Dialogue*, using the ship analogy to argue that no mechanical experiment can detect uniform motion. Newton's Principia (1687) formalized this principle in the laws of motion and the Galilean transformation of coordinates between inertial frames.

The systematic study of the Galilean group and its Lie algebra waited until the development of Lie group theory in the late 19th century. Sophus Lie's program (1880s-1890s) provided the general framework for continuous groups, and the Galilean group became a standard example. However, the specific Lie-algebraic structure of the Galilean group -- the bracket $\{K_i, P_j\} = M\delta_{ij}$ -- was not emphasized in the classical mechanics literature until the 20th century.

Eugene Wigner, in his 1939 paper "On Unitary Representations of the Inhomogeneous Lorentz Group," classified the representations of the Poincare group and showed that mass and spin label the irreducible representations. The analogous classification for the Galilean group was carried out by several authors in the 1950s, culminating in Bargmann's 1954 paper "On Unitary Ray Representations of Continuous Groups" (*Annals of Mathematics* **59**, 1-46), which established the central extension and showed that mass is the parameter labelling the projective representations. Bargmann showed that the Galilean group has one substantive central extension, classified by a single real number (the mass), and that this extension is essential for the quantum-mechanical representation theory.

The Inonu-Wigner contraction (1953) clarified the relationship between the Galilean and Poincare groups: the Galilean group is obtained from the Poincare group in the limit $c \to \infty$, and the central extension of the Galilean algebra arises from the contraction of the Poincare algebra. This provides a systematic derivation of the mass central charge from relativistic physics.

**Synthesis.** The Galilean group is the foundational reason that all isolated Newtonian systems share the same ten conserved quantities; the central insight is that the symmetries of Galilean spacetime (homogeneity and isotropy of space, homogeneity of time, equivalence of inertial frames) form a 10-dimensional Lie group whose Noether charges are energy, momentum, angular momentum, and center-of-mass motion. This is exactly the content of the Galilean Lie algebra and its Poisson-bracket realization. The consequences extend beyond classical mechanics: the Bargmann central extension makes mass a central charge that governs the projective representations of the Galilean group in quantum mechanics, and the Inonu-Wigner contraction connects the Galilean algebra to the Poincare algebra of special relativity. Putting these together, the ten integrals of motion are not ten independent facts but a single algebraic structure -- the coadjoint orbit of the Galilean group -- that constrains the dynamics of every isolated Newtonian system.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Galileo's *Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems* (1632) contains the earliest clear statement of the relativity principle. The famous ship passage (Day Two) argues that observations made inside a smoothly sailing ship cannot determine its speed. This was a direct challenge to the Aristotelian worldview, which held that motion required a cause and could be detected. Galileo's insight was that uniform motion is indistinguishable from rest -- not merely as a practical matter but as a fundamental principle of nature.

Newton incorporated the Galilean relativity principle into his laws of motion (1687). The first law (inertia) states that a body moves at constant velocity unless acted upon by a force; this is the statement that the rest frame of a free particle is an inertial frame. The second and third laws, taken together, imply that the equations of motion are invariant under Galilean transformations. Newton himself noted this invariance in the Scholium to the Principia, though he did not develop its algebraic structure.

The algebraic study of the Galilean group began with the development of Lie theory in the 1880s. Sophus Lie's program of classifying continuous transformation groups provided the natural language for the Galilean group, but the application to physics was not systematic until the 20th century. The key figure was Eugene Wigner, who in 1939 classified the irreducible unitary representations of the Poincare group and showed that mass and spin label the elementary particles. Wigner's approach demonstrated that the symmetry group of spacetime determines the possible types of particles through its representation theory.

Valya Bargmann, a student of Wigner, extended this program to the Galilean group in a series of papers in the early 1950s, culminating in his 1954 Annals of Mathematics paper. Bargmann's crucial discovery was that the Galilean group admits projective representations that are not genuine representations, and these projective representations are classified by the total mass. This was surprising: the Poincare group has no such projective representations (its second cohomology group vanishes). The mass superselection rule -- the impossibility of forming coherent superpositions of states with different total mass -- is a direct consequence of the Bargmann central extension.

The Inonu-Wigner contraction (1953) showed that the Galilean group is a contraction of the Poincare group in the limit $c \to \infty$. Under this contraction, the Poincare algebra contracts to the Galilean algebra, and the $H/c^2$ term in the Poincare bracket $\{K_i, P_j\} = (H/c^2)\delta_{ij}$ becomes the central charge $M\delta_{ij}$ in the Galilean algebra. The central extension is thus a relic of the finite speed of light, surviving in the non-relativistic limit.

The philosophical significance of the Galilean group is that it encodes the structure of Newtonian spacetime in algebraic form. The ten symmetries are not assumptions but consequences of the structure of space and time: space is homogeneous (same at every point) and isotropic (same in every direction), time is homogeneous (same at every moment), and inertial frames are equivalent (same physics from every uniformly moving viewpoint). The ten integrals of motion are the physical manifestation of these geometric properties. The Bargmann central extension adds a further dimension: mass is not an arbitrary parameter but a geometric object (a central charge) arising from the structure of the Galilean group. In quantum mechanics, this geometric interpretation of mass becomes a physical constraint (the superselection rule).
