## Intuition [Beginner]

Hit a block of metal with a hammer and two kinds of waves race through it. One is a **compression wave** — regions of the metal squeeze together and spring apart, like a Slinky being pushed end-to-end. The other is a **shear wave** — layers of metal slide sideways past each other, like shaking a rope up and down.

These are **longitudinal** and **transverse** waves. In a longitudinal wave, the motion of the material is along the direction the wave travels. In a transverse wave, the motion is perpendicular. Sound in air is purely longitudinal (air has no shear stiffness), but in a solid, both types propagate.

The tool that unifies the description is the **stress tensor** $\sigma_{ij}$. This $3 \times 3$ matrix encodes the force per unit area acting on every surface through every point of the body. The diagonal entries are normal stresses (compression or tension). The off-diagonal entries are shear stresses. The tensor is symmetric ($\sigma_{ij} = \sigma_{ji}$) because otherwise the material would spin — angular momentum conservation forces the symmetry.

The wave speeds depend on the material's elastic constants. For an isotropic solid, there are only two independent constants: the **Lame parameters** $\lambda$ and $\mu$. The longitudinal wave speed is $c_L = \sqrt{(\lambda + 2\mu)/\rho}$ and the transverse wave speed is $c_T = \sqrt{\mu/\rho}$, where $\rho$ is the density. Since $\lambda > 0$ and $\mu > 0$, the longitudinal wave is always faster — typically about twice as fast.

## Worked example [Beginner]

For steel with density $\rho = 7800\ \mathrm{kg/m}^3$, Young's modulus $E = 200\ \mathrm{GPa}$, and Poisson's ratio $\nu = 0.3$, compute the longitudinal and transverse wave speeds.

The Lame parameters are $\mu = E/(2(1+\nu)) = 200/(2 \cdot 1.3) \approx 76.9\ \mathrm{GPa}$ and $\lambda = E\nu/((1+\nu)(1-2\nu)) = 200 \cdot 0.3/(1.3 \cdot 0.4) \approx 115.4\ \mathrm{GPa}$.

Longitudinal speed: $c_L = \sqrt{(115.4 + 2 \cdot 76.9) \times 10^9 / 7800} = \sqrt{269.2 \times 10^9 / 7800} = \sqrt{3.45 \times 10^7} \approx 5875\ \mathrm{m/s}$.

Transverse speed: $c_T = \sqrt{76.9 \times 10^9 / 7800} = \sqrt{9.86 \times 10^6} \approx 3140\ \mathrm{m/s}$.

The ratio $c_L/c_T \approx 1.87$. In earthquakes, the P-wave arrives first, then the S-wave about 1.9 times later — the basis for earthquake early-warning systems.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**The displacement field.** Let $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}, t)$ be the displacement of a material point from its reference position $\mathbf{x}$. The **strain tensor** is

$$\varepsilon_{ij} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j} + \frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i}\right).$$

This symmetric tensor measures the local deformation (stretching and shearing) of the material.

**The Cauchy stress tensor.** The **Cauchy stress principle** states that the internal force per unit area on any surface with unit normal $\hat{n}$ at point $\mathbf{x}$ is $\mathbf{t} = \sigma \hat{n}$, where $\sigma$ is the stress tensor. Cauchy's theorem proves that the existence of $\sigma$ follows from balance of linear momentum and the tetrahedron argument. Symmetry $\sigma_{ij} = \sigma_{ji}$ follows from balance of angular momentum.

**The equations of motion.** Newton's second law for an infinitesimal volume element gives

$$\rho\,\ddot{u}_i = \frac{\partial \sigma_{ij}}{\partial x_j} + f_i,$$

where $f_i$ is the body force per unit volume. This is the **Cauchy momentum equation**.

**Linear elasticity (Hooke's law).** For small strains, the stress is linear in the strain:

$$\sigma_{ij} = C_{ijkl}\varepsilon_{kl},$$

where $C_{ijkl}$ is the fourth-order **elastic stiffness tensor**. For an isotropic material, $C_{ijkl} = \lambda\delta_{ij}\delta_{kl} + \mu(\delta_{ik}\delta_{jl} + \delta_{il}\delta_{jk})$, giving

$$\sigma_{ij} = \lambda\,\varepsilon_{kk}\delta_{ij} + 2\mu\,\varepsilon_{ij}.$$

Substituting into the Cauchy equation gives the **Navier equation**:

$$\rho\,\ddot{\mathbf{u}} = (\lambda + \mu)\nabla(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}) + \mu\nabla^2\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{f}.$$

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Separation into longitudinal and transverse waves).**

The Navier equation for the displacement field $\mathbf{u}$ (with $\mathbf{f} = 0$) is:

$$\rho\,\ddot{\mathbf{u}} = (\lambda + \mu)\nabla(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}) + \mu\nabla^2\mathbf{u}.$$

Decompose $\mathbf{u}$ into its **irrotational** (longitudinal) and **solenoidal** (transverse) parts via the Helmholtz decomposition: $\mathbf{u} = \nabla\phi + \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ where $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{A} = 0$.

The scalar potential $\phi$ (the irrotational part) satisfies

$$\rho\,\ddot\phi = (\lambda + 2\mu)\nabla^2\phi,$$

a wave equation with speed $c_L = \sqrt{(\lambda + 2\mu)/\rho}$.

The vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ (the solenoidal part) satisfies

$$\rho\,\ddot{\mathbf{A}} = \mu\nabla^2\mathbf{A},$$

a wave equation with speed $c_T = \sqrt{\mu/\rho}$.

The derivation works by substituting $\mathbf{u} = \nabla\phi + \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ into the Navier equation, using $\nabla\times\nabla\phi = 0$ (the curl of a gradient vanishes) and $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{A}) = 0$ (the divergence of a curl vanishes), and separating the two components.

Plane-wave solutions: a longitudinal wave has displacement along the propagation direction ($\mathbf{u} \parallel \mathbf{k}$) and speed $c_L$. A transverse wave has displacement perpendicular to the propagation direction ($\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{k}$) and speed $c_T$. Since $c_L > c_T$, the longitudinal wave always arrives first.

**Bridge.** The Helmholtz decomposition and the separation into P-waves and S-waves builds toward the analysis of seismic wave propagation in geophysics, where the speed contrast between P and S waves is the primary diagnostic for Earth's internal structure. The foundational reason the separation works is that the isotropic elastic stiffness tensor has exactly two independent parameters, which generate two and only two wave speeds — this generalises to anisotropic media where the Christoffel equation produces three wave speeds (one quasi-longitudinal, two quasi-transverse). The central insight is that the number of independent wave modes equals the number of independent elastic constants, and the Navier equation is the load-bearing structure that connects the constitutive law (Hooke's law) to the wave speeds. This is dual to the analysis of electromagnetic waves in anisotropic media [10.04.03], where the dielectric tensor plays the role of the stiffness tensor.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the wave equation for the scalar potential $\phi$ from the Navier equation by substituting $\mathbf{u} = \nabla\phi$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $\mathbf{u} = \nabla\phi$: $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} = \nabla^2\phi$ and $\nabla\times\mathbf{u} = 0$. Substitute into the Navier equation.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\rho\,\nabla\ddot\phi = (\lambda+\mu)\nabla(\nabla^2\phi) + \mu\nabla^2(\nabla\phi) = (\lambda+\mu)\nabla(\nabla^2\phi) + \mu\nabla(\nabla^2\phi) = (\lambda+2\mu)\nabla(\nabla^2\phi)$. Taking the gradient outside: $\nabla[\rho\ddot\phi - (\lambda+2\mu)\nabla^2\phi] = 0$. The bracketed expression is spatially constant; choosing the constant to be zero (appropriate for localised disturbances) gives $\rho\ddot\phi = (\lambda+2\mu)\nabla^2\phi$, a wave equation with speed $c_L = \sqrt{(\lambda+2\mu)/\rho}$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the kinetic energy density $T = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho|\dot{\mathbf{u}}|^2$ and the strain energy density $W = \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_{ij}\varepsilon_{ij}$ sum to a conserved quantity for plane-wave solutions of the Navier equation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(T + W)$ and use the Navier equation to show it equals a spatial divergence.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = \rho\dot{u}_i\ddot{u}_i = \dot{u}_i\frac{\partial\sigma_{ij}}{\partial x_j}$ (using the Cauchy equation without body forces). $\frac{\partial W}{\partial t} = \sigma_{ij}\dot\varepsilon_{ij} = \sigma_{ij}\frac{\partial\dot{u}_i}{\partial x_j}$ (since $\dot\varepsilon_{ij} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial\dot{u}_i/\partial x_j + \partial\dot{u}_j/\partial x_i)$ and $\sigma$ is symmetric). So $\frac{\partial}{\partial t}(T+W) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j}(\sigma_{ij}\dot{u}_i)$, a divergence. Integrating over all space (or one wavelength for a plane wave), the integral vanishes, confirming energy conservation.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the reflection and transmission coefficients for a longitudinal wave incident normally on a planar interface between two elastic media with impedances $Z_1 = \rho_1 c_{L1}$ and $Z_2 = \rho_2 c_{L2}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Match the displacement and the normal stress at the interface.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write the incident wave as $u_i = A_i e^{i(k_1 x - \omega t)}$, the reflected wave as $u_r = A_r e^{i(-k_1 x - \omega t)}$, and the transmitted wave as $u_t = A_t e^{i(k_2 x - \omega t)}$. Continuity of displacement at $x = 0$: $A_i + A_r = A_t$. Continuity of normal stress $\sigma_{xx} = (\lambda_1 + 2\mu_1)\partial u/\partial x$: $Z_1(A_i - A_r) = Z_2 A_t$. Solving: reflection coefficient $R = A_r/A_i = (Z_2 - Z_1)/(Z_1 + Z_2)$ and transmission coefficient $T = A_t/A_i = 2Z_2/(Z_1 + Z_2)$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the **Christoffel equation** for plane waves in an anisotropic elastic medium: $\Gamma_{ik}p_k = \rho c^2 p_i$ where $\Gamma_{ik} = C_{ijkl}n_j n_l$ is the Christoffel matrix and $n_j$ is the propagation direction unit vector.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute a plane wave $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{p}\,f(\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{x} - ct)$ into the anisotropic Navier equation.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The anisotropic Navier equation is $\rho\ddot{u}_i = C_{ijkl}\partial^2 u_l/\partial x_j\partial x_k$. For $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{p}f(\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{x} - ct)$: $\ddot{u}_i = c^2 p_i f''$ and $\partial^2 u_l/\partial x_j\partial x_k = n_j n_k p_l f''$. Substituting: $\rho c^2 p_i f'' = C_{ijkl}n_j n_k p_l f''$. Defining $\Gamma_{il} = C_{ijkl}n_j n_k$: $\Gamma_{il}p_l = \rho c^2 p_i$. This is an eigenvalue problem: for each propagation direction $\mathbf{n}$, the eigenvalues of $\Gamma$ give the three wave speeds, and the eigenvectors give the polarisation directions. For isotropic media, $\Gamma$ has eigenvalues $\lambda + 2\mu$ (simple, longitudinal) and $\mu$ (double, transverse).
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the strain tensor $\varepsilon_{ij}$ is the symmetric part of the displacement gradient: that is, any antisymmetric contribution to $\partial u_i/\partial x_j$ represents a rigid rotation, not a deformation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Decompose $\partial u_i/\partial x_j = \varepsilon_{ij} + \omega_{ij}$ where $\omega_{ij} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial u_i/\partial x_j - \partial u_j/\partial x_i)$ is the rotation tensor. Show $\omega_{ij}$ corresponds to an infinitesimal rotation.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The displacement gradient decomposes as $\partial u_i/\partial x_j = \varepsilon_{ij} + \omega_{ij}$ where $\varepsilon_{ij} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial u_i/\partial x_j + \partial u_j/\partial x_i)$ is the strain (symmetric) and $\omega_{ij} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial u_i/\partial x_j - \partial u_j/\partial x_i)$ is the rotation (antisymmetric). For a rigid rotation $\mathbf{u} = \boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{x}$ with $|\boldsymbol{\Omega}| \ll 1$: $u_i = \epsilon_{ijk}\Omega_j x_k$, so $\partial u_i/\partial x_k = \epsilon_{ijk}\Omega_j$. The symmetric part vanishes: $\varepsilon_{ik} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\epsilon_{ijk}\Omega_j + \epsilon_{kji}\Omega_j) = 0$ (since $\epsilon_{ijk} = -\epsilon_{kji}$). The antisymmetric part is $\omega_{ik} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\epsilon_{ijk}\Omega_j - \epsilon_{kji}\Omega_j) = \epsilon_{ijk}\Omega_j$, which encodes the rotation. Hence only the symmetric part $\varepsilon$ represents deformation; $\omega$ is pure rotation.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the energy flux vector (the **Umov-Poynting vector** for elastic waves) $\mathbf{S} = -\sigma\cdot\dot{\mathbf{u}}$ and show that $\frac{\partial e}{\partial t} + \nabla\cdot\mathbf{S} = 0$ where $e = T + W$ is the energy density.

Start from the energy balance derived in Exercise 4 and identify the flux term.

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

Mathlib does not contain the Cauchy stress tensor, the strain tensor, the Navier equation, or the theory of elastic waves. The closest infrastructure is the matrix algebra and the analysis of PDEs. Formalising continuum mechanics would require: the definition of stress as a linear map from normals to tractions; Cauchy's theorem (existence of the stress tensor); the strain-displacement relation; Hooke's law as a fourth-order constitutive tensor; and the Navier equation as a second-order PDE for the displacement field. The plane-wave analysis requires spectral theory for the Christoffel matrix. None of this infrastructure exists in Mathlib. `lean_status: none` reflects the gap.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Rayleigh surface waves.** At a free surface of an elastic half-space, the Navier equation admits a solution that decays exponentially with depth and propagates along the surface: the **Rayleigh wave**. Its speed $c_R$ satisfies $c_R < c_T < c_L$ and is the root of a cubic equation in $(c_R/c_T)^2$. For $\nu = 0.25$, $c_R \approx 0.92 c_T$. Rayleigh waves are the most destructive in earthquakes because their energy is confined to the surface.

**Love waves.** In a layered medium (a soft layer over a stiff half-space), horizontally polarised shear waves can be trapped in the layer and propagate as guided modes — **Love waves**. They are dispersive (unlike body waves), with phase speed depending on frequency.

**Stoneley interface waves.** At the interface between two solids, a wave analogous to the Rayleigh wave propagates along the interface: the **Stoneley wave**. It exists only when the elastic contrast between the two media is small enough.

**Synthesis.** The Cauchy stress tensor and the Navier equation constitute the load-bearing structure of linear elastodynamics: the stress tensor encodes the internal forces, Hooke's law provides the constitutive closure, and the Navier equation governs the resulting wave motion. The central insight is that the symmetry of the stress tensor (angular momentum balance) and the linearity of Hooke's law (small-strain assumption) together produce exactly two wave modes with well-defined speeds, and the Helmholtz decomposition provides the tool that separates them. This generalises from isotropic to anisotropic media (the Christoffel equation produces three modes), and the foundational reason the decomposition works is that the elastic stiffness tensor has the same symmetries as the strain tensor — a consequence of energy conservation. The Rayleigh, Love, and Stoneley waves are boundary-guided modes that emerge from the same Navier equation with boundary conditions, and putting these together, seismology reads the Earth's interior structure from the arrival times and polarisations of these waves, making elastic wave theory the backbone of geophysical imaging.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Cauchy's stress theorem).** Let $\mathbf{t}(\hat{n}, \mathbf{x})$ be the traction vector on a surface with outward normal $\hat{n}$ at point $\mathbf{x}$ in a continuum body. If balance of linear momentum holds and $\mathbf{t}$ depends linearly on $\hat{n}$, then there exists a unique second-order tensor field $\sigma(\mathbf{x})$ such that $\mathbf{t} = \sigma\hat{n}$.

*Proof sketch (Cauchy's tetrahedron argument).* Consider a tetrahedron with three faces having outward normals $-\hat{e}_1, -\hat{e}_2, -\hat{e}_3$ and a fourth face with normal $\hat{n}$. Let the areas of the coordinate faces be $\Delta S_i = n_i \Delta S$ where $\Delta S$ is the area of the slant face. Balance of linear momentum on the tetrahedron in the limit as the tetrahedron shrinks to a point (so that volume forces scale as $h^3$ and surface forces as $h^2$) gives $\mathbf{t}(\hat{n}) = n_i \mathbf{t}(\hat{e}_i)$. Defining $\sigma_{ij} = t_j(\hat{e}_i)$ (the $j$-component of the traction on the face with normal $\hat{e}_i$), this reads $t_j = \sigma_{ij}n_i$, i.e., $\mathbf{t} = \sigma^T\hat{n}$. Relabelling (or defining $\sigma$ with the transpose) gives $\mathbf{t} = \sigma\hat{n}$. Uniqueness follows from the linearity in $\hat{n}$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Continuum mechanics field theory [09.07.01]** provides the Lagrangian framework from which the stress tensor and strain tensor are derived as the continuum limit of discrete lattice forces.

- **Field Lagrangians [09.02.06]** give the Lagrangian density for the elastic medium from which the Navier equation is derived by the Euler-Lagrange equations.

- **Ideal fluid mechanics [09.07.03]** is the special case $\mu = 0$ of the Navier-Stokes equations; the stress tensor reduces to $\sigma_{ij} = -p\delta_{ij}$ (isotropic pressure only, no shear).

- **Viscous flow [09.07.04]** adds dissipative terms to the stress tensor, leading from the Navier equation to the Navier-Stokes equations.

- **EM waves in matter [10.04.03]** have an analogous structure: the dielectric tensor plays the role of the stiffness tensor, and the wave equation for $\mathbf{E}$ has the same separation into ordinary and extraordinary modes.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

**Augustin-Louis Cauchy** introduced the stress tensor in 1822 in his memoir *Recherches sur l'equilibre et le mouvement interieur des corps solides ou fluides*, establishing continuum mechanics on a rigorous mathematical foundation. The strain tensor was formalised by **George Green** in 1839. The Navier equation was derived by **Claude-Louis Navier** in 1821 (for one-dimensional rods) and generalised to three dimensions by Cauchy.

The Lame parameters were introduced by **Gabriel Lame** in his *Lecons sur la theorie mathematique de l'elasticite des corps solides* (1852). The distinction between longitudinal and transverse waves was known experimentally from seismology: **R. D. Oldham** identified P-waves and S-waves in seismograms in 1900, and **Love** developed the theory of surface waves in *Some Problems of Geodynamics* (1911).

The Cauchy stress theorem is one of the foundational results of continuum mechanics, establishing that the internal force system in a continuous body is completely described by a second-order tensor field. The theorem assumes that the traction depends continuously on the surface normal; violations of this assumption (as in granular media or materials with microstructure) require generalised stress measures.
