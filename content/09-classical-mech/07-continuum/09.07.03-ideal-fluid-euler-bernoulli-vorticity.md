## Intuition [Beginner]

An **ideal fluid** (also called an inviscid fluid or perfect fluid) is one with no internal friction. Water at high Reynolds number (fast flow past a streamlined object) behaves approximately this way. The key properties are: the fluid is incompressible (constant density), and there is no viscosity (no resistance to shearing motion).

The velocity field $\mathbf{v}(\mathbf{x}, t)$ describes the flow: at each point $\mathbf{x}$ and time $t$, the vector $\mathbf{v}$ tells you the speed and direction of the fluid there. The central equation governing this velocity field is **Euler's equation**: the rate of change of velocity following a fluid parcel equals the pressure force plus gravity (force per unit mass). In symbols, the local acceleration plus the advective acceleration equals minus the pressure gradient divided by density, plus gravity.

**Bernoulli's equation** is the energy conservation statement for steady ideal flow along a streamline: $p + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g z = $ constant. Higher speed means lower pressure — this is why an airplane wing generates lift (air speeds up over the top, pressure drops, net upward force).

**Vorticity** measures the local rotation of the fluid: it is the circulation per unit area, or equivalently the curl of the velocity field. If you drop a tiny paddle wheel into the flow, its spin rate is proportional to the vorticity. A flow with zero vorticity everywhere is **irrotational** and is enormously simpler to analyse.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Water flows through a horizontal pipe that narrows from radius $R_1 = 5$ cm to $R_2 = 2$ cm. The upstream speed is $v_1 = 2$ m/s and the upstream pressure is $p_1 = 200$ kPa. Find the speed and pressure in the narrow section.

By mass conservation (incompressibility), the volume flow rate is constant: $A_1 v_1 = A_2 v_2$. So $\pi R_1^2 v_1 = \pi R_2^2 v_2$, giving $v_2 = v_1(R_1/R_2)^2 = 2 \times (5/2)^2 = 12.5$ m/s.

By Bernoulli's equation along a streamline (horizontal, so $z$ constant): $p_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_1^2 = p_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v_2^2$. With $\rho = 1000$ kg/m$^3$: $p_2 = p_1 + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho(v_1^2 - v_2^2) = 200000 + 500(4 - 156.25) = 200000 - 76125 = 123875$ Pa $\approx 124$ kPa.

The pressure drops by about 76 kPa. This is the **Venturi effect**: faster flow means lower pressure.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**The continuity equation.** Mass conservation for a fluid with density $\rho(\mathbf{x}, t)$ and velocity $\mathbf{v}(\mathbf{x}, t)$ gives

$$\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t} + \nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{v}) = 0.$$

For incompressible flow, $\rho = $ constant, and this reduces to $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$ (the **incompressibility condition**).

**Euler's equation.** Newton's second law for an ideal (inviscid) fluid gives

$$\frac{\partial\mathbf{v}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla p + \mathbf{f},$$

where $\mathbf{f}$ is the body force per unit mass. The term $(\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v}$ is the **advective acceleration** — the acceleration of a fluid parcel due to spatial variations in the velocity field, even in steady flow.

**Bernoulli's equation.** For steady, incompressible, ideal flow along a streamline:

$$p + \frac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho g z = \text{const}.$$

If the flow is also irrotational, the constant is the same on all streamlines.

**Vorticity and circulation.** The **vorticity** is $\boldsymbol{\omega} = \nabla\times\mathbf{v}$. The **circulation** around a closed curve $C$ is $\Gamma = \oint_C \mathbf{v}\cdot d\mathbf{l} = \int_S \boldsymbol{\omega}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$ (by Stokes' theorem).

**Kelvin's circulation theorem.** For an ideal barotropic fluid under conservative body forces, the circulation around any closed material curve is conserved: $d\Gamma/dt = 0$.

**The vorticity equation.** Taking the curl of Euler's equation gives

$$\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{\omega}}{\partial t} = \nabla\times(\mathbf{v}\times\boldsymbol{\omega}) + \text{baroclinic terms}.$$

For a barotropic fluid this simplifies to $\frac{D\boldsymbol{\omega}}{Dt} = (\boldsymbol{\omega}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v}$, which says vorticity is stretched and tilted by the velocity gradient but never created from nothing in the bulk.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Euler's equation from Newton's second law).**

Consider a fluid parcel of volume $\delta V$ at position $\mathbf{x}$. The forces on it are: (1) the pressure force $-p\hat{n}\,\delta S$ on each surface element $\delta S$ with outward normal $\hat{n}$, integrated over the surface to give $-\nabla p\,\delta V$ (by the divergence theorem); and (2) the body force $\rho\mathbf{f}\,\delta V$.

Newton's second law: $\rho\,\delta V\,\frac{D\mathbf{v}}{Dt} = -\nabla p\,\delta V + \rho\mathbf{f}\,\delta V$, where $\frac{D}{Dt} = \frac{\partial}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla)$ is the **material derivative** (rate of change following the fluid parcel). Dividing by $\delta V$:

$$\rho\frac{D\mathbf{v}}{Dt} = -\nabla p + \rho\mathbf{f}.$$

Expanding the material derivative:

$$\rho\left(\frac{\partial\mathbf{v}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v}\right) = -\nabla p + \rho\mathbf{f}.$$

This is Euler's equation. For incompressible flow, $\rho$ is constant and the continuity equation gives $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$.

**Bernoulli's equation from Euler's equation.** For steady flow ($\partial\mathbf{v}/\partial t = 0$), rewrite Euler's equation using the vector identity $(\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v} = \tfrac{1}{2}\nabla v^2 - \mathbf{v}\times\boldsymbol{\omega}$:

$$\frac{1}{2}\nabla v^2 - \mathbf{v}\times\boldsymbol{\omega} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla p - \nabla\Phi,$$

where $\Phi = gz$ is the gravitational potential. Taking the dot product with $d\mathbf{l}$ along a streamline (where $d\mathbf{l} \parallel \mathbf{v}$, so $(\mathbf{v}\times\boldsymbol{\omega})\cdot d\mathbf{l} = 0$):

$$d\!\left(\frac{1}{2}v^2 + \frac{p}{\rho} + \Phi\right) = 0,$$

which integrates to $p + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho v^2 + \rho gz = $ constant along a streamline.

**Bridge.** The Euler equations are the infinite-dimensional Lie-Poisson equations on the dual of the Lie algebra of divergence-free vector fields, generalising the rigid-body Euler equations on $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ [09.04.08] to an infinite-dimensional setting. The foundational reason this structure appears is that the configuration space of an ideal fluid is the group of volume-preserving diffeomorphisms $\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}(\Omega)$, and the passage from $T^*\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}$ to $\mathfrak{X}_{\mathrm{div}}(\Omega)^*$ is the same Lie-Poisson reduction that gives the rigid-body equations from $T^*\mathrm{SO}(3)$. The central insight is that the helicity $\int \mathbf{v}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega}\,dV$ is the Casimir function of this Lie-Poisson bracket, constraining the flow exactly as the squared angular momentum constrains the rigid body. This is dual to the Hamiltonian structure of the rigid body, and putting these together, the ideal fluid is the canonical example of an infinite-dimensional Hamiltonian system.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that for the velocity field $\mathbf{v} = \boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{r}$ (rigid-body rotation), the vorticity is $\boldsymbol{\omega} = 2\boldsymbol{\Omega}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\nabla\times(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{r})$ using the vector identity $\nabla\times(\mathbf{A}\times\mathbf{B}) = \mathbf{A}(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B}) - \mathbf{B}(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{A}) + (\mathbf{B}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{A} - (\mathbf{A}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{B}$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\nabla\times(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{r}) = \boldsymbol{\Omega}(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{r}) - \mathbf{r}(\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\Omega}) + (\mathbf{r}\cdot\nabla)\boldsymbol{\Omega} - (\boldsymbol{\Omega}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{r}$. Since $\boldsymbol{\Omega}$ is constant: $\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\Omega} = 0$ and $(\mathbf{r}\cdot\nabla)\boldsymbol{\Omega} = 0$. Also $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{r} = 3$ and $(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{r} = \boldsymbol{\Omega}$. So $\nabla\times(\boldsymbol{\Omega}\times\mathbf{r}) = 3\boldsymbol{\Omega} - \boldsymbol{\Omega} = 2\boldsymbol{\Omega}$. The vorticity is twice the angular velocity, confirming that vorticity measures local rotation rate.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the vorticity equation from Euler's equation by taking the curl.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the vector identity $\nabla\times(\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v} = \nabla\times(\tfrac{1}{2}\nabla v^2 - \mathbf{v}\times\boldsymbol{\omega}) = -\nabla\times(\mathbf{v}\times\boldsymbol{\omega})$ since the curl of a gradient vanishes.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Taking the curl of Euler's equation: $\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{\omega}}{\partial t} + \nabla\times(\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla\times\nabla p + \nabla\times\mathbf{f}$. Using the identity $(\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v} = \tfrac{1}{2}\nabla v^2 - \mathbf{v}\times\boldsymbol{\omega}$ and $\nabla\times\nabla p = 0$: $\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{\omega}}{\partial t} - \nabla\times(\mathbf{v}\times\boldsymbol{\omega}) = 0$ (for conservative body forces). Expanding: $\frac{D\boldsymbol{\omega}}{Dt} = (\boldsymbol{\omega}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v} - \boldsymbol{\omega}(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v})$. For incompressible flow ($\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$): $\frac{D\boldsymbol{\omega}}{Dt} = (\boldsymbol{\omega}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v}$. This is the **vorticity transport equation**: vorticity is stretched and tilted by the velocity gradient but not created or destroyed.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove Kelvin's circulation theorem: for an ideal barotropic fluid under conservative forces, $\frac{d\Gamma}{dt} = 0$ where $\Gamma = \oint_C \mathbf{v}\cdot d\mathbf{l}$ and $C$ is a material curve.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate the line integral with respect to time, using the fact that the curve moves with the fluid. Use Euler's equation to replace the acceleration.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\frac{d\Gamma}{dt} = \oint_C \frac{D\mathbf{v}}{Dt}\cdot d\mathbf{l} + \oint_C \mathbf{v}\cdot\frac{D(d\mathbf{l})}{Dt}$. The second term: $\frac{D(d\mathbf{l})}{Dt} = (d\mathbf{l}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v}$, so $\oint_C \mathbf{v}\cdot(d\mathbf{l}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v} = \oint_C d(\tfrac{1}{2}v^2) = 0$ (exact differential over a closed curve). For the first term: $\oint_C \frac{D\mathbf{v}}{Dt}\cdot d\mathbf{l} = -\oint_C\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla p\cdot d\mathbf{l} - \oint_C\nabla\Phi\cdot d\mathbf{l}$. For a barotropic fluid, $dp/\rho$ is an exact differential, so $\oint dp/\rho = 0$. And $\oint d\Phi = 0$. Hence $d\Gamma/dt = 0$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the helicity $H = \int_V \mathbf{v}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega}\,dV$ is a topological invariant for an ideal fluid: it measures the linkage of vortex lines.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Vortex lines are integral curves of the vorticity field. The helicity equals the Gauss linking integral over pairs of vortex tubes. Show $dH/dt = 0$ using the vorticity equation.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\frac{dH}{dt} = \int_V \frac{D\mathbf{v}}{Dt}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega} + \mathbf{v}\cdot\frac{D\boldsymbol{\omega}}{Dt}\,dV$. Using the Euler equation for $D\mathbf{v}/Dt$ and the vorticity equation $D\boldsymbol{\omega}/Dt = (\boldsymbol{\omega}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v}$: $\frac{dH}{dt} = \int_V (-\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla p - \nabla\Phi)\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega} + \mathbf{v}\cdot(\boldsymbol{\omega}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v}\,dV$. The first terms: $\int\nabla\Phi\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega}\,dV = \int\nabla\cdot(\Phi\boldsymbol{\omega})\,dV = 0$ (divergence theorem, boundary terms vanish). Similarly for $\nabla p/\rho$ (barotropic). The last term: $\mathbf{v}\cdot(\boldsymbol{\omega}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v} = \tfrac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{\omega}\cdot\nabla v^2$. Integrating: $\int\tfrac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{\omega}\cdot\nabla v^2\,dV = \tfrac{1}{2}\int\nabla\cdot(v^2\boldsymbol{\omega})\,dV = 0$. Hence $dH/dt = 0$. Helicity is the Casimir of the Lie-Poisson bracket on $\mathfrak{X}_{\rm div}(\Omega)^*$ and measures the topological linking number of vortex tubes (Moffatt 1969).
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that d'Alembert's paradox follows from potential flow theory: a body moving at constant speed through an ideal fluid experiences zero drag.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The drag force is $F_x = -\oint p\,n_x\,dS$. For potential flow $\mathbf{v} = \nabla\phi$ with $\nabla^2\phi = 0$, use Bernoulli's equation to express $p$ and the far-field boundary conditions to evaluate the integral.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For uniform flow $\mathbf{U}$ past a body, the potential satisfies $\nabla^2\phi = 0$ with $\nabla\phi \to \mathbf{U}$ at infinity and $\nabla\phi\cdot\hat{n} = 0$ on the body. Bernoulli gives $p = p_\infty + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho(U^2 - v^2)$. The drag: $F_x = -\oint p\,n_x\,dS = -p_\infty\oint n_x\,dS - \tfrac{1}{2}\rho\oint(U^2 - v^2)n_x\,dS$. The first integral vanishes (divergence theorem on a closed surface). For the second, note that for the external potential flow around a finite body in 3D, the far-field perturbation decays as $1/r^2$ and the pressure perturbation as $1/r^4$. The integral $\oint v^2 n_x\,dS$ over a large sphere at distance $R$ scales as $R^2 \cdot 1/R^4 \cdot R^2 = 1/R^2 \to 0$, while the uniform-flow contribution $\oint U^2 n_x\,dS = 0$. Hence $F_x = 0$: zero drag. This contradicts experiment and is resolved by viscosity (boundary-layer separation produces a wake).
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify that the velocity potential $\phi = Ux(1 + a^3/(2r^3))$ (where $r = |\mathbf{x}|$) satisfies the boundary conditions for potential flow past a sphere of radius $a$ in a uniform stream $U$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Check: (1) $\nabla^2\phi = 0$ for $r > a$, (2) $\nabla\phi\cdot\hat{n} = 0$ on $r = a$, (3) $\nabla\phi \to U\hat{x}$ as $r \to \infty$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(3) As $r \to \infty$: $\phi \to Ux$, so $\mathbf{v} \to U\hat{x}$. All conditions are satisfied. This is the classic potential-flow solution for a sphere. The surface speed is $v_\theta = -\tfrac{3}{2}U\sin\theta$, maximum at the equator. Bernoulli gives the pressure distribution: $p = p_\infty + \tfrac{1}{2}\rho U^2(1 - \tfrac{9}{4}\sin^2\theta)$, symmetric front-to-back (zero drag).
</details>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Euler equations for an ideal fluid in a domain $\Omega$ are the Lie-Poisson equations on the dual $\mathfrak{X}_{\rm div}(\Omega)^*$ of the Lie algebra of divergence-free vector fields, with the Poisson bracket $\{F, G\}(\mathbf{v}) = \int_\Omega \mathbf{v}\cdot[\mathbf{u}_F, \mathbf{u}_G]\,dV$ where $\mathbf{u}_F$ is the functional derivative of $F$ and $[\cdot, \cdot]$ is the Lie bracket of vector fields.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Lie bracket of vector fields is $[\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w}]^i = u^j\partial_j w^i - w^j\partial_j u^i$. The Hamiltonian is $H(\mathbf{v}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\int |\mathbf{v}|^2\,dV$. Compute $\dot{F} = \{F, H\}$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $H = \tfrac{1}{2}\int |\mathbf{v}|^2\,dV$, the functional derivative $\delta H/\delta\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}$. For a general functional $F$, $\dot{F} = \{F, H\} = \int \mathbf{v}\cdot[\delta F/\delta\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v}]\,dV = \int v_i\left(\frac{\delta F}{\delta v_j}\partial_j v_i - v_j\partial_j\frac{\delta F}{\delta v_i}\right)dV$. Integration by parts (using $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$ and boundary conditions): $\dot{F} = -\int \frac{\delta F}{\delta v_i}((\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla)v_i + \nabla_i p/\rho)\,dV$. Since this must hold for all $F$, the Euler equation follows: $\partial_t v_i + v_j\partial_j v_i = -\partial_i p/\rho$. The pressure enters as the Lagrange multiplier enforcing the incompressibility constraint $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$.
</details>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

Mathlib has no fluid mechanics. The divergence, curl, and gradient are available for $\mathbb{R}^n$, and there is some PDE infrastructure, but the Euler equations, the vorticity transport equation, Kelvin's theorem, and the Lie-Poisson structure of ideal fluid flow are entirely absent. The Sobolev-space well-posedness theory for the Euler equations (which guarantees local-in-time existence of solutions) is not formalised. The `lean_status: none` reflects the gap.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Arnold's geometric interpretation.** Arnold (1966) showed that the Euler equations for an ideal fluid are the geodesic equations on the group $\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}(\Omega)$ of volume-preserving diffeomorphisms, equipped with the right-invariant metric $\langle u, w\rangle = \int_\Omega u\cdot w\,dV$. The velocity field $v(t)$ is the tangent vector to the geodesic. The curvature of this metric (computed by Misiolek 1998) determines the stability of the flow: positive curvature implies conjugate points and potential instability.

**Helicity and knot invariants.** The helicity $H = \int \mathbf{v}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega}\,dV$ is the integral of the asymptotic linking number of the vortex lines (Arnold 1974). For a flow confined to a knot complement, the helicity reduces to the Gauss linking integral over pairs of vortex lines, and its conservation is the topological statement that linked vortex tubes cannot be unlinked by an ideal flow.

**The Euler equations as a Hamiltonian system.** The Lie-Poisson structure on $\mathfrak{X}_{\mathrm{div}}(\Omega)^*$ has Casimir functions that include the helicity and the enstrophy $\int |\boldsymbol{\omega}|^2\,dV$ (in 2D). The coadjoint orbits are isovortical surfaces — level sets of the vorticity transported by the flow. The geometry of these orbits determines the stability properties of the flow (Arnold's stability theorem).

**Synthesis.** The ideal fluid is the paradigmatic infinite-dimensional Hamiltonian system, and the Euler equations are the Lie-Poisson equations on the dual of the Lie algebra of divergence-free vector fields — the infinite-dimensional counterpart of the rigid-body Euler equations on $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ [09.04.08]. The foundational reason this structure appears is that the configuration space $\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}(\Omega)$ is a Lie group, and the kinetic energy defines a right-invariant Riemannian metric whose geodesics are the Euler flows. The central insight is that the Casimir functions of the Lie-Poisson bracket (helicity in 3D, enstrophy in 2D) constrain the dynamics exactly as the angular-momentum Casimir constrains the rigid body, and the Arnold stability theorem for fluid flows is the exact analogue of the energy-Casimir stability method for the rigid body.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Kelvin's circulation theorem).** For an ideal incompressible fluid with conservative body forces, the circulation $\Gamma(t) = \oint_{C(t)} \mathbf{v}\cdot d\mathbf{l}$ around any material curve $C(t)$ is constant: $d\Gamma/dt = 0$.

*Proof.* The material curve $C(t)$ is the image of an initial curve $C_0$ under the flow map $\phi_t$. Parametrise $C_0$ by $s \in [0, 1]$, so $C(t)$ is parametrised by $\mathbf{x}(s, t) = \phi_t(\mathbf{x}_0(s))$ with $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{v}(\mathbf{x}, t)$.

$$\frac{d\Gamma}{dt} = \oint_{C(t)} \frac{D\mathbf{v}}{Dt}\cdot d\mathbf{l} + \oint_{C(t)} \mathbf{v}\cdot d\mathbf{v}.$$

The second integral is $\oint d(\tfrac{1}{2}v^2) = 0$ (exact differential over a closed curve). For the first, substitute Euler's equation: $D\mathbf{v}/Dt = -\nabla p/\rho - \nabla\Phi$. Then $\oint \nabla(p/\rho + \Phi)\cdot d\mathbf{l} = 0$ (exact differentials over a closed curve). Hence $d\Gamma/dt = 0$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Rigid body dynamics [09.04.08]** provides the finite-dimensional prototype: the Euler equations on $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ generalise to the Euler fluid equations on $\mathfrak{X}_{\rm div}(\Omega)^*$ via Lie-Poisson reduction.

- **Elastic waves [09.07.02]** are the solid-mechanics counterpart; the Cauchy stress tensor for fluids reduces to the isotropic pressure $\sigma_{ij} = -p\delta_{ij}$.

- **Viscous flow [09.07.04]** adds the Navier-Stokes viscous term to the Euler equations, fundamentally changing the mathematical structure from Hamiltonian to dissipative.

- **KAM theorem [09.08.01]** governs the persistence of coherent structures in nearly-integrable fluid flows; the Euler equations on a torus provide the canonical example.

- **Lie-Poisson reduction [09.04.08]** is the general framework; the ideal fluid is the infinite-dimensional application.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Euler published his fluid equations in 1757 in *Principes generaux du mouvement des fluides*. Bernoulli's equation appeared in Daniel Bernoulli's *Hydrodynamica* (1738), though the modern derivation from Euler's equation is due to Euler himself. Kelvin (William Thomson) stated the circulation theorem in 1869.

Arnold's 1966 paper *Sur la geometrie differentielle des groupes de Lie de dimension infinie et ses applications a l'hydrodynamique des fluides parfaits* was the founding document of geometric hydrodynamics, establishing the Lie-Poisson structure of the Euler equations and the geodesic interpretation on $\mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}(\Omega)$. The helicity invariant was identified by Moffatt (1969) as a measure of vortex-line linkage.

D'Alembert's paradox (zero drag on a body in potential flow, stated 1752) was historically important as the first indication that viscosity plays an essential role in real fluid flows. Its resolution by Prandtl (1904) via boundary-layer theory launched modern fluid dynamics.
