## Intuition [Beginner]

A canonical transformation is a change of coordinates in phase space that preserves the structure of Hamilton's equations. Not every change of variables does this — only those that respect the special geometry of phase space.

For linear changes of variables, the ones that work form a group: the **symplectic group** $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$. An element of this group is a $2n \times 2n$ matrix $M$ that satisfies $M^TJM = J$, where $J$ is the standard symplectic matrix

$$J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & I_n \\ -I_n & 0 \end{pmatrix}.$$

The condition $M^TJM = J$ says that $M$ preserves the symplectic form — the pairing between position and momentum that makes Hamilton's equations work. Think of $J$ as a "pairing machine" that takes two phase-space vectors and returns a number (their symplectic product). A symplectic matrix $M$ is one for which this pairing gives the same answer before and after the transformation.

For a single degree of freedom ($n = 1$), $\mathrm{Sp}(2, \mathbb{R})$ consists of $2 \times 2$ matrices with determinant 1 — exactly $\mathrm{SL}(2, \mathbb{R})$. These are the area-preserving linear maps of the plane, and area preservation is Liouville's theorem at the linear level. For $n > 1$, $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ is a proper subgroup of $\mathrm{SL}(2n, \mathbb{R})$: symplectic maps preserve not just volume but the specific position-momentum pairing structure.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Check whether the matrix $M = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix}$ is symplectic.

Compute $M^TJM = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1/2 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1/2 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.$

This is not equal to $J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, so $M$ is not symplectic. The matrix stretches the $q$-direction by 2 and compresses the $p$-direction by $1/2$, which preserves area ($\det M = 1$) but scrambles the $q$-$p$ pairing.

Now try $M' = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1/2 \end{pmatrix}$. Wait — that is the same matrix. Try instead $N = \begin{pmatrix} a & 0 \\ 0 & 1/a \end{pmatrix}$. Computing $N^TJN$: we get $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1/a \\ -a & 0 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} a & 0 \\ 0 & 1/a \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1/a^2 \\ -a^2 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$. This equals $J$ only when $a^2 = 1$, i.e., $a = \pm 1$. So scaling $q$ and inversely scaling $p$ is NOT symplectic unless $a = 1$ — despite preserving area. Symplecticity is strictly stronger than area preservation.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $V$ be a $2n$-dimensional real vector space equipped with a symplectic form $\omega$ (a non-degenerate, closed 2-form). In the standard basis, $\omega$ is represented by the matrix $J = \begin{pmatrix}0 & I_n \\ -I_n & 0\end{pmatrix}$.

**Definition.** A linear map $M : V \to V$ is **symplectic** if $\omega(Mu, Mv) = \omega(u, v)$ for all $u, v \in V$. In matrix notation: $M^TJM = J$.

The **symplectic group** $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ is the group of all symplectic $2n \times 2n$ matrices under matrix multiplication.

**Block structure.** Writing $M = \begin{pmatrix}A & B \\ C & D\end{pmatrix}$ with $n \times n$ blocks, the condition $M^TJM = J$ is equivalent to:

$$A^TC = C^TA, \quad B^TD = D^TB, \quad A^TD - C^TB = I_n.$$

These are the **symplectic conditions** on the blocks. For $n = 1$ they reduce to $\det M = ad - bc = 1$.

**Eigenvalue structure.** If $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $M \in \mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$, then so are $\bar\lambda$, $1/\lambda$, and $1/\bar\lambda$. The eigenvalues come in quartets $\{\lambda, \bar\lambda, 1/\lambda, 1/\bar\lambda\}$, with special cases for real eigenvalues (pairs $\lambda, 1/\lambda$) and unit-modulus eigenvalues (pairs $e^{i\theta}, e^{-i\theta}$).

**The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$.** The tangent space at the identity consists of matrices $X$ satisfying $JX + X^TJ = 0$, or equivalently $JX$ is symmetric. Writing $X = \begin{pmatrix}a & b \\ c & d\end{pmatrix}$, the condition gives $b = b^T$, $c = c^T$, $d = -a^T$. The dimension of $\mathfrak{sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ is $n(2n+1)$.

**Williamson's theorem.** For any positive-definite symmetric $2n \times 2n$ matrix $S$, there exists $M \in \mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ such that $M^TSM = \mathrm{diag}(\Lambda, \Lambda)$ where $\Lambda = \mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$ with $\lambda_i > 0$ the **symplectic eigenvalues** of $S$. This is the symplectic normal form and underlies the definition of the covariance matrix of a Gaussian quantum state.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (The symplectic eigenvalue structure).**

Let $M \in \mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ with $M^TJM = J$. Then $M^{-1} = -JM^TJ$ (from rearranging the symplectic condition). If $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $M$ with eigenvector $v$, then $Mv = \lambda v$.

Consider the transpose equation. Since $M^{-1} = -JM^TJ$, we have $JM^{-T}J = -M^T$... wait. From $M^TJM = J$ we get $M^{-1} = J^{-1}M^{-T}J^{-T}J = -JM^TJ$ (using $J^{-1} = -J$). So $M^{-1} = -JM^TJ$.

If $Mv = \lambda v$, then $M^{-1}v = \lambda^{-1}v$. But $M^{-1} = -JM^TJ$, so $-JM^TJv = \lambda^{-1}v$, i.e., $M^T(Jv) = -\lambda^{-1}(Jv)$. This means $-\lambda^{-1}$ is an eigenvalue of $M^T$. Since $M$ and $M^T$ have the same eigenvalues, $-\lambda^{-1}$ is an eigenvalue of $M$. Similarly, taking complex conjugates, $\bar\lambda$ and $-\bar\lambda^{-1}$ are eigenvalues.

For the standard symplectic form (the one used throughout physics), the eigenvalue symmetry is $\lambda \to \bar\lambda$ (complex conjugation) and $\lambda \to 1/\lambda$ (inversion), plus the pairing of eigenvalues in the quartets described above.

**Bridge.** The eigenvalue structure of $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ builds toward the stability analysis of periodic orbits in Hamiltonian systems [09.08.02], where the linearised Poincare map is symplectic and its eigenvalues determine whether nearby trajectories remain bounded (elliptic case, $|lambda| = 1$) or diverge (hyperbolic case, $|\lambda| \neq 1$). The central insight is that the symplectic constraint on the eigenvalues restricts the possible bifurcation scenarios for Hamiltonian systems much more tightly than for general ODEs: Hamiltonian period-doubling bifurcations, for example, require eigenvalue quartets on the unit circle to collide at $\pm 1$ and then leave the circle in reciprocal pairs. This is dual to the constraint that Hamiltonian flows preserve the symplectic form, and putting these together gives the KAM persistence theorem [09.08.01] its specific structure — the Diophantine condition on frequency vectors is the resonance-avoidance condition that prevents eigenvalue collisions.

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

Mathlib defines the symplectic group in `Mathlib.LinearAlgebra.SymplecticGroup` as the set of matrices $M$ satisfying $M^TJM = J$ for the standard $J$. It proves basic properties including $\det M = 1$ and the group structure. The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ is partially developed. What is missing: Williamson's theorem and symplectic eigenvalues; the Maslov index and the topology of $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ (specifically that $\pi_1(\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ for $n \geq 1$); the Lagrangian Grassmannian as a homogeneous space; and the classification of linear symplectic maps into elliptic, hyperbolic, and loxodromic types. The `lean_status: none` reflects these gaps.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Topology of $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$.** The symplectic group has the homotopy type of $\mathrm{U}(n)$ via the deformation retract $M \mapsto (MM^T)^{-1/2}M$ (polar decomposition). In particular, $\pi_1(\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ for $n \geq 1$, generated by the loop $t \mapsto \begin{pmatrix}\cos t & \sin t \\ -\sin t & \cos t\end{pmatrix}$ in $\mathrm{Sp}(2, \mathbb{R})$. The fundamental group being $\mathbb{Z}$ is what makes the Maslov index well-defined: it counts how many times a path in $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ wraps around the generator.

**The Lagrangian Grassmannian.** The space $\Lambda(n)$ of Lagrangian subspaces of $(\mathbb{R}^{2n}, \omega)$ is a compact manifold of dimension $n(n+1)/2$, diffeomorphic to $\mathrm{U}(n)/\mathrm{O}(n)$. The Maslov index is an element of $H^1(\Lambda(n), \mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, the universal class that generates the cohomology. It appears in the WKB phase correction (Maslov 1965) and in the definition of the Conley-Zehnder index for periodic orbits in symplectic topology.

**Williamson's theorem and normal forms.** Every quadratic Hamiltonian $H = \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{z}^TS\mathbf{z}$ can be brought into normal form by a symplectic linear transformation. Williamson's theorem (1936) classifies the normal forms into three types: elliptic ($\lambda_i$ purely imaginary), hyperbolic ($\lambda_i$ real), and loxodromic ($\lambda_i$ complex with neither purely real nor purely imaginary). The normal form of an elliptic Hamiltonian is $H = \sum \tfrac{\omega_i}{2}(q_i^2 + p_i^2)$, a sum of harmonic oscillators — the linearised flow near a stable equilibrium.

**Synthesis.** The symplectic group $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ is the linear shadow of the symplectomorphism group $\mathrm{Symp}(M, \omega)$: just as symplectic matrices preserve the linear symplectic form on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$, symplectomorphisms preserve the nonlinear symplectic form on a manifold. The central insight is that the algebraic structure of $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ — its eigenvalue constraints, its topology, its Lie algebra — constrains the local behaviour of Hamiltonian flows exactly as the global structure of $\mathrm{Symp}(M, \omega)$ constrains their global behaviour. The generalises from linear canonical transformations to nonlinear ones via the Darboux theorem [09.04.05], and the symplectic eigenvalues of Williamson's theorem are the linear precursors of the action variables of the Liouville-Arnold theorem [09.06.02].

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Det M = 1 for symplectic matrices).** Every $M \in \mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ satisfies $\det M = 1$.

*Proof.* From $M^TJM = J$, take determinants: $\det(M^T)\det(J)\det(M) = \det(J)$, so $(\det M)^2 \det J = \det J$. Since $\det J = 1$ (computable directly or by row/column expansion), $(\det M)^2 = 1$, giving $\det M = \pm 1$.

The identity matrix is symplectic with determinant $+1$. The map $M \mapsto \det M$ is continuous on $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ and takes only values $\pm 1$. Since $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ is connected (it is the image of the connected set $\mathfrak{sp}(2n, \mathbb{R}) \times \mathbb{R}$ under the exponential map and matrix multiplication), $\det M = +1$ for all $M$. Alternatively, the polar decomposition $M = PO$ with $P$ positive-definite symplectic and $O \in \mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R}) \cap \mathrm{O}(2n)$ shows $\det M = \det O$. But $O \in \mathrm{U}(n) \subset \mathrm{O}(2n)$ (via the complex structure $J$), so $\det O = |\det U|^2 = 1$ for $U \in \mathrm{U}(n)$. $\square$

**Proposition (Exponential map lands in Sp).** For any $X \in \mathfrak{sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ (i.e., $JX + X^TJ = 0$), $\exp(X) \in \mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$.

*Proof.* Define $M(t) = \exp(tX)$. Then $\frac{d}{dt}(M^TJM) = M^T X^T JM + M^T JXM = M^T(X^TJ + JX)M = 0$ using the Lie algebra condition. So $M^TJM$ is constant in $t$. At $t = 0$, $M(0) = I$ and $I^TJI = J$. Hence $M(t)^TJM(t) = J$ for all $t$. Setting $t = 1$: $\exp(X)^TJ\exp(X) = J$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Symplectic structure [09.04.05]** introduces the symplectic form $\omega$ and Darboux's theorem; $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ is the group of linear transformations preserving the coordinate expression of $\omega$.

- **Canonical transformations [09.05.01]** are the nonlinear generalisation of symplectic matrices; linear canonical transformations are exactly the elements of $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$.

- **Liouville's theorem [09.04.04]** (phase-space volume preservation) follows from $\det M = 1$ at the linear level and from $\phi_t^*\omega^n = \omega^n$ at the nonlinear level.

- **Normal modes [09.02.04]** are found by symplectically diagonalising the quadratic Hamiltonian; Williamson's theorem is the tool that guarantees this is always possible.

- **KAM theorem [09.08.01]** requires the linearised Poincare map to be symplectic; the eigenvalue constraints of $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})$ restrict the possible bifurcations that can occur near an invariant torus.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The symplectic group was first studied systematically by **Hermann Weyl** in *The Classical Groups* (1939), though the underlying structure (linear canonical transformations) had been used in celestial mechanics since the 19th century. The name "symplectic" is due to Weyl, who replaced the Greek root "complex" with the cognate "symplectic" to avoid confusion with complex numbers.

**John Williamson** (1936, 1937, 1939) proved the normal-form theorem for quadratic Hamiltonians under symplectic conjugation in a series of papers that remained relatively unknown until their rediscovery by the symplectic topology community in the 1980s. The theorem is now central to both classical mechanics (linearised stability analysis) and quantum optics (Gaussian states and their symplectic invariants).

The Maslov index was introduced by **Victor Maslov** in 1965 and given its rigorous cohomological interpretation by **V. I. Arnold** (1967). The topological result $\pi_1(\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R})) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ is a consequence of the polar decomposition $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R}) \simeq \mathrm{U}(n)$ and was known classically.
