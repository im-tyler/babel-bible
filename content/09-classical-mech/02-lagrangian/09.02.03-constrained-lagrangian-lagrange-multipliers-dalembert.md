## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine a bead threaded on a stiff wire bent into a hoop. The bead can slide along the wire but cannot leave it. The wire is a **constraint** — it restricts the bead's possible positions to a one-dimensional curve in space.

In the Lagrangian framework, you normally handle constraints by choosing coordinates that automatically satisfy them. For the bead on the hoop, you use the angle along the hoop as your single coordinate, and the constraint is built in.

But sometimes you want to know the **constraint force** — how hard the wire pushes on the bead. Or the constraint might be too complicated to eliminate by a coordinate choice. This is where **Lagrange multipliers** come in.

The idea: instead of eliminating the constraint, keep all the coordinates but add a new unknown variable (the multiplier) for each constraint. The multiplier appears in the equations of motion exactly where the constraint force would appear. Solving the extended equations gives you both the motion and the constraint force simultaneously.

The multiplier has a direct physical meaning. For a constraint $f(q) = 0$, the constraint force is proportional to the gradient of $f$ — it pushes in the direction perpendicular to the constraint surface. The multiplier is the proportionality constant, and it adjusts dynamically so that the constraint is always satisfied.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A bead of mass $m$ slides on a vertical circular wire of radius $R$. Use a Lagrange multiplier to find the equation of motion and the constraint force.

The bead has two coordinates $(x, y)$ but is constrained to the circle $x^2 + y^2 = R^2$. Using the angle $\theta$ from the bottom, the position is $(R\sin\theta, -R\cos\theta)$ and the Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}mR^2\dot\theta^2 + mgR\cos\theta$.

The Euler-Lagrange equation gives $mR^2\ddot\theta = -mgR\sin\theta$, or $\ddot\theta = -(g/R)\sin\theta$. This is a pendulum equation.

To find the constraint force (the normal force of the wire on the bead), keep Cartesian coordinates and add a multiplier. The Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2) - mgy$ with constraint $f = x^2 + y^2 - R^2 = 0$.

The extended equations are $m\ddot{x} = 2\lambda x$ and $m\ddot{y} = -mg + 2\lambda y$. At the bottom of the hoop ($\theta = 0$, $x = 0$, $y = -R$, $\dot\theta = 0$): the radial equation gives $2\lambda(-R) = -mg + m v^2/R$ (centripetal acceleration), so the constraint force is $N = 2\lambda R = mg - mv^2/R$. For the bead at rest, $N = mg$ — the wire supports the full weight.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Extended Euler-Lagrange equations with Lagrange multipliers.** For a system with Lagrangian $L(q, \dot{q}, t)$ and holonomic constraints $f_\alpha(q, t) = 0$ ($\alpha = 1, \ldots, k$), the equations of motion are

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot{q}^i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} \;=\; \sum_{\alpha=1}^k \lambda_\alpha \frac{\partial f_\alpha}{\partial q^i}, \qquad i = 1, \ldots, n,
$$

together with the constraint equations

$$
f_\alpha(q, t) \;=\; 0, \qquad \alpha = 1, \ldots, k.
$$

This gives $n + k$ equations for the $n + k$ unknowns $(q^1, \ldots, q^n, \lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k)$.

The **generalised constraint force** is $Q_i^{(c)} = \sum_\alpha \lambda_\alpha \partial f_\alpha/\partial q^i$. The time derivative of the constraint yields $\sum_i (\partial f_\alpha/\partial q^i)\dot{q}^i + \partial f_\alpha/\partial t = 0$, which is used to determine the multipliers.

**Variational derivation.** The extended equations arise from the modified variational principle: extremise $\int L\,dt$ subject to $f_\alpha = 0$ using Lagrange multipliers. The augmented action is

$$
S \;=\; \int_0^T \left(L(q, \dot{q}, t) - \sum_{\alpha=1}^k \lambda_\alpha(t)\, f_\alpha(q, t)\right) dt,
$$

where the $\lambda_\alpha(t)$ are now functions of time (a multiplier for each instant). Varying $q^i$ and $\lambda_\alpha$ independently gives the extended Euler-Lagrange equations and the constraint equations.

**Relation to D'Alembert's principle.** The extended equations are equivalent to D'Alembert's principle $\sum_i (Q_i - [d/dt(\partial L/\partial\dot{q}^i) - \partial L/\partial q^i])\delta q^i = 0$ for all virtual displacements satisfying $\sum_i (\partial f_\alpha/\partial q^i)\delta q^i = 0$. The multipliers enforce this orthogonality condition between the "unbalanced force" and the space of virtual displacements.

### Constrained variation formula

For $k$ holonomic constraints on an $n$-dimensional system, the number of degrees of freedom is $n - k$. One can either: (a) solve the constraints for $k$ coordinates in terms of $n - k$ independent ones and use the standard Euler-Lagrange equations; or (b) keep all $n$ coordinates and introduce $k$ Lagrange multipliers. Method (a) eliminates the constraint forces; method (b) retains them. Both give the same dynamics; method (b) additionally computes the constraint forces.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Key result (Constraint force from Lagrange multipliers).** *For a particle of mass $m$ moving in a potential $V(\mathbf{r})$ subject to the holonomic constraint $f(\mathbf{r}) = 0$, the constraint force is $\mathbf{F}^{(c)} = \lambda \nabla f$, and the multiplier satisfies*

$$
\lambda \;=\; \frac{-m(\dot{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\nabla)^2 f + \nabla V \cdot\nabla f / m \cdot |\nabla f|^2}{|\nabla f|^2},
$$

*evaluated on the constraint surface.*

**Proof.** The extended equations in Cartesian coordinates are $m\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = -\nabla V + \lambda\nabla f$. The constraint $f = 0$ implies $\dot{f} = \nabla f \cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}} = 0$ and $\ddot{f} = \nabla f \cdot \ddot{\mathbf{r}} + (\dot{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\nabla)^2 f = 0$. Substituting $\ddot{\mathbf{r}}$:

$$
\frac{1}{m}\nabla f \cdot (-\nabla V + \lambda\nabla f) + (\dot{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\nabla)^2 f \;=\; 0.
$$

Solving for $\lambda$:

$$
\lambda \;=\; \frac{m(\dot{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\nabla)^2 f - \nabla V\cdot\nabla f}{|\nabla f|^2}.
$$

The first term in the numerator is the centripetal contribution from the curvature of the constraint surface; the second is the projection of the applied force onto the normal. $\square$

**Worked example: bead on a wire hoop.** A bead of mass $m$ slides on a circular wire of radius $R$ in a vertical plane, with constraint $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2 - R^2 = 0$. The constraint gradient is $\nabla f = (2x, 2y)$, so the constraint force is radial: $\mathbf{F}^{(c)} = 2\lambda(x, y)$.

At the top of the hoop ($x = 0$, $y = R$), if the bead has speed $v$: $(\dot{\mathbf{r}}\cdot\nabla)^2 f = 2\dot{x}^2 + 2\dot{y}^2 = 2v^2$ and $\nabla V\cdot\nabla f = (0, mg)\cdot(0, 2R) = 2mgR$. Also $|\nabla f|^2 = 4R^2$. So $\lambda = (m \cdot 2v^2 - 2mgR)/(4R^2) = (mv^2 - mgR)/(2R^2)$. The constraint force magnitude is $|2\lambda R| = |mv^2/R - mg|$. For the bead to maintain contact at the top, we need $mv^2/R \geq mg$, giving $v^2 \geq gR$.

**Bridge.** The Lagrange multiplier method builds toward the Dirac-Bergmann theory of constrained Hamiltonian systems [09.04.01], where primary and secondary constraints are classified and the multipliers are related to gauge freedoms; the foundational reason is that the multiplier $\lambda$ is dual to the constraint $f = 0$, and this is exactly the algebraic structure that appears in constrained Hamiltonian systems. The central insight — that constraint forces are normal to the constraint surface — appears again in the symplectic treatment of constrained dynamics, and putting these together, the Lagrange multiplier formulation provides the computational bridge from eliminating constraints (Lagrangian coordinates) to computing them (multipliers).

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

A particle moves in a plane under no applied forces, constrained to the unit circle $x^2 + y^2 = 1$. Write the extended Euler-Lagrange equations and show that the particle moves with constant angular velocity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2)$ with constraint $f = x^2 + y^2 - 1 = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Extended equations: $m\ddot{x} = 2\lambda x$ and $m\ddot{y} = 2\lambda y$. Differentiating the constraint: $x\dot{x} + y\dot{y} = 0$ and $x\ddot{x} + \dot{x}^2 + y\ddot{y} + \dot{y}^2 = 0$. Substituting: $2\lambda(x^2+y^2) + m(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2) = 0$, giving $\lambda = -m(\dot{x}^2+\dot{y}^2)/2$. Since $|\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2 = \dot{x}^2+\dot{y}^2 = \text{const}$ (the constraint force does no work on the circle), $\lambda$ is constant and the particle moves at constant speed. In polar coordinates, $\dot\theta = v/R = \text{const}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

A particle of mass $m$ is constrained to move on the surface of a cone $z = r$ (in cylindrical coordinates $r, \phi, z$) in a uniform gravitational field $V = mgz$. Use the constraint to eliminate $z$ and write the Lagrangian in terms of $r$ and $\phi$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute $z = r$ and $\dot{z} = \dot{r}$ into $L = T - V$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot{r}^2 + r^2\dot\phi^2 + \dot{r}^2) - mgr = \tfrac{1}{2}m(2\dot{r}^2 + r^2\dot\phi^2) - mgr$. The coordinate $\phi$ is cyclic, so $p_\phi = mr^2\dot\phi = \ell$ is conserved. The effective one-dimensional Lagrangian for $r$ is $L_{\rm eff} = m\dot{r}^2 - \ell^2/(2mr^2) - mgr$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

A particle of mass $m$ moves in a vertical plane, constrained to the parabola $y = x^2$. Use a Lagrange multiplier to find the equations of motion and the constraint force. Find the condition for equilibrium.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Constraint: $f(x,y) = y - x^2 = 0$. Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2) - mgy$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Extended equations: $m\ddot{x} = -2\lambda x$ and $m\ddot{y} = -mg + \lambda$. The constraint gives $\dot{y} = 2x\dot{x}$ and $\ddot{y} = 2\dot{x}^2 + 2x\ddot{x}$. Substituting $\ddot{x}$: $\ddot{y} = 2\dot{x}^2 + 2x(-2\lambda x/m) = 2\dot{x}^2 - 4\lambda x^2/m$. From the second Euler-Lagrange equation: $\ddot{y} = -g + \lambda/m$. Setting equal: $\lambda/m - g = 2\dot{x}^2 - 4\lambda x^2/m$, giving $\lambda(1 + 4x^2) = m(g + 2\dot{x}^2)$, so $\lambda = m(g + 2\dot{x}^2)/(1 + 4x^4)$. The constraint force is $\mathbf{F}^{(c)} = \lambda\nabla f = \lambda(-2x, 1)$. At equilibrium ($\dot{x} = 0$, $\ddot{x} = 0$): $\lambda = mg/(1+4x^2)$ and the normal force balances gravity's component perpendicular to the parabola. The only equilibrium point is $x = 0$ (the vertex), where $\lambda = mg$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A simple pendulum (mass $m$, length $\ell$) is driven by a time-dependent constraint: the pivot oscillates vertically as $y_0(t) = A\cos\omega t$. Write the constraint and the Lagrangian, and find the equation of motion for the pendulum angle.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The bob's position is $x = \ell\sin\theta$, $y = -\ell\cos\theta + A\cos\omega t$. This is a rheonomic (time-dependent) constraint.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The constraint is built into the coordinate choice: $x^2 + (y - A\cos\omega t)^2 = \ell^2$. The bob's velocity: $\dot{x} = \ell\dot\theta\cos\theta$, $\dot{y} = \ell\dot\theta\sin\theta - A\omega\sin\omega t$. The kinetic energy is $T = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\ell^2\dot\theta^2 + A^2\omega^2\sin^2\omega t - 2A\omega\ell\dot\theta\sin\theta\sin\omega t)$. The potential is $V = mg(-\ell\cos\theta + A\cos\omega t)$. The Lagrangian gives the equation of motion: $m\ell^2\ddot\theta + m\ell A\omega^2\cos\omega t\sin\theta = -mg\ell\sin\theta$. Dividing by $m\ell^2$: $\ddot\theta + (g/\ell)\sin\theta = (A\omega^2/\ell)\cos\omega t\sin\theta$. This is the Mathieu equation in the small-angle limit — a parametrically driven oscillator.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Two masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ are connected by a rigid rod of length $\ell$. They move in a plane under no external forces. Write the constraint and show that the centre of mass moves with constant velocity while the system rotates at constant angular velocity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Constraint: $(x_1-x_2)^2 + (y_1-y_2)^2 = \ell^2$. Use centre-of-mass and relative coordinates.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Introduce $\mathbf{R} = (m_1\mathbf{r}_1 + m_2\mathbf{r}_2)/(m_1+m_2)$ and $\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2$. The constraint is $|\mathbf{r}|^2 = \ell^2$. The Lagrangian separates: $L = \tfrac{1}{2}(m_1+m_2)|\dot{\mathbf{R}}|^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\mu|\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2$ where $\mu = m_1m_2/(m_1+m_2)$. The constraint force on $\mathbf{R}$ is zero (no external force), so $\ddot{\mathbf{R}} = 0$ — uniform centre-of-mass motion. For $\mathbf{r}$: in polar coordinates with $r = \ell$ fixed, $\mu\ell^2\ddot\theta = 0$ (no torque), giving $\dot\theta = \text{const}$. The system translates uniformly and rotates uniformly — a free rigid body in two dimensions.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the work done by the constraint force $\mathbf{F}^{(c)} = \lambda\nabla f$ on a trajectory satisfying $f = 0$ is zero when $f$ is time-independent.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\mathbf{F}^{(c)}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}} = \lambda\nabla f\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}}$ and use the time derivative of the constraint.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The constraint $f = 0$ implies $\dot{f} = \nabla f\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}} = 0$ (for scleronomic constraints). The power is $P = \mathbf{F}^{(c)}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}} = \lambda(\nabla f\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}}) = \lambda\dot{f} = 0$. So the constraint force does zero work. This is consistent with the virtual-work principle: constraint forces perpendicular to the constraint surface do no work along the surface.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

A particle of mass $m$ slides on the inside of a smooth paraboloid of revolution $z = (x^2+y^2)/(2a)$ in a uniform gravitational field. Find the condition for circular orbits at height $z_0$ and determine their stability.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use cylindrical coordinates $(r, \phi, z)$ with constraint $z = r^2/(2a)$. The angular momentum $p_\phi$ is conserved.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Eliminating $z$: $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m[(1+r^2/a^2)\dot{r}^2 + r^2\dot\phi^2] - mgr^2/(2a)$. The conjugate momentum $\ell = mr^2\dot\phi$ is conserved. The effective radial Lagrangian gives $m(1+r^2/a^2)\ddot{r} + mr\dot{r}^2/a^2 - \ell^2/(mr^3) + mgr/a = 0$. For circular orbit ($\dot{r} = 0$, $\ddot{r} = 0$): $\ell^2/(mr_0^3) = mgr_0/a$, giving $\ell^2 = m^2gr_0^4/a$. The height is $z_0 = r_0^2/(2a)$.

For stability, linearise around $r_0$: let $r = r_0 + \epsilon$. The coefficient of $\epsilon$ in the linearised equation is $m(1+r_0^2/a^2)\ddot\epsilon + [-3\ell^2/(mr_0^4) + mg/a]\epsilon = 0$. Substituting $\ell^2 = m^2gr_0^4/a$: the bracket becomes $-3mg/a + mg/a = -2mg/a$. This is negative (restoring), so $\omega^2 = 2g/(a(1+r_0^2/a^2))$ and the orbit is stable for all $r_0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the extended Euler-Lagrange equations from the modified action principle $S = \int_0^T [L - \sum_\alpha \lambda_\alpha f_\alpha]\,dt$ by varying independently in $q^i$ and $\lambda_\alpha$. State where the assumption $\delta q^i(t_0) = \delta q^i(t_1) = 0$ is used.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Vary $q^i \to q^i + \delta q^i$ and $\lambda_\alpha \to \lambda_\alpha + \delta\lambda_\alpha$. The boundary terms in the integration by parts vanish because $\delta q = 0$ at the endpoints.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define $L' = L - \sum_\alpha \lambda_\alpha f_\alpha$. Varying $q^i$: $\delta S = \int_0^T [\partial L'/\partial q^i - d/dt(\partial L'/\partial\dot{q}^i)]\delta q^i\,dt + [\partial L'/\partial\dot{q}^i \delta q^i]_0^T$. The boundary term vanishes because $\delta q^i(0) = \delta q^i(T) = 0$. Since $\delta q^i$ is arbitrary, $\partial L'/\partial q^i - d/dt(\partial L'/\partial\dot{q}^i) = 0$, which gives $d/dt(\partial L/\partial\dot{q}^i) - \partial L/\partial q^i = -\sum_\alpha \lambda_\alpha \partial f_\alpha/\partial q^i$. Varying $\lambda_\alpha$: $\delta S = -\int_0^T f_\alpha\delta\lambda_\alpha\,dt = 0$ for arbitrary $\delta\lambda_\alpha$, giving $f_\alpha = 0$. The endpoint assumption is used only in the integration by parts for the $q^i$ variation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

A particle moves on a sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2 = R^2$ under a potential $V = k(x^2+y^2)$. Show that the angular momentum about the $z$-axis is conserved, and explain why the $x$- and $y$-components of angular momentum are not.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use spherical coordinates $(R, \theta, \phi)$. The Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}mR^2(\dot\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,\dot\phi^2) - kR^2\sin^2\theta$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In spherical coordinates, $L = \tfrac{1}{2}mR^2(\dot\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,\dot\phi^2) - kR^2\sin^2\theta$. The coordinate $\phi$ is cyclic ($\partial L/\partial\phi = 0$), so $p_\phi = mR^2\sin^2\theta\,\dot\phi$ is conserved. This is exactly the $z$-component of angular momentum: $L_z = m(x\dot{y} - y\dot{x}) = mR^2\sin^2\theta\,\dot\phi$.

The $x$- and $y$-components are not conserved because the potential $V = kR^2\sin^2\theta = kR^2(1-\cos^2\theta)$ breaks the rotation symmetry about the $x$ and $y$ axes. Only rotations about $z$ preserve the potential and the constraint simultaneously. The torque about $z$ vanishes: $\tau_z = -\partial V/\partial\phi = 0$. About $x$ or $y$, $\tau \neq 0$ in general.

</details>

</aside>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The Lagrange multiplier method extends Mathlib's unconstrained Euler-Lagrange machinery to systems with holonomic constraints. The main formalisation gap is the construction of the constrained action functional $S' = \int(L - \sum\lambda_\alpha f_\alpha)\,dt$ and its variation, together with the identification of $\lambda_\alpha\nabla f_\alpha$ as the constraint force. This would require combining Mathlib's calculus of variations on manifolds with the submanifold geometry of constraint surfaces.

## Advanced results: Dirac brackets and the geometry of constraints [Master]

### Multiple constraints and constraint manifolds

When multiple holonomic constraints $f_1, \ldots, f_k$ are present, the constraint surface $S = \{q : f_\alpha(q) = 0, \alpha = 1, \ldots, k\}$ is a submanifold of codimension $k$ provided the gradients $\nabla f_\alpha$ are linearly independent. The constraint forces span the normal bundle of $S$, and the multipliers $\lambda_\alpha$ are determined by the requirement that the trajectory remain on $S$.

### Non-holonomic constraints in the Lagrangian framework

Non-holonomic constraints $g_\alpha(q, \dot{q}) = 0$ cannot be handled by simply reducing the number of coordinates. The standard approach is to impose them via the **Lagrange-d'Alembert equations**:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot{q}^i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} \;=\; \sum_\alpha \mu_\alpha \frac{\partial g_\alpha}{\partial\dot{q}^i}.
$$

Note the crucial difference: the multipliers multiply $\partial g_\alpha/\partial\dot{q}^i$, not $\partial g_\alpha/\partial q^i$. This is because non-holonomic constraints restrict velocities, not positions.

**Proposition.** *For a rolling disk (thin disk of radius $r$, mass $m$, rolling without slipping on a horizontal plane), the Lagrange-d'Alembert equations give four equations for four unknowns $(x, y, \theta, \phi)$ plus two multipliers $(\mu_1, \mu_2)$ from the two rolling constraints.*

*Proof sketch.* The configuration is $(x, y)$ for the contact point, $\phi$ for the rotation angle, and $\theta$ for the heading. The Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2) + \tfrac{1}{4}mr^2(\dot\phi^2 + 2\dot\theta^2)$ (translational plus rotational kinetic energy). The rolling constraints are $g_1 = \dot{x} - r\dot\phi\cos\theta = 0$ and $g_2 = \dot{y} - r\dot\phi\sin\theta = 0$. The Lagrange-d'Alembert equations give the multipliers $\mu_1, \mu_2$ as the friction forces at the contact point. $\square$

### From Lagrange multipliers to Dirac brackets

In the Hamiltonian framework, holonomic constraints become relations among the phase-space variables. Dirac's theory of constrained Hamiltonian systems classifies constraints as **primary** (arising from the Legendre transform) and **secondary** (arising from consistency conditions). The Lagrange multipliers of the Lagrangian formulation correspond to the undetermined velocities of primary constraints in the Dirac theory. The passage from Lagrange multipliers to Dirac brackets is the Hamiltonian counterpart of the material in this unit.

**Synthesis.** The Lagrange multiplier method is the foundational reason that constrained dynamics can be formulated without eliminating coordinates; the central insight is that the multiplier is dual to the constraint, encoding the force needed to maintain it. This is exactly the algebraic structure that Dirac exploited in his theory of constrained Hamiltonian systems. Putting these together, the multiplier formulation provides the computational bridge from the Lagrangian coordinate-elimination strategy to the Hamiltonian constraint-classification strategy. The generalisation to field theories with gauge constraints — where the multipliers become gauge fields — completes the bridge from classical mechanics to modern theoretical physics.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Lagrange introduced the multiplier method in *Mechanique analytique* (1788), building on D'Alembert's principle from 1743. The key innovation was recognising that the multipliers have a direct mechanical interpretation as constraint forces. This unified the treatment of all constraint types under a single algebraic framework.

Dirac's theory of constrained Hamiltonian systems (1950) extended the multiplier idea to the Hamiltonian setting, where the distinction between first-class and second-class constraints has deep physical meaning: first-class constraints generate gauge transformations, and the associated multipliers represent genuine physical freedom (gauge choice), while second-class constraints are eliminable by the Dirac bracket.

A philosophical observation: the multiplier method reveals that constraints have a dual nature. As equations ($f = 0$), they restrict the configuration space. As forces ($\lambda\nabla f$), they act on the system. The multiplier $\lambda$ is the link between these two aspects — it is determined by the constraint equation but enters the dynamics as a force. This duality between kinematic restriction and dynamic enforcement is a structural feature of all constrained systems, from classical pendulums to gauge field theories.
