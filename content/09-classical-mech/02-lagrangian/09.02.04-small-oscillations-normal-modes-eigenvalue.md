## Intuition [Beginner]

Hang two pendulums side by side and connect them with a weak spring. Pull one pendulum to the side and release it. At first, only the first pendulum swings. Gradually the second pendulum picks up the motion while the first slows down. After a while the first pendulum is nearly still and the second swings at full amplitude. Then the process reverses. Energy sloshes back and forth between the two pendulums.

This is a coupled-oscillator system. The spring connects the two pendulums, allowing them to exchange energy. The key mathematical insight is that there are two special ways to start the system in which the energy does not slosh — each pendulum swings at its own constant amplitude forever. These special patterns of motion are called **normal modes**. Every other motion is a mixture of the two normal modes, and the beating (energy sloshing) you observe is the interference between the two slightly different frequencies of the two modes.

For any system of coupled oscillators near a stable equilibrium — whether it is two pendulums, three masses on springs, or the atoms in a crystal lattice — the motion can always be decomposed into independent normal modes. Each mode oscillates at its own characteristic frequency. The frequencies are the eigenvalues of a matrix equation, and the mode shapes (the pattern of displacements) are the eigenvectors. This is why eigenvalues and eigenvectors from linear algebra appear so prominently in physics: the normal-mode problem is a generalised eigenvalue problem.

The number of normal modes equals the number of degrees of freedom. For two coupled pendulums there are two modes. For a molecule of $N$ atoms there are $3N - 6$ vibrational modes (subtracting 3 translational and 3 rotational degrees of freedom). For a crystal lattice with $N$ unit cells and $p$ atoms per cell, there are $3pN$ vibrational modes, which in the continuum limit become the acoustic and optical phonon branches of solid-state physics.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture two identical pendulums of length $L$ and mass $m$, connected by a spring of constant $k$ at height $h$ above the pivot. Let $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ be the small angular displacements.

Normal mode 1 (symmetric): both pendulums swing together. The spring does not stretch. The frequency is $\omega_1 = \sqrt{g/L}$, the same as a single uncoupled pendulum.

Normal mode 2 (antisymmetric): the pendulums swing opposite to each other. The spring stretches maximally. The frequency is $\omega_2 = \sqrt{g/L + 2kh^2/(mL^2)}$, higher than $\omega_1$ because the spring provides an extra restoring force.

![Two pendulums connected by a horizontal spring. Mode 1: both swing right together (spring unstretched). Mode 2: left pendulum swings right, right pendulum swings left (spring compressed). The general motion is a superposition of both modes, producing the characteristic beating pattern.](/img/09.02.04-coupled-pendulum-normal-modes-placeholder.svg)

Any initial condition can be decomposed into a combination of these two modes. If only one pendulum is displaced, both modes are excited equally. The beating frequency is $\omega_2 - \omega_1$, and the period of energy transfer is $T_{\mathrm{beat}} = 2\pi/(\omega_2 - \omega_1)$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Two equal masses $m$ are connected by three springs of equal constant $k$: wall--spring--mass--spring--mass--spring--wall. Find the normal-mode frequencies and mode shapes.

Let $x_1$ and $x_2$ be the displacements from equilibrium. The spring forces are: on mass 1, $-kx_1 - k(x_1 - x_2) = -2kx_1 + kx_2$; on mass 2, $-kx_2 - k(x_2 - x_1) = -2kx_2 + kx_1$. Newton's second law gives

$$
m\ddot{x}_1 = -2kx_1 + kx_2, \qquad m\ddot{x}_2 = kx_1 - 2kx_2.
$$

Look for solutions where both masses oscillate at the same frequency: $x_1(t) = A_1 \cos(\omega t)$, $x_2(t) = A_2 \cos(\omega t)$. Substituting:

$$
-m\omega^2 A_1 = -2kA_1 + kA_2, \qquad -m\omega^2 A_2 = kA_1 - 2kA_2.
$$

This is a matrix eigenvalue problem. In matrix form:

$$
\begin{pmatrix} 2k & -k \\ -k & 2k \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} A_1 \\ A_2 \end{pmatrix} = m\omega^2 \begin{pmatrix} A_1 \\ A_2 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Setting $\lambda = m\omega^2$: the characteristic equation is $(2k - \lambda)^2 - k^2 = 0$, giving $\lambda = k$ or $\lambda = 3k$.

Mode 1: $\lambda = k$, $\omega_1 = \sqrt{k/m}$. The eigenvector satisfies $(2k - k)A_1 - kA_2 = 0$, so $A_1 = A_2$. Both masses move in the same direction by the same amount (the central spring is unstretched).

Mode 2: $\lambda = 3k$, $\omega_2 = \sqrt{3k/m}$. The eigenvector satisfies $(2k - 3k)A_1 - kA_2 = 0$, so $A_1 = -A_2$. The masses move in opposite directions by the same amount (the central spring is maximally stretched or compressed).

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Consider a mechanical system with $n$ generalised coordinates $q^1, \ldots, q^n$ and a stable equilibrium point at $q^i = 0$. Expand the Lagrangian $L = T - V$ about this equilibrium. The kinetic energy is

$$
T = \frac{1}{2} M_{ij} \dot{q}^i \dot{q}^j,
$$

where $M_{ij} = M_{ji}$ is the **mass matrix** (or inertia matrix), which is symmetric and positive-definite. The potential energy expanded to second order is

$$
V = \frac{1}{2} K_{ij} q^i q^j,
$$

where $K_{ij} = K_{ji} = \partial^2 V / \partial q^i \partial q^j |_{q=0}$ is the **stiffness matrix** (or force-constant matrix), which is symmetric. Stability requires $K$ to be positive-semidefinite (all eigenvalues non-negative).

The Lagrangian $L = \frac{1}{2} M_{ij} \dot{q}^i \dot{q}^j - \frac{1}{2} K_{ij} q^i q^j$ yields the equations of motion

$$
M_{ij} \ddot{q}^j + K_{ij} q^j = 0.
$$

In matrix form: $M \ddot{\mathbf{q}} + K \mathbf{q} = 0$.

### The generalised eigenvalue problem

Seek harmonic solutions $\mathbf{q}(t) = \mathbf{a} \cos(\omega t)$, where $\mathbf{a}$ is a constant amplitude vector. Substituting:

$$
(- \omega^2 M + K) \mathbf{a} = 0.
$$

This is a **generalised eigenvalue problem**: $K\mathbf{a} = \omega^2 M \mathbf{a}$. Nonzero solutions exist when

$$
\det(K - \omega^2 M) = 0.
$$

This is the **secular equation** (also called the characteristic or frequency equation). It is a polynomial of degree $n$ in $\omega^2$.

Since $M$ is positive-definite and $K$ is symmetric, the eigenvalues $\omega_1^2, \ldots, \omega_n^2$ are all real. If $K$ is positive-definite, all $\omega_\alpha^2 > 0$ and the frequencies are real. If $K$ is positive-semidefinite with zero eigenvalues, the corresponding modes have $\omega = 0$ — these are zero-frequency modes representing free translations or rotations of the system.

### Simultaneous diagonalisation and normal coordinates

The eigenvectors $\mathbf{a}^{(\alpha)}$ satisfy the orthogonality relations

$$
\mathbf{a}^{(\alpha)\top} M \mathbf{a}^{(\beta)} = \delta_{\alpha\beta}, \qquad \mathbf{a}^{(\alpha)\top} K \mathbf{a}^{(\beta)} = \omega_\alpha^2 \delta_{\alpha\beta}.
$$

These are the $M$-orthonormality and $K$-orthogonality conditions. Define the **modal matrix** $A = [\mathbf{a}^{(1)} | \cdots | \mathbf{a}^{(n)}]$. Then $A^\top M A = I$ and $A^\top K A = \Omega^2 = \mathrm{diag}(\omega_1^2, \ldots, \omega_n^2)$.

The **normal coordinates** $\eta_\alpha$ are defined by $\mathbf{q} = A\boldsymbol{\eta}$, or equivalently $\eta_\alpha = \mathbf{a}^{(\alpha)\top} M \mathbf{q}$. In normal coordinates the Lagrangian is diagonal:

$$
L = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{\alpha=1}^n (\dot{\eta}_\alpha^2 - \omega_\alpha^2 \eta_\alpha^2).
$$

Each normal coordinate satisfies an independent harmonic-oscillator equation $\ddot{\eta}_\alpha + \omega_\alpha^2 \eta_\alpha = 0$. The general solution is $\eta_\alpha(t) = C_\alpha \cos(\omega_\alpha t) + D_\alpha \sin(\omega_\alpha t)$, and the physical coordinates are reconstructed by $\mathbf{q}(t) = \sum_\alpha \mathbf{a}^{(\alpha)} \eta_\alpha(t)$.

## Key result [Intermediate+]

**Key result (Normal-mode theorem).** *Let $M$ be a real symmetric positive-definite $n \times n$ matrix and $K$ a real symmetric positive-semidefinite $n \times n$ matrix. Then there exists a matrix $A$ such that $A^\top M A = I$ and $A^\top K A = \mathrm{diag}(\omega_1^2, \ldots, \omega_n^2)$, where $\omega_\alpha^2 \geq 0$ are the generalised eigenvalues of $K\mathbf{a} = \omega^2 M \mathbf{a}$. The columns of $A$ are the eigenvectors (normal modes), and the transformation $\mathbf{q} = A\boldsymbol{\eta}$ decouples the system into $n$ independent harmonic oscillators.*

The proof proceeds by noting that $M^{-1/2} K M^{-1/2}$ is a real symmetric matrix (since $K$ is symmetric), and by the spectral theorem it is orthogonally diagonalisable: $Q^\top (M^{-1/2} K M^{-1/2}) Q = \Omega^2$ for some orthogonal $Q$. Setting $A = M^{-1/2} Q$ gives $A^\top M A = Q^\top Q = I$ and $A^\top K A = \Omega^2$. The positive-semidefiniteness of $K$ guarantees $\omega_\alpha^2 \geq 0$.

**Bridge.** This result builds toward the perturbation theory of [09.02.05], where anharmonic terms in the potential couple the normal modes computed here and generate secular drift; it also generalises to the continuum limit in [09.07.02] where the discrete mass matrix becomes the density field and the stiffness matrix becomes the elastic-modulus tensor, and the normal-mode frequencies become the dispersion relation of elastic waves. The foundational reason the eigenvalue problem appears is that the quadratic approximation to the Lagrangian near equilibrium always produces two symmetric forms (kinetic and potential) that can be simultaneously diagonalised; this is exactly the content of the principal-axes theorem for quadratic forms, and it generalises to infinite dimensions as Sturm-Liouville theory.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Three equal masses $m$ are connected in a line by four equal springs $k$ (wall--spring--mass--spring--mass--spring--mass--spring--wall). Write the equations of motion in matrix form and identify $M$ and $K$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each interior mass feels two springs; the end masses each feel one spring and one wall spring.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$M = mI_3$. The stiffness matrix is

$$
K = k \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

This is the tridiagonal Toeplitz matrix that appears throughout lattice dynamics. The eigenvalues are $2k(1 - \cos(j\pi/4))$ for $j = 1, 2, 3$, giving normal frequencies $\omega_j^2 = (2k/m)(1 - \cos(j\pi/4))$. Explicitly: $\omega_1^2 = (2-\sqrt{2})k/m$, $\omega_2^2 = 2k/m$, $\omega_3^2 = (2+\sqrt{2})k/m$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify the $M$-orthonormality relation for the normal modes of the two-mass three-spring system with $m_1 = m_2 = m$, $k_1 = k_3 = k$, $k_2 = k$ (the equal case). Show that $\mathbf{a}^{(1)\top} M \mathbf{a}^{(2)} = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The eigenvectors are proportional to $(1, 1)$ and $(1, -1)$. Normalise them.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathbf{a}^{(1)} \propto (1, 1)^\top$ and $\mathbf{a}^{(2)} \propto (1, -1)^\top$. With $M = mI$, the $M$-inner product is $m\,\mathbf{a}^{(1)} \cdot \mathbf{a}^{(2)} = m(1 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot (-1)) = 0$. To normalise: $m\,|\mathbf{a}^{(1)}|^2 = m(1^2 + 1^2) = 2m$, so the normalised eigenvectors are $\mathbf{a}^{(1)} = (1/\sqrt{2m})(1, 1)^\top$ and $\mathbf{a}^{(2)} = (1/\sqrt{2m})(1, -1)^\top$. These satisfy $\mathbf{a}^{(\alpha)\top} M \mathbf{a}^{(\beta)} = \delta_{\alpha\beta}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

A double pendulum consists of a mass $m_1$ on a rod of length $l_1$ pivoted at the ceiling, and a mass $m_2$ on a rod of length $l_2$ pivoted at $m_1$. For small oscillations about the vertical, write the kinetic and potential energies to quadratic order and identify the mass matrix and stiffness matrix. Take $m_1 = m_2 = m$ and $l_1 = l_2 = l$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The coordinates are $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$. The position of $m_2$ involves both angles. Expand the trigonometric functions for small angles.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Position of $m_1$: $(l\sin\theta_1, -l\cos\theta_1)$. Position of $m_2$: $(l\sin\theta_1 + l\sin\theta_2, -l\cos\theta_1 - l\cos\theta_2)$. For small angles:

$$
T \approx \frac{1}{2}ml^2(2\dot\theta_1^2 + \dot\theta_2^2 + 2\dot\theta_1\dot\theta_2).
$$

So $M = ml^2 \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$.

$$
V = -mgl\cos\theta_1 - mg(l\cos\theta_1 + l\cos\theta_2) \approx mgl(\theta_1^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\theta_2^2).
$$

Wait — need to be more careful. $V = -mgl\cos\theta_1 - mg(l\cos\theta_1 + l\cos\theta_2)$, expanded: $V \approx -2mgl + mgl\theta_1^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}mgl\theta_2^2$. Dropping the constant and taking second derivatives: $K = mgl \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$.

The secular equation is $\det(K - \omega^2 M) = 0$, i.e., $\det\begin{pmatrix} 2mgl - 2ml^2\omega^2 & -ml^2\omega^2 \\ -ml^2\omega^2 & mgl - ml^2\omega^2 \end{pmatrix} = 0$. Dividing by $m^2 l^2$: $(2g - 2l\omega^2)(g - l\omega^2) - l^2\omega^4 = 0$, giving $2g^2 - 4gl\omega^2 + 2l^2\omega^4 - l^2\omega^4 = 0$, or $\omega^4 - 4(g/l)\omega^2 + 2(g/l)^2 = 0$. The solutions are $\omega^2 = (g/l)(2 \pm \sqrt{2})$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the normal-mode transformation $\mathbf{q} = A\boldsymbol{\eta}$ decouples the Lagrangian. Compute $L$ in normal coordinates and verify that it is a sum of independent harmonic-oscillator Lagrangians.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute $\mathbf{q} = A\boldsymbol{\eta}$ and $\dot{\mathbf{q}} = A\dot{\boldsymbol{\eta}}$ into $L = \tfrac{1}{2}\dot{\mathbf{q}}^\top M \dot{\mathbf{q}} - \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{q}^\top K \mathbf{q}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$L = \tfrac{1}{2}(A\dot{\boldsymbol{\eta}})^\top M (A\dot{\boldsymbol{\eta}}) - \tfrac{1}{2}(A\boldsymbol{\eta})^\top K (A\boldsymbol{\eta}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\dot{\boldsymbol{\eta}}^\top (A^\top M A) \dot{\boldsymbol{\eta}} - \tfrac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{\eta}^\top (A^\top K A) \boldsymbol{\eta}$. By the normal-mode theorem, $A^\top M A = I$ and $A^\top K A = \Omega^2 = \mathrm{diag}(\omega_1^2, \ldots, \omega_n^2)$. Therefore $L = \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_\alpha (\dot\eta_\alpha^2 - \omega_\alpha^2 \eta_\alpha^2)$, a sum of $n$ independent harmonic oscillators, each with its own frequency $\omega_\alpha$. The Euler-Lagrange equation for $\eta_\alpha$ is $\ddot\eta_\alpha + \omega_\alpha^2 \eta_\alpha = 0$, confirming the decoupling.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

A linear chain of $N$ equal masses $m$ connected by identical springs $k$ between nearest neighbours, with free ends (no walls). Write the stiffness matrix $K$ and the eigenvalue equation. Show that the eigenvectors are $\mathbf{a}^{(j)}_n = \cos(j\pi(n-\tfrac{1}{2})/N)$ for $j = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1$ and the eigenvalues are $\omega_j^2 = (4k/m)\sin^2(j\pi/(2N))$. Identify the zero-frequency mode.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Free boundary conditions mean the first and last masses each feel only one spring. The zero-frequency mode corresponds to a uniform translation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The stiffness matrix for free ends is $K_{nn} = k$ for $n = 1, N$ (end masses) and $K_{nn} = 2k$ for interior masses; $K_{n,n\pm 1} = -k$. The eigenvalue equation is $K\mathbf{a} = m\omega^2 \mathbf{a}$. Substituting the proposed eigenvector $a_n = \cos(j\pi(n-1/2)/N)$ and using the discrete second-difference identity gives $(Ka)_n = 4k\sin^2(j\pi/(2N))\,a_n$, confirming the eigenvalue $\omega_j^2 = (4k/m)\sin^2(j\pi/(2N))$.

For $j = 0$: $\omega_0 = 0$ and $a_n = \cos(0) = 1$ for all $n$. This is the uniform translation mode — all masses displaced by the same amount, no spring is compressed, no restoring force. This zero-frequency mode exists because the system has a continuous translation symmetry (no walls to break it). The remaining $N - 1$ modes are oscillatory with frequencies $0 < \omega_1 < \omega_2 < \cdots < \omega_{N-1}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the normal-mode frequencies are real and non-negative when $M$ is positive-definite and $K$ is positive-semidefinite. Show that positive-definiteness of $K$ corresponds to strict stability (all $\omega_\alpha > 0$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take the eigenvalue equation $K\mathbf{a} = \omega^2 M\mathbf{a}$ and contract with $\mathbf{a}^*$ (the conjugate of $\mathbf{a}$).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$K\mathbf{a} = \omega^2 M\mathbf{a}$. Contract: $\mathbf{a}^{*\top} K \mathbf{a} = \omega^2 \mathbf{a}^{*\top} M \mathbf{a}$. Both $\mathbf{a}^{*\top} K \mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{a}^{*\top} M \mathbf{a}$ are real (by Hermiticity of $K$ and $M$). Since $M$ is positive-definite, $\mathbf{a}^{*\top} M \mathbf{a} > 0$ for $\mathbf{a} \neq 0$. Therefore $\omega^2 = (\mathbf{a}^{*\top} K \mathbf{a}) / (\mathbf{a}^{*\top} M \mathbf{a})$ is real and non-negative when $K$ is positive-semidefinite ($\mathbf{a}^{*\top} K \mathbf{a} \geq 0$). If $K$ is positive-definite, then $\mathbf{a}^{*\top} K \mathbf{a} > 0$, so $\omega^2 > 0$ and all frequencies are positive.

A zero eigenvalue ($\omega^2 = 0$) occurs when $\mathbf{a}$ lies in the null space of $K$, meaning the potential has a flat direction — the system is free to move along that direction without any restoring force. This corresponds to a continuous symmetry (translation or rotation) or a marginally stable direction.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The CO$_2$ molecule is linear: O--C--O. Treating it as three masses on a line with nearest-neighbour spring constants $k$, find the three normal-mode frequencies and describe the motion in each mode. Which modes are infrared active (involve a changing dipole moment)?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set up the problem in centre-of-mass coordinates. The oxygen masses are equal ($m_O$), the carbon mass is $m_C$. One mode is the overall translation (zero frequency).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $x_1, x_2, x_3$ be displacements of O, C, O. The mass matrix is $M = \mathrm{diag}(m_O, m_C, m_O)$ and the stiffness matrix is $K = k\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 1\end{pmatrix}$. The zero-frequency mode is $(1, 1, 1)^\top$ (uniform translation). The symmetric stretch mode has $(1, 0, 1)^\top$ (both oxygens move outward while the carbon stays fixed — wait, this is not exactly right because of centre-of-mass conservation). More precisely, the two oscillatory modes are:

Symmetric stretch: both O atoms move in opposite directions to the C atom. Frequency $\omega_s^2 = k(1/m_O + 2/m_C)$. The C moves opposite to keep the centre of mass fixed.

Asymmetric stretch: one O moves toward C while the other moves away. Frequency $\omega_a^2 = k/m_O$.

The asymmetric stretch is IR active because it creates a changing dipole moment (the charge centre shifts as one O approaches and the other recedes). The symmetric stretch is IR inactive (the molecule remains symmetric). In practice CO$_2$ has $\nu_1 \approx 1388$ cm$^{-1}$ (symmetric, Raman active only), $\nu_3 \approx 2349$ cm$^{-1}$ (asymmetric, IR active — the main greenhouse-gas absorption band).

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### Degeneracy and symmetry

When two or more normal-mode frequencies coincide, the system has a **degeneracy**. Degeneracy almost always arises from a symmetry of the system. For example, a molecule with a three-fold rotational axis has doubly degenerate vibrational modes: the rotation maps one mode into another with the same frequency.

For a degenerate eigenvalue $\omega^2$ with multiplicity $d$, any linear combination of the $d$ eigenvectors is also an eigenvector. The eigenvectors spanning the degenerate subspace are not unique; any $M$-orthonormal basis of the subspace serves. This freedom reflects the underlying symmetry group.

### Damped normal modes

Adding linear damping forces $-C_{ij}\dot{q}^j$ to the equations of motion gives $M\ddot{\mathbf{q}} + C\dot{\mathbf{q}} + K\mathbf{q} = 0$. If the damping matrix $C$ is simultaneously diagonalisable with $M$ and $K$ (proportional damping: $C = \alpha M + \beta K$), then the normal coordinates also diagonalise $C$, and each normal mode decays independently with its own damping rate. If $C$ does not commute with $M$ and $K$ in this sense, the modes couple through the damping and the simple normal-mode decomposition fails.

### Forced oscillations and resonance

When a harmonic driving force $\mathbf{F}(t) = \mathbf{F}_0 \cos(\Omega t)$ is applied to a subset of the coordinates, the response in normal coordinates is

$$
\eta_\alpha(t) = \frac{f_\alpha}{\omega_\alpha^2 - \Omega^2} \cos(\Omega t),
$$

where $f_\alpha = \mathbf{a}^{(\alpha)\top} \mathbf{F}_0$ is the projection of the driving force onto mode $\alpha$. Resonance occurs when $\Omega$ approaches any $\omega_\alpha$. A force that is orthogonal to a particular mode ($f_\alpha = 0$) does not excite that mode at all — this is the principle behind vibration isolation.

### Connection to quantum mechanics

The normal-mode Hamiltonian is a sum of independent harmonic oscillators: $H = \sum_\alpha \frac{1}{2}(p_\alpha^2 + \omega_\alpha^2 \eta_\alpha^2)$. Upon quantisation, each mode becomes an independent quantum harmonic oscillator with energy levels $E_{n_\alpha} = \hbar\omega_\alpha(n_\alpha + \frac{1}{2})$. The quantised excitations of the vibrational normal modes of a crystal lattice are **phonons**, the quasiparticles of solid-state physics. The normal-mode decomposition of the classical problem is the prerequisite for the second-quantised phonon Hamiltonian.

### The monatomic chain and the long-wavelength limit

Consider an infinite chain of identical masses $m$ connected by springs $k$, with lattice spacing $a$. The equation of motion for mass $n$ is $m\ddot{u}_n = k(u_{n+1} - 2u_n + u_{n-1})$. Seeking plane-wave solutions $u_n = A e^{i(qna - \omega t)}$ gives the **dispersion relation**

$$
\omega^2(q) = \frac{4k}{m}\sin^2\left(\frac{qa}{2}\right).
$$

The wave vector $q$ takes values in the first Brillouin zone $[-\pi/a, \pi/a]$. At long wavelengths ($qa \ll 1$), $\omega \approx \sqrt{k/m}\,qa = c_s q$ where $c_s = a\sqrt{k/m}$ is the speed of sound. This is the **acoustic branch** — the discrete lattice reduces to a continuum elastic medium at wavelengths much longer than the lattice spacing. The continuum limit of the chain equation is the wave equation $\partial^2 u/\partial t^2 = c_s^2 \partial^2 u/\partial x^2$, whose normal modes are the Fourier components $e^{i(qx - \omega t)}$ with linear dispersion $\omega = c_s |q|$.

### The diatomic chain and optical modes

When the lattice has two different masses $M$ and $m$ alternating, the dispersion relation splits into two branches. The **acoustic branch** has $\omega \to 0$ as $q \to 0$ (long-wavelength translations). The **optical branch** has $\omega \to \omega_0 > 0$ as $q \to 0$ (the two sublattices oscillate against each other even at long wavelength). In ionic crystals the optical mode carries an oscillating electric dipole, which couples to electromagnetic radiation — hence the name "optical." The gap between the acoustic and optical branches is a **band gap** in the phonon spectrum; no propagating modes exist at frequencies within the gap.

### Stability analysis via the Hessian

The condition for stable equilibrium is that the Hessian $K_{ij} = \partial^2 V/\partial q^i \partial q^j$ be positive-semidefinite. If $K$ has a negative eigenvalue, the equilibrium is **unstable**: small displacements along the corresponding eigenvector direction grow exponentially. If $K$ has a zero eigenvalue, the equilibrium is **marginally stable** or **neutrally stable** along that direction — the system can drift without restoring force. The number of zero eigenvalues equals the number of continuous symmetries of the potential (by Noether's theorem, each continuous symmetry produces a zero-frequency mode). A molecule free in space has six zero-frequency modes: three translations and three rotations.

### The Rayleigh quotient for frequencies

The normal-mode frequencies can be characterised variationally via the **Rayleigh quotient**. For any trial displacement $\mathbf{q}$, define

$$
R[\mathbf{q}] = \frac{\mathbf{q}^\top K \mathbf{q}}{\mathbf{q}^\top M \mathbf{q}}.
$$

The stationary values of $R$ are the eigenvalues $\omega_\alpha^2$, and the stationarity condition $\delta R = 0$ recovers the generalised eigenvalue equation $K\mathbf{q} = \omega^2 M\mathbf{q}$. The minimum of $R$ over all $\mathbf{q}$ gives $\omega_1^2$ (the fundamental frequency), and the Courant-Fischer min-max principle gives the higher frequencies. This variational characterisation is the basis of the Rayleigh-Ritz method for computing approximate normal modes in engineering applications.

**Synthesis.** The normal-mode analysis puts together the Lagrangian quadratic expansion, the generalised eigenvalue problem, and simultaneous diagonalisation of two quadratic forms; the central insight is that stability of an equilibrium point guarantees the positive-semidefiniteness of both $M$ and $K$, and the spectral theorem for symmetric matrices then ensures the existence of real normal frequencies and orthogonal mode shapes. This generalises directly to the continuum limit where the discrete eigenvalue problem becomes a Sturm-Liouville problem for wave modes, and the quantum-mechanical promotion of each normal mode to an independent oscillator is the foundational reason that phonons, photons, and all quantum-field-theoretic quasiparticles share the harmonic-oscillator structure. Putting these together, the normal-mode decomposition is the bridge from classical mechanics to quantum field theory.

## Connections [Master]

- The Euler-Lagrange equations [09.02.02] are the starting point; the small-oscillation expansion linearises them about equilibrium.
- The eigenvalue theory from linear algebra [01.01.12] provides the mathematical foundation for the generalised eigenvalue problem.
- The elastic-wave unit [09.07.02] treats the continuum limit of the lattice normal-mode problem; the discrete frequencies become the phonon dispersion relation.
- The Lagrangian perturbation theory [09.02.05] adds cubic and quartic terms to the quadratic potential, coupling the normal modes and generating nonlinear phenomena.
- The action-angle variables [09.06.01] provide the canonical framework for the nonlinear generalisation of normal-mode analysis.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The analysis of small oscillations and normal modes originates with Daniel Bernoulli's 1753 work on the vibrating string, which identified the harmonic overtones as the natural modes of the system. Lagrange's *Mechanique analytique* (1788) gave the first systematic treatment of coupled oscillators using what we now call the Lagrangian approach. The matrix formulation and the connection to the eigenvalue problem were made explicit by Rayleigh in *The Theory of Sound* (1877), which remains the standard reference for the acoustical applications.

The generalised eigenvalue problem $K\mathbf{a} = \omega^2 M\mathbf{a}$ is a reformulation of the simultaneous-diagonalisation problem for two quadratic forms, solved by Weierstrass and Kronecker in the 19th century. Its appearance in mechanics is an instance of a broader pattern: whenever a physical system is linearised near a stable equilibrium, the resulting equations of motion involve two symmetric forms that must be simultaneously diagonalised. This pattern repeats in quantum mechanics (the Hartree-Fock equations), structural engineering (finite-element modal analysis), and control theory (linear system diagonalisation).
