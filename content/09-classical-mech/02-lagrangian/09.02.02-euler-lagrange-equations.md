## Intuition [Beginner]

In the previous unit [09.02.01] you saw the action principle: nature picks the path that makes $S$ stationary. But "stationary" is a condition on the whole path — how do you actually *find* such a path? The **Euler-Lagrange equations** are the answer. They are a set of differential equations that every stationary-action path must satisfy. Solve them (with the right boundary conditions) and you have the physical trajectory.

Think of it this way. The action principle is the *goal* ("find the path with stationary $S$"). The Euler-Lagrange equations are the *recipe* for achieving that goal. You write down the Lagrangian $L = T - V$ in whatever coordinates you like, plug it into the Euler-Lagrange equation, and out come the equations of motion — no force diagrams required.

The Euler-Lagrange equation for one coordinate $q$ reads: take the slope of $L$ with respect to $\dot q$ (velocity), differentiate that in time, and subtract the slope of $L$ with respect to $q$ (position). Set the result to zero. Using subscript notation for "slope of $L$ with respect to":

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\big(L_{\dot q}\big) - L_q = 0.
$$

For each generalised coordinate you have one such equation. A system with three degrees of freedom gives three coupled second-order differential equations.

Here is the key insight that makes this powerful: the Euler-Lagrange equations give the correct equations of motion in *any* coordinate system. Newton's $F = ma$ requires Cartesian components or careful resolution of forces. The Euler-Lagrange equations require only that you write $L = T - V$ in your chosen coordinates and turn the crank.

Consider a simple pendulum: a mass $m$ on a rigid rod of length $\ell$, swinging under gravity. In Cartesian coordinates you would need the tension force (unknown!) and two equations coupled by the constraint $x^2 + y^2 = \ell^2$. In the angular coordinate $\theta$, the Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2\dot\theta^2 + mg\ell\cos\theta$. One equation of motion, one unknown, no constraint forces. The Euler-Lagrange equation handles the constraint automatically because the coordinate $\theta$ already respects it.

The quantity $p = L_{\dot q}$ (the slope of $L$ with respect to velocity) is called the **conjugate momentum** (or **generalised momentum**). For $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2 - V(q)$, this gives $p = m\dot q$ — ordinary momentum. But in general $p$ need not equal mass times velocity. When $L$ depends on $q$ and $\dot q$ through an electromagnetic potential, the conjugate momentum picks up a vector-potential term.

If $L$ does not depend on a particular coordinate $q^i$ — that coordinate is called **cyclic** — then the Euler-Lagrange equation for $q^i$ reduces to $\frac{d}{dt}(L_{\dot q^i}) = 0$, meaning the conjugate momentum $p_i$ is conserved. Conservation laws fall out of the coordinate structure of $L$ with no extra work.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A simple pendulum has mass $m$ on a rigid rod of length $\ell$, swinging under gravity $g$. Describe the motion using the Euler-Lagrange equation.

**Step 1. Choose a coordinate.** Use the angle $\theta$ from the downward vertical. The mass moves on a circle of radius $\ell$, so its speed is $\ell\dot\theta$.

**Step 2. Write the Lagrangian.** Kinetic energy: $T = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\ell\dot\theta)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2\dot\theta^2$. Potential energy (zero at the bottom): $V = -mg\ell\cos\theta$. So $L = T - V = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2\dot\theta^2 + mg\ell\cos\theta$.

**Step 3. Compute the two pieces of the Euler-Lagrange equation.**

The slope of $L$ with respect to velocity $\dot\theta$: $L_{\dot\theta} = m\ell^2\dot\theta$.

Differentiate this in time: $\frac{d}{dt}(m\ell^2\dot\theta) = m\ell^2\ddot\theta$.

The slope of $L$ with respect to position $\theta$: $L_\theta = -mg\ell\sin\theta$.

**Step 4. Assemble.** The Euler-Lagrange equation $\frac{d}{dt}(L_{\dot\theta}) - L_\theta = 0$ gives:

$$
m\ell^2\ddot\theta - (-mg\ell\sin\theta) = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \ddot\theta + \frac{g}{\ell}\sin\theta = 0.
$$

This is the exact nonlinear pendulum equation. For small angles, $\sin\theta \approx \theta$, giving simple harmonic motion with angular frequency $\omega = \sqrt{g/\ell}$. The entire derivation required no force diagrams, no tension calculation, and no constraint equations — just the Lagrangian and the Euler-Lagrange recipe.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $Q$ be a smooth $n$-dimensional configuration manifold with local coordinates $(q^1, \ldots, q^n)$. A smooth curve $\gamma : [t_1, t_2] \to Q$ has tangent vector $\dot\gamma(t) = (\dot q^1(t), \ldots, \dot q^n(t))$. The Lagrangian $L : TQ \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is a smooth function on the tangent bundle and time.

**Hamilton's principle** [09.02.01] states that the physical path makes the action $S[\gamma] = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(\gamma(t), \dot\gamma(t), t)\, dt$ stationary under variations vanishing at the endpoints. The first-variation computation (integration by parts of the $\dot\eta$ term, using the boundary conditions $\eta(t_1) = \eta(t_2) = 0$) yields the necessary and sufficient condition:

$$
\boxed{\;\;\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} = 0, \qquad i = 1, \ldots, n.\;\;}
$$

These are the **Euler-Lagrange equations**. Each is a second-order ODE for the coordinate $q^i(t)$. The system of $n$ equations determines the trajectory given initial conditions $q^i(t_0)$ and $\dot q^i(t_0)$.

**Derivation from Hamilton's principle.** Consider a variation $\gamma_\epsilon(t) = \gamma(t) + \epsilon\eta(t)$ with $\eta(t_1) = \eta(t_2) = 0$. The first variation of $S$ is:

$$
\delta S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i}\eta^i + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\dot\eta^i\right) dt.
$$

Integrate the second term by parts. The boundary term $\big[\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\eta^i\big]_{t_1}^{t_2}$ vanishes because $\eta$ vanishes at both endpoints. The result is:

$$
\delta S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\right) \eta^i\, dt.
$$

By the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations — if a continuous function $f(t)$ satisfies $\int f(t)\eta(t)\, dt = 0$ for all smooth $\eta$ vanishing at the endpoints, then $f \equiv 0$ — the integrand must vanish pointwise. The Euler-Lagrange equations follow.

**Conjugate momentum.** The quantity

$$
p_i := \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}
$$

is the **conjugate momentum** (also **canonical momentum** or **generalised momentum**) associated with $q^i$. For $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2 - V(q)$ this gives $p = m\dot q$, the ordinary momentum. In general $p_i$ may depend on both $q$ and $\dot q$.

**Cyclic coordinates.** If $\partial L / \partial q^i = 0$ (the coordinate $q^i$ is absent from $L$), the Euler-Lagrange equation reduces to $\dot p_i = 0$: the conjugate momentum is conserved. Such a coordinate is called **cyclic** (or **ignorable**). Each cyclic coordinate reduces the effective dimension of the problem by one, since $p_i$ can be treated as a constant and $q^i$ recovered later by integrating $\dot q^i$.

**The total time derivative.** Note the distinction between $\partial L / \partial q^i$ (partial derivative, holding $\dot q$ and $t$ fixed) and $\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}$ (total time derivative along a trajectory, which expands via the chain rule as $\frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial \dot q^j \partial \dot q^i}\ddot q^j + \frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial q^j \partial \dot q^i}\dot q^j + \frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial t\, \partial \dot q^i}$). This distinction is the single most common source of computational error.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **The Euler-Lagrange equations are not $\partial L / \partial q = 0$.** They involve the total time derivative of $\partial L / \partial \dot q$, not just the partial derivative of $L$ with respect to $q$. A Lagrangian can have no explicit $q$-dependence and still produce nontrivial dynamics (the free particle: $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2$, $\frac{d}{dt}(m\dot q) = 0$, giving $\ddot q = 0$ — constant velocity, not "nothing happens").

- **Conjugate momentum is not always $m\dot q$.** In an electromagnetic field with $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot{\mathbf{q}}|^2 + e\mathbf{A}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{q}} - eV$, the conjugate momentum is $\mathbf{p} = m\dot{\mathbf{q}} + e\mathbf{A}$, not the kinetic momentum $m\dot{\mathbf{q}}$. The difference is physically significant: under a gauge transformation $\mathbf{A} \to \mathbf{A} + \nabla\chi$, the canonical momentum changes but the equations of motion do not.

- **The Euler-Lagrange equations require differentiability of $L$.** If $L$ is not at least $C^2$ in $(q, \dot q)$, the derivation via integration by parts breaks down. Systems with friction (Rayleigh dissipation), impacts, or discontinuous potentials require modified frameworks.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Equivalence of Euler-Lagrange equations and Newton's second law for holonomic systems).** *Consider $N$ particles with masses $m_\alpha$ in a potential $V(\mathbf{r}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_N)$ subject to holonomic constraints $\phi_a(\mathbf{r}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{r}_N) = 0$, $a = 1, \ldots, k$. Let $(q^1, \ldots, q^n)$ with $n = 3N - k$ be independent generalised coordinates that solve the constraints. Then the Euler-Lagrange equations for the Lagrangian $L = T - V$ (expressed in the $q^i$) are equivalent to Newton's second law including the constraint forces.*

**Proof.** Write the particle positions as functions of the generalised coordinates: $\mathbf{r}_\alpha = \mathbf{r}_\alpha(q^1, \ldots, q^n, t)$. The kinetic energy is

$$
T = \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_\alpha m_\alpha |\dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha|^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_\alpha m_\alpha \left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{r}_\alpha}{\partial q^i}\dot q^i + \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}_\alpha}{\partial t}\right)^2.
$$

For time-independent constraints ($\partial \mathbf{r}_\alpha / \partial t = 0$), $T$ is a homogeneous quadratic in the $\dot q^i$ (plus lower-order terms if constraints are time-dependent).

Compute $\partial T / \partial \dot q^i$:

$$
\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot q^i} = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha \dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}_\alpha}{\partial q^i}.
$$

Take the total time derivative:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot q^i} = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha \ddot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}_\alpha}{\partial q^i} + \sum_\alpha m_\alpha \dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha \cdot \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial \mathbf{r}_\alpha}{\partial q^i}.
$$

The second term simplifies by exchanging the order of differentiation ($\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial \mathbf{r}_\alpha}{\partial q^i} = \frac{\partial \dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha}{\partial q^i}$, which follows from $\partial/\partial q^i$ commuting with $d/dt$):

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial T}{\partial \dot q^i} = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha \ddot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}_\alpha}{\partial q^i} + \frac{\partial T}{\partial q^i}.
$$

Also, $\partial T / \partial q^i = \sum_\alpha m_\alpha \dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha \cdot (\partial \dot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha / \partial q^i)$. Substituting into the Euler-Lagrange equation $\frac{d}{dt}(\partial T / \partial \dot q^i) - \partial T / \partial q^i + \partial V / \partial q^i = 0$:

$$
\sum_\alpha m_\alpha \ddot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}_\alpha}{\partial q^i} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial q^i} = 0.
$$

Now apply Newton's second law for the constrained system: $m_\alpha \ddot{\mathbf{r}}_\alpha = -\nabla_{\mathbf{r}_\alpha} V + \mathbf{R}_\alpha$ where $\mathbf{R}_\alpha$ are the constraint forces. The constraint forces satisfy the **virtual work principle** — they are orthogonal to the allowed displacements — so $\sum_\alpha \mathbf{R}_\alpha \cdot (\partial \mathbf{r}_\alpha / \partial q^i) = 0$. The remaining term gives:

$$
\sum_\alpha (-\nabla_{\mathbf{r}_\alpha} V) \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}_\alpha}{\partial q^i} + \frac{\partial V}{\partial q^i} = 0,
$$

which holds identically by the chain rule ($\partial V / \partial q^i = \sum_\alpha \nabla_{\mathbf{r}_\alpha} V \cdot \partial \mathbf{r}_\alpha / \partial q^i$). The constraint forces cancel automatically. The Euler-Lagrange equations are Newton's second law with the constraints absorbed into the coordinate choice. ∎

### Worked example: the Atwood machine

Two masses $m_1$ and $m_2$ hang from a massless, inextensible rope over a frictionless pulley. The constraint is $x_1 + x_2 = \ell$ (constant rope length), giving one degree of freedom. Choose $q = x_1$ (the downward displacement of $m_1$), so $x_2 = \ell - q$ and $\dot x_2 = -\dot q$.

The Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}(m_1 + m_2)\dot q^2 + (m_1 - m_2)gq$ (plus a constant from the $\ell$ terms). The Euler-Lagrange equation:

$$
(m_1 + m_2)\ddot q - (m_1 - m_2)g = 0 \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \ddot q = \frac{(m_1 - m_2)g}{m_1 + m_2}.
$$

The acceleration is constant: if $m_1 > m_2$, mass 1 descends and mass 2 ascends. The tension $T$ in the rope is not needed for the equation of motion — it is a constraint force that drops out of the Euler-Lagrange framework. If you want $T$, recover it from Newton's second law for either mass: $T = 2m_1 m_2 g / (m_1 + m_2)$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A particle of mass $m$ moves in the plane under the central potential $V(r) = -k/r$ (Kepler problem). Write the Lagrangian in polar coordinates $(r, \theta)$, identify the cyclic coordinate, and write both Euler-Lagrange equations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In polar coordinates, $T = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot r^2 + r^2\dot\theta^2)$. Check whether $\theta$ appears in $L$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot r^2 + r^2\dot\theta^2) + k/r$. The coordinate $\theta$ is cyclic ($\partial L / \partial \theta = 0$), so $p_\theta = mr^2\dot\theta = \ell$ is conserved (angular momentum). The two Euler-Lagrange equations are:

- For $r$: $m\ddot r - mr\dot\theta^2 + k/r^2 = 0$, or $m\ddot r = mr\dot\theta^2 - k/r^2$.
- For $\theta$: $\frac{d}{dt}(mr^2\dot\theta) = 0$, giving $r^2\dot\theta = \text{const}$.

Substituting $\dot\theta = \ell/(mr^2)$ into the radial equation gives $m\ddot r = \ell^2/(m r^3) - k/r^2$, the effective one-dimensional radial equation with centrifugal barrier $\ell^2/(2mr^2)$ built into the dynamics.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

A bead of mass $m$ slides without friction on a wire bent into the shape $y = f(x)$ in a vertical plane, under gravity $g$ (with $y$ upward). Write the Lagrangian in terms of $x$ alone and derive the Euler-Lagrange equation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The constraint $y = f(x)$ gives $\dot y = f'(x)\dot x$. Express $T$ and $V$ in terms of $x$ and $\dot x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$T = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot x^2 + \dot y^2) = \tfrac{1}{2}m(1 + f'^2)\dot x^2$, $V = mgy = mgf(x)$. So $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(1 + f'^2)\dot x^2 - mgf(x)$.

$\partial L / \partial \dot x = m(1 + f'^2)\dot x$. $\partial L / \partial x = m f'f''\dot x^2 - mgf'$. Euler-Lagrange:

$$
m(1 + f'^2)\ddot x + mf'f''\dot x^2 + mgf' = 0.
$$

For the special case $f(x) = x^2/(2R)$ (parabolic wire): $f' = x/R$, $f'' = 1/R$, and the equation becomes $(1 + x^2/R^2)\ddot x + (\dot x^2 x)/R^2 + gx/R = 0$. Near the bottom ($x$ small) this reduces to $\ddot x + gx/R = 0$ — harmonic oscillation with frequency $\sqrt{g/R}$.

</details>

</aside>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$L' = L + (\partial F/\partial q)\dot q + \partial F/\partial t$. Then $\partial L' / \partial \dot q = \partial L / \partial \dot q + \partial F / \partial q$ and $\partial L' / \partial q = \partial L / \partial q + (\partial^2 F / \partial q^2)\dot q + \partial^2 F / \partial q\partial t$. The Euler-Lagrange equation for $L'$:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q} + \frac{\partial F}{\partial q}\right) - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q} - \frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial q^2}\dot q - \frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial q\partial t} = 0.
$$

Expanding the total time derivative: $\frac{d}{dt}(\partial F / \partial q) = (\partial^2 F / \partial q^2)\dot q + \partial^2 F / \partial q\partial t$. These cancel the extra terms, leaving exactly the Euler-Lagrange equation for $L$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

A double pendulum consists of a mass $m_1$ on a rod of length $\ell_1$ attached to a fixed pivot, and a mass $m_2$ on a rod of length $\ell_2$ attached to $m_1$. Using the angles $\theta_1$ and $\theta_2$ from the vertical, write the Lagrangian (you may leave the kinetic energy of $m_2$ in expanded form). Identify any cyclic coordinates.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The position of $m_2$ is $x_2 = \ell_1\sin\theta_1 + \ell_2\sin\theta_2$, $y_2 = -\ell_1\cos\theta_1 - \ell_2\cos\theta_2$. Compute $\dot x_2^2 + \dot y_2^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$T_1 = \tfrac{1}{2}m_1\ell_1^2\dot\theta_1^2$. For $m_2$: $\dot x_2 = \ell_1\dot\theta_1\cos\theta_1 + \ell_2\dot\theta_2\cos\theta_2$, $\dot y_2 = \ell_1\dot\theta_1\sin\theta_1 + \ell_2\dot\theta_2\sin\theta_2$. So $|\dot{\mathbf{r}}_2|^2 = \ell_1^2\dot\theta_1^2 + \ell_2^2\dot\theta_2^2 + 2\ell_1\ell_2\dot\theta_1\dot\theta_2\cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2)$.

$L = \tfrac{1}{2}(m_1 + m_2)\ell_1^2\dot\theta_1^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}m_2\ell_2^2\dot\theta_2^2 + m_2\ell_1\ell_2\dot\theta_1\dot\theta_2\cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2) + (m_1 + m_2)g\ell_1\cos\theta_1 + m_2 g\ell_2\cos\theta_2$.

Neither $\theta_1$ nor $\theta_2$ is cyclic — no conserved momenta. The system is non-integrable for generic initial conditions and exhibits chaotic behaviour, a classic example in nonlinear dynamics.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

A particle of mass $m$ and charge $e$ moves in three dimensions in a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = B\hat{z}$, described by the vector potential $\mathbf{A} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{B}\times\mathbf{q} = \tfrac{1}{2}B(-y, x, 0)$. The Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot{\mathbf{q}}|^2 + e\mathbf{A}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{q}}$. Compute the Euler-Lagrange equations and show they reproduce the Lorentz force $m\ddot{\mathbf{q}} = e\dot{\mathbf{q}}\times\mathbf{B}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write out $L$ in components: $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot x^2 + \dot y^2 + \dot z^2) + \tfrac{1}{2}eB(x\dot y - y\dot x)$. Compute the Euler-Lagrange equations for $x$, $y$, $z$ separately.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot x^2 + \dot y^2 + \dot z^2) + \tfrac{1}{2}eB(x\dot y - y\dot x)$.

For $x$: $\partial L / \partial \dot x = m\dot x - \tfrac{1}{2}eBy$, $\partial L / \partial x = \tfrac{1}{2}eB\dot y$. EL: $m\ddot x - \tfrac{1}{2}eB\dot y - \tfrac{1}{2}eB\dot y = 0$, so $m\ddot x = eB\dot y$.

For $y$: $\partial L / \partial \dot y = m\dot y + \tfrac{1}{2}eBx$, $\partial L / \partial y = -\tfrac{1}{2}eB\dot x$. EL: $m\ddot y + \tfrac{1}{2}eB\dot x + \tfrac{1}{2}eB\dot x = 0$, so $m\ddot y = -eB\dot x$.

For $z$: $\partial L / \partial \dot z = m\dot z$, $\partial L / \partial z = 0$. EL: $m\ddot z = 0$.

Combining: $m\ddot x = e\dot y B$, $m\ddot y = -e\dot x B$, $m\ddot z = 0$. In vector form this is $m\ddot{\mathbf{q}} = e\dot{\mathbf{q}}\times\mathbf{B}$ — the Lorentz force for a uniform magnetic field. The motion in the $xy$-plane is circular with cyclotron frequency $\omega_c = eB/m$, and the $z$-motion is free. The conjugate momenta are $p_x = m\dot x - \tfrac{1}{2}eBy$ and $p_y = m\dot y + \tfrac{1}{2}eBx$ — neither is the mechanical momentum.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

Consider a Lagrangian $L(q, \dot q)$ that does not depend explicitly on time. Prove that the quantity $H = \dot q^i \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i} - L$ is conserved along solutions of the Euler-Lagrange equations. (This is the **energy function**; it becomes the Hamiltonian after the Legendre transform [09.04.01].)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $dH/dt$ along a solution. Use the chain rule for $dL/dt$, substitute the Euler-Lagrange equations, and simplify.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$dH/dt = \frac{d}{dt}(\dot q^i p_i) - dL/dt = \ddot q^i p_i + \dot q^i \dot p_i - dL/dt$. Now $dL/dt = (\partial L / \partial q^i)\dot q^i + (\partial L / \partial \dot q^i)\ddot q^i = \dot p_i \dot q^i + p_i \ddot q^i$ (using the EL equation $\partial L / \partial q^i = \dot p_i$). So $dH/dt = \ddot q^i p_i + \dot q^i \dot p_i - \dot q^i \dot p_i - p_i \ddot q^i = 0$.

For $L = T - V$ with $T$ a homogeneous quadratic in $\dot q$, Euler's theorem on homogeneous functions gives $\dot q^i(\partial T / \partial \dot q^i) = 2T$, so $H = 2T - (T - V) = T + V$ — the total energy. When $T$ is not purely quadratic (e.g., time-dependent constraints), $H$ is still conserved but need not equal $T + V$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, symbolic).**

A spherical pendulum (mass $m$ on a rigid rod of length $\ell$, no restriction to a plane) has Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2(\dot\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,\dot\varphi^2) + mg\ell\cos\theta$. Write the two Euler-Lagrange equations, identify the conserved quantity, and reduce the problem to a single effective equation for $\theta$. Find the effective potential and describe the qualitative motion.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\varphi$ is cyclic — its conjugate momentum is conserved. Substitute the constant angular momentum into the $\theta$ equation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\varphi$ is cyclic: $p_\varphi = m\ell^2\sin^2\theta\,\dot\varphi = \ell_z$ (constant). The Euler-Lagrange equations:

- $\theta$: $m\ell^2\ddot\theta - m\ell^2\sin\theta\cos\theta\,\dot\varphi^2 - mg\ell\sin\theta = 0$.
- $\varphi$: $\frac{d}{dt}(m\ell^2\sin^2\theta\,\dot\varphi) = 0$.

Substituting $\dot\varphi = \ell_z/(m\ell^2\sin^2\theta)$ into the $\theta$ equation:

$$
m\ell^2\ddot\theta = \frac{\ell_z^2\cos\theta}{m\ell^2\sin^3\theta} + mg\ell\sin\theta.
$$

This is $\ddot\theta = -dV_{\rm eff}/d\theta$ where the effective potential is $V_{\rm eff}(\theta) = \ell_z^2/(2m\ell^2\sin^2\theta) - mg\ell\cos\theta$. The first term is a centrifugal barrier (diverges as $\theta \to 0$ or $\theta \to \pi$), and the second is the gravitational potential. The motion in $\theta$ oscillates between two turning points where $E = V_{\rm eff}(\theta)$, while $\varphi$ precesses — the pendulum bob traces a rosette pattern on the sphere.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The Euler-Lagrange equations on a manifold

The coordinate-free statement of the Euler-Lagrange framework uses the geometry of the tangent bundle $TQ$. Let $Q$ be a smooth $n$-dimensional manifold, $TQ$ its tangent bundle with projection $\pi : TQ \to Q$, and $L : TQ \to \mathbb{R}$ a smooth Lagrangian. The **vertical endomorphism** $S : T(TQ) \to T(TQ)$ is defined in local coordinates by $S = dq^i \otimes \frac{\partial}{\partial \dot q^i}$; it projects any tangent vector to $TQ$ onto its vertical component (the part tangent to the fibres). The **Cartan 1-form** (or **Liouville form on $TQ$**) is $\theta_L = S^*(dL) = \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i} dq^i$, and the **Cartan 2-form** is $\omega_L = -d\theta_L$.

A smooth curve $\gamma : [t_1, t_2] \to Q$ lifts to a curve $\dot\gamma : [t_1, t_2] \to TQ$ via $\dot\gamma(t) = (\gamma(t), \dot\gamma(t))$. The **Euler-Lagrange vector field** $\Gamma_L$ on $TQ$ is defined by the condition $i_{\Gamma_L}\omega_L = dE_L$ where $E_L = \dot q^i(\partial L / \partial \dot q^i) - L$ is the energy function. In coordinates, $\Gamma_L = \dot q^i\partial/\partial q^i + \ddot q^i \partial/\partial \dot q^i$ where the $\ddot q^i$ are determined by the Euler-Lagrange equations. The integral curves of $\Gamma_L$ are the physical trajectories.

The regularity condition $\det(\partial^2 L / \partial \dot q^i \partial \dot q^j) \neq 0$ ensures that the Euler-Lagrange equations can be solved for $\ddot q^i$ as functions of $(q, \dot q)$, making $\Gamma_L$ a genuine second-order vector field on $TQ$. For singular Lagrangians (degenerate Hessian), the Euler-Lagrange equations define a *differential-algebraic* system, and the Dirac-Bergmann constraint algorithm is needed to extract a consistent dynamical system.

### Constrained systems and Lagrange multipliers

For holonomic constraints $\phi_a(q) = 0$, $a = 1, \ldots, k$, the constrained Euler-Lagrange equations are

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} = \lambda_a \frac{\partial \phi_a}{\partial q^i},
$$

where $\lambda_a(t)$ are **Lagrange multipliers** — the constraint forces. Combined with the constraints $\phi_a = 0$, this is a system of $n + k$ equations in $n + k$ unknowns $(q^1, \ldots, q^n, \lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k)$. The right-hand side $\lambda_a \partial\phi_a / \partial q^i$ is the generalised force of constraint.

For nonholonomic constraints (constraints on velocities that are not integrable to position constraints, e.g., a disk rolling without slipping), the Lagrange multiplier method still applies but the variational derivation is more subtle — the d'Alembert principle, not Hamilton's principle, is the correct starting point.

### The Euler-Lagrange equations in field theory

The action principle generalises from particle mechanics to classical field theory. For a field $\phi(x)$ (which may have multiple components) on spacetime, the Lagrangian density $\mathcal{L}(\phi, \partial_\mu\phi, x)$ replaces $L$, and the action is $S[\phi] = \int \mathcal{L}\, d^4x$. The Euler-Lagrange equations become

$$
\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\phi^a} - \frac{\partial}{\partial x^\mu}\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial(\partial_\mu\phi^a)} = 0.
$$

This single equation generates Maxwell's equations (from the electromagnetic Lagrangian $\mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{4}F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}$), the Klein-Gordon equation (from $\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}\partial_\mu\phi\,\partial^\mu\phi - \tfrac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2$), the Dirac equation, and the Einstein field equations (from the Einstein-Hilbert action $\mathcal{L} = \sqrt{-g}\,R$). The field-theoretic Euler-Lagrange equations are developed in [10.09.01].

### Second-order Euler-Lagrange equations and the Helmholtz conditions

Expanding the total time derivative $\frac{d}{dt}(\partial L / \partial \dot q^i)$ gives the **second-order form**:

$$
\frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial \dot q^i \partial \dot q^j}\ddot q^j + \frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial \dot q^i \partial q^j}\dot q^j + \frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial \dot q^i \partial t} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} = 0.
$$

The Hessian matrix $W_{ij} = \partial^2 L / \partial \dot q^i \partial \dot q^j$ determines whether the equations can be solved for the accelerations $\ddot q^j$. If $W$ is invertible (the **regular** or **non-degenerate** case), $\ddot q^j = W^{ji}(\partial L / \partial q^i - \partial^2 L / \partial \dot q^i \partial q^j \dot q^j - \partial^2 L / \partial \dot q^i \partial t)$, and the Euler-Lagrange equations define a unique second-order flow on $TQ$. If $W$ is singular, the system is constrained in phase space — the Dirac-Bergmann theory of constrained Hamiltonian systems handles this case.

The inverse problem — given a system of second-order ODEs $E_i(q, \dot q, \ddot q) = 0$, does there exist a Lagrangian $L$ with $E_i = $ the Euler-Lagrange equations? — is answered by the **Helmholtz conditions**: a system is variational (derivable from a Lagrangian) if and only if it satisfies certain integrability conditions. For one degree of freedom the condition is always satisfiable (the Jacobi last-multiplier method). For $n \geq 2$ degrees of freedom the Helmholtz conditions are nontrivial and generically restrictive.

### Degenerate Lagrangians and gauge systems

Gauge theories (electromagnetism, Yang-Mills, general relativity) in their natural Lagrangian formulations have degenerate Hessians — the Hessian rank is less than $\dim Q$ because gauge symmetries produce zero eigenvalues. The Euler-Lagrange equations for such systems do not uniquely determine $\ddot q$; some degrees of freedom remain undetermined (the gauge freedoms). The Dirac-Bergmann algorithm identifies the constraints, classifies them as first-class (generating gauge transformations) or second-class (removable by gauge fixing), and constructs a reduced phase space on which the dynamics is deterministic.

## Connections [Master]

- **Action principle [09.02.01]** — the Euler-Lagrange equations are the differential-equation consequence of the stationary-action condition; this unit takes the variational principle and turns it into concrete ODEs.

- **Newton's laws [09.01.02]** — the Euler-Lagrange equations reproduce $F = ma$ in Cartesian coordinates for the standard Lagrangian $L = T - V$; they generalise Newton's second law to arbitrary coordinate systems with built-in constraint handling.

- **Noether's theorem [09.03.01]** — applies the Euler-Lagrange framework to prove that continuous symmetries of $L$ generate conserved quantities; the cyclic-coordinate observation in this unit is the simplest case.

- **Legendre transform [09.04.01]** — converts the second-order Euler-Lagrange system on $TQ$ into the first-order Hamilton's equations on $T^*Q$ via the conjugate momenta $p_i = \partial L / \partial \dot q^i$ introduced here.

- **Hamilton's equations [09.04.02]** — the Hamiltonian reformulation of the Euler-Lagrange equations, obtained by Legendre transforming the Lagrangian; the two formulations are equivalent for hyper-regular Lagrangians.

- **Conservation laws [09.01.03]** — energy conservation (from the Beltrami identity) and momentum conservation (from cyclic coordinates) are direct consequences of the Euler-Lagrange equations and the structure of $L$.

- **Classical field theory [10.09.01]** (pending) — the field-theoretic Euler-Lagrange equations generalise the particle version from curves in $Q$ to field configurations on spacetime.

- **Path integrals [12.10.01]** (pending) — the classical path in the Feynman path integral is identified as the stationary-action path satisfying the Euler-Lagrange equations; quantum fluctuations are deviations from this path.

- **Geometric mechanics [09.09.01]** (pending) — the coordinate-free formulation of the Euler-Lagrange equations on the tangent bundle $TQ$, with the Cartan 2-form $\omega_L$ and the Euler-Lagrange vector field $\Gamma_L$.

- **Calculus of variations [02.14.01]** (pending) — the Euler-Lagrange equations were originally derived for general variational problems (shortest path, minimal surface, brachistochrone), of which mechanics is a particular case.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The Euler-Lagrange equations have a dual authorship spanning half a century. Euler, in his *Methodus inveniendi lineas curvas maximi minimive proprietate gaudentes* (1744), derived the general differential equation for extremals of a variational problem $\int f(y, y', x)\, dx = 0$, producing $f_y - \frac{d}{dx}f_{y'} = 0$ — what we now call the Euler equation for a single-variable variational problem. Euler's derivation used polygonal approximations to the curve, discretising the integral and passing to the continuum limit.

Lagrange, at age 19 in 1755, wrote to Euler with a far more elegant derivation using the modern method of introducing a variation $\delta y$, integrating by parts, and using the arbitrariness of $\delta y$ to conclude the pointwise condition. Euler recognised the superiority of Lagrange's approach and adopted it, renaming the method "the calculus of variations" (a name Lagrange had coined). Lagrange then spent the next three decades building this variational framework into a complete reformulation of mechanics, published as *Mecanique analytique* (1788) — a treatise famously containing not a single diagram, consisting entirely of algebraic and variational arguments.

The term "Euler-Lagrange equations" reflects this joint contribution: Euler's equation for the general variational problem, Lagrange's method of derivation and its systematic application to all of mechanics. The notation $\partial L / \partial \dot q$ for the conjugate momentum and the full multi-coordinate generalisation are due to Lagrange.

The philosophical significance of the Euler-Lagrange equations is that they separate the *kinematics* (the choice of coordinates and the form of $T$) from the *dynamics* (the form of $V$). Given any configuration space and any potential, the equations of motion follow by a single mechanical procedure. This universality — one recipe for all of mechanics — was the decisive argument for the variational approach and the direct ancestor of the Lagrangian formulation of modern field theory.
