## Intuition [Beginner]

Newton's laws say: here are the forces, now compute the acceleration, then integrate twice to get the motion. The action principle says something radically different: of all the paths a particle *could* take between two points at two times, the one it *actually* takes is the path that makes a certain number — the **action** — as small as possible (more precisely, stationary).

What is this number? For each candidate path, you compute the **Lagrangian** at every point along the path. The Lagrangian is kinetic energy minus potential energy: $L = T - V$. The action is the accumulated sum of $L$ over the entire path — for a continuous path, this is $S = $ the area under the $L$-versus-time curve from $t_1$ to $t_2$, which equals the area under the $T$ curve minus the area under the $V$ curve.

Nature picks the path that makes $S$ stationary — meaning that small wiggles of the path do not change $S$ to first order. For a ball thrown in a parabolic arc, the actual parabola is the one path between the throw and the catch for which $S$ is stationary. Every other nearby curve (a slightly higher arc, a slightly lower one) gives a larger action.

Why should you care? Three reasons.

First, the action principle is **equivalent** to Newton's laws for standard mechanical systems. Every Newtonian $F = ma$ problem can be reformulated as "find the path that makes $S$ stationary," and you get the same answer. But the action principle works in situations where Newton's laws are awkward: systems with constraints (a bead on a wire, a pendulum with a rigid rod), systems with many particles, systems in curvilinear coordinates.

Second, the action principle is **coordinate-independent**. Newton's $F = ma$ requires you to choose coordinates and decompose forces into components. The action $S$ is a single number — it does not care what coordinates you compute it in. This makes it the natural starting point for general relativity and quantum field theory.

Third, the action principle is **the bridge to quantum mechanics**. In Feynman's path-integral formulation, every path contributes, weighted by $e^{iS/\hbar}$. In the classical limit ($\hbar \to 0$), the paths near the stationary-action path reinforce each other and all others cancel — recovering the action principle. Classical mechanics is quantum mechanics in the limit where the action is much larger than Planck's constant.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A free particle (no forces, so $V = 0$) moves in one dimension from $x(0) = 0$ to $x(2) = 10$ metres in 2 seconds. Its Lagrangian is $L = T = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot x^2$ (kinetic energy only, no potential). What path $x(t)$ makes the action stationary?

The action is $S = $ the accumulated kinetic energy over 2 seconds. For a straight-line path $x(t) = 5t$ (constant velocity $v = 5$ m/s), the kinetic energy is constant at $\tfrac{1}{2}m(25)$, so $S = \tfrac{1}{2}m \times 25 \times 2 = 25m$ joule-seconds.

Now try a detoured path: $x(t) = 5t + \epsilon\sin(\pi t)$, which also connects the endpoints (the sine term is zero at $t = 0$ and $t = 2$). Its velocity is $\dot x = 5 + \epsilon\pi\cos(\pi t)$, so $\dot x^2 = 25 + 10\epsilon\pi\cos(\pi t) + \epsilon^2\pi^2\cos^2(\pi t)$. The action is:

$S = \tfrac{1}{2}m \times 25 \times 2 + \tfrac{1}{2}m \times 10\epsilon\pi \times 0 + \tfrac{1}{2}m\epsilon^2\pi^2 \times 1 = 25m + 0 + \tfrac{1}{2}m\epsilon^2\pi^2$.

The first-order term vanishes (the cosine term averages to zero over the full interval). The action differs from the straight-line value only at *second order* in $\epsilon$. The straight-line path has **stationary action** — the first variation $\delta S$ is zero.

What this tells us: a free particle, subject to no forces, travels in a straight line at constant speed — Newton's first law recovered from the action principle. The action is stationary (not necessarily minimal) at the physical path. For this particular example it happens to be a minimum; for other systems it can be a saddle point.

**Exercise (medium, numeric).**

A 1 kg particle in a gravitational field with potential $V = -mgx$ (with $g = 9.8$ m/s², force in the $+x$ direction) moves from $x(0) = 0$ to $x(1) = 4.9$ m in 1 second. Compute the Lagrangian $L$ and the action $S$ for the physical (constant-acceleration) path $x(t) = 4.9t^2$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $Q$ be the configuration space of a mechanical system (a smooth manifold of dimension $n$, the number of degrees of freedom). A **path** is a smooth curve $\gamma : [t_1, t_2] \to Q$ with fixed endpoints $\gamma(t_1) = q_1$ and $\gamma(t_2) = q_2$.

The **Lagrangian** is a smooth function $L : TQ \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ on the tangent bundle (position-velocity space) and time. For a standard mechanical system, $L(q, \dot q, t) = T(q, \dot q) - V(q, t)$ where $T$ is the kinetic energy and $V$ the potential energy.

The **action functional** $S : \mathcal{P} \to \mathbb{R}$ is defined on the space $\mathcal{P}$ of smooth paths with fixed endpoints by

$$
S[\gamma] \;:=\; \int_{t_1}^{t_2} L(\gamma(t), \dot\gamma(t), t)\, dt.
$$

**Hamilton's principle of stationary action.** *The physical trajectory of the system is the path $\gamma$ for which the action is stationary under variations that vanish at the endpoints:*

$$
\delta S \;=\; 0 \qquad \text{for all } \delta\gamma \text{ with } \delta\gamma(t_1) = \delta\gamma(t_2) = 0.
$$

To compute $\delta S$, consider a one-parameter family of paths $\gamma_\epsilon(t) = \gamma(t) + \epsilon\,\eta(t)$ where $\eta(t_1) = \eta(t_2) = 0$ and $\epsilon$ is small. The **first variation** is

$$
\delta S \;:=\; \frac{d}{d\epsilon}\bigg|_{\epsilon=0} S[\gamma_\epsilon] \;=\; \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i}\eta^i + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\dot\eta^i\right) dt.
$$

Integrating the second term by parts (the boundary term vanishes because $\eta(t_1) = \eta(t_2) = 0$):

$$
\delta S \;=\; \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\right) \eta^i\, dt.
$$

Since $\eta^i(t)$ is arbitrary (subject to the endpoint conditions), $\delta S = 0$ for all $\eta$ if and only if

$$
\boxed{\;\;\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i} \;=\; 0, \qquad i = 1, \ldots, n.\;\;}
$$

These are the **Euler-Lagrange equations**, derived fully and applied in [09.02.02]. For this unit, the key point is the *principle itself*: the Euler-Lagrange equations are the condition for the action to be stationary.

**The action principle and Newton's second law.** For a single particle in Cartesian coordinates with $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2 - V(\mathbf{r})$, the Euler-Lagrange equations give $m\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = -\nabla V = \mathbf{F}$. So Newton's second law is recovered as a special case of the action principle for the standard Lagrangian $L = T - V$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **The action is not necessarily minimised.** The principle is "stationary action," not "least action." For most simple mechanical systems over short times the stationary path is indeed a minimum, but for the harmonic oscillator with period $T$, paths of duration greater than $T/2$ have the stationary path as a saddle point. The correct terminology is **Hamilton's principle** or **the principle of stationary action**.

- **The Lagrangian is not unique.** If $L$ and $L' = L + \frac{d}{dt}F(q, t)$ differ by a total time derivative, they yield the same Euler-Lagrange equations and the same physical trajectories. This **gauge freedom** in the Lagrangian is important for electromagnetic potentials and gauge field theories.

- **The action principle requires fixed endpoints.** The variation $\delta\gamma$ must vanish at $t_1$ and $t_2$. Free-endpoint variations give additional boundary conditions (natural boundary conditions) but change the variational problem.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Hamilton's principle is equivalent to Newton's second law for standard mechanical systems).** *Let a particle of mass $m$ move in a potential $V(\mathbf{r})$ with no constraints. The action $S[\gamma] = \int_{t_1}^{t_2}\big(\tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot{\mathbf{r}}|^2 - V(\mathbf{r})\big)\, dt$ is stationary at $\gamma$ if and only if $\gamma$ satisfies $m\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = -\nabla V$ (Newton's second law with conservative force).*

**Proof.** The Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot x^2 + \dot y^2 + \dot z^2) - V(x, y, z)$. The Euler-Lagrange equation for the $x$-component is:

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial x} - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot x} \;=\; -\frac{\partial V}{\partial x} - \frac{d}{dt}(m\dot x) \;=\; -\frac{\partial V}{\partial x} - m\ddot x \;=\; 0.
$$

Rearranging: $m\ddot x = -\partial V/\partial x$. The $y$ and $z$ components give identical equations. Together:

$$
m\ddot{\mathbf{r}} \;=\; -\nabla V \;=\; \mathbf{F},
$$

which is Newton's second law for a conservative force. The converse follows by running the derivation in reverse: if $m\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = -\nabla V$, then the Euler-Lagrange equations hold, and therefore $\delta S = 0$ for all variations. ∎

**Corollary.** *For any system with a standard Lagrangian $L = T(\dot q) - V(q)$, Hamilton's principle reproduces Newton's second law in generalised coordinates. The Euler-Lagrange equations are the coordinate-independent generalisation of $F = ma$.*

The power of this equivalence is that the action principle works in *any* coordinate system without recomputing force components. For a pendulum, writing $L$ in the angular coordinate $\theta$ and applying the action principle gives the equation of motion directly, without resolving forces along and perpendicular to the string.

### Worked example: the brachistochrone

Find the curve $y(x)$ between $(0, 0)$ and $(x_0, y_0)$ (with $y$ positive downward) along which a bead slides frictionlessly under gravity in the least time.

The transit time is $T = \int ds/v$ where $ds = \sqrt{1 + y'^2}\, dx$ and $v = \sqrt{2gy}$ (energy conservation). So the functional to minimise is:

$$
T[y] \;=\; \int_0^{x_0} \sqrt{\frac{1 + y'^2}{2gy}}\, dx.
$$

The integrand $f(y, y') = \sqrt{(1 + y'^2)/(2gy)}$ has no explicit $x$-dependence, so the Beltrami identity gives $f - y'(\partial f/\partial y') = C$. Computing:

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial y'} \;=\; \frac{y'}{\sqrt{2gy(1+y'^2)}}, \qquad f - y'\frac{\partial f}{\partial y'} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2gy(1+y'^2)}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2g}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{y(1+y'^2)}}.
$$

Setting this equal to a constant $1/\sqrt{2ga}$: $y(1+y'^2) = 2a$. Parametrising $y' = \cot(\theta/2)$ gives the **cycloid** $x = a(\theta - \sin\theta)$, $y = a(1 - \cos\theta)$. The brachistochrone is a cycloid — the same curve generated by a point on a rolling wheel. This was the problem that launched the calculus of variations (Johann Bernoulli, 1696).

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Write the Lagrangian for a simple pendulum (mass $m$, length $\ell$, angle $\theta$ from vertical, gravity $g$). Identify the kinetic and potential energy contributions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The bob moves on a circle of radius $\ell$. Express $T$ and $V$ in terms of $\theta$ and $\dot\theta$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$T = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2\dot\theta^2$. $V = -mg\ell\cos\theta$ (zero at the pivot, negative at the bottom). $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2\dot\theta^2 + mg\ell\cos\theta$. The angular coordinate $\theta$ is the single generalised coordinate; the constraint "rod of length $\ell$" is built into the coordinate choice, not imposed as an extra force.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that adding a total derivative $dF(q,t)/dt$ to the Lagrangian does not change the Euler-Lagrange equations. (This is the gauge freedom of the Lagrangian.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the change in the action: $\Delta S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{dF}{dt}\, dt$. What does this equal?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\Delta S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \frac{dF}{dt}\, dt = F(q(t_2), t_2) - F(q(t_1), t_1)$. This depends only on the fixed endpoints, not on the path in between. So $\delta(\Delta S) = 0$ for all variations that vanish at the endpoints. The Euler-Lagrange equations from $L + dF/dt$ are identical to those from $L$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify directly that the straight-line path $x(t) = x_1 + (x_2 - x_1)(t - t_1)/(t_2 - t_1)$ is a stationary point of $S = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot x^2\, dt$ by computing the first variation for a perturbation $x(t) + \epsilon\eta(t)$ with $\eta(t_1) = \eta(t_2) = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\dot x$ for the straight line (it is constant), then expand $S[\gamma_\epsilon]$ to first order in $\epsilon$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $x(t) = x_1 + c(t - t_1)$ with $c = (x_2 - x_1)/(t_2 - t_1)$, $\dot x = c$. The perturbed path: $\dot x_\epsilon = c + \epsilon\dot\eta$. $S_\epsilon = \tfrac{1}{2}m\int_{t_1}^{t_2}(c^2 + 2c\epsilon\dot\eta + \epsilon^2\dot\eta^2)\, dt$. First variation: $\delta S = mc\epsilon\int_{t_1}^{t_2}\dot\eta\, dt = mc\epsilon[\eta(t_2) - \eta(t_1)] = 0$. The boundary conditions kill the first-variation term exactly. So $\delta S = 0$ for any $\eta$ — the straight line is stationary.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A particle of mass $m$ moves on the surface of a cone with half-angle $\alpha$ (tip at origin, axis along $z$) in a gravitational field $V = mgz$. Write the Lagrangian in the coordinates $(r, \varphi)$ where $r$ is the distance from the tip and $\varphi$ the azimuthal angle. (The constraint $z = r\cos\alpha$ is built in.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On the cone, the particle has $z = r\cos\alpha$ and the tangential displacement is $ds^2 = dr^2 + r^2\sin^2\alpha\, d\varphi^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$T = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot r^2 + r^2\sin^2\alpha\,\dot\varphi^2)$. $V = mgr\cos\alpha$. $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot r^2 + r^2\sin^2\alpha\,\dot\varphi^2) - mgr\cos\alpha$. Two degrees of freedom ($r$ and $\varphi$); $\varphi$ is cyclic, so angular momentum is conserved — an immediate consequence of the action principle without ever computing a force component.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

For the pendulum Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2\dot\theta^2 + mg\ell\cos\theta$, compute the action for the physical path $\theta(t) = \theta_0\cos\omega t$ (small oscillation approximation) over one quarter-period $T/4 = \pi/(2\omega)$, where $\omega = \sqrt{g/\ell}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute the path into $L$, expand to second order in $\theta_0$ (small-angle), and integrate.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\dot\theta = -\theta_0\omega\sin\omega t$. $T = \tfrac{1}{2}m\ell^2\theta_0^2\omega^2\sin^2\omega t = \tfrac{1}{2}mg\ell\theta_0^2\sin^2\omega t$ (using $\omega^2 = g/\ell$). The Lagrangian is $L = T + mg\ell\cos\theta$ (as stated, with $V = -mg\ell\cos\theta$). For small $\theta_0$: $\cos\theta \approx 1 - \theta^2/2$, so $mg\ell\cos\theta \approx mg\ell - \tfrac{1}{2}mg\ell\theta_0^2\cos^2\omega t$. Thus $L \approx mg\ell + \tfrac{1}{2}mg\ell\theta_0^2(\sin^2\omega t - \cos^2\omega t) = mg\ell - \tfrac{1}{2}mg\ell\theta_0^2\cos 2\omega t$. Integrating over $[0, \pi/(2\omega)]$: $S = mg\ell \cdot \tfrac{\pi}{2\omega} - \tfrac{1}{2}mg\ell\theta_0^2 \cdot [\sin 2\omega t / (2\omega)]_0^{\pi/(2\omega)} = \tfrac{\pi mg\ell}{2\omega} - 0 = \tfrac{\pi mg\ell}{2\omega} = \tfrac{\pi}{2}m\sqrt{g\ell^3}$. The oscillating $\theta_0^2$ term integrates to zero over a quarter period, but the constant $mg\ell$ contributes $\tfrac{\pi mg\ell}{2\omega}$. (The constant can be removed by shifting the potential zero to the bottom of the swing — $V \approx \tfrac{1}{2}mg\ell\theta^2$ instead of $V = -mg\ell\cos\theta$ — which is a gauge transformation $L \to L + d(mg\ell\,t)/dt$ and shifts $S$ by a fixed amount without changing the equations of motion.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the Beltrami identity: if the Lagrangian $L(q, \dot q)$ has no explicit time dependence, then the quantity $H = \dot q^i \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i} - L$ is conserved along solutions of the Euler-Lagrange equations. (This is a precursor to the Hamiltonian, developed in [09.04.01].)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $dL/dt$ along a solution using the chain rule, then substitute the Euler-Lagrange equations.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$dL/dt = (\partial L/\partial q^i)\dot q^i + (\partial L/\partial \dot q^i)\ddot q^i + \partial L/\partial t$. With $\partial L/\partial t = 0$ and the Euler-Lagrange equation $\partial L/\partial q^i = \frac{d}{dt}(\partial L/\partial \dot q^i)$:

$dL/dt = \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\right)\dot q^i + \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\ddot q^i = \frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\dot q^i\right)$.

So $dL/dt = \frac{d}{dt}(\dot q^i \partial L/\partial \dot q^i)$, which rearranges to $\frac{d}{dt}(\dot q^i \partial L/\partial \dot q^i - L) = 0$. The conserved quantity $H = \dot q^i p_i - L$ (with $p_i = \partial L/\partial \dot q^i$) is the **energy function** — the precursor of the Hamiltonian. For $L = T - V$ with $T$ quadratic in $\dot q$ and $V$ independent of $\dot q$, $H = T + V$ is the total energy.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The shortest-distance functional between two points in the plane is $S[y] = \int_{x_1}^{x_2} \sqrt{1 + y'^2}\, dx$. Show that the Euler-Lagrange equation for this functional is the equation of a straight line.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The integrand $f(y, y') = \sqrt{1 + y'^2}$ has no explicit $y$-dependence, so the Euler-Lagrange equation simplifies: $\partial f/\partial y' = C$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$f = \sqrt{1 + y'^2}$. Since $\partial f/\partial y = 0$, the Euler-Lagrange equation reduces to $\frac{d}{dx}(\partial f/\partial y') = 0$, i.e., $\partial f/\partial y' = C$. Computing: $y'/\sqrt{1 + y'^2} = C$, so $y'^2 = C^2(1 + y'^2)$, giving $y'^2(1 - C^2) = C^2$, thus $y' = \text{const}$. Integrating: $y = ax + b$, a straight line. The shortest distance between two points in a Euclidean plane is a straight line — a geometric fact recovered from the variational principle.

## The action principle in geometric and quantum contexts [Master]

The intermediate-tier treatment works in coordinates. The coordinate-free version lifts the action functional to the space of smooth paths in the configuration manifold $Q$.

**The variational principle on manifolds.** Let $\gamma : [t_1, t_2] \to Q$ be a smooth curve. A **variation** of $\gamma$ is a smooth map $\Gamma : (-\epsilon, \epsilon) \times [t_1, t_2] \to Q$ with $\Gamma(0, t) = \gamma(t)$ and $\Gamma(s, t_1) = \gamma(t_1)$, $\Gamma(s, t_2) = \gamma(t_2)$ for all $s$. The **variation field** $V(t) = \partial\Gamma/\partial s|_{s=0}$ is a vector field along $\gamma$ vanishing at the endpoints. The first variation is

$$
\delta S \;=\; \int_{t_1}^{t_2} \left(\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} - \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\right) V^i\, dt \;=\; 0
$$

in any local coordinates, and by the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations, the Euler-Lagrange equations hold in every coordinate chart.

**The second variation and stability.** The **second variation** $\delta^2 S$ determines whether the stationary path is a minimum, maximum, or saddle point. For the standard Lagrangian $L = T - V$ on a Riemannian manifold, $\delta^2 S$ is related to the **Jacobi equation** for geodesic deviations. Paths for which $\delta^2 S > 0$ for all variations are **conjugate-point-free** — the exponential map is a local diffeomorphism near such paths, and the action is genuinely minimised. The appearance of conjugate points marks the transition from minimum to saddle.

**Hamilton-Jacobi connection.** The action $S$, regarded as a function of the endpoint $q_2$ and the upper time limit $t_2$ (with $q_1$ and $t_1$ fixed), satisfies the **Hamilton-Jacobi equation**

$$
\frac{\partial S}{\partial t_2} + H\left(q_2, \frac{\partial S}{\partial q_2}\right) = 0.
$$

This is the link between the Lagrangian (variational) and Hamiltonian (PDE) viewpoints. The action is the generating function of the time-evolution map, and the Hamilton-Jacobi equation is the PDE that this generating function satisfies. This connection is developed in [09.05.01].

**Quantum-mechanical path integral.** Feynman's 1948 formulation replaces the classical statement "the physical path has stationary action" with the quantum statement "the probability amplitude is a sum over all paths, weighted by $e^{iS/\hbar}$":

$$
\langle q_2 | q_1 \rangle \;=\; \int_{\mathcal{P}} e^{iS[\gamma]/\hbar}\, \mathcal{D}\gamma.
$$

In the classical limit $S \gg \hbar$, the phase $e^{iS/\hbar}$ oscillates rapidly for paths where $S$ is not stationary, and these paths cancel by destructive interference. Only near the stationary-action path do nearby paths have nearly the same phase and contribute constructively. This is the **stationary-phase approximation** for the path integral, and it recovers the classical action principle in the limit $\hbar \to 0$.

**Field-theoretic generalisation.** The action principle extends from particle paths to field configurations. For a scalar field $\phi(x)$ on spacetime, $S[\phi] = \int \mathcal{L}(\phi, \partial_\mu\phi)\, d^4x$, and the Euler-Lagrange equation becomes $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\phi - \partial_\mu(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial(\partial_\mu\phi)) = 0$. This is the starting point for all classical and quantum field theories, including the electromagnetic Lagrangian for Maxwell's equations [10.09.01] and the Standard Model Lagrangian for particle physics [12.11.01].

## Connections [Master]

- **Newton's laws [09.01.02]** are recovered from the action principle in Cartesian coordinates with the standard Lagrangian $L = T - V$.

- **Conservation laws [09.01.03]** are re-derived from the action principle via the Beltrami identity (energy conservation from time-translation invariance) and cyclic coordinates (momentum conservation from translation invariance in a coordinate).

- **Euler-Lagrange equations [09.02.02]** are the differential-equation form of the stationary-action condition derived in this unit.

- **Noether's theorem [09.03.01]** generalises the Beltrami identity and cyclic-coordinate observations: every continuous symmetry of $L$ produces a conserved quantity.

- **Legendre transform [09.04.01]** converts the Lagrangian $L(q, \dot q)$ to the Hamiltonian $H(q, p)$, and the action principle on $TQ$ to Hamilton's equations on $T^*Q$.

- **Classical field theory [10.09.01]** (pending) lifts the action principle from particle paths to field configurations, yielding Maxwell's equations from the EM Lagrangian.

- **Path integrals [12.10.01]** (pending) replace the classical stationary-action selection with a quantum sum over all paths.

- **Philosophy of physics [20.07.01]** (pending) — the teleological character of the action principle ("nature chooses the best path") is a focal point for debates about determinism and final causes.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The action principle originated not in mechanics but in optics. Fermat (1662) proposed that light follows the path of least time between two points (Fermat's principle), explaining refraction. Maupertuis (1744) conjectured a similar principle for mechanics — "nature acts by the shortest path" — but his formulation was vague and his priority dispute with Konig was acrimonious.

Euler, responding to Maupertuis, formalised the variational calculus in *Methodus inveniendi lineas curvas maximi minimive proprietate gaudentes* (1744), deriving what are now called the Euler equations for variational problems. Lagrange, in *Mecanique analytique* (1788), reformulated all of mechanics as the consequence of a single variational principle — the principle of virtual work extended to dynamics via d'Alembert's principle — and derived the general equations of motion (the Euler-Lagrange equations in modern terminology) without drawing a single diagram.

Hamilton, in his two papers on dynamics (1834, 1835), unified the optical and mechanical variational principles by introducing the action $S$ as a function of both endpoints and time, deriving the Hamilton-Jacobi equation, and recognising the analogy with the eikonal equation of geometric optics. This optical-mechanical analogy was the direct inspiration for de Broglie's matter waves (1924) and Schrodinger's wave equation (1926), which was discovered by analogy with the Hamilton-Jacobi equation.

The philosophical content of the action principle is distinctive. Unlike Newton's laws, which are local (force at a point determines acceleration at that point), the action principle is *global* (the entire path is selected by a condition on the whole trajectory). This teleological character — nature "knows" the endpoint and "chooses" accordingly — troubled Maupertuis's contemporaries and remains a subject of philosophical analysis. The resolution in quantum mechanics is that the action principle is an approximation: all paths contribute, and the stationary-action path dominates because of phase coherence, not because nature "chooses" it.
