## Intuition [Beginner]

Picture a guitar string. At rest, it is a straight line. Pluck it, and a wave travels along the string, bounces off the fixed ends, and produces the note you hear.

A guitar string is not a single particle. It is a continuous object — a one-dimensional medium with a mass spread along its length. To describe its motion, you need a **field**: a quantity defined at every point along the string at every moment in time. The field is the displacement $u(x, t)$ — how far the string has moved from its rest position at position $x$ and time $t$.

The key insight is that the Lagrangian framework extends naturally from particles to fields. For a single particle, the Lagrangian $L = T - V$ is a function of position, velocity, and time. For a field, you replace the particle Lagrangian with a **Lagrangian density** — the Lagrangian per unit length (or per unit area, or per unit volume, depending on the dimension). The total Lagrangian is the integral of the density over the whole system.

The Euler-Lagrange equations then become **partial** differential equations (PDEs) instead of ordinary differential equations (ODEs). For the vibrating string, the Euler-Lagrange equation for the field $u(x, t)$ is the **wave equation**: the second time derivative of $u$ equals the wave speed squared times the second spatial derivative of $u$. This single equation describes all wave motion on the string.

## Worked example [Beginner]

An elastic string of length $L$ and mass per unit length $\mu$ is fixed at both ends and stretched with tension $\tau$. At rest the string lies along the $x$-axis. When plucked, the transverse displacement is $u(x, t)$.

The kinetic energy of a small piece of string of length $dx$ is $\tfrac{1}{2}(\mu\, dx)\dot{u}^2$. The potential energy stored in the stretching of that piece is approximately $\tfrac{1}{2}\tau(u')^2\, dx$ (where $u' = du/dx$) for small displacements. Adding up all the pieces along the string gives the total kinetic energy $T$ (a sum of $\tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2$ over all $dx$ segments) and the total potential energy $V$ (a sum of $\tfrac{1}{2}\tau(u')^2$ over all $dx$ segments).

The Lagrangian is $L = T - V$, which is the sum over all pieces of the quantity $\tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}\tau(u')^2$. This quantity in brackets is the **Lagrangian density**: $\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}\tau(u')^2$.

For a string of length $L = 1$ m, mass density $\mu = 0.01$ kg/m, and tension $\tau = 100$ N, the wave speed is $c = \sqrt{\tau/\mu} = 100$ m/s. The fundamental frequency is $c/(2L) = 50$ Hz — a low A note.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**From discrete to continuum.** Consider $N$ masses $m$ connected by springs of constant $k$ and equilibrium spacing $a$. The displacement of the $i$-th mass from equilibrium is $q_i(t)$. The Lagrangian is

$$
L \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^N \left[\frac{1}{2}m\dot{q}_i^2 - \frac{1}{2}k(q_{i+1} - q_i)^2\right].
$$

Define the mass density $\mu = m/a$, the elastic modulus $\kappa = ka$, the displacement field $u(x_i, t) = q_i(t)$, and the spatial coordinate $x_i = ia$. The finite difference becomes $q_{i+1} - q_i \approx a\,\partial u/\partial x$. Substituting:

$$
L \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^N a\left[\frac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 - \frac{1}{2}\kappa\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\right)^2\right] \;\xrightarrow{a \to 0}\; \int_0^L \mathcal{L}\left(u, \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}, \dot{u}, x, t\right)\, dx,
$$

where the **Lagrangian density** is

$$
\mathcal{L} \;=\; \frac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 - \frac{1}{2}\kappa\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\right)^2.
$$

**Euler-Lagrange equations for fields.** For a Lagrangian density $\mathcal{L}(\phi, \partial_\mu\phi, x^\mu)$ depending on a field $\phi$ and its first derivatives, the action is $S[\phi] = \int \mathcal{L}\, d^{n+1}x$ (integrating over space and time). Requiring $\delta S = 0$ for arbitrary compactly-supported variations $\delta\phi$ gives the **Euler-Lagrange field equations**:

$$
\boxed{\;\;\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\phi} - \frac{\partial}{\partial x^\mu}\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial(\partial_\mu\phi)} \;=\; 0.\;\;}
$$

In 1+1 dimensions (one space, one time), with $\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu(\partial u/\partial t)^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa(\partial u/\partial x)^2$:

$$
\mu\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} - \kappa\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \;=\; 0,
$$

the **wave equation** with speed $c = \sqrt{\kappa/\mu}$.

**Higher-dimensional fields.** In $d$ spatial dimensions, the Lagrangian density depends on $\phi$ and $\nabla\phi$, and the Euler-Lagrange equation becomes

$$
\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\phi} - \nabla\cdot\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial(\nabla\phi)} - \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\dot\phi} \;=\; 0.
$$

For an elastic membrane (2D drumhead) with displacement $u(x, y, t)$ and surface tension $\sigma$:

$$
\mu\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} - \sigma\nabla^2 u \;=\; 0,
$$

the two-dimensional wave equation.

### The elastic rod: longitudinal vibrations

An elastic rod of cross-section $A$, Young's modulus $E$, and density $\rho$ undergoes longitudinal displacement $u(x, t)$. The strain is $\epsilon = \partial u/\partial x$ and the stress is $\sigma = E\epsilon$. The Lagrangian density is

$$
\mathcal{L} \;=\; \frac{1}{2}\rho A\dot{u}^2 - \frac{1}{2}EA\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\right)^2,
$$

giving the wave equation $\rho\ddot{u} = E\,\partial^2 u/\partial x^2$ with longitudinal wave speed $c_L = \sqrt{E/\rho}$. For steel ($E \approx 200$ GPa, $\rho \approx 8000$ kg/m$^3$), $c_L \approx 5000$ m/s.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Key result (Wave equation from the Euler-Lagrange field equations).** *The one-dimensional wave equation is the Euler-Lagrange equation for the Lagrangian density $\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa(u')^2$.*

**Proof.** The Lagrangian density depends on the field $u$, its time derivative $\dot{u} = \partial u/\partial t$, and its spatial derivative $u' = \partial u/\partial x$. The action is

$$
S[u] \;=\; \int_{t_0}^{t_1}\int_0^L \mathcal{L}(u, \dot{u}, u', x, t)\, dx\, dt.
$$

Varying $u \to u + \delta u$ with $\delta u$ vanishing on the boundary of the space-time domain:

$$
\delta S \;=\; \int\int \left[\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\dot{u}}\delta\dot{u} + \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial u'}\delta u' + \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial u}\delta u\right] dx\, dt.
$$

Integrating by parts in $t$ for the first term and $x$ for the second:

$$
\delta S \;=\; \int\int \left[-\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\dot{u}} - \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial u'} + \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial u}\right]\delta u\, dx\, dt.
$$

The boundary terms vanish because $\delta u = 0$ on the boundary. Since $\delta u$ is arbitrary in the interior:

$$
\frac{\partial}{\partial t}\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\dot{u}} + \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial u'} - \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial u} \;=\; 0.
$$

Substituting $\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa(u')^2$: $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\dot{u} = \mu\dot{u}$, $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial u' = -\kappa u'$, $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial u = 0$. Therefore:

$$
\mu\ddot{u} - \kappa u'' \;=\; 0. \quad\square
$$

**Worked example: normal modes of the string.** For fixed boundary conditions $u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0$, seek solutions of the form $u(x,t) = X(x)T(t)$. Substituting into $\mu\ddot{u} = \kappa u''$ and dividing by $XT$: $\mu\ddot{T}/T = \kappa X''/X = -\mu\omega^2$ (separation constant). This gives $X'' + (\mu\omega^2/\kappa)X = 0$ with $X(0) = X(L) = 0$. The solutions are $X_n(x) = \sin(n\pi x/L)$ with frequencies $\omega_n = n\pi c/L$ where $c = \sqrt{\kappa/\mu}$.

**Bridge.** The field Lagrangian builds toward the full Lagrangian field theories of continuum mechanics and quantum field theory; the foundational reason is that the continuum limit of discrete systems produces a Lagrangian density whose Euler-Lagrange equation is a PDE governing the field dynamics. This is exactly the structure that quantum field theory inherits — the scalar field Lagrangian for the Klein-Gordon equation differs from the elastic string Lagrangian only by a mass term. The central insight — that the wave equation is a variational equation — appears again in the treatment of elastic media in three dimensions [09.07.01], and putting these together, the continuum limit provides the bridge from particle mechanics to field physics.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the Lagrangian density $\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa(u')^2$ has dimensions of energy per unit length.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Check that each term has units of [mass][velocity]$^2$/[length].

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mu$ has units kg/m, $\dot{u}$ has units m/s, so $\mu\dot{u}^2$ has units kg$\cdot$m$^2$/s$^2\cdot$(1/m) = J/m. Similarly $\kappa$ has units N = kg$\cdot$m/s$^2$, $(u')^2$ is dimensionless (m/m), so $\kappa(u')^2$ has units N = J/m. Both terms have units of energy per unit length.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Verify that $u(x,t) = f(x - ct) + g(x + ct)$ satisfies the wave equation $\mu\ddot{u} = \kappa u''$ with $c = \sqrt{\kappa/\mu}$ for any twice-differentiable functions $f$ and $g$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\ddot{u}$ and $u''$ using the chain rule.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $u = f(x - ct)$: $\dot{u} = -cf'$, $\ddot{u} = c^2 f''$; $u' = f'$, $u'' = f''$. So $\mu\ddot{u} = \mu c^2 f'' = \kappa f'' = \kappa u''$. Similarly for $g(x+ct)$. By linearity, $f + g$ is also a solution. This is d'Alembert's general solution: right-moving wave $f$ plus left-moving wave $g$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Add a restoring force (Hooke's law per unit length) to the string by including a potential term $\tfrac{1}{2}\alpha u^2$ in the Lagrangian density. Derive the field equation and identify the dispersion relation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa(u')^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}\alpha u^2$. The new term contributes $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial u = -\alpha u$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Euler-Lagrange equation becomes $\mu\ddot{u} - \kappa u'' + \alpha u = 0$. For a plane-wave ansatz $u = e^{i(kx - \omega t)}$: $-\mu\omega^2 + \kappa k^2 + \alpha = 0$, giving $\omega^2 = \kappa k^2/\mu + \alpha/\mu = c^2 k^2 + \omega_0^2$, where $\omega_0 = \sqrt{\alpha/\mu}$ is the natural frequency. The dispersion relation $\omega = \sqrt{c^2 k^2 + \omega_0^2}$ shows that the phase velocity $\omega/k$ depends on $k$ — the medium is dispersive. This is the Klein-Gordon equation (classical version) with "mass" $\omega_0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

A heavy elastic string hangs vertically under gravity. Write the Lagrangian density including the gravitational potential, and derive the equation of motion for transverse vibrations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The gravitational potential energy per unit length is $\mu g u$ (transverse displacement against gravity is zero for vertical hanging — use the longitudinal displacement instead). Actually, for a vertical string with transverse displacement $u(x,t)$, the gravitational potential doesn't depend on $u$ to first order.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a vertical string with coordinate $x$ measured downward from the top, the tension at position $x$ is $T(x) = \mu g(L - x)$ (weight of string below). The Lagrangian density is $\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}T(x)(u')^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}\mu g(L-x)(u')^2$. The Euler-Lagrange equation is $\mu\ddot{u} - \partial/\partial x[\mu g(L-x)u'] = 0$, giving $\ddot{u} = g\,\partial/\partial x[(L-x)u'] = g[-u' + (L-x)u'']$. This is a wave equation with position-dependent wave speed $c(x) = \sqrt{g(L-x)}$, faster near the top and zero at the bottom.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the energy $E = \int_0^L [\tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa(u')^2]\, dx$ is conserved for solutions of the wave equation with fixed or free boundary conditions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $dE/dt$ and integrate by parts in $x$, using the wave equation and the boundary conditions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$dE/dt = \int_0^L [\mu\dot{u}\ddot{u} + \kappa u'\dot{u}']\, dx$. Using $\mu\ddot{u} = \kappa u''$ and $\dot{u}' = (\dot{u})'$: $dE/dt = \int_0^L [\kappa u''\dot{u} + \kappa u'(\dot{u})']\, dx = \kappa\int_0^L d/dx[u'\dot{u}]\, dx = \kappa[u'\dot{u}]_0^L$. For fixed ends ($u = 0$ at $x = 0, L$): $\dot{u} = 0$ there, so $dE/dt = 0$. For free ends ($u' = 0$ at boundaries): same result. In either case, energy is conserved.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the Euler-Lagrange equation for the Lagrangian density $\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}|\nabla\phi|^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2$ in three spatial dimensions, where $\phi(\mathbf{x}, t)$ is a scalar field. Identify the resulting PDE.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Euler-Lagrange equation is $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\phi - \nabla\cdot\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial(\nabla\phi) - \partial/\partial t(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\dot\phi) = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\phi = -m^2\phi$, $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial(\nabla\phi) = \nabla\phi$, $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\dot\phi = 0$ (no time derivative in this Lagrangian — add $\tfrac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2$ for dynamics). Including the kinetic term: $\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}\dot\phi^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}|\nabla\phi|^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}m^2\phi^2$. Then $\ddot\phi - \nabla^2\phi + m^2\phi = 0$. This is the **Klein-Gordon equation** — the relativistic wave equation for a scalar field of mass $m$. In the units $c = 1$, the dispersion relation is $\omega^2 = k^2 + m^2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

For the discrete chain of $N$ masses connected by springs, derive the normal-mode frequencies $\omega_n$ and show they converge to the continuum frequencies $n\pi c/L$ in the limit $N \to \infty$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The equations of motion are $m\ddot{q}_i = k(q_{i+1} - 2q_i + q_{i-1})$. Try $q_i = A\sin(n\pi i/N)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $q_i = A\sin(n\pi i/N)\cos(\omega t)$: $q_{i+1} - 2q_i + q_{i-1} = 2A\sin(n\pi i/N)[\cos(n\pi/N) - 1]\cos(\omega t) = -4A\sin(n\pi i/N)\sin^2(n\pi/2N)\cos(\omega t)$. The equation of motion gives $m\omega^2 = 4k\sin^2(n\pi/2N)$, so $\omega_n = 2\sqrt{k/m}\sin(n\pi/2N)$. In the continuum limit $N \to \infty$ with $L = Na$ fixed: $\omega_n = 2\sqrt{k/m}\cdot n\pi/(2N) = n\pi\sqrt{k/(mN^2)} = n\pi\sqrt{ka/(maN^2)} = n\pi\sqrt{\kappa/(\mu L^2)} = n\pi c/L$. The discrete dispersion relation becomes the continuum linear relation as expected.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Compute the stress-energy tensor $T^{\mu\nu}$ for the one-dimensional elastic string Lagrangian density, and show that energy and momentum are conserved.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The stress-energy tensor is $T^{\mu\nu} = (\partial^\mu\phi)(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial(\partial_\nu\phi)) - g^{\mu\nu}\mathcal{L}$. In 1+1 dimensions, $\mu, \nu \in \{0, 1\}$ with $x^0 = t$, $x^1 = x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_0 u)^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}c^2(\partial_1 u)^2$ (setting $\mu = 1$ for simplicity): $T^{00} = \tfrac{1}{2}\dot{u}^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}c^2(u')^2$ (energy density), $T^{01} = -c^2\dot{u}u'$ (energy flux = momentum density times $c^2$), $T^{10} = -\dot{u}u'$ (momentum density), $T^{11} = \tfrac{1}{2}\dot{u}^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}c^2(u')^2$ (stress). Conservation $\partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0$: $\partial_0 T^{00} + \partial_1 T^{01} = 0$ (energy conservation) and $\partial_0 T^{10} + \partial_1 T^{11} = 0$ (momentum conservation). Both follow from the wave equation by direct computation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

A nonlinear string has Lagrangian density $\mathcal{L} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa(u')^2 - \tfrac{1}{4}\beta(u')^4$. Derive the equation of motion and discuss how the nonlinear term affects wave propagation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The nonlinear term modifies the Euler-Lagrange equation. The wave speed becomes amplitude-dependent.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial u' = -\kappa u' - \beta(u')^3$. The Euler-Lagrange equation is $\mu\ddot{u} - \kappa u'' - \beta\partial/\partial x[(u')^3] = \mu\ddot{u} - \kappa u'' - 3\beta(u')^2 u'' = 0$. This can be written as $\mu\ddot{u} = [\kappa + 3\beta(u')^2]u''$. The effective stiffness is $\kappa + 3\beta(u')^2$, so the local wave speed is $c_{\rm eff} = \sqrt{[\kappa + 3\beta(u')^2]/\mu}$. For $\beta > 0$, regions of larger strain propagate faster — the wave steepens on the leading edge, producing shock-like behaviour. For $\beta < 0$, the opposite occurs. This is the classical origin of nonlinear wave phenomena: the wave equation ceases to be linear and superposition fails.

</details>

</aside>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The Euler-Lagrange equations for fields require a calculus of variations on infinite-dimensional function spaces. Mathlib has the finite-dimensional calculus of variations machinery but not the field-theoretic extension. The continuum limit (passage from discrete to continuous) would require assembling Mathlib's measure-theory integration with its finite-dimensional Lagrangian mechanics. The stress-energy tensor derivation requires tangent-space computations on the space of field configurations.

## Advanced results: stress-energy tensor and the continuum in higher dimensions [Master]

### The stress-energy tensor from Noether's theorem

For a field theory with Lagrangian density $\mathcal{L}(\phi, \partial_\mu\phi)$ in $(n+1)$-dimensional spacetime, translation invariance in each spacetime direction produces a conserved current. The full set of $(n+1)$ conserved currents assembles into the **canonical stress-energy tensor**:

$$
T^\mu{}_\nu \;=\; \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial(\partial_\mu\phi)}\partial_\nu\phi - \delta^\mu_\nu\mathcal{L},
$$

satisfying $\partial_\mu T^{\mu\nu} = 0$ on solutions of the Euler-Lagrange equations. The component $T^{00}$ is the energy density, $T^{0i}$ is the momentum density (or energy flux), and $T^{ij}$ is the stress tensor.

For the elastic string: $T^{00} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa(u')^2$ (energy per unit length), $T^{01} = -\kappa\dot{u}u'$ (power flux), and $T^{11} = \tfrac{1}{2}\mu\dot{u}^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\kappa(u')^2$ (tension). The conservation law $\partial_0 T^{00} + \partial_1 T^{01} = 0$ states that the rate of change of energy in a segment equals the net energy flux through its boundaries.

### Three-dimensional elastic media

In three dimensions, the displacement field $\mathbf{u}(\mathbf{x}, t)$ is a vector field. The strain tensor is $\epsilon_{ij} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_i u_j + \partial_j u_i)$ and for an isotropic elastic solid with Lam\'e parameters $\lambda$ and $\mu$, the Lagrangian density is

$$
\mathcal{L} \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\rho|\dot{\mathbf{u}}|^2 - \mu\,\epsilon_{ij}\epsilon_{ij} - \tfrac{1}{2}\lambda(\epsilon_{kk})^2.
$$

The Euler-Lagrange equations give the **Navier equations** of elasticity:

$$
\rho\ddot{\mathbf{u}} \;=\; \mu\nabla^2\mathbf{u} + (\lambda + \mu)\nabla(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}).
$$

These support two types of waves: longitudinal (P-waves, speed $\sqrt{(\lambda + 2\mu)/\rho}$) and transverse (S-waves, speed $\sqrt{\mu/\rho}$). Seismology uses this framework to model earthquake wave propagation through the Earth's interior.

### Gauge fields and field Lagrangians

The simplest gauge field theory — the free electromagnetic field — has Lagrangian density

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\rm EM} \;=\; -\frac{1}{4}F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu},
$$

where $F_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu A_\nu - \partial_\nu A_\mu$ is the electromagnetic field tensor. The Euler-Lagrange equations for $A_\mu$ give Maxwell's equations in vacuum. This is the prototype for all gauge field theories in modern physics, and its Lagrangian structure is a direct descendant of the elastic-string Lagrangian studied here.

**Synthesis.** The continuum limit of discrete Lagrangian systems provides the foundational reason that field theories inherit the variational structure of particle mechanics; the central insight is that the Lagrangian density replaces the particle Lagrangian, and the Euler-Lagrange PDE replaces the Euler-Lagrange ODE. This is exactly the structure that quantum field theory, continuum mechanics, and general relativity all share. Putting these together, the wave equation derived here is the simplest example of a field equation obtained from a variational principle, and the generalisation to higher dimensions, nonlinear media, and gauge fields completes the bridge from classical particle mechanics to modern theoretical physics.

## Connections [Master]

- **[09.07.01]** Continuum mechanics extends the one-dimensional elastic rod to three-dimensional elastic media with tensor-valued strain and stress; the Lagrangian density framework developed here is the starting point.
- **[09.02.02]** The Euler-Lagrange equations for fields generalise the finite-dimensional Euler-Lagrange equations by replacing ordinary derivatives with partial derivatives and sums with integrals.
- **[09.03.01]** Noether's theorem for fields produces the stress-energy tensor as the conserved current associated with spacetime translation invariance; this unit's field Lagrangian provides the concrete setting.
- **[12.03.01]** Quantum field theory begins with the same Lagrangian densities, promoting the fields to operator-valued distributions; the Klein-Gordon Lagrangian studied here is the free scalar field of QFT.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The passage from discrete to continuous mechanics was a central theme of 18th-century mathematical physics. Euler (1744) derived the equations for the vibrating string using a continuum model, while Lagrange (1759) provided the variational foundation. The d'Alembert general solution $u = f(x-ct) + g(x+ct)$ for the wave equation appeared in 1747 and was one of the first PDE solutions ever found.

The Lagrangian density formulation was systematised by Hilbert in 1915 as part of his axiomatic approach to general relativity, where the Einstein-Hilbert action $\int R\sqrt{-g}\,d^4x$ generates the Einstein field equations through the Euler-Lagfield equations for the metric tensor $g_{\mu\nu}$. The same formalism was adopted by Dirac, Heisenberg, and Pauli for quantum field theory in the 1920s and 1930s.

A philosophical point: the continuum limit is not merely a mathematical convenience. The physical question "is matter fundamentally discrete or continuous?" is left open by classical mechanics — the continuum limit produces the same predictions as the discrete model when the wavelength greatly exceeds the lattice spacing. Only at atomic scales does the discrete structure become detectable. The Lagrangian density formalism is agnostic about this question; it applies equally well to fundamental fields (electromagnetism) and to emergent continuum descriptions of discrete systems (elastic solids).
