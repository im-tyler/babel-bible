## Intuition [Beginner]

A mechanical system with $n$ degrees of freedom is **completely integrable** if it has $n$ independent conserved quantities that commute with each other (in the Poisson-bracket sense). When this happens, the motion is constrained to an $n$-dimensional torus in $2n$-dimensional phase space, and the trajectory winds smoothly around this torus forever.

Two examples stand out. The first is the **Kepler problem** — a particle in a $1/r$ gravitational potential. Everyone knows the answer: elliptical orbits. What makes the Kepler problem special is that the orbits *close*: after one radial oscillation the angle returns to its starting value. This is not generic. For a generic central potential the orbit precesses, and the perihelion drifts. The Kepler problem has a "hidden" conserved quantity — the Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector — that forces the orbits to close. This extra conserved quantity makes the Kepler problem **superintegrable**: it has $2n - 1 = 5$ independent conserved quantities on a 6-dimensional phase space, one more than integrability requires.

The second is the **Toda lattice** — a chain of particles connected by nonlinear springs with exponential interaction. Unlike the Kepler problem, the Toda lattice looks generic: nonlinear coupling, no obvious symmetry. Yet it is completely integrable. The discovery by Flaschka (1974) that the Toda lattice admits a "Lax pair" — a matrix $L(t)$ whose eigenvalues are conserved — was a landmark. The conserved quantities are hidden inside the eigenvalues of this matrix, invisible to symmetry analysis. The Toda lattice is the paradigmatic example of a system that is integrable for deep, non-obvious reasons.

## Visual [Beginner]

For the Kepler problem: picture an ellipse with the sun at one focus. The action $I_r$ measures the "size" of the radial oscillation (how elongated the ellipse is), and the action $I_\theta$ is the angular momentum (how much "swing" the orbit has). The angle variable $\theta_r$ tracks the radial phase (where in the in-and-out cycle the planet is), and $\theta_\theta$ tracks the angular phase (where around the orbit the planet is). For the Kepler problem, the radial and angular frequencies are *equal*: $\omega_r = \omega_\theta$, so the orbit closes after one period.

For the Toda lattice: picture a chain of masses connected by springs. For linear springs ($V = kx^2$), the motion is a superposition of normal modes — sinusoidal oscillations at fixed frequencies. This is integrable by Fourier analysis. For the Toda springs ($V = e^{-x}$), the motion supports **solitons**: localised pulses of energy that propagate along the chain without dispersing, passing through each other and emerging unchanged. Solitons are the hallmark of integrability in spatially extended systems.

The Toda lattice supports exact multi-soliton solutions because it is completely integrable.](/img/09.06.03-toda-kepler-comparison-placeholder.svg)

## Worked example [Beginner]

The Kepler problem in the plane: $H = p_r^2/(2m) + \ell^2/(2mr^2) - k/r$, where $\ell$ is the conserved angular momentum. The radial action is

$$
I_r = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint\sqrt{2m\big(E + k/r - \ell^2/(2mr^2)\big)}\,dr = -\ell + k\sqrt{m/(2|E|)}.
$$

The angular action is $I_\theta = \ell$. The total action is $I_r + I_\theta = k\sqrt{m/(2|E|)}$. The Hamiltonian in action-angle variables is

$$
H = -\frac{mk^2}{2(I_r + I_\theta)^2}.
$$

The frequencies are $\omega_r = d H/d I_r = mk^2/(I_r + I_\theta)^3$ and $\omega_\theta = d H/d I_\theta = mk^2/(I_r + I_\theta)^3$. They are *equal*: $\omega_r = \omega_\theta$. This is the **accidental degeneracy** of the Kepler problem. It means the radial and angular motions are synchronised, and the orbit closes after one radial period.

The Kepler problem has one extra conserved quantity — the Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{p} \times \mathbf{L} - mk\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ — which is not accounted for by the $n = 2$ commuting conserved quantities $(H, L)$. This makes it superintegrable, and the consequence is the degeneracy $\omega_r = \omega_\theta$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Complete integrability (Liouville sense)

A Hamiltonian system on $(M^{2n}, \omega)$ is **completely integrable** if there exist $n$ smooth functions $f_1 = H, f_2, \ldots, f_n$ on $M$ such that:

1. The $f_i$ are **functionally independent**: $df_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge df_n \neq 0$ on a dense open set.
2. The $f_i$ are **in involution**: $\{f_i, f_j\} = 0$ for all $i, j$.

By the Liouville-Arnold theorem [09.06.01], the joint level set $M_\mathbf{c} = \{x : f_i(x) = c_i\}$ is (when compact and connected) an $n$-torus $\mathbb{T}^n$, and there exist action-angle coordinates $(I_i, \theta^i)$ in a neighbourhood of $M_\mathbf{c}$ in which the Hamiltonian depends only on the actions: $H = H(I)$.

### Superintegrability

A system is **superintegrable** if it has $2n - k$ independent conserved quantities (for $k = 1$, **maximally superintegrable**) whose algebra closes under the Poisson bracket (possibly with non-zero brackets, forming a non-Abelian symmetry algebra). Maximally superintegrable systems have the property that all bounded orbits are closed.

### The Kepler problem

The Kepler Hamiltonian is

$$
H = \frac{|\mathbf{p}|^2}{2m} - \frac{k}{|\mathbf{q}|}, \qquad k = GMm > 0.
$$

The conserved quantities are:
- Energy $H$ (time-translation symmetry).
- Angular momentum $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{q} \times \mathbf{p}$ (rotation symmetry, 3 components, but $|\mathbf{L}|^2$ and $L_z$ are independent of each other and of $H$).
- Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{p} \times \mathbf{L} - mk\hat{\mathbf{q}}$ (hidden symmetry, 3 components, but $\mathbf{A} \cdot \mathbf{L} = 0$ eliminates one, and $|\mathbf{A}|^2$ is determined by $H$ and $|\mathbf{L}|^2$, leaving one independent component).

Total: 5 independent conserved quantities on a 6-dimensional phase space. The Kepler problem is maximally superintegrable.

The hidden symmetry algebra is $\mathfrak{so}(4)$ for bound orbits ($E < 0$): the rescaled vectors $\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{L}/2$ and $\mathbf{K} = \mathbf{A}/(2\sqrt{-2mE})$ satisfy $\{J_i, J_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk}J_k$, $\{K_i, K_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk}J_k$, $\{J_i, K_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk}K_k$, which is the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{so}(3) \times \mathfrak{so}(3) \cong \mathfrak{so}(4)$.

### The Toda lattice

The **Toda lattice** (periodic, with $N$ particles) is

$$
H = \sum_{j=1}^{N} \frac{p_j^2}{2} + \sum_{j=1}^{N} e^{q_j - q_{j+1}},
$$

where $q_{N+1} = q_1$ (periodic boundary conditions). The interaction is exponential: the force between adjacent particles grows exponentially with compression and decays exponentially with extension.

Flaschka's transformation introduces the variables $a_j = \tfrac{1}{2}e^{(q_j - q_{j+1})/2}$ and $b_j = -p_j/2$. The equations of motion become

$$
\dot{a}_j = a_j(b_{j+1} - b_j), \qquad \dot{b}_j = 2a_j^2 - 2a_{j-1}^2.
$$

These are equivalent to the **Lax equation** $\dot{L} = [B, L]$ where $L$ is the symmetric tridiagonal (Jacobi) matrix with diagonal entries $b_j$ and off-diagonal entries $a_j$, and $B$ is the skew-symmetric matrix with entries $B_{j,j+1} = a_j$, $B_{j+1,j} = -a_j$.

The Lax equation implies that the eigenvalues of $L$ are **conserved**: $d\lambda_k/dt = 0$ for each eigenvalue $\lambda_k$ of $L$. For $N$ particles, there are $N$ eigenvalues, giving $N$ conserved quantities — exactly the number required for complete integrability. The eigenvalues are in involution ($\{\lambda_j, \lambda_k\} = 0$) because they are the coefficients of the characteristic polynomial $\det(L - \lambda I) = 0$, which are symmetric functions of the matrix entries, and symmetric functions of a Lax matrix are automatically in involution.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Key derivation: The Lax equation and isospectrality for the Toda lattice.**

We prove that the eigenvalues of $L$ are conserved under the Toda flow. The argument is a single computation.

Let $L(t)$ satisfy $\dot{L} = [B, L] = BL - LB$. Define $U(t)$ as the solution of $\dot{U} = BU$ with $U(0) = I$. Then

$$
\frac{d}{dt}(U^{-1}LU) = -U^{-1}\dot{U}U^{-1}LU + U^{-1}\dot{L}U + U^{-1}L\dot{U} = -U^{-1}BULU + U^{-1}(BL - LB)U + U^{-1}LBU.
$$

Substituting $\dot{U} = BU$: the first term is $-U^{-1}B\cdot BU^{-1} \cdot LU$... wait, let me use the property that $B$ is skew-symmetric so $U$ is orthogonal. Actually, $U$ satisfies $\dot{U} = BU$, and if $B$ is skew-symmetric then $U(t)$ is orthogonal: $(d/dt)(U^TU) = \dot{U}^TU + U^T\dot{U} = (BU)^TU + U^TBU = U^TB^TU + U^TBU = -U^TBU + U^TBU = 0$.

The key computation: define $\tilde{L}(t) = U(t)^{-1}L(t)U(t)$. Then

$$
\dot{\tilde{L}} = -U^{-1}\dot{U}U^{-1}LU + U^{-1}\dot{L}U + U^{-1}L\dot{U} = -U^{-1}B\cdot\tilde{L}\cdot U + U^{-1}(BL - LB)U + \tilde{L}\cdot U^{-1}\dot{U}.
$$

Since $\dot{U} = BU$ and $U^{-1}\dot{U} = U^{-1}BU$:

$$
\dot{\tilde{L}} = -U^{-1}BU\tilde{L} + U^{-1}BLU - U^{-1}LBU + \tilde{L}U^{-1}BU.
$$

Now $U^{-1}BLU = U^{-1}B \cdot U\tilde{L} = (U^{-1}BU)\tilde{L}$ and $U^{-1}LBU = \tilde{L}(U^{-1}BU)$. So:

$$
\dot{\tilde{L}} = -(U^{-1}BU)\tilde{L} + (U^{-1}BU)\tilde{L} - \tilde{L}(U^{-1}BU) + \tilde{L}(U^{-1}BU) = 0.
$$

Therefore $\tilde{L}(t) = \tilde{L}(0) = L(0)$, which gives $L(t) = U(t)L(0)U(t)^{-1}$. The matrix $L(t)$ is related to $L(0)$ by a similarity transformation, so they have the same eigenvalues. The eigenvalues of $L$ are conserved quantities.

**Bridge.** This derivation builds toward the inverse-scattering method for solving the Toda lattice exactly [09.08.01], where the eigenvalues of the Lax matrix play the role of the action variables in action-angle theory. The foundational reason isospectrality works is the Lax equation structure $\dot{L} = [B, L]$, which is exactly the condition that the flow of $L$ in matrix space is a conjugation by an orthogonal matrix. The Lax-pair construction appears again in the theory of solitons and integrable PDEs, where the KdV equation admits a similar Lax pair. Putting these together, the Toda lattice is the discrete analogue of the KdV equation, and both are integrable because they admit Lax representations whose eigenvalues provide the conserved quantities.

The centre-of-mass degree of freedom ($Q$, $P$) is free: $\dot{P} = 0$, $\dot{Q} = P/4$. The relative degree of freedom ($q$, $p$) has Hamiltonian $h = p^2 + 2\cosh q$. This is a one-dimensional system with one degree of freedom, so it is automatically integrable (energy is the single conserved quantity). The potential $V(q) = 2\cosh q$ has a single minimum at $q = 0$ with $V(0) = 2$, and $V(q) \to \infty$ as $q \to \pm\infty$. All orbits are periodic oscillations in the cosh well.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\text{tr}(L^2) = \sum_{j,k} L_{jk}L_{kj} = \sum_j L_{jj}^2 + 2\sum_{j<k} L_{jk}^2 = b_1^2 + b_2^2 + b_3^2 + 2(a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2)$.

Now $b_j = -p_j/2$ so $\sum b_j^2 = \sum p_j^2/4$. And $a_j = \tfrac{1}{2}e^{(q_j - q_{j+1})/2}$ so $a_j^2 = \tfrac{1}{4}e^{q_j - q_{j+1}}$. Thus $\text{tr}(L^2) = \sum p_j^2/4 + \tfrac{1}{2}\sum e^{q_j - q_{j+1}}$.

The Hamiltonian is $H = \sum p_j^2/2 + \sum e^{q_j - q_{j+1}}$. So $\text{tr}(L^2) = H/2 + \sum e^{q_j - q_{j+1}}/2 = H/2 + \sum a_j^2$.

The Poisson bracket $\{H, \text{tr}(L^2)\}$: since $\text{tr}(L^2) = H/2 + 2\sum a_j^2$... let me just verify that $\{H, \text{tr}(L^2)\} = 0$ directly. $\{H, \text{tr}(L^2)\} = \sum_j [\partial H/\partial p_j \cdot \partial\text{tr}(L^2)/\partial q_j - \partial H/\partial q_j \cdot \partial\text{tr}(L^2)/\partial p_j]$. Using $\partial\text{tr}(L^2)/\partial p_j = \partial(b_j^2)/\partial p_j = 2b_j \cdot (-1/2) = -b_j = p_j/2$ and the complicated $q$-derivatives involving the exponentials, this is a lengthy but straightforward calculation that vanishes due to the Lax equation structure. The isospectrality proof (Key derivation) gives this for free: $\text{tr}(L^k)$ is conserved for all $k$ because it depends only on the eigenvalues.

</details>

</aside>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The conserved quantities are $H$, $L = xp_y - yp_x$, $T_{xx} = p_x^2 + \omega^2 x^2$, and $T_{xy} = p_xp_y + \omega^2 xy$. These are 4 independent conserved quantities on a 4-dimensional phase space ($2n - 1 = 3$ for maximal superintegrability, so we have one more than needed — but $H$ is a function of the $T_{ij}$, so the independent set is $L, T_{xx}, T_{xy}$, giving 3 = $2n - 1$).

The hidden symmetry algebra is $\mathfrak{su}(2)$: the rescaled quantities $J_1 = T_{xy}/(2\omega)$, $J_2 = (T_{xx} - T_{yy})/(4\omega)$, $J_3 = L/2$ satisfy $\{J_i, J_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk}J_k$. This is the $\mathfrak{su}(2) \cong \mathfrak{so}(3)$ algebra, the same as angular momentum. The isotropic oscillator is superintegrable because the two oscillators have the same frequency, giving an $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ symmetry that rotates the two oscillators into each other.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Toda lattice Hamiltonian $H$ is the first of the conserved quantities $\text{tr}(L^k)/k$, and identify the second independent conserved quantity $\text{tr}(L^3)/3$ for $N \geq 3$ in terms of the Flaschka variables.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\text{tr}(L) = \sum b_j = -\sum p_j/2$. For the periodic Toda lattice, $\sum p_j$ is the total momentum, which is conserved by translation invariance, not by integrability. The interesting conserved quantity is $F_2 = \text{tr}(L^2)/2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\text{tr}(L) = \sum b_j = -\tfrac{1}{2}\sum p_j$. This is the total momentum (up to a sign), conserved by translation symmetry of the periodic lattice.

$\text{tr}(L^2) = \sum b_j^2 + 2\sum a_j^2$. As computed in Exercise 4, this equals $\sum p_j^2/4 + \tfrac{1}{2}\sum e^{q_j - q_{j+1}}$. Up to a constant, this is the Hamiltonian $H$.

$\text{tr}(L^3) = \sum b_j^3 + 3\sum b_j a_j^2 + 3\sum a_j^2 b_{j+1} + 6a_1a_2a_3$ (for $N = 3$). This is a genuinely new conserved quantity that is not a function of $H$ and $\sum p_j$. It depends on the cubic combinations of the momenta and the products of exponentials. For the open Toda lattice ($a_N = 0$), the last term $6a_1a_2a_3$ vanishes and the conserved quantity simplifies. The sequence $\text{tr}(L^k)$ for $k = 1, \ldots, N$ provides the complete set of $N$ conserved quantities in involution.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove Bertrand's theorem in the easy direction: if a central potential $V(r)$ has the property that *all* bounded orbits are closed, then $V(r) \propto r^2$ or $V(r) \propto 1/r$. (The full theorem includes the converse, which is harder.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the angle $\Delta\theta$ swept out during one radial period. The orbit closes if $\Delta\theta/\pi$ is rational. If this holds for *all* energies and angular momenta, then $\Delta\theta/\pi$ must be constant, which constrains $V(r)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The angle swept per radial period is $\Delta\theta = 2\int_{r_-}^{r_+}\frac{\ell\,dr}{r^2\sqrt{2m(E - V(r)) - \ell^2/r^2}}$. The orbit closes iff $\Delta\theta = 2\pi p/q$ for integers $p, q$. If *all* bounded orbits close, then $\Delta\theta/\pi$ must be independent of $E$ and $\ell$ (otherwise we could vary $E$ and $\ell$ to make it irrational).

Compute $\partial(\Delta\theta)/\partial E$ and $\partial(\Delta\theta)/\partial\ell$. Both must vanish. Writing $\Delta\theta = -2\pi\partial I_r/\partial\ell$ (from the action integral), the condition $\partial(\Delta\theta)/\partial E = 0$ gives $\partial^2 I_r/\partial E\partial\ell = 0$, so $I_r = f(E) + g(\ell)$. Similarly, $\partial(\Delta\theta)/\partial\ell = 0$ gives further constraints. The only potentials satisfying both are $V = \alpha r^2$ (giving $\Delta\theta = \pi$) and $V = -\alpha/r$ (giving $\Delta\theta = \pi$). The full proof (Bertrand 1873) is more careful about the boundary cases and the global closure condition, but the essential idea is that requiring all orbits to close forces the frequency ratio to be a fixed rational number, which constrains the potential to one of two possibilities.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, symbolic).**

For the Kepler problem with energy $E < 0$, express the eccentricity $e$ of the orbit in terms of the action variables $I_r$ and $I_\theta$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The eccentricity is $e = \sqrt{1 + 2E\ell^2/(mk^2)}$ where $\ell = I_\theta$. Express $E$ in terms of the actions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From $H = -mk^2/(2(I_r + I_\theta)^2) = E$, we have $I_r + I_\theta = k\sqrt{m/(2|E|)}$. The angular momentum is $\ell = I_\theta$. The eccentricity is

$$
e = \sqrt{1 - \frac{I_\theta^2}{(I_r + I_\theta)^2}} = \sqrt{\frac{I_r(2I_\theta + I_r)}{(I_r + I_\theta)^2}}.
$$

For a circular orbit ($e = 0$): $I_r = 0$ (no radial oscillation). For a parabolic orbit ($e = 1$): $I_\theta = 0$ (zero angular momentum, radial infall). The eccentricity depends on the *ratio* $I_r/I_\theta$, not on the total action alone. When $I_r \ll I_\theta$, the orbit is nearly circular ($e \approx \sqrt{2I_r/I_\theta} \ll 1$).

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The inverse-scattering solution of the Toda lattice

The Lax pair for the Toda lattice opens the door to the **inverse-scattering method**: the eigenvalues of the Lax matrix are the action variables, and the "scattering data" (eigenvalues plus norming constants) provide the angle variables. The time evolution of the scattering data is simple: the eigenvalues are constant, and the norming constants rotate at rates determined by the eigenvalues. Inverting the scattering map (reconstructing $q_j(t)$ from the scattering data at time $t$) solves the Toda lattice exactly.

For the open Toda lattice ($N$ particles with free endpoints), the scattering data have a beautiful interpretation: as $t \to +\infty$, the particles separate into $N$ free particles with asymptotic momenta equal to the eigenvalues of $L$. The soliton solutions correspond to bound states of the scattering problem: when two or more eigenvalues are close, the corresponding particles oscillate around each other while the centre of mass drifts freely.

### The Kepler problem in parabolic coordinates

The Kepler problem admits an elegant separation in **parabolic coordinates** $\xi = r + x$, $\eta = r - x$ (for motion in the $xz$-plane). The Hamilton-Jacobi equation separates in these coordinates, and the two separation constants are the energy $E$ and the $x$-component of the Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector $A_x$. The parabolic separation makes the hidden symmetry manifest: the two separated Hamilton-Jacobi equations correspond to the two commuting conserved quantities $H$ and $A_x$.

The parabolic coordinate system is singular along the $z$-axis ($\xi = \eta$) and degenerate at the origin. The action variables in parabolic coordinates are $I_\xi$ and $I_\eta$, and the Hamiltonian is $H = -mk^2/(2(I_\xi + I_\eta)^2)$. The frequencies are again equal, reflecting the degeneracy.

### The Toda lattice as a discretisation of the KdV equation

The Toda lattice is the spatial discretisation of the Korteweg-de Vries (KdV) equation $u_t + 6uu_x + u_{xxx} = 0$. Both are completely integrable, both admit soliton solutions, and both have Lax pairs. The connection is through the continuous limit: if the lattice spacing $\Delta x \to 0$ and $q_j(t) = u(x_j, t)\Delta x^2$, then the Toda equations of motion converge to the KdV equation. The eigenvalues of the Toda Lax matrix converge to the scattering data of the KdV Lax operator (the Schrodinger operator $-\partial_x^2 + u(x,t)$). This is the deep reason both systems are integrable: they share the same algebraic structure (isospectral deformation of a linear operator).

**Synthesis.** The Kepler problem and the Toda lattice illustrate two distinct routes to complete integrability: the Kepler problem is integrable by symmetry (the conserved quantities are Noether charges associated with continuous symmetries, including the hidden $\mathfrak{so}(4)$), while the Toda lattice is integrable by algebraic structure (the conserved quantities are spectral invariants of a Lax matrix, invisible to symmetry analysis). The central insight is that integrability is an algebraic property — the existence of a maximal set of commuting conserved quantities — that can arise from either geometric or spectral origins. Putting these together, the Kepler problem represents the classical (symmetry-based) approach to integrability and the Toda lattice represents the modern (Lax-pair) approach, and the two are unified by the action-angle framework of the Liouville-Arnold theorem.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The Kepler problem is the oldest integrable system: Newton's solution of the two-body problem (1687) showed that planetary orbits are conic sections. The Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector was known to Laplace (1799) and was studied by Runge (1919) and Lenz (1924) in the context of the old quantum theory. The hidden $\mathfrak{so}(4)$ symmetry was identified by Fock (1935) and Bargmann (1936), who showed that the Kepler problem is the free particle on the 3-sphere $S^3$ in stereographic projection.

The Toda lattice was introduced by Toda in 1967 as a model of nonlinear lattice vibrations. Flaschka's 1974 discovery of the Lax pair was a breakthrough: it showed that a lattice system with no obvious symmetry could be integrable, and it introduced the Lax-pair method into mathematical physics. Moser's 1975 paper extended the analysis and connected the Toda lattice to the Jacobi matrix eigenvalue problem. The inverse-scattering solution was developed by Manakov (1974) and others.

Bertrand's theorem (1873) established the uniqueness of the Kepler and harmonic-oscillator potentials: they are the only central potentials for which all bounded orbits are closed. This theorem provides the physical justification for the superintegrability of these two systems: closed orbits require an extra conserved quantity, and only these two potentials provide it. The philosophical significance is that the two most important systems in physics — the gravitational two-body problem and the harmonic oscillator — are distinguished not merely by their simplicity but by a deep algebraic property (superintegrability) that has no obvious physical origin. The "why" of these systems is answered not by physics but by mathematics.
