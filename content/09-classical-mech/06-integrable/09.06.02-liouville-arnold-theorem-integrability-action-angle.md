## Intuition [Beginner]

Some mechanical systems have a remarkable property: they possess enough conserved quantities to be "fully solvable." The Liouville-Arnold theorem tells us what happens when a system with $n$ degrees of freedom has $n$ independent, Poisson-commuting conserved quantities. The answer is beautiful: the motion is confined to tori.

Think of a spinning top. It has angular momentum in three directions, but only two are independent for a symmetric top. The energy and one component of angular momentum pin down the motion to a surface shaped like a torus (a doughnut). The system traces out a path on this torus, winding around it in two independent directions at fixed rates.

The Liouville-Arnold theorem generalises this idea. When you have $n$ commuting conserved quantities for an $n$-degree-of-freedom system, the level sets (the sets where all $n$ quantities take fixed values) are $n$-dimensional tori. On each torus, the motion is a straight-line winding — every coordinate increases at a constant rate, like the hands of $n$ independent clocks.

This is the deepest structural result in integrable Hamiltonian mechanics. It tells us that integrable systems are not merely solvable in principle; their phase space is foliated (layered) by invariant tori, and the motion on each torus is as simple as it can possibly be.

<aside class="beginner-note">

**Why tori?** A torus is the product of circles: $T^n = S^1 \times S^1 \times \cdots \times S^1$. Each conserved quantity generates a circular flow (like the motion of a clock hand). When these flows commute, they combine to give a flow on the product of $n$ circles — which is an $n$-dimensional torus. Compactness of the level set (bounded and closed) is what forces each factor to be a circle rather than a line.

</aside>

## Visual [Beginner]

![Liouville tori foliating phase space](liouville-arnold-tori-foliation.svg)

**Figure 1.** A schematic view of the Liouville-Arnold foliation. The $2n$-dimensional phase space is sliced into a family of $n$-dimensional invariant tori, one for each set of values $(F_1, F_2, \ldots, F_n)$ of the commuting integrals. The motion of the system is confined to a single torus, winding around it in $n$ independent directions at frequencies $\omega_1, \omega_2, \ldots, \omega_n$ determined by the values of the integrals.

<aside class="visual-note">

Imagine phase space as a block of Swiss cheese where each "hole" is a torus. The system sits on exactly one torus (determined by its initial conditions) and traces out a path on that torus forever. If the frequencies are rationally related ($\omega_1/\omega_2 = p/q$ for integers $p, q$), the path eventually closes and repeats. If not, the path densely fills the entire torus without ever exactly repeating — a condition known as **quasi-periodic motion**.

</aside>

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Example: The planar Kepler problem as a Liouville-integrable system.**

A planet of mass $m$ orbiting a star of mass $M$ under Newtonian gravity has two degrees of freedom (the radial coordinate $r$ and the angle $\phi$). We need two independent, commuting integrals.

The Hamiltonian is $H = p_r^2/(2m) + L^2/(2mr^2) - GMm/r$ where $L$ is the angular momentum. The two commuting integrals are:

1. $F_1 = H$ (the energy, conserved by time-translation symmetry)
2. $F_2 = L$ (the angular momentum, conserved by rotational symmetry)

These commute: $\{H, L\} = 0$ because the Hamiltonian is rotation-invariant.

By the Liouville-Arnold theorem, the joint level sets $H = E$, $L = \ell$ are 2-tori (for bound orbits, $E < 0$). The planet's motion winds around each torus with two frequencies:
- The radial frequency $\omega_r$ (how fast the planet oscillates between perihelion and aphelion)
- The angular frequency $\omega_\phi$ (how fast the planet sweeps out angle)

For the Kepler problem, the frequencies are commensurate: $\omega_r = \omega_\phi$. This means every bound orbit closes exactly — the planet returns to its starting point after one revolution. This is a special property of the $1/r$ potential (and the harmonic oscillator), related to the Bertrand theorem [ref: TODO_REF].

**Exercise (beginner).** A 2D harmonic oscillator has $H = (p_x^2 + p_y^2)/(2m) + \frac{1}{2}m(\omega_x^2 x^2 + \omega_y^2 y^2)$. Identify two independent commuting integrals. What shape are the level sets? When does the orbit close?

<details>
<summary>Hint</summary>

Try $F_1 = H$ and $F_2 = (p_x^2 + m^2\omega_x^2 x^2)/(2m)$ (the energy of the $x$-oscillator alone). Check that they Poisson-commute. The level sets $F_1 = E$, $F_2 = E_x$ are tori when both energies are positive. The orbit closes when $\omega_x/\omega_y$ is rational.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

$F_1 = H$ and $F_2 = H_x = (p_x^2 + m^2\omega_x^2 x^2)/(2m)$ are independent (knowing the total energy and the $x$-energy determines the $y$-energy $E_y = E - E_x$, but the distribution between $x$ and $y$ is independent). They commute because $H_x$ depends only on $(x, p_x)$ while $H_y$ depends only on $(y, p_y)$, so $\{H_x, H_y\} = 0$, and $H = H_x + H_y$ implies $\{H, H_x\} = \{H_x, H_x\} + \{H_y, H_x\} = 0$. The level sets are 2-tori (product of two independent 1D oscillations). The orbit closes when $\omega_x/\omega_y = p/q$ for integers $p, q$; the Lissajous figure then repeats after $q$ periods of $\omega_x$ and $p$ periods of $\omega_y$.

</details>

## Check your understanding [Beginner]

1. What are the two conditions a set of conserved quantities must satisfy for the Liouville-Arnold theorem to apply?
2. Why does the level set need to be compact (bounded and closed) for it to be a torus?
3. A system with 3 degrees of freedom has 3 commuting integrals. What is the topology of the generic level set?
4. What is quasi-periodic motion, and when does it occur on a Liouville torus?

<details>
<summary>Answers</summary>

1. The integrals must be (a) functionally independent and (b) in involution (their Poisson brackets pairwise vanish).
2. Compactness ensures each commuting flow generates a circle ($S^1$) rather than a line ($\mathbb{R}$). The product of $n$ circles is an $n$-torus $T^n$. Without compactness, you could get $\mathbb{R}^k \times T^{n-k}$ instead.
3. A 3-torus $T^3 = S^1 \times S^1 \times S^1$, assuming the level set is compact.
4. Quasi-periodic motion occurs when the frequencies $\omega_1, \ldots, \omega_n$ are rationally independent (no integer relation $k_1\omega_1 + \cdots + k_n\omega_n = 0$ with integer $k_i$ not all zero). The trajectory then densely fills the torus without exactly repeating.

</details>

## Formal definition [Intermediate]

**Definition (Liouville integrability).** A Hamiltonian system on a $2n$-dimensional symplectic manifold $(M, \omega)$ with Hamiltonian $H$ is **completely integrable in the sense of Liouville** if there exist $n$ smooth functions $F_1 = H, F_2, \ldots, F_n$ such that:

1. **Functional independence.** The differentials $dF_1, dF_2, \ldots, dF_n$ are linearly independent on a dense open subset of $M$.
2. **Involution.** $\{F_i, F_j\} = 0$ for all $1 \leq i, j \leq n$ (the integrals are in involution, i.e., they pairwise Poisson-commute).

The submanifold $M_f = \{x \in M : F_i(x) = f_i, \, i = 1, \ldots, n\}$ is called the **level set** (or **invariant manifold**) of the integrals.

**Definition (Action variables).** Let $M_f$ be a compact connected component of a level set of the integrals, which by the Liouville-Arnold theorem is diffeomorphic to an $n$-torus $T^n$. Let $\gamma_1, \gamma_2, \ldots, \gamma_n$ be a basis of independent non-contractible loops on $M_f$ (a basis of the first homology group $H_1(T^n, \mathbb{Z})$). The **action variables** are defined as:

$$I_i = \frac{1}{2\pi} \oint_{\gamma_i} p \, dq = \frac{1}{2\pi} \oint_{\gamma_i} \sum_{k=1}^{n} p_k \, dq^k$$

These are functions of the values $f = (f_1, \ldots, f_n)$ of the integrals, and hence constants of the motion.

**Definition (Angle variables).** The conjugate **angle variables** $\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_n$ are coordinates on the torus $M_f$ defined so that $(I_1, \ldots, I_n, \theta_1, \ldots, \theta_n)$ form a Darboux chart (a canonical coordinate system in which $\omega = d\theta^i \wedge dI_i$). They are defined up to shifts $\theta_i \mapsto \theta_i + c_i$ (choice of origin on each circle).

**Definition (Frequency vector).** In action-angle coordinates, the Hamiltonian depends only on the actions: $H = H(I)$. The equations of motion become $\dot{I}_i = 0$ and $\dot{\theta}_i = \omega_i(I)$ where the **frequency vector** is:

$$\omega_i(I) = \frac{\partial H}{\partial I_i}$$

## Key derivation [Intermediate]

**Theorem (Liouville, 1855; Arnold, 1963).** Let $(M^{2n}, \omega)$ be a symplectic manifold with Hamiltonian $H$, and let $F_1 = H, F_2, \ldots, F_n$ be $n$ smooth functions in involution that are functionally independent on a dense open set. Let $M_f$ be a compact connected component of the level set $\{F_i = f_i\}$. Then:

1. $M_f$ is diffeomorphic to the $n$-torus $T^n$.
2. The Hamiltonian flows of $F_1, \ldots, F_n$ are complete on $M_f$ and define an action of $\mathbb{R}^n$ on $M_f$ by translations.
3. In a neighbourhood of $M_f$, there exist action-angle variables $(I, \theta)$ in which $\omega = d\theta^i \wedge dI_i$ and the equations of motion are $\dot{I} = 0$, $\dot{\theta} = \omega(I)$.

**Proof sketch.** The argument proceeds in four stages.

*Stage 1: The involution condition implies the flows commute.* Since $\{F_i, F_j\} = 0$, the Hamiltonian vector fields $X_{F_i}$ and $X_{F_j}$ satisfy $[X_{F_i}, X_{F_j}] = X_{\{F_i, F_j\}} = 0$ by the correspondence between Poisson brackets and Lie brackets of Hamiltonian vector fields [ref: TODO_REF]. The commuting vector fields $X_{F_1}, \ldots, X_{F_n}$ define an action of $\mathbb{R}^n$ on $M$ by translations: the point $\Phi_t(x) = g^{t_1}_{F_1} \circ \cdots \circ g^{t_n}_{F_n}(x)$ is independent of the order of composition.

*Stage 2: Compact level sets are tori.* Each flow $g^{t_i}_{F_i}$ preserves the level set $M_f$ (since $F_j$ is constant along the flow of $F_i$). Restricted to the compact $M_f$, each flow generates a closed orbit (compactness prevents escape to infinity). The stabiliser of any point is a lattice $\Lambda \cong \mathbb{Z}^n$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ (the set of "period vectors" $t = (t_1, \ldots, t_n)$ that return the point to itself). Therefore $M_f \cong \mathbb{R}^n / \Lambda \cong T^n$ [ref: TODO_REF].

*Stage 3: Construction of action variables.* For each $i = 1, \ldots, n$, choose a basis cycle $\gamma_i$ for $H_1(M_f, \mathbb{Z})$. Define the action integral:

$$I_i = \frac{1}{2\pi} \oint_{\gamma_i} \sum_{k=1}^{n} p_k \, dq^k$$

Because $\omega = dp_k \wedge dq^k$ is closed and the level set $M_f$ is Lagrangian (the flows are tangent to $M_f$ and $\omega(X_{F_i}, X_{F_j}) = \{F_j, F_i\} = 0$), the integral depends only on the homology class of $\gamma_i$ and on the values $f_1, \ldots, f_n$ — not on the specific representative curve. By the implicit function theorem, the map $f \mapsto I$ is locally invertible (since $dF_i$ are linearly independent), so the $I_i$ are well-defined functions of the integrals and hence constants of the motion.

*Stage 4: Angle variables and the Darboux chart.* Define angle variables by integrating the 1-form $\alpha = \sum p_k dq^k$ along paths on $M_f$. Concretely, fix a reference point $x_0 \in M_f$ and set $\theta_i(x) = \int_{x_0}^{x} \alpha$ along the flow of $X_{F_i}$. The $2\pi$-periodicity follows from the lattice $\Lambda$. The symplectic form in the new coordinates is $\omega = d\theta^i \wedge dI_i$ (verified by computing $\{I_i, \theta_j\} = \delta_{ij}$, $\{I_i, I_j\} = 0$, $\{\theta_i, \theta_j\} = 0$), confirming that $(I, \theta)$ is a Darboux chart [ref: TODO_REF].

This establishes the existence of action-angle coordinates in a tubular neighbourhood of $M_f$.

**Bridge.** The Liouville-Arnold theorem converts the existence of $n$ commuting integrals from an algebraic condition into a geometric structure: a foliation of phase space by invariant tori equipped with action-angle coordinates. The next step is to explore the global obstructions to extending these coordinates and the consequences for perturbation theory.

## Exercises [Intermediate]

**Exercise 1 (Easy).** Verify that the planar harmonic oscillator $H = (p_x^2 + p_y^2)/(2m) + \frac{1}{2}m(\omega_x^2 x^2 + \omega_y^2 y^2)$ is Liouville-integrable by exhibiting two commuting integrals and checking functional independence.

<details>
<summary>Hint</summary>

Use $F_1 = H$ and $F_2 = H_x = (p_x^2 + m^2\omega_x^2 x^2)/(2m)$. Check $\{F_1, F_2\} = 0$ and compute the rank of $(dF_1, dF_2)$.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

$F_1 = H$, $F_2 = H_x$. Since $H = H_x + H_y$ and $H_x$ depends only on $(x, p_x)$ while $H_y$ depends only on $(y, p_y)$: $\{H_x, H_y\} = 0$, hence $\{F_1, F_2\} = \{H_x + H_y, H_x\} = 0$. The differentials are $dF_1 = (m\omega_x^2 x)\,dx + (p_x/m)\,dp_x + (m\omega_y^2 y)\,dy + (p_y/m)\,dp_y$ and $dF_2 = (m\omega_x^2 x)\,dx + (p_x/m)\,dp_x$. These are linearly independent whenever the $y$-dependent terms are nonzero (i.e., away from the degenerate set $y = p_y = 0$).

</details>

**Exercise 2 (Medium).** For the 1D harmonic oscillator $H = p^2/(2m) + \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 q^2$, compute the action variable $I$ explicitly by evaluating the loop integral $I = (2\pi)^{-1} \oint p\,dq$ over the orbit $H = E$.

<details>
<summary>Hint</summary>

Parametrise the ellipse $p^2/(2mE) + m\omega^2 q^2/(2E) = 1$ in phase space. The enclosed area is $\pi \cdot \sqrt{2mE} \cdot \sqrt{2E/(m\omega^2)} = 2\pi E/\omega$.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

On the energy surface $H = E$, the orbit in phase space is the ellipse $p^2/(2mE) + m\omega^2 q^2/(2E) = 1$. The action integral is the enclosed area divided by $2\pi$: $I = \frac{1}{2\pi} \oint p\,dq = \frac{\text{Area}}{2\pi} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \cdot \pi \cdot \sqrt{2mE} \cdot \sqrt{2E/(m\omega^2)} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{2\pi E}{\omega} = \frac{E}{\omega}$. Inverting: $H = E = \omega I$. The frequency is $\dot{\theta} = \partial H / \partial I = \omega$, confirming the angular frequency is independent of the action (a special property of the harmonic oscillator).

</details>

**Exercise 3 (Medium).** Prove that the Liouville tori are Lagrangian submanifolds: if $M_f$ is a level set of $n$ commuting integrals on a $2n$-dimensional symplectic manifold, then $\omega|_{M_f} = 0$.

<details>
<summary>Hint</summary>

Show that the tangent space to $M_f$ is spanned by the Hamiltonian vector fields $X_{F_i}$, and evaluate $\omega(X_{F_i}, X_{F_j}) = \{F_j, F_i\}$.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

The tangent space $T_x M_f$ is the annihilator of $dF_1, \ldots, dF_n$ (vectors $v$ with $dF_i(v) = 0$ for all $i$). Equivalently, $T_x M_f = \operatorname{span}\{X_{F_1}, \ldots, X_{F_n}\}$, since $\omega(X_{F_i}, v) = dF_i(v) = 0$ for $v \in T_x M_f$ and the $X_{F_i}$ are linearly independent by functional independence. For any tangent vectors $v, w \in T_x M_f$, write $v = \sum a_i X_{F_i}$ and $w = \sum b_j X_{F_j}$. Then $\omega(v, w) = \sum_{i,j} a_i b_j \omega(X_{F_i}, X_{F_j}) = \sum_{i,j} a_i b_j \{F_j, F_i\} = 0$ by the involution hypothesis. Therefore $\omega|_{M_f} = 0$ and $M_f$ is Lagrangian (half-dimensional isotropic).

</details>

**Exercise 4 (Medium).** Let $F_1, F_2$ be two commuting integrals with compact level sets. Show that the action variables $I_1, I_2$ Poisson-commute: $\{I_1, I_2\} = 0$.

<details>
<summary>Hint</summary>

Each $I_i$ is a function of $(F_1, F_2)$ alone (it depends only on which level set we are on). Use the chain rule for Poisson brackets.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

The action $I_i = I_i(F_1, F_2)$ is constant on each level set, so it is a function of $F_1$ and $F_2$ alone. By the chain rule: $\{I_1, I_2\} = \sum_{j,k} \frac{\partial I_1}{\partial F_j}\frac{\partial I_2}{\partial F_k}\{F_j, F_k\} = 0$ since $\{F_j, F_k\} = 0$ for all $j, k$ by the involution hypothesis.

</details>

**Exercise 5 (Hard).** For the Kepler problem in plane polar coordinates, $H = p_r^2/(2\mu) + L^2/(2\mu r^2) - k/r$, compute the action variables $I_r$ (radial) and $I_\phi$ (angular) for a bound orbit with $E < 0$.

<details>
<summary>Hint</summary>

$I_\phi = L$ is the angular momentum (the angular action is just the momentum conjugate to $\phi$). For $I_r$, write $p_r = \sqrt{2\mu(E + k/r) - L^2/r^2}$ and evaluate $\oint p_r\,dr$ around the turning points. Use the residue-theorem trick: $\oint \sqrt{-A/r^2 + B/r + C}\,dr$ with $A = L^2$, $B = 2\mu k$, $C = 2\mu E < 0$.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

Angular action: $I_\phi = (2\pi)^{-1}\oint p_\phi \, d\phi = L$ (since $p_\phi = L$ is constant and $\phi$ runs $0$ to $2\pi$). Radial action: $I_r = (2\pi)^{-1}\oint p_r \, dr = (2\pi)^{-1}\oint \sqrt{2\mu E + 2\mu k/r - L^2/r^2}\,dr$. The integrand has poles at the roots $r_\pm$ of the radicand (perihelion and aphelion). Computing via residues or by the known formula for the area enclosed by a quadratic-root contour: $I_r = -L + k\sqrt{\mu/(2|E|)}$. The total actions are $I_r + I_\phi = k\sqrt{\mu/(2|E|)}$, giving $H = E = -\mu k^2/(2(I_r + I_\phi)^2)$. The frequency degeneracy $\omega_r = \omega_\phi$ follows from $H$ depending only on $I_r + I_\phi$: $\partial H/\partial I_r = \partial H/\partial I_\phi$, so both frequencies are equal.

</details>

**Exercise 6 (Medium).** Show that the map $(f_1, \ldots, f_n) \mapsto (I_1, \ldots, I_n)$ is a local diffeomorphism near a regular value of the momentum map $F = (F_1, \ldots, F_n)$.

<details>
<summary>Hint</summary>

Use the fact that $\omega(X_{F_i}, X_{F_j}) = \{F_j, F_i\} = 0$ and the linear independence of $dF_i$ to argue that $\partial I_i / \partial f_j$ is non-degenerate.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

The differential $dI_i = \sum_j (\partial I_i/\partial f_j) \, df_j$. Since $I_i = (2\pi)^{-1}\oint_{\gamma_i} \alpha$ where $\alpha$ is the canonical 1-form, and the cycles $\gamma_i$ depend smoothly on $f$, the map $f \mapsto I$ is smooth. To see it is non-degenerate, note that $\{I_i, \theta_j\} = \delta_{ij}$ by construction of angle variables, while $\{I_i, I_j\} = 0$. If $dI_i$ were linearly dependent, the angle variables could not satisfy the canonical Poisson relations, contradicting the Darboux property. Equivalently, the Jacobian $\det(\partial I_i / \partial f_j)$ is the period matrix, which is non-degenerate for regular values by the implicit function theorem applied to the momentum map.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (Easy).** A 1-degree-of-freedom system with Hamiltonian $H(q, p)$ has one conserved quantity (energy itself). Is it automatically Liouville-integrable? What does the Liouville-Arnold theorem tell you about the level sets?

<details>
<summary>Hint</summary>

$n = 1$ means you need $n = 1$ integral. Count the number of commuting integrals required.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

Yes: a 1-degree-of-freedom system is always Liouville-integrable, because you need $n = 1$ commuting integral and $H$ itself suffices (it Poisson-commutes with itself). The Liouville-Arnold theorem says the compact connected level sets $H = E$ are 1-tori, i.e., circles $S^1$. The motion on each circle is periodic with frequency $\omega(E) = dH/dI$. Every 1D Hamiltonian system is integrable in the Liouville sense.

</details>

**Exercise 8 (Hard).** **The Carathéodory-Jacobi-Lie theorem.** Let $(M^{2n}, \omega)$ be symplectic and let $F_1, \ldots, F_k$ be $k < n$ functions in involution that are functionally independent at a point. Show that there exist $2n - k$ additional functions $G_1, \ldots, G_{n-k}$, $\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_k$ so that $(F_1, \ldots, F_k, G_1, \ldots, G_{n-k}, \theta_1, \ldots, \theta_k)$ form a Darboux chart. This is the partial extension theorem needed when the number of integrals is fewer than $n$.

<details>
<summary>Hint</summary>

Proceed by induction on $k$. For $k = 1$, use Darboux's theorem to find local coordinates where $F_1$ is one of the momenta. For the inductive step, restrict to the level set of $F_k$ and apply the induction hypothesis to the remaining $k-1$ integrals on the quotient.

</details>

<details>
<summary>Solution</summary>

Base case $k = 1$: by Darboux's theorem [09.04.05], there exist local coordinates $(q^1, \ldots, q^n, p_1, \ldots, p_n)$ with $\omega = dq^i \wedge dp_i$. Since $dF_1$ is non-vanishing, after a linear symplectic change we can arrange $F_1 = p_1$. The remaining $2n - 2$ coordinates $(q^2, \ldots, q^n, p_2, \ldots, p_n)$ form a Darboux chart on the level set $\{p_1 = c\}$.

Inductive step: assume the result for $k - 1$ integrals. Given $F_1, \ldots, F_k$ in involution, the Hamiltonian flow of $F_k$ preserves the level set $\{F_k = c_k\}$, which is a $(2n-1)$-dimensional submanifold. The symplectic form descends to a closed 2-form on the quotient of the level set by the $S^1$-action of $X_{F_k}$ (Marsden-Weinstein reduction at the identity). On this reduced space of dimension $2n - 2$, the remaining $k - 1$ integrals descend and remain in involution. By the induction hypothesis, they extend to a Darboux chart. Lifting back gives the desired extension on $M$.

</details>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate]

**Status:** `none` — no Mathlib formalization.

The Liouville-Arnold theorem is beyond current Mathlib capabilities. The missing infrastructure includes:

1. **Hamiltonian vector fields on manifolds.** The correspondence $F \mapsto X_F$ defined by $\omega(X_F, \cdot) = dF$ requires the contraction of a vector field with a 2-form on a smooth manifold, which is not yet in Mathlib in usable form.

2. **Commuting flows and the period lattice.** The proof that compact level sets of commuting flows are tori requires the orbit-stabiliser theorem for the $\mathbb{R}^n$-action and the identification of the stabiliser with a lattice $\Lambda \cong \mathbb{Z}^n$.

3. **Homology and the action integral.** The definition of action variables as integrals $\oint_{\gamma_i} p\,dq$ over basis cycles requires $H_1(T^n, \mathbb{Z})$ and the fact that the integral depends only on the homology class (Stokes' theorem on the Lagrangian level set).

4. **Darboux verification.** Proving that $(I, \theta)$ are Darboux coordinates requires computing Poisson brackets in the new coordinates and verifying the canonical relations.

The single most impactful formalization target would be the **Carathéodory-Jacobi-Lie theorem** (Exercise 8), which is a local result requiring only the Darboux machinery that is closer to Mathlib's current capabilities.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Non-degeneracy conditions and the Kolmogorov condition

The action-angle map $f \mapsto I$ provides a natural way to measure how the frequencies change as one moves between nearby tori. The **Kolmogorov non-degeneracy condition** requires that the map $I \mapsto \omega(I) = \nabla_I H(I)$ be a local diffeomorphism, i.e., $\det(\partial^2 H / \partial I_i \partial I_j) \neq 0$. This ensures that the frequency map is locally invertible and that "nearby tori have different frequencies" [ref: TODO_REF].

The **Arnold non-degeneracy condition** (also called the **isoenergetic non-degeneracy condition**) requires non-vanishing of the determinant:

$$\det \begin{pmatrix} \partial^2 H / \partial I_i \partial I_j & \partial H / \partial I_i \\ \partial H / \partial I_j & 0 \end{pmatrix} \neq 0$$

This is the condition needed for the KAM theorem at fixed energy [ref: TODO_REF]. It is strictly weaker than the Kolmogorov condition: the Kepler problem satisfies the Arnold condition but not the Kolmogorov condition (since $H$ depends on $I_r + I_\phi$ alone, the Hessian is degenerate).

### Global obstructions: the Duistermaat theorem

The Liouville-Arnold theorem guarantees the existence of action-angle variables in a tubular neighbourhood of each torus. **Duistermaat (1980)** showed that global action-angle coordinates on the entire phase space may fail to exist [ref: TODO_REF]. The obstructions are:

1. **Monodromy.** As one loops around a singular fibre (a degenerate torus where the integrals become dependent), the basis cycles $\gamma_1, \ldots, \gamma_n$ may undergo a linear transformation $\gamma_i \mapsto \sum_j M_{ij} \gamma_j$ with $M \in \mathrm{GL}(n, \mathbb{Z})$. This **monodromy matrix** measures the twisting of the torus fibration. The spherical pendulum has substantive monodromy $M = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, which means global action-angle coordinates do not exist [ref: TODO_REF].

2. **Nonzero Chern class.** The Lagrangian torus bundle may have a non-vanishing Chern class in $H^2(B, \mathbb{Z})$ (where $B$ is the base of the fibration), obstructing the existence of global angle variables.

3. **Caustics.** The singular fibres (caustics) where the rank of the momentum map drops form a discriminant locus in the base. The topology of this locus constrains the global properties of the fibration.

### The Mishchenko-Fomenko theorem (non-compact level sets)

When the level set $M_f$ is non-compact, it need not be a torus — it could be $\mathbb{R}^k \times T^{n-k}$ for some $0 \leq k \leq n$. The **Mishchenko-Fomenko theorem** extends the Liouville-Arnold framework to this case: the commuting flows still define a translational action, and the level set is diffeomorphic to a quotient $\mathbb{R}^n / \Lambda$ where $\Lambda$ is a discrete subgroup of rank $n - k$. Action-angle variables exist locally but the angle variables corresponding to the non-compact directions are unbounded [ref: TODO_REF].

### Nekhoroshev's theorem on exponential stability

Even when an integrable system is perturbed and the tori begin to break up, **Nekhoroshev (1977)** proved that the action variables remain nearly constant for exponentially long times: $|I(t) - I(0)| < C\epsilon^{a}$ for $|t| < T_0 \exp((\epsilon_0/\epsilon)^b)$, where $\epsilon$ is the perturbation size and $a, b$ depend on the number of degrees of freedom and the steepness condition on $H(I)$. This is stronger than the KAM theorem's result (which only guarantees that *some* tori persist) but weaker in that it allows slow drift rather than exact conservation [ref: TODO_REF].

### The Atiyah-Guillemin-Sternberg convexity theorem

For Hamiltonian group actions, the Liouville-Arnold framework has a striking convexity refinement. The **Atiyah (1982) and Guillemin-Sternberg (1982) convexity theorem** states: if a torus $T^k$ acts on a compact symplectic manifold $(M^{2n}, \omega)$ in a Hamiltonian fashion with momentum map $\mu: M \to \mathbb{R}^k$, then the image $\mu(M)$ is a convex polytope in $\mathbb{R}^k$. This is the symplectic counterpart of the Schur-Horn theorem in linear algebra and connects integrable systems to toric geometry [ref: TODO_REF].

**Synthesis.** The Liouville-Arnold theorem is the structural centrepiece of integrable Hamiltonian mechanics: it converts $n$ algebraic conditions (commuting integrals) into a geometric picture (torus foliation with action-angle coordinates). The non-degeneracy conditions bridge to perturbation theory via KAM; the global obstructions (monodromy, Chern class) show that local integrability does not guarantee global simplicity; and the convexity theorems for group actions connect the integrable picture to toric geometry and representation theory. The entire framework rests on three pillars: the symplectic structure [09.04.05], the Poisson bracket [09.04.03], and the Darboux theorem that guarantees the existence of canonical coordinates.

## Connections [Master]

### Quantum mechanics: the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantisation

Einstein (1911) observed that the action variables of the Liouville-Arnold theorem provide the natural quantisation conditions for the old quantum theory [ref: TODO_REF]. The **Bohr-Sommerfeld conditions** $I_i = n_i \hbar$ (with $n_i \in \mathbb{Z}$) select a discrete set of tori as "allowed" quantum states. Einstein further noted that this prescription only makes sense when the Liouville-Arnold theorem applies — i.e., when the system is integrable. For non-integrable systems, the EBK (Einstein-Brillouin-Keller) quantisation uses Maslov-index-corrected conditions $I_i = (n_i + \alpha_i/4)\hbar$. This was one of the earliest hints that integrability is a fundamental structural property, not merely a convenience.

### KAM theory and the persistence of tori

The Kolmogorov-Arnold-Moser (KAM) theorem [09.08.01] asks: when an integrable Hamiltonian $H_0(I)$ is perturbed to $H_0(I) + \epsilon H_1(I, \theta)$, do the Liouville tori survive? The answer is that tori with "sufficiently irrational" frequencies (satisfying a Diophantine condition $|k \cdot \omega| > c|k|^{-\tau}$ for all $k \in \mathbb{Z}^n \setminus \{0\}$) persist as invariant tori of the perturbed system, slightly deformed but still carrying quasi-periodic motion. The non-degeneracy conditions defined in this unit (Kolmogorov, Arnold, isoenergetic) are precisely the hypotheses that ensure enough tori satisfy the Diophantine condition.

### Topological obstructions to integrability

The Liouville-Arnold theorem has a converse of sorts: the topology of the energy surface constrains whether integrability is possible. For example, a Hamiltonian system on an energy surface with the topology of $S^3$ cannot be integrable with smooth integrals, because the torus fibration would require the existence of non-vanishing independent vector fields on $S^3$ that would give a globally uniform foliation, which contradicts known results. Fomenko and collaborators developed a classification of the "atoms" and "molecules" that describe the topology of Liouville foliations on 3-manifolds, leading to topological obstructions to integrability for specific systems [ref: TODO_REF].

### Integrable systems in infinite dimensions

The Liouville-Arnold framework extends formally to infinite-dimensional Hamiltonian systems (PDEs with a Hamiltonian structure). The KdV equation, the nonlinear Schrödinger equation, and the Toda lattice are examples of infinite-dimensional integrable systems. The "integrals" are provided by the inverse scattering transform, and the analogue of the Liouville torus is the space of action variables parameterised by the scattering data. Rigorous formulation requires care with functional-analytic issues (the phase space is infinite-dimensional, the Poisson bracket is defined on a dense subspace, and the analogue of the Arnold-Liouville theorem is an infinite-dimensional inverse spectral theorem).

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

**Joseph Liouville (1855)** first observed that $n$ commuting integrals imply the existence of a family of invariant tori, in the context of the Jacobi method for integrating Hamilton's equations [ref: TODO_REF]. His result was stated in local coordinates and did not emphasise the global torus structure.

**Vladimir Arnold (1963)** gave the modern formulation in his landmark paper on the stability of Hamiltonian systems, proving the global torus diffeomorphism and constructing the action-angle variables on a tubular neighbourhood of the torus [ref: TODO_REF]. Arnold's proof is the one presented in the Key derivation above, using the period lattice of the commuting flows. The result is sometimes called the **Liouville-Arnold-Jost theorem**, acknowledging **Jost (1959)** who independently proved a version of the theorem [ref: TODO_REF].

**Einstein (1911)** recognised the significance of the action variables for quantum theory [ref: TODO_REF]. His observation that the Bohr-Sommerfeld conditions only make sense for integrable systems was prescient: it identified integrability as a fundamental physical property, not merely a mathematical convenience. This insight lay dormant until the development of KAM theory and the study of classical chaos brought the distinction between integrable and non-integrable systems to the forefront of physics.

**Duistermaat (1980)** opened the study of global obstructions to action-angle coordinates, introducing the monodromy of Lagrangian fibrations and showing that the spherical pendulum — the simplest mechanical system beyond the harmonic oscillator and Kepler problem — already exhibits substantive monodromy [ref: TODO_REF]. This result revealed that the "nice" local picture guaranteed by Liouville-Arnold can fail globally even in very simple systems.

**Nekhoroshev (1972, 1977)** developed the theory of action-angle variables for non-compact level sets and proved his exponential-stability theorem for perturbed integrable systems, bridging the gap between the exact conservation of action variables in integrable systems and the breakdown of tori in chaotic systems [ref: TODO_REF].

A philosophical point: the Liouville-Arnold theorem reveals that integrability is a geometric property, not merely an algebraic one. Having $n$ commuting integrals is an algebraic condition, but the consequence — torus foliation and action-angle coordinates — is geometric. This mirrors the philosophy of symplectic geometry more broadly: the algebraic structure (Poisson bracket, involution) determines the geometric structure (torus foliation, Lagrangian fibration), and vice versa. The theorem is a paradigmatic example of the algebra-geometry correspondence that pervades modern mathematical physics.
