## Intuition [Beginner]

Phase space — the space of positions and momenta — has a special geometric structure that makes Hamiltonian mechanics work. This structure is called **symplectic**.

The key idea: phase space is not just a blank canvas. It has a built-in way to measure areas (actually, oriented areas of two-dimensional slices). This area-measuring rule is the **symplectic form**, and it is what makes Hamilton's equations take their characteristic symmetric shape.

The symplectic form assigns a number to every pair of directions at each point. For the coordinate directions $q$ and $p$, the form gives $+1$. For $q$ and $q$, it gives $0$. For $p$ and $p$, it gives $0$. And for $p$ and $q$, it gives $-1$. This assignment is antisymmetric (swapping the order flips the sign) and non-degenerate (every direction pairs nontrivially with some other direction).

**Darboux's theorem** says something remarkable: this symplectic structure is locally unique. No matter what coordinates you use, you can always find new coordinates (called Darboux coordinates) in which the symplectic form looks exactly like the standard $dq \wedge dp$ form. There are no "local invariants" of symplectic geometry — unlike Riemannian geometry, where curvature is a local invariant.

This means symplectic geometry is "softer" than Riemannian geometry. All the interesting structure is global, not local.

## Visual [Beginner]

![A phase space portrait showing symplectic area. An ellipse in the qp-plane represents a region of phase space. Arrows show the symplectic form evaluating to +1 on the pair (q-direction, p-direction) and -1 on (p-direction, q-direction).](/img/09.04.05-symplectic-darboux-placeholder.svg)

The symplectic form measures the oriented area of projections onto each $q^i$-$p_i$ plane. Darboux's theorem guarantees that near any point, coordinates can be chosen so these measurements take the standard form.

## Worked example [Beginner]

For a 2D phase space with coordinates $(q, p)$, the symplectic form evaluates on basis vectors as: $\omega(\hat{q}, \hat{p}) = +1$, $\omega(\hat{p}, \hat{q}) = -1$, $\omega(\hat{q}, \hat{q}) = 0$, $\omega(\hat{p}, \hat{p}) = 0$. In the language of differential forms: $\omega = dq \wedge dp$.

For a 4D phase space $(q_1, q_2, p_1, p_2)$: $\omega = dq_1 \wedge dp_1 + dq_2 \wedge dp_2$. The form measures the oriented area in each conjugate $(q_i, p_i)$ plane and adds them up.

Now consider a coordinate change: $Q = q\cos\alpha + p\sin\alpha$, $P = -q\sin\alpha + p\cos\alpha$ (a rotation in phase space). Then $dQ \wedge dP = (\cos\alpha\, dq + \sin\alpha\, dp) \wedge (-\sin\alpha\, dq + \cos\alpha\, dp) = \cos^2\alpha\, dq \wedge dp - \sin^2\alpha\, dp \wedge dq = dq \wedge dp$. The symplectic form is preserved — this rotation is a canonical transformation.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Symplectic vector space.** A **symplectic vector space** is a finite-dimensional vector space $V$ equipped with a bilinear form $\omega : V \times V \to \mathbb{R}$ that is (i) antisymmetric ($\omega(v, w) = -\omega(w, v)$) and (ii) non-degenerate ($\omega(v, w) = 0$ for all $w$ implies $v = 0$). A standard result: every symplectic vector space has even dimension $2n$ and admits a basis in which $\omega$ takes the canonical form $J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & I_n \\ -I_n & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Symplectic manifold.** A **symplectic manifold** $(M, \omega)$ is a smooth manifold $M$ equipped with a closed ($d\omega = 0$) and non-degenerate 2-form $\omega$. Non-degeneracy means that at each point $p \in M$, the map $v \mapsto \omega_p(v, \cdot)$ from $T_pM$ to $T_p^*M$ is an isomorphism. Closedness ($d\omega = 0$) is an integrability condition.

The dimension of a symplectic manifold is always even: $\dim M = 2n$.

**The canonical symplectic form on $T^*Q$.** For a configuration manifold $Q$, the cotangent bundle $T^*Q$ carries a canonical symplectic form. The **Liouville 1-form** (tautological 1-form) is $\theta \in \Omega^1(T^*Q)$ defined by $\theta_\alpha(v) = \alpha(d\pi(v))$ for $\alpha \in T^*Q$ and $v \in T_\alpha(T^*Q)$. In local coordinates $(q^i, p_i)$: $\theta = p_i\, dq^i$. The **canonical symplectic form** is $\omega = -d\theta = dq^i \wedge dp_i$.

**Darboux's theorem.** *Let $(M, \omega)$ be a $2n$-dimensional symplectic manifold. For every point $p \in M$, there exists a neighbourhood $U$ of $p$ and local coordinates $(q^1, \ldots, q^n, p_1, \ldots, p_n)$ on $U$ such that $\omega|_U = \sum_i dq^i \wedge dp_i$.*

### Comparison with Riemannian geometry

| Property | Riemannian $(M, g)$ | Symplectic $(M, \omega)$ |
|---|---|---|
| Local normal form | $g = \delta_{ij}\, dx^i dx^j$ at one point only | $\omega = dq^i \wedge dp_i$ in an open neighbourhood |
| Local invariant | Curvature tensor | None (Darboux) |
| Dimension | Any | Even only |
| Existence on $M$ | Always (partition of unity) | Topological constraints ($\omega^n$ must be a volume form; $H^{2}(M) \neq 0$ may be needed) |

**Symplectomorphism.** A diffeomorphism $\Phi : M \to M$ with $\Phi^*\omega = \omega$ is a **symplectomorphism**. In Darboux coordinates, this is a canonical transformation. The symplectomorphisms form a group $\mathrm{Symp}(M, \omega)$; a theorem of Eliashberg and Gromov shows that $\mathrm{Symp}(M, \omega)$ is $C^0$-closed in the diffeomorphism group, exhibiting **symplectic rigidity**.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Key result (Darboux's theorem via the Moser trick).** *Let $\omega_0$ and $\omega_1$ be two symplectic forms on a manifold $M$ that agree at a point $p$: $\omega_0(p) = \omega_1(p)$. Then there exist neighbourhoods $U_0, U_1$ of $p$ and a diffeomorphism $\Phi : U_0 \to U_1$ with $\Phi^*\omega_1 = \omega_0$ and $\Phi(p) = p$.*

**Proof (Moser's trick).** Consider the family $\omega_t = (1-t)\omega_0 + t\omega_1$ for $t \in [0, 1]$. Since $\omega_0(p) = \omega_1(p)$, the forms $\omega_t$ are all non-degenerate near $p$. The derivative $d\omega_t/dt = \omega_1 - \omega_0 =: \sigma$. By the Poincar\'e lemma (applied in a neighbourhood of $p$), since $d\sigma = d\omega_1 - d\omega_0 = 0$, there exists a 1-form $\tau$ with $d\tau = \sigma$ and $\tau(p) = 0$.

Define the time-dependent vector field $X_t$ by $i_{X_t}\omega_t = -\tau$ (solvable because $\omega_t$ is non-degenerate). Let $\Phi_t$ be the flow of $X_t$ with $\Phi_0 = \mathrm{id}$. Then:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\Phi_t^*\omega_t = \Phi_t^*(\mathcal{L}_{X_t}\omega_t + \dot\omega_t) = \Phi_t^*(di_{X_t}\omega_t + i_{X_t}d\omega_t + \sigma) = \Phi_t^*(d(-\tau) + 0 + d\tau) = 0.
$$

So $\Phi_t^*\omega_t$ is constant: $\Phi_1^*\omega_1 = \Phi_0^*\omega_0 = \omega_0$. Set $\Phi = \Phi_1$. $\square$

**Corollary (Darboux's theorem).** *Apply the result with $\omega_0 = $ the standard form $dq^i \wedge dp_i$ on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ and $\omega_1 = $ the given symplectic form, after choosing a linear isomorphism of the tangent spaces that matches the forms at $p$.*

**Bridge.** The symplectic structure builds toward the full geometric formulation of Hamiltonian mechanics and the classification of integrable systems; the foundational reason is that Darboux's theorem guarantees local coordinates in which Hamilton's equations take their canonical form, and this is exactly the starting point for the action-angle variable construction [09.06.01]. The central insight — that symplectic geometry has no local invariants — distinguishes it fundamentally from Riemannian geometry. Putting these together, the symplectic form is the geometric object that unifies the Hamiltonian equations, the Poisson bracket, the Liouville volume, and the canonical transformations into a single coherent framework. This structure appears again in the Liouville-Arnold theorem [09.06.02] and generalises to Poisson manifolds where the form may be degenerate.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the standard symplectic matrix $J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & I \\ -I & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ satisfies $J^2 = -I$ and $J^T J = I$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Direct matrix multiplication.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$J^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & I \\ -I & 0 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & I \\ -I & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -I & 0 \\ 0 & -I \end{pmatrix} = -I$. $J^T = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -I \\ I & 0 \end{pmatrix} = -J$. So $J^TJ = (-J)(J) = -J^2 = -(-I) = I$. The symplectic matrix is both orthogonal-like ($J^TJ = I$) and satisfies $J^2 = -I$ (like the complex unit $i$).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that a $2n \times 2n$ matrix $M$ is symplectic ($M^TJM = J$) if and only if it preserves the Poisson bracket: $\{f \circ M, g \circ M\} = \{f, g\} \circ M$ for all $f, g$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Express the Poisson bracket in matrix notation: $\{f, g\} = (\nabla f)^T J (\nabla g)$ where $\nabla f$ is the gradient in combined $(q, p)$ coordinates.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In matrix notation, $\{f, g\}(z) = (\nabla_z f)^T J (\nabla_z g)$ where $z = (q, p)$. Under $z' = Mz$: $\nabla_z(f \circ M) = M^T \nabla_{z'} f$ (chain rule). So $\{f \circ M, g \circ M\}(z) = (M^T \nabla f)^T J (M^T \nabla g) = (\nabla f)^T M J M^T (\nabla g)$. For this to equal $\{f, g\}(Mz) = (\nabla f)^T J (\nabla g)$ for all $f, g$, we need $MJM^T = J$. Taking the transpose: $MJM^T = J$ is equivalent to $M^TJM = J$ (using $J^T = -J$ and $J^{-1} = -J$). So $M$ preserves the bracket iff $M$ is symplectic.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the Liouville volume form $\omega^n/n!$ for the standard symplectic form $\omega = dq^i \wedge dp_i$ on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$. Show that it equals $dq^1 \wedge dp_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge dq^n \wedge dp_n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The wedge product $\omega^n = \omega \wedge \cdots \wedge \omega$ ($n$ times) gives $n!$ copies of the volume form, one for each permutation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\omega = \sum_i dq^i \wedge dp_i$. The $n$-fold wedge $\omega^n = \sum_{i_1, \ldots, i_n} dq^{i_1} \wedge dp_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge dq^{i_n} \wedge dp_{i_n}$. Only the terms where $(i_1, \ldots, i_n)$ is a permutation of $(1, \ldots, n)$ survive (all indices distinct). There are $n!$ such permutations, and each gives the same form $dq^1 \wedge dp_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge dq^n \wedge dp_n$ up to sign (the sign of the permutation), but the sum of $n!$ identical terms divided by $n!$ gives exactly one copy. So $\omega^n/n! = dq^1 \wedge dp_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge dq^n \wedge dp_n$. This is the Liouville volume, and its preservation under Hamiltonian flow is Liouville's theorem.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the cotangent bundle $T^*S^1$ of the circle is a symplectic manifold with the canonical symplectic form. Identify the Darboux coordinates.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$S^1$ has local coordinate $\theta$, and $T^*S^1$ has coordinates $(\theta, p_\theta)$ with $\omega = d\theta \wedge dp_\theta$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$Q = S^1$ with coordinate $\theta$ (angle, periodic mod $2\pi$). The cotangent bundle $T^*S^1$ is a cylinder with coordinates $(\theta, p_\theta)$ where $p_\theta$ is the angular momentum. The tautological 1-form is $\theta = p_\theta\, d\theta$ and $\omega = d\theta \wedge dp_\theta$. This is closed ($d\omega = 0$) and non-degenerate. The Darboux coordinates are $(\theta, p_\theta)$ themselves. The Hamiltonian flow of $H = p_\theta^2/(2I)$ (free rigid body in 2D) gives $\dot\theta = p_\theta/I$, $\dot{p}_\theta = 0$ — uniform rotation at constant angular momentum.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the symplectic form on a compact manifold $M$ cannot be exact (i.e., $\omega = d\alpha$ for some 1-form $\alpha$) unless $M$ has zero volume.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If $\omega = d\alpha$, then $\omega^n = d\alpha \wedge \omega^{n-1} = d(\alpha \wedge \omega^{n-1})$. Apply Stokes' theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $\omega = d\alpha$, then $\omega^n = (d\alpha) \wedge \omega^{n-1} = d(\alpha \wedge \omega^{n-1})$ (using $d\omega = 0$ and the Leibniz rule for $d$). The volume form is $\omega^n/n! = d(\alpha \wedge \omega^{n-1}/n!)$. By Stokes' theorem, the volume of $M$ is $\int_M \omega^n/n! = \int_M d(\alpha \wedge \omega^{n-1}/n!) = \int_{\partial M} \alpha \wedge \omega^{n-1}/n! = 0$ (since $M$ has no boundary). So the volume is zero, contradicting non-degeneracy (which guarantees $\omega^n/n!$ is a positive volume form). Therefore $\omega$ cannot be exact on a compact manifold. This is why $S^2$ with the area form is symplectic but $S^2$ with an exact form is not.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the linear symplectic group $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{R}) = \{M \in \mathrm{GL}(2n, \mathbb{R}) : M^TJM = J\}$ is a Lie group of dimension $n(2n+1)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The condition $M^TJM = J$ is a matrix equation. The Lie algebra consists of matrices $X$ with $X^TJ + JX = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The defining condition $M^TJM = J$ gives $\mathrm{GL}(2n)$ constraints. Near $M = I$: perturb $M = I + \epsilon X$. Then $(I + \epsilon X)^TJ(I + \epsilon X) = J + \epsilon(X^TJ + JX) + O(\epsilon^2) = J$. So the Lie algebra is $\mathfrak{sp}(2n) = \{X : X^TJ + JX = 0\}$. Writing $X = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{pmatrix}$ in $n \times n$ blocks: $X^TJ + JX = \begin{pmatrix} C - C^T & A^T + D \\ -(A + D^T) & B - B^T \end{pmatrix} = 0$. So $C = C^T$ (symmetric), $B = B^T$ (symmetric), $D = -A^T$. The free parameters are $A$ ($n^2$), $B$ ($n(n+1)/2$), and $C$ ($n(n+1)/2$), giving $n^2 + n(n+1) = n(2n+1)$ dimensions.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the pullback of the symplectic form under the Hamiltonian flow equals the symplectic form: $\phi_t^*\omega = \omega$.

</details>

Since this holds at every point and for all time, $\phi_t^*\omega = \omega$ for all $t$. This is the geometric content of "Hamiltonian flow preserves the symplectic structure."

</details>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The formalisation of Darboux's theorem requires: (1) the canonical symplectic form on $T^*Q$ as a closed, non-degenerate 2-form; (2) the Moser trick (a homotopy argument using the Poincar\'e lemma and the flow of a time-dependent vector field); and (3) the construction of the local coordinate chart. Mathlib has the ingredients for (1) (tangent/cotangent bundles, exterior algebra) but not assembled into the symplectic structure. The Moser trick requires existence of solutions to time-dependent ODEs and the Poincar\'e lemma, both available in Mathlib.

## Advanced results: symplectic rigidity and the group of symplectomorphisms [Master]

### Symplectic rigidity

A fundamental result of Gromov (1985) is the **non-squeezing theorem**: a symplectic transformation of $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ cannot map a ball of radius $R$ into a cylinder of radius $r < R$ when the cylinder is defined by $q_1^2 + p_1^2 \leq r^2$. This is a rigidity result: volume-preserving transformations can squeeze balls arbitrarily (by the same volume, a ball can be deformed into a thin cylinder), but symplectic transformations cannot.

**Proposition (Gromov's non-squeezing theorem).** *If $\Phi : \mathbb{R}^{2n} \to \mathbb{R}^{2n}$ is a symplectomorphism, then $\Phi(B_R) \subset Z_r$ implies $r \geq R$, where $B_R$ is the ball of radius $R$ and $Z_r = \{(q, p) : q_1^2 + p_1^2 \leq r^2\}$ is the symplectic cylinder.*

The proof uses $J$-holomorphic curves and is beyond the scope of this unit. The theorem illustrates that symplectic geometry imposes stronger constraints than volume preservation: the symplectic form controls area in each conjugate plane, not just the total volume.

### The Hofer metric

The group of Hamiltonian symplectomorphisms $\mathrm{Ham}(M, \omega)$ carries a remarkable bi-invariant metric, the **Hofer metric**, defined by

$$
d(\phi, \psi) \;=\; \inf_{H} \int_0^1 \left(\max_M H_t - \min_M H_t\right) dt,
$$

where the infimum is over all Hamiltonians $H_t$ generating a path from $\phi$ to $\psi$. The existence of this metric (proved by Hofer, 1990) is another manifestation of symplectic rigidity.

**Synthesis.** The symplectic structure is the foundational reason that Hamiltonian mechanics has a geometric formulation; the central insight is that Darboux's theorem eliminates all local invariants, making symplectic geometry "softer" than Riemannian geometry yet still rigid enough to support the non-squeezing theorem and the Hofer metric. This is exactly the duality that makes symplectic geometry both tractable (locally canonical) and rich (globally substantive). Putting these together, the symplectic form unifies the Hamiltonian equations, the Poisson bracket, the Liouville volume, and the canonical transformations, and the generalisation to Poisson manifolds extends this framework to systems with degeneracies. The symplectic structure appears again in the action-angle variable construction [09.06.01] and the Liouville-Arnold theorem [09.06.02].

## Connections [Master]

- **[09.04.02]** Hamilton's equations are the integral curves of the Hamiltonian vector field defined by the symplectic form via $i_{X_H}\omega = dH$.
- **[09.04.03]** The Poisson bracket is the algebraic shadow of the symplectic form: $\{f, g\} = \omega(X_f, X_g)$.
- **[09.06.01]** Action-angle variables are Darboux coordinates adapted to an integrable system; the existence of these coordinates is guaranteed by Darboux's theorem plus the involution condition.
- **[09.06.02]** The Liouville-Arnold theorem constructs action-angle coordinates using the symplectic structure of the invariant tori.
- **[12.03.01]** Quantum phase space inherits the symplectic structure via the Weyl quantisation map; the canonical commutation relations are the quantised symplectic form.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The symplectic form was first introduced implicitly by Lagrange (1808) in his study of planetary perturbations, where he noticed that certain combinations of orbital elements satisfied simple differential equations. The explicit geometric formulation is due to Cartan (1922), who introduced the exterior calculus of differential forms and recognised that the canonical equations are the integral curves of a Hamiltonian vector field.

The term "symplectic" was coined by Weyl (1939) as a calque of "complex" (both from the Greek for "interwoven"), replacing the older term "complex group" for what is now called the symplectic group. The modern symplectic geometry programme was initiated by Arnold (1960s-1970s), who recognised that Darboux's theorem, the Poincar\'e integral invariant, and the Hamiltonian flow preservation are all facets of a single geometric structure.

Gromov's non-squeezing theorem (1985) was a watershed: it showed that symplectic geometry is not merely the study of a closed non-degenerate 2-form but a genuinely new geometric discipline with its own rigidity phenomena. The subsequent development of symplectic topology (Floer homology, quantum cohomology, Fukaya categories) has transformed the field.

A philosophical point: symplectic geometry occupies a middle ground between the "rigid" world of Riemannian geometry (with its curvature invariants) and the "flexible" world of topology (with no local invariants at all). Darboux's theorem says the local structure is canonical, but Gromov's theorem says the global structure is not. This tension between local uniformity and global richness is the driving force behind modern symplectic geometry.
