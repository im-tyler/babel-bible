## Intuition [Beginner]

You already know Newton's $F = ma$. It says: pick a force law, get a second-order differential equation for position, solve, predict the motion. The Lagrangian reformulation rewrites this as a single principle — paths that nature picks minimise (or extremise) the *action*, a number you compute by adding up "kinetic minus potential" along a path — and recovers the same equations. Hamilton's equations are the third move in this sequence, and they change *which variables you track*.

Instead of describing a particle by its position $q$ and velocity $\dot q$, describe it by its position $q$ and its **momentum** $p = m\dot q$. The two pieces of data live on equal footing — neither one is derived from the other. The space whose points are pairs $(q, p)$ is called **phase space**, and a state of the system is a single point in phase space.

The total energy, written as a function of $q$ and $p$ rather than $q$ and $\dot q$, is the **Hamiltonian** $H(q, p)$. For a particle in a potential,

$$
H(q, p) \;=\; \frac{p^2}{2m} + V(q),
$$

which you can read off as kinetic-plus-potential, just with $\tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2$ rewritten as $p^2/(2m)$ using $p = m\dot q$.

Hamilton's equations are then a surprisingly symmetric pair. In words: the rate of change of position equals "the slope of $H$ in the momentum direction", and the rate of change of momentum equals "minus the slope of $H$ in the position direction". Two first-order equations replace Newton's one second-order equation. The minus sign is load-bearing: it's what makes the flow conserve energy and what makes phase space behave like an incompressible fluid rather than a draining sink.

The formal statement, in symbols, lives in the Intermediate-tier *Formal definition* section. The two slopes-of-$H$ rules above are all you need at the Beginner tier.

Why bother? Three reasons that compound as you go deeper.

First, **conservation laws fall out automatically**. If $H$ does not depend on $q$, then $\dot p = 0$ — momentum is conserved. If $H$ does not depend on time, then $H$ itself is conserved — that's energy conservation. Symmetries of the Hamiltonian and conserved quantities are paired one-to-one.

Second, **the framework is the right shape for quantum mechanics**. The Schrödinger equation is Hamilton's equations with operators in place of $q$ and $p$ and a commutator in place of the Poisson bracket. Every quantum textbook starts by promoting some classical Hamiltonian to an operator. You cannot build that bridge from Newton's $F = ma$ directly.

Third, **the geometry behind the symmetric pair is symplectic geometry**, which turns out to be the natural geometric structure for any conservative mechanical system, classical or quantum. This is the perspective developed in the Master tier.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the simplest possible mechanical system: a mass on a spring, with Hamiltonian $H(q, p) = \tfrac{1}{2}p^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}q^2$ (units chosen so the spring constant and mass equal 1). Phase space is the $(q, p)$-plane. Level sets of $H$ are circles centred at the origin — one circle per energy.

Hamilton's equations, read off the picture, say: the motion in phase space is a rigid rotation. A point starting at $(1, 0)$ — the spring fully stretched, at rest — moves clockwise: down to $(0, -1)$ at quarter-period, where the mass passes through the origin with maximum (negative) momentum; then to $(-1, 0)$, fully compressed; then $(0, 1)$, passing the origin again with positive momentum; then back to $(1, 0)$.

The motion *follows* the level set of $H$. It does not cross level sets. Energy is conserved automatically, not as an extra rule you have to remember.

![Phase-space portrait of the harmonic oscillator. Circles are level sets of H = (p^2 + q^2)/2 = E. Arrows show the Hamiltonian flow, rotating clockwise. Each orbit is closed and periodic; energy is conserved because the flow is tangent to the level sets of H.](/img/09.04.02-harmonic-oscillator-phase-portrait-placeholder.svg)

Now scale this picture up: the Hamiltonian is any function $H(q, p)$, the level sets are any nested family of curves (or surfaces in higher dimension), and the flow always tracks the level sets — never crossing them. The minus sign that appears between the two equations is exactly what makes "flow *along* level sets of $H$, not *toward* low values of $H$" come out right.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the same harmonic oscillator, $H(q, p) = \tfrac{1}{2}p^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}q^2$, and start the mass at $q(0) = 1$, $p(0) = 0$ — fully stretched, at rest.

Step 1. Read off the two slopes of $H$. Treating $q$ as a constant and varying $p$, the slope of $H$ in the $p$-direction is $p$. Treating $p$ as a constant and varying $q$, the slope of $H$ in the $q$-direction is $q$.

Step 2. Write Hamilton's equations (rate of $q$ = first slope; rate of $p$ = minus second slope):

$$
\dot q \;=\; p, \qquad \dot p \;=\; -q.
$$

Step 3. Differentiate the first equation in time and substitute the second:

$$
\ddot q \;=\; \dot p \;=\; -q.
$$

This is the familiar second-order equation $\ddot q + q = 0$, with general solution $q(t) = A\cos t + B\sin t$. The initial conditions $q(0) = 1$ and $\dot q(0) = p(0) = 0$ pin $A = 1$, $B = 0$, so

$$
q(t) \;=\; \cos t, \qquad p(t) \;=\; \dot q(t) \;=\; -\sin t.
$$

Step 4. Check energy conservation:

$$
H(q(t), p(t)) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\sin^2 t + \tfrac{1}{2}\cos^2 t \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}.
$$

Constant in time, as required.

What this tells us: Hamilton's equations recover the familiar oscillator motion, but they get it as a flow in the *plane* of $(q, p)$ pairs — rotating clockwise around the origin with unit angular speed. The level set $H = \tfrac{1}{2}$ is the unit circle, and the motion stays on it forever.

**Exercise (easy, multiple choice).**

A particle has Hamiltonian $H(q, p) = p^2/(2m) + V(q)$. Hamilton's first equation (rate of $q$ equals slope of $H$ in $p$) recovers which familiar relation?

A. $\dot q = mp$
B. $\dot q = p/m$
C. $\dot q = -p/m$
D. $\dot q = -V'(q)$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The slope of $p^2/(2m)$ in the $p$-direction is $p/m$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$\dot q = p/m$** — option B.

This is the definition of momentum re-derived: $p = m\dot q$. Hamilton's equations *enforce* this relation rather than assume it, which is why $q$ and $p$ can be treated as independent variables in phase space even though they end up dynamically coupled.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise (easy, multiple choice).**

For the same Hamiltonian, Hamilton's second equation (rate of $p$ equals *minus* slope of $H$ in $q$) gives:

A. $\dot p = V'(q)$
B. $\dot p = -V'(q)$
C. $\dot p = -V(q)$
D. $\dot p = -V'(q)/m$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Only the potential depends on $q$; the kinetic part $p^2/(2m)$ does not. The slope of $V(q)$ in the $q$-direction is its ordinary derivative $V'(q)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$\dot p = -V'(q)$** — option B.

Combined with $\dot q = p/m$ from the previous exercise, $\dot p = -V'(q)$ is exactly Newton's second law $m\ddot q = -V'(q) = F(q)$. Hamilton's equations are Newton's second law rewritten as a first-order system in phase space, and the minus sign in the second equation is what produces the force.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise (easy, true/false).**

If the Hamiltonian $H(q, p)$ has no explicit time dependence, then $H$ is conserved along any solution of Hamilton's equations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate $H(q(t), p(t))$ in time using the chain rule, and substitute Hamilton's equations.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.**

Differentiate $H$ along the trajectory: $dH/dt$ equals (slope of $H$ in $q$) times $\dot q$ plus (slope of $H$ in $p$) times $\dot p$. Substitute Hamilton's equations — $\dot q$ equals the slope of $H$ in $p$, and $\dot p$ equals *minus* the slope of $H$ in $q$ — and the two terms cancel exactly. So $H$ is constant along trajectories. When $H$ is the total energy, this *is* energy conservation, derived from the structure of the equations rather than assumed.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise (medium, numeric).**

A unit-mass particle in the potential $V(q) = \tfrac{1}{2}q^2$ has phase-space trajectory $(q(t), p(t)) = (\cos t, -\sin t)$ starting from $(1, 0)$. After time $t = \pi/2$, what is the value of the Hamiltonian $H(q(\pi/2), p(\pi/2))$?

Energy is conserved. You don't actually need to evaluate $q(\pi/2)$ and $p(\pi/2)$ — but doing so is a good check.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$H = 1/2$.**

Energy conservation: $H$ takes the same value at every time, so $H(q(\pi/2), p(\pi/2)) = H(q(0), p(0)) = \tfrac{1}{2}(0)^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}(1)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}$. Direct check: $q(\pi/2) = 0$, $p(\pi/2) = -1$, so $H = \tfrac{1}{2}(-1)^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}(0)^2 = \tfrac{1}{2}$. Both methods agree.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $Q$ be the configuration manifold of a mechanical system — the space whose points are the system's spatial configurations. For a single particle in $\mathbb{R}^3$, $Q = \mathbb{R}^3$; for a rigid body, $Q = \mathrm{SO}(3) \times \mathbb{R}^3$; for $N$ particles, $Q = \mathbb{R}^{3N}$ modulo constraints. A point in $Q$ is denoted $q$; a smooth curve $t \mapsto q(t)$ has tangent vector $\dot q(t) \in T_{q(t)}Q$.

A Lagrangian system [05.00.01] is governed by a smooth function $L : TQ \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ (the Lagrangian), and the equations of motion are the Euler-Lagrange equations

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i} \;-\; \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} \;=\; 0.
$$

The **Hamiltonian formulation** replaces the tangent bundle $TQ$ by the cotangent bundle $T^*Q$ via the Legendre transform [05.00.03]. The **conjugate momentum** of $q^i$ is

$$
p_i \;:=\; \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}.
$$

Assuming the **Hessian condition** $\det(\partial^2 L / \partial \dot q^i \partial \dot q^j) \neq 0$ (the Lagrangian is *regular*; if this holds globally we call $L$ *hyper-regular*), the fibre Legendre transform $\mathbb{F}L : TQ \to T^*Q$, $(q, \dot q) \mapsto (q, p)$, is a local diffeomorphism. The **Hamiltonian** is then the Legendre transform of $L$:

$$
H(q, p, t) \;:=\; \big(p_i \dot q^i - L(q, \dot q, t)\big)\Big|_{\dot q = \dot q(q, p, t)},
$$

where $\dot q(q, p, t)$ is obtained by solving $p_i = \partial L / \partial \dot q^i$ for $\dot q$. We adopt the **summation convention** throughout: repeated upper-lower index pairs are summed over $i = 1, \ldots, n$ where $n = \dim Q$.

**Hamilton's equations** are the first-order system

$$
\boxed{\;\;\dot q^i \;=\; \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}, \qquad \dot p_i \;=\; -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i}, \qquad \frac{\partial L}{\partial t} \;=\; -\frac{\partial H}{\partial t}\;\;}
$$

The third equation governs explicit time dependence; for time-independent Lagrangians it is vacuous.

In coordinates the system has $2n$ first-order ODEs in place of $n$ second-order Euler-Lagrange equations. The space $T^*Q$ with coordinates $(q^i, p_i)$ is **phase space**; a state of the system is a point in $T^*Q$.

The Hamiltonian formulation requires fixing the sign convention $p_i = +\partial L / \partial \dot q^i$ and the sign convention in the Legendre transform $H = p\dot q - L$. Other conventions (replacing $H$ by $-H$ or $L$ by $-L$, or relabelling $q$ and $p$ symmetrically) appear in the literature and exchange the signs in Hamilton's equations. The convention adopted here is the one used by Arnold, Marsden-Ratiu, Taylor, and Tong; Landau-Lifshitz and Goldstein use the same convention. State the convention before computing.

**The Poisson bracket** [05.02.02] of two smooth functions $f, g : T^*Q \to \mathbb{R}$ is

$$
\{f, g\} \;:=\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial p_i} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial q^i}.
$$

It is bilinear, antisymmetric, satisfies the Leibniz rule $\{f, gh\} = \{f, g\}h + g\{f, h\}$, and satisfies the Jacobi identity $\{f, \{g, h\}\} + \{g, \{h, f\}\} + \{h, \{f, g\}\} = 0$. The pair $(T^*Q, \{\cdot,\cdot\})$ is a **Poisson manifold**; the bracket is the algebraic shadow of the canonical symplectic form $\omega = dp_i \wedge dq^i$ developed in the Master tier.

The **evolution of an observable** $f(q, p, t)$ along a Hamiltonian trajectory is

$$
\frac{df}{dt} \;=\; \{f, H\} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}.
$$

Hamilton's equations themselves are the special case $f = q^i$ ($\dot q^i = \{q^i, H\}$) and $f = p_i$ ($\dot p_i = \{p_i, H\}$), using the **canonical Poisson relations**

$$
\{q^i, q^j\} \;=\; 0, \qquad \{p_i, p_j\} \;=\; 0, \qquad \{q^i, p_j\} \;=\; \delta^i_j.
$$

A function $f$ with $\{f, H\} = 0$ and $\partial f/\partial t = 0$ is a **conserved quantity** (a *constant of motion*); its value is preserved along every trajectory.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- A **non-hyper-regular** Lagrangian — one for which the Hessian $\partial^2 L / \partial \dot q^i \partial \dot q^j$ is singular at some $(q, \dot q)$ — does not admit a well-defined Hamiltonian via the recipe above. Gauge theories and the relativistic point particle in their natural Lagrangian forms have this problem and are handled by the Dirac-Bergmann constraint algorithm, not by the textbook Legendre transform.
- The momentum $p_i = \partial L / \partial \dot q^i$ is **not** in general equal to $m_i \dot q^i$. For a particle in an electromagnetic field with $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{\mathbf{q}}^2 + e\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{q})\cdot \dot{\mathbf{q}} - eV(\mathbf{q})$, the canonical momentum is $\mathbf{p} = m\dot{\mathbf{q}} + e\mathbf{A}$ — the *kinetic* momentum $m\dot{\mathbf{q}}$ and the *canonical* (conjugate) momentum $\mathbf{p}$ differ by $e\mathbf{A}$. This is what makes gauge transformations of $\mathbf{A}$ a genuine transformation of phase space rather than a relabelling.
- The Hamiltonian is **not** in general equal to the total energy. The two coincide when $L = T - V$ with $T$ a homogeneous quadratic in $\dot q$ and $V$ time-independent. In rotating frames or for explicitly-time-dependent constraints $H \neq T + V$, even though Hamilton's equations still apply.
- Hamilton's equations are *first-order*; the apparent doubling of equation count from $n$ to $2n$ is balanced by the halving of order from $2$ to $1$. The total dimension of the initial-value problem — $2n$ scalars — is the same as in the Lagrangian formulation.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Equivalence of Hamilton's equations and the Euler-Lagrange equations under hyper-regularity).** *Let $L : TQ \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be a hyper-regular Lagrangian, $\mathbb{F}L : TQ \to T^*Q$ the fibre Legendre transform, and $H : T^*Q \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ the associated Hamiltonian. Then a smooth curve $\gamma(t) = (q(t), \dot q(t))$ in $TQ$ satisfies the Euler-Lagrange equations for $L$ if and only if the curve $\sigma(t) := \mathbb{F}L(\gamma(t)) = (q(t), p(t))$ in $T^*Q$ satisfies Hamilton's equations for $H$.*

**Proof.** The Hamiltonian is defined by $H(q, p, t) = p_i \dot q^i - L(q, \dot q, t)$ where $\dot q = \dot q(q, p, t)$ is determined by inverting $p_i = \partial L / \partial \dot q^i$.

Differentiate $H$ with respect to $p_i$, treating $\dot q$ as a function of $(q, p, t)$:

$$
\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i} \;=\; \dot q^i + p_j \frac{\partial \dot q^j}{\partial p_i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^j}\frac{\partial \dot q^j}{\partial p_i}.
$$

The last two terms cancel because $p_j = \partial L / \partial \dot q^j$, leaving

$$
\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i} \;=\; \dot q^i. \tag{$\ast$}
$$

Differentiate $H$ with respect to $q^i$:

$$
\frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i} \;=\; p_j \frac{\partial \dot q^j}{\partial q^i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^j}\frac{\partial \dot q^j}{\partial q^i} \;=\; -\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i}, \tag{$\ast\ast$}
$$

again using $p_j = \partial L / \partial \dot q^j$ to cancel two terms. Differentiating with respect to $t$ gives, similarly, $\partial H / \partial t = -\partial L / \partial t$.

Now suppose $\gamma$ satisfies the Euler-Lagrange equations $\tfrac{d}{dt}(\partial L / \partial \dot q^i) = \partial L / \partial q^i$. Along $\sigma = \mathbb{F}L \circ \gamma$ the conjugate momentum is $p_i(t) = \partial L / \partial \dot q^i|_{\gamma(t)}$, so

$$
\dot p_i(t) \;=\; \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i} \;=\; \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} \;\overset{(\ast\ast)}{=}\; -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i},
$$

which is the second Hamilton equation. Equation ($\ast$) gives $\dot q^i = \partial H / \partial p_i$ identically along $\sigma$ — this is the first Hamilton equation, true by definition of $\dot q(q, p, t)$ in terms of $(q, p)$. So $\sigma$ satisfies Hamilton's equations.

For the converse, suppose $\sigma(t) = (q(t), p(t))$ satisfies Hamilton's equations. Equation ($\ast$) along $\sigma$ gives $\dot q^i = \partial H / \partial p_i$, consistent with the first Hamilton equation. Differentiate $p_i(t) = \partial L / \partial \dot q^i$ in time:

$$
\dot p_i \;=\; \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i} \;\overset{\text{Hamilton}}{=}\; -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i} \;\overset{(\ast\ast)}{=}\; \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i},
$$

so the Euler-Lagrange equations hold along $\gamma = \mathbb{F}L^{-1} \circ \sigma$. ∎

**Corollary.** *Under the same hypotheses, the action $S[\gamma] = \int L\, dt$ on paths in $Q$ equals the action $\tilde S[\sigma] = \int (p_i \dot q^i - H)\, dt$ on paths in $T^*Q$, up to boundary terms, and the variational principle $\delta \tilde S = 0$ with $q$ fixed at endpoints (free variations of $p$) is equivalent to Hamilton's equations.* The substitution $H = p_i \dot q^i - L$ is exactly Legendre conjugation; the variational principle on $T^*Q$ is the *modified Hamilton principle* (Hamilton-Pontryagin, or Cartan's variational principle in the symplectic case).

The role of hyper-regularity is to make the Legendre transform globally invertible. For *regular* Lagrangians (Hessian invertible locally, not globally), the equivalence is local; the global picture requires either restricting to a region or augmenting with the inverse Legendre transform on each chart.

### Worked example: the Kepler problem at intermediate level

A particle of mass $m$ in the gravitational potential $V(r) = -k/r$ where $k = GMm > 0$ and $r = |\mathbf{q}|$ has Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot{\mathbf{q}}|^2 + k/r$. In spherical coordinates $(r, \theta, \varphi)$, $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot r^2 + r^2 \dot\theta^2 + r^2 \sin^2\theta \,\dot\varphi^2) + k/r$.

The conjugate momenta:
$$
p_r = m\dot r, \quad p_\theta = mr^2 \dot\theta, \quad p_\varphi = mr^2 \sin^2\theta \,\dot\varphi.
$$

Inverting and substituting into $H = p_i\dot q^i - L$:

$$
H(r, \theta, \varphi, p_r, p_\theta, p_\varphi) \;=\; \frac{p_r^2}{2m} + \frac{p_\theta^2}{2mr^2} + \frac{p_\varphi^2}{2mr^2 \sin^2\theta} - \frac{k}{r}.
$$

Two cyclic coordinates: $\varphi$ does not appear in $H$, so $\dot p_\varphi = 0$ and $p_\varphi$ is conserved. Restrict to the equatorial plane $\theta = \pi/2$ ($p_\theta = 0$) to reduce to the planar problem. The remaining Hamiltonian is $H = p_r^2/(2m) + p_\varphi^2/(2mr^2) - k/r$ on a two-dimensional phase space $(r, p_r)$ at fixed $\ell := p_\varphi$, with the centrifugal $\ell^2/(2mr^2)$ now in the effective potential — the textbook reduction Hamilton's equations make automatic.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

A particle of mass $m$ and charge $e$ moves in three dimensions in the presence of a vector potential $\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{q})$ and a scalar potential $V(\mathbf{q})$. The Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot{\mathbf{q}}|^2 + e\mathbf{A}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{q}} - eV$. Compute the conjugate momentum $\mathbf{p}$ and the Hamiltonian $H(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{p})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The conjugate momentum is not the kinetic momentum $m\dot{\mathbf{q}}$ in this case.

</details>

$\mathbf{p} = \partial L / \partial \dot{\mathbf{q}} = m\dot{\mathbf{q}} + e\mathbf{A}$. Solving for $\dot{\mathbf{q}} = (\mathbf{p} - e\mathbf{A})/m$ and substituting:

$$
H \;=\; \mathbf{p}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{q}} - L \;=\; \frac{|\mathbf{p} - e\mathbf{A}(\mathbf{q})|^2}{2m} + eV(\mathbf{q}).
$$

This is the *minimal-coupling* Hamiltonian, the starting point for the quantum-mechanical treatment of a charged particle in an electromagnetic field. The replacement $\mathbf{p} \to \mathbf{p} - e\mathbf{A}$ is what makes gauge invariance compatible with quantum mechanics.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

For any smooth observable $f(q, p, t)$ on phase space, derive

$$
\frac{df}{dt} \;=\; \{f, H\} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}.
$$

State explicitly which step uses Hamilton's equations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Start with the chain rule on the curve $(q(t), p(t))$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Chain rule: $df/dt = (\partial f/\partial q^i)\dot q^i + (\partial f/\partial p_i)\dot p_i + \partial f/\partial t$. Substitute Hamilton's equations $\dot q^i = \partial H/\partial p_i$ and $\dot p_i = -\partial H/\partial q^i$:

$$
\frac{df}{dt} \;=\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} \;=\; \{f, H\} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}.
$$

The substitution step uses Hamilton's equations; the rest is the chain rule and the definition of the Poisson bracket. As a corollary, conservation of $f$ is exactly $\{f, H\} + \partial f/\partial t = 0$, and for time-independent $f$ it becomes $\{f, H\} = 0$.

</details>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify directly that $H$ is conserved along the flow it generates (assume $\partial H/\partial t = 0$). Then compute $\{q^i, q^j\}$, $\{p_i, p_j\}$, and $\{q^i, p_j\}$ from the Poisson-bracket definition.

Use Exercise 4 with $f = H$ for the first part. The canonical brackets follow from the definition of the bracket and the independence of the coordinate functions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For the canonical brackets, recall the definition $\{f, g\} = (\partial f/\partial q^k)(\partial g/\partial p_k) - (\partial f/\partial p_k)(\partial g/\partial q^k)$. The coordinate function $q^i$ has $\partial q^i/\partial q^k = \delta^i_k$ and $\partial q^i/\partial p_k = 0$; similarly for $p_j$. So

\{q^i, q^j\} = \delta^i_k \cdot 0 - 0 \cdot \delta^j_k = 0, \quad \{p_i, p_j\} = 0 - 0 = 0, \quad \{q^i, p_j\} = \delta^i_k \delta^k_j - 0 = \delta^i_j.

These are the **canonical Poisson relations**. Their algebraic shadow under quantisation $\{\cdot, \cdot\} \to (i\hbar)^{-1}[\cdot, \cdot]$ is the Heisenberg algebra $[\hat q^i, \hat p_j] = i\hbar \delta^i_j$.

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

For the planar Kepler problem with Hamiltonian $H(r, \varphi, p_r, p_\varphi) = p_r^2/(2m) + p_\varphi^2/(2mr^2) - k/r$, write out Hamilton's equations, identify the cyclic coordinate, name the conserved quantity, and reduce the radial motion to an effective one-dimensional problem.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A cyclic coordinate is one that does not appear in $H$. Its conjugate momentum is automatically conserved.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Hamilton's equations:

\dot r = p_r/m, \quad \dot \varphi = p_\varphi/(mr^2), \quad \dot p_r = p_\varphi^2/(mr^3) - k/r^2, \quad \dot p_\varphi = 0.

This is a one-dimensional Hamiltonian system in the effective potential $V_{\rm eff}(r) = \ell^2/(2mr^2) - k/r$ — the centrifugal barrier plus the gravitational well. **Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector $\mathbf{A} := \mathbf{p}\times \mathbf{L} - mk\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ (where $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{q}\times\mathbf{p}$ is angular momentum and $\hat{\mathbf{r}} = \mathbf{q}/|\mathbf{q}|$) is conserved along the Kepler Hamiltonian flow with $H = |\mathbf{p}|^2/(2m) - k/|\mathbf{q}|$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\{A_i, H\}$. The brute-force calculation works; cleaner: compute $d\mathbf{A}/dt$ along the flow using $\dot{\mathbf{p}} = -k\mathbf{q}/r^3$, $\dot{\mathbf{q}} = \mathbf{p}/m$, and $\mathbf{L}$ constant.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathbf{L}$ is conserved (rotation symmetry, or direct: $\dot{\mathbf{L}} = \dot{\mathbf{q}}\times\mathbf{p} + \mathbf{q}\times\dot{\mathbf{p}} = (\mathbf{p}/m)\times\mathbf{p} + \mathbf{q}\times(-k\mathbf{q}/r^3) = 0$). For the second piece, $d(mk\hat{\mathbf{r}})/dt = mk\,d(\mathbf{q}/r)/dt = mk\big(\dot{\mathbf{q}}/r - \mathbf{q}\dot r/r^2\big)$. With $\dot r = \mathbf{q}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{q}}/r = \mathbf{q}\cdot\mathbf{p}/(mr)$, this becomes $mk\big(\mathbf{p}/(mr) - \mathbf{q}(\mathbf{q}\cdot\mathbf{p})/(mr^3)\big) = (k/r)\mathbf{p} - (k/r^3)\mathbf{q}(\mathbf{q}\cdot\mathbf{p})$.

Subtracting: $\dot{\mathbf{A}} = (-k/r^3)\mathbf{q}(\mathbf{q}\cdot\mathbf{p}) + (k/r)\mathbf{p} - (k/r)\mathbf{p} + (k/r^3)\mathbf{q}(\mathbf{q}\cdot\mathbf{p}) = 0$.

The Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector is the third independent conserved quantity (after $\mathbf{L}$ and $H$) that makes the Kepler problem **superintegrable**: 5 independent conserved quantities on a 6-dimensional phase space — orbits close exactly, and the "hidden" $\mathrm{SO}(4)$ symmetry of the bound-state Coulomb problem is generated by $\mathbf{L}$ and a rescaled $\mathbf{A}$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Jacobi identity for the Poisson bracket implies that the Poisson bracket of two conserved quantities is itself conserved. (This is **Poisson's theorem**.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the Jacobi identity with $H$ in one of the three slots.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $f$ and $g$ are time-independent and satisfy $\{f, H\} = \{g, H\} = 0$. The Jacobi identity reads

$$
\{f, \{g, H\}\} + \{g, \{H, f\}\} + \{H, \{f, g\}\} = 0.
$$

By hypothesis the first two terms vanish, leaving $\{H, \{f, g\}\} = 0$, i.e., $\{\{f, g\}, H\} = 0$ by antisymmetry. So $\{f, g\}$ is conserved.

Poisson's theorem is what makes the rep-theory shadow of integrable systems work: conserved quantities form a Lie algebra under the Poisson bracket. For the Kepler problem the conserved quantities $\mathbf{L}, \mathbf{A}, H$ close into an $\mathfrak{so}(4)$ Lie algebra on bound orbits — the algebraic origin of the hydrogen-atom degeneracy in quantum mechanics.

</details>

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

A relativistic free particle has Lagrangian $L = -mc^2\sqrt{1 - |\dot{\mathbf{q}}|^2/c^2}$. Compute the conjugate momentum, attempt the Hamiltonian construction, and discuss what the result means.

Solve for $|\dot{\mathbf{q}}|^2$ in terms of $|\mathbf{p}|$: from $|\mathbf{p}|^2 = \gamma^2 m^2 |\dot{\mathbf{q}}|^2$ and $\gamma^2 = 1/(1 - |\dot{\mathbf{q}}|^2/c^2)$, one finds $|\dot{\mathbf{q}}|^2 = c^2 |\mathbf{p}|^2/(m^2 c^2 + |\mathbf{p}|^2)$. $$

This is the **relativistic energy-momentum relation** $E = \sqrt{m^2 c^4 + |\mathbf{p}|^2 c^2}$. **Exercise 10 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Hamiltonian flow preserves the **symplectic 2-form** $\omega = dp_i \wedge dq^i$ on phase space, in the following sense: if $\phi_t : T^*Q \to T^*Q$ denotes the time-$t$ evolution under Hamilton's equations, then $\phi_t^* \omega = \omega$ for all $t$.

This is **the** structural property of Hamiltonian flow. Taking the $n$-th exterior power gives the **Liouville volume** $\omega^n/n!$ — also preserved — and this is *Liouville's theorem* on phase-space volume conservation. The symplectic preservation is strictly stronger than volume preservation.

## Geometric formulation: Hamilton's equations on a symplectic manifold [Master]

The intermediate-tier statement of Hamilton's equations privileges Darboux coordinates $(q^i, p_i)$; the underlying object is coordinate-free. Let $Q$ be a smooth manifold of dimension $n$ and let $T^*Q$ be its cotangent bundle, with projection $\pi : T^*Q \to Q$.

There is a canonical 1-form on $T^*Q$, the **Liouville (tautological) 1-form** $\theta \in \Omega^1(T^*Q)$, defined intrinsically by

$$
\theta_\alpha(v) \;:=\; \alpha(d\pi_\alpha(v)) \qquad \text{for } \alpha \in T^*Q, \ v \in T_\alpha(T^*Q).
$$

In Darboux coordinates $(q^i, p_i)$ on $T^*Q$ where $p_i$ are the fibre coordinates dual to $dq^i$ on the base, $\theta = p_i dq^i$. The **canonical symplectic 2-form** is

$$
\omega \;:=\; -d\theta \;=\; dq^i \wedge dp_i,
$$

a closed ($d\omega = 0$) and non-degenerate 2-form on $T^*Q$ [05.02.05]. Note the sign convention is fixed: $\omega = -d\theta$ with $\theta = p_i dq^i$ gives $\omega = dq^i\wedge dp_i$; the opposite sign convention $\omega = d\theta = dp_i \wedge dq^i$ appears in some sources (Marsden-Ratiu; we follow the same convention here, having earlier written $\omega = dp_i \wedge dq^i$ — both differ from Arnold's by an overall sign, exchanging $X_H$ with $-X_H$ but leaving Hamilton's equations as stated invariant under the simultaneous swap of conventions). Within a single document, fix the convention and stay with it. Throughout this unit, $\omega = dq^i \wedge dp_i$ and Hamilton's equations are as stated above.

**Darboux's theorem** [05.01.04] guarantees that any 2$n$-dimensional symplectic manifold $(M, \omega)$ admits, around any point, local coordinates $(q^1, \ldots, q^n, p_1, \ldots, p_n)$ in which $\omega = dq^i \wedge dp_i$. So the cotangent-bundle picture is locally the universal example; abstract symplectic manifolds (e.g., $\mathbb{C}P^n$ with the Fubini-Study form, or coadjoint orbits) are not cotangent bundles globally, but Hamilton's equations look the same in any Darboux chart.

**Hamiltonian vector field** [05.02.01]. For $H \in C^\infty(M)$ on a symplectic manifold $(M, \omega)$, the **Hamiltonian vector field** $X_H$ is the unique vector field satisfying

$$
\boxed{\;\;i_{X_H} \omega \;=\; dH.\;\;}
$$

Non-degeneracy of $\omega$ makes the equation $i_X \omega = dH$ uniquely solvable for $X$, so $X_H$ exists and is unique. In Darboux coordinates, writing $X_H = A^i \partial_{q^i} + B_i \partial_{p_i}$ and computing $i_{X_H}\omega = i_{X_H}(dq^i \wedge dp_i) = A^i dp_i - B_i dq^i$, the equation $i_{X_H}\omega = dH = (\partial H/\partial q^i)dq^i + (\partial H/\partial p_i)dp_i$ matches coefficients to give $A^i = \partial H/\partial p_i$ and $B_i = -\partial H/\partial q^i$, i.e.,

$$
X_H \;=\; \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial}{\partial q^i} \;-\; \frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial}{\partial p_i}.
$$

Hamilton's equations are the integral-curve equations of $X_H$: $\dot \gamma(t) = X_H(\gamma(t))$.

**Symplectic-form preservation.** By Cartan's magic formula $\mathcal{L}_{X_H}\omega = di_{X_H}\omega + i_{X_H}d\omega = d(dH) + 0 = 0$, using $d^2 = 0$ and the closedness of $\omega$. So the flow $\phi_t$ of $X_H$ preserves $\omega$:

$$
\phi_t^* \omega \;=\; \omega \qquad \text{for all } t \in \mathbb{R}.
$$

A diffeomorphism $\psi : M \to M$ with $\psi^*\omega = \omega$ is a **symplectomorphism** (also called a **canonical transformation**). The Hamiltonian flow is therefore a one-parameter family of symplectomorphisms.

**Liouville's theorem** [05.02.07]. The top exterior power $\omega^n / n!$ is a volume form on $M^{2n}$ (the **Liouville volume**). Since $\omega$ is preserved, so is $\omega^n / n!$ — phase-space volume is conserved under Hamiltonian flow. This is *Liouville's theorem on phase-space volume*: incompressibility of the Hamiltonian flow regarded as a fluid on $M$.

The symplectic preservation $\phi_t^*\omega = \omega$ is strictly stronger than volume preservation: it preserves $\omega$, $\omega^2/2!$, …, $\omega^n/n!$ — the entire family of symplectic invariants — and additionally fixes the *pairing structure* on tangent vectors. Symplectic flows are volume-preserving, but volume-preserving flows on $M^{2n}$ for $n \geq 2$ are *not* in general symplectic; Eliashberg's symplectic-rigidity theorem (1989) is the deep statement that distinguishes them at $C^0$.

**Poisson bracket as the symplectic shadow.** The Poisson bracket [05.02.02] is constructed from $\omega$ via

$$
\{f, g\} \;:=\; \omega(X_f, X_g) \;=\; X_f(g) \;=\; -X_g(f).
$$

The Leibniz rule for $\{\cdot, \cdot\}$ is built into the vector-field interpretation; antisymmetry comes from antisymmetry of $\omega$; the **Jacobi identity** is equivalent to $d\omega = 0$ via the calculation

$$
\{f, \{g, h\}\} + \text{cyclic} \;=\; -d\omega(X_f, X_g, X_h) \;=\; 0.
$$

So *closedness of $\omega$ is exactly the Jacobi identity for $\{\cdot, \cdot\}$*. This is the structural reason classical mechanics has a Lie-algebra-of-observables structure under the Poisson bracket — and the structural reason quantum mechanics, which replaces $\{\cdot,\cdot\}$ by $(i\hbar)^{-1}[\cdot,\cdot]$, must have $[\hat A, \hat B] = i\hbar\,\widehat{\{A, B\}} + O(\hbar^2)$ at the semiclassical level. The Jacobi identity for commutators is automatic for associative algebras; the corresponding classical statement requires the additional input $d\omega = 0$.

**Hamilton's equations as a moment map.** A more abstract restatement: the time-1 flow of $X_H$ is the unique symplectomorphism whose generating function (in the sense developed in the next unit) is $H$. This perspective routes Hamilton's equations through the variational principle on $T^*Q$ — the modified Hamilton principle — and is the entry point to **canonical transformations** as the symplectomorphism group of $(T^*Q, \omega)$ and **Hamilton-Jacobi theory** as the analysis of distinguished generating functions.

## Canonical transformations and generating functions [Master]

A diffeomorphism $\Phi : T^*Q \to T^*Q$ with $\Phi^*\omega = \omega$ is a **canonical transformation** (synonym: symplectomorphism). Hamiltonian flows are a one-parameter subgroup of the canonical-transformation group; abstract canonical transformations are the discrete cousins.

Hamilton's equations are **covariant under canonical transformations** in a precise sense: if $\Phi(q, p) = (Q(q, p), P(q, p))$ is canonical, and $K(Q, P) := H(\Phi^{-1}(Q, P))$ is the pulled-back Hamiltonian, then a trajectory of $X_H$ on the source side is mapped to a trajectory of $X_K$ on the target side. Choosing $\Phi$ wisely — to bring $K$ into a form with as many cyclic coordinates as possible — is the strategic content of **Hamilton-Jacobi theory**: find a canonical transformation under which $K$ depends only on the new momenta $P$, in which case the new equations of motion reduce to $\dot P = 0$, $\dot Q = \partial K/\partial P =$ constant, and the system is *solved by quadrature*.

Canonical transformations are classified by **generating functions**, four standard types corresponding to which pair of $\{q, p, Q, P\}$ is taken as independent variables. The simplest is type-1: $F_1(q, Q, t)$ with $p_i = \partial F_1/\partial q^i$, $P_i = -\partial F_1/\partial Q^i$, $K = H + \partial F_1/\partial t$. The other three types differ by Legendre transforms in $(p, q)$ or $(P, Q)$. The **Hamilton-Jacobi equation**

$$
H\Big(q, \frac{\partial S}{\partial q}, t\Big) + \frac{\partial S}{\partial t} \;=\; 0
$$

is the equation for a type-2 generating function $S(q, P, t)$ that achieves the maximum-simplification target ($K \equiv 0$, all new momenta conserved). Its theory and its limit relation to the eikonal equation of geometric optics — and via the WKB approximation to the Schrödinger equation — are the subject of unit [09.05.01].

## Integrable systems and the Liouville-Arnold theorem [Master]

A Hamiltonian system on $(M^{2n}, \omega)$ is **integrable in the Liouville sense** if it admits $n$ functionally independent conserved quantities $f_1 = H, f_2, \ldots, f_n$ whose pairwise Poisson brackets all vanish ($\{f_i, f_j\} = 0$). The conserved quantities are said to be *in involution*.

**Liouville-Arnold theorem.** *Let $f_1, \ldots, f_n$ be functionally independent conserved quantities in involution on a $2n$-dimensional symplectic manifold $M$, with $f_1 = H$. Fix a level set $M_c := \{x \in M : f_i(x) = c_i\}$ for $c = (c_1, \ldots, c_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n$, and assume $M_c$ is compact and connected. Then:*

*(i) $M_c$ is diffeomorphic to the $n$-torus $\mathbb{T}^n$;*

*(ii) there exist coordinates $(\phi^1, \ldots, \phi^n) \pmod{2\pi}$ on $M_c$ in which the Hamiltonian flow is linear: $\dot \phi^i = \omega^i(c)$;*

*(iii) on a neighbourhood of $M_c$, there exist symplectic coordinates $(I_1, \ldots, I_n, \phi^1, \ldots, \phi^n)$ — **action-angle coordinates** — with $\omega = dI_i \wedge d\phi^i$ and $H = H(I)$ depending only on the actions. Hamilton's equations are $\dot I_i = 0$, $\dot \phi^i = \partial H/\partial I_i$, integrable explicitly by quadrature.*

The Liouville-Arnold theorem is proven by analysing the $\mathbb{R}^n$-action of the commuting flows of the $X_{f_i}$ on $M_c$ — the orbit-stabiliser theorem makes $M_c$ a quotient $\mathbb{R}^n / \Lambda$ for some lattice $\Lambda \cong \mathbb{Z}^n$, and the compactness hypothesis closes the argument [05.09.01]. Proof details live in Arnold §49-50 and Abraham-Marsden §5.

Examples of integrable systems where this machinery applies in full: the harmonic oscillator in any dimension; the Kepler problem (Liouville-integrable on each energy surface, *superintegrable* with an extra conserved quantity — the Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector — that closes orbits); the spherical pendulum on each energy surface generically; the Euler top (free rigid body in $\mathbb{R}^3$, three conserved quantities $H, \mathbf{L}^2, L_z$); the Lagrange and Kovalevskaya tops; the Toda lattice. Beyond these the situation is generically *non-integrable*: typical Hamiltonian systems are chaotic, the KAM theorem governs the persistence of integrable tori under small perturbations [05.09.01], and Arnold diffusion describes the slow drift across resonances on long timescales.

## Connection to quantum mechanics [Master]

The semiclassical correspondence is realised via **canonical quantisation**, the map sending classical observables $f(q, p)$ to operators $\hat f$ on a Hilbert space, with the Poisson bracket sent to the commutator divided by $i\hbar$:

$$
\{f, g\} \;\longmapsto\; \frac{1}{i\hbar}[\hat f, \hat g].
$$

Hamilton's equation $df/dt = \{f, H\}$ becomes the Heisenberg equation $d\hat f/dt = (i\hbar)^{-1}[\hat f, \hat H]$. The canonical Poisson relations $\{q^i, p_j\} = \delta^i_j$ become the Heisenberg commutation relations $[\hat q^i, \hat p_j] = i\hbar \delta^i_j$. The Hamilton-Jacobi equation becomes the WKB limit of the Schrödinger equation. The Lagrangian-side variational principle becomes the Feynman path-integral. Symplectic geometry is the geometry of the classical limit of quantum mechanics, and the structural reason it appears at all is that the Lie algebra of self-adjoint operators on a Hilbert space is non-abelian and quantum observables form a *non-commutative* version of the Poisson algebra.

The correspondence is faithful for *quadratic* observables (the harmonic oscillator quantises exactly), and breaks at higher polynomial orders by operator-ordering ambiguities — Groenewold's theorem (1946) shows no homomorphism extends to all polynomial observables. The corrections to the naive quantisation rule are organised by formal deformation quantisation (Bayen-Flato-Fronsdal-Lichnerowicz-Sternheimer 1978) and the Kontsevich formality theorem (1997), which constructs a $\star$-product on any Poisson manifold.

For the QM side proper, see unit [12.02.01] (Hilbert space formalism) and [12.10.01] (path integrals), and the connections drawn there.

## Connections [Master]

- **Legendre transform [05.00.03]** is the bridge from the Lagrangian to the Hamiltonian formulation. The hyper-regularity condition there is what gives the Hessian-invertibility hypothesis in the Equivalence Theorem above.

- **Hamiltonian vector field [05.02.01]** is the coordinate-free object that Hamilton's equations describe. The relation $i_{X_H}\omega = dH$ is the *defining* equation; the coordinate Hamilton's equations are its expression in a Darboux chart.

- **Poisson bracket [05.02.02]** is the algebraic shadow of the symplectic form. The evolution equation $df/dt = \{f, H\}$ packages Hamilton's equations and the chain rule into one line.

- **Cotangent bundle as canonical symplectic manifold [05.02.05]** is where the symplectic form $\omega = dq^i \wedge dp_i$ comes from. Any configuration manifold's cotangent bundle is automatically symplectic; this is why Hamiltonian mechanics is the natural formulation on a manifold rather than only on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$.

- **Darboux theorem [05.01.04]** says every symplectic manifold looks locally like $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ with $\omega = dq^i \wedge dp_i$. This is what makes the coordinate expression of Hamilton's equations universal.

- **Liouville volume and Liouville's theorem [05.02.07]** is the immediate consequence of $\phi_t^*\omega = \omega$: phase-space volume is preserved. Statistical-mechanics derivations of the microcanonical ensemble take this conservation law as a primitive.

- **Noether's theorem [05.00.04]** in its Hamiltonian form: continuous symmetries of $H$ are in one-to-one correspondence with conserved quantities, packaged via the moment map for the symmetry group's action on $T^*Q$.

- **Hamilton-Jacobi equation [09.05.01]** (pending) is the canonical-transformation strategy taken to its limit; the WKB approximation of the Schrödinger equation reduces to it in the classical limit.

- **KAM theorem [05.09.01]** describes what survives of Hamilton's-equation integrability when an integrable Hamiltonian is perturbed. The starting point is always a Hamiltonian flow on a symplectic manifold; the conclusion is about a specific class of invariant tori for the perturbed flow.

- **Geometric mechanics [09.09.01]** (pending) generalises Hamilton's equations to Poisson manifolds, coadjoint orbits, and the Lie-Poisson framework on duals of Lie algebras — where infinite-dimensional examples (ideal fluids via Arnold 1966, plasma physics, BBGKY hierarchies) live.

- **Canonical quantisation and the path integral [12.02.01, 12.10.01]** (pending) take this unit's Poisson algebra and Hamiltonian as the classical limit of the quantum-mechanical operator algebra and Hamiltonian.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Hamilton introduced his equations in two papers in *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society*, 1834 and 1835 [ref: TODO_REF Hamilton 1834], building on his earlier work on geometrical optics. The 1834 paper, *On a general method in dynamics*, derived the equations as a recasting of Lagrange's *Mécanique analytique* (1788) in which the action $S$, regarded as a function of endpoint and time, satisfies the partial differential equation now bearing his name. Jacobi extended the framework in lectures published posthumously in 1866 [ref: TODO_REF Jacobi 1866], emphasising the canonical-transformation viewpoint and the integration of mechanics by separation of variables in the Hamilton-Jacobi equation. The 19th century closed with the framework fully developed analytically; the 20th opened with Poincaré's *Méthodes nouvelles de la mécanique céleste* (1892-99) recasting the question as one of qualitative dynamics on phase space.

The geometric reformulation — Hamilton's equations as the integral curves of a Hamiltonian vector field on a symplectic manifold — emerged through the work of Cartan (the *Leçons sur les invariants intégraux*, 1922), with Weyl, Liouville, and Birkhoff contributing along the way. Arnold's *Mathematical Methods of Classical Mechanics* (1974, English translation 1978; second edition 1989) [ref: TODO_REF Arnold 1989] consolidated the geometric viewpoint into a canonical reference. Marsden-Ratiu's *Introduction to Mechanics and Symmetry* (1994, second edition 1999) [ref: TODO_REF Marsden-Ratiu 1999] extended it to the equivariant / Lie-group / momentum-map framework now standard in geometric mechanics. The Liouville-Arnold theorem on integrable systems was proved in Arnold's 1963 *Russian Math. Surveys* paper [ref: TODO_REF Arnold 1963]; the KAM theorem, settled in Arnold's 1963 paper and Moser's 1962 smooth-twist version, characterises the persistence of integrability under perturbation [05.09.01].

Hamiltonian flow is the canonical object of classical determinism. The 1814 *Essai philosophique sur les probabilités* of Laplace [ref: TODO_REF Laplace 1814] put the determinism thesis in its sharpest form: an intelligence that knew the initial $(q, p)$ of every particle and the laws of motion would know all of past and future. Mathematically, this is exactly the statement that a Hamiltonian flow on $T^*Q$ is uniquely determined by its initial point and the equation $\dot \gamma = X_H \circ \gamma$. The thesis was challenged on multiple fronts in the 20th century: by quantum mechanics (no joint sharp values of $q$ and $p$); by Poincaré's discovery of chaotic orbits in the three-body problem (sensitivity to initial conditions makes prediction infeasible even when the equations are deterministic); and by the singularity theorems of Hawking and Penrose in general relativity (deterministic evolution can break down at finite proper time). Hamilton's equations remain the cleanest setting in which to state the classical-determinism thesis precisely, and the cleanest contrast against the quantum-mechanical and chaotic and relativistic deviations.
