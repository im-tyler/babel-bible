## Intuition [Beginner]

A spinning top is the simplest mechanical system whose dynamics cannot be reduced to point particles. The top has a mass distribution spread through space, and the way it spins depends on how that mass is arranged. A thin rod spins stably about its long axis. A flat disk spins stably about the axis perpendicular to its face. But try to spin either one about an axis at an angle, and the motion is complicated — the spin axis itself precesses and wobbles.

The key quantity is the **angular momentum** $\mathbf{L}$, a vector that encodes both how fast the body rotates and about which axis. For a single point mass in a circle, $\mathbf{L}$ points along the rotation axis and its length is $mvr$. For an extended body, you add up the contributions from every mass element, and the result depends on the body's **moment of inertia tensor** — a $3 \times 3$ matrix $I$ that encodes how mass is distributed relative to each axis.

In body-fixed coordinates (attached to the rigid body), the inertia tensor $I$ is a constant matrix. The angular momentum in body coordinates is $\mathbf{L}_{\rm body} = I\boldsymbol{\omega}$ where $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ is the angular velocity. The diagonal entries $I_1, I_2, I_3$ of $I$ (in the principal-axis basis where $I$ is diagonal) are the **principal moments of inertia**.

**Euler's equations** describe how the body-frame angular velocity evolves when no external torques act. For a body with three distinct principal moments $I_1, I_2, I_3$, they read:

$$I_1 \dot\omega_1 = (I_2 - I_3)\omega_2\omega_3, \quad I_2 \dot\omega_2 = (I_3 - I_1)\omega_3\omega_1, \quad I_3 \dot\omega_3 = (I_1 - I_2)\omega_1\omega_2.$$

Each equation involves the product of the other two components. This coupling is what makes rigid-body rotation rich: the three components chase each other in a complicated dance that, for a torque-free symmetric top, traces out a closed curve called a **polhode** on the energy ellipsoid.

## Visual [Beginner]

The angular-momentum vector $\mathbf{L}$ is fixed in space (no torques). The angular-velocity vector $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ traces a curve on two intersecting surfaces: the energy ellipsoid $\tfrac{1}{2}(I_1\omega_1^2 + I_2\omega_2^2 + I_3\omega_3^2) = E$ and the angular-momentum sphere $I_1^2\omega_1^2 + I_2^2\omega_2^2 + I_3^2\omega_3^2 = L^2$.

![Poinsot construction for a torque-free asymmetric top. The energy ellipsoid (grey) rolls without slipping on the invariant plane perpendicular to L. The polhode (the curve traced by omega on the energy ellipsoid) winds around the intermediate axis and closes near the major and minor axes.](/img/09.04.08-poinsot-construction-placeholder.svg)

The most striking feature: rotation about the axis with the **intermediate** moment of inertia is unstable. Spin a book about its long axis (stable), or about the axis perpendicular to its face (stable), but try to spin it about the remaining axis and it tumbles chaotically. This is the **tennis racket theorem** (also called the intermediate-axis theorem).

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take a rigid body with principal moments $I_1 = 1$, $I_2 = 2$, $I_3 = 3$ (in suitable units). Start it spinning nearly about the $x_1$-axis with a small perturbation: $\omega_1(0) = 10$, $\omega_2(0) = 0.1$, $\omega_3(0) = 0$.

Euler's equations give:
$$\dot\omega_1 = \frac{(I_2 - I_3)}{I_1}\omega_2\omega_3 = \frac{(2-3)}{1}\omega_2\omega_3 = -\omega_2\omega_3,$$
$$\dot\omega_2 = \frac{(I_3 - I_1)}{I_2}\omega_3\omega_1 = \frac{(3-1)}{2}\omega_3\omega_1 = \omega_3\omega_1,$$
$$\dot\omega_3 = \frac{(I_1 - I_2)}{I_3}\omega_1\omega_2 = \frac{(1-2)}{3}\omega_1\omega_2 = -\frac{\omega_1\omega_2}{3}.$$

At $t = 0$: $\dot\omega_1 = 0$, $\dot\omega_2 = 0$, $\dot\omega_3 = -10 \cdot 0.1/3 = -1/3$. So $\omega_3$ begins to decrease (become negative). At the next instant, $\omega_3 \neq 0$ and the $\dot\omega_1$ and $\dot\omega_2$ equations activate. The perturbation stays small: $\omega_2$ and $\omega_3$ oscillate with small amplitude while $\omega_1$ stays near 10. Rotation about the axis of smallest moment of inertia is stable.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **rigid body** is a system of particles constrained so that all inter-particle distances are fixed. The configuration space is the **special Euclidean group** $\mathrm{SE}(3) = \mathbb{R}^3 \rtimes \mathrm{SO}(3)$, where the $\mathbb{R}^3$ factor encodes the position of the centre of mass and the $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ factor encodes the orientation. For the rotational dynamics (centre of mass fixed or decoupled), the configuration space is $\mathrm{SO}(3)$.

The **inertia tensor** of a rigid body about a fixed point is the symmetric positive-definite matrix

$$I_{jk} = \int_V \rho(\mathbf{r})\bigl(|\mathbf{r}|^2 \delta_{jk} - r_j r_k\bigr)\,dV,$$

where $\rho$ is the mass density. In the principal-axis basis, $I = \mathrm{diag}(I_1, I_2, I_3)$ with $I_1 \leq I_2 \leq I_3$ (assuming distinct values).

The **body-frame angular momentum** is $\boldsymbol{\Pi} = I\boldsymbol{\omega}$, with components $\Pi_i = I_i\omega_i$ in the principal basis. The rotational kinetic energy is $T = \tfrac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{\omega}\cdot I\boldsymbol{\omega} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\Pi_1^2/I_1 + \Pi_2^2/I_2 + \Pi_3^2/I_3)$.

**Euler's equations** (torque-free case) in terms of body angular momentum $\boldsymbol{\Pi}$ are

$$\dot\Pi_1 = \frac{I_2 - I_3}{I_2 I_3}\Pi_2\Pi_3, \quad \dot\Pi_2 = \frac{I_3 - I_1}{I_3 I_1}\Pi_3\Pi_1, \quad \dot\Pi_3 = \frac{I_1 - I_2}{I_1 I_2}\Pi_1\Pi_2.$$

These are Hamilton's equations for the function $H(\boldsymbol{\Pi}) = \tfrac{1}{2}(\Pi_1^2/I_1 + \Pi_2^2/I_2 + \Pi_3^2/I_3)$ with respect to the **Lie-Poisson bracket** on $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$:

$$\{F, G\}(\boldsymbol{\Pi}) = -\boldsymbol{\Pi}\cdot\bigl(\nabla F \times \nabla G\bigr).$$

Written component-wise:

$$\{\Pi_i, \Pi_j\} = -\epsilon_{ijk}\Pi_k,$$

where $\epsilon_{ijk}$ is the Levi-Civita symbol. This bracket satisfies the Jacobi identity (because it arises from the Lie algebra structure of $\mathfrak{so}(3)$) and is **degenerate**: the function $C(\boldsymbol{\Pi}) = \tfrac{1}{2}|\boldsymbol{\Pi}|^2$ satisfies $\{C, F\} = 0$ for all $F$, making $C$ a **Casimir function**. The Casimir constrains the dynamics to spheres $|\boldsymbol{\Pi}|^2 = $ constant in angular-momentum space.

Hamilton's equations $\dot{F} = \{F, H\}$ reproduce Euler's equations:

$$\dot\Pi_i = \{\Pi_i, H\} = -\epsilon_{ijk}\Pi_j\frac{\Pi_k}{I_k}.$$

The conserved quantities are the Hamiltonian $H$ (energy) and the Casimir $C$ (squared angular-momentum magnitude). These two functions define the intersection of an energy ellipsoid and an angular-momentum sphere — the **herpolhode** curves that describe the body-frame motion.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Euler's equations from the Lie-Poisson bracket).**

The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ consists of $3 \times 3$ skew-symmetric matrices. The map $\hat{\cdot} : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathfrak{so}(3)$ sending $\mathbf{v}$ to the matrix $\hat{v}_{jk} = -\epsilon_{jkl}v_l$ identifies $\mathbb{R}^3$ with $\mathfrak{so}(3)$. The Lie bracket is $[\hat{u}, \hat{v}] = \widehat{u \times v}$, the vector cross product.

The dual $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ is identified with $\mathbb{R}^3$ via the trace pairing $\langle \boldsymbol{\Pi}, \hat{\xi}\rangle = \boldsymbol{\Pi}\cdot\xi$. The **coadjoint action** of $R \in \mathrm{SO}(3)$ on $\boldsymbol{\Pi} \in \mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ is $\mathrm{Ad}^*_{R^{-1}}\boldsymbol{\Pi} = R\boldsymbol{\Pi}$ — rotation of the angular-momentum vector.

The **Lie-Poisson bracket** (also called the **Kirillov-Kostant-Souriau bracket**) on $\mathfrak{so}(3)^* \cong \mathbb{R}^3$ is

$$\{F, G\}_{-}(\boldsymbol{\Pi}) := -\boldsymbol{\Pi}\cdot\bigl(\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\Pi}}F \times \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\Pi}}G\bigr).$$

The minus sign denotes the **minus Lie-Poisson bracket**, which corresponds to reduction by the left action of $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ on $T^*\mathrm{SO}(3)$ (body-frame reduction). The plus sign would correspond to spatial-frame reduction.

The **Casimir function** $C(\boldsymbol{\Pi}) = \tfrac{1}{2}|\boldsymbol{\Pi}|^2$ satisfies $\{C, F\}_- = 0$ for all $F$ because $\nabla C = \boldsymbol{\Pi}$ and $\boldsymbol{\Pi}\cdot(\boldsymbol{\Pi}\times \nabla F) = 0$ identically. The level sets $C = $ constant are the coadjoint orbits — spheres centred at the origin in $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$.

The Hamiltonian $H(\boldsymbol{\Pi}) = \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_i \Pi_i^2/I_i$ generates the flow

$$\dot\Pi_i = \{\Pi_i, H\}_- = -\epsilon_{ijk}\Pi_j\frac{\Pi_k}{I_k}.$$

Writing out the three components:

$$\dot\Pi_1 = -\Pi_2 \frac{\Pi_3}{I_3} + \Pi_3 \frac{\Pi_2}{I_2} = \left(\frac{1}{I_2} - \frac{1}{I_3}\right)\Pi_2\Pi_3,$$

and cyclically for $\dot\Pi_2$, $\dot\Pi_3$. Converting to angular velocity $\omega_i = \Pi_i/I_i$:

$$I_1\dot\omega_1 = (I_2 - I_3)\omega_2\omega_3, \quad I_2\dot\omega_2 = (I_3 - I_1)\omega_3\omega_1, \quad I_3\dot\omega_3 = (I_1 - I_2)\omega_1\omega_2.$$

These are **Euler's equations** for a free rigid body.

**Bridge.** The Lie-Poisson bracket on $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ builds toward the general theory of Poisson reduction on duals of Lie algebras, where the same construction produces the Euler equations for ideal fluids on $\mathfrak{X}_{\rm div}(\Omega)^*$ [09.07.03] and the Vlasov-Poisson equation on the dual of the Lie algebra of Hamiltonian functions on phase space. The central insight is that the rigid body is not a special case but the prototype: every Hamiltonian system obtained by reducing $T^*G$ by a Lie-group action inherits a Lie-Poisson bracket on $\mathfrak{g}^*$, and the Casimir functions of this bracket constrain the reduced dynamics to coadjoint orbits. This is exactly the geometric structure that makes the rigid body integrable (two conserved quantities $H, C$ on a three-dimensional Poisson manifold, reducing motion to one-dimensional curves on the intersection surfaces) and generalises to the Lie-Poisson integrability of the ideal fluid, the KdV equation, and other infinite-dimensional systems.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Compute the Poisson bracket $\{\Pi_1, \Pi_2\}$ on $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ directly from the definition $\{F, G\} = -\boldsymbol{\Pi}\cdot(\nabla F \times \nabla G)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\Pi}}\Pi_1 = \hat{e}_1$ and $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\Pi}}\Pi_2 = \hat{e}_2$. Compute $\hat{e}_1 \times \hat{e}_2$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\hat{e}_1 \times \hat{e}_2 = \hat{e}_3$, so $\{\Pi_1, \Pi_2\} = -\boldsymbol{\Pi}\cdot\hat{e}_3 = -\Pi_3$. The full bracket algebra is $\{\Pi_i, \Pi_j\} = -\epsilon_{ijk}\Pi_k$, the structure constants of $\mathfrak{so}(3)$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Verify directly from Euler's equations that $\tfrac{d}{dt}|\boldsymbol{\Pi}|^2 = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $2\Pi_1\dot\Pi_1 + 2\Pi_2\dot\Pi_2 + 2\Pi_3\dot\Pi_3$ using Euler's equations.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\tfrac{d}{dt}|\boldsymbol{\Pi}|^2 = 2\Pi_1\dot\Pi_1 + 2\Pi_2\dot\Pi_2 + 2\Pi_3\dot\Pi_3$. Substituting Euler's equations:

$2\Pi_1\left(\frac{1}{I_2} - \frac{1}{I_3}\right)\Pi_2\Pi_3 + 2\Pi_2\left(\frac{1}{I_3} - \frac{1}{I_1}\right)\Pi_3\Pi_1 + 2\Pi_3\left(\frac{1}{I_1} - \frac{1}{I_2}\right)\Pi_1\Pi_2 = 2\Pi_1\Pi_2\Pi_3\left(\frac{1}{I_2} - \frac{1}{I_3} + \frac{1}{I_3} - \frac{1}{I_1} + \frac{1}{I_1} - \frac{1}{I_2}\right) = 0$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

For the symmetric top ($I_1 = I_2 \neq I_3$), show that Euler's equations integrate to $\omega_3 = $ constant and $\omega_1 + i\omega_2 = A e^{i\Omega t}$ for some $\Omega$. Find $\Omega$ in terms of $I_1, I_3, \omega_3$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

With $I_1 = I_2$, the equation for $\dot\omega_3$ gives $\omega_3 = $ const. Then the equations for $\omega_1, \omega_2$ form a linear system.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\dot\omega_3 = (I_1 - I_2)\omega_1\omega_2/I_3 = 0$ since $I_1 = I_2$, so $\omega_3 = $ constant. Then $\dot\omega_1 = (I_2 - I_3)\omega_2\omega_3/I_1 = \Omega_0\omega_2$ and $\dot\omega_2 = (I_3 - I_1)\omega_3\omega_1/I_2 = -\Omega_0\omega_1$, where $\Omega_0 = (I_1 - I_3)\omega_3/I_1$. This is the system $\dot\omega_1 = \Omega_0\omega_2$, $\dot\omega_2 = -\Omega_0\omega_1$, whose solution is $\omega_1 + i\omega_2 = Ae^{-i\Omega_0 t}$ with $\Omega = -\Omega_0 = (I_3 - I_1)\omega_3/I_1$. The angular velocity vector precesses around the body 3-axis at rate $\Omega$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Linearise Euler's equations about the fixed point $\boldsymbol{\omega} = (0, 0, \omega_3^0)$ (rotation about the 3-axis). Find the eigenvalues and determine the stability condition.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $\omega_i = \delta\omega_i$ for $i = 1, 2$ and linearise the right-hand side, keeping only terms linear in $\delta\omega_1, \delta\omega_2$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The linearised equations are $\delta\dot\omega_1 = \frac{(I_2 - I_3)}{I_1}\omega_3^0\,\delta\omega_2$ and $\delta\dot\omega_2 = \frac{(I_3 - I_1)}{I_2}\omega_3^0\,\delta\omega_1$. The eigenvalue equation is $\lambda^2 = \frac{(I_2 - I_3)(I_3 - I_1)}{I_1 I_2}(\omega_3^0)^2$. For stability, $\lambda^2 < 0$, which requires $(I_2 - I_3)(I_3 - I_1) < 0$, i.e., either $I_3 > I_2 > I_1$ (3 is the largest) or $I_3 < I_1 < I_2$ (3 is the smallest). Rotation about the intermediate axis gives $\lambda^2 > 0$ — unstable.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the function $C(\boldsymbol{\Pi}) = \tfrac{1}{2}|\boldsymbol{\Pi}|^2$ is a Casimir of the Lie-Poisson bracket: $\{C, F\}_- = 0$ for every smooth $F$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\nabla C = \boldsymbol{\Pi}$. Then $\nabla C \times \nabla F = \boldsymbol{\Pi} \times \nabla F$, and $\boldsymbol{\Pi}\cdot(\boldsymbol{\Pi}\times\nabla F) = 0$ because the triple scalar product vanishes when two vectors are identical.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\{C, F\}_- = -\boldsymbol{\Pi}\cdot(\nabla C \times \nabla F) = -\boldsymbol{\Pi}\cdot(\boldsymbol{\Pi}\times\nabla F)$. The triple scalar product $\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b})$ vanishes identically (the cross product $\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}$ is perpendicular to $\mathbf{a}$). Hence $\{C, F\}_- = 0$ for all $F$. The level sets of $C$ are spheres $|\boldsymbol{\Pi}| = $ const, and the dynamics is constrained to these spheres — the coadjoint orbits of $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ acting on $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Reduce the free rigid body from $T^*\mathrm{SO}(3)$ to $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ via left trivialisation. Show that the canonical symplectic form on $T^*\mathrm{SO}(3)$ reduces to the Lie-Poisson bracket on $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Left trivialise: $T^*\mathrm{SO}(3) \cong \mathrm{SO}(3) \times \mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ via $(R, \Pi) = (R, R^{-1}\Pi_{\rm space})$. The reduced dynamics on $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ is obtained by dropping the $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ variable via $\mathrm{SO}(3)$-invariance of $H$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $\Phi : T^*\mathrm{SO}(3) \to \mathrm{SO}(3) \times \mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ by $\Phi(R, p) = (R, R^{-1}p)$ where $p$ is spatial angular momentum and $\Pi = R^{-1}p$ is body angular momentum. The canonical 1-form on $T^*G$ pulls back to $\theta = \Pi\cdot R^{-1}dR$ (the right Maurer-Cartan form paired with $\Pi$). Its exterior derivative gives $\omega = d\theta$. Reducing by the left $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ action quotienting out the $R$ variable, the Poisson bracket inherited from $\omega$ on the quotient $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ is exactly the Lie-Poisson bracket $\{F, G\}_- = -\Pi\cdot(\nabla F \times \nabla G)$. The key calculation uses the Maurer-Cartan equation $d(R^{-1}dR) = -\tfrac{1}{2}[R^{-1}dR, R^{-1}dR]$ and the identification of the cross product with the $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ Lie bracket. See Marsden-Ratiu §10.3 for the complete derivation.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

For the **Lagrange top** (a symmetric rigid body with $I_1 = I_2$, spinning about its symmetry axis with one point fixed in a gravitational field), write the full Hamiltonian including gravity and show that the reduced system is integrable. Identify the three conserved quantities.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The gravitational potential depends on the Euler angles. Two of the three conserved quantities are the angular momentum about the vertical axis and the angular momentum about the body symmetry axis. The third is energy.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In Euler angles $(\phi, \theta, \psi)$: the Hamiltonian is $H = \tfrac{p_\theta^2}{2I_1} + \tfrac{(p_\phi - p_\psi\cos\theta)^2}{2I_1\sin^2\theta} + \tfrac{p_\psi^2}{2I_3} + Mgl\cos\theta$ where $M$ is the total mass, $l$ is the distance from the fixed point to the centre of mass, and $g$ is gravitational acceleration. The three conserved quantities are: (1) $p_\phi$ (angular momentum about the vertical axis, conjugate to the cyclic coordinate $\phi$), (2) $p_\psi$ (angular momentum about the body symmetry axis, conjugate to the cyclic coordinate $\psi$), and (3) $H$ itself (energy, since $H$ has no explicit time dependence). Three conserved quantities on a four-dimensional phase space (after eliminating $\phi$ and $\psi$) makes the system integrable by the Liouville-Arnold theorem. The motion reduces to quadrature involving elliptic integrals.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Lie-Poisson bracket $\{F, G\} = -\boldsymbol{\Pi}\cdot(\nabla F \times \nabla G)$ satisfies the Jacobi identity by relating it to the Jacobi identity of the vector cross product on $\mathbb{R}^3$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Identify $\nabla F$ with an element of $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ via the hat map. The Jacobi identity for the bracket on $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ is the dual of the Jacobi identity for the Lie bracket on $\mathfrak{so}(3)$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $X_F = \nabla F$ and $X_G = \nabla G$. Then $\{F, G\}(\Pi) = -\Pi\cdot(X_F \times X_G)$. The Jacobiator is $\{F, \{G, H\}\} + \text{cyclic}$. Computing:

$\{G, H\}(\Pi) = -\Pi\cdot(X_G \times X_H)$, so $\nabla_\Pi\{G, H\} = -(X_G \times X_H)$ (independent of $\Pi$). Then $\{F, \{G, H\}\} = -\Pi\cdot(X_F \times (X_G \times X_H)) = -\Pi\cdot(X_F \times (X_G \times X_H))$.

By the vector triple product identity $\mathbf{a}\times(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{b}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{c}) - \mathbf{c}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})$:

$X_F \times (X_G \times X_H) + X_G \times (X_H \times X_F) + X_H \times (X_F \times X_G) = 0$

This is the Jacobi identity for the cross product, which follows from the associativity of the quaternions or directly from the definition. Hence $\{F, \{G, H\}\} + \text{cyclic} = -\Pi \cdot 0 = 0$.
</details>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

Mathlib has the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ as skew-symmetric $3\times 3$ matrices with the commutator bracket, and the identification of $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ with $\mathbb{R}^3$ via the cross product. It has matrix Lie groups and smooth manifold structures on them. It does not contain: the dual $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ as a Poisson manifold; the Lie-Poisson (Kirillov-Kostant-Souriau) bracket; the Casimir function; the identification of coadjoint orbits with spheres; the Hamiltonian $H(\Pi) = \sum \Pi_i^2/(2I_i)$ and its flow; or the reduction of $T^*\mathrm{SO}(3)$ to $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$. The key formalization step is constructing the Lie-Poisson bracket on $\mathfrak{g}^*$ for a general Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ in Mathlib, then specializing to $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Lie-Poisson reduction.** The passage from $T^*\mathrm{SO}(3)$ to $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ is an instance of **Poisson reduction**. For any Lie group $G$ acting on $T^*G$ by left (or right) translations, the quotient $T^*G/G \cong \mathfrak{g}^*$ inherits a Poisson structure — the Lie-Poisson bracket. The reduction preserves the Hamiltonian nature of the flow: Hamilton's equations on $T^*G$ project to Lie-Poisson equations on $\mathfrak{g}^*$. For $G = \mathrm{SO}(3)$ this gives Euler's equations. For $G = \mathrm{Diff}_{\mathrm{vol}}(\Omega)$ (volume-preserving diffeomorphisms of a domain) it gives the Euler equations of ideal fluid mechanics [09.07.03]. For $G = \mathrm{SO}(n)$ with $n > 3$ it gives the Euler equations of a rigid body in $n$ dimensions.

**Coadjoint orbits.** The symplectic leaves of the Lie-Poisson bracket on $\mathfrak{g}^*$ are the **coadjoint orbits** $\mathcal{O}_\mu = \{\mathrm{Ad}^*_{g^{-1}}\mu : g \in G\}$. For $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ these are the spheres $|\boldsymbol{\Pi}|^2 = $ const (for $\boldsymbol{\Pi} \neq 0$) and the origin $\{0\}$. Each nonzero coadjoint orbit is a two-dimensional symplectic manifold — the **Kirillov-Kostant-Souriau symplectic form** on the orbit is $\omega_\mu(\mathrm{ad}^*_\xi\mu, \mathrm{ad}^*_\eta\mu) = \langle\mu, [\xi, \eta]\rangle$. The Casimir functions are constant on each orbit; the Hamiltonian dynamics is confined to a single orbit.

**Stability by energy-Casimir method.** The stability of the equilibrium points of Euler's equations can be determined by the **energy-Casimir method**: construct a function $\Phi(H, C)$ that has a critical point at the equilibrium and check the definiteness of its second variation. For rotation about the axis with the largest (or smallest) moment of inertia, $\delta^2\Phi$ is definite, confirming nonlinear stability. This result was first proved by Arnol'd (1965) using the general method of Lagrange-Dirichlet stability for Hamiltonian systems with Casimir constraints.

**Synthesis.** The rigid body is the paradigmatic example of a Hamiltonian system whose phase space is not a cotangent bundle but a Poisson manifold — the dual of a Lie algebra equipped with the Lie-Poisson bracket. The foundational reason this structure appears is that rotational mechanics has a symmetry group ($\mathrm{SO}(3)$) whose action on the full phase space $T^*\mathrm{SO}(3)$ can be quotiented out, and the quotient inherits a Poisson structure from the canonical symplectic form. This generalises to every Lie-group symmetry in mechanics: the ideal fluid [09.07.03], the plasma, the Maxwell-Vlasov system, and the KdV equation all have Lie-Poisson formulations on infinite-dimensional duals of Lie algebras. The Casimir functions of the Lie-Poisson bracket are the topological invariants of the coadjoint orbits — squared angular momentum for the rigid body, helicity for the fluid — and they constrain the reduced dynamics exactly as the energy-momentum constraints do for finite-dimensional systems.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Stability of rotation about the principal axes).** Consider Euler's equations for a rigid body with $I_1 < I_2 < I_3$. Rotation about the 1-axis ($\boldsymbol{\Pi}_0 = (\Pi_1^0, 0, 0)$) and about the 3-axis ($\boldsymbol{\Pi}_0 = (0, 0, \Pi_3^0)$) are nonlinearly stable. Rotation about the 2-axis is unstable.

*Proof.* Define $V(\boldsymbol{\Pi}) = H(\boldsymbol{\Pi}) - \frac{1}{2}\lambda|\boldsymbol{\Pi}|^2$ where $\lambda$ is chosen so that $\nabla V|_{\Pi_0} = 0$. For rotation about the 1-axis, $\lambda = 1/I_1$. The second variation at $\Pi_0 = (\Pi_1^0, 0, 0)$ is

$$\delta^2 V = \left(\frac{1}{I_2} - \frac{1}{I_1}\right)(\delta\Pi_2)^2 + \left(\frac{1}{I_3} - \frac{1}{I_1}\right)(\delta\Pi_3)^2.$$

Since $I_1 < I_2 < I_3$, both coefficients are positive, so $\delta^2 V$ is positive definite. By the energy-Casimir stability theorem (Arnold 1965), the equilibrium is nonlinearly stable. The same argument works for the 3-axis with $\lambda = 1/I_3$, giving $\delta^2 V = (\frac{1}{I_1} - \frac{1}{I_3})(\delta\Pi_1)^2 + (\frac{1}{I_2} - \frac{1}{I_3})(\delta\Pi_2)^2 > 0$.

For the 2-axis, $\lambda = 1/I_2$ gives $\delta^2 V = (\frac{1}{I_1} - \frac{1}{I_2})(\delta\Pi_1)^2 + (\frac{1}{I_3} - \frac{1}{I_2})(\delta\Pi_3)^2$. Since $I_1 < I_2 < I_3$, the first coefficient is negative and the second is positive: $\delta^2 V$ is indefinite. The eigenvalues of the linearised system have opposite sign, confirming instability. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Poisson brackets [09.04.03]** introduce the general Poisson-bracket formalism; the Lie-Poisson bracket on $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ is the first naturally-occurring example of a Poisson bracket that does not come from a symplectic form on the ambient space.

- **Symplectic structure [09.04.05]** develops the symplectic manifold framework; the coadjoint orbits of $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ (spheres) are symplectic submanifolds carrying the Kirillov-Kostant-Souriau form, and the Lie-Poisson bracket restricts to the canonical bracket on each orbit.

- **Euler's fluid equations [09.07.03]** are the Lie-Poisson equations on $\mathfrak{X}_{\rm div}(\Omega)^*$, the exact infinite-dimensional analogue of Euler's rigid-body equations; the helicity is the Casimir function, and Arnold's geometric proof of stability parallels the energy-Casimir method here.

- **Legendre transform [09.04.01]** connects the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formulations of the rigid body; the body-frame angular velocity $\omega$ is the Lagrangian variable and $\Pi = I\omega$ is the conjugate momentum obtained by Legendre transform.

- **Liouville-Arnold theorem [09.06.02]** applies to the free rigid body (integrable with two conserved quantities $H$ and $C$ on a three-dimensional Poisson space); the action-angle variables on each coadjoint orbit describe the polhode explicitly.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Euler derived his equations for rigid-body rotation in 1765 in *Theoria motus corporum solidorum seu rigidorum*, building on his earlier discovery (1752) of the decomposition of general motion into translation of the centre of mass and rotation about it. The equations bear his name because they were the first description of rigid-body dynamics that correctly accounted for the non-commutativity of rotations.

The geometric interpretation — Euler's equations as Hamilton's equations on $\mathfrak{so}(3)^*$ — emerged much later. The Lie-Poisson bracket was implicit in the work of **Lie** (1890) on continuous transformation groups and was made explicit by **Kirillov** (1962), **Kostant** (1970), and **Souriau** (1970) in the context of geometric quantisation and symplectic reduction. **Arnold** (1966) recognized that the Euler equations for ideal fluids are the infinite-dimensional Lie-Poisson equations on $\mathfrak{X}_{\rm div}(\Omega)^*$, opening the field of geometric hydrodynamics.

The tennis racket theorem (intermediate-axis instability) was known experimentally for centuries but was first proved rigorously by **Vladimir Arnold** and **Jacques Henrard** in the 1960s. It has recently been generalized by **Van Handel** and others to quantum spin systems, where the same instability manifests as a phase transition in the quantum dynamics of asymmetric tops.

The Casimir function $C = \tfrac{1}{2}|\boldsymbol{\Pi}|^2$ is named after **Hendrik Casimir**, who identified the analogous quantity $C = \tfrac{1}{2}\int |\mathbf{B}|^2\,dV$ in magnetohydrodynamics. The general theory of Casimir functions on Poisson manifolds was developed by **Marsden and Weinstein** (1974, 1983) as part of the reduction theory for Hamiltonian systems with symmetry.
