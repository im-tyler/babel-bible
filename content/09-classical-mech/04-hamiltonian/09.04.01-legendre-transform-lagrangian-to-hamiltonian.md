## Intuition [Beginner]

The Lagrangian describes a mechanical system using two kinds of variables: positions $q$ (where things are) and velocities $\dot q$ (how fast things move). The Hamiltonian describes the *same* system using positions $q$ and **momenta** $p$ (how hard things would push back if you tried to stop them). The Legendre transform is the mathematical machine that swaps velocity for momentum while preserving all the physics.

Why swap? Velocities are tied to the Lagrangian framework; momenta are the natural variables for the Hamiltonian framework, which leads to quantum mechanics, statistical mechanics, and the geometry of phase space. The Legendre transform is the bridge between these two descriptions of the same physics.

Think of it like a change of currency. A price can be quoted in dollars or in euros — same value, different unit. The Legendre transform is the exchange rate. It takes a function of one variable and produces a function of a *different* variable, encoding the same information in a new form.

Here is a graphical analogy. Take a smooth, upward-curving (convex) function $y = f(x)$. For each slope $p = f'(x)$, draw the tangent line to the curve. That tangent line has a $y$-intercept — the height where the tangent line crosses the $y$-axis. The Legendre transform $f^*(p)$ is the *negative* of that intercept. Equivalently, $f^*(p) = px - f(x)$, evaluated at the $x$ whose slope equals $p$.

This is not just algebra. It says: instead of describing the curve by its height at each position $x$, describe it by the tangent-line intercept at each slope $p$. For a convex curve, the mapping from $x$ to slope $p$ is one-to-one, so no information is lost.

Now apply this to mechanics. The Lagrangian $L(q, \dot q)$ is a function of velocity $\dot q$ (at fixed position $q$). Define the momentum as the "slope" of $L$ with respect to $\dot q$:

$$
p = \frac{dL}{d\dot q}.
$$

The Legendre transform gives a new function of $p$ instead of $\dot q$:

$$
H(q, p) = p\,\dot q - L(q, \dot q),
$$

where $\dot q$ on the right is understood as the velocity that corresponds to the given $p$ (found by inverting $p = dL/d\dot q$). This $H$ is the **Hamiltonian**.

A concrete example: a free particle of mass $m$. The Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2$. The slope is $p = m\dot q$, so $\dot q = p/m$. The Hamiltonian is $H = p \cdot (p/m) - \tfrac{1}{2}m(p/m)^2 = p^2/(2m)$. The Legendre transform converts a parabola in $\dot q$ into a parabola in $p$ — same shape, different variable. Both encode "kinetic energy," but one uses velocity and the other uses momentum.

For a particle in a potential $V(q)$, the Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2 - V(q)$. The slope is still $p = m\dot q$ (the potential does not depend on velocity). The Hamiltonian becomes $H = p^2/(2m) + V(q)$ — kinetic plus potential energy, now written in terms of $q$ and $p$.

The Legendre transform is **invertible**. Starting from $H(q, p)$, you can recover $L(q, \dot q)$ by the same formula run backwards: $L = p\dot q - H$, with $p$ expressed in terms of $\dot q$. No information is lost in the exchange.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The harmonic oscillator: a mass $m$ on a spring with constant $k$. The Lagrangian is

$$
L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}kq^2.
$$

**Step 1. Compute the momentum.** The slope of $L$ with respect to $\dot q$ is

$$
p = \frac{dL}{d\dot q} = m\dot q.
$$

This is the familiar definition of momentum for a particle of mass $m$.

**Step 2. Invert to find velocity.** Solve for $\dot q$ in terms of $p$:

$$
\dot q = \frac{p}{m}.
$$

**Step 3. Compute the Hamiltonian.** Apply the Legendre transform formula $H = p\dot q - L$, substituting $\dot q = p/m$:

$$
H = p \cdot \frac{p}{m} - \left(\tfrac{1}{2}m\frac{p^2}{m^2} - \tfrac{1}{2}kq^2\right) = \frac{p^2}{m} - \frac{p^2}{2m} + \tfrac{1}{2}kq^2.
$$

Simplifying:

$$
H(q, p) = \frac{p^2}{2m} + \tfrac{1}{2}kq^2.
$$

This is kinetic energy $p^2/(2m)$ plus potential energy $\tfrac{1}{2}kq^2$ — the total energy, now expressed as a function of position $q$ and momentum $p$. The Legendre transform has converted the Lagrangian (kinetic minus potential, in velocity language) into the Hamiltonian (kinetic plus potential, in momentum language).

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $Q$ be an $n$-dimensional configuration manifold with local coordinates $q^1, \ldots, q^n$. The Lagrangian is a smooth function $L : TQ \times \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ with coordinates $(q^i, \dot q^i, t)$ on the tangent bundle.

The **conjugate momentum** of the $i$-th coordinate is

$$
p_i := \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}(q, \dot q, t).
$$

This defines a map $\mathbb{F}L : TQ \times \mathbb{R} \to T^*Q \times \mathbb{R}$, the **fibre Legendre transform**, sending $(q, \dot q, t) \mapsto (q, p, t)$ where $p_i = \partial L / \partial \dot q^i$. The map is fibre-preserving: it acts independently on each tangent space $T_q Q$, mapping it to the corresponding cotangent space $T^*_q Q$.

The Legendre transform is locally invertible when the **Hessian matrix**

$$
W_{ij}(q, \dot q, t) := \frac{\partial^2 L}{\partial \dot q^i\, \partial \dot q^j}
$$

is non-degenerate (i.e., $\det W \neq 0$). By the inverse function theorem, $\mathbb{F}L$ is then a local diffeomorphism on each fibre. A Lagrangian for which $W$ is non-degenerate at every point is called **regular**; if $\mathbb{F}L$ is a global diffeomorphism, the Lagrangian is **hyper-regular**.

When $L$ is hyper-regular, the inverse map $\dot q^i = \dot q^i(q, p, t)$ exists globally, and the **Hamiltonian** is defined by

$$
\boxed{\;H(q, p, t) \;:=\; p_i\, \dot q^i(q, p, t) \;-\; L\big(q, \dot q(q, p, t), t\big).\;}
$$

This is the Legendre transform of $L$ along the fibres of $TQ \to Q$. The summation convention applies: $p_i \dot q^i = p_1\dot q^1 + \cdots + p_n\dot q^n$.

The physical interpretation is that $H$ encodes the same dynamics as $L$ but in the "dual" variables $(q, p)$ on the cotangent bundle $T^*Q$ — phase space — rather than the tangent-bundle variables $(q, \dot q)$. The two descriptions are equivalent whenever the Legendre transform is invertible.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **The momentum $p_i$ is not always $m_i\dot q^i$.** For a charged particle in a magnetic field with $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot{\mathbf{q}}|^2 + e\mathbf{A}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{q}} - eV$, the canonical momentum is $\mathbf{p} = m\dot{\mathbf{q}} + e\mathbf{A}$, not $m\dot{\mathbf{q}}$. The vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ contributes an extra term. This distinction between canonical momentum and kinetic momentum is central to gauge theories.

- **The Hamiltonian is not always $T + V$.** For a Lagrangian of the standard form $L = T(\dot q) - V(q)$ with $T$ a homogeneous quadratic in $\dot q$, the Legendre transform yields $H = T + V$. But if $L$ includes terms linear in $\dot q$ (as in the electromagnetic example above), or if the kinetic energy is not quadratic (as in relativistic mechanics), then $H \neq T + V$. The Legendre-transform formula is the reliable definition; $H = T + V$ is a special case.

- **Singular Lagrangians break the construction.** If $\det W = 0$ at some $(q, \dot q)$, the map $\dot q \mapsto p$ is not locally invertible there, and the Hamiltonian cannot be defined by the standard recipe. Gauge theories and the reparametrised relativistic particle have this property. Handling singular Lagrangians requires the Dirac-Bergmann constraint algorithm, which extends the Legendre-transform idea to constrained phase spaces.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (The Legendre transform preserves the time derivative and converts EL equations to Hamilton's equations).** *Let $L(q, \dot q, t)$ be a regular Lagrangian with Hamiltonian $H(q, p, t)$ defined by the Legendre transform. Then:*

*(i) $dH/dt = -\,dL/dt$ along any physical trajectory (so $H$ is conserved if and only if $L$ is time-independent).*

*(ii) The Euler-Lagrange equations for $L$ are equivalent to Hamilton's equations $\dot q^i = \partial H / \partial p_i$, $\dot p_i = -\partial H / \partial q^i$ for $H$.*

**Proof of (i).** The Hamiltonian is $H = p_i\dot q^i - L$ where $p_i = \partial L/\partial\dot q^i$. Differentiate with respect to $t$ along a trajectory:

$$
\frac{dH}{dt} = \dot p_i\dot q^i + p_i\ddot q^i - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i}\dot q^i - \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q^i}\ddot q^i - \frac{\partial L}{\partial t}.
$$

The $p_i\ddot q^i$ and $(\partial L/\partial\dot q^i)\ddot q^i$ terms cancel because $p_i = \partial L/\partial\dot q^i$. If the trajectory satisfies the Euler-Lagrange equations, $\dot p_i = d(\partial L/\partial\dot q^i)/dt = \partial L/\partial q^i$, so the $\dot p_i\dot q^i$ and $(\partial L/\partial q^i)\dot q^i$ terms also cancel, leaving $dH/dt = -\partial L/\partial t$. In particular, if $L$ has no explicit time dependence, $dH/dt = 0$ and $H$ is conserved. If $L$ is time-independent, $H$ is the conserved energy. $\square$

**Proof of (ii).** Take the partial derivatives of $H$ with respect to $p_i$ and $q^i$, treating $\dot q$ as a function of $(q, p, t)$.

First, differentiate with respect to $p_i$:

$$
\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i} = \dot q^i + p_j\frac{\partial\dot q^j}{\partial p_i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q^j}\frac{\partial\dot q^j}{\partial p_i}.
$$

The last two terms cancel because $p_j = \partial L/\partial\dot q^j$, giving

$$
\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i} = \dot q^i. \tag{1}
$$

Next, differentiate with respect to $q^i$:

$$
\frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i} = p_j\frac{\partial\dot q^j}{\partial q^i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q^j}\frac{\partial\dot q^j}{\partial q^i}.
$$

Again $p_j = \partial L/\partial\dot q^j$ cancels two terms, leaving

$$
\frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i} = -\frac{\partial L}{\partial q^i}. \tag{2}
$$

Now assume the Euler-Lagrange equations hold: $d(\partial L/\partial\dot q^i)/dt = \partial L/\partial q^i$. Along the Legendre-transformed trajectory, $p_i(t) = \partial L/\partial\dot q^i$, so $\dot p_i = d(\partial L/\partial\dot q^i)/dt = \partial L/\partial q^i$. By equation (2), $\partial L/\partial q^i = -\partial H/\partial q^i$, hence $\dot p_i = -\partial H/\partial q^i$. Equation (1) gives $\dot q^i = \partial H/\partial p_i$. These are Hamilton's equations. The converse follows by running the same algebra in reverse. $\square$

### Worked example: the relativistic free particle

A free relativistic particle has Lagrangian $L = -mc^2\sqrt{1 - \dot q^2/c^2}$ (one spatial dimension for simplicity, $\dot q = dq/dt$). The conjugate momentum is

$$
p = \frac{\partial L}{\partial\dot q} = \frac{m\dot q}{\sqrt{1 - \dot q^2/c^2}} = \gamma m\dot q,
$$

the relativistic momentum, where $\gamma = (1 - \dot q^2/c^2)^{-1/2}$. Solve for $\dot q$: squaring $p = \gamma m\dot q$ and using $\gamma^2 = 1/(1 - \dot q^2/c^2)$, one finds $\dot q = pc/\sqrt{p^2 + m^2c^2}$.

Now compute $H = p\dot q - L$:

$$
H = \frac{p^2c}{\sqrt{p^2 + m^2c^2}} + mc^2\sqrt{1 - \frac{p^2c^{-2}}{p^2c^{-2} + m^2}}.
$$

Simplifying the square root and combining:

$$
H = c\sqrt{p^2 + m^2c^2}.
$$

This is the relativistic energy-momentum relation $E = \sqrt{m^2c^4 + p^2c^2}$. The Legendre transform has produced the relativistic energy as a function of momentum — the Hamiltonian for a free relativistic particle.

A particle of mass $m$ moves in the potential $V(q) = \alpha q$ (constant force $\alpha > 0$). The Lagrangian is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2 - \alpha q$. Compute $p$, invert to find $\dot q(p)$, and compute $H(q, p)$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

For the harmonic oscillator $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}kq^2$, compute the Hessian matrix $W = \partial^2 L/\partial\dot q^2$ and verify it is non-degenerate (confirming the Lagrangian is regular).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For a single degree of freedom, the Hessian is a $1 \times 1$ matrix.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$W = \partial^2 L/\partial\dot q^2 = m$. Since $m > 0$, $\det W = m \neq 0$. The Lagrangian is regular (hyper-regular, in fact — the inverse $\dot q = p/m$ exists globally). For any Lagrangian of the form $L = T(\dot q) - V(q)$ with $T$ a positive-definite quadratic in $\dot q$, the Hessian is constant and positive-definite, guaranteeing hyper-regularity.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

A charged particle in a uniform magnetic field (along $z$) with vector potential $\mathbf{A} = (-By/2, Bx/2, 0)$ has Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot x^2 + \dot y^2 + \dot z^2) + \tfrac{1}{2}eB(x\dot y - y\dot x)$. Compute the conjugate momenta $p_x$, $p_y$, $p_z$ and the Hamiltonian $H$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The magnetic term is linear in velocity, so the momenta pick up a contribution from the vector potential.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$p_x = m\dot x - \tfrac{1}{2}eBy$, $p_y = m\dot y + \tfrac{1}{2}eBx$, $p_z = m\dot z$. Invert: $\dot x = (p_x + \tfrac{1}{2}eBy)/m$, $\dot y = (p_y - \tfrac{1}{2}eBx)/m$, $\dot z = p_z/m$.

$$
H = \frac{1}{2m}\left[\left(p_x + \tfrac{1}{2}eBy\right)^2 + \left(p_y - \tfrac{1}{2}eBx\right)^2 + p_z^2\right].
$$

This is the Hamiltonian for cyclotron motion. The canonical momenta differ from the kinetic momenta $m\dot x$, $m\dot y$ by gauge-dependent terms proportional to $B$. The Hamiltonian itself is gauge-invariant — a different choice of $\mathbf{A}$ changes $p_x, p_y$ but not the physical predictions.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that if $L$ is independent of $t$, then $H$ is also independent of $t$, and the value of $H$ is conserved along any solution of the Euler-Lagrange equations. (This reproves the Beltrami identity from a Hamiltonian perspective.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the relation $\partial H/\partial t = -\partial L/\partial t$ derived in the proof of the key theorem, and then compute $dH/dt$ along a trajectory using the chain rule.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From the proof of part (i): $\partial H/\partial t = -\partial L/\partial t$. If $L$ is time-independent, $\partial L/\partial t = 0$, so $\partial H/\partial t = 0$ — $H$ has no explicit time dependence. Now $dH/dt = (\partial H/\partial q^i)\dot q^i + (\partial H/\partial p_i)\dot p_i + \partial H/\partial t$. On a solution of Hamilton's equations, $\dot q^i = \partial H/\partial p_i$ and $\dot p_i = -\partial H/\partial q^i$, so the first two terms cancel: $dH/dt = \partial H/\partial t = 0$. Energy conservation follows from the structure of the equations and the time-independence of $L$, with no further input required.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify directly that the Legendre transform is its own inverse for a free particle: starting from $H(p) = p^2/(2m)$, compute $L(\dot q) = p\dot q - H$ where $p = dH/d\dot q$ (the "reverse" Legendre transform) and confirm you recover $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the reverse transform, treat $H$ as a function of $p$ and define $\dot q = dH/dp$. Then $L = p\dot q - H$ with $p = p(\dot q)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\dot q = dH/dp = p/m$, so $p = m\dot q$. Then $L = p\dot q - H = m\dot q \cdot \dot q - (m\dot q)^2/(2m) = m\dot q^2 - m\dot q^2/2 = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot q^2$. The original Lagrangian is recovered. The Legendre transform applied twice returns to the starting function, provided the function is convex (or concave) so the transform is well-defined at each step.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

The Lagrangian for a particle in a rotating frame (angular velocity $\omega$ about the $z$-axis) in two dimensions is $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m[\dot x^2 + \dot y^2 + 2\omega(x\dot y - y\dot x) + \omega^2(x^2 + y^2)]$. Compute the momenta and the Hamiltonian. Is $H = T + V$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Coriolis term is linear in velocity; the centrifugal term is a potential. Compute $p_x$ and $p_y$ carefully.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$p_x = m(\dot x - \omega y)$, $p_y = m(\dot y + \omega x)$. Inverting: $\dot x = (p_x + m\omega y)/m$, $\dot y = (p_y - m\omega x)/m$.

$$
H = \frac{1}{2m}\left[(p_x + m\omega y)^2 + (p_y - m\omega x)^2\right] - \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2(x^2 + y^2).
$$

Expanding the squares: $H = (p_x^2 + p_y^2)/(2m) + \omega(x p_y - y p_x)$. This is *not* equal to $T + V$ in the inertial frame; it is the **Jacobi integral** (energy in the rotating frame, with the centrifugal potential subtracted). The Hamiltonian in a rotating frame includes the Coriolis coupling and differs from the naive $T + V$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m(\dot r^2 + r^2\dot\theta^2) + \tfrac{k}{r}$ describes a particle in a central potential in polar coordinates. Compute the conjugate momenta $p_r$ and $p_\theta$, compute the Hamiltonian, and identify the cyclic coordinate and its conserved quantity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A cyclic coordinate is one that does not appear in $L$ (and hence not in $H$). Its conjugate momentum is conserved.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$p_r = m\dot r$, $p_\theta = mr^2\dot\theta$. Inverting: $\dot r = p_r/m$, $\dot\theta = p_\theta/(mr^2)$. The Hamiltonian:

$$
H = \frac{p_r^2}{2m} + \frac{p_\theta^2}{2mr^2} - \frac{k}{r}.
$$

The coordinate $\theta$ is cyclic (absent from $L$ and hence from $H$), so $p_\theta$ is conserved — this is angular momentum conservation. The $p_\theta^2/(2mr^2)$ term acts as a centrifugal barrier in the effective radial potential. Fixing $p_\theta = \ell$ reduces the system to one degree of freedom $(r, p_r)$ with $H_{\rm eff} = p_r^2/(2m) + \ell^2/(2mr^2) - k/r$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Consider the singular Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}(\dot q_1 - \dot q_2)^2$. Compute the Hessian matrix $W_{ij} = \partial^2 L/\partial\dot q^i\partial\dot q^j$ and show it is degenerate. Compute the momenta and explain why the Hamiltonian construction fails.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $L$ and compute the second derivatives with respect to both velocities. A degenerate Hessian means $\det W = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$L = \tfrac{1}{2}\dot q_1^2 - \dot q_1\dot q_2 + \tfrac{1}{2}\dot q_2^2$. The Hessian is $W = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, with $\det W = 0$. The momenta are $p_1 = \dot q_1 - \dot q_2$ and $p_2 = -\dot q_1 + \dot q_2 = -p_1$. The momenta satisfy the **primary constraint** $p_1 + p_2 = 0$, meaning the map $(\dot q_1, \dot q_2) \mapsto (p_1, p_2)$ is not invertible — it maps a two-dimensional velocity space onto a one-dimensional subspace of momentum space. The standard Legendre transform cannot produce a Hamiltonian on the full four-dimensional phase space; instead, the system lives on the constraint surface $p_1 + p_2 = 0$. This is the starting point of the Dirac-Bergmann theory of constrained Hamiltonian systems.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the Legendre transform of a convex function $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is itself convex. (This guarantees that the Hamiltonian, viewed as a function of $p$ at fixed $q$, inherits good geometric properties from the Lagrangian viewed as a function of $\dot q$.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $d^2 f^*/dp^2$ using the relation $df^*/dp = x(p)$ where $p = f'(x)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $f^*(p) = px(p) - f(x(p))$ where $p = f'(x)$ determines $x$ as a function of $p$. Differentiate: $df^*/dp = x + p\,dx/dp - f'(x)\,dx/dp = x$ (since $p = f'$). Differentiate again: $d^2f^*/dp^2 = dx/dp = 1/f''(x)$. Since $f$ is convex, $f'' > 0$, so $d^2f^*/dp^2 > 0$. The Legendre transform of a convex function is convex.

This has a direct mechanical consequence: if $L$ is strictly convex in $\dot q$ (which holds whenever $T$ is positive-definite quadratic in $\dot q$), then $H$ is strictly convex in $p$. In multiple degrees of freedom, the Hessian $\partial^2 H/\partial p_i\partial p_j$ is the matrix inverse of the Lagrangian Hessian $\partial^2 L/\partial\dot q^i\partial\dot q^j$ — a special case of the duality between a matrix and its inverse.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, symbolic).**

A particle constrained to move on a sphere of radius $R$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ has Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}mR^2(\dot\theta^2 + \sin^2\theta\,\dot\varphi^2)$ in spherical coordinates (with $r = R$ fixed). Compute the Hamiltonian and show that $H = T$ — there is no potential, so the Hamiltonian is purely kinetic.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Legendre transform of a purely kinetic Lagrangian on a Riemannian manifold always gives a Hamiltonian that equals the kinetic energy expressed in momentum variables.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$p_\theta = mR^2\dot\theta$, $p_\varphi = mR^2\sin^2\theta\,\dot\varphi$. Inverting: $\dot\theta = p_\theta/(mR^2)$, $\dot\varphi = p_\varphi/(mR^2\sin^2\theta)$.

$$
H = \frac{p_\theta^2}{2mR^2} + \frac{p_\varphi^2}{2mR^2\sin^2\theta}.
$$

Indeed $H = T$: the Hamiltonian is the kinetic energy expressed in the $(q, p)$ variables instead of $(q, \dot q)$. The $\varphi$-coordinate is cyclic, so $p_\varphi$ (the $z$-component of angular momentum) is conserved. This example illustrates how the Legendre transform handles curved configuration spaces — the metric on the sphere (encapsulated in the $\sin^2\theta$ factor) is absorbed into the momentum-velocity relation and reappears in the $p$-dependence of $H$.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The fibre derivative and hyper-regularity

The Legendre transform in mechanics is not the Legendre transform of a single function. It is a **fibre derivative**: for each base point $q \in Q$, the map $\dot q \mapsto p$ is the derivative of the restricted function $L_q := L(q, \cdot, t) : T_q Q \to \mathbb{R}$. The fibre Legendre transform $\mathbb{F}L : TQ \to T^*Q$ is

$$
\mathbb{F}L(q, \dot q) = \left(q, \, d(L_q)_{\dot q}\right),
$$

where $d(L_q)_{\dot q} \in T^*_q Q$ is the fibre derivative at $\dot q$. Regularity of $L$ means $d(L_q)$ is a local diffeomorphism on each fibre; hyper-regularity means it is a global diffeomorphism.

Hyper-regularity holds for the standard mechanical Lagrangian $L(q, \dot q) = \tfrac{1}{2}g_{ij}(q)\dot q^i\dot q^j - V(q)$ where $g$ is a Riemannian metric on $Q$. The fibre derivative is $p_i = g_{ij}(q)\dot q^j$, and the non-degeneracy of $g$ makes this invertible: $\dot q^i = g^{ij}(q)p_j$, where $g^{ij}$ is the inverse metric. The Hamiltonian becomes

$$
H(q, p) = \tfrac{1}{2}g^{ij}(q)p_ip_j + V(q),
$$

the metric dual of the kinetic energy plus the potential. The Legendre transform is the musical isomorphism $\flat : TQ \to T^*Q$ determined by the metric, and its inverse is $\sharp : T^*Q \to TQ$.

### Singular Lagrangians and the Dirac-Bergmann algorithm

When $\det W = 0$, the fibre Legendre transform maps $TQ$ onto a proper submanifold of $T^*Q$ — the **primary constraint surface** $\Phi = \{(q, p) : \phi_a(q, p) = 0\}$ for some functions $\phi_a$. The Hamiltonian $H = p_i\dot q^i - L$ is not uniquely determined on $T^*Q$ because the map $(q, \dot q) \mapsto (q, p)$ is many-to-one; instead one defines a **total Hamiltonian** $H_T = H + u^a\phi_a$ with undetermined multipliers $u^a$.

Consistency conditions — requiring that the constraints be preserved under the time evolution generated by $H_T$ — produce **secondary constraints** and may fix the multipliers $u^a$ or generate further constraints iteratively. The algorithm terminates when all constraints are consistent and the multipliers are either fixed or correspond to genuine gauge freedom. The result is a Hamiltonian system on the constrained phase space equipped with the **Dirac bracket**, a modified Poisson bracket that respects the constraints.

This is the standard route for gauge theories. The electromagnetic Lagrangian $L = -\tfrac{1}{4}F_{\mu\nu}F^{\mu\nu}$ in four dimensions has a singular Hessian (the time derivative of $A_0$ does not appear). The Dirac-Bergmann analysis produces the primary constraint $\pi^0 = 0$ (where $\pi^\mu$ is the conjugate momentum of $A_\mu$), the secondary Gauss-law constraint $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho$, and identifies the gauge freedom as the arbitrariness of $A_0$.

### Finsler generalisation

If the kinetic energy is replaced by a **Finsler function** $F(q, \dot q)$ — positively homogeneous of degree 1 in $\dot q$ and satisfying convexity but not necessarily arising from a Riemannian metric — the fibre derivative still defines a map $\mathbb{F}L : TQ \to T^*Q$, but the Hamiltonian is now $H = p_i\dot q^i - L = F^2(q, \dot q)/2 + V(q)$ where $F^*$ is the **co-Finsler metric** on $T^*Q$. The Legendre transform maps the Finsler manifold $(Q, F)$ to the co-Finsler manifold $(Q, F^*)$. Hyper-regularity holds for strongly convex Finsler functions. This framework covers Lagrangians with non-quadratic kinetic terms (relativistic particles, certain $p$-adic models) without leaving the Legendre-transform paradigm.

### Symplectic geometry of the Legendre transform

The cotangent bundle $T^*Q$ carries the canonical symplectic form $\omega = dq^i \wedge dp_i$. The Legendre transform $\mathbb{F}L : TQ \to T^*Q$ pulls $\omega$ back to a 2-form $\omega_L := (\mathbb{F}L)^*\omega$ on $TQ$. For a hyper-regular Lagrangian, $\omega_L$ is symplectic (closed and non-degenerate), and the Euler-Lagrange flow on $(TQ, \omega_L)$ is mapped by $\mathbb{F}L$ to the Hamiltonian flow on $(T^*Q, \omega)$ — the Legendre transform is a **symplectomorphism** between $(TQ, \omega_L)$ and $(T^*Q, \omega)$. This is the geometric content of the equivalence between Lagrangian and Hamiltonian mechanics: they are the same dynamics on two different but symplectomorphic spaces.

### The Legendre transform in thermodynamics

The same mathematical operation appears in thermodynamics [11.01.02]. The internal energy $U(S, V)$ is a function of entropy $S$ and volume $V$. The Helmholtz free energy $F = U - TS$ is the Legendre transform of $U$ with respect to $S$ (swapping $S$ for temperature $T = \partial U/\partial S$). The enthalpy $H = U + PV$ is the Legendre transform with respect to $V$ (swapping $V$ for pressure $P = -\partial U/\partial V$). The Gibbs free energy $G = U - TS + PV$ is the double Legendre transform. Each thermodynamic potential is adapted to a different set of natural variables, just as the Hamiltonian is adapted to $(q, p)$ rather than $(q, \dot q)$.

## Connections [Master]

- **Action principle [09.02.01]** — the Lagrangian $L(q, \dot q, t)$ that enters the Legendre transform is the same function whose stationary action produces the Euler-Lagrange equations. The Legendre transform does not change the physics; it repackages it.

- **Euler-Lagrange equations [09.02.02]** — the key theorem proves that the EL equations and Hamilton's equations are equivalent under the Legendre transform. The EL equations are the Lagrangian shadow; Hamilton's equations are the Hamiltonian shadow of the same dynamics.

- **Conservation laws [09.01.03]** — the energy function $h = \dot q^i p_i - L$ that appears as an intermediate in the Beltrami identity is the Hamiltonian evaluated on shell. Energy conservation is $dH/dt = 0$ when $L$ (hence $H$) is time-independent.

- **Hamilton's equations [09.04.02]** — this unit constructs the Hamiltonian $H(q, p)$ and proves the equivalence with the EL equations; the next unit develops the full dynamical theory (Poisson brackets, symplectic structure, integrable systems) built on $H$.

- **Canonical transformations [09.05.01]** — canonical transformations are symplectomorphisms of $T^*Q$. The four standard types of generating function are related to each other by Legendre transforms of the same kind developed here.

- **Thermodynamic potentials [11.01.02]** — the Helmholtz free energy, enthalpy, and Gibbs free energy are Legendre transforms of the internal energy $U(S, V)$ with respect to $S$ and/or $V$. The mathematical operation is identical; only the physical interpretation of the variables changes.

- **Geometric mechanics [09.09.01]** — the fibre Legendre transform $\mathbb{F}L : TQ \to T^*Q$ is the bridge between Lagrangian mechanics on the tangent bundle and Hamiltonian mechanics on the cotangent bundle. Its symplectic geometry is the foundation of the geometric mechanics programme.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The Legendre transform originates in number theory and geometry, not in physics. Adrien-Marie Legendre introduced it in 1787 in the context of his work on elliptic integrals and the calculus of variations, as a technique for transforming between a function and its dual description via tangent-line intercepts. The core observation — that a smooth convex curve can be described equally well by its points or by its tangent lines — is purely geometric.

William Rowan Hamilton applied this mathematical device to mechanics in his 1834 paper "On a general method in dynamics." Hamilton's insight was that the passage from Lagrangian to Hamiltonian mechanics is exactly the Legendre transform of the Lagrangian with respect to the velocity variables. The result — a first-order system in twice as many variables — was not merely a computational convenience. It revealed phase space as the natural arena for mechanics and made the symplectic structure of dynamics visible for the first time.

Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi recognised that Hamilton's formulation opened the door to a theory of canonical transformations and a PDE (the Hamilton-Jacobi equation) whose complete solution solves the mechanical problem by quadrature. Jacobi's "Vorlesungen uber Dynamik" (1842, published posthumously in 1866) systematised the Hamiltonian framework and established the Legendre transform as a permanent fixture of analytical mechanics.

The Legendre transform acquired a second, independent life in thermodynamics through the work of Massieu (1869), Gibbs (1876), and Planck (1897), who introduced the thermodynamic potentials (free energies, enthalpy) as Legendre transforms of the internal energy. The recognition that the *same* mathematical operation underlies both the passage from Lagrangian to Hamiltonian mechanics and the passage from one thermodynamic potential to another is a 20th-century synthesis, articulated in the axiomatic thermodynamics of Callen (1960) and the geometric thermodynamics of Hermann (1973) and Mrugala (1978).
