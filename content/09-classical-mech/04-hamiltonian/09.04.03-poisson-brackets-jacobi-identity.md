## Intuition [Beginner]

The Poisson bracket is a machine that takes two functions on phase space and produces a third. Its physical meaning: **how much does one quantity change along the flow generated by another?** If you fix a function $g$, the bracket $\{f, g\}$ measures the rate at which $f$ changes as you follow the trajectory that $g$ would generate if it were the Hamiltonian. This is the deepest structural operation in classical mechanics. Everything -- equations of motion, conservation laws, the connection to quantum mechanics -- flows from it.

The most important bracket is $\{f, H\}$, the bracket of any observable $f$ with the Hamiltonian $H$. This gives the time rate of change of $f$ along physical trajectories (plus any explicit time dependence of $f$ itself). If $\{f, H\} = 0$ and $f$ has no explicit time dependence, then $f$ is a **constant of motion** -- it is conserved.

The canonical bracket relations are the DNA of the theory. For a single pair of position $q$ and momentum $p$:

$$
\{q, q\} = 0, \qquad \{p, p\} = 0, \qquad \{q, p\} = 1.
$$

Positions do not "interfere" with other positions, momenta do not interfere with other momenta, but position and momentum *do* interfere. This is the classical shadow of the Heisenberg uncertainty principle: $q$ and $p$ are conjugate variables, and their non-zero bracket encodes the fact that they are not simultaneously specifiable without trade-offs.

A powerful result called **Poisson's theorem** states that if $f$ and $g$ are both conserved, then their Poisson bracket $\{f, g\}$ is also conserved. Conserved quantities close under the bracket operation -- they form an algebra. This means you can *generate* new conservation laws from ones you already know. For example, if two components of angular momentum are conserved, the third must be as well, because their brackets produce each other.

### Angular momentum: a first algebra

The clearest example of the bracket at work is angular momentum. In three dimensions, $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}$, with components:

$$
L_x = yp_z - zp_y, \qquad L_y = zp_x - xp_z, \qquad L_z = xp_y - yp_x.
$$

Their Poisson brackets satisfy:

$$
\{L_x, L_y\} = L_z, \qquad \{L_y, L_z\} = L_x, \qquad \{L_z, L_x\} = L_y.
$$

This is the angular momentum algebra. The three components *close* under the bracket -- bracketing any two gives the third (up to sign). If a system conserves $L_x$ and $L_y$ (meaning $\{L_x, H\} = 0$ and $\{L_y, H\} = 0$), Poisson's theorem guarantees that $\{L_x, L_y\} = L_z$ is also conserved. The algebra *forces* the third conservation law.

This angular momentum algebra is the same structure that appears in quantum mechanics as the commutation relations $[\hat{L}_x, \hat{L}_y] = i\hbar \hat{L}_z$. The Poisson bracket is the classical version of the quantum commutator.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute the Poisson bracket $\{L_x, L_y\}$ from the definitions, using only the canonical relations $\{q^i, p_j\} = \delta^i_j$ (which equals 1 when the indices match, 0 otherwise) and the rules that the bracket is linear in each argument and satisfies a product rule.

The components are $L_x = yp_z - zp_y$ and $L_y = zp_x - xp_z$. By linearity:

$$
\{L_x, L_y\} = \{yp_z - zp_y,\; zp_x - xp_z\}.
$$

Expanding using bilinearity, there are four terms. The product rule (Leibniz rule) says $\{fg, h\} = \{f, h\}g + f\{g, h\}$. Applying this systematically:

The only non-zero elementary brackets are $\{x, p_x\} = 1$, $\{y, p_y\} = 1$, $\{z, p_z\} = 1$. All other brackets of a coordinate with a momentum are zero. And any bracket of two coordinates or two momenta is zero.

Working through each term, the cross-dependencies cancel, and the surviving contribution is:

$$
\{L_x, L_y\} = xp_y - yp_x = L_z.
$$

The other two relations follow by the same calculation with cyclic permutation of labels $(x \to y \to z \to x)$:

$$
\{L_y, L_z\} = L_x, \qquad \{L_z, L_x\} = L_y.
$$

The angular momentum components form a closed algebra under the Poisson bracket. This closure is not a coincidence -- it reflects the rotational symmetry of space. The Poisson bracket of two generators of rotation is another generator of rotation.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $(M, \omega)$ be a symplectic manifold of dimension $2n$ with local Darboux coordinates $(q^1, \ldots, q^n, p_1, \ldots, p_n)$ in which $\omega = dq^i \wedge dp_i$. The **Poisson bracket** of two smooth functions $f, g \in C^\infty(M)$ is

$$
\boxed{\;\{f, g\} \;:=\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial p_i} \;-\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial q^i}\;}
$$

with the Einstein summation convention on repeated indices. Equivalently, in the combined coordinate $z = (q^1, \ldots, q^n, p_1, \ldots, p_n)$:

$$
\{f, g\} = J^{ij}\frac{\partial f}{\partial z^i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial z^j}
$$

where $J$ is the $2n \times 2n$ symplectic matrix $J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & I_n \\ -I_n & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

### Algebraic properties

The Poisson bracket satisfies four fundamental identities:

1. **Bilinearity**: $\{af + bg,\, h\} = a\{f, h\} + b\{g, h\}$ for all $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$.
2. **Antisymmetry**: $\{f, g\} = -\{g, f\}$.
3. **Leibniz rule** (derivation property): $\{f,\, gh\} = \{f, g\}h + g\{f, h\}$.
4. **Jacobi identity**: $\{f, \{g, h\}\} + \{g, \{h, f\}\} + \{h, \{f, g\}\} = 0$.

Properties (1), (2), and (4) make $(C^\infty(M), \{\cdot, \cdot\})$ a **Lie algebra**. Adding (3) makes it a **Poisson algebra** -- a Lie algebra that is also a commutative algebra, with the Leibniz rule ensuring compatibility between the bracket and the pointwise product of functions.

### Canonical relations

The coordinate functions satisfy:

$$
\{q^i, q^j\} = 0, \qquad \{p_i, p_j\} = 0, \qquad \{q^i, p_j\} = \delta^i_j.
$$

These are the **fundamental Poisson brackets**. Every other bracket can be computed from them using the algebraic properties above. In the symplectic-matrix notation, they are simply $\{z^i, z^j\} = J^{ij}$.

### Hamilton's equations in bracket form

Hamilton's equations [09.04.02] become a single equation. For any observable $f(q, p, t)$:

$$
\frac{df}{dt} = \{f, H\} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}.
$$

Setting $f = q^i$ recovers $\dot{q}^i = \partial H / \partial p_i$ and setting $f = p_i$ recovers $\dot{p}_i = -\partial H / \partial q^i$. The bracket form unifies both halves of Hamilton's equations into one statement: **time evolution is the Poisson bracket with the Hamiltonian**.

A function $f$ is a **constant of motion** (first integral) if and only if $\{f, H\} = 0$ and $\partial f / \partial t = 0$. The bracket with $H$ is the Lie derivative of $f$ along the Hamiltonian flow.

### Poisson's theorem

**Theorem.** *If $f$ and $g$ are constants of motion ($\{f, H\} = \{g, H\} = 0$ and $\partial f/\partial t = \partial g/\partial t = 0$), then their Poisson bracket $\{f, g\}$ is also a constant of motion.*

*Proof.* By the Jacobi identity applied to the triple $(f, g, H)$:

$$
\{H, \{f, g\}\} = -\{f, \{g, H\}\} - \{g, \{H, f\}\} = -\{f, 0\} - \{g, 0\} = 0. \qquad \square
$$

The constants of motion form a **Lie subalgebra** under the Poisson bracket. If you know two conserved quantities, Poisson's theorem gives you a third (which may be zero, functionally dependent on the originals, or genuinely new).

### The angular momentum $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ algebra

For the angular momentum components $L_i = \epsilon_{ijk} q^j p_k$ (where $\epsilon_{ijk}$ is the Levi-Civita symbol), the Poisson brackets close:

$$
\{L_i, L_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk} L_k.
$$

This is the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{so}(3) \cong \mathfrak{su}(2)$ of the rotation group. Defining $J_k = L_k / \hbar$ (purely as a rescaling, no quantum mechanics involved), the brackets become $\{J_i, J_j\} = \epsilon_{ijk} J_k / \hbar$, recovering the standard normalisation in which the quantum commutator $[\hat{J}_i, \hat{J}_j] = i\hbar \epsilon_{ijk} \hat{J}_k$ is the canonical quantisation of the classical bracket.

The Casimir of this algebra is $L^2 = L_x^2 + L_y^2 + L_z^2$. Direct computation confirms $\{L^2, L_i\} = 0$ for each $i$: the Casimir Poisson-commutes with every generator. For a rotationally symmetric Hamiltonian ($H$ depends on $\mathbf{L}$ only through $L^2$), all three components $L_i$ are individually conserved (since $\{L_i, H\} = 0$), not just $L^2$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Jacobi identity for the canonical Poisson bracket).** *The canonical Poisson bracket on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ satisfies the Jacobi identity.*

**Proof.** Write the bracket as $\{f, g\} = J^{ij}(\partial_i f)(\partial_j g)$ where $\partial_i$ denotes partial differentiation with respect to the $i$-th component of the combined coordinate $z = (q, p)$ and $J^{ij}$ is the constant symplectic matrix. Then:

$$
\{f, \{g, h\}\} = J^{ij}\partial_i f \cdot \partial_j\!\left(J^{kl}\partial_k g \cdot \partial_l h\right).
$$

Expanding the inner derivative by the product rule:

$$
\partial_j\!\left(\partial_k g \cdot \partial_l h\right) = (\partial_j\partial_k g)\,\partial_l h + \partial_k g\,(\partial_j\partial_l h).
$$

Therefore:

$$
\{f, \{g, h\}\} = J^{ij}J^{kl}\!\left[\partial_i f\, \partial_j\partial_k g\, \partial_l h + \partial_i f\, \partial_k g\, \partial_j\partial_l h\right].
$$

The full Jacobi sum $\{f, \{g, h\}\} + \{g, \{h, f\}\} + \{h, \{f, g\}\}$ produces six classes of terms. The terms involving second derivatives of $g$ appear as:

$$
J^{ij}J^{kl}\,\partial_i f\, \partial_j\partial_k g\, \partial_l h + J^{ij}J^{kl}\,\partial_i g\, \partial_j\partial_k h\, \partial_l f + J^{ij}J^{kl}\,\partial_i h\, \partial_j\partial_k f\, \partial_l g.
$$

Since $J^{ij}$ is antisymmetric ($J^{ij} = -J^{ji}$), relabelling dummy indices $i \leftrightarrow j$ in alternate terms and using the symmetry of mixed partial derivatives ($\partial_j\partial_k = \partial_k\partial_j$) shows these terms cancel in pairs. The terms involving first derivatives of all three functions similarly cancel by the antisymmetry of $J$ and the symmetry of second derivatives. The crucial fact is that $J^{ij}$ is **constant** (independent of $z$), so no derivatives of $J$ appear and the cancellation is exact. $\square$

**Remark.** On a general symplectic manifold $(M, \omega)$, the Poisson bracket is defined coordinate-independently by $\{f, g\} = \omega(X_f, X_g)$ where $X_f$ is the Hamiltonian vector field of $f$ (defined by $\iota_{X_f}\omega = df$). The Jacobi identity for this bracket is equivalent to the **closedness** condition $d\omega = 0$. The canonical bracket on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ corresponds to $\omega = dq^i \wedge dp_i$, which is automatically closed because it is a constant form.

### Liouville's theorem via Poisson brackets

The Hamiltonian flow $\Phi_t$ generated by $H$ preserves the Poisson bracket:

$$
\{f \circ \Phi_t,\, g \circ \Phi_t\} = \{f, g\} \circ \Phi_t.
$$

This is because the Hamiltonian flow is a symplectomorphism ($\Phi_t^*\omega = \omega$), and the Poisson bracket is defined by $\omega$. Differentiating at $t = 0$:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\{f \circ \Phi_t, g \circ \Phi_t\}\bigg|_{t=0} = \{\{f, H\}, g\} + \{f, \{g, H\}\} = -\{H, \{f, g\}\} = \{f, g\} \circ X_H,
$$

which vanishes when integrated, confirming preservation. Since the Liouville volume form $\Omega = \omega^n / n!$ can be expressed in terms of the bracket structure, the preservation of $\{f, g\}$ under the flow implies the preservation of phase-space volume. This is **Liouville's theorem** in its algebraic form: Hamiltonian flow is incompressible in phase space [09.04.04].

Rearrange the Jacobi identity $\{f, \{g, h\}\} + \{g, \{h, f\}\} + \{h, \{f, g\}\} = 0$ using antisymmetry.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Jacobi identity gives $\{f, \{g, h\}\} + \{g, \{h, f\}\} + \{h, \{f, g\}\} = 0$. By antisymmetry: $\{g, \{h, f\}\} = -\{g, \{f, h\}\}$ and $\{h, \{f, g\}\} = -\{\{f, g\}, h\}$. So: $\{f, \{g, h\}\} - \{g, \{f, h\}\} - \{\{f, g\}, h\} = 0$, giving $\{f, \{g, h\}\} = \{\{f, g\}, h\} + \{g, \{f, h\}\}$. This is the derivation property of $\mathrm{ad}_f$ with respect to the bracket.

</details>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that a canonical transformation (symplectomorphism $\Phi$) preserves the Poisson bracket: $\{f \circ \Phi,\, g \circ \Phi\} = \{f, g\} \circ \Phi$ for all $f, g$. Conclude that the Poisson bracket is an intrinsic object on the symplectic manifold, independent of the choice of Darboux coordinates.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the coordinate-free definition $\{f, g\} = \omega(X_f, X_g)$ and the fact that $\Phi^*\omega = \omega$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Hamiltonian vector field of $f \circ \Phi$ satisfies $X_{f \circ \Phi} = (\Phi^{-1})_* X_f$ (pushforward of $X_f$ by $\Phi^{-1}$). Then:

$$\{f \circ \Phi, g \circ \Phi\} = \omega(X_{f \circ \Phi}, X_{g \circ \Phi}) = \omega((\Phi^{-1})_* X_f, (\Phi^{-1})_* X_g).$$

By the symplectomorphism condition $\Phi^*\omega = \omega$, pulling back by $\Phi$ gives $\omega = \Phi^*\omega$, so $\omega((\Phi^{-1})_* X_f, (\Phi^{-1})_* X_g) = (\Phi^*\omega)(X_f, X_g) \circ \Phi^{-1} = \omega(X_f, X_g) \circ \Phi^{-1}$. Since $\Phi$ is a diffeomorphism, this equals $\{f, g\} \circ \Phi$. The bracket is invariant under all symplectomorphisms and is therefore a geometric object on $(M, \omega)$.

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The Poisson bracket is a central missing piece in Mathlib's Hamiltonian mechanics formalisation. The specific ingredients needed are:

1. The Poisson bracket as a bilinear map $\{\cdot, \cdot\} : C^\infty(M) \times C^\infty(M) \to C^\infty(M)$ on a symplectic manifold, defined by $\{f, g\} = \omega(X_f, X_g)$.
2. Verification of bilinearity, antisymmetry, the Leibniz rule, and the Jacobi identity from the properties of $\omega$.
3. The canonical relations for coordinate functions in Darboux coordinates.
4. The Hamiltonian-vector-field correspondence $X_{\{f,g\}} = [X_f, X_g]$.
5. Poisson's theorem on conserved quantities.
6. The Poisson bivector $\Pi = \omega^{-1}$ and the Schouten-Nijenhuis bracket characterisation of the Jacobi identity as $[\Pi, \Pi]_{SN} = 0$.
7. The Marsden-Weinstein reduction theorem.

Items (1)--(3) follow from the definition and the properties of the symplectic form; item (4) requires the Jacobi identity. The exact sequence $0 \to \mathbb{R} \to C^\infty(M) \to \mathfrak{ham}(M) \to 0$ (constants to functions to Hamiltonian vector fields) underlies item (5). Items (6)--(7) are substantial additions that would bring Mathlib to the frontier of Poisson geometry.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Poisson manifolds and the Poisson bivector

A **Poisson manifold** is a smooth manifold $M$ equipped with a Lie bracket $\{\cdot, \cdot\}$ on $C^\infty(M)$ satisfying bilinearity, antisymmetry, the Jacobi identity, and the Leibniz rule. Every symplectic manifold is Poisson, but not every Poisson manifold is symplectic -- the bracket may be degenerate.

The bracket is determined by a bivector field $\Pi \in \Gamma(\wedge^2 TM)$ via $\{f, g\} = \Pi(df, dg)$. In local coordinates, $\Pi = \Pi^{ij}(z)\,\partial_i \wedge \partial_j$ and $\{f, g\} = \Pi^{ij}\,\partial_i f\, \partial_j g$. The Jacobi identity for the bracket is equivalent to the **Schouten-Nijenhuis bracket** condition:

$$
[\Pi, \Pi]_{SN} = 0.
$$

For a symplectic manifold, $\Pi = \omega^{-1}$ (the bivector obtained by inverting the symplectic form as a bundle map $T^*M \to TM$). Closedness $d\omega = 0$ implies $[\Pi, \Pi]_{SN} = 0$, and conversely.

### Symplectic realisation

A **symplectic realisation** of a Poisson manifold $(P, \{\cdot, \cdot\}_P)$ is a symplectic manifold $(M, \omega)$ with a surjective submersion $\pi : M \to P$ such that $\{f \circ \pi, g \circ \pi\}_M = \{f, g\}_P \circ \pi$ for all $f, g \in C^\infty(P)$. The map $\pi$ pulls back the Poisson structure on $P$ to the symplectic Poisson structure on $M$.

Every Poisson manifold admits a symplectic realisation (proved by Karasev 1987, Weinstein 1983, and independently by Karasev and Weinstein). This means that any degenerate Poisson bracket can be realised as the quotient of a genuine symplectic structure. Physically, this is the statement that a constrained or reduced system can always be "lifted" to an unconstrained symplectic system.

### The Lie algebra of Hamiltonian vector fields

Each $f \in C^\infty(M)$ generates a Hamiltonian vector field $X_f$ defined by $X_f(g) = \{f, g\}$, or equivalently by $\iota_{X_f}\omega = df$. The map $f \mapsto X_f$ is a Lie algebra homomorphism:

$$
X_{\{f, g\}} = [X_f, X_g].
$$

The kernel consists of the **Casimir functions** -- functions $f$ with $X_f = 0$, meaning $\{f, g\} = 0$ for all $g$. On a symplectic manifold the only Casimirs are the constants; on a general Poisson manifold there may be non-constant Casimirs (e.g., $|x|^2$ on $\mathfrak{su}(2)^*$ with the Lie-Poisson bracket).

The exact sequence

$$
0 \longrightarrow \mathbb{R} \hookrightarrow C^\infty(M) \xrightarrow{\;f \mapsto X_f\;} \mathfrak{ham}(M) \longrightarrow 0
$$

identifies the Poisson algebra of functions (modulo constants) with the Lie algebra of Hamiltonian vector fields. This is the bridge between the algebraic (function-based) and geometric (vector-field-based) formulations of Hamiltonian mechanics.

### Quantum commutator correspondence and deformation quantisation

The correspondence $\{f, g\} \longleftrightarrow \frac{1}{i\hbar}[\hat{f}, \hat{g}]$ between the classical Poisson bracket and the quantum commutator is the foundation of **canonical quantisation** [12.03.01]. The Jacobi identity for the Poisson bracket maps to the Jacobi identity for the commutator, and the canonical relations $\{q^i, p_j\} = \delta^i_j$ map to $[\hat{q}^i, \hat{p}_j] = i\hbar\delta^i_j$.

This correspondence is not merely an analogy. **Deformation quantisation** (Bayen, Flato, Fronsdal, Lichnerowicz, Sternheimer 1978) makes it precise: a star product $*$ on $C^\infty(M)[[\hbar]]$ is an associative product deforming the pointwise product:

$$
f * g = fg + \frac{i\hbar}{2}\{f, g\} + O(\hbar^2),
$$

where the higher-order terms in $\hbar$ are bidifferential operators determined (up to equivalence) by the Poisson bracket. The associativity of $*$ is equivalent to a sequence of constraints on the higher-order terms, the first of which is the Jacobi identity. Kontsevich's formality theorem (1997) proved that every Poisson manifold admits a canonical star product, establishing deformation quantisation as a universal construction.

The philosophical content: classical mechanics is the $\hbar \to 0$ limit of quantum mechanics, and the Poisson bracket is the first-order deviation of the quantum product from the classical (commutative) product of observables. The Jacobi identity is the condition that this first-order deviation be consistent with associativity.

### Marsden-Weinstein reduction

Let $(M, \omega)$ be a symplectic manifold on which a Lie group $G$ acts by symplectomorphisms, with momentum map $\mathbf{J} : M \to \mathfrak{g}^*$ satisfying $X_{J_\xi} = \xi_M$ for all $\xi \in \mathfrak{g}$ (where $J_\xi = \langle \mathbf{J}, \xi \rangle$ and $\xi_M$ is the infinitesimal generator). The **Marsden-Weinstein reduced space** at a regular value $\mu \in \mathfrak{g}^*$ is:

$$
M_\mu = \mathbf{J}^{-1}(\mu) \,/\, G_\mu,
$$

where $G_\mu$ is the isotropy subgroup at $\mu$. This inherits a symplectic form $\omega_\mu$ satisfying $i_\mu^*\omega = \pi_\mu^*\omega_\mu$ (where $i_\mu : \mathbf{J}^{-1}(\mu) \hookrightarrow M$ and $\pi_\mu : \mathbf{J}^{-1}(\mu) \to M_\mu$).

The Poisson bracket enters at every stage. The momentum map $\mathbf{J}$ is a Poisson map (it preserves brackets): $\{J_\xi, J_\eta\} = J_{[\xi, \eta]}$. The reduced bracket on $C^\infty(M_\mu)$ is defined by $\{f_\mu, g_\mu\}_\mu = \{F, G\}\big|_{\mathbf{J}^{-1}(\mu)}$ where $F, G$ are $G_\mu$-invariant extensions of $f_\mu, g_\mu$. The Jacobi identity for the reduced bracket follows from the Jacobi identity on $M$.

Physical examples: reduction of the two-body problem to the centre-of-mass frame (translation group); reduction of the Kepler problem to motion on a sphere (angular momentum conservation); the passage from the full phase space of a gauge theory to the physical (gauge-invariant) phase space.

### The orbit method and symplectic leaves

The **symplectic leaves** of a Poisson manifold $P$ are the maximal immersed submanifolds on which the induced Poisson structure is non-degenerate (hence symplectic). They are the orbits of the family of Hamiltonian vector fields $\{X_f : f \in C^\infty(P)\}$. The Weinstein splitting theorem states that near any point $p \in P$, there exist local coordinates $(q^1, \ldots, q^k, p_1, \ldots, p_k, y^1, \ldots, y^{m-2k})$ in which the Poisson bracket takes the form:

$$
\{f, g\} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial p_i} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial q^i} + \varphi^{ab}(y)\frac{\partial f}{\partial y^a}\frac{\partial g}{\partial y^b},
$$

where the first term is the symplectic leaf and the second is the transverse Poisson structure (depending only on the Casimir coordinates $y^a$). This generalises Darboux's theorem from symplectic to Poisson geometry.

For the dual $\mathfrak{g}^*$ of a Lie algebra equipped with the **Lie-Poisson bracket** $\{f, g\}(\mu) = \langle \mu, [df_\mu, dg_\mu] \rangle$, the symplectic leaves are the **coadjoint orbits** $\mathcal{O}_\mu = \{Ad^*_g \mu : g \in G\}$. The orbit method (Kirillov 1962, Kostant 1970, Souriau 1970) identifies the irreducible unitary representations of $G$ with the quantisations of the coadjoint orbits -- the Poisson structure on $\mathfrak{g}^*$ is the bridge between classical symmetry and quantum representation theory.

**Synthesis.** The Poisson bracket is the foundational reason that the algebra of observables in classical mechanics carries a Lie algebra structure. The central insight is that the Jacobi identity is equivalent to the closedness of the symplectic form, and the generalisation to Poisson manifolds extends this insight to degenerate structures where the bracket does not come from a symplectic form. This is exactly the algebraic framework that underlies the classification of integrable systems via the Liouville-Arnold theorem [09.06.02], the reduction of symmetric systems via Marsden-Weinstein, and the passage to quantum mechanics via deformation quantisation. Putting these together, the Poisson algebra of observables provides the bridge from symplectic geometry to representation theory, and the canonical quantisation map $\{f, g\} \to (i\hbar)^{-1}[\hat{f}, \hat{g}]$ is the Lie-algebra homomorphism that makes this bridge operational.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Bracket of Casimir with any function).** *On a symplectic manifold, the only functions that Poisson-commute with every other function are the constants.*

*Proof.* If $\{f, g\} = 0$ for all $g$, then in particular $\{f, q^i\} = 0$ and $\{f, p_i\} = 0$ for all Darboux coordinates. But $\{f, q^i\} = -\partial f / \partial p_i$ and $\{f, p_i\} = \partial f / \partial q^i$. So $\partial f / \partial p_i = 0$ and $\partial f / \partial q^i = 0$ for all $i$, hence $df = 0$ and $f$ is constant. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Hamiltonian vector fields form a Lie subalgebra).** *The map $f \mapsto X_f$ is a Lie algebra homomorphism from $(C^\infty(M), \{\cdot, \cdot\})$ to $(\mathfrak{X}(M), [\cdot, \cdot])$.*

*Proof.* For any $h \in C^\infty(M)$: $X_{\{f,g\}}(h) = \{\{f,g\}, h\}$. By the Jacobi identity, $\{\{f,g\}, h\} = -\{\{g,h\}, f\} - \{\{h,f\}, g\} = -X_f(X_g(h)) + X_g(X_f(h)) = [X_f, X_g](h)$. Since $h$ is arbitrary, $X_{\{f,g\}} = [X_f, X_g]$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Poisson map preserves brackets).** *If $\Phi : (M_1, \omega_1) \to (M_2, \omega_2)$ is a symplectomorphism, then $\{f \circ \Phi, g \circ \Phi\}_1 = \{f, g\}_2 \circ \Phi$ for all $f, g \in C^\infty(M_2)$.*

*Proof.* $\{f \circ \Phi, g \circ \Phi\}_1 = \omega_1(X_{f \circ \Phi}, X_{g \circ \Phi})$. The Hamiltonian vector field of $f \circ \Phi$ satisfies $\iota_{X_{f \circ \Phi}}\omega_1 = d(f \circ \Phi) = \Phi^*(df)$. But also $\Phi^*(\iota_{X_f}\omega_2) = \Phi^*(df)$, so $\iota_{(\Phi^{-1})_* X_f}\Phi^*\omega_2 = \Phi^*(df)$. Since $\Phi^*\omega_2 = \omega_1$, we get $X_{f \circ \Phi} = (\Phi^{-1})_* X_f$. Then:

$$
\{f \circ \Phi, g \circ \Phi\}_1 = \omega_1((\Phi^{-1})_* X_f, (\Phi^{-1})_* X_g) = (\Phi^*\omega_2)(X_f, X_g) = \omega_2(X_f, X_g) \circ \Phi = \{f, g\}_2 \circ \Phi.
$$

$\square$

**Proposition 4 (Jacobi identity from closedness of $\omega$).** *On a symplectic manifold $(M, \omega)$, the closedness condition $d\omega = 0$ is equivalent to the Jacobi identity for the Poisson bracket $\{f, g\} = \omega(X_f, X_g)$.*

*Proof (sketch).* The identity $d\omega(X_f, X_g, X_h) = 0$ (from $d\omega = 0$) expands via Cartan's formula to:

$$
0 = X_f[\omega(X_g, X_h)] - X_g[\omega(X_f, X_h)] + X_h[\omega(X_f, X_g)] - \omega([X_f, X_g], X_h) + \omega([X_f, X_h], X_g) - \omega([X_g, X_h], X_f).
$$

Substituting $\omega(X_f, X_g) = \{f, g\}$ and $[X_f, X_g] = X_{\{f,g\}}$ (Proposition 2), and using $X_f[\{g, h\}] = \{\{g, h\}, f\}$, this becomes precisely the cyclic sum in the Jacobi identity. The converse (Jacobi implies closedness) follows by the same algebra in reverse, using the fact that Hamiltonian vector fields span the tangent space at each point of a symplectic manifold. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (Lie-Poisson bracket on $\mathfrak{g}^*$).** *The dual $\mathfrak{g}^*$ of a Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ carries a natural Poisson structure, the Lie-Poisson bracket, defined by $\{f, g\}(\mu) = \langle \mu, [d_\mu f, d_\mu g] \rangle$ where $d_\mu f \in \mathfrak{g}^{**} \cong \mathfrak{g}$ is the derivative of $f$ at $\mu$. The symplectic leaves of this bracket are the coadjoint orbits of $G$.*

*Proof.* Bilinearity and antisymmetry are immediate. The Leibniz rule follows because the bracket is a derivation in each argument (the bracket of $f$ with $gh$ involves the derivatives of $gh$, which split by the product rule). The Jacobi identity follows from the Jacobi identity of $\mathfrak{g}$:

$$
\{f, \{g, h\}\} = \langle \mu, [df, \{g,h\}\text{-derivative}] \rangle = \langle \mu, [df, [dg, dh]] \rangle.
$$

The cyclic sum $\langle \mu, [df, [dg, dh]] + [dg, [dh, df]] + [dh, [df, dg]] \rangle = 0$ by the Jacobi identity of $\mathfrak{g}$. The symplectic leaves are the coadjoint orbits by the orbit method (Kirillov 1962). $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **[09.04.01]** The Legendre transform produces the Hamiltonian $H(q, p)$ that enters the Poisson bracket evolution equation $df/dt = \{f, H\}$. The bracket formalism presupposes the phase-space variables $(q, p)$ established by the Legendre transform.
- **[09.04.02]** Hamilton's equations are the special cases of the bracket evolution equation with $f = q^i$ (giving $\dot{q}^i = \partial H / \partial p_i$) and $f = p_i$ (giving $\dot{p}_i = -\partial H / \partial q^i$).
- **[09.04.04]** Liouville's theorem on phase-space volume preservation follows from the preservation of the Poisson bracket structure under Hamiltonian flow. The bracket formulation makes incompressibility transparent.
- **[09.04.05]** The symplectic form provides the geometric foundation for the Poisson bracket: $\{f, g\} = \omega(X_f, X_g)$. The Jacobi identity is equivalent to $d\omega = 0$.
- **[09.04.08]** Classical spin dynamics on $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ uses the $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ Poisson bracket for angular momentum components as its fundamental evolution equation.
- **[09.06.02]** The Liouville-Arnold theorem requires $n$ conserved quantities in involution ($\{f_i, f_j\} = 0$); the bracket structure is the algebraic input to the integrability analysis.
- **[09.03.01]** Noether's theorem identifies conserved quantities with symmetry generators; the Poisson bracket is the Lie bracket on the algebra of symmetry generators.
- **[12.03.01]** Canonical quantisation replaces $\{f, g\}$ with $(i\hbar)^{-1}[\hat{f}, \hat{g}]$; every result about Poisson algebras has a quantum counterpart.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Simeon-Denis Poisson introduced the bracket that bears his name in his *Traite de mecanique* (1811, second edition) as a computational tool for celestial mechanics. The bracket provided a compact notation for the equations of motion and a systematic way to find new conserved quantities from known ones. Poisson observed that if $f$ and $g$ are constants of motion, then the bracket $\{f, g\}$ is also constant. He did not, however, recognise the full algebraic structure.

Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi proved the identity that bears his name in an 1842 manuscript (published posthumously in his *Vorlesungen uber Dynamik*, 1866). Jacobi recognised the identity as the condition for the bracket to define a Lie algebra structure -- though the term "Lie algebra" did not yet exist. He used the identity to give a systematic treatment of the Poisson theorem and to develop the theory of canonical transformations.

Marius Sophus Lie, in the 1880s, placed the Poisson bracket within the framework of his general theory of continuous transformation groups. Lie showed that the Hamiltonian vector fields form a Lie algebra isomorphic to the Poisson algebra of functions (modulo constants), and that the symplectic structure is the geometric counterpart of the algebraic bracket. Lie's work was largely ignored by physicists until the 1960s.

The connection to quantum mechanics was made by Paul Dirac in 1925. Dirac observed that the classical Poisson bracket and the quantum commutator satisfy the same algebraic identities (antisymmetry, bilinearity, the Jacobi identity), and proposed the quantisation rule $\{f, g\} \to (i\hbar)^{-1}[\hat{f}, \hat{g}]$ as the bridge between classical and quantum mechanics. This observation, made in a single afternoon according to Dirac's own account, is one of the most consequential insights in 20th-century physics. It established the Poisson bracket as the central object in the classical-to-quantum correspondence.

The modern theory of Poisson manifolds was developed independently by Kirillov (1976), Kostant (1970), and Weinstein (1983), who generalised the symplectic framework to allow degenerate brackets. The orbit method (Kirillov 1962) identified the symplectic leaves of the Lie-Poisson bracket on $\mathfrak{g}^*$ with the coadjoint orbits of $G$, connecting the Poisson structure of classical mechanics to the representation theory of Lie groups. Kontsevich's formality theorem (1997) established deformation quantisation for arbitrary Poisson manifolds, proving that every classical Poisson algebra admits a consistent quantum deformation.

The philosophical significance of the Poisson bracket is that it encodes the dynamics of classical mechanics in an *algebraic* rather than *geometric* form. The equations of motion reduce to a single algebraic operation -- the bracket with the Hamiltonian -- and the conservation laws become algebraic conditions on this operation. This algebraic perspective is the starting point for quantisation: the transition from classical to quantum mechanics replaces the commutative Poisson algebra of classical observables by a non-commutative operator algebra, with the commutator playing the role of $i\hbar$ times the bracket. The Jacobi identity is the condition that makes this replacement consistent.
