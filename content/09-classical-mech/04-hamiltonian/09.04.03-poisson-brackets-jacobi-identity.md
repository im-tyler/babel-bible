## Intuition [Beginner]

The Poisson bracket is a mathematical operation that takes two functions on phase space and produces a third. Think of it as a way to measure how much two observables "interfere" with each other.

The most important property: the time evolution of any observable $f(q, p)$ is determined by its Poisson bracket with the Hamiltonian $H$. If the bracket $\{f, H\}$ is zero and $f$ does not depend explicitly on time, then $f$ is conserved.

The bracket has a symmetric partner structure: positions and momenta satisfy $\{q, p\} = 1$ (when properly normalised), but $\{q, q\} = 0$ and $\{p, p\} = 0$. This is the classical shadow of the quantum uncertainty principle: position and momentum are "conjugate" — they do not commute.

A beautiful result called **Poisson's theorem** says that if two quantities are conserved, their Poisson bracket is also conserved. This means conserved quantities form a closed algebra — you can generate new conservation laws from existing ones.

## Visual [Beginner]

![A table showing the canonical Poisson brackets. Rows and columns labelled by q and p. The diagonal entries {q,q} and {p,p} are zero. The off-diagonal entry {q,p} equals 1, and {p,q} equals -1.](/img/09.04.03-poisson-bracket-canonical-placeholder.svg)

The canonical bracket relations $\{q, q\} = 0$, $\{p, p\} = 0$, $\{q, p\} = 1$ are the classical analogue of the Heisenberg commutation relations $[\hat{q}, \hat{q}] = 0$, $[\hat{p}, \hat{p}] = 0$, $[\hat{q}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute the Poisson bracket of the angular momentum components $L_x$ and $L_y$ in three dimensions. The angular momentum is $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p}$, so $L_x = yp_z - zp_y$ and $L_y = zp_x - xp_z$.

The bracket $\{L_x, L_y\}$ involves summing over the three coordinate pairs $(x, p_x)$, $(y, p_y)$, $(z, p_z)$. Each term picks up contributions from the dependence on coordinates and momenta. Computing term by term and using the canonical relations $\{q^i, p_j\} = \delta^i_j$:

$\{L_x, L_y\} = L_z$.

This is the angular momentum algebra. The three components close under the bracket, forming the Lie algebra of SO(3). If $L_x$ and $L_y$ are conserved ($\{L_x, H\} = 0$ and $\{L_y, H\} = 0$), then by Poisson's theorem, $L_z$ is also conserved.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Definition.** The Poisson bracket of two smooth functions $f, g$ on phase space $(q^1, \ldots, q^n, p_1, \ldots, p_n)$ is

$$
\{f, g\} \;:=\; \frac{\partial f}{\partial q^i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial p_i} - \frac{\partial f}{\partial p_i}\frac{\partial g}{\partial q^i}.
$$

**Algebraic properties.** The Poisson bracket satisfies:

1. **Bilinearity**: $\{af + bg, h\} = a\{f, h\} + b\{g, h\}$
2. **Antisymmetry**: $\{f, g\} = -\{g, f\}$
3. **Leibniz rule**: $\{f, gh\} = \{f, g\}h + g\{f, h\}$
4. **Jacobi identity**: $\{f, \{g, h\}\} + \{g, \{h, f\}\} + \{h, \{f, g\}\} = 0$

Properties (1)-(4) make the space of smooth functions on phase space a **Poisson algebra**. Properties (1), (2), and (4) make it a **Lie algebra** (the Leibniz rule is an additional compatibility between the Lie bracket and the multiplication of functions).

**Canonical relations.** The coordinate functions satisfy

$$
\{q^i, q^j\} = 0, \qquad \{p_i, p_j\} = 0, \qquad \{q^i, p_j\} = \delta^i_j.
$$

**Time evolution.** For any observable $f(q, p, t)$:

$$
\frac{df}{dt} = \{f, H\} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial t}.
$$

Hamilton's equations themselves are the special cases $f = q^i$ and $f = p_i$. A function $f$ with $\{f, H\} = 0$ and $\partial f/\partial t = 0$ is a **constant of motion**.

### Poisson's theorem

**Theorem.** *If $f$ and $g$ are constants of motion ($\{f, H\} = \{g, H\} = 0$, $\partial f/\partial t = \partial g/\partial t = 0$), then $\{f, g\}$ is also a constant of motion.*

*Proof.* By the Jacobi identity: $\{H, \{f, g\}\} = -\{f, \{g, H\}\} - \{g, \{H, f\}\} = 0$. $\square$

This means the constants of motion form a Lie subalgebra under the Poisson bracket.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Key result (Jacobi identity for the canonical Poisson bracket).** *The canonical Poisson bracket on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ satisfies the Jacobi identity.*

**Proof.** Write the bracket as $\{f, g\} = J^{ij}(\partial_i f)(\partial_j g)$ where $\partial_i$ denotes the $i$-th partial derivative in the combined $(q, p)$ coordinates and $J^{ij}$ is the constant symplectic matrix with block form $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & I \\ -I & 0 \end{pmatrix}$. Then

$$
\{f, \{g, h\}\} = J^{ij}\partial_i f \cdot \partial_j(J^{kl}\partial_k g \cdot \partial_l h).
$$

Expanding the inner derivative: $\partial_j(\partial_k g \cdot \partial_l h) = \partial_j\partial_k g \cdot \partial_l h + \partial_k g \cdot \partial_j\partial_l h$. So

$$
\{f, \{g, h\}\} = J^{ij}J^{kl}[\partial_i f \cdot \partial_j\partial_k g \cdot \partial_l h + \partial_i f \cdot \partial_k g \cdot \partial_j\partial_l h].
$$

The full Jacobi sum $\{f, \{g, h\}\} + \{g, \{h, f\}\} + \{h, \{f, g\}\}$ has six types of terms. The terms involving second derivatives of a single function pair as:

$$
J^{ij}J^{kl}[\partial_i f \cdot \partial_j\partial_k g \cdot \partial_l h + \partial_i g \cdot \partial_j\partial_k h \cdot \partial_l f + \partial_i h \cdot \partial_j\partial_k f \cdot \partial_l g] + \text{symmetric partner}.
$$

Since $J^{ij}$ is antisymmetric ($J^{ij} = -J^{ji}$), relabelling $i \leftrightarrow j$ and $k \leftrightarrow l$ in alternate terms shows that the sum vanishes identically. The key fact is that $J^{ij}$ is constant (independent of $q, p$), so no derivatives of $J$ appear. $\square$

**Remark.** On a general symplectic manifold, the Jacobi identity for the bracket $\{f, g\} = \omega(X_f, X_g)$ is equivalent to the closedness condition $d\omega = 0$. The canonical bracket on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$ corresponds to the constant symplectic form $dq^i \wedge dp_i$, which is automatically closed.

**Bridge.** The Poisson bracket algebra builds toward the full symplectic-geometric formulation of Hamiltonian mechanics [09.04.05]; the foundational reason is that the bracket is the algebraic shadow of the symplectic form, and this is exactly the structure that encodes the dynamics in a coordinate-free way. The Jacobi identity appears again in the proof of the Liouville-Arnold theorem [09.06.02], where the involutivity condition $\{f_i, f_j\} = 0$ is the algebraic requirement for integrability. Putting these together, the Poisson algebra of observables is the central insight that connects classical mechanics to quantum mechanics via canonical quantisation, and the generalisation to Poisson manifolds (where the bracket need not come from a symplectic form) extends this framework to systems with gauge symmetries and constraints.

Verify the Leibniz rule $\{f, gh\} = \{f, g\}h + g\{f, h\}$ by direct computation from the definition of the Poisson bracket.

Compute $\{q^2, p^2\}$ for a one-dimensional system with coordinate $q$ and momentum $p$.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the angular momentum components in three dimensions satisfy $\{L_x, L_y\} = L_z$, $\{L_y, L_z\} = L_x$, $\{L_z, L_x\} = L_y$. This is the $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ Lie algebra.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$L_x = yp_z - zp_y$, $L_y = zp_x - xp_z$, $L_z = xp_y - yp_x$. Use the canonical relations.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\{L_x, L_y\} = \{yp_z - zp_y, zp_x - xp_z\}$. Using bilinearity and antisymmetry, compute each pair. The nonzero brackets among coordinates and momenta are $\{x, p_x\} = 1$, $\{y, p_y\} = 1$, $\{z, p_z\} = 1$. Term by term: $\{yp_z, zp_x\} + \{yp_z, -xp_z\} + \{-zp_y, zp_x\} + \{-zp_y, -xp_z\}$. Using the Leibniz rule: $\{yp_z, zp_x\} = y\{p_z, z\}p_x = -yp_x$ (since $\{p_z, z\} = -1$). $\{yp_z, -xp_z\} = -x\{yp_z, p_z\} = 0$. $\{-zp_y, zp_x\} = 0$. $\{-zp_y, -xp_z\} = x\{z, p_z\}p_y = xp_y$. Sum: $-yp_x + xp_y = L_z$. The other two relations follow by cyclic permutation.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

For a particle in a central force with Hamiltonian $H = |\mathbf{p}|^2/(2m) + V(r)$, show that $\{L_z, H\} = 0$ using the angular momentum algebra.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$L_z$ commutes with any function of $r = |\mathbf{r}|$ and $|\mathbf{p}|$ because these are rotationally invariant.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$H = (p_x^2 + p_y^2 + p_z^2)/(2m) + V(\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2})$. Using the Leibniz rule and linearity: $\{L_z, p_x^2 + p_y^2 + p_z^2\}/(2m) = [p_x\{L_z, p_x\} + p_y\{L_z, p_y\} + p_z\{L_z, p_z\}]/m$. Since $L_z = xp_y - yp_x$: $\{L_z, p_x\} = \{xp_y, p_x\} = -p_y$, $\{L_z, p_y\} = \{xp_y, p_y\} - \{yp_x, p_y\} = x - 0 = x$... wait, let me redo: $\{L_z, p_x\} = -p_y$, $\{L_z, p_y\} = p_x$, $\{L_z, p_z\} = 0$. So $\{L_z, |\mathbf{p}|^2\} = 2(p_x(-p_y) + p_y p_x) = 0$. For the potential: $\{L_z, V(r)\} = \partial V/\partial r \cdot \{L_z, r\}$. Since $r^2 = x^2+y^2+z^2$: $\{L_z, r^2\} = 2x\{L_z, x\} + 2y\{L_z, y\} = 2x(-y) + 2y(x) = 0$. So $\{L_z, V(r)\} = 0$. Therefore $\{L_z, H\} = 0$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that $\{f, \{g, h\}\} = \{\{f, g\}, h\} + \{g, \{f, h\}\}$ (this is the bracket analogue of the Leibniz rule for the action of $f$ on brackets).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the Jacobi identity to rearrange terms.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Jacobi identity is $\{f, \{g, h\}\} + \{g, \{h, f\}\} + \{h, \{f, g\}\} = 0$. Rearranging the second term: $\{g, \{h, f\}\} = -\{g, \{f, h\}\}$. Rearranging the third: $\{h, \{f, g\}\} = \{\{f, g\}, h\}$ (using antisymmetry: $\{h, A\} = -\{A, h\}$). So: $\{f, \{g, h\}\} - \{g, \{f, h\}\} + \{\{f, g\}, h\} = 0$, giving $\{f, \{g, h\}\} = \{g, \{f, h\}\} - \{\{f, g\}, h\} = \{g, \{f, h\}\} + \{h, \{f, g\}\}$. The cleaner form: $\{f, \{g, h\}\} = \{\{f, g\}, h\} + \{g, \{f, h\}\}$. This shows that the operation $\mathrm{ad}_f(g) := \{f, g\}$ is a derivation of the Poisson algebra.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove Poisson's theorem: if $\{f, H\} = 0$ and $\{g, H\} = 0$ with $f$ and $g$ time-independent, then $\{\{f, g\}, H\} = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the Jacobi identity with the three functions $f$, $g$, $H$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Jacobi identity: $\{f, \{g, H\}\} + \{g, \{H, f\}\} + \{H, \{f, g\}\} = 0$. The first term is $\{f, 0\} = 0$. The second is $\{g, 0\} = 0$. So $\{H, \{f, g\}\} = 0$, i.e., $\{\{f, g\}, H\} = 0$ by antisymmetry. Therefore $\{f, g\}$ is also a constant of motion. This theorem is the Poisson-algebra analogue of the statement that the commutator of two symmetry generators is also a symmetry generator.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

The Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector for the Kepler problem is $\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{p}\times\mathbf{L} - mk\hat{\mathbf{r}}$. Compute $\{A_x, A_y\}$ and show that on the energy surface $H = E < 0$, the algebra closes to $\mathfrak{so}(4)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The bracket $\{A_x, A_y\}$ should be proportional to $L_z$ times a function of $H$. On the bound-state energy surface, this gives the $\mathfrak{so}(4)$ algebra.

</details>

</details>

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that a canonical transformation (symplectomorphism) preserves the Poisson bracket: if $\Phi$ is a symplectomorphism, then $\{f\circ\Phi, g\circ\Phi\} = \{f, g\}\circ\Phi$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the coordinate-free definition $\{f, g\} = \omega(X_f, X_g)$ and the fact that $\Phi^*\omega = \omega$.

</details>

The Hamiltonian vector field of $f\circ\Phi$ is $\Phi^*X_f$ (the pullback of the vector field). In coordinates: $(X_{f\circ\Phi}) = (D\Phi)^{-1}X_f\circ\Phi$. ## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The Poisson bracket is a central missing piece in Mathlib's Hamiltonian mechanics formalisation. The ingredients needed are: (1) the Poisson bracket as a bilinear map on smooth functions on a symplectic manifold; (2) verification of bilinearity, antisymmetry, Leibniz rule, and Jacobi identity; (3) the canonical relations for coordinate functions; (4) Poisson's theorem; and (5) the isomorphism between the Lie algebra of Hamiltonian vector fields and the Poisson algebra of smooth functions modulo constants. Items (1)-(3) follow from the definition and the properties of the symplectic form; item (5) requires the exactness of the sequence $0 \to \mathbb{R} \to C^\infty(M) \to \mathfrak{ham}(M) \to 0$.

## Advanced results: Poisson manifolds and the Lie-algebraic structure [Master]

### Poisson manifolds

A **Poisson manifold** is a smooth manifold $M$ equipped with a Poisson bracket $\{\cdot, \cdot\}$ on $C^\infty(M)$ satisfying bilinearity, antisymmetry, the Leibniz rule, and the Jacobi identity. Every symplectic manifold is a Poisson manifold, but not every Poisson manifold is symplectic (the bracket may be degenerate).

The Poisson bracket is determined by a bivector field $\Pi \in \Gamma(\wedge^2 TM)$ (the Poisson bivector) via $\{f, g\} = \Pi(df, dg)$. The Jacobi identity for the bracket is equivalent to $[\Pi, \Pi]_{SN} = 0$, where $[\cdot, \cdot]_{SN}$ is the Schouten-Nijenhuis bracket of multivector fields.

**Proposition.** *On a symplectic manifold, the Poisson bivector is $\Pi = \omega^{-1}$ (the inverse of the symplectic form, viewed as a bivector). The Jacobi identity follows from $d\omega = 0$.*

### The Lie algebra of Hamiltonian vector fields

Each smooth function $f$ on a Poisson manifold generates a **Hamiltonian vector field** $X_f$ defined by $X_f(g) = \{f, g\}$. The map $f \mapsto X_f$ is a Lie algebra homomorphism:

$$
X_{\{f, g\}} = [X_f, X_g].
$$

The kernel consists of the Casimir functions ($f$ with $X_f = 0$, i.e., $\{f, g\} = 0$ for all $g$). On a symplectic manifold the only Casimirs are the constants; on a general Poisson manifold there may be non-constant Casimirs.

### The Gelfand-Kirillov dimension and the orbit structure

The symplectic leaves of a Poisson manifold are the maximal immersed submanifolds on which the induced Poisson structure is non-degenerate (hence symplectic). The Weinstein splitting theorem states that near any point, a Poisson manifold splits as a product of a symplectic leaf and a transverse Poisson structure. This generalises Darboux's theorem from symplectic to Poisson geometry.

**Synthesis.** The Poisson bracket is the foundational reason that the algebra of observables in classical mechanics carries a Lie algebra structure; the central insight is that the Jacobi identity is equivalent to the closedness of the symplectic form, and the generalisation to Poisson manifolds extends this insight to degenerate structures. This is exactly the algebraic framework that underlies the classification of integrable systems via the Liouville-Arnold theorem. Putting these together, the Poisson algebra of observables provides the bridge from symplectic geometry to representation theory, and the canonical quantisation map $\{f, g\} \to (i\hbar)^{-1}[\hat{f}, \hat{g}]$ is the Lie-algebra homomorphism that makes this bridge operational.

## Connections [Master]

- **[09.04.02]** Hamilton's equations are the special case of the bracket evolution equation with $f = q^i$ or $f = p_i$.
- **[09.04.05]** The symplectic form provides the geometric foundation for the Poisson bracket: $\{f, g\} = \omega(X_f, X_g)$.
- **[09.06.02]** The Liouville-Arnold theorem requires $n$ conserved quantities in involution ($\{f_i, f_j\} = 0$); the bracket structure is the algebraic input.
- **[12.03.01]** Canonical quantisation replaces $\{f, g\}$ with $(i\hbar)^{-1}[\hat{f}, \hat{g}]$; every result about Poisson algebras has a quantum counterpart.
- **[09.03.01]** Noether's theorem identifies conserved quantities with symmetry generators; the Poisson bracket is the Lie bracket on the algebra of symmetry generators.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Poisson introduced the bracket in his *Trait\'e de m\'ecanique* (1811) as a computational tool for celestial mechanics. The bracket provided a compact notation for the equations of motion and a systematic way to find new conserved quantities from known ones. Jacobi proved the identity that bears his name in 1841, recognising it as the condition for the bracket to define a Lie algebra structure.

The connection to Lie algebra theory was made explicit by Lie in the 1880s, who showed that the Hamiltonian vector fields form a Lie algebra isomorphic to the Poisson algebra of functions. This perspective was revived in the 1960s by Kirillov, Kostant, and Souriau, who developed the orbit method for representation theory using the symplectic leaves of Poisson manifolds.

The philosophical significance of the Poisson bracket is that it encodes the dynamics in an algebraic rather than geometric form. The equations of motion are replaced by a single algebraic operation — the bracket with the Hamiltonian — and the conservation laws become algebraic conditions on this operation. This algebraic perspective is the starting point for quantisation: the transition from classical to quantum mechanics replaces the Poisson algebra of observables by a non-commutative operator algebra, with the commutator playing the role of the bracket.
