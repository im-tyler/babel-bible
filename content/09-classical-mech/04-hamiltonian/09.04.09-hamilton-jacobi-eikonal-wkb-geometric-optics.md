## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine you are standing at one side of a landscape and want to walk to the other side by the fastest route. The travel time depends on your path — uphill is slower, downhill is faster. You could solve Newton's equations for your body moving under gravity and friction, tracing out the full trajectory. But there is a shortcut: compute the "shortest-time function" $T(\mathbf{x})$, which records the minimum travel time from your starting point to any position $\mathbf{x}$. This function encodes all the optimal trajectories implicitly — the gradient of $T$ at any point tells you which direction to walk.

The **Hamilton-Jacobi equation** is this idea applied to mechanics. Instead of tracking a particle's position and momentum along a trajectory, you track a function $S(q, t)$ called **Hamilton's principal function** (the action evaluated along the optimal path from a starting point to $(q, t)$). This function satisfies a partial differential equation — the Hamilton-Jacobi equation — and once you have a complete solution $S$, you can read off all possible trajectories without solving any differential equations at all. Every initial condition maps to a different choice of integration constants in $S$.

The connection to optics is the deep part. In the early 19th century, Hamilton studied optics first and then realised that the same mathematics — rays of light following the gradient of a "wavefront" function — applies to mechanical trajectories following the gradient of the action. The **eikonal equation** of geometric optics (which governs how light rays bend through a medium with varying refractive index) is literally the same equation as the Hamilton-Jacobi equation, with the action playing the role of the optical path length.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture wavefronts of light spreading out from a point source through a medium. The wavefronts are surfaces of constant phase. Light rays are always perpendicular to the wavefronts — they point in the direction of steepest phase increase. Now replace "light" with "particle" and "phase" with "action." The surfaces of constant $S$ are like wavefronts, and particle trajectories are perpendicular to them.

![Wavefronts (level sets of S) and trajectories (perpendicular to wavefronts) for a free particle. The wavefronts are planes of constant x - p*t/m, and the trajectories are straight lines perpendicular to them. In a potential, wavefronts curve and trajectories bend accordingly.](/img/09.04.09-hj-wavefronts-placeholder.svg)

When the potential is zero, the wavefronts are flat and the trajectories are straight lines. When the potential varies, the wavefronts curve and the trajectories bend — exactly like light bending through a graded-index lens.

## Worked example [Beginner]

For a free particle in one dimension with Hamiltonian $H = p^2/(2m)$, the Hamilton-Jacobi equation says: "the rate of change of $S$ with respect to time equals minus the Hamiltonian evaluated with $p$ replaced by the slope of $S$ in $q$." In symbols:

$$\frac{1}{2m}\left(\frac{dS}{dq}\right)^2 + \frac{dS}{dt} = 0.$$

Try a solution of the form $S = W(q) - Et$ where $E$ is a constant (the energy). The time rate of $S$ is $-E$ and the slope in $q$ is $dW/dq$. Substituting:

$$\frac{1}{2m}\left(\frac{dW}{dq}\right)^2 = E, \quad \text{so} \quad \frac{dW}{dq} = \pm\sqrt{2mE}.$$

Integrating: $W = \pm\sqrt{2mE}\,q + \text{const}$, giving $S = \pm\sqrt{2mE}\,q - Et + \alpha$. The momentum is $p = dS/dq = \pm\sqrt{2mE}$, and the position follows from $\beta = -dS/dE = t \mp q/\sqrt{2E/m}$, giving $q(t) = \pm\sqrt{2E/m}\,t + q_0$. The free-particle trajectory is recovered without ever solving a differential equation — just by differentiating the action function.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $H(q, p, t)$ be a Hamiltonian on $T^*Q \times \mathbb{R}$. **Hamilton's principal function** $S(q, \alpha, t)$ is the value of the action integral

$$S(q, \alpha, t) = \int_{t_0}^{t} L(q(t'), \dot{q}(t'), t')\,dt'$$

evaluated along the unique classical trajectory from initial condition parametrised by $\alpha = (\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n)$ at time $t_0$ to the final configuration $q$ at time $t$.

**The Hamilton-Jacobi equation.** $S$ satisfies the first-order nonlinear PDE

$$\boxed{H\!\left(q^1, \ldots, q^n, \frac{\partial S}{\partial q^1}, \ldots, \frac{\partial S}{\partial q^n}, t\right) + \frac{\partial S}{\partial t} = 0.}$$

A **complete solution** (or **complete integral**) of the Hamilton-Jacobi equation is a solution $S(q^1, \ldots, q^n, \alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n, t)$ depending on $n$ independent non-additive constants $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_n$ such that $\det(\partial^2 S / \partial q^i \partial\alpha_j) \neq 0$.

**Hamilton-Jacobi theorem.** If $S(q, \alpha, t)$ is a complete solution of the Hamilton-Jacobi equation, then the $2n$ equations

$$p_i = \frac{\partial S}{\partial q^i}, \qquad \beta^i = \frac{\partial S}{\partial \alpha_i},$$

where $\beta = (\beta^1, \ldots, \beta^n)$ are $n$ new constants, implicitly define the general solution $(q(t), p(t))$ of Hamilton's equations parametrised by the $2n$ constants $(\alpha, \beta)$.

**Separation of variables.** If the Hamiltonian permits a complete solution of the form $S = \sum_i S_i(q^i, \alpha, t)$ where each $S_i$ depends on only one coordinate, the Hamilton-Jacobi equation separates into $n$ independent ODEs. This occurs when the Hamiltonian has $n$ functionally independent commuting first integrals — the condition for Liouville integrability [09.06.02].

**The eikonal equation.** For time-independent $H$, separate $S = W(q) - Et$ where $W$ is Hamilton's characteristic function. The time-independent Hamilton-Jacobi equation is

$$H\!\left(q, \frac{\partial W}{\partial q}\right) = E.$$

This is the **eikonal equation**. In geometric optics, $W$ is the optical path length (eikonal), $E$ is the frequency, and the light rays are the gradient lines of $W$. In mechanics, the trajectories are the gradient lines of $W$ in configuration space.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (The Hamilton-Jacobi equation from the action).**

Let $(q(t), p(t))$ be a solution of Hamilton's equations with initial conditions $(q_0, p_0)$ at time $t_0$. Define the action as a function of the endpoint:

$$S(q, t) = \int_{t_0}^{t} L(q, \dot{q}, t')\,dt'.$$

Vary the endpoint $(q, t)$ while keeping the initial point fixed. The variation gives (using the fundamental lemma of the calculus of variations and the fact that the trajectory satisfies the Euler-Lagrange equations):

$$\delta S = p_i\,\delta q^i + \left.L\right|_{t}^{t}\delta t - H\,\delta t = p_i\,\delta q^i - H\,\delta t.$$

(The boundary term from varying the endpoint time cancels the explicit $L\,\delta t$ term, leaving $-H\,\delta t$.) Therefore

$$\frac{\partial S}{\partial q^i} = p_i, \qquad \frac{\partial S}{\partial t} = -H(q, p, t) = -H\!\left(q, \frac{\partial S}{\partial q}, t\right).$$

Rearranging:

$$H\!\left(q, \frac{\partial S}{\partial q}, t\right) + \frac{\partial S}{\partial t} = 0.$$

This is the Hamilton-Jacobi equation. The momentum is recovered as $p = \nabla_q S$.

**The WKB ansatz.** Write the quantum-mechanical wave function as $\psi(q, t) = A(q, t)\exp(iS(q,t)/\hbar)$. Substituting into the time-dependent Schrodinger equation $i\hbar\,\partial_t\psi = \hat{H}\psi$ and taking the limit $\hbar \to 0$, the leading-order equation for the phase $S$ is exactly the Hamilton-Jacobi equation. The amplitude $A$ satisfies a transport equation that expresses probability conservation along the classical trajectories. This is the **WKB approximation** (Wentzel, Kramers, Brillouin, 1926), and the Hamilton-Jacobi equation is the mathematical content of the statement "quantum mechanics reduces to classical mechanics in the limit $\hbar \to 0$."

**Bridge.** The Hamilton-Jacobi equation builds toward the Schrodinger equation [12.02.01] via the WKB correspondence — the phase of the quantum wave function satisfies the classical Hamilton-Jacobi equation at leading order, and the corrections are organised in powers of $\hbar$. The foundational reason this works is that the canonical transformation generated by $S$ is the classical shadow of the unitary time-evolution operator $U(t) = e^{-i\hat{H}t/\hbar}$: the classical action is the logarithm of the quantum propagator. This appears again in the eikonal equation of geometric optics [10.08.04], where the short-wavelength limit of the wave equation produces the same Hamilton-Jacobi structure, and the central insight is that Hamilton's mechanics was originally modelled on geometric optics — the analogy is not a post-hoc observation but the historical route by which Hamilton discovered his equations.

For the harmonic oscillator $H = p^2/(2m) + \tfrac{1}{2}kq^2$, separate the Hamilton-Jacobi equation $S = W(q) - Et$ and solve for $W(q)$.

The time-independent HJ equation is $\tfrac{1}{2m}(dW/dq)^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}kq^2 = E$.

$\frac{dW}{dq} = \pm\sqrt{2mE - mkq^2}$. Integrating: $W = \pm\int\sqrt{2mE - mkq^2}\,dq = \pm\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{mk}\left(q\sqrt{\frac{2E}{k} - q^2} + \frac{2E}{k}\arcsin\!\left(q\sqrt{\frac{k}{2E}}\right)\right) + \text{const}$. The momentum is $p = dW/dq = \pm\sqrt{2mE - mkq^2}$, and the trajectory follows from $\beta = -\partial S/\partial E$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

For a particle in a uniform gravitational field $V = mgq$ (one dimension), write the Hamilton-Jacobi equation and verify that $S(q, t) = \pm\frac{2}{3g}\sqrt{2m}\bigl(E - mgq\bigr)^{3/2} - Et$ is a solution (up to an additive constant).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\partial S/\partial q$ and $\partial S/\partial t$ and check that they satisfy $p^2/(2m) + mgq + \partial S/\partial t = 0$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\partial S/\partial q = \mp\sqrt{2m(E - mgq)} = p$. Then $p^2/(2m) = E - mgq$, so $p^2/(2m) + mgq = E$. And $\partial S/\partial t = -E$. Hence $p^2/(2m) + mgq + \partial S/\partial t = E - E = 0$. The Hamilton-Jacobi equation is satisfied.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

For the Kepler problem in plane polar coordinates, $H = \tfrac{1}{2m}(p_r^2 + p_\varphi^2/r^2) - k/r$, write the Hamilton-Jacobi equation, separate variables, and identify the two separated equations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Try $S = R(r) + \Phi(\varphi) - Et$. The $H$ equation separates because $\varphi$ is cyclic.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The HJ equation is $\frac{1}{2m}\bigl((\partial S/\partial r)^2 + (\partial S/\partial\varphi)^2/r^2\bigr) - k/r + \partial S/\partial t = 0$. Write $S = R(r) + \Phi(\varphi) - Et$. Then $\partial S/\partial\varphi = d\Phi/d\varphi$ must be constant since $\varphi$ is cyclic: $d\Phi/d\varphi = \ell$ (angular momentum). The equation becomes $\frac{1}{2m}\bigl((dR/dr)^2 + \ell^2/r^2\bigr) - k/r = E$. The separated equations are: $d\Phi/d\varphi = \ell$ and $\frac{dR}{dr} = \pm\sqrt{2mE + 2mk/r - \ell^2/r^2}$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the characteristics of the Hamilton-Jacobi equation (the curves along which initial data propagate) are exactly Hamilton's equations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The method of characteristics for a first-order PDE $F(q, p, S, \nabla S) = 0$ gives characteristic ODEs $\dot{q}^i = \partial F/\partial p_i$, $\dot{p}_i = -\partial F/\partial q^i$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Hamilton-Jacobi equation is $F(q, p, S, t) = H(q, p, t) + \partial S/\partial t = 0$ with $p_i = \partial S/\partial q^i$. The characteristic equations are $\dot{q}^i = \partial H/\partial p_i$, $\dot{p}_i = -\partial H/\partial q^i$, $\dot{S} = p_i\dot{q}^i - H = L$. The first two are exactly Hamilton's equations. The third is the statement that $S$ changes at rate $L$ along the trajectory — consistent with $S$ being the action integral. So the characteristic curves of the HJ equation are the classical trajectories.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

A wave function is written as $\psi = A\exp(iS/\hbar)$. Substitute into the one-dimensional Schrodinger equation $i\hbar\,\partial_t\psi = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\partial_x^2\psi + V(x)\psi$ and extract the leading-order equation as $\hbar \to 0$. Identify the result.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\partial_t\psi$ and $\partial_x^2\psi$ keeping terms of order $\hbar^0$ and $\hbar^1$. At leading order, the $\hbar^0$ terms give the equation for $S$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\partial_t\psi = (i\dot{S}/\hbar)(\psi)$ and $\partial_x^2\psi = \bigl(-S_x^2/\hbar^2 + iS_{xx}/\hbar\bigr)\psi$. Substituting: $i\hbar(i\dot{S}/\hbar)\psi = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}(-S_x^2/\hbar^2 + iS_{xx}/\hbar)\psi + V\psi$. The $O(\hbar^1)$ terms give a transport equation for $A$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Explain the **Maslov index**: why does the WKB approximation fail at classical turning points (where $p = 0$), and how does the Maslov correction restore uniform validity?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

At a turning point, $p = dW/dq = 0$, so the map $(q, p) \to (q, \alpha)$ via $\alpha = \partial S/\partial q$ is singular. The WKB wave function $\psi \sim 1/\sqrt{p}$ diverges.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The WKB wave function is $\psi \sim A/\sqrt{p}\,e^{iW/\hbar}$. At a classical turning point $q_t$ where $E = V(q_t)$, $p(q_t) = 0$ and the amplitude diverges — the semiclassical approximation breaks because the wavelength $\lambda = h/p$ diverges and the "slowly varying envelope" assumption fails. The fix (Maslov, 1965; also Langer 1937) is to switch to momentum representation near the turning point, where $q(p)$ is a good coordinate and the WKB approximation is regular. The matching of the two WKB regions produces a phase shift of $\pi/4$ per turning point, and the total phase accumulated over a complete libration is $\oint p\,dq - \pi\hbar$. The $-\pi\hbar$ is the **Maslov index** contribution. For a bound state with quantum number $n$, the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantisation condition becomes $\oint p\,dq = (n + 1/2)h$, where the extra $1/2$ is the Maslov correction for the two turning points of each libration.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that for any canonical transformation generated by $F_2(q, P, t)$, the new Hamiltonian is $K = H + \partial F_2/\partial t$. Then show that the Hamilton-Jacobi equation is the condition that $K = 0$, i.e., the transformation makes all new momenta constant.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The generating function relations are $p = \partial F_2/\partial q$, $Q = \partial F_2/\partial P$, $K = H + \partial F_2/\partial t$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A type-2 generating function $F_2(q, P, t)$ produces the canonical transformation $p_i = \partial F_2/\partial q^i$, $Q^i = \partial F_2/\partial P_i$, $K(Q, P, t) = H(q, p, t) + \partial F_2/\partial t$. In terms of the old variables, $K = H(q, \partial F_2/\partial q, t) + \partial F_2/\partial t$. The Hamilton-Jacobi strategy is to find $F_2$ such that $K = 0$. This gives $H(q, \partial F_2/\partial q, t) + \partial F_2/\partial t = 0$, which is exactly the Hamilton-Jacobi equation with $F_2 = S$. When $K = 0$, the new Hamilton's equations are $\dot{P}_i = 0$ and $\dot{Q}^i = 0$: all new variables are constants, and the system is solved by quadrature. The old variables are recovered via $p = \partial S/\partial q$ and $\beta = -\partial S/\partial\alpha$.
</details>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

Mathlib does not contain the Hamilton-Jacobi equation or the theory of generating functions for canonical transformations in a formalised form. The theory of first-order PDEs and their characteristics is absent. The closest infrastructure is the symplectic linear algebra and smooth manifold theory that underlies the Hamiltonian formalism, and the ODE existence theory that guarantees the characteristic curves exist. Formalising the Hamilton-Jacobi theorem — that a complete solution of the HJ equation generates the general solution of Hamilton's equations — would require developing the method of characteristics for first-order PDEs, the theory of generating functions, and the inverse function theorem in the setting of canonical coordinates. This is a substantial undertaking; `lean_status: none` reflects the gap.

## Advanced results [Master]

**The geometric optics limit.** The eikonal equation $|\nabla W|^2 = 2m(E - V(q))$ is the short-wavelength limit of the Helmholtz equation $(\nabla^2 + k^2 n^2)\psi = 0$ where $k = \sqrt{2mE}/\hbar$ and $n^2(q) = 1 - V(q)/E$. The ansatz $\psi = A e^{ikW}$ substituted into Helmholtz gives $|\nabla W|^2 = n^2 + O(1/k)$; at leading order this is the eikonal equation. The $O(1/k)$ corrections are the diffraction effects that geometric optics misses. The identical structure appears in the WKB limit of the Schrodinger equation with $k \sim 1/\hbar$.

**Fermat's principle and Maupertuis' principle.** Fermat's principle (light travels along paths of stationary optical path length $\int n\,ds$) is the variational principle corresponding to the eikonal equation. Maupertuis' principle (a mechanical trajectory at fixed energy $E$ makes the abbreviated action $\int p\,dq$ stationary) is the variational principle corresponding to the time-independent Hamilton-Jacobi equation. The two principles are the same equation with different names: $\int n\,ds = \int\sqrt{2m(E-V)}\,|dq| = \int p\,dq$.

**The Maslov index and the metaplectic correction.** The WKB approximation breaks at caustics (focal points where neighbouring trajectories intersect and the amplitude $1/\sqrt{|\partial^2 S/\partial q\partial\alpha|}$ diverges). The **Maslov canonical operator** (Maslov 1965, Hörmander 1971) repairs this by passing through the caustic in momentum representation, where the Lagrangian submanifold is transverse to the $p$-fibres. The phase shift accumulated per caustic is $\pm\pi/2$ — the **Maslov index** — and the corrected WKB wave function is $\psi(q) = \sum_{\text{branches}} \frac{A_j(q)}{\sqrt{|\det\partial^2 S_j/\partial q\partial\alpha|}}\exp\!\bigl(iS_j(q)/\hbar - i\pi\nu_j/4\bigr)$ where $\nu_j$ is the Maslov index of the $j$-th branch. This is the semiclassical trace formula (Gutzwiller 1971) in its simplest form.

**Synthesis.** The Hamilton-Jacobi equation is the bridge between classical mechanics and wave mechanics — the central insight is that particle trajectories are the characteristics (gradient lines) of a scalar function $S$ satisfying a first-order PDE, and that the Schrodinger equation is the second-order PDE whose short-wavelength limit recovers this first-order PDE. The analogy with optics is not decorative but structural: Hamilton's original formulation of mechanics was explicitly modelled on geometric optics, and the discovery that the Schrodinger equation stands to the Hamilton-Jacobi equation as wave optics stands to geometric optics was the founding insight of quantum mechanics. The Maslov index, the WKB expansion, and the Gutzwiller trace formula all build toward this correspondence from the classical side, while the path integral [12.10.01] builds toward it from the quantum side. Putting these together, the Hamilton-Jacobi equation is the load-bearing structure of the classical-quantum correspondence, and its separation of variables is the bridge from classical integrability [09.06.02] to the exact solvability of quantum systems with the same symmetry group.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (The Hamilton-Jacobi theorem).** Let $S(q, \alpha, t)$ be a complete solution of the Hamilton-Jacobi equation $H(q, \nabla_q S, t) + \partial_t S = 0$, with $\det(\partial^2 S/\partial q^i \partial\alpha_j) \neq 0$. Define $p_i = \partial S/\partial q^i$ and $\beta^i = \partial S/\partial\alpha_i$. Then $(q(t), p(t))$ defined implicitly by $\beta^i = \partial S/\partial\alpha_i$ and $p_i = \partial S/\partial q^i$ satisfies Hamilton's equations for $H$.

*Proof.* The implicit function theorem guarantees that $\beta^i = \partial S/\partial\alpha_i(q, \alpha, t)$ can be solved for $\alpha = \alpha(q, \beta, t)$ (hence also for $q = q(\alpha, \beta, t)$) in a neighbourhood of any point where $\det(\partial^2 S/\partial q\partial\alpha) \neq 0$.

Since $\beta$ and $\alpha$ are constants by construction, differentiate $\beta^i = \partial S/\partial\alpha_i$ with respect to $t$ along the trajectory:

$$0 = \frac{d}{dt}\frac{\partial S}{\partial\alpha_i} = \frac{\partial^2 S}{\partial\alpha_i\partial q^j}\dot{q}^j + \frac{\partial^2 S}{\partial\alpha_i\partial t}.$$

Now differentiate the HJ equation $H(q, \nabla_q S, t) + \partial_t S = 0$ with respect to $\alpha_i$:

$$\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_j}\frac{\partial^2 S}{\partial q^j \partial\alpha_i} + \frac{\partial^2 S}{\partial t\,\partial\alpha_i} = 0.$$

Comparing the two expressions and using the non-degeneracy of $\partial^2 S/\partial q\partial\alpha$, conclude $\dot{q}^j = \partial H/\partial p_j$ — the first Hamilton equation.

For the second equation, differentiate $p_i = \partial S/\partial q^i$ in time:

$$\dot{p}_i = \frac{\partial^2 S}{\partial q^i\partial q^j}\dot{q}^j + \frac{\partial^2 S}{\partial q^i\partial t}.$$

Differentiate the HJ equation with respect to $q^i$:

$$\frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i} + \frac{\partial H}{\partial p_j}\frac{\partial^2 S}{\partial q^j\partial q^i} + \frac{\partial^2 S}{\partial t\,\partial q^i} = 0.$$

Substituting $\dot{q}^j = \partial H/\partial p_j$ and $p_i = \partial S/\partial q^i$:

$$\dot{p}_i = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q^i}.$$

This is the second Hamilton equation. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Canonical transformations [09.05.01]** are generated by $F_2(q, P, t) = S(q, \alpha, t)$ with $P = \alpha$; the Hamilton-Jacobi equation is the condition that the new Hamiltonian $K = 0$.

- **Action-angle variables [09.06.01]** are obtained by separation of variables in the Hamilton-Jacobi equation for integrable systems; the action variables $J_i$ are the $\alpha_i$ constants that appear in the separated solution.

- **The Schrodinger equation [12.02.01]** is the second-order PDE whose $\hbar \to 0$ limit is the Hamilton-Jacobi equation; the WKB construction makes this precise.

- **Geometric optics [10.08.04]** derives the eikonal equation from the wave equation in the short-wavelength limit; this is the electromagnetic analogue of the WKB approximation.

- **Path integrals [12.10.01]** express the quantum propagator as a sum over classical paths weighted by $\exp(iS/\hbar)$; in the stationary-phase approximation, the dominant contribution comes from the classical trajectory that satisfies the HJ equation.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Hamilton discovered what is now called the Hamilton-Jacobi equation in 1827 while studying geometric optics, not mechanics. His *Theory of Systems of Rays* (1827) introduced the eikonal equation for light and the associated ray equations. He recognised in 1833-1835 that the same mathematics applies to particle mechanics — the action plays the role of the optical path length, and particle trajectories are the "rays" of the mechanics "wave." This optical-mechanical analogy lay dormant until Schrodinger seized upon it in 1926 as the route from classical to quantum mechanics: if the Hamilton-Jacobi equation is the eikonal equation of some wave theory, then the wave equation itself must be the quantum-mechanical wave equation.

Jacobi developed the separation-of-variables technique in his *Vorlesungen uber Dynamik* (lectures 1842-43, published 1866), showing that the Hamilton-Jacobi equation is separable precisely when the Hamiltonian system is integrable in the Liouville sense. The Hamilton-Jacobi theorem (that a complete solution generates the general solution of Hamilton's equations) is proved in every mechanics textbook but is historically due to Jacobi rather than Hamilton.

The WKB method was developed independently by Wentzel, Kramers, and Brillouin in 1926 to compute approximate quantum-mechanical wave functions. The connection to the Hamilton-Jacobi equation was noted immediately — the leading-order WKB phase satisfies the HJ equation. The Maslov index was introduced by Victor Maslov in his 1965 monograph *Theorie des perturbations et methodes asymptotiques*, with the rigorous framework developed by Hormander (1971) in the context of Fourier integral operators.
