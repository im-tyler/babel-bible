## Intuition [Beginner]

A grandfather clock's pendulum slowly loses energy to friction. As it loses energy, the amplitude decreases and the frequency increases slightly. But the ratio of energy to frequency -- a quantity called the "action" -- stays almost constant. Even as the pendulum's energy changes significantly over many swings, the action barely changes. This is an **adiabatic invariant**: a quantity that is conserved when the system's parameters change slowly compared to the oscillation period.

More generally, consider a mechanical system whose Hamiltonian depends on a parameter that changes slowly in time. A pendulum whose length is slowly shortened. A charged particle spiralling in a magnetic field that slowly increases. A planet in an orbit that slowly shrinks due to tidal friction. In all these cases, the action variable $J = \oint p\,dq / (2\pi)$ (the area enclosed by the orbit in phase space, divided by $2\pi$) is approximately conserved. The approximation is excellent: the error is exponentially small in the ratio of the oscillation frequency to the rate of parameter change.

The mathematical framework for studying such slowly-perturbed systems is **Hamiltonian perturbation theory**. The unperturbed system is integrable (soluble by action-angle variables [09.06.01]). The perturbation breaks integrability, but if it is small, the system is **nearly integrable**: most of phase space is still filled with invariant tori that are only slightly deformed from their unperturbed shapes. The action variables on these tori are approximately conserved, and the deviation from exact conservation can be computed order by order in the perturbation strength.

The central idea is the distinction between **slow variables** and **fast variables**. In a near-integrable system, the angles $\theta$ change rapidly (at a rate set by the unperturbed frequencies $\omega$), while the actions $J$ drift slowly (at a rate proportional to the small perturbation strength $\epsilon$). Over one fast oscillation, the slow variables barely change. Over many fast oscillations, the slow variables accumulate a net drift. The **averaging principle** says that this slow drift is well captured by replacing the fast oscillatory forcing with its average over one period. The averaged system is simpler (the fast dependence is gone) but retains the correct slow-time dynamics.

This separation of timescales is what makes perturbation theory work. The fast oscillations cancel out over long times (their net effect is small), while the slow secular changes accumulate. The averaging method extracts the secular drift while discarding the rapid oscillations that average to zero.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a phase-space portrait of a harmonic oscillator: nested ellipses (level sets of $H$). Now slowly change the spring constant $k$. The ellipses deform and shift, but the enclosed area (the action $J$) stays nearly constant. As $k$ increases, the ellipses shrink in the $q$-direction but expand in the $p$-direction, preserving area. This is the phase-space manifestation of the adiabatic theorem.

![Phase-space of a harmonic oscillator with slowly increasing spring constant. The orbit starts as a large ellipse and shrinks to a smaller one. The enclosed area (shaded) remains approximately constant — this is the adiabatic invariance of the action.](/img/09.04.06-adiabatic-invariant-action-placeholder.svg)

For a faster change in $k$, the area is less well conserved: the orbit does not have time to adjust smoothly, and the phase-space area fluctuates. The key parameter is the ratio $\tau/T$, where $\tau$ is the timescale of the parameter change and $T$ is the oscillation period. When $\tau/T \gg 1$ (many oscillations during one parameter change), the action is an excellent invariant.

Now picture the phase space of a near-integrable system with two degrees of freedom. The unperturbed system has nested invariant tori. The perturbation distorts most tori slightly, but near resonances (where the frequency ratio is a rational number), the tori break up and are replaced by chains of islands and chaotic layers. The KAM theorem guarantees that sufficiently irrational tori survive, while rational ones are destroyed.

![Poincare section of a near-integrable system. Most orbits lie on slightly deformed tori (closed curves), but near resonances, chains of islands appear and thin chaotic layers separate the surviving tori.](/img/09.04.06-poincare-section-near-integrable-placeholder.svg)

## Worked example [Beginner]

A simple pendulum of length $l$ and mass $m$ oscillates with small amplitude. The string is slowly pulled through the support, shortening the length at rate $dl/dt = -\epsilon$ where $\epsilon$ is much smaller than $\sqrt{g/l}$. How does the amplitude change?

The action is $J = E/\omega$ where $E$ is the energy and $\omega = \sqrt{g/l}$ is the frequency. Adiabatic invariance says $J$ is approximately constant, so $E/\omega$ stays constant, i.e., $E\sqrt{l}$ stays constant.

The energy is $E = \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 A^2$ where $A$ is the amplitude. So $m\omega^2 A^2 \sqrt{l}/2$ is constant. With $\omega = \sqrt{g/l}$, this gives $mg A^2 / (2\sqrt{l})$ is constant, hence $A \propto l^{1/4}$.

As the pendulum shortens, the amplitude *decreases* but only as $l^{1/4}$. The energy *increases* (as $l^{-1/2}$) because the tension does work pulling the string through. The frequency *increases* (as $l^{-1/2}$) because the pendulum is shorter. The action $J = E/\omega$ stays constant. This result, first derived by Rayleigh, is one of the earliest examples of an adiabatic invariant.

Consider the numbers. Start with $l = 1$ m, $A = 0.1$ m, $g = 9.8$ m/s$^2$. Then $\omega = 3.13$ rad/s, $E = 0.049$ J, and $J = 0.0157$ J s. Now shorten to $l = 0.5$ m. The new amplitude is $A = 0.1 \times (0.5)^{1/4} = 0.084$ m. The new frequency is $\omega = 4.43$ rad/s. The new energy is $E = J\omega = 0.0157 \times 4.43 = 0.0695$ J. The energy increased by 42% while the length halved -- and the action stayed constant to high accuracy.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Near-integrable Hamiltonian systems

A Hamiltonian system with $n$ degrees of freedom is **integrable** [09.06.01] when it possesses $n$ independent constants of motion in involution. By the Liouville-Arnold theorem, the motion is then confined to $n$-dimensional tori parameterised by action-angle variables $(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{J})$, with Hamilton's equations taking the simple form $\dot{\theta}^\alpha = \omega^\alpha(\mathbf{J})$, $\dot{J}_\alpha = 0$.

A **near-integrable** system is one whose Hamiltonian differs from an integrable one by a small perturbation:

$$
H(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{J}) = H_0(\mathbf{J}) + \epsilon H_1(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{J}),
$$

where $\epsilon \ll 1$ is the perturbation parameter and $H_1$ is $2\pi$-periodic in each angle $\theta^\alpha$. The full equations of motion are:

$$
\dot{\theta}^\alpha = \omega^\alpha(\mathbf{J}) + \epsilon \frac{\partial H_1}{\partial J_\alpha}, \qquad \dot{J}_\alpha = -\epsilon \frac{\partial H_1}{\partial \theta^\alpha}.
$$

The actions are no longer constant: they drift slowly, at rate $O(\epsilon)$. The question is whether this drift remains bounded over long times.

### Canonical perturbation theory

The strategy is to find a near-identity canonical transformation $(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{J}) \to (\boldsymbol{\phi}, \mathbf{I})$ that eliminates the angle dependence of the perturbation order by order. The generating function is expanded as:

$$
S(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{I}, \epsilon) = \boldsymbol{\theta}\cdot\mathbf{I} + \epsilon S_1(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{I}) + \epsilon^2 S_2(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{I}) + \cdots
$$

The transformation gives $\mathbf{J} = \mathbf{I} + \epsilon\,\partial S_1/\partial\boldsymbol{\theta} + O(\epsilon^2)$ and $\boldsymbol{\phi} = \boldsymbol{\theta} + \epsilon\,\partial S_1/\partial\mathbf{I} + O(\epsilon^2)$. Substituting into $H$ and collecting powers of $\epsilon$:

- **$O(1)$**: $K_0(\mathbf{I}) = H_0(\mathbf{I})$.
- **$O(\epsilon)$**: $K_1(\mathbf{I}) = \omega^\alpha(\mathbf{I})\,\partial S_1/\partial\theta^\alpha + H_1(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{I})$.

The $O(\epsilon)$ balance yields the **homological equation**:

$$
\boxed{\;\omega^\alpha(\mathbf{I})\,\frac{\partial S_1}{\partial \theta^\alpha} + H_1(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{I}) = K_1(\mathbf{I})\;}
$$

Decompose $H_1$ into its Fourier series $H_1 = \sum_{\mathbf{k}} h_{\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{I})\,e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\theta}}$. Setting $K_1 = h_{\mathbf{0}}(\mathbf{I}) = \bar{H}_1(\mathbf{I})$ (the average over all angles), the equation for $S_1$ becomes:

$$
i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega})\,s_{\mathbf{k}} + h_{\mathbf{k}} = 0 \quad\text{for } \mathbf{k}\neq 0,
$$

giving $s_{\mathbf{k}} = -h_{\mathbf{k}}/(i\,\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega})$. This is solvable provided $\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega} \neq 0$ for all non-zero integer vectors $\mathbf{k}$: the **non-resonance condition**.

### Secular terms and the small-divisor problem

When $\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega} = 0$ for some non-zero integer vector $\mathbf{k}$, the system is **resonant** and the corresponding Fourier mode cannot be eliminated. The generating function $S_1$ has a singularity (division by zero), and the perturbation expansion breaks down.

Even away from exact resonances, the small divisors $|\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega}|$ for large $|\mathbf{k}|$ make the coefficients $s_{\mathbf{k}}$ grow, and the perturbation series may diverge. Poincare showed that the series is generally divergent: the small divisors accumulate and prevent convergence. This is the **small-divisor problem**, the central obstacle to perturbation theory.

Naive perturbation expansions also produce **secular terms**: terms that grow without bound in time (e.g., $\epsilon t\cos\omega t$), making the expansion useless for long times. The canonical perturbation method avoids secular terms at each fixed order by absorbing them into frequency corrections. But the divergence of the full series means that this procedure cannot be continued to arbitrary order.

### The averaging principle

The **averaging principle** replaces the oscillatory perturbation $H_1(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{J})$ with its average over the fast angles:

$$
\bar{H}_1(\mathbf{J}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^n}\int_0^{2\pi}\!\cdots\!\int_0^{2\pi} H_1(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{J})\,d\theta^1\cdots d\theta^n = \sum_{\mathbf{k}} h_{\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{J})\,\delta_{\mathbf{k},\mathbf{0}}.
$$

The averaged equations $\dot{I}_\alpha = -\epsilon\,\partial\bar{H}_1/\partial\theta^\alpha = 0$ predict exact conservation of actions, since $\bar{H}_1$ has no angle dependence. The **averaging theorem** makes this precise:

**Theorem (Averaging).** *Let $H = H_0(\mathbf{J}) + \epsilon H_1(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{J})$ be analytic in a domain $D$. Suppose the unperturbed frequencies satisfy no resonances of order $\leq K$ in $D$ (i.e., $|\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega}| \geq c > 0$ for all integer vectors $\mathbf{k}$ with $0 < |\mathbf{k}| \leq K$). Then the averaged system approximates the true system with error $O(\epsilon)$ over times $O(1/\epsilon)$. That is, $|J_\alpha(t) - I_\alpha(t)| = O(\epsilon)$ for $0 \leq t \leq C/\epsilon$.*

The proof constructs the first-order normal form via the generating function $S_1$ that eliminates the oscillatory part of $H_1$. The remaining Hamiltonian $K_0(\mathbf{I}) + \epsilon K_1(\mathbf{I}) + O(\epsilon^2)$ has actions that are constant to $O(\epsilon)$ over the stated time interval.

### Adiabatic invariants

A **parameter-dependent Hamiltonian** $H(q, p, \lambda(t))$ depends on a slowly varying parameter $\lambda$ with $d\lambda/dt = \epsilon \ll 1$. For fixed $\lambda$, the system is integrable with action $J(\lambda) = \oint p\,dq/(2\pi)$. The **adiabatic theorem** states:

**Theorem (Adiabatic invariance).** *If the parameter $\lambda(t)$ changes smoothly from $\lambda_0$ to $\lambda_1$ over a time $\tau = O(1/\epsilon)$, then $|J(\tau) - J(0)| = O(\epsilon)$. For analytic systems, $|J(\tau) - J(0)| = O(e^{-c/\epsilon})$ for some $c > 0$.*

The exponential accuracy for analytic systems means that the action is conserved to extraordinary precision even for moderately slow parameter changes. The constant $c$ depends on the analyticity domain of the Hamiltonian.

The physical content is simple: if you change a parameter slowly, the system adjusts quasi-statically, always remaining close to an orbit of the instantaneous Hamiltonian. The action (phase-space area) is preserved during this adjustment because the Hamiltonian flow is area-preserving at each instant. The error comes from the finite (though small) rate of change, which pushes the system slightly off the instantaneous orbit.

### Example: Fermi acceleration

A particle bounces elastically between two walls separated by distance $L$. The walls slowly move together at rate $dL/dt = -\epsilon$. The action is $J = mvL/\pi$ (where $v$ is the speed between bounces). Adiabatic invariance gives $mvL/\pi$ is constant, so $v \propto 1/L$. As the walls converge, the particle speeds up. Each bounce off an approaching wall adds a small increment to the speed; over many bounces, these increments accumulate. This is the mechanism behind **Fermi acceleration** of cosmic rays: charged particles bounce between approaching magnetic shock fronts and gain energy with each reflection. Enrico Fermi proposed this mechanism in 1949 to explain the high energies of cosmic rays.

The adiabatic invariant is the phase-space area: the orbit in the $(q, p)$-plane is a rectangle of width $2L$ and height $2mv$. The area $4mvL$ is conserved (divided by $2\pi$ gives the action $J = 2mvL/\pi$). As $L$ decreases, $v$ must increase to keep the area constant.

### Example: Particle in a magnetic bottle

A charged particle gyrates in a magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ with cyclotron frequency $\omega_c = eB/(mc)$. The magnetic moment $\mu = mv_\perp^2/(2B)$ (where $v_\perp$ is the velocity perpendicular to $\mathbf{B}$) is an adiabatic invariant when $B$ varies slowly over one gyro-orbit.

The action for the cyclotron motion is $J_\perp = mv_\perp^2/(2\omega_c) = mc\mu/e$. Adiabatic invariance of $J_\perp$ is equivalent to conservation of $\mu$. As the particle moves into a region of stronger $B$, conservation of $\mu$ forces $v_\perp^2$ to increase proportionally to $B$, while conservation of total kinetic energy $E = \tfrac{1}{2}m(v_\perp^2 + v_\parallel^2)$ forces the parallel velocity $v_\parallel$ to decrease. At the **mirror point**, $v_\parallel = 0$ and the particle reflects. A **magnetic bottle** traps particles between two regions of strong field. This is the principle behind plasma confinement in magnetic fusion devices and the trapping of radiation belts around planets.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation: First-order normal form and elimination of fast angles.**

Seek a generating function $S(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{I}, \epsilon) = \boldsymbol{\theta}\cdot\mathbf{I} + \epsilon S_1(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{I})$ that transforms $H = H_0(\mathbf{J}) + \epsilon H_1(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{J})$ to $K = K_0(\mathbf{I}) + \epsilon K_1(\mathbf{I}) + O(\epsilon^2)$ where $K_1$ depends only on $\mathbf{I}$.

The transformation gives $\mathbf{J} = \partial S/\partial\boldsymbol{\theta} = \mathbf{I} + \epsilon\,\partial S_1/\partial\boldsymbol{\theta}$ and $\boldsymbol{\phi} = \partial S/\partial\mathbf{I} = \boldsymbol{\theta} + \epsilon\,\partial S_1/\partial\mathbf{I}$. The new Hamiltonian $K(\boldsymbol{\phi}, \mathbf{I}) = H(\boldsymbol{\theta}(\boldsymbol{\phi}, \mathbf{I}),\, \mathbf{J}(\boldsymbol{\phi}, \mathbf{I}))$ is expanded to $O(\epsilon)$ by Taylor expanding $H_0(\mathbf{J})$ around $\mathbf{I}$:

$$
H_0(\mathbf{J}) = H_0(\mathbf{I}) + \frac{\partial H_0}{\partial J_\alpha}\bigg|_{\mathbf{I}} \cdot \epsilon\,\frac{\partial S_1}{\partial \theta^\alpha} + O(\epsilon^2) = H_0(\mathbf{I}) + \epsilon\,\omega^\alpha(\mathbf{I})\,\frac{\partial S_1}{\partial \theta^\alpha} + O(\epsilon^2).
$$

Collecting the $O(\epsilon)$ terms:

$$
K_1(\mathbf{I}) = \omega^\alpha(\mathbf{I})\,\frac{\partial S_1}{\partial \theta^\alpha} + H_1(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{I}).
$$

Expand $H_1$ and $S_1$ in Fourier series: $H_1 = \sum_{\mathbf{k}} h_{\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{I})\,e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\theta}}$, $S_1 = \sum_{\mathbf{k}} s_{\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{I})\,e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\theta}}$. The homological equation becomes, for each $\mathbf{k} \neq 0$:

$$
i(\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega}(\mathbf{I}))\,s_{\mathbf{k}} + h_{\mathbf{k}} = 0 \implies s_{\mathbf{k}} = \frac{-h_{\mathbf{k}}}{i\,\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega}(\mathbf{I})}.
$$

Setting $K_1 = h_{\mathbf{0}} = \bar{H}_1$ (the angle-average of $H_1$) completes the construction. The new Hamiltonian is:

$$
K(\boldsymbol{\phi}, \mathbf{I}) = H_0(\mathbf{I}) + \epsilon\,\bar{H}_1(\mathbf{I}) + O(\epsilon^2).
$$

Since $K$ is independent of $\boldsymbol{\phi}$ to this order, the actions $\mathbf{I}$ are constant up to $O(\epsilon^2)$ corrections, and the angles advance with the corrected frequency $\boldsymbol{\omega} + \epsilon\,\partial\bar{H}_1/\partial\mathbf{I}$.

**The non-resonance condition is the key requirement.** At resonances ($\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega} = 0$ for some $\mathbf{k}$), the denominator vanishes and the transformation fails. The resonant Fourier modes remain in the normal form and drive slow exchange of action between the resonant degrees of freedom.

**Bridge.** This derivation builds toward the KAM theorem [09.08.01], which addresses what happens when the perturbation is treated globally rather than order by order: the surviving tori are those whose frequencies satisfy a Diophantine condition $|\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega}| \geq c/|\mathbf{k}|^\tau$ for all $\mathbf{k}$. The averaging method is the first step in a hierarchy of normal forms that culminate in the KAM theorem for eternal stability of sufficiently irrational tori.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

For a harmonic oscillator $H = p^2/(2m) + \tfrac{1}{2}kq^2$ with action $J = E/\omega$, express $J$ in terms of $m$, $k$, and the amplitude $A$.

$E = \tfrac{1}{2}kA^2$ and $\omega = \sqrt{k/m}$.

</details>

$J = E/\omega = \tfrac{1}{2}kA^2 / \sqrt{k/m} = \tfrac{1}{2}A^2\sqrt{km}$. Alternatively, the area in phase space is the area of the ellipse $q = A\cos\theta$, $p = -m\omega A\sin\theta$, which is $\pi \times A \times m\omega A = \pi m\omega A^2$. Dividing by $2\pi$: $J = m\omega A^2/2 = A^2\sqrt{km}/2$, confirming the result.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 2 (medium, symbolic).**

A particle bounces elastically between two walls separated by distance $L$. The walls are slowly moved together at rate $dL/dt = -\epsilon$. The particle has speed $v$. Show that the action $J = mvL/\pi$ is an adiabatic invariant and find how $v$ depends on $L$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The orbit in phase space is a rectangle. Compute the enclosed area and divide by $2\pi$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The particle travels from one wall to the other at constant speed $v$, bounces, and returns. In phase space, the orbit is a rectangle: forward trip at $p = +mv$, backward trip at $p = -mv$, width $2L$. The enclosed area is $\oint p\,dq = 2 \times mv \times L = 2mvL$ (two legs of the journey, each contributing $mvL$). So $J = 2mvL/(2\pi) = mvL/\pi$.

Adiabatic invariance gives $mvL/\pi$ is constant, hence $v \propto 1/L$. As the walls move together, the particle speeds up inversely with the distance. This is the Fermi acceleration mechanism for cosmic rays.

</details>

</aside>

A charged particle gyrates in a uniform magnetic field $B$ with cyclotron frequency $\omega_c = eB/(mc)$. The magnetic field is slowly increased from $B_0$ to $2B_0$. Using adiabatic invariance of the magnetic moment $\mu = mv_\perp^2/(2B)$, find the factor by which $v_\perp$ increases.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mu = E_\perp/B$ is the adiabatic invariant. Express in terms of $v_\perp$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mu = mv_\perp^2/(2B)$ is constant. When $B$ doubles: $v_\perp^2 \propto B$, so $v_\perp \propto \sqrt{B}$. The final velocity is $v_{\perp,f} = v_{\perp,0}\sqrt{2B_0/B_0} = v_{\perp,0}\sqrt{2}$. The perpendicular kinetic energy doubles. This is the principle behind magnetic mirrors and bottles: particles are confined by regions of strong field because adiabatic invariance of $\mu$ converts perpendicular kinetic energy into parallel kinetic energy and vice versa.

</details>

For a near-integrable system $H = H_0(J) + \epsilon H_1(\theta, J)$ with one degree of freedom, derive the first-order correction to the action. Show that $\Delta J = \epsilon\,\partial S_1/\partial\theta + O(\epsilon^2)$ and that this oscillatory correction has zero mean.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The generating function $S = \theta I + \epsilon S_1(\theta, I)$ gives $J = I + \epsilon\,\partial S_1/\partial\theta$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From $J = I + \epsilon\,\partial S_1/\partial\theta$, the correction is $\Delta J = J - I = \epsilon\,\partial S_1/\partial\theta$. From the homological equation: $\omega\,\partial S_1/\partial\theta + H_1(\theta, I) = \bar{H}_1(I)$, so $\partial S_1/\partial\theta = -(H_1 - \bar{H}_1)/\omega$. Therefore $\Delta J = -\epsilon(H_1(\theta, I) - \bar{H}_1(I))/\omega$. The average of $H_1 - \bar{H}_1$ over $\theta$ vanishes by construction, so the mean correction is zero. The action oscillates with amplitude $O(\epsilon/\omega)$ but has no secular drift to first order.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

The Hamiltonian for a perturbed pendulum is $H = \tfrac{1}{2}p^2 - \cos q + \epsilon\cos(2q)$. Write the action for the unperturbed pendulum and identify the resonant condition.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the unperturbed pendulum, the action is $J = \oint p\,dq/(2\pi)$, an elliptic integral depending on energy. The perturbation $\cos(2q)$ has Fourier mode $k = 2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For the unperturbed pendulum $H_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}p^2 - \cos q$, the action is $J(E) = \oint\sqrt{2(E + \cos q)}\,dq/(2\pi)$, an elliptic integral. For small oscillations ($E$ slightly above $-1$), $J \approx E/\omega_0$ where $\omega_0 = 1$.

The perturbation $\epsilon\cos(2q)$ has a single Fourier mode at $k = 2$. The resonant condition is $k\omega = 2\omega = 0$, i.e., $\omega = 0$, which occurs at the separatrix ($E = 1$). Away from the separatrix, $\omega \neq 0$ and the perturbation is non-resonant: it can be eliminated by the normal-form transformation. Near the separatrix, the frequency approaches zero and the perturbation becomes resonant, leading to chaotic layer formation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

A one-dimensional system has $H = p^2/(2m) + V(q) + \epsilon W(q)$ where $W(q)$ is a small perturbation to the potential. Show that the first-order correction to the action vanishes after averaging, and explain why this means the action is conserved to $O(\epsilon)$ over times $O(1/\epsilon)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In action-angle variables $(\theta, J)$ for the unperturbed system, $W(q) = W(q(\theta, J))$ is periodic in $\theta$. Compute $\bar{H}_1$ and the averaged action evolution.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In action-angle variables, $W(q(\theta, J))$ is $2\pi$-periodic in $\theta$. The average $\bar{W}(J) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint W(q(\theta, J))\,d\theta$ depends only on $J$. The averaged equations give $\dot{J} = -\epsilon\,\partial\bar{W}/\partial\theta = 0$ (the average has no $\theta$-dependence). So the averaged action is exactly constant. The true action oscillates with amplitude $O(\epsilon)$ but has no secular drift to first order. Secular effects arise only from higher-order terms involving resonances between the perturbation and the unperturbed motion. This is a general property: any perturbation depending only on position (not on momenta or angles independently) produces only oscillatory corrections to the action at first order.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

A Kepler orbit has action variables $J_r = -\ell + k\sqrt{m/(2|E|)}$ and $J_\varphi = \ell$ (where $\ell$ is angular momentum and $k$ is the gravitational coupling). If the gravitational constant $k$ is slowly decreased (e.g., by mass loss of the central body), which action variables are adiabatic invariants? What happens to the semi-major axis?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Both action variables are adiabatic invariants. Express the semi-major axis $a$ in terms of the actions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For the Kepler problem, $J_r + J_\varphi = k\sqrt{m/(2|E|)}$ and $a = k/(2|E|)$. So $a = 2(J_r + J_\varphi)^2/(mk^2)$. If $k$ decreases slowly, adiabatic invariance of $J_r + J_\varphi = k\sqrt{m/(2|E|)}$ gives $|E| \propto k^2$, so $a = k/(2|E|) \propto 1/k$. The orbit expands as the gravitational constant decreases. This is the gravitational analogue of Fermi acceleration: as the central mass loses mass through stellar wind, the orbiting body gains energy and moves outward. This mechanism explains the observed expansion of planetary orbits around evolving stars.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

The Hannay angle is the geometric phase acquired by the angle variable when the parameters of an integrable system are slowly varied around a closed loop in parameter space. For a general one-degree-of-freedom system with action $J$ and parameter $\lambda$ varied around a closed loop $C$, show that the Hannay angle is $\Delta\theta_{\mathrm{Hannay}} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial J}\oint_C \bar{A}(J, \lambda)\,d\lambda$, where $\bar{A} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint (\partial H/\partial\lambda)\,d\theta/\omega$ is the averaged connection. Evaluate this for a harmonic oscillator with slowly varying frequency.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Hannay angle arises from the derivative of the averaged generating function with respect to the action. For the harmonic oscillator, $\partial H/\partial\omega$ depends on $J$ in a simple way.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The generating function for the adiabatic transformation has an averaged part $\bar{S}(J, \lambda)$ satisfying $\partial\bar{S}/\partial\lambda = -\bar{A}(J,\lambda)$. The Hannay angle is the change in the angle variable after one loop: $\Delta\theta_{\mathrm{Hannay}} = -\partial(\oint \bar{A}\,d\lambda)/\partial J$.

For a harmonic oscillator $H = \omega(J) J$ with $\omega = dH/dJ$, the parameter is $\omega$ itself. $\partial H/\partial\omega = J$. The averaged connection is $\bar{A} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\oint J\,d\theta/\omega = J/\omega$. The Hannay angle is $\Delta\theta = -\partial(J\oint d\omega/\omega)/\partial J = -\partial(J\ln\omega)/\partial J = -\ln\omega$. But for a closed loop in $\omega$-space, $\oint d\omega/\omega = \oint d\ln\omega = 0$, so the Hannay angle vanishes. For a one-dimensional harmonic oscillator, the geometric phase is zero. This is consistent with the fact that the harmonic oscillator has no anharmonicity to couple the angle evolution to the parameter variation. The Hannay angle is nonzero for nonlinear oscillators, where the frequency depends on the action.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

### Resonances and resonance layers

The normal-form transformation fails when $\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega}(\mathbf{J}) = 0$ for some integer vector $\mathbf{k} \neq \mathbf{0}$. At such resonances, the generating function $S_1$ is singular and the perturbation cannot be fully eliminated. The remaining resonant terms in the normal form couple the actions at the resonant frequency ratio, producing slow exchange of energy between modes.

For $n$ degrees of freedom, the condition $\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega} = 0$ defines a hypersurface in action space (the **resonance surface**). The density of resonance surfaces increases with the order $|\mathbf{k}|$: high-order resonances fill action space densely. Between resonance surfaces, the normal form is well-defined and the actions are approximately conserved. Near resonance surfaces, the dynamics is governed by a resonant normal form that may include pendulum-like phase portraits with separatrices and chaotic layers.

The width of a resonance of order $|\mathbf{k}|$ scales as $\epsilon^{1/2|\mathbf{k}|}$. Low-order resonances ($|\mathbf{k}| = 1, 2, 3$) are wide and create large chaotic zones. High-order resonances are narrow and have negligible effect. The phase space is a hierarchical structure: large regions of near-integrable motion separated by thin chaotic layers around low-order resonances, with thinner sublayers around higher-order resonances nested within.

### The Kolmogorov normal form and KAM theorem preview

Kolmogorov's insight (1954) was to fix the frequency vector $\boldsymbol{\omega}_0$ rather than the action, and construct a sequence of canonical transformations that converge to a torus with exactly the prescribed frequency. The Kolmogorov normal form is:

$$
K(\boldsymbol{\phi}, \mathbf{I}) = \boldsymbol{\omega}_0 \cdot\mathbf{I} + O(|\mathbf{I}|^2),
$$

where the torus $\mathbf{I} = 0$ in the new coordinates is invariant under the flow, and the motion on it is quasiperiodic with frequency $\boldsymbol{\omega}_0$. The construction is a Newton-type iteration (quadratic convergence) rather than a power-series expansion, which is why it avoids the small-divisor divergence.

The **Diophantine condition** $|\mathbf{k}\cdot\boldsymbol{\omega}_0| \geq c/|\mathbf{k}|^\tau$ for all $\mathbf{k} \neq 0$ ensures that the frequencies are "sufficiently irrational." This is a stronger condition than mere non-resonance: it requires the frequencies to be bounded away from all rational linear combinations at a controlled rate. The set of Diophantine vectors has full Lebesgue measure in $\mathbb{R}^n$, meaning that "almost all" frequency vectors satisfy the condition.

The KAM theorem [38.07.01] then states that for a near-integrable analytic Hamiltonian $H = H_0(\mathbf{J}) + \epsilon H_1(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{J})$ with non-degenerate $H_0$ (the frequency map $\mathbf{J} \mapsto \boldsymbol{\omega}(\mathbf{J})$ has full rank), there exists $\epsilon_0 > 0$ such that for $|\epsilon| < \epsilon_0$, a set of invariant tori of positive measure survives the perturbation. The surviving tori carry quasiperiodic motion with Diophantine frequencies, and their total measure approaches full measure as $\epsilon \to 0$. The tori that are destroyed are those near rational frequency ratios (low-order resonances).

### Nekhoroshev estimates

For systems where KAM does not apply (e.g., the perturbation exceeds the KAM threshold, or $H_0$ is degenerate), **Nekhoroshev's theorem** (1977) provides long-time stability bounds:

**Theorem (Nekhoroshev).** *Let $H = H_0(\mathbf{J}) + \epsilon H_1(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{J})$ be analytic, with $H_0$ satisfying a steepness condition (e.g., $H_0$ is convex or quasi-convex). Then there exist constants $a, b, c, C > 0$ such that:*

$$
|\mathbf{J}(t) - \mathbf{J}(0)| \leq C\epsilon^a \qquad\text{for } |t| \leq T_0\,e^{c/\epsilon^b}.
$$

The exponents $a, b$ depend on the number of degrees of freedom $n$: for convex $H_0$, one can take $a = 1/(2n)$ and $b = 1/(2n)$. The stability time is **exponentially long** in $1/\epsilon$: not forever, but for times that are effectively infinite for practical purposes.

Nekhoroshev's theorem explains the long-term stability of the solar system on timescales of billions of years, despite the chaotic dynamics demonstrated by Lyapunov exponent calculations. The steepness condition on $H_0$ is generic (satisfied by most physically relevant Hamiltonians), so the theorem applies broadly. The result is sharp in the sense that Arnold diffusion (slow drift along resonance channels) eventually carries trajectories away from their initial tori, but only on timescales exponentially long in $1/\epsilon$.

### Lie transform perturbation theory

The canonical perturbation theory using generating functions (the Poincare-von Zeipel method) has a drawback: the transformation is defined implicitly (the new and old variables are mixed in the generating function), and computing higher-order terms requires increasingly complicated algebra. **Lie transform perturbation theory** (Hori 1966, Deprit 1969, Kamel 1969) provides a cleaner alternative.

A Lie transform is a canonical transformation generated by a Hamiltonian-like function $W(\boldsymbol{\theta}, \mathbf{J}, \epsilon)$ via the operator $\exp(L_W)$, where $L_W f = \{f, W\}$ is the Lie derivative (the Poisson bracket with $W$). The transformed Hamiltonian is:

$$
K = \exp(L_W) H = H + \{H, W\} + \tfrac{1}{2}\{\{H, W\}, W\} + \cdots.
$$

Expand $W = \epsilon W_1 + \epsilon^2 W_2 + \cdots$ and collect powers of $\epsilon$. At each order, a homological equation determines $W_k$ and $K_k$:

- $O(\epsilon)$: $\{H_0, W_1\} + H_1 = K_1$ (same as the generating-function method).
- $O(\epsilon^2)$: $\{H_0, W_2\} + \tfrac{1}{2}\{\{H_0, W_1\}, W_1\} + \{H_1, W_1\} = K_2$.

The advantage is that the transformation is explicit (the Lie series gives the new variables directly as functions of the old ones), and the algebraic structure is more transparent: each order involves only Poisson brackets of previously computed functions. The Lie transform is the basis of modern computer-algebra implementations of perturbation theory (e.g., in celestial mechanics and beam physics).

### Adiabatic invariants in plasma physics

The adiabatic invariance of the magnetic moment $\mu = mv_\perp^2/(2B)$ is the workhorse of plasma confinement theory. In a tokamak, charged particles spiral around magnetic field lines with cyclotron frequency $\omega_c = eB/(mc)$. If the magnetic field varies slowly over one gyro-orbit (i.e., the Larmor radius $\rho_L = mv_\perp/(eB)$ is small compared to the scale length of $B$), then $\mu$ is conserved to exponential accuracy.

A magnetised particle has three adiabatic invariants corresponding to three timescales:

1. **Magnetic moment** $\mu = mv_\perp^2/(2B)$: conserved over the gyro-period $T_c = 2\pi/\omega_c$.
2. **Bounce action** $J_\parallel = \oint mv_\parallel\,dl$: conserved over the bounce period between mirror points.
3. **Drift action** (magnetic flux) $\Phi = \oint \psi\,d\alpha$: conserved over the drift period around the torus.

Each invariant is associated with a periodic motion on a successively slower timescale. The gyro-motion is fastest, the bounce motion is intermediate, and the drift motion is slowest. The hierarchy of timescales ($\omega_c \gg \omega_b \gg \omega_d$) ensures that each invariant is well-conserved on the timescale of the next faster motion.

Breaking of these invariants (by collisions, fluctuations, or resonances) is the primary mechanism for particle and energy loss from magnetic confinement devices. Understanding when and how invariants break is central to fusion plasma physics.

**Synthesis.** Hamiltonian perturbation theory provides a unified framework for near-integrable systems through three complementary results: the averaging principle controls short-time dynamics, the KAM theorem guarantees the survival of most invariant tori for all time, and Nekhoroshev estimates bound the drift of actions over exponentially long times when KAM does not apply. The central insight is that the fast oscillatory part of the perturbation can be systematically eliminated by near-identity canonical transformations (whether via generating functions or Lie transforms), leaving a residual Hamiltonian that captures the slow secular evolution. The non-resonance condition is the foundational requirement: away from resonances, the perturbation has no secular effect on the actions. Adiabatic invariants extend this framework to systems with slowly varying parameters, where the action is conserved to exponential accuracy. Together, these results explain why most mechanical systems are far more regular than naive perturbation theory would suggest.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Perturbation theory in classical mechanics originated in the work of Lagrange and Laplace on celestial mechanics (late 18th century), who sought to compute the long-term evolution of planetary orbits under mutual gravitational perturbations. The key difficulty -- the appearance of secular terms (unbounded growth in the perturbation series) -- was recognised by Poincare, who showed in *Les Methodes Nouvelles de la Mecanique Celeste* (1892--1899) that the perturbation series generally diverges. Poincare's proof of divergence was itself a landmark: it demonstrated that the Newtonian programme of computing the exact future of the solar system by successive approximations is impossible in principle, not merely in practice. The divergence does not mean the approximations are useless -- they are accurate over finite times -- but it means that the infinite series does not converge.

The averaging method was formalised by Krylov, Bogoliubov, and Mitropolsky in the 1930s--1950s. Krylov and Bogoliubov introduced the method of averaging for nonlinear oscillations, providing rigorous error bounds and establishing the connection between averaged equations and the true dynamics. Their work was motivated by engineering problems in radio electronics and nonlinear vibrations. Bogoliubov and Mitropolsky's book *Asymptotic Methods in the Theory of Nonlinear Oscillations* (1955) became the standard reference.

Kolmogorov's 1954 address at the International Congress of Mathematicians in Amsterdam introduced the idea of fixing the frequency and constructing convergent transformations via a Newton-type iteration. Arnold (1963) developed this into a full proof for Hamiltonian systems, and Moser (1962) proved an analogous result for area-preserving maps (the twist theorem). Together, these results form the KAM theorem. Nekhoroshev's 1977 thesis provided the long-time stability bounds for systems outside the KAM regime.

Lie transform perturbation theory was developed independently by Hori (1966), Deprit (1969), and Kamel (1969). It replaced the older Poincare-von Zeipel method with a more algebraic and computationally efficient framework. The Lie transform is now the standard tool in celestial mechanics, satellite dynamics, and accelerator physics.

The concept of adiabatic invariance was introduced by Ehrenfest (1916) in the context of the old quantum theory. Ehrenfest's hypothesis -- that the action $J = nh$ (Bohr-Sommerfeld quantisation) should be preserved under slow changes to the system -- was used to derive selection rules for quantum transitions. Although the old quantum theory was superseded by Schrodinger's wave mechanics (1926), the adiabatic theorem survived as a rigorous result in classical mechanics. Hannay's 1985 discovery of the geometric phase in classical mechanics (the Hannay angle), and Berry's 1984 discovery of the analogous phase in quantum mechanics (the Berry phase), revealed a deep connection between adiabatic evolution and the topology of the parameter space. The Hannay-Berry connection shows that adiabatic invariants are not merely approximately conserved quantities; they are the classical shadow of a geometric structure (a connection on a fibre bundle) that governs the phase of the wave function in quantum mechanics.

The philosophical significance of perturbation theory lies in the tension between integrability and chaos. The Newtonian worldview suggests that the motion of the solar system is, in principle, exactly predictable. Poincare's discovery that the perturbation series diverges shattered this expectation: exact prediction is impossible, not because of measurement error, but because of the mathematical structure of the equations themselves. The KAM theorem partially restored the picture -- most orbits are regular and predictable -- but the gaps between KAM tori admit chaotic diffusion (Arnold diffusion), so the long-term future of any specific orbit is genuinely uncertain. The Nekhoroshev theorem provides a practical resolution: the uncertainty manifests only on timescales exponentially long in the perturbation strength, which for the solar system is far longer than its age.
