## Intuition [Beginner]

A **Lyapunov exponent** measures how fast two nearby trajectories separate. If you start two pendulums with almost identical initial conditions — differing by a millionth of a degree — and watch them evolve, the separation might grow exponentially: $\Delta(t) \approx \Delta_0 \, e^{\lambda t}$. The rate $\lambda$ is the Lyapunov exponent. If $\lambda > 0$, the system is chaotic: tiny errors in initial conditions blow up exponentially, and long-term prediction becomes impossible.

For a system with $n$ degrees of freedom, there are $2n$ Lyapunov exponents (one for each direction in phase space). The largest one, $\lambda_1$, determines the dominant stretching rate. For a chaotic system, $\lambda_1 > 0$.

The **double pendulum** is the classic demonstration. A pendulum with a second pendulum attached to its bob has two degrees of freedom. For small swings, it behaves regularly. For large swings, it becomes chaotic: two double pendulums started with imperceptibly different initial conditions diverge rapidly, producing completely different motions within seconds.

## Visual [Beginner]

| Regime | Largest Lyapunov exponent $\lambda_1$ | Behaviour |
|---|---|---|
| Regular (periodic) | $\lambda_1 = 0$ | Trajectories maintain separation |
| Quasi-periodic | $\lambda_1 = 0$ | Torus motion, no stretching |
| Weakly chaotic | $\lambda_1 \approx 0.01$ | Slow divergence |
| Strongly chaotic | $\lambda_1 \gg 0$ | Rapid, exponential divergence |

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider the map $x_{n+1} = 2x_n \pmod{1}$ on the interval $[0, 1)$. Two points separated by $\delta_0$ become separated by $\delta_1 = 2\delta_0$, $\delta_2 = 4\delta_0$, and in general $\delta_n = 2^n \delta_0$ (as long as the separation is less than 1).

The Lyapunov exponent is the exponential growth rate:

$$\lambda = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \ln \frac{\delta_n}{\delta_0} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n} \ln 2^n = \ln 2 \approx 0.693$$

So $\lambda = \ln 2 > 0$: the map is chaotic. The doubling map is one of the simplest chaotic systems.

For comparison, the map $x_{n+1} = x_n/2$ has $\delta_n = \delta_0/2^n$, giving $\lambda = -\ln 2 < 0$: nearby points converge, and the system is stable.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\phi^t : M \to M$ be a flow on a smooth manifold $M$. The **Lyapunov exponent** in the direction of a tangent vector $\mathbf{v}$ at the point $x_0$ is

$$\lambda(x_0, \mathbf{v}) = \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{1}{t} \ln \|D\phi^t(x_0) \cdot \mathbf{v}\|$$

where $D\phi^t(x_0)$ is the Jacobian of the flow (the **tangent map**).

**Oseledets multiplicative ergodic theorem.** For a smooth flow $\phi^t$ preserving a measure $\mu$ on a compact manifold, for $\mu$-almost every $x_0$ there exist numbers $\lambda_1 > \lambda_2 > \cdots > \lambda_s$ (the **Lyapunov spectrum**) and a filtration $T_{x_0}M = V_1 \supset V_2 \supset \cdots \supset V_s = \{0\}$ of the tangent space such that for $\mathbf{v} \in V_i \setminus V_{i+1}$:

$$\lambda(x_0, \mathbf{v}) = \lambda_i$$

The numbers $\lambda_i$ are constant $\mu$-almost everywhere and are called the **Lyapunov exponents** of the system.

For a Hamiltonian system with $n$ degrees of freedom, the flow is on a $2n$-dimensional phase space. The symplectic structure constrains the Lyapunov spectrum to be symmetric about zero: the exponents come in pairs $\pm \lambda_i$, and possibly some zero exponents. The sum $\sum_i \lambda_i = 0$ (Liouville's theorem).

**Computation (Benettin's algorithm).** The standard numerical method evolves a trajectory $\mathbf{x}(t)$ and a tangent vector $\mathbf{v}(t)$ simultaneously. The tangent vector satisfies the **variational equation**:

$$\dot{\mathbf{v}} = D\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{v}$$

where $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ is the original system. To prevent numerical overflow, the tangent vector is periodically renormalised (every $\tau$ time units):

$$\lambda_1 \approx \frac{1}{N\tau} \sum_{k=1}^{N} \ln \|\mathbf{v}(k\tau)\|$$

For the full spectrum, Gram-Schmidt orthogonalisation of $2n$ tangent vectors is used, yielding all $2n$ exponents simultaneously.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Derivation (Lyapunov spectrum of the double pendulum).**

The double pendulum has masses $m_1, m_2$, lengths $l_1, l_2$, angles $\theta_1, \theta_2$ measured from the vertical. The Lagrangian is:

$$L = \tfrac{1}{2}(m_1 + m_2)l_1^2\dot{\theta}_1^2 + \tfrac{1}{2}m_2 l_2^2\dot{\theta}_2^2 + m_2 l_1 l_2 \dot{\theta}_1\dot{\theta}_2\cos(\theta_1 - \theta_2) + (m_1 + m_2)g l_1\cos\theta_1 + m_2 g l_2\cos\theta_2$$

The Euler-Lagrange equations give a system of two coupled second-order ODEs, which we write as a first-order system in the four-dimensional phase space $(\theta_1, \theta_2, \dot{\theta}_1, \dot{\theta}_2)$.

The Jacobian $D\mathbf{f}$ of the right-hand side is a $4 \times 4$ matrix depending on the current state. The variational equation $\dot{\mathbf{v}} = D\mathbf{f} \cdot \mathbf{v}$ is a linear ODE with time-dependent coefficients (the trajectory $\mathbf{x}(t)$ provides the coefficients).

**Key result.** For the double pendulum with $m_1 = m_2 = 1$, $l_1 = l_2 = 1$, $g = 9.8$:

- For small energies ($E \ll mgl$), the motion is approximately two coupled linear oscillators. The Lyapunov exponents are all zero (quasi-periodic motion on a 2-torus).
- For $E \approx 2mgl$ (energy near the inverted position), the motion becomes chaotic. Numerical computation gives $\lambda_1 \approx 1$ (depending on the exact energy), with the spectrum $\{\lambda_1, 0, 0, -\lambda_1\}$ consistent with a Hamiltonian system: two degrees of freedom, one positive exponent, one zero exponent (along the flow), one zero exponent (if a second integral exists), and one negative exponent.

The transition to chaos is gradual: as energy increases, the largest Lyapunov exponent grows from zero. The Poincare section transitions from smooth curves (KAM tori) to a mixture of islands and chaotic sea.

**Bridge.** The Lyapunov exponent computation for the double pendulum connects to the general theory of Lyapunov exponents for Hamiltonian systems [38.07.01], where the Oseledets theorem guarantees the existence of the spectrum almost everywhere. The foundational reason the Hamiltonian constraint forces the Lyapunov spectrum to be symmetric about zero is that the flow preserves the symplectic form, and the Jacobian $D\phi^t$ is a symplectic matrix, whose eigenvalues come in reciprocal pairs $\mu, 1/\mu$, so $\ln|\mu|$ and $-\ln|\mu|$ both appear. The central insight is that the Lyapunov exponent is the definitive diagnostic for chaos: $\lambda_1 > 0$ implies sensitive dependence on initial conditions, which is the operational definition of deterministic chaos. This connects the geometric picture (horseshoes [09.08.02], homoclinic tangles) to a computable number, and via the Pesin formula, to the metric entropy (the rate of information production).

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

For the map $x_{n+1} = r x_n (1 - x_n)$ (logistic map), compute the Lyapunov exponent $\lambda$ for $r = 2$ analytically.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $r = 2$, the fixed point is $x^* = 1 - 1/r = 1/2$. The Lyapunov exponent at a fixed point is $\lambda = \ln|f'(x^*)|$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The fixed point is $x^* = 1/2$ (since $2x^*(1 - x^*) = x^*$ gives $x^* = 1/2$). The derivative is $f'(x) = r(1 - 2x)$. At $x^* = 1/2$: $f'(1/2) = 2(1 - 1) = 0$. The Lyapunov exponent is $\lambda = \ln|0| = -\infty$. This means the fixed point is superstable: trajectories converge to it faster than exponentially.
</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

For the doubling map $x_{n+1} = 2x_n \pmod{1}$, show that $\lambda = \ln 2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$f'(x) = 2$ everywhere (except at the discontinuity). The Lyapunov exponent is $\lambda = \lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\ln|f'(x_n)|$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The derivative $f'(x) = 2$ for all $x \in (0,1)$ (away from the discontinuity at $x = 1/2$). The Lyapunov exponent is $\lambda = \lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\ln 2 = \ln 2$. This holds for almost every orbit (the set of measure zero hitting the discontinuity is negligible).
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

A 1D map has $f'(x_n) = 2$ for even $n$ and $f'(x_n) = 1/3$ for odd $n$. Compute the Lyapunov exponent.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\lambda = \lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\ln|f'(x_n)|$. Average over two iterations.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Over two iterations: $\frac{1}{2}(\ln 2 + \ln(1/3)) = \frac{1}{2}(\ln 2 - \ln 3) = \frac{1}{2}\ln(2/3) = \ln\sqrt{2/3} \approx -0.203$. Since $\lambda < 0$, the system is stable despite occasional stretching. The contraction (factor $1/3$) dominates the stretching (factor 2) on average.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that for a Hamiltonian system with $n$ degrees of freedom, the $2n$ Lyapunov exponents sum to zero: $\sum_{i=1}^{2n} \lambda_i = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The sum of Lyapunov exponents equals the long-time average of $\text{tr}(D\mathbf{f})$. For a Hamiltonian system, $\text{tr}(D\mathbf{f}) = 0$ (the divergence vanishes by Liouville's theorem).
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the definition, the sum of all Lyapunov exponents is $\sum_i \lambda_i = \lim_{t\to\infty}\frac{1}{t}\ln|\det(D\phi^t)|$. For a Hamiltonian flow $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$, the divergence $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{f} = \text{tr}(D\mathbf{f}) = 0$ (Hamiltonian flows are divergence-free). By Liouville's theorem, $\det(D\phi^t) = e^{\int_0^t \text{tr}(D\mathbf{f})\,dt'} = 1$ for all $t$. Therefore $\sum_i \lambda_i = \lim_{t\to\infty}\frac{1}{t}\ln 1 = 0$.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

State and explain the **Pesin formula**: for a smooth, ergodic, measure-preserving diffeomorphism on a compact manifold, the metric entropy (Kolmogorov-Sinai entropy) equals the sum of the positive Lyapunov exponents: $h_{KS} = \sum_{\lambda_i > 0} \lambda_i$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each positive Lyapunov exponent represents an expanding direction that generates information. The KS entropy measures the rate of information production. Pesin (1977) showed these are equal.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Pesin's formula (1977).** For a $C^2$ diffeomorphism $f$ preserving a smooth (absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue) ergodic measure $\mu$ on a compact manifold:

$$h_\mu(f) = \sum_{\lambda_i > 0} \lambda_i \cdot m_i$$

where $h_\mu(f)$ is the Kolmogorov-Sinai (metric) entropy and $m_i$ is the multiplicity of $\lambda_i$. The interpretation: each positive Lyapunov exponent contributes to the rate at which the system generates information (or, equivalently, the rate at which an observer must acquire information to track the orbit). For the doubling map: $h_{KS} = \lambda_1 = \ln 2$. For the Smale horseshoe (conjugate to the shift on 2 symbols): $h_{KS} = \log 2$ (in natural log), matching the topological entropy. The Pesin formula bridges the geometric (Lyapunov exponents) and information-theoretic (entropy) characterisations of chaos.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

The **Kaplan-Yorke conjecture** relates the Lyapunov spectrum to the fractal (information) dimension of the attractor: $d_{KY} = j + \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{j}\lambda_i}{|\lambda_{j+1}|}$ where $j$ is the largest integer with $\sum_{i=1}^{j}\lambda_i \geq 0$. Compute $d_{KY}$ for the Lorenz attractor with $\lambda_1 \approx 0.906$, $\lambda_2 = 0$, $\lambda_3 \approx -14.57$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$j = 1$ since $\lambda_1 > 0$ but $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \lambda_3 < 0$. Use the formula $d_{KY} = 1 + \lambda_1/|\lambda_3|$.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Sort exponents: $\lambda_1 \approx 0.906 > \lambda_2 = 0 > \lambda_3 \approx -14.57$. Check: $\lambda_1 = 0.906 \geq 0$, so $j = 1$. But $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = 0.906 \geq 0$, and $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \lambda_3 = 0.906 + 0 - 14.57 < 0$. So $j = 2$ (since $\sum_{i=1}^{2}\lambda_i = 0.906 \geq 0$ but $\sum_{i=1}^{3}\lambda_i < 0$). Then $d_{KY} = 2 + 0.906/14.57 \approx 2.062$. The Lorenz attractor has fractal dimension approximately 2.06 — slightly above 2, meaning it is a fractal surface in 3D phase space.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, symbolic).**

Explain Benettin's algorithm for computing $\lambda_1$ numerically. Why is periodic renormalisation necessary?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The tangent vector grows as $e^{\lambda_1 t}$ and will eventually overflow. Renormalise periodically and accumulate the logarithms of the norms.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Benettin's algorithm.** 1. Choose initial conditions $\mathbf{x}_0$ and a random unit tangent vector $\mathbf{v}_0$. 2. Integrate the system $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})$ and the variational equation $\dot{\mathbf{v}} = D\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})\cdot\mathbf{v}$ simultaneously for time $\tau$. 3. Record $\alpha_1 = \ln\|\mathbf{v}(\tau)\|$. 4. Renormalise: $\mathbf{v}_1 = \mathbf{v}(\tau)/\|\mathbf{v}(\tau)\|$. 5. Repeat from step 2 with $\mathbf{v}_1$ as the new initial tangent vector. 6. After $N$ steps: $\lambda_1 \approx \frac{1}{N\tau}\sum_{k=1}^{N}\alpha_k$.

Renormalisation is necessary because $\|\mathbf{v}(t)\| \sim e^{\lambda_1 t}$ grows exponentially and would overflow floating-point arithmetic in a few hundred steps. The renormalisation prevents overflow while preserving the growth rate information in the accumulated logarithms.
</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, symbolic).**

For a 2D Hamiltonian system (two degrees of freedom), the Lyapunov spectrum is $\{\lambda_1, 0, 0, -\lambda_1\}$. Explain each exponent.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

One zero exponent corresponds to the flow direction (energy conservation). The second zero may correspond to a second integral. The pair $\pm\lambda_1$ comes from the symplectic symmetry.
</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

- $\lambda_1 > 0$: the dominant stretching direction in phase space (the unstable direction of the horseshoe-like dynamics).
- $0$: the direction along the flow (a trajectory displaced along the flow stays displaced by the same amount — no stretching or contraction along the orbit itself).
- $0$: if a second integral of motion exists (KAM tori), motion along this direction is bounded; otherwise, this exponent may be zero only approximately.
- $-\lambda_1$: the contracting direction, forced by the symplectic structure. The Jacobian $D\phi^t$ is a symplectic matrix, so if one direction stretches by $e^{\lambda_1 t}$, another must contract by $e^{-\lambda_1 t}$ to preserve the symplectic area. The sum $\lambda_1 + 0 + 0 + (-\lambda_1) = 0$ is consistent with Liouville's theorem.
</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**The multiplicative ergodic theorem (Oseledets 1968).** The MET guarantees that Lyapunov exponents exist almost everywhere (with respect to any ergodic invariant measure) for smooth dynamical systems. This is the existence theorem that justifies the entire computational enterprise: without it, we would not know that the limit defining $\lambda$ converges.

**Pesin entropy formula (Pesin 1977).** For $C^2$ diffeomorphisms preserving a smooth ergodic measure, $h_{KS} = \sum_{\lambda_i > 0}\lambda_i$. This establishes the fundamental link between geometric chaos (stretching rates) and information-theoretic chaos (entropy production). The formula was extended to the "generalised" version by Ledrappier and Young (1985), which holds for arbitrary invariant measures and includes the "entropy production per expanding direction."

**Ruelle's inequality.** For any $C^1$ diffeomorphism and any invariant measure $\mu$: $h_\mu(f) \leq \int \sum_{\lambda_i(x)>0}\lambda_i(x)\,d\mu(x)$. This is the generalisation of Pesin's formula to non-smooth measures and provides an upper bound on the entropy.

**The variational principle for Lyapunov exponents.** The Lyapunov exponents depend on the invariant measure. Different measures on the same system can have different Lyapunov spectra. The "natural" measure (SRB measure, Sinai-Ruelle-Bowen) typically maximises the entropy and has the largest Lyapunov exponents.

**Synthesis.** The Lyapunov exponent is the quantitative bridge between the geometric picture of chaos (horseshoes [09.08.02], homoclinic tangles) and the information-theoretic picture (metric entropy, information production). The foundational theorem is Oseledets' multiplicative ergodic theorem, which guarantees the existence of the Lyapunov spectrum almost everywhere. The Pesin formula then equates the sum of positive Lyapunov exponents to the Kolmogorov-Sinai entropy, establishing that the geometric stretching rate equals the rate of information production. Putting these together, the Lyapunov spectrum provides a complete quantitative characterisation of chaotic dynamics: it determines the divergence rate ($\lambda_1$), the information production rate ($h_{KS}$), and (via Kaplan-Yorke) the fractal dimension of the chaotic invariant set [09.08.05]. For Hamiltonian systems, the symplectic constraint forces the spectrum to be symmetric about zero, and the existence of even one positive exponent (together with the horseshoe theorem [09.08.02]) implies the full structure of deterministic chaos.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Lyapunov exponents of the doubling map).** The doubling map $f(x) = 2x \pmod{1}$ has $\lambda = \ln 2$ at every point $x$ whose orbit does not hit the discontinuity.

*Proof.* For any $x \in [0,1)$ whose orbit avoids $1/2$ (a set of full Lebesgue measure), $f'(x) = 2$ at every iterate. The Lyapunov exponent is:

$$\lambda(x) = \lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\ln|f'(f^n(x))| = \lim_{N\to\infty}\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}\ln 2 = \ln 2$$

The limit exists and equals $\ln 2$ for every such orbit (not just almost everywhere). The doubling map is uniformly expanding: the Lyapunov exponent is constant. $\square$

**Proposition (Symplectic symmetry of Lyapunov exponents).** For a Hamiltonian flow on a $2n$-dimensional symplectic manifold, the Lyapunov exponents come in pairs $\pm\lambda$ (with possible zeros).

*Proof.* The tangent map $D\phi^t(x_0)$ is a symplectic linear map: it preserves the symplectic form $\omega$. That is, $(D\phi^t)^*\omega = \omega$. If $\mu$ is an eigenvalue of $D\phi^t$ with eigenvector $\mathbf{v}$, then $1/\mu$ is also an eigenvalue. This follows from the symplectic property: if $A^T J A = J$ (the matrix form of symplecticity), then $A^{-1} = -J A^T J$, and the eigenvalues of $A$ and $A^{-1}$ are reciprocals. Taking logarithms: if $\lambda$ is a Lyapunov exponent (from the eigenvalue $\mu = e^{\lambda t}$), then $-\lambda$ is also a Lyapunov exponent (from the eigenvalue $1/\mu = e^{-\lambda t}$). $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **KAM theorem [09.08.01]** describes the regular islands where $\lambda_1 = 0$; the chaotic sea has $\lambda_1 > 0$.
- **Horseshoes [09.08.02]** generate positive Lyapunov exponents via stretching; the horseshoe has $\lambda = \ln 2$.
- **Period-doubling [09.08.04]** is the route by which $\lambda_1$ transitions from negative (stable fixed point) through zero (bifurcation point) to positive (chaos).
- **Strange attractors [09.08.05]** are characterised by $\lambda_1 > 0$ and fractal dimension $d_{KY} > 0$.
- **Liouville's theorem [09.04.04]** forces $\sum \lambda_i = 0$ for Hamiltonian systems.
- **Ergodic theory [38.07.01]** provides the Oseledets theorem and Pesin formula.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Lyapunov (1857–1918) introduced what are now called Lyapunov functions and exponents in his 1892 doctoral dissertation on the stability of motion. His original definition was for the stability of equilibrium points; the extension to chaotic systems (where the exponent is defined along an orbit, not at a fixed point) came much later.

Oseledets proved the multiplicative ergodic theorem in 1968, providing the rigorous foundation for the existence of Lyapunov exponents. Pesin proved the entropy formula in 1977, and the generalisation by Ledrappier and Young (1985) is one of the deepest results in smooth ergodic theory.

Benettin, Galgani, Giorgilli, and Strelcyn (1980) developed the practical algorithm for computing Lyapunov exponents from numerical simulations, making the theory computationally accessible. This algorithm is now standard in nonlinear dynamics.

The philosophical significance is that the Lyapunov exponent quantifies the "butterfly effect": the rate at which small perturbations grow. Weather systems have $\lambda_1 \approx 0.5\ \text{day}^{-1}$, meaning predictability is lost on a timescale of $1/\lambda_1 \approx 2$ days — the fundamental limit on weather forecasting, not a limitation of our models.
