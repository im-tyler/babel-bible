## Intuition [Beginner]

The previous unit built a continuous-time chain one jump at a time: wait an exponential time, jump, wait again. That is a recipe for a single sample path. Now we want a different object, a table. For each pair of states $i$ and $j$ and each elapsed time $t$, write down the probability $P_{ij}(t)$ that a chain started at $i$ sits at $j$ exactly $t$ time units later. As $t$ grows, every entry in this table changes smoothly, and the whole family of tables, one for each $t$, is called the transition semigroup.

Two facts pin the table down. The first is a stitching rule: to get from $i$ to $j$ in time $s + t$, the chain must be somewhere at the intermediate time $s$, so the table at time $s + t$ is built by multiplying the table at time $s$ by the table at time $t$. The second is that the rate table $Q$ from the previous unit is the instantaneous push. At time zero the table is the identity (you are where you started), and $Q$ tells you the direction and speed it first moves.

From the push $Q$ you can recover the whole evolving table, and there are two natural ways to set up the bookkeeping. You can condition on what the chain does in its very first instant, an argument that pushes $Q$ in on the left and gives the backward equation. Or you can condition on what it does in its very last instant before time $t$, pushing $Q$ in on the right and giving the forward equation. Both are the statement "rate of change of the table equals the table composed with the push," differing only in which side the push acts.

When the rates are bounded, both equations agree and have one clean answer: the table is the matrix exponential of $t$ times $Q$, written $P(t) = e^{tQ}$, the exact analogue of how a single number grows as $e^{rt}$ under a constant growth rate $r$. The only place the two equations can disagree is when the chain can explode; then there is a smallest honest answer, and the forward bookkeeping can leak probability that the backward one keeps.

The one-sentence takeaway: the transition table $P(t)$ evolves by composing with the rate table $Q$ — on the left in the backward equation, on the right in the forward equation — and for bounded rates the unique answer is the matrix exponential $e^{tQ}$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We compute the transition table for a two-state chain that switches back and forth. State $1$ leaves for state $2$ at rate $a = 1$, and state $2$ leaves for state $1$ at rate $b = 2$. So the rate table is $Q$ with first row $(-1, 1)$ and second row $(2, -2)$: each diagonal is the negative of its row's off-diagonal entry, and each row sums to zero.

Step 1. Write what the table must satisfy at the start. At time $0$ the chain is where it started, so $P(0)$ is the identity: first row $(1, 0)$, second row $(0, 1)$.

Step 2. Find the long-run table. After a very long time the starting point is forgotten, and the chain spends time in each state in proportion to how slowly it leaves. State $1$ leaves at rate $1$ and state $2$ at rate $2$, so the long-run fractions are $b / (a + b) = 2/3$ in state $1$ and $a / (a + b) = 1/3$ in state $2$. Both rows of $P(t)$ drift toward $(2/3, 1/3)$.

Step 3. Fill in the time dependence. The gap between $P(t)$ and the long-run table shrinks at the combined rate $a + b = 3$, decaying like $e^{-3t}$. So the probability of being in state $1$ having started in state $1$ is
$$P_{11}(t) = \tfrac23 + \tfrac13 e^{-3t}.$$

Step 4. Sanity-check two times. At $t = 0$ this gives $2/3 + 1/3 = 1$, correct: you start in state $1$. As $t$ grows large $e^{-3t}$ fades and $P_{11}(t)$ settles to $2/3$, the long-run value. The companion entry $P_{12}(t) = 1 - P_{11}(t) = 1/3 - (1/3) e^{-3t}$ runs from $0$ up to $1/3$.

Step 5. Read the push at the start. The rate of change of $P_{11}$ at time zero is the slope of $2/3 + (1/3)e^{-3t}$ at $t = 0$, which is $(1/3)(-3) = -1$. That is exactly $Q_{11} = -1$: the rate table $Q$ is the instantaneous push on the transition table at time zero.

What this tells us: the whole table was determined by two ingredients, the starting identity and the rate table $Q$, and its entries are simple decaying exponentials settling to the long-run split. The slope at time zero recovered $Q$ directly, which is the meaning of calling $Q$ the generator.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $I$ is a countable state space, $Q = (q_{ij})$ a stable conservative $Q$-matrix on $I$ with jump matrix $\Pi$ and rates $q_i = -q_{ii}$ [37.05.08], and $(X_t)_{t \geq 0}$ the associated minimal right-continuous process with explosion time $\zeta$. All differentiation-under-summation steps invoke the dominated convergence theorem for the Lebesgue integral [02.07.04].

**Definition (transition function and semigroup).** The *transition function* of the minimal chain is $P_{ij}(t) = \mathbb{P}_i(X_t = j)$ for $t \geq 0$, $i, j \in I$, with $P(t) = (P_{ij}(t))_{i,j \in I}$. The family $(P(t))_{t \geq 0}$ is a *transition semigroup*: it satisfies
$$P(0) = I_d, \qquad P(s + t) = P(s)\,P(t) \ \text{ for } s, t \geq 0, \qquad P_{ij}(t) \geq 0, \quad \sum_{j} P_{ij}(t) \leq 1.$$
The middle identity is the *continuous-time Chapman–Kolmogorov equation*. The semigroup is *standard* if it is right-continuous at $0$, $P(t) \to I_d$ entrywise as $t \downarrow 0$; the minimal chain's transition function is always standard. The semigroup is *honest* (or *stochastic*) if $\sum_j P_{ij}(t) = 1$ for all $i, t$, which holds iff the chain is non-explosive; otherwise it is *substochastic*, the deficit $1 - \sum_j P_{ij}(t) = \mathbb{P}_i(\zeta \leq t)$ being the probability of having exploded by time $t$.

**Definition (the $Q$-matrix as infinitesimal generator).** The generator is recovered as the right derivative of the semigroup at $0$:
$$q_{ij} = P'_{ij}(0) = \lim_{t \downarrow 0} \frac{P_{ij}(t) - \delta_{ij}}{t},$$
so $q_{ij} = \lim_{t \downarrow 0} P_{ij}(t)/t$ for $j \neq i$ (the instantaneous transition rate) and $q_{ii} = \lim_{t \downarrow 0} (P_{ii}(t) - 1)/t = -q_i$ (minus the instantaneous exit rate).

**Definition (backward and forward equations).** A standard transition function $P(t)$ satisfies the *Kolmogorov backward equation* if
$$P'(t) = Q\,P(t), \qquad \text{i.e. } P'_{ij}(t) = \sum_{k} q_{ik}\,P_{kj}(t) = -q_i\,P_{ij}(t) + \sum_{k \neq i} q_{ik}\,P_{kj}(t),$$
and the *Kolmogorov forward equation* (the *master equation* in the applied literature) if
$$P'(t) = P(t)\,Q, \qquad \text{i.e. } P'_{ij}(t) = \sum_{k} P_{ik}(t)\,q_{kj} = -q_j\,P_{ij}(t) + \sum_{k \neq j} P_{ik}(t)\,q_{kj}.$$
Both are subject to the initial condition $P(0) = I_d$. The backward equation differentiates in the *starting* state $i$ (the generator acts on the left), the forward equation in the *terminal* state $j$ (the generator acts on the right).

**Definition (minimal solution).** Among all nonnegative solutions $(\tilde P(t))$ of the backward system with $\tilde P(0) = I_d$ and $\tilde P_{ij}(t) \geq 0$, the *minimal solution* is the one with $P_{ij}(t) \leq \tilde P_{ij}(t)$ entrywise for every other nonnegative solution; it exists, is unique, and equals the minimal chain's transition function. The minimal solution is honest iff the chain is non-explosive; when explosion has positive probability there exist strictly larger honest solutions corresponding to processes restarted after explosion.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Backward and forward are not always equivalent.* For bounded $Q$ they coincide and both equal $e^{tQ}$. For unbounded $Q$ the minimal transition function always solves the backward equation, but it solves the forward equation only under an extra summability condition; Kolmogorov's own example exhibits a chain whose minimal function fails the forward equation.

- *The semigroup law is composition, not addition.* $P(s + t) = P(s)P(t)$ is a matrix product summing over the intermediate state, not $P(s) + P(t)$. The additive shadow $x(t) = e^{rt}$ misleads only if one forgets that the exponent here is a non-commutative matrix.

- *$P(t) = e^{tQ}$ requires bounded $Q$.* The power series $\sum_n t^n Q^n / n!$ converges in operator norm only when $\|Q\| < \infty$. For unbounded $Q$ the exponential series can diverge entrywise even though the minimal transition function exists and is finite; the exponential formula is then replaced by the minimal-solution construction.

- *Substochastic is not a modelling error.* Row sums below one record real probability mass lost to explosion, not a failure to normalise. Forcing $\sum_j P_{ij}(t) = 1$ by hand corresponds to choosing a particular honest extension of the minimal chain, a modelling decision about post-explosion behaviour.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (the backward equation for the minimal chain).** Let $P(t)$ be the transition function of the minimal chain of a stable conservative $Q$-matrix $Q$. Then $P(t)$ is the minimal nonnegative solution of the backward equation
$$P'_{ij}(t) = \sum_{k} q_{ik}\,P_{kj}(t), \qquad P_{ij}(0) = \delta_{ij},$$
equivalently, in integrated form,
$$P_{ij}(t) = \delta_{ij}\,e^{-q_i t} + \int_0^t q_i e^{-q_i s} \sum_{k \neq i} \pi_{ik}\,P_{kj}(t - s)\, ds. \tag{$\ast$}$$
[ref: TODO_REF Norris 1997 §2.8]

*Proof.* Fix $i$ and condition on the first holding time $S_1 \sim \mathrm{Exp}(q_i)$ and the first jump $Y_1 \sim \pi_{i\cdot}$, independent by the competing-exponentials law of [37.05.08]. Assume first $q_i > 0$.

**Step 1 (first-jump decomposition).** Decompose the event $\{X_t = j\}$ according to whether the first jump has occurred by time $t$. If $S_1 > t$, the chain still sits at $i$, contributing $\mathbb{P}_i(X_t = j,\, S_1 > t) = \delta_{ij}\,\mathbb{P}_i(S_1 > t) = \delta_{ij} e^{-q_i t}$. If $S_1 = s \leq t$, the chain jumps to $Y_1 = k$ with probability $\pi_{ik}$ and, by the strong Markov property at the jump time [37.05.08], evolves as a fresh copy started at $k$ for the remaining time $t - s$:
$$P_{ij}(t) = \delta_{ij} e^{-q_i t} + \int_0^t q_i e^{-q_i s} \sum_{k \neq i} \pi_{ik}\,P_{kj}(t - s)\, ds,$$
which is ($\ast$); the density of $S_1$ at $s$ is $q_i e^{-q_i s}$.

**Step 2 (differentiate).** Substitute $u = t - s$ to move the $t$-dependence out of the integrand: writing $g(t) = \sum_{k \neq i} \pi_{ik} P_{kj}(t)$,
$$P_{ij}(t) = \delta_{ij} e^{-q_i t} + q_i e^{-q_i t} \int_0^t e^{q_i u}\, g(u)\, du.$$
The integrand is bounded ($0 \leq g \leq 1$) and continuous in $u$, so the integral is differentiable in $t$ with derivative $e^{q_i t} g(t)$; dominated convergence [02.07.04] licenses interchanging the sum over $k$ with the limit. Differentiating,
$$P'_{ij}(t) = -q_i \delta_{ij} e^{-q_i t} - q_i^2 e^{-q_i t}\!\int_0^t \!\! e^{q_i u} g(u)\, du + q_i e^{-q_i t} \cdot e^{q_i t} g(t) = -q_i P_{ij}(t) + q_i g(t).$$

**Step 3 (read off the generator).** Since $q_i \pi_{ik} = q_{ik}$ for $k \neq i$ and $-q_i = q_{ii}$,
$$P'_{ij}(t) = -q_i P_{ij}(t) + \sum_{k \neq i} q_{ik} P_{kj}(t) = \sum_{k} q_{ik} P_{kj}(t),$$
the backward equation. For $q_i = 0$ the state is absorbing, $P_{ij}(t) = \delta_{ij}$, and both sides vanish, so the equation holds with every term zero.

**Minimality.** Iterating ($\ast$) expresses $P_{ij}(t)$ as the increasing limit of the $n$-jump truncations $P^{(n)}_{ij}(t) = \mathbb{P}_i(X_t = j,\, J_n > t)$, the probability of reaching $j$ using at most $n$ jumps. Any nonnegative solution $\tilde P$ of the backward system satisfies the same integral recursion and hence $\tilde P_{ij}(t) \geq P^{(n)}_{ij}(t)$ for every $n$ by induction on $n$ (the base case $\tilde P \geq P^{(0)} = \delta_{ij} e^{-q_i t}$ is the $S_1 > t$ term). Letting $n \to \infty$ with monotone convergence [02.07.04] gives $\tilde P_{ij}(t) \geq P_{ij}(t)$, so $P$ is minimal. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem builds toward the entire operator-semigroup picture of Markov processes and appears again in the Hille–Yosida theory, where $Q = P'(0)$ is recovered as the generator of the contraction semigroup $P(t)$ on $\ell^1(I)$. The foundational reason the backward equation is the canonical one is that it comes from conditioning on the *first* instant, where the strong Markov property applies cleanly to the freshly-restarted post-jump chain; the forward equation conditions on the *last* instant and needs an extra summability hypothesis to control the infinitely many ways of arriving at $j$. This is exactly the asymmetry that the minimal solution resolves: the backward minimal solution always exists, and putting these together, the equality $P(t) = e^{tQ}$ for bounded $Q$ is the special case in which both equations collapse to the single matrix-exponential flow. The central insight is that $Q$ generates the semigroup by acting on either side, and the construction generalises the discrete one-step generator $P - I_d$ of [37.05.01]; here the $Q$-matrix on a countable set is dual to the second-order diffusion generator of [02.15.03], the backward equation being the Kolmogorov equation on observables and the forward equation the Fokker–Planck equation on densities.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

For a bounded $Q$ with $\|Q\| < \infty$, show directly from $P(t) = e^{tQ} = \sum_{n \geq 0} t^n Q^n / n!$ that $P(t)$ satisfies both the backward and the forward equation and the semigroup law.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate the norm-convergent series term by term; use $Q e^{tQ} = e^{tQ} Q$ because $Q$ commutes with its own powers.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The series converges in operator norm since $\sum_n t^n \|Q\|^n / n! = e^{t\|Q\|} < \infty$, so term-by-term differentiation is valid: $\frac{d}{dt} e^{tQ} = \sum_{n \geq 1} t^{n-1} Q^n / (n-1)! = Q\,e^{tQ} = e^{tQ}\,Q$, the last equality because every $Q^n$ commutes with $Q$. Thus $P'(t) = Q P(t) = P(t) Q$, both equations at once. The semigroup law $e^{(s+t)Q} = e^{sQ}e^{tQ}$ follows from the Cauchy product of the two absolutely convergent series, $\sum_{m,n} s^m t^n Q^{m+n}/(m! n!) = \sum_k (s+t)^k Q^k / k!$ by the binomial theorem, valid because $Q$ commutes with itself. Boundedness is exactly what makes forward and backward agree.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the forward equation for the minimal chain by a last-jump decomposition: condition $\{X_t = j\}$ on the time $s$ of the last jump before $t$ and the state $k$ it jumped from. State the extra summability assumption that the derivation requires.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

After the last jump into $j$ the chain holds $j$ for the remaining time $t - s$ without jumping, contributing $e^{-q_j (t-s)}$; sum over the prior state $k$ that supplied the jump.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Conditioning on the last jump occurring at time $s$ into state $j$ from some $k \neq j$, and then holding $j$ for time $t - s$:
$$P_{ij}(t) = \delta_{ij} e^{-q_j t} + \int_0^t \Big( \sum_{k \neq j} P_{ik}(s)\, q_{kj} \Big) e^{-q_j (t - s)}\, ds.$$
Differentiating in $t$ gives $P'_{ij}(t) = -q_j P_{ij}(t) + \sum_{k \neq j} P_{ik}(t) q_{kj} = \sum_k P_{ik}(t) q_{kj}$, the forward equation. The interchange of $\frac{d}{dt}$ with the infinite sum $\sum_{k \neq j} P_{ik}(s) q_{kj}$ requires this sum to converge uniformly enough to differentiate under it — automatic for bounded $Q$, but a genuine extra hypothesis when $\sup_k q_k = \infty$, which is why the forward equation can fail for the minimal chain while the backward equation always holds.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, multiple choice).**

For an explosive minimal chain, what is the row-sum deficit $1 - \sum_j P_{ij}(t)$?

A. Always zero — transition functions are stochastic
B. The probability $\mathbb{P}_i(\zeta \leq t)$ of having exploded by time $t$
C. The probability of being in an absorbing state
D. The total jump rate $q_i$ times $t$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Probability mass leaves $I$ exactly when the path reaches the cemetery state past explosion.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** The minimal chain sends mass to the cemetery $\partial$ at explosion, so $\sum_j P_{ij}(t) = \mathbb{P}_i(X_t \in I) = \mathbb{P}_i(\zeta > t)$ and the deficit is $\mathbb{P}_i(\zeta \leq t)$. A holds only in the honest (non-explosive) case; C confuses absorption (which keeps mass in $I$) with explosion; D is dimensionally a rate times time, not a probability.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $N(t)$ be a Poisson process of rate $\lambda$, viewed as a pure-birth chain on $\{0, 1, 2, \dots\}$ with $q_{n, n+1} = \lambda$. Solve the forward equation for $P_{0n}(t) = \mathbb{P}_0(N(t) = n)$ and recover the Poisson law $e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^n / n!$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The forward equation is $P'_{0n}(t) = \lambda P_{0,n-1}(t) - \lambda P_{0n}(t)$; solve recursively starting from $P_{00}(t) = e^{-\lambda t}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The generator has $q_{n,n+1} = \lambda$, $q_{nn} = -\lambda$, so the forward equation $P'(t) = P(t)Q$ reads $P'_{0n}(t) = \lambda P_{0,n-1}(t) - \lambda P_{0n}(t)$ for $n \geq 1$ and $P'_{00}(t) = -\lambda P_{00}(t)$. From the last, $P_{00}(t) = e^{-\lambda t}$. Set $P_{0n}(t) = e^{-\lambda t} a_n(t)$; substituting, $e^{-\lambda t}(a_n' - \lambda a_n) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t} a_{n-1} - \lambda e^{-\lambda t} a_n$, so $a_n'(t) = \lambda a_{n-1}(t)$ with $a_0 = 1$ and $a_n(0) = 0$. Integrating gives $a_n(t) = (\lambda t)^n / n!$ by induction. Hence $P_{0n}(t) = e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^n / n!$, the Poisson$(\lambda t)$ mass function. The rates are bounded ($q_n = \lambda$), so forward and backward agree and the chain is honest; the Poisson process is the canonical non-explosive pure-birth chain.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that for a bounded $Q$ the matrix exponential $P(t) = e^{tQ}$ is the *unique* solution of the backward equation with $P(0) = I_d$, so minimality is automatic in the bounded case.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If $\tilde P$ also solves $\tilde P' = Q \tilde P$, $\tilde P(0) = I_d$, consider $D(t) = e^{-tQ}\tilde P(t)$ and differentiate.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\tilde P$ be any solution of $\tilde P'(t) = Q\tilde P(t)$ with $\tilde P(0) = I_d$, and set $D(t) = e^{-tQ}\tilde P(t)$, which is well-defined since $\|Q\| < \infty$ makes $e^{-tQ}$ a bounded operator. Differentiating with the product rule, $D'(t) = -Q e^{-tQ}\tilde P(t) + e^{-tQ}\tilde P'(t) = -Q e^{-tQ}\tilde P(t) + e^{-tQ} Q \tilde P(t)$. Since $Q$ commutes with $e^{-tQ}$, the two terms cancel, so $D'(t) = 0$ and $D(t) = D(0) = I_d$. Hence $\tilde P(t) = e^{tQ} D(t) = e^{tQ}$, the unique solution. For bounded $Q$ there is therefore nothing to minimise: backward, forward, and the matrix exponential all single out the same honest semigroup. Non-uniqueness, and with it the need for the minimal solution, is strictly an unbounded-rate phenomenon tied to explosion.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The theory beyond the construction turns on four facts: the semigroup law promotes the differential equations to a flow; the backward equation always holds while the forward equation needs a summability hypothesis; the minimal solution coincides with the minimal-process transition function and is honest iff Reuter's criterion holds; and the bounded case collapses everything to the matrix exponential, identifying $Q$ as the generator dual to the diffusion operator of [02.15.03].

**Theorem 1 (semigroup, generator, and the two equations).** Let $P(t)$ be the standard transition function of the minimal chain of a stable conservative $Q$. Then $(P(t))_{t \geq 0}$ is a strongly continuous substochastic semigroup on $\ell^\infty(I)$ (and a contraction semigroup on $\ell^1(I)$), with $P(0) = I_d$, $P(s+t) = P(s)P(t)$, and generator $Q = \lim_{t \downarrow 0} t^{-1}(P(t) - I_d)$ in the entrywise sense. $P(t)$ solves the backward equation $P'(t) = Q P(t)$ for every stable conservative $Q$. It solves the forward equation $P'(t) = P(t)Q$ provided the additional summability holds: for the minimal chain this is guaranteed when $Q$ is bounded, and more generally when no probability mass accumulates at infinity in finite time through the terminal state.

**Theorem 2 (the minimal solution and honesty).** The transition function of the minimal chain is the minimal nonnegative solution of the backward system, equal to the increasing limit of the $n$-jump truncations $P^{(n)}_{ij}(t) = \mathbb{P}_i(X_t = j,\, J_n > t)$. It is honest, $\sum_j P_{ij}(t) = 1$ for all $i, t$, iff the chain is non-explosive, iff Reuter's criterion holds: the only bounded $[0,1]$-solution of $(Q - I_d)z = 0$ is $z \equiv 0$ [37.05.08]. When explosion has positive probability, the minimal solution is strictly substochastic and is the smallest member of a family of honest solutions of the *backward* equation, indexed by the boundary conditions describing how the process is restarted after explosion; these larger solutions generally fail the forward equation.

**Theorem 3 (bounded $Q$: the matrix exponential).** If $\sup_i q_i < \infty$, then $P(t) = e^{tQ} = \sum_{n \geq 0} t^n Q^n / n!$, the series converging in operator norm on $\ell^\infty(I)$. This $P(t)$ is the unique solution of the backward equation with $P(0) = I_d$, satisfies the forward equation identically, is honest ($P(t)\mathbf{1} = \mathbf{1}$ from $Q\mathbf{1} = 0$), and is entrywise nonnegative. Finite state spaces are the special case, where $e^{tQ}$ is computed by diagonalising or Jordan-decomposing the finite matrix $Q$: eigenvalue $0$ supplies the stationary row, the remaining eigenvalues (with nonpositive real part) supply the transient exponential modes.

**Theorem 4 (Kolmogorov's example: forward $\neq$ backward).** There is a stable conservative $Q$ on a countable $I$ whose minimal transition function $P(t)$ solves the backward equation but not the forward equation: differentiating the forward integral recursion produces a strict inequality $P'_{ij}(t) < \sum_k P_{ik}(t) q_{kj}$ because mass arrives at $j$ through unboundedly fast intermediate states faster than the term-by-term sum accounts for. The asymmetry is structural: the backward equation conditions on the first instant, where the strong Markov property restarts a single fresh chain; the forward equation conditions on the last instant, summing over infinitely many possible penultimate states whose contribution need not be differentiable under the sum when $\sup_k q_k = \infty$ [ref: TODO_REF Kolmogorov 1931].

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason the subject closes up is that the generator $Q$ determines the semigroup, and putting these together, the minimal nonnegative solution of the backward equation is exactly the transition function of the jump-and-hold process of [37.05.08] — the analytic object and the pathwise object are the same, the first-jump integral recursion being the bridge between them. The backward equation is the central insight in its purest form: conditioning on the first instant restarts one chain by the strong Markov property, so $Q$ acts on the left with no summability cost, and this is exactly why the backward minimal solution always exists while the forward equation is contingent. Honesty is dual to Reuter's criterion through the maximal solution $z_i = \mathbb{E}_i[e^{-\zeta}]$ of $(Q - I_d)z = 0$, so the row-sum deficit of $P(t)$ generalises the explosion functional to every finite horizon. For bounded $Q$ the whole structure collapses to $P(t) = e^{tQ}$, the matrix exponential that is dual to the diffusion semigroup $e^{t\mathcal{L}}$ of [02.15.03]: there the backward equation is Kolmogorov's equation on observables and the forward equation is the Fokker–Planck equation on densities, here they are the same pair on a countable set, so the backward equation, the forward equation, the minimal solution, and the matrix exponential are four presentations of the single fact that an infinitesimal generator drives a Markov semigroup whenever its mass does not run off to infinity in finite time.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Chapman–Kolmogorov for the minimal chain).** The transition function satisfies $P(s + t) = P(s) P(t)$ for all $s, t \geq 0$.

*Proof.* For the minimal chain, condition on the state at the intermediate time $s$. By the Markov property at the deterministic time $s$ [37.05.08],
$$P_{ij}(s + t) = \mathbb{P}_i(X_{s+t} = j) = \sum_{k \in I} \mathbb{P}_i(X_s = k)\,\mathbb{P}_k(X_t = j) = \sum_k P_{ik}(s) P_{kj}(t),$$
the sum being over $k \in I$ (the exploded paths contribute nothing to either side, as $X_{s+t} = j \in I$ forces $\zeta > s + t$, hence $X_s = k \in I$). This is the $(i,j)$ entry of $P(s)P(t)$; the interchange of $\mathbb{P}_i$ with the countable sum is justified by nonnegativity and Tonelli [02.07.04]. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (the generator exists; $Q = P'(0)$).** For the standard transition function of a stable conservative $Q$, $\lim_{t \downarrow 0} (P_{ij}(t) - \delta_{ij})/t = q_{ij}$ for all $i, j$.

*Proof.* From the integrated backward equation ($\ast$) with $j = i$, $P_{ii}(t) \geq e^{-q_i t}$, so $\limsup_{t \downarrow 0}(1 - P_{ii}(t))/t \leq \lim (1 - e^{-q_i t})/t = q_i$; the matching lower bound comes from the probability of at least one jump by time $t$ being $1 - e^{-q_i t} + o(t)$, giving $\lim (1 - P_{ii}(t))/t = q_i = -q_{ii}$. For $j \neq i$, ($\ast$) gives $P_{ij}(t) = \int_0^t q_i e^{-q_i s} \sum_{k \neq i} \pi_{ik} P_{kj}(t-s)\, ds$; dividing by $t$ and letting $t \downarrow 0$, only the $k = j$ term survives to first order (others need an extra jump, contributing $o(t)$), yielding $\lim P_{ij}(t)/t = q_i \pi_{ij} = q_{ij}$. Dominated convergence [02.07.04] controls the sum. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (forward equation under bounded rates).** If $\sup_i q_i = \bar q < \infty$, the minimal transition function satisfies the forward equation $P'(t) = P(t) Q$.

*Proof.* Boundedness makes $\|Q\|_{\ell^\infty} = 2\bar q < \infty$, so $P(t) = e^{tQ}$ by Theorem 3, and term-by-term differentiation of the norm-convergent exponential series gives $P'(t) = e^{tQ} Q = P(t) Q$ since $Q$ commutes with $e^{tQ}$. Equivalently, in the last-jump recursion of Exercise 5 the inner sum $\sum_{k \neq j} P_{ik}(s) q_{kj}$ is dominated by $\bar q \sum_k P_{ik}(s) \leq \bar q$, uniformly in $s$, so differentiation under the integral and under the sum is licensed by dominated convergence [02.07.04], producing $P'_{ij}(t) = -q_j P_{ij}(t) + \sum_{k \neq j} P_{ik}(t) q_{kj} = (P(t)Q)_{ij}$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (honesty $\Leftrightarrow$ non-explosion).** The minimal transition function is honest, $\sum_j P_{ij}(t) = 1$ for all $i, t$, iff the chain is non-explosive.

*Proof.* The minimal chain takes values in $I$ exactly on $\{\zeta > t\}$ and is sent to the cemetery $\partial \notin I$ on $\{\zeta \leq t\}$ [37.05.08]. Hence $\sum_{j \in I} P_{ij}(t) = \mathbb{P}_i(X_t \in I) = \mathbb{P}_i(\zeta > t)$. This equals $1$ for all $t$ iff $\mathbb{P}_i(\zeta = \infty) = 1$, i.e. non-explosion. By Reuter's criterion [37.05.08] this is equivalent to $z \equiv 0$ being the only bounded $[0,1]$-solution of $(Q - I_d)z = 0$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (finite state space: spectral form of $e^{tQ}$).** If $|I| < \infty$ and $Q$ is diagonalisable with eigenvalues $0 = \mu_0, \mu_1, \dots, \mu_{m}$ (all with $\mathrm{Re}\,\mu_r \leq 0$) and right/left eigenvectors $v_r, u_r$, then $P(t) = e^{tQ} = \sum_{r} e^{\mu_r t}\, v_r u_r^\top$, with the $\mu_0 = 0$ term $v_0 u_0^\top$ the stationary projection.

*Proof.* Diagonalise $Q = V \Lambda V^{-1}$ with $\Lambda = \mathrm{diag}(\mu_r)$; then $e^{tQ} = V e^{t\Lambda} V^{-1} = \sum_r e^{\mu_r t} v_r u_r^\top$, where $v_r$ is the $r$-th column of $V$ and $u_r^\top$ the $r$-th row of $V^{-1}$, so $u_s^\top v_r = \delta_{rs}$. Conservativity $Q\mathbf{1} = 0$ makes $\mathbf{1}$ a right $0$-eigenvector, $v_0 \propto \mathbf{1}$; the corresponding left eigenvector $u_0$ is the stationary distribution $\pi$ (when $Q$ is irreducible), so the $r = 0$ term is $\mathbf{1}\pi^\top$, the rank-one stationary projection each row converges to. The remaining eigenvalues have strictly negative real part for an irreducible finite chain, so $e^{\mu_r t} \to 0$ and $P(t) \to \mathbf{1}\pi^\top$ exponentially, recovering convergence to equilibrium. (For non-diagonalisable $Q$, replace the spectral sum by the Jordan form, where blocks contribute $t^k e^{\mu_r t}$ terms.) $\square$

**Proposition 6 (the minimal solution is dominated by any honest solution).** If $\tilde P(t)$ is any standard honest solution of the backward equation with $\tilde P(0) = I_d$, then the minimal solution $P$ satisfies $P_{ij}(t) \leq \tilde P_{ij}(t)$ for all $i, j, t$.

*Proof.* Both $P$ and $\tilde P$ satisfy the integral recursion ($\ast$) (any backward solution does, by integrating against $e^{-q_i s}$). The $n$-jump truncations $P^{(n)}_{ij}(t) = \mathbb{P}_i(X_t = j, J_n > t)$ satisfy $P^{(0)} = \delta_{ij} e^{-q_i t} \leq \tilde P_{ij}(t)$ (the $S_1 > t$ term is common to both), and the recursion is monotone: $P^{(n)} \leq \tilde P$ implies $P^{(n+1)}_{ij}(t) = \delta_{ij}e^{-q_i t} + \int_0^t q_i e^{-q_i s}\sum_{k \neq i}\pi_{ik} P^{(n)}_{kj}(t-s)ds \leq \tilde P_{ij}(t)$. By induction $P^{(n)} \leq \tilde P$ for all $n$; letting $n \to \infty$ with monotone convergence [02.07.04], $P_{ij}(t) = \lim_n P^{(n)}_{ij}(t) \leq \tilde P_{ij}(t)$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The construction of [37.05.08] supplies everything the equations operate on: the minimal right-continuous process, its explosion time $\zeta$, and the first-jump strong Markov decomposition are precisely the ingredients of the backward integral recursion ($\ast$), and the row-sum deficit of $P(t)$ is the explosion functional of that unit evaluated at a finite horizon, so honesty here is identical to Reuter's criterion there.

- The Lebesgue integral and its convergence theorems [02.07.04] are the analytic engine throughout: monotone convergence assembles the minimal solution as the increasing limit of $n$-jump truncations, and dominated convergence licenses the differentiation-under-the-summation steps that turn the integral recursions into the backward and forward differential equations, without which the term-by-term passage to $P'(t) = QP(t)$ would be unjustified.

- The continuous-state diffusion generator [02.15.03] is the parallel theory on a continuum: the Kolmogorov backward equation $\partial_t u = \mathcal{L}u$ on observables and the Fokker–Planck (Kolmogorov forward) equation $\partial_t \rho = \mathcal{L}^* \rho$ on densities are the differential-operator analogues of $P'(t) = QP(t)$ and $P'(t) = P(t)Q$, with $\mathcal{L}$ a second-order elliptic operator in place of the row-zero-sum matrix $Q$, and explosion of the diffusion mirroring the explosion that makes the forward matrix equation fail.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Andrei N. Kolmogorov introduced both differential equations in his 1931 *Mathematische Annalen* memoir on the analytic methods of probability theory [ref: TODO_REF Kolmogorov 1931]. Starting from the transition function $p_{ij}(t)$ and the semigroup relation $p_{ik}(s+t) = \sum_j p_{ij}(s)p_{jk}(t)$, he derived the system that now bears his name: the backward equation, obtained by differentiating in the initial variable, and the forward equation, by differentiating in the terminal variable. He already noted that the forward (then called the "second") system can fail to have a unique solution, the analytic signature of what would later be understood as explosion and boundary behaviour.

William Feller, in his 1940 *Transactions of the American Mathematical Society* paper on purely discontinuous Markoff processes [ref: TODO_REF Feller 1940], studied the integro-differential equations for jump processes on countable state spaces and isolated the *minimal* solution of the backward system, proving its existence and showing it is the transition function of a genuine sub-Markovian process. Feller's minimal solution is the object this unit constructs probabilistically through the jump-and-hold dynamics, and his analysis of when it is honest is the analytic side of the explosion dichotomy.

Gerd E. H. Reuter, in his 1957 *Acta Mathematica* paper [ref: TODO_REF Reuter 1957], characterised honesty of the minimal solution through the vanishing of bounded solutions of $(Q - I_d)z = 0$ and placed the matrix $Q$ within the contraction-semigroup framework on $\ell^1$, connecting the Kolmogorov equations to the Hille–Yosida generation theory developed in the same decade. Kai Lai Chung's monograph [ref: TODO_REF Chung 1967] systematised the backward-versus-forward distinction and the role of the minimal solution, the treatment this unit follows.
