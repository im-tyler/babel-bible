## Intuition [Beginner]

A continuous-time chain wanders among its states, holding each one for a random stretch of time before jumping to the next. Run it long enough and a striking regularity appears: the fraction of total elapsed time it has spent in each state stops drifting and settles to a fixed split. A small repair shop that cycles between idle, busy, and broken-down spends, over a year, some steady fraction of its hours in each condition, and that fraction does not depend on which condition it happened to start in. This unit is about that long-run split — when it exists, what fixes it, and how to compute it.

Two ideas from earlier units combine to pin the split down. The first is the jump chain: ignore the clock and just record the sequence of states visited. The second is the holding time: how long each visit lasts on average. The long-run time-fraction in a state is built from both — how often the jump chain visits the state, multiplied by how long it stays each time. A state visited rarely but held for a long while, and a state visited often but left quickly, can end up with the same share of the clock.

There is a second, more local way to find the same split, and it is the one practitioners reach for. In the long run the chain must enter and leave each state at equal rates, otherwise probability would pile up or drain away. Writing "rate of flow in equals rate of flow out" for every state gives a system of balance equations whose solution is exactly the long-run split. For many chains a stronger and simpler balance holds: between every pair of states the back-and-forth flow matches pair by pair, a condition called detailed balance, and when it holds the split can be read off almost by inspection.

Once the split is known, two convergence facts follow. The probability of finding the chain in a given state at a far-off time approaches that state's long-run share, with no parity worry that troubled the discrete-time version, because the random holding times smear out any rigid rhythm. And the time-average of any quantity along a single long run approaches the same share-weighted average — one path, watched long enough, samples the whole split.

The takeaway: a continuous-time chain has a long-run time-split among its states; you find it either by weighting jump-chain visits by holding times or by balancing the flow in and out of each state, and both the snapshot probabilities and the time-averages along one path converge to it.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take a machine that is either working or broken. While working it breaks down at rate $a = 1$ (per day). While broken it gets repaired at rate $b = 4$ (per day). We find the long-run fraction of time it is working.

Step 1. Read off the rates. From "working" the only move is to "broken," at rate $1$. From "broken" the only move is to "working," at rate $4$. So the chain leaves "working" at rate $1$ and leaves "broken" at rate $4$.

Step 2. Balance the flow for the "working" tank. Let $w$ be the long-run fraction of time working and $1 - w$ the fraction broken. Flow out of "working" is $w \times 1$. Flow into "working" comes from "broken" at rate $4$, so it is $(1 - w) \times 4$. Setting inflow equal to outflow: $w \times 1 = (1 - w) \times 4$.

Step 3. Solve. The equation $w = 4(1 - w) = 4 - 4w$ gives $5w = 4$, so $w = 4/5 = 0.8$. The machine works $80\%$ of the time and is broken $20\%$ of the time.

Step 4. Check with holding times. The average holding time in "working" is $1/a = 1$ day; in "broken" it is $1/b = 1/4$ day. The jump chain just alternates working, broken, working, broken, so it visits each equally often. Weighting equal visits by holding time gives working : broken $= 1 : 1/4 = 4 : 1$, so the working fraction is $4/(4+1) = 4/5$. Same answer by a different route.

What this tells us: the long-run split came out two ways. Balancing flow in and out of a state gave $w = b/(a+b) = 4/5$ directly. Weighting how often the jump chain visits a state by how long it stays gave the same $4/5$. The first method scales to many states as a system of balance equations; the second explains *why* those equations are right.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $I$ is a countable state space, $Q = (q_{ij})$ a stable conservative $Q$-matrix with jump matrix $\Pi = (\pi_{ij})$ and exit rates $q_i = -q_{ii}$ [37.05.08], $(X_t)_{t \geq 0}$ the associated minimal right-continuous chain with explosion time $\zeta$, and $(P(t))_{t \geq 0}$ its transition semigroup, $P_{ij}(t) = \mathbb{P}_i(X_t = j)$, satisfying $P(0) = I_d$, $P(s+t) = P(s)P(t)$, and the backward/forward equations of [37.05.09]. The embedded jump chain $(Y_n)_{n \geq 0}$ is the discrete-time chain with matrix $\Pi$; its recurrence, invariant measures, and positive/null classification are as in [37.05.02] and [37.05.05]. We assume $Q$ irreducible (equivalently $\Pi$ irreducible) and, unless stated, non-explosive, so $\zeta = \infty$ a.s. and $P(t)$ is honest.

**Definition (recurrence and transience).** A state $i$ is *recurrent* for the continuous-time chain if $\mathbb{P}_i(\{t : X_t = i\} \text{ is unbounded}) = 1$, and *transient* if this probability is $0$. For a non-explosive irreducible chain, $i$ is recurrent iff $i$ is recurrent for the jump chain $\Pi$; recurrence and transience are class properties shared by all states. The return time to $i$ is $T_i := \inf\{t \geq J_1 : X_t = i\}$, where $J_1$ is the first jump time. A recurrent state is *positive recurrent* if $\mathbb{E}_i[T_i] < \infty$ and *null recurrent* if $\mathbb{E}_i[T_i] = \infty$.

**Definition (invariant measure and invariant distribution).** A measure $\mu = (\mu_i)_{i \in I}$, $\mu_i \geq 0$, is *invariant* for $Q$ if
$$\mu Q = 0, \qquad \text{i.e.} \quad \sum_{i \in I} \mu_i\, q_{ij} = 0 \ \text{ for every } j \in I,$$
equivalently $-\mu_j q_j + \sum_{i \neq j} \mu_i q_{ij} = 0$ (outflow rate $=$ inflow rate at $j$). An *invariant distribution* $\pi$ is an invariant measure that is a probability vector, $\sum_i \pi_i = 1$. For a non-explosive chain, $\pi Q = 0$ with $\sum_i \pi_i = 1$ holds iff $\pi P(t) = \pi$ for all $t \geq 0$ (time-stationarity of the semigroup).

**Definition (the jump-chain bridge).** If $\mu$ is invariant for $Q$, then $\nu_i := \mu_i q_i$ is invariant for the jump chain, $\nu \Pi = \nu$; conversely if $\nu \Pi = \nu$ then $\mu_i := \nu_i / q_i$ satisfies $\mu Q = 0$. Thus continuous-time invariance weights the jump-chain invariant measure by the mean holding time $1/q_i$:
$$\pi_i = \frac{\nu_i / q_i}{\sum_k \nu_k / q_k}, \qquad \nu = \nu\Pi.$$

**Definition (detailed balance / reversibility).** The chain is in *detailed balance* with respect to a measure $\mu$ if
$$\mu_i\, q_{ij} = \mu_j\, q_{ji} \qquad \text{for all } i, j \in I.$$
A chain admitting a detailed-balance probability distribution $\pi$ is *reversible*: the stationary chain $(X_t)_{0 \leq t \leq T}$ has the same law as its time-reversal $(X_{T-t})_{0 \leq t \leq T}$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *The continuous-time invariant is not the jump-chain invariant.* $\pi Q = 0$ and $\nu \Pi = \nu$ are different equations; they agree only when all exit rates $q_i$ are equal. Forgetting the $1/q_i$ holding-time weight is the most common error: a state the jump chain visits often but leaves quickly carries less clock-time than its visit count suggests.

- *Positive recurrence of the chain is not positive recurrence of the jump chain.* The continuous-time chain is positive recurrent iff $Q$ admits an invariant *distribution*, which can hold even when the jump chain is null recurrent (slow-but-long visits), and can fail when the jump chain is positive recurrent (fast visits with $\sum_k \nu_k/q_k = \infty$). The holding times decouple the two notions.

- *No aperiodicity is required for convergence.* In discrete time, periodicity blocks $p^{(n)}_{ij} \to \pi_j$ [37.05.06]. In continuous time the exponential holding times have no period, so $P_{ij}(t) \to \pi_j$ holds for every irreducible non-explosive positive-recurrent chain with no parity hypothesis.

- *Detailed balance is sufficient but not necessary for invariance.* Summing $\mu_i q_{ij} = \mu_j q_{ji}$ over $i$ gives $\sum_i \mu_i q_{ij} = \mu_j \sum_i q_{ji}$; using $\sum_{i} q_{ji} = 0$ (conservativity, with the $i=j$ term) yields $(\mu Q)_j = 0$. The converse fails: a directed cycle $1 \to 2 \to 3 \to 1$ at equal rates has a uniform invariant distribution but no detailed-balance measure, since reverse rates vanish.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (invariant distribution from the jump chain; convergence to equilibrium).** Let $Q$ be irreducible and non-explosive on $I$. Then $Q$ has an invariant distribution $\pi$ iff the chain is positive recurrent, in which case $\pi$ is unique and given by
$$\pi_i = \frac{1}{q_i\, \mathbb{E}_i[T_i]} > 0, \qquad i \in I, \tag{$\ast$}$$
equivalently $\pi_i = (\nu_i / q_i) / \sum_k (\nu_k / q_k)$ where $\nu$ is the (essentially unique) invariant measure of the jump chain $\Pi$. Moreover, for every $i, j$ and every initial distribution $\lambda$,
$$P_{ij}(t) \to \pi_j \quad\text{and}\quad \|\lambda P(t) - \pi\|_{\mathrm{TV}} \to 0 \qquad (t \to \infty),$$
with no aperiodicity hypothesis. [ref: TODO_REF Norris 1997 §3.5-3.8]

*Proof.* **Existence and the formula.** Suppose the chain is positive recurrent. The jump chain $\Pi$ is recurrent (recurrence is shared, since the chain visits a state unboundedly often in time iff $Y_n$ returns infinitely often), so by [37.05.05] it has an invariant measure $\nu$, unique up to scale, with $\nu_i > 0$. Set $\mu_i = \nu_i / q_i$. Then for each $j$,
$$(\mu Q)_j = \sum_i \mu_i q_{ij} = -\mu_j q_j + \sum_{i \neq j} \frac{\nu_i}{q_i} q_i \pi_{ij} = -\nu_j + \sum_{i \neq j} \nu_i \pi_{ij} = -\nu_j + (\nu \Pi)_j = 0,$$
using $q_{ij} = q_i \pi_{ij}$ for $i \neq j$, $\mu_j q_j = \nu_j$, and $\pi_{jj} = 0$. So $\mu Q = 0$. The total mass is $\sum_i \mu_i = \sum_i \nu_i / q_i$, which is finite iff the chain is positive recurrent: by the renewal identity (Proposition 2 below) $\mathbb{E}_i[T_i] = (\sum_k \nu_k / q_k)/\nu_i$ when $\nu$ is normalized so that $\nu_i$ counts mean jump-chain visits to $k$ per excursion from $i$, whence $\sum_k \mu_k = \nu_i \mathbb{E}_i[T_i]$ and $\pi_i = \mu_i / \sum_k \mu_k = (\nu_i/q_i)/(\nu_i \mathbb{E}_i[T_i]) = 1/(q_i \mathbb{E}_i[T_i])$, which is ($\ast$). Normalizing $\mu$ to total mass $1$ gives the invariant distribution $\pi$. Uniqueness follows from uniqueness of $\nu$ up to scale.

Conversely, if $Q$ has an invariant distribution $\pi$, then $\nu_i = \pi_i q_i$ is an invariant measure for $\Pi$ with finite weighted mass $\sum_i \nu_i / q_i = \sum_i \pi_i = 1 < \infty$, forcing positive recurrence.

**Convergence to equilibrium.** Fix a small $h > 0$ and consider the $h$-skeleton, the discrete-time chain with one-step matrix $P(h)$. Because $P(h) = e^{hQ}$ has strictly positive diagonal (a state can stay put over $[0,h]$ with probability $\geq e^{-q_i h} > 0$) and is irreducible (irreducibility of $Q$ gives $P_{ij}(h) > 0$ for all $i, j$ when the chain is irreducible and non-explosive, as a positive holding-then-jumping path has positive density), the skeleton is irreducible and aperiodic. Its invariant distribution is $\pi$, since $\pi P(h) = \pi$ from $\pi Q = 0$. By the discrete-time convergence theorem [37.05.06], $P_{ij}(nh) = (P(h)^n)_{ij} \to \pi_j$ as $n \to \infty$. To pass from the skeleton to continuous $t$, note $t \mapsto P_{ij}(t)$ is uniformly continuous (it is Lipschitz with constant $\leq 2 q_j$ on bounded-rate skeleton steps, and in general uniformly continuous by the semigroup contraction $\|\mu P(s) - \nu P(s)\|_{\mathrm{TV}} \leq \|\mu - \nu\|_{\mathrm{TV}}$ inherited from each row being a distribution), so a sequence along the lattice $nh$ that converges forces the continuous limit; varying $h$ over a rationally independent pair removes any lattice gap. Hence $P_{ij}(t) \to \pi_j$, and $\|\lambda P(t) - \pi\|_{\mathrm{TV}} \to 0$ by dominated convergence over the countable sum. The exponential holding times supply the aperiodicity for free: $P(h)$ has positive diagonal for every $h > 0$, so no periodicity obstruction can arise. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem builds toward the entire equilibrium theory of continuous-time Markov processes and appears again in queueing theory, where $\pi$ is the stationary queue-length law and ($\ast$) is the mean-busy-cycle formula. The foundational reason the continuous-time invariant differs from the jump-chain invariant is the holding-time weight $1/q_i$: this is exactly the bridge $\pi_i = (\nu_i/q_i)/\sum_k(\nu_k/q_k)$ that converts visit-frequency into clock-fraction, and it generalises the discrete reciprocal-return-time formula $\pi_i = 1/\mathbb{E}_i[T_i]$ of [37.05.05] by inserting the exit rate. The convergence half is dual to the discrete coupling theorem [37.05.06] but cleaner, because the random holding times erase periodicity; putting these together, the snapshot law $P_{ij}(t)$ and the invariant $\pi$ are linked by the same positive-recurrence input that the next result reads pathwise, and the central insight is that one continuous-time chain carries two stationary objects — the time-stationary $\pi$ with $\pi Q = 0$ and the jump-stationary $\nu$ with $\nu \Pi = \nu$ — bridged by the mean holding time.

Which equation does the *continuous-time* invariant distribution satisfy?

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

A birth-death chain on $\{0, 1, 2, \dots\}$ has birth rates $q_{n,n+1} = \lambda$ and death rates $q_{n,n-1} = \mu$ (with $\lambda < \mu$). Find the invariant distribution via detailed balance and identify it.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Detailed balance reads $\pi_n \lambda = \pi_{n+1}\mu$; iterate from $\pi_0$ and normalize a geometric series.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Detailed balance between $n$ and $n+1$ is $\pi_n q_{n,n+1} = \pi_{n+1} q_{n+1,n}$, i.e. $\pi_n \lambda = \pi_{n+1}\mu$, so $\pi_{n+1} = (\lambda/\mu)\pi_n$ and $\pi_n = (\lambda/\mu)^n \pi_0$. Writing $\rho = \lambda/\mu < 1$, normalization $\sum_n \rho^n \pi_0 = \pi_0/(1-\rho) = 1$ gives $\pi_0 = 1 - \rho$, so $\pi_n = (1-\rho)\rho^n$, the geometric distribution — the stationary queue length of an M/M/1 queue. Since detailed balance holds, the chain is reversible. For $\lambda \geq \mu$ the series diverges and no invariant distribution exists (the queue is transient or null recurrent).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that any measure $\mu$ satisfying detailed balance $\mu_i q_{ij} = \mu_j q_{ji}$ for all $i, j$ is invariant, $\mu Q = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Sum the detailed-balance identity over $i$ and use conservativity $\sum_i q_{ji} = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix $j$ and sum $\mu_i q_{ij} = \mu_j q_{ji}$ over all $i$ (including $i = j$, where both sides are $\mu_j q_{jj}$):
$$\sum_i \mu_i q_{ij} = \mu_j \sum_i q_{ji}.$$
By conservativity each row of $Q$ sums to zero, $\sum_i q_{ji} = 0$, so the right side vanishes and $(\mu Q)_j = \sum_i \mu_i q_{ij} = 0$. As $j$ was arbitrary, $\mu Q = 0$, so $\mu$ is invariant. Detailed balance is the pairwise (reversible) refinement of the global balance $\mu Q = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

For an irreducible non-explosive chain with invariant distribution $\pi$, verify directly that $\pi P(t) = \pi$ for all $t \geq 0$, using $\pi Q = 0$ and $P(t) = e^{tQ}$ (assume $Q$ bounded).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $\pi e^{tQ}$ as a series and apply $\pi Q = 0$ term by term.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For bounded $Q$, $P(t) = e^{tQ} = \sum_{n \geq 0} t^n Q^n / n!$ converges in operator norm [37.05.09]. Then
$$\pi P(t) = \pi \sum_{n \geq 0} \frac{t^n Q^n}{n!} = \pi I_d + \sum_{n \geq 1} \frac{t^n}{n!} (\pi Q) Q^{n-1} = \pi,$$
since $\pi Q = 0$ kills every term with $n \geq 1$. Thus $\pi$ is time-stationary for the whole semigroup, not merely fixed by the generator. The differentiated form recovers $\pi Q = 0$ as the infinitesimal version of $\pi P(t) = \pi$: $\frac{d}{dt}\big|_{0}\pi P(t) = \pi Q = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

State and prove the continuous-time ergodic theorem for an irreducible positive-recurrent non-explosive chain: for bounded $f : I \to \mathbb{R}$,
$$\frac{1}{t}\int_0^t f(X_s)\, ds \to \sum_i \pi_i f(i) \quad \text{a.s. as } t \to \infty.$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply renewal-reward to the i.i.d. excursions of $X$ between successive visits to a fixed state $i$; the reward over a cycle is $\int f(X_s)\,ds$ across that excursion, and the cycle length has mean $\mathbb{E}_i[T_i]$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix a reference state $i$ and let $0 = R_0 < R_1 < R_2 < \cdots$ be the successive return times to $i$, so the excursions $(X_s)_{R_{k-1} \leq s < R_k}$ are i.i.d. by the strong Markov property at the return times, with cycle length $L_k = R_k - R_{k-1}$ of mean $\mathbb{E}_i[T_i] = 1/(q_i \pi_i)$ from ($\ast$). The reward over cycle $k$ is $V_k = \int_{R_{k-1}}^{R_k} f(X_s)\,ds$; these are i.i.d. with mean $\mathbb{E}_i[V_1] = \mathbb{E}_i\big[\int_0^{T_i} f(X_s)\,ds\big] = \sum_j f(j)\,\mathbb{E}_i[\text{time in } j \text{ before } T_i] = \sum_j f(j)\,\pi_j\,\mathbb{E}_i[T_i]$, the last step because the expected time spent in $j$ per excursion from $i$ is $\pi_j \mathbb{E}_i[T_i]$ (the invariant law is exactly the long-run occupation fraction). By the strong law of large numbers, $\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n V_k \to \mathbb{E}_i[V_1]$ and $\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=1}^n L_k \to \mathbb{E}_i[T_i]$ a.s. Let $N(t) = \max\{n : R_n \leq t\}$, the number of completed cycles by time $t$; positive recurrence gives $N(t) \to \infty$ a.s. The renewal-reward ratio then gives
$$\frac{1}{t}\int_0^t f(X_s)\,ds = \frac{\sum_{k \leq N(t)} V_k + o(R_{N(t)})}{t} \to \frac{\mathbb{E}_i[V_1]}{\mathbb{E}_i[T_i]} = \sum_j \pi_j f(j),$$
the boundary partial-cycle term vanishing in the ratio because $R_{N(t)}/t \to 1$ a.s. and $f$ is bounded. This is the pathwise twin of the distributional limit $P_{ij}(t) \to \pi_j$.

</details>

</aside>

Give an irreducible chain that is positive recurrent in continuous time but whose jump chain is *null* recurrent, exhibiting that the holding times decouple the two notions. (Construct the rates explicitly.)

Take a jump chain that is null recurrent (e.g. simple symmetric random walk on $\mathbb{Z}$ is null recurrent but on $\mathbb{Z}$ is not — use instead a null-recurrent jump chain on $\mathbb{N}$) and choose exit rates $q_i$ so that $\sum_i \nu_i / q_i < \infty$ even though $\sum_i \nu_i = \infty$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let the jump chain $\Pi$ be a null-recurrent irreducible chain on $I = \{0,1,2,\dots\}$ with invariant measure $\nu_i$ satisfying $\nu_i > 0$ and $\sum_i \nu_i = \infty$ (null recurrence of $\Pi$ means its invariant measure has infinite total mass). For instance a reflecting random walk tuned so that $\nu_i \sim 1$ for all $i$, giving $\sum_i \nu_i = \infty$. Now choose exit rates $q_i = 2^i$. The continuous-time invariant measure is $\mu_i = \nu_i / q_i \sim 2^{-i}$, with
$$\sum_i \mu_i = \sum_i \nu_i / q_i \sim \sum_i 2^{-i} = 2 < \infty,$$
so $\mu$ normalizes to an invariant *distribution* $\pi$ and the continuous-time chain is positive recurrent, while $\Pi$ remains null recurrent. The fast-growing exit rates make far-out states held for vanishingly short times, so they contribute finite total clock-time despite being visited with infinite aggregate frequency. (One checks non-explosion separately; bounding the chain to keep $\sum 1/q_i$ along any path infinite, e.g. by a confining drift in $\Pi$, secures it.) The construction shows positive recurrence of $Q$ is governed by $\sum_i \nu_i / q_i$, a holding-time-weighted sum that the jump-chain classification alone cannot see.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

The long-run theory rests on four facts: recurrence is inherited from the jump chain but positive recurrence is a holding-time-weighted refinement; the invariant distribution solves $\pi Q = 0$ and is the mean-holding-time reweighting of the jump-chain invariant measure; convergence to equilibrium holds with no periodicity hypothesis because exponential holding times carry no phase; and detailed balance is the reversible special case in which the invariant measure is read off pairwise.

**Theorem 1 (recurrence dichotomy and the invariant measure).** Let $Q$ be irreducible and non-explosive. The chain is recurrent iff its jump chain $\Pi$ is recurrent, and then $Q$ has an invariant measure $\mu$, unique up to scalar, with $\mu_i = \nu_i / q_i > 0$ where $\nu \Pi = \nu$. The chain is positive recurrent iff $\sum_i \mu_i < \infty$, iff an invariant *distribution* exists, iff $\mathbb{E}_i[T_i] < \infty$ for one (equivalently every) $i$; then $\pi_i = 1/(q_i \mathbb{E}_i[T_i])$. Positive and null recurrence are class properties, but the continuous-time class can differ from the jump-chain class: $\Pi$ positive recurrent with $\sum_i \nu_i/q_i = \infty$ gives a null-recurrent $Q$, and $\Pi$ null recurrent with $\sum_i \nu_i/q_i < \infty$ gives a positive-recurrent $Q$.

**Theorem 2 (the two stationary measures and Asmussen's bridge).** An irreducible recurrent $Q$ carries two distinct stationary objects: the *time-stationary* measure $\mu$ with $\mu Q = 0$, governing $\mu P(t) = \mu$ and the long-run clock-occupation $\frac1t\int_0^t \mathbf 1_{X_s = i}\,ds \to \mu_i / \sum_k \mu_k$; and the *jump-stationary* measure $\nu$ with $\nu \Pi = \nu$, governing the long-run jump-count frequency $\frac1n \#\{m < n : Y_m = i\} \to \nu_i / \sum_k \nu_k$. They are related by $\nu_i = \mu_i q_i$, the *rate-conversion identity* [ref: TODO_REF Asmussen 2003]: visit-frequency times holding-time is clock-fraction. The PASTA principle and the rate-conservation law of queueing theory are corollaries — a Poisson arrival sees the time-stationary law $\mu$, not the embedded jump law $\nu$.

**Theorem 3 (convergence with no aperiodicity; spectral form on finite $I$).** For irreducible non-explosive positive-recurrent $Q$, $P_{ij}(t) \to \pi_j$ for all $i, j$, and $\|\lambda P(t) - \pi\|_{\mathrm{TV}} \to 0$ for every $\lambda$, with no parity hypothesis. On a finite state space, $P(t) = e^{tQ} = \sum_r e^{\mu_r t} v_r u_r^\top$ [37.05.09] with $\mu_0 = 0$ supplying the rank-one stationary projection $\mathbf 1 \pi^\top$ and the remaining eigenvalues having strictly negative real part for an irreducible $Q$, so convergence is exponential with rate the spectral gap $\min_{r \geq 1}|\mathrm{Re}\,\mu_r|$. There is no imaginary-axis obstruction analogous to the discrete unit circle's roots of unity: irreducibility alone forces $0$ to be a simple eigenvalue and all others into the open left half-plane.

**Theorem 4 (detailed balance, reversibility, and the cycle condition).** $Q$ admits a detailed-balance measure $\mu$ ($\mu_i q_{ij} = \mu_j q_{ji}$) iff Kolmogorov's *cycle condition* holds: for every finite cycle $i_0, i_1, \dots, i_n = i_0$,
$$\prod_{k=0}^{n-1} q_{i_k i_{k+1}} = \prod_{k=0}^{n-1} q_{i_{k+1} i_k}.$$
When it holds, $\mu$ is invariant (Exercise 4) and the stationary chain is reversible: $(X_t)_{0 \leq t \leq T} \stackrel{d}{=} (X_{T-t})_{0 \leq t \leq T}$. Reversibility makes $Q$ self-adjoint on $\ell^2(\pi)$, so its spectrum is real, the convergence rate is the spectral gap of a symmetric operator, and the powerful variational (Dirichlet-form) machinery applies. Birth-death chains are automatically reversible (every cycle is a back-and-forth, so the cycle condition is vacuous), which is why their invariant law ($\pi_n = \pi_0 \prod_{k<n} \lambda_k/\mu_{k+1}$) is computed by inspection.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason continuous-time long-run behaviour splits cleanly is the embedding: the chain is the jump chain $\Pi$ run on a clock with exponential holding times, and every long-run quantity factors into a jump-chain part and a holding-time part. This is exactly why recurrence is inherited from $\Pi$ while positive recurrence is the holding-time-weighted refinement $\sum_i \nu_i / q_i < \infty$, and the bridge $\nu_i = \mu_i q_i$ generalises the discrete reciprocal-return-time formula by inserting the exit rate — visit-frequency is dual to clock-fraction through the mean holding time. Putting these together, the invariant distribution $\pi Q = 0$, the convergence $P_{ij}(t) \to \pi_j$, and the ergodic average $\frac1t\int_0^t f(X_s)ds \to \sum_i \pi_i f(i)$ are three readings of the single positive-recurrence input $\pi_i = 1/(q_i \mathbb{E}_i[T_i])$: the first is its existence, the second its distributional pull, the third its pathwise law of large numbers via renewal-reward over excursions. The central insight is that the random clock buys two things the discrete theory cannot have for free — periodicity disappears, so convergence needs no aperiodicity, and reversibility, when Kolmogorov's cycle condition holds, makes $Q$ self-adjoint on $\ell^2(\pi)$, so the same invariant $\pi$ that the renewal argument produces also organises the spectral theory, and the bridge between the analytic semigroup $e^{tQ}$ of [37.05.09] and the pathwise excursion structure of [37.05.08] is complete.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (invariance is equivalent to time-stationarity).** For a non-explosive chain, a probability vector $\pi$ satisfies $\pi Q = 0$ iff $\pi P(t) = \pi$ for all $t \geq 0$.

*Proof.* If $\pi P(t) = \pi$ for all $t$, differentiate at $t = 0$: $\frac{d}{dt}\big|_0 \pi P(t) = \pi P'(0) = \pi Q = 0$, the derivative existing by [37.05.09]. Conversely, suppose $\pi Q = 0$. The forward equation [37.05.09] gives $\frac{d}{dt}(\pi P(t)) = \pi P'(t) = \pi P(t) Q$ (using $P'(t) = P(t)Q$, valid for the honest non-explosive chain). Set $g(t) = \pi P(t)$, a probability vector for each $t$; then $g'(t) = g(t) Q$ with $g(0) = \pi$. The constant function $h(t) \equiv \pi$ also solves $h' = hQ = 0 = \pi Q$ with $h(0) = \pi$. By uniqueness of the bounded solution of the forward system with given initial data (the $\ell^1$-contraction estimate $\|g(t) - h(t)\|_1 \leq \int_0^t \|(g-h)(s)Q\|_1 ds$ with Grönwall when $Q$ is bounded, and the minimal-solution uniqueness of [37.05.09] in general), $g \equiv h$, so $\pi P(t) = \pi$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (the renewal identity for the return time).** For an irreducible positive-recurrent chain with jump-chain invariant measure $\nu$ normalized by $\nu_i = 1$ (so $\nu_k = \mathbb{E}_i[\text{jump-chain visits to } k \text{ before returning to } i]$), the mean return time is $\mathbb{E}_i[T_i] = \sum_k \nu_k / q_k$, and hence $\pi_i = 1/(q_i \mathbb{E}_i[T_i])$.

*Proof.* Decompose the excursion from $i$ back to $i$ by the states the jump chain visits. Over one excursion the chain visits state $k$ a mean number $\nu_k$ of times (the regeneration formula for the jump chain's invariant measure [37.05.05]), and each visit lasts an independent $\mathrm{Exp}(q_k)$ holding time of mean $1/q_k$, independent of the visit count by the strong Markov property [37.05.08]. By Wald's identity the total expected time of the excursion is $\mathbb{E}_i[T_i] = \sum_k \nu_k \cdot \frac{1}{q_k} = \sum_k \nu_k / q_k$. The invariant distribution is $\pi_k = \mu_k / \sum_j \mu_j$ with $\mu_k = \nu_k / q_k$, so $\pi_i = (\nu_i / q_i)/\sum_j (\nu_j/q_j) = (1/q_i)/\mathbb{E}_i[T_i]$, giving $\pi_i = 1/(q_i \mathbb{E}_i[T_i])$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (the $h$-skeleton is irreducible and aperiodic).** If $Q$ is irreducible and non-explosive, then for every $h > 0$ the matrix $P(h)$ is irreducible and aperiodic, with invariant distribution $\pi$ whenever $\pi Q = 0$.

*Proof.* Irreducibility of $Q$ gives, for each ordered pair $(i,j)$, a path $i = k_0 \to k_1 \to \cdots \to k_m = j$ of positive jump probabilities; the event of taking these jumps within $[0,h]$ with all holding times summing to less than $h$ has positive probability, so $P_{ij}(h) > 0$. Hence $P(h)$ is irreducible. For aperiodicity, $P_{ii}(h) \geq \mathbb{P}_i(J_1 > h) = e^{-q_i h} > 0$, a positive self-loop, so the period of $i$ in $P(h)$ divides $1$ and equals $1$. Finally $\pi P(h) = \pi e^{hQ}$ when $Q$ is bounded equals $\pi$ by Proposition 1's series argument; in the unbounded case $\pi P(h) = \pi$ follows from $\pi P(t) = \pi$ (Proposition 1). $\square$

**Proposition 4 (convergence to equilibrium).** For irreducible non-explosive positive-recurrent $Q$ with invariant $\pi$, $P_{ij}(t) \to \pi_j$ for all $i, j$ and $\|\lambda P(t) - \pi\|_{\mathrm{TV}} \to 0$ for every $\lambda$.

*Proof.* By Proposition 3 the skeleton $P(h)$ is irreducible aperiodic with invariant $\pi$, so the discrete convergence theorem [37.05.06] gives $P_{ij}(nh) \to \pi_j$ as $n \to \infty$, for each fixed $h$. The map $t \mapsto P_{ij}(t)$ is uniformly continuous: for $s \leq t$, $|P_{ij}(t) - P_{ij}(s)| = |(P(s)(P(t-s) - I_d))_{ij}| \leq \sum_k P_{ik}(s)|P_{kj}(t-s) - \delta_{kj}|$, and $\sum_k P_{ik}(s)\,\mathbb{P}(\text{a jump in } [0, t-s]) \leq 1 - e^{-\sup}$ tends to $0$ uniformly as $t - s \downarrow 0$ on any set where exit rates are bounded; in general uniform continuity follows from the total-variation contraction $\|\mu P(\delta) - \mu\|_{\mathrm{TV}} \to 0$ as $\delta \downarrow 0$ for each row. Given $\varepsilon$, pick $h$ with the modulus of continuity below $\varepsilon$ over $[0,h]$ and $n_0$ with $|P_{ij}(nh) - \pi_j| < \varepsilon$ for $n \geq n_0$; then for $t \in [nh, (n+1)h]$, $|P_{ij}(t) - \pi_j| < 2\varepsilon$. Hence $P_{ij}(t) \to \pi_j$. Summing, $\|\lambda P(t) - \pi\|_{\mathrm{TV}} = \frac12\sum_j |\sum_i \lambda_i P_{ij}(t) - \pi_j| \to 0$ by dominated convergence (each term $\to 0$, dominated by $\pi_j + \sum_i \lambda_i P_{ij}(t) \leq \pi_j + (\lambda P(t))_j$ summable to $2$). $\square$

**Proposition 5 (continuous-time ergodic theorem).** For irreducible positive-recurrent non-explosive $Q$ and bounded $f : I \to \mathbb{R}$, $\frac1t\int_0^t f(X_s)\,ds \to \sum_i \pi_i f(i)$ a.s.

*Proof.* As in Exercise 7, the successive returns to a fixed state $i$ cut the path into i.i.d. excursions by the strong Markov property [37.05.08]. The cycle length has mean $\mathbb{E}_i[T_i] < \infty$ (positive recurrence) and the reward $V = \int_0^{T_i} f(X_s)\,ds$ has mean $\mathbb{E}_i[V] = \sum_j f(j)\,\mathbb{E}_i[\text{time in } j \text{ per cycle}] = \sum_j f(j)\,\pi_j \mathbb{E}_i[T_i]$, the occupation identity from Proposition 2. The renewal-reward theorem gives $\frac1t\int_0^t f(X_s)\,ds \to \mathbb{E}_i[V]/\mathbb{E}_i[T_i] = \sum_j \pi_j f(j)$ a.s., the partial last cycle negligible because $f$ is bounded and $R_{N(t)}/t \to 1$. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (detailed balance characterises reversibility).** A probability $\pi$ with $\pi_i > 0$ satisfies detailed balance $\pi_i q_{ij} = \pi_j q_{ji}$ for all $i, j$ iff the stationary chain $(X_t)_{0 \leq t \leq T}$ has the same finite-dimensional laws as its time-reversal $(X_{T-t})_{0 \leq t \leq T}$.

*Proof.* The reversed stationary process is Markov with generator $\hat q_{ij} = \pi_j q_{ji}/\pi_i$ (the reversed rates: $\hat q_{ij}\pi_i = \pi_j q_{ji}$ is the stationary flux $j \to i$ relabelled). The reversal equals the forward chain in law iff $\hat q_{ij} = q_{ij}$ for all $i, j$, i.e. $\pi_j q_{ji}/\pi_i = q_{ij}$, i.e. $\pi_i q_{ij} = \pi_j q_{ji}$ — exactly detailed balance. (One checks $\hat Q$ is a conservative $Q$-matrix: $\sum_j \hat q_{ij} = \sum_j \pi_j q_{ji}/\pi_i = (\pi Q)_i/\pi_i \cdot(-1)$-corrected $= 0$ since $\pi Q = 0$, using that $\pi$ is invariant, which detailed balance guarantees by Exercise 4.) Detailed balance further makes $Q$ self-adjoint on $\ell^2(\pi)$: $\langle Qf, g\rangle_\pi = \sum_{i,j}\pi_i q_{ij} f_j g_i = \sum_{i,j}\pi_j q_{ji} f_j g_i = \langle f, Qg\rangle_\pi$, so the spectrum of $Q$ is real and the convergence rate is the spectral gap of a symmetric operator. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The Q-matrix, jump chain, and holding-time construction [37.05.08] is the substrate of every result here: recurrence is inherited from the jump chain $\Pi$, the mean holding time $1/q_i$ is the weight converting the jump-stationary $\nu$ into the time-stationary $\mu$, and the i.i.d. excursion structure that the strong Markov property at return times produces is exactly what powers the renewal-reward proof of the ergodic theorem — the continuous-time return time $T_i$ and its finiteness are the positive-recurrence datum read pathwise.

- The transition semigroup and Kolmogorov equations [37.05.09] supply the analytic side: $\pi Q = 0$ is the infinitesimal form of time-stationarity $\pi P(t) = \pi$, the forward equation gives the uniqueness argument identifying the two, and the matrix exponential $P(t) = e^{tQ}$ on finite state spaces furnishes the spectral form $\sum_r e^{\mu_r t}v_r u_r^\top$ whose $\mu_0 = 0$ term is the stationary projection and whose gap is the exponential convergence rate.

- The discrete invariant-measure and positive/null-recurrence theory [37.05.05] is the template this unit reweights: the reciprocal-return-time formula $\pi_i = 1/\mathbb{E}_i[T_i]$ becomes $\pi_i = 1/(q_i\mathbb{E}_i[T_i])$, the regeneration construction of the jump-chain invariant measure $\nu$ is imported wholesale, and the positive/null dichotomy is refined by the holding-time-weighted sum $\sum_i \nu_i/q_i$ that can flip the classification between the chain and its jump chain.

- The discrete convergence-to-equilibrium and coupling theory [37.05.06] is applied to the $h$-skeleton to drive the continuous-time convergence, but with the periodicity hypothesis removed: the exponential holding times make every skeleton $P(h)$ aperiodic, so the coupling theorem's aperiodicity requirement is automatically met, and the meeting-time tail there becomes the spectral-gap decay here.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The systematic theory of stationary and limiting distributions for continuous-time chains grew from Kolmogorov's analytic foundations of the 1930s, but the reversibility strand has a sharper origin. Andrei N. Kolmogorov's 1936 *Mathematische Annalen* note on the theory of Markov chains [ref: TODO_REF Kolmogorov 1936] isolated the condition under which a chain's stationary process is statistically indistinguishable from its time-reversal, proving that reversibility is equivalent to the cycle condition on transition rates now called Kolmogorov's criterion. This converted a probabilistic symmetry into a checkable algebraic identity on the rate matrix.

The probabilistic computation of the invariant distribution through the embedded jump chain and the holding times — the bridge $\pi_i = (\nu_i/q_i)/\sum_k(\nu_k/q_k)$ and the return-time formula $\pi_i = 1/(q_i\mathbb{E}_i[T_i])$ — is the form given in Norris [ref: TODO_REF Norris 1997], following the renewal-theoretic tradition of Feller and Chung. The distinction between the time-stationary measure $\mu$ with $\mu Q = 0$ and the jump-stationary measure $\nu$ with $\nu\Pi = \nu$, and its consequences for queueing networks (PASTA, rate conservation), is developed systematically by Asmussen [ref: TODO_REF Asmussen 2003]. Frank Kelly's monograph on reversibility and stochastic networks [ref: TODO_REF Kelly 1979] made detailed balance the organizing principle for product-form equilibrium distributions of interacting queueing systems, where the cycle condition's failure or success determines whether a network has a tractable stationary law.
