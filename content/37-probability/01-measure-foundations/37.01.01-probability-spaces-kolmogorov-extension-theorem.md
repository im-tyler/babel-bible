## Intuition [Beginner]

Probability begins with a bookkeeping question: before any experiment runs, what could possibly happen, and how should we weigh each possibility? A probability space is the answer written down carefully. It has three parts. The first is a list of every outcome the experiment could produce, called the sample space. The second is a collection of questions we are allowed to ask about the outcome, called events, where each event is a bundle of outcomes. The third is a rule that assigns each event a weight between zero and one, the probability, with the whole sample space weighing exactly one.

Think of rolling a die. The sample space is the six faces. An event might be "the result is even," which bundles together the faces two, four, and six. The weight rule gives this event the value one half for a fair die. The two requirements that make this honest bookkeeping are that the weight of an impossible event is zero, the weight of the certain event is one, and the weight of a bundle made from separate non-overlapping events is the sum of their individual weights.

The subtle part is what happens with infinitely many outcomes. If you flip a coin forever, a single outcome is an entire endless sequence of heads and tails, and there are uncountably many such sequences. You cannot weigh each one individually and add them up. Instead you only ever directly weigh events that depend on finitely many flips at a time, such as "the first three flips are heads." These finite-window events are called cylinder events, because they pin down a few coordinates and leave the rest free.

The Kolmogorov extension theorem is the promise that this works. If you specify the weights for every finite window in a way that does not contradict itself, then there is one and only one weight rule on the full infinite space of sequences that agrees with all of your finite-window weights at once. The non-contradiction requirement is called consistency: the weights you assign to the first three flips must match what you get from the weights on the first four flips after ignoring the fourth.

The one-sentence takeaway: a probability space is a sample space plus a list of askable events plus a weight rule summing to one, and the Kolmogorov extension theorem builds the weight rule on an infinite space out of consistent weights on finite windows.

The picture behind the extension theorem is that all these finite-window weight charts, one for each window size, fit together like nested rulers. Consistency is the statement that the nested rulers agree wherever they overlap, and the theorem says the nested rulers determine a single weight rule on the whole infinite row.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We build the probability rule for two independent fair coin flips and check it extends a third flip consistently.

Step 1. The sample space for two flips is the four patterns HH, HT, TH, TT. Each flip is fair and the two flips are independent, so each pattern has weight one quarter: the weight of a two-flip pattern is the weight of its first flip, one half, times the weight of its second flip, one half, giving one quarter.

Step 2. Check the event "first flip is heads." This event bundles HH and HT. Its weight is one quarter plus one quarter, which is one half. That matches the weight of a single heads on the first flip, as it must.

Step 3. Now widen to three flips. The eight patterns each get weight one eighth, again because each is one half times one half times one half.

Step 4. Verify consistency between the three-flip weights and the two-flip weights. Take the two-flip pattern HT. In the three-flip world it corresponds to the two patterns HTH and HTT, since the third flip is free. Their weights are one eighth plus one eighth, which is one quarter. That equals the original two-flip weight of HT.

Step 5. Read off the conclusion. The three-flip weights, when you ignore the third flip by summing over its two values, reproduce the two-flip weights exactly. The family of weights for every window size agrees on overlaps, so it is consistent.

What this tells us: the finite-window weights for independent fair coins are consistent, so the extension theorem guarantees one weight rule on the infinite space of all coin-flip sequences. That rule is the probability model for flipping a coin forever, and it is built entirely from the simple finite-window weights we just checked.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Definition (probability space).** A *probability space* is a triple $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ where $\Omega$ is a set (the *sample space*), $\mathcal{F}$ is a $\sigma$-algebra of subsets of $\Omega$ [02.07.01] (the *events*), and $P : \mathcal{F} \to [0, 1]$ is a *probability measure*: a countably additive set-function with $P(\Omega) = 1$. Countable additivity means $P(\bigsqcup_{n} A_n) = \sum_n P(A_n)$ for any countable disjoint family $A_n \in \mathcal{F}$. A probability space is a normalized measure space, so the entire integration apparatus of measure theory applies with total mass one.

**Definition (random variable and its law).** Given a measurable space $(E, \mathcal{E})$, a *random variable* with values in $E$ is a measurable map $X : (\Omega, \mathcal{F}) \to (E, \mathcal{E})$, that is, $X^{-1}(B) \in \mathcal{F}$ for every $B \in \mathcal{E}$. The *law* (or *distribution*) of $X$ is the pushforward measure $P_X = X_* P = P \circ X^{-1}$ on $(E, \mathcal{E})$, defined by $P_X(B) = P(X^{-1}(B)) = P(X \in B)$. The pushforward is a probability measure on $E$, and the change-of-variables identity $\int_E f \, dP_X = \int_\Omega (f \circ X) \, dP$ holds for every non-negative measurable $f : E \to [0, \infty]$.

**Definition (independence of $\sigma$-algebras).** Sub-$\sigma$-algebras $\mathcal{G}_1, \ldots, \mathcal{G}_n \subseteq \mathcal{F}$ are *independent* if $P(A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) = \prod_{i=1}^n P(A_i)$ for every choice $A_i \in \mathcal{G}_i$. An arbitrary family $(\mathcal{G}_i)_{i \in I}$ is independent if every finite subfamily is. Random variables $(X_i)_{i \in I}$ are independent when the generated $\sigma$-algebras $\sigma(X_i)$ are independent; equivalently the joint law of any finite subfamily is the product [02.07.07] of the marginal laws.

**Definition (cylinder $\sigma$-algebra on a product).** Let $(E_t, \mathcal{E}_t)_{t \in T}$ be a family of measurable spaces indexed by a set $T$, and let $\Omega = \prod_{t \in T} E_t$ with coordinate projections $\pi_t : \Omega \to E_t$. For a finite subset $S = \{t_1, \ldots, t_n\} \subseteq T$ write $\pi_S = (\pi_{t_1}, \ldots, \pi_{t_n}) : \Omega \to \prod_{t \in S} E_t = E_S$. A *cylinder set* with base $S$ is a set $\pi_S^{-1}(B)$ for $B \in \mathcal{E}_S = \bigotimes_{t \in S} \mathcal{E}_t$. The cylinder sets over all finite $S$ form an algebra $\mathcal{A}$. The *cylinder* (or *product*) *$\sigma$-algebra* is $\mathcal{E}_T = \bigotimes_{t \in T} \mathcal{E}_t = \sigma(\mathcal{A})$, the smallest $\sigma$-algebra making every $\pi_t$ measurable.

**Definition (finite-dimensional distributions and consistency).** A *finite-dimensional distribution* (f.d.d.) family is a collection of probability measures $\mu_S$ on $(E_S, \mathcal{E}_S)$, one for each finite $S \subseteq T$. The family is *consistent* (or *projective*) if it is stable under marginalization: whenever $S \subseteq S'$ are finite, the canonical projection $p_{S' S} : E_{S'} \to E_S$ satisfies $(p_{S' S})_* \mu_{S'} = \mu_S$. Concretely, integrating out the extra coordinates of $\mu_{S'}$ returns $\mu_S$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *The cylinder $\sigma$-algebra is much smaller than the power set.* For $T$ uncountable, every set in $\mathcal{E}_T$ depends on only countably many coordinates: if $A \in \mathcal{E}_T$ there is a countable $T_0 \subseteq T$ with $A = \pi_{T_0}^{-1}(\pi_{T_0}(A))$. Hence the singleton $\{\omega\}$ for a fixed path $\omega$ is generally *not* in $\mathcal{E}_T$, and sets like "the path is everywhere continuous" need not be measurable. This is why path-regularity is a separate construction (a modification or a measure on a smaller path space), not automatic from the extension.

- *Consistency is necessary but the topological hypothesis is also load-bearing.* A consistent f.d.d. family need not extend to a countably additive measure if the coordinate spaces are arbitrary. The cylinder pre-measure $\mu_0$ is always finitely additive on $\mathcal{A}$; countable additivity (needed for Carathéodory [02.07.02]) requires a regularity/inner-compactness input. The standard sufficient hypothesis is that each $E_t$ is a Polish space (or standard Borel). Andersen and Jessen exhibited consistent families on general spaces with no extension.

- *Pairwise independence is strictly weaker than independence.* Three events can be pairwise independent yet not mutually independent: on two fair coin flips let $A = \{$first H$\}$, $B = \{$second H$\}$, $C = \{$flips differ$\}$. Each pair is independent ($P = 1/4$) but $P(A \cap B \cap C) = 0 \neq 1/8 = P(A)P(B)P(C)$. Independence is a condition on the joint law of *every* finite subfamily, not just pairs.

- *The law lives downstream; the space is interchangeable.* Two different probability spaces can carry random variables with the same law. The pushforward $P_X$ records everything statistically observable about $X$; the underlying $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ is scaffolding. This is why the canonical space $\Omega = \prod_t E_t$ with $X_t = \pi_t$ is a universal model: any process is realized on it by the extension theorem.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Kolmogorov / Daniell extension; Kolmogorov 1933 *Grundbegriffe* §III; Daniell 1919 *Ann. of Math.* 20, 281).** Let $T$ be an index set and let each $(E_t, \mathcal{E}_t)$ be a *standard Borel space* (a Borel subset of a Polish space with its Borel $\sigma$-algebra); for concreteness take each $E_t = \mathbb{R}$ with $\mathcal{E}_t = \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R})$. Let $(\mu_S)_{S \subseteq T \text{ finite}}$ be a consistent family of finite-dimensional distributions. Then there exists a unique probability measure $P$ on the cylinder $\sigma$-algebra $(\Omega, \mathcal{E}_T)$, $\Omega = \prod_{t \in T} E_t$, such that
$$(\pi_S)_* P = \mu_S \quad \text{for every finite } S \subseteq T.$$
Equivalently, $P(\pi_S^{-1}(B)) = \mu_S(B)$ for all finite $S$ and $B \in \mathcal{E}_S$.

*Proof.* Define the cylinder pre-measure $\mu_0$ on the algebra $\mathcal{A}$ of cylinder sets by $\mu_0(\pi_S^{-1}(B)) = \mu_S(B)$.

**Step 1 (well-definedness).** A cylinder set has many representations: $\pi_S^{-1}(B) = \pi_{S'}^{-1}(B')$ whenever $S \subseteq S'$ and $B' = p_{S'S}^{-1}(B)$. Consistency gives $\mu_{S'}(B') = \mu_{S'}(p_{S'S}^{-1}(B)) = ((p_{S'S})_* \mu_{S'})(B) = \mu_S(B)$, so $\mu_0$ does not depend on the representing base. For two arbitrary representations, pass to the common refinement $S \cup S'$ and apply consistency to both. Hence $\mu_0$ is well-defined on $\mathcal{A}$, and $\mu_0(\Omega) = \mu_\emptyset(E_\emptyset) = 1$.

**Step 2 (finite additivity).** If $C_1, C_2 \in \mathcal{A}$ are disjoint, choose a finite base $S$ large enough to express both ($C_i = \pi_S^{-1}(B_i)$ with $B_1, B_2$ disjoint, by enlarging bases as in Step 1). Then $\mu_0(C_1 \sqcup C_2) = \mu_S(B_1 \sqcup B_2) = \mu_S(B_1) + \mu_S(B_2) = \mu_0(C_1) + \mu_0(C_2)$, using additivity of the single measure $\mu_S$.

**Step 3 (countable additivity on the algebra — the compactness step).** By the standard equivalence, a finitely additive probability $\mu_0$ on an algebra is countably additive if and only if it is *continuous at $\emptyset$*: for every decreasing sequence $C_1 \supseteq C_2 \supseteq \cdots$ in $\mathcal{A}$ with $\bigcap_n C_n = \emptyset$, one has $\mu_0(C_n) \to 0$. We prove the contrapositive: suppose $\mu_0(C_n) \geq \varepsilon > 0$ for all $n$; we show $\bigcap_n C_n \neq \emptyset$.

Each $C_n$ has a finite base; by enlarging we may take the bases to be an increasing sequence $S_1 \subseteq S_2 \subseteq \cdots$ with $C_n = \pi_{S_n}^{-1}(B_n)$, $B_n \in \mathcal{E}_{S_n}$. Each $E_{S_n}$ is a Polish space, so its Borel probability $\mu_{S_n}$ is *inner regular* by compact sets (tightness on Polish spaces). Choose a compact $K_n \subseteq B_n$ with $\mu_{S_n}(B_n \setminus K_n) < \varepsilon \, 2^{-n-1}$, and set $D_n = \pi_{S_n}^{-1}(K_n) \subseteq C_n$. Replacing $D_n$ by $D_1 \cap \cdots \cap D_n$ (still a compact-based cylinder, still inside $C_n$), the inclusion-exclusion estimate $\mu_0(C_n \setminus \bigcap_{k \leq n} D_k) \leq \sum_{k \leq n} \mu_0(C_k \setminus D_k) < \varepsilon/2$ gives $\mu_0(\bigcap_{k \leq n} D_k) > \varepsilon/2 > 0$, so each $\bigcap_{k \leq n} D_k$ is nonempty.

Pick $\omega^{(n)} \in \bigcap_{k \leq n} D_k$. Its projection $\pi_{S_1}(\omega^{(n)})$ lies in the compact $K_1$ for all $n$; pass to a subsequence converging in $K_1$. Diagonalize over the increasing bases $S_m$: by repeatedly extracting subsequences, obtain a single subsequence along which $\pi_{S_m}(\omega^{(n)})$ converges in $K_m$ for every $m$. The limits are coordinatewise consistent and define a point $\omega^* \in \Omega$ with $\pi_{S_m}(\omega^*) \in K_m$ for all $m$, hence $\omega^* \in D_m \subseteq C_m$ for all $m$. Thus $\bigcap_n C_n \neq \emptyset$, the desired contradiction. So $\mu_0$ is countably additive on $\mathcal{A}$.

**Step 4 (Carathéodory extension).** A countably additive probability on the algebra $\mathcal{A}$ extends, by the Carathéodory extension theorem [02.07.02], to a measure $P$ on $\sigma(\mathcal{A}) = \mathcal{E}_T$. Since $\mu_0(\Omega) = 1$, $P$ is a probability measure, and $P|_{\mathcal{A}} = \mu_0$ gives $(\pi_S)_* P = \mu_S$ for every finite $S$.

**Step 5 (uniqueness).** The cylinder sets form a $\pi$-system generating $\mathcal{E}_T$, and any two probability measures agreeing on a generating $\pi$-system agree on the $\sigma$-algebra (Dynkin's $\pi$-$\lambda$ theorem). Two measures with the same f.d.d.'s agree on all cylinders, hence coincide. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem builds toward the entire probabilistic spine and appears again in every later construction of a stochastic process from its finite-dimensional laws. The foundational reason it is the right organizing tool is that it converts an analytically intractable object — a measure on an uncountable-dimensional path space — into the elementary data of consistent finite-dimensional marginals. This is exactly the move that constructs an i.i.d. sequence (take $\mu_S = \bigotimes_{t \in S} \nu$ for a fixed marginal $\nu$, automatically consistent by Fubini-Tonelli [02.07.07]) and, more generally, any process once its f.d.d.'s are specified. The compactness argument of Step 3 generalises the tightness criterion that recurs in weak-convergence theory, and the Carathéodory backbone is exactly the one used to build Lebesgue and product measures [02.07.02], so putting these together the extension theorem is the probabilistic face of the same outer-measure machine. The central insight is that consistency of marginals plus a topological regularity hypothesis on the coordinate spaces is necessary and sufficient to manufacture a single coherent law, and this law is the canonical model on which the process realizes as the coordinate maps.

On the canonical space of three i.i.d. Bernoulli$(1/2)$ coordinates, compute $P(\pi_1 = 1, \pi_3 = 1)$ (the second coordinate is free). Give a decimal to three places.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, true-false).**

If $(\mu_S)$ is a consistent family of finite-dimensional distributions on a product of Polish spaces, the singleton event $\{\omega\}$ for a fixed path $\omega$ is always an element of the cylinder $\sigma$-algebra.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Every cylinder-$\sigma$-algebra set depends on at most countably many coordinates. How many coordinates does a singleton constrain when $T$ is uncountable?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**False.** When $T$ is uncountable, every set in $\mathcal{E}_T$ is determined by countably many coordinates, but $\{\omega\}$ constrains *all* coordinates. So the singleton is generally not measurable. The extension produces a genuine probability measure on $\mathcal{E}_T$, but it says nothing about non-cylinder sets such as fixed paths or the set of continuous paths; those require a separate construction.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the family $\mu_S = \bigotimes_{t \in S} \nu$, for a fixed probability measure $\nu$ on $\mathbb{R}$ and every finite $S \subseteq T$, is consistent. Conclude the existence of an i.i.d. sequence with marginal $\nu$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Marginalizing a product measure over one factor returns the product of the remaining factors; this is Fubini-Tonelli [02.07.07].

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $S \subseteq S'$ be finite and $p = p_{S'S}$ the projection dropping the coordinates in $S' \setminus S$. For a rectangle $B = \prod_{t \in S} B_t \in \mathcal{E}_S$,
$$(p_* \mu_{S'})(B) = \mu_{S'}\big(p^{-1}(B)\big) = \mu_{S'}\Big(\prod_{t \in S} B_t \times \prod_{t \in S' \setminus S} \mathbb{R}\Big) = \prod_{t \in S} \nu(B_t) \cdot \prod_{t \in S' \setminus S} \nu(\mathbb{R}) = \prod_{t \in S} \nu(B_t) = \mu_S(B),$$
using $\nu(\mathbb{R}) = 1$ and the product formula, which is Tonelli applied to the indicator of the rectangle. Rectangles form a generating $\pi$-system for $\mathcal{E}_S$, so $p_* \mu_{S'} = \mu_S$ on all of $\mathcal{E}_S$. The family is consistent. By the Kolmogorov extension theorem there is a unique $P$ on $\mathbb{R}^T$ with these marginals; the coordinate maps $X_t = \pi_t$ are then i.i.d. with law $\nu$, since their joint law on any finite $S$ is the product $\bigotimes_{t\in S} \nu$, which is exactly independence plus identical marginals.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $X : \Omega \to E$ be a random variable with law $P_X = X_* P$. Prove the change-of-variables identity $\int_E f \, dP_X = \int_\Omega f(X) \, dP$ for every non-negative measurable $f : E \to [0, \infty]$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the standard machine: indicators, then simple functions, then monotone limits.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For an indicator $f = \chi_B$ with $B \in \mathcal{E}$: $\int_E \chi_B \, dP_X = P_X(B) = P(X^{-1}(B)) = \int_\Omega \chi_B(X) \, dP$, since $\chi_B(X(\omega)) = \chi_{X^{-1}(B)}(\omega)$. By linearity the identity extends to non-negative simple functions $s = \sum_k c_k \chi_{B_k}$. For general non-negative measurable $f$, take simple $s_n \uparrow f$; then $s_n \circ X \uparrow f \circ X$, and the monotone convergence theorem on both sides gives $\int_E f \, dP_X = \lim_n \int_E s_n \, dP_X = \lim_n \int_\Omega s_n(X) \, dP = \int_\Omega f(X) \, dP$. This identity is what lets every expectation $\mathbb{E}[f(X)]$ be computed on $E$ against the law $P_X$, never referencing $\Omega$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, multiple choice).**

Which condition on a finitely additive cylinder pre-measure $\mu_0$ on the algebra $\mathcal{A}$ is equivalent to countable additivity?

A. $\mu_0(\Omega) = 1$
B. $\mu_0$ is monotone: $C \subseteq D$ implies $\mu_0(C) \leq \mu_0(D)$
C. $\mu_0$ is continuous at $\emptyset$: $C_n \downarrow \emptyset$ in $\mathcal{A}$ implies $\mu_0(C_n) \to 0$
D. $\mu_0$ vanishes on every singleton

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

This is the criterion the compactness step of the proof establishes for the cylinder pre-measure.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Continuity at $\emptyset$.** For a finite probability content on an algebra, countable additivity is equivalent to continuity from above at the empty set. The compactness/tightness argument (Step 3 of the proof) verifies exactly this for $\mu_0$ when the coordinate spaces are Polish. A is automatic and insufficient; B follows from finite additivity alone; D is neither necessary nor sufficient.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Give an example of three events that are pairwise independent but not mutually independent, and verify both claims by direct computation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Two fair coin flips suffice. Let $C$ be the event that the two flips differ.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On $\Omega = \{HH, HT, TH, TT\}$ with uniform $P$, let $A = \{HH, HT\}$ (first H), $B = \{HH, TH\}$ (second H), $C = \{HT, TH\}$ (flips differ). Each has probability $1/2$. Pairwise: $A \cap B = \{HH\}$, $P = 1/4 = P(A)P(B)$; $A \cap C = \{HT\}$, $P = 1/4 = P(A)P(C)$; $B \cap C = \{TH\}$, $P = 1/4 = P(B)P(C)$. So all three pairs are independent. But $A \cap B \cap C = \emptyset$, so $P(A \cap B \cap C) = 0 \neq 1/8 = P(A)P(B)P(C)$. Mutual independence fails. This shows independence is a joint condition on the whole family, not a union of pairwise conditions.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the uniqueness clause of the Kolmogorov extension theorem: a probability measure on $\mathcal{E}_T$ is determined by its finite-dimensional distributions.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The cylinder sets form a $\pi$-system generating $\mathcal{E}_T$. Apply Dynkin's $\pi$-$\lambda$ theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $P, Q$ be probability measures on $(\Omega, \mathcal{E}_T)$ with the same f.d.d.'s, so $P(\pi_S^{-1}(B)) = \mu_S(B) = Q(\pi_S^{-1}(B))$ for every finite $S$ and $B \in \mathcal{E}_S$. The collection $\mathcal{A}$ of all cylinder sets is closed under finite intersection: $\pi_S^{-1}(B) \cap \pi_{S'}^{-1}(B') = \pi_{S \cup S'}^{-1}(B \cap B')$ after pulling $B, B'$ back to the common base. So $\mathcal{A}$ is a $\pi$-system, and $P = Q$ on $\mathcal{A}$. Let $\mathcal{D} = \{A \in \mathcal{E}_T : P(A) = Q(A)\}$. Then $\mathcal{D}$ contains $\Omega$, is closed under proper differences ($A \subseteq B$ gives $P(B \setminus A) = P(B) - P(A) = Q(B) - Q(A)$), and is closed under increasing countable unions (continuity from below). Thus $\mathcal{D}$ is a $\lambda$-system containing the $\pi$-system $\mathcal{A}$. By Dynkin's $\pi$-$\lambda$ theorem, $\mathcal{D} \supseteq \sigma(\mathcal{A}) = \mathcal{E}_T$, so $P = Q$ everywhere.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Exhibit a consistent finite-dimensional family on non-Polish coordinate spaces that admits no countably additive extension, illustrating the necessity of the topological hypothesis.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take a countably additive structure on each coordinate that becomes purely finitely additive after marginalizing — the classical Andersen-Jessen / Dieudonné example uses a sequence of non-Borel coordinate sets or an interval with a non-regular content.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A clean route (Dieudonné 1948; see Halmos *Measure Theory* §49): let $T = \mathbb{N}$ and let each coordinate space be a fixed uncountable set $E$ with the $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{E}$ of countable-or-cocountable subsets, equipped with the $0$–$1$ measure $\nu$ giving cocountable sets mass $1$. Define $\mu_S$ on $E^S$ to be the corresponding product-type law concentrated, in the limit, on the diagonal-type set of eventually-equal sequences. Each $\mu_S$ is a countably additive probability and the family is consistent (marginalizing preserves the cocountable structure). The cylinder set $C_n = \{\omega : \omega_1, \ldots, \omega_n \text{ all lie in a fixed countable set}\}$ can be arranged to decrease to $\emptyset$ while $\mu_0(C_n)$ stays bounded below: the pre-measure assigns each finite window mass $1$ but the intersection is empty because the "mass" escapes to coordinates not pinned by any finite window. Continuity at $\emptyset$ fails, so $\mu_0$ is only finitely additive on $\mathcal{A}$ and has no countably additive extension to $\mathcal{E}_T$. The obstruction is precisely the absence of inner-compact regularity: on a Polish coordinate space the tightness step rescues continuity at $\emptyset$, and here it cannot. This is why the theorem's hypothesis is *standard Borel / Polish coordinates*, not merely consistency.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The structure organizing the foundations splits into the canonical-space realization, the projective-limit formulation, the role of regularity, the regular-conditional-distribution refinement, and the special-case constructions (i.i.d. sequences, Markov chains via Ionescu-Tulcea, Gaussian processes) that the extension theorem subsumes or complements.

**Theorem 1 (canonical realization).** For any consistent f.d.d. family on standard Borel coordinates, the canonical space $(\Omega, \mathcal{E}_T, P) = (\prod_t E_t, \bigotimes_t \mathcal{E}_t, P)$ with coordinate processes $X_t = \pi_t$ realizes a process whose finite-dimensional distributions are exactly the prescribed $\mu_S$. Every process with those f.d.d.'s on any other space has the same law as the coordinate process under the law-isomorphism $\omega \mapsto (X_t(\omega))_t$. The canonical space is therefore universal: it is the terminal object among realizations, and statistical statements transfer along the law map.

**Theorem 2 (projective-limit formulation; Bochner).** A consistent family $(\mu_S)$ is a projective system of probability measures along the directed set of finite subsets ordered by inclusion, with bonding maps the marginal projections. The Kolmogorov measure $P$ is the projective limit $\varprojlim \mu_S$ in the category of probability spaces, characterized by $(\pi_S)_* P = \mu_S$ together with the universal property that any cone of measure-preserving maps factors uniquely through $P$. Bochner's theorem on projective limits gives existence under the same regularity hypothesis; the cylinder-algebra proof and the projective-limit proof are two presentations of one construction.

**Theorem 3 (Nelson regularity / inner-compact criterion; Nelson 1959 *Ann. of Math.* 69, 630).** Countable additivity of the cylinder pre-measure holds whenever each $\mu_S$ is inner regular by compact sets in a compatible topology on $E_S$ that is compatible across marginalization. Polish coordinate spaces satisfy this; more generally, a *perfect* family or a *Radon* family suffices. The criterion isolates the exact analytic input: the algebraic data (consistency) is never enough on its own — a topological tightness ingredient is required, and it is sharp in the sense of the Dieudonné counterexample.

**Theorem 4 (regular conditional distributions and disintegration).** On standard Borel spaces, for a random variable $X$ and a sub-$\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{G}$, there is a *regular conditional distribution*: a kernel $\kappa : \Omega \times \mathcal{E} \to [0,1]$ with $\kappa(\omega, \cdot)$ a probability measure and $\kappa(\cdot, B)$ a version of $P(X \in B \mid \mathcal{G})$. The disintegration theorem factors a joint law into a marginal and a measurable family of conditionals, $\mu(dx\,dy) = \mu_1(dx)\,\kappa(x, dy)$. This refines the product construction: independence is the special case $\kappa(x, \cdot) = \mu_2$ constant in $x$. Standard Borel structure is exactly what makes such regular versions exist.

**Theorem 5 (Ionescu-Tulcea: extension without topology for kernels).** If instead of a consistent family one is given a sequence of probability kernels $\kappa_n : E_1 \times \cdots \times E_{n} \rightsquigarrow E_{n+1}$ and an initial law $\nu_1$, the Ionescu-Tulcea theorem constructs a measure on $\prod_n E_n$ with the prescribed conditionals, requiring *no topological hypothesis* on the $E_n$. This is the natural tool for Markov chains and for any sequential model defined by transition kernels. The Kolmogorov theorem and the Ionescu-Tulcea theorem are complementary: Kolmogorov needs consistency plus topology and handles arbitrary index sets; Ionescu-Tulcea needs a kernel sequence (an ordered index) and dispenses with topology.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason the probability spine can be built at all is that the Kolmogorov extension theorem reduces the construction of a measure on an uncountable-dimensional space to consistent finite-dimensional data, and this is exactly the move that recurs at every level above it. Putting these together, the canonical-space realization (Theorem 1) makes the coordinate process the universal model, the projective-limit formulation (Theorem 2) is dual to the cylinder-algebra construction and exhibits $P$ as $\varprojlim \mu_S$, and the regularity criterion (Theorem 3) isolates the load-bearing tightness input that the algebraic consistency cannot supply. The central insight is that consistency generalises the Fubini-Tonelli product structure [02.07.07]: an i.i.d. law is the product family, a Markov chain is the kernel-composed family, and a Gaussian process is the family of multivariate normals with a consistent covariance — each a different consistent system fed to the same machine. The bridge is between the outer-measure Carathéodory backbone [02.07.02] and the entire theory of stochastic processes: the extension theorem appears again in the construction of Brownian motion (where Kolmogorov supplies the law on $\mathbb{R}^{[0,\infty)}$ and a separate continuity theorem selects a continuous modification), in the strong law and the ergodic theorem (which live on the canonical i.i.d. space), and in the disintegration theory (Theorem 4) that conditions the canonical law. This is the structural fact that organises the foundations: every later existence result is a specialization, and the regularity-versus-kernels dichotomy (Kolmogorov versus Ionescu-Tulcea) is the recurring decision about which hypothesis to pay for.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (countable additivity equals continuity at $\emptyset$).** Let $\mu_0$ be a finitely additive probability content on an algebra $\mathcal{A}$. Then $\mu_0$ is countably additive on $\mathcal{A}$ (in the sense that $\bigsqcup_n C_n = C \in \mathcal{A}$ with $C_n \in \mathcal{A}$ disjoint implies $\mu_0(C) = \sum_n \mu_0(C_n)$) if and only if $C_n \downarrow \emptyset$ in $\mathcal{A}$ implies $\mu_0(C_n) \to 0$.

*Proof.* ($\Rightarrow$) If $C_n \downarrow \emptyset$, the differences $D_n = C_n \setminus C_{n+1}$ are disjoint with $C_m = \bigsqcup_{n \geq m} D_n$ (union in $\mathcal{A}$ as a tail). Countable additivity gives $\mu_0(C_1) = \sum_{n \geq 1} \mu_0(D_n) < \infty$, so the tail $\mu_0(C_m) = \sum_{n \geq m} \mu_0(D_n) \to 0$. ($\Leftarrow$) Suppose continuity at $\emptyset$. Let $C = \bigsqcup_n C_n \in \mathcal{A}$ with $C_n$ disjoint in $\mathcal{A}$. The remainders $R_N = C \setminus \bigsqcup_{n \leq N} C_n$ lie in $\mathcal{A}$, decrease to $\emptyset$, so $\mu_0(R_N) \to 0$. Finite additivity gives $\mu_0(C) = \sum_{n \leq N} \mu_0(C_n) + \mu_0(R_N)$; letting $N \to \infty$ yields $\mu_0(C) = \sum_n \mu_0(C_n)$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (well-definedness of the cylinder pre-measure).** Under consistency, $\mu_0(\pi_S^{-1}(B)) = \mu_S(B)$ is independent of the chosen base representation.

*Proof.* Suppose $\pi_S^{-1}(B) = \pi_{S'}^{-1}(B')$. Let $U = S \cup S'$ with projections $q : E_U \to E_S$, $q' : E_U \to E_{S'}$. Pulling back, $q^{-1}(B) = q'^{-1}(B')$ as subsets of $E_U$ (both equal $\pi_U(\pi_S^{-1}(B))$'s base). Consistency gives $\mu_S(B) = (q_* \mu_U)(B) = \mu_U(q^{-1}(B)) = \mu_U(q'^{-1}(B')) = (q'_* \mu_U)(B') = \mu_{S'}(B')$. Hence the two candidate values coincide. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (tightness on Polish spaces).** Every Borel probability measure $\mu$ on a Polish space $E$ is inner regular by compact sets: $\mu(B) = \sup\{\mu(K) : K \subseteq B \text{ compact}\}$ for every Borel $B$.

*Proof.* Fix a complete separable metric. For $\varepsilon > 0$ and each $n$, separability gives countably many closed balls of radius $1/n$ covering $E$; finitely many of them, say with union $F_n$, satisfy $\mu(F_n) > 1 - \varepsilon 2^{-n}$. The set $K = \bigcap_n F_n$ is closed and totally bounded, hence compact by completeness, with $\mu(E \setminus K) \leq \sum_n \varepsilon 2^{-n} = \varepsilon$. Thus $\mu(K) > 1 - \varepsilon$. Inner regularity of Borel sets then follows from outer/inner regularity by closed/open sets (standard for metric measures) intersected with this compact exhaustion. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (the compactness/diagonal step).** With the notation of Step 3 of the Key theorem, if $\mu_0(C_n) \geq \varepsilon$ for all $n$ then $\bigcap_n C_n \neq \emptyset$.

*Proof.* As constructed, $D_n = \bigcap_{k \leq n} \pi_{S_k}^{-1}(K_k)$ are nonempty compact-based cylinders with $D_n \subseteq C_n$ and $D_{n+1} \subseteq D_n$. Pick $\omega^{(n)} \in D_n$. The points $\pi_{S_1}(\omega^{(n)}) \in K_1$ (compact) admit a convergent subsequence; inductively refine so that for each fixed $m$, $\pi_{S_m}(\omega^{(n)})$ converges in $K_m$ along a single diagonal subsequence. The limits $a_m \in K_m$ are consistent under the projections $E_{S_{m+1}} \to E_{S_m}$ (projections are continuous, limits commute), so they assemble into $\omega^* \in \Omega$ with $\pi_{S_m}(\omega^*) = a_m \in K_m$. Then $\omega^* \in \pi_{S_m}^{-1}(K_m) \supseteq D_m$-defining sets, giving $\omega^* \in C_m$ for every $m$, so $\omega^* \in \bigcap_m C_m$. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (uniqueness via $\pi$-$\lambda$).** A probability measure on $\mathcal{E}_T$ is determined by its values on cylinders.

*Proof.* As in Exercise 7: cylinders form a $\pi$-system generating $\mathcal{E}_T$; the agreement set of two probability measures is a $\lambda$-system; Dynkin's theorem forces agreement on $\sigma$(cylinders) $= \mathcal{E}_T$. $\square$

**Proposition 6 (pushforward is a probability measure).** If $X : (\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P) \to (E, \mathcal{E})$ is measurable then $P_X = P \circ X^{-1}$ is a probability measure on $(E, \mathcal{E})$.

*Proof.* $P_X(E) = P(X^{-1}(E)) = P(\Omega) = 1$. For disjoint $B_n \in \mathcal{E}$, the preimages $X^{-1}(B_n)$ are disjoint in $\mathcal{F}$ and $X^{-1}(\bigsqcup_n B_n) = \bigsqcup_n X^{-1}(B_n)$, so countable additivity of $P$ transfers: $P_X(\bigsqcup_n B_n) = P(\bigsqcup_n X^{-1}(B_n)) = \sum_n P(X^{-1}(B_n)) = \sum_n P_X(B_n)$. Non-negativity is inherited. $\square$

**Proposition 7 (consistency of i.i.d. and of Gaussian families).** The product family $\mu_S = \bigotimes_{t \in S} \nu$ is consistent (Exercise 3). The Gaussian family $\mu_S = \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma_S)$ with $\Sigma_S = (c(s, t))_{s, t \in S}$, for a symmetric positive-semidefinite kernel $c : T \times T \to \mathbb{R}$, is consistent.

*Proof.* For the Gaussian case, marginalizing a multivariate normal $\mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma_{S'})$ onto the coordinates in $S \subseteq S'$ yields $\mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma_S)$, because the marginal of a jointly Gaussian vector is Gaussian with the corresponding sub-covariance block (the projection of a normal law is normal, with covariance the principal submatrix). Positive-semidefiniteness of $c$ guarantees each $\Sigma_S$ is a valid covariance, so each $\mu_S$ exists. Marginal-consistency is the sub-block identity, so the family is projective and the extension yields a centered Gaussian process with covariance $c$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The product-measure and Fubini-Tonelli theory [02.07.07] is the finite-dimensional engine of consistency: an i.i.d. family is the projective system of product measures, and marginalizing a product is precisely a Tonelli integration over the dropped coordinates. The extension theorem is the infinite-index continuation of the finite product construction, so this unit generalises [02.07.07] from finite to arbitrary index sets.

- The Carathéodory extension and outer-measure construction [02.07.02] supplies the backbone of Step 4: the cylinder pre-measure, once shown countably additive on the algebra, is extended to the generated $\sigma$-algebra by the same outer-measure machine that builds Lebesgue measure. The probabilistic content is entirely in establishing countable additivity; the lift is borrowed verbatim from [02.07.02].

- The $\sigma$-algebra and Borel-space foundations [02.07.01] define the cylinder $\sigma$-algebra and the standard-Borel hypothesis that makes the regularity step work; measurability of the coordinate maps and of random variables is exactly the framework of [02.07.01], and the countable-coordinate-dependence phenomenon is a structural fact about product $\sigma$-algebras introduced there.

- The elementary rules and distributions of probability [26.02.01] are the concrete shadow of this unit: the axioms $P(\Omega) = 1$ and additivity, independence as a product rule, and named distributions all reappear here as the measure-theoretic objects they abstract, with [26.02.01]'s finite and discrete cases embedded as the f.d.d.'s the extension theorem stitches together.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The measure-theoretic foundation of probability was settled by Andrei Kolmogorov's 1933 monograph *Grundbegriffe der Wahrscheinlichkeitsrechnung* [ref: TODO_REF Kolmogorov 1933], which identified a random experiment with a measure space of total mass one and a random variable with a measurable function. The extension theorem occupies the final chapter (§III), where Kolmogorov constructs measures on infinite-dimensional product spaces from consistent finite-dimensional distributions, the existence result that legitimized the study of stochastic processes as honest mathematical objects rather than heuristic limits.

The analytic heart of the construction predates Kolmogorov. Percy Daniell's 1919 paper *Integrals in an infinite number of dimensions* [ref: TODO_REF Daniell 1919] built integration on infinite products and proved the extension for product-type families, and the result is often called the Daniell-Kolmogorov theorem in recognition. The Carathéodory extension machinery it relies on came from Constantin Carathéodory's 1918 *Vorlesungen über reelle Funktionen* [ref: TODO_REF Caratheodory 1918], the outer-measure axiomatization used identically for Lebesgue measure.

The topological hypothesis was clarified over the following decades. Jean Dieudonné (1948) and the work surveyed by Erik Sparre Andersen and Børge Jessen produced consistent families on non-regular coordinate spaces with no countably additive extension, showing that consistency alone is insufficient. Edward Nelson's 1959 *Regular probability measures on function space* [ref: TODO_REF Nelson 1959] gave a clean inner-compactness criterion, and K. R. Parthasarathy's 1967 *Probability Measures on Metric Spaces* [ref: TODO_REF Parthasarathy 1967] established the theorem in its standard Polish-space form, with the Ionescu-Tulcea kernel construction providing the complementary topology-free route for sequentially defined models.
