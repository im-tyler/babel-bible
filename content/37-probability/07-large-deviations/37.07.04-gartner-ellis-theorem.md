## Intuition [Beginner]

Cramér's theorem priced the rare deviations of an average of many independent, identical samples. But most real data are neither independent nor identical: today's measurement leans on yesterday's, a Markov chain remembers its last state, a Gaussian process is correlated across its whole length. The Gärtner-Ellis theorem keeps the rare-event accounting of Cramér while throwing out the independence that made it easy.

The trick is to stop asking about single samples and ask only one thing of the whole sequence: how fast does its tuned-average grow? Take your quantity $Z_n$ — already an average-like object built from $n$ pieces — and tune it with a dial $\lambda$, exactly as in Cramér. Look at the logarithm of the tuned expectation, divided by $n$ to keep it from blowing up. If, as $n$ grows, this settles down to a definite limiting curve $\Lambda(\lambda)$, that single curve is all the dependence structure you need. You never have to know how the pieces correlate; you only need the limit to exist.

From that limiting curve the rate function is read off by the same geometric flip as before: the Legendre-Fenchel transform turns the dial-language curve $\Lambda$ into the outcome-language cost $\Lambda^*$. The cost of seeing $Z_n$ near a forbidden value $x$ decays like $e^{-n\Lambda^*(x)}$.

There is one new piece of fine print, and it has teeth. To make the cheap upper bound and the constructive lower bound meet — to push the rare event into existence by tilting, as Cramér did — the limiting curve must be *steep*: its slope must run off to infinity as the dial approaches the edge of where the tuned average stays finite. Steepness guarantees that every target value is hit by some dial setting, so the tilt that realises it always exists. When the curve is steep and smooth, the two bounds close and the rate is exactly $\Lambda^*$. When it is not, the lower bound can leak at the values no dial can reach.

So Gärtner-Ellis is Cramér with the independence removed and one regularity condition added: give me a limiting tuned-growth curve that is smooth and steep, and I will give you the exponential cost of every deviation, dependence and all.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider a two-state weather chain: each day is Sun ($+1$) or Rain ($-1$). It is sticky — a sunny day is followed by sun with probability $0.8$, a rainy day by rain with probability $0.8$. Let $Z_n$ be the average daily score over $n$ days. The days are *not* independent, so Cramér does not apply directly; we use the Gärtner-Ellis input instead.

**Step 1. Identify the tuned-growth limit.** For such a chain the limiting curve $\Lambda(\lambda)$ is the logarithm of the largest growth-rate of the tuned transition table — its dominant eigenvalue. For this symmetric chain one can show the limit works out to
$$
\Lambda(\lambda) = \log\Big( 0.8\cosh\lambda + \sqrt{0.64\cosh^2\lambda - 0.36}\,\Big).
$$
We do not need its derivation here, only its values, which we read off numerically.

**Step 2. Locate the typical value.** The slope at zero, $\Lambda'(0)$, is the long-run average score. By symmetry $\Lambda'(0) = 0$: equal sun and rain, average $0$. That is the no-cost value.

**Step 3. Price a deviation by the flip.** Ask the cost of the average sitting at $x = 0.5$ (three-quarters sunny). The flip gives $\Lambda^*(0.5) = \lambda^* \cdot 0.5 - \Lambda(\lambda^*)$ at the dial $\lambda^*$ whose slope matches $0.5$. Solving $\Lambda'(\lambda^*) = 0.5$ numerically gives $\lambda^* \approx 0.60$, with $\Lambda(0.60) \approx 0.236$, so
$$
\Lambda^*(0.5) = 0.60 \times 0.5 - 0.236 = 0.300 - 0.236 = 0.064.
$$

**Step 4. Read off the probability scale.** Over $n = 100$ days, the chance the average sits near $0.5$ decays like $e^{-n\Lambda^*(0.5)} = e^{-100 \times 0.064} = e^{-6.4} \approx 1.7 \times 10^{-3}$.

**What this tells us.** The dependence did not change the *shape* of the recipe at all — one limiting curve, one flip, one exponent. The stickiness of the weather is fully absorbed into the curve $\Lambda$; everything downstream is identical to Cramér. That absorption is the whole point of Gärtner-Ellis.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\{Z_n\}_{n\ge 1}$ be a sequence of $\mathbb{R}^d$-valued random vectors on a common probability space (no independence, no identical distribution assumed). The object that replaces the single-sample cumulant generating function of Cramér is its scaled limit.

**Definition (scaled / limiting logarithmic moment generating function).** For each $n$ set
$$
\Lambda_n(\lambda) := \log \mathbb{E}\, e^{n\langle \lambda, Z_n\rangle}, \qquad \lambda \in \mathbb{R}^d,
$$
the logarithmic moment generating function of $Z_n$ at speed $n$. The **limiting logarithmic moment generating function** is the pointwise limit, assumed to exist in $(-\infty, +\infty]$,
$$
\boxed{\;\Lambda(\lambda) \;:=\; \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n}\,\Lambda_n(\lambda) \;=\; \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\log\mathbb{E}\, e^{n\langle\lambda, Z_n\rangle}.\;}
$$
We assume throughout that $0 \in \operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)$, where $\operatorname{dom}\Lambda = \{\lambda : \Lambda(\lambda) < \infty\}$. The function $\Lambda$ is convex (a pointwise limit of the convex $\tfrac1n\Lambda_n$, each convex by Hölder as in [37.07.03]) and satisfies $\Lambda(0) = 0$.

For an i.i.d. average $Z_n = \bar S_n = \tfrac1n\sum_{i=1}^n X_i$, one has $\Lambda_n(\lambda) = \log\mathbb{E}\,e^{\langle\lambda, S_n\rangle} = n\log\mathbb{E}\,e^{\langle\lambda, X_1\rangle}$, so $\Lambda(\lambda) = \log\mathbb{E}\,e^{\langle\lambda,X_1\rangle}$ is exactly the Cramér cumulant generating function — Gärtner-Ellis specialises to Cramér [37.07.02] in that case.

**Definition (Fenchel-Legendre rate function).** The candidate rate function is the conjugate of $\Lambda$ from [37.07.03],
$$
\Lambda^*(x) := \sup_{\lambda \in \mathbb{R}^d}\big(\langle\lambda, x\rangle - \Lambda(\lambda)\big), \qquad x \in \mathbb{R}^d.
$$
By the conjugate machinery of [37.07.03], $\Lambda^*$ is convex, lower-semicontinuous, non-negative, and — because $0 \in \operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)$ — a **good** rate function with compact sublevel sets.

**Definition (exposed point and exposing hyperplane).** A point $x \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is an **exposed point** of $\Lambda^*$ if there exists $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}^d$ (the **exposing hyperplane**) such that
$$
\langle\lambda, x\rangle - \Lambda^*(x) > \langle\lambda, y\rangle - \Lambda^*(y) \quad\text{for all } y \neq x.
$$
Write $\mathcal{F}$ for the set of exposed points whose exposing $\lambda$ lies in $\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)$.

**Definition (essential smoothness / steepness).** $\Lambda$ is **essentially smooth** if $\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)\neq\varnothing$, $\Lambda$ is differentiable on $\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)$, and $\Lambda$ is **steep**: $|\nabla\Lambda(\lambda_k)|\to\infty$ whenever $\lambda_k\to\lambda_0\in\partial(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)$, as in [37.07.03].

**Theorem statement (Gärtner-Ellis).** Assume $\Lambda$ exists as above with $0\in\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)$ and is lower-semicontinuous.

*(Upper bound.) For every closed $F\subseteq\mathbb{R}^d$,*
$$
\limsup_n \tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in F)\le -\inf_{x\in F}\Lambda^*(x).
$$
*(Lower bound at exposed points.) For every open $G\subseteq\mathbb{R}^d$,*
$$
\liminf_n \tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in G)\ge -\inf_{x\in G\cap\mathcal{F}}\Lambda^*(x).
$$
*(Full LDP.) If $\Lambda$ is additionally essentially smooth, then $\mathcal{F}=\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*)$ in the sense that the exposed lower bound matches the upper bound, and $\{Z_n\}$ satisfies the LDP at speed $1/n$ with good rate function $\Lambda^*$.*

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **Existence of $\Lambda$ is a real hypothesis, not automatic.** If the scaled log moment generating functions oscillate — e.g. $Z_n$ alternates between two laws with different cumulant behaviour along even and odd $n$ — the limit $\Lambda(\lambda)$ may fail to exist, and Gärtner-Ellis says nothing. One must verify convergence, typically via a spectral or mixing argument, before invoking the theorem.
- **The bare theorem gives only an exposed-point lower bound.** Without essential smoothness, the lower bound is $-\inf_{G\cap\mathcal{F}}\Lambda^*$, which can be strictly larger (a weaker bound) than $-\inf_G\Lambda^*$ when $G$ meets $\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*$ only at non-exposed points. The full LDP with rate $\Lambda^*$ requires steepness; otherwise the true rate may be the lower-semicontinuous regularisation of $\Lambda^*$ restricted to exposed points.
- **$\Lambda$ may be non-differentiable, signalling a phase transition.** A corner in $\Lambda$ corresponds to a flat segment (an affine stretch) in $\Lambda^*$, i.e. an interval of values sharing one tilt. This is not a pathology to be smoothed away; in statistical mechanics it is a first-order phase transition, and the non-exposed points on the flat segment are exactly where the lower bound needs care.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

We prove the Gärtner-Ellis theorem in $\mathbb{R}^d$ in the two-step form: the Chernoff upper bound from convexity of $\Lambda$ alone, then the exposed-point tilting lower bound, then the essential-smoothness upgrade that makes them meet. The arguments are the Cramér arguments of [37.07.02] run against the limiting $\Lambda$ rather than a single-sample cumulant generating function.

**Theorem (Gärtner-Ellis).** *Let $\{Z_n\}$ in $\mathbb{R}^d$ have limiting logarithmic moment generating function $\Lambda(\lambda)=\lim_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{E}\,e^{n\langle\lambda,Z_n\rangle}$, lower-semicontinuous with $0\in\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)$. Then the upper bound holds on all closed sets and the lower bound holds at exposed points with interior exposing tilt; if $\Lambda$ is essentially smooth, $\{Z_n\}$ satisfies the LDP at speed $1/n$ with good rate $\Lambda^*$.*

**Proof — upper bound (Chernoff against $\Lambda$).** Fix $\lambda\in\operatorname{dom}\Lambda$. By Markov's inequality applied to the non-negative variable $e^{n\langle\lambda,Z_n\rangle}$, for the closed half-space $H_{\lambda,a}=\{x:\langle\lambda,x\rangle\ge\langle\lambda,a\rangle\}$,
$$
\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in H_{\lambda,a})=\mathbb{P}(\langle\lambda,Z_n\rangle\ge\langle\lambda,a\rangle)\le e^{-n\langle\lambda,a\rangle}\mathbb{E}\,e^{n\langle\lambda,Z_n\rangle}=e^{-n\langle\lambda,a\rangle+\Lambda_n(\lambda)}.
$$
Taking $\tfrac1n\log$ and $\limsup_n$, and using $\tfrac1n\Lambda_n(\lambda)\to\Lambda(\lambda)$,
$$
\limsup_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in H_{\lambda,a})\le -\big(\langle\lambda,a\rangle-\Lambda(\lambda)\big).
$$
Now let $K$ be compact and $m=\inf_K\Lambda^*$. For each $x\in K$ pick $\lambda_x$ with $\langle\lambda_x,x\rangle-\Lambda(\lambda_x)>\min(m,\,1/\delta)-\delta$ (possible by definition of $\Lambda^*$, capping at a large value where $\Lambda^*(x)=+\infty$); the open half-spaces $\{y:\langle\lambda_x,y\rangle>\langle\lambda_x,x\rangle-\delta\}$ cover $K$, so a finite subcover $x_1,\dots,x_N$ exists. Then $\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in K)\le\sum_{i=1}^N\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in H_{\lambda_{x_i},\,x_i-\delta'})$, and the finite-union (max) rule of [37.07.01] gives
$$
\limsup_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in K)\le -\min_i\big(\langle\lambda_{x_i},x_i\rangle-\Lambda(\lambda_{x_i})\big)+O(\delta)\le -(m-O(\delta)).
$$
Letting $\delta\downarrow0$ gives $\limsup_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in K)\le-\inf_K\Lambda^*$ on compacts. Exponential tightness — supplied by finiteness of $\Lambda$ on a ball $\bar B(0,\rho)\subset\operatorname{dom}\Lambda$, which forces $\Lambda^*(x)\ge\rho\|x\|-\max_{\|\lambda\|\le\rho}\Lambda(\lambda)\to\infty$ as in [37.07.03] Exercise 7 — upgrades the compact bound to all closed sets via [37.07.01].

**Proof — lower bound at exposed points (tilting against $\Lambda$).** Let $x$ be an exposed point with exposing tilt $\eta\in\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)$, so $\langle\eta,x\rangle-\Lambda^*(x)>\langle\eta,y\rangle-\Lambda^*(y)$ for $y\neq x$, equivalently $\Lambda^*(x)=\langle\eta,x\rangle-\Lambda(\eta)$ with $\eta\in\partial\Lambda^*(x)$ (Fenchel-Young equality [37.07.03]). Fix open $G\ni x$ and $\delta>0$ with $B(x,\delta)\subseteq G$. Define the tilted laws $\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n$ of $Z_n$ by
$$
\frac{d\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n}{d\mathbb{P}}=\exp\!\big(n\langle\eta,Z_n\rangle-\Lambda_n(\eta)\big),
$$
a probability measure since $\mathbb{E}\,e^{n\langle\eta,Z_n\rangle-\Lambda_n(\eta)}=1$. Reversing the change of measure on $A_n=\{Z_n\in B(x,\delta)\}$,
$$
\mathbb{P}(A_n)=\widetilde{\mathbb{E}}_n\big[\mathbf 1_{A_n}\,e^{-n\langle\eta,Z_n\rangle+\Lambda_n(\eta)}\big].
$$
On $A_n$, $\langle\eta,Z_n\rangle\le\langle\eta,x\rangle+\|\eta\|\delta$, so $e^{-n\langle\eta,Z_n\rangle}\ge e^{-n\langle\eta,x\rangle-n\|\eta\|\delta}$, giving
$$
\mathbb{P}(A_n)\ge e^{-n\langle\eta,x\rangle-n\|\eta\|\delta+\Lambda_n(\eta)}\,\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n(A_n).
$$
Taking $\tfrac1n\log$, using $\tfrac1n\Lambda_n(\eta)\to\Lambda(\eta)$ and $\Lambda^*(x)=\langle\eta,x\rangle-\Lambda(\eta)$,
$$
\liminf_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(A_n)\ge -\Lambda^*(x)-\|\eta\|\delta+\liminf_n\tfrac1n\log\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n(A_n).
$$
It remains to show $\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n(A_n)\to1$, i.e. that under the tilt $Z_n$ concentrates at $x$. The tilted scaled log moment generating function is $\lambda\mapsto\Lambda(\eta+\lambda)-\Lambda(\eta)$, whose gradient at $\lambda=0$ is $\nabla\Lambda(\eta)=x$ (the exposing relation reads $x=\nabla\Lambda(\eta)$). Its conjugate has a unique zero at $x$; applying the already-proved upper bound under $\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n$ to the closed set $B(x,\delta)^c$ shows $\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n(B(x,\delta)^c)\to0$, hence $\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n(A_n)\to1$ and its $\tfrac1n\log\to0$. Therefore $\liminf_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(A_n)\ge-\Lambda^*(x)-\|\eta\|\delta$; let $\delta\downarrow0$. Taking the supremum over exposed $x\in G$ gives $\liminf_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in G)\ge-\inf_{G\cap\mathcal F}\Lambda^*$.

**Proof — the essential-smoothness upgrade.** When $\Lambda$ is essentially smooth, steepness makes $\nabla\Lambda$ a surjection of $\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)$ onto $\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*)$: every $x\in\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*)$ equals $\nabla\Lambda(\eta)$ for some interior $\eta$, and convex duality [37.07.03] makes such $x$ exposed with exposing tilt $\eta$. Thus $\mathcal F\supseteq\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*)$, and since $\Lambda^*$ is good (hence $\inf_G\Lambda^*=\inf_{G\cap\operatorname{int}\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*}\Lambda^*$ by lower-semicontinuity), the exposed lower bound equals $-\inf_G\Lambda^*$. The matching upper and lower bounds are the LDP with good rate $\Lambda^*$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem **builds toward** the entire dependent-data large-deviations toolkit — Markov-chain occupation costs, queueing under correlated input, Gaussian-field excursions — and **appears again in** the Donsker-Varadhan theory [37.07.06], where the limiting $\Lambda$ is computed as a principal eigenvalue and then fed into this exact conjugation. **This is exactly** the Cramér proof of [37.07.02] with the single-sample cumulant generating function replaced by the limit $\Lambda$: the Chernoff upper bound and the exponential-tilting lower bound are reused verbatim, now indexed by $\Lambda_n$. **The central insight** is that all dependence is compressed into one convex limit $\Lambda$, after which the rare-event cost is its conjugate by the same Fenchel-Young equality of [37.07.03]; **putting these together**, Gärtner-Ellis **generalises** Cramér [37.07.02] from independent to arbitrary correlated sequences and **is dual to** the moment description through the Legendre-Fenchel transform, with steepness the precise condition that makes the construction's optimal tilt always exist.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

A two-state Markov chain on $\{0,1\}$ has additive functional $S_n=\sum_{i=1}^n f(X_i)$ with $f(0)=0,f(1)=1$. The limiting curve is $\Lambda(\lambda)=\log\rho(P_\lambda)$, the log of the dominant eigenvalue of the tilted matrix $P_\lambda=P\,\mathrm{diag}(e^{\lambda f(0)},e^{\lambda f(1)})$. For $P=\begin{psmallmatrix}1-a&a\\b&1-b\end{psmallmatrix}$, write $P_\lambda$ and the characteristic equation for $\rho(P_\lambda)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Multiply column $1$ by $e^{0}=1$ and column $2$ by $e^{\lambda}$, then solve $\det(P_\lambda-\rho I)=0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$P_\lambda=\begin{psmallmatrix}1-a & a\,e^{\lambda}\\ b & (1-b)e^{\lambda}\end{psmallmatrix}$. The characteristic equation $\det(P_\lambda-\rho I)=0$ is
$$
\rho^2-\big((1-a)+(1-b)e^{\lambda}\big)\rho+e^{\lambda}\big((1-a)(1-b)-ab\big)=0,
$$
so $\rho(P_\lambda)=\tfrac12\Big[(1-a)+(1-b)e^{\lambda}+\sqrt{((1-a)+(1-b)e^{\lambda})^2-4e^{\lambda}((1-a)(1-b)-ab)}\Big]$ and $\Lambda(\lambda)=\log\rho(P_\lambda)$. This $\Lambda$ is the Gärtner-Ellis input for the chain's additive functional; the Perron-Frobenius eigenvalue is smooth and steep, so the full LDP holds with rate $\Lambda^*$.

</details>

</aside>

Show that if $\Lambda(\lambda)=\tfrac12\langle\Sigma\lambda,\lambda\rangle$ for a symmetric positive-definite $\Sigma$ (the general Gaussian limit), then $\Lambda^*(x)=\tfrac12\langle\Sigma^{-1}x,x\rangle$, and confirm $\Lambda$ is essentially smooth so the full LDP holds.

Maximising $\langle\lambda,x\rangle-\tfrac12\langle\Sigma\lambda,\lambda\rangle$ gives stationary point $x-\Sigma\lambda=0$, i.e. $\lambda=\Sigma^{-1}x$, and $\Lambda^*(x)=\langle\Sigma^{-1}x,x\rangle-\tfrac12\langle\Sigma\Sigma^{-1}x,\Sigma^{-1}x\rangle=\tfrac12\langle\Sigma^{-1}x,x\rangle$. Hence Gärtner-Ellis gives the full LDP with the Gaussian rate $\tfrac12\langle\Sigma^{-1}x,x\rangle$; $\Sigma$ is the long-run covariance, into which all correlations are folded.

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Carry out the tilting lower bound for a half-line: with $d=1$, $x>\Lambda'(0)$ an exposed point with interior exposing tilt $\eta>0$ ($\Lambda'(\eta)=x$), show $\liminf_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(Z_n\ge x)\ge-\Lambda^*(x)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Tilt by $\eta$; restrict to $\{x\le Z_n<x+\delta\}$ and bound the density factor $e^{-n\eta Z_n}$ there using $\eta>0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Tilt by $\eta$ via $d\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n/d\mathbb{P}=e^{n\eta Z_n-\Lambda_n(\eta)}$. Then
$$
\mathbb{P}(Z_n\ge x)\ge\widetilde{\mathbb{E}}_n\big[\mathbf 1_{\{x\le Z_n<x+\delta\}}e^{-n\eta Z_n+\Lambda_n(\eta)}\big].
$$
On $\{Z_n<x+\delta\}$ with $\eta>0$, $e^{-n\eta Z_n}\ge e^{-n\eta(x+\delta)}$, so
$$
\mathbb{P}(Z_n\ge x)\ge e^{-n\eta(x+\delta)+\Lambda_n(\eta)}\,\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n(x\le Z_n<x+\delta).
$$
Take $\tfrac1n\log$: using $\tfrac1n\Lambda_n(\eta)\to\Lambda(\eta)$ and $\Lambda^*(x)=\eta x-\Lambda(\eta)$, the exponential factor tends to $-\Lambda^*(x)-\eta\delta$. Under $\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n$ the tilted curve $\Lambda(\eta+\cdot)-\Lambda(\eta)$ has slope $x$ at $0$, so $Z_n$ concentrates at $x$ and $\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n(x\le Z_n<x+\delta)$ is bounded below by a positive constant (mass on one side of the tilted mean), giving $\tfrac1n\log\to0$. **Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Exhibit a non-steep limit $\Lambda$ where the bare Gärtner-Ellis lower bound fails to reach $-\inf_G\Lambda^*$, and identify the non-exposed values.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reuse $\Lambda(\lambda)=\sqrt{1+\lambda^2}$ from [37.07.03]: $\nabla\Lambda$ has bounded range $(-1,1)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose a sequence $Z_n$ has limit $\Lambda(\lambda)=\sqrt{1+\lambda^2}-1$ (subtracting $1$ enforces $\Lambda(0)=0$), which is smooth and strictly convex with $\Lambda'(\lambda)=\lambda/\sqrt{1+\lambda^2}\in(-1,1)$, bounded — hence not steep. The conjugate is $\Lambda^*(x)=1-\sqrt{1-x^2}$ on $[-1,1]$ and $+\infty$ outside. Only $x\in(-1,1)$ are realised as $\Lambda'(\eta)$ for finite $\eta$, so the exposed set is $\mathcal F=(-1,1)$; the endpoints $x=\pm1$, where $\Lambda^*=1$, are non-exposed (no finite tilt reaches them). For $G$ a neighbourhood of $1$ within $[1,\infty)$, $\inf_{G\cap\mathcal F}\Lambda^*$ is taken over points $<1$ and the bare lower bound is governed by interior values, while the true infimum $\inf_G\Lambda^*=\Lambda^*(1)=1$ is attained only at the non-exposed endpoint. Steepness is exactly what would close this gap; without it the lower bound is genuinely weaker.

</details>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the limiting curve $\Lambda(\lambda)=\lim_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{E}\,e^{n\langle\lambda,Z_n\rangle}$ is convex whenever the limit exists, with no independence assumption on $Z_n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each $\tfrac1n\Lambda_n$ is convex by Hölder; a pointwise limit of convex functions is convex.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix $\theta\in[0,1]$ and $\lambda_1,\lambda_2$. For each $n$, applying Hölder's inequality with exponents $1/\theta,1/(1-\theta)$ to the factorisation $e^{n\langle\theta\lambda_1+(1-\theta)\lambda_2,Z_n\rangle}=e^{n\theta\langle\lambda_1,Z_n\rangle}\cdot e^{n(1-\theta)\langle\lambda_2,Z_n\rangle}$ gives
$$
\mathbb{E}\,e^{n\langle\theta\lambda_1+(1-\theta)\lambda_2,Z_n\rangle}\le\big(\mathbb{E}\,e^{n\langle\lambda_1,Z_n\rangle}\big)^{\theta}\big(\mathbb{E}\,e^{n\langle\lambda_2,Z_n\rangle}\big)^{1-\theta},
$$
which uses no independence — only the single-expectation Hölder inequality. Taking $\tfrac1n\log$, $\tfrac1n\Lambda_n(\theta\lambda_1+(1-\theta)\lambda_2)\le\theta\tfrac1n\Lambda_n(\lambda_1)+(1-\theta)\tfrac1n\Lambda_n(\lambda_2)$. Passing to the limit $n\to\infty$ preserves the inequality, so $\Lambda(\theta\lambda_1+(1-\theta)\lambda_2)\le\theta\Lambda(\lambda_1)+(1-\theta)\Lambda(\lambda_2)$: $\Lambda$ is convex. Convexity is what guarantees $\Lambda^*$ is a sensible (convex, lsc) rate function via [37.07.03].

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

### Markov additive functionals and the tilted-generator eigenvalue

For an ergodic finite-state Markov chain $\{X_i\}$ with transition matrix $P$ and a function $f$, the additive functional $S_n=\sum_{i=1}^n f(X_i)$ has $Z_n=S_n/n$ with limiting curve
$$
\Lambda(\lambda)=\log\rho\big(P_\lambda\big),\qquad (P_\lambda)_{xy}=P_{xy}\,e^{\lambda f(y)},
$$
where $\rho(P_\lambda)$ is the Perron-Frobenius eigenvalue of the **tilted matrix** $P_\lambda$. That this is the limit follows from $\mathbb{E}\,e^{\lambda S_n}=\langle\mu_0,P_\lambda^{\,n}\mathbf 1\rangle$ and the spectral-gap asymptotics $P_\lambda^{\,n}\sim\rho(P_\lambda)^n\,v_\lambda w_\lambda^\top$; the irreducibility and positivity of $P_\lambda$ (for $\lambda$ where entries stay finite) make $\rho(P_\lambda)$ a simple, analytic, strictly-log-convex eigenvalue by Perron-Frobenius, hence $\Lambda$ is smooth and steep on the interior of its domain [ref: TODO_REF den Hollander §III.4]. Gärtner-Ellis then yields the LDP for $S_n/n$ with rate $\Lambda^*$, the **level-1** rate for the chain. Contracting the level-2 occupation-measure LDP (whose rate is the Donsker-Varadhan functional) along the map $\nu\mapsto\int f\,d\nu$ recovers the same $\Lambda^*$, exhibiting Gärtner-Ellis as the contracted shadow of the empirical-measure principle.

### Stationary Gaussian processes and the spectral-density rate

For a centred stationary Gaussian sequence with summable autocovariance $r(k)=\operatorname{Cov}(X_0,X_k)$ and spectral density $g(\omega)=\sum_k r(k)e^{ik\omega}$, the sample mean $Z_n=\bar X_n$ has $\operatorname{Var}(n\bar X_n)=\sum_{|k|<n}(n-|k|)r(k)\sim n\,g(0)$, so $\Lambda_n(\lambda)=\tfrac12\lambda^2\operatorname{Var}(n\bar X_n)/n\cdot n$ yields
$$
\Lambda(\lambda)=\tfrac12\,g(0)\,\lambda^2,\qquad \Lambda^*(x)=\frac{x^2}{2\,g(0)}.
$$
The long-run variance $g(0)=\sum_k r(k)$ — the spectral density at frequency zero — is the single scalar into which all temporal correlation collapses. Because $\operatorname{dom}\Lambda=\mathbb{R}$ has empty boundary, $\Lambda$ is steep by vacuity and the full LDP holds. Quadratic functionals (e.g. empirical covariances) give a non-Gaussian $\Lambda$ involving $\log\det$ of a tilted Toeplitz operator, where steepness must be checked against the spectral support, and phase-transition corners can appear when $g$ touches zero.

### The role of steepness and the gap at non-exposed points

The proof delivers the upper bound from convexity of $\Lambda$ alone and the lower bound only at exposed points. The map closing the gap is $\nabla\Lambda:\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)\to\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*)$. Steepness forces this map to be onto: as $\eta$ runs to the boundary of $\operatorname{dom}\Lambda$, $|\nabla\Lambda(\eta)|\to\infty$, so the image exhausts $\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*)$, and every interior target is exposed with interior exposing tilt. Drop steepness and $\nabla\Lambda$ has bounded range; targets beyond that range are non-exposed, the tilt realising them does not exist, and the lower bound stalls — the same loss of surjectivity of $\nabla\Lambda$ recorded for $\sqrt{1+\lambda^2}$ in [37.07.03], now deciding whether the dependent-data LDP rate is the full conjugate or only its exposed restriction. A non-differentiable $\Lambda$ (a corner) marks a flat segment of $\Lambda^*$ and, in statistical-mechanics applications, a first-order phase transition.

### The abstract sub-additive route

When the scaled log moment generating function is hard to compute, the rate can still be defined intrinsically. If $c_n(x)=-\log\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in B(x,\delta))$ is approximately super-additive, $c_{m+n}\le c_m+c_n+o(m+n)$, Fekete's lemma makes $\lim_n\tfrac1n c_n(x)$ exist and serve as the rate, with no recourse to $\Lambda$. This **abstract Cramér theorem** of Bahadur and Zabell [ref: TODO_REF Bahadur & Zabell 1979] subsumes Gärtner-Ellis: when $\Lambda$ exists and is essentially smooth, the Fekete limit equals $\Lambda^*$, but the sub-additive formulation continues to apply to stationary mixing sequences and Banach-space-valued averages where no manageable $\Lambda$ is available [ref: TODO_REF Bryc 1990].

**Synthesis.** **The central insight** of Gärtner-Ellis is that arbitrary dependence in $\{Z_n\}$ is compressed into a single convex limit $\Lambda(\lambda)=\lim_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{E}\,e^{n\langle\lambda,Z_n\rangle}$, after which the rare-event cost **is exactly** its Fenchel-Legendre conjugate $\Lambda^*$, so the principle **is** the dependent-data realisation of the conjugacy proved in [37.07.03]. **The foundational reason** the bounds coincide is that both compute the same supremum: the Chernoff upper bound optimises the exponential tilt and the tilting lower bound realises the optimal tilt as a change of measure, with Fenchel-Young equality identifying them at exposed points — **this is exactly** the two-bets structure of Cramér [37.07.02], now run against $\Lambda$ rather than a single-sample cumulant generating function. **Putting these together**, Gärtner-Ellis **generalises** Cramér from independent to correlated data and **is dual to** the moment-generating description, while **the bridge is** steepness: it makes $\nabla\Lambda$ surject onto $\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*)$, so every interior value is exposed and the exposed lower bound upgrades to the full LDP. The construction **appears again in** the Donsker-Varadhan eigenvalue formula [37.07.06], which supplies the explicit $\Lambda$ for Markov additive functionals, and **the bridge is** that the level-1 rate $\Lambda^*$ proved here is the contraction of the level-2 occupation-measure rate along the mean map.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Chernoff upper bound on a half-space against $\Lambda$).** *Let $\{Z_n\}$ in $\mathbb{R}^d$ have $\tfrac1n\Lambda_n(\lambda)\to\Lambda(\lambda)$ for $\lambda\in\operatorname{dom}\Lambda$. For the half-space $H=\{x:\langle\lambda,x\rangle\ge\langle\lambda,a\rangle\}$, $\limsup_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in H)\le-(\langle\lambda,a\rangle-\Lambda(\lambda))$.*

*Proof.* Markov's inequality on the non-negative variable $e^{n\langle\lambda,Z_n\rangle}$:
$$
\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in H)=\mathbb{P}(\langle\lambda,Z_n\rangle\ge\langle\lambda,a\rangle)\le e^{-n\langle\lambda,a\rangle}\mathbb{E}\,e^{n\langle\lambda,Z_n\rangle}=e^{-n\langle\lambda,a\rangle+\Lambda_n(\lambda)}.
$$
Take $\tfrac1n\log$ to get $\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in H)\le-\langle\lambda,a\rangle+\tfrac1n\Lambda_n(\lambda)$, then $\limsup_n$ and $\tfrac1n\Lambda_n(\lambda)\to\Lambda(\lambda)$ give the claim. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (compact upper bound).** *Under the hypotheses of Proposition 1 with $0\in\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)$, for every compact $K$, $\limsup_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in K)\le-\inf_K\Lambda^*$.*

*Proof.* Fix $\delta>0$. For each $x\in K$ choose $\lambda_x\in\operatorname{dom}\Lambda$ with $\langle\lambda_x,x\rangle-\Lambda(\lambda_x)\ge\min(\Lambda^*(x),1/\delta)-\delta$, possible by definition of $\Lambda^*(x)=\sup_\lambda(\langle\lambda,x\rangle-\Lambda(\lambda))$. The open half-spaces $U_x=\{y:\langle\lambda_x,y\rangle>\langle\lambda_x,x\rangle-\delta\}$ contain $x$ and cover $K$; extract a finite subcover $U_{x_1},\dots,U_{x_N}$. Each $U_{x_i}\subseteq\overline{U_{x_i}}=H_{\lambda_{x_i},x_i-\delta'}$ (a closed half-space with the constant shifted by $\delta\|\lambda_{x_i}\|$), so by Proposition 1 and the finite-union rule of [37.07.01],
$$
\limsup_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in K)\le-\min_{1\le i\le N}\big(\langle\lambda_{x_i},x_i\rangle-\Lambda(\lambda_{x_i})-\delta\|\lambda_{x_i}\|\big)\le-\big(\inf_K\Lambda^*-c\delta\big),
$$
for a constant $c$ from the bounded selected tilts. Let $\delta\downarrow0$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (exposed-point lower bound).** *Let $x$ be exposed for $\Lambda^*$ with exposing tilt $\eta\in\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)$ and $\nabla\Lambda(\eta)=x$. Then for every open $G\ni x$, $\liminf_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(Z_n\in G)\ge-\Lambda^*(x)$.*

*Proof.* Pick $\delta>0$ with $B(x,\delta)\subseteq G$ and let $A_n=\{Z_n\in B(x,\delta)\}$. Define $\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n$ by $d\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n/d\mathbb{P}=e^{n\langle\eta,Z_n\rangle-\Lambda_n(\eta)}$ (total mass $1$). Reversing the tilt,
$$
\mathbb{P}(A_n)=\widetilde{\mathbb{E}}_n\big[\mathbf 1_{A_n}e^{-n\langle\eta,Z_n\rangle+\Lambda_n(\eta)}\big]\ge e^{-n\langle\eta,x\rangle-n\|\eta\|\delta+\Lambda_n(\eta)}\,\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n(A_n),
$$
using $\langle\eta,Z_n\rangle\le\langle\eta,x\rangle+\|\eta\|\delta$ on $A_n$. Take $\tfrac1n\log$; the exponential prefactor tends to $-(\langle\eta,x\rangle-\Lambda(\eta))-\|\eta\|\delta=-\Lambda^*(x)-\|\eta\|\delta$ by Fenchel-Young equality at the exposed point [37.07.03]. Under $\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n$ the scaled log moment generating function is $\lambda\mapsto\Lambda(\eta+\lambda)-\Lambda(\eta)$, with gradient $\nabla\Lambda(\eta)=x$ at $\lambda=0$ and conjugate vanishing only at $x$; applying Proposition 2 under $\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n$ to the compact-in-$G^c$ pieces of $B(x,\delta)^c$ gives $\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n(A_n)\to1$, so $\tfrac1n\log\widetilde{\mathbb{P}}_n(A_n)\to0$. Hence $\liminf_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(A_n)\ge-\Lambda^*(x)-\|\eta\|\delta$; let $\delta\downarrow0$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (essential smoothness gives the full LDP).** *If $\Lambda$ is essentially smooth and lsc with $0\in\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)$, then every $x\in\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*)$ is exposed with interior exposing tilt, and $\{Z_n\}$ satisfies the LDP at speed $1/n$ with good rate $\Lambda^*$.*

*Proof.* By steepness, $\nabla\Lambda:\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)\to\mathbb{R}^d$ has image all of $\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*)$: differentiability and strict convexity (on the relative interior) make $\nabla\Lambda$ injective, and steepness ($|\nabla\Lambda(\eta_k)|\to\infty$ at the domain boundary) prevents the image from having a finite boundary inside $\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*)$, so the open image equals $\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*)$ [37.07.03]. For each such $x=\nabla\Lambda(\eta)$, the Fenchel-Young equality and strict convexity give the strict inequality defining exposedness with exposing tilt $\eta\in\operatorname{int}(\operatorname{dom}\Lambda)$. Hence Proposition 3 applies at every interior $x$, and since $\Lambda^*$ is good (lsc with compact sublevels, by $0\in\operatorname{int}\operatorname{dom}\Lambda$ as in [37.07.03]) we have $\inf_G\Lambda^*=\inf_{G\cap\operatorname{int}\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*}\Lambda^*$ for open $G$. Thus the lower bound reads $-\inf_G\Lambda^*$, matching Proposition 2's upper bound extended to closed sets by exponential tightness [37.07.01]. The two bounds are the LDP. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Gärtner-Ellis is the dependent-data generalisation of Cramér's theorem [37.07.02]: the Chernoff upper bound and the exponential-tilting lower bound are imported wholesale, with the single-sample cumulant generating function replaced by the limiting $\Lambda$, so the i.i.d. case where $\Lambda(\lambda)=\log\mathbb{E}\,e^{\langle\lambda,X_1\rangle}$ recovers Cramér exactly.

- The conjugacy $I=\Lambda^*$, the Fenchel-Young equality at exposed points, the essential-smoothness/steepness condition, and the goodness of $\Lambda^*$ are all the convex-duality results of [37.07.03]; steepness there decides surjectivity of $\nabla\Lambda$, which is precisely what turns the exposed-point lower bound into the full LDP here.

- The Donsker-Varadhan variational formula [37.07.06] computes the limiting $\Lambda$ for a Markov additive functional as the log principal eigenvalue of the tilted generator, supplying the explicit input that Gärtner-Ellis conjugates; the level-1 rate $\Lambda^*$ proved here is the contraction of that unit's level-2 occupation-measure rate along the integration map $\nu\mapsto\int f\,d\nu$.

- Sanov's theorem [37.07.05] is the level-2 empirical-measure principle whose projective-limit machinery underlies abstract Gärtner-Ellis in function spaces; restricting Sanov to a bounded test function and contracting gives a scalar Gärtner-Ellis statement with the same limiting log moment generating function as input.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Jürgen Gärtner proved the dependent-data theorem in 1977 [ref: TODO_REF Gärtner 1977] (Theory of Probability and its Applications 22, 24-39), studying large deviations from the invariant measure of a diffusion and isolating the convergence of the scaled logarithmic moment generating function as the only structural input needed. The $\mathbb{R}^d$ formulation under the now-standard essential-smoothness hypothesis, with the exposed-point analysis controlling the lower bound, is due to Richard Ellis in 1984 [ref: TODO_REF Ellis 1984] (Annals of Probability 12, 1-12), whence the theorem's joint attribution. The systematic textbook treatment, including the half-space cover for the upper bound and the steepness upgrade, is that of Dembo and Zeitouni [ref: TODO_REF Dembo & Zeitouni §2.3].

The abstract sub-additive route that subsumes the theorem when $\Lambda$ is intractable was developed by Bahadur and Zabell in 1979 [ref: TODO_REF Bahadur & Zabell 1979] (Annals of Probability 7, 587-621) for averages in general vector spaces, and extended to stationary mixing sequences by Bryc [ref: TODO_REF Bryc 1990]. The Markov-additive-functional applications, where $\Lambda$ is the Perron-Frobenius eigenvalue of a tilted kernel, connect the theorem to the Donsker-Varadhan large-deviation theory of occupation measures developed across the same period; the identification of $\Lambda$ with a free energy and $\Lambda^*$ with an entropy places the non-differentiable case of $\Lambda$ in correspondence with first-order phase transitions, the reading emphasised in Ellis's monograph and in den Hollander's account [ref: TODO_REF den Hollander §III.4].
