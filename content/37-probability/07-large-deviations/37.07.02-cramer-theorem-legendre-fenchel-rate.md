## Intuition [Beginner]

The law of large numbers promises that the average of many independent copies of a random quantity settles near its mean. Cramér's theorem answers the next question: when the average refuses to settle there — when it lands at some other value instead — how unlikely is that, exactly? The answer is an exponential decay, and Cramér's theorem hands you the precise number in the exponent.

That number comes from a single auxiliary function. Take your random quantity and look at the average of its exponential, tuned by a dial $\lambda$: large positive $\lambda$ rewards large outcomes, large negative $\lambda$ rewards small ones. The logarithm of this tuned average, as a function of the dial, is the **cumulant generating function** $\Lambda(\lambda)$. It is a smooth bowl-shaped curve that packages every moment of the variable at once. Cramér's insight is that the cost of seeing the average sit at a target value $x$ is obtained from $\Lambda$ by a geometric flip — the Legendre-Fenchel transform — that turns "dial language" into "outcome language."

Here is the picture for the two halves of the proof, which you have already met one piece of. To show a value is *at most* so likely, you bet on the dial: pick a $\lambda$, apply the exponential Markov inequality, and read off a bound. Optimising the dial gives the cheapest bound, and that cheapest bound is exactly the transformed cost. This is the upper half — the Chernoff bound.

To show the value is *at least* that likely, you cannot just wait for it; you have to make it happen. You re-weight the experiment so that the rare target becomes the new typical value — this re-weighting is called **tilting** — and under the re-weighted world the ordinary law of large numbers does the work. Then you carefully account for how much you paid to re-weight. The bill is, once again, the transformed cost. The two halves meet, and the exponential rate is pinned down.

So Cramér's theorem is two bets that agree: an upper bound bought by choosing the best dial, and a lower bound bought by tilting the experiment toward the rare event. Their meeting point is the rate function, and it is the conjugate of $\Lambda$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Roll a fair six-sided die many times and average the faces. The mean of one roll is $3.5$. We ask the cost of the average landing at $x = 4$ instead, using a small piece of Cramér's recipe.

**Step 1. Build the tuned average.** For one die with faces $1$ through $6$, the tuned average is the ordinary average of the six tuned weights $e^{\lambda}, e^{2\lambda}, \dots, e^{6\lambda}$, and $\Lambda(\lambda)$ is the logarithm of that average. We need the dial $\lambda$ that makes the re-weighted outcome equal to our target $4$.

**Step 2. Match the target by the mean-under-tilt rule.** Tilting by $\lambda$ re-weights face $k$ by $e^{\lambda k}$, so the re-weighted mean is the weighted face-total divided by the weight-total. Trying $\lambda = 0.18$, the six weights $e^{0.18 k}$ are $1.197, 1.433, 1.716, 2.054, 2.460, 2.945$, with weight-total $11.805$; the weighted face-total is $1.197 + 2.866 + 5.148 + 8.216 + 12.30 + 17.67 = 47.40$, so the re-weighted mean is $47.40 / 11.805 = 4.015 \approx 4$. Good enough for a hand computation.

**Step 3. Read off the cost.** The cost is $I(4) = \lambda\cdot 4 - \Lambda(\lambda)$ at this $\lambda$. Here $\Lambda(0.18) = \log(11.805/6) = \log(1.9675) = 0.6768$, so
$$
I(4) = 0.18\times 4 - 0.6768 = 0.72 - 0.6768 = 0.0432.
$$

**Step 4. Read off the probability scale.** Over $n = 200$ rolls, the chance the average sits near $4$ decays like $e^{-nI(4)} = e^{-200\times 0.0432} = e^{-8.64} \approx 1.8\times 10^{-4}$.

**What this tells us.** A half-point shift in the die average, from $3.5$ to $4$, already costs about two parts in ten thousand at two hundred rolls — and it grows exponentially harsher with more rolls. The cost came from one dial setting and one subtraction, which is the whole computational content of Cramér's theorem at this level.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be independent and identically distributed random vectors in $\mathbb{R}^d$ defined on a common probability space, with law $\mu$. Write $S_n = X_1 + \cdots + X_n$ and let
$$
\bar S_n := \frac{S_n}{n} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i
$$
denote the **empirical mean**. By the strong law of large numbers [37.02.02], if $\mathbb{E}\|X_1\| < \infty$ then $\bar S_n \to \mu_* := \mathbb{E}X_1$ almost surely; Cramér's theorem quantifies the exponentially small probability that $\bar S_n$ instead lies in a set away from $\mu_*$.

**Definition (cumulant generating function).** The **logarithmic moment generating function** (cumulant generating function) of $X_1$ is
$$
\Lambda(\lambda) := \log \mathbb{E}\, e^{\langle \lambda, X_1\rangle}, \qquad \lambda \in \mathbb{R}^d,
$$
with values in $(-\infty, +\infty]$ and $\langle\lambda,x\rangle = \sum_i \lambda_i x_i$. Its effective domain is $\mathcal{D}_\Lambda = \{\lambda : \Lambda(\lambda) < \infty\}$. The function $\Lambda$ is convex and lower-semicontinuous, $\Lambda(0) = 0$, and $\nabla\Lambda(\lambda) = \mathbb{E}_\lambda X_1$ is the mean of the **tilted law** $\mathbb{P}_\lambda$ wherever $\Lambda$ is differentiable [37.07.03].

**Definition (Cramér rate function).** The **Cramér rate function** is the Legendre-Fenchel conjugate of $\Lambda$,
$$
\boxed{\;I(x) \;=\; \Lambda^*(x) \;:=\; \sup_{\lambda \in \mathbb{R}^d} \big( \langle \lambda, x\rangle - \Lambda(\lambda)\big), \qquad x \in \mathbb{R}^d.\;}
$$
By the conjugate machinery of [37.07.03], $\Lambda^*$ is convex, lower-semicontinuous, and non-negative with $\Lambda^*(\mu_*) = 0$; when $0 \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$ it is moreover a **good** rate function — its sublevel sets $\{x : \Lambda^*(x) \le \alpha\}$ are compact. These are exactly the properties the LDP framework of [37.07.01] demands of a rate function.

**Definition (exponential tilt / change of measure).** For $\lambda \in \mathcal{D}_\Lambda$, the **tilted law** $\mathbb{P}_\lambda$ of $X_1$ is the probability measure with Radon-Nikodym derivative
$$
\frac{d\mathbb{P}_\lambda}{d\mu}(y) = e^{\langle\lambda, y\rangle - \Lambda(\lambda)}.
$$
This is a probability measure because $\int e^{\langle\lambda,y\rangle - \Lambda(\lambda)}\,\mu(dy) = e^{-\Lambda(\lambda)}\mathbb{E}\,e^{\langle\lambda,X_1\rangle} = 1$. Under $\mathbb{P}_\lambda$ the $X_i$ remain i.i.d. with mean $\nabla\Lambda(\lambda)$ and cumulant generating function $\lambda' \mapsto \Lambda(\lambda + \lambda') - \Lambda(\lambda)$.

**Theorem statement (Cramér).** If $0 \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$, the empirical means $\{\bar S_n\}$ satisfy the large deviation principle on $\mathbb{R}^d$ at speed $a_n = 1/n$ with good rate function $I = \Lambda^*$: for every closed $F$ and open $G$,
$$
\limsup_{n} \tfrac{1}{n}\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \in F) \le -\inf_{F}\Lambda^*, \qquad \liminf_{n} \tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \in G) \ge -\inf_{G}\Lambda^*.
$$

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **Heavy tails break the rate function, not just the constants.** If $X_1$ is standard Cauchy then $\mathbb{E}\,e^{\lambda X_1} = \infty$ for every $\lambda \neq 0$, so $\mathcal{D}_\Lambda = \{0\}$, $\Lambda \equiv 0$ on its domain, and $\Lambda^* \equiv 0$. The empirical mean has no exponential concentration at all — the rate function is identically zero and Cramér's theorem says nothing because its hypothesis $0 \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$ fails. Exponential decay of large deviations requires light (sub-exponential-tail) variables.
- **The conjugate can be non-strictly-convex, and then the LDP rate is flat.** For a variable supported on $\{0,1\}$ but degenerate ($\mathbb{P}(X=1)=1$), $\Lambda(\lambda) = \lambda$, so $\Lambda^*(1) = 0$ and $\Lambda^*(x) = +\infty$ for $x \neq 1$. The rate function is an indicator, not a smooth valley; Cramér still holds, with the deterministic limit reflected as an infinite cost off the support.
- **The infimum is over the right topological side.** The upper bound is read on the *closure* of a set and the lower bound on its *interior*; using the same set for both can give a strictly smaller upper exponent than is correct, exactly the open/closed asymmetry isolated in [37.07.01].

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

We prove Cramér's theorem in one dimension ($d = 1$), the case that exhibits both mechanisms cleanly; the $\mathbb{R}^d$ extension is taken up at Master tier. Assume $0 \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$, so all exponential moments are finite near the origin and $\Lambda$ is smooth and strictly convex on $\operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$ (strict convexity unless $X_1$ is degenerate).

**Theorem (Cramér in $\mathbb{R}$).** *Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. real random variables with $0 \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$ and mean $\mu_* = \mathbb{E}X_1$. Then $\{\bar S_n\}$ satisfies the LDP at speed $1/n$ with good rate function $\Lambda^*$. In particular, for $x > \mu_*$,*
$$
\lim_{n}\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \ge x) = -\Lambda^*(x).
$$

**Proof — upper bound (Chernoff).** Fix a closed set $F$ and let $x \in F$ with $x \ge \mu_*$ (the case $x \le \mu_*$ is symmetric under $X \mapsto -X$). For any $\lambda \ge 0$, Markov's inequality applied to the non-negative variable $e^{\lambda S_n}$ gives, using independence,
$$
\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \ge x) = \mathbb{P}(S_n \ge nx) \le e^{-\lambda n x}\,\mathbb{E}\,e^{\lambda S_n} = e^{-\lambda n x}\big(\mathbb{E}\,e^{\lambda X_1}\big)^n = e^{-n(\lambda x - \Lambda(\lambda))}.
$$
Taking $\tfrac1n\log$ and optimising over $\lambda \ge 0$,
$$
\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \ge x) \le -\sup_{\lambda \ge 0}(\lambda x - \Lambda(\lambda)).
$$
For $x \ge \mu_* = \Lambda'(0)$ the unconstrained supremum $\Lambda^*(x) = \sup_{\lambda\in\mathbb{R}}(\lambda x - \Lambda(\lambda))$ is attained at some $\lambda_x \ge 0$, because $\lambda \mapsto \lambda x - \Lambda(\lambda)$ has derivative $x - \Lambda'(\lambda)$ which is non-negative at $\lambda = 0$; hence the constraint $\lambda \ge 0$ is inactive and $\sup_{\lambda\ge0} = \Lambda^*(x)$. Therefore $\limsup_n \tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \ge x) \le -\Lambda^*(x)$. For a general closed $F$, $\Lambda^*$ is convex with minimum $0$ at $\mu_*$, so it is non-increasing to the left of $\mu_*$ and non-decreasing to the right; covering $F$ by the two rays $(-\infty, x^-]$ and $[x^+, \infty)$ where $x^\pm$ are the points of $F$ nearest $\mu_*$ on each side, and using the finite-union (max) rule of [37.07.01], yields $\limsup_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \in F) \le -\inf_F\Lambda^*$.

**Proof — lower bound (tilting).** It suffices to prove that for every $x \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_{\Lambda^*}$ and every $\delta > 0$,
$$
\liminf_n \tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(|\bar S_n - x| < \delta) \ge -\Lambda^*(x),
$$
since every open $G$ contains such a ball around any of its points. Fix $x$ at which the supremum defining $\Lambda^*(x)$ is attained at an interior $\lambda_x \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$ with $\Lambda'(\lambda_x) = x$ (a so-called **exposed point**; this holds throughout $\operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_{\Lambda^*}$ under the standing hypothesis). Introduce the tilted law $\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}$ with $d\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}/d\mu = e^{\lambda_x y - \Lambda(\lambda_x)}$; under it the $X_i$ are i.i.d. with mean $\Lambda'(\lambda_x) = x$ and finite variance $\Lambda''(\lambda_x)$. Reversing the change of measure on the event $A_n = \{|\bar S_n - x| < \delta\}$,
$$
\mathbb{P}(A_n) = \mathbb{E}_{\lambda_x}\Big[\mathbf{1}_{A_n}\,e^{-\lambda_x S_n + n\Lambda(\lambda_x)}\Big].
$$
On $A_n$ one has $S_n = n\bar S_n \in (n(x-\delta), n(x+\delta))$, so $e^{-\lambda_x S_n} \ge e^{-n\lambda_x x - n|\lambda_x|\delta}$ (taking the worse sign), giving
$$
\mathbb{P}(A_n) \ge e^{-n(\lambda_x x - \Lambda(\lambda_x)) - n|\lambda_x|\delta}\,\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}(A_n) = e^{-n\Lambda^*(x) - n|\lambda_x|\delta}\,\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}(A_n),
$$
where the Fenchel-Young equality $\Lambda^*(x) = \lambda_x x - \Lambda(\lambda_x)$ at the exposed tilt was used [37.07.03].

Under $\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}$ the mean of $\bar S_n$ is exactly $x$, so the weak law of large numbers gives $\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}(A_n) \to 1$, hence $\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}(A_n) \to 0$. Therefore
$$
\liminf_n \tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(A_n) \ge -\Lambda^*(x) - |\lambda_x|\delta,
$$
and letting $\delta \downarrow 0$ yields the claim. The full lower bound on open $G$ follows by taking the supremum over $x \in G$. Combined with goodness of $\Lambda^*$ from [37.07.03], the two bounds constitute the LDP. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem **builds toward** the entire applied large-deviations toolkit — hypothesis testing, queueing overflow, statistical-mechanics entropy — and **appears again in** the Gärtner-Ellis theorem [37.07.04], where the identical Chernoff-and-tilting pair is run against a limiting cumulant generating function instead of a single-sample one. **This is exactly** the realisation of the abstract weak-LDP-plus-tightness scheme of [37.07.01]: the tilting lower bound and Chernoff upper bound *are* the local content that produces the weak LDP, and finiteness of $\Lambda$ near $0$ supplies the exponential tightness that upgrades it. **The central insight** is that the upper bound optimises a free dial $\lambda$ while the lower bound realises the optimal dial as a change of measure, so the supremum defining $\Lambda^*$ is computed twice — once as a bound, once as a construction — and the answers coincide by Fenchel-Young equality. **Putting these together**, Cramér's theorem **generalises** the law of large numbers [37.02.02] from a statement about where the mean goes to a statement about the price of going elsewhere, and **is dual to** the moment-generating description through the Legendre-Fenchel transform of [37.07.03].

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Compute the Cramér rate function for i.i.d. standard Gaussian variables, where $\Lambda(\lambda) = \tfrac12\lambda^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Maximise $\lambda x - \tfrac12\lambda^2$ over $\lambda$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The stationary condition is $x - \lambda = 0$, so $\lambda = x$, giving $\Lambda^*(x) = x\cdot x - \tfrac12 x^2 = \tfrac12 x^2$. The Gaussian rate function is $\tfrac12 x^2$, so $\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \ge x) \approx e^{-n x^2/2}$ for $x > 0$, consistent with $\bar S_n \sim N(0, 1/n)$ and the Gaussian tail $e^{-nx^2/2}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

For i.i.d. exponential variables with rate $1$ (mean $1$), $\Lambda(\lambda) = -\log(1-\lambda)$ for $\lambda < 1$. Compute $\Lambda^*(x)$ for $x > 0$ and verify $\Lambda^*(1) = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Maximise $\lambda x + \log(1-\lambda)$; the stationary point gives $\lambda = 1 - 1/x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Differentiate $\lambda x + \log(1-\lambda)$ in $\lambda$: $x - \tfrac{1}{1-\lambda} = 0$ gives $1 - \lambda = 1/x$, i.e. $\lambda = 1 - 1/x$ (admissible $\lambda < 1$ iff $x > 0$). Substituting, $\Lambda^*(x) = (1 - 1/x)x + \log(1/x) = x - 1 - \log x$ for $x > 0$, and $+\infty$ for $x \le 0$. At $x = 1$, $\Lambda^*(1) = 1 - 1 - \log 1 = 0$, the mean, as required. The function $x - 1 - \log x$ is the standard exponential/Gamma rate function.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show directly that the tilted law $\mathbb{P}_\lambda$ with $d\mathbb{P}_\lambda/d\mu = e^{\lambda y - \Lambda(\lambda)}$ has mean $\Lambda'(\lambda)$ and variance $\Lambda''(\lambda)$, assuming $\lambda \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate $\Lambda(\lambda) = \log\int e^{\lambda y}\mu(dy)$ once and twice, justifying differentiation under the integral near an interior point.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $M(\lambda) = \int e^{\lambda y}\mu(dy) = e^{\Lambda(\lambda)}$. For $\lambda$ interior to $\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$ the exponential moments are finite in a neighbourhood, so differentiation under the integral is justified: $M'(\lambda) = \int y\,e^{\lambda y}\mu(dy)$ and $M''(\lambda) = \int y^2 e^{\lambda y}\mu(dy)$. Then $\Lambda'(\lambda) = M'/M = \int y\,e^{\lambda y - \Lambda}\mu(dy) = \mathbb{E}_\lambda Y$, the tilted mean. Differentiating again, $\Lambda''(\lambda) = M''/M - (M'/M)^2 = \mathbb{E}_\lambda Y^2 - (\mathbb{E}_\lambda Y)^2 = \operatorname{Var}_\lambda Y \ge 0$. Thus $\Lambda'$ is the tilted mean (matching the target $x$ at the optimal tilt) and $\Lambda''$ is the tilted variance, re-confirming convexity of $\Lambda$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that $\Lambda^*(x) = 0$ if and only if $x = \mu_* = \mathbb{E}X_1$, when $X_1$ is non-degenerate with $0 \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $\Lambda^* \ge 0$, the equality $\Lambda^*(\mu_*) = 0$, and strict convexity of $\Lambda$ on the interior of its domain.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Non-negativity gives $\Lambda^*(x) \ge \langle 0, x\rangle - \Lambda(0) = 0$. At $x = \mu_*$, the choice $\lambda = 0$ is stationary because $\partial_\lambda(\lambda x - \Lambda(\lambda))|_{\lambda=0} = x - \Lambda'(0) = x - \mu_* = 0$; since $\lambda x - \Lambda(\lambda)$ is concave its stationary point is the maximum, so $\Lambda^*(\mu_*) = 0 - \Lambda(0) = 0$. Conversely if $\Lambda^*(x) = 0$ then $\langle\lambda, x\rangle \le \Lambda(\lambda)$ for all $\lambda$, with equality forced at $\lambda = 0$; the supporting condition $\Lambda(\lambda) \ge \Lambda(0) + \langle x, \lambda - 0\rangle$ means $x \in \partial\Lambda(0)$. Since $\Lambda$ is differentiable at the interior point $0$, $\partial\Lambda(0) = \{\Lambda'(0)\} = \{\mu_*\}$, so $x = \mu_*$. Strict convexity makes $\mu_*$ the unique zero.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Carry out the tilting lower bound explicitly for $\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \ge x)$ with $x > \mu_*$, showing $\liminf_n \tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \ge x) \ge -\Lambda^*(x)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Tilt by $\lambda_x$ with $\Lambda'(\lambda_x) = x$; restrict to the event $\{x \le \bar S_n < x + \delta\}$ and bound the density factor there.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\lambda_x > 0$ solve $\Lambda'(\lambda_x) = x$ (it exists for $\mu_* < x < \operatorname{ess\,sup}X_1$ by strict convexity of $\Lambda$). Tilt by $\lambda_x$ and write, for $\delta > 0$,
$$
\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \ge x) \ge \mathbb{P}(x \le \bar S_n < x + \delta) = \mathbb{E}_{\lambda_x}\big[\mathbf{1}_{\{x \le \bar S_n < x+\delta\}}\,e^{-\lambda_x S_n + n\Lambda(\lambda_x)}\big].
$$
On the event $S_n < n(x+\delta)$ and $\lambda_x > 0$, $e^{-\lambda_x S_n} \ge e^{-n\lambda_x(x+\delta)}$, so
$$
\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \ge x) \ge e^{-n(\lambda_x x - \Lambda(\lambda_x)) - n\lambda_x\delta}\,\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}(x \le \bar S_n < x+\delta) = e^{-n\Lambda^*(x) - n\lambda_x\delta}\,\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}(x \le \bar S_n < x+\delta),
$$
using $\Lambda^*(x) = \lambda_x x - \Lambda(\lambda_x)$. Under $\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}$ the mean of $\bar S_n$ is $x$, so by the central limit theorem (or the weak law plus symmetry) $\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}(x \le \bar S_n < x+\delta) \to \tfrac12$ (mass on one side of the mean), in particular bounded below by a constant, so its $\tfrac1n\log$ tends to $0$. Hence $\liminf_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n\ge x) \ge -\Lambda^*(x) - \lambda_x\delta$; let $\delta\downarrow 0$.

</details>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the $\mathbb{R}^d$ Chernoff upper bound on a convex closed set $C$: $\limsup_n \tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \in C) \le -\inf_{x\in C}\Lambda^*(x)$, using one supporting half-space.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Separate $\mu_*$ (or the constrained minimiser) from $C$ by a hyperplane; reduce the event to a half-space and apply the scalar Chernoff bound along the normal direction.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $m = \inf_{x\in C}\Lambda^*(x)$; assume $m < \infty$ and $\mu_* \notin C$ (else $m = 0$ and the bound reads $\le 0$, which always holds). By Hahn-Banach separation [02.11.02], since $C$ is closed convex and the minimiser $x_* \in \partial C$ of the convex $\Lambda^*$ over $C$ satisfies $\nabla\Lambda^*(x_*) = \lambda_*$ pointing out of $C$, there is a half-space $H = \{x : \langle\lambda_*, x\rangle \ge \langle\lambda_*, x_*\rangle\} \supseteq C$. Then
$$
\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \in C) \le \mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \in H) = \mathbb{P}(\langle\lambda_*, S_n\rangle \ge n\langle\lambda_*, x_*\rangle) \le e^{-n(\langle\lambda_*, x_*\rangle - \Lambda(\lambda_*))} = e^{-n\Lambda^*(x_*)},
$$
the middle inequality being the scalar Chernoff bound applied to the real variable $\langle\lambda_*, X_i\rangle$ whose cumulant generating function at parameter $1$ is $\Lambda(\lambda_*)$, and the last equality the Fenchel-Young equality at $x_*$. Since $\Lambda^*(x_*) = m$, taking $\tfrac1n\log$ and $\limsup$ gives the bound. For general (non-convex) closed $F$ the argument is run on the convex sublevel sets of $\Lambda^*$; this is the content of the Master-tier $\mathbb{R}^d$ proof.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that without the hypothesis $0 \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$ — when $\mathcal{D}_\Lambda = \{0\}$ — the conclusion of Cramér's theorem can degenerate, by exhibiting a variable for which $\Lambda^* \equiv 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take a symmetric heavy-tailed variable with no finite exponential moments, e.g. one with density $\propto (1+|y|)^{-3}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $X_1$ have a symmetric density $c(1+|y|)^{-3}$ (finite mean $0$, finite variance). For any $\lambda \neq 0$, $\mathbb{E}\,e^{\lambda X_1} = \infty$ because the tail $e^{\lambda y}(1+|y|)^{-3}$ is non-integrable, so $\Lambda(\lambda) = +\infty$ for $\lambda \neq 0$ and $\Lambda(0) = 0$; thus $\mathcal{D}_\Lambda = \{0\}$. Then $\Lambda^*(x) = \sup_\lambda(\lambda x - \Lambda(\lambda)) = 0\cdot x - 0 = 0$ for every $x$ (only $\lambda = 0$ contributes a finite value). The rate function is identically zero, so the candidate LDP bound $\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \in F) \approx e^{-n\inf_F\Lambda^*} = e^0 = 1$ is vacuous: there is no exponential concentration. (Indeed for such heavy tails the empirical mean's large deviations decay only polynomially, governed by a single big jump, not exponentially.) The hypothesis $0\in\operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$ is exactly what excludes this collapse.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### Cramér in $\mathbb{R}^d$: the convex upper bound and exposed-point lower bound

In $d$ dimensions the upper bound is assembled from supporting half-spaces and the lower bound from exposed points. For the **upper bound**, a compact set is covered by finitely many half-spaces $H_i = \{x : \langle\lambda_i, x\rangle \ge \langle\lambda_i, x\rangle_i\}$, on each of which the scalar Chernoff estimate applies to $\langle\lambda_i, X\rangle$; the finite-union rule of [37.07.01] combines them, and exponential tightness — supplied by finiteness of $\Lambda$ on a ball around $0$ via the coercive functional $U(x) = \|x\|$ — extends the bound from compacts to all closed sets [ref: TODO_REF Dembo & Zeitouni §2.2]. For the **lower bound**, one shows $\liminf_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \in G) \ge -\Lambda^*(x)$ for every **exposed point** $x$ of $\Lambda^*$ with exposing hyperplane in $\operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$, by tilting along the exposing direction exactly as in $d = 1$; the convex-duality theorem that exposed points are dense in $\operatorname{dom}\Lambda^*$ (under $0\in\operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$) upgrades the pointwise bound to all open sets. The result is the LDP with good rate $\Lambda^*$ on $\mathbb{R}^d$.

### The role of exposed points and the gap when steepness fails

An $x$ is **exposed** for $\Lambda^*$ if there is $\lambda$ with $\Lambda^*(y) > \Lambda^*(x) + \langle\lambda, y - x\rangle$ for all $y \neq x$, i.e. a hyperplane touching $\Lambda^*$ only at $x$. The lower bound needs the exposing $\lambda$ to lie in $\operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$ so the tilt $\mathbb{P}_\lambda$ is defined and has mean $x$. When $\Lambda$ is **steep** ($|\nabla\Lambda(\lambda)| \to \infty$ as $\lambda \to \partial\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$), every $x \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_{\Lambda^*}$ is exposed with exposing tilt interior, and the lower bound holds throughout. Without steepness, points $x$ corresponding to boundary tilts may fail to be exposed, and the rate function can drop below $\Lambda^*$ at such $x$ — the same loss of surjectivity of $\nabla\Lambda$ seen for $\sqrt{1+\lambda^2}$ in [37.07.03], now controlling whether the LDP rate is the full conjugate or only its exposed restriction.

### Bahadur-Rao exact asymptotics

Cramér's theorem gives the exponential rate; the prefactor is the **Bahadur-Rao** refinement [ref: TODO_REF Bahadur & Rao 1960]. For a non-lattice real variable and $x > \mu_*$ with optimal tilt $\lambda_x$,
$$
\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \ge x) = \frac{e^{-n\Lambda^*(x)}}{\lambda_x\sqrt{2\pi n\,\Lambda''(\lambda_x)}}\,\big(1 + o(1)\big).
$$
The exponential factor is Cramér; the algebraic prefactor $1/(\lambda_x\sqrt{2\pi n\Lambda''(\lambda_x)})$ comes from a local central limit theorem under the tilted law, where $\bar S_n$ is asymptotically Gaussian with variance $\Lambda''(\lambda_x)/n$ centred at $x$. This exhibits the tilting argument as not merely a bound but the exact saddle-point evaluation: the tilt that achieves $\Lambda^*(x)$ is the saddle of the inverse-Laplace integral for the density of $\bar S_n$.

### Sub-additivity and the abstract Cramér theorem

The i.i.d. structure can be relaxed to a sub-additivity condition. If $c_n(x) := -\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \in B(x,\delta))$ satisfies an approximate super-additivity $c_{m+n} \le c_m + c_n + o(m+n)$, the limit $\lim_n \tfrac1n c_n(x)$ exists by Fekete's lemma and defines the rate function intrinsically, with no recourse to $\Lambda$. This **abstract Cramér theorem** (Bahadur-Zabell) recovers $\Lambda^*$ when the increments are i.i.d. but extends to additive functionals of Markov chains and stationary sequences, where $\Lambda$ is replaced by the limiting log-spectral-radius of a tilted transition kernel — the bridge to Gärtner-Ellis [37.07.04].

**Synthesis.** **The central insight** of Cramér's theorem is that one convex function $\Lambda$ and its conjugate $\Lambda^*$ encode the entire large-deviation behaviour of i.i.d. averages, so the principle **is exactly** the LDP realisation of the conjugacy proved abstractly in [37.07.03], with the goodness of $\Lambda^*$ inherited from finiteness of $\Lambda$ near the origin. **The foundational reason** the upper and lower bounds coincide is that both compute the same supremum $\Lambda^*(x) = \sup_\lambda(\langle\lambda,x\rangle - \Lambda(\lambda))$ — the Chernoff bound optimises the dial, the tilting construction realises the optimal dial as a change of measure, and Fenchel-Young equality identifies the two. **Putting these together** with the strong law [37.02.02] shows Cramér **generalises** the law of large numbers from concentration at $\mu_*$ to the exact exponential price of any deviation, while **the bridge is** exponential tightness: finiteness of $\Lambda$ on a neighbourhood of $0$ both makes $\Lambda^*$ good and supplies the tightness that upgrades the weak LDP of [37.07.01] to the full principle. The construction **appears again in** Sanov's theorem [37.07.06] as the level-1 contraction of the level-2 empirical-measure LDP, and **is dual to** the Gärtner-Ellis route [37.07.04] which keeps the conjugacy machinery while discarding independence.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Chernoff upper bound, scalar form).** *Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. real with $\Lambda(\lambda) = \log\mathbb{E}\,e^{\lambda X_1}$. For every $x \in \mathbb{R}$, $\limsup_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \ge x) \le -\Lambda^*(x)$ when $x \ge \mu_*$, and symmetrically for $x \le \mu_*$.*

*Proof.* For $\lambda \ge 0$, Markov's inequality on $e^{\lambda S_n} \ge 0$ gives $\mathbb{P}(S_n \ge nx) \le e^{-\lambda nx}\mathbb{E}\,e^{\lambda S_n} = e^{-n(\lambda x - \Lambda(\lambda))}$ by independence. Hence $\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \ge x) \le -(\lambda x - \Lambda(\lambda))$ for every $\lambda \ge 0$, so taking the infimum over the bound, $\le -\sup_{\lambda\ge0}(\lambda x - \Lambda(\lambda))$. For $x \ge \mu_* = \Lambda'(0)$, the concave map $\lambda \mapsto \lambda x - \Lambda(\lambda)$ has non-negative derivative $x - \Lambda'(0)$ at $0$, so its maximiser lies in $[0,\infty)$ and $\sup_{\lambda\ge0} = \sup_{\lambda\in\mathbb{R}} = \Lambda^*(x)$. The $\limsup$ over $n$ of a constant-in-$n$ bound is the bound itself. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (tilting lower bound at an exposed point).** *Let $x$ be exposed for $\Lambda^*$ with exposing tilt $\lambda_x \in \operatorname{int}\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$ satisfying $\Lambda'(\lambda_x) = x$. Then for every open $G \ni x$, $\liminf_n \tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \in G) \ge -\Lambda^*(x)$.*

*Proof.* Choose $\delta > 0$ with $B(x,\delta) \subseteq G$. Tilt by $\lambda_x$: under $\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}$ with $d\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}/d\mu = e^{\lambda_x y - \Lambda(\lambda_x)}$, the $X_i$ are i.i.d. with mean $\Lambda'(\lambda_x) = x$ and finite variance $\Lambda''(\lambda_x)$ (Exercise 4). Changing measure back,
$$
\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n \in B(x,\delta)) = \mathbb{E}_{\lambda_x}\big[\mathbf{1}_{\{\bar S_n \in B(x,\delta)\}}\,e^{-\lambda_x S_n + n\Lambda(\lambda_x)}\big] \ge e^{-n\lambda_x x - n|\lambda_x|\delta + n\Lambda(\lambda_x)}\,\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}(\bar S_n \in B(x,\delta)),
$$
where on the event $|\bar S_n - x| < \delta$ one bounds $-\lambda_x S_n \ge -n\lambda_x x - n|\lambda_x|\delta$. The exponent is $-n(\lambda_x x - \Lambda(\lambda_x)) - n|\lambda_x|\delta = -n\Lambda^*(x) - n|\lambda_x|\delta$ by Fenchel-Young equality at the exposed point. Since $\mathbb{E}_{\lambda_x}\bar S_n = x$, the weak law gives $\mathbb{P}_{\lambda_x}(\bar S_n \in B(x,\delta)) \to 1$, so its $\tfrac1n\log \to 0$. Thus $\liminf_n\tfrac1n\log\mathbb{P}(\bar S_n\in G) \ge -\Lambda^*(x) - |\lambda_x|\delta$, and $\delta\downarrow0$ closes it. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (the tilted measure is a probability measure with shifted cumulants).** *For $\lambda \in \mathcal{D}_\Lambda$, the tilt $\mathbb{P}_\lambda$ defined by $d\mathbb{P}_\lambda/d\mu = e^{\langle\lambda,y\rangle - \Lambda(\lambda)}$ is a probability measure, and its cumulant generating function is $\Lambda_\lambda(\theta) = \Lambda(\lambda + \theta) - \Lambda(\lambda)$.*

*Proof.* Total mass: $\int e^{\langle\lambda,y\rangle - \Lambda(\lambda)}\mu(dy) = e^{-\Lambda(\lambda)}\int e^{\langle\lambda,y\rangle}\mu(dy) = e^{-\Lambda(\lambda)}\cdot e^{\Lambda(\lambda)} = 1$, and the density is non-negative, so $\mathbb{P}_\lambda$ is a probability measure equivalent to $\mu$ on $\mathcal{D}_\Lambda$. Its moment generating function at $\theta$ is
$$
\int e^{\langle\theta,y\rangle}\,d\mathbb{P}_\lambda = \int e^{\langle\theta,y\rangle}e^{\langle\lambda,y\rangle - \Lambda(\lambda)}\mu(dy) = e^{-\Lambda(\lambda)}\int e^{\langle\lambda+\theta,y\rangle}\mu(dy) = e^{\Lambda(\lambda+\theta) - \Lambda(\lambda)},
$$
so $\Lambda_\lambda(\theta) = \log$ of this is $\Lambda(\lambda+\theta) - \Lambda(\lambda)$. Differentiating at $\theta = 0$ recovers $\nabla\Lambda(\lambda)$ as the tilted mean, the fact powering Proposition 2. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The rate function $\Lambda^*$ of this unit is precisely the abstract good rate function whose axioms are laid out in [37.07.01]; that unit's weak-LDP-plus-exponential-tightness upgrade is the scaffold on which the present Chernoff-and-tilting proof hangs, and the exponential tightness it requires is supplied here by finiteness of $\Lambda$ on a neighbourhood of the origin.

- The conjugacy $I = \Lambda^*$ and the convexity, goodness, and Fenchel-Young equality used throughout are imported wholesale from [37.07.03]; the optimal exponential tilt $\lambda_x$ realising the cost is the subgradient $\lambda_x \in \partial\Lambda^*(x)$, the equality case of the Fenchel-Young inequality proved there.

- The law of large numbers [37.02.02] is the degenerate $\inf I = 0$ statement underneath Cramér: $\Lambda^*$ vanishes only at $\mu_*$, so the empirical mean concentrates there at the exponential rate $\Lambda^*$, sharpening "the average converges to the mean" into "the average leaves the mean only with exponentially small probability."

- The $\mathbb{R}^d$ upper bound's reduction to supporting half-spaces is an application of the Hahn-Banach separation theorem [02.11.02]; the same geometric separation that powers biconjugation in [37.07.03] here separates a convex deviation set from the mean to produce the dominating half-space on which the scalar Chernoff bound acts.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Harald Cramér proved the one-dimensional theorem in 1938 [ref: TODO_REF Cramér 1938] for sums of i.i.d. variables possessing an analytic moment generating function, identifying the exponential rate as the conjugate of the cumulant generating function; he worked within the analytic-density framework of the Edgeworth and saddle-point tradition rather than the modern measure-theoretic LDP. The change-of-measure (tilting) technique that gives the lower bound was implicit in Cramér's saddle-point analysis and was made into a clean probabilistic argument by Esscher, whose name attaches to the "Esscher transform" in actuarial mathematics — the same exponential re-weighting. The exponential Markov inequality underlying the upper bound was systematised by Herman Chernoff in 1952 [ref: TODO_REF Chernoff 1952] in the context of asymptotically efficient hypothesis tests, and now carries his name.

The removal of the analyticity hypothesis and the recasting in the abstract LDP language is due to the development by Bahadur, Zabell, Lanford, Ellis, and others through the 1960s and 1970s; the exposed-point analysis controlling the $\mathbb{R}^d$ lower bound and the steepness condition were clarified by Gärtner and by Ellis [ref: TODO_REF Ellis 1985]. Bahadur and Rao computed the exact prefactor in 1960 [ref: TODO_REF Bahadur & Rao 1960], showing Cramér's exponential rate to be the leading term of a full saddle-point expansion. The systematic textbook treatment, including the convex-duality and exposed-point machinery used here, is that of Dembo and Zeitouni [ref: TODO_REF Dembo & Zeitouni §2.2].
