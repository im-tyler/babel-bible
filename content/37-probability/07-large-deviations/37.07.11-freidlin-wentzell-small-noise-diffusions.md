## Intuition [Beginner]

Picture a ball rolling in a landscape of hills and valleys, nudged forever by tiny random kicks. When the kicks are very weak, the ball mostly does the sensible thing: it rolls downhill and settles in the nearest valley, tracing almost exactly the path the landscape alone would dictate. But once in a very long while the kicks conspire, all pushing the same way at the same time, and the ball climbs out of its valley and over a ridge into the next one. FreidlinŌĆōWentzell theory is the precise accounting of how rare such a climb is and, just as importantly, *which* climbing route the ball almost always takes when it does happen.

The key idea is that not all unlikely paths are equally unlikely. To make the ball follow a chosen route that fights the landscape, the random kicks have to supply exactly the missing push at every instant. A route that fights hard needs a lot of conspiring kicks; a route that mostly goes with the flow needs few. We can score every candidate route by a single number ŌĆö its **cost** ŌĆö that measures the total amount of against-the-grain pushing it demands. Going with the landscape costs nothing; fighting it costs more the harder and longer you fight.

When the noise is small, the chance of seeing a given route is set almost entirely by its cost: a route of cost $c$ has probability roughly $e^{-c/\varepsilon}$, where $\varepsilon$ measures how weak the kicks are. Because this shrinks fast as the kicks weaken, the cheapest route that does the job dominates everything else. So to answer "how does the ball escape its valley?" you do not average over all escapes ŌĆö you find the single least-cost climb, the **most probable escape path**, and read the escape rate off its cost.

This least-cost number, measured from a valley bottom to any target point, is called the **quasi-potential**. In a simple landscape it is just the height of the ridge you must clear ŌĆö twice the energy barrier. The headline consequence is the escape-time law: the typical time the ball waits before jumping out of its valley grows like $e^{V/\varepsilon}$, where $V$ is the quasi-potential height of the lowest ridge. Halve the noise and you roughly square the waiting time. This single exponential, known to chemists as the Arrhenius law, governs how long a metastable state survives.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the simplest escape problem: a particle on a line in the double-well landscape with potential $U(x) = \tfrac14 x^4 - \tfrac12 x^2$, kicked by weak noise. The two valley bottoms sit at $x = -1$ and $x = +1$, and the ridge between them is the top at $x = 0$. We compute the cost of escaping the right valley.

**Step 1. Find the heights.** Plug in the three special points. At a valley bottom $x = 1$: $U(1) = \tfrac14 - \tfrac12 = -\tfrac14$. At the ridge $x = 0$: $U(0) = 0$. So the floor sits at height $-\tfrac14$ and the ridge at height $0$.

**Step 2. Measure the barrier.** The barrier is how far uphill you must climb from the valley floor to the ridge: $\Delta U = U(0) - U(1) = 0 - (-\tfrac14) = \tfrac14$.

**Step 3. Read off the quasi-potential.** For a particle in a potential landscape, the least-cost climb out of a well is twice the barrier height (the factor of two comes from the cost-accounting, which charges for fighting the downhill pull on the way up). So the quasi-potential of the escape is $V = 2\,\Delta U = 2 \times \tfrac14 = \tfrac12$.

**Step 4. Estimate the waiting time.** The typical time before the particle jumps to the other valley grows like $e^{V/\varepsilon} = e^{1/(2\varepsilon)}$. If the noise level is $\varepsilon = 0.1$, this is about $e^{5} \approx 148$ time units; at $\varepsilon = 0.05$ it is about $e^{10} \approx 22000$ ŌĆö roughly the square, for half the noise.

**What this tells us.** The whole escape problem collapses to one number, the barrier the cheapest path must clear, and that number sits in an exponent. Small changes in the barrier or the noise produce enormous changes in the waiting time. This is why metastable states ŌĆö a supercooled liquid, a folded protein, a magnetised domain ŌĆö can persist for astronomically long times and then flip almost instantly: the wait is exponential in a barrier the system rarely has the energy to clear.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $W=(W_t)_{t\in[0,T]}$ is a standard $m$-dimensional Brownian motion on a filtered space, with the It├┤ integral and It├┤'s formula of [02.15.02], and the small-noise SDE is read through the strong-solution theory of [02.15.03]. The drift $b:\mathbb{R}^d\to\mathbb{R}^d$ and dispersion $\sigma:\mathbb{R}^d\to\mathbb{R}^{d\times m}$ are bounded and globally Lipschitz, and $a:=\sigma\sigma^{\mathsf T}$ is assumed uniformly positive-definite (so $\sigma$ has a bounded left inverse $\sigma^{-1}$ on the relevant subspace; in the model case $m=d$ and $\sigma$ is invertible).

**Definition (small-noise diffusion).** For $\varepsilon>0$ the **small-noise diffusion** is the strong solution $X^\varepsilon=(X^\varepsilon_t)_{t\in[0,T]}$ of
$$
dX^\varepsilon_t = b(X^\varepsilon_t)\,dt + \sqrt{\varepsilon}\,\sigma(X^\varepsilon_t)\,dW_t,\qquad X^\varepsilon_0=x_0,
$$
viewed as a random element of the path space $C_{x_0}([0,T];\mathbb{R}^d)$ with the uniform topology. As $\varepsilon\to0$ the noise switches off and $X^\varepsilon$ converges (in probability, uniformly) to the deterministic flow $\dot\phi^0=b(\phi^0)$, $\phi^0_0=x_0$, the integral curve of the drift field [02.12.01].

**Definition (CameronŌĆōMartin / absolutely continuous paths).** A path $\phi\in C_{x_0}([0,T];\mathbb{R}^d)$ is **admissible** if it is absolutely continuous with $\phi_0=x_0$ and $\dot\phi\in L^2([0,T];\mathbb{R}^d)$; equivalently $\phi=x_0+\int_0^\cdot\dot\phi_s\,ds$ with $\dot\phi$ square-integrable. Write $H_{x_0}$ for the set of admissible paths.

**Definition (FreidlinŌĆōWentzell action functional).** The **rate function** (action) on $C_{x_0}([0,T];\mathbb{R}^d)$ is
$$
I_{0,T}(\phi)\;=\;\begin{cases}\displaystyle \tfrac12\int_0^T \big|\sigma^{-1}(\phi_t)\big(\dot\phi_t-b(\phi_t)\big)\big|^2\,dt, & \phi\in H_{x_0},\\[2mm] +\infty, & \text{otherwise.}\end{cases}
$$
The integrand $\tfrac12|\sigma^{-1}(\dot\phi-b)|^2 = \tfrac12\langle \dot\phi-b,\,a^{-1}(\dot\phi-b)\rangle$ is the local **cost density**: it vanishes exactly along the drift flow $\dot\phi=b(\phi)$ and grows quadratically with the *control* $u=\sigma^{-1}(\dot\phi-b)$ the noise must supply to force the path off the flow. $I_{0,T}$ is a good rate function: its sublevel sets $\{\phi:I_{0,T}(\phi)\le\alpha\}$ are compact in the uniform topology (Arzel├ĀŌĆōAscoli, via the $H^1$ bound the action provides).

**Definition (quasi-potential).** For points $x,y\in\mathbb{R}^d$ the **quasi-potential** is the least action to travel from $x$ to $y$ over an unbounded time horizon,
$$
V(x,y)\;=\;\inf\big\{\,I_{0,T}(\phi): T>0,\ \phi\in H,\ \phi_0=x,\ \phi_T=y\,\big\}.
$$
When $b=-\nabla U$ and $\sigma=\mathrm{Id}$ (a **reversible/gradient** system), $V(x,y)=2\big(U(y)-U(x)\big)$ for $y$ reachable uphill, the factor $2$ being the signature of the time-reversal computation below.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **The action is not the kinetic energy $\tfrac12\int|\dot\phi|^2$.** That is the Schilder rate for pure Brownian motion ($b=0$, $\sigma=\mathrm{Id}$). The drift enters through the *shifted* velocity $\dot\phi-b(\phi)$: a path that rides the drift costs nothing no matter how fast it moves, while standing still ($\dot\phi=0$) at a non-equilibrium point costs $\tfrac12\int|\sigma^{-1}b|^2>0$.
- **The quasi-potential is generally not symmetric.** $V(x,y)\ne V(y,x)$ unless the system is reversible. For a genuinely non-gradient drift (with rotational part) the cheapest uphill route and the cheapest downhill route have different costs, and the equilibrium-statistics formula $V=2\Delta U$ fails; one must solve a HamiltonŌĆōJacobi equation for $V$ instead.
- **Exit cost is read at the boundary, not the target.** For escape from a domain $D$ with attractor $x^*$, the controlling quantity is $\bar V=\inf_{y\in\partial D}V(x^*,y)$, the cheapest cost to *reach the boundary*, not the cost to any interior point; the most probable exit concentrates on the boundary minimiser $y^*$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

We prove the FreidlinŌĆōWentzell sample-path LDP by contracting Schilder's Gaussian LDP through the It├┤ solution map, using the contraction principle [37.07.08].

**Theorem (FreidlinŌĆōWentzell).** *Under the standing hypotheses, the laws of $\{X^\varepsilon\}$ on $C_{x_0}([0,T];\mathbb{R}^d)$ satisfy the large deviation principle at speed $\varepsilon$ with the good rate function $I_{0,T}$:*
$$
-\inf_{\phi\in G^\circ} I_{0,T}(\phi)\le \liminf_{\varepsilon\to0}\varepsilon\log\mathbb{P}(X^\varepsilon\in G^\circ)\le \limsup_{\varepsilon\to0}\varepsilon\log\mathbb{P}(X^\varepsilon\in \bar G)\le -\inf_{\phi\in\bar G} I_{0,T}(\phi)
$$
*for every measurable $G$.* [ref: TODO_REF Dembo & Zeitouni ┬¦5.6]

**Proof.** *Schilder input.* The scaled driving path $\sqrt\varepsilon\,W$ satisfies the LDP on $C_0([0,T];\mathbb{R}^m)$ at speed $\varepsilon$ with the good Schilder rate
$$
J(g)=\begin{cases}\tfrac12\int_0^T|\dot g_t|^2\,dt, & g\in H_0,\\ +\infty,&\text{otherwise,}\end{cases}
$$
the kinetic-energy action on the CameronŌĆōMartin space (the Gaussian special case $\Lambda^*(v)=\tfrac12|v|^2$ of the sample-path principles assembled in the projective-limit chapter).

*The It├┤ map.* Let $\Phi^\varepsilon:C_0([0,T];\mathbb{R}^m)\to C_{x_0}([0,T];\mathbb{R}^d)$ send a driver $w$ to the solution of $X=x_0+\int_0^\cdot b(X)\,ds+\sqrt\varepsilon\int_0^\cdot\sigma(X)\,dw$, so $X^\varepsilon=\Phi^\varepsilon(W)$. The map is not continuous in the uniform topology (the It├┤ integral is not a continuous functional of $w$), so the contraction principle [37.07.08] does not apply to $\Phi^\varepsilon$ directly. Two repairs make it work. First, for the *skeleton* map at $\varepsilon=0$ one solves the controlled ODE
$$
\dot\phi=b(\phi)+\sigma(\phi)\dot g,\qquad \phi_0=x_0,\qquad \phi=\Phi^0(g),
$$
which *is* continuous on the CameronŌĆōMartin space $H_0$ (Gronwall: a Lipschitz vector field driven by an $H^1$ control depends continuously on the control). Second, one shows $\Phi^\varepsilon(W)$ and the controlled skeleton differ negligibly on the exponential scale: $\{\Phi^\varepsilon(\sqrt\varepsilon W)\}$ and the family obtained by freezing $\sigma$ along the skeleton are exponentially equivalent, so $\Phi^\varepsilon$ is an **exponentially good** approximation of a continuous map in the sense of the approximation theory feeding the contraction principle. The It├┤ map is therefore continuous *where it matters* ŌĆö on the finite-action paths that carry the LDP.

*Contraction.* Applying the contraction principle [37.07.08] to the continuous skeleton map $\Phi^0:H_0\to H_{x_0}$ transports the Schilder rate to
$$
I_{0,T}(\phi)=\inf\big\{J(g): \Phi^0(g)=\phi\big\}=\inf\Big\{\tfrac12\textstyle\int_0^T|\dot g|^2: \dot\phi=b(\phi)+\sigma(\phi)\dot g\Big\}.
$$
Solving the constraint for the control gives $\dot g=\sigma^{-1}(\phi)(\dot\phi-b(\phi))$ (the unique least-norm solution when $a=\sigma\sigma^{\mathsf T}$ is invertible, by the pseudo-inverse identity $\sigma^{\mathsf T}(\sigma\sigma^{\mathsf T})^{-1}$ in the over-determined case), whence
$$
I_{0,T}(\phi)=\tfrac12\int_0^T|\sigma^{-1}(\phi_t)(\dot\phi_t-b(\phi_t))|^2\,dt,
$$
for $\phi\in H_{x_0}$, and $+\infty$ off $H_{x_0}$ (no control of finite energy produces a non-$H^1$ path). The exponential equivalence of $\Phi^\varepsilon(W)$ with the contracted family transfers the LDP from the skeleton to $X^\varepsilon$ itself, by the rate-invariance of exponentially equivalent families. Goodness of $I_{0,T}$ is inherited from goodness of $J$ under the proper continuous contraction. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem **builds toward** the exit problem and the EyringŌĆōKramers law of the Master tier, and **appears again in** every metastability computation, where the action functional is minimised to find the most probable escape path. **This is exactly** the contraction principle [37.07.08] applied to the It├┤ solution map: Schilder's kinetic-energy rate for the driver is pushed forward through the SDE's solution operator, and the quadratic control cost $\tfrac12|\dot g|^2$ becomes the shifted-velocity cost $\tfrac12|\sigma^{-1}(\dot\phi-b)|^2$ after solving the control constraint. **The foundational reason** the drift appears only through $\dot\phi-b$ is that riding the deterministic flow [02.15.03] is free ŌĆö it needs no noise ŌĆö so the action charges only the *deviation* from the flow, and the dispersion $\sigma$ sets the exchange rate between control energy and path velocity. **Putting these together**, the FreidlinŌĆōWentzell rate **generalises** Schilder ($b=0,\sigma=\mathrm{Id}$ recovers $\tfrac12\int|\dot\phi|^2$) and **is dual to** the generator picture of [02.15.03]: the action is the Lagrangian whose Hamiltonian is the symbol $H(x,p)=\langle b,p\rangle+\tfrac12\langle p,a\,p\rangle$ of the generator, and the quasi-potential solves the stationary HamiltonŌĆōJacobi equation $H(x,\nabla V)=0$. **The bridge is** the identification of "rare path of a small-noise diffusion" with "least-control-energy trajectory of a deterministic control system", the variational shadow that Schilder casts through the It├┤ map.

Show that for a gradient system $b=-\nabla U$, $\sigma=\mathrm{Id}$, the time-reversed path $\tilde\phi_t=\phi_{T-t}$ satisfies $I_{0,T}(\tilde\phi)=I_{0,T}(\phi)+2\big(U(\phi_T)-U(\phi_0)\big)$, and deduce $V(x,y)=2(U(y)-U(x))$ for an uphill minimiser.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $\tfrac12|\dot\phi+\nabla U|^2=\tfrac12|\dot\phi-\nabla U|^2+2\langle\dot\phi,\nabla U\rangle$ and integrate the cross term to a perfect difference $U(\phi_T)-U(\phi_0)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $b=-\nabla U$ the cost density is $\tfrac12|\dot\phi+\nabla U(\phi)|^2$. Expand:
$$
\tfrac12|\dot\phi+\nabla U|^2=\tfrac12|\dot\phi-\nabla U|^2+2\langle\dot\phi,\nabla U\rangle=\tfrac12|\dot\phi-\nabla U|^2+2\tfrac{d}{dt}U(\phi_t).
$$
Integrating, $I_{0,T}(\phi)=\tfrac12\int|\dot\phi-\nabla U|^2\,dt+2(U(\phi_T)-U(\phi_0))$. The first term is the action of the *time-reversed downhill* flow and is non-negative, vanishing precisely when $\dot\phi=\nabla U(\phi)$ ŌĆö the uphill anti-gradient flow. Minimising the action subject to $\phi_0=x,\phi_T=y$ thus drives the first term to $0$ along the reversed relaxation path, leaving $V(x,y)=2(U(y)-U(x))$ for $y$ uphill of $x$. The cheapest climb is the exact time-reversal of the deterministic descent, and its cost is twice the potential difference.

</details>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the HamiltonŌĆōJacobi (eikonal) equation satisfied by the quasi-potential $V(x^*,\cdot)$ of a system with attractor $x^*$. Use the Legendre dual $H(x,p)=\sup_v(\langle p,v\rangle-L(x,v))$ of the Lagrangian $L(x,v)=\tfrac12|\sigma^{-1}(x)(v-b(x))|^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $H$ explicitly; the quasi-potential is the value function of a calculus-of-variations problem, so it solves $H(x,\nabla V)=0$ off the attractor.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Legendre transform of $L(x,v)=\tfrac12\langle v-b,a^{-1}(v-b)\rangle$ (with $a=\sigma\sigma^{\mathsf T}$) is
$$
H(x,p)=\langle p,b(x)\rangle+\tfrac12\langle p,a(x)p\rangle,
$$
obtained by maximising $\langle p,v\rangle-L$ over $v$, optimal $v^*=b+a p$. As a least-action value function over paths ending at the running point, $V(x^*,\cdot)$ is a viscosity solution of the stationary HamiltonŌĆōJacobi equation
$$
H(x,\nabla V)=\langle\nabla V,b\rangle+\tfrac12\langle\nabla V,a\,\nabla V\rangle=0,\qquad V(x^*)=0,\ V\ge0.
$$
Note $H$ is exactly the symbol of the diffusion generator $L=\langle b,\nabla\rangle+\tfrac12\sum a^{ij}\partial_i\partial_j$ of [02.15.03] with $\partial\mapsto p$, so the quasi-potential is the zero-energy characteristic of the generator's symbol. In the gradient case $b=-\nabla U,a=\mathrm{Id}$ the nontrivial solution is $V=2U$, recovering Exercise 3.

</details>

State and justify the lower bound on the mean exit time: for a domain $D$ with a single stable attractor $x^*$ and $\bar V=\inf_{y\in\partial D}V(x^*,y)$, show $\liminf_{\varepsilon\to0}\varepsilon\log\mathbb{E}_{x^*}[\tau_D]\ge\bar V-\delta$ for every $\delta>0$, where $\tau_D$ is the first exit time.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Any exit path from $x^*$ to $\partial D$ has action $\ge\bar V$; the LDP upper bound on reaching $\partial D$ in time $T$ gives a probability $\le e^{-(\bar V-\delta)/\varepsilon}$, and the exit time is geometric in this small probability.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix $T$ and $\delta>0$. The event $\{\tau_D\le T\}$ requires the path to travel from $x^*$ to $\partial D$ in time $T$, a closed set of paths whose minimal action is $\ge\bar V$ (every such path costs at least the cheapest boundary-reaching cost). By the FreidlinŌĆōWentzell upper bound, $\limsup_\varepsilon\varepsilon\log\mathbb{P}_{x^*}(\tau_D\le T)\le-(\bar V-\tfrac\delta2)$ for small $\varepsilon$, so $p_\varepsilon:=\mathbb{P}_{x^*}(\tau_D\le T)\le e^{-(\bar V-\delta)/\varepsilon}$. Partitioning time into blocks of length $T$ and using the strong Markov property (each block independently fails to exit with probability $\ge 1-p_\varepsilon$), $\tau_D$ stochastically dominates $T$ times a geometric variable of success $p_\varepsilon$, so $\mathbb{E}_{x^*}[\tau_D]\ge T(1-p_\varepsilon)/p_\varepsilon\ge\tfrac{T}{2}e^{(\bar V-\delta)/\varepsilon}$ for small $\varepsilon$. Taking $\varepsilon\log$ and $\liminf$ gives $\liminf_\varepsilon\varepsilon\log\mathbb{E}_{x^*}[\tau_D]\ge\bar V-\delta$. The matching upper bound (a cheap control steering to the boundary in bounded time with probability $\gtrsim e^{-(\bar V+\delta)/\varepsilon}$) yields $\lim_\varepsilon\varepsilon\log\mathbb{E}_{x^*}[\tau_D]=\bar V$.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The exit problem and the EyringŌĆōKramers / Arrhenius law

Let $D$ be a bounded domain with smooth boundary whose deterministic flow $\dot\phi=b(\phi)$ has a single asymptotically stable equilibrium $x^*\in D$ attracting all of $\bar D$, and set $\bar V=\inf_{y\in\partial D}V(x^*,y)$. The first exit time $\tau_D=\inf\{t:X^\varepsilon_t\notin D\}$ obeys the **Arrhenius law** [ref: TODO_REF Freidlin & Wentzell Ch. 4]
$$
\lim_{\varepsilon\to0}\varepsilon\log\mathbb{E}_{x}[\tau_D]=\bar V\qquad\text{uniformly in }x\text{ on compacts of }D,
$$
and the **exit location** concentrates: for any neighbourhood $N$ of the boundary minimiser $y^*=\arg\min_{\partial D}V(x^*,\cdot)$, $\mathbb{P}_x(X^\varepsilon_{\tau_D}\in N)\to1$. The mechanism is the variational dichotomy: reaching the boundary requires action $\ge\bar V$, so the wait is exponentially long; but a control of action just above $\bar V$ steers to $y^*$ in bounded time, so the wait is no longer than $e^{(\bar V+\delta)/\varepsilon}$. The exit is thus a rare event that, when it happens, follows the single least-action path through $y^*$ ŌĆö the most probable exit path, which for a gradient system is the time-reversed relaxation over the lowest saddle on $\partial D$.

The exponential rate $\bar V$ is the large-deviation content; the *sharp* prefactor is finer. For the reversible gradient case $b=-\nabla U$, $\sigma=\mathrm{Id}$, with $U$ having a quadratic well at $x^*$ (Hessian $\nabla^2U(x^*)$) and the relevant saddle $z^*$ on $\partial D$ having a single negative Hessian eigenvalue $-\lambda^-(z^*)<0$, the **sharp EyringŌĆōKramers formula** reads [ref: TODO_REF Bovier & den Hollander Ch. 9]
$$
\mathbb{E}_{x^*}[\tau_D]\sim\frac{2\pi}{\lambda^-(z^*)}\sqrt{\frac{|\det\nabla^2U(z^*)|}{\det\nabla^2U(x^*)}}\;\exp\!\Big(\tfrac{2\,(U(z^*)-U(x^*))}{\varepsilon}\Big).
$$
The exponent is the FreidlinŌĆōWentzell quasi-potential $\bar V=2(U(z^*)-U(x^*))$; the prefactor is a ratio of Gaussian widths at the well and the saddle (curvatures), with the unstable eigenvalue $\lambda^-(z^*)$ setting the rate at which probability leaks across the saddle. The modern derivation routes the prefactor through Newtonian capacities (BovierŌĆōEckhoffŌĆōGayrerŌĆōKlein), tying the escape rate to the spectral gap of the generator: the smallest non-zero eigenvalue of $-L$ is asymptotically $1/\mathbb{E}_{x^*}[\tau_D]$, the metastable relaxation rate.

### Metastability and the hierarchy of cycles

When the deterministic system has several stable equilibria $x_1^*,\dots,x_n^*$, the small-noise diffusion spends exponentially long stretches near one equilibrium, then makes a rapid transition to another, then waits again ŌĆö a **metastable** Markov jump chain on the set of wells, with transition rates $\exp(-V(x_i^*,x_j^*)/\varepsilon)$ governed by the pairwise quasi-potentials. Freidlin and Wentzell organise the long-time behaviour by a **hierarchy of cycles**: at the coarsest time scale the process equilibrates within a basin; at successively longer (exponentially separated) scales it overcomes successively higher barriers, the order of transitions fixed by a minimal-spanning-tree / $W$-graph computation on the quasi-potential matrix [ref: TODO_REF Freidlin & Wentzell Ch. 6]. The invariant measure concentrates, as $\varepsilon\to0$, on the global minimiser of a graph-theoretic potential $W(x_i^*)$ built from the spanning trees, the large-deviation generalisation of the Gibbs measure picking out the deepest well. This cycle hierarchy is the rigorous backbone of simulated annealing, where a slowly decreasing $\varepsilon=\varepsilon(t)$ is tuned so the process funnels into the global minimum.

### Non-gradient drifts and the HamiltonŌĆōJacobi route

When $b$ is not a gradient, the quasi-potential is no longer $2U$ and is generally non-symmetric. It is the value function of the deterministic control problem and solves the stationary HamiltonŌĆōJacobi equation $H(x,\nabla V)=\langle b,\nabla V\rangle+\tfrac12\langle\nabla V,a\,\nabla V\rangle=0$, whose Hamiltonian is the symbol of the generator [02.15.03]. The drift decomposes (FreidlinŌĆōWentzell / Graham) as $b=-\tfrac12 a\nabla V+\ell$ with $\langle\ell,\nabla V\rangle=0$: a gradient-like part $-\tfrac12 a\nabla V$ driving relaxation down the quasi-potential and a transverse circulation $\ell$ that does no work against $V$. The most probable exit path is the *instanton*: the heteroclinic orbit of the doubled (Hamiltonian) dynamics $\dot x=\partial_pH$, $\dot p=-\partial_xH$ on the zero-energy surface $H=0$, climbing from the attractor along $p=\nabla V$ to the saddle. This Hamiltonian/instanton formulation is the bridge from FreidlinŌĆōWentzell theory to the path-integral and WKB treatments of rare events in physics.

**Synthesis.** **The central insight** is that a small-noise diffusion is, on the large-deviation scale, a deterministic control system: the action is the control energy, the most probable rare path is the optimal control, and the quasi-potential is the value function, so rare-event behaviour is read off a HamiltonŌĆōJacobi problem with no probability left in it. **This is exactly** the contraction of Schilder's Gaussian rate through the It├┤ map [37.07.08], **putting these together** with the SDE solution theory [02.15.03] so the driver's kinetic-energy cost becomes the shifted-velocity action. **The foundational reason** the exit time is $e^{\bar V/\varepsilon}$ is the variational dichotomy ŌĆö reaching the boundary costs at least $\bar V$, and a near-optimal control achieves it ŌĆö so the LDP bounds pin the exponent exactly, while the sharp Kramers prefactor **generalises** this to next order through capacities and Hessians. **The bridge is** the dual reading of one object: the action is the Lagrangian, the generator's symbol its Hamiltonian, the quasi-potential the zero-energy HamiltonŌĆōJacobi solution, and the metastable jump rates $e^{-V(x_i,x_j)/\varepsilon}$ assemble into the cycle hierarchy. This **is dual to** the generator picture of [02.15.03], where the escape rate is the principal spectral gap of $-L$. So FreidlinŌĆōWentzell theory is Hamilton's least-action principle for the rare excursions of a noisy flow, and **appears again in** reaction-rate theory, stochastic resonance, and simulated annealing.

## Full proof set [Master]

The FreidlinŌĆōWentzell sample-path LDP is proved in full in the Key theorem section. The exit-problem and gradient-structure claims are recorded here.

**Proposition 1 (gradient quasi-potential).** *For $b=-\nabla U$ and $\sigma=\mathrm{Id}$ with $U\in C^2$, the quasi-potential from a critical point $x$ to a point $y$ reachable along an anti-gradient orbit satisfies $V(x,y)=2(U(y)-U(x))$, attained by the time-reversed relaxation path.*

*Proof.* For any admissible $\phi$ with $\phi_0=x,\phi_T=y$, the cost density expands as
$$
\tfrac12|\dot\phi+\nabla U(\phi)|^2=\tfrac12|\dot\phi-\nabla U(\phi)|^2+2\langle\dot\phi,\nabla U(\phi)\rangle=\tfrac12|\dot\phi-\nabla U(\phi)|^2+2\tfrac{d}{dt}U(\phi_t).
$$
Integrating over $[0,T]$, $I_{0,T}(\phi)=\tfrac12\int_0^T|\dot\phi-\nabla U(\phi)|^2\,dt+2(U(y)-U(x))$. The first term is non-negative and vanishes iff $\dot\phi=\nabla U(\phi)$ for a.e. $t$, i.e. $\phi$ follows the *reversed* (uphill, anti-gradient) flow. Such a connecting orbit exists for $y$ on the unstable manifold structure reachable from $x$ (taking $T\to\infty$ to accommodate the asymptotic approach to critical points), and along it $I=2(U(y)-U(x))$. Hence the infimum over $T>0$ and admissible $\phi$ equals $2(U(y)-U(x))$, attained in the limit by the time-reversed relaxation. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (lower bound on the mean exit time).** *Let $D$ be bounded with attractor $x^*$ and $\bar V=\inf_{\partial D}V(x^*,\cdot)$. Then $\liminf_{\varepsilon\to0}\varepsilon\log\mathbb{E}_{x^*}[\tau_D]\ge\bar V$.*

*Proof.* Fix $\delta>0$ and a horizon $T$. The set $A_T=\{\phi\in C_{x^*}([0,T]):\phi_t\in\partial D\text{ for some }t\le T\}$ is closed and every $\phi\in A_T$ has $I_{0,T}(\phi)\ge\inf\{I_{0,T}(\psi):\psi\text{ hits }\partial D\}\ge\bar V$ (a boundary-hitting path realises a connection of cost $\ge V(x^*,\partial D)=\bar V$). The FreidlinŌĆōWentzell upper bound gives $\limsup_\varepsilon\varepsilon\log\mathbb{P}_{x^*}(X^\varepsilon\in A_T)\le-\inf_{A_T}I_{0,T}\le-\bar V$, so $p_\varepsilon:=\mathbb{P}_{x^*}(\tau_D\le T)\le e^{-(\bar V-\delta)/\varepsilon}$ for small $\varepsilon$. By the strong Markov property at the times $T,2T,\dots$, starting each block from a point that has not yet exited (and using uniformity of the bound over compacts of $D$ near $x^*$, together with the fact that the flow returns to a neighbourhood of $x^*$), the number of length-$T$ blocks before exit dominates a geometric variable with success probability $p_\varepsilon$. Hence $\mathbb{E}_{x^*}[\tau_D]\ge T\cdot\tfrac{1-p_\varepsilon}{p_\varepsilon}\ge\tfrac{T}{2}\,e^{(\bar V-\delta)/\varepsilon}$ for small $\varepsilon$. Taking $\varepsilon\log$ and $\liminf$, then $\delta\downarrow0$, gives $\liminf_\varepsilon\varepsilon\log\mathbb{E}_{x^*}[\tau_D]\ge\bar V$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (upper bound on the mean exit time).** *Under the hypotheses of Proposition 2, $\limsup_{\varepsilon\to0}\varepsilon\log\mathbb{E}_{x^*}[\tau_D]\le\bar V$, so the limit equals $\bar V$.*

*Proof.* Fix $\delta>0$. By definition of $\bar V$ there is a finite time $T_0$ and an admissible control path $\phi^*$ from (a neighbourhood of) $x^*$ to the boundary minimiser $y^*\in\partial D$ with $I_{0,T_0}(\phi^*)<\bar V+\tfrac\delta2$. The FreidlinŌĆōWentzell lower bound, applied to a uniform tube of paths around $\phi^*$ that exit $D$ by time $T_0$, gives
$$
\liminf_\varepsilon\varepsilon\log\mathbb{P}_{x}(\tau_D\le T_0)\ge-I_{0,T_0}(\phi^*)>-(\bar V+\delta)
$$
uniformly for $x$ in a neighbourhood of $x^*$. Thus $q_\varepsilon:=\inf_x\mathbb{P}_x(\tau_D\le T_0)\ge e^{-(\bar V+\delta)/\varepsilon}$ for small $\varepsilon$. Since from any starting point the process either exits within $T_0$ with probability $\ge q_\varepsilon$ or (with the remaining probability) is found near $x^*$ after a further bounded time by attraction of the deterministic flow, the exit time is stochastically dominated by $T_0$ times a geometric variable of success $q_\varepsilon$: $\mathbb{E}_{x^*}[\tau_D]\le \frac{C T_0}{q_\varepsilon}\le C T_0\,e^{(\bar V+\delta)/\varepsilon}$. Taking $\varepsilon\log$, $\limsup$, and $\delta\downarrow0$ gives $\limsup_\varepsilon\varepsilon\log\mathbb{E}_{x^*}[\tau_D]\le\bar V$. With Proposition 2 the limit is exactly $\bar V$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (HamiltonŌĆōJacobi characterisation of the quasi-potential).** *Off the attractor $x^*$, the quasi-potential $V(x^*,\cdot)$ is a viscosity solution of $\langle b,\nabla V\rangle+\tfrac12\langle\nabla V,a\,\nabla V\rangle=0$ with $V(x^*)=0$, $V\ge0$, where $a=\sigma\sigma^{\mathsf T}$.*

*Proof.* The quasi-potential is the value function $V(y)=\inf\{I_{0,T}(\phi):\phi_0=x^*,\phi_T=y,T>0\}$ of a deterministic Bolza control problem with Lagrangian $L(x,v)=\tfrac12\langle v-b,a^{-1}(v-b)\rangle$. By the dynamic programming principle, value functions of such problems are viscosity solutions of the stationary HamiltonŌĆōJacobi equation $H(x,\nabla V)=0$, where $H(x,p)=\sup_v(\langle p,v\rangle-L(x,v))$ is the Legendre dual. Computing the supremum: $\partial_v(\langle p,v\rangle-\tfrac12\langle v-b,a^{-1}(v-b)\rangle)=p-a^{-1}(v-b)=0$ gives the optimal $v^*=b+a p$, and substituting,
$$
H(x,p)=\langle p,b+ap\rangle-\tfrac12\langle ap,a^{-1}ap\rangle=\langle p,b\rangle+\langle p,ap\rangle-\tfrac12\langle p,ap\rangle=\langle p,b\rangle+\tfrac12\langle p,ap\rangle.
$$
Hence $V$ solves $\langle b,\nabla V\rangle+\tfrac12\langle\nabla V,a\nabla V\rangle=0$. The boundary data $V(x^*)=0$ holds because the constant path at the attractor has zero action, and $V\ge0$ since the action is non-negative. This $H$ is the principal symbol of the generator $L$ of [02.15.03] under $\nabla\mapsto p$, identifying the quasi-potential as the generator's zero-energy characteristic. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

The contraction principle [37.07.08] is the engine of the entire unit: the FreidlinŌĆōWentzell rate is Schilder's kinetic-energy rate pushed forward through the SDE's solution (It├┤) map, with the quasi-potential a minimisation of the contracted action. Because the It├┤ map is not uniformly continuous, the contraction is applied to the continuous controlled-ODE skeleton and transferred to the diffusion by exponential equivalence ŌĆö exactly the exponentially-good-approximation extension of the contraction principle developed there, so this unit is the flagship application of that machinery to an infinite-dimensional, non-continuous transport.

The It├┤ integral and It├┤'s formula [02.15.02] supply the stochastic integral $\sqrt\varepsilon\int\sigma(X)\,dW$ that the small-noise SDE runs on, and the non-continuity of $w\mapsto\int\sigma\,dw$ in the uniform topology is precisely the obstruction the proof must route around; It├┤'s formula is also what produces the generator symbol $H(x,p)$ appearing as the Hamiltonian of the quasi-potential.

Stochastic differential equations, diffusions, and the generator [02.15.03] is the substrate: the small-noise diffusion is the SDE of that unit with dispersion $\sqrt\varepsilon\,\sigma$, its $\varepsilon\to0$ limit is the drift flow, and the exit problem refines that unit's Dynkin/exit-time analysis. The Hamiltonian $H(x,p)=\langle b,p\rangle+\tfrac12\langle p,ap\rangle$ of the quasi-potential is the symbol of that unit's generator $L$, and the sharp Kramers prefactor is governed by the spectral gap of $-L$, making the metastable escape rate the principal eigenvalue of the generator built there.

Schilder's theorem (co-produced as [37.07.10] in this wave) is the Gaussian input contracted here: it is the $b=0,\sigma=\mathrm{Id}$ instance whose kinetic-energy rate $\tfrac12\int|\dot\phi|^2$ becomes the shifted action $\tfrac12\int|\sigma^{-1}(\dot\phi-b)|^2$ after the It├┤ map, so the FreidlinŌĆōWentzell theorem is Schilder plus drift-and-dispersion transport.

The G├żrtnerŌĆōEllis theorem [37.07.04] supplies the finite-dimensional marginal LDPs (from the cumulant generating function of increments) that the sample-path principle perturbs, and the half-space tightness criteria there are what close the finite-time-marginal estimates into the full path-space principle that FreidlinŌĆōWentzell specialises to the small-noise scaling.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The physics came first. In 1935 Henry Eyring's transition-state theory (Journal of Chemical Physics 3, 107ŌĆō115) [ref: TODO_REF Eyring 1935] expressed a chemical reaction rate as an Arrhenius exponential of an activation energy times a frequency prefactor, and in 1940 Hendrik Kramers (Physica 7, 284ŌĆō304) [ref: TODO_REF Kramers 1940] modelled the reacting molecule as a Brownian particle escaping a potential well, computing the mean escape time as $e^{\Delta U/(k_BT)}$ with a curvature-dependent prefactor ŌĆö the first derivation of what is now the EyringŌĆōKramers law. These were physical computations on the FokkerŌĆōPlanck equation, without a general large-deviation principle behind them.

The rigorous and general theory is due to Mark Freidlin and Alexander Wentzell, whose 1970 work and the 1979 monograph *Random Perturbations of Dynamical Systems* [ref: TODO_REF Freidlin & Wentzell Ch. 4] established the sample-path LDP for $dX^\varepsilon=b(X^\varepsilon)dt+\sqrt\varepsilon\,\sigma(X^\varepsilon)dW$ with the action functional $I(\phi)=\tfrac12\int|\sigma^{-1}(\dot\phi-b)|^2$, and used it to solve the exit problem, prove the Arrhenius law $\varepsilon\log\mathbb{E}\tau_D\to\bar V$, and organise multi-well metastability through the hierarchy of cycles. Their route ŌĆö contracting Schilder's 1966 Gaussian asymptotics [ref: TODO_REF Schilder 1966] through the SDE solution map ŌĆö placed the action-functional rate on the same footing as Mogulskii's trajectory principle, and identified the quasi-potential as the value function of a deterministic control problem solving a HamiltonŌĆōJacobi equation. The sharp prefactor, beyond the large-deviation exponent, was obtained rigorously much later by Bovier, Eckhoff, Gayrard, and Klein through a potential-theoretic (capacity) analysis tying the escape rate to the spectral gap of the generator [ref: TODO_REF Bovier & den Hollander Ch. 9]. The lineage runs from a chemist's reaction-rate formula to a theorem about the spectrum of a second-order elliptic operator, each layer sharpening the previous: Eyring and Kramers fixed the form, Freidlin and Wentzell fixed the exponent rigorously and in full generality, and the potential-theoretic school fixed the constant.
