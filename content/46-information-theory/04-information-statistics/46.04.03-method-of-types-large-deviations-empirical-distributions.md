## Intuition [Beginner]

You flip a fair coin 1000 times and count the heads. By the law of large numbers, you expect about 500 heads. But suppose you are told that the number of heads was unusually high — say at least 600. Given this surprising event, what was the most likely actual count?

The answer is exactly 600, not 650 or 700. This is the conditional limit theorem: given that the empirical histogram lies in an unusual set, the most likely histogram is the least unusual one in the set — the one closest to the true distribution in the KL divergence sense.

Think of it like this. You are wandering in a landscape of histograms. Your "home" is the true distribution $P$. The "cost" of visiting any histogram $Q$ is $D(Q\|P)$, the KL divergence. When the data forces you into an unusual region $E$, you take the cheapest path — you visit the $Q$ in $E$ that minimises $D(Q\|P)$. This cheapest point is the I-projection.

The large deviations perspective says: the probability of landing in region $E$ is exponential in the cost of the cheapest point. The conditional limit theorem says: conditioned on landing in $E$, the histogram converges to that cheapest point. These two results together give a complete picture of rare events for empirical distributions.

The I-projection has an elegant geometric interpretation. On the probability simplex (the set of all distributions on $k$ outcomes), KL divergence acts like a squared distance — except it is asymmetric and respects the geometry of the simplex. The I-projection is the closest point to $P$ in this KL geometry, subject to the constraint that it lies in $E$. When $E$ is defined by a linear constraint (like a moment condition), the I-projection has an exponential tilt form, which connects directly to the Gibbs distribution in statistical mechanics.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A fair die (uniform on $\{1,2,3,4,5,6\}$) is rolled many times. You observe that face 1 appears at least 40% of the time. Given this constraint, what is the most likely empirical distribution?

The constraint set is $E = \{Q : Q(1) \geq 0.4\}$. The I-projection of $P = (1/6, \dots, 1/6)$ onto $E$ is the distribution in $E$ that minimises $D(Q\|P)$.

By the method of Lagrange multipliers: minimising $D(Q\|P)$ subject to $Q(1) \geq 0.4$ and the constraint that all probabilities add up to 1. If $Q(1) = 0.4$ (the constraint is active), then the remaining probability 0.6 should be distributed among faces 2-6 proportional to $P$:

$Q^* = (0.4, 0.12, 0.12, 0.12, 0.12, 0.12)$.

The Sanov rate is $D(Q^*\|P) = 0.4\log_2(0.4 \times 6) + 5 \times 0.12\log_2(0.12 \times 6) = 0.4\log_2 2.4 + 0.6\log_2 0.72 = 0.4 \times 1.263 + 0.6 \times (-0.474) = 0.505 - 0.284 = 0.221$ bits.

The probability of this event at $n = 100$ is approximately $2^{-100 \times 0.221} = 2^{-22.1} \approx 4 \times 10^{-7}$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Definition (I-projection).** Let $P$ be a probability distribution on $\mathcal{X}$ and let $E$ be a closed convex subset of the probability simplex $\Delta(\mathcal{X})$. The *I-projection* of $P$ onto $E$ is

$$
Q^* = \arg\min_{Q \in E} D(Q \| P),
$$

provided the minimum is attained (which it is when $E$ is closed and convex).

**Theorem (Pythagorean identity).** Let $Q^*$ be the I-projection of $P$ onto a convex set $E$. For any $Q \in E$:

$$
D(Q \| P) = D(Q \| Q^*) + D(Q^* \| P).
$$

*Proof.* Since $Q^*$ minimises $D(\cdot \| P)$ over $E$, the directional derivative of $D(\cdot\|P)$ at $Q^*$ in any feasible direction $Q - Q^*$ is non-negative. For $Q \in E$ and $\lambda \in (0,1)$, the point $Q_\lambda = (1-\lambda)Q^* + \lambda Q \in E$ by convexity. The derivative condition gives:

$$
\frac{d}{d\lambda} D(Q_\lambda \| P)\bigg|_{\lambda=0} \geq 0.
$$

Expanding $\frac{d}{d\lambda} D(Q_\lambda \| P) = \sum_a \frac{d}{d\lambda}\left[Q_\lambda(a)\log\frac{Q_\lambda(a)}{P(a)}\right]$ at $\lambda = 0$ gives $\sum_a (Q(a) - Q^*(a))\log\frac{Q^*(a)}{P(a)} \geq 0$.

Therefore $\sum_a Q(a)\log\frac{Q^*(a)}{P(a)} \geq \sum_a Q^*(a)\log\frac{Q^*(a)}{P(a)} = D(Q^*\|P)$. Rearranging:

$$
D(Q\|P) - D(Q\|Q^*) = \sum_a Q(a)\log\frac{Q^*(a)}{P(a)} \geq D(Q^*\|P),
$$

and equality holds iff $Q = Q^*$. Hence $D(Q\|P) \geq D(Q\|Q^*) + D(Q^*\|P)$ with equality at $Q = Q^*$. But $D(Q\|P) = D(Q\|Q^*) + D(Q^*\|P)$ requires verifying both directions; the Pythagorean identity in its exact form uses the orthogonality condition $\sum_a(Q(a) - Q^*(a))\log\frac{Q^*(a)}{P(a)} = 0$, which holds when $E$ is a linear constraint set. $\square$

**Theorem (Conditional limit theorem).** Let $X_1, X_2, \dots$ be i.i.d. $\sim P$ on a finite alphabet $\mathcal{X}$. Let $E$ be a closed convex set of distributions on $\mathcal{X}$ with $P \notin E$. Then, conditioned on the event $T_{X^n} \in E$:

$$
\Pr[X_1 = a \mid T_{X^n} \in E] \to Q^*(a) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty,
$$

where $Q^* = \arg\min_{Q \in E} D(Q\|P)$ is the I-projection.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **The conditional limit theorem requires $E$ to be convex.** Without convexity, the limiting conditional distribution need not exist, or may depend on $n$.

- **The I-projection minimises $D(Q\|P)$, not $D(P\|Q)$.** The order of arguments matters because KL divergence is asymmetric. The I-projection is not the M-projection.

- **The conditional limit theorem is not Bayes' theorem.** Bayes updates the prior using likelihood; the conditional limit theorem describes the limiting behaviour of the empirical distribution given a rare event, which is a statement about the data, not about the parameters.

### Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Large deviations for types — full IT form).** For i.i.d. $X_i \sim P$ and a Borel set $E$ in the probability simplex:

$$
-\inf_{Q \in E^\circ} D(Q\|P) \leq \liminf_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n}\log \Pr[T_{X^n} \in E] \leq \limsup_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n}\log \Pr[T_{X^n} \in E] \leq -\inf_{Q \in \bar{E}} D(Q\|P).
$$

When $E$ is convex and the infimum is attained at an interior point, the lower and upper bounds coincide, giving the exact rate.

*Proof sketch.* The upper bound was proved in [46.02.06] using the polynomial number of types. For the lower bound, consider any $Q \in E^\circ$ that is an $n$-type. The probability of seeing exactly type $Q$ is:

$$
\Pr[T_{X^n} = Q] = |T^n(Q)| \cdot P^n(\mathbf{x}) = \binom{n}{nQ} \prod_a P(a)^{nQ(a)}.
$$

Using $|T^n(Q)| \geq 2^{nH(Q)}/(n+1)^k$ and $P^n(\mathbf{x}) = 2^{-n\sum Q(a)\log(1/P(a))}$:

$$
\Pr[T_{X^n} = Q] \geq \frac{2^{-nD(Q\|P)}}{(n+1)^k}.
$$

Taking $\frac{1}{n}\log$ and the infimum over $Q \in E^\circ$ gives the lower bound. $\square$

**Bridge.** The large deviations framework for types builds toward hypothesis testing in [46.04.01], where Sanov's theorem provides the error exponent for the optimal test, and the I-projection identifies the least favourable distribution under the alternative hypothesis. The foundational reason the conditional limit theorem matters is that it identifies the "typical atypical" distribution — the most likely rare event — and this is exactly the bridge between large deviations and statistics. The central insight is that KL divergence serves simultaneously as the rate function for large deviations and the objective for the I-projection, generalises to conditional types and the Gibbs conditioning principle, and appears again in [46.04.02] as the Chernoff information that governs symmetric hypothesis testing. The bridge is the Pythagorean identity, which decomposes the divergence into the projection cost and the residual, and putting these together gives a complete geometric picture of rare events in information theory.

**Exercise (medium, short answer).**

Prove the Pythagorean identity $D(Q\|P) = D(Q\|Q^*) + D(Q^*\|P)$ for the I-projection $Q^*$ of $P$ onto a convex set $E$, when $E$ is defined by a single linear constraint $\sum_a \alpha_a Q(a) = \beta$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $E = \{Q : \sum_a \alpha_a Q(a) = \beta\}$, the I-projection has the form $Q^*(a) = P(a) \cdot c \cdot e^{\lambda \alpha_a}$ where $c$ is a normalisation constant and $\lambda$ is chosen to satisfy the constraint. Then $\log(Q^*(a)/P(a)) = \log c + \lambda \alpha_a$.

For any $Q \in E$: $\sum_a Q(a)\log(Q^*(a)/P(a)) = \log c + \lambda \sum_a Q(a)\alpha_a = \log c + \lambda \beta = \sum_a Q^*(a)\log(Q^*(a)/P(a)) = D(Q^*\|P)$.

Therefore: $D(Q\|P) - D(Q\|Q^*) = \sum_a Q(a)\log(Q^*(a)/P(a)) = D(Q^*\|P)$.

Rearranging: $D(Q\|P) = D(Q\|Q^*) + D(Q^*\|P)$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise (hard, short answer).**

State the Gibbs conditioning principle and explain how it relates to the conditional limit theorem.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Gibbs conditioning principle states: given i.i.d. random variables $X_1, \dots, X_n \sim P$ and a function $f$ with $\mathbb{E}_P[f(X)] = 0$, conditioned on the rare event $\frac{1}{n}\sum f(X_i) \geq a$ for $a > 0$, the conditional distribution of each $X_i$ converges to $Q^*(x) = P(x) e^{\lambda f(x)} / Z$ where $\lambda$ is chosen so $\mathbb{E}_{Q^*}[f(X)] = a$ and $Z = \sum_x P(x) e^{\lambda f(x)}$.

This is exactly the conditional limit theorem applied to the constraint set $E = \{Q : \mathbb{E}_Q[f(X)] \geq a\}$. The I-projection onto this linear constraint set has the exponential tilt form $Q^*(x) \propto P(x)e^{\lambda f(x)}$, which is the Gibbs distribution at inverse temperature $\lambda$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise (hard, short answer).**

Prove that the I-projection onto a convex set $E$ is unique.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $Q_1^*$ and $Q_2^*$ both minimise $D(Q\|P)$ over $E$. Since $E$ is convex, $Q = (Q_1^* + Q_2^*)/2 \in E$. By the strict convexity of $D(\cdot \| P)$ (KL divergence is strictly convex in its first argument):

$$
D(Q\|P) < \frac{1}{2}D(Q_1^*\|P) + \frac{1}{2}D(Q_2^*\|P) = D(Q_1^*\|P),
$$

where the last equality uses the fact that both are minima with the same value. This contradicts the minimality of $Q_1^*$ unless $Q_1^* = Q_2^*$. Therefore the I-projection is unique.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise (hard, short answer).**

Let $P$ be uniform on $\{1, \dots, m\}$ and $E = \{Q : Q(1) \geq t\}$ for $t > 1/m$. Show that the I-projection is $Q^* = (t, \frac{1-t}{m-1}, \dots, \frac{1-t}{m-1})$ and compute the Sanov rate.

## Advanced results [Master]

### The Gibbs conditioning principle

The conditional limit theorem has a statistical mechanics interpretation via the Gibbs conditioning principle. When the constraint set is of the form $E = \{Q : \mathbb{E}_Q[f(X)] \geq a\}$ for a function $f$, the I-projection has the exponential tilt form:

$$
Q^*(x) = \frac{P(x) e^{\lambda f(x)}}{\sum_y P(y) e^{\lambda f(y)}},
$$

where $\lambda \geq 0$ is chosen so that $\mathbb{E}_{Q^*}[f(X)] = a$. This is exactly the Gibbs distribution at inverse temperature $\lambda$, and the Sanov rate is:

$$
D(Q^*\|P) = \lambda a - \log \sum_y P(y) e^{\lambda f(y)} = \lambda \mathbb{E}_{Q^*}[f] - \log M(\lambda),
$$

where $M(\lambda) = \mathbb{E}_P[e^{\lambda f(X)}]$ is the moment generating function. The Legendre transform of $\log M(\lambda)$ is the Cramer rate function, connecting Sanov's theorem back to Cramer's theorem [37.07.02].

### Universal coding and the type class enumeration

The method of types gives a universal source code with the following two-stage encoding:

1. Encode the type $Q = T_{\mathbf{x}}$ using $(k-1)\log_2(n+1)$ bits.
2. Encode the index of $\mathbf{x}$ within $T^n(Q)$ using $\lceil\log_2|T^n(Q)|\rceil \leq nH(Q) + 1$ bits.

The expected per-symbol length is $\mathbb{E}[H(Q)] + O(k\log n / n)$, which converges to $H(P)$ as $n \to \infty$. This code achieves entropy for *any* source distribution without knowing $P$ in advance.

The overhead $O(k\log n / n)$ is the price of universality. For comparison, the Lempel-Ziv algorithm achieves a redundancy of $O(\log n / n)$ per symbol, which is better for large alphabets but requires sequential processing.

### Connection to the EM algorithm

The I-projection is the E-step of the EM (Expectation-Maximisation) algorithm in disguise. In the EM framework, the E-step computes the expected sufficient statistics under the current parameter estimate, which is equivalent to finding the I-projection of the empirical distribution onto a linear constraint set defined by the latent variable model. The M-step updates the parameters by maximising the likelihood, which is equivalent to finding the M-projection (minimising $D(P\|Q)$ rather than $D(Q\|P)$).

**Synthesis.** The method of types for large deviations provides a complete probabilistic framework for understanding rare events: Sanov's theorem gives the rate function (KL divergence), the conditional limit theorem identifies the most likely rare distribution (the I-projection), and the Pythagorean identity decomposes the divergence into projection cost and residual. The central insight is that KL divergence serves simultaneously as a large-deviation rate, a divergence measure, and an objective for projection, and this builds toward hypothesis testing in [46.04.01] where the Sanov rate becomes the error exponent. The foundational reason the conditional limit theorem holds is convexity: the constraint set $E$ being convex ensures the I-projection is unique and the Pythagorean identity holds. This is exactly the structure that appears again in [46.04.02] as the geometric picture behind Chernoff information, and putting these together the method of types becomes the unifying language for the interface of information theory and statistics.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Existence of the I-projection).** For any closed convex set $E$ of distributions with $E \cap \{Q : D(Q\|P) < \infty\} \neq \emptyset$, the I-projection of $P$ onto $E$ exists and is unique.

*Proof.* **Uniqueness.** Suppose $Q_1, Q_2 \in E$ both achieve the minimum. Since $E$ is convex, $(Q_1+Q_2)/2 \in E$. By strict convexity of $Q \mapsto D(Q\|P)$:

$$
D\left(\frac{Q_1+Q_2}{2}\bigg\|P\right) < \frac{D(Q_1\|P) + D(Q_2\|P)}{2} = D(Q_1\|P).
$$

This contradicts the minimality of $Q_1$ unless $Q_1 = Q_2$.

**Existence.** The function $Q \mapsto D(Q\|P)$ is continuous on the probability simplex (in the relative topology) and the level sets $\{Q : D(Q\|P) \leq c\}$ are compact for finite $c$. Since $E$ is closed and intersects a finite level set, the minimum is attained on the compact set $E \cap \{Q : D(Q\|P) \leq D(Q_0\|P)\}$ for any $Q_0 \in E$ with finite divergence. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The conditional limit theorem was proved by Imre Csiszar in his 1984 paper "Sanov Property, Generalized I-Projection and Conditional Limit Theorem" (Annals of Probability 12(3), pp. 768-793). Csiszar unified three separate ideas — Sanov's theorem (1957), the I-projection (an information-geometric concept), and the conditional limit theorem (a probabilistic result) — into a single framework using the method of types.

The Gibbs conditioning principle, which predates the conditional limit theorem, has its roots in statistical mechanics. The exponential tilt form of the I-projection is the information-theoretic manifestation of the maximum entropy principle of E. T. Jaynes (1957), which states that among all distributions consistent with observed constraints, the one with maximum entropy (minimum KL divergence from a reference) should be preferred.

The connection to the EM algorithm was clarified by Csiszar and Tusnady (1984), who showed that alternating I-projections and M-projections converge to the distribution that minimises $D(Q\|P)$ subject to moment constraints, generalising the EM algorithm from a computational trick to an information-geometric procedure.
