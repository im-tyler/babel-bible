## Intuition [Beginner]

A proposition is a statement that is either true or false. "The sky is blue" is a proposition. "What time is it?" is not, because it is a question rather than a statement with a determinate truth value. Propositional logic studies how propositions combine using connectives like "and", "or", "not", and "if...then", and how the truth of complex statements depends on the truth of their components.

Think of propositional logic as the grammar of reasoning. Just as sentences have grammatical structure with subjects, verbs, and objects, arguments have logical structure built from propositions and connectives. Understanding this structure lets you evaluate whether an argument is valid regardless of what the propositions are about. The content does not matter; only the form matters. Two arguments about completely different subjects can share the same logical form, and if the form is valid, both arguments are valid.

The simplest connective is negation. If $p$ is "It is raining", then $\neg p$ (read "not $p$") is "It is not raining". Negation flips the truth value: if $p$ is true, $\neg p$ is false, and vice versa. Negation applies to a single proposition, making it a unary connective. You can negate compound propositions too: $\neg(p \land q)$ means "it is not the case that both $p$ and $q$".

Conjunction connects two propositions with "and". The statement $p \land q$ is true only when both $p$ and $q$ are true. If either is false, the whole conjunction is false. "The car is red and the car is fast" is true only if both claims hold. This matches the everyday use of "and" when making a joint claim about two things that must both be true for the overall claim to hold.

Disjunction connects propositions with "or". In logic, "or" is inclusive: $p \lor q$ is true when at least one of $p$ or $q$ is true, including when both are true. "I will study or I will exercise" is true if you study, if you exercise, or if you do both. This differs from the exclusive "or" of menus ("soup or salad"), which means exactly one but not both. When logicians need exclusivity they write $(p \lor q) \land \neg(p \land q)$.

The conditional $p \to q$ (read "if $p$ then $q$") is the most subtle connective. It is false in exactly one case: when the antecedent $p$ is true and the consequent $q$ is false. In all other cases the conditional is true. This definition surprises many beginners. "If the moon is made of cheese, then $2 + 2 = 5$" is true because the antecedent is false. The conditional does not claim a causal connection between $p$ and $q$; it claims that you never encounter $p$ true and $q$ false simultaneously.

The biconditional $p \leftrightarrow q$ (read "$p$ if and only if $q$") is true when $p$ and $q$ have the same truth value. It is equivalent to $(p \to q) \land (q \to p)$: each implies the other. The biconditional captures the notion of equivalence: two propositions that agree in truth value under every circumstance.

These five connectives are the building blocks of propositional logic. Any compound proposition, no matter how complex, is built from simpler propositions combined with these connectives. The truth value of the compound is entirely determined by the truth values of its components and the rules for each connective. This mechanical, truth-functional character is what makes propositional logic so tractable and so powerful. Understanding connectives gives you a toolkit for analyzing any argument that can be expressed in propositional form.

Why does any of this matter outside a logic classroom? Because propositional logic underlies every digital circuit, every database query, every search engine, and every verification system in existence. When your phone unlocks with Face ID, a chain of logical operations verifies your identity. When a database retrieves your banking records, it evaluates a logical formula against millions of rows. When a self-driving car decides whether to brake, it reasons about propositions like "there is a pedestrian ahead" and "the car is moving faster than 30 mph." Propositional logic is the hidden infrastructure of the digital world.

The systematic study of these connectives and their properties began in earnest in the late nineteenth century, but the underlying ideas are ancient. The Stoics studied the logic of "if...then" statements. Medieval logicians analyzed the properties of negation and disjunction. What modern propositional logic adds is precision: exact definitions, systematic notation, and mechanical methods for evaluating any argument. This precision is what allows us to automate logical reasoning and build the logical infrastructure that modern technology depends on.

## Visual [Beginner]

A truth table lists every possible combination of truth values for the propositions involved and shows the resulting truth value of the compound proposition. For a single proposition $p$ there are two possibilities: true (T) or false (F).

| $p$ | $\neg p$ |
|-----|----------|
| T   | F        |
| F   | T        |

For two propositions there are four possible combinations. The truth table for all five basic connectives:

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \land q$ | $p \lor q$ | $p \to q$ | $p \leftrightarrow q$ |
|-----|-----|-------------|------------|-----------|----------------------|
| T   | T   | T           | T          | T         | T                    |
| T   | F   | F           | T          | F         | F                    |
| F   | T   | F           | T          | T         | F                    |
| F   | F   | F           | F          | T         | T                    |

With $n$ distinct atomic propositions there are $2^n$ rows. For three that is 8 rows; for four, 16; for five, 32. This exponential growth makes truth tables impractical for large formulas but manageable for small examples that illustrate logical principles.

![Diagram showing compound propositions built from atomic propositions using logical connectives arranged as a tree. Each connective (AND, OR, NOT, IMPLIES) is a node with inputs from below producing output above. The diagram resembles a digital circuit schematic.](/img/24.01.01-propositional-logic-circuit-placeholder.svg)

The diagram illustrates the tree structure of compound propositions. Evaluating one is like computing a circuit: start at the leaves with assigned truth values and propagate upward through connective gates until reaching the root.

Truth tables also reveal a fundamental fact about propositional logic: it is truth-functional. The truth value of any compound proposition depends only on the truth values of its components, not on their meaning or content. This is both a strength and a limitation. It is a strength because it makes evaluation mechanical and algorithmic. It is a limitation because some natural-language constructions resist truth-functional treatment. "It is possible that $p$" is not truth-functional: knowing whether $p$ is true or false does not determine whether it is possible. This limitation motivates the development of modal logic, which extends propositional logic with operators for necessity and possibility.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider the compound proposition $\neg(p \land q) \to (\neg p \lor \neg q)$. Build the truth table step by step, working from the inside out.

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \land q$ | $\neg(p \land q)$ | $\neg p$ | $\neg q$ | $\neg p \lor \neg q$ | Result |
|-----|-----|-------------|---------------------|----------|----------|-------------------------|--------|
| T   | T   | T           | F                   | F        | F        | F                       | T      |
| T   | F   | F           | T                   | F        | T        | T                       | T      |
| F   | T   | F           | T                   | T        | F        | T                       | T      |
| F   | F   | F           | T                   | T        | T        | T                       | T      |

The final column is all true. This is a tautology: true regardless of the truth values of $p$ and $q$. This is one of De Morgan's laws: $\neg(p \land q)$ is logically equivalent to $\neg p \lor \neg q$.

A tautology is true in every row. A contradiction is false in every row. A contingent proposition is true in some rows and false in others.

Now consider the argument: "If it rains, the ground gets wet. It is raining. Therefore, the ground gets wet." Symbolically: premises $p \to q$ and $p$; conclusion $q$. This is modus ponens.

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \to q$ |
|-----|-----|-----------|
| T   | T   | T         |
| T   | F   | F         |
| F   | T   | T         |
| F   | F   | T         |

The only row where both $p \to q$ and $p$ are true is row 1. There $q$ is also true. No counterexample: the argument is valid.

Contrast with affirming the consequent: "If it rains, the ground gets wet. The ground is wet. Therefore, it rained." Row 3 ($p = F, q = T$) is a counterexample: both premises are true but the conclusion is false. The ground could be wet from a sprinkler. This argument form is invalid.

A third example: check whether $p \to (q \to p)$ is a tautology.

| $p$ | $q$ | $q \to p$ | $p \to (q \to p)$ |
|-----|-----|-----------|---------------------|
| T   | T   | T         | T                   |
| T   | F   | T         | T                   |
| F   | T   | F         | T                   |
| F   | F   | T         | T                   |

It is a tautology. A true proposition is implied by anything. This is one of the paradoxes of material implication.

A fourth example: determine whether $(p \to q) \lor (q \to p)$ is a tautology, contradiction, or contingent.

| $p$ | $q$ | $p \to q$ | $q \to p$ | $(p \to q) \lor (q \to p)$ |
|-----|-----|-----------|-----------|------------------------------|
| T   | T   | T         | T         | T                            |
| T   | F   | F         | T         | T                            |
| F   | T   | T         | F         | T                            |
| F   | F   | T         | T         | T                            |

This is a tautology. For any two propositions, at least one of $p \to q$ or $q \to p$ must be true. This follows from the fact that at least one of $p$ or $q$ must have the right truth value to make its conditional true. This tautology is sometimes called the "linear ordering principle" because it guarantees that for any two propositions, the conditional goes through in at least one direction.

A fifth example: check whether $p \leftrightarrow \neg p$ is a tautology or contradiction.

| $p$ | $\neg p$ | $p \leftrightarrow \neg p$ |
|-----|----------|------------------------------|
| T   | F        | F                            |
| F   | T        | F                            |

This is a contradiction: a proposition can never be equivalent to its own negation. This confirms the law of non-contradiction at the propositional level.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **propositional language** $\mathcal{L}$ has propositional variables $\{p_1, p_2, \ldots\}$ and connectives $\neg, \land, \lor, \to, \leftrightarrow$. **Well-formed formulas** (wffs) are defined recursively: every variable is a wff; if $\varphi$ is a wff then $\neg\varphi$ is a wff; if $\varphi,\psi$ are wffs then $(\varphi \land \psi)$, $(\varphi \lor \psi)$, $(\varphi \to \psi)$, $(\varphi \leftrightarrow \psi)$ are wffs; nothing else is a wff.

The **unique readability theorem** guarantees each wff has exactly one parse tree. The proof proceeds by induction on formula length, exploiting the fact that parentheses make grouping explicit.

A **valuation** is a function $v : \{p_i\} \to \{T,F\}$. Every valuation extends uniquely to all wffs via the truth-table clauses:

- $v(\neg \varphi) = T$ iff $v(\varphi) = F$
- $v(\varphi \land \psi) = T$ iff $v(\varphi) = T$ and $v(\psi) = T$
- $v(\varphi \lor \psi) = T$ iff $v(\varphi) = T$ or $v(\psi) = T$
- $v(\varphi \to \psi) = T$ iff $v(\varphi) = F$ or $v(\psi) = T$
- $v(\varphi \leftrightarrow \psi) = T$ iff $v(\varphi) = v(\psi)$

A wff is a **tautology** ($\models \varphi$) if $v(\varphi)=T$ for all $v$. A **contradiction** if $v(\varphi)=F$ for all $v$. **Contingent** otherwise.

$\varphi \equiv \psi$ (**logical equivalence**) means $v(\varphi)=v(\psi)$ for all $v$, equivalently $\varphi \leftrightarrow \psi$ is a tautology.

### Key equivalences

De Morgan: $\neg(p \land q) \equiv \neg p \lor \neg q$ and $\neg(p \lor q) \equiv \neg p \land \neg q$.

Conditional: $p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q$ and $p \to q \equiv \neg q \to \neg p$ (contrapositive).

Double negation: $\neg\neg p \equiv p$.

Distributive: $p \land (q \lor r) \equiv (p \land q) \lor (p \land r)$ and $p \lor (q \land r) \equiv (p \lor q) \land (p \lor r)$.

Absorption: $p \land (p \lor q) \equiv p$ and $p \lor (p \land q) \equiv p$.

### Semantic entailment

$\Gamma \models \varphi$ means every valuation making all of $\Gamma$ true makes $\varphi$ true. An argument form is valid iff its premises semantically entail its conclusion. Semantic entailment is the formal counterpart of "the conclusion follows logically from the premises." The notation $\Gamma \models \varphi$ captures the idea that the truth of the premises guarantees the truth of the conclusion, with no exceptions.

### Functional completeness

A connective set is **functionally complete** if every truth function is expressible. $\{\neg, \land\}$, $\{\neg, \lor\}$, the Sheffer stroke (NAND), and Peirce arrow (NOR) are each functionally complete. This fact has practical importance in digital circuit design, where it means any Boolean circuit can be built from a single type of gate.

### Normal forms

A **literal** is a variable or its negation. A **clause** is a disjunction of literals. A formula is in **conjunctive normal form** (CNF) if it is a conjunction of clauses. A formula is in **disjunctive normal form** (DNF) if it is a disjunction of conjunctions of literals (called minterms). Every propositional formula has equivalent CNF and DNF forms.

The conversion from an arbitrary formula to CNF or DNF can cause exponential blowup in formula size. For example, the formula $(p_1 \land q_1) \lor (p_2 \land q_2) \lor \cdots \lor (p_n \land q_n)$ has a DNF with $n$ minterms but its equivalent CNF has $2^n$ clauses. This exponential blowup is a serious practical concern.

The **Tseitin transformation** (1968) circumvents this problem by converting to equisatisfiable (rather than equivalent) CNF in linear time. It introduces fresh variables for each subformula, building the CNF incrementally. The resulting formula is not logically equivalent to the original, but it is satisfiable if and only if the original is. Since SAT solvers only need equisatisfiability, this is sufficient.

CNF is the standard input format for SAT solvers. A formula in CNF is often written as a set of clauses, each clause a set of literals. The empty clause represents falsity (a contradiction), and the empty set of clauses represents truth (a tautology).

### The deduction theorem

The **deduction theorem** for propositional logic states: $\Gamma \cup \{\varphi\} \models \psi$ if and only if $\Gamma \models \varphi \to \psi$. This theorem connects semantic entailment to the conditional. It says that adding a premise $\varphi$ and deriving $\psi$ is equivalent to deriving the conditional $\varphi \to \psi$ from the remaining premises alone. The deduction theorem holds for propositional logic and for first-order logic (but not for all modal logics).

The deduction theorem is fundamental to the practice of logical reasoning. When you want to prove a conditional $\varphi \to \psi$, the deduction theorem tells you it suffices to assume $\varphi$ and derive $\psi$. This pattern — assume the antecedent, derive the consequent, conclude the conditional — is the most common proof strategy in all of mathematics.

### Substitution and replacement

Two further structural principles govern propositional reasoning. The **substitution theorem** states: if $\varphi$ is a tautology and $\psi$ is any formula, then the result of uniformly substituting $\psi$ for a propositional variable in $\varphi$ is also a tautology. For example, since $p \lor \neg p$ is a tautology, substituting $q \land r$ for $p$ yields $(q \land r) \lor \neg(q \land r)$, which is also a tautology.

The **replacement theorem** states: if $\varphi$ and $\psi$ are logically equivalent, then replacing $\varphi$ by $\psi$ in any formula produces a logically equivalent formula. For example, since $p \to q$ is equivalent to $\neg p \lor q$, we can replace any conditional by its disjunctive equivalent and preserve logical equivalence. These two theorems justify the algebraic manipulation of logical formulas that is central to proof by equivalence.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

### Soundness and Completeness of Propositional Logic

**Soundness.** If $\Gamma \vdash \varphi$ then $\Gamma \models \varphi$. **Completeness.** If $\Gamma \models \varphi$ then $\Gamma \vdash \varphi$.

**Soundness proof.** By induction on the length of the proof. Every axiom is a tautology, verified by truth table. Every inference rule preserves truth. For modus ponens, the key case: if $p \to q$ and $p$ are both true under valuation $v$, then $q$ must also be true under $v$, by the truth-table definition of the conditional. Since proofs start with tautologies (true under every valuation) and each step preserves truth, the conclusion is true whenever the assumptions are.

The soundness theorem provides a guarantee: no valid proof can lead from true premises to a false conclusion. This is essential for the reliability of formal reasoning. Without soundness, a proof system would be useless — it could "prove" things that are not actually entailed by the premises. Every proposed proof system must first be shown sound before it can be trusted.

**Completeness proof (sketch).** Contrapositive: $\Gamma \not\models \varphi$ implies $\Gamma \not\vdash \varphi$. Enumerate all formulas $\psi_1, \psi_2, \ldots$ and build a maximally consistent set $\Gamma^*$ by adding each $\psi_i$ to $\Gamma$ if the result stays consistent and does not prove $\varphi$. The resulting $\Gamma^*$ is maximally consistent: it is consistent, and adding any formula not already in it produces an inconsistency.

This maximal consistent set has powerful properties. For every formula $\psi$, exactly one of $\psi$ or $\neg\psi$ belongs to $\Gamma^*$. If $\psi \to \chi \in \Gamma^*$ and $\psi \in \Gamma^*$, then $\chi \in \Gamma^*$. Every theorem of the proof system belongs to $\Gamma^*$.

Define a valuation $v$ by setting $v(p_i) = T$ if and only if $p_i \in \Gamma^*$. By induction on formula complexity, $v(\psi) = T$ if and only if $\psi \in \Gamma^*$ for every formula $\psi$. The base case holds by definition. For negation: $v(\neg\psi) = T$ iff $v(\psi) = F$ iff $\psi \notin \Gamma^*$ iff $\neg\psi \in \Gamma^*$. For conjunction: $v(\psi \land \chi) = T$ iff $v(\psi) = T$ and $v(\chi) = T$ iff $\psi \in \Gamma^*$ and $\chi \in \Gamma^*$ iff $\psi \land \chi \in \Gamma^*$ (by maximal consistency). Other connectives follow from equivalences.

Since $\Gamma \subseteq \Gamma^*$ and $\neg\varphi \in \Gamma^*$, we get $v(\varphi) = F$ while all formulas in $\Gamma$ are true under $v$. Thus $\Gamma \not\models \varphi$.

This proof strategy — extending a consistent set to a maximal consistent set and reading off a model — is a prototype for completeness proofs in stronger systems, including first-order logic. The key idea is that maximal consistency provides enough information to define a valuation. Every formula's membership in $\Gamma^*$ determines its truth value, and the maximal consistency properties ensure that the connective clauses are satisfied.

### The deduction theorem and its proof

The deduction theorem states: $\Gamma \cup \{\varphi\} \vdash \psi$ if and only if $\Gamma \vdash \varphi \to \psi$. This holds for any standard axiomatization of propositional logic that includes modus ponens as its sole inference rule.

**Proof.** The "if" direction is straightforward: from $\Gamma \vdash \varphi \to \psi$ and the assumption $\varphi$, one application of modus ponens gives $\psi$.

The "only if" direction is proved by induction on the length of the proof of $\psi$ from $\Gamma \cup \{\varphi\}$. If $\psi$ is an axiom or a member of $\Gamma$, then $\Gamma \vdash \psi$ and $\Gamma \vdash \psi \to (\varphi \to \psi)$ (an axiom), so by modus ponens $\Gamma \vdash \varphi \to \psi$. If $\psi = \varphi$, then $\varphi \to \varphi$ is provable from $\Gamma$ alone (it is a tautology, hence provable by completeness). If $\psi$ was derived by modus ponens from $\chi \to \psi$ and $\chi$, then by the induction hypothesis $\Gamma \vdash \varphi \to (\chi \to \psi)$ and $\Gamma \vdash \varphi \to \chi$. Using the axiom $(\varphi \to (\chi \to \psi)) \to ((\varphi \to \chi) \to (\varphi \to \psi))$ and two applications of modus ponens, we obtain $\Gamma \vdash \varphi \to \psi$.

### Decidability and computational complexity

Propositional logic is **decidable**: there exists an effective procedure that, given any formula $\varphi$, determines in finite time whether $\varphi$ is a tautology. The truth-table method is such an algorithm: construct the full truth table with $2^n$ rows (where $n$ is the number of distinct propositional variables) and check if the final column is all T. This runs in time $O(2^n \cdot m)$ where $m$ is the formula length.

The decidability of propositional logic stands in sharp contrast to first-order logic, which is undecidable (Church's theorem, 1936). There is no algorithm that can determine, for every first-order sentence, whether it is valid. Propositional logic is one of the richest decidable logical systems, and its decidability is what makes automated reasoning feasible.

The satisfiability problem (SAT) asks whether there exists a valuation making a given formula true. SAT is NP-complete (Cook-Levin 1971). This means that SAT is among the hardest problems in NP: any problem whose solutions can be verified in polynomial time can be encoded as a SAT instance. The P versus NP question — whether every problem in NP can also be solved in polynomial time — is equivalent to asking whether SAT has a polynomial-time algorithm. This is one of the seven Clay Millennium Prize Problems, with a one million dollar bounty. Most complexity theorists believe P is not equal to NP, but a proof remains elusive despite decades of effort.

Even without resolving P versus NP, practical SAT solving has advanced enormously. Modern CDCL solvers handle industrial instances with millions of variables. SAT solvers are used in hardware verification (checking that a chip design meets its specification), software model checking (verifying that a program satisfies safety properties), AI planning (finding sequences of actions to achieve a goal), and cryptanalysis (breaking cryptographic protocols). The efficiency of SAT solvers on real-world instances, despite theoretical worst-case hardness, is one of the great success stories of computer science.

**Exercise 4 (hard).** Prove $\{\to, \neg\}$ is functionally complete.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From $p \to q \equiv \neg p \lor q$: disjunction $p \lor q \equiv \neg p \to q$. Conjunction $p \land q \equiv \neg(\neg p \to \neg q)$. Biconditional $p \leftrightarrow q \equiv \neg(\neg(p \to q) \to \neg(q \to p))$. All connectives expressed.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard).** Prove every propositional formula has an equivalent DNF.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For each true-row $r$ of $\varphi$ with variables $p_1,\ldots,p_n$, build conjunction $C_r$ of literals matching that row. Each $C_r$ is true in exactly one row. The disjunction over all true-rows matches $\varphi$ everywhere. Contradictions use $p_1 \land \neg p_1$.

</details>

**Exercise 6 (hard).** Construct the truth table for $(p \to q) \land (q \to r) \to (p \to r)$ and verify it is a tautology. This is the principle of hypothetical syllogism.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### The Compactness Theorem for Propositional Logic

The compactness theorem states that a (possibly infinite) set of formulas $\Gamma$ is satisfiable if and only if every finite subset of $\Gamma$ is satisfiable. This is one of the most important meta-theorems in logic, bridging finitary and infinitary reasoning in a way that has profound consequences across mathematics.

**Proof via completeness.** By the completeness theorem, $\Gamma$ is satisfiable if and only if $\Gamma$ is consistent. If $\Gamma$ is inconsistent, then $\Gamma \vdash \bot$ for some contradiction $\bot$. But proofs are finite objects: a proof from $\Gamma$ uses only finitely many members of $\Gamma$. So some finite subset $\Gamma_0 \subseteq \Gamma$ already proves $\bot$, making $\Gamma_0$ inconsistent and hence unsatisfiable. Conversely, if every finite subset of $\Gamma$ is satisfiable, then every finite subset is consistent. If $\Gamma$ itself were inconsistent, some finite subset would be, contradicting the hypothesis. So $\Gamma$ is consistent and hence satisfiable.

**Direct proof via Koenig's lemma.** An alternative proof builds an infinite binary tree of partial valuations. Level $n$ consists of all partial valuations of $\{p_1, \ldots, p_n\}$ that can be extended to satisfy some finite subset of $\Gamma$. Each node at level $n$ has at most two children (extending the partial valuation to $p_{n+1}$). By hypothesis, every level is nonempty. By Koenig's lemma — every infinite finitely-branching tree has an infinite path — there is an infinite path through the tree. This path defines a total valuation satisfying all of $\Gamma$.

**Applications in graph theory.** The compactness theorem implies that if every finite subgraph of an infinite graph is $k$-colorable, then the entire graph is $k$-colorable. The proof introduces propositional variables $p_{v,i}$ meaning "vertex $v$ gets color $i$" and writes clauses expressing the coloring constraints: each vertex gets exactly one color, and no two adjacent vertices share a color. Every finite subset of these clauses corresponds to a finite subgraph, which is $k$-colorable by hypothesis.

**Applications in algebra.** Compactness proves the existence of nonstandard models of arithmetic. Extend the theory of arithmetic with infinitely many new constants $c_1, c_2, \ldots$ and axioms $c_i > n$ for each standard natural number $n$. Every finite subset is satisfiable (by choosing the constants large enough), so by compactness the whole set is satisfiable, yielding a model with infinitely large elements. This construction is fundamental to nonstandard analysis.

**Applications in topology.** Compactness for propositional logic is equivalent to the compactness of the product topology on $\{T, F\}^{\mathbb{N}}$, which is a special case of Tychonoff's theorem for products of finite discrete spaces. The equivalence reveals a deep connection between logical satisfiability and topological compactness.

### Craig Interpolation and Beth Definability

Craig's interpolation theorem (1957) states: if $\varphi \models \psi$, then there exists a formula $\theta$ such that $\varphi \models \theta$ and $\theta \models \psi$, and every propositional variable occurring in $\theta$ also occurs in both $\varphi$ and $\psi$. If $\varphi$ and $\psi$ share no propositional variables, then either $\varphi$ is a contradiction or $\psi$ is a tautology.

The interpolant $\theta$ serves as a bridge between $\varphi$ and $\psi$ using only vocabulary common to both. This is a conservative reasoning principle: any logical consequence of $\varphi$ that can be expressed in the shared language already follows from $\varphi$ in that shared language alone.

Lyndon's interpolation theorem refines Craig's result by requiring that the interpolant use each variable with the same polarity in both formulas. A variable occurs positively in a formula if it appears under an even number of negations, and negatively if under an odd number. Lyndon's theorem connects interpolation to the monotonicity properties of Boolean functions.

Beth's definability theorem follows from interpolation: if a theory implicitly defines a propositional variable (fixing its value in every model of the theory), then the theory explicitly defines it (the variable is equivalent to a formula not containing it). This connects logical definability to questions in the philosophy of language about whether theoretical terms can be eliminated in favor of observational ones. The theorem says that implicit and explicit definability coincide for propositional logic.

### Post's Lattice

Emil Post classified all clones — closed sets of Boolean functions under composition — on a two-element set in 1941. The resulting structure, called Post's lattice, is a complete classification of all possible functionally complete sets and their inclusion relationships. Every clone corresponds to a set of truth-table columns closed under composition and containing all projections. Post showed there are exactly countably many such clones, organized into a lattice with the set of all Boolean functions at the top and the set of projections at the bottom.

This classification has direct applications in circuit design: it tells you the minimum set of gate types needed to build any Boolean circuit, and which combinations of gates are sufficient. A circuit designer who knows Post's lattice can determine whether a given collection of gate types is functionally complete, and if not, what minimal additions would make it so. The lattice also connects to universal algebra through clone theory, which classifies the term operations of general algebraic structures.

### SAT and Computational Complexity

The Boolean satisfiability problem (SAT) asks: given a propositional formula, does there exist a valuation making it true? SAT was the first problem shown to be NP-complete, by Stephen Cook in 1971 and independently by Leonid Levin in 1973. NP-completeness means SAT is in NP (a yes answer can be verified in polynomial time by checking the proposed valuation) and every problem in NP can be reduced to SAT in polynomial time.

The P versus NP question is equivalent to asking whether SAT has a polynomial-time algorithm. This is one of the seven Clay Millennium Prize Problems, with a one million dollar bounty. Most computer scientists believe P is not equal to NP, meaning no polynomial algorithm for SAT exists, but a proof remains elusive.

Modern SAT solvers using conflict-driven clause learning (CDCL) handle millions of variables in practice, despite the worst-case exponential complexity. CDCL combines systematic search with efficient clause learning from conflicts: when the solver encounters a contradiction, it analyzes the conflict to learn a new clause that prevents similar conflicts in the future. Key techniques include variable ordering heuristics (VSIDS), restarts, and preprocessing. The annual SAT Competition drives ongoing improvements in solver performance.

Proof complexity studies the length of proofs in various systems. Resolution proofs, the theoretical foundation of CDCL, have known exponential lower bounds for certain formula families (pigeonhole formulas, Tseitin formulas). These lower bounds translate into performance guarantees: any CDCL solver, regardless of implementation details, must take exponential time on these formulas. Stronger proof systems (cutting planes, Frege systems) can produce shorter proofs, but no known system produces polynomial-size proofs for all tautologies unless NP equals co-NP.

## Connections [Master]

This unit connects to several other areas of the curriculum, each connection enriching both propositional logic and the connected field.

- **Predicate logic [24.02.01].** Propositional logic treats atomic propositions as indivisible units. Predicate logic breaks them open, analyzing internal structure with quantifiers ($\forall$, $\exists$) and variables. The connectives and truth-functional semantics carry over directly from propositional logic, but the expressive power increases dramatically. The completeness proof for predicate logic is substantially harder than for propositional logic and requires the Henkin construction.

- **Boolean algebra [01.XX].** The algebraic structure of propositional logic under $\land$, $\lor$, and $\neg$ is a Boolean algebra. The laws of propositional logic (commutativity, associativity, distributivity, complement) mirror the laws of set operations (intersection, union, complement). This is not a coincidence: both are instances of the same abstract algebraic structure discovered by George Boole in 1854. Stone's representation theorem shows that every Boolean algebra is isomorphic to a field of sets.

- **Digital circuit design [25.05.01].** Every digital circuit is a physical realization of a propositional formula. Logic gates (AND, OR, NOT, NAND, NOR) implement connectives. Truth tables describe circuit behavior. Functional completeness corresponds to the fact that NAND gates alone suffice to build any digital circuit. This connection between logic and hardware is one of the most productive in intellectual history.

- **Automated reasoning and formal verification [25.09.01].** SAT solvers, which determine the satisfiability of propositional formulas, are the backbone of modern automated reasoning tools. Model checking, program verification, and planning in AI all rely on efficient propositional reasoning. SAT solving has become a general-purpose reasoning engine for combinatorial problems across computer science.

- **Philosophy of logic [20.01.01].** The truth-functional account of the conditional is philosophically controversial. The "paradoxes of material implication" — that a false antecedent or a true consequent each suffice to make the conditional true — conflict with intuitive judgments about conditionals in natural language. Relevance logic, modal logic, and counterfactual logic all attempt to address these issues by imposing additional constraints on the conditional.

- **Probability and Bayesian reasoning [24.06.01].** Propositional logic is the foundation for probabilistic reasoning. A probability distribution over propositional variables assigns a number between 0 and 1 to each possible world (each row of the truth table). Bayesian reasoning updates these probabilities in light of evidence, using the logical structure of propositions to constrain the probability calculus.

- **Complexity theory [25.03.01].** SAT, the satisfiability problem for propositional logic, is the canonical NP-complete problem. The entire theory of NP-completeness starts from propositional logic, and many fundamental questions in computational complexity are, at their core, questions about the difficulty of propositional reasoning.

- **Database theory [25.07.01].** SQL WHERE clauses are propositional formulas evaluated against rows. Each row in a table corresponds to a valuation, and the WHERE clause is a formula that must evaluate to true for the row to be included in the result. Conjunction (AND), disjunction (OR), and negation (NOT) in SQL correspond exactly to the truth-functional connectives studied in this unit. The theory of relational algebra, which underpins database query languages, is deeply connected to propositional and predicate logic through the relational calculus.

- **Set theory and combinatorics [01.XX].** The power set of a set with $n$ elements has $2^n$ members, just as a truth table with $n$ propositional variables has $2^n$ rows. The correspondence between subsets and truth-value assignments is the foundation of the algebraic approach to propositional logic. Boolean algebra unifies propositional logic, set theory, and switching circuits under a single abstract framework.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

### Ancient logic

Formal logic begins with Aristotle's *Organon*, a collection of works on reasoning assembled by his followers after his death around 322 BCE. Aristotle's theory of the syllogism — a systematization of valid argument forms using categorical propositions ("All A are B", "No A are B", "Some A are B", "Some A are not B") — was the dominant logical system for over two thousand years. It is remarkable for its rigor and systematicity, given that it was developed in the fourth century BCE.

Aristotle identified 256 possible syllogistic forms and determined which are valid using a combination of conversion rules, reduction to perfect syllogisms, and counterexample construction. His system was not propositional in the modern sense — it operated on categorical propositions with quantifiers ("all", "some", "no") rather than on atomic propositions combined with truth-functional connectives. But it established the fundamental idea that logical validity is a matter of form, not content.

The Stoics, particularly Chrysippus (c. 279-206 BCE), developed an alternative logical system much closer to modern propositional logic. Stoic logic treated whole propositions as basic units and studied inference patterns involving "if...then", "and", "or", and "not". Chrysippus identified several valid inference schemata now recognized as rules of propositional logic, including modus ponens and a form of disjunction elimination. His work was largely ignored during the medieval period and rediscovered only in the twentieth century by Jan Lukasiewicz and Benson Mates.

The Stoics also introduced the idea of propositional variables. Where Aristotle used letters to stand for terms within categorical propositions ("All A are B"), the Stoics used ordinal numbers to stand for whole propositions ("If the first, then the second; the first; therefore the second"). This innovation — reasoning with placeholders for arbitrary propositions — is the direct ancestor of modern propositional logic.

Medieval logicians including William of Ockham (c. 1287-1347) and Walter Burley developed sophisticated theories of propositions and consequences. The Scholastics explored term semantics, supposition theory, and obligations disputations — structured debates governed by logical rules. Much of this work anticipated modern developments in logic and the philosophy of language, but was largely forgotten until the twentieth century.

### The Frege-Russell revolution

Modern propositional logic emerged from Gottlob Frege's *Begriffsschrift* (1879), which introduced a formal language for mathematical reasoning. Frege's notation was cumbersome — a two-dimensional diagrammatic script — but his conceptual innovations were transformative: truth-functional connectives, material implication, and formal inference rules.

Frege's larger project, the *Grundgesetze der Arithmetik* (1893, 1903), aimed to derive arithmetic from purely logical principles. The project was undermined by Russell's paradox (1901), which revealed an inconsistency in Frege's system. Frege received a letter from Bertrand Russell pointing out the paradox just as the second volume of *Grundgesetze* was going to press. Frege added an appendix acknowledging the problem but never found a satisfactory resolution. Nevertheless, the logical apparatus survived and became the foundation of modern logic.

Bertrand Russell and Alfred North Whitehead's *Principia Mathematica* (1910-1913) aimed to derive all of mathematics from logical principles while avoiding the paradoxes. The work consumed ten years of their lives and filled three massive volumes. Though Gödel's incompleteness theorems (1931) showed the project cannot fully succeed, the logical apparatus developed in *Principia* became standard. The notation and proof procedures used in propositional and predicate logic today descend directly from this work.

### Truth tables and tautologies

The truth-table method is attributed to both Ludwig Wittgenstein's *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* (1922) and Emil Post's doctoral dissertation (1920), independently. Wittgenstein used truth tables to show that all logical propositions are tautologies — statements that are true regardless of how things stand in the world. This insight, that logical truths are empty of factual content, became central to logical positivism and the Vienna Circle.

For Wittgenstein, the truth table revealed something profound about the nature of logic. A tautology says nothing about the world: it is true under every possible arrangement of facts. A contradiction also says nothing: it is false under every arrangement. Both are senseless — they have no content. Only contingent propositions, which are true under some arrangements and false under others, carry genuine information. Logic, on this view, is not a body of truths about the world but a scaffold for constructing meaningful statements.

Post went further, providing the first systematic study of truth-functional completeness. He proved that the set of all truth functions is generated by negation and either conjunction or disjunction, and he classified all closed sets of truth functions under composition — the result now known as Post's lattice. Post's work laid the groundwork for the algebraic theory of Boolean functions and has applications in circuit design, coding theory, and universal algebra.

### Tarski and the semantic tradition

Alfred Tarski's work in the 1930s on the concept of truth in formalized languages established the model-theoretic tradition in logic. Tarski showed how to define truth for formal languages in a way that avoids the paradoxes that arise when a language contains its own truth predicate. His approach — defining truth relative to a model or interpretation — is the foundation of the semantic notions used throughout this unit: valuation, entailment, satisfiability.

Tarski's Convention T requires that any adequate truth definition imply all instances of the schema: "$\varphi$ is true if and only if $p$", where $\varphi$ is the name of the sentence and $p$ is the sentence itself. For propositional logic, this amounts to requiring that the truth definition agree with the truth-table definitions for each connective. Tarski's work showed that such a definition can be given consistently by working in a metalanguage that is stronger than the object language.

### The conditional in philosophy

The material conditional ($p \to q$, defined as $\neg p \lor q$) has been the subject of intense philosophical debate. The "paradoxes of material implication" include: a false statement implies anything ($\neg p \models p \to q$), and a true statement is implied by anything ($q \models p \to q$). These seem counterintuitive.

Paul Grice's theory of conversational implicature explains why: asserting $p \to q$ conversationally implicates a connection between $p$ and $q$, even though the literal meaning requires none. When someone says "If it rains, the picnic will be cancelled," they implicate that rain would cause cancellation, not merely that rain and cancellation never co-occur by accident.

Alternative logics address the semantic gap. Relevance logic requires a genuine connection between antecedent and consequent, rejecting the principle that a false antecedent makes the conditional true. Modal logic introduces necessity and possibility, analyzing "if $p$ then necessarily $q$" rather than the truth-functional material conditional. Counterfactual logic (developed by Robert Stalnaker and David Lewis in the 1960s-1970s) handles conditionals with false antecedents — "If it had rained, the picnic would have been cancelled" — which the material conditional cannot distinguish from "If it had rained, the picnic would not have been cancelled." Each of these systems enriches the conditional beyond the simple truth-functional definition used in this unit.

### The foundational crisis and its legacy

The development of propositional and predicate logic was driven by the desire to secure the foundations of mathematics. Russell's paradox (1901) triggered the foundational crisis. Three programs responded: logicism (reduce math to logic), formalism (prove consistency finitarily), and intuitionism (reject excluded middle). Gödel's incompleteness theorems (1931) showed logicism and formalism cannot fully succeed.

But the tools developed — formal languages, proof systems, model theory, recursion theory — became mathematical logic and theoretical computer science. The soundness and completeness of propositional logic remain among the few areas where the foundational program achieved its full aims. Propositional logic is both sound and complete, decidable, and compact: a rare convergence of syntactic and semantic perspectives that makes it the foundation on which all of mathematical logic is built.

The legacy of the foundational crisis extends beyond logic itself. Alan Turing's work on computability (1936), which grew out of the same concerns about formal systems that motivated Gödel, laid the groundwork for the modern computer. Claude Shannon's 1937 master's thesis showed that Boolean algebra (and hence propositional logic) describes the behavior of electrical switching circuits, founding digital circuit design. The attempt to secure the foundations of mathematics thus produced the two pillars of the digital age: the theory of computation and the design of digital circuits.

These historical connections are not mere curiosities. They illustrate a general pattern: the pursuit of rigorous foundations, even when it fails in its stated aim, generates tools and insights that transform unrelated fields. Propositional logic, developed to settle questions about mathematical certainty, became the operating system of the information age.
