## Intuition [Beginner]

A fallacy is an error in reasoning that makes an argument seem persuasive when it should not be. Fallacies are not just false statements — they are broken patterns of inference. A false premise makes an argument unsound, but a fallacy makes an argument invalid or deceptive even when the premises might be true.

Consider this argument: "My grandfather smoked a pack a day and lived to 90, so smoking is not harmful." The premise is true (some heavy smokers live long lives), but the inference is broken. One data point cannot refute a statistical generalization. This is the hasty generalization fallacy — drawing a sweeping conclusion from insufficient evidence.

Informal fallacies differ from the formal errors studied in propositional logic. A formal fallacy like affirming the consequent ($p \to q, q$, therefore $p$) is invalid regardless of content. Informal fallacies depend on content and context: the same argument form might be reasonable in one context and fallacious in another. "Experts say X, so X is probably true" is a reasonable appeal to authority when the experts are relevant and qualified, but becomes a fallacious appeal to authority when they are not.

Learning fallacies serves two purposes. First, it helps you spot bad arguments in editorials, advertisements, political speeches, and social media. Second, and more valuable, it helps you avoid making bad arguments yourself. Naming the error clarifies the thinking. Third, and often overlooked, fallacy study reveals the gap between how arguments should work and how they actually work in practice — a gap that is central to understanding human reasoning in all its complexity.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Example 1: Ad Hominem.**

"Senator Chen's tax plan should be rejected. He was caught lying about his military service." The premise (Chen lied about his service) may be true, and Chen's character may be relevant to whether you trust him personally. But the argument commits ad hominem because it attacks the person rather than the plan. A tax plan stands or falls on its economic merits, not the moral character of its proposer.

**Example 2: Appeal to Popularity.**

"Millions of people believe in astrology, so there must be something to it." The number of believers is not evidence for the truth of the belief. At various points in history, most people believed the earth was flat, that disease was caused by bad air, and that the sun orbited the earth. Widespread belief can result from cultural tradition, cognitive bias, or lack of information, not from truth.

**Example 3: False Dilemma.**

"Either we cut social spending or the deficit will destroy the economy. We cannot let the deficit destroy the economy, so we must cut social spending." This presents only two options when others exist: raise revenue, restructure debt, grow the economy, or some combination. False dilemmas work by eliminating the options you would actually prefer.

**Example 4: Straw Man.**

A city council member proposes adding bike lanes to a major road. An opponent says: "The council wants to ban cars entirely and force everyone to ride bicycles. This is absurd." The original proposal was moderate (add bike lanes). The straw man version is extreme (ban cars). Attacking the extreme version is easier than engaging with the actual proposal.

**Example 5: Post Hoc.**

"The stock market crashed the day after the president signed the trade bill. The bill caused the crash." Temporal sequence does not establish causation. The crash might have resulted from an earnings report, a Federal Reserve announcement, or market psychology. The post hoc fallacy (short for "post hoc ergo propter hoc" — after this, therefore because of this) confuses correlation with causation.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

An **informal fallacy** is a pattern of reasoning that fails to provide adequate support for its conclusion, where the failure depends on the content, context, or pragmatics of the argument rather than on its logical form alone. This definition distinguishes informal fallacies from formal fallacies (like affirming the consequent), which are invalid regardless of content.

Following Hamblin (1970), fallacies can be analyzed as failures of dialectical obligation. In a reasoned dialogue, participants have obligations: to support their claims with relevant reasons, to address objections, to interpret opponents charitably. Fallacies are violations of these obligations. The ad hominem fallacy violates the obligation to address the argument rather than the person. The straw man fallacy violates the obligation to represent opponents fairly.

### Classification of informal fallacies

**Fallacies of relevance** (also called fallacies of diversion) have premises that are logically disconnected from the conclusion:

- **Ad hominem (abusive):** Attacking the character of the arguer rather than the argument.
- **Ad hominem (circumstantial):** Claiming the arguer's circumstances invalidate their position.
- **Ad hominem (tu quoque):** "You do it too" — dismissing an argument because the arguer does not follow their own advice.
- **Appeal to authority (ad verecundiam):** Citing an authority outside their area of expertise, or citing authority where evidence is available.
- **Appeal to popularity (ad populum):** Arguing that a claim is true because many people believe it.
- **Appeal to ignorance (ad ignorantiam):** Arguing that a claim is true because it has not been proven false (or vice versa).
- **Appeal to emotion (ad misericordiam, ad baculum):** Using pity, fear, or other emotions in place of evidence.
- **Red herring:** Introducing an irrelevant topic to divert attention from the original issue.

**Fallacies of ambiguity** exploit unclear or shifting language:

- **Equivocation:** Using a word with multiple meanings in different parts of the argument.
- **Amphiboly:** Exploiting grammatical ambiguity to misrepresent a claim.
- **Composition:** Inferring that what is true of the parts must be true of the whole.
- **Division:** Inferring that what is true of the whole must be true of the parts.

**Fallacies of weak induction** have premises that provide some support but not enough:

- **Hasty generalization:** Drawing a general conclusion from a sample that is too small or unrepresentative.
- **False cause (post hoc, cum hoc):** Mistaking correlation for causation, or inferring causation from temporal sequence.
- **Slippery slope:** Arguing that a first step will lead to a chain of events ending in disaster, without establishing the causal links.
- **Weak analogy:** Drawing a conclusion from an analogy where the relevant similarities are insufficient.

**Fallacies of presumption** involve hidden assumptions that are false or unproven:

- **Begging the question (petitio principii):** Assuming the conclusion as a premise, either directly or through a chain of reasoning that presupposes it.
- **False dilemma (black-and-white):** Presenting only two options when more exist.
- **Complex question:** Phrasing a question that presupposes an unestablished fact.

### The pragma-dialectical approach

Van Eemeren and Grootendorst (1992, 2004) developed a systematic theory of fallacies within their pragma-dialectical model of argumentation. On their view, fallacies are violations of the rules for critical discussion — a model of how reasonable dispute resolution should proceed.

The ten rules of critical discussion cover: the freedom rule (parties must not prevent each other from advancing arguments), the obligation rule (a party who advances a standpoint must defend it when challenged), the standpoint rule (an attack must relate to the standpoint), the relevance rule (only relevant arguments count), the unexpressed premise rule (a party must accept unexpressed premises that are logically required), the starting point rule (parties may not falsely present something as an accepted starting point or deny an accepted starting point), the argument scheme rule (a standpoint must be defended by argument schemes that are valid), the validity rule (the reasoning in the argument must be logically valid), the closure rule (a failed defense requires the arguer to retract the standpoint), and the usage rule (parties must not use ambiguous or unclear formulations).

Each fallacy corresponds to a violation of one or more rules. Ad hominem violates the freedom rule. Red herring violates the relevance rule. Equivocation violates the usage rule. This classification is more systematic than the traditional lists and connects fallacies to a normative model of discourse.

### Argument reconstruction

Before evaluating an argument for fallacies, it must be reconstructed — translated from natural language into a form that makes its logical structure explicit. Argument reconstruction involves several steps:

1. **Identify the conclusion.** What is the arguer trying to establish? Look for indicator words like "therefore", "so", "hence", "it follows that", or "which proves that".

2. **Identify the explicit premises.** What reasons does the arguer give? Look for "because", "since", "given that", "for the reason that".

3. **Identify implicit premises.** Most natural-language arguments leave some premises unstated. "Socrates is a philosopher, so he is wise" has the implicit premise "All philosophers are wise" (or something weaker). Identifying implicit premises requires charity: assume the arguer intended a valid or strong argument and fill in what is needed.

4. **Assess the reconstructed argument.** Is it deductively valid? Inductively strong? Does it commit a fallacy?

The principle of charity requires interpreting arguments in their strongest reasonable form. This means giving the arguer the benefit of the doubt on ambiguous premises and reconstructing the argument to be as strong as the content allows. A straw man reconstruction of an opponent's argument is itself a form of fallacious reasoning.

### The burden of proof

Many fallacies involve misallocation of the burden of proof. In reasoned discourse, the party making a claim bears the burden of proving it. The appeal to ignorance fallacy ("you cannot prove it is false, so it is true") shifts the burden of proof from the claimant to the challenger.

The burden of proof is not always straightforward. In criminal law, the prosecution bears the burden of proving guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. In science, the burden falls on the researcher proposing a new theory. In everyday argument, the burden often falls on whoever makes the more controversial claim. Disputes about where the burden lies are themselves a common source of argumentative deadlock.

Walton (1988) identified several factors that affect burden allocation: the type of dialogue (persuasion, negotiation, inquiry, deliberation), the initial commitments of the parties, the forum (legal, scientific, political), and the consequences of being wrong. A claim that would impose costs on others carries a heavier burden of proof than a claim that only affects the speaker.

### Fallacies in visual and multimedia arguments

Fallacies occur not only in verbal arguments but in visual and multimedia contexts. Political advertisements use images that evoke emotional responses (appeal to emotion). Charts with truncated axes or inconsistent scales misrepresent data (a visual form of false cause or hasty generalization). Memes combine images with text to create straw man versions of opposing positions.

Visual fallacies are particularly effective because images bypass rational evaluation. A photograph of a long line at a polling place, paired with the caption "This is what voter suppression looks like," presents a visual argument that may commit a hasty generalization (one long line does not establish systematic suppression) but the emotional impact of the image makes the logical weakness harder to notice.

The analysis of visual arguments extends fallacy theory into semiotics and visual rhetoric, requiring tools beyond those of traditional logic. Kjeldsen (2012) and Birdsell and Groarke (2007) have developed frameworks for identifying and evaluating visual arguments, arguing that visual fallacies should be analyzed using the same principles as verbal fallacies but with attention to the specific affordances of visual communication.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

### Key result: No Decidable Procedure for Fallacy Detection

There is no effective procedure that, given an arbitrary natural-language argument, correctly determines whether it commits an informal fallacy. This is not a theorem in the mathematical sense but a result about the irreducibly contextual nature of informal reasoning.

**Argument by cases.** Consider the argument form "Expert E says P, therefore P." Whether this is a legitimate appeal to authority or the fallacy of ad verecundiam depends on contextual factors that no algorithm can evaluate:

1. Is E an expert in the relevant domain?
2. Is P within the domain of E's expertise?
3. Is there a consensus among experts on P?
4. Is P the kind of claim that expertise can settle?
5. Is E biased or compromised?

Each of these questions requires background knowledge, judgment about relevance, and sensitivity to the specific situation. Two arguments with identical surface form can be reasonable in one context and fallacious in another.

**Further argument from the open-texture of concepts.** The concept of "relevance" — central to the definition of many fallacies — is itself context-dependent and open-textured (in Waismann's sense). What counts as relevant in a scientific debate differs from what counts as relevant in a political debate or a legal proceeding. The same argument move (appealing to consequences, citing authority, drawing an analogy) can be appropriate or fallacious depending on the dialectical situation.

This result explains why fallacy detection resists automation and why teaching fallacies requires practice with varied examples rather than mechanical application of rules. It also explains why the same argument can be classified differently by different authorities: the boundary between a legitimate rhetorical device and a fallacy is fuzzy and context-dependent.

**Exercise 6 (hard).** Explain the difference between begging the question and drawing a valid inference from a universally accepted premise. Use examples.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Begging the question (petitio principii) occurs when the conclusion is assumed in the premises, so the argument provides no independent support. Example: "The Bible is the word of God because it says so in the Bible." The premise (the Bible claims divine authorship) presupposes what needs to be established (that the Bible's claims are true).

Drawing a valid inference from an accepted premise is different: "All mammals are warm-blooded. Whales are mammals. Therefore, whales are warm-blooded." The premises are not identical to the conclusion, and the inference provides genuine support even though the conclusion follows from the premises.

The boundary case: "Mathematical objects exist because our best mathematical theories refer to them." The premise (theories refer to mathematical objects) and the conclusion (mathematical objects exist) are not identical, but critics argue the premise already presupposes the conclusion by taking reference at face value. Whether this is question-begging depends on philosophical commitments about reference and ontology.

**Exercise 7 (hard).** Analyze the following argument. Therefore, schools should provide free breakfast to all students, because if they do not, children will go hungry, fail their exams, drop out of school, and end up unemployed or in prison."

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The argument contains multiple elements. The initial premise (breakfast correlates with test performance) is a legitimate appeal to empirical evidence. The second part commits a slippery slope fallacy: no breakfast leads to hunger, then to test failure, then to dropping out, then to unemployment or prison. Each causal link is asserted without evidence. Many students who skip breakfast do fine on tests, many who fail tests do not drop out, and many who drop out find employment. The chain of consequences is not established.

The argument also contains elements of appeal to emotion (invoking children going hungry, ending up in prison) and a false dilemma (either provide free breakfast or accept disastrous outcomes). A stronger argument would focus on the evidence for the breakfast-performance link and the cost-benefit analysis of the breakfast program.

## Advanced results [Master]

### The Hamblin Revolution

Charles Hamblin's *Fallacies* (1970) transformed the study of informal fallacies. Before Hamblin, fallacies were treated as a checklist of errors, passed down from Aristotle through medieval textbooks with little critical examination. Hamblin showed that the standard treatment was incoherent: definitions were inconsistent, examples were often miscategorized, and the relationship between formal validity and informal goodness was unexplored.

Hamblin proposed a dialectical foundation for fallacies: errors should be understood as violations of the rules governing reasoned dialogue, not as violations of formal logic. This insight launched the informal logic movement and led to the development of formal dialectical systems (Hamblin 1970, 1971; Mackenzie 1979, 1990; Walton and Krabbe 1995) in which the rules of dialogue are precisely specified and fallacies are defined as rule violations.

Hamblin also demonstrated that the Aristotelian fallacies (particularly ignoratio elenchi — "ignorance of refutation") were more nuanced than the textbook tradition suggested. Aristotle's original treatment in the *Sophistical Refutations* was situated within his theory of dialectical debate, and the fallacies were errors relative to specific dialectical obligations, not abstract logical errors. The textbook tradition had decontextualized and simplified Aristotle's analysis to the point of distortion.

Hamblin's critique was devastating. After surveying dozens of logic textbooks, he found that no two agreed on the definition of "fallacy," many included examples that were not fallacious by their own definitions, and most presented Aristotle's list without engaging with his original analysis. The "standard treatment" — as Hamblin derisively called it — was a fossil: a curricular artifact that persisted through inertia rather than intellectual merit. Hamblin's challenge required the field to rebuild fallacy theory from the ground up, either by reconnecting it to dialectical contexts (as Walton and van Eemeren would do) or by abandoning the category entirely (as some philosophers have suggested).

### Argumentation schemes

Walton, Reed, and Macagno (2008) developed a comprehensive theory of argumentation schemes — stereotypical patterns of reasoning that represent common forms of argument. Each scheme has a set of critical questions that must be answered satisfactorily for the argument to be acceptable. Fallacies occur when a scheme is used without adequately addressing its critical questions.

The **scheme for argument from expert opinion** has the form: "Source E is an expert in domain D. E asserts that P (which is in domain D). Therefore, P is tentatively accepted." The critical questions include: Is E really an expert in D? Is E's assertion based on evidence? Is P consistent with what other experts in D say? Is E biased? The argument from expert opinion becomes an ad verecundiam fallacy when these critical questions have negative answers that are ignored.

The **scheme from consequences** has the form: "If A is adopted, then consequences C will occur. C is good (or bad). Therefore, A should be adopted (or rejected)." The critical questions include: Will C really occur? Are there other consequences that offset C? Is C really good (or bad)? The argument from consequences becomes a fallacious appeal to consequences when the desirability of C is treated as evidence for the truth of P rather than as a practical reason for action.

The **scheme from analogy** has the form: "Case C1 is similar to case C2 in properties P1...Pn. C1 has additional property Q. Therefore, C2 also has property Q." The critical questions include: Are C1 and C2 similar in the properties relevant to Q? Are there relevant dissimilarities? Is the analogy strong enough to support the conclusion? The argument from analogy becomes a weak analogy fallacy when the relevant similarities are insufficient.

### Fallacies and cognitive biases

Research in cognitive psychology has identified systematic patterns of faulty reasoning that correspond to traditional informal fallacies:

**Confirmation bias** (seeking evidence that supports your view and ignoring contradictory evidence) corresponds to a form of begging the question: treating your conclusion as established before examining the evidence.

**The availability heuristic** (judging probability by how easily examples come to mind) corresponds to hasty generalization: drawing conclusions from the most memorable or vivid instances rather than from representative samples.

**The conjunction fallacy** (rating a conjunction as more probable than one of its conjuncts, as in the Linda problem) corresponds to a form of false cause: treating co-occurring characteristics as more likely than either alone.

**Framing effects** (different evaluations of the same information depending on how it is presented) correspond to a form of equivocation: the same facts lead to different conclusions depending on the linguistic frame.

The correspondence between fallacies and biases suggests that fallacies are not merely logical errors but reflect deep features of human cognition. The heuristics that produce biases (availability, representativeness, anchoring) are generally adaptive — they produce good answers quickly in most situations — but lead to systematic errors in specific contexts. Teaching fallacy recognition may be understood as training people to recognize the contexts where their default heuristics fail.

### Fallacy theory in computing

Automated argument analysis is an active research area in computational linguistics and AI. Systems like Argument Mining (Lippi and Torroni 2016) use machine learning to identify argument structures in natural language text. Fallacy detection has been approached as a classification task: given a text segment, determine which (if any) fallacy it commits.

The computational approach faces the contextual challenge described in the key result above: the same surface form can be reasonable or fallacious depending on context. Current systems achieve moderate accuracy on clear-cut cases but struggle with borderline and context-dependent cases. The lack of a decidable procedure for fallacy detection is not merely a theoretical limitation but a practical one: current NLP systems cannot reliably distinguish legitimate from fallacious arguments in natural text.

### Cross-cultural perspectives on fallacies

The standard Western taxonomy of fallacies reflects Western argumentative norms. Cross-cultural research in argumentation theory suggests that what counts as a fallacy varies across cultures and communicative practices.

In many East Asian philosophical traditions, the distinction between argument from authority and legitimate deference to wisdom is drawn differently. Confucian discourse traditions treat respect for established knowledge as a virtue, not a logical error, and the burden of proof falls differently than in Socratic dialectic. The appeal to tradition — a fallacy in Western logic — carries more weight in cultures that prioritize continuity and precedent.

In Indigenous knowledge traditions, narrative and experiential evidence play roles that Western logic would classify as anecdotal (hasty generalization) or emotional (appeal to emotion). But these forms of evidence serve different epistemic functions within their traditions, and applying Western fallacy categories without sensitivity to these differences constitutes a form of epistemic imperialism.

Research by Sakamoto and Akashi (2017) on Japanese argumentation shows that the indirect, contextual reasoning valued in Japanese discourse avoids the direct confrontation that Western fallacy theory presupposes. Arguments that would be classified as "vague" or "irrelevant" in the Western tradition may be effective and appropriate within their cultural context.

These observations do not undermine fallacy theory but complicate it. They suggest that fallacies are defined relative to argumentative norms, and these norms vary across cultures. A truly universal fallacy theory would need to account for this variability without collapsing into relativism.

### Fallacies and the law

Legal reasoning provides a rich domain for studying fallacies because legal argumentation is highly structured, extensively documented, and consequential. Courts routinely encounter and address fallacies in legal arguments:

**Appeal to authority** in legal reasoning takes the form of citing precedent (stare decisis). Citing binding precedent is not fallacious — it is required by the legal system. But citing a precedent from a different jurisdiction, or from a case with materially different facts, can be a form of ad verecundiam.

**False dilemma** appears in legal arguments that present only two interpretations of a statute or contract. Courts often identify intermediate interpretations that the parties have not considered.

**Begging the question** in statutory interpretation occurs when a judge interprets a vague term in a way that presupposes the desired outcome. The legal realist critique of formalism argues that much judicial reasoning is question-begging: the judge decides the outcome first and then constructs the reasoning to support it.

**Ad hominem** in cross-examination is regulated by rules of evidence. Attacking a witness's credibility is permitted (and often necessary), but personal attacks unrelated to credibility are not. The legal system draws a principled distinction between legitimate credibility attacks and fallacious ad hominem arguments.

The study of fallacies in legal reasoning connects informal logic to jurisprudence, demonstrating that fallacy theory has practical applications in domains where the quality of reasoning directly affects people's lives.

## Connections [Master]

- **Propositional logic [24.01.01].** Formal fallacies (affirming the consequent, denying the antecedent) are violations of the inference rules of propositional logic. Informal fallacies go beyond these formal structures, involving content, context, and pragmatics. Propositional logic provides the background against which informal fallacies are defined: a formally valid argument that is nonetheless defective commits an informal fallacy.

- **Deductive reasoning [24.04.01].** Fallacies of presumption (begging the question, false dilemma) involve hidden assumptions that undermine deductive validity. Understanding deductive structure is necessary for identifying when an argument's premises fail to support its conclusion.

- **Inductive reasoning [24.05.01].** Fallacies of weak induction (hasty generalization, false cause, weak analogy) involve errors in probabilistic reasoning. The principles of good inductive inference — adequate sample size, controlled comparison, relevant similarities — define the standards that these fallacies violate.

- **Cognitive biases [24.07.01].** Many informal fallacies correspond to well-documented cognitive biases. The correspondence is not accidental: fallacies are the argumentative manifestations of the same heuristics that produce cognitive biases. Understanding both provides a richer picture of human reasoning.

- **Critical thinking in practice [24.08.01].** Fallacy recognition is a core skill for evaluating arguments in media, science, and everyday life. The taxonomy of fallacies provides a vocabulary for articulating why an argument fails, making criticism more precise and constructive.

- **Rhetoric and philosophy of language [20.XX].** Fallacies involving ambiguity (equivocation, amphiboly) depend on features of natural language that formal logic abstracts away. The philosophy of language — particularly theories of meaning, reference, and speech acts — provides tools for analyzing these fallacies at a deeper level.

- **Decision theory [24.06.01].** False dilemmas and slippery slope arguments distort decision-making by misrepresenting the option space. Decision theory provides tools for correctly identifying available options, assessing probabilities, and evaluating expected outcomes. Understanding how fallacies disrupt this process helps improve decision quality.

- **Ethics [20.XX].** Many fallacies have moral dimensions. The ad hominem fallacy harms individuals by attacking character instead of arguments. Straw man arguments misrepresent others, violating norms of intellectual honesty. Appeal to emotion manipulates rather than persuades. The connection between fallacies and ethics explains why fallacy avoidance is not merely a logical requirement but a moral one.

- **Science and the scientific method [03.XX].** Fallacies of weak induction (hasty generalization, false cause) are common errors in scientific reasoning. The scientific method — with its emphasis on controlled experiments, statistical significance, replication, and peer review — can be understood as a systematic defense against these fallacies. Understanding why these procedures exist requires understanding the fallacies they prevent.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

### Aristotle's Sophistical Refutations

Aristotle's *Sophistical Refutations* (c. 350 BCE) is the first systematic treatment of fallacies. Aristotle identified thirteen sophistical refutations — arguments that appear to refute an opponent's thesis but fail to do so. The list includes both linguistic fallacies (equivocation, amphiboly, composition, division, accent, figure of speech) and extra-linguistic fallacies (accident, secundum quid, ignoratio elenchi, petitio principii, non-cause as cause, many questions).

Aristotle situated fallacies within his theory of dialectical debate. In a dialectical exchange, one party (the questioner) tries to refute the other's thesis by deriving a contradiction from it. A sophistical refutation appears to succeed but fails because of a fallacy. Aristotle's analysis was aimed at training debaters to resist sophistical tactics.

The distinction between "sophistical" and "genuine" refutations depends on the dialectical context: a genuine refutation contradicts the thesis itself (not merely something related to it), on the basis of its own subject matter (not an accident), and without presupposing the original thesis. These conditions define what counts as a legitimate argument in dialectical debate.

### Medieval treatments

Medieval logicians expanded and refined Aristotle's list. Peter of Spain's *Tractatus* (13th century) and William of Sherwood's *Introductiones in Logicam* presented fallacies as part of the logical curriculum, alongside syllogistic, supposition theory, and obligations. The medieval approach was more systematic than Aristotle's: fallacies were classified by their causal mechanism (the linguistic or conceptual error that produces the appearance of validity) and illustrated with a wider range of examples.

The theory of obligations — structured dialectical games where one party (the "opponent") poses propositions and the other (the "respondent") must accept or deny them according to rules — provided a formal framework for studying fallacies in dialogue. Obligations games specified exactly what counts as a legitimate response, making it possible to identify precisely where a sophistical argument goes wrong.

### The Enlightenment and the textbook tradition

John Locke's *Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1690) identified four kinds of argument that people use to persuade others: argument from cause, argument from testimony, argument from custom, and argument from passion. Locke's classification was less formal than Aristotle's but more attuned to the psychological dimensions of persuasion. He recognized that people are moved not only by evidence and logic but by habit and emotion — a point that anticipates modern research on motivated reasoning and cognitive bias.

Locke was particularly critical of "enthusiasm" — the claim to private revelation as a source of knowledge. His critique anticipates the modern distinction between justified belief and mere conviction: enthusiasm bypasses the ordinary standards of evidence and argument, substituting subjective feeling for objective proof. The enthusiast's argument is not merely weak but immune to rational correction, because it takes the standards of rational assessment themselves to be subordinate to the inner experience.

Isaac Watts's *Logick* (1725) and Richard Whately's *Elements of Logic* (1826) established the textbook tradition of fallacy instruction that continues to the present day. These works standardized the list of fallacies, provided standard examples, and presented fallacy detection as a practical skill for educated people. The textbook tradition simplified Aristotle's dialectical framework in favor of a checklist approach: learn the list, spot the errors.

Whately's treatment was more sophisticated than most textbooks acknowledge. He distinguished between "logical" fallacies (errors in the form of reasoning) and "non-logical" or "material" fallacies (errors in the content), and he recognized that the same argument could be fallacious or sound depending on the context. Whately also noted that many fallacies work by exploiting the audience's lack of attention: the arguer introduces an irrelevant premise so smoothly that the audience does not notice the disconnect.

### Mill's treatment

John Stuart Mill's *A System of Logic* (1843) devoted careful attention to fallacies of induction, which he classified into five categories: fallacies of simple inspection (assuming a proposition is self-evident when it is not), fallacies of observation (misobservation or non-observation of relevant facts), fallacies of generalization (including hasty generalization and bad statistics), fallacies of ratiocination (formal errors in deductive reasoning), and fallacies of confusion (ambiguity and equivocation).

Mill's classification was more empirically grounded than Aristotle's. Rather than deriving fallacy categories from the structure of the syllogism, Mill derived them from the actual ways that inductive reasoning goes wrong. His classification of fallacies of observation anticipated modern concerns about biased data collection and sampling methodology. His treatment of fallacies of generalization anticipated modern concerns about statistical inference and the misuse of p-values.

Mill also identified what he called the "fallacy of many questions" — the practice of framing a question so that any answer concedes a hidden premise. "Have you stopped cheating on your taxes?" presupposes that you once cheated, regardless of whether you answer yes or no. This fallacy, which Mill traced back to the Sophists, remains common in political interviews and cross-examination.

### The informal logic movement

The informal logic movement emerged in the 1970s and 1980s as a response to the perceived inadequacy of formal logic for teaching critical thinking. Key figures include Ralph Johnson and J. Anthony Blair (Canada), Frans van Eemeren and Rob Grootendorst (Netherlands), and Douglas Walton (Canada).

Johnson and Blair's *Logical Self-Defense* (1977) argued that formal logic courses failed to equip students with the skills needed to evaluate real arguments. Real arguments, they observed, are typically not deductively valid — they rely on probabilistic reasoning, expert testimony, analogy, and causal inference. A critical thinking course should teach students to evaluate these informal patterns of reasoning.

Van Eemeren and Grootendorst's pragma-dialectical theory (1984, 1992, 2004) provided a normative framework: argumentation is a form of discourse aimed at resolving a difference of opinion, and fallacies are violations of the rules that govern this discourse. Their approach is systematic and comprehensive, connecting fallacies to speech act theory, discourse analysis, and the philosophy of language.

### Walton's pragmatic theory

Douglas Walton's prolific work on fallacies (1987-2014) developed a pragmatic theory in which fallacies are understood as misuse of argumentation schemes. On Walton's view, the same argument form can be reasonable or fallacious depending on the dialectical context and the burden of proof. An appeal to authority is reasonable when the authority is relevant and the claim is within their expertise, but fallacious when these conditions are not met.

Walton's approach avoids the problem of "false positives" that plagues the checklist approach. The traditional treatment classifies all appeals to authority as fallacious, but in practice we routinely and correctly rely on expert testimony. Walton's scheme-based approach explains why: the argumentation scheme for expert opinion is valid, but it carries critical questions that must be answered. The fallacy lies not in using the scheme but in failing to address the critical questions.

### Computational approaches

Recent work in computational argumentation applies machine learning to fallacy detection. Habernal et al. (2018) created a corpus of argumentative texts annotated for fallacy types and trained classifiers on this data. Results show that while some fallacies (like ad hominem) can be detected with reasonable accuracy, others (like equivocation and begging the question) resist automatic classification.

The challenge is not merely technical but conceptual. Fallacy detection requires understanding the argument's structure, the dialectical context, and the background knowledge relevant to assessing the premises. These requirements go beyond what current NLP systems can reliably extract from text. Progress in this area depends on advances in argument mining, natural language understanding, and knowledge representation.

### Fallacies and propaganda

The study of fallacies intersects with the study of propaganda and disinformation. Propaganda techniques — card stacking (selective presentation of evidence), bandwagon (appeal to popularity), transfer (associating a cause with positive symbols), name-calling (ad hominem) — are systematic applications of informal fallacies at scale.

The Institute for Propaganda Analysis (1937-1942) identified seven common propaganda devices: name-calling, glittering generalities, transfer, testimonial, plain folks, card stacking, and bandwagon. Each corresponds to one or more traditional fallacies. The IPA's work demonstrated that fallacy theory has practical applications in media literacy and civic education. The Institute was founded in response to the rise of totalitarian propaganda in the 1930s, and its materials were widely used in American schools to teach students to recognize manipulative rhetoric.

Modern disinformation campaigns exploit fallacies systematically. Bot networks create the appearance of widespread agreement (appeal to popularity). Deepfake videos create false evidence (a technological form of false testimony). Cherry-picked data creates misleading statistics (hasty generalization). The study of fallacies in the digital age requires attention to the specific affordances of social media platforms, including algorithmic amplification, filter bubbles, and the speed of information spread. The old problem of distinguishing good arguments from bad ones is now compounded by the problem of distinguishing authentic sources from fabricated ones.

Social media platforms present particular challenges for fallacy detection. The character limit on platforms like Twitter encourages oversimplification, making false dilemmas and straw man arguments almost inevitable. The algorithmic curation of feeds creates filter bubbles that reinforce existing beliefs through selection bias, a structural form of confirmation bias. The anonymity afforded by many platforms removes the social costs of ad hominem attacks, making them more frequent and more extreme. Understanding these platform-specific dynamics is essential for applying fallacy theory to contemporary discourse.

### Ad verecundiam in science

The role of authority in scientific reasoning is more nuanced than the traditional fallacy category suggests. Scientific knowledge is largely authority-based: most scientists accept results in fields outside their expertise on the basis of expert testimony and peer review. The question is when this reliance is legitimate and when it becomes fallacious.

John Hardwig (1985, 1991) argued that epistemic dependence on experts is not merely common but rationally required: no individual can personally verify even a small fraction of scientific knowledge. The rational response to this situation is to develop criteria for evaluating expert testimony: track record, institutional affiliation, peer consensus, and the quality of evidence presented. The ad verecundiam fallacy occurs not when you trust an expert but when you trust the wrong expert for the wrong reasons.

The replication crisis in psychology and other sciences (2010-present) illustrates the complexity. Many published findings failed to replicate, despite being produced by experts at prestigious institutions and published in top journals. The failure was not individual incompetence but systemic: publication incentives, statistical malpractice, and community-wide confirmation bias produced a body of "expert" knowledge that was unreliable. The lesson for fallacy theory is that even legitimate appeals to authority can be undermined by structural problems in the knowledge-producing community.

### Dialectical shifts and fallacies

Walton (1995) introduced the concept of dialectical shifts — transitions from one type of dialogue to another during an argument. A fallacy often involves an illicit dialectical shift: the argument appears to be one type of dialogue (e.g., a rational persuasion dialogue) but is actually another (e.g., a negotiation or a quarrel).

For example, a political debate is ostensibly a deliberation dialogue (aimed at deciding what to do), but candidates often shift to a persuasion dialogue (aimed at winning votes) or even a quarrel (aimed at attacking the opponent). An ad hominem attack that would be inappropriate in a deliberation dialogue may be effective in a persuasion context where the goal is to reduce the opponent's credibility with voters.

Recognizing dialectical shifts is crucial for fair fallacy assessment. An argument that is fallacious in one dialogue type may be appropriate in another. The error lies not in the argument itself but in presenting an argument appropriate to one context as if it were appropriate to another. This is the pragmatic heart of Walton's theory: fallacies are context-sensitive violations of dialectical norms.

### Philosophical debates about fallacy theory

The very concept of a "fallacy" is philosophically contested. Some theorists (Govier 1987, Finocchiaro 1987) argue that the traditional fallacy categories are too vague and overlapping to be useful. Others (Woods and Walton 1989) argue that fallacies are not genuine categories at all but pedagogical constructions that distort the complexity of real arguments.

A deeper debate concerns whether fallacies are fundamentally logical errors (failures of inference), epistemic errors (failures of evidence assessment), or dialectical errors (failures of discourse). The logical view treats fallacies as violations of validity, the epistemic view treats them as violations of rational belief formation, and the dialectical view treats them as violations of discourse norms. Each view captures part of the truth but none is complete on its own.

### Teaching fallacies

The pedagogical question of how to teach fallacy recognition has practical importance. Research in critical thinking education suggests that simply learning a list of fallacy names and definitions is insufficient for developing genuine critical thinking skills. Students who can pass a fallacy identification quiz often fail to identify fallacies in unconstrained natural-language contexts.

More effective approaches include: analyzing extended arguments (not just isolated examples), comparing strong and weak arguments of the same form, reconstructing arguments before evaluating them, and practicing argument construction as well as argument criticism. The goal is not to produce students who can label fallacies but to develop the habit of asking "what would make this argument good?" and then checking whether those conditions are met.

The "argument mapping" approach (van Gelder 2005) uses visual diagrams to represent the logical structure of arguments, making the relationships between premises and conclusions explicit. Research suggests that argument mapping improves critical thinking skills more than traditional lecture-based instruction, because it forces students to engage with argument structure rather than surface features.
