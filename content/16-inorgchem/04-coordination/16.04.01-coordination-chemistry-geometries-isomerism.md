## Intuition [Beginner]

A coordination compound has a central metal ion surrounded by molecules or ions called **ligands** that donate electron pairs to the metal. The number of donor atoms bonded to the metal is the **coordination number**. The most common coordination number is 6, giving octahedral geometry. Coordination number 4 gives either tetrahedral or square-planar geometry.

Ligands are classified by how many donor atoms they have. A **monodentate** ligand donates one electron pair (e.g., NH$_3$, Cl$^-$, H$_2$O). A **bidentate** ligand has two donor atoms that simultaneously bind the metal (e.g., ethylenediamine, en = NH$_2$CH$_2$CH$_2$NH$_2$). **Polydentate** ligands with three or more donor atoms are called **chelating** ligands (from the Greek "claw"). EDTA, a hexadentate ligand, wraps around the metal with six donor atoms and forms exceptionally stable complexes.

**Isomerism** in coordination compounds means different compounds with the same formula. **Geometric isomers** differ in how ligands are arranged around the metal (cis vs trans). **Optical isomers** are mirror images that cannot be superimposed (enantiomers). **Linkage isomers** differ in which atom of an ambidentate ligand binds the metal (e.g., NO$_2^-$ can bind through N or through O).

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Draw all isomers of [Co(NH$_3$)$_4$Cl$_2$]$^+$ and identify cis/trans.**

The complex has coordination number 6 (octahedral). Four NH$_3$ ligands and two Cl$^-$ ligands surround the Co$^{3+}$ centre.

**Trans isomer.** The two Cl$^-$ ligands sit at opposite vertices of the octahedron (180 degrees apart). This is unique — there is only one trans arrangement. The four NH$_3$ ligands occupy the remaining equatorial positions.

**Cis isomer.** The two Cl$^-$ ligands sit at adjacent vertices (90 degrees apart). All cis arrangements are equivalent by rotation — there is only one distinct cis isomer.

So there are exactly **two** geometric isomers: cis and trans. They can be distinguished by their physical properties (different colours, different dipole moments) and by their chemical reactivity.

**Exercise (medium, short-answer).**

Explain the chelate effect: why is [Ni(en)$_3$]$^{2+}$ more stable than [Ni(NH$_3$)$_6$]$^{2+}$, despite both having six nitrogen donors bonded to Ni$^{2+}$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider the entropy change when one en molecule displaces two NH$_3$ molecules.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The chelate effect is primarily entropic. Consider the equilibrium for replacing six NH$_3$ by three en:

$$\mathrm{[Ni(NH_3)_6]^{2+} + 3\,en \rightleftharpoons [Ni(en)_3]^{2+} + 6\,NH_3}$$

Three molecules of en replace six molecules of NH$_3$, giving a net increase of three molecules of free species in solution. This positive entropy change ($\Delta S > 0$) makes $\Delta G = \Delta H - T\Delta S$ more negative, favouring the chelate complex. The enthalpy contribution ($\Delta H$) is often similar for both complexes because the donor atoms are the same (nitrogen), but the entropy advantage is decisive.

</details>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Coordination number and geometry.** The coordination number (CN) determines the geometry:

| CN | Geometry | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| 2 | Linear | [Ag(NH$_3$)$_2$]$^+$, [AuCl$_2$]$^-$ |
| 4 | Tetrahedral | [Zn(NH$_3$)$_4$]$^{2+}$, [CoCl$_4$]$^{2-}$ |
| 4 | Square planar | [Pt(NH$_3$)$_4$]$^{2+}$, [Ni(CN)$_4$]$^{2-}$ |
| 6 | Octahedral | [Co(NH$_3$)$_6$]$^{3+}$, [Fe(CN)$_6$]$^{4-}$ |

Square planar is preferred by d$^8$ low-spin metals (Ni$^{2+}$, Pd$^{2+}$, Pt$^{2+}$, Au$^{3+}$) because the large CFSE from removing the two axial ligands stabilises the planar geometry.

**Isomer types in coordination compounds:**

1. **Geometric (cis-trans):** Different spatial arrangement of ligands. Requires at least two identical ligands on a complex with restricted geometry.

2. **Optical (enantiomeric):** Mirror-image, non-superimposable structures. Requires a complex with no mirror planes or inversion centre (e.g., [Co(en)$_3$]$^{3+}$, cis-[Co(NH$_3$)$_2$(en)$_2$]$^{3+}$).

3. **Linkage:** Ambidentate ligand binds through different donor atoms. NO$_2^-$ (nitro, N-bound vs nitrito, O-bound), SCN$^-$ (thiocyanato, S-bound vs isothiocyanato, N-bound).

4. **Ionisation:** Different ligands inside vs outside the coordination sphere. [Co(NH$_3$)$_5$SO$_4$]Br vs [Co(NH$_3$)$_5$Br]SO$_4$.

5. **Coordination (hydrate):** Water inside vs outside the sphere. [Cr(H$_2$O)$_6$]Cl$_3$ vs [Cr(H$_2$O)$_5$Cl]Cl$_2\cdot$H$_2$O.

**Ligand denticity and the chelate effect in detail.** The chelate effect can be quantified. For the reaction [Ni(NH$_3$)$_6$]$^{2+}$ + 3 en $\to$ [Ni(en)$_3$]$^{2+}$ + 6 NH$_3$, the stepwise stability constants are $\log K_1 \approx 7.5$, $\log K_2 \approx 6.4$, $\log K_3 \approx 4.6$ for en (three steps), compared to $\log\beta_6 \approx 8.6$ for six NH$_3$ (six steps). The overall formation constant for the chelate is larger because the entropy contribution from releasing three additional molecules of NH$_3$ drives the equilibrium.

The macrocyclic effect is an extension: macrocyclic ligands (like cyclam, a tetraaza macrocycle) form even more stable complexes than equivalent open-chain chelates because the pre-organised macrocycle pays a lower conformational entropy cost upon binding.

**Hard-soft acid-base (HSAB) principle and ligand preference.** Metal ions are classified as hard (small, high charge, low polarisability: Al$^{3+}$, Ti$^{4+}$, Cr$^{3+}$, Fe$^{3+}$), borderline (Fe$^{2+}$, Co$^{2+}$, Ni$^{2+}$, Cu$^{2+}$, Zn$^{2+}$), or soft (large, low charge, high polarisability: Pd$^{2+}$, Pt$^{2+}$, Ag$^+$, Hg$^{2+}$). Hard acids prefer hard bases (F$^-$, OH$^-$, NH$_3$, H$_2$O). Soft acids prefer soft bases (I$^-$, CN$^-$, CO, PR$_3$, S$^{2-}$). This principle predicts ligand preferences: Pt$^{2+}$ forms more stable complexes with CN$^-$ than with NH$_3$, while Al$^{3+}$ forms more stable complexes with F$^-$ than with I$^-$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **Not all four-coordinate complexes are tetrahedral.** d$^8$ metals (Pd, Pt, Au) strongly prefer square planar. The choice between tetrahedral and square planar depends on the CFSE difference: if the CFSE gain from square planar exceeds the ligand-ligand repulsion cost, square planar wins.

- **Optical isomerism requires non-superimposable mirror images, not just chirality.** A tetrahedral complex with four different monodentate ligands is chiral but cannot have optical isomers detectable by standard methods because enantiomers interconvert too rapidly.

**Ligand substitution mechanisms.** Coordination complexes undergo ligand substitution via two main mechanisms:

1. **Associative (A):** The incoming ligand attacks the metal to form a five- or seven-coordinate intermediate, then the outgoing ligand departs. Rate = $k[\text{complex}][\text{incoming ligand}]$. Common for square-planar complexes (Pt(II), Pd(II)) where there is an accessible coordination site perpendicular to the plane.

2. **Dissociative (D):** The outgoing ligand leaves first, creating a vacant coordination site, then the incoming ligand binds. Rate = $k[\text{complex}]$ (independent of incoming ligand concentration). Common for octahedral complexes of the first-row transition metals, where the coordination number is already six and the metal is sterically saturated.

Most octahedral substitutions proceed by an interchange mechanism (I$_a$ or I$_d$) intermediate between pure A and D, where bond making and bond breaking are concurrent but with varying degrees of assistance from the incoming ligand. The mechanism depends on the metal, the oxidation state, and the specific ligands involved.

**Kinetic vs thermodynamic control in synthesis.** The distinction between kinetic and thermodynamic products is operationally important. [Co(NH$_3$)$_5$Cl]$^{2+}$ forms rapidly when CoCl$_2$ is treated with NH$_3$ in air (kinetic product — Cl$^-$ remains bound because substitution at Co(III) is slow). But the thermodynamically more stable product is [Co(NH$_3$)$_6$]$^{3+}$, which can be obtained by prolonged heating or by using a silver salt to remove the Cl$^-$ as AgCl, forcing substitution. The inertness of Co(III) complexes (low-spin d$^6$, large CFSE, high activation barrier for substitution) is the kinetic reason that so many Co(III) isomers were historically isolable — they provided the experimental basis for Werner's coordination theory.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Proposition (Chelate effect: entropy analysis).** *For the reaction $\mathrm{[M(L)_n] + n/2\,(L\text{-}L) \to [M(L\text{-}L)_{n/2}] + n\,L}$ where L is a monodentate ligand and L-L is a bidentate analogue, the entropy change is positive because the number of free molecules in solution increases.*

**Proof.** The reaction replaces $n$ monodentate ligands with $n/2$ bidentate ligands. Starting state: 1 complex + $n/2$ free bidentate ligands = $1 + n/2$ species. Ending state: 1 complex + $n$ free monodentate ligands = $1 + n$ species. The increase in the number of independent translational degrees of freedom (from $n/2$ to $n$ free molecules) gives $\Delta S > 0$ in solution, where translational entropy dominates. At room temperature, the $T\Delta S$ term typically exceeds any enthalpy difference, making $\Delta G < 0$ and the chelate complex thermodynamically favoured. $\blacksquare$

How many geometric isomers exist for [Co(NH$_3$)$_3$Cl$_3$]? Draw them.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Three NH$_3$ and three Cl$^-$ on an octahedron. Consider whether the three Cl can be on one face (facial) or around the equator (meridional).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Two geometric isomers:

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (hard, short-answer).**

[Co(en)$_3$]$^{3+}$ exists as a pair of optical isomers. Explain the structural basis for the chirality and predict whether the complex would show optical activity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Three bidentate en ligands wrap around the octahedron in a helical pattern. What symmetry elements are absent?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

[Co(en)$_3$]$^{3+}$ has D$_3$ symmetry — a three-fold rotation axis but no mirror planes or inversion centre. The three en ligands, each spanning cis positions on the octahedron, trace a helical path around the metal. The helix can be left-handed ($\Lambda$) or right-handed ($\Delta$), giving two non-superimposable mirror images.

The complex shows optical activity: the $\Lambda$ and $\Delta$ enantiomers rotate plane-polarised light in opposite directions. They can be separated by chiral resolving agents and have identical chemical properties except in chiral environments (e.g., reactions with other chiral molecules, circular dichroism spectroscopy).

This was one of the first coordination compounds shown to exhibit optical isomerism (Werner, 1911), providing key evidence for the octahedral coordination geometry.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (hard, short-answer).**

The trans-effect series in square-planar Pt(II) complexes ranks ligands by their ability to labilise the ligand trans to themselves: CN$^-$ > CO > C$_2$H$_4$ > PR$_3$ > NH$_3$ > OH$^-$ > Cl$^-$ > Br$^-$ > I$^-$. Use this series to predict the product of the reaction of [PtCl$_4$]$^{2-}$ with (a) NH$_3$, then (b) NH$_3$ again, then (c) Cl$^-$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The incoming ligand preferentially replaces the ligand trans to the weakest trans-directing ligand. In [PtCl$_4$]$^{2-}$, all ligands are Cl$^-$ (same trans-effect), so the first NH$_3$ can substitute any Cl.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Step (a): [PtCl$_4$]$^{2-}$ + NH$_3$ $\to$ [PtCl$_3$(NH$_3$)]$^-$ + Cl$^-$. NH$_3$ replaces one Cl$^-$ (all equivalent in the starting square-planar complex).

Step (b): [PtCl$_3$(NH$_3$)]$^-$ + NH$_3$ $\to$ cis-[PtCl$_2$(NH$_3$)$_2$] + Cl$^-$. The second NH$_3$ replaces the Cl$^-$ that is trans to the other Cl$^-$ (since Cl$^-$ has a stronger trans-effect than NH$_3$, the Cl trans to Cl is more labile than the Cl trans to NH$_3$). This gives the cis isomer.

Step (c): cis-[PtCl$_2$(NH$_3$)$_2$] + Cl$^-$ $\to$ [PtCl$_3$(NH$_3$)]$^-$ + NH$_3$. Wait — this is the reverse of step (b). The Cl$^-$ replaces NH$_3$ trans to Cl$^-$ (because Cl$^-$ has a stronger trans effect than NH$_3$, the NH$_3$ trans to Cl is labilised). This gives [PtCl$_3$(NH$_3$)]$^-$, the same product as step (a).

The trans-effect series is the tool for rational synthesis of specific isomers of square-planar complexes: the order of addition determines the geometry.

</details>

</aside>

[Pt(NH$_3$)Cl(py)(NO$_2$)] (where py = pyridine) has how many distinct geometric isomers? Identify each.

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Predict whether [PtCl$_4$]$^{2-}$ or [Pt(CN)$_4$]$^{2-}$ is more stable thermodynamically and explain why.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Both are square-planar d$^8$ Pt(II) complexes. Consider the strength of the metal-ligand bond and the CFSE contribution.

</details>

The CFSE for d$^8$ square planar increases with $\Delta$, and CN$^-$ produces the largest $\Delta$. Additionally, Pt(II) is a soft metal that forms strong bonds with the soft CN$^-$ ligand (HSAB principle: soft-soft interactions are favoured).

## The trans-effect and kinetic vs thermodynamic control [Master]

The trans-effect is a kinetic phenomenon: a ligand that is a strong trans-director labilises the ligand opposite to it, making that position more reactive toward substitution. The series runs roughly: CN$^-$ ~ CO ~ C$_2$H$_4$ > PR$_3$ > H$^-$ > CH$_3^-$ > SC(NH$_2$)$_2$ > Ph$^-$ > NO$_2^-$ ~ I$^-$ ~ SCN$^-$ > Br$^-$ > Cl$^-$ > py > NH$_3$ > OH$^-$ > H$_2$O.

The mechanism is twofold. Strong sigma-donor ligands (H$^-$, CH$_3^-$) labilise the trans position by weakening the trans Pt-L bond through sigma competition (both ligands compete for the same metal orbital). Strong pi-acceptor ligands (CO, CN$^-$, C$_2$H$_4$) stabilise the transition state for associative substitution by accepting electron density from the metal, facilitating the five-coordinate intermediate.

The trans-effect is operationally distinct from thermodynamic stability. A complex synthesised under kinetic control (using trans-effect-directed substitutions) may be thermodynamically less stable than an alternative isomer. The kinetic product forms faster; the thermodynamic product is more stable. Heating or prolonged reaction can convert the kinetic product to the thermodynamic one.

### Nomenclature of coordination compounds

IUPAC naming rules for coordination compounds follow a systematic pattern:

1. Name the cation first, then the anion (as in simple ionic compounds).
2. Within the coordination sphere, list ligands in alphabetical order (ignoring prefixes).
3. Use prefixes for multiple identical ligands: di-, tri-, tetra-, penta-, hexa-. For complex ligand names that already contain these prefixes, use bis-, tris-, tetrakis-.
4. Anionic ligands end in -o (chloro, cyano, hydroxo, thiocyanato).
5. Neutral ligands use their molecular name (ammine for NH$_3$, aqua for H$_2$O, carbonyl for CO, nitrosyl for NO).
6. The metal name follows the ligands. For anionic complexes, add -ate to the metal name (ferrate, cobaltate, cuprate, nickelate, platinate).
7. The oxidation state is given in Roman numerals in parentheses after the metal name.

Examples: [Co(NH$_3$)$_6$]Cl$_3$ = hexaamminecobalt(III) chloride. K$_3$[Fe(CN)$_6$] = potassium hexacyanoferrate(III). [Pt(NH$_3$)$_2$Cl$_2$] = diamminedichloroplatinum(II).

### Stability constants

The thermodynamic stability of a coordination compound is quantified by the overall formation constant $\beta_n$ and the stepwise formation constants $K_1, K_2, \ldots, K_n$:

$$\mathrm{M + nL \rightleftharpoons ML_n}, \quad \beta_n = \frac{[\mathrm{ML_n}]}{[\mathrm{M}][\mathrm{L}]^n}$$

For [Cu(NH$_3$)$_4$]$^{2+}$: $\log\beta_4 \approx 13.3$. The formation is strongly favoured but stepwise: $K_1 > K_2 > K_3 > K_4$ because each successive NH$_3$ binds to a less coordinatively unsaturated (and hence less reactive) complex.

The Irving-Williams series orders the stability of complexes with the same ligand across the first-row transition metals: Mn$^{2+}$ < Fe$^{2+}$ < Co$^{2+}$ < Ni$^{2+}$ < Cu$^{2+}$ > Zn$^{2+}$. The trend reflects increasing CFSE from Mn to Cu, with Cu$^{2+}$ being the most stable because of the additional Jahn-Teller stabilisation. The drop at Zn$^{2+}$ (d$^{10}$, no CFSE) confirms the CFSE origin of the trend.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Alfred Werner (Nobel Prize, 1913) established the coordination theory: metal ions form primary (ionic) and secondary (coordination) bonds with defined geometries. His prediction and experimental confirmation of optical isomerism in [Co(en)$_3$]$^{3+}$ proved the octahedral geometry. The trans-effect was systematised by Chernyaev in the 1920s for Pt(II) chemistry.

The philosophical note: coordination chemistry demonstrates that the same metal ion can form dramatically different compounds with different properties (colour, magnetism, reactivity) depending solely on the ligands. The metal provides the scaffold; the ligands determine the character.
