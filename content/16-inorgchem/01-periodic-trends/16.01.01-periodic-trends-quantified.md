## Intuition [Beginner]

The periodic table is not just a lookup chart. It encodes regular patterns in how atoms behave — how easily they lose electrons, gain electrons, share electrons, and how big they are. These patterns are called **periodic trends**, and they follow directly from two facts: nuclear charge increases across a period, and filled electron shells shield the outer electrons from that charge.

**Ionisation energy** is the energy required to remove the outermost electron from a neutral atom. It generally increases from left to right across a period because the nuclear charge grows while the shielding stays roughly constant — the outer electron feels a stronger pull. It decreases from top to bottom because the outer electron sits in a higher shell, farther from the nucleus.

**Electron affinity** is the energy released when an atom gains an electron. Halogens (Group 17) have the most negative (most favourable) electron affinities because adding one electron completes their outer shell. Noble gases have near-zero electron affinities because their shells are already full.

**Electronegativity** is a combined measure of an atom's tendency to attract electrons in a chemical bond. The Pauling scale (0 to 4) ranks fluorine at 3.98, the most electronegative element. Electronegativity increases across a period and decreases down a group, following the same logic as ionisation energy.

**Atomic radius** decreases across a period (increasing nuclear charge pulls electrons in) and increases down a group (adding new shells). Ionic radii follow the same trend, with cations smaller than their parent atoms (lost electrons reduce electron-electron repulsion and increase effective nuclear charge per remaining electron) and anions larger (added electrons increase repulsion).

**Diagonal relationships** are striking similarities between elements diagonally adjacent in the periodic table: Li resembles Mg, Be resembles Al, and B resembles Si. These arise because the diagonal pairs have similar charge-density ratios (the increase in charge across a period roughly compensates for the increase in size down the group).

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Plot first ionisation energies for Na–Ar and explain the dips at Al and S.**

The first ionisation energies (kJ/mol) are:

| Element | Na | Mg | Al | Si | P | S | Cl | Ar |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| IE$_1$ | 496 | 738 | 578 | 786 | 1012 | 1000 | 1251 | 1521 |

The overall trend increases from Na to Ar. Two dips break the smooth rise.

**Dip at Al.** Magnesium has the electron configuration $[\mathrm{Ne}]\,3s^2$ — a filled 3s subshell. The outermost electron is a 3s electron held by the full +12 nuclear charge (minus shielding). Aluminium has $[\mathrm{Ne}]\,3s^2\,3p^1$. The 3p electron being removed is in a higher-energy orbital than the 3s, and it is better shielded from the nucleus by the filled 3s$^2$ subshell. The combination of higher energy and better shielding makes the 3p electron easier to remove than a 3s electron, giving Al a lower ionisation energy than Mg despite having one more proton.

**Dip at S.** Phosphorus has $[\mathrm{Ne}]\,3s^2\,3p^3$ — a half-filled 3p subshell (one electron in each of the three p-orbitals, all parallel by Hund's rule). Sulphur has $[\mathrm{Ne}]\,3s^2\,3p^4$. The fourth 3p electron must pair with one of the three already-occupied orbitals, creating electron-electron repulsion within that orbital. This intra-orbital repulsion makes the paired electron slightly easier to remove, lowering S's ionisation energy below P's.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**First ionisation energy** ($IE_1$): $\mathrm{X(g) \to X^+(g) + e^-}$, $\Delta H = IE_1$. Second ionisation energy ($IE_2$): $\mathrm{X^+(g) \to X^{2+}(g) + e^-}$, and so on. $IE_2 > IE_1$ always, because the electron is removed from a cation (stronger effective nuclear charge). Large jumps in $IE_n$ vs $IE_{n-1}$ signal the start of a new shell (removing a core electron).

**Electron affinity** ($EA$): $\mathrm{X(g) + e^- \to X^-(g)}$, $\Delta H = EA$. By convention, exothermic electron attachment has $EA < 0$ (IUPAC) or $EA > 0$ (older texts). Chlorine has the most exothermic $EA$ ($-349\,\mathrm{kJ/mol}$); fluorine's is $-328\,\mathrm{kJ/mol}$ (less exothermic despite higher electronegativity, because the small F$^-$ ion has high electron-electron repulsion).

**Electronegativity scales.**

- **Pauling scale**: based on bond-energy data. $\chi_A - \chi_B = 0.102\sqrt{\Delta}$, where $\Delta = D_{AB} - \sqrt{D_{AA} \cdot D_{BB}}$ is the extra ionic stabilisation of the heteronuclear bond. Range: $\chi_{Cs} = 0.79$ to $\chi_F = 3.98$.

- **Mulliken scale**: $\chi_M = (IE + EA)/2$, the average of ionisation energy and electron affinity. Directly connected to atomic properties rather than bond energies. Correlates linearly with the Pauling scale.

- **Allred-Rochow scale**: $\chi_{AR} = 0.359\,Z_{eff}/r^2 + 0.744$, where $Z_{eff}$ is the effective nuclear charge and $r$ is the covalent radius in Angstroms. Based on the electrostatic force felt by a bonding electron.

**Atomic and ionic radii.** Atomic radius is measured from covalent bond lengths (half the distance between identical bonded atoms), van der Waals radii, or metallic radii. The **effective nuclear charge** $Z_{eff} = Z - \sigma$ (where $\sigma$ is the shielding constant from Slater's rules) governs the trend. Across a period, $Z_{eff}$ increases (shielding does not keep up with increasing $Z$), pulling electrons in. Down a group, new shells are added faster than $Z_{eff}$ increases, so the radius grows.

**Diagonal relationships.** Li–Mg, Be–Al, B–Si share chemical similarities because the diagonal pairs have similar charge-to-radius ratios. For example, both Li and Mg form covalent organometallic compounds (organolithium and Grignard reagents) rather than purely ionic bonds, and both form nitrides with N directly ($\mathrm{Li_3N}$, $\mathrm{Mg_3N_2}$) while other Group 1 metals do not.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **Electron affinity does not always become more exothermic across a period.** Nitrogen ($EA = +7\,\mathrm{kJ/mol}$, slightly endothermic) is an anomaly: the added electron must pair with an existing electron in a half-filled $p^3$ subshell, losing exchange stabilisation.

- **The lanthanide contraction makes period 6 transition metals smaller than expected.** The filling of the 4f subshell across the lanthanides poorly shields the 5d electrons, making the period 6 transition metals (Hf, Ta, W) nearly the same size as their period 5 congeners (Zr, Nb, Mo). This has major consequences for their chemistry.

- **Ionic radius depends on coordination number and spin state.** High-spin Fe$^{2+}$ is larger than low-spin Fe$^{2+}$ because the additional electron in the $e_g$ set in the high-spin form occupies antibonding orbitals that push the ligands outward.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Proposition (Slater's rules for effective nuclear charge).** *For an electron in the $ns$ or $np$ orbital of an atom with nuclear charge $Z$, the effective nuclear charge is $Z_{eff} = Z - \sigma$, where $\sigma$ is calculated by: (a) electrons in groups higher (further from nucleus) than the electron of interest contribute 0 to $\sigma$; (b) other electrons in the same group ($ns, np$) contribute 0.35 each; (c) electrons in the $(n-1)$ shell contribute 0.85 each; (d) electrons in shells $(n-2)$ and lower contribute 1.00 each.*

This is an empirical rule, not a theorem in the mathematical sense, but it produces quantitative predictions that match experimental trends.

**Example.** For the 3s electron of Al ($Z = 13$, electron configuration $1s^2\,2s^2\,2p^6\,3s^2\,3p^1$):

$\sigma = (2 \times 0.85)_{\text{from 2s,2p}} + (8 \times 0.85)_{\text{from 2s,2p}} \ldots$

Correct calculation: the 3s electron sees: the other 3s electron (0.35), the two 3p electrons — wait, for Al with $3s^2 3p^1$, when removing a 3p electron:

$\sigma = 2 \times 0.35\ (\text{other 3s,3p}) + 8 \times 0.85\ (\text{2s,2p}) + 2 \times 1.00\ (\text{1s}) = 0.70 + 6.80 + 2.00 = 9.50$.

$Z_{eff} = 13 - 9.50 = 3.50$.

For the 3s electron of Mg ($Z = 12$, $1s^2\,2s^2\,2p^6\,3s^2$):

$\sigma = 1 \times 0.35\ (\text{other 3s}) + 8 \times 0.85\ (\text{2s,2p}) + 2 \times 1.00\ (\text{1s}) = 0.35 + 6.80 + 2.00 = 9.15$.

$Z_{eff} = 12 - 9.15 = 2.85$.

So $Z_{eff}$ increases from Mg to Al (2.85 to 3.50), yet the ionisation energy *dips* — the 3p electron in Al is in a higher-energy orbital despite the larger $Z_{eff}$. The orbital energy difference ($3p$ vs $3s$) outweighs the $Z_{eff}$ increase, illustrating that Slater's rules capture shielding but not orbital energy.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Using Slater's rules, calculate $Z_{eff}$ for the valence electron of sodium ($Z = 11$, $[\mathrm{Ne}]\,3s^1$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The 3s electron sees: no other electrons in its group, 8 electrons in the $n=2$ shell (contribute 0.85 each), 2 electrons in the $n=1$ shell (contribute 1.00 each).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\sigma = 0 \times 0.35 + 8 \times 0.85 + 2 \times 1.00 = 0 + 6.80 + 2.00 = 8.80$.

$Z_{eff} = 11 - 8.80 = 2.20$.

Na's valence electron feels only 2.20 units of positive charge despite $Z = 11$. The inner shells shield most of the nuclear charge, which is why Na readily loses its outer electron.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, short-answer).**

Arrange the following atoms in order of increasing atomic radius: N, O, F, Na, Mg.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Na and Mg are in period 3 (much larger than period 2). Within period 2, radius decreases from N to F.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

F < O < N < Mg < Na.

Within period 2, radius decreases from N to F (increasing $Z_{eff}$). Na and Mg (period 3) are much larger than any period 2 element because they have an additional electron shell. Na is larger than Mg because Mg's higher $Z_{eff}$ pulls its electrons in.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, short-answer).**

Explain the anomaly in nitrogen's electron affinity ($EA \approx +7\,\mathrm{kJ/mol}$, slightly endothermic) compared to carbon ($EA = -122\,\mathrm{kJ/mol}$) and oxygen ($EA = -141\,\mathrm{kJ/mol}$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Nitrogen has a half-filled p-subshell ($2p^3$). What happens to exchange energy when you add a fourth electron?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Nitrogen's $2p^3$ configuration has three unpaired electrons, one in each p-orbital. This gives maximum exchange stabilisation (three parallel electrons can exchange in $3!/2!1! = 3$ ways, contributing a large exchange energy). Adding a fourth electron to form $2p^4$ forces it to pair with one of the existing electrons, losing a unit of exchange stabilisation and incurring intra-orbital repulsion. The net effect: the energy gained from the added electron's attraction to the nucleus barely exceeds the exchange-stabilisation loss and repulsion cost, making the process nearly thermoneutral or slightly endothermic.

Carbon ($2p^2$, adding to give $2p^3$) gains exchange stabilisation, and oxygen ($2p^4$, adding to give $2p^5$) approaches a filled subshell, so both have strongly exothermic electron affinities.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Using Slater's rules, compare $Z_{eff}$ for the 4s electron of K ($Z = 19$) and the 3d electron of Sc ($Z = 21$). Explain why the 4s orbital fills before 3d.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Calculate $Z_{eff}$ for each. The key is that 4s electrons penetrate through the 3d shell to feel more nuclear charge than the naive ordering would suggest.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

K 4s electron: $\sigma = 8 \times 0.85\ (\text{3s,3p}) + 10 \times 1.00\ (\text{1s,2s,2p}) = 6.80 + 10.0 = 16.80$. $Z_{eff} = 19 - 16.80 = 2.20$.

Sc 3d electron: $\sigma = 1 \times 0.35\ (\text{other 3d}) + 9 \times 1.00\ (\text{1s,2s,2p,3s,3p}) = 0.35 + 9.0 = 9.35$. $Z_{eff} = 21 - 9.35 = 11.65$.

The 3d electron in Sc has much higher $Z_{eff}$ than the 4s electron in K, but the 4s orbital is at a higher principal quantum level and penetrates closer to the nucleus than expected. In K, the 4s orbital is lower in energy than 3d because the 4s electron penetrates through the 3s/3p core (it has non-zero probability density near the nucleus), feeling more effective nuclear charge than a simple $n$-based argument would predict. Once 3d begins filling (at Sc and beyond), the increasing $Z_{eff}$ eventually makes 3d lower than 4s, which is why transition metals lose 4s electrons before 3d when forming cations.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, short-answer).**

The lanthanide contraction causes the ionic radii of period 6 transition metals to be nearly identical to their period 5 congeners (e.g., Zr and Hf both have ionic radius ~72 pm in the +4 state). Explain the physical origin of the lanthanide contraction and predict its consequence for the chemistry of Pt vs Pd.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Across the lanthanides (Ce to Lu), the 4f subshell fills. How well do f-electrons shield?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The lanthanide contraction arises because the 14 electrons filling the 4f subshell across the lanthanide series (Ce to Lu) shield the nuclear charge poorly. The 4f orbitals are diffuse and have poor radial overlap with the 5d and 6s valence electrons. As $Z$ increases by 14 across the series, the shielding increases by much less than 14, so $Z_{eff}$ at the valence shell increases steadily. The result: the period 6 transition metals (Hf through Au) contract to nearly the same size as their period 5 congeners (Zr through Ag).

For Pt vs Pd: the lanthanide contraction makes Pt nearly the same size as Pd, but with higher nuclear charge. The consequence is that Pt has a higher charge density, stronger relativistic effects (contraction of the 6s orbital), and consequently higher ionisation energies, stronger metal-ligand bonds, and greater kinetic inertness compared to Pd. Pt complexes are generally more substitution-inert and more resistant to oxidation than their Pd analogues — a pattern exploited in catalysis (Pd for fast cross-coupling, Pt for more controlled, selective transformations).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Compute the Mulliken electronegativity for chlorine, given $IE_1 = 1251\,\mathrm{kJ/mol}$ and $EA = -349\,\mathrm{kJ/mol}$. Convert to the Pauling scale using the approximate conversion $\chi_P \approx 0.336(\chi_M - 0.615)$ where $\chi_M$ is in eV.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Convert both IE and EA to eV first (1 eV = 96.485 kJ/mol), then average.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$IE_1 = 1251/96.485 = 12.97\,\mathrm{eV}$. $EA = 349/96.485 = 3.62\,\mathrm{eV}$ (using magnitude).

$\chi_M = (12.97 + 3.62)/2 = 8.30\,\mathrm{eV}$.

$\chi_P \approx 0.336 \times (8.30 - 0.615) = 0.336 \times 7.685 = 2.58$.

The actual Pauling electronegativity of Cl is 3.16. The discrepancy illustrates that the linear conversion is approximate — Mulliken and Pauling scales measure different things (Mulliken: atomic properties; Pauling: bond energies) and the correlation is rough.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Critically evaluate the statement: "Electronegativity is not an observable quantity." What does this imply for the different electronegativity scales, and can any of them be derived from first principles?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider whether electronegativity corresponds to a single measurable quantity or a composite concept. What does each scale actually measure?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Electronegativity is not a quantum-mechanical observable — there is no operator whose eigenvalue gives the electronegativity. It is a composite concept that quantifies the tendency of an atom to attract electrons in a molecule, and different scales measure it through different proxies:

- Pauling scale: bond dissociation energies (measures the ionic stabilisation of heteronuclear bonds).
- Mulliken scale: $(IE + EA)/2$ (measures atomic properties directly, closest to a first-principles definition).
- Allred-Rochow scale: $Z_{eff}/r^2$ (measures the electrostatic force on a bonding electron).
- Allen scale: average one-electron energy of the valence-shell electrons (computable from spectroscopic data).

The Mulliken and Allen scales are closest to first-principles definitions because they use atomic spectroscopic data rather than molecular bond energies. However, even these require the concept of "an atom in a molecule" — an atom does not have an electronegativity in isolation; it is a property of the atom *in the context of a bond*. This is why DFT-based definitions (Parr, Donnelly, Levy, Palke: $\chi = -\mu = -(\partial E/\partial N)$, the negative of the chemical potential as the number of electrons varies) provide the most rigorous framework: they define electronegativity as a derivative of the total energy with respect to electron number, connecting it to the fundamental concept of chemical potential.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Predict and explain the following comparisons: (a) Why is $IE_2$ of Na (4562 kJ/mol) enormously larger than $IE_1$ (496 kJ/mol)? (b) Why is $IE_2$ of Mg (1451 kJ/mol) only about twice $IE_1$ (738 kJ/mol)?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider which shell the electron is being removed from in each case. $IE_2$ of Na removes a core electron; $IE_2$ of Mg removes a valence electron.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) Na's $IE_1$ removes the single 3s valence electron ($[\mathrm{Ne}]\,3s^1 \to [\mathrm{Ne}]$), leaving a noble-gas core. $IE_2$ must remove a 2p electron from the stable neon core, which is held much more tightly (smaller orbital, higher $Z_{eff}$, no shielding from outer electrons). The jump from 496 to 4562 kJ/mol (a factor of 9.2) signals that a new shell is being opened — the hallmark of a core electron.

(b) Mg's $IE_1$ removes one of the two 3s electrons ($[\mathrm{Ne}]\,3s^2 \to [\mathrm{Ne}]\,3s^1$). $IE_2$ removes the remaining 3s electron ($[\mathrm{Ne}]\,3s^1 \to [\mathrm{Ne}]$). Both are valence electrons in the same shell, so the increase is modest (738 to 1451, a factor of 2.0) — it reflects only the increased $Z_{eff}$ from removing the shielding contribution of the first electron. There is no shell boundary crossed. $IE_3$ of Mg (7733 kJ/mol) would show the enormous jump corresponding to breaking into the neon core.

</details>

</aside>

## Relativistic effects on periodic trends [Master]

For heavy elements (Z > ~50), relativistic effects significantly modify periodic trends. Electrons in s and p orbitals near the nucleus reach speeds approaching the speed of light, increasing their effective mass and contracting the orbital. The contraction of s and p orbitals increases shielding of the d and f orbitals, which expand.

Consequences: mercury is a liquid at room temperature (the 6s contraction stabilises the 6s$^2$ pair, reducing metallic bonding). Gold has its distinctive yellow colour (the 5d to 6s gap narrows due to relativistic contraction, shifting absorption into the blue). Thallium prefers the +1 oxidation state (the 6s$^2$ inert pair effect, stabilised relativistically). These effects grow roughly as $Z^2$ and are fully significant for the 5d and 6p elements.

## Connections [Master]

- **Atomic orbitals from H-atom QM [14.04.01]** — the orbital shapes, quantum numbers, and energy ordering that underlie all periodic trends come from solving the Schrodinger equation for hydrogen-like atoms and extending to multi-electron atoms via the aufbau principle.

- **Crystal field theory [16.03.01]** — the d-orbital splitting patterns and their variation across the transition series depend on the periodic trends in d-orbital energy and ionic radius.

- **Organometallic chemistry [16.05.01]** — the 18-electron rule and the stability of organometallic complexes depend on the electronegativity and orbital energies of the central metal, both periodic-trend properties.

- **Bond polarity and molecular polarity [14.02.01]** — electronegativity differences determine bond polarity, which governs intermolecular forces, solubility, and reactivity.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Mendeleev published the first recognisable periodic table in 1869, predicting the existence and properties of then-undiscovered elements (gallium, germanium, scandium). Moseley's X-ray spectroscopy (1913) established that the periodic law is a function of atomic number (nuclear charge), not atomic mass. The quantum-mechanical explanation came with the Schrodinger equation (1926) and the development of the aufbau principle, Hund's rules, and Slater's rules through the 1930s.

The periodic table is one of the most powerful organising principles in science. It is not a theory but a pattern — the periodic law — that any correct atomic theory must explain. The fact that the periodic law follows from the quantum mechanics of multi-electron atoms is one of the great achievements of 20th-century physics.
