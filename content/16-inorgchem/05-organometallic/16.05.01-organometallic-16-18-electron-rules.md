## Intuition [Beginner]

Organometallic compounds contain at least one metal-carbon bond. This sounds simple, but the chemistry is remarkably diverse: ferrocene (iron sandwiched between two carbon rings), Grignard reagents (Mg bonded to carbon), and Wilkinson's catalyst (Rh with phosphine and carbon ligands) are all organometallic.

The central organising principle is the **18-electron rule**: stable organometallic complexes of transition metals tend to have 18 valence electrons. This is the closed-shell configuration where all nine valence orbitals (one s, three p, five d) are filled. Counting to 18 works like this:

- The metal contributes its d-electrons plus the two s-electrons it would have in the neutral atom (use the group number for the neutral metal, then adjust for charge).
- Each ligand contributes a defined number of electrons: CO, PR$_3$, and halides contribute 2 each; alkenes contribute 2; cyclopentadienyl (Cp) contributes 5; benzene contributes 6.

When the count is 18, the complex is generally stable. Counts of 16 are common for square-planar complexes (d$^8$ metals). Counts above 18 are rare and indicate instability.

**Oxidative addition** is a key reaction type: a metal complex in a low oxidation state "oxidises" itself by inserting into a bond (e.g., X-Y), increasing its oxidation state by 2 and gaining two new ligands. The reverse is **reductive elimination**.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Ferrocene Fe(C$_5$H$_5$)$_2$: count electrons and explain why it is stable (18e).**

Step 1. Metal electron count. Iron is in Group 8, so neutral Fe contributes 8 valence electrons. Ferrocene is neutral (no charge on the complex), so we use 8.

Step 2. Ligand electron count. Each cyclopentadienyl anion (Cp$^-$) is a 6-electron pi-donor (5 carbons contributing one electron each from the aromatic pi system, plus the negative charge gives one more). However, the standard counting convention treats each Cp as contributing 5 electrons when considered as a neutral radical (Cp-dot). Using the covalent model: Fe(0) has 8 electrons, each Cp radical contributes 5. Total: $8 + 5 + 5 = 18$.

Step 3. Check the rule. 18 electrons = closed shell. Ferrocene is indeed exceptionally stable — it survives in air, resists oxidation below 400 degrees C, and can be sublimed. The 18-electron configuration gives a filled set of bonding MOs and empty antibonding MOs, maximising the bonding interaction.

In electron counting for organometallic complexes, a CO ligand contributes:

A. 1 electron
B. 2 electrons
C. 4 electrons
D. 6 electrons

CO is a two-electron sigma donor (donates its lone pair on carbon to the metal).

**B.** CO donates its carbon lone pair to the metal in a sigma bond, contributing 2 electrons. It also accepts metal electron density through pi-back-bonding (into the CO pi-star orbital), but this does not change the electron count — back-bonding is a bonding interaction, not an additional electron donation.

**Exercise (easy, true/false).**

A 16-electron square-planar complex is necessarily unstable.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

d$^8$ metals (Pd, Pt, Ni, Rh, Ir) in square-planar geometry typically have 16 electrons.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**False.** 16-electron square-planar complexes are common and stable for d$^8$ metals. The square-planar geometry uses only eight of the nine valence orbitals (the p$_z$ orbital perpendicular to the plane is empty and high in energy), making 16 electrons the closed-shell count for this geometry. Vaska's complex (IrCl(CO)(PPh$_3$)$_2$) is a well-known 16-electron complex that is perfectly stable.

</details>

**Exercise (medium, short-answer).**

Count the valence electrons for Cr(CO)$_6$ and determine whether it obeys the 18-electron rule.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Cr is Group 6. Each CO contributes 2 electrons.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Cr is in Group 6, contributing 6 electrons. Six CO ligands each contribute 2: $6 \times 2 = 12$. Total: $6 + 12 = 18$ electrons.

Cr(CO)$_6$ obeys the 18-electron rule and is a stable, diamagnetic, volatile solid. The strong-field CO ligands produce a large $\Delta_o$, making the low-spin d$^6$ configuration ($t_{2g}^6$) particularly stable.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise (medium, short-answer).**

In the oxidative addition of H$_2$ to Vaska's complex IrCl(CO)(PPh$_3$)$_2$, the Ir(I) (d$^8$) is oxidised to Ir(III) (d$^6$). Show that the electron count changes from 16 to 18 and explain why this drives the reaction.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Ir(I) in the starting complex: Group 9, +1 charge = d$^8$ = 8 metal electrons. Ligands: Cl (2), CO (2), 2 PPh$_3$ (4), total = 16. After adding H$_2$ (2 new H ligands, each contributing 1 electron): count changes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Starting: Ir(I) = d$^8$ = 8e. Cl$^-$ = 2e, CO = 2e, 2 PPh$_3$ = 4e. Total = $8 + 2 + 2 + 4 = 16$e.

After oxidative addition: Ir(III) = d$^6$ = 6e (oxidation state increased by 2). Ligands: Cl$^-$ = 2e, CO = 2e, 2 PPh$_3$ = 4e, 2 H = 2e. Total = $6 + 2 + 2 + 4 + 2 = 16$e. Wait — the total is still 16 because the H$_2$ molecule contributes 2 electrons (one from each H). Actually: H$_2$ is a neutral molecule, and in oxidative addition it adds as two H ligands. Each H contributes 1 electron. So total = $6 + 2 + 2 + 4 + 2 = 16$e.

Actually, the common formulation: the 16-electron Ir(I) complex is coordinatively unsaturated and reacts with H$_2$ to give the 18-electron Ir(III) dihydride. Let me recount: Ir(I) = Group 9, charge = +1, so d electrons = $9 - 1 = 8$. The neutral electron count: Ir(0) = 9e, minus 1 for the +1 charge = 8e. Ligands: Cl (2e as anionic), CO (2e), 2 PPh$_3$ (4e). Total = 16e.

After H$_2$ oxidative addition: Ir(III), d$^6$ = 6e. Ligands: Cl (2e), CO (2e), 2 PPh$_3$ (4e), 2H (2e). Total = $6 + 2 + 2 + 4 + 2 = 16$e. In the covalent (neutral) counting model, oxidative addition does not change the total count.

In the ionic counting model: Ir$^{+}$ = 8e + Cl$^-$ (2e) + CO (2e) + 2PPh$_3$ (4e) = 16e. After: Ir$^{3+}$ = 6e + Cl$^-$ (2e) + CO (2e) + 2PPh$_3$ (4e) + 2H$^-$ (2e each) = $6 + 2 + 2 + 4 + 4 = 18$e. In ionic counting, each H is counted as H$^-$ contributing 2e, giving 18. The preference for 18 drives the oxidative addition of H$_2$.

</details>

</aside>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**18-electron rule.** For a transition metal in an organometallic complex, the total valence electron count is:

$$n_{total} = n_{metal} + \sum_i n_{ligand,i} - q_{complex}$$

where $n_{metal}$ is the group number (number of valence electrons in the neutral atom), $n_{ligand,i}$ is the electron contribution of each ligand, and $q_{complex}$ is the charge on the complex (ionic model).

**Ligand electron contributions (ionic model):**

| Ligand | Electrons | Ligand | Electrons |
|---|---|---|---|
| H$^-$ | 2 | CO | 2 |
| Cl$^-$, Br$^-$, I$^-$ | 2 | PR$_3$ | 2 |
| CH$_3^-$, C$_6$H$_5^-$ | 2 | H$_2$C=CH$_2$ | 2 |
| CN$^-$ | 2 | Cp ($\eta^5$-C$_5$H$_5^-$) | 6 |
| NO (linear) | 3 | Benzene ($\eta^6$-C$_6$H$_6$) | 6 |

**Oxidative addition:** ML$_n$ + X-Y $\to$ L$_n$M(X)(Y). The metal oxidation state increases by 2, the coordination number increases by 2, and the electron count increases by 2 (from the X-Y bond electrons).

**Reductive elimination:** L$_n$M(X)(Y) $\to$ ML$_n$ + X-Y. Reverse of oxidative addition.

**Metallocenes:** Sandwich compounds with two Cp ligands. Ferrocene (FeCp$_2$, 18e) is the archetype. Other examples: cobaltocene (CoCp$_2$, 19e, one unpaired electron, reactive), nickelocene (NiCp$_2$, 20e, unstable), chromocene (CrCp$_2$, 16e).

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **The 18-electron rule is a guideline, not a law.** Many stable complexes have 16 or even 14 electrons. Square-planar d$^8$ complexes (16e) are among the most important in catalysis.

- **Not all ligands are 2-electron donors.** Cp is a 6-electron donor (5 carbons coordinated in an eta-5 fashion). Allyl can be 2e (eta-1) or 4e (eta-3).

- **Oxidative addition requires a coordinatively unsaturated metal.** An 18-electron complex cannot undergo oxidative addition without first losing a ligand (dissociation) to create a vacant site.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Proposition (18-electron rule as closed-shell criterion).** *For a transition metal in an octahedral field with both sigma and pi ligand interactions, the nine valence orbitals (one s, three p, five d) form nine bonding/antibonding pairs. Filling all nine bonding MOs requires 18 electrons, giving maximum stability.*

**Proof.** In the MO picture, the metal's nine valence orbitals (5d + 3p + 1s = 9) interact with ligand orbital SALCs. The interaction produces nine bonding MOs (stabilised, occupied in the 18e complex) and nine antibonding MOs (destabilised, empty). Filling all nine bonding MOs with 18 electrons maximises the bonding energy and gives a closed-shell configuration with a large HOMO-LUMO gap. This is the same principle as the noble-gas electron configuration in atomic structure. $\blacksquare$

Count the valence electrons for Mn(CO)$_5$(CH$_3$). Is this complex expected to be stable?

Mn is Group 7. Five CO = 10e, CH$_3$ = 2e (as alkyl).

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (hard, short-answer).**

Describe the catalytic cycle of Wilkinson's catalyst (RhCl(PPh$_3$)$_3$) for the hydrogenation of alkenes. Identify the oxidative addition and reductive elimination steps.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Wilkinson's catalyst is a 16-electron Rh(I) complex. The cycle involves H$_2$ addition, alkene coordination, insertion, and elimination.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

1. **Dissociation.** RhCl(PPh$_3$)$_3$ (16e) loses one PPh$_3$ to give RhCl(PPh$_3$)$_2$ (14e, coordinatively unsaturated).

2. **Oxidative addition of H$_2$.** RhCl(PPh$_3$)$_2$ + H$_2$ $\to$ RhCl(H)$_2$(PPh$_3$)$_2$ (18e, Rh(III) dihydride).

3. **Alkene coordination.** RhCl(H)$_2$(PPh$_3$)$_2$ + alkene $\to$ RhCl(H)$_2$(alkene)(PPh$_3$)$_2$ (may lose one PPh$_3$ to stay at 18e or go through a 20e intermediate that rapidly rearranges).

4. **Migratory insertion (hydride migration).** One hydride migrates to the coordinated alkene, forming an alkyl group: RhCl(H)(alkyl)(PPh$_3$)$_2$.

5. **Reductive elimination.** The remaining hydride and the alkyl group eliminate as the alkane product: RhCl(PPh$_3$)$_2$ + alkane. Rh is reduced from Rh(III) back to Rh(I), regenerating the active 14-electron catalyst.

The net reaction: H$_2$ + alkene $\to$ alkane, catalysed by a Rh complex that cycles between Rh(I) and Rh(III).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (hard, short-answer).**

Explain the concept of pi-back-bonding in metal-CO complexes and predict how the C-O stretching frequency changes as more electron density is placed on the metal.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Back-bonding donates metal d-electron density into the CO pi-star orbital. What does partial occupation of pi-star do to the C-O bond order?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Pi-back-bonding involves donation of electron density from filled metal d-orbitals (of $t_{2g}$ symmetry) into the empty pi-star antibonding orbitals of CO. This strengthens the metal-CO bond (synergic bonding: sigma donation from C to metal, pi back-donation from metal to CO) but weakens the C-O bond because the pi-star orbital is antibonding with respect to C-O.

As more electron density is placed on the metal (e.g., a more electron-rich metal centre or additional electron-donating ligands), the back-bonding increases, more pi-star character is populated, and the C-O bond weakens further. This lowers the C-O stretching frequency in the IR spectrum. Free CO stretches at 2143 cm$^{-1}$; Ni(CO)$_4$ at ~2057 cm$^{-1}$; [Co(CO)$_4$]$^-$ at ~1890 cm$^{-1}$ (the anionic complex has more electron density on Co, more back-bonding, lower frequency). The IR frequency is a direct probe of back-bonding strength.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, short-answer).**

Predict whether the following complexes obey the 18-electron rule: (a) TiCp$_2$Cl$_2$, (b) Mo(CO)$_6$, (c) CoCp$_2$. For those that do not, explain why they are still isolable.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Count electrons for each. Not all stable complexes need 18 electrons.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) TiCp$_2$Cl$_2$: Ti(IV) = d$^0$ = 0e + 2 Cp (12e) + 2 Cl$^-$ (4e) = **16e**. Does not obey the 18-electron rule. Stable because Ti(IV) has no d-electrons to participate in pi-back-bonding, and the complex is in the maximum oxidation state — there is no energetic benefit from adding more electrons. 16e is sufficient when the metal is in a high oxidation state with only sigma-donor ligands.

(b) Mo(CO)$_6$: Mo(0) = d$^6$ = 6e + 6 CO (12e) = **18e**. Obeys the rule. Stable diamagnetic complex.

(c) CoCp$_2$: Co(II) = d$^7$ = 7e + 2 Cp (10e) = **17e**. Does not obey the rule. Cobaltocene is a 17-electron complex with one unpaired electron. It is isolable but highly reactive — it is a strong reducing agent, readily losing one electron to form the stable 18-electron [CoCp$_2$]$^+$ cation. The 17e count reflects the balance between Co's electron count and the ligand field: the single extra electron sits in an antibonding orbital, making the complex a good electron donor.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Describe the difference between oxidative addition and sigma-bond metathesis. Why do early transition metals (Groups 3–5) favour sigma-bond metathesis while late transition metals (Groups 8–10) favour oxidative addition?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Early metals are in high oxidation states and electrophilic. Late metals are in low oxidation states and nucleophilic. What happens to the oxidation state in each mechanism?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Oxidative addition:** ML$_n$ + X-Y $\to$ L$_n$M(X)(Y). The metal oxidation state increases by 2 and two new bonds form. Requires a low-valent, electron-rich metal that can accommodate the increased coordination number and oxidation state.

**Sigma-bond metathesis:** L$_n$M-R + H-X $\to$ L$_n$M-X + R-H (four-centre transition state). The metal oxidation state does not change. The reaction proceeds through a concerted four-membered ring transition state where bonds break and form simultaneously.

Early transition metals (Groups 3–5) favour sigma-bond metathesis because: (1) they are already in high oxidation states (d$^0$–d$^2$), making further oxidation (oxidative addition) thermodynamically unfavourable; (2) they are electrophilic and lack the electron density needed for oxidative addition; (3) the d$^0$ configuration cannot accommodate the two-electron oxidation.

Late transition metals (Groups 8–10) favour oxidative addition because: (1) they are in low oxidation states (d$^8$–d$^{10}$), making oxidation favourable; (2) they are electron-rich and nucleophilic, able to donate electron density into the X-Y sigma-star orbital; (3) the resulting higher oxidation state is stabilised by the soft, pi-accepting ligands typically present.

</details>

</aside>

## Connections [Master]

- **Crystal field theory [16.03.01]** — the 18-electron rule is the closed-shell criterion in the MO/ligand-field framework.

- **Coordination chemistry [16.04.01]** — organometallic complexes are coordination compounds with carbon-based ligands; the same geometry and isomerism principles apply.

- **Retrosynthetic analysis [15.10.01]** — Pd-catalysed cross-coupling (Suzuki, Stille, Negishi, Heck), olefin metathesis (Grubbs), and hydroformylation are organometallic catalytic methods central to modern synthetic planning.

- **Bioinorganic chemistry [16.06.01]** — metalloenzymes with metal-carbon bonds (vitamin B12 coenzyme, hydrogenase Ni-Fe centres) use organometallic bonding.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Grignard reagents (RMgX, Nobel Prize 1912) were the first widely used organometallic reagents. Ferrocene was discovered in 1951 and its sandwich structure determined by Wilkinson and Fischer (Nobel Prize, 1973). Ziegler and Natta's metallocene catalysts for polymerisation (Nobel Prize, 1963) launched industrial organometallic chemistry. The 2010 Nobel Prize (Heck, Negishi, Suzuki) recognised Pd-catalysed cross-coupling, now the most widely used C-C bond-forming reaction in medicinal chemistry.

### Hapticity and multi-electron ligands

The hapticity $\eta^n$ of a ligand specifies how many contiguous atoms of the ligand are bonded to the metal. This is critical for electron counting:

| Ligand | Hapticity | Electron donation |
|---|---|---|
| Cp (cyclopentadienyl) | $\eta^5$ | 6e (ionic) / 5e (covalent) |
| Benzene | $\eta^6$ | 6e |
| Allyl | $\eta^3$ | 4e |
| Allyl | $\eta^1$ | 2e |
| Butadiene | $\eta^4$ | 4e |
| Cycloheptatrienyl (Cht) | $\eta^7$ | 7e |

Hapticity changes during reactions. For example, a $\eta^5$-Cp ligand can slip to $\eta^3$ to open a coordination site, then return to $\eta^5$ after the substrate binds. This "ring slippage" mechanism is important in catalytic cycles where the metal needs to cycle between 18 and 16 electron counts.

### Metal-metal bonding

Organometallic compounds can contain direct metal-metal bonds. Mn$_2$(CO)$_{10}$ has a Mn-Mn single bond: each Mn(CO)$_5$ fragment contributes 17 electrons, and the Mn-Mn bond provides one additional electron to each fragment, bringing both to 18. Co$_2$(CO)$_8$ similarly has a Co-Co bond. The electron counting extends naturally: each metal-metal bond contributes 1 electron to each metal's count.

Multiple metal-metal bonds occur in compounds like Re$_2$Cl$_8^{2-}$ (quadruple bond: one sigma, two pi, one delta). The delta bond arises from overlap of d$_{xy}$ orbitals on the two metals, a bonding interaction unique to metals with accessible d-orbitals. Metal-metal bonding is the molecular precursor to the extended metallic bonding found in bulk metals and clusters.

### Key catalytic applications

Organometallic catalysts are central to industrial chemistry and pharmaceutical synthesis. A few landmark examples:

- **Ziegler-Natta polymerisation:** TiCl$_4$/AlEt$_3$ catalysts polymerise ethylene and propylene at mild conditions. The active site is a Ti(III) or Ti(IV) alkyl complex that undergoes repeated alkene insertion into the Ti-C bond.
- **Hydroformylation:** Co or Rh carbonyl catalysts convert alkenes + CO + H$_2$ to aldehydes. The mechanism involves alkene coordination, CO insertion, and hydrogenolysis.
- **Olefin metathesis:** Mo or Ru carbenes (Grubbs catalysts) exchange alkylidene fragments between alkenes. The mechanism proceeds through a metallacyclobutane intermediate.
- **Pd cross-coupling:** Pd(0) complexes catalyse C-C bond formation between aryl/vinyl halides and organometallic nucleophiles (boronic acids in Suzuki, organotin in Stille, organozinc in Negishi). The catalytic cycle is: oxidative addition of Ar-X to Pd(0), transmetallation with the nucleophile, reductive elimination of the coupled product.

These catalytic cycles all follow the same logical pattern: the metal shuttles between two oxidation states (typically 0 and +2), and the 18-electron rule governs the stability of the intermediates.

### The isolobal analogy

Hoffmann's isolobal analogy connects fragments of organometallic molecules to simpler organic species. Two fragments are isolobal if they have the same number of frontier orbitals, the same number of electrons in those orbitals, and similar symmetry and energy. For example, Mn(CO)$_5$ (17e, one orbital with one electron) is isolobal with CH$_3$ (one sp$^3$ orbital with one electron). Both form single bonds to partners with one electron: Mn$_2$(CO)$_{10}$ is analogous to C$_2$H$_6$ (ethane), and Mn(CO)$_5$CH$_3$ is analogous to C$_2$H$_6$ viewed as CH$_3$-CH$_3$.

The isolobal analogy allows chemists to predict the structures of unknown organometallic compounds by analogy with known organic molecules. Fe(CO)$_4$ (16e, two frontier orbitals with two electrons) is isolobal with CH$_2$ (carbene), and Fe$_2$(CO)$_8$ is analogous to cyclopropane when bridging CO ligands are included. Fe(CO)$_3$ is isolobal with CH$^+$, and Fe$_3$(CO)$_{12}$ has a structure analogous to cyclopropane with three CH groups. This powerful unifying concept earned Hoffmann the Nobel Prize in 1981.
