## Intuition [Beginner]

In unit 16.03.02 we saw how six ligands in an octahedron split the five d-orbitals into a higher pair ($e_g$) and a lower trio ($t_{2g}$). But not every complex is octahedral. Two other geometries matter enormously: **tetrahedral** (four ligands at the corners of a tetrahedron) and **square planar** (four ligands in one plane).

A tetrahedral complex inverts the octahedral splitting. The doubly degenerate $e$ set ($d_{z^2}$, $d_{x^2-y^2}$) drops to lower energy and the triply degenerate $t_2$ set ($d_{xy}$, $d_{xz}$, $d_{yz}$) rises. The gap $\Delta_t$ is smaller — roughly 4/9 of the octahedral splitting $\Delta_o$ for the same metal and ligand. Because the gap is so small, tetrahedral complexes are almost always high-spin.

A square-planar complex is what you get by removing the two axial ligands from an octahedron. The $d_{x^2-y^2}$ orbital, which points directly at the four remaining in-plane ligands, shoots up to become the highest-energy orbital. This makes square-planar geometry especially favourable for d$^8$ metals like Pt$^{2+}$ and Au$^{3+}$, where the destabilising $d_{x^2-y^2}$ orbital stays empty.

**Tanabe-Sugano diagrams** are the tool that ties everything together. Each diagram plots the energies of all electronic states of a d$^n$ configuration against the crystal field strength. You read off transition energies as vertical lines at the appropriate field strength, directly predicting the positions of d-d absorption bands in the UV-Vis spectrum.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider the tetrahedral complex $[\mathrm{CoCl}_4]^{2-}$. Cobalt(II) is d$^7$. In a tetrahedral field the splitting is inverted ($e$ below $t_2$) and small ($\Delta_t \approx 3300$ cm$^{-1}$). Because $\Delta_t$ is much smaller than the pairing energy, the complex is high-spin.

Fill the tetrahedral levels: the lower $e$ pair gets two electrons (one each, parallel spins), and the upper $t_2$ trio gets five electrons. By Hund's rule, each of the three $t_2$ orbitals gets one electron first, then the remaining two pair up. The result is three unpaired electrons. The deep blue colour of $[\mathrm{CoCl}_4]^{2-}$ comes from d-d transitions within the $t_2$ set and from $e \to t_2$ excitations.

Now compare with the square-planar complex $[\mathrm{PtCl}_4]^{2-}$. Platinum(II) is also d$^8$. In square-planar geometry the $d_{x^2-y^2}$ orbital sits very high — so high that all eight d-electrons fill the four lower orbitals completely. The complex is diamagnetic (zero unpaired electrons) and pale yellow, absorbing in the near-UV.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Tetrahedral splitting.** For four identical ligands at the vertices of a regular tetrahedron, the point group is $T_d$ (order 24, no inversion centre). The five d-orbitals decompose under $T_d$ as

$$
V_d \;=\; e \;\oplus\; t_2,
$$

where $e = \{d_{z^2}, d_{x^2-y^2}\}$ (doubly degenerate, dimension 2) and $t_2 = \{d_{xy}, d_{xz}, d_{yz}\}$ (triply degenerate, dimension 3). Unlike $O_h$, the $g$ subscript is dropped because $T_d$ has no centre of inversion. The splitting is inverted relative to octahedral: $e$ sits lower, $t_2$ sits higher. The tetrahedral splitting parameter is

$$
\Delta_t \;=\; E(t_2) - E(e) \;=\; \tfrac{4}{9}\,\Delta_o
$$

for the same metal ion and ligand type. The $4/9$ factor arises from two geometric contributions: (a) four ligands instead of six ($2/3$ factor) and (b) no ligand sits directly on a Cartesian axis (additional $2/3$ factor from angular averaging of orbital-ligand overlap), giving $\tfrac{2}{3} \times \tfrac{2}{3} = \tfrac{4}{9}$.

The barycentre rule applies: $2\,E(e) + 3\,E(t_2) = 0$, yielding $E(e) = -\tfrac{3}{5}\Delta_t$ and $E(t_2) = +\tfrac{2}{5}\Delta_t$.

**Square-planar splitting.** The square-planar geometry ($D_{4h}$ point group, order 16) is derived from octahedral by removing two axial ligands. The five d-orbitals split into four energy levels:

$$
d_{x^2-y^2}\;(b_{1g}) \;\gg\; d_{xy}\;(b_{2g}) \;>\; d_{z^2}\;(a_{1g}) \;>\; d_{xz},\,d_{yz}\;(e_g).
$$

The $d_{x^2-y^2}$ orbital ($b_{1g}$) is far above the others because its lobes point directly at all four equatorial ligands. The energy gap between $b_{1g}$ and $b_{2g}$ is approximately $1.3\,\Delta_o$, exceeding the original octahedral splitting because the in-plane ligands move closer when axial ligands are removed.

**Spinels and tetrahedral vs octahedral site preference.** In spinel oxides $\mathrm{AB}_2\mathrm{O}_4$, metal ions occupy both tetrahedral and octahedral sites. The **site preference** is governed by CFSE: an ion with large octahedral CFSE (d$^3$: Cr$^{3+}$, or low-spin d$^6$: Co$^{3+}$) prefers octahedral sites; an ion with zero or negative octahedral-site preference (d$^0$, d$^5$ HS, d$^{10}$) accepts tetrahedral sites. A **normal spinel** has A on tetrahedral sites and B on octahedral sites (e.g., $\mathrm{MgAl}_2\mathrm{O}_4$). An **inverse spinel** has half the B ions on tetrahedral sites and A on octahedral sites (e.g., $\mathrm{Fe}_3\mathrm{O}_4 = \mathrm{Fe}^{3+}[\mathrm{Fe}^{2+}\mathrm{Fe}^{3+}]\mathrm{O}_4$). The inversion is driven by CFSE: Fe$^{3+}$ (d$^5$, zero CFSE in either site) has no preference, so Fe$^{2+}$ (d$^6$, gains CFSE on the octahedral site) occupies the octahedral positions.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **$\Delta_t = \tfrac{4}{9}\Delta_o$ is an approximation, not an exact relation.** The $4/9$ factor assumes identical metal-ligand distances in both geometries. In practice, tetrahedral complexes often have shorter M-L bonds (four ligands repel each other less than six), which can increase $\Delta_t$ somewhat. The qualitative point — tetrahedral splitting is roughly half the octahedral splitting — is robust.

- **Square-planar is not simply "flat tetrahedral."** The two geometries have different point groups ($D_{4h}$ vs $T_d$), different character tables, and different orbital-ordering patterns. A square-planar complex can be understood as the limit of progressive tetragonal elongation of an octahedron, not as a distorted tetrahedron.

- **Tanabe-Sugano diagrams are configuration-specific.** There is a different diagram for each d$^n$ configuration ($d^2$ through $d^8$). You cannot use the $d^3$ diagram to analyse a $d^6$ complex. Each diagram encodes the full set of term energies for that one configuration as a function of $\Delta_o/B$.

## Key result [Intermediate+]

**Result (Tetrahedral splitting ratio).** *For a transition-metal ion with a given set of identical ligands, the tetrahedral crystal field splitting is related to the octahedral splitting by* $\Delta_t = \tfrac{4}{9}\Delta_o$*. This relation follows from the geometry of the ligand positions relative to the d-orbital lobes and holds to first order in the point-charge model.*

The derivation proceeds by computing the matrix elements of the ligand-field potential in $T_d$ symmetry, analogously to the $O_h$ calculation of unit 16.03.02 but with four ligands at the tetrahedral positions $(a,a,a)$, $(a,-a,-a)$, $(-a,a,-a)$, $(-a,-a,a)$ rather than six on the Cartesian axes. The potential has lower symmetry and fewer ligands; evaluating the crystal field integrals with the $T_d$ character table confirms the $e \oplus t_2$ decomposition and the inverted ordering. The numerical $4/9$ ratio emerges from the angular-overlap integrals.

**Corollary (Tetrahedral high-spin rule).** *Because $\Delta_t < P$ for all realistic first-row transition-metal complexes, tetrahedral complexes are always high-spin for d$^4$ through d$^7$ configurations.*

### Worked example: interpreting the spectrum of $[\mathrm{CoCl}_4]^{2-}$

Cobalt(II) is d$^7$. In tetrahedral geometry the ground state is $^4A_2$ (from the $^4F$ free-ion term). The spin-allowed d-d transitions are:

$$
{}^4A_2 \to {}^4T_2, \qquad {}^4A_2 \to {}^4T_1(F), \qquad {}^4A_2 \to {}^4T_1(P).
$$

Observed bands for $[\mathrm{CoCl}_4]^{2-}$: approximately 3500 cm$^{-1}$ (near-IR, $^4A_2 \to {}^4T_2$), 7500 cm$^{-1}$ (near-IR, $^4A_2 \to {}^4T_1(F)$), and 15,000 cm$^{-1}$ (visible, $^4A_2 \to {}^4T_1(P)$, giving the deep blue colour). The lowest transition gives $\Delta_t \approx 3500$ cm$^{-1}$ directly, consistent with $\tfrac{4}{9} \times \Delta_o$ for the corresponding octahedral complex $[\mathrm{Co}(\mathrm{H_2O})_6]^{2+}$ ($\Delta_o \approx 9300$ cm$^{-1}$, giving $\tfrac{4}{9} \times 9300 \approx 4100$ cm$^{-1}$, within the expected approximation error).

## Tanabe-Sugano diagrams: construction and interpretation [Master]

A Tanabe-Sugano diagram for a d$^n$ configuration plots the energy of every electronic term (in units of the Racah parameter $B$, as $E/B$) against the crystal field strength ($\Delta_o/B$). The ground state is always plotted as the horizontal axis ($E/B = 0$). These diagrams were introduced by Tanabe and Sugano in 1954 [ref: TODO_REF Miessler Ch. 8] and remain the primary tool for assigning d-d spectra.

**Construction.** The free-ion terms for each d$^n$ configuration are derived from Russell-Saunders coupling: the direct products of $n$ spin-orbitals are decomposed into antisymmetrised linear combinations classified by total $L$ and $S$, using the vector coupling scheme and the Pauli principle. Each free-ion term $^{2S+1}L$ then splits in $O_h$ (or $T_d$) by subduction of $D^{(L)}$ from SO(3) to the point group, as developed in unit 16.03.02. The resulting sub-terms have point-group symmetry labels ($A_{2g}$, $T_{2g}$, etc.) and multiplicities equal to the dimension of the irrep.

The energies of all sub-terms are then computed as functions of $\Delta/B$ by diagonalising the full d-electron Hamiltonian — including the inter-electron Coulomb repulsion (parameterised by Racah parameters $B$ and $C$) and the crystal field potential (parameterised by $\Delta$) — in the basis of d-orbital Slater determinants. The matrix elements separate into a field-independent part (the free-ion repulsion, set by $B$ and $C$) and a field-dependent part (proportional to $\Delta$). Diagonalising at each value of $\Delta/B$ traces out the curves on the diagram.

**Configuration interaction.** The key technical subtlety is that sub-terms of the same symmetry and spin multiplicity mix as $\Delta/B$ changes. For example, in d$^3$ the $^4T_{1g}$ sub-term arises from both the $^4F$ and $^4P$ free-ion terms. These two $^4T_{1g}$ components are not independent levels — they are coupled by the off-diagonal matrix element of the crystal field, producing two non-crossing curves that repel each other. This **configuration interaction** is what gives the Tanabe-Sugano diagrams their characteristic curved, non-crossing line patterns. The diagonalisation automatically includes all configuration interaction; the resulting curves cannot intersect where the symmetries and multiplicities match (non-crossing rule).

**Reading the diagram.** To assign a spectrum, determine $\Delta/B$ for the complex (from the lowest spin-allowed transition, which directly gives $\Delta$). Draw a vertical line at that $\Delta/B$ value. The intersections of this line with the energy-level curves give the transition energies. Compare with observed band positions to extract $B$ (and confirm or determine $C/B$).

**The d$^6$ diagram and spin crossover.** The d$^6$ Tanabe-Sugano diagram is the most instructive for understanding spin crossover. At low $\Delta_o/B$, the ground state is the high-spin $^5T_{2g}$ term ($t_{2g}^4 e_g^2$, four unpaired electrons). As $\Delta_o/B$ increases, the low-spin $^1A_{1g}$ term ($t_{2g}^6 e_g^0$, zero unpaired electrons) drops below $^5T_{2g}$ at a critical value $\Delta_o/B \approx 20$ (for $C/B \approx 4.5$, typical of first-row metals). The diagram has a visible discontinuity at this crossover: the energy zero switches from $^5T_{2g}$ to $^1A_{1g}$, and all curves change slope. Complexes near this crossover — such as $[\mathrm{Fe}(\mathrm{phen})_2(\mathrm{NCS})_2]$ — can be thermally or optically switched between spin states, a phenomenon central to molecular spintronics.

**Hole formalism.** The Tanabe-Sugano diagram for d$^{n}$ in octahedral geometry is related to d$^{10-n}$ by the hole formalism: replacing each electron by a "hole" (vacancy) maps d$^2 \leftrightarrow$ d$^8$, d$^3 \leftrightarrow$ d$^7$, d$^4 \leftrightarrow$ d$^6$, with d$^5$ being self-dual. This is a consequence of electron-hole symmetry in the d-shell: the Coulomb repulsion and exchange terms have the same structure for electrons and holes. The hole formalism also connects octahedral d$^{n}$ to tetrahedral d$^{10-n}$: a d$^7$ ion in $T_d$ uses the d$^3$ octahedral Tanabe-Sugano diagram (with $\Delta_t$ substituted for $\Delta_o$).

## Orgel diagrams and the d$^1$/d$^9$ case [Master]

For d$^1$ and d$^9$ configurations, only one electron (or one hole) is present, so there is no inter-electron repulsion to complicate the energy levels. The **Orgel diagram** — a simpler precursor to the Tanabe-Sugano diagram — suffices. It plots the single-electron transition energy as a linear function of $\Delta$:

- d$^1$ in $O_h$: one transition $^2T_{2g} \to {}^2E_g$, energy $= \Delta_o$.
- d$^9$ in $O_h$: one transition $^2E_g \to {}^2T_{2g}$, energy $= \Delta_o$ (hole picture inverts the levels).
- d$^1$ in $T_d$: one transition $^2E \to {}^2T_2$, energy $= \Delta_t$.
- d$^9$ in $T_d$: one transition $^2T_2 \to {}^2E$, energy $= \Delta_t$.

The Orgel diagram is a straight line — no curvature, no configuration interaction, no term crossings. For d$^2$ and beyond, inter-electron repulsion introduces the nonlinear behaviour that necessitates the full Tanabe-Sugano treatment.

The d$^1$ case ($[\mathrm{Ti}(\mathrm{H_2O})_6]^{3+}$) was discussed in unit 16.03.02 as the simplest example of a d-d spectrum: a single band at $\Delta_o$, slightly broadened and asymmetric due to Jahn-Teller distortion of the excited state. The d$^9$ case ($[\mathrm{Cu}(\mathrm{H_2O})_6]^{2+}$) is the hole complement: also a single band, but strongly distorted by the permanent Jahn-Teller distortion in the ground state (unit 16.03.04 will develop this).

## Selection rules and transition intensities [Master]

The d-d transitions that produce the colours of transition-metal complexes are subject to two selection rules that determine their intensity.

**The Laporte (parity) selection rule.** In a centrosymmetric point group ($O_h$, $D_{4h}$), transitions between states of the same parity ($g \to g$ or $u \to u$) are forbidden. All d-d transitions are $g \to g$ and are therefore Laporte-forbidden. They gain intensity through:

1. **Vibronic coupling.** Asymmetric vibrations temporarily break the inversion centre, allowing the $g \to g$ transition to borrow intensity from an allowed ($g \to u$) charge-transfer transition. This is the dominant mechanism for centrosymmetric complexes.
2. **Static distortion.** Jahn-Teller-distorted complexes (Cu$^{2+}$, d$^9$) lack a strict inversion centre and have intrinsically more intense d-d bands.

Tetrahedral complexes ($T_d$) lack an inversion centre entirely, so the Laporte rule does not strictly apply. The d-d bands of tetrahedral complexes are typically 10–100 times more intense than their octahedral counterparts ($\epsilon \approx 100\text{--}1000$ vs $\epsilon \approx 10\text{--}100$ M$^{-1}$cm$^{-1}$).

**The spin selection rule** ($\Delta S = 0$). Transitions that change the total spin multiplicity are forbidden. Spin-allowed bands ($\Delta S = 0$) are orders of magnitude more intense than spin-forbidden ones. For d$^6$ low-spin ($^1A_{1g}$ ground state), all spin-allowed transitions go to singlet excited states; spin-forbidden transitions to triplet states are detectable but very weak.

The intensity ordering is:

| Transition type | $\epsilon$ (M$^{-1}$cm$^{-1}$) |
|---|---|
| Spin-allowed, Laporte-allowed (charge transfer) | $10^3$–$10^5$ |
| Spin-allowed, Laporte-forbidden, no inversion centre (tetrahedral d-d) | $10^2$–$10^3$ |
| Spin-allowed, Laporte-forbidden, centrosymmetric (octahedral d-d) | $10$–$10^2$ |
| Spin-forbidden (d-d) | $< 1$ |

## The nephelauxetic effect and quantitative fitting [Master]

The Racah parameter $B$ measured from a complex is always smaller than the free-ion value $B_0$. The ratio $\beta = B/B_0$ quantifies the **nephelauxetic effect** — the reduction of inter-electron repulsion due to covalent delocalisation of the d-electrons onto the ligand framework. This effect was introduced in unit 16.03.01; here we connect it to the Tanabe-Sugano fitting procedure.

When fitting observed band positions to a Tanabe-Sugano diagram, one extracts both $\Delta$ and $B$ simultaneously. The lowest spin-allowed transition gives $\Delta$ directly. The spacing between the higher transitions depends on $B$: a smaller $B$ compresses the upper levels. For $[\mathrm{Cr}(\mathrm{H_2O})_6]^{3+}$ (d$^3$), the three bands are at 17,400, 24,600, and 38,000 cm$^{-1}$. Fitting to the d$^3$ Tanabe-Sugano diagram gives $\Delta_o = 17{,}400$ cm$^{-1}$ and $B \approx 918$ cm$^{-1}$, compared to the free-ion $B_0 \approx 1030$ cm$^{-1}$ for Cr$^{3+}$. The nephelauxetic ratio $\beta = 0.89$ indicates modest covalent delocalisation with water ligands.

The nephelauxetic series for ligands (decreasing $\beta$) is:

$$
\mathrm{F}^- > \mathrm{H_2O} > \mathrm{NH_3} > \mathrm{en} > \mathrm{Cl}^- > \mathrm{CN}^- > \mathrm{Br}^- > \mathrm{I}^-.
$$

Ligands at the right produce greater d-electron delocalisation (more covalent bonding) and smaller $B$. The nephelauxetic series does not parallel the spectrochemical series exactly: CN$^-$ is the strongest-field ligand but is not the most nephelauxetic, because $\pi$-back-bonding (which lowers $t_{2g}$ and increases $\Delta_o$) is a different interaction from the overall delocalisation that reduces $B$.

The nephelauxetic effect also depends on the metal. Going down a group, $\beta$ decreases (more delocalisation) because 4d and 5d orbitals extend further and overlap more with ligand orbitals. For a given ligand, $\beta(5d) < \beta(4d) < \beta(3d)$.

Quantitative spectrum fitting for tetrahedral complexes follows the same procedure with the appropriate Tanabe-Sugano diagram (or, via the hole formalism, the octahedral d$^{10-n}$ diagram with $\Delta_t$ substituted). The smaller $\Delta_t$ means the transitions fall at lower energy (near-IR and visible rather than visible and UV), and the larger $\beta$ typical of the more covalent tetrahedral M-L bond compresses the upper levels further.

## Historical notes [Master]

The Tanabe-Sugano diagrams were published by Yukito Tanabe and Satoru Sugano in two back-to-back papers in the *Journal of the Physical Society of Japan* in 1954 ("On the absorption spectra of complex ions I and II," JPSJ **9**, 753–766 and 766–779) [ref: TODO_REF Miessler Ch. 8]. Their work systematised the energy-level problem for all d$^n$ configurations in octahedral symmetry, producing the set of seven diagrams (d$^2$ through d$^8$) that remain the standard reference in textbooks. The diagrams were computed by hand diagonalisation of the d-electron Hamiltonian at many values of $\Delta_o/B$ — a feat of patient calculation without modern computers.

The **Orgel diagrams** — simpler energy-level diagrams for d$^1$ and d$^9$ — were published by Leslie Orgel in 1955 as part of his broader programme to make ligand field theory accessible to chemists. Orgel's 1960 monograph *An Introduction to Transition-Metal Chemistry: Ligand-Field Theory* (Methuen) bridged the physics-oriented Bethe-Van Vleck crystal field theory with the chemical intuition of spectrochemical trends.

The **nephelauxetic effect** was identified and named by Claus Schäffer and Christian Klixbüll Jørgensen in the late 1950s. Jørgensen's systematic measurements of $\beta = B/B_0$ across hundreds of complexes established the nephelauxetic series and connected it to covalent delocalisation — providing one of the earliest quantitative probes of covalent bonding in coordination compounds.

The **spin-crossover** phenomenon was first reported by Cambi and Szego in 1931 for iron(III) dithiocarbamate complexes, but its full explanation in terms of the Tanabe-Sugano diagram crossover awaited the development of ligand field theory in the 1960s. Modern spin-crossover research, pioneered by Philipp Gütlich and others from the 1980s onward, exploits the bistability of d$^6$ Fe(II) complexes near the high-spin/low-spin crossover for molecular switches and sensors.

The **angular overlap model** (AOM) of Schäffer and Jørgensen (1965) provided a parameterisation scheme that transfers seamlessly across geometries — octahedral, tetrahedral, square-planar — using local metal-ligand overlap parameters ($e_\sigma$, $e_\pi$) rather than global $\Delta$ values. The AOM unified the diverse splitting patterns into a single framework and remains the workhorse for quantitative ligand-field analysis short of full DFT calculations.
