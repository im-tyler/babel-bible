## Intuition [Beginner]

Transition metals form coloured, magnetic compounds because their d-electrons are split into different energy levels by the surrounding ligands. Crystal field theory explains how this splitting works.

The five d-orbitals on a free metal ion all have the same energy — they are degenerate. When six ligands surround the metal in an octahedral arrangement (along the x, y, and z axes), two orbitals ($d_{z^2}$ and $d_{x^2-y^2}$) point directly at the ligands and feel strong electron-electron repulsion. The other three ($d_{xy}$, $d_{xz}$, $d_{yz}$) point between the ligands and feel less repulsion. The result: the five d-orbitals split into a higher pair ($e_g$) and a lower trio ($t_{2g}$), separated by the crystal field splitting energy $\Delta_o$.

The magnitude of $\Delta_o$ depends on the ligand. The **spectrochemical series** ranks ligands from weak-field (small $\Delta_o$, like I$^-$ and Br$^-$) to strong-field (large $\Delta_o$, like CN$^-$ and CO). Weak-field ligands tend to give high-spin complexes (many unpaired electrons); strong-field ligands give low-spin complexes (fewer unpaired electrons).

**Crystal field stabilisation energy (CFSE)** measures how much the d-electron configuration lowers the total energy relative to the spherically symmetric free ion. Each electron in the $t_{2g}$ set stabilises by $-0.4\Delta_o$; each in the $e_g$ set destabilises by $+0.6\Delta_o$. The total CFSE is the sum of all contributions.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**For [Fe(H$_2$O)$_6$]$^{2+}$ (high-spin d$^6$) vs [Fe(CN)$_6$]$^{4-}$ (low-spin d$^6$), calculate CFSE and predict magnetic moments.**

**[Fe(H$_2$O)$_6$]$^{2+}$.** Water is a weak-field ligand, $\Delta_o < P$ (pairing energy). High-spin filling: $t_{2g}^4 e_g^2$. Four electrons in $t_{2g}$ (three unpaired, one paired), two in $e_g$ (one each).

CFSE = $4 \times (-0.4\Delta_o) + 2 \times (+0.6\Delta_o) = -1.6\Delta_o + 1.2\Delta_o = -0.4\Delta_o$.

Unpaired electrons: 4 (one in each of $t_{2g}$ slots 2,3,4 and $e_g$ slots 1,2, minus the paired one). Actually: $t_{2g}^4 = \uparrow\downarrow, \uparrow, \uparrow$ (one pair, two singles) and $e_g^2 = \uparrow, \uparrow$. Total unpaired: 2 + 2 = **4**.

Magnetic moment (spin-only): $\mu = \sqrt{4(4+2)}\,\mu_B = \sqrt{24}\,\mu_B \approx 4.9\,\mu_B$.

**[Fe(CN)$_6$]$^{4-}$.** CN$^-$ is a strong-field ligand, $\Delta_o > P$. Low-spin filling: $t_{2g}^6 e_g^0$.

CFSE = $6 \times (-0.4\Delta_o) = -2.4\Delta_o$ (minus $2P$ for two extra pairings).

Unpaired electrons: 0 (all paired). **Diamagnetic.**

The same metal ion gives opposite magnetic behaviour depending on the ligand — one of the cleanest confirmations of crystal field theory.

**Additional worked example: d$^4$ high-spin vs low-spin.**

For d$^4$ in octahedral geometry, the high-spin configuration is $t_{2g}^3 e_g^1$ and the low-spin is $t_{2g}^4 e_g^0$.

High-spin: CFSE = $3(-0.4\Delta_o) + 1(+0.6\Delta_o) = -0.6\Delta_o$. Unpaired electrons: 4.

Low-spin: CFSE = $4(-0.4\Delta_o) = -1.6\Delta_o$, minus one pairing energy $P$ (the fourth electron pairs in $t_{2g}$ instead of going into $e_g$). Unpaired electrons: 2.

Low-spin is favoured when $-1.6\Delta_o - P < -0.6\Delta_o$, i.e., $\Delta_o > P$. Cr$^{2+}$ (d$^4$) is high-spin with water ligands ($\Delta_o \approx 14{,}000$ cm$^{-1}$, $P \approx 20{,}000$ cm$^{-1}$) but low-spin with CN$^-$ ($\Delta_o \approx 26{,}000$ cm$^{-1}$). This prediction is confirmed experimentally: [Cr(H$_2$O)$_6$]$^{2+}$ has four unpaired electrons ($\mu \approx 4.9\,\mu_B$) and is paramagnetic, while [Cr(CN)$_6$]$^{4-}$ has two unpaired electrons ($\mu \approx 2.8\,\mu_B$).

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Octahedral splitting.** The d-orbital basis decomposes under O$_h$ as $E_g \oplus T_{2g}$, with $E_g$ higher by $\Delta_o$. The barycentre rule: $E(E_g) = +\frac{3}{5}\Delta_o$ above the free-ion average, $E(T_{2g}) = -\frac{2}{5}\Delta_o$ below.

**Tetrahedral splitting.** Under T$_d$, the splitting inverts ($e$ below, $t_2$ above) and $\Delta_t = \frac{4}{9}\Delta_o$ for the same metal-ligand pair. Tetrahedral complexes are essentially always high-spin because $\Delta_t$ is small.

**CFSE formula.** For octahedral:

$$\text{CFSE} = n(t_{2g}) \times (-0.4\Delta_o) + n(e_g) \times (+0.6\Delta_o)$$

For pairing penalties, subtract $nP$ where $n$ is the number of electron pairs beyond Hund's minimum.

**Factors affecting $\Delta_o$:**

1. **Nature of the ligand.** The spectrochemical series orders ligands by the $\Delta_o$ they produce (see table above). Pi-acceptor ligands (CO, CN$^-$) give the largest $\Delta_o$; pi-donor ligands (I$^-$, Br$^-$) give the smallest.

2. **Charge on the metal.** Higher charge draws ligands closer, increasing the metal-ligand electrostatic interaction and raising $\Delta_o$. Compare: [Fe(H$_2$O)$_6$]$^{2+}$ ($\Delta_o \approx 10{,}400$ cm$^{-1}$) vs [Fe(H$_2$O)$_6$]$^{3+}$ ($\Delta_o \approx 14{,}000$ cm$^{-1}$).

3. **Principal quantum number.** $\Delta_o$ increases down a group because the 4d and 5d orbitals extend further from the nucleus and overlap more with the ligands. This is why second- and third-row transition metals are almost always low-spin: even weak-field ligands produce a $\Delta_o$ larger than the pairing energy.

**Spin-only magnetic moment:** $\mu_{s.o.} = \sqrt{n(n+2)}\,\mu_B$ where $n$ is the number of unpaired electrons.

**Spectrochemical series (selected):**

$$\text{I}^- < \text{Br}^- < \text{Cl}^- < \text{F}^- < \text{OH}^- < \text{H}_2\text{O} < \text{NH}_3 < \text{en} < \text{NO}_2^- < \text{CN}^- < \text{CO}$$

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **CFSE is not the only contribution to complex stability.** The Irving-Williams series ($\text{Mn}^{2+} < \text{Fe}^{2+} < \text{Co}^{2+} < \text{Ni}^{2+} < \text{Cu}^{2+} > \text{Zn}^{2+}$) reflects CFSE plus metal-specific effects like Jahn-Teller distortion.

- **The point-charge model underestimates $\Delta_o$.** The electrostatic model gives $\Delta_o$ values roughly an order of magnitude too small. Covalent bonding (ligand field theory) is needed for quantitative agreement.

The Jahn-Teller theorem predicts distortion for certain configurations. For d$^9$ in octahedral geometry, describe the expected distortion and its effect on the energy levels.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

## Beyond crystal field theory: ligand field theory overview [Master]

Crystal field theory treats ligands as point charges. It correctly predicts splitting patterns and qualitative trends but underestimates $\Delta_o$ by an order of magnitude. Ligand field theory (LFT) extends CFT by including covalent metal-ligand overlap:

- Sigma-donor ligands (NH$_3$, H$_2$O) interact only through sigma bonding, producing moderate $\Delta_o$.
- Pi-donor ligands (halides, OH$^-$) donate electron density into metal $t_{2g}$, raising it and reducing $\Delta_o$.
- Pi-acceptor ligands (CO, CN$^-$) accept metal $t_{2g}$ electron density via back-bonding, lowering $t_{2g}$ and increasing $\Delta_o$.

The spectrochemical series is explained by LFT: it tracks the pi-donor to pi-non-interacting to pi-acceptor progression. This is developed in detail in 16.03.02.

### Quantitative spectrochemical data

The table below gives representative $\Delta_o$ values (in cm$^{-1}$) for first-row transition-metal complexes, illustrating the dependence on both the metal and the ligand:

| [V(H$_2$O)$_6$]$^{3+}$ | d$^2$ | 18,500 |
| [Cr(H$_2$O)$_6$]$^{3+}$ | d$^3$ | 17,400 |
| [Cr(NH$_3$)$_6$]$^{3+}$ | d$^3$ | 21,500 |
| [Cr(CN)$_6$]$^{3-}$ | d$^3$ | 26,600 |
| [Co(H$_2$O)$_6$]$^{3+}$ | d$^6$ | 18,600 |
| [Co(NH$_3$)$_6$]$^{3+}$ | d$^6$ | 22,900 |
| [Co(CN)$_6$]$^{3-}$ | d$^6$ | 34,800 |

Three trends are immediately visible: (1) For the same metal and oxidation state, $\Delta_o$ increases across the spectrochemical series. (2) For the same ligand, $\Delta_o$ increases with oxidation state (compare Fe$^{2+}$ vs Fe$^{3+}$ with the same ligands). (3) For the same ligand and oxidation state, $\Delta_o$ increases going down a group (4d > 3d, 5d > 4d), which is why second- and third-row transition metals are essentially always low-spin.

### Tanabe-Sugano diagrams

A Tanabe-Sugano diagram plots the energy of each excited state (as $E/B$, where $B$ is the Racah parameter) against $\Delta_o/B$ for a given d$^n$ configuration. These diagrams are the primary tool for interpreting d-d absorption spectra:

- Each line represents a term ($^4T_{2g}$, $^4T_{1g}$, etc.) derived from the free-ion term after splitting in the O$_h$ field.
- The ground state is plotted as the horizontal axis ($E = 0$). For configurations with a spin crossover (d$^4$ through d$^7$), the diagram has a vertical line at the crossover $\Delta_o/B$ value where the ground state changes from high-spin to low-spin.
- The observed absorption bands correspond to vertical transitions from the ground state to excited states at the fixed $\Delta_o/B$ of the complex.

For d$^3$ (e.g., Cr$^{3+}$), the three spin-allowed transitions are $^4A_{2g} \to {}^4T_{2g}$, $^4A_{2g} \to {}^4T_{1g}(F)$, and $^4A_{2g} \to {}^4T_{1g}(P)$. The lowest-energy transition directly gives $\Delta_o$. The spacing between the two higher transitions gives the Racah parameter $B$, which measures the interelectronic repulsion (reduced from the free-ion value $B_0$ by covalent electron delocalisation — the **nephelauxetic effect**).

### The nephelauxetic effect

The Racah parameter $B$ measured in a complex is always smaller than the free-ion value $B_0$. This reduction ($\beta = B/B_0 < 1$) is called the nephelauxetic effect (Greek for "cloud-expanding"). Covalent bonding delocalises the d-electrons over a larger volume, reducing interelectronic repulsion. The nephelauxetic series ranks ligands by $\beta$: F$^-$ > H$_2$O > NH$_3$ > en > Cl$^-$ > CN$^-$ > Br$^-$ > I$^-$ (most covalent = smallest $\beta$).

### Selection rules and intensities

d-d transitions are formally **Laporte-forbidden** (same parity: $g \to g$ in O$_h$). They gain intensity only through vibronic coupling (vibrational distortion breaks the centre of symmetry) or through static distortion (e.g., Jahn-Teller). As a result, d-d bands are weak ($\epsilon \approx 10$–100 M$^{-1}$cm$^{-1}$) compared to charge-transfer bands ($\epsilon \approx 10^3$–$10^4$). Spin-forbidden transitions ($\Delta S \neq 0$) are even weaker ($\epsilon < 1$).

The spin selection rule ($\Delta S = 0$) means that only transitions between states of the same spin multiplicity are intense. The observed colour of transition-metal complexes arises from these weak, Laporte-forbidden, spin-allowed d-d bands. Charge-transfer bands (LMCT or MLCT) are intense but typically lie in the UV, outside the visible range.

### Crystal field theory for other geometries

**Square-planar geometry** (D$_{4h}$): Derived from the octahedral splitting by removing the two axial ligands. The $d_{z^2}$ drops significantly (no axial repulsion), and $d_{x^2-y^2}$ rises to become the highest orbital (maximum repulsion from the four in-plane ligands). The ordering is: $d_{x^2-y^2}$ >> $d_{xy}$ > $d_{z^2}$ > $d_{xz}, d_{yz}$. The very high energy of $d_{x^2-y^2}$ explains why d$^8$ metals (which leave this orbital empty) strongly prefer square-planar geometry. The splitting between $d_{x^2-y^2}$ and $d_{xy}$ is approximately $\Delta_1 \approx 1.3\Delta_o$ for a typical Pt(II) complex — larger than the original octahedral splitting because the in-plane ligands are brought closer when the axial ligands are removed.

**Tetragonal distortion** (Jahn-Teller): For unevenly occupied $e_g$ (d$^4$ high-spin, d$^9$), the octahedron distorts axially (elongation or compression). Axial elongation lowers $d_{z^2}$ and raises $d_{x^2-y^2}$, splitting the $e_g$ pair. The energy gain from the distortion is the Jahn-Teller stabilisation energy, typically 1–2 eV for d$^9$ Cu$^{2+}$.

**Square-based pyramidal and trigonal bipyramidal** geometries (CN = 5) have intermediate splitting patterns between octahedral and tetrahedral. These are encountered in five-coordinate intermediates during ligand substitution reactions at octahedral metal centres.

## Connections [Master]

- **Symmetry and group theory [16.02.01]** — the $E_g \oplus T_{2g}$ decomposition is derived from the O$_h$ character table using the machinery of character theory.

- **Periodic trends [16.01.01]** — the d-orbital electron configurations that determine CFSE are a direct consequence of the periodic trends in orbital filling.

- **Coordination chemistry [16.04.01]** — coordination numbers, geometries, and thermodynamic stabilities are partly governed by CFSE.

- **Organometallic chemistry [16.05.01]** — the 18-electron rule is the closed-shell criterion in the LFT MO framework.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Hans Bethe introduced crystal field theory in 1929 to explain spectral term splittings in crystals. Van Vleck extended it to magnetism in the 1930s. Orgel and Griffith developed ligand field theory in the 1950s, incorporating covalent bonding. Tanabe and Sugano produced their systematic energy diagrams in 1954. The theory remains the conceptual framework for understanding transition-metal chemistry, even though DFT calculations now handle the quantitative details.
