## Intuition [Beginner]

Solids are not just frozen liquids. Many solids have their atoms arranged in highly ordered, repeating patterns called **crystal lattices**. The arrangement of ions in these lattices — and the bonding between them — determines whether a solid is a hard insulator (NaCl), a soft conductor (copper), or a semiconductor (silicon).

**Ionic solids** (NaCl, CsCl, ZnS) are built from alternating positive and negative ions held together by electrostatic attraction. The arrangement maximises cation-anion contacts while minimising anion-anion and cation-cation repulsion. The structure type depends on the **radius ratio** — the ratio of the cation radius to the anion radius. Small cations fit into tetrahedral holes; larger cations need octahedral holes; very large cations need cubic holes.

**Metallic solids** have a lattice of positive metal ions surrounded by a delocalised "sea" of electrons. The electrons are not bound to any particular atom but move freely throughout the lattice, which is why metals conduct electricity.

**Band theory** explains conductivity by looking at the energy levels in a solid. In an isolated atom, electrons occupy discrete energy levels. In a solid with $10^{23}$ atoms, these levels merge into continuous **bands**. The highest occupied band is the **valence band**; the next band up is the **conduction band**. The gap between them determines the material's electrical behaviour:

- **Metal**: valence and conduction bands overlap. Electrons move freely. Good conductor.
- **Semiconductor**: small band gap (0.1–3 eV). Electrons can be thermally excited across the gap. Conductivity increases with temperature.
- **Insulator**: large band gap (>3 eV). Electrons cannot reach the conduction band at ordinary temperatures.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Calculate the packing efficiency of the NaCl structure (coordination number 6).**

In the NaCl structure, the ions touch along the edge of the unit cell. The unit cell parameter is $a = 2(r_+ + r_-)$, where $r_+$ is the cation radius and $r_-$ is the anion radius.

The NaCl unit cell contains 4 formula units of NaCl (4 Na$^+$ and 4 Cl$^-$). The volume occupied by the ions is:

$$V_{ions} = 4 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r_+^3 + 4 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r_-^3$$

The unit cell volume is $V_{cell} = a^3 = [2(r_+ + r_-)]^3 = 8(r_+ + r_-)^3$.

The packing efficiency is:

$$\text{PE} = \frac{V_{ions}}{V_{cell}} = \frac{(16\pi/3)(r_+^3 + r_-^3)}{8(r_+ + r_-)^3}$$

For NaCl: $r_+ = 102\,\mathrm{pm}$ (Na$^+$), $r_- = 181\,\mathrm{pm}$ (Cl$^-$).

$V_{ions} = (16\pi/3)(102^3 + 181^3) \times 10^{-36}\,\mathrm{m}^3 = (16\pi/3)(1{,}061{,}208 + 5{,}929{,}741) \times 10^{-36} = (16\pi/3)(6{,}990{,}949) \times 10^{-36}$.

$V_{cell} = 8(102 + 181)^3 \times 10^{-36} = 8(283)^3 \times 10^{-36} = 8 \times 22{,}665{,}187 \times 10^{-36} = 181{,}321{,}496 \times 10^{-36}$.

$\text{PE} = 16\pi \times 6{,}990{,}949 / (3 \times 181{,}321{,}496) = 16\pi \times 6{,}990{,}949 / 543{,}964{,}488 = 0.643 = 64.3\%$.

About 64% of the volume is occupied by ions; the remaining 36% is empty space.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise (medium, short-answer).**

Explain why silicon doped with phosphorus (n-type) has higher conductivity than pure silicon.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Phosphorus has one more valence electron than silicon. Where does the extra electron go?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Silicon has four valence electrons. Phosphorus has five. When a P atom substitutes for a Si atom in the lattice, four of its electrons form bonds with neighbouring Si atoms, but the fifth electron is unbound — it occupies a **donor level** just below the conduction band (about 0.045 eV below it for P in Si). This electron is easily thermally excited into the conduction band, providing charge carriers (electrons) without requiring excitation across the full 1.1 eV band gap.

The result is n-type semiconductor (n for negative charge carriers). The conductivity increases because the doping introduces additional charge carriers at an energy level that requires only a small thermal kick to enter the conduction band.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise (medium, short-answer).**

Describe the CsCl structure and explain why it has a higher coordination number than NaCl.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Cs$^+$ is much larger than Na$^+$. What does a larger cation allow?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The CsCl structure has Cs$^+$ at the centre of a cube with Cl$^-$ at each of the eight corners (or vice versa). The coordination number is 8 (each ion has eight nearest neighbours of opposite charge).

Cs$^+$ is much larger than Na$^+$ (167 pm vs 102 pm), giving a larger radius ratio ($r_+/r_- = 167/181 = 0.92$). The radius ratio rules predict: ratio 0.414–0.732 favours octahedral coordination (NaCl type, CN = 6), and ratio > 0.732 favours cubic coordination (CsCl type, CN = 8). The larger cation fills a bigger cavity (the cubic hole at the centre of the unit cell) and makes more cation-anion contacts.

</details>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Crystal structures.** A crystal is a periodic array of atoms described by a lattice and a basis. The lattice is defined by three basis vectors $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}$, and the atomic positions are $\mathbf{R} = n_1\mathbf{a} + n_2\mathbf{b} + n_3\mathbf{c} + \mathbf{d}_j$ where $n_i$ are integers and $\mathbf{d}_j$ are the basis atom positions.

**Common structure types:**

| Structure | CN | Description |
|---|---|---|
| NaCl (rock salt) | 6:6 | FCC anions, all octahedral holes filled |
| CsCl | 8:8 | Simple cubic, one ion at body centre |
| ZnS (zinc blende) | 4:4 | FCC anions, half tetrahedral holes filled |
| CaF$_2$ (fluorite) | 8:4 | FCC cations, all tetrahedral holes filled |
| Diamond | 4 | FCC with half tetrahedral holes, same atom |

**Radius ratio rules.** The structure type is predicted by $r_+/r_-$:

| $r_+/r_-$ range | Hole type | Predicted structure |
|---|---|---|
| < 0.225 | Linear | Few stable ionic structures |
| 0.225–0.414 | Tetrahedral | ZnS type (CN = 4) |
| 0.414–0.732 | Octahedral | NaCl type (CN = 6) |
| > 0.732 | Cubic | CsCl type (CN = 8) |

**Band theory.** In a solid of N atoms, each atomic orbital broadens into a band containing N states. The **valence band** is the highest occupied band at T = 0 K. The **conduction band** is the lowest unoccupied band. The **band gap** $E_g$ is the energy difference between the top of the valence band and the bottom of the conduction band.

- Metal: $E_g = 0$ (bands overlap or partially filled).
- Semiconductor: $0 < E_g < \approx 3$ eV.
- Insulator: $E_g > \approx 3$ eV.

**Defects.** Real crystals are not perfect. **Point defects** include:

- Vacancy: an atom missing from a lattice site.
- Interstitial: an extra atom in a non-lattice site.
- Frenkel defect: vacancy + interstitial of the same species (conserved atom count).
- Schottky defect: paired cation and anion vacancies (conserved stoichiometry).

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **The radius ratio rules are approximate.** Many real structures deviate from the predictions because covalent bonding contributions, polarisability, and lattice energy also influence the structure type.

- **Band gap is not the same as the HOMO-LUMO gap of a single molecule.** Bands are collective properties of $10^{23}$ atoms; the gap is a bulk property, not a molecular one.

- **Metals are not just "electron sea" materials.** The band picture is more precise: metals have partially filled bands where the Fermi level sits within a band, allowing electrons near the Fermi level to accelerate under an applied field.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Proposition (Packing efficiency of the NaCl structure).** *The packing efficiency of the NaCl structure, assuming hard-sphere ions, is $\text{PE} = \frac{\pi}{3}\cdot\frac{r_+^3 + r_-^3}{(r_+ + r_-)^3}$ where $r_+$ and $r_-$ are the cation and anion radii.*

**Proof.** The NaCl unit cell has lattice parameter $a = 2(r_+ + r_-)$. The cell contains 4 Na$^+$ (1 at corner contributions: $8 \times 1/8 = 1$ at centre of edges, etc. Actually: 4 Na$^+$ from edge-centre positions and 1 from body centre: $12 \times 1/4 + 1 \times 1 = 4$; similarly 4 Cl$^-$ from face-centre positions: $6 \times 1/2 + 8 \times 1/8 = 4$) and 4 Cl$^-$.

Volume of ions: $V_{ions} = 4 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r_+^3 + 4 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r_-^3 = \frac{16\pi}{3}(r_+^3 + r_-^3)$.

Volume of cell: $V_{cell} = a^3 = 8(r_+ + r_-)^3$.

$\text{PE} = \frac{V_{ions}}{V_{cell}} = \frac{16\pi(r_+^3 + r_-^3)}{3 \times 8(r_+ + r_-)^3} = \frac{2\pi(r_+^3 + r_-^3)}{3(r_+ + r_-)^3}$.

For equal-sized spheres ($r_+ = r_-$): $\text{PE} = \frac{2\pi \cdot 2r^3}{3 \cdot 8r^3} = \frac{4\pi}{24} = \frac{\pi}{6} \approx 52.4\%$. $\blacksquare$

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, short-answer).**

Predict the structure type for KCl, given $r$(K$^+$) = 138 pm and $r$(Cl$^-$) = 181 pm.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Calculate $r_+/r_-$ and consult the radius ratio rules.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$r_+/r_- = 138/181 = 0.762$. This is above 0.732, predicting the **CsCl structure** (CN = 8).

However, KCl actually crystallises in the NaCl structure at room temperature (CN = 6). The radius ratio rules are approximate and can fail when the ratio is near a boundary. The NaCl structure is adopted because the lattice energy is slightly more favourable for the NaCl arrangement at standard conditions. The CsCl structure appears only at higher temperatures or pressures for KCl.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (medium, short-answer).**

Calculate the packing efficiency for the CsCl structure assuming equal-sized spheres ($r_+ = r_- = r$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The CsCl unit cell has one Cs$^+$ at the body centre and 8 Cl$^-$ at the corners. The body diagonal = $2(r_+ + r_-) = 4r$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For equal spheres, the body diagonal = $4r$ (two radii from each ion touching). The lattice parameter $a = 4r/\sqrt{3}$.

The cell contains 1 Cs$^+$ (body centre) and 1 Cl$^-$ ($8 \times 1/8 = 1$ from corners). Total ions: 2.

$V_{ions} = 2 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 = \frac{8\pi r^3}{3}$.

$V_{cell} = a^3 = (4r/\sqrt{3})^3 = 64r^3/(3\sqrt{3})$.

$\text{PE} = \frac{8\pi r^3/3}{64r^3/(3\sqrt{3})} = \frac{8\pi}{3} \times \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{64} = \frac{8\pi\sqrt{3}}{64} = \frac{\pi\sqrt{3}}{8} \approx 0.680 = 68.0\%$.

The CsCl structure has higher packing efficiency than NaCl (52.4% for equal spheres) because the larger cation fits the bigger cubic hole.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, short-answer).**

Explain why the conductivity of a semiconductor increases with temperature, while the conductivity of a metal decreases.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Semiconductors: more charge carriers at higher T. Metals: more scattering at higher T.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Semiconductor:** The conductivity $\sigma = ne\mu$ depends on the carrier concentration $n$ and the mobility $\mu$. As temperature increases, more electrons gain enough thermal energy to cross the band gap from the valence band to the conduction band. The carrier concentration $n$ increases exponentially with temperature ($n \propto \exp(-E_g/2k_BT)$), causing conductivity to increase sharply.

**Metal:** Metals already have a large number of charge carriers (the conduction band is partially filled). The carrier concentration is essentially constant with temperature. However, increasing temperature causes the metal ions to vibrate more vigorously, increasing the scattering of electrons by the lattice (phonon scattering). This reduces the electron mobility $\mu$, causing conductivity to decrease. The temperature coefficient of resistivity is positive for metals and negative for semiconductors.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (hard, short-answer).**

A Schottky defect in NaCl consists of one Na$^+$ vacancy and one Cl$^-$ vacancy. Derive the expression for the equilibrium concentration of Schottky defects as a function of temperature, and estimate the fraction of vacant sites at 1000 K given a defect formation energy of 2.3 eV.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The equilibrium defect concentration minimises the Gibbs free energy. For Schottky defects: $n_s/N \approx \exp(-\Delta H_s / 2k_BT)$ where $n_s$ is the number of defect pairs and N is the number of ion pairs.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For Schottky defects, the equilibrium concentration is derived by minimising the Gibbs free energy $G = H - TS$ with respect to the number of defect pairs $n_s$. The enthalpy cost is $n_s \Delta H_s$ (the formation enthalpy per defect pair). The configurational entropy gain is $\Delta S = k_B \ln \Omega$ where $\Omega$ is the number of ways to distribute $n_s$ cation vacancies among $N$ cation sites and $n_s$ anion vacancies among $N$ anion sites.

Minimising $G$ with respect to $n_s$ gives:

$$\frac{n_s}{N} \approx \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta H_s}{2k_BT}\right)$$

At T = 1000 K: $k_BT = 8.617 \times 10^{-5} \times 1000 = 0.0862\,\mathrm{eV}$.

$n_s/N = \exp(-2.3/(2 \times 0.0862)) = \exp(-13.3) = 1.7 \times 10^{-6}$.

About 2 parts per million of sites are vacant at 1000 K. This is small but significant — these vacancies are responsible for ionic conductivity in NaCl at high temperatures, because ions can hop into adjacent vacancies.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, short-answer).**

Describe the zinc blende (ZnS) structure and explain why it is adopted by many compound semiconductors (GaAs, InP, CdTe) rather than the NaCl structure.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

ZnS has CN = 4 (tetrahedral). What does this imply about the bonding?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The zinc blende structure has an FCC lattice of S$^{2-}$ (or As$^{3-}$, etc.) with Zn$^{2+}$ (or Ga$^{3+}$, etc.) occupying half of the tetrahedral holes. The coordination number is 4 (tetrahedral), and each ion has four bonds directed toward the corners of a tetrahedron.

Compound semiconductors (GaAs, InP, CdTe) adopt this structure rather than NaCl because their bonding has a significant **covalent** component. Tetrahedral coordination is the natural geometry for sp$^3$ hybridised bonds — the same hybridisation that gives diamond and silicon their tetrahedral structure. The directional nature of covalent bonds favours the tetrahedral arrangement over the isotropic octahedral arrangement of the NaCl structure.

The degree of covalency correlates with the electronegativity difference between the two elements. GaAs (small $\Delta\chi$) is highly covalent and adopts zinc blende. NaCl (large $\Delta\chi$) is highly ionic and adopts the NaCl structure. Intermediate compounds (e.g., CdS) can adopt either structure depending on conditions.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Explain the concept of a **Frenkel defect** and compare it with a Schottky defect. Under what conditions does each dominate?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A Frenkel defect is a vacancy-interstitial pair. A Schottky defect is a pair of vacancies. Which type preserves charge neutrality? Which is more likely for small, highly charged ions?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Frenkel defect:** An atom leaves its lattice site and moves to an interstitial position, creating a vacancy-interstitial pair. The atom count is preserved. Frenkel defects are favoured when one type of ion is small enough to fit into interstitial sites — typically the cation. Example: AgCl, where Ag$^+$ is small enough to occupy interstitial sites, and Frenkel defects on the Ag sublattice dominate.

**Schottky defect:** A pair of vacancies (one cation, one anion) is created, maintaining charge neutrality but reducing the total atom count. Schottky defects are favoured when both ions are similar in size and neither fits easily into interstitial sites. Example: NaCl, where both ions are relatively large and Schottky defects dominate.

The key factors: (1) the relative sizes of the ions (small cations favour Frenkel); (2) the charge on the ions (highly charged ions are reluctant to occupy interstitial sites because of the electrostatic penalty, favouring Schottky); (3) the openness of the structure (structures with large interstitial spaces favour Frenkel). In practice, AgCl and AgBr are Frenkel-dominated, while NaCl and KCl are Schottky-dominated.

</details>

</aside>

## Beyond simple ionic models: covalent networks and extended solids [Master]

Diamond, silicon, and germanium crystallise in the diamond cubic structure: each atom is tetrahedrally bonded to four neighbours by covalent bonds, forming an extended three-dimensional network. The structure is an FCC lattice with a two-atom basis. The packing efficiency is only 34% (much lower than ionic structures), but the strong directional covalent bonds make these materials extremely hard (diamond) or semiconducting (Si, Ge).

Graphite, by contrast, has layered covalent bonding within the planes (hexagonal sheets of sp$^2$ carbon) and weak van der Waals interactions between the layers. This anisotropy gives graphite its lubricating properties (layers slide over each other) and its electrical conductivity (delocalised pi-electrons within the plane).

Fullerenes (C$_{60}$, carbon nanotubes) and graphene (single graphite sheets) represent intermediate structures between molecular and extended solids. Their properties arise from the interplay of curvature, dimensionality, and conjugation in the carbon network.

## Connections [Master]

- **Periodic trends [16.01.01]** — ionic radii determine structure types through radius ratio rules; electronegativity differences determine the degree of ionic vs covalent character.

- **Crystal field theory [16.03.01]** — the d-orbital splitting in transition-metal oxides and other solids is a solid-state extension of molecular crystal field theory.

- **Molecular orbital theory [14.05.01]** — band theory is the large-N limit of MO theory: the discrete molecular orbitals of a molecule become continuous bands in a solid.

- **Organometallic chemistry [16.05.01]** — extended metal-metal bonding in solids (metallic bonding) is related to the multi-centre bonding in metal clusters.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

The X-ray diffraction method (Bragg, 1913, Nobel Prize) enabled the determination of crystal structures. The concept of the crystal lattice was formalised by Bravais in 1850. Band theory was developed by Bloch, Wilson, and others in the late 1920s and 1930s, building on the newly established quantum mechanics. The semiconductor revolution (Shockley, Bardeen, Brattain, Nobel Prize 1956) transformed solid-state chemistry from an academic discipline into the foundation of modern electronics.

The philosophical point: a solid is not a collection of independent molecules. The properties of a crystal — its conductivity, transparency, hardness, melting point — emerge from the collective behaviour of $10^{23}$ atoms arranged in a periodic lattice. Band theory captures this collective physics, but the reduction from atomic properties to bulk behaviour is not straightforward and genuinely emergent.
