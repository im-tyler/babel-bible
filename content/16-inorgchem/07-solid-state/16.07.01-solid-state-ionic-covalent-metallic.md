## Intuition [Beginner]

Solids are not just frozen liquids. Many solids have their atoms arranged in highly ordered, repeating patterns called **crystal lattices**. The arrangement of ions in these lattices — and the bonding between them — determines whether a solid is a hard insulator (NaCl), a soft conductor (copper), or a semiconductor (silicon).

**Ionic solids** (NaCl, CsCl, ZnS) are built from alternating positive and negative ions held together by electrostatic attraction. The arrangement maximises cation-anion contacts while minimising anion-anion and cation-cation repulsion. The structure type depends on the **radius ratio** — the ratio of the cation radius to the anion radius. Small cations fit into tetrahedral holes; larger cations need octahedral holes; very large cations need cubic holes.

**Metallic solids** have a lattice of positive metal ions surrounded by a delocalised "sea" of electrons. The electrons are not bound to any particular atom but move freely throughout the lattice, which is why metals conduct electricity.

**Covalent network solids** (diamond, silicon) have atoms connected by shared-electron bonds in all three dimensions. Every atom is locked in place by strong directional bonds, making diamond the hardest natural material. Graphite, also carbon, uses a different bonding pattern — strong bonds within each sheet but weak forces between sheets — making it soft enough to write with.

## Visual [Beginner]

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Calculate the packing efficiency of the NaCl structure (coordination number 6).**

In the NaCl structure, the ions touch along the edge of the unit cell. The unit cell parameter is $a = 2(r_+ + r_-)$, where $r_+$ is the cation radius and $r_-$ is the anion radius.

The NaCl unit cell contains 4 formula units of NaCl (4 Na$^+$ and 4 Cl$^-$). The volume occupied by the ions is:

$$V_{ions} = 4 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r_+^3 + 4 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r_-^3$$

The unit cell volume is $V_{cell} = a^3 = [2(r_+ + r_-)]^3 = 8(r_+ + r_-)^3$.

The packing efficiency is:

$$\text{PE} = \frac{V_{ions}}{V_{cell}} = \frac{(16\pi/3)(r_+^3 + r_-^3)}{8(r_+ + r_-)^3}$$

For NaCl: $r_+ = 102\,\mathrm{pm}$ (Na$^+$), $r_- = 181\,\mathrm{{pm}}$ (Cl$^-$).

$V_{ions} = (16\pi/3)(102^3 + 181^3) \times 10^{-36}\,\mathrm{m}^3 = (16\pi/3)(1{,}061{,}208 + 5{,}929{,}741) \times 10^{-36} = (16\pi/3)(6{,}990{,}949) \times 10^{-36}$.

$V_{cell} = 8(102 + 181)^3 \times 10^{-36} = 8(283)^3 \times 10^{-36} = 8 \times 22{,}665{,}187 \times 10^{-36} = 181{,}321{,}496 \times 10^{-36}$.

$\text{PE} = 16\pi \times 6{,}990{,}949 / (3 \times 181{,}321{,}496) = 16\pi \times 6{,}990{,}949 / 543{,}964{,}488 = 0.643 = 64.3\%$.

About 64% of the volume is occupied by ions; the remaining 36% is empty space.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Crystal structures.** A crystal is a periodic array of atoms described by a lattice and a basis. The lattice is defined by three basis vectors $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}$, and the atomic positions are $\mathbf{R} = n_1\mathbf{a} + n_2\mathbf{b} + n_3\mathbf{c} + \mathbf{d}_j$ where $n_i$ are integers and $\mathbf{d}_j$ are the basis atom positions.

**The 14 Bravais lattices** classify all distinct three-dimensional periodic lattices into crystal systems (cubic, tetragonal, orthorhombic, hexagonal, trigonal, monoclinic, triclinic) with centring types (primitive, body-centred, face-centred, base-centred). The cubic system has three Bravais lattices: primitive (P), body-centred (I), and face-centred (F). Most common inorganic structures use cubic or hexagonal lattices.

**Common structure types:**

| Structure | CN | Description |
|---|---|---|
| NaCl (rock salt) | 6:6 | FCC anions, all octahedral holes filled |
| CsCl | 8:8 | Simple cubic, one ion at body centre |
| ZnS (zinc blende) | 4:4 | FCC anions, half tetrahedral holes filled |
| CaF$_2$ (fluorite) | 8:4 | FCC cations, all tetrahedral holes filled |
| Diamond | 4 | FCC with half tetrahedral holes, same atom |

**Radius ratio rules.** The structure type is predicted by $r_+/r_-$:

| $r_+/r_-$ range | Hole type | Predicted structure |
|---|---|---|
| < 0.225 | Linear | Few stable ionic structures |
| 0.225–0.414 | Tetrahedral | ZnS type (CN = 4) |
| 0.414–0.732 | Octahedral | NaCl type (CN = 6) |
| > 0.732 | Cubic | CsCl type (CN = 8) |

**Madelung constants and lattice energy.** The lattice energy of an ionic crystal is the sum of all Coulomb interactions. The **Madelung constant** $M$ captures the geometry-dependent part:

$$U_{lattice} = -\frac{N_A M z_+ z_- e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r_0}\left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)$$

where $z_+, z_-$ are ion charges, $r_0$ is the nearest-neighbour distance, $n$ is the Born exponent (typically 8–12), and $M$ is the Madelung constant. For NaCl, $M = 1.7476$; for CsCl, $M = 1.7627$; for ZnS (zinc blende), $M = 1.6381$. The Madelung constant is always larger for higher-coordination structures, but the total lattice energy also depends on $r_0$ — a subtle balance that the radius-ratio rules approximate.

**Band theory.** In a solid of N atoms, each atomic orbital broadens into a band containing N states. The **valence band** is the highest occupied band at T = 0 K. The **conduction band** is the lowest unoccupied band. The **band gap** $E_g$ is the energy difference between the top of the valence band and the bottom of the conduction band.

- Metal: $E_g = 0$ (bands overlap or partially filled).
- Semiconductor: $0 < E_g < \approx 3$ eV.
- Insulator: $E_g > \approx 3$ eV.

**Defects.** Real crystals are not perfect. **Point defects** include:

- Vacancy: an atom missing from a lattice site.
- Interstitial: an extra atom in a non-lattice site.
- Frenkel defect: vacancy + interstitial of the same species (conserved atom count).
- Schottky defect: paired cation and anion vacancies (conserved stoichiometry).

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **The radius ratio rules are approximate.** Many real structures deviate from the predictions because covalent bonding contributions, polarisability, and lattice energy also influence the structure type. KCl ($r_+/r_- = 0.76$) should adopt CsCl structure by the rules but crystallises in NaCl structure at room temperature.

- **Band gap is not the same as the HOMO-LUMO gap of a single molecule.** Bands are collective properties of $10^{23}$ atoms; the gap is a bulk property, not a molecular one. The HOMO-LUMO gap of an isolated NaCl molecule tells you nothing about the band gap of NaCl crystal.

- **Metals are not just "electron sea" materials.** The band picture is more precise: metals have partially filled bands where the Fermi level sits within a band, allowing electrons near the Fermi level to accelerate under an applied field. The "sea" metaphor captures the delocalisation but not the band-structure mechanism of conduction.

- **Close-packed does not mean densest overall.** FCC and HCP are the densest arrangements of *equal* spheres (74%), but binary structures with two ion sizes can achieve higher packing efficiencies. NaCl achieves 67.6% packing for its actual ion sizes, which exceeds the 52.4% of equal-sphere simple cubic.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Proposition (Packing efficiency of the NaCl structure).** *The packing efficiency of the NaCl structure, assuming hard-sphere ions, is $\text{PE} = \frac{\pi}{3}\cdot\frac{r_+^3 + r_-^3}{(r_+ + r_-)^3}$ where $r_+$ and $r_-$ are the cation and anion radii.*

**Proof.** The NaCl unit cell has lattice parameter $a = 2(r_+ + r_-)$. The cell contains 4 Na$^+$ (from edge-centre positions: $12 \times 1/4 + 1 \times 1 = 4$) and 4 Cl$^-$ (from face-centre and corner positions: $6 \times 1/2 + 8 \times 1/8 = 4$).

Volume of ions: $V_{ions} = 4 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r_+^3 + 4 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r_-^3 = \frac{16\pi}{3}(r_+^3 + r_-^3)$.

Volume of cell: $V_{cell} = a^3 = 8(r_+ + r_-)^3$.

$\text{PE} = \frac{V_{ions}}{V_{cell}} = \frac{16\pi(r_+^3 + r_-^3)}{3 \times 8(r_+ + r_-)^3} = \frac{2\pi(r_+^3 + r_-^3)}{3(r_+ + r_-)^3}$.

For equal-sized spheres ($r_+ = r_-$): $\text{PE} = \frac{2\pi \cdot 2r^3}{3 \cdot 8r^3} = \frac{4\pi}{24} = \frac{\pi}{6} \approx 52.4\%$. $\blacksquare$

**Bridge.** This packing-efficiency formula builds toward the Madelung-constant treatment of lattice energies in the Advanced results section below, and appears again in [12.17.01] (condensed-matter physics) where the connection between packing geometry and elastic moduli is developed. The foundational reason packing efficiency matters is that ionic radii determine both the crystal structure and the interionic distances that enter the Coulomb sum; this is exactly the input the Madelung calculation requires. The bridge is between the geometric problem of sphere packing and the energetic problem of lattice stabilisation.

**Exercise 3 (medium, short-answer).**

Calculate the packing efficiency for the CsCl structure assuming equal-sized spheres ($r_+ = r_- = r$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The CsCl unit cell has one Cs$^+$ at the body centre and 8 Cl$^-$ at the corners. The body diagonal = $2(r_+ + r_-) = 4r$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For equal spheres, the body diagonal = $4r$ (two radii from each ion touching). The lattice parameter $a = 4r/\sqrt{3}$.

The cell contains 1 Cs$^+$ (body centre) and 1 Cl$^-$ ($8 \times 1/8 = 1$ from corners). Total ions: 2.

$V_{ions} = 2 \times \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 = \frac{8\pi r^3}{3}$.

$V_{cell} = a^3 = (4r/\sqrt{3})^3 = 64r^3/(3\sqrt{3})$.

$\text{PE} = \frac{8\pi r^3/3}{64r^3/(3\sqrt{3})} = \frac{8\pi}{3} \times \frac{3\sqrt{3}}{64} = \frac{8\pi\sqrt{3}}{64} = \frac{\pi\sqrt{3}}{8} \approx 0.680 = 68.0\%$.

The CsCl structure has higher packing efficiency than NaCl (52.4% for equal spheres) because the larger cation fills the bigger cubic hole.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Explain why the conductivity of a semiconductor increases with temperature, while the conductivity of a metal decreases.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Semiconductors: more charge carriers at higher T. Metals: more scattering at higher T.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Semiconductor:** The conductivity $\sigma = ne\mu$ depends on the carrier concentration $n$ and the mobility $\mu$. As temperature increases, more electrons gain enough thermal energy to cross the band gap from the valence band to the conduction band. The carrier concentration $n$ increases exponentially with temperature ($n \propto \exp(-E_g/2k_BT)$), causing conductivity to increase sharply.

**Metal:** Metals already have a large number of charge carriers (the conduction band is partially filled). The carrier concentration is essentially constant with temperature. However, increasing temperature causes the metal ions to vibrate more vigorously, increasing the scattering of electrons by the lattice (phonon scattering). This reduces the electron mobility $\mu$, causing conductivity to decrease. The temperature coefficient of resistivity is positive for metals and negative for semiconductors.

</details>

</aside>

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

A Schottky defect in NaCl consists of one Na$^+$ vacancy and one Cl$^-$ vacancy. Derive the expression for the equilibrium concentration of Schottky defects as a function of temperature, and estimate the fraction of vacant sites at 1000 K given a defect formation energy of 2.3 eV.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The equilibrium defect concentration minimises the Gibbs free energy. For Schottky defects: $n_s/N \approx \exp(-\Delta H_s / 2k_BT)$ where $n_s$ is the number of defect pairs and N is the number of ion pairs.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For Schottky defects, the equilibrium concentration is derived by minimising the Gibbs free energy $G = H - TS$ with respect to the number of defect pairs $n_s$. The enthalpy cost is $n_s \Delta H_s$ (the formation enthalpy per defect pair). The configurational entropy gain is $\Delta S = k_B \ln \Omega$ where $\Omega$ is the number of ways to distribute $n_s$ cation vacancies among $N$ cation sites and $n_s$ anion vacancies among $N$ anion sites.

Minimising $G$ with respect to $n_s$ gives:

$$\frac{n_s}{N} \approx \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta H_s}{2k_BT}\right)$$

At T = 1000 K: $k_BT = 8.617 \times 10^{-5} \times 1000 = 0.0862\,\mathrm{eV}$.

$n_s/N = \exp(-2.3/(2 \times 0.0862)) = \exp(-13.3) = 1.7 \times 10^{-6}$.

About 2 parts per million of sites are vacant at 1000 K. This is small but significant — these vacancies are responsible for ionic conductivity in NaCl at high temperatures, because ions can hop into adjacent vacancies.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Describe the zinc blende (ZnS) structure and explain why it is adopted by many compound semiconductors (GaAs, InP, CdTe) rather than the NaCl structure.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

ZnS has CN = 4 (tetrahedral). What does this imply about the bonding?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The zinc blende structure has an FCC lattice of S$^{2-}$ (or As$^{3-}$, etc.) with Zn$^{2+}$ (or Ga$^{3+}$, etc.) occupying half of the tetrahedral holes. The coordination number is 4 (tetrahedral), and each ion has four bonds directed toward the corners of a tetrahedron.

Compound semiconductors (GaAs, InP, CdTe) adopt this structure rather than NaCl because their bonding has a significant **covalent** component. Tetrahedral coordination is the natural geometry for sp$^3$ hybridised bonds — the same hybridisation that gives diamond and silicon their tetrahedral structure. The directional nature of covalent bonds favours the tetrahedral arrangement over the isotropic octahedral arrangement of the NaCl structure.

The degree of covalency correlates with the electronegativity difference between the two elements. GaAs (small electronegativity difference) is highly covalent and adopts zinc blende. NaCl (large electronegativity difference) is highly ionic and adopts the NaCl structure. Intermediate compounds (e.g., CdS) can adopt either structure depending on conditions.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Explain the concept of a **Frenkel defect** and compare it with a Schottky defect. Under what conditions does each dominate?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A Frenkel defect is a vacancy-interstitial pair. A Schottky defect is a pair of vacancies. Which type preserves charge neutrality? Which is more likely for small, highly charged ions?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Frenkel defect:** An atom leaves its lattice site and moves to an interstitial position, creating a vacancy-interstitial pair. The atom count is preserved. Frenkel defects are favoured when one type of ion is small enough to fit into interstitial sites — typically the cation. Example: AgCl, where Ag$^+$ is small enough to occupy interstitial sites, and Frenkel defects on the Ag sublattice dominate.

**Schottky defect:** A pair of vacancies (one cation, one anion) is created, maintaining charge neutrality but reducing the total atom count. Schottky defects are favoured when both ions are similar in size and neither fits easily into interstitial sites. Example: NaCl, where both ions are relatively large and Schottky defects dominate.

The key factors: (1) the relative sizes of the ions (small cations favour Frenkel); (2) the charge on the ions (highly charged ions are reluctant to occupy interstitial sites because of the electrostatic penalty, favouring Schottky); (3) the openness of the structure (structures with large interstitial spaces favour Frenkel). In practice, AgCl and AgBr are Frenkel-dominated, while NaCl and KCl are Schottky-dominated.

## The Bloch theorem and the origin of band structure [Master]

The quantitative foundation of band theory is the **Bloch theorem**, proved by Felix Bloch in 1928 [ref: TODO_REF Bloch 1928]. In a periodic potential $V(\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{R}) = V(\mathbf{r})$ for all lattice vectors $\mathbf{R}$, every one-electron wavefunction can be written as

$$\psi_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r}) = e^{i\mathbf{k}\cdot\mathbf{r}}\,u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r})$$

where $u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r})$ has the periodicity of the lattice ($u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{R}) = u_{n\mathbf{k}}(\mathbf{r})$), $\mathbf{k}$ is the crystal wave vector in the first Brillouin zone, and $n$ is the band index. The energy eigenvalues $E_n(\mathbf{k})$ form continuous **bands** as $\mathbf{k}$ varies across the Brillouin zone.

The Bloch theorem is the solid-state counterpart of the molecular-orbital construction for molecules. For a diatomic molecule, two atomic orbitals combine into a bonding and an antibonding molecular orbital — two discrete levels. For a crystal of $N$ atoms on a one-dimensional lattice with spacing $a$, the analogous construction yields a band of $N$ states spanning wave vectors $k = -\pi/a$ to $+\pi/a$. The bandwidth — the energy range spanned by the band — depends on the overlap between neighbouring orbitals: large overlap gives wide bands (metals), small overlap gives narrow bands (insulators). When $N \sim 10^{23}$ the states within a band are so closely spaced that the band is effectively continuous.

The **nearly-free electron model** treats the conduction electrons as free particles perturbed by a weak periodic potential. The free-electron parabola $E = \hbar^2 k^2 / 2m$ is modified at the Brillouin-zone boundaries ($k = \pm \pi/a$, $k = \pm 2\pi/a$, etc.), where Bragg reflection opens energy gaps. The band gap at the first zone boundary is proportional to the Fourier component of the lattice potential at the corresponding reciprocal-lattice vector. This model captures the essential physics: periodicity produces gaps, and the size of the gap determines whether the material is a metal, semiconductor, or insulator.

The **tight-binding model** takes the opposite limit: electrons are tightly bound to atoms, and inter-atomic overlap is treated as a perturbation. The band dispersion for a simple s-band on a one-dimensional chain with nearest-neighbour overlap integral $\gamma$ and on-site energy $\epsilon$ is

$$E(k) = \epsilon - 2\gamma\cos(ka)$$

The bandwidth is $4\gamma$, directly proportional to the overlap. The tight-binding picture is the natural one for d-electrons in transition-metal oxides, where narrow bands and strong electron correlations give rise to Mott insulators, magnetism, and unconventional superconductivity.

The **reciprocal lattice** and **Brillouin zone** provide the geometric framework for describing band structure. For a direct lattice with basis vectors $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}$, the reciprocal lattice vectors are $\mathbf{a}^* = 2\pi(\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})/V_{cell}$, $\mathbf{b}^* = 2\pi(\mathbf{c} \times \mathbf{a})/V_{cell}$, $\mathbf{c}^* = 2\pi(\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b})/V_{cell}$, where $V_{cell} = \mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})$. The first Brillouin zone is the Wigner-Seitz cell of the reciprocal lattice — the set of points closer to the origin than to any other reciprocal lattice point. All distinct electronic states are labelled by wave vectors within the first Brillouin zone; states outside it are related by a reciprocal-lattice translation and represent the same physical state.

The connection between reciprocal space and X-ray diffraction is the **Laue condition**: diffraction occurs when the scattering vector equals a reciprocal lattice vector $\mathbf{G}$, i.e., $\mathbf{k}' - \mathbf{k} = \mathbf{G}$. This condition is equivalent to Bragg's law ($n\lambda = 2d\sin\theta$) but expressed in the reciprocal-space language that naturally describes both diffraction and band structure. The same reciprocal lattice that indexes X-ray diffraction spots also indexes the Bloch wave vectors — the geometric bridge between crystallography and electronic structure.

## Madelung constants, lattice energies, and the Born-Lande framework [Master]

The lattice energy of an ionic crystal is the enthalpy change when one mole of solid is formed from its gaseous ions. It is a central quantitative prediction of the ionic model, and its calculation requires summing the Coulomb interaction of every ion with every other ion in the crystal.

For a crystal with ions of charges $z_+$ and $z_-$ at nearest-neighbour distance $r_0$, the Coulomb contribution per formula unit is

$$U_{Coulomb} = -\frac{M z_+ z_- e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r_0}$$

where the **Madelung constant** $M$ is a purely geometric sum:

$$M = \sum_j \frac{(\pm 1)}{p_j}$$

Here $p_j = r_j / r_0$ is the distance to ion $j$ in units of the nearest-neighbour distance, and the sign is positive for unlike charges and negative for like charges. The sum is conditionally convergent — its value depends on the order of summation, and physical convergence requires a spherical or shell-by-shell summation that preserves charge neutrality at each stage. For NaCl, the Ewald summation technique gives $M = 1.74756...$. For CsCl, $M = 1.76267...$. For the zinc blende structure, $M = 1.63806...$.

The full **Born-Lande equation** adds a short-range repulsive term of the form $B/r^n$ (where $n$ is the Born exponent, typically 8–12 for different ion types):

$$U_{lattice} = -\frac{N_A M z_+ z_- e^2}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r_0}\left(1 - \frac{1}{n}\right)$$

The Born exponent $n$ is determined from compressibility data: stiffer ions (small, high charge) have larger $n$. For NaCl, $n \approx 8$, giving $U_{lattice} = -787$ kJ/mol, in good agreement with the experimental value from the Born-Haber cycle.

The **Born-Haber cycle** provides an independent experimental route to the lattice energy via Hess's law:

$$U_{lattice} = \Delta H_f - \Delta H_{sub} - \text{IE} - \frac{1}{2}\Delta H_{diss} - \text{EA}$$

where $\Delta H_f$ is the enthalpy of formation, $\Delta H_{sub}$ is the sublimation enthalpy of the metal, IE is the ionisation energy, $\Delta H_{diss}$ is the bond dissociation enthalpy of the non-metal, and EA is the electron affinity. Agreement between the Born-Lande prediction and the Born-Haber measurement to within 1–3% validates the ionic model for strongly ionic compounds like NaCl and KCl. Discrepancies larger than 5% signal significant covalent character (e.g., AgCl, where the Born-Lande prediction overestimates the lattice energy because the model ignores covalent contributions to bonding).

**The Kapustinskii equation** provides a useful approximation that eliminates the need to know the specific crystal structure:

$$U_{lattice} \approx \frac{-1200.5 \nu |z_+||z_-|}{r_+ + r_-}\left(1 - \frac{0.345}{r_+ + r_-}\right) \quad \text{kJ/mol}$$

where $\nu$ is the number of ions per formula unit (2 for NaCl, 3 for CaF$_2$) and $r_+, r_-$ are the ionic radii in Angstroms. The Kapustinskii equation reproduces the Born-Lande energies to within 5% for most ionic compounds, making it a practical tool for estimating lattice energies of hypothetical or poorly characterised compounds. The equation works because the Madelung constant varies relatively little across common structure types (1.64 to 1.76), and the Kapustinskii equation effectively uses an averaged value. It is particularly valuable for predicting the stability of unknown compounds: if the Born-Haber cycle with the Kapustinskii estimate gives a positive formation enthalpy, the compound is thermodynamically unstable with respect to its elements. Conversely, a large negative lattice energy from the Kapustinskii equation predicts a stable compound even before synthesis is attempted — a predictive power that has been confirmed for numerous ionic solids synthesised after the equation was first proposed by Anatoli Kapustinskii in 1956.

## Band theory in detail: metals, semiconductors, and insulators [Master]

The classification of solids by their electronic band structure rests on three quantitative quantities: the band gap $E_g$, the density of states at the Fermi level $g(E_F)$, and the effective mass $m^*$ of charge carriers.

**Metals** have a partially filled band or overlapping bands. The **Fermi level** $E_F$ lies within a band, and the density of states at $E_F$ is non-zero. Electrons at the Fermi level can be accelerated by an arbitrarily small electric field, giving metallic conduction. The free-electron model predicts the electronic specific heat $C_e = \gamma T$ where $\gamma \propto g(E_F)$, and the Sommerfeld model gives the correct numerical prefactor. The Wiedemann-Franz law relates thermal conductivity to electrical conductivity through the Lorenz number $L = \kappa/(\sigma T) = \pi^2 k_B^2 / (3e^2)$, a direct consequence of the fact that both heat and charge are carried by the same electrons.

**Semiconductors** have a filled valence band separated from an empty conduction band by a gap $E_g$ in the range 0.1–3 eV. At $T = 0$ K the material is an insulator; at finite temperature, thermal excitation promotes a fraction $\sim \exp(-E_g / 2k_BT)$ of electrons across the gap. The intrinsic carrier concentration in a semiconductor is

$$n_i = \sqrt{N_c N_v}\,\exp\!\left(-\frac{E_g}{2k_BT}\right)$$

where $N_c$ and $N_v$ are the effective density of states in the conduction and valence bands, proportional to $(m^* k_B T)^{3/2}$. For silicon at room temperature ($E_g = 1.12$ eV, $m^*_e \approx 1.08\,m_e$, $m^*_h \approx 0.56\,m_e$), $n_i \approx 1.5 \times 10^{10}$ cm$^{-3}$ — vastly smaller than the carrier concentration in a metal ($\sim 10^{22}$ cm$^{-3}$).

**Doping** controls the carrier concentration in semiconductors over many orders of magnitude. Substituting a group-V atom (phosphorus) for a group-IV atom (silicon) introduces a donor level about 0.045 eV below the conduction band. At room temperature, thermal energy ionises essentially all donors, giving $n \approx N_D$ (the donor concentration). For $N_D = 10^{17}$ cm$^{-3}$, the conductivity increases by a factor of $10^7$ relative to intrinsic silicon. The doping framework — n-type (donors, electron majority carriers) and p-type (acceptors, hole majority carriers) — is the basis of semiconductor device physics.

**Insulators** have $E_g > 3$ eV. Diamond ($E_g = 5.5$ eV), NaCl ($E_g \approx 8.5$ eV), and SiO$_2$ ($E_g \approx 9$ eV) are insulators. The intrinsic carrier concentration at room temperature is vanishingly small ($\sim 10^{-30}$ cm$^{-3}$ for diamond), and no practical level of doping can produce metallic conduction because the donor or acceptor levels are too deep within the gap. The distinction between semiconductor and insulator is one of degree, not kind: both have band gaps, but the gap is small enough for thermal activation in semiconductors and too large in insulators.

**The density of states** $g(E)$ counts the number of electronic states per unit energy per unit volume. For a free electron in three dimensions, $g(E) \propto \sqrt{E}$, giving the characteristic parabolic density of states that rises from the bottom of the band. The integrated density of states up to the Fermi energy equals the electron density. In metals, $g(E_F)$ is large and the electronic specific heat is $C_e = (\pi^2/3)k_B^2 T \cdot g(E_F)$ — linear in temperature, in contrast to the $T^3$ lattice specific heat that dominates at lower temperatures. The ratio of the measured electronic specific heat to the free-electron prediction defines the **effective mass enhancement** $m^*/m_e$, which reveals the strength of electron-electron and electron-phonon interactions beyond the independent-electron picture.

**Optical properties** of solids are determined by interband transitions. When a photon with energy $h\nu > E_g$ is absorbed, an electron is promoted from the valence band to the conduction band. The absorption coefficient $\alpha$ rises steeply above the band-edge energy, and the functional form of the onset — direct gap ($\alpha \propto \sqrt{h\nu - E_g}$) versus indirect gap ($\alpha \propto (h\nu - E_g)^2$, involving a phonon for momentum conservation) — is a diagnostic for the band structure. Silicon has an indirect gap of 1.12 eV (the valence-band maximum and conduction-band minimum occur at different k-points), while GaAs has a direct gap of 1.42 eV (both extrema at the same k-point), which is why GaAs is used for LEDs and laser diodes while silicon is not.

## Defect thermodynamics and ionic conductivity [Master]

Point defects in ionic crystals are not merely imperfections — they are thermodynamically required. At any temperature above absolute zero, the Gibbs free energy $G = H - TS$ is minimised by creating a finite concentration of defects, because the enthalpy cost of creating the defect is offset by the configurational entropy gained by distributing defect sites among the available lattice positions.

For Schottky defects in a crystal with $N$ ion pairs and $n_s$ defect pairs, the configurational entropy is

$$\Delta S_{config} = k_B \ln\!\left[\frac{N!}{(N-n_s)!\,n_s!}\right]^2 \approx 2k_B\left[N\ln N - (N-n_s)\ln(N-n_s) - n_s\ln n_s\right]$$

Minimising $G = n_s \Delta H_s - T\Delta S_{config}$ with respect to $n_s$ yields the equilibrium concentration:

$$\frac{n_s}{N} = \exp\!\left(-\frac{\Delta H_s}{2k_BT}\right)$$

The factor of 2 in the exponent arises because each Schottky defect creates two vacancies (one cation, one anion). The analogous expression for Frenkel defects is $n_f/N = \exp(-\Delta H_f / 2k_BT)$, where $\Delta H_f$ is the Frenkel formation enthalpy and the factor of 2 reflects the two-site (vacancy + interstitial) nature of the defect.

**Ionic conductivity** arises from the migration of ions via vacancies or interstitial sites. The conductivity is

$$\sigma = \frac{n_d z^2 e^2 a^2 \nu_0}{k_BT}\exp\!\left(-\frac{E_m}{k_BT}\right)$$

where $n_d$ is the defect concentration, $a$ is the jump distance, $\nu_0$ is the attempt frequency ($\sim 10^{13}$ Hz, the phonon frequency), and $E_m$ is the migration energy barrier. Combining the defect concentration and the migration activation energy gives the total activation energy for ionic conduction:

$$E_a = \frac{\Delta H_s}{2} + E_m$$

For NaCl, $\Delta H_s = 2.3$ eV, $E_m = 0.65$ eV, giving $E_a = 1.8$ eV. The measured conductivity of NaCl increases from $\sim 10^{-14}$ S/cm at 400 K to $\sim 10^{-4}$ S/cm at 800 K, following the Arrhenius law with this activation energy.

**Fast ion conductors** (solid electrolytes) exploit structures with intrinsically high defect concentrations or low migration barriers. The archetype is AgI above 147 degrees C (the $\alpha$-AgI phase), where the iodide sublattice is bcc and the silver ions occupy a disordered subset of 42 possible sites per unit cell. The silver-ion conductivity reaches 1.3 S/cm — comparable to liquid electrolytes. Other fast ion conductors include Na-$\beta$-alumina (Na$^+$ conductor, used in sodium-sulfur batteries), YSZ (yttria-stabilised zirconia, O$^{2-}$ conductor, used in solid-oxide fuel cells), and LiPON (lithium phosphorus oxynitride, Li$^+$ conductor, used in thin-film batteries). The design principle is a rigid anion framework with interconnected channels through which the mobile cation can hop with low migration barriers.

**Non-stoichiometry** is another consequence of defect thermodynamics. Many transition-metal oxides exist over a range of compositions: Fe$_{1-x}$O (wustite, $0.05 < x < 0.15$), TiO$_{2-x}$ ($0 < x < 0.01$), and UO$_{2+x}$ ($0 < x < 0.25$). In Fe$_{1-x}$O, the dominant defects are cation vacancies compensated by Fe$^{3+}$ ions (two Fe$^{3+}$ per vacancy, maintaining charge neutrality). The non-stoichiometric composition arises because the free energy of the crystal is minimised at a point where the enthalpy cost of creating defects is balanced by the entropy gain — the same thermodynamic argument as for intrinsic defects, but now driven by the external oxygen partial pressure. The equilibrium oxygen content depends on temperature and $p_{O_2}$ through the point-defect chemistry, and the electrical conductivity of the oxide can change by orders of magnitude as the defect concentration varies with the ambient atmosphere — the basis of semiconductor gas sensors (SnO$_2$ for CO detection, ZnO for hydrogen sensing).

**Colour centres** (F-centres, from the German *Farbzentrum*) are point defects that trap electrons and produce characteristic optical absorption. An F-centre in NaCl is an electron trapped at an anion vacancy. The trapped electron occupies a hydrogen-like orbital bound by the electrostatic potential of the surrounding cations, and the optical transition energy falls in the visible range — giving otherwise colourless NaCl a yellow-brown colour when irradiated. The absorption wavelength shifts systematically with the lattice parameter: $\lambda \propto a^2$ for alkali halides, as expected for a particle-in-a-box model where the box size is set by the vacancy radius. F-centres are the simplest example of defect spectroscopy and connect solid-state chemistry to quantum mechanics through the particle-in-a-box treatment of the trapped electron.

## Covalent network solids and extended structures [Master]

Diamond, silicon, and germanium crystallise in the diamond cubic structure: each atom is tetrahedrally bonded to four neighbours by covalent bonds, forming an extended three-dimensional network. The structure is an FCC lattice with a two-atom basis. The packing efficiency is only 34% (much lower than ionic structures), but the strong directional covalent bonds make these materials extremely hard (diamond) or semiconducting (Si, Ge).

The diamond structure can be understood as two interpenetrating FCC lattices offset by $(1/4, 1/4, 1/4)a$. Each atom forms four sp$^3$-hybridised bonds, and every bond angle is the tetrahedral angle 109.47 degrees. The unit cell contains 8 atoms. The large void fraction (66%) accommodates the directional bonding geometry but leaves substantial empty space, which is why silicon can accommodate dopant atoms substitutionally — the lattice is spacious enough at the atomic level.

Graphite, by contrast, has layered covalent bonding within the planes (hexagonal sheets of sp$^2$ carbon) and weak van der Waals interactions between the layers. This anisotropy gives graphite its lubricating properties (layers slide over each other) and its electrical conductivity (delocalised pi-electrons within the plane). The in-plane C-C distance is 142 pm (shorter than diamond's 154 pm, reflecting the partial double-bond character of the sp$^2$ bonds), while the interlayer spacing is 335 pm.

Fullerenes (C$_{60}$), carbon nanotubes, and graphene represent intermediate structures between molecular and extended solids. Their properties arise from the interplay of curvature, dimensionality, and conjugation in the carbon network. Graphene, a single graphite sheet, is a zero-gap semiconductor (or semimetal) with a linear dispersion relation $E = \pm\hbar v_F |k|$ near the Dirac points, giving electrons an effective speed $v_F \approx 10^6$ m/s and zero effective mass. This unusual dispersion — a consequence of the honeycomb lattice symmetry — produces the half-integer quantum Hall effect at room temperature and underpins the entire field of two-dimensional materials.

The connection between bonding type and band structure is direct. In diamond, the sp$^3$ bonds produce a large splitting between the bonding (valence) and antibonding (conduction) bands, giving $E_g = 5.5$ eV. In silicon, the larger atomic size and longer bond length reduce the overlap integral, narrowing the gap to 1.12 eV. In tin, the trend continues: $\alpha$-Sn (diamond structure) is a semiconductor with $E_g = 0.08$ eV, and $\beta$-Sn (metallic structure) is a metal. The crossover from insulator to semiconductor to metal as you go down Group 14 is a direct manifestation of the relationship between orbital overlap, bandwidth, and band gap.

**Silicates and other extended anion structures** represent a different class of covalent network solids. In silicate minerals, the SiO$_4$ tetrahedron serves as the fundamental building block. These tetrahedra can link through shared oxygen corners to form isolated ions (orthosilicates, e.g., olivine Mg$_2$SiO$_4$), chains (pyroxenes, e.g., diopside CaMgSi$_2$O$_6$), double chains (amphiboles), sheets (micas and clays), or full three-dimensional frameworks (quartz SiO$_2$, feldspars). The degree of polymerisation correlates with the Si:O ratio and determines the physical properties: isolated tetrahedra give dense, high-melting minerals, while sheet silicates are soft and cleave easily along the sheet planes. The aluminium substitution for silicon (charge-balanced by additional cations) generates the enormous variety of aluminosilicate minerals that make up the bulk of the Earth's crust. These structures are not ionic in the simple NaCl sense — the Si-O bond has substantial covalent character, and the directional tetrahedral geometry constrains the possible structures far more than the radius-ratio rules for ionic compounds.

**Metastable polymorphs and phase transitions** are common in covalent and partially covalent solids. Carbon adopts diamond, graphite, fullerenes, nanotubes, and amorphous forms depending on the conditions of formation. SiO$_2$ has $\alpha$-quartz, $\beta$-quartz, tridymite, cristobalite, coesite, and stishovite (where silicon is in octahedral rather than tetrahedral coordination, formed at high pressure). ZrO$_2$ exists in monoclinic, tetragonal, and cubic polymorphs; the tetragonal-to-monoclinic martensitic transformation is exploited in transformation-toughened zirconia ceramics. The phase behaviour of these solids reflects the competition between directional covalent bonding preferences and the higher coordination favoured by denser packing at high pressure.

## Connections [Master]

- **Periodic trends quantified [16.01.01].** Ionic radii determine structure types through the radius-ratio rules, and electronegativity differences govern the degree of ionic versus covalent character in the bonding. The periodic trends across the s-block, p-block, and d-block predict which structure types (NaCl, CsCl, ZnS, NiAs) each binary compound will adopt, and the Madelung-constant framework quantifies the lattice-energy consequences of those choices.

- **Molecular orbital theory [14.05.01].** Band theory is the large-N limit of MO theory: the discrete molecular orbitals of a molecule become continuous bands in a solid, and the HOMO-LUMO gap of a molecule corresponds to the band gap of a semiconductor. The tight-binding model used in solid-state physics is mathematically identical to the Huckel MO method used for pi-systems in organic chemistry, differing only in the lattice periodicity of the solid. This is exactly the bridge between the molecular-orbital framework and the collective electronic properties of crystals.

- **Crystal field theory [16.03.01].** The d-orbital splitting in transition-metal oxides and other solids is a solid-state extension of molecular crystal field theory. In a perovskite like SrTiO$_3$, the Ti$^{4+}$ ion sits in an octahedral oxygen cage that splits the d-orbitals into $t_{2g}$ and $e_g$ sets, and the bandwidth of these d-bands controls whether the material is a metal (NaWO$_3$), a semiconductor (SrTiO$_3$), or a Mott insulator (LaTiO$_3$) despite having the same crystal structure and the same nominal d-electron count.

- **Condensed-matter physics [12.17.01].** The physics-side extension of this unit develops the Bloch theorem, reciprocal-lattice construction, Fermi surfaces, and semiconductor device physics at full mathematical rigour. This chemistry unit provides the crystal-structure vocabulary and the qualitative band-theory framework that the physics treatment builds on; the bridge is that the same crystal structures appear in both, with the physics adding dynamical and transport calculations.

- **Metalloenzymes and bioinorganic chemistry [16.06.01].** Metal centres in metalloenzymes are characterised by X-ray diffraction of protein crystals, a technique that depends on the same Bragg-diffraction physics introduced here for inorganic crystal-structure determination. The crystallographic symmetry concepts, unit cells, and space groups used in protein crystallography are a direct extension of the solid-state crystallography developed in this unit.

- **Organometallic chemistry [16.05.01].** Extended metal-metal bonding in metallic solids (the delocalised band picture) is related to the multi-centre bonding in metal clusters. The same orbital-overlap arguments that rationalise the bonding in Mn$_2$(CO)$_{10}$ extend to the metallic bond in bulk manganese, differing in degree of delocalisation but not in kind.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

X-ray crystallography originated with the 1913 paper of W. H. Bragg and W. L. Bragg [ref: TODO_REF Bragg 1913], who showed that X-rays diffracted by crystals produce patterns from which atomic positions can be reconstructed. The Braggs determined the structures of NaCl, KCl, diamond, and CaF$_2$ in a single year, founding both solid-state chemistry and the broader field of structural science. W. L. Bragg received the Nobel Prize in 1915 at age 25, the youngest laureate in history. The concept of the crystal lattice itself had been formalised earlier by Auguste Bravais in 1850, who classified the 14 distinct lattice types in three dimensions from purely mathematical considerations — decades before any experimental technique could verify them.

Band theory was developed by Felix Bloch in 1928 [ref: TODO_REF Bloch 1928], who proved that electron wavefunctions in a periodic potential take the form of plane waves modulated by lattice-periodic functions — the Bloch theorem. Alan Herries Wilson in 1931 used Bloch's result to distinguish metals from insulators by the filling of energy bands, and introduced the concept of the band gap as the defining property of semiconductors. The nearly-free electron model and the tight-binding model were both developed in the late 1920s and 1930s, with the Kronig-Penney model (1931) providing an exactly solvable one-dimensional illustration. The semiconductor revolution followed: William Shockley, John Bardeen, and Walter Brattain invented the transistor at Bell Labs in 1947, receiving the Nobel Prize in 1956, and transformed solid-state chemistry from an academic discipline into the foundation of modern electronics.

The Born-Lande framework for ionic lattice energies was developed by Max Born and Alfred Lande in 1918, building on the Madelung constant introduced by Erwin Madelung in 1909. The Ewald summation technique (Paul Ewald, 1921) solved the conditional-convergence problem of the Madelung sum, enabling accurate lattice-energy calculations that remain in use today. The Born-Haber cycle, developed by Max Born and Fritz Haber in 1919, connected the theoretical lattice energy to experimental thermochemical data, providing the first quantitative validation of the ionic model.

The philosophical point about solids is that they are not collections of independent molecules. The properties of a crystal — conductivity, transparency, hardness, melting point — emerge from the collective behaviour of $10^{23}$ atoms arranged in a periodic lattice. Band theory captures this collective physics, but the reduction from atomic properties to bulk behaviour is not straightforward. A single silicon atom has discrete energy levels; a silicon crystal has bands. The band gap is a property of the lattice, not of any individual atom or bond, and this is what makes solid-state chemistry a distinct discipline rather than an application of molecular chemistry to large aggregates.
