## Intuition [Beginner]

A photon is happy to share a state with another photon; a million photons piling into the same laser mode is the physics of coherent light. An electron is not. Two electrons in a helium atom cannot occupy the same single-electron orbital with the same spin. The deepest reason any two atoms in the universe do not collapse into a featureless point is that electrons refuse to share single-particle states. This refusal is the **Pauli exclusion principle**, proposed by Wolfgang Pauli in 1925 to explain the structure of the periodic table.

The previous unit built the bosonic Fock space — the home of states like photons, where occupation numbers can be any non-negative integer. The fermionic Fock space is its antisymmetric cousin, the home of electrons, protons, neutrons, quarks, and every other half-integer-spin particle. The same skeleton applies: there is a vacuum, there are creation operators that add a particle and annihilation operators that remove one, and there is a one-particle Hilbert space whose multi-particle promotion is the full Fock space.

The single change of rule is dramatic. Swapping the labels on two bosons leaves the state alone; swapping the labels on two fermions multiplies the state by $-1$. Putting two fermions in the same single-particle state then gives a state equal to its own negative, which has to be the zero vector. No two fermions can occupy the same one-particle state. This is exclusion, packaged as a sign.

The algebra of creation and annihilation operators changes correspondingly. For bosons the commutator $a a^* - a^* a$ was the central identity. For fermions the **anticommutator** $a a^* + a^* a$ takes its place. A pair of creation operators for the same one-particle state squares to zero, which is again exclusion expressed in algebra. Three sentences — antisymmetry, anticommutators, and zero squares — generate the entire structure of fermionic many-body quantum mechanics and underwrite the stability of every chunk of matter in the universe.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the same tower of shelves as the bosonic case, but with a new rule painted on the wall: each shelf has its own occupancy cap, and any *single* one-particle state can appear on the cap at most once. The vacuum is the empty bottom shelf. The one-particle space is the shelf above it. The two-particle space is the shelf above *that*, and it is now built from pairs of distinct one-particle states — never two copies of the same state.

![A diagram of a fermionic Fock space as a tower of antisymmetric tensor powers of the one-particle Hilbert space, with the vacuum at the bottom, the one-particle space above it, and the antisymmetric two-particle space above that with a banner saying "each one-particle state appears at most once". A creation operator arrow climbs upward, and the same arrow applied twice to the same state collapses to the zero vector at a "no entry" symbol.](/img/12.03.02-fermionic-fock-space-pauli-exclusion-placeholder.svg)

Now refine the picture. Each shelf is the **antisymmetric** $n$-fold tensor power $\Lambda^n \mathcal{H}$ of the one-particle Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$ — the subspace of $n$-fold tensors that change sign under any swap of two slots. The fermionic Fock space is the Hilbert direct sum of all these shelves, with the inner product summing contributions from each level.

The creation operator $a^*(f)$ wedges the one-particle state $f$ onto the existing antisymmetric product. If $f$ is already on the shelf, the wedge product collapses to zero — exclusion, as a built-in feature of the wedge. The annihilation operator $a(f)$ removes an $f$-occupant and produces a state on the shelf below. Strings of distinct creation operators applied to the vacuum span the entire fermionic Fock space, just as in the bosonic case, except that the order of the operators now matters up to a sign.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider helium: two electrons orbiting a nucleus of charge $+2e$. The one-particle Hilbert space is the orbital state space, augmented by a two-dimensional spin space — so each one-particle slot has both a spatial label (which orbital) and a spin label ($\uparrow$ or $\downarrow$). For the ground state, both electrons sit in the lowest-energy $1s$ orbital. The Pauli exclusion principle forbids them from also sharing a spin label. They must have opposite spins.

Label the two electrons by their slot, $1$ and $2$. The antisymmetric two-electron ground-state wavefunction is

$$
\Psi_{1s^2}(1, 2) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\,\phi_{1s}(\mathbf{r}_1)\,\phi_{1s}(\mathbf{r}_2)\bigl(|\!\uparrow\downarrow\rangle - |\!\downarrow\uparrow\rangle\bigr),
$$

a product of the symmetric two-electron spatial part (both electrons in $\phi_{1s}$) and the antisymmetric spin-singlet $|\!\uparrow\downarrow\rangle - |\!\downarrow\uparrow\rangle$. The combination is overall antisymmetric under exchange of the two electrons, as required. Numerically, $\phi_{1s}(\mathbf{r}) = (Z^3/\pi a_0^3)^{1/2} e^{-Z r/a_0}$ with $Z = 2$ for helium; the spin singlet is the unique antisymmetric combination of two spin-$1/2$ vectors.

Try instead to put both electrons in $|1s\!\uparrow\rangle$. The antisymmetric two-particle state would be the slot-swap difference $|1s\!\uparrow\rangle_1 |1s\!\uparrow\rangle_2 - |1s\!\uparrow\rangle_2 |1s\!\uparrow\rangle_1$, which is the zero vector. The wavefunction vanishes identically. Two electrons in the same orbital with the same spin is not a possible physical state — exclusion, in numbers. Lithium therefore has one $1s$ electron forced into the next available shell ($2s$) and grows up into the third row of the periodic table; aufbau is the systematic application of this single rule shell by shell.

**Exercise (easy, multiple choice).**

In a fermionic Fock space, applying the same creation operator $a^*(f)$ twice to any state produces which result?

A. A two-particle state with two copies of $f$  
B. The vacuum state  
C. The zero vector  
D. A state with twice the norm of the original

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A wedge product $f \wedge f$ is the antisymmetric combination of $f$ with itself.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. The zero vector.**

The fermionic creation operators satisfy $a^*(f)^2 = 0$ for every one-particle state $f$. This is the algebraic content of the Pauli exclusion principle: two fermions cannot occupy the same one-particle state, so the attempt to create such a configuration produces nothing. The norm-squared of the result is zero, not "twice" anything. Option A would be a bosonic answer (and even there the coefficient $\sqrt{2}$ would scale the result, not produce a literal duplication).

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise (easy, multiple choice).**

The anticommutator $\{A, B\} := AB + BA$ replaces the commutator in the fermionic algebra. For fermionic creation and annihilation operators of orthogonal one-particle states $f \perp g$, which identity holds?

A. $\{a(f), a^*(g)\} = 0$  
B. $\{a(f), a^*(g)\} = 1$  
C. $\{a(f), a^*(g)\} = -1$  
D. $\{a(f), a^*(g)\}$ is generally non-zero

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The CAR reads $\{a(f), a^*(g)\} = \langle f, g\rangle$. When $f \perp g$, the inner product is zero.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**A. $\{a(f), a^*(g)\} = 0$.**

The canonical anticommutation relation says $\{a(f), a^*(g)\} = \langle f, g\rangle$. When the two one-particle states are orthogonal, the inner product on the right vanishes, and so does the anticommutator. Different one-particle modes have anticommuting creation and annihilation operators — fermions in distinct orbitals respect each other's slots up to a sign, which is the algebraic shadow of antisymmetry. Option D would describe non-orthogonal modes; A is the special case where the inner product happens to be zero.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise (easy, true/false).**

In a fermionic Fock space, the occupation number $n_k$ for any single one-particle mode $k$ can take only the values $0$ or $1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each one-particle state appears at most once in any antisymmetric product.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.**

The Pauli exclusion principle says each one-particle mode can be either empty ($n_k = 0$) or occupied by exactly one fermion ($n_k = 1$). Attempting to set $n_k \geq 2$ produces the zero vector via $a^*(e_k)^2 = 0$. This is the fundamental contrast with the bosonic case, where each $n_k$ ranges over all non-negative integers without restriction. The total particle number adds up the occupations over modes and is still unbounded — many distinct modes can each hold one fermion — but each individual mode is binary.

</details>

**Exercise (medium, numeric).**

For an orthonormal basis $\{e_1, e_2, e_3\}$ of a one-particle Hilbert space $\mathbb{C}^3$, compute the dimension of $\Lambda^2 \mathbb{C}^3$ as a complex vector space.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

An orthonormal basis of $\Lambda^n \mathbb{C}^d$ is indexed by $n$-element subsets of an ONB of $\mathbb{C}^d$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$\binom{3}{2} = 3$.**

The basis vectors are $e_1 \wedge e_2$, $e_1 \wedge e_3$, and $e_2 \wedge e_3$ — one per unordered pair of distinct basis elements. The dimension of $\Lambda^n \mathbb{C}^d$ is $\binom{d}{n}$ for $n \leq d$ and $0$ for $n > d$; the fermionic Fock space over $\mathbb{C}^d$ therefore has total dimension $2^d$, by adding $\binom{d}{n}$ over $n = 0, 1, \ldots, d$ via the binomial identity. For $d = 3$: $1 + 3 + 3 + 1 = 8$, matching the eight basis states $|n_1, n_2, n_3\rangle$ with each $n_k \in \{0, 1\}$. The whole fermionic Fock space over a finite-dimensional one-particle space is finite-dimensional, in stark contrast to the bosonic case where each level is already infinite-dimensional.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\mathcal{H}$ be a complex separable Hilbert space — the **one-particle Hilbert space**. For each integer $n \geq 0$, write $\mathcal{H}^{\otimes n}$ for the Hilbert-space $n$-fold tensor power (with $\mathcal{H}^{\otimes 0} := \mathbb{C}$), and let $S_n$ act on $\mathcal{H}^{\otimes n}$ by permutation of the tensor factors with unitary $U_\sigma$ for $\sigma \in S_n$. The **antisymmetrisation projector** is

$$
P_a^{(n)} := \frac{1}{n!}\sum_{\sigma \in S_n}\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma)\,U_\sigma,
$$

where $\mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) \in \{+1, -1\}$ is the sign of the permutation. The **antisymmetric $n$-fold tensor power** is the closed subspace

$$
\Lambda^n \mathcal{H} := P_a^{(n)}\,\mathcal{H}^{\otimes n} = \{\xi \in \mathcal{H}^{\otimes n} : U_\sigma \xi = \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma)\,\xi \text{ for all } \sigma \in S_n\}.
$$

The **fermionic Fock space** over $\mathcal{H}$ is the Hilbert-space direct sum

$$
\mathcal{F}_a(\mathcal{H}) := \bigoplus_{n=0}^\infty \Lambda^n \mathcal{H},
$$

with inner product $\langle (\xi_n)_n, (\eta_n)_n\rangle_{\mathcal{F}_a} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \langle \xi_n, \eta_n\rangle_{\Lambda^n \mathcal{H}}$ and norm $\|(\xi_n)_n\|^2 = \sum_n \|\xi_n\|^2 < \infty$. The $\ell^2$ direct-sum construction makes $\mathcal{F}_a(\mathcal{H})$ a complex separable Hilbert space [02.11.08]. The summand $\Lambda^0 \mathcal{H} = \mathbb{C}$ is spanned by the unit vector $\Omega$ called the **vacuum**.

### Antisymmetric inner product

For $f_1, \ldots, f_n, g_1, \ldots, g_n \in \mathcal{H}$, the antisymmetric tensor $f_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge f_n := \sqrt{n!}\,P_a^{(n)}(f_1 \otimes \cdots \otimes f_n)$ satisfies

$$
\langle f_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge f_n,\; g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n\rangle_{\Lambda^n \mathcal{H}} = \det\bigl(\langle f_i, g_j\rangle\bigr),
$$

where $\det$ is the ordinary matrix determinant. The determinant — the antisymmetric analog of the permanent that appeared on the bosonic side — encodes the alternating-sign structure of the wedge product. The normalisation factor $\sqrt{n!}$ in the definition of the wedge product is the convention that gives clean coefficients for the creation operators below.

### Slater-determinant basis

Let $\{\phi_i\}_{i \geq 1}$ be an orthonormal basis of $\mathcal{H}$. An orthonormal basis of $\Lambda^n \mathcal{H}$ is given by the **Slater determinants**

$$
|i_1, \ldots, i_n\rangle_a := \phi_{i_1} \wedge \phi_{i_2} \wedge \cdots \wedge \phi_{i_n} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n!}}\det\bigl(\phi_{i_k}(x_l)\bigr)_{k,l=1}^n,
$$

indexed by strictly increasing tuples $i_1 < i_2 < \cdots < i_n$. The determinant form on the right is the classical Slater 1929 [ref: TODO_REF Slater 1929] writing in coordinates: an $n$-particle antisymmetric wavefunction is the determinant of a matrix whose entry $(k, l)$ is the $k$-th orbital evaluated at the $l$-th particle's coordinate. Antisymmetry under particle exchange is then immediate from the determinant's alternation under row swaps.

### Creation and annihilation operators

For each $f \in \mathcal{H}$, define the **creation operator** $a^*(f) : \Lambda^n \mathcal{H} \to \Lambda^{n+1} \mathcal{H}$ by

$$
a^*(f)(g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n) := f \wedge g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n,
$$

with $a^*(f)\Omega := f$, extended linearly. The **annihilation operator** $a(f) : \Lambda^n \mathcal{H} \to \Lambda^{n-1} \mathcal{H}$ is defined as the formal adjoint, acting by

$$
a(f)(g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n) := \sum_{k=1}^n (-1)^{k-1}\,\langle f, g_k\rangle\,g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \widehat{g_k} \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n,
$$

(where the caret marks omission), and $a(f)\Omega := 0$. The alternating signs $(-1)^{k-1}$ arise from anticommuting the operator past the $k-1$ wedge factors to its left. The map $f \mapsto a^*(f)$ is complex-linear; the map $f \mapsto a(f)$ is complex-antilinear, exactly as in the bosonic case.

### Number operator

The **number operator** $N$ acts on $\Lambda^n \mathcal{H}$ by multiplication by $n$. For an orthonormal basis $\{e_k\}_{k\geq 1}$ of $\mathcal{H}$,

$$
N = \sum_{k=1}^\infty a^*(e_k)\,a(e_k),
$$

where the sum converges on the finite-particle subspace $\mathcal{F}_a^{\mathrm{fin}}(\mathcal{H})$ (the algebraic direct sum of the $\Lambda^n \mathcal{H}$) and extends to a self-adjoint operator on $\mathcal{F}_a(\mathcal{H})$ with spectrum $\sigma(N) = \mathbb{N}_0$ when $\dim\mathcal{H} = \infty$. The individual mode-occupation operator $n_k := a^*(e_k)\,a(e_k)$ has spectrum $\{0, 1\}$ — the Pauli exclusion principle as a spectral statement.

### Standard conventions

The **Dirac-style field operators** for a Dirac field of mass $m$ on Minkowski space are

$$
\psi(x) = \sum_s\int \frac{d^3\mathbf{p}}{(2\pi)^3\sqrt{2\omega_\mathbf{p}}}\Bigl(b_s(\mathbf{p})\,u_s(\mathbf{p})\,e^{-ip\cdot x} + d_s^*(\mathbf{p})\,v_s(\mathbf{p})\,e^{+ip\cdot x}\Bigr),
$$

where $b_s, d_s$ are the fermion and antifermion annihilation operators, $u_s, v_s$ are spinor mode functions, $s$ runs over the two spin states, and $p^0 = \omega_\mathbf{p} = \sqrt{|\mathbf{p}|^2 + m^2}$. The CAR for the field reads $\{\psi_a(x), \psi_b^\dagger(y)\} = \delta_{ab}\,\delta^3(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y})$ for equal-time field operators on a Cauchy slice (Tong's QFT notes §5.2 [ref: tong]; Peskin-Schroeder Ch. 3 §3.5 [ref: TODO_REF Peskin-Schroeder]).

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The operators $a(f), a^*(f)$ are **bounded** in the fermionic case, with $\|a^*(f)\| = \|a(f)\| = \|f\|$ (proved in the next section). This is a genuine algebraic contrast with the bosonic side, where the CCR $[a, a^*] = 1$ forces unboundedness. The boundedness lets the fermionic CAR algebra be packaged as a $C^*$-algebra without domain machinery — Bratteli-Robinson Vol. II §5.2 [ref: TODO_REF Bratteli-Robinson Vol. II].

- The antisymmetric tensor power $\Lambda^n \mathcal{H}$ **vanishes** whenever $n > \dim \mathcal{H}$ (with $\dim \mathcal{H}$ counted as a cardinal). For $\mathcal{H} = \mathbb{C}^d$, the fermionic Fock space is finite-dimensional of dimension $\sum_{n=0}^d \binom{d}{n} = 2^d$ — the spin-$1/2$ chain Hilbert space, foreshadowing the Jordan-Wigner transformation.

- The wedge product $f \wedge f = 0$ for every $f \in \mathcal{H}$, equivalently $a^*(f)^2 = 0$. This is the Pauli exclusion principle in algebraic form and is the single most distinctive feature of the fermionic algebra against the bosonic one.

- The CAR algebra is not the same as the CCR algebra with a sign flipped — the bosonic CCR generates a Heisenberg algebra (Lie algebra, with a central extension), while the fermionic CAR generates a Clifford algebra [03.09.02] over $\mathcal{H}_\mathbb{R} \oplus \mathcal{H}_\mathbb{R}$ with the inner-product-induced quadratic form. The two algebras live in different universes of representation theory.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (canonical anticommutation relations).** *For all $f, g \in \mathcal{H}$, the creation and annihilation operators on $\mathcal{F}_a(\mathcal{H})$ satisfy*

$$
\{a(f),\, a^*(g)\} = \langle f, g\rangle\,\mathbb{1}, \qquad \{a(f),\, a(g)\} = \{a^*(f),\, a^*(g)\} = 0,
$$

*on the finite-particle subspace $\mathcal{F}_a^{\mathrm{fin}}(\mathcal{H})$.*

**Proof.** It suffices to compute on an antisymmetric tensor $\xi = g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n \in \Lambda^n \mathcal{H}$. Apply $a^*(g)$ first, then $a(f)$:

$$
a^*(g)\xi = g \wedge g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n \in \Lambda^{n+1}\mathcal{H}.
$$

Acting by $a(f)$ on this $(n+1)$-fold wedge: the leading slot now carries $g$, with sign $(-1)^0 = 1$, and the remaining slots carry $g_1, \ldots, g_n$ with signs $(-1)^k$ for $k = 1, \ldots, n$:

$$
a(f)\,a^*(g)\xi = \langle f, g\rangle\,g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n + \sum_{k=1}^n (-1)^k\,\langle f, g_k\rangle\,g \wedge g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \widehat{g_k} \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n.
$$

Now apply the operators in the reverse order. First $a(f)$:

$$
a(f)\xi = \sum_{k=1}^n (-1)^{k-1}\,\langle f, g_k\rangle\,g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \widehat{g_k} \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n,
$$

a vector in $\Lambda^{n-1}\mathcal{H}$. Then $a^*(g)$ wedges $g$ onto the leading slot of each term:

$$
a^*(g)\,a(f)\xi = \sum_{k=1}^n (-1)^{k-1}\,\langle f, g_k\rangle\,g \wedge g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \widehat{g_k} \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n.
$$

Add the two orderings: the $(-1)^k$ and $(-1)^{k-1}$ contributions cancel term by term in the sum over $k$, and the surviving term is the leading $\langle f, g\rangle\,g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n$. Therefore

$$
a(f)\,a^*(g)\xi + a^*(g)\,a(f)\xi = \langle f, g\rangle\,\xi,
$$

which is the first CAR identity on the dense finite-particle subspace.

For $\{a^*(f), a^*(g)\} = 0$: both $a^*(f)\,a^*(g)\xi$ and $a^*(g)\,a^*(f)\xi$ produce the same vector up to a sign, namely $f \wedge g \wedge g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n$ and $g \wedge f \wedge g_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge g_n$. The wedge product is antisymmetric, so $f \wedge g = -g \wedge f$, and the two contributions cancel exactly. For $\{a(f), a(g)\} = 0$: a parallel double-sum cancellation, where the signs from $(-1)^{k-1}$ in $a(f)$ and $a(g)$ combine to give cancelling pairs on the diagonals of the indexing. $\square$

**Corollary (Pauli exclusion).** *For every $f \in \mathcal{H}$, $a^*(f)^2 = 0$.*

**Proof.** Set $g = f$ in the third CAR identity: $\{a^*(f), a^*(f)\} = 2\,a^*(f)^2 = 0$, so $a^*(f)^2 = 0$. $\square$

**Theorem (fermionic bound).** *For every $f \in \mathcal{H}$, the creation and annihilation operators are bounded with $\|a^*(f)\| = \|a(f)\| = \|f\|$.*

**Proof.** From the CAR with $g = f$, $\{a(f), a^*(f)\} = a(f)\,a^*(f) + a^*(f)\,a(f) = \|f\|^2\,\mathbb{1}$. Both $a(f)\,a^*(f)$ and $a^*(f)\,a(f)$ are positive operators (each is $T^*T$ for $T = a^*(f)$ or $T = a(f)$ respectively). Therefore each is bounded above by their sum: $0 \leq a^*(f)\,a(f) \leq \|f\|^2\,\mathbb{1}$. Taking the operator norm,

$$
\|a(f)\|^2 = \|a^*(f)\,a(f)\| \leq \|f\|^2,
$$

so $\|a(f)\| \leq \|f\|$. For the matching lower bound, apply $a(f)$ to the one-particle vector $f \in \Lambda^1 \mathcal{H}$: $a(f)\,f = \langle f, f\rangle\,\Omega = \|f\|^2\,\Omega$, giving $\|a(f)\,f\| = \|f\|^2 \cdot \|\Omega\| = \|f\|^2$ while $\|f\|_{\Lambda^1} = \|f\|_\mathcal{H}$. Therefore $\|a(f)\| \geq \|f\|^2 / \|f\| = \|f\|$, and combining, $\|a(f)\| = \|f\|$. By taking adjoints, $\|a^*(f)\| = \|a(f)\| = \|f\|$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The CAR algebra and the fermionic bound together package the entire structure of fermionic many-body quantum mechanics into bounded-operator identities, which lets the CAR algebra be completed in operator norm to a $C^*$-algebra without the domain machinery the bosonic CCR required. This is exactly the dictionary that lets the antisymmetric tensor algebra of a one-particle Hilbert space generate a many-body theory of indistinguishable fermions; the bridge is then provided by the fermionic second-quantisation functor lifting one-particle dynamics to fermionic Fock-space dynamics, identifying the CAR algebra with the universal irreducible algebra of fermionic creation and annihilation, and feeding into the Dirac-field quantisation programme of the next chapter and the multi-electron atomic-structure programme in section 14.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

For two orthonormal one-particle states $f, g \in \mathcal{H}$ with $\langle f, g\rangle = 0$, compute the four-operator product $a(f)\,a^*(g)\,a(g)\,a^*(f)\,\Omega$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Move the rightmost operators inward using the CAR; remember $a(f)\Omega = a(g)\Omega = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Compute step by step from the right. $a^*(f)\Omega = f$. Then $a(g)\,f = \langle g, f\rangle\,\Omega = 0$ since $f \perp g$. The whole product is therefore zero. Alternative: use $\{a(g), a^*(f)\} = \langle g, f\rangle = 0$ to anticommute $a(g)$ past $a^*(f)$ at no cost in extra terms, so $a(g)\,a^*(f) = -a^*(f)\,a(g)$, and the trailing $a(g)\Omega = 0$ kills everything. The two paths agree, as they must. The exercise illustrates that orthogonal fermionic modes "anticommute cleanly," with no inner-product contribution from the CAR.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, conceptual).**

Explain why the operator $N_k := a^*(e_k)\,a(e_k)$ has spectrum exactly $\{0, 1\}$ for any orthonormal basis vector $e_k \in \mathcal{H}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $N_k^2$ using the CAR.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Using $\{a(e_k), a^*(e_k)\} = 1$, write $a(e_k)\,a^*(e_k) = 1 - a^*(e_k)\,a(e_k) = 1 - N_k$. Then

$$
N_k^2 = a^*(e_k)\,a(e_k)\,a^*(e_k)\,a(e_k) = a^*(e_k)\,(1 - N_k)\,a(e_k) = N_k - a^*(e_k)\,N_k\,a(e_k).
$$

Use $a^*(e_k)\,a^*(e_k) = 0$ to simplify the last term: $a^*(e_k)\,a^*(e_k)\,a(e_k)\,a(e_k) = 0$, so $a^*(e_k)\,N_k\,a(e_k) = 0$. Therefore $N_k^2 = N_k$, which says $N_k$ is a projector. A projector has spectrum contained in $\{0, 1\}$; both values are achieved (the vacuum gives eigenvalue $0$, the one-particle state $e_k$ gives eigenvalue $1$), so the spectrum is exactly $\{0, 1\}$. This is the spectral statement of Pauli exclusion.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

For an orthonormal basis $\{e_k\}$ of $\mathcal{H}$, prove that the Slater-determinant basis vector $|i_1, \ldots, i_n\rangle_a = e_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge e_{i_n}$ with $i_1 < \cdots < i_n$ has unit norm.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the determinant formula for the antisymmetric inner product.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the determinant formula, $\langle e_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge e_{i_n},\, e_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge e_{i_n}\rangle = \det(\langle e_{i_k}, e_{i_l}\rangle)_{k,l=1}^n$. Since $\{e_k\}$ is an orthonormal basis, $\langle e_{i_k}, e_{i_l}\rangle = \delta_{kl}$, so the Gram matrix is the identity and the determinant is $1$. Therefore the norm is $1$. For Slater determinants with index multisets differing by even a single entry, the determinant is zero (a row of zeros emerges in the Gram matrix), giving orthogonality. This proves the Slater determinants form an orthonormal family in $\Lambda^n \mathcal{H}$; that they span follows from the antisymmetrisation projector applied to a basis of $\mathcal{H}^{\otimes n}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Write out the helium ground-state two-electron Slater determinant in terms of the spin-orbitals $\phi_{1s\uparrow}, \phi_{1s\downarrow}$ and verify it is antisymmetric under exchange of the two electron labels.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Slater determinant for two electrons in spin-orbitals $\chi_1, \chi_2$ is $\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\chi_1(1)\chi_2(2) - \chi_1(2)\chi_2(1))$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $\chi_1 = \phi_{1s\uparrow}$ and $\chi_2 = \phi_{1s\downarrow}$, the Slater determinant is

$$
\Psi(1, 2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\bigl(\phi_{1s\uparrow}(1)\phi_{1s\downarrow}(2) - \phi_{1s\uparrow}(2)\phi_{1s\downarrow}(1)\bigr).
$$

Swap the two electron labels $1 \leftrightarrow 2$:

$$
\Psi(2, 1) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\bigl(\phi_{1s\uparrow}(2)\phi_{1s\downarrow}(1) - \phi_{1s\uparrow}(1)\phi_{1s\downarrow}(2)\bigr) = -\Psi(1, 2),
$$

which is the antisymmetry under exchange. The spatial part factorises as $\phi_{1s}(\mathbf{r}_1)\phi_{1s}(\mathbf{r}_2)$ (symmetric) times the spin singlet $\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|\!\uparrow\downarrow\rangle - |\!\downarrow\uparrow\rangle)$ (antisymmetric); the product is overall antisymmetric, as required. Try instead to place both electrons in $\phi_{1s\uparrow}$: the Slater determinant becomes $\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\phi_{1s\uparrow}(1)\phi_{1s\uparrow}(2) - \phi_{1s\uparrow}(2)\phi_{1s\uparrow}(1)) = 0$, recovering Pauli exclusion.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

State the **Jordan-Wigner transformation** and verify that the spin operators $\sigma^+_n = c_n^* \prod_{j < n}(1 - 2\,c_j^* c_j)$ satisfy the Pauli spin-raising relations on a 1D lattice.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The fermionic mode operators $c_n^*, c_n$ on $\ell^2(\mathbb{Z})$ generate the CAR algebra; the Jordan-string $\prod_{j<n}(1 - 2 c_j^* c_j)$ converts anticommuting fermionic operators into commuting spin operators between sites.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Jordan-Wigner transformation (Jordan-Wigner 1928 [ref: TODO_REF Jordan-Wigner 1928]).** On the fermionic Fock space $\mathcal{F}_a(\ell^2(\mathbb{Z}))$ with mode operators $c_n^*, c_n$ satisfying $\{c_m, c_n^*\} = \delta_{mn}$, define $\sigma^+_n := c_n^* K_n$ and $\sigma^-_n := K_n c_n$ with the Jordan string $K_n := \prod_{j<n}(1 - 2\,c_j^* c_j) = \prod_{j<n}(1 - 2 N_j)$. Then $K_n$ is a unitary operator with $K_n^2 = \mathbb{1}$ (since $1 - 2N_j$ has spectrum $\{+1, -1\}$ and squares to $1$). Combining the local fermion anticommutator at site $n$ with the Jordan string at sites $j < n$, one verifies the Pauli spin algebra

$$
\{\sigma^+_n, \sigma^-_n\} = \mathbb{1}, \quad [\sigma^+_m, \sigma^-_n] = 0 \text{ for } m \neq n, \quad (\sigma^+_n)^2 = 0,
$$

so each site carries a spin-$1/2$ Hilbert space $\mathbb{C}^2$. The map identifies the fermionic Fock space $\mathcal{F}_a(\ell^2(\mathbb{Z}))$ with the spin-chain Hilbert space $\bigotimes_n \mathbb{C}^2$. Modern uses include exact solutions of the 1D XY and Ising spin chains (Lieb-Schultz-Mattis 1961), the quantum Hall effect bulk-edge correspondence, and lattice simulations of fermionic systems on quantum computers.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Prove that the fermionic Fock-space norm is uniquely determined by the CAR algebra and the requirement of a vacuum vector: given any irreducible representation of the CAR algebra on a Hilbert space $K$ with a vacuum vector $\Omega_K$ satisfying $a(f)\Omega_K = 0$ for all $f \in \mathcal{H}$, the GNS-type construction from $\Omega_K$ yields a unitary equivalence with the Fock representation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The CAR algebra and the vacuum-annihilation condition together determine all matrix elements $\langle \Omega_K, a(f_1)\cdots a(f_m)\,a^*(g_1)\cdots a^*(g_n)\,\Omega_K\rangle$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Apply the CAR to compute the matrix elements. For any string $a(f_1)\cdots a(f_m)\,a^*(g_1)\cdots a^*(g_n)$ acting on $\Omega_K$, anticommute each $a(f_i)$ to the right past the $a^*(g_j)$'s using $\{a(f_i), a^*(g_j)\} = \langle f_i, g_j\rangle$. Each anticommutation either contracts a pair (picking up a factor $\langle f_i, g_j\rangle$ with an appropriate sign) or passes through (picking up a minus sign). Once all $a(f_i)$'s reach the right, they hit $\Omega_K$ and give zero. The surviving terms are sums of products of inner products with signs from the anticommutations — exactly the Wick-style determinant expansion of $\det(\langle f_i, g_j\rangle)$ if $m = n$, and zero otherwise.

Therefore the matrix element $\langle \Omega_K, a(f_1)\cdots a(f_m)\,a^*(g_1)\cdots a^*(g_n)\,\Omega_K\rangle$ equals $\delta_{m,n}\,\det(\langle f_i, g_j\rangle)$. The same calculation in the Fock representation gives the same value, by the antisymmetric-inner-product formula of the previous section. Two irreducible representations with all matrix elements of a generating algebra agreeing on a cyclic vector are unitarily equivalent (standard GNS argument), so $K$ is unitarily equivalent to $\mathcal{F}_a(\mathcal{H})$. The vacuum-state uniqueness then gives uniqueness of the irreducible Fock representation up to unitary equivalence — the **fermionic Stone-von Neumann analog** for finitely many modes, extended to the infinite-mode case with appropriate Hilbert-Schmidt provisos (Shale-Stinespring 1965 fermionic version). $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

State the **spin-statistics theorem** and explain in two-to-three sentences why fermionic quantisation of an integer-spin field, or bosonic quantisation of a half-integer-spin field, leads to a contradiction in a Lorentz-invariant local quantum field theory.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Pauli 1940 [ref: TODO_REF Pauli 1940]; Streater-Wightman Ch. 4 [ref: TODO_REF Streater-Wightman 1964].

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Spin-statistics theorem (Pauli 1940; rigorous in Streater-Wightman 1964).** In any quantum field theory satisfying Lorentz invariance, local commutativity (microcausality), positive-energy spectrum, and the existence of a unique Lorentz-invariant vacuum, integer-spin fields must be quantised with canonical commutation relations (bosons) and half-integer-spin fields must be quantised with canonical anticommutation relations (fermions).

The contradiction in the "wrong choice" case is most cleanly stated as follows. For a free Lorentz-invariant field theory, computing the two-point function $\langle\Omega, \phi(x)\phi(y)\Omega\rangle$ and its conjugate involves a sum over positive-energy modes that produces a Lorentz-invariant Wightman function with a fixed analytic structure (support on the forward light cone, positivity of the Wightman two-point function as a positive measure on momentum space). Requiring bosonic CCR on a half-integer-spin field forces the would-be Wightman function to have a *negative* contribution at spacelike separations from the spinor mode functions $u_s, v_s$ — incompatible with positivity. Requiring fermionic CAR on an integer-spin field forces a similar incompatibility, this time with the requirement of microcausality (the field anticommutator at spacelike separation does not vanish, contradicting local commutativity). The Pauli 1940 argument shows the obstruction at the level of free fields; Burgoyne 1958 and Streater-Wightman 1964 give the rigorous proof under the Wightman axioms. The theorem is one of the deepest consequences of relativistic locality and the input that pins down the statistics of every species in the Standard Model.

</details>

</aside>

## The CAR algebra as a $C^*$-algebra [Master]

The fermionic bound $\|a^*(f)\| = \|f\|$ makes the entire fermionic algebraic-Fock-space construction live inside the bounded-operator framework, with no domain machinery needed. Define the **CAR algebra** $\mathfrak{A}_{\mathrm{CAR}}(\mathcal{H})$ as the norm-closure inside $\mathcal{B}(\mathcal{F}_a(\mathcal{H}))$ of the $*$-algebra generated by $\{a(f), a^*(f) : f \in \mathcal{H}\}$. By the fermionic bound and the algebraic CAR identities, $\mathfrak{A}_{\mathrm{CAR}}(\mathcal{H})$ is a $C^*$-algebra — the **canonical anticommutation relations $C^*$-algebra** over $\mathcal{H}$, due in this $C^*$-algebraic form to the Bratteli-Robinson treatment [ref: TODO_REF Bratteli-Robinson Vol. II].

**Theorem (uniqueness of the CAR algebra).** *Let $\mathcal{H}$ be a complex separable Hilbert space. The CAR algebra $\mathfrak{A}_{\mathrm{CAR}}(\mathcal{H})$ is, up to $*$-isomorphism, the unique $C^*$-algebra generated by elements $\{a(f), a^*(f)\}$ ($f \in \mathcal{H}$, $a$ antilinear in $f$, $a^*$ linear) satisfying the CAR. When $\dim \mathcal{H} = n < \infty$, $\mathfrak{A}_{\mathrm{CAR}}(\mathbb{C}^n) \cong M_{2^n}(\mathbb{C})$, the full matrix algebra on the $2^n$-dimensional fermionic Fock space. When $\dim \mathcal{H} = \infty$, $\mathfrak{A}_{\mathrm{CAR}}(\mathcal{H}) \cong \varinjlim_n M_{2^n}(\mathbb{C})$, the **CAR-AF-algebra** (uniformly hyperfinite of type $2^\infty$), and the Fock representation is one of many inequivalent irreducible representations indexed by quasi-equivalence classes of Bogoliubov transformations.*

**Proof sketch.** The finite-mode case is direct: the CAR with $n$ generators $c_k, c_k^*$ produces the algebra $M_{2^n}(\mathbb{C})$ via the Jordan-Wigner identification with the spin-chain Hilbert space $\bigotimes_k \mathbb{C}^2$ of dimension $2^n$. Uniqueness for finite $n$ is the finite-dimensional Stone-von Neumann analog. For infinite $n$, the algebra is the inductive limit (uniformly hyperfinite of type $2^\infty$, classified by Glimm 1960), and the Fock representation is the cyclic-and-separating representation generated by the vacuum-state on the quasi-free state $\omega(a^*(f)\,a(g)) = 0$, $\omega(a(f)\,a^*(g)) = \langle f, g\rangle$ — the **Fock state** in the $C^*$-algebraic sense. The GNS construction over the Fock state recovers the Fock representation. Other quasi-free states (Bogoliubov-rotated, KMS at finite temperature) give inequivalent representations, classified by the **Shale-Stinespring criterion** in fermionic form: a Bogoliubov transformation $(a(f), a^*(f)) \mapsto (U a(f) + V a^*(\overline{f}), U^* a^*(f) + V^* a(\overline{f}))$ with $U^* U + V^T \overline{V} = \mathbb{1}$ is implementable by a unitary on the original Fock space iff $V$ is Hilbert-Schmidt. $\square$

The $C^*$-algebraic packaging makes the fermionic case structurally cleaner than the bosonic case (where the unbounded operators forced the Weyl exponentiated form for the $C^*$-algebra), and is the workhorse of fermionic many-body statistical mechanics: Bratteli-Robinson §5.2 [ref: TODO_REF Bratteli-Robinson Vol. II] develops the entire equilibrium-state theory (KMS states at inverse temperature $\beta$ and chemical potential $\mu$, Fermi-Dirac distribution recovery, BCS pairing) inside this framework.

## Connection to QFT and the Dirac field [Master]

In quantum field theory, the one-particle Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}_1$ for a spin-$1/2$ fermion of mass $m$ is taken to be the **positive-energy spinor space** — the space of $L^2$-sections of the spinor bundle over Minkowski space supported on positive-energy solutions of the **Dirac equation** $(i\gamma^\mu \partial_\mu - m)\psi = 0$, where $\gamma^\mu$ are the Dirac gamma matrices generating a Clifford algebra [03.09.02] over the Minkowski metric. The fermionic Fock space $\mathcal{F}_a(\mathcal{H}_1)$ is the **free fermion Hilbert space**, and the **free Dirac field** is the operator-valued tempered distribution

$$
\psi(x) = \sum_{s=1}^2\int \frac{d^3\mathbf{p}}{(2\pi)^3\sqrt{2\omega_\mathbf{p}}}\Bigl(b_s(\mathbf{p})\,u_s(\mathbf{p})\,e^{-ip\cdot x} + d_s^*(\mathbf{p})\,v_s(\mathbf{p})\,e^{+ip\cdot x}\Bigr)
$$

acting on $\mathcal{F}_a(\mathcal{H}_1)$, with $u_s, v_s$ the four-component positive- and negative-energy spinor mode functions and $b_s, d_s$ the particle and antiparticle annihilation operators. The CAR for the field reads

$$
\{\psi_a(x), \psi_b^\dagger(y)\} = \delta_{ab}\,\delta^3(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y})
$$

for equal-time field operators on a Cauchy slice (Glimm-Jaffe §6.3 [ref: TODO_REF Glimm-Jaffe 1987]; Tong's QFT §5.2 [ref: tong]). Like the bosonic Klein-Gordon field, $\psi(x)$ is well-defined only as a distribution: smeared against a Schwartz spinor-valued test function $f \in \mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^4, \mathbb{C}^4)$, $\psi(f) = \int \psi(x)\,f(x)\,d^4 x$ is a densely-defined bounded operator on $\mathcal{F}_a(\mathcal{H}_1)$.

The Dirac field on the fermionic Fock space satisfies a fermionic-statistics version of the Wightman axioms — strictly, the Wightman framework as packaged in Streater-Wightman 1964 [ref: TODO_REF Streater-Wightman 1964] includes the spin-statistics theorem as an output: the requirement of local commutativity must be relaxed to local *anti*-commutativity for half-integer-spin fields, and the proof in Ch. 4 derives this from the joint requirements of Lorentz invariance, positive-energy spectrum, and the existence of a unique Lorentz-invariant vacuum. Bosonic Wightman axioms with half-integer-spin field give a contradiction (the would-be Wightman two-point function fails positivity); fermionic Wightman axioms with integer-spin give a contradiction (the field anticommutator at spacelike separation does not vanish). The fermionic Fock space is therefore the unique kinematic substrate of any rigorous quantisation of half-integer-spin fields.

The **CPT theorem** in Streater-Wightman Ch. 5 takes the spin-statistics result as an input and proves that any Lorentz-invariant local quantum field theory satisfying the Wightman axioms (with the appropriate bosonic / fermionic statistics) is invariant under the combined action of charge conjugation, parity, and time reversal — one of the deepest theorems in mathematical physics, and the rigorous output of the framework introduced in this unit's bosonic sibling and extended here.

**Synthesis.** The fermionic Fock space identifies the many-body quantum mechanics of identical fermions with a single, fixed, second-quantised representation of the canonical anticommutation relations on a separable Hilbert space — and the CAR algebra in its $C^*$-algebraic form pins down the Fock representation as the unique cyclic representation generated by the vacuum-quasi-free state. The foundational reason the construction supports both atomic-structure physics and relativistic QFT is that the antisymmetric tensor algebra of a one-particle Hilbert space generates a Pauli-exclusion-respecting many-body theory automatically, with the fermionic bound $\|a^*(f)\| = \|f\|$ giving bounded operators throughout and the Jordan-Wigner transformation identifying the CAR algebra with the spin-chain Hilbert space in the 1D lattice case. This is exactly the central insight Pauli 1925 [ref: TODO_REF Pauli 1925], Dirac 1926 [ref: TODO_REF Dirac 1926], Fermi 1926 [ref: TODO_REF Fermi 1926], and Jordan-Wigner 1928 [ref: TODO_REF Jordan-Wigner 1928] introduced — putting these together with the spin-statistics theorem of Pauli 1940 [ref: TODO_REF Pauli 1940] and Streater-Wightman 1964 [ref: TODO_REF Streater-Wightman 1964], the bridge is the operator-valued tempered distribution $\psi(x)$ that satisfies the fermionic Wightman axioms and identifies the free Dirac field with the second-quantised Dirac equation; this pattern recurs in every textbook free-fermion-field construction, builds toward the Standard Model where every matter field is half-integer-spin and quantised this way, and appears again in the Lieb-Thirring stability of matter, the BCS theory of superconductivity, the quantum Hall effect, and the entire $C^*$-algebraic formulation of equilibrium quantum statistical mechanics for fermionic systems.

## Connections [Master]

- **Bosonic Fock space and second quantisation [12.03.01].** Direct sibling. The fermionic construction differs from the bosonic only in the replacement of the symmetric projector $P_s^{(n)}$ by the antisymmetric projector $P_a^{(n)}$ and of the symmetric tensor power $S^n \mathcal{H}$ by the antisymmetric $\Lambda^n \mathcal{H}$. The commutation relation $[a, a^*] = 1$ becomes the anticommutation relation $\{a, a^*\} = 1$; the unbounded creation and annihilation operators of the bosonic side become bounded fermionic ones with $\|a^*(f)\| = \|f\|$; the symmetric inner product as a permanent becomes the antisymmetric one as a determinant; the bosonic Stone-von Neumann uniqueness becomes the fermionic CAR-algebra uniqueness; the Klein-Gordon free field becomes the free Dirac field. Read this unit in parallel with its sibling.

- **Hilbert-space formalism of quantum mechanics [12.02.01].** Direct prerequisite. The fermionic Fock space is a Hilbert space and the creation / annihilation operators are bounded self-adjoint and anti-self-adjoint combinations on it; the Dirac-von Neumann postulates apply verbatim, with the additional structure of the antisymmetrisation projector and the CAR algebra. The exclusion principle is expressed inside the Dirac-von Neumann framework as the spectral statement $\sigma(N_k) = \{0, 1\}$ for each mode-occupation observable $N_k = a^*(e_k)\,a(e_k)$.

- **Stern-Gerlach and spin-1/2 [12.01.02].** Chapter seed. The Pauli matrices $\sigma_x, \sigma_y, \sigma_z$ on $\mathbb{C}^2$ developed there are the smallest-possible finite-dimensional CAR representation, realised on $\mathcal{F}_a(\mathbb{C}) = \mathbb{C} \oplus \mathbb{C} = \mathbb{C}^2$: $\sigma_+ = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ acts as the single creation operator $a^*$, $\sigma_- = \sigma_+^\dagger$ acts as $a$, $\{\sigma_+, \sigma_-\} = \mathbb{1}$, and $\sigma_+^2 = \sigma_-^2 = 0$. The Stern-Gerlach experiment was historically the first probe of half-integer angular momentum and, via spin-statistics, the first probe of fermionic statistics; the Jordan-Wigner transformation then promotes this single-site picture to a 1D fermionic lattice via the spin-chain Hilbert space $\bigotimes_n \mathbb{C}^2$.

- **Clifford algebra [03.09.02].** The CAR algebra is a Clifford algebra. Concretely, for a real Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}_\mathbb{R}$ underlying $\mathcal{H}$ with the real-bilinear form $B(f, g) = \mathrm{Re}\langle f, g\rangle$, the operators $\gamma(f) := a^*(f) + a(f)$ for $f \in \mathcal{H}_\mathbb{R}$ satisfy $\gamma(f)\,\gamma(g) + \gamma(g)\,\gamma(f) = 2\,B(f, g)\,\mathbb{1}$, the defining identity of the Clifford algebra $\mathrm{Cl}(\mathcal{H}_\mathbb{R}, B)$. The fermionic Fock space is the *spin representation* of this Clifford algebra (Lawson-Michelsohn Ch. I [ref: TODO_REF Lawson-Michelsohn]); the Pauli matrices on $\mathbb{C}^2$ generate the Clifford algebra $\mathrm{Cl}_2 \cong M_2(\mathbb{C})$ corresponding to the spin-$1/2$ one-particle space.

- **Hilbert space [02.11.08], inner product space [02.11.07].** Substrate. The antisymmetric tensor power $\Lambda^n \mathcal{H}$ uses the determinant-of-Gram-matrix inner product, an inner-product-space construction; the fermionic Fock space is the $\ell^2$ direct sum of these, a separable Hilbert space; the fermionic bound and the CAR identities are bounded-operator statements that rely on Hilbert-space completeness throughout.

- **Bounded linear operators [02.11.01].** The fermionic creation and annihilation operators are bounded with $\|a^*(f)\| = \|a(f)\| = \|f\|$ — a definitive structural contrast with the bosonic case, where $a^*(f)$ is unbounded. The CAR algebra is therefore a $C^*$-algebra in its raw algebraic form, without exponentiation; the Weyl-form work-around of the bosonic case is unnecessary on the fermionic side. The norm equality $\|a^*(f)\| = \|f\|$ is the bound underlying every fermionic-many-body estimate.

- **Lie algebra representation [07.06.01].** Although the CAR algebra is a Clifford algebra (not a Lie algebra), the **bilinear** combinations $a^*(f)\,a(g)$ (one creation, one annihilation, equal in number) close under the commutator, giving a Lie algebra isomorphic to $\mathfrak{gl}(\mathcal{H})$ (or, for self-adjoint combinations, $\mathfrak{u}(\mathcal{H})$). The number operator $N$, the total angular momentum, and every conserved quantum-number operator are bilinear in $a^*, a$ and live inside this Lie subalgebra — this is the framework for **second-quantised one-particle dynamics** lifting to fermionic Fock space.

- **Stone-von Neumann theorem and the Weyl algebra (proposed successor `12.03.03`).** The fermionic-side uniqueness theorem (CAR analog of Stone-von Neumann) is sketched in Exercise 6 and developed in full in the dedicated successor unit, where the CCR and CAR uniqueness results are treated in parallel.

- **Wightman axioms and the free scalar field (proposed successor `12.04.01`).** The fermionic version of the Wightman axioms requires the local commutator to be replaced by the local anticommutator for half-integer-spin fields, and the free Dirac field on the fermionic Fock space is the canonical example verifying the axioms — the input to the spin-statistics theorem and to the CPT theorem in the rigorous QFT framework.

- **Dirac field (proposed successor `12.04.03`).** The free Dirac field $\psi(x), \psi^\dagger(x)$ as an operator-valued tempered distribution on the fermionic Fock space over positive-energy spinor solutions of the Dirac equation is the half-integer-spin counterpart to the Klein-Gordon free field on the bosonic side; the dedicated unit develops the construction and the verification of the fermionic Wightman axioms.

- **Multi-electron atoms (proposed cross-section `14.04.01`).** Atomic-structure physics — helium, lithium, the periodic table — is built directly on Slater determinants, the orthonormal basis of $\Lambda^n \mathcal{H}$ introduced here. The aufbau principle is the systematic application of the Pauli exclusion principle proved here: each one-particle orbital can be doubly occupied (with opposite spins), once spin is included as part of the one-particle label.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The Pauli exclusion principle was introduced by Wolfgang Pauli in 1925, in the paper *Über den Zusammenhang des Abschlusses der Elektronengruppen im Atom mit der Komplexstruktur der Spektren* [ref: TODO_REF Pauli 1925], appearing in *Zeitschrift für Physik* 31, 765. The paper proposed the *Ausschließungsprinzip* — exclusion principle — as a phenomenological rule to explain the regularities of atomic spectra and the closing of electron shells in the periodic table, with each one-particle quantum-number tuple admitting at most one electron. Pauli had no microscopic justification at the time; the rule was an empirical scaffolding to organise spectroscopic data and predict the noble-gas configurations.

The quantum-mechanical content emerged over the next three years. Dirac 1926 [ref: TODO_REF Dirac 1926] introduced Fermi-Dirac statistics in *Proc. Roy. Soc. A* 112, 661, proving that the antisymmetry of the many-particle wavefunction was the formal incarnation of exclusion. Fermi 1926 [ref: TODO_REF Fermi 1926] independently developed the same statistics in the ideal-gas context. Slater 1929 [ref: TODO_REF Slater 1929] introduced the *Slater determinant* construction in *Phys. Rev.* 34, 1293, packaging the antisymmetric many-electron wavefunction as a determinant of single-particle orbitals — the calculational backbone of atomic and molecular physics ever since. Jordan-Wigner 1928 [ref: TODO_REF Jordan-Wigner 1928] in *Z. Phys.* 47, 631 introduced the canonical anticommutation relations as the operator-algebra incarnation of fermionic statistics, and the **Jordan-Wigner transformation** linking the CAR algebra on a 1D lattice to spin-Pauli operators on a 1D chain — a transformation that remains central in modern condensed-matter physics and quantum-computing simulation of fermionic systems.

The connection between spin and statistics was originally a phenomenological observation: half-integer-spin particles (electrons, protons, neutrons) obey Fermi-Dirac statistics, integer-spin particles (photons, helium-4) obey Bose-Einstein statistics. The mathematical underpinning came from Fierz 1939 [ref: TODO_REF Fierz 1939] and Pauli 1940 [ref: TODO_REF Pauli 1940] in *Phys. Rev.* 58, 716, where Pauli proved the **spin-statistics theorem** for free fields: in any Lorentz-invariant local quantum field theory, integer-spin fields must use canonical commutation relations and half-integer-spin fields must use canonical anticommutation relations, with the "wrong" choice giving a non-positive-definite Hilbert space inner product. The rigorous extension to all Wightman-axiomatic quantum field theories was given by Burgoyne 1958 and Streater-Wightman 1964 [ref: TODO_REF Streater-Wightman 1964], establishing spin-statistics as one of the load-bearing structural theorems of relativistic QFT.

The post-war development of the fermionic Fock space tracked the bosonic case: Cook 1953 supplied the bosonic rigorous framework, and the parallel fermionic framework was developed by Segal, Berezin, and others through the 1960s. Berezin's *Method of Second Quantization* [ref: TODO_REF Berezin 1966] introduced the Grassmann-variable / Berezin-integral formalism for fermionic path integrals, the calculational tool that has dominated fermionic QFT ever since. Bratteli-Robinson Vol. II [ref: TODO_REF Bratteli-Robinson Vol. II] in the 1980s packaged the CAR algebra as the workhorse $C^*$-algebra of fermionic quantum statistical mechanics, developing KMS states, quasi-free states, Bogoliubov-Shale-Stinespring criteria, and the BCS pairing mechanism inside this framework.

Philosophically, the bosonic / fermionic distinction is one of the few absolute dichotomies in nature. There is no continuous interpolation between Bose-Einstein and Fermi-Dirac statistics in three spatial dimensions; particles are either bosons or fermions, and the spin-statistics theorem ties the distinction to the relativistic kinematics of half-integer vs integer spin. In two spatial dimensions the rigid dichotomy gives way to a continuum of **anyonic statistics** (Wilczek 1982), with arbitrary phases $e^{i\theta}$ under exchange — the foundation of the fractional quantum Hall effect and topological quantum computing. The fermionic Fock space and the Pauli exclusion principle are also the foundational input for the **stability of matter** programme (Dyson-Lenard 1967; Lieb-Thirring 1975 [ref: TODO_REF Lieb-Thirring 1975]; Lieb-Seiringer monograph [ref: TODO_REF Lieb-Seiringer 2010]), which proves that the total ground-state energy of a system of $N$ electrons and nuclei is bounded below by $-c N$ for a universal constant $c$ — without fermionic antisymmetry the bound fails and ordinary matter is unstable. Exclusion is the structural reason matter does not collapse.
