## Intuition [Beginner]

In classical mechanics, a particle gets from point A to point B along a single path — the one that makes the action stationary (unit 09.02.01). You solve Newton's equations, get one trajectory, done.

Quantum mechanics says something stranger. The particle does not commit to a single path. It explores every possible route from A to B simultaneously — straight lines, curves, zigzags, paths that loop the moon and come back. Each path contributes a complex number called an *amplitude*. The amplitude has two parts: a magnitude (same for every path, for a free particle) and a phase that depends on the action $S$ of that path.

The phase is $S/\hbar$, where $\hbar$ is the reduced Planck constant. When you add up all these amplitudes — one per path — the total amplitude is what you observe as the probability of the particle arriving at B. Probabilities come from squaring the total amplitude.

Why does classical physics look like it has a single path? Because for macroscopic objects, $S$ is enormous compared to $\hbar$. Neighbouring paths have wildly different phases, so their amplitudes cancel. Only near the classical path — where the action is stationary, meaning nearby paths have almost the same $S$ — do the amplitudes add constructively instead of cancelling. The classical path "wins" by destructive elimination of everything else.

This picture is due to Richard Feynman. It is an alternative to the Schrodinger equation — you get the same predictions by summing amplitudes over paths rather than by solving a differential equation for a wave function. The two formulations are equivalent, in the same way that Lagrangian and Newtonian mechanics are equivalent descriptions of the same physics.

The path-integral picture has one enormous advantage: it generalises. When you move from one particle to quantum fields — the subject of quantum field theory — the Schrodinger equation becomes unwieldy, but the path integral adapts naturally. You sum over field configurations instead of particle paths. The machinery is the same; the space being summed over gets bigger.

A second advantage is visual. The double-slit experiment makes perfect sense in this language: the particle goes through slit one and slit two, each route contributes an amplitude, the two amplitudes interfere, and the interference pattern on the screen is the squared total amplitude. No mysterious "wave-particle duality" language required — just "add up every path, square the result."

The price you pay is mathematical. The sum over all paths is not an ordinary sum. There are infinitely many paths, and the "measure" that weights them is not a standard mathematical measure. Physicists handle this by discretising time into many small steps, writing a finite-dimensional integral for each step, and taking the limit as the steps shrink. The limit exists and gives the right answers, but putting it on rigorous footing is a deep problem in mathematical physics.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a particle starting at position $x = 0$ at time $t = 0$ and ending at $x = L$ at time $t = T$. Draw the $(x, t)$-plane with $t$ on the vertical axis and $x$ horizontal. Mark the start point $(0, 0)$ at the bottom and the end point $(L, T)$ at the top.

Now draw every possible curve connecting these two points. Straight line. Gentle arc. Wild zigzag. A curve that goes to $x = 1000$ and back. Each curve is one "path" — one history the particle could have. In the path integral, every one of these curves contributes an amplitude.

![Space-time diagram showing multiple paths connecting point (0,0) to point (L,T). The classical straight-line path is highlighted; several zigzag and curved alternatives fan out around it. A phase arrow (unit circle) is drawn next to each path, with arrows nearly aligned near the classical path and randomly oriented far from it, illustrating constructive and destructive interference.](/img/12.10.01-path-integral-spacetime-placeholder.svg)

Near the classical path (the straight line, for a free particle), the phase arrows nearly agree — they point in roughly the same direction. Far from the classical path, the arrows point every which way and cancel when you add them. This is the stationary-phase condition in action.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The simplest system is a free particle of mass $m$ moving in one dimension — no potential, no forces. The classical path from $(0, 0)$ to $(x, T)$ is a straight line at constant velocity $v = x/T$.

In the path-integral picture, every path from $(0, 0)$ to $(x, T)$ contributes an amplitude with phase $S/\hbar$, where $S$ is the action of that path. For a free particle the action of the classical path is $S_{\rm cl} = mx^2/(2T)$.

The key mathematical fact: when you add up all paths, the Gaussian integrals collapse into a single expression. The total amplitude — the *propagator* — comes out as

$$
K(x, T) \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{m}{2\pi i \hbar T}}\;\exp\!\Big(\frac{i\,m\,x^2}{2\,\hbar\,T}\Big).
$$

The square-root out front sets the overall normalisation (so that the total probability of arriving *somewhere* is 1). The exponential carries the classical action in its phase. For large $T$ the propagator spreads out — the particle becomes more delocalised — consistent with wave-packet spreading.

The classical limit: when $S_{\rm cl}/\hbar$ is large, the phase oscillates rapidly as $x$ varies, and the propagator is peaked near the classical trajectory. When $S_{\rm cl}/\hbar$ is small (light particles, short distances), the phase varies slowly and quantum spreading is significant.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The **propagator** (or kernel) $K(x_f, t_f;\, x_i, t_i)$ is the probability amplitude for a particle starting at position $x_i$ at time $t_i$ to arrive at position $x_f$ at time $t_f$. In the canonical formalism it is the matrix element of the time-evolution operator:

$$
K(x_f, t_f;\, x_i, t_i) \;=\; \langle x_f \vert\, e^{-iH(t_f - t_i)/\hbar}\,\vert x_i \rangle.
$$

Feynman's path integral expresses this propagator as a sum over all paths $x(t)$ with $x(t_i) = x_i$ and $x(t_f) = x_f$:

$$
\boxed{\;K(x_f, t_f;\, x_i, t_i) \;=\; \int \mathcal{D}x(t)\; e^{iS[x]/\hbar}\;}
$$

where $S[x] = \int_{t_i}^{t_f} L(x, \dot{x}, t)\, dt$ is the classical action evaluated on the path $x(t)$, and $\mathcal{D}x(t)$ denotes the functional integration measure over paths.

The measure $\mathcal{D}x(t)$ is defined by **time-slicing**. Divide the interval $[t_i, t_f]$ into $N$ equal segments of duration $\epsilon = (t_f - t_i)/N$. Insert complete sets of position eigenstates at each time slice:

$$
K \;=\; \lim_{N \to \infty} \left(\frac{m}{2\pi i \hbar \epsilon}\right)^{\!N/2} \int \prod_{j=1}^{N-1} dx_j\;\exp\!\left(\frac{i}{\hbar}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} \epsilon\, L\!\left(\frac{x_{k+1}+x_k}{2},\, \frac{x_{k+1}-x_k}{\epsilon},\, t_k\right)\right).
$$

Each intermediate point $x_j$ is integrated over $(-\infty, \infty)$. The prefactor $(m/(2\pi i\hbar\epsilon))^{N/2}$ is the free-particle normalisation from the Gaussian integral at each time slice. The midpoint discretisation (Weyl ordering) is chosen to maintain unitarity.

For a particle with Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 - V(x)$, the time-sliced exponent becomes

$$
\frac{i}{\hbar}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} \left[\frac{m(x_{k+1}-x_k)^2}{2\epsilon} - \epsilon\, V\!\left(\frac{x_{k+1}+x_k}{2}\right)\right].
$$

The kinetic piece is quadratic in the differences $(x_{k+1}-x_k)$, which makes the intermediate Gaussian integrals tractable. The potential piece evaluates at the midpoint and couples the slices.

The time-slicing definition is the operational meaning of $\mathcal{D}x(t)$. For quadratic Lagrangians (free particle, harmonic oscillator), the Gaussian integrals can be performed exactly and the $N \to \infty$ limit computed in closed form. For general potentials, the path integral is evaluated perturbatively or numerically.

**Semiclassical expansion.** Writing $x(t) = x_{\rm cl}(t) + \delta x(t)$ where $x_{\rm cl}$ is the classical path (stationary point of $S$) and expanding the action to second order in $\delta x$ gives

$$
K \;\approx\; \mathcal{N}\;\exp\!\left(\frac{iS[x_{\rm cl}]}{\hbar}\right) \times \left(\det \frac{\delta^2 S}{\delta x^2}\right)^{\!-1/2},
$$

where the functional determinant (the Van Vleck determinant) encodes fluctuations around the classical path. Higher-order terms in $\delta x$ give loop corrections in the diagrammatic expansion.

### Counterexamples and caveats

- The measure $\mathcal{D}x(t)$ is **not** a genuine countably-additive measure on a $\sigma$-algebra of path-space subsets. The oscillatory factor $e^{iS/\hbar}$ prevents this. Rigorous constructions exist for the Euclidean (Wick-rotated) version via the Wiener measure; the Minkowski-signature version requires additional regularisation.
- The path integral is **not** an integral over a single path. It is a functional integral over an infinite-dimensional space of paths. The notation $\mathcal{D}x(t)$ is a formal symbol defined by the time-slicing limit.
- For particles with spin, or for systems with gauge redundancy, the naive measure $\mathcal{D}x(t)$ is incorrect and must be supplemented by a Jacobian (the Faddeev-Popov determinant) or replaced by a coherent-state path integral with a Berry-phase term.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Equivalence of the path integral and the Schrodinger equation).** *The propagator $K(x_f, t;\, x_i, 0)$ defined by the time-sliced path integral satisfies $(i\hbar\,\partial/\partial t - H_x)K = 0$ with initial condition $K(x_f, 0;\, x_i, 0) = \delta(x_f - x_i)$, where $H_x = -\hbar^2/(2m)\,\partial^2/\partial x_f^2 + V(x_f)$ is the Hamiltonian acting on the $x_f$ variable.*

**Proof.** We derive the Schrodinger equation from the composition property of the propagator. For an infinitesimal time step $\epsilon$, the time-sliced propagator is

$$
K(x, \epsilon;\, x', 0) \;=\; \left(\frac{m}{2\pi i \hbar \epsilon}\right)^{\!1/2} \exp\!\left[\frac{i}{\hbar}\left(\frac{m(x-x')^2}{2\epsilon} - \epsilon\, V\!\left(\frac{x+x'}{2}\right)\right)\right].
$$

The composition law states that propagating for time $t + \epsilon$ equals propagating for time $t$ followed by one more slice:

$$
\psi(x, t+\epsilon) \;=\; \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} K(x, \epsilon;\, x', 0)\;\psi(x', t)\, dx'.
$$

Write $x' = x + \eta$ and expand. The exponent contains $m\eta^2/(2\epsilon)$, which is of order $\hbar$ when $\eta \sim \sqrt{\hbar\epsilon/m}$ (the dominant contribution). Expand the integrand to first order in $\epsilon$ and second order in $\eta$:

$$
\psi(x, t+\epsilon) \;\approx\; \left(\frac{m}{2\pi i \hbar \epsilon}\right)^{\!1/2} \int d\eta\; \exp\!\left(\frac{im\eta^2}{2\hbar\epsilon}\right)\left[1 - \frac{i\epsilon V(x)}{\hbar}\right]\left[\psi(x,t) - \eta\,\psi'(x,t) + \frac{\eta^2}{2}\,\psi''(x,t)\right].
$$

The Gaussian integrals are: $\int d\eta\, e^{im\eta^2/(2\hbar\epsilon)} = \sqrt{2\pi i\hbar\epsilon/m}$; the linear term in $\eta$ vanishes by symmetry; $\int d\eta\, \eta^2\, e^{im\eta^2/(2\hbar\epsilon)} = (i\hbar\epsilon/m)\sqrt{2\pi i\hbar\epsilon/m}$. Dividing by the normalisation and collecting:

$$
\psi(x, t+\epsilon) \;=\; \psi(x,t) - \frac{i\epsilon}{\hbar}\,V(x)\,\psi(x,t) + \frac{i\hbar\epsilon}{2m}\,\psi''(x,t).
$$

Rearranging, dividing by $\epsilon$, and taking $\epsilon \to 0$:

$$
i\hbar\,\frac{\partial \psi}{\partial t} \;=\; -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\,\frac{\partial^2 \psi}{\partial x^2} + V(x)\,\psi,
$$

which is the Schrodinger equation. The initial condition $K(x,0;x',0) = \delta(x-x')$ follows from the identity $\lim_{\epsilon \to 0}(m/(2\pi i\hbar\epsilon))^{1/2}\exp(im(x-x')^2/(2\hbar\epsilon)) = \delta(x-x')$. $\square$

**Corollary.** *The path integral propagator equals the canonical-quantisation propagator: $K_{\rm path\text{-}integral} = K_{\rm Schrodinger}$. The two formulations of quantum mechanics produce identical physical predictions.*

The proof above demonstrates the time-slicing route. An alternative route, historically earlier, proceeds via the Trotter product formula: $e^{-iHt/\hbar} = \lim_{N\to\infty}(e^{-iV\epsilon/(2\hbar)}\,e^{-iT\epsilon/\hbar}\,e^{-iV\epsilon/(2\hbar)})^N$ with symmetric (Strang) splitting, and inserting position eigenstates between each factor.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

For a free particle in one dimension, the classical action for the straight-line path from $(0, 0)$ to $(x, T)$ is $S_{\rm cl} = mx^2/(2T)$. Evaluate the propagator $K(x, T;\, 0, 0) = \sqrt{m/(2\pi i\hbar T)}\,e^{iS_{\rm cl}/\hbar}$ at $x = 0$. What is $|K(0, T;\, 0, 0)|^2$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

At $x = 0$ the exponential factor is $e^0 = 1$. Square the modulus of what remains.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$K(0, T;\, 0, 0) = \sqrt{m/(2\pi i\hbar T)}$, so $|K|^2 = m/(2\pi\hbar T)$. This is not a probability density for arriving at $x = 0$ — the propagator itself is a probability-density amplitude, and $|K|^2$ has dimensions of inverse length. The total probability requires integrating over all $x$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the free-particle propagator $K(x, T;\, 0, 0) = \sqrt{m/(2\pi i\hbar T)}\,e^{imx^2/(2\hbar T)}$ satisfies the free Schrodinger equation $i\hbar\,\partial K/\partial T = -(\hbar^2/2m)\,\partial^2 K/\partial x^2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate $K$ with respect to $T$ and with respect to $x$. The normalisation prefactor has a $T^{-1/2}$ dependence that contributes a term; combine it with the derivative of the exponential.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $A = \sqrt{m/(2\pi i\hbar)}$. Then $K = AT^{-1/2}e^{i\alpha x^2/T}$ where $\alpha = m/(2\hbar)$.

$\partial K/\partial T = A\left[-\tfrac{1}{2}T^{-3/2}e^{i\alpha x^2/T} + T^{-1/2}(-i\alpha x^2/T^2)e^{i\alpha x^2/T}\right] = K\left[-\tfrac{1}{2T} - \tfrac{i\alpha x^2}{T^2}\right]$.

$\partial K/\partial x = K \cdot 2i\alpha x/T$. $\partial^2 K/\partial x^2 = K\left[(2i\alpha/T)^2 x^2 + 2i\alpha/T\right] = K\left[-4\alpha^2 x^2/T^2 + 2i\alpha/T\right]$.

Now $i\hbar\,\partial K/\partial T = i\hbar K[-1/(2T) - i\alpha x^2/T^2] = K[-i\hbar/(2T) + \hbar\alpha x^2/T^2]$.

$-(\hbar^2/2m)\partial^2 K/\partial x^2 = -(\hbar^2/2m)K[-4\alpha^2 x^2/T^2 + 2i\alpha/T] = K[2\hbar^2\alpha^2 x^2/(mT^2) - i\hbar^2\alpha/(mT)]$.

Since $\alpha = m/(2\hbar)$: $\hbar^2\alpha/m = \hbar/2$ and $2\hbar^2\alpha^2/m = \hbar^2 \cdot m/(2\hbar^2) = m\alpha$. The two sides match.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the free-particle propagator by performing the Gaussian integrals in the time-sliced path integral with $N$ time slices and taking $N \to \infty$. Start from

$$
K_N(x, T;\, 0, 0) = \left(\frac{m}{2\pi i\hbar\epsilon}\right)^{\!N/2}\int \prod_{j=1}^{N-1} dx_j\;\exp\!\left(\frac{im}{2\hbar\epsilon}\sum_{k=0}^{N-1}(x_{k+1}-x_k)^2\right)
$$

with $x_0 = 0$, $x_N = x$, and $\epsilon = T/N$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Do the $x_1$ integral first. It is Gaussian with a quadratic exponent depending on $x_1$ and $x_2$. The result combines with the next integral. Iterate, keeping track of the accumulating normalisation and the quadratic form in $x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The $x_1$ integral: $\int dx_1 \exp\!\big[\frac{im}{2\hbar\epsilon}(x_1^2 + (x_2-x_1)^2)\big] = \int dx_1 \exp\!\big[\frac{im}{2\hbar\epsilon}(2x_1^2 - 2x_1 x_2 + x_2^2)\big]$.

Completing the square in $x_1$: $2(x_1 - x_2/2)^2 + x_2^2/2$. The Gaussian integral gives $\sqrt{\pi \cdot i\hbar\epsilon/(m \cdot 2)} \cdot e^{imx_2^2/(4\hbar\epsilon)}$.

After integrating out $x_1$ the effective action for $x_2$ is $\frac{im}{2\hbar\epsilon}\frac{x_2^2}{2}$, which has the same form as before but with an extra factor of $1/2$. By induction, after integrating out $x_1, \ldots, x_{N-1}$, the result is $\sqrt{m/(2\pi i\hbar N\epsilon)} \cdot e^{imx^2/(2\hbar N\epsilon)} = \sqrt{m/(2\pi i\hbar T)}\,e^{imx^2/(2\hbar T)}$. The key step is that each Gaussian integration absorbs one factor of the prefactor while modifying the remaining quadratic form — the recurrence $a_n = na_1$ for the effective inverse-width parameter gives the final answer.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

The harmonic oscillator has Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2$. The classical action from $(0, 0)$ to $(x, T)$ is $S_{\rm cl} = \tfrac{m\omega}{2\sin\omega T}\,x^2\cos\omega T$. (You may use this without proof.) Write the semiclassical propagator $K_{\rm WKB} = \mathcal{N}\,e^{iS_{\rm cl}/\hbar}$, identifying the normalisation $\mathcal{N}$ by requiring unitarity ($\int |K|^2\,dx$ appropriately). Check that $\mathcal{N}$ reduces to the free-particle answer when $\omega \to 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Van Vleck determinant for one degree of freedom is $\sqrt{-(1/2\pi i\hbar)\,\partial^2 S_{\rm cl}/\partial x\,\partial x'}$. For the free particle this gives $\sqrt{m/(2\pi i\hbar T)}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\partial S_{\rm cl}/\partial x = m\omega x\cos\omega T/\sin\omega T$, so $\partial^2 S_{\rm cl}/\partial x^2 = m\omega\cos\omega T/\sin\omega T = m\omega\cot\omega T$.

The Van Vleck formula gives $\mathcal{N} = \sqrt{m\omega/(2\pi i\hbar \sin\omega T)}$, so

$$
K_{\rm WKB}(x, T;\, 0, 0) = \sqrt{\frac{m\omega}{2\pi i\hbar \sin\omega T}}\;\exp\!\left(\frac{im\omega x^2}{2\hbar}\,\frac{\cos\omega T}{\sin\omega T}\right).
$$

As $\omega \to 0$: $\sin\omega T \approx \omega T$ and $\cos\omega T/\sin\omega T \approx 1/(\omega T)$, giving $\mathcal{N} \to \sqrt{m/(2\pi i\hbar T)}$ and the exponent $\to imx^2/(2\hbar T)$. This matches the free-particle propagator.

In fact this semiclassical result is exact for the harmonic oscillator (the action is quadratic, so there are no higher-order corrections).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

The composition law for propagators is $K(x_f, t_f;\, x_i, t_i) = \int K(x_f, t_f;\, x, t)\,K(x, t;\, x_i, t_i)\,dx$ for any intermediate time $t$. Verify this for the free-particle propagator by computing the Gaussian integral on the right-hand side and showing it equals $K(x_f, t_f;\, x_i, t_i)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write out both propagators in the integral, combine the exponentials into a single quadratic in $x$, complete the square, and evaluate the Gaussian.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\tau_1 = t - t_i$, $\tau_2 = t_f - t$. The product under the integral sign is

$$
\sqrt{\frac{m}{2\pi i\hbar\tau_2}}\sqrt{\frac{m}{2\pi i\hbar\tau_1}}\int dx\;\exp\!\left[\frac{im}{2\hbar}\left(\frac{(x_f-x)^2}{\tau_2} + \frac{(x-x_i)^2}{\tau_1}\right)\right].
$$

The exponent in $x$ is $(im/(2\hbar))[(x^2/\tau_2 - 2x_fx/\tau_2 + x_f^2/\tau_2) + (x^2/\tau_1 - 2x_ix/\tau_1 + x_i^2/\tau_1)]$. Completing the square: $x^2(\tau_1+\tau_2)/(\tau_1\tau_2) - 2x(x_f\tau_1+x_i\tau_2)/(\tau_1\tau_2)$ gives centre at $x_0 = (x_f\tau_1+x_i\tau_2)/(\tau_1+\tau_2)$.

After the Gaussian integration the result is $\sqrt{m/(2\pi i\hbar(\tau_1+\tau_2))}\exp[im(x_f-x_i)^2/(2\hbar(\tau_1+\tau_2))]$, which is $K(x_f, t_f;\, x_i, t_i)$ since $\tau_1+\tau_2 = t_f - t_i$.

</details>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

For the harmonic oscillator, expand the action $S[x]$ about the classical path $x_{\rm cl}(t)$ to second order in the fluctuation $\eta(t) = x(t) - x_{\rm cl}(t)$ with $\eta(0) = \eta(T) = 0$. Show that the fluctuation operator is $-\frac{d^2}{dt^2} - \omega^2$ acting on $\eta$, and that the path integral over $\eta$ produces the Van Vleck determinant $\sqrt{m\omega/(2\pi i\hbar \sin\omega T)}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $S[x_{\rm cl}+\eta] = S[x_{\rm cl}] + \int dt\,\eta(t)\,\delta S/\delta x(t)|_{x_{\rm cl}} + \frac{1}{2}\int dt\,dt'\,\eta(t)\,\delta^2 S/(\delta x(t)\delta x(t'))\,\eta(t')$. The first variation vanishes because $x_{\rm cl}$ is stationary.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$S = \int_0^T [\tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 x^2]\,dt$. Expand $x = x_{\rm cl} + \eta$:

$\dot{x} = \dot{x}_{\rm cl} + \dot{\eta}$, so $\dot{x}^2 = \dot{x}_{\rm cl}^2 + 2\dot{x}_{\rm cl}\dot{\eta} + \dot{\eta}^2$ and $x^2 = x_{\rm cl}^2 + 2x_{\rm cl}\eta + \eta^2$.

The zeroth-order term is $S[x_{\rm cl}]$. The first-order term is $\int_0^T [m\dot{x}_{\rm cl}\dot{\eta} - m\omega^2 x_{\rm cl}\eta]\,dt = 0$ by integration by parts and the Euler-Lagrange equation for $x_{\rm cl}$.

The second-order term is $\frac{1}{2}\int_0^T [m\dot{\eta}^2 - m\omega^2\eta^2]\,dt = -\frac{m}{2}\int_0^T \eta(t)[\frac{d^2}{dt^2}+\omega^2]\eta(t)\,dt$ after integrating by parts (the boundary terms vanish since $\eta(0) = \eta(T) = 0$).

The fluctuation path integral is $\int \mathcal{D}\eta\,e^{-\frac{im}{2\hbar}\int_0^T \eta(-\ddot{\eta} - \omega^2\eta)\,dt}$. This is a Gaussian functional integral with operator $A = -d^2/dt^2 - \omega^2$ on the space of functions vanishing at $t=0, T$. The eigenvalues are $\lambda_n = (n\pi/T)^2 - \omega^2$ for $n = 1, 2, \ldots$. The determinant $(\det A)^{-1/2}$ evaluates, via zeta-function regularisation, to $\sqrt{m\omega/(2\pi i\hbar \sin\omega T)}$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The path integral for a particle in a linear potential $V(x) = -Fx$ (constant force $F$) has classical action $S_{\rm cl} = \tfrac{m(x_f-x_i)^2}{2T} + \tfrac{F(x_f+x_i)T}{2} - \tfrac{F^2 T^3}{24m}$. Derive the propagator by performing the Gaussian path integral with this action and the appropriate Van Vleck determinant.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 + Fx$ is quadratic in $\dot{x}$ but linear in $x$. The fluctuation operator is $-md^2/dt^2$ (same as the free particle) because $\partial^2 V/\partial x^2 = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $V''(x) = 0$, the fluctuation operator is $-md^2/dt^2$ — identical to the free particle. The Van Vleck determinant is $\sqrt{-(1/2\pi i\hbar)\,\partial^2 S_{\rm cl}/\partial x_f \partial x_i}$.

$\partial S_{\rm cl}/\partial x_f = m(x_f-x_i)/T + FT/2$, so $\partial^2 S_{\rm cl}/\partial x_f\partial x_i = -m/T$.

Therefore $\mathcal{N} = \sqrt{m/(2\pi i\hbar T)}$ — same prefactor as the free particle — and

$$
K(x_f, T;\, x_i, 0) = \sqrt{\frac{m}{2\pi i\hbar T}}\;\exp\!\left[\frac{i}{\hbar}\left(\frac{m(x_f-x_i)^2}{2T} + \frac{F(x_f+x_i)T}{2} - \frac{F^2 T^3}{24m}\right)\right].
$$

The linear potential shifts the phase but does not alter the Gaussian fluctuation structure. This is the propagator for a particle in a uniform gravitational field (or a charged particle in a uniform electric field).

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, conceptual).**

Explain why the path integral for a particle in a potential $V(x) = \lambda x^4$ (quartic oscillator) cannot be evaluated in closed form, and describe the perturbative strategy for computing it.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The action $S = \int (\tfrac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 - \lambda x^4)\,dt$ is not quadratic in $x$. What happens to the Gaussian integration when the exponent is quartic?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The quartic term makes the action non-quadratic, so the path integral is no longer a Gaussian functional integral and cannot be performed in closed form. The perturbative strategy treats $\lambda$ as small, expands $\exp(i\lambda \int x^4\,dt/\hbar) = 1 - (i\lambda/\hbar)\int x^4\,dt + \ldots$, and evaluates each term using the free-particle (Gaussian) path integral as a reference. Each term is a moment $\langle x(t_1)x(t_2)x(t_3)x(t_4)\rangle_0$ of the free-particle distribution, computed via Wick's theorem as a sum over pairings. The result organises into Feynman diagrams: vertices from the quartic interaction, propagator lines from the free-particle kernel, and loop diagrams carrying the quantum corrections. The perturbation series is asymptotic (zero radius of convergence in $\lambda$), reflecting the fact that the quartic potential is not close to the free particle for any fixed $\lambda > 0$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

Derive the initial condition $K(x, 0;\, x', 0) = \delta(x - x')$ from the time-sliced expression for the free-particle propagator, using the representation of the Dirac delta as $\lim_{\epsilon \to 0}(m/(2\pi i\hbar\epsilon))^{1/2}e^{im(x-x')^2/(2\hbar\epsilon)}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show the Gaussian is a nascent delta: it integrates to 1, and for any test function $f$, $\int dx'\,f(x')(m/(2\pi i\hbar\epsilon))^{1/2}e^{im(x-x')^2/(2\hbar\epsilon)} \to f(x)$ as $\epsilon \to 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $g_\epsilon(x) = (m/(2\pi i\hbar\epsilon))^{1/2}e^{imx^2/(2\hbar\epsilon)}$. For real $x \neq 0$, the phase $imx^2/(2\hbar\epsilon) = imx^2/(2\hbar\epsilon)$ has unit modulus, so $|g_\epsilon| = (m/(2\pi\hbar\epsilon))^{1/2} \to \infty$ as $\epsilon \to 0$ at $x = 0$ and the oscillations become infinitely rapid for $x \neq 0$ (Riemann-Lebesgue). The Fresnel integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g_\epsilon(x)\,dx = 1$ for all $\epsilon > 0$ (evaluate by completing the square or analytic continuation from the Gaussian).

For any Schwartz function $f$: $\int f(x')g_\epsilon(x-x')\,dx'$. Change variable $y = x'(m/(2\hbar\epsilon))^{1/2}$; as $\epsilon \to 0$ the integral is dominated by $y$ near $0$, so $f(x') \approx f(x)$ and the integral $\to f(x)\int g_\epsilon(x-x')\,dx' = f(x)$. This is the defining property of the delta function: $K(x, 0;\, x', 0) = \delta(x - x')$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 10 (hard, symbolic).**

A charged particle in a uniform magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = B\hat{z}$ has Lagrangian $L = \tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot{\mathbf{q}}|^2 + \tfrac{eB}{2c}(q_x \dot{q}_y - q_y \dot{q}_x)$. Write the path integral for this system and explain why the linear-in-velocity term (the magnetic coupling) does not spoil the Gaussian nature of the path integral.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Lagrangian is still quadratic in $\dot{\mathbf{q}}$ and $\mathbf{q}$. A term linear in $\dot{q}$ shifts the centre of the Gaussian but does not change the integration measure.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The path integral is $K(\mathbf{q}_f, T;\, \mathbf{q}_i, 0) = \int \mathcal{D}\mathbf{q}(t)\;\exp\!\big[\frac{i}{\hbar}\int_0^T \big(\tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot{\mathbf{q}}|^2 + \tfrac{eB}{2c}(q_x\dot{q}_y - q_y\dot{q}_x)\big)\,dt\big]$.

The action is quadratic in the dynamical variables: $\tfrac{1}{2}m|\dot{\mathbf{q}}|^2$ is quadratic in $\dot{\mathbf{q}}$, and $q_x\dot{q}_y - q_y\dot{q}_x$ is bilinear in $(q, \dot{q})$. After time-slicing, the integrand at each step is $\exp[\frac{i}{\hbar}(a_{jk}q_j q_k + b_{jk}q_j \dot{q}_k + c_{jk}\dot{q}_j\dot{q}_k)]$ — a multivariate Gaussian in the position variables. The linear-in-velocity term acts like a linear term in the exponent after integration by parts (shifting the Gaussian centre), and the result is still a Gaussian integral with a modified quadratic form. The propagator for the Landau problem (charged particle in a magnetic field) is therefore computable in closed form: the spectrum consists of Landau levels $E_n = \hbar\omega_c(n+\tfrac{1}{2})$ where $\omega_c = eB/(mc)$, and the propagator is a product of a harmonic-oscillator kernel and a free-particle kernel in the $\hat{z}$-direction.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

### Wick rotation and the Euclidean path integral

The oscillatory integral $e^{iS/\hbar}$ is ill-defined as a Lebesgue integral because the integrand has unit modulus on an infinite-dimensional space. The rigorous construction proceeds via **Wick rotation**: substitute $t \to -i\tau$ (real time to imaginary time), which sends $e^{iS/\hbar} \to e^{-S_E/\hbar}$ where $S_E$ is the **Euclidean action**

$$
S_E[x] \;=\; \int_0^{T_E} \left[\tfrac{1}{2}m\left(\frac{dx}{d\tau}\right)^{\!2} + V(x)\right]d\tau.
$$

The Euclidean action is the action with the sign of the potential reversed: kinetic plus potential (not kinetic minus potential). The factor $e^{-S_E/\hbar}$ is a genuine decaying exponential, and the resulting functional integral is the **Wiener integral** — rigorously defined by the Wiener measure on continuous paths. The Wiener measure exists, is unique (subject to normalisation), and satisfies the Kolmogorov consistency conditions.

The connection to statistical mechanics is immediate. The Euclidean path integral over periodic paths $x(0) = x(T_E)$ with $T_E = \beta\hbar$ (where $\beta = 1/(k_B T_{\rm temp})$ is the inverse temperature) gives the **partition function**:

$$
Z(\beta) \;=\; \int \mathcal{D}x(\tau)\;e^{-S_E[x]/\hbar}\Big|_{\text{periodic},\; T_E = \beta\hbar} \;=\; \mathrm{Tr}\;e^{-\beta \hat{H}}.
$$

This is the bridge to unit [11.04.01]: the partition function of statistical mechanics is a Wick-rotated path integral. The trace arises because periodic boundary conditions in imaginary time enforce the trace over the Hilbert space.

**Osterwalder-Schrader reconstruction.** For a Euclidean field theory satisfying reflection positivity (the Osterwalder-Schrader axioms), one can analytically continue back to real time and recover a unitary quantum field theory. This is the rigorous pathway: define the theory in Euclidean signature, prove the axioms, reconstruct the Minkowski theory. The Schwinger functions (Euclidean $n$-point functions) analytically continue to the Wightman functions (Minkowski $n$-point functions). Reflection positivity is the Euclidean shadow of unitarity.

### Semiclassical (WKB) expansion

The formal stationary-phase evaluation of the path integral gives the **semiclassical expansion**. Write $x(t) = x_{\rm cl}(t) + \sqrt{\hbar}\,y(t)$ where $x_{\rm cl}$ is the classical path and $y$ is the quantum fluctuation. The action expands as

$$
\frac{S[x_{\rm cl}+\sqrt{\hbar}\,y]}{\hbar} \;=\; \frac{S[x_{\rm cl}]}{\hbar} + \frac{1}{2}\langle y, A\, y\rangle + \sqrt{\hbar}\,S_3[y] + \hbar\,S_4[y] + \ldots
$$

where $A = \delta^2 S/\delta x^2|_{x_{\rm cl}}$ is the fluctuation operator (second functional derivative), $S_3$ collects cubic terms, $S_4$ collects quartic terms, and $\langle y, Ay\rangle$ is the quadratic form. The path integral becomes

$$
K \;=\; e^{iS_{\rm cl}/\hbar}\;\int \mathcal{D}y\;e^{\frac{i}{2}\langle y,Ay\rangle}\left(1 + i\sqrt{\hbar}\,S_3[y] + i\hbar\,S_4[y] - \frac{\hbar}{2}S_3[y]^2 + \ldots\right).
$$

The leading term gives the Van Vleck propagator. The $\sqrt{\hbar}$ term vanishes by symmetry (odd moments of a Gaussian). The $\hbar$ correction is the **one-loop term**, expressed as a determinant ratio involving $A$ and the free-particle fluctuation operator. Higher terms give multi-loop corrections.

For one-dimensional systems the fluctuation determinant evaluates to $\sqrt{m/(2\pi i\hbar)}\,|\partial^2 S_{\rm cl}/\partial x_f \partial x_i|^{1/2}$ (the Van Vleck-Morette determinant). The Van Vleck formula $K_{\rm WKB} = \sqrt{-\partial^2 S_{\rm cl}/(2\pi i\hbar\,\partial x_f\partial x_i)}\,e^{iS_{\rm cl}/\hbar}$ is exact for quadratic Lagrangians (free particle, harmonic oscillator, particle in a uniform field) and asymptotic for general potentials.

**Maslov indices.** At conjugate points — where $\partial^2 S_{\rm cl}/\partial x_f\partial x_i = 0$ — the Van Vleck formula breaks down and the propagator acquires a phase correction (a Maslov index of $\pi/2$ per conjugate point). The corrected formula is $K = \sum_{\rm paths} \sqrt{|\partial^2 S_{\rm cl}/\partial x_f\partial x_i|/(2\pi i\hbar)}\,e^{iS_{\rm cl}/\hbar - i\pi \mu/2}$ where $\mu$ is the Maslov index of the path. This sum-over-classical-paths formula is the exact semiclassical propagator.

### Instantons and tunnelling

For a particle in a double-well potential $V(x) = \lambda(x^2 - a^2)^2$, the two minima at $x = \pm a$ are separated by a barrier. Classically, a particle at rest in one well stays there forever. Quantum mechanically, it tunnels.

In the Euclidean path integral, the tunnelling amplitude is dominated by **instantons** (also called *bounces*): finite-action classical solutions of the Euclidean equations of motion $m\,d^2 x/d\tau^2 = V'(x)$ (note: no minus sign on the potential, since the Euclidean action has $+V$). The instanton solution connecting $-a$ to $+a$ is

$$
x_{\rm inst}(\tau) \;=\; a\,\tanh\!\left(\omega(\tau - \tau_0)/2\right)
$$

where $\omega = \sqrt{8\lambda a^2/m}$ and $\tau_0$ is the instanton centre (a free parameter from time-translation invariance). The Euclidean action of the instanton is $S_0 = m\omega a^2 \cdot \text{const}$, and the tunnelling amplitude is $\sim e^{-S_0/\hbar}$.

The instanton contributes to the path integral as a saddle point in the Euclidean functional integral. The fluctuation analysis around the instanton has a zero mode (from the $\tau_0$ translation symmetry, handled by the Faddeev-Popov method — replace the collective coordinate $\tau_0$ by the instanton position) and a discrete negative mode (the bounce mode, signalling that the instanton is a saddle, not a minimum, of the Euclidean action).

Multi-instanton configurations (a sequence of instantons and anti-instantons) sum to give the ground-state energy splitting

$$
\Delta E \;=\; \hbar\omega\,\sqrt{\frac{S_0}{2\pi\hbar}}\;e^{-S_0/\hbar}\,(1 + O(\hbar)),
$$

which is the characteristic exponentially-small tunnelling splitting. The same instanton technology applies to vacuum decay in quantum field theory (false-vacuum tunnelling, Coleman 1977) and to monopole and sphaleron processes in gauge theories.

### Coherent-state and phase-space path integrals

The configuration-space path integral above uses position eigenstates. An alternative uses coherent states $|z\rangle = e^{-|z|^2/2}e^{z\hat{a}^\dagger}|0\rangle$ for the harmonic oscillator, with $z = (q + ip)/\sqrt{2\hbar}$. The coherent-state path integral is

$$
K(z_f, t_f;\, z_i, t_i) \;=\; \int \mathcal{D}z\,\mathcal{D}\bar{z}\;\exp\!\left[\frac{i}{\hbar}\int_{t_i}^{t_f}\left(\frac{i\hbar}{2}(\bar{z}\dot{z} - z\dot{\bar{z}}) - H(z, \bar{z})\right)dt\right],
$$

where the boundary terms are handled by the overlap $\langle z|z'\rangle = e^{-|z-z'|^2/2}$ and the first term in the exponent is the **Berry phase** (symplectic potential) arising from the non-orthogonality of coherent states. This form generalises naturally to spin systems (spin-coherent-state path integral with a Wess-Zumino term) and to field-theoretic coherent states.

## Connections [Master]

- **Action principle [09.02.01].** The classical limit of the path integral ($\hbar \to 0$) recovers the principle of least action. The stationary-phase condition $\delta S = 0$ is exactly the condition that constructive interference selects the classical path. The path integral makes the action principle look like an emergent property of quantum interference rather than a fundamental law.

- **Time evolution [12.03.01].** The propagator $K = \langle x_f|e^{-iHt/\hbar}|x_i\rangle$ is the time-evolution operator in the position basis. The path integral provides an explicit construction of this operator without diagonalising $H$.

- **Quantum harmonic oscillator [12.04.02].** The QHO path integral is exactly solvable because the action is quadratic. The result is the Mehler kernel, and the energy spectrum $E_n = \hbar\omega(n+\tfrac{1}{2})$ can be extracted by expanding in energy eigenstates. The QHO is the testing ground for every path-integral technique.

- **Partition function and Wick rotation [11.04.01].** The Euclidean path integral over periodic paths with period $\beta\hbar$ equals the partition function $Z = \mathrm{Tr}\,e^{-\beta\hat{H}}$. This connection makes the path integral the unifying formalism for quantum mechanics and statistical mechanics.

- **Hamilton's equations [09.04.02].** The classical paths that dominate the semiclassical path integral satisfy the Euler-Lagrange equations, which are equivalent to Hamilton's equations on phase space. The Van Vleck determinant involves the second mixed derivative of the classical action, which is the inverse of the symplectic two-form restricted to the Lagrangian submanifold.

- **Hamilton-Jacobi equation [09.05.01].** The phase $S_{\rm cl}/\hbar$ of the semiclassical propagator satisfies the Hamilton-Jacobi equation. The WKB approximation to the Schrodinger wave function is the semiclassical path integral with the prefactor given by transport equations along the classical trajectories.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Dirac first identified the Lagrangian's role in quantum mechanics in 1933, in a paper that went largely unnoticed. He observed that the transformation function $\langle q_{t+dt}|q_t\rangle$ is proportional to $e^{iL\,dt/\hbar}$ and speculated that the full transition amplitude might involve "some kind of integral over all paths." Dirac did not pursue this; the calculation was too technically demanding for the tools of the time.

Fifteen years later, Feynman — a graduate student at Princeton under Wheeler — picked up Dirac's remark and developed it into a complete formulation of non-relativistic quantum mechanics. His 1948 paper in *Reviews of Modern Physics*, *Space-Time Approach to Non-Relativistic Quantum Mechanics* [ref: TODO_REF Feynman 1948], defined the path integral by time-slicing, derived the free-particle and harmonic-oscillator propagators, and proved equivalence with the Schrodinger equation. The paper is a landmark of 20th-century physics: it recast quantum mechanics in a manifestly spacetime-based, Lagrangian language, making the connection to classical mechanics transparent and opening the door to quantum field theory through functional integrals over fields.

Feynman and Hibbs' *Quantum Mechanics and Path Integrals* (1965, McGraw-Hill) [ref: TODO_REF Feynman & Hibbs 1965] was the first textbook treatment. It remains the standard reference for the non-relativistic theory, despite being unfinished (Feynman's notes were assembled by Hibbs and later corrected by Styer in the 2005 Dover edition).

The mathematical status of the path integral remains subtle. The Minkowski-signature (real-time) path integral is not defined as a Lebesgue integral — the oscillatory integrand prevents this. Constructive quantum field theory (Glimm, Jaffe, and others, 1960s-1980s) succeeded in making path integrals rigorous for spacetime dimensions $d \leq 2$ (and partially for $d = 3$) using the Euclidean (Wick-rotated) formulation and the Wiener measure. The physically relevant case $d = 4$ remains open and is related to one of the Clay Millennium Prize Problems (Yang-Mills existence and mass gap). The path integral's mathematical status is thus: an extraordinarily powerful heuristic that produces correct answers verified by canonical methods, whose rigorous underpinning is incomplete at the frontier.

Philosophically, the path integral makes quantum mechanics look like a *generalised* classical mechanics — not a replacement, but an extension in which the classical action principle is the saddle point of a sum over histories. This perspective reduces the distance between classical and quantum physics and makes the correspondence principle look like a consequence of wave interference rather than an independent postulate.
