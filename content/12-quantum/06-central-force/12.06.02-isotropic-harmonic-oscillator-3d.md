## Intuition [Beginner]

A mass held by three identical springs, one along each Cartesian axis, oscillates in three dimensions. Each axis acts as an independent one-dimensional harmonic oscillator (unit 12.04.02) with the same frequency $\omega$. The potential energy is $V(x,y,z) = \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2(x^2 + y^2 + z^2) = \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 r^2$, depending only on the distance $r$ from the origin. This is the **isotropic** (equal in all directions) three-dimensional harmonic oscillator.

Because each axis is independent, the total energy is the sum of three one-dimensional oscillator energies: $E = E_{n_x} + E_{n_y} + E_{n_z}$ with each $E_{n_i} = \hbar\omega(n_i + 1/2)$. Combining, the energy is $E_N = \hbar\omega(N + 3/2)$ where $N = n_x + n_y + n_z = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ is the total quantum number. The spacing between adjacent levels is $\hbar\omega$, the same as in one dimension. The ground-state energy is $3\hbar\omega/2$, three times the zero-point energy of one oscillator — the particle cannot rest at the origin in any of the three directions.

The ground state is unique: $n_x = n_y = n_z = 0$. The first excited level ($N = 1$) has three states: the energy quantum can sit on the $x$-axis, the $y$-axis, or the $z$-axis. This is a **three-fold degeneracy**. For $N = 2$ there are six states. The pattern is the number of ways to distribute $N$ indistinguishable quanta among three bins, which is $(N+1)(N+2)/2$. This degeneracy grows quadratically with $N$, much faster than the hydrogen atom's linear degeneracy $n^2$, and the two systems have fundamentally different symmetry origins.

The degeneracy is **accidental** in the sense that it exceeds what rotational symmetry alone predicts. A generic central potential $V(r)$ produces energies that depend on both a radial quantum number and the angular momentum $\ell$. The isotropic oscillator has the same energy for states with different $\ell$ values, a consequence of the larger SU(3) symmetry group hidden in the Hamiltonian.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Ground-state energy.** The lowest energy occurs when all three quantum numbers are zero:

$$E_0 = \hbar\omega(0 + 1/2) + \hbar\omega(0 + 1/2) + \hbar\omega(0 + 1/2) = \frac{3\hbar\omega}{2}.$$

Each axis contributes its own zero-point energy $\hbar\omega/2$.

**Degeneracy of the $N = 3$ level.** The formula $(N+1)(N+2)/2$ gives $(4)(5)/2 = 10$ states. Verify by listing: distribute 3 quanta among 3 bins. The configurations are (300), (030), (003), (210), (201), (120), (021), (102), (012), (111) — ten in total, matching the formula.

**Transition energy.** A particle in the $N = 2$ level emits a photon and drops to $N = 1$. The photon energy is $E_2 - E_1 = \hbar\omega(2 + 3/2) - \hbar\omega(1 + 3/2) = \hbar\omega$. The transition energy is always $\hbar\omega$ between adjacent levels, regardless of $N$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A particle of mass $m$ moves in three dimensions under the potential

$$
V(r) = \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 r^2,
$$

where $r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}$ and $\omega > 0$ is the oscillator frequency. The Hilbert space is $\mathcal{H} = L^2(\mathbb{R}^3)$. The Hamiltonian is

$$
\hat H = \frac{\hat p_x^2 + \hat p_y^2 + \hat p_z^2}{2m} + \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2(x^2 + y^2 + z^2).
$$

### Cartesian separation

Because $V$ is a sum of three independent terms, the Hamiltonian separates as $\hat H = \hat H_x + \hat H_y + \hat H_z$ where each $\hat H_i$ is a one-dimensional harmonic oscillator Hamiltonian. The energy eigenstates are products

$$
\psi_{n_x n_y n_z}(x,y,z) = \psi_{n_x}(x)\,\psi_{n_y}(y)\,\psi_{n_z}(z),
$$

where $\psi_{n_i}$ is the $n_i$-th eigenstate of the 1D oscillator (unit 12.04.02). The total energy is

$$
\boxed{E_N = \hbar\omega\!\left(N + \tfrac{3}{2}\right), \qquad N = n_x + n_y + n_z = 0, 1, 2, \ldots}
$$

The **degeneracy** — the number of ordered triples $(n_x, n_y, n_z)$ with $n_x + n_y + n_z = N$ — is

$$
g(N) = \frac{(N+1)(N+2)}{2}.
$$

This is a standard combinatorial result: the number of ways to place $N$ identical objects in 3 ordered bins is $\binom{N+2}{2} = (N+1)(N+2)/2$.

The Cartesian basis states are eigenstates of $\hat H_x$, $\hat H_y$, $\hat H_z$ individually, and therefore also eigenstates of the total Hamiltonian. They are not, however, eigenstates of $\hat L^2$ or $\hat L_z$ in general.

### Spherical separation

Since $V(r)$ depends only on $r$, the Hamiltonian commutes with $\hat{\mathbf{L}}^2$ and $\hat L_z$, and simultaneous eigenstates have the form

$$
\psi_{n_r \ell m}(r, \theta, \varphi) = R_{n_r \ell}(r)\,Y_\ell^m(\theta, \varphi),
$$

where $n_r = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ is the radial quantum number (number of radial nodes), $\ell = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ is the orbital angular momentum, and $m = -\ell, \ldots, +\ell$. Substituting into the Schrodinger equation and introducing $u(r) = rR(r)$ gives the radial equation

$$
-\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\frac{d^2 u}{dr^2} + \left[\frac{\hbar^2 \ell(\ell+1)}{2mr^2} + \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 r^2\right]u = E\,u, \qquad u(0) = 0.
$$

The effective potential is the centrifugal barrier $\hbar^2\ell(\ell+1)/(2mr^2)$ plus the parabolic well $\tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 r^2$. Introducing dimensionless variables $\rho = r/\ell_{\text{osc}}$ where $\ell_{\text{osc}} = \sqrt{\hbar/(m\omega)}$ is the oscillator length, and $\varepsilon = E/(\hbar\omega)$, the radial equation becomes

$$
\frac{d^2 u}{d\rho^2} - \left[\frac{\ell(\ell+1)}{\rho^2} + \rho^2 - 2\varepsilon\right]u = 0.
$$

For large $\rho$, the equation gives $u \sim \rho^{n_r}e^{-\rho^2/2}$ (Gaussian decay, in contrast to hydrogen's exponential decay $e^{-\rho}$). Writing $u(\rho) = \rho^{\ell+1}e^{-\rho^2/2}v(\rho)$ and substituting yields an equation solved by the **associated Laguerre polynomials** $L_{n_r}^{\ell+1/2}(\rho^2)$. Normalisability requires $n_r$ to be a non-negative integer, and the energy eigenvalues are

$$
\boxed{E_{n_r, \ell} = \hbar\omega\!\left(2n_r + \ell + \tfrac{3}{2}\right).}
$$

The quantum number $N = 2n_r + \ell$ labels the energy level. For a given $N$, the angular momentum takes values $\ell = N, N-2, N-4, \ldots, 0$ or $1$ (same parity as $N$). The radial quantum number is $n_r = (N - \ell)/2$. Summing the degeneracy from the spherical side:

$$
g(N) = \sum_{\ell} (2\ell + 1) = \sum_{\substack{\ell = 0 \\ \ell \equiv N \pmod{2}}}^{N} (2\ell + 1) = \frac{(N+1)(N+2)}{2},
$$

recovering the Cartesian result from a completely different decomposition.

The normalised radial wave functions are

$$
R_{n_r \ell}(r) = \sqrt{\frac{2\,n_r!}{\ell_{\text{osc}}^3\,\Gamma(n_r + \ell + 3/2)}}\;\left(\frac{r}{\ell_{\text{osc}}}\right)^{\!\ell}\;e^{-r^2/(2\ell_{\text{osc}}^2)}\;L_{n_r}^{\ell+1/2}\!\!\left(\frac{r^2}{\ell_{\text{osc}}^2}\right),
$$

where $\Gamma$ is the gamma function. For the ground state ($n_r = 0$, $\ell = 0$): $R_{00}(r) = (2/(\pi^{1/2}\ell_{\text{osc}}^3))^{1/2}\,e^{-r^2/(2\ell_{\text{osc}}^2)}$, a three-dimensional Gaussian.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The ground state of the 3D oscillator has $\ell = 0$ and energy $3\hbar\omega/2$, not $\hbar\omega/2$. The 1D ground-state energy is a component, not the total.
- The energy depends on $N = 2n_r + \ell$, not on $n_r$ and $\ell$ independently. States with the same $N$ but different $(n_r, \ell)$ are degenerate. This is the accidental degeneracy.
- The Gaussian decay $e^{-r^2/(2\ell_{\text{osc}}^2)}$ is faster than hydrogen's exponential $e^{-r/(na_0)}$ at large $r$. The oscillator confines the particle more tightly than the Coulomb potential.
- The parity constraint $\ell \equiv N \pmod{2}$ means that even-$N$ levels contain only even $\ell$ (s, d, g, ...) and odd-$N$ levels contain only odd $\ell$ (p, f, h, ...). This follows from the wave function's behaviour under $\mathbf{r} \to -\mathbf{r}$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Spectrum and degeneracy of the 3D isotropic harmonic oscillator).** *The Hamiltonian $\hat H = \hat{\mathbf{p}}^2/(2m) + \tfrac{1}{2}m\omega^2 r^2$ on $L^2(\mathbb{R}^3)$ has pure point spectrum $E_N = \hbar\omega(N + 3/2)$ for $N = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ with degeneracy $(N+1)(N+2)/2$. The Cartesian eigenstates $\psi_{n_x n_y n_z} = \psi_{n_x}(x)\psi_{n_y}(y)\psi_{n_z}(z)$ with $n_x + n_y + n_z = N$ and the spherical eigenstates $\psi_{n_r \ell m} = R_{n_r \ell}(r)Y_\ell^m(\theta,\varphi)$ with $2n_r + \ell = N$ are two complete bases for the $N$-th energy eigenspace, related by a unitary change of basis.*

**Proof.**

*Step 1: Cartesian separation.* Write $\hat H = \hat H_x + \hat H_y + \hat H_z$. The three operators commute: $[\hat H_x, \hat H_y] = 0$ etc. because they depend on different coordinates. The eigenvalues are sums: $E = E_{n_x} + E_{n_y} + E_{n_z} = \hbar\omega(n_x + n_y + n_z + 3/2) = \hbar\omega(N + 3/2)$.

*Step 2: Degeneracy count.* The number of non-negative integer solutions to $n_x + n_y + n_z = N$ is the number of ways to insert 2 dividers among $N + 2$ positions: $\binom{N+2}{2} = (N+1)(N+2)/2$.

*Step 3: Spherical separation.* In spherical coordinates, substituting $\psi = R(r)Y_\ell^m(\theta,\varphi)$ and using the Laplacian decomposition produces the radial equation for $u = rR$. The asymptotic analysis gives $u \sim e^{-\rho^2/2}$ for large $\rho$ (from the $\rho^2$ term in the potential) and $u \sim \rho^{\ell+1}$ for small $\rho$ (from the centrifugal barrier). Writing $u = \rho^{\ell+1}e^{-\rho^2/2}v(\rho^2)$ with $\rho = r/\ell_{\text{osc}}$ and converting to the variable $s = \rho^2$ yields

$$
s\frac{d^2v}{ds^2} + \left(\ell + \tfrac{3}{2} - s\right)\frac{dv}{ds} + n_r\,v = 0,
$$

the associated Laguerre equation. Normalisability requires $n_r$ to be a non-negative integer, giving $E = \hbar\omega(2n_r + \ell + 3/2)$.

*Step 4: Equality of degeneracy.* For energy $E_N = \hbar\omega(N + 3/2)$, the constraint $2n_r + \ell = N$ restricts $\ell$ to values with the same parity as $N$, ranging from $0$ (or $1$) up to $N$. Summing:

$$
\sum_{\substack{\ell=0 \\ \ell \equiv N \pmod{2}}}^{N}(2\ell+1) = \frac{(N+1)(N+2)}{2},
$$

matching the Cartesian count. Both bases span the same subspace of dimension $(N+1)(N+2)/2$. $\square$

**Corollary (Parity of oscillator eigenstates).** *The $N$-th energy eigenspace has definite parity $(-1)^N$ under $\mathbf{r} \to -\mathbf{r}$. In the spherical decomposition, only angular momenta $\ell$ with the same parity as $N$ appear.*

**Bridge.** The two bases — Cartesian $(n_x, n_y, n_z)$ and spherical $(n_r, \ell, m)$ — describe the same eigenspace from different perspectives. The Cartesian basis diagonalises the three independent oscillator number operators; the spherical basis diagonalises $\hat{\mathbf{L}}^2$ and $\hat L_z$. The existence of multiple complete bases for each degenerate eigenspace is a direct signature of the enlarged SU(3) dynamical symmetry, developed in the Master tier. The result also builds toward the nuclear shell model, where nucleons fill the oscillator shells $N = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ with the degeneracy $(N+1)(N+2)/2$ determining the magic numbers before spin-orbit splitting is introduced.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

The 3D oscillator has nine operators $a_i^\dagger a_j$ (for $i, j = x, y, z$). Show that these commute with $\hat H$. Explain why this implies that the symmetry group of the 3D isotropic oscillator is larger than SO(3).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each $a_i^\dagger a_j$ changes one quantum from direction $j$ to direction $i$ (or vice versa), preserving $N = n_x + n_y + n_z$. Since $E_N$ depends only on $N$, these operators preserve the energy.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$[\hat H, a_i^\dagger a_j] = [\sum_k \hbar\omega(a_k^\dagger a_k + 1/2),\, a_i^\dagger a_j]$. Using $[a_k^\dagger a_k, a_i^\dagger a_j] = \delta_{ki}a_k^\dagger a_j - \delta_{kj}a_i^\dagger a_k$:

$\sum_k [a_k^\dagger a_k, a_i^\dagger a_j] = a_i^\dagger a_j - a_i^\dagger a_j = 0$.

So $a_i^\dagger a_j$ commutes with $N = \sum_k a_k^\dagger a_k$ and hence with $\hat H = \hbar\omega(N + 3/2)$. These nine operators form the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{u}(3)$, with the traceless part generating $\mathfrak{su}(3)$. Rotational SO(3) is generated by the three antisymmetric combinations $a_i^\dagger a_j - a_j^\dagger a_i$ (proportional to $L_k$). The six symmetric traceless combinations $a_i^\dagger a_j + a_j^\dagger a_i - \frac{2}{3}\delta_{ij}N$ are additional conserved quantities absent for a generic central potential, confirming that the symmetry group SU(3) is strictly larger than SO(3).

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).**

Prove that the parity constraint $\ell \equiv N \pmod{2}$ follows from the energy formula $N = 2n_r + \ell$ and the requirement that $n_r$ is a non-negative integer.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From $N = 2n_r + \ell$, we have $\ell = N - 2n_r$. Since $n_r$ is a non-negative integer, $2n_r$ is an even integer. Therefore $\ell$ and $N$ differ by an even number: $\ell \equiv N \pmod{2}$.

Consequences:
- Even $N$: only even $\ell$ ($0, 2, 4, \ldots, N$) appear.
- Odd $N$: only odd $\ell$ ($1, 3, 5, \ldots, N$) appear.

This parity restriction has a physical origin: the oscillator potential $V \propto r^2$ is even under parity, so energy eigenstates have definite parity $(-1)^\ell$. The Cartesian states have parity $(-1)^N$ (each quantum in any direction flips the sign once). Consistency demands $(-1)^\ell = (-1)^N$, giving $\ell \equiv N \pmod{2}$.

## Advanced results [Master]

### The SU(3) dynamical symmetry

The accidental degeneracy of the isotropic oscillator — the independence of energy from $\ell$ — is explained by a dynamical symmetry larger than the geometric rotation group SO(3). Define three sets of ladder operators:

$$
a_i = \sqrt{\frac{m\omega}{2\hbar}}\left(x_i + \frac{ip_i}{m\omega}\right), \qquad a_i^\dagger = \sqrt{\frac{m\omega}{2\hbar}}\left(x_i - \frac{ip_i}{m\omega}\right), \qquad i = x, y, z.
$$

These satisfy $[a_i, a_j^\dagger] = \delta_{ij}$ and $[a_i, a_j] = 0$. The Hamiltonian is $\hat H = \hbar\omega(\sum_i a_i^\dagger a_i + 3/2)$. The nine operators

$$
T_{ij} = a_i^\dagger a_j, \qquad i, j = x, y, z,
$$

all commute with $\hat H$ (they redistribute quanta among the three directions without changing $N$). Their commutation relations

$$
[T_{ij}, T_{kl}] = T_{il}\delta_{jk} - T_{kj}\delta_{li}
$$

are those of $\mathfrak{gl}(3)$. The trace $T = \sum_i T_{ii} = N$ (the number operator) generates a $\mathfrak{u}(1)$ factor, and the eight traceless combinations generate $\mathfrak{su}(3)$.

The three angular momentum operators are the antisymmetric combinations $L_k = -i\hbar(a_i^\dagger a_j - a_j^\dagger a_i)$ (with $(i,j,k)$ cyclic), generating the $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ subalgebra of $\mathfrak{su}(3)$. The five remaining independent operators are the symmetric traceless combinations, which are the quadrupole operators $Q_{ij} = a_i^\dagger a_j + a_j^\dagger a_i - \frac{2}{3}\delta_{ij}N$. These connect states of different $\ell$ within the same $N$-shell.

The $N$-th energy eigenspace carries the symmetric representation $[N, 0, 0]$ of SU(3), which decomposes under the SO(3) subgroup as

$$
[N, 0, 0] \big|_{\mathrm{SO}(3)} = \bigoplus_{\substack{\ell = 0 \\ \ell \equiv N \pmod{2}}}^{N} D_\ell.
$$

This Clebsch-Gordan decomposition is exactly the pattern $\ell = N, N-2, \ldots, 0$ or $1$ seen in the spherical decomposition. The dimension of $[N, 0, 0]$ is $(N+1)(N+2)/2$, recovering the degeneracy.

### Comparison with hydrogen atom degeneracy

Both the 3D oscillator and the hydrogen atom have accidental degeneracy beyond what SO(3) requires, but the patterns differ fundamentally.

| Property | Hydrogen atom | 3D isotropic oscillator |
|----------|--------------|------------------------|
| Energy levels | $E_n = -13.6/n^2$ | $E_N = \hbar\omega(N + 3/2)$ |
| Level spacing | Decreases ($\sim 1/n^3$) | Constant ($\hbar\omega$) |
| Degeneracy | $n^2$ | $(N+1)(N+2)/2$ |
| Symmetry group | SO(4) | SU(3) |
| Hidden conserved quantity | Runge-Lenz vector | Quadrupole tensor $T_{ij}$ |
| Allowed $\ell$ per level | $0, 1, \ldots, n-1$ | Same parity as $N$ |

The hydrogen atom has SO(4) symmetry because the $1/r$ potential conserves the Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector. The oscillator has SU(3) symmetry because the $r^2$ potential conserves the quadrupole operators $a_i^\dagger a_j$. These are the only two central potentials in three dimensions with dynamical symmetries larger than SO(3) — a consequence of the Bertrand theorem, which states that the only central potentials producing closed orbits for all bound states are $1/r$ and $r^2$.

The degeneracy growth also differs: hydrogen's $n^2$ grows quadratically, while the oscillator's $(N+1)(N+2)/2 \sim N^2/2$ also grows quadratically but with half the coefficient. For a given quantum number, hydrogen has approximately twice the degeneracy because it allows all $\ell$ values up to $n - 1$, whereas the oscillator restricts $\ell$ to the same parity as $N$.

### The radial wave functions and associated Laguerre polynomials

The radial equation for $u(r) = rR(r)$ with the substitution $\rho = r/\ell_{\text{osc}}$ becomes

$$
\frac{d^2u}{d\rho^2} = \left[\frac{\ell(\ell+1)}{\rho^2} + \rho^2 - (2N + 3)\right]u.
$$

The substitution $u(\rho) = \rho^{\ell+1}e^{-\rho^2/2}v(\rho)$ and the change of variable $s = \rho^2$ transform this into the associated Laguerre equation

$$
s\frac{d^2v}{ds^2} + (\ell + 3/2 - s)\frac{dv}{ds} + n_r\,v = 0,
$$

whose polynomial solutions are $L_{n_r}^{\ell+1/2}(s)$. The appearance of the half-integer parameter $\ell + 1/2$ (as opposed to the integer parameter $2\ell + 1$ in hydrogen) reflects the different asymptotic behaviour: Gaussian decay ($e^{-\rho^2}$) for the oscillator versus exponential decay ($e^{-\rho}$) for hydrogen.

The first few radial functions (for $\ell = 0$):

$$
R_{0,0}(r) \propto e^{-r^2/(2\ell_{\text{osc}}^2)}, \qquad R_{1,0}(r) \propto \left(\frac{3}{2} - \frac{r^2}{\ell_{\text{osc}}^2}\right)e^{-r^2/(2\ell_{\text{osc}}^2)}.
$$

For $\ell = 1$:

$$
R_{0,1}(r) \propto \frac{r}{\ell_{\text{osc}}}\,e^{-r^2/(2\ell_{\text{osc}}^2)}.
$$

The radial functions are closely related to the radial distributions of the hydrogen atom, but the Gaussian envelope $e^{-r^2/(2\ell_{\text{osc}}^2)}$ produces a qualitatively different confinement profile.

### Application to the nuclear shell model

The isotropic harmonic oscillator potential is the starting point for the nuclear shell model (Mayer 1949, Haxel, Jensen, and Suess 1949). Nucleons (protons and neutrons) in a nucleus experience a mean-field potential approximated by a harmonic well. The oscillator shells and their degeneracies are:

| $N$ | $\ell$ values | Spatial degeneracy $(N+1)(N+2)/2$ | Cumulative (with spin) |
|-----|---------------|-----------------------------------|------------------------|
| 0 | 0 | 1 | 2 |
| 1 | 1 | 3 | 8 |
| 2 | 0, 2 | 6 | 20 |
| 3 | 1, 3 | 10 | 40 |
| 4 | 0, 2, 4 | 15 | 70 |

Including spin degeneracy (factor of 2 per spatial state), the cumulative occupancies are 2, 8, 20, 40, 70. The first three — 2, 8, 20 — are magic numbers for light nuclei, corresponding to particularly stable nuclear configurations (analogous to noble gas electron configurations in atomic physics).

The magic numbers 28, 50, 82, 126 observed in heavier nuclei do not match the pure oscillator prediction. They are recovered by adding a spin-orbit coupling term $V_{\text{so}} \propto \hat{\mathbf{L}} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{S}}$ to the oscillator potential, which splits each $(n_r, \ell)$ level into two sub-shells with $j = \ell \pm 1/2$. The spin-orbit splitting grows with $\ell$ and reshuffles the level ordering, producing the observed magic numbers. The oscillator + spin-orbit model is the nuclear analogue of the hydrogen + fine-structure treatment in atomic physics.

### Coherent states in three dimensions

The 3D coherent state is the tensor product of three 1D coherent states:

$$
|\boldsymbol{\alpha}\rangle = |\alpha_x\rangle \otimes |\alpha_y\rangle \otimes |\alpha_z\rangle = D(\boldsymbol{\alpha})|0\rangle,
$$

where $\boldsymbol{\alpha} = (\alpha_x, \alpha_y, \alpha_z) \in \mathbb{C}^3$ and $D(\boldsymbol{\alpha}) = \exp(\sum_i \alpha_i a_i^\dagger - \alpha_i^* a_i)$ is the 3D displacement operator. Each component satisfies $a_i|\boldsymbol{\alpha}\rangle = \alpha_i|\boldsymbol{\alpha}\rangle$. In position space the wave function is a displaced 3D Gaussian that oscillates along the classical trajectory $\mathbf{r}(t) = \sqrt{2\hbar/(m\omega)}\;\text{Re}(\boldsymbol{\alpha}\,e^{-i\omega t})$, maintaining its shape without spreading.

The SU(3) symmetry acts on coherent states by unitary transformations in the complex $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$-space, preserving the total photon number $|\boldsymbol{\alpha}|^2$ while redistributing the displacement among the three axes.

**Synthesis.** The 3D isotropic harmonic oscillator is the second great example (after hydrogen) of a quantum system whose hidden dynamical symmetry produces accidental degeneracy. The SU(3) symmetry, generated by the nine bilinear operators $a_i^\dagger a_j$, explains why all $(N+1)(N+2)/2$ states with the same $N$ share the same energy regardless of their angular momentum. The two natural bases — Cartesian (separated by axis) and spherical (separated by $r$ and angular variables) — are related by a unitary change of basis within each energy eigenspace, and the Clebsch-Gordan decomposition of the SU(3) representation $[N,0,0]$ under the SO(3) subgroup reproduces the angular momentum content $\ell = N, N-2, \ldots$. This structure, together with the spin-orbit extension, forms the theoretical foundation of the nuclear shell model and provides the conceptual bridge between the 1D oscillator algebra (unit 12.04.02) and the many-body physics of nuclei and quantum dots.

## Connections [Master]

The 3D isotropic harmonic oscillator connects to a wide range of physical systems across physics and chemistry.

In nuclear physics, the oscillator potential is the zeroth-order mean-field model for nucleons. The shell structure with cumulative occupancies 2, 8, 20, 40, 70 is the starting point for the nuclear shell model (Mayer, Jensen, Haxel, and Suess, 1949; Nobel Prize 1963). The observed magic numbers 2, 8, 20, 28, 50, 82, 126 are recovered by adding a strong spin-orbit coupling that splits each oscillator shell. The oscillator + spin-orbit Hamiltonian is the nuclear analogue of the hydrogen atom with fine structure.

In atomic physics, the 3D oscillator describes quantum dots (artificial atoms) where electrons are confined in all three dimensions by semiconductor heterostructures. The confinement potential is approximately parabolic, and the energy levels and degeneracies are those of the 3D oscillator. The shell structure of quantum dots — with filling at 2, 8, 20 electrons — mirrors the nuclear magic numbers and produces "artificial atoms" whose properties can be tuned by adjusting the dot size and shape.

In molecular physics, the small-amplitude vibrations of polyatomic molecules are decomposed into normal modes, each an independent 1D oscillator. When several modes have the same frequency (degenerate vibrations), the system is described by an isotropic multidimensional oscillator, and the degeneracy follows the same combinatorial formula.

The mathematical structure — the SU(3) Lie algebra generated by bilinear products of creation and annihilation operators — appears identically in the quark model of hadrons (Gell-Mann, 1961; Nobel Prize 1969). The meson and baryon multiplets are SU(3) representations, and the oscillator wave functions provide an explicit spatial realisation of the same group theory. The connection between nuclear shell structure and the quark model is that both use SU(3) as the organising symmetry, though for different physical reasons (dynamical symmetry in the oscillator case, flavour symmetry in the quark case).

The comparison with hydrogen (unit 12.06.01) is instructive. Both systems have accidental degeneracy, but from different symmetry groups: SO(4) for hydrogen (conserving the Runge-Lenz vector) and SU(3) for the oscillator (conserving the quadrupole tensor). Bertrand's theorem identifies these as the only two central potentials with closed orbits and hence with dynamical symmetries beyond SO(3). The general lesson is that degeneracy beyond what rotational symmetry requires signals a hidden symmetry, and identifying that symmetry is the key to understanding the spectrum.

## Historical notes [Master]

The three-dimensional harmonic oscillator was first studied in the context of the old quantum theory by Schrodinger (1926) as part of his wave-mechanics programme. The Cartesian separation was immediate and natural; the spherical solution in terms of associated Laguerre polynomials with half-integer parameters appeared somewhat later.

The SU(3) dynamical symmetry was identified by Jauch and Hill (1940) and developed systematically by Elliott (1958) in the context of nuclear physics. Elliott showed that the SU(3) classification of oscillator states provides a natural framework for understanding deformed nuclei and collective rotational bands. The Elliott SU(3) model remains a cornerstone of nuclear structure theory.

The nuclear shell model was proposed independently by Mayer (1949) and by Haxel, Jensen, and Suess (1949). The key insight — that a strong spin-orbit coupling is needed to reproduce the observed magic numbers — was initially surprising because the spin-orbit coupling in atomic physics is a small relativistic correction. In nuclei the spin-orbit term is large and attractive, fundamentally reshaping the level ordering. Mayer and Jensen shared the 1963 Nobel Prize in Physics for this work.

The connection to the quark model (Gell-Mann 1961, Ne'eman 1961) is a mathematical parallel rather than a physical identity: the same SU(3) representation theory classifies both nuclear oscillator shells and hadron multiplets, but the quantum numbers have different physical meanings (spatial excitation versus flavour).
