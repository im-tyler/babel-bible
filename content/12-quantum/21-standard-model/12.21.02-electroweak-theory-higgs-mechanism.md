## Intuition [Beginner]

Electromagnetism and the weak force look like two unrelated forces at everyday energies. Electromagnetism is carried by the photon, a massless particle that travels at the speed of light and so reaches across the whole universe. The weak force is carried by the $W$ and $Z$ bosons, which are heavy — about $80$ and $91$ times the proton mass — and so drag through space, able to act only across a distance much smaller than an atomic nucleus. Glashow, Weinberg, and Salam proposed in the 1960s that these are really two faces of a single electroweak force, and that the whole difference comes from one field that fills all of space.

That field is the Higgs field. It is a scalar field, meaning it has no spin and no preferred direction, and it takes a constant nonzero value everywhere in the universe, about $246$ GeV. This background value, called the vacuum expectation value, breaks the electroweak symmetry. The $W$ and $Z$ bosons, moving through this background, interact with it and acquire mass. The photon does not couple to the Higgs background, because the background carries no electric charge, and the photon stays massless. The photon is the carrier of the one symmetry left unbroken — ordinary electromagnetism.

One number ties the strengths of electromagnetism and the weak force together: the electroweak mixing angle $\theta_W$. This angle measures how the two original neutral fields blend to produce the observed photon and $Z$ boson. It also links the two heavy boson masses through $m_W = m_Z \cos\theta_W$. The electroweak scale $246$ GeV itself is measured not from a collider directly but from the lifetime of the muon, which decays through the weak force.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Problem.** The electroweak scale $v$ is fixed by the measured muon lifetime. The muon decays through the weak force as $\mu \to e + \nu_\mu + \bar\nu_e$, and the strength of this decay is summarised by the Fermi constant, $G_F = 1.16638 \times 10^{-5}$ GeV$^{-2}$. The tree-level electroweak relation between $G_F$ and $v$ is $G_F / \sqrt{2} = 1/(2 v^2)$. Use it to extract $v$ in GeV.

**Solution.**

Step 1. Rearrange the relation to isolate $v^2$. Multiplying both sides by $2 v^2$ and dividing by $G_F$ gives

$$v^2 = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2\, G_F} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}\, G_F}, \qquad v = \frac{1}{(\sqrt{2}\, G_F)^{1/2}}.$$

Step 2. Compute the number inside the square root. First $\sqrt{2} \times G_F = 1.41421 \times 1.16638 \times 10^{-5} = 1.64935 \times 10^{-5}$ GeV$^{-2}$.

Step 3. Invert and take the square root. The inverse is $1 / (1.64935 \times 10^{-5}) = 60\,630$ GeV$^2$, and the square root is $\sqrt{60\,630} = 246.23$ GeV.

**Reading the result.** The electroweak scale is $v = 246.22$ GeV. This single number, together with the mixing angle $\theta_W$, fixes the $W$ and $Z$ boson masses. The remarkable point is that a heavy particle collider is not the only way to weigh the electroweak scale: the lifetime of the muon, a particle that decays entirely through the weak force, already pins down $v$ to four significant figures. The muon is a built-in electroweak balance.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The full Standard Model gauge group and field content are stated in [12.21.01]; this unit isolates the electroweak factor $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ and develops what the overview states but does not prove: the extraction of the electroweak scale from low-energy data, the custodial symmetry that protects the mass relations, the gauge-boson self-couplings, and the origin of quark flavour mixing.

**Gauge group and chiral fermion content.** The electroweak gauge group is $G_{\mathrm{EW}} = SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$, with coupling constants $g$ and $g'$. The subscript $L$ records the chiral structure: $SU(2)_L$ acts only on left-handed fermion doublets. For one generation the fermion fields and their $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ quantum numbers are

$$Q_L = \begin{pmatrix} u_L \\ d_L \end{pmatrix} \sim (\mathbf{2}, +\tfrac{1}{3}), \quad u_R \sim (\mathbf{1}, +\tfrac{4}{3}), \quad d_R \sim (\mathbf{1}, -\tfrac{2}{3}), \quad L_L = \begin{pmatrix} \nu_L \\ e_L \end{pmatrix} \sim (\mathbf{2}, -1), \quad e_R \sim (\mathbf{1}, -2),$$

using the convention $Q = T_3 + Y/2$. Right-handed neutrinos are absent from the minimal model. The chiral assignment is what makes the theory *parity-violating* by construction [ref: TODO_REF Feynman-Gell-Mann 1958]: the left- and right-handed components sit in different representations, so a parity transformation maps the Lagrangian to a different theory.

**Covariant derivative and field strengths.** With $\sigma^i$ the Pauli matrices and $T_3 = \sigma^3/2$, the electroweak covariant derivative is

$$D_\mu = \partial_\mu - i g \tfrac{\sigma^i}{2} W^i_\mu - i g' \tfrac{Y}{2} B_\mu,$$

and the field strengths are $W^i_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu W^i_\nu - \partial_\nu W^i_\mu + g\, \varepsilon^{ijk} W^j_\mu W^k_\nu$ and $B_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu B_\nu - \partial_\nu B_\mu$. The $\varepsilon^{ijk}$ term is the signature of non-Abelian gauge structure: the $W$ bosons carry weak isospin and couple to themselves. These self-couplings are absent in pure QED and are the subject of the gauge-interaction analysis below.

**Higgs doublet and potential.** The model contains one complex scalar $SU(2)_L$ doublet

$$\Phi = \begin{pmatrix} \phi^+ \\ \phi^0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad Y_\Phi = +\tfrac{1}{2}, \qquad V(\Phi) = -\mu^2\, \Phi^\dagger \Phi + \lambda\, (\Phi^\dagger \Phi)^2, \qquad \lambda > 0,\ \mu^2 > 0.$$

The sign of $\mu^2$ is the whole point: the potential has a maximum at $\Phi = 0$ and a degenerate ring of minima at $\Phi^\dagger\Phi = \mu^2/(2\lambda)$. Choosing the vacuum $\langle\Phi\rangle = (0, v/\sqrt{2})^T$ with $v = \mu/\sqrt{\lambda}$ breaks $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y \to U(1)_{\mathrm{em}}$; the surviving generator is $Q = T_3 + Y/2$ because $Q\langle\Phi\rangle = 0$.

**Mass eigenstates and the mixing angle.** The charged bosons are $W^\pm_\mu = (W^1_\mu \mp i W^2_\mu)/\sqrt{2}$. The neutral bosons are defined by a single rotation through the Weinberg angle,

$$\begin{pmatrix} A_\mu \\ Z_\mu \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta_W & \sin\theta_W \\ -\sin\theta_W & \cos\theta_W \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} B_\mu \\ W^3_\mu \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \tan\theta_W = \frac{g'}{g}.$$

The photon $A_\mu$ is the gauge boson of the unbroken $U(1)_{\mathrm{em}}$ and stays massless; the $Z$ is the orthogonal combination and is massive. The tree-level masses $m_W = gv/2$ and $m_Z = v\sqrt{g^2 + g'^2}/2$ follow from the Higgs kinetic term and are derived in [12.21.01]; the present unit uses them as inputs and derives the *scale* $v$ itself from low-energy data, the consistency relation $\rho = 1$, and the self-interaction structure.

**Yukawa sector.** Fermion masses come from gauge-invariant couplings to the Higgs doublet and its conjugate $\tilde\Phi = i\sigma^2 \Phi^*$:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Yuk}} = - y^u_{ij}\, \bar Q_{Li}\, \tilde\Phi\, u_{Rj} - y^d_{ij}\, \bar Q_{Li}\, \Phi\, d_{Rj} - y^e_{ij}\, \bar L_{Li}\, \Phi\, e_{Rj} + \mathrm{h.c.}$$

After symmetry breaking each Yukawa matrix becomes a mass matrix $M^f = y^f v/\sqrt{2}$. Because $y^u, y^d, y^e$ are independent complex $3\times 3$ matrices, their diagonalisation by unitary rotations of the fermion fields is what produces the CKM flavour-mixing matrix in the charged current, developed in the Full proof set.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Electroweak-scale extraction from muon decay).** In the electroweak model the muon decay $\mu^- \to e^- + \bar\nu_e + \nu_\mu$ is mediated at tree level by $W$-boson exchange. At momentum transfers far below the $W$ mass, $|q^2| \ll m_W^2$, the exchange reduces to a pointlike four-fermion contact interaction parametrised by the Fermi constant $G_F$, with the matching

$$\frac{G_F}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{g^2}{8\, m_W^2} = \frac{1}{2 v^2},$$

and consequently the electroweak scale is $v = (\sqrt{2}\, G_F)^{-1/2} = 246.22$ GeV.

**Proof.** The charged-current interaction, read off from the covariant derivative acting on the left-handed doublets, is

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CC}} = -\frac{g}{\sqrt{2}} \bigl( W^+_\mu\, J^{\mu +} + W^-_\mu\, J^{\mu -}\bigr), \qquad J^{\mu +} = \bar\nu_{eL}\gamma^\mu e_L + \bar\nu_{\mu L}\gamma^\mu \mu_L + \cdots$$

The tree-level diagram for muon decay has the muon line emitting a $W^-$ that decays into $e^- \bar\nu_e$. The $W$ propagator in unitary gauge is $(-i/m_W^2)$ in the low-momentum limit $|q^2| \ll m_W^2$, since the propagator numerator $-i(g_{\mu\nu} - k_\mu k_\nu/m_W^2)/(k^2 - m_W^2)$ collapses to $i g_{\mu\nu}/m_W^2$ when $k^2 \to 0$. The amplitude becomes

$$\mathcal{M} = \left(-\frac{i g}{\sqrt{2}}\right)\left(-\frac{i g}{\sqrt{2}}\right) \frac{i}{m_W^2}\, [\bar u_{\nu_\mu}\gamma^\mu(1-\gamma^5) u_\mu]\,[\bar u_e \gamma_\mu(1-\gamma^5) v_{\bar\nu_e}] = \frac{i g^2}{8 m_W^2}\, [\cdots],$$

where the factor $1/\sqrt{2}$ at each vertex combines with the V-A structure, and the standard normalisation absorbs the factors of $2$ to give the $1/8$ [ref: TODO_REF Fermi 1933]. Comparing with the Fermi contact interaction

$$\mathcal{M}_{\mathrm{Fermi}} = i\,\frac{G_F}{\sqrt{2}}\, [\bar u_{\nu_\mu}\gamma^\mu(1-\gamma^5) u_\mu]\,[\bar u_e \gamma_\mu(1-\gamma^5) v_{\bar\nu_e}],$$

read off the matching

$$\frac{G_F}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{g^2}{8 m_W^2}.$$

Now substitute the tree-level $W$-mass relation $m_W = gv/2$ from [12.21.01]. Then $m_W^2 = g^2 v^2/4$, so

$$\frac{g^2}{8 m_W^2} = \frac{g^2}{8 \cdot (g^2 v^2/4)} = \frac{1}{2 v^2}.$$

Equating the two expressions for $G_F/\sqrt{2}$ gives $v^2 = 1/(\sqrt{2}\, G_F)$, hence

$$v = \frac{1}{(\sqrt{2}\, G_F)^{1/2}}.$$

With the measured value extracted from the muon lifetime, $G_F = 1.1663787 \times 10^{-5}$ GeV$^{-2}$, one obtains $\sqrt{2}\, G_F = 1.64935 \times 10^{-5}$ GeV$^{-2}$, its reciprocal $60\,630$ GeV$^2$, and

$$v = \sqrt{60\,630}\ \mathrm{GeV} = 246.22\ \mathrm{GeV}. \qquad \square$$

The agreement with the independent determinations of $v$ from the $W$ mass ($v = 2m_W/g$) and from the $Z$ mass, each fixed through the mixing angle, is the empirical content of electroweak unification: a scale measured in muon decay predicts the heavy boson masses.

**Bridge.** This derivation is the foundational reason the electroweak scale is an experimentally fixed number rather than a free parameter: the muon lifetime measures $G_F$, the $W$-exchange diagram fixes $G_F/\sqrt{2} = g^2/(8 m_W^2)$, and the Higgs vacuum ties this to $1/(2v^2)$. The relation $G_F/\sqrt{2} = 1/(2v^2)$ is exactly the bridge between the low-energy four-fermion theory of Fermi and the spontaneously broken gauge theory of Weinberg and Salam, and it builds toward the precision-electroweak programme in which the one-percent-level deviations of $m_W$, $\sin^2\theta_W$, and the $Z$-pole observables from these tree-level formulas are used to fit the top mass and the Higgs mass before either was directly measured. The same scale $v$ appears again in [12.18.01] as the order parameter of the general Higgs mechanism, and the central insight is that the pattern of electroweak masses is determined once $v$, $\theta_W$, and the Yukawa couplings are specified.

<ol class="exercise__options" type="A">

<li>$Z_\mu = B_\mu$ and $A_\mu = W^3_\mu$.</li>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>
<p><strong>A.</strong> The mass eigenstates are a rotation of the gauge-eigenstate neutral fields $(B, W^3)$ by $\theta_W$. The combination $A_\mu = \sin\theta_W W^3_\mu + \cos\theta_W B_\mu$ is the one that does <em>not</em> couple to the Higgs vacuum (it leaves $\langle\Phi\rangle$ invariant), so it is the massless photon; the orthogonal combination $Z_\mu = \cos\theta_W W^3_\mu - \sin\theta_W B_\mu$ couples to the vacuum and is massive. Option B swaps them and would make the photon massive; options C and D deny the mixing, which contradicts the off-diagonal structure of the mass matrix.</p>

<p>The tree-level muon decay width is $\Gamma(\mu \to e\,\bar\nu_e\,\nu_\mu) = G_F^2\, m_\mu^5 / (192\pi^3)$. Explain qualitatively why the width scales as $G_F^2 m_\mu^5$: identify the origin of the $G_F^2$ (two powers of the coupling), the $m_\mu^5$ (phase space and the dimensionality of $G_F$), and the $1/\pi^3$ (three-body phase space). Then estimate the muon lifetime $\tau_\mu = 1/\Gamma$ in seconds.</p>
<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>
<p>$G_F$ has dimensions of (mass)$^{-2}$, so $G_F^2 m_\mu^5$ has dimensions of mass, as a width must. Convert GeV$^{-1}$ to seconds using $\hbar = 6.58\times10^{-25}$ GeV$\cdot$s.</p>
</details>
<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>
<p><strong>Scaling.</strong> The amplitude is linear in $G_F$ (one contact vertex), so the width, which is $|\mathcal{M}|^2$, carries $G_F^2$. Because $G_F$ has mass dimension $-2$, the combination $G_F^2 m_\mu^5$ has dimension $+1$ (a width). The five powers of $m_\mu$ are fixed by dimensional analysis: with only $G_F$ and $m_\mu$ available, $G_F^2 m_\mu^5$ is the unique width-scale combination (the electron and neutrino masses are negligible). The $1/192\pi^3$ is the fully integrated three-body phase space in the muon rest frame.</p>
<p><strong>Estimate.</strong> With $G_F = 1.166\times10^{-5}$ GeV$^{-2}$, $m_\mu = 0.10566$ GeV: $\Gamma = (1.166\times10^{-5})^2 (0.10566)^5/(192\pi^3) = (1.36\times10^{-10})(1.32\times10^{-5})/5966 = 3.0\times10^{-19}$ GeV. Then $\tau = \hbar/\Gamma = 6.58\times10^{-25}/3.0\times10^{-19} = 2.19\times10^{-6}$ s, matching the measured $2.197\times10^{-6}$ s. The agreement is what makes the muon lifetime the cleanest measurement of $G_F$ and hence of $v$. $\square$</p>

Convert to the charged basis $W^\pm = (W^1 \mp iW^2)/\sqrt2$, and use $W^3 = \cos\theta_W Z + \sin\theta_W A$ to read off the photon and $Z$ couplings. The identity $g\sin\theta_W = e$ turns the $SU(2)$ coupling into the electric charge.</p>

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

<p><strong>Derivation.</strong> The cubic part of $-\tfrac14 W^i_{\mu\nu}W^{i\mu\nu}$ is $-g\,\varepsilon^{ijk}(\partial_\mu W^i_\nu)W^{j\mu}W^{k\nu}$. In the charged basis the combination $\varepsilon^{3jk}W^j W^k = -i(W^+W^- - W^-W^+)$, so the piece coupling one neutral field to two charged bosons is $-ig\,(\partial_\mu W^+_\nu - \partial_\nu W^+_\mu)W^{-\mu}W^{3\nu} + \mathrm{h.c.}$</p>

<p>Substituting $W^3 = \cos\theta_W Z + \sin\theta_W A$ splits this into a $W^+W^-Z$ vertex (coefficient $-ig\cos\theta_W$) and a $W^+W^-A$ vertex (coefficient $-ig\sin\theta_W = -ie$). Fourier-transforming with $\partial_\mu \to -ip_\mu$ and symmetrising over the three external legs gives the momentum-space vertices: the photon vertex carries $ie$ times the antisymmetric tensor $[(p-q)_\lambda g_{\mu\nu} + (q-k)_\mu g_{\nu\lambda} + (k-p)_\nu g_{\lambda\mu}]$, and the $Z$ vertex has the identical structure with $e \to g\cos\theta_W$. The antisymmetry under leg exchange, inherited from $\varepsilon^{ijk}$, is the defining feature and the reason the photon couples to the $W$ with exactly the electric charge $e$.</p>

<p>These vertices have no analogue in QED (the photon does not couple to itself); their existence is a direct prediction of the non-Abelian $SU(2)_L$ structure and was measured at LEP2 in $e^+e^- \to W^+W^-$, where the diagram with a photon or $Z$ splitting into $W^+W^-$ is essential for the cancellation that keeps the cross section unitary at high energy. $\square$</p>

Show that for the production of longitudinally polarised $W$ bosons ($W_L^+ W_L^-$), the amplitudes from the two $s$-channel diagrams grow with energy as $\sim E^2/m_W^2$ (individually violating perturbative unitarity), and that the neutrino-exchange diagram cancels this growth precisely, leaving a total amplitude that is constant or falls at high energy. Identify which electroweak gauge identity is responsible for the cancellation.</p>

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

<p>The longitudinal polarisation vector is $\varepsilon_L^\mu(p) \approx p^\mu/m_W + \mathcal{O}(m_W/E)$ at high energy, so each emission of a $W_L$ through a gauge current brings a factor $p^\mu/m_W$. The $s$-channel amplitude then grows as $E^2/m_W^2$. The $t$-channel $\nu_e$ diagram couples to the same $SU(2)_L$ current, with a coupling fixed by gauge invariance.</p>

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

<p><strong>Argument.</strong> For $W_L$, $\varepsilon_L^\mu \to p^\mu/m_W$ as $E \gg m_W$. The $s$-channel $\gamma$-exchange amplitude behaves as $\mathcal{M}_\gamma \sim (e^2/s)\cdot(p^\mu/m_W)\cdot(p'^\mu/m_W)\cdot(\text{currents}) \sim e^2 E^2/m_W^2$, growing without bound. The $Z$-exchange amplitude $\mathcal{M}_Z$ has the same growth. Individually these violate perturbative unitarity, since $|\mathcal{M}|$ should not grow faster than $E^0$.</p>

<p>The cancellation: the $t$-channel $\nu_e$-exchange diagram, in which the incoming $e^-$ emits a $W^-$ coupling to the $e^+$ line, contributes an amplitude $\mathcal{M}_\nu$ that also grows as $E^2/m_W^2$ but with the opposite sign. The cancellation $\mathcal{M}_\gamma + \mathcal{M}_Z + \mathcal{M}_\nu = \mathcal{O}(E^0)$ is exact because all three diagrams couple to the same $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ gauge current, and the relative coefficients are fixed by the single coupling $g$ and the mixing angle $\theta_W$.</p>

<p>The identity responsible is gauge invariance itself — the Slavnov-Taylor identity (the non-Abelian Ward identity) of the $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ theory [ref: TODO_REF 't Hooft-Veltman 1972]. This is the cleanest demonstration that the electroweak theory is unitary at high energy <em>only</em> because the gauge structure is present: without the $Z$ diagram, or without the neutrino diagram, the cross section would diverge. It was verified at LEP2 to $\sqrt{s} = 209$ GeV. $\square$</p>

## Advanced results [Master]

The Key derivation shows how the electroweak scale is fixed by the muon lifetime. Three further structural facts about the electroweak theory carry the unit beyond the overview: the custodial symmetry that protects the mass relations, the non-Abelian gauge self-couplings and their high-energy unitarity, and the flavour structure encoded in the CKM matrix.

**Custodial symmetry and the $\rho$ parameter.** The tree-level identity $\rho \equiv m_W^2/(m_Z^2\cos^2\theta_W) = 1$ proved in Exercise 5 is not an accident of the doublet representation: it is enforced by a global $SU(2)$ symmetry of the Higgs sector called the custodial symmetry [ref: TODO_REF Georgi-Nanopoulos 1979]. Assemble the Higgs doublet $\Phi = (\phi^+, \phi^0)^T$ into the $2\times 2$ matrix

$$H = \begin{pmatrix} \phi^{0*} & \phi^+ \\ -\phi^- & \phi^0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \det H = \Phi^\dagger\Phi.$$

Under a *global* $SU(2)_L \times SU(2)_R$ acting as $H \to L H R^\dagger$, the potential $V = -\mu^2 \det H + \lambda(\det H)^2$ is invariant (it depends only on $\det H = \Phi^\dagger\Phi$, and $\det(LHR^\dagger) = \det H$ since $L, R \in SU(2)$). The vacuum $\langle H \rangle = (v/\sqrt2)\,\mathbf{1}_{2\times 2}$ is proportional to the identity, so it is invariant under the *diagonal* subgroup $SU(2)_{L+R} \equiv SU(2)_V$, the custodial symmetry. The three $W$ bosons transform as a triplet under $SU(2)_V$ when the hypercharge coupling $g'$ is set to zero, so the custodial symmetry forces $m_{W^1} = m_{W^2} = m_{W^3}$; because $W^3$ mixes with $B$ only through the $g'$ coupling, the relation $m_W = m_Z\cos\theta_W$ — equivalently $\rho = 1$ — holds at tree level for any number of Higgs doublets.

The custodial symmetry is broken by the hypercharge coupling $g' \neq 0$ and, more importantly, by the Yukawa couplings, because a mass splitting within an $SU(2)_L$ doublet (such as $m_t \gg m_b$) is not invariant under $SU(2)_V$. The leading one-loop correction is

$$\Delta\rho \equiv \rho - 1 = \frac{3 G_F\, m_t^2}{8\sqrt2\, \pi^2} \approx 0.96\times10^{-2},$$

the dominant piece of the electroweak precision fit; the measured value $\rho_{\mathrm{exp}} \approx 1.0004$ agrees with this prediction once the full set of radiative corrections is included. This is why precision electroweak data could predict the top mass ($m_t \approx 170$ GeV) before the top quark was directly discovered in 1995, and is the cleanest evidence that the Higgs transforms as an isospin-$1/2$ doublet rather than a higher representation.

**Gauge self-couplings and high-energy unitarity.** The triple-gauge vertices $W^+W^-\gamma$ and $W^+W^-Z$ derived in Exercise 6, together with the quartic vertices $W^+W^-W^+W^-$, $W^+W^-\gamma\gamma$, and $W^+W^-Z\gamma$, are the electroweak fingerprints of non-Abelian gauge structure: the $W$ boson carries electric charge $e$ and couples to the photon, a feature absent in any Abelian theory. Their direct measurement at LEP2 ($e^+e^- \to W^+W^-$, $\sqrt{s}$ up to $209$ GeV) and at the LHC confirmed the predicted couplings to a few percent [ref: TODO_REF 't Hooft-Veltman 1972].

The deeper role of these couplings is unitarity. As shown in Exercise 7, the amplitude for producing longitudinal $W$ bosons in $e^+e^-$ annihilation grows as $E^2/m_W^2$ in any single diagram, threatening perturbative unitarity at energies of a few TeV. The gauge diagrams (photon and $Z$ exchange) and the $t$-channel neutrino diagram cancel this growth exactly, a cancellation enforced by the Slavnov-Taylor identity of $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$. Without the Higgs mechanism — equivalently, without the $W$ acquiring its mass from a scalar condensate — the cancellation would fail and the theory would become non-unitary at energies around $1$ TeV. This is the real theoretical content of the unitarity argument: the Higgs boson is not an optional extra but the agent that keeps weak-interaction scattering finite, and its $125$ GeV mass was within the range suggested by this unitarity bound.

**Flavour and the CKM matrix.** The Yukawa matrices $y^u, y^d, y^e$ are a priori arbitrary complex $3\times 3$ matrices. After symmetry breaking, the mass matrices $M^u = y^u v/\sqrt2$ and $M^d = y^d v/\sqrt2$ are diagonalised by bi-unitary transformations $U_L^{u\dagger} M^u U_R^u = \mathrm{diag}(m_u, m_c, m_t)$ and $U_L^{d\dagger} M^d U_R^d = \mathrm{diag}(m_d, m_s, m_b)$. Rewriting the charged current in the mass basis,

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CC}} = -\frac{g}{\sqrt2} W^+_\mu\, \bar u_{Li}\gamma^\mu (V_{\mathrm{CKM}})_{ij} d_{Lj} + \mathrm{h.c.}, \qquad V_{\mathrm{CKM}} = U_L^{u\dagger} U_L^d,$$

produces the Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa matrix $V_{\mathrm{CKM}}$, a unitary $3\times 3$ matrix parametrised by three mixing angles and one CP-violating phase. The neutral currents, by contrast, remain flavour-diagonal after diagonalisation — the Glashow-Iliopoulos-Maiani (GIM) mechanism — so there are no tree-level flavour-changing neutral currents, a prediction confirmed to high precision (the absence of $\mu \to e\gamma$ at tree level, the suppression of $K^0$-$\bar K^0$ mixing). The unitarity of $V_{\mathrm{CKM}}$ (each row and column summing to one) is tested at the $10^{-3}$ level and is the tightest consistency check of the three-generation electroweak flavour sector. CP violation enters through the single complex phase of $V_{\mathrm{CKM}}$ and is, within the Standard Model, the only source of CP violation in quark interactions — insufficient by itself to explain the baryon asymmetry of the universe, which is one of the motivations for beyond-the-Standard-Model CP-violating sectors.

**Precision electroweak.** The custodial result, the gauge self-couplings, and the CKM unitarity together constitute the precision-electroweak programme: the tree-level predictions of the Glashow-Weinberg-Salam model, corrected by one-loop electroweak diagrams (the oblique parameters $S$, $T$, $U$ summarising gauge-boson self-energies), fit dozens of measured observables — $m_W$, $\sin^2\theta_W$, the $Z$-pole asymmetries, partial widths, and the CKM elements — with the top mass and the Higgs mass as the only large radiative inputs. The agreement of the fitted Higgs mass with the directly observed $125$ GeV scalar [ref: TODO_REF ATLAS-CMS 2012] is the most stringent single test of the electroweak theory and the closing argument for the Higgs doublet as the agent of symmetry breaking.

**Synthesis.** Electroweak theory is the central insight that two apparently different forces are one gauge structure broken by a scalar condensate. The Fermi constant $G_F$ fixes the scale $v = 246$ GeV from muon decay, the Weinberg angle $\theta_W$ fixes the coupling ratio, and from these two numbers plus the Yukawa matrices the model predicts the $W$ and $Z$ masses, the photon's masslessness, the gauge self-couplings, and the flavour structure. The custodial symmetry is the foundational reason $\rho = 1$ at tree level, the Higgs mechanism generalises to any spontaneously broken gauge theory, and the gauge-boson self-couplings are dual to the high-energy unitarity that only the full gauge structure — photon, $Z$, and neutrino diagrams together — preserves. Putting these together yields a renormalisable, unitary, flavour-consistent theory tested at the per-mille level at LEP and the LHC. The bridge is the gauge principle: a locally realised $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ symmetry, spontaneously broken to $U(1)_{\mathrm{em}}$, determines the dynamics, the mass spectrum, and the pattern of self-interactions from one measured scale and one measured angle.

## Full proof set [Master]

The Key derivation gives the extraction of the electroweak scale $v$ in full. Three further structural facts are proved here: the custodial-symmetry protection of $\rho = 1$, the triple gauge couplings from the $SU(2)_L$ field strength, and the emergence of the CKM matrix from the Yukawa diagonalisation.

**Proposition (Custodial symmetry and $\rho = 1$).** In the electroweak model with a single Higgs doublet of hypercharge $Y = +1/2$, the global symmetry of the Higgs potential contains a custodial $SU(2)_V$ under which the three $W$ bosons (in the limit $g' \to 0$) transform as a triplet. Consequently the tree-level electroweak $\rho$ parameter satisfies $\rho \equiv m_W^2/(m_Z^2\cos^2\theta_W) = 1$.

*Proof.* Write the Higgs doublet $\Phi = (\phi^+, \phi^0)^T$ as the $2\times 2$ matrix $H = (\tilde\Phi, \Phi)$, that is,

$$H = \begin{pmatrix} \phi^{0*} & \phi^+ \\ -\phi^- & \phi^0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \text{so that} \quad H^\dagger H = (\Phi^\dagger\Phi)\,\mathbf{1}_{2\times 2}, \quad \det H = \Phi^\dagger\Phi.$$

Under a *global* transformation $(L, R) \in SU(2)_L \times SU(2)_R$, define $H \to L H R^\dagger$. The Higgs potential depends on $H$ only through $\det H = \Phi^\dagger\Phi$, and $\det(LHR^\dagger) = \det(L)\det(H)\det(R^\dagger) = \det H$ (since $L, R \in SU(2)$ have unit determinant), so $V(H) = -\mu^2 \det H + \lambda(\det H)^2$ is invariant under the full global $SU(2)_L \times SU(2)_R$.

The vacuum is $\langle\Phi\rangle = (0, v/\sqrt2)^T$, giving $\langle H\rangle = (v/\sqrt2)\,\mathbf{1}_{2\times 2}$, which is proportional to the identity. It is preserved by the diagonal subgroup $L = R$, i.e. by $SU(2)_V \equiv \{(U,U) : U \in SU(2)\}$: $(U,U)$ sends $\langle H\rangle \to U\langle H\rangle U^\dagger = \langle H\rangle$. Hence the global symmetry is broken $SU(2)_L \times SU(2)_R \to SU(2)_V$ spontaneously, and $SU(2)_V$ is the custodial symmetry.

Now gauge only the $SU(2)_L$ factor (set $g' = 0$, so there is no hypercharge gauge field $B$). The three gauge bosons $W^1, W^2, W^3$ transform as a triplet under the gauged $SU(2)_L$, and because the unbroken global symmetry is the diagonal $SU(2)_V \cong SU(2)_L$, they transform as a triplet under $SU(2)_V$ as well. The mass term $(D_\mu\Phi)^\dagger(D^\mu\Phi)|_{\mathrm{vac}}$, being $SU(2)_V$-invariant and built from an $SU(2)_V$-triplet of gauge fields, must be proportional to $\delta^{ij} W^i_\mu W^{j\mu}$: it cannot distinguish the three directions. Hence $m_{W^1} = m_{W^2} = m_{W^3} \equiv m_W$. Turning on the hypercharge coupling $g'$ gauges a $U(1)$ subgroup of $SU(2)_R$, breaking custodial $SU(2)_V$ explicitly; the neutral combination $W^3$ mixes with $B$ to form $Z$ and $A$, and the $W$ triplet splits as $m_Z = m_W/\cos\theta_W$ while the photon stays massless. Therefore

$$\rho \equiv \frac{m_W^2}{m_Z^2 \cos^2\theta_W} = \frac{m_W^2}{m_W^2} = 1$$

at tree level for any number of Higgs doublets. $\square$

The result extends to arbitrary Higgs representations: a single Higgs multiplet of weak isospin $T$ and hypercharge $Y$ (with a neutral component, $T_3 = -Y/2$) contributes $\rho = 3\,T(T+1)/(2Y^2)$... the precise general formula, but for the doublet $T = 1/2$, $Y = 1$ this reduces to $\rho = 1$, while higher representations give $\rho \neq 1$ — which is why the measured $\rho \approx 1.0004$ selects the doublet.

**Proposition (Triple gauge couplings from the $SU(2)_L$ field strength).** The non-Abelian kinetic term $-\tfrac14 W^i_{\mu\nu}W^{i\mu\nu}$ produces a cubic gauge vertex $W^+_\mu W^-_\nu A_\lambda$ with electric charge $e = g\sin\theta_W$ and a cubic vertex $W^+W^-Z$ with coupling $g\cos\theta_W$, both with the fully antisymmetric three-leg tensor structure.

*Proof.* The cubic piece of $-\tfrac14 W^i_{\mu\nu}W^{i\mu\nu}$ is

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cubic}} = -g\,\varepsilon^{ijk}\, (\partial_\mu W^i_\nu)\, W^{j\mu}\, W^{k\nu}.$$

Convert to the charged basis using $W^1 = (W^+ + W^-)/\sqrt2$, $W^2 = i(W^+ - W^-)/\sqrt2$. The Levi-Civita contractions give $\varepsilon^{3jk}W^j W^k = -i(W^+W^- - W^-W^+)$, so the term coupling one neutral ($W^3$) to two charged bosons is

$$\mathcal{L}_{W^+W^-W^3} = -ig\, W^3_{\mu\nu}\, W^{+\mu} W^{-\nu} + ig\,(\partial_\mu W^+_\nu - \partial_\nu W^+_\mu)\,W^{-\mu}W^{3\nu} + \mathrm{h.c.},$$

with $W^3_{\mu\nu} \equiv \partial_\mu W^3_\nu - \partial_\nu W^3_\mu$. Substituting $W^3 = \cos\theta_W Z + \sin\theta_W A$,

$$\mathcal{L}_{W^+W^-A} = -ie\,(\partial_\mu W^+_\nu - \partial_\nu W^+_\mu)\,W^{-\mu} A^{\nu} + \mathrm{h.c.}, \qquad \mathcal{L}_{W^+W^-Z} = -ig\cos\theta_W\,(\partial_\mu W^+_\nu - \partial_\nu W^+_\mu)\,W^{-\mu} Z^{\nu} + \mathrm{h.c.},$$

using $g\sin\theta_W = e$. Fourier-transforming with the plane-wave replacement $\partial_\mu \to -ip_\mu$ and symmetrising over the three external legs yields the momentum-space vertices stated in Exercise 6: the photon vertex has coupling $ie$ times the antisymmetric tensor $[(p-q)_\lambda g_{\mu\nu} + \text{cycl.}]$, and the $Z$ vertex has the identical structure with $e \to g\cos\theta_W$. The antisymmetry under leg exchange, inherited from $\varepsilon^{ijk}$, is the defining feature and the reason the photon couples to the $W$ with exactly the electric charge $e$. $\square$

The quartic vertices — $W^+W^-W^+W^-$, $W^+W^-\gamma\gamma$, $W^+W^-Z\gamma$, $W^+W^-ZZ$ — arise from the $(g\,\varepsilon^{ijk}W^j_\mu W^k_\nu)^2$ part of $W^i_{\mu\nu}W^{i\mu\nu}$ by the same conversion. They were measured at the LHC in vector-boson scattering $pp \to W^+W^+ jj$ and agree with the electroweak prediction.

**Proposition (CKM matrix from Yukawa diagonalisation).** In the three-generation electroweak model, diagonalising the up- and down-type quark mass matrices $M^u, M^d$ by bi-unitary transformations produces a unitary charged-current mixing matrix $V_{\mathrm{CKM}} = U_L^{u\dagger} U_L^d$, while the neutral currents remain flavour-diagonal.

*Proof.* After symmetry breaking, the quark mass terms are

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{mass}} = -\bar u_{L}\, M^u\, u_{R} - \bar d_{L}\, M^d\, d_{R} + \mathrm{h.c.}, \qquad M^u = y^u v/\sqrt2, \quad M^d = y^d v/\sqrt2.$$

A general complex square matrix admits a singular-value decomposition $M = U_L^\dagger\, \mathrm{diag}(m_i)\, U_R$ with $U_L, U_R$ unitary. Apply this to $M^u$ and $M^d$ separately, defining $U_L^u, U_R^u, U_L^d, U_R^d$ such that

$$U_L^{u\dagger} M^u U_R^u = \mathrm{diag}(m_u, m_c, m_t), \qquad U_L^{d\dagger} M^d U_R^d = \mathrm{diag}(m_d, m_s, m_b).$$

Rotate the fermion fields to the mass basis: $u_L^{\mathrm{weak}} = U_L^u\, u_L^{\mathrm{mass}}$, $d_L^{\mathrm{weak}} = U_L^d\, d_L^{\mathrm{mass}}$, and similarly for the right-handed fields. The mass terms become diagonal by construction.

The charged current in the weak basis is $-(g/\sqrt2) W^+_\mu\, \bar u_L^{\mathrm{weak}}\gamma^\mu d_L^{\mathrm{weak}}$. In the mass basis this becomes

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{CC}} = -\frac{g}{\sqrt2} W^+_\mu\, \bar u_L^{\mathrm{mass}}\, U_L^{u\dagger}\gamma^\mu U_L^d\, d_L^{\mathrm{mass}} = -\frac{g}{\sqrt2} W^+_\mu\, \bar u_{Li}\gamma^\mu (V_{\mathrm{CKM}})_{ij}\, d_{Lj},$$

with $V_{\mathrm{CKM}} = U_L^{u\dagger} U_L^d$. As a product of unitary matrices, $V_{\mathrm{CKM}}$ is unitary. A $3\times 3$ unitary matrix has $9$ real parameters; $5$ phases can be absorbed into rephasings of the six quark fields (minus one overall phase), leaving $3$ mixing angles and $1$ CP-violating phase — the standard Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa parametrisation.

The neutral current is $-(g/\cos\theta_W)(Z_\mu/2)\,\bar u_L^{\mathrm{weak}} T_3 \gamma^\mu u_L^{\mathrm{weak}} + \ldots$ on both $u$ and $d$ sectors. Because the same unitary rotation $U_L^u$ appears on both the $\bar u_L$ and $u_L$ sides of the neutral current, $U_L^{u\dagger} U_L^u = \mathbf{1}$ cancels: the neutral current is flavour-diagonal in the mass basis, with no mixing matrix. This is the GIM mechanism: flavour-changing neutral currents vanish at tree level. $\square$

The three propositions together establish the structural content of the electroweak theory that the overview states: the scale is fixed by muon decay (Key derivation), the mass relations are protected by custodial symmetry, the gauge bosons self-couple as the non-Abelian structure demands, and the flavour structure is entirely encoded in the single unitary matrix $V_{\mathrm{CKM}}$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The electroweak theory was assembled in three stages over forty years, beginning with Fermi's 1933 theory of beta decay [ref: TODO_REF Fermi 1933]. Fermi proposed that the decay $n \to p\, e^-\, \bar\nu_e$ proceeds through a pointlike four-fermion contact interaction with a single coupling $G_F$. The theory was strikingly successful at describing nuclear beta spectra and muon decay, but it was manifestly non-renormalisable: the amplitude for any higher-order process diverged, and at energies of order $1/\sqrt{G_F} \sim 300$ GeV the perturbation series broke down entirely. The Fermi constant, a dimensionful coupling, was the sign that the weak force had a finite range and an intermediate mediator.

The discovery of the $V\!-\!A$ chiral structure by Feynman and Gell-Mann and by Sudarshan and Marshak in 1957–1958 [ref: TODO_REF Feynman-Gell-Mann 1958], after the fall of parity conservation in 1957, established that the weak charged current couples only to left-handed fermions. This observation became the seed of the gauge structure: the left-handed doublet assignment $(u_L, d_L)^T$, $(\nu_L, e_L)^T$ of the electroweak model is the direct expression of the $V\!-\!A$ current. Glashow in 1961 [ref: TODO_REF Glashow 1961] then proposed the $SU(2) \times U(1)$ gauge structure and the mixing angle, but his model left the $W$ and $Z$ bosons massless — a gauge boson cannot acquire a mass by hand without destroying gauge invariance — and so the model was not yet a physical theory of the weak force.

The decisive step was the application of the Higgs-Englert-Brout mechanism [ref: TODO_REF Higgs 1964] to the Glashow model by Weinberg (1967) [ref: TODO_REF Weinberg 1967] and independently by Salam (1968) [ref: TODO_REF Salam 1968]. By introducing a single complex scalar doublet $\Phi$ with a vacuum expectation value, the $W$ and $Z$ acquired masses proportional to $v = 246$ GeV while the photon stayed massless, and the model predicted a new weak neutral current mediated by the $Z$. The prediction of the neutral current was confirmed by the Gargamelle experiment at CERN in 1973 [ref: TODO_REF Hasert 1973], the first direct evidence for the electroweak mixing structure; the $W$ and $Z$ bosons themselves were discovered at the CERN SPS collider in 1983, with the mass ratio $m_W/m_Z$ matching $\cos\theta_W$ to one percent.

The model was accepted as a fundamental theory only after 't Hooft and Veltman proved in 1971–1972 [ref: TODO_REF 't Hooft-Veltman 1972] that spontaneously broken non-Abelian gauge theories are renormalisable. This is the technical content of the unitarity argument of Exercise 7: the gauge cancellations that keep $e^+e^- \to W^+W^-$ finite at high energy are the same cancellations that control the ultraviolet divergences loop by loop, and a finite set of counterterms absorbs them. Without this proof the predictions of the theory would have been arbitrary; with it, the precision-electroweak programme of LEP (1989–2000) and the SLC could measure the model's parameters to the per-mille level and use the fits to predict the top mass (1995, agreement) and the Higgs mass (2012, agreement). The discovery of the $125$ GeV Higgs boson by ATLAS and CMS [ref: TODO_REF ATLAS-CMS 2012] closed the programme at the level of the particle content; the measured Higgs couplings to $W$, $Z$, top, bottom, and tau agree with the Yukawa-sector predictions of this unit within current experimental precision.

Philosophically, the electroweak theory is the paradigm of a *gauge principle* theory: the entire Lagrangian is fixed, up to numerical parameters, by specifying the gauge group $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$, the fermion representations, and the Higgs representation. The mass spectrum, the mixing angle, the gauge self-couplings, and the flavour matrix all follow from these choices together with the two measured numbers $v$ and $\theta_W$ and the Yukawa matrices. The deep fact is that a locally realised internal symmetry, once allowed to be spontaneously broken, determines not only the dynamics but the pattern of masses and interactions — mass is not an input but a derived consequence of how the scalar vacuum respects a subgroup. The remaining unexplained inputs — the values of the Yukawa couplings spanning six orders of magnitude, the single CP-violating phase, the number of generations — are the open questions of flavour physics, and they mark the boundary of what the gauge principle alone can explain.
