## Intuition [Beginner]

The Standard Model is the single quantum theory that describes everything physicists have directly detected except gravity. Three of the four fundamental forces — electromagnetism, the weak force, and the strong force — are different facets of one gauge theory. Its symmetry group is the product $SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)$. Gravity, described by general relativity, is the one force this framework does not contain. Within this one structure every experimentally confirmed particle and every measured scattering probability fits.

The matter particles come in three nearly identical families called generations. Each generation contains a charged lepton (electron, muon, tau), its neutrino, an up-type quark (up, charm, top), and a down-type quark (down, strange, bottom). The generations are almost exact copies of one another; they differ only in mass. The top quark weighs about 173 GeV, the up quark only about 0.002 GeV. Why nature repeats itself three times, and why the masses span thirteen orders of magnitude, remains unanswered.

At very high energy, electromagnetism and the weak force merge into a single electroweak force. At ordinary energies they look distinct because the Higgs field, spread uniformly through all of space with a constant value of about 246 GeV, breaks the electroweak symmetry. This breaking gives mass to the $W$ and $Z$ bosons — the carriers of the weak force — while leaving the photon massless. Massless carriers travel at the speed of light, which is why electromagnetic radiation crosses the universe.

The strong force, described by quantum chromodynamics or QCD, behaves in the opposite way to electromagnetism as distance shrinks. At short distances — equivalently at high energy — the strong coupling becomes small. Quarks and gluons inside a proton then barely interact, a property called asymptotic freedom. At long distances the coupling grows and traps quarks and gluons into color-neutral packages called hadrons, of which the proton and neutron are the familiar examples. No isolated quark has ever been observed.

The Standard Model predicts particle masses, decay rates, and collision probabilities with stunning accuracy. The electron's magnetic moment agrees with the theory to better than one part in $10^{12}$. Yet the model leaves central facts unexplained: it has no candidate for dark matter, it gives neutrinos zero mass in its minimal form (experiment shows they have mass), it cannot produce the matter-antimatter imbalance of the universe, and it does not contain gravity. These gaps drive the search for physics beyond the Standard Model.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Problem.** The Weinberg angle $\theta_W$ relates the $W$ and $Z$ boson masses through $m_W = m_Z \cos\theta_W$. Using the measured masses $m_W = 80.4$ GeV and $m_Z = 91.2$ GeV, extract $\cos\theta_W$ and $\sin^2\theta_W$, then use the tree-level Higgs relation $m_W = g v / 2$ with vacuum expectation value $v = 246$ GeV to read off the weak coupling $g$ and the electric charge $e = g \sin\theta_W$.

**Solution.**

Step 1. The ratio gives $\cos\theta_W = m_W / m_Z = 80.4 / 91.2 = 0.8816$.

Step 2. Then $\sin^2\theta_W = 1 - \cos^2\theta_W = 1 - 0.7772 = 0.2228$, and $\sin\theta_W = \sqrt{0.2228} = 0.4720$.

Step 3. The coupling follows from $g = 2 m_W / v = (2 \times 80.4) / 246 = 0.6537$.

Step 4. The electric charge is $e = g \sin\theta_W = 0.6537 \times 0.4720 = 0.3085$. The fine-structure constant at the $Z$ mass is then $\alpha(M_Z) = e^2 / (4\pi) = 0.0952 / 12.57 = 0.00757$, giving $\alpha(M_Z)^{-1} \approx 132$.

**Reading the result.** The measured value is $\alpha(M_Z)^{-1} \approx 128$; the small gap is the work of loop corrections — top-quark and Higgs loops — added on top of this tree-level estimate. The key point is that one number, the Higgs vacuum value $v = 246$ GeV, together with the measured mixing angle, fixes both the weak boson masses and the strength of electromagnetism at collider energies. That internal consistency is the fingerprint of electroweak unification.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Gauge group and field content.** The Standard Model is the gauge theory with group $G_{\mathrm{SM}} = SU(3)_c \times SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$, with chiral fermion matter in three generations, one complex scalar Higgs doublet $\Phi$, and the gauge fields $G^a_\mu$ ($a = 1,\dots,8$), $W^i_\mu$ ($i = 1,2,3$), $B_\mu$. The $SU(3)_c$ factor is the strong color group of QCD; the $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ factor is electroweak, acting only on left-handed fermion doublets. The couplings are $g_s$ (strong), $g$ (weak isospin), $g'$ (hypercharge).

The non-Abelian field strengths are

$$G^a_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu G^a_\nu - \partial_\nu G^a_\mu + g_s f^{abc} G^b_\mu G^c_\nu,$$

$$W^i_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu W^i_\nu - \partial_\nu W^i_\mu + g\, \varepsilon^{ijk} W^j_\mu W^k_\nu, \qquad B_{\mu\nu} = \partial_\mu B_\nu - \partial_\nu B_\mu,$$

where $f^{abc}$ are the $SU(3)$ structure constants and $\varepsilon^{ijk}$ the Levi-Civita symbol of $SU(2)$. The self-interaction terms $g_s f^{abc} G^b_\mu G^c_\nu$ and $g\,\varepsilon^{ijk} W^j_\mu W^k_\nu$ are the hallmark of non-Abelian gauge theory and the origin of asymptotic freedom.

**Fermion representation (one generation).** Each generation contains the left-handed quark doublet $Q_L = (u_L, d_L)^T$ with $Y = +1/3$, the right-handed up quark $u_R$ with $Y = +4/3$, the right-handed down quark $d_R$ with $Y = -2/3$, the left-handed lepton doublet $L_L = (\nu_L, e_L)^T$ with $Y = -1$, and the right-handed charged lepton $e_R$ with $Y = -2$. (Equivalently, half these values if $Q = T_3 + Y/2$ is used; the physics is unchanged.) The electric charge is recovered by the Gell-Mann–Nishijima relation

$$Q = T_3 + \tfrac{1}{2} Y,$$

with $T_3$ the third weak-isospin generator. So the up quark has $Q = +1/2 + 1/6 = +2/3$ and the down quark has $Q = -1/2 + 1/6 = -1/3$.

**The Standard Model Lagrangian.** With the covariant derivative

$$D_\mu = \partial_\mu - i g_s t^a G^a_\mu - i g \tfrac{\sigma^i}{2} W^i_\mu - i g' \tfrac{Y}{2} B_\mu,$$

($t^a = \lambda^a/2$ the $SU(3)$ generators, $\sigma^i$ the Pauli matrices), the full Lagrangian reads

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{SM}} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{gauge}} + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Higgs}} + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Yukawa}} + \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{fermion\,kin}},$$

with

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{gauge}} = -\tfrac{1}{4} G^a_{\mu\nu} G^{a\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{4} W^i_{\mu\nu} W^{i\mu\nu} - \tfrac{1}{4} B_{\mu\nu} B^{\mu\nu},$$

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Higgs}} = (D_\mu \Phi)^\dagger (D^\mu \Phi) - V(\Phi), \qquad V(\Phi) = -\mu^2 \Phi^\dagger \Phi + \lambda (\Phi^\dagger \Phi)^2,$$

$$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Yukawa}} = - y^d_{ij}\, \bar Q_{Li} \Phi\, d_{Rj} - y^u_{ij}\, \bar Q_{Li} \tilde\Phi\, u_{Rj} - y^e_{ij}\, \bar L_{Li} \Phi\, e_{Rj} + \mathrm{h.c.},$$

and $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{fermion\,kin}} = \bar Q_L i\gamma^\mu D_\mu Q_L + \bar u_R i\gamma^\mu D_\mu u_R + \cdots$ summing over all fermion fields. The Higgs doublet is

$$\Phi = \begin{pmatrix} \phi^+ \\ \phi^0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad Y_\Phi = +\tfrac{1}{2}, \qquad \tilde\Phi \equiv i\sigma^2 \Phi^* = \begin{pmatrix} \phi^{0*} \\ -\phi^- \end{pmatrix},$$

and the $3\times 3$ Yukawa matrices $y^u, y^d, y^e$ encode the (a priori arbitrary) fermion masses and mixings.

**Spontaneous symmetry breaking.** For $\mu^2 > 0$, the Higgs potential has a degenerate ring of minima at $\Phi^\dagger\Phi = \mu^2 / (2\lambda)$. Choosing the vacuum

$$\langle \Phi \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ v \end{pmatrix}, \qquad v = \frac{\mu}{\sqrt{\lambda}} \approx 246\ \mathrm{GeV},$$

breaks $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y \to U(1)_{\mathrm{em}}$. The unbroken generator is $Q = T_3 + Y/2$; the photon $A_\mu$ is its gauge boson and remains massless, while the orthogonal combination acquires mass.

**Physical gauge bosons.** The charged bosons $W^\pm_\mu = (W^1_\mu \mp i W^2_\mu)/\sqrt{2}$ and the neutral bosons

$$\begin{pmatrix} A_\mu \\ Z_\mu \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta_W & \sin\theta_W \\ -\sin\theta_W & \cos\theta_W \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} B_\mu \\ W^3_\mu \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \tan\theta_W = \frac{g'}{g},$$

define the Weinberg angle $\theta_W$. The masses and the electric charge that follow are derived in §Key derivation below.

**Anomaly cancellation.** The chiral fermion content above is consistent (the gauge symmetry survives quantisation) if and only if every gauge anomaly vanishes. The most stringent check is the mixed $[SU(3)_c]^2 U(1)_Y$, $[SU(2)_L]^2 U(1)_Y$, and $[U(1)_Y]^3$ anomalies. Each cancels separately within a single generation because the hypercharges sum to zero — verified in the Full proof set.

## Key derivation [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Electroweak mass generation, Glashow-Weinberg-Salam).** In the Standard Model with Higgs vacuum $\langle\Phi\rangle = (0, v/\sqrt{2})^T$, the gauge bosons acquire the tree-level masses

$$m_W = \tfrac{1}{2} g v \approx 80.4\ \mathrm{GeV}, \qquad m_Z = \tfrac{1}{2} \sqrt{g^2 + g'^2}\, v \approx 91.2\ \mathrm{GeV}, \qquad m_\gamma = 0,$$

the photon is the massless gauge boson of the unbroken $U(1)_{\mathrm{em}}$, the Weinberg angle satisfies $\cos\theta_W = m_W / m_Z = g / \sqrt{g^2 + g'^2}$, the electric charge is $e = g \sin\theta_W$, the Higgs boson has mass $m_H = \sqrt{2}\, \mu = \sqrt{2\lambda}\, v \approx 125\ \mathrm{GeV}$, and every massive fermion $f$ acquires mass $m_f = y_f v / \sqrt{2}$ from its Yukawa coupling $y_f$.

**Proof.** Expand the Higgs kinetic term $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{Higgs}} \supset (D_\mu\Phi)^\dagger(D^\mu\Phi)$ around the vacuum. With $\Phi = (0, (v+H)/\sqrt{2})^T$ (where $H$ is the physical Higgs fluctuation), the covariant derivative acts as

$$D_\mu \Phi = \left( \partial_\mu - \tfrac{i}{2}\begin{pmatrix} g W^3_\mu + g' B_\mu & g(W^1_\mu - i W^2_\mu) \\ g(W^1_\mu + i W^2_\mu) & -g W^3_\mu + g' B_\mu \end{pmatrix} \right) \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ v/\sqrt{2} \end{pmatrix}.$$

The lower component gives

$$D_\mu \Phi \Big|_{\mathrm{vac}} = -\frac{i}{2\sqrt{2}} \begin{pmatrix} g(W^1_\mu - i W^2_\mu)\, v \\ (-g W^3_\mu + g' B_\mu)\, v \end{pmatrix} = -\frac{i}{2\sqrt{2}} \begin{pmatrix} g\sqrt{2}\, W^-_\mu\, v \\ -\sqrt{g^2 + g'^2}\, Z_\mu\, v \end{pmatrix},$$

where in the second component we rotated to the mass basis $Z_\mu = \cos\theta_W\, W^3_\mu - \sin\theta_W\, B_\mu$ with $\cos\theta_W = g/\sqrt{g^2+g'^2}$, $\sin\theta_W = g'/\sqrt{g^2+g'^2}$, using $-g W^3_\mu + g' B_\mu = -\sqrt{g^2+g'^2}\, Z_\mu$.

Taking the modulus squared,

$$\tfrac{1}{2} (D_\mu\Phi)^\dagger(D^\mu\Phi)\Big|_{\mathrm{vac}} = \tfrac{1}{4} g^2 v^2\, W^-_\mu W^{+\mu} + \tfrac{1}{8}(g^2 + g'^2) v^2\, Z_\mu Z^\mu.$$

Comparing with the Proca mass terms $\tfrac{1}{2} m_W^2 W^+_\mu W^{-\mu} + \tfrac{1}{2} m_Z^2 Z_\mu Z^\mu$ reads off

$$m_W^2 = \tfrac{1}{4} g^2 v^2, \qquad m_Z^2 = \tfrac{1}{4}(g^2 + g'^2) v^2.$$

Hence $m_W = gv/2$ and $m_Z = v\sqrt{g^2+g'^2}/2$. The orthogonal neutral combination $A_\mu = \sin\theta_W W^3_\mu + \cos\theta_W B_\mu$ does not couple to the vacuum at all, since the vacuum is neutral ($Q\langle\Phi\rangle = 0$), so the photon is massless: $m_\gamma = 0$. Dividing, $\cos\theta_W = m_W/m_Z = g/\sqrt{g^2+g'^2}$.

The electric charge. In the unbroken $U(1)_{\mathrm{em}}$, the gauge coupling is the coefficient of the photon in the neutral current. Expanding $-g W^3_\mu T_3 - g' B_\mu (Y/2)$ in the mass basis and matching the coefficient of $A_\mu$ gives

$$e = g \sin\theta_W = g' \cos\theta_W = \frac{g g'}{\sqrt{g^2 + g'^2}}.$$

The Higgs mass. Expanding the potential around the minimum with $\Phi^\dagger\Phi = (v+H)^2/2$,

$$V = -\tfrac{\mu^2}{2}(v+H)^2 + \tfrac{\lambda}{4}(v+H)^4.$$

The stationary condition $dV/dH|_{H=0} = 0$ fixes $v^2 = \mu^2/\lambda$. The quadratic term in $H$ is $\frac{1}{2}(2\mu^2) H^2$, so $m_H^2 = 2\mu^2 = 2\lambda v^2$ and $m_H = \sqrt{2\lambda}\, v = \sqrt{2}\,\mu \approx 125$ GeV, fixing $\lambda \approx 0.129$ from the measured $m_H$ and $v$.

The fermion masses. Inserting the vacuum into the down-type and charged-lepton Yukawa couplings, $\bar Q_L \Phi d_R \to (v/\sqrt{2})\, \bar d_L d_R$ (and similarly $\bar L_L \Phi e_R \to (v/\sqrt{2})\bar e_L e_R$), producing Dirac mass matrices $M^d = y^d v/\sqrt{2}$ and $M^e = y^e v/\sqrt{2}$. The up-type masses come from $\bar Q_L \tilde\Phi u_R \to (v/\sqrt{2})\, \bar u_L u_R$, giving $M^u = y^u v/\sqrt{2}$. After diagonalising these matrices by unitary rotations of the fermion fields, the physical masses are $m_f = y_f v/\sqrt{2}$, ranging from $m_e \approx 0.511$ MeV ($y_e \approx 3\times 10^{-6}$) to $m_t \approx 173$ GeV ($y_t \approx 1$). $\square$

**Bridge.** This derivation is the foundational reason the weak bosons are massive while the photon is not: the Higgs vacuum expectation value breaks $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ down to $U(1)_{\mathrm{em}}$, and the surviving gauge symmetry is exactly electromagnetism. The mass relation $m_W = m_Z \cos\theta_W$ is precisely the tree-level electroweak prediction that loop corrections (the radiative programme of [12.16.01]) later refine. The Higgs mechanism generalises to any spontaneously broken gauge theory, and this is exactly the structure that appears again in [12.18.01] (the general Higgs mechanism) and that builds toward grand-unified theories in which the full $SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)$ descends from a larger simple group. The bridge is that mass, in the Standard Model, is not an input but a derived consequence of how the scalar vacuum respects a subgroup of the original gauge symmetry.

<p>After electroweak symmetry breaking, which Standard Model gauge bosons are massive?</p>
<ol class="exercise__options" type="A">
<li>Only the photon $A_\mu$; all others are massless.</li>
<li>The eight gluons and the photon; $W^\pm$ and $Z$ are massive.</li>
<li>The $W^+$, $W^-$, and $Z$; the photon and the gluons remain massless.</li>
<li>All twelve gauge bosons become massive.</li>
</ol>
<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>
<p><strong>C.</strong> The Higgs vacuum carries no color and no electric charge ($Q\langle\Phi\rangle = 0$), so $SU(3)_c$ and $U(1)_{\mathrm{em}}$ remain unbroken and their bosons (the eight gluons and the photon) stay massless. Only the generators of $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ that do <em>not</em> annihilate the vacuum — three of the four — acquire masses: $W^+$, $W^-$, $Z$. This counting (three massive, one massless, plus eight massless gluons) is the content of the Higgs mechanism in the electroweak sector.</p>

<p>Show that the minimal Standard Model has no gauge-invariant neutrino mass term at any renormalisable order, and explain why adding a right-handed neutrino $\nu_R$ with $Y = 0$ repairs this.</p>
<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>
<p>A Dirac mass needs a term $\bar L_L \tilde\Phi \nu_R$. With no $\nu_R$ present, can you build a gauge-invariant bilinear in $\nu_L$ alone? Check the representation $(\mathbf{1}, \mathbf{2}, -1)$ of $L_L$ and whether two copies can form a singlet.</p>
</details>
<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>
<p><strong>Argument.</strong> A Lorentz-invariant fermion mass term couples left- and right-handed fields as $\bar\nu_L \nu_R$ (Dirac) or is a Majorana bilinear $\nu_L^T C \nu_L$ (Majorana). The Dirac option is unavailable in the minimal SM because there is no $\nu_R$ field. The Majorana option is unavailable at the renormalisable (dimension-3) level because $\nu_L^T C \nu_L$ transforms as the symmetric product of two $(\mathbf{1}, \mathbf{2}, -1)$ representations, which decomposes as $(\mathbf{1}, \mathbf{3}, -2) \oplus (\mathbf{1}, \mathbf{1}, -2)$ — neither piece is a gauge singlet, so the bilinear is not invariant under $SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$. Adding $\nu_R$ in the singlet representation $(\mathbf{1}, \mathbf{1}, 0)$ allows the Yukawa coupling $-y^\nu \bar L_L \tilde\Phi\, \nu_R$, which after symmetry breaking generates a Dirac mass $m_\nu = y^\nu v/\sqrt{2}$. Because $\nu_R$ is a complete gauge singlet, a bare Majorana mass $(M/2)\bar\nu_R^c \nu_R$ is also allowed, leading via the seesaw mechanism to $m_\nu \sim y^{\nu\,2} v^2 / (2M)$ — naturally small for large $M$. $\square$</p>
</details>

<p class="exercise__label"><strong>Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).</strong></p>

<p>Derive the one-loop QCD beta function $\beta(g_s) = \mu\,dg_s/d\mu = -g_s^3/(16\pi^2)\,(11 - 2 n_f/3) + \mathcal{O}(g_s^5)$ for $SU(3)_c$ with $n_f$ active quark flavours, by counting the gauge-boson and fermion contributions to the one-loop gluon self-energy. Show that for $n_f \le 16$ the coupling decreases with energy (asymptotic freedom).</p>

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

<p>The gauge-boson loop (two gluons and one ghost running in the loop) contributes $+11/3$ per adjoint colour factor; each quark flavour contributes $-2/3$ through the quark-antiquark bubble. The sign is fixed by the requirement that the bare coupling absorb the divergence.</p>

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

<p><strong>Derivation sketch.</strong> The one-loop gluon self-energy (vacuum polarisation) receives two contributions: (i) the gluon-plus-ghost loop, a purely gauge-field diagram that exists only in non-Abelian theories and contributes $+(11/3)\, C_2(G)\, g_s^2/(16\pi^2)$ to the beta-function coefficient with $C_2(G) = 3$ for $SU(3)$; (ii) the quark-antiquark bubble, contributing $-(2/3)\, T(R)\, n_f\, g_s^2/(16\pi^2)$ with $T(R) = 1/2$ for the fundamental. Adding, the coefficient of $-g_s^3/(16\pi^2)$ is $(11/3) C_2(G) - (4/3) T(R) n_f = 11 - 2 n_f/3$ for $SU(3)$.</p>

<p>The sign convention is fixed by renormalising the bare coupling $g_0 = \mu^{\varepsilon/2} Z_g g_s$ in dimensional regularisation, with $Z_g = 1 - (g_s^2/(16\pi^2\varepsilon))(11 - 2n_f/3) + \cdots$, and reading off $\beta = -\varepsilon g_s/2 - (g_s/2)\, d\log Z_g^2/d\log\mu$. In four dimensions ($\varepsilon \to 0$) this gives the quoted result. The coefficient $b_0 = 11 - 2 n_f/3$ is positive for $n_f \le 16$, so $\beta < 0$: as $\mu$ increases, $g_s$ decreases — asymptotic freedom. Integrating, $\alpha_s(\mu) = 2\pi/[b_0 \log(\mu/\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}})]$ with $\Lambda_{\mathrm{QCD}} \approx 200$ MeV. $\square$</p>

</details>

(a) Recover $v^2 = \mu^2/\lambda$ from the stationary condition. (b) Show the physical Higgs boson has mass $m_H = \sqrt{2\lambda}\,v$. <p>Parameterise the full Higgs doublet as $\Phi = e^{i\pi^a \sigma^a/v}\,(0, (v+H)/\sqrt{2})^T$ (unitary gauge). The fields $\pi^a$ are the Goldstone modes. Substitute into $(D_\mu\Phi)^\dagger(D^\mu\Phi)$ and into $V$.</p>

Setting $dV/dH|_{H=0} = -\mu^2 v + \lambda v^3 = 0$ gives $v^2 = \mu^2/\lambda$ for $v \ne 0$. The $\pi^0$ is eaten by $Z_\mu$ identically. ## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The Standard Model is not formalised in Mathlib at the time of writing, and formalising it is not a near-term target. What would be required, in increasing order of difficulty: (i) a typed declaration of the gauge group $SU(3)_c \times SU(2)_L \times U(1)_Y$ with its Lie algebra and structure constants, building on Mathlib's existing matrix-Lie-algebra and root-system infrastructure; (ii) a representation-theoretic encoding of the fermion content (the $(\mathbf{3}, \mathbf{2}, 1/3)$, $(\mathbf{3}, \mathbf{1}, 4/3)$, $\dots$ assignment) with the Gell-Mann–Nishijima charge formula as a computable function; (iii) the classical Lagrangian as a typed mathematical object (sum of gauge, Higgs, Yukawa, kinetic terms); (iv) the BRST-gauge-fixed perturbation series and dimensional-regularised loop integrals, depending on a relativistic-QFT package that does not yet exist in Mathlib; (v) the tree-level electroweak mass relations of the Key derivation as a verified calculation; (vi) the anomaly-cancellation identities of the Full proof set as Lean theorems.

Steps (i)–(iii) are within reach of the current Mathlib Lie-theory API and would make a valuable intermediate target — a machine-checked statement of the Standard Model Lagrangian and of its classical consistency conditions. Steps (iv)–(vi) depend on the broader physics-formalisation roadmap and are years away. The `lean_mathlib_gap` field in the frontmatter records the gap in detail; the present unit ships with `lean_status: none` and no `lean_module`.

## Advanced results [Master]

The Standard Model as written above is the most precisely tested quantum theory ever constructed. Beyond the tree-level mass spectrum of the Key derivation, its quantitative predictions rest on three pillars: the renormalisability proof for spontaneously broken gauge theories, the running of the three gauge couplings under the renormalisation group, and the operator-product expansion of QCD that turns short-distance scattering into hadronic predictions.

**Renormalisability of the electroweak theory.** The Glashow-Weinberg-Salam model was proposed in 1961–1968, but it was not accepted as a physical theory of the weak interaction until 't Hooft and Veltman proved in 1971–1972 that spontaneously broken non-Abelian gauge theories are renormalisable [ref: TODO_REF 't Hooft-Veltman 1972]. Their proof, made tractable by the invention of dimensional regularisation, showed that the Slavnov-Taylor identities (the non-Abelian generalisation of the Ward-Takahashi identity of [12.16.01]) control the divergences order by order and that a finite set of counterterms absorbs them. Without this proof the predictions of the theory would have been arbitrary — every loop order could have introduced a new free parameter — and the precision electroweak programme would have been impossible.

**Running couplings and grand unification.** The three gauge couplings $g_1 = \sqrt{5/3}\,g'$ (GUT-normalised hypercharge), $g_2 = g$, and $g_3 = g_s$ each run with energy according to their one-loop beta functions:

$$\frac{1}{\alpha_i(\mu)} = \frac{1}{\alpha_i(M_Z)} + \frac{b_i}{2\pi}\log\frac{\mu}{M_Z},$$

with $b_1 = 41/10$, $b_2 = -19/6$, $b_3 = -7$ in the Standard Model. Because $b_3 < 0$ the strong coupling decreases (asymptotic freedom), because $b_2 < 0$ the weak coupling also decreases, and because $b_1 > 0$ the hypercharge coupling increases. Using the measured boundary values $\alpha_1(M_Z)^{-1} \approx 59$, $\alpha_2(M_Z)^{-1} \approx 29$, $\alpha_3(M_Z)^{-1} \approx 8.5$, the three couplings approach one another at around $10^{13}$–$10^{15}$ GeV but do not meet at a single point. In the minimal supersymmetric Standard Model (MSSM) the beta-function coefficients shift and the three couplings meet to high precision near $2\times10^{16}$ GeV — one of the standard pieces of evidence cited for low-energy supersymmetry, though not by itself conclusive.

The minimal $SU(5)$ grand-unified theory of Georgi and Glashow (1974) [ref: TODO_REF Georgi-Glashow 1974] embeds $SU(3) \times SU(2) \times U(1)$ in a single simple group and predicts that quarks and leptons are different components of the same multiplets. Its most striking prediction is proton decay (for example $p \to e^+ \pi^0$), mediated by the superheavy $X$ and $Y$ gauge bosons. The minimal $SU(5)$ prediction $\tau_p \sim 10^{31}$ years has been ruled out by Super-Kamiokande (lower bound $\tau_p > 10^{34}$ years); supersymmetric $SU(5)$ also has problems, but the broader idea of unification remains the leading organising principle for physics beyond the Standard Model.

**Perturbative QCD and the parton model.** Asymptotic freedom, discovered by Politzer and by Gross-Wilczek in 1973 [ref: TODO_REF Politzer 1973], is what makes the strong interaction calculable at all. At momentum transfers of order the $Z$ mass the coupling is $\alpha_s(M_Z) \approx 0.118$ — small enough for perturbation theory. Deep inelastic scattering, the Drell-Yan process, $e^+e^-$ annihilation into hadrons, and jet production at colliders are all computed by treating the incoming hadron as a collection of quasi-free partons (quarks and gluons) described by parton distribution functions, convolved with perturbatively calculable hard-scattering cross sections. The non-perturbative part (the parton distributions, hadronisation) is factored out and either measured or modelled on the lattice. This separation, the factorisation theorem of QCD, is the operational core of essentially every prediction at the LHC.

**Open problems.** Five empirical facts do not fit inside the Standard Model as written.

(i) *Neutrino masses.* Oscillation experiments (Super-Kamiokande 1998, SNO 2001, KamLAND) show that neutrinos have small but nonzero mass differences $\Delta m^2_{21} \approx 7.5\times10^{-5}$ eV$^2$ and $|\Delta m^2_{31}| \approx 2.5\times10^{-3}$ eV$^2$. The minimal Standard Model predicts $m_\nu = 0$. The minimal extension adds right-handed neutrinos (gauge singlets) and either Dirac masses or Majorana masses via the seesaw mechanism, in which case the smallness of $m_\nu$ points to a very heavy seesaw scale $M \sim 10^{14}$ GeV.

(ii) *Dark matter.* Cosmological data (galaxy rotation curves, gravitational lensing, the cosmic microwave background, the bullet cluster) show that about 27 percent of the energy density of the universe is non-baryonic, non-relativistic, and dark. The Standard Model's only weakly-interacting stable candidate — the neutrino — is too light by orders of magnitude to be the dominant component. Leading candidates (the axion, the lightest supersymmetric particle, WIMPs) all lie beyond the Standard Model.

(iii) *Baryon asymmetry.* The universe contains matter but essentially no antimatter, with a baryon-to-photon ratio $\eta_B \approx 6\times10^{-10}$. Sakharov's three conditions (baryon-number violation, C and CP violation, departure from thermal equilibrium) can be met in principle by electroweak sphaleron processes and the CKM matrix, but the Standard Model produces a baryon asymmetry many orders of magnitude too small. Additional CP-violating phases (for example in the neutrino sector via leptogenesis) are needed.

(iv) *The strong CP problem.* QCD admits a CP-violating parameter $\theta$ through the term $(\theta g_s^2/32\pi^2) G\tilde G$, which would generate a neutron electric dipole moment. Experiment bounds $|\theta| < 10^{-10}$ with no Standard Model explanation. The Peccei-Quinn mechanism (and its pseudo-Nambu-Goldstone boson, the axion) was proposed to set $\theta \to 0$ dynamically, but no axion has yet been detected.

(v) *The hierarchy (naturalness) problem.* The Higgs mass parameter $\mu^2$ receives radiative corrections $\delta\mu^2 \sim \Lambda_{\mathrm{UV}}^2$ that are quadratically sensitive to the ultraviolet cutoff. Keeping $m_H \approx 125$ GeV when the cutoff might be the Planck scale $10^{19}$ GeV requires fine-tuning the bare parameter against the loop correction to one part in $10^{34}$. Supersymmetry, composite Higgs models, and extra-dimensional setups are all proposed resolutions; none has been confirmed at the LHC.

The absence of gravity from the Standard Model is a sixth, structural gap: the Standard Model is a quantum field theory on a fixed Minkowski background, with no dynamical spacetime metric, and every attempt to quantise general relativity perturbatively yields a non-renormalisable theory. This is the deepest open problem of fundamental physics and the entry point to string theory, loop quantum gravity, and asymptotic safety.

**Synthesis.** The Standard Model is the central insight of modern particle physics: three forces, three generations, and one scalar field, organised by gauge symmetry and its spontaneous breaking. The electroweak unification generalises the QED renormalisation programme of [12.16.01], and asymptotic freedom is dual to confinement — the same running coupling that vanishes at high energy diverges at the QCD scale, trapping quarks into hadrons. Putting these together yields a renormalisable, anomaly-free, unitary theory tested to one part in $10^{12}$ in precision observables. The framework appears again in every proposed extension — supersymmetry, grand unification, extra dimensions, axion dark matter — and the bridge is the gauge principle itself: locally realised internal symmetry determines the dynamics, the particle content, and the pattern of masses. The hierarchy problem, the absence of gravity, neutrino masses, dark matter, and the baryon asymmetry mark the limits of this synthesis and the boundary of known physics.

## Full proof set [Master]

The Key derivation gives the electroweak mass spectrum in full. Three further structural facts about the Standard Model are stated and proved (or proof-sketched) here: anomaly cancellation per generation, the sign of the QCD beta function, and the consistency of the tree-level mass relations.

**Proposition (Anomaly cancellation within one generation).** The chiral fermion content of one Standard Model generation cancels the cubic $[U(1)_Y]^3$, the mixed gravitational-$U(1)_Y$, the mixed $[SU(2)_L]^2 U(1)_Y$, and the mixed $[SU(3)_c]^2 U(1)_Y$ gauge anomalies.

*Proof.* Using the convention $Q = T_3 + Y/2$ (so $Y_{Q_L} = 1/3$, $Y_{u_R} = 4/3$, $Y_{d_R} = -2/3$, $Y_{L_L} = -1$, $Y_{e_R} = -2$) with right-handed fermions rewritten as the left-handed charge-conjugates $\bar f_R^c$ carrying $-Y$, and a colour multiplicity factor of $3$ on every quark. The cubic hypercharge anomaly coefficient is

$$\sum_{\mathrm{LH}} Y^3 = 3\!\left[\left(\tfrac{1}{3}\right)^3 + \left(\tfrac{1}{3}\right)^3\right] + (-1)^3 + (-1)^3 - 3\!\left[\left(\tfrac{4}{3}\right)^3 + \left(-\tfrac{2}{3}\right)^3\right] - (-2)^3$$

$$= 3\cdot\tfrac{2}{27} - 2 - 3\cdot\tfrac{56}{27} + 8 = \tfrac{6}{27} - 2 - \tfrac{168}{27} + 8 = \tfrac{-162}{27} + 6 = -6 + 6 = 0,$$

using $(4/3)^3 + (-2/3)^3 = (64 - 8)/27 = 56/27$. The mixed gravitational-$U(1)_Y$ anomaly cancels because $\sum_{\mathrm{LH}} Y = 3\cdot(1/3 + 1/3) + (-1) + (-1) - 3\cdot(4/3 - 2/3) - (-2) = 2 - 2 - 2 + 2 = 0$. The mixed $[SU(2)_L]^2 U(1)_Y$ anomaly is proportional to $\sum_{\mathrm{doublets}} Y$: three coloured quark doublets contribute $3\cdot(1/3) = 1$ and one lepton doublet contributes $-1$, giving $1 + (-1) = 0$. The mixed $[SU(3)_c]^2 U(1)_Y$ anomaly is proportional to $\sum_{\mathrm{LH, coloured}} Y$: from $Q_L$ (two isospin components, three colours) $3\cdot 2\cdot(1/3) = 2$; from $u_R^c$ (three colours, $Y = -4/3$) $3\cdot(-4/3) = -4$; from $d_R^c$ (three colours, $Y = +2/3$) $3\cdot(2/3) = 2$; sum $= 2 - 4 + 2 = 0$. All four anomalies vanish within one generation. $\square$

The fact that anomaly cancellation works out generation by generation — rather than requiring contributions from all three generations combined — is a strong hint that each generation is a consistent building block, and it constrains severely any extension of the fermion content (every new chiral fermion must be added in an anomaly-free combination).

**Proposition (Asymptotic freedom of QCD).** The one-loop beta function of $SU(3)_c$ QCD with $n_f$ quark flavours is $\beta(g_s) = -[g_s^3/(16\pi^2)]\,(11 - 2 n_f/3) + \mathcal{O}(g_s^5)$, and is negative for $n_f \le 16$.

*Proof sketch.* The one-loop gluon vacuum polarisation has two pieces. The gluon-plus-ghost loop, present only because the gluon carries color (the adjoint representation), gives a contribution $+(11/3)\,C_2(G)$ to the coefficient with $C_2(G) = N_c = 3$ for $SU(3)$. The quark-antiquark bubble, with $n_f$ flavours in the fundamental, gives $-(4/3)\,T(R)\,n_f$ with $T(R) = 1/2$. The full one-loop coefficient of $-g_s^3/(16\pi^2)$ is therefore $b_0 = (11/3)N_c - (2/3)n_f = 11 - 2n_f/3$. The sign is fixed by the renormalisation condition $g_0 = \mu^{\varepsilon/2}Z_g g_s$ in dimensional regularisation: the pole in $Z_g$ carries the opposite sign from the fermion-bubble contribution because gauge invariance (encoded in the Slavnov-Taylor identities) forces the longitudinal part of the gluon propagator to renormalise consistently with the vertex. For $n_f \le 16$, $b_0 > 0$ and $\beta < 0$, so $g_s$ decreases as $\mu$ increases — asymptotic freedom. (Detailed proof: Peskin-Schroeder §16; Weinberg Vol. II §18.) $\square$

**Proposition (Tree-level mass-spectrum consistency).** The tree-level electroweak mass relations $m_W = gv/2$, $m_Z = v\sqrt{g^2+g'^2}/2$, $\cos\theta_W = m_W/m_Z$, and $e = g\sin\theta_W$ are mutually consistent and reproduce the measured $W$, $Z$ masses and the $Z$-pole electric charge to within one percent.

*Proof.* From the Key derivation, $m_W/m_Z = g/\sqrt{g^2+g'^2} = \cos\theta_W$, so $\cos\theta_W = m_W/m_Z$ is an identity, not a separate input. Using the measured $m_W = 80.379$ GeV, $m_Z = 91.1876$ GeV gives $\cos\theta_W = 0.8819$, $\sin^2\theta_W = 1 - 0.7777 = 0.2223$ (the on-shell value). Then $v = 2m_W/g = 2m_W \sin\theta_W / e$, and inverting with $e = \sqrt{4\pi\alpha(M_Z)}$ gives $v \approx 246$ GeV, matching the Fermi constant extraction $v = (2^{1/4} G_F)^{-1/2} = 246.22$ GeV. The independent determinations of $v$ from the $W$ mass, the $Z$ mass, the muon lifetime (via $G_F$), and the Higgs couplings all agree to better than one percent at tree level, with the residual absorbed by electroweak radiative corrections. The internal consistency of four independent measurements of $v$ is the empirical confirmation of the Glashow-Weinberg-Salam structure. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The Standard Model is the product of a twenty-year consolidation that began with the failure of the Fermi theory of weak interactions and ended with the 2012 discovery of the Higgs boson. The electroweak sector was assembled in three steps. Glashow (1961) [ref: TODO_REF Glashow 1961] proposed the $SU(2)\times U(1)$ gauge structure and introduced the mixing angle that bears Weinberg's name, but his model left the gauge bosons massless and so could not yet be a physical weak-interaction theory. Weinberg (1967) [ref: TODO_REF Weinberg 1967] and independently Salam (1968) [ref: TODO_REF Salam 1968] added the Higgs doublet and its vacuum expectation value, generating the $W$ and $Z$ masses while keeping the photon massless, and predicting a new neutral-current interaction mediated by the $Z$.

The Weinberg-Salam model was largely ignored for four years. The reason was that no one knew whether a spontaneously broken non-Abelian gauge theory was renormalisable: if every loop order introduced a new divergence, the theory would make no predictions and the elegance of the gauge structure would be physically meaningless. 't Hooft and Veltman settled the question in 1971–1972 [ref: TODO_REF 't Hooft-Veltman 1972] by proving renormalisability of spontaneously broken gauge theories, using the dimensional-regularisation scheme they had just invented. The proof transformed electroweak theory from speculation into the leading candidate for a fundamental description of the weak force. The 1973 discovery of the neutral current at CERN (the Gargamelle bubble chamber) confirmed the $Z$ boson's existence, and the 1983 direct discovery of the $W$ and $Z$ at the CERN SPS closed the electroweak case at the level of the gauge bosons.

The strong-interaction sector had a parallel history. By the late 1960s the hadron spectrum, the deep inelastic scattering data, and the failure of every field-theoretic model of the strong force had led many physicists to abandon QFT for the strong interaction in favour of the S-matrix programme. Gross, Wilczek, and Politzer in 1973 [ref: TODO_REF Politzer 1973] showed — initially to their own surprise, while checking whether non-Abelian theories could evade the call for a cutoff — that the $SU(3)$ colour gauge theory has a negative beta function: the coupling vanishes at high energy. This was asymptotic freedom, and it immediately explained why the parton model of deep inelastic scattering worked. The same calculation implied that the coupling grows at low energy, giving a dynamical (if still not fully rigorous) account of confinement. The strong CP problem, the theta vacuum, and the axion were recognised within a few years.

The Standard Model as a single structure was consolidated through the 1970s, with the third generation (the tau lepton in 1975, the bottom quark in 1977, the top quark in 1995) filling in the fermion content and the asymptotic-freedom-based prediction of the weak-isospin structure of the $Z$ confirmed at LEP in 1989–2000. The one missing particle was the Higgs boson itself. Its discovery by ATLAS and CMS at the LHC on 4 July 2012 [ref: TODO_REF ATLAS-CMS 2012], at a mass of approximately 125 GeV, completed the particle content of the Standard Model and confirmed the Higgs mechanism as the source of electroweak symmetry breaking. The 1979 Nobel Prize had already recognised Glashow, Salam, and Weinberg for the electroweak theory; the 1999 prize recognised 't Hooft and Veltman for the renormalisability proof; the 2004 prize recognised Gross, Wilczek, and Politzer for asymptotic freedom; and the 2013 prize recognised Englert and Higgs for the Higgs mechanism.

Philosophically, the Standard Model is the strongest argument ever made for the Heuristic of Gauge Symmetry: the deep structure of a physical theory is a symmetry, and the dynamics, the particle content, and the mass spectrum all follow once the symmetry and the way it is broken are specified. The Standard Model Lagrangian is fixed almost completely by (i) the choice of gauge group $SU(3)\times SU(2)\times U(1)$, (ii) the choice of fermion representations (one generation of the matter above, copied three times), (iii) the choice of Higgs representation, and (iv) the numerical values of the coupling constants and Yukawa matrices. The number of measured inputs is about nineteen (three gauge couplings, the Higgs VEV and quartic, six quark masses, three mixing angles and one CP phase, three charged-lepton masses, three neutrino mass-squared differences and three mixing angles). From these nineteen numbers the Standard Model predicts every other particle-physics observable ever measured — thousands of cross sections, decay rates, asymmetries, and precision electroweak quantities — with no known contradiction.

The remaining nineteen free parameters are themselves the principal philosophical embarrassment of the Standard Model. The theory does not explain why there are three generations, why the Yukawa couplings span six orders of magnitude, why the strong CP angle is so small, or why the cosmological constant is so tiny. Each of these questions points toward a deeper theory — a grand unified theory, a flavour theory, an axion solution to strong CP, a solution to the cosmological constant problem — but none has been confirmed. The Standard Model is therefore both the triumph and the boundary of reductionist particle physics: a complete description of the strong, weak, and electromagnetic interactions within its domain, and a clear signpost of the physics that lies beyond it.
