## Intuition [Beginner]

A quantum state is not a point in phase space. In classical mechanics, a particle has a definite position and momentum — a single point $(q, p)$ in phase space. In quantum mechanics the state of a system lives in an abstract vector space called a **Hilbert space**. The objects in this space are called **kets**, written $|\psi\rangle$.

Every ket has a mirror image called a **bra**, written $\langle\phi|$. Sandwiching a bra and a ket gives an inner product: $\langle\phi|\psi\rangle$ is a complex number. The absolute square $|\langle\phi|\psi\rangle|^2$ is the probability of finding the system in state $|\phi\rangle$ when it is prepared in state $|\psi\rangle$.

A set of **basis kets** spans the space, like unit vectors in 3D but potentially infinite in number. Any state is a **superposition** — a weighted sum — of basis kets. The Stern-Gerlach experiment (unit 12.01.02) uses a two-dimensional Hilbert space: $|\!\uparrow\rangle$ and $|\!\downarrow\rangle$ form a basis, and a general spin state is $\alpha|\!\uparrow\rangle + \beta|\!\downarrow\rangle$ with $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$.

## Visual [Beginner]

![A unit vector in a two-dimensional Hilbert space spanned by up and down basis kets, with its projections onto each basis direction. The squared lengths of the projections are the Born-rule probabilities.](/img/12.02.01-ket-bra-projections-placeholder.svg)

Picture the Hilbert space as an arrow space. The state $|\psi\rangle$ is one arrow at unit length. The basis kets $|\!\uparrow\rangle$ and $|\!\downarrow\rangle$ point along two perpendicular directions. The inner product $\langle\uparrow\!|\psi\rangle$ is the shadow that $|\psi\rangle$ casts onto the $|\!\uparrow\rangle$ direction, and $|\langle\uparrow\!|\psi\rangle|^2$ is the squared length of that shadow — the probability of measuring spin up. The two squared shadows always sum to one, because the arrow has unit length.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A spin-1/2 particle is prepared in the state $|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|\!\uparrow\rangle + \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|\!\downarrow\rangle$.

What is the probability of measuring spin up?

The inner product is $\langle\uparrow\!|\psi\rangle = 1/\sqrt{2}$. The probability is the absolute square:

$$|\langle\uparrow\!|\psi\rangle|^2 = \left|\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right|^2 = \frac{1}{2}.$$

The probability of measuring spin down is also $1/2$. The two probabilities sum to one, as required by normalisation.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **Hilbert space** $\mathcal{H}$ is a complete complex inner-product space. Explicitly, it carries an inner product $\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle : \mathcal{H} \times \mathcal{H} \to \mathbb{C}$ satisfying:

1. **Conjugate symmetry:** $\langle\phi, \psi\rangle = \overline{\langle\psi, \phi\rangle}$.
2. **Linearity in the second argument:** $\langle\phi, \alpha\psi_1 + \beta\psi_2\rangle = \alpha\langle\phi, \psi_1\rangle + \beta\langle\phi, \psi_2\rangle$.
3. **Positive-definiteness:** $\langle\psi, \psi\rangle \geq 0$ with equality iff $\psi = 0$.
4. **Completeness:** every Cauchy sequence in $\mathcal{H}$ converges.

The norm is $\|\psi\| := \sqrt{\langle\psi, \psi\rangle}$. A **pure quantum state** is a unit vector $|\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}$ with $\|\psi\| = 1$, identified up to an overall phase: $|\psi\rangle \sim e^{i\theta}|\psi\rangle$.

**Dirac notation.** Elements of $\mathcal{H}$ are **kets** $|\psi\rangle$. By the Riesz representation theorem, every continuous linear functional on $\mathcal{H}$ has the form $\psi \mapsto \langle\phi, \psi\rangle$ for a unique $\phi \in \mathcal{H}$. This functional is the **bra** $\langle\phi|$. The pairing $\langle\phi|\psi\rangle := \langle\phi, \psi\rangle$ is the inner product.

**Orthonormal bases and completeness.** An **orthonormal basis** $\{|n\rangle\}_{n}$ satisfies $\langle m|n\rangle = \delta_{mn}$ and spans a dense subspace of $\mathcal{H}$. For a separable Hilbert space (countable basis), every $|\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}$ decomposes as

$$|\psi\rangle = \sum_n |n\rangle\langle n|\psi\rangle,$$

and the **resolution of the identity** holds:

$$\sum_n |n\rangle\langle n| = \mathbb{1}.$$

The coefficients $c_n = \langle n|\psi\rangle$ satisfy Parseval's relation $\sum_n |c_n|^2 = \|\psi\|^2$. For a unit state, $\sum_n |c_n|^2 = 1$ — this is the normalisation condition that makes probabilities sum to one.

**The superposition principle.** If $|\phi\rangle$ and $|\psi\rangle$ are states, then so is $\alpha|\phi\rangle + \beta|\psi\rangle$ for any complex $\alpha, \beta$ with $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$. This is not a metaphor: the linear combination is itself a valid state, and in general no measurement can reveal "which component" without disturbing the system.

**Outer products and projectors.** The **outer product** $|\psi\rangle\langle\phi|$ is the rank-one operator defined by $|\psi\rangle\langle\phi|(\xi) = \langle\phi|\xi\rangle\,|\psi\rangle$. For a unit ket $|\psi\rangle$, the operator $P_\psi := |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|$ is the orthogonal projector onto the line spanned by $|\\psi\rangle$.

**The Born rule.** If the system is in state $|\psi\rangle$ and an observable with orthonormal eigenstates $\{|n\rangle\}$ is measured, the probability of outcome $n$ is

$$\mathrm{Prob}(n \mid \psi) \;=\; |\langle n|\psi\rangle|^2.$$

**The collapse postulate.** After a measurement yielding outcome $n$, the state becomes $|n\rangle$ (up to phase). This is the projection postulate: $|\psi\rangle \to P_n|\psi\rangle / \|P_n|\psi\rangle\|$.

**The density operator (pure states).** A pure state $|\psi\rangle$ can equivalently be described by the density operator $\rho = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|$, a positive trace-one projection. The expectation of an observable $A$ is $\langle A\rangle = \mathrm{tr}(\rho A) = \langle\psi|A|\psi\rangle$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- A Hilbert space is not just an inner-product space; **completeness** is load-bearing. The space of continuous functions on $[0,1]$ with the $L^2$ inner product is an inner-product space but not complete — its completion is $L^2([0,1])$, which is a Hilbert space.
- The bra $\langle\phi|$ is not the same object as the ket $|\phi\rangle$. They live in different spaces: kets in $\mathcal{H}$, bras in the dual $\mathcal{H}^*$. The Riesz isomorphism identifies them, but the distinction matters when domain issues arise for unbounded operators.
- The inner product $\langle\phi|\psi\rangle$ is linear in the *second* argument (physics convention). Mathematics texts conventionally make it linear in the first. The two conventions conjugate all formulas involving the inner product; state the convention before computing.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Spectral decomposition for normal operators — finite-dimensional case).** *Let $A$ be a normal operator on a finite-dimensional Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$ (i.e., $AA^\dagger = A^\dagger A$). Then $\mathcal{H}$ admits an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors of $A$, and $A$ decomposes as*

$$A \;=\; \sum_n a_n\, |n\rangle\langle n|,$$

*where $a_n \in \mathbb{C}$ are the eigenvalues and $\{|n\rangle\}$ the corresponding orthonormal eigenvectors.*

For **self-adjoint** (Hermitian) operators ($A = A^\dagger$), the eigenvalues $a_n$ are real. For **unitary** operators ($A^\dagger = A^{-1}$), the eigenvalues lie on the unit circle $|a_n| = 1$. The self-adjoint case is the one relevant to quantum observables: real eigenvalues are the possible measurement outcomes.

**Proof (finite-dimensional, self-adjoint case).** By induction on $\dim\mathcal{H}$.

*Base case:* $\dim\mathcal{H} = 1$. Any linear operator is multiplication by a scalar. Self-adjointness forces this scalar to be real. Done.

*Inductive step:* Assume the theorem holds for all Hilbert spaces of dimension $< n$. Let $\dim\mathcal{H} = n$ and let $A = A^\dagger$.

Step 1. The fundamental theorem of algebra guarantees that the characteristic polynomial of $A$ has at least one root $a_1 \in \mathbb{C}$. Self-adjointness forces $a_1 \in \mathbb{R}$: if $A|v\rangle = a_1|v\rangle$ with $|v\rangle \neq 0$, then $\langle v|A|v\rangle = a_1\langle v|v\rangle$. The left side is $\langle Av|v\rangle = \overline{a_1}\langle v|v\rangle$ by self-adjointness. So $a_1 = \overline{a_1}$, hence $a_1 \in \mathbb{R}$.

Step 2. Let $|1\rangle$ be a unit eigenvector for $a_1$, and let $V_1 = \{|\psi\rangle : \langle 1|\psi\rangle = 0\}$ be the orthogonal complement of $\mathrm{span}\{|1\rangle\}$. Self-adjointness of $A$ preserves $V_1$: if $\langle 1|\psi\rangle = 0$, then $\langle 1|A\psi\rangle = \langle A1|\psi\rangle = a_1\langle 1|\psi\rangle = 0$.

Step 3. Restrict $A$ to $V_1$, which has dimension $n - 1$. By the inductive hypothesis, $V_1$ has an orthonormal basis $\{|2\rangle, \ldots, |n\rangle\}$ of eigenvectors of $A$. Together with $|1\rangle$, these form an orthonormal eigenbasis for all of $\mathcal{H}$.

Step 4. Read off the decomposition $A = \sum_{k=1}^n a_k |k\rangle\langle k|$ by evaluating both sides on each basis vector. $\square$

**Physical significance.** The spectral decomposition is the mathematical content of the measurement postulate. An observable (self-adjoint operator) carves the Hilbert space into orthogonal "answer directions" — one per eigenvalue. The Born rule says: measure the squared overlap of the state with each answer direction. The decomposition theorem guarantees that these directions exist, are orthogonal, and span the space. Without this theorem, the measurement postulate has no well-defined basis to project onto.

**Infinite-dimensional remark.** The theorem extends to normal operators on infinite-dimensional separable Hilbert spaces via the spectral theorem for bounded normal operators, which replaces the finite sum with a projection-valued-measure decomposition. The finite-dimensional proof above captures all the structural physics without the functional-analytic overhead; the infinite-dimensional refinement is developed in the Master tier.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Verify that the Pauli matrix $\sigma_z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$ is Hermitian, find its eigenvalues and orthonormal eigenvectors, and write its spectral decomposition in Dirac notation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\sigma_z^\dagger = \sigma_z^T = \sigma_z$ since all entries are real and the matrix is symmetric. Read off eigenvectors from the diagonal.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\sigma_z$ is real symmetric, hence Hermitian. Eigenvalues: $+1$ with eigenvector $|\!\uparrow\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\end{pmatrix}$ and $-1$ with eigenvector $|\!\downarrow\rangle = \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\end{pmatrix}$. Spectral decomposition:

$$\sigma_z = (+1)|\!\uparrow\rangle\langle\uparrow\!| + (-1)|\!\downarrow\rangle\langle\downarrow\!|.$$

The eigenvalues $\pm 1$ correspond to the measurement outcomes "spin up" ($+\hbar/2$) and "spin down" ($-\hbar/2$) up to the factor $\hbar/2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the resolution of the identity $\mathbb{1} = |\!\uparrow\rangle\langle\uparrow\!| + |\!\downarrow\rangle\langle\downarrow\!|$ holds by computing its matrix in the $\{|\!\uparrow\rangle, |\!\downarrow\rangle\}$ basis.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Express each outer product as a $2 \times 2$ matrix and add them.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$|\!\uparrow\rangle\langle\uparrow\!| = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&0\end{pmatrix}$ and $|\!\downarrow\rangle\langle\downarrow\!| = \begin{pmatrix}0&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}$. Their sum is $\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix} = \mathbb{1}$.

This is the simplest instance of the completeness relation: inserting $\mathbb{1} = \sum_n |n\rangle\langle n|$ between a bra and a ket is the algebraic operation that generates the Born-rule probabilities.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $|\psi\rangle = \alpha|\!\uparrow\rangle + \beta|\!\downarrow\rangle$ with $|\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1$. Use the resolution of the identity to show that $\langle\psi|\psi\rangle = 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Insert $\mathbb{1} = |\!\uparrow\rangle\langle\uparrow\!| + |\!\downarrow\rangle\langle\downarrow\!|$ between $\langle\psi|$ and $|\psi\rangle$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$$\langle\psi|\psi\rangle = \langle\psi|\mathbb{1}|\psi\rangle = \langle\psi|\uparrow\rangle\langle\uparrow\!|\psi\rangle + \langle\psi|\downarrow\rangle\langle\downarrow\!|\psi\rangle = |\alpha|^2 + |\beta|^2 = 1.$$

The resolution of the identity converts the abstract norm into a sum of squared amplitudes — exactly the normalisation condition.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the outer product $P = |\!\uparrow\rangle\langle\uparrow\!|$ applied to a general state $|\psi\rangle = \alpha|\!\uparrow\rangle + \beta|\!\downarrow\rangle$. Show that $P^2 = P$ and $P^\dagger = P$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the outer product directly. For the projector properties, use $\langle\uparrow\!|\uparrow\rangle = 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$P|\psi\rangle = |\!\uparrow\rangle\langle\uparrow\!|\psi\rangle = \alpha|\!\uparrow\rangle$. The projector kills the $|\!\downarrow\rangle$ component and keeps only the $|\!\uparrow\rangle$ component with its amplitude.

$P^2 = |\!\uparrow\rangle\langle\uparrow\!|\uparrow\rangle\langle\uparrow\!| = |\!\uparrow\rangle \cdot 1 \cdot \langle\uparrow\!| = P$.

$P^\dagger = (|\!\uparrow\rangle\langle\uparrow\!|)^\dagger = |\!\uparrow\rangle\langle\uparrow\!| = P$.

These two properties ($P^2 = P$ and $P^\dagger = P$) are the defining properties of an orthogonal projector.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

A state $|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}|\!\uparrow\rangle + \frac{i}{\sqrt{2}}|\!\downarrow\rangle$ is measured with the observable $\sigma_z$. Compute the expectation value $\langle\psi|\sigma_z|\psi\rangle$ using the spectral decomposition.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute $\sigma_z = |\!\uparrow\rangle\langle\uparrow\!| - |\!\downarrow\rangle\langle\downarrow\!|$ and evaluate.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$$\langle\psi|\sigma_z|\psi\rangle = |\langle\uparrow\!|\psi\rangle|^2 - |\langle\downarrow\!|\psi\rangle|^2 = \left|\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right|^2 - \left|\frac{i}{\sqrt{2}}\right|^2 = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{2} = 0.$$

The expectation value is zero because the state has equal probability of spin up and spin down. The phase $i$ does not affect $\sigma_z$ expectation values — only the moduli matter for diagonal observables.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

The density operator for a pure state $|\psi\rangle$ is $\rho = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|$. Show that $\rho^2 = \rho$ and $\mathrm{tr}\,\rho = 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $\langle\psi|\psi\rangle = 1$ for the first property. Compute the trace in any orthonormal basis.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\rho^2 = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|\psi\rangle\langle\psi| = |\psi\rangle \cdot 1 \cdot \langle\psi| = \rho$.

For the trace: pick any orthonormal basis $\{|n\rangle\}$. Then $\mathrm{tr}\,\rho = \sum_n \langle n|\psi\rangle\langle\psi|n\rangle = \sum_n |\langle n|\psi\rangle|^2 = \|\psi\|^2 = 1$ by Parseval's relation.

The conditions $\rho^2 = \rho$ and $\mathrm{tr}\,\rho = 1$ characterise pure-state density operators. Mixed states satisfy $\mathrm{tr}\,\rho = 1$ but $\rho^2 \neq \rho$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality in a Hilbert space, $|\langle\phi|\psi\rangle|^2 \leq \langle\phi|\phi\rangle\langle\psi|\psi\rangle$, becomes an equality if and only if $|\phi\rangle$ and $|\psi\rangle$ are linearly dependent.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider the norm of $|\phi\rangle - \lambda|\psi\rangle$ for $\lambda = \langle\psi|\phi\rangle / \langle\psi|\psi\rangle$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $\langle\psi|\psi\rangle = 0$ then $|\psi\rangle = 0$ and both sides vanish. Assume $\langle\psi|\psi\rangle > 0$. Set $\lambda = \langle\psi|\phi\rangle / \langle\psi|\psi\rangle$. Then:

$$0 \leq \|\phi - \lambda\psi\|^2 = \langle\phi|\phi\rangle - \frac{|\langle\phi|\psi\rangle|^2}{\langle\psi|\psi\rangle}.$$

Rearranging gives Cauchy-Schwarz. Equality holds iff $\|\phi - \lambda\psi\| = 0$, i.e., $|\phi\rangle = \lambda|\psi\rangle$ — the two kets are linearly dependent.

This inequality underpins the Born rule: the probability $|\langle n|\psi\rangle|^2 \leq \|\psi\|^2 = 1$ is always at most one.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).**

Let $\{|n\rangle\}$ be an orthonormal basis for a finite-dimensional Hilbert space. Show that the completeness relation $\sum_n |n\rangle\langle n| = \mathbb{1}$ is independent of the choice of orthonormal basis: if $\{|m'\rangle\}$ is another orthonormal basis, then $\sum_m |m'\rangle\langle m'| = \sum_n |n\rangle\langle n|$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand each $|m'\rangle$ in the $\{|n\rangle\}$ basis and use orthonormality of the primed basis.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $|m'\rangle = \sum_n |n\rangle\langle n|m'\rangle$. Then:

$$\sum_m |m'\rangle\langle m'| = \sum_m \left(\sum_n |n\rangle\langle n|m'\rangle\right)\left(\sum_k \langle m'|k\rangle\langle k|\right) = \sum_{n,k} |n\rangle\left(\sum_m \langle n|m'\rangle\langle m'|k\rangle\right)\langle k|.$$

The parenthetical sum is $\langle n|k\rangle = \delta_{nk}$ by completeness of the primed basis. So:

$$\sum_{n,k} |n\rangle\,\delta_{nk}\,\langle k| = \sum_n |n\rangle\langle n|.$$

The resolution of the identity is basis-independent — it is a statement about the Hilbert space, not about any particular coordinate system.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).**

Let $A$ be a self-adjoint operator with spectral decomposition $A = \sum_n a_n |n\rangle\langle n|$. Show that for any polynomial $p$, the **functional calculus** identity $p(A) = \sum_n p(a_n) |n\rangle\langle n|$ holds. Deduce that $e^{iAt}$ is unitary when $A$ is self-adjoint.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Verify the identity for monomials by induction on degree, then extend to polynomials by linearity. For the unitary deduction, use the spectral form.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $p(x) = x$: $A = \sum_n a_n |n\rangle\langle n|$ by hypothesis.

For $p(x) = x^k$: by induction, $A^k = A^{k-1} \cdot A = (\sum_n a_n^{k-1} |n\rangle\langle n|)(\sum_m a_m |m\rangle\langle m|) = \sum_n a_n^k |n\rangle\langle n|$, using $\langle n|m\rangle = \delta_{nm}$.

By linearity, $p(A) = \sum_n p(a_n) |n\rangle\langle n|$ for any polynomial.

For $e^{iAt}$ (defined via the power series), the functional calculus gives $e^{iAt} = \sum_n e^{ia_n t} |n\rangle\langle n|$. Then:

$$(e^{iAt})^\dagger = \sum_n e^{-ia_n t} |n\rangle\langle n| = e^{-iAt},$$

and $e^{iAt}(e^{iAt})^\dagger = \sum_n |n\rangle\langle n| = \mathbb{1}$. So $e^{iAt}$ is unitary. This is the structural reason time evolution preserves probabilities.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, conceptual).**

The collapse postulate says that upon measuring outcome $n$, the state becomes $|n\rangle$. Argue that this is an instance of applying the projector $P_n = |n\rangle\langle n|$ followed by renormalisation, and explain why the post-measurement state is an eigenstate of the measured observable with the observed eigenvalue.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $P_n|\psi\rangle$ and identify what remains after normalisation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Before measurement, $|\psi\rangle = \sum_m c_m |m\rangle$. The projector $P_n = |n\rangle\langle n|$ acts as:

$$P_n|\psi\rangle = |n\rangle\langle n|\psi\rangle = c_n |n\rangle.$$

After normalising: $|n\rangle\langle n|\psi\rangle / \|c_n|n\rangle\| = |n\rangle \cdot c_n / |c_n|$, which is $|n\rangle$ up to an overall phase (physically the same state).

The post-measurement state $|n\rangle$ is an eigenstate of the observable $A = \sum_k a_k |k\rangle\langle k|$ with eigenvalue $a_n$: $A|n\rangle = a_n |n\rangle$. So a repeat measurement of the same observable returns $a_n$ with certainty — matching the experimental fact established in the Stern-Gerlach sequential measurements of unit 12.01.02.

## Rigged Hilbert spaces and the continuous spectrum [Master]

The Hilbert-space framework presented above handles discrete spectra cleanly: orthonormal eigenvectors, discrete sums, finite-dimensional spectral decompositions. Physical observables like position and momentum have **continuous spectra** — no normalisable eigenvector exists within $\mathcal{H}$ itself. The formal ket $|x\rangle$, ubiquitous in physics, is not an element of $L^2(\mathbb{R})$.

The rigorous resolution is the **rigged Hilbert space** (also called the **Gelfand triple**):

$$\Phi \subset \mathcal{H} \subset \Phi'.$$

Here $\Phi$ is a dense subspace of $\mathcal{H}$ carrying a stronger topology (typically a nuclear space — e.g., Schwartz space $\mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R}^n)$ of rapidly decreasing smooth functions), and $\Phi'$ is its topological dual (the space of tempered distributions). The eigenkets $|x\rangle$ and $|p\rangle$ live in $\Phi'$, not in $\mathcal{H}$.

**Dirac delta normalisation.** The position eigenkets satisfy the distributional identity $\langle x|y\rangle = \delta(x - y)$ and the continuous completeness relation

$$\int |x\rangle\langle x|\, dx = \mathbb{1},$$

understood as a weak integral on $\Phi$. A state $|\psi\rangle \in \mathcal{H}$ has the expansion $|\psi\rangle = \int \psi(x)|x\rangle\, dx$ where $\psi(x) = \langle x|\psi\rangle \in L^2(\mathbb{R})$ is the position-space wavefunction. The Born rule for position becomes $\mathrm{Prob}(x \in [a,b]) = \int_a^b |\psi(x)|^2\, dx$.

The rigged-Hilbert-space framework was developed by Gelfand and Kostyuchenko in the 1960s (Gelfand-Vilenkin, *Generalized Functions*, Vol. 4, 1964) and independently by Berezanskii and by Maurin (*General Eigenfunction Expansions and Unitary Representations of Topological Groups*, 1968). It gives rigorous meaning to every formal manipulation with Dirac kets that physicists perform, without requiring the kets to be honest vectors in $\mathcal{H}$.

**Stone's theorem.** The connection between self-adjoint operators and one-parameter unitary groups is given by Stone's theorem: a self-adjoint operator $H$ on $\mathcal{H}$ generates a strongly continuous one-parameter unitary group $U(t) = e^{-iHt/\hbar}$, and conversely every such group has a unique self-adjoint generator. This is the rigorous mathematical content of the Schrödinger equation $i\hbar\, d|\psi\rangle/dt = H|\psi\rangle$. Stone 1932 (Ann. Math. 33, 643) is the originator; the proof routes through the spectral theorem.

## Connections [Master]

The Hilbert-space formalism of quantum mechanics connects to classical mechanics via **canonical quantisation**: the classical phase space $T^*Q$ with its Poisson bracket $\{\cdot, \cdot\}$ is replaced by the Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$ with the commutator $(i\hbar)^{-1}[\cdot, \cdot]$. The correspondence $q^i \mapsto \hat{q}^i$, $p_j \mapsto \hat{p}_j$, $\{q^i, p_j\} = \delta^i_j \mapsto [\hat{q}^i, \hat{p}_j] = i\hbar\,\delta^i_j$ is the algebraic bridge from unit 09.04.02 to this unit.

The Dirac notation and the resolution of identity are the computational backbone of every subsequent quantum-mechanics unit: operators and observables (12.02.02), the Schrödinger equation (12.03.01), angular momentum (12.05.01), perturbation theory (12.07.01), and the path integral (12.10.01). Each takes the Hilbert space, its inner product, and its orthonormal bases as primitive inputs.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

The Hilbert-space formalism emerged in three stages. **Hilbert** (1912, *Grundzüge einer allgemeinen Theorie der linearen Integralgleichungen*) developed the spectral theory of bounded quadratic forms — the mathematical substrate — without quantum-mechanical application. **Dirac** (1930, *The Principles of Quantum Mechanics*) introduced the bra-ket notation, the delta function, and the physicist's operational calculus with continuous spectra, all justified by formal manipulation rather than rigour. **von Neumann** (1932, *Mathematische Grundlagen der Quantenmechanik*) placed the entire theory on rigorous mathematical footing: Hilbert spaces, the spectral theorem for unbounded self-adjoint operators, and the density-operator formalism. The tension between Dirac's freewheeling calculus and von Neumann's rigour was resolved by the Gelfand triple construction of the 1960s.

The **reconstruction theorem** of quantum logic (Birkhoff-von Neumann 1936, Piron 1964, Soler 1995) shows that the lattice of closed subspaces of a Hilbert space — equivalently, the lattice of projectors — is the unique lattice-theoretic structure that satisfies the axioms of an orthomodular lattice admitting an infinite orthonormal set over the field of real, complex, or quaternionic numbers. This reconstructs the Hilbert space from the quantum logic of propositions, a perspective that motivated the entire quantum-logic programme.
