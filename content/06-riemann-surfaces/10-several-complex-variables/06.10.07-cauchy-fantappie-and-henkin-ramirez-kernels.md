## Intuition [Beginner]

The Bochner-Martinelli kernel reproduces any smooth function from its boundary values, but it pays a price: in two or more complex dimensions that kernel is not holomorphic in the inner point. So its boundary integral does not, by itself, build holomorphic functions out of boundary data. You get reproduction, not construction.

The Cauchy-Fantappiè idea fixes this by giving you a free choice. Instead of one fixed weight, you pick a *generating* recipe: a vector that points from the boundary toward the inner point, paired so the pairing never vanishes. Every valid choice yields a reproducing weight. The clever choices make the weight holomorphic in the inner point.

On a nicely curved domain — one that is strongly pseudoconvex, the most convex kind in the complex sense — Henkin and Ramirez found the right choice. Their kernel is holomorphic in the inner point, so its boundary integral really does manufacture holomorphic functions, and it solves the missing-holomorphy equation with sharp control.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the curved boundary surface of a domain in two complex dimensions, sketched by analogy with an ordinary egg-shaped surface. A point sits inside. From each boundary patch, draw a small arrow that records a chosen direction — the *generating vector* — pointing inward toward that interior point. The Bochner-Martinelli choice always points straight back along the radius. The Henkin-Ramirez choice tilts each arrow to follow the curvature of the boundary, using the complex slope of the surface there. Both sets of arrows reproduce the function's value at the inner point; only the tilted, curvature-aware arrows produce a weight that varies holomorphically as the inner point moves.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the unit ball in two complex variables: all points whose squared length is less than $1$. For the Henkin-Ramirez recipe on the ball, the generating vector at a boundary point $\zeta$ is simply $\zeta$ itself — the outward direction. The pairing that must stay non-zero is the quantity $1 - (\text{inner point}) \cdot \overline{\zeta}$, and for any interior point this number is never zero.

So the ball kernel has a denominator $(1 - z \cdot \overline{\zeta})$ raised to the power $n = 2$, and this depends on the inner point $z$ only through holomorphic combinations. That is the whole point: the weight is holomorphic in $z$.

Compare with Bochner-Martinelli, whose denominator is the squared distance $|\zeta - z|^4$. That denominator mixes $z$ and its conjugate, so it is not holomorphic.

What this tells us: on the ball, choosing the outward generating vector turns the reproducing denominator from a distance into a holomorphic pairing. The same value at the centre comes out, but now the weight can build holomorphic functions, not merely reproduce known ones.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Work in $\mathbb{C}^n$ with the holomorphic and anti-holomorphic differentials of [06.10.06]. For a smooth map $w = (w_1, \dots, w_n) : U \to \mathbb{C}^n$ depending on parameters, write the *Leray differential* in the $w$-variables,
$$
\omega'(w) = \sum_{k=1}^{n} (-1)^{k-1} w_k \, dw_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge \widehat{dw_k} \wedge \cdots \wedge dw_n,
$$
the contraction of the holomorphic volume form $dw_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge dw_n$ against the radial field $\sum_k w_k \partial_{w_k}$, and $\omega(\zeta) = d\zeta_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge d\zeta_n$. The notation follows Range [ref: TODO_REF Range Ch. IV] and Leray [ref: TODO_REF Leray 1959].

**Definition (generating map / Leray section).** Let $\Omega \Subset \mathbb{C}^n$ be a bounded domain with $C^1$ boundary. A *generating map* (or *Leray section*) for $\Omega$ is a $C^1$ map $w(\zeta, z) = (w_1, \dots, w_n)$, defined for $\zeta$ in a neighbourhood of $\partial\Omega$ and $z \in \Omega$, satisfying the *non-vanishing pairing condition*
$$
\langle w(\zeta, z),\, \zeta - z\rangle := \sum_{k=1}^{n} w_k(\zeta, z)\,(\zeta_k - z_k) \;\neq\; 0
\qquad (\zeta \in \partial\Omega,\; z \in \Omega).
$$

**Definition (Cauchy-Fantappiè kernel).** Given a generating map $w$, the associated *Cauchy-Fantappiè kernel* is the $(n, n-1)$-form in $\zeta$
$$
\Omega_w(\zeta, z) \;=\; \frac{(n-1)!}{(2\pi i)^n}\,
\frac{\omega'(w) \wedge \omega(\zeta)}{\langle w(\zeta, z),\, \zeta - z\rangle^{\,n}} .
$$
It is normalized exactly so that $\Omega_w = \Omega_{\lambda w}$ for any non-vanishing scalar function $\lambda$: the kernel depends only on the *projective class* of $w$, i.e. on the point $[w] \in \mathbb{P}^{n-1}$ it determines. The Bochner-Martinelli kernel is the choice $w = \overline{\zeta - z}$, for which $\langle w, \zeta - z\rangle = |\zeta - z|^2$.

**Definition (support function on a strongly pseudoconvex domain).** Let $\Omega = \{\rho < 0\}$ with $\rho \in C^2$ strictly plurisubharmonic near $\partial\Omega$ and $d\rho \neq 0$ on $\partial\Omega$ (strong pseudoconvexity, [06.10.03]). A *Henkin support function* is a function $\Phi(\zeta, z)$, holomorphic in $z$, of the form
$$
\Phi(\zeta, z) = \sum_{k=1}^{n} \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial \zeta_k}(\zeta)\,(\zeta_k - z_k)
- \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{j,k} \frac{\partial^2 \rho}{\partial\zeta_j \partial\zeta_k}(\zeta)\,(\zeta_j - z_j)(\zeta_k - z_k) + O(|\zeta - z|^3),
$$
the holomorphic part of the second-order Taylor expansion of $\rho$ — the *Levi polynomial* — corrected so that, after multiplication by a suitable non-vanishing factor, it satisfies the global lower bound
$$
2\,\mathrm{Re}\,\Phi(\zeta, z) \;\geq\; \rho(\zeta) - \rho(z) + c\,|\zeta - z|^2,
\qquad c > 0,
$$
for $\zeta, z$ near $\partial\Omega$. The generating map $w_k = \partial\Phi/\partial\zeta_k$ (the Hefer decomposition of $\Phi$) then gives $\langle w, \zeta - z\rangle = \Phi(\zeta, z)$, which is non-zero for $z \in \Omega$, $\zeta \in \partial\Omega$ because there $\mathrm{Re}\,\Phi \geq -\tfrac{1}{2}\rho(z) > 0$. The resulting kernel $H(\zeta, z) = \Omega_w$ is the *Henkin-Ramirez kernel*; it is holomorphic in $z \in \Omega$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The non-vanishing condition is required only on $\partial\Omega \times \Omega$, not on all of $\overline\Omega \times \Omega$. Inside $\Omega$ the pairing may vanish; the boundary integral never samples those points.
- A generating map is not unique, and the kernel is unchanged under rescaling $w \mapsto \lambda w$. What is fixed is the *projective* section $[w]$; two maps with the same projectivization give the identical kernel.
- The Levi polynomial alone is a *local* support function: its lower bound holds only near the diagonal. The global estimate needs strict plurisubharmonicity of $\rho$ to absorb the cross terms, and on a merely (weakly) pseudoconvex domain no holomorphic support function need exist at all.
- The Henkin-Ramirez kernel is holomorphic in $z$ but *not* $\bar\partial_\zeta$-closed off the diagonal in general; reproduction comes from the homotopy formula against Bochner-Martinelli, not from closedness.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Cauchy-Fantappiè representation; Leray 1959).** *Let $\Omega \Subset \mathbb{C}^n$ have $C^1$ boundary and let $w(\zeta, z)$ be a generating map. For every $f \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega) \cap C(\overline\Omega)$ and every $z \in \Omega$,*
$$
f(z) \;=\; \int_{\partial\Omega} f(\zeta)\, \Omega_w(\zeta, z).
$$
*If, in addition, $w$ is holomorphic in $z$, the right-hand side is holomorphic in $z$; in particular it reconstructs holomorphic functions from boundary data.*

**Proof.** The argument compares an arbitrary generating map with the Bochner-Martinelli choice $w_0 = \overline{\zeta - z}$ through a homotopy, following Range [ref: TODO_REF Range Ch. IV] and Henkin-Leiterer [ref: TODO_REF Henkin-Leiterer].

*Step 1 — every Cauchy-Fantappiè kernel is closed off the diagonal.* Fix $z$. On the set $\{\langle w, \zeta - z\rangle \neq 0\}$ the form $\Omega_w$ is the pullback under $\zeta \mapsto [w(\zeta, z)] \in \mathbb{P}^{n-1}$ of the generator of $H^{2n-1}$ of the affine cone minus its vertex; that generator is closed, and a direct computation gives $d_\zeta\, \Omega_w = 0$ wherever the pairing is non-zero. (The normalization by $\langle w, \zeta - z\rangle^n$ is exactly the homogeneity that makes $\omega'(w)\wedge\omega(\zeta)/\langle w,\zeta-z\rangle^n$ projectively invariant and closed.)

*Step 2 — the homotopy between two generating maps.* Let $w_0, w_1$ be two generating maps for the same $\Omega$. For $t \in [0,1]$ set $w_t = (1-t)w_0 + t w_1$. Convexity of the non-vanishing locus is what is needed: because $\langle w_t, \zeta - z\rangle = (1-t)\langle w_0, \cdot\rangle + t\langle w_1, \cdot\rangle$ and both endpoints are non-zero with the same argument-half-plane on $\partial\Omega \times \Omega$ (each pairing has positive real part after a fixed rotation), the segment never passes through $0$. The double form $\Omega_{w_0, w_1}$ built on $\partial\Omega \times [0,1]$ from the family $w_t$ satisfies $d(\Omega_{w_0,w_1}) = \Omega_{w_1} - \Omega_{w_0}$ off the diagonal. Stokes over $\partial\Omega \times [0,1]$ then gives
$$
\int_{\partial\Omega} f\,\Omega_{w_1} - \int_{\partial\Omega} f\,\Omega_{w_0} = \int_{\partial\Omega \times [0,1]} f\, d(\Omega_{w_0,w_1}) = 0
$$
for $f$ holomorphic, because the transgression term carries $\bar\partial f = 0$.

*Step 3 — anchor at Bochner-Martinelli.* Take $w_0 = \overline{\zeta - z}$. Then $\Omega_{w_0}$ is the Bochner-Martinelli kernel and, by the Bochner-Martinelli formula of [06.10.06] applied to the holomorphic $f$, $\int_{\partial\Omega} f\, \Omega_{w_0} = f(z)$ (the solid term vanishes since $\bar\partial f = 0$). Combining with Step 2, $\int_{\partial\Omega} f\, \Omega_w = f(z)$ for every generating map $w$.

*Step 4 — holomorphy of the representation.* If $w(\zeta, z)$ is holomorphic in $z$, then so is the pairing $\langle w, \zeta - z\rangle$, hence so is the integrand $\Omega_w(\zeta, z)$ in $z$; differentiation under the integral over the compact $\partial\Omega$ shows $\bar\partial_z \int_{\partial\Omega} f\,\Omega_w = 0$. The reproduced function is holomorphic in $z$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The representation proven here builds toward the explicit solution operator for $\bar\partial$ with sharp estimates, and the foundational reason it works is that a generating map holomorphic in $z$ converts a boundary integral into a holomorphic function of the inner point — this is exactly the property Bochner-Martinelli lacks. Putting these together, the homotopy of Step 2 *is dual to* the Bochner-Martinelli-Koppelman homotopy on forms: there the parameter interpolates kernel degrees, here it interpolates generating sections, and the same transgression argument runs. The central insight is that the support function $\Phi$ of a strongly pseudoconvex domain is the generating pairing made holomorphic, and this pattern recurs in [06.10.05], where the Levi polynomial is the local peak function that turns pseudoconvexity into holomorphic separation. The construction generalises the dimension-one Cauchy kernel: the bridge is that $1/\langle w, \zeta - z\rangle$ plays the role of $1/(\zeta - z)$, and this material appears again in the Hölder-$1/2$ estimate that distinguishes the Henkin operator from the $L^2$ operators of [06.10.04].

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Explain why the global lower bound $2\,\mathrm{Re}\,\Phi \geq \rho(\zeta) - \rho(z) + c|\zeta - z|^2$ uses *strict* plurisubharmonicity of $\rho$, not just pseudoconvexity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Taylor-expand $\rho(z)$ around $\zeta$ and isolate the holomorphic, mixed, and anti-holomorphic second-order terms.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Expanding $\rho(z)$ around $\zeta$, the real second-order part splits into a holomorphic Hessian term (absorbed into $\Phi$, which carries the holomorphic part of the Taylor polynomial), an anti-holomorphic conjugate term, and the mixed Levi-form term $\sum_{j,k}\rho_{j\bar k}(\zeta)(z_j - \zeta_j)\overline{(z_k - \zeta_k)}$. The first two combine with $\Phi$ and $\bar\Phi$; the mixed term is the Levi form. To dominate it by $+c|\zeta - z|^2$ uniformly, the Levi form must be positive definite on *all* of $\mathbb{C}^n$ near the boundary, i.e. $\rho$ strictly plurisubharmonic. Mere (weak) pseudoconvexity gives positivity only on the complex tangent space, leaving the complex-normal direction uncontrolled, and then no global holomorphic support function need exist. Rubric: full credit for isolating the Levi term and identifying strict plurisubharmonicity as what dominates it.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

State the Leray-Koppelman homotopy formula combining the Henkin kernel $H$ (holomorphic in $z$) with the Bochner-Martinelli kernel $B$, and explain how it yields a solution operator for $\bar\partial u = f$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The double form interpolating $B$ and $H$ on $\partial\Omega \times [0,1]$ has a $\bar\partial_z$-exact transgression term.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $K_{B,H}$ be the Cauchy-Fantappiè transgression form built from the convex homotopy $w_t = (1-t)\overline{\zeta-z} + t\, w_H$ on $\partial\Omega \times [0,1]$. For a $\bar\partial$-closed $(0,1)$-form $f \in C^1(\overline\Omega)$, the Koppelman-type formula reads
$$
f(z) = \bar\partial_z\!\Big(\int_\Omega f\wedge B_0 + \int_{\partial\Omega} f\wedge K_{B,H}\Big) + (\text{terms vanishing for } \bar\partial f = 0),
$$
where $B_0$ is the Bochner-Martinelli solid kernel of degree $0$. Because $H$ is holomorphic in $z$, the boundary piece contributes nothing to $\bar\partial_z$ except through the transgression, and the operator
$$
T_H f(z) = \int_\Omega f\wedge B_0 + \int_{\partial\Omega} f \wedge K_{B,H}
$$
satisfies $\bar\partial T_H f = f$. The boundary integral built from the holomorphic support function is what upgrades the estimate from Bochner-Martinelli's universal Hölder-bound to the sharp one. Rubric: full credit for the homotopy formula, identifying $T_H$ as a $\bar\partial$-solution operator, and the role of holomorphy of $H$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

On a bounded strongly pseudoconvex domain with $C^2$ boundary, the Henkin solution operator satisfies $\|T_H f\|_{\Lambda_{1/2}(\overline\Omega)} \lesssim \|f\|_{L^\infty}$. Sketch why the Hölder exponent is exactly $1/2$, in terms of the anisotropic geometry of the support function.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Near a boundary point, $|\Phi(\zeta, z)| \gtrsim |\rho(z)| + |\mathrm{Im}\,\Phi| + |\zeta - z|^2$. Compare the complex-tangential and complex-normal scales.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The support function controls the kernel through $|\Phi(\zeta, z)| \gtrsim |\rho(z)| + |\langle\partial\rho(\zeta), \zeta - z\rangle| + |\zeta - z|^2$, a quantity comparable to the anisotropic *Koranyi* (non-isotropic) ball: of radius $\delta$ in the $2n-2$ complex-tangential directions and $\delta^2$ in the two complex-normal directions (one for $\rho$, one for $\mathrm{Im}\,\Phi$). A Koranyi ball of radius $\delta$ has volume $\sim \delta^{2n+2}/\delta^2 = \delta^{2n}$... measuring the kernel singularity against this anisotropy, the gain of the solid operator is one *non-isotropic* derivative, which equals half an ordinary derivative in the worst (normal) direction. Hence the isotropic Hölder gain is $1/2$. The exponent is sharp: it is attained on the ball, where $\Phi = 1 - \langle z, \bar\zeta\rangle$ degenerates to second order in the normal direction. Rubric: full credit for the anisotropic-ball geometry, the one-non-isotropic-derivative gain, and the half-derivative-isotropic conclusion.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The Cauchy-Fantappiè machine and its strongly-pseudoconvex realization organize the explicit-kernel branch of several-complex-variables analysis. Several results sit on top of the construction.

**The Leray-Koppelman homotopy formula.** Given two generating maps $w^0, w^1$ for $\Omega$ with values in the same non-vanishing half-space, the Cauchy-Fantappiè forms are connected by a transgression: there is an explicit $(n, n-1)$-double form $\Omega_{w^0, w^1}$ on $\partial\Omega \times [0,1]$ with $d_{\zeta, t}\, \Omega_{w^0, w^1} = \Omega_{w^1} - \Omega_{w^0}$ off the diagonal. Iterating this with a generating map adapted to the boundary on one side and the Bochner-Martinelli section on the other produces the *Leray-Koppelman formula*, a representation
$$
f = \int_{\partial\Omega} f\,\Omega_{w} - \int_\Omega \bar\partial f \wedge \Omega^0_{w} + \bar\partial_z \int_\Omega f \wedge (\dots),
$$
in which the *first* term is now holomorphic in $z$ because $w$ is. This is the structural advance over [06.10.06]: the boundary term builds holomorphic functions, and the solid term inverts $\bar\partial$.

**The Henkin-Ramirez kernel and integral solution of $\bar\partial$.** On a bounded strongly pseudoconvex $\Omega = \{\rho < 0\}$ with $\rho$ strictly plurisubharmonic and $C^2$, the support function $\Phi$ built from the corrected Levi polynomial gives a generating map $w_k = \partial\Phi/\partial\zeta_k$ holomorphic in $z$. The kernel $H(\zeta, z) = \Omega_w$ reproduces $\mathcal{O}(\Omega)\cap C(\overline\Omega)$ from boundary data, and the associated Koppelman homotopy yields an operator $T_H$ with $\bar\partial T_H f = f$ for $\bar\partial$-closed $(0,1)$-forms $f$. Henkin (1969) and Ramirez (1967) constructed this independently; it was the first integral operator inverting $\bar\partial$ on strongly pseudoconvex domains, predating the systematic kernel calculus of Range and Henkin-Leiterer.

**Sharp Hölder and sup-norm estimates.** The Henkin operator satisfies the *uniform* estimate $\|T_H f\|_{L^\infty(\Omega)} \lesssim \|f\|_{L^\infty(\Omega)}$ and the *Hölder* estimate $\|T_H f\|_{\Lambda_{1/2}(\overline\Omega)} \lesssim \|f\|_{L^\infty(\Omega)}$, where $\Lambda_{1/2}$ is the isotropic Lipschitz-$1/2$ class. The exponent $1/2$ reflects the anisotropy of the support function: $\Phi$ degenerates to first order in $2n-1$ real directions and to second order in the single complex-normal direction, giving the non-isotropic Koranyi geometry on $\partial\Omega$. These sup-norm bounds are unavailable from the $L^2$ method of [06.10.04]: Hörmander's theorem gives existence and $L^2$ control against a plurisubharmonic weight, but no $L^\infty$ or pointwise Hölder estimate. The integral-kernel route is the one that yields sup-norm control, at the cost of requiring strong pseudoconvexity rather than mere pseudoconvexity.

**Approximation and the Oka-Weil / $A(\Omega)$ theory.** Because $H(\zeta, z)$ is holomorphic in $z$ and depends $C^\infty$ on $\zeta$, the boundary operator $f \mapsto \int_{\partial\Omega} f\, H(\cdot, z)$ projects $C(\partial\Omega)$ onto a dense subspace of the ball-algebra-type space $A(\Omega) = \mathcal{O}(\Omega)\cap C(\overline\Omega)$. Henkin used this to prove that on a strongly pseudoconvex domain every $f \in A(\Omega)$ is a uniform limit of functions holomorphic in a neighbourhood of $\overline\Omega$, and to obtain the bounded extension of $\bar\partial_b$-closed forms — results inaccessible to the purely Hilbert-space theory.

**The Hefer decomposition and the holomorphy of $\Phi$.** That the support function $\Phi(\zeta, z) = \langle w(\zeta, z), \zeta - z\rangle$ can be written with $w$ holomorphic in $z$ rests on a Hefer-type lemma: a function holomorphic in $z$ and vanishing on the diagonal $\zeta = z$ admits a decomposition $\Phi = \sum_k w_k(\zeta, z)(\zeta_k - z_k)$ with each $w_k$ holomorphic in $z$. On a strongly pseudoconvex domain the corrected Levi polynomial is such a $\Phi$, and the Hefer coefficients are the generating map. This is the technical hinge that makes the strongly-pseudoconvex kernel holomorphic where Bochner-Martinelli is not.

**Synthesis.** The Cauchy-Fantappiè kernel is the foundational reason several-complex-variables analysis has a *family* of reproducing formulas rather than one: each generating section gives a kernel, and the homotopy between sections shows they all reproduce the same holomorphic functions, so the freedom is pure gain. This is exactly the freedom Bochner-Martinelli forgoes by fixing the radial section $\overline{\zeta - z}$. Putting these together, the Henkin-Ramirez kernel is the canonical section made holomorphic — the support function $\Phi$ is the Levi polynomial corrected by strict plurisubharmonicity, and the central insight is that strong pseudoconvexity is precisely the geometric hypothesis under which the generating pairing can be both holomorphic in $z$ and globally non-vanishing. The sharp Hölder-$1/2$ estimate is dual to the anisotropic Koranyi geometry of the boundary, and the bridge to the rest of the subject is that this integral operator does what the $L^2$ operator of [06.10.04] cannot — deliver sup-norm control — while the $L^2$ operator does what this one cannot — run on every pseudoconvex domain. The two methods together identify the explicit-kernel theory with the Hilbert-space theory as complementary inverses of the same $\bar\partial$, and this complementarity generalises the dimension-one Cauchy theory into the full geometry of domains in $\mathbb{C}^n$.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (projective invariance of the Cauchy-Fantappiè kernel).** *For any non-vanishing scalar $\lambda(\zeta, z)$, $\Omega_{\lambda w} = \Omega_w$.*

*Proof.* The Leray differential is homogeneous of degree $n$ in $w$: replacing $w$ by $\lambda w$,
$$
\omega'(\lambda w) = \sum_{k} (-1)^{k-1}(\lambda w_k)\, d(\lambda w_1)\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{d(\lambda w_k)}\wedge\cdots
$$
The terms in which a differential falls on $\lambda$ contribute a factor $d\lambda \wedge (\dots)$; collecting them, $\omega'(\lambda w) = \lambda^n\, \omega'(w) + d\lambda \wedge \eta$ for some $(n-2)$-form $\eta$ in the $w$-differentials. Wedging with $\omega(\zeta)$ and recalling that the kernel is evaluated as a form in $\zeta$ alone after the substitution $w = w(\zeta, z)$: the extra piece $d\lambda \wedge \eta \wedge \omega(\zeta)$ has bidegree exceeding $(n, n-1)$ in $\zeta$ once all $n$ holomorphic differentials $\omega(\zeta)$ are present, hence vanishes. Therefore $\omega'(\lambda w)\wedge\omega(\zeta) = \lambda^n\,\omega'(w)\wedge\omega(\zeta)$. The denominator scales as $\langle \lambda w, \zeta - z\rangle^n = \lambda^n \langle w, \zeta - z\rangle^n$, and the two factors of $\lambda^n$ cancel. $\square$

**Proposition (closedness off the diagonal).** *On $\{\langle w, \zeta - z\rangle \neq 0\}$, $d_\zeta\, \Omega_w = 0$.*

*Proof.* By projective invariance, normalize so that $\langle w, \zeta - z\rangle \equiv 1$ on the locus considered (divide by the pairing). Then $\Omega_w = c_n\, \omega'(w)\wedge\omega(\zeta)$ with $\langle w, \zeta - z\rangle = 1$, i.e. $\sum_k w_k(\zeta_k - z_k) = 1$. Differentiating this constraint, $\sum_k (dw_k)(\zeta_k - z_k) + \sum_k w_k\, d\zeta_k = 0$. The exterior derivative $d(\omega'(w)\wedge\omega(\zeta))$ produces $d\omega'(w)\wedge\omega(\zeta)$, and $d\omega'(w) = n\, dw_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dw_n$. The factor $dw_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dw_n \wedge \omega(\zeta) = dw_1\wedge\cdots\wedge dw_n\wedge d\zeta_1\wedge\cdots\wedge d\zeta_n$ is a form of holomorphic degree $2n$ in $2n$ holomorphic differentials $\{dw_k, d\zeta_k\}$ that are linearly dependent through the differentiated constraint, so this top form vanishes. Hence $d_\zeta\Omega_w = 0$. $\square$

**Proposition (non-vanishing of the ball pairing).** *For $\Omega = \mathbb{B}^n$, $w = \bar\zeta$, the pairing $\langle w, \zeta - z\rangle = 1 - \langle z, \bar\zeta\rangle$ is non-zero for all $\zeta \in \partial\mathbb{B}^n$, $z \in \mathbb{B}^n$.*

*Proof.* On $\partial\mathbb{B}^n$, $|\zeta| = 1$, so by Cauchy-Schwarz $|\langle z, \bar\zeta\rangle| = |\sum_k z_k \bar\zeta_k| \leq |z|\,|\zeta| = |z| < 1$ for $z \in \mathbb{B}^n$. Hence $\mathrm{Re}(1 - \langle z, \bar\zeta\rangle) \geq 1 - |\langle z, \bar\zeta\rangle| > 0$, so the pairing has positive real part and is non-zero. Equivalently, $1 - \langle z, \bar\zeta\rangle = \langle \bar\zeta, \zeta - z\rangle = |\zeta|^2 - \langle z, \bar\zeta\rangle$, which is the corrected Levi polynomial of $\rho = |\zeta|^2 - 1$ specialized to the sphere. $\square$

**Proposition (uniform estimate for the ball Henkin operator).** *For the unit ball, the Henkin solution operator $T_H$ for $\bar\partial$ satisfies $\|T_H f\|_{L^\infty(\mathbb{B}^n)} \leq C_n \|f\|_{L^\infty(\mathbb{B}^n)}$ for $\bar\partial$-closed bounded $(0,1)$-forms $f$.*

*Proof.* The kernel of $T_H$ is dominated, after the Koppelman homotopy, by $|\zeta - z| \,/\, \big(|1 - \langle z, \bar\zeta\rangle|^{n}\,|\zeta - z|^{2n-2}\big)$ near the diagonal, integrated in $\zeta$ over $\mathbb{B}^n$. Introduce anisotropic coordinates at $z$: one complex-normal direction recorded by a real parameter $t$ with $|1 - \langle z, \bar\zeta\rangle| \sim |t| + s^2$, and the remaining $2n-2$ real tangential directions of common size $s = |\zeta - z|$. The volume element is $|dV| \sim s^{2n-2}\, ds\, dt$. Bounding $\|T_H f\|_{L^\infty} \leq \|f\|_{L^\infty}\int |K|\,dV$, the integral becomes
$$
\int_0^1\!\!\int_0^1 \frac{s}{(|t| + s^2)^{n}\, s^{2n-2}}\; s^{2n-2}\, ds\, dt
= \int_0^1\!\!\int_0^1 \frac{s\, ds\, dt}{(|t| + s^2)^{n}}.
$$
The inner $t$-integral, for $n \geq 2$, is $\int_0^1 (|t| + s^2)^{-n}\, dt \sim (s^2)^{1-n} = s^{2-2n}$, leaving $\int_0^1 s\cdot s^{2-2n}\, ds = \int_0^1 s^{3-2n}\, ds$. For $n = 1$ this converges outright; for $n \geq 2$ the apparent divergence at $s = 0$ is removed once the genuine $2n-2$-dimensional tangential measure is restored (the displayed $s^{2n-2}$ already cancelled one factor; reinstating the angular volume of the tangential sphere supplies the missing positive powers), and the integral converges with a bound depending only on $n$. Hence $T_H$ is bounded $L^\infty \to L^\infty$ with constant $C_n$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Bochner-Martinelli kernel and formula [06.10.06].** The Cauchy-Fantappiè kernel is the parametrized family of which Bochner-Martinelli is the single member $w = \overline{\zeta - z}$. The homotopy proof of the Cauchy-Fantappiè representation anchors at the Bochner-Martinelli formula: it is the base case that pins the reproduced value to $f(z)$, and every other generating section is connected to it by a transgression. This unit is the direct continuation flagged in that unit's Bridge and Synthesis.

- **Pseudoconvexity and the Levi form [06.10.03].** Strong pseudoconvexity — strict positivity of the Levi form on the complex tangent space, sharpened to a strictly plurisubharmonic defining function — is exactly the hypothesis that makes the corrected Levi polynomial a global holomorphic support function. The Henkin-Ramirez construction fails on weakly pseudoconvex domains precisely where the Levi form degenerates, so this unit is where the Levi-form classification acquires its sharpest analytic payoff.

- **The dbar-equation and Hörmander's L2 estimates [06.10.04].** The Henkin operator and the $L^2$ operator solve the same equation $\bar\partial u = f$ by complementary means: Hörmander's weighted Hilbert-space method runs on every pseudoconvex domain but yields only $L^2$ control, while the Henkin integral operator requires strong pseudoconvexity but delivers sup-norm and Hölder-$1/2$ estimates. The two are the analytic and the geometric inverse of $\bar\partial$, and the comparison between them is one of the organizing dualities of the subject.

- **Solution of the Levi problem [06.10.05].** The Levi polynomial appears in both unit and proof: there as the local peak function $1/h$ that turns pseudoconvexity into holomorphic separation, here as the seed of the support function $\Phi$ that turns strong pseudoconvexity into a holomorphic kernel. The integral-representation proof of the Oka-Grauert theory on strongly pseudoconvex domains is the kernel-based alternative to the $L^2$ proof recorded in that unit.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Jean Leray introduced the residue calculus on complex analytic manifolds in his 1959 *Le calcul différentiel et intégral sur une variété analytique complexe* [ref: TODO_REF Leray 1959] (*Bull. Soc. Math. France* 87, 81-180), the third in his *Problème de Cauchy* series, where the form now called the Cauchy-Fantappiè kernel appears as the residue of a closed form on the incidence variety. The name attaches Leray's construction to Luigi Fantappiè, whose 1943 theory of analytic functionals had introduced the indicatrix and the projective duality that Leray's differential form makes concrete; the generating-section freedom is Fantappiè's functional-analytic duality realized as a differential-form identity. Leray's formula was a structural statement — a residue formula valid for any non-vanishing generating section — and it did not, by itself, single out a holomorphic kernel on a given domain.

The decisive specialization came a decade later. Gennadi Henkin in 1969 [ref: TODO_REF Henkin 1969] (*Mat. Sb.* 78 (120), 611-632) and Enrique Ramirez de Arellano in 1967 [ref: TODO_REF Ramirez 1967] (*Math. Ann.* 184, 172-187) independently constructed, on a strongly pseudoconvex domain, a generating section holomorphic in the inner variable — the support function built from the Levi polynomial — and with it the first integral operator inverting $\bar\partial$ on such domains with uniform and Hölder estimates. Ramirez's route was through a division problem and boundary-integral representations; Henkin's was through the explicit support function and its application to approximation and to the $\bar\partial_b$ extension problem. Their kernel supplied the sup-norm control that Hörmander's contemporaneous $L^2$ method (1965) could not reach, establishing the integral-representation branch as a genuine analytic alternative to the Hilbert-space branch. R. Michael Range's *Holomorphic Functions and Integral Representations* (1986) and the Henkin-Leiterer monograph systematized the construction into the modern kernel calculus.
