## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

This pack supplements Krantz's *Function Theory of Several Complex Variables*: the rigidity that distinguishes $\mathbb{C}^n$ ($n\geq 2$) from $\mathbb{C}$, expressed through the Hartogs extension phenomenon, domains of holomorphy and holomorphic convexity, plurisubharmonicity and the Levi form, the $\bar\partial$-equation with Hörmander $L^2$ estimates, and the Bergman kernel. Its problems exercise the units on domains of holomorphy and holomorphic convexity [06.10.01], plurisubharmonic functions [06.10.02], pseudoconvexity and the Levi form [06.10.03], the $\bar\partial$-equation and Hörmander $L^2$ estimates [06.10.04], the Bergman kernel and metric [06.10.08], and the Hartogs phenomenon [06.07.02].

The pack collects ten problems — two easy, five medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units rather than as a standalone development. The problems are grouped by theme: Hartogs extension and the failure of one-variable intuition (easy/medium), plurisubharmonicity and Levi-form computations on standard domains (medium), the $\bar\partial$-$L^2$ existence theorem and its corollaries (hard), and the Bergman kernel's reproducing and transformation properties (medium/hard). Conventions follow Krantz: a domain $\Omega\subset\mathbb{C}^n$ is *Levi pseudoconvex* at a boundary point if the Levi form $\mathcal L_\rho(p; w) = \sum_{j,k}\frac{\partial^2\rho}{\partial z_j\partial\bar z_k}(p)\,w_j\bar w_k$ of a defining function $\rho$ is $\geq 0$ on the complex tangent space $\{w : \sum_j\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial z_j}(p)w_j = 0\}$; the Bergman space is $A^2(\Omega) = \mathcal O(\Omega)\cap L^2(\Omega)$.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one problem in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining nine follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead problem.** *Prove the Hartogs extension phenomenon on the Hartogs figure: every $f$ holomorphic on the figure $H = \{(z,w)\in\Delta^2 : |z| > 1 - \varepsilon\}\cup\{|w| < \varepsilon\}$ extends holomorphically to the full bidisc $\Delta^2$.*

**Solution.** Write $\Delta = \{|z| < 1\}$ and fix the Hartogs figure $H\subset\Delta^2$ as stated: it contains a neighbourhood of the boundary annulus in the first variable together with a thin slab near $w = 0$. For each fixed $z\in\Delta$, consider the circle $|w| = r$ with $1 - \varepsilon < $ chosen so that the slice $\{z\}\times\{|w| = r\}$ lies in $H$ for some admissible $r$ close to $1$; concretely, for every $z\in\Delta$ the circle $\{z\}\times\{|w| = 1 - \varepsilon/2\}$ lies in $H$ because $|z| < 1$ is covered through the $|w| < \varepsilon$ slab continued by the $|z| > 1 - \varepsilon$ shell. Define

$$
F(z,w) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|\zeta| = 1 - \varepsilon/2}\frac{f(z,\zeta)}{\zeta - w}\,d\zeta, \qquad |w| < 1 - \varepsilon/2.
$$

For each fixed $z$, $w\mapsto f(z,w)$ is holomorphic on the disc $|w| < 1 - \varepsilon/2$ where the slice is interior to $H$, so by the one-variable Cauchy integral formula $F(z,w) = f(z,w)$ there. The integral $F$ is holomorphic in $(z,w)$ jointly: it is holomorphic in $w$ (parameter integral of a Cauchy kernel) and holomorphic in $z$ (differentiate under the integral; $f(z,\zeta)$ is holomorphic in $z$ and the contour is fixed). By Osgood's lemma, separate holomorphy plus local boundedness gives joint holomorphy, so $F\in\mathcal O(\Delta^2)$.

Finally $F = f$ on the part of $H$ where the Cauchy formula applies, and both are holomorphic, so by the identity theorem $F = f$ on all of $H$. Thus $F$ is the required extension to $\Delta^2$. $\square$

This is the engine of the Hartogs phenomenon: holomorphic functions of $n\geq 2$ variables cannot have isolated or compact singularities, and they extend across "holes" that one-variable functions need not. The Cauchy-integral-in-one-slice construction is the cheapest proof; the Bochner-Martinelli kernel gives the coordinate-free version. Krantz Ch. 2.

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).** *No isolated zeros, no isolated singularities in $\mathbb{C}^n$, $n\geq 2$.*

Show that a holomorphic function on a domain $\Omega\subset\mathbb{C}^n$ with $n\geq 2$ cannot have an isolated zero, and (Hartogs) cannot have an isolated singularity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the zero: if $f(p) = 0$ for isolated $p$, restrict to a complex line through $p$ to get a one-variable function with an isolated zero — but the zero set of $f$, where nonempty, has complex codimension at most $1$, so real dimension $\geq 2n - 2\geq 2 > 0$. For the singularity, use Hartogs extension.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Isolated zeros.* Suppose $f\in\mathcal O(\Omega)$, $f\not\equiv 0$, and $f(p) = 0$. The zero set $V = f^{-1}(0)$ is a complex-analytic hypersurface near $p$ (where $f$ is not identically zero), of complex dimension $n - 1$, hence real dimension $2(n-1) = 2n - 2$. For $n\geq 2$ this is $\geq 2 > 0$, so $V$ is positive-dimensional near $p$ and $p$ is not isolated in $V$. Concretely, by the Weierstrass preparation theorem one may, after a linear change, write $f$ near $p$ as a Weierstrass polynomial in one variable with coefficients holomorphic in the others; its zero set is a branched cover over a polydisc in the remaining $n - 1$ variables — never a single point.

*Isolated singularities.* If $f$ is holomorphic on $\Omega\setminus\{p\}$ with $p$ isolated, surround $p$ by a Hartogs figure (a small punctured polydisc contains one). By the Hartogs extension theorem (the lead problem), $f$ extends holomorphically across $p$. So an isolated singularity is removable; equivalently, holomorphic functions of $n\geq 2$ variables have no isolated singularities.

This is the first and most striking rigidity of higher dimensions: the Riemann-sphere picture of isolated poles has no several-variable analogue. Krantz Ch. 2.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *The Bergman kernel of the unit disc.*

Compute the Bergman kernel $K_\Delta(z,w)$ of the unit disc $\Delta\subset\mathbb{C}$ using the orthonormal basis $e_n(z) = \sqrt{\frac{n+1}{\pi}}\,z^n$ of $A^2(\Delta)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$K(z,w) = \sum_n e_n(z)\overline{e_n(w)}$. Sum the resulting geometric-type series $\sum_n (n+1)(z\bar w)^n$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The monomials $z^n$ are orthogonal in $A^2(\Delta)$, with $\|z^n\|^2 = \int_\Delta|z|^{2n}\,dA = \int_0^1\int_0^{2\pi}r^{2n}\,r\,d\theta\,dr = 2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{2n+2} = \frac{\pi}{n+1}$. So $e_n(z) = \sqrt{\frac{n+1}{\pi}}\,z^n$ is orthonormal.

The Bergman kernel is

$$
K_\Delta(z,w) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty e_n(z)\overline{e_n(w)} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{n+1}{\pi}(z\bar w)^n = \frac{1}{\pi}\sum_{n=0}^\infty (n+1)\,t^n,\quad t = z\bar w.
$$

Using $\sum_{n=0}^\infty(n+1)t^n = \frac{1}{(1 - t)^2}$ for $|t| < 1$,

$$
K_\Delta(z,w) = \frac{1}{\pi}\cdot\frac{1}{(1 - z\bar w)^2}.
$$

So $K_\Delta(z,w) = \dfrac{1}{\pi(1 - z\bar w)^2}$. On the diagonal, $K_\Delta(z,z) = \frac{1}{\pi(1 - |z|^2)^2}\to\infty$ as $z\to\partial\Delta$ — the kernel blows up at the boundary, the source of the Bergman metric's completeness. Krantz, Bergman-kernel chapter.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).** *Levi form of the ball.*

Compute the Levi form of the unit ball $B = \{z\in\mathbb{C}^n : |z|^2 < 1\}$ with defining function $\rho(z) = |z|^2 - 1$, and verify the ball is strongly pseudoconvex.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\frac{\partial^2\rho}{\partial z_j\partial\bar z_k} = \delta_{jk}$, so the Levi form is $\sum_j|w_j|^2 = |w|^2$ on the complex tangent space. It is strictly positive for $w\neq 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $\rho(z) = |z|^2 - 1 = \sum_j z_j\bar z_j - 1$, the complex Hessian is

$$
\frac{\partial^2\rho}{\partial z_j\partial\bar z_k} = \frac{\partial}{\partial z_j}\bar z_k = \delta_{jk}.
$$

So the Levi form at a boundary point $p$ (with $|p| = 1$) is

$$
\mathcal L_\rho(p; w) = \sum_{j,k}\delta_{jk}\,w_j\bar w_k = \sum_j|w_j|^2 = |w|^2.
$$

On the complex tangent space $T^{1,0}_p\partial B = \{w : \sum_j\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial z_j}(p)\,w_j = 0\} = \{w : \sum_j\bar p_j w_j = 0\}$, the Levi form is still $|w|^2$, which is strictly positive for every $w\neq 0$ in that subspace.

A strictly positive Levi form on the complex tangent space (for all nonzero $w$) is exactly the definition of **strong** (strict) pseudoconvexity. So the ball is strongly pseudoconvex at every boundary point. The ball and the polydisc are the two running examples in Krantz; the ball is strongly pseudoconvex everywhere, while the polydisc is (weakly) pseudoconvex but not strongly so on its distinguished boundary edges. Krantz Ch. 3.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *$\log|f|$ and $|f|^2$ are plurisubharmonic.*

Let $f$ be holomorphic on $\Omega\subset\mathbb{C}^n$. Show that $\log|f|$ and $|f|^2$ are plurisubharmonic.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A function is plurisubharmonic if its restriction to every complex line is subharmonic. On a line, $f$ restricts to a one-variable holomorphic function, and $\log|g|$, $|g|^2$ are subharmonic for one-variable holomorphic $g$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A function $u$ is plurisubharmonic (PSH) iff for every complex line $\ell: \zeta\mapsto a + \zeta b$ ($a\in\Omega$, $b\in\mathbb{C}^n$), the restriction $\zeta\mapsto u(a + \zeta b)$ is subharmonic on its domain in $\mathbb{C}$.

Restrict $f$ to such a line: $g(\zeta) = f(a + \zeta b)$ is holomorphic in the single variable $\zeta$.

*$\log|f|$.* On the line, $\log|f| = \log|g|$. For one-variable holomorphic $g$, $\log|g|$ is subharmonic: away from zeros of $g$ it is harmonic (real part of the holomorphic $\log g$, locally), and at zeros $\log|g|\to-\infty$, where subharmonicity is the sub-mean-value inequality $\log|g(\zeta_0)| = -\infty\leq$ average — automatic. So $\log|g|$ is subharmonic, hence $\log|f|$ is PSH.

*$|f|^2$.* On the line, $|f|^2 = |g|^2$. Compute the Laplacian: $\Delta|g|^2 = 4\partial_\zeta\partial_{\bar\zeta}(g\bar g) = 4|g'|^2\geq 0$ (since $g$ is holomorphic, $\partial_{\bar\zeta}g = 0$ and $\partial_\zeta\bar g = 0$). A function with $\Delta\geq 0$ is subharmonic, so $|g|^2$ is subharmonic, hence $|f|^2$ is PSH.

Plurisubharmonicity of $\log|f|$ is the cornerstone: it makes $-\log d(\cdot,\partial\Omega)$-type exhaustion functions plurisubharmonic on pseudoconvex domains and drives the Cartan-Thullen and $\bar\partial$-$L^2$ machinery. Krantz Ch. 3.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).** *Holomorphic convexity of a domain of holomorphy.*

Show that if $\Omega\subset\mathbb{C}^n$ is a domain of holomorphy, then it is holomorphically convex: for every compact $K\subset\Omega$, its holomorphic hull $\widehat K_\Omega = \{z\in\Omega : |f(z)|\leq\sup_K|f|\ \forall f\in\mathcal O(\Omega)\}$ is compact.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\widehat K_\Omega$ is closed in $\Omega$ and bounded; the issue is that it might approach $\partial\Omega$. Use that on a domain of holomorphy there is, for each boundary point, a holomorphic function that is unbounded there — so the hull stays away from the boundary.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\widehat K_\Omega$ is bounded (it lies in the bounded set $\{|z_j|\leq\sup_K|z_j|\}$, taking $f = z_j$) and relatively closed in $\Omega$ (intersection of closed sets $\{|f|\leq\sup_K|f|\}$). Compactness in $\Omega$ fails only if $\widehat K_\Omega$ accumulates at $\partial\Omega$, i.e. $\operatorname{dist}(\widehat K_\Omega, \partial\Omega) = 0$.

Suppose $\Omega$ is a domain of holomorphy. The Cartan-Thullen theorem characterises this by: $\operatorname{dist}(\widehat K_\Omega, \partial\Omega) = \operatorname{dist}(K, \partial\Omega)$ for every compact $K$ (the hull does not get closer to the boundary than $K$ already is). Granting that characterisation — or arguing directly — fix a sequence $z_m\in\widehat K_\Omega$ with $z_m\to z_*\in\partial\Omega$. Because $\Omega$ is a domain of holomorphy, there is $f\in\mathcal O(\Omega)$ that does not extend past $z_*$, and one can arrange $|f|$ to be unbounded along a sequence approaching $z_*$ (functions singular exactly at the boundary exist on a domain of holomorphy). Then $\sup_K|f| < \infty$ but $|f(z_m)|\to\infty$, contradicting $z_m\in\widehat K_\Omega$ (which forces $|f(z_m)|\leq\sup_K|f|$).

Hence $\widehat K_\Omega$ stays a positive distance from $\partial\Omega$; being bounded, closed in $\Omega$, and bounded away from $\partial\Omega$, it is compact. So $\Omega$ is holomorphically convex. The converse (holomorphically convex ⟹ domain of holomorphy) together with this is the Cartan-Thullen theorem. Krantz Ch. 2.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).** *Bergman kernel transformation under biholomorphism.*

Let $\Phi:\Omega_1\to\Omega_2$ be a biholomorphism with complex Jacobian $J_\Phi = \det\Phi'$. Show the Bergman kernels transform as $K_{\Omega_1}(z,w) = J_\Phi(z)\,K_{\Omega_2}(\Phi z,\Phi w)\,\overline{J_\Phi(w)}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The map $f\mapsto (f\circ\Phi)\cdot J_\Phi$ is a unitary isomorphism $A^2(\Omega_2)\to A^2(\Omega_1)$ (change of variables: $|J_\Phi|^2$ is the real Jacobian). A unitary intertwines the reproducing kernels.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define $U: A^2(\Omega_2)\to A^2(\Omega_1)$ by $(Ug)(z) = g(\Phi(z))\,J_\Phi(z)$. This is unitary: for $g\in A^2(\Omega_2)$, by the holomorphic change of variables $w = \Phi(z)$ (whose real Jacobian determinant is $|J_\Phi(z)|^2$),

$$
\|Ug\|_{A^2(\Omega_1)}^2 = \int_{\Omega_1}|g(\Phi z)|^2|J_\Phi(z)|^2\,dV(z) = \int_{\Omega_2}|g(w)|^2\,dV(w) = \|g\|_{A^2(\Omega_2)}^2,
$$

and $U$ is onto with inverse built from $\Phi^{-1}$. So $U$ is a unitary isomorphism of Bergman spaces.

Take an orthonormal basis $\{e_k\}$ of $A^2(\Omega_2)$; then $\{Ue_k\}$ is an orthonormal basis of $A^2(\Omega_1)$. The reproducing kernel is the sum $K(z,w) = \sum_k e_k(z)\overline{e_k(w)}$ over any orthonormal basis. Hence

$$
K_{\Omega_1}(z,w) = \sum_k (Ue_k)(z)\,\overline{(Ue_k)(w)} = \sum_k e_k(\Phi z)J_\Phi(z)\,\overline{e_k(\Phi w)\,J_\Phi(w)}.
$$

Pulling out the Jacobian factors (independent of $k$),

$$
K_{\Omega_1}(z,w) = J_\Phi(z)\,\overline{J_\Phi(w)}\sum_k e_k(\Phi z)\overline{e_k(\Phi w)} = J_\Phi(z)\,K_{\Omega_2}(\Phi z,\Phi w)\,\overline{J_\Phi(w)}.
$$

This transformation rule is what makes the Bergman kernel a biholomorphic invariant in the appropriate sense, and it is the reason the Bergman metric (built from $\partial\bar\partial\log K(z,z)$) is biholomorphically invariant. Krantz, Bergman-kernel chapter.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).** *From $\bar\partial$-solvability to the Cousin I problem.*

Assume that on a pseudoconvex $\Omega\subset\mathbb{C}^n$ every $\bar\partial$-closed $(0,1)$-form is $\bar\partial$-exact (Hörmander's theorem). Solve the additive Cousin I problem: given an open cover $\{U_i\}$ and holomorphic $f_{ij}\in\mathcal O(U_i\cap U_j)$ with $f_{ij} + f_{jk} = f_{ik}$, find holomorphic $g_i\in\mathcal O(U_i)$ with $g_i - g_j = f_{ij}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use a smooth partition of unity $\{\varphi_k\}$ to build smooth solutions $h_i = \sum_k\varphi_k f_{ik}$ with $h_i - h_j = f_{ij}$, then correct by solving $\bar\partial u = \bar\partial h_i$ (a globally defined $(0,1)$-form) to make the pieces holomorphic.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Smooth solution.* Take a smooth partition of unity $\{\varphi_k\}$ subordinate to $\{U_k\}$. Define $h_i = \sum_k\varphi_k\,f_{ik}\in C^\infty(U_i)$ (each summand is extended by zero off the support of $\varphi_k$, legitimate since $f_{ik}$ is defined on $U_i\cap U_k\supset\operatorname{supp}\varphi_k\cap U_i$). On $U_i\cap U_j$, using the cocycle relation $f_{ik} - f_{jk} = f_{ik} + f_{kj} = f_{ij}$,

$$
h_i - h_j = \sum_k\varphi_k(f_{ik} - f_{jk}) = \sum_k\varphi_k\,f_{ij} = f_{ij},
$$

since $\sum_k\varphi_k = 1$. So $\{h_i\}$ solves Cousin I smoothly, but the $h_i$ are not holomorphic.

*Holomorphic correction.* Since $f_{ij}$ is holomorphic, $\bar\partial h_i = \bar\partial h_j$ on overlaps, so the $(0,1)$-forms $\bar\partial h_i$ glue to a single global form $\omega\in\Omega^{0,1}(\Omega)$. It is $\bar\partial$-closed: $\bar\partial\omega = \bar\partial\bar\partial h_i = 0$. By the assumed Hörmander solvability on the pseudoconvex $\Omega$, there is $u\in C^\infty(\Omega)$ with $\bar\partial u = \omega$, i.e. $\bar\partial u = \bar\partial h_i$ on each $U_i$.

Set $g_i = h_i - u$. Then $\bar\partial g_i = \bar\partial h_i - \bar\partial u = 0$, so $g_i\in\mathcal O(U_i)$, and on overlaps

$$
g_i - g_j = (h_i - u) - (h_j - u) = h_i - h_j = f_{ij}.
$$

Thus $\{g_i\}$ is a holomorphic Cousin I solution. The same $\bar\partial$-engine, applied to a multiplicative cocycle via $\log$, handles Cousin II (subject to a topological obstruction). This is the structural payoff of the $\bar\partial$-$L^2$ theory: pseudoconvex ⟹ $\bar\partial$-solvable ⟹ Cousin problems solvable. Krantz Ch. 4.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).** *Hörmander's weighted estimate forces solvability — the model computation.*

On a pseudoconvex domain with strictly plurisubharmonic weight $\varphi$, the key inequality is $\|f\|_\varphi^2\leq C\big(\|\bar\partial^*_\varphi f\|^2 + \|\bar\partial f\|^2\big)$ for $f$ in the domain of the adjoint. Explain, via the basic Hilbert-space duality argument, how this a priori estimate yields a solution $u$ of $\bar\partial u = g$ with the $L^2$ bound $\|u\|_\varphi^2\leq C\|g\|_\varphi^2$ for $\bar\partial$-closed $g$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Define the linear functional $L(\bar\partial^*_\varphi f) = \langle f, g\rangle_\varphi$ on the range of $\bar\partial^*_\varphi$. The estimate bounds it: $|L(\bar\partial^*_\varphi f)|\leq\|f\|_\varphi\|g\|_\varphi\leq C^{1/2}\|g\|_\varphi\|\bar\partial^*_\varphi f\|$. Apply Riesz representation / Hahn-Banach.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Work in the weighted Hilbert spaces $L^2_{(0,q)}(\Omega,\varphi)$. Let $g$ be a $\bar\partial$-closed $(0,1)$-form with $\|g\|_\varphi < \infty$. We seek $u\in L^2(\Omega,\varphi)$ with $\bar\partial u = g$, characterised weakly by $\langle u, \bar\partial^*_\varphi f\rangle_\varphi = \langle g, f\rangle_\varphi$ for all $f$ in the domain of $\bar\partial^*_\varphi$.

*Defining the functional.* On $g\in\ker\bar\partial$, one splits any $f$ into a part in $\ker\bar\partial$ and an orthogonal part; the estimate is needed precisely on $\ker\bar\partial$. For $f\in\operatorname{Dom}(\bar\partial^*_\varphi)\cap\ker\bar\partial$, the a priori inequality reduces (since $\bar\partial f = 0$) to $\|f\|_\varphi^2\leq C\|\bar\partial^*_\varphi f\|^2$. Define

$$
L(\bar\partial^*_\varphi f) := \langle f, g\rangle_\varphi.
$$

This is well-defined on the range $\{\bar\partial^*_\varphi f\}$: if $\bar\partial^*_\varphi f = 0$ then $\|f\|_\varphi^2\leq C\cdot 0 = 0$, so $f = 0$ and $\langle f, g\rangle = 0$.

*Boundedness.* By Cauchy-Schwarz and the estimate,

$$
|L(\bar\partial^*_\varphi f)| = |\langle f, g\rangle_\varphi|\leq\|f\|_\varphi\,\|g\|_\varphi\leq C^{1/2}\|\bar\partial^*_\varphi f\|\,\|g\|_\varphi.
$$

So $L$ is a bounded linear functional on the subspace $\{\bar\partial^*_\varphi f\}\subset L^2(\Omega,\varphi)$ with norm $\leq C^{1/2}\|g\|_\varphi$.

*Representation.* By Hahn-Banach extend $L$ to all of $L^2(\Omega,\varphi)$ preserving the norm, then by Riesz representation there is $u\in L^2(\Omega,\varphi)$ with $L(\psi) = \langle\psi, u\rangle_\varphi$ and $\|u\|_\varphi\leq C^{1/2}\|g\|_\varphi$. Taking $\psi = \bar\partial^*_\varphi f$,

$$
\langle\bar\partial^*_\varphi f, u\rangle_\varphi = L(\bar\partial^*_\varphi f) = \langle f, g\rangle_\varphi\quad\forall f,
$$

which says exactly $\bar\partial u = g$ in the weak sense. Squaring the norm bound gives $\|u\|_\varphi^2\leq C\|g\|_\varphi^2$.

This duality-plus-a-priori-estimate is the entire architecture of Hörmander's $L^2$ method: the geometry (pseudoconvexity, strict plurisubharmonicity of $\varphi$) is spent establishing the inequality, and the functional analysis converts it into existence with bounds. Krantz Ch. 4; Hörmander Ch. 4.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *The Levi problem direction: pseudoconvex ⟹ domain of holomorphy (outline via $\bar\partial$).*

Outline how the $\bar\partial$-$L^2$ existence theorem proves that a (smoothly bounded) pseudoconvex domain $\Omega$ is a domain of holomorphy, by constructing, for each boundary point, a holomorphic function singular exactly there.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

At a boundary point $p$ use the Levi polynomial to build a local holomorphic function $h$ singular at $p$ and nonvanishing on a neighbourhood intersected with $\overline\Omega\setminus\{p\}$. Globalise $1/h$ by cutting off and correcting the resulting $\bar\partial$ error using Hörmander.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix $p\in\partial\Omega$ with defining function $\rho$. The **Levi polynomial** of $\rho$ at $p$ is

$$
L_p(z) = \sum_j\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial z_j}(p)(z_j - p_j) + \frac12\sum_{j,k}\frac{\partial^2\rho}{\partial z_j\partial z_k}(p)(z_j - p_j)(z_k - p_k),
$$

a holomorphic polynomial. A Taylor expansion of $\rho$ shows $2\operatorname{Re}L_p(z) = -\rho(z) + (\text{Levi form})(z - p) + o(|z - p|^2)$. Where the domain is pseudoconvex (Levi form $\geq 0$ on the complex tangent space), this gives $\operatorname{Re}L_p(z)\geq 0$ on $\overline\Omega$ near $p$ with equality only at $p$ — so $L_p$ is nonvanishing on $(\overline\Omega\cap U)\setminus\{p\}$ for a small neighbourhood $U$, and $1/L_p$ is holomorphic there and blows up at $p$.

*Globalisation.* Let $\chi$ be a cutoff supported in $U$, $\equiv 1$ near $p$. The function $\chi/L_p$ is smooth on $\Omega$ and holomorphic near $p$, but $\bar\partial(\chi/L_p) = (\bar\partial\chi)/L_p =: \alpha$ is a smooth $\bar\partial$-closed $(0,1)$-form supported away from $p$ (where $\bar\partial\chi$ lives), so $\alpha$ is bounded — in particular in $L^2(\Omega,\varphi)$ for a suitable plurisubharmonic weight. By Hörmander's theorem on the pseudoconvex $\Omega$, solve $\bar\partial u = \alpha$ with $u\in L^2$, and the weight can be chosen so that $u$ is forced to vanish to high order at $p$ (or at least to be bounded near $p$).

Set $F = \chi/L_p - u$. Then $\bar\partial F = \alpha - \alpha = 0$, so $F\in\mathcal O(\Omega)$, and near $p$, $F = 1/L_p - u$ is singular (blows up) at $p$ because $1/L_p\to\infty$ while $u$ stays bounded. So $F$ is a holomorphic function on $\Omega$ that cannot be continued across $p$.

Since such an $F$ exists for every boundary point $p$, no boundary point admits holomorphic extension, i.e. $\Omega$ is a domain of holomorphy. This is the solution of the Levi problem via the $\bar\partial$-method (Hörmander). Krantz Ch. 3-4.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 10 (medium, numeric).** *Bergman kernel of the polydisc by the product rule.*

Compute the Bergman kernel of the bidisc $\Delta^2 = \Delta\times\Delta\subset\mathbb{C}^2$, using the product structure $A^2(\Omega_1\times\Omega_2)$ and the disc kernel from Exercise 2.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For a product domain, an orthonormal basis is the set of products $e_m(z_1)e_n(z_2)$, so $K_{\Omega_1\times\Omega_2}((z_1,z_2),(w_1,w_2)) = K_{\Omega_1}(z_1,w_1)\,K_{\Omega_2}(z_2,w_2)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a product domain $\Omega_1\times\Omega_2$, if $\{e_m\}$ is an orthonormal basis of $A^2(\Omega_1)$ and $\{f_n\}$ of $A^2(\Omega_2)$, then the products $\{e_m\otimes f_n : (z_1,z_2)\mapsto e_m(z_1)f_n(z_2)\}$ form an orthonormal basis of $A^2(\Omega_1\times\Omega_2)$ (Fubini factorises the inner product, and the products span by holomorphic separation of variables). Therefore the Bergman kernel factorises:

$$
K_{\Omega_1\times\Omega_2}\big((z_1,z_2),(w_1,w_2)\big) = \sum_{m,n} e_m(z_1)f_n(z_2)\overline{e_m(w_1)f_n(w_2)} = K_{\Omega_1}(z_1,w_1)\,K_{\Omega_2}(z_2,w_2).
$$

Using the disc kernel $K_\Delta(z,w) = \dfrac{1}{\pi(1 - z\bar w)^2}$ from Exercise 2,

$$
K_{\Delta^2}\big((z_1,z_2),(w_1,w_2)\big) = \frac{1}{\pi(1 - z_1\bar w_1)^2}\cdot\frac{1}{\pi(1 - z_2\bar w_2)^2} = \frac{1}{\pi^2(1 - z_1\bar w_1)^2(1 - z_2\bar w_2)^2}.
$$

The polydisc kernel is the product of disc kernels. Contrast this with the ball $B^2$, whose Bergman kernel $K_{B^2}(z,w) = \frac{2}{\pi^2(1 - z\cdot\bar w)^3}$ does **not** factorise — reflecting that the ball is not biholomorphic to the bidisc (Poincaré's theorem). Krantz, Bergman-kernel chapter.

</details>
