## Intuition [Beginner]

A region in complex space is called a *domain of holomorphy* when it is the natural home of some holomorphic function: a function that lives on the region and refuses to extend past any part of its wall. The previous chapter showed that such regions are special. In one variable every open region qualifies, but in several variables most regions leak — a holomorphic function on them automatically spreads to a larger region, so they are not the true home of anything.

Eugenio Levi noticed, around 1910, that the wall of a leak-free region has to curve the right way. His condition is local: stand at any boundary point and check how the wall bends in the complex directions tangent to it. A region passing this test everywhere is called *pseudoconvex*. Levi proved that every domain of holomorphy passes his test. The reverse question stayed open for forty years: if a region passes the local curvature test at every wall point, must it be a genuine domain of holomorphy?

The answer is yes. That positive answer is the Levi problem, and its resolution is the structural centrepiece of several-variable theory.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a region whose boundary wall, viewed near one point, bulges inward like the inside of a bowl when you look only along the complex directions. Pseudoconvexity is exactly this one-sided bulge, checked at every point of the wall and in every complex tangent direction at once. A flat wall, or one that dimples outward, fails the test and lets functions leak through.

The Levi problem asks you to manufacture, from this purely local curvature data, a single global holomorphic function that blows up at a chosen wall point and so cannot be continued past it. The picture below shows the strategy. Near the target point you write down an easy local function that already blows up there. It is not yet holomorphic on the whole region, so it has a small defect. A correction term, supplied by solving a standard auxiliary equation, cancels the defect everywhere while leaving the blow-up intact.

The barrier that makes the correction possible is the height $-\log d$, where $d$ is the distance to the wall. On a pseudoconvex region this height curves upward along every complex line, and that upward curve is precisely the budget that pays for the correction.

![A pseudoconvex region with an inward-bulging boundary wall; near a chosen wall point a local function blows up, and a small correction term turns it into a global holomorphic function that cannot cross the wall.](/img/solution-of-the-levi-problem-placeholder.svg)

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the simplest several-variable region: the ball $B$ of radius $1$ in two complex variables, the set of points $(z_1, z_2)$ with $|z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 < 1$. Its wall is the sphere where $|z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 = 1$. Is this region a domain of holomorphy?

First, the local test. The wall is the level set of $\rho(z) = |z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 - 1$, and this $\rho$ curves upward in every complex direction — it is the squared distance to the origin, shifted down by one. So the ball passes Levi's curvature test at every wall point. It is pseudoconvex.

Now build a function that blows up at a chosen wall point, say $p = (1, 0)$. Try $f(z) = 1/(1 - z_1)$. As the point slides toward $p$, the denominator $1 - z_1$ goes to $0$, so $f$ shoots to infinity exactly at $p$. And $f$ is already holomorphic on the whole ball, because $1 - z_1$ never vanishes there: if $z_1 = 1$ then $|z_1|^2 = 1$ and the point is on the wall, not inside. No correction term is even needed here.

What this tells us: the ball is a domain of holomorphy, certified by an honest function blowing up at its boundary, and the only ingredient was the upward curvature of the wall. For more complicated pseudoconvex regions the blow-up function is harder to write by hand, and the correction term in the picture does the work that $1 - z_1$ did for free here.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}^n$ is open and connected. Two predicates from the foundations of this chapter are in play.

From [06.10.01], $\Omega$ is a *domain of holomorphy* if there is no pair of nonempty open sets $\Omega_1 \subseteq \Omega_2$ with $\Omega_1 \subseteq \Omega \cap \Omega_2$, $\Omega_2 \not\subseteq \Omega$, and $\Omega_2$ connected, such that every $f \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ has a holomorphic continuation to $\Omega_2$ agreeing with $f$ on $\Omega_1$. Equivalently (Cartan–Thullen), $\Omega$ is holomorphically convex: for every compact $K \Subset \Omega$ the hull
$$
\hat K_\Omega = \{ z \in \Omega : |f(z)| \le \sup_K |f| \ \text{for all } f \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega)\}
$$
is again relatively compact in $\Omega$.

From [06.10.02], a function $\varphi : \Omega \to [-\infty, \infty)$ is *plurisubharmonic* (PSH) if it is upper semicontinuous and subharmonic on every complex line. A *continuous plurisubharmonic exhaustion* is a continuous PSH $\varphi : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ whose sublevel sets $\Omega_c = \{z : \varphi(z) < c\}$ are relatively compact in $\Omega$ for all $c$.

**Definition (pseudoconvex domain).** $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}^n$ is *pseudoconvex* if it admits a continuous plurisubharmonic exhaustion function. Equivalently $-\log d(z, \partial\Omega)$ is plurisubharmonic on $\Omega$ (Hartogs pseudoconvexity), and — for $\Omega$ with $C^2$ boundary defined by $\rho$ — the *Levi form*
$$
L_\rho(p; w) = \sum_{j,k=1}^n \frac{\partial^2 \rho}{\partial z_j \, \partial \bar z_k}(p)\, w_j \bar w_k \ \ge 0
\qquad \text{for all } w \text{ with } \sum_j \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial z_j}(p)\, w_j = 0,
$$
the inequality holding on the complex tangent space at every boundary point $p$. The equivalence of these three formulations is developed in [06.10.03].

**The Levi problem.** The implication *domain of holomorphy $\Rightarrow$ pseudoconvex* is the elementary direction: if $\Omega$ is a domain of holomorphy then $-\log d(\cdot, \partial\Omega)$ is PSH, proved by a Hartogs-figure / Kontinuitätssatz argument. The *Levi problem* is the converse,
$$
\Omega \ \text{pseudoconvex} \implies \Omega \ \text{is a domain of holomorphy},
$$
equivalently *pseudoconvex $\Rightarrow$ holomorphically convex*. Its resolution closes the equivalence chain
$$
\text{domain of holomorphy} \iff \text{holomorphically convex} \iff \text{pseudoconvex}.
$$

The strategy is *separation by a single function*: to a boundary point $p_0$ one attaches an $f \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ unbounded along a sequence approaching $p_0$. The existence of such $f$ for every $p_0$ forces $\hat K_\Omega \Subset \Omega$, hence holomorphic convexity. The obstruction to writing $f$ down directly is the overdetermined nature of the Cauchy–Riemann system; the tool that removes it is the solvability of $\bar\partial u = g$ with control, the several-variable Hörmander theory built in [06.10.04] over the dimension-one $\bar\partial$ of [06.04.05].

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (solution of the Levi problem).** *Let $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}^n$ be pseudoconvex. Then $\Omega$ is a domain of holomorphy; equivalently $\Omega$ is holomorphically convex.* [ref: TODO_REF Krantz Ch. 4]

The proof takes for granted one analytic input, stated here as a hypothesis and proved in [06.10.04].

**$L^2$ existence hypothesis (Hörmander).** *Let $\Omega$ be pseudoconvex and $\varphi \in C^2(\Omega)$ strictly plurisubharmonic with Levi form bounded below by $c(z) > 0$. For every $(0,1)$-form $g$ with $\bar\partial g = 0$ and $\int_\Omega |g|^2 e^{-\varphi}\, dV < \infty$ there is $u \in L^2_{\mathrm{loc}}(\Omega)$ with $\bar\partial u = g$ and*
$$
\int_\Omega |u|^2 e^{-\varphi}\, dV \ \le \ \int_\Omega \frac{|g|^2}{c}\, e^{-\varphi}\, dV .
$$
[ref: TODO_REF Hörmander 1965]

*Proof of Theorem.* It suffices to prove holomorphic convexity, since by Cartan–Thullen [06.10.01] that is equivalent to being a domain of holomorphy. Fix a boundary point $p_0 \in \partial\Omega$ (or the point at infinity if $\Omega$ is unbounded) and a sequence $z_\nu \in \Omega$ with $z_\nu \to p_0$. It is enough to produce $f \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ with $|f(z_\nu)| \to \infty$: then $p_0 \notin \hat K_\Omega$ for any compact $K$ with $z_\nu \notin K$ eventually, so $\hat K_\Omega$ has no limit point on $\partial\Omega$ and is relatively compact.

**Step 1: a local peak.** Choose a holomorphic *local separating function* near $p_0$. By pseudoconvexity one may take a strictly PSH exhaustion $\psi$ and a defining geometry in which there is a holomorphic polynomial $h$ on a neighbourhood $U$ of $p_0$ with $h(p_0) = 0$ and $\mathrm{Re}\, h < 0$ on $U \cap \Omega \setminus \{p_0\}$ to second order — the *Levi polynomial* of a smooth strictly pseudoconvex defining function, $h(z) = \sum_j \rho_{z_j}(p_0)(z_j - p_{0,j}) + \tfrac12 \sum_{j,k} \rho_{z_j z_k}(p_0)(z_j - p_{0,j})(z_k - p_{0,k})$. Then $s = 1/h$ is holomorphic on $U \cap \Omega$ and $|s| \to \infty$ as $z \to p_0$. (For a general pseudoconvex $\Omega$ one first exhausts by smooth strictly pseudoconvex subdomains, by the smooth strictly PSH approximation of [06.10.02], and runs the construction on each.)

**Step 2: cut off.** Let $\chi \in C^\infty_c(U)$ equal $1$ near $p_0$. The function $\chi s$ is smooth on $\Omega$ and agrees with $s$ near $p_0$, but it is not holomorphic: $g := \bar\partial(\chi s) = (\bar\partial\chi)\, s$. Because $\bar\partial\chi$ is supported in the annular region where $\chi$ transitions — bounded away from $p_0$ — and $s$ is bounded there, $g$ is a smooth $\bar\partial$-closed $(0,1)$-form, supported off $p_0$, with $g \in L^2$ against any weight.

**Step 3: correct.** Choose the weight $\varphi$ so heavily plurisubharmonic near $p_0$ that
$$
\int_\Omega |g|^2 e^{-\varphi}\, dV < \infty \quad\text{while}\quad e^{-\varphi} \ \text{is non-integrable near } p_0 .
$$
Concretely add to a strictly PSH exhaustion a term $N \log|h|^{-2}$ or $2n\log|z - p_0|^{-1} \cdot$ (a PSH bump), forcing a pole-strength singularity of the weight at $p_0$; since $g$ vanishes near $p_0$ this leaves $\int|g|^2 e^{-\varphi} < \infty$. The $L^2$ existence hypothesis yields $u \in L^2_{\mathrm{loc}}$ with $\bar\partial u = g$ and $\int_\Omega |u|^2 e^{-\varphi}\, dV < \infty$. The non-integrability of $e^{-\varphi}$ at $p_0$ forces $u(p_0) = 0$ in the strong sense that $u \to 0$ along $z_\nu$, because a function with $\int |u|^2 e^{-\varphi} < \infty$ cannot be bounded away from $0$ where $e^{-\varphi}$ blows up.

**Step 4: assemble.** Set $f = \chi s - u$. Then
$$
\bar\partial f = \bar\partial(\chi s) - \bar\partial u = g - g = 0,
$$
so $f$ is holomorphic on $\Omega$ (a distributional solution of $\bar\partial f = 0$ is holomorphic, by Weyl/elliptic regularity, [06.04.05]). Near $p_0$, $f = s - u$, and along $z_\nu \to p_0$ one has $|s(z_\nu)| \to \infty$ while $u(z_\nu) \to 0$; hence $|f(z_\nu)| \to \infty$. This is the separating function. Sweeping $p_0$ over $\partial\Omega$ gives $\hat K_\Omega \Subset \Omega$ for every compact $K$, so $\Omega$ is holomorphically convex and therefore a domain of holomorphy. $\square$

**Bridge.** This proof builds toward every later theorem of the chapter that runs on the $\bar\partial$ machine, and the cut-off-and-correct pattern appears again in the Cousin problems and the integral-kernel solution operators. The foundational reason a purely local curvature condition produces a global function is the positivity in the $L^2$ estimate: this is exactly the Levi form of the weight $\varphi$, the same Hermitian form whose nonnegativity defined plurisubharmonicity in [06.10.02], now carrying enough strict positivity to dominate the defect $g$. Putting these together, the Levi problem identifies pseudoconvexity with holomorphic convexity — the geometric input with the function-theoretic output — and the bridge is the weighted solvability of $\bar\partial u = g$, which converts a local peak $1/h$ into a global holomorphic separator. The central insight is that the overdetermined Cauchy–Riemann system, the obstruction that made several-variable theory hard, becomes the instrument of its solution once one controls $\bar\partial$ in $L^2$; this generalises the dimension-one fact that any $\bar\partial$-equation on a planar domain is solvable, which is why every planar region is a domain of holomorphy.

**Exercise 1 (easy, short-answer).**

State precisely which direction of the equivalence "domain of holomorphy $\iff$ pseudoconvex" is the *easy* half and name the tool that proves it.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

One direction is a Kontinuitätssatz / Hartogs-figure argument on $-\log d(\cdot,\partial\Omega)$; the other is the deep one.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The easy half is *domain of holomorphy $\Rightarrow$ pseudoconvex*: if $\Omega$ is a domain of holomorphy then $-\log d(z,\partial\Omega)$ is plurisubharmonic, proved by the continuity theorem (Kontinuitätssatz) — a family of analytic discs whose boundaries stay in $\Omega$ cannot have interiors escaping, which is the plurisubharmonicity of the boundary-distance height. The deep half, the converse, is the Levi problem proper. Rubric: full credit for naming the correct direction and the Kontinuitätssatz / boundary-distance PSH tool.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

For the unit ball $\mathbb{B}^n = \{|z| < 1\}$ with defining function $\rho(z) = |z|^2 - 1$, compute the Levi form $L_\rho(p; w)$ and confirm that $\mathbb{B}^n$ is pseudoconvex.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The complex Hessian of $|z|^2 = \sum_j z_j\bar z_j$ is the identity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The mixed second derivatives are $\partial^2\rho/\partial z_j\partial\bar z_k = \delta_{jk}$, so $L_\rho(p; w) = \sum_j |w_j|^2 = |w|^2 \ge 0$ for all $w$, with strict positivity for $w \ne 0$. The Levi form is positive definite on the whole space, a fortiori on the complex tangent space, so $\mathbb{B}^n$ is strictly pseudoconvex, hence pseudoconvex. Rubric: full credit for $L_\rho = |w|^2$ and the conclusion of (strict) pseudoconvexity.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, short-answer).**

In Step 3 of the Key-theorem proof the weight $\varphi$ is engineered to be non-integrable at $p_0$. Explain precisely why this forces the correction $u$ to vanish at $p_0$, and where the hypothesis that $g$ is supported away from $p_0$ is used.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A finite value $\int |u|^2 e^{-\varphi}\,dV$ near a point where $e^{-\varphi}$ diverges constrains $u$; and $\int |g|^2 e^{-\varphi}$ must stay finite for the existence theorem to apply.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The existence theorem returns $u$ with $\int_\Omega |u|^2 e^{-\varphi}\,dV < \infty$. If $e^{-\varphi}$ is non-integrable on every neighbourhood of $p_0$, then any measurable $u$ with finite weighted norm must satisfy $\liminf_{z\to p_0} |u(z)| = 0$ — were $|u| \ge \varepsilon > 0$ on a neighbourhood, the integral would inherit the divergence of $\int e^{-\varphi}$. After the elliptic regularity that makes $f$ (hence $u = \chi s - f$) holomorphic near $p_0$, this $\liminf$ upgrades to a genuine limit $u(z_\nu)\to 0$. The support hypothesis on $g$ is exactly what keeps $\int |g|^2 e^{-\varphi}<\infty$ despite the singular weight: $g$ vanishes near $p_0$, so the divergence of $e^{-\varphi}$ at $p_0$ never multiplies a nonzero $g$. Rubric: full credit for the non-integrability argument forcing $u\to 0$ and for locating the support hypothesis in the finiteness of $\int|g|^2 e^{-\varphi}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that an increasing union $\Omega = \bigcup_\nu \Omega_\nu$ of domains of holomorphy with $\Omega_\nu \Subset \Omega_{\nu+1}$ is pseudoconvex, and explain why this lets the Levi-problem proof reduce a general pseudoconvex $\Omega$ to bounded strictly pseudoconvex pieces.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$-\log d(\cdot,\partial\Omega) = \sup_\nu(-\log d(\cdot,\partial\Omega_\nu))$ is an increasing limit; and each domain of holomorphy is pseudoconvex.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Each $\Omega_\nu$ is pseudoconvex (domain of holomorphy $\Rightarrow$ pseudoconvex), so $-\log d(\cdot,\partial\Omega_\nu)$ is PSH. On compacts, $-\log d(\cdot,\partial\Omega)$ is the increasing limit of $-\log d(\cdot,\partial\Omega_\nu)$, and an increasing limit of PSH functions that stays upper semicontinuous is PSH; hence $\Omega$ is pseudoconvex. Conversely, every pseudoconvex $\Omega$ is the increasing union of its strictly-pseudoconvex sublevel sets $\{\varphi < c\}$ of a smooth strictly PSH exhaustion (regularise the exhaustion by [06.10.02] and add $\varepsilon|z|^2$). The Behnke–Stein theorem then propagates the domain-of-holomorphy property from the pieces to the union, so it suffices to solve the Levi problem on bounded strictly pseudoconvex domains. Rubric: full credit for the increasing-limit PSH argument and the reduction to bounded strictly pseudoconvex exhausting pieces.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Give the analytic-polyhedron / Oka–Weil consequence: deduce from holomorphic convexity that on a domain of holomorphy $\Omega$, every $f$ holomorphic on a neighbourhood of a compact $K$ with $K = \hat K_\Omega$ is a uniform limit on $K$ of functions in $\mathcal{O}(\Omega)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

When $K$ equals its hull, $K$ is cut out by finitely many $\{|f_i| \le 1\}$ up to $\varepsilon$; expand in the corresponding polydisc coordinates.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $K = \hat K_\Omega$, for any neighbourhood $V \supseteq K$ there are $f_1,\dots,f_m \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ with $K \subseteq P = \{z\in\Omega : |f_i(z)| < 1\} \Subset V$, an analytic polyhedron. The map $z \mapsto (z, f_1(z),\dots,f_m(z))$ embeds a neighbourhood of $\overline P$ into a polydisc in $\mathbb{C}^{n+m}$ as a closed submanifold; a function holomorphic near $K$ pulls back from a polydisc neighbourhood, where it has a convergent power series, and the partial sums — polynomials in $z$ and the $f_i$, hence elements of $\mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ — converge uniformly on $K$. This is the Oka–Weil theorem; it is the approximation companion of the Levi-problem solution. Rubric: full credit for the analytic-polyhedron embedding and the polynomial-in-$(z,f_i)$ approximation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove the *globalisation* step: assuming $\bar\partial u = g$ is solvable in $L^2(\Omega, e^{-\varphi})$ for every smooth strictly PSH weight $\varphi$ and every $\bar\partial$-closed $g \in L^2$, show that for *each* boundary point and *each* approach sequence there is a single $F \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ unbounded on a dense set of $\partial\Omega$ simultaneously.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take a countable dense set of boundary points, build a separating $f_k$ for each with $\sup_{K_k}|f_k|$ small, and sum a rapidly weighted series.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\{p_k\}$ be a countable dense subset of $\partial\Omega$ and let $K_1 \Subset K_2 \Subset \cdots$ exhaust $\Omega$ by compacts. For each $k$ the Key theorem gives $f_k \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ with $|f_k| \to \infty$ along a sequence approaching $p_k$; rescaling and subtracting a constant, arrange $\sup_{K_k}|f_k| \le 2^{-k}$ and pick points $w_k \to p_k$ with $|f_k(w_k)| \ge k + \sum_{j<k}|f_j(w_k)|$. Set $F = \sum_k f_k$. The bound $\sup_{K_k}|f_k|\le 2^{-k}$ makes the tail $\sum_{j\ge k} f_j$ converge uniformly on $K_{k-1}$, so $F \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega)$. At $w_k$, $|F(w_k)| \ge |f_k(w_k)| - \sum_{j<k}|f_j(w_k)| - \sum_{j>k}|f_j(w_k)| \ge k - 1$, so $F$ is unbounded near every $p_k$, hence near a dense subset of $\partial\Omega$. Such an $F$ has $\Omega$ as its exact domain of holomorphy. Rubric: full credit for the weighted dense-summation construction and the lower bound forcing unboundedness at each $p_k$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

The *Hartogs triangle* $T = \{(z_1, z_2) : |z_1| < |z_2| < 1\}$ is a domain of holomorphy, yet it has a boundary point where no smooth defining function exists. Reconcile this with the Levi-form definition of pseudoconvexity, and state which characterisation of pseudoconvexity certifies $T$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Levi-form test needs $C^2$ boundary; the PSH-exhaustion test does not.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Levi-form condition presupposes a $C^2$ defining function, which fails at the boundary point $(0,0)$ of $T$ where the boundary is not a smooth hypersurface. The correct, fully general certificate is the PSH-exhaustion (Hartogs) characterisation, which makes no smoothness assumption: $T$ admits the continuous PSH exhaustion $\varphi(z) = \max\big(-\log(|z_2| - |z_1|),\, -\log(1 - |z_2|),\, |z|^2\big)$, whose sublevel sets are relatively compact in $T$ and which is PSH as a maximum of PSH functions. So $T$ is pseudoconvex by the exhaustion criterion, and by the Levi-problem theorem it is a domain of holomorphy — consistent with the classical fact that holomorphic functions on $T$ do not all extend across $(0,0)$. The example shows why the analytic (exhaustion) formulation, not the geometric (Levi-form) one, is the load-bearing definition in the proof. Rubric: full credit for locating the failure in the $C^2$ hypothesis and naming the PSH-exhaustion certificate.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

The Levi-problem theorem is the analytic core of a wider equivalence and the seed of two structural generalisations: Grauert's solution on complex manifolds and the sheaf-theoretic reformulation through Cartan's Theorems A and B.

**The full equivalence and its proof economy.** On $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}^n$ the four conditions — domain of holomorphy; holomorphic convexity; existence of a continuous PSH exhaustion (pseudoconvexity); and, for $C^2$ boundary, Levi pseudoconvexity — are equivalent. Three of the implications are elementary: holomorphic convexity $\Rightarrow$ domain of holomorphy is Cartan–Thullen [06.10.01]; domain of holomorphy $\Rightarrow$ pseudoconvex is the Kontinuitätssatz; Levi pseudoconvex $\Rightarrow$ PSH-exhaustible uses the Levi form of $-\log d$. The single deep implication is pseudoconvex $\Rightarrow$ holomorphically convex, the Key theorem, and the whole weight of the subject rests on it. The economy of the $L^2$ proof is that it never constructs the separating function explicitly; it constructs only the correction $u$, abstractly, from a positivity estimate, and lets $\chi s - u$ inherit holomorphy from $\bar\partial$-closedness.

**Strict pseudoconvexity and the Levi polynomial.** When $\partial\Omega$ is $C^2$ and the Levi form is strictly positive, the Levi polynomial $h_{p_0}$ of Step 1 is a genuine local holomorphic support function: $\mathrm{Re}\, h_{p_0}(z) \le -c\, |z - p_0|^2$ on $\partial\Omega$ near $p_0$, so $1/h_{p_0}$ peaks at $p_0$ from the start and the correction $u$ is needed only to globalise, not to create, the singularity. This is the regime where the integral-kernel methods of Henkin and Ramirez later supply an *explicit* solution operator for $\bar\partial$ with sup-norm and Hölder estimates, replacing the abstract Hilbert-space $u$ by a kernel integral. The weakly pseudoconvex case (Levi form merely $\ge 0$, e.g. $\{|z_1|^2 + |z_2|^4 < 1\}$) has no holomorphic support function in general, and the $L^2$ method is then not a convenience but a necessity.

**Grauert's theorem and Stein manifolds.** Grauert (1958) extended the solution from domains in $\mathbb{C}^n$ to complex manifolds: a complex manifold carrying a strictly PSH exhaustion is *Stein*, and the Levi problem on manifolds asks whether a holomorphically convex manifold with no compact positive-dimensional analytic subsets is Stein. Grauert's affirmative answer runs the same $\bar\partial$ engine, now with cohomology vanishing $H^q(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $q \ge 1$ and coherent $\mathcal{F}$ on Stein $X$ standing in for the single-function separation. The dimension-one shadow of this is in [06.09.02]: every open Riemann surface is Stein, and Theorems A and B hold there.

**Bremermann's and Norguet's routes.** Oka's 1953 proof, Bremermann's and Norguet's independent 1954 proofs, and the later Hörmander $L^2$ proof differ in the analytic engine but share the architecture: reduce to local peak functions, solve a $\bar\partial$-type problem with a plurisubharmonic budget, globalise. Oka used his own coherence and the "Oka principle" of trading analytic for topological data; Hörmander mechanised the budget into the single weighted inequality used in the Key theorem. The kernel route (Grauert–Lieb, Henkin) and the $\bar\partial$-Neumann route (Kohn) give the regularity refinements that the abstract $L^2$ solution alone does not.

**Synthesis.** The solution of the Levi problem is the foundational reason several-variable complex analysis is a structural theory rather than a catalogue of examples: it identifies a purely local boundary-curvature condition with a global function-theoretic one, and the identification is mediated entirely by the solvability of $\bar\partial$ against a plurisubharmonic weight. Putting these together, the three pictures that [06.10.01], [06.10.02], and [06.10.03] developed in parallel — holomorphic convexity, plurisubharmonic exhaustion, Levi-form positivity — collapse onto one another, and the central insight is that the overdetermined Cauchy–Riemann operator, which made the subject hard, is exactly the operator whose controlled invertibility makes it tractable. This is dual to the Cartan–Thullen picture: where Cartan–Thullen reads holomorphic convexity off the radius of convergence of power series, the Levi solution reads it off the Levi form of a weight, and the two readings agree because both are measuring the same plurisubharmonic budget. The pattern recurs across the rest of the chapter: the Cousin problems, the Oka–Weil approximation theorem, and the integral-kernel constructions are all instances of "solve $\bar\partial$ with a PSH weight, then globalise," and each builds toward the boundary-regularity theory of strongly pseudoconvex domains where the abstract solution is sharpened to an explicit kernel.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (easy direction via the Kontinuitätssatz).** *If $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}^n$ is a domain of holomorphy, then $\varphi = -\log d(\cdot, \partial\Omega)$ is plurisubharmonic, so $\Omega$ is pseudoconvex.*

*Proof.* Fix a complex line $\lambda \mapsto a + \lambda b$ and a closed disc $\overline{D}$ in its parameter on which $a + \lambda b \in \Omega$. Write $g(\lambda) = \varphi(a + \lambda b) = -\log d(a+\lambda b, \partial\Omega)$; subharmonicity of $g$ is what must be shown. Suppose not: then there is a harmonic $H$ on $D$, continuous up to $\partial D$, with $g \le H$ on $\partial D$ but $g(\lambda_0) > H(\lambda_0)$ for some interior $\lambda_0$. Let $h$ be a holomorphic function on $D$ with $\mathrm{Re}\, h = H$. The family of analytic discs $\zeta \mapsto a + \lambda b + \zeta\, e^{-h(\lambda)} v$, for unit vectors $v$ and $|\zeta|$ small, has boundaries (as $\lambda$ runs over $\partial D$) staying inside $\Omega$ because $|e^{-h(\lambda)}| = e^{-H(\lambda)} \le e^{-g(\lambda)} = d(a+\lambda b,\partial\Omega)$ keeps the perturbed point within the boundary distance. By the continuity theorem for domains of holomorphy (a Hartogs figure cannot be a domain of holomorphy), the centres $a + \lambda b$ over the interior also satisfy the distance bound $d \ge e^{-H}$, i.e. $g \le H$ on $D$, contradicting $g(\lambda_0) > H(\lambda_0)$. Hence $g$ is subharmonic, $\varphi$ is PSH, and $\Omega$ is pseudoconvex. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (separation forces holomorphic convexity).** *Let $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}^n$ be open. Suppose that for every $p_0 \in \partial\Omega$ and every sequence $z_\nu \to p_0$ in $\Omega$ there is $f \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ with $\sup_\nu |f(z_\nu)| = \infty$. Then $\hat K_\Omega \Subset \Omega$ for every compact $K \Subset \Omega$, i.e. $\Omega$ is holomorphically convex.*

*Proof.* The hull $\hat K_\Omega$ is closed in $\Omega$ and bounded (it lies in the polynomial hull of $K$, which is compact when $\Omega$ is bounded; the unbounded case is handled by also separating the point at infinity). It remains to show $\hat K_\Omega$ has positive distance to $\partial\Omega$. If not, there is a sequence $w_\nu \in \hat K_\Omega$ with $w_\nu \to p_0 \in \partial\Omega$. By hypothesis pick $f \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ with $|f(w_{\nu})| \to \infty$ along a subsequence. But $w_\nu \in \hat K_\Omega$ means $|f(w_\nu)| \le \sup_K |f| < \infty$ for the fixed $f \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega)$, a contradiction. Therefore $d(\hat K_\Omega, \partial\Omega) > 0$, and being closed and bounded with positive boundary distance, $\hat K_\Omega$ is relatively compact in $\Omega$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (the singular weight kills the correction).** *Let $\Omega$ be pseudoconvex, $p_0 \in \partial\Omega$, and let $\varphi$ be a weight with $\int_{B(p_0,r)\cap\Omega} e^{-\varphi}\, dV = \infty$ for all $r > 0$. If $u \in L^2_{\mathrm{loc}}(\Omega)$ satisfies $\int_\Omega |u|^2 e^{-\varphi}\, dV < \infty$ and $u$ is holomorphic on $B(p_0, r_0) \cap \Omega$, then $u(z) \to 0$ as $z \to p_0$ within $\Omega$.*

*Proof.* Suppose, for contradiction, that there is $\varepsilon > 0$ and a sequence $z_\nu \to p_0$ with $|u(z_\nu)| \ge \varepsilon$. By holomorphy of $u$ near $p_0$ and the mean-value property, $|u|$ cannot oscillate faster than its sup over small balls; concretely there is $\rho_\nu \downarrow 0$ and $c > 0$ with $|u| \ge \varepsilon/2$ on $B(z_\nu, c\,\rho_\nu) \cap \Omega$, balls of comparable size that accumulate at $p_0$. Choosing a subsequence so these balls are disjoint and contained in $B(p_0, r)\cap\Omega$,
$$
\int_\Omega |u|^2 e^{-\varphi}\, dV \ \ge\ \sum_\nu \int_{B(z_\nu, c\rho_\nu)} \frac{\varepsilon^2}{4}\, e^{-\varphi}\, dV \ =\ \frac{\varepsilon^2}{4}\sum_\nu \int_{B(z_\nu, c\rho_\nu)} e^{-\varphi}\, dV .
$$
The balls fill a positive fraction of every neighbourhood of $p_0$ (a Whitney-type covering of the approach region), so the right side inherits the divergence $\int_{B(p_0,r)\cap\Omega} e^{-\varphi} = \infty$ and the left side is forced to be infinite — contradicting $\int |u|^2 e^{-\varphi} < \infty$. Hence no such $\varepsilon$ exists and $u(z) \to 0$ as $z \to p_0$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (holomorphic convexity is biholomorphically invariant; the conclusion transfers).** *If $\Phi : \Omega' \to \Omega$ is a biholomorphism and $\Omega$ is a domain of holomorphy, then so is $\Omega'$.*

*Proof.* Pullback $f \mapsto f\circ\Phi$ is a $\mathbb{C}$-algebra isomorphism $\mathcal{O}(\Omega) \to \mathcal{O}(\Omega')$ commuting with sup-norms over corresponding compacts, since $\Phi$ is a homeomorphism. For compact $K' \Subset \Omega'$ set $K = \Phi(K') \Subset \Omega$. For $z' \in \Omega'$ and every $g = f\circ\Phi \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega')$, $|g(z')| = |f(\Phi(z'))|$ and $\sup_{K'}|g| = \sup_K |f|$, so $z' \in \hat{K'}_{\Omega'}$ iff $\Phi(z') \in \hat K_\Omega$; that is, $\hat{K'}_{\Omega'} = \Phi^{-1}(\hat K_\Omega)$. Since $\Omega$ is a domain of holomorphy, $\hat K_\Omega \Subset \Omega$, and applying the homeomorphism $\Phi^{-1}$ to a relatively compact set gives $\hat{K'}_{\Omega'} \Subset \Omega'$. Thus $\Omega'$ is holomorphically convex, hence a domain of holomorphy by Cartan–Thullen. The Levi-problem theorem is therefore a biholomorphic invariant, as pseudoconvexity is, consistent with the equivalence. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Domains of holomorphy and holomorphic convexity [06.10.01].** The Levi-problem theorem supplies the missing implication of the Cartan–Thullen circle: pseudoconvex $\Rightarrow$ holomorphically convex. Where [06.10.01] characterised domains of holomorphy through power-series radius equality and the convex hull $\hat K_\Omega$, this unit certifies that property from boundary geometry alone, closing the equivalence chain that organises the chapter.

- **Plurisubharmonic functions [06.10.02].** The proof runs entirely on the PSH weight $\varphi$ and its strictly positive Levi form; the singular-weight construction of Step 3 and the kill-the-correction Proposition 3 are pure pluripotential theory. The boundary-distance height $-\log d(\cdot,\partial\Omega)$ introduced there is both the certificate of pseudoconvexity and the raw material of the exhaustion the proof exhausts by.

- **Pseudoconvexity and the Levi form [06.10.03].** This unit is the structural payoff of the pseudoconvexity theory: it shows the local Levi-form condition is not merely necessary but sufficient for domain-of-holomorphy status. The Levi polynomial used in Step 1 and the strictly-pseudoconvex sharpening in Advanced results are read directly off the Levi form developed there.

- **The $\bar\partial$ problem with $L^2$ estimates [06.10.04].** The Key theorem cites the Hörmander weighted existence theorem as its sole analytic input; that unit proves the input via the Bochner–Kodaira–Morrey–Kohn identity. The Levi problem is the first and most important application of the $L^2$ machine, and the smooth strictly-PSH-weight regularisation needed there is supplied by [06.10.02].

- **The one-variable $\bar\partial$-Hilbert PDE [06.04.05].** The elliptic regularity that turns the distributional solution $\bar\partial f = 0$ into a genuine holomorphic $f$, and the dimension-one solvability that makes every planar region a domain of holomorphy, are the $n=1$ ancestors of the several-variable argument. The several-variable theory is exactly the obstruction-laden generalisation of that solvable one-variable equation.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Eugenio Elia Levi, in a 1910 study of essential singularities of functions of two or more complex variables [ref: TODO_REF Krantz Ch. 4], isolated the second-order boundary condition now bearing his name and proved its necessity for a domain of holomorphy. The sufficiency — whether Levi's local condition forces the global property — became known as the Levi problem and resisted resolution for over four decades. Kiyoshi Oka announced and proved the affirmative answer for $\mathbb{C}^2$ in the 1940s and for general $\mathbb{C}^n$ in his ninth memoir [ref: TODO_REF Oka 1953] (*Japanese Journal of Mathematics* 23, 97–155), working through his own coherence theory and the device of attaching to a pseudoconvex domain a sequence of analytic polyhedra. Independently and almost simultaneously, Hans-Joachim Bremermann [ref: TODO_REF Bremermann 1954] (*Mathematische Annalen* 128, 63–91) and François Norguet [ref: TODO_REF Norguet 1954] (*Bulletin de la Société Mathématique de France* 82, 137–159) gave their own proofs in 1954, and the three names are jointly attached to the theorem.

The proof presented here is not the original. It is the streamlined $L^2$ proof made possible by Lars Hörmander's 1965 existence theorem for the $\bar\partial$ operator [ref: TODO_REF Hörmander 1965] (*Acta Mathematica* 113, 89–152), which replaced Oka's coherence-and-polyhedra machinery with a single weighted inequality of Bochner–Kodaira type. Hans Grauert had meanwhile, in 1958, transported the whole question to complex manifolds and solved the Levi problem there, identifying the strictly-PSH-exhaustible manifolds with the Stein manifolds and thereby fusing the analytic Levi problem with Henri Cartan's sheaf-cohomological Theorems A and B. The convergence of these routes — Oka's coherence, Hörmander's $L^2$ estimates, Grauert's bumping and Cartan's sheaves — is why a single boundary-curvature inequality came to control the entire global function theory of pseudoconvex domains.
