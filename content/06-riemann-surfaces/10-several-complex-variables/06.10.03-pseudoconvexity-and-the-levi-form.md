## Intuition [Beginner]

A region in the plane is convex when, for any two of its points, the straight segment between them stays inside. In several complex variables the right notion of "good shape" is weaker and more subtle. You do not test the region against all straight lines. You test it against the special slices that the complex structure cares about: complex lines, the flat two-dimensional sheets that look like a copy of the ordinary plane.

A *pseudoconvex* region is one that curves the right way along exactly these complex slices, and is allowed to dip along the others. It is the largest class of regions for which several-variable function theory behaves well, and it turns out to be precisely the class with no leak from the previous units: the regions where holomorphic functions genuinely stop at the wall.

The barometer is a single number attached to each boundary point. Slide a complex slice up against the boundary so it touches at one point. If the boundary bends away from the slice, that point is good; if it bends into the slice, the region leaks there. That bending number is the *Levi form*.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the boundary wall of a region in complex space and a flat complex sheet pressed against it, touching at a single point and otherwise staying outside. At a pseudoconvex boundary point the wall curls away from the sheet on both sides, the way the inside of a bowl curls away from a tabletop laid across its rim. At a bad point the wall bulges into the sheet, like a hill poking up through the tabletop, and through that bulge functions can leak out.

The picture below shows the boundary of a region with a complex tangent sheet at one point. The Levi form measures the gap between the wall and the sheet just off the contact point: positive gap means the wall retreats and the point is good; a gap of the wrong sign means the wall advances and the region fails the test there.

The barrier that certifies the whole region at once is the height $-\log d(z)$, where $d(z)$ is the distance to the wall. When this barrier curves upward along every complex slice, the region is pseudoconvex everywhere.

![The boundary of a region in several complex variables with a complex tangent sheet at a point; the Levi form measures whether the wall retreats from the sheet (good) or advances into it (leaky), and the boundary-distance barrier rises near the wall.](/img/pseudoconvexity-and-the-levi-form-placeholder.svg)

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the unit ball in two complex variables: all points where $|z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 < 1$. Is it pseudoconvex?

The wall is the sphere where $|z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 = 1$. A natural height function that is negative inside, zero on the wall, and positive outside is $\rho(z) = |z_1|^2 + |z_2|^2 - 1$. The bending of the wall along a complex slice is read off from the second-order behaviour of this height. For the ball, that bending number works out to $|w_1|^2 + |w_2|^2$ for a slice direction $w$, which is positive for every nonzero direction. The wall retreats from every complex sheet in every direction.

So the ball passes the test at every boundary point with room to spare. It is *strictly* pseudoconvex: the bending is not merely nonnegative but strictly positive everywhere. This matches the picture of a round bowl, whose inside curls away from any flat sheet laid across it.

Now contrast a region with a flat spot in a complex direction, say a piece of wall that contains a whole complex disc. Along that disc the wall does not retreat at all; the bending number is zero. Such a point is on the boundary of being good. Push the wall slightly the wrong way and the bending turns negative, and at that moment the region leaks.

What this tells us: pseudoconvexity is the demand that the wall never bend into a complex sheet. The ball satisfies it everywhere; a region with a complex-flat wall sits on the edge; and a region whose wall bulges into a complex sheet fails, and through that failure functions escape.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}^n$ is a nonempty open set, $\delta(z) = d(z, \partial\Omega)$ is the Euclidean distance to the boundary, and $\mathrm{PSH}(\Omega)$ is the cone of plurisubharmonic functions of [06.10.02]. We use the sign convention $\rho < 0$ *inside* $\Omega$ throughout, so that the outward direction is where $\rho$ increases.

**Definition (Hartogs pseudoconvexity).** $\Omega$ is *(Hartogs) pseudoconvex* if the function $-\log\delta(z) = -\log d(z, \partial\Omega)$ is plurisubharmonic on $\Omega$. (When $\Omega = \mathbb{C}^n$ one sets $\delta \equiv +\infty$ and declares $\Omega$ pseudoconvex.)

This is the global, boundary-distance form. There is an equivalent form in terms of exhaustion functions and, for smooth boundaries, a local form in terms of the Levi form.

**Definition (defining function).** A $C^2$ *defining function* for a domain $\Omega$ with $C^2$ boundary near $p \in \partial\Omega$ is a real $C^2$ function $\rho$ on a neighbourhood $U$ of $p$ with $\Omega \cap U = \{\rho < 0\}$, $\partial\Omega \cap U = \{\rho = 0\}$, and $d\rho \ne 0$ on $\{\rho = 0\}$. Writing $\partial\rho = \sum_j (\partial\rho/\partial z_j)\, dz_j$, the *complex tangent space* to $\partial\Omega$ at $p$ is

$$
T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega \;=\; \Big\{\, w \in \mathbb{C}^n \;:\; \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial\rho}{\partial z_j}(p)\, w_j = 0 \,\Big\},
$$

the largest complex-linear subspace of the real tangent hyperplane; it has complex dimension $n - 1$.

**Definition (Levi form and Levi pseudoconvexity).** The *Levi form* of $\rho$ at $p$ is the Hermitian form

$$
\mathcal{L}_\rho(p; w) \;=\; \sum_{j,k=1}^n \frac{\partial^2\rho}{\partial z_j\, \partial\bar z_k}(p)\, w_j \bar w_k .
$$

A domain $\Omega$ with $C^2$ boundary is *Levi pseudoconvex* at $p \in \partial\Omega$ if $\mathcal{L}_\rho(p; w) \ge 0$ for all $w \in T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega$, and *strictly* (or *strongly*) Levi pseudoconvex at $p$ if $\mathcal{L}_\rho(p; w) > 0$ for all nonzero $w \in T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega$. It is Levi pseudoconvex if this holds at every boundary point.

The Levi form on $T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega$ does not depend on the choice of defining function (Proposition 1 of the Full proof set). The value on directions outside $T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega$ does depend on $\rho$ and carries no geometric meaning — this is why the form is read only on the complex tangent space.

**Definition (PSH exhaustion).** Recall from [06.10.02] that $\varphi : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ is an *exhaustion* if every sublevel set $\Omega_c = \{\varphi < c\}$ is relatively compact in $\Omega$, and a *PSH exhaustion* if additionally $\varphi \in \mathrm{PSH}(\Omega)$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The Levi form is read only on the complex tangent space $T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega$, never on all of $\mathbb{C}^n$. On the full space one can change the sign of $\mathcal{L}_\rho(p; w)$ for non-tangential $w$ by replacing $\rho$ with $e^{\lambda\rho} - 1$; only the restriction to $T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega$ is invariant.

- Pseudoconvexity is strictly weaker than convexity. A bounded convex domain is Levi pseudoconvex, but the converse fails: the worm domains and the Hartogs triangle complements are pseudoconvex yet far from convex. Convexity tests the real Hessian on all real tangent directions; pseudoconvexity tests only the complex Hessian on complex tangent directions.

- A boundary may be Levi pseudoconvex with the Levi form vanishing identically on $T^{\mathbb{C}}_p$ (the *weakly pseudoconvex*, or *Levi-flat*, case), as for the boundary $\{|z_2| = 1\}$ inside a polydisc. Weak pseudoconvexity is genuinely harder than strict, and many sharp $\bar\partial$ estimates degrade there.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (equivalence of pseudoconvexity notions).** *Let $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}^n$ be open. Consider the conditions:*

1. *$\Omega$ is Hartogs pseudoconvex, i.e. $-\log\delta \in \mathrm{PSH}(\Omega)$;*
2. *$\Omega$ admits a continuous plurisubharmonic exhaustion function;*
3. *(if $\partial\Omega$ is $C^2$) $\Omega$ is Levi pseudoconvex, i.e. $\mathcal{L}_\rho(p; w) \ge 0$ for all $p \in \partial\Omega$ and all $w \in T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega$.*

*Then (1) $\Leftrightarrow$ (2) always, and (1) $\Leftrightarrow$ (3) when $\partial\Omega$ is $C^2$.*

The proof of (1) $\Leftrightarrow$ (3) rests on relating the Levi form of the defining function $\rho$ to the Levi form of the distance function $\delta$, which differ by a controlled boundary term [ref: TODO_REF Krantz Ch. 2].

**Lemma (Levi form of a defining function controls $-\log\delta$ near the boundary).** *Let $\Omega$ have $C^2$ boundary with defining function $\rho$ normalised so that $|\nabla\rho| = 1$ on $\partial\Omega$ (the signed-distance normalisation, $\rho = -\delta$ near $\partial\Omega$ up to $C^2$ error). For $z$ near $\partial\Omega$ and any $w \in \mathbb{C}^n$,*

$$
\sum_{j,k}\frac{\partial^2(-\log\delta)}{\partial z_j\,\partial\bar z_k}(z)\, w_j\bar w_k \;=\; \frac{1}{\delta(z)}\,\mathcal{L}_\rho(z; w) \;+\; \frac{1}{\delta(z)^2}\Big|\sum_j \frac{\partial\rho}{\partial z_j}(z)\, w_j\Big|^2 \;+\; O(1)\,|w|^2 ,
$$

*where the $O(1)$ term is bounded uniformly near $\partial\Omega$.*

*Proof of Lemma.* Write $u = -\log\delta = -\log(-\rho)$ near $\partial\Omega$, valid because $\rho = -\delta + O(\delta^2)$ under the normalisation. Differentiating $u = -\log(-\rho)$,

$$
\frac{\partial u}{\partial z_j} = \frac{1}{-\rho}\,\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial z_j} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial z_j},
\qquad
\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial z_j\,\partial\bar z_k} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial^2\rho}{\partial z_j\,\partial\bar z_k} + \frac{1}{\rho^2}\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial z_j}\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial\bar z_k}.
$$

Since $-\rho = \delta + O(\delta^2)$, the first term is $\delta^{-1}\mathcal{L}_\rho(z; \cdot) + O(1)$ and the second is $\delta^{-2}|\sum_j(\partial\rho/\partial z_j)w_j|^2 + O(\delta^{-1})$ when contracted against $w \otimes \bar w$; absorbing the lower-order corrections into the $O(1)|w|^2$ term gives the stated identity. $\square$

*Proof of Theorem.*

*(1) $\Rightarrow$ (2).* If $\Omega = \mathbb{C}^n$, take $\varphi = |z|^2$. Otherwise $-\log\delta \in \mathrm{PSH}(\Omega)$ by hypothesis, and $\varphi(z) = \max(-\log\delta(z),\, |z|^2)$ is a maximum of two PSH functions, hence PSH ([06.10.02]), continuous, and an exhaustion: a sublevel set forces both $\delta(z) > e^{-c}$ (bounded away from the boundary) and $|z|^2 < c$ (bounded), so it is relatively compact in $\Omega$.

*(2) $\Rightarrow$ (1).* Suppose $\Omega$ has a continuous PSH exhaustion $\varphi$. We must show $-\log\delta$ is PSH. The key is the *disc-continuity criterion*: $-\log\delta$ is PSH if and only if for every analytic disc $f : \overline{\mathbb{D}} \to \Omega$ (continuous on $\overline{\mathbb{D}}$, holomorphic on $\mathbb{D}$, with values in $\mathbb{C}^n$) one has $-\log\delta(f(0)) \le \sup_{|\zeta|=1}\big(-\log\delta(f(\zeta))\big)$, equivalently $\delta(f(0)) \ge \inf_{|\zeta|=1}\delta(f(\zeta))$. Fix such a disc and any unit vector $b \in \mathbb{C}^n$; the shifted disc $\zeta \mapsto f(\zeta) + tb$ stays in $\Omega$ for $t$ below $\inf_{|\zeta|=1}\delta(f(\zeta))$, because $\varphi \circ (f + tb)$ is subharmonic in $\zeta$ and bounded on the boundary circle by $\sup_\varphi$ over the boundary disc, so by the maximum principle the shifted disc cannot escape the sublevel set its boundary lies in. Letting $t$ increase to the boundary distance over the rim and applying this to the centre gives $\delta(f(0)) \ge \inf_{|\zeta|=1}\delta(f(\zeta))$, the disc inequality. Hence $-\log\delta$ is PSH.

*(1) $\Rightarrow$ (3).* Let $p \in \partial\Omega$ and $w \in T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega$, so $\sum_j(\partial\rho/\partial z_j)(p)\,w_j = 0$. In the Lemma's identity the middle term vanishes at the boundary in the limit, leaving

$$
\sum_{j,k}\frac{\partial^2(-\log\delta)}{\partial z_j\,\partial\bar z_k}(z)\,w_j\bar w_k = \frac{1}{\delta(z)}\,\mathcal{L}_\rho(z; w) + O(1)|w|^2
$$

as $z \to p$ along the inner normal with $w$ kept in (the nearby) complex tangent space. Plurisubharmonicity of $-\log\delta$ forces the left side $\ge 0$; multiplying by $\delta(z) \to 0^+$ and taking the limit gives $\mathcal{L}_\rho(p; w) \ge 0$. As $p, w$ were arbitrary, $\Omega$ is Levi pseudoconvex.

*(3) $\Rightarrow$ (1).* This is the harder direction. Suppose $\Omega$ is Levi pseudoconvex. One first shows $-\log\delta$ is PSH on a neighbourhood of $\partial\Omega$ inside $\Omega$: by the Lemma, on the complex tangent space the Hessian of $-\log\delta$ is $\delta^{-1}\mathcal{L}_\rho \ge 0$ up to $O(1)$, and on the missing one-dimensional normal complement the term $\delta^{-2}|\sum_j(\partial\rho/\partial z_j)w_j|^2$ dominates, being of order $\delta^{-2}$ against the $O(\delta^{-1})$ corrections; so the full complex Hessian of $-\log\delta$ is positive semidefinite near $\partial\Omega$. Away from $\partial\Omega$ one corrects by passing to $\varphi = -\log\delta + C|z|^2$ on a compact inner region and gluing (the gluing lemma for PSH functions, [06.10.02] Exercise 6), producing a global continuous PSH exhaustion; then (2) $\Rightarrow$ (1) returns plurisubharmonicity of $-\log\delta$ itself. $\square$

**Bridge.** This equivalence builds toward the solution of the Levi problem and appears again in the Hörmander $L^2$ existence theorem for $\bar\partial$, where condition (2) supplies the strictly PSH weight whose Levi form makes the basic estimate close. The foundational reason the three notions coincide is the Lemma: the complex Hessian of the boundary-distance barrier $-\log\delta$ is the Levi form of the boundary divided by the distance, so a local sign condition on $\partial\Omega$ and a global convexity condition on the barrier are the same datum measured at two scales. This is exactly the bridge promised in [06.10.01] and [06.10.02]: the distance function $-\log d(z,\partial\Omega)$ identifies the geometric boundary form with the global no-leak property. The equivalence generalises holomorphic convexity — replacing the test family $\mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ of [06.10.01] by the PSH functions of [06.10.02] converts holomorphic convexity into pseudoconvexity — and the central insight is that putting these together yields the easy half of the Levi problem: every domain of holomorphy is pseudoconvex, proved next.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Compute the Levi form of the ball defining function $\rho(z) = |z|^2 - 1 = \sum_j z_j\bar z_j - 1$ on $\mathbb{C}^n$ and confirm the ball is strictly pseudoconvex.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate: $\partial^2(z_j\bar z_j)/\partial z_j\partial\bar z_j = 1$, off-diagonal entries vanish.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The complex Hessian of $\rho$ is the identity matrix: $\partial^2\rho/\partial z_j\partial\bar z_k = \delta_{jk}$. So $\mathcal{L}_\rho(p; w) = \sum_j|w_j|^2 = |w|^2 > 0$ for every nonzero $w$, in particular for nonzero $w \in T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\mathbb{B}^n$. The ball is therefore strictly Levi pseudoconvex. Rubric: full credit for the identity Hessian and the strict positivity $|w|^2 > 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, true/false).**

Every bounded convex domain in $\mathbb{C}^n$ with $C^2$ boundary is Levi pseudoconvex.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Convexity gives nonnegativity of the *real* Hessian of $\rho$ on the real tangent space; the Levi form is a restriction of a piece of that to complex tangent directions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.** Feedback-correct: for a convex domain with $C^2$ boundary one may take $\rho$ convex, so its real Hessian is positive semidefinite on the tangent hyperplane; the Levi form $\mathcal{L}_\rho(p; w)$ is exactly the complex (Hermitian) part of that Hessian evaluated on $w \in T^{\mathbb{C}}_p$, hence $\ge 0$. So convex $\Rightarrow$ Levi pseudoconvex. Feedback-wrong: the converse is false — pseudoconvexity is strictly weaker than convexity.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that the polydisc $P(0,1) = \{|z_j| < 1,\ j = 1,\dots,n\}$ is pseudoconvex by exhibiting a continuous PSH exhaustion, without computing any Levi form (the boundary is only Lipschitz, not $C^2$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The functions $-\log(1 - |z_j|^2)$ are PSH on the polydisc; combine them.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For each $j$, $-\log(1 - |z_j|^2)$ is PSH on $P(0,1)$: it is a function of $z_j$ alone equal to $-\log$ of $(1-|z_j|^2)$, and $-\log(1 - |\lambda|^2)$ is subharmonic (indeed convex increasing in $|\lambda|^2$) on the one-variable disc, so it slices to subharmonic in every direction. The sum $\varphi(z) = \sum_{j=1}^n -\log(1 - |z_j|^2)$ is then PSH, continuous, and an exhaustion: $\varphi < c$ forces each $|z_j|^2 < 1 - e^{-c}/n \cdot(\dots)$, in any case each $|z_j|$ bounded away from $1$, so $\{\varphi < c\} \Subset P(0,1)$. By the equivalence theorem, the polydisc is pseudoconvex. Rubric: full credit for the PSH sum and the exhaustion property; the boundary-distance form $-\log\delta$ equals $-\log\min_j(1-|z_j|)$ and is also PSH.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $\rho(z) = \mathrm{Re}(z_n) + |z_1|^2 + \cdots + |z_{n-1}|^2$ near $0$, defining a domain $\{\rho < 0\}$. Compute $T^{\mathbb{C}}_0\partial\Omega$ and the Levi form on it, and classify the point $0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathrm{Re}(z_n) = \tfrac12(z_n + \bar z_n)$, so $\partial\rho/\partial z_n = 1/2$ and the complex tangent space is $\{w_n = 0\}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Here $\partial\rho/\partial z_n(0) = 1/2$ and $\partial\rho/\partial z_j(0) = \bar z_j|_0 = 0$ for $j < n$, so $T^{\mathbb{C}}_0\partial\Omega = \{w : \tfrac12 w_n = 0\} = \{w_n = 0\}$, of complex dimension $n-1$. The complex Hessian is $\partial^2\rho/\partial z_j\partial\bar z_k = \delta_{jk}$ for $j,k \le n-1$ and $0$ if either index is $n$. On $T^{\mathbb{C}}_0$ (where $w_n = 0$), $\mathcal{L}_\rho(0; w) = \sum_{j=1}^{n-1}|w_j|^2 > 0$ for nonzero $w$. So $0$ is a strictly pseudoconvex point; this $\rho$ is the local model (the *Siegel/paraboloid* normal form) of a strictly pseudoconvex boundary. Rubric: full credit for $T^{\mathbb{C}}_0 = \{w_n = 0\}$ and the strictly positive Levi form there.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that an intersection $\Omega = \Omega_1 \cap \Omega_2$ of two pseudoconvex domains (with $\Omega$ open and nonempty) is pseudoconvex.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Express $\delta_\Omega$ in terms of $\delta_{\Omega_1}$ and $\delta_{\Omega_2}$, or combine the two exhaustions by a maximum.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On $\Omega = \Omega_1 \cap \Omega_2$ the distance satisfies $\delta_\Omega = \min(\delta_{\Omega_1}, \delta_{\Omega_2})$, so $-\log\delta_\Omega = \max(-\log\delta_{\Omega_1}, -\log\delta_{\Omega_2})$. Each $-\log\delta_{\Omega_i}$ is PSH on $\Omega_i \supseteq \Omega$ by hypothesis, and the maximum of two PSH functions is PSH ([06.10.02]); hence $-\log\delta_\Omega$ is PSH on $\Omega$ and $\Omega$ is pseudoconvex. (Equivalently, take $\varphi = \max(\varphi_1, \varphi_2, |z|^2)$ of the two exhaustions.) Rubric: full credit for the $\min$-distance identity and the max-stability of PSH.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove that an increasing union $\Omega = \bigcup_j \Omega_j$ of pseudoconvex domains with $\Omega_j \subseteq \Omega_{j+1}$ is pseudoconvex (Behnke–Stein for pseudoconvexity).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The boundary distances increase: $\delta_{\Omega_j} \le \delta_{\Omega_{j+1}}$ on $\Omega_j$, so $-\log\delta_{\Omega_j}$ decreases; relate the limit to $-\log\delta_\Omega$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix a point $z_0 \in \Omega$; it lies in some $\Omega_{j_0}$ and in all later $\Omega_j$. On any compact $K \Subset \Omega$, $K \subseteq \Omega_j$ for large $j$, and $\delta_{\Omega_j}(z) \uparrow \delta_\Omega(z)$ as $j \to \infty$ for each $z \in \Omega$ (the regions grow, so the distance to the boundary grows, monotonically), with the convergence locally uniform by Dini since the $\delta_{\Omega_j}$ are increasing continuous and the limit $\delta_\Omega$ is continuous. Hence $-\log\delta_{\Omega_j} \downarrow -\log\delta_\Omega$ pointwise on $\Omega$. Each $-\log\delta_{\Omega_j}$ is PSH on $\Omega_j$; restricting to a fixed compact and using that a decreasing limit of PSH functions is PSH ([06.10.02] Exercise 5), $-\log\delta_\Omega$ is PSH on every compact, hence on $\Omega$. So $\Omega$ is pseudoconvex. This is the pseudoconvex analogue of the holomorphic-convexity Behnke–Stein result of [06.10.01]. Rubric: full credit for the monotone increase of $\delta_{\Omega_j}$ and the decreasing-PSH-limit argument.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove the *easy half of the Levi problem*: every domain of holomorphy $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}^n$ is pseudoconvex. Use the Kontinuitätssatz (continuity principle) for analytic discs.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If $-\log\delta$ failed to be PSH, a family of analytic discs would push their centres toward the boundary while their boundaries stay away from it; a domain of holomorphy forbids this.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $\Omega$ is a domain of holomorphy. We verify the disc inequality $\delta(f(0)) \ge \inf_{|\zeta|=1}\delta(f(\zeta))$ for every analytic disc $f : \overline{\mathbb{D}} \to \Omega$, which is equivalent to $-\log\delta \in \mathrm{PSH}(\Omega)$. Suppose not: there is a disc with $\delta(f(0)) < \inf_{|\zeta|=1}\delta(f(\zeta)) =: 2r$. Translate the disc family $f_t = f + tb$ in some direction $b$; the boundary circles $f_t(\partial\mathbb{D})$ stay at distance $> r$ from $\partial\Omega$ for $|t| \le r$, forming a compact family $S = \bigcup_{|t|\le r} f_t(\partial\mathbb{D}) \Subset \Omega$, while some centre $f_{t_0}(0)$ reaches $\partial\Omega$. By the maximum principle, every $g \in \mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ satisfies $\sup_{f_t(\overline{\mathbb{D}})}|g| = \sup_{f_t(\partial\mathbb{D})}|g| \le \sup_S |g|$, so the centres $f_t(0)$ all lie in the holomorphically convex hull $\hat S_\Omega$ of [06.10.01]. But a domain of holomorphy is holomorphically convex (Cartan–Thullen, [06.10.01]), so $\hat S_\Omega \Subset \Omega$, contradicting a centre reaching $\partial\Omega$. Hence the disc inequality holds and $\Omega$ is pseudoconvex. Rubric: full credit for the disc-translation family, the hull containment via the maximum principle, and the Cartan–Thullen contradiction.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

Pseudoconvexity is the geometric face of the structure theory begun in [06.10.01] and [06.10.02]. The results below sharpen the Levi-form picture, record the bumping and exhaustion technology that the $\bar\partial$ method consumes, and state the full Levi problem whose easy half was proved above.

**Smoothing and strict exhaustions.** A pseudoconvex $\Omega$ admits not merely a continuous PSH exhaustion but a *smooth strictly* PSH exhaustion. Starting from $\varphi_0 = \max(-\log\delta, |z|^2)$, one regularises by the convolution of [06.10.02] on each shell, glues the smoothings across shells with the PSH gluing lemma, and adds a rapidly growing convex function of $|z|^2$ to upgrade semidefiniteness to definiteness. The resulting $\varphi \in C^\infty(\Omega)$ with $\mathcal{L}_\varphi > 0$ everywhere is the weight that the Hörmander $L^2$ existence theorem requires; the smooth strictly PSH exhaustion is, in effect, the analytic incarnation of pseudoconvexity.

**Levi-flat boundaries and the foliation.** When the Levi form vanishes identically on $T^{\mathbb{C}}\partial\Omega$ along a boundary piece, that piece is *Levi-flat*. The complex tangent spaces $T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega$ then integrate to a foliation of the boundary by complex hypersurfaces — the Levi foliation. The model is $\{\mathrm{Re}(z_n) = 0\}$, foliated by the affine hyperplanes $\{z_n = it\}$. Levi-flat hypersurfaces are the boundary between strict pseudoconvexity and pseudoconcavity and are the source of the worst regularity behaviour for the $\bar\partial$-Neumann problem.

**Bumping and local-to-global.** Strict pseudoconvexity is stable and localisable: near a strictly pseudoconvex boundary point one can *bump* the domain outward, enlarging $\Omega$ to a strictly larger pseudoconvex $\tilde\Omega$ that agrees with $\Omega$ away from the point, by adding a small multiple of a peak function $|z - p|^2 - \varepsilon$ to the defining function. This bumping is the mechanism behind Oka's solution of the Levi problem on strongly pseudoconvex domains: one solves $\bar\partial$ on the slightly larger domain and restricts. Weakly pseudoconvex points admit no such universal bump, which is why the general Levi problem needed the global $L^2$ method rather than a local kernel construction.

**The Levi problem.** The converse of the easy half — *every pseudoconvex domain is a domain of holomorphy* — is the Levi problem proper, solved by Oka for $n = 2$ in 1942 and in general by Oka, Bremermann, and Norguet in 1953–1954, and re-proved analytically by Hörmander's 1965 weighted $L^2$ estimates for $\bar\partial$. Combined with Cartan–Thullen ([06.10.01]) and the PSH-hull identity ([06.10.02]), it closes the circle: for a domain $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}^n$,

$$
\text{domain of holomorphy} \iff \text{holomorphically convex} \iff \text{pseudoconvex} \iff \text{PSH-exhaustible} \iff H^{q\ge 1}(\Omega, \mathcal{O}) = 0.
$$

The Levi form sits at the boundary-geometry end of this chain; the cohomological vanishing sits at the analytic end; and $-\log\delta$ is the function that ties them.

**Synthesis.** Pseudoconvexity is the foundational reason the structure theory of $\mathbb{C}^n$ has a local-geometric criterion at all: the Levi form converts the global no-leak condition of [06.10.01] into a pointwise sign condition on the boundary, and this is exactly the same datum as the plurisubharmonicity of the barrier $-\log\delta$, read at the scale $\delta \to 0$. Putting these together, the geometric notion (Levi form $\ge 0$ on the complex tangent space), the potential-theoretic notion (continuous PSH exhaustion), and the function-theoretic notion (holomorphic convexity, [06.10.01]) are three faces of one structure, and the central insight is that the boundary-distance height $-\log d(z,\partial\Omega)$ is the universal certificate identifying all three. The easy half of the Levi problem is dual to Cartan–Thullen: where Cartan–Thullen produces a singular function from a non-compact hull, the disc-continuity argument produces a hull point from a failure of plurisubharmonicity, and the bridge is in both cases the holomorphically convex hull. This pattern recurs throughout the chapter: the smooth strictly PSH exhaustion built here is the weight on which the Hörmander $L^2$ theory runs, and the strict Levi form is the positivity that makes its basic estimate close.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (the Levi form on $T^{\mathbb{C}}_p$ is independent of the defining function).** *Let $\rho_1, \rho_2$ be two $C^2$ defining functions for $\Omega$ near $p \in \partial\Omega$. Then $\mathcal{L}_{\rho_1}(p; w)$ and $\mathcal{L}_{\rho_2}(p; w)$ are positive multiples of each other for $w \in T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega$; in particular the sign of the Levi form on the complex tangent space is well defined.*

*Proof.* Two defining functions for the same boundary near $p$ satisfy $\rho_2 = h\,\rho_1$ with $h$ a positive $C^1$ function (where $\rho_1 \ne 0$, and $h = |\nabla\rho_2|/|\nabla\rho_1| > 0$ on $\{\rho_1 = 0\}$ by comparing gradients of two functions with the same zero set and nonvanishing differential). Compute the complex Hessian by the product rule:

$$
\frac{\partial^2\rho_2}{\partial z_j\,\partial\bar z_k} = h\,\frac{\partial^2\rho_1}{\partial z_j\,\partial\bar z_k} + \frac{\partial h}{\partial z_j}\frac{\partial\rho_1}{\partial\bar z_k} + \frac{\partial h}{\partial\bar z_k}\frac{\partial\rho_1}{\partial z_j} + \rho_1\frac{\partial^2 h}{\partial z_j\,\partial\bar z_k}.
$$

Evaluate at $p$ (so $\rho_1(p) = 0$, killing the last term) and contract against $w \otimes \bar w$ with $w \in T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega$, i.e. $\sum_j(\partial\rho_1/\partial z_j)(p)\,w_j = 0$ and its conjugate $\sum_k(\partial\rho_1/\partial\bar z_k)(p)\,\bar w_k = 0$. The two cross terms vanish because each contracts a derivative of $\rho_1$ against $w$ or $\bar w$ in a complex-tangent direction. What survives is $\mathcal{L}_{\rho_2}(p; w) = h(p)\,\mathcal{L}_{\rho_1}(p; w)$ with $h(p) > 0$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (strict pseudoconvexity is an open, biholomorphically invariant condition).** *Let $\Omega$ have $C^2$ boundary. The set of strictly pseudoconvex boundary points is open in $\partial\Omega$, and strict pseudoconvexity at $p$ is preserved by any biholomorphism defined near $p$.*

*Proof.* Openness: $\mathcal{L}_\rho(p; w)$ depends continuously on $p$, and positive-definiteness of a continuously varying Hermitian form on the continuously varying subspace $T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega$ is an open condition (the minimal eigenvalue over the unit sphere of the tangent space is continuous and positive at $p$, hence positive nearby). Invariance: let $F$ be biholomorphic near $p$ with $F(p) = q$, and let $\sigma$ be a defining function for $F(\Omega)$ near $q$, so $\rho = \sigma \circ F$ defines $\Omega$ near $p$. Since $F$ is holomorphic, $\partial F_m/\partial\bar z_k = 0$, and the chain rule gives

$$
\frac{\partial^2\rho}{\partial z_j\,\partial\bar z_k}(p) = \sum_{m,l}\frac{\partial^2\sigma}{\partial \zeta_m\,\partial\bar\zeta_l}(q)\,\frac{\partial F_m}{\partial z_j}(p)\,\overline{\frac{\partial F_l}{\partial z_k}(p)} + \sum_m \frac{\partial\sigma}{\partial\zeta_m}(q)\frac{\partial^2 F_m}{\partial z_j\,\partial\bar z_k}(p).
$$

The second sum vanishes because each $F_m$ is holomorphic, so $\partial^2 F_m/\partial z_j\partial\bar z_k = 0$. Hence $\mathcal{L}_\rho(p; w) = \mathcal{L}_\sigma(q; F'(p)w)$, where $F'(p)$ is the holomorphic Jacobian, and $F'(p)$ maps $T^{\mathbb{C}}_p\partial\Omega$ isomorphically onto $T^{\mathbb{C}}_q\partial F(\Omega)$. Positive-definiteness of $\mathcal{L}_\sigma$ on the latter therefore transfers to positive-definiteness of $\mathcal{L}_\rho$ on the former. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (a strictly pseudoconvex point is locally biholomorphic to a strictly convex one).** *If $\partial\Omega$ is strictly Levi pseudoconvex at $p$, there is a local biholomorphism $F$ near $p$ such that $F(\Omega)$ is strictly convex near $F(p)$.*

*Proof.* Choose holomorphic coordinates centred at $p$ in which the defining function has the second-order Taylor expansion

$$
\rho(z) = \mathrm{Re}(z_n) + \sum_{j,k}\frac{\partial^2\rho}{\partial z_j\partial\bar z_k}(0)\,z_j\bar z_k + \mathrm{Re}\Big(\sum_{j,k}\frac{\partial^2\rho}{\partial z_j\partial z_k}(0)\,z_j z_k\Big) + o(|z|^2),
$$

after the linear change putting $\partial\rho(0)$ in the $z_n$-direction. The holomorphic change of variable $w_n = z_n + \sum_{j,k}(\partial^2\rho/\partial z_j\partial z_k)(0)\,z_j z_k$, $w_j = z_j$ ($j < n$) absorbs the holomorphic Hessian term $\mathrm{Re}(\sum z_j z_k\,\partial^2\rho/\partial z_j\partial z_k)$ into $\mathrm{Re}(w_n)$. In the new coordinates the second-order part is $\mathrm{Re}(w_n) + \mathcal{L}_\rho(0; w)$ with the Levi form positive definite on the complex tangent $\{w_n = 0\}$. Adding $C(\mathrm{Re}\,w_n)^2$ for large $C$ (a further $C^2$ change of defining function not affecting the zero set to second order) makes the *real* Hessian positive definite on the whole real tangent hyperplane, so $F(\Omega) = \{w : \text{new }\rho < 0\}$ is strictly convex near $0$. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (continuity principle obstructs leakage).** *Let $\Omega$ be pseudoconvex and $\{f_t : \overline{\mathbb{D}} \to \mathbb{C}^n\}_{t \in [0,1]}$ a continuous family of analytic discs with $\bigcup_t f_t(\partial\mathbb{D}) \Subset \Omega$ and $f_0(\overline{\mathbb{D}}) \subseteq \Omega$. Then $f_t(\overline{\mathbb{D}}) \subseteq \Omega$ for all $t$.*

*Proof.* Let $\varphi$ be a continuous PSH exhaustion of $\Omega$ (Theorem, condition (2)). The boundary union $S = \bigcup_t f_t(\partial\mathbb{D})$ is compact in $\Omega$, so $M := \sup_S\varphi < \infty$. For each $t$ with $f_t(\overline{\mathbb{D}}) \subseteq \Omega$, the composition $\varphi\circ f_t$ is subharmonic on $\mathbb{D}$ and continuous on $\overline{\mathbb{D}}$, so by the maximum principle $\sup_{\overline{\mathbb{D}}}\varphi\circ f_t = \sup_{\partial\mathbb{D}}\varphi\circ f_t \le M$; thus $f_t(\overline{\mathbb{D}})$ lies in the sublevel set $\{\varphi \le M\} \Subset \Omega$. Let $T = \{t : f_t(\overline{\mathbb{D}}) \subseteq \Omega\}$. It contains $0$, is open (a disc in the open set $\{\varphi < M+1\}\Subset\Omega$ stays in $\Omega$ under small perturbation), and is closed: if $t_n \to t$ with $f_{t_n}(\overline{\mathbb{D}}) \subseteq \{\varphi\le M\}$, the limit disc $f_t(\overline{\mathbb{D}})$ lies in the compact $\{\varphi \le M\}\Subset\Omega$ by continuity. So $T = [0,1]$. This is the precise sense in which pseudoconvexity forbids the disc-leakage that destroys a domain of holomorphy, and it is the engine of Exercise 7. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Plurisubharmonic functions [06.10.02].** This unit is the geometric payoff of the PSH theory: pseudoconvexity is defined through the plurisubharmonicity of $-\log d(z,\partial\Omega)$, and the equivalence with a continuous PSH exhaustion is proved using the maximum principle, the max-stability, and the decreasing-limit and gluing lemmas established there. The Levi form here is the boundary restriction of the complex Hessian that defined plurisubharmonicity.

- **Domains of holomorphy and holomorphic convexity [06.10.01].** The easy half of the Levi problem proved in Exercise 7 and Proposition 4 shows every domain of holomorphy is pseudoconvex, using Cartan–Thullen and the holomorphically convex hull directly. Pseudoconvexity is the local-geometric reformulation of the global holomorphic convexity characterised there; the full Levi problem is the converse, completing the equivalence.

- **Holomorphic functions of several variables [06.07.01].** The defining function, the complex tangent space, and the Wirtinger derivatives $\partial/\partial z_j$, $\partial/\partial\bar z_k$ that build the Levi form are the multi-variable calculus of that unit. The vanishing of the second sum in Propositions 2 and 3 is exactly the holomorphy condition $\partial F_m/\partial\bar z_k = 0$, so the Levi form's biholomorphic invariance is a genuinely complex-analytic fact with no real-variable shadow.

- **Dirichlet problem and the Perron method [06.01.24].** The disc-continuity criterion and the subharmonicity of $\varphi\circ f$ on a complex line reduce pseudoconvexity to one-variable subharmonic potential theory; the maximum principle that drives Proposition 4 is the several-variable lift of the one-variable maximum principle developed there.

- **The $\bar\partial$ problem with $L^2$ estimates [06.04.05].** The smooth strictly PSH exhaustion constructed in the Advanced results is the weight on which the Hörmander weighted $L^2$ existence theorem runs; the strict Levi form is the positive curvature term in the Bochner–Kodaira basic estimate, and the solution of the full Levi problem is what that estimate ultimately delivers. The dimension-one $\bar\partial$-Hilbert theory there is the seed of this several-variable machinery.

- **Survey of Cartan–Serre Stein theory in higher dimensions [06.09.08].** Pseudoconvexity is the manifold-level hypothesis under which a complex manifold is Stein; the equivalence chain of the Advanced results is the open-domain case of the general Stein theory surveyed there, with the Levi form supplying the boundary criterion for relatively compact pseudoconvex pieces.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The boundary positivity condition now bearing his name was found by Eugenio Elia Levi in 1910 in *Studii sui punti singolari essenziali delle funzioni analitiche di due o più variabili complesse* [ref: TODO_REF Levi 1910] (*Annali di Matematica Pura ed Applicata* (3) 17, 61–87). Levi analysed the boundaries across which a holomorphic function of two variables cannot be continued and isolated the second-order condition — the nonnegativity of what is now the Levi form on the complex tangent space — that the boundary of a domain of holomorphy must satisfy. He proved the necessity (the easy half) and conjectured the sufficiency, which became the Levi problem.

The reframing that made the problem tractable was the introduction of plurisubharmonic functions and the recognition that the right global object is not the boundary form alone but the function $-\log d(z, \partial\Omega)$ on the whole domain. Kiyoshi Oka, in the sixth of his memoirs *Sur les fonctions analytiques de plusieurs variables* [ref: TODO_REF Oka 1942] (*Tôhoku Mathematical Journal* 49, 15–52), worked with the class he called *fonctions pseudoconvexes* and established the equivalence of pseudoconvexity with the existence of a plurisubharmonic exhaustion, then solved the Levi problem for $n = 2$. The general solution followed in 1953–1954 through Oka's ninth memoir and the independent work of Hans-Joachim Bremermann and François Norguet, and Lars Hörmander's 1965 $L^2$ estimates for $\bar\partial$ supplied a uniform analytic proof valid in all dimensions.

The local-to-global passage in Proposition 3 — that a strictly pseudoconvex boundary point is biholomorphic to a strictly convex one — is the device by which Oka reduced the strongly pseudoconvex case to convexity, where domains of holomorphy were already understood; the weakly pseudoconvex case, where no such local convexification exists, is what forced the global potential-theoretic and PDE methods. Pseudoconvexity thereby occupies the centre of the subject: it is the boundary-geometric condition that Levi found, the potential-theoretic condition that Oka and Lelong formalised, and the function-theoretic condition that Cartan and Thullen characterised, identified through the single barrier $-\log d(z,\partial\Omega)$.
