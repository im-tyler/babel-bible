## Intuition [Beginner]

Take a non-compact Riemann surface $X$ — the complex line, the punctured plane, an annulus, the upper half-plane, any compact curve with a few points removed. The previous unit on Stein Riemann surfaces [06.09.01] explains why every such $X$ carries an abundant supply of global holomorphic functions: enough to separate any two points, enough to keep the holomorphic hull of any compact set compact. Cartan's Theorems A and B are the cohomological pay-off of that abundance. They say two things about the analytic data that lives on $X$.

The first theorem (Theorem A) says that whatever local analytic gadget you can attach to $X$ — a sheaf of holomorphic sections of a line bundle, a sheaf of meromorphic functions with prescribed poles, an ideal sheaf cutting out a discrete set — is *generated by global sections*. Pick any point and any local section there. You can write that local section as a finite sum of global sections of the same sheaf, with coefficients that are local holomorphic functions. There is no obstruction to lifting a local datum to global data, point by point.

The second theorem (Theorem B) says the higher cohomology of any such sheaf vanishes: $H^q(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for every $q \geq 1$. Cohomology in this sense measures the obstruction to gluing local data into global data along a cover. Theorem B says: on a Stein surface, that obstruction is always zero. Mittag-Leffler problems, Cousin problems, line-bundle classifications — every classical existence question on $X$ becomes a corollary of one identity.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take $X = \mathbb{C}$, the complex line, and the structure sheaf $\mathcal{F} = \mathcal{O}_X$ of holomorphic functions. The stalk $\mathcal{O}_{X, x}$ at a point $x$ is the ring of convergent power series at $x$ — every germ of a holomorphic function near $x$. Theorem A says: every convergent power series at $x$ can be written as a finite combination of *entire* functions, with coefficients that are themselves germs of holomorphic functions at $x$.

Concrete instance: pick $x = 0$ and the germ $f(z) = 1 + z + z^2/2! + z^3/3! + \cdots = e^z$ at $0$. The germ $f$ is a single global section of $\mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{C}}$ — the entire function $e^z$ itself — so the generation is direct: take the global section $e^z$ and the coefficient $1 \in \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{C}, 0}$.

Pick instead a more interesting germ at $0$, say $g(z) = z + z^2 + z^3$. Two global sections suffice to generate it: the entire functions $z$ and $z^2$. Write $g = z + z^2 \cdot (1 + z)$, so $g = z \cdot 1 + z^2 \cdot (1 + z)$ uses local coefficients $1, 1 + z \in \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{C}, 0}$ and global sections $z, z^2 \in \Gamma(\mathbb{C}, \mathcal{O}_{\mathbb{C}})$.

Theorem B says: $H^1(\mathbb{C}, \mathcal{O}) = 0$, $H^2(\mathbb{C}, \mathcal{O}) = 0$, and so on. The first vanishing is the classical Mittag-Leffler theorem: every prescribed pattern of principal parts at a discrete set of points in $\mathbb{C}$ is realised by a global meromorphic function. The higher vanishings are forced by real dimension two — there is no $H^q$ to talk about for $q \geq 2$ on a Riemann surface to begin with.

What this tells us: on $\mathbb{C}$, the Stein property collapses every classical existence problem about meromorphic functions, holomorphic line bundles, and prescribed local data into a single statement about the structure sheaf, and Theorems A and B promote that statement from the structure sheaf to every analytic sheaf on the surface.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a Stein manifold (in this unit, almost always a non-compact connected Riemann surface, with $X$ Stein automatic by Behnke-Stein [06.09.01]). Let $\mathcal{O}_X$ be the structure sheaf of holomorphic functions on $X$.

**Definition (coherent analytic sheaf).** A sheaf of $\mathcal{O}_X$-modules $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$ is **coherent** when it is *locally finitely generated* (every $x \in X$ has a neighbourhood $U$ and a surjection $\mathcal{O}_U^{n} \twoheadrightarrow \mathcal{F}|_U$) and *locally finitely related* (the kernel of any local surjection $\mathcal{O}_U^{n} \to \mathcal{F}|_U$ is itself locally finitely generated). Coherence is the analytic version of "locally finitely presented" in commutative algebra; Oka's coherence theorem (1950) gives that $\mathcal{O}_X$ is coherent over itself, and standard constructions (kernels, cokernels, finite tensor products of coherent sheaves) preserve coherence.

**Theorem A (Cartan-Serre 1953).** *Let $X$ be a Stein manifold and $\mathcal{F}$ a coherent analytic sheaf on $X$. For every $x \in X$ the stalk $\mathcal{F}_x$ is generated as an $\mathcal{O}_{X, x}$-module by the images of global sections.* Equivalently, the natural evaluation map

$$
\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}) \otimes_{\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X)} \mathcal{O}_{X, x} \longrightarrow \mathcal{F}_x
$$

is surjective for every $x \in X$.

**Theorem B (Cartan-Serre 1953).** *Let $X$ be a Stein manifold and $\mathcal{F}$ a coherent analytic sheaf on $X$. Then $H^q(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for every $q \geq 1$.*

**Combined consequence.** The functor $\Gamma(X, -)$ from coherent analytic sheaves on $X$ to $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$-modules is exact: every short exact sequence of coherent sheaves $0 \to \mathcal{F}' \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}'' \to 0$ on $X$ gives a short exact sequence of global sections $0 \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}') \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}) \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}'') \to 0$. Coherent sheaves on a Stein manifold behave like modules over the ring $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$.

**Notation.** $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})$ denotes global sections; $\mathcal{F}_x$ the stalk at $x$; $H^q(X, \mathcal{F})$ the $q$-th sheaf cohomology, computed via Čech with respect to a Stein cover or via the Dolbeault fine resolution [06.04.05]; $\mathcal{O}_{X, x}$ the local ring of holomorphic germs at $x$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Theorem B fails on compact Riemann surfaces* of positive genus. For $X$ compact of genus $g \geq 1$, $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X) = \mathbb{C}^g \neq 0$. Theorem B's hypothesis is the Stein property, which non-compact non-singular complex manifolds may or may not have in dimension $\geq 2$ but have automatically in dimension one once non-compact.
- *Coherence is required.* The vanishing $H^q(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ does not hold for every sheaf — only coherent ones. The constant sheaf $\underline{\mathbb{Z}}_X$ on a torus minus a point has nonzero $H^1$, for instance, because it is not coherent as an $\mathcal{O}_X$-module.
- *Theorem A is generation, not local equality.* The map $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}) \to \mathcal{F}_x$ (evaluation at $x$) need not be surjective; the surjective map is the *evaluation tensored with the local ring*, $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}) \otimes \mathcal{O}_{X, x} \to \mathcal{F}_x$. Local coefficients are essential.
- *Theorem B does not say $H^0$ vanishes.* Global sections of $\mathcal{F}$ form $H^0(X, \mathcal{F})$, which is in general infinite-dimensional on a non-compact $X$. The vanishing kicks in only at $q \geq 1$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Cartan-Serre 1953, dimension-one case).** *Let $X$ be a non-compact connected Riemann surface and $\mathcal{F}$ a coherent analytic sheaf on $X$. Then $H^q(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for every $q \geq 1$ (Theorem B), and the natural evaluation map $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}) \otimes_{\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X)} \mathcal{O}_{X, x} \to \mathcal{F}_x$ is surjective for every $x \in X$ (Theorem A).*

**Proof.** The argument runs in five steps: a Stein exhaustion, the $\bar\partial$-vanishing on each piece, propagation to coherent sheaves by the structure-theorem reduction, a Mittag-Leffler limit passage to the union, and the deduction of Theorem A from Theorem B.

*Step 1 — Stein exhaustion.* Behnke-Stein [06.09.01] gives a Runge exhaustion $X_1 \Subset X_2 \Subset \cdots$ with $\bigcup_n X_n = X$ and each $X_n$ relatively compact and Runge in $X$. Each $X_n$ is itself Stein (open subset of a non-compact RS, or directly verified from the strictly subharmonic exhaustion restricted to $X_n$).

*Step 2 — vanishing for $\mathcal{O}_X$ on each piece.* On each relatively compact pseudoconvex (Stein) $X_n$, Hörmander's weighted $L^2$-existence theorem [06.04.05] solves $\bar\partial u = g$ for every smooth $\bar\partial$-closed $g$ on $X_n$. The Dolbeault comparison [06.04.05] identifies $H^1(X_n, \mathcal{O}_{X_n})$ with the cokernel of $\bar\partial : C^\infty(X_n) \to C^\infty_{(0,1)}(X_n)$, which Hörmander's theorem makes zero. For $q \geq 2$, real dimension two on a Riemann surface forces $H^q(X_n, \mathcal{O}_{X_n}) = 0$ on dimensional grounds. So $H^q(X_n, \mathcal{O}_{X_n}) = 0$ for every $q \geq 1$.

*Step 3 — extension to coherent $\mathcal{F}$ on $X_n$.* Locally on a relatively compact open $U \subset X_n$ the coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ admits a finite-rank presentation $\mathcal{O}_U^{n_1} \to \mathcal{O}_U^{n_0} \to \mathcal{F}|_U \to 0$. Iterating the kernel-of-presentation argument and using Hilbert's syzygy theorem in dimension one (the local rings $\mathcal{O}_{X_n, x}$ are regular of Krull dimension one, hence have global homological dimension one), one obtains a finite resolution $0 \to \mathcal{O}_{X_n}^{m} \to \mathcal{O}_{X_n}^{n} \to \mathcal{F} \to 0$ on each $X_n$ — a *length-one* resolution because the surface is one-dimensional. Apply the long exact sequence in cohomology to this short exact sequence of sheaves: each term involves only $H^q(X_n, \mathcal{O}_{X_n})$ for $q \in \{0, 1\}$, and $H^q$ vanishes for $q \geq 1$ by Step 2. The connecting maps then force $H^q(X_n, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $q \geq 1$ as well.

*Step 4 — limit passage.* The full surface $X = \bigcup_n X_n$ is the increasing union of the Stein opens. The Čech complex of $\mathcal{F}$ on $X$ relative to a refinement of the Stein-cover of each $X_n$ is the inverse limit (in cochain degree, direct limit in section degree) of the Čech complexes on each $X_n$. The Mittag-Leffler condition on the inverse system $\{H^0(X_n, \mathcal{F})\}$ — each restriction $H^0(X_{n+1}, \mathcal{F}) \to H^0(X_n, \mathcal{F})$ has dense image in the natural Fréchet topology, by the Runge property of $X_n$ in $X_{n+1}$ — is the input that allows the inverse limit to commute with cohomology. Concretely: a cocycle $\xi$ on $X$ restricts to cocycles $\xi_n$ on $X_n$, each of which is a coboundary by Step 3, and the Mittag-Leffler condition lets one assemble the local primitives $\eta_n$ into a global primitive $\eta$ on $X$ by uniformly approximating each correction term $\eta_{n+1} - \eta_n$ on $X_{n-1}$ (Runge) and summing the geometric corrections. The assembled $\eta$ is a global primitive of $\xi$, and $H^q(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $q \geq 1$.

*Step 5 — Theorem A from Theorem B.* Given $x \in X$ and a germ $\sigma \in \mathcal{F}_x$, choose a neighbourhood $U$ of $x$ on which $\sigma$ extends to a section $\tilde \sigma \in \Gamma(U, \mathcal{F})$. Consider the sheaf morphism $\mathcal{O}_X \to \mathcal{F}$ defined locally as $1 \mapsto \tilde \sigma$ on $U$ and zero outside, with kernel $\mathcal{K}$ a coherent subsheaf of $\mathcal{O}_X$ (locally cut out by the annihilator of $\tilde \sigma$). Wrap into the short exact sequence $0 \to \mathcal{K} \to \mathcal{O}_X \to \mathcal{O}_X / \mathcal{K} \to 0$, with $\mathcal{O}_X / \mathcal{K}$ the skyscraper or rank-one piece supported on the locus where $\tilde \sigma$ has a nonzero annihilator. The long exact sequence in cohomology gives

$$
\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X) \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X / \mathcal{K}) \to H^1(X, \mathcal{K}) \to \cdots,
$$

and Theorem B (applied to the coherent $\mathcal{K}$) makes $H^1(X, \mathcal{K}) = 0$, so the connecting map is zero and the global-section evaluation surjects onto $\mathcal{O}_X / \mathcal{K}$, hence onto a generator of $\mathcal{F}_x$ at $x$. The germ $\sigma$ thereby lies in the $\mathcal{O}_{X, x}$-submodule generated by global sections, and Theorem A holds. $\square$

The five-step structure follows Forster §29 (exhaustion + Hörmander on each piece + Mittag-Leffler limit + A-from-B deduction) and dovetails with Hörmander Ch. III (the weighted $L^2$-method as the analytic engine, with the same exhaustion and limit-passage logic). The dimension-one specialisation is shorter than the general Cartan-Serre argument: the structure theorem on the local rings $\mathcal{O}_{X, x}$ in dimension one gives length-one resolutions automatically, where the higher-dimensional case needs an induction on the resolution length.

**Bridge.** The proof binds two pieces of upstream machinery into one statement. From [06.09.01] comes the Stein property: a strictly subharmonic exhaustion exists on every non-compact RS, and the holomorphic-convex hull of every compact set is compact. From [06.04.05] comes the Hilbert-space framework for $\bar\partial$: $\bar\partial$ is a closed densely-defined operator on $L^2$-spaces, with a self-adjoint Hodge-Laplace and finite-dimensional kernels on compact pieces, and Hörmander's weighted estimate solves the equation $\bar\partial u = g$ with quantitative control on Stein domains. The theorem fuses these inputs: Stein gives the geometry on which the $\bar\partial$-PDE is solvable, the $L^2$-PDE produces the cohomological vanishing on each Stein piece, and the exhaustion glues the local vanishings into a global one. Theorem A is then a one-step exact-sequence consequence of Theorem B. Cartan and Serre's 1953 paper extracted this chain in arbitrary complex dimension, with the length-one syzygy of dimension one replaced by an induction on syzygy length using Hilbert's general theorem on regular local rings; the dimension-one case in Forster §29 is the cleanest expression because the syzygy collapses to one step. The downstream consequence is that every classical existence problem on a non-compact RS — Cousin I, Cousin II, Mittag-Leffler, Runge approximation, Picard-group triviality — collapses to a single corollary of $H^1(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$, with $\mathcal{F}$ the appropriate ideal or quotient sheaf encoding the problem's local data.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $X$ be a non-compact Riemann surface and $D = p_1 + \cdots + p_k$ a divisor of $k$ distinct points. Show that the sequence

$$
0 \to \mathcal{O}_X(-D) \to \mathcal{O}_X \to \mathcal{O}_D \to 0
$$

induces an isomorphism $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X) / \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X(-D)) \cong \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_D) = \mathbb{C}^k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the long exact sequence in cohomology and Theorem B to kill the $H^1$-correction term.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The short exact sequence is the standard "ideal-of-a-divisor" sequence; $\mathcal{O}_X(-D)$ is the sheaf of holomorphic functions vanishing along $D$, and $\mathcal{O}_D$ is the skyscraper sheaf $\bigoplus_i \mathbb{C}_{p_i}$ on the discrete set $\{p_1, \ldots, p_k\}$. The long exact sequence in cohomology reads

$$
0 \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X(-D)) \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X) \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_D) \to H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X(-D)) \to H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X) \to \cdots.
$$

By Theorem B applied to the coherent sheaves $\mathcal{O}_X(-D)$ and $\mathcal{O}_X$, both $H^1$-terms vanish. The connecting map's target is therefore zero, and the four-term exact sequence reduces to a short exact

$$
0 \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X(-D)) \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X) \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_D) \to 0,
$$

giving $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X) / \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X(-D)) \cong \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_D) = \mathbb{C}^k$. Rubric: full credit for the long exact sequence + double application of Theorem B + identification with $\mathbb{C}^k$.

</details>

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that the Cousin I problem on a non-compact Riemann surface $X$ — given an open cover $\{U_i\}$ and meromorphic functions $f_i \in \mathcal{M}(U_i)$ with $f_i - f_j \in \mathcal{O}(U_i \cap U_j)$, find a global meromorphic $f \in \mathcal{M}(X)$ with $f - f_i \in \mathcal{O}(U_i)$ — is always solvable.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The obstruction is the cohomology class $[\{f_i - f_j\}] \in H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The differences $g_{ij} = f_i - f_j$ are holomorphic on $U_i \cap U_j$ by hypothesis, satisfy the cocycle condition $g_{ij} + g_{jk} + g_{ki} = 0$ on triple overlaps automatically, and define a Čech 1-cocycle on the cover $\{U_i\}$ with values in $\mathcal{O}_X$. The class $[g_{ij}] \in H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$ is the Cousin I obstruction: it is the coboundary of a global $f$ exactly when $g_{ij} = h_i - h_j$ for some $h_i \in \mathcal{O}(U_i)$, in which case $f$ defined by $f = f_i - h_i$ on $U_i$ is well-defined globally and meromorphic, with $f - f_i \in \mathcal{O}(U_i)$. By Theorem B applied to the coherent sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X$ on the Stein surface $X$, $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X) = 0$, so the cocycle is a coboundary and the Cousin I problem is solvable. Rubric: full credit for the cocycle + Theorem B + assembly of global $f$.

</details>

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that the Cousin II problem on a non-compact connected Riemann surface $X$ is always solvable: given a cover $\{U_i\}$ and non-zero meromorphic $f_i \in \mathcal{M}^*(U_i)$ with $f_i / f_j \in \mathcal{O}^*(U_i \cap U_j)$, there exists a global $f \in \mathcal{M}^*(X)$ with $f / f_i \in \mathcal{O}^*(U_i)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Cousin II obstruction lives in $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^*)$, which the exponential exact sequence relates to $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$ and $H^2(X, \mathbb{Z})$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The ratios $g_{ij} = f_i / f_j$ are holomorphic and non-vanishing on the overlaps, satisfy the multiplicative cocycle condition $g_{ij} \cdot g_{jk} = g_{ik}$, and define a class in $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^*) = \mathrm{Pic}(X)$. The exponential sequence $0 \to \mathbb{Z} \to \mathcal{O}_X \to \mathcal{O}_X^* \to 0$ gives the long exact sequence

$$
H^1(X, \mathbb{Z}) \to H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X) \to H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^*) \to H^2(X, \mathbb{Z}).
$$

By Theorem B (Cartan-Serre on the Stein surface $X$), $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X) = 0$. By the topology of a non-compact connected real-2-surface, $H^2(X, \mathbb{Z}) = 0$. Squeezed between two zeros, $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^*) = 0$, and the multiplicative cocycle is a coboundary $g_{ij} = h_i / h_j$ with $h_i \in \mathcal{O}^*(U_i)$. The function $f$ defined by $f = f_i / h_i$ on $U_i$ glues to a global non-zero meromorphic function on $X$ with $f / f_i = 1 / h_i \in \mathcal{O}^*(U_i)$. Rubric: full credit for the exponential sequence + Theorem B + topological vanishing + assembly.

**Exercise 6 (medium, short-answer).**

Let $X$ be a non-compact Riemann surface, $K \subset X$ compact, and $f \in \mathcal{O}(U)$ holomorphic on a neighbourhood $U$ of $K$. Show, using Theorem B for $\mathcal{O}_X$ and a cutoff $\bar\partial$ argument, that for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $F \in \mathcal{O}(X)$ with $\sup_K |F - f| < \varepsilon$ (Runge approximation, with the standard topological hypothesis on $X \setminus K$).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Multiply $f$ by a smooth cutoff supported in $U$, set up a global $\bar\partial$-equation on $X$, and use Theorem B to solve.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Choose a smooth cutoff $\chi$ with $\chi \equiv 1$ on a neighbourhood of $K$ and $\mathrm{supp}\, \chi \Subset U$. The function $\chi f$ extends by zero to a smooth function on $X$, and $\bar\partial(\chi f) = (\bar\partial \chi) f$ is supported in the compact annulus $U \setminus K$. The class $[\bar\partial(\chi f)] \in H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$ vanishes by Theorem B (alternatively, the Dolbeault comparison [06.04.05] identifies this $H^1$ with the cokernel of $\bar\partial$, and the cokernel is zero on the Stein $X$). So there exists $u \in C^\infty(X)$ with $\bar\partial u = \bar\partial(\chi f)$. The function $F = \chi f - u$ is then holomorphic on $X$ (since $\bar\partial F = 0$ globally) and equals $f - u$ on a neighbourhood of $K$. Hörmander's weighted estimate [06.04.05] makes $\|u\|_{L^2(\phi)}$ controllable by $\|\bar\partial \chi \cdot f\|_{L^2(\phi)}$, which is small in sup norm on $K$ (with the Sobolev embedding from $L^2$ to sup, given the topological hypothesis on $X \setminus K$). Iterating with shrinking $\varepsilon$ produces uniform approximation of $f$ on $K$ by global holomorphic $F$. Rubric: full credit for the cutoff + $\bar\partial$-solve + Theorem B + sup-norm control.

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Let $X$ be a Stein Riemann surface and $\mathcal{F}$ a coherent analytic sheaf on $X$. Show that for every finite set $\{x_1, \ldots, x_k\} \subset X$ and every choice of germs $\sigma_i \in \mathcal{F}_{x_i}$, there exists a single global section $s \in \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})$ whose germ at each $x_i$ equals $\sigma_i$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply Theorem A at each point separately, then combine the local-coefficient combinations using Cousin / Mittag-Leffler interpolation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Theorem A at each $x_i$, the stalk $\mathcal{F}_{x_i}$ is generated by global sections: for each $i$ there exist global sections $s_i^{(1)}, \ldots, s_i^{(n_i)} \in \Gamma(X, \mathcal{F})$ and germs $a_i^{(j)} \in \mathcal{O}_{X, x_i}$ such that $\sigma_i = \sum_j a_i^{(j)} \cdot (s_i^{(j)})_{x_i}$. The local-coefficient germs $a_i^{(j)} \in \mathcal{O}_{X, x_i}$ together specify a Mittag-Leffler datum on the discrete divisor $D = \sum_i x_i$ for the finitely many sheaves $\mathcal{O}_X$. ## Advanced results [Master]

The Cartan-Serre framework was published in two stages: the 1951–53 Cartan séminaire at the École Normale Supérieure, where Henri Cartan and Jean-Pierre Serre developed Theorems A and B for Stein manifolds in arbitrary complex dimension, and the 1953 *Comptes Rendus* note *Un théorème de finitude concernant les variétés analytiques compactes* (CRAS 237) [ref: TODO_REF Cartan-Serre 1953], which gave the published companion. The séminaire treatment proves the theorems for any Stein manifold $M$ of complex dimension $n$, with the analytic engine being Cartan-Thullen's exhaustion-by-pseudoconvex-domains and a Mittag-Leffler / Schwartz finiteness limit-passage argument on the Fréchet space of global sections. The dimension-one specialisation runs faster: the local rings $\mathcal{O}_{X, x}$ on a Riemann surface are regular of Krull dimension one, hence have global homological dimension one, so every coherent sheaf admits a length-one resolution by free $\mathcal{O}_X^k$, and the long exact sequence in cohomology immediately reduces to the structure-sheaf case.

**Hörmander's $L^2$-PDE proof (1965).** The modern analytic engine for Theorem B is Hörmander's 1965 *$L^2$-estimates and existence theorems for the $\bar\partial$-operator* [ref: TODO_REF Hörmander 1965] (*Acta Math.* 113). On a Stein manifold $M$ with strictly plurisubharmonic exhaustion $\phi$, the weighted estimate

$$
\|u\|_{L^2(e^{-\phi})}^2 \leq C \|g\|_{L^2(e^{-\phi})}^2
$$

for the canonical solution $u$ of $\bar\partial u = g$ on $\bar\partial$-closed $g$ is the input that produces the cohomological vanishing in arbitrary complex dimension. The Bochner-Kodaira-Nakano identity bounds the weighted Dolbeault Laplacian below by a positive constant times the curvature of the chosen Hermitian weight; the Riesz representation theorem on the resulting weighted Hilbert space gives a unique $L^2$-bounded solution. In dimension one, the curvature reduces to the Levi form $\partial \bar\partial \phi$ (positive by hypothesis), and the estimate specialises to Hörmander Ch. III's elementary version. The Hörmander route makes the proof fully analytical and avoids the Schwartz-finiteness limit-passage of the Cartan séminaire.

**Forster's elementary proof in dimension one.** Forster *Lectures on Riemann Surfaces* §29 [ref: TODO_REF Forster] gives an alternative route in dimension one that avoids the $L^2$-method. Forster's setup uses the Runge exhaustion of [06.09.01] and the elementary $\bar\partial$-solvability on each $X_n$ (proved by direct integral kernels: the Cauchy-Pompeiu formula on each disc, glued by partitions of unity). The Mittag-Leffler / Schwartz finiteness limit-passage on the Fréchet space of holomorphic functions is the input that promotes vanishing on each $X_n$ to vanishing on $X = \bigcup_n X_n$. Forster's route is cleaner pedagogically; Hörmander's $L^2$-route generalises to higher dimension and to twisted sheaves with curvature constraints (Kodaira vanishing, Nakano vanishing, Demailly's analytic continuation theorems). On a Riemann surface the two routes prove the same theorem.

**Cousin problems and the corollary list.** Theorem B for the structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X$ on a non-compact Riemann surface is the cohomological reformulation of the classical Cousin I problem (additive principal parts), originally posed by Pierre Cousin in 1895. Theorem B for $\mathcal{O}_X^*$ (via the exponential sequence and the topology of a non-compact connected surface) is the reformulation of Cousin II (multiplicative divisors). The Mittag-Leffler theorem of 1884, proved on the plane by Magnus Mittag-Leffler, is the statement that prescribed-principal-parts data on $\mathbb{C}$ is realised by a global meromorphic function — and is the special case $X = \mathbb{C}$ of the Cousin I corollary. The classical statement of Runge's theorem (1885), that holomorphic functions on a compact set with simply connected complement are uniformly approximable by polynomials, generalises to Stein Riemann surfaces as: holomorphic functions on a relatively compact pseudoconvex $K \subset X$ are uniformly approximable on $K$ by global $\mathcal{O}(X)$ functions, with the standard topological hypothesis "$X \setminus K$ has no relatively compact components" providing the obstruction-free regime.

**Higher-dimensional Theorem A / B and GAGA.** The Cartan-Serre theorems on Stein manifolds of arbitrary complex dimension are the analytic analogue of Serre's *Faisceaux algébriques cohérents* (1955) [ref: TODO_REF Serre 1955] vanishing on affine schemes: for an affine scheme $X = \mathrm{Spec}\, A$ and a quasi-coherent $\mathcal{F}$, $H^q(X_{\mathrm{Zar}}, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $q \geq 1$. The two vanishings — analytic on Stein, algebraic on affine — are mirror statements and the foundation of the dictionary between analytic and algebraic geometry. Serre's GAGA (1956) *Géométrie algébrique et géométrie analytique* gives the comparison theorem on the projective side: on a smooth projective variety, coherent algebraic sheaves and coherent analytic sheaves have the same cohomology, and the global-section functors agree. The Stein-versus-affine pairing on the open side is more delicate (Stein implies affine for embedded varieties; the converse fails for Serre's 1953 example of a Stein manifold not algebraisable as an affine variety).

**The Oka principle.** A homotopy-theoretic refinement of Theorem A, due to Kiyoshi Oka 1939 and Hans Grauert 1957, says that on a Stein manifold $X$ every continuous section of a holomorphic fibre bundle is homotopic to a holomorphic section, and the holomorphic sections form a deformation retract of the continuous ones. For line bundles on a Stein RS, the Oka principle reduces to the topological classification ($\mathrm{Pic}(X) = H^2(X, \mathbb{Z})$ topologically, which is zero for a non-compact connected RS), recovering the Picard-group triviality. The Oka principle is the "soft" / homotopy-theoretic shadow of Theorem A and remains an active topic in modern complex analysis (Forstnerič 2011 *Stein Manifolds and Holomorphic Mappings* gives the contemporary monograph treatment).

**Stein theory and finite-presentation.** On a non-compact RS the Stein property has a third equivalent characterisation: the Fréchet algebra $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$ separates points and the structure morphism $X \to \mathrm{Specm}\, \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$ to the maximal-ideal spectrum (with the Gelfand topology) is a homeomorphism. The Cartan-Serre theorems convert this Fréchet-algebra picture into a category-equivalence statement: coherent analytic sheaves on the Stein $X$ correspond to finitely-presented modules over $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$, with global sections as the equivalence functor. The exactness of $\Gamma(X, -)$ promoted by Theorem B is exactly the property that distinguishes a "tame" geometric category (Stein, affine) from a "wild" one (compact Kähler, projective).

**Synthesis.** Cartan's Theorems A and B are the cohomological pay-off of the Stein hypothesis: the structure of a complex manifold on $X$ guaranteeing enough global holomorphic functions for $\bar\partial$-PDE solvability, exhaustion by relatively compact pseudoconvex pieces, and a strictly plurisubharmonic Morse function. Once that geometric hypothesis is in place, Theorem B converts every coherent-sheaf cohomological obstruction in positive degree to zero, and Theorem A converts every local-section datum to a global one. On a non-compact Riemann surface — where Behnke-Stein makes the Stein hypothesis automatic — the two theorems consolidate the classical Mittag-Leffler, Cousin I, Cousin II, Runge, and Picard-group questions into a single corollary $H^q(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$. The dimension-one specialisation runs in five steps, with the length-one syzygy collapsing the Cartan-Serre induction and the Hörmander / Forster $\bar\partial$-solvability furnishing the analytic engine. In higher dimension the proof retains the same skeleton — Stein exhaustion, $L^2$-solvability on each piece, limit passage — with a longer syzygy induction and the Bochner-Kodaira-Nakano curvature estimate doing the heavy lifting. The bridge to algebraic geometry runs through Serre's affine-scheme vanishing and GAGA on projective varieties; the bridge to symplectic topology runs through the Cieliebak-Eliashberg Stein-Weinstein duality (recorded in [06.09.01]). On a Riemann surface, the entire analytic spine of complex curve theory is captured by one identity: $H^1(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for every coherent $\mathcal{F}$, with everything else flowing as a corollary.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Lemma (Oka coherence).** *The structure sheaf $\mathcal{O}_X$ of a complex manifold $X$ is coherent over itself.*

*Proof.* Oka 1950 proves coherence of $\mathcal{O}_X$ over itself by an explicit local-presentation argument: on a polydisc, the kernel of any surjection $\mathcal{O}^k \to \mathcal{O}$ is finitely generated by an explicit Weierstrass-preparation calculation. The Riemann-surface case is an immediate specialisation (each local ring $\mathcal{O}_{X, x}$ is a discrete valuation ring, hence Noetherian and of homological dimension one). $\square$

**Lemma ($\bar\partial$-solvability on Stein).** *Let $X$ be a Stein Riemann surface and $\phi : X \to \mathbb{R}$ a strictly subharmonic exhaustion. For every $g \in C^\infty_{(0, 1)}(X)$ that is $\bar\partial$-closed (which is automatic on a 1-dimensional manifold) and has $\|g\|_{L^2(e^{-\phi})} < \infty$, there exists $u \in C^\infty(X)$ with $\bar\partial u = g$ and $\|u\|_{L^2(e^{-\phi})} \leq \|g\|_{L^2(e^{-\phi})}$.*

*Proof.* Hörmander's $L^2$-method [06.04.05] in dimension one. Equip $X$ with a Hermitian metric whose Kähler form is $\omega = i \partial \bar\partial \phi$ (positive on $X$ by strict subharmonicity). Form the weighted $L^2$-space $L^2_{(0, q)}(X, e^{-\phi})$. The unbounded operator $\bar\partial : L^2_{(0, 0)} \to L^2_{(0, 1)}$ is closed and densely defined, with Hilbert adjoint $\bar\partial^*$. The Bochner-Kodaira-Nakano identity in dimension one reads $\langle \bar\partial^* g, \bar\partial^* g \rangle \geq \|g\|^2$ for $g \in \mathrm{dom}(\bar\partial^*) \cap \ker \bar\partial$, the lower-bound estimate that promotes $\bar\partial$ to a bijection from a closed subspace of $L^2_{(0, 0)}$ onto $\ker \bar\partial \subset L^2_{(0, 1)}$. The Riesz representation theorem on the linear functional $L_g : v \mapsto \langle v, g \rangle$ on $\mathrm{dom}(\bar\partial^*)$ produces $u \in L^2$ with $\bar\partial u = g$ and $\|u\| \leq \|g\|$. Elliptic regularity on the Cauchy-Riemann operator (an elliptic first-order operator on a Riemann surface) bootstraps the $L^2$-solution to a $C^\infty$-solution via Sobolev embedding. The Forster §28 alternative (Cauchy-Pompeiu kernels glued by Runge approximation) is an elementary route to the same statement. $\square$

**Lemma (Vanishing of $H^q(X_n, \mathcal{O}_{X_n})$).** *For each relatively compact Stein open $X_n \Subset X$ in the exhaustion, $H^q(X_n, \mathcal{O}_{X_n}) = 0$ for every $q \geq 1$.*

*Proof.* The Dolbeault comparison [06.04.05] identifies $H^1(X_n, \mathcal{O}_{X_n})$ with the cokernel $C^\infty_{(0, 1)}(X_n) / \bar\partial C^\infty(X_n)$. The previous lemma makes $\bar\partial$ surjective on $X_n$, so the cokernel is zero. For $q \geq 2$, the Riemann surface has real dimension two, and the Dolbeault complex is concentrated in cohomological degrees $0$ and $1$, so $H^q = 0$ on dimensional grounds. $\square$

**Lemma (Vanishing of $H^q(X_n, \mathcal{F})$ for coherent $\mathcal{F}$).** *For every coherent analytic sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ on a relatively compact Stein open $X_n$, $H^q(X_n, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for every $q \geq 1$.*

*Proof.* Locally on $X_n$, $\mathcal{F}$ admits a finite presentation $\mathcal{O}_{X_n}^a \to \mathcal{O}_{X_n}^b \to \mathcal{F} \to 0$. By the Hilbert-syzygy theorem in dimension one (the local rings are regular of Krull dimension one, hence have global homological dimension one), the kernel of the right-hand map is itself locally free of finite rank, giving a length-one resolution

$$
0 \to \mathcal{O}_{X_n}^a \to \mathcal{O}_{X_n}^b \to \mathcal{F} \to 0.
$$

The long exact sequence in cohomology and the previous lemma (vanishing of $H^q(X_n, \mathcal{O}_{X_n})$ for $q \geq 1$) force $H^q(X_n, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for every $q \geq 1$ — the connecting maps have zero source and zero target. $\square$

**Lemma (Mittag-Leffler condition on the Stein exhaustion).** *The inverse system $\{H^0(X_n, \mathcal{F})\}$ of global sections of a coherent analytic sheaf $\mathcal{F}$ along a Stein exhaustion $X_1 \Subset X_2 \Subset \cdots$ satisfies the Mittag-Leffler condition: each restriction $H^0(X_{n+1}, \mathcal{F}) \to H^0(X_n, \mathcal{F})$ has dense image in the natural Fréchet topology.*

*Proof.* The Runge property of $X_n$ in $X_{n+1}$ (Behnke-Stein lemma in [06.09.01]) gives uniform approximability of every $f \in \Gamma(X_n, \mathcal{F})$ on every compact $K \subset X_n$ by elements of $\Gamma(X_{n+1}, \mathcal{F})$. The natural Fréchet topology on $\Gamma(X_n, \mathcal{F})$ is the projective limit over compact $K \subset X_n$ of sup-norm topologies on $\Gamma(K, \mathcal{F})$, and density on each $K$ is precisely Runge approximation. The Mittag-Leffler condition is the abstract formulation of "every term in the inverse system can be uniformly approximated by terms in the next term" — and is what makes the inverse limit commute with cohomology in a Fréchet category. $\square$

**Theorem (Cartan's Theorem B for Stein RS, full statement).** *Let $X$ be a non-compact connected Riemann surface and $\mathcal{F}$ a coherent analytic sheaf on $X$. Then $H^q(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for every $q \geq 1$.*

*Proof.* Combine the four lemmas. The Stein exhaustion $X_n \Subset X$ exists by Behnke-Stein [06.09.01]. On each $X_n$, $H^q(X_n, \mathcal{F}|_{X_n}) = 0$ for $q \geq 1$ by Lemma 4. The Mittag-Leffler condition on $\{H^0(X_n, \mathcal{F})\}$ from Lemma 5 makes the inverse limit commute with cohomology: $H^q(X, \mathcal{F}) = \varprojlim_n H^q(X_n, \mathcal{F}|_{X_n}) = 0$ for $q \geq 1$. Concretely, given a global Čech 1-cocycle $\xi$ on $X$, restrict to a 1-cocycle $\xi_n$ on each $X_n$; by Lemma 4 each $\xi_n$ is a coboundary $\xi_n = d \eta_n$; the Mittag-Leffler condition lets one modify each $\eta_n$ by a constant cocycle (uniformly approximable on $X_{n-1}$) to make the sequence $\{\eta_n\}$ converge in the Fréchet topology to a global $\eta$ with $d \eta = \xi$ on every $X_n$, hence on $X$. The cocycle $\xi$ is a coboundary, $H^1(X, \mathcal{F}) = 0$. Higher $H^q$ vanish on dimensional grounds. $\square$

**Corollary (Cartan's Theorem A for Stein RS).** *Let $X$ be a non-compact connected Riemann surface and $\mathcal{F}$ a coherent analytic sheaf on $X$. For every $x \in X$, the natural evaluation map $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}) \otimes_{\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X)} \mathcal{O}_{X, x} \to \mathcal{F}_x$ is surjective.*

*Proof.* Given a germ $\sigma \in \mathcal{F}_x$, the local section extension to a neighbourhood $U$ of $x$ is $\tilde \sigma \in \Gamma(U, \mathcal{F})$. The kernel sheaf $\mathcal{K}$ of the morphism $\mathcal{O}_X \to \mathcal{F}$ defined by $1 \mapsto \tilde \sigma$ on $U$ and zero outside is coherent (kernels of sheaf maps between coherent sheaves are coherent). The short exact sequence $0 \to \mathcal{K} \to \mathcal{O}_X \to \mathcal{O}_X / \mathcal{K} \to 0$ produces

$$
\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X) \to \Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X / \mathcal{K}) \to H^1(X, \mathcal{K}) \to \cdots.
$$

By Theorem B applied to the coherent $\mathcal{K}$, $H^1(X, \mathcal{K}) = 0$, the connecting map is zero, and the global-section map is surjective onto $\mathcal{O}_X / \mathcal{K}$. Pulling back along the natural map $\mathcal{O}_X / \mathcal{K} \to \mathcal{F}$ at $x$, the germ $\sigma$ lies in the image of the evaluation map $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}) \otimes \mathcal{O}_{X, x} \to \mathcal{F}_x$, as required. $\square$

**Corollary (Exactness of $\Gamma(X, -)$).** *The functor $\Gamma(X, -)$ from coherent analytic sheaves on a Stein Riemann surface $X$ to $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$-modules is exact.*

*Proof.* Left-exactness holds for any sheaf cohomology computation. Right-exactness on a short exact sequence $0 \to \mathcal{F}' \to \mathcal{F} \to \mathcal{F}'' \to 0$ of coherent sheaves follows from Theorem B applied to $\mathcal{F}'$: the connecting map $\Gamma(X, \mathcal{F}'') \to H^1(X, \mathcal{F}')$ in the long exact sequence has zero target. $\square$

**Corollary (Cousin I, Cousin II, Mittag-Leffler).** *On a non-compact connected Riemann surface $X$, the Cousin I problem (additive principal parts) and the Cousin II problem (multiplicative divisors) are always solvable; in particular the classical Mittag-Leffler theorem holds in the form: for any prescribed principal-parts datum at a discrete set of points of $X$, there exists a global meromorphic function realising it.*

*Proof.* Cousin I obstruction lies in $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X) = 0$ by Theorem B applied to the coherent $\mathcal{O}_X$. Cousin II obstruction lies in $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^*)$, and the exponential sequence $0 \to \mathbb{Z} \to \mathcal{O}_X \to \mathcal{O}_X^* \to 0$ together with $H^2(X, \mathbb{Z}) = 0$ (non-compact connected real-2-surface) and Theorem B for $\mathcal{O}_X$ squeezes $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^*) = 0$ between two zeros. Mittag-Leffler is the special case Cousin I + the principal-parts sheaf $\mathcal{M}_X / \mathcal{O}_X$, identified with a coherent skyscraper supported on a discrete divisor. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Stein Riemann surfaces [06.09.01].** The geometric input to Theorems A and B: the existence of a strictly subharmonic exhaustion, holomorphic-convex hulls, and holomorphic separability. Behnke-Stein 1949 makes the hypothesis automatic on every non-compact RS; the unit [06.09.01] establishes this and equips the theorem environment of [06.09.02] with the analytic geometry needed to run the Cartan-Serre proof.

- **Hilbert-space PDE for $\bar\partial$ [06.04.05].** The analytic engine of Theorem B in the Hörmander route: on a Stein manifold with strictly plurisubharmonic exhaustion, the weighted $L^2$-existence theorem solves $\bar\partial u = g$ for every $\bar\partial$-closed $g$, with quantitative norm control. The Dolbeault comparison from [06.04.05] then identifies the cokernel of $\bar\partial$ with $H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X)$, and the $L^2$-existence makes that cokernel zero. The same machinery on each piece $X_n$ of a Stein exhaustion produces the local vanishings used in Step 2 of the proof above.

- **Čech cohomology of holomorphic line bundles [06.04.02].** The cohomological language in which Theorem B is stated: $H^q(X, \mathcal{F})$ is computed via Čech with respect to a Stein cover, or equivalently via the Dolbeault fine resolution. The Mittag-Leffler / Cousin / Picard corollaries of Theorems A and B are reformulations of the cohomological vanishing of specific Čech cohomology groups attached to the structure sheaf and the multiplicative-units sheaf. The non-compact case here is the negation of the compact-case generic non-vanishing recorded in [06.04.02] (where $H^1$ on a compact RS of genus $g$ has dimension $g$).

- **Holomorphic line bundle on a Riemann surface [06.05.02].** The Picard group $\mathrm{Pic}(X) = H^1(X, \mathcal{O}_X^*)$ vanishes on a non-compact RS by Theorem B applied through the exponential sequence, recovering the holomorphic-triviality of every line bundle. The line-bundle classification on a Riemann surface is therefore concentrated in the compact case ([06.04.01], [06.04.04]); the non-compact Stein case trivialises the entire structure.

- **Riemann-Roch theorem for compact Riemann surfaces [06.04.01].** The compact-case Riemann-Roch identity $\dim H^0(L) - \dim H^1(L) = \deg L + 1 - g$ has the cohomological correction $H^1$ explicitly because the Stein property fails. The non-compact Stein case kills the right-hand cohomology entirely. The dichotomy compact-vs-Stein on a Riemann surface is the dichotomy substantive-Riemann-Roch-correction-vs-no-correction.

- **Serre duality on a curve [06.04.04].** Serre duality $H^1(X, L) \cong H^0(X, K_X \otimes L^{-1})^*$ on a compact RS pairs the $H^1$ that survives compact regimes with a dual $H^0$. On a non-compact Stein RS, Theorem B kills the left-hand side and the "duality" degenerates: both sides are zero or infinite-dimensional with no canonical pairing. Theorems A and B replace the duality pairing with the simpler statement that the cohomological obstruction vanishes outright.

- **Meromorphic function [06.01.05].** The Mittag-Leffler theorem (1884 plane case, Behnke-Stein 1949 RS case) is the corollary of Theorem B saying every prescribed principal-parts datum on a discrete divisor of a non-compact RS is realised by a global meromorphic function. The Cousin I framework records the same statement in cohomological language.

- **Riemann mapping theorem [06.01.06].** The Riemann mapping theorem identifies every simply connected proper open subset of $\mathbb{C}$ as biholomorphic to the unit disc. Combined with Theorem B, every such region admits a strictly subharmonic exhaustion, $H^1(\mathbb{D}, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for coherent $\mathcal{F}$, and the structure sheaf's global-section algebra is the complete metric space $\mathcal{O}(\mathbb{D})$. The Stein-rigidity of simply connected non-compact Riemann surfaces feeds directly into Theorem B's universal vanishing.

- **Hartogs phenomenon [06.07.02].** Hartogs' theorem on automatic extension of holomorphic functions across a punctured polydisc in $\mathbb{C}^n$ (for $n \geq 2$) is the obstruction to non-Stein-ness in higher dimension: the punctured polydisc fails Theorem B precisely because Hartogs-extension forces too many global sections, breaking the holomorphic-convex-hull condition. On a Riemann surface this phenomenon does not occur, and the higher-dimensional analytic-extension question is replaced by the universal vanishing of Theorem B.

- **Holomorphic functions of several complex variables [06.07.01].** The higher-dimensional Cartan-Serre statement of Theorems A and B, valid on every Stein manifold of dimension $n$, is the analytic engine of every multi-variable function-theoretic existence problem. The dimension-one specialisation collapses to the curve case treated here; the higher-dimensional case is the substantive content of the Cartan séminaire and Hörmander's monograph.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Henri Cartan and Jean-Pierre Serre proved Theorems A and B in the 1951–53 Cartan séminaire at the École Normale Supérieure [ref: TODO_REF Cartan 1951–53], with the published companion appearing in 1953 in the *Comptes Rendus de l'Académie des Sciences* as Cartan-Serre 1953 *Un théorème de finitude concernant les variétés analytiques compactes* [ref: TODO_REF Cartan-Serre 1953] (CRAS 237, 128–130). The séminaire treatment defined a Stein manifold of arbitrary complex dimension as a holomorphically convex and holomorphically separable complex manifold (extending Stein 1951's foundational definition), and proved that on such a manifold every coherent analytic sheaf has all positive-degree cohomology vanishing (Theorem B) and is generated by global sections at every point (Theorem A). The proof in the séminaire used Cartan-Thullen's exhaustion-by-pseudoconvex-domains and a Schwartz-finiteness limit-passage on the Fréchet space of global sections; the dimension-one specialisation, recorded in Forster *Lectures on Riemann Surfaces* §29 [ref: TODO_REF Forster], runs faster because the local rings on a Riemann surface are regular of Krull dimension one.

Lars Hörmander's 1965 *$L^2$-estimates and existence theorems for the $\bar\partial$-operator* [ref: TODO_REF Hörmander 1965] (*Acta Math.* 113, 89–152) supplied the modern analytic engine for Theorem B. The weighted $L^2$-method on a strictly plurisubharmonic exhaustion produces the cohomological vanishing as a direct corollary of the Bochner-Kodaira-Nakano curvature estimate and the Riesz representation theorem, replacing the séminaire's Schwartz-finiteness argument with a self-contained PDE proof. Hörmander's monograph *An Introduction to Complex Analysis in Several Variables* [ref: TODO_REF Hörmander HSCV] (North-Holland 1973, Ch. III) gives the textbook treatment.

Hans Grauert and Reinhold Remmert's *Theory of Stein Spaces* [ref: TODO_REF Grauert-Remmert] (Springer Grundlehren 236, 1979) is the canonical reference for Stein theory in the analytic-spaces setting (extending the manifold framework to allow analytic singularities). Theorems A and B in the Grauert-Remmert framework apply to coherent sheaves on Stein analytic spaces, where the local rings need no longer be regular but are Noetherian by Oka coherence. The Riemann-surface case here is the smooth specialisation in dimension one.

Pierre Cousin's 1895 paper *Sur les fonctions de $n$ variables complexes* (*Acta Math.* 19, 1–61) posed the Cousin I and Cousin II problems on bidiscs in $\mathbb{C}^n$, providing the classical motivation for the cohomological framework that Cartan-Serre and Stein later formalised. Mittag-Leffler's 1884 theorem on $\mathbb{C}$ (*Acta Math.* 4, 1–79) is the planar special case of Cousin I, which Theorems A and B generalise to every non-compact Riemann surface and to every Stein manifold. Behnke and Stein's 1949 *Entwicklung analytischer Funktionen auf Riemannschen Flächen* [ref: TODO_REF Behnke-Stein 1949] (*Math. Ann.* 120, 430–461) closed the dimension-one half of the problem by establishing that every non-compact Riemann surface is Stein, which made the Cartan-Serre theorems unconditional in that setting.

Jean-Pierre Serre's *Faisceaux algébriques cohérents* [ref: TODO_REF Serre 1955] (*Ann. of Math.* 61, 1955, 197–278) developed the algebraic-side counterpart on schemes: for an affine scheme $X = \mathrm{Spec}\, A$ and a quasi-coherent sheaf $\mathcal{F}$, $H^q(X_{\mathrm{Zar}}, \mathcal{F}) = 0$ for $q \geq 1$. The pairing of Cartan-Serre 1953 (analytic, on Stein manifolds) with Serre 1955 (algebraic, on affine schemes) established the coherent-cohomology vanishing as a uniform principle across the analytic and algebraic categories, and Serre's GAGA in 1956 (*Géométrie algébrique et géométrie analytique*) made the comparison theorem precise on the projective side.
