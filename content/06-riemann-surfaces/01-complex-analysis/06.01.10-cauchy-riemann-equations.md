## Intuition [Beginner]

A complex-valued function $f(z)$ of a complex input has both a real part $u$ and an imaginary part $v$. Write $z = x + i y$, so $f(z) = u(x, y) + i v(x, y)$. The function is called **holomorphic** when it has a complex derivative — a single complex number $f'(z_0)$ that describes how $f$ stretches and rotates the plane near $z_0$. The **Cauchy-Riemann equations** are the pair of conditions on $u$ and $v$ that make this possible:

$$
u_x = v_y, \qquad u_y = -v_x.
$$

Picture a holomorphic function as a local rotate-and-zoom of the plane. The real part $u$ moves left-to-right and the imaginary part $v$ moves up-and-down; for the combined motion to be a single complex multiplication, the way $u$ varies in $x$ must mirror the way $v$ varies in $y$, and the way $u$ varies in $y$ must be the negative of the way $v$ varies in $x$. The two equations encode that mirror relationship.

The coupling has a striking consequence. Knowing one of the two real parts up to a constant determines the other. Given a harmonic function $u$ (a real function with a flat average — see the worked example), there is essentially one $v$ that fits together with it to make a holomorphic $f = u + i v$. The $v$ is called the **harmonic conjugate** of $u$.

## Visual [Beginner]

A small square in the $z$-plane is mapped by a holomorphic $f$ to a small square in the $f(z)$-plane, rotated and scaled but not skewed. A small square mapped by a non-holomorphic function deforms into a parallelogram with angles bent.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take $f(z) = z^2$. Write $z = x + iy$. Then

$$
f(z) = (x + iy)^2 = x^2 + 2ixy + i^2 y^2 = (x^2 - y^2) + i(2xy).
$$

So the real and imaginary parts are $u(x, y) = x^2 - y^2$ and $v(x, y) = 2xy$. Compute the four partial derivatives:

$$
u_x = 2x, \qquad u_y = -2y, \qquad v_x = 2y, \qquad v_y = 2x.
$$

Now check the two Cauchy-Riemann equations. The first asks $u_x = v_y$, and $2x = 2x$ — confirmed. The second asks $u_y = -v_x$, and $-2y = -(2y)$ — confirmed. The function $z^2$ is holomorphic on the entire plane.

For contrast, take $g(z) = \bar z$, the complex conjugate. Writing $\bar z = x - iy$, the real and imaginary parts are $u = x$ and $v = -y$. The partial derivatives are $u_x = 1$, $u_y = 0$, $v_x = 0$, $v_y = -1$. The first Cauchy-Riemann equation asks $u_x = v_y$, but $1 \neq -1$. The check fails, so $\bar z$ is not holomorphic.

What this tells us: the Cauchy-Riemann equations are a quick test for holomorphicity that needs only the four partial derivatives. Polynomials in $z$ pass; the conjugation map $\bar z$ fails the test in the simplest possible way.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ be open, and let $f : \Omega \to \mathbb{C}$ be a function. Identify $\mathbb{C}$ with $\mathbb{R}^2$ via $z = x + iy \leftrightarrow (x, y)$ and write $f(x + iy) = u(x, y) + i v(x, y)$ with $u, v : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$.

The **complex derivative** of $f$ at $z_0 \in \Omega$ is the limit

$$
f'(z_0) := \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(z_0 + h) - f(z_0)}{h}, \qquad h \in \mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\},
$$

provided the limit exists independently of the direction along which $h \to 0$. When it does, $f$ is called **complex-differentiable at $z_0$**; if this holds at every point of $\Omega$, $f$ is **holomorphic on $\Omega$**.

The **Cauchy-Riemann equations** at $(x_0, y_0)$ are the pair of identities

$$
u_x(x_0, y_0) = v_y(x_0, y_0), \qquad u_y(x_0, y_0) = -v_x(x_0, y_0),
$$

where the subscripts denote partial derivatives. In matrix form, the real Jacobian

$$
Df(x_0, y_0) = \begin{pmatrix} u_x & u_y \\ v_x & v_y \end{pmatrix} \bigg|_{(x_0, y_0)}
$$

has the structure

$$
Df = \begin{pmatrix} a & -b \\ b & a \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{with } a = u_x, \; b = v_x,
$$

which is precisely the matrix of multiplication by the complex number $a + ib$ on $\mathbb{R}^2 \cong \mathbb{C}$. The Cauchy-Riemann system is the exact algebraic condition for $Df$ to belong to the subring $\mathbb{C} \subset \mathrm{Mat}_2(\mathbb{R})$ of complex-multiplication matrices. [ref: TODO_REF Ahlfors Ch. 2 §2.1]

**Wirtinger derivatives.** Introduce the operators

$$
\partial := \tfrac{1}{2}\left(\partial_x - i \partial_y\right), \qquad \bar\partial := \tfrac{1}{2}\left(\partial_x + i \partial_y\right),
$$

acting on $C^1$ functions $f : \Omega \to \mathbb{C}$. A direct expansion gives

$$
\bar\partial f = \tfrac{1}{2}\big((u_x - v_y) + i(v_x + u_y)\big),
$$

so the Cauchy-Riemann equations are equivalent to the single complex equation $\bar\partial f = 0$. Equivalently, $f$ is holomorphic iff $f$ is annihilated by $\bar\partial$, the *anti-holomorphic Cauchy-Riemann operator*.

**Harmonicity.** A real-valued $C^2$ function $u : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ is **harmonic** if it satisfies Laplace's equation $\Delta u := u_{xx} + u_{yy} = 0$. If $f = u + iv$ is holomorphic with $u, v$ of class $C^2$, then differentiating $u_x = v_y$ once more in $x$ gives $u_{xx} = v_{yx}$, and differentiating $u_y = -v_x$ in $y$ gives $u_{yy} = -v_{xy}$, so $u_{xx} + u_{yy} = v_{yx} - v_{xy} = 0$ by equality of mixed partials. The same argument gives $\Delta v = 0$. Thus the real and imaginary parts of a holomorphic function are both harmonic. (The smoothness assumption $u, v \in C^2$ is automatic once $f$ is known to be holomorphic, since holomorphic functions are real-analytic.)

**Harmonic conjugate.** Given a harmonic $u : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ on a simply-connected open $\Omega$, a **harmonic conjugate** of $u$ is a harmonic $v : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ such that $f := u + iv$ is holomorphic. The harmonic conjugate is unique up to additive constant. [ref: TODO_REF Ahlfors Ch. 3 §3.2]

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Existence of partials is not enough.* The function $f(z) = e^{-1/z^4}$ for $z \neq 0$ and $f(0) = 0$ has $u_x(0, 0) = u_y(0, 0) = v_x(0, 0) = v_y(0, 0) = 0$, so the Cauchy-Riemann equations hold at the origin, yet $f$ is not complex-differentiable at $0$. The converse direction of the Cauchy-Riemann equivalence requires real-differentiability of $u, v$ at the point, not merely existence of partials. The Looman-Menchoff theorem provides a refined converse that weakens this hypothesis substantially.
- *The harmonic-conjugate domain hypothesis is genuine.* On the punctured plane $\Omega = \mathbb{C}^*$, the harmonic function $u(x, y) = \log\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ admits no single-valued harmonic conjugate: any local choice glues into the multivalued $\arg z$, whose monodromy around the origin is $2\pi$. Simply-connectedness of $\Omega$ is needed for global existence.
- *Anti-holomorphic functions satisfy a sign-flipped Cauchy-Riemann system.* The map $f(z) = \bar z$ has $u_x = 1, v_y = -1$ and $u_y = 0, v_x = 0$: the first Cauchy-Riemann equation fails. The anti-holomorphic Cauchy-Riemann system $u_x = -v_y, u_y = v_x$ characterises maps annihilated by $\partial$ rather than $\bar\partial$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Cauchy-Riemann equivalence).** *Let $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ be open, $f : \Omega \to \mathbb{C}$ a function, and $z_0 = x_0 + iy_0 \in \Omega$. Write $f = u + iv$. Then $f$ is complex-differentiable at $z_0$ if and only if both of the following hold:*

1. *$u, v$ are real-differentiable at $(x_0, y_0)$,*
2. *the Cauchy-Riemann equations $u_x = v_y$ and $u_y = -v_x$ hold at $(x_0, y_0)$.*

*In that case the complex derivative satisfies $f'(z_0) = u_x(x_0, y_0) + i v_x(x_0, y_0)$.* [ref: TODO_REF Ahlfors Ch. 2 §2.1]

**Proof.** ($\Rightarrow$) Assume $f$ is complex-differentiable at $z_0$ with derivative $f'(z_0) = \alpha + i\beta$. The defining limit

$$
\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(z_0 + h) - f(z_0)}{h} = \alpha + i\beta
$$

holds for every direction of $h \to 0$. Restrict $h$ to the real axis, $h = t \in \mathbb{R}$. The increment in the input is $(x_0 + t) - (x_0) = t$ in the $x$-coordinate and $0$ in the $y$-coordinate, so

$$
\frac{f(z_0 + t) - f(z_0)}{t} = \frac{u(x_0 + t, y_0) - u(x_0, y_0)}{t} + i \frac{v(x_0 + t, y_0) - v(x_0, y_0)}{t}.
$$

Taking $t \to 0$, the existence of the complex limit forces the existence of $u_x(x_0, y_0)$ and $v_x(x_0, y_0)$, with

$$
\alpha + i\beta = u_x(x_0, y_0) + i v_x(x_0, y_0).
$$

Now restrict $h$ to the imaginary axis, $h = it$ with $t \in \mathbb{R}$. The increment is $0$ in $x$ and $t$ in $y$, so

$$
\frac{f(z_0 + it) - f(z_0)}{it} = \frac{1}{i}\left(\frac{u(x_0, y_0 + t) - u(x_0, y_0)}{t} + i \frac{v(x_0, y_0 + t) - v(x_0, y_0)}{t}\right).
$$

Using $1/i = -i$, the limit as $t \to 0$ is

$$
\alpha + i\beta = -i u_y(x_0, y_0) + v_y(x_0, y_0) = v_y(x_0, y_0) - i u_y(x_0, y_0).
$$

Equating the two expressions for $\alpha + i\beta$, the real and imaginary parts give $\alpha = u_x = v_y$ and $\beta = v_x = -u_y$. These are the Cauchy-Riemann equations, and the formula $f'(z_0) = u_x + i v_x$ falls out. Real-differentiability of $u, v$ at $(x_0, y_0)$ follows from the existence of all four partials together with the complex-differentiability hypothesis, which gives a stronger linear approximation.

($\Leftarrow$) Assume $u, v$ are real-differentiable at $(x_0, y_0)$ and the Cauchy-Riemann equations hold there. Write the Cauchy-Riemann values $a := u_x(x_0, y_0) = v_y(x_0, y_0)$ and $b := v_x(x_0, y_0) = -u_y(x_0, y_0)$. Real-differentiability of $u$ gives

$$
u(x_0 + s, y_0 + t) - u(x_0, y_0) = a \cdot s - b \cdot t + o(\sqrt{s^2 + t^2}),
$$

and similarly real-differentiability of $v$ gives

$$
v(x_0 + s, y_0 + t) - v(x_0, y_0) = b \cdot s + a \cdot t + o(\sqrt{s^2 + t^2}).
$$

Combine these into $f = u + iv$ with $h = s + it$:

$$
f(z_0 + h) - f(z_0) = (a \cdot s - b \cdot t) + i(b \cdot s + a \cdot t) + o(|h|).
$$

The bracketed real-linear expression equals $(a + ib)(s + it) = (a + ib) h$, as a direct expansion confirms. Therefore

$$
f(z_0 + h) - f(z_0) = (a + ib) h + o(|h|),
$$

which is precisely the statement that $f$ is complex-differentiable at $z_0$ with $f'(z_0) = a + ib = u_x + i v_x$. $\square$

The forward direction uses only two directional limits — real and imaginary — to deduce the four-fold partial-derivative identity. The backward direction repackages the two real total-differential approximations into the single complex linear approximation, with the Cauchy-Riemann identities supplying the matching coefficients $a$ and $b$ that make the real linear map a complex multiplication.

**Bridge.** The Cauchy-Riemann equivalence is the structural pivot from which the rest of one-complex-variable analysis unfolds. First, it builds toward the *Cauchy integral formula and Cauchy's theorem* (06.01.02), whose proofs rest on the closedness of the form $f(z) \, dz$ — closedness that is exactly the Cauchy-Riemann condition in disguise via $\bar\partial f = 0$. Second, it builds toward the theory of *harmonic functions on the disk and Poisson integrals* (06.01.11): the real part of a holomorphic function is harmonic, and conversely every harmonic function on a simply-connected domain is the real part of a holomorphic function with explicit Poisson-integral representation. Third, it builds toward *conformal mapping*: the matrix structure $Df = \begin{pmatrix} a & -b \\ b & a \end{pmatrix}$ is rotation-and-scaling, so a holomorphic map with non-zero derivative preserves angles, and the Cauchy-Riemann system is the exact local condition for conformality. Fourth, it builds toward *several-complex-variables analysis* through the Wirtinger formalism $\bar\partial f = 0$: in $n$ variables one demands $\bar\partial_j f = 0$ for each $j$, and the resulting Cauchy-Riemann system has the elliptic-overdetermined character that underlies the entire theory of holomorphic functions on $\mathbb{C}^n$ and complex manifolds. Putting these four together, the Cauchy-Riemann equations are the foundational reason two-dimensional harmonic problems collapse to one-dimensional complex analysis — the central insight Riemann built his theory of complex functions on.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Verify the Cauchy-Riemann equations for $f(z) = z^3$ at every point of the plane.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $(x + iy)^3$ using the binomial formula; read off $u$ and $v$; compute the four partials.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$(x + iy)^3 = x^3 + 3ix^2 y + 3i^2 xy^2 + i^3 y^3 = (x^3 - 3xy^2) + i(3x^2 y - y^3)$. So $u = x^3 - 3xy^2$ and $v = 3x^2 y - y^3$. Partials: $u_x = 3x^2 - 3y^2$, $u_y = -6xy$, $v_x = 6xy$, $v_y = 3x^2 - 3y^2$. Check: $u_x = v_y$ gives $3x^2 - 3y^2 = 3x^2 - 3y^2$ ✓, and $u_y = -v_x$ gives $-6xy = -(6xy) = -6xy$ ✓. Cauchy-Riemann holds at every point. $\square$

</details>

Show that $f(z) = e^z = e^x(\cos y + i \sin y)$ satisfies the Cauchy-Riemann equations on $\mathbb{C}$, and read off $f'(z)$ from the formula $f'(z) = u_x + i v_x$.

The real and imaginary parts are $u = e^x \cos y$ and $v = e^x \sin y$.

Partials: $u_x = e^x \cos y$, $u_y = -e^x \sin y$, $v_x = e^x \sin y$, $v_y = e^x \cos y$. Check Cauchy-Riemann: $u_x = e^x \cos y = v_y$ ✓ and $u_y = -e^x \sin y = -v_x$ ✓. Derivative: $f'(z) = u_x + i v_x = e^x \cos y + i e^x \sin y = e^x(\cos y + i \sin y) = e^z$. So $(e^z)' = e^z$ on $\mathbb{C}$, recovering the familiar identity. $\square$

</details>

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Find the harmonic conjugate of $u(x, y) = x^2 - y^2 + 3x$ on the plane, then identify the resulting holomorphic function $f = u + iv$ in closed form in $z$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the Cauchy-Riemann equations as a pair of differential equations for $v$. Integrate $v_y = u_x$ in $y$, then differentiate in $x$ and match against $-u_y$ to pin down the constant.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Compute $u_x = 2x + 3$ and $u_y = -2y$. The first Cauchy-Riemann equation $v_y = u_x = 2x + 3$ integrates in $y$ to $v(x, y) = 2xy + 3y + \varphi(x)$ for some function $\varphi$ of $x$ alone. Differentiate in $x$: $v_x = 2y + \varphi'(x)$. The second Cauchy-Riemann equation $v_x = -u_y = 2y$ forces $\varphi'(x) = 0$, so $\varphi$ is a constant $c$. The harmonic conjugate is $v(x, y) = 2xy + 3y + c$. Closed form: $f(z) = u + iv = (x^2 - y^2 + 3x) + i(2xy + 3y + c) = z^2 + 3z + ic$. The arbitrary additive constant in $v$ corresponds to the arbitrary imaginary additive constant in $f$. $\square$

</details>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the function $f(z) = |z|^2 = x^2 + y^2$ is complex-differentiable only at $z_0 = 0$, and compute $f'(0)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $u = x^2 + y^2$ and $v = 0$. Determine the points where the Cauchy-Riemann equations hold, then check real-differentiability there.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Partials: $u_x = 2x$, $u_y = 2y$, $v_x = v_y = 0$. The first Cauchy-Riemann equation $u_x = v_y$ reads $2x = 0$, forcing $x = 0$. The second $u_y = -v_x$ reads $2y = 0$, forcing $y = 0$. So the only point at which the Cauchy-Riemann equations are satisfied is $z_0 = 0$. At that point $u, v$ are both polynomial and so real-differentiable, with all four partials zero; the equivalence theorem applies and $f'(0) = u_x(0, 0) + i v_x(0, 0) = 0$. At every other point $f$ fails the Cauchy-Riemann test, so it is not complex-differentiable. $\square$

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Translate the Cauchy-Riemann equations into the Wirtinger statement $\bar\partial f = 0$ by direct computation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply $\bar\partial = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_x + i \partial_y)$ to $f = u + iv$ and split into real and imaginary parts.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Compute $\bar\partial f = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_x + i \partial_y)(u + iv) = \tfrac{1}{2}(u_x + i v_x + i u_y - v_y) = \tfrac{1}{2}\big((u_x - v_y) + i(v_x + u_y)\big)$. The complex equation $\bar\partial f = 0$ is equivalent to the simultaneous vanishing of the real and imaginary parts: $u_x - v_y = 0$ and $v_x + u_y = 0$. These are exactly the Cauchy-Riemann equations $u_x = v_y$ and $u_y = -v_x$. $\square$

</details>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that on a simply-connected open set $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}$, every harmonic $u : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ admits a harmonic conjugate $v$, unique up to additive constant.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider the differential $1$-form $\omega = -u_y \, dx + u_x \, dy$ on $\Omega$. Compute $d\omega$ and use simple-connectedness to find a primitive.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define $\omega := -u_y \, dx + u_x \, dy$ on $\Omega$. The exterior derivative is $d\omega = (-u_{yy} - u_{xx}) \, dx \wedge dy = -\Delta u \, dx \wedge dy = 0$ since $u$ is harmonic. So $\omega$ is a closed $1$-form on the simply-connected $\Omega$. By the Poincaré lemma for simply-connected domains in $\mathbb{R}^2$, $\omega$ has a primitive: a real $C^2$ function $v : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$ with $dv = \omega$, i.e. $v_x = -u_y$ and $v_y = u_x$. These are exactly the Cauchy-Riemann equations; setting $f := u + iv$ then satisfies the equivalence theorem's hypotheses and is holomorphic. Uniqueness: if $v_1, v_2$ are two harmonic conjugates, then $v_1 - v_2$ has $(v_1 - v_2)_x = (v_1 - v_2)_y = 0$, so $v_1 - v_2$ is a constant on the connected $\Omega$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Compute the Wirtinger derivatives $\partial f$ and $\bar\partial f$ for $f(z) = z \bar z = |z|^2$, and verify that the Cauchy-Riemann equation $\bar\partial f = 0$ fails everywhere except at $z = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the chain-rule formulas $\partial z = 1, \bar\partial z = 0, \partial \bar z = 0, \bar\partial \bar z = 1$, treating $z$ and $\bar z$ as independent in Wirtinger calculus.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Treat $z$ and $\bar z$ as independent variables. Then $\partial(z \bar z) = (\partial z)\bar z + z(\partial \bar z) = 1 \cdot \bar z + z \cdot 0 = \bar z$, and $\bar\partial(z \bar z) = (\bar\partial z)\bar z + z(\bar\partial \bar z) = 0 \cdot \bar z + z \cdot 1 = z$. So $\partial f = \bar z$ and $\bar\partial f = z$. The Cauchy-Riemann equation $\bar\partial f = 0$ reduces to $z = 0$, confirming that $|z|^2$ is complex-differentiable only at the origin. As a check, $\partial f(0) + i \cdot \mathrm{Im}\,\partial f(0) = \bar 0 = 0$ recovers $f'(0) = 0$, agreeing with Exercise 4. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Exhibit a harmonic function on the punctured plane $\mathbb{C}^* := \mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}$ that admits no globally single-valued harmonic conjugate, and explain how the obstruction is measured.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Take $u(x, y) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log(x^2 + y^2) = \log|z|$. Try to integrate the candidate $v$ around a circle enclosing the origin.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $u(x, y) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log(x^2 + y^2)$. Compute $u_x = x/(x^2 + y^2)$, $u_y = y/(x^2 + y^2)$, and verify $\Delta u = 0$ on $\mathbb{C}^*$ — a direct computation gives $u_{xx} + u_{yy} = (y^2 - x^2)/(x^2 + y^2)^2 + (x^2 - y^2)/(x^2 + y^2)^2 = 0$. The closed $1$-form $\omega = -u_y \, dx + u_x \, dy = (-y \, dx + x \, dy)/(x^2 + y^2)$ is the angle form $d\theta$, whose integral around the unit circle is $2\pi \neq 0$. Hence $\omega$ has no global primitive on $\mathbb{C}^*$. Equivalently, the holomorphic candidate $f = u + iv$ would have to be a branch of $\log z$, which has monodromy $2\pi i$ around the origin and no single-valued representative on $\mathbb{C}^*$. The obstruction is measured by the cohomology class $[\omega] \in H^1_{\mathrm{dR}}(\mathbb{C}^*; \mathbb{R}) \cong \mathbb{R}$, with value $2\pi$ on the standard generator. Simple-connectedness of the domain kills this cohomology and restores existence. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Looman-Menchoff theorem.** The hypothesis that $u, v$ are real-differentiable in the Cauchy-Riemann equivalence theorem can be substantially weakened. The Looman-Menchoff theorem states that if $f : \Omega \to \mathbb{C}$ is continuous on an open $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}$, the four partial derivatives $u_x, u_y, v_x, v_y$ exist everywhere on $\Omega$ (not necessarily continuous), and the Cauchy-Riemann equations hold pointwise on $\Omega$, then $f$ is holomorphic on $\Omega$. The continuity hypothesis is essential — Menchoff showed it cannot be dropped — and the proof proceeds by a Baire-category argument identifying a large subset of $\Omega$ on which $f$ is locally analytic, followed by an analytic-continuation extension. [ref: TODO_REF Ahlfors Ch. 2 §2.1 remarks]

**The $\bar\partial$ operator on $C^\infty$ and currents.** The Wirtinger characterisation $\bar\partial f = 0$ extends past $C^1$ to distributions: a locally integrable $f : \Omega \to \mathbb{C}$ is *weakly holomorphic* if $\bar\partial f = 0$ in the sense of distributions, i.e. $\int_\Omega f \, \bar\partial \varphi \, dz \, d\bar z = 0$ for every test function $\varphi$ compactly supported in $\Omega$. The Weyl-Lewy lemma (Weyl 1940) shows weakly holomorphic functions coincide with classical holomorphic functions: $\bar\partial f = 0$ in distributions implies $f$ is smooth and classically holomorphic. This is the prototype of elliptic regularity for systems of overdetermined PDE.

**Hartogs's theorem.** In several complex variables, holomorphicity is defined as the simultaneous Cauchy-Riemann conditions $\bar\partial_j f = 0$ for each variable $z_j$. Hartogs (1906) discovered the striking phenomenon that *separate* holomorphicity — holomorphicity in each variable with the others held fixed — already implies *joint* holomorphicity (and in particular joint continuity), with no a priori continuity hypothesis. The proof uses subharmonic-function methods and the maximum principle on bidiscs, and the result distinguishes complex analysis sharply from real analysis: a function of two real variables that is separately $C^\infty$ in each variable can fail to be jointly continuous. [ref: TODO_REF Hartogs 1906]

**Wirtinger calculus and the complex de Rham complex.** The decomposition of the complexified exterior algebra $\Lambda^* T^*_\mathbb{C} \Omega$ into types splits the exterior derivative as $d = \partial + \bar\partial$ on a complex manifold $\Omega$, with

$$
\partial : \mathcal{A}^{p, q}(\Omega) \to \mathcal{A}^{p+1, q}(\Omega), \qquad \bar\partial : \mathcal{A}^{p, q}(\Omega) \to \mathcal{A}^{p, q+1}(\Omega).
$$

The Dolbeault cohomology groups $H^{p, q}_{\bar\partial}(\Omega)$ measure obstructions to solving $\bar\partial u = f$ on $\Omega$. For a smooth $\bar\partial$-closed $(0, 1)$-form $f$ on $\mathbb{C}^n$, the Dolbeault lemma constructs a smooth $u$ with $\bar\partial u = f$ on any pseudoconvex open set, generalising the harmonic-conjugate construction beyond simply-connected planar domains.

**Hodge theory in the plane.** On a compact Riemann surface $X$ (in particular $\mathbb{C}_\infty$, see [06.01.07]) the operator $\bar\partial$ acts on the smooth $(0, 1)$-forms with Hodge decomposition

$$
\mathcal{A}^{0, 1}(X) = \mathcal{H}^{0, 1}(X) \oplus \mathrm{im}\, \bar\partial \oplus \mathrm{im}\, \bar\partial^*,
$$

with $\mathcal{H}^{0, 1}(X)$ the finite-dimensional space of harmonic representatives. On $\mathbb{C}_\infty$ this group vanishes, recovering the global solvability of $\bar\partial$ on the sphere; on a torus $\mathbb{C}/\Lambda$ it is one-dimensional. The Cauchy-Riemann equations sit at the level of $0$-forms in this complex, with their kernel the holomorphic functions.

**Elliptic regularity for general elliptic operators.** The Cauchy-Riemann system $\bar\partial f = 0$ is the simplest example of an *elliptic* PDE: the symbol $\sigma(\bar\partial)(\xi) = \tfrac{1}{2}(\xi_x + i\xi_y)$ is non-zero for every non-zero cotangent vector $\xi \in \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus \{0\}$. Solutions of elliptic PDEs with $C^\infty$ coefficients are automatically $C^\infty$ (interior elliptic regularity), and when the equation has analytic coefficients the solutions are real-analytic (Petrovskii 1939; Morrey 1958). For Cauchy-Riemann this is the statement that holomorphic functions are real-analytic — the bootstrap from $\bar\partial f = 0$ to convergent Taylor expansion is the foundational instance of the general elliptic-regularity philosophy. [ref: TODO_REF Hörmander Ch. 1–2]

**Conformality.** A real-differentiable map $f : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}^2$ with non-singular Jacobian is *conformal at $z_0$* (preserves angles and orientation) iff $Df(z_0)$ is a positive multiple of a rotation matrix — equivalently $Df(z_0) = \begin{pmatrix} a & -b \\ b & a \end{pmatrix}$ with $a^2 + b^2 > 0$. This is exactly the Cauchy-Riemann condition together with $f'(z_0) \neq 0$. Hence a holomorphic function with non-vanishing derivative is conformal at every point, and conformality of a smooth orientation-preserving $f$ is equivalent to holomorphicity with $f' \neq 0$.

**Synthesis.** The Cauchy-Riemann equations are the foundational pivot of one-complex-variable analysis, and the synthesis runs in four directions. First, the synthesis with **integration theory**: the closedness of the form $f(z) \, dz$ — Cauchy's theorem — is the Cauchy-Riemann equation $\bar\partial f = 0$ rephrased through the language of differential forms, and every theorem about complex contour integration is a corollary statement about a closed $1$-form on a planar domain. Second, the synthesis with **harmonic-function theory**: the real and imaginary parts of a holomorphic function are harmonic, and on simply-connected domains every harmonic function is the real part of a holomorphic one. Two-dimensional Dirichlet problems for $\Delta u = 0$ thereby collapse into one-dimensional problems about holomorphic functions — the structural reason for the disproportionate analytic power of complex analysis in two real dimensions.

Third, the synthesis with **conformal mapping**: the Cauchy-Riemann condition is precisely the local condition for an orientation-preserving smooth map to preserve angles, and the Riemann mapping theorem [06.01.06] is the global existence statement that simply-connected proper subdomains of $\mathbb{C}$ are conformally equivalent to the disk. The Cauchy-Riemann system supplies the differential equation; the Riemann mapping theorem supplies its existence theorem. Fourth, the synthesis with **several-complex-variables analysis and complex geometry**: $\bar\partial$ extends to the Dolbeault complex on $\mathbb{C}^n$ and on complex manifolds, with the cohomology $H^{p, q}_{\bar\partial}$ measuring obstructions to higher-dimensional Cauchy-Riemann problems. Hartogs's separately-holomorphic theorem and Hörmander's $L^2$-estimates for $\bar\partial$ are the higher-dimensional descendants of the planar Cauchy-Riemann story, and the elliptic-overdetermined nature of $\bar\partial$ in $n$ variables is the analytic backbone of Stein manifolds, sheaf cohomology of coherent analytic sheaves, and Kodaira's vanishing theorems.

## Full proof set [Master]

The advanced results assembled above follow from the proofs given in earlier sections together with classical results recorded in Ahlfors *Complex Analysis* Ch. 2 §2.1 and Ch. 3 §3.2, Hörmander *An Introduction to Complex Analysis in Several Variables* Chs. 1–2, and the original sources. Five reference points: (a) the Looman-Menchoff theorem (Looman 1923; Menchoff 1936) uses a Baire-category decomposition $\Omega = \bigcup_n F_n$ where each $F_n$ is the closed set on which the four partials are bounded by $n$, applying Egorov's theorem and the dominated-convergence approach to verify $f$ is locally given by a Cauchy integral on the interior of each $F_n$, then propagating analyticity via the identity theorem. (b) The Weyl-Lewy lemma proceeds by convolving with a smooth approximate identity to get a $C^\infty$ family $f_\varepsilon$ satisfying $\bar\partial f_\varepsilon = 0$, hence classically holomorphic, and verifying that $f_\varepsilon \to f$ in $L^1_{\mathrm{loc}}$; the local-uniform-convergence-of-holomorphic-functions theorem then gives $f$ classically holomorphic. (c) Hartogs's theorem (Hartogs 1906) uses subharmonicity of $\log|f|$ in each variable separately, combined with a Hartogs-figure argument that propagates analyticity from a polydisc to a larger one. (d) The Dolbeault lemma proceeds by reducing to the one-variable Cauchy-Riemann equation $\bar\partial u = f$ for $f \in \mathcal{A}^{0, 1}_c(\mathbb{C})$ compactly supported, where the explicit solution is

$$
u(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_\mathbb{C} \frac{f(\zeta) \, d\zeta \wedge d\bar\zeta}{\zeta - z},
$$

via the Cauchy-Pompeiu formula; iterating over variables and using a partition-of-unity argument gives the multi-variable statement on pseudoconvex domains. (e) Elliptic regularity for $\bar\partial$ follows from the Calderón-Zygmund $L^p$-theory of singular integrals applied to the convolution kernel $1/z$, combined with a bootstrap from $L^p$ to $C^{k, \alpha}$ via the Morrey embedding. ## Connections [Master]

- **Holomorphic function [06.01.01]** — The Cauchy-Riemann equivalence is the precise differential characterisation of holomorphicity: a function is holomorphic iff its real and imaginary parts are real-differentiable and satisfy the Cauchy-Riemann system. Every theorem about holomorphic functions ultimately routes through the Cauchy-Riemann conditions at the level of the local differential geometry, with the equivalence theorem supplying the bridge between the analytic limit-definition and the partial-differential-equation reformulation.

- **Cauchy integral formula [06.01.02]** — Cauchy's theorem $\oint_\gamma f(z) \, dz = 0$ for a closed contour bounding a region of holomorphicity is the integrated form of the closed-$1$-form statement $d(f \, dz) = 0$, which holds because $\bar\partial f = 0$. The Cauchy integral formula's representation of $f$ at interior points by boundary values is the dual integration-by-parts statement that the operator $\bar\partial$ has a fundamental solution $1/(\pi z)$, and the Cauchy-Pompeiu formula extends the representation to non-holomorphic functions with a correction term involving $\bar\partial f$.

- **Riemann mapping theorem [06.01.06]** — The Cauchy-Riemann equations characterise conformality at each point, and the Riemann mapping theorem is the global existence statement that every simply-connected proper subdomain of $\mathbb{C}$ is conformally equivalent to the unit disk. The local differential-equation system $u_x = v_y, u_y = -v_x$ supplies the pointwise condition; the Riemann mapping theorem supplies the global existence of a function satisfying it that also realises a prescribed conformal equivalence.

- **Riemann sphere [06.01.07]** — On the Riemann sphere $\mathbb{C}_\infty = \mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{C})$ the Cauchy-Riemann equations apply chart-by-chart: a function $\mathbb{C}_\infty \to \mathbb{C}_\infty$ is holomorphic iff its expression in each affine chart satisfies the Cauchy-Riemann system. The transition map $z \mapsto 1/z$ is itself a Möbius transformation, hence holomorphic, and the chart-compatibility of the Cauchy-Riemann conditions is what allows the local differential characterisation to globalise into the Riemann-surface structure.

- **Harmonic functions on the disk and Poisson integrals [06.01.11, pending]** — The real and imaginary parts of a holomorphic function are harmonic, and the harmonic-conjugate construction inverts this: given a harmonic $u$ on a simply-connected domain, there is a holomorphic $f$ with $\mathrm{Re}\, f = u$ unique up to imaginary additive constant. The Poisson integral on the disk extends this to harmonic functions with prescribed boundary values, and the conjugate Poisson kernel produces the harmonic conjugate $v$ from the boundary data of $u$ — a constructive solution of the harmonic-conjugate problem.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Jean le Rond d'Alembert wrote down the equations now bearing Cauchy and Riemann's names in his *Essai d'une nouvelle théorie de la résistance des fluides* (1752), as the conditions for a planar fluid flow to be both incompressible and irrotational — the velocity field $(u, -v)$ then has potential $u$ and stream function $v$ with the relations $u_x = v_y$ and $u_y = -v_x$. Leonhard Euler, in *Principia motus fluidorum* (1757) and *De repraesentatione superficiei sphaericae super plano* (1777), gave the equations a complex-function-theoretic reading, observing that the conditions characterise functions of $x + iy$ in a sense that he made precise for analytic functions defined by power series. [ref: TODO_REF Cauchy 1825]

Augustin-Louis Cauchy placed complex differentiation on systematic ground in his *Mémoire sur les intégrales définies* (1814) and the foundational *Mémoire sur les intégrales définies prises entre des limites imaginaires* (1825), where he treated the equations as identities that hold for differentiable complex functions and proved the integral theorem now bearing his name. Bernhard Riemann, in his 1851 Inaugural Dissertation *Grundlagen für eine allgemeine Theorie der Functionen einer veränderlichen complexen Grösse* at Göttingen under Gauss, made the Cauchy-Riemann equations the *defining* property of complex-analytic — he called them *monogenic* — functions, breaking from the power-series approach of Cauchy and Weierstrass in favour of a geometric-function-theoretic standpoint. Riemann's choice of starting point is the structural origin of the modern view that holomorphicity is a local differential-equation property of a complex function, rather than a convergent-series property. [ref: TODO_REF Riemann 1851 dissertation]

Wilhelm Wirtinger, in *Zur formalen Theorie der Funktionen von mehr komplexen Veränderlichen* (1927), introduced the operators $\partial = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_x - i\partial_y)$ and $\bar\partial = \tfrac{1}{2}(\partial_x + i\partial_y)$, condensing the Cauchy-Riemann system to the single complex equation $\bar\partial f = 0$ and opening the modern several-complex-variables and complex-manifold formulations. Friedrich Hartogs's discovery (1906) that separate holomorphicity implies joint holomorphicity initiated the systematic study of holomorphic functions in several variables, and Lars Hörmander's $L^2$-estimates for $\bar\partial$ in the 1960s completed the analytic-elliptic-PDE programme that the Cauchy-Riemann equations seeded two centuries earlier.
