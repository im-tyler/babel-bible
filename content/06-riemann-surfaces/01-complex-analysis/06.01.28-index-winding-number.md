## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine walking around a tree while holding a rope tied to it. If you walk around the tree once and return to your starting point, the rope wraps around the tree exactly one time. Walk around it twice in the same direction, and the rope wraps twice. Walk around it once in the opposite direction, and the rope wraps negative one times (it unwinds).

The winding number is this rope-counting idea made precise for any closed curve in the plane around any point. Given a closed loop (a curve that returns to its starting point) and a point $a$ that does not lie on the loop, the winding number $n(\gamma, a)$ counts how many times the loop wraps around $a$, with counterclockwise wrapping counted positively and clockwise wrapping counted negatively.

The winding number is always a whole number: $0, 1, 2, \ldots$ or $-1, -2, \ldots$. If the point $a$ is outside the loop, the winding number is $0$ (the loop does not enclose the point). If the loop winds counterclockwise around $a$ three times, the winding number is $3$.

Why does this concept exist? The winding number is the topological invariant underlying Cauchy's integral formula and the residue theorem. It measures the relationship between a closed curve and the points in its complement, and this measurement is the engine that powers complex integration.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider the circle of radius $2$ centred at the origin, traversed once counterclockwise. Compute the winding number around the point $a = 1$ (inside the circle) and around $b = 5$ (outside the circle).

Step 1. The point $a = 1$ lies inside the circle of radius $2$ centred at the origin, since $|1| = 1 < 2$. The circle winds once counterclockwise around $a$, so $n(\gamma, 1) = 1$.

Step 2. The point $b = 5$ lies outside the circle since $|5| = 5 > 2$. The circle does not enclose $b$, so $n(\gamma, 5) = 0$.

Step 3. If the same circle were traversed three times counterclockwise, the winding number around $a = 1$ would be $3$, not $1$.

What this tells us: the winding number counts net encirclements. A point inside a once-traversed circle gets winding number $1$; a point outside gets $0$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\gamma : [0, 1] \to \mathbb{C}$ be a piecewise $C^1$ closed curve and let $a \in \mathbb{C}$ with $a \notin \gamma([0,1])$. The **winding number** (or **index**) of $\gamma$ around $a$ is

$$
n(\gamma, a) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \oint_\gamma \frac{dz}{z - a}.
$$

The integral is well-defined because $1/(z - a)$ is continuous on the compact set $\gamma([0,1])$ and hence integrable. The factor $1/(2\pi i)$ normalises the integral so that the result is an integer.

An equivalent characterisation: writing $\gamma(t) - a = r(t) e^{i\theta(t)}$ with a continuous choice of the argument $\theta(t)$, the winding number is $n(\gamma, a) = (\theta(1) - \theta(0))/(2\pi)$. This is the net change in the argument of $\gamma(t) - a$ divided by $2\pi$, which counts the number of full rotations of the position vector from $a$ to $\gamma(t)$.

**Definition (Homotopy of curves).** Two closed curves $\gamma_0, \gamma_1 : [0,1] \to U$ in an open set $U$ are **homotopic** (as closed curves in $U$) if there exists a continuous map $H : [0,1] \times [0,1] \to U$ with $H(0, t) = \gamma_0(t)$, $H(1, t) = \gamma_1(t)$, and $H(s, 0) = H(s, 1)$ for all $s$. [ref: TODO_REF Ahlfors Ch. 4]

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *The winding number is not defined when $a$ lies on $\gamma$.* The integrand $1/(z - a)$ has a pole on the contour, so the integral is not defined. The winding number requires $a \notin \gamma([0,1])$.
- *The curve must be closed.* The winding number is defined only for closed curves ($\gamma(0) = \gamma(1)$). For an open curve, the integral $(1/2\pi i)\int_\gamma dz/(z-a)$ is generally not an integer.
- *The winding number is not the geometric "number of crossings."* A figure-eight curve crosses itself once but can have winding number $0$ around a point near the crossing, because the two lobes contribute $+1$ and $-1$ respectively.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Integer-valued and locally constant).** *Let $\gamma$ be a piecewise $C^1$ closed curve and $a \notin \gamma([0,1])$. Then:*

*(i) $n(\gamma, a)$ is an integer.*

*(ii) $a \mapsto n(\gamma, a)$ is constant on each connected component of $\mathbb{C} \setminus \gamma([0,1])$.*

*Proof of (i).* Parametrise $\gamma$ by $\gamma : [0,1] \to \mathbb{C}$ with $\gamma(0) = \gamma(1)$. Define

$$
F(t) = \exp\!\left(\int_0^t \frac{\gamma'(u)}{\gamma(u) - a}\,du\right).
$$

Differentiate using the chain rule:

$$
F'(t) = F(t) \cdot \frac{\gamma'(t)}{\gamma(t) - a}.
$$

Now compute the derivative of $F(t)/(\gamma(t) - a)$:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\left(\frac{F(t)}{\gamma(t) - a}\right) = \frac{F'(t)(\gamma(t) - a) - F(t)\gamma'(t)}{(\gamma(t) - a)^2} = \frac{F(t)\gamma'(t) - F(t)\gamma'(t)}{(\gamma(t) - a)^2} = 0.
$$

Hence $F(t)/(\gamma(t) - a)$ is constant. At $t = 0$: $F(0) = \exp(0) = 1$, so $F(0)/(\gamma(0) - a) = 1/(\gamma(0) - a)$. At $t = 1$: $F(1)/(\gamma(1) - a) = 1/(\gamma(0) - a)$ since $\gamma(1) = \gamma(0)$. Therefore $F(1) = 1$, which means

$$
\exp\!\left(\int_0^1 \frac{\gamma'(t)}{\gamma(t) - a}\,dt\right) = \exp(2\pi i \cdot n(\gamma, a)) = 1.
$$

Since $e^{2\pi i k} = 1$ if and only if $k \in \mathbb{Z}$, the winding number $n(\gamma, a)$ is an integer. $\square$

*Proof of (ii).* Let $a, b$ lie in the same connected component of $\mathbb{C} \setminus \gamma([0,1])$. There exists a continuous path $\sigma : [0,1] \to \mathbb{C} \setminus \gamma([0,1])$ with $\sigma(0) = a$ and $\sigma(1) = b$. The function

$$
g(s) = n(\gamma, \sigma(s)) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{dz}{z - \sigma(s)}
$$

is continuous in $s$ (the denominator is bounded away from zero since $\sigma(s) \notin \gamma([0,1])$). By part (i), $g(s)$ is integer-valued. A continuous integer-valued function on $[0,1]$ is constant, so $n(\gamma, a) = n(\gamma, b)$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The integer-valuedness of the winding number builds toward [06.01.03] the residue theorem, where it appears again as the multiplicity factor counting how many times the contour encloses each singularity. The foundational reason the winding number is central to complex analysis is that it is exactly the topological measurement that makes Cauchy's integral formula work: the factor $1/(z-a)$ integrated around a loop picks up $2\pi i$ for each net encirclement of $a$. The central insight is that the winding number identifies the homotopy class of a closed curve in $\mathbb{C} \setminus \{a\}$ with an integer, and this is the bridge from topology to analysis. The pattern generalises through the argument principle (counting zeros and poles by the winding number of $f \circ \gamma$ around the origin) and Rouche's theorem (comparing winding numbers of two functions on the same contour).

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the homotopy invariance of the winding number: if $\gamma_0$ and $\gamma_1$ are homotopic closed curves in $\mathbb{C} \setminus \{a\}$, then $n(\gamma_0, a) = n(\gamma_1, a)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the fact that $1/(z - a)$ is holomorphic on $\mathbb{C} \setminus \{a\}$ and apply Cauchy's theorem for the homotopy.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $H : [0,1]^2 \to \mathbb{C} \setminus \{a\}$ be a homotopy with $H(0, t) = \gamma_0(t)$ and $H(1, t) = \gamma_1(t)$. Define $g(s) = n(H(s, \cdot), a)$ for each fixed $s$. Then

$$
g(s) = \frac{1}{2\pi i} \int_0^1 \frac{\partial H/\partial t(s, t)}{H(s, t) - a}\,dt.
$$

Since $H$ is continuous and $H(s, t) \neq a$ for all $(s, t)$, the integrand is continuous in $(s, t)$, so $g(s)$ is continuous in $s$. By the integer-valuedness of the winding number, $g(s)$ is continuous and integer-valued, hence constant. Therefore $n(\gamma_0, a) = g(0) = g(1) = n(\gamma_1, a)$. $\square$

</details>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the **argument principle** for a meromorphic function $f$ with no zeros or poles on a piecewise $C^1$ closed curve $\gamma$:

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f'(z)}{f(z)}\,dz = Z - P
$$

where $Z$ is the number of zeros and $P$ the number of poles of $f$ inside $\gamma$, counted with multiplicity. Hint: write $f'(z)/f(z) = d/dz[\log f(z)]$ and use the winding number.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Near a zero of order $k$, $f(z) = (z-a)^k g(z)$ with $g(a) \neq 0$, so $f'(z)/f(z) = k/(z-a) + g'(z)/g(z)$. Similarly for poles. Apply the residue theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Near a zero $a$ of order $k$: $f(z) = (z - a)^k g(z)$ where $g$ is holomorphic with $g(a) \neq 0$. Then $f'(z)/f(z) = k/(z - a) + g'(z)/g(z)$, and $g'/g$ is holomorphic near $a$. The residue of $f'/f$ at $a$ is $k$.

Near a pole $b$ of order $m$: $f(z) = (z - b)^{-m} h(z)$ where $h$ is holomorphic with $h(b) \neq 0$. Then $f'(z)/f(z) = -m/(z - b) + h'(z)/h(z)$, and $h'/h$ is holomorphic near $b$. The residue of $f'/f$ at $b$ is $-m$.

Now observe that $f'/f$ is meromorphic inside and on $\gamma$ with poles only at the zeros and poles of $f$. By the residue theorem:

$$
\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f'(z)}{f(z)}\,dz = \sum_{\text{zeros}} \operatorname{Res}(f'/f, a_j) + \sum_{\text{poles}} \operatorname{Res}(f'/f, b_k) = \sum_j k_j - \sum_m m_k = Z - P.
$$

Equivalently, the integral equals the winding number of $f \circ \gamma$ around the origin: $n(f \circ \gamma, 0)$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

Prove **Rouche's theorem**: if $f$ and $g$ are holomorphic inside and on a piecewise $C^1$ closed curve $\gamma$, and $|f(z) - g(z)| < |f(z)|$ for all $z \in \gamma$, then $f$ and $g$ have the same number of zeros inside $\gamma$, counted with multiplicity.

Show that $f_t(z) = f(z) + t(g(z) - f(z))$ has no zeros on $\gamma$ for $t \in [0, 1]$, then track the winding number of $f_t \circ \gamma$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$$
|f_t(z)| = |f(z) + t(g(z) - f(z))| \geq |f(z)| - t|g(z) - f(z)| > |f(z)| - |f(z)| = 0.
$$

The inequality holds because $t \leq 1$ and $|g(z) - f(z)| < |f(z)|$ on $\gamma$. So $f_t$ has no zeros on $\gamma$ for any $t \in [0, 1]$.

The function $N(t)$ is integer-valued and continuous (the roots of $f_t$ vary continuously with $t$, and no root crosses $\gamma$), hence constant. Therefore $N(0) = N(1)$: $f$ and $g$ have the same number of zeros inside $\gamma$. $\square$

## Advanced results [Master]

**Argument principle.** For $f$ meromorphic with no zeros or poles on a closed curve $\gamma$, the number of zeros minus poles inside $\gamma$ is $n(f \circ \gamma, 0)$. This is the bridge from the winding number to root-counting: the winding number of the image curve $f(\gamma)$ around the origin encodes the zero-pole balance of $f$ inside the domain. The argument principle appears throughout complex analysis as the primary tool for localising and counting solutions to $f(z) = w$. [ref: TODO_REF Conway Ch. IV]

**Rouche's theorem.** If $|f - g| < |f|$ on $\gamma$, then $f$ and $g$ have the same number of zeros inside $\gamma$. Rouche's theorem converts an inequality on a boundary into equality of interior root counts. The standard application is the localisation of polynomial roots: if $p(z) = z^n + a_{n-1}z^{n-1} + \cdots + a_0$ and $R > 1 + \max|a_k|$, then on $|z| = R$ the dominant term $z^n$ satisfies $|a_{n-1}z^{n-1} + \cdots + a_0| < |z^n|$, so $p$ has exactly $n$ zeros inside $|z| < R$ (the fundamental theorem of algebra).

**Degree of a map.** For a continuous map $f : S^1 \to S^1$, the winding number $\deg(f) = n(f \circ \gamma, 0)$ where $\gamma$ is any parametrisation of $S^1$, is well-defined and is called the **topological degree** of $f$. Homotopic maps have the same degree, and the converse holds: two maps $S^1 \to S^1$ are homotopic if and only if they have the same degree. This is the prototypical homotopy-classification result and appears again in [06.01.07] the Riemann sphere as the degree of a rational map.

**Jordan curve theorem (winding-number form).** For a Jordan curve $\gamma$ (a simple closed curve), the complement $\mathbb{C} \setminus \gamma([0,1])$ has exactly two connected components: the bounded interior (where $n(\gamma, a) = \pm 1$) and the unbounded exterior (where $n(\gamma, a) = 0$). The winding number distinguishes the two components and provides the analytic characterisation of "inside" versus "outside" a Jordan curve.

**Cauchy's integral formula via the winding number.** If $f$ is holomorphic on an open set $U$ and $\gamma$ is a null-homologous cycle in $U$ (a cycle with $n(\gamma, a) = 0$ for all $a \notin U$), then for $z \in U \setminus \gamma([0,1])$:

$$
n(\gamma, z) \cdot f(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f(w)}{w - z}\,dw.
$$

This is the general form of Cauchy's integral formula, where the winding number is the multiplicity factor that appears whenever a contour wraps around a singularity.

**Synthesis.** The winding number is the foundational reason that topology controls complex analysis, and the central insight is that the integer $n(\gamma, a)$ identifies the homotopy class of a closed curve in $\mathbb{C} \setminus \{a\}$ with an element of $\mathbb{Z}$. Putting these together with the Cauchy integral formula, every contour integral of a holomorphic function is determined by the winding numbers of the contour around the singularities of the integrand. This is exactly the structure that generalises through the argument principle (where $f \circ \gamma$ replaces $\gamma$ and the winding number counts zeros) and Rouche's theorem (where the winding number provides stability under perturbation). The bridge is between the continuous world of curves and the discrete world of integers, and the winding number is the map that carries the topological data into the analytic formulas.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Cauchy's theorem for a disc).** *If $f$ is holomorphic on an open set containing the closed disc $\overline{D(a, r)}$, then $\oint_{|z-a|=r} f(z)\,dz = 0$.*

*Proof.* Let $\gamma(t) = a + re^{2\pi i t}$ for $t \in [0, 1]$. Since $f$ is holomorphic on a neighbourhood of $\overline{D(a, r)}$, it has a power series expansion $f(z) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n (z - a)^n$ converging uniformly on $|z - a| = r$. Then

$$
\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n \oint_\gamma (z-a)^n\,dz.
$$

For $n \neq -1$: $(z-a)^n$ has the antiderivative $(z-a)^{n+1}/(n+1)$ which is single-valued, so the integral over the closed curve vanishes. For $n = -1$: $(1/2\pi i)\oint_\gamma (z-a)^{-1}\,dz = n(\gamma, a) = 1$. But $n = -1$ does not appear in the power series (which starts at $n = 0$), so every term vanishes and the integral is $0$. $\square$

**Proposition (Winding number under composition).** *If $f$ is holomorphic on a neighbourhood of $\gamma([0,1])$ and $f(\gamma(t)) \neq w$ for all $t$, then $n(f \circ \gamma, w) = (1/2\pi i)\oint_\gamma f'(z)/(f(z) - w)\,dz$.*

*Proof.* Parametrise $\gamma$ by $t \in [0, 1]$. Then $(f \circ \gamma)(t) = f(\gamma(t))$ and $(f \circ \gamma)'(t) = f'(\gamma(t)) \cdot \gamma'(t)$. By definition:

$$
n(f \circ \gamma, w) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_0^1 \frac{(f \circ \gamma)'(t)}{(f \circ \gamma)(t) - w}\,dt = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_0^1 \frac{f'(\gamma(t))}{f(\gamma(t)) - w} \cdot \gamma'(t)\,dt.
$$

Substituting $z = \gamma(t)$, $dz = \gamma'(t)\,dt$, this becomes $(1/2\pi i)\oint_\gamma f'(z)/(f(z) - w)\,dz$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Residue theorem [06.01.03].** The winding number is the multiplicity factor in the residue theorem: $\oint_\gamma f(z)\,dz = 2\pi i \sum n(\gamma, a_k) \operatorname{Res}(f, a_k)$. Each singularity contributes its residue weighted by the number of times the contour winds around it. The residue theorem is the direct generalisation of the winding-number integral from $1/(z-a)$ to arbitrary meromorphic functions.

- **Power series and Laurent series [06.01.27].** The winding number integral $(1/2\pi i)\oint_\gamma f(z)/(z-a)^{n+1}\,dz$ extracts the Laurent coefficients of $f$ around $a$. For $n = 0$ this gives the function value via Cauchy's formula; for general $n$ it gives the coefficient $c_n$ in the Laurent expansion. The winding number is the normalising factor that makes these coefficient extractions work.

- **Cauchy integral formula [06.01.02].** Cauchy's integral formula $f(a) \cdot n(\gamma, a) = (1/2\pi i)\oint_\gamma f(z)/(z-a)\,dz$ is the winding-number weighted evaluation formula for holomorphic functions. When $n(\gamma, a) = 1$ (a single counterclockwise encirclement), this reduces to the classical formula. The winding number is the geometric factor that makes the formula correct for contours of arbitrary winding.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Gauss 1813 [ref: TODO_REF Gauss 1813], in work on hypergeometric series, implicitly used the winding number as a topological invariant counting the number of times a curve encircles a point. The concept remained informal in Gauss's work but laid the groundwork for the topological viewpoint. Cauchy 1825 [ref: TODO_REF Cauchy 1825], in his *Memoire sur les integrales definies*, introduced the contour integral and proved Cauchy's theorem, which in its general form relies on the winding number as the multiplicity factor.

The modern formulation, with the winding number defined as $(1/2\pi i)\oint_\gamma dz/(z-a)$ and proved to be integer-valued, is due to the systematic development of complex integration in the late 19th century. The homotopy invariance and the identification of the winding number with the topological degree were crystallised in the work of Brouwer 1911 on the degree of a map. The argument principle and Rouche's theorem, which convert winding numbers into root-counting tools, were established by Rouche 1862 and are the primary computational applications. The canonical modern treatments are Ahlfors *Complex Analysis* Ch. 4 and Conway *Functions of One Complex Variable* Ch. IV [ref: TODO_REF Conway Ch. IV].
