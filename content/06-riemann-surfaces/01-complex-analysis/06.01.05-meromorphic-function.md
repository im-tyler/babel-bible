## Intuition [Beginner]

A holomorphic function is smooth everywhere it is defined. A meromorphic function is almost that good: it is holomorphic except at a few isolated points where it blows up to infinity in a controlled, predictable way. Those points are called poles. The simplest example is $1/z$, which is perfectly well behaved away from the origin and races to infinity as you approach it.

The key word is *controlled*. Near a pole, the function looks like a fixed negative power of distance — like $1/z$ or $1/z^2$ — times something tame. You can read off exactly how fast it blows up: that integer is the order of the pole. So a pole is an infinity you can fully account for.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Look at $f(z) = \dfrac{z+1}{z(z-2)}$. The numerator and denominator are both ordinary polynomials. Wherever the denominator is nonzero the function is a smooth ratio. The only special points are where the denominator vanishes: $z=0$ and $z=2$.

At $z=0$ the function behaves like $\dfrac{0+1}{0 \cdot (0-2)}$, which races to infinity like $1/z$, so $0$ is a pole of order one. At $z=2$ the same thing happens, another order-one pole. Plug in a safe point such as $z=1$: $f(1) = \dfrac{2}{1 \cdot (-1)} = -2$, a perfectly ordinary value.

The lesson: a meromorphic function is a holomorphic function with a short, explicit list of poles, and at each pole you can name exactly how strong the blow-up is.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}$ be open and connected. A function $f$ is **meromorphic** on $\Omega$ if there is a discrete (hence isolated, with no limit point in $\Omega$) set $P \subset \Omega$ such that $f$ is holomorphic on $\Omega \setminus P$ and has a pole at each point of $P$.

A point $a$ is a **pole of order $m \ge 1$** if, in a punctured neighborhood, $f(z) = (z-a)^{-m} g(z)$ with $g$ holomorphic near $a$ and $g(a) \neq 0$; equivalently $(z-a)^m f(z)$ extends holomorphically and nonzero across $a$. Dually, $a$ is a **zero of order $m$** if $f(z) = (z-a)^m g(z)$ with $g$ holomorphic and $g(a) \neq 0$. Both cases are instances of the **local normal form**: near any point, a meromorphic $f$ not identically zero satisfies $f(z) = (z-a)^{k} u(z)$ with $u$ holomorphic, $u(a)\neq 0$, and $k \in \mathbb{Z}$ — positive at a zero, negative at a pole, the **order** $\operatorname{ord}_a f := k$. [ref: TODO_REF Ahlfors — Complex Analysis]

This local normal form is exactly the ratio-of-holomorphic picture: locally $f = g/h$ with $g,h$ holomorphic, $h \not\equiv 0$, since $(z-a)^k u$ is such a ratio. Globally, the meromorphic functions on $\Omega$ form a field $\mathcal{M}(\Omega)$ under pointwise operations — the field of fractions of the integral domain $\mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ of holomorphic functions when $\Omega$ is a domain. [ref: TODO_REF Forster — Lectures on Riemann Surfaces]

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (classification of isolated singularities; Weierstrass).** Let $f$ be holomorphic on a punctured disk $0 < |z-a| < R$. Exactly one of three cases holds. (i) *Removable*: $f$ extends holomorphically across $a$; this happens precisely when $f$ is bounded near $a$ (Riemann's removable-singularity theorem). (ii) *Pole*: $|f(z)| \to \infty$ as $z \to a$; then $f(z)=(z-a)^{-m}u(z)$ with $u$ holomorphic and $u(a)\neq0$ for some $m\ge1$. (iii) *Essential*: neither of the above; the values of $f$ on every punctured neighborhood of $a$ are dense in $\mathbb{C}$ (Casorati 1868, Weierstrass).

**Proof.** *(i) Riemann's theorem.* Suppose $|f| \le M$ near $a$. Set $h(z) = (z-a)^2 f(z)$ for $z\neq a$ and $h(a)=0$. Then $h$ is complex-differentiable at $a$ with $h'(a)=0$ since $|h(z)/(z-a)| = |z-a|\,|f(z)| \le M|z-a| \to 0$, and holomorphic elsewhere, so $h$ is holomorphic on the full disk. Its Taylor series begins at order $\ge 2$: $h(z) = (z-a)^2 g(z)$ with $g$ holomorphic. Hence $f = g$ extends across $a$.

*(ii) Pole.* If $|f| \to \infty$, then $1/f$ is holomorphic and bounded near $a$ with $1/f \to 0$, so by (i) it extends with a zero at $a$ of some order $m$: $1/f = (z-a)^m v$, $v(a)\neq 0$. Inverting, $f = (z-a)^{-m} u$ with $u = 1/v$ holomorphic and $u(a)\neq 0$.

*(iii) Casorati-Weierstrass.* Suppose the singularity is neither removable nor a pole, yet the image $f(\{0<|z-a|<r\})$ were not dense: there exist $w_0\in\mathbb{C}$ and $\delta>0$ with $|f(z)-w_0|\ge\delta$ for all such $z$. Then $\phi(z) = 1/(f(z)-w_0)$ is holomorphic and bounded by $1/\delta$ on the punctured disk, so by (i) it extends holomorphically across $a$. If $\phi(a)\neq0$ then $f = w_0 + 1/\phi$ is bounded near $a$, a removable singularity. If $\phi(a)=0$ then $f - w_0$ has a pole. Either outcome contradicts the assumption, so the image is dense. [ref: TODO_REF Casorati 1868 — Teorica delle funzioni di variabili complesse]

Picard's *great theorem* sharpens density dramatically: on every punctured neighborhood of an essential singularity, $f$ assumes every complex value, with at most one exception, infinitely often — see [06.01.21].

**Bridge.** This classification is the foundational reason a meromorphic function is exactly a holomorphic function whose only singularities are poles: removable singularities are filled in and essential ones are forbidden by definition. The local normal form $f=(z-a)^k u$ proved here **builds toward** the divisor calculus of compact Riemann surfaces, where the integers $\operatorname{ord}_a f$ are bookkept globally; **this is exactly** the data of the divisor $(f)=\sum_a \operatorname{ord}_a(f)\,a$. The same trichotomy **appears again in** [06.01.27] as the structural meaning of the Laurent expansion — finitely many negative terms is a pole, infinitely many is essential — and **putting these together**, the foundational reason the field $\mathcal{M}(\Omega)$ is well behaved is that every local obstruction is one of three named, classified shapes.

**Exercise 3 (medium, short answer).** Classify the singularity of $e^{1/z}$ at $z=0$ and justify your answer.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare the values along $z\to 0^+$ on the real axis versus along the negative real axis.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Essential. Along $z\to0^+$ real, $e^{1/z}\to\infty$; along $z\to0^-$ real, $e^{1/z}\to0$; the limit does not exist and is not uniformly infinite, ruling out removable and pole. The Laurent series $\sum_{n\ge0} z^{-n}/n!$ has infinitely many negative powers.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).** State the Laurent coefficient formula on an annulus $r<|z-a|<R$ and name the contour used.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

It is a Cauchy-type integral over a circle inside the annulus.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$c_n = \dfrac{1}{2\pi i}\displaystyle\oint_{|w-a|=\rho} \dfrac{f(w)}{(w-a)^{n+1}}\,dw$ for any $\rho \in (r,R)$, the contour being a positively oriented circle inside the annulus; $f(z)=\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n (z-a)^n$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short answer).** Why do the meromorphic functions on a domain form a field, while the holomorphic functions form only a ring?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

What is $1/f$ when $f$ has a zero?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $f\not\equiv0$ is meromorphic, its zeros are isolated, and $1/f$ has a pole exactly where $f$ has a zero — still meromorphic. So every nonzero element is invertible: $\mathcal{M}(\Omega)$ is a field. For holomorphic $f$, $1/f$ is not holomorphic at a zero, so $\mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ is only a ring.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short answer).** Prove that if $f$ is holomorphic and bounded on a punctured disk, the singularity is removable — using the Laurent expansion rather than Riemann's auxiliary function.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Bound $|c_n|$ via the coefficient integral over a small circle.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $|f|\le M$, estimate $|c_n| = \left|\tfrac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|w-a|=\rho} f(w)(w-a)^{-n-1}dw\right| \le M\rho^{-n}$. For $n<0$, let $\rho\to0$: $\rho^{-n}=\rho^{|n|}\to0$, forcing $c_n=0$. So no negative powers occur and $f$ extends holomorphically.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).** Reformulate "meromorphic on $\Omega$" as a statement about a map into the Riemann sphere $\widehat{\mathbb{C}}=\mathbb{C}\cup\{\infty\}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Assign the value $\infty$ at each pole and check holomorphy in the chart $1/w$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A meromorphic $f$ is the same as a holomorphic map $F:\Omega\to\widehat{\mathbb{C}}$ that is not identically $\infty$: set $F=f$ off the poles and $F(a)=\infty$ at each pole. In the chart $w\mapsto 1/w$ near $\infty$, $1/F=1/f$ is holomorphic with a zero of order $m$ at a pole of order $m$, so $F$ is holomorphic there.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**The Laurent expansion (Laurent 1843).** The classification of singularities is a corollary of a single existence-and-uniqueness theorem on an annulus, which we now state with proof.

*Theorem.* Let $f$ be holomorphic on the annulus $A = \{\, r < |z-a| < R \,\}$, $0\le r<R\le\infty$. Then $f$ has a unique expansion $f(z) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n (z-a)^n$, converging locally uniformly on $A$, with $c_n = \tfrac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|w-a|=\rho} f(w)(w-a)^{-n-1}\,dw$ for any $\rho\in(r,R)$.

*Proof (the two-circle Cauchy argument).* Fix $z\in A$ and choose radii $r<\rho_1<|z-a|<\rho_2<R$. The Cauchy integral formula on the bounded region between the two circles (whose boundary is the outer circle minus the inner one) gives
$$ f(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|w-a|=\rho_2}\frac{f(w)}{w-z}\,dw \;-\; \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|w-a|=\rho_1}\frac{f(w)}{w-z}\,dw. $$
On the outer circle $|w-a|=\rho_2>|z-a|$, expand $\tfrac{1}{w-z} = \tfrac{1}{(w-a)}\cdot\tfrac{1}{1-\frac{z-a}{w-a}} = \sum_{n\ge0}\tfrac{(z-a)^n}{(w-a)^{n+1}}$, geometric and uniformly convergent; integrating term by term yields $\sum_{n\ge0} c_n(z-a)^n$ with the stated $c_n$. On the inner circle $|w-a|=\rho_1<|z-a|$, expand instead $\tfrac{-1}{w-z}=\tfrac{1}{(z-a)}\cdot\tfrac{1}{1-\frac{w-a}{z-a}} = \sum_{m\ge0}\tfrac{(w-a)^m}{(z-a)^{m+1}}$; integrating gives $\sum_{n\le-1} c_n(z-a)^n$. Summing the two pieces produces the bilateral series.

*Independence of $\rho$ and uniqueness.* The integrand $f(w)(w-a)^{-n-1}$ is holomorphic on $A$, so by Cauchy's theorem the coefficient integral is the same for every $\rho\in(r,R)$. For uniqueness, if $\sum c_n(z-a)^n = \sum c_n'(z-a)^n$ on $A$, the difference is a convergent bilateral series equal to zero; multiplying by $(z-a)^{-k-1}$ and integrating over a circle (term-by-term, valid by local uniform convergence) isolates $2\pi i\,(c_k - c_k') = 0$, since $\oint (z-a)^{j}\,dz = 2\pi i\,\delta_{j,-1}$. [ref: TODO_REF Laurent 1843 — Extension du theoreme de Cauchy]

The classification now reads off the **principal part** $\sum_{n<0}c_n(z-a)^n$: empty means removable, finitely many terms (lowest index $-m$) means a pole of order $m$, infinitely many means essential. This is precisely the analytic content of the Casorati-Weierstrass and Picard theorems.

**Meromorphic functions as maps to the sphere.** Sending each pole to $\infty\in\widehat{\mathbb{C}}$ realizes any meromorphic $f:\Omega\to\widehat{\mathbb{C}}$ as a holomorphic map of complex manifolds, where $\widehat{\mathbb{C}}\cong\mathbb{P}^1$ carries charts $z$ and $1/w$. On a *compact* Riemann surface $X$ this is decisive: the only globally holomorphic functions are constants, but $\mathcal{M}(X)$ is a rich field — a finite extension of $\mathbb{C}(z)$ — and nonconstant elements are branched covers $X\to\mathbb{P}^1$. [ref: TODO_REF Forster — Lectures on Riemann Surfaces]

**Synthesis.** The local normal form, the Laurent expansion, and the trichotomy of singularities are one theorem viewed three ways, and **putting these together** gives the structural identity *meromorphic = holomorphic map to the sphere*. **This is exactly** the bridge from analysis to geometry: the integer orders $\operatorname{ord}_a f$ that the normal form supplies are the local data, and assembling them into the divisor $(f)=\sum_a\operatorname{ord}_a(f)\,a$ **generalises** the elementary count "zeros minus poles" into the divisor-and-line-bundle dictionary of compact curves. **The central insight** is that allowing poles — and only poles — converts the rigid ring $\mathcal{O}(\Omega)$ into the field $\mathcal{M}(\Omega)$, and the foundational reason the global theory of compact Riemann surfaces works is that this field is large enough to separate points and embed the surface, **which is dual to** the topological fact that the divisor of a global meromorphic function on a compact surface has degree zero. The story **builds toward** Riemann-Roch [06.04.01], which counts meromorphic functions with prescribed poles, and the Mittag-Leffler [06.01.18] and Weierstrass-factorization [06.01.17] theorems, which build meromorphic functions to order from prescribed principal parts and zeros.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (degree-zero divisor and the field structure).** Let $f,g\in\mathcal{M}(\Omega)$ with $g\not\equiv0$ on a domain $\Omega$. Then $f/g\in\mathcal{M}(\Omega)$, and for any point $a$, $\operatorname{ord}_a(f/g)=\operatorname{ord}_a f - \operatorname{ord}_a g$ and $\operatorname{ord}_a(fg)=\operatorname{ord}_a f + \operatorname{ord}_a g$.

*Proof.* Near $a$ write the local normal forms $f=(z-a)^{j}u$ and $g=(z-a)^{k}v$ with $u,v$ holomorphic units ($u(a),v(a)\neq0$). Then $fg=(z-a)^{j+k}(uv)$ with $uv$ a holomorphic unit, establishing additivity of order under products and showing $fg$ is meromorphic. Likewise $f/g = (z-a)^{j-k}(u/v)$, and $u/v$ is holomorphic and nonzero near $a$ because $v(a)\neq0$; hence $f/g$ has local normal form with order $j-k$ and is meromorphic. Since $a$ was arbitrary and the zeros of $g$ are isolated (as $g\not\equiv0$), $f/g$ has only isolated poles. The order map $\operatorname{ord}_a:\mathcal{M}(\Omega)^\times\to\mathbb{Z}$ is thus a group homomorphism for each $a$, which is the local source of the divisor homomorphism $f\mapsto(f)$ and, in the compact case, of the degree-zero relation $\deg(f)=0$.

This proposition is the engine behind the field axioms for $\mathcal{M}(\Omega)$: closure under inverse is the case $f\equiv1$, giving $\operatorname{ord}_a(1/g)=-\operatorname{ord}_a g$, so $1/g$ is meromorphic whenever $g\not\equiv0$. [ref: TODO_REF Conway — Functions of One Complex Variable I]

## Connections [Master]

- [06.01.01] supplies holomorphy and the Taylor expansion that the local normal form $f=(z-a)^k u$ refines; a meromorphic function is built from holomorphic units times integer powers, so it is the smallest extension of the holomorphic class admitting controlled infinities.

- [06.01.27] develops the Laurent and power-series expansion in full; this unit proves the annulus existence-and-uniqueness theorem and reads the singularity trichotomy off the principal part, so the two units share the bilateral-series machinery.

- [06.01.21] is Picard's great theorem, the dramatic sharpening of the Casorati-Weierstrass density statement proved here: near an essential singularity every value but at most one is taken infinitely often, while [06.01.20] gives Picard's little theorem for entire functions.

- [06.01.18] (Mittag-Leffler) and [06.01.17] (Weierstrass factorization) are the converse constructions — building meromorphic functions with prescribed principal parts and zeros — and [06.04.01] (Riemann-Roch) counts meromorphic functions with poles bounded by a divisor.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Cauchy's residue calculus made the behavior of functions at isolated singularities computable, but the clean trichotomy was the work of Weierstrass, who classified isolated singularities into removable, polar, and essential types, and of Casorati, whose 1868 *Teorica delle funzioni di variabili complesse* established that the image of a function near an essential singularity is dense — the result now bearing both their names. [ref: TODO_REF Casorati 1868 — Teorica delle funzioni di variabili complesse] The expansion that organizes all three cases was published by Pierre Alphonse Laurent in 1843 as an extension of Cauchy's theorem to an annulus, the bilateral series carrying negative powers being the decisive innovation. [ref: TODO_REF Laurent 1843 — Extension du theoreme de Cauchy] Riemann's 1851 dissertation reframed the whole subject: multivalued analytic expressions live on their natural surfaces, and the meromorphic functions on a compact surface form the finite-dimensional algebra encoding its geometry. The modern viewpoint — meromorphic functions as holomorphic maps to the Riemann sphere, the field $\mathcal{M}(X)$ as the function field of an algebraic curve — is the synthesis of Riemann's geometry with the Weierstrass-Casorati-Laurent local theory, presented in Ahlfors and Forster. [ref: TODO_REF Forster — Lectures on Riemann Surfaces]
