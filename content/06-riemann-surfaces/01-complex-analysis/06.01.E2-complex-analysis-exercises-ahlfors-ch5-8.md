## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

This pack supplements the second half of Ahlfors: the residue calculus pushed into real-integral evaluation and root counting, the theory of entire and meromorphic functions through infinite products and the gamma function, harmonic-function theory and the Dirichlet problem, the Riemann mapping theorem by normal families, and analytic continuation with the monodromy theorem. Its problems exercise the units on the residue theorem [06.01.03], the argument principle and Rouché [06.01.13], harmonic functions on the plane [06.01.11], the Riemann mapping theorem [06.01.06], analytic continuation [06.01.04], the Weierstrass factorization theorem [06.01.17], the gamma function [06.01.15], and normal families and Montel's theorem [06.01.14].

The pack collects ten problems — two easy, five medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units rather than as a standalone development. The problems are grouped by theme: residue and Rouché root-counting (easy/medium), gamma-function and infinite-product identities (medium), harmonic functions and the Poisson kernel (medium), and the Riemann mapping / monodromy circle of ideas (hard). Conventions follow Ahlfors: the argument principle counts $Z - P = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint\frac{f'}{f}\,dz = n(f\circ\gamma, 0)$ (zeros minus poles inside $\gamma$); the gamma function is normalised by $\Gamma(z+1) = z\Gamma(z)$, $\Gamma(1) = 1$; the Poisson kernel on the unit disc is $P_r(\theta) = \frac{1 - r^2}{1 - 2r\cos\theta + r^2}$.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one problem in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining nine follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead problem.** *Prove the reflection formula $\displaystyle\Gamma(z)\Gamma(1-z) = \frac{\pi}{\sin\pi z}$ for $0 < \operatorname{Re} z < 1$, assuming the Weierstrass product for $\Gamma$ and the product for $\sin$.*

**Solution.** Use the Weierstrass product
$$
\frac{1}{\Gamma(z)} = z\,e^{\gamma z}\prod_{n=1}^\infty\left(1 + \frac{z}{n}\right)e^{-z/n},
$$
where $\gamma$ is the Euler-Mascheroni constant. Replacing $z$ by $-z$,
$$
\frac{1}{\Gamma(-z)} = -z\,e^{-\gamma z}\prod_{n=1}^\infty\left(1 - \frac{z}{n}\right)e^{z/n}.
$$
Multiply the two products. The $e^{\pm\gamma z}$ factors cancel and the $e^{\mp z/n}$ factors cancel term by term, leaving
$$
\frac{1}{\Gamma(z)\,\Gamma(-z)} = -z^2\prod_{n=1}^\infty\left(1 - \frac{z^2}{n^2}\right).
$$
Now invoke the Euler product for the sine, $\sin\pi z = \pi z\prod_{n=1}^\infty\left(1 - \frac{z^2}{n^2}\right)$, so $\prod_{n=1}^\infty\left(1 - \frac{z^2}{n^2}\right) = \frac{\sin\pi z}{\pi z}$. Substituting,
$$
\frac{1}{\Gamma(z)\,\Gamma(-z)} = -z^2\cdot\frac{\sin\pi z}{\pi z} = -\frac{z\sin\pi z}{\pi}.
$$
Finally use the functional equation $\Gamma(1 - z) = -z\,\Gamma(-z)$, i.e. $\Gamma(-z) = -\Gamma(1-z)/z$. Then
$$
\frac{1}{\Gamma(z)\,\Gamma(-z)} = \frac{1}{\Gamma(z)\cdot(-\Gamma(1-z)/z)} = -\frac{z}{\Gamma(z)\Gamma(1-z)}.
$$
Equating the two expressions, $-\frac{z}{\Gamma(z)\Gamma(1-z)} = -\frac{z\sin\pi z}{\pi}$, hence
$$
\Gamma(z)\,\Gamma(1-z) = \frac{\pi}{\sin\pi z}.\qquad\square
$$

The reflection formula is the keystone identity of the gamma function: setting $z = \tfrac12$ recovers $\Gamma(\tfrac12) = \sqrt\pi$, and combined with the duplication formula it pins down $\Gamma$ on the whole plane. This is Ahlfors §5.2.4. The mechanism — multiply two Weierstrass products and match against the sine product — is the workhorse of classical special-function manipulation.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).** *Counting zeros with Rouché.*

How many zeros (with multiplicity) does $p(z) = z^4 - 5z + 1$ have inside the unit disc $|z| < 1$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On $|z| = 1$, compare $p$ with the dominant term $-5z$. Rouché: if $|f - g| < |g|$ on the boundary, then $f$ and $g$ have the same number of zeros inside.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take $g(z) = -5z$ and $f(z) = p(z) = z^4 - 5z + 1$, so $f - g = z^4 + 1$. On $|z| = 1$,

$$
|f(z) - g(z)| = |z^4 + 1|\leq |z|^4 + 1 = 2, \qquad |g(z)| = 5|z| = 5.
$$

Since $2 < 5$, Rouché's theorem applies: $p$ and $-5z$ have the same number of zeros inside $|z| < 1$. The function $-5z$ has exactly one zero there (at $z = 0$, simple). Therefore $p$ has exactly **one** zero in the unit disc.

(The other three zeros of the quartic lie outside; one can check $|p(z)|$ is dominated by $z^4$ on a large circle, accounting for all four roots.) Ahlfors §4.4.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *A gamma-function value.*

Using $\Gamma(z+1) = z\Gamma(z)$ and $\Gamma(\tfrac12) = \sqrt\pi$, compute $\Gamma(\tfrac72)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the recursion three times: $\Gamma(\tfrac72) = \tfrac52\Gamma(\tfrac52) = \tfrac52\cdot\tfrac32\Gamma(\tfrac32) = \cdots$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Apply $\Gamma(z+1) = z\Gamma(z)$ repeatedly down to $\Gamma(\tfrac12)$:

$$
\Gamma(\tfrac72) = \tfrac52\,\Gamma(\tfrac52) = \tfrac52\cdot\tfrac32\,\Gamma(\tfrac32) = \tfrac52\cdot\tfrac32\cdot\tfrac12\,\Gamma(\tfrac12) = \frac{15}{8}\sqrt\pi.
$$

So $\Gamma(\tfrac72) = \dfrac{15\sqrt\pi}{8}$. The half-integer values of $\Gamma$ are always rational multiples of $\sqrt\pi$, which is why they appear in the volumes of spheres and Gaussian moment integrals. Ahlfors §5.2.4.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).** *A real integral with a simple pole on the contour, via the argument that a sine integral converges.*

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{\sin x}{x}\,dx$ using a contour with a small indentation around the origin.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider $\oint\frac{e^{iz}}{z}\,dz$ over the contour $[-R,-\varepsilon]\cup C_\varepsilon\cup[\varepsilon,R]\cup C_R$, with $C_\varepsilon$ a small semicircle above the origin and $C_R$ the large upper semicircle. The integrand is holomorphic inside, so the total is $0$; take imaginary parts.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $f(z) = e^{iz}/z$. Over the indented contour (real axis from $-R$ to $-\varepsilon$, small upper semicircle $C_\varepsilon$ around $0$ traversed clockwise, real axis from $\varepsilon$ to $R$, large upper semicircle $C_R$), $f$ is holomorphic inside, so by Cauchy's theorem the total integral is $0$.

On the large semicircle $C_R$, by Jordan's lemma (the $e^{iz}$ factor decays in the upper half-plane), $\int_{C_R}f\,dz\to 0$ as $R\to\infty$.

On the small semicircle $C_\varepsilon$ (radius $\varepsilon$, clockwise, half-turn around the simple pole at $0$ with residue $\operatorname*{Res}_{0}f = e^{i0} = 1$), the standard small-arc lemma gives $\int_{C_\varepsilon}f\,dz\to -\pi i\cdot 1 = -\pi i$ as $\varepsilon\to 0$ (the minus sign because $C_\varepsilon$ is traversed clockwise, the factor $\pi i$ because it is a half-circle).

The two real pieces combine to the principal value $\mathrm{p.v.}\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{e^{ix}}{x}\,dx$. Summing the four contributions to zero:

$$
\mathrm{p.v.}\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{e^{ix}}{x}\,dx - \pi i = 0 \implies \mathrm{p.v.}\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{e^{ix}}{x}\,dx = \pi i.
$$

Taking imaginary parts ($\operatorname{Im} e^{ix} = \sin x$, and $\sin x/x$ is even with no singularity, so the principal value equals the ordinary integral):

$$
\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{\sin x}{x}\,dx = \operatorname{Im}(\pi i) = \pi.
$$

The indentation technique is the residue toolkit's handling of poles on the contour: a half-circle around a simple pole contributes $\pm\pi i\cdot\text{Res}$, half of a full $2\pi i\cdot\text{Res}$. Ahlfors §4.5.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *Hurwitz's theorem from Rouché.*

Let $f_n\to f$ uniformly on compact subsets of a domain $\Omega$, with each $f_n$ holomorphic and nowhere zero. Show that $f$ is either identically zero or nowhere zero.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Suppose $f\not\equiv 0$ but $f(z_0) = 0$. Zeros of holomorphic functions are isolated, so on a small circle $|z - z_0| = r$, $f$ is bounded below by some $m > 0$. Apply Rouché to $f$ and $f_n$ on that circle.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $f\not\equiv 0$. The limit $f$ is holomorphic (uniform limits of holomorphic functions on compacts are holomorphic). If $f$ had a zero at $z_0$, then since the zeros of a nonzero holomorphic function are isolated, choose $r > 0$ so small that $f$ has no zero on the punctured disc $0 < |z - z_0|\leq r$; in particular $z_0$ is the only zero in $|z - z_0|\leq r$. On the circle $C: |z - z_0| = r$, $f$ is continuous and nonzero, so $m := \min_{C}|f| > 0$.

By uniform convergence on the compact set $C$, choose $N$ so that $|f_n - f| < m\leq |f|$ on $C$ for all $n\geq N$. By Rouché's theorem, $f_n$ and $f$ have the same number of zeros inside $C$. But $f$ has at least one zero there (at $z_0$), while $f_n$ has none (each $f_n$ is nowhere zero) — contradiction.

Therefore $f$ has no zero, i.e. $f$ is nowhere zero. So $f$ is either identically zero or nowhere zero. This is Hurwitz's theorem; it is the tool that guarantees the Riemann mapping limit is injective (a uniform limit of injective holomorphic functions is injective or constant). Ahlfors §4.4, §6.1.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).** *The Poisson integral solves the Dirichlet problem on the disc.*

Let $u(re^{i\theta}) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} P_r(\theta - t)\,f(t)\,dt$ with $P_r(\theta) = \frac{1 - r^2}{1 - 2r\cos\theta + r^2}$ and $f$ continuous on the circle. Show $u$ is harmonic in the disc.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$P_r(\theta) = \operatorname{Re}\frac{1 + z}{1 - z}$ with $z = re^{i\theta}$. So $u$ is the real part of a holomorphic function of $z$, hence harmonic.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $z = re^{i\theta}$. The Poisson kernel is the real part of the Herglotz kernel:

$$
\operatorname{Re}\frac{1 + ze^{-it}}{1 - ze^{-it}} = \frac{1 - |z|^2}{|1 - ze^{-it}|^2} = \frac{1 - r^2}{1 - 2r\cos(\theta - t) + r^2} = P_r(\theta - t),
$$

using $|1 - ze^{-it}|^2 = 1 - 2r\cos(\theta - t) + r^2$.

Therefore

$$
u(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\operatorname{Re}\!\left(\frac{1 + ze^{-it}}{1 - ze^{-it}}\right) f(t)\,dt = \operatorname{Re}\,F(z), \quad F(z) := \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1 + ze^{-it}}{1 - ze^{-it}}\,f(t)\,dt.
$$

For each fixed $t$, the integrand $\frac{1 + ze^{-it}}{1 - ze^{-it}}$ is holomorphic in $z$ on the disc $|z| < 1$ (its only singularity is at $z = e^{it}$, on the boundary). Differentiating under the integral sign (justified by uniform bounds on compact subdiscs) shows $F$ is holomorphic. The real part of a holomorphic function is harmonic, so $u = \operatorname{Re} F$ satisfies $\Delta u = 0$ in the disc.

That $u$ attains the boundary values $f$ continuously is the approximate-identity property of $P_r$; together these solve the Dirichlet problem on the disc. Ahlfors §6.3. The general-domain version comes via Perron's method [06.01.24].

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).** *Mean-value property and the maximum principle.*

Show that a harmonic function $u$ on a domain satisfies the mean-value property $u(a) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} u(a + re^{i\theta})\,d\theta$ for every disc $\overline{D(a,r)}\subset\Omega$, and deduce the maximum principle.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Locally $u = \operatorname{Re} f$ for a holomorphic $f$; apply the Cauchy integral formula to $f$ at the center and take real parts. For the maximum principle, an interior maximum would force $u$ constant on a disc, then on all of $\Omega$ by connectedness.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Mean-value.* On the simply-connected disc $D(a,R)\supset\overline{D(a,r)}$, $u$ has a harmonic conjugate $v$, so $f = u + iv$ is holomorphic there. By Cauchy's integral formula at the center,

$$
f(a) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z-a|=r}\frac{f(z)}{z-a}\,dz = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} f(a + re^{i\theta})\,d\theta,
$$

parametrising $z = a + re^{i\theta}$, $dz = ire^{i\theta}d\theta$. Taking real parts gives the mean-value property for $u$.

*Maximum principle.* Suppose $u$ attains a maximum $M$ at an interior point $a$. By the mean-value property over any small circle, $M = u(a) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}u(a + re^{i\theta})\,d\theta$. Since each $u(a + re^{i\theta})\leq M$ and the average equals $M$, the integrand must equal $M$ for all $\theta$ (a continuous function $\leq M$ with average $M$ is identically $M$). So $u\equiv M$ on a disc about $a$. The set $\{u = M\}$ is then open; it is also closed in $\Omega$ (preimage of a point under continuous $u$), so by connectedness $u\equiv M$ on all of $\Omega$.

Hence a non-constant harmonic function has no interior maximum (or minimum, applying the result to $-u$). Ahlfors §6.3.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (medium, proof).** *Schwarz lemma and automorphisms of the disc.*

Show that every holomorphic bijection $\varphi:\mathbb{D}\to\mathbb{D}$ of the unit disc has the form $\varphi(z) = e^{i\alpha}\dfrac{z - a}{1 - \bar a z}$ for some $\alpha\in\mathbb{R}$ and $a\in\mathbb{D}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Let $a = \varphi^{-1}(0)$ and $\psi_a(z) = \frac{z - a}{1 - \bar a z}$, a disc automorphism with $\psi_a(a) = 0$. Then $g = \varphi\circ\psi_a^{-1}$ fixes $0$; apply the Schwarz lemma to $g$ and to $g^{-1}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Blaschke factor $\psi_a(z) = \frac{z - a}{1 - \bar a z}$ is a holomorphic bijection of $\mathbb{D}$ (it maps $\partial\mathbb{D}$ to itself since $|\psi_a(z)| = 1$ for $|z| = 1$, and $\psi_a(a) = 0$, $a\in\mathbb{D}$). Let $a = \varphi^{-1}(0)$ and set $g = \varphi\circ\psi_a^{-1}$. Then $g:\mathbb{D}\to\mathbb{D}$ is a holomorphic bijection with $g(0) = \varphi(\psi_a^{-1}(0)) = \varphi(a) = 0$.

Apply the Schwarz lemma to $g$: since $g(0) = 0$ and $g:\mathbb{D}\to\mathbb{D}$, $|g(z)|\leq|z|$. Apply it also to $g^{-1}$ (which fixes $0$ and maps $\mathbb{D}\to\mathbb{D}$): $|g^{-1}(w)|\leq|w|$, i.e. $|z|\leq|g(z)|$. Combining, $|g(z)| = |z|$ for all $z$. The equality case of the Schwarz lemma forces $g(z) = e^{i\alpha}z$ for some real $\alpha$.

Therefore $\varphi = g\circ\psi_a$, i.e.

$$
\varphi(z) = e^{i\alpha}\,\psi_a(z) = e^{i\alpha}\,\frac{z - a}{1 - \bar a z}.
$$

This is the full automorphism group $\operatorname{Aut}(\mathbb{D})$, a three-real-parameter group ($\alpha$ and $a\in\mathbb{D}$). It is the holomorphic isometry group of the Poincaré disc, the model behind the Schwarz-Pick theorem. Ahlfors §6.1-6.2.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *The Riemann mapping theorem: extracting the limit.*

In the proof of the Riemann mapping theorem one considers the family $\mathcal F$ of injective holomorphic $f:\Omega\to\mathbb{D}$ with $f(z_0) = 0$, and a maximising sequence $f_n$ with $|f_n'(z_0)|\to\sup_{\mathcal F}|f'(z_0)|$. Show that a subsequence converges, locally uniformly, to an injective $f\in\mathcal F$ achieving the supremum.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathcal F$ is uniformly bounded (into $\mathbb{D}$), hence normal by Montel's theorem [06.01.14]. Extract a locally-uniformly-convergent subsequence; use Hurwitz to keep injectivity and Cauchy's estimate to pass $|f_n'(z_0)|$ to the limit.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Every $f\in\mathcal F$ maps into $\mathbb{D}$, so $\mathcal F$ is uniformly bounded by $1$ on $\Omega$. By Montel's theorem [06.01.14], $\mathcal F$ is a normal family: the maximising sequence $f_n$ has a subsequence (rename it $f_n$) converging locally uniformly to a holomorphic $f:\Omega\to\overline{\mathbb{D}}$.

*The limit lands in $\mathcal F$.* By Cauchy's estimate / uniform convergence of derivatives, $f_n'(z_0)\to f'(z_0)$, so $|f'(z_0)| = \sup_{\mathcal F}|f'(z_0)| =: S$. Because the supremum $S$ is positive (e.g. a Möbius map into the disc gives a competitor with nonzero derivative), $f'(z_0)\neq 0$, so $f$ is nonconstant. Also $f(z_0) = \lim f_n(z_0) = 0$.

*Injectivity.* Each $f_n$ is injective. Fix any $w_0\in\Omega$ and apply Hurwitz's theorem (Exercise 4) to $g_n(z) := f_n(z) - f_n(w_0)$ on $\Omega\setminus\{w_0\}$: each $g_n$ is nowhere zero there (injectivity), so the limit $g(z) = f(z) - f(w_0)$ is either nowhere zero on $\Omega\setminus\{w_0\}$ or identically zero. The latter would make $f$ constant, excluded above. Hence $f(z)\neq f(w_0)$ for $z\neq w_0$; as $w_0$ was arbitrary, $f$ is injective.

*Range.* The maximum modulus principle gives $|f| < 1$ in the open $\Omega$ (an interior boundary value $|f| = 1$ would violate it), so $f$ maps into $\mathbb{D}$. Thus $f\in\mathcal F$ and achieves the supremum $S$.

A separate argument (the "square-root trick") shows this extremal $f$ is onto $\mathbb{D}$, completing the Riemann mapping theorem. This exercise isolates the compactness core: normal family ⟹ convergent subsequence, Hurwitz ⟹ injective limit. Ahlfors §6.1.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *The monodromy theorem.*

Let $\Omega$ be simply connected and suppose a holomorphic germ at $z_0\in\Omega$ can be analytically continued along every path in $\Omega$. Show the continuations agree, defining a single-valued holomorphic function on $\Omega$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Two paths with the same endpoints are homotopic in a simply-connected domain. Show continuation is invariant under homotopy: the set of $t$ for which the continuations along the homotoped paths agree is open and closed.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix the germ $f_{z_0}$ at $z_0$. By hypothesis it continues along every path; we must show the result of continuing to a point $w$ is independent of the path from $z_0$ to $w$.

Let $\gamma_0,\gamma_1$ be two paths from $z_0$ to $w$ in $\Omega$. Since $\Omega$ is simply connected, they are homotopic rel endpoints: there is $H:[0,1]\times[0,1]\to\Omega$ with $H(\cdot,0) = \gamma_0$, $H(\cdot,1) = \gamma_1$, $H(0,s) = z_0$, $H(1,s) = w$. Let $f_s$ denote the germ at $w$ obtained by continuing $f_{z_0}$ along $H(\cdot,s)$.

Claim: $s\mapsto f_s$ is locally constant, hence constant. Fix $s_0$. Continuation along $H(\cdot,s_0)$ is realised by a finite chain of overlapping discs on which power-series elements agree. For $s$ near $s_0$, the path $H(\cdot,s)$ stays inside the same chain of discs (uniform continuity of $H$ on the compact square, Lebesgue number argument), so it produces the *same* terminal germ at $w$: $f_s = f_{s_0}$. Thus the set $\{s : f_s = f_{s_0}\}$ is open; the same argument shows its complement is open, so by connectedness of $[0,1]$ it is all of $[0,1]$. Hence $f_0 = f_1$: continuation along $\gamma_0$ and $\gamma_1$ give the same germ at $w$.

Therefore the value at $w$ is path-independent. Defining $F(w)$ as the common germ's value at $w$ for each $w\in\Omega$ yields a single-valued function, holomorphic because it is locally given by the power-series elements. This is the monodromy theorem; its standard application is constructing a global $\log$ or $\sqrt{\cdot}$ on any simply-connected domain avoiding the relevant branch points. Ahlfors §8.1.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 10 (hard, numeric).** *Counting zeros of $e^z - 3z$ in the unit disc by the argument principle.*

How many zeros does $f(z) = e^z - 3z$ have inside $|z| < 1$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare with $g(z) = -3z$ on $|z| = 1$ using Rouché: $|f - g| = |e^z|\leq e^{\operatorname{Re} z}\leq e$ on the circle, while $|g| = 3$. But $e\approx 2.718 < 3$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Set $g(z) = -3z$, so $f(z) - g(z) = e^z$. On the unit circle $|z| = 1$, write $z = x + iy$ with $x^2 + y^2 = 1$, so $x\in[-1,1]$. Then

$$
|f(z) - g(z)| = |e^z| = e^{\operatorname{Re} z} = e^{x}\leq e^{1} = e\approx 2.718,
$$

while $|g(z)| = 3|z| = 3$. Since $e < 3$, we have $|f - g| < |g|$ on $|z| = 1$, so Rouché's theorem gives: $f$ and $g$ have the same number of zeros inside the unit disc.

The function $g(z) = -3z$ has exactly one zero inside $|z| < 1$ (at $z = 0$, simple). Therefore $f(z) = e^z - 3z$ has exactly **one** zero in the unit disc.

(Numerically, the equation $e^x = 3x$ has a real root near $x\approx 0.619$, confirming a real zero in $(0,1)$; Rouché shows it is the only one in the whole disc.) This is the argument principle in its Rouché packaging: count zeros of the perturbation by counting zeros of the dominant term. Ahlfors §4.4.
