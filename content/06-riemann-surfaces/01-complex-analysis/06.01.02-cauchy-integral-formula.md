## Intuition [Beginner]

Stand at a point $a$ inside a region where a holomorphic function $f$ lives. Walk around $a$ on a small circle and, as you go, average the values of $f$ you meet. The astonishing fact is that this average is exactly $f(a)$, the value back at the center. Boundary data, blended evenly, reconstructs the interior.

This works because holomorphic functions have no local "lumps." They obey a rigid balance: whatever $f$ does on one side of $a$ is compensated on the opposite side. So smoothing $f$ over a full loop cancels all the variation and leaves only the central value.

The Cauchy integral formula makes this precise. It says the value at any interior point is a weighted loop integral of the boundary values, with a weight that counts how the loop winds around $a$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take $f(z) = z^2 + 3$ and the point $a = 0$. Sample $f$ on the unit circle, writing points as $z = \cos t + i\sin t$ for $t$ from $0$ to one full turn. Then $z^2$ traces a circle that wraps around twice as $z$ goes around once, but it is centered at $0$, so its average over a full loop is $0$. The constant $3$ averages to $3$.

So the loop average of $f$ is $0 + 3 = 3$, which is exactly $f(0) = 0 + 3 = 3$. The center value reappeared.

What this shows: the wiggly part $z^2$ averages away because it is balanced around the center, and only the value $f(0)$ survives. This is the whole formula in miniature.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $f$ be holomorphic on an open set $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{C}$. Recall (06.01.01) that holomorphic means complex-differentiable at every point of $\Omega$. The **Cauchy integral formula** on a closed disc states: if $\overline{D(a_0,r)} \subset \Omega$ and $\gamma$ is its positively oriented boundary circle, then for every $a$ in the open disc,

$$f(a) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f(\zeta)}{\zeta - a}\,d\zeta. \qquad [\text{ref: TODO\_REF Ahlfors §4.1-4.2}]$$

The kernel $(\zeta - a)^{-1}$ is the **Cauchy kernel**. The **winding number** (index) of a closed curve $\gamma$ about a point $a \notin \gamma$ is

$$n(\gamma, a) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{d\zeta}{\zeta - a} \in \mathbb{Z},$$

an integer counting signed revolutions (06.01.28). The general **homological form** weights the boundary value by this index: if $\gamma$ is a cycle null-homologous in $\Omega$ (meaning $n(\gamma, w) = 0$ for all $w \notin \Omega$), then $n(\gamma,a)\,f(a) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f(\zeta)}{\zeta - a}\,d\zeta$ for all $a \in \Omega \setminus \gamma$. A **cycle** is a finite integer combination of closed curves; integrals over it are the corresponding sums.

This representation is invariant under biholomorphic change of coordinate: pulling $f$ and $\gamma$ back through a holomorphic chart change carries the formula to itself, which is why it transports verbatim onto charts of a Riemann surface.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Goursat's triangle theorem, the engine of Cauchy's theorem).** Let $f$ be holomorphic on an open set $\Omega$ and let $T \subset \Omega$ be a solid triangle. Then $\oint_{\partial T} f(\zeta)\,d\zeta = 0$. No assumption on continuity of $f'$ is needed.

**Proof (Goursat's bisection argument).** Write $I = \oint_{\partial T} f\,d\zeta$ and set $|I| = \eta$. Join the midpoints of the three sides of $T$, splitting it into four congruent sub-triangles $T^{(1)}, T^{(2)}, T^{(3)}, T^{(4)}$, each similar to $T$ at half scale. The interior midpoint edges are each traversed once in both directions when we sum the four boundary integrals, so the inner contributions cancel and

$$\oint_{\partial T} f\,d\zeta = \sum_{j=1}^4 \oint_{\partial T^{(j)}} f\,d\zeta.$$

At least one sub-triangle, call it $T_1$, satisfies $\bigl|\oint_{\partial T_1} f\,d\zeta\bigr| \ge \eta/4$. Bisect $T_1$ the same way to get $T_2$ with $\bigl|\oint_{\partial T_2}\bigr| \ge \eta/16$, and iterate. This produces nested triangles $T \supset T_1 \supset T_2 \supset \cdots$ with $\bigl|\oint_{\partial T_n} f\,d\zeta\bigr| \ge \eta/4^n$. The perimeter and diameter halve each step: $\operatorname{diam}(T_n) = 2^{-n}\operatorname{diam}(T)$ and $\operatorname{length}(\partial T_n) = 2^{-n}\operatorname{length}(\partial T)$.

By completeness the nested compact triangles shrink to a single point $z_* \in T$. Holomorphy at $z_*$ gives, for any $\varepsilon > 0$, a radius within which

$$f(\zeta) = f(z_*) + f'(z_*)(\zeta - z_*) + R(\zeta), \qquad |R(\zeta)| \le \varepsilon\,|\zeta - z_*|.$$

The affine part $f(z_*) + f'(z_*)(\zeta - z_*)$ has an exact primitive, so its integral over any closed polygon is $0$. Thus $\oint_{\partial T_n} f\,d\zeta = \oint_{\partial T_n} R(\zeta)\,d\zeta$. For $n$ large enough that $T_n$ lies inside the $\varepsilon$-radius, the standard length estimate gives

$$\Bigl|\oint_{\partial T_n} R\,d\zeta\Bigr| \le \varepsilon \cdot \operatorname{diam}(T_n)\cdot \operatorname{length}(\partial T_n) = \varepsilon\, 4^{-n}\, \operatorname{diam}(T)\,\operatorname{length}(\partial T).$$

Combining with the lower bound $\eta/4^n \le |\oint_{\partial T_n} f|$ and canceling $4^{-n}$ yields $\eta \le \varepsilon\,\operatorname{diam}(T)\,\operatorname{length}(\partial T)$. Since $\varepsilon$ is arbitrary, $\eta = 0$, so $I = 0$. $\blacksquare$ [ref: TODO_REF Goursat 1900 Sur la definition generale des fonctions analytiques Trans AMS 1]

From the triangle theorem one builds a primitive on any disc (integrate along axis-parallel paths), obtains $\oint_\gamma f = 0$ on the disc, and then derives the disc Cauchy formula by applying the vanishing integral to the holomorphic function $g(\zeta) = (f(\zeta) - f(a))/(\zeta - a)$, defined at $a$ by $g(a) = f'(a)$, leaving $f(a)\cdot n(\gamma,a)\cdot 2\pi i = \oint_\gamma f/(\zeta - a)\,d\zeta$ with $n(\gamma,a) = 1$ for the boundary circle.

**Bridge.** The bisection argument here *builds toward* the homological Cauchy theorem proved in the full set below, and the same vanishing-integral mechanism *appears again in* the residue theorem (06.01.03), where punctures replace the smooth interior. **The foundational reason** the formula is so powerful is that it converts a two-dimensional interior problem into a one-dimensional boundary integral; **this is exactly** the reduction that **generalises** to higher derivatives and to power-series expansion. **Putting these together**, Goursat's local cancellation is the structural seed from which analyticity, Liouville's theorem, and the entire residue calculus of the strand grow.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Cauchy estimates and Liouville.** Apply the higher-derivative formula on the circle $|\zeta - a| = r$. If $|f| \le M$ there, the length estimate gives $|f^{(n)}(a)| \le n!\,M/r^n$. For a bounded *entire* function ($M$ fixed, $r$ arbitrary), letting $r \to \infty$ forces $f'(a) = 0$ at every $a$; hence $f$ is constant. This is **Liouville's theorem**. Its immediate corollary is the **Fundamental Theorem of Algebra**: a non-constant polynomial $p$ with no root makes $1/p$ a bounded entire function, contradicting Liouville unless $p$ is constant. Gauss gave geometric proofs; the Liouville route is the cleanest analytic one.

**Higher derivatives and analyticity.** Differentiating the disc formula under the integral sign $n$ times in $a$ produces $f^{(n)}(a) = \frac{n!}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f(\zeta)}{(\zeta - a)^{n+1}}\,d\zeta$. In particular a holomorphic function is automatically infinitely differentiable. Expanding the Cauchy kernel as a geometric series in $(a - a_0)/(\zeta - a_0)$ and integrating term by term yields the convergent Taylor series $f(a) = \sum_{n\ge 0} \frac{f^{(n)}(a_0)}{n!}(a-a_0)^n$ on the largest disc inside $\Omega$. Thus **analyticity** (local power-series representability) and **holomorphy** (complex differentiability) coincide — the central rigidity of the theory.

**Morera and stability.** The converse to Goursat is Morera's theorem: if $f$ is continuous and $\oint_{\partial T} f = 0$ for every triangle, then $f$ is holomorphic. Together with the uniform-limit theorem (a locally uniform limit of holomorphic functions is holomorphic, since the Cauchy formula passes to the limit), these results make holomorphy a closed, stable condition — the bedrock for normal families (06.01.14) and the construction of zeta and theta functions later in the strand.

**Synthesis.** The disc formula, the Cauchy estimates, Liouville, the Fundamental Theorem of Algebra, and the analyticity-equals-holomorphy equivalence are a single result viewed from different angles: each one *builds toward* the next, and the chain *appears again in* every downstream tool of the strand. **The foundational reason** all of complex analysis is so rigid is that boundary data determines the interior through one integral; **this is exactly** why bounded entire functions cannot vary, why polynomials must factor, and why differentiability *generalises* instantly to infinite differentiability. **The central insight** — averaging a holomorphic function over a circle returns its center value — **is dual to** the algebraic statement that the Cauchy kernel inverts the boundary operator on holomorphic functions. **Putting these together**, the formula is the structural engine from which residues, the argument principle, and analytic continuation are all read off.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Dixon's proof of the homological Cauchy integral formula, 1971).** Let $f$ be holomorphic on an open set $\Omega$ and let $\gamma$ be a cycle in $\Omega$ that is null-homologous, i.e. $n(\gamma, w) = 0$ for every $w \in \mathbb{C}\setminus\Omega$. Then for all $a \in \Omega \setminus \gamma$,

$$n(\gamma, a)\, f(a) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f(\zeta)}{\zeta - a}\,d\zeta.$$

**Proof.** Define $g : \Omega \times \Omega \to \mathbb{C}$ by

$$g(z,w) = \begin{cases}\dfrac{f(w) - f(z)}{w - z}, & w \ne z,\\[2mm] f'(z), & w = z.\end{cases}$$

Using the power-series representation of $f$ from the Advanced section, $g$ is continuous on $\Omega \times \Omega$ and holomorphic in each variable separately; in particular $z \mapsto g(z,w)$ is holomorphic for each fixed $w$. Define

$$h(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma g(z,\zeta)\,d\zeta, \qquad z \in \Omega.$$

By continuity and Morera applied across the integral, $h$ is holomorphic on $\Omega$. Now let $\Omega' = \{z \notin \gamma : n(\gamma, z) = 0\}$, an open set, and on it define $h'(z) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f(\zeta)}{\zeta - z}\,d\zeta$. For $z \in \Omega \cap \Omega'$ the two expressions agree, because there $\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f(z)}{\zeta - z}\,d\zeta = f(z)\,n(\gamma,z) = 0$, so $h(z) = h'(z)$. Hence $h$ and $h'$ glue to a single function $H$ holomorphic on $\Omega \cup \Omega'$.

The null-homology hypothesis says every point of $\mathbb{C}\setminus\Omega$ has index $0$, so $\mathbb{C}\setminus\Omega \subseteq \Omega'$; therefore $\Omega \cup \Omega' = \mathbb{C}$ and $H$ is **entire**. On the unbounded component of the complement of $\gamma$ the index is $0$ and $\oint_\gamma \frac{f(\zeta)}{\zeta - z}\,d\zeta \to 0$ as $|z| \to \infty$, so $H(z) \to 0$. A bounded entire function tending to $0$ is identically $0$ by Liouville. Therefore $H \equiv 0$, and in particular for $a \in \Omega\setminus\gamma$,

$$0 = h(a) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f(\zeta) - f(a)}{\zeta - a}\,d\zeta = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_\gamma \frac{f(\zeta)}{\zeta - a}\,d\zeta - n(\gamma,a)\,f(a),$$

which rearranges to the claimed formula. $\blacksquare$ [ref: TODO_REF Dixon 1971 A brief proof of Cauchy's integral theorem Proc AMS 29]

Taking $f \equiv 1$ in the displayed identity recovers Cauchy's theorem on cycles, $\oint_\gamma f(\zeta)\,d\zeta = 0$, by applying the formula to the holomorphic antiderivative; the formula and the theorem are thus packaged together. The genius of Dixon's argument is that it replaces all of the older homotopy bookkeeping with a single entire function killed by Liouville. [ref: TODO_REF Ahlfors §4.1-4.2]

## Connections [Master]

- [06.01.01] supplies holomorphy, the hypothesis on which Goursat's triangle theorem and the entire-function argument both rest; without complex-differentiability the remainder estimate in the bisection proof fails.

- [06.01.03] the residue theorem is the direct generalization of this formula to functions with isolated singularities, and [06.01.27] (power series and Laurent series) is the expansion this unit's higher-derivative formula produces; the coefficients $c_n = f^{(n)}(a_0)/n!$ are exactly the Cauchy integrals.

- [06.01.28] (index / winding number) provides the integer $n(\gamma,a)$ weighting the homological formula, and [06.01.20] (Picard's little theorem) uses Liouville-style entire-function rigidity in the same spirit, escalating from "bounded implies constant" to "omits two values implies constant."

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Cauchy's 1825 memoir on definite integrals taken between imaginary limits introduced contour integration as a device that extracts interior values from boundary data, founding the residue calculus along the way [ref: TODO_REF Cauchy 1825 Memoire sur les integrales definies prises entre des limites imaginaires]. Cauchy's own proof of the integral theorem assumed the derivative was continuous. In 1900 Édouard Goursat removed that crutch with the triangle bisection argument reproduced above, showing complex-differentiability alone suffices and thereby clarifying that holomorphy is the right primitive notion [ref: TODO_REF Goursat 1900 Sur la definition generale des fonctions analytiques Trans AMS 1]. The modern homological formulation — replacing fragile homotopy arguments with winding numbers and a single entire function annihilated by Liouville — was distilled by John Dixon in a celebrated two-page note [ref: TODO_REF Dixon 1971 A brief proof of Cauchy's integral theorem Proc AMS 29]. The philosophical lesson is that holomorphicity converts a two-dimensional interior problem into one-dimensional boundary data, the rigidity that distinguishes complex analysis from its real counterpart.
