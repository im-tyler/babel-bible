## Intuition [Beginner]

Take the reciprocals of the natural numbers $1, 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, \ldots$ and raise each to a power $s$. The sum $1 + 1/2^s + 1/3^s + \ldots$ is the Riemann zeta function $\zeta(s)$. For $s = 2$ the sum converges to the specific value $\pi^2/6$; for $s = 4$ it converges to $\pi^4/90$. At $s = 1$ the series is the harmonic series, which diverges to infinity.

The zeta function matters because it connects two worlds that seem unrelated: the smooth world of calculus and the discrete world of prime numbers. Euler showed that $\zeta(s)$ can be rewritten as a product over all primes $2, 3, 5, 7, 11, \ldots$. This product formula means that every zero of $\zeta(s)$ encodes information about how the primes are distributed among the integers.

Why does this concept exist? The zeta function is the analytic engine behind the prime number theorem, which counts how many primes exist below a given number. The Riemann hypothesis, the most famous unsolved problem in mathematics, conjectures that all the zeros of $\zeta(s)$ in the critical strip $0 < \operatorname{Re}(s) < 1$ lie on the single line $\operatorname{Re}(s) = 1/2$.

## Visual [Beginner]

![Plot of the Riemann zeta function for real s > 0, showing the pole at s = 1 and convergence toward 1 for large s.](/img/riemann-zeta-function-placeholder.svg)

The pole at $s = 1$ is the reason the harmonic series diverges: the function simply has no finite value there.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute partial sums of $\zeta(2)$ and compare with the known value $\pi^2/6$.

Step 1. The first four terms give $1 + 1/4 + 1/9 + 1/16 = 1 + 0.25 + 0.111 + 0.0625 = 1.4236$.

Step 2. Adding the next six terms through $1/100$ gives $1.4236 + 1/25 + 1/36 + 1/49 + 1/64 + 1/81 + 1/100 = 1.4236 + 0.04 + 0.0278 + 0.0204 + 0.0156 + 0.0123 + 0.01 = 1.5497$.

Step 3. The exact value is $\pi^2/6 \approx 1.6449$. The partial sum through $1/100$ captures about $94\%$ of the total. Adding more terms approaches $1.6449$ from below, but convergence is slow: each new term contributes only a small amount.

What this tells us: the zeta function at $s = 2$ converges, but the convergence rate is moderate because the terms $1/n^2$ shrink only quadratically. At larger values of $s$ the terms shrink faster and convergence accelerates.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $s \in \mathbb{C}$ with $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$. The **Riemann zeta function** is defined by the Dirichlet series

$$
\zeta(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s}.
$$

Convergence for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$ follows from the integral test comparison: $\sum_{n=1}^{N} n^{-s}$ is bounded by $1 + \int_1^N x^{-s}\,dx = 1 + (N^{1-s} - 1)/(1-s)$, which converges as $N \to \infty$ when $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$. The series converges absolutely and uniformly on compact subsets of $\{\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1\}$, so $\zeta(s)$ is holomorphic on this half-plane. [ref: TODO_REF Stein-Shakarchi Ch. 6]

**Definition (Euler product).** For $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$, the zeta function factors over the primes:

$$
\zeta(s) = \prod_{p \text{ prime}} \frac{1}{1 - p^{-s}}.
$$

This factorisation is the bridge between analysis and number theory: the zeta function on the left is an analytic object defined by a sum over all natural numbers, and the product on the right encodes the multiplicative structure of the integers in terms of prime powers.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *The Dirichlet series does not converge for $\operatorname{Re}(s) \leq 1$.* At $s = 1$ the series is the harmonic series, which diverges. For $\operatorname{Re}(s) < 1$ the terms $n^{-s}$ do not decrease fast enough for convergence. The function must be extended analytically beyond the half-plane of convergence.
- *The Euler product does not converge at $\operatorname{Re}(s) = 1$.* The boundary $\operatorname{Re}(s) = 1$ is the natural boundary for the product representation; at $s = 1$ the product diverges because $\sum_p p^{-1}$ diverges.
- *$\zeta(-1) = -1/12$ does not mean $1 + 2 + 3 + \ldots = -1/12$.* The value $\zeta(-1)$ is obtained by analytic continuation, not by summing the divergent series. The Dirichlet series diverges at $s = -1$; the value $-1/12$ comes from the functional equation.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Euler product convergence).** *For $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$, the zeta function has the absolutely convergent product representation*

$$
\zeta(s) = \prod_{p \text{ prime}} \frac{1}{1 - p^{-s}},
$$

*where the product converges absolutely. In particular, $\zeta(s) \neq 0$ for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$.*

*Proof.* Fix $s$ with $\sigma := \operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$. The geometric series gives

$$
\frac{1}{1 - p^{-s}} = 1 + p^{-s} + p^{-2s} + p^{-3s} + \cdots
$$

for each prime $p$. Take the finite product over the first $k$ primes $p_1, \ldots, p_k$:

$$
\prod_{j=1}^{k} \frac{1}{1 - p_j^{-s}} = \prod_{j=1}^{k} \left(1 + p_j^{-s} + p_j^{-2s} + \cdots\right).
$$

Expanding the product, each factor contributes a power of $p_j^{-s}$, and by unique factorisation of integers, every positive integer $n$ whose prime factorisation uses only the primes $p_1, \ldots, p_k$ appears exactly once as a term $n^{-s}$. Hence

$$
\prod_{j=1}^{k} \frac{1}{1 - p_j^{-s}} = \sum_{n \in S_k} \frac{1}{n^s}
$$

where $S_k$ is the set of positive integers whose prime factors all lie in $\{p_1, \ldots, p_k\}$. As $k \to \infty$, every positive integer eventually appears in some $S_k$, so the partial products converge to the full Dirichlet series:

$$
\prod_{j=1}^{k} \frac{1}{1 - p_j^{-s}} \to \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^s} = \zeta(s).
$$

For absolute convergence of the product: $\sum_p |1 - (1 - p^{-s})^{-1}| = \sum_p |p^{-s}/(1 - p^{-s})| \leq \sum_p p^{-\sigma}/(1 - 2^{-\sigma})$ for $\sigma > 1$, and this converges because $\sum_p p^{-\sigma} \leq \sum_n n^{-\sigma} < \infty$ for $\sigma > 1$. An absolutely convergent infinite product of non-zero factors is non-zero, so $\zeta(s) \neq 0$ for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Euler product builds toward [06.01.04] analytic continuation, where the zeta function appears again as the central example of extending a function beyond its half-plane of convergence to a meromorphic function on all of $\mathbb{C}$. The foundational reason the zeta function is central to number theory is that the Euler product identifies it as the generating function of the primes, and this is exactly the bridge from analysis to arithmetic. The pattern generalises through Dirichlet $L$-functions (replacing $\zeta(s)$ with $L(s, \chi) = \sum \chi(n)/n^s$ for a Dirichlet character $\chi$), and putting these together identifies the zeta function as the simplest member of a vast family of $L$-functions whose analytic properties control arithmetic distribution questions. The Euler product appears again in [06.01.27] power series as the prototypical example of an Euler-type product representation of an analytic function.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that $\zeta(s) \neq 0$ for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$ directly from the Euler product.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The product converges absolutely (proved in the Key theorem), and an absolutely convergent product of non-zero factors has a non-zero limit. Hence $\zeta(s) \neq 0$ for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$. $\square$

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that $\sum_{p \text{ prime}} p^{-\sigma}$ converges for $\sigma > 1$ by comparing it with the zeta function.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the Euler product and take logarithms, or note that the sum over primes is a sub-sum of $\sum_n n^{-\sigma} = \zeta(\sigma)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since every prime is a positive integer, $p^{-\sigma} \leq n^{-\sigma}$ when $p = n$, so $\sum_p p^{-\sigma} \leq \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n^{-\sigma} = \zeta(\sigma) < \infty$ for $\sigma > 1$. Alternatively, from the Euler product: $\log \zeta(\sigma) = -\sum_p \log(1 - p^{-\sigma}) = \sum_p (p^{-\sigma} + p^{-2\sigma}/2 + \cdots) \geq \sum_p p^{-\sigma}$, and $\log \zeta(\sigma)$ is finite for $\sigma > 1$, so $\sum_p p^{-\sigma}$ converges. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).**

Compute $\zeta(0)$ using the functional equation $\zeta(s) = 2^s \pi^{s-1} \sin(\pi s/2) \Gamma(1-s) \zeta(1-s)$, given $\zeta(1) = \infty$ is a pole. Instead, use the known analytic-continuation value. State the answer.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The value $\zeta(0)$ is obtained by analytic continuation, not from the Dirichlet series. Use the fact that $\zeta(0) = -1/2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$-1/2$.** By analytic continuation, $\zeta(0) = -1/2$. This cannot be obtained by plugging $s = 0$ into the Dirichlet series (which gives $1 + 1 + 1 + \cdots$, divergent). The value follows from the functional equation at $s = 0$: $\zeta(0) = 2^0 \pi^{-1} \sin(0) \Gamma(1) \zeta(1)$. Since $\sin(0) = 0$ and $\zeta(1) = \infty$, one must take the limit, which gives $-1/2$. The residue at the pole $s = 1$ is $1$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the estimate $\zeta(\sigma) \leq 1 + \int_1^{\infty} x^{-\sigma}\,dx = \sigma/(\sigma - 1)$ for $\sigma > 1$, and use it to show that $\zeta(\sigma) \to 1$ as $\sigma \to \infty$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare the sum with an integral using the fact that $n^{-\sigma}$ is decreasing in $n$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $x \mapsto x^{-\sigma}$ is decreasing for $\sigma > 0$:

$$
\sum_{n=2}^{N} n^{-\sigma} \leq \int_1^{N} x^{-\sigma}\,dx = \frac{1 - N^{1-\sigma}}{\sigma - 1}.
$$

Letting $N \to \infty$ for $\sigma > 1$: $\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} n^{-\sigma} \leq 1/(\sigma - 1)$. Hence $\zeta(\sigma) \leq 1 + 1/(\sigma - 1) = \sigma/(\sigma - 1)$. As $\sigma \to \infty$, the bound $\sigma/(\sigma-1) \to 1$, and since $\zeta(\sigma) \geq 1$ (the first term), $\zeta(\sigma) \to 1$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that $\zeta(s)$ has a simple pole at $s = 1$ with residue $1$. Outline: write $\zeta(s) - 1/(s-1) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (n^{-s} - \int_n^{n+1} x^{-s}\,dx)$ and show this converges to a holomorphic function on $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the integral comparison $n^{-s} - \int_n^{n+1} x^{-s}\,dx = \int_n^{n+1} (n^{-s} - x^{-s})\,dx$ and bound $|n^{-s} - x^{-s}|$ for $x \in [n, n+1]$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define $g(s) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(n^{-s} - \int_n^{n+1} x^{-s}\,dx\right)$ for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 0$. For each $n$ and $x \in [n, n+1]$:

$$
|n^{-s} - x^{-s}| = |s \int_n^x t^{-s-1}\,dt| \leq |s| \cdot n^{-\operatorname{Re}(s)-1}.
$$

Hence $|n^{-s} - \int_n^{n+1} x^{-s}\,dx| \leq |s| \cdot n^{-\operatorname{Re}(s)-1}$, and $\sum_n |s| n^{-\operatorname{Re}(s)-1}$ converges for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 0$. By the Weierstrass $M$-test, $g(s)$ is holomorphic on $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 0$.

Now compute $\int_1^{N} x^{-s}\,dx = (N^{1-s} - 1)/(1-s)$. As $N \to \infty$ for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$, $N^{1-s} \to 0$, so $\int_1^{\infty} x^{-s}\,dx = 1/(s-1)$. Therefore

$$
\zeta(s) = g(s) + \int_1^{\infty} x^{-s}\,dx = g(s) + \frac{1}{s-1}
$$

for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$. Since $g$ is holomorphic on $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 0$, this representation shows $\zeta(s)$ extends meromorphically to $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 0$ with a simple pole at $s = 1$ with residue $1$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Assuming the functional equation $\xi(s) := \pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)\zeta(s) = \xi(1-s)$ and the fact that $\zeta(s)$ has no zeros on $\operatorname{Re}(s) = 1$, prove that $\zeta(s)$ has no zeros on $\operatorname{Re}(s) = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the functional equation at $s$ and $1-s$. If $\zeta(s_0) = 0$ with $\operatorname{Re}(s_0) = 0$, what does the functional equation say about $\zeta(1-s_0)$?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $\zeta(s_0) = 0$ with $\operatorname{Re}(s_0) = 0$. Then $1 - s_0$ has $\operatorname{Re}(1 - s_0) = 1$, placing it on the line $\operatorname{Re}(s) = 1$. From the functional equation:

$$
\pi^{-s_0/2}\Gamma(s_0/2)\zeta(s_0) = \pi^{-(1-s_0)/2}\Gamma((1-s_0)/2)\zeta(1-s_0).
$$

If $\zeta(s_0) = 0$, the left side vanishes. The Gamma function $\Gamma((1-s_0)/2)$ is finite (the only poles of $\Gamma$ are at non-positive integers, and $(1-s_0)/2$ has real part $1/2$). The factor $\pi^{-(1-s_0)/2}$ is non-zero. Hence $\zeta(1-s_0) = 0$, contradicting the hypothesis that $\zeta$ has no zeros on $\operatorname{Re}(s) = 1$. Therefore $\zeta(s) \neq 0$ for $\operatorname{Re}(s) = 0$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Functional equation (Riemann 1859).** The completed zeta function $\xi(s) = \pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)\zeta(s)$ satisfies $\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)$ for all $s \in \mathbb{C}$ [ref: TODO_REF Riemann 1859]. Equivalently,

$$
\zeta(s) = 2^s \pi^{s-1} \sin\!\left(\frac{\pi s}{2}\right) \Gamma(1-s) \zeta(1-s).
$$

The functional equation is the engine of analytic continuation: it extends $\zeta(s)$ from the half-plane $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$ to a meromorphic function on all of $\mathbb{C}$ with a unique simple pole at $s = 1$ with residue $1$. The symmetry $s \leftrightarrow 1 - s$ means the behaviour of $\zeta(s)$ for $\operatorname{Re}(s) < 0$ is determined by its behaviour for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$.

**Elementary zeros.** The functional equation forces $\zeta(-2n) = 0$ for $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$. At $s = -2n$, the factor $\sin(\pi s/2) = \sin(-n\pi) = 0$, while $\Gamma(1-s) = \Gamma(1+2n)$ is finite and non-zero, and $\zeta(1-s) = \zeta(1+2n)$ is finite and non-zero. The factor $\Gamma(s/2)$ in the completed zeta function has poles at $s = 0, -2, -4, \ldots$, and these poles are cancelled by the elementary zeros of $\zeta$, making $\xi(s)$ an entire function of order one.

**Zero-free region.** The zeta function has no zeros for $\operatorname{Re}(s) \geq 1$. The proof that $\zeta(1 + it) \neq 0$ for $t \neq 0$ uses the Euler product and the inequality $3 + 4\cos\theta + \cos 2\theta \geq 0$ applied to $\log|\zeta(\sigma)|^3 |\zeta(\sigma + it)|^4 |\zeta(\sigma + 2it)|$ near $\sigma = 1$. Combined with the functional equation, this gives $\zeta(s) \neq 0$ for $\operatorname{Re}(s) \geq 1$ and (via reflection) for $\operatorname{Re}(s) \leq 0$ except at the elementary zeros. The remaining region $0 < \operatorname{Re}(s) < 1$ is the *critical strip*, where the substantive zeros lie.

**Hadamard-de la Vallee Poussin zero-free region.** There exists a constant $c > 0$ such that $\zeta(s) \neq 0$ for $\operatorname{Re}(s) \geq 1 - c/\log(|\operatorname{Im}(s)| + 2)$ [ref: TODO_REF Titchmarsh Ch. 3]. This is the strongest known effective zero-free region and is the input to the prime number theorem with error term.

**Prime number theorem (Hadamard 1896, de la Vallee Poussin 1896).** The number of primes below $x$, denoted $\pi(x)$, satisfies $\pi(x) \sim x/\ln x$ as $x \to \infty$ [ref: TODO_REF Edwards Ch. 1]. The proof proceeds by showing that $\zeta(1 + it) \neq 0$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$, then inverting the relation $\log \zeta(s) = \sum_p p^{-s} + O(1)$ via a contour integral to obtain the asymptotic. The error term $|\pi(x) - \operatorname{Li}(x)|$ is controlled by the location of the substantive zeros: the Riemann hypothesis is equivalent to $|\pi(x) - \operatorname{Li}(x)| = O(x^{1/2+\epsilon})$ for every $\epsilon > 0$.

**Riemann hypothesis.** All substantive zeros of $\zeta(s)$ lie on the critical line $\operatorname{Re}(s) = 1/2$ [ref: TODO_REF Edwards Ch. 1]. This remains the most famous unsolved problem in mathematics. Numerical verification confirms the hypothesis for the first $10^{13}$ zeros. The functional equation and the Euler product are the two structural facts that constrain the zeros; the hypothesis asserts that these constraints force the zeros onto the symmetry line of the functional equation.

**Synthesis.** The Riemann zeta function is the foundational reason that analytic methods control arithmetic questions about primes. The central insight is that the Euler product identifies $\zeta(s)$ with the primes, and the functional equation identifies the left and right halves of the complex plane, putting these together so that the distribution of primes is controlled by the location of the zeros of $\zeta(s)$ in the critical strip. This is exactly the structure that generalises through the Langlands programme: every $L$-function has an Euler product, a functional equation, and an analytic continuation, and the pattern recurs across Dirichlet $L$-functions, automorphic $L$-functions, and the $L$-functions of elliptic curves. The bridge is between the discrete multiplicative structure of $\mathbb{Z}$ and the continuous analytic structure of $\mathbb{C}$, and $\zeta(s)$ is the single function that carries the full dictionary between these two worlds.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Zero-free region: $\zeta(1 + it) \neq 0$).** *For all $t \in \mathbb{R}$, $\zeta(1 + it) \neq 0$.*

*Proof.* For $\sigma > 1$, take the real part of $\log \zeta(s)$ via the Euler product:

$$
\log |\zeta(\sigma)| = -\sum_p \log(1 - p^{-\sigma}) = \sum_p \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{p^{-k\sigma}}{k}.
$$

For $\sigma > 1$ and $t \in \mathbb{R}$, consider the non-negative quantity:

$$
3\log|\zeta(\sigma)| + 4\log|\zeta(\sigma + it)| + \log|\zeta(\sigma + 2it)| \geq 0.
$$

This follows from the inequality $3 + 4\cos\theta + \cos 2\theta = 2(1 + \cos\theta)^2 \geq 0$ applied to the real parts of $-k\log p$ at arguments $0$, $t\log p$, and $2t\log p$ in the Euler product expansion. Therefore

$$
|\zeta(\sigma)|^3 |\zeta(\sigma + it)|^4 |\zeta(\sigma + 2it)| \geq 1.
$$

Now use the pole at $s = 1$: $\zeta(\sigma) \sim 1/(\sigma - 1)$ as $\sigma \to 1^+$. If $\zeta(1 + it) = 0$ for some $t \neq 0$, then $|\zeta(\sigma + it)| = O(\sigma - 1)$ as $\sigma \to 1^+$. The factor $|\zeta(\sigma + 2it)|$ remains bounded near $\sigma = 1$ (it has no pole). Hence the left side behaves like $(\sigma-1)^{-3} \cdot (\sigma-1)^4 \cdot O(1) = O(\sigma - 1) \to 0$ as $\sigma \to 1^+$, contradicting the lower bound of $1$. Therefore $\zeta(1 + it) \neq 0$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$. $\square$

**Proposition (Residue at the pole).** *The zeta function has a simple pole at $s = 1$ with residue $1$.*

*Proof.* From Exercise 7, the representation $\zeta(s) = g(s) + 1/(s-1)$ holds in a neighbourhood of $s = 1$, where $g(s)$ is holomorphic at $s = 1$ with $g(1) = \gamma$ (the Euler-Mascheroni constant). Therefore $(s-1)\zeta(s) = (s-1)g(s) + 1 \to 1$ as $s \to 1$, establishing a simple pole with residue $1$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Gamma function [06.01.15].** The functional equation of the zeta function involves $\Gamma(s/2)$ as the normalising factor in the completed zeta function $\xi(s) = \pi^{-s/2}\Gamma(s/2)\zeta(s)$. The Gamma function's poles at non-positive integers cancel the elementary zeros of $\zeta(s)$, making $\xi(s)$ entire. The Gamma function is the structural partner of $\zeta(s)$ in the functional equation, and the reflection formula for $\Gamma$ parallels the symmetry $\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)$.

- **Power series and Laurent series [06.01.27].** The Dirichlet series $\zeta(s) = \sum n^{-s}$ is the prototypical example of a Dirichlet series, a variant of the power series where the index appears in the exponent rather than the coefficient. The theory of convergence and analytic continuation for Dirichlet series parallels the radius-of-convergence theory for power series, with the abscissa of convergence playing the role of the radius of convergence.

- **Analytic continuation [06.01.04].** The zeta function is the central example of analytic continuation beyond a half-plane of convergence: the Dirichlet series converges only for $\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1$, yet the functional equation extends $\zeta(s)$ to a meromorphic function on all of $\mathbb{C}$. This continuation pattern is the template for all $L$-functions in number theory.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Euler 1737 [ref: TODO_REF Euler 1737], in his *Variae observationes circa series infinitas*, discovered the product formula $\zeta(s) = \prod_p (1 - p^{-s})^{-1}$ and used it to give a new proof that there are infinitely many primes: if $\zeta(s)$ diverges as $s \to 1^+$ and the product converges for any finite set of primes, the set of primes must be infinite. Euler also evaluated $\zeta(2) = \pi^2/6$ via the Bernoulli numbers, establishing the connection between the zeta function and special values.

Riemann 1859 [ref: TODO_REF Riemann 1859], in his eight-page memoir *Ueber die Anzahl der Primzahlen unter einer gegebenen Grosse*, introduced the analytic continuation of $\zeta(s)$ to the full complex plane via the functional equation, identified the elementary zeros at $s = -2, -4, -6, \ldots$, and conjectured that the substantive zeros lie on the line $\operatorname{Re}(s) = 1/2$. The modern theory, including the prime number theorem (proved independently by Hadamard and de la Vallee Poussin in 1896 using the zero-free region $\zeta(1 + it) \neq 0$) and the vast machinery of $L$-functions, all descend from Riemann's single paper. The canonical references are Edwards *Riemann's Zeta Function* (1974) and Titchmarsh *The Theory of the Riemann Zeta-Function* (1951) [ref: TODO_REF Titchmarsh].
