## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

This pack supplements the first four chapters of Ahlfors: the geometry of the extended plane, holomorphy and the Cauchy-Riemann equations, conformal and Möbius maps, power and Laurent series, and the Cauchy theory in its integral-formula and residue forms. Its problems exercise the units on holomorphic functions [06.01.01], the Cauchy-Riemann equations and harmonic conjugates [06.01.10], Möbius transformations [06.01.08], the Riemann sphere [06.01.07], power and Laurent series [06.01.27], the Cauchy integral formula [06.01.02], the index of a closed curve [06.01.28], and the residue theorem [06.01.03].

The pack collects ten problems — three easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units rather than as a standalone development. The problems are grouped by Ahlfors chapter: the algebra and geometry of holomorphy and the sphere (easy), conformal/Möbius maps and power-series manipulation (medium), and Cauchy's theorem with its contour-integration consequences (hard). Conventions follow Ahlfors: $\mathbb{C}_\infty = \mathbb{C}\cup\{\infty\}$ is the Riemann sphere, the cross-ratio is $(z_1,z_2,z_3,z_4) = \frac{z_1-z_3}{z_1-z_4}\cdot\frac{z_2-z_4}{z_2-z_3}$, and the winding number (index) of a closed curve $\gamma$ about $a\notin\gamma$ is $n(\gamma,a) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_\gamma\frac{dz}{z-a}$.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one problem in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining nine follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead problem.** *Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\theta}{a + \cos\theta}$ for $a > 1$ by contour integration.*

**Solution.** Substitute $z = e^{i\theta}$, so $dz = iz\,d\theta$ and $\cos\theta = \tfrac12(z + z^{-1})$. As $\theta$ runs over $[0,2\pi)$, $z$ traverses the unit circle $|z| = 1$ once positively. Then $d\theta = dz/(iz)$ and

$$
\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\theta}{a + \cos\theta} = \oint_{|z|=1}\frac{1}{a + \tfrac12(z + z^{-1})}\cdot\frac{dz}{iz} = \oint_{|z|=1}\frac{2\,dz}{i\,(z^2 + 2az + 1)}.
$$

The denominator $z^2 + 2az + 1$ has roots $z_\pm = -a\pm\sqrt{a^2 - 1}$. Since $a > 1$, both roots are real and negative with $z_+ z_- = 1$, so exactly one root lies inside the unit disc, namely $z_+ = -a + \sqrt{a^2-1}$ (it satisfies $|z_+| < 1$ because $|z_-| = 1/|z_+| > 1$). The residue of the integrand at $z_+$ is

$$
\operatorname*{Res}_{z=z_+}\frac{2}{i\,(z - z_+)(z - z_-)} = \frac{2}{i\,(z_+ - z_-)} = \frac{2}{i\cdot 2\sqrt{a^2-1}} = \frac{1}{i\sqrt{a^2-1}}.
$$

By the residue theorem the integral is $2\pi i$ times this residue:

$$
\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{d\theta}{a + \cos\theta} = 2\pi i\cdot\frac{1}{i\sqrt{a^2-1}} = \frac{2\pi}{\sqrt{a^2-1}}.\qquad\square
$$

The substitution $z = e^{i\theta}$ converting a trigonometric integral over $[0,2\pi)$ into a contour integral over $|z| = 1$ is the prototype of the residue-evaluation toolkit. Every rational-in-$\cos,\sin$ integral reduces this way; the only work is locating which poles fall inside the unit disc. This is Ahlfors §4.3, the residue calculus applied to real integrals.

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).** *Cauchy-Riemann forces holomorphy of $z\mapsto z^2$ but not of $z\mapsto\bar z$.*

Verify directly from the Cauchy-Riemann equations that $f(z) = z^2$ is holomorphic on all of $\mathbb{C}$, while $g(z) = \bar z$ is holomorphic nowhere.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $z = x + iy$ and separate real and imaginary parts. The Cauchy-Riemann equations are $u_x = v_y$ and $u_y = -v_x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $f(z) = z^2 = (x^2 - y^2) + i(2xy)$ we have $u = x^2 - y^2$, $v = 2xy$. Then $u_x = 2x = v_y$ and $u_y = -2y = -v_x$, so the Cauchy-Riemann equations hold at every point, and the partials are continuous, so $f$ is holomorphic on $\mathbb{C}$ with $f'(z) = u_x + i v_x = 2x + i\,2y = 2z$.

For $g(z) = \bar z = x - iy$ we have $u = x$, $v = -y$. Then $u_x = 1$ but $v_y = -1$, so $u_x = v_y$ fails everywhere. Hence $g$ is holomorphic at no point, even though it is smooth as a map $\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}^2$. This is the basic Ahlfors §2.1 contrast: real-differentiability is necessary but the Cauchy-Riemann constraint is the extra condition that defines complex differentiability.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Chordal distance on the Riemann sphere.*

Under stereographic projection from the north pole, the chordal (spherical) distance between the images of $z, w\in\mathbb{C}$ on the unit sphere is $d(z,w) = \dfrac{2|z - w|}{\sqrt{1 + |z|^2}\sqrt{1 + |w|^2}}$. Compute the chordal distance between $z = 0$ and $w = \infty$, and between $z = 1$ and $w = -1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $w = \infty$, take the limit of the formula as $|w|\to\infty$: $d(z,\infty) = \dfrac{2}{\sqrt{1+|z|^2}}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $z = 0$, $w = \infty$: $d(0,\infty) = \dfrac{2}{\sqrt{1 + 0}} = 2$. This is the diameter of the unit sphere — $0$ maps to the south pole, $\infty$ to the north pole, and they are antipodal.

For $z = 1$, $w = -1$: $d(1,-1) = \dfrac{2\cdot|1 - (-1)|}{\sqrt{1+1}\sqrt{1+1}} = \dfrac{2\cdot 2}{2} = 2$. The points $1$ and $-1$ map to antipodal points on the equator, again at chordal distance $2$.

These confirm the chordal metric is bounded by $2$ (the sphere's diameter), unlike the Euclidean metric on $\mathbb{C}$. Ahlfors §1.4.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, numeric).** *Radius of convergence of a power series.*

Find the radius of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{n!}{n^n}z^n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the ratio test, $R = \lim_{n\to\infty} |a_n/a_{n+1}|$, and Stirling or the limit $(1 + 1/n)^n\to e$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $a_n = n!/n^n$,

$$
\frac{a_n}{a_{n+1}} = \frac{n!/n^n}{(n+1)!/(n+1)^{n+1}} = \frac{(n+1)^{n+1}}{(n+1)\,n^n} = \frac{(n+1)^n}{n^n} = \left(1 + \frac1n\right)^n.
$$

Taking $n\to\infty$, this tends to $e$. By the ratio test the radius of convergence is $R = e$.

The series converges for $|z| < e$ and diverges for $|z| > e$. This is a standard Ahlfors §3.1 power-series computation; the appearance of $e$ comes from the $n^n$ growth in the coefficients.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *Möbius maps preserve the cross-ratio.*

Show that any Möbius transformation $T(z) = \dfrac{az + b}{cz + d}$ (with $ad - bc\neq 0$) preserves the cross-ratio: $(Tz_1, Tz_2, Tz_3, Tz_4) = (z_1, z_2, z_3, z_4)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $Tz_i - Tz_j = \dfrac{(ad-bc)(z_i - z_j)}{(cz_i + d)(cz_j + d)}$, then form the cross-ratio; every denominator factor and the $(ad-bc)$ cancel.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For any two points,

$$
Tz_i - Tz_j = \frac{az_i + b}{cz_i + d} - \frac{az_j + b}{cz_j + d} = \frac{(az_i+b)(cz_j+d) - (az_j+b)(cz_i+d)}{(cz_i+d)(cz_j+d)}.
$$

Expanding the numerator, the $acz_iz_j$ and $bd$ terms cancel and the rest collect to $(ad - bc)(z_i - z_j)$. Hence

$$
Tz_i - Tz_j = \frac{(ad - bc)(z_i - z_j)}{(cz_i+d)(cz_j+d)}.
$$

Now form the cross-ratio $(Tz_1,Tz_2,Tz_3,Tz_4) = \dfrac{Tz_1 - Tz_3}{Tz_1 - Tz_4}\cdot\dfrac{Tz_2 - Tz_4}{Tz_2 - Tz_3}$. Substituting the formula, each of the four differences contributes a factor $(ad - bc)$ — two in the numerator and two in the denominator, cancelling — and the linear denominators $(cz_i + d)$ each appear once in the numerator and once in the denominator across the product, cancelling as well. What remains is

$$
\frac{z_1 - z_3}{z_1 - z_4}\cdot\frac{z_2 - z_4}{z_2 - z_3} = (z_1,z_2,z_3,z_4).
$$

The cross-ratio is the fundamental Möbius invariant: it is the unique (up to convention) projective invariant of four points on $\mathbb{C}_\infty$, and invariance characterises Möbius maps among the homographies. Ahlfors §2.4.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).** *Cauchy's estimate and Liouville's theorem.*

Let $f$ be entire and suppose $|f(z)|\leq M$ for all $z$. Use the Cauchy integral formula for $f'$ to derive Cauchy's estimate on a circle of radius $R$, then let $R\to\infty$ to conclude $f$ is constant (Liouville).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$f'(a) = \dfrac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z-a|=R}\dfrac{f(z)}{(z-a)^2}\,dz$. Bound the integral by (length)$\times$(max integrand).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the Cauchy integral formula for the derivative,

$$
f'(a) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_{|z-a|=R}\frac{f(z)}{(z-a)^2}\,dz.
$$

On the circle $|z - a| = R$ we have $|z - a|^2 = R^2$ and $|f(z)|\leq M$, and the circle has length $2\pi R$. The standard ML-estimate gives

$$
|f'(a)|\leq \frac{1}{2\pi}\cdot\frac{M}{R^2}\cdot 2\pi R = \frac{M}{R}.
$$

This is Cauchy's estimate. Since $f$ is entire the formula holds for every $R > 0$, and the bound $M$ is independent of $R$. Letting $R\to\infty$ forces $|f'(a)| = 0$. As $a$ was arbitrary, $f'\equiv 0$, so $f$ is constant. This is Liouville's theorem, and it immediately yields the fundamental theorem of algebra: a nonconstant polynomial $p$ with no zero would make $1/p$ a bounded entire function, hence constant — contradiction. Ahlfors §4.2-4.3.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).** *Laurent expansion in an annulus.*

Find the Laurent expansion of $f(z) = \dfrac{1}{(z-1)(z-2)}$ valid in the annulus $1 < |z| < 2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Partial fractions: $f(z) = \dfrac{1}{z-2} - \dfrac{1}{z-1}$. In the annulus, expand $\dfrac{1}{z-1}$ in powers of $1/z$ (since $|z| > 1$) and $\dfrac{1}{z-2}$ in powers of $z$ (since $|z| < 2$).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Partial fractions give $f(z) = \dfrac{1}{z-2} - \dfrac{1}{z-1}$.

For the first term, since $|z| < 2$, write $\dfrac{1}{z-2} = -\dfrac{1}{2}\cdot\dfrac{1}{1 - z/2} = -\dfrac{1}{2}\sum_{n=0}^\infty\left(\dfrac{z}{2}\right)^n = -\sum_{n=0}^\infty\dfrac{z^n}{2^{n+1}}$ (geometric, $|z/2| < 1$).

For the second term, since $|z| > 1$, write $\dfrac{1}{z-1} = \dfrac{1}{z}\cdot\dfrac{1}{1 - 1/z} = \dfrac{1}{z}\sum_{m=0}^\infty\dfrac{1}{z^m} = \sum_{m=1}^\infty\dfrac{1}{z^m}$ (geometric, $|1/z| < 1$).

Therefore in $1 < |z| < 2$,

$$
f(z) = -\sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{z^n}{2^{n+1}} - \sum_{m=1}^\infty\frac{1}{z^m}.
$$

The expansion has both a regular part (nonnegative powers, from the pole at $2$ outside the annulus) and a principal part (negative powers, from the pole at $1$ inside the annulus). A different annulus ($0 < |z| < 1$ or $|z| > 2$) would give a different Laurent series for the same function — the series is annulus-dependent. Ahlfors §3.1, §4.3.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, proof).** *Möbius map sending the upper half-plane to the disc.*

Find a Möbius transformation carrying the upper half-plane $\mathbb{H} = \{\operatorname{Im} z > 0\}$ conformally onto the unit disc $\mathbb{D} = \{|w| < 1\}$, and verify it sends the real axis to the unit circle.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Try $w = T(z) = \dfrac{z - i}{z + i}$. Check that $T(i) = 0$ (center to center) and that real $z$ gives $|w| = 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take $T(z) = \dfrac{z - i}{z + i}$, with $ad - bc = i - (-i) = 2i\neq 0$, so it is a genuine Möbius map.

It sends $i\mapsto 0$, placing the interior point $i\in\mathbb{H}$ at the center of $\mathbb{D}$. For real $z = x$,

$$
|T(x)| = \frac{|x - i|}{|x + i|} = \frac{\sqrt{x^2 + 1}}{\sqrt{x^2 + 1}} = 1,
$$

so the real axis (the boundary $\partial\mathbb{H}$) maps onto the unit circle $\partial\mathbb{D}$. A Möbius map is a bijection of $\mathbb{C}_\infty$ and is continuous, so it carries the connected region $\mathbb{H}$ onto one of the two components of $\mathbb{C}_\infty\setminus\partial\mathbb{D}$; since the interior point $i\mapsto 0\in\mathbb{D}$, it maps $\mathbb{H}$ onto $\mathbb{D}$. Möbius maps are conformal where defined (the derivative is nonzero), so this is a conformal equivalence $\mathbb{H}\xrightarrow{\sim}\mathbb{D}$.

This is the Cayley transform, the bridge between the upper-half-plane and disc models used throughout hyperbolic geometry and the Schwarz-Pick theory. Ahlfors §2.4, §3.1.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Homotopy form of Cauchy's theorem.*

Let $f$ be holomorphic on an open set $\Omega$ and let $\gamma_0,\gamma_1$ be two closed curves in $\Omega$ that are freely homotopic in $\Omega$ through closed curves. Show $\displaystyle\int_{\gamma_0} f\,dz = \int_{\gamma_1} f\,dz$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Let $H(s,t)$ be the homotopy, $H(\cdot,0) = \gamma_0$, $H(\cdot,1) = \gamma_1$. The function $I(t) = \int_{H(\cdot,t)} f\,dz$ is differentiable in $t$; differentiate under the integral sign and use that $f$ has a local primitive.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Parametrise the homotopy as a continuous (we may smooth it) map $H:[0,1]\times[0,1]\to\Omega$ with $H(s,0) = \gamma_0(s)$, $H(s,1) = \gamma_1(s)$, and $H(0,t) = H(1,t)$ for all $t$ (closed curves). Define

$$
I(t) = \int_0^1 f\big(H(s,t)\big)\,\partial_s H(s,t)\,ds.
$$

Differentiating in $t$ and using $\partial_t\partial_s H = \partial_s\partial_t H$,

$$
I'(t) = \int_0^1\Big[f'(H)\,\partial_t H\,\partial_s H + f(H)\,\partial_s\partial_t H\Big]\,ds = \int_0^1\partial_s\big[f(H)\,\partial_t H\big]\,ds,
$$

since $\partial_s[f(H)\,\partial_t H] = f'(H)\,\partial_s H\,\partial_t H + f(H)\,\partial_s\partial_t H$. By the fundamental theorem of calculus this is $\big[f(H)\,\partial_t H\big]_{s=0}^{s=1}$. Because the homotopy keeps the curves closed, $H(0,t) = H(1,t)$ and $\partial_t H(0,t) = \partial_t H(1,t)$, so the boundary term vanishes: $I'(t) = 0$.

Hence $I$ is constant, so $I(0) = I(1)$, i.e. $\int_{\gamma_0} f\,dz = \int_{\gamma_1} f\,dz$. This is the homotopy form of Cauchy's theorem; specialising $\gamma_1$ to a constant curve recovers the statement that the integral of a holomorphic function over a null-homotopic loop is zero. The homological form (Ahlfors §4.1, Dixon's proof) is the refinement that replaces "homotopic" with "homologous" via winding numbers. Ahlfors §3.4-4.1.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, numeric).** *A rational improper integral by a semicircular contour.*

Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{dx}{x^4 + 1}$ by closing the contour in the upper half-plane.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The poles in the upper half-plane are $e^{i\pi/4}$ and $e^{3i\pi/4}$. The large semicircle contributes nothing as $R\to\infty$ because the integrand decays like $R^{-4}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The poles of $1/(z^4 + 1)$ are the fourth roots of $-1$: $z_k = e^{i\pi(2k+1)/4}$ for $k = 0,1,2,3$. The two in the upper half-plane are $z_0 = e^{i\pi/4}$ and $z_1 = e^{3i\pi/4}$. At a simple pole $z_j$ of $1/(z^4+1)$, the residue is $1/(4z_j^3) = z_j/(4z_j^4) = z_j/(4\cdot(-1)) = -z_j/4$ (using $z_j^4 = -1$).

So the residues are $-z_0/4 = -\tfrac14 e^{i\pi/4}$ and $-z_1/4 = -\tfrac14 e^{3i\pi/4}$. Their sum:

$$
-\frac14\left(e^{i\pi/4} + e^{3i\pi/4}\right) = -\frac14\left(\tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}(1 + i) + \tfrac{\sqrt2}{2}(-1 + i)\right) = -\frac14\cdot\sqrt2\, i = -\frac{\sqrt2}{4}i.
$$

Closing with the upper semicircle $\Gamma_R$ of radius $R$: on $\Gamma_R$ the integrand is $O(R^{-4})$ and the arc has length $\pi R$, so its contribution is $O(R^{-3})\to 0$. Thus

$$
\int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{dx}{x^4 + 1} = 2\pi i\sum(\text{residues}) = 2\pi i\cdot\left(-\frac{\sqrt2}{4}i\right) = \frac{\pi\sqrt2}{2} = \frac{\pi}{\sqrt2}.
$$

The semicircular-contour method works for any rational $P/Q$ with $\deg Q\geq\deg P + 2$ and no real poles: the arc decays, and the integral is $2\pi i$ times the sum of upper-half-plane residues. Ahlfors §4.3.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, proof).** *The winding number is a constant integer on each component of the complement.*

Let $\gamma$ be a piecewise-$C^1$ closed curve in $\mathbb{C}$ and let $a\notin\gamma$. Show that $n(\gamma,a) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\int_\gamma\frac{dz}{z-a}$ is an integer, and that $a\mapsto n(\gamma,a)$ is constant on each connected component of $\mathbb{C}\setminus\gamma$ and vanishes on the unbounded component.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For integrality, parametrise $\gamma:[0,1]\to\mathbb{C}$ and consider $h(t) = \int_0^t\frac{\gamma'(s)}{\gamma(s)-a}\,ds$; show $e^{-h(t)}(\gamma(t)-a)$ is constant, forcing $e^{h(1)} = 1$. For local constancy, the integral depends continuously on $a$, and a continuous integer-valued function is locally constant.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Integrality.* Let $\gamma:[0,1]\to\mathbb{C}$ parametrise the curve with $\gamma(0) = \gamma(1)$, and set $h(t) = \int_0^t\dfrac{\gamma'(s)}{\gamma(s) - a}\,ds$, so $h(1) = 2\pi i\,n(\gamma,a)$. Consider $\varphi(t) = e^{-h(t)}\big(\gamma(t) - a\big)$. Then

$$
\varphi'(t) = e^{-h(t)}\Big(-h'(t)(\gamma(t)-a) + \gamma'(t)\Big) = e^{-h(t)}\Big(-\gamma'(t) + \gamma'(t)\Big) = 0,
$$

using $h'(t) = \gamma'(t)/(\gamma(t)-a)$. So $\varphi$ is constant: $\varphi(1) = \varphi(0)$, i.e. $e^{-h(1)}(\gamma(1)-a) = \gamma(0) - a$. Since $\gamma(1) = \gamma(0)\neq a$, this gives $e^{-h(1)} = 1$, hence $h(1)\in 2\pi i\,\mathbb{Z}$ and $n(\gamma,a)\in\mathbb{Z}$.

*Local constancy.* As a function of $a$, the integrand $1/(z-a)$ is continuous in $a$ uniformly for $z\in\gamma$ (a compact set) as long as $a$ stays off $\gamma$, so $a\mapsto n(\gamma,a)$ is continuous on $\mathbb{C}\setminus\gamma$. A continuous integer-valued function is locally constant, hence constant on each connected component.

*Vanishing at infinity.* On the unbounded component pick $|a|$ very large; then $|1/(z-a)|\leq 1/(|a| - \max_\gamma|z|)\to 0$, so $|n(\gamma,a)|\leq\frac{1}{2\pi}\cdot\text{length}(\gamma)\cdot\frac{1}{|a|-\max|z|}< 1$ for $|a|$ large. Being an integer of modulus less than $1$, it is $0$; by constancy it is $0$ on the whole unbounded component.

This is the basic topology of the index that underlies the homological Cauchy theorem and the argument principle. Ahlfors §3.4, §4.1.

</details>

</aside>
