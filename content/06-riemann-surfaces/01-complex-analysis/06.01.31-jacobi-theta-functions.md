## Intuition [Beginner]

A periodic function repeats when you slide its input by a fixed step: $\sin(x)$ comes back to itself every $2\pi$. The Jacobi theta functions are a family of four functions built to be the master raw material out of which doubly-periodic functions on a lattice are assembled. Each one is an infinite sum of waves whose sizes shrink very fast, so the sum converges and defines a smooth complex function.

The shrinking is controlled by a single number $q$, called the **nome**, with $|q| < 1$. Think of $q$ as a dial. When $q$ is close to $0$, only the first wave matters and the function is nearly constant. As $q$ grows toward the edge $|q| = 1$, more and more waves contribute and the function develops sharp, repeating features.

The four functions are close cousins: $\theta_3$ is the plain version, and $\theta_1, \theta_2, \theta_4$ are obtained by small shifts and sign changes. One of them, $\theta_1$, is special because it has a single zero at the centre, which makes it the right building block for functions that need to vanish at prescribed places.

Why do these four functions exist as a named family? Because they package three deep facts at once: a product formula that factors each sum into a clean infinite product, a set of transformation rules that say how they behave when the dial $q$ is changed in a structured way, and a web of polynomial identities relating their central values. Those three facts underlie the heat equation, elliptic functions, and modular forms.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the complex plane and a sum of cosine-like waves stacked on top of one another. The first wave is tall and slow; each later wave is shorter and faster, scaled down by a higher power of the nome $q$. Adding them gives a single bumpy curve that repeats horizontally with period $\pi$ and, when you shift in the slanted lattice direction, repeats up to a predictable stretching factor.

![A stack of cosine waves of decreasing amplitude, scaled by powers of the nome q, summing to a single periodic theta curve. The curve repeats horizontally with period pi and, along the slanted lattice direction, repeats up to a multiplier. The function theta-1 is shown with a single zero at the origin.](/img/jacobi-theta-functions-placeholder.svg)

The picture makes one thing vivid: the four theta functions differ only by where their peaks and zeros sit. Sliding the whole curve by a quarter-period or a half-period turns one theta function into another, which is the visual shadow of the algebraic identities relating them.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take $\theta_3$ at the centre $z = 0$, where it becomes a pure power series in the nome:

$$
\theta_3(0\mid\tau) = 1 + 2q + 2q^4 + 2q^9 + 2q^{16} + \cdots,
$$

with one term $2q^{n^2}$ for each positive whole number $n$, plus the leading $1$. The exponents are the perfect squares $1, 4, 9, 16$.

Step 1. Pick a small nome, say $q = 0.1$. Then $q = 0.1$, $q^4 = 0.0001$, $q^9$ is a billionth, and everything after is negligible.

Step 2. Add the surviving terms: $1 + 2(0.1) + 2(0.0001) = 1 + 0.2 + 0.0002 = 1.2002$. So $\theta_3(0\mid\tau) \approx 1.2002$ for this nome.

Step 3. Notice the structure: the function counts squares. The coefficient of $q^n$ in $\theta_3(0)$ records how many ways $n$ is a sum of squares, which is why theta functions tie complex analysis to counting problems in number theory.

What this tells us: even at a single point, a theta function is an organised bookkeeping device. The fast shrinkage of $q^{n^2}$ makes the value easy to compute, and the exponents being squares is the seed of the deep modular transformation rules covered in the later tiers.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix $\tau$ in the upper half-plane $\mathbb{H} = \{\tau : \operatorname{Im}\tau > 0\}$ and set the **nome** $q = e^{\pi i \tau}$, so $|q| < 1$. The four **Jacobi theta functions** of $z \in \mathbb{C}$ are

$$
\theta_3(z\mid\tau) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} q^{n^2} e^{2 i n z}, \qquad
\theta_4(z\mid\tau) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} (-1)^n q^{n^2} e^{2 i n z},
$$

$$
\theta_2(z\mid\tau) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} q^{(n+\frac12)^2} e^{(2n+1) i z}, \qquad
\theta_1(z\mid\tau) = -i\sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} (-1)^n q^{(n+\frac12)^2} e^{(2n+1) i z}.
$$

Each series converges absolutely and uniformly on compact subsets of $\mathbb{C} \times \mathbb{H}$, since $|q^{n^2}| = e^{-\pi n^2 \operatorname{Im}\tau}$ decays super-exponentially in $n$; hence each $\theta_j$ is holomorphic in both variables. The function $\theta_1$ is odd in $z$ and the other three are even; $\theta_1$ has a simple zero at $z = 0$, while $\theta_2, \theta_3, \theta_4$ are non-zero there.

The **quasi-periodicity** under the two lattice steps reads, for $\theta_3$,

$$
\theta_3(z + \pi \mid \tau) = \theta_3(z\mid\tau), \qquad
\theta_3(z + \pi\tau \mid \tau) = q^{-1} e^{-2 i z}\,\theta_3(z\mid\tau),
$$

with parallel multipliers for the others (the $\pi\tau$-shift produces the factor $q^{-1} e^{-2iz}$ up to a sign that distinguishes the four). [ref: TODO_REF Whittaker-Watson Ch. 21]

The **theta-nullwerte** are the central values $\theta_2(0\mid\tau)$, $\theta_3(0\mid\tau)$, $\theta_4(0\mid\tau)$; the fourth, $\theta_1(0\mid\tau)$, vanishes, and the relevant invariant is the derivative $\theta_1'(0\mid\tau)$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *The horizontal period is $\pi$, not $2\pi$.* Each term carries $e^{2inz}$, so shifting $z$ by $\pi$ multiplies $e^{2inz}$ by $e^{2\pi i n} = 1$. Using $2\pi$ as the period double-counts the lattice and produces the wrong fundamental domain.

- *$\theta_1$ is not even.* The $(-1)^n$ weight combined with the half-integer frequencies makes $\theta_1(-z\mid\tau) = -\theta_1(z\mid\tau)$. Treating $\theta_1$ as even erases its defining simple zero at the origin and breaks every elliptic-function construction that uses it.

- *The $\pi\tau$-shift is a multiplier, not a true period.* The function is only **quasi**-periodic in the lattice direction: $\theta_3(z + \pi\tau) = q^{-1} e^{-2iz}\theta_3(z)$ acquires a non-constant exponential factor. The theta functions are sections of a line bundle on the torus, not functions on it; ratios of thetas with matching multipliers descend to the torus.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Modular transformation of $\theta_3$ via Poisson summation).** *For $\tau \in \mathbb{H}$,*

$$
\theta_3\!\left(0 \;\middle|\; -\tfrac{1}{\tau}\right) = \sqrt{-i\tau}\;\theta_3(0\mid\tau),
$$

*where $\sqrt{\,\cdot\,}$ is the branch positive on the positive real axis. Together with the period $\theta_3(0\mid\tau+1) = \theta_2(0\mid\tau)$-type relabelling, the two substitutions $\tau \mapsto \tau + 1$ and $\tau \mapsto -1/\tau$ generate the modular action on the theta-nullwerte, permuting $\theta_2 \leftrightarrow \theta_4$ under $\tau \mapsto -1/\tau$.*

*Proof.* Write the central value as a Gaussian sum. With $q = e^{\pi i \tau}$,

$$
\theta_3(0\mid\tau) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} e^{\pi i \tau n^2} = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} f(n), \qquad f(x) = e^{\pi i \tau x^2}.
$$

For $\operatorname{Im}\tau > 0$ the function $f$ is a Schwartz function (a Gaussian with negative real part in the exponent after the rotation), so the **Poisson summation formula** applies:

$$
\sum_{n} f(n) = \sum_{m} \hat f(m), \qquad \hat f(m) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{\pi i \tau x^2} e^{-2\pi i m x}\, dx.
$$

Complete the square in the exponent: $\pi i \tau x^2 - 2\pi i m x = \pi i \tau (x - m/\tau)^2 - \pi i m^2/\tau$. The Gaussian integral evaluates (rotating the contour, valid since $\operatorname{Im}\tau > 0$) to

$$
\hat f(m) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{-i\tau}}\, e^{-\pi i m^2/\tau}.
$$

Substituting back,

$$
\theta_3(0\mid\tau) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{-i\tau}} \sum_{m} e^{-\pi i m^2/\tau} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{-i\tau}}\,\theta_3\!\left(0 \;\middle|\; -\tfrac{1}{\tau}\right),
$$

since replacing $\tau$ by $-1/\tau$ in the defining series sends $e^{\pi i \tau m^2}$ to $e^{-\pi i m^2/\tau}$. Rearranging gives $\theta_3(0\mid-1/\tau) = \sqrt{-i\tau}\,\theta_3(0\mid\tau)$.

The same Poisson computation applied to the shifted lattices (half-integer offsets in $n$ or alternating signs) yields the companion rules; the half-integer offset that defines $\theta_2$ is exchanged with the alternating sign that defines $\theta_4$ under $\tau \mapsto -1/\tau$, which is the permutation $\theta_2 \leftrightarrow \theta_4$. Under $\tau \mapsto \tau + 1$ the nome picks up $q \mapsto e^{\pi i}q = -q$, fixing $\theta_4$ and exchanging $\theta_2$ with $\theta_3$ up to a root of unity. $\square$

**Bridge.** This transformation law builds toward the entire theory of modular forms [21.04.01] and appears again in the modularity proof for the elliptic genus [03.06.23], where $\theta_1$'s rule is the load-bearing input. The foundational reason the rule holds is self-duality of the Gaussian under the Fourier transform: Poisson summation converts a sum over a lattice into a sum over its dual, and the dual of $\mathbb{Z}$ rescaled by $\tau$ is $\mathbb{Z}$ rescaled by $-1/\tau$. This is exactly the analytic substrate that the rigidity theorem [03.06.24] invokes without deriving, and the central insight is that modular invariance of physical partition functions is the visible face of this lattice duality. Putting these together, the theta multiplier $\sqrt{-i\tau}$ is the half-integral weight that makes $\eta^{24}$ and $\Delta$ honest modular forms.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify the quasi-periodicity relation $\theta_3(z + \pi\tau \mid \tau) = q^{-1} e^{-2iz}\,\theta_3(z\mid\tau)$ directly from the series.

Shift $z \mapsto z + \pi\tau$ inside $e^{2inz}$, then re-index the summation by $n \mapsto n - 1$.

Substituting, $\theta_3(z + \pi\tau) = \sum_n q^{n^2} e^{2in(z + \pi\tau)} = \sum_n q^{n^2} e^{2inz} e^{2\pi i n\tau}$. **Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that $\theta_1(z\mid\tau)$ is an odd function of $z$ and deduce $\theta_1(0\mid\tau) = 0$.

Replace $z$ by $-z$ in the series and re-index $n \mapsto -n - 1$.

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

In the frequency convention used here ($e^{2inz}$ in each term), the theta functions satisfy a heat equation $\partial_\tau \theta = c\,\partial_z^2 \theta$ for a fixed constant $c$. Determine $c$ by matching the term-by-term derivatives for $\theta_3$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate $q^{n^2} e^{2inz} = e^{\pi i \tau n^2} e^{2inz}$ once in $\tau$ and twice in $z$, then take the ratio of the two results.

</details>

**$c = -\dfrac{\pi i}{4}$, equivalently $4i\,\partial_\tau\theta = \pi\,\partial_z^2\theta$.** For the general term $u_n = e^{\pi i \tau n^2} e^{2inz}$: $\partial_\tau u_n = \pi i n^2\, u_n$ and $\partial_z^2 u_n = (2in)^2 u_n = -4n^2 u_n$. </details>

\theta_1(z\mid\tau) = 2 q^{1/4}\sin z \prod_{n\ge1}(1 - q^{2n})(1 - q^{2n}e^{2iz})(1 - q^{2n}e^{-2iz}).

The factor $2\sin z = -i(e^{iz} - e^{-iz})$ supplies the simple zero of $\theta_1$ at $z = 0$ and at every lattice point, consistent with $\theta_1$ being the odd theta with one zero per fundamental cell. $\square$

## Advanced results [Master]

**Jacobi triple product.** The identity

$$
\theta_3(z\mid\tau) = \prod_{n=1}^{\infty}(1 - q^{2n})(1 + q^{2n-1}e^{2iz})(1 + q^{2n-1}e^{-2iz})
$$

factors the theta series into an infinite product. The proof identifies both sides as the unique (up to scale) holomorphic function with the prescribed quasi-periods and a single zero per cell at $z = \pi/2 + \pi\tau/2$, then fixes the constant by comparing the $q^0$ coefficient. Specialising $z$ recovers Euler's pentagonal number theorem and Gauss's identities; the product form makes the zeros of each theta function manifest, which the series does not. [ref: TODO_REF Mumford 1983]

**Theta-nullwerte and the quartic identity.** Writing $\vartheta_j = \theta_j(0\mid\tau)$, the product formula gives

$$
\vartheta_2 = 2q^{1/4}\prod_{n\ge1}(1-q^{2n})(1+q^{2n})^2, \quad
\vartheta_3 = \prod_{n\ge1}(1-q^{2n})(1+q^{2n-1})^2, \quad
\vartheta_4 = \prod_{n\ge1}(1-q^{2n})(1-q^{2n-1})^2.
$$

Jacobi's **aequatio identica satis abstrusa** (his own phrase) is the quartic relation

$$
\vartheta_3^4 = \vartheta_2^4 + \vartheta_4^4,
$$

a polynomial identity among the three nullwerte that is equivalent to the statement that the modular lambda function $\lambda = \vartheta_2^4/\vartheta_3^4$ satisfies $\lambda + (1 - \lambda) = 1$ with $1 - \lambda = \vartheta_4^4/\vartheta_3^4$. This single identity is the analytic core of the level-two modular structure.

**Relation to the Weierstrass $\wp$-function.** The logarithmic second derivative of $\theta_1$ recovers the elliptic function machinery of [06.01.25]. Working with the lattice of periods $\pi, \pi\tau$,

$$
\wp(u\mid\tau) = -\frac{d^2}{du^2}\log\theta_1(u\mid\tau) - \frac{\pi^2}{3}\,\frac{\theta_1'''(0)}{\theta_1'(0)},
$$

where the constant is fixed so that the Laurent expansion of $\wp$ at $u = 0$ has no constant term. Because $\theta_1$ has exactly one simple zero per cell, $\partial_u^2\log\theta_1$ has exactly one double pole per cell, matching $\wp$. The theta functions thereby supply a holomorphic "potential" whose curvature is the meromorphic $\wp$.

**Modular discriminant and the eta function.** Combining the nullwerte product formulas yields $\vartheta_2\vartheta_3\vartheta_4 = 2\eta(\tau)^3$, where $\eta(\tau) = q^{1/12}\prod_{n\ge1}(1-q^{2n})$ is the Dedekind eta function. Hence the modular discriminant is

$$
\Delta(\tau) = (2\pi)^{12}\eta(\tau)^{24} = 2^{-8}(2\pi)^{12}(\vartheta_2\vartheta_3\vartheta_4)^8,
$$

a weight-$12$ cusp form on $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$. The $j$-invariant of [06.01.26] is then $j = E_4^3/\Delta$ up to normalisation, tying the theta nullwerte to the entire modular hierarchy.

**Synthesis.** The four Jacobi theta functions are the foundational reason that elliptic and modular objects share one analytic substrate, and the central insight is that a single Gaussian lattice sum is simultaneously a section of a line bundle on the torus, a solution of the heat equation, and a half-integral-weight modular form. This is exactly the structure that the modular transformation law packages: Poisson summation is dual to lattice rescaling, the triple product is dual to the prescribed zero divisor, and the quartic identity is dual to the level-two covering of the modular curve. Putting these together, the relation $\Delta = 2^{-8}(\vartheta_2\vartheta_3\vartheta_4)^8$ generalises from genus one to the Riemann theta on Jacobians [06.06.05], and the bridge from $\theta_1$ to $\wp$ generalises the Weierstrass factorisation [06.01.25] to the modular setting. The pattern recurs in the elliptic genus [03.06.23], whose modularity is the theta transformation law in disguise, and in the Witten genus, where the theta substrate of loop-space index theory is the same Gaussian self-duality seen here.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Quasi-periodicity multipliers of $\theta_1$).** *The odd theta function satisfies $\theta_1(z + \pi\mid\tau) = -\theta_1(z\mid\tau)$ and $\theta_1(z + \pi\tau\mid\tau) = -q^{-1}e^{-2iz}\theta_1(z\mid\tau)$.*

*Proof.* From $\theta_1(z) = -i\sum_n (-1)^n q^{(n+1/2)^2} e^{(2n+1)iz}$, the shift $z \mapsto z + \pi$ multiplies the term of index $n$ by $e^{(2n+1)\pi i} = e^{\pi i} = -1$ for every $n$ (since $2n$ is even), giving $\theta_1(z+\pi) = -\theta_1(z)$. For the second shift, $z \mapsto z + \pi\tau$ multiplies the index-$n$ term by $e^{(2n+1)\pi i \tau} = q^{2n+1}$. The summand becomes $-i(-1)^n q^{(n+1/2)^2 + 2n + 1} e^{(2n+1)iz}$. Now $(n + 1/2)^2 + 2n + 1 = n^2 + n + 1/4 + 2n + 1 = (n+3/2)^2 - 1 = (n' + 1/2)^2 - 1$ with $n' = n + 1$. Re-indexing $n \mapsto n' = n+1$ (which flips $(-1)^n$ to $-(-1)^{n'}$) and factoring $q^{-1}$ and the residual $e^{-2iz}$ from the shift of frequency yields $\theta_1(z + \pi\tau) = -q^{-1}e^{-2iz}\theta_1(z)$. $\square$

**Proposition (Theta-multiplier consistency).** *The transformation $\theta_1(z\mid -1/\tau) = -i(-i\tau)^{1/2} e^{i\tau z^2/\pi}\,\theta_1(z\tau\mid\tau)$ is consistent with the period and the $-1/\tau$ rules generating a projective $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ action.*

*Proof.* Apply the Poisson-summation argument of the Key Theorem to the full $z$-dependent series rather than the nullwert. Completing the square in $\pi i \tau (n + 1/2)^2 + (2n+1)iz$ produces a Gaussian whose Fourier dual reproduces the series for $\theta_1$ with $\tau$ replaced by $-1/\tau$, $z$ rescaled to $z\tau$, and an overall Gaussian prefactor $e^{i\tau z^2/\pi}$ together with the half-integral multiplier $(-i\tau)^{1/2}$. The factor $-i$ is fixed by matching $\theta_1'(0)$ on both sides. Composing this with $\tau \mapsto \tau + 1$ (which sends $q \mapsto -q$ and multiplies $\theta_1$ by $e^{\pi i/4}$) realises the two generators $S = \begin{psmallmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{psmallmatrix}$ and $T = \begin{psmallmatrix}1&1\\0&1\end{psmallmatrix}$. The associated multipliers form an eighth-root-of-unity system, so the action is projective; passing to $\theta_1^8$ or to $\Delta = 2^{-8}(\vartheta_2\vartheta_3\vartheta_4)^8$ clears the multiplier and gives an honest weight-$12$ modular form. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Weierstrass $\wp$-function [06.01.25].** The function $\wp$ is the negative second logarithmic derivative of $\theta_1$ (after rescaling the lattice to periods $\pi, \pi\tau$): the single simple zero of $\theta_1$ per cell becomes the single double pole of $\wp$ per cell. Where [06.01.25] builds elliptic functions from lattice sums with subtracted corrections, the theta presentation builds the same field from a holomorphic potential, and the addition formula for $\wp$ becomes the three-term Riemann theta relation among shifted $\theta_1$ values.

- **Modular function and $j$-invariant [06.01.26].** The theta-nullwerte assemble into the lambda function $\lambda = \vartheta_2^4/\vartheta_3^4$, a Hauptmodul for the level-two congruence subgroup, and the $j$-invariant is the degree-six rational function of $\lambda$ given by $j = 256(1-\lambda+\lambda^2)^3/(\lambda^2(1-\lambda)^2)$. The quartic identity $\vartheta_3^4 = \vartheta_2^4 + \vartheta_4^4$ is exactly the relation $\lambda + (1-\lambda) = 1$ that makes this rational map well-defined on the modular curve.

- **Modular forms on $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ [21.04.01].** The transformation law derived here exhibits each theta-nullwert as a modular form of half-integral weight $1/2$ for a congruence subgroup, and the product $\Delta = 2^{-8}(2\pi)^{12}(\vartheta_2\vartheta_3\vartheta_4)^8 = (2\pi)^{12}\eta^{24}$ is the weight-$12$ cusp form generating the ideal of cusp forms. Thus the theory of [21.04.01] inherits its first explicit cusp form from the theta machinery.

- **Elliptic genus [03.06.23].** The Ochanine and Witten genera are built from the power series $x/2 \cdot \theta_1'(0)/\theta_1(x/2)$ (up to normalisation), so the modularity of these genera is precisely the theta transformation law proved here applied with a formal variable. The rigidity theorem [03.06.24] consumes $\theta_1(z/\tau, -1/\tau) = -i(-i\tau)^{1/2}e^{i\pi z^2/\tau}\theta_1(z,\tau)$ as its key analytic input, which this unit supplies.

- **Riemann theta on Jacobians [06.06.05].** The genus-$g$ Riemann theta $\Theta$ is a multivariable generalisation summed over $\mathbb{Z}^g$ with a period matrix in place of the single $\tau$; in genus one it reduces to $\theta_3$. The two objects are different — capital $\Theta$ is a function of $g$ complex variables and a symmetric period matrix, while the Jacobi thetas here are functions of one variable and one $\tau$ — but they share the Poisson-summation transformation principle, generalised to the full symplectic group $\mathrm{Sp}(2g,\mathbb{Z})$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Jacobi 1829 [ref: TODO_REF Jacobi 1829], in *Fundamenta Nova Theoriae Functionum Ellipticarum* (Konigsberg), introduced the theta functions as the fundamental building blocks of elliptic-function theory, deriving the triple product and the quartic identity he memorably labelled *aequatio identica satis abstrusa* — "a rather abstruse identity." Jacobi's insight was that the awkward double periodicity of elliptic functions could be traded for the cleaner quasi-periodicity of theta functions, which are entire rather than meromorphic. This trade — meromorphic functions as ratios of holomorphic sections — is the historical seed of the modern theory of line bundles on abelian varieties.

The modular transformation $\theta_3(0\mid-1/\tau) = \sqrt{-i\tau}\,\theta_3(0\mid\tau)$ was already implicit in work on the heat equation and theta-series reciprocity; its derivation by Poisson summation became the template for the functional equations of Riemann's zeta function (via the theta-zeta integral) and of general $L$-functions. Whittaker and Watson [ref: TODO_REF Whittaker-Watson Ch. 21] codified the four-function notation that remains standard, and Mumford's *Tata Lectures on Theta* [ref: TODO_REF Mumford 1983] recast the whole subject in the language of algebraic geometry, presenting the Jacobi thetas as the genus-one case of theta functions on abelian varieties and connecting them to the representation theory of the Heisenberg group. Chandrasekharan [ref: TODO_REF Chandrasekharan 1985] gives the bridge from the nullwerte to the modular forms of level one.
