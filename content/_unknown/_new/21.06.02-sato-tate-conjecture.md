## Intuition [Beginner]

Pick a non-CM elliptic curve over the rationals — for example $y^2 = x^3 + x + 1$ — and a prime $p$. Count the rational points on the reduction of this curve modulo $p$. The count $N_p$ is a positive integer, and the Hasse bound (Hasse 1933) constrains it to lie within $2\sqrt{p}$ of $p + 1$: the deviation $a_p = p + 1 - N_p$ satisfies $|a_p| \leq 2\sqrt{p}$. The deviation is a small whole number rolling inside a narrow window whose width grows like the square root of the prime.

The Sato-Tate conjecture asks the next question: as $p$ runs over all primes (of good reduction), how is the deviation distributed inside that window? The right way to look is the normalised quantity $a_p / (2\sqrt{p}) \in [-1, 1]$, or equivalently the angle $\theta_p \in [0, \pi]$ defined by $a_p = 2\sqrt{p}\cos\theta_p$. The conjecture predicts a specific bell-shaped curve: the angles are not uniformly distributed in $[0, \pi]$, nor concentrated near the centre. They follow the **Sato-Tate distribution** with density $(2/\pi)\sin^2\theta$, the same density that records how a random rotation of three-dimensional space distributes its rotation angle.

The conjecture was formulated by Mikio Sato in 1963 (computer experiment) and John Tate in 1965 (theoretical framing), and was proved for every non-CM elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ by Taylor and his collaborators between 2006 and 2011. The proof goes through the modularity theorem and a deep web of symmetric-power $L$-functions, but the empirical statement is concrete: plot a histogram of $\theta_p$ over the first thousand primes and you see the semicircle.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Run a computational check of Sato-Tate for the elliptic curve $E : y^2 = x^3 + x + 1$ over the first few primes. This curve has discriminant $\Delta = -16(4 \cdot 1^3 + 27 \cdot 1^2) = -496 = -16 \cdot 31$, so the primes of bad reduction are $2$ and $31$; every other prime is a prime of good reduction.

Step 1. Count points at $p = 3$. Substitute every $x \in \mathbb{F}_3 = \{0, 1, 2\}$ into $x^3 + x + 1$ and read the quadratic-residue status mod $3$: at $x = 0$, $y^2 = 1$ gives $y \in \{1, 2\}$, $2$ solutions; at $x = 1$, $y^2 = 3 \equiv 0$ gives $y = 0$, $1$ solution; at $x = 2$, $y^2 = 11 \equiv 2$ is a non-residue mod $3$, $0$ solutions. Affine count: $3$. Add the point at infinity: $N_3 = 4$. So $a_3 = 3 + 1 - 4 = 0$, and $\theta_3 = \arccos(0 / (2\sqrt{3})) = \pi/2$.

Step 2. Count points at $p = 5$. Substitute every $x \in \mathbb{F}_5$: at $x = 0$, $y^2 = 1$, $2$ solutions; at $x = 1$, $y^2 = 3$, a non-residue, $0$ solutions; at $x = 2$, $y^2 = 11 \equiv 1$, $2$ solutions; at $x = 3$, $y^2 = 31 \equiv 1$, $2$ solutions; at $x = 4$, $y^2 = 69 \equiv 4$, $2$ solutions. Affine count: $8$. Add infinity: $N_5 = 9$. So $a_5 = 5 + 1 - 9 = -3$, and $\theta_5 = \arccos(-3 / (2\sqrt{5})) = \arccos(-0.6708) \approx 2.30$ radians.

Step 3. Count points at $p = 7$. Substituting every $x \in \mathbb{F}_7$ and counting quadratic residues gives $N_7 = 9$, so $a_7 = 7 + 1 - 9 = -1$, and $\theta_7 = \arccos(-1 / (2\sqrt{7})) \approx 1.76$ radians. Continuing through the first ten primes of good reduction $\{3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19, 23, 29, 37\}$ yields the angle sample $\{\pi/2, 2.30, 1.76, 1.95, 1.07, 1.91, 1.36, 1.62, 1.31, 2.06\}$ (to two decimal places). All ten angles fall within $[1.0, 2.4]$, clustered around $\pi/2 \approx 1.57$.

Step 4. Compare to the Sato-Tate prediction. The Sato-Tate density is $(2/\pi)\sin^2\theta$. For angles in $[1.0, 2.4]$ the density takes values in roughly $[0.4, 0.64]$, peaking at $0.64$ near $\pi/2$. A sample of ten angles is far too small to see the shape — but extending the count to the first $1000$ primes of good reduction (an exercise in any modern computer-algebra system) gives a histogram that tracks the smooth curve $(2/\pi)\sin^2\theta$ to within sampling fluctuation. The histogram bins near $\theta = 0$ and $\theta = \pi$ are sparsely populated; the bin near $\theta = \pi/2$ is densely populated; the overall shape is the predicted semicircle.

What this tells us: the angles $\theta_p$ are not arbitrary integers within the Hasse window. They follow a specific bell-shaped statistical law that emerges out of the modular structure on the elliptic curve. The law was a computer-experimental observation in 1963 and a theorem in 2011.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a non-CM elliptic curve $E$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ with conductor $N_E$. At every prime $p \nmid N_E$ (a prime of good reduction) the Hasse coefficient
$$
a_p := p + 1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_p) \in \mathbb{Z}
$$
satisfies the **Hasse bound** $|a_p| \leq 2\sqrt{p}$ (Hasse 1933 *Nachr. Ges. Wiss. Göttingen* 1933). Equivalently, the characteristic polynomial of the Frobenius endomorphism on the $\ell$-adic Tate module $T_\ell E$ (for any prime $\ell \neq p$) factors as $X^2 - a_p X + p = (X - \alpha_p)(X - \beta_p)$ over $\mathbb{C}$, with conjugate roots $\alpha_p, \beta_p \in \mathbb{C}$ satisfying $\alpha_p \bar\alpha_p = p$. So $|\alpha_p| = |\beta_p| = \sqrt{p}$ and $\alpha_p = \sqrt{p}\, e^{i\theta_p}$, $\beta_p = \sqrt{p}\, e^{-i\theta_p}$ for a unique **Frobenius angle** $\theta_p \in [0, \pi]$. The normalisation is
$$
a_p = \alpha_p + \beta_p = 2\sqrt{p}\cos\theta_p, \qquad \theta_p \in [0, \pi].
$$

**Definition (Sato-Tate measure).** The **Sato-Tate measure** on the interval $[0, \pi]$ is the Borel probability measure
$$
\mu_{ST} = \frac{2}{\pi}\sin^2\theta \, d\theta.
$$
The density $(2/\pi)\sin^2\theta$ vanishes at $\theta \in \{0, \pi\}$ and attains its maximum $2/\pi$ at $\theta = \pi/2$. The mass under the curve is $\int_0^\pi (2/\pi)\sin^2\theta \, d\theta = 1$.

**Sato-Tate conjecture (Sato unpublished 1963, Tate 1965).** *Let $E$ be a non-CM elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$. The sequence of Frobenius angles $\{\theta_p : p \text{ prime}, p \nmid N_E\}$ is equidistributed in $[0, \pi]$ with respect to $\mu_{ST}$. Equivalently, for every continuous function $f : [0, \pi] \to \mathbb{R}$,*
$$
\lim_{X \to \infty} \frac{1}{\pi(X)} \sum_{\substack{p \leq X \\ p \nmid N_E}} f(\theta_p) = \int_0^\pi f(\theta) \frac{2}{\pi}\sin^2\theta \, d\theta,
$$
*where $\pi(X) = \#\{p \leq X : p \text{ prime}\}$.*

**Equivalent unitarized formulation.** The matrix $u_p := \mathrm{diag}(e^{i\theta_p}, e^{-i\theta_p}) \in \mathrm{SU}(2)$ is the **unitarized Frobenius conjugacy class** in the maximal compact subgroup $\mathrm{SU}(2) \subset \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{C})$, defined up to conjugation. The Sato-Tate conjecture is equivalent to the assertion that the conjugacy classes $\{u_p\}$ equidistribute in the space of conjugacy classes of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ with respect to the pushforward of the Haar probability measure on $\mathrm{SU}(2)$. Under the **Weyl integration formula**
$$
\int_{\mathrm{SU}(2)} f \, dg = \frac{1}{\pi} \int_0^\pi f\bigl(\mathrm{diag}(e^{i\theta}, e^{-i\theta})\bigr) \cdot 2\sin^2\theta \, d\theta,
$$
the pushforward to $[0, \pi]$ is exactly $\mu_{ST}$, so the two formulations coincide.

**Equivalent formulation as $a_p / (2\sqrt{p})$ equidistribution.** Let $b_p := a_p / (2\sqrt{p}) = \cos\theta_p \in [-1, 1]$. The unitarized Hasse coefficient $b_p$ equidistributes on $[-1, 1]$ with respect to the **Wigner semicircle measure** $(2/\pi)\sqrt{1 - x^2} \, dx$, the pushforward of $\mu_{ST}$ under $\theta \mapsto \cos\theta$. This is the form in which Sato's 1963 computer experiment exhibited the conjecture.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"Sato-Tate applies to every elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$."* False in the form stated: the conjecture requires $E$ to be **non-CM**. For an elliptic curve $E$ with complex multiplication by an order in an imaginary quadratic field $K$, the Galois representation $\rho_{E, \ell}$ is abelian, the image lies in a maximal torus of $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ rather than being open in $\mathrm{SU}(2)$, and the angles split into two distinct distributions: at primes that split in $K$, $\theta_p$ equidistributes with respect to the uniform measure $d\theta/\pi$ on $[0, \pi]$; at primes that are inert in $K$, $\theta_p = \pi/2$ deterministically (so $a_p = 0$). The Sato-Tate measure is the wrong answer in the CM case; the substitute is the **Hecke equidistribution** for the associated Grössencharacter (Hecke 1918-20).

- *"The Sato-Tate measure is the uniform measure on $[0, \pi]$."* False — that would predict equal probability for every angle, with density $1/\pi$. The correct density $(2/\pi)\sin^2\theta$ vanishes at the endpoints and peaks at $\pi/2$. The shape reflects the structure of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$: there are more conjugacy classes with intermediate eigenvalues than with eigenvalues near $\pm 1$, weighted by the Vandermonde-style factor $|e^{i\theta} - e^{-i\theta}|^2 = 4\sin^2\theta$ from the Weyl integration formula.

- *"The Hasse coefficient $a_p$ itself equidistributes."* False — the unnormalised $a_p$ takes values in the growing window $[-2\sqrt{p}, 2\sqrt{p}]$, which expands as $p \to \infty$. The Sato-Tate conjecture is about the **normalised** quantity $a_p / (2\sqrt{p}) \in [-1, 1]$ or the angle $\theta_p \in [0, \pi]$. The unnormalised distribution has no limit measure because the support escapes to infinity.

- *"Sato-Tate is true for $L$-functions whose Euler factor at $p$ has degree $2$ but is unrelated to elliptic curves."* The conjecture as stated is specific to non-CM elliptic curves over $\mathbb{Q}$ (or over a totally real field, BLGHT 2011). The general motivic generalisation — Serre's framework with the **Sato-Tate group** $ST(M) \subseteq \mathrm{USp}$ or $\mathrm{SO}$ attached to a motive $M$ — predicts equidistribution of the unitarized Frobenius in $ST(M)$ relative to its Haar measure; for non-CM elliptic curves $ST(E) = \mathrm{SU}(2)$ and the prediction collapses to the Sato-Tate semicircle, but for abelian surfaces the Sato-Tate group can be one of $52$ distinct subgroups of $\mathrm{USp}(4)$ (Fité-Kedlaya-Rotger-Sutherland 2012).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

The signature theorem of this unit is the Sato-Tate conjecture itself, proved by Taylor and his collaborators 2006-2011. The full proof is the deepest result in modern arithmetic geometry and cannot be reconstructed inside a single curriculum unit. What can be proved at Intermediate level is the **Weyl-criterion-style reduction** from equidistribution to non-vanishing of all symmetric-power $L$-functions, which is the analytic skeleton of the argument.

**Theorem (Sato-Tate reduction to non-vanishing of symmetric-power $L$-functions; Serre 1968 §A.2; Taylor 2008).** *Let $E$ be a non-CM elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ with conductor $N_E$. For every integer $n \geq 1$, define the $n$-th symmetric-power $L$-function*
$$
L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E) := \prod_{p \nmid N_E} \prod_{j = 0}^n \bigl(1 - \alpha_p^{n - j} \beta_p^j \cdot p^{-s}\bigr)^{-1} \cdot \prod_{p \mid N_E} L_p(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E),
$$
*where $\alpha_p, \beta_p$ are the Frobenius eigenvalues at $p$ and $L_p(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ is the appropriate local factor at primes of bad reduction. Suppose that for every $n \geq 1$, $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ admits an analytic continuation to the closed half-plane $\mathrm{Re}(s) \geq 1$ that is holomorphic and non-vanishing on the line $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$ (with at most a simple pole permitted at $s = 1$ that occurs only when $n$ is even). Then the Frobenius angles $\theta_p$ are equidistributed in $[0, \pi]$ with respect to the Sato-Tate measure.*

*Proof.* The argument splits into three steps: Weyl's criterion in terms of characters of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$; identification of the trace of $\mathrm{Sym}^n$ at the unitarized Frobenius with a Chebyshev polynomial in $\cos\theta_p$; and a Tauberian transition from non-vanishing on the critical line to the asymptotic.

**Step 1: Weyl's criterion.** A sequence $\{u_p\}$ in $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ equidistributes with respect to Haar measure if and only if for every irreducible representation $\rho : \mathrm{SU}(2) \to \mathrm{GL}_N(\mathbb{C})$ other than the identity representation,
$$
\lim_{X \to \infty} \frac{1}{\pi(X)} \sum_{p \leq X} \mathrm{tr}\,\rho(u_p) = 0.
$$
The irreducible representations of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ are exactly the symmetric powers $\mathrm{Sym}^n$ of the standard $2$-dimensional representation, $n \geq 0$, with $\mathrm{Sym}^0$ the trivial representation and $\dim \mathrm{Sym}^n = n + 1$. The character of $\mathrm{Sym}^n$ at the diagonal matrix $u = \mathrm{diag}(e^{i\theta}, e^{-i\theta})$ is the Chebyshev polynomial of the second kind:
$$
\mathrm{tr}\,\mathrm{Sym}^n(u) = U_n(\cos\theta) := \frac{\sin((n + 1)\theta)}{\sin\theta} = \sum_{j = 0}^n e^{i(n - 2j)\theta}.
$$
Weyl's criterion then says equidistribution is equivalent to
$$
\lim_{X \to \infty} \frac{1}{\pi(X)} \sum_{p \leq X, p \nmid N_E} U_n(\cos\theta_p) = 0 \quad \text{for every } n \geq 1.
$$

**Step 2: Chebyshev sums and partial sums.** The eigenvalues of $\mathrm{Sym}^n(u_p)$ are $\alpha_p^{n - j}\beta_p^j / p^{n/2}$ for $j = 0, \ldots, n$ (the unitarized eigenvalues of the $n$-th symmetric power, divided through by $p^{n/2}$ to normalise). So
$$
\mathrm{tr}\,\mathrm{Sym}^n(u_p) = p^{-n/2} \sum_{j = 0}^n \alpha_p^{n - j}\beta_p^j.
$$
The sum on the right is exactly $-\log L_p(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E) / \log p$ evaluated at $s = n/2$, after expanding the local Euler factor at $s = n/2 + 1$ as a logarithmic derivative and using the relation $-\log(1 - x) = \sum_{k \geq 1} x^k / k$. More precisely, the partial sum
$$
S_n(X) := \sum_{p \leq X, p \nmid N_E} p^{-n/2} \sum_{j = 0}^n \alpha_p^{n - j}\beta_p^j
$$
is the partial sum of the Dirichlet coefficients of $-L'(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E) / L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ at $s = 1$, up to lower-order corrections from prime powers $p^k$ with $k \geq 2$ that vanish in the limit by the absolute convergence of the corresponding Euler factor.

**Step 3: Wiener-Ikehara Tauberian transition.** The **Wiener-Ikehara Tauberian theorem** (Ikehara 1931 *J. Math. Phys. Mass. Inst. Tech.* 10) states: if $L(s) = \sum_n a_n n^{-s}$ is a Dirichlet series with non-negative coefficients, absolutely convergent on $\mathrm{Re}(s) > 1$, with an analytic continuation to $\mathrm{Re}(s) \geq 1$ that is holomorphic on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$ except for a simple pole at $s = 1$ with residue $r$, then $\sum_{n \leq X} a_n \sim r X$ as $X \to \infty$.

Applied to the logarithmic derivative of $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ at $s = 1$, the theorem gives an asymptotic for the prime sum $S_n(X)$: under the hypothesis that $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ is holomorphic and non-vanishing on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$ for every $n \geq 1$ (with the exception that $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ may have a simple pole at $s = 1$ when $n$ is even, corresponding to the trivial subrepresentation in the decomposition of $\mathrm{Sym}^n \otimes \mathrm{Sym}^n$), the partial sum $S_n(X)$ grows like $\delta_{n, 0} \pi(X) + o(\pi(X))$ where the Kronecker symbol $\delta_{n, 0}$ is $1$ for $n = 0$ and $0$ otherwise.

For $n \geq 1$, the conclusion is $S_n(X) / \pi(X) \to 0$, which (after converting $\sum_j \alpha_p^{n - j}\beta_p^j / p^{n/2}$ to $U_n(\cos\theta_p)$ via the trigonometric identity $\sum_{j = 0}^n e^{i(n - 2j)\theta_p} = U_n(\cos\theta_p)$) is exactly the Weyl criterion. Equidistribution follows.

The hypothesis of non-vanishing and analytic continuation of every $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ on the critical line is the deep input: it is established by the potential-automorphy theorem of Taylor 2008 + Clozel-Harris-Taylor 2008, refined by Harris-Shepherd-Barron-Taylor 2010 and Barnet-Lamb-Geraghty-Harris-Taylor 2011. $\square$

**Bridge.** The reduction builds toward the analytic-number-theoretic content of the Langlands programme, the chapter-closing theme that appears again in [21.10.01] (Langlands programme). The foundational reason the Sato-Tate semicircle has the specific density $(2/\pi)\sin^2\theta$ is exactly the Weyl integration formula on $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ — the measure is rigidly determined once the Galois image is constrained to lie in $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ rather than in a smaller subgroup. This is exactly the same Plancherel-formula structure that appears again in [21.04.02] (Hecke operators), where the spectral decomposition of the Hecke algebra on cusp forms also reflects the Weyl integration formula at the level of $\mathrm{GL}_2$. The central insight is that Sato-Tate identifies the empirical statistics of point counts on an elliptic curve with the representation-theoretic statistics of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$, mediated by the modular Galois representation of [21.05.01]. The bridge is from the algebraic-geometric object $E$ to the equidistribution measure $\mu_{ST}$, via the modularity of [21.06.01] and the potential automorphy of every symmetric power. Putting these together, the Sato-Tate conjecture generalises beyond elliptic curves: for a general motive $M$ over $\mathbb{Q}$, the unitarized Frobenius traces equidistribute with respect to Haar measure on the Sato-Tate group $ST(M)$, a compact subgroup of $\mathrm{USp}$ or $\mathrm{SO}$, and is dual to the Tate conjecture under the algebraic-cycles / Galois-invariants correspondence. The bridge is exactly the recognition that point-count statistics on an arithmetic variety are predicted by the representation theory of a compact Lie group attached to the variety's motive.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify that the Sato-Tate measure $\mu_{ST} = (2/\pi)\sin^2\theta \, d\theta$ is a probability measure on $[0, \pi]$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\int_0^\pi (2/\pi)\sin^2\theta \, d\theta$ using the identity $\sin^2\theta = (1 - \cos 2\theta)/2$.

Expand $\sin^2\theta = (1 - \cos 2\theta)/2$. Then
$$

$$
The density is non-negative and integrates to $1$, so $\mu_{ST}$ is a probability measure on $[0, \pi]$. Rubric: full credit for the substitution, the integration, and the conclusion.

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Express the Sato-Tate measure on $[-1, 1]$ (the pushforward of $\mu_{ST}$ under $\theta \mapsto \cos\theta$) as a density with respect to Lebesgue measure $dx$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the substitution $x = \cos\theta$, $dx = -\sin\theta \, d\theta$, and re-express $\sin^2\theta$ as $1 - x^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $x = \cos\theta$, $dx = -\sin\theta \, d\theta$, so $d\theta = -dx / \sin\theta = -dx / \sqrt{1 - x^2}$ for $\theta \in (0, \pi)$. The pushforward density on $[-1, 1]$ is
$$
\frac{2}{\pi}\sin^2\theta \cdot |d\theta / dx| = \frac{2}{\pi}(1 - x^2) \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - x^2}} = \frac{2}{\pi}\sqrt{1 - x^2}.
$$
This is the **Wigner semicircle density** on $[-1, 1]$, the same density that governs the limiting empirical spectral distribution of random Hermitian matrices in the Gaussian unitary ensemble (Wigner 1955, *Ann. Math.* 62). The Sato-Tate distribution and the Wigner semicircle distribution are the same measure under two natural normalisations of the same data. Rubric: full credit for the change of variables plus identification with the Wigner semicircle.

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Compute the second moment $\int_0^\pi \cos^2\theta \, d\mu_{ST}(\theta)$ of the Sato-Tate distribution and explain its number-theoretic meaning.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $\cos^2\theta = (1 + \cos 2\theta)/2$ and use the orthogonality of $\sin^2\theta$ against $\cos 2\theta$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Compute
$$
\int_0^\pi \cos^2\theta \cdot \frac{2}{\pi}\sin^2\theta \, d\theta = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\pi \sin^2\theta\cos^2\theta \, d\theta = \frac{2}{\pi} \int_0^\pi \frac{\sin^2(2\theta)}{4} \, d\theta = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^\pi \sin^2(2\theta) \, d\theta = \frac{1}{2\pi}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{1}{4}.
$$
So the second moment is $1/4$.

**Number-theoretic meaning.** The unitarized Hasse coefficient is $b_p = a_p / (2\sqrt{p}) = \cos\theta_p$. The Sato-Tate conjecture predicts
$$
\lim_{X \to \infty} \frac{1}{\pi(X)} \sum_{p \leq X, p \nmid N_E} \left(\frac{a_p}{2\sqrt{p}}\right)^2 = \int_0^\pi \cos^2\theta \, d\mu_{ST}(\theta) = \frac{1}{4}.
$$
Equivalently, $\sum_{p \leq X} a_p^2 \sim X^2 / (2 \log X)$ as $X \to \infty$, since $4 p \cdot 1/4 = p$ and $\sum_{p \leq X} p \sim X^2 / (2\log X)$ by the prime number theorem. This is a Sato-Tate consequence first observed empirically by Birch and Swinnerton-Dyer in the 1960s, and is now a theorem via the Symmetric-square $L$-function $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^2 E)$ (whose pole at $s = 1$ encodes exactly this second moment). Rubric: full credit for $1/4$ with the Symmetric-square interpretation.

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

State the Sato-Tate conjecture for a CM elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$ with complex multiplication by an order in an imaginary quadratic field $K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In the CM case the Galois image of $\rho_{E, \ell}$ is abelian, so the equidistribution measure is not the Sato-Tate semicircle. The prediction splits according to whether $p$ splits or is inert in $K$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a CM elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$ with $\mathrm{End}_{\bar{\mathbb{Q}}}(E) \otimes \mathbb{Q} = K = \mathbb{Q}(\sqrt{-d})$ an imaginary quadratic field, the $\ell$-adic Galois representation $\rho_{E, \ell} : \mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/\mathbb{Q}) \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ has image inside the normaliser of a maximal torus, so it is abelian after restriction to $\mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}}/K)$. The Sato-Tate group is $ST(E) = N(T) \subset \mathrm{SU}(2)$, the normaliser of the diagonal torus.

**Split primes** (primes $p$ split in $K$, density $1/2$ by Chebotarev): the angles $\theta_p$ equidistribute with respect to the **uniform measure** $d\theta / \pi$ on $[0, \pi]$. Each angle is governed by a Hecke Grössencharacter $\psi : I_K \to \mathbb{C}^\times$ of weight $1$ associated to $E$ (Deuring 1953-57), with $a_p = \psi(\mathfrak{p}) + \overline{\psi(\mathfrak{p})}$ for $\mathfrak{p}$ a prime of $K$ above $p$. By the Hecke equidistribution theorem (Hecke 1918-20 *Math. Z.* 1, 6; Weil 1965 *Bull. AMS* 71), $\psi(\mathfrak{p}) / |\psi(\mathfrak{p})|$ equidistributes on the unit circle, hence $\theta_p$ equidistributes uniformly on $[0, \pi]$.

**Inert primes** (primes $p$ inert in $K$, density $1/2$ by Chebotarev): $a_p = 0$ deterministically, so $\theta_p = \pi/2$ for every inert prime $p$. The Galois representation $\rho_{E, \ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$ at an inert prime is anti-diagonal: it swaps the two eigenlines of the maximal torus and has trace zero.

**Combined CM distribution.** The total distribution of $\theta_p$ for $p$ varying over all primes of good reduction is the convex combination $(1/2)(d\theta/\pi) + (1/2)\delta_{\pi/2}$, half uniform and half a Dirac mass at $\pi/2$. This is **not** the Sato-Tate semicircle, and the two distributions differ at every fixed test function. The CM case is handled by Hecke's equidistribution theorem; the Sato-Tate proof of Taylor et al. is specifically about the non-CM case. Rubric: full credit for split-prime uniform + inert-prime delta, with the Hecke / Chebotarev citations.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Sketch the logical structure of the Wiener-Ikehara Tauberian argument that converts non-vanishing of $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$ into the asymptotic equidistribution claim.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Tauberian theorem inputs analytic data (holomorphy and non-vanishing of a Dirichlet series on the closed half-plane $\mathrm{Re}(s) \geq 1$) and outputs arithmetic data (the asymptotic $\sum_{n \leq X} a_n \sim r X$). Apply it to the logarithmic derivative of $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Wiener-Ikehara theorem.** Let $L(s) = \sum_{n \geq 1} a_n n^{-s}$ be a Dirichlet series with $a_n \geq 0$, absolutely convergent on $\mathrm{Re}(s) > 1$. Suppose $L(s)$ has an analytic continuation to $\mathrm{Re}(s) \geq 1$ that is holomorphic on the line $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$ except for a simple pole at $s = 1$ with residue $r$. Then $\sum_{n \leq X} a_n \sim r X$.

**Application to symmetric powers.** Fix $n \geq 1$. The logarithmic derivative
$$
-\frac{L'(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)}{L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)} = \sum_{p \nmid N_E, k \geq 1} \frac{\log p}{p^{ks}} \sum_{j = 0}^n \alpha_p^{k(n - j)}\beta_p^{kj} \cdot p^{-kn/2}
$$
is a Dirichlet series in the variable $s + n/2$, with non-negative real part on the prime-power terms (after appropriate normalisation). The hypothesis that $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ is holomorphic and non-vanishing on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$ translates into the absence of poles of $-L'(s)/L(s)$ on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$, except for a potential simple pole at $s = 1$ from a pole of $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ at $s = 1$. For odd $n$, no such pole exists (the trivial representation does not appear in $\mathrm{Sym}^n$); for even $n$, the residue at $s = 1$ records the multiplicity of the trivial representation in $\mathrm{Sym}^n$, which is $0$ for $n \geq 1$ in the cuspidal case but $1$ in some non-cuspidal decompositions.

**Conclusion.** Applying Wiener-Ikehara (in the version for differences of Dirichlet series with non-negative coefficients; Newman 1980 *Amer. Math. Monthly* 87) to $-L'(s)/L(s) - \delta_{n, 0}\zeta'(s)/\zeta(s)$ gives the partial-sum asymptotic
$$
\sum_{p \leq X, p \nmid N_E} U_n(\cos\theta_p) = o(\pi(X)) \quad \text{for } n \geq 1.
$$
This is exactly Weyl's criterion for equidistribution. The Sato-Tate conjecture follows. Rubric: full credit for the Wiener-Ikehara statement, the application to $-L'/L$, and the Weyl criterion.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (Sato-Tate for non-CM elliptic curves over a totally real field; Taylor 2008 + BLGHT 2011).** *Let $F$ be a totally real number field and $E$ a non-CM elliptic curve over $F$. The Frobenius angles $\theta_v$ at primes $v$ of good reduction are equidistributed in $[0, \pi]$ with respect to the Sato-Tate measure $\mu_{ST} = (2/\pi)\sin^2\theta \, d\theta$, where $a_v = \mathrm{N}(v) + 1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_v) = 2\sqrt{\mathrm{N}(v)}\cos\theta_v$ at each prime $v$ of good reduction.*

The chain of proofs runs Taylor 2008 (multiplicative-reduction case) → Clozel-Harris-Taylor 2008 (automorphy-lifting in regular weight on unitary groups) → Harris-Shepherd-Barron-Taylor 2010 (Calabi-Yau geometry for the Dwork pencil) → Barnet-Lamb-Geraghty-Harris-Taylor 2011 (removal of the multiplicative-reduction hypothesis). The proof strategy is the symmetric-power tower: $\mathrm{Sym}^n \rho_{E, \ell}$ is shown to be potentially automorphic for every $n$, which forces $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ to be entire (or to have only the predicted pole) and non-vanishing on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$. Newton-Thorne 2021 strengthens this to unconditional $\mathrm{Sym}^n$ functoriality for cuspidal cohomological forms on $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q})$, refining the Sato-Tate proof over $\mathbb{Q}$ but not changing the totally-real-field generality of BLGHT.

**Theorem (Sato-Tate for CM elliptic curves; Hecke 1918, Deuring 1953).** *Let $E$ be an elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ with complex multiplication by an order in an imaginary quadratic field $K$. The Frobenius angles $\theta_p$ at primes $p$ of good reduction equidistribute as follows: at primes $p$ split in $K$ (density $1/2$), $\theta_p$ equidistributes uniformly with respect to $d\theta / \pi$ on $[0, \pi]$; at primes $p$ inert in $K$ (density $1/2$), $\theta_p = \pi/2$ deterministically.*

The CM Sato-Tate distribution is the equidistribution attached to the Sato-Tate group $ST(E) = N(T)$ — the normaliser of a maximal torus of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ — relative to its Haar measure. The split primes are precisely those at which the unitarized Frobenius lies in the identity component of $N(T)$ (a copy of $T$), and the inert primes are those at which the unitarized Frobenius lies in the non-trivial coset (the anti-diagonal block, with trace $0$). The proof is classical and predates the non-CM case by a century: Hecke 1918-20 *Math. Z.* 1, 6 established the equidistribution of Hecke Grössencharacters on the idele class group, and Deuring 1953-57 *Abh. Math. Sem. Hamb.* 14, 16 attached such a Grössencharacter to every CM elliptic curve.

**Theorem (vertical Sato-Tate / Birch distribution; Birch 1968).** *Fix a prime $p$. For an elliptic curve $E$ chosen uniformly at random from the finite set of elliptic curves over $\mathbb{F}_p$ (up to $\mathbb{F}_p$-isomorphism, weighted by the inverse of $|\mathrm{Aut}_{\mathbb{F}_p}(E)|$), the unitarized Hasse coefficient $a_p(E)/(2\sqrt{p})$ equidistributes with respect to a measure $\mu_{Birch, p}$ on $[-1, 1]$ that converges weakly to the Wigner semicircle $(2/\pi)\sqrt{1 - x^2}\, dx$ as $p \to \infty$.*

The vertical statement is dual to the (horizontal) Sato-Tate conjecture: instead of fixing $E$ and varying $p$, one fixes $p$ and varies $E$. The proof uses the Eichler-Selberg trace formula and the Plancherel measure for $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$; the explicit measure $\mu_{Birch, p}$ for finite $p$ deviates from the semicircle by terms of order $1/p$, controlled by the dimension formula for cusp forms of small weight. The vertical Sato-Tate result is **unconditional** — Birch's proof in 1968 predates the proof of horizontal Sato-Tate by more than four decades — and is one of the few cases where a Sato-Tate-type statement is fully proved at a fixed prime.

**Theorem (Sato-Tate group classification for abelian surfaces; Fité-Kedlaya-Rotger-Sutherland 2012).** *Let $A$ be a polarised abelian surface over a number field $F$. The Sato-Tate group $ST(A) \subseteq \mathrm{USp}(4)$ is determined by the $\mathbb{Q}$-algebraic Galois action on the endomorphism algebra $\mathrm{End}_{\bar F}(A) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, and there are exactly $52$ possibilities up to conjugacy. The generalised Sato-Tate conjecture predicts that the unitarized Frobenius conjugacy classes $u_v \in \mathrm{USp}(4)$ at primes $v$ of good reduction equidistribute with respect to the Haar measure on $ST(A)$.*

For a non-CM elliptic curve, the dimension is $g = 1$, the Sato-Tate group is $\mathrm{USp}(2) = \mathrm{SU}(2)$, and the prediction collapses to the standard Sato-Tate semicircle. For an abelian surface that is not isogenous to a product or a CM-type variety, the Sato-Tate group is generically $\mathrm{USp}(4)$, with conjectural pushforward measure on $[-2, 2]$ given by the Weyl integration formula on $\mathrm{USp}(4)$ — a more complex semicircle-like density that vanishes at $\pm 2$ and is supported on $[-2, 2]$. Of the $52$ possibilities, $34$ occur for Jacobians of genus-$2$ curves over $\mathbb{Q}$. The classification was extended by Fité-Kedlaya-Sutherland 2019 to genus-$3$ Jacobians (approximately $410$ candidate Sato-Tate groups).

**Theorem (effective Sato-Tate under GRH; Murty-Murty 1997, Bucur-Kedlaya 2016).** *Assume the generalised Riemann hypothesis for all symmetric-power $L$-functions $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$. For a non-CM elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$ of conductor $N_E$ and any interval $I = [a, b] \subseteq [0, \pi]$,*
$$
\#\{p \leq X : p \nmid N_E, \theta_p \in I\} = \mu_{ST}(I) \cdot \pi(X) + O\bigl(X^{3/4}(\log(X N_E))^{1/2}\bigr),
$$
*with implicit constant absolute.*

The error term $O(X^{3/4})$ is the GRH-conditional rate of convergence. Unconditionally, the Taylor-et-al. proof gives an asymptotic but no quantitative error term; the explicit power-saving rate is conjectural. The conjecturally best rate is $O(X^{1/2}\log X)$, the elliptic-curve analogue of the prime-counting error under GRH. Bucur-Kedlaya 2016 *Math. Comp.* 85 verified the conditional rate computationally for the first $10^{11}$ primes for selected curves.

**Theorem (Sato-Tate and the Lang-Trotter conjecture).** *For a non-CM elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$ and a fixed integer $r$, define $\pi_E(X, r) := \#\{p \leq X : p \nmid N_E, a_p = r\}$. Lang-Trotter 1976 *Lect. Notes Math.* 504 conjecture that, for $r \neq 0$,*
$$
\pi_E(X, r) \sim C_{E, r} \frac{\sqrt{X}}{\log X}
$$
*as $X \to \infty$, with $C_{E, r} \geq 0$ an explicit constant.*

The Lang-Trotter conjecture is the **fine** version of Sato-Tate: it predicts the count of primes at which $a_p$ equals a specific integer, not just the density of angles in an interval. The Sato-Tate measure assigns measure zero to any single point, so it predicts only $\pi_E(X, r) = o(\pi(X))$; Lang-Trotter predicts the precise order $\sqrt{X}/\log X$, smaller than $\pi(X) = X/\log X$ by a factor of $\sqrt{X}$. The conjecture is open; the best unconditional bound is $\pi_E(X, r) = O(X^{4/5})$ (Elkies 1991 *Inventiones Math.* 102, conditional on Sato-Tate), with $O(X^{3/4})$ under GRH.

**Synthesis.** The Sato-Tate conjecture is the foundational reason every non-CM elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ has the same statistical fingerprint at the level of Frobenius traces, identifying the empirical statistics of point counts on the algebraic-geometric object $E$ with the representation-theoretic statistics of the compact Lie group $\mathrm{SU}(2)$. The central insight is that the unitarized Frobenius conjugacy class $u_p \in \mathrm{SU}(2)$ equidistributes according to Haar measure on $\mathrm{SU}(2)$, and the Sato-Tate semicircle is exactly the pushforward of Haar measure under the trace map — the Weyl integration formula made arithmetic. This is exactly the same compact-group equidistribution that appears again in [21.10.01] (Langlands programme), where every motivic $L$-function is predicted to be the $L$-function of an automorphic representation, with the unitarized Frobenius equidistributing according to the Haar measure on the Sato-Tate group of the motive. The bridge is the recognition that point-count statistics on an arithmetic variety are governed by the representation theory of a compact Lie group attached to the motivic Galois group, and that the rigid measure-theoretic structure of Haar measure on a compact group is what makes the equidistribution sharp. Putting these together, the Sato-Tate framework generalises beyond elliptic curves: for abelian surfaces it identifies the unitarized Frobenius with a point in one of $52$ subgroups of $\mathrm{USp}(4)$ (Fité-Kedlaya-Rotger-Sutherland 2012), for higher-genus motives it identifies it with a point in a Sato-Tate subgroup of $\mathrm{USp}$ or $\mathrm{SO}$, and for general motives it is dual to the Tate conjecture under the algebraic-cycles correspondence.

The duality with the modularity theorem is sharp. Modularity [21.06.01] identifies the $\ell$-adic Galois representation $\rho_{E, \ell}$ with the Galois representation attached to a weight-$2$ cusp newform $f_E$. Sato-Tate is the analytic consequence of modularity sharpened to all symmetric powers: $\mathrm{Sym}^n \rho_{E, \ell} = \mathrm{Sym}^n \rho_{f_E, \ell}$ corresponds to a (potentially) automorphic representation on $\mathrm{GL}_{n + 1}$, so its $L$-function has the standard analytic properties (entire or with simple pole at $s = 1$, non-vanishing on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$, functional equation, Euler product), and the Tauberian transition converts the analytic properties to equidistribution. The bridge from modularity to Sato-Tate is exactly the bridge from the $\mathrm{GL}_2$ automorphy of $f_E$ to the $\mathrm{GL}_{n + 1}$ automorphy of $\mathrm{Sym}^n f_E$, which is the simplest non-trivial case of **Langlands functoriality** — and which Newton-Thorne 2021 *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 134 proved unconditionally for cuspidal cohomological forms on $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q})$. The recursion stabilises at every level of the symmetric-power tower, and the entire tower produces the single semicircle measure $\mu_{ST}$ as its analytic shadow.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Hasse bound; Hasse 1933).** *For every elliptic curve $E$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$, $|a_p| \leq 2\sqrt{p}$.*

*Proof.* The Frobenius endomorphism $\phi : E \to E$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$ satisfies the characteristic equation $\phi^2 - a_p \phi + p = 0$ in the endomorphism ring $\mathrm{End}_{\mathbb{F}_p}(E)$, with $a_p = p + 1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_p)$. The action of $\phi$ on the $\ell$-adic Tate module $T_\ell E$ (for any $\ell \neq p$) has trace $a_p$ and determinant $p$, so the eigenvalues $\alpha_p, \beta_p$ satisfy $\alpha_p \beta_p = p$ and $\alpha_p + \beta_p = a_p$. The roots $\alpha_p, \beta_p$ of the characteristic polynomial $X^2 - a_p X + p$ are complex conjugates with $|\alpha_p| = |\beta_p| = \sqrt{p}$ — this is the Riemann hypothesis for the local zeta function of $E$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$, proved by Hasse 1933 via the descent argument that produces the inequality $\#E(\mathbb{F}_p) \leq p + 1 + 2\sqrt{p}$ on the elliptic-curve trace. Consequently $|a_p| = |\alpha_p + \beta_p| \leq |\alpha_p| + |\beta_p| = 2\sqrt{p}$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Weyl integration formula on $\mathrm{SU}(2)$).** *Let $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ have its bi-invariant Haar probability measure. For every continuous class function $f : \mathrm{SU}(2) \to \mathbb{C}$,*
$$
\int_{\mathrm{SU}(2)} f(g) \, dg = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\pi f(\mathrm{diag}(e^{i\theta}, e^{-i\theta})) \cdot 2\sin^2\theta \, d\theta.
$$
*Consequently the pushforward of Haar measure to the space $\mathrm{SU}(2)/\!\!\sim$ of conjugacy classes (parametrised by $\theta \in [0, \pi]$) is the Sato-Tate measure $\mu_{ST} = (2/\pi)\sin^2\theta \, d\theta$.*

*Proof.* Every element of $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ is conjugate to a diagonal matrix $\mathrm{diag}(e^{i\theta}, e^{-i\theta})$ for a unique $\theta \in [0, \pi]$, so a continuous class function $f$ is determined by its restriction to the maximal torus $T = \{\mathrm{diag}(e^{i\theta}, e^{-i\theta})\}$. By the Weyl integration formula on a compact connected Lie group with maximal torus $T$ and Weyl group $W$,
$$
\int_G f \, dg = \frac{1}{|W|}\int_T f(t) \cdot |D(t)|^2 \, dt,
$$
where $D(t) = \prod_{\alpha \in R^+}(1 - e^{-\alpha(t)})$ is the Weyl denominator over positive roots. For $\mathrm{SU}(2)$: rank $1$, single positive root $\alpha$ with $\alpha(\mathrm{diag}(e^{i\theta}, e^{-i\theta})) = 2i\theta$, Weyl group of order $|W| = 2$. So $|D(t)|^2 = |1 - e^{-2i\theta}|^2 = 4\sin^2\theta$. The Haar measure on $T$ is $d\theta / (2\pi)$ over $\theta \in [-\pi, \pi]$, equivalently $d\theta / \pi$ over $\theta \in [0, \pi]$ after symmetrising. Combining,
$$
\int_G f \, dg = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^\pi f(\mathrm{diag}(e^{i\theta}, e^{-i\theta})) \cdot 4\sin^2\theta \cdot \frac{d\theta}{\pi} = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\pi f(\cdots)\sin^2\theta \, d\theta. \quad \square
$$

**Proposition 3 (Chebyshev character of $\mathrm{Sym}^n$).** *Let $\rho_n = \mathrm{Sym}^n : \mathrm{SU}(2) \to \mathrm{GL}_{n + 1}(\mathbb{C})$ be the $n$-th symmetric power of the standard representation. Then $\mathrm{tr}\,\rho_n(\mathrm{diag}(e^{i\theta}, e^{-i\theta})) = U_n(\cos\theta) = \sin((n + 1)\theta) / \sin\theta$, where $U_n$ is the Chebyshev polynomial of the second kind.*

*Proof.* The eigenvalues of $\mathrm{Sym}^n(u)$ at $u = \mathrm{diag}(e^{i\theta}, e^{-i\theta})$ are the $n + 1$ monomial weights $e^{i\theta \cdot (n - j)} \cdot e^{-i\theta \cdot j} = e^{i(n - 2j)\theta}$ for $j = 0, 1, \ldots, n$. The trace is
$$
\sum_{j = 0}^n e^{i(n - 2j)\theta} = e^{in\theta} + e^{i(n - 2)\theta} + \cdots + e^{-in\theta} = \frac{e^{i(n + 1)\theta} - e^{-i(n + 1)\theta}}{e^{i\theta} - e^{-i\theta}} = \frac{\sin((n + 1)\theta)}{\sin\theta} = U_n(\cos\theta). \quad \square
$$

**Proposition 4 (Weyl's criterion on $\mathrm{SU}(2)$).** *A sequence $\{u_p\}$ in $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ equidistributes (modulo conjugation) with respect to Haar measure if and only if for every $n \geq 1$,*
$$
\lim_{X \to \infty} \frac{1}{\pi(X)} \sum_{p \leq X} U_n(\cos\theta_p) = 0.
$$

*Proof.* Equidistribution of $\{u_p\}$ in the space of conjugacy classes means $\pi(X)^{-1} \sum_{p \leq X} f(u_p) \to \int_{\mathrm{SU}(2)} f \, dg$ for every continuous class function $f$. By Peter-Weyl, the matrix coefficients of irreducible representations span a dense subspace of $L^2(\mathrm{SU}(2))$ in class-function form, the characters $\chi_{\rho_n}$ for $n \geq 0$. So it suffices to test against $f = \chi_{\rho_n}$ for $n \geq 0$. For $n = 0$ (the trivial representation), both sides equal $1$. For $n \geq 1$, the right-hand side is $\int_{\mathrm{SU}(2)} \chi_{\rho_n} \, dg = 0$ by orthogonality of characters. The left-hand side is $\pi(X)^{-1}\sum_{p \leq X} U_n(\cos\theta_p)$ by Proposition 3. Equidistribution is exactly the vanishing of these sums in the limit. $\square$

**Proposition 5 (Tauberian transition via Wiener-Ikehara).** *Suppose $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ admits an analytic continuation to $\mathrm{Re}(s) \geq 1$ that is holomorphic on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$ and (for $n \geq 1$) has no pole at $s = 1$. Then*
$$
\sum_{p \leq X, p \nmid N_E} U_n(\cos\theta_p) = o(\pi(X))
$$
*as $X \to \infty$.*

*Proof.* Consider the Dirichlet-series logarithmic derivative
$$
\Lambda_n(s) := -\frac{L'(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)}{L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)} = \sum_p \sum_{k \geq 1} \frac{(\log p)\, a_n(p^k)}{p^{ks}}, \qquad a_n(p) = \sum_{j = 0}^n \alpha_p^{n - j}\beta_p^j \cdot p^{-n/2}.
$$
The coefficient at the prime $p$ (the $k = 1$ term) is $a_n(p) \cdot (\log p) / p^s$. Under the normalisation $|\alpha_p| = |\beta_p| = \sqrt{p}$ embedded in the unitarized form, $a_n(p) = U_n(\cos\theta_p)$ by Proposition 3 (with the eigenvalues $\alpha_p / \sqrt{p} = e^{i\theta_p}$). The prime-power terms $k \geq 2$ contribute a Dirichlet series absolutely convergent on $\mathrm{Re}(s) > 1/2$, hence negligible in the Tauberian regime $\mathrm{Re}(s) \to 1^+$.

By the hypothesis, $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ is holomorphic and non-vanishing on the closed half-plane $\mathrm{Re}(s) \geq 1$ (with no pole at $s = 1$ for $n \geq 1$), so the logarithmic derivative $\Lambda_n(s)$ is holomorphic on $\mathrm{Re}(s) \geq 1$ (no pole at $s = 1$, no pole on the critical line from a zero of $L$). The Wiener-Ikehara Tauberian theorem (Ikehara 1931 *J. Math. Phys. MIT* 10; Newman 1980 *Amer. Math. Monthly* 87 for the modern elementary proof) then gives the asymptotic
$$
\sum_{p \leq X, p \nmid N_E} U_n(\cos\theta_p) \cdot \log p = o(X)
$$
as $X \to \infty$ (the dominant-pole residue at $s = 1$ is zero). Partial summation against $1/\log p$ converts to $\sum_p U_n(\cos\theta_p) = o(\pi(X))$ by the prime number theorem. $\square$

**Theorem 6 (Sato-Tate from non-vanishing of all symmetric powers; conditional version).** *Suppose, for a non-CM elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$, that for every $n \geq 1$ the $n$-th symmetric-power $L$-function $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ admits an analytic continuation to $\mathrm{Re}(s) \geq 1$ that is holomorphic and non-vanishing on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$ except for at most a simple pole at $s = 1$ when $n$ is even (and never for $n$ odd). Then the Frobenius angles $\theta_p$ equidistribute on $[0, \pi]$ with respect to $\mu_{ST}$.*

*Proof.* By Proposition 5, the hypothesis forces $\sum_p U_n(\cos\theta_p) = o(\pi(X))$ for every $n \geq 1$. By Proposition 4 (Weyl's criterion), the unitarized Frobenius classes $u_p$ equidistribute in $\mathrm{SU}(2)/\!\!\sim$ with respect to the pushforward of Haar measure. By Proposition 2 (Weyl integration formula), the pushforward to the conjugacy-class space $[0, \pi]$ is the Sato-Tate measure $\mu_{ST}$. Combining, the angles $\theta_p$ equidistribute with respect to $\mu_{ST}$ on $[0, \pi]$. $\square$

**Theorem 7 (Sato-Tate, unconditional; Taylor 2008, BLGHT 2011).** *For every non-CM elliptic curve $E/\mathbb{Q}$, the Frobenius angles $\theta_p$ equidistribute on $[0, \pi]$ with respect to $\mu_{ST}$.*

*Proof (sketch).* By Theorem 6 it suffices to verify the analytic hypothesis on every symmetric power. The proof is the **potential automorphy** argument of Taylor and his collaborators:

(a) **Modularity of $E$** (Wiles 1995 + BCDT 2001): $\rho_{E, \ell}$ corresponds to a weight-$2$ cusp newform $f_E$ on $\Gamma_0(N_E)$ with rational Hecke eigenvalues, equivalently $E$ is isogenous to the modular abelian variety $A_{f_E}$. See [21.06.01].

(b) **Potential automorphy of symmetric powers** (Taylor 2008, BLGHT 2011): for each $n \geq 1$, there exists a totally real solvable Galois extension $F_n / \mathbb{Q}$ such that $\mathrm{Sym}^n \rho_{E, \ell} |_{G_{F_n}}$ corresponds to a cuspidal automorphic representation $\Pi_n$ of $\mathrm{GL}_{n + 1}(\mathbb{A}_{F_n})$ with appropriate weight and conductor. The proof goes through modularity-lifting on unitary groups: the residual mod-$\ell$ representation of $\mathrm{Sym}^n \rho_{E, \ell}$ is shown to be residually automorphic over an auxiliary field $F$ via Clozel-Harris-Taylor's $R = T$ theorem on $\mathrm{GU}(n + 1)$, and the Calabi-Yau Dwork-family construction of Harris-Shepherd-Barron-Taylor 2010 supplies the residual modularity at a key auxiliary prime. BLGHT 2011 removes the multiplicative-reduction hypothesis required by Taylor 2008 via a $p$-adic deformation argument at additive primes.

(c) **Brauer-induction descent**: the $L$-function of $\Pi_n$ over $F_n$, combined with the standard properties of automorphic $L$-functions on $\mathrm{GL}_{n + 1}$ over a number field (Jacquet-Shalika 1976 *Amer. J. Math.* 98: meromorphic continuation, functional equation, non-vanishing on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$), and Brauer-induced back down to $\mathbb{Q}$, yields the required analytic properties of $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ over $\mathbb{Q}$: meromorphic on $\mathbb{C}$, holomorphic on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$ (with possible simple pole at $s = 1$ when $n$ is even, from the trivial subrepresentation of $\mathrm{Sym}^{2k}$ paired with itself), and non-vanishing on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$ (Jacquet-Shalika).

(d) Apply Theorem 6 to conclude. $\square$

The Newton-Thorne 2021 strengthening removes the "potential" in (b): for cuspidal cohomological forms on $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q})$ (which includes every weight-$2$ newform $f_E$ for a non-CM elliptic curve), $\mathrm{Sym}^n f_E$ is **automorphic** on $\mathrm{GL}_{n + 1}(\mathbb{Q})$ unconditionally, without passing to a solvable extension. This shortens the proof of Sato-Tate over $\mathbb{Q}$ but does not change the totally-real-field generality of BLGHT.

**Proposition 8 (Wiener-Ikehara Tauberian theorem; Ikehara 1931).** *Let $L(s) = \sum_{n \geq 1} a_n n^{-s}$ be a Dirichlet series with $a_n \geq 0$, absolutely convergent on $\mathrm{Re}(s) > 1$. Suppose $L(s) - r/(s - 1)$ has an analytic continuation to an open neighbourhood of $\mathrm{Re}(s) \geq 1$ that is continuous on the closed half-plane. Then $\sum_{n \leq X} a_n \sim r X$ as $X \to \infty$.*

*Proof (sketch following Newman 1980).* Set $A(X) = \sum_{n \leq X} a_n$. The Mellin transform identity
$$
L(s) = s \int_1^\infty A(X) X^{-s - 1} dX
$$
(valid for $\mathrm{Re}(s) > 1$) shifts to the line $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$ by contour deformation, the deformation being permitted by the continuity hypothesis on the closed half-plane. The dominant contribution at $s = 1$ comes from the simple-pole residue $r$, giving $A(X) \sim r X$ by the inverse Mellin transform applied to the residue. Newman's elementary proof avoids the full machinery of Fourier-analytic Tauberian theorems by using contour integration on a finite rectangle and explicit error-estimate bookkeeping; the result is the same. $\square$

The Wiener-Ikehara theorem is the analytic engine of the Sato-Tate proof. It converts the analytic statement "non-vanishing on $\mathrm{Re}(s) = 1$" into the arithmetic statement "$\sum_{n \leq X} a_n \sim r X$", and the arithmetic statement applied to symmetric-power $L$-functions is exactly what Weyl's criterion demands.

## Connections [Master]

- **Modularity theorem and BSD conjecture [21.06.01].** Modularity is the input on which the Sato-Tate proof rests: every non-CM elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ corresponds to a weight-$2$ cusp newform $f_E$, and the symmetric-power $L$-functions $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ that Taylor et al. prove (potentially) automorphic are the symmetric powers of the modular Galois representation $\rho_{f_E, \ell}$. Without modularity (Wiles 1995 + BCDT 2001) Sato-Tate has no proof; with modularity, the further step is automorphy of every symmetric power. Sato-Tate is the equidistribution refinement that complements the rank refinement of BSD: BSD predicts the order of vanishing of $L(E, s)$ at $s = 1$, Sato-Tate predicts the distribution of the local Euler factors that build $L(E, s)$ in the first place.

- **$\ell$-adic Galois representations [21.05.01].** The Frobenius trace $a_p = \mathrm{tr}\,\rho_{E, \ell}(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$ at a prime of good reduction is the data on which Sato-Tate is stated. The Sato-Tate group $ST(E) \subseteq \mathrm{SU}(2)$ is the Zariski closure of the image of $\rho_{E, \ell}$ intersected with the maximal compact subgroup, and the Sato-Tate measure is the pushforward of Haar measure on $ST(E)$ under the trace map. The non-CM hypothesis is exactly the hypothesis that $ST(E) = \mathrm{SU}(2)$ rather than a smaller subgroup; Serre's open-image theorem (Serre 1972 *Invent. Math.* 15) confirms the Zariski-density side of this picture.

- **Hecke operators and Hecke algebra [21.04.02].** The symmetric-power $L$-function $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$ is, on the modular-form side, the $L$-function of $\mathrm{Sym}^n f_E$, defined via the Hecke eigenvalues $a_p(f_E)$ at primes of good reduction. The Hecke algebra $\mathbb{T}$ acts on cusp forms, and the action of $\mathrm{Sym}^n$ at each prime is encoded in the Chebyshev polynomial $U_n(a_p / (2\sqrt{p}))$ of Hecke eigenvalues. The Plancherel formula for cusp forms, and the spectral decomposition of the Hecke algebra, are the modular-form-side counterpart of the Weyl integration formula on $\mathrm{SU}(2)$.

- **Elliptic curves [04.04.03].** The algebraic-geometric foundation. The Hasse coefficient $a_p$ is defined via the point count $\#E(\mathbb{F}_p)$, computed on the reduction of the Weierstrass model at $p$. The Hasse bound, the Frobenius endomorphism, and the local zeta function are developed in [04.04.03]; Sato-Tate is the statistical refinement that records how the deviations $a_p$ from $p + 1$ distribute as $p$ varies. The bridge from [04.04.03] to the present unit is the recognition that point-count data, viewed in the Hasse window, follows a compact-group equidistribution.

- **Langlands programme [21.10.01].** Forward-connected (to be produced). Sato-Tate is the simplest analytic instance of Langlands functoriality: the symmetric-power transfer $\mathrm{Sym}^n : \mathrm{GL}_2 \to \mathrm{GL}_{n + 1}$ predicted by Langlands carries every weight-$2$ cusp newform on $\mathrm{GL}_2$ to a cuspidal automorphic form on $\mathrm{GL}_{n + 1}$. Newton-Thorne 2021 proved this functoriality unconditionally for cuspidal cohomological forms over $\mathbb{Q}$. The analytic consequence is the non-vanishing and analytic continuation of every $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$, which is exactly the input to the Sato-Tate theorem.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Mikio Sato conducted an unpublished computer experiment in 1963 [ref: TODO_REF] at the University of Tokyo, computing the Frobenius traces $a_p$ for several elliptic curves over $\mathbb{Q}$ at the first thousand primes and plotting the histogram of $a_p / (2\sqrt{p})$. The empirical distribution he observed matched the Wigner semicircle $(2/\pi)\sqrt{1 - x^2}$, and Sato communicated the observation to Tate, who provided the theoretical framing in terms of analytic continuation of symmetric-power $L$-functions. Tate's article "Algebraic cycles and poles of zeta functions" in *Arithmetical Algebraic Geometry* (Schilling, ed., Harper & Row 1965) [ref: TODO_REF] is the first written record of the conjecture, with attribution to Sato and a connection drawn to the conjectural analytic properties of the $L$-functions $L(s, \mathrm{Sym}^n E)$.

Jean-Pierre Serre 1968 *Abelian $\ell$-adic Representations and Elliptic Curves* (Benjamin) [ref: TODO_REF] reformulated Sato-Tate in the modern Galois-theoretic / Lie-group framework, identifying the equidistribution measure as the pushforward of Haar measure on $\mathrm{SU}(2)$ under the trace map. Serre's monograph established the language of Sato-Tate groups as compact subgroups of $\mathrm{USp}$ controlled by the Galois action on Tate modules. Birch 1968 *Mathematika* 15 [ref: TODO_REF] independently established the **vertical** analogue of the conjecture — equidistribution of $a_p(E)/(2\sqrt{p})$ as $E$ varies over elliptic curves over a fixed $\mathbb{F}_p$, with $p \to \infty$ — via the Plancherel formula for $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_p)$. The vertical statement is unconditional from 1968 onward, four decades before the horizontal statement.

The horizontal Sato-Tate conjecture remained open for forty years. Partial results accumulated: Serre 1989 *Astérisque* 168-169 proved Sato-Tate for elliptic curves with non-trivial action of a Hecke algebra of dimension $\geq 2$ over $\mathbb{Q}$ (a narrow class); Kim-Sarnak 2002 *Duke Math. J.* 112 proved Sato-Tate for $\mathrm{Sym}^3$ and $\mathrm{Sym}^4$ via functoriality results of Kim 2003 *J. Amer. Math. Soc.* 16, Kim-Shahidi 2002 *Duke Math. J.* 112, and Shahidi's Langlands-Shahidi method. The full proof for non-CM elliptic curves over $\mathbb{Q}$ with at least one prime of multiplicative reduction was given by Taylor 2008 *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 108 [ref: TODO_REF] in tandem with Clozel-Harris-Taylor 2008 *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 108 [ref: TODO_REF] (the modularity-lifting companion on unitary groups). The Calabi-Yau Dwork-family input was supplied by Harris-Shepherd-Barron-Taylor 2010 *Ann. Math.* 171 [ref: TODO_REF]. The most general statement — Sato-Tate for every non-CM elliptic curve over a totally real number field — was completed by Barnet-Lamb-Geraghty-Harris-Taylor 2011 *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 114 [ref: TODO_REF], removing the multiplicative-reduction hypothesis via a wild $p$-adic deformation argument at additive primes.

The Fité-Kedlaya-Rotger-Sutherland 2012 *Compos. Math.* 148 classification [ref: TODO_REF] of the Sato-Tate groups for abelian surfaces enumerates the $52$ possibilities and verifies the equidistribution prediction computationally for representative cases. Newton-Thorne 2021 *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 134 [ref: TODO_REF] proved unconditional $\mathrm{Sym}^n$ functoriality for cuspidal cohomological forms on $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q})$, refining the potential-automorphy chain of Taylor 2008-BLGHT 2011 and giving a cleaner proof of Sato-Tate over $\mathbb{Q}$.

Manin-Panchishkin *Introduction to Modern Number Theory* (Springer EMS 49, 2nd ed. 2005) Ch. 6 §6 [ref: TODO_REF] codifies the Sato-Tate framework for the modern graduate student, with the status of the conjecture recorded as of 2005 — at that date, partial results through $\mathrm{Sym}^9$ were known via Kim-Sarnak 2002 and Kim 2004, and the full conjecture was conjectural. The Manin-Panchishkin treatment frames Sato-Tate as the natural sharpening of the Hasse bound and as the analytic shadow of the Langlands functoriality conjecture for symmetric powers.
