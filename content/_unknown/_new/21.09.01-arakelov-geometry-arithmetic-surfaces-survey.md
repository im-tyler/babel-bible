## Intuition [Beginner]

A classical surface, like a smooth complex projective surface, has a beautiful intersection theory. Two curves on it cross at a finite collection of points, and one can count those points with signs to get an integer, the intersection number. This number drives almost every theorem about surfaces — the Bezout count of where two algebraic curves meet, the adjunction formula that computes the genus of a curve from its self-intersection, the Riemann-Roch theorem that bounds the dimension of sections of a line bundle. The theory works because the surface is compact, complete, and equidimensional.

Now replace the complex surface with an arithmetic surface: a two-dimensional object that sits over the ring of integers $\mathbb{Z}$ instead of over a field of complex numbers. The arithmetic dimensions are one geometric direction (the fibres above each prime $p$, each of which is a curve in characteristic $p$) and one arithmetic direction (the primes themselves, walking through $2, 3, 5, 7, \ldots$). The picture wants to be a surface, but it lacks a top — there is no "fibre at infinity" matching the fibres above each finite prime. The arithmetic line $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$ is one-dimensional but not compact in the algebro-geometric sense.

This is the gap that Suren Arakelov closed in 1974. His idea: complete the missing fibre at infinity by hand, using complex-analytic data — a Kähler metric on the complex fibre $X(\mathbb{C})$ together with Green's functions encoding how curves meet at archimedean places. With this archimedean data added, an arithmetic surface acquires a genuine intersection pairing that is a real number, and every classical theorem about surfaces lifts to an arithmetic theorem about $\mathcal{X} \to \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$. The pay-off is enormous: Faltings's proof of the Mordell conjecture, Vojta's height inequality machinery, and the modern viewpoint on the abc conjecture all sit inside this framework.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute the arithmetic degree of the hermitian line bundle $\widehat{\mathcal{O}(D)}$ for a single point divisor $D = [P]$ on the projective line over $\mathbb{Z}$.

Step 1. The arithmetic surface is $\mathcal{X} = \mathbb{P}^1_\mathbb{Z}$, the projective line over $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$. This is the simplest arithmetic surface: a flat projective scheme over $\mathbb{Z}$ whose fibre over each prime $p$ is the projective line $\mathbb{P}^1_{\mathbb{F}_p}$ in characteristic $p$, and whose archimedean fibre is the Riemann sphere $\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{C}) = S^2$.

Step 2. Pick a rational point on $\mathbb{P}^1_\mathbb{Z}$, say $P = [0 : 1]$, the origin. The Weil divisor $D = [P]$ is a horizontal section of the structure morphism $\mathcal{X} \to \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$ — it picks out one point in each fibre, including in the archimedean fibre $\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{C})$ where it picks out the origin $0 \in S^2$.

Step 3. The Kähler metric on the Riemann sphere is the Fubini-Study metric $\omega_{FS} = \frac{1}{2\pi}\frac{|dz|^2}{(1 + |z|^2)^2}$, normalised so that the total volume is $1$. The Green's function $g_P(z) = -\log|z|^2 + \log(1 + |z|^2)$ is the canonical solution of the equation that says $d d^c g_P = \omega_{FS} - \delta_P$, with $\delta_P$ the point mass at $0$.

Step 4. The arithmetic divisor is the pair $\widehat{D} = (D, g_P)$ combining the geometric divisor $D = [P]$ with the Green's function $g_P$ at the archimedean place. The arithmetic degree of $\widehat{D}$ is the real number $\widehat{\deg}(\widehat{D})$, which for a point on $\mathbb{P}^1_\mathbb{Z}$ is computed by combining the finite-place contributions (the heights of the prime ideals of the section) with the archimedean contribution from $g_P$.

Step 5. For the rational point $P = [0 : 1]$ with coordinates of size $1$ at every place, the finite-place contributions vanish and the archimedean contribution reduces to the integral of $\frac{1}{2} g_P$ against $\omega_{FS}$ over the Riemann sphere $S^2$, which by a standard computation evaluates to $\frac{1}{2}$. So $\widehat{\deg}(\widehat{D}) = \frac{1}{2}$.

What this tells us: the arithmetic degree on $\mathbb{P}^1_\mathbb{Z}$ is a real number, not an integer. The classical degree of a divisor on $\mathbb{P}^1$ over a field is an integer (the count of points with multiplicity), but in the arithmetic setting the archimedean Green's-function integral contributes a real number that records how the divisor sits inside the Kähler-metric geometry of the complex fibre. Arakelov's framework lets every classical intersection number on a surface become a real number on the arithmetic surface, with the archimedean correction supplied by Green's functions.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $K$ be a number field with ring of integers $\mathcal{O}_K$, and let $\pi : \mathcal{X} \to S = \mathrm{Spec}(\mathcal{O}_K)$ be an **arithmetic surface**: a regular, flat, projective scheme of relative dimension one over $S$, with generic fibre $X_K = \mathcal{X} \times_S \mathrm{Spec}(K)$ a smooth projective curve over $K$. For each archimedean place $\sigma : K \hookrightarrow \mathbb{C}$, the base change $X_\sigma = \mathcal{X} \times_S \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{C})_\sigma$ is a compact Riemann surface, equipped with a chosen Kähler form $\mu_\sigma$.

A **Green's function** for a divisor $D = \sum_i n_i [P_i]$ on a compact Riemann surface $(X, \mu)$ is a function $g : X \setminus |D| \to \mathbb{R}$ smooth away from the support of $D$ and satisfying:
$$
d d^c g + \delta_D = [\deg D] \cdot \mu,
$$
where $d d^c = \frac{i}{2\pi} \partial \overline{\partial}$ is the canonical $(1,1)$-current operator, $\delta_D$ is the integration current along $D$, and the equation is required to hold as currents of bidegree $(1,1)$. The Green's function is determined up to addition of a real constant; the standard normalisation requires $\int_X g \, \mu = 0$.

The **group of arithmetic divisors** on $\mathcal{X}$ is the direct sum
$$
\widehat{\mathrm{Div}}(\mathcal{X}) = \mathrm{Div}(\mathcal{X}) \oplus \bigoplus_{\sigma : K \hookrightarrow \mathbb{C}} (\text{Green's functions on } X_\sigma) / (\text{constants}),
$$
with the constants quotient accounting for the gauge freedom in the Green's-function normalisation. An element is a pair $\widehat{D} = (D, g_\bullet)$ with $D$ a Weil divisor on $\mathcal{X}$ and $g_\sigma$ a Green's function for $D_\sigma$ on $X_\sigma$ at each archimedean place.

A **meromorphic function** $f \in K(\mathcal{X})^\times$ defines the **arithmetic principal divisor**
$$
\widehat{\mathrm{div}}(f) = (\mathrm{div}(f), \, -\log|f|^2_\sigma),
$$
where $\mathrm{div}(f)$ is the usual divisor of zeros and poles, and $-\log|f|^2_\sigma$ is the Green's function determined by the absolute value of $f$ at the archimedean place $\sigma$. The **arithmetic Picard group** is the quotient
$$
\widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X}) = \widehat{\mathrm{Div}}(\mathcal{X}) / \widehat{\mathrm{div}}(K(\mathcal{X})^\times).
$$

The **arithmetic intersection pairing** is the bilinear map
$$
\widehat{(\,\cdot\,)} : \widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X}) \times \widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X}) \to \mathbb{R}, \qquad \widehat{D} \cdot \widehat{E} = \sum_{\mathfrak{p}} (D \cdot E)_\mathfrak{p} \log N(\mathfrak{p}) + \frac{1}{2} \sum_{\sigma} \int_{X_\sigma} g_{D,\sigma} \cdot (d d^c g_{E,\sigma} + \delta_{E_\sigma}),
$$
where the first sum runs over finite primes $\mathfrak{p}$ of $\mathcal{O}_K$ and $(D \cdot E)_\mathfrak{p}$ is the intersection multiplicity in the fibre over $\mathfrak{p}$, and the second sum runs over the archimedean places. The pairing is symmetric, bilinear, and well-defined on the Picard group (it descends through the principal-divisor relation).

A **hermitian line bundle** on $\mathcal{X}$ is a pair $\widehat{L} = (L, h_\bullet)$ with $L$ a line bundle on $\mathcal{X}$ and $h_\sigma$ a smooth hermitian metric on $L_\sigma$ at each archimedean place $\sigma$. Hermitian line bundles form a group $\widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X})$ isomorphic to the arithmetic Picard group above: a hermitian line bundle $\widehat{L}$ with global section $s$ defines the arithmetic divisor $\widehat{\mathrm{div}}(s) = (\mathrm{div}(s), -\log|s|^2_h)$, and this map identifies the two groups.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The arithmetic intersection number is a real number, not an integer. The archimedean Green's-function integrals contribute irrational real numbers in general, and even the simplest examples (such as a rational point on $\mathbb{P}^1_\mathbb{Z}$ in the worked example) produce rational but non-integer values.
- The Kähler form $\mu_\sigma$ on the archimedean fibre is part of the data, not a derived quantity. Different choices of $\mu_\sigma$ yield different arithmetic pairings, and the canonical choice (the volume form induced by the Arakelov metric in the Faltings sense) is what produces the cleanest formulae. The dependence on $\mu_\sigma$ is functorial but explicit.
- Arakelov's framework treats archimedean places symmetrically with finite primes through a uniform pairing formula, but the formula is *not* a direct generalisation of the finite-place pairing: the archimedean contribution involves the Kähler form and the integration of Green's functions, while the finite-place contribution is the sum of local intersection multiplicities weighted by $\log N(\mathfrak{p})$. The matching is structural and conceptual rather than literal.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Arakelov's arithmetic intersection pairing; Arakelov 1974 [ref: TODO_REF]).** *Let $\pi : \mathcal{X} \to \mathrm{Spec}(\mathcal{O}_K)$ be an arithmetic surface. The arithmetic intersection pairing*
$$
\widehat{(\,\cdot\,)} : \widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X}) \times \widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X}) \to \mathbb{R}
$$
*defined above is symmetric, bilinear, and well-defined: it descends through the principal-divisor relation in each variable. Moreover, the pairing satisfies the **projection formula**: for a finite-type morphism $\varphi : \mathcal{X}' \to \mathcal{X}$ of arithmetic surfaces,*
$$
\varphi_*(\widehat{D}') \cdot \widehat{E} = \widehat{D}' \cdot \varphi^*(\widehat{E})
$$
*holds in $\mathbb{R}$ for any arithmetic divisors $\widehat{D}'$ on $\mathcal{X}'$ and $\widehat{E}$ on $\mathcal{X}$.*

**Proof.** Bilinearity and symmetry are visible from the defining formula: the finite-place sum $\sum_\mathfrak{p} (D \cdot E)_\mathfrak{p} \log N(\mathfrak{p})$ is bilinear and symmetric by the corresponding properties of the geometric intersection multiplicity $(D \cdot E)_\mathfrak{p}$ on the fibre over $\mathfrak{p}$, and the archimedean term $\frac{1}{2}\sum_\sigma \int_{X_\sigma} g_{D,\sigma}(d d^c g_{E,\sigma} + \delta_{E_\sigma})$ is bilinear in $(g_{D,\sigma}, g_{E,\sigma})$ by linearity of integration and of the operator $d d^c$. Symmetry of the archimedean term uses a Stokes-type identity (Green's reciprocity): for smooth functions $g_1, g_2$ on a compact Kähler surface $(X, \mu)$ with $d d^c g_i + \delta_{D_i} = \deg(D_i) \cdot \mu$,
$$
\int_X g_1 (d d^c g_2 + \delta_{D_2}) = \int_X g_2 (d d^c g_1 + \delta_{D_1}),
$$
which follows by integration by parts using that $X$ is closed without boundary.

For well-definedness on the Picard group, the principal-divisor relation $\widehat{\mathrm{div}}(f) = (\mathrm{div}(f), -\log|f|^2_\sigma)$ must pair to zero with every arithmetic divisor. Compute: the finite-place contribution is $\sum_\mathfrak{p} (\mathrm{div}(f) \cdot E)_\mathfrak{p} \log N(\mathfrak{p})$, which by the product formula for the function field $K(\mathcal{X})$ equals minus the archimedean contribution. The archimedean piece is $\frac{1}{2}\sum_\sigma \int_{X_\sigma} (-\log|f|^2_\sigma)(d d^c g_{E,\sigma} + \delta_{E_\sigma})$; using the Poincaré-Lelong identity $d d^c(-\log|f|^2) = \delta_{\mathrm{div}(f)} - \mathrm{curvature \, contribution}$ and Stokes, this matches the negative of the finite-place sum. The two contributions cancel by the product formula, giving the principal-divisor invariance.

The projection formula is established by base-change compatibility: at each finite prime $\mathfrak{p}$, $(\varphi_* D' \cdot E)_\mathfrak{p} = (D' \cdot \varphi^* E)_\mathfrak{p}$ holds by the projection formula for geometric intersection on the fibre, and at each archimedean place, the integration is compatible with proper pushforward and pullback of Green's functions via the standard change-of-variables identity for integrals of currents. Summing over places gives the global projection formula. $\square$

**Bridge.** The arithmetic intersection pairing builds toward every Arakelov-theoretic Diophantine application: heights of points on varieties, the Faltings height of an abelian variety, the canonical height on a Jacobian, and the effective bounds in the Vojta-Bombieri-Gubler programme. The foundational reason it works is exactly the global symmetry given by Green's reciprocity: the archimedean Stokes identity is dual to the finite-place product formula, and putting these together gives one pairing that respects the arithmetic-geometric symmetry. This is exactly the central insight Arakelov isolated in 1974: the arithmetic surface $\mathcal{X} \to \mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$ acquires a complete intersection theory once one identifies the archimedean Green-function integration as the dual partner to the finite-place log-norm sum. The bridge is the recognition that the arithmetic pairing generalises the classical intersection pairing on a complex projective surface and appears again in [21.09.02] (Faltings height and Mordell), where the Faltings height $h_F(A)$ of an abelian variety is computed as an arithmetic self-intersection of a hermitian line bundle on the moduli space. Putting these together, one arithmetic-intersection framework produces every classical Diophantine invariant — heights, Faltings heights, canonical heights on Jacobians — and identifies them with one another at the level of arithmetic Chow groups.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (arithmetic Riemann-Roch for arithmetic surfaces; Faltings 1984 [ref: TODO_REF]).** *Let $\pi : \mathcal{X} \to S = \mathrm{Spec}(\mathcal{O}_K)$ be an arithmetic surface with hermitian line bundle $\widehat{L} = (L, h)$. The arithmetic Euler characteristic*
$$
\widehat{\chi}(\widehat{L}) = \chi(\mathcal{X}, L) + \tau(\widehat{L}; h)
$$
*combining the algebraic Euler characteristic with the archimedean Quillen-metric correction $\tau$ satisfies*
$$
\widehat{\chi}(\widehat{L}) = \frac{1}{2}\widehat{L} \cdot (\widehat{L} - \widehat{K}) + \widehat{\chi}(\mathcal{O}_\mathcal{X}) - \frac{1}{12}\delta_F,
$$
*where $\widehat{K}$ is the arithmetic canonical class (the cotangent bundle $\Omega^1_{\mathcal{X}/S}$ equipped with the Arakelov metric), $\widehat{L} \cdot \widehat{L}$ and $\widehat{L} \cdot \widehat{K}$ are arithmetic intersection numbers in $\mathbb{R}$, and the Faltings delta invariant $\delta_F = \sum_\sigma \delta(X_\sigma)$ sums the Faltings $\delta$-functions $\delta(X_\sigma) \in \mathbb{R}$ of each archimedean fibre $X_\sigma$. The invariant $\delta(X_\sigma)$ depends only on the complex structure of $X_\sigma$ as a Riemann surface, and reduces in genus one to a normalisation of the Faltings height of the elliptic curve.*

This is the arithmetic analogue of the classical Riemann-Roch theorem for a smooth projective surface, which says $\chi(L) = \frac{1}{2}L \cdot (L - K) + \chi(\mathcal{O}_X)$. The new feature in the arithmetic setting is the archimedean correction term $-\frac{1}{12}\delta_F$, which is the differential-geometric input from the Bismut-Gillet-Soulé curvature formula for the Quillen metric on the determinant of cohomology of the archimedean fibres. The factor $-\frac{1}{12}$ matches the classical heat-kernel / zeta-function-regularisation coefficient familiar from string-theoretic anomaly computations and from the Selberg-trace formula on a Riemann surface.

**Theorem (Gillet-Soulé arithmetic Riemann-Roch in higher dimension; Gillet-Soulé 1992 [ref: TODO_REF]).** *For a smooth projective morphism $\pi : \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ of arithmetic varieties with hermitian vector bundle $\widehat{E}$ on $\mathcal{X}$, the arithmetic Chern character of the direct image satisfies*
$$
\widehat{\mathrm{ch}}(R\pi_* \widehat{E}) = \pi_*(\widehat{\mathrm{ch}}(\widehat{E}) \cdot \widehat{\mathrm{Td}}(T_{\mathcal{X}/\mathcal{Y}})) - \pi_*(\widehat{\mathrm{ch}}(\widehat{E}) \cdot R(T_{\mathcal{X}/\mathcal{Y}}))
$$
*in $\widehat{\mathrm{CH}}^*(\mathcal{Y}) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$, where the first term is the classical Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch contribution adapted to arithmetic Chow groups, and the correction $R(T_{\mathcal{X}/\mathcal{Y}})$ is the Bismut-Gillet-Soulé characteristic class involving analytic torsion of the fibres.*

This is the generalisation of Faltings 1984 to arbitrary dimension. The arithmetic Chow group $\widehat{\mathrm{CH}}^p(\mathcal{X})$ for an arithmetic variety $\mathcal{X}$ of dimension $d + 1$ over $\mathbb{Z}$ is the quotient of pairs $(Z, g_Z)$ — codimension-$p$ cycle plus a Green current — by the appropriate equivalence relation, and it carries an intersection product, Chern classes, and a degree map to $\mathbb{R}$. The Bismut-Gillet-Soulé curvature formula identifies the correction term $R$ in terms of the Ray-Singer analytic torsion of the relative dualising complex.

**Theorem (Vojta's height inequality; Vojta 1991 [ref: TODO_REF]).** *Let $C$ be a smooth projective curve of genus $g \geq 2$ over a number field $K$. There exist constants $c_1, c_2 > 0$, depending on $C$ and on a choice of arithmetic model $\mathcal{C} \to \mathrm{Spec}(\mathcal{O}_K)$ equipped with the Arakelov metric, such that for any pair of $K$-rational points $P, Q \in C(K)$ with $P \neq Q$, the arithmetic intersection number on the self-product $\mathcal{C} \times_{\mathcal{O}_K} \mathcal{C}$ satisfies*
$$
(\Delta - \pi_1^* \theta_P - \pi_2^* \theta_Q) \cdot (\Delta - \pi_1^* \theta_P - \pi_2^* \theta_Q) \geq c_1 h(P, Q) - c_2,
$$
*where $\Delta$ is the arithmetic diagonal, $\theta_P, \theta_Q$ are the symmetrised theta divisors associated to $P, Q$, and $h(P, Q)$ is the arithmetic height of the pair. Combined with the elementary gap principle on rational points, this gives a new proof of the Mordell conjecture.*

Vojta's 1991 paper recasts Faltings's 1983 proof of the Mordell conjecture in the language of Arakelov intersection theory on the self-product of an arithmetic curve. The inequality is an Arakelov analogue of Mumford's gap principle for points on a curve in its Jacobian, and it implies that $C(K)$ is finite by an explicit counting argument once the constants $c_1, c_2$ are controlled. The Arakelov framework makes the bounds effective in principle (though the constants involved are not small enough for practical computation of rational points).

**Theorem (Faltings height of an abelian variety; Faltings 1983 [ref: TODO_REF]).** *Let $A$ be an abelian variety of dimension $g$ over a number field $K$. The Faltings height $h_F(A) \in \mathbb{R}$ is defined as the arithmetic degree*
$$
h_F(A) = \frac{1}{[K : \mathbb{Q}]} \widehat{\deg}\, \pi_* \widehat{\omega}_{A/\mathcal{O}_K},
$$
*where $\pi : \mathcal{A} \to \mathrm{Spec}(\mathcal{O}_K)$ is the Néron model of $A$, $\widehat{\omega}_{A/\mathcal{O}_K}$ is the relative dualising sheaf with the Faltings metric (the $L^2$ metric on its archimedean fibres), and $\widehat{\deg}$ is the arithmetic degree of a hermitian line bundle on $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathcal{O}_K)$. The Faltings height is the key invariant in Faltings's proof of the Mordell, Shafarevich, and Tate conjectures.*

The Faltings height refines the classical naïve height of an abelian variety by including the archimedean Arakelov-metric correction, and it satisfies a finiteness theorem: for any bound $B \in \mathbb{R}$, the set of isomorphism classes of abelian varieties over $K$ of dimension $g$ with $h_F(A) \leq B$ is finite. This is the input that Faltings used to prove the Shafarevich conjecture (finiteness of abelian varieties with given dimension, conductor, and semistability type), which together with the Tate conjecture and the Parshin-Kodaira construction yields the Mordell conjecture.

**Theorem (the abc conjecture viewed through Arakelov).** *The abc conjecture of Masser-Oesterlé (1985, 1988) states that for every $\varepsilon > 0$, the inequality $|abc| \leq C(\varepsilon) \cdot \mathrm{rad}(abc)^{1+\varepsilon}$ holds for all coprime triples $(a, b, c)$ of nonzero integers with $a + b = c$. In Arakelov-theoretic language, abc is equivalent to the statement that the arithmetic height of an algebraic point on $\mathbb{P}^1 \setminus \{0, 1, \infty\}$ is bounded by the arithmetic genus of the integral model plus a term linear in $\log \mathrm{rad}$, where the heights and arithmetic genus are computed using the arithmetic intersection pairing on the relevant arithmetic surface. The Vojta-Mochizuki framework presents abc as a Diophantine inequality on the moduli space of elliptic curves, with the proof (Mochizuki 2012-2024, contested) using inter-universal Teichmüller theory built on top of Arakelov foundations.*

The Arakelov-theoretic reformulation of abc is one of the strongest motivations for the framework: it gives a unified language in which heights, intersection numbers, and Diophantine inequalities sit in one place. Whether Mochizuki's 2012 proof of abc via inter-universal Teichmüller theory is correct remains contested in the research community as of 2026; the contested-but-not-refuted status itself reflects the depth of Arakelov-theoretic foundations underlying the programme.

**Synthesis.** The foundational reason Arakelov geometry exists is the asymmetry between finite and archimedean places: a number field $K$ has finitely many finite primes plus a finite number of archimedean places, the product formula $\sum_v v(f) \log N_v = 0$ couples them in one structural identity, and any intersection theory on an arithmetic surface that respects the product formula must include archimedean data. This is exactly the central insight Arakelov isolated in 1974: by supplying Kähler metrics and Green's functions at the archimedean places, one obtains an arithmetic intersection pairing that descends through principal arithmetic divisors and respects the product formula. Putting these together, the arithmetic-intersection framework generalises classical surface intersection theory to arithmetic surfaces, the arithmetic Riemann-Roch theorem (Faltings 1984, Gillet-Soulé 1992) generalises classical Riemann-Roch with the Bismut-Gillet-Soulé curvature correction as the new archimedean term, and the height inequalities of Vojta-Bombieri-Gubler identify Diophantine finiteness with arithmetic intersection-number positivity. The bridge is the recognition that the arithmetic Picard group $\widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X})$ is dual to the geometric Picard group of the complex fibre via the Green's-function pairing, and that this duality is exactly what makes the Faltings height, the Néron-Tate height, and the canonical height on a Jacobian into different facets of one arithmetic intersection-theoretic invariant.

Several apparently distinct invariants are identified by the Arakelov framework. The Faltings height of an abelian variety is the arithmetic degree of the Faltings metric on the relative dualising sheaf, computed via the arithmetic intersection pairing on $\mathcal{A} \to \mathrm{Spec}(\mathcal{O}_K)$. The Néron-Tate canonical height on a Jacobian is the arithmetic self-intersection of the theta divisor with the Arakelov metric. The naive height of a point on projective space is the arithmetic degree of the corresponding hermitian line bundle. The bridge is that all of these are different presentations of arithmetic intersection numbers on the appropriate arithmetic variety, and the unifying framework is Gillet-Soulé arithmetic K-theory. The Bismut-Gillet-Soulé curvature formula identifies the archimedean correction term $R(T_{\mathcal{X}/\mathcal{Y}})$ in arithmetic Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch with the Ray-Singer analytic torsion of the relative dualising complex, and this in turn is dual to the heat-kernel asymptotic on a Riemann surface — a remarkable analytic-arithmetic correspondence whose origin is precisely the Arakelov programme. The framework appears again in [21.09.02] (Faltings height and the Mordell conjecture), where the abstract setup of the present unit is specialised to the moduli space of abelian varieties to produce the Diophantine finiteness statement that finalises Faltings's 1983 theorem.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (well-definedness of the arithmetic pairing).** *The arithmetic intersection pairing $\widehat{(\,\cdot\,)} : \widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X}) \times \widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X}) \to \mathbb{R}$ descends to a well-defined map on the arithmetic Picard group.*

**Proof.** We must show that pairing with a principal arithmetic divisor gives zero. Take $\widehat{\mathrm{div}}(f) = (\mathrm{div}(f), -\log|f|^2_\sigma)$ for a non-zero meromorphic function $f \in K(\mathcal{X})^\times$, and let $\widehat{E} = (E, g_{E, \sigma})$ be an arithmetic divisor with $E$ horizontal (we may assume this by linearity and the vanishing pairing of vertical-with-vertical at the archimedean places).

The finite-place contribution to $\widehat{\mathrm{div}}(f) \cdot \widehat{E}$ is
$$
\sum_\mathfrak{p} (\mathrm{div}(f) \cdot E)_\mathfrak{p} \log N(\mathfrak{p}) = \sum_\mathfrak{p} v_\mathfrak{p}(f|_E) \log N(\mathfrak{p}),
$$
the sum of the finite-place valuations of $f$ restricted to $E$, weighted by $\log N(\mathfrak{p})$.

The archimedean contribution is
$$
\frac{1}{2}\sum_\sigma \int_{X_\sigma} (-\log|f|^2_\sigma)(d d^c g_{E, \sigma} + \delta_{E_\sigma}).
$$
Apply the **Poincaré-Lelong formula** $d d^c(-\log|f|^2) = \delta_{\mathrm{div}(f)} - \deg(\mathrm{div}(f)) \cdot \mu$ to integrate by parts. The integration is by Stokes, using that $X_\sigma$ is closed without boundary, so we may move $d d^c$ from one argument to the other:
$$
\int_{X_\sigma} (-\log|f|^2_\sigma) \cdot d d^c g_{E, \sigma} = \int_{X_\sigma} g_{E, \sigma} \cdot d d^c (-\log|f|^2_\sigma) = \int_{X_\sigma} g_{E, \sigma} \cdot \delta_{\mathrm{div}(f)_\sigma} - \deg(\mathrm{div}(f)) \int_{X_\sigma} g_{E, \sigma} \, \mu.
$$
Adding the $(-\log|f|^2_\sigma) \cdot \delta_{E_\sigma}$ piece gives the value $-\log|f(E)|^2_\sigma$ summed over the points of $E_\sigma$. The Green's-function normalisation $\int g_{E, \sigma} \, \mu = 0$ kills the second term, leaving
$$
\frac{1}{2}\sum_\sigma \int_{X_\sigma} (-\log|f|^2_\sigma)(d d^c g_{E, \sigma} + \delta_{E_\sigma}) = -\sum_\sigma \log|f(E)|_\sigma.
$$

Summing the finite-place and archimedean contributions,
$$
\widehat{\mathrm{div}}(f) \cdot \widehat{E} = \sum_\mathfrak{p} v_\mathfrak{p}(f|_E) \log N(\mathfrak{p}) - \sum_\sigma \log|f(E)|_\sigma = \sum_v v(f|_E) \log N_v,
$$
where the last sum runs over all places of $K$ (with the archimedean log absolute values having $\log N_v = 1$). The **product formula** for the number field $K$ asserts $\sum_v v(f|_E) \log N_v = 0$ for every non-zero $f|_E \in K^\times$, so the pairing vanishes. $\square$

**Proposition (symmetry of the arithmetic pairing).** *The arithmetic intersection pairing $\widehat{(\,\cdot\,)}$ is symmetric in its two arguments.*

**Proof.** Bilinearity is visible from the formula. Symmetry of the finite-place piece is symmetry of the geometric intersection multiplicity $(D \cdot E)_\mathfrak{p} = (E \cdot D)_\mathfrak{p}$ on each fibre, established by the symmetry of the Tor-functor definition of intersection multiplicity.

Symmetry of the archimedean piece reduces to **Green's reciprocity**: for smooth functions $g_1, g_2$ on a compact Kähler manifold $(X, \mu)$ with $d d^c g_i + \delta_{D_i} = \deg(D_i) \cdot \mu$,
$$
\int_X g_1 (d d^c g_2 + \delta_{D_2}) = \int_X g_2 (d d^c g_1 + \delta_{D_1}).
$$
Expand the left side: $\int g_1 \, d d^c g_2 + \int g_1 \cdot \delta_{D_2} = \int g_1 \, d d^c g_2 + g_1(D_2)$. By integration by parts on the closed manifold $X$, $\int g_1 \, d d^c g_2 = \int g_2 \, d d^c g_1$. Substituting $d d^c g_i = \deg(D_i) \mu - \delta_{D_i}$ inside the integrand gives $\int g_2 \, d d^c g_1 = \deg(D_1) \int g_2 \, \mu - g_2(D_1)$, and similarly for $g_1$. Combining the four pieces, both sides of Green's reciprocity simplify to $\deg(D_1) \int g_2 \, \mu + \deg(D_2) \int g_1 \, \mu - g_1(D_2) - g_2(D_1)$ (with sign carefully tracked), which is symmetric in $(g_1, D_1) \leftrightarrow (g_2, D_2)$.

Combining the finite-place and archimedean symmetries gives the symmetry of the arithmetic pairing. $\square$

**Proposition (projection formula for the arithmetic pairing).** *Let $\varphi : \mathcal{X}' \to \mathcal{X}$ be a finite-type morphism of arithmetic surfaces. For any arithmetic divisor $\widehat{D}'$ on $\mathcal{X}'$ and $\widehat{E}$ on $\mathcal{X}$,*
$$
\varphi_*(\widehat{D}') \cdot \widehat{E} = \widehat{D}' \cdot \varphi^*(\widehat{E}).
$$

**Proof.** Apply the classical projection formula on each fibre at a finite prime $\mathfrak{p}$: for the geometric intersection multiplicity,
$$
(\varphi_* D' \cdot E)_\mathfrak{p} = (D' \cdot \varphi^* E)_\mathfrak{p},
$$
holding by the classical projection formula on the fibre $\mathcal{X}'_\mathfrak{p} \to \mathcal{X}_\mathfrak{p}$, established via Tor-functor base change. Summing over finite primes weighted by $\log N(\mathfrak{p})$ gives the finite-place piece of the desired identity.

At each archimedean place $\sigma$, the morphism $\varphi$ induces a holomorphic map $\varphi_\sigma : X'_\sigma \to X_\sigma$ of compact Riemann surfaces. Green's functions transform contravariantly under pullback: $\varphi_\sigma^*(g_{E, \sigma}) = g_{\varphi^* E, \sigma}$ (up to a normalisation constant compatible with the gauge freedom). The integration is by change-of-variables for currents:
$$
\int_{X'_\sigma} (\varphi_\sigma^* g_{E, \sigma}) (d d^c g_{D', \sigma} + \delta_{D'_\sigma}) = \int_{X_\sigma} g_{E, \sigma}(\varphi_{\sigma *}(d d^c g_{D', \sigma} + \delta_{D'_\sigma})) = \int_{X_\sigma} g_{E, \sigma}(d d^c g_{\varphi_* D', \sigma} + \delta_{(\varphi_* D')_\sigma}),
$$
giving the archimedean piece of the projection formula. Adding the finite-place and archimedean pieces yields the global identity. $\square$

**Theorem (arithmetic Riemann-Roch for arithmetic surfaces), proof sketch — see Faltings 1984 [ref: TODO_REF].** The full proof combines the classical Riemann-Roch theorem for surfaces ($\chi(L) = \frac{1}{2}L(L - K) + \chi(\mathcal{O}_X)$) on each finite-prime fibre with the differential-geometric input from the **Bismut-Gillet-Soulé curvature formula** at the archimedean places. The Quillen metric on the determinant of cohomology $\lambda(L) = \det R\pi_* L$ has curvature form computed by the Bismut-Gillet-Soulé theorem; integrating the curvature against the fundamental class of each archimedean fibre and combining with the algebraic Euler characteristic produces the arithmetic Euler characteristic $\widehat{\chi}(\widehat{L})$. The Faltings delta invariant $\delta(X_\sigma)$ arises as the Ray-Singer analytic torsion of the Laplacian on $X_\sigma$ in genus $g \geq 1$, and the coefficient $-\frac{1}{12}$ matches the heat-kernel zeta-function regularisation coefficient for the Laplacian on a Riemann surface. Each ingredient is itself a substantial theorem (the BGS curvature formula occupies the three-paper 1988 series; the structural identification of $\delta$ requires the Polyakov-Alvarez formula); the assembly into the arithmetic Riemann-Roch statement is what Faltings 1984 [ref: TODO_REF] established, with the Gillet-Soulé 1992 generalisation [ref: TODO_REF] extending to arbitrary dimension. $\square$

**Theorem (arithmetic Hodge-index), proof.** Let $V_{\mathrm{vert}} \subset \widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X}) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ be the subspace spanned by vertical divisor classes, and let $\widehat{D} \in V_{\mathrm{vert}}^\perp$. We show $\widehat{D} \cdot \widehat{D} \leq 0$ with equality iff $\widehat{D} \in V_{\mathrm{vert}}$ modulo principal divisors.

By the geometric Hodge-index theorem on each finite-prime fibre $\mathcal{X}_\mathfrak{p}$, the intersection pairing on $\mathrm{Pic}(\mathcal{X}_\mathfrak{p}) \otimes \mathbb{Q}$ has signature $(1, n_\mathfrak{p} - 1)$ for some $n_\mathfrak{p}$, with the positive eigenvector spanned by the fibre class itself. Restricted to the orthogonal complement of the fibre class, the pairing is negative-semidefinite. Summing over finite primes weighted by $\log N(\mathfrak{p})$ preserves this: the finite-place contribution to $\widehat{D} \cdot \widehat{D}$ for $\widehat{D} \in V_{\mathrm{vert}}^\perp$ is $\leq 0$, with equality iff the geometric part of $\widehat{D}$ is a $\mathbb{Q}$-combination of fibre classes plus a principal divisor.

For the archimedean piece, the variational principle for Green's functions implies that the self-pairing $\frac{1}{2}\int_{X_\sigma} g_{D, \sigma}(d d^c g_{D, \sigma} + \delta_{D_\sigma})$ is negative for nonzero $g_{D, \sigma}$ orthogonal to the constants — this is the standard fact that the Laplacian on a compact Riemann surface has nonnegative eigenvalues, with the zero eigenvalue corresponding to constants. Summing the negative-semidefinite finite-place contribution with the negative archimedean contribution gives $\widehat{D} \cdot \widehat{D} \leq 0$.

The equality case follows by tracking when the inequalities are saturated: the finite-place contribution vanishes iff the geometric part of $\widehat{D}$ is vertical (plus principal), and the archimedean contribution vanishes iff $g_{D, \sigma}$ is constant — equivalently iff $\widehat{D}$ has no archimedean variation, which is the residual content of being principal. Combining, $\widehat{D} \cdot \widehat{D} = 0$ iff $\widehat{D}$ is principal plus vertical modulo torsion. $\square$

**Theorem (Vojta's height inequality), stated without proof — see Vojta 1991 [ref: TODO_REF].** The inequality
$$
(\Delta - \pi_1^* \theta_P - \pi_2^* \theta_Q) \cdot (\Delta - \pi_1^* \theta_P - \pi_2^* \theta_Q) \geq c_1 h(P, Q) - c_2
$$
combines the arithmetic Hodge-index theorem on $\mathcal{C} \times_S \mathcal{C}$ (giving $\geq 0$ for self-intersections of arithmetic divisors orthogonal to the fibre classes) with explicit estimates on the archimedean Green's-function contribution to the difference $\Delta - \pi_1^* \theta_P - \pi_2^* \theta_Q$. The combined effect is a quadratic lower bound on the arithmetic height that conflicts with the elementary gap principle ($h(R) \leq C \cdot d(R)^2$ for points $R$ in an arithmetic progression on a curve, where $d$ measures the "step"), forcing finiteness of $C(K)$. Vojta's 1991 paper [ref: TODO_REF] gives the full argument; Bombieri-Gubler 2006 Ch. 11 [ref: TODO_REF] is the modern textbook treatment.

## Connections [Master]

- **Intersection pairing on a surface [04.05.06].** The classical intersection pairing on a smooth complex projective surface, $\mathrm{Pic}(X) \times \mathrm{Pic}(X) \to \mathbb{Z}$, is the geometric input that Arakelov's framework generalises. The arithmetic intersection pairing $\widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X}) \times \widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X}) \to \mathbb{R}$ restricts to the geometric pairing on each finite-prime fibre, with the archimedean Green's-function integral supplying the new term. The arithmetic pairing is a real number rather than an integer because the archimedean correction introduces irrational contributions in general.

- **Adjunction formula [04.05.07].** The classical adjunction $D \cdot (D + K) = 2g(D) - 2$ on a complex projective surface generalises to the arithmetic adjunction $\widehat{D} \cdot (\widehat{D} + \widehat{K}) = -2 \widehat{\chi}(\mathcal{O}_D)$ on an arithmetic surface, where $\widehat{\chi}(\mathcal{O}_D)$ is the arithmetic Euler characteristic and the arithmetic canonical class $\widehat{K}$ is the cotangent bundle equipped with the Arakelov metric. For a horizontal section (rational point), the formula computes the logarithmic norm of the discriminant.

- **Riemann-Roch theorem for surfaces [04.05.08].** The classical Riemann-Roch $\chi(L) = \frac{1}{2}L(L - K) + \chi(\mathcal{O}_X)$ on a smooth projective surface lifts in the arithmetic setting to Faltings 1984's arithmetic Riemann-Roch $\widehat{\chi}(\widehat{L}) = \frac{1}{2}\widehat{L}(\widehat{L} - \widehat{K}) + \widehat{\chi}(\mathcal{O}_\mathcal{X}) - \frac{1}{12}\delta_F$ on an arithmetic surface. The new term $-\frac{1}{12}\delta_F$ is the differential-geometric correction from the archimedean Quillen metric and the Bismut-Gillet-Soulé curvature formula.

- **Hermitian / Kähler form [03.02.11].** The Kähler form $\mu_\sigma$ on the archimedean fibre $X_\sigma$ is the differential-geometric input to the Arakelov programme. The choice of Kähler form determines the Green's functions and hence the arithmetic intersection pairing; the canonical choice (the Arakelov metric in the sense of Faltings 1984) makes the formulae cleanest and produces the Faltings delta invariant in the arithmetic Riemann-Roch theorem.

- **Picard group [04.05.02].** The classical Picard group $\mathrm{Pic}(\mathcal{X})$ of an arithmetic surface is the abelian group of line bundles up to isomorphism. The arithmetic Picard group $\widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X})$ enriches this with archimedean hermitian metric data, producing a real-vector-space extension of the classical Picard group. The arithmetic intersection pairing is bilinear on $\widehat{\mathrm{Pic}}(\mathcal{X})$ and provides the Diophantine-geometric pay-off of the framework.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Suren Arakelov, working in Moscow in the early 1970s, observed a structural lacuna in the algebraic-geometric formalism of arithmetic surfaces: a smooth projective variety over $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$ behaves like a complete two-dimensional object in many ways, but the standard intersection theory of complete varieties fails on it because the arithmetic base $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathbb{Z})$ is not proper — there is no fibre at the archimedean place to balance the fibres at the finite primes. Arakelov's 1974 paper *Intersection theory of divisors on an arithmetic surface*, published in the *Izvestiya Akademii Nauk SSSR* (Vol. 38, 1179-1192) [ref: TODO_REF], supplied the missing fibre at infinity by adding a Kähler metric on the complex fibre $X(\mathbb{C})$ together with Green's functions encoding archimedean intersection data. The construction made the arithmetic surface into a "complete" two-dimensional object with a real-valued intersection pairing, and opened the route to a complete arithmetic version of classical surface theory.

The framework was elaborated and consolidated over the following decade. Faltings's 1984 paper *Calculus on arithmetic surfaces* (Annals of Mathematics 119, 387-424) [ref: TODO_REF] established the arithmetic Riemann-Roch theorem for arithmetic surfaces, introducing the Faltings delta invariant $\delta_F$ as the archimedean correction term and establishing arithmetic adjunction and an arithmetic Hodge-index theorem. Gillet and Soulé extended the framework to arbitrary dimension in their 1990 paper *Arithmetic intersection theory* (Publications Mathématiques de l'IHÉS 72, 93-174) [ref: TODO_REF], replacing Green's functions by Green currents on $X(\mathbb{C})$, and their 1992 follow-up [ref: TODO_REF] established the arithmetic Grothendieck-Riemann-Roch theorem with the Bismut-Gillet-Soulé curvature formula as the archimedean correction. Bismut, Gillet, and Soulé's three-paper 1988 series in *Communications in Mathematical Physics* (Vol. 115) provided the differential-geometric input — the curvature of the Quillen metric on the determinant of cohomology — that made the higher-dimensional arithmetic Riemann-Roch theorem work.

The Diophantine pay-off came rapidly. Faltings's 1983 proof of the Mordell conjecture (the *Invent. Math.* paper on the Tate conjecture for abelian varieties) used the Faltings height $h_F(A)$, defined as an arithmetic degree on $\mathrm{Spec}(\mathcal{O}_K)$ via the Arakelov framework. Vojta's 1991 paper *Siegel's theorem in the compact case* (Inventiones Mathematicae 105, 547-560) [ref: TODO_REF] recast Faltings's argument in the language of Arakelov intersection theory on the self-product $\mathcal{C} \times_{\mathcal{O}_K} \mathcal{C}$ of an arithmetic curve, producing a uniform height-inequality framework that has since been extended to the Bombieri-Gubler textbook treatment (Cambridge 2006) [ref: TODO_REF]. The Arakelov framework also underlies the Vojta-Mochizuki programme on the abc conjecture, in which abc is recast as a Diophantine inequality on an arithmetic moduli space; whether Mochizuki's 2012-2024 inter-universal Teichmüller theory provides a correct proof remains contested in the research community as of 2026.
