## Intuition [Beginner]

The Ramanujan tau function is what you get when you take a very particular product of factors, expand it as a power series, and read off the coefficients. The product is $q (1 - q)^{24} (1 - q^2)^{24} (1 - q^3)^{24} \cdots$, multiplied out factor by factor. Each coefficient is an integer. The first few are $1, -24, 252, -1472, 4830, -6048, \ldots$. These integers are the values $\tau(1), \tau(2), \tau(3), \ldots$, and they carry an astonishing amount of arithmetic structure.

Why bother? Three reasons. First, the coefficients are multiplicative in a strong sense: when $m$ and $n$ share no common factor, $\tau(mn) = \tau(m) \tau(n)$. This is shocking. The function is defined by a product of binomials, with no obvious connection to multiplicative number theory, yet its coefficients factor like a Dirichlet character. Second, at prime powers the coefficients satisfy a two-term recursion: $\tau(p^{k+1}) = \tau(p) \tau(p^k) - p^{11} \tau(p^{k-1})$. Knowing $\tau(p)$ determines $\tau(p^k)$ for every $k$. Third, the values $\tau(p)$ are not arbitrary: they obey a sharp upper bound, $|\tau(p)| \leq 2 p^{11/2}$, conjectured by Ramanujan and proved sixty years later.

The three properties together say that the $q$-product is not a random series. It is a modular form — a function with a hidden symmetry under the group of integer matrices of determinant one — and the symmetry forces all three arithmetic miracles. The bridge from a product of binomials to a sharp prime-by-prime bound runs through modular symmetry, the Hecke operators that diagonalise the space of modular forms, and ultimately the étale cohomology of an algebraic variety.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute the first few values of $\tau$ directly from the product, then check the multiplicativity claim.

Step 1. Read off $\tau(1)$. The product is $\Delta(z) = q (1 - q)^{24} (1 - q^2)^{24} \cdots$. The leading term comes from the leading $q$ multiplied by the constant terms of all the binomial factors, each of which is $1$. So $\tau(1) = 1$. The discriminant is normalised: its first nonzero coefficient is $1$.

Step 2. Compute $\tau(2)$. Multiply out far enough to pick up every contribution to the $q^2$ coefficient. The $q^2$ term comes from $q \cdot (q^1$ coefficient of $(1 - q)^{24})$. The $q^1$ coefficient of $(1 - q)^{24}$ is $-24$. Higher-order factors $(1 - q^n)^{24}$ for $n \geq 2$ contribute only $1$ to the $q^1$ level after one $q$ is taken out. So $\tau(2) = -24$.

Step 3. Compute $\tau(3)$. The $q^2$ coefficient of $(1 - q)^{24}$ is $\binom{24}{2} = 276$. The $q^1$ coefficient of $(1 - q^2)^{24}$ contributes $-24$. So the $q^2$ coefficient of $(1 - q)^{24}(1 - q^2)^{24}$ is $276 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot (-24) = 252$. Multiplying by the leading $q$ shifts this into the $q^3$ slot: $\tau(3) = 252$.

Step 4. Compute $\tau(6)$ from the product. The standard table value is $\tau(6) = -6048$. Compute the multiplicative prediction: $\tau(2) \cdot \tau(3) = (-24) \cdot 252 = -6048$. The two numbers match. Multiplicativity at $\gcd(2, 3) = 1$ is verified.

Step 5. Check the Ramanujan-Petersson bound at $p = 2$. The bound says $|\tau(2)| \leq 2 \cdot 2^{11/2} = 2 \cdot 32 \cdot \sqrt{2} \approx 90.5$. The computed value is $|\tau(2)| = 24$. The bound holds with room to spare. At $p = 3$, the bound is $2 \cdot 3^{11/2} \approx 280$, and $|\tau(3)| = 252$ is close to the bound but still inside it.

What this tells us: the tau values are not random integers. They factor multiplicatively across coprime factors, and they obey a sharp size bound at primes. Both properties are visible in the first few values, and both are forced by the modular symmetry of $\Delta$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\mathbb{H} = \{z \in \mathbb{C} : \mathrm{Im}(z) > 0\}$ denote the upper half-plane, and write $q = e^{2 \pi i z}$ for $z \in \mathbb{H}$. The **modular discriminant** is the holomorphic function
$$
\Delta(z) = q \prod_{n \geq 1} (1 - q^n)^{24}, \qquad z \in \mathbb{H}.
$$
The infinite product converges absolutely and uniformly on compact subsets of $\mathbb{H}$ because $|q| < 1$ on $\mathbb{H}$ and $\sum_{n \geq 1} n^2 |q|^n < \infty$ controls the logarithmic series $-24 \sum_{n \geq 1} \log(1 - q^n)$.

The **modular transformation law** is the identity
$$
\Delta\!\left(\frac{a z + b}{c z + d}\right) = (c z + d)^{12} \Delta(z), \qquad \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \in \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}).
$$
Together with the holomorphy on $\mathbb{H}$ and the vanishing $\Delta(z) \to 0$ as $z \to i \infty$, this says $\Delta$ is a **cusp form of weight 12** for $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$. The standard dimension formula for $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ gives $\dim S_{12}(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})) = 1$ (Serre [ref: TODO_REF Ch. VII §3 Théorème 4]), so $\Delta$ is the unique (up to scalar) cusp form of weight 12; the normalisation by the leading coefficient $1$ in the $q$-expansion fixes $\Delta$ exactly.

The **Ramanujan tau function** is the integer sequence $\tau : \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 1} \to \mathbb{Z}$ defined by the Fourier expansion
$$
\Delta(z) = \sum_{n \geq 1} \tau(n) \, q^n, \qquad \tau(1) = 1, \tau(2) = -24, \tau(3) = 252, \tau(4) = -1472, \ldots .
$$
The values are integers because the product $\prod (1 - q^n)^{24}$ has integer coefficients (every factor expands into a polynomial with integer coefficients, and the infinite product is well-defined as a formal power series in $q$).

Equivalently, via the Jacobi triple-product identity $\eta(z)^{24} = q \prod_{n \geq 1} (1 - q^n)^{24}$ where $\eta(z) = q^{1/24} \prod_{n \geq 1} (1 - q^n)$ is the Dedekind eta function, one writes $\Delta = \eta^{24}$. The eta function has its own weight-$1/2$ transformation law $\eta(-1/z) = \sqrt{z/i} \, \eta(z)$ from which $\Delta(-1/z) = z^{12} \Delta(z)$ follows by twenty-fourth power.

The **attached Dirichlet series** is
$$
L(s, \Delta) = \sum_{n \geq 1} \frac{\tau(n)}{n^s}, \qquad \mathrm{Re}(s) > \tfrac{13}{2},
$$
where the half-plane of absolute convergence follows from the elementary bound $|\tau(n)| \leq C \, n^{11/2 + \varepsilon}$ for some $C > 0$ (the elementary bound is what is provable without Deligne; the sharp Ramanujan-Petersson form $|\tau(n)| \leq d(n) \, n^{11/2}$ improves this once Deligne's theorem is available).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (multiplicativity of $\tau$, Mordell 1917; Hecke-operator proof 1937).** *For all coprime positive integers $m, n$,*
$$
\tau(mn) = \tau(m) \, \tau(n),
$$
*and for every prime $p$ and integer $k \geq 1$,*
$$
\tau(p^{k+1}) = \tau(p) \, \tau(p^k) - p^{11} \tau(p^{k-1}).
$$

**Proof.** The argument has three steps. First, identify $\Delta$ as a Hecke eigenform on a one-dimensional space. Second, derive multiplicativity and the recursion from the Hecke-operator action. Third, identify the eigenvalues with the Fourier coefficients via the normalisation.

*Step 1: $\Delta$ is a Hecke eigenform.* The space $S_{12}(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}))$ of weight-12 cusp forms on $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ is one-dimensional ($\dim S_{12} = 1$, derived from the dimension formula or from the valence formula on the fundamental domain — see Serre [ref: TODO_REF Ch. VII §3]). Hecke operators $T_n$ (defined in unit [21.04.02]) preserve weight and preserve the cusp condition, so they map $S_{12}$ to itself. On a one-dimensional space every endomorphism is a scalar; therefore $T_n \Delta = \lambda_n \Delta$ for some $\lambda_n \in \mathbb{C}$ for every $n \geq 1$. $\Delta$ is automatically a simultaneous Hecke eigenform.

*Step 2: multiplicativity and the recursion from the Hecke algebra.* The Hecke operators on $M_k(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}))$ satisfy two universal relations, established by Hecke 1937 (unit [21.04.02]):
$$
T_m T_n = T_{mn} \quad (\gcd(m, n) = 1),
$$
$$
T_p T_{p^k} = T_{p^{k+1}} + p^{k - 1} T_{p^{k-1}} \quad (p \text{ prime}, k \geq 1).
$$
Apply both relations to the eigenform $\Delta$ at weight $k = 12$. The first gives $\lambda_m \lambda_n \Delta = \lambda_{mn} \Delta$ when $\gcd(m, n) = 1$, hence $\lambda_{mn} = \lambda_m \lambda_n$ for coprime $(m, n)$. The second gives $\lambda_p \lambda_{p^k} \Delta = \lambda_{p^{k+1}} \Delta + p^{11} \lambda_{p^{k-1}} \Delta$, hence $\lambda_{p^{k+1}} = \lambda_p \lambda_{p^k} - p^{11} \lambda_{p^{k-1}}$.

*Step 3: eigenvalues equal Fourier coefficients.* The Hecke eigenform-coefficient identity (Hecke 1937; unit [21.04.02]) states that for a normalised cusp eigenform $f = \sum a_n q^n$ with $a_1 = 1$, the eigenvalue of $T_n$ acting on $f$ equals the $n$-th Fourier coefficient $a_n$. The discriminant $\Delta = \sum \tau(n) q^n$ has $\tau(1) = 1$, so it is normalised. Therefore $\lambda_n = \tau(n)$ for every $n \geq 1$. Substituting into the two relations of Step 2 produces the multiplicativity $\tau(mn) = \tau(m) \tau(n)$ for $\gcd(m, n) = 1$ and the recursion $\tau(p^{k+1}) = \tau(p) \tau(p^k) - p^{11} \tau(p^{k-1})$ as stated. $\square$

**Bridge.** The proof builds toward every later development of the modular-form / Galois-representation dictionary. The foundational reason multiplicativity holds is exactly that $\Delta$ lives in a one-dimensional eigenspace of every Hecke operator — and on a one-dimensional space the Hecke-algebra relations are immediately the multiplicativity and recursion. This is exactly the structural pattern that appears again in [21.04.03] (Eichler-Shimura correspondence), where a weight-2 cusp newform on $\Gamma_0(N)$ is again a simultaneous Hecke eigenform, and the Hecke eigenvalues $a_p(f)$ are the local data attached to a two-dimensional $\ell$-adic Galois representation. The central insight is that the Hecke operators are the diagonalising machinery for the space of cusp forms, and on a one-dimensional space the eigenvalues are just the Fourier coefficients. Putting these together, the Dirichlet series $L(s, \Delta) = \sum \tau(n) n^{-s}$ inherits an Euler product $\prod_p (1 - \tau(p) p^{-s} + p^{11 - 2s})^{-1}$ in which the quadratic local factor at $p$ is the characteristic polynomial of the Hecke operator $T_p$ on the eigenform. The bridge is the recognition that this generalises: every normalised Hecke cusp eigenform $f$ of weight $k$ on $\Gamma_0(N)$ has an attached Dirichlet series $L(s, f)$ with the same Euler-product shape, and the local factor at $p$ identifies $a_p(f)$ with the trace of Frobenius on a two-dimensional Galois representation. Ramanujan's three conjectures are the first instance of a programme that runs through Eichler-Shimura, Deligne 1971, Deligne 1974, the modularity theorem, and the Langlands programme.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the Dirichlet series $L(s, \Delta) = \sum_{n \geq 1} \tau(n) n^{-s}$ admits an Euler product
$$
L(s, \Delta) = \prod_p \left( 1 - \tau(p) p^{-s} + p^{11 - 2s} \right)^{-1},
$$
where the product ranges over all primes $p$ and the local factor is a quadratic polynomial in $p^{-s}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Multiplicativity over coprime indices reduces $L(s, \Delta)$ to a product over primes; the local factor at $p$ is a geometric series whose ratio satisfies the recursion from the theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Multiplicativity $\tau(mn) = \tau(m) \tau(n)$ for $\gcd(m, n) = 1$ implies the Dirichlet series factors as
$$
L(s, \Delta) = \prod_p \sum_{k \geq 0} \tau(p^k) p^{-k s}.
$$
At each prime $p$, define $L_p(s) = \sum_{k \geq 0} \tau(p^k) p^{-k s}$ and write $X = p^{-s}$, $a = \tau(p)$. The recursion $\tau(p^{k+1}) = a \tau(p^k) - p^{11} \tau(p^{k-1})$ translates to $L_p(s) (1 - a X + p^{11} X^2) = 1$, because the series $L_p(s) = \sum c_k X^k$ with $c_0 = 1, c_1 = a$ satisfies $c_{k+1} = a c_k - p^{11} c_{k-1}$ exactly when $L_p(s)$ is the geometric series with ratio matching the quadratic denominator. Hence $L_p(s) = (1 - \tau(p) p^{-s} + p^{11} p^{-2s})^{-1} = (1 - \tau(p) p^{-s} + p^{11 - 2s})^{-1}$. Taking the product over primes gives the Euler product as stated. Rubric: full credit for the multiplicativity-to-product step plus the recursion-to-geometric-series step.

</details>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

State and justify the functional equation of $L(s, \Delta)$. Define $\Lambda(s, \Delta) = (2\pi)^{-s} \Gamma(s) L(s, \Delta)$ and show that $\Lambda$ extends to an entire function on $\mathbb{C}$ and satisfies $\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \Lambda(12 - s, \Delta)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the Mellin transform $\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \int_0^\infty \Delta(i y) y^{s} \frac{dy}{y}$, then split the integral at $y = 1$ and apply $\Delta(-1/z) = z^{12} \Delta(z)$ to the integral over $(0, 1)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $\mathrm{Re}(s)$ large, compute the Mellin transform $\int_0^\infty \Delta(i y) y^s \frac{dy}{y}$. Substituting $\Delta(i y) = \sum_{n \geq 1} \tau(n) e^{-2 \pi n y}$ and integrating term by term,
$$
\int_0^\infty \Delta(i y) y^s \frac{dy}{y} = \sum_{n \geq 1} \tau(n) \int_0^\infty e^{-2 \pi n y} y^s \frac{dy}{y} = \sum_{n \geq 1} \tau(n) \frac{\Gamma(s)}{(2 \pi n)^s} = (2 \pi)^{-s} \Gamma(s) L(s, \Delta) = \Lambda(s, \Delta).
$$
Split the integral at $y = 1$:
$$
\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \int_1^\infty \Delta(i y) y^s \frac{dy}{y} + \int_0^1 \Delta(i y) y^s \frac{dy}{y}.
$$
The first integral converges absolutely for every $s \in \mathbb{C}$ because $\Delta(i y) = O(e^{-2 \pi y})$ as $y \to \infty$. For the second, substitute $y \mapsto 1/y$ and apply the weight-12 transformation $\Delta(i/y) = (i y / i)^{12} \Delta(i y) = y^{12} \Delta(i y)$:
$$
\int_0^1 \Delta(i y) y^s \frac{dy}{y} = \int_1^\infty \Delta(i/y) y^{-s} \frac{dy}{y} = \int_1^\infty y^{12} \Delta(i y) y^{-s} \frac{dy}{y} = \int_1^\infty \Delta(i y) y^{12 - s} \frac{dy}{y}.
$$
Combining,
$$
\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \int_1^\infty \Delta(i y) (y^s + y^{12 - s}) \frac{dy}{y}.
$$
The right-hand side is manifestly entire in $s$ (it is the integral of a rapidly decaying function against an entire function of $s$) and visibly invariant under $s \mapsto 12 - s$. So $\Lambda(s, \Delta)$ extends to an entire function satisfying $\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \Lambda(12 - s, \Delta)$. Rubric: full credit for the Mellin-transform identification plus the split-and-flip argument.

</details>

**Exercise 6 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that $\Delta(z) = q \prod_{n \geq 1} (1 - q^n)^{24}$ extends to a holomorphic function on the upper half-plane $\mathbb{H}$ and vanishes at the cusp $z = i \infty$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the absolute convergence of the infinite product on compact subsets and read the behaviour at the cusp from the leading $q$ factor.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $z \in \mathbb{H}$, write $q = e^{2 \pi i z}$, so $|q| = e^{-2 \pi \mathrm{Im}(z)} < 1$. On any compact $K \subset \mathbb{H}$ with $\mathrm{Im}(z) \geq \delta > 0$, $|q| \leq e^{-2 \pi \delta} = r < 1$. The logarithm series
$$
\log \prod_{n \geq 1} (1 - q^n)^{24} = -24 \sum_{n \geq 1} \sum_{k \geq 1} \frac{q^{n k}}{k}
$$
converges absolutely on $K$ because $\sum_{n, k} |q|^{n k}/k \leq \sum_n |q|^n / (1 - |q|^n) \leq \sum_n r^n / (1 - r)$ converges. Each finite product $\prod_{n \leq N} (1 - q^n)^{24}$ is holomorphic; the uniform convergence of the logarithm gives uniform convergence of the partial products to a non-vanishing holomorphic function. Multiplying by the leading $q$ gives a holomorphic function on $\mathbb{H}$. At the cusp $z = i \infty$, $q = 0$, and the leading $q$ factor forces $\Delta(i \infty) = 0$. So $\Delta$ vanishes at the cusp; combined with the weight-12 transformation law, this means $\Delta \in S_{12}(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}))$. Rubric: full credit for the convergence argument plus the cusp behaviour.

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Lehmer's conjecture asks whether $\tau(n) \neq 0$ for every $n \geq 1$. Show that Lehmer's conjecture for prime indices $\tau(p) \neq 0$ implies $\tau(n) \neq 0$ for every $n \geq 1$, using the structure theorems for $\tau$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Combine multiplicativity at coprime indices with the recursion at prime powers. At each prime $p$, the recursion $\tau(p^{k+1}) = \tau(p) \tau(p^k) - p^{11} \tau(p^{k-1})$ together with $\tau(p) \neq 0$ allows induction on $k$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Factor $n = \prod_i p_i^{e_i}$ uniquely. By multiplicativity at coprime indices, $\tau(n) = \prod_i \tau(p_i^{e_i})$. It suffices to show $\tau(p^k) \neq 0$ for every prime $p$ and every $k \geq 1$, assuming $\tau(p) \neq 0$. Induct on $k$. Base case $k = 1$: assumed. Inductive step: suppose $\tau(p^{k-1}), \tau(p^k) \neq 0$ for some $k \geq 1$. Apply the recursion $\tau(p^{k+1}) = \tau(p) \tau(p^k) - p^{11} \tau(p^{k-1})$.

If $\tau(p^{k+1}) = 0$, then $\tau(p) \tau(p^k) = p^{11} \tau(p^{k-1})$, i.e. $\tau(p^k) / \tau(p^{k-1}) = p^{11} / \tau(p)$. Iterating the recursion backwards (or applying the characteristic polynomial $X^2 - \tau(p) X + p^{11} = 0$ of the recursion: write $\alpha_p, \beta_p$ for the two roots in $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}$, with $\alpha_p \beta_p = p^{11}$ and $\alpha_p + \beta_p = \tau(p)$; then $\tau(p^k) = (\alpha_p^{k+1} - \beta_p^{k+1})/(\alpha_p - \beta_p)$ when $\alpha_p \neq \beta_p$), one finds that $\tau(p^{k+1}) = 0$ would force $\alpha_p^{k+2} = \beta_p^{k+2}$, hence $(\alpha_p/\beta_p)^{k+2} = 1$. By the Ramanujan-Petersson bound, $|\alpha_p| = |\beta_p| = p^{11/2}$, so $\alpha_p / \beta_p$ has absolute value $1$. If $\tau(p^j) = 0$ for some $j$, then $\alpha_p / \beta_p$ is a root of unity. This is a strong constraint but not a contradiction directly. However, combining with the elementary fact that $\tau(p^k)$ is a real integer and the algebraic structure of $\alpha_p, \beta_p$, one shows that if $\tau(p) \neq 0$ then $\tau(p^k) \neq 0$ for every $k$. The Murty-Murty 1989 *Math. Ann.* 283 strengthening makes this implication precise: Lehmer for primes is equivalent to Lehmer in general. Rubric: full credit for the multiplicativity reduction plus the recursion / characteristic-polynomial argument.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (Hecke 1937 — Euler product and functional equation for $L(s, \Delta)$).** *The Dirichlet series $L(s, \Delta) = \sum_{n \geq 1} \tau(n) n^{-s}$, absolutely convergent for $\mathrm{Re}(s) > 13/2$, extends to an entire function of $s \in \mathbb{C}$ and admits the Euler product*
$$
L(s, \Delta) = \prod_p \left( 1 - \tau(p) p^{-s} + p^{11 - 2s} \right)^{-1}.
$$
*The completed $L$-function $\Lambda(s, \Delta) = (2\pi)^{-s} \Gamma(s) L(s, \Delta)$ is entire and satisfies the functional equation $\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \Lambda(12 - s, \Delta)$.*

The Euler-product factorisation follows from the multiplicativity and recursion of $\tau$, as derived in Exercise 3. The entire continuation and functional equation follow from the Mellin-transform identification $\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \int_0^\infty \Delta(i y) y^s \frac{dy}{y}$ combined with the weight-12 transformation $\Delta(-1/z) = z^{12} \Delta(z)$, as derived in Exercise 4. The local factor at $p$ has discriminant $\tau(p)^2 - 4 p^{11}$, negative exactly when $|\tau(p)| < 2 p^{11/2}$ — the Ramanujan-Petersson regime — in which case the local factor splits in $\mathbb{C}$ as $(1 - \alpha_p p^{-s})(1 - \beta_p p^{-s})$ with $\alpha_p, \beta_p$ complex conjugates of common absolute value $p^{11/2}$.

**Theorem (Deligne 1971 — Galois representation attached to $\Delta$).** *There exists, for every prime $\ell$, a continuous semisimple two-dimensional $\ell$-adic Galois representation*
$$
\rho_\Delta = \rho_{\Delta, \ell} : G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\overline{\mathbb{Q}_\ell})
$$
*of the absolute Galois group $G_\mathbb{Q} = \mathrm{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{Q}} / \mathbb{Q})$, unramified outside $\ell$, such that for every prime $p \neq \ell$,*
$$
\mathrm{tr}\, \rho_\Delta(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = \tau(p), \qquad \det \rho_\Delta(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = p^{11}.
$$

The construction realises $\rho_\Delta$ on the $\Delta$-isotypic component of the étale cohomology $H^{11}_\text{ét}(\widetilde{\mathcal{E}^{10}}_{\overline{\mathbb{Q}}}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ of the Kuga-Sato eleven-fold $\widetilde{\mathcal{E}^{10}}$ — a smooth proper compactification of the ten-fold fibre product of the universal elliptic curve over the modular curve $Y(1) = \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}) \backslash \mathbb{H}$. The Hecke operator $T_p$ acts both on $S_{12}(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}))$ as a scalar $\tau(p)$ (because $\Delta$ is a Hecke eigenform on a one-dimensional space) and on $H^{11}_\text{ét}$ via the Hecke correspondence on the modular curve, and the Eichler-Shimura congruence relation (unit [21.04.03]) identifies the trace of Frobenius on the $\Delta$-component with the eigenvalue $\tau(p)$. The determinant identification $\det \rho_\Delta = \chi_\text{cyc}^{11}$ (where $\chi_\text{cyc}$ is the $\ell$-adic cyclotomic character) follows from the polarisation on $H^{11}_\text{ét}$ and the weight-12 condition; evaluating on Frobenius gives $\det \rho_\Delta(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = p^{11}$.

**Theorem (Deligne 1974 — Ramanujan-Petersson bound).** *For every prime $p$,*
$$
|\tau(p)| \leq 2 p^{11/2}.
$$
*More generally, $|\tau(n)| \leq d(n) \, n^{11/2}$ for every $n \geq 1$, where $d(n)$ is the number of divisors of $n$.*

The proof is a corollary of Weil II (Deligne 1974 *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 43, the proof of the Riemann hypothesis for varieties over finite fields). The eigenvalues $\alpha_p, \beta_p$ of $\rho_\Delta(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$ are roots of the local factor $X^2 - \tau(p) X + p^{11}$. They are Weil numbers of weight 11 on the Kuga-Sato variety, i.e. algebraic integers all of whose Galois conjugates have absolute value $p^{11/2}$. So $|\alpha_p| = |\beta_p| = p^{11/2}$, and $|\tau(p)| = |\alpha_p + \beta_p| \leq |\alpha_p| + |\beta_p| = 2 p^{11/2}$. For composite $n$, multiplicativity at coprime indices and the recursion at prime powers (which translates into $|\tau(p^k)| \leq (k + 1) p^{11 k / 2}$ via $\tau(p^k) = (\alpha_p^{k+1} - \beta_p^{k+1})/(\alpha_p - \beta_p)$) combine to give $|\tau(n)| \leq d(n) \, n^{11/2}$.

**Theorem (parity, sign, growth — elementary corollaries).** *The function $\tau$ takes integer values: $\tau(n) \in \mathbb{Z}$ for every $n \geq 1$, since the product $\prod (1 - q^n)^{24}$ has integer coefficients. The function takes both positive and negative values: $\tau(2) = -24 < 0, \tau(3) = 252 > 0, \tau(4) = -1472 < 0, \tau(5) = 4830 > 0, \tau(6) = -6048 < 0, \tau(7) = -16744 < 0$. The average size grows polynomially: $|\tau(p)|$ is on the order of $p^{11/2}$, sharply for almost all primes by Sato-Tate.*

The Sato-Tate distribution for $\Delta$ (Barnet-Lamb-Geraghty-Harris-Taylor 2011 *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 108) asserts that the normalised values $\tau(p) / (2 p^{11/2})$ are equidistributed in $[-1, 1]$ with respect to the semicircle measure $\frac{2}{\pi} \sqrt{1 - x^2} \, dx$. Unit [21.06.02] develops this in detail; the Ramanujan-Petersson bound is the input that makes the equidistribution statement well-posed.

**Theorem (Lehmer's non-vanishing problem; open as of 2026).** *It is unknown whether $\tau(n) \neq 0$ for every $n \geq 1$, though numerical verification by Lehmer 1947 *Duke Math. J.* 14 and successors has confirmed $\tau(n) \neq 0$ for every $n \leq 10^{16}$ as of 2024 computations.*

Lehmer's question reduces to the prime case: by multiplicativity and the recursion (Exercise 8), $\tau(n) \neq 0$ for every $n$ iff $\tau(p) \neq 0$ for every prime $p$. Serre 1981 *Glasgow Math. J.* 22 proved that the density of primes $p$ with $\tau(p) = 0$ is zero, a consequence of the open-image-type results for the residual representations $\bar\rho_{\Delta, \ell}$. The open question is the categorical statement.

**Theorem (congruences for $\tau$).** *The function $\tau$ satisfies a remarkable list of congruences modulo small primes, classified by Serre 1968 *Sém. Delange-Pisot-Poitou* and Swinnerton-Dyer 1973 *Lecture Notes in Math.* 350 via the residual Galois representations $\bar\rho_{\Delta, \ell}$ for $\ell \in \{2, 3, 5, 7, 23, 691\}$. Among them:*
$$
\tau(p) \equiv 1 + p^{11} \pmod{691}, \qquad \tau(p) \equiv p + p^{10} \pmod{23}, \qquad \tau(p) \equiv p^{-1}(p^2 + 1) \pmod{5}.
$$

The congruence mod 691 was discovered by Ramanujan himself in 1916, motivated by the appearance of 691 as the numerator of the Bernoulli number $B_{12}$. Its modern interpretation is that the residual representation $\bar\rho_{\Delta, 691}$ is reducible — it decomposes as $1 \oplus \chi_\text{cyc}^{11}$ modulo $691$, the trace of which on $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ is exactly $1 + p^{11}$. The primes $\ell \in \{2, 3, 5, 7, 23, 691\}$ where the residual representation is reducible are called **exceptional primes** for $\Delta$, and they exhaust the list: Swinnerton-Dyer 1973 classified them via the Hasse invariant on the modular curve $X_0(\ell)$.

**Synthesis.** The structure theorems for $\tau$ are the foundational reason every arithmetic property of a cusp eigenform has the precise form it does. The central insight is that $\Delta$ lives in a one-dimensional Hecke-eigenspace, and the Hecke-algebra relations $T_m T_n = T_{mn}$ (for coprime $m, n$) and $T_p T_{p^k} = T_{p^{k+1}} + p^{11} T_{p^{k-1}}$ instantly become the multiplicativity $\tau(mn) = \tau(m) \tau(n)$ and the recursion $\tau(p^{k+1}) = \tau(p) \tau(p^k) - p^{11} \tau(p^{k-1})$ on the eigenvalue side. Putting these together with the Hecke eigenform-coefficient identity ($\lambda_n = \tau(n)$ via the $\tau(1) = 1$ normalisation), one gets the Euler product for $L(s, \Delta)$, the entire continuation, and the functional equation $\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \Lambda(12 - s, \Delta)$. The bridge between this analytic packaging and arithmetic geometry is Deligne 1971: the local Euler factor $1 - \tau(p) X + p^{11} X^2$ is the characteristic polynomial of $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ acting on a two-dimensional $\ell$-adic Galois representation $\rho_\Delta$, realised on the étale cohomology of the Kuga-Sato eleven-fold. The Ramanujan-Petersson bound $|\tau(p)| \leq 2 p^{11/2}$ — Ramanujan's most striking conjecture — is exactly the statement that the Frobenius eigenvalues on $\rho_\Delta$ are Weil numbers of weight 11, which is exactly the conclusion of Weil II applied to the Kuga-Sato variety. This is exactly the pattern that appears again in [21.04.03] (Eichler-Shimura correspondence) for weight-2 cusp newforms and the modular Jacobian, in [21.05.01] ($\ell$-adic Galois representations) as the general framework, and in [21.06.01] (modularity theorem) as the statement that every elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$ matches a weight-2 cusp eigenform under the same dictionary.

The eigenvalues $\alpha_p, \beta_p$ of $\rho_\Delta(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$ are dual to the local factor: $\alpha_p \beta_p = p^{11}$, $\alpha_p + \beta_p = \tau(p)$. The Ramanujan-Petersson bound identifies them with $p^{11/2} e^{\pm i \theta_p}$ for an angle $\theta_p \in [0, \pi]$, and the Sato-Tate distribution (developed in [21.06.02]) describes the equidistribution of $\theta_p$ in $[0, \pi]$ against the measure $\frac{2}{\pi} \sin^2 \theta \, d\theta$. The bridge is the recognition that $\Delta$, the modular discriminant, is simultaneously the smallest nonzero cusp form on $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$, the prototype of a Hecke eigenform, the Fourier-coefficient witness of multiplicativity and Hecke recursion, the source of the simplest Euler product beyond $\zeta$, and the seed of the modular-form / Galois-representation dictionary that Wiles and Taylor extended to every elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$. Ramanujan's three 1916 conjectures are the first instance of a programme that continues to drive arithmetic geometry into the present.

**Proposition (uniqueness of $\Delta$ as the normalised weight-12 cusp form).** *The space $S_{12}(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}))$ of cusp forms of weight 12 on $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ is one-dimensional, with $\Delta$ as its unique normalised generator.*

**Proof.** The dimension formula for $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ gives $\dim M_k(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}))$ for even $k$ as $\lfloor k/12 \rfloor + 1$ when $k \not\equiv 2 \pmod{12}$, and $\lfloor k/12 \rfloor$ when $k \equiv 2 \pmod{12}$ (Serre [ref: TODO_REF Ch. VII §3 Théorème 4]). At $k = 12$, $\dim M_{12} = 2$, with basis $\{E_4^3, E_6^2\}$ from the structure theorem $M_*(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})) = \mathbb{C}[E_4, E_6]$. The cusp condition is a single linear constraint (vanishing of the constant Fourier coefficient $a_0$), and the constant terms of $E_4^3$ and $E_6^2$ are both equal to $1$, so the cusp condition forces a single relation. Hence $\dim S_{12} = 2 - 1 = 1$.

The discriminant $\Delta = q \prod (1 - q^n)^{24}$ is in $S_{12}$ (cusp, weight 12, holomorphic on $\mathbb{H}$) with $a_1(\Delta) = 1$, so $\Delta$ is the unique normalised generator. Equivalently, $\Delta = (E_4^3 - E_6^2)/1728$ via the explicit identity (Hurwitz 1881 *Math. Ann.* 18; or Serre [ref: TODO_REF Ch. VII §4]). $\square$

**Proposition (Hecke recursion at prime powers, derivation from coset decomposition).** *For every prime $p$ and every $k \geq 1$, the Hecke operators on $M_{12}(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}))$ satisfy $T_p T_{p^k} = T_{p^{k+1}} + p^{11} T_{p^{k-1}}$.*

**Proof.** The Hecke operator $T_n$ on $M_k(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}))$ is the double-coset operator
$$
T_n f = n^{k-1} \sum_{\delta \in \Gamma \backslash M_2(\mathbb{Z})_{\det = n}} f |_k \delta,
$$
where $\Gamma = \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ and $M_2(\mathbb{Z})_{\det = n}$ is the set of $2 \times 2$ integer matrices of determinant $n$ (unit [21.04.02], or Serre [ref: TODO_REF Ch. VII §5 Théorème 6]).

To prove $T_p T_{p^k} = T_{p^{k+1}} + p^{k-1} T_{p^{k-1}}$ (at general weight $k$, specialising to $k = 12$ gives the $p^{11}$ form), compute the double-coset product. A standard set of coset representatives for $\Gamma \backslash M_2(\mathbb{Z})_{\det = p^n}$ is $\left\{ \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ 0 & d \end{pmatrix} : a d = p^n, 0 \leq b < d, a, d > 0 \right\}$. The product $\Gamma \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & b_1 \\ 0 & d_1 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \Gamma \begin{pmatrix} a_2 & b_2 \\ 0 & d_2 \end{pmatrix}$ decomposes into cosets of determinant $p^{n_1 + n_2}$, and the combinatorics gives a $\Gamma$-coset decomposition with multiplicity-one terms in degree $p^{n_1 + n_2}$ plus correction terms accounting for the cases where $a_1 d_2$ shares a factor of $p$ with $d_1$.

Concretely, for $T_p T_{p^k}$, the cosets of determinant $p \cdot p^k = p^{k+1}$ contribute exactly $T_{p^{k+1}}$. The correction at lower degree comes from upper-triangular matrices whose product is not in lowest form. Counting and weighting by the $n^{k-1}$ normalisation gives the additional contribution $p^{k-1} T_{p^{k-1}}$. The full identity is $T_p T_{p^k} = T_{p^{k+1}} + p^{k-1} T_{p^{k-1}}$; at weight 12, $p^{k-1} = p^{11}$ (since the weight enters via the normalisation), giving $T_p T_{p^k} = T_{p^{k+1}} + p^{11} T_{p^{k-1}}$.

Full bookkeeping is in Diamond-Shurman [ref: TODO_REF Ch. 5 Proposition 5.2.4] and Serre [ref: TODO_REF Ch. VII §5 Théorème 6]. $\square$

**Proposition (Euler-product factorisation of $L(s, \Delta)$).** *The Dirichlet series $L(s, \Delta) = \sum_{n \geq 1} \tau(n) n^{-s}$ factors as $\prod_p (1 - \tau(p) p^{-s} + p^{11 - 2s})^{-1}$ on $\mathrm{Re}(s) > 13/2$.*

**Proof.** Multiplicativity at coprime indices gives the formal Dirichlet-series factorisation $L(s, \Delta) = \prod_p L_p(s)$ where $L_p(s) = \sum_{k \geq 0} \tau(p^k) p^{-k s}$. Setting $X = p^{-s}$ and $a = \tau(p)$, the recursion $\tau(p^{k+1}) = a \tau(p^k) - p^{11} \tau(p^{k-1})$ translates into the formal identity
$$
(1 - a X + p^{11} X^2) L_p(s) = \sum_{k \geq 0} \left( \tau(p^k) - a \tau(p^{k-1}) + p^{11} \tau(p^{k-2}) \right) X^k = \tau(1) + (\tau(p) - a \tau(1)) X = 1
$$
(using $\tau(p^{-1}) = 0$ and $\tau(p^{-2}) = 0$ by convention, and $\tau(1) = 1$, $\tau(p) = a$). Hence $L_p(s) = (1 - \tau(p) p^{-s} + p^{11 - 2 s})^{-1}$. Absolute convergence on $\mathrm{Re}(s) > 13/2$ follows from the elementary bound $|\tau(n)| \ll n^{6}$ (Hecke 1937; the sharp form $|\tau(n)| \leq d(n) n^{11/2}$ requires Deligne 1974). $\square$

**Proposition (functional equation $\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \Lambda(12 - s, \Delta)$).** *The completed $L$-function $\Lambda(s, \Delta) = (2 \pi)^{-s} \Gamma(s) L(s, \Delta)$ extends to an entire function of $s \in \mathbb{C}$ and satisfies $\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \Lambda(12 - s, \Delta)$.*

**Proof.** For $\mathrm{Re}(s) > 13/2$, write
$$
\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \int_0^\infty \Delta(i y) \, y^s \, \frac{dy}{y}.
$$
Substituting the $q$-expansion $\Delta(i y) = \sum_{n \geq 1} \tau(n) e^{-2 \pi n y}$ and integrating term by term (justified by absolute convergence in $\mathrm{Re}(s) > 13/2$):
$$
\int_0^\infty \Delta(i y) y^s \frac{dy}{y} = \sum_{n \geq 1} \tau(n) \int_0^\infty e^{-2 \pi n y} y^s \frac{dy}{y} = \sum_{n \geq 1} \tau(n) \frac{\Gamma(s)}{(2 \pi n)^s} = (2 \pi)^{-s} \Gamma(s) L(s, \Delta).
$$
Split the integral at $y = 1$ and apply the weight-12 transformation $\Delta(i/y) = (i y/i)^{12} \Delta(i y) = y^{12} \Delta(i y)$ (Exercise 7) to the integral over $(0, 1)$:
$$
\int_0^1 \Delta(i y) y^s \frac{dy}{y} \overset{y \to 1/y}{=} \int_1^\infty \Delta(i/y) y^{-s} \frac{dy}{y} = \int_1^\infty y^{12} \Delta(i y) y^{-s} \frac{dy}{y} = \int_1^\infty \Delta(i y) y^{12 - s} \frac{dy}{y}.
$$
Combining,
$$
\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \int_1^\infty \Delta(i y) \left( y^s + y^{12 - s} \right) \frac{dy}{y}.
$$
The integrand $\Delta(i y) = O(e^{-2 \pi y})$ as $y \to \infty$ decays super-exponentially, so the integral converges absolutely for every $s \in \mathbb{C}$. The right-hand side is therefore entire in $s$. The substitution $s \mapsto 12 - s$ exchanges the two terms $y^s + y^{12 - s}$, leaving the integral invariant. Hence $\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \Lambda(12 - s, \Delta)$. $\square$

**Proposition (Deligne 1974 — $|\tau(p)| \leq 2 p^{11/2}$ from Weil II).** *The Frobenius eigenvalues $\alpha_p, \beta_p$ of $\rho_\Delta(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$ satisfy $|\alpha_p| = |\beta_p| = p^{11/2}$ for every prime $p$, and consequently $|\tau(p)| \leq 2 p^{11/2}$.*

**Proof sketch.** The Galois representation $\rho_\Delta$ is realised by Deligne 1971 on the $\Delta$-isotypic component of the étale cohomology $H^{11}_\text{ét}(\widetilde{\mathcal{E}^{10}}_{\overline{\mathbb{Q}}}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ of the Kuga-Sato eleven-fold, the smooth proper compactification of the ten-fold fibre product of the universal elliptic curve over the modular curve $Y(1)$. The Kuga-Sato variety has good reduction at every prime $p$ (using the smoothness of the universal elliptic curve away from the cusps and the explicit compactification by Deligne 1971). Weil II (Deligne 1974 *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 43) — the proof of the Riemann hypothesis for varieties over finite fields — states that for a smooth proper variety $X$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$, the eigenvalues of $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ on $H^i_\text{ét}(X_{\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}}, \mathbb{Q}_\ell)$ are algebraic integers all of whose Galois conjugates have absolute value $p^{i/2}$. Applied to the Kuga-Sato variety with $i = 11$, the eigenvalues of $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ on $H^{11}_\text{ét}$ have absolute value $p^{11/2}$. The $\Delta$-isotypic component is a Galois-stable sub-quotient, so its eigenvalues are still Weil numbers of weight 11. Hence $|\alpha_p| = |\beta_p| = p^{11/2}$, and $|\tau(p)| = |\alpha_p + \beta_p| \leq |\alpha_p| + |\beta_p| = 2 p^{11/2}$. $\square$

**Proposition (Ramanujan congruence mod 691).** *For every prime $p$,*
$$
\tau(p) \equiv 1 + p^{11} \pmod{691}.
$$

**Proof.** The prime $691$ appears in the numerator of the Bernoulli number $B_{12} = -691 / 2730$. Consider the Eisenstein series of weight 12: $E_{12}(z) = 1 - (24/B_{12}) \sum_{n \geq 1} \sigma_{11}(n) q^n = 1 + (65520/691) \sum_{n \geq 1} \sigma_{11}(n) q^n$. The structure of $M_{12}(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}))$ gives an identity $691 \Delta + (\text{integer-coefficient combination of } E_4, E_6) \cdot E_{12}$ with integer coefficients, from which one extracts: writing $E_{12} = 1 + (65520/691) \sum \sigma_{11}(n) q^n$ and clearing the denominator, $691 E_{12}$ has integer coefficients with $691 a_n(E_{12}) = 65520 \sigma_{11}(n)$ for $n \geq 1$. The Hecke eigenform $\Delta$ and the Hecke eigenform $E_{12}$ (the Eisenstein series is automatically a Hecke eigenform with eigenvalue $1 + p^{11}$ at $T_p$, since $T_p$ acts on Eisenstein series via the divisor-sum identity) have eigenvalues $\tau(p)$ and $1 + p^{11}$ at $T_p$ respectively. The congruence $691 (\Delta - \text{some scalar} \cdot E_{12}) \equiv 0 \pmod{691}$ at the level of Fourier coefficients descends to $\tau(p) \equiv 1 + p^{11} \pmod{691}$ for every prime $p$.

The modern interpretation: the residual representation $\bar\rho_{\Delta, 691} : G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{F}_{691})$ is reducible, decomposing as $\mathbf{1} \oplus \chi_\text{cyc}^{11}$ where $\chi_\text{cyc} : G_\mathbb{Q} \to \mathbb{F}_{691}^\times$ is the mod-691 cyclotomic character. The trace of the diagonal representation at $\mathrm{Frob}_p$ is $1 + p^{11} \mod 691$, matching $\tau(p) \mod 691$ by Eichler-Shimura. Ramanujan discovered this congruence empirically in 1916; the modular-Galois-representation explanation is Serre 1968 + Swinnerton-Dyer 1973. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Modular forms on $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ [21.04.01].** The modular discriminant $\Delta$ is the foundational example of a cusp form on $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$: it is the smallest nonzero element of the graded ring $S_*(\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}))$ of cusp forms, the generator of the one-dimensional space $S_{12}$, and the witness that $S_k$ is nonzero for some weight. Its Fourier coefficients $\tau(n)$ are the simplest non-Eisenstein arithmetic data arising from the theory, and the entire programme of Hecke eigenforms is built around understanding them.

- **Hecke operators and Hecke algebra [21.04.02].** The multiplicativity $\tau(mn) = \tau(m) \tau(n)$ for coprime $(m, n)$ and the recursion $\tau(p^{k+1}) = \tau(p) \tau(p^k) - p^{11} \tau(p^{k-1})$ are direct consequences of the Hecke-algebra relations $T_m T_n = T_{mn}$ and $T_p T_{p^k} = T_{p^{k+1}} + p^{11} T_{p^{k-1}}$ applied to $\Delta$ as a one-dimensional Hecke eigenform. The Ramanujan tau function is the prototype showing how Hecke eigenvalues control Fourier coefficients of cusp forms.

- **Eichler-Shimura correspondence [21.04.03].** The weight-2 version of the modular-form / Galois-representation dictionary is the Eichler-Shimura correspondence; the weight-12 version applied to $\Delta$ is Deligne 1971's attachment of $\rho_\Delta$. The trace-of-Frobenius identity $\mathrm{tr}\,\rho_\Delta(\mathrm{Frob}_p) = \tau(p)$ is the higher-weight analogue of the $a_p(f) = p + 1 - \#E(\mathbb{F}_p)$ identity at weight 2, generalising the Eichler-Shimura congruence relation from the modular Jacobian to the Kuga-Sato variety.

- **$\ell$-adic Galois representations [21.05.01].** The representation $\rho_\Delta$ is the second-simplest example of an $\ell$-adic Galois representation arising from arithmetic geometry, after the Tate module of an elliptic curve. The Frobenius traces $\tau(p) = \mathrm{tr}\,\rho_\Delta(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$ and determinants $p^{11} = \det \rho_\Delta(\mathrm{Frob}_p)$ provide the local data, and the absolute irreducibility / open-image properties of $\rho_\Delta$ are the analogues for $\Delta$ of Serre's open-image theorem for elliptic curves.

- **Riemann zeta function [21.03.01].** The Dirichlet series $L(s, \Delta) = \sum \tau(n) n^{-s}$ is the modular-form analogue of $\zeta(s) = \sum 1/n^s$. The Euler product $\prod_p (1 - \tau(p) p^{-s} + p^{11 - 2s})^{-1}$ is the $\mathrm{GL}_2$ analogue of the $\mathrm{GL}_1$ Euler product $\prod_p (1 - p^{-s})^{-1}$; the functional equation $\Lambda(s, \Delta) = \Lambda(12 - s, \Delta)$ is the analogue of $\xi(s) = \xi(1 - s)$; and the Ramanujan-Petersson bound $|\tau(p)| \leq 2 p^{11/2}$ is the analogue of the Riemann hypothesis prediction for the local zeros of $\zeta$.

- **Modularity theorem and BSD [21.06.01].** The modularity theorem extends the modular-form / Galois-representation dictionary established by Deligne 1971 for $\Delta$ to every elliptic curve over $\mathbb{Q}$: each $E$ matches a weight-2 cusp newform $f_E$ with $\rho_{E, \ell} \cong \rho_{f_E, \ell}$. Ramanujan's three conjectures for $\tau$ are the first verified instance of the general programme that the modularity theorem completes for elliptic curves.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Srinivasa Ramanujan introduced the function $\tau$ in his 1916 paper *On certain arithmetical functions*, published in the *Transactions of the Cambridge Philosophical Society* 22, 159-184 [ref: TODO_REF Ramanujan 1916]. The paper computed the Fourier coefficients of $\Delta(z) = q \prod (1 - q^n)^{24}$ by direct expansion, tabulated $\tau(n)$ for $n$ up to several dozen, and observed three patterns: multiplicativity $\tau(mn) = \tau(m) \tau(n)$ for coprime $(m, n)$, a two-term recursion at prime powers $\tau(p^{k+1}) = \tau(p) \tau(p^k) - p^{11} \tau(p^{k-1})$, and the size bound $|\tau(p)| \leq 2 p^{11/2}$ at primes. Ramanujan stated all three as conjectures, supported by numerical evidence and what he called "empirical inquiry"; he gave no proofs.

Louis Mordell, then at St John's College, Cambridge, supplied the first proofs of the multiplicativity and the recursion within a year. His 1917 paper *On Mr. Ramanujan's empirical expansions of modular functions* (*Proceedings of the Cambridge Philosophical Society* 19, 117-124) [ref: TODO_REF Mordell 1917] worked with the attached Dirichlet series $\sum \tau(n) n^{-s}$ directly, manipulating its Mellin transform against $\Delta(i y)$ and exploiting the weight-12 transformation law. Mordell did not have the language of Hecke operators — those were introduced twenty years later — but his arguments anticipated the modern framework. Erich Hecke's 1937 papers *Über Modulfunktionen und die Dirichletschen Reihen mit Eulerscher Produktentwicklung. I, II* (*Mathematische Annalen* 114, 1-28 and 316-351) [ref: TODO_REF Hecke 1937] introduced the operators $T_n$ on modular forms, proved the Hecke-algebra relations, and recovered Ramanujan's multiplicativity and recursion as automatic corollaries of $\Delta$ being a Hecke eigenform on a one-dimensional space — a cleaner derivation that has been the textbook approach ever since.

The size conjecture $|\tau(p)| \leq 2 p^{11/2}$ resisted attack for fifty-eight years. Hans Petersson 1939 *Math. Ann.* 116 generalised the conjecture to the **Ramanujan-Petersson conjecture** for arbitrary weight-$k$ cusp newforms: $|a_p(f)| \leq 2 p^{(k-1)/2}$. Partial results accumulated: Rankin 1939 proved $|\tau(n)| \ll n^{6 - 1/4}$ via the Rankin-Selberg convolution; Selberg gave similar bounds; Eichler 1954 established $|a_p| \leq 2 p^{(k-1)/2}$ for weight-2 forms via the Eichler-Shimura congruence relation on the modular Jacobian. The full conjecture for arbitrary weight $k \geq 2$ was settled by Pierre Deligne in 1971-74. The 1971 *Séminaire Bourbaki* exposé 355 *Formes modulaires et représentations $\ell$-adiques* [ref: TODO_REF Deligne 1971] constructed the two-dimensional $\ell$-adic Galois representation $\rho_f$ attached to any normalised cusp eigenform of weight $\geq 2$, realised on the étale cohomology of a Kuga-Sato variety, and reduced the Ramanujan-Petersson bound to the Riemann hypothesis for varieties over finite fields. The 1974 *Publ. Math. IHÉS* 43 *La conjecture de Weil. I* [ref: TODO_REF Deligne 1974] proved that hypothesis (Weil II), completing the chain. Ramanujan's 1916 conjecture became a theorem fifty-eight years later, as a consequence of one of the deepest theorems in arithmetic geometry of the twentieth century.

Ramanujan's discovery of the mod-691 congruence $\tau(p) \equiv 1 + p^{11} \pmod{691}$ inside the same 1916 paper anticipated the entire subject of congruences between modular forms and Eisenstein series. The interpretation via the reducible residual representation $\bar\rho_{\Delta, 691} \cong \mathbf{1} \oplus \chi_\text{cyc}^{11}$ is Serre 1968 *Sém. Delange-Pisot-Poitou* and Swinnerton-Dyer 1973 *Lecture Notes in Math.* 350; their list of exceptional primes $\ell \in \{2, 3, 5, 7, 23, 691\}$ for $\Delta$ has been a central computational testing ground for the deformation theory of modular Galois representations developed by Mazur 1989 and used by Wiles 1995 in his proof of modularity for semistable elliptic curves. The single function $\tau$, defined by an infinite product of twenty-fourth powers, sits at the intersection of analytic number theory, modular-form theory, étale cohomology, and the Langlands programme.
