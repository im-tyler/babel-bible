## Intuition [Beginner]

*Schur-Weyl duality* is one of the most elegant facts in representation theory. Take an $n$-fold tensor product of a vector space — the space whose elements are $n$-fold formal products of vectors, written $V^{(n)}$ for now (the standard symbol is introduced at the Intermediate tier). Two groups act on this space at the same time: the general linear group $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ acts on every factor simultaneously (the diagonal action), and the symmetric group $S_n$ acts by permuting the $n$ factors. These two actions commute. Schur (1901) and Weyl (1939) discovered that the two actions are not just compatible — they are *dual* in a very strong sense.

The duality says the $n$-fold tensor power breaks into a beautifully clean direct sum, indexed by partitions $\lambda$ of $n$. Each summand is the tensor product of an irreducible representation of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ paired with an irreducible representation of the symmetric group. There is no overlap and no waste: every partition contributes exactly one piece, and the matching is bijective.

The reason this matters: it gives a way to read off everything you might want to know about $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ from the symmetric group, and vice versa. The character theory of $S_n$ — which is purely combinatorial and lives on partitions — controls the character theory of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$. Two areas of mathematics that look completely separate at first inspection are revealed to be different sides of one coin.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Work the case $n = 2$ explicitly. Take $V = \mathbb{C}^2$, the simplest non-zero vector space where the picture is rich. The tensor square of $V$ is a 4-dimensional space spanned by four formal products: take the two basis vectors $e_1, e_2$ and form the four formal products $(e_1, e_1)$, $(e_1, e_2)$, $(e_2, e_1)$, $(e_2, e_2)$ where the first entry sits in the first tensor factor and the second entry sits in the second.

Step 1. The symmetric group $S_2$ has two elements: the identity and the swap $\tau$. The swap sends the formal product $(e_i, e_j)$ to $(e_j, e_i)$. So $\tau$ fixes $(e_1, e_1)$ and $(e_2, e_2)$, and swaps $(e_1, e_2)$ with $(e_2, e_1)$.

Step 2. Decompose by the eigenspaces of $\tau$. The vectors fixed by $\tau$ are those with eigenvalue $+1$: a basis is $(e_1, e_1)$, $(e_2, e_2)$, and the symmetric combination $(e_1, e_2) + (e_2, e_1)$. This is a 3-dimensional space, the symmetric square $\mathrm{Sym}^2 V$.

Step 3. The vectors flipped in sign by $\tau$ are those with eigenvalue $-1$: a basis is the antisymmetric combination $(e_1, e_2) - (e_2, e_1)$. This is 1-dimensional, the alternating square $\Lambda^2 V$.

Step 4. Now check the matching with $\mathrm{GL}(V)$. The general linear group $\mathrm{GL}(2, \mathbb{C})$ acts on the tensor square by acting on every factor simultaneously: $g$ sends the formal product $(v, w)$ to $(gv, gw)$. This action commutes with the swap $\tau$ because $\tau$ does not see which factor is "first". So $\mathrm{Sym}^2 V$ and $\Lambda^2 V$ are both stable under $\mathrm{GL}(2, \mathbb{C})$, and each is an irreducible $\mathrm{GL}(2, \mathbb{C})$-representation. The 3-dimensional symmetric square corresponds to the partition $\lambda = (2)$; the 1-dimensional alternating square (the determinant representation) corresponds to $\lambda = (1, 1)$.

Step 5. Read off Schur-Weyl: the tensor square of $V$ is the direct sum of two pieces. One piece is the symmetric square paired with the unit representation of $S_2$; the other is the alternating square paired with the sign representation of $S_2$. Each partition $\lambda$ of $2$ contributes one summand. The dimensions check: $3 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 1 = 4$, the dimension of the tensor square.

What this tells us: the two ways the tensor square can break up — by the swap action of $S_2$ on one side, and by the action of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ on the other — are matched to each other exactly. The matching is indexed by partitions of $n = 2$. The same pattern works for any $n$, and that is the content of Schur-Weyl duality.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a finite-dimensional complex vector space $V$ of dimension $d$, and an integer $n \geq 1$. The $n$-fold tensor power $V^{\otimes n}$ carries two commuting actions:

**Definition (diagonal $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-action).** For $g \in \mathrm{GL}(V)$ and a pure tensor $v_1 \otimes v_2 \otimes \cdots \otimes v_n \in V^{\otimes n}$,
$$
g \cdot (v_1 \otimes v_2 \otimes \cdots \otimes v_n) := (g v_1) \otimes (g v_2) \otimes \cdots \otimes (g v_n).
$$
This extends $\mathbb{C}$-linearly to all of $V^{\otimes n}$. The action defines a representation $\rho : \mathrm{GL}(V) \to \mathrm{GL}(V^{\otimes n})$.

**Definition (place-permutation $S_n$-action).** For $\sigma \in S_n$,
$$
\sigma \cdot (v_1 \otimes v_2 \otimes \cdots \otimes v_n) := v_{\sigma^{-1}(1)} \otimes v_{\sigma^{-1}(2)} \otimes \cdots \otimes v_{\sigma^{-1}(n)}.
$$
This defines a representation $\pi : S_n \to \mathrm{GL}(V^{\otimes n})$. The two actions commute: $\rho(g) \pi(\sigma) = \pi(\sigma) \rho(g)$ for all $g \in \mathrm{GL}(V)$, $\sigma \in S_n$, since the $\mathrm{GL}$-action treats every factor identically.

**Definition (Weyl module / Schur functor).** For a partition $\lambda \vdash n$ with $\ell(\lambda) \leq d = \dim V$, the *Weyl module* (equivalently, the *Schur functor* applied to $V$) is the $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-representation
$$
\mathbb{S}^\lambda V := \mathrm{Hom}_{S_n}(S^\lambda, V^{\otimes n}),
$$
where $S^\lambda$ is the Specht module [07.05.03] of shape $\lambda$. Equivalently, $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ is the $\lambda$-isotypic component of $V^{\otimes n}$ regarded as an $S_n$-module, separated out by the Young symmetriser $c_\lambda \in \mathbb{C}[S_n]$:
$$
\mathbb{S}^\lambda V = c_\lambda \cdot V^{\otimes n}.
$$
The first form makes the $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-action manifest; the second form gives an explicit construction via projection.

**Definition (Young symmetriser).** For a tableau $T$ of shape $\lambda$, let $R(T)$ and $C(T)$ be the row and column stabilisers in $S_n$. The *Young symmetriser* is
$$
c_T := \Big(\sum_{\sigma \in R(T)} \sigma\Big) \cdot \Big(\sum_{\tau \in C(T)} \mathrm{sgn}(\tau) \tau\Big) \in \mathbb{C}[S_n].
$$
Up to a scalar, $c_T^2 = (n!/\dim S^\lambda) \cdot c_T$, so a suitably normalised $c_T$ is an idempotent projector onto a copy of the Specht module $S^\lambda$. The image $c_T \cdot V^{\otimes n}$ is the Weyl module $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The bound $\ell(\lambda) \leq d$ is sharp: if $\ell(\lambda) > \dim V$, then $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V = 0$. The reason is that the column antisymmetriser kills any tensor with a repeated factor, and if a column has more entries than $d$, every filling forces a repeat.
- The action of $S_n$ on $V^{\otimes n}$ is by *place permutation*, not by acting on labels. With place permutation, the inverse appears in the formula above so that the action is a left action.
- The decomposition holds in characteristic $0$. In characteristic $p$, the double commutant argument fails (Wedderburn-Artin needs semisimplicity), and the duality is replaced by Schur algebra theory and Green's polynomial representations [ref: TODO_REF Green 1980 LNM 830].

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Schur-Weyl duality; Schur 1901, Weyl 1939).** *Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional complex vector space of dimension $d$, and let $n \geq 1$. Under the commuting actions of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ and $S_n$, the tensor power decomposes as a $(\mathrm{GL}(V) \times S_n)$-module as*
$$
V^{\otimes n} = \bigoplus_{\substack{\lambda \vdash n \\ \ell(\lambda) \leq d}} \mathbb{S}^\lambda V \otimes S^\lambda,
$$
*where the sum is over partitions $\lambda$ of $n$ with at most $d$ parts, $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ is the irreducible $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-representation with highest weight $\lambda$ (the Weyl module), and $S^\lambda$ is the irreducible $S_n$-representation (the Specht module of shape $\lambda$). Each $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ is non-zero exactly when $\ell(\lambda) \leq d$.*

The matching is bijective: distinct partitions give distinct irreducible bimodule summands, and every irreducible bimodule summand of $V^{\otimes n}$ has this form.

**Proof.** The argument is the double commutant theorem applied to the pair of subalgebras of $\mathrm{End}(V^{\otimes n})$ generated by the two actions. Write
$$
A := \rho(\mathbb{C}[\mathrm{GL}(V)]) \subseteq \mathrm{End}(V^{\otimes n}), \qquad B := \pi(\mathbb{C}[S_n]) \subseteq \mathrm{End}(V^{\otimes n}),
$$
for the subalgebras spanned by the two actions.

**Step 1 ($B = A'$).** The centraliser of $A$ in $\mathrm{End}(V^{\otimes n})$ consists of all endomorphisms commuting with the diagonal $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-action. By a classical theorem of Schur (his 1901 thesis result), the algebra of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-equivariant endomorphisms of $V^{\otimes n}$ is exactly the algebra of *symmetric tensors* of $\mathrm{End}(V)^{\otimes n}$, generated by the place-permutation action of $S_n$. The argument: the polynomial functions on $\mathrm{End}(V)$ that are $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-invariant under the conjugation $\mathrm{GL}(V) \times \mathrm{End}(V)^{\otimes n} \to \mathrm{End}(V)^{\otimes n}$ given by simultaneous conjugation are generated by traces of words, which after polarisation reduce to symmetric combinations of the place-permutation operators. Hence $B = A' := \{x \in \mathrm{End}(V^{\otimes n}) : xa = ax \text{ for all } a \in A\}$.

**Step 2 ($A = B'$).** The reverse statement is symmetric. The endomorphisms commuting with the place-permutation $S_n$-action on $V^{\otimes n}$ are exactly the diagonal $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-action (extended to its linear span). This is the easier direction: any operator commuting with every $\sigma \in S_n$ must preserve the symmetric-tensor decomposition $V^{\otimes n} = \bigoplus_\lambda V^{\otimes n}[\lambda]$, and on each isotypic component the only $S_n$-equivariant endomorphisms commuting with diagonal $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ are the diagonal-action operators themselves. Equivalently: $A$ is the image of the group algebra of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$, which by Burnside's density theorem maps surjectively onto $\mathrm{End}_{S_n}(V^{\otimes n})$ (the centraliser of $B$). Hence $A = B'$.

**Step 3 (Wedderburn-Artin / double commutant).** Both algebras $A$ and $B$ are semisimple (over $\mathbb{C}$): $B$ is the image of the semisimple group algebra $\mathbb{C}[S_n]$, and $A$ is the image of $\mathbb{C}[\mathrm{GL}(V)]$ on the algebraic representation $V^{\otimes n}$, which by complete reducibility of polynomial $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-representations is semisimple. The double commutant theorem (a corollary of Wedderburn-Artin for semisimple algebras) then gives a bimodule decomposition
$$
V^{\otimes n} = \bigoplus_i U_i \otimes W_i,
$$
where $\{U_i\}$ are the distinct irreducible $A$-modules appearing in $V^{\otimes n}$, $\{W_i\}$ are the distinct irreducible $B$-modules appearing in $V^{\otimes n}$, and $U_i \leftrightarrow W_i$ is a bijection between the two index sets.

**Step 4 (identifying the bijection).** The irreducible $B$-modules appearing in $V^{\otimes n}$ are exactly the Specht modules $S^\lambda$ for partitions $\lambda \vdash n$ with $\ell(\lambda) \leq d$; the cut-off comes from the fact that a Specht module $S^\lambda$ with $\ell(\lambda) > d$ has its corresponding Young symmetriser annihilating $V^{\otimes n}$ (any column of length exceeding $\dim V$ forces an antisymmetric repeat among $d$-dimensional vectors, which vanishes). Define $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ to be the irreducible $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-summand paired with $S^\lambda$ under the bimodule decomposition: $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V := \mathrm{Hom}_{S_n}(S^\lambda, V^{\otimes n})$. Then by the double commutant decomposition,
$$
V^{\otimes n} = \bigoplus_{\substack{\lambda \vdash n \\ \ell(\lambda) \leq d}} \mathbb{S}^\lambda V \otimes S^\lambda,
$$
and the matching $\lambda \leftrightarrow (\mathbb{S}^\lambda V, S^\lambda)$ is bijective by construction.

**Step 5 (irreducibility of $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$).** The Weyl module $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ is irreducible as a $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-representation because the bimodule decomposition pairs distinct irreducibles on each side: $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{GL}(V) \times S_n}(\mathbb{S}^\lambda V \otimes S^\lambda, \mathbb{S}^\mu V \otimes S^\mu) = 0$ for $\lambda \neq \mu$, and the scalar-only $\mathrm{GL}(V) \times S_n$-endomorphism algebra forces $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ irreducible by Schur's lemma [07.01.02] applied to each factor.

This completes the proof. $\square$

**Bridge.** Schur-Weyl duality builds toward the structure of every polynomial representation of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ and identifies the character theory of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ with the character theory of $S_n$. The foundational reason it holds is that the diagonal action of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ on $V^{\otimes n}$ and the place-permutation action of $S_n$ are dual to each other in the precise sense of the double commutant theorem. This is exactly the same pattern that appears again in [07.06.11] (representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_2$), where the simplest case $d = 2$ identifies the Weyl modules with the symmetric powers $\mathrm{Sym}^n V$ — the irreducible representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_2(\mathbb{C})$. The central insight is that the bimodule decomposition of $V^{\otimes n}$ identifies $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-irreducibles with $S_n$-irreducibles via partitions of $n$, and the Schur functor $V \mapsto \mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ is the natural-transformation packaging of this identification. The bridge is the recognition that polynomial functors on the category of vector spaces — Schur functors — generalise to a complete description of polynomial $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-representations, with character theory governed by the Schur polynomials $s_\lambda$. Putting these together, the Frobenius character formula identifies the symmetric-group characters with the Schur polynomials, and the Weyl character formula for $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ then follows as a corollary of the duality.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that the Young symmetriser $c_\lambda \in \mathbb{C}[S_n]$ satisfies $c_\lambda^2 = \mu \cdot c_\lambda$ for a non-zero scalar $\mu$, and identify $\mu$ in terms of $|S_n|$ and $\dim S^\lambda$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Young symmetriser projects onto a copy of the Specht module $S^\lambda$ inside the group algebra. Use the fact that $\mathbb{C}[S_n] \cdot c_\lambda$ is isomorphic to $S^\lambda$ as a left $S_n$-module.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The left ideal $\mathbb{C}[S_n] \cdot c_\lambda \subseteq \mathbb{C}[S_n]$ is isomorphic to the Specht module $S^\lambda$ as a left $S_n$-module. Right multiplication by $c_\lambda$ then defines a $\mathbb{C}$-linear endomorphism of $S^\lambda$, which by Schur's lemma is a scalar $\mu$. Compute the trace: the trace of right-multiplication by $c_\lambda$ on $\mathbb{C}[S_n]$ is the coefficient of the identity in $c_\lambda$, which is $1$ (by inspection of the Young-symmetriser formula). Dividing by the multiplicity $\dim S^\lambda$ of $S^\lambda$ in $\mathbb{C}[S_n]$, the scalar of right-multiplication on $S^\lambda$ is $\mu = n!/\dim S^\lambda$. So $c_\lambda^2 = (n!/\dim S^\lambda) \cdot c_\lambda$. Normalising: the idempotent $\tilde{c}_\lambda := (\dim S^\lambda / n!) \cdot c_\lambda$ satisfies $\tilde{c}_\lambda^2 = \tilde{c}_\lambda$ and projects onto $S^\lambda$. Rubric: full credit for the scalar $\mu = n!/\dim S^\lambda$ with the trace argument.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Use Schur-Weyl duality to derive the Frobenius character formula relating the symmetric-group character $\chi^\lambda$ with the Schur polynomial $s_\lambda$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the trace of $\rho(g) \pi(\sigma)$ on $V^{\otimes n}$ in two ways: by the Schur-Weyl decomposition, and by direct computation using the eigenvalues of $g$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $g \in \mathrm{GL}(V)$ have eigenvalues $x_1, \ldots, x_d$. Let $\sigma \in S_n$ have cycle type $\mu = (1^{m_1} 2^{m_2} \cdots)$. Compute the trace $\mathrm{tr}(\rho(g) \pi(\sigma))$ on $V^{\otimes n}$. By Schur-Weyl: $\mathrm{tr}(\rho(g) \pi(\sigma)) = \sum_{\lambda \vdash n} s_\lambda(x_1, \ldots, x_d) \cdot \chi^\lambda(\sigma)$, where $s_\lambda$ is the character of $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ as a function of the eigenvalues of $g$. By direct computation: $\mathrm{tr}(\rho(g) \pi(\sigma)) = \prod_k p_k(x_1, \ldots, x_d)^{m_k}$, where $p_k(x_1, \ldots, x_d) = x_1^k + \cdots + x_d^k$ is the $k$-th power-sum symmetric polynomial; the direct argument uses that on a $k$-cycle of $\sigma$, the trace of the cyclic permutation of $k$ tensor factors with diagonal $\mathrm{GL}$-action is exactly $p_k$. Equating the two computations gives the *Frobenius character formula*: $p_\mu := \prod_k p_{\mu_k} = \sum_\lambda \chi^\lambda(\sigma) \cdot s_\lambda$. Inverting via orthogonality of irreducible characters yields the dual form $s_\lambda = \sum_\mu z_\mu^{-1} \chi^\lambda(\mu) p_\mu$ with $z_\mu = \prod_k k^{m_k} m_k!$. Rubric: full credit for the two trace computations and the Frobenius formula.

</details>

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that for $\lambda = (n)$ (single row), $\mathbb{S}^{(n)} V = \mathrm{Sym}^n V$, and for $\lambda = (1^n)$ (single column), $\mathbb{S}^{(1^n)} V = \Lambda^n V$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $\lambda = (n)$ the Young symmetriser is the row-symmetriser $\sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \sigma$ alone (column stabiliser is the identity). For $\lambda = (1^n)$ the symmetriser is the column-antisymmetriser $\sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) \sigma$ alone.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $\lambda = (n)$: a tableau of shape $(n)$ has one row and no non-identity columns. The Young symmetriser $c_{(n)} = \sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \sigma$ is the *symmetrising operator*. Applied to $V^{\otimes n}$, the image is the subspace of fully symmetric tensors, which is exactly $\mathrm{Sym}^n V$. Hence $\mathbb{S}^{(n)} V = \mathrm{Sym}^n V$ with $\dim = \binom{d+n-1}{n}$.

For $\lambda = (1^n)$: a tableau of shape $(1^n)$ has one column and no non-identity rows. The Young symmetriser $c_{(1^n)} = \sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) \sigma$ is the *antisymmetrising operator*. Applied to $V^{\otimes n}$, the image is the subspace of fully antisymmetric tensors, which is exactly $\Lambda^n V$. Hence $\mathbb{S}^{(1^n)} V = \Lambda^n V$ with $\dim = \binom{d}{n}$ (zero if $n > d$, matching the length-bound).

The two extreme partitions recover the two classical functors. Every other partition gives a Schur functor strictly between symmetric and antisymmetric. Rubric: full credit for both identifications plus the dimension formulas.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Derive the Weyl character formula for $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ from Schur-Weyl duality and the Jacobi-Trudi identity for Schur polynomials.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The character of $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ as a $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-representation is the Schur polynomial $s_\lambda(x_1, \ldots, x_d)$ in the eigenvalues. The Jacobi-Trudi identity gives $s_\lambda$ as a determinant.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Schur-Weyl, the character of $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ at $g \in \mathrm{GL}(V)$ with eigenvalues $x_1, \ldots, x_d$ is the Schur polynomial $s_\lambda(x_1, \ldots, x_d)$. The Jacobi-Trudi identity expresses $s_\lambda$ as a ratio of determinants:
$$
s_\lambda(x_1, \ldots, x_d) = \frac{\det\big(x_i^{\lambda_j + d - j}\big)_{1 \leq i, j \leq d}}{\det\big(x_i^{d - j}\big)_{1 \leq i, j \leq d}},
$$
where the denominator is the Vandermonde determinant $\prod_{i < j}(x_i - x_j)$. The numerator is the *alternant* indexed by the highest weight $\lambda + \delta$, where $\delta = (d-1, d-2, \ldots, 1, 0)$ is the half-sum of positive roots of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ in the standard convention. Recognising the Vandermonde as the Weyl denominator $\prod_{\alpha > 0} (e^{\alpha/2} - e^{-\alpha/2})$ evaluated at $g$, and the numerator as the antisymmetrisation $\sum_w \mathrm{sgn}(w) e^{w(\lambda + \delta)}$ summed over the Weyl group $S_d$, the formula becomes the *Weyl character formula*
$$
\chi_\lambda(g) = \frac{\sum_{w \in S_d} \mathrm{sgn}(w) e^{w(\lambda + \delta)}(g)}{\prod_{\alpha > 0}(e^{\alpha/2} - e^{-\alpha/2})(g)}.
$$
The duality gives the formula for $\mathrm{GL}(V)$; specialising to a maximal compact $\mathrm{U}(d) \subset \mathrm{GL}(V)$ and using the unitarian trick (Weyl 1925-26 *Mathematische Zeitschrift*) extends the formula to the compact-group setting. Rubric: full credit for the Jacobi-Trudi-to-Weyl-character derivation.

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove that the centraliser algebra $\mathrm{End}_{\mathrm{GL}(V)}(V^{\otimes n})$ is surjected onto by $\mathbb{C}[S_n]$, and identify the kernel of the surjection.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The image is the centraliser algebra; the kernel is the annihilator of $V^{\otimes n}$ in $\mathbb{C}[S_n]$. Express the kernel in terms of Specht modules $S^\lambda$ for $\ell(\lambda) > \dim V$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The map $\pi : \mathbb{C}[S_n] \to \mathrm{End}_{\mathrm{GL}(V)}(V^{\otimes n})$ sends $\sigma \mapsto \pi(\sigma)$, the place-permutation action. By Step 1 of the Schur-Weyl proof, the image is exactly the centraliser of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ in $\mathrm{End}(V^{\otimes n})$, so $\pi$ is surjective onto $\mathrm{End}_{\mathrm{GL}(V)}(V^{\otimes n})$. The kernel is the annihilator
$$
\ker \pi = \{x \in \mathbb{C}[S_n] : x \cdot v = 0 \text{ for all } v \in V^{\otimes n}\}.
$$
By the Wedderburn decomposition $\mathbb{C}[S_n] = \bigoplus_{\lambda \vdash n} \mathrm{End}(S^\lambda)$, the annihilator of $V^{\otimes n}$ is the sum of the matrix-algebra summands $\mathrm{End}(S^\lambda)$ for which $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V = 0$, i.e., for $\ell(\lambda) > d = \dim V$. So
$$
\ker \pi = \bigoplus_{\substack{\lambda \vdash n \\ \ell(\lambda) > d}} \mathrm{End}(S^\lambda),
$$
and the image $\mathrm{End}_{\mathrm{GL}(V)}(V^{\otimes n}) \cong \bigoplus_{\lambda \vdash n,\, \ell(\lambda) \leq d} \mathrm{End}(S^\lambda)$. When $n \leq d$ the kernel vanishes and $\pi$ is an algebra isomorphism — the so-called *stable range* of Schur-Weyl. The image algebra in general is the *Schur algebra* $S_\mathbb{C}(d, n)$ studied by Green (1980). Rubric: full credit for the kernel identification and the surjectivity argument.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (Schur-Weyl in the stable range; $n \leq d$).** *When $n \leq d = \dim V$, the place-permutation map $\pi : \mathbb{C}[S_n] \to \mathrm{End}_{\mathrm{GL}(V)}(V^{\otimes n})$ is an algebra isomorphism, and every partition $\lambda \vdash n$ contributes a non-zero summand $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V \otimes S^\lambda$ to the Schur-Weyl decomposition. The total number of summands is $p(n)$, the number of partitions of $n$.*

In the stable range, the duality is at its sharpest: no length-bound cuts off any partition, and the $S_n$-side and $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-side are in perfect dual correspondence. For $n > d$, partitions $\lambda$ with $\ell(\lambda) > d$ are excised on the $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-side (the corresponding $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V = 0$), and the map $\pi$ acquires a kernel of size $\sum_{\ell(\lambda) > d} (\dim S^\lambda)^2$.

**Theorem (Frobenius character formula; Frobenius 1900 *Sitzungsberichte Berlin*).** *Let $\sigma \in S_n$ have cycle type $\mu = (1^{m_1} 2^{m_2} \cdots)$. The character of the Specht module $S^\lambda$ is*
$$
\chi^\lambda(\sigma) = \sum_{w \in S_d} \mathrm{sgn}(w) \cdot [\text{coefficient of } x_1^{\lambda_1 + d - 1} x_2^{\lambda_2 + d - 2} \cdots x_d^{\lambda_d}] \prod_{k} p_k(x_1, \ldots, x_d)^{m_k},
$$
*where $p_k = \sum_i x_i^k$ is the $k$-th power-sum, and $d \geq \ell(\lambda)$ is any sufficiently large parameter. Equivalently, the symmetric polynomial identity $\sum_{\lambda \vdash n} \chi^\lambda(\mu) s_\lambda = p_\mu$ holds in the ring of symmetric polynomials.*

The Frobenius formula is the character-theoretic content of Schur-Weyl: the symmetric-group characters and the $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-characters (Schur polynomials) are dual under the standard inner product on the ring of symmetric functions. The transition matrix between the bases $\{s_\lambda\}$ and $\{p_\mu\}$ is the character table of $S_n$.

**Theorem (Weyl character formula for $\mathrm{GL}(V)$; Weyl 1925-26 *Mathematische Zeitschrift*).** *For $\lambda$ a dominant weight of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ (a partition with $\ell(\lambda) \leq d$), the character of $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ at $g \in \mathrm{GL}(V)$ with eigenvalues $x_1, \ldots, x_d$ is the Schur polynomial*
$$
\chi_{\mathbb{S}^\lambda V}(g) = s_\lambda(x_1, \ldots, x_d) = \frac{\det\big(x_i^{\lambda_j + d - j}\big)_{i,j}}{\det\big(x_i^{d - j}\big)_{i,j}}.
$$
*This is the Weyl character formula for the general linear group, derived in 1925-26 by the unitarian trick from the representation theory of the compact unitary group $\mathrm{U}(d)$.*

Schur-Weyl gives the formula via the Frobenius-character side: $s_\lambda$ is the character of $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ by the duality, and the Jacobi-Trudi alternant representation of $s_\lambda$ is the Weyl character formula in its determinantal form.

**Theorem (Schur functors as polynomial functors; Schur 1901 dissertation, modern form Macdonald 1995 §I.7).** *The assignment $V \mapsto \mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ is a polynomial functor from finite-dimensional complex vector spaces to themselves, homogeneous of degree $n = |\lambda|$. The composition of Schur functors is governed by the Littlewood-Richardson coefficients:*
$$
\mathbb{S}^\lambda V \otimes \mathbb{S}^\mu V = \bigoplus_\nu c^\nu_{\lambda \mu} \cdot \mathbb{S}^\nu V,
$$
*and the Schur polynomials $s_\lambda$ form a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of the ring $\Lambda$ of symmetric functions, multiplying as $s_\lambda \cdot s_\mu = \sum_\nu c^\nu_{\lambda \mu} s_\nu$.*

The polynomial-functor formulation extends Schur-Weyl from $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ to the categorical setting: Schur functors are the universal polynomial functors on vector spaces, and any natural transformation between polynomial functors of the same degree factors through the Schur decomposition.

**Theorem ($\mathfrak{sl}_2$ case as smallest Schur-Weyl).** *For $V = \mathbb{C}^2$, Schur-Weyl gives*
$$
V^{\otimes n} = \bigoplus_{k = 0}^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} \mathrm{Sym}^{n - 2k} V \otimes S^{(n-k, k)}
$$
*where $\mathrm{Sym}^{n-2k} V$ is the irreducible $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-representation of highest weight $n - 2k$. The decomposition is the algebraic version of angular-momentum addition in physics: adding $n$ spin-$1/2$ particles gives total spins $n/2, n/2 - 1, \ldots, 0$ or $1/2$, with multiplicities counted by $\dim S^{(n-k, k)}$.*

The $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ case is the simplest substantive example of Schur-Weyl and the input to the angular-momentum coupling rules in quantum mechanics. The Clebsch-Gordan coefficients on the physics side are the matrix elements of the Schur-Weyl isomorphism.

**Theorem (centraliser-algebra interpretation).** *The Schur algebra $S_\mathbb{C}(d, n) := \mathrm{End}_{S_n}(V^{\otimes n})$ where $V = \mathbb{C}^d$ has the same finite-dimensional representation theory as polynomial $\mathrm{GL}_d$-representations of degree $n$. Green's monograph (1980 LNM 830) treats Schur-Weyl from this algebra-theoretic side and extends the theory to arbitrary infinite fields and to quantum groups.*

The Schur algebra is the algebraic skeleton of the polynomial representation theory of $\mathrm{GL}_d$. Over a field of positive characteristic, Schur-Weyl in its bimodule form fails (Wedderburn-Artin requires semisimplicity), but the Schur algebra $S_k(d, n)$ retains a rich representation theory described by Green's quasi-hereditary algebra structure, with implications for modular representation theory.

**Synthesis.** Schur-Weyl duality is the foundational reason every polynomial representation of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ is built from the symmetric-group representations via Schur functors. The central insight is that the two natural actions on $V^{\otimes n}$ — diagonal $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ and place-permutation $S_n$ — are dual to each other in the precise sense of the double commutant theorem, with the duality indexed by partitions of $n$. This is exactly the same pattern that appears again in [07.06.11] (representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_2$), where the simplest case $\dim V = 2$ recovers the angular-momentum decomposition that physicists call "adding spin-1/2 particles" and that mathematicians call "Clebsch-Gordan coupling". Putting these together, the duality identifies the character theory of $S_n$ (Frobenius's formula) with the character theory of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ (Weyl's formula via Jacobi-Trudi alternants); the bridge is the symmetric-function ring $\Lambda$, whose Schur basis $s_\lambda$ encodes both sides.

The duality generalises in three directions. Replacing $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ with the orthogonal or symplectic group, and $S_n$ with the Brauer algebra, gives the Brauer-Weyl duality of Brauer 1937 *Annals of Mathematics*. Replacing the field $\mathbb{C}$ with a positive-characteristic field, and the group algebra with the Schur algebra, gives Green's modular Schur-Weyl. Replacing $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ with the quantum group $U_q(\mathfrak{gl}_n)$ and $S_n$ with the Hecke algebra $H_q(S_n)$ gives the Jimbo-Drinfeld quantum Schur-Weyl duality, which is dual to itself in the precise sense that the duality identifies the centraliser of one quantum group with the other. The same skeleton — two algebras acting on a tensor power, each one the centraliser of the other, with irreducibles paired by a combinatorial index set — recurs through every flavour of representation theory and is what justifies treating "Schur-Weyl-type duality" as a structural principle rather than a single theorem.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Theorem (Schur-Weyl duality), proof.** Given in the Intermediate-tier section: double commutant theorem applied to the pair $(A, B) = (\rho(\mathbb{C}[\mathrm{GL}(V)]), \pi(\mathbb{C}[S_n]))$ acting on $V^{\otimes n}$. The two-sided centraliser identifications $A = B'$ and $B = A'$ come from Schur's 1901 result on $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-equivariant tensor endomorphisms (Step 1) and the Burnside density theorem (Step 2). Wedderburn-Artin for semisimple algebras over $\mathbb{C}$ then yields the bimodule decomposition. The partition $\lambda \leftrightarrow (\mathbb{S}^\lambda V, S^\lambda)$ matching is bijective by construction, with the length-bound $\ell(\lambda) \leq d$ coming from the vanishing of the Young symmetriser on tensors of "too few" factors. $\square$

**Proposition (Schur-Weyl in the stable range, $n \leq d$), proof.** When $n \leq d$, every partition $\lambda \vdash n$ has $\ell(\lambda) \leq n \leq d$, so the length-bound is automatic and every partition contributes. The annihilator of $V^{\otimes n}$ in $\mathbb{C}[S_n]$ — namely $\bigoplus_{\ell(\lambda) > d} \mathrm{End}(S^\lambda)$ — is the zero subalgebra. Hence $\pi : \mathbb{C}[S_n] \to \mathrm{End}_{\mathrm{GL}(V)}(V^{\otimes n})$ is injective, and by Schur-Weyl surjective, hence an algebra isomorphism. The total summand count is $p(n)$, the number of partitions of $n$. $\square$

**Proposition (Frobenius character formula), proof sketch.** Compute the bivariate character $\mathrm{tr}(\rho(g) \pi(\sigma))$ on $V^{\otimes n}$ in two ways. By Schur-Weyl, it equals $\sum_\lambda \chi_{\mathbb{S}^\lambda V}(g) \cdot \chi^\lambda(\sigma) = \sum_\lambda s_\lambda(x_1, \ldots, x_d) \chi^\lambda(\sigma)$, where $x_1, \ldots, x_d$ are eigenvalues of $g$. By direct computation, for $\sigma$ of cycle type $\mu$, the trace equals $p_\mu(x_1, \ldots, x_d) = \prod_k p_k^{m_k}$, where the factor $p_k$ records the trace of $g^k$ on each $k$-cycle of $\sigma$ acting on $V^{\otimes k}$. Equating: $\sum_\lambda \chi^\lambda(\mu) s_\lambda = p_\mu$. Inverting via Schur-polynomial orthogonality gives the dual formula $s_\lambda = \sum_\mu z_\mu^{-1} \chi^\lambda(\mu) p_\mu$. Both sides live in the ring of symmetric functions $\Lambda$ in infinitely many variables. $\square$

**Proposition (Weyl character formula for $\mathrm{GL}(V)$), proof.** By Schur-Weyl, the character of $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ at $g$ with eigenvalues $x_1, \ldots, x_d$ is the Schur polynomial $s_\lambda(x_1, \ldots, x_d)$. The Jacobi-Trudi identity expresses
$$
s_\lambda(x_1, \ldots, x_d) = \frac{\det(x_i^{\lambda_j + d - j})_{i, j}}{\det(x_i^{d-j})_{i, j}}.
$$
The numerator is the alternant $a_{\lambda + \delta}$, where $\delta = (d-1, \ldots, 1, 0)$ is the half-sum of positive roots of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$; the denominator is the Vandermonde $\prod_{i<j}(x_i - x_j) = a_\delta$. Recognising the numerator as $\sum_{w \in S_d} \mathrm{sgn}(w) x^{w(\lambda + \delta)}$ and the denominator as the Weyl denominator gives the Weyl character formula in its standard form. $\square$

**Proposition (Schur functor decomposition), proof.** The polynomial-functor structure is built directly into the construction: $\mathbb{S}^\lambda$ is defined on a vector space $V$ by $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V = c_\lambda \cdot V^{\otimes n}$ for the Young symmetriser $c_\lambda$, and this assignment extends to morphisms by functoriality of tensor powers. Polynomial-functor-ness follows because $\mathbb{S}^\lambda(V) \subseteq V^{\otimes n}$ as a sub-functor; tensor power is polynomial of degree $n$. For the Littlewood-Richardson decomposition of $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V \otimes \mathbb{S}^\mu V$: applying Schur-Weyl to the tensor power $V^{\otimes (n+m)}$ with $n = |\lambda|, m = |\mu|$, and selecting the $(\lambda, \mu)$-isotypic component under the embedding $S_n \times S_m \hookrightarrow S_{n+m}$, gives the formula
$$
\mathrm{Ind}_{S_n \times S_m}^{S_{n+m}}(S^\lambda \boxtimes S^\mu) = \bigoplus_\nu c^\nu_{\lambda \mu} S^\nu,
$$
where $c^\nu_{\lambda \mu}$ are the Littlewood-Richardson coefficients. Dualising under Schur-Weyl translates this to the Schur-functor side. $\square$

**Proposition ($\mathfrak{sl}_2$ case decomposition), proof.** For $V = \mathbb{C}^2$, the partitions of $n$ with at most $2$ parts are $(n - k, k)$ for $0 \leq k \leq \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$. The Weyl module $\mathbb{S}^{(n-k, k)} \mathbb{C}^2$ has highest weight $\lambda = (n-k) - k = n - 2k$ in the standard $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-convention, with dimension $n - 2k + 1$. The Specht module $S^{(n-k, k)}$ has dimension $\binom{n}{k} - \binom{n}{k-1}$ by the hook length formula [07.05.02]. The total dimension check:
$$
\sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor n/2 \rfloor} (n - 2k + 1) \left[\binom{n}{k} - \binom{n}{k-1}\right] = 2^n,
$$
which can be verified by a generating-function computation or by recognising the left side as the dimension of $V^{\otimes n} = (\mathbb{C}^2)^{\otimes n} = \mathbb{C}^{2^n}$. The physical interpretation: $n$ spin-$1/2$ particles couple to total spin $j = n/2 - k$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$, with multiplicity $\dim S^{(n-k, k)}$ given by the corresponding Catalan-like count. $\square$

**Proposition (centraliser-algebra interpretation), stated without proof — see Green 1980 *Polynomial Representations of* $\mathrm{GL}_n$ LNM 830 [ref: TODO_REF].** The Schur algebra $S_\mathbb{C}(d, n) = \mathrm{End}_{S_n}(V^{\otimes n})$ has finite-dimensional category of modules equivalent to the polynomial $\mathrm{GL}_d$-representations of degree $n$. The argument is Morita-theoretic: $V^{\otimes n}$ is a finitely generated projective generator for $S_\mathbb{C}(d, n)$, and the functor $M \mapsto V^{\otimes n} \otimes_{S_\mathbb{C}(d, n)} M$ is an equivalence of categories. Over a positive-characteristic field $k$, the Schur algebra $S_k(d, n)$ replaces Schur-Weyl in its bimodule form, retaining a rich representation theory but without the double commutant decomposition. The proof in Green's monograph extends to quantum Schur-Weyl, with $S_k(d, n)$ replaced by the $q$-Schur algebra and $\mathbb{C}[S_n]$ by the Hecke algebra. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Symmetric group representation [07.05.01].** Schur-Weyl duality matches the irreducible representations of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ with the irreducible representations of $S_n$ via partitions $\lambda \vdash n$. The $S_n$-side is exactly the foundational classification of [07.05.01]: irreducibles indexed by partitions, with characters governed by the Frobenius formula. Schur-Weyl is the bridge that exports this classification to the general-linear group.

- **Specht module [07.05.03].** The $S_n$-irreducible $S^\lambda$ in the bimodule decomposition is the Specht module of [07.05.03]. The Schur functor $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ is constructed as $\mathrm{Hom}_{S_n}(S^\lambda, V^{\otimes n})$, which makes the Specht module the structural input on the symmetric-group side of the duality. The Young symmetriser $c_\lambda \in \mathbb{C}[S_n]$ that projects onto $S^\lambda$ is the same operator that cuts out $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ from $V^{\otimes n}$.

- **Representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_2(\mathbb{C})$ [07.06.11].** The simplest case of Schur-Weyl duality is $V = \mathbb{C}^2$, where $\mathrm{GL}(V) = \mathrm{GL}(2, \mathbb{C})$ and the Weyl modules are the symmetric powers $\mathrm{Sym}^n V$ — the irreducible representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_2$. The Schur-Weyl decomposition $V^{\otimes n} = \bigoplus_k \mathrm{Sym}^{n-2k} V \otimes S^{(n-k, k)}$ is the algebraic content of the Clebsch-Gordan coupling rule for adding spin-1/2 systems in quantum mechanics.

- **Representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_3(\mathbb{C})$ [07.06.12].** Taking $V = \mathbb{C}^3$ and $\mathrm{GL}(V) = \mathrm{GL}(3, \mathbb{C})$, Schur-Weyl duality is the route by which the Fulton-Harris framework derives the $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ irreducibles $V_{a, b}$ from the symmetric-group side: the Weyl module $\mathbb{S}^\lambda \mathbb{C}^3$ for $\lambda = (\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \lambda_3)$ with $\ell(\lambda) \leq 3$ is the irreducible $\mathrm{GL}(3, \mathbb{C})$-representation of highest weight $\lambda - (\lambda_3, \lambda_3, \lambda_3)$, which descends to the $\mathfrak{sl}_3$-irreducible $V_{a, b}$ with $a = \lambda_1 - \lambda_2$, $b = \lambda_2 - \lambda_3$. The hexagonal weight diagram of $V_{a, b}$ at [07.06.12] is therefore the Schur-functor image of the partition $(a + b, b, 0)$, and the Weyl dimension formula $\dim V_{a, b} = (a+1)(b+1)(a+b+2)/2$ is the Schur polynomial $s_{(a+b, b, 0)}(x_1, x_2, x_3)$ evaluated at $x_i = 1$. Schur-Weyl is the foundational bridge that exports the partition combinatorics of $S_n$ to the rank-two highest-weight classification.

- **Tensor product of representations [07.01.06].** Schur-Weyl is a statement about the structure of the tensor power $V^{\otimes n}$, the iterated tensor product of [07.01.06]. The duality identifies the structure of this iterated tensor as a $(\mathrm{GL}(V) \times S_n)$-bimodule and extracts polynomial $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-representations as factors. The Littlewood-Richardson rule extends this to tensor products of Schur functors, $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V \otimes \mathbb{S}^\mu V$.

- **Schur's lemma [07.01.02].** The irreducibility of each Schur functor $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ as a $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-representation follows from Schur's lemma applied to the bimodule structure: distinct $(\lambda, \mu)$-isotypic components have no inter-twiners, forcing $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ irreducible. The lemma is invoked at the final step of the double-commutant proof to separate the Wedderburn matrix-algebra summands into irreducible factors.

- **Weyl character formula [07.06.07].** The character of $\mathbb{S}^\lambda V$ as a $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-representation is the Schur polynomial $s_\lambda(x_1, \ldots, x_d)$. The Jacobi-Trudi expression of $s_\lambda$ as a ratio of alternants is the Weyl character formula for $\mathrm{GL}(V)$, and Schur-Weyl duality provides one of the routes (originally Weyl's 1925-26 derivation) for proving it. The Weyl-character-formula unit [07.06.07] gives the abstract Lie-algebraic form; Schur-Weyl gives the concrete-tensor-power realisation.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Issai Schur's 1901 Berlin dissertation *Über eine Klasse von Matrizen, die sich einer gegebenen Matrix zuordnen lassen* introduced the algebra of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-equivariant tensor endomorphisms, identified it with the symmetric-tensor algebra of $\mathrm{End}(V)^{\otimes n}$, and proved the surjectivity of $\mathbb{C}[S_n] \to \mathrm{End}_{\mathrm{GL}(V)}(V^{\otimes n})$ [ref: TODO_REF]. The 1901 work was the originator of the duality phenomenon, though Schur did not state it in the bimodule-decomposition form that is now standard. Schur's PhD advisor at Berlin was Frobenius, whose 1900 paper *Über die Charaktere der symmetrischen Gruppe* (Sitzungsberichte der Königlich Preussischen Akademie der Wissenschaften zu Berlin 1900, 516-534) [ref: TODO_REF] had established the character theory of $S_n$ via the Frobenius character formula. The 1901 thesis was Schur's translation of his teacher's symmetric-group character theory into the parallel setting of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$.

Hermann Weyl's 1925-26 papers in *Mathematische Zeitschrift* (*Theorie der Darstellung kontinuierlicher halbeinfacher Gruppen durch lineare Transformationen*, Math. Z. 23 (1925), 271-309; 24 (1926), 328-376, 377-395, 789-791) [ref: TODO_REF] introduced the *unitarian trick* and derived the Weyl character formula for compact Lie groups. The corresponding character formula for $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ followed by passage from the compact $\mathrm{U}(d)$ to $\mathrm{GL}(d, \mathbb{C})$ via algebraic continuation. Weyl's 1939 monograph *The Classical Groups: Their Invariants and Representations* (Princeton University Press) [ref: TODO_REF] consolidated the Schur-Weyl story into its modern form, naming the duality and presenting the bimodule decomposition explicitly. The duality is named for Schur and Weyl because of these complementary contributions: Schur 1901 for the centraliser algebra side, Weyl 1939 for the bimodule formulation.

The modern symmetric-function side was developed by Alfred Young (1900-1933 series in Proceedings of the London Mathematical Society) [ref: TODO_REF], who built the explicit projectors $c_\lambda$ now called Young symmetrisers; by Wilhelm Specht (1935 *Mathematische Zeitschrift*) [ref: TODO_REF], who gave the modular-friendly construction of $S_n$-irreducibles; and by Donald Knutson (1973 *$\lambda$-rings and the Representation Theory of the Symmetric Group*) [ref: TODO_REF], who packaged the polynomial-functor formulation. James 1978 *The Representation Theory of the Symmetric Group* (LNM 682) [ref: TODO_REF] gave the canonical modern textbook treatment of the $S_n$-side. Macdonald 1995 *Symmetric Functions and Hall Polynomials* (Oxford University Press, 2nd ed.) [ref: TODO_REF] treats the symmetric-function side, with Chapter I §7 devoted to the Schur basis and its duality properties. Green 1980 *Polynomial Representations of $\mathrm{GL}_n$* (Springer LNM 830) [ref: TODO_REF] handles the polynomial-representation theory in arbitrary characteristic via the Schur algebra. Quantum Schur-Weyl was introduced by Jimbo 1986 and developed by Drinfeld; Brauer-Weyl duality for orthogonal and symplectic groups dates to Brauer 1937 *Annals of Mathematics* 38, 857-872 [ref: TODO_REF].
