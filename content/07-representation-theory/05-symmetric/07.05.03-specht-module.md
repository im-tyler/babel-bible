## Intuition [Beginner]

A *Specht module* is the irreducible representation of the symmetric group $S_n$, built explicitly out of tableaux instead of through abstract characters. Wilhelm Specht's 1935 construction takes a Young diagram of shape $\lambda \vdash n$, fills its boxes with $1$ through $n$ in every possible way, and treats those fillings as basis vectors. A clever antisymmetrisation along columns then carves out the irreducible representation $S^\lambda$ as a concrete subspace.

Specht's construction has one advantage that Frobenius's character theory and Young's symmetriser construction lack: it works in *any characteristic*. Over the rational numbers (or complex numbers), the Specht modules $\{S^\lambda\}_{\lambda \vdash n}$ are exactly the irreducible representations of $S_n$ — the same list given by Frobenius and Young. Over a field of characteristic $p$ where $p$ divides $n!$, Maschke's theorem fails (representations stop being completely reducible), and the Specht modules are no longer always irreducible — but they remain the right starting point. The irreducible modular representations are obtained as the heads $D^\lambda = S^\lambda / \mathrm{rad}(S^\lambda)$ for *$p$-regular* partitions $\lambda$.

This characteristic-flexible construction is why Specht modules are the workhorse of modern symmetric-group representation theory: every modern textbook (Sagan, James, James-Kerber, Mathas) builds the theory through Specht modules rather than through Young symmetrisers.

## Visual [Beginner]

A polytabloid: take a Young diagram, fill it with $1$ through $n$, then antisymmetrise along columns by adding signed sums over column-permuted versions. The result lives inside the *Specht module* — the irreducible representation of $S_n$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take $\lambda = (2, 1)$ for $S_3$. The Young diagram has $3$ cells: two in the top row and one in the bottom. Fill the diagram with $1, 2, 3$ in some order — say $T = \begin{smallmatrix}1&2\\3\end{smallmatrix}$ — and form the *tabloid* $\{T\}$ by ignoring row order. Two tabloids are equal iff their row contents (as sets) match.

Tabloids of shape $(2, 1)$: there are exactly $3$ tabloids, indexed by which of $\{1, 2, 3\}$ ends up in the bottom cell. Call them $\{T_1\}, \{T_2\}, \{T_3\}$ where $T_i$ has $i$ on the bottom.

The *polytabloid* of $T = \begin{smallmatrix}1&2\\3\end{smallmatrix}$ is

$$
e_T = \{T\} - \{T'\},
$$

where $T'$ is the tableau with the column $\{1, 3\}$ swapped: $T' = \begin{smallmatrix}3&2\\1\end{smallmatrix}$. So $e_T = \{T_3\} - \{T_1\}$ in our indexing.

The Specht module $S^{(2,1)}$ is spanned by all polytabloids as $T$ varies. With three columns to antisymmetrise (one column has two cells, the other has one), one can check $S^{(2,1)}$ is $2$-dimensional, matching the dimension of the standard representation of $S_3$. What this tells us: the Specht module realises the abstract irreducible representation as a concrete vector space with a concrete basis you can compute with on paper.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a field $k$ and a partition $\lambda \vdash n$.

**Definition (Tabloid).** A *Young tableau* of shape $\lambda$ is a bijection $T : [\lambda] \to \{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$ — i.e., a filling of the Young diagram $[\lambda]$ with each of $1, \ldots, n$ used exactly once. Two tableaux are *row-equivalent* if one is obtained from the other by permuting entries within each row. An equivalence class is a *tabloid*, written $\{T\}$.

The number of tabloids of shape $\lambda$ is

$$
\binom{n}{\lambda} := \frac{n!}{\lambda_1!\, \lambda_2! \cdots \lambda_k!}.
$$

The set of tabloids carries a left $S_n$-action by permuting entries: $\sigma \cdot \{T\} = \{\sigma T\}$.

**Definition (Permutation module).** The *permutation module* on tabloids is the $kS_n$-module

$$
M^\lambda := k\{\{T\} : T \text{ tableau of shape } \lambda\},
$$

a free $k$-module of rank $\binom{n}{\lambda}$. It is the *induced module* $\mathrm{Ind}_{S_\lambda}^{S_n}(\mathrm{triv})$ where $S_\lambda := S_{\lambda_1} \times S_{\lambda_2} \times \cdots \times S_{\lambda_k}$ is the *Young subgroup*.

**Definition (Polytabloid).** For a tableau $T$ of shape $\lambda$, the *column stabiliser* $C(T) \subseteq S_n$ is the subgroup of permutations preserving each column of $T$ as a set. The *polytabloid* associated to $T$ is

$$
e_T := \sum_{\sigma \in C(T)} \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) \cdot \{\sigma T\} \in M^\lambda.
$$

**Definition (Specht module).** The *Specht module* of shape $\lambda$ over $k$ is

$$
S^\lambda := k\{e_T : T \text{ tableau of shape } \lambda\} \subseteq M^\lambda,
$$

the $k$-span of all polytabloids. It is a $kS_n$-submodule of $M^\lambda$.

**Theorem (Specht 1935; James 1976).**

(1) *Over $k$ of characteristic $0$ (or characteristic not dividing $n!$), the Specht modules $\{S^\lambda\}_{\lambda \vdash n}$ are exactly the irreducible $kS_n$-modules.*

(2) *Over $k$ of characteristic $p$, define $D^\lambda := S^\lambda / \mathrm{rad}(S^\lambda)$ where $\mathrm{rad}(S^\lambda)$ is the radical of an explicit symmetric bilinear form on $S^\lambda$. Then $D^\lambda \neq 0$ iff $\lambda$ is $p$-regular (no part is repeated $p$ times), and $\{D^\lambda : \lambda \vdash n,\, p\text{-regular}\}$ are exactly the irreducible $kS_n$-modules.*

**Standard basis (Specht-James-Kerber).** The polytabloids $\{e_T : T \text{ standard}\}$ — those with $T$ a *standard Young tableau*, increasing along rows and down columns — form a $\mathbb{Z}$-basis of $S^\lambda$ (over any base ring including $\mathbb{Z}$). Hence

$$
\dim_k S^\lambda = f^\lambda = \frac{n!}{\prod_{(i,j) \in [\lambda]} h(i, j)},
$$

independently of the field $k$, recovering the hook length formula from [07.05.02].

**Counterexamples to common slips.**

- $S^\lambda$ is *not* a permutation module — the polytabloids are signed sums of tabloids, not single tabloids.
- $D^\lambda$ for $p$-regular $\lambda$ is not the same as $S^\lambda$ — the former is the irreducible head, the latter is generally reducible in modular characteristic.
- $p$-regularity means *no part repeated $p$ times*; $\lambda = (3, 3, 3)$ is *not* $3$-regular even though $|\lambda| = 9$ has no special divisibility issue.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (James submodule theorem, 1976).** *Let $U \subseteq M^\lambda$ be a $kS_n$-submodule. Then either $S^\lambda \subseteq U$ or $U \subseteq (S^\lambda)^\perp$, where the orthogonal is taken with respect to the natural symmetric bilinear form $\langle \{T\}, \{T'\}\rangle = \delta_{\{T\}, \{T'\}}$ on $M^\lambda$.*

This dichotomy is the structural backbone of modular representation theory of $S_n$: it forces the radical of the Specht module to be $S^\lambda \cap (S^\lambda)^\perp$, identifying $D^\lambda = S^\lambda / (S^\lambda \cap (S^\lambda)^\perp)$ as the irreducible head.

**Proof sketch.**

*Step 1 (Garnir relations).* For a tableau $T$ and a pair of subsets $X \subseteq \mathrm{column}(T, j), Y \subseteq \mathrm{column}(T, j+1)$ with $|X| + |Y| > $ length of column $j$, the *Garnir element* $G_{X, Y} := \sum_{\sigma} \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) \sigma$ (sum over coset representatives of $S_X \times S_Y$ in $S_{X \cup Y}$) annihilates $\{T\}$ in $M^\lambda$. The Garnir relations express how column antisymmetrisation interacts with row contents.

*Step 2 (Standard basis).* Using Garnir relations, every polytabloid $e_T$ can be reduced to a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of *standard polytabloids* $e_{T_0}$ for $T_0$ a standard Young tableau. So $\{e_T : T \text{ standard}\} = \{e_T : T \in \mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)\}$ spans $S^\lambda$, and a careful count (or an inductive dimension argument) shows it is a basis.

*Step 3 (Submodule dichotomy).* Suppose $U \subseteq M^\lambda$ is a submodule and $u \in U$ is non-zero. Express $u = \sum_T c_T \{T\}$. Apply the *signed column sum* operator $\kappa_T = \sum_{\sigma \in C(T)} \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) \sigma$ to $u$. By a calculation involving Garnir relations and the row-stabiliser action,

$$
\kappa_T \cdot \{T'\} = \begin{cases} \pm e_T & \text{if } \{T'\} = \{T\} \\ 0 \text{ or torsion} & \text{otherwise (depends on characteristic).} \end{cases}
$$

If for some $T$ the coefficient $c_T$ in $u$ is non-zero, then $\kappa_T \cdot u$ is a non-zero scalar multiple of $e_T$. So $e_T \in U$ for some $T$, and by $S_n$-equivariance, $S^\lambda = \langle e_T : T \rangle \subseteq U$. Otherwise, $u \in (S^\lambda)^\perp$ for the natural pairing. Hence the dichotomy: $S^\lambda \subseteq U$ or $U \subseteq (S^\lambda)^\perp$. $\square$

The proof in characteristic $0$ follows directly: the symmetric bilinear form is non-degenerate (since $|S_n|$ is invertible), so $S^\lambda \cap (S^\lambda)^\perp = 0$ and $S^\lambda$ is irreducible. In characteristic $p$ dividing $n!$, the form may degenerate on $S^\lambda$, and the radical $S^\lambda \cap (S^\lambda)^\perp$ produces $D^\lambda = S^\lambda / \mathrm{rad}$ as the irreducible head. **Bridge.** The construction here builds toward later units of the strand, where the same pattern is taken up at higher structure. The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

For $\lambda = (n)$ (single row) and $\lambda = (1^n)$ (single column), identify the Specht modules $S^{(n)}$ and $S^{(1^n)}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For a single row, the column stabiliser is the identity subgroup; for a single column, all of $S_n$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\lambda = (n)$ (single row): the column stabiliser of any tableau is the identity subgroup (each column has just one cell). The polytabloid $e_T = \{T\}$, the single tabloid (since all tableaux of shape $(n)$ are row-equivalent). $S^{(n)}$ is $1$-dimensional, with $S_n$ acting as the identity scalar: this is the *unit representation*.

$\lambda = (1^n)$ (single column): the column stabiliser is all of $S_n$. The polytabloid $e_T = \sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) \cdot \{\sigma T\}$. Since each tableau of shape $(1^n)$ is its own equivalence class (rows are length $1$, so row equivalence is the identity relation), the polytabloid is the *signed sum of all $n!$ orderings*. $S^{(1^n)}$ is $1$-dimensional, with $S_n$ acting by the sign character: this is the *sign representation*.

In both cases the dimension matches $f^\lambda = 1$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the standard polytabloids $\{e_T : T \in \mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)\}$ are linearly independent in $M^\lambda$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Define a partial order on standard tableaux and look at the leading term.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define the *last-letter order* on standard tableaux: $T < T'$ if for some $k$, the cell containing $k$ in $T$ lies in a strictly earlier column than the cell containing $k$ in $T'$, and they agree on cells $\{1, \ldots, k-1\}$. This is a total order.

For $T \in \mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)$, expand $e_T = \sum_{\sigma \in C(T)} \mathrm{sgn}(\sigma) \{\sigma T\}$. The "leading tabloid" is $\{T\}$ itself (with coefficient $+1$). Crucially, no $T' \in \mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)$ with $T' > T$ appears in the expansion of $e_T$: any tabloid in the expansion comes from column-permuting $T$, which can only move entries within columns and cannot make a previously-standard $T$ into a different standard $T' > T$ in last-letter order.

So the matrix expressing $\{e_T : T \in \mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)\}$ in terms of the tabloid basis $\{\{T\}\}$ is upper triangular with $1$'s on the diagonal (with respect to last-letter order). Hence the polytabloids are linearly independent. $\square$

This argument shows $|\mathrm{SYT}(\lambda)| \leq \dim S^\lambda$. Combined with the Garnir-relations argument that standard polytabloids span, equality holds and $\dim S^\lambda = f^\lambda$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why does Maschke's theorem fail for $kS_n$ when $\mathrm{char}(k) = p$ divides $n!$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Maschke's averaging argument requires dividing by $|G|$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Maschke's theorem says: for a finite group $G$ and a field $k$ with $\mathrm{char}(k) \nmid |G|$, every $kG$-module is semisimple (every short exact sequence splits). The proof averages a projection over $G$ and divides by $|G|$:

$$
\pi_G := \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} g \cdot \pi \cdot g^{-1}.
$$

When $\mathrm{char}(k) \mid |G|$, the scalar $1/|G|$ is undefined (zero in $k$), and the averaging argument fails. For $G = S_n$, $|G| = n!$, so any prime $p \leq n$ divides $|S_n|$ and Maschke's theorem fails over $\bar{\mathbb{F}}_p$.

**Concrete failure.** Over $\bar{\mathbb{F}}_2$, the natural permutation representation of $S_2 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ on $\bar{\mathbb{F}}_2^2$ has the diagonal subspace $\langle (1, 1) \rangle$ as a submodule, but no complementary $S_2$-stable line: the swap $\sigma$ has matrix $\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}$, which over $\bar{\mathbb{F}}_2$ is upper-triangularisable but not diagonalisable. So the representation is *indecomposable but not irreducible* — it has a unique simple submodule and a unique simple quotient, both $1$-dimensional.

This is exactly the kind of phenomenon Specht's construction captures: $S^{(2)}$ over $\bar{\mathbb{F}}_2$ is indecomposable, and its head $D^{(2)}$ is the unique simple module.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

State the *branching rule* for Specht modules: $\mathrm{Res}^{S_n}_{S_{n-1}} S^\lambda$ decomposes as a direct sum of Specht modules over removable corners of $\lambda$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Removing a cell from $\lambda$ gives a partition of $n - 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Branching rule (over characteristic $0$).** Let $\lambda \vdash n$. A *removable corner* of $\lambda$ is a cell $(i, \lambda_i)$ such that $\lambda - e_i$ is still a partition (i.e., the cell can be removed without violating $\lambda_i \geq \lambda_{i+1}$). Then

$$
\mathrm{Res}^{S_n}_{S_{n-1}} S^\lambda \cong \bigoplus_{c \text{ removable}} S^{\lambda - c},
$$

where the sum is over removable corners $c$ of $\lambda$.

**Proof sketch.** In each standard tableau $T$ of shape $\lambda$, the entry $n$ must occupy a removable corner (it is the largest entry, and it must be at the end of its row and column). Group standard tableaux of shape $\lambda$ by which removable corner contains $n$; removing $n$ from $T$ gives a standard tableau of shape $\lambda \setminus c$. So the polytabloid basis decomposes as a direct sum over removable corners, matching the claimed direct sum decomposition.

**Dual: induction.** $\mathrm{Ind}^{S_{n+1}}_{S_n} S^\lambda \cong \bigoplus_{c \text{ addable}} S^{\lambda + c}$, where the sum is over addable corners.

**Significance.** The branching rule combined with induction gives the *Young's lattice* — a graded poset of partitions with edges $\lambda \to \lambda + c$ — and the symmetric-group representation theory becomes entirely combinatorial on this lattice. The branching rule is also the seed of the theory of *crystal graphs* in higher rank: the branching of $\mathrm{GL}_d$-irreducibles to $\mathrm{GL}_{d-1}$ is identical in form, with crystal operators encoding the branching combinatorially. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

State the *Mullineux conjecture* (proved by Ford-Kleshchev 1997): describe the action of tensoring $D^\lambda$ with the sign character in characteristic $p$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

In characteristic $0$, $V^\lambda \otimes \mathrm{sgn} = V^{\lambda'}$. The modular case is more subtle.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Mullineux involution / conjecture (Mullineux 1979; proved Ford-Kleshchev 1997, Bessenrodt-Olsson 1998).** In characteristic $p$, define an involution $m_p$ on $p$-regular partitions of $n$ — the *Mullineux involution* — by an explicit combinatorial procedure on the *$p$-rim* of the Young diagram. Then

$$
D^\lambda \otimes \mathrm{sgn} \cong D^{m_p(\lambda)}.
$$

**Combinatorial recipe.** The $p$-rim of $\lambda$ is the set of cells $(i, j)$ such that $(i+1, j+1) \notin \lambda$, taken from the bottom-right and broken into pieces of size $\leq p$ at corners. Removing the $p$-rim, then removing the $p$-rim of the result, etc., gives a sequence of strips. The Mullineux image $m_p(\lambda)$ is constructed by re-attaching these strips with adjusted lengths according to the $p$-rim sizes.

**Comparison with characteristic $0$.** In characteristic $0$: $V^\lambda \otimes \mathrm{sgn} \cong V^{\lambda'}$ (transpose). The Mullineux involution in characteristic $p$ replaces transposition: $m_p \neq (-)' $ in general, though the two coincide for various special families (e.g., $p$-cores).

**Significance.** Mullineux's conjecture connects the classification of irreducible modular representations to the combinatorics of the $p$-rim. It is the modular analogue of the elementary characteristic-$0$ identity $V^\lambda \otimes \mathrm{sgn} = V^{\lambda'}$, and its proof (after eighteen years as a conjecture) used the *Lascoux-Leclerc-Thibon* (LLT) algorithm and *quantum group* methods at $q = -1$. Mullineux's original 1979 algorithmic description was equivalent to but not manifestly identical to the rim-removal recipe; reconciling the two was part of Ford-Kleshchev's proof.

The Mullineux conjecture is one of the central results in modular representation theory of $S_n$, and its proof methods (LLT, Hecke algebras at roots of unity, $\mathfrak{sl}_p$-crystal structure on $p$-regular partitions) underlie much of contemporary research in the field.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Decomposition matrices and the Carter conjecture.** For the $p$-modular representation theory of $S_n$, the *decomposition matrix* $D = (d_{\lambda \mu})$ records the multiplicities $[S^\lambda : D^\mu]$ of irreducible composition factors of Specht modules. Key facts:

- $d_{\lambda \mu} = 0$ unless $\lambda \trianglelefteq \mu$ in dominance order ($\mu$ dominates $\lambda$); $d_{\lambda \lambda} = 1$ for $p$-regular $\lambda$.
- The $p$-modular characters of $S_n$ are determined by the decomposition matrix, but computing it for general $n$ and $p$ remains an open problem.
- *James's conjecture* (1990) predicted the decomposition numbers in terms of *adjustment matrices* and the LLT algorithm; resolved positively for $p \geq h(\lambda) \cdot e$ where $e$ is the quantum characteristic.

**Hecke algebras and $q$-Schur algebras.** The *Iwahori-Hecke algebra* $H_n(q)$ deforms $\mathbb{C}[S_n]$ via parameter $q$; the *$q$-Schur algebra* $S_q(n, r)$ generalises Schur-Weyl duality to the $q$-deformed setting. At roots of unity, $H_n(q)$ behaves analogously to $\mathbb{F}_p[S_n]$ in modular representation theory, and the *(quantum) Specht modules* over $H_n(q)$ provide the analogous building blocks. The *LLT algorithm* (Lascoux-Leclerc-Thibon 1996) computes decomposition numbers of Hecke algebras at roots of unity via crystals of affine $\mathfrak{sl}_e$.

**Categorification.** The categorical structures of Khovanov-Lauda-Rouquier (KLR algebras / quiver Hecke algebras, mid-2000s) categorify the Hecke algebra and reveal the modular representation theory of $S_n$ as a piece of an *affine $\mathfrak{sl}_p$-action* on the direct sum $\bigoplus_n \mathrm{Rep}(S_n)$. This affine Lie-theoretic structure had been conjectured by Lascoux-Leclerc-Thibon and Ariki and was made categorical by Khovanov-Lauda and Rouquier.

**Schur-Weyl duality in characteristic $p$.** The classical Schur-Weyl duality $V^{\otimes n} \cong \bigoplus_\lambda S^\lambda V \otimes V^\lambda$ extends to characteristic $p$ but with subtleties: the Schur algebra $S(n, r) := \mathrm{End}_{S_r}(V^{\otimes r})$ is not always semisimple, the polynomial $\mathrm{GL}$-representations and the $S_n$-Specht modules form a *highest-weight category* with quasi-hereditary structure (Cline-Parshall-Scott 1988), and the decomposition matrix factors through both rep theories.

**Cellular algebras (Graham-Lehrer 1996).** The group algebra $kS_n$ is a *cellular algebra* with cell modules the Specht modules. This abstract framework subsumes Specht modules, Brauer algebras, and Hecke algebras, and provides a uniform framework for the structure of cell modules: each cell module has a natural bilinear form whose radical is the largest proper submodule, and the simple heads exhaust the irreducibles.

**Plancherel measure and asymptotics.** For random $\lambda \vdash n$ chosen with probability $(f^\lambda)^2 / n!$ (Plancherel measure), the rescaled diagram converges to the Vershik-Kerov-Logan-Shepp limit shape; the longest row scales as $\sqrt{n}$ with Tracy-Widom fluctuations (Baik-Deift-Johansson 1999). These deep probabilistic results connect symmetric-group rep theory to integrable probability and random matrix theory.

**Synthesis.** This construction generalises the pattern fixed in [07.05.01] (symmetric group representation), with the symmetric data replaced by its skew or twisted analogue. Read in the opposite direction, the construction is dual to the metric story: complements and orthogonality are taken with respect to the bilinear datum of this unit, not a metric, and the resulting category of subobjects is the one the rest of the strand classifies. The central insight is that this datum identifies algebra with geometry: functions become vector fields, subspaces become quotients, and invariants become cohomology classes — and that identification is the engine driving every theorem downstream.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Wilhelm Specht (1907-1980) was a German mathematician who completed his doctorate in Leipzig in 1932 under Heinrich Brandt, then habilitated in Königsberg in 1937. His 1935 paper *Die irreduziblen Darstellungen der symmetrischen Gruppe*, published in *Mathematische Zeitschrift* 39 (1935) 696-711, gave the polytabloid construction that now bears his name. The motivation was concrete: Specht wanted a construction of the irreducible representations of $S_n$ that did not depend on choosing a base field where Maschke's theorem applies. Young's 1900-1933 symmetriser construction worked over $\mathbb{C}$ but the proofs of irreducibility relied on full reducibility. Frobenius's 1900 character calculation gave the dimensions and characters but not an explicit module. Specht's polytabloid antisymmetrisation produced an actual module, defined over any base ring, whose irreducibility over $\mathbb{C}$ could then be verified directly via the bilinear form.

The deeper significance — that Specht modules are well-defined over any field, even when the resulting irreducibility theorem fails — was not fully appreciated until *modular representation theory* of finite groups developed in the 1940s-1960s through the work of Brauer (decomposition theory) and others. The systematic application of Specht modules to modular representation theory of $S_n$ began with Gordon James's 1976 *Cambridge Tracts* article and his definitive 1978 monograph in the Springer Lecture Notes series (LNM 682), which proved the submodule theorem, the standard basis theorem, and the classification of modular irreducibles via $p$-regular partitions and the heads $D^\lambda$. James-Kerber's two-volume *Encyclopedia* article (1981) consolidated the field.

Subsequent developments connected Specht modules to deep structures elsewhere in mathematics. The Lascoux-Leclerc-Thibon algorithm (1996) revealed an affine Lie-theoretic structure on the modular characters, with the decomposition numbers of $S_n$ over $\bar{\mathbb{F}}_p$ controlled by the canonical basis of the basic representation of $\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}_p}$. The Mullineux conjecture (Mullineux 1979, proved Ford-Kleshchev 1997 and Bessenrodt-Olsson 1998) gave the modular analogue of $V^\lambda \otimes \mathrm{sgn} \cong V^{\lambda'}$. The categorification programme of Khovanov-Lauda (mid-2000s) and Rouquier elevated the Hecke algebra and quantum group structure to a fully categorical level, with Specht modules appearing as classes in a $2$-categorical action.

The Specht module construction is now the standard starting point for modular representation theory of $S_n$, with five major monographs (Sagan, James, James-Kerber, Mathas, Klesh­chev) covering the field at increasing levels of depth. Specht's original 1935 paper, only sixteen pages long and written in clear mathematical prose, remains a remarkable example of how a single careful construction — an antisymmetrised sum over a column stabiliser — can launch an entire research field that continues to develop ninety years later.
