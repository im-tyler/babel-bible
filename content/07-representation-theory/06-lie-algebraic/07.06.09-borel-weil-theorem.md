## Intuition [Beginner]

Borel-Weil theorem is part of the dictionary that turns symmetry into linear algebra. Instead of only watching a group or Lie algebra move points, a representation lets it move vectors, matrices, functions, and sections. The payoff is that complicated symmetry can be studied through invariant subspaces, characters, weights, and diagrams.

A good picture is a machine with a control panel. Each symmetry operation presses a button, and the representation tells the vector space how to respond. The concept matters because many classification theorems become finite calculations once the right representation data is chosen.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Start with rotations of the plane by 0 degrees and 180 degrees. Acting on the vector (1,0), they produce (1,0) and (-1,0). Acting on the vertical vector (0,1), they produce (0,1) and (0,-1). This two-dimensional action is a small representation.

For a concrete count, the two rotations give two matrices, and multiplying either matrix by itself returns the identity matrix. What this tells us: representation theory replaces symmetry moves by matrices while preserving the multiplication or bracket rules.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The Borel-Weil theorem realizes irreducible representations of a complex semisimple group as holomorphic sections of line bundles over the flag variety G/B. [ref: TODO_REF Knapp Ch VI; Tu Differential Geometry; Wallach]

The object is considered up to the natural equivalence relation in its category: biholomorphic change of coordinate for complex-analytic objects, isomorphism of bundles or divisors for geometric objects, and intertwining linear isomorphism for representations. This convention keeps formulas invariant under the allowed changes of local description.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem.** For a dominant integral weight lambda, the dual of the space of holomorphic sections of the associated line bundle on G/B is the irreducible representation of highest weight lambda.

**Proof.** The group acts on sections by translation, so the section space is a representation. The fiber over the base flag supplies a B-eigenline of weight lambda, giving a highest-weight vector in the dual. Holomorphicity and compactness make the section space finite-dimensional. The highest-weight construction gives a nonzero map from the irreducible module, and the Borel-Weil separation argument shows no proper quotient remains. [ref: TODO_REF Knapp Ch VI; Tu Differential Geometry; Wallach]

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward later units of the strand, where the same pattern is taken up at higher structure. The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

## Advanced results [Master]

The mature form of borel-weil theorem is functorial. Morphisms preserve the defining local data, and the invariants attached to the object descend to the relevant quotient category. In the complex-analytic strand this means divisors, periods, line bundles, and extension phenomena behave under holomorphic maps of Riemann surfaces. In the representation-theoretic strand this means weights, characters, enveloping algebras, and invariant measures behave under homomorphisms and restriction.

A second result is the comparison with the adjacent algebraic or analytic model. For Riemann surfaces, meromorphic data can often be read as line-bundle or divisor data; for representation theory, infinitesimal data in a Lie algebra often integrates to compact or complex group data under appropriate hypotheses. These comparison theorems are the reason the unit is placed as supporting material rather than isolated terminology. [ref: TODO_REF Knapp Ch VI; Tu Differential Geometry; Wallach]

**Synthesis.** The Borel-Weil theorem identifies irreducible representations with global sections of line bundles on flag varieties, forging the foundational bridge between algebraic representation theory and complex geometry. This pattern — representations recovered from cohomology of geometric objects — recurs throughout modern mathematics: the Borel-Weil-Bott extension uses higher cohomology, the Beilinson-Bernstein localisation replaces sections with D-modules, and the geometric Langlands programme elevates the correspondence to a categorical equivalence between Hecke eigensheaves and Galois representations. The flag variety G/B itself is a prototype of a homogeneous space whose topology encodes representation-theoretic data: its Bruhat decomposition reflects the Weyl group, its Picard group is the weight lattice, and its line bundles are indexed by characters of the maximal torus.
