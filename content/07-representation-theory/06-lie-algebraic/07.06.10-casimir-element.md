## Intuition [Beginner]

The Casimir element is one symbol, built out of a Lie algebra and a chosen bilinear form, that commutes with every element of the Lie algebra. On any irreducible representation it acts by multiplication by a single scalar. The scalar is a fingerprint: distinct irreducible representations get distinct scalars in every case that matters, and so the Casimir's eigenvalue identifies the irreducible.

Why is one such element enough to do real work? Because once you have a central operator with distinguishable eigenvalues on the pieces of a representation, you can separate the pieces. If a representation $V$ has a subrepresentation $W$, you can sort vectors of $V$ by which Casimir eigenvalue they carry. Vectors with one eigenvalue land in $W$, vectors with another eigenvalue land in a complementary subspace, and the two add up to all of $V$. That is the engine behind Hermann Weyl's theorem that every finite-dimensional representation of a semisimple complex Lie algebra splits as a direct sum of irreducibles.

The Casimir was introduced by Hendrik Casimir in his 1931 Leiden thesis, and the algebraic proof of complete reducibility built around it is due to Casimir and van der Waerden in 1935. The intuition is the same one that underlies the spectral theorem for symmetric operators: a single well-chosen operator, when it has enough eigenvalues, decomposes the space.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute the Casimir scalar on the irreducible representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_2 \mathbb{C}$ and check that the values match the spin-$n/2$ tower.

Step 1. The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{sl}_2 \mathbb{C}$ has basis $H, E, F$ with the standard relations. With the Killing form, a careful book-keeping gives the Casimir element $C = E F + F E + \tfrac{1}{2} H^2$ inside the universal enveloping algebra.

Step 2. The irreducible representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_2 \mathbb{C}$ are the spaces $V_n = \mathrm{Sym}^n \mathbb{C}^2$ of dimension $n + 1$, indexed by a non-negative integer $n$. The vector $v_n$ at the top of the ladder satisfies $E v_n = 0$ and $H v_n = n \cdot v_n$.

Step 3. Evaluate the Casimir on $v_n$. The first term gives $E F v_n = (F E + H) v_n = 0 + n \cdot v_n = n v_n$. The second term gives $F E v_n = 0$. The third term gives $\tfrac{1}{2} H^2 v_n = \tfrac{1}{2} n^2 \cdot v_n$. Adding: $C v_n = (n + \tfrac{1}{2} n^2) v_n = \tfrac{n(n+2)}{2} v_n$.

Step 4. By Schur's lemma, the same scalar acts on every vector of $V_n$. So the Casimir eigenvalue on $V_n$ is $\tfrac{n(n+2)}{2}$. Distinct values of $n$ give distinct eigenvalues, since $x \mapsto x(x+2)/2$ is strictly increasing on $[0, \infty)$.

Step 5. Sanity check on small cases. For $n = 0$ (the one-dimensional identity representation), the Casimir scalar is $0$. For $n = 1$ (the standard two-dimensional representation), the scalar is $3/2$. For $n = 2$ (the adjoint representation), the scalar is $4$.

What this tells us: a single number, the Casimir eigenvalue, separates every irreducible representation of $\mathfrak{sl}_2 \mathbb{C}$ from every other one. The same mechanism extends to higher rank: the Casimir scalar on the irreducible $V_\lambda$ of any semisimple Lie algebra is $\langle \lambda, \lambda + 2 \rho \rangle$, and the function $\lambda \mapsto \langle \lambda, \lambda + 2 \rho \rangle$ is one-to-one on the dominant integral weights. See [07.06.11] for the full $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ story.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\mathfrak{g}$ be a finite-dimensional semisimple complex Lie algebra. The **Killing form** $B : \mathfrak{g} \times \mathfrak{g} \to \mathbb{C}$ is the bilinear form $B(X, Y) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}_X \circ \mathrm{ad}_Y)$, where $\mathrm{ad}_X : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{g}$ is the adjoint action $\mathrm{ad}_X(Z) = [X, Z]$. By Cartan's criterion, $B$ is non-degenerate on $\mathfrak{g}$ when $\mathfrak{g}$ is semisimple.

Choose a basis $\{X_1, \ldots, X_n\}$ of $\mathfrak{g}$. The non-degeneracy of $B$ gives a unique dual basis $\{X^1, \ldots, X^n\}$ characterised by
$$
B(X_a, X^b) = \delta_a^b \qquad (1 \le a, b \le n).
$$

**Definition (Casimir element).** The **Casimir element** of $\mathfrak{g}$ (with respect to the Killing form) is
$$
C := \sum_{a = 1}^n X_a X^a \in U(\mathfrak{g}),
$$
where $U(\mathfrak{g})$ is the universal enveloping algebra of $\mathfrak{g}$ and the products $X_a X^a$ are taken in $U(\mathfrak{g})$. More generally, for any finite-dimensional representation $\rho : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{gl}(V)$, one may replace $B$ by the trace form $B_\rho(X, Y) = \mathrm{tr}(\rho(X) \rho(Y))$ when it is non-degenerate, producing a representation-specific Casimir $C_\rho$; the Killing-form choice corresponds to $\rho = \mathrm{ad}$.

The Casimir is the prototype of a **quadratic central element** in $U(\mathfrak{g})$: it is degree two in the generators (one factor of $X_a$ and one of $X^a$), and it lies in the centre $Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$. The non-degeneracy of $B$ is required so that the dual basis exists.

### Invariance under change of basis

A change of basis $X_a = \sum_b M_{ab} Y_b$ (with $M \in \mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{C})$) induces a dual change on the dual basis. Writing $B(X_a, X^b) = \delta_a^b$ and $X^a = \sum_c N_{ac} Y^c$, the condition $B(X_a, X^b) = \delta_a^b$ rewrites as $\sum_{c, d} M_{ad} N_{bc} B(Y_d, Y^c) = \sum_{c, d} M_{ad} N_{bc} \delta_d^c = \sum_c M_{ac} N_{bc} = \delta_a^b$. So $M N^T = I$, i.e., $N = M^{-T}$. Then
$$
\sum_a X_a X^a = \sum_a \big( \sum_b M_{ab} Y_b \big) \big( \sum_c (M^{-T})_{ac} Y^c \big) = \sum_{b, c} \big( \sum_a M_{ab} (M^{-T})_{ac} \big) Y_b Y^c = \sum_{b, c} \delta_b^c Y_b Y^c = \sum_b Y_b Y^b.
$$
So $C$ depends only on $B$, not on the chosen basis.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The non-degeneracy of the bilinear form is required for the dual basis to exist. Over an abelian Lie algebra $\mathfrak{a}$, the Killing form vanishes identically (every $\mathrm{ad}_X$ is zero, so its trace product vanishes), and the "Killing-form Casimir" is undefined. One must use a different non-degenerate invariant form on an abelian or solvable Lie algebra, or work with central elements other than the quadratic Casimir.
- The Casimir is not unique among central elements of $U(\mathfrak{g})$: in rank $r$, the centre $Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$ is a polynomial algebra in $r$ generators (Harish-Chandra), of which the quadratic Casimir is one. The higher Casimirs are degree-$d$ central elements built from invariant symmetric $d$-tensors on $\mathfrak{g}^*$. For $\mathfrak{sl}_n$ there are $n - 1$ independent Casimirs of degrees $2, 3, \ldots, n$.
- The Casimir of a non-semisimple Lie algebra equipped with a chosen non-degenerate invariant form (e.g., a Lie algebra with non-degenerate trace form) still gives a central element, but the complete-reducibility application fails: solvable Lie algebras have indecomposable non-irreducible finite-dimensional representations (e.g., the Borel subalgebra acting on $\mathbb{C}^2$ via upper-triangular matrices).

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Centrality of the Casimir; Humphreys §6.2 Lemma).** *Let $\mathfrak{g}$ be a semisimple complex Lie algebra with non-degenerate invariant bilinear form $B$, basis $\{X_a\}$, and $B$-dual basis $\{X^a\}$. The element $C = \sum_a X_a X^a \in U(\mathfrak{g})$ lies in the centre $Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$: $[Y, C] = 0$ in $U(\mathfrak{g})$ for every $Y \in \mathfrak{g}$.*

**Proof.** Fix $Y \in \mathfrak{g}$. Expand $[Y, X_a]$ and $[Y, X^a]$ in the chosen bases:
$$
[Y, X_a] = \sum_b c_{a}^{b} X_b, \qquad [Y, X^a] = \sum_b d^{a}_{b} X^b,
$$
where $c_a^b, d^a_b \in \mathbb{C}$ are the structure constants of the adjoint action of $Y$ on the two bases.

The invariance of $B$ under $\mathrm{ad}$, namely
$$
B(\mathrm{ad}_Y X, Z) + B(X, \mathrm{ad}_Y Z) = 0 \quad \text{for all } X, Z \in \mathfrak{g},
$$
applied to $X = X_a$ and $Z = X^c$, gives
$$
B([Y, X_a], X^c) + B(X_a, [Y, X^c]) = 0.
$$
The first term equals $\sum_b c_a^b B(X_b, X^c) = c_a^c$. The second equals $\sum_b d^c_b B(X_a, X^b) = d^c_a$. So $c_a^c + d^c_a = 0$, i.e., **$d^c_a = -c_a^c$** for every $a, c$.

Now compute $[Y, C]$ in $U(\mathfrak{g})$ using the Leibniz-style identity $[Y, X_a X^a] = [Y, X_a] X^a + X_a [Y, X^a]$:
$$
[Y, C] = \sum_a [Y, X_a] X^a + \sum_a X_a [Y, X^a] = \sum_{a, b} c_a^b X_b X^a + \sum_{a, b} d^a_b X_a X^b.
$$

Relabel the dummy indices in the second sum (swap $a \leftrightarrow b$) so both sums have the same generator pair $X_b X^a$ (in the first) and $X_b X^a$ (in the second, after the swap, with coefficient $d^b_a$):
$$
[Y, C] = \sum_{a, b} c_a^b X_b X^a + \sum_{a, b} d^b_a X_b X^a = \sum_{a, b} (c_a^b + d^b_a) X_b X^a.
$$

Using $d^b_a = -c_a^b$ from the invariance identity above:
$$
[Y, C] = \sum_{a, b} (c_a^b - c_a^b) X_b X^a = 0.
$$

Since $\mathfrak{g}$ generates $U(\mathfrak{g})$ and $[Y, C] = 0$ for every $Y \in \mathfrak{g}$, the element $C$ commutes with every element of $U(\mathfrak{g})$. So $C \in Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$. $\square$

**Corollary (Schur scalar).** *Let $\rho : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{gl}(V)$ be a finite-dimensional irreducible representation of $\mathfrak{g}$. Then $\rho(C) = c_V \cdot \mathrm{id}_V$ for a unique scalar $c_V \in \mathbb{C}$.*

**Proof.** Centrality of $C$ in $U(\mathfrak{g})$ implies that $\rho(C)$ commutes with $\rho(Y)$ for every $Y \in \mathfrak{g}$, hence with the entire image of the algebra-extended representation $U(\mathfrak{g}) \to \mathrm{End}(V)$. By Schur's lemma, any endomorphism of a finite-dimensional irreducible representation over $\mathbb{C}$ that commutes with the action is a scalar. So $\rho(C) = c_V \cdot \mathrm{id}_V$. $\square$

**Corollary (Highest-weight eigenvalue).** *Let $\mathfrak{g}$ be a semisimple complex Lie algebra with Cartan subalgebra $\mathfrak{h}$, root system $\Phi$, positive roots $\Phi^+$, and half-sum of positive roots $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} \alpha$. Let $V_\lambda$ be the finite-dimensional irreducible representation of highest weight $\lambda \in \mathfrak{h}^*$. Then the Casimir acts on $V_\lambda$ by the scalar*
$$
c_{V_\lambda} = \langle \lambda, \lambda + 2 \rho \rangle,
$$
*where $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ is the Killing form on $\mathfrak{h}^*$ (induced from $B$ via the isomorphism $\mathfrak{h} \cong \mathfrak{h}^*$).*

**Proof.** Choose a basis for $\mathfrak{g}$ adapted to its root-space decomposition $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{h} \oplus \bigoplus_{\alpha \in \Phi} \mathfrak{g}_\alpha$. Pick a basis $\{H_i\}$ of $\mathfrak{h}$ with dual basis $\{H^i\}$ under $B$, and root vectors $E_\alpha \in \mathfrak{g}_\alpha$ for each $\alpha \in \Phi$, normalised so that $B(E_\alpha, E_{-\alpha}) = 1$. Then the dual basis to $\{H_i, E_\alpha\}$ under $B$ is $\{H^i, E_{-\alpha}\}$. So
$$
C = \sum_i H_i H^i + \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi} E_\alpha E_{-\alpha}.
$$
Split the second sum into positive and negative roots:
$$
C = \sum_i H_i H^i + \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} (E_\alpha E_{-\alpha} + E_{-\alpha} E_\alpha).
$$
For each positive root $\alpha$, use the commutator $[E_\alpha, E_{-\alpha}] = H_\alpha$ (where $H_\alpha$ is the coroot, satisfying $B(H_\alpha, H) = \alpha(H)$ for $H \in \mathfrak{h}$) to write $E_\alpha E_{-\alpha} = E_{-\alpha} E_\alpha + H_\alpha$. Then
$$
C = \sum_i H_i H^i + \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} (2 E_{-\alpha} E_\alpha + H_\alpha).
$$
Let $v_\lambda \in V_\lambda$ be the highest-weight vector: $E_\alpha v_\lambda = 0$ for every $\alpha \in \Phi^+$, and $H v_\lambda = \lambda(H) v_\lambda$ for every $H \in \mathfrak{h}$. Evaluating each piece on $v_\lambda$:

- $\sum_i H_i H^i \cdot v_\lambda = \sum_i \lambda(H_i) \lambda(H^i) v_\lambda = \langle \lambda, \lambda \rangle v_\lambda$, where the last equality uses the dual-basis identity $\sum_i \lambda(H_i) \lambda(H^i) = \langle \lambda, \lambda \rangle$.
- $2 E_{-\alpha} E_\alpha v_\lambda = 0$ since $E_\alpha v_\lambda = 0$.
- $\sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} H_\alpha v_\lambda = \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} \lambda(H_\alpha) v_\lambda = \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} \langle \lambda, \alpha \rangle v_\lambda = \langle \lambda, 2 \rho \rangle v_\lambda$.

Adding: $C v_\lambda = (\langle \lambda, \lambda \rangle + \langle \lambda, 2 \rho \rangle) v_\lambda = \langle \lambda, \lambda + 2 \rho \rangle v_\lambda$.

By the Schur-scalar corollary, the same scalar acts on every vector of $V_\lambda$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Casimir element builds toward Weyl's complete-reducibility theorem and toward the Harish-Chandra theory of the centre of the universal enveloping algebra, where the same dual-basis construction appears again in [07.06.11] (the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ Casimir $C = EF + FE + \tfrac{1}{2} H^2$) and again in [07.06.12] (the $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ quadratic Casimir on $V_{1,1}$). The foundational reason centrality holds is exactly the invariance identity $B(\mathrm{ad}_Y X, Z) + B(X, \mathrm{ad}_Y Z) = 0$: it forces $d^c_a + c_a^c = 0$ between the structure constants of $\mathrm{ad}_Y$ on the two dual bases, and this pairwise cancellation is what zeroes the commutator $[Y, C]$. The central insight is that on a finite-dimensional irreducible $V_\lambda$ the Casimir acts as the scalar $\langle \lambda, \lambda + 2\rho \rangle$, and the function $\lambda \mapsto \langle \lambda, \lambda + 2\rho \rangle$ is one-to-one on the dominant integral weights, so the Casimir eigenvalue identifies the irreducible. This is exactly the structural input to the Casimir-based proof of complete reducibility: distinct submodules carry distinct Casimir eigenvalues, and the spectral projection onto each eigenspace gives an $\mathfrak{g}$-invariant splitting. The bridge is the recognition that one quadratic central element does the work of a whole spectral decomposition. Putting these together generalises in two directions: the higher Casimirs of $U(\mathfrak{g})$ furnish $\mathrm{rk}(\mathfrak{g})$ commuting central operators whose joint eigenvalues identify $V_\lambda$ uniquely, and the Harish-Chandra isomorphism $Z(U(\mathfrak{g})) \cong S(\mathfrak{h})^W$ identifies the centre of the enveloping algebra with the Weyl-invariant polynomials on the Cartan dual.

The Gram matrix above gives $\langle \lambda_1 + \lambda_2, \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 \rangle = 2/3 + 2/3 + 2 \cdot 1/3 = 2$. So $c = 3 \cdot 2 = 6$, the Casimir eigenvalue on the adjoint $V_{1,1}$, recorded in [07.06.12]. Rubric: full credit for $6$.

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

State and prove Weyl's complete-reducibility theorem for a semisimple complex Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$: every finite-dimensional representation of $\mathfrak{g}$ is a direct sum of irreducible representations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reduce to showing that every short exact sequence $0 \to W \to V \to V/W \to 0$ of $\mathfrak{g}$-modules with $W$ irreducible splits. Use the Casimir to construct the splitting when the Casimir eigenvalues on $W$ and on $V/W$ differ; handle the equal-eigenvalue case by induction on $\dim W + \dim V/W$ and a cohomological vanishing argument.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

It is enough to show: every short exact sequence $0 \to W \to V \to V/W \to 0$ of finite-dimensional $\mathfrak{g}$-modules splits. Indeed, complete reducibility then follows by induction on $\dim V$.

Case 1: $W$ and $V/W$ have different Casimir eigenvalues. Say $C$ acts as $c_1$ on $W$ and $c_2$ on $V/W$ with $c_1 \neq c_2$. Then $C - c_2 \cdot \mathrm{id}$ acts as $c_1 - c_2 \neq 0$ on $W$ and as $0$ on $V/W$. The endomorphism $P = (C - c_2 \mathrm{id}) / (c_1 - c_2)$ is the identity on $W$ and zero on $V/W$ — but actually we need a projection $V \to W$. The map $C - c_2 \mathrm{id} : V \to V$ has image inside $W$ (since it kills $V/W$) and acts as the scalar $c_1 - c_2$ on $W$, so the rescaled map $P = (C - c_2 \mathrm{id}) / (c_1 - c_2)$ is a $\mathfrak{g}$-module map $V \to W$ restricting to the identity on $W$. Its kernel is a $\mathfrak{g}$-invariant complement: $V = W \oplus \ker P$.

Case 2: Equal Casimir eigenvalues. Reduce by induction on $\dim V$. If $V/W$ is reducible, find a submodule $0 \neq U/W \subsetneq V/W$; inductive hypothesis applied to $0 \to W \to U \to U/W \to 0$ (with $\dim U < \dim V$) splits this, then applied to $0 \to U \to V \to V/U \to 0$ gives the further splitting. If $V/W$ is irreducible with the same Casimir as $W$ (hence isomorphic to a quotient of $W$ if irreducible — but $W$ is itself irreducible by hypothesis, so $W \cong V/W$), the splitting argument requires a cohomological input: $\mathrm{Ext}^1_{\mathfrak{g}}(V/W, W) = 0$ for finite-dimensional $\mathfrak{g}$-modules over a semisimple Lie algebra. The vanishing is proved by an explicit Casimir-based homotopy in the bar complex, or alternatively by the unitarian trick (the compact real form's representations admit invariant Hermitian inner products by averaging, giving orthogonal-complement splittings).

Either way, $V = W \oplus W'$ for some $\mathfrak{g}$-invariant complement $W' \cong V/W$. Rubric: full credit for Case 1 plus a clear statement of the inductive reduction and cohomological closing argument in Case 2.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (Casimir scalar via the bilinear form on $\mathfrak{h}^*$; Humphreys §23.2).** *For a semisimple complex Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ with Cartan subalgebra $\mathfrak{h}$, positive roots $\Phi^+$, half-sum $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} \alpha$, and highest weight $\lambda \in \mathfrak{h}^*$ dominant integral, the Casimir element $C$ acts on $V_\lambda$ by the scalar*
$$
c_{V_\lambda} = \langle \lambda, \lambda + 2 \rho \rangle = \langle \lambda + \rho, \lambda + \rho \rangle - \langle \rho, \rho \rangle.
$$
*The second form (Freudenthal) packages the eigenvalue as the difference of two squared lengths, where $\langle \rho, \rho \rangle = \tfrac{1}{12} \dim(\mathfrak{g})$ by the strange formula of Freudenthal-de Vries.*

The two formulae are algebraic identities: $\langle \lambda + \rho, \lambda + \rho \rangle - \langle \rho, \rho \rangle = \langle \lambda, \lambda \rangle + 2 \langle \lambda, \rho \rangle = \langle \lambda, \lambda + 2 \rho \rangle$. The second form is the one that generalises cleanly to the Freudenthal multiplicity formula for weight multiplicities of $V_\lambda$ and to the proof of the Weyl character formula via the Casimir [07.06.07].

**Theorem (Weyl complete-reducibility; Casimir-van der Waerden 1935).** *Every finite-dimensional representation of a semisimple complex Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ is a direct sum of finite-dimensional irreducible representations.*

The proof reduces to splitting short exact sequences $0 \to W \to V \to V/W \to 0$. When the Casimir eigenvalues on $W$ and $V/W$ differ, the operator $(C - c_2)/(c_1 - c_2)$ is a $\mathfrak{g}$-invariant projection $V \to W$ restricting to the identity on $W$. When they agree, an inductive argument plus the cohomological vanishing $\mathrm{Ext}^1_{\mathfrak{g}}(V/W, W) = 0$ produces the splitting. The cohomological vanishing has two classical proofs: Casimir-van der Waerden's explicit homotopy in the bar complex of $U(\mathfrak{g})$, and Weyl's earlier unitarian-trick argument using averaging over the compact real form.

**Theorem (Centre of the universal enveloping algebra; Harish-Chandra 1951).** *The centre $Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$ of the universal enveloping algebra of a semisimple complex Lie algebra of rank $r$ is a polynomial algebra in $r$ algebraically independent generators. There is a canonical isomorphism (the Harish-Chandra isomorphism)*
$$
\gamma : Z(U(\mathfrak{g})) \xrightarrow{\cong} S(\mathfrak{h})^W
$$
*onto the Weyl-invariant polynomials on the Cartan subalgebra, after a shift by $\rho$. The quadratic Casimir element is one generator (of degree two); the higher generators have degrees $d_1, \ldots, d_r$ equal to the degrees of the fundamental invariants of the Weyl group.*

For type $A_{n-1}$ (the case $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{sl}_n$), the degrees are $2, 3, 4, \ldots, n$, so $Z(U(\mathfrak{sl}_n))$ has $n - 1$ generators of those degrees. The quadratic Casimir is the first; the higher Casimirs come from the elementary symmetric polynomials in the Cartan-eigenvalues, transported back to $U(\mathfrak{g})$ via the inverse Harish-Chandra projection.

**Theorem (Infinitesimal character).** *Each finite-dimensional irreducible representation $V_\lambda$ of a semisimple $\mathfrak{g}$ has a well-defined infinitesimal character: an algebra homomorphism $\chi_\lambda : Z(U(\mathfrak{g})) \to \mathbb{C}$ given by evaluation on the highest-weight vector. The map $\lambda \mapsto \chi_\lambda$ is the composition of the Harish-Chandra isomorphism with evaluation at $\lambda + \rho$, and it factors through the Weyl group: $\chi_\lambda = \chi_\mu$ iff $\lambda + \rho$ and $\mu + \rho$ lie in the same $W$-orbit on $\mathfrak{h}^*$. For dominant integral $\lambda$, the orbit $W(\lambda + \rho)$ contains a unique dominant element, so $\chi_\lambda$ identifies $\lambda$ uniquely.*

The infinitesimal character is the higher-rank refinement of the Casimir eigenvalue. The quadratic Casimir's eigenvalue $\langle \lambda, \lambda + 2\rho \rangle$ is the value of $\chi_\lambda$ on one specific central element. The full character $\chi_\lambda : Z(U(\mathfrak{g})) \to \mathbb{C}$ records the joint eigenvalue on all of $Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$ and is the input to the representation-theoretic side of the Langlands correspondence.

**Theorem (Casimir acts on Verma modules).** *Let $M(\lambda)$ be the Verma module of $\mathfrak{g}$ with highest weight $\lambda \in \mathfrak{h}^*$ (not required to be dominant or integral). Then $C$ acts on $M(\lambda)$ as the scalar $\langle \lambda, \lambda + 2 \rho \rangle$. Consequently, two Verma modules $M(\lambda)$ and $M(\mu)$ admit a non-zero $\mathfrak{g}$-homomorphism only if $\chi_\lambda = \chi_\mu$, equivalently iff $\mu = w(\lambda + \rho) - \rho$ for some $w \in W$.*

The Casimir's action on $M(\lambda)$ is the same scalar as on the irreducible quotient $L(\lambda)$, since the highest-weight vector is shared. The infinitesimal-character obstruction is what controls the block decomposition of category $\mathcal{O}$ [07.06.06]: Vermas with non-linked infinitesimal characters live in distinct blocks and cannot map into one another.

**Theorem (Casimir and the Laplacian on a compact Lie group).** *Let $G$ be a compact connected Lie group with Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$, equipped with a bi-invariant Riemannian metric whose restriction to $\mathfrak{g}$ is $-B$ for $B$ the Killing form. Then the Casimir element $C$ acts on smooth functions $f : G \to \mathbb{C}$ (via the left-regular representation differentiated to $\mathfrak{g}$) as $-\Delta_G$, the Laplace-Beltrami operator on $G$. On the matrix coefficients of an irreducible representation $V_\lambda$, $\Delta_G$ acts by the eigenvalue $-\langle \lambda, \lambda + 2\rho \rangle$.*

This is the Casimir's analytic incarnation: on compact Lie groups, the Casimir is (up to sign) the Laplacian. The Peter-Weyl theorem decomposes $L^2(G)$ as a Hilbert direct sum $\bigoplus_\lambda V_\lambda \otimes V_\lambda^*$ of matrix coefficients, and the Laplacian acts by the explicit Casimir eigenvalue on each summand. Heat-kernel and harmonic analysis on $G$ is therefore controlled by the Casimir spectrum.

**Theorem (Casimir from a non-degenerate invariant form, semisimple case).** *Every finite-dimensional semisimple complex Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ admits a non-degenerate invariant symmetric bilinear form, namely the Killing form. The Killing form is unique up to a positive scalar on each simple summand; on a simple Lie algebra, every invariant symmetric bilinear form is a constant multiple of the Killing form. So the Casimir element is well-defined up to a scalar normalisation.*

This rigidity is what makes the Casimir an intrinsic invariant of $\mathfrak{g}$. On non-simple semisimple algebras $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{g}_1 \oplus \mathfrak{g}_2$, an invariant form may scale the two summands independently, giving a two-parameter family of Casimirs; the corresponding scalars $c_{V_\lambda}$ scale linearly with the form. The Killing-form choice is the canonical normalisation.

**Synthesis.** The Casimir element is the foundational reason representation theory of a semisimple complex Lie algebra has the spectral character it does. The central insight is that one degree-two central element of $U(\mathfrak{g})$ — built from a chosen invariant bilinear form via the dual-basis construction $C = \sum_a X_a X^a$ — acts as a scalar on each irreducible representation, and the scalar identifies the irreducible. This is exactly what powers the Casimir-van der Waerden 1935 algebraic proof of Weyl's complete-reducibility theorem: distinct Casimir eigenvalues on submodule and quotient give a $\mathfrak{g}$-invariant projection, and the equal-eigenvalue case is handled by induction plus cohomological vanishing. Putting these together with Harish-Chandra's 1951 theorem on the centre, the Casimir is one of $\mathrm{rk}(\mathfrak{g})$ algebraically independent generators of $Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$, and the joint spectrum of all $r$ generators on an irreducible $V_\lambda$ is the infinitesimal character $\chi_\lambda$, which generalises the single-scalar Casimir to a complete homomorphism $Z(U(\mathfrak{g})) \to \mathbb{C}$.

The bridge is the recognition that the Casimir is one face of three interconnected structures. On the algebraic side, $C \in Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$ and the Harish-Chandra isomorphism $Z(U(\mathfrak{g})) \cong S(\mathfrak{h})^W$ identifies the centre with Weyl-invariant polynomials. On the analytic side, on a compact Lie group with bi-invariant metric $C$ is the Laplacian, and its spectrum is the joint spectrum of the heat operator on $L^2(G)$. On the geometric side, the Casimir acts on Verma modules with the same eigenvalue as on irreducibles, and the resulting infinitesimal-character obstruction controls the block decomposition of category $\mathcal{O}$ [07.06.06] and the Beilinson-Bernstein localisation onto twisted $\mathcal{D}$-modules on the flag variety. The same pattern appears again in [07.06.11] (the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ Casimir $C = EF + FE + \tfrac{1}{2} H^2$ acting by $n(n+2)/2$ on $V_n$) and again in [07.06.12] (the $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ Casimir acting by $6$ on the adjoint $V_{1,1}$); each is the rank-one or rank-two specialisation of the same Harish-Chandra structure. The bridge is that one quadratic central element identifies its representation by its eigenvalue, separates submodules by spectral projection, generates the centre in the rank-one case, and incarnates as a Laplacian in the compact case — four different presentations of the same algebraic engine.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Independence of basis).** *The Casimir element $C = \sum_a X_a X^a \in U(\mathfrak{g})$ is independent of the choice of basis $\{X_a\}$ of $\mathfrak{g}$.*

**Proof.** Let $\{X_a\}$ and $\{Y_b\}$ be two bases of $\mathfrak{g}$ with $X_a = \sum_b M_{ab} Y_b$ and respective $B$-dual bases $\{X^a\}, \{Y^b\}$. Write $X^a = \sum_c N_{ac} Y^c$. The dual-basis condition $B(X_a, X^b) = \delta_a^b$ expands as
$$
\delta_a^b = B(X_a, X^b) = \sum_{c, d} M_{ad} N_{bc} B(Y_d, Y^c) = \sum_{c, d} M_{ad} N_{bc} \delta_d^c = \sum_c M_{ac} N_{bc} = (M N^T)_{ab}.
$$
So $M N^T = I$, i.e., $N = M^{-T}$ (the inverse transpose). Compute:
$$
\sum_a X_a X^a = \sum_a \Big( \sum_b M_{ab} Y_b \Big) \Big( \sum_c N_{ac} Y^c \Big) = \sum_{b, c} \Big( \sum_a M_{ab} N_{ac} \Big) Y_b Y^c.
$$
The inner sum is $\sum_a M_{ab} N_{ac} = (M^T N)_{bc} = (N M^T)^T_{cb}{}_{} = $... compute directly: $\sum_a M_{ab} N_{ac} = \sum_a (M^T)_{ba} (M^{-T})_{ac} = (M^T M^{-T})_{bc} = \delta_b^c$. So
$$
\sum_a X_a X^a = \sum_{b, c} \delta_b^c Y_b Y^c = \sum_b Y_b Y^b. \qquad \square
$$

**Proposition (Centrality, full version).** *For $\mathfrak{g}$ a Lie algebra (not necessarily semisimple) equipped with a non-degenerate invariant symmetric bilinear form $B$, the element $C = \sum_a X_a X^a \in U(\mathfrak{g})$ commutes with every element of $\mathfrak{g}$, hence with all of $U(\mathfrak{g})$.*

**Proof.** Fix $Y \in \mathfrak{g}$ and expand $[Y, X_a] = \sum_b c_a^b X_b$, $[Y, X^a] = \sum_b d^a_b X^b$. The invariance of $B$ gives, for each pair $a, c$,
$$
B([Y, X_a], X^c) = -B(X_a, [Y, X^c]),
$$
which evaluates to $c_a^c = -d^c_a$, i.e., $d^c_a = -c_a^c$.

Apply Leibniz in $U(\mathfrak{g})$: $[Y, X_a X^a] = [Y, X_a] X^a + X_a [Y, X^a]$. Sum over $a$:
$$
[Y, C] = \sum_{a, b} c_a^b X_b X^a + \sum_{a, b} d^a_b X_a X^b.
$$
Relabel $a \leftrightarrow b$ in the second sum and use $d^a_b = -c_b^a$:
$$
[Y, C] = \sum_{a, b} c_a^b X_b X^a + \sum_{a, b} (-c_a^b) X_b X^a = 0.
$$
Since $Y$ was arbitrary in $\mathfrak{g}$ and $\mathfrak{g}$ generates $U(\mathfrak{g})$ as an associative algebra, $C \in Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$. $\square$

**Proposition (Schur scalar).** *On a finite-dimensional irreducible representation $\rho : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{gl}(V)$ over $\mathbb{C}$, $\rho(C) = c_V \mathrm{id}_V$ for a unique scalar $c_V \in \mathbb{C}$.*

**Proof.** Since $C \in Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$, $\rho(C)$ commutes with every $\rho(Y)$ for $Y \in \mathfrak{g}$. By Schur's lemma applied to the irreducible representation $V$ over the algebraically closed field $\mathbb{C}$, any endomorphism of $V$ commuting with the action is a scalar. So $\rho(C) = c_V \mathrm{id}_V$. $\square$

**Proposition (Highest-weight eigenvalue).** *For $\mathfrak{g}$ semisimple with Cartan $\mathfrak{h}$, positive roots $\Phi^+$, $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} \alpha$, and $V_\lambda$ the finite-dimensional irreducible of dominant integral highest weight $\lambda$, the Casimir eigenvalue is $c_{V_\lambda} = \langle \lambda, \lambda + 2\rho \rangle$.*

**Proof.** Decompose $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{h} \oplus \bigoplus_{\alpha \in \Phi} \mathfrak{g}_\alpha$ as root spaces, with the Killing form pairing $\mathfrak{g}_\alpha$ to $\mathfrak{g}_{-\alpha}$ non-degenerately and vanishing on $\mathfrak{g}_\alpha \times \mathfrak{g}_\beta$ for $\beta \neq -\alpha$. Choose a basis $\{H_i\}$ of $\mathfrak{h}$ with $B$-dual basis $\{H^i\}$, and root vectors $E_\alpha \in \mathfrak{g}_\alpha$, $E_{-\alpha} \in \mathfrak{g}_{-\alpha}$ normalised so that $B(E_\alpha, E_{-\alpha}) = 1$. Then $\{H_i\} \cup \{E_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in \Phi}$ is a basis of $\mathfrak{g}$ with $B$-dual basis $\{H^i\} \cup \{E_{-\alpha}\}_{\alpha \in \Phi}$ (the dual of $E_\alpha$ is $E_{-\alpha}$). So
$$
C = \sum_i H_i H^i + \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi} E_\alpha E_{-\alpha}.
$$
Split the root sum into positive and negative parts. For each $\alpha \in \Phi^+$, pair $E_\alpha E_{-\alpha}$ with $E_{-\alpha} E_\alpha$ (which is the $-\alpha$-summand pairing with $\alpha$). Use $[E_\alpha, E_{-\alpha}] = B(E_\alpha, E_{-\alpha}) H_\alpha = H_\alpha$ where $H_\alpha \in \mathfrak{h}$ is the coroot defined by $B(H_\alpha, H) = \alpha(H)$ for $H \in \mathfrak{h}$. Then $E_\alpha E_{-\alpha} = E_{-\alpha} E_\alpha + H_\alpha$ in $U(\mathfrak{g})$, so
$$
C = \sum_i H_i H^i + \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} \big( 2 E_{-\alpha} E_\alpha + H_\alpha \big).
$$

Apply $C$ to the highest-weight vector $v_\lambda \in V_\lambda$. By definition $E_\alpha v_\lambda = 0$ for $\alpha \in \Phi^+$ and $H v_\lambda = \lambda(H) v_\lambda$ for $H \in \mathfrak{h}$. So $E_{-\alpha} E_\alpha v_\lambda = 0$, and
$$
C v_\lambda = \sum_i \lambda(H_i) \lambda(H^i) v_\lambda + \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} \lambda(H_\alpha) v_\lambda.
$$

The first sum is $\langle \lambda, \lambda \rangle v_\lambda$ by the dual-basis identity (the Killing form on $\mathfrak{h}^*$ pairs $\lambda$ with itself by $\sum_i \lambda(H_i) \lambda(H^i)$ when $\{H_i\}, \{H^i\}$ are dual bases of $\mathfrak{h}$).

The second sum: $\lambda(H_\alpha) = \langle \lambda, \alpha \rangle$ by definition of $H_\alpha$. So $\sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} \lambda(H_\alpha) = \langle \lambda, \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} \alpha \rangle = \langle \lambda, 2 \rho \rangle$.

Adding: $C v_\lambda = (\langle \lambda, \lambda \rangle + \langle \lambda, 2\rho \rangle) v_\lambda = \langle \lambda, \lambda + 2\rho \rangle v_\lambda$. By the Schur-scalar proposition, the same scalar acts on every vector of $V_\lambda$. $\square$

**Theorem (Weyl complete-reducibility, full algebraic proof).** *Every finite-dimensional representation $V$ of a semisimple complex Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ is a direct sum of finite-dimensional irreducible representations.*

**Proof.** It is enough to show that every short exact sequence $0 \to W \to V \to V/W \to 0$ of finite-dimensional $\mathfrak{g}$-modules splits; complete reducibility then follows by induction on $\dim V$. Refine further: by induction on $\dim V$, we may assume $W$ is irreducible. (Choose any irreducible submodule $W_0 \subseteq W$, apply the inductive hypothesis to $0 \to W_0 \to V \to V/W_0 \to 0$ to split off $W_0$, then to the smaller sequence inside the complementary summand.) So assume $W$ is irreducible.

Case 1: the Casimir acts on $W$ as $c_1$ and on $V/W$ with at least one eigenvalue $c_2 \neq c_1$. Then the operator $(C - c_2 \mathrm{id}_V)$ on $V$ maps $V$ into $W$ (since it kills the $c_2$-eigenspace, which contains a non-zero submodule of $V/W$ — refine the argument: extract that submodule's preimage and run the splitting in stages, ending at the case where $V/W$ is irreducible with Casimir scalar $c_2$). In the irreducible-$V/W$ subcase, $(C - c_2 \mathrm{id}_V) : V \to W$ acts as $c_1 - c_2 \neq 0$ on $W$. Define $P = (C - c_2 \mathrm{id}_V) / (c_1 - c_2)$; this is a $\mathfrak{g}$-module endomorphism of $V$ (since $C$ commutes with the action), restricts to the identity on $W$, and has image inside $W$. So $V = W \oplus \ker(P)$, with $\ker(P)$ a $\mathfrak{g}$-invariant complement of $W$.

Case 2: the Casimir agrees on $W$ and $V/W$, both irreducible. Then $W \cong V/W$ as $\mathfrak{g}$-modules (since both have the same Casimir eigenvalue and are irreducible; for $\mathfrak{g}$ semisimple, isomorphism class is determined by the joint eigenvalue of $Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$, which the quadratic Casimir alone fails to detect in general — the full argument uses all of $Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$ via Harish-Chandra; here we assume the simpler $W \cong V/W$ case for clarity). The splitting reduces to showing $\mathrm{Ext}^1_{\mathfrak{g}}(W, W) = 0$ for $W$ finite-dimensional irreducible. One proof (Whitehead's lemma): for finite-dimensional modules over a semisimple Lie algebra, $H^1(\mathfrak{g}, M) = 0$ and $H^2(\mathfrak{g}, M) = 0$ for every $M$. The first vanishing implies every derivation is inner; the second implies every extension of $\mathfrak{g}$-modules splits. Whitehead's lemmas are proved by an explicit Casimir-based homotopy in the Chevalley-Eilenberg complex: the operator $C$ acts on $C^k(\mathfrak{g}, M)$ as a scalar that is non-zero on the $\mathrm{Ext}$-relevant pieces (after the rho-shift), and the inverse of this scalar produces an explicit contracting homotopy.

Alternative proof (unitarian trick of Weyl): $\mathfrak{g}$ has a compact real form $\mathfrak{g}_\mathbb{R}$ whose complexification recovers $\mathfrak{g}$. The corresponding compact connected Lie group $G_\mathbb{R}$ exists by the integration theorem of Cartan. Every finite-dimensional representation of $\mathfrak{g}$ restricts to a representation of $\mathfrak{g}_\mathbb{R}$, which exponentiates to a representation of $G_\mathbb{R}$; this admits an invariant Hermitian inner product by averaging the Haar measure of $G_\mathbb{R}$. The orthogonal complement $W^\perp$ is a $\mathfrak{g}_\mathbb{R}$-invariant complement of $W$, and complexifying gives a $\mathfrak{g}$-invariant complement.

Either route gives the splitting, completing the inductive step. $\square$

**Proposition (Harish-Chandra projection, statement and rank-one example).** *The Harish-Chandra projection $\gamma : Z(U(\mathfrak{g})) \to S(\mathfrak{h})$ sends a central element $z$ to the polynomial $\gamma(z)(\lambda) = $ (the scalar by which $z$ acts on $V_\lambda^{}$, viewed as a polynomial function of $\lambda$). Up to a shift by $\rho$, $\gamma$ is an isomorphism onto $S(\mathfrak{h})^W$. For $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{sl}_2 \mathbb{C}$ with Casimir $C = EF + FE + \tfrac{1}{2} H^2$, the projection sends $C \mapsto \tfrac{1}{2} H^2 + H$ in $\mathbb{C}[H] = S(\mathfrak{h})$, which after the $\rho$-shift $H \mapsto H - 1$ is invariant under the Weyl reflection $H \mapsto -H$.*

**Proof for $\mathfrak{sl}_2$.** Evaluate $C$ on the highest-weight vector $v_n$ of $V_n$: $C v_n = (n + \tfrac{1}{2} n^2) v_n = (\tfrac{1}{2} n^2 + n) v_n$. So $\gamma(C)$ is the polynomial $\lambda \mapsto \tfrac{1}{2} \lambda^2 + \lambda$ on $\mathfrak{h}^*$, i.e., $\gamma(C) = \tfrac{1}{2} H^2 + H \in \mathbb{C}[H]$. After the $\rho$-shift $H \mapsto H - 1$ (since $\rho = 1$ for $\mathfrak{sl}_2$), this becomes $\tfrac{1}{2}(H - 1)^2 + (H - 1) = \tfrac{1}{2} H^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}$, which is a polynomial in $H^2$, hence invariant under $H \mapsto -H$. So $\gamma$ lands inside $\mathbb{C}[H]^W$ where $W = \mathbb{Z}/2$ acts by $H \mapsto -H$. The full Harish-Chandra theorem says $\gamma$ is an isomorphism onto $\mathbb{C}[H]^W = \mathbb{C}[H^2]$, recovering the centre $Z(U(\mathfrak{sl}_2 \mathbb{C})) = \mathbb{C}[C]$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Universal enveloping algebra [07.06.02].** The Casimir lives in $U(\mathfrak{g})$, the universal enveloping algebra of $\mathfrak{g}$; without this ambient associative algebra the product $X_a X^a$ has no home. The centre $Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$ that contains the Casimir is a polynomial algebra in $\mathrm{rk}(\mathfrak{g})$ generators by the Harish-Chandra theorem, with the quadratic Casimir as one generator and higher-degree Casimirs as the rest. Every central element acts as a scalar on each irreducible by Schur's lemma, but the quadratic Casimir alone is enough to separate irreducibles for $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ and is the workhorse in the complete-reducibility proof.

- **Representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_2 \mathbb{C}$ [07.06.11].** The rank-one specialisation: $\mathfrak{sl}_2 \mathbb{C}$ has Casimir $C = EF + FE + \tfrac{1}{2} H^2$ acting on the spin-$n/2$ representation $V_n = \mathrm{Sym}^n \mathbb{C}^2$ by the scalar $n(n+2)/2$. The Casimir-based proof of complete reducibility runs cleanly in this rank-one setting: distinct values of $n$ give distinct eigenvalues, the projection $(C - n(n+2)/2)/(c_1 - c_2)$ splits short exact sequences with unequal Casimirs, and the cohomological vanishing $\mathrm{Ext}^1_{\mathfrak{sl}_2}(V_n, V_n) = 0$ closes the equal-eigenvalue case. The unit [07.06.11] is the canonical worked example of the Casimir machinery.

- **Representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ [07.06.12].** The rank-two specialisation: $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ has quadratic Casimir acting on $V_{a, b}$ with eigenvalue $\langle \lambda, \lambda + 2\rho \rangle$ where $\lambda = a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2$ and $\rho = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$; on the adjoint $V_{1, 1}$ the scalar is $6$. In rank two there is also a cubic Casimir (the second generator of $Z(U(\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}))$), and the joint spectrum of quadratic and cubic Casimirs gives the full infinitesimal character $\chi_\lambda$. The unit [07.06.12] computes the quadratic Casimir on the adjoint representation as a worked example.

- **Root system [07.06.03].** The eigenvalue formula $c_{V_\lambda} = \langle \lambda, \lambda + 2\rho \rangle$ is computed from the root system: $\rho = \tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} \alpha$ is determined by a choice of positive roots, the inner product $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ is the Killing form restricted to $\mathfrak{h}^*$, and the coroots $H_\alpha$ used in the decomposition $C = \sum_i H_i H^i + \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+}(2 E_{-\alpha} E_\alpha + H_\alpha)$ are root-system data. The Casimir thus depends on $\mathfrak{g}$ only through its root system and the Killing-form normalisation.

- **Weyl character formula [07.06.07].** The Weyl character formula expresses the character of $V_\lambda$ as a quotient of antisymmetrised exponentials indexed by the Weyl group, and the proof via Casimir uses the eigenvalue identity $\langle \lambda + \rho, \lambda + \rho \rangle - \langle \rho, \rho \rangle = \langle \lambda, \lambda + 2\rho \rangle$ together with a Casimir-based positivity argument to extract the character from the Verma-module embedding $M(\lambda) \subseteq M(\lambda) \otimes \mathbb{C}$.

- **Verma module [07.06.06].** The Casimir acts on $M(\lambda)$ with the same scalar $\langle \lambda, \lambda + 2\rho \rangle$ as on the irreducible quotient $L(\lambda)$, since the highest-weight vector is shared. The infinitesimal-character obstruction $\chi_\lambda = \chi_\mu$ iff $\mu \in W \cdot (\lambda + \rho) - \rho$ controls the block decomposition of category $\mathcal{O}$: Verma modules with non-linked infinitesimal characters cannot admit non-zero homomorphisms, and the Casimir is the simplest detector of this linkage.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Hendrik Casimir introduced the element that bears his name in his 1931 Leiden dissertation *Über die Konstruktion einer zu den irreduziblen Darstellungen halbeinfacher kontinuierlicher Gruppen gehörigen Differentialgleichung* [ref: TODO_REF], written under Paul Ehrenfest with mathematical input from Bartel van der Waerden. The thesis was motivated by quantum mechanics: Casimir sought a second-order differential operator on a Lie group manifold whose eigenvalue on the matrix coefficients of an irreducible representation would identify the representation, generalising the angular-momentum operator $L^2 = L_x^2 + L_y^2 + L_z^2$ of quantum mechanics from the rotation group $\mathrm{SO}(3)$ to an arbitrary semisimple Lie group. Casimir's construction — pick a basis of the Lie algebra, take its dual under the Killing form, and form the sum of products in the enveloping algebra — produced exactly such an operator, with the property that it commutes with the group action and acts as a scalar on each irreducible. The eigenvalue $L^2 v = \ell(\ell + 1) v$ of quantum angular momentum is the rank-one $\mathfrak{su}(2)$ Casimir formula in disguise: the scalar $n(n+2)/2$ for the spin-$n/2$ representation rescales to $\ell(\ell+1)$ under the half-integer spin convention $\ell = n/2$.

The algebraic proof of complete reducibility came four years later in the joint Casimir-van der Waerden paper *Algebraischer Beweis der vollständigen Reduzibilität der Darstellungen halbeinfacher Liescher Gruppen* (Math. Ann. 111, 1935, 1-12) [ref: TODO_REF]. The result that every finite-dimensional representation of a semisimple Lie algebra is a direct sum of irreducibles had been established by Weyl in 1925-26 [ref: TODO_REF] via the unitarian trick: the analytic argument that a compact real form $\mathfrak{g}_\mathbb{R}$ exists, its representations integrate to a compact group $G_\mathbb{R}$, every finite-dimensional representation of $G_\mathbb{R}$ admits an invariant Hermitian inner product by averaging over Haar measure, and orthogonal complements give invariant splittings. Weyl's argument was geometric and analytic, requiring the integration theorem (every Lie algebra has a corresponding Lie group, due to Cartan 1930) and the existence and uniqueness of Haar measure. Casimir and van der Waerden's contribution was an algebraic proof using only the Casimir and the Chevalley-Eilenberg cohomology, with no integration. Their argument: distinct Casimir eigenvalues separate submodules via the operator $(C - c_2)/(c_1 - c_2)$; equal eigenvalues are handled by an explicit Casimir-based homotopy showing $H^1(\mathfrak{g}, M) = 0$ and $H^2(\mathfrak{g}, M) = 0$ (Whitehead's lemmas). The algebraic proof transferred Weyl's analytic theorem into a purely algebraic context, and the same argument applies to representations over any algebraically closed field of characteristic zero, not just $\mathbb{C}$.

The structural understanding deepened with Harish-Chandra's 1951 paper *On some applications of the universal enveloping algebra of a semisimple Lie algebra* (Trans. Amer. Math. Soc. 70, 28-96) [ref: TODO_REF]. Harish-Chandra identified the centre $Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$ with the Weyl-invariant polynomials $S(\mathfrak{h})^W$ on the Cartan dual, after a $\rho$-shift. The quadratic Casimir is one of $\mathrm{rk}(\mathfrak{g})$ algebraically independent generators of $Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$, with higher-degree Casimirs filling out the rest; for type $A_{n-1}$ the degrees are $2, 3, \ldots, n$. Harish-Chandra's isomorphism is what makes infinitesimal characters work: the joint eigenvalue of all of $Z(U(\mathfrak{g}))$ on $V_\lambda$ is a Weyl-orbit on $\mathfrak{h}^*$, and the orbit identifies $\lambda + \rho$ up to the Weyl group. The Casimir, in this larger picture, is one face of a polynomial-algebra-worth of central operators, and the spectral decomposition by Casimir is the rank-one shadow of the full infinitesimal-character decomposition. The same machinery later carried into Beilinson-Bernstein's 1981 localisation theorem, the Kazhdan-Lusztig conjecture, and the geometric Langlands programme, in each case with the Casimir-derived infinitesimal character controlling block structure.
