## Intuition [Beginner]

A Lie algebra representation turns each element of a Lie algebra into a matrix acting on a vector space. The defining rule: brackets in the algebra become matrix commutators. If $X$ and $Y$ are elements of the Lie algebra with matrices $\rho(X)$ and $\rho(Y)$, then the matrix for $[X,Y]$ equals $\rho(X)\rho(Y) - \rho(Y)\rho(X)$.

This matters because a Lie algebra captures infinitesimal symmetries — small rotations, small stretches — and a representation tells you how those infinitesimal symmetries move vectors. Once you pick a representation, you can compute everything with ordinary matrix algebra.

The simplest case is the adjoint representation, where $X$ acts on the whole algebra by the bracket: $X$ sends $Y$ to $[X,Y]$. The vector space is the Lie algebra itself, and each element becomes a matrix through its own bracket action.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ consists of $2 \times 2$ complex matrices with trace zero. Three generators span it: $H$ with diagonal entries $1$ and $-1$, $E$ with a $1$ in the top-right corner, and $F$ with a $1$ in the bottom-left corner. All other entries are zero.

In the standard $2$-dimensional representation, each generator acts by its own matrix. The bracket $[E,F] = H$ is verified by matrix multiplication: $EF - FE = H$. The vector $v_1 = (1,0)$ satisfies $Hv_1 = v_1$, so its weight is $1$. The vector $v_2 = (0,1)$ has weight $-1$. These two weight spaces span the whole space.

A $3$-dimensional representation has weights $2, 0, -2$ on three successive basis vectors. The operator $E$ shifts weight up and $F$ shifts weight down. The vector with weight $2$ is the highest weight vector — applying $E$ to it gives zero, so you cannot go higher.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **representation** of a Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ on a vector space $V$ is a Lie algebra homomorphism $\rho : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{gl}(V)$. Concretely, $\rho$ is linear and satisfies $\rho([X,Y]) = [\rho(X), \rho(Y)]$ for all $X, Y \in \mathfrak{g}$, where the bracket on the right is the commutator of endomorphisms. [ref: TODO_REF Humphreys §6; Fulton-Harris §9]

The **adjoint representation** $\text{ad} : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{gl}(\mathfrak{g})$ is defined by $\text{ad}(X)(Y) = [X,Y]$. The Jacobi identity guarantees that ad is a homomorphism: $\text{ad}([X,Y]) = [\text{ad}(X), \text{ad}(Y)]$.

A representation is **irreducible** if $V$ has no proper nonzero subspace preserved by every $\rho(X)$ — that is, no subspace $W$ with $0 \subsetneq W \subsetneq V$ satisfying $\rho(X)(W) \subseteq W$ for all $X \in \mathfrak{g}$.

Given a Cartan subalgebra $\mathfrak{h} \subset \mathfrak{g}$, a **weight** of $\rho$ is a linear functional $\lambda \in \mathfrak{h}^*$ for which the **weight space** $V_\lambda = \{v \in V : \rho(H)v = \lambda(H)v \text{ for all } H \in \mathfrak{h}\}$ is nonzero. The **highest weight** is a weight $\lambda$ such that $\lambda + \alpha$ is not a weight for any positive root $\alpha$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem.** The differential of a finite-dimensional Lie group representation is a Lie algebra representation. If $R : G \to \text{GL}(V)$ is a smooth group homomorphism, then $\rho = dR_e : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{gl}(V)$ satisfies $\rho([X,Y]) = [\rho(X),\rho(Y)]$.

**Proof.** Let $X, Y$ be left-invariant vector fields on $G$. The differential $dR$ sends left-invariant vector fields to vector fields on $\text{GL}(V)$. Because $R$ is a homomorphism, $dR$ preserves Lie brackets of vector fields. Evaluating at the identity, the bracket of left-invariant vector fields on $G$ maps to the commutator of the corresponding endomorphisms of $V$. Therefore $\rho([X,Y]) = [\rho(X),\rho(Y)]$. [ref: TODO_REF Humphreys §6; Fulton-Harris §9]

**Bridge.** This theorem connects the group-level representation theory of [07.01.01] to the infinitesimal Lie-algebraic setting developed here. The same bracket-preservation pattern reappears in [07.06.02] when the universal enveloping algebra functorially extends $\rho$ to an algebra homomorphism, and again in [07.06.06] where Verma modules are built from highest-weight data. Together, these three constructions form the chain that passes from smooth symmetries through their linearized infinitesimal versions to the combinatorial classification of all irreducible representations.

**Exercise 2 (easy, short answer).** For $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ with generators $H, E, F$, verify that $\text{ad}(H)(E) = [H,E] = 2E$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the commutator of the matrix $H$ with the matrix $E$.

$HE - EH$: multiply the diagonal matrix $H$ (entries $1, -1$) by the upper-triangular $E$ (entry $1$ at position $(1,2)$). The product $HE$ has $1$ at $(1,2)$, while $EH$ has $-1$ at $(1,2)$. Their difference gives $2$ at $(1,2)$, which is $2E$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short answer).** Show that the standard $2$-dimensional representation of $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ is irreducible.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show that any nonzero vector generates the whole space under the action of $H$, $E$, and $F$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take any nonzero $v \in \mathbb{C}^2$. If $v$ is not an eigenvector of $H$, then $v$ and $Hv$ are linearly independent, spanning $\mathbb{C}^2$. If $v$ has weight $1$, then $Fv$ has weight $-1$ and is nonzero, so $v$ and $Fv$ span $\mathbb{C}^2$. In both cases the invariant subspace generated by $v$ is all of $\mathbb{C}^2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).** Explain the relationship between weights of a representation of $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ and eigenvalues of $\rho(H)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Cartan subalgebra of $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{C})$ is spanned by $H$ alone, so a weight $\lambda$ is determined by $\lambda(H)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A weight $\lambda$ is determined by the single number $\lambda(H)$. The weight space $V_\lambda$ is the eigenspace of $\rho(H)$ with eigenvalue $\lambda(H)$. The weights of any finite-dimensional representation are integers, symmetrically spaced around zero.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The highest weight theorem classifies all finite-dimensional irreducible representations of a semisimple Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$. For each dominant integral weight $\lambda$ there exists exactly one irreducible representation $V(\lambda)$ with highest weight $\lambda$, up to isomorphism. The correspondence $\lambda \mapsto V(\lambda)$ is a bijection between dominant integral weights and isomorphism classes of irreducible representations. The proof uses the Verma module $M(\lambda)$ constructed in [07.06.06] and its unique irreducible quotient.

The Weyl complete reducibility theorem states that every finite-dimensional representation of a semisimple Lie algebra decomposes as a direct sum of irreducible representations. This can fail for non-semisimple algebras: the $2$-dimensional representation of the Lie algebra of upper-triangular $2 \times 2$ matrices is not completely reducible because the span of $(1,0)$ is invariant but has no invariant complement. [ref: TODO_REF Humphreys §6; Fulton-Harris §9]

**Synthesis.** The representation theory of a semisimple Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ is controlled by three interconnected structures: the root system of [07.06.03] provides the skeletal geometry that organizes weight spaces, the universal enveloping algebra of [07.06.02] furnishes the algebraic framework in which highest-weight constructions live, and the Weyl group of [07.06.04] governs the symmetries of the weight lattice. Together with the highest weight theorem and complete reducibility, these ingredients produce a complete classification: every finite-dimensional representation is a direct sum of irreducible ones, and each irreducible is determined by its dominant integral highest weight. This classification is the organizing principle behind every downstream unit in the Lie-algebraic strand.

## Full proof set [Master]

The differential theorem proved in the Key theorem section is the foundation. The full proof of the highest weight theorem proceeds through several stages. First, the Poincare-Birkhoff-Witt theorem gives a basis for the universal enveloping algebra. Second, Verma modules $M(\lambda)$ are constructed as quotients of the enveloping algebra. Third, each $M(\lambda)$ has a unique maximal submodule, giving the irreducible quotient $V(\lambda)$. Fourth, for dominant integral $\lambda$, the quotient $V(\lambda)$ is finite-dimensional. Fifth, any finite-dimensional irreducible representation with highest weight $\lambda$ is isomorphic to $V(\lambda)$. The Weyl complete reducibility theorem uses the Casimir element to split exact sequences. Full details appear in Humphreys and Fulton-Harris. [ref: TODO_REF Humphreys §6; Fulton-Harris §9]

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Lie algebra representations first appeared implicitly in the work of Lie and Killing on continuous transformation groups in the 1880s and 1890s. Cartan's 1894 thesis on the structure of Lie algebras made the infinitesimal theory autonomous. The adjoint representation was the primary computational tool: its eigenvalues (the roots) revealed the algebra's structure. The systematic study of representations as independent objects began with Cartan and Weyl in the 1920s. Weyl's proof of complete reducibility — originally via the "unitarian trick" linking compact and complex groups — showed that semisimple Lie algebra representation theory is particularly clean: every representation splits into irreducibles, and irreducibles are classified by discrete combinatorial data. This passage from continuous symmetry to discrete classification is one of the deep themes of twentieth-century mathematics. [ref: TODO_REF Cartan 1894; Humphreys §6; Fulton-Harris §9]
