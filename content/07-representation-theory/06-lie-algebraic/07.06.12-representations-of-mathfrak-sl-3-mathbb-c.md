## Intuition [Beginner]

The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ is the next step after $\mathfrak{sl}_2 \mathbb{C}$, and its representation theory is the first place where the ladder picture of rank one gives way to a genuinely two-dimensional weight picture. Instead of a single linear chain of weight spaces, the weights of an irreducible representation of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ sit at the points of a hexagonal grid in a plane. The convex hull of these points is a hexagon, and the symmetries of the hexagon are exactly the Weyl group of the root system $A_2$.

There are two Cartan generators, $H_1$ and $H_2$, that play the role of $H$ in the rank-one story. They commute, so every irreducible representation can be simultaneously diagonalised: each vector has a definite pair of eigenvalues, called its weight, drawn in a plane. Six raising and lowering operators move between weight spaces in six directions — the six roots of $A_2$, arranged like the spokes of a regular hexagon.

Two representations stand out as building blocks. The standard representation $V = \mathbb{C}^3$ has weights at the three vertices of an upward-pointing triangle. The dual representation $V^* = \mathbb{C}^3$ has weights at the three vertices of a downward-pointing triangle. Every other irreducible is built from tensor products of $V$ and $V^*$, indexed by two non-negative integers $(a, b)$ counting the symmetric powers in each direction. The dimension grows as a polynomial of degree three in $(a, b)$, and the adjoint representation $V_{1,1}$ of dimension eight (the famous "eight-fold way" of particle physics) is the rank-two analogue of the spin-one rung of $\mathfrak{sl}_2$.

The hexagon is the rank-two replacement for the linear ladder. Each irreducible representation $V_{a,b}$ has weights filling a hexagon (or a triangle, in the boundary cases $a = 0$ or $b = 0$) with multiplicities determined by how far inside the hexagon a weight lies — the inner layers stack up multiplicities while the outermost layer always has multiplicity one.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Build the adjoint representation $V_{1,1}$ of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ explicitly. The space is $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ itself, the eight-dimensional space of traceless $3 \times 3$ matrices, and the action is the commutator: $X \cdot Y = [X, Y]$ for $X \in \mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ and $Y \in \mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$.

Step 1. Pick a basis of the Cartan subalgebra. Set $H_1 = \mathrm{diag}(1, -1, 0)$ and $H_2 = \mathrm{diag}(0, 1, -1)$. These commute: $[H_1, H_2] = 0$.

Step 2. Pick basis vectors for the six off-diagonal directions. Write $E_{ij}$ for the matrix with a 1 in the $(i, j)$ slot and zeros elsewhere. The six off-diagonal $E_{ij}$ with $i \neq j$ each lie in $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$.

Step 3. Read off the action of $H_1$ on $E_{12}$. Compute $[H_1, E_{12}] = H_1 E_{12} - E_{12} H_1 = 1 \cdot E_{12} - (-1) \cdot E_{12} = 2 E_{12}$. Similarly $[H_2, E_{12}] = 0 \cdot E_{12} - 1 \cdot E_{12} = -E_{12}$. So $E_{12}$ is a weight vector with weight $(2, -1)$ — this is the simple root $\alpha_1$ in coordinates.

Step 4. Repeat for all six off-diagonal directions. The weights come out as $\alpha_1 = (2, -1)$ for $E_{12}$, $\alpha_2 = (-1, 2)$ for $E_{23}$, $\alpha_1 + \alpha_2 = (1, 1)$ for $E_{13}$, and the negatives $-\alpha_1, -\alpha_2, -(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2)$ for $E_{21}, E_{32}, E_{31}$. The six weights form a regular hexagon centred at the origin.

Step 5. The remaining two dimensions are the Cartan subalgebra itself. Both $H_1$ and $H_2$ have weight $(0, 0)$: each commutes with itself. So the zero-weight space has dimension two, and the total weight-multiplicity count is $6 \times 1 + 1 \times 2 = 8 = \dim \mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$.

What this shows: the adjoint representation is exactly the hexagonal weight diagram, with six outer dots of multiplicity one and one inner dot of multiplicity two at the centre. The integer parameters $(a, b) = (1, 1)$ identify this irreducible as $V_{1, 1}$, and the Weyl dimension formula confirms $\dim V_{1, 1} = (1 + 1)(1 + 1)(1 + 1 + 2)/2 = 8$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The **Lie algebra $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$** is the eight-dimensional complex Lie algebra of traceless $3 \times 3$ complex matrices under the commutator bracket. A standard basis adapted to the root-space decomposition consists of two diagonal Cartan generators
$$
H_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad H_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix},
$$
and the six off-diagonal matrix units $E_{ij}$ for $i \neq j$, where $E_{ij}$ has a $1$ in the $(i, j)$ entry and zeros elsewhere. The bracket relations are determined by $[E_{ij}, E_{k\ell}] = \delta_{jk} E_{i\ell} - \delta_{\ell i} E_{kj}$ together with the diagonal-bracket relations $[H_a, E_{ij}] = (\delta_{ai} - \delta_{aj} - \delta_{a, i-1} + \delta_{a, j-1}) E_{ij}$ for the chosen Cartan basis. Equivalently, writing $L_i \in \mathfrak{h}^*$ for the linear functional reading off the $i$-th diagonal entry of a diagonal matrix, the off-diagonal $E_{ij}$ is a weight vector of weight $L_i - L_j$ on the Cartan subalgebra $\mathfrak{h}$.

The **Cartan subalgebra** $\mathfrak{h} \subseteq \mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ is the two-dimensional abelian subalgebra of diagonal traceless matrices, spanned by $H_1$ and $H_2$ (or equivalently by any two of $L_1 - L_2$, $L_2 - L_3$, $L_1 - L_3$). The **root system** $\Phi \subseteq \mathfrak{h}^*$ is the set of non-zero weights of the adjoint action of $\mathfrak{h}$ on $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$:
$$
\Phi = \{ \pm(L_1 - L_2), \pm(L_2 - L_3), \pm(L_1 - L_3) \},
$$
six vectors arranged hexagonally in the two-dimensional space $\mathfrak{h}^*_{\mathbb{R}}$. The **simple roots** are $\alpha_1 = L_1 - L_2$ and $\alpha_2 = L_2 - L_3$, and the **positive roots** are $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_1 + \alpha_2$. The **Cartan matrix** with respect to this base is
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix},
$$
where $A_{ij} = \alpha_i(H_{\alpha_j}) = 2 (\alpha_i, \alpha_j)/(\alpha_j, \alpha_j)$ and the inner product on $\mathfrak{h}^*$ is normalised so that simple roots have squared length $2$.

A **representation** of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ on a complex vector space $V$ is a Lie algebra homomorphism $\rho : \mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C} \to \mathfrak{gl}(V)$. A **weight vector** of weight $\mu \in \mathfrak{h}^*$ is a non-zero $v \in V$ satisfying $\rho(H) v = \mu(H) v$ for all $H \in \mathfrak{h}$. The **weight space** $V_\mu$ is the simultaneous $\mathfrak{h}$-eigenspace at weight $\mu$. Because the off-diagonal generators shift weights — $\rho(E_{ij}) V_\mu \subseteq V_{\mu + L_i - L_j}$ by the same calculation as in the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ case — every finite-dimensional representation decomposes as $V = \bigoplus_{\mu \in \mathfrak{h}^*} V_\mu$.

A **highest-weight vector** is a weight vector $v_\lambda$ annihilated by all the positive root operators $\rho(E_{12}), \rho(E_{23}), \rho(E_{13})$. The **fundamental weights** $\lambda_1, \lambda_2 \in \mathfrak{h}^*$ are defined by duality with the simple coroots: $\lambda_i(H_{\alpha_j}) = \delta_{ij}$. Concretely, $\lambda_1 = L_1$ and $\lambda_2 = L_1 + L_2 = -L_3$ when restricted to the $\mathfrak{h}^*$ subject to $L_1 + L_2 + L_3 = 0$. A weight $\lambda$ is **integral dominant** when $\lambda = a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2$ with $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$. Writing $V_{a, b}$ for the irreducible representation with highest weight $a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2$ unties the parametrisation: $V_{1, 0} = V$ is the defining representation on $\mathbb{C}^3$ with highest weight $L_1 = \lambda_1$, and $V_{0, 1} = V^*$ is its dual with highest weight $-L_3 = \lambda_2$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The Cartan subalgebra is **two-dimensional**, not three. Three diagonal matrices $\mathrm{diag}(a_1, a_2, a_3)$ span a three-dimensional space, but the trace constraint $a_1 + a_2 + a_3 = 0$ cuts one dimension. The two generators $H_1, H_2$ chosen above form a basis of the resulting $2$-plane.
- The Weyl group of $A_2$ is the symmetric group $S_3$, acting on the three weights $L_1, L_2, L_3$ by permutation, of order $6$ — not $\mathbb{Z}/2$ as in the rank-one case. The hexagonal weight diagram has the dihedral symmetry of the regular hexagon, generated by the two simple reflections $s_{\alpha_1}, s_{\alpha_2}$.
- The two fundamental representations $V$ and $V^*$ are **not isomorphic** — they have opposite highest weights $\lambda_1$ versus $\lambda_2$ and their weight diagrams are mirror images. For $\mathfrak{sl}_2$, by contrast, $V \cong V^*$ because there is one fundamental and the Weyl group identifies a weight with its negative.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Classification of finite-dimensional irreducible representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$).** *The finite-dimensional irreducible representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ are in bijection with pairs of non-negative integers $(a, b) \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}^2$. For every such pair there exists a finite-dimensional irreducible representation $V_{a, b}$ of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$, unique up to isomorphism, characterised by the existence of a highest-weight vector $v_{a, b} \in V_{a, b}$ annihilated by $E_{12}, E_{23}, E_{13}$ and satisfying $H_1 v_{a, b} = a v_{a, b}$, $H_2 v_{a, b} = b v_{a, b}$. Conversely, every finite-dimensional irreducible representation of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ is isomorphic to some $V_{a, b}$.*

**Proof.** The proof has three parts: existence (construct $V_{a, b}$), uniqueness (any two irreducibles with the same highest weight are isomorphic), and integrality (the highest weight must be integral dominant).

**Part 1: integrality from existence of a highest-weight vector.** Let $W$ be a finite-dimensional irreducible representation. The Cartan subalgebra $\mathfrak{h}$ acts by commuting diagonalisable operators on $W$ (by $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-triple arguments applied to each $H_{\alpha_i}$; each acts diagonalisably because $W$ restricts to a finite-dimensional representation of the corresponding $\mathfrak{sl}_2$, which is completely reducible into the $V_n$ each diagonalising $H$). So $W$ decomposes as $W = \bigoplus_\mu W_\mu$ into $\mathfrak{h}$-weight spaces, and the positive root operators $E_{12}, E_{23}, E_{13}$ shift weight by $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_1 + \alpha_2$ respectively.

Choose a partial order on weights: $\mu \succeq \nu$ when $\mu - \nu$ is a non-negative integer combination of positive roots. Finite-dimensionality of $W$ together with the weight-shift property implies the set of weights of $W$ has a maximal element $\lambda$ with respect to $\succeq$. Any weight vector $v_\lambda \in W_\lambda$ is annihilated by $E_{12}, E_{23}, E_{13}$ (since $\lambda + \alpha_i$ is strictly greater than $\lambda$, but $\lambda$ is maximal, so the corresponding image is zero). So $v_\lambda$ is a highest-weight vector.

Restrict the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-triple $(H_{\alpha_1}, E_{12}, E_{21})$ to act on $W$. By the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ classification (see [07.06.11]), the chain of vectors $v_\lambda, E_{21} v_\lambda, E_{21}^2 v_\lambda, \ldots$ generates a finite-dimensional $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-submodule that decomposes into irreducibles $V_n$, each with integer top eigenvalue $n$. The top eigenvalue on the chain through $v_\lambda$ is $\lambda(H_{\alpha_1}) = a$, so $a \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$. The same argument with the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-triple $(H_{\alpha_2}, E_{23}, E_{32})$ forces $b = \lambda(H_{\alpha_2}) \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$. So $\lambda = a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2$ is integral dominant.

**Part 2: existence.** Given $(a, b) \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}^2$, construct $V_{a, b}$ as the irreducible quotient of the Verma module $M(a, b) = U(\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}) \otimes_{U(\mathfrak{b})} \mathbb{C}_{a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2}$, where $\mathfrak{b}$ is the Borel subalgebra spanned by $\mathfrak{h}$ and the positive root operators, and $\mathbb{C}_\lambda$ is the one-dimensional $\mathfrak{b}$-module on which $\mathfrak{h}$ acts by $\lambda$ and the positive root operators act by zero. The Verma module is infinite-dimensional but admits a unique maximal proper submodule $N(a, b)$, and the quotient $L(a, b) = M(a, b)/N(a, b)$ is the irreducible highest-weight module of weight $a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2$.

The argument that $L(a, b)$ is finite-dimensional uses the integral-dominance condition $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ together with the structure of the Weyl group. By the Harish-Chandra/Verma analysis (see [07.06.06]), the submodule generated by $F^{a + 1}_{\alpha_1} v_\lambda$ and $F^{b + 1}_{\alpha_2} v_\lambda$ (where $F_{\alpha_i} = E_{i+1, i}$) is the kernel making $L(a, b)$ finite-dimensional, with weight set equal to the Weyl-group orbit of weights $\mu$ with $\mu \preceq a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2$ and $\mu$ in the convex hull of the Weyl-orbit of $a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2$. Setting $V_{a, b} = L(a, b)$ completes the existence step.

**Part 3: uniqueness.** Suppose $W$ and $W'$ are two finite-dimensional irreducibles of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ with the same highest weight $\lambda = a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2$. Pick highest-weight vectors $v \in W$ and $v' \in W'$. The tensor product $W \otimes W'^*$ contains a $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$-invariant element by the matrix-coefficient $\phi(w) = \langle v', \pi'(g) v' \rangle$ where $g \in U(\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C})$ acts to take $v$ to $w$; equivalently, by Schur's lemma the space $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathfrak{sl}_3}(W, W')$ is one-dimensional, generated by the unique morphism sending $v$ to $v'$. This morphism is non-zero, hence an isomorphism by irreducibility. So $W \cong W'$, completing the uniqueness step. $\square$

**Theorem (Weyl dimension formula for $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$).** *The dimension of the irreducible representation $V_{a, b}$ is*
$$
\dim V_{a, b} = \tfrac{1}{2} (a + 1)(b + 1)(a + b + 2).
$$

**Proof.** Apply the Weyl dimension formula (see [07.06.08]):
$$
\dim V_\lambda = \prod_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} \frac{(\lambda + \rho, \alpha)}{(\rho, \alpha)},
$$
where $\rho = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = (1, 1)$ in fundamental-weight coordinates is the half-sum of positive roots. The three positive roots of $A_2$ are $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_1 + \alpha_2$. In coordinates where the inner product is the standard Euclidean one rescaled so $(\alpha_i, \alpha_i) = 2$:
- $(\rho, \alpha_1) = 1$, $(\rho, \alpha_2) = 1$, $(\rho, \alpha_1 + \alpha_2) = 2$;
- $(\lambda + \rho, \alpha_1) = a + 1$, $(\lambda + \rho, \alpha_2) = b + 1$, $(\lambda + \rho, \alpha_1 + \alpha_2) = a + b + 2$.

Multiplying the three ratios: $\dim V_{a, b} = \frac{(a+1)(b+1)(a+b+2)}{1 \cdot 1 \cdot 2}$, as claimed. $\square$

**Bridge.** This dimension formula builds toward the general Weyl character formula and identifies the integer-pair classification with explicit polynomial growth in $(a, b)$. The foundational reason the highest-weight theorem works in rank two is exactly the rank-one ladder structure applied along each of the two simple roots: the integrality $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ is the same integrality that forced $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ in the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ case (see [07.06.11]), now propagated along two independent simple-root directions instead of one. This is exactly the central insight that generalises to all of Cartan-Killing: every irreducible representation of a semisimple Lie algebra is determined by an integral dominant highest weight, and the dimension is computed by the Weyl product formula over positive roots. The hexagonal weight diagram of $V_{a, b}$ is dual to the integer-pair parametrisation through the explicit map from a dominant weight to the convex hull of its Weyl-orbit, and putting these together identifies $V_{a, b}$ with the data $(a, b)$ in a Weyl-symmetric and combinatorially explicit way. The bridge is that the two fundamental representations $V$ and $V^*$ of $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ generalise the single fundamental representation of $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ to a rank-two pattern, and the Weyl dimension formula appears again in [07.06.08] (Weyl dimension formula) as the universal answer that specialises here to $(a+1)(b+1)(a+b+2)/2$.

**Exercise 8 (hard, short-answer).**

State and outline a proof of the general fact: $\mathfrak{sl}_{n+1} \mathbb{C}$ has exactly $n$ fundamental representations, given by the exterior powers $\Lambda^k V$ of the defining representation $V = \mathbb{C}^{n+1}$ for $k = 1, 2, \ldots, n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Identify the highest weight of $\Lambda^k V$ and verify it is the $k$-th fundamental weight $\lambda_k$. The rank of $\mathfrak{sl}_{n+1}$ is $n$, so there are $n$ fundamental weights.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{sl}_{n+1} \mathbb{C}$ has Cartan subalgebra of dimension $n$ (diagonal traceless matrices), giving rank $n$. The simple roots are $\alpha_k = L_k - L_{k+1}$ for $k = 1, \ldots, n$, where $L_i$ reads off the $i$-th diagonal entry. The fundamental weights $\lambda_k$ are dual to the simple coroots $H_{\alpha_k}$, with explicit formula $\lambda_k = L_1 + L_2 + \cdots + L_k$.

The exterior power $\Lambda^k V$ has basis $\{e_{i_1} \wedge e_{i_2} \wedge \cdots \wedge e_{i_k} : 1 \leq i_1 < i_2 < \cdots < i_k \leq n + 1\}$ of dimension $\binom{n+1}{k}$. The action of $\mathfrak{h}$ is diagonal in this basis: the weight of $e_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge e_{i_k}$ is $L_{i_1} + L_{i_2} + \cdots + L_{i_k}$. The highest weight is achieved at $(i_1, \ldots, i_k) = (1, 2, \ldots, k)$, giving weight $L_1 + L_2 + \cdots + L_k = \lambda_k$.

To verify irreducibility: the cyclic submodule generated by the highest-weight vector $e_1 \wedge \cdots \wedge e_k$ under the action of the negative root operators reaches every $e_{i_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge e_{i_k}$ by applying lowering operators iteratively, so the entire $\Lambda^k V$ is the irreducible submodule. By the classification theorem, $\Lambda^k V \cong V_{\lambda_k}$, the $k$-th fundamental representation. The count $k = 1, \ldots, n$ gives all $n$ fundamentals.

For $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ specifically: $\Lambda^1 V = V$ is the standard, and $\Lambda^2 V \cong V^*$ via the natural pairing $\Lambda^2 V \otimes V \to \Lambda^3 V \cong \mathbb{C}$, identifying $\Lambda^2 V$ with the dual. So the two fundamentals are $V = V_{1, 0}$ and $V^* = V_{0, 1}$, recovering the rank-two count. Rubric: full credit for the highest-weight identification plus the count argument and the $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ specialisation.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (Convex-hull description of weights).** *Let $V_{a, b}$ be the irreducible representation of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ with highest weight $\lambda = a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2$. The set of weights of $V_{a, b}$ is the intersection of the Weyl-group orbit $W \cdot \lambda$ with the convex hull $\mathrm{conv}(W \cdot \lambda) \subset \mathfrak{h}^*_{\mathbb{R}}$, together with all weights $\mu$ in this convex hull satisfying $\mu - \lambda \in \mathbb{Z} \Phi$ (the root lattice). The multiplicities are determined by the Freudenthal recursion*
$$
((\lambda + \rho, \lambda + \rho) - (\mu + \rho, \mu + \rho)) m_\mu = 2 \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} \sum_{k \geq 1} m_{\mu + k\alpha} (\mu + k\alpha, \alpha).
$$
*The outermost weights (those on the boundary of the convex hull, in the Weyl-orbit of $\lambda$) have multiplicity exactly $1$, and each interior shell increases multiplicities by $1$ until the central shell is reached.*

This is the rank-two version of the convex-hull description that already appears for $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ as a line segment $[-n, n]$ with multiplicity $1$ at each integer point. The hexagonal weight diagram is the Weyl orbit of the highest weight, and the shells from the boundary inward have multiplicities $1, 2, 3, \ldots$ stabilising once a shell becomes triangular (in pure $V_{a, 0}$ or $V_{0, b}$ cases the diagram is a single triangle and all multiplicities are $1$).

**Theorem (Clebsch-Gordan decomposition for fundamental representations).** *The tensor products of the two fundamental representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ decompose as*
$$
V \otimes V \cong V_{2, 0} \oplus V_{0, 1} = \mathrm{Sym}^2(V) \oplus \Lambda^2(V),
$$
$$
V \otimes V^* \cong V_{1, 1} \oplus V_{0, 0} = \mathrm{Ad}(\mathfrak{sl}_3) \oplus \mathbb{C},
$$
$$
V^* \otimes V^* \cong V_{0, 2} \oplus V_{1, 0} = \mathrm{Sym}^2(V^*) \oplus \Lambda^2(V^*).
$$
*The first and third decompositions express the splitting of a $9$-dimensional tensor product into symmetric and antisymmetric parts; the middle decomposition identifies the adjoint $V_{1, 1}$ as the traceless part of $V \otimes V^* \cong \mathrm{End}(V)$.*

This is the rank-two analogue of the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ Clebsch-Gordan rule $V_m \otimes V_n = \bigoplus_k V_{m + n - 2k}$. The middle identity $V \otimes V^* = V_{1, 1} \oplus \mathbb{C}$ is the canonical Lie-algebraic statement that the endomorphism algebra of $V$ decomposes as the trace-zero subspace (the adjoint) plus the scalar subspace (the identity). In physics this is the SU(3) tensor decomposition $3 \otimes \bar{3} = 8 \oplus 1$ — the eight-fold way of Gell-Mann and Ne'eman, where the eight is the adjoint and the one is the trace.

**Theorem (Steinberg / Littlewood-Richardson for $\mathfrak{sl}_3$).** *The Clebsch-Gordan multiplicities for $V_{a_1, b_1} \otimes V_{a_2, b_2}$ are given by the rank-two specialisation of the Littlewood-Richardson rule, equivalently by Steinberg's character formula:*
$$
\chi_{V_{a_1, b_1}} \chi_{V_{a_2, b_2}} = \sum_{(a, b)} N^{(a, b)}_{(a_1, b_1), (a_2, b_2)} \chi_{V_{a, b}},
$$
*where $N^{(a, b)}_{(a_1, b_1), (a_2, b_2)}$ counts semistandard Young tableaux of shape $(a + b, b)$ (in the two-row Young-diagram language for $\mathfrak{sl}_3$) with content determined by the input weights, modulo a Weyl-group anti-symmetrisation.*

The general $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ Clebsch-Gordan rule is not as compact as the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ formula but is still computable in polynomial time and gives the full structure of the representation ring of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$. The rule appears again in [07.06.07] (Weyl character formula) as a character-theoretic identity.

**Theorem (Casimir element and central characters).** *The quadratic Casimir element*
$$
C = \tfrac{1}{2} H_1^2 + \tfrac{1}{2} H_2^2 + \tfrac{1}{2} H_1 H_2 + \tfrac{1}{2} H_2 H_1 + \sum_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} (E_\alpha F_\alpha + F_\alpha E_\alpha)
$$
*lies in the centre of the universal enveloping algebra $U(\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C})$. On the irreducible representation $V_{a, b}$ it acts as the scalar*
$$
C |_{V_{a, b}} = (\lambda + \rho, \lambda + \rho) - (\rho, \rho) = \tfrac{1}{3}(a^2 + b^2 + ab) + a + b,
$$
*where $\lambda = a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2$ and $\rho = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$.*

The Casimir scalar in rank two is no longer a single integer parameter but a quadratic polynomial in $(a, b)$. The Harish-Chandra centre $Z(U(\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}))$ is a polynomial algebra in two generators (the quadratic and cubic Casimirs), reflecting the rank-two parametrisation; this is the rank-two instance of the general Harish-Chandra isomorphism $Z(U(\mathfrak{g})) \cong S(\mathfrak{h})^W$.

**Theorem (Borel-Weil realisation for $\mathfrak{sl}_3$).** *Every irreducible $V_{a, b}$ of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ is realised geometrically as $H^0(\mathrm{SL}_3 \mathbb{C} / B, \mathcal{L}_{a, b})$, the space of holomorphic global sections of the line bundle $\mathcal{L}_{a, b}$ on the flag variety $\mathrm{SL}_3 \mathbb{C} / B = \mathrm{Fl}_3(\mathbb{C})$ (the full flag variety of $\mathbb{C}^3$) twisted by the character $(a, b)$ of the maximal torus.*

This is the geometric counterpart to the algebraic classification, and the rank-two case of the Borel-Weil theorem (see [07.06.09]). The flag variety $\mathrm{Fl}_3(\mathbb{C})$ is a six-dimensional complex projective variety, the simplest case of a complete flag variety beyond $\mathbb{C}P^1 = \mathrm{Fl}_2(\mathbb{C})$, and the line bundles $\mathcal{L}_{a, b}$ are indexed by pairs of non-negative integers exactly as the irreducible representations are.

**Synthesis.** The representation theory of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ is the foundational reason that the rank-two case of Cartan-Killing classification works, and it is exactly the rank-one $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ ladder structure ([07.06.11]) propagated along two independent simple-root directions. The central insight is that every irreducible representation $V_{a, b}$ is generated by a highest-weight vector annihilated by both positive simple-root operators, with the two independent integers $(a, b)$ replacing the single integer $n$ of $\mathfrak{sl}_2$; this is exactly the local model for every weight chain in every higher-rank Lie algebra, with the rank two simply tracking two simultaneously available raising-lowering directions instead of one. The hexagonal weight diagram with its $S_3$ Weyl symmetry and shell-by-shell multiplicity structure builds toward the general weight-multiplicity machinery of arbitrary semisimple Lie algebras, with the Weyl-orbit-and-convex-hull description appearing again in [07.06.07] (Weyl character formula) as the universal recipe. The Weyl dimension formula $(a + 1)(b + 1)(a + b + 2)/2$ generalises the rank-one $\dim V_n = n + 1$ by adding a quadratic factor for the third positive root $\alpha_1 + \alpha_2$.

Putting these together, the pattern is clear: $\mathfrak{sl}_{n + 1}$ has $n$ fundamental representations (the exterior powers $\Lambda^k V$), a Cartan subalgebra of dimension $n$, a root system $A_n$ with $n(n + 1)$ roots, a Weyl group $S_{n + 1}$, and a Weyl dimension formula given by an $\binom{n + 1}{2}$-fold product over positive roots — and every step of this pattern is the rank-$n$ propagation of the rank-one engine. The bridge between $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ and the geometric / topological side of representation theory is the Borel-Weil theorem [07.06.09]: $V_{a, b}$ is geometrically the space of holomorphic sections of the line bundle $\mathcal{L}_{a, b}$ on the flag variety $\mathrm{SL}_3 \mathbb{C}/B = \mathrm{Fl}_3(\mathbb{C})$, identifying algebraic highest-weight data with cohomological data on a complex projective variety.

The duality between the algebraic and the combinatorial picture sharpens at rank two. The Freudenthal multiplicity formula computes the integer multiplicity of every weight in $V_{a, b}$ recursively from the highest weight inward, with the shells of the hexagonal diagram carrying multiplicities $1, 2, 3, \ldots$ in stair-step fashion until the central shell is reached. The Weyl-orbit-and-convex-hull description identifies the weight set with the lattice points of the convex polygon $\mathrm{conv}(S_3 \cdot (a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2))$. Steinberg's general Clebsch-Gordan formula gives the tensor-product decomposition $V_{a_1, b_1} \otimes V_{a_2, b_2}$ via inclusion-exclusion over $S_3$, generalising the Pieri rule and culminating in the full Littlewood-Richardson description for $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ via two-row Young tableaux. Quantum deformation produces $U_q(\mathfrak{sl}_3)$ with the same Cartan-and-root-data, with crystal bases (Kashiwara 1991) decorating the hexagonal weight diagram by Kashiwara operators that move along the six root directions; the classical limit $q \to 1$ recovers the $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ representation theory of this unit. Affine deformation produces $\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}_3}$ with infinite-dimensional level-and-weight integrable representations, and the extended Dynkin diagram for $\widehat{\mathfrak{sl}_3}$ adds a third node forming a triangle, recapitulating the rank-two hexagonal symmetry at the affine level. The bridge is that the hexagonal weight diagram of $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ is the local model for every two-dimensional weight projection in every higher-rank semisimple Lie algebra, just as the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ ladder is the local model for every one-dimensional weight chain.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Theorem (classification of irreducible $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$-representations), proof.** Given in full in the Intermediate-tier section: integrality $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ follows from applying the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-classification ([07.06.11]) to each of the two $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-triples $(H_{\alpha_i}, E_{\alpha_i}, F_{\alpha_i})$ for $i = 1, 2$. Existence of $V_{a, b}$ as the irreducible quotient of the Verma module $M(a, b)$ uses the integrality to ensure finite-dimensionality. Uniqueness follows from Schur's lemma applied to the one-dimensional space of highest-weight maps between two irreducibles with the same highest weight. $\square$

**Proposition (root-space decomposition of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$).** *Under the adjoint action of the Cartan subalgebra $\mathfrak{h}$ spanned by $H_1, H_2$, the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ decomposes as $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C} = \mathfrak{h} \oplus \bigoplus_{\alpha \in \Phi} \mathfrak{g}_\alpha$ where $\Phi = \{\pm \alpha_1, \pm \alpha_2, \pm(\alpha_1 + \alpha_2)\}$ and each root space $\mathfrak{g}_\alpha$ is one-dimensional, spanned by the appropriate $E_{ij}$.*

**Proof.** The Cartan subalgebra $\mathfrak{h}$ has dimension $2$, spanned by $H_1 = \mathrm{diag}(1, -1, 0)$ and $H_2 = \mathrm{diag}(0, 1, -1)$. The total dimension of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ is $3^2 - 1 = 8$, leaving $6$ dimensions for the root spaces.

Compute the action of $H_1$ and $H_2$ on each off-diagonal matrix unit. For $E_{ij}$ with $i \neq j$:
$$
[H_1, E_{ij}] = (\delta_{1i} - \delta_{1j} + \delta_{2j} - \delta_{2i}) E_{ij},
$$
$$
[H_2, E_{ij}] = (\delta_{2i} - \delta_{2j} + \delta_{3j} - \delta_{3i}) E_{ij}.
$$
A short check: $[H_1, E_{12}] = (1 - 0 + 1 - 0) E_{12} = 2 E_{12}$ (since $\delta_{2j} = \delta_{22} = 1$, $\delta_{1i} = \delta_{11} = 1$), and similarly all six $E_{ij}$ for $i \neq j$ are simultaneous $H_1, H_2$ eigenvectors. The resulting weights are
$$
E_{12} \mapsto (2, -1) = \alpha_1, \quad E_{23} \mapsto (-1, 2) = \alpha_2, \quad E_{13} \mapsto (1, 1) = \alpha_1 + \alpha_2,
$$
$$
E_{21} \mapsto (-2, 1) = -\alpha_1, \quad E_{32} \mapsto (1, -2) = -\alpha_2, \quad E_{31} \mapsto (-1, -1) = -\alpha_1 - \alpha_2.
$$
The six off-diagonal $E_{ij}$ each span a distinct one-dimensional weight space, completing the decomposition. $\square$

**Proposition (Casimir centrality for $\mathfrak{sl}_3$).** *The quadratic Casimir element $C = \sum_a B(X_a, X_a)^{-1} X_a^2$, computed with respect to the Killing form $B$ on a basis $\{X_a\}$ of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$, lies in the centre of the universal enveloping algebra $U(\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C})$.*

**Proof.** The Killing form $B(X, Y) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Y)$ on $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ restricts to $\mathfrak{h} \times \mathfrak{h}$ as a non-degenerate bilinear form (the diagonal-traceless form scaled by a factor of $2 (n + 1) = 8$ for $n + 1 = 3$). On root spaces $\mathfrak{g}_\alpha \times \mathfrak{g}_{-\alpha}$ it is non-degenerate, and it pairs $\mathfrak{g}_\alpha$ with $\mathfrak{g}_\beta$ to zero when $\alpha + \beta \neq 0$ (by the invariance $B([X, Y], Z) = B(X, [Y, Z])$ together with the weight argument).

The centrality $[Y, C] = 0$ for all $Y \in \mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ follows from the general identity for the quadratic Casimir of any Lie algebra equipped with a non-degenerate invariant bilinear form: choose a basis $\{X_a\}$ with dual basis $\{X^a\}$ such that $B(X_a, X^b) = \delta_a^b$, define $C = \sum_a X_a X^a$, and compute $[Y, C] = \sum_a [Y, X_a] X^a + \sum_a X_a [Y, X^a] = \sum_{a, b} B([Y, X_a], X^b) X_b X^a + \sum_{a, b} X_a B([Y, X^b], X_b)^{-1} X^a \cdot \delta_b^a$. A telescoping argument using the symmetry $B([Y, X_a], X_b) = -B(X_a, [Y, X_b])$ (the invariance of $B$) cancels every term in pairs, giving $[Y, C] = 0$. The argument applies in particular to $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$, identifying the explicit quadratic Casimir of the theorem above as central. The scalar eigenvalue on $V_{a, b}$ is computed by evaluating $C$ on the highest-weight vector and using $E_\alpha v_\lambda = 0$ for positive roots. $\square$

**Proposition (Weyl dimension formula, $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ case).** *The dimension of $V_{a, b}$ is $(a + 1)(b + 1)(a + b + 2)/2$.*

**Proof.** The general Weyl dimension formula (see [07.06.08]) reads $\dim V_\lambda = \prod_{\alpha \in \Phi^+} (\lambda + \rho, \alpha)/(\rho, \alpha)$ where $\rho$ is the half-sum of positive roots. For $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ with positive roots $\Phi^+ = \{\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_1 + \alpha_2\}$ and $\rho = \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$ (using the standard identity that $\rho$ equals the sum of fundamental weights):
$$
(\rho, \alpha_1) = (\lambda_1 + \lambda_2, \alpha_1) = (\lambda_1, \alpha_1) + (\lambda_2, \alpha_1) = 1 + 0 = 1,
$$
using $(\lambda_i, \alpha_j) = \delta_{ij} (\alpha_j, \alpha_j)/2 = \delta_{ij}$ in the rescaled normalisation $(\alpha_i, \alpha_i) = 2$. Similarly $(\rho, \alpha_2) = 1$ and $(\rho, \alpha_1 + \alpha_2) = 2$. For $\lambda + \rho = (a + 1) \lambda_1 + (b + 1) \lambda_2$:
$$
(\lambda + \rho, \alpha_1) = a + 1, \quad (\lambda + \rho, \alpha_2) = b + 1, \quad (\lambda + \rho, \alpha_1 + \alpha_2) = a + b + 2.
$$
The product gives $\dim V_{a, b} = (a + 1)(b + 1)(a + b + 2)/(1 \cdot 1 \cdot 2) = (a + 1)(b + 1)(a + b + 2)/2$. $\square$

**Proposition (Clebsch-Gordan $V \otimes V^* \cong V_{1, 1} \oplus \mathbb{C}$).** *The tensor product of the standard and dual representations decomposes as $V \otimes V^* \cong V_{1, 1} \oplus V_{0, 0}$.*

**Proof.** Dimension check: $\dim(V \otimes V^*) = 3 \cdot 3 = 9 = 8 + 1 = \dim V_{1, 1} + \dim V_{0, 0}$.

Identify the zero-weight component. The natural pairing $V \otimes V^* \to \mathbb{C}$ given by $v \otimes \phi \mapsto \phi(v)$ is a $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$-equivariant map, with kernel of dimension $9 - 1 = 8$. This kernel is the space of traceless tensors, i.e., $\mathrm{End}_0(V) = \{T \in \mathrm{End}(V) : \mathrm{tr}(T) = 0\}$, which is exactly $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$. The action of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ on $\mathrm{End}_0(V)$ by $X \cdot T = X T - T X$ is the adjoint action, which is the irreducible representation $V_{1, 1}$ by the root-space decomposition argument.

The identification of the adjoint representation as $V_{1, 1}$: the highest weight of the adjoint representation is the highest root $\alpha_1 + \alpha_2 = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$, exactly the highest weight of $V_{1, 1}$ corresponding to $(a, b) = (1, 1)$. So $V \otimes V^* \cong V_{1, 1} \oplus V_{0, 0}$ as claimed. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_2 \mathbb{C}$ [07.06.11].** The rank-one case is the direct prerequisite and the technical engine: the integrality $a, b \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ of the highest-weight classification for $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ follows from applying the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ classification along each of the two $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-triples $(H_{\alpha_i}, E_{\alpha_i}, F_{\alpha_i})$ for $i = 1, 2$. Every weight chain in $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ is a direct sum of rank-one chains, each of which is a $V_n$ from the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ classification. The hexagonal weight diagram of $V_{a, b}$ projects along any one simple-root direction to a union of $\mathfrak{sl}_2$-ladders, recovering the rank-one picture as a slice.

- **Lie algebra representation [07.06.01].** The general definition of a Lie algebra representation specialises to the rank-two case here: every finite-dimensional representation of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ decomposes as a direct sum of irreducible $V_{a, b}$, parametrised by integral dominant highest weights. The same definitional framework that gave the $V_n$ classification for $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ ([07.06.11]) gives the $V_{a, b}$ classification here, with the same Casimir-element and weight-space machinery propagating to higher rank.

- **Root system [07.06.03].** The root system of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ is $A_2$, with six roots arranged in a regular hexagon. The Cartan matrix is $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$, and the Weyl group is $S_3$. The general theory of root systems classifies the rank-two simply-laced case as exactly $A_2$, and the $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ representation theory of this unit is the canonical realisation. The fundamental weights $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ are the dual basis to the simple coroots and parametrise the irreducible representations.

- **Verma module [07.06.06].** The Verma module $M(a, b) = U(\mathfrak{sl}_3) \otimes_{U(\mathfrak{b})} \mathbb{C}_{a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2}$ is the infinite-dimensional universal highest-weight module, and the finite-dimensional irreducible $V_{a, b}$ is the unique irreducible quotient. The kernel making the quotient finite-dimensional is generated by $F_{\alpha_1}^{a + 1} v_\lambda$ and $F_{\alpha_2}^{b + 1} v_\lambda$ (the Serre relations applied to the integrality conditions), exactly mirroring the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ case where the kernel was generated by $F^{n + 1} v_n$.

- **Weyl character formula [07.06.07].** The character of $V_{a, b}$ is computed by the Weyl character formula as a ratio of alternating sums over the Weyl group $S_3$: $\chi_{V_{a, b}} = \sum_{w \in S_3} (-1)^{\ell(w)} e^{w(\lambda + \rho)} / \sum_{w \in S_3} (-1)^{\ell(w)} e^{w \rho}$, where $\ell$ is the length of $w$ and the denominator is the Weyl denominator. The rank-two character is no longer a single rational expression in a single variable but a ratio of three-term polynomials in two formal variables, reflecting the two Cartan directions.

- **Weyl dimension formula [07.06.08].** The general Weyl dimension formula specialises to $\dim V_{a, b} = (a + 1)(b + 1)(a + b + 2)/2$ for $\mathfrak{sl}_3$, a polynomial of degree three in $(a, b)$ generalising the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ formula $\dim V_n = n + 1$. The product over positive roots picks up three factors, one per positive root of $A_2$, exactly the rank-two pattern.

- **Borel-Weil theorem [07.06.09].** The Borel-Weil theorem realises every $V_{a, b}$ as $H^0(\mathrm{Fl}_3(\mathbb{C}), \mathcal{L}_{a, b})$, the space of holomorphic sections of a line bundle on the full flag variety of $\mathbb{C}^3$. The flag variety is a six-dimensional complex projective variety, and the line bundles are indexed by pairs of non-negative integers exactly matching the highest-weight classification. This is the rank-two generalisation of the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ realisation on $\mathbb{C}P^1$.

- **Schur-Weyl duality [07.05.04].** The $V_{a, b}$ classification carried out here through the highest-weight machinery is recovered by Schur-Weyl duality applied to $V = \mathbb{C}^3$: each Weyl module $\mathbb{S}^\lambda \mathbb{C}^3$ for a partition $\lambda$ with $\ell(\lambda) \leq 3$ is the irreducible $\mathrm{GL}(3, \mathbb{C})$-representation of highest weight $\lambda$, which descends to $V_{a, b}$ with $a = \lambda_1 - \lambda_2$ and $b = \lambda_2 - \lambda_3$. Schur-Weyl is therefore the route by which the $S_n$-side combinatorics of Young symmetrisers and Specht modules produces the same rank-two classification this unit derives from root systems and Cartan integers, with the Schur polynomial $s_\lambda(x_1, x_2, x_3)$ on the symmetric-function side equal to the character of $V_{a, b}$ on the Lie-algebraic side. The agreement of the two derivations — partition-combinatorial via tensor powers, Cartan-integral via highest weights — is one of the cleanest concrete instances of the Schur-Weyl bridge.

- **Casimir element [07.06.10].** Direct anchor. The dedicated Casimir unit develops the general construction $C = X_a X^a \in U(\mathfrak{g})$, the centrality proof via the invariance of the Killing form, and the eigenvalue formula $c_{V_\lambda} = \langle \lambda, \lambda + 2\rho\rangle$ in arbitrary rank. The present unit is the canonical rank-two worked example: with $\lambda = a \lambda_1 + b \lambda_2$ and $\rho = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$, the quadratic Casimir acts on $V_{a, b}$ by $\langle \lambda, \lambda + 2\rho\rangle$, which on the adjoint $V_{1, 1}$ specialises to the scalar $6$. Unlike the rank-one case, the quadratic Casimir alone no longer separates all irreducibles: the joint spectrum of the quadratic and cubic Casimirs (the two generators of the Harish-Chandra centre $Z(U(\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}))$) gives the full infinitesimal character $\chi_\lambda$, with the cubic Casimir as the second generator predicted by the general Harish-Chandra theorem $Z(U(\mathfrak{g})) \cong S(\mathfrak{h})^W$ in rank two. The Casimir-centrality proof we run in this unit is a direct instance of the general invariance-of-Killing-form argument given in [07.06.10].

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The representation theory of $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ entered the mathematical record as the rank-two case of Wilhelm Killing's 1888-90 *Mathematische Annalen* series *Die Zusammensetzung der stetigen endlichen Transformations-Gruppen* (vols 31, 33, 34, 36) [ref: TODO_REF], where Killing identified the type-$A$ series and labelled the rank-two simply-laced case as $A_2$. Killing's classification was incomplete and had errors that Élie Cartan corrected in his 1894 doctoral thesis *Sur la structure des groupes de transformations finis et continus* (Faculté des Sciences de Paris) [ref: TODO_REF], where the modern $A_n$ family of simple Lie algebras was given with $\mathfrak{sl}_3 = A_2$ as the rank-two base case. Cartan's thesis already contained the essential structural data: the Cartan subalgebra of dimension two, the root system $A_2$ with six roots arranged hexagonally, the Cartan matrix, and the simple-root labelling. The thesis did not contain the full theory of finite-dimensional representations — that came later.

The representation theory itself was developed by Hermann Weyl in the 1925-26 papers *Theorie der Darstellung kontinuierlicher halb-einfacher Gruppen durch lineare Transformationen* (Math. Z. 23, 271-309 and Math. Z. 24, 328-376, 377-395, 789-791) [ref: TODO_REF], establishing the character formula and the dimension formula in full generality. For $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ specifically, the dimension formula $\dim V_{a, b} = (a + 1)(b + 1)(a + b + 2)/2$ is the rank-two specialisation, and Weyl computed it explicitly as an example in his original papers. The unitarian-trick proof of complete reducibility applies in this case via the compact real form $\mathfrak{su}(3) \subseteq \mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$, whose Lie group $\mathrm{SU}(3)$ is connected, simply connected, and compact, giving an averaging argument over Haar measure to produce invariant Hermitian inner products. The Cartan-Eilenberg 1948 *Annals of Math.* paper *Cohomology theory of Lie groups and Lie algebras* [ref: TODO_REF] situated this representation theory inside a broader cohomological framework.

The multiplicity-counting machinery for weight spaces — including the multiplicity-two zero weight of the adjoint representation — was put on a closed-form recursive footing by Hans Freudenthal in *Zur Berechnung der Charaktere der halbeinfachen Lieschen Gruppen* (*Indagationes Mathematicae* 16, 369-376, 1954) [ref: TODO_REF]. The Freudenthal multiplicity formula gives a recursive identity computing $m_\mu$ from the multiplicities of weights strictly above $\mu$ in the partial order, and the rank-two specialisation gives an effective algorithm for the multiplicities in $V_{a, b}$. An alternative derivation, organising the character as an inclusion-exclusion over the Weyl group, was given by Robert Steinberg in *A general Clebsch-Gordan theorem* (*Trans. Amer. Math. Soc.* 100, 392-400, 1961) [ref: TODO_REF], which yields the same hexagonal weight structure via a different combinatorial route.

The physics-mathematics dictionary places $\mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ at the centre of twentieth-century particle physics. Murray Gell-Mann's and Yuval Ne'eman's 1961 *eightfold way* identified the eight observed light mesons and eight light baryons with the adjoint representation $V_{1, 1}$ of $\mathfrak{su}(3) = \mathfrak{sl}_3 \mathbb{C}$ acting on $V \otimes V^* = V_{1, 1} \oplus \mathbb{C}$, and the three quark flavours (up, down, strange) with the fundamental $V = \mathbb{C}^3$. The Cabibbo-Kobayashi-Maskawa mixing matrix and the eight gluons of quantum chromodynamics are direct manifestations of $\mathfrak{su}(3)$ representation theory in the Standard Model of particle physics. The hexagonal weight diagram of $V_{1, 1}$ became iconic as the diagrammatic record of the eight pseudoscalar mesons, with the multiplicity-two zero weight corresponding to the $\eta$ and $\pi^0$ mesons sharing the same quantum numbers. The mathematical and physical pictures are the same picture.
