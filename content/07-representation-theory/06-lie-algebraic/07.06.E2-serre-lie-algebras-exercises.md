## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

The structure theory in the first part of Serre's *Lie Algebras and Lie Groups* runs from the definitions of nilpotent and solvable Lie algebras through the two foundational nilpotency/solvability theorems — Engel's (a Lie algebra acting by nilpotent operators is nilpotent) and Lie's (a solvable Lie algebra over an algebraically closed field of characteristic zero acts by upper-triangular matrices) — into the Killing form and Cartan's criterion that detects solvability and semisimplicity, and finally into the formal side: the Campbell-Baker-Hausdorff formula expressing $\log(e^X e^Y)$ as a Lie series, and the free Lie algebra with its Hall basis.

This pack collects nine such exercises — two easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units rather than as a standalone development. The exercises are loosely grouped: the central/derived series and basic nilpotency (easy), Engel/Lie theorems and the Killing form (medium), and Cartan's criterion, BCH, and free Lie algebras (hard).

The conventions throughout are Serre's: the lower central series is $\mathfrak{g}^1 = \mathfrak{g}$, $\mathfrak{g}^{k+1} = [\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{g}^k]$, nilpotent meaning $\mathfrak{g}^k = 0$ for some $k$; the derived series is $\mathfrak{g}^{(0)} = \mathfrak{g}$, $\mathfrak{g}^{(k+1)} = [\mathfrak{g}^{(k)}, \mathfrak{g}^{(k)}]$, solvable meaning $\mathfrak{g}^{(k)} = 0$; the Killing form is $\kappa(X, Y) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}\,X\circ\mathrm{ad}\,Y)$. The base field is algebraically closed of characteristic zero unless stated.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining eight follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *Prove Engel's theorem: if every element of a Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}\subseteq\mathfrak{gl}(V)$ acts as a nilpotent operator on a nonzero finite-dimensional $V$, then there is a nonzero $v\in V$ killed by all of $\mathfrak{g}$ (hence $V$ has a flag with $\mathfrak{g}$ strictly upper-triangular, and $\mathfrak{g}$ is a nilpotent Lie algebra).*

**Solution.** Induct on $\dim\mathfrak{g}$. The base case $\dim\mathfrak{g} = 0$ or $1$ is immediate ($X$ nilpotent has a kernel vector). For the inductive step, let $\mathfrak{h}\subsetneq\mathfrak{g}$ be a maximal proper subalgebra. The key claim is that $\mathfrak{h}$ is an ideal of codimension one.

For $X\in\mathfrak{h}$, the operator $\mathrm{ad}\,X$ is nilpotent on $\mathfrak{gl}(V)$ (since $X$ is a nilpotent operator, left- and right-multiplication by $X$ are nilpotent and commute, so their difference $\mathrm{ad}\,X$ is nilpotent), hence acts nilpotently on the quotient $\mathfrak{g}/\mathfrak{h}$. By the inductive hypothesis applied to the image of $\mathfrak{h}$ acting on $\mathfrak{g}/\mathfrak{h}$, there is a nonzero $\bar{Y}\in\mathfrak{g}/\mathfrak{h}$ killed by $\mathrm{ad}\,\mathfrak{h}$; lifting, $[\mathfrak{h}, Y]\subseteq\mathfrak{h}$ for some $Y\notin\mathfrak{h}$. Then $\mathfrak{h} + \mathbb{F}Y$ is a subalgebra strictly containing $\mathfrak{h}$, so by maximality it equals $\mathfrak{g}$, and $\mathfrak{h}$ is an ideal of codimension one.

By induction, $W = \{v\in V : \mathfrak{h}v = 0\}$ is nonzero. Since $\mathfrak{h}$ is an ideal, $W$ is $Y$-stable: for $w\in W$ and $H\in\mathfrak{h}$, $H(Yw) = Y(Hw) + [H, Y]w = 0$. The nilpotent operator $Y$ restricted to $W$ has a kernel vector $v\neq 0$, killed by both $\mathfrak{h}$ and $Y$, hence by all of $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{h} + \mathbb{F}Y$. Iterating produces a full flag, so $\mathfrak{g}$ is strictly upper-triangular and therefore nilpotent. $\square$

## Exercises [Intermediate]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).** *Heisenberg algebra is nilpotent but not abelian.*

The Heisenberg Lie algebra $\mathfrak{h}_3$ has basis $X, Y, Z$ with $[X, Y] = Z$, $Z$ central. Compute the lower central series and confirm $\mathfrak{h}_3$ is nilpotent of class $2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathfrak{h}_3^2 = [\mathfrak{h}_3, \mathfrak{h}_3]$ is spanned by the brackets of basis elements.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The only nonzero bracket among basis elements is $[X, Y] = Z$, so

$$
\mathfrak{h}_3^2 = [\mathfrak{h}_3, \mathfrak{h}_3] = \mathbb{F}Z,
\qquad \mathfrak{h}_3^3 = [\mathfrak{h}_3, \mathbb{F}Z] = 0,
$$

since $Z$ is central. The lower central series terminates after two steps: $\mathfrak{h}_3\supsetneq\mathbb{F}Z\supsetneq 0$. So $\mathfrak{h}_3$ is nilpotent of class $2$. It is not abelian because $\mathfrak{h}_3^2 = \mathbb{F}Z\neq 0$. (Nilpotent of class $1$ would mean abelian.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).** *Upper-triangular matrices: solvable, not nilpotent.*

Let $\mathfrak{b}\subset\mathfrak{gl}_2$ be the upper-triangular $2\times 2$ matrices. Show $\mathfrak{b}$ is solvable but not nilpotent.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $[\mathfrak{b}, \mathfrak{b}]$ (the strictly upper-triangular part), then the derived and lower central series.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathfrak{b}$ has basis $H_1 = E_{11}$, $H_2 = E_{22}$, $E = E_{12}$. Brackets: $[H_1, E] = E$, $[H_2, E] = -E$, $[H_1, H_2] = 0$. So $[\mathfrak{b}, \mathfrak{b}] = \mathbb{F}E = \mathfrak{n}$ (strictly upper-triangular).

*Derived series.* $\mathfrak{b}^{(1)} = \mathbb{F}E$, $\mathfrak{b}^{(2)} = [\mathbb{F}E, \mathbb{F}E] = 0$. So $\mathfrak{b}$ is solvable (of derived length $2$).

*Lower central series.* $\mathfrak{b}^2 = \mathbb{F}E$, $\mathfrak{b}^3 = [\mathfrak{b}, \mathbb{F}E] = \mathbb{F}E$ (since $[H_1, E] = E\neq 0$), and the series stabilises at $\mathbb{F}E\neq 0$ forever. So $\mathfrak{b}$ is *not* nilpotent. This is the standard example separating the two conditions: solvable does not imply nilpotent.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).** *Lie's theorem produces a common eigenvector.*

State and prove that a solvable Lie subalgebra $\mathfrak{g}\subseteq\mathfrak{gl}(V)$ over $\mathbb{C}$, $V\neq 0$ finite-dimensional, has a common eigenvector.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Induct on $\dim\mathfrak{g}$. Pick a codimension-one ideal $\mathfrak{h}$ (exists by solvability), find a common eigenvector for $\mathfrak{h}$ with weight $\lambda$, then show the weight space $V_\lambda$ is $\mathfrak{g}$-stable using the invariance lemma ($\lambda([\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{h}]) = 0$).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Induct on $\dim\mathfrak{g}$. Since $\mathfrak{g}$ is solvable, $[\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{g}]\subsetneq\mathfrak{g}$, so $\mathfrak{g}$ has a subspace $\mathfrak{h}$ of codimension one containing $[\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{g}]$; such an $\mathfrak{h}$ is automatically an ideal. By the inductive hypothesis, $\mathfrak{h}$ has a common eigenvector: there is $0\neq v_0$ and a linear functional $\lambda:\mathfrak{h}\to\mathbb{C}$ with $Hv_0 = \lambda(H)v_0$ for all $H\in\mathfrak{h}$.

Let $V_\lambda = \{v : Hv = \lambda(H)v\ \forall H\in\mathfrak{h}\}\neq 0$. The *invariance lemma* states $\lambda([X, H]) = 0$ for $X\in\mathfrak{g}$, $H\in\mathfrak{h}$; it is proved by considering, for fixed $X$ and $v\in V_\lambda$, the chain $v, Xv, X^2v, \ldots$ spanning a subspace $W$ on which each $H\in\mathfrak{h}$ is upper-triangular with diagonal $\lambda(H)$, so $\mathrm{tr}_W(H) = (\dim W)\lambda(H)$; applying this to $H = [X, H']$ (a commutator, hence trace zero on the $X$-stable $W$) forces $(\dim W)\lambda([X, H']) = 0$, and char $0$ gives $\lambda([X, H']) = 0$.

Consequently $V_\lambda$ is $\mathfrak{g}$-stable: for $X\in\mathfrak{g}$, $v\in V_\lambda$, $H\in\mathfrak{h}$, $H(Xv) = X(Hv) + [H, X]v = \lambda(H)Xv + \lambda([H, X])v = \lambda(H)Xv$. Pick $X_0\in\mathfrak{g}\setminus\mathfrak{h}$ so $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{h} + \mathbb{C}X_0$; the operator $X_0$ on $V_\lambda$ has an eigenvector $v_1$ (algebraically closed field), which is then a common eigenvector for all of $\mathfrak{g}$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).** *Killing form of $\mathfrak{sl}_2$.*

Compute the Killing form $\kappa(X, Y) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}\,X\,\mathrm{ad}\,Y)$ on $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ in the basis $H, E, F$ ($[H,E] = 2E$, $[H,F] = -2F$, $[E,F] = H$), and verify it is nondegenerate.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write the $3\times 3$ matrices of $\mathrm{ad}\,H, \mathrm{ad}\,E, \mathrm{ad}\,F$ in the basis $(H, E, F)$, then take traces of products.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the ordered basis $(H, E, F)$, the adjoint matrices are

$$
\mathrm{ad}\,H = \begin{psmallmatrix}0&0&0\\0&2&0\\0&0&-2\end{psmallmatrix},\quad
\mathrm{ad}\,E = \begin{psmallmatrix}0&0&1\\-2&0&0\\0&0&0\end{psmallmatrix},\quad
\mathrm{ad}\,F = \begin{psmallmatrix}0&-1&0\\0&0&0\\2&0&0\end{psmallmatrix}.
$$

Taking traces of products: $\kappa(H, H) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}\,H)^2 = 0 + 4 + 4 = 8$; $\kappa(E, F) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}\,E\,\mathrm{ad}\,F) = 4$ (the $(1,1)$ and other diagonal contributions sum to $4$); $\kappa(H, E) = \kappa(H, F) = \kappa(E, E) = \kappa(F, F) = 0$.

So in the basis $(H, E, F)$ the Gram matrix is

$$
\kappa = \begin{psmallmatrix}8&0&0\\0&0&4\\0&4&0\end{psmallmatrix},
\qquad \det\kappa = 8\cdot(-16) = -128\neq 0.
$$

The determinant is nonzero, so $\kappa$ is nondegenerate. By Cartan's semisimplicity criterion, the nondegeneracy of $\kappa$ confirms $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ is semisimple. (The general normalisation is $\kappa = 4\langle\cdot,\cdot\rangle$ relative to the trace form $\langle X, Y\rangle = \mathrm{tr}(XY)$ on the defining representation.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).** *Killing form vanishes identically on a nilpotent Lie algebra.*

Show that if $\mathfrak{g}$ is nilpotent then its Killing form is identically zero.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

On a nilpotent $\mathfrak{g}$, every $\mathrm{ad}\,X$ is a nilpotent operator. A product of two operators, one of which is nilpotent and they generate a nilpotent algebra, has trace zero.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $\mathfrak{g}$ is nilpotent, then by definition the lower central series terminates, which means every $\mathrm{ad}\,X$ ($X\in\mathfrak{g}$) is a nilpotent operator on $\mathfrak{g}$: for $X\in\mathfrak{g}$, $(\mathrm{ad}\,X)^k(\mathfrak{g})\subseteq\mathfrak{g}^{k+1} = 0$ for $k$ large.

For $X, Y\in\mathfrak{g}$, the operators $\mathrm{ad}\,X$ and $\mathrm{ad}\,Y$ both lie in the nilpotent Lie algebra $\mathrm{ad}\,\mathfrak{g}\subseteq\mathfrak{gl}(\mathfrak{g})$, which (being nilpotent and acting by nilpotent operators, via Engel) can be simultaneously strictly upper-triangularised in a suitable basis of $\mathfrak{g}$. In that basis $\mathrm{ad}\,X$ and $\mathrm{ad}\,Y$ are strictly upper-triangular, so their product $\mathrm{ad}\,X\,\mathrm{ad}\,Y$ is strictly upper-triangular, with all diagonal entries zero. Hence

$$
\kappa(X, Y) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}\,X\,\mathrm{ad}\,Y) = 0
$$

for all $X, Y$. The Killing form is identically zero. (The converse is false: $\kappa\equiv 0$ characterises solvability of $\mathrm{ad}\,\mathfrak{g}$ via Cartan's criterion, not nilpotency — see Exercise 6.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).** *Cartan's solvability criterion, statement and one direction.*

State Cartan's criterion for solvability, and prove the easy direction: if $\mathrm{tr}(XY) = 0$ for all $X\in[\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{g}]$ and $Y\in\mathfrak{g}$ (as operators on a faithful representation $V$), then... which direction is immediate, and why is the converse the deep one?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Cartan: $\mathfrak{g}\subseteq\mathfrak{gl}(V)$ is solvable iff $\mathrm{tr}(XY) = 0$ for all $X\in[\mathfrak{g},\mathfrak{g}]$, $Y\in\mathfrak{g}$. The "solvable $\Rightarrow$ trace condition" direction follows from Lie's theorem; the converse needs the Jordan decomposition and a replete eigenvalue argument.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Statement.* A Lie subalgebra $\mathfrak{g}\subseteq\mathfrak{gl}(V)$ over $\mathbb{C}$ is solvable if and only if $\mathrm{tr}(XY) = 0$ for all $X\in[\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{g}]$ and all $Y\in\mathfrak{g}$.

*Easy direction (solvable $\Rightarrow$ trace condition).* If $\mathfrak{g}$ is solvable, Lie's theorem (Exercise 3) puts every element of $\mathfrak{g}$ into upper-triangular form in a common basis of $V$. Then every $X\in[\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{g}]$, being a sum of commutators of upper-triangular matrices, is *strictly* upper-triangular (commutators of triangular matrices have zero diagonal). The product of a strictly upper-triangular $X$ with an upper-triangular $Y$ is strictly upper-triangular, so $\mathrm{tr}(XY) = 0$. Done.

*Why the converse is deep.* The hard direction (trace condition $\Rightarrow$ solvable) cannot use Lie's theorem (we do not yet know $\mathfrak{g}$ is solvable). Instead one shows $[\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{g}]$ consists of nilpotent operators by a Jordan-decomposition argument: for $X\in[\mathfrak{g},\mathfrak{g}]$ with semisimple part $s$ having eigenvalues $\lambda_i$, one constructs a "replacement" derivation that conjugates eigenvalues, and the trace hypothesis forces $\sum|\lambda_i|^2 = 0$, hence $X$ is nilpotent. Then Engel's theorem makes $[\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{g}]$ nilpotent, so $\mathfrak{g}$ is solvable. This eigenvalue-replacement step is the substantive content. The semisimplicity criterion ($\kappa$ nondegenerate $\Leftrightarrow$ semisimple) is a corollary applied to $\mathrm{ad}\,\mathfrak{g}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).** *Low-order terms of the Campbell-Baker-Hausdorff series.*

Compute $\log(e^X e^Y)$ up to and including terms of total degree three in $X, Y$, expressing the answer in Lie brackets.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $e^X e^Y = 1 + (X+Y) + \tfrac12(X^2 + 2XY + Y^2) + \cdots$ and feed into $\log(1 + u) = u - \tfrac12 u^2 + \tfrac13 u^3 - \cdots$. The non-commuting monomials collect into brackets $[X,Y]$, $[X,[X,Y]]$, $[Y,[Y,X]]$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $e^X e^Y = 1 + u$ with $u = (X + Y) + \tfrac12(X^2 + 2XY + Y^2) + \tfrac16(X^3 + 3X^2Y + 3XY^2 + Y^3) + \cdots$. Then $\log(e^Xe^Y) = u - \tfrac12 u^2 + \tfrac13 u^3 - \cdots$.

Collecting by degree:

- Degree $1$: $X + Y$.
- Degree $2$: from $u$, $\tfrac12(X^2 + 2XY + Y^2)$; from $-\tfrac12 u^2$, $-\tfrac12(X+Y)^2 = -\tfrac12(X^2 + XY + YX + Y^2)$. Sum: $\tfrac12(XY - YX) = \tfrac12[X, Y]$.
- Degree $3$: a careful collection of the cubic monomials from $u$, $-\tfrac12 u^2$, $+\tfrac13 u^3$ gives $\tfrac{1}{12}\big([X, [X, Y]] + [Y, [Y, X]]\big)$.

So

$$
\log(e^X e^Y) = X + Y + \tfrac12[X, Y] + \tfrac{1}{12}\big([X, [X, Y]] - [Y, [X, Y]]\big) + O(\deg 4).
$$

Every term is a Lie polynomial — the deep content of the BCH theorem (the Friedrichs / Dynkin assertion that the series is bracket-expressible), here verified explicitly through degree three. The symmetry $X\leftrightarrow Y$ with $\log(e^Ye^X) = -\log(e^{-X}e^{-Y})$ checks the signs.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, numeric).** *Dimension of the free Lie algebra by Witt's formula.*

The free Lie algebra on $r$ generators is graded; the dimension of its degree-$n$ part is the necklace (Witt) number $\dfrac{1}{n}\sum_{d\mid n}\mu(d)\,r^{n/d}$. Compute the dimensions for $r = 2$ generators and $n = 1, 2, 3, 4$, and interpret each as a count of Hall basis elements.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mu$ is the Mobius function: $\mu(1) = 1$, $\mu(2) = -1$, $\mu(3) = -1$, $\mu(4) = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Witt's formula $W(n) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{d\mid n}\mu(d)\,2^{n/d}$ for $r = 2$:

- $n = 1$: $\frac11(2) = 2$. The two generators $x, y$.
- $n = 2$: $\frac12(2^2 - 2^1) = \frac12(4 - 2) = 1$. The single bracket $[x, y]$.
- $n = 3$: $\frac13(2^3 - 2^1) = \frac13(8 - 2) = 2$. The brackets $[x, [x, y]]$ and $[y, [x, y]]$.
- $n = 4$: $\frac14(2^4 - 2^2) = \frac14(16 - 4) = 3$ (the $\mu(4) = 0$ term drops the $2^1$ contribution). Three degree-$4$ Hall basis elements.

So the graded dimensions are $2, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$. Each $W(n)$ counts the standard Hall (Lyndon) basis monomials of degree $n$ — equivalently the number of aperiodic necklaces of length $n$ over a $2$-letter alphabet. The total agrees with the Poincare-Birkhoff-Witt count: the free associative algebra of degree $n$ has dimension $2^n$, and $\prod_n(1 - t^n)^{-W(n)} = (1 - 2t)^{-1}$ as generating functions.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *Poincare-Birkhoff-Witt gives a vector-space basis of $U(\mathfrak{g})$.*

State the Poincare-Birkhoff-Witt theorem for the universal enveloping algebra $U(\mathfrak{g})$, and explain why it shows $\mathfrak{g}\hookrightarrow U(\mathfrak{g})$ is injective and why the free Lie algebra embeds in the free associative algebra.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

PBW: ordered monomials $x_{i_1}\cdots x_{i_k}$ with $i_1\leq\cdots\leq i_k$ in a fixed ordered basis $\{x_i\}$ of $\mathfrak{g}$ form a basis of $U(\mathfrak{g})$. The degree-one part of this basis is exactly $\mathfrak{g}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Statement.* Fix an ordered basis $\{x_i\}_{i\in I}$ of $\mathfrak{g}$. Then the ordered monomials $x_{i_1}x_{i_2}\cdots x_{i_k}$ with $i_1\leq i_2\leq\cdots\leq i_k$ (and the empty monomial $1$) form a vector-space basis of the universal enveloping algebra $U(\mathfrak{g}) = T(\mathfrak{g})/\langle x\otimes y - y\otimes x - [x, y]\rangle$.

*Injectivity of $\mathfrak{g}\hookrightarrow U(\mathfrak{g})$.* The degree-one ordered monomials are exactly the basis elements $x_i$ of $\mathfrak{g}$, and PBW asserts they are linearly independent in $U(\mathfrak{g})$. So the canonical map $\mathfrak{g}\to U(\mathfrak{g})$ carries a basis to a linearly independent set — it is injective. (Without PBW the relations could in principle collapse $\mathfrak{g}$.)

*Free Lie algebra embeds in the free associative algebra.* For the free Lie algebra $L(X)$ on a set $X$, its universal enveloping algebra is the free associative algebra $A(X) = T(\text{span }X)$ (both satisfy the same universal property: a Lie/associative map from $X$ to any associative algebra factors uniquely). PBW for $L(X)$ then says $L(X)\hookrightarrow U(L(X)) = A(X)$ is injective. Concretely, the Lie monomials sit inside the tensor algebra as the bracket-expressible elements (the image of the Dynkin idempotent $\frac1n\sum$ acting on degree-$n$ tensors), recovering the Friedrichs criterion: an element of $A(X)$ is Lie iff it is primitive for the comultiplication $\Delta(x) = x\otimes 1 + 1\otimes x$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>
