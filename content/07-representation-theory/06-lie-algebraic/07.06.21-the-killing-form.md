## Intuition [Beginner]

A Lie algebra is a vector space whose elements can be combined with a bracket. To do geometry on it you want a way to measure lengths and angles, that is, an inner product. The Killing form is the most natural such measurement, and the remarkable thing is that the algebra builds it out of nothing but its own bracket.

Here is the recipe. Each element $X$ of the algebra acts on the whole algebra by bracketing: send $Y$ to $[X,Y]$. That action is a matrix, written $\mathrm{ad}_X$. To pair two elements $X$ and $Y$, multiply their matrices $\mathrm{ad}_X$ and $\mathrm{ad}_Y$ and take the trace of the product. The number you get is $B(X,Y)$, the Killing form.

Why care about a single number attached to each pair? Because its size tells you what kind of algebra you have. If the form is fat and healthy, with no element invisible to it, the algebra is semisimple, the well-behaved case where every representation splits into pieces. If some element is invisible to the form, the algebra has a soft, solvable part. So one bilinear form reads off the deep structure of the whole algebra.

The trace form is the same idea in any representation: instead of the bracket action, use the matrices of any chosen action and take the trace of their product. The Killing form is the special case where the chosen action is the bracket itself.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the Lie algebra as a flat space of arrows. Each arrow $X$ comes with a stirring motion $\mathrm{ad}_X$ that pushes every other arrow around by bracketing. To compare two arrows $X$ and $Y$, run their stirring motions back to back, $\mathrm{ad}_X$ then $\mathrm{ad}_Y$, and record how much the combined motion returns vectors to themselves. That returned-to-itself amount is the trace, and it is the Killing number $B(X,Y)$.

![A flat space of arrows representing a Lie algebra; each arrow X carries a circular stirring motion labelled ad_X. Two arrows X and Y are paired by composing their stirring motions and reading off the trace, producing the Killing number B of X and Y. A shaded region marks the radical: arrows invisible to the form.](/img/the-killing-form-placeholder.svg)

The shaded region in the picture is the radical of the form, the arrows that the Killing form cannot detect. When the shaded region shrinks to a point, the form is non-degenerate and the algebra is semisimple. When the shaded region swallows everything, the bracket action is too weak to measure and the algebra is solvable. The single picture thus encodes Cartan's two criteria at once.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Compute the Killing form of $\mathfrak{sl}_2 \mathbb{C}$ on its standard basis and read off the answer.

Step 1. The basis is $H, E, F$ with $[H,E] = 2E$, $[H,F] = -2F$, $[E,F] = H$. Each element acts on the three-dimensional space spanned by $H, E, F$ through the bracket, giving a $3 \times 3$ matrix.

Step 2. Build $\mathrm{ad}_H$. It sends $H$ to $0$, sends $E$ to $2E$, sends $F$ to $-2F$. So in the basis order $H, E, F$ the matrix is diagonal with entries $0, 2, -2$.

Step 3. Pair $H$ with itself. The product $\mathrm{ad}_H \cdot \mathrm{ad}_H$ is diagonal with entries $0, 4, 4$, and its trace is $8$. So $B(H,H) = 8$.

Step 4. Pair $E$ with $F$. A short computation of $\mathrm{ad}_E$ and $\mathrm{ad}_F$ gives the product trace $B(E,F) = 4$, while $B(E,E) = B(F,F) = 0$ and $B(H,E) = B(H,F) = 0$.

Step 5. Compare with the general formula. For $\mathfrak{sl}_n$ the Killing form is $B(X,Y) = 2n \, \mathrm{tr}(XY)$. With $n = 2$ and the standard $2 \times 2$ matrices, $\mathrm{tr}(H^2) = 2$ gives $B(H,H) = 4 \cdot 2 = 8$, matching Step 3, and $\mathrm{tr}(EF) = 1$ gives $B(E,F) = 4$, matching Step 4.

What this tells us: the form is non-degenerate. Its matrix in the basis $H, E, F$ has determinant $-128$, which is non-zero, so no element is invisible to it. By Cartan's criterion that is exactly the statement that $\mathfrak{sl}_2 \mathbb{C}$ is semisimple.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\mathfrak{g}$ be a finite-dimensional Lie algebra over a field $k$ of characteristic zero, with adjoint action $\mathrm{ad}_X : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{g}$, $\mathrm{ad}_X(Z) = [X, Z]$.

**Definition (Killing form).** The **Killing form** of $\mathfrak{g}$ is the symmetric bilinear form
$$
B(X, Y) = \mathrm{tr}\big(\mathrm{ad}_X \circ \mathrm{ad}_Y\big), \qquad X, Y \in \mathfrak{g}.
$$
Symmetry follows from $\mathrm{tr}(AB) = \mathrm{tr}(BA)$.

**Definition (trace form).** More generally, for any finite-dimensional representation $\rho : \mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{gl}(V)$, the **trace form** of $\rho$ is
$$
B_\rho(X, Y) = \mathrm{tr}\big(\rho(X) \rho(Y)\big), \qquad X, Y \in \mathfrak{g}.
$$
The Killing form is the trace form of the adjoint representation: $B = B_{\mathrm{ad}}$.

**Associativity (invariance).** Every trace form, and in particular the Killing form, is **associative** (also called $\mathrm{ad}$-invariant):
$$
B\big([X, Y], Z\big) = B\big(X, [Y, Z]\big) \qquad \text{for all } X, Y, Z \in \mathfrak{g}.
$$
Equivalently $B(\mathrm{ad}_Y X, Z) + B(X, \mathrm{ad}_Y Z) = 0$: the operator $\mathrm{ad}_Y$ is skew with respect to $B$. This identity is the engine behind the construction of the Casimir element in [07.06.10].

### Radical and Cartan's criteria

The **radical** of $B$ is the subspace
$$
\mathrm{rad}\, B = \{ X \in \mathfrak{g} : B(X, Y) = 0 \text{ for all } Y \in \mathfrak{g} \}.
$$
Associativity forces $\mathrm{rad}\, B$ to be an ideal of $\mathfrak{g}$. Cartan's two criteria, recorded in [07.06.16], are stated through the form:

- **Solvability criterion.** $\mathfrak{g}$ is solvable if and only if $B(X, Y) = 0$ for all $X \in \mathfrak{g}$ and $Y \in [\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{g}]$.
- **Semisimplicity criterion.** $\mathfrak{g}$ is semisimple if and only if $B$ is non-degenerate, that is $\mathrm{rad}\, B = 0$.

### Behaviour on ideals and direct sums

Let $\mathfrak{a} \subseteq \mathfrak{g}$ be an ideal. Because $[\mathfrak{a}, \mathfrak{g}] \subseteq \mathfrak{a}$, the operator $\mathrm{ad}_X$ for $X \in \mathfrak{a}$ maps $\mathfrak{g}$ into $\mathfrak{a}$, and the trace over $\mathfrak{g}$ of $\mathrm{ad}_X \circ \mathrm{ad}_Y$ for $X, Y \in \mathfrak{a}$ equals the trace over $\mathfrak{a}$ alone. So **the Killing form of $\mathfrak{a}$ is the restriction of the Killing form of $\mathfrak{g}$**: $B_{\mathfrak{a}} = B_{\mathfrak{g}}\big|_{\mathfrak{a} \times \mathfrak{a}}$. For a direct sum of ideals $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{g}_1 \oplus \mathfrak{g}_2$, the form is block-diagonal, $B_{\mathfrak{g}} = B_{\mathfrak{g}_1} \oplus B_{\mathfrak{g}_2}$, since $[\mathfrak{g}_1, \mathfrak{g}_2] = 0$ makes the cross terms vanish.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Associativity of the Killing form; Humphreys §5.1).** *Let $\mathfrak{g}$ be a finite-dimensional Lie algebra. For all $X, Y, Z \in \mathfrak{g}$,*
$$
B\big([X, Y], Z\big) = B\big(X, [Y, Z]\big).
$$

**Proof.** Write $\mathrm{ad}$ for the adjoint representation. The defining property of a representation gives $\mathrm{ad}_{[X,Y]} = [\mathrm{ad}_X, \mathrm{ad}_Y] = \mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Y - \mathrm{ad}_Y \mathrm{ad}_X$, an identity in $\mathfrak{gl}(\mathfrak{g})$. Therefore
$$
B([X,Y], Z) = \mathrm{tr}\big(\mathrm{ad}_{[X,Y]} \, \mathrm{ad}_Z\big) = \mathrm{tr}\big((\mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Y - \mathrm{ad}_Y \mathrm{ad}_X)\, \mathrm{ad}_Z\big).
$$
Expand and apply the cyclic property of the trace, $\mathrm{tr}(PQR) = \mathrm{tr}(QRP)$, to the second term:
$$
= \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Y \mathrm{ad}_Z) - \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}_Y \mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Z) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Y \mathrm{ad}_Z) - \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Z \mathrm{ad}_Y).
$$
The right-hand side equals $\mathrm{tr}\big(\mathrm{ad}_X (\mathrm{ad}_Y \mathrm{ad}_Z - \mathrm{ad}_Z \mathrm{ad}_Y)\big) = \mathrm{tr}\big(\mathrm{ad}_X \, \mathrm{ad}_{[Y,Z]}\big) = B(X, [Y,Z])$. The same computation, with $\rho$ in place of $\mathrm{ad}$, proves associativity for every trace form $B_\rho$. $\square$

**Corollary (radical is an ideal).** *The radical of an associative form $B$ is an ideal: if $X \in \mathrm{rad}\, B$ then $[Y, X] \in \mathrm{rad}\, B$ for all $Y$.*

**Proof.** For any $Z \in \mathfrak{g}$, associativity gives $B([Y, X], Z) = -B([X, Y], Z) = -B(X, [Y, Z]) = 0$, since $X \in \mathrm{rad}\, B$. So $[Y, X] \in \mathrm{rad}\, B$. $\square$

**Bridge.** Associativity is the foundational reason every later construction in this chapter works. It builds toward the Casimir element of [07.06.10], where the skew-symmetry $B(\mathrm{ad}_Y X, Z) + B(X, \mathrm{ad}_Y Z) = 0$ is exactly the cancellation that forces the quadratic central element to commute with all of $\mathfrak{g}$; this is exactly the same identity recycled. It generalises the elementary fact that an invariant inner product turns the adjoint action into skew operators, the statement that the structure constants are totally antisymmetric in the Killing basis. The central insight is that one associative non-degenerate form is enough to identify $\mathfrak{g} \cong \mathfrak{g}^*$ as $\mathfrak{g}$-modules, which is the bridge from the abstract bracket to the root-space geometry of [07.06.18]. Putting these together, the Killing form appears again in [07.06.16] as the detector of semisimplicity and in [07.06.10] as the raw material of the Casimir, so the associativity proved here is the single hinge on which both the structure theory and the representation theory of semisimple Lie algebras turn.

**Exercise 3 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that the Killing form of an ideal $\mathfrak{a} \subseteq \mathfrak{g}$ equals the restriction of the Killing form of $\mathfrak{g}$ to $\mathfrak{a} \times \mathfrak{a}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $X \in \mathfrak{a}$, $\mathrm{ad}_X$ maps $\mathfrak{g}$ into $\mathfrak{a}$. Compute the trace over $\mathfrak{g}$ by splitting $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{a} \oplus \mathfrak{c}$ for a complement $\mathfrak{c}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Pick a complement so $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{a} \oplus \mathfrak{c}$ as vector spaces. For $X, Y \in \mathfrak{a}$, the operator $\mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Y$ maps $\mathfrak{g}$ into $[\mathfrak{a}, \mathfrak{g}] \subseteq \mathfrak{a}$, hence kills the $\mathfrak{c}$-block in the trace. In block form $\mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Y$ has the shape $\begin{psmallmatrix} * & * \\ 0 & 0 \end{psmallmatrix}$ relative to $\mathfrak{a} \oplus \mathfrak{c}$, so its trace over $\mathfrak{g}$ equals the trace of the upper-left block, the trace of $\mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Y$ acting on $\mathfrak{a}$ alone. That upper-left block is $\mathrm{ad}^{\mathfrak{a}}_X \mathrm{ad}^{\mathfrak{a}}_Y$, so $B_{\mathfrak{g}}(X, Y) = B_{\mathfrak{a}}(X, Y)$. Rubric: full credit for the block-triangular trace argument.

**Exercise 5 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove that if $\mathfrak{g}$ is semisimple and $\mathfrak{a} \subseteq \mathfrak{g}$ is an ideal, then $\mathfrak{a}^{\perp}$ (its orthogonal complement under the Killing form) is also an ideal and $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{a} \oplus \mathfrak{a}^{\perp}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use associativity to show $\mathfrak{a}^{\perp}$ is an ideal, then use non-degeneracy and the fact that $\mathfrak{a} \cap \mathfrak{a}^{\perp}$ is an ideal on which the Killing form vanishes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

First, $\mathfrak{a}^{\perp}$ is an ideal: for $Y \in \mathfrak{g}$, $X \in \mathfrak{a}^{\perp}$, and $A \in \mathfrak{a}$, associativity gives $B([Y, X], A) = -B(X, [Y, A]) = 0$ because $[Y, A] \in \mathfrak{a}$ and $X \perp \mathfrak{a}$; so $[Y, X] \in \mathfrak{a}^{\perp}$. Next, the intersection $\mathfrak{a} \cap \mathfrak{a}^{\perp}$ is an ideal on which $B$ vanishes identically, hence by Cartan's solvability criterion it is solvable; a solvable ideal of a semisimple algebra is zero. So $\mathfrak{a} \cap \mathfrak{a}^{\perp} = 0$. Rubric: full credit for the ideal property, the vanishing-on-the-intersection argument, and the dimension count. This is the inductive step in the decomposition of a semisimple algebra into simple ideals.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Explicit Killing forms of the classical algebras.** For the matrix algebras in their defining representations the Killing form is a constant multiple of the trace form $\mathrm{tr}(XY)$, with the constant the dual Coxeter number times two. For $\mathfrak{sl}_n = A_{n-1}$ one has $B(X, Y) = 2n \, \mathrm{tr}(XY)$. For $\mathfrak{so}_n = B_{(n-1)/2}, D_{n/2}$ one has $B(X, Y) = (n - 2)\, \mathrm{tr}(XY)$. For $\mathfrak{sp}_{2n} = C_n$ one has $B(X, Y) = (2n + 2)\, \mathrm{tr}(XY)$. Each constant is computed by summing the squares of the eigenvalues of $\mathrm{ad}_H$ for a Cartan element $H$, the contributions of the roots, recovering $2 h^\vee \, \mathrm{tr}(XY)$ with $h^\vee$ the dual Coxeter number.

**Non-degeneracy and the structure theorem.** Cartan's semisimplicity criterion turns the analytic statement "$\det B \neq 0$" into the structural statement "$\mathfrak{g}$ has no non-zero solvable ideal". Combined with Exercise 5, non-degeneracy yields the decomposition of a semisimple Lie algebra into an orthogonal direct sum of simple ideals, each carrying a non-degenerate restricted Killing form. This is the master structural use of the form, recorded in [07.06.16].

**The compact real form and negative-definiteness.** A semisimple complex Lie algebra $\mathfrak{g}$ has a distinguished real form $\mathfrak{u}$, the **compact real form**, on which the Killing form is **negative-definite**: $B(X, X) < 0$ for $X \neq 0$. The negative-definiteness is the algebraic shadow of compactness of the corresponding Lie group, since $-B$ is then an $\mathrm{Ad}$-invariant inner product and integrating it produces a bi-invariant Riemannian metric. This is Weyl's unitarian trick in infinitesimal form: it converts representation-theoretic problems for the complex algebra into questions about a compact group, where averaging over the group is available. The cross-link is to [07.07.05] on the double cover $\mathrm{SU}(2) \to \mathrm{SO}(3)$, the simplest compact real form in action.

**The dual Casimir.** Non-degeneracy of $B$ gives a canonical element $\Omega \in \mathfrak{g} \otimes \mathfrak{g}$, the inverse of $B$ regarded as a map $\mathfrak{g} \to \mathfrak{g}^*$. Its image in the universal enveloping algebra is the **quadratic Casimir** of [07.06.10]. The Killing form is therefore not merely a measuring stick but the literal raw material from which the central Casimir operator is forged; without non-degeneracy the dual element does not exist.

**Synthesis.** The Killing form is the central insight that unifies the structure theory and the representation theory of semisimple Lie algebras: one associative bilinear form, built from the bracket alone, is exactly what is needed to detect semisimplicity, to split the algebra into simple ideals, to pin down the compact real form, and to manufacture the Casimir. This is exactly the dual-basis input that the Casimir construction of [07.06.10] consumes, and it generalises the elementary inner-product geometry of $\mathfrak{su}_2$ to every semisimple algebra at once. The foundational reason these threads bind together is associativity, which makes $\mathrm{ad}$ skew and so identifies $\mathfrak{g}$ with $\mathfrak{g}^*$ as a module; putting these together, the bridge from Cartan's criteria of [07.06.16] to the root-space decomposition of [07.06.18] and onward to Weyl complete reducibility is the single non-degenerate form studied here. The pattern recurs in every later chapter where an invariant form appears, from the trace forms of affine Kac-Moody algebras to the Cartan-Killing metric of symmetric spaces.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Non-degeneracy $\iff$ semisimplicity, the semisimple direction).** *Let $\mathfrak{g}$ be a finite-dimensional Lie algebra over a field of characteristic zero. If the Killing form $B$ is non-degenerate, then $\mathfrak{g}$ is semisimple.*

**Proof.** Suppose $B$ is non-degenerate; we show the solvable radical $\mathrm{Rad}(\mathfrak{g})$ is zero. Let $\mathfrak{a}$ be an abelian ideal of $\mathfrak{g}$ (the last non-zero term of the derived series of $\mathrm{Rad}(\mathfrak{g})$ is such an ideal, so it suffices to rule these out). Take $X \in \mathfrak{a}$ and $Y \in \mathfrak{g}$. The operator $T = \mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Y$ maps $\mathfrak{g}$ into $\mathfrak{a}$ (because $\mathrm{ad}_X$ lands in $\mathfrak{a}$, as $\mathfrak{a}$ is an ideal), and then maps $\mathfrak{a}$ into $[\mathfrak{a}, \mathfrak{a}] = 0$ (because $\mathfrak{a}$ is abelian, and $\mathrm{ad}_Y$ followed by $\mathrm{ad}_X$ on an element already in $\mathfrak{a}$ uses $[\mathfrak{a},\mathfrak{a}]=0$ after re-bracketing). Hence $T^2 = 0$, so $T$ is nilpotent and $\mathrm{tr}(T) = 0$. Therefore $B(X, Y) = \mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Y) = 0$ for all $Y \in \mathfrak{g}$, which places $X$ in $\mathrm{rad}\, B = 0$. So $\mathfrak{a} = 0$, the radical has no abelian ideal in its derived series, forcing $\mathrm{Rad}(\mathfrak{g}) = 0$, and $\mathfrak{g}$ is semisimple. $\square$

**Proposition (Killing form of a direct sum).** *If $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{g}_1 \oplus \mathfrak{g}_2$ is a direct sum of ideals, then $B_{\mathfrak{g}} = B_{\mathfrak{g}_1} \oplus B_{\mathfrak{g}_2}$, and $\mathfrak{g}_1 \perp \mathfrak{g}_2$ under $B_{\mathfrak{g}}$.*

**Proof.** Since $[\mathfrak{g}_1, \mathfrak{g}_2] = 0$, for $X_1 \in \mathfrak{g}_1$ and $X_2 \in \mathfrak{g}_2$ the composite $\mathrm{ad}_{X_1} \mathrm{ad}_{X_2}$ maps $\mathfrak{g}_2 \to 0$ and $\mathfrak{g}_1 \to [\mathfrak{g}_2, \mathfrak{g}_1] = 0$, so it is zero and $B_{\mathfrak{g}}(X_1, X_2) = 0$; the summands are orthogonal. For $X, Y \in \mathfrak{g}_1$, the operator $\mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Y$ kills $\mathfrak{g}_2$ and preserves $\mathfrak{g}_1$, so its trace over $\mathfrak{g}$ equals its trace over $\mathfrak{g}_1$, giving $B_{\mathfrak{g}}(X, Y) = B_{\mathfrak{g}_1}(X, Y)$; symmetrically for $\mathfrak{g}_2$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- Cartan's criterion for solvability and semisimplicity [07.06.16] is the headline application: it restates both criteria through the Killing form, with semisimplicity equivalent to the non-degeneracy proved here and solvability equivalent to the vanishing of $B$ on $\mathfrak{g} \times [\mathfrak{g}, \mathfrak{g}]$.

- The Casimir element [07.06.10] is built directly from the Killing form: non-degeneracy supplies the dual basis $B(X_a, X^b) = \delta_a^b$, and the associativity identity proved here is exactly the cancellation that makes the resulting element central in the universal enveloping algebra.

- The root-space decomposition [07.06.18] uses the restriction of the Killing form to the Cartan subalgebra to identify $\mathfrak{h} \cong \mathfrak{h}^*$ and to convert roots into vectors, so the inner product on the weight lattice that organises all of highest-weight theory is the Killing form in disguise.

- The double cover $\mathrm{SU}(2) \to \mathrm{SO}(3)$ [07.07.05] is the prototype compact real form, the smallest case where the negative-definiteness of the Killing form produces a bi-invariant metric and Weyl's unitarian trick becomes a concrete averaging over a compact group.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Wilhelm Killing introduced the form in his 1888-1890 series *Die Zusammensetzung der stetigen endlichen Transformationsgruppen* [ref: TODO_REF Killing 1888], in the course of attempting to classify all simple Lie algebras. Killing wrote down the characteristic equation of the operator $\mathrm{ad}_X$ and the invariant quadratic form on the structure constants; the symmetric bilinear form $\mathrm{tr}(\mathrm{ad}_X \mathrm{ad}_Y)$ that now bears his name appears there as the second coefficient of that characteristic polynomial. Killing's work was visionary but error-prone, and it was Élie Cartan who, in his 1894 thesis [ref: TODO_REF Cartan 1894], gave the rigorous treatment, proving the non-degeneracy criterion that converts the classification problem into linear algebra. A historical irony preserved in the name is that the "Killing form" was used systematically first by Cartan, and the attribution to Killing was settled only later, partly through Bourbaki's standardisation of the terminology.

The philosophical lesson is that a single invariant, extracted from an algebraic structure by its own internal action, can encode that structure's deepest dichotomy. The Killing form converts the qualitative question "is this algebra well-behaved?" into the quantitative question "is this determinant non-zero?". This move, replacing a structural property by the non-vanishing of a computable invariant, is a recurring strategy across mathematics, and the Killing form is one of its earliest and cleanest instances.
