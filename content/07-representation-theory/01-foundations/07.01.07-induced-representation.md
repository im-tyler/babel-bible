## Intuition [Beginner]

The *induced representation* is a construction that takes a representation $W$ of a subgroup $H \leq G$ and produces a representation of the larger group $G$. The procedure is to "lift" $W$ to $G$ by allowing it to be transported around by all the cosets of $H$ in $G$.

The intuition: think of $W$ as living over the single coset $H$ in $G$. The induced representation creates a copy of $W$ for each coset of $H$, and lets $G$ permute these copies according to how it permutes cosets, mixed with the original $H$-action on each individual copy.

The formula is concrete: $\dim \mathrm{Ind}_H^G W = [G : H] \cdot \dim W$, the index of $H$ in $G$ times the dimension of $W$. This is one of the most powerful constructions in representation theory: it lets you build representations of larger groups from understood representations of subgroups, and it is *adjoint* (in a precise sense) to the *restriction* operation that takes a $G$-representation and forgets down to an $H$-representation. This adjoint relationship is *Frobenius reciprocity* (unit [07.01.08]).

Frobenius introduced the construction in 1898 as part of his investigations into how characters of subgroups relate to characters of the full group. Induction is the foundation of *Mackey theory* (Mackey 1949–52), the theory of $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_p)$-representations, the local Langlands programme, and modern automorphic forms.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take $G = S_3$ and $H = \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} = \{e, (123), (132)\}$, the cyclic subgroup of order 3. The index is $[S_3 : H] = 6/3 = 2$.

Let $W = \mathbb{C}_\omega$ be the 1-dimensional representation of $H$ where the generator $(123)$ acts by $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$. Then $\dim \mathrm{Ind}_H^{S_3} W = 2 \cdot 1 = 2$.

The induced representation has basis indexed by coset representatives $\{e, (12)\}$ — two copies of $W$, one for each coset. The action of $(123) \in S_3$ on the basis vector indexed by the identity coset multiplies by $\omega$ (since $(123) \in H$ acts on $W$ by $\omega$); the action of $(12)$ swaps the cosets.

What this tells us: starting from a 1-dimensional representation of a subgroup of order 3, we built a 2-dimensional representation of $S_3$. By Frobenius reciprocity, this induced representation contains the standard 2-dimensional representation of $S_3$ — and indeed for $\omega \neq 1$ the induced representation is exactly the standard representation. For $\omega = 1$ (the principal representation of $H$), the induced representation contains both the principal and the sign characters of $S_3$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $G$ be a group, $H \leq G$ a subgroup, and $(W, \rho)$ a representation of $H$ over a field $k$. The **induced representation** $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$ is constructed as follows.

**Tensor-product (left-module) construction.** Form the $kH$-bimodule $kG$ (with left $kG$-action and right $kH$-action), and define

$$
\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W := kG \otimes_{kH} W,
$$

where $kG$ is viewed as a right $kH$-module via right multiplication and $W$ as a left $kH$-module via $\rho$. The left $kG$-action on the first factor descends to give the $G$-action on the induced representation.

**Cosets-and-functions construction.** Choose coset representatives $g_1, \ldots, g_r$ for $G/H$ (so $G = \bigsqcup_i g_i H$). Then

$$
\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W \cong \bigoplus_{i=1}^r g_i \otimes W \cong W^{\oplus r}, \qquad r = [G:H],
$$

as $k$-vector spaces. The action of $g \in G$ permutes the summands according to the action of $G$ on $G/H$, with twists by $H$: if $g \cdot g_i H = g_j H$, then $g \cdot g_i = g_j h_{g, i}$ for some $h_{g, i} \in H$, and $g$ sends $g_i \otimes w$ to $g_j \otimes \rho(h_{g, i}) w$.

**Equivalent definitions.**

- *Functions transformation:* $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$ is isomorphic to the space of functions $f : G \to W$ satisfying $f(gh) = \rho(h)^{-1} f(g)$ for all $g \in G$, $h \in H$, with $G$ acting by left translation $(g' \cdot f)(g) := f(g'^{-1} g)$.
- *Adjoint construction:* $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G$ is the left adjoint to the restriction functor $\mathrm{Res}_H^G : \mathrm{Rep}(G) \to \mathrm{Rep}(H)$ — see Frobenius reciprocity, unit [07.01.08].

**Theorem (Character of induced representation, Frobenius 1898).** *For finite $G$ and $H \leq G$, the character of $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$ at $g \in G$ is*

$$
\chi_{\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W}(g) = \frac{1}{|H|} \sum_{x \in G : x^{-1} g x \in H} \chi_W(x^{-1} g x).
$$

*Equivalently, summing over coset representatives,*

$$
\chi_{\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W}(g) = \sum_{i : g_i^{-1} g g_i \in H} \chi_W(g_i^{-1} g g_i),
$$

*where the sum runs over those coset representatives $g_i$ for which $g_i^{-1} g g_i \in H$ [ref: TODO_REF Serre §7.2].*

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Induced character formula).** *Let $G$ be a finite group, $H \leq G$, $W$ an $H$-representation with character $\psi$. Then*

$$
\chi_{\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W}(g) = \frac{1}{|H|} \sum_{x \in G : x^{-1} g x \in H} \psi(x^{-1} g x).
$$

**Proof.** Choose coset representatives $g_1, \ldots, g_r$ for $G/H$, so $G = \bigsqcup_i g_i H$. As a $k$-vector space, $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$ has basis $\{g_i \otimes w_j\}$ where $\{w_j\}$ is a basis of $W$. The $G$-action on $g \cdot (g_i \otimes w_j)$: write $g g_i = g_{\sigma(i)} h_i$ for some permutation $\sigma$ of cosets and some $h_i \in H$. Then

$$
g \cdot (g_i \otimes w_j) = (g g_i) \otimes w_j = (g_{\sigma(i)} h_i) \otimes w_j = g_{\sigma(i)} \otimes \rho(h_i) w_j.
$$

The matrix entry of $g$ on $\{g_i \otimes w_j\}$ takes $(g_i \otimes w_j)$ to $(g_{\sigma(i)} \otimes \rho(h_i) w_j)$. The trace contributes only from indices $i$ where $\sigma(i) = i$, i.e., $g g_i \in g_i H$, equivalently $g_i^{-1} g g_i \in H$. For such $i$, the contribution to the trace is $\mathrm{tr}(\rho(h_i))|_{V} = \psi(h_i) = \psi(g_i^{-1} g g_i)$.

Summing over fixed cosets:

$$
\chi_{\mathrm{Ind}}(g) = \sum_{i : g_i^{-1} g g_i \in H} \psi(g_i^{-1} g g_i).
$$

To convert to the symmetric formula, observe that as $x$ ranges over the coset $g_i H$, the conjugate $x^{-1} g x$ ranges over $H$-conjugates of $g_i^{-1} g g_i$. The class function $\psi$ is invariant on $H$-conjugacy classes, so

$$
\sum_{x \in g_i H} \psi(x^{-1} g x) = |H| \cdot \psi(g_i^{-1} g g_i),
$$

provided $g_i^{-1} g g_i \in H$, and zero otherwise. Summing:

$$
\sum_{x \in G : x^{-1} g x \in H} \psi(x^{-1} g x) = \sum_{i : g_i^{-1} g g_i \in H} |H| \psi(g_i^{-1} g g_i) = |H| \cdot \chi_{\mathrm{Ind}}(g). \quad \square
$$

This induced-character formula is one of the most useful tools in representation theory: it lets you compute characters of induced representations by summing the subgroup-character over conjugates that land back in the subgroup [ref: TODO_REF Frobenius 1898].

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Compute $\dim \mathrm{Ind}_H^{S_4} W$ where $H = A_4 \leq S_4$ and $W$ is the standard 3-dimensional representation of $A_4$.

Index $[S_4 : A_4] = 2$.

</details>

$\dim \mathrm{Ind}_{A_4}^{S_4} W = [S_4 : A_4] \cdot \dim W = 2 \cdot 3 = 6$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).**

Show that induction in stages: $\mathrm{Ind}_K^G \circ \mathrm{Ind}_H^K = \mathrm{Ind}_H^G$ for $H \leq K \leq G$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Both sides are $kG \otimes_{kH} W$ — use associativity of tensor product.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathrm{Ind}_K^G(\mathrm{Ind}_H^K W) = kG \otimes_{kK} (kK \otimes_{kH} W) \cong (kG \otimes_{kK} kK) \otimes_{kH} W \cong kG \otimes_{kH} W = \mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$, using associativity of tensor product and $kG \otimes_{kK} kK \cong kG$. $\square$

This is *induction in stages* and is fundamental in Mackey theory.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).**

Compute the character of $\mathrm{Ind}_{H}^{S_3} \chi_\omega$ where $H = \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ embedded as the alternating subgroup, and $\chi_\omega$ sends the generator $(123)$ to $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the Frobenius induced-character formula at each conjugacy class of $S_3$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Coset representatives: $\{e, (12)\}$. So we sum over $i = 1, 2$ where $g_i^{-1} g g_i \in H$.

At $g = e$: $\chi_{\mathrm{Ind}}(e) = \chi_\omega(e) + \chi_\omega(e) = 1 + 1 = 2$ (both cosets contribute).

At $g = (12)$ (a transposition): $e^{-1} (12) e = (12) \notin H$. $(12)^{-1} (12) (12) = (12) \notin H$. No contribution; $\chi_{\mathrm{Ind}}((12)) = 0$.

At $g = (123)$ (a 3-cycle): $e^{-1} (123) e = (123) \in H$, contributing $\chi_\omega((123)) = \omega$. $(12)^{-1} (123) (12) = (12)(123)(12) = (132) \in H$, contributing $\chi_\omega((132)) = \omega^2$. Total: $\chi_{\mathrm{Ind}}((123)) = \omega + \omega^2 = -1$.

So $\chi_{\mathrm{Ind}} = (2, 0, -1)$ on classes $\{e\}, \{$trans$\}, \{$3-cycle$\}$. This matches the standard representation $\chi_{\mathrm{std}}$ of $S_3$. So $\mathrm{Ind}_H^{S_3} \chi_\omega \cong V_{\mathrm{std}}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Show that $\mathrm{Ind}_{\{e\}}^G \mathbb{C} \cong \mathbb{C}G$ as $G$-representations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Coset representatives of $\{e\}$ in $G$ are the elements of $G$ themselves.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $H = \{e\}$, $kG \otimes_{kH} W = kG \otimes_k W = kG$ (since $W = k = \mathbb{C}$ and $kH = k$). The $G$-action is left multiplication on $kG$ — exactly the regular representation. $\square$

This identifies the regular representation as $\mathrm{Ind}_{\{e\}}^G \mathbb{C}$, the induced representation from the identity subgroup. By Frobenius reciprocity (unit [07.01.08]), every irreducible appears in $\mathrm{Ind}_{\{e\}}^G \mathbb{C}$, recovering the regular-representation decomposition.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Verify that the dimension formula $\dim \mathrm{Ind}_H^G W = [G:H] \cdot \dim W$ follows from the tensor-product construction.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$kG$ is free as a right $kH$-module with rank $[G:H]$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The right $kH$-module structure on $kG$ is free with basis given by coset representatives $\{g_1, \ldots, g_r\}$ of $G/H$: every element of $kG$ is a unique $kH$-linear combination of $g_i$'s on the left. So $kG \cong (kH)^{\oplus r}$ as right $kH$-modules.

Then $kG \otimes_{kH} W \cong (kH)^{\oplus r} \otimes_{kH} W \cong W^{\oplus r}$ as $k$-vector spaces, with $r = [G:H]$. Taking dimensions: $\dim \mathrm{Ind}_H^G W = r \cdot \dim W = [G:H] \cdot \dim W$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

State and prove the *Mackey decomposition formula* for $\mathrm{Res}_K^G \circ \mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$ where $H, K \leq G$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Decompose $G$ into double cosets $K \backslash G / H$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Mackey's formula.** For $H, K \leq G$ and a representation $W$ of $H$,

$$
\mathrm{Res}_K^G \mathrm{Ind}_H^G W \cong \bigoplus_{x \in K \backslash G / H} \mathrm{Ind}_{K \cap xHx^{-1}}^K \mathrm{Res}_{K \cap xHx^{-1}}^{xHx^{-1}} (^x W),
$$

where the sum runs over double-coset representatives $x \in K \backslash G / H$, $^x W$ denotes the $xHx^{-1}$-representation obtained by twisting $W$ via $w \mapsto x w x^{-1}$.

*Proof sketch.* Write $G = \bigsqcup_{x} K x H$ as a disjoint union of double cosets. For each double coset, the corresponding piece of $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W \cong kG \otimes_{kH} W$, restricted to $K$, is itself a $K$-induced representation (from the subgroup $K \cap xHx^{-1}$). Combining gives the formula.

This decomposes the restriction of an induced representation as a sum of induced representations from intersection subgroups, indexed by double cosets. It is the key tool in *Mackey theory* (Mackey 1949–52) and underlies many irreducibility criteria.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

Use the induced-character formula to compute $\langle \chi_{\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W}, \chi_V \rangle_G$ in terms of $\langle \chi_W, \chi_{\mathrm{Res}_H^G V} \rangle_H$ (Frobenius reciprocity at the character level).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute the induced-character formula and reorder the sum.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By definition,

$$
\langle \chi_{\mathrm{Ind} W}, \chi_V \rangle_G = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} \chi_{\mathrm{Ind} W}(g) \overline{\chi_V(g)}.
$$

Substitute the induced-character formula:

$$
= \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_g \frac{1}{|H|} \sum_{x : x^{-1} g x \in H} \chi_W(x^{-1} g x) \overline{\chi_V(g)}.
$$

Change variables: let $h = x^{-1} g x \in H$ (so $g = x h x^{-1}$). As $g$ ranges over $G$ and $x$ ranges over $G$ with $x^{-1} g x \in H$, the pair $(g, x)$ ranges over pairs $(x h x^{-1}, x)$ with $h \in H$, $x \in G$. So the double sum becomes

$$
= \frac{1}{|G| |H|} \sum_{x \in G} \sum_{h \in H} \chi_W(h) \overline{\chi_V(x h x^{-1})}.
$$

Use $\overline{\chi_V(x h x^{-1})} = \overline{\chi_V(h)}$ (class function) and the inner sum becomes

$$
\sum_h \chi_W(h) \overline{\chi_V(h)} = |H| \cdot \langle \chi_W, \chi_{\mathrm{Res} V} \rangle_H.
$$

The outer sum over $x$ is just $|G|$ copies, so the $|G|$'s cancel:

$$
\langle \chi_{\mathrm{Ind} W}, \chi_V \rangle_G = \langle \chi_W, \chi_{\mathrm{Res} V} \rangle_H. \quad \square
$$

This is *Frobenius reciprocity at the character level*. The full categorical adjunction $\mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W, V) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_H(W, \mathrm{Res}_H^G V)$ is unit [07.01.08].

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Mackey's irreducibility criterion (1951).** The induced representation $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$ is irreducible iff for every $g \in G \setminus H$, the conjugate representation $^g W$ on $g H g^{-1}$ is disjoint (no common irreducible constituents) from $W$ on $H \cap g H g^{-1}$. This was the foundational result of Mackey theory (Mackey, *On induced representations of groups*, 1951).

**Brauer's induction theorem (1951).** Every irreducible character of a finite group $G$ over $\mathbb{C}$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of characters $\mathrm{Ind}_E^G \psi$ where $E$ ranges over *elementary subgroups* (subgroups of the form $C \times P$ with $C$ cyclic, $P$ a $p$-group, $\gcd(|C|, p) = 1$) and $\psi$ ranges over 1-dimensional characters of such $E$. This deep theorem powers Artin's conjecture on Artin $L$-functions and the rationality of character values.

**Induced representations of Lie groups.** For a Lie group $G$ with closed subgroup $H$, the induced representation $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$ on a finite-dimensional unitary representation $W$ of $H$ is constructed as smooth sections of the homogeneous vector bundle $G \times_H W \to G/H$. For $G$ semisimple and $H$ a parabolic subgroup, the induced representations are *parabolic induction*, the building blocks of admissible representations of real reductive Lie groups (Harish-Chandra) and of $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_p)$ representations (Bernstein-Zelevinsky).

**Local Langlands correspondence.** The local Langlands correspondence for $\mathrm{GL}_n(F)$ (Harris-Taylor 2001, Henniart 2000) parameterises irreducible admissible representations of $\mathrm{GL}_n(F)$ ($F$ a non-archimedean local field) by $n$-dimensional representations of the Weil-Deligne group of $F$. The match between "induced representation of $\mathrm{GL}_n(F)$ from a parabolic subgroup" and "Galois representation reducible as a sum" is one of the most striking features of the correspondence.

**Frobenius's theorem on Frobenius groups.** A finite group $G$ acting transitively on a set $X$ such that every non-identity element fixes at most one point is called a *Frobenius group*. Frobenius (1901) proved that the elements of $G$ fixing no point, together with the identity, form a normal subgroup. The proof crucially uses induced characters; no proof avoiding character theory has been found.

**Induction and tensor product (Mackey's projection formula).** The *projection formula* relates induction and tensor product:

$$
\mathrm{Ind}_H^G(W \otimes \mathrm{Res}_H^G V) \cong (\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W) \otimes V,
$$

a $G$-equivariant isomorphism for any $H$-representation $W$ and $G$-representation $V$. This is the non-commutative analogue of "factor out what you can": if part of the input is already a $G$-representation, you can pull it out.

## Full proof set [Master]

The induced-character formula is proved in the key-theorem section. Frobenius reciprocity at the character level is proved in Exercise 7 by direct manipulation of characters; the categorical version is in unit [07.01.08]. Induction in stages and the dimension formula are proved in Exercises 2 and 5 respectively. Mackey's decomposition formula (Exercise 6) is the core of Mackey theory; its proof requires double-coset analysis and is in Serre §7.4 or Curtis-Reiner §10. Brauer's induction theorem is one of the deepest results in finite-group character theory; the proof occupies a chapter in Serre, *Linear Representations of Finite Groups* Part III (Chapters 9–10), using the integrality of character values via algebraic-integer arguments. The Lie-theoretic statements (parabolic induction, local Langlands) are stated without proof here; standard references are Knapp *Representation Theory of Semisimple Groups*, Bump *Lie Groups* Ch. 8, and Bushnell-Henniart *The Local Langlands Conjecture for $\mathrm{GL}(2)$*.

## Connections [Master]

- **Group representation [07.01.01]** — induction is one of the four basic operations on representations (alongside direct sum, tensor product, restriction).
- **Frobenius reciprocity [07.01.08]** — induction is left adjoint to restriction; this adjunction is the structural backbone of Mackey theory.
- **Regular representation [07.01.05]** — the regular representation is $\mathrm{Ind}_{\{e\}}^G \mathbb{C}$, the induced representation from the identity subgroup.
- **Character of a representation [07.01.03]** — the induced character formula computes characters explicitly.
- **Tensor product of representations [07.01.06]** — the projection formula connects induction with tensor products via Mackey decomposition.
- **Group [01.02.01]** — induction is a construction depending on the subgroup structure of $G$.
- **Highest weight representation [07.03.01]** — parabolic induction (the Lie-group analogue) is fundamental to the construction of admissible representations.
- **Cartan-Weyl classification [07.04.01]** — induced representations from parabolic subgroups are central to the structure of complex/real reductive Lie group representations.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Ferdinand Frobenius introduced the induced representation in his 1898 paper *Über Relationen zwischen den Charakteren einer Gruppe und denen ihrer Untergruppen* (Sitzungsberichte der königlich preussischen Akademie der Wissenschaften zu Berlin), continuing his investigations of finite-group character theory begun in 1896. The motivation was directly stated in the title: relations between characters of a group $G$ and characters of its subgroups $H \leq G$. Frobenius defined what is now called *Frobenius reciprocity* at the level of characters and gave the explicit induced-character formula proved in the key-theorem section.

The brilliance of Frobenius's construction was that it produced representations of $G$ from any starting point — even a 1-dimensional character of an arbitrarily small subgroup. By varying the subgroup and the input character, one could in principle construct enough representations to span the irreducible representations of $G$. This intuition was made precise much later by Brauer's induction theorem (1951): every irreducible character of $G$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of characters induced from 1-dimensional characters of *elementary subgroups*. The depth of Brauer's theorem comes from the integrality of character values, which Frobenius could not have anticipated in 1898.

George Mackey systematised induced representations in the late 1940s and early 1950s. His three-paper series *On induced representations of groups* (Amer. J. Math. 1949), *Induced representations of locally compact groups, I* (Ann. Math. 1952), and *II* (Ann. Math. 1953) extended the theory from finite groups to locally compact topological groups, providing the framework for Harish-Chandra's classification of admissible representations of real reductive Lie groups (1950s–1970s) and Bernstein-Zelevinsky's classification of admissible representations of $\mathrm{GL}_n$ over a $p$-adic field (1976–77). The local Langlands correspondence — proved for $\mathrm{GL}_n$ by Harris-Taylor (2001) and Henniart (2000), extended to general reductive groups by Fargues-Scholze (2021) — sits at the modern apex of induced-representation theory, parameterising representations of $p$-adic groups by Galois-side data via parabolic induction and Bernstein blocks.
