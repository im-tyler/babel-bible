## Intuition [Beginner]

The *orthogonality relations* are the central computational machinery of character theory: irreducible characters of a finite group, viewed as functions $G \to \mathbb{C}$, behave like an orthonormal basis with respect to a natural inner product. This means you can compute the irreducible decomposition of any representation using nothing but inner products of characters — a concrete arithmetic operation.

The intuition: think of the space of *class functions* on $G$ (functions constant on conjugacy classes) as a finite-dimensional Hermitian inner-product space. The dimension equals the number of conjugacy classes. The remarkable fact is that the irreducible characters form an orthonormal basis of this space, with the inner product given by averaging the product $f_1(g) \overline{f_2(g)}$ over all $g \in G$ and dividing by $|G|$.

So computing how many copies of an irreducible $V_i$ appear inside a representation $V$ is just computing the inner product of $\chi_V$ with $\chi_{V_i}$. There is also a *column orthogonality* relation: a dual pairing computed across irreducibles instead of across group elements.

This was Frobenius's 1896 discovery, refined by his student Schur in 1905 using *Schur's lemma*. The Schur-style derivation is the modern textbook approach.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ has 3 conjugacy classes (each element is its own class) and 3 irreducible 1-dimensional representations: $\chi_k(j) = \omega^{jk}$ where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$ and $k \in \{0, 1, 2\}$.

Verify orthogonality of $\chi_0$ and $\chi_1$:

$$
\langle \chi_0, \chi_1 \rangle = \frac{1}{3}\big(\overline{\chi_1(0)} + \overline{\chi_1(1)} + \overline{\chi_1(2)}\big) = \frac{1}{3}(1 + \overline{\omega} + \overline{\omega^2}) = \frac{1}{3} \cdot 0 = 0
$$

(since $1 + \omega + \omega^2 = 0$ for any nontrivial cube root of unity).

Verify normalisation of $\chi_1$:

$$
\langle \chi_1, \chi_1 \rangle = \frac{1}{3}(|\omega^0|^2 + |\omega|^2 + |\omega^2|^2) = \frac{1}{3}(1 + 1 + 1) = 1.
$$

What this tells us: the three characters are orthonormal as predicted, and any complex-valued class function on $\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ can be expanded uniquely as a $\mathbb{C}$-linear combination of $\chi_0, \chi_1, \chi_2$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $G$ be a finite group. A **class function** on $G$ is a function $f : G \to \mathbb{C}$ that is constant on conjugacy classes: $f(x g x^{-1}) = f(g)$ for all $x, g \in G$. Denote the space of class functions by $\mathrm{Cl}(G) \subset \mathbb{C}^G$.

The **inner product** on $\mathrm{Cl}(G)$ is

$$
\langle f_1, f_2 \rangle_G := \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} f_1(g) \overline{f_2(g)}.
$$

Equivalently, with conjugacy classes $C_1, \ldots, C_r$ and class sizes $|C_i|$,

$$
\langle f_1, f_2 \rangle_G = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{i=1}^r |C_i| \cdot f_1(C_i) \overline{f_2(C_i)}.
$$

**Theorem (Row orthogonality, Schur 1905).** *Let $V_1, V_2$ be irreducible complex representations of $G$. Then*

$$
\langle \chi_{V_1}, \chi_{V_2} \rangle_G = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } V_1 \cong V_2, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}
$$

**Theorem (Column orthogonality).** *For $g, h \in G$,*

$$
\sum_{V \text{ irrep}} \chi_V(g) \overline{\chi_V(h)} = \begin{cases} |C_G(g)| & \text{if } g \sim h \text{ (conjugate)}, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise,} \end{cases}
$$

*where $C_G(g)$ is the centraliser of $g$ in $G$ and $|C_G(g)| = |G|/|\mathrm{Conj}(g)|$ by orbit-stabiliser [ref: TODO_REF Serre §2.3].*

**Corollary (Number of irreducibles).** *The number of inequivalent irreducible complex representations of $G$ equals the number of conjugacy classes of $G$.* The irreducible characters $\{\chi_{V_i}\}$ are orthonormal in $\mathrm{Cl}(G)$ and span it, hence they form a basis of cardinality $\dim_{\mathbb{C}} \mathrm{Cl}(G) = $ (number of conjugacy classes).

**Corollary (Dimension formula).** *For a finite group $G$ over $\mathbb{C}$, $\sum_i (\dim V_i)^2 = |G|$, summed over irreducible representations.*

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (First orthogonality, Schur-style derivation).** *For irreducible complex representations $V_1, V_2$ of a finite group $G$,*

$$
\langle \chi_{V_1}, \chi_{V_2} \rangle_G = \delta_{V_1 \cong V_2}.
$$

**Proof.** Define the *averaging operator* on $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathbb{C}}(V_2, V_1)$:

$$
A : \mathrm{Hom}(V_2, V_1) \to \mathrm{Hom}(V_2, V_1), \qquad A(\varphi) := \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} \rho_1(g) \circ \varphi \circ \rho_2(g)^{-1}.
$$

*Step 1.* The image of $A$ lies in $\mathrm{Hom}_G(V_2, V_1)$, the space of $G$-equivariant maps. Indeed, for $h \in G$,

$$
\rho_1(h) \circ A(\varphi) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_g \rho_1(h) \rho_1(g) \varphi \rho_2(g)^{-1} = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_g \rho_1(hg) \varphi \rho_2(g)^{-1} \rho_2(h)^{-1} \rho_2(h) = A(\varphi) \circ \rho_2(h),
$$

reindexing $g' = hg$ in the sum.

*Step 2.* For $\varphi \in \mathrm{Hom}_G(V_2, V_1)$, $A(\varphi) = \varphi$ since each summand equals $\varphi$.

*Step 3 (Schur's lemma).* By unit [07.01.02], $\mathrm{Hom}_G(V_2, V_1)$ is 0-dimensional if $V_1 \not\cong V_2$ and 1-dimensional (spanned by any chosen isomorphism) if $V_1 \cong V_2$.

*Step 4.* Therefore $A$ is a projection onto a subspace of $\mathrm{Hom}(V_2, V_1)$ of dimension $\delta_{V_1 \cong V_2}$. The trace of $A$ is the dimension of its image:

$$
\mathrm{tr}(A) = \delta_{V_1 \cong V_2}.
$$

*Step 5.* Compute $\mathrm{tr}(A)$ another way. For each $g$, $\varphi \mapsto \rho_1(g) \varphi \rho_2(g)^{-1}$ has trace $\chi_{V_1}(g) \cdot \overline{\chi_{V_2}(g)}$ on $\mathrm{Hom}(V_2, V_1) \cong V_1 \otimes V_2^*$ (using $\chi_{V_2^*}(g) = \overline{\chi_{V_2}(g)}$). Averaging:

$$
\mathrm{tr}(A) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} \chi_{V_1}(g) \overline{\chi_{V_2}(g)} = \langle \chi_{V_1}, \chi_{V_2} \rangle_G.
$$

Equating the two expressions for $\mathrm{tr}(A)$:

$$
\langle \chi_{V_1}, \chi_{V_2} \rangle_G = \delta_{V_1 \cong V_2}. \quad \square
$$

This is the Schur-lemma-based derivation that became standard following Schur's 1905 paper [ref: TODO_REF Schur 1905]. Frobenius's original 1896 proof avoided Schur's lemma (which Schur introduced in 1901) and proceeded by direct manipulation of the regular representation.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward later units of the strand, where the same pattern is taken up at higher structure. The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

The Klein four-group $V_4 = \{e, a, b, c\}$ (with $a^2 = b^2 = c^2 = e$ and $ab = c$) has 4 conjugacy classes (it's abelian) and 4 1-dimensional irreducible representations. Use orthogonality to find $\sum_V (\dim V)^2$.

$\sum_V (\dim V)^2 = 1^2 + 1^2 + 1^2 + 1^2 = 4 = |V_4|$. ✓ Consistent with abelian groups having only 1-dimensional irreducibles, as Schur's lemma predicts.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

Use column orthogonality to compute $\sum_V (\dim V)^2$ for $G$ of order $n$, taking $g = h = e$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\chi_V(e) = \dim V$ and $C_G(e) = G$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $g = h = e$, column orthogonality gives $\sum_V \chi_V(e) \overline{\chi_V(e)} = |C_G(e)| = |G| = n$. Since $\chi_V(e) = \dim V$, this is $\sum_V (\dim V)^2 = n$. So column orthogonality at the identity recovers the dimension formula.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).**

The symmetric group $S_3$ has irreducible characters

| | $e$ (size 1) | trans (size 3) | 3-cycle (size 2) |
|---|---|---|---|
| $\chi_1$ | 1 | 1 | 1 |
| $\chi_{\mathrm{sgn}}$ | 1 | $-1$ | 1 |

Verify $\langle \chi_{\mathrm{std}}, \chi_{\mathrm{std}} \rangle_{S_3} = 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Sum class size $\times$ $|\chi(g)|^2$ over classes, divide by $|G| = 6$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\langle \chi_{\mathrm{std}}, \chi_{\mathrm{std}} \rangle = \frac{1}{6}(1 \cdot 4 + 3 \cdot 0 + 2 \cdot 1) = \frac{6}{6} = 1$. ✓ Confirms $\chi_{\mathrm{std}}$ is the character of an irreducible representation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Show that the irreducible characters span the space of class functions on a finite group $G$ over $\mathbb{C}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Suppose $f$ is a class function with $\langle f, \chi_V \rangle = 0$ for all irreducible $V$; deduce $f = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $f \in \mathrm{Cl}(G)$ satisfies $\langle f, \chi_V \rangle = 0$ for every irreducible $V$. Define the operator $T_V := \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} \overline{f(g)} \rho_V(g)$ on $V$. This is $G$-equivariant: for any $h \in G$, $\rho_V(h) T_V \rho_V(h)^{-1} = T_V$ by the class-function property of $f$ (relabelling the sum).

By Schur, $T_V = \lambda_V \cdot \mathrm{id}_V$ for some scalar. Take traces: $\mathrm{tr}(T_V) = \lambda_V \dim V = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_g \overline{f(g)} \chi_V(g) = \langle \chi_V, f \rangle = 0$. So $\lambda_V = 0$ and $T_V = 0$ for every irreducible $V$.

By complete reducibility (Maschke, unit [07.02.01]), every representation of $G$ is a direct sum of irreducibles, so the operator $\frac{1}{|G|} \sum_g \overline{f(g)} \rho_V(g) = 0$ on every representation. Apply to the regular representation: $\sum_g \overline{f(g)} e_g = 0$ in $\mathbb{C}G$, so $f(g) = 0$ for all $g$. Hence $f = 0$, as required. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Derive the dimension formula $|G| = \sum_V (\dim V)^2$ from the regular representation and orthogonality.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Decompose the regular character using the multiplicity formula.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Recall $\chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(e) = |G|$ and $\chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(g) = 0$ for $g \neq e$. Compute the multiplicity of irreducible $V$ in the regular representation:

$$
n_V = \langle \chi_{\mathrm{reg}}, \chi_V \rangle_G = \frac{1}{|G|} \chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(e) \overline{\chi_V(e)} = \frac{1}{|G|} \cdot |G| \cdot \overline{\dim V} = \dim V.
$$

So the regular representation decomposes as $\mathbb{C}G \cong \bigoplus_V V^{\oplus \dim V}$. Take dimensions:

$$
|G| = \dim \mathbb{C}G = \sum_V (\dim V) \cdot (\dim V) = \sum_V (\dim V)^2. \quad \square
$$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Prove the column orthogonality relation $\sum_V \chi_V(g) \overline{\chi_V(h)} = |C_G(g)| \delta_{[g] = [h]}$ from row orthogonality.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the irreducible characters as an orthonormal basis of $\mathrm{Cl}(G)$ and the indicator class functions $\mathbf{1}_{[g]}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The class functions $\mathbf{1}_{[g]}$ (indicator of the conjugacy class of $g$) are an orthogonal basis of $\mathrm{Cl}(G)$ with $\langle \mathbf{1}_{[g]}, \mathbf{1}_{[g]} \rangle_G = |[g]|/|G|$. So $\mathbf{1}_{[g]} / \sqrt{|[g]|/|G|}$ are orthonormal. Both these and the irreducible characters are orthonormal bases.

The matrix expressing one basis in terms of the other has rows indexed by irreducibles $V$ and columns indexed by conjugacy classes $[g]$, with entry $\chi_V(g) \sqrt{|[g]|/|G|}$. This matrix is unitary (square — same dimension — with orthonormal rows). Unitarity means orthonormal columns:

$$
\sum_V \overline{\chi_V(g)} \chi_V(h) \sqrt{|[g]||[h]|}/|G| = \delta_{[g], [h]}.
$$

Multiplying both sides by $|G| / \sqrt{|[g]||[h]|}$, when $[g] = [h]$, $|[g]| = |[h]| = |G|/|C_G(g)|$, so

$$
\sum_V \overline{\chi_V(g)} \chi_V(g) = |G| / |[g]| = |C_G(g)|. \quad \square
$$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The character table of a finite group $G$ is the $r \times r$ matrix $X = (\chi_{V_i}(g_j))$, where $V_i$ run over irreducibles and $g_j$ over class representatives. State the orthogonality relations as matrix equations, and give the form of $X^{-1}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use diagonal weight matrices for class sizes and conjugacy class structure.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $X$ be the $r \times r$ character table matrix, $D_C = \mathrm{diag}(|C_1|, \ldots, |C_r|)$ the diagonal matrix of class sizes, and $D_G = |G| \cdot I$. Row orthogonality says

$$
\frac{1}{|G|} X D_C X^* = I, \qquad \text{i.e., } X D_C X^* = |G| \cdot I,
$$

where $X^* = \overline{X}^T$. So $X^{-1} = \frac{1}{|G|} D_C X^*$.

Column orthogonality is the dual: $X^* X / |G| \cdot D_C^{-1}$ is the identity, equivalently

$$
X^* X = |G| \cdot D_C^{-1} = \mathrm{diag}(|C_G(g_1)|, \ldots, |C_G(g_r)|),
$$

since $|C_G(g)| = |G|/|[g]|$. So $X X^* = \mathrm{diag}(|G|/|C_i|) \cdot |G|$ as well, recovering row orthogonality.

These matrix relations make character tables algorithmically computable: given any partial table satisfying both orthogonalities and the dimension condition $\sum |\chi_{V_i}(e)|^2 = |G|$, one can often complete the table by linear algebra alone.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**The character table of a group.** Each finite group $G$ has a character table — a square matrix whose rows are irreducible characters and columns are conjugacy classes. The orthogonality relations make this matrix unitary (after rescaling rows and columns by class sizes). Character tables for many small groups are tabulated in *Atlas of Finite Groups* (Conway et al., 1985); the character tables for the sporadic simple groups were a major project of 20th-century group theory.

**Burnside's lemma in character form.** The number of orbits of $G$ on a set $X$ equals $\frac{1}{|G|} \sum_g |\mathrm{Fix}(g, X)|$, which is the inner product $\langle \chi_X, \mathbf{1} \rangle_G$ where $\chi_X$ is the permutation character. Generalised: the number of inequivalent permutation representations of $G$ on $n$ points is computable by Pólya enumeration techniques rooted in character theory.

**Frobenius reciprocity (character form).** For $H \leq G$ and characters $\psi$ of $H$, $\chi$ of $G$,

$$
\langle \mathrm{Ind}_H^G \psi, \chi \rangle_G = \langle \psi, \mathrm{Res}_H^G \chi \rangle_H,
$$

a special case of the adjunction proved in unit [07.01.08]. The character of $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G \psi$ is given by Frobenius's induction formula.

**Mackey's irreducibility criterion.** For an induced representation $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G \psi$ to be irreducible, it suffices that for every $g \in G \setminus H$, the conjugate character $\psi^g$ on $H \cap g^{-1} H g$ is disjoint from $\psi$. Mackey's 1951 paper *On induced representations of groups* developed this systematically.

**$L^2$ orthogonality for compact groups.** For a compact topological group $G$ with normalised Haar measure $\mu$, the orthogonality relations become integrals:

$$
\int_G \chi_V(g) \overline{\chi_W(g)} d\mu(g) = \delta_{V \cong W}.
$$

This is the Peter-Weyl theorem applied to characters: $L^2(G)^G$ (the space of class functions in $L^2(G)$) is the closed Hilbert-space span of irreducible characters, and they form an orthonormal basis. The Weyl integration formula reduces these integrals to integrals over a maximal torus weighted by the Weyl denominator.

**Modular orthogonality (Brauer).** Over fields of characteristic $p$ dividing $|G|$, ordinary orthogonality fails (the inner product loses positive-definiteness on the modular group algebra). Brauer's theory replaces the orthogonality of ordinary characters with relations between *Brauer characters* (defined on $p$-regular elements) and *decomposition matrices* connecting characteristic 0 to characteristic $p$.

**Synthesis.** This construction generalises the pattern fixed in [07.01.03] (character of a representation), with the symmetric data replaced by its skew or twisted analogue. Read in the opposite direction, the construction is dual to the metric story: complements and orthogonality are taken with respect to the bilinear datum of this unit, not a metric, and the resulting category of subobjects is the one the rest of the strand classifies. The central insight is that this datum identifies algebra with geometry: functions become vector fields, subspaces become quotients, and invariants become cohomology classes — and that identification is the engine driving every theorem downstream.

## Full proof set [Master]

The Schur-lemma-based proof of row orthogonality is given in the key-theorem section. Column orthogonality follows from row orthogonality by the unitary-matrix argument in Exercise 6. The completeness of irreducible characters in $\mathrm{Cl}(G)$ (Exercise 4) uses the regular representation and Maschke's theorem [07.02.01]. The dimension formula $|G| = \sum (\dim V)^2$ (Exercise 5) follows from regular-representation decomposition. Frobenius's original 1896 derivation, working without Schur's lemma, used a direct manipulation of the central idempotents in $\mathbb{C}G$; this approach is available in Curtis-Reiner *Representation Theory of Finite Groups and Associative Algebras*. Brauer's theorem for modular characters and Mackey's irreducibility criterion are stated without proof here; the standard references are Serre (Part III), Curtis-Reiner Vol. I, and Navarro *Character Theory and the McKay Conjecture*.

## Connections [Master]

- **Character of a representation [07.01.03]** — orthogonality is the structural backbone making characters a complete invariant.

- **Schur's lemma [07.01.02]** — Schur's lemma directly powers the orthogonality relations via the averaging argument.

- **Frobenius reciprocity [07.01.08]** — orthogonality combined with reciprocity gives the character-level adjunction $\langle \mathrm{Ind} \psi, \chi \rangle_G = \langle \psi, \mathrm{Res} \chi \rangle_H$.

- **Peter-Weyl theorem [07.07.02]** — the $L^2$-orthogonality of characters on compact groups generalises the finite-group statement.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Frobenius derived the orthogonality relations in his 1896 paper *Über die Charaktere endlicher Gruppen* simultaneously with the introduction of characters themselves. His method was direct: working with the *group determinant* problem posed by Dedekind, Frobenius factorised the central idempotents in $\mathbb{C}G$ corresponding to irreducible representations, and the orthogonality relations emerged as algebraic identities between these idempotents. The argument is self-contained but technical; it was published in the *Sitzungsberichte der königlich preussischen Akademie der Wissenschaften zu Berlin*, Frobenius's habitual venue.

Issai Schur, in his 1901 dissertation under Frobenius, proved what is now known as *Schur's lemma* — the dichotomy that an equivariant linear map between irreducible representations is either zero or an isomorphism. In his 1905 paper *Neue Begründung der Theorie der Gruppencharaktere* (Sitzungsberichte), Schur observed that orthogonality could be derived directly from this lemma via an averaging argument. The Schur derivation, presented in the key-theorem section above, is conceptually transparent: orthogonality is the trace of a Schur-projection. This Schur-style derivation has been the standard textbook approach since the early 20th century (Burnside 1911 second edition, Frobenius's lecture notes 1907, Speiser 1923).

The compact-group generalisation came with Hermann Weyl, who in his *Theorie der Darstellung kontinuierlicher halb-einfacher Gruppen* (1925–26) used the Peter-Weyl theorem (proved jointly with Fritz Peter in 1927) to extend orthogonality from finite groups to compact Lie groups, with Haar measure replacing counting measure. Alfréd Haar's 1933 paper *Der Massbegriff in der Theorie der kontinuierlichen Gruppen* secured Haar measure for general compact groups, completing the analytical foundation. Richard Brauer's 1935 papers introduced *modular orthogonality* — the analogue of Frobenius-Schur orthogonality in characteristic $p$ dividing $|G|$, where ordinary orthogonality breaks down and is replaced by relations between Brauer characters and decomposition matrices.
