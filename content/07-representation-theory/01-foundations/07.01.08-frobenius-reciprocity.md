## Intuition [Beginner]

*Frobenius reciprocity* is one of the most influential identities in mathematics: it states that the operations of *induction* (lifting a subgroup-representation to a $G$-representation) and *restriction* (forgetting a $G$-representation down to an $H$-representation) are *adjoint*. Specifically, $G$-equivariant maps from an induced representation match $H$-equivariant maps to a restricted representation:

$$
\mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W, V) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_H(W, \mathrm{Res}_H^G V).
$$

The intuition: induction and restriction are paired, like adjoints in linear algebra. To compute "how many copies of an irreducible $V$ of $G$ appear in the induced $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$", you can equivalently compute "how many copies of $W$ appear inside the restriction of $V$ to $H$." This duality reduces a calculation on the larger group $G$ to a (typically simpler) calculation on the subgroup $H$.

Frobenius proved this for finite groups in 1898, giving in concrete form the inner-product identity $\langle \chi_{\mathrm{Ind} W}, \chi_V \rangle_G = \langle \chi_W, \chi_{\mathrm{Res} V} \rangle_H$. He could not have known that he was discovering an instance of a vastly more general phenomenon: *adjoint functors* in category theory, formalised by Daniel Kan in 1958. Frobenius reciprocity is the *prototype* of all categorical adjunctions in mathematics, predating category theory by 60 years.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take $G = S_3$ and $H = \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} = \{e, (123), (132)\}$. We saw in unit [07.01.07] that the induced representation $\mathrm{Ind}_H^{S_3} \chi_\omega$ from the 1-dimensional character $\chi_\omega$ on $H$ has character $(2, 0, -1)$ on the conjugacy classes $\{e\}, \{$trans$\}, \{$3-cycle$\}$ of $S_3$.

To compute how many copies of the standard 2-dim representation $V_{\mathrm{std}}$ of $S_3$ appear in $\mathrm{Ind}_H^{S_3} \chi_\omega$, Frobenius reciprocity says: instead, compute how many copies of $\chi_\omega$ appear in $\mathrm{Res}_H^{S_3} V_{\mathrm{std}}$.

The restriction of $V_{\mathrm{std}}$ to $H$: looking at the character of $V_{\mathrm{std}}$ on $H$, it takes values $\chi_{V_{\mathrm{std}}}(e) = 2$, $\chi_{V_{\mathrm{std}}}((123)) = -1$, $\chi_{V_{\mathrm{std}}}((132)) = -1$. The 1-dim characters of $H = \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ are $\chi_0 = (1, 1, 1)$, $\chi_\omega = (1, \omega, \omega^2)$, $\chi_{\omega^2} = (1, \omega^2, \omega)$.

Inner product $\langle \chi_{V_{\mathrm{std}}}|_H, \chi_\omega \rangle_H = \frac{1}{3}(2 \cdot 1 + (-1) \overline{\omega} + (-1) \overline{\omega^2}) = \frac{1}{3}(2 - \omega^2 - \omega) = \frac{1}{3}(2 - (-1)) = 1$.

What this tells us: $\chi_\omega$ appears once in $\mathrm{Res}_H V_{\mathrm{std}}$. By Frobenius reciprocity, $V_{\mathrm{std}}$ appears once in $\mathrm{Ind}_H^{S_3} \chi_\omega$. Computing both sides: $\mathrm{Ind}_H^{S_3} \chi_\omega$ has dimension 2 = $\dim V_{\mathrm{std}}$, so the induction is exactly $V_{\mathrm{std}}$, with the multiplicity 1 confirmed.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $G$ be a group, $H \leq G$ a subgroup, $W$ a representation of $H$, and $V$ a representation of $G$, all over a field $k$. The **restriction** $\mathrm{Res}_H^G V$ is $V$ with the $G$-action restricted to $H$; the **induction** $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$ is the $G$-representation $kG \otimes_{kH} W$ (see unit [07.01.07]).

**Theorem (Frobenius reciprocity, Frobenius 1898).** *For any $H$-representation $W$ and $G$-representation $V$, there is a natural isomorphism of $k$-vector spaces*

$$
\mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W, V) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_H(W, \mathrm{Res}_H^G V).
$$

*This is the assertion that the induction functor $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G : \mathrm{Rep}(H) \to \mathrm{Rep}(G)$ is left adjoint to the restriction functor $\mathrm{Res}_H^G : \mathrm{Rep}(G) \to \mathrm{Rep}(H)$.*

**Explicit form of the isomorphism.** A $G$-equivariant map $\Phi : \mathrm{Ind}_H^G W \to V$ is determined by its restriction to the "base copy" $1 \otimes W \subset kG \otimes_{kH} W$, which gives a $k$-linear map $\varphi : W \to V$. The $G$-equivariance of $\Phi$ together with the $H$-equivariance of the embedding $1 \otimes \cdot$ forces $\varphi$ to be $H$-equivariant: $\varphi(\rho_W(h) w) = \rho_V(h) \varphi(w)$ for $h \in H$. Conversely, any $H$-equivariant $\varphi : W \to \mathrm{Res}_H^G V$ extends uniquely to a $G$-equivariant $\Phi : \mathrm{Ind}_H^G W \to V$ via $\Phi(g \otimes w) := \rho_V(g) \varphi(w)$.

**Theorem (Character form, Frobenius 1898).** *For finite $G$ and $H \leq G$, irreducible characters $\psi$ of $H$ and $\chi$ of $G$,*

$$
\langle \chi_{\mathrm{Ind}_H^G \psi}, \chi \rangle_G = \langle \psi, \chi |_H \rangle_H.
$$

*Equivalently, the multiplicity of an irreducible $V$ of $G$ in $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$ equals the multiplicity of $W$ in $\mathrm{Res}_H^G V$ [ref: TODO_REF Serre §7.2].*

**Right adjoint.** The restriction functor $\mathrm{Res}_H^G$ also has a *right* adjoint, the *coinduced* representation

$$
\mathrm{Coind}_H^G W := \mathrm{Hom}_{kH}(kG, W),
$$

with $G$-action by right translation. For finite $G$, $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W \cong \mathrm{Coind}_H^G W$ (the two adjoints agree); this isomorphism is the *self-duality* of finite-group induction. For infinite groups (or in the homological algebra of $kG$-modules), the two diverge in general.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Frobenius reciprocity).** *Let $G$ be a group, $H \leq G$, $W$ an $H$-representation and $V$ a $G$-representation, all over a field $k$. Then there is a natural $k$-linear isomorphism*

$$
\mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W, V) \xrightarrow{\sim} \mathrm{Hom}_H(W, \mathrm{Res}_H^G V).
$$

**Proof.** Define maps in both directions and check they are mutually inverse.

*Forward map $\Phi : \mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W, V) \to \mathrm{Hom}_H(W, V|_H)$.* For $\Psi \in \mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W, V)$, define $\Phi(\Psi) : W \to V$ by $\Phi(\Psi)(w) := \Psi(1 \otimes w)$. We check $H$-equivariance: for $h \in H$,

$$
\Phi(\Psi)(\rho_W(h) w) = \Psi(1 \otimes \rho_W(h) w) = \Psi(h \otimes w) = \rho_V(h) \Psi(1 \otimes w) = \rho_V(h) \Phi(\Psi)(w),
$$

using $1 \otimes \rho_W(h) w = h \otimes w$ in $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W = kG \otimes_{kH} W$ (since the tensor is over $kH$) and the $G$-equivariance of $\Psi$.

*Backward map $\Phi' : \mathrm{Hom}_H(W, V|_H) \to \mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W, V)$.* For $\varphi \in \mathrm{Hom}_H(W, V|_H)$, define $\Phi'(\varphi) : kG \otimes_{kH} W \to V$ by $\Phi'(\varphi)(g \otimes w) := \rho_V(g) \varphi(w)$, extended $k$-linearly.

This is well-defined on the tensor product over $kH$: for $h \in H$,

$$
\Phi'(\varphi)((g h) \otimes w) = \rho_V(gh) \varphi(w) = \rho_V(g) \rho_V(h) \varphi(w) = \rho_V(g) \varphi(\rho_W(h) w) = \Phi'(\varphi)(g \otimes \rho_W(h) w),
$$

using $H$-equivariance of $\varphi$. So $\Phi'$ respects the equivalence $g h \otimes w = g \otimes h w$ in the tensor product.

$G$-equivariance of $\Phi'(\varphi)$: for $g' \in G$,

$$
\Phi'(\varphi)(g' \cdot (g \otimes w)) = \Phi'(\varphi)((g' g) \otimes w) = \rho_V(g' g) \varphi(w) = \rho_V(g') \rho_V(g) \varphi(w) = \rho_V(g') \Phi'(\varphi)(g \otimes w).
$$

*Inverse property.* Compute $\Phi \circ \Phi'$ and $\Phi' \circ \Phi$:

$\Phi(\Phi'(\varphi))(w) = \Phi'(\varphi)(1 \otimes w) = \rho_V(1) \varphi(w) = \varphi(w)$, so $\Phi \circ \Phi' = \mathrm{id}$.

$\Phi'(\Phi(\Psi))(g \otimes w) = \rho_V(g) \Phi(\Psi)(w) = \rho_V(g) \Psi(1 \otimes w) = \Psi(g \otimes w)$ (by $G$-equivariance of $\Psi$), so $\Phi' \circ \Phi = \mathrm{id}$.

Both maps are $k$-linear by construction, and naturality (functoriality in $W$ and $V$) follows from the explicit formulas. $\square$

**Corollary (Character form, finite groups).** *For finite $G$ and complex representations,*

$$
\langle \chi_{\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W}, \chi_V \rangle_G = \dim \mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W, V) = \dim \mathrm{Hom}_H(W, V|_H) = \langle \chi_W, \chi_V|_H \rangle_H.
$$

This is the version Frobenius proved in 1898; the categorical-adjunction formulation became standard only after Eilenberg-Mac Lane (1945) and Kan (1958).

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Use Frobenius reciprocity to compute the multiplicity of an irreducible $V$ of $G$ inside $\mathrm{Ind}_{\{e\}}^G \mathbb{C}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply Frobenius: $\dim \mathrm{Hom}_G(V, \mathrm{Ind}_{\{e\}} \mathbb{C}) = \dim \mathrm{Hom}_{\{e\}}(V, \mathbb{C}) = \dim V$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Frobenius reciprocity, $\mathrm{Hom}_G(V, \mathrm{Ind}_{\{e\}} \mathbb{C}) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\{e\}}(V|_{\{e\}}, \mathbb{C})$. The latter is just the dual space $V^*$, with $\dim = \dim V$. So $V$ appears with multiplicity $\dim V$ in the regular representation $\mathrm{Ind}_{\{e\}}^G \mathbb{C} \cong \mathbb{C}G$, recovering Frobenius's regular-representation theorem.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).**

Show that $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G$ is left adjoint to $\mathrm{Res}_H^G$ (i.e., the adjunction defines a unit and counit).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Unit: $W \to \mathrm{Res}_H^G \mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$ via $w \mapsto 1 \otimes w$. Counit: $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G \mathrm{Res}_H^G V \to V$ via $g \otimes v \mapsto \rho_V(g) v$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Unit.** $\eta_W : W \to (\mathrm{Res}_H^G \mathrm{Ind}_H^G) W = (kG \otimes_{kH} W)|_H$, $\eta_W(w) := 1 \otimes w$. This is $H$-equivariant: $\eta_W(\rho_W(h) w) = 1 \otimes \rho_W(h) w = h \otimes w = \rho_{\mathrm{Ind}}(h)(1 \otimes w) = \rho_{\mathrm{Ind}}(h) \eta_W(w)$.

**Counit.** $\varepsilon_V : (\mathrm{Ind}_H^G \mathrm{Res}_H^G) V = kG \otimes_{kH} V \to V$, $\varepsilon_V(g \otimes v) := \rho_V(g) v$. This is $G$-equivariant: $\varepsilon_V(g'(g \otimes v)) = \varepsilon_V((g' g) \otimes v) = \rho_V(g' g) v = \rho_V(g') \varepsilon_V(g \otimes v)$.

**Triangle identities.** Check $\mathrm{Res}(\varepsilon) \circ \eta_{\mathrm{Res}} = \mathrm{id}$: for $v \in V$, $\eta(v) = 1 \otimes v \in (\mathrm{Ind} \mathrm{Res} V)|_H$, and $\varepsilon(1 \otimes v) = \rho_V(1) v = v$. ✓ The other triangle identity is similar. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).**

For $G = S_3$ and $H = \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ embedded as the alternating subgroup $A_3$, compute the multiplicity of $V_{\mathrm{std}}$ (the 2-dim standard representation) in $\mathrm{Ind}_H^{S_3} \chi_0$, where $\chi_0$ is the principal character of $H$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use Frobenius: multiplicity = $\langle \chi_0, \chi_{V_{\mathrm{std}}}|_H \rangle_H$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\chi_{V_{\mathrm{std}}}|_H = (2, -1, -1)$ on $H = \{e, (123), (132)\}$. $\chi_0 = (1, 1, 1)$.

$\langle \chi_0, \chi_{V_{\mathrm{std}}}|_H \rangle_H = \frac{1}{3}(2 \cdot 1 + (-1) \cdot 1 + (-1) \cdot 1) = \frac{1}{3}(2 - 1 - 1) = 0$.

So $V_{\mathrm{std}}$ does *not* appear in $\mathrm{Ind}_H^{S_3} \chi_0$. Indeed, $\mathrm{Ind}_H^{S_3} \chi_0 \cong \chi_1 \oplus \chi_{\mathrm{sgn}}$ (the principal and sign characters of $S_3$), of total dimension 2 = $[S_3:H] \cdot 1$. ✓

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Use Frobenius reciprocity to show that $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G$ takes irreducibles to (possibly reducible) representations, but $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W = 0$ implies $W = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

If $\mathrm{Ind} W = 0$, then $\mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind} W, V) = 0$ for all $V$. Apply Frobenius.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W = 0$, then for every $G$-representation $V$, $\mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind} W, V) = 0$. By Frobenius reciprocity, $\mathrm{Hom}_H(W, V|_H) = 0$ for every $G$-representation $V$.

Take $V = \mathrm{Ind}_H^G W = 0$ — this is empty. Take instead any $V$ such that $W$ embeds in $V|_H$ (e.g., the regular representation of $G$, which restricted to $H$ contains every irreducible $H$-representation, by the regular-representation argument applied to $H$). Then $\mathrm{Hom}_H(W, V|_H) \neq 0$ unless $W = 0$. So $W = 0$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Verify the *projection formula*: for any $G$-representation $V$ and $H$-representation $W$,

$$
\mathrm{Ind}_H^G(W \otimes \mathrm{Res}_H^G V) \cong \mathrm{Ind}_H^G(W) \otimes V.
$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the tensor-product description of induction and the fact that $V$ is already a $G$-representation, so the action commutes with tensoring.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In the tensor-product description, $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G(W \otimes \mathrm{Res} V) = kG \otimes_{kH} (W \otimes V)$ where $V$ is viewed as $H$-representation by restriction. The map

$$
kG \otimes_{kH} (W \otimes V) \to (kG \otimes_{kH} W) \otimes V, \qquad g \otimes (w \otimes v) \mapsto (g \otimes w) \otimes \rho_V(g) v,
$$

is well-defined on the tensor product over $kH$ (using $H$-equivariance of $V$ as an $H$-subrepresentation) and is $G$-equivariant. The inverse map sends $(g \otimes w) \otimes v$ to $g \otimes (w \otimes \rho_V(g)^{-1} v)$. $\square$

The projection formula is the *Mackey projection formula* and is fundamental in Mackey theory and induced representations of Lie groups.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Show that the regular representation decomposition $\mathbb{C}G = \bigoplus_V V^{\oplus \dim V}$ is a consequence of Frobenius reciprocity and the dimension formula $\dim \mathrm{Hom}_G(V, V) = 1$ for irreducible $V$ over $\mathbb{C}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathbb{C}G = \mathrm{Ind}_{\{e\}}^G \mathbb{C}$. Apply Frobenius and Schur's lemma.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathbb{C}G = \mathrm{Ind}_{\{e\}}^G \mathbb{C}$ (Exercise 4 of [07.01.07]). For an irreducible $V$ of $G$,

$$
\dim \mathrm{Hom}_G(V, \mathbb{C}G) = \dim \mathrm{Hom}_{\{e\}}(V, \mathbb{C}) = \dim V^* = \dim V,
$$

by Frobenius reciprocity (with the convention that the multiplicity of $V$ in $\mathbb{C}G$ equals $\dim \mathrm{Hom}_G(V, \mathbb{C}G)$, by Schur's lemma).

So the multiplicity of every irreducible $V$ in $\mathbb{C}G$ is $\dim V$. By Maschke + Schur, $\mathbb{C}G \cong \bigoplus_V V^{\oplus \dim V}$. $\square$

This recovers Frobenius's 1898 theorem on the regular representation purely from Frobenius reciprocity and Schur's lemma.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Explain how Frobenius reciprocity is the prototype of categorical adjunction.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare with the tensor-Hom adjunction, the free-forgetful adjunction, and other classical adjunctions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A pair of functors $(F, G)$ between categories $\mathcal{C}, \mathcal{D}$ is *adjoint* (with $F$ left adjoint to $G$, written $F \dashv G$) if there is a natural isomorphism

$$
\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(F(X), Y) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(X, G(Y)).
$$

Frobenius reciprocity is exactly this with $\mathcal{C} = \mathrm{Rep}(H)$, $\mathcal{D} = \mathrm{Rep}(G)$, $F = \mathrm{Ind}_H^G$, $G = \mathrm{Res}_H^G$.

Other classical adjunctions of the same shape:

- *Tensor-Hom adjunction:* $\mathrm{Hom}_R(M \otimes_S N, P) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_S(N, \mathrm{Hom}_R(M, P))$ for bimodules.
- *Free-forgetful:* $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{Grp}}(F(S), G) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{Set}}(S, U(G))$ where $F$ is the free-group functor and $U$ is the underlying-set functor.
- *Sheafification-forgetful:* $\mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{Sh}}(\widetilde{P}, F) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_{\mathrm{Pre}}(P, U F)$ for presheaves $P$ and sheaves $F$.

The 1958 monograph of Daniel Kan, *Adjoint functors*, formalised the general notion. Frobenius's 1898 special case predated Kan's framework by 60 years; it was understood as a categorical adjunction only retrospectively (Mac Lane *Categories for the Working Mathematician*, 1971). The recognition that Frobenius reciprocity is "the same kind of statement as" the tensor-Hom adjunction unified vast tracts of mathematics under a single conceptual umbrella.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Frobenius reciprocity for compact Lie groups.** For a compact Lie group $G$ with closed subgroup $H$, the same adjunction holds:

$$
\mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W, V) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_H(W, \mathrm{Res}_H^G V),
$$

where $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$ is constructed as smooth sections of the homogeneous vector bundle $G \times_H W$ over $G/H$. The multiplicity of an irreducible $V$ of $G$ in $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W$ equals the multiplicity of $W$ in $V|_H$ — the *branching multiplicity*.

**Branching laws.** For semisimple Lie groups $G \supset H$, the decomposition of $V|_H$ into irreducible $H$-representations is encoded in *branching laws*. For $\mathrm{GL}_n \supset \mathrm{GL}_{n-1}$ (the "Gelfand-Tsetlin" case), the branching is *multiplicity-free*: each irreducible of $H$ appears at most once in $V|_H$, indexed by *Gelfand-Tsetlin patterns*. For $\mathrm{SO}_n \supset \mathrm{SO}_{n-1}$, similar branching laws hold. These are central to Gelfand-Tsetlin bases and the geometry of flag manifolds.

**Iwahori-Matsumoto isomorphism.** For a $p$-adic reductive group $G$ with Iwahori subgroup $I$, the Hecke algebra $H(G, I)$ is isomorphic to a *finite Hecke algebra* (Iwahori-Matsumoto 1965). Frobenius reciprocity relates representations of $G$ with Iwahori-fixed vectors to representations of this finite Hecke algebra, providing a bridge between $p$-adic representation theory and finite Hecke combinatorics.

**Bernstein-Zelevinsky parabolic induction.** For $G = \mathrm{GL}_n(F)$ with parabolic subgroup $P = M N$ (Levi-unipotent factorisation), the induced representation $\mathrm{Ind}_P^G \sigma$ from a representation $\sigma$ of the Levi $M$ (extended to $P$ as the identity on $N$) gives the *parabolically induced representation*. By Frobenius reciprocity, $\mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_P^G \sigma, \pi) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_M(\sigma, \mathrm{Jac}_P \pi)$ where $\mathrm{Jac}_P$ is the *Jacquet functor* (the $N$-coinvariants). The Bernstein-Zelevinsky classification (1976–77) of irreducible admissible representations of $\mathrm{GL}_n(F)$ proceeds by analysing parabolic induction and Jacquet functors via this adjunction.

**Geometric induction.** In *geometric* representation theory (Beilinson-Bernstein localisation, perverse sheaves on flag varieties), the adjunction $\mathrm{Ind} \dashv \mathrm{Res}$ is replaced by a six-functor formalism: pullback and pushforward along maps of flag varieties induce convolution operations on perverse sheaves, with adjunctions implementing Frobenius reciprocity in the geometric setting. Kazhdan-Lusztig polynomials and the Kazhdan-Lusztig conjecture (proved by Beilinson-Bernstein and Brylinski-Kashiwara, 1981) sit at the apex of this geometric Frobenius reciprocity.

## Full proof set [Master]

The categorical proof of Frobenius reciprocity is given in the key-theorem section. The character form follows by taking dimensions. The unit and counit of the adjunction are constructed in Exercise 2; the triangle identities are routine verification. The projection formula (Exercise 5) and its applications to Mackey theory occupy chapters of Curtis-Reiner Vol. I and Serre Part III. Lie-theoretic Frobenius reciprocity for compact groups uses the Peter-Weyl theorem (unit [07.07.02]) and is in Knapp Ch. VIII; the parabolic version for $p$-adic groups is in Bushnell-Henniart and the Bernstein notes. The geometric version (Beilinson-Bernstein localisation) is beyond the scope of this unit; standard references are Hotta-Takeuchi-Tanisaki *D-Modules, Perverse Sheaves, and Representation Theory* and the surveys of Soergel.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Ferdinand Frobenius proved the reciprocity that bears his name in his 1898 paper *Über Relationen zwischen den Charakteren einer Gruppe und denen ihrer Untergruppen* (Sitzungsberichte der königlich preussischen Akademie der Wissenschaften zu Berlin), the same paper that introduced the induced representation construction. Frobenius stated reciprocity in its character form: for finite $G$ and $H \leq G$, irreducible characters $\psi$ of $H$ and $\chi$ of $G$,

$$
\langle \chi_{\mathrm{Ind} \psi}, \chi \rangle_G = \langle \psi, \chi|_H \rangle_H.
$$

This was a striking duality: the multiplicity of $\chi$ inside the induced character of $\psi$ equals the multiplicity of $\psi$ inside the restricted character of $\chi$. Frobenius derived it by direct manipulation of the induced-character formula, without any modern category-theoretic vocabulary.

The structural significance of Frobenius's discovery only became clear in the mid-20th century. Daniel Kan's 1958 paper *Adjoint functors* (Trans. AMS) introduced the general categorical notion, abstracting the pattern shared by tensor-Hom, free-forgetful, sheafification-forgetful, induction-restriction, and dozens of other classical constructions. Saunders Mac Lane's textbook *Categories for the Working Mathematician* (1971) consolidated the categorical perspective and identified Frobenius reciprocity as a paradigmatic example. The recognition that Frobenius's 1898 character identity was a 60-year-old special case of a then-emerging general theory of adjunctions retroactively elevated reciprocity to one of the most important early instances of category-theoretic thinking in mathematics.

The 20th-century extension to Lie groups began with Hermann Weyl (1925–26) for compact Lie groups, where the unitarian trick reduced the analysis to a Peter-Weyl-style decomposition of $L^2(G)$. Harish-Chandra's mid-century classification of admissible representations of real reductive Lie groups (1950s–1970s) and Bernstein-Zelevinsky's 1976–77 papers on $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb{Q}_p)$ both centrally relied on parabolic induction and Frobenius reciprocity in the form $\mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_P \sigma, \pi) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_M(\sigma, \mathrm{Jac}_P \pi)$, with the Jacquet functor as the right-adjoint companion to parabolic induction. The geometric Langlands programme, developed since the 1980s by Drinfeld, Beilinson-Drinfeld, Frenkel-Gaitsgory and others, lifts Frobenius reciprocity to the level of categories of perverse sheaves on flag varieties — the Eisenstein and constant-term functors satisfy a six-functor reciprocity that is a far-reaching geometric analogue of Frobenius's 1898 identity.
