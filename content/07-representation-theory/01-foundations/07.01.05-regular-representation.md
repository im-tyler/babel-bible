## Intuition [Beginner]

The *regular representation* is the natural action of a group $G$ on itself by left multiplication, lifted from a set action to a vector-space action. Form the *group algebra* $\mathbb{C}G$: the vector space with basis $\{e_g : g \in G\}$ indexed by group elements. Then $G$ acts on $\mathbb{C}G$ by sending $e_h$ to $e_{gh}$ — the regular representation $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}$.

Why does this object matter? It is the universal representation in the following sense: every irreducible representation $V$ of $G$ over $\mathbb{C}$ appears inside $\mathbb{C}G$ with multiplicity exactly $\dim V$. This is one of the most beautiful "coincidences" in finite group theory and gives the dimension formula

$$
|G| = \dim \mathbb{C}G = \dim V_1^2 + \dim V_2^2 + \cdots + \dim V_r^2,
$$

where $V_1, \ldots, V_r$ are the irreducible representations of $G$.

Frobenius introduced the regular representation in 1898 as the main computational object of finite-group representation theory. It is where you find every irreducible at once, weighted by dimension. Computations of character tables, character orthogonality, and the central decomposition of $\mathbb{C}G$ as a sum of matrix-algebra blocks all run through the regular representation.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} = \{0, 1, 2\}$ has group algebra $\mathbb{C}[\mathbb{Z}/3] = \mathbb{C} e_0 \oplus \mathbb{C} e_1 \oplus \mathbb{C} e_2$. The regular representation acts:

- $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}(0) = I$ (identity)
- $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}(1) e_0 = e_1$, $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}(1) e_1 = e_2$, $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}(1) e_2 = e_0$ — a 3-cycle on the basis
- $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}(2)$ is the inverse cycle.

The matrix of $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}(1)$ in the basis $(e_0, e_1, e_2)$ is

$$
\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}(1) = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

What this tells us: the regular representation of $\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ has dimension 3 (= the order of the group), and it is a permutation representation. Its character is $\chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(0) = 3, \chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(1) = 0, \chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(2) = 0$ (counting fixed basis vectors). Decomposing: $\chi_{\mathrm{reg}}$ inner-products to 1 with each of the 3 1-dimensional characters of $\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$, so $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}} \cong \chi_0 \oplus \chi_1 \oplus \chi_2$. Each 1-dim irreducible appears once — and indeed $1 + 1 + 1 = 3 = |\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}|$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $G$ be a finite group and $k$ a field. The **group algebra** $kG$ is the $k$-vector space with basis $\{e_g : g \in G\}$, equipped with the $k$-algebra multiplication $e_g \cdot e_h = e_{gh}$ extended linearly. As a $k$-algebra, $kG \cong k[X]/I$ where $I$ encodes the group relations — but the basis-indexed-by-$G$ description is the working one.

The **left regular representation** is $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}^L : G \to \mathrm{GL}(kG)$, $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}^L(g) e_h := e_{gh}$, extended linearly to all of $kG$.

The **right regular representation** is $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}^R : G \to \mathrm{GL}(kG)$, $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}^R(g) e_h := e_{h g^{-1}}$ (the inverse is needed to make this a left action). The two regular representations commute and together give a $G \times G$-action on $kG$.

When unspecified, "regular representation" means the left regular representation.

**Theorem (Character of the regular representation).** *For a finite group $G$,*

$$
\chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(e) = |G|, \qquad \chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(g) = 0 \text{ for } g \neq e.
$$

**Proof.** The matrix of $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}(g)$ in the basis $\{e_h\}$ is a permutation matrix: it has a 1 in row $e_{gh}$, column $e_h$. The trace counts fixed basis vectors, $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}(g) e_h = e_h$ iff $gh = h$ iff $g = e$. So all diagonal entries are 1 if $g = e$ (giving $|G|$) and 0 otherwise. $\square$

**Theorem (Decomposition of the regular representation).** *For a finite group $G$ over $\mathbb{C}$,*

$$
\mathbb{C}G \cong \bigoplus_{V \text{ irrep}} V^{\oplus \dim V} \cong \bigoplus_V V \otimes V^*,
$$

*as $G$-representations and as $\mathbb{C}G$-bimodules respectively. Each irreducible $V$ appears with multiplicity exactly $\dim V$ [ref: TODO_REF Serre §2.4].*

**Corollary (Sum of squares).**

$$
|G| = \sum_{V \text{ irrep}} (\dim V)^2.
$$

This is the "sum of squares" formula, one of the most striking identities in finite group theory: the order of the group equals the sum of squared dimensions of its irreducible complex representations.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Frobenius 1898, regular-representation decomposition).** *Let $G$ be a finite group and let $V_1, \ldots, V_r$ be a complete list of irreducible complex representations of $G$. Then*

$$
\rho_{\mathrm{reg}} \cong \bigoplus_{i=1}^r V_i^{\oplus \dim V_i}.
$$

**Proof.** By Maschke's theorem [07.02.01], $\mathbb{C}G$ decomposes as a direct sum of irreducibles, so $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}} = \bigoplus_i V_i^{\oplus n_i}$ for some non-negative integer multiplicities $n_i$. Compute $n_i$ by the multiplicity formula from unit [07.01.04]:

$$
n_i = \langle \chi_{\mathrm{reg}}, \chi_{V_i} \rangle_G = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} \chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(g) \overline{\chi_{V_i}(g)}.
$$

Since $\chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(g) = 0$ for $g \neq e$ and $\chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(e) = |G|$, the sum collapses to a single term:

$$
n_i = \frac{1}{|G|} \cdot |G| \cdot \overline{\chi_{V_i}(e)} = \overline{\dim V_i} = \dim V_i,
$$

since $\dim V_i$ is a positive integer hence equal to its conjugate. So $n_i = \dim V_i$ as claimed. $\square$

**Corollary.** Taking dimensions on both sides,

$$
|G| = \sum_{i=1}^r (\dim V_i)^2.
$$

This dimension identity, due to Frobenius, is the fundamental quantitative relation between the order of a finite group and the dimensions of its irreducible representations [ref: TODO_REF Frobenius 1898].

**Bimodule version.** The group algebra $\mathbb{C}G$ has commuting left and right $G$-actions, hence is a $\mathbb{C}G$-$\mathbb{C}G$-bimodule, equivalently a $G \times G$-representation. As such it decomposes:

$$
\mathbb{C}G \cong \bigoplus_{V \text{ irrep}} V \otimes V^*,
$$

where $V \otimes V^*$ has the $G \times G$-action via the left factor on $V$ and the right factor on $V^*$. This is the *Wedderburn decomposition* of $\mathbb{C}G$ as a semisimple algebra, identifying $\mathbb{C}G \cong \prod_i \mathrm{End}_{\mathbb{C}}(V_i) \cong \prod_i M_{\dim V_i}(\mathbb{C})$ as algebras.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward later units of the strand, where the same pattern is taken up at higher structure. The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

How many copies of the principal (1-dimensional, all-ones) representation appear in the regular representation of $G$ of order $|G|$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the multiplicity formula.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**1.** The principal representation has dimension 1, so it appears with multiplicity $\dim = 1$. Concretely, the line spanned by $\sum_{g \in G} e_g$ is the unique 1-dimensional invariant subspace of $\mathbb{C}G$ on which $G$ acts as the identity.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the left and right regular representations of $G$ commute on $\mathbb{C}G$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\rho_L(g) \rho_R(h) e_x$ and $\rho_R(h) \rho_L(g) e_x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For any basis vector $e_x$:

$\rho_L(g) \rho_R(h) e_x = \rho_L(g) e_{x h^{-1}} = e_{g x h^{-1}}$.

$\rho_R(h) \rho_L(g) e_x = \rho_R(h) e_{gx} = e_{g x h^{-1}}$.

Both equal $e_{g x h^{-1}}$, so $\rho_L(g) \rho_R(h) = \rho_R(h) \rho_L(g)$ on every basis vector, hence on all of $\mathbb{C}G$. $\square$

The commuting actions make $\mathbb{C}G$ a $G \times G$-representation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Show that the multiplicity of an irreducible $V$ in the regular representation $\mathbb{C}G$ equals $\dim_{\mathbb{C}} \mathrm{Hom}_G(V, \mathbb{C}G)$, and compute this directly to be $\dim V$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A $G$-equivariant map $V \to \mathbb{C}G$ is determined by where it sends a chosen basis of $V$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Schur's lemma [07.01.02], $\dim_{\mathbb{C}} \mathrm{Hom}_G(V, V_i) = \delta_{V \cong V_i}$, so for $\mathbb{C}G \cong \bigoplus_i V_i^{\oplus n_i}$,

$$
\dim \mathrm{Hom}_G(V, \mathbb{C}G) = \sum_i n_i \dim \mathrm{Hom}_G(V, V_i) = n_V.
$$

Compute directly: a $G$-equivariant map $\varphi : V \to \mathbb{C}G$ is determined by $\varphi(v_0)$ for any chosen $v_0 \in V$. The image $\varphi(v_0) \in \mathbb{C}G$ can be any element such that the assignment $\varphi(g v_0) := \rho_{\mathrm{reg}}^L(g) \varphi(v_0)$ extends $\mathbb{C}$-linearly. Since $V = \mathbb{C}G \cdot v_0$ for an irreducible $V$ generated by $v_0$, the map $\varphi$ is uniquely determined by $\varphi(v_0)$, which can be any element of $\mathbb{C}G$ — actually, of the $V$-isotypic component, which is $V \otimes V^*$ of dimension $(\dim V)^2$, and the cyclic vector picks out a copy of $V^*$. So $\dim \mathrm{Hom}_G(V, \mathbb{C}G) = \dim V^* = \dim V$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).**

The symmetric group $S_4$ has order 24 and 5 irreducible complex representations of dimensions $1, 1, 2, 3, 3$. Verify the regular representation decomposition $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}} = \chi_1 \oplus \chi_{\mathrm{sgn}} \oplus 2 \cdot V_2 \oplus 3 \cdot V_3 \oplus 3 \cdot V_3'$ holds dimensionally.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Multiplicity = dimension; sum of (dim) × (dim) = 24.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Multiplicities $= $ dimensions: $1, 1, 2, 3, 3$. Decomposition: $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}} = 1 \cdot \chi_1 + 1 \cdot \chi_{\mathrm{sgn}} + 2 \cdot V_2 + 3 \cdot V_3 + 3 \cdot V_3'$ as representations.

Total dimension: $1 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 1 + 2 \cdot 2 + 3 \cdot 3 + 3 \cdot 3 = 1 + 1 + 4 + 9 + 9 = 24 = |S_4|$. ✓

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Prove that for an *abelian* group $G$, the regular representation decomposes as the direct sum of all 1-dimensional characters $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}} \cong \bigoplus_{\chi \in \widehat{G}} \chi$ where $\widehat{G}$ is the dual group.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For abelian $G$, all irreducibles are 1-dimensional, and there are $|G|$ of them.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For abelian $G$, every irreducible complex representation is 1-dimensional (Exercise 3 of [07.01.02]). The number of inequivalent irreducibles equals the number of conjugacy classes, which for abelian $G$ is $|G|$ (each element is its own class). So there are $|G|$ inequivalent 1-dim characters $\chi_1, \ldots, \chi_{|G|}$.

By Frobenius's theorem, each appears in $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}}$ with multiplicity $\dim \chi_i = 1$. So $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}} = \bigoplus_i \chi_i$, the direct sum over all characters. The dimension check: $|G| = \sum 1 = |\widehat{G}|$. ✓

This is the *Pontryagin duality* for finite abelian groups: $\widehat{\widehat{G}} \cong G$, and the regular representation decomposition $\mathbb{C}G \cong \bigoplus_\chi \mathbb{C}_\chi$ is the *Fourier transform* on $G$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

Identify $\mathbb{C}G$ with $\bigoplus_V \mathrm{End}_{\mathbb{C}}(V)$ as $\mathbb{C}$-algebras (the *Wedderburn decomposition*).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each irreducible $V$ corresponds to a primitive central idempotent in $\mathbb{C}G$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The $G \times G$-decomposition gives $\mathbb{C}G \cong \bigoplus_V V \otimes V^*$ as $\mathbb{C}$-vector spaces. The product on $\mathbb{C}G$ corresponds to a product on each summand; on $V \otimes V^*$, it is the natural product of the algebra $\mathrm{End}_{\mathbb{C}}(V)$ via the canonical isomorphism $V \otimes V^* \cong \mathrm{End}_{\mathbb{C}}(V)$ ($v \otimes \varphi \mapsto (w \mapsto \varphi(w) v)$).

The maps $\mathbb{C}G \to V \otimes V^* \cong M_{\dim V}(\mathbb{C})$ are algebra homomorphisms (each is the action of $\mathbb{C}G$ on $V$ by linear extension, i.e., the $\mathbb{C}G$-module structure on $V$). They combine into a $\mathbb{C}$-algebra isomorphism

$$
\mathbb{C}G \xrightarrow{\sim} \prod_V \mathrm{End}_{\mathbb{C}}(V) \cong \prod_V M_{\dim V}(\mathbb{C}).
$$

This is the *Artin-Wedderburn theorem* for the semisimple algebra $\mathbb{C}G$. The primitive central idempotents are

$$
e_V = \frac{\dim V}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} \overline{\chi_V(g)} \, e_g \in \mathbb{C}G,
$$

projecting $\mathbb{C}G$ onto its $V$-isotypic component $V \otimes V^*$. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Wedderburn decomposition.** For a finite group $G$ over $\mathbb{C}$ (or any algebraically closed field of characteristic coprime to $|G|$),

$$
\mathbb{C}G \cong \prod_{V \text{ irrep}} M_{\dim V}(\mathbb{C}),
$$

a product of matrix algebras. This is the special case of the Artin-Wedderburn theorem for semisimple algebras: every finite-dimensional semisimple $\mathbb{C}$-algebra is a product of matrix algebras over $\mathbb{C}$. The primitive central idempotents (which decompose $\mathbb{C}G$ into matrix-algebra summands) are explicit:

$$
e_V = \frac{\dim V}{|G|} \sum_g \overline{\chi_V(g)} e_g.
$$

**Real Wedderburn.** Over $\mathbb{R}$, the group algebra $\mathbb{R}G$ decomposes as a product of matrix algebras over the *Schur indices* of $G$ — division algebras $\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}, \mathbb{H}$ — recording the *Frobenius-Schur indicators* of irreducible characters: $\nu(\chi) = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_g \chi(g^2) \in \{1, 0, -1\}$, classifying real, complex, and quaternionic irreducibles respectively.

**$L^2$-regular representation for compact groups.** For a compact group $G$ with Haar measure $\mu$, the regular representation acts on $L^2(G, \mu)$, and the *Peter-Weyl theorem* (1927) gives the decomposition

$$
L^2(G) \cong \bigoplus_{V \text{ irreducible unitary}} V \otimes V^*,
$$

with the right side a Hilbert direct sum. The matrix coefficients $g \mapsto \langle \rho_V(g) v, w \rangle$ form a complete orthogonal system in $L^2(G)$.

**Plancherel measure.** For non-compact locally compact groups, the regular representation on $L^2(G)$ decomposes as a *direct integral* of irreducible unitary representations against the *Plancherel measure*. For semisimple Lie groups, the explicit Plancherel formula (Harish-Chandra 1976) is one of the deepest results in 20th-century representation theory, using the wave-packet construction and the determination of tempered representations.

**Group cohomology and the regular representation.** The cohomology $H^*(G, \mathbb{C}G)$ vanishes in positive degrees by an averaging argument; this makes the regular representation an *injective* (and *projective*) module in $\mathbb{C}G$-Mod. For modular representations (characteristic dividing $|G|$), the regular representation is no longer semisimple, and its block structure organises the *modular representation theory* of $G$ (Brauer 1935+).

**Synthesis.** This construction generalises the pattern fixed in [07.01.01] (group representation), with the symmetric data replaced by its skew or twisted analogue. Read in the opposite direction, the construction is dual to the metric story: complements and orthogonality are taken with respect to the bilinear datum of this unit, not a metric, and the resulting category of subobjects is the one the rest of the strand classifies. The central insight is that this datum identifies algebra with geometry: functions become vector fields, subspaces become quotients, and invariants become cohomology classes — and that identification is the engine driving every theorem downstream.

## Full proof set [Master]

The decomposition $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}} = \bigoplus V_i^{\oplus \dim V_i}$ proved in the key-theorem section uses character orthogonality (unit [07.01.04]) and the multiplicity formula. The bimodule decomposition $\mathbb{C}G \cong \bigoplus V \otimes V^*$ uses the additional structure of the $G \times G$-action and Schur's lemma applied to $\mathrm{End}_G$ on each isotypic component. The Artin-Wedderburn algebra isomorphism is a standard application of the structure of semisimple algebras (Curtis-Reiner Vol. I, Lang *Algebra* Ch. XVII). The Peter-Weyl theorem for compact groups is proved in unit [07.07.02]; the Plancherel formula for reductive Lie groups is beyond the scope of the introductory unit and referenced to Knapp's *Representation Theory of Semisimple Groups*.

## Connections [Master]

- **Group representation [07.01.01]** — the regular representation is the principal example, the universal home for all irreducibles.

- **Schur's lemma [07.01.02]** — Schur's lemma identifies the multiplicity of $V$ in $\mathbb{C}G$ as $\dim V$.

- **Character of a representation [07.01.03]** — the character $\chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(e) = |G|$, $\chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(g) = 0$ for $g \neq e$ is the defining feature.

- **Character orthogonality [07.01.04]** — orthogonality drives the multiplicity formula and the dimension identity $|G| = \sum (\dim V)^2$.

- **Maschke's theorem [07.02.01]** — complete reducibility makes the regular representation a finite direct sum of irreducibles.

- **Frobenius reciprocity [07.01.08]** — the regular representation is $\mathrm{Ind}_{\{e\}}^G \mathbb{C}$, the induced representation from the identity subgroup.

- **Peter-Weyl theorem [07.07.02]** — the $L^2$-decomposition of compact groups generalises the regular-representation decomposition to compact Lie groups.

- **Cartan-Weyl classification [07.04.01]** — the dimension formula $\sum (\dim V)^2 = |G|$ is the algebraic counterpart of the analytic statement for compact Lie groups.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Ferdinand Frobenius introduced the regular representation in his 1898 paper *Über Relationen zwischen den Charakteren einer Gruppe und denen ihrer Untergruppen* (Sitzungsberichte der königlich preussischen Akademie der Wissenschaften zu Berlin). The paper, written two years after his foundational *Über die Charaktere endlicher Gruppen*, was concerned with relations between the characters of a group $G$ and characters of its subgroups $H \leq G$ — what would become *induced representations* and *Frobenius reciprocity* (units [07.01.07] and [07.01.08]). The regular representation appears in this paper as the universal computational object: by setting $H = \{e\}$, one obtains the regular representation as $\mathrm{Ind}_{\{e\}}^G \mathbb{C}$.

The key identity $|G| = \sum_i (\dim V_i)^2$ has historical significance beyond its algebraic content. It places severe constraints on the dimensions of irreducibles: for any finite group $G$, there are positive integers $d_1, \ldots, d_r$ with $\sum d_i^2 = |G|$ and (Schur 1904) each $d_i$ divides $|G|$ — a far-from-obvious fact that contributed to the determination of character tables for many groups. The relation between conjugacy classes and irreducibles ($r$ on both sides) — a *Hecke-Brauer* duality with no canonical bijection in general — remains one of the most striking and least-explained features of finite group theory.

Hermann Weyl's 1925–26 papers extended the regular-representation framework to compact Lie groups via the *unitarian trick*: every compact group has a Haar measure, and averaging over Haar measure replaces averaging over $|G|^{-1} \sum_g$. The $L^2$-decomposition of $L^2(G)$ for compact $G$ — the Peter-Weyl theorem proved by Fritz Peter and Weyl in 1927 — is the direct analogue of Frobenius's regular-representation decomposition. For non-compact groups, the $L^2$-decomposition becomes a direct integral against a Plancherel measure; for reductive Lie groups, the explicit determination of the Plancherel measure was completed by Harish-Chandra in the 1970s after decades of work and is widely regarded as one of the most monumental achievements of mid-20th-century mathematics.
