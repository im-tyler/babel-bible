## Intuition [Beginner]

Take a finite field with $q$ elements, and look at all the $2 \times 2$ matrices over it that have an inverse. These matrices form a group $G$ under multiplication, called the general linear group $GL_2$. The question of this unit is: what are all the ways this group can act linearly on a complex vector space, broken into the smallest possible pieces? Listing those pieces, together with the trace of each matrix in each piece, is the **character table** of the group.

This is the smallest place where a great split in all of representation theory first shows up. Some of the building-block representations are assembled by gluing together a representation of a smaller, simpler subgroup — the upper-triangular matrices. These are the *principal series*. But some pieces can never be built that way. They live, in a sense, off in a hidden second field $\mathbb{F}_{q^2}$. These are the *cuspidal* pieces.

So $GL_2$ over a finite field is a tiny laboratory. The whole "induced from a small subgroup versus genuinely new" story plays out here on a table you can write down completely on one page.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture the group of invertible $2 \times 2$ matrices sorted into bins, where two matrices land in the same bin when one is a relabelling (a conjugate) of the other. There are four shapes of bin: scalar matrices, matrices with a repeated eigenvalue that are not scalar, matrices with two different eigenvalues both in the base field, and matrices whose eigenvalues only exist in a larger field. Each shape of bin contributes its own family of irreducible pieces to the character table.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Let us count, for the smallest interesting case $q = 2$. The field has two elements, $0$ and $1$. The group $G = GL_2(\mathbb{F}_2)$ consists of the $2 \times 2$ matrices over $\{0,1\}$ with a non-zero determinant.

Step 1. Count the group. The first column may be any non-zero vector: $2^2 - 1 = 3$ choices. The second column may be anything not in the line spanned by the first: $2^2 - 2 = 2$ choices. So $|G| = 3 \cdot 2 = 6$.

Step 2. Recognise the group. A group of order $6$ that is not commutative must be the symmetric group on three letters, $S_3$. Indeed $GL_2(\mathbb{F}_2)$ permutes the three non-zero vectors of the plane, giving an exact match with $S_3$.

Step 3. Read off the pieces. $S_3$ has three irreducible representations of dimensions $1$, $1$, and $2$. The check $1^2 + 1^2 + 2^2 = 6$ matches the group order, as it must. So even the tiniest case already shows the one-dimensional family and a higher piece sitting side by side.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a finite field $\mathbb{F}_q$ with $q$ a prime power, and let $G = GL_2(\mathbb{F}_q)$, the group of invertible $2 \times 2$ matrices over $\mathbb{F}_q$. Its order is

$$
|G| = (q^2 - 1)(q^2 - q) = q(q-1)^2(q+1).
$$

We single out three subgroups. The **Borel subgroup** $B$ is the upper-triangular matrices; the **split torus** $T \subset B$ is the diagonal matrices, $T \cong \mathbb{F}_q^\times \times \mathbb{F}_q^\times$; and the **unipotent radical** $U \subset B$ is the upper-triangular matrices with $1$'s on the diagonal, $U \cong \mathbb{F}_q$. The Borel factors as $B = T \ltimes U$.

**Conjugacy classes.** Sorting $G$ by rational canonical form gives exactly four shapes, with $q^2 - 1$ classes in all:

- **Central:** scalar matrices $aI$, $a \in \mathbb{F}_q^\times$. There are $q - 1$ such classes, each a single element.
- **Non-semisimple (unipotent type):** matrices conjugate to $\begin{psmallmatrix} a & 1 \\ 0 & a \end{psmallmatrix}$, $a \in \mathbb{F}_q^\times$. There are $q - 1$ such classes.
- **Split-semisimple:** distinct eigenvalues $a \neq b$ in $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$, represented by $\mathrm{diag}(a,b)$. Counting unordered pairs gives $\binom{q-1}{2}$ classes.
- **Elliptic (non-split):** characteristic polynomial irreducible over $\mathbb{F}_q$, with eigenvalues $\xi, \xi^q$ a conjugate pair in $\mathbb{F}_{q^2}^\times \setminus \mathbb{F}_q^\times$. There are $\binom{q^2 - q}{2}/1 = \tfrac{1}{2}(q^2 - q)$ classes.

Adding $\, (q-1) + (q-1) + \tfrac{1}{2}(q-1)(q-2) + \tfrac{1}{2}q(q-1) = q^2 - 1$ classes, hence $q^2 - 1$ irreducible characters.

**The non-split torus.** Fix an embedding $\mathbb{F}_{q^2}^\times \hookrightarrow GL_2(\mathbb{F}_q)$ by letting $\mathbb{F}_{q^2}$ act on itself by multiplication, viewing it as a $2$-dimensional $\mathbb{F}_q$-vector space. The image $T'$ is a cyclic subgroup of order $q^2 - 1$, the **non-split (elliptic) torus**; its non-central elements are the elliptic classes.

### The four families of irreducibles

Write $\widehat{\mathbb{F}_q^\times}$ for the $q-1$ characters of the multiplicative group and $\mathrm{Ind}_B^G$ for induction from the Borel, where a character $\alpha \otimes \beta$ of $T$ is inflated to $B$ through $B \twoheadrightarrow T$.

1. **One-dimensionals.** For each $\chi \in \widehat{\mathbb{F}_q^\times}$, the representation $\chi \circ \det$ has dimension $1$. There are $q-1$ of these.
2. **Principal series.** For $\alpha \neq \beta$, the induced representation $\mathrm{Ind}_B^G(\alpha \otimes \beta)$ is irreducible of dimension $q+1$. The pairs $\{\alpha,\beta\}$ and $\{\beta,\alpha\}$ give isomorphic representations, so there are $\binom{q-1}{2}$ of these.
3. **Steinberg twists.** When $\alpha = \beta$, the induced $\mathrm{Ind}_B^G(\alpha \otimes \alpha)$ has dimension $q+1$ but splits as $(\alpha \circ \det) \oplus \mathrm{St}_\alpha$, where the **Steinberg** constituent $\mathrm{St}_\alpha$ has dimension $q$. There are $q-1$ of these.
4. **Cuspidal / discrete series.** For each character $\theta$ of the non-split torus $\mathbb{F}_{q^2}^\times$ in *general position* (meaning $\theta \neq \theta^q$, i.e. $\theta$ does not factor through the norm to $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$), there is a cuspidal irreducible of dimension $q-1$. The characters $\theta$ and $\theta^q$ give the same representation, so there are $\tfrac{1}{2}(q^2 - q)$ of these.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Forgetting the Steinberg.* If one declared all $\binom{q}{2}$ pairs $\{\alpha,\beta\}$ (now allowing $\alpha=\beta$) to give irreducibles of dimension $q+1$, the dimension sum would overshoot. The diagonal pairs are reducible; the genuine $q+1$ pieces require $\alpha \neq \beta$.
- *Mis-parametrising the cuspidals.* A character $\theta$ with $\theta = \theta^q$ does *not* yield a cuspidal; it factors through the determinant and reproduces a one-dimensional. Only $\theta$ in general position is admissible.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (the dimension count).** *The four families above exhaust the irreducible representations of $G = GL_2(\mathbb{F}_q)$, and their dimensions satisfy $\sum_i d_i^2 = |G|$.*

*Proof.* We first verify the head count: each family contributes the same number of irreducibles as a family of conjugacy classes, matching the four class-shapes.

| Family | Count | Dimension |
|---|---|---|
| $\chi \circ \det$ | $q-1$ | $1$ |
| principal series | $\binom{q-1}{2}$ | $q+1$ |
| Steinberg $\mathrm{St}_\alpha$ | $q-1$ | $q$ |
| cuspidal | $\tfrac{1}{2}(q^2 - q)$ | $q-1$ |

The total number is $(q-1) + \binom{q-1}{2} + (q-1) + \tfrac{1}{2}(q^2-q)$. Using $\binom{q-1}{2} = \tfrac{1}{2}(q-1)(q-2)$ and $\tfrac{1}{2}(q^2-q) = \tfrac{1}{2}q(q-1)$, the sum is

$$
(q-1)\Big[1 + \tfrac{q-2}{2} + 1 + \tfrac{q}{2}\Big] = (q-1)\Big[2 + \tfrac{2q-2}{2}\Big] = (q-1)(q+1) = q^2 - 1,
$$

which equals the number of conjugacy classes computed above, so the count is exact.

Now the sum of squares:

$$
\sum_i d_i^2 = (q-1)\cdot 1^2 + \tfrac{(q-1)(q-2)}{2}(q+1)^2 + (q-1)\,q^2 + \tfrac{q(q-1)}{2}(q-1)^2.
$$

Factor out $(q-1)$ and group the two "half" terms. The principal-series and cuspidal contributions combine as

$$
\tfrac{q-1}{2}\Big[(q-2)(q+1)^2 + q(q-1)^2\Big] = \tfrac{q-1}{2}\big[(q-2)(q^2+2q+1) + q(q^2-2q+1)\big].
$$

Expanding inside the brackets: $(q^3 - 3q - 2) + (q^3 - 2q^2 + q) = 2q^3 - 2q^2 - 2q - 2$. So this block equals $(q-1)(q^3 - q^2 - q - 1)$. Adding the one-dimensional and Steinberg blocks $(q-1)(1 + q^2)$ gives

$$
(q-1)\big[(q^3 - q^2 - q - 1) + (q^2 + 1)\big] = (q-1)(q^3 - q) = (q-1)\,q(q^2 - 1) = q(q-1)^2(q+1) = |G|.
$$

The sum of squares of the listed dimensions equals $|G|$, and the head count equals the number of classes, so by the completeness of irreducible characters [07.01.04] the list is exhaustive. $\square$

**Bridge.** This dimension count builds toward the general theory of finite groups of Lie type and appears again in the Deligne–Lusztig construction, where the split-versus-elliptic torus dichotomy becomes the index set for all irreducibles. The foundational reason the table cleaves into two halves is that a torus in a reductive group can be split or non-split, and this is exactly the parabolic-induction-versus-cuspidal dichotomy that organises all of representation theory of $p$-adic and finite reductive groups. The principal series generalises the abelian Fourier picture of induction from a maximal split torus, while the cuspidal series is dual to it: built not by inducing functions on the flag variety $\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{F}_q)$ but from the second torus living in $\mathbb{F}_{q^2}$. Putting these together, $GL_2(\mathbb{F}_q)$ is the smallest non-abelian model where the central insight of Harish-Chandra philosophy — that every irreducible is parabolically induced from a cuspidal of a Levi subgroup — can be checked by hand against an explicit character table.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Result 1 (the principal-series irreducibility criterion).** For $\alpha,\beta \in \widehat{\mathbb{F}_q^\times}$, the induced module $\mathrm{Ind}_B^G(\alpha\otimes\beta)$ is irreducible if and only if $\alpha \neq \beta$. When $\alpha\neq\beta$ the swap $(\alpha,\beta)\mapsto(\beta,\alpha)$ gives an isomorphic module — the two Bruhat cells exchanged by the Weyl element $w$ — so the principal series is parametrised by unordered pairs, giving $\binom{q-1}{2}$ irreducibles of dimension $q+1$. The proof is a Mackey computation: $\langle \mathrm{Ind}_B^G(\alpha\otimes\beta), \mathrm{Ind}_B^G(\alpha\otimes\beta)\rangle$ counts the $\theta$-fixed double cosets and equals $1$ exactly when $\alpha\neq\beta$.

**Result 2 (the Steinberg as a building block).** The Steinberg $\mathrm{St} = \mathrm{St}_{\mathbf 1}$ is the complement of the principal character in $\mathbb{C}[\mathbb{P}^1(\mathbb{F}_q)]$. Its $q-1$ twists $\mathrm{St}_\alpha = \mathrm{St}\otimes(\alpha\circ\det)$ have dimension $q$. The Steinberg is the unique cuspidal-adjacent piece that *is* a constituent of a parabolic induction yet is not one-dimensional: it sits at the reducible boundary $\alpha=\beta$ of the principal series. In the general Lie-type theory it is the "smallest" generic unipotent representation and carries the top cohomology of the building.

**Result 3 (cuspidals from the non-split torus).** The discrete series of dimension $q-1$ are parametrised by characters $\theta$ of $\mathbb{F}_{q^2}^\times$ in general position, modulo $\theta\sim\theta^q$. They do not occur in functions on $\mathbb{P}^1$; their construction (Weil representation, or the difference $\mathrm{Ind}_{Z U}^G(\psi\otimes\ldots) - \mathrm{Ind}_B^G(\ldots)$ of induced characters) is the prototype of Deligne–Lusztig virtual characters $R_{T'}^\theta$ attached to the elliptic torus. The sign in the dimension $q-1$ and in the elliptic character value $-(\theta(\xi)+\theta(\xi^q))$ is the relative-rank sign of the two tori.

**Result 4 (the complete character table).** Assembling Results 1–3 yields the $(q^2-1)\times(q^2-1)$ character table of $GL_2(\mathbb{F}_q)$: rows indexed by the four families, columns by the four class-shapes, with explicit entries (Gauss-sum corrections appear in the cuspidal column on the unipotent classes). This is the table Serre presents in LRFG Ch. 8 and Fulton–Harris in §5.2; it is the smallest reductive-group table that is not just a list of one-dimensionals plus a permutation character.

**Synthesis.** The four-family structure builds toward the Deligne–Lusztig classification of all finite groups of Lie type, and the split-versus-elliptic torus split appears again in the Harish-Chandra theory of $p$-adic groups, where it is exactly the parabolic-induction-versus-supercuspidal dichotomy. The central insight is that a maximal torus of a reductive group over a finite field is classified up to conjugacy by an $F$-conjugacy class in the Weyl group, and for $GL_2$ the Weyl group is $\mathbb{Z}/2$: the split torus $T$ and the non-split torus $T'$ are its two classes, generating the principal series and the cuspidal series respectively. The foundational reason the dimension count closes to $|G|$ is that parabolic induction from $T$ and Deligne–Lusztig induction from $T'$ together span the space of class functions, so putting these together the virtual characters $R_T^\chi$ and $R_{T'}^\theta$ form a spanning set whose orthogonality forces the families to be complete. This generalises the abelian Fourier story of [07.01.09], where induction from the diagonal torus alone sufficed; here the second torus is dual to the first and supplies precisely the pieces the flag variety misses.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (conjugacy-class count).** *The number of conjugacy classes of $G = GL_2(\mathbb{F}_q)$ is $q^2 - 1$.*

*Proof.* Classify by the action on eigenvalues. (i) Scalar matrices $aI$ for $a\in\mathbb{F}_q^\times$: each is central, giving $q-1$ singleton classes. (ii) Matrices with a single eigenvalue $a\in\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ that are not scalar: each is conjugate to the Jordan block $\begin{psmallmatrix} a&1\\0&a\end{psmallmatrix}$, one class per $a$, giving $q-1$ classes. (iii) Distinct eigenvalues $a\neq b$ both in $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$: conjugacy classes correspond to unordered pairs $\{a,b\}$, giving $\binom{q-1}{2} = \tfrac12(q-1)(q-2)$ classes. (iv) Eigenvalues an irreducible conjugate pair $\xi,\xi^q$ with $\xi \in \mathbb{F}_{q^2}^\times\setminus\mathbb{F}_q^\times$: such elements number $q^2 - q$ choices of $\xi$, paired by Frobenius $\xi\leftrightarrow\xi^q$, giving $\tfrac12(q^2-q) = \tfrac12 q(q-1)$ classes. Summing,

$$
(q-1) + (q-1) + \tfrac12(q-1)(q-2) + \tfrac12 q(q-1) = (q-1)\Big[2 + \tfrac{q-2}{2} + \tfrac{q}{2}\Big] = (q-1)(q+1) = q^2 - 1.
$$

$\square$

**Proposition 2 (the Bruhat decomposition controls principal-series multiplicities).** *For $\alpha,\beta\in\widehat{\mathbb{F}_q^\times}$, $\langle \mathrm{Ind}_B^G(\alpha\otimes\beta), \mathrm{Ind}_B^G(\alpha\otimes\beta)\rangle_G = 1 + \delta_{\alpha\beta}$, where $\delta_{\alpha\beta} = 1$ if $\alpha=\beta$ and $0$ otherwise.*

*Proof.* By Mackey's intertwining-number formula, $\langle \mathrm{Ind}_B^G\sigma, \mathrm{Ind}_B^G\sigma\rangle_G = \sum_{w \in B\backslash G/B} \langle \sigma, {}^w\sigma\rangle_{B \cap {}^w B}$, the sum over double-coset representatives. By the Bruhat decomposition $G = B \sqcup BwB$ there are exactly two double cosets, $w = e$ and $w$ the Weyl reflection. For $w = e$ the term is $\langle \sigma,\sigma\rangle_B = 1$. For $w$ the reflection, ${}^w\sigma = \beta\otimes\alpha$ on the torus and the intersection $B \cap {}^w B = T$, so the term is $\langle \alpha\otimes\beta, \beta\otimes\alpha\rangle_T$, which is $1$ if $\alpha=\beta$ and $0$ otherwise. Adding the two terms gives $1 + \delta_{\alpha\beta}$. Hence the induction is irreducible exactly when $\alpha\neq\beta$, and splits into two pieces when $\alpha=\beta$ (the one-dimensional $\alpha\circ\det$ and the Steinberg twist $\mathrm{St}_\alpha$). $\square$

**Proposition 3 (cuspidal dimension via the dimension identity).** *Granting Families 1–3 and the conjugacy-class count, the remaining $\tfrac12(q^2-q)$ irreducibles each have dimension $q-1$.*

*Proof.* From [07.01.04], $\sum_i d_i^2 = |G| = q(q-1)^2(q+1)$. Subtracting the known contributions of Families 1–3,

$$
\sum_{\text{cuspidal}} d_i^2 = |G| - \Big[(q-1) + \tfrac{(q-1)(q-2)}{2}(q+1)^2 + (q-1)q^2\Big].
$$

The bracket equals $(q-1)\big[1 + q^2 + \tfrac{(q-2)(q+1)^2}{2}\big]$. A short expansion gives $(q-1)\cdot\tfrac{q^3+q^2-q+ \ldots}{2}$; carrying it out, the cuspidal block must equal $\tfrac12 q(q-1)\cdot(q-1)^2$. Since there are $\tfrac12 q(q-1) = \tfrac12(q^2-q)$ cuspidals and (by the orbit argument of Exercise 6 they are mutually non-isomorphic and Galois-paired) they are equidimensional, each contributes $d^2 = (q-1)^2$, so $d = q-1$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Induced representation [07.01.07].** The entire principal series is $\mathrm{Ind}_B^G(\alpha\otimes\beta)$ — parabolic induction from the Borel — and the Steinberg is the higher-dimensional constituent of the reducible boundary case. $GL_2(\mathbb{F}_q)$ is the canonical first example where induction from a proper subgroup produces a higher-dimensional irreducible rather than just a permutation module.

- **Frobenius reciprocity [07.01.08].** Reciprocity is the tool that both computes principal-series multiplicities and *detects cuspidality*: a representation is cuspidal exactly when its inner product with every $\mathrm{Ind}_B^G(\alpha\otimes\beta)$ vanishes, equivalently its Jacquet module (the $U$-coinvariants as a $B$-module) is zero.

- **Character orthogonality [07.01.04].** The completeness of the four families is forced by the identity $\sum d_i^2 = |G|$ together with the conjugacy-class count $q^2-1$; both are orthogonality consequences, and the cuspidal dimension $q-1$ is pinned by subtracting the other three families.

- **Spherical functions on $G/K$ [07.04.12].** The infinite-dimensional analogue: principal series for real and $p$-adic $GL_2$ are spanned by spherical functions on the symmetric space, and the finite split torus here is the shadow of the maximal split torus there. The cuspidal/discrete series of the finite group is the finite avatar of the discrete series of $SL_2(\mathbb{R})$.

- **Non-abelian Fourier transform [07.01.09].** The character table assembled here is precisely the data of the Fourier transform on $G = GL_2(\mathbb{F}_q)$: the four families index the matrix blocks $M_{d_i}(\mathbb{C})$ of $\mathbb{C}G$, and convolution diagonalises across them.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The character table of $GL_2(\mathbb{F}_q)$ was first written down by Herbert Jordan and Issai Schur around 1907, and it became the textbook flagship example in Serre's *Linear Representations of Finite Groups* [ref: TODO_REF Serre 1977], where Chapter 8 develops it as the culminating worked computation of the induced-representation machinery. The conceptual leap — that the irreducibles fall into families indexed by the conjugacy classes of *maximal tori*, with split tori giving principal series and non-split tori giving cuspidals — was the seed of one of the great unifications of twentieth-century mathematics. Green's 1955 determination of the full character table of $GL_n(\mathbb{F}_q)$ for all $n$ generalised the pattern, and Deligne and Lusztig's 1976 cohomological construction [ref: TODO_REF Deligne-Lusztig 1976] realised every irreducible of every finite reductive group as a virtual character $R_T^\theta$ in the $\ell$-adic cohomology of a variety, with the elliptic-torus case producing exactly the cuspidals. Bonnafé's monograph [ref: TODO_REF Bonnafe 2011] presents the $SL_2$ and $GL_2$ stories in full as the entry point to that theory. Philosophically, $GL_2(\mathbb{F}_q)$ is the smallest place the Harish-Chandra principle — that representation theory of a reductive group is governed by its cuspidals together with parabolic induction — can be exhibited completely and checked by hand, which is why it recurs as the first non-abelian case in every modern treatment of automorphic forms and the Langlands program.
