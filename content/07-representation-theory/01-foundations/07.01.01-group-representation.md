## Intuition [Beginner]

A *group representation* is a way of having a group act on a vector space by linear transformations. If $G$ is a group of symmetries — rotations of a square, permutations of coloured balls, internal symmetries of a particle — then a representation tells you how those symmetries shuffle vectors in some space.

The point is that linear algebra is much easier than abstract group theory: by representing a group as matrices acting on $\mathbb{C}^n$, you get to use eigenvalues, traces, characteristic polynomials, and tensor products. Understanding representations of $G$ unlocks deep information about $G$ itself.

Why this matters: in physics, particles are organised by representations of symmetry groups (rotation, Lorentz, internal gauge groups). In number theory, Galois representations encode the deepest information about number fields. In chemistry, molecular vibrations decompose into representations of point groups. Representation theory is the bridge between abstract symmetry and concrete structure.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The cyclic group $\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} = \{0, 1, 2\}$ has a natural 1-dimensional complex representation: $\rho(k) = \omega^k$ where $\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}$ is a cube root of unity. So $\rho(0) = 1$, $\rho(1) = \omega$, $\rho(2) = \omega^2$. Group multiplication $j + k \mod 3$ becomes complex multiplication $\omega^{j+k}$.

A higher-dimensional representation: $\mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z}$ acts on $\mathbb{R}^2$ by rotation by $2\pi/3$. The rotation matrix $R = \begin{pmatrix} \cos(2\pi/3) & -\sin(2\pi/3) \\ \sin(2\pi/3) & \cos(2\pi/3) \end{pmatrix}$ satisfies $R^3 = I$, so $\rho(k) = R^k$ defines a representation. Over $\mathbb{C}$, this 2-dimensional representation splits into two 1-dimensional representations (irreducible pieces).

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $G$ be a group and $k$ a field. A **representation** of $G$ over $k$ is a pair $(V, \rho)$ where $V$ is a $k$-vector space and $\rho : G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)$ is a group homomorphism (i.e., $\rho(gh) = \rho(g)\rho(h)$ and $\rho(e) = I$). When $V$ is finite-dimensional, $\rho$ takes values in the matrix group $\mathrm{GL}_n(k)$ for $n = \dim V$.

Equivalently: a representation is a *left $kG$-module* structure on $V$, where $kG = \bigoplus_{g \in G} k \cdot e_g$ is the **group algebra** (a $k$-algebra with multiplication $e_g \cdot e_h = e_{gh}$). Linear extension of $\rho$ gives the action.

**Basic notions.**

- A **subrepresentation** is a $G$-invariant subspace $W \subseteq V$: $\rho(g)(W) \subseteq W$ for all $g \in G$.
- A representation is **irreducible** if it has no subrepresentations except $0$ and $V$ itself.
- A representation is **completely reducible** (or *semisimple*) if every subrepresentation has a complementary subrepresentation: $V = W \oplus W'$ where both are $G$-invariant.
- A **morphism** of representations $(V_1, \rho_1) \to (V_2, \rho_2)$ is a linear map $\varphi : V_1 \to V_2$ with $\varphi \circ \rho_1(g) = \rho_2(g) \circ \varphi$ for all $g$. Such a $\varphi$ is called a $G$-**equivariant** linear map or **intertwiner**.

**Operations on representations.**

- *Direct sum*: $\rho_1 \oplus \rho_2 : G \to \mathrm{GL}(V_1 \oplus V_2)$, block-diagonal.
- *Tensor product*: $\rho_1 \otimes \rho_2 : G \to \mathrm{GL}(V_1 \otimes V_2)$, $g \mapsto \rho_1(g) \otimes \rho_2(g)$.
- *Dual*: $\rho^* : G \to \mathrm{GL}(V^*)$, $g \mapsto (\rho(g^{-1}))^T$.
- *Hom*: $\mathrm{Hom}(V_1, V_2)$ becomes a representation, $g \cdot \varphi := \rho_2(g) \circ \varphi \circ \rho_1(g)^{-1}$.

**Maschke's theorem.** *Every finite-dimensional representation of a finite group $G$ over a field $k$ of characteristic 0 (or coprime to $|G|$) is completely reducible.*

This is the cornerstone of finite-group representation theory: any representation decomposes as a direct sum of irreducible representations, with the irreducible pieces being the "atoms" of the theory.

**Character of a representation.** The **character** of $\rho$ is the function $\chi_\rho : G \to k$, $\chi_\rho(g) := \mathrm{tr}(\rho(g))$. Characters are *class functions* (constant on conjugacy classes) and the *orthogonality relations* make them a powerful tool: characters of distinct irreducible representations are orthogonal under the natural inner product on $L^2(G)$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Maschke).** *Let $G$ be a finite group and $k$ a field of characteristic 0 (or characteristic $p$ with $p \nmid |G|$). Every finite-dimensional representation of $G$ over $k$ is completely reducible.*

**Proof.** Let $(V, \rho)$ be a representation and $W \subseteq V$ a subrepresentation. We construct a $G$-invariant complement $W'$.

*Step 1 (averaging).* Pick *any* linear projection $\pi : V \to W$ (i.e., $\pi(W) \subseteq W$ and $\pi|_W = \mathrm{id}_W$). Define the *averaged projection*

$$
\tilde{\pi} := \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} \rho(g) \circ \pi \circ \rho(g)^{-1}.
$$

*Step 2 (verification).*

- $\tilde{\pi}$ maps $V$ into $W$: $\rho(g)$ preserves $W$, $\pi$ maps to $W$, so each term lands in $W$.
- $\tilde{\pi}|_W = \mathrm{id}_W$: for $w \in W$, $\rho(g)^{-1} w \in W$, so $\pi(\rho(g)^{-1} w) = \rho(g)^{-1} w$, hence each term gives $\rho(g) \rho(g)^{-1} w = w$, and the average is $w$.
- $\tilde{\pi}$ is $G$-equivariant: for $h \in G$,
$$
\rho(h) \circ \tilde{\pi} = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_g \rho(h) \rho(g) \pi \rho(g)^{-1} = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_g \rho(hg) \pi \rho(g)^{-1} = \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g'} \rho(g') \pi \rho(g')^{-1} \rho(h) = \tilde{\pi} \circ \rho(h),
$$
substituting $g' = hg$.

*Step 3 (complement).* Set $W' := \ker(\tilde{\pi})$. Since $\tilde{\pi}$ is $G$-equivariant, $W'$ is a $G$-invariant subspace. The standard projection identity gives $V = W \oplus W'$.

By induction on $\dim V$, $V$ decomposes into irreducible pieces. $\square$

The use of $\frac{1}{|G|}$ requires $|G|$ invertible in $k$; over $\mathbb{Q}, \mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}$ (characteristic 0), this is always true. Over fields of characteristic $p \mid |G|$, *modular representation theory* takes over with very different phenomena (Brauer theory).

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [07.01.02] (schur's lemma), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [07.03.01] (highest weight representation). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Show that the constant representation $\rho_{\mathrm{const}} : G \to \mathrm{GL}_1(k)$, $g \mapsto 1$, is a representation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Check the homomorphism property: $\rho(gh) = \rho(g)\rho(h)$ and $\rho(e) = 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\rho(gh) = 1 = 1 \cdot 1 = \rho(g) \cdot \rho(h)$ and $\rho(e) = 1$. So $\rho$ is a homomorphism, hence a representation. The constant-one representation has dimension 1 and is irreducible (no nonzero proper subspaces). $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

How many irreducible 1-dimensional representations of $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ over $\mathbb{C}$ are there?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A 1-dim representation of $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ over $\mathbb{C}$ is determined by where the generator goes; it must go to an $n$-th root of unity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A 1-dim representation $\rho : \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{C}^\times$ is determined by $\rho(1) =: \omega$, which must satisfy $\omega^n = 1$ since $n \cdot 1 = 0$ in $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$. So $\omega \in \{1, e^{2\pi i / n}, e^{4\pi i/n}, \ldots, e^{2\pi i (n-1)/n}\}$ — exactly $n$ choices. So there are $n$ inequivalent 1-dim irreducibles.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the regular representation of a finite group $G$ on $kG$ (the group algebra) by left multiplication is a representation, and compute its character.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Left multiplication by $g$ permutes the basis $\{e_h : h \in G\}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define $\rho(g) : kG \to kG$ by $\rho(g)(e_h) = e_{gh}$. This is a permutation matrix with respect to the basis $\{e_h\}_{h \in G}$, with $\rho(g) e_h = e_{gh}$. The map $\rho(g_1 g_2) e_h = e_{g_1 g_2 h} = \rho(g_1) e_{g_2 h} = \rho(g_1) \rho(g_2) e_h$, confirming $\rho(g_1 g_2) = \rho(g_1) \rho(g_2)$.

The trace of a permutation matrix is the number of fixed points: $\rho(g) e_h = e_h$ iff $gh = h$, iff $g = e$. So $\chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(e) = |G|$ and $\chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(g) = 0$ for $g \neq e$.

By the theorem $\rho_{\mathrm{reg}} = \bigoplus_{V \text{ irrep}} (\dim V) \cdot V$, the regular representation contains every irreducible $V$ with multiplicity $\dim V$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

The symmetric group $S_3$ has 3 irreducible complex representations of dimensions $1, 1, 2$. Verify the dimension formula $\sum_i (\dim V_i)^2 = |S_3|$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$|S_3| = 6$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$1^2 + 1^2 + 2^2 = 1 + 1 + 4 = 6 = |S_3|$. ✓

The formula $\sum_i (\dim V_i)^2 = |G|$ holds for any finite group: it expresses the dimension of $kG$ as the sum of dimensions $V_i \otimes V_i^*$ for each irreducible $V_i$ in the decomposition $kG \cong \bigoplus_i V_i \otimes V_i^*$ as bimodules.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why does Maschke's theorem fail for representations of $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$ (characteristic $p$)?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The averaging operator $\frac{1}{|G|} \sum$ requires $|G|^{-1}$ in the field.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In $\mathbb{F}_p$, $|G| = p \equiv 0 \mod p$, so $|G|^{-1}$ doesn't exist. The averaging operator is not defined.

Concretely: consider the 2-dim representation of $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$ where the generator acts by $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$. The subspace spanned by $(1, 0)$ is invariant, but it has *no invariant complement*: any vector $(a, 1)$ is sent to $(a + 1, 1)$, so no 1-dim subspace transverse to $(1, 0)$ is invariant. The representation is reducible but not completely reducible — Maschke fails.

This is the start of *modular representation theory*: in characteristic dividing $|G|$, the structure becomes much richer (and more complicated).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

State the orthogonality relations for characters of irreducible representations of a finite group $G$ over $\mathbb{C}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Characters are orthonormal in $L^2(G)$ with the normalised inner product.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define the inner product on functions $G \to \mathbb{C}$:

$$
\langle f_1, f_2 \rangle := \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} f_1(g) \overline{f_2(g)}.
$$

**Orthogonality of characters.** If $V_1, V_2$ are irreducible complex representations of $G$, then

$$
\langle \chi_{V_1}, \chi_{V_2} \rangle = \delta_{V_1, V_2} = \begin{cases} 1 & V_1 \cong V_2 \\ 0 & V_1 \not\cong V_2. \end{cases}
$$

**Column orthogonality (the dual statement).** For $g, h \in G$,

$$
\sum_{V \text{ irrep}} \chi_V(g) \overline{\chi_V(h)} = \begin{cases} |C_G(g)| & g \sim h \\ 0 & g \not\sim h. \end{cases}
$$

These orthogonality relations make character theory a powerful computational tool: representations are determined up to isomorphism by their characters, and decomposition $V \cong \bigoplus n_i V_i$ is computed by inner products $n_i = \langle \chi_V, \chi_{V_i} \rangle$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

What is the relationship between conjugacy classes of $G$ and irreducible representations of $G$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The number of irreducible representations equals the number of conjugacy classes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a finite group $G$ over $\mathbb{C}$, the number of distinct irreducible representations equals the number of conjugacy classes. This is one of the deepest "coincidences" of finite group theory.

The bijection is implicit in *character theory*: characters $\chi_V$ are class functions, so they live in the space of class functions $\mathbb{C}^{\text{conj classes}}$ which has dimension equal to the number of conjugacy classes. The orthogonality relations show that the characters $\{\chi_V : V \text{ irreducible}\}$ form an orthonormal basis of this space, so the count matches.

A canonical bijection between conjugacy classes and irreducibles is *not* given for general groups. For some specific groups (symmetric groups via Young diagrams, or finite reductive groups via Deligne-Lusztig theory) explicit bijections do exist and are highly subtle.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Frobenius reciprocity.** For a subgroup $H \leq G$, the operations of *induction* $\mathrm{Ind}_H^G : \mathrm{Rep}(H) \to \mathrm{Rep}(G)$ and *restriction* $\mathrm{Res}_H^G : \mathrm{Rep}(G) \to \mathrm{Rep}(H)$ are adjoint:

$$
\mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W, V) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_H(W, \mathrm{Res}_H^G V).
$$

This is the foundation of induced representations and Mackey theory.

**Burnside's $p^a q^b$ theorem.** Any finite group of order $p^a q^b$ (where $p, q$ are primes) is solvable. Proved using character-theoretic methods.

**Brauer's theorem on induced characters.** Every irreducible character of $G$ over $\mathbb{C}$ is a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of characters induced from "elementary" subgroups. This is the foundation of the *integrality* of character values and applications to local-global principles.

**Modular representation theory (Brauer).** When characteristic $p$ divides $|G|$, representations of $G$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$ have "$p$-modular" character theory, with *blocks* and *defect groups* organising the structure.

**Tannakian formalism.** A category equipped with a fibre functor to $\mathrm{Vec}_k$ is equivalent to the category of representations of an *affine group scheme*. This is the categorical reconstruction of $G$ from its representation theory.

**Langlands philosophy.** Automorphic representations of reductive groups parameterise Galois representations, providing the deepest known link between representation theory and number theory.

**Synthesis.** This construction generalises the pattern fixed in [01.02.01] (group), with the symmetric data replaced by its skew or twisted analogue. Read in the opposite direction, the construction is dual to the metric story: complements and orthogonality are taken with respect to the bilinear datum of this unit, not a metric, and the resulting category of subobjects is the one the rest of the strand classifies. The central insight is that this datum identifies algebra with geometry: functions become vector fields, subspaces become quotients, and invariants become cohomology classes — and that identification is the engine driving every theorem downstream.

## Connections [Master]

- **Group [01.02.01]** — the abstract algebraic structure being represented.

- **Lie group [03.03.01]** — continuous groups have continuous (smooth) representations; rep theory of compact Lie groups parallels finite groups.

- **Lie algebra [03.04.01]** — the differential of a Lie group representation gives a Lie algebra representation; Lie algebra reps are often more tractable.

- **Group algebra** — representations of $G$ over $k$ ↔ left $kG$-modules; representation theory ↔ module theory of group algebras.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Group representation theory began with Ferdinand Frobenius and his student Issai Schur in the 1890s. Frobenius's 1896 paper introduced characters and the regular representation; Schur's 1901 dissertation proved Schur's lemma and established the orthogonality of characters via integration over $G$.

The early 20th century saw the theory mature through Hermann Weyl (compact Lie groups, the *unitarian trick*), Élie Cartan (Cartan classification of complex simple Lie algebras), and Wigner-Bargmann (representations of the Lorentz and Poincaré groups in physics). By the 1950s, the Cartan-Weyl theory of compact semisimple Lie groups was complete.

Mid-century: the theory of *infinite-dimensional unitary representations* (Gelfand, Harish-Chandra, Mackey) extended the picture to non-compact reductive groups, opening the door to automorphic forms and the Langlands programme. The 1980s-2000s witnessed the parallel rise of *quantum groups* (Drinfeld, Jimbo) and *categorification* (Crane-Frenkel, Khovanov), revealing deeper categorical structures.

Today representation theory is the lingua franca of mathematical physics (gauge theories, conformal field theory, integrable systems), number theory (Galois representations, Langlands), and combinatorics (Young diagrams, crystal bases, cluster algebras). The principle that *symmetries should be studied through their actions on linear spaces* remains as fertile as ever.
