## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Fulton-Harris develops the representation theory of semisimple Lie algebras through a long chain of examples: the irreducibles of $\mathfrak{sl}_2\mathbb{C}$ indexed by a single non-negative integer, those of $\mathfrak{sl}_3\mathbb{C}$ laid out on the weight lattice, the general highest-weight theory organised by root systems and the Weyl group, and the bridge to the symmetric group through Schur-Weyl duality and Young symmetrizers. Many of its exercises cut across several of these threads at once: a Clebsch-Gordan computation uses the $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ classification and the character ring together; a dimension count uses the Weyl dimension formula and the hook-length formula together.

This pack collects ten such exercises — three easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units rather than as a standalone development. The exercises are loosely grouped: $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ and $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ irreducibles and tensor decompositions (easy to medium), weights, roots, and the Weyl character/dimension formulae (medium), and the Schur-Weyl/Young-symmetrizer combinatorics (hard).

The conventions throughout are Fulton-Harris's: $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ has standard basis $H, X, Y$ with $[H, X] = 2X$, $[H, Y] = -2Y$, $[X, Y] = H$; the irreducible of highest weight $n$ is written $V_n = \mathrm{Sym}^n(\mathbb{C}^2)$ with $\dim V_n = n + 1$; weights are recorded as integer eigenvalues of $H$ (or of the diagonal Cartan for $\mathfrak{sl}_3$).

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining nine follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *Classify the finite-dimensional irreducible representations of $\mathfrak{sl}_2\mathbb{C}$ and read off the Clebsch-Gordan rule $V_a\otimes V_b$.*

**Solution.** Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional irreducible $\mathfrak{sl}_2\mathbb{C}$-representation. Since $H$ acts on the finite-dimensional space $V$, it has an eigenvector; from the commutation relations, $H(Xv) = (\lambda + 2)Xv$ and $H(Yv) = (\lambda - 2)Yv$ whenever $Hv = \lambda v$, so $X$ raises and $Y$ lowers the $H$-eigenvalue by $2$. Finite-dimensionality forces a highest-weight vector $v_0$ with $Xv_0 = 0$ and $Hv_0 = nv_0$ for some scalar $n$.

Set $v_k = Y^k v_0$. Then $Hv_k = (n - 2k)v_k$ and one computes by induction $Xv_k = k(n - k + 1)v_{k-1}$. The chain $v_0, v_1, \ldots$ must terminate: there is a least $m$ with $v_{m+1} = 0$, and then $0 = Xv_{m+1} = (m+1)(n - m)v_m$ forces $n = m$. So $n$ is a non-negative integer, $\dim V = n + 1$, and the weights are $n, n-2, \ldots, -n$ each with multiplicity one. This is $V_n = \mathrm{Sym}^n(\mathbb{C}^2)$.

For the tensor product, the character (the formal sum of weights) of $V_n$ is $\chi_n = q^n + q^{n-2} + \cdots + q^{-n} = \frac{q^{n+1} - q^{-(n+1)}}{q - q^{-1}}$. Characters multiply under $\otimes$, and the ring of such symmetric Laurent polynomials has $\{\chi_n\}$ as a basis. Multiplying $\chi_a\chi_b$ and re-expanding gives, for $a\geq b$,

$$
V_a\otimes V_b \;\cong\; V_{a+b}\oplus V_{a+b-2}\oplus\cdots\oplus V_{a-b}.
$$

This is the Clebsch-Gordan rule. The summands run from $|a - b|$ to $a + b$ in steps of $2$, exactly $b + 1$ of them, and a dimension check confirms $(a+1)(b+1) = \sum_{j=0}^{b}(a + b - 2j + 1)$. $\square$

## Exercises [Intermediate]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).** *Decompose $V_3\otimes V_2$ for $\mathfrak{sl}_2$.*

Using the Clebsch-Gordan rule, decompose $V_3\otimes V_2$ into irreducibles and verify the dimension count.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The summands run from $|3 - 2| = 1$ to $3 + 2 = 5$ in steps of $2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Clebsch-Gordan with $a = 3$, $b = 2$, the summands run from $a - b = 1$ to $a + b = 5$ in steps of $2$:

$$
V_3\otimes V_2 \;\cong\; V_5\oplus V_3\oplus V_1.
$$

Dimension check: $\dim V_3\cdot\dim V_2 = 4\cdot 3 = 12$, and $\dim V_5 + \dim V_3 + \dim V_1 = 6 + 4 + 2 = 12$. The three summands are exactly $b + 1 = 3$ in number.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Dimension of an $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ irreducible by the Weyl dimension formula.*

The irreducible of $\mathfrak{sl}_3\mathbb{C}$ with highest weight $(a, b)$ in the basis of fundamental weights $\omega_1, \omega_2$ has dimension
$$
\dim V_{(a,b)} = \tfrac12(a + 1)(b + 1)(a + b + 2).
$$
Compute the dimensions for $(a, b) = (1, 0)$, $(0, 1)$, $(1, 1)$, and $(2, 0)$, and identify each representation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$(1,0)$ is the standard $\mathbb{C}^3$, $(0,1)$ its dual, $(1,1)$ the adjoint, $(2,0)$ the symmetric square $\mathrm{Sym}^2\mathbb{C}^3$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Plugging into $\tfrac12(a+1)(b+1)(a+b+2)$:

- $(1,0)$: $\tfrac12\cdot 2\cdot 1\cdot 3 = 3$ — the standard representation $\mathbb{C}^3$.
- $(0,1)$: $\tfrac12\cdot 1\cdot 2\cdot 3 = 3$ — the dual $(\mathbb{C}^3)^*$.
- $(1,1)$: $\tfrac12\cdot 2\cdot 2\cdot 4 = 8$ — the adjoint representation $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ itself.
- $(2,0)$: $\tfrac12\cdot 3\cdot 1\cdot 4 = 6$ — the symmetric square $\mathrm{Sym}^2\mathbb{C}^3$.

The two fundamental representations $\mathbb{C}^3$ and $(\mathbb{C}^3)^*$ are distinct (unlike $\mathfrak{sl}_2$, where every representation is self-dual), reflecting the order-two diagram automorphism of $A_2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, symbolic).** *The Casimir eigenvalue on $V_n$.*

The quadratic Casimir of $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ is $C = \tfrac12 H^2 + XY + YX$. Show it acts on $V_n$ as the scalar $\tfrac12 n(n+2)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$C$ is central, so by Schur's lemma it is a scalar on the irreducible $V_n$. Evaluate it on the highest-weight vector $v_0$, using $Xv_0 = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$C = \tfrac12 H^2 + XY + YX$ is central (it commutes with $H, X, Y$), so by Schur's lemma it acts as a single scalar on the irreducible $V_n$. Evaluate on the highest-weight vector $v_0$ with $Hv_0 = nv_0$ and $Xv_0 = 0$. Rewrite $XY + YX = 2YX + [X, Y] = 2YX + H$. Then

$$
Cv_0 = \tfrac12 H^2 v_0 + (2YX + H)v_0 = \tfrac12 n^2 v_0 + (0 + n)v_0 = \tfrac12(n^2 + 2n)v_0 = \tfrac12 n(n+2)v_0,
$$

using $Xv_0 = 0$. So $C = \tfrac12 n(n+2)\,\mathrm{Id}$ on $V_n$. The eigenvalue $\tfrac12 n(n+2) = \tfrac12\langle\lambda, \lambda + 2\rho\rangle$ matches the general Casimir eigenvalue $\langle\lambda, \lambda + 2\rho\rangle$ up to the normalisation of the Killing form.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *Weights of the standard $\mathfrak{sl}_3$-representation and its dual.*

Let $\mathfrak{h}\subset\mathfrak{sl}_3$ be the diagonal Cartan with linear functionals $L_1, L_2, L_3$ (the diagonal entries, subject to $L_1 + L_2 + L_3 = 0$). List the weights of the standard representation $\mathbb{C}^3$ and of its dual $(\mathbb{C}^3)^*$, and show the two have no weight in common.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The standard representation has weights $L_1, L_2, L_3$ on the coordinate basis. Dualising negates weights.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

On the coordinate basis $e_1, e_2, e_3$ of $\mathbb{C}^3$, a diagonal $H = \mathrm{diag}(h_1, h_2, h_3)$ acts by $He_i = h_i e_i$, so $e_i$ has weight $L_i$. The weights of $\mathbb{C}^3$ are $\{L_1, L_2, L_3\}$, the three vertices of a triangle in the plane $L_1 + L_2 + L_3 = 0$.

The dual representation has weights negated: $\{-L_1, -L_2, -L_3\}$, the vertices of the inverted triangle.

These two weight sets are disjoint: $L_i = -L_j$ would force $L_i + L_j = 0$, hence $L_k = 0$ (since the three sum to zero), but $L_k$ is a nonzero functional on $\mathfrak{h}$. So no weight is shared. The two inequivalent fundamental representations sit as opposite triangles, and their non-isomorphism is the failure of self-duality that distinguishes $A_2$ from $A_1$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).** *The Weyl character formula recovers Clebsch-Gordan for $\mathfrak{sl}_2$.*

Specialise the Weyl character formula to $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ and use it to re-derive the Clebsch-Gordan decomposition of $V_a\otimes V_b$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ the Weyl denominator is $q - q^{-1}$ and the character of $V_n$ is $\chi_n = (q^{n+1} - q^{-(n+1)})/(q - q^{-1})$. Multiply $\chi_a\chi_b$ and divide.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Weyl character formula for $\mathfrak{sl}_2$ gives $\chi_n = \dfrac{q^{n+1} - q^{-(n+1)}}{q - q^{-1}}$ (numerator the antisymmetrised highest weight $\rho = 1$, denominator the Weyl denominator). Take $a\geq b$ and compute

$$
\chi_a\,\chi_b = \frac{q^{a+1} - q^{-(a+1)}}{q - q^{-1}}\cdot\big(q^{b} + q^{b-2} + \cdots + q^{-b}\big),
$$

using the polynomial form of $\chi_b$. Distributing, $\chi_a\chi_b = \sum_{j=0}^{b}\dfrac{q^{a+1+b-2j} - q^{-(a+1+b-2j)}}{q - q^{-1}} = \sum_{j=0}^{b}\chi_{a+b-2j}$.

Reading off the indices, the irreducible characters appearing are $\chi_{a+b}, \chi_{a+b-2}, \ldots, \chi_{a-b}$, each with multiplicity one. Since characters of irreducibles are linearly independent, this is the decomposition:

$$
V_a\otimes V_b \cong V_{a+b}\oplus V_{a+b-2}\oplus\cdots\oplus V_{a-b}.
$$

The character ring isomorphism turns the tensor decomposition into ordinary multiplication of Laurent polynomials.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, numeric).** *Hook-length formula for the dimension of a Specht module.*

Compute $\dim S^\lambda$, the dimension of the irreducible $S_n$-representation indexed by $\lambda = (3, 2)$ (so $n = 5$), via the hook-length formula $\dim S^\lambda = n!/\prod_{c}h(c)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Draw the Young diagram of $(3,2)$ and compute the hook length $h(c) = (\text{arm}) + (\text{leg}) + 1$ at each of the five boxes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Young diagram of $\lambda = (3, 2)$ has boxes in rows of lengths $3$ and $2$. Hook lengths (arm to the right plus leg below plus one), box by box:

- Row 1: $(1,1)$ has arm $2$, leg $1$, hook $4$; $(1,2)$ has arm $1$, leg $1$, hook $3$; $(1,3)$ has arm $0$, leg $0$, hook $1$.
- Row 2: $(2,1)$ has arm $1$, leg $0$, hook $2$; $(2,2)$ has arm $0$, leg $0$, hook $1$.

Product of hooks: $4\cdot 3\cdot 1\cdot 2\cdot 1 = 24$. With $n! = 5! = 120$,

$$
\dim S^{(3,2)} = \frac{120}{24} = 5.
$$

Check against the standard-Young-tableau count: there are exactly $5$ standard fillings of $(3,2)$, matching $\dim S^{(3,2)} = 5$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, proof).** *Roots and the Weyl group of $\mathfrak{sl}_3$.*

Describe the root system of $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ as a subset of the plane $L_1 + L_2 + L_3 = 0$, and identify its Weyl group.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The roots are $L_i - L_j$ for $i\neq j$. Reflections in the root hyperplanes permute the $L_i$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The roots of $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ are $\alpha_{ij} = L_i - L_j$ for $i\neq j$, six in total, forming the hexagonal root system $A_2$. A choice of simple roots is $\alpha_1 = L_1 - L_2$ and $\alpha_2 = L_2 - L_3$; the positive roots are $\alpha_1, \alpha_2, \alpha_1 + \alpha_2 = L_1 - L_3$. The angle between $\alpha_1$ and $\alpha_2$ is $120^\circ$, and all roots have equal length (the system is simply laced).

The Weyl group is generated by the reflections $s_{\alpha_{ij}}$ in the hyperplanes $L_i = L_j$. Such a reflection swaps $L_i$ and $L_j$, so the Weyl group acts on $\{L_1, L_2, L_3\}$ by all permutations:

$$
W(A_2)\cong S_3,
$$

the symmetric group on three letters, of order $6$. The six chambers of the hexagon correspond to the six elements of $S_3$, and $\rho = \alpha_1 + \alpha_2$ (half the sum of positive roots, times two) sits at the center of the dominant chamber.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Schur-Weyl duality on $(\mathbb{C}^n)^{\otimes d}$.*

State and prove the double-centralizer half of Schur-Weyl duality: on $V^{\otimes d}$ with $V = \mathbb{C}^n$, the image of $\mathbb{C}[S_d]$ (permuting factors) and the image of $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ (diagonal action) are each other's commutants. Deduce the decomposition $V^{\otimes d} = \bigoplus_\lambda S^\lambda\otimes \mathbb{S}_\lambda(V)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

One inclusion (the $S_d$-image lands in the $\mathrm{GL}$-commutant) is immediate from commuting actions. For the reverse, use that the $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-image is spanned by $g^{\otimes d}$ and these span the symmetric tensors in $\mathrm{End}(V)^{\otimes d}$. Then apply the double-commutant theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$S_d$ acts on $V^{\otimes d}$ by permuting tensor factors and $\mathrm{GL}(V)$ acts diagonally by $g\cdot(v_1\otimes\cdots\otimes v_d) = gv_1\otimes\cdots\otimes gv_d$. These commute, so each image lands in the other's commutant.

Let $A = $ image of $\mathbb{C}[S_d]$ and $B = $ image of $\mathbb{C}[\mathrm{GL}(V)]$ in $\mathrm{End}(V^{\otimes d})$. The span of $\{g^{\otimes d} : g\in\mathrm{GL}(V)\}$ equals the span of all $g^{\otimes d}$ for $g\in\mathrm{End}(V)$ (a Zariski-density argument), which is exactly the space of $S_d$-symmetric tensors $(\mathrm{End} V)^{\otimes d, S_d}$. A linear operator commutes with the $S_d$-action iff it is $S_d$-symmetric, so $B = A'$ (the commutant of $A$). Since $A = \mathbb{C}[S_d]/\mathrm{ann}$ is semisimple, the double-commutant theorem gives $A = B'$ as well.

By the double-centralizer theorem for the commuting actions of the two semisimple algebras $A$ and $B$, the bimodule $V^{\otimes d}$ decomposes multiplicity-free as

$$
V^{\otimes d} \cong \bigoplus_{\lambda\,\vdash\, d,\;\ell(\lambda)\leq n} S^\lambda\otimes \mathbb{S}_\lambda(V),
$$

where $S^\lambda$ is the irreducible $S_d$-module (Specht module) and $\mathbb{S}_\lambda(V)$ is the irreducible $\mathrm{GL}(V)$-module (the Schur functor applied to $V$), nonzero exactly when $\ell(\lambda)\leq n = \dim V$. The Schur functors $\mathbb{S}_\lambda$ thus arise as the $\lambda$-isotypic pieces under $S_d$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *Young symmetrizer is idempotent up to a scalar.*

For a Young tableau of shape $\lambda\vdash d$, with row-symmetrizer $a_\lambda = \sum_{p\in P}p$ and column-antisymmetrizer $b_\lambda = \sum_{q\in Q}\mathrm{sgn}(q)q$, the Young symmetrizer is $c_\lambda = a_\lambda b_\lambda$. Show $c_\lambda^2 = n_\lambda c_\lambda$ for a nonzero scalar $n_\lambda$, and identify $n_\lambda = d!/\dim S^\lambda$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show $a_\lambda x\,b_\lambda$ is a scalar multiple of $c_\lambda$ for every $x\in\mathbb{C}[S_d]$ (a "key lemma"). Apply with $x = b_\lambda a_\lambda$ to get $c_\lambda^2 = n_\lambda c_\lambda$. The image of $c_\lambda$ is the Specht module, so the trace of the projection $c_\lambda/n_\lambda$ equals $\dim S^\lambda$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Key lemma.* For any $x\in\mathbb{C}[S_d]$, $a_\lambda\, x\, b_\lambda = k(x)\, c_\lambda$ for a scalar $k(x)$. This follows because $a_\lambda(\sigma)b_\lambda = a_\lambda b_\lambda = c_\lambda$ scaled by $\pm 1$ for permutations $\sigma$ that lie in the row-then-column structure, and any $\sigma$ outside that structure contains a transposition fixed by some row group and some column group, which makes $a_\lambda\sigma b_\lambda = 0$ by the sign mismatch.

Applying the lemma with $x = b_\lambda a_\lambda$,

$$
c_\lambda^2 = a_\lambda b_\lambda a_\lambda b_\lambda = a_\lambda(b_\lambda a_\lambda)b_\lambda = k\, c_\lambda
$$

for a scalar $k =: n_\lambda$. To evaluate $n_\lambda$, note $e_\lambda = c_\lambda/n_\lambda$ is idempotent and its image is the Specht module $S^\lambda$, so $e_\lambda$ is a projection onto a subspace isomorphic to $\dim S^\lambda$ copies of $S^\lambda$ inside the regular representation. The trace of right-multiplication by $e_\lambda$ on $\mathbb{C}[S_d]$ equals $\dim S^\lambda\cdot(\text{that count})$; a direct trace computation gives $\mathrm{tr}(c_\lambda) = d!\cdot(\text{coefficient of identity in }c_\lambda) = d!$, while $\mathrm{tr}(c_\lambda) = n_\lambda\,\mathrm{tr}(e_\lambda) = n_\lambda\dim S^\lambda$. Hence

$$
n_\lambda = \frac{d!}{\dim S^\lambda}.
$$

For $\lambda = (d)$ (one row) $c_\lambda = a_\lambda$ projects onto the unit-character line, $\dim S^\lambda = 1$, $n_\lambda = d!$; for $\lambda = (1^d)$ (one column) it projects onto the sign line, same scalar.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, proof).** *Frobenius character formula consistency: dimension of $\mathbb{S}_\lambda(\mathbb{C}^n)$.*

The dimension of the Schur functor $\mathbb{S}_\lambda(\mathbb{C}^n)$ is given by the Weyl dimension formula for $\mathrm{GL}_n$:
$$
\dim\mathbb{S}_\lambda(\mathbb{C}^n) = \prod_{1\leq i < j\leq n}\frac{\lambda_i - \lambda_j + j - i}{j - i}.
$$
Verify, for $\lambda = (2,1)$ and $n = 3$, that this equals the count from the standard tableaux of shape $\lambda$ with entries in $\{1, \ldots, n\}$ (semistandard tableaux), and reconcile with Schur-Weyl.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the product over the three pairs $(i,j)\in\{(1,2),(1,3),(2,3)\}$ with $\lambda = (2,1,0)$. Separately count semistandard Young tableaux of shape $(2,1)$ filled from $\{1,2,3\}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take $\lambda = (2, 1, 0)$ for $n = 3$. The three factors:

- $(i,j) = (1,2)$: $\dfrac{\lambda_1 - \lambda_2 + 1}{1} = \dfrac{2 - 1 + 1}{1} = 2$.
- $(i,j) = (1,3)$: $\dfrac{\lambda_1 - \lambda_3 + 2}{2} = \dfrac{2 - 0 + 2}{2} = 2$.
- $(i,j) = (2,3)$: $\dfrac{\lambda_2 - \lambda_3 + 1}{1} = \dfrac{1 - 0 + 1}{1} = 2$.

Product: $2\cdot 2\cdot 2 = 8$. So $\dim\mathbb{S}_{(2,1)}(\mathbb{C}^3) = 8$, the adjoint representation of $\mathrm{GL}_3$ (modulo the determinant twist, the $\mathfrak{sl}_3$ adjoint of Exercise 2).

Semistandard count: tableaux of shape $(2,1)$ with entries in $\{1,2,3\}$, weakly increasing along rows and strictly increasing down columns. Enumerating by the column pair (top-left $<$ bottom-left) and the top-right entry $\geq$ top-left: the count is exactly $8$. This is the Kostka-number total $\sum_\mu K_{\lambda\mu}$ over all content $\mu$, equivalently $s_\lambda(1,1,1)$.

Reconciliation with Schur-Weyl: in $V^{\otimes 3}$ with $V = \mathbb{C}^3$, the $\lambda = (2,1)$ isotypic piece is $S^{(2,1)}\otimes\mathbb{S}_{(2,1)}(\mathbb{C}^3)$ with $\dim S^{(2,1)} = 2$ and $\dim\mathbb{S}_{(2,1)} = 8$, contributing $16$. Summing over $\lambda\in\{(3),(2,1),(1^3)\}$ gives $1\cdot 10 + 2\cdot 8 + 1\cdot 1 = 27 = 3^3 = \dim V^{\otimes 3}$, confirming the decomposition.

</details>

</aside>
