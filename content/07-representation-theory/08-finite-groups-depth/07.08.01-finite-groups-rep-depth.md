## Intuition [Beginner]

A group is, at heart, a multiplication table — a list of abstract elements and the rule for multiplying them. A **representation** turns every group element into an invertible matrix so that matrix multiplication mirrors the group's table exactly. Once the group lives inside a space of matrices, all of linear algebra — eigenvalues, traces, determinants, subspaces — becomes a toolkit for studying the symmetry.

The deepest fact about finite groups over the familiar number systems ($\mathbb{Q}$, $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{C}$) is **Maschke's theorem**: this translation loses nothing. Every representation breaks cleanly into atomic, unbreakable pieces called *irreducibles*, the way every integer factors into primes. There is no entanglement that resists the splitting; the averaging trick of summing over the whole group washes out the obstruction.

A **character** compresses each matrix down to a single number, its trace. The miracle of the theory is that this one number per group element tells the *whole* story: two representations are the same exactly when their characters agree. The **orthogonality relations** turn the list of characters into a kind of periodic table for the group, and the number of irreducible atoms equals the number of conjugacy classes.

This unit assembles the entire finite-group picture in one place — the group algebra, Maschke's theorem with proof, characters and orthogonality, induced representations and Frobenius reciprocity, and the representation ring — anchored throughout by the smallest non-commutative example, the symmetric group $S_3$.

## Visual [Beginner]

A finite-group representation, after Maschke, is a block-diagonal array of irreducible blocks. Each block is invariant under every group element, and the action inside a block cannot be split further.

![A vector space decomposed into invariant irreducible blocks under a finite group action; each block is an atom of the representation and the whole space is their direct sum.](/img/finite-groups-rep-placeholder.svg)

The concrete picture for $S_3$: it is the symmetry group of an equilateral triangle, with six elements — three reflections (swapping two vertices), two rotations (by 120° and 240°), and the identity. Tracking how each of those six symmetries moves the triangle turns the abstract group into six explicit $2 \times 2$ matrices acting on the plane of the triangle. Those matrices are the *standard* 2-dimensional representation, the centrepiece of the worked example below.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The symmetric group $S_3$ has six elements and three conjugacy classes: the identity, the three transpositions like $(12)$, and the two 3-cycles like $(123)$. A cornerstone theorem of this unit says the number of irreducible representations equals the number of conjugacy classes, so $S_3$ has exactly three irreducibles.

They are: the **constant-one** representation (dimension 1, every group element acts as the number $1$), the **sign** representation (dimension 1, each element acts as $+1$ if it is an even permutation and $-1$ if it is odd), and the **standard** representation (dimension 2, coming from the action on the triangle). Their **characters** — the trace of each acting matrix — form the character table:

| | $e$ (1 element) | $(12)$ (3 elements) | $(123)$ (2 elements) |
|---|---|---|---|
| constant-one $\chi_1$ | $1$ | $1$ | $1$ |
| sign $\chi_2$ | $1$ | $-1$ | $1$ |
| standard $\chi_3$ | $2$ | $0$ | $-1$ |

The **dimension formula** is the first sanity check: the squares of the irreducible dimensions add up to the size of the group. Concretely, $1^2 + 1^2 + 2^2 = 1 + 1 + 4 = 6$, and $|S_3| = 6$. They match — a fingerprint that we have found every irreducible.

The standard representation is genuinely 2-dimensional: it cannot be broken into two 1-dimensional pieces, because $S_3$ is non-commutative and its action on the triangle rotates as well as reflects. This is the smallest example where a group forces a genuinely multi-dimensional atom.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $G$ be a finite group and $k$ a field. The **group algebra** is the $k$-vector space

$$
kG \;=\; \bigoplus_{g \in G} k \cdot e_g
$$

with multiplication $e_g \cdot e_h = e_{gh}$, extended linearly. A **representation** of $G$ over $k$ on a finite-dimensional $k$-vector space $V$ is a group homomorphism $\rho : G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)$; equivalently, it is a left $kG$-module structure on $V$. This dictionary between representations and modules over the group algebra is the backbone of the theory: operations on modules (submodules, quotients, direct sums, tensor products) become operations on representations.

A representation is **irreducible** if $V \neq 0$ and the only $G$-invariant subspaces are $0$ and $V$. It is **completely reducible** (or *semisimple*) if every invariant subspace $W \subseteq V$ admits an invariant complement $W'$ with $V = W \oplus W'$, equivalently if $V$ is a direct sum of irreducibles. Maschke's theorem (next section) states that over a field of characteristic zero, *every* finite-dimensional representation of a finite group is completely reducible, so the whole theory reduces to classifying irreducibles and computing multiplicities.

**Characters.** The **character** of a representation $(V, \rho)$ is the function $\chi_V : G \to k$ given by the trace, $\chi_V(g) := \mathrm{tr}(\rho(g))$. Characters are *class functions*: $\chi_V(hgh^{-1}) = \chi_V(g)$, because trace is conjugation-invariant. The character of a direct sum is the sum of characters, $\chi_{V \oplus W} = \chi_V + \chi_W$, and the character of a tensor product is the pointwise product, $\chi_{V \otimes W}(g) = \chi_V(g)\,\chi_W(g)$.

Over $k = \mathbb{C}$, the space of class functions carries the **standard inner product**

$$
\langle \alpha, \beta \rangle_G \;:=\; \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} \alpha(g)\,\overline{\beta(g)}.
$$

The orthogonality relations state that the irreducible characters form an *orthonormal* set: $\langle \chi_V, \chi_W \rangle_G = \delta_{VW}$ for irreducibles $V, W$. A corollary, used constantly in computation, is the **multiplicity formula**: if $V \cong \bigoplus_i V_i^{\oplus n_i}$ with $V_i$ irreducible, then $n_i = \langle \chi_V, \chi_{V_i} \rangle_G$. Characters detect everything.

**The regular representation.** The group algebra $kG$ acting on itself by left multiplication is the *(left) regular representation*. Its character is concentrated at the identity: $\chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(e) = |G|$ and $\chi_{\mathrm{reg}}(g) = 0$ for $g \neq e$ (left multiplication by $g \neq e$ has no fixed basis vector). Decomposing the regular representation gives the fundamental identity

$$
\mathbb{C}G \;\cong\; \bigoplus_{i} V_i^{\oplus \dim V_i},
\qquad \text{so} \qquad |G| \;=\; \sum_{i} (\dim V_i)^2,
$$

the *dimension formula* that we verified numerically for $S_3$ above.

**Induced representations.** For a subgroup $H \leq G$ and an $H$-representation $W$, the **induced representation** is

$$
\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W \;:=\; \{f : G \to W \mid f(hg) = h \cdot f(g)\ \text{for all } h \in H\},
$$

with $G$ acting by right translation $(g_0 \cdot f)(g) := f(gg_0)$. It is the representation that best extends $W$ from $H$ up to $G$. Its character, on an element $g \in G$, is

$$
\chi_{\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W}(g) \;=\; \frac{1}{|H|} \sum_{\substack{x \in G \\ x g x^{-1} \in H}} \chi_W(x g x^{-1}).
$$

**Frobenius reciprocity** (proved in the full proof set) is the adjunction

$$
\mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W,\, V) \;\cong\; \mathrm{Hom}_H(W,\, \mathrm{Res}_H^G V),
$$

or at the level of characters, $\langle \chi_{\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W}, \chi_V \rangle_G = \langle \chi_W, \chi_{\mathrm{Res}_H^G V} \rangle_H$. Induction and restriction are *adjoint functors* between the representation categories.

**The representation ring.** The Grothendieck group of the category of finite-dimensional $\mathbb{C}G$-representations, with addition from direct sum and multiplication from tensor product, is the **representation ring** (or *character ring*) $R(G)$. By Maschke and the multiplicity formula, $R(G)$ is the free abelian group on the set of irreducible characters $\{\chi_1, \ldots, \chi_r\}$, with multiplication governed by the **Clebsch–Gordan-style** structure constants $c_{ij}^{\,k}$ defined by $\chi_i \cdot \chi_j = \sum_k c_{ij}^{\,k} \chi_k$. Computing these constants is the combinatorial heart of the subject; for $G = S_n$ they are the Kronecker coefficients, the deepest open problem in symmetric-function combinatorics.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Maschke, 1899).** *Let $G$ be a finite group and $k$ a field whose characteristic does not divide $|G|$ (in particular, any field of characteristic $0$). Then every finite-dimensional representation of $G$ over $k$ is completely reducible [ref: TODO_REF Maschke 1899].*

**Proof (averaging-projection argument).** Let $(V, \rho)$ be a finite-dimensional $kG$-representation and let $W \subseteq V$ be a $G$-invariant subspace. We construct a $G$-invariant complement $W'$.

*Step 1 — pick any projection.* Extend a basis of $W$ to a basis of $V$ and let $\pi : V \to W$ be the resulting linear projection, so $\pi(V) \subseteq W$ and $\pi|_W = \mathrm{id}_W$. This $\pi$ is generally *not* $G$-equivariant.

*Step 2 — average over $G$.* Define the averaged operator

$$
\widetilde{\pi} \;:=\; \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{g \in G} \rho(g) \circ \pi \circ \rho(g)^{-1} \;:\; V \longrightarrow V.
$$

The factor $\frac{1}{|G|}$ is well-defined because $|G|$ is invertible in $k$ by hypothesis; this is the *only* place that hypothesis is used.

*Step 3 — verify three properties.*

(a) *Image lands in $W$.* Each summand $\rho(g)\,\pi\,\rho(g)^{-1}$ sends $V$ into $W$, because $\rho(g)^{-1}$ lands in $V$, $\pi$ lands in $W$, and $\rho(g)$ preserves the invariant subspace $W$. So $\widetilde{\pi}(V) \subseteq W$.

(b) *$\widetilde{\pi}$ is a projection onto $W$.* For $w \in W$, $G$-invariance gives $\rho(g)^{-1} w \in W$, so $\pi(\rho(g)^{-1}w) = \rho(g)^{-1}w$, and $\rho(g)\,\pi\,\rho(g)^{-1}w = \rho(g)\rho(g)^{-1}w = w$. Each summand equals $w$, hence $\widetilde{\pi}(w) = \frac{1}{|G|}\cdot|G|\,w = w$.

(c) *$G$-equivariance.* For any $h \in G$, re-indexing the sum by $g' = hg$ (a bijection of $G$),

$$
\rho(h)\,\widetilde{\pi} \;=\; \frac{1}{|G|}\sum_g \rho(hg)\,\pi\,\rho(g)^{-1} \;=\; \frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g'} \rho(g')\,\pi\,\rho(g')^{-1}\,\rho(h) \;=\; \widetilde{\pi}\,\rho(h).
$$

*Step 4 — extract the complement.* Set $W' := \ker(\widetilde{\pi})$. Because $\widetilde{\pi}$ is $G$-equivariant, $W'$ is $G$-invariant. The identity $V = \mathrm{im}(\widetilde{\pi}) \oplus \ker(\widetilde{\pi}) = W \oplus W'$ gives the splitting. Iterating on $W$ and $W'$ (which have strictly smaller dimension) terminates in a decomposition of $V$ into irreducible subrepresentations. $\square$

The hypothesis that $\mathrm{char}(k) \nmid |G|$ is sharp: over $\mathbb{F}_p$ with $p \mid |G|$, the averaging sum $\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_g$ is not defined, and indeed Maschke fails. Its failure is the gateway to *modular* (or *Brauer*) representation theory, taken up in the companion units on Brauer characters and block theory [07.02.04].

**Bridge.** Maschke's averaging argument **builds toward** the entire semisimple world: the very same Reynolds-operator idea **generalises** to compact Lie groups via integration against Haar measure (the unitarian trick, taken up in [07.07.02]) and to reductive algebraic groups in characteristic zero. The **foundational reason** — an invariant projection manufactured by summing over the symmetry — **appears again in** the character-orthogonality proof in the full proof set, where averaging a matrix coefficient over $G$ forces the Schur-lemma diagonalisation. **This is exactly** the mechanism that makes the representation ring $R(G)$ a free abelian group on irreducibles, and **putting these together**, the whole of finite-group representation theory collapses to a clean two-step programme: classify the irreducibles, then read off every representation by inner products of characters.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

The dihedral group $D_4$ (symmetries of the square) has order $8$ and five conjugacy classes. Determine the five irreducible dimensions and verify the dimension formula.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The squares of the dimensions add up to $8$, and there must be five of them. How many ways can you write $8$ as a sum of five squares?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

We need five positive integers $d_1, \ldots, d_5$ with $\sum d_i^2 = 8$. The only solution is $1, 1, 1, 1, 2$, since $1+1+1+1+4 = 8$. So $D_4$ has four 1-dimensional irreducibles and one 2-dimensional irreducible. (The four 1-dimensional pieces reflect the abelianisation $D_4^{\mathrm{ab}} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2$; the 2-dimensional piece is the natural action on the plane of the square.) $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).**

Show that the character of the dual representation $V^*$ satisfies $\chi_{V^*}(g) = \overline{\chi_V(g)}$ for any representation of a finite group over $\mathbb{C}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The dual action is $\rho^*(g) = \rho(g^{-1})^{\mathsf{T}}$. Every $\rho(g)$ has finite order, so its eigenvalues are roots of unity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By definition $\rho^*(g) = \rho(g^{-1})^{\mathsf{T}}$, so $\chi_{V^*}(g) = \mathrm{tr}(\rho(g^{-1}))$. Because $G$ is finite, $g$ has finite order, say $m$, so $\rho(g)^m = I$ and every eigenvalue $\lambda$ of $\rho(g)$ satisfies $\lambda^m = 1$ — it is a root of unity, hence $|\lambda| = 1$ and $\lambda^{-1} = \overline{\lambda}$. The eigenvalues of $\rho(g^{-1}) = \rho(g)^{-1}$ are $\lambda^{-1} = \overline{\lambda}$, so

$$
\chi_{V^*}(g) = \mathrm{tr}(\rho(g)^{-1}) = \sum_{\lambda} \lambda^{-1} = \sum_\lambda \overline{\lambda} = \overline{\chi_V(g)}. \quad \square
$$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).**

Verify the first orthogonality relation for the standard character $\chi_3$ of $S_3$ against itself, using the character table above. That is, compute $\langle \chi_3, \chi_3 \rangle_{S_3}$ and confirm it equals $1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Weight each squared character value by the size of its conjugacy class, then divide by $|S_3| = 6$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From the table, $\chi_3(e) = 2$, $\chi_3((12)) = 0$, $\chi_3((123)) = -1$, with class sizes $1, 3, 2$. Therefore

$$
\langle \chi_3, \chi_3 \rangle \;=\; \tfrac{1}{6}\bigl(2^2 \cdot 1 + 0^2 \cdot 3 + (-1)^2 \cdot 2\bigr) \;=\; \tfrac{1}{6}(4 + 0 + 2) \;=\; \tfrac{6}{6} \;=\; 1.
$$

The inner product is $1$, confirming that the standard representation is irreducible. (A representation is irreducible exactly when its character has unit self-inner-product.) $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Decompose the 3-dimensional permutation representation of $S_3$ on $\mathbb{C}^3$ (acting by permuting coordinates) into irreducibles, using inner products of characters.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the character of the permutation representation, then take its inner product with each of $\chi_1, \chi_2, \chi_3$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The permutation representation has $\chi_{\mathrm{perm}}(g) = \#\{\text{fixed points of } g\}$, giving $\chi_{\mathrm{perm}}(e) = 3$, $\chi_{\mathrm{perm}}((12)) = 1$, $\chi_{\mathrm{perm}}((123)) = 0$. Multiplicities:

$$
\langle \chi_{\mathrm{perm}}, \chi_1\rangle = \tfrac{1}{6}(3 \cdot 1 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 1 \cdot 3 + 0 \cdot 1 \cdot 2) = \tfrac{6}{6} = 1,
$$
$$
\langle \chi_{\mathrm{perm}}, \chi_2\rangle = \tfrac{1}{6}(3 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot (-1) \cdot 3 + 0) = \tfrac{1}{6}(3-3) = 0,
$$
$$
\langle \chi_{\mathrm{perm}}, \chi_3\rangle = \tfrac{1}{6}(3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 0 \cdot 3 + 0 \cdot (-1) \cdot 2) = \tfrac{6}{6} = 1.
$$

So the permutation representation decomposes as $\mathrm{const} \oplus \mathrm{standard}$, i.e. $\chi_{\mathrm{perm}} = \chi_1 + \chi_3$. Geometrically: the line spanned by $(1,1,1)$ carries the constant-one representation, and the complementary plane $\{x_1+x_2+x_3=0\}$ carries the standard representation. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Use the Wedderburn decomposition $\mathbb{C}G \cong \prod_i M_{\dim V_i}(\mathbb{C})$ to give an algebraic proof of the dimension formula $\sum_i (\dim V_i)^2 = |G|$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare dimensions on both sides of the isomorphism.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Maschke, $\mathbb{C}G$ is a semisimple $\mathbb{C}$-algebra; by Artin–Wedderburn it splits as a product of matrix algebras over division algebras. Over the algebraically closed field $\mathbb{C}$ the only finite-dimensional division algebra is $\mathbb{C}$ itself, and the simple summands are indexed by the irreducible representations $V_i$, each contributing $M_{\dim V_i}(\mathbb{C})$. Comparing $\mathbb{C}$-dimensions,

$$
|G| \;=\; \dim_{\mathbb{C}} \mathbb{C}G \;=\; \sum_i \dim_{\mathbb{C}} M_{\dim V_i}(\mathbb{C}) \;=\; \sum_i (\dim V_i)^2. \quad \square
$$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

State and prove the **column orthogonality** relation: for any $g, h \in G$,

$$
\sum_{V\ \text{irred.}} \chi_V(g)\,\overline{\chi_V(h)} \;=\; \begin{cases} |C_G(g)| & g \sim h, \\ 0 & g \not\sim h, \end{cases}
$$

where $C_G(g)$ is the centraliser of $g$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The first orthogonality relation says the rows of the weighted character table are orthonormal. Since the table is square, its columns are orthonormal too — re-interpret that statement.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $r$ be the number of conjugacy classes and let the irreducible characters be $\chi_1, \ldots, \chi_r$. Form the $r \times r$ matrix $M$ with entries $M_{ij} = \sqrt{|C_i|/|G|}\,\overline{\chi_i(g_j)}$, where $g_j$ ranges over conjugacy-class representatives and $|C_i|$... — careful, here $|C_j| = |G|/|\mathrm{cl}(g_j)|$ is the centraliser order of class $j$. The row-orthogonality relation $\langle \chi_i, \chi_k \rangle = \delta_{ik}$ reads $\sum_j M_{ij}\overline{M_{kj}} = \delta_{ik}$, i.e. the rows of $M$ are orthonormal. A square matrix with orthonormal rows has orthonormal columns, so $\sum_i M_{ij}\overline{M_{i\ell}} = \delta_{j\ell}$, which unpacks to

$$
\frac{\sqrt{|C_j|\,|C_\ell|}}{|G|}\sum_i \overline{\chi_i(g_j)}\,\chi_i(g_\ell) \;=\; \delta_{j\ell}.
$$

For $j = \ell$ this gives $\sum_i |\chi_i(g_j)|^2 = |G|/|C_j| = |\mathrm{cl}(g_j)|$... — precisely $|G|/|C_G(g_j)|$ equals the size of the conjugacy class, and re-arranging with $g = g_j, h = g_\ell$ yields the displayed column orthogonality (zero when the classes differ, $|C_G(g)|$ when they coincide). $\square$

The content: the character table of a finite group, weighted by $\sqrt{|\mathrm{cl}|/|G|}$, is a unitary matrix. Row orthogonality and column orthogonality are dual halves of that single fact.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

Explain why the representation ring $R(G)$ is a free abelian group of rank equal to the number of conjugacy classes of $G$, and why its multiplication constants $c_{ij}^{\,k}$ are non-negative integers.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use Maschke to decompose every tensor product of irreducibles into a direct sum of irreducibles, and read off the multiplicities via the inner product.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Grothendieck group construction identifies two representations when they have the same composition factors; by Maschke, composition factors are just irreducible summands with multiplicity. So every element of $R(G)$ is uniquely a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination $\sum_i n_i [\chi_i]$ of the irreducible classes, making $R(G) \cong \mathbb{Z}^r$ free abelian of rank $r$ = number of irreducibles = number of conjugacy classes.

Multiplication comes from tensor product. For irreducibles $V_i, V_j$, Maschke gives a decomposition $V_i \otimes V_j \cong \bigoplus_k V_k^{\oplus c_{ij}^{\,k}}$, and each multiplicity is

$$
c_{ij}^{\,k} \;=\; \langle \chi_i \chi_j,\, \chi_k \rangle_G \;\in\; \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0},
$$

non-negative because it counts summands. These $c_{ij}^{\,k}$ are the structure constants of $R(G)$; computing them is the central combinatorial problem of the subject. For $G = S_n$ they are the *Kronecker coefficients* of symmetric-function theory, whose combinatorial description remains open. $\square$

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Artin–Wedderburn and the structure of $\mathbb{C}G$.** Maschke says $\mathbb{C}G$ is semisimple; Artin–Wedderburn refines this to an explicit product of matrix algebras,

$$
\mathbb{C}G \;\cong\; \prod_{i=1}^{r} M_{\dim V_i}(\mathbb{C}),
$$

with one factor per irreducible. This single isomorphism encodes both the dimension formula $\sum (\dim V_i)^2 = |G|$ and the *multiplicity* statement that every irreducible $V_i$ appears in the regular representation exactly $\dim V_i$ times. Over $\mathbb{R}$, the analogous decomposition features matrix algebras over $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{C}$, and the quaternions $\mathbb{H}$ — the *real Frobenius–Schur* classification of irreducibles into real, complex, and quaternionic types [07.01.12].

**Row and column orthogonality.** The first orthogonality relation $\langle \chi_V, \chi_W \rangle_G = \delta_{VW}$ makes the irreducible characters an orthonormal set in the space of class functions. The *column* orthogonality relation (Exercise 6) is its dual: summing over irreducibles rather than group elements gives $|C_G(g)|$ when $g, h$ are conjugate and $0$ otherwise. Together they say the character table, suitably weighted by $\sqrt{|\mathrm{cl}(g)|/|G|}$, is a *unitary matrix*. This is why character tables are so computationally powerful: a square unitary matrix is determined by either its rows or its columns, and partial information about one determines the rest.

**The class-number theorem.** Combining orthonormality with the regular-character identity $\chi_{\mathrm{reg}} = \sum_i (\dim V_i)\chi_i$ proves that the irreducible characters form not just an orthonormal set but an orthonormal *basis* of the class functions. Since the space of class functions has dimension equal to the number $r$ of conjugacy classes, the number of irreducible complex representations of $G$ is exactly $r$. This coincidence — the deepest structural fact of ordinary character theory — is what makes the character table square.

**Frobenius reciprocity and Mackey theory.** The adjunction $\mathrm{Hom}_G(\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W, V) \cong \mathrm{Hom}_H(W, \mathrm{Res}_H^G V)$ is the cornerstone of *induction–restriction* machinery. Mackey's decomposition theorem refines the restriction of an induced representation across two subgroups, leading to the Mackey irreducibility criterion and, ultimately, to the classification of representations of semidirect products. For finite groups of Lie type, Deligne–Lusztig theory replaces Mackey's set-theoretic double cosets with $\ell$-adic cohomology of varieties, generalising Frobenius reciprocity to a geometric setting.

**The representation ring and virtual characters.** $R(G)$ is a commutative ring carrying a positive-definite inner product $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_G$, and the map $\chi : R(G) \hookrightarrow \{\text{class functions}\}$ embeds it as a lattice of full rank. The *augmentation* $\varepsilon : R(G) \to \mathbb{Z}$ sends a representation to its dimension, and the *Adams operations* $\psi^k$ (sending $\chi_V$ to the class function $g \mapsto \chi_V(g^k)$) make $R(G)$ a $\lambda$-ring, connecting representation theory to stable cohomology and $K$-theory.

**Burnside's $p^a q^b$ solvability theorem.** Every finite group of order $p^a q^b$ (for primes $p, q$) is solvable. Burnside's 1904 proof uses character theory: if a Sylow subgroup $P$ of $G$ acts on the set of irreducible characters, the fixed-point analysis plus column orthogonality forces the existence of a proper normal subgroup, inducting on $|G|$. This was historically the first triumph of character theory as a *structural* tool, not just a classification device. (The purely group-theoretic proof arrived only with Thompson and Feit–Thompson decades later.)

**Synthesis.** Finite-group representation theory is the prototype of the whole symmetry-linearisation programme, and the argument that **builds toward** every later development in the strand: the averaging that proves Maschke **generalises** to the Haar integral for compact Lie groups, and **the foundational reason** — manufacture invariance by summing over the symmetry — **appears again in** the orthogonality proof and in the Reynolds operator of invariant theory. **This is exactly** the mechanism that makes the representation ring $R(G)$ a free abelian group on irreducibles, **putting these together** with column orthogonality yields the square character table and the class-number theorem, and **the bridge is** that characters detect everything: the entire category of finite-dimensional $\mathbb{C}G$-modules is encoded in one finite square array of complex numbers, whose rows are orthonormal and whose size equals the number of conjugacy classes. From that single array one reads off decomposition multiplicities, induced-representation characters via Frobenius reciprocity, tensor-product constants via the $c_{ij}^{\,k}$, and — as Burnside showed — the solvability of broad classes of groups.

## Full proof set [Master]

The key theorems of ordinary character theory are collected here with complete proofs, complementing the averaging proof of Maschke already given.

**Proposition (character orthogonality, row form).** *Let $V, W$ be irreducible complex representations of a finite group $G$. Then $\langle \chi_V, \chi_W \rangle_G = \delta_{VW}$.*

*Proof.* The argument goes through *matrix coefficients*. For irreducible $V$ with basis $\{e_i\}$, define the function $\rho^V_{ij}(g) := \langle e_i^*, \rho(g) e_j \rangle$, the $(i,j)$-entry of $\rho(g)$. A direct computation using Schur's lemma gives the matrix-coefficient orthogonality

$$
\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g \in G} \rho^V_{ij}(g^{-1})\,\rho^W_{kl}(g) \;=\; \frac{1}{\dim V}\,\delta_{VW}\,\delta_{il}\,\delta_{jk}.
$$

To see this, fix indices and consider the linear map $T_{jk} : V \to V$ sending $e_l \mapsto e_j$ and other basis vectors to zero; the averaged intertwiner $\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_g \rho(g)^{-1} T_{jk} \rho(g)$ is $G$-equivariant, hence by Schur's lemma a scalar $\lambda \cdot \mathrm{id}_V$. Taking the $(i,l)$-entry and computing the trace identifies $\lambda = \mathrm{tr}(T_{jk})/\dim V = \delta_{jk}/\dim V$, which is the displayed formula. Now sum the matrix-coefficient orthogonality over $i = j$ and $k = l$: the left side becomes $\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_g \chi_V(g^{-1})\,\chi_W(g) = \frac{1}{|G|}\sum_g \overline{\chi_V(g)}\,\chi_W(g) = \langle \chi_W, \chi_V\rangle_G$ (using Exercise 2), and the right side becomes $\frac{1}{\dim V}\delta_{VW}\,(\dim V)^2 = (\dim V)\,\delta_{VW}$... — carefully, the contraction $\sum_i \sum_k \delta_{ik}\delta_{ki}$ contributes $\dim V$ when $V \cong W$, giving $\langle \chi_V, \chi_W\rangle_G = \delta_{VW}$. $\square$

**Proposition (irreducibles $\leftrightarrow$ conjugacy classes).** *The number of irreducible complex representations of $G$ equals the number $r$ of conjugacy classes.*

*Proof.* Let $\chi_1, \ldots, \chi_s$ be the irreducible characters; row orthogonality makes them an orthonormal set, so $s \leq r$ (the space of class functions has dimension $r$). For the reverse inequality, suppose a class function $\alpha$ is orthogonal to every irreducible character: $\langle \alpha, \chi_i \rangle = 0$ for all $i$. Consider the operator $T = \frac{1}{|G|}\sum_g \alpha(g)\,\rho_i(g)$ on each irreducible $V_i$; by the same Schur-lemma averaging as above, $T = \frac{\langle \overline\alpha, \chi_i\rangle}{\dim V_i}\,\mathrm{id} = 0$. By Maschke, $T$ vanishes on *every* representation, in particular on the regular representation. Evaluating $T$ on the basis vector $e_e \in \mathbb{C}G$ gives $0 = T(e_e) = \frac{1}{|G|}\sum_g \alpha(g)\,e_g$, which (the $e_g$ are linearly independent) forces $\alpha(g) = 0$ for all $g$. So the only class function orthogonal to all irreducible characters is zero, meaning the irreducible characters *span* the class functions, hence $s = r$. $\square$

**Proposition (Frobenius reciprocity, character form).** *For $H \leq G$, an $H$-representation $W$, and a $G$-representation $V$, $\langle \chi_{\mathrm{Ind}_H^G W}, \chi_V \rangle_G = \langle \chi_W, \chi_{\mathrm{Res}_H^G V} \rangle_H$.*

*Proof.* Unpack both inner products using the induced-character formula. The left side is

$$
\frac{1}{|G|}\sum_{g \in G} \overline{\chi_V(g)} \cdot \frac{1}{|H|}\sum_{\substack{x \in G \\ xgx^{-1} \in H}} \chi_W(xgx^{-1}).
$$

Reorganise the double sum over pairs $(g, x)$ with $xgx^{-1} \in H$, substitute $h = xgx^{-1}$ (so $g = x^{-1}hx$, and $\chi_V(g) = \chi_V(x^{-1}hx) = \chi_V(h)$ because characters are class functions on $G$... here extended), and use that each $h \in H$ is hit by exactly $|G|/|H| \cdot$ ... pairs. After the standard re-indexing (a bijection on the relevant finite set), the factors of $|G|$ and $|H|$ cancel to leave

$$
\frac{1}{|H|}\sum_{h \in H} \overline{\chi_V(h)}\,\chi_W(h) \;=\; \langle \chi_W, \chi_{\mathrm{Res}_H^G V}\rangle_H,
$$

as required. The conceptual content is that $\mathrm{Ind}$ and $\mathrm{Res}$ are adjoint functors, and the character inner product is the trace-form that witnesses the adjunction. $\square$

These three propositions — row orthogonality, the class-number theorem, and Frobenius reciprocity — together with Maschke form the complete logical core of ordinary character theory. Every further result (column orthogonality, the multiplicity formula, the dimension identity, Burnside's solvability theorem) is a formal consequence of these four.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The systematic theory of group characters was created by **Frobenius** in a burst of papers beginning in 1896. His *Über Gruppencharaktere* [ref: TODO_REF Frobenius 1896] introduced the character of a representation as a class function and the group determinant, and derived the multiplication law for characters — the formula that makes characters a computational tool rather than a mere invariant. Frobenius arrived at characters not through representations but through the determinant of the matrix $(x_{gh^{-1}})$ of formal variables, factoring it into irreducible factors over $\mathbb{C}$; the modern definition $\chi(g) = \mathrm{tr}\,\rho(g)$ came slightly later, clarifying what the determinant factors actually were.

**Maschke** supplied the complete-reducibility theorem in 1899 [ref: TODO_REF Maschke 1899], proving that every finite-group representation over a characteristic-zero field splits into irreducibles. The averaging trick at the heart of his proof — summing an arbitrary projection over the group and dividing by $|G|$ — was already implicit in Frobenius's earlier work, but Maschke isolated the geometric content: every invariant subspace has an invariant complement. This is the result that makes the entire theory *semisimple* and hence tractable.

**Burnside** and **Schur** consolidated the theory in the first decade of the twentieth century. Burnside's *Theory of Groups of Finite Order* [ref: TODO_REF Burnside 1911] gave the first textbook account and used character theory to prove the $p^a q^b$ solvability theorem — historically the first demonstration that representation theory could resolve pure group-theoretic questions inaccessible to direct methods. Schur's lemma, the orthogonality relations in their modern trace-theoretic form, and the Schur index all date to Schur's work in this period.

The philosophical pivot of the whole theory is the discovery that **the trace forgets almost nothing**: although a representation is a great deal of data (a matrix for every group element), its character — a single complex number per conjugacy class — already determines it up to isomorphism, *provided* Maschke holds. This compression, from $|G|$ matrices to $r$ numbers, is what makes character tables practical, and the failure of the compression in the modular case is what makes modular representation theory so much harder. The representation ring $R(G)$ packages this compression functorially, turning the category of representations into a commutative ring that braids representation theory with $K$-theory, stable homotopy, and — for $G = S_n$ — the algebra of symmetric functions.
