## Intuition [Beginner]

Metaphysics is the study of what there is and what it is like. It asks the questions that sit beneath every special science: what is a thing, what makes two things the same, what is it for one thing to cause another, and what could have been otherwise.

A coffee cup on a desk is one thing. But it is also a collection of atoms, and it is also the cup you drank from this morning. Which of these is the real cup? When the cup breaks, the atoms remain but the cup is gone — what changed? These are metaphysical questions.

Metaphysics does not replace physics. It clarifies the categories — object, property, cause, possibility — that any physical theory must use.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider a wooden ship, the Ship of Theseus, whose planks are replaced one by one over years until no original plank remains. Is it still the same ship?

Step 1. The atoms are different, so if identity tracks matter, it is a different ship.

Step 2. The form and function are continuous, so if identity tracks structure, it is the same ship.

Step 3. The puzzle forces a choice: identity is either material continuity or structural continuity, and the two come apart.

What this tells us: identity is not a single obvious notion; metaphysics makes the competing criteria explicit so the choice can be reasoned about.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Metaphysics is the branch of philosophy investigating the most general features of reality. Its central notions are [ref: TODO_REF Aristotle Metaphysics IV]:

- **Ontology**: the study of what exists; the inventory of categories (substances, properties, events, relations).
- **Identity**: the relation everything bears to itself and to nothing else, governed by Leibniz's law ($x=y$ iff every property of $x$ is a property of $y$).
- **Persistence**: the conditions under which an object at one time is identical to an object at another.
- **Causation**: the productive or dependence relation between events.
- **Modality**: the distinctions among necessity, possibility, and contingency, formalised in modal logic.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Identity is not similarity.* Two things can be qualitatively identical (all properties shared) without being numerically identical; perfect clones are distinct objects.
- *Causation is not constant conjunction.* Hume's regularity analysis is a starting point, not the final word; counterfactual and interventionist analyses address its gaps.
- *Possibility is not thinkability.* What is conceivable may be metaphysically impossible (e.g. water being something other than H2O, on Kripke's account).

## Key argument [Intermediate+]

**Leibniz's law and the identity of indiscernibles.** If $x$ and $y$ share all properties, then $x = y$.

*Argument.* Suppose $x$ and $y$ share all properties but $x \neq y$. Then $x$ has the property of being identical to $x$, which $y$ lacks (since $y \neq x$). This contradicts the supposition that they share all properties. Hence $x = y$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This argument builds toward [42.02.01] (model theory), where identity and indiscernibility are formalised within first-order semantics, and appears again in [20.12.01] (philosophy of language), where Kripke's rigid designation uses modalised identity to fix reference across possible worlds. The foundational reason identity matters across metaphysics, logic, and language is that it sets the conditions under which two descriptions pick out one thing; putting these together, the bridge is that the same identity relation underwrites reference, persistence, and modality, and the pattern generalises to the equivalence relations of mathematics and the type theories of computer science.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Reconstruct Hume's regularity analysis of causation and state the primary objection to it.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Constant conjunction; the objection concerns the absence of necessity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Hume: an event $B$ is caused by $A$ when $A$-type events are constantly conjoined with $B$-type events and $B$ follows $A$. Counterfactual and interventionist theories (Lewis, Woodward) attempt to restore necessity via dependence.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

State the problem of persistence and contrast the endurance and perdurance theories.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Whether objects are wholly present at each time, or extended through time as four-dimensional worms.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

## Advanced results [Master]

Contemporary metaphysics has several live research programs. **Modal realism** (Lewis 1986) analyses modality via concrete possible worlds, yielding an elegant semantics for modal logic at the cost of ontological exuberance; alternatives include ersatz worlds and linguistic theories of modality. **Grounding and fundamentality** analyse the asymmetric dependence of the derivative on the fundamental — a revival of Aristotelian metaphysical structure expressed in precise contemporary terms [ref: TODO_REF Lewis 1986].

**Mereology** (the logic of parts and wholes) formalises the composition relation and debates whether arbitrary sums exist (universalism) or only restricted sums (nihilism, organicism). **Causal theories** have proliferated since Hume: counterfactual (Lewis's closest-world semantics), interventionist (Woodward), and dispositional approaches each address a different gap in regularity accounts.

In philosophy of mind, the **problem of mental causation** asks how mental states can cause physical effects if the physical domain is causally closed — a metaphysical tension driving the philosophy-of-mind chapter [20.13.01].

**Synthesis.** Metaphysics supplies the categories every other philosophical and scientific domain presupposes: the identity relation formalised here builds toward [20.12.01] (philosophy of language), where Kripke's rigid designation extends identity into modal contexts, appears again in [20.13.01] (philosophy of mind) in debates about whether mental states are identical to physical states, the foundational reason these debates recur is that any theory of anything must commit on what its basic objects are and how they persist, the bridge is that the same identity-modality-persistence triad underwrites reference, personhood, and lawhood, and the pattern generalises to the equivalence-and-canonical-form reasoning that pervades mathematics; putting these together, metaphysics is the load-bearing substrate of the philosophical curriculum, the chapter whose questions the other philosophy chapters presuppose.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Necessity of identity).** If $x = y$ then necessarily $x = y$.

*Argument.* Suppose $x = y$. In any possible world in which $x$ exists, $x$ is identical to $x$ and hence (by substitution) to $y$. Therefore there is no possible world in which $x$ exists and $x \neq y$. So $x = y$ is necessary. $\square$

This is Kripke's central modal result: identity, where it holds, holds of necessity. It drives the conclusion that identities involving natural kinds (water = H2O) are necessary a posteriori, resolving puzzles about reference and modality that pre-Kripke descriptivist theories could not handle.

## Connections [Master]

- **Philosophy of language [20.12.01].** Kripke's rigid designation and the necessity of identity, established here, fix how proper names and natural-kind terms refer across modal contexts.

- **Philosophy of mind [20.13.01].** Debates about whether mental states are identical to physical states inherit the identity-relation framework developed here and apply it to the mental-physical relation.

- **Modal logic [42.02.01].** The formal semantics for necessity and possibility, developed in model theory, supplies the technical apparatus that rigorous metaphysical claims about modality require.

- **History of philosophy [20.15.01].** Aristotle's metaphysics, Leibniz's monadology, and Kant's transcendental idealism are the historical sources of the contemporary problems framed here.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Aristotle's *Metaphysics* (Book IV) defined the discipline as the study of "being qua being" — the most general features of what is, abstracting from any particular science [ref: TODO_REF Aristotle Metaphysics IV]. The medieval scholastics systematised his categories. Leibniz's *Monadology* (1714) introduced identity-of-indiscernibles and the principle of sufficient reason.

The modern revival came with Kripke's 1970 Princeton lectures, published as *Naming and Necessity* (1980), which overturned descriptivist theories of reference by arguing that proper names rigidly designate their bearers and that identity claims are necessary where they hold [ref: TODO_REF Kripke 1980]. David Lewis's *On the Plurality of Worlds* (1986) provided the most systematic metaphysical theory of modality, defending modal realism at the cost of admitting concrete possible worlds.

The grounding literature of the twenty-first century (Schaffer, Fine, Rosen) revived the Aristotelian idea of metaphysical dependence, framing it in precise contemporary terms and reconnecting metaphysics to its foundational origins.
