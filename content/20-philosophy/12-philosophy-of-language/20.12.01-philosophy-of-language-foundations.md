## Intuition [Beginner]

Philosophy of language asks how words, sounds, and marks manage to be about things. A spoken word is just vibrations in the air; a written word is ink on a page. Yet by them we reach planets, numbers, and people who died long ago. How does the noise get a world into the conversation?

This is the problem of meaning and reference. It splits into sharper questions. What does a name like "Aristotle" pick out? What fixes what it picks out? And what is the difference, if any, between what a sentence means and what a speaker does in saying it.

Two puzzles drive the subject. The first: "the morning star" and "the evening star" both name the planet Venus, yet the phrases feel different, and "the morning star is the evening star" says something a person could learn. The second: saying "I promise" does not describe a promise, it makes one. Words, it seems, both represent the world and act on it.

A complete theory of language must account for both.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Frege's puzzle, stated in one concrete case. The planet Venus shines brightly just before dawn (the "morning star") and again just after sunset (the "evening star"). Ancient astronomers took these for two different bodies.

Step 1. Both phrases refer to the same object: Venus.

Step 2. Yet "the morning star is the evening star" is informative, a discovery, while "the morning star is the morning star" tells you nothing new.

Step 3. If reference were the whole of meaning, the two sentences would be equally empty. They are not. So meaning has a second ingredient: not just what is referred to, but how it is presented.

What this tells us: a theory of meaning cannot be only a theory of reference. It must also explain the how, the cognitive route a phrase gives to its object.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Philosophy of language is the study of the relations among language, meaning, and the world. Its central notions, following the primary literature [ref: TODO_REF Frege 1892], are:

- **Reference (Bedeutung)**: the object an expression picks out; for a singular term, its bearer; for a predicate, its extension.
- **Sense (Sinn)**: the mode of presentation by which an expression gives a thinker access to its reference; senses are shareable, objective contents that determine reference without being identical to it.
- **Intension**: a function from possible worlds to extensions; the truth-condition-relevant contribution of an expression (Carnap, Montague).
- **Extension**: the actual-world value of an expression; for a predicate, the set of objects it applies to.
- **Proposition**: the structured or functional content of a declarative sentence; the primary bearer of truth and falsity.
- **Speech act**: an act performed in saying something, analysed into the locutionary (saying it with sense and reference), the illocutionary (doing in saying: asserting, promising, ordering), and the perlocutionary (bringing about an effect on the hearer).

A theory of reference states a relation $\mathrm{Ref}(e, w) = o$ between an expression $e$, a possible world $w$, and an object $o$. Frege's two-level theory distinguishes sense from reference; Russell's theory of descriptions analyses definite descriptions as quantified phrases rather than singular terms; Kripke's rigid designation makes $\mathrm{Ref}$ constant across $w$ for proper names.

### Two live positions on reference

The reference relation is contested. The **descriptivist** position (Frege 1892; Russell 1905; Searle 1958) holds that a name refers because it is associated with a description, or a cluster of descriptions, satisfied uniquely by the bearer. The **direct-reference** position (Kripke 1980; Putnam 1975; Kaplan 1989) holds that a name refers directly, fixed by an initial baptism and a causal-historical chain of transmission, with no descriptive content mediating. The disagreement is not merely terminological: on the descriptivist view, "Aristotle might not have been a philosopher" is false or truth-valueless, because the description fixes the modal profile; on the direct-reference view it is true, because the man Aristotle could have led another life.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Sense is not connotation.* Sense is an objective, truth-relevant mode of presentation determining reference; connotation is a subjective association that varies from speaker to speaker.
- *Reference is not meaning.* Two expressions can share a reference and differ in meaning, which is the whole force of the morning-star puzzle.
- *Use is not a fourth ingredient.* "Meaning as use" (later Wittgenstein) is a reconception of meaning, not an extra layer stacked beside sense and reference.

## Key argument [Intermediate+]

**Russell's analysis of definite descriptions (1905).** Consider "The present King of France is bald." If the description "the present King of France" has reference, then there is a King; if it has none, the sentence would seem to lack a truth value. Yet the sentence feels false rather than truth-valueless, and it appears to entail "there is a King of France."

Russell's resolution is that surface grammar misleads. The sentence is not of subject-predicate form but a quantified structure:

$$\text{"The } F \text{ is } G\text{"} \;\equiv\; \exists x \bigl( F(x) \,\wedge\, \forall y\,(F(y) \to y=x) \,\wedge\, G(x) \bigr).$$

So "The present King of France is bald" comes out false, because there is no unique King, and it entails existence and uniqueness. Three claims, existence, uniqueness, and predication, are packed into one grammatical subject [ref: TODO_REF Russell 1905].

**Kripke's modal argument against descriptivism (1980).** Suppose, with the descriptivist, that "Aristotle" means "the pupil of Plato who wrote the Metaphysics." Then in any possible world, "Aristotle" denotes whatever satisfies that description. But there is a possible world in which Aristotle died in infancy and never met Plato; in that world the description denotes no one, or someone else, yet the name still refers to Aristotle. So the description cannot constitute the name's meaning. Proper names are **rigid designators**: they denote the same object in every world in which that object exists [ref: TODO_REF Kripke 1980].

The consequence is that identity statements between rigid designators, such as "Hesperus is Phosphorus," are necessary where true, yet known only a posteriori: the **necessary a posteriori**.

*Argument reconstructed.* (1) Names are rigid designators. (2) If $a$ and $b$ are rigid and $a = b$, then in every world in which both exist, $a = b$. (3) Therefore $a = b$ is necessary. (4) Yet such identities are discovered empirically. So necessity and a-priority come apart.

**Bridge.** This argument builds toward [42.02.01] (model theory and formal semantics), where rigid designation is precisified as a reference function constant across possible worlds within Kripke semantics, and appears again in [20.11.01] (metaphysics), where Kripke's necessity of identity grounds the modal metaphysics of natural kinds. The foundational reason reference theories recur across philosophy is that any claim about what is said presupposes a theory of how words hook onto objects; this is exactly the load that sense and reference carry; putting these together, the bridge is that sense, reference, and use form a single three-level structure, and the pattern generalises from natural language into the formal languages of logic, type theory, and programming.

## Advanced results [Master]

Three programmes dominate the contemporary literature, each a stance on what meaning is. They are not three stages of a settled consensus but live, partly incompatible positions; the field has not converged.

**Direct reference and the necessary a posteriori.** Kripke (1980) and Putnam (1975) argued that natural-kind terms ("water," "gold") and proper names are rigid designators fixed by causal-historical chains rather than descriptions. The identity "water is $\mathrm{H_2O}$" is, if true, necessary: there is no possible world in which the very stuff water is something other than $\mathrm{H_2O}$. Yet the identity is known only through chemistry. This separates the epistemic, a priori against a posteriori, from the metaphysical, necessary against contingent, a separation the descriptivist tradition could not accommodate [ref: TODO_REF Kripke 1980]. The **two-dimensionalist** reply (Chalmers, Jackson) argues that the necessity is an artefact of ignoring the *epistemic* intension, on which "water is $\mathrm{H_2O}$" is contingent; the dispute between direct reference and two-dimensionalism remains open.

**Meaning as use.** The later Wittgenstein rejected the search for a uniform essence of meaning in favour of **language games**: meaning is the role an expression plays in a rule-governed social practice. "The meaning of a word is its use in the language" (*Philosophical Investigations* §43) is a line from the primary text, offered as a slogan and explicitly hedged there, not a definition of the field. The secondary literature (Baker and Hacker; Kripke's *Wittgenstein on Rules and Private Language*) debates whether it yields a genuine sceptical paradox about rule-following. Read as a normative proposal, that philosophical puzzles about meaning dissolve once use is surveyed, it is contested rather than established.

**Speech acts and pragmatics.** Austin (1962) showed that many utterances do not describe but perform: promising, ordering, marrying, sentencing. Their success turns on **felicity conditions** (authority, procedure, sincerity), not on truth. Searle (1969) systematised illocutionary force into a taxonomy; Grice (1975) analysed speaker meaning and conversational implicature. The normative claim that assertion is governed by a constitutive rule, for instance "assert only what you know," is a live position in the knowledge-norm debate (Williamson and others) and not settled doctrine.

**Synthesis.** The three programmes, sense and reference, rigid designation, and meaning as use, build toward a single layered picture in which reference fixes the object, sense fixes the cognitive route, and use fixes the social force; this picture appears again in [20.11.01] (metaphysics), where rigid designation grounds necessary identity, and in [42.02.01] (model theory), where Kripke semantics formalises the possible-worlds reference function. The foundational reason these strands belong to one subject is that speaking is at once representing, referring, and acting; this is exactly why a philosophical account of language cannot stop at truth-conditions; putting these together, the bridge is that sense, reference, and use are three faces of the relation between words and world, and the pattern generalises into formal semantics and the type theories that ground programming languages.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Necessity of identity for rigid designators).** If $a$ and $b$ are rigid designators and $a = b$, then necessarily $a = b$.

*Argument.* Suppose $a = b$. Since $a$ is rigid, in every possible world in which the bearer of $a$ exists, $a$ denotes that same object; likewise $b$ denotes its bearer in every world in which that bearer exists. Because $a = b$ in the actual world, $a$ and $b$ have one and the same bearer. Hence in every world in which the bearer exists, $a$ denotes it and $b$ denotes it, so $a = b$ holds there. There is no world in which the bearer exists and $a \neq b$. Therefore $a = b$ is necessary. $\square$

This is Kripke's argument that identity between rigid designators is necessary wherever it holds. Its force depends on rigidity: a non-rigid descriptor such as "the inventor of the zip" can denote different objects in different worlds, so its identities can be contingent. Rigid designation is precisely what blocks that escape, linking the semantic thesis about names to the modal thesis about necessity, and underwriting the necessary-a-posteriori verdicts for natural-kind identities such as "water is $\mathrm{H_2O}$."

## Connections [Master]

- **Metaphysics [20.11.01].** Kripke's rigid designation and the necessity of identity, established in the metaphysics chapter, are the load-bearing apparatus for the necessary-a-posteriori identities developed here; philosophy of language supplies the semantic side of a modal thesis that metaphysics states in ontological terms.

- **Formal semantics and model theory [42.02.01].** Truth-conditional semantics, possible-worlds intensions, and Kripke semantics for modal logic supply the formal machinery into which the reference and intension notions of this unit are precisified; the boundary between philosophical and mathematical semantics runs directly through this connection.

- **Linguistics [51.01.01].** Speech-act theory and pragmatics, developed here in the work of Austin, Searle, and Grice, are imported by empirical linguistics under the heading of pragmatics, completing the levels of linguistic description that phonetics and syntax begin.

- **Philosophy of mind [20.13.01].** The content of mental states, what a thought is about, is analysed with the same sense/reference and intensional apparatus as linguistic meaning, making the theory of mental content a direct downstream application of the theory of language developed here.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Frege's "Über Sinn und Bedeutung" (1892) introduced the sense/reference distinction to solve the puzzle that identity statements can be informative: co-referential terms differ in Sinn, the mode of presentation, though sharing Bedeutung, the object picked out [ref: TODO_REF Frege 1892]. This is the founding move of analytic philosophy of language.

Russell's "On Denoting" (1905) attacked Frege's treatment of definite descriptions and empty names, replacing sense with the quantificational analysis that bears his name; Russell himself called it a paradigm of logical analysis. Wittgenstein's *Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus* (1921) built a picture theory of meaning on these foundations; his *Philosophical Investigations* (1953) repudiated that theory in favour of meaning as use, the most consequential internal reversal in the tradition [ref: TODO_REF Wittgenstein 1953].

The causal-historical turn came with Kripke's 1970 Princeton lectures, published as *Naming and Necessity* (1980), and Putnam's "The Meaning of 'Meaning'" (1975); together they replaced descriptivism with direct reference and reopened the modal questions that the earlier tradition had largely closed. Austin's 1955 William James lectures, published as *How to Do Things with Words* (1962), founded speech-act theory; Searle's *Speech Acts* (1969) and Grice's "Logic and Conversation" (1975) extended it into the pragmatics that now frames empirical linguistics [ref: TODO_REF Austin 1962].
