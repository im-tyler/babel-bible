## Intuition [Beginner]

Philosophy of mind asks what kind of thing a mind is, and how it fits into a world that physics describes as matter in motion. You have a body, a material object bound by the same laws as a stone. You also have thoughts, pains, and a private point of view. The puzzle is how these two pictures join.

This is the mind-body problem. It is older than any current science. In the seventeenth century Descartes argued that mind and body are two different substances: matter, which takes up space, and mind, which thinks. On his view a human is a thinking substance joined to an extended body. The difficulty is how two such different things interact at all, how a mere intention can move an arm.

Most contemporary philosophers reject Descartes's two-substances picture and accept physicalism: the world is wholly physical, and the mental must somehow be part of it. The disagreement is about how. Three answers dominate. Identity theory says mental states are brain states. Functionalism says they are functional roles, defined by what they do. Anomalous monism holds that each mental event is physical while there can be no strict laws joining the two vocabularies.

Two further features sharpen the dispute. First, qualia: the felt qualities of experience, such as the redness of red or the ache of pain. Second, intentionality: the aboutness of thought, the way a belief is aimed at the world. A full account of mind must handle both, and the hard problem asks why either should accompany a physical process at all.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Frank Jackson's knowledge argument (1982) isolates the question of felt experience in a single case. Mary is a brilliant neuroscientist raised in a black-and-white room. She has never seen a coloured object. Through textbooks and instruments she has learned every physical fact about colour vision: the wavelengths, the retinal cones, the cortical pathways, the lot.

Then Mary leaves the room and sees a red rose for the first time. She has the experience of red. The question: does she learn something new when she sees red, something she could not have known from all her physical facts?

Step 1. Before release, Mary knew every physical fact about colour vision. Step 2. On release she learns what red looks like. Step 3. So there is a fact about colour experience that is not a physical fact. If so, physicalism is incomplete.

What this tells us: the argument does not settle that physicalism is false. It sets the burden. A physicalist must explain why Mary gains nothing new, or how a new-seeming fact is nonetheless physical. The detailed responses — the ability hypothesis, the phenomenal-concepts strategy — are tracked in [20.06.01].

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Philosophy of mind is the study of the mental and its place in nature. Its central notions, following the primary literature [ref: TODO_REF Descartes 1641], [ref: TODO_REF Kim], are:

- **The mental**: states and events that have a subjective character or are directed at content — beliefs, desires, pains, sensations, thoughts, moods.

- **The physical**: states and events describable in the vocabulary of physics and the special sciences that reduce, in principle, to it — mass, charge, neural firing, chemical gradient.

- **Substance dualism**: the thesis that mind and body are distinct substances; matter is extended, mind is thinking, and they interact causally [ref: TODO_REF Descartes 1641].

- **Physicalism**: the thesis that everything, including the mental, is physical; nothing non-physical exists. Two main forms follow.

- **Type-identity theory (reductive physicalism)**: each mental state *type* is identical to a physical state type; pain = C-fibre firing, an a posteriori identity.

- **Functionalism**: a mental state is the *functional role* it occupies — the pattern of causal relations among sensory inputs, behavioural outputs, and other mental states; what realises the role can vary.

- **Anomalous monism**: every mental *event* (token) is a physical event, yet there are no strict psychophysical *laws*; the mental supervenes on the physical without being reducible to it [ref: TODO_REF Davidson 1970].

- **Supervenience**: the mental supervenes on the physical when, necessarily, no mental difference is possible without a physical difference. Formally, mental property $M$ supervenes on physical base $P$ when $\Box\,\forall x\,\forall y\,((P_x \wedge P_y) \to (M_x \leftrightarrow M_y))$.

- **Qualia** (singular *quale*): the subjective, phenomenal characters of experience — the "what it is like" to see red, taste salt, feel pain.

- **Intentionality**: the directedness or aboutness of mental states; a belief is *about* the world, a desire is *for* a state of affairs. Brentano took intentionality to be the mark of the mental [ref: TODO_REF Brentano 1874].

- **The hard problem of consciousness**: the question of why and how physical processes give rise to subjective experience at all, distinct from the "easy" functional problems of how a system discriminates, integrates, and reports.

### Two live positions on each contested question

The mind-body problem is contested at every level, and the literature requires at least two positions on each.

**On qualia.** The *property-dualist* position (Chalmers 1996) holds that phenomenal properties are non-physical properties of physical systems: complete physical knowledge leaves out what experience is like [ref: TODO_REF Chalmers 1996]. The *physicalist* position (Loar, Papineau, Balog — the phenomenal-concepts strategy) holds that phenomenal properties are identical to physical properties, and the appearance of a gap is produced by the special character of phenomenal concepts, not by a gap in the world. The disagreement is not terminological: the property dualist predicts an explanatory gap that no physical theory closes; the physicalist predicts a gap that closes once concepts are understood.

**On realisation.** The *functionalist* position holds that mental states are multiply realisable across substrates — carbon, silicon, alien biology — provided the functional organisation is right [ref: TODO_REF Putnam 1967]. The *biological-naturalist* position (Searle) and the *type-identity* position hold that mental states depend on specific biological (or physical) realisers; a silicon system that mimics pain's functional profile is not in pain. Which side one takes fixes whether artificial minds are in principle possible, a normatively loaded consequence for AI ethics and moral status.

**On mental causation.** Non-reductive physicalists hold that the mental, though supervenient, has its own causal efficacy. The *causal-exclusion* argument (Kim) replies that if every physical event has a sufficient physical cause, the mental cause is either identical to the physical cause or it is causally idle. The dispute is unresolved and structures the rest of the chapter.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Physicalism is not behaviourism.* Behaviourism identifies mental states with dispositions to behave; functionalism and identity theory both allow inner states with causal depth. A paralysed person in pain still feels pain.
- *Functionalism is not the claim that anything could be a mind.* Functionalism requires the right causal organisation; a random heap of sand realises no mental state, however large.
- *Supervenience is not reduction.* That the mental supervenes on the physical says there is no mental difference without a physical difference; it does not say mental vocabulary can be eliminated or replaced.
- *Intentionality is not phenomenal consciousness.* A belief can be about Paris without any distinctive feel; a pain can have a feel without being about anything. Block's distinction between access and phenomenal consciousness tracks a related but separate split.

## Key argument [Intermediate+]

**Putnam's multiple-realizability argument against type-identity theory (1967).** Type-identity says each mental state type $M$ is identical to a single physical state type $P$. Consider pain. If pain is identical to C-fibre firing (its human realiser), then an octopus, an advanced robot, or a silicon-based alien in pain could not exist, because none of them realises C-fibre firing. Yet each of these is conceivable and, on independent grounds, plausible: an octopus with a different neurochemistry can be in pain. So pain is not identical to C-fibre firing, nor to any single physical state type [ref: TODO_REF Putnam 1967].

*Argument reconstructed.*

1. If type-identity holds, each mental type is identical to exactly one physical type.
2. The same mental type (pain) can be realised by distinct physical types in different systems — neural tissue in humans, a different chemistry in octopuses, silicon in a robot.
3. If a type is identical to exactly one physical type, it cannot be realised by distinct physical types.
4. Therefore pain is not identical to any one physical type, and type-identity theory is false.

The conclusion forces a choice. **Functionalism** accepts it and relocates mental types at the level of functional role, which many substrates can fill. **The type-identity theorist** resists by restricting the identity to a species, or by allowing a disjunctive physical realiser. The disjunctive move is itself contested: a long disjunction "C-fibre firing, or octopus-state-17, or silicon-pattern-K" looks gerrymandered, and disjunctive properties seem to lack the causal unity that a genuine physical kind requires.

**Bridge.** This argument builds toward [20.11.01] (metaphysics), where the identity relation and the type/token distinction carry the metaphysics of properties and laws, and appears again in [20.06.01] (consciousness), where functionalism's role-based account meets the phenomenal residue of qualia. The foundational reason the argument matters is that any answer to the mind-body problem must take a stance on whether mental types are identical to, or merely realised by, physical types; this is exactly the load the identity relation carries across metaphysics and mind; putting these together, the bridge is that one and the same type/token distinction orders the debates about identity, realisation, and supervenience, and the pattern generalises into the multiple-realisation and realiser-dependence reasoning that recurs across cognitive science and the special sciences.

## Advanced results [Master]

Three live disputes carry the contemporary literature, each with at least two defended positions; none is settled.

**The hard problem and property dualism.** Chalmers argues that the explanatory gap between physical process and phenomenal experience is real and unbridgeable by any third-person theory: a being physically identical to you but lacking experience (a philosophical zombie) is conceivable, and what is ideally conceivable is metaphysically possible, so the physical facts do not entail the phenomenal facts [ref: TODO_REF Chalmers 1996]. The *property-dualist* conclusion is that phenomenal properties are irreducible non-physical properties of physical systems. The *physicalist* reply (the phenomenal-concepts strategy; type-B physicalism) accepts the appearance of a gap but traces it to the special first-person mode of presentation built into phenomenal concepts: the same physical property is grasped under two concepts, and the gap is conceptual rather than ontological. A further reply denies the conceivability premise outright, holding that a being physically identical to you is, by necessity, conscious. The deep point of contention is whether conceivability is a reliable guide to metaphysical possibility — the same question that Kripke's a posteriori necessities raise for the identity "water is H$_2$O". The phenomenal side of this debate, including the scientific theories (integrated information theory, global workspace theory), is developed in [20.06.01] and [20.06.03]; the present unit owns the metaphysical map.

**Anomalous monism and the causal-exclusion problem.** Davidson's anomalous monism secures token identity — every mental event is a physical event — while denying type identity and strict psychophysical laws, on the grounds that the mental is governed by principles of rationality that have no echo in physical law [ref: TODO_REF Davidson 1970]. Kim's exclusion argument replies that this is unstable: if every physical event has a sufficient physical cause, and mental-to-physical causation is not to be redundant overdetermination, then the mental cause must be identical to, or reducible to, its physical base. The non-reductive physicalist insists on distinct mental causation; the exclusionist concludes that genuine mental causation requires reduction; the epiphenomenalist accepts that the mental, qua mental, is causally idle. This is a contested question with at least three live positions, and the choice carries normative weight — whether responsibility, agency, and personhood can be sustained depends on it.

**Intentionality and its relation to consciousness.** Brentano's thesis that intentionality is the mark of the mental [ref: TODO_REF Brentano 1874] is resisted by those who note that there are states with phenomenal character but no determinate object (a free-floating anxiety) and states with intentionality but no distinctive feel (a tacit belief). Two positions structure the dispute. *Representationalism* (Tye, Dretske) identifies phenomenal character with a certain kind of intentional content, so that qualia reduce to representational properties. *Qualia realism* holds that phenomenal character outruns representational content and must be admitted in its own right. Block's distinction between access consciousness (information globally available for reasoning and report) and phenomenal consciousness (the felt quality) organises the empirical theories and keeps the two notions from being conflated [ref: TODO_REF Block].

These three disputes share a structure: each turns on whether the mental can be identified with, reduced to, or merely realised by something physical, and each leaves a residue — qualia, mental causation, subjective character — that resists clean absorption. The normative claim that this residue matters for ethics, law, and AI is itself a position in the literature, not a premise; functionalists and identity theorists are entitled to deny it.

**Synthesis.** The positions surveyed here build toward a single map of the mind-body problem: dualism, identity, functionalism, anomalous monism, and property dualism are stances on one continuous question about how the mental relates to the physical, and that map appears again in [20.06.01] (consciousness), where the hard problem and the scientific theories carry the phenomenal side forward, and in [20.11.01] (metaphysics), where supervenience and causal closure receive their general treatment. The foundational reason these strands belong together is that every stance commits on identity, realisation, and causation at once; this is exactly why no partial answer survives contact with the exclusion problem; the central insight is that the mental and the physical are related not by reduction alone but by layered dependence; putting these together, the bridge is that the same identity-modality-causation triad orders the whole chapter, and the pattern generalises into the philosophy of language (intentionality as content), cognitive science (functional architecture), and the special sciences (multiple realisation).

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Kim's causal-exclusion argument against irreducibly mental causation).** Under physical causal closure and the supervenience of the mental on the physical, mental causation that is distinct from physical causation is excluded.

*Argument.* Let $M$ be a mental event that allegedly causes a physical event $P^*$. Let $P$ be $M$'s physical supervenience base, so that $M$ could not occur without $P$.

1. **Physical causal closure.** Every physical event with a sufficient cause has a sufficient physical cause. So $P^*$ has a sufficient physical cause, call it $P$.
2. **Supervenience.** $M$ supervenes on $P$; there is no mental difference without a physical difference, so $M$ is fixed by $P$.
3. **No systematic overdetermination.** Causes are not, as a rule, doubly sufficient; we should not posit two independent sufficient causes for every behavioural effect.
4. **Exclusion.** $P$ is already a sufficient physical cause of $P^*$. If $M$ is a further cause distinct from $P$, then $P^*$ is overdetermined by $P$ and $M$, contrary to (3). So either $M$ is identical to $P$ (reduction), or $M$ is causally idle with respect to $P^*$ (epiphenomenalism).

Therefore irreducibly mental causation — distinct from, and not identical to, its physical base — is excluded. $\square$

The argument is valid given (1)–(3). The non-reductive physicalist resists by rejecting (3), allowing benign overdetermination, or by denying that $M$ and $P$ are causes at the same level; Kim replies that such moves either collapse into reduction or leave the mental description without work to do. Davidson's anomalous monism, in particular, is targeted: by securing token identity while denying type identity, it leaves the mental description of an event without a distinct causal role, since the physical description already supplies the cause.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Descartes's *Meditations on First Philosophy* (1641), especially the Sixth Meditation, introduced substance dualism as a "real distinction" between mind and body: the essence of mind is to think, the essence of body is to be extended, and the two are separable in principle even if joined in fact [ref: TODO_REF Descartes 1641]. The interaction problem — how an unextended mind moves extended matter — has haunted dualism ever since, and is the main reason contemporary dualists retreat to property dualism.

Brentano's *Psychology from an Empirical Standpoint* (1874) revived intentionality as the mark of the mental, drawing on medieval Aristotelian sources to argue that every mental act is directed at an object, a feature absent from physical phenomena [ref: TODO_REF Brentano 1874]. The twentieth century's analytic phase displaced Brentano with behaviourism, then replaced behaviourism in turn. Putnam's "Psychological Predicates" (1967) argued that mental states are multiply realisable and so cannot be type-identical to brain states, opening the way for functionalism [ref: TODO_REF Putnam 1967]. Davidson's "Mental Events" (1970) defended anomalous monism, a token-identity theory without psychophysical laws, and set the causal-exclusion problem that still organises the field [ref: TODO_REF Davidson 1970].

Nagel's "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (1974) reframed the mind-body problem around subjective character: there is something it is like to be a bat, and no objective description captures it [ref: TODO_REF Nagel 1974]. Jackson's "Epiphenomenal Qualia" (1982) sharpened the same point with Mary's room [ref: TODO_REF Jackson 1982]. Chalmers's *The Conscious Mind* (1996) synthesised these into the hard problem and a property-dualist framework defended by the conceivability of zombies [ref: TODO_REF Chalmers 1996], a primary text rather than a commentary. The field has not converged: physicalism, property dualism, panpsychism, and illusionism all retain active defenders.
