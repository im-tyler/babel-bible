## Intuition [Beginner]

The Hodge Laplacian is the form-valued version of the Laplace operator. It applies not only to scalar functions, but also to fields that measure circulation, flux, or volume.

FEEC solves this problem by keeping the exact, coexact, and harmonic parts visible. This matters because the harmonic part records topology, while the exact and coexact parts are controlled by the derivative operators.

The mixed finite element method introduces an extra unknown so the second-order Hodge Laplacian can be solved through a first-order system.

The goal is a mesh method that respects the same decomposition as the smooth problem.

## Visual [Beginner]

![A mixed Hodge Laplacian solve showing exact, coexact, and harmonic components represented on a mesh complex.](/img/mixed-fem-for-the-hodge-laplacian-placeholder.svg)

The discrete method approximates the form and its auxiliary coexact variable while tracking harmonic modes.

## Worked example [Beginner]

For ordinary Poisson, one can solve for a scalar potential. For a form-valued Hodge Laplacian, the unknown may represent a circulation field or flux field instead.

If the domain has a hole, there can be a harmonic mode going around that hole. A stable method must not mistake this mode for numerical noise.

What this tells us: the mixed Hodge Laplacian is where FEEC's topology-preserving design becomes necessary, not decorative.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $(W,d)$ be a Hilbert de Rham complex, with $W^k=H\Lambda^k(\Omega)$ in the standard FEEC setting. The Hodge Laplacian on $k$-forms is
$$
L=dd^*+d^*d.
$$
The mixed formulation introduces
$$
\sigma=d^*u,
$$
so that the problem is posed in first-order form.

A common weak mixed Hodge Laplacian formulation seeks
$$
(\sigma,u,p)\in V^{k-1}\times V^k\times \mathfrak H^k
$$
such that
$$
\langle \sigma,\tau\rangle-\langle u,d\tau\rangle=0
\quad\text{for all }\tau\in V^{k-1},
$$
$$
\langle d\sigma,v\rangle+\langle du,dv\rangle+\langle p,v\rangle
=\langle f,v\rangle
\quad\text{for all }v\in V^k,
$$
and
$$
\langle u,q\rangle=0
\quad\text{for all }q\in\mathfrak H^k.
$$
The harmonic variable $p$ and side condition enforce solvability modulo the kernel.

The FEEC discretization replaces $V^j$ by finite element spaces $V_h^j=\Lambda_h^j$ in a subcomplex and replaces $\mathfrak H^k$ by the discrete harmonic space $\mathfrak H_h^k$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The harmonic side condition is not optional on topologically nontrivial domains.
- The mixed formulation is not the same as applying scalar Poisson componentwise.
- The convergence theorem depends on the subcomplex and bounded projection structure, not only on polynomial degree.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (FEEC mixed Hodge Laplacian stability and convergence).** If the finite element spaces form a subcomplex and admit a bounded cochain projection, then the discrete mixed Hodge Laplacian problem is stable and converges to the continuous mixed solution with the FEEC a priori estimate pattern of [24.03.06].

**Proof.** The continuous mixed Hodge Laplacian is a saddle-point problem on a Hilbert complex. Its well-posedness depends on the Hodge decomposition, the Poincare inequality on the orthogonal complement of harmonic forms, and the harmonic side condition.

The finite element spaces form a subcomplex by [24.03.04]. The bounded cochain projection from [24.03.05] transfers the continuous Poincare inequality to a discrete Poincare inequality with constants independent of the mesh. This controls the discrete exact and coexact parts.

The discrete harmonic space approximates the continuous harmonic space through the projection/cohomology comparison. Therefore the kernel is handled correctly rather than producing spurious topological modes.

With stability in hand, Galerkin orthogonality and compatible approximation by polynomial differential forms give the error estimate from [24.03.06]. $\square$

**Bridge.** This unit applies the smooth Hodge Laplacian of [03.04.15] using the FEEC convergence machinery of [24.03.06]. It is the model application from which Maxwell [24.04.02] and elasticity [24.04.03] inherit the broader idea: preserve the underlying complex, then discretize.

## Advanced results [Master]

The mixed Hodge Laplacian is the canonical FEEC model problem because it uses all parts of the Hilbert-complex structure: exterior derivative, adjoint derivative, harmonic forms, Poincare estimates, and cohomology.

In block form, the discrete system has variables corresponding to $\sigma_h$, $u_h$, and $p_h$. The first block equation defines the auxiliary variable weakly. The second block equation represents the Hodge Laplacian equation. The third block equation imposes orthogonality to discrete harmonic forms.

The resulting matrix is a saddle-point system. It is sparse because the finite element bases are local, but it is indefinite because of the mixed structure and harmonic constraint. Solver design therefore uses block preconditioners, auxiliary-space methods, and multigrid ideas adapted to the de Rham complex.

The FEEC convergence theorem ensures that the method is stable under mesh refinement. The proof does not depend on accidental vector identities in one dimension or three dimensions; it uses Hilbert-complex arguments that apply across form degrees.

For simply connected domains and certain boundary conditions, the harmonic space may vanish in a given degree. In that case the side condition simplifies. On domains with nontrivial topology, the harmonic block is essential and carries the discrete cohomology information.

**Synthesis.** Mixed FEM for the Hodge Laplacian is FEEC's central application. The smooth operator decomposes forms into exact, coexact, and harmonic pieces; the finite element method must approximate all three in a compatible complex. The bounded projection and convergence theorem ensure that the discrete saddle-point system respects this structure and converges for the right reason.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (mixed formulation recovers the Hodge Laplacian).** If $\sigma=d^*u$ and the weak equations hold, then $u$ solves the Hodge Laplacian equation modulo the harmonic side condition.

**Proof.** The first weak equation states that $\sigma$ is the weak adjoint derivative of $u$. Substituting this into the second equation gives the weak action of $dd^*u+d^*du$, with the harmonic multiplier enforcing solvability against the kernel. Thus the mixed system represents $Lu=f$ on the orthogonal complement of harmonic forms. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (discrete harmonic side condition gives uniqueness modulo kernel).** If $u_h$ is required to be orthogonal to $\mathfrak H_h^k$, then the discrete solution is fixed within the orthogonal complement of the discrete harmonic kernel.

**Proof.** The Hodge Laplacian vanishes on harmonic forms. Without an orthogonality condition, adding a discrete harmonic form to a solution does not change the Laplacian equation. Requiring
$$
\langle u_h,q_h\rangle=0
$$
for every $q_h\in\mathfrak H_h^k$ selects the unique representative orthogonal to the discrete kernel. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (FEEC convergence applies).** The mixed Hodge Laplacian discretization converges under the assumptions of [24.03.06].

**Proof.** The spaces are a finite element subcomplex, and the bounded cochain projection exists by assumption. Therefore the hypotheses of the FEEC convergence theorem apply. Stability follows from the discrete Poincare estimate and harmonic-space control; the a priori estimate follows from compatible approximation and Galerkin orthogonality. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Arnold, Falk, and Winther use the Hodge Laplacian as the guiding model problem for finite element exterior calculus [ref: TODO_REF Arnold-Falk-Winther]. It is general enough to include the key analytic and topological issues but structured enough to reveal the role of complexes.

The mixed formulation reflects a broader lesson from mixed finite element methods: rewriting a second-order equation as a first-order system can expose the right variables and stability conditions.

FEEC's contribution is to show that for differential-form PDEs, the right variables are organized by the de Rham complex. The numerical method converges because it preserves that organization.
