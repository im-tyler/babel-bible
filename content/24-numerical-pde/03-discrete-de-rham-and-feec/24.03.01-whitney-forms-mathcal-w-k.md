## Intuition [Beginner]

Whitney forms turn mesh data into differential forms.

On a triangulated domain, a zero-cochain stores values on vertices, a one-cochain stores values on edges, a two-cochain stores values on faces, and so on. Whitney's construction spreads each of these discrete pieces across nearby simplices as a piecewise polynomial form.

This gives a bridge between topology and numerical analysis. The mesh still remembers cochains and incidence relations, but the resulting object is a form that can be integrated, differentiated, and used in finite element methods.

In FEEC, Whitney forms are the lowest-order example of structure-preserving finite element spaces.

## Visual [Beginner]

![A triangle with vertex, edge, and face data blended into scalar, line, and area-shaped Whitney forms.](/img/whitney-forms-mathcal-w-k-placeholder.svg)

Whitney forms attach a basis form to each oriented simplex and blend it using barycentric coordinates.

## Worked example [Beginner]

On an interval, the Whitney zero-forms are the familiar tent functions. One is equal to one at the left endpoint and fades to zero at the right endpoint; the other does the opposite.

The Whitney one-form represents the oriented edge. Integrating it over that edge gives one, while integrating it over the opposite orientation gives minus one.

What this tells us: Whitney forms are finite element basis functions with built-in orientation and integration rules.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $[v_0,\ldots,v_k]$ be an oriented $k$-simplex in a simplicial mesh, and let $\lambda_i$ denote the barycentric coordinate associated to $v_i$. The Whitney $k$-form attached to this oriented simplex is
$$
\mathcal W^{[v_0,\ldots,v_k]}
=k!\sum_{j=0}^k(-1)^j\lambda_j\,
d\lambda_0\wedge\cdots\wedge\widehat{d\lambda_j}\wedge\cdots\wedge d\lambda_k.
$$
The hat means that the indicated factor is omitted.

For $k=0$, this gives the barycentric coordinate functions:
$$
\mathcal W^{[v_i]}=\lambda_i.
$$
For $k=1$, the edge form is
$$
\mathcal W^{[v_i,v_j]}=\lambda_i\,d\lambda_j-\lambda_j\,d\lambda_i.
$$

The key interpolation property is integration duality:
$$
\int_{\sigma}\mathcal W^{\tau}=
\begin{cases}
1,&\sigma=\tau\text{ with the same orientation},\\
-1,&\sigma=\tau\text{ with the opposite orientation},\\
0,&\sigma\ne\tau
\end{cases}
$$
for matching-dimensional simplices in the local complex.

The span of Whitney $k$-forms is the lowest-order finite element space for $k$-forms. These spaces assemble into the lowest-order discrete de Rham complex.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- Whitney forms are not arbitrary polynomial forms. Their basis is tied to oriented simplices and barycentric coordinates.
- Orientation matters. Reversing the orientation of a simplex changes the sign of the associated Whitney form.
- Whitney forms are lowest-order FEEC spaces; higher-order FEEC spaces require polynomial differential forms beyond Whitney's original basis.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Whitney map is compatible with coboundary and exterior derivative).** Let $W$ be the Whitney map from simplicial cochains to piecewise differential forms. Then
$$
d\,W(c)=W(\delta c),
$$
where $\delta$ is the simplicial coboundary.

**Proof.** It is enough to check the identity on an oriented simplex basis cochain, because both sides are linear. The Whitney form of a simplex is built from barycentric coordinates and their exterior derivatives. Applying $d$ differentiates the barycentric-coordinate coefficients and uses $d^2\lambda_i=0$.

The resulting expression is an alternating sum of Whitney forms attached to the cofaces with the same incidence signs that appear in the simplicial coboundary. Those signs are exactly the orientation signs in the boundary/coboundary operator. Therefore exterior differentiation of the Whitney form reproduces the Whitney form of the coboundary cochain.

Equivalently, integration over every oriented simplex tests both sides in the same way. Stokes' theorem turns integration of $dW(c)$ over a simplex into integration of $W(c)$ over its boundary, which is precisely the simplicial coboundary pairing. Since Whitney basis forms are unisolvent under the simplex integration degrees of freedom, the two forms agree. $\square$

**Bridge.** Whitney forms connect the cochain language of [03.04.13] to the differential-form language of [03.04.02]. They extend the mesh basis intuition from [24.02.01] to all form degrees. In FEEC, this is the first concrete example of a discrete de Rham complex, preparing [24.03.03] and [24.03.04].

## Advanced results [Master]

Whitney forms realize simplicial cochains as piecewise polynomial differential forms. They are local, geometrically defined, and compatible with restriction to faces. These properties make them the natural lowest-order finite element spaces in the de Rham complex.

The de Rham map goes in the opposite direction: it sends a differential $k$-form to the simplicial $k$-cochain obtained by integrating over each oriented $k$-simplex. The Whitney map and de Rham map are not inverse on all forms, but together they build a bridge between smooth de Rham theory and simplicial cohomology.

In low degrees, Whitney forms recover familiar finite elements. Whitney zero-forms are continuous piecewise linear Lagrange basis functions. Whitney one-forms are the lowest-order edge elements. In three dimensions, Whitney two-forms correspond to lowest-order face/flux elements, and Whitney three-forms correspond to piecewise constant volume forms.

The identity $dW=W\delta$ is the earliest commuting-diagram mechanism in this story. It says that topology and calculus agree at the mesh level. FEEC generalizes this mechanism to higher-order polynomial differential forms and bounded cochain projections.

Whitney forms also clarify why degrees of freedom are integrals over geometric entities. A $k$-form naturally integrates over a $k$-dimensional simplex. This pairing between form degree and simplex dimension is the organizing principle behind compatible finite element spaces.

**Synthesis.** Whitney forms are the first place where mesh topology, differential forms, and finite element approximation become one construction. They convert oriented simplices into basis forms, preserve coboundary/exterior-derivative structure, and recover the lowest-order compatible elements used in computation. FEEC takes Whitney's insight and builds a full approximation theory around it.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (degree-zero Whitney forms form a partition of unity).** On a simplex, the Whitney zero-forms satisfy
$$
\sum_i \mathcal W^{[v_i]}=1.
$$

**Proof.** Since $\mathcal W^{[v_i]}=\lambda_i$, the claim is the barycentric-coordinate identity
$$
\sum_i\lambda_i=1.
$$
$\square$

**Proposition 2 (edge Whitney form changes sign under orientation reversal).** For an edge,
$$
\mathcal W^{[v_j,v_i]}=-\mathcal W^{[v_i,v_j]}.
$$

**Proof.** By the one-form formula,
$$
\mathcal W^{[v_j,v_i]}=\lambda_j\,d\lambda_i-\lambda_i\,d\lambda_j
=-(\lambda_i\,d\lambda_j-\lambda_j\,d\lambda_i).
$$
This is $-\mathcal W^{[v_i,v_j]}$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Stokes proof of cochain compatibility).** The identity $dW=W\delta$ follows from Stokes' theorem and simplex integration degrees of freedom.

**Proof.** Let $\sigma$ be an oriented simplex of dimension one higher than the cochain degree. Then
$$
\int_\sigma dW(c)=\int_{\partial\sigma}W(c)
$$
by Stokes' theorem. The right side is the simplicial pairing of $c$ with $\partial\sigma$, which is the pairing of $\delta c$ with $\sigma$. By the defining integration degrees of freedom for Whitney forms, this equals
$$
\int_\sigma W(\delta c).
$$
Since the simplex integration degrees of freedom determine the Whitney form in the space, the identity follows. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Differential forms [03.04.02].** Whitney forms are piecewise polynomial differential forms attached to mesh simplices.

- **Singular cohomology [03.04.13].** Simplicial cochains and coboundaries are the topological input.

- **Classical conforming FEM [24.02.01].** Whitney zero-forms recover the lowest-order scalar conforming finite element basis.

- **Nédélec edge elements [24.03.02].** Whitney one-forms are the lowest-order edge elements in the FEEC sequence.

- **Discrete de Rham complex [24.03.04].** Whitney forms give the prototype finite-dimensional subcomplex.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Whitney introduced these forms in *Geometric Integration Theory* as part of a program connecting integration, cochains, and differential forms [ref: TODO_REF Whitney]. This predates modern FEEC but already contains the key compatibility idea.

Computational electromagnetism later rediscovered the practical importance of Whitney forms because edge and face quantities are naturally integrated over geometric mesh entities [ref: TODO_REF Bossavit] [ref: TODO_REF Hiptmair].

Arnold, Falk, and Winther placed Whitney forms inside the broader FEEC framework as the lowest-order case of compatible finite element spaces [ref: TODO_REF Arnold-Falk-Winther]. The modern lesson is that a stable finite element space should respect the same algebraic topology as the continuous differential complex.
