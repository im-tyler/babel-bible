## Intuition [Beginner]

The finite element method solves a weak PDE by replacing an infinite set of possible functions with a finite set of mesh-based functions.

For a conforming method, every finite element trial function already belongs to the correct Sobolev space. This means the discrete problem is a smaller version of the continuous weak problem, not a different problem with incompatible functions.

The simplest example uses piecewise linear tent functions on a triangulated domain. The unknown solution is approximated by choosing coefficients for those local basis functions.

The main error question is: how close can this finite-dimensional space get to the true weak solution?

## Visual [Beginner]

![A curved solution over a domain approximated by piecewise linear finite element tent functions on a triangular mesh.](/img/classical-conforming-fem-galerkin-cea-bramble-hilbert-placeholder.svg)

The mesh-based function is rougher than the true solution, but it lives in the same weak energy space.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Suppose a one-dimensional interval is split into four smaller intervals. A piecewise linear finite element function is determined by its values at the mesh nodes.

Between adjacent nodes, the graph is a straight line. Across the whole interval, the graph is continuous but usually has corners at the nodes.

This is why Sobolev spaces matter: the function is not classically smooth everywhere, but it is still a valid weak trial function for many elliptic problems.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $V$ be a Hilbert space and let
$$
a(u,v)=F(v)\quad\text{for all }v\in V
$$
be a continuous coercive weak problem as in [24.01.03]. A **conforming Galerkin method** chooses a finite-dimensional subspace $V_h\subset V$ and solves:
$$
a(u_h,v_h)=F(v_h)\quad\text{for all }v_h\in V_h.
$$

For a scalar second-order elliptic problem, $V$ is often $H^1_0(\Omega)$ and $V_h$ is a space of continuous piecewise polynomials on a shape-regular triangulation.

Galerkin orthogonality says
$$
a(u-u_h,v_h)=0\quad\text{for all }v_h\in V_h.
$$

**Céa's lemma** states that if $a$ is continuous with constant $M$ and coercive with constant $\alpha$, then
$$
\|u-u_h\|_V\leq \frac{M}{\alpha}\inf_{v_h\in V_h}\|u-v_h\|_V.
$$
Thus the FEM error is controlled by the best approximation error in the finite element space.

The **Bramble-Hilbert lemma** supplies local polynomial approximation estimates in Sobolev norms. In a typical form, if $u$ has $m$ derivatives and polynomials of degree less than $m$ are reproduced, then local interpolation error is bounded by a mesh-size power times a Sobolev seminorm of $u$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- Galerkin approximation is not automatically good; it is good when the space approximates well and the weak problem is stable.
- Céa's lemma gives quasi-best approximation, not an exact error value.
- Bramble-Hilbert is an approximation theorem, not a PDE existence theorem.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Céa's lemma).** Let $a$ be continuous and coercive on $V$, with constants $M$ and $\alpha$. Let $u\in V$ solve the continuous problem and $u_h\in V_h$ solve the conforming Galerkin problem. Then
$$
\|u-u_h\|_V\leq \frac{M}{\alpha}\inf_{v_h\in V_h}\|u-v_h\|_V.
$$

**Proof.** For any $v_h\in V_h$, coercivity gives
$$
\alpha\|u-u_h\|_V^2\leq a(u-u_h,u-u_h).
$$
Write
$$
u-u_h=(u-v_h)+(v_h-u_h).
$$
Since $v_h-u_h\in V_h$, Galerkin orthogonality gives
$$
a(u-u_h,v_h-u_h)=0.
$$
Therefore
$$
a(u-u_h,u-u_h)=a(u-u_h,u-v_h).
$$
Continuity gives
$$
a(u-u_h,u-v_h)\leq M\|u-u_h\|_V\|u-v_h\|_V.
$$
If $u\ne u_h$, divide by $\|u-u_h\|_V$ to obtain
$$
\|u-u_h\|_V\leq \frac{M}{\alpha}\|u-v_h\|_V.
$$
Taking the infimum over $v_h\in V_h$ proves the claim. $\square$

**Bridge.** This unit turns the weak formulation of [24.01.03] into the classical conforming FEM pipeline. Sobolev approximation estimates from [24.01.01] become numerical error estimates through Céa's lemma and Bramble-Hilbert. The next step, [24.02.02], applies the same variational logic to mixed Poisson, where conformity and stability require different spaces and the Babuška-Brezzi condition from [24.01.04].

## Advanced results [Master]

The conforming FEM error pipeline has three ingredients: stability of the weak problem, Galerkin orthogonality, and approximation by local polynomials.

For Lagrange finite elements on a shape-regular triangulation, local interpolation operators reproduce polynomials up to a fixed degree. Bramble-Hilbert estimates the interpolation error by comparing the target function to the best local polynomial approximation. Scaling from a reference element to a physical element produces powers of the mesh diameter.

In a typical elliptic estimate for degree $r$ elements, if the exact solution has sufficient Sobolev regularity, then the $H^1$ error decays like a power of $h$ determined by the polynomial degree and smoothness. The $L^2$ error often improves through a duality argument, sometimes called the Aubin-Nitsche trick.

The assembled finite element system has entries
$$
A_{ij}=a(\phi_j,\phi_i),\qquad F_i=F(\phi_i),
$$
where $\{\phi_i\}$ is a basis of $V_h$. Local support of basis functions makes the stiffness matrix sparse, which is one reason FEM is computationally effective.

Classical conforming FEM is the scalar baseline for FEEC. Later FEEC units replace scalar $H^1$ Lagrange spaces with differential-form spaces that conform to $H\Lambda^k$, but the logic remains recognizable: choose conforming finite-dimensional spaces, prove stability, estimate approximation, and conclude convergence.

**Synthesis.** Classical conforming FEM works because the discrete space is a stable subspace of the weak problem. Céa's lemma reduces PDE error to approximation theory; Bramble-Hilbert supplies local polynomial approximation rates; assembly turns the weak equations into sparse linear algebra. FEEC generalizes this pattern from scalar spaces to de Rham complexes.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (finite-dimensional well-posedness).** If $a$ is coercive on $V$, then the conforming Galerkin problem has a unique solution in $V_h$.

**Proof.** Since $V_h$ is a finite-dimensional subspace of $V$, the restrictions of $a$ and $F$ to $V_h$ are continuous. Coercivity on $V$ restricts to coercivity on $V_h$. Applying Lax-Milgram on the Hilbert space $V_h$ gives existence and uniqueness. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (symmetric energy projection).** If $a$ is symmetric and coercive, then $u_h$ is the best approximation to $u$ in the energy norm $\|w\|_a^2=a(w,w)$.

**Proof.** For any $v_h\in V_h$,
$$
u-v_h=(u-u_h)+(u_h-v_h).
$$
Galerkin orthogonality gives $a(u-u_h,u_h-v_h)=0$. Therefore
$$
\|u-v_h\|_a^2=\|u-u_h\|_a^2+\|u_h-v_h\|_a^2\geq \|u-u_h\|_a^2.
$$
Thus $u_h$ minimizes the energy error over $V_h$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Bramble-Hilbert use pattern).** Suppose an interpolation operator $I_h$ satisfies a local estimate
$$
\|u-I_hu\|_{H^1(K)}\leq C h_K^r |u|_{H^{r+1}(K)}
$$
on each element $K$. Then Céa's lemma gives a global $H^1$ FEM error estimate of the same order on a shape-regular mesh.

**Proof.** Choose $v_h=I_hu$ in Céa's lemma. Sum the squared local interpolation estimates over the mesh. Shape regularity keeps the constants uniform over elements, yielding
$$
\|u-u_h\|_{H^1(\Omega)}\leq C h^r |u|_{H^{r+1}(\Omega)}.
$$
$\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Weak formulation of elliptic PDE [24.01.03].** Conforming FEM is the finite-dimensional Galerkin restriction of a weak problem.

- **Sobolev spaces [24.01.01].** Error estimates are measured in Sobolev norms and seminorms.

- **Babuška-Brezzi condition [24.01.04].** Mixed FEM requires a saddle-point stability condition beyond scalar coercivity.

- **Mixed FEM for Poisson [24.02.02].** The mixed method adapts Galerkin principles to flux and scalar unknowns in different spaces.

- **Whitney forms [24.03.01].** Whitney forms are the lowest-order FEEC analogue of mesh-based conforming basis functions.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Courant's 1943 paper is often cited as an early finite element ancestor because it used piecewise linear functions in variational problems on triangulated domains [ref: TODO_REF Courant].

Galerkin's earlier residual-orthogonality idea and the later Lax-Milgram framework supplied the abstract variational setting. Céa's lemma then clarified why conforming Galerkin methods are quasi-best approximations [ref: TODO_REF Cea].

Bramble and Hilbert supplied the approximation-theoretic estimate that turns polynomial reproduction into Sobolev error bounds [ref: TODO_REF Bramble-Hilbert]. Ciarlet systematized the finite element definition through the triple of element domain, local polynomial space, and degrees of freedom [ref: TODO_REF Ciarlet].
