## Intuition [Beginner]

A Sobolev space measures two things at once: how large a function is and how rough its changes are.

Classical calculus asks for derivatives to exist point by point. PDEs and numerical methods often need a more flexible idea. A function may have corners, jumps in slope, or only average smoothness, while still having enough structure for an energy calculation.

Sobolev spaces make that possible. They treat derivatives in a weak, averaged sense and then put a norm on the function plus its derivatives.

For finite elements, this is the right language because piecewise polynomial functions are often not classically smooth across element boundaries, but they still have the exact weak smoothness needed by the PDE.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Imagine a function shaped like a tent: it rises in a straight line, reaches a peak, and then falls in a straight line. At the peak there is a corner, so the ordinary derivative at that exact point is not defined.

Even so, the slope is meaningful on each side. The corner is only one point, and energy calculations based on average slope still make sense.

That is why a tent function is a natural finite-element basis function. It is not perfectly smooth, but it belongs to the basic energy space used for many elliptic PDEs.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $\Omega\subset\mathbb R^n$ be an open set. For an integer $k\geq 0$ and $1\leq p\leq\infty$, the Sobolev space $W^{k,p}(\Omega)$ consists of functions $u\in L^p(\Omega)$ whose weak derivatives $D^\alpha u$ belong to $L^p(\Omega)$ for every multi-index $\alpha$ with $|\alpha|\leq k$.

For $1\leq p<\infty$, the norm is
$$
\|u\|_{W^{k,p}(\Omega)}
=\left(\sum_{|\alpha|\leq k}\|D^\alpha u\|_{L^p(\Omega)}^p\right)^{1/p}.
$$
For $p=\infty$, one uses the maximum of the essential suprema of the weak derivatives.

The Hilbert-space case is
$$
H^k(\Omega)=W^{k,2}(\Omega).
$$
Fractional spaces $H^s(\Omega)$ can be defined by Fourier methods on $\mathbb R^n$, by interpolation, or by restriction and extension from ambient spaces, depending on the setting.

A weak derivative is defined by integration by parts: a locally integrable function $v$ is the weak derivative $D^\alpha u$ if
$$
\int_\Omega u\,D^\alpha\phi
=(-1)^{|\alpha|}\int_\Omega v\,\phi
$$
for every smooth compactly supported test function $\phi$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- Sobolev differentiability is not the same as pointwise differentiability.
- The space $H^1(\Omega)$ is not usually a space of continuous functions in high dimension.
- Boundary values of Sobolev functions require trace theorems; they are not automatic pointwise restrictions.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (completeness of $W^{k,p}$).** For $1\leq p\leq\infty$, the space $W^{k,p}(\Omega)$ is a Banach space. In particular, $H^k(\Omega)$ is a Hilbert space.

**Proof.** Let $(u_j)$ be a Cauchy sequence in $W^{k,p}(\Omega)$. Then for each multi-index $\alpha$ with $|\alpha|\leq k$, the sequence $(D^\alpha u_j)$ is Cauchy in $L^p(\Omega)$. Since $L^p(\Omega)$ is complete, there are functions $v_\alpha\in L^p(\Omega)$ such that
$$
D^\alpha u_j\to v_\alpha
$$
in $L^p$.

For $\alpha=0$, write $u=v_0$. It remains to check that $v_\alpha$ is the weak derivative $D^\alpha u$. For each test function $\phi$, the weak derivative identity holds for $u_j$:
$$
\int_\Omega u_j\,D^\alpha\phi
=(-1)^{|\alpha|}\int_\Omega D^\alpha u_j\,\phi.
$$
Passing to the limit in $L^p$ and using the dual pairing with the fixed smooth compactly supported function $\phi$ gives
$$
\int_\Omega u\,D^\alpha\phi
=(-1)^{|\alpha|}\int_\Omega v_\alpha\,\phi.
$$
Hence $v_\alpha=D^\alpha u$ weakly. Thus $u\in W^{k,p}(\Omega)$ and $u_j\to u$ in the Sobolev norm.

When $p=2$, the norm comes from the inner product
$$
\langle u,v\rangle_{H^k}
=\sum_{|\alpha|\leq k}\langle D^\alpha u,D^\alpha v\rangle_{L^2},
$$
so $H^k(\Omega)$ is a Hilbert space. $\square$

**Bridge.** Sobolev spaces build from [02.11.04] because their completeness is inherited from $L^p$ Banach spaces. They feed directly into [24.01.03], where elliptic PDEs are rewritten as variational problems on Sobolev spaces, and into [24.01.02], where the same idea is applied to differential forms. The foundational reason is that weak derivatives preserve integration-by-parts identities while allowing rougher functions. This is exactly what finite element methods need: basis functions may be piecewise polynomial rather than classically smooth, but the weak formulation still makes sense.

## Advanced results [Master]

The main Sobolev-space tools for elliptic PDE are traces, extensions, embeddings, and compact embeddings.

The trace theorem says that on a sufficiently regular bounded domain, boundary restriction extends continuously from smooth functions to Sobolev functions. For example, the trace map sends $H^1(\Omega)$ continuously to a fractional Sobolev space on $\partial\Omega$. This is what makes Dirichlet boundary conditions meaningful for weak solutions.

Extension theorems say that functions on a regular domain can be extended to the ambient Euclidean space without losing Sobolev regularity. These results let one transfer Fourier-space arguments from $\mathbb R^n$ to domains.

Sobolev embedding theorems compare differentiability and integrability. Roughly, enough weak derivatives in $L^p$ force improved integrability, and in stronger regimes force continuity. The precise exponents depend on dimension, which is why high-dimensional PDE requires more regularity to obtain pointwise control.

The Rellich-Kondrachov theorem gives compact embeddings on bounded regular domains. A standard case is that $H^1(\Omega)$ embeds compactly into $L^2(\Omega)$ when $\Omega$ is bounded and Lipschitz. This compactness is one of the engines behind existence proofs for elliptic PDE and convergence arguments for numerical approximations.

**Compact-embedding proof sketch.** On a cube, translate estimates show that bounded sets in $H^1$ are uniformly stable under small translations in $L^2$. The Kolmogorov-Riesz compactness criterion then gives precompactness in $L^2$. On a bounded Lipschitz domain, an extension operator transfers the problem to a cube or to $\mathbb R^n$ with bounded support. Therefore every bounded sequence in $H^1(\Omega)$ has a subsequence converging in $L^2(\Omega)$ [ref: TODO_REF Adams-Fournier].

In finite element analysis, the most visible Sobolev spaces are $H^1(\Omega)$ for scalar elliptic problems, $H^1_0(\Omega)$ for homogeneous Dirichlet problems, $L^2(\Omega)$ for forcing and error measurement, and later $H(\mathrm{curl})$ and $H(\mathrm{div})$ for vector and form-valued problems. The latter are best understood in the differential-form language of [24.01.02].

**Synthesis.** Sobolev spaces are the functional-analytic compromise between classical differentiability and measurable roughness. They retain integration by parts, completeness, Hilbert geometry in the $p=2$ case, and enough compactness to prove existence and convergence. This makes them the natural substrate for weak PDE formulations and finite element methods.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (weak derivatives agree with classical derivatives).** If $u$ is continuously differentiable and its classical derivative $D_i u$ is locally integrable, then $D_i u$ is also the weak derivative.

**Proof.** For every compactly supported smooth test function $\phi$, ordinary integration by parts gives
$$
\int_\Omega u\,D_i\phi=-\int_\Omega D_i u\,\phi,
$$
with no boundary term because $\phi$ has compact support. This is exactly the weak derivative identity. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (closedness of the weak derivative operator).** If $u_j\to u$ in $L^p$ and $D_i u_j\to v$ in $L^p$, then $v=D_i u$ weakly.

**Proof.** For every compactly supported smooth test function $\phi$,
$$
\int_\Omega u_j\,D_i\phi=-\int_\Omega D_i u_j\,\phi.
$$
Passing to the limit in the two $L^p$ pairings gives
$$
\int_\Omega u\,D_i\phi=-\int_\Omega v\,\phi.
$$
Hence $v$ is the weak derivative of $u$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Poincare inequality gives an equivalent norm on $H^1_0$).** On a bounded regular domain, the seminorm $\|\nabla u\|_{L^2}$ is equivalent to the full $H^1$ norm on $H^1_0(\Omega)$.

**Proof.** The full norm controls the gradient seminorm by definition. The reverse estimate follows from the Poincare inequality
$$
\|u\|_{L^2}\leq C\|\nabla u\|_{L^2}
$$
for $u\in H^1_0(\Omega)$. Therefore
$$
\|u\|_{H^1}^2=\|u\|_{L^2}^2+\|\nabla u\|_{L^2}^2
\leq (C^2+1)\|\nabla u\|_{L^2}^2.
$$
Thus the two norms are equivalent. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Sobolev introduced the functional-analytic spaces now bearing his name in the 1930s as part of the modern theory of PDE [ref: TODO_REF Sobolev]. The decisive move was to measure differentiability through integral norms and distributional identities rather than pointwise formulas.

Later PDE texts such as Evans and monographs such as Adams-Fournier systematized the modern toolkit: weak derivatives, traces, extensions, embeddings, and compactness [ref: TODO_REF Evans] [ref: TODO_REF Adams-Fournier].

Finite element theory adopted Sobolev spaces because variational methods naturally measure energy rather than pointwise smoothness. A piecewise linear finite element function may not be classically twice differentiable, but it can still be a valid $H^1$ trial function. This is the bridge from analysis to computation.
