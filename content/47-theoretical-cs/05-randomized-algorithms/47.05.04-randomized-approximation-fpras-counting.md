## Intuition [Beginner]

Counting is harder than deciding. Asking "does a satisfying assignment exist?" is one thing. Asking "how many satisfying assignments are there?" is another entirely. The first is NP. The second is #P, a class that captures counting problems, and it is believed to be much harder.

But we can often estimate the count without computing it exactly. Think of estimating the number of fish in a lake. You cannot count every fish, but you can catch some, tag them, release them, catch more, and see what fraction are tagged. This Monte Carlo approach gives an estimate whose accuracy improves with more samples.

A Fully Polynomial-Time Randomised Approximation Scheme (FPRAS) does exactly this for counting problems. Given a counting function $f$ and an error parameter $\varepsilon$, it runs in time polynomial in the input size and $1/\varepsilon$, and outputs an estimate within a factor of $(1 + \varepsilon)$ of the true count with high probability. The key insight is that random sampling, done carefully, can turn an intractable exact counting problem into a tractable approximate one.

Two landmark examples drive the theory. Counting satisfying assignments of a DNF formula has a simple FPRAS using the Karp-Luby method. Approximating the permanent of a matrix (equivalent to counting perfect matchings) has a sophisticated FPRAS via Markov chain Monte Carlo.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We estimate the number of satisfying assignments of a DNF formula using the Karp-Luby method.

Formula: $\varphi = (x_1 \wedge x_2) \vee (\bar{x}_3 \wedge x_4)$ over 4 variables.

**Step 1: Identify the clauses.** $C_1 = x_1 \wedge x_2$ has $2^{4-2} = 4$ satisfying assignments (the two specified variables are fixed, the other two are free). $C_2 = \bar{x}_3 \wedge x_4$ also has $2^{4-2} = 4$ satisfying assignments.

**Step 2: Compute the union size.** The total number of assignments satisfying at least one clause is at most $|S_1| + |S_2| = 4 + 4 = 8$, but this overcounts assignments satisfying both clauses. Both $C_1$ and $C_2$ are satisfied when $x_1 = 1, x_2 = 1, x_3 = 0, x_4 = 1$ — only one assignment. So $|S_1 \cup S_2| = 4 + 4 - 1 = 7$.

**Step 3: Estimate via sampling.** The Karp-Luby trick: instead of sampling from $\{0,1\}^4$, sample uniformly from $S_1 \cup S_2$ (the satisfying assignments) and use the estimator $X = (|S_1| + |S_2|) \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{sample is in exactly one } S_i]$. The simpler version: sample a random clause $C_i$ with probability $|S_i| / (|S_1| + |S_2|)$, then sample a random assignment from $S_i$, and check if it satisfies any earlier clause.

The estimate is $\hat{N} = |S_1| \cdot (\text{fraction of samples unique to } C_1) + |S_2| \cdot (\text{fraction unique to } C_2)$. With enough samples, this converges to 7.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Definition (FPRAS).** A fully polynomial-time randomised approximation scheme for a function $f : \Sigma^* \to \mathbb{N}$ is a randomised algorithm $A$ that, on input $x \in \Sigma^*$ and $\varepsilon, \delta \in (0, 1)$, runs in time $\text{poly}(|x|, 1/\varepsilon, \log(1/\delta))$ and outputs $A(x)$ satisfying:

$$\Pr[(1 - \varepsilon) f(x) \leq A(x) \leq (1 + \varepsilon) f(x)] \geq 1 - \delta.$$

**Definition (#P).** A function $f : \Sigma^* \to \mathbb{N}$ is in #P if there exists a polynomial-time nondeterministic Turing machine $M$ such that $f(x) = |{\text{accepting paths of } M \text{ on } x}|$.

**Definition (permanent).** The permanent of an $n \times n$ matrix $A$ is:

$$\text{perm}(A) = \sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \prod_{i=1}^n a_{i, \sigma(i)}.$$

Unlike the determinant, the permanent does not include signs from the permutation parity.

**Definition (DNF counting).** Given a DNF formula $\varphi = C_1 \vee C_2 \vee \cdots \vee C_m$ over $n$ variables, compute $|\{x \in \{0,1\}^n : \varphi(x) = 1\}|$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"FPRAS means the algorithm is always correct."* No. It has a failure probability $\delta$ that can be made arbitrarily small by repetition. A single run has at least 2/3 success probability.

- *"The permanent equals the determinant."* The determinant has signs: $\det(A) = \sum_\sigma \text{sgn}(\sigma) \prod_i a_{i,\sigma(i)}$. The permanent drops the signs. This small difference makes the determinant computable in polynomial time but the permanent #P-complete.

- *"If a problem has an FPRAS, then exact counting is also easy."* No. DNF counting is #P-complete (so exact counting is hard), but it has an FPRAS. Approximate counting can be much easier than exact counting.

### Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Karp-Luby FPRAS for DNF counting).** *There is an FPRAS for counting the number of satisfying assignments of a DNF formula.*

*Proof sketch.* Let $\varphi = C_1 \vee \cdots \vee C_m$ be a DNF formula over $n$ variables. For each clause $C_i$, let $S_i = \{x \in \{0,1\}^n : C_i(x) = 1\}$ be the set of assignments satisfying $C_i$. We want $|\bigcup_i S_i|$.

*The estimator.* Define $\Omega = \{(i, x) : x \in S_i\}$ with $|\Omega| = \sum_i |S_i|$. Each element of $\Omega$ is a (clause, satisfying assignment) pair. Sample uniformly from $\Omega$ by first picking clause $C_i$ with probability $|S_i| / |\Omega|$, then picking $x \in S_i$ uniformly at random.

Define $Y = |\Omega|$ if the sampled pair $(i, x)$ satisfies $x \notin S_j$ for all $j < i$ (i.e., $x$ does not satisfy any earlier clause), and $Y = 0$ otherwise.

*Expectation.* $E[Y] = |\Omega| \cdot \Pr[\text{no earlier clause satisfied}] = |\Omega| \cdot |\bigcup_i S_i| / |\Omega| \cdot (\text{correction})$. More precisely, each assignment $x \in \bigcup_i S_i$ contributes to $Y$ exactly once (when it is sampled with its minimum-index satisfied clause). So $E[Y] = |\bigcup_i S_i|$.

*Variance bound.* The ratio $E[Y^2] / E[Y]^2$ is bounded by a polynomial because each clause has at most $2^{n - |C_i|}$ satisfying assignments, and the number of overlapping clauses is bounded by the formula structure. Taking $O(1/\varepsilon^2)$ independent samples and averaging gives a $(1 \pm \varepsilon)$ estimate with constant probability. Repeating and taking the median boosts to probability $1 - \delta$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Karp-Luby estimator is the foundational reason DNF counting admits an FPRAS: by sampling from the union of satisfying sets via a weighted clause distribution, the variance is controlled enough for polynomial-sample convergence, and this builds toward the Markov chain methods needed for harder counting problems like the permanent where direct sampling from the solution space is impossible. This is exactly the pattern that appears again in the JSV algorithm where a rapidly-mixing Markov chain replaces uniform sampling, and it generalises to the entire field of approximate counting via self-reducibility. The central insight is that the gap between #P-completeness and approximability is bridged by random sampling with controlled variance, and putting these together with the hashing techniques of [47.05.01] yields the full machinery of approximate counting.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short answer).**

Explain why there is no FPRAS for counting satisfying assignments of a CNF formula unless NP = RP.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If there were an FPRAS for #SAT (CNF), then we could determine whether a CNF formula is satisfiable by running the FPRAS and checking whether the estimate is positive or zero. An FPRAS with $\varepsilon < 1$ on a satisfiable formula would return a positive number (since $f(x) \geq 1$ and the estimate is at least $(1-\varepsilon)f(x) > 0$), while on an unsatisfiable formula the true count is 0 and the estimate must be 0 (since the only possible output consistent with $(1-\varepsilon) \cdot 0 \leq \hat{f} \leq (1+\varepsilon) \cdot 0$ is $\hat{f} = 0$). This would solve SAT in randomised polynomial time, so NP $\subseteq$ RP.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the permanent of a 0-1 matrix $A$ equals the number of perfect matchings in the bipartite graph $G = (U \cup V, E)$ where $U = \{u_1, \ldots, u_n\}$, $V = \{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$, and $(u_i, v_j) \in E$ iff $a_{ij} = 1$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A perfect matching in $G$ is a set $M \subseteq E$ of $n$ edges such that every vertex is incident to exactly one edge of $M$. Equivalently, $M = \{(u_1, v_{\sigma(1)}), \ldots, (u_n, v_{\sigma(n)})\}$ for some permutation $\sigma$ where all edges exist. The edge $(u_i, v_{\sigma(i)})$ exists iff $a_{i,\sigma(i)} = 1$. So $\prod_i a_{i,\sigma(i)} = 1$ iff $\sigma$ corresponds to a valid perfect matching. Summing over all permutations: $\text{perm}(A) = \sum_\sigma \prod_i a_{i,\sigma(i)}$ counts exactly the number of valid perfect matchings.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

The self-reducibility of the permanent means $\text{perm}(A) = a_{11} \cdot \text{perm}(A_{11}) + \ldots + a_{1n} \cdot \text{perm}(A_{1n})$ where $A_{1j}$ is the minor obtained by deleting row 1 and column $j$. Show how this self-reducibility can be used to construct a telescoping-product estimator for $\text{perm}(A)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Define $Z_i = \text{perm}(A_i)$ where $A_i$ is the matrix after fixing the first $i$ rows, and estimate each ratio $Z_i / Z_{i-1}$ separately.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Define $A^{(0)} = A$. For $k = 1, \ldots, n$, let $p_k = \Pr[\text{column } j \text{ chosen for row } k] = a_{k,j} \cdot \text{perm}(A^{(k-1)}_{kj}) / \text{perm}(A^{(k-1)})$. The self-reducibility gives $\text{perm}(A^{(k-1)}) = \sum_j a_{kj} \cdot \text{perm}(A^{(k-1)}_{kj})$. Define $Z_k = \text{perm}(A^{(k)})$ where $A^{(k)}$ is the $(n-k) \times (n-k)$ minor after fixing rows $1, \ldots, k$. Then $\text{perm}(A) = Z_0 = (Z_0/Z_1) \cdot (Z_1/Z_2) \cdots (Z_{n-1}/Z_n)$ where $Z_n = 1$.

Each ratio $Z_{k-1}/Z_k$ equals $1/p_k$ where $p_k$ is the probability of choosing column $j_k$ for row $k$ under the conditional distribution. An FPRAS estimates each $p_k$ (hence each ratio) by sampling from the distribution over columns using the JSV Markov chain. The product of $n$ estimated ratios gives the permanent estimate. The total error is controlled because each ratio estimate contributes a multiplicative $(1 \pm \varepsilon/n)$ factor, and the product telescopes. Rubric: full credit for setting up the telescoping product and connecting each ratio to the conditional probability.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the Markov chain for sampling perfect matchings (used in the JSV algorithm) is aperiodic and irreducible on the state space of all perfect and near-perfect matchings of a bipartite graph.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show that every state has a self-loop (aperiodicity) and that any matching can be reached from any other via a sequence of edge switches (irreducibility).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The state space consists of all matchings (perfect and near-perfect, where a near-perfect matching misses exactly two vertices, one from each side). The transitions are: (1) select an edge $e$ uniformly at random; if $e \in M$, remove it with probability $1/2$; if $e \notin M$, add it if both endpoints are free; (2) select a 2-edge path $(e_1, e_2)$ and swap (add $e_1$, remove $e_2$ or vice versa).

*Self-loop (aperiodicity):* For any matching $M$, the transition "select an edge $e \in M$ and choose not to remove it" gives a self-loop with positive probability. So all states have self-loops, implying aperiodicity.

*Irreducibility:* Given two matchings $M_1$ and $M_2$, we show a path exists. The symmetric difference $M_1 \triangle M_2$ is a collection of alternating paths and cycles. Each alternating cycle can be traversed by a sequence of swap transitions: walk around the cycle, swapping edges in and out. Each alternating path can be handled by adding the free edge, then swapping along the path. Repeating this for all components of $M_1 \triangle M_2$ transforms $M_1$ into $M_2$. Near-perfect matchings are included because an edge removal creates one. So the chain is irreducible. Rubric: full credit for proving both aperiodicity and irreducibility.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short answer).**

Explain why the FPRAS for the permanent of nonnegative matrices does not extend to matrices with negative entries. What additional barrier arises?

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The FPRAS for nonnegative permanents relies on the Markov chain sampling matchings from a probability distribution proportional to $\prod_e w_e$. When weights are nonnegative, this defines a valid probability distribution. With negative entries, the "distribution" assigns negative weights to some matchings, making it impossible to sample directly. The permanent becomes a signed sum $\sum_\sigma \text{sgn}(\sigma) \prod a_{i,\sigma(i)}$, where cancellations between positive and negative terms can reduce the magnitude drastically relative to the individual terms. The signal-to-noise ratio blows up: the permanent might be close to zero while individual terms are large, making relative approximation impossible without exact computation. This is why computing the permanent of general integer matrices is #P-hard to even approximate within any factor.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

Three directions extend the basic FPRAS framework.

**Result 1 (rapid mixing via canonical paths).** The JSV Markov chain achieves rapid mixing in time $O(n^7 \log(1/\varepsilon))$. The proof uses the canonical paths method: for each pair of states $(M_1, M_2)$, define a canonical path through the state space. The congestion of the canonical paths bounds the spectral gap, which bounds the mixing time. The key technical ingredient is the fact that the space of perfect and near-perfect matchings forms a connected graph under the switch operation, and the number of canonical paths through any transition edge is bounded by a polynomial in $n$ [ref: TODO_REF JSV 2004].

**Result 2 (FPRAS for other #P-complete problems).** Several other #P-complete counting problems admit FPRAS: the number of independent sets in a bipartite graph (via the hard-core model Markov chain), the number of linear extensions of a partial order (via Karzanov-Khachiyan chain), and the partition function of the ferromagnetic Ising model (via Jerrum-Sinclair). The common technique is: identify a Markov chain on the solution space, prove rapid mixing (via canonical paths or coupling), and use the mixing time to bound the sample complexity for the estimator [ref: TODO_REF Arora-Barak Ch. 17].

**Result 3 (connections to approximate counting and approximate sampling).** The equivalence between approximate counting and approximate sampling (Jerrum, Valiant and Vazirani 1986) states that for self-reducible problems, an FPRAS exists iff there is a polynomial-time algorithm for approximate uniform sampling from the solution space. This equivalence is the structural reason why FPRAS constructions always go through a sampling algorithm: the JSV chain samples matchings approximately uniformly, and the telescoping product converts this into a count estimate [ref: TODO_REF Arora-Barak Ch. 17].

**Synthesis.** The FPRAS framework is the foundational reason that #P-completeness does not preclude efficient approximate counting: self-reducibility connects counting to sampling, and rapidly-mixing Markov chains provide the sampling engine. The central insight is that the variance of a well-designed estimator can be bounded independently of the exact count, so a polynomial number of samples suffices for a $(1 \pm \varepsilon)$ estimate. This builds toward the derandomisation questions of [47.05.05] where eliminating the randomness from these schemes would imply collapse of the polynomial hierarchy, and this is exactly the pattern that appears again in [47.02.07] where Toda's theorem shows that the counting hierarchy contains the entire polynomial hierarchy. Putting these together with the hashing techniques of [47.05.01] yields the full picture: randomised approximate counting is possible for a rich class of #P-complete problems, and the boundary between approximable and inapproximable counting problems maps to the boundary between NP and harder complexity classes.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Karp-Luby estimator is unbiased).** *The Karp-Luby estimator $Y$ for $|\bigcup_{i=1}^m S_i|$ satisfies $E[Y] = |\bigcup_{i=1}^m S_i|$.*

*Proof.* The sample space is $\Omega = \{(i, x) : x \in S_i\}$ with uniform distribution. Define $Y(i, x) = |\Omega|$ if $x \notin S_j$ for all $j < i$, and $Y(i, x) = 0$ otherwise.

For each $x \in \bigcup_i S_i$, let $i^*(x) = \min\{i : x \in S_i\}$. The pair $(i^*(x), x) \in \Omega$ satisfies the condition $x \notin S_j$ for $j < i^*(x)$, so $Y(i^*(x), x) = |\Omega|$.

For any other pair $(i, x)$ with $i > i^*(x)$ and $x \in S_i$, we have $x \in S_{i^*(x)}$ with $i^*(x) < i$, so $Y(i, x) = 0$.

For pairs $(i, x)$ with $i < i^*(x)$ and $x \in S_i$, this is impossible since $i^*(x) = \min\{i : x \in S_i\}$.

So $Y$ is nonzero only on pairs $(i^*(x), x)$, one per element $x \in \bigcup_i S_i$. Therefore:

$$E[Y] = \frac{1}{|\Omega|} \sum_{(i,x) \in \Omega} Y(i, x) = \frac{1}{|\Omega|} \cdot |\bigcup_i S_i| \cdot |\Omega| = |\bigcup_i S_i|. \quad \square$$

**Proposition 2 (sample complexity of FPRAS).** *If an unbiased estimator $Y$ for $f(x)$ satisfies $E[Y^2] \leq c \cdot f(x)^2$ for some constant $c$, then $O(c/\varepsilon^2)$ independent copies of $Y$ suffice to obtain a $(1 \pm \varepsilon)$-estimate of $f(x)$ with probability at least 2/3.*

*Proof.* Let $\bar{Y} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^N Y_k$ where the $Y_k$ are independent copies of $Y$. By Chebyshev's inequality:

$$\Pr[|\bar{Y} - f(x)| > \varepsilon f(x)] \leq \frac{\text{Var}(\bar{Y})}{\varepsilon^2 f(x)^2} = \frac{E[Y^2] - f(x)^2}{N \varepsilon^2 f(x)^2} \leq \frac{c - 1}{N \varepsilon^2}.$$

Setting $N = 3(c-1)/\varepsilon^2$ makes this at most $1/3$. So $\Pr[(1-\varepsilon)f(x) \leq \bar{Y} \leq (1+\varepsilon)f(x)] \geq 2/3$. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The theory of approximate counting was pioneered by Karp and Luby (1983) who gave the first FPRAS for DNF counting. The broader framework connecting counting and sampling was developed by Jerrum, Valiant and Vazirani (1986), who proved the equivalence of approximate counting and approximate sampling for self-reducible problems. The crowning achievement is the Jerrum-Sinclair-Vigoda algorithm (2004) for the permanent, which resolved a major open problem by providing an FPRAS for one of the most natural #P-complete functions.

The permanent has a storied history: it was defined by Cauchy in 1812 alongside the determinant, and its computational complexity was studied by Marcus and Minc from the 1960s. Valiant's 1979 proof that computing the permanent of a 0-1 matrix is #P-complete (even for 0-1 matrices) established that exact computation is intractable. The JSV algorithm showed that approximate computation is feasible, completing the picture [ref: TODO_REF JSV 2004].

The philosophical lesson is that the gap between exact and approximate computation can be vast: a problem can be #P-complete (and hence at least as hard as any NP problem) yet admit an efficient randomised approximation. Randomness, in this context, is not a convenience but a necessity—derandomising the JSV algorithm would imply a collapse of the polynomial hierarchy.
