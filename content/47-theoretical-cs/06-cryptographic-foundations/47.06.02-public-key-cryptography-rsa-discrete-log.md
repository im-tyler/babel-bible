## Intuition [Beginner]

Symmetric cryptography uses the same key to encrypt and decrypt. The problem: how do two people agree on a shared key without meeting? Public-key cryptography solves this elegantly. Everyone has two keys: a public key (shared with the world) and a private key (kept secret). Anyone can encrypt a message using your public key, but only you can decrypt it using your private key.

The trick relies on mathematical asymmetry. RSA uses the fact that multiplying two large primes is easy, but factoring their product is hard. Diffie-Hellman uses the fact that computing $g^a \bmod p$ is easy, but recovering $a$ from $g^a \bmod p$ (the discrete logarithm problem) is hard.

RSA works as follows. You pick two large primes $p$ and $q$, multiply them to get $N = pq$, and publish $N$ along with an encryption exponent $e$. To send you a message $m$, someone computes $m^e \bmod N$. You decrypt by computing $(m^e)^d \bmod N = m$ using a secret decryption exponent $d$. The relationship between $e$ and $d$ involves Euler's totient function, and computing $d$ from $N$ and $e$ requires factoring $N$, which is believed to be hard.

The beauty of public-key cryptography is that it transforms the key-distribution problem into a computational-hardness problem. Security is not absolute but conditional: if factoring is hard (or discrete log is hard), then the system is secure.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We walk through RSA with toy parameters. Pick primes $p = 11$ and $q = 3$.

**Key generation.** $N = 11 \times 3 = 33$. $\phi(N) = (11-1)(3-1) = 20$. Choose $e = 3$ (coprime to 20). Compute $d = e^{-1} \bmod 20$. We need $3d \equiv 1 \pmod{20}$. Testing: $3 \times 7 = 21 \equiv 1 \pmod{20}$, so $d = 7$.

Public key: $(N, e) = (33, 3)$. Private key: $d = 7$.

**Encryption.** Message $m = 14$. Compute $c = 14^3 \bmod 33$. $14^2 = 196 = 5 \times 33 + 31$, so $14^2 \equiv 31 \pmod{33}$. Then $14^3 \equiv 31 \times 14 = 434 = 13 \times 33 + 5$, so $c = 5$.

**Decryption.** Compute $m = 5^7 \bmod 33$. $5^2 = 25$, $5^4 = 625 = 18 \times 33 + 31$, so $5^4 \equiv 31$. $5^7 = 5^4 \times 5^2 \times 5 = 31 \times 25 \times 5 = 3875 = 117 \times 33 + 14$, so $m = 14$. Correct.

**Why it works.** Euler's theorem says $m^{\phi(N)} \equiv 1 \pmod{N}$ when $\gcd(m, N) = 1$. Since $ed = 3 \times 7 = 21 = 1 + 1 \times 20 = 1 + \phi(N)$, we have $m^{ed} = m^{1 + \phi(N)} = m \cdot m^{\phi(N)} \equiv m \cdot 1 = m \pmod{N}$.

**Exercise (easy, true-false).**

True or false: RSA encryption is secure because the RSA function $m \mapsto m^e \bmod N$ is a one-way function.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**False (nuanced).** The RSA function is a *trapdoor* one-way function: it is one-way without the trapdoor (the factorisation of $N$), but easy to invert with the trapdoor ($d$ computed from the factorisation). A plain one-way function would not allow decryption. The trapdoor property is what makes public-key cryptography possible.

</details>

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Definition (RSA assumption).** Given a product $N = pq$ of two random $n$-bit primes and a public exponent $e$ coprime to $\phi(N)$, for every PPT algorithm $A$:

$$\Pr[A(N, e, m^e \bmod N) = m] \leq \text{negl}(n)$$

where $m$ is chosen uniformly from $\mathbb{Z}_N^*$.

**Definition (discrete logarithm problem).** Given a cyclic group $G$ of prime order $q$, a generator $g$, and $h = g^a$ for random $a \in \mathbb{Z}_q$, the discrete logarithm problem is to find $a$.

**Definition (CDH assumption).** For a cyclic group $G$ with generator $g$, given $(g, g^a, g^b)$ for random $a, b$, no PPT algorithm can compute $g^{ab}$ with non-negligible probability.

**Definition (DDH assumption).** For a cyclic group $G$ with generator $g$, no PPT algorithm can distinguish $(g, g^a, g^b, g^{ab})$ from $(g, g^a, g^b, g^c)$ for random $a, b, c$ with non-negligible advantage.

**Definition (IND-CPA security).** A public-key encryption scheme $(KeyGen, Enc, Dec)$ is IND-CPA secure if no PPT adversary $A$ can win the following game with non-negligible advantage: (1) $A$ receives the public key $pk$, (2) $A$ submits two messages $m_0, m_1$, (3) the challenger encrypts $m_b$ for random $b$, (4) $A$ outputs a guess $b'$, and $A$ wins if $b' = b$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"RSA with $e = 3$ is always insecure."* RSA with small $e$ is vulnerable to specific attacks (e.g., Coppersmith's attack when $m$ is much smaller than $N$), but not inherently insecure when proper padding (OAEP) is used.

- *"Diffie-Hellman is the same as RSA."* DH is based on the discrete logarithm problem in a group; RSA is based on factoring. They are different hardness assumptions, though both are broken by Shor's quantum algorithm.

- *"IND-CPA security means the encryption is unbreakable."* IND-CPA only protects against passive eavesdroppers who see ciphertexts. It does not protect against chosen-ciphertext attacks (IND-CCA), which is the standard security requirement for real systems.

### Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (RSA correctness).** *Let $N = pq$ for distinct primes $p, q$, $e$ coprime to $\phi(N) = (p-1)(q-1)$, and $d = e^{-1} \bmod \phi(N)$. Then for all $m \in \mathbb{Z}_N^*$, $(m^e)^d \equiv m \pmod{N}$.*

*Proof.* Since $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(N)}$, we have $ed = 1 + k\phi(N)$ for some integer $k$. By Euler's theorem, $m^{\phi(N)} \equiv 1 \pmod{N}$ for any $m$ coprime to $N$. Therefore:

$$m^{ed} = m^{1 + k\phi(N)} = m \cdot (m^{\phi(N)})^k \equiv m \cdot 1^k = m \pmod{N}.$$

This works even when $\gcd(m, N) \neq 1$ (i.e., when $m$ is a multiple of $p$ or $q$), by applying the Chinese Remainder Theorem: modulo $p$, $m^{ed} \equiv m^{1+k(p-1)(q-1)} \equiv m \cdot (m^{p-1})^{k(q-1)} \equiv m \cdot 1 = m \pmod{p}$ by Fermat's little theorem (if $p \nmid m$; if $p | m$ then both sides are 0 mod $p$). Similarly mod $q$. By CRT, $m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod{N}$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The RSA construction is the foundational reason public-key cryptography works: the algebraic structure of modular arithmetic provides a function that is a permutation (so decryption is always possible) yet one-way without the trapdoor (so only the private key holder can decrypt). This builds toward the Diffie-Hellman key exchange which achieves a different goal (key agreement rather than encryption) using a different algebraic structure (group exponentiation rather than ring exponentiation). The central insight is that trapdoor one-way functions arise from the gap between computing a function and inverting it, and this is exactly the pattern that appears again in [47.06.04] where digital signatures use the same RSA function in reverse (sign with $d$, verify with $e$). Putting these together with the one-way function theory of [47.06.01] shows that public-key cryptography requires structured hardness assumptions (factoring, discrete log) that go beyond the generic OWF, and this structural requirement is the reason public-key primitives cannot be constructed from generic OWFs via black-box reductions.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Compute RSA decryption for $p = 7$, $q = 11$, $e = 7$, and ciphertext $c = 32$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$N = 77$, $\phi(N) = 6 \times 10 = 60$. Compute $d = 7^{-1} \bmod 60$. $7 \times 43 = 301 = 5 \times 60 + 1$, so $d = 43$. Decrypt: $m = 32^{43} \bmod 77$. Using repeated squaring: $32^2 = 1024 \equiv 1024 - 13 \times 77 = 1024 - 1001 = 23 \pmod{77}$. $32^4 \equiv 23^2 = 529 \equiv 529 - 6 \times 77 = 529 - 462 = 67 \equiv -10$. $32^8 \equiv 100 \equiv 23$. $32^{16} \equiv 23^2 = 529 \equiv 67 \equiv -10$. $32^{32} \equiv 100 \equiv 23$. $32^{43} = 32^{32} \times 32^8 \times 32^2 \times 32 = 23 \times 23 \times 23 \times 32$. $23 \times 23 = 529 \equiv 67$. $67 \times 23 = 1541 \equiv 1541 - 20 \times 77 = 1541 - 1540 = 1$. $1 \times 32 = 32$. So $m = 32$. (This makes sense: $m = 32$, $c = 32^7 \bmod 77 = 32$, so $32^7 \equiv 32 \bmod 77$, meaning $32^6 \equiv 1 \bmod 77$, which is true since $32^6 = (32^3)^2 = 32768^2$ ... actually $\text{ord}(32) = 6$ divides $\phi(77) = 60$.)

**Exercise 2 (easy, true-false).**

True or false: in Diffie-Hellman, the shared secret $g^{ab}$ is computed without Alice or Bob ever sending $a$ or $b$ to each other.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**True.** Alice sends $A = g^a \bmod p$ and Bob sends $B = g^b \bmod p$. Alice computes $B^a = g^{ab}$ and Bob computes $A^b = g^{ab}$. Neither sends their private exponent.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that if the DDH assumption holds in a group $G$, then the CDH assumption also holds in $G$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose CDH does not hold: there exists PPT algorithm $A$ that computes $g^{ab}$ from $(g, g^a, g^b)$ with non-negligible probability. We build a DDH distinguisher $D$: given $(g, g^a, g^b, g^c)$, run $A$ on $(g, g^a, g^b)$ to get candidate $h$. If $h = g^c$, output 1 (DDH tuple). Otherwise output 0 (random tuple).

On DDH tuples $(g, g^a, g^b, g^{ab})$: $A$ outputs $g^{ab}$ with non-negligible probability, so $D$ outputs 1 with non-negligible probability. On random tuples $(g, g^a, g^b, g^c)$ for random $c$: $A$ outputs $g^{ab}$ which equals $g^c$ only with negligible probability $1/|G|$ (since $c$ is uniform and independent of $a, b$). So $D$ distinguishes with non-negligible advantage, contradicting DDH.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that textbook RSA (without padding) is not IND-CPA secure.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The adversary $A$ receives public key $(N, e)$. It submits two messages $m_0 = 1$ and $m_1 = 2$. The challenger encrypts $m_b$: $c = m_b^e \bmod N$. If $b = 0$, $c = 1^e = 1$. If $b = 1$, $c = 2^e \bmod N \neq 1$ (for $N > 2$). The adversary checks: if $c = 1$, output $b' = 0$; otherwise output $b' = 1$. This succeeds with probability 1, breaking IND-CPA completely.

Even with messages that are not readily distinguishable, RSA is deterministic: encrypting the same message always gives the same ciphertext. So $A$ can encrypt both candidate messages itself and compare. Formally: $A$ computes $c_0 = m_0^e \bmod N$ and $c_1 = m_1^e \bmod N$, receives $c$, and outputs $b'$ such that $c_{b'} = c$. This succeeds with probability 1.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short answer).**

Explain why Shor's quantum algorithm breaks both RSA and Diffie-Hellman. What is the common structure it exploits?

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Both RSA and Diffie-Hellman reduce to finding the period of a function. For RSA (factoring $N = pq$), the function is $f(x) = a^x \bmod N$ for random $a$ coprime to $N$. The period $r$ satisfies $a^r \equiv 1 \pmod{N}$. If $r$ is even, then $(a^{r/2})^2 \equiv 1 \pmod{N}$, so $\gcd(a^{r/2} - 1, N)$ gives a factor of $N$ with probability $\geq 1/2$. For discrete log ($h = g^a \bmod p$, find $a$), the function is $f(x, y) = g^x h^y \bmod p$ with period $(a, -1)$ in $(\mathbb{Z}_p)^2$. Shor's algorithm uses the quantum Fourier transform to find the period in polynomial time, breaking both problems. The common structure is a periodic function over a finite abelian group, and quantum Fourier sampling extracts the period efficiently.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that RSA with $e = 2$ (Rabin encryption) is as hard to invert as factoring $N = pq$. Specifically, show that an algorithm $A$ that computes square roots modulo $N$ can be used to factor $N$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Given $N = pq$ and a square-root oracle $A$ that on input $y$ returns $x$ with $x^2 \equiv y \pmod{N}$:

Pick a random $r \in \mathbb{Z}_N^*$. Compute $y = r^2 \bmod N$. Feed $y$ to $A$ to get $r'$ with $r'^2 \equiv r^2 \pmod{N}$. Then $(r')^2 - r^2 \equiv 0 \pmod{N}$, so $(r' - r)(r' + r) \equiv 0 \pmod{N}$.

If $r' \not\equiv \pm r \pmod{N}$, then neither $r' - r$ nor $r' + r$ is divisible by $N$ (since $0 < |r' \pm r| < N$ with high probability), but their product is. This means $\gcd(r' - r, N)$ is a nontrivial factor of $N$.

The probability that $r' \equiv \pm r \pmod{N}$ is $1/2$ (since $y = r^2$ has four square roots modulo $N$: $\pm r \bmod p$ and $\pm r \bmod q$, combined via CRT into four roots, two of which are $\pm r$). So the algorithm succeeds with probability $1/2$ per trial, and expected $O(1)$ trials suffice. Rubric: full credit for the $\gcd$ argument and the $1/2$ probability analysis.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The ElGamal encryption scheme works as follows in a group $G$ of order $q$ with generator $g$: key generation picks random $x \in \mathbb{Z}_q$ and sets $pk = g^x$, $sk = x$; encryption of message $m$ picks random $r$ and outputs $(g^r, m \cdot (pk)^r)$; decryption of $(c_1, c_2)$ computes $m = c_2 / c_1^x$. Prove that ElGamal is IND-CPA secure if and only if DDH holds in $G$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*DDH implies IND-CPA.* In the IND-CPA game, the adversary receives $pk = g^x$ and challenge ciphertext $(g^r, m_b \cdot g^{xr})$. The value $g^{xr} = (g^x)^r = pk^r$ is the Diffie-Hellman shared secret. By DDH, $(g, g^x, g^r, g^{xr})$ is indistinguishable from $(g, g^x, g^r, g^s)$ for random $s$. If we replace $g^{xr}$ with a random $g^s$, the ciphertext becomes $(g^r, m_b \cdot g^s)$, and $m_b \cdot g^s$ is uniformly random (since $g^s$ is a uniform group element masking $m_b$). So the adversary gains no information about $b$.

*IND-CPA implies DDH.* Suppose DDH does not hold: there exists a distinguisher $D$ for $(g, g^a, g^b, g^c)$ vs. $(g, g^a, g^b, g^{ab})$. We build an IND-CPA adversary $A$ for ElGamal: receive $pk = g^a$, submit $m_0 = 1, m_1 = g^{c-ab}$ (computed using the DDH challenge), receive ciphertext $(g^r, m_b \cdot g^{ar}) = (g^b, m_b \cdot g^{ab})$ where we identify $r = b$. If $c = ab$, then $m_1 = g^0 = 1 = m_0$ and both messages are identical; if $c \neq ab$, then $m_1 \neq m_0$, and $A$ can distinguish. A cleaner reduction: given a DDH challenge $(g, g^a, g^b, g^c)$, set $pk = g^a$, encrypt the identity element to get $(g^b, g^c)$, and use $D$ to check if this is a valid ElGamal ciphertext (which requires $g^c = g^{ab}$). Rubric: full credit for both directions.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short answer).**

Why is the Learning With Errors (LWE) problem considered quantum-resistant, unlike RSA and discrete log?

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

LWE asks: given a matrix $A$ and vector $b = As + e \bmod q$ for secret $s$ and small error vector $e$, find $s$. The best known classical and quantum algorithms for LWE require $2^{O(n)}$ time for $n$-dimensional instances (the best algorithms are lattice reduction algorithms like BKZ, which run in $2^{O(n/\log n)}$ time). Unlike the period-finding structure exploited by Shor's algorithm for factoring and discrete log, LWE does not reduce to finding the period of a function over a finite abelian group. The quantum Fourier transform does not directly help with lattice problems. LWE is also connected to worst-case lattice problems (GapSVP, SIVP) via the Regev reduction (2005), so solving LWE on average would solve these worst-case problems, which have resisted quantum attack for decades.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

Three results sharpen our understanding of public-key cryptography's foundations and limitations.

**Result 1 (random oracle model and beyond).** The security of RSA-OAEP (RSA with Optimal Asymmetric Encryption Padding) was proven IND-CCA secure in the random oracle model by Bellare and Rogaway (1994), assuming the RSA problem is hard. Fujisaki, Okamoto, Pointcheval and Stern (2004) proved that any IND-CPA scheme can be transformed into an IND-CCA scheme in the random oracle model. However, Canetti, Goldreich and Halevi (1998) showed that there exist schemes secure in the random oracle model that are insecure when the random oracle is instantiated with any concrete hash function [ref: TODO_REF Arora-Barak Ch. 10].

**Result 2 (lattice-based cryptography and quantum resistance).** Regev (2005) introduced the Learning With Errors problem and proved that solving LWE on average is as hard as solving worst-case lattice problems (GapSVP, SIVP) for quantum algorithms. This worst-case-to-average-case reduction is the foundational reason lattice-based cryptography is believed to be quantum-resistant. The NIST post-quantum standardisation process selected Kyber (key encapsulation) and Dilithium (signatures), both based on module-LWE [ref: TODO_REF Shor 1997].

**Result 3 (Impagliazzo's five worlds).** Impagliazzo (1995) described five possible complexity-theoretic worlds, ordered by the strength of available cryptographic primitives. In Pessiland (NP is hard on average but one-way functions do not exist), no public-key cryptography is possible. In Minicrypt (OWFs exist but not trapdoor OWFs), symmetric cryptography exists but public-key cryptography does not. In Cryptomania (trapdoor OWFs exist), full public-key cryptography is available. The question of which world we inhabit is the central open problem of cryptographic foundations [ref: TODO_REF Arora-Barak Ch. 10].

**Synthesis.** Public-key cryptography rests on structured hardness assumptions that go beyond generic one-way functions: factoring and discrete logarithm provide the algebraic structure needed for trapdoor operations, and this builds toward the quantum-resistance question where lattice problems replace number-theoretic problems as the hardness foundation. The central insight is that the algebraic structure enabling public-key encryption (modular exponentiation, group operations) is also the structure that quantum algorithms exploit (period finding), and this is exactly the pattern that appears again in [47.06.03] where zero-knowledge proofs require similar algebraic structure for their interaction protocols. Putting these together with the one-way function theory of [47.06.01] shows that the cryptographic landscape is stratified: generic OWFs yield symmetric cryptography, structured assumptions yield public-key cryptography, and quantum-resistant assumptions yield post-quantum cryptography, each layer requiring more specific and more structured hardness.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (RSA correctness via CRT).** *For $N = pq$, $ed \equiv 1 \pmod{\phi(N)}$, and any $m \in \mathbb{Z}_N$: $m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod{N}$.*

*Proof.* It suffices to show $m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod{p}$ and $m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod{q}$, then invoke CRT.

*Case 1: $p \nmid m$.* By Fermat's little theorem, $m^{p-1} \equiv 1 \pmod{p}$. Since $ed = 1 + k(p-1)(q-1)$: $m^{ed} = m^{1 + k(p-1)(q-1)} = m \cdot (m^{p-1})^{k(q-1)} \equiv m \cdot 1 = m \pmod{p}$.

*Case 2: $p | m$.* Then $m \equiv 0 \pmod{p}$ and $m^{ed} \equiv 0 \equiv m \pmod{p}$.

Similarly for $q$. By CRT, since $m^{ed} \equiv m$ mod both $p$ and $q$, and $\gcd(p, q) = 1$, we have $m^{ed} \equiv m \pmod{pq = N}$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (DDH implies IND-CPA for ElGamal).** *If DDH holds in $G$, then ElGamal encryption in $G$ is IND-CPA secure.*

*Proof.* Consider the IND-CPA game: the adversary receives $pk = g^x$ and challenge ciphertext $(c_1, c_2) = (g^r, m_b \cdot g^{xr})$.

Define hybrid $H_0$: the real game with $b = 0$. Define hybrid $H_1$: replace $g^{xr}$ with $g^s$ for random $s$. By DDH, $(g, g^x, g^r, g^{xr})$ and $(g, g^x, g^r, g^s)$ are indistinguishable, so $H_0 \approx H_1$.

In $H_1$, the ciphertext is $(g^r, m_b \cdot g^s)$ where $g^s$ is a uniform group element independent of everything else. So $m_b \cdot g^s$ is uniformly distributed in $G$, regardless of $b$. The adversary's advantage in $H_1$ is exactly 0.

Since $H_0$ and $H_1$ are indistinguishable, the adversary's advantage in $H_0$ is at most negligibly more than 0. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- [47.06.01] — One-way functions are the generic primitive; RSA and discrete logarithm are concrete instantiations that additionally provide the trapdoor property needed for public-key cryptography.

- [21.01.04] — Fermat's little theorem, Euler's theorem, and the Chinese Remainder Theorem are the number-theoretic foundations of RSA correctness.

- [21.01.01] — The Euclidean algorithm and Bézout's identity are used to compute the private key $d = e^{-1} \bmod \phi(N)$.

- [47.06.03] — Zero-knowledge proofs use the discrete logarithm setting for Schnorr protocols and other interactive proof systems.

- [47.06.04] — Digital signatures use the same RSA key generation in reverse: sign with the private exponent $d$, verify with the public exponent $e$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Public-key cryptography was invented independently by Diffie and Hellman (1976) and by Merkle (1975, published 1978). RSA was proposed by Rivest, Shamir and Adleman in 1977. The Diffie-Hellman key exchange was the first practical method for establishing a shared secret over an insecure channel.

Shor's 1994 discovery of a polynomial-time quantum algorithm for factoring and discrete logarithm transformed the field. It demonstrated that the security of RSA and Diffie-Hellman is conditional not just on classical hardness but on the assumption that large-scale quantum computers cannot be built. This sparked the development of post-quantum cryptography, culminating in the NIST standardisation process (2017-2024).

The philosophical significance is that security in cryptography is always relative to a computational model and a set of hardness assumptions. What is secure today may not be secure tomorrow if those assumptions are broken—by classical algorithms, quantum algorithms, or new mathematics [ref: TODO_REF Arora-Barak Ch. 10].
