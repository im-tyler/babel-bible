## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine a network of pipes connecting a water source to a destination. Each pipe has a maximum capacity — it can carry only so much water per second. You want to push as much water as possible from source to destination without exceeding any pipe's limit.

This is the maximum flow problem. The answer is governed by a beautiful duality: the maximum amount you can push through the network equals the minimum capacity of any "bottleneck" cut. A cut is a partition of the network into two sides, with the source on one side and the destination on the other, and the cut's capacity is the total capacity of pipes crossing from the source side to the destination side.

The Ford-Fulkerson method finds the maximum flow by repeatedly searching for a path from source to destination that can carry more flow (an augmenting path), then pushing as much additional flow as that path allows. Each such push increases the total flow. When no augmenting path exists, the flow is maximum, and the method also identifies a minimum cut.

The max-flow min-cut theorem is one of the most widely applied results in combinatorial optimisation. It connects a maximisation problem (find the most flow) with a minimisation problem (find the smallest cut) and proves they always give the same value.

## Visual [Beginner]

A 4-node flow network:

```
         10
    s ──────────→ a
    │             │
    │5            │8
    ↓             ↓
    b ──────────→ t
         7

Current flow: s→a: 8, a→t: 8, s→b: 3, b→t: 3
Residual: s→a: 2, a→s: 8, s→b: 2, b→s: 3, a→t: 0, t→a: 8, b→t: 4, t→b: 3
```

| Cut | Source side | Sink side | Cut capacity |
|---|---|---|---|
| $\{s\}, \{a, b, t\}$ | $\{s\}$ | $\{a, b, t\}$ | $10 + 5 = 15$ |
| $\{s, a\}, \{b, t\}$ | $\{s, a\}$ | $\{b, t\}$ | $8 + 5 = 13$ |
| $\{s, b\}, \{a, t\}$ | $\{s, b\}$ | $\{a, t\}$ | $10 + 7 = 17$ |
| $\{s, a, b\}, \{t\}$ | $\{s, a, b\}$ | $\{t\}$ | $8 + 7 = 15$ |

The minimum cut has capacity 13, which equals the maximum flow.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider the 4-node network above with source $s$, sink $t$, and intermediate nodes $a$, $b$. Capacities: $c(s,a) = 10$, $c(s,b) = 5$, $c(a,t) = 8$, $c(b,t) = 7$.

**Step 1.** Find an augmenting path: $s \to a \to t$. Residual capacity along this path is $\min(10, 8) = 8$. Push 8 units. Now $f(s,a) = 8$, $f(a,t) = 8$.

**Step 2.** Find another augmenting path: $s \to b \to t$. Residual capacity is $\min(5, 7) = 5$. Push 5 units. Now $f(s,b) = 5$, $f(b,t) = 5$.

**Step 3.** Search for another augmenting path. From $s$, edge $s \to a$ has residual 2 (capacity 10 minus flow 8), edge $s \to b$ has residual 0. From $a$, edge $a \to t$ has residual 0. No augmenting path exists.

Wait — let us check if there is a path through $a \to b$ or $b \to a$ (if such edges exist). In this network there are no direct edges between $a$ and $b$. So no augmenting path exists.

**Step 4.** Total flow = $8 + 5 = 13$. The minimum cut is $\{s, a\}$ vs. $\{b, t\}$ with capacity $c(a,t) + c(s,b) = 8 + 5 = 13$. The max-flow min-cut theorem is satisfied.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Definition (flow network).** A *flow network* is a tuple $G = (V, E, s, t, c)$ where $(V, E)$ is a directed graph, $s \in V$ is the *source*, $t \in V$ is the *sink* ($s \ne t$), and $c : E \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$ is the *capacity function*. We assume $(v, u) \notin E$ whenever $(u, v) \in E$ (no anti-parallel edges; these can be handled by introducing a dummy vertex).

**Definition (flow).** A *flow* in $G$ is a function $f : V \times V \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfying:

1. **Capacity constraint:** For all $u, v \in V$: $f(u, v) \le c(u, v)$.
2. **Skew symmetry:** For all $u, v \in V$: $f(u, v) = -f(v, u)$.
3. **Flow conservation:** For all $u \in V \setminus \{s, t\}$: $\sum_{v \in V} f(u, v) = 0$.

The *value* of the flow is $|f| = \sum_{v \in V} f(s, v)$.

**Definition (residual network).** Given a flow $f$ in $G$, the *residual capacity* of $(u, v)$ is $c_f(u, v) = c(u, v) - f(u, v)$. The *residual network* $G_f = (V, E_f, s, t, c_f)$ where $E_f = \{(u,v) : c_f(u,v) > 0\}$.

**Definition (cut).** An *s-t cut* is a partition $(S, T)$ of $V$ with $s \in S$ and $t \in T$. The *capacity* of the cut is $c(S, T) = \sum_{u \in S, v \in T} c(u, v)$. The *flow across the cut* is $f(S, T) = \sum_{u \in S, v \in T} f(u, v)$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"The maximum flow equals the capacity of every cut."* No. It equals the capacity of the *minimum* cut. Other cuts may have larger capacity.

- *"Residual capacity is always the capacity minus the flow on the forward edge."* It is. But the residual network also contains reverse edges with capacity equal to the current flow, allowing flow to be redirected.

- *"Ford-Fulkerson always terminates in polynomial time."* Not with arbitrary augmenting path choices. Using DFS, the method can take exponentially many iterations on networks with irrational capacities. The Edmonds-Karp algorithm (BFS for shortest augmenting paths) guarantees $O(VE^2)$ time.

### Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (max-flow min-cut).** *In any flow network, the value of the maximum flow equals the capacity of the minimum s-t cut.*

*Proof.* We show three equivalent statements: (1) $f$ is a maximum flow, (2) there is no augmenting path in $G_f$, (3) $|f| = c(S, T)$ for some cut $(S, T)$.

(1 $\Rightarrow$ 2): If an augmenting path existed, we could increase $|f|$, contradicting maximality.

(2 $\Rightarrow$ 3): Let $S$ be the set of vertices reachable from $s$ in $G_f$, and $T = V \setminus S$. Since no augmenting path exists, $t \in T$. For any $u \in S, v \in T$: $(u,v) \notin E_f$, so $c_f(u,v) = 0$, meaning $f(u,v) = c(u,v)$. Also for $v \in T, u \in S$: $f(v,u) = -f(u,v)$, and since $(v,u) \notin E_f$, $c_f(v,u) = 0$ means $f(v,u) \le 0$, so $f(u,v) \ge 0$, but more importantly $f(v,u) = 0$. Therefore $|f| = f(S, T) = \sum_{u \in S, v \in T} f(u,v) = \sum_{u \in S, v \in T} c(u,v) = c(S, T)$.

(3 $\Rightarrow$ 1): For any flow $f'$ and any cut $(S, T)$: $|f'| \le c(S, T)$ (each edge contributes at most its capacity). So $|f'| \le c(S, T) = |f|$, proving $f$ is maximum. $\square$

**Bridge.** The max-flow min-cut theorem is the foundational reason duality pervades combinatorial optimisation: the equality $|f^*| = c(S^*)$ connects a maximisation and a minimisation problem, and every application of network flow reduces to finding this correspondence. This builds toward bipartite matching in [47.04.04], where the max-flow formulation of matching yields Koenig's theorem and Hall's marriage theorem as corollaries of the min-cut, and it appears again in [47.02.03] where NP-completeness reductions often encode Boolean constraints as flow networks. The central insight is that the residual network is dual to the cut structure, and putting these together gives a polynomial-time algorithm (Edmonds-Karp) that simultaneously computes the maximum flow and the minimum cut.

Run the Edmonds-Karp algorithm (BFS for shortest augmenting path) on the network: $V = \{s, a, b, t\}$, $E = \{(s,a), (s,b), (a,b), (a,t), (b,t)\}$, capacities $c(s,a) = 10, c(s,b) = 8, c(a,b) = 3, c(a,t) = 5, c(b,t) = 10$. Report the maximum flow and the minimum cut.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short answer).**

Prove that for any flow $f$ and any s-t cut $(S, T)$, the flow value satisfies $|f| = f(S, T) - f(T, S)$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By flow conservation, for any $u \in S \setminus \{s\}$: $\sum_{v} f(u,v) = 0$, so $\sum_{v \in S} f(u,v) + \sum_{v \in T} f(u,v) = 0$. Summing over all $u \in S$: $\sum_{u \in S} \sum_{v \in S} f(u,v) + \sum_{u \in S, v \in T} f(u,v) = 0$. The first sum vanishes by skew symmetry. So $f(S,S) + f(S,T) = 0$ gives $f(S,T) = -f(S,S)$. But also $|f| = \sum_v f(s,v) = f(S,T) - f(T,S)$, by expanding and using flow conservation on internal vertices.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short answer).**

Explain why the Edmonds-Karp algorithm runs in $O(VE^2)$ time.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Each BFS takes $O(E)$ time. The key insight is that the shortest-path distance from $s$ to any vertex $v$ in the residual network is non-decreasing over iterations. An edge $(u,v)$ can become a "critical edge" (whose residual capacity equals the augmenting path bottleneck) at most $O(V)$ times. Since there are $O(E)$ edges, the total number of augmentations is $O(VE)$. Total time: $O(VE) \times O(E) = O(VE^2)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that maximum bipartite matching reduces to max-flow by constructing a flow network from a bipartite graph $G = (L \cup R, E)$ and proving that the maximum matching size equals the maximum flow value.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Construct network $G' = (V', E', s, t, c)$ where $V' = \{s, t\} \cup L \cup R$. Edges: $(s, u)$ for all $u \in L$ with capacity 1, $(u, v)$ for all $(u,v) \in E$ with capacity 1, and $(v, t)$ for all $v \in R$ with capacity 1. Any matching $M$ in $G$ gives a flow of value $|M|$ by routing 1 unit along each matched edge. Conversely, any integral flow of value $k$ gives a matching of size $k$: the unit flows on edges from $L$ to $R$ form a matching (capacity-1 constraints on source and sink edges ensure each vertex is matched at most once). By integrality of the flow, the maximum matching size equals the maximum flow.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

Prove that the maximum flow value is at most the capacity of any s-t cut.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For any flow $f$ and any s-t cut $(S, T)$: $|f| = f(S,T) - f(T,S) \le f(S,T) \le \sum_{u \in S, v \in T} f(u,v) \le \sum_{u \in S, v \in T} c(u,v) = c(S,T)$. The first inequality uses $f(T,S) \ge 0$ (flow from sink side toward source side is non-negative by the capacity constraint on reverse edges). The second uses the capacity constraint $f(u,v) \le c(u,v)$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, short answer).**

Describe a flow network with integer capacities where the Ford-Fulkerson method (using DFS for augmenting paths) takes $\Omega(E \cdot f^*)$ iterations, where $f^*$ is the maximum flow value.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Consider the "Zwick network": source $s$, sink $t$, and two intermediate vertices $a, b$. Edges: $(s, a)$ with capacity $M$, $(s, b)$ with capacity $M$, $(a, b)$ with capacity 1, $(a, t)$ with capacity $M$, $(b, t)$ with capacity $M$. If DFS always chooses the augmenting path $s \to a \to b \to t$ followed by $s \to b \to a \to t$ (using the reverse edge), each iteration increases the flow by only 1. After $2M$ iterations, the flow reaches $2M$. Total: $\Omega(M)$ iterations for a network with $E = 5$ and $f^* = 2M$.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (Edmonds-Karp: $O(VE^2)$ time).** The Edmonds-Karp algorithm, which uses BFS to find shortest augmenting paths in the residual network, terminates in at most $O(VE)$ augmentations, for a total running time of $O(VE^2)$ [ref: TODO_REF CLRS §26.2]. The proof shows that the shortest-path distance from $s$ to any vertex in the residual network is non-decreasing, and each edge can be critical (the bottleneck of an augmenting path) at most $O(V/2)$ times.

**Theorem 2 (integrality theorem).** If all capacities in a flow network are integers, then the Ford-Fulkerson method produces a maximum flow with integer values on all edges. The maximum flow value is itself an integer [ref: TODO_REF CLRS §26.2]. This is because each augmentation adds an integer amount (the minimum of integer residual capacities), so integrality is preserved throughout. The integrality theorem is the reason max-flow reduces to integer optimisation problems like bipartite matching.

**Theorem 3 (push-relabel: $O(V^2\sqrt{E})$ time).** The push-relabel algorithm of Goldberg and Tarjan (1988) maintains a "preflow" (which may violate conservation by allowing excess at vertices) and a "height" function $h: V \to \mathbb{N}$ with $h(s) = |V|$, $h(t) = 0$. Operations: *push* sends flow from a vertex with excess to a neighbour of lower height; *relabel* increases a vertex's height when it cannot push. The algorithm runs in $O(V^2\sqrt{E})$ time using the gap heuristic, improving on Edmonds-Karp for dense graphs [ref: TODO_REF Tarjan 1983].

**Synthesis.** The max-flow min-cut theorem is the foundational reason duality structures appear throughout combinatorial optimisation: the equality between maximum flow and minimum cut transforms maximisation into minimisation and vice versa, with each side providing insight into the other. The central insight is that the residual network is the dual object — it encodes the "room for improvement" in the current flow, and its structure reveals the minimum cut, and putting these together with the integrality theorem gives polynomial-time solutions to a vast family of combinatorial problems. This builds toward bipartite matching in [47.04.04] and the Hungarian algorithm, and appears again in the LP duality that underlies the ellipsoid method and interior-point methods of [44.02.01].

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (max-flow min-cut theorem).** *The value of the maximum flow equals the capacity of the minimum s-t cut.*

*Proof.* See the Key theorem section above. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (weak duality).** *For any flow $f$ and any s-t cut $(S,T)$: $|f| \le c(S,T)$.*

*Proof.* $|f| = \sum_{v} f(s,v) = f(S,T) - f(T,S)$ by flow conservation on internal vertices of $S$. Since $f(T,S) \ge 0$ and $f(u,v) \le c(u,v)$ for all $(u,v)$: $|f| \le f(S,T) \le c(S,T)$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (integrality).** *If all capacities are integers, Ford-Fulkerson produces an integer maximum flow.*

*Proof.* Initially $f = 0$ (integer). Each augmentation adds $c_f(p) = \min_{(u,v) \in p} c_f(u,v)$ units of flow. The residual capacities $c_f(u,v) = c(u,v) - f(u,v)$ are differences of integers, hence integers. So $c_f(p)$ is a positive integer. The augmented flow remains integer. By induction, the final flow is integer. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The max-flow min-cut theorem was first proved by Lester Ford and Delbert Fulkerson in 1956 in their paper "Maximal Flow Through a Network" [ref: TODO_REF CLRS §26.2]. Their proof introduced the augmenting path method and established the fundamental duality between flow and cut. Peter Elias, Amiel Feinstein, and Claude Shannon independently proved an equivalent result in the context of information theory in 1956, showing that the maximum rate of reliable communication through a network of noisy channels is determined by a minimum cut.

The Edmonds-Karp algorithm was published by Jack Edmonds and Richard Karp in 1972, showing that BFS-based augmenting path selection gives polynomial-time performance. Andrew Goldberg and Robert Tarjan introduced the push-relabel method in 1988, achieving the best known running time for general flow networks and opening the door to efficient parallel implementations.
