## Intuition [Beginner]

Some problems have a convenient property: once you find a solution, checking it is easy. A jigsaw puzzle is hard to solve, but verifying that a completed puzzle is correct takes a glance. A Sudoku is hard to fill in, but checking a filled grid is quick.

NP-complete problems are the hardest problems in NP — the hardest problems where solutions can be checked quickly. If you could solve any one of them quickly, you could solve all of them quickly, because they are all secretly the same problem in disguise.

The disguises are called reductions. A reduction from problem A to problem B is a fast translation: it converts any instance of A into an instance of B such that the answer is preserved. If you can solve B, the reduction lets you solve A by translation. When A reduces to B and B is in NP, then B is at least as hard as A.

SAT asks: given a Boolean formula, is there a way to set its variables to true and false that makes the whole formula true? Cook and Levin proved that SAT is NP-complete: every problem in NP reduces to SAT. This was the first known NP-complete problem.

From SAT, a chain of reductions connects dozens of seemingly unrelated problems. 3SAT (a restricted form of SAT) reduces to CLIQUE (does a graph have a group of mutually connected vertices of a given size?), which reduces to VERTEX-COVER (can you delete a small set of vertices to eliminate all edges?), and so on. Each reduction opens a new window into the same underlying hardness.

The reduction chain connecting NP-complete problems:

```
     ↓   ↓

     |
     ↓

```

|---|---|---|
| SAT | Boolean formula | Is it satisfiable? |
| 3SAT | Formula with 3-literal clauses | Is it satisfiable? |
| CLIQUE | Graph $G$, integer $k$ | Does $G$ have a $k$-clique? |
| VERTEX-COVER | Graph $G$, integer $k$ | Does $G$ have a $k$-vertex cover? |
| HAM-CYCLE | Graph $G$ | Does $G$ have a Hamiltonian cycle? |

## Worked example [Beginner]

We reduce 3SAT to CLIQUE.

Given a 3SAT formula $\phi = (l_{1,1} \lor l_{1,2} \lor l_{1,3}) \wedge \cdots \wedge (l_{k,1} \lor l_{k,2} \lor l_{k,3})$ with $k$ clauses, build a graph $G$ as follows. Create three vertices per clause, labelled $v_{i,1}, v_{i,2}, v_{i,3}$ for clause $i$. Connect two vertices $v_{i,a}$ and $v_{j,b}$ (with $i \ne j$) if their corresponding literals are compatible: the literal $l_{i,a}$ is not the negation of $l_{j,b}$.

Claim: $\phi$ is satisfiable iff $G$ has a clique of size $k$.

If $\phi$ is satisfiable, each clause has at least one true literal. Pick one true literal per clause. The corresponding vertices form a clique: any two of them are from different clauses, and their literals are both true under the satisfying assignment, so they cannot be negations of each other.

If $G$ has a clique of size $k$, then the clique contains one vertex per clause (since there are no edges within a clause group). Set each literal in the clique to true. Since clique vertices are compatible, no literal contradicts another. Assign remaining variables arbitrarily. This satisfies $\phi$.

Example: $\phi = (x \lor y \lor \neg z) \wedge (\neg x \lor z \lor y)$. The graph has 6 vertices. Vertex $(1,x)$ connects to $(2,z)$ and $(2,y)$ but not to $(2,\neg x)$ (conflict). Vertex $(1,y)$ connects to $(2,\neg x)$, $(2,z)$, and $(2,y)$. Setting $x = \text{true}, y = \text{true}, z = \text{true}$ satisfies $\phi$. The corresponding clique picks $(1,x)$ and $(2,z)$ (or other valid pairs).

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Definition (NP-completeness).** A language $L$ is *NP-complete* if:

1. $L \in \mathsf{NP}$.
2. For every $L' \in \mathsf{NP}$, $L' \le_p L$ (every NP problem polynomial-time reduces to $L$).

**Theorem (Cook-Levin).** *SAT is NP-complete.*

The proof constructs, for any NP machine $M$ and input $w$, a Boolean formula $\phi_{M,w}$ of size polynomial in $|w|$ such that $\phi_{M,w}$ is satisfiable iff $M$ accepts $w$. The formula encodes the entire computation tableau: rows are time steps, columns are tape cells, and variables $x_{i,j,s}$ indicate that cell $j$ at time $i$ contains symbol $s$. Clauses enforce: (a) a valid start configuration, (b) each cell has exactly one symbol, (c) each window of three consecutive cells transitions correctly according to $M$'s transition function, and (d) some cell contains an accept state [ref: TODO_REF Sipser §7.4].

**Reduction 1: SAT to 3SAT.** Given a SAT instance $\phi$, convert each clause $C = (l_1 \lor l_2 \lor \cdots \lor l_k)$ with $k > 3$ into a chain of 3-literal clauses using fresh variables $y_1, \ldots, y_{k-3}$:

$(l_1 \lor l_2 \lor y_1) \wedge (\neg y_1 \lor l_3 \lor y_2) \wedge \cdots \wedge (\neg y_{k-3} \lor l_{k-1} \lor l_k)$.

This gadget is satisfiable iff the original clause is. The reduction produces a 3CNF formula of size $O(|\phi|)$.

**Reduction 2: 3SAT to CLIQUE.** Given a 3CNF formula with $k$ clauses, construct the graph described in the worked example. The graph has $3k$ vertices and at most $9k^2$ edges. $G$ has a $k$-clique iff $\phi$ is satisfiable.

**Reduction 3: CLIQUE to VERTEX-COVER.** $G$ has a clique of size $k$ iff $\overline{G}$ (the complement graph) has a vertex cover of size $|V| - k$. The complement is computable in polynomial time.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"NP means 'not polynomial.'"* NP stands for Nondeterministic Polynomial time. It is the class of problems solvable in polynomial time on a nondeterministic machine. Many NP problems have no known polynomial-time algorithm, but NP does not mean "hard."

- *"NP-complete means 'the hardest problem there is.'"* NP-complete means the hardest problem *in NP*. There are much harder problems outside NP (e.g., the halting problem, which is undecidable).

- *"A reduction from A to B means A and B are equally hard."* A reduction $A \le_p B$ means B is *at least as hard as* A. B could be harder. NP-completeness of B means B is at least as hard as every NP problem.

### Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (3SAT to HAM-CYCLE reduction).** *3SAT $\le_p$ HAM-CYCLE.*

*Proof sketch.* Given a 3CNF formula $\phi$ with $n$ variables and $k$ clauses, construct a graph $G$ with a Hamiltonian cycle iff $\phi$ is satisfiable. The construction uses two types of gadgets.

**Variable gadget.** For each variable $x_i$, create a diamond-shaped subgraph of 12 vertices arranged so that a Hamiltonian cycle traversing this subgraph must go either left-to-right (encoding $x_i = \text{true}$) or right-to-left (encoding $x_i = \text{false}$).

**Clause gadget.** For each clause $C_j$, create a single node. This node can be visited by the Hamiltonian cycle only by "detouring" through the variable gadget corresponding to a literal in $C_j$ that is set to true. If $x_i$ appears positively in $C_j$, the detour uses the rightward path through the $x_i$ gadget; if $\neg x_i$ appears, it uses the leftward path.

The graph has $O(n + k)$ vertices and edges, and the construction is polynomial. A Hamiltonian cycle in $G$ corresponds to a consistent truth assignment that satisfies every clause, and vice versa. $\square$

**Bridge.** The chain of reductions from SAT through 3SAT, CLIQUE, VERTEX-COVER, and HAM-CYCLE builds toward the entire theory of NP-completeness as a unified phenomenon: all NP-complete problems are polynomial-time equivalent, and this is exactly the foundational reason that resolving P vs NP for any one of them resolves it for all. The reductions appear again in [47.02.04] where the polynomial hierarchy layers additional quantifier alternations on top of NP, and in [47.04.04] where bipartite matching provides a contrasting example of a graph problem that sits firmly in P. The central insight is that the reduction is a constructive encoding of one problem's structure into another's, and this is exactly what makes NP-completeness a robust and transferable notion. Putting these together with the Cook-Levin theorem shows that SAT is the universal NP problem: every NP computation can be encoded as a Boolean formula, and from there, translated into the language of graphs, numbers, or any other NP-complete domain.

Show that CLIQUE is in NP by describing a polynomial-time verifier.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A certificate for CLIQUE is a set $S$ of $k$ vertices. The verifier checks: (1) $|S| = k$, (2) for every pair $u, v \in S$ with $u \ne v$, the edge $\{u,v\}$ is in $G$. This takes $O(k^2)$ time, which is polynomial in the input size.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Reduce CLIQUE to INDEPENDent-Set: given a graph $G$ and integer $k$, does $G$ have an independent set (a set of vertices with no edges between them) of size $k$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$S$ is a clique in $G$ iff $S$ is an independent set in $\overline{G}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$G$ has a clique of size $k$ iff $\overline{G}$ has an independent set of size $k$. The reduction computes $\overline{G}$ (flip all edges) and outputs $(\overline{G}, k)$. A set $S$ of vertices is mutually adjacent in $G$ iff they are mutually non-adjacent in $\overline{G}$. The reduction runs in $O(|V|^2)$ time.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that VERTEX-COVER is NP-complete by showing CLIQUE $\le_p$ VERTEX-COVER.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$S$ is a clique of size $k$ in $G$ iff $V \setminus S$ is a vertex cover of size $|V| - k$ in $\overline{G}$. Proof: $S$ is a clique in $G$ means every pair in $S$ has an edge in $G$, which means no pair in $S$ has an edge in $\overline{G}$. So every edge of $\overline{G}$ must touch $V \setminus S$, making $V \setminus S$ a vertex cover of $\overline{G}$. The reduction outputs $(\overline{G}, |V| - k)$. VERTEX-COVER is in NP (verify by checking all edges touch the proposed cover), so it is NP-complete.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Convert the clause $(x_1 \lor x_2 \lor x_3 \lor x_4 \lor x_5)$ into an equivalent set of 3-literal clauses using the standard reduction from SAT to 3SAT.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Introduce fresh variables $y_1, y_2$:

$(x_1 \lor x_2 \lor y_1) \wedge (\neg y_1 \lor x_3 \lor y_2) \wedge (\neg y_2 \lor x_4 \lor x_5)$.

If the original clause is satisfied by some literal, set the $y$ variables to route the satisfaction: e.g., if $x_3$ is true, set $y_1 = \text{true}$ and $y_2 = \text{false}$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that SUBSET-SUM is NP-complete by reducing 3SAT to it. SUBSET-SUM asks: given a set $S$ of integers and a target $t$, is there a subset $S' \subseteq S$ with $\sum_{s \in S'} s = t$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use a base-10 (or higher) encoding where each digit position corresponds to a variable or a clause. Create two numbers per variable (one for true, one for false) and two slack numbers per clause.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Given a 3CNF formula with $n$ variables and $k$ clauses, represent numbers in base $B = k + 1$ (to prevent carries between digit positions). Each number has $n + k$ digits: the first $n$ digits correspond to variables, the last $k$ to clauses.

For each variable $x_i$: create number $v_i$ with digit 1 in position $i$ (variable position) and digit 1 in each clause position $j$ where $x_i$ appears positively; and number $v_i'$ with digit 1 in position $i$ and digit 1 in each clause position $j$ where $\neg x_i$ appears.

For each clause $C_j$: create two slack numbers $s_j, s_j'$ with digit 0 everywhere except position $n+j$, where they have digits 1 and 2 respectively.

Target $t$ has digit 1 in each variable position and digit 3 in each clause position.

The subset picks exactly one of $\{v_i, v_i'\}$ per variable (contributing 1 to position $i$) and some slacks to bring each clause position up to 3. The clause position gets 1 from each satisfied literal plus slacks to reach 3. A satisfying assignment maps to a subset summing to $t$, and vice versa.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

Explain why the reduction from 3SAT to CLIQUE would fail if we allowed edges within the same clause group.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Edges within a clause group would allow the clique to contain multiple vertices from the same clause. These vertices could correspond to contradictory literals (e.g., $x$ and $\neg x$ within the same clause). The clique would no longer correspond to a consistent truth assignment, because a single clause cannot simultaneously satisfy two mutually contradictory literals. The requirement that edges only go between different clause groups ensures the clique picks exactly one literal per clause, and compatibility edges ensure no two chosen literals contradict each other.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the language $\text{EXACT-COVER} = \{\langle S_1, \ldots, S_m \rangle : \text{there exist disjoint } S_{i_1}, \ldots, S_{i_k} \text{ whose union is } S_1 \cup \cdots \cup S_m\}$ is NP-complete by reducing 3SAT to it.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Given a 3CNF formula with variables $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ and clauses $C_1, \ldots, C_k$, construct sets over the universe $U = \{x_1, \ldots, x_n, x_1', \ldots, x_n', C_1, \ldots, C_k\}$. For each variable $x_i$: include set $T_i = \{x_i\} \cup \{C_j : x_i \text{ appears in } C_j\}$ and set $F_i = \{x_i'\} \cup \{C_j : \neg x_i \text{ appears in } C_j\}$. Each element $x_i$ and $x_i'$ appears in exactly two sets ($T_i$ and $F_i$), forcing an exact cover to choose exactly one of them (encoding the truth assignment). Each clause element $C_j$ appears in exactly the $T_i$ and $F_i$ sets for literals in $C_j$, so it is covered iff at least one of its literals is set to true. The exact cover exists iff the formula is satisfiable. EXACT-COVER is in NP (verify by checking disjointness and coverage), so it is NP-complete.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

Three directions extend the NP-completeness landscape.

**Theorem 1 (thousands of NP-complete problems).** Garey and Johnson's 1979 catalogue *Computers and Intractability* lists over 300 NP-complete problems across graph theory, number theory, algebra, combinatorics, and logic [ref: TODO_REF Sipser §7.5]. The catalogue demonstrates that NP-completeness is not a rare property but a pervasive feature of combinatorial problems. Key additions include: 3-COLORING (chromatic number at most 3), KNAPSACK (the optimisation version of SUBSET-SUM), and LONGEST-PATH (does the graph have a simple path of length $\ge k$?).

**Theorem 2 (strong NP-completeness).** A problem is *strongly NP-complete* if it remains NP-complete even when all numerical inputs are bounded by a polynomial in the input length. SUBSET-SUM is NP-complete but not strongly NP-complete (it has a pseudopolynomial $O(nt)$ dynamic programming algorithm). 3-PARTITION (can $3n$ numbers be partitioned into $n$ triples of equal sum?) is strongly NP-complete. The distinction matters for approximation: problems that are not strongly NP-complete often have efficient approximation schemes [ref: TODO_REF CLRS §34.5].

**Theorem 3 (NP-completeness of approximate counting).** Approximating the number of satisfying assignments of a SAT formula to within a factor of $2^{n^{1-\varepsilon}}$ for any $\varepsilon > 0$ is NP-hard. This connects NP-completeness to the counting class $\#\mathsf{P}$ studied in [47.02.07], where the exact counting version of SAT ($\#$SAT) is $\#\mathsf{P}$-complete by Toda's theorem.

**Synthesis.** The collection of NP-complete problems is the foundational reason that P-vs-NP is the central question of computational complexity: the central insight is that thousands of seemingly different problems across mathematics and computer science are connected by a web of polynomial-time reductions, and this is exactly what makes a positive resolution of P-vs-NP so impactful. This builds toward the polynomial hierarchy in [47.02.04] where the NP level is extended by alternating quantifiers, and it appears again in [47.03.05] where the PCP theorem characterises NP by probabilistic proof checking. The generalises from individual reductions to a structural theory of computational hardness, and putting these together with the approximation results shows that NP-completeness has consequences even when we relax the requirement of exact solutions.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (SAT to 3SAT reduction correctness).** *The standard clause-splitting reduction converts a SAT instance $\phi$ to a 3SAT instance $\phi'$ such that $\phi$ is satisfiable iff $\phi'$ is satisfiable.*

*Proof.* For each clause $C = (l_1 \lor \cdots \lor l_k)$ with $k > 3$, replace with the gadget $(l_1 \lor l_2 \lor y_1) \wedge (\neg y_1 \lor l_3 \lor y_2) \wedge \cdots \wedge (\neg y_{k-3} \lor l_{k-1} \lor l_k)$ using fresh variables $y_1, \ldots, y_{k-3}$.

Forward: if $C$ is satisfied by some $l_m$, set $y_1 = \cdots = y_{m-2} = \text{true}$ and $y_{m-1} = \cdots = y_{k-3} = \text{false}$. Clause $j$ for $j \le m-2$ is satisfied by $y_j$; clause $m-1$ (if it exists) is satisfied by $l_m$; clauses $j > m-1$ are satisfied by $\neg y_{j-1}$.

Backward: if the gadget is satisfied, at least one $l_m$ must be true. If all $l_m$ were false, then $y_1 = \text{true}$ (from the first clause), $y_2 = \text{true}$ (from the second), ..., $y_{k-3} = \text{true}$. But the last clause would be $(\neg y_{k-3} \lor \text{false} \lor \text{false}) = \text{false}$, a contradiction. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (CLIQUE to VERTEX-COVER reduction).** *$G$ has a clique of size $k$ iff $\overline{G}$ has a vertex cover of size $|V| - k$.*

*Proof.* $S \subseteq V$ is a clique in $G$ iff every pair in $S$ has an edge in $G$ iff no pair in $S$ has a non-edge in $G$ iff every non-edge of $G$ has at least one endpoint in $V \setminus S$ iff $V \setminus S$ is a vertex cover of $\overline{G}$ (since non-edges of $G$ are edges of $\overline{G}$). The sizes match: $|V \setminus S| = |V| - k$. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Stephen Cook proved the NP-completeness of SAT in his 1971 paper "The Complexity of Theorem-Proving Procedures," presented at the ACM Symposium on Theory of Computing. He showed that any problem in NP can be encoded as a Boolean satisfiability problem, establishing SAT as the canonical NP-complete problem. Leonid Levin independently proved the same result in 1973 in the Soviet Union [ref: TODO_REF Sipser §7.4].

Richard Karp's 1972 paper "Reducibility Among Combinatorial Problems" transformed Cook's result from an isolated theorem into a practical tool. Karp showed that 21 fundamental problems — including CLIQUE, VERTEX-COVER, HAM-CYCLE, SUBSET-SUM, and 3-COLORING — are all NP-complete. His paper established the reduction methodology as the standard technique for proving NP-completeness and created the first map of the NP-complete landscape.

Garey and Johnson's 1979 book *Computers and Intractability: A Guide to the Theory of NP-Completeness* systematised the field, providing a catalogue of over 300 NP-complete problems and a standard format for NP-completeness proofs. Their appendix listing known NP-complete problems by problem domain remains a standard reference.

The philosophical significance of NP-completeness lies in the observation that many problems arising naturally in engineering, operations research, and mathematics are equivalent in difficulty to SAT. This provides strong evidence that P $\ne$ NP, because resolving any one of these problems would resolve all of them simultaneously.
