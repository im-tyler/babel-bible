## Intuition [Beginner]

Some problems are easy to solve. Sorting a list of numbers, finding the shortest path between two cities on a map, checking whether a number is prime — all of these can be done by algorithms that finish quickly, even for large inputs. "Quickly" here means the running time grows at most as a polynomial function of the input size: quadratic, cubic, and so on.

Other problems seem hard to solve but easy to check. Given a Sudoku puzzle, finding the solution might take a lot of searching, but if someone hands you a filled-in grid, you can verify it is correct just by scanning the rows, columns, and boxes. This is the essence of the class NP: problems where a proposed solution can be verified in polynomial time.

The question "does P equal NP?" asks whether every problem whose solution can be quickly checked can also be quickly solved. It is one of the seven Clay Millennium Prize Problems, with a one-million-dollar reward for its resolution. Most researchers believe P does not equal NP, but a proof remains elusive.

A polynomial-time reduction is a way of saying "if I could solve problem B quickly, I could also solve problem A quickly." It works by transforming any instance of A into an instance of B using a polynomial-time algorithm. If B is easy, then A becomes easy via the reduction. This is the tool that lets us compare the difficulty of problems without solving them.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider the Clique problem: given a graph $G$ and an integer $k$, does $G$ contain a clique (a completely connected subgraph) of size $k$?

This problem is in NP. Here is why: if someone claims "yes, there is a clique of size $k$" and gives you the set of $k$ vertices, you can check by verifying that every pair among those $k$ vertices is connected by an edge. There are $k(k-1)/2$ pairs to check, which is polynomial in the input size. So a proposed solution can be verified quickly.

Is Clique in P? No polynomial-time algorithm is known for general graphs. The best known algorithms take time exponential in $k$ (and for large $k$, exponential in the graph size). But nobody has proved that no polynomial-time algorithm exists.

A polynomial-time reduction from 3SAT to Clique would show: if you could solve Clique quickly, you could also solve 3SAT quickly. Since 3SAT is NP-complete (proved in [47.02.02]), this would make Clique NP-complete too.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A Turing machine (as defined in [42.04.01]) $M$ *decides* a language $L$ if for every input $w$, $M$ halts and accepts when $w \in L$ and halts and rejects when $w \notin L$. The *time complexity* of $M$ is the function $T_M : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$ where $T_M(n)$ is the maximum number of steps $M$ takes on any input of length $n$.

**Definition (TIME and P).** For a function $T : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$:

$$\mathsf{TIME}(T(n)) = \{ L : \text{there exists a TM } M \text{ deciding } L \text{ with } T_M(n) = O(T(n)) \}.$$

The class **P** (polynomial time) is:

$$\mathsf{P} = \bigcup_{k=1}^{\infty} \mathsf{TIME}(n^k).$$

**Definition (NP, verifier definition).** A language $L$ is in **NP** if there exists a polynomial $p$ and a polynomial-time Turing machine $V$ (the *verifier*) such that for all $x$:

$$x \in L \iff \exists c \in \{0,1\}^{p(|x|)} \text{ with } V(x, c) = 1.$$

The string $c$ is called a *certificate* (or *witness*). The verifier $V$ runs in time polynomial in $|x|$.

**Definition (NP, NDTM definition).** A language $L$ is in **NP** if there exists a polynomial-time nondeterministic Turing machine $N$ that accepts $L$: $x \in L$ iff there exists an accepting computation path of $N$ on $x$ of length at most $p(|x|)$ for some polynomial $p$.

The two definitions are equivalent: the certificate in the verifier definition encodes the sequence of nondeterministic choices in the NDTM definition.

**Definition (polynomial-time reduction).** A language $A$ is *polynomial-time reducible* to a language $B$, written $A \le_p B$, if there exists a polynomial-time computable function $f : \Sigma^* \to \Sigma^*$ such that for all $x$:

$$x \in A \iff f(x) \in B.$$

The relation $\le_p$ is reflexive and transitive. If $A \le_p B$ and $B \in \mathsf{P}$, then $A \in \mathsf{P}$ (compose the reduction with the algorithm for $B$).

**Definition (NP-completeness).** A language $L$ is *NP-complete* if $L \in \mathsf{NP}$ and for every $L' \in \mathsf{NP}$, $L' \le_p L$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"NP means 'not polynomial.'"* NP stands for "nondeterministic polynomial time." It is a class of problems solvable in polynomial time on a nondeterministic machine, not a class of problems that are not polynomial.

- *"A problem in NP cannot be solved in polynomial time."* P is a subset of NP, so every P problem is in NP. NP contains P; the question is whether it contains anything more.

- *"A polynomial-time reduction from A to B means A and B have the same difficulty."* It means A is at most as hard as B (up to polynomial overhead). B could be much harder. Transitivity allows chaining: $A \le_p B \le_p C$ means $A \le_p C$.

### Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (P is closed under complement; NP is not known to be).** *If $L \in \mathsf{P}$, then $\overline{L} \in \mathsf{P}$. The same is not known for NP (i.e., it is unknown whether $\mathsf{NP} = \mathsf{coNP}$).*

*Proof.* Let $M$ be a polynomial-time TM deciding $L$. Construct $M'$ that on input $x$ runs $M$ on $x$ and flips the answer: if $M$ accepts, $M'$ rejects; if $M$ rejects, $M'$ accepts. Then $M'$ decides $\overline{L}$ in the same time as $M$. $\square$

The asymmetry for NP is fundamental: a verifier for $L$ checks existence of a good certificate, but $\overline{L}$ requires checking that *no* certificate exists, which is a universal quantifier. The question $\mathsf{NP} \stackrel{?}{=} \mathsf{coNP}$ is open and is related to, but distinct from, the P vs NP question.

**Bridge.** The P vs NP question is the foundational reason complexity theory has a structural backbone: the polynomial-time reduction $\le_p$ creates a partial order on computational problems, and NP-complete problems sit at the top of this order within NP. This builds toward the Cook-Levin theorem in [47.02.02], where SAT is shown to be NP-complete by encoding arbitrary NP computations as Boolean formulas, and it appears again in [47.02.03] where the reduction chain SAT $\le_p$ 3SAT $\le_p$ Clique $\le_p$ ... classifies dozens of problems as NP-complete at once. The central insight is that polynomial-time reductions transport both algorithms (if B is in P, so is A) and hardness (if A is NP-hard and $A \le_p B$, then B is NP-hard), and putting these together gives the entire edifice of NP-completeness.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="true-false" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, true-false).**

True or false: if $A \le_p B$ and $B \le_p A$, then both $A$ and $B$ are in P.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**False.** The mutual reductions only show that $A$ and $B$ have the same difficulty (up to polynomial overhead). Both could be NP-complete, or both could be in P, or both could be of unknown status. Mutual reduction does not place either in P.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the language $\text{PATH} = \{\langle G, s, t \rangle : G \text{ has a path from } s \text{ to } t\}$ is in P.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Run BFS or DFS from $s$ in $G$. These algorithms run in time $O(|V| + |E|)$, which is polynomial in the input size. Accept iff $t$ is reached. So PATH is in P.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the language $\text{COMPOSITE} = \{n : n \text{ is a composite number}\}$ is in NP by describing a verifier and its certificate.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Certificate: a factor $d$ with $1 < d < n$. Verifier: check that $d$ divides $n$ (compute $n \bmod d = 0$). Division takes polynomial time in the number of bits of $n$. If such $d$ exists, $n$ is composite. Conversely, if $n$ is composite, a factor exists as certificate.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short answer).**

Prove that if $A \le_p B$ and $B \in \mathsf{P}$, then $A \in \mathsf{P}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compose the reduction $f$ with the polynomial-time algorithm for $B$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $f$ be the polynomial-time reduction from $A$ to $B$, computable in time $p_1(n)$. Let $M_B$ decide $B$ in time $p_2(n)$. The composition algorithm for $A$: on input $x$, compute $y = f(x)$ (time $p_1(|x|)$), then run $M_B$ on $y$ (time $p_2(|y|) \le p_2(p_1(|x|))$, since $|y| \le p_1(|x|)$). The total time is $p_1(n) + p_2(p_1(n))$, a polynomial. Accept iff $M_B$ accepts. Rubric: full credit for the composition argument and the bound on $|y|$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short answer).**

Prove that the reduction $\le_p$ is transitive: if $A \le_p B$ and $B \le_p C$, then $A \le_p C$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $f$ reduce $A$ to $B$ in time $p_1(n)$, and $g$ reduce $B$ to $C$ in time $p_2(n)$. Then $h(x) = g(f(x))$ is a reduction from $A$ to $C$: $x \in A \iff f(x) \in B \iff g(f(x)) \in C \iff h(x) \in C$. The function $h$ is computable in time $p_1(n) + p_2(p_1(n))$, a polynomial. So $A \le_p C$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the language $\text{SUBSET-SUM} = \{\langle S, t \rangle : S \text{ is a set of integers with a subset summing to } t\}$ is in NP.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Certificate: the subset $S' \subseteq S$ of integers. Verifier: compute the sum of elements in $S'$ and check it equals $t$. Addition takes polynomial time in the total bit-length of the numbers. If such a subset exists, the certificate is valid. Conversely, if no subset sums to $t$, no certificate can pass verification.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

Prove that if $L$ is NP-complete and $L \in \mathsf{P}$, then $\mathsf{P} = \mathsf{NP}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Every language in NP reduces to $L$ by NP-completeness, and $L$ is in P.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $L$ be NP-complete and $L \in \mathsf{P}$. For any $L' \in \mathsf{NP}$, since $L$ is NP-complete, $L' \le_p L$. Since $L \in \mathsf{P}$, by Exercise 4, $L' \in \mathsf{P}$. So $\mathsf{NP} \subseteq \mathsf{P}$. Combined with $\mathsf{P} \subseteq \mathsf{NP}$, we get $\mathsf{P} = \mathsf{NP}$. Rubric: full credit for the two-step argument (reduction + closure).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short answer).**

State and prove Ladner's theorem: if $\mathsf{P} \ne \mathsf{NP}$, then there exists a language in $\mathsf{NP} \setminus \mathsf{P}$ that is not NP-complete.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Construct a language that is "padded" SAT, where the padding grows at a rate determined by a function that diagonalises against all polynomial-time machines and all polynomial-time reductions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The proof constructs a language $L_H$ by padding SAT with a function $H$: $L_H = \{x01^{H(|x|)} : x \in \text{SAT}\}$ where $H(n)$ is the smallest $i \le \log n$ such that the $i$-th polynomial-time machine $M_i$ correctly decides SAT on all inputs of length $\le \log n$ (or $H(n) = \log n$ if no such $i$ exists). The function $H$ grows slowly enough that $L_H \in \mathsf{NP}$ (the padding is efficiently checkable), but fast enough that $L_H \notin \mathsf{P}$ (diagonalising against each $M_i$) and $L_H$ is not NP-complete (any reduction from SAT to $L_H$ would force $H$ to be polynomially bounded, contradicting the diagonalisation). The full proof is in Arora & Barak §2.3.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

Two structural results illuminate the landscape defined by P, NP, and reductions.

**Theorem 1 (time hierarchy).** For any time-constructible functions $f, g$ with $f(n) \log f(n) = o(g(n))$, $\mathsf{TIME}(f(n)) \subsetneq \mathsf{TIME}(g(n))$ [ref: TODO_REF Arora & Barak §1.6]. The proof is a diagonalisation argument: a universal machine simulates every $f(n)$-time machine on its own encoding and flips the answer, running in $g(n)$ time. This shows that giving a Turing machine more time strictly increases its power — there are problems solvable in $n^3$ time but not in $n^2$ time, problems solvable in $2^n$ time but not in polynomial time, and so on.

**Theorem 2 (Cook-Levin theorem, statement).** The language $\text{SAT} = \{\varphi : \varphi \text{ is a satisfiable Boolean formula}\}$ is NP-complete [ref: TODO_REF Arora & Barak §2.3]. The proof constructs, for any NP machine $N$ and input $x$, a Boolean formula $\varphi_{N,x}$ whose satisfying assignments encode accepting computation paths of $N$ on $x$. The full proof is developed in [47.02.02].

**Theorem 3 (robustness of P).** The class P is the same regardless of whether we use single-tape Turing machines, multi-tape Turing machines, or RAM machines. A single-tape TM can simulate a $k$-tape TM with quadratic overhead: $\mathsf{TIME}_k(T(n)) \subseteq \mathsf{TIME}_1(T(n)^2)$. A RAM machine can be simulated by a multi-tape TM with polynomial overhead [ref: TODO_REF Arora & Barak §1.3]. This robustness is why P is a natural class: it does not depend on the specific computational model.

**Synthesis.** The P vs NP question is the foundational reason complexity theory organises problems by difficulty: polynomial-time reductions create a partial order, NP-complete problems are the maximal elements within NP, and the time hierarchy theorem ensures that this landscape has genuine structure (more time yields more power). The central insight is that the reduction $\le_p$ transports both upper bounds (membership in P) and lower bounds (NP-hardness), and putting these together with the Cook-Levin theorem of [47.02.02] and the classical NP-completeness reductions of [47.02.03] classifies hundreds of natural problems into a small number of equivalence classes. This builds toward the polynomial hierarchy of [47.02.04], where the alternation of existential and universal quantifiers creates an infinite hierarchy of complexity classes, and appears again in the randomised classes of [47.03.01] where probabilistic computation potentially extends P.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (equivalence of NP definitions).** *The verifier definition and the NDTM definition of NP are equivalent.*

*Proof.* (Verifier $\Rightarrow$ NDTM): Let $V$ be a polynomial-time verifier for $L$ with certificate bound $p$. Construct an NDTM $N$ that on input $x$ nondeterministically guesses a certificate $c$ of length $p(|x|)$ and runs $V(x, c)$. If $x \in L$, some certificate causes $V$ to accept, so $N$ has an accepting path. If $x \notin L$, no certificate is accepted. (NDTM $\Rightarrow$ Verifier): Let $N$ be a polynomial-time NDTM for $L$. The certificate encodes the sequence of nondeterministic choices (at each branch point, which transition to take). The verifier $V$ simulates $N$ deterministically using the choices from the certificate and accepts iff the simulation accepts. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (closure under polynomial-time reductions).** *If $A \le_p B$ and $B \in \mathsf{P}$, then $A \in \mathsf{P}$.*

*Proof.* Let $f$ reduce $A$ to $B$ in time $p_1(n)$, and $M_B$ decide $B$ in time $p_2(n)$. On input $x$, compute $f(x)$ in time $p_1(|x|)$, then run $M_B$ on $f(x)$ in time $p_2(|f(x)|) \le p_2(p_1(|x|))$. Total time: $p_1(n) + p_2(p_1(n))$, a polynomial. Accept iff $M_B$ accepts. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (linear speedup).** *If $L \in \mathsf{TIME}(T(n))$ and $c > 0$, then $L \in \mathsf{TIME}(c \cdot T(n) + O(n))$.*

*Proof.* Let $M$ decide $L$ in time $T(n)$. Construct $M'$ with a larger tape alphabet that encodes $k$ symbols of $M$'s tape in one cell of $M'$'s tape, for $k$ large enough that the overhead of processing $k$ symbols is less than $c \cdot k$ steps. The simulation of one step of $M$ takes $O(1)$ steps of $M'$ (scan the local $k$-block, compute the new $k$-block, write it back). Choosing $k$ sufficiently large gives the speedup factor. The initial encoding of the input takes $O(n)$ steps. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The class P was first studied by Alan Cobham (1964) and Jack Edmonds (1965), who independently argued that polynomial time captures the intuitive notion of "efficient computation." Cobham's paper "The Intrinsic Computational Difficulty of Functions" defined P using Turing machines and proved its robustness under model changes. Edmonds, in his paper on maximum matching, argued that polynomial-time algorithms represent "good" algorithms.

The class NP was defined by Stephen Cook in his 1971 paper "The Complexity of Theorem-Proving Procedures," where he also proved the NP-completeness of SAT. Richard Karp independently proved NP-completeness for 21 additional problems in his 1972 paper "Reducibility Among Combinatorial Problems," establishing the pervasiveness of NP-completeness across combinatorics, logic, and optimisation [ref: TODO_REF Arora & Barak §2.1].

Leonid Levin, working independently in the Soviet Union, also proved a version of the Cook-Levin theorem around 1971, and the result is sometimes called the Cook-Levin theorem in recognition of both contributions. The P vs NP question was formalised in these papers and has remained the central open problem in computational complexity theory.
