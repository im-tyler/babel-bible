## Intuition [Beginner]

NP captures problems with a simple structure: "does there exist a solution such that it works?" You propose a solution, and a quick check confirms or denies it. The existential quantifier "there exists" is the key.

But many natural problems have richer structure. "For every input, does there exist a certificate such that for all tests, the certificate passes?" This has three quantifiers: for all, there exists, for all. The polynomial hierarchy organises problems by how many quantifier alternations they need.

Think of a game between two players. Alice and Bob alternate moves. Alice wants to reach a winning position; Bob wants to prevent it. If Alice moves first and the game lasts one move, the question "can Alice win?" is an NP problem (one existential quantifier: does there exist a move for Alice that wins?). If Bob responds, the question becomes "does there exist a move for Alice such that for all responses by Bob, Alice wins?" This is the next level up.

An oracle machine is a hypothetical computer that can instantly solve a certain class of problems, for free. Think of it as having a magic black box: you write a question on a tape, the box instantly gives the answer, and you continue computing. The polynomial hierarchy is built by giving NP machines increasingly powerful oracles. Level one is NP itself. Level two is NP with an oracle for NP. Level three is NP with an oracle for level two, and so on.

The central question is whether the hierarchy is strict — whether each level is genuinely harder than the one below — or whether it collapses at some finite level. Most researchers believe the hierarchy is strict, but this is not known. A collapse at any level would be a major structural result with consequences throughout complexity theory.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The problem $\text{MIN-DNF}$ asks: given a Boolean formula $\phi$ and an integer $k$, is there a DNF formula with at most $k$ terms that agrees with $\phi$ on all inputs of length $n$?

This problem has a "for all / there exists" structure: for all inputs $x$, there exists a short formula that matches $\phi(x)$.More precisely: does there exist a DNF $\psi$ with $\le k$ terms such that for all inputs $x$, $\psi(x) = \phi(x)$?

The quantifier pattern is "exists, for all": first existentially choose the short formula, then universally check all inputs. This places MIN-DNF in $\Sigma_2^p$.

Compare with SAT, which has a single "there exists" quantifier (does there exist a satisfying assignment?). SAT is in $\Sigma_1^p = \mathsf{NP}$. Adding one more quantifier bump takes us from NP to the second level of the polynomial hierarchy.

If $\mathsf{NP} = \mathsf{coNP}$, then the entire hierarchy collapses to NP: every level would be no harder than the first. Most complexity theorists believe this does not happen, but proving it remains open.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Definition (oracle Turing machine).** An *oracle Turing machine* $M^A$ is a Turing machine with a special *query tape*, a *query state* $q_{\text{query}}$, and access to an oracle language $A$. When $M^A$ enters $q_{\text{query}}$, the string $y$ on the query tape is tested for membership in $A$ in one step: the machine transitions to $q_{\text{yes}}$ if $y \in A$ and $q_{\text{no}}$ if $y \notin A$.

Define $\mathsf{P}^A$ as the class of languages decided by polynomial-time oracle TMs with oracle $A$. Define $\mathsf{NP}^A$ analogously.

**Definition (polynomial hierarchy).** Define inductively:

$$\Sigma_0^p = \Pi_0^p = \mathsf{P}, \qquad \Sigma_{k+1}^p = \mathsf{NP}^{\Sigma_k^p}, \qquad \Pi_{k+1}^p = \mathsf{coNP}^{\Sigma_k^p}, \qquad \mathsf{PH} = \bigcup_{k \ge 0} \Sigma_k^p.$$

Equivalently, $\Delta_{k+1}^p = \mathsf{P}^{\Sigma_k^p}$.

**Theorem (quantifier characterisation).** *$L \in \Sigma_k^p$ iff there exists a polynomial-time TM $M$ and polynomials $p_1, \ldots, p_k$ such that:*

$$x \in L \iff \exists u_1 \in \{0,1\}^{p_1(|x|)} \, \forall u_2 \in \{0,1\}^{p_2(|x|)} \cdots Q_k u_k \in \{0,1\}^{p_k(|x|)} \, M(x, u_1, \ldots, u_k) = 1,$$

*where $Q_k = \exists$ if $k$ is odd and $Q_k = \forall$ if $k$ is even.*

*Proof.* By induction on $k$. Base $k = 0$: $\Sigma_0^p = \mathsf{P}$, and the formula has zero quantifiers, so $x \in L$ iff $M(x) = 1$.

Inductive step: $L \in \Sigma_{k+1}^p = \mathsf{NP}^{\Sigma_k^p}$. By definition of NP with a $\Sigma_k^p$ oracle, $x \in L$ iff there exists a certificate $u_1$ of polynomial length such that the polynomial-time verification with oracle queries to $\Sigma_k^p$ accepts. Each oracle query to $\Sigma_k^p$ is answered by the inductive hypothesis: $y \in \Sigma_k^p$ iff $\exists u_2 \forall u_3 \cdots Q_k u_{k+1} M'(y, u_2, \ldots, u_{k+1})$. Combining the existential $u_1$ with the quantifier prefix from the oracle query gives the $\exists \forall \exists \cdots$ pattern of length $k+1$. $\square$

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"The polynomial hierarchy is just NP stacked on top of itself."* The hierarchy alternates between NP and coNP at each level. $\Sigma_2^p = \mathsf{NP}^{\mathsf{NP}}$, but the inner NP oracle itself queries a coNP-like structure at lower levels.

- *"$\Pi_k^p$ is always the complement of $\Sigma_k^p$."* Yes, by definition $\Pi_k^p = \mathsf{co}\Sigma_k^p$. But this does not mean they are different: if $\Sigma_k^p = \Pi_k^p$, the hierarchy collapses at level $k$.

- *"Oracle results resolve the polynomial hierarchy."* Oracle results (like Baker-Gill-Solovay) show that relativising techniques cannot resolve P-vs-NP, but they do not directly address whether PH collapses.

### Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (collapse).** *If $\Sigma_k^p = \Pi_k^p$ for some $k \ge 1$, then $\mathsf{PH} = \Sigma_k^p$.*

*Proof.* Suppose $\Sigma_k^p = \Pi_k^p$. We show $\Sigma_{k+1}^p = \Sigma_k^p$.

By definition, $\Sigma_{k+1}^p = \mathsf{NP}^{\Sigma_k^p}$. A language $L \in \Sigma_{k+1}^p$ has the form $x \in L \iff \exists u_1 \forall u_2 \cdots Q_{k+1} u_{k+1} M(x, u_1, \ldots, u_{k+1}) = 1$. The inner formula $\forall u_2 \cdots Q_{k+1} u_{k+1} M(x, u_1, \ldots, u_{k+1}) = 1$ defines a language in $\Pi_k^p$. By assumption $\Pi_k^p = \Sigma_k^p$, this language is in $\Sigma_k^p$, and the NP machine with $\Sigma_k^p$ oracle can decide it directly. But the outer $\exists u_1$ makes this an $\mathsf{NP}^{\Sigma_k^p} = \Sigma_{k+1}^p$ computation. Since the oracle query language is already in $\Sigma_k^p$, the outer existential quantifier just adds one more level that is absorbed by the oracle: $\Sigma_{k+1}^p = \mathsf{NP}^{\Sigma_k^p} = \mathsf{NP}^{\Pi_k^p} = \mathsf{NP}^{\Sigma_k^p} = \Sigma_{k+1}^p$, and with $\Pi_k^p = \Sigma_k^p$ the oracle query for the universal quantifier is also in $\Sigma_k^p$, so $\Sigma_{k+1}^p = \Sigma_k^p$. By induction, $\Sigma_j^p = \Sigma_k^p$ for all $j \ge k$. $\square$

**Theorem (Karp-Lipton).** *If $\mathsf{SAT} \in \mathsf{P/poly}$, then $\mathsf{PH} \subseteq \Sigma_2^p \cap \Pi_2^p$.*

*Proof sketch.* $\mathsf{SAT} \in \mathsf{P/poly}$ means there exist polynomial-size circuits $C_n$ deciding SAT on $n$-variable instances. A $\Sigma_2^p$ machine can existentially guess the circuit $C$ and universally verify its correctness on all inputs (the verification is in coNP, which is $\Pi_1^p \subseteq \Pi_2^p$). Once $C$ is verified, it can replace any SAT oracle query in a $\Sigma_k^p$ computation, reducing $\Sigma_{k+1}^p$ to $\Sigma_k^p$ with a circuit. Unwinding, every $\Sigma_k^p$ computation collapses to $\Sigma_2^p$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The polynomial hierarchy builds toward the entire edifice of structural complexity by providing a quantitative measure of quantifier complexity: the central insight is that the number of quantifier alternations in a problem's logical description determines its position in the hierarchy, and this is exactly what makes PH a natural generalisation of NP and coNP. This appears again in [47.03.03] where circuit lower bounds against AC0 imply that PH does not collapse, and in [47.03.04] where the IP = PSPACE theorem shows that polynomial space equals the class of languages with interactive proofs, strictly containing PH if the hierarchy is proper. The foundational reason the hierarchy matters is that its collapse or non-collapse has cascading consequences: if PH collapses, then many structural results in complexity theory follow as corollaries. Putting these together with the Karp-Lipton theorem shows that the polynomial hierarchy is the natural staging ground for understanding the power of non-uniform computation (circuits) versus uniform computation (Turing machines).

Show that $\Sigma_1^p = \mathsf{NP}$ directly from the definition.

$\Sigma_1^p = \mathsf{NP}^{\Sigma_0^p} = \mathsf{NP}^{\mathsf{P}}$. An NP machine with a P oracle is still an NP machine: the polynomial-time oracle queries can be simulated directly by the NP machine's polynomial-time computation. So $\Sigma_1^p = \mathsf{NP}$.

True or false: $\Pi_1^p = \mathsf{coNP}$.

**True.** $\Pi_1^p = \mathsf{coNP}^{\Sigma_0^p} = \mathsf{coNP}^{\mathsf{P}} = \mathsf{coNP}$. A coNP machine with a P oracle is still coNP, since the P queries can be computed directly.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that $\mathsf{PH} \subseteq \mathsf{PSPACE}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show that $\Sigma_k^p \subseteq \mathsf{PSPACE}$ for each $k$ by computing the quantified formula in polynomial space.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the quantifier characterisation, $L \in \Sigma_k^p$ iff $x \in L \iff \exists u_1 \forall u_2 \cdots Q_k u_k \, M(x, u_1, \ldots, u_k) = 1$. A polynomial-space machine evaluates this formula by iterating over all possible values of $u_1$ (existential: accept if any works), for each $u_1$ iterating over $u_2$ (universal: accept if all work), and so on. Each $u_i$ has polynomial length, so the machine needs only polynomial space to store the current assignment $(u_1, \ldots, u_k)$ and run $M$. So $\Sigma_k^p \subseteq \mathsf{PSPACE}$ for each $k$, and $\mathsf{PH} = \bigcup_k \Sigma_k^p \subseteq \mathsf{PSPACE}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that $\Sigma_2^p$-SAT (satisfiability of formulas of the form $\exists u_1 \forall u_2 \, \phi(u_1, u_2)$ where $\phi$ is a CNF formula) is $\Sigma_2^p$-complete.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Adapt the Cook-Levin construction to encode the NP computation with NP oracle as a quantified Boolean formula.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the quantifier characterisation, any $L \in \Sigma_2^p$ can be expressed as $x \in L \iff \exists u_1 \forall u_2 \, M(x, u_1, u_2) = 1$ for some polynomial-time TM $M$. Using the Cook-Levin construction on $M$, the condition $M(x, u_1, u_2) = 1$ is equivalent to a CNF formula $\phi_{x, u_1, u_2}$ being satisfiable. The Cook-Levin reduction gives a polynomial-time computable formula, so $x \in L \iff \exists u_1 \forall u_2 \exists w \, \phi_{x, u_1, u_2}(w) = 1$. Merging $\exists u_2 \exists w$ into $\exists u_2'$ and absorbing into the quantifier prefix gives a $\Sigma_2^p$-SAT instance. The reduction is polynomial-time, so $\Sigma_2^p$-SAT is $\Sigma_2^p$-complete.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that if $\mathsf{P} = \mathsf{NP}$, then $\mathsf{PH} = \mathsf{P}$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that $\Delta_2^p = \mathsf{P}^{\mathsf{NP}}$ contains both $\mathsf{NP}$ and $\mathsf{coNP}$, and give an example of a problem believed to be in $\Delta_2^p \setminus (\mathsf{NP} \cup \mathsf{coNP})$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\Delta_2^p = \mathsf{P}^{\mathsf{NP}}$ contains $\mathsf{NP}$ because a $\mathsf{P}^{\mathsf{NP}}$ machine can simply not use its oracle (or use it for vacuous queries). Similarly, $\mathsf{coNP} \subseteq \mathsf{P}^{\mathsf{NP}}$ because a coNP computation can be decided by querying the NP oracle for the complementary language. The problem of finding the lexicographically largest satisfying assignment of a SAT formula (called MAX-SAT-LEX) is in $\Delta_2^p$: use binary search with SAT oracle queries to determine each bit. This problem is believed to be outside NP and coNP because it requires multiple adaptive NP queries.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

Explain why the Karp-Lipton theorem ($\mathsf{SAT} \in \mathsf{P/poly} \Rightarrow \mathsf{PH} \subseteq \Sigma_2^p$) provides evidence that SAT does not have polynomial-size circuits.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If SAT had polynomial-size circuits, the polynomial hierarchy would collapse to its second level. Since most complexity theorists believe PH is infinite (strictly increasing at each level), the contrapositive provides evidence that SAT is not in P/poly. Moreover, Kannan's theorem shows that $\Sigma_2^p$ contains languages outside P/poly, so PH collapsing to $\Sigma_2^p$ would be a weaker statement than the PH being infinite — but still considered unlikely.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that $\mathsf{PH} \subseteq \mathsf{P}^{\#\mathsf{P}}$ (the class of languages decidable in polynomial time with a $\#\mathsf{P}$ oracle).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\#\mathsf{P}$ can compute SAT (by checking if the count is positive). So $\mathsf{P}^{\#\mathsf{P}} \supseteq \mathsf{P}^{\mathsf{NP}}$. Now extend this to higher levels.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A $\#\mathsf{P}$ oracle can decide SAT (query the number of satisfying assignments; if positive, the formula is satisfiable). So $\mathsf{P}^{\#\mathsf{P}} \supseteq \mathsf{P}^{\mathsf{NP}} = \Delta_2^p \supseteq \Sigma_1^p$. Now for $\Sigma_2^p$: a $\Sigma_2^p$ computation is $\exists u_1 \forall u_2 \, M(x, u_1, u_2)$. For each fixed $u_1$, the condition $\forall u_2 \, M(x, u_1, u_2)$ is a coNP question, which the $\#$P oracle can decide. So the $\exists u_1$ search can be done by a $\mathsf{P}^{\#\mathsf{P}}$ machine iterating over $u_1$ and using the $\#\mathsf{P}$ oracle for each. More generally, each $\Sigma_k^p$ computation uses $k$ alternating quantifiers, and a $\#\mathsf{P}$ oracle can resolve any NP or coNP query at the innermost level, collapsing the entire quantifier prefix to a polynomial-time computation with $\#\mathsf{P}$ oracle access. So $\mathsf{PH} \subseteq \mathsf{P}^{\#\mathsf{P}}$.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

Three structural results about the polynomial hierarchy have far-reaching consequences.

**Theorem 1 (PH and PSPACE).** $\mathsf{PH} \subseteq \mathsf{PSPACE}$. Moreover, $\mathsf{PH} = \mathsf{PSPACE}$ iff $\mathsf{PH}$ collapses to some finite level. The proof uses the fact that PSPACE can evaluate any constant number of alternating quantifiers over polynomial-length strings by reusing space [ref: TODO_REF Arora & Barak §5.3].

If PSPACE strictly contains PH, then there are polynomial-space computations that cannot be expressed as any constant number of quantifier alternations. This is widely believed but unproven.

**Theorem 2 (Sipser-Gacs-Lautemann).** $\mathsf{BPP} \subseteq \Sigma_2^p \cap \Pi_2^p$. This places the class of languages decidable by randomised polynomial-time algorithms inside the second level of the polynomial hierarchy. Combined with the Karp-Lipton theorem, this shows that if NP has small circuits, then BPP is contained in a low level of PH [ref: TODO_REF Arora & Barak §7.4].

**Theorem 3 (Yap's theorem).** If $\Sigma_k^p \subseteq \Pi_k^p$ or $\Pi_k^p \subseteq \Sigma_k^p$, then $\mathsf{PH} = \Sigma_k^p$. This strengthens the basic collapse theorem by requiring containment in only one direction rather than equality. The proof uses a padding argument that converts the one-directional containment into equality at a higher level, then applies the standard collapse theorem.

**Synthesis.** The polynomial hierarchy is the foundational reason that quantifier complexity is a meaningful measure of computational difficulty: the central insight is that the hierarchy captures the structure of alternating existential and universal choices, and this is exactly what makes it a natural setting for studying games, optimisation, and logical definability. This builds toward the circuit lower bounds of [47.03.03] where showing that parity is not in AC0 implies that PH does not collapse. The hierarchy appears again in [47.03.05] where the PCP theorem provides a probabilistic characterisation of NP ($\Sigma_1^p$), and in [47.05.03] where Miller-Rabin primality testing places a concrete number-theoretic problem inside BPP $\subseteq \Sigma_2^p$. The generalises from the single-quantifier NP/coNP framework to a multi-quantifier structure, and putting these together with the collapse theorems shows that the polynomial hierarchy is a finely balanced structure where even small changes (like NP having small circuits) have cascading consequences.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (quantifier characterisation of $\Sigma_k^p$).** *$L \in \Sigma_k^p$ iff there exist a polynomial-time TM $M$ and polynomials $p_1, \ldots, p_k$ such that $x \in L \iff \exists u_1 \forall u_2 \cdots Q_k u_k \, M(x, u_1, \ldots, u_k) = 1$.*

*Proof.* By induction on $k$. Base $k = 0$: $\Sigma_0^p = \mathsf{P}$, and the zero-quantifier formula is $x \in L \iff M(x) = 1$.

Step: $L \in \Sigma_{k+1}^p = \mathsf{NP}^{\Sigma_k^p}$. By definition of NP with a $\Sigma_k^p$ oracle, there is a polynomial-time oracle TM $N^O$ such that $x \in L \iff \exists c \in \{0,1\}^{p(|x|)} \, N^O(x, c)$ accepts. $N$ makes at most $q(|x|)$ oracle queries. Each query $y_i$ is in $\Sigma_k^p$, so by induction $y_i \in O \iff \exists v_{i,1} \forall v_{i,2} \cdots Q_k v_{i,k} \, M_i(y_i, v_{i,1}, \ldots, v_{i,k}) = 1$. The NP computation existentially guesses $c$ and the answers to all oracle queries. To verify the answers, it must check the $\Sigma_k^p$ membership of each queried string. Combining the existential guess of $c$ with the quantifier prefixes from the oracle verification gives the $\exists \forall \cdots$ pattern of length $k + 1$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (collapse at level $k$).** *If $\Sigma_k^p = \Pi_k^p$, then $\Sigma_{k+1}^p = \Sigma_k^p$.*

*Proof.* $\Sigma_{k+1}^p = \mathsf{NP}^{\Sigma_k^p}$. An NP machine with $\Sigma_k^p$ oracle existentially guesses $u_1$, then uses the oracle to decide a $\Pi_k^p$ predicate. Since $\Pi_k^p = \Sigma_k^p$, the oracle query is itself in $\Sigma_k^p$. But the NP machine already has a $\Sigma_k^p$ oracle, so it can answer this query directly. The existential guess $u_1$ combined with the $\Sigma_k^p$ oracle gives a $\Sigma_k^p$ computation (the existential quantifier is absorbed by the NP machine). So $\Sigma_{k+1}^p \subseteq \Sigma_k^p$, and $\Sigma_k^p \subseteq \Sigma_{k+1}^p$ is immediate. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- [47.02.01] — P and NP are the first level of the polynomial hierarchy; the definitions of polynomial-time reductions and NP-completeness provide the base on which the hierarchy is built.

- [47.02.02] — The Cook-Levin theorem and NP-completeness of SAT provide the oracle queries that define higher levels of the hierarchy.

- [47.03.01] — BPP is contained in $\Sigma_2^p \cap \Pi_2^p$ by the Sipser-Gacs-Lautemann theorem, connecting randomised computation to the polynomial hierarchy.

- [47.03.03] — Circuit lower bounds against AC0 imply that PH does not collapse; the parity function's absence from AC0 is a key structural consequence.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The polynomial hierarchy was introduced by Larry Stockmeyer in his 1977 PhD thesis and independently by Albert Meyer. Their insight was that the NP-coNP dichotomy generalises naturally to higher levels of quantifier alternation, and that this hierarchy captures a wide range of natural problems that do not fit cleanly into NP or coNP [ref: TODO_REF Arora & Barak §5.1].

The Karp-Lipton theorem (1980) connected the polynomial hierarchy to circuit complexity by showing that if NP problems have polynomial-size circuits, then the hierarchy collapses. This established the first major link between uniform computation (TMs) and non-uniform computation (circuits), a theme that runs through much of modern complexity theory.

The oracle machine model was introduced by Turing in his 1938 PhD thesis under Church, where he considered machines with an "oracle" for the halting problem. Baker, Gill, and Solovay (1975) used oracle machines to show that P-vs-NP cannot be resolved by relativising techniques: there exist oracles $A$ with $\mathsf{P}^A = \mathsf{NP}^A$ and oracles $B$ with $\mathsf{P}^B \ne \mathsf{NP}^B$.
