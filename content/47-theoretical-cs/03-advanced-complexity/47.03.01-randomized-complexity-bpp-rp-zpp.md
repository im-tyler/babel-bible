## Intuition [Beginner]

Sometimes flipping a coin helps. Imagine you are at a fork in a maze and do not know which way to go. If you choose randomly, you will eventually find the exit, even though any single guess might be wrong. Randomised algorithms work the same way: they make random choices during execution and have a small probability of giving the wrong answer, but by repeating the computation a few times, the error probability drops to nearly zero.

The class BPP (Bounded-error Probabilistic Polynomial time) contains every problem that can be solved by a randomised algorithm in polynomial time with error probability at most one third. Why one third? Because the exact constant does not matter: by running the algorithm a few dozen times and taking a majority vote, you can drive the error probability down to less than one in a billion.

Some problems have an even stronger guarantee. RP (Randomised Polynomial time) is the class of problems where the algorithm never says "yes" when the answer is "no" (no false positives), but it might say "no" when the answer is "yes" (a false negative) with probability at most one half. If the algorithm says "yes," you know the answer is correct. If it says "no," run it again.

The Schwartz-Zippel lemma is the workhorse of randomised algebra. It says: if you have two different multivariate polynomials, the chance that they agree at a random point is small. This lets you test whether two polynomials are identical by plugging in random values — a simple, fast, and highly effective strategy.

## Visual [Beginner]

The relationships between randomised complexity classes:

```
P ⊆ ZPP ⊆ RP ⊆ BPP
              ⊆ coRP ⊆ BPP

ZPP = RP ∩ coRP  (zero-error, expected polynomial time)
RP:  one-sided error (no false positives)
coRP: one-sided error (no false negatives)
BPP: two-sided error (bounded in both directions)
```

| Class | Error type | Guarantee | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| ZPP | Zero error | Always correct, expected poly-time | Las Vegas algorithms |
| RP | One-sided | If yes, always correct; if no, may be wrong | Polynomial identity testing |
| BPP | Two-sided | Correct with probability $\ge 2/3$ | Majority vote after repetition |
| P | No error | Always correct, worst-case poly-time | Deterministic algorithms |

## Worked example [Beginner]

You have two polynomials over the integers: $P(x, y) = (x + y)^3$ and $Q(x, y) = x^3 + 3x^2 y + 3xy^2 + y^3$. Are they the same polynomial?

Expanding $P$ gives $x^3 + 3x^2 y + 3xy^2 + y^3$, which matches $Q$. But what if $P$ and $Q$ had thousands of terms and expanding was impractical?

The Schwartz-Zippel lemma gives a shortcut. Pick random values for $x$ and $y$, say $x = 7$ and $y = 3$. Compute $P(7, 3) = 1000$ and $Q(7, 3) = 1000$. They agree. But this could happen by coincidence even if $P \ne Q$.

The lemma says: if $P \ne Q$, then the probability that $P$ and $Q$ agree at a random point chosen from a set $S$ is at most $d / |S|$, where $d$ is the degree. Here $d = 3$. If we pick $x, y$ from $\{1, 2, \ldots, 100\}$, the error probability is at most $3/100 = 3\%$.

Repeat with new random values. After $k$ independent tests, the error drops to at most $(3/100)^k$. After 5 tests, the error is less than one in two million. This is polynomial identity testing — a fast, practical randomised algorithm for a fundamental algebraic question.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A *probabilistic Turing machine* (PTM) is a Turing machine with access to a coin-flip tape: at each step, the machine can read the next bit from a tape initialised with independent uniform random bits. The output of a PTM on input $x$ is a random variable.

**Definition (BPP).** A language $L \in \mathsf{BPP}$ if there exists a polynomial-time PTM $M$ such that for all $x$:

$$\Pr[M(x) = L(x)] \ge \frac{2}{3},$$

where $L(x) = 1$ if $x \in L$ and $L(x) = 0$ otherwise. The probability is over the random bits of $M$.

**Definition (RP).** A language $L \in \mathsf{RP}$ if there exists a polynomial-time PTM $M$ such that for all $x$:

- If $x \in L$: $\Pr[M(x) = 1] \ge \frac{1}{2}$.
- If $x \notin L$: $\Pr[M(x) = 1] = 0$.

**Definition (coRP).** $L \in \mathsf{coRP}$ iff $\overline{L} \in \mathsf{RP}$.

**Definition (ZPP).** $\mathsf{ZPP} = \mathsf{RP} \cap \mathsf{coRP}$. Equivalently, $L \in \mathsf{ZPP}$ iff there exists an expected-polynomial-time PTM $M$ that never errs: $M$ either outputs the correct answer or outputs a special "don't know" symbol $\bot$, and the probability of $\bot$ is at most $1/2$.

**Lemma (Schwartz-Zippel).** Let $P \in \mathbb{F}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$ be a nonzero polynomial of total degree $d$ over a field $\mathbb{F}$. Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{F}$ be a finite subset. Then:

$$\Pr_{r_1, \ldots, r_n \in S}[P(r_1, \ldots, r_n) = 0] \le \frac{d}{|S|}.$$

Each $r_i$ is chosen independently and uniformly from $S$ [ref: TODO_REF Arora & Barak §7.3].

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *"BPP means the algorithm is correct with probability at least 2/3, which is too weak for practice."* Error reduction by repetition drives the error probability to $2^{-k}$ with $O(k)$ repetitions. Any desired confidence level is achievable with modest overhead.

- *"RP algorithms have 50 percent error rate."* The error is one-sided and only on "yes" instances. On "no" instances the algorithm is always correct. By repetition, the false-negative rate drops to $2^{-k}$.

- *"Schwartz-Zippel requires the field to be finite."* The lemma works over any field. The random evaluation points must come from a finite subset $S$, but the polynomial itself can live over $\mathbb{Q}$, $\mathbb{R}$, or any field.

### Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Schwartz-Zippel lemma).** *Let $P \in \mathbb{F}[x_1, \ldots, x_n]$ be nonzero of total degree $d$. Then for $r_1, \ldots, r_n$ chosen independently and uniformly from $S \subseteq \mathbb{F}$: $\Pr[P(r_1, \ldots, r_n) = 0] \le d/|S|$.*

*Proof.* By induction on $n$. Base case $n = 1$: a nonzero univariate polynomial of degree $d$ has at most $d$ roots, so $\Pr[P(r_1) = 0] \le d/|S|$.

Inductive step: write $P = \sum_{i=0}^{k} x_1^i \cdot Q_i(x_2, \ldots, x_n)$ where $Q_k \ne 0$ and $k \le d$. Then:

$$\Pr[P(r_1, \ldots, r_n) = 0] \le \Pr[Q_k(r_2, \ldots, r_n) = 0] + \Pr[P(r_1, \ldots, r_n) = 0 \mid Q_k(r_2, \ldots, r_n) \ne 0].$$

By the inductive hypothesis, $\Pr[Q_k = 0] \le (d - k)/|S|$. Conditioned on $Q_k \ne 0$, the polynomial $P(r_1, \ldots)$ is a nonzero polynomial in $x_1$ of degree $k$, so $\Pr[P = 0 \mid Q_k \ne 0] \le k/|S|$. Adding: $(d - k)/|S| + k/|S| = d/|S|$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Schwartz-Zippel lemma is the foundational reason polynomial identity testing (PIT) is in coRP: two distinct polynomials can agree at a random point only with low probability, so disagreement at a random point is a reliable certificate of difference. This builds toward the derandomisation programme in [47.05.01], where universal hash families use a similar probabilistic argument for load balancing, and it appears again in [47.05.03] where Miller-Rabin primality testing applies the same error-reduction-by-repetition strategy. The central insight is that random evaluation converts an algebraic identity question into a numeric computation, and putting these together with Chernoff-bound amplification gives a practical, highly reliable randomised algorithm.

What is the error probability after running a BPP algorithm 100 times and taking the majority vote, if the single-run error is $1/3$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the Chernoff bound: the error is $2^{-\Omega(k)}$ for $k$ repetitions.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the Chernoff bound, the error after $k = 100$ repetitions is at most $e^{-2k(2/3 - 1/2)^2} = e^{-2 \cdot 100 \cdot (1/6)^2} = e^{-200/36} \approx e^{-5.56} < 0.004$. More precisely, the error is $2^{-\Omega(100)}$, which is astronomically small — far below $2^{-30}$.

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Consider $P(x, y) = x^3 y - x y^3$ and $Q(x, y) = 0$. Using Schwartz-Zippel with $S = \{1, 2, \ldots, 50\}$, bound the probability that a random evaluation gives $P(r_1, r_2) = 0$ when $P \ne 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The total degree of $P$ is $\deg(x^3 y) = 4$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$P(x, y) = xy(x^2 - y^2) = xy(x - y)(x + y)$. The total degree is $d = 4$. By Schwartz-Zippel: $\Pr[P(r_1, r_2) = 0] \le d/|S| = 4/50 = 0.08$. Note that $P$ actually has more structure (it factors into degree-1 pieces), so the true root set is larger than a generic degree-4 polynomial, but the Schwartz-Zippel bound already gives an 8% error rate.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, short answer).**

Prove that $\mathsf{ZPP} = \mathsf{RP} \cap \mathsf{coRP}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathsf{ZPP} \subseteq \mathsf{RP}$: run the ZPP machine and treat $\bot$ as "no." $\mathsf{RP} \cap \mathsf{coRP} \subseteq \mathsf{ZPP}$: run both the RP and coRP machines.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\mathsf{ZPP} \subseteq \mathsf{RP}$: Given a ZPP machine $M$ for $L$, define an RP machine $M'$ that runs $M$ and outputs "yes" if $M$ outputs "yes," outputs "no" if $M$ outputs "no" or $\bot$. Then $x \notin L \Rightarrow M'$ never outputs "yes" (since $M$ never errs), and $x \in L \Rightarrow M'$ outputs "yes" with probability $\ge 1/2$ (since $\Pr[M = \bot] \le 1/2$). Similarly $\mathsf{ZPP} \subseteq \mathsf{coRP}$.

$\mathsf{RP} \cap \mathsf{coRP} \subseteq \mathsf{ZPP}$: Given $M_{\text{RP}}$ for $L$ and $M_{\text{coRP}}$ for $\overline{L}$, run both. If $M_{\text{RP}}$ says "yes," output "yes" (correct by RP guarantee). If $M_{\text{coRP}}$ says "yes" (meaning $x \notin L$), output "no" (correct by coRP guarantee). If both say "no," output $\bot$. The probability of $\bot$ is at most $1/4$ (both fail to recognise their side) by the union bound, so the expected running time is polynomial.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short answer).**

Prove the error-reduction theorem for BPP: if $L \in \mathsf{BPP}$, then for every polynomial $p$, there exists a BPP machine for $L$ with error at most $2^{-p(n)}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Run the original BPP machine $k = O(p(n))$ times and take the majority vote. Apply the Chernoff bound.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $M$ be a BPP machine for $L$ with error $1/3$. Run $M$ independently $k$ times on input $x$, producing outputs $Y_1, \ldots, Y_k \in \{0, 1\}$. Let $Y = \sum Y_i$ and output the majority (1 if $Y > k/2$, else 0). If the correct answer is 1, each $Y_i = 1$ with probability $\ge 2/3$, so $\mathbb{E}[Y] \ge 2k/3$. The error (outputting 0) requires $Y \le k/2$, a deviation of $\ge k/6$ from the mean. By the Chernoff bound: $\Pr[Y \le k/2] \le e^{-2k(1/6)^2} = e^{-k/18}$. Setting $k = 18 p(n) \ln 2$ gives error $\le 2^{-p(n)}$. The running time is $O(k \cdot T_M(n))$, polynomial. Rubric: full credit for the Chernoff-bound calculation and the choice of $k$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, short answer).**

Show that polynomial identity testing (PIT) — given two arithmetic circuits computing polynomials $P$ and $Q$, decide if $P = Q$ — is in coRP.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The algorithm: choose random $r_1, \ldots, r_n$ from a set $S$ of size $2d$, evaluate both $P(r)$ and $Q(r)$, and output "different" if they disagree, "same" if they agree. If $P = Q$, the algorithm always outputs "same" (no false positives — coRP has one-sided error on the "same" side). If $P \ne Q$, the polynomial $P - Q$ is nonzero of degree $d$, so by Schwartz-Zippel, $\Pr[P(r) = Q(r)] \le d/|S| = d/(2d) = 1/2$. So the algorithm outputs "same" with probability at most $1/2$ when $P \ne Q$, satisfying the coRP guarantee. Rubric: full credit for recognising the one-sided error structure and applying Schwartz-Zippel.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

Three structural results sharpen the relationship between randomised and deterministic computation.

**Theorem 1 (BPP $\subseteq$ P/poly).** Every language in BPP has polynomial-size circuits [ref: TODO_REF Arora & Barak §7.5]. The proof uses the probabilistic method: a BPP machine $M$ uses $m$ random bits, and there are $2^m$ possible random strings. By averaging, for every input $x$ of length $n$, at most $2^m/3$ random strings cause $M$ to err. A counting argument shows there exists a fixed set of $O(n)$ random strings (an "advice" string) that works for all inputs of length $n$; this advice string defines a polynomial-size circuit.

**Theorem 2 (Sipser-Gacs-Lautemann: BPP $\subseteq \Sigma_2^p \cap \Pi_2^p$).** BPP is contained in the second level of the polynomial hierarchy [ref: TODO_REF Arora & Barak §7.6]. The proof uses the pairwise independence hashing technique: the acceptance condition of a BPP machine can be expressed as a $\Sigma_2$ predicate ("there exist shifts such that for all inputs, the majority of shifted random strings give the correct answer"). This places BPP inside the polynomial hierarchy and suggests that randomisation does not add much power beyond determinism, assuming the polynomial hierarchy does not collapse.

**Theorem 3 (derandomisation conjectures).** Several hardness-vs-randomness tradeoffs relate derandomisation to circuit lower bounds. The Impagliazzo-Wigderson theorem (1997) shows that if there exists a language in E = DTIME($2^{O(n)}$) that requires exponential-size circuits, then BPP = P. The proof constructs a pseudorandom generator from the hard language, whose output is indistinguishable from truly random bits by any polynomial-time algorithm. This is the strongest evidence that BPP = P: it reduces the question to a (plausible) circuit lower bound [ref: TODO_REF Arora & Barak §7.5].

**Synthesis.** The randomised complexity classes BPP, RP, and ZPP are the foundational reason randomised algorithms are practical: error reduction via repetition drives the failure probability to negligible levels, and the Schwartz-Zippel lemma provides a general-purpose randomised test for algebraic identities. The central insight is that randomness is a resource that, like time and space, can be quantified and controlled, and putting these together with the Sipser-Gacs-Lautemann theorem places BPP inside the polynomial hierarchy of [47.02.04], suggesting randomisation does not add fundamental power. This builds toward the derandomisation programme of [47.05.01], where universal hash families replace true randomness with structured pseudorandom objects, and appears again in [47.05.03] where Miller-Rabin primality testing demonstrates a natural problem in BPP (and now in P, after AKS 2002).

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (error reduction for BPP).** *If $L \in \mathsf{BPP}$, then for every constant $c > 0$ there is a BPP machine for $L$ with error at most $c$.*

*Proof.* Let $M$ have error $1/3$. Run $M$ independently $k$ times and take the majority. Let $X_i$ be the indicator of correct output on run $i$. Each $X_i$ has $\mathbb{E}[X_i] \ge 2/3$ and the $X_i$ are independent. The majority errs iff $\sum X_i \le k/2$, a deviation of $\ge k/6$ below the mean $\mu \ge 2k/3$. By the Chernoff bound: $\Pr[\sum X_i \le k/2] \le e^{-2(k/6)^2/k} = e^{-k/18}$. For $k \ge 18 \ln(1/c)$, the error is at most $c$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Schwartz-Zippel, full proof by induction).** *Let $P \ne 0$ have total degree $d$. Then $\Pr_{r \in S^n}[P(r) = 0] \le d/|S|$.*

*Proof.* Induction on $n$. For $n = 1$, a univariate polynomial of degree $d$ has at most $d$ roots, so $\Pr \le d/|S|$. For $n > 1$, write $P = \sum_{i=0}^k x_1^i Q_i(x_2, \ldots, x_n)$ with $Q_k \ne 0$, $\deg(Q_k) \le d - k$. Then:

$$\Pr[P(r) = 0] = \Pr[Q_k = 0] + \Pr[P = 0 \mid Q_k \ne 0] \cdot \Pr[Q_k \ne 0] \le \frac{d-k}{|S|} + \frac{k}{|S|} \cdot 1 = \frac{d}{|S|},$$

where $\Pr[Q_k = 0] \le (d-k)/|S|$ by induction and $\Pr[P = 0 \mid Q_k \ne 0] \le k/|S|$ by the univariate case applied to $P$ as a polynomial in $x_1$ with coefficients evaluated at $r_2, \ldots, r_n$. $\square$

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Randomised algorithms entered computational complexity through the work of Michael Rabin (1976) and Robert Solovay and Volker Strassen (1977), who independently discovered randomised primality tests. Rabin's paper "Probabilistic Algorithm for Testing Primality" introduced the concept of a probabilistic algorithm with bounded one-sided error (the class RP), and the Solovay-Strassen test provided an alternative with similar guarantees [ref: TODO_REF Arora & Barak §7.1].

The class BPP was formally defined by John Gill (1977) in his paper "Computational Complexity of Probabilistic Turing Machines." The Schwartz-Zippel lemma was discovered independently by Jack Schwartz (1980) and Richard Zippel (1979). Schwartz's paper "Fast Probabilistic Algorithms for Verification of Polynomial Identities" established the lemma for multivariate polynomials over arbitrary fields and used it for polynomial identity testing.

The Sipser-Gacs-Lautemann theorem (1983) showed that BPP resides in the polynomial hierarchy, providing the first evidence that randomisation does not dramatically increase computational power. The Impagliazzo-Wigderson theorem (1997) strengthened this by showing that BPP = P under plausible hardness assumptions, essentially reducing derandomisation to circuit lower bounds.
