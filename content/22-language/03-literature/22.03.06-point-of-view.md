## Intuition [Beginner]

Every story is told from somewhere. The **point of view** (POV) is the position from which the narrative is delivered -- who is telling the story, how much they know, and how close they are to the characters and events. Change the point of view, and you change the entire story. Imagine *The Great Gatsby* told by Gatsby himself, or *Harry Potter* told by Voldemort. The events would be the same, but the reader's experience would be completely different.

**First person** ("I") puts the reader inside a single character's head. The narrator is a participant in the story, and the reader sees everything through that character's eyes. Holden Caulfield's narration in *The Catcher in the Rye* immediately establishes his voice, his attitude, and his limitations. The reader knows only what Holden knows, sees only what Holden sees, and must judge Holden's account while recognizing that Holden may be unreliable.

**Third person** uses "he," "she," or "they" and offers a range of possibilities. **Third person omniscient** means the narrator knows everything about all characters -- their thoughts, feelings, histories, and futures. The narrator of Leo Tolstoy's *Anna Karenina* can move freely between characters, revealing Levin's inner struggles and Anna's emotional turmoil with equal authority.

**Third person limited** follows one character closely, revealing that character's inner life while describing other characters only from the outside. **Third person objective** (or dramatic) reports only what can be seen and heard, like a camera, without entering any character's mind.

**Second person** ("you") is rare but powerful when used well. In Jay McInerney's *Bright Lights, Big City*, the narration creates a voice that is simultaneously the character talking to himself and a narrator addressing the character. The effect is disorienting, which suits the novel's portrait of a man losing control.

The choice of POV is never neutral. It determines what information the reader receives, whose perspective shapes that information, and what remains hidden. A first-person narrator cannot know what other characters are thinking. An omniscient narrator can know everything but must choose what to reveal and when.

Point of view is not unique to Western fiction. In the oral storytelling traditions of the Ashanti people, the griot narrates from a position of communal knowledge, adopting what might be called a cultural omniscience -- the griot knows the story's outcome, the characters' fates, and the moral lessons the story carries, because the story belongs to the community rather than to any individual narrator. The POV is neither first-person nor third-person but collective.

In the Japanese literary tradition, the *I-novel* (watakushi shosetsu) developed as a distinctive form that blends autobiography and fiction. The narrator shares the author's name and biographical details, creating a point of view that exists at the boundary between first-person confession and third-person observation. The works of Natsume Soseki, particularly *Kokoro* (1914), use this ambiguous POV to explore the relationship between public persona and private self.

In the Arabic literary tradition, the *maqama* uses a dual point of view: a narrator who describes the exploits of a trickster figure, creating a gap between what the narrator observes and what the trickster intends. The POV is structured around dramatic irony -- the narrator (and the reader) knows less than the trickster, and the pleasure of the form comes from the gradual revelation of the trick.

## Worked Example [Beginner]

Read these three versions of the same scene:

**Version A (First person):** "I walked into the kitchen and saw Maria standing at the counter with her back to me. She was crying. I wanted to reach out, but something held me back. What had I done wrong?"

**Version B (Third person limited):** "James walked into the kitchen and saw Maria standing at the counter with her back to him. She was crying. He wanted to reach out, but something held him back. What had he done wrong?"

**Version C (Third person omniscient):** "James walked into the kitchen and saw Maria standing at the counter with her back to him. She was crying -- not because of anything he had done, but because the letter from her mother had arrived that morning, and with it the old grief she thought she had buried. James wanted to reach out, but something held him back, a fear of making things worse that he had carried since childhood."

**Step 1: Compare the information.** Version A gives one character's inner experience directly ("I wanted"). Version B gives the same experience in third person ("He wanted"). Version C gives *both* characters' inner experiences -- Maria's grief and James's fear -- which no single character in the scene could know.

**Step 2: Compare the effect.** In Versions A and B, the reader is aligned with James. Maria's tears are mysterious, and the reader shares James's uncertainty. In Version C, the reader knows more than James. The reader understands Maria's tears immediately, which changes the emotional register from mystery to dramatic irony.

**Step 3: Evaluate the trade-offs.** The omniscient version provides more information but less tension. The limited versions create suspense (why is Maria crying?) but restrict understanding. Neither is better -- the choice depends on what the writer wants the scene to accomplish.

**Step 4: Cross-cultural comparison.** In the fiction of Naguib Mahfouz, the choice of POV carries political implications. *Midaq Alley* (1947) uses a third-person omniscient narrator who knows the thoughts and histories of all the alley's inhabitants, creating a portrait of a community rather than an individual. The omniscient POV reflects the Arabic literary value of communal narrative -- the story belongs to everyone.

In the fiction of Chinua Achebe, *Things Fall Apart* uses a third-person narrator who moves between Igbo perspectives and, briefly, the colonial perspective. The shift in POV at the novel's end is its most devastating moment: the reader who has seen the world through Igbo eyes is suddenly forced to see it through the eyes of the District Commissioner, and the reduction in understanding is shocking.

## Formal Definition [Intermediate+]

**Point of view** (also called narrative perspective or focalization) refers to the position or perspective from which a narrative is presented, including the relationship between the narrator and the story, the degree of access to characters' consciousness, and the epistemological and ideological stance from which events are rendered.

### Classification

Franz Stanzel proposed a triadic model based on three axes:
- **Person** (first vs. third person narrative situation)
- **Perspective** (internal vs. external focalization)
- **Mode** (telling vs. showing)

Gerard Genette refined this with the concept of **focalization**:
- **Zero focalization**: The narrator knows more than the character (equivalent to omniscient).
- **Internal focalization**: The narrator knows exactly what the character knows (equivalent to limited).
- **External focalization**: The narrator knows less than the character, describing only observable behavior.

### Major Types

- **First-person narrative**: The narrator is a character in the story, using "I." May be the protagonist (Nick Carraway in *The Great Gatsby*), a minor character (Ishmael in *Moby-Dick*), or an observer. Always limited to the narrator's knowledge and subject to the narrator's biases.
- **Second-person narrative**: The narrator uses "you," addressing the protagonist. Creates a complex identification between reader, narrator, and character. Relatively rare in sustained narrative.
- **Third-person omniscient**: An external narrator with unrestricted knowledge of all characters' thoughts, feelings, and histories, as well as events occurring in multiple locations. May offer judgments and commentary.
- **Third-person limited (or close)**: An external narrator who restricts knowledge to one character's (or a few characters') consciousness. The reader has access to the focal character's inner life but not to other characters'.
- **Third-person objective (or dramatic)**: An external narrator who reports only observable actions and dialogue, without access to any character's inner life. The reader must infer thoughts and feelings from behavior.

### Related Concepts

- **Free indirect discourse**: A technique that merges third-person narration with a character's voice, presenting the character's thoughts without attribution ("She was furious. How dare he?"). Flaubert pioneered this technique; it became central to modernist fiction.
- **Stream of consciousness**: An extreme form of internal focalization that presents a character's thoughts in the associative, fragmented form they actually occur, without narrative mediation. Associated with Joyce, Woolf, and Faulkner.
- **Multiple focalization**: A narrative that shifts focalization among several characters, providing different perspectives on the same events. Used by Faulkner in *The Sound and the Fury* and by Kurosawa in the film *Rashomon*.

### Point of View Across Literary Traditions

The concept of POV varies across literary cultures in ways that reveal fundamental assumptions about the relationship between narrator, audience, and truth. In the Sanskrit dramatic tradition, the concept of *sahrdaya* (the ideal spectator) implies a point of view that is not aligned with any character but with the audience's collective aesthetic experience. The dramatic text assumes a spectator who understands the conventions of the genre and can appreciate the irony that arises from knowing more than the characters.

In the Chinese literary tradition, the classical novel often employs a point of view that might be called "historical omniscience." The narrator of *Romance of the Three Kingdoms* (fourteenth century) presents events with the authority of a historian recording what has already happened. The narrator knows the outcome of every battle and the fate of every character, and this knowledge shapes the narrative tone. The POV reflects the Confucian understanding that history teaches moral lessons, and the narrator's omniscience serves a didactic purpose.

In the Persian literary tradition, the narrative poem (*masnavi*) often uses a first-person narrator who is simultaneously the poet, a character in the story, and a spiritual guide. Rumi's *Masnavi* (thirteenth century) shifts between these roles fluidly, creating a POV that operates on multiple levels simultaneously. The reader is addressed as a spiritual seeker, a listener to a story, and a participant in a mystical experience.

In the Yoruba novel, Daniel O. Fagunwa's *Forest of a Thousand Daemons* (1938) uses a first-person narrator who is simultaneously an adventurer and a moral guide. The narrator's first-person account of his journey through a supernatural forest is framed as a story told to instruct the listener, combining the intimacy of personal narrative with the authority of communal wisdom.

In the fiction of Jamaica Kincaid, the use of second-person narration in *Girl* (1983) creates a POV that is simultaneously the voice of the mother giving instructions and the voice of the daughter receiving them. The second person ("Wash the white clothes on Monday and put them on the stone heap") collapses the distance between speaker and listener, creating a POV that is neither first-person nor third-person but a hybrid that enacts the power dynamics of the mother-daughter relationship.

In the fiction of the South African novelist J.M. Coetzee, POV operates as a site of ethical investigation. *Disgrace* (1999) uses a third-person limited POV aligned with David Lurie, a disgraced academic. The reader is forced to see the world through Lurie's eyes -- including his rationalizations for his own misconduct. The limited POV creates discomfort: the reader must inhabit a perspective they may find morally repugnant, raising questions about the relationship between narrative identification and ethical judgment.

In the fiction of the Korean novelist Hwang Sok-yong, POV operates through the tension between individual memory and collective history. In *The Guest* (2001), the first-person narrator returns to his hometown and discovers that his memories of the Korean War conflict with the memories of the community. The limited first-person POV becomes a vehicle for exploring the unreliability of individual memory and the necessity of communal testimony.

In the oral storytelling traditions of the Inuit peoples, POV is shaped by the relationship between humans and animals. Many stories are narrated from the animal's perspective, or from a perspective that includes both human and animal consciousness. This dual POV reflects the Inuit understanding that animals have their own perspectives, intentions, and knowledge -- a worldview in which the boundary between human and animal consciousness is permeable.

## Advanced Results: Point of View in Theory and Practice [Master]

**Formalist / New Critical approaches.** Formalists analyze POV as a structural choice with aesthetic consequences. Wayne Booth's *The Rhetoric of Fiction* (1961) was a landmark study, arguing that the "implied author" -- the version of the author constructed by the text -- communicates through choices of narrative distance, reliability, and perspective. Booth challenged the modernist dogma that "showing" (dramatic POV) is always superior to "telling" (authorial commentary).

**Structuralist approaches.** Genette's narratology systematized the analysis of POV through the concept of focalization, distinguishing between *who sees* (the focalizer) and *who speaks* (the narrator). This distinction resolved confusions in earlier criticism that conflated the narrator's voice with the character's perspective. Mieke Bal extended Genette's framework, arguing that focalization always involves a "focalizer" (the consciousness through which events are filtered) and a "focalized object" (what is perceived).

**Post-structuralist approaches.** Post-structuralist critics challenge the stability of POV categories. If language itself is inherently unstable, then the distinction between narrator and character, insider and outsider, reliable and unreliable is never secure. Derrida's critique of the "metaphysics of presence" applies to narrative: there is no pure, unmediated access to a character's consciousness, only the textual effects that create the *illusion* of such access.

**Marxist approaches.** Marxist critics examine how POV encodes ideological positions. The omniscient narrator of the nineteenth-century realist novel (Balzac, Dickens, Tolstoy) was read by Lukacs as expressing the bourgeois conviction that the social world could be comprehensively known and represented. Modernist fragmentation of POV (Faulkner, Woolf) was read by some Marxist critics as reflecting the disintegration of bourgeois social coherence.

More recently, Marxist critics have analyzed how the choice of whose perspective is centered and whose is marginalized reflects power structures. The question "Whose story is told?" is always also the question "Whose story is silenced?" The omniscient narrator who claims to see everything may in fact be performing a kind of epistemological imperialism -- the assumption that all perspectives can be mastered by a single consciousness.

**Feminist approaches.** Feminist narratology, developed by Susan Lanser, analyzes how gender shapes narrative perspective. Lanser argues that the authority of the omniscient narrator is gendered -- the "authorial voice" of the nineteenth-century novel is coded as male, while female narrators are often restricted to limited or first-person perspectives. Feminist writers have experimented with POV to challenge these conventions: Jean Rhys's *Wide Sargasso Sea* re-centers the perspective of Bertha Mason, the madwoman excluded from Jane Eyre's first-person narrative.

Toni Morrison's *Beloved* (1987) uses a radical form of multiple focalization that includes the perspective of the dead baby's ghost. The ghost's POV -- fragmented, pre-linguistic, hovering between presence and absence -- challenges the assumption that consciousness must be coherent, linear, or even alive to have a point of view. Morrison's technique expands the concept of POV to include perspectives that Western narrative theory has traditionally excluded.

**Postcolonial approaches.** Postcolonial critics examine how colonial narratives center the colonizer's perspective and marginalize or silence the colonized. Chinua Achebe's *Things Fall Apart* performs a deliberate reversal of POV, presenting Igbo culture from the inside and rendering the colonial perspective as alien and intrusive. The brief shift to the colonial POV in the novel's final paragraph exposes how narrative perspective is an instrument of power.

In the fiction of Salman Rushdie, POV becomes a site of cultural collision. *Midnight's Children* (1981) uses a first-person narrator whose stream-of-consciousness narration blends English, Hindi, and Urdu, creating a POV that is linguistically hybrid. The narrator's perspective embodies the postcolonial condition -- caught between cultures, speaking a language that belongs to the colonizer while thinking in a language that belongs to the colonized.

In the fiction of Jamaica Kincaid, the first-person POV carries the weight of colonial history. The narrator of *Lucy* (1990) is a young woman from the Caribbean working as an au pair in the United States, and her first-person account reveals the gap between how she sees herself and how the American family sees her. The limited POV is itself a political statement: the colonized subject's perspective has been excluded from narrative authority, and Kincaid's decision to center it is an act of epistemic justice.

**Reader-response approaches.** Reader-response theorists emphasize that POV creates a "reading position" -- a set of expectations, sympathies, and knowledge that the reader is invited to occupy. Judith Fetterley's *The Resisting Reader* (1978) argued that canonical texts often construct a male reading position that female readers must either accept or resist. The act of reading from a POV not one's own is both an imaginative achievement and a potential site of ideological coercion.

**Cognitive approaches.** Cognitive narratologists have examined how readers process POV shifts. Research suggests that readers maintain a "mental model" of the narrative situation that includes the current focalizer, and that POV shifts require readers to update this model. The cognitive cost of frequent POV shifts may explain why readers sometimes find heavily focalized texts (like Faulkner's *The Sound and the Fury*) difficult: the reader must constantly rebuild their mental model of who is perceiving the events.

Cross-cultural cognitive research has found that readers from different literary traditions process POV differently. Readers steeped in the Western novel tradition, with its emphasis on individual consciousness, tend to expect deep interior access and find external focalization austere. Readers from traditions that emphasize communal narration (West African oral storytelling, for instance) may find the Western fixation on individual consciousness narrow and solipsistic.

### POV and Unreliability

One of the most important developments in the theory of POV is the concept of the unreliable narrator -- a first-person narrator whose account of events cannot be trusted. The concept was named by Wayne Booth in *The Rhetoric of Fiction* (1961), though the technique is much older. The narrator of Laurence Sterne's *Tristram Shandy* (1759-67) is unreliable because he is too digressive and self-absorbed to tell a coherent story. The narrator of Ford Madox Ford's *The Good Soldier* (1915) is unreliable because he does not understand the events he is describing, even as he narrates them.

Unreliable narration is not limited to Western fiction. In the fiction of Haruki Murakami, the first-person narrators are often unreliable in a distinctive way: they are not dishonest but limited, unable to perceive the supernatural dimensions of the situations they find themselves in. The reader gradually recognizes that the narrator's account is incomplete -- that there are aspects of reality the narrator cannot see. This form of unreliability reflects the Japanese literary tradition's comfort with ambiguity and the limits of individual perception.

In the fiction of the Egyptian novelist Alaa Al Aswany, *The Yacoubian Building* (2002) uses multiple third-person limited POVs to create a portrait of contemporary Cairo. Each character's section reveals their private thoughts and motivations, but no single character understands the full picture. The reader, who has access to all perspectives, sees connections that the characters cannot. The multiple POVs create a form of communal knowledge that no individual possesses.

### POV and Empathy

Research in cognitive literary studies has examined the relationship between POV and empathy. Studies by Suzanne Keen and others suggest that first-person and close third-person narration tend to generate more empathetic responses than omniscient or objective narration. The reader who is invited into a character's consciousness is more likely to feel empathy for that character, even if the character's actions are morally questionable.

This finding has implications for the ethics of narrative. If POV shapes empathy, and empathy shapes moral judgment, then the choice of whose perspective to center in a narrative is an ethical choice with real consequences. A novel that centers the perspective of a perpetrator generates a different moral response than one that centers the perspective of a victim. A news report that presents events from the police perspective generates a different response than one that presents the same events from the perspective of the person stopped by the police. The study of literary POV sharpens awareness of how narrative perspective shapes not only aesthetic experience but moral and political judgment.

## Connections [Master]

This unit connects to the surrounding language sequence through prerequisites (22.01.03) and successors (22.03.07, 22.03.10). In the wider Babel Bible map, point of view links to philosophy (where questions of perspective and objectivity are central to epistemology), to rhetoric (where the choice of speaker and audience shapes argument), to psychology (where theory of mind is the cognitive capacity that allows us to attribute mental states to others), and to political science (where the question of whose perspective counts is foundational to democratic theory).

The study of POV connects to film theory, where the camera creates a literal point of view. The distinction between subjective camera (showing what a character sees) and objective camera (showing the scene from an external position) mirrors the literary distinction between internal and external focalization. Film theorists like Edward Branigan have developed detailed analyses of cinematic POV that parallel Genette's analysis of literary focalization.

It connects to journalism, where the choice of whose perspective to center in a news story has political consequences. The same event looks different when reported from the perspective of the police, the protester, the bystander, or the distant editor. The study of literary POV sharpens the reader's awareness of how perspective shapes even supposedly objective reporting.

It connects to artificial intelligence, where the problem of representing multiple perspectives is central to multi-agent systems. An AI system that cannot represent the knowledge and beliefs of different agents cannot plan effectively in social situations. The literary study of POV provides a rich vocabulary for thinking about how perspectives differ, overlap, and conflict.

It connects to psychoanalysis, where the concept of transference -- the patient's projection of feelings about significant others onto the analyst -- is a form of POV confusion. The patient sees the analyst through the lens of past relationships, creating a distorted perspective that the analytic process works to reveal and correct. The literary concept of the unreliable narrator has a structural parallel in the psychoanalytic concept of resistance.

It connects to ethics and moral philosophy. The philosopher Martha Nussbaum has argued that literature plays a unique role in moral education because it requires readers to adopt perspectives different from their own. The act of reading a first-person narrative from the perspective of someone of a different race, gender, class, or culture exercises the same imaginative capacity that is required for empathy in real life. The study of POV, in this view, is not merely an academic exercise but a form of moral training.

It connects to music, where the concept of "voice" has both literal and metaphorical dimensions. In polyphonic music, multiple voices sing simultaneously, each with its own melodic line, creating a texture in which no single voice dominates. Bakhtin's concept of the "polyphonic novel" draws on this musical analogy: the novel that gives equal weight to multiple character perspectives resembles the polyphonic musical composition in which each voice retains its integrity while contributing to the whole.

## Historical and Philosophical Context [Master]

The earliest narratives were predominantly third-person omniscient. Homer's epics feature a narrator who knows the thoughts of all characters and the will of the gods. The omniscient perspective was natural to a culture that understood reality as governed by divine forces accessible to the inspired poet.

But the history of POV extends well beyond the Western tradition. In the *Mahabharata* (circa fourth century BCE), the framing device creates multiple nested POVs. The story is narrated by a bard who heard it from another bard, who heard it from a sage, who heard it from the characters themselves. Each layer of narration adds a perspective, and the reader must hold all perspectives simultaneously. The nested POV reflects the Hindu philosophical understanding that reality is maya (illusion), and that every account of events is already a mediation through a particular consciousness.

In the storytelling traditions of the Aboriginal peoples of Australia, POV is shaped by the Dreaming -- the eternal present in which ancestral beings created the landscape. A story told from the Dreaming perspective is not narrated from the point of view of any individual but from the point of view of the land itself. The storyteller speaks for the country, and the POV is ecological rather than individual. Kim Scott's *That Deadman Dance* (2010) draws on this tradition, creating a narrative perspective that includes the land as a perceiving subject rather than an inert setting.

The novel, as it developed in the eighteenth century, experimented with POV. Daniel Defoe's *Robinson Crusoe* (1719) and *Moll Flanders* (1722) use first-person narration to create the illusion of authentic autobiography. Samuel Richardson's *Pamela* (1740) and *Clarissa* (1748) use the epistolary form (letters written by characters) to create multiple first-person perspectives.

The nineteenth century saw the dominance of the omniscient narrator in the realist novel. Balzac, Dickens, Tolstoy, and George Eliot employed narrators who moved freely among characters, offering social commentary and moral judgment. This omniscience reflected a confidence -- or at least an aspiration -- that the social world could be comprehensively represented.

Henry James was the pivotal figure in the shift toward limited POV. In his prefaces and critical writings, James argued for the "central intelligence" -- a single character whose consciousness serves as the lens through which events are filtered. James believed that limiting POV increased dramatic intensity and psychological depth.

The modernists radicalized James's innovations. James Joyce's *Ulysses* (1922) moves through multiple styles and focalizations, culminating in the famous final chapter -- a sustained stream of consciousness from Molly Bloom's perspective, delivered without punctuation. Virginia Woolf's *Mrs Dalloway* (1925) shifts focalization among characters through free indirect discourse. William Faulkner's *The Sound and the Fury* (1929) presents four sections with radically different POVs.

In the Japanese *I-novel* tradition, the distinction between fiction and autobiography dissolved. The works of Osamu Dazai, particularly *No Longer Human* (1948), use a first-person POV that is so intensely self-scrutinizing that it creates a hall-of-mirrors effect: the narrator observes himself observing himself, and the reader cannot determine where the self ends and the performance begins.

In the fiction of the Nigerian novelist Flora Nwapa, the first-person POV is used to give voice to women whose perspectives had been excluded from both colonial and postcolonial narratives. *Efuru* (1966) presents its protagonist's inner life with a directness and authority that challenges the assumption that African women's stories must be told by someone else. The POV is itself a political act.

In the storytelling traditions of the Maori people, the concept of *whakapapa* (genealogy) creates a POV that extends across generations. A story told from the perspective of the ancestors includes the knowledge of what has happened and what will happen, because the ancestors exist outside of linear time. Patricia Grace's *Potiki* (1986) draws on this tradition, creating a narrative perspective that includes the voices of the dead alongside the voices of the living.

Postmodernism further fragmented POV. John Fowles's *The French Lieutenant's Woman* (1969) features a narrator who alternately claims omniscience and denies it, commenting on the fictionality of the enterprise. The boundaries between narrator, character, and author became deliberately unstable.

In the fiction of the Argentine writer Jorge Luis Borges, POV operates as a philosophical puzzle. "The Garden of Forking Paths" (1941) presents a first-person narrator who is simultaneously a Chinese spy, a descendant of a legendary novel-creator, and a character in a story that may be the very novel his ancestor imagined. The POV collapses the distinction between fiction and reality, reader and character, narrator and author. Borges's technique reveals that POV is not merely a narrative choice but an epistemological condition: we always see the world from somewhere, and that "somewhere" shapes everything we perceive.

The philosopher Mikhail Bakhtin argued that the novel is distinguished from other literary forms by its capacity for "dialogism" -- the inclusion of multiple voices and perspectives within a single narrative. Bakhtin contrasted the novel's polyphonic structure with the monologic structure of the epic, in which a single authoritative voice dominates. The development of POV in the novel can be read as the progressive realization of Bakhtin's dialogic ideal: from the single voice of the epic singer, to the authoritative narrator of the realist novel, to the multiple, conflicting voices of modernist and postmodernist fiction.
