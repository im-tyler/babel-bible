## Intuition [Beginner]

When you say "it's raining cats and dogs," nobody looks outside expecting to see animals falling from the sky. You are using **figurative language** -- words that carry meaning beyond their dictionary definition. **Literal language**, by contrast, means exactly what it says. If you announce "it is raining heavily," each word corresponds to its straightforward, factual meaning.

The distinction matters because writers constantly move between these two registers. A science textbook relies almost entirely on literal language: "Water freezes at zero degrees Celsius." A novel might describe the same fact figuratively: "The lake pulled a sheet of ice over its face." Both convey information, but the figurative version does additional work -- it startles, it paints a picture, it makes you feel the cold.

Every word in English has a **denotation** (its dictionary meaning) and often a **connotation** (the associations and feelings it carries). "Home" denotes a place where someone lives, but it connotes warmth, belonging, safety. "House" denotes the same structure but carries fewer emotional associations.

Writers exploit this gap between denotation and connotation constantly. When Shakespeare has Macbeth say "life's but a walking shadow," he is not making a factual claim about biology. He is using figurative language to compress an entire philosophy about mortality into five words. The denotation of "shadow" is a dark area produced by an object blocking light; the connotation includes insubstantiality, impermanence, and the ghostly remnant of something once solid.

The same technique operates in popular culture. When a songwriter writes "I'm on fire," no one reaches for an extinguisher. The figurative meaning (intense emotion, usually passion or ambition) is immediately recovered because listeners share the convention. When a sports commentator says "the crowd exploded," no one expects rubble. These examples show that the literal-figurative distinction is not a rare literary phenomenon but a constant feature of everyday language.

Figurative language is not the exclusive property of the Western canon. In the oral traditions of West Africa, the griot (storyteller) uses proverbs and figurative comparisons to compress wisdom into memorable phrases. The Ashanti proverb "The lizard that jumped from the high iroko tree said he would praise himself if no one else did" uses a figurative scenario to convey a literal truth about self-reliance.

In Japanese literature, Sei Shonagon's *The Pillow Book* (c. 1000 CE) uses figurative lists and comparisons to capture the texture of court life. When she writes "Things that gain by being painted: autumn evenings," she is using figurative language to compare a visual medium to a temporal experience. In Arabic poetry, the pre-Islamic *qasida* tradition relies on elaborate figurative descriptions of desert landscapes, using metaphor and image to evoke an entire world of longing and loss.

Chinua Achebe's *Things Fall Apart* moves between Igbo proverbs (figurative language embedded in daily speech) and the literal, bureaucratic language of the colonial administration. The clash between these two registers is itself a theme: the figurative richness of Igbo discourse represents cultural vitality, while the flat literalism of the colonial office represents reduction and control. When Achebe writes "Proverbs are the palm-oil with which words are eaten," he is using a figurative statement about figurative language -- a meta-proverb that tells you everything about how the Igbo understand the power of words.

In Latin American literature, figurative language reaches extraordinary density. Gabriel Garcia Marquez's *One Hundred Years of Solitude* describes a character so beautiful she ascends to heaven while folding laundry -- a figurative event presented with literal matter-of-factness. This technique, called magical realism, collapses the boundary between literal and figurative, making the impossible feel as ordinary as the weather. Pablo Neruda's poetry likewise moves between literal description of Chilean landscapes and figurative evocations of love and political passion.

Indigenous literary traditions often make the literal-figurative distinction difficult to apply. In Leslie Marmon Silko's *Ceremony*, the landscape of the American Southwest is simultaneously a literal physical environment and a figurative space alive with ancestral stories. The rocks, rivers, and mountains are not symbols of stories; they *are* the stories. This way of understanding language and landscape challenges the Western assumption that the literal and figurative are separate categories.

The same challenge appears in the works of Kim Scott, whose novels about Noongar culture in Western Australia present a world in which country is not a figurative metaphor for belonging but the literal ground of identity. In these traditions, what Western criticism would call "figurative" is simply how things are. The Western literal-figurative distinction may itself be a cultural artifact rather than a universal feature of language.

In the Middle Eastern tradition, the poetry of Rumi (Jalal ad-Din Muhammad Rumi, 13th century) uses figurative language to approach experiences that literal language cannot reach. His famous line "The wound is the place where the Light enters you" is neither literally true (light does not enter physical wounds) nor merely decorative. It is a figurative statement that aims to transform the reader's understanding of suffering. The Sufi tradition from which Rumi writes treats figurative language not as a literary technique but as a spiritual necessity -- the only language adequate to the experience of the divine.

## Worked Example [Beginner]

Consider the opening line of Charles Dickens's *A Tale of Two Cities*:

> "It was the best of times, it was the worst of times, it was the age of wisdom, it was the age of foolishness, it was the epoch of belief, it was the epoch of incredulity, it was the season of Light, it was the season of Darkness, it was the spring of hope, it was the winter of despair, we had everything before us, we had nothing before us, we were all going direct to Heaven, we were all going direct the other way."

**Step 1: Identify literal elements.** The sentence is literally describing a historical period -- France and England in the years before and during the French Revolution. Those are real places and a real era. The reference to "Heaven" invokes a real religious concept.

**Step 2: Identify figurative elements.** "Best of times" and "worst of times" cannot both be literally true of the same moment for the same people. "Season of Light" and "season of Darkness" are figurative -- they do not refer to actual seasons or to literal light and dark. "Spring of hope" and "winter of despair" transfer the literal qualities of seasons to emotional states.

**Step 3: Analyze the gap.** Literally, the late eighteenth century had economic growth and political upheaval. Figuratively, Dickens is saying that extremes coexist -- that prosperity for some meant misery for others. The figurative language captures what a purely literal summary could not: the simultaneity of opposites. This technique of pairing contradictory figurations is called **antithesis**, and it is central to Dickens's method throughout the novel.

**Step 4: Determine effect.** By refusing to settle on a single literal description, Dickens forces the reader to hold two contradictory ideas at once. That tension mirrors the tension of the novel itself. The figurative language does not merely decorate the literal facts -- it creates a way of thinking that the reader must inhabit for the entire book.

**Step 5: Cross-cultural comparison.** Dickens's technique of using contradictory figurative pairs has parallels in other traditions. In the Taoist tradition of Chinese philosophy, Laozi writes "The Tao that can be told is not the eternal Tao" -- a paradox that uses figurative negation to point toward a truth that literal language cannot reach. In the poetry of Rumi, the thirteenth-century Persian mystic, figurative language is used to describe experiences that are by definition beyond literal description: "The wound is the place where the Light enters you." The wound is figurative, the light is figurative, and the combination points toward a spiritual truth that neither term could convey alone.

**Step 6: Consider oral traditions.** In many oral storytelling traditions, the distinction between literal and figurative is less sharply drawn than in Western literary criticism. When a Maori storyteller describes the formation of a landscape by the god Maui, the story is simultaneously a literal origin narrative, a figurative account of cultural identity, and a practical guide to reading the landscape.

The Western critic who asks "is this literal or figurative?" may be imposing a binary that the tradition itself does not recognize. Similarly, in the *Sundiata* epic of West Africa, the supernatural elements (sorcery, prophecy, transformation) are neither "literal" nor "figurative" in the Western sense -- they are part of a narrative framework in which the figurative and the literal coexist without contradiction.

## Formal Definition [Intermediate+]

**Literal language** is discourse in which the speaker's intended meaning corresponds directly to the conventional (denotative) meanings of the words used, governed by the semantic rules of the language. **Figurative language** is discourse in which the speaker's intended meaning departs from the conventional meanings of the words, requiring the listener or reader to construct an inferred meaning through processes of analogy, comparison, substitution, or contradiction.

The major categories of figurative language include:

- **Tropes** (figures that change or transform the meaning of a word or phrase): metaphor, metonymy, synecdoche, irony, hyperbole, litotes, periphrasis, personification.
- **Schemes** (figures that rearrange or pattern words without necessarily changing their meaning): repetition (anaphora, epistrophe), parallelism, chiasmus, inversion.

**Denotation** is the referential or "dictionary" meaning of a word -- the class of objects, actions, or qualities it picks out. **Connotation** is the network of secondary associations, emotional coloring, and cultural overtones attached to a word. The distinction was formally developed in semiotics by Roland Barthes, who identified a first-order system of denotation and a second-order system of connotation (myth).

Paul Grice's theory of conversational implicature provides a pragmatic account: figurative language often involves a deliberate "flouting" of the maxim of quality (truthfulness) or manner (clarity), prompting the listener to recover the intended meaning through inference. When a speaker says "War is peace," the literal claim is false (flouting the maxim of quality), and the listener must determine whether the speaker is being ironic, paradoxical, or satirical.

The challenge of recovering figurative meaning is compounded in cross-cultural communication. A figurative expression that is transparent in one culture may be opaque or misleading in another. When the Yoruba say "A child's fingers are not scalded by a piece of hot yam which he is given to eat," the figurative meaning (a gift does not harm the recipient) is immediately clear to a Yoruba speaker but may require explanation for a reader unfamiliar with West African food culture. The effort of interpretation is part of the point: figurative language rewards the reader who brings knowledge and attention to the text.

John Searle's speech act theory provides another framework. Searle argued that figurative utterances are a type of indirect speech act in which the speaker means something different from what is literally said. The key insight is that figurative language is not a violation of communicative norms but a systematic exploitation of them. Speakers use figurative language because it communicates more efficiently, more vividly, or more persuasively than literal language could.

### Subtypes and Related Distinctions

- **Dead metaphor**: A figurative expression so conventionalized that speakers no longer process it figuratively (e.g., "the leg of a table," "the mouth of a river"). Lakoff and Johnson argued that dead metaphors are not dead at all -- they continue to structure thought, even when speakers no longer notice them.
- **Idiom**: A fixed phrase whose meaning cannot be derived from the literal meanings of its constituent words (e.g., "kick the bucket" meaning to die). Idioms vary dramatically across languages and cultures.
- **Analogy**: A broader category of figurative comparison that includes metaphor and simile but also extends to argumentative structures.

- **Synecdoche**: A figure in which a part stands for the whole ("all hands on deck") or the whole for a part. In African oral traditions, synecdoche is a common feature of praise poetry.
- **Metonymy**: A figure in which something associated with an object stands for the object itself ("the crown" for the monarchy). Metonymy is particularly important in postcolonial literature.

### Cross-Cultural Dimensions

The literal-figurative distinction operates differently across languages and literary traditions. In Arabic, the concept of *majaz* (metaphorical or figurative language) has been debated by scholars since the classical period, with important implications for Quranic interpretation. The question of whether certain Quranic passages should be read literally or figuratively has been a central concern of Islamic theology.

In Chinese literary criticism, the concept of *bi* (comparison, analogous to simile) and *xing* (evocation, in which an image is presented that sets the emotional tone without explicit comparison) have been fundamental categories since the *Classic of Poetry* (Shijing), compiled around 1000 BCE. The *xing* has no exact Western equivalent -- it is a technique in which a literal image opens a poem and figuratively evokes an emotional state without asserting a direct comparison.

In Sanskrit poetics, the concept of *dhvani* (suggestion or resonance) describes meaning that is not literally stated but suggested by the words. Anandavardhana's *Dhvanyaloka* (ninth century CE) argued that the highest poetry is that in which the suggested meaning overshadows the literal meaning. This theory recognizes that figurative language operates not by replacing literal meaning but by adding a layer that transforms it.

### The Literal-Figurative Boundary in Different Registers

The boundary between literal and figurative shifts depending on the register of discourse. In scientific writing, the ideal is literal precision, yet even scientists rely on figurative models: the atom is described as a "solar system," electrical current "flows" like water, and natural selection "selects" as if it were a conscious agent. These are not merely pedagogical simplifications -- they are figurative frameworks that shape scientific reasoning.

In legal language, the distinction between literal and figurative interpretation of statutes has real consequences. When the United States Constitution refers to "cruel and unusual punishment," courts must decide whether to interpret these words literally (according to their meaning at the time of writing) or figuratively (according to evolving standards of decency). The choice between literal and figurative interpretation is itself a legal and political act.

In everyday conversation, speakers move between literal and figurative modes constantly and often unconsciously. When someone says "I'm dying of embarrassment," no one calls an ambulance. The figurative meaning is recovered instantly because speakers share conventions for processing exaggeration. These conventions vary across cultures: what counts as a normal figurative intensifier in one culture may be taken literally -- or missed entirely -- in another.

### Figurative Language and Power

The ability to use and interpret figurative language is a form of cultural capital. In many societies, mastery of figurative expression -- proverbs, metaphors, riddles -- is a marker of education, intelligence, and social status. The griots of West Africa are respected precisely because of their skill with figurative language. In Western academic culture, the ability to recognize literary allusions and interpret figurative language is a gateway to educational advancement.

This means that the literal-figurative distinction has political dimensions. Who decides what counts as "figurative"? Who is authorized to interpret figurative language? When a poet writes figuratively about political oppression, the figurative mode may provide cover -- the authorities cannot prosecute a metaphor -- but it may also make the message inaccessible to those who lack the interpretive tools to decode it. Ngugi wa Thiong'o's argument for writing in African languages is, in part, an argument for making figurative expression accessible to the people whose experience it represents.

The political dimension extends to the reception of figurative language. Studies in sociolinguistics have shown that figurative language is often evaluated differently depending on who uses it. A politician who uses vivid figurative language may be praised as "eloquent" or dismissed as "all rhetoric, no substance." The evaluation depends not on the figurative language itself but on the speaker's social position. This means that the literal-figurative distinction is never purely linguistic -- it is always entangled with questions of power, authority, and cultural capital.

## Advanced Results: Critical Theory and the Literal-Figurative Divide [Master]

**Formalist / New Critical approaches.** The New Critics treated figurative language as the defining feature of literary discourse. Cleanth Brooks argued that poetry achieves its meaning through "the language of paradox" -- the tensions, ambiguities, and ironic resolutions produced by figurative expression. I.A. Richards distinguished between the "tenor" and "vehicle" of a metaphor, treating figurative language as a cognitive act rather than mere ornament. The New Critical emphasis on close reading was ideally suited to identifying and interpreting figurative language, and the analysis of figurative patterns became the core method of practical criticism in the mid-twentieth century.

**Structuralist approaches.** Structuralists analyze figurative language as operations within the semiotic system of the text. Roman Jakobson identified metaphor and metonymy as the two fundamental poles of language -- the paradigmatic axis (selection by similarity) and the syntagmatic axis (combination by contiguity). Figurative language, in this view, reveals the deep binary structures that organize meaning. Jakobson further argued that certain forms of aphasia involve the loss of one pole or the other: some patients can only use metonymy, others only metaphor, revealing that these two figurative modes are neurologically as well as linguistically fundamental.

**Post-structuralist approaches.** Derrida's critique of metaphor destabilizes the literal/figurative binary itself. In "White Mythology," Derrida argues that all philosophical concepts are ultimately rooted in metaphors that philosophy has "forgotten" were figurative. The literal is not prior to the figurative; rather, the literal is figurative language that has been naturalized. This has radical implications: if all language is ultimately figurative, then the distinction between literal and figurative is a matter of degree and convention, not of kind. The deconstructive project involves tracing the figurative residues in supposedly literal discourse, revealing the instability of every conceptual foundation.

Paul de Man extended this analysis, arguing that figurative language is not a transparent medium but a mechanism that creates the illusion of meaning. In "The Rhetoric of Blindness," de Man demonstrated that even the most rigorous philosophical texts depend on figurative constructions that undermine their own arguments. The literal-figurative distinction, far from being a stable boundary, is a site of perpetual slippage.

**Marxist approaches.** Marxist critics examine how figurative language can either reveal or obscure material conditions. Georg Lukacs criticized modernism's reliance on metaphor and symbolism as a retreat from historical reality, while Bertolt Brecht's "alienation effect" used figurative distancing to make social relations visible. Raymond Williams analyzed how certain metaphors (e.g., "the market," "the invisible hand") naturalize economic ideologies, making historically specific arrangements of power appear as natural features of the world.

The Marxist tradition is particularly alert to how figurative language can serve what Williams called "structures of feeling" -- the implicit, often unconscious emotional and ideological frameworks that shape a society's experience of reality. Figurative language both expresses and conceals these structures, and the critic's task is to make them visible.

**Feminist approaches.** Feminist critics have analyzed how figurative language encodes gendered power. Judith Butler's theory of performativity draws on the figurative nature of all identity categories. Helene Cixous advocated for *ecriture feminine*, a mode of writing that resists the dominant (masculine) figurative conventions of Western literature. Metaphors of penetration, enclosure, and territory have been analyzed as encoding patriarchal assumptions about gender and agency.

The feminist analysis of figurative language extends to the history of literary criticism itself. Women's writing has historically been dismissed as "flowery" or "overly figurative" -- a judgment that reflects the gendered devaluation of the figurative in favor of the (supposedly masculine) literal. Recovering and revaluing the figurative strategies of women writers has been a central project of feminist literary criticism.

**Postcolonial approaches.** Postcolonial critics examine how the language of colonization -- and resistance to it -- operates figuratively. Frantz Fanon analyzed how the colonized subject's relationship to the colonizer's language is fundamentally metaphorical: speaking French in Martinique is not a neutral act but a figurative performance of identity that carries the weight of colonial history. Ngugi wa Thiong'o's decision to write in Gikuyu rather than English was a rejection of the colonizer's figurative universe and an assertion of indigenous modes of figurative expression.

Salman Rushdie's use of magical realism and hybrid figurative language has been read as a strategy for representing postcolonial experience that cannot be captured in the colonizer's literal discourse. The figurative mode allows Rushdie to represent the simultaneity of contradictory realities that characterizes postcolonial life -- the coexistence of tradition and modernity, myth and history, local and global.

**Reader-response approaches.** Reader-response critics emphasize that figurative meaning is not fixed in the text but constructed by the reader. Wolfgang Iser's concept of "gaps" (Leerstellen) applies directly to figurative language: the reader must actively fill in the meaning of a metaphor or symbol. Stanley Fish argued that interpretive communities share conventions for processing figurative language, meaning that what "counts" as figurative is itself a social phenomenon. The same passage may be read as richly figurative by one community and as straightforwardly literal by another.

This insight has particular force in cross-cultural reading. When a Western reader encounters an African proverb, the figurative meaning may be missed or misinterpreted because the reader lacks the cultural knowledge that the proverb presupposes. Conversely, an African reader may find Western literary figurative language opaque or strange. The reader-response approach reminds us that figurative language is not a self-evident feature of the text but an effect that depends on the reader's cultural equipment.

**Ecocritical approaches.** Ecocriticism examines how figurative language constructs the relationship between humans and the natural world. The Western tradition of personifying nature ("Mother Nature," "the angry storm") has been criticized for projecting human qualities onto a non-human world that has its own integrity. Indigenous literary traditions, by contrast, often present the natural world as literally alive with meaning rather than figuratively animated by human projection. The ecocritical approach asks whether figurative language about nature distances us from the natural world (by treating it as a symbol or metaphor) or connects us to it (by recognizing the kinship between human and non-human experience).

**Queer theory approaches.** Queer theorists have analyzed how figurative language can subvert normative categories. Eve Kosofsky Sedgwick's work on the epistemology of the closet examines how figurative language (coding, innuendo, double meaning) allows queer experience to be both expressed and concealed. The figurative mode is not merely a literary choice but a survival strategy in cultures that criminalize or stigmatize queer identity. Contemporary queer writers like Ocean Vuong use figurative language to explore the boundaries between literal and figurative identity -- between the body as it is and the body as it is imagined, desired, or feared.

**Cognitive approaches.** George Lakoff and Mark Johnson's *Metaphors We Live By* (1980) argued that figurative language is not a literary decoration but a fundamental structure of human thought. Their cognitive theory demonstrated that abstract reasoning is structured by systematic metaphorical mappings: we understand ARGUMENT as WAR, TIME as MONEY, LOVE as a JOURNEY. These are not occasional poetic figures but pervasive conceptual structures that shape how we think, reason, and act. This work dissolved the boundary between literary and ordinary language use, suggesting that the literal-figurative distinction is not a property of language itself but a product of how we attend to (or ignore) the figurative dimensions of everyday speech.

Lakoff and Johnson's work has been extended by research in cognitive science, neuroscience, and cross-cultural psychology. Studies have shown that figurative processing activates different brain regions than literal processing, suggesting that the distinction is not merely analytical but neurological. Cross-cultural research has revealed that while the capacity for figurative thought appears to be universal, the specific figurative mappings vary dramatically across cultures. The metaphor TIME IS MONEY, for instance, is natural in capitalist societies but far less intuitive in societies where time is understood cyclically rather than linearly.

### The Literal-Figurative Distinction in Digital Culture

The rise of digital communication has created new challenges for the literal-figurative distinction. In online discourse, the absence of tone of voice, facial expression, and social context makes it difficult to distinguish literal from figurative statements. Sarcasm, irony, and hyperbole are routinely misinterpreted as literal claims. The development of emojis, "/s" markers (indicating sarcasm), and other paralinguistic cues represents an attempt to restore the figurative-literal signaling that face-to-face conversation provides automatically.

The challenge of figurative language for artificial intelligence is particularly revealing. Large language models can produce figurative language but struggle to interpret it reliably. The statement "I'm so hungry I could eat a horse" is correctly identified as hyperbolic by most human readers, but an AI system might process it as a literal claim about appetite. This difficulty reveals something important about figurative language: understanding it requires not just linguistic knowledge but world knowledge, cultural knowledge, and the ability to infer a speaker's intentions -- capacities that remain at the frontier of AI research.

### Figurative Language and Translation

The translation of figurative language presents particular challenges. A literal translation of a figurative expression often produces nonsense; a figurative translation may preserve the meaning but lose the specific imagery. When Naguib Mahfouz's Arabic novels are translated into English, the figurative richness of the original -- its proverbs, its Qur'anic allusions, its specifically Arabic metaphors -- must be either preserved (at the risk of incomprehensibility) or adapted (at the risk of losing the cultural specificity that gives the figurative language its power).

The debate over translation strategies reflects deeper questions about the literal-figurative distinction. Is the "meaning" of a figurative expression its literal content, its figurative content, or some combination? When a translator replaces an Arabic proverb with an English proverb that carries a similar figurative meaning but completely different literal imagery, is the translation faithful or fraudulent? These questions have no easy answers, and they reveal that the literal-figurative distinction is not just a literary-critical tool but a fundamental problem in the philosophy of language.

## Historical and Philosophical Context [Master]

The distinction between literal and figurative language dates to classical rhetoric. Aristotle's *Poetics* and *Rhetoric* treated metaphor as the master figure of speech, arguing that the ability to perceive resemblances -- and to express them figuratively -- was a mark of genius. The Roman rhetorician Quintilian systematized the classification of tropes and schemes, creating a taxonomy that persisted for nearly two millennia.

But the history of this distinction is not exclusively Western. In ancient India, the Sanskrit grammarians and literary theorists developed sophisticated analyses of figurative language that in some respects anticipated modern Western theory by over a millennium. Bhartrihari (fifth century CE) argued that language and reality are inseparable -- that the structure of language shapes the structure of thought. This view, radical in its implications, anticipates by centuries the cognitive linguistics of Lakoff and Johnson. The Sanskrit theorists also developed the concept of *vakrokti* (oblique or crooked speech), which describes the way figurative language departs from straightforward literal expression to create aesthetic and intellectual pleasure.

In China, the *Classic of Poetry* (Shijing, compiled c. 1000 BCE) established the categories of *fu* (direct description), *bi* (comparison), and *xing* (evocation) that remain fundamental to Chinese literary criticism. The *xing* is particularly interesting because it occupies a space that Western criticism does not neatly recognize: it is neither purely literal nor purely figurative, but a mode in which a concrete image opens a space of emotional resonance without asserting a specific comparison. A *xing* might begin a poem with "The osprey calls on the islet in the river" without explicitly stating what the osprey signifies -- the reader is left to sense the connection rather than decode it.

Liu Xie's *The Literary Mind and the Carving of Dragons* (Wenxin Diaolong, fifth century CE), one of the most comprehensive works of Chinese literary criticism, devotes entire chapters to figurative language. Liu Xie analyzed the use of comparison (*bi*) and association (*xing*) with a precision that rivals the best Western rhetorical theory. He argued that figurative language is not merely ornamental but essential to the communication of complex truths, and that the highest literature achieves a seamless fusion of literal and figurative meaning.

In the Islamic world, the debate over literal and figurative interpretation of the Quran shaped centuries of theological and philosophical inquiry. The Mutazilites argued for allegorical interpretation of seemingly anthropomorphic passages (God's "hand," God's "face"), while the Asharites insisted on the primacy of literal interpretation, even when the literal meaning was paradoxical. This debate was not merely academic -- it had profound implications for political authority, since the power to interpret scripture was the power to govern. The Arabic literary tradition also produced Abd al-Qahir al-Jurjani's *Asrar al-Balagha* (Secrets of Eloquence, eleventh century), a work of figurative-language theory that analyzed the cognitive processes underlying metaphorical comprehension with a sophistication that Western criticism would not match until the twentieth century.

In the medieval West, figurative language was central to biblical hermeneutics. The fourfold scheme of scriptural interpretation (literal, allegorical, moral, anagogical) institutionalized the idea that texts carry meaning beyond their literal sense. Thomas Aquinas argued that scripture uses figurative language because it is fitting for spiritual truths to be conveyed through material images -- the figurative mode is not a deficiency but an adaptation to the limits of human understanding. Dante's *Letter to Can Grande* explicitly framed the *Divine Comedy* as operating on multiple levels of figurative meaning, and the poem itself is a masterwork of sustained figurative construction.

The Enlightenment rationalists grew suspicious of figurative language. In *An Essay Concerning Human Understanding* (1690), John Locke called figurative speech "perfect cheats" that manipulate passion rather than inform reason. Thomas Hobbes, in *Leviathan* (1651), argued that the abuse of language -- particularly figurative language -- was a source of political conflict. This suspicion of figurative language persisted in certain traditions of analytic philosophy, where the ideal of a clear, literal, logically precise language was seen as the proper goal of philosophical discourse.

The Romantic poets reversed this valuation, treating figurative language -- particularly metaphor and symbol -- as the highest form of cognition. Wordsworth argued that poetry is "the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings," and that figurative language was the natural expression of imaginative vision. Coleridge's distinction between fancy and imagination, and his concept of the "symbol" as a participation in the reality it represents, elevated figurative language to an almost metaphysical status. The Romantic valorization of figurative language was, in part, a reaction against the Enlightenment's narrowing of legitimate discourse to the literal and the rational. But it was also a recognition that figurative language does something that literal language cannot: it creates experiences, not just information.

In the Japanese literary tradition, the concept of *mono no aware* (the pathos of things) depends on a figurative mode in which the external world reflects and resonates with internal emotional states. This is not identical to the Western pathetic fallacy -- it is a more fundamental orientation toward the relationship between mind and world, one in which the literal and the figurative are not opposed but interpenetrate. The haiku tradition, from Basho to the present, achieves its effects by presenting literal images that vibrate with figurative resonance. Basho's "An old pond / A frog jumps in / The sound of water" describes a literal scene, but readers have found in it figurative meanings ranging from Zen enlightenment to the impermanence of beauty.

The Yoruba literary tradition of Nigeria offers yet another perspective on the literal-figurative distinction. Yoruba oral literature, including *oriki* (praise poetry), *ijala* (hunters' chants), and *ewi* (philosophical poetry), uses figurative language as a fundamental organizing principle. A Yoruba praise poem might describe a chief as "the lion whose roar shakes the forest" -- not as a decorative metaphor but as a statement that, within the figurative economy of the tradition, carries real political weight. The figurative description is not a "substitute" for a literal one; it is the primary mode of expression.

In the pre-Columbian Americas, the Maya *Popol Vuh* uses figurative language to describe the creation of the world and the adventures of the hero twins. The figurative mode is not an ornament added to a literal narrative -- it is the medium through which sacred truth is communicated. When the text describes gods creating humans from maize, the figurative image simultaneously describes a literal agricultural practice (maize cultivation), a figurative cosmological principle (humans as products of the earth), and a sacred truth (the divine origin of human life).

The twentieth century brought the most radical rethinking. Max Black's interaction theory of metaphor (1954) challenged the traditional substitution view, arguing that metaphor creates new meaning rather than replacing literal meaning with figurative decoration. George Lakoff and Mark Johnson's *Metaphors We Live By* (1980) demonstrated that metaphor pervades everyday thought, not just poetry. Their cognitive theory argued that abstract reasoning is structured by systematic metaphorical mappings (ARGUMENT IS WAR, TIME IS MONEY, LOVE IS A JOURNEY). This work dissolved the boundary between literary and ordinary language use.

In postcolonial literary theory, the literal-figurative distinction took on new political significance. Ngugi wa Thiong'o argued that the colonizer's language imposes a literal-figurative framework that distorts the colonized subject's experience. Writing in an African language is not merely a practical choice but an assertion of a different figurative universe -- a world in which the relationship between words and things is organized according to indigenous rather than colonial principles.

The twenty-first century has seen the emergence of new figurative forms in digital culture. Internet memes, for instance, function as a kind of distributed figurative language: an image, phrase, or template carries figurative meaning that is shared across a community of users. The meme is literally an image but figuratively a commentary on politics, identity, or culture. The rapid evolution of meme culture demonstrates that figurative language is not a static inheritance from the past but a living, evolving capacity that adapts to new media and new social conditions.
