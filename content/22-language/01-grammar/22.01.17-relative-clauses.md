A **relative clause** is a dependent clause that describes a noun. It gives you extra information about which person, thing, or place you are talking about.

Understanding relative clauses is important because they allow you to add detail and precision to your sentences. Without relative clauses, you would have to use many short, separate sentences to convey the same information. Relative clauses let you pack more information into each sentence, making your writing more efficient and more sophisticated.

There are two kinds:

1. **Defining (restrictive) relative clause:** Identifies which noun we mean. The sentence would be incomplete or unclear without it.
   - "The student **who scored the highest** won a scholarship."
   - Without the relative clause, "The student won a scholarship" is vague -- which student?

2. **Non-defining (non-restrictive) relative clause:** Adds extra information that is nice to know but not essential. The sentence makes sense without it.
   - "My sister**, who lives in Paris,** is visiting next week."
   - Without the relative clause, "My sister is visiting next week" is perfectly clear -- the Paris detail is bonus information.

Relative clauses are introduced by **relative pronouns**:

| Pronoun | Used for |
|---------|----------|
| who | people (subject) |
| whom | people (object) |
| whose | possession (people or things) |
| which | things |
| that | people or things (defining clauses only) |

### How to identify relative clauses

To identify a relative clause, look for a group of words that begins with a relative pronoun (who, whom, whose, which, that) or a relative adverb (where, when, why) and contains a verb. The clause will modify a noun that comes immediately before it. If you remove the relative clause, the sentence should still be grammatically complete (though it may lose some information).

### Why commas matter

The difference between defining and non-defining relative clauses is marked by commas in writing. If the clause is essential for identifying the noun, do not use commas. If the clause just adds extra information, use commas to set it off. Getting the commas wrong can change the meaning of the sentence. "The employees who complained were fired" means only the complainers were fired. "The employees, who complained, were fired" means all the employees were fired and all of them complained.
 This is one of the most important punctuation distinctions in English, and mastering it is essential for clear writing.

## Visual [Beginner]

```
DEFINING relative clause (no commas):

  The book   [that I read]   was excellent.
  -------    -------------   -------
  noun       relative         main clause
             clause           continues
             (identifies
              WHICH book)

NON-DEFINING relative clause (commas around it):

  The Eiffel Tower,  [which was built in 1889],  is iconic.
  ---------------    ------------------------    ---------
  noun               relative clause              main clause
                     (extra info, already          continues
                      clear WHICH tower)
```

```
When can you OMIT the relative pronoun?

  Object position in a DEFINING clause:
    "The book (that) I read was excellent."     -- OK to omit
    "The person (whom) I called was busy."      -- OK to omit

  Subject position -- NEVER omit:
    "The person who called me was busy."        -- "who" is required
    "The book that is on the table is mine."    -- "that" is required

  Non-defining clauses -- NEVER omit:
    "Paris, which I love, is beautiful."        -- "which" is required
```

## Worked example [Beginner]

Identify and analyze the relative clause in each sentence.

**1. "The car that she bought is red."**
- Relative clause: "that she bought"
- Type: defining (no commas -- we need this to know which car).
- Relative pronoun: "that" (refers to "the car," acts as object of "bought").
- The pronoun can be omitted: "The car she bought is red."

**2. "My father, who is a doctor, works long hours."**
- Relative clause: "who is a doctor"
- Type: non-defining (set off by commas -- we already know who "my father" is).
- Relative pronoun: "who" (refers to "my father," acts as subject).
- The pronoun CANNOT be omitted (subject position, and it is non-defining).

**3. "The author whose book I read is giving a talk."**
- Relative clause: "whose book I read"
- Type: defining (tells us which author).
- Relative pronoun: "whose" (indicates possession -- the book belongs to the author).


**4. "The city where I was born has changed a lot."**
- Relative clause: "where I was born"
- Type: defining (tells us which city).
- Relative adverb: "where" (refers to "the city," indicates place).

**5. "Shakespeare, who wrote Hamlet, was born in Stratford-upon-Avon."**
- Relative clause: "who wrote Hamlet"
- Type: non-defining (set off by commas -- Shakespeare is already identified by name).
- Relative pronoun: "who" (refers to "Shakespeare," acts as subject).

**6. "The reason why she left is still unknown."**
- Relative clause: "why she left"
- Type: defining (tells us which reason).
- Relative adverb: "why" (refers to "the reason").

### Common mistakes with relative clauses

Several common mistakes occur with relative clauses. First, using "that" in a non-defining clause: "Paris, that is beautiful, is my favourite city" should be "Paris, which is beautiful, is my favourite city." Second, omitting the relative pronoun in subject position: "The person called me was my brother" should be "The person who called me was my brother." Third, using incorrect pronoun case: "The person who I gave the book" should be "The person to whom I gave the book" or "The person who I gave the book to." Fourth, mismatching pronoun and antecedent: "The book who I read" should be "The book that I read" or "The book which I read."

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Prescriptive vs. descriptive perspectives

Prescriptive grammar teaches several rules about relative clauses that do not reflect how most English speakers actually use the language. The rule restricting "that" to defining clauses and "which" to non-defining clauses was invented by Fowler in 1906 and does not reflect the grammar of most English speakers, who freely use "which" in defining clauses. The rule restricting "whose" to animate antecedents is also prescriptive; most speakers use "whose" with inanimate antecedents without hesitation. Understanding the difference between prescriptive rules and actual usage helps students write in a way that is both grammatically correct and appropriate for their audience.

### World Englishes and relative clauses

Different varieties of English use relative clauses differently. In Indian English, speakers may use "which" where British English would use "who" for human antecedents. In Singapore Colloquial English, relative clauses may be formed with "that" in contexts where Standard English would use "who." In African American Vernacular English, relative clauses may omit the relative pronoun more freely than in Standard English. These differences reflect the influence of substrate languages and the independent development of English varieties.

### ESL transfer errors with relative clauses

ESL learners often struggle with relative clauses because their first language may form relative clauses differently. Speakers of languages that place the relative clause before the noun (like Japanese, Korean, and Turkish) may produce sentences like "The I-read book is good" instead of "The book that I read is good." Speakers of languages without relative pronouns may produce sentences without any relativizer: "The book I read is good" is correct in English, but "The person I called was busy" sounds incomplete without a relative pronoun to some ears. Speakers of languages with resumptive pronouns (where a pronoun inside the relative clause refers back to the head noun) may produce sentences like "The man who I saw him was tall."



A **relative clause** is a postnominal dependent clause that modifies a head noun. It contains a gap (or a resumptive pronoun) that is co-indexed with the head noun, which serves as its **antecedent**.

**Formal structure:**

```
[NP ... [N head noun] ... [CP [C rel pronoun] ... gap ... ]]
```

The relative pronoun has a dual function: it serves as the complementizer/relativizer introducing the clause, and it bears a grammatical role (subject, object, possessive, oblique) inside the relative clause.

**Defining vs. non-defining:**

| Property | Defining (restrictive) | Non-defining (non-restrictive) |
|----------|----------------------|-------------------------------|
| Function | Restricts the reference of the head noun | Adds supplementary information |
| Punctuation | No commas | Set off by commas |
| Relative pronouns | *who, whom, whose, which, that* | *who, whom, whose, which* (not *that*) |
| Pronoun omission | Possible in object position | Not possible |
| Entailment | "The students who passed celebrated" does not entail all students passed | "My brother, who lives in Tokyo, is visiting" entails my brother lives in Tokyo |

**Relative pronoun selection by grammatical role:**

| Role in relative clause | Pronoun | Example |
|------------------------|---------|---------|
| Subject | who / that / which | the person **who** called |
| Direct object | whom / who / that / zero | the person **(whom)** I saw |
| Indirect object | whom / who / that / zero | the person **(whom)** I gave the book |
| Oblique (object of preposition) | whom / who / which / that / zero | the person **to whom** I spoke |
| Possessive | whose | the person **whose** car was stolen |

## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

1. **That vs. which.** In defining clauses, both *that* and *which* can refer to things ("the book that/which I read"). Many style guides (particularly American) recommend *that* for defining clauses and *which* for non-defining clauses, but this is a prescriptive convention, not a grammatical rule. In non-defining clauses, *that* is genuinely ungrammatical.

2. **Pied-piping vs. preposition stranding.** When the relative pronoun is the object of a preposition, the preposition can appear before the pronoun (**pied-piping**) or at the end of the clause (**stranding**): "the person **to whom** I spoke" (formal, pied-piped) vs. "the person **(who)** I spoke **to**" (informal, stranded). Preposition stranding is characteristic of English and a few other Germanic languages; it is rare cross-linguistically.

3. **Relative clauses with whose.** *Whose* indicates possession and can modify both animate and inanimate head nouns: "the author whose book I read" (animate) and "the company whose profits increased" (inanimate). Some prescriptive guides restrict *whose* to animates, recommending *of which* for things, but this restriction is not observed in standard usage.

4. **Sentential relative clauses.** *Which* can also refer to an entire preceding clause rather than a single noun: "She arrived late, **which** annoyed everyone." Here *which* has a clause as its antecedent, not an NP. This construction is always non-defining.

5. **Stacked relative clauses.** Multiple relative clauses can modify the same head noun: "the book [that I read] [that changed my perspective]." Stacked relatives are always defining and are processed incrementally, which can create processing difficulty with three or more stacked clauses.

### Relative clauses in academic writing

Academic writing uses relative clauses extensively to pack information into sentences. Research papers typically contain sentences like: "The participants who completed the survey were divided into two groups based on the criteria that were established in the previous study, which investigated the effects of sleep deprivation on cognitive performance." This sentence contains three relative clauses stacked and embedded, conveying a large amount of information in a single sentence.

Learning to read and write these complex relative clause structures is essential for academic success. Students who cannot parse relative clauses will struggle with academic texts, and students who cannot construct them will struggle to express complex ideas.

### Relative clauses in legal language

Legal language uses relative clauses to specify conditions and exceptions precisely. "Any person who knowingly violates the provisions of this section, which are set forth in subsection (b), shall be subject to the penalties described in section 5." The relative clauses define who is subject to the penalties and identify which provisions are relevant. The precision of relative clauses makes them essential for legal drafting, though the resulting complexity can make legal texts difficult to read.

### Relative clauses in technical writing

Technical writing uses relative clauses to define terms and specify conditions. "The component that connects the power supply to the motherboard must be rated for at least 500 watts." The defining relative clause specifies which component is being discussed. In technical documentation, defining relative clauses are more common than non-defining ones because precision is more important than supplementary detail.


### Relative clauses in fiction

Fiction writers use relative clauses to create descriptive detail and to control pacing. A sentence with a relative clause slows the reader down and adds information: "The old house, which had been abandoned for decades, creaked in the wind." The non-defining relative clause adds atmosphere without interrupting the main narrative. Defining relative clauses can create urgency: "The man who had been following her suddenly appeared at the corner." Here the relative clause identifies the man, creating suspense.

### Relative clauses in news writing

News writing uses relative clauses to add background information efficiently. "The president, who took office in January, announced a new economic policy that would affect millions of Americans." This sentence contains two relative clauses: a non-defining clause adding background about the president and a defining clause specifying which policy. The use of relative clauses allows journalists to pack context into a single sentence, which is important in news writing where space is limited.

### Relative clauses in everyday speech

In everyday speech, relative clauses are often simpler and shorter than in writing. Speakers frequently use contact clauses (relative clauses without an overt relative pronoun): "The guy I met yesterday was really nice." They also use "that" more frequently than "who" or "which": "The thing that bothers me is the cost." The use of "whom" in spoken relative clauses is increasingly rare: "The person who I saw" is more common than "The person whom I saw" in speech.

### Relative clauses and sentence complexity

Relative clauses are one of the primary sources of sentence complexity in English. A single sentence can contain multiple relative clauses, each modifying a different noun: "The book that I bought yesterday, which was recommended by the professor who teaches my literature class, was excellent." This sentence contains three relative clauses, each embedded within the previous one. Understanding how to parse such sentences is essential for reading academic and professional texts.

### Teaching relative clauses

Relative clauses are typically taught in the context of sentence combining. Students are given two simple sentences and asked to combine them using a relative clause: "I met a woman. The woman speaks six languages." becomes "I met a woman who speaks six languages." This exercise teaches students to use relative clauses to create more sophisticated sentences. It also teaches them to identify the shared noun ("the woman") and to replace it with a relative pronoun ("who").

### Relative clauses and commas: a practical guide

The comma rule for relative clauses is one of the most commonly misunderstood punctuation rules in English. Here is a simple test: if you can remove the relative clause without changing the meaning of the sentence, use commas. If removing the relative clause changes the meaning (specifically, if it makes the sentence vague or unclear), do not use commas. "My brother, who lives in London, is visiting" -- you can remove "who lives in London" and still know who is visiting (your brother). "The student who scored highest won the prize" -- you cannot remove "who scored highest" without making it unclear which student won.

### Relative clauses and antecedent ambiguity

Sometimes a relative clause is ambiguous because it is unclear which noun it modifies. "I saw the man with the telescope that was broken" -- does "that was broken" modify "the telescope" or "the man"? Usually context and common sense resolve the ambiguity, but in careful writing, it is better to restructure the sentence to avoid ambiguity: "I saw the man who had the broken telescope" or "I saw the man with the telescope, which was broken."

### Relative clauses with quantifiers

Relative clauses can modify quantified noun phrases: "all the students who passed," "some of the books that were recommended," "none of the people who were invited." These constructions combine quantification with restriction, and the relative clause defines the subset that the quantifier applies to. "All the students who passed received certificates" means that every member of the set defined by "students who passed" received a certificate.

### Reduced relative clauses

English has reduced relative clauses, which are relative clauses with the relative pronoun and the auxiliary verb omitted. "The book written by Smith" is a reduced relative clause (full form: "the book that was written by Smith"). "The students studying in the library" is a reduced relative clause (full form: "the students who are studying in the library"). Reduced relative clauses are common in academic and formal writing, where they create a more concise style.




### Relative adverbs

In addition to relative pronouns, English has relative adverbs: **where** (place), **when** (time), and **why** (reason). These function similarly to relative pronouns but introduce adverbial information. "The house where I grew up" is equivalent to "The house in which I grew up." "The day when we met" is equivalent to "The day on which we met." "The reason why she left" is equivalent to "The reason for which she left." Relative adverbs are more common in informal English, while the preposition + relative pronoun constructions are more formal.

## Linguistic theory [Master]

The syntax of relative clauses has been one of the most extensively debated topics in generative grammar. Two main families of analysis exist:

**The raising analysis** (Vergnaud 1974; Kayne 1994; Bhatt 2002): The relative pronoun (or the head noun itself) originates inside the relative clause and moves to the left edge. Under the **matching analysis** (Chomsky 1965; Sauerland 1998), an internal head is matched with (and deleted in favor of) the external head. Under the **head-raising analysis** (Vergnaud 1974; Kayne 1994), the head noun literally moves from its base position inside the relative clause to the head position outside it.

Evidence for the raising analysis includes connectivity effects: binding and scope relations inside the relative clause behave as if the head noun were in its base position. "The picture of himself that John likes" allows *himself* to be bound by *John*, suggesting that "the picture of himself" was originally the object of *likes* and underwent raising.

**The adjunct analysis** (Chomsky 1977; Safir 1986): The relative clause is adjoined to the head noun (as an NP adjunct) or to a projection containing the head noun (as a CP adjunct). Under this view, the head noun is base-generated in its surface position and is not moved from inside the relative clause.

**de Vries (2002)** proposes a unified analysis in which all relative clauses involve a **determiner phrase** containing a relative CP, with the head noun generated either inside or outside the CP depending on construction type.

### The Accessibility Hierarchy in detail

**Cross-linguistic typology** (Keenan and Comrie 1977): Languages differ in which grammatical roles can be relativized. The **Accessibility Hierarchy** predicts that if a language can relativize role N, it can relativize all roles higher on the hierarchy:

Subject > Direct Object > Indirect Object > Oblique > Genitive > Object of Comparison

English can relativize all positions on the hierarchy. Many languages can only relativize subjects, or subjects and direct objects. Malagasy can only relativize subjects. Kinyarwanda can relativize subjects and objects. Basque can relativize subjects, objects, and indirect objects. The hierarchy captures a universal tendency for subjects to be the most easily relativized role and objects of comparison to be the most difficult.

### Island constraints and relative clauses

**Island constraints** apply to relative clauses. Extraction out of a relative clause is severely restricted: "Which book do you know the person who wrote?" is ungrammatical because the relative clause is a **complex NP island** (Ross 1967). This is one of the strongest island effects in English and has been used as evidence for the hierarchical structure of sentences.

### That-trace effects in relative clauses

In standard English, the relative pronoun *that* cannot be used when it is followed by a trace in subject position: "Who is the person that left?" is degraded compared to "Who is the person who left?" This parallels the that-trace effect in complement clauses and is attributed to the Empty Category Principle constraint, which requires certain empty categories to be properly governed.

### Relative clauses and processing

Relative clauses have been extensively studied in psycholinguistics. **Subject-relative clauses** ("The reporter who attacked the senator admitted the error") are generally easier to process than **object-relative clauses** ("The reporter who the senator attacked admitted the error"). This processing asymmetry has been attributed to several factors, including the distance between the head noun and the gap, the expectation of subject-first structure, and working memory constraints.

Centre-embedded relative clauses ("The rat that the cat that the dog chased killed ate the cheese") are notoriously difficult to process because they require the reader to hold multiple incomplete clauses in working memory. This processing difficulty provides evidence for the psychological reality of hierarchical sentence structure.

### Relative clauses in different languages

Languages form relative clauses in fundamentally different ways. English uses the prenominal strategy with a relative pronoun that follows the head noun. Japanese and Korean use the prenominal strategy, placing the relative clause before the head noun. Arabic and Hebrew use a mixed strategy with a relative pronoun that agrees with the head noun. Some languages, like Bambara, use a correlative strategy, placing the relative clause first and connecting it to the main clause with a demonstrative.

These cross-linguistic differences have implications for language teaching. ESL learners from different language backgrounds will have different difficulties with English relative clauses, depending on how relative clauses are formed in their first language.


### Relative clauses and information structure

The choice between a defining and non-defining relative clause affects information structure. A defining relative clause introduces new information that restricts the reference of the head noun. A non-defining relative clause adds background information about a noun that is already identified. This distinction is important for discourse coherence: defining relative clauses advance the discourse by narrowing reference, while non-defining relative clauses enrich the discourse by adding detail.

### The syntax of relative clauses in dependency grammar

In dependency grammar, a relative clause is analysed as a dependent of the head noun it modifies. The relative pronoun serves as the link between the head noun and the dependent clause. This analysis captures the modification relationship between the relative clause and the head noun without positing movement or empty categories. Dependency grammar provides an alternative to the movement-based analyses of generative grammar and is widely used in computational linguistics.
 Dependency grammar provides a simpler and more computationally tractable framework for representing relative clause structure than movement-based approaches.

### Relative clauses and corpus linguistics

Corpus studies of relative clauses have produced several important findings. In spoken English, "that" is the most common relativizer, followed by the zero relative. In written English, "which" and "who" are more common than "that." Non-defining relative clauses are more common in writing than in speech. These findings confirm that the prescriptive rule restricting "which" to non-defining clauses does not reflect actual usage, since "which" is commonly used in defining clauses in British English writing.

Corpus studies have also shown that the choice of relativizer is influenced by the grammatical role of the relative pronoun (subject vs. object), the animacy of the antecedent (human vs. non-human), and the formality of the register. In informal speech, "that" and the zero relative dominate. In formal writing, "who" and "which" are more common.

### Relative clauses and language change

The system of relative pronouns in English has been changing for centuries and continues to change. "Whom" is declining in all contexts and may eventually disappear from the language. "That" is becoming more common in formal writing. The zero relative is becoming more accepted in contexts where it was previously avoided. These changes reflect the broader tendency of English to simplify its grammatical system over time.

The decline of "whom" is particularly noteworthy. In the 18th and 19th centuries, "whom" was obligatory in object position. Today, "who" has largely replaced "whom" in speech and informal writing, and "whom" is used primarily in very formal contexts. This change is part of the broader loss of case marking in English, which has been progressing since Old English.
 The decline of case marking is one of the most significant grammatical changes in the history of English.


### The great vowel shift and relative pronouns

The Great Vowel Shift (roughly 1400-1700) changed the pronunciation of English relative pronouns. "Who" changed from a vowel quality similar to modern "hoe" to its modern pronunciation. "Which" changed its vowel from a short sound to the modern short-i pronunciation. These pronunciation changes coincided with the grammaticalization of these words as relative pronouns, and the new pronunciations helped distinguish the relative use from the interrogative use.

### Relative clauses in Bible translations

The history of Bible translations provides a window into the development of English relative clauses. The King James Bible (1611) uses relative clauses freely, with "which" used for both human and non-human antecedents and "that" used in both defining and non-defining clauses. Modern Bible translations follow more restrictive conventions, using "who" for humans, "which" for things, and "that" primarily in defining clauses. This shift reflects the adoption of prescriptive rules about relative pronoun usage in the centuries between the two translations.

### Relative clauses and the plain language movement

The plain language movement advocates for simpler, clearer writing in legal, government, and business documents. One of the movement's recommendations is to reduce the use of relative clauses, particularly stacked and embedded relatives, in favour of simpler sentence structures. Research has shown that documents with fewer relative clauses are understood more accurately and more quickly than documents with many relative clauses. However, the recommendation is not to eliminate relative clauses entirely but to use them judiciously, ensuring that each relative clause adds necessary information.

### The future of relative clauses in English

The system of relative pronouns in English is likely to continue simplifying. "Whom" will probably disappear entirely from everyday usage within a few generations. "Which" may continue to decline in defining clauses, with "that" becoming the dominant relativizer for defining clauses and "which" reserved for non-defining clauses. The zero relative is likely to become even more common in speech and informal writing. These changes reflect the ongoing evolution of English grammar toward simpler, more regular patterns.
 The pace of change is accelerating in the digital age, where informal writing styles are influencing formal conventions.


### Relative clauses in signed languages

Signed languages also have relative clause constructions, though they are formed differently from spoken language relative clauses. In American Sign Language (ASL), relative clauses are marked by specific non-manual signals (facial expressions and head movements) and may be placed before or after the head noun. The existence of relative clauses in signed languages is evidence that relative clause formation is a universal feature of human language, not specific to the spoken modality.

### Relative clauses and artificial languages

Constructed languages like Esperanto and Lojban have explicit rules for forming relative clauses. Esperanto uses the particle "kiu" as a relative pronoun, with case marking to indicate its grammatical role. Lojban uses a different system that avoids the ambiguities of natural language relative clauses. The design of relative clause systems in constructed languages reveals the challenges that relative clauses pose for precise communication.


### Free relatives and ever-words

Free relative clauses use "ever" words as relativizers: "whoever," "whatever," "wherever," "whenever." "Whoever finishes first wins" contains a free relative clause that functions as the subject of the sentence. Unlike ordinary relative clauses, free relatives do not have an explicit antecedent. The "ever" word simultaneously functions as the relativizer and as an indefinite pronoun inside the clause.

### Contact relative clauses

Some English dialects use contact relative clauses, which are relative clauses without any overt relative pronoun or complementizer. "The man I saw yesterday was my teacher" is a standard example of a contact relative clause. In some non-standard dialects, contact relatives can appear in subject position: "The man come yesterday was my teacher" (meaning "The man who came yesterday was my teacher"). These constructions are common in informal speech and in certain regional dialects.
 Contact relatives are also found in many English-based creoles, where they are a feature of the basilectal grammar.

### Connections to formal semantics

Relative clauses are central to the study of formal semantics because they involve intersection of sets. "The students who passed the exam" denotes the intersection of the set of students and the set of people who passed the exam. This logical analysis, developed by Montague and others, provides a precise way to understand the meaning of relative clauses and their interactions with quantifiers, negation, and other logical operators.

The distinction between defining and non-defining relative clauses corresponds to a semantic difference. Defining relative clauses intersect two sets. Non-defining relative clauses do not intersect; instead, they add a property to a set that is already identified. This semantic difference is reflected in the punctuation conventions that distinguish the two types.

### Connections to rhetoric

Relative clauses are a powerful rhetorical tool because they allow writers to add information without starting a new sentence. This keeps the discourse flowing and maintains the connection between the main assertion and its supporting details. Skilled writers use relative clauses to embed qualifications, evidence, and examples within the sentence that states the main claim, creating a tight weave of assertion and support.

However, overusing relative clauses can make writing dense and difficult to read. When a sentence contains multiple relative clauses, each one adds a layer of complexity that the reader must process. The key to effective rhetorical use of relative clauses is to use them sparingly and to ensure that each one adds information that is directly relevant to the main point of the sentence.

### Connections to literary analysis

Literary writers use relative clauses to create effects that range from the comic to the tragic. Jane Austen uses non-defining relative clauses to deliver ironic commentary: "It is a truth universally acknowledged, that a single man in possession of a good fortune, must be in want of a wife." The relative structure here sets up expectations that are then subverted. Ernest Hemingway avoids relative clauses to create a sense of immediacy and directness. The contrast between these two styles illustrates how relative clauses affect literary tone.

### Connections to psychology

The processing of relative clauses is one of the most studied topics in psycholinguistics. Research has shown that object-relative clauses are harder to process than subject-relative clauses, that centre-embedded relatives are harder than right-branching relatives, and that the processing difficulty increases with the distance between the head noun and the gap. These findings have implications for writing: writers who understand the processing difficulty of different relative clause structures can design sentences that are easier to read.

### Connections to language acquisition

Children acquire relative clauses in a predictable order that mirrors the Accessibility Hierarchy. Subject relatives are acquired first, followed by object relatives, followed by oblique relatives. Genitive relatives (with "whose") are acquired last. This acquisitional sequence reflects the increasing grammatical complexity of the different relative clause types. The order of acquisition is remarkably consistent across languages, suggesting that it reflects universal properties of the human language faculty.

### Connections to computer science

Relative clauses are a major challenge for natural language processing systems. Identifying the boundaries of a relative clause, determining which noun it modifies, and resolving the gap inside the clause all require sophisticated grammatical analysis. Modern NLP systems use dependency parsers and neural networks to handle relative clauses, but accuracy on complex cases remains below human performance.

### Connections to philosophy

The study of relative clauses connects to philosophy through the concept of reference. A defining relative clause helps the listener identify which entity is being referred to. A non-defining relative clause provides additional information about an entity that is already identified. This distinction between reference and description is central to the philosophy of language and has been discussed by philosophers from Frege to Kripke.


### Connections to mathematics

In mathematics, the notation used for set definitions is directly analogous to relative clauses. "The set of x such that x > 0" corresponds to "the things that are greater than zero." The mathematical notation is a formalised version of the natural language relative clause. This connection between relative clauses and mathematical notation is one of the ways in which natural language grammar underpins mathematical reasoning.


### Connections to music

Relative clauses appear in song lyrics, where they are used to tell stories and describe emotions. "The girl who I saw at the party" is a common pattern in popular music. The use of relative clauses in lyrics demonstrates their naturalness and communicative power: they allow the songwriter to add identifying information about a person or thing in a single phrase, creating a vivid image in the listener's mind.

### Connections to journalism

Journalists use relative clauses to add context and background to news stories. Because space is limited in news writing, relative clauses allow journalists to pack additional information into each sentence. However, too many relative clauses can make a news story difficult to read, so journalists are trained to use them judiciously and to prefer simpler constructions when possible.

### Non-finite relative clauses

Non-finite relative clauses use participle or infinitive forms instead of a finite verb. "The man standing by the door" (reduced from "the man who is standing by the door"). "The book to read" (reduced from "the book that you should read"). "The students selected for the team" (reduced from "the students who were selected for the team"). Non-finite relative clauses are more concise and are common in formal and academic writing.

### Relative clauses and pronoun resolution

When a sentence contains a relative clause, the reader must resolve the pronoun reference inside the clause. In "The teacher who praised the student was pleased," the relative pronoun "who" refers to "the teacher." In "The student who the teacher praised was pleased," the relative pronoun "who" refers to "the student." The ease of pronoun resolution depends on the structure of the relative clause and the position of the gap.
 Research in psycholinguistics has shown that subject-relative clauses are easier to process than object-relative clauses because the gap is closer to the relative pronoun and the sentence maintains a canonical word order.

### Relative clauses in email and digital communication

In digital communication, relative clauses are used more simply than in formal writing. Email and text messages tend to use short relative clauses with "that" as the relativizer: "the person that I told you about." Complex relative clauses with "whom" and pied-piped prepositions are rare in digital communication, reflecting the informal register of these media.



### Connections to education

Teaching relative clauses is a standard part of writing instruction at every level. In elementary school, students learn to identify relative clauses and the relative pronouns that introduce them. In middle school and high school, students learn the distinction between defining and non-defining clauses and the punctuation rules that distinguish them. In college, students learn to use relative clauses effectively in academic writing and to analyse the relative clause structures in the texts they read.

Research in second language acquisition has shown that relative clauses are one of the last grammatical structures to be fully acquired, particularly object relative clauses and genitive relative clauses. This finding has implications for language teaching: instructors should not expect students to master all relative clause types immediately, but should introduce them gradually in the order predicted by the Accessibility Hierarchy.

### Relative clauses in advertising

Advertising uses relative clauses strategically to make claims that are technically true but potentially misleading. "The only product that is recommended by four out of five dentists" uses a defining relative clause to suggest exclusivity. "Our new formula, which has been clinically tested, delivers results" uses a non-defining relative clause to add authority without specifying the details of the testing. Understanding how relative clauses are used in advertising is a form of media literacy.

### Relative clauses in scientific writing

Scientific writing uses relative clauses extensively to specify conditions, define terms, and report results. "The compound that was synthesised using the new method showed a 30% increase in efficiency" uses a defining relative clause to specify which compound. "The results, which are consistent with previous findings, suggest that the hypothesis is correct" uses a non-defining relative clause to add supporting evidence. The precise use of relative clauses is essential for scientific communication.

### Sentence combining with relative clauses

Sentence combining is a particularly effective technique for teaching relative clauses. Students are given pairs of sentences and asked to combine them: "The scientist won the Nobel Prize. She discovered a new element." becomes "The scientist who discovered a new element won the Nobel Prize." This exercise teaches students to identify shared referents, choose appropriate relative pronouns, and construct grammatically correct relative clauses. Research has shown that sentence combining exercises improve both the accuracy and the complexity of students' writing.


## Historical context [Master]

Old English had a rich system of relative clauses using inflected relative pronouns (*se, seo, that*) that agreed with the antecedent in gender, number, and case. The uninflected particle *the* (ancestor of *that*) was also used as a relativizer.

### Old English relative clauses

In Old English, relative clauses were introduced by inflected demonstrative pronouns that agreed with their antecedents in gender, number, and case. The demonstrative "se" (masculine), "seo" (feminine), and "that" (neuter) served as relative pronouns, with different forms for different cases. This system was more complex than Modern English but also more precise, because the case marking on the relative pronoun indicated its grammatical role inside the relative clause.

During Middle English, the loss of case inflection on the demonstrative/relative pronouns led to ambiguity. *That* became the default relativizer, and *which* (originally an interrogative pronoun) was recruited as a relative pronoun for inanimates. *Who/whom* (also originally interrogative) was pressed into service as a relative pronoun for humans during the 15th-16th centuries, partly under the influence of Latin relative clauses.

### The influence of Latin

Latin had a sophisticated system of relative clauses with fully inflected relative pronouns. As English writers sought to emulate Latin style, they adopted more complex relative clause structures. The recruitment of "who" and "which" as relative pronouns was partly influenced by the Latin distinction between "qui" (who) and "quod" (which). The result was a richer system of relative pronouns than Old English had, but one that was still simpler than the Latin system.

### The development of the zero relative

The omission of the relative pronoun (the "zero relative") is a distinctly English phenomenon, rare in other Germanic languages. It became common in Middle English and is now fully standard in defining clauses when the pronoun is in object position. The zero relative developed because the relative pronoun in object position is grammatically redundant -- the gap inside the clause marks the position of the relativized element without an overt pronoun.

### The Fowler prescriptive rule

The prescriptive rule restricting *that* to defining clauses and *which* to non-defining clauses has no basis in the history of English or in the grammar of most speakers. It was promoted by H.W. Fowler in *The King's English* (1906) and *A Dictionary of Modern English Usage* (1926) and was taken up by American style guides. British English continues to use *which* freely in defining clauses. The rule is a stylistic preference, not a grammatical law, though following it can improve clarity by making the defining/non-defining distinction explicit.

### Relative clauses in Early Modern English

In Early Modern English, relative clauses were used more freely than in Modern English. Shakespeare used "which" for human antecedents, "who" for inanimate antecedents, and "that" in both defining and non-defining clauses. The rigid prescriptive rules that govern relative pronoun usage today did not exist in Shakespeare's time. This freedom allowed for more variety in expression, though it also created more ambiguity.

### Relative clauses in the 18th and 19th centuries

The 18th century saw the beginning of prescriptive rules about relative pronouns. Robert Lowth's *A Short Introduction to English Grammar* (1762) established the rule that "who" should be used for people and "which" for things. This rule was not based on the actual usage of good writers but on Lowth's belief that English grammar should follow logical principles. Despite its artificial origin, the rule became widely accepted and is still taught today.

The 19th century saw the consolidation of prescriptive rules about relative clauses. Lindley Murray's *English Grammar* (1795) reinforced Lowth's rules and added new ones, including the recommendation that "whom" should be used for object position. The result was a set of rules that was more rigid than the actual usage of most speakers, a situation that persists today.

### Connections to philosophy of language

The study of relative clauses connects to philosophy through the concept of definite descriptions. Bertrand Russell's theory of descriptions (1905) analyses "the F that Gs" as asserting that there exists exactly one thing that is both F and G. This logical analysis captures the meaning of defining relative clauses and has been influential in both philosophy and linguistics.

### Connections to formal logic

In formal logic, relative clauses correspond to restricted quantification. "All students who passed the exam received a certificate" corresponds to the logical formula: for all x, if x is a student and x passed the exam, then x received a certificate. The relative clause "who passed the exam" restricts the domain of the quantifier "all students." This correspondence between relative clauses and restricted quantification is used in formal semantics and computational linguistics.

### The evolution of relative clauses in written English

The frequency of relative clauses in written English has increased over the centuries. Old English prose used fewer relative clauses than Modern English prose. Middle English saw an increase, partly due to the influence of Latin and French. Early Modern English saw a further increase, with writers like Milton and Browne using elaborate relative clause structures. Contemporary English uses relative clauses frequently but with less elaboration than the prose of earlier centuries.

### Relative clauses and grammaticalization

The history of English relative pronouns is a story of grammaticalization -- the process by which words with concrete meanings become grammatical markers. "Who" and "which" were originally interrogative pronouns with concrete meanings ("who" meant "what person," "which" meant "what thing"). Over centuries, they lost their interrogative meaning and became grammatical markers that introduce relative clauses. "That" was originally a demonstrative pronoun meaning "that one" and similarly became a relativizer.
