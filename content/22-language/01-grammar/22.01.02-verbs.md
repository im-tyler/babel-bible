## Intuition [Beginner]

A verb is a word that describes an action or a state. It tells you what is happening. "The dog **runs**." "The sky **is** blue." "She **thought** about lunch." Every sentence needs at least one verb -- without one, nothing is happening.

Verbs are the engine of a sentence. Nouns name the players; verbs say what the players do. "The bird **sang**." The bird is the noun. "Sang" is the verb. It is the difference between a still photograph and a movie.

Verbs do more than describe physical actions. They also describe mental activities ("she **wondered** about the answer"), states of being ("the water **is** cold"), possession ("he **has** a car"), and experiences ("they **felt** the earthquake"). Any word that tells you what a subject does, is, or experiences is probably a verb.

Many verbs change form to show **when** something happens (tense). "I **walk**" (present -- happening now or generally). "I **walked**" (past -- already happened). "I **will walk**" (future -- will happen). These tense changes are one of the most important features of English verbs.

Some verbs work alone: "She **slept**." Others need **helping verbs** (also called auxiliary verbs) to complete their meaning: "She **has slept**." "She **is sleeping**." "She **can sleep**." The helping verbs (*has, is, can, will, do, should, might, could*) add information about tense, aspect, and possibility to the main verb.

Verbs also change to match their subject in a limited way. "I walk" but "she **walks**." "I am" but "she **is**." This matching is called **agreement**, and in modern English it is fairly simple -- mostly just the *-s* ending on third-person singular present tense verbs. Older forms of English had much richer agreement (Old English verbs agreed with their subjects in person and number across all tenses), and many other languages still do.

A special kind of verb is the **linking verb** (also called a copular verb). Linking verbs do not express actions. Instead, they connect the subject to a description. The most common linking verb is *be*: "The sky **is** blue." But others exist: "The soup **smells** delicious," "She **became** a doctor," "The music **sounds** loud," "He **grew** impatient." In each case, the verb links the subject to a quality or identity rather than describing an action the subject performs.

English also has many **phrasal verbs** -- combinations of a verb and a small word (particle) that together create a new meaning. "Give up" means to stop trying. "Look forward to" means to anticipate eagerly. "Put off" means to postpone. "Figure out" means to solve or understand. There are thousands of phrasal verbs in English, and they are one of the hardest parts of the language for learners because their meanings cannot be guessed from the individual words.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Find the verbs in each sentence.

**Sentence 1:** "The cat sat on the mat and stared at the window."

- **sat** -- what the cat did (action, past tense)
- **stared** -- what the cat did (action, past tense)

"On," "at," and "the" are not verbs. "Cat," "mat," and "window" are nouns. The verbs tell you what happened: the cat sat and then stared.

**Sentence 2:** "The students have been studying all night for the exam."

- **have** -- helping verb (auxiliary)
- **been** -- helping verb (auxiliary)
- **studying** -- main verb (present participle)

Three verbs working together: *have + been + studying* = present perfect progressive tense, describing an action that started in the past and is still ongoing.

**Sentence 3:** "Langston Hughes wrote about dreams that never came true."

- **wrote** -- main verb (past tense of "write," irregular)
- **came** -- verb inside the relative clause (past tense of "come," irregular)

"Wrote" and "came" are both irregular past-tense verbs. They do not follow the regular *-ed* pattern. English has about 200-250 irregular verbs, and they are among the most commonly used words in the language.

What all three examples show: verbs answer the question "what happened?" or "what does it do?" or "what is it?" in a sentence.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **verb** is a word that expresses an action, occurrence, or state of being and that can function as the head of a verb phrase. In English, verbs are the word class that:

1. Inflects for **tense**: *walk/walked* (present/past)
2. Shows **agreement** with the subject in person and number (limited in English): *I walk / she walks*
3. Can be marked for **aspect** and **mood**: *is walking* (progressive), *walked* (perfect), *walk!* (imperative)
4. Occupies the **predicator** position in clause structure

### Main verbs and auxiliary verbs

**Main verbs** carry the lexical meaning: *run, think, build, know, wonder, create*.

**Auxiliary verbs** (or "helping verbs") support the main verb by adding tense, aspect, mood, or voice:

- **Primary auxiliaries**: *be, have, do* -- "She **is** running," "They **have** finished," "I **do** understand"
- **Modal auxiliaries**: *can, could, will, would, shall, should, may, might, must* -- "You **must** leave"

A verb phrase can chain auxiliaries: "She **will have been** working." The main verb is *work*; the auxiliaries carry tense, aspect, and mood. English allows up to four auxiliaries in a single verb phrase (*will have been being examined*), though such constructions are rare outside passive-voice academic prose.

### Transitive and intransitive verbs

**Transitive verbs** require a direct object: "She **threw** the ball." Without the object ("She threw"), the sentence feels incomplete.

**Intransitive verbs** do not take a direct object: "He **arrived**." You cannot "arrive something."

Some verbs are **ditransitive**, taking two objects: "She **gave** him the book" -- indirect object (*him*) and direct object (*the book*).

Many verbs shift between transitive and intransitive use: "I **eat**" (intransitive) vs. "I **eat** rice" (transitive). This is called the **ergative alternation** in linguistics. "The window broke" (intransitive -- the window broke on its own) vs. "I broke the window" (transitive -- someone caused the breaking). The same verb, different argument structure.

### Verbs across languages

The English verb system is relatively simple morphologically compared to languages like Spanish, Arabic, or Japanese. Spanish verbs inflect for six person/number combinations in each tense (yo hablo, tu hablas, el habla, nosotros hablamos, vosotros hablais, ellos hablan). Arabic verbs inflect for person, number, gender, tense, mood, and voice simultaneously. Japanese verbs do not inflect for person or number at all but have elaborate politeness marking.

For ESL learners, the English verb system presents challenges in tense-aspect combinations (the difference between "I have lived" and "I lived"), phrasal verbs ("give up," "look forward to," "put off"), and irregular past forms. Learners from Mandarin Chinese, which has no morphological tense marking, often struggle with the obligatory tense marking in English. Learners from Spanish or Arabic, which have richer agreement systems, may overgeneralise English agreement rules.

The concept of **aspect** is particularly challenging for speakers of languages that do not grammaticalise it. Mandarin Chinese marks aspect through particles (*le, zhe, guo*) rather than through verb morphology. Russian has a thoroughgoing perfective/imperfective distinction where every verb comes in pairs. English speakers learning Russian must choose between perfective and imperfective for every verb they use, while Russian speakers learning English must learn the more nuanced English aspectual system (simple, progressive, perfect, perfect progressive) that uses auxiliaries rather than lexical pairs.

The English **do**-support system is unusual cross-linguistically. English uses *do* as a "dummy" auxiliary in questions ("Do you like it?"), negation ("I don't like it"), and emphasis ("I *do* like it"). Most languages form questions through word order inversion alone and negation through a simple negative particle. The *do*-support system emerged in Early Modern English (15th-16th centuries) and is one of the distinctive features of English grammar.

### The passive voice and verbs

Verbs participate in the **voice** system, which indicates the relationship between the verb's arguments and grammatical roles. English has active voice ("the dog chased the cat") and passive voice ("the cat was chased by the dog"). The passive is formed with *be* + past participle. Not all verbs can form passives -- only transitive verbs (those with objects) can promote their object to subject position. "She slept" has no passive ("was slept" is ungrammatical), but "she built the house" passivises to "the house was built."

Passive voice serves important discourse functions. It allows the speaker to focus on the patient rather than the agent ("the window was broken" -- we care about the window, not who broke it), to omit the agent when it is unknown or irrelevant ("the money was stolen"), or to deliberately obscure responsibility ("mistakes were made"). The choice between active and passive is not just grammatical but rhetorical.

### Linking (copular) verbs

Not all verbs express actions. **Linking verbs** (also called copular verbs) connect the subject to a description or identification: *be, seem, become, appear, feel, look, sound, taste, smell, grow, remain, stay, turn, prove*. In "the soup tastes salty," "tastes" links "the soup" to the adjective "salty" -- it does not describe an action but rather a state. Many linking verbs can also function as action verbs: "she looked at the painting" (action) vs. "she looked tired" (linking).

### Counter-examples to common slips

- **Not every *-ing* word is a verb.** "The running **water**" -- "running" modifies "water" (a participle functioning as an adjective). "I enjoy **running**" -- "running" is a gerund (a verb form functioning as a noun).
- **Not every past-tense *-ed* word is a verb.** "A **learned** scholar" -- "learned" here is an adjective derived from the verb but functioning to describe the noun.
- **Imperatives have an implied subject.** "Sit down" means "You sit down." The verb is "sit"; the subject "you" is understood.

## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

### Verb tense

English has two morphological tenses (marked on the verb itself):

- **Present**: *walk/walks*
- **Past**: *walked*

Other time references use auxiliaries:
- **Future**: *will walk*
- **Present perfect**: *has walked*
- **Past perfect**: *had walked*
- **Future perfect**: *will have walked*

The relationship between tense (grammatical marking) and time (actual when) is not one-to-one. "I am leaving tomorrow" uses present tense to describe future time. "If I knew, I would tell you" uses past tense to describe a hypothetical present. Tense is grammar; time is meaning. This distinction is crucial for understanding how English works and for avoiding the common oversimplification that "past tense means past time."

### Verb aspect

**Aspect** describes how an action unfolds over time, distinct from when it occurs:

- **Simple**: *I walked* -- the action as a whole
- **Progressive** (continuous): *I was walking* -- the action in progress
- **Perfect**: *I had walked* -- the action completed relative to a reference point
- **Perfect progressive**: *I had been walking* -- in-progress action viewed from a completion point

### Finite and non-finite verbs

**Finite verbs** carry tense and can serve as the main verb of a clause: *walks, walked, is*.

**Non-finite verbs** do not carry tense and cannot serve as the main verb alone:
- **Infinitive**: *(to) walk*
- **Gerund**: *walking* (functions as a noun)
- **Participle**: *walking / walked* (functions as an adjective or in auxiliary constructions)

### Phrasal verbs

English has thousands of **phrasal verbs** -- combinations of a verb with a particle (preposition or adverb) that create a meaning different from the original verb: *give up* (surrender), *look after* (care for), *put off* (postpone), *figure out* (solve), *run into* (meet unexpectedly).

Phrasal verbs are a major challenge for ESL learners because their meanings are often idiomatic -- you cannot deduce the meaning of "give up" from "give" + "up." Many phrasal verbs have Latinate single-word equivalents (*give up* = *surrender*, *look after* = *supervise*, *put off* = *postpone*), and the Latinate version typically belongs to a more formal register.

### Moods

English verbs express three **moods**:

- **Indicative** (statements and questions): "She walks."
- **Imperative** (commands): "Walk!"
- **Subjunctive** (hypotheticals, wishes, demands): "If I **were** you..." / "I demand that she **leave** immediately."

The subjunctive is fading in informal English ("if I was you" is common in speech) but remains important in formal writing and in constructions like "it is essential that she **be** informed."

### Code-switching and verb systems

Bilingual speakers who regularly use two or more languages engage in **code-switching** -- alternating between languages within a conversation or even within a sentence. Research on Spanish-English bilinguals in the United States shows that code-switching at verb boundaries is particularly common, because the verb carries the syntactic frame of the clause. A speaker might say "I was *caminando* home" (mixing English and Spanish), with the Spanish gerund *caminando* ("walking") embedded in an English syntactic frame.

Code-switching is not a sign of confused or deficient language ability. It is a sophisticated communicative strategy that requires fluency in both languages and an implicit understanding of where language boundaries can be crossed. Linguists like Poplack (1980) have shown that code-switching follows systematic grammatical constraints -- speakers do not switch at random points but at specific syntactic boundaries.

### Verbs in World Englishes

Verb usage varies across World Englishes in systematic ways. Indian English commonly uses the progressive with stative verbs ("I am knowing him for ten years"), reflecting the influence of Hindi and other Indian languages where the progressive is more widely used. Nigerian English often uses the simple present where British English would use the present perfect ("I already eat" instead of "I have already eaten"). Singapore English (Singlish) has a distinctive aspectual particle system: "eat already" (completive), "eating" (progressive), "eat also eat" (concessive).

These features are not errors. They are grammatical patterns that have developed through language contact and are rule-governed within each variety. Descriptive linguists treat them as legitimate features of the respective World English, though prescriptive educators may correct them in formal writing contexts.

### Verbs in literature

Authors make deliberate verb choices that shape tone, pacing, and meaning. Compare two passages describing the same event:

"He walked to the door. He opened it. He looked outside." (Simple past, short verbs, staccato rhythm -- Hemingwayesque)

"He had been making his way toward the door, had been reaching for the handle when suddenly it burst open, sending him stumbling backward." (Past perfect progressive, complex verb phrases, extended duration -- Dickensian)

The choice between simple and complex verb forms, between short and long verb phrases, between active and passive, is not just grammatical -- it is a stylistic and rhetorical decision. Writers like Hemingway favoured simple verbs in simple tenses for directness. Writers like Faulkner layered auxiliaries and subordinate clauses for density and complexity. The verb system provides a wide range of expressive possibilities, and learning to use them is central to learning to write well.

### Verb choice and power

Verbs are the most powerful part of speech for shaping how readers interpret events. Consider the difference between "the soldier **eliminated** the target," "the soldier **killed** the enemy," and "the soldier **murdered** the civilian." Each verb describes the same basic event (one person ending another's life), but each carries vastly different connotations of legitimacy, moral judgment, and emotional tone. *Eliminated* is clinical and detached, suggesting a military operation. *Killed* is neutral. *Murdered* is condemnatory, implying illegitimacy and moral culpability.

In journalism, the choice between "died" and "was killed," between "said" and "claimed," between "announced" and "admitted" is never neutral. Editors make verb choices that frame events, and readers who understand the verb system can recognise these framing choices and evaluate them critically.

In advertising, verbs are chosen to provoke action: "buy now," "discover the difference," "experience luxury," "transform your life." The imperative mood and dynamic verb choices are deliberate rhetorical strategies. Understanding how verbs work is therefore not just a grammar exercise -- it is a tool for critical reading and informed citizenship.

### Verbs in poetry

William Carlos Williams' "so much depends / upon / a red wheel / barrow" uses no finite verb at all -- the absence of verbs creates a still, frozen quality. When verbs do appear in short poems, they carry enormous weight. Gwendolyn Brooks' "We real cool. We / Left school. We / Lurk late. We / Strike straight" uses present-tense verbs without *-s* marking, creating a collective voice that defies standard agreement rules.

### Verb frequency and corpus data

Corpus linguistics shows that a small number of verbs account for a disproportionate share of verb tokens in English. The top ten most frequent verbs in most corpora are: *be, have, do, say, go, get, make, know, think, take*. These high-frequency verbs are mostly irregular, mostly Germanic in origin, and mostly polysemous -- each has multiple meanings. *Get* alone has over a dozen senses (obtain, become, understand, cause to do, arrive, etc.) and participates in dozens of phrasal verbs (*get up, get over, get by, get away with, get along*).

The most frequent verbs are also the most grammaticalised -- they serve auxiliary functions in addition to their lexical meanings. *Have* is both a main verb ("I have a car") and an auxiliary ("I have eaten"). *Be* is both a main verb ("She is happy") and an auxiliary ("She is running"). *Do* is both a main verb ("I did the work") and an auxiliary ("Do you understand?"). This dual function is called **grammaticalisation** -- the process by which content words gradually become function words over time.

### Verb-first languages

While English typically uses Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) order, about 9% of the world's languages are Verb-Subject-Object (VSO), placing the verb first. Welsh, Arabic, Hawaiian, and Tagalog are all VSO languages. In Welsh, "Darllenodd y dyn y llyfr" means "Read the man the book" (the man read the book). In Hawaiian, "Hele au i ke kula" means "Go I to school" (I go to school).

These word-order differences affect how speakers process sentences. In SVO languages like English, the subject is established first, then the action. In VSO languages, the action is presented first, and the participants are identified afterward. Some linguists have argued that this reflects a deeper cognitive preference for event-first vs. participant-first processing, though this remains debated.

### Verbs and artificial intelligence

The problem of verb interpretation is central to natural language processing (NLP) and artificial intelligence. When an AI system encounters the sentence "The dog ate the bone in the kitchen," it must determine: did the eating happen in the kitchen, or was the bone located in the kitchen? This **attachment ambiguity** depends on how the verb phrase is structured, and resolving it requires world knowledge about typical events.

Similarly, verb **frame identification** -- determining how many arguments a verb takes and what roles they fill -- is a major NLP task. "Give" takes three arguments (giver, gift, receiver), "put" takes three (putter, thing put, location), "sleep" takes one (sleeper). Correctly identifying these frames is essential for machine translation, question answering, and text understanding. The PropBank and VerbNet projects in computational linguistics catalogue the argument structures of thousands of English verbs for this purpose.

Verbs also pose challenges for machine translation. English phrasal verbs (*give up, take off, put down*) often have single-word equivalents in other languages, and translating them requires understanding the idiomatic meaning rather than the literal components. The verb *give up* has nothing to do with giving or moving upward; it means "to surrender or quit." A machine translation system that renders *give up* literally into French (*donner en haut*) would produce nonsense. The correct French equivalent is *abandonner*. This gap between literal composition and actual meaning is one of the hardest problems in computational linguistics.

Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have improved verb interpretation considerably. Models trained on billions of words of text can often infer the correct meaning of ambiguous verbs from context, resolving attachment ambiguities and identifying idiomatic phrasal verb senses with high accuracy. However, these models still struggle with rare verb senses, novel metaphors, and verbs used in domain-specific technical contexts where the ordinary meaning does not apply. The verb *compile* means something different in computing than in everyday English, and *run* has specialised meanings in baseball, finance, medicine, and theatre. Disambiguating these senses remains an active and important area of research.

## Linguistic theory [Master]

### Vendler's aspectual classes

Vendler (1957) classified verbs into four aspectual categories based on their temporal properties:

| Class | Duration | Completion | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **States** | ongoing | no endpoint | *know, believe, love* |
| **Activities** | ongoing | no natural endpoint | *run, push, swim* |
| **Achievements** | instantaneous | yes | *notice, reach, break* |
| **Accomplishments** | durative | natural endpoint | *build a house, write a letter* |

This classification is not arbitrary. Each class behaves differently with temporal modifiers. You can say "she ran for an hour" (activity + duration) but not "she noticed the sign for an hour" (achievement + duration is odd without shifting the meaning). These distributional facts are evidence that aspectual class is a real grammatical property, not just a semantic intuition.

The Vendler classification has consequences for language pedagogy. Stative verbs resist the progressive (*"I am knowing"), which is why ESL learners who overgeneralise the progressive produce errors. Achievement verbs are odd with "slowly" (*"she slowly noticed"). Activities combine naturally with "stop" but accomplishments with "finish." These collocational patterns follow from the semantic properties of each verb class.

### Thematic roles and argument structure

Every verb defines a set of **thematic roles** (also called theta-roles or participant roles) that its arguments must fill. "Give" requires three: an agent (the giver), a theme (the thing given), and a recipient (the receiver). The verb's **argument structure** specifies how many and what kind of arguments it takes.

Common thematic roles include: **Agent** (volitional actor), **Patient** (entity affected by action), **Theme** (entity in motion or being described), **Experiencer** (entity undergoing a mental state), **Stimulus** (entity causing a mental state), **Beneficiary** (entity for whose benefit an action is done), **Instrument** (tool used), **Location**, **Source**, **Goal**.

The **Theta Criterion** (Chomsky 1981) states that each argument bears exactly one thematic role and each thematic role is assigned to exactly one argument. Violations produce ungrammaticality: "She gave the book" is incomplete (missing the recipient role); "She gave gave the book" is incoherent (one argument, two roles).

### Verbs as predicates

In formal semantics, a verb denotes a **predicate** -- a function from individuals to truth values. "Runs" corresponds to $\lambda x.\, runs(x)$, a function that takes an individual and returns *true* if that individual runs. This view treats verbs as the semantic backbone of clause structure: the verb provides the predicate, the noun phrases provide the arguments, and the sentence asserts that the predicate holds of those arguments.

The formal connection: a transitive verb like "loves" corresponds to $\lambda y.\,\lambda x.\, loves(x,y)$ -- a curried function taking two arguments in sequence. This is the basis of Montague semantics and connects directly to the lambda calculus and type theory used in the math and logic units.

### Davidson's event semantics

Davidson (1967) proposed that action sentences contain a hidden **event variable**. "She ran" is analysed as $\exists e(run(e) \land Agent(e, she))$, read as "there exists an event of running whose agent is she." This treatment elegantly handles adverbial modification: "She ran quickly in the park yesterday" adds three predicates to the event variable: $quick(e)$, $in\_park(e)$, and $yesterday(e)$. The adverbs are predicates of the event, not of the subject.

Event semantics predicts that adverbs can be reordered without changing the truth conditions (they all modify the same event), which matches intuition. It also explains why "she ran deliberately" entails "she ran" (the event exists in both sentences) but "she almost ran" does not entail "she ran" (the event is in the scope of a negation-like operator).

### Cross-linguistic verb typology

Languages differ dramatically in how much information they encode on the verb. At one extreme, languages like Mandarin and Vietnamese have minimal verb morphology -- no tense, no agreement, no aspect inflection on the verb itself (though Mandarin does have aspect particles). At the other extreme, languages like Navajo and Inuktitut encode subject, object, tense, aspect, mood, and direction on the verb in a single polymorphemic word. A single Inuktitut verb form can encode what would require an entire sentence in English.

The Bantu language family (Swahili, Zulu, Shona) has particularly rich verbal agreement. In Swahili, the verb *ninakupenda* means "I love you," where *ni-* (I), *-na-* (present tense), *-ku-* (you, object), and *-penda* (love) are all fused into a single word. English speakers learning Swahili must internalise this complex agreement system, while Swahili speakers learning English must learn to separate what their language fuses together.

### Connections

Verbs are central to every area of linguistic theory. In syntax, they head the verb phrase and determine clause structure through their argument structure. In semantics, they denote predicates or events. In morphology, they carry the most inflectional complexity of any word class in most languages. In psycholinguistics, they are the primary determinant of sentence processing difficulty -- the choice of verb determines how readers parse the rest of the clause.

### Verbs and critical reading

Understanding verbs is essential for critical reading. When a news article says "the police **dispersed** the crowd" rather than "the police **attacked** the protesters," the verb choice frames the event differently. When a politician says "we **invested** in infrastructure" rather than "we **spent** money on roads," the verb *invest* implies future returns that *spend* does not. When a company says it "**streamlined** operations" rather than "**fired** workers," the abstract verb obscures the human impact.

These verb choices are not just stylistic preferences. They are rhetorical moves that shape how readers understand events, assign responsibility, and form judgments. A reader who understands how verbs work -- what they imply, what they omit, what they foreground -- is a more critical and more informed reader. This is why the study of verbs belongs not only in grammar class but in any context where language is used to inform, persuade, or mislead.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

### The history of English verbs

"Verb" derives from Latin *verbum* (word), reflecting the Roman grammarians' view that the verb is the most important word in the sentence. The Greek grammatical tradition (Dionysius Thrax) used *rhema*, meaning "that which is said" -- the predicate.

The Indo-European verb system was heavily inflected for tense, aspect, mood, voice, person, and number. Old English inherited much of this complexity: verbs conjugated for person (1st, 2nd, 3rd), number (singular, plural), tense (present, past), mood (indicative, subjunctive, imperative), and had separate forms for the infinitive, participle, and imperative. The verb *singan* ("to sing") had over a dozen distinct forms.

Middle English (11th-15th centuries) saw massive simplification. Person and number distinctions eroded. The subjunctive became marginal. Many strong (irregular) verbs regularised (*helpan/holp* became *help/helped*). The surviving irregular verbs are precisely the most frequently used ones (*be, have, do, go, say, make, know, think, take, see, come, want, look, use, find, give, tell, work, call, try, ask, need, feel, become, leave, put, mean, keep, let, begin, seem, help, show, hear, play, run, move, live, believe, bring, happen, write, provide, sit, stand, lose, pay, meet, include, continue, set, learn, change, lead, understand, watch, follow, stop, create, speak, read, allow, add, spend, grow, open, walk, win, offer, remember, love, consider, appear, buy, wait, serve, die, send, expect, build, stay, fall, cut, reach, kill, remain, suggest, raise, pass, sell, require, report, decide, pull*).

### The prescriptivism-descriptivism debate and verbs

Prescriptive grammar has had strong opinions about verbs. The prohibition on split infinitives ("to boldly go" was condemned in favour of "to go boldly") originated with 18th-century grammarians who wanted English to follow Latin, where the infinitive is a single word and cannot be split. The rule has no basis in English grammar -- English infinitives are two words and have been split since Middle English -- but it persisted in style guides for over two centuries.

The prohibition on ending sentences with prepositions is similarly based on Latin and similarly irrelevant to English. Winston Churchill reportedly said "This is the sort of nonsense up with which I will not put" to parody the rule.

More consequential is the debate over irregular verbs. Children learning English pass through a stage where they regularise irregular verbs (*goed, catched, runned*). This is not an error but a sign that they have internalised the regular rule and are overapplying it. They later learn the irregular forms one by one. The U-shaped learning curve (*went* -> *goed* -> *went*) is a classic finding in language acquisition research.

### AAVE and verb features

African American Vernacular English (AAVE) has several distinctive verb features. The most studied is **habitual *be***: "She be working" means "She works habitually/regularly," not "She is working right now." Standard English has no single verb form that marks habituality; it uses adverbs ("She always works") or the simple present with habitual interpretation. AAVE marks it morphologically on the verb itself.

AAVE also shows **aspectual *done***: "She done finished" marks completive aspect (the action is completely finished). And it often omits copular *be* in present-tense statives: "She tired" instead of "She is tired." This is not random omission but a systematic grammatical rule of AAVE -- *be* is omitted in present-tense stative contexts but retained in habitual (*be* + V-ing) and past tense.

### Verbs and the philosophy of action

The philosophy of language has devoted significant attention to verbs of action. What does it mean to say someone "did" something? Davidson (1967) argued that action sentences entail the existence of an event, which means events are real entities in our ontology. Others (Vendler, Kenny) have argued that the Vendler classification maps onto deep metaphysical distinctions: states are not events at all, while accomplishments are events with a causal structure.

The distinction between *doing* and *allowing to happen* is encoded in verb choice ("she killed him" vs. "she let him die") and has significant moral and legal implications. The philosophy of action uses linguistic analysis of verbs to explore these distinctions, connecting grammar directly to ethics and law.

### The great vowel shift and irregular verbs

The irregular verbs of modern English are partly the result of the **Great Vowel Shift** (15th-18th centuries), which dramatically changed English pronunciation. Many verbs that were once regular (or at least phonologically predictable) became irregular in their spelling because the vowel shifts affected different forms differently. The alternation between *sing/sang/sung* reflects an older Germanic ablaut (vowel gradation) pattern that was once perfectly regular for an entire class of "strong" verbs. Over centuries, most of these verbs regularised to the "weak" *-ed* pattern, but the most frequently used ones retained their old forms because high-frequency words resist regularisation (Bybee 1995).

The verb *go/went* is a particularly interesting case. *Went* was originally the past tense of *wend* ("to direct one's course"), not of *go*. At some point during Middle English, speakers began using *went* as the past of *go* by a process called **suppletion** -- replacing a form with an entirely different word from a related paradigm. Suppletion occurs in many languages for the most frequent words: French *aller/ir-* (go/go-), Spanish *ir/fue* (go/went). It is another consequence of high frequency: the most common words are the most likely to develop irregular forms and the most resistant to regularisation.

### The future of English verbs

Contemporary English shows several ongoing changes in the verb system. The subjunctive continues to decline in informal usage ("if I was you" is now more common than "if I were you" in speech). The *got*/*gotten* distinction (American English has preserved *gotten* as the past participle of *get*, while British English has largely lost it) shows how the verb system diverges across regional varieties. The progressive is expanding into new contexts -- some speakers now accept stative progressives like "I'm loving it" that would have been ungrammatical a century ago.

These changes are not deterioration. They are the natural evolution of a living language, driven by the same forces that shaped the verb system in every previous century: frequency of use, analogy with existing patterns, contact with other languages, and the communicative needs of speakers. Descriptive linguists observe and document these changes; prescriptive grammarians resist them. Both roles are legitimate, but understanding the difference between description and prescription is essential for anyone studying language.

### Verbs in the digital age

Digital communication has introduced new verb-related conventions. In text messaging and social media, verbs are often omitted entirely ("home safe" = "I arrived home safely"). The progressive *-ing* form has become productive in new ways ("I can't even" functions as a verbless expression of overwhelm). New verbs are coined constantly: *to google, to tweet, to DM, to ghost, to unfriend, to doomscroll*. These novel verbs follow existing English patterns (*google/googled/googling* follows the regular paradigm), showing that the morphological system of English verbs remains productive even as the lexicon expands.

The passive voice has also shifted pragmatically in digital contexts. "Mistakes were made" was always a way to avoid responsibility, but in social media, passive constructions like "I was hacked" or "my tweet was taken out of context" serve specific face-saving functions that would have been expressed differently in pre-digital communication.
