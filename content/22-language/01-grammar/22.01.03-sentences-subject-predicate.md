## Intuition [Beginner]

A sentence is a complete thought. It has two parts: a **subject** (who or what the sentence is about) and a **predicate** (what the subject does or is).

"The bird **sings**." Subject = the bird. Predicate = sings.

"The old man **walked slowly to the store**." Subject = the old man. Predicate = walked slowly to the store. The predicate can be long -- it includes the verb and everything that goes with it.

Every sentence needs both. "The bird" by itself is not a sentence -- nothing is happening. "Sings" by itself is not a complete sentence either -- who sings? Put them together and you have a complete thought.

Think of the subject as the "who" and the predicate as the "what about them." The subject names a person, place, thing, or idea. The predicate tells you what that person, place, thing, or idea is doing, or what state it is in.

Some sentences are short: "Birds fly." The subject is "birds" and the predicate is "fly." Some are much longer: "The old stone bridge over the river that runs through our valley collapsed during the storm last Tuesday night." The subject is the entire long noun phrase "The old stone bridge over the river that runs through our valley" and the predicate is "collapsed during the storm last Tuesday night." Whether short or long, every complete sentence splits into these two parts.

In questions, the subject and predicate are rearranged. "Did the bird sing?" still has "the bird" as subject and "sing" as the main verb, but the auxiliary "did" moves to the front. In commands, the subject is hidden. "Close the door" means "You close the door." The subject "you" is understood but not spoken. These variations will be explored in detail later.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Split this sentence into subject and predicate: "The small puppy chewed the new shoe."

Subject: "The small puppy" -- who the sentence is about.
Predicate: "chewed the new shoe" -- what the subject did.

To find the subject, ask "who?" or "what?" before the verb. What chewed the shoe? The small puppy. To find the predicate, find the verb and take everything from there to the end.

Here is a second example: "My older brother plays guitar in a band."

Ask "who plays guitar?" -- my older brother. That is the subject. "Plays guitar in a band" is the predicate. Notice that the subject includes the words "my" and "older" because they tell us which brother.

Here is a third example with a longer predicate: "The students in the back row quietly finished their test before the bell rang."

Ask "who finished?" -- the students in the back row. That is the subject. "Quietly finished their test before the bell rang" is the predicate. The predicate can contain a lot of information.

What this tells us: every complete sentence can be split this way. If it cannot, it is either a fragment (missing a piece) or a run-on (two sentences jammed together without a proper join).

A fourth example shows a different kind of subject: "Walking to school takes twenty minutes."

Ask "what takes twenty minutes?" -- walking to school. The subject here is not a noun but a gerund phrase (a verb form ending in *-ing* that functions as a noun). The predicate is "takes twenty minutes." Subjects do not have to be simple nouns or pronouns.

A fifth example shows an inverted sentence: "On the hill stood an old church."

Ask "what stood on the hill?" -- an old church. Even though "on the hill" comes first, it is not the subject. The subject is "an old church," which follows the verb. Inverted sentences are common in descriptive writing: "Here comes the bus," "Out ran the children," "Down fell the rain."

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **sentence** is a grammatically complete construction consisting of at least one **subject** and one **predicate**. The subject is a noun phrase (or pronoun) that identifies what the sentence is about. The predicate is a verb phrase that says something about the subject [ref: TODO_REF].

### Sentence types by purpose

| Type | Function | End mark | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Declarative** | States a fact or opinion | Period | The sky is blue. |
| **Interrogative** | Asks a question | Question mark | Is the sky blue? |
| **Imperative** | Gives a command | Period or exclamation | Look at the sky. |
| **Exclamatory** | Expresses strong emotion | Exclamation mark | How blue the sky is! |

Each sentence type has a different subject-predicate arrangement. Declarative sentences follow the standard subject-then-predicate order. Interrogative sentences often invert the subject and the auxiliary verb: "Has she arrived?" Imperative sentences have an implied subject ("you"): "Close the door" means "You close the door." Exclamatory sentences begin with "how" or "what" and reposition elements for emphasis: "How blue the sky is" places the adjective before the subject.

### Sentence types by structure

| Type | Structure | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Simple** | One independent clause | The dog barked. |
| **Compound** | Two independent clauses joined | The dog barked, and the cat ran. |
| **Complex** | One independent + one or more dependent clauses | The dog barked because it heard a noise. |
| **Compound-complex** | Two+ independent + one+ dependent | The dog barked, and the cat ran because it was scared. |

A **clause** is a group of words with its own subject and predicate. An **independent clause** can stand alone as a sentence. A **dependent clause** has a subject and predicate but cannot stand alone -- it begins with a subordinating conjunction (*because, although, when, if, since*) or a relative pronoun (*who, which, that*). "Because it heard a noise" is a dependent clause: it has a subject ("it") and a predicate ("heard a noise"), but it does not express a complete thought on its own.

### The subject

The subject is typically a noun phrase. It can be:

- A single noun or pronoun: "Birds fly."
- A noun with modifiers: "The large brown eagle soared."
- A gerund phrase: "Swimming in the ocean is dangerous."
- A noun clause: "What she said surprised everyone."
- An infinitive phrase: "To leave now would be rude."

In English, the subject usually comes before the verb (SVO word order), but inversions exist: "Down came the rain" (verb before subject). Questions invert subject and auxiliary: "Has she arrived?" (auxiliary before subject). In poetry and creative writing, inversions are common for rhetorical effect: "Blessed are the meek" places the adjective "blessed" before the verb, and the subject "the meek" after it.

### The predicate

The predicate is centred on the verb and includes everything else in the clause that is not the subject:

- **Verb only**: "It rained." (predicate = rained)
- **Verb + direct object**: "She read the book." (predicate = read the book)
- **Verb + indirect object + direct object**: "He gave her flowers." (predicate = gave her flowers)
- **Verb + subject complement**: "The cake smells delicious." (predicate = smells delicious)

The predicate can also include adverbial modifiers that describe how, when, where, or why the action occurs: "She read the book quietly in the library yesterday." All of "read the book quietly in the library yesterday" is the predicate.

### Fragments and run-ons

A **fragment** is an incomplete sentence -- it is missing a subject, a predicate, or both. "Because it rained." (fragment -- what happened because it rained?) Fragments are common in speech and creative writing but are errors in formal writing.

A **run-on** joins two independent clauses without proper punctuation or conjunction. "It rained the game was cancelled." Two sentences fused. Fixed with a period ("It rained. The game was cancelled."), a semicolon ("It rained; the game was cancelled."), or a conjunction ("It rained, so the game was cancelled.").

### Counter-examples to common slips

- **Commands have an implied subject.** "Close the door" is a complete sentence. The subject is "you" (understood). The predicate is "close the door."
- **"There" and "it" can be dummy subjects.** "There is a problem" -- "there" is a dummy subject; the real subject is "a problem." "It is raining" -- "it" is a dummy subject with no referent.
- **Not every period marks the end of a sentence.** Abbreviations (Dr., St., etc.) contain periods but do not end sentences.

### World Englishes: sentence patterns

Sentence structure varies across World Englishes. In Indian English, sentences often use a subject-verb-object pattern influenced by Hindi and other Indian languages, where the verb typically appears at the end of the clause. A Hindi speaker might produce: "What you are doing?" rather than "What are you doing?" -- the subject "you" stays before the verb "are doing" rather than inverting with the auxiliary. This is not an error but a systematic pattern reflecting the speaker's language background.

In Singapore English (Singlish), sentences frequently drop the subject when it is understood from context: "Can or not?" means "Is this acceptable?" The subject "this" or "it" is omitted, following a pattern common in Malay and Chinese, which are pro-drop languages. In Nigerian English, sentences may include the serial comma and different conjunction patterns that reflect Yoruba, Igbo, or Hausa sentence structure beneath English vocabulary.

### Cross-linguistic comparison

English uses Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) as its basic word order, but this is just one of several possibilities found across the world's languages. About 35% of languages are SOV (Japanese, Korean, Turkish, Hindi), about 41% are SVO (English, Mandarin Chinese, French, Swahili), about 9% are VSO (Welsh, Arabic, Tagalog), and smaller percentages use VOS, OVS, or OSV.

In Japanese, "Watashi wa ringo o taberu" means "I apple eat" (SOV). The verb always comes at the end of the clause. The subject "watashi" (I) comes first, followed by the object "ringo" (apple), and the verb "taberu" (eat) comes last. Despite the different word order, the sentence still has a subject and a predicate -- the predicate is simply positioned after the object.

In Welsh, a VSO language, "Darllenodd y dyn y llyfr" means "Read the man the book" -- the verb comes first. The subject is "y dyn" (the man) and the object is "y llyfr" (the book). Again, the subject-predicate distinction holds, but the order is different.

These cross-linguistic differences show that the subject-predicate structure is not unique to English. Every known language has a way to distinguish between what a sentence is about (the topic or subject) and what is said about it (the comment or predicate). What varies is the order in which these elements appear.

### Descriptive vs. prescriptive perspectives on sentence structure

Prescriptive grammar teaches that every sentence must have a subject and a verb, that fragments are errors, and that run-ons must be corrected. These rules are useful for formal writing: academic essays, business reports, legal documents. They help writers produce clear, unambiguous prose.

Descriptive linguistics observes that actual language use is more flexible. In spoken English, fragments are the norm, not the exception. "Because internet." "So tired." "Nice one." These are complete utterances in context, even though they lack a subject or a verb. Conversation analysis shows that speakers routinely produce sentence fragments that are perfectly understood because the context supplies the missing elements.

In creative writing, fragments are a deliberate stylistic choice. Cormac McCarthy's novels contain hundreds of fragments: "The man took the boy's hand. Out on the road." The fragment "Out on the road" has no verb, but in context it creates a cinematic, image-driven effect. Breaking prescriptive rules is part of the writer's toolkit.

### AAVE and code-switching

African American Vernacular English (AAVE) has systematic sentence patterns that differ from Standard American English. One well-studied feature is **habitual *be***: "She be working" means "She is usually/habitually working," not "She is currently working." The uninflected *be* marks a habitual aspect that Standard English expresses with adverbs ("She usually works" or "She is always working"). This is not a missing verb form but a grammatically distinct system with its own rules.

AAVE also features **aspectual *done***: "I done told you" means "I have already told you," using *done* as a completive aspect marker. Again, this is a systematic grammatical pattern, not an error. Research by William Labov in the 1960s and 1970s demonstrated that AAVE has consistent, rule-governed grammar that differs from Standard English but is equally complex and logical.

Code-switching -- the practice of alternating between AAVE and Standard English depending on context -- is a sophisticated linguistic skill, not a deficiency. Speakers who code-switch are navigating two grammatical systems fluently, selecting the appropriate sentence patterns for each social context. Educators who understand this can help students develop proficiency in Standard English for academic contexts while respecting the grammatical integrity of AAVE.

### ESL transfer errors in sentence structure

Speakers of different first languages produce systematic sentence-structure errors in English. These errors are not random but reflect the grammar of the speaker's native language:

- **Arabic speakers** may write "The man who he is tall" (doubly marked relative clause) because Arabic requires a resumptive pronoun in relative clauses.
- **Spanish speakers** may write "The girl beautiful" (adjective after noun) because Spanish places most adjectives after the noun they modify.
- **Mandarin speakers** may produce topic-comment structures: "This book, I already read" because Mandarin frequently front-topics elements.
- **Japanese speakers** may write "Yesterday I to the store went" because Japanese places the verb at the end.
- **Russian speakers** may omit articles: "I have dog" because Russian has no articles.
- **Hindi speakers** may place the verb at the end: "I the book read" because Hindi is SOV.

Understanding these transfer patterns helps teachers anticipate errors and address them systematically, rather than treating each one as an isolated mistake.

### Sentence structure in legal and scientific writing

Different disciplines have characteristic sentence patterns. Legal writing favours long, complex sentences with multiple subordinate clauses: "The defendant, having been previously convicted of a similar offence and having been duly warned by the court, shall be sentenced to a term of imprisonment not exceeding five years." The subject "the defendant" is separated from the main predicate "shall be sentenced" by a long participial phrase. This structure is typical of legal prose and contributes to its difficulty.

Scientific writing uses passive constructions and nominalised predicates: "The reaction was observed to proceed at a rate proportional to the concentration of the catalyst." The subject "the reaction" is a noun formed from a verb (to react), and the predicate "was observed to proceed" uses both passive voice and an infinitive complement. These patterns foreground the experiment and background the researcher, which is a convention of scientific objectivity.

Journalistic writing favours shorter sentences and active voice: "The mayor announced a new policy today. The policy will take effect next month. Residents can apply for permits starting Monday." Each sentence is short and declarative, with a clear subject and predicate. This style prioritises clarity and readability for a general audience.

### Sentence diagramming

Sentence diagramming is a visual method for analysing sentence structure. Developed by Alonzo Reed and Brainerd Kellogg in the 1870s, diagramming places the subject on a horizontal line to the left of a vertical dividing line, and the predicate to the right:

```
The dog  |  chased the ball
---------+------------------
  Subj.  |    Predicate
```

Modifiers (adjectives, adverbs, prepositional phrases) are placed on diagonal lines below the word they modify. Direct objects are placed on a horizontal line below the verb, separated by another vertical line. While diagramming has fallen out of favour in many modern curricula, it provides a useful visual tool for understanding how sentences are structured.

Modern linguistics uses tree diagrams rather than Reed-Kellogg diagrams. A tree diagram shows the hierarchical structure of a sentence:

```
        S
       / \
      NP   VP
     / \   / \
    Det  N  V   NP
    |    |  |  / \
   the  dog chased the ball
```

Each node in the tree represents a phrase (S = sentence, NP = noun phrase, VP = verb phrase, Det = determiner, N = noun, V = verb). The tree shows that "the ball" is a constituent of the verb phrase, not a separate element at the same level as "the dog."

## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

### Topic vs. subject

In many languages, the topic (what the sentence is about) and the subject (the grammatical entity performing the action) are distinct. In English, they usually coincide: "The dog bit the man" has "the dog" as both subject and topic. But consider: "As for the man, the dog bit him." Here "the man" is the topic (what we are talking about) but "the dog" is the grammatical subject (the entity performing the biting).

In Japanese and Korean, topic and subject are marked with different particles. Japanese uses "wa" for topic and "ga" for subject: "Zou wa hana ga nagai" literally means "Elephant [topic], nose [subject] is-long" -- "As for elephants, their noses are long." The topic sets the frame; the subject identifies the grammatical entity within that frame.

English does not mark topic and subject with separate particles, but intonation and word order serve a similar function. Sentence-initial adverbials often function as topics: "In the morning, I drink coffee" sets "in the morning" as the temporal frame (topic) while "I" remains the grammatical subject.

### Sentence complexity in corpus data

Corpus linguistics reveals that most written English sentences are complex or compound-complex. Simple sentences account for only about 25-30% of sentences in academic prose. The average sentence length in academic writing is 20-25 words; in newspaper writing, 15-20 words; in speech, 8-12 words. These differences reflect the different demands of each register: academic writing requires precise qualification (hence subordinate clauses), while speech favours shorter, more direct constructions.

Spoken language also features more disfluencies -- false starts, repetitions, and repairs that break the clean subject-predicate structure of written sentences. "I think that the, the thing about it is that, you know, the government really should..." This is a perfectly normal spoken utterance but would be marked as a run-on or fragment in written form. The difference illustrates that written and spoken language follow different conventions, even though both are governed by the same underlying subject-predicate structure.

### The passive voice and subject reassignment

The passive voice changes which noun phrase occupies the subject position. "The dog bit the man" (active) has "the dog" as subject. "The man was bitten by the dog" (passive) has "the man" as subject, even though the man is the patient (the one receiving the action), not the agent (the one performing it).

Passive constructions are grammatically complete and sometimes necessary. In scientific writing, the passive is conventional: "The solution was heated to 100 degrees" foregrounds the procedure and background the researcher. In news reporting, the passive can be used to obscure agency: "Mistakes were made" avoids specifying who made them.

Critics of passive voice (including many writing guides) argue that it produces weaker, less direct prose. The advice is partly sound: "I made mistakes" is more direct than "Mistakes were made." But the blanket prohibition on passive voice is a prescriptive oversimplification. Skilled writers use both active and passive strategically, choosing which noun phrase to place in the subject position based on what they want to emphasise.

<p>The sentence "It is important to study grammar" has two levels of structure. Identify the dummy subject, the real subject, and explain why the dummy is used.</p>
<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>
<p>Dummy subject: "It." Real subject: "to study grammar" (an infinitive phrase). The dummy "it" fills the subject position so the verb "is" has something to agree with, while the heavier infinitive phrase is moved to the end of the sentence. This is called **extraposition** and is a standard pattern in English: "It is [adjective] to [verb phrase]." Without the dummy, the sentence would be "To study grammar is important," which is grammatical but sounds formal and stiff.</p>

## Linguistic theory [Master]

### Constituency and phrase structure

The subject-predicate split corresponds to a deeper structural claim: sentences have **constituent structure**. The sentence $S$ decomposes into a noun phrase $NP$ (the subject) and a verb phrase $VP$ (the predicate) [ref: TODO_REF]:

$$S \to NP\ VP$$

This is the fundamental rewrite rule of phrase structure grammar (Chomsky 1957). The $VP$ itself decomposes: $VP \to V\ NP$ (for transitive verbs), $VP \to V\ PP$ (for verbs with prepositional complements), and so on. The resulting tree structure captures the intuition that "the tall woman in the red coat" forms a single unit (constituent) that behaves differently from the separate words taken individually.

**Evidence for constituency.** Constituency is not just a theoretical claim. It is supported by distributional tests:

1. **Substitution**: a constituent can be replaced by a single word. "The tall woman in the red coat opened the door" $\to$ "She opened the door." The pronoun "she" replaces the entire subject NP.
2. **Movement**: a constituent can be moved as a unit. "She opened the door" $\to$ "The door, she opened." The object NP moves to the front as a unit.
3. **Coordination**: constituents of the same type can be joined. "She opened the door and closed the window." Two VPs coordinated.
4. **Clefting**: "It was the door that she opened." The cleft construction isolates the constituent "the door" as the focus.
5. **Constituent response**: "What did she open?" "The door." The response is a single constituent, not a random collection of words.

These tests provide empirical evidence that sentences are not flat strings of words but hierarchically organised structures. The constituency hypothesis predicts which groups of words behave as units, and the prediction is confirmed by the distributional evidence.

### X-bar theory

The simple $S \to NP\ VP$ rule hides internal structure. **X-bar theory** (Jackendoff 1977, Chomsky 1981) proposes that every phrase has a three-level structure:

$$XP \to \text{Specifier},\ X'$$
$$X' \to X,\ \text{Complement}$$

Where $X$ is the **head** of the phrase (a noun for NP, a verb for VP), $X'$ is the intermediate projection, and $XP$ is the maximal projection. For the noun phrase "the tall woman":

- Head ($N$): *woman*
- Complement: (none in this case)
- Adjunct: *tall* (modifies the head)
- Specifier: *the* (determiner)

This theory predicts cross-categorial uniformity: noun phrases, verb phrases, adjective phrases, and prepositional phrases all share the same internal geometry. The prediction is borne out across languages. A verb phrase "read the book carefully" has the same structure: head (*read*), complement (*the book*), adjunct (*carefully*). The parallelism is not accidental -- it reflects a deep structural property of human language.

### Dependency grammar

An alternative to constituency is **dependency grammar** (Tesniere 1959), which dispenses with phrase nodes entirely. Instead, each word is a node, and edges connect heads to dependents:

```
opened (root)
  |-- subject: woman
  |     |-- det: the
  |     |-- adj: tall
  |-- object: door
        |-- det: the
```

"Opened" is the root. "Woman" depends on "opened" (as its subject). "The" depends on "woman" (as its determiner). No phrase nodes are needed. Dependency grammar underlies most modern NLP parsing (Universal Dependencies, de Marneffe et al. 2021) and connects to the graph-theoretic formalisms used elsewhere in the curriculum.

The choice between constituency and dependency is not purely theoretical -- it has practical consequences for how we build computational tools for language. Constituency-based parsers produce tree structures that capture hierarchical grouping; dependency parsers produce flatter structures that capture word-to-word relationships. Both representations are used in modern NLP, and the choice depends on the task.

### Comprehending sentences: psycholinguistics

Psycholinguistic research shows that readers and listeners process sentences incrementally -- they do not wait for the end of a sentence to begin interpreting it. Eye-tracking studies reveal that readers commit to a syntactic parse within the first few hundred milliseconds of encountering each word. When the initial parse turns out to be wrong (a "garden path" sentence), readers must reanalyse, which takes additional time and cognitive effort.

Garden path sentences are well-formed sentences that temporarily mislead the parser. "The horse raced past the barn fell" is grammatical but difficult to read. Most readers initially interpret "raced past the barn" as the main predicate (the horse raced past the barn), but then encounter "fell" and must reanalyse: "raced past the barn" is actually a reduced relative clause modifying "the horse," and "fell" is the main predicate. The sentence means "The horse [that was] raced past the barn fell."

Garden path effects provide evidence for the psychological reality of syntactic structure. If readers were not building tree structures as they read, they would not be misled by structural ambiguities. The fact that reanalysis costs time and effort shows that syntactic parsing is a real cognitive process, not just a theoretical construct.

### The minimal sentence

What is the smallest possible English sentence? In spoken English, single-word sentences are common: "Go." "Yes." "Help." These are complete communicative acts, but only "Go" and "Help" have a clear subject-predicate structure (both are imperatives with an implied subject "you"). "Yes" is a discourse particle, not a sentence with subject and predicate.

In formal written English, the shortest complete sentence with an explicit subject and predicate is typically a two-word sentence: "Birds fly." "She waited." "It rained." Each has a one-word subject and a one-word predicate (the verb). The shortest possible written English sentence is widely cited as "I am." or "Go." -- though the latter has only an implied subject.

The question of minimal sentence length has practical implications for language education and for the design of writing assessments that require "complete sentences." Understanding that a complete sentence needs only a subject and a predicate, even when each is a single word, helps students distinguish between fragments and genuine sentences.

### The verb-second constraint (comparative)

English is largely SVO, but German, Dutch, and the Scandinavian languages enforce **verb-second (V2) word order**: in main clauses, the finite verb must occupy the second position, regardless of what comes first.

- German: "Heute **gehe** ich ins Kino" (Today **go** I to the cinema)
- English: "Today I **am going** to the cinema"

The verb moves to a structural position (C, the complementiser head in generative syntax) that English fills only in questions and certain inversions. This parametric difference is a core example in comparative syntax. Old English was also a V2 language, and the loss of V2 in Middle English is one of the most significant word-order changes in the history of English.

### Sentences in formal semantics

In formal semantics, a declarative sentence denotes a **proposition** -- a function from possible worlds to truth values. The sentence "The cat is on the mat" is true in worlds where a unique cat is located on a unique mat, and false otherwise. The subject "the cat" provides a referent (an individual), and the predicate "is on the mat" provides a property (a function from individuals to truth values). Combining them yields a truth value.

This is Montague's insight (1970s): natural language composition mirrors function application. The predicate is a function, the subject is an argument, and the sentence is the result of applying the function to the argument. This parallel between syntax and semantics -- the subject-predicate structure of sentences mirrors the argument-function structure of semantic composition -- is one of the deepest results in linguistic theory.

### Sentences in literature

Sentence structure is a powerful literary tool. Ernest Hemingway favoured short, simple sentences: "It was a good morning. There were high white clouds above the mountains. It had rained a little in the night and it was fresh and cool." The simple sentence structure mirrors the spare, direct quality of the prose.

By contrast, William Faulkner used long, complex, and compound-complex sentences that pile clause upon clause: "They endured the shame and the pity and the fear and then, when they were old enough, they began to endure the pride too, the fierce pride that would not let them admit defeat even when they knew they were beaten." The complex sentence structure captures the accumulation of experience and emotion.

Toni Morrison varied sentence length and complexity to create rhythm and emotional intensity. In *Beloved*, some passages use fragments ("Freeing yourself was one thing, claiming ownership of that freed self was another.") while others use long, flowing complex sentences. The variation itself is meaningful -- it mirrors the psychological states of the characters.

James Baldwin's essays use compound and compound-complex sentences to build argumentative weight: "I do not mean to be sentimental about suffering -- enough is certainly as good as a feast -- but people who cannot suffer can never grow up, can never discover who they are." The structure allows Baldwin to build, qualify, and extend his argument within a single sentence.

### Sentence-level ambiguity

Some sentences are structurally ambiguous -- they can be parsed in more than one way. "I saw the man with the telescope" has two readings: (1) I used a telescope to see the man, or (2) I saw a man who was holding a telescope. The ambiguity arises because "with the telescope" can attach to the verb "saw" (as an instrument) or to the noun "man" (as a modifier).

Similarly, "Flying planes can be dangerous" is ambiguous: (1) Planes that are flying can be dangerous, or (2) The act of flying planes can be dangerous. In the first reading, "flying" is an adjective modifying "planes." In the second, "flying planes" is a gerund phrase (the subject) and "can be dangerous" is the predicate.

These ambiguities are not curiosities -- they are central to the study of syntax. If sentences were flat strings of words, ambiguity would be random. The fact that ambiguities are systematic (they arise from alternative tree structures) is evidence for hierarchical constituent structure. A parser that assigns tree structures to sentences must choose between competing parses, and the choice determines the meaning.

### Sentences and truth conditions

In the philosophy of language, a declarative sentence has **truth conditions** -- the conditions under which it would be true. "The cat is on the mat" is true if and only if there exists a unique cat, there exists a unique mat, and the cat is located on the mat. The subject contributes the referent (the cat) and the predicate contributes the property (being on the mat). Together they determine the truth conditions of the sentence.

This analysis, developed by Gottlob Frege and Bertrand Russell, treats sentences as the basic units of meaning. A sentence says something that can be true or false; a phrase (a subject without a predicate, or a predicate without a subject) does not. Understanding sentence structure -- the division between subject and predicate, the ways sentences combine into larger structures, and the systematic ambiguity that arises from alternative tree structures -- is foundational for all further study of grammar, logic, and rhetoric. Every subsequent unit in this sequence presupposes the subject-predicate analysis established here.

### Digital-age sentence patterns

Digital communication has introduced new sentence patterns. Text messages and social media posts frequently use fragments, one-word sentences, and non-standard punctuation. "ok." "will do" "sent it" are complete communicative acts in context, even though they lack a subject or a full predicate. The question of whether these count as "sentences" depends on whether we apply prescriptive grammar rules or descriptive communicative criteria.

Linguistic research on computer-mediated communication (CMC) shows that digital writers are not abandoning grammar but adapting it to a new medium. The constraints of small screens and fast-paced communication favour brevity, but the underlying subject-predicate structure still governs how these shortened forms are interpreted. "Sent it" is understood as "[I] sent it" -- the subject is recoverable from context.

## Historical context [Master]

The subject-predicate analysis descends from Aristotle's *Peri Hermeneias* (On Interpretation), where every statement is analysed as a combination of a subject (*hypokeimenon*, "that which underlies") and a predicate (*kategoroumenon*, "that which is said of it"). Aristotle's insight was that a meaningful assertion requires both a thing to talk about and something to say about it. This binary split survived through the Stoic logicians, medieval scholastic grammar, the Port-Royal Grammar (1660), and into modern linguistics.

The Port-Royal Grammar (Arnauld and Lancelot, 1660) extended Aristotle's analysis by proposing that the structure of sentences reflects the structure of thought. Their central claim -- that grammar is the rational analysis of thought -- influenced Noam Chomsky's rationalist approach to syntax in the 1950s and 1960s. Chomsky's *Syntactic Structures* (1957) formalised the subject-predicate split as a rewrite rule in a generative grammar, giving it mathematical precision for the first time.

In the 19th century, the historical linguists Franz Bopp and Jacob Grimm established that sentence structure changes over time, just as sounds and vocabulary do. Old English was a heavily inflected language where word order was relatively free because case endings indicated grammatical roles. As the inflections eroded during the Middle English period, word order became increasingly fixed, and the subject-verb-object pattern became the norm. This historical change explains why English has stricter word order than languages like Russian or Latin, where case endings still mark grammatical roles.

The 18th century saw the rise of prescriptive grammar, driven by a desire to standardise English sentence structure for an expanding reading public. Robert Lowth's *A Short Introduction to English Grammar* (1762) codified rules about sentence completeness, proper subject-verb agreement, and the avoidance of fragments and run-ons. Many of Lowth's rules -- including the prohibition on ending sentences with prepositions and the rule against splitting infinitives -- were based on Latin grammar rather than on the actual structure of English, but they became entrenched in educational practice and remain influential today.

The 19th century saw the historical linguists Franz Bopp and Jacob Grimm establish that sentence structure changes over time, just as sounds and vocabulary do. Their comparative work on Indo-European languages showed that the subject-predicate distinction is ancient, traceable back to Proto-Indo-European, but the specific ways it is expressed vary across related languages. Sanskrit, Greek, Latin, and Old English all mark subject and predicate differently, yet all share the fundamental binary structure.

The 20th century saw two major refinements. Phrase structure grammar (Chomsky 1957) formalised the hierarchical organisation of sentences. Dependency grammar (Tesniere 1959) offered an alternative without phrase nodes. Both approaches are still used today, and the choice between them has practical implications for computational linguistics and natural language processing.

### Head-Driven Phrase Structure Grammar

Head-Driven Phrase Structure Grammar (HPSG, Pollard & Sag 1994) offers yet another approach. In HPSG, each word and phrase is described by a feature structure -- a set of attribute-value pairs that specify syntactic category, agreement features, semantic content, and argument structure. The head of a phrase (typically the verb in a VP, the noun in an NP) determines the properties of the entire phrase.

For the sentence "The dog chased the ball," the verb "chased" is the head of the verb phrase. Its feature structure specifies that it requires a subject (third person singular) and a direct object. The noun phrase "the dog" satisfies the subject requirement, and "the ball" satisfies the object requirement. The sentence is well-formed because all requirements are satisfied.

HPSG is **constraint-based** rather than **derivational**: instead of applying transformation rules to generate sentences, it specifies constraints that well-formed sentences must satisfy. This makes it well-suited for computational implementation and for handling the many exceptions and irregularities that occur in natural language.

### The pro-drop parameter

Some languages allow subjects to be omitted when they are understood from context. This is called **pro-drop** (pronoun dropping). Italian, Spanish, Arabic, Japanese, Korean, and Mandarin Chinese are all pro-drop languages. In Italian, "Piove" means "It rains" -- no subject pronoun is needed. In Mandarin, "chi le" (eat completed) can mean "I ate" or "she ate" depending on context.

English is not a pro-drop language: "Rains" is not a complete sentence. But English does allow subject omission in specific contexts: imperative sentences ("Close the door" = "You close the door"), diary style ("Went to the store. Bought milk."), and coordinate structures ("She opened the door and walked inside" -- the second clause has no overt subject).

The pro-drop parameter is one of the most studied parameters in comparative syntax. Languages that allow pro-drop typically have rich verb inflection (Italian, Spanish) or discourse-oriented syntax (Japanese, Mandarin). English, with its relatively poor verb inflection, requires overt subjects in most contexts. The loss of verb inflection in Middle English is thought to be connected to the loss of pro-drop.

### Trees and formal language theory

The idea that sentences have tree structure connects grammar to formal language theory in computer science. Chomsky (1957) defined a hierarchy of formal languages based on the types of rules needed to generate them. Regular languages (Type 3) can be described by regular expressions. Context-free languages (Type 2) can be described by context-free grammars (like the $S \to NP\ VP$ rule). Context-sensitive languages (Type 1) and recursively enumerable languages (Type 0) are more powerful.

Most linguists agree that natural language syntax is at least context-free, and some constructions (cross-serial dependencies in Swiss German, certain centre-embeddings) may require mildly context-sensitive power. This connection between linguistics and computer science is one of the most productive interdisciplinary bridges in the study of language, and it means that tools and concepts from the formal language theory unit in the computer science strand are directly applicable to the analysis of sentence structure.
