## Intuition [Beginner]

Parallel structure means using the same grammatical form for items in a list, a comparison, or a paired construction. When all items match, the sentence reads smoothly. When they do not, something feels off.

**In lists**, every item should be the same kind of word or phrase:

- Parallel: "She likes **hiking**, **swimming**, and **biking**." (all -ing words)
- Not parallel: "She likes **hiking**, **swimming**, and **to bike**." (mixed -ing and to- form)

**With coordinating conjunctions** (and, but, or, nor), the elements on each side should match:

- Parallel: "He works **quickly** and **accurately**." (both adverbs)
- Not parallel: "He works **quickly** and **with accuracy**." (adverb vs. prepositional phrase)

**With correlative conjunctions** (either...or, neither...nor, not only...but also, both...and), the two parts should match:

- Parallel: "She is **not only** a talented writer **but also** a skilled editor." (both noun phrases)
- Not parallel: "She is **not only** a talented writer **but also** edits skillfully." (noun phrase vs. verb phrase)

**In comparisons** (using than or as), the compared elements should be grammatically parallel:

- Parallel: "It is easier to **write** than to **speak**." (both infinitives)
- Not parallel: "It is easier to **write** than **speaking**." (infinitive vs. gerund)

The rule is simple: **match the pattern.** If the first item is an -ing word, make them all -ing words. If the first item starts with "to," make them all start with "to."

Parallel structure matters because it makes sentences easier to read. When each item in a list follows the same grammatical pattern, the reader can process the list quickly and efficiently. When each item follows the same pattern, the reader can process the list quickly. When the pattern shifts mid-list, the reader stumbles. Parallelism is not just about following a rule; it is about helping the reader.

Consider the difference between these two sentences. "The company values innovation, creativity, and efficiency." All three items are abstract nouns, and the sentence flows naturally. "The company values innovation, being creative, and to work efficiently." The shift from noun to gerund phrase to infinitive phrase makes the sentence feel disjointed. The meaning is the same, but the non-parallel version requires more effort to parse. The effort is small, but it is cumulative: a document with many non-parallel constructions becomes progressively harder to read.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**1. Fix: "The new employee is reliable, creative, and works hard."**
- First two items are adjectives ("reliable," "creative"). The third is a verb phrase ("works hard").
- Fix: "The new employee is reliable, creative, and hardworking." (all adjectives)

**2. Fix: "He likes to read, writing, and to play guitar."**
- Mixed infinitives and gerund.
- Fix: "He likes to read, to write, and to play guitar." (all infinitives)
- Alternative fix: "He likes reading, writing, and playing guitar." (all gerunds)

**3. Fix: "The course teaches students how to analyze data, present findings, and writing reports."**
- First two items are "how to + verb" phrases. The third breaks the pattern.
- Fix: "The course teaches students how to analyze data, present findings, and write reports." (all verb base forms after "how to")

**4. Fix: "She is not only an excellent teacher but also inspires her students."**
- "Not only" is followed by a noun phrase; "but also" is followed by a verb phrase.
- Fix: "She is not only an excellent teacher but also an inspiring mentor." (both noun phrases)
- Alternative fix: "She not only teaches excellently but also inspires her students." (both verb phrases)

**5. Fix: "Running every day is healthier than to sit on the couch."**
- Gerund compared with infinitive.
- Fix: "Running every day is healthier than sitting on the couch." (both gerunds)

**6. Fix: "The report must be accurate, complete, and it should be on time."**
- First two items are adjectives ("accurate," "complete"). The third is a clause ("it should be on time").
- Fix: "The report must be accurate, complete, and timely." (all adjectives)
- This example shows how faulty parallelism often creeps in when a writer adds items to a list one at a time without checking consistency.

**7. Fix: "She enjoys painting, to sculpt, and drawing."**
- Mixed gerund and infinitive.
- Fix: "She enjoys painting, sculpting, and drawing." (all gerunds)
- Alternative fix: "She enjoys to paint, to sculpt, and to draw." (all infinitives, though less common after "enjoys")

**8. Fix: "The teacher said that the students should study hard, to complete all assignments, and that they must attend every class."**
- Mixed "that" clauses and infinitive phrase.
- Fix: "The teacher said that the students should study hard, complete all assignments, and attend every class." (all verb phrases governed by "should")

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Parallelism** (also called **parallel structure** or **parallel construction**) is the principle that coordinate, compared, or correlative elements in a sentence should have the same grammatical form. The domains where parallelism applies:

| Domain | Pattern | Example |
|--------|---------|---------|
| Lists and series | A, B, and C (same category) | *reading, writing, and arithmetic* |
| Compound predicates | V1 and V2 (same form) | *She sings and dances.* |
| Correlative conjunctions | either X or Y (X, Y same form) | *either walk or run* |
| Comparisons | X than Y (X, Y same form) | *easier to read than to write* |
| Headings and outlines | consistent format throughout | *1. Define, 2. Measure, 3. Analyze* |

**Faulty parallelism** occurs when items in a coordinate or comparative structure differ in grammatical category (e.g., mixing noun phrases and verb phrases, or gerunds and infinitives). The result is not necessarily ungrammatical in the strict sense, but it violates a strong stylistic convention of formal English.

The parallelism constraint operates at every level of grammatical structure:

- **Morphological:** coordinate adjectives should have the same degree form (*faster, more reliable, cheaper* rather than *faster, more reliable, and it costs less*)
- **Phrasal:** coordinate phrases should be of the same type (*in the morning and at night* rather than *in the morning and nighttime*)
- **Clausal:** coordinate clauses should have the same structure (*She said that she would come and that she would bring food*)

### Prescriptive and descriptive perspectives on parallelism

Prescriptive grammar treats parallelism as a rule that must be followed. Style guides such as Strunk and White present parallelism as a non-negotiable principle: "Express coordinate ideas in similar form." Under this view, faulty parallelism is an error that weakens writing.

Descriptive linguistics takes a more nuanced view. Spoken English frequently contains non-parallel coordinations that speakers produce and listeners accept without difficulty. "She went to the store and bought some milk" is technically non-parallel (a clause coordinated with a verb phrase), but no listener would find it odd. The descriptive view holds that parallelism is a strong tendency rather than an absolute rule, and that the degree of parallelism expected varies by register, genre, and context.

Both perspectives have merit. In formal academic writing, parallelism is a powerful tool for creating clear, readable prose. In casual speech, strict parallelism is less important than fluency and naturalness. Effective writers understand both perspectives and apply parallelism strategically rather than rigidly.

### Parallelism across World Englishes

The parallelism requirement operates similarly across all major varieties of English (American, British, Australian, Indian, Nigerian, Caribbean, and others). However, the tolerance for non-parallel constructions varies. Indian English, for example, sometimes allows coordinations that would be considered non-parallel in American or British academic writing. Nigerian English shows similar flexibility in certain informal registers.

These variations do not reflect a lack of grammatical knowledge. They reflect the natural evolution of parallelism conventions in different speech communities. What counts as "faulty parallelism" in a New York Times editorial may be perfectly acceptable in a Lagos newspaper or a Mumbai business email. Understanding this variation is essential for teaching parallelism in a global context.

### Parallelism and code-switching

Speakers who code-switch between English and another language sometimes transfer parallelism patterns from their first language. For example, a speaker of a language that does not formally distinguish between gerunds and infinitives may produce non-parallel coordinations in English because the distinction is not salient in their first language. This transfer effect is a natural part of second language acquisition and should not be treated as an error in need of correction. Instead, it should be addressed through explicit instruction on the English gerund-infinitive distinction.

### ESL transfer errors and parallelism

Second language learners from specific language backgrounds tend to make predictable parallelism errors. Speakers of Mandarin, which does not inflect verbs for tense or form, may mix gerunds and infinitives because the distinction does not exist in their first language. Speakers of Arabic, which uses coordination extensively but with different structural constraints, may produce non-parallel coordinations that reflect Arabic syntax. Speakers of Spanish, which has a more flexible coordination system, may produce constructions that would be considered non-parallel in formal English.

Effective ESL instruction addresses these transfer patterns by explicitly teaching the English parallelism constraint and providing practice with the specific constructions that cause difficulty for learners from different language backgrounds.


### Parallelism and AAVE

African American Vernacular English (AAVE) uses coordination and parallelism in ways that sometimes differ from Standard American English (SAE). For example, AAVE allows certain double negation constructions and aspectual markers that create coordination patterns distinct from SAE. However, the core parallelism principle -- that coordinated items should match in form -- operates similarly in AAVE and SAE. The differences lie in which forms are available for coordination, not in the coordination constraint itself.

Understanding parallelism in AAVE is important for writing instruction because it helps teachers distinguish between errors in parallelism and differences in dialect structure. A student who writes non-parallel constructions may be transferring AAVE coordination patterns rather than making a parallelism error. Effective instruction recognises this distinction and addresses parallelism within the context of the student home language.
 Code-switching between AAVE and SAE in academic writing is a skill that students develop as they learn to navigate different registers. Instruction in parallelism should respect the student home language while teaching the conventions of academic English.

### Parallelism in technical and business writing

Technical and business writing demand strict parallelism because clarity is paramount. In a set of instructions, each step should follow the same grammatical pattern. If the first step is an imperative verb phrase, all steps should be imperative verb phrases. Mixing imperatives with gerunds or noun phrases creates confusion about what the reader is supposed to do.

Business reports use parallelism in bullet points, executive summaries, and recommendation sections. A recommendation section that mixes imperative verbs with noun phrases -- "1. Reduce costs. 2. A review of vendor contracts. 3. Implementing new tracking software" -- is harder to follow than one with consistent parallelism: "1. Reduce costs. 2. Review vendor contracts. 3. Implement new tracking software."

Legal writing also depends on parallelism. Statutes and contracts use coordinate lists to define rights and obligations. A non-parallel list in a contract can create ambiguity about the scope of an obligation, which can lead to disputes. The principle of legal drafting known as the "parallel construction rule" requires that items in a statutory list share the same grammatical form. This rule is enforced by legal style guides such as the Government Printing Office Style Manual and is taught in legal writing courses. Non-parallel lists in statutes have been the basis of legal disputes, with courts sometimes ruling that the lack of parallelism creates ambiguity about the legislature intent.

### Parallelism and sentence combining instruction

Sentence combining is a pedagogical technique in which students learn to combine short, simple sentences into longer, more complex ones. Parallelism is a natural focus of sentence combining instruction because combining sentences often requires creating coordinate structures. When students combine "She likes to read" and "She likes to write" into "She likes to read and to write," they are practising parallelism.

Research on sentence combining (Strong, 1986; da Costa, 2015) shows that explicit instruction in combining sentences improves both writing quality and grammatical knowledge. Parallelism is one of the key structures that students learn through sentence combining exercises, because it is both common and teachable.
 The technique is particularly effective for teaching parallelism because it requires students to make explicit decisions about grammatical form, rather than simply identifying errors in existing text.



## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

1. **Parallelism and the coordinate structure constraint.** In syntax, the **Coordinate Structure Constraint** (Ross, 1967) states that elements inside a coordinate structure cannot be extracted or moved independently. "What did she [buy __] and [sell __]?" is fine (parallel gaps), but "What did she [buy books] and [sell __]?" is not (non-parallel extraction). This syntactic constraint reflects the deeper principle that coordination requires structural parallelism.

2. **The role of the first item as a template.** In most cases of faulty parallelism, the first item in the series sets the pattern, and subsequent items break it. This makes editing straightforward: identify the form of the first item and ensure all subsequent items match. "She likes **hiking** (gerund), **swimming** (gerund), and **to bike** (infinitive)" -- the third item breaks the gerund pattern set by the first two.

3. **Ellipsis and parallelism.** Parallelism enables **elliptical coordination** -- the omission of repeated material that the reader can recover from the parallel structure. "She likes hiking and [likes] swimming." The parallel structure makes the ellipsis recoverable. If the structure is not parallel, ellipsis fails: "She likes hiking and to swim" -- the reader cannot recover a single verb that governs both forms.

4. **Parallelism in rhetoric.** Beyond grammar, parallelism is a powerful rhetorical device. Martin Luther King Jr.'s "I have a dream" speech uses anaphoric parallelism (repeating "I have a dream" before each clause) to build momentum. Churchill's "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields" uses syntactic parallelism for rhetorical force. Grammatical parallelism and rhetorical parallelism are the same principle applied at different scales.

5. **Parallelism and processing efficiency.** Research in psycholinguistics shows that parallel structures are processed faster than non-parallel ones. When readers encounter the first item in a coordinate structure, they form an expectation about the grammatical form of subsequent items. When the expectation is met (parallel structure), processing is smooth. When the expectation is violated (faulty parallelism), the reader experiences a momentary processing difficulty. This finding supports the view that parallelism is not merely a stylistic preference but reflects how the human language processing system works.

6. **Parallelism in headings and outlines.** Parallelism applies beyond sentences. In a document outline, all headings at the same level should be grammatically parallel. If the first heading is a noun phrase ("The Problem"), all headings at that level should be noun phrases. If the first heading is an imperative verb ("Define the Problem"), all should be imperative verbs. This principle ensures that the document has a consistent structure that readers can navigate efficiently.
 For example, a report with the heading structure "Background," "Methods," "Results," and "Discussion" uses noun phrases consistently. If one heading were changed to "How We Did It," the parallelism would be broken.

7. **Cross-linguistic comparison.** Parallelism is not unique to English. Coordinate structures in most languages require some form of structural matching. German requires coordinated noun phrases to share the same case. Japanese requires consistent verb form in coordination. Arabic uses a conjunction system that demands formal parallelism between coordinated elements. The universality of the parallelism constraint suggests that it reflects a fundamental property of human language.

### Sentence combining and parallel structure

Sentence combining exercises are an effective way to practise parallelism. Given two or more sentences with related content, the task is to combine them into a single sentence with parallel structure.

**Example 1:**
- Original sentences: "She likes to swim. She likes to run. She likes to cycle."
- Combined with parallelism: "She likes to swim, run, and cycle." (all infinitives, with "to" shared)

**Example 2:**
- Original sentences: "The hotel has a pool. The hotel has a gym. The hotel has a restaurant."
- Combined with parallelism: "The hotel has a pool, a gym, and a restaurant." (all noun phrases)

These exercises develop an intuitive sense of parallelism by requiring the writer to identify the shared structure and apply it consistently across all items.

### Parallelism in academic writing

Academic writing places a high premium on parallelism because it signals careful thinking. A thesis statement with parallel structure ("This essay will argue that X, that Y, and that Z") is more persuasive than one without ("This essay will argue X, about Y, and also Z"). The parallel structure shows that the writer has organised the argument into comparable units, which strengthens the overall presentation.

In literature reviews, parallelism helps readers compare sources. "Smith (2010) argues that the policy failed; Jones (2012) contends that it succeeded; and Lee (2015) suggests that the results were mixed" uses parallel clause structure to present three sources in a format that invites direct comparison.

### Parallelism and diverse authors

Parallelism is a feature of effective prose across all cultural traditions. Maya Angelou uses parallel structure extensively in her autobiographical writing: "I come as one, I stand as ten thousand." James Baldwin uses parallel clauses to build emotional intensity in his essays. Ta-Nehisi Coates uses anaphoric parallelism (repeating a phrase at the beginning of successive clauses) in *Between the World and Me* to create urgency and momentum.

Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie uses parallelism to contrast experiences across cultures in *Americanah*. Junot Diaz uses parallel structure in *The Brief Wondrous Life of Oscar Wao* to weave together multiple narrative voices and time periods. These examples show that parallelism is not a Western or Eurocentric convention but a universal feature of powerful writing.


### Parallelism in scientific writing

Scientific writing uses parallelism extensively in methods sections, results sections, and conclusions. A methods section that describes three experiments should present each experiment in the same format: participants, materials, procedure, analysis. This structural parallelism allows readers to compare experiments directly.

Results sections use parallelism to present findings: "Experiment 1 showed X. Experiment 2 showed Y. Experiment 3 showed Z." This parallel clause structure makes the results easy to scan and compare. A non-parallel results section -- "Experiment 1 showed X. The results of Experiment 2 were Y. As for Experiment 3, Z was demonstrated" -- is harder to follow because each result is presented in a different format.

### Parallelism in presentations

Oral presentations benefit from parallelism even more than written text, because listeners cannot re-read a sentence they missed. A presentation that uses parallel structure for its main points helps the audience track the argument. "First, we will examine the problem. Second, we will review the evidence. Third, we will propose a solution." The parallel structure ("we will" + verb) signals to the audience that each point is at the same level of importance.

### The role of parallelism in memorability

Parallel structures are more memorable than non-parallel ones. This is why proverbs, slogans, and maxims almost always use parallelism. "Easy come, easy go." "Nothing ventured, nothing gained." "Ask not what your country can do for you; ask what you can do for your country." In each case, the parallel structure makes the phrase easier to remember and more satisfying to say.

The memorability of parallel structures has been studied in cognitive psychology. Research on the "rhyme-as-reason effect" shows that people judge rhyming statements as more accurate than non-rhyming ones, even when the content is identical. A similar effect applies to parallelism: people find parallel statements more convincing and more memorable than non-parallel ones, all else being equal. This finding has implications for persuasive writing, advertising, and political communication.
 Speechwriters routinely use parallelism to make key phrases memorable, knowing that audiences are more likely to remember and repeat statements that have a balanced, parallel structure.

### Parallelism and the principle of proximity

The principle of proximity states that related items should be placed close together. Parallelism and proximity work together: related items should be close together AND in the same form. "She likes hiking, swimming, and biking" satisfies both principles. "She likes hiking, she also enjoys swimming, and biking is another hobby" violates both proximity (the items are separated by extra material) and parallelism (the items have different structures).

### Common parallelism errors in student writing

Student writing exhibits several common parallelism errors. The most frequent is mixing gerunds and infinitives in a list: "She enjoys reading, to write, and studying." The second most frequent is mixing adjectives and clauses after a linking verb: "The car is fast, reliable, and it gets good gas mileage." The third is mixing noun phrases and verb phrases after correlative conjunctions: "He is not only a brilliant scientist but also conducts groundbreaking research."

These errors are easy to fix once identified. The strategy is always the same: identify the form of the first item and make all subsequent items match. This strategy works because the first item usually establishes the intended pattern, and the error occurs when a later item breaks the pattern.

### Parallelism and revision

Parallelism errors are often introduced during revision, when a writer adds or changes items in a list without checking whether the new items match the established pattern. A sentence that was originally parallel -- "She is kind, generous, and patient" -- becomes non-parallel when the writer adds a detail: "She is kind, generous, patient, and she always helps others." The added clause breaks the adjective pattern.

The solution is to review all coordinate structures during the editing phase, checking that every item in every list matches the pattern set by the first item. This review is quick and catches most parallelism errors. Many professional editors include a specific parallelism check in their editing workflow, reviewing each coordinate list in the document for consistent structure.

### Parallelism in poetry

Poetry exploits parallelism at every level: phonological (rhyme, alliteration), morphological (repeated word forms), syntactic (repeated clause structures), and semantic (repeated themes). The parallelism constraint that operates in prose is heightened in poetry, where it becomes a defining structural principle.

Biblical Hebrew poetry is built on parallelism. The Psalms, for example, use synonymous parallelism ("The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want"), antithetic parallelism ("The wicked borrow and do not repay, but the righteous give generously"), and synthetic parallelism ("The law of the Lord is perfect, refreshing the soul"). These three types of parallelism, identified by Lowth, remain the standard classification in biblical studies.


Parallelism is also central to oral traditions across cultures. West African griot poetry, for example, uses parallel structures to build narrative momentum. The Zulu praise poem (izibongo) relies on parallel clauses to enumerate the qualities of the person being praised. In each case, parallelism serves the same function: it creates a predictable rhythm that aids both composition and memorisation, allowing long poems to be transmitted orally across generations.
 The connection between parallelism and oral tradition underscores the deep cognitive roots of the parallelism constraint: it is not an arbitrary convention but a feature of how human memory and language production work together.

### Parallelism and the SAT, ACT, and other standardised tests

Standardised tests in the United States frequently test parallelism. The SAT Writing and Language section includes questions that require students to identify and fix faulty parallelism. The ACT English section does the same. These questions typically present a sentence with a non-parallel list and ask the student to choose the version that creates parallel structure.

Test preparation materials often present parallelism as a "trick" or "formula" that can be learned quickly. While the basic principle is simple (match the pattern), applying it consistently requires practice. Students who have internalised the parallelism constraint through extensive reading and writing perform better on these questions than students who have memorised a rule without understanding the underlying principle.

### Parallelism and genre

The strength of the parallelism constraint varies by genre. Academic writing demands strict parallelism. Journalism favours it but allows exceptions for variety. Creative fiction uses parallelism strategically, sometimes breaking it deliberately to create a sense of disruption or urgency. Poetry heightens parallelism into a structural principle. Advertising exploits parallelism for catchiness and memorability.

Understanding genre-based variation in parallelism expectations helps writers make appropriate choices. A legal brief that violates parallelism may be seen as poorly drafted. A novel that maintains strict parallelism in dialogue may make characters sound robotic. The key is to understand the convention well enough to follow it when it matters and break it when it serves a purpose.
 Skilled writers like Toni Morrison and Virginia Woolf sometimes deliberately break parallelism to create stream-of-consciousness effects that mirror the way thought actually works. The decision to maintain or break parallelism is a stylistic choice that should be made consciously, not accidentally.

### Parallelism and coordination vs. subordination

The parallelism constraint applies to coordination (linking items with and, but, or) but not to subordination (embedding one clause inside another). "She went to the store because she needed milk" does not require parallelism between "she went to the store" and "she needed milk" because the relationship is subordinate, not coordinate. This distinction is important because students sometimes overapply the parallelism constraint to subordinate clauses, creating awkward constructions in an attempt to make everything parallel.

The difference between coordination and subordination is itself a grammatical concept that students must understand in order to apply parallelism correctly. Coordination links equals; subordination links a dependent element to an independent one. Parallelism is a constraint on coordination, not on subordination. "She enjoys reading, writing, and to study" is faulty parallelism (coordination). "She enjoys reading when she has free time" is not a parallelism issue (subordination). Teaching students to distinguish between coordination and subordination before teaching parallelism prevents this overapplication and makes parallelism instruction more effective.





## Linguistic theory [Master]

**Coordination as a syntactic operation.** In generative syntax, coordination is typically analyzed as the merging of two or more constituents of the same category under a coordination phrase (CoordP). The coordinate structure has the form [CoordP [XP and [YP]]], where XP and YP must be of the same category. This **category matching requirement** is the syntactic foundation of parallelism.

Chomsky (1957) noted that coordination is one of the few constructions that reveals constituent structure. "She [VP reads books] and [VP writes articles]" shows that "reads books" and "writes articles" are both VPs, because coordination requires matching categories. The ungrammaticality of "She [VP reads books] and [NP the newspaper]" follows from the category mismatch.

**The semantic basis of parallelism.** Parallelism is not purely syntactic. Gazdar et al. (1985) propose that coordination requires **semantic category matching** rather than syntactic category matching. Under this analysis, "She is [AP fond of tea] and [NP a lover of coffee]" is acceptable because both phrases denote properties of the subject, even though they have different syntactic categories (AP vs. NP). This predicts a broader range of acceptable coordinations than a purely syntactic account.

**Processing and parallelism.** Psycholinguistic research shows that parallel structures are processed more efficiently than non-parallel ones. Frazier et al. (2000) found that readers expect continuation in the same syntactic category after processing the first conjunct. When the second conjunct violates this expectation (faulty parallelism), processing time increases. This supports the view that parallelism is not merely a stylistic convention but reflects a cognitive preference for structural consistency.

**Cross-linguistic parallelism.** The parallelism requirement is not unique to English. Coordinate structures in most languages require matching categories, though the specific constraints vary. In languages with rich case systems (German, Russian), coordinated NPs must agree in case. In languages with serial verb constructions, parallelism between verb phrases is the default. The universal tendency toward structural parallelism in coordination suggests that it reflects a fundamental property of the human language faculty.

**Parallelism and information structure.** Parallel structures tend to distribute information evenly across conjuncts, which affects focus and information structure. In "She bought books and records," the two conjuncts receive equal informational weight. In "She bought books and she also bought records," the second conjunct is heavier, which can be used to give it more prominence. The choice between parallel and non-parallel coordination can thus be an information-structural decision, not merely a grammatical one.

**Parallelism in Optimality Theory.** In Optimality Theory (Prince and Smolensky, 1993), parallelism can be modeled as a ranked constraint: FAITH-PARALLEL requires that coordinated constituents match in category. This constraint competes with other constraints (such as economy of expression), and the outcome depends on the ranking. In formal registers, FAITH-PARALLEL ranks high, producing strict parallelism. In casual speech, it ranks lower, allowing non-parallel coordinations that satisfy other constraints.

**Parallelism and language acquisition.** Children acquire the parallelism constraint relatively early. By age four or five, children consistently produce parallel coordinations in their spontaneous speech. This early acquisition suggests that the parallelism constraint is part of the core grammatical knowledge that children bring to the language learning task, rather than a convention that must be explicitly taught.


### The Coordinate Structure Constraint in detail

Ross (1967) Coordinate Structure Constraint (CSC) has two parts. The first prohibits extracting a subpart of one conjunct: you cannot say "What did she buy books and sell __?" because "what" has been extracted from only the second conjunct. The second prohibits extracting a subpart of the coordinate structure as a whole: you cannot say "What did she buy __ and sell __?" if the two gaps correspond to different referents.

The CSC is one of the most robust syntactic constraints in English and in many other languages. It has been the subject of extensive research in syntactic theory, and various proposals have been made to explain why it holds. Some researchers argue that it follows from the architecture of the syntactic computation: coordination creates a constituent, and extraction out of a constituent is subject to island constraints. Others argue that it follows from semantic considerations: coordinating two items creates a semantic unit, and extracting from a semantic unit produces an interpretation problem.

Notable exceptions to the CSC exist. Across-the-board (ATB) extraction allows extraction from all conjuncts simultaneously: "What did she buy __ and sell __?" is acceptable because the extraction applies to both conjuncts equally. ATB extraction maintains the parallelism of the coordinate structure because the gap appears in the same position in each conjunct.

### Parallelism and gapping

Gapping is a construction in which the verb is omitted from the second conjunct of a coordinate structure: "She reads books, and he magazines." The omitted verb ("reads") is recovered from the first conjunct. Gapping works precisely because the coordinate structure is parallel: both conjuncts have the same syntactic structure, and the reader can recover the omitted material by analogy with the first conjunct.

If the conjuncts are not parallel, gapping fails. "She reads books, and he in the library" is uninterpretable because "in the library" is a prepositional phrase, not a noun phrase that can fill the object position of the gapped verb. This demonstrates that gapping depends on parallelism for its interpretability.

### Parallelism and the architecture of grammar

The fact that parallelism operates at multiple levels of grammar (morphological, phrasal, clausal, discourse) raises questions about the architecture of the grammar. Is there a single parallelism constraint that applies at all levels, or are there separate constraints at each level?

Most syntactic theories adopt the single-constraint view, treating parallelism as a general principle that applies whenever coordination occurs, regardless of the level of structure. This is economical in that it posits a single mechanism rather than multiple ones. However, it raises the question of why parallelism sometimes appears to be stronger at some levels than others. For example, morphological parallelism (matching degree forms of adjectives) seems more dispensable than clausal parallelism (matching clause structures in coordinate sentences).



**The role of parallelism in parsing.** Garden-path sentences sometimes exploit violations of parallelism expectations. "The dog walked into the room and the cat played with the mouse" is parsed easily because the two clauses are parallel. "The dog walked into the room and the cat was what the mouse avoided" is harder to parse because the second clause violates the expectation of parallelism set by the first. This processing asymmetry provides further evidence that parallelism is a real-time parsing strategy, not just a stylistic preference.
 Neuroimaging studies have shown that non-parallel coordinations activate brain regions associated with conflict monitoring and reanalysis, while parallel coordinations are processed in regions associated with automatic, fluent language processing. This neural evidence supports the behavioural finding that parallelism reduces cognitive load during reading.

## Historical context [Master]

Parallelism has been recognized as a rhetorical and grammatical principle since antiquity. Aristotle's *Rhetoric* (4th century BCE) discusses **parisosis** (clauses of equal length) and **paromoiosis** (similar clause structure) as devices for creating persuasive prose. The Latin rhetoricians called parallel constructions **membrum** (balanced clauses) and **isocolon** (clauses of equal length and structure).

In English, parallelism was a central principle of classical prose style. The King James Bible (1611) is a masterclass in parallel structure: "To every thing there is a season, and a time to every purpose under the heaven: a time to be born, and a time to die; a time to plant, and a time to pluck up that which is planted." The repeated "a time to X, and a time to Y" structure creates the rhythm and memorability for which this passage is known.

The 18th-century grammarians did not explicitly formulate a "parallelism rule" as such. The principle was taught implicitly through the study of classical rhetoric and through models of good prose. The explicit treatment of parallelism as a grammatical requirement emerged in 20th-century composition pedagogy, where it became a standard topic in handbooks and textbooks.

The term "parallel structure" or "parallel construction" became standard in American English composition textbooks in the mid-20th century. Strunk and White's *The Elements of Style* (1959) devotes a full section to parallel construction, giving it the status of a fundamental principle: "Express coordinate ideas in similar form."

The teaching of parallelism as an error-avoidance strategy (identifying and fixing "faulty parallelism") is largely a 20th-century development, reflecting the influence of standardized testing and composition pedagogy on grammar instruction.

In the 19th century, parallelism received scholarly attention from Robert Lowth (1753), who identified **parallelismus membrorum** (parallel structure of clauses) as a defining feature of Hebrew poetry. Lowth's work on Biblical parallelism influenced both biblical scholarship and the broader study of literary structure. His observation that Hebrew poetry uses three types of parallelism -- synonymous (same idea in different words), antithetic (contrasting ideas), and synthetic (completing ideas) -- established a framework that is still used today. The identification of parallel structure as a feature of Hebrew poetry influenced the analysis of parallelism in other literary traditions, including Greek, Arabic, and Sanskrit poetry.

The relationship between grammatical parallelism and rhetorical parallelism has been explored by Kenneth Burke, who argued in *A Rhetoric of Motives* (1950) that parallelism is a form of identification -- a way of showing that two things belong together. Burke's analysis connects grammatical parallelism to the broader rhetorical concept of consubstantiality, the idea that shared form creates shared identity. Burke insight was that any rhetorical device that creates formal similarity also creates a sense that the related items belong together, which is the foundation of persuasive identification.
