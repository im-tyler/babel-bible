## Intuition [Beginner]

Four common errors weaken writing. Each has a simple fix.

**1. Sentence fragments** -- an incomplete sentence presented as if it were complete.

- Fragment: "Because it was raining." (missing the main clause -- what happened?)
- Fix: "Because it was raining, we stayed inside."
- Fragment: "Walking to the store." (no subject)
- Fix: "I was walking to the store."

The test: can the words stand alone as a complete sentence? If not, they are a fragment.

**2. Run-on sentences** -- two independent clauses jammed together without proper punctuation or conjunctions.

- Run-on: "It was late we went home."
- Fix: "It was late, so we went home." (comma + conjunction)
- Fix: "It was late; we went home." (semicolon)
- Fix: "It was late. We went home." (period)

A **comma splice** is a type of run-on where only a comma joins the clauses:
- Comma splice: "It was late, we went home."

**3. Dangling modifiers** -- a descriptive phrase that does not attach to the right noun.

- Dangling: "Walking down the street, the trees looked beautiful." (The trees were walking?)
- Fix: "Walking down the street, I thought the trees looked beautiful."
- Dangling: "Having finished dinner, the TV was turned on." (The TV finished dinner?)
- Fix: "Having finished dinner, we turned on the TV."

**4. Misplaced modifiers** -- a modifier placed too far from the word it modifies.

- Misplaced: "She almost ate all the cookies." (She almost ate them but did not?)
- Fix: "She ate almost all the cookies." (She ate nearly every one.)
- Misplaced: "I found a gold watch walking through the park." (The watch was walking?)
- Fix: "Walking through the park, I found a gold watch."
 The principle is to place the modifier next to the word it modifies, so the reader can immediately see the connection between the modifier and the modified word.

## Visual [Beginner]

### Why these errors matter

These four errors are among the most common in student writing, and they are also among the most important to fix. Fragments confuse the reader by presenting incomplete thoughts as complete ones. Run-ons overwhelm the reader by jamming too many ideas together without proper boundaries. Dangling modifiers create unintentional comedy by attributing actions to the wrong noun. Misplaced modifiers create ambiguity that can change the meaning of the sentence. Learning to identify and fix these errors is one of the most valuable skills a writer can develop.
 By mastering the identification and correction of these four errors, writers can produce prose that is clearer, more precise, and more effective.



```
FRAGMENT TEST:
  Can it stand alone as a complete sentence?
  Does it have a subject + verb + complete thought?

  "Because it was raining."         --> FRAGMENT (subordinating conjunction)
  "Which was very interesting."     --> FRAGMENT (relative pronoun start)
  "Running through the field."      --> FRAGMENT (no subject)
  "She ran to the store."           --> COMPLETE SENTENCE


RUN-ON vs. CORRECT:

  RUN-ON:     "It was late we went home."
                              (nothing between clauses)

  COMMA SPLICE: "It was late, we went home."
                              (only a comma -- not enough)

  FIXED:      "It was late, so we went home."   (comma + conjunction)
              "It was late; we went home."       (semicolon)
              "It was late. We went home."       (period)

  "Having studied all night, the exam was easy."

   This phrase describes who studied -- but "the exam" is the subject.

  OR:     "On Tuesdays, she eats only vegetables."
```

## Worked example [Beginner]

**1. Fix the fragment: "After the game ended. We went out for pizza."**
- "After the game ended" is a dependent clause (starts with "After").
- Fix: "After the game ended, we went out for pizza." (combine into one sentence)
- Alternative fix: "The game ended. We went out for pizza." (remove "After" to make it independent)

**2. Fix the run-on: "The concert was amazing we danced all night"**
- Two independent clauses with nothing between them.
- Fix: "The concert was amazing. We danced all night." (period)
- Fix: "The concert was amazing; we danced all night." (semicolon)
- Fix: "The concert was amazing, and we danced all night." (comma + conjunction)

**3. Fix the dangling modifier: "Hungry from the hike, the sandwich tasted wonderful."**
- "Hungry from the hike" should describe a person, not "the sandwich."
- Fix: "Hungry from the hike, I thought the sandwich tasted wonderful."
- Fix: "Hungry from the hike, we found that the sandwich tasted wonderful."

**4. Fix the misplaced modifier: "He nearly finished all fifty problems in minutes."**
- "Nearly finished" means he did not quite finish. Is that the intended meaning?
- If he completed almost all problems: "He finished nearly all fifty problems in minutes."

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Prescriptive vs. descriptive perspectives

Prescriptive grammar treats these errors as violations of fixed rules. Descriptive linguistics recognises that fragments, comma splices, and dangling modifiers are common in natural language and follow systematic patterns. The prescriptive rules are conventions of formal edited prose, not universal laws of grammar. Understanding the difference helps writers make informed choices about when to follow the rules and when to deviate for effect.

### World Englishes and common errors

Different varieties of English have different patterns of common errors. Indian English may use comma splices more freely than British or American English. Singapore Colloquial English uses different patterns of clause combining that may appear as run-ons in Standard English. African American Vernacular English has different conventions for sentence structure that may produce patterns considered errors in Standard English. These differences reflect the legitimate grammatical systems of different English varieties, not deficits in writing skill.

### ESL transfer errors

ESL learners make systematic errors that reflect transfer from their first language. Speakers of languages without articles (Chinese, Japanese, Russian) may produce fragment-like constructions. Speakers of languages that allow comma splices (German, French) may overuse commas between independent clauses. Speakers of languages with different modifier placement (Japanese, Korean) may produce misplaced modifiers. Understanding transfer effects helps teachers address these errors more effectively.
 By anticipating which errors students from specific language backgrounds are likely to make, teachers can design targeted instruction that addresses these patterns directly.

### Common errors in academic writing

Academic writing is particularly susceptible to certain errors. Fragments occur when students write complex sentences with multiple subordinate clauses and lose track of the main clause. Run-ons occur when students try to pack too much information into a single sentence. Dangling modifiers occur when students use passive voice with participial introductions. Misplaced modifiers occur when students place adverbs in ambiguous positions.

### Common errors in business writing

Business writing is susceptible to fragments (particularly in bullet points and presentations), comma splices (in hastily written email), and misplaced modifiers (in technical specifications). The consequences of errors in business writing can be significant: a misplaced modifier in a contract can create legal ambiguity, and a comma splice in a technical specification can cause confusion about the procedure being described.

### Common errors in legal writing

Legal writing requires extreme precision, and common errors can have serious consequences. A misplaced modifier in a statute can change the legal meaning of a provision. A comma splice in a contract can create ambiguity about the parties obligations. Legal drafting guides devote considerable attention to avoiding these errors.



**Sentence fragment.** A sentence fragment is a string of words punctuated as a sentence that lacks one or more of the requirements for a complete sentence: a subject, a finite verb, or an independent clause structure. Common sources:

| Type | Pattern | Example |
|------|---------|---------|
| Subordinate clause fragment | Dependent clause standing alone | *Because she left early.* |
| Relative clause fragment | Relative clause standing alone | *Which surprised everyone.* |
| Participial phrase fragment | -ing or -en phrase standing alone | *Running through the field.* |
| Appositive fragment | Noun phrase standing alone | *A tall woman with red hair.* |
| Infinitive phrase fragment | to + verb phrase standing alone | *To finish the project on time.* |

Not all fragments are errors. Fragments are conventional in dialogue, advertising, informal writing, and some literary styles. They are errors only in formal academic and professional prose.

**Run-on sentence.** A run-on sentence (also called a **fused sentence**) joins two or more independent clauses without appropriate punctuation or conjunctions. A **comma splice** is a subset of run-on in which only a comma separates the clauses.

| Error type | Pattern | Example |
|------------|---------|---------|
| Fused sentence | IC IC (nothing) | *It was late we went home.* |
| Comma splice | IC, IC (comma only) | *It was late, we went home.* |

Corrections require one of: (a) period, (b) semicolon, (c) comma + coordinating conjunction, or (d) subordination of one clause.

**Dangling modifier.** A dangling modifier is an introductory participial, infinitive, or elliptical clause whose implied subject does not match the subject of the main clause. Formally: the modifier requires a semantic agent, and the syntactic subject of the main clause is not a plausible agent for the action described by the modifier.

- "Having finished dinner, the TV was turned on." -- "Having finished dinner" implies a person as agent, but the subject is "the TV."

**Misplaced modifier.** A misplaced modifier is placed too far from the word it modifies, creating ambiguity or an unintended reading. The principle of **adjacency** states that modifiers should be placed as close as possible to the word they modify.

## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

1. **Acceptable fragments.** Not all fragments are errors. Imperative sentences ("Stop.") and elliptical responses ("Yes." / "To the store.") are complete in their contexts. Literary and rhetorical fragments ("Never again.") are conventional in fiction, poetry, and persuasive writing. Advertising uses fragments deliberately ("Now with 20% more."). The error is the **unintentional** fragment in formal prose.


### Fragments in advertising and marketing

Advertising uses fragments deliberately for impact. "New and improved." "Now with 20% more." "Limited time only." These fragments are not errors because they are intentional and because the conventions of advertising allow for them. Understanding the difference between intentional and unintentional fragments helps writers make deliberate choices about when to use fragments and when to avoid them.

### Fragments in fiction

Fiction writers use fragments to create effects. Short fragments create a sense of urgency or incompleteness. "He turned. The door. The darkness beyond." The fragments mimic the rapid perception of someone in a stressful situation. Long fragments create a sense of trailing off or uncertainty. "She wanted to say something. Anything that would make it better. But." The fragment at the end suggests the inability to complete the thought.

### Fragments in poetry

Poetry uses fragments extensively. Many poems consist entirely of fragments, using the space between words and lines to create meaning that would be lost in prose. E.E. Cummings is famous for his use of fragments, creating poems that challenge the reader to reconstruct the grammatical structure from fragments of language. The use of fragments in poetry demonstrates that grammar rules are conventions, not laws, and that skilled writers can break them for artistic effect.

### Run-ons in stream-of-consciousness writing

Stream-of-consciousness writing uses run-ons deliberately to represent the flow of thought. James Joyce *Ulysses* contains sentences that span pages without proper punctuation. Virginia Woolf used run-ons to create the impression of thought flowing without interruption. These literary uses of run-ons demonstrate that the prohibition against run-ons is a convention of formal prose, not a universal law of language.

### Dangling modifiers in academic writing

Dangling modifiers are particularly common in academic writing because academic writers frequently use passive voice with participial introductions. "Having been conducted over three years, the results were significant" is a dangling modifier because "the results" did not conduct themselves -- the study was conducted. The fix is to use active voice: "The researchers conducted the study over three years and found significant results." Or to supply the agent: "Having conducted the study over three years, the researchers found significant results."

### Misplaced modifiers in legal writing

Misplaced modifiers in legal writing can have serious consequences. "The tenant shall not keep pets that are dangerous on the premises" is ambiguous: does "on the premises" modify "keep" (the keeping is on the premises) or "dangerous" (the dangerous pets are on the premises)? The fix is to restructure the sentence to eliminate the ambiguity: "The tenant shall not keep dangerous pets on the premises."

### The squinting modifier

A squinting modifier (also called an ambiguous modifier) is one that could modify either the word before it or the word after it. "She told him yesterday she was leaving" -- does "yesterday" modify "told" or "was leaving"? The fix is to move the modifier to an unambiguous position: "Yesterday, she told him she was leaving" or "She told him she was leaving yesterday." Squinting modifiers are a subtype of misplaced modifier that is particularly common in spoken English, where intonation disambiguates.

### Split infinitives

Split infinitives occur when a word (usually an adverb) is placed between "to" and the verb: "to boldly go." The prohibition against split infinitives is a prescriptive rule that was introduced in the 19th century by analogy with Latin, where infinitives cannot be split because they are single words. In English, split infinitives are fully grammatical and are often clearer than the alternatives. "To boldly go" is more natural than "to go boldly" or "boldly to go." The prohibition against split infinitives is an example of a prescriptive rule that does not reflect the grammar of English.
 The prohibition is one of the most widely criticised prescriptive rules in English grammar, and most modern style guides accept split infinitives when they improve clarity.

### Common errors and revision strategies

Effective revision strategies can help writers identify and fix common errors. Reading aloud helps identify fragments and run-ons because the ear catches what the eye misses. Checking each sentence for a subject and verb helps identify fragments. Checking each sentence boundary for proper punctuation helps identify run-ons. Checking each introductory phrase against the main clause subject helps identify dangling modifiers. Checking each modifier against the word it modifies helps identify misplaced modifiers. These strategies are simple but effective.
 and should be part of every writer routine. The goal of proofreading is not perfection but reducing error frequency to a level that does not interfere with communication.

### The role of grammar checkers

Grammar checkers can help identify common errors, but they are not reliable enough to replace careful editing. Grammar checkers often flag correct sentences as errors and miss genuine errors. They struggle with complex sentences where the error is ambiguous. They cannot determine whether a fragment is intentional or unintentional. They cannot determine whether a run-on is a deliberate stylistic choice or an error. Grammar checkers are useful as a first pass, but careful human editing is still necessary.


2. **The comma splice register continuum.** The comma splice exists on a register continuum. It is common and largely unremarked in informal writing (personal email, text messages, social media). It appears in literary fiction as a stylistic device (joining short, related clauses for flow). It is conventionally an error only in formal edited prose (academic writing, professional reports, journalism). Treating it as always and everywhere wrong misrepresents actual usage.

3. **Dangling modifiers and implicit subjects.** The dangling modifier problem arises because introductory modifying clauses have **implicit subjects** -- the reader infers who or what is performing the action. When the main clause subject does not match the implied agent, the result is a dangling modifier. This can be subtle with passive voice: "Having been thoroughly cleaned, the room looked spotless" is acceptable (the room was cleaned), but "Having been thoroughly cleaned, the janitor was satisfied" is a dangler (the janitor was not cleaned).

4. **Misplaced modifiers and scope ambiguity.** Misplaced modifiers create scope ambiguity. "She almost failed every exam" has two readings: (a) she came close to failing every single exam, and (b) she failed nearly every exam. The placement of "almost" determines scope -- before "failed" (scope over the verb) vs. before "every" (scope over the quantifier). This is not merely a punctuation issue but a semantic one.

### Common errors in different genres

The frequency of common errors varies across genres. Student writing has the highest rate of fragments and run-ons. Informal writing (email, social media) has the highest rate of comma splices. Academic writing has the highest rate of dangling modifiers (because of frequent use of passive voice with participial introductions). Business writing has the highest rate of misplaced modifiers (because of the need to pack complex information into sentences). Understanding these genre-specific patterns helps editors and teachers target their instruction.

### Teaching common errors

Teaching common errors is most effective when it is embedded in the context of writing rather than taught as isolated exercises. Students who can identify clause boundaries can fix fragments and run-ons. Students who understand the relationship between introductory modifiers and main clause subjects can fix dangling modifiers. Students who understand the principle of adjacency can fix misplaced modifiers. The key is to teach the grammatical principles behind the errors rather than the errors themselves.

## Linguistic theory [Master]

**Fragments in syntax.** From a generative perspective, sentence fragments are not "incomplete sentences" but rather sentences with phonologically null (elided) material. Merchant (2004) proposes that fragments like "To the store" (in answer to "Where are you going?") involve **sluicing** or related ellipsis phenomena: the full structure (*I am going to the store*) is syntactically present but unpronounced. Under this analysis, fragments are grammatically complete at the level of syntactic representation; what is "missing" is only at the phonological level.

This analysis predicts that fragments should obey syntactic constraints, which they do. A fragment answer to a question must match the category of the questioned element: "Where are you going?" --> "To the store" (PP), not *"The store" (NP). The fragment inherits syntactic properties from the antecedent clause.

**Run-ons and coordination.** The comma splice can be analyzed as **asyndetic coordination** -- coordination without an overt conjunction. Asyndetic coordination is grammatical in many languages and in some English constructions ("I came, I saw, I conquered"). The prohibition against asyndetic coordination of independent clauses in formal English is a prescriptive convention, not a universal grammatical constraint. Corpus studies (Biber et al., 1999) find comma splices in all written registers except the most carefully edited prose.

**Dangling modifiers and control theory.** The dangling modifier problem is related to the syntactic notion of **control**. In a sentence like "Having finished dinner, we turned on the TV," the implicit subject of "having finished" is controlled by the matrix subject "we." This is an instance of **obligatory subject control** -- the participial clause requires its subject to be co-referential with the matrix subject. Dangling modifiers arise when this control requirement is violated: the matrix subject is not a suitable controller for the participial clause.

The interesting theoretical question is whether the acceptability of dangling modifiers is gradient. Some danglers are plainly unacceptable ("Having finished dinner, the TV was turned on"), but others are marginal or acceptable to many speakers ("Having finished dinner, the evening was still young" -- where "the evening" is not an agent but a temporal frame). Kjellmer (2003) found that acceptability correlates with the degree of subject mismatch: mismatches involving animate subjects for inanimate modifiers are judged worse than those involving temporal or abstract subjects.

The gradient nature of acceptability suggests that the dangling modifier is not a categorical error but a matter of degree. This finding challenges the prescriptive approach to grammar instruction, which treats dangling modifiers as always wrong. A more nuanced approach recognises that some dangling modifiers are more acceptable than others and that the degree of acceptability depends on the semantic relationship between the modifier and the main clause subject.

### Fragments and ellipsis in discourse

Fragments in discourse are not errors but instances of ellipsis. When someone asks "Where are you going?" and the response is "To the store," the fragment is a grammatically complete answer that uses ellipsis to avoid repeating information that is already in the discourse context. Merchant (2004) analysis of fragments as ellipsis phenomena explains why fragments obey syntactic constraints even though they appear to be incomplete.

### Run-ons and genre

The acceptability of run-ons varies by genre. In academic writing, run-ons are almost always unacceptable. In fiction, run-ons are common and often deliberate. In email and text messages, comma splices are so common that they are barely noticed. In legal writing, run-ons can create serious ambiguity. These genre differences show that the prohibition against run-ons is not a grammatical law but a convention that applies more strictly in some genres than in others.

### Dangling modifiers across languages

Not all languages have dangling modifiers in the same way. In pro-drop languages like Spanish and Italian, the subject of a participial clause can be omitted without creating ambiguity because the verb agreement indicates the subject. In languages with different modifier placement (like Japanese), the concept of a dangling modifier does not arise because the modifier always precedes the noun it modifies. These cross-linguistic differences are important for understanding why ESL learners from certain language backgrounds are more prone to dangling modifier errors.

### Common errors and language change

The errors discussed in this unit are not static; their status changes over time. What is considered an error in one century may be accepted in the next. The split infinitive was once considered a serious error; today it is widely accepted. The comma splice was once common in all registers; today it is restricted to informal writing. The dangling modifier was not discussed as an error until the 20th century. These changes reflect the evolving nature of prescriptive grammar conventions.

### Common errors in scientific writing

Scientific writing is susceptible to specific patterns of errors. Fragments occur when methods sections are written as bullet-point lists without complete sentences. Dangling modifiers occur when passive voice is used with participial introductions: "Having been measured, the data were analysed" is a dangling modifier because "the data" did not measure themselves. The fix is to use active voice or to supply the agent: "After the researchers measured the samples, the data were analysed."
 The key principle in scientific writing is to ensure that the subject of each sentence is a plausible agent for the action described by any introductory modifier.

### The comma splice in literature

Many celebrated writers have used comma splices deliberately. Jane Austen, Charles Dickens, and Ernest Hemingway all used comma splices in their writing. In each case, the comma splice was a stylistic choice, not an error. The comma splice can create a flowing, conversational rhythm that the period disrupts. This is why the comma splice is more common in fiction than in academic writing: fiction values rhythm and flow, while academic writing values precision and clarity.

### Modifier placement and meaning

Modifier placement is not just a matter of grammar; it affects meaning. "Only she told him the truth" means she was the only person who told him the truth. "She only told him the truth" means she told him nothing but the truth. "She told only him the truth" means she told the truth to no one else. "She told him only the truth" means she told him nothing but the truth. Each placement of "only" creates a different meaning, and the writer must choose the placement that conveys the intended meaning.
 This example demonstrates why modifier placement is not just a matter of grammatical correctness but of semantic precision. A misplaced modifier does not just violate a rule; it changes what the sentence means.


### Common errors and proofreading

Proofreading is the final step in the writing process, and it is the step where common errors are most likely to be caught. Effective proofreading strategies include reading the text aloud (which helps identify fragments and run-ons), reading the text backwards sentence by sentence (which helps identify errors by removing the distraction of content), and checking each sentence for the four common errors discussed in this unit. These strategies are simple but effective, and they should be part of every writer routine.

### Fragments in dialogue

In fiction, fragments are conventional in dialogue because they mirror the way people actually speak. "Where are you going?" "Out." "Out where?" "Just out." The fragment "Out" and "Just out" are not errors in dialogue because they reflect natural speech patterns. Writers who write dialogue in complete, grammatically correct sentences create dialogue that sounds stiff and unnatural. The key is to distinguish between narrative prose (where fragments are generally errors) and dialogue (where fragments are conventional).

### Run-ons and coordination

Run-ons can be seen as a failure of coordination. In standard English, coordinate clauses must be joined by a coordinating conjunction, a semicolon, or a period. When the writer omits the conjunction and the punctuation, the result is a run-on. The fix is to add the appropriate conjunction or punctuation. The choice of conjunction or punctuation determines the relationship between the clauses: a period creates two separate assertions, a semicolon creates a single complex assertion, and a conjunction specifies the logical relationship (addition, contrast, cause, etc.).

### Common errors and standardised tests

Standardised tests (SAT, ACT, GRE, TOEFL) frequently test the ability to identify and fix common errors. Questions about fragments, run-ons, dangling modifiers, and misplaced modifiers appear regularly on these tests. Preparation for these tests should focus on the grammatical principles behind the errors rather than on memorising specific error patterns. Students who understand clause structure, modifier placement, and punctuation rules will perform well on these questions regardless of the specific sentences they encounter.

### The dangling modifier and scientific writing

The dangling modifier is particularly problematic in scientific writing because of the frequent use of passive voice. "After being heated to 100 degrees, the solution was stirred" is technically a dangling modifier because "the solution" was not heated -- the solution was heated by the researcher. However, in scientific writing, this construction is widely accepted because the agent (the researcher) is understood from context. The acceptability of dangling modifiers in scientific writing illustrates the genre-specific nature of prescriptive grammar rules.

### Common errors and digital communication

Digital communication has changed the frequency and nature of common errors. Text messages and social media posts are full of fragments and run-ons, which are accepted as part of the informal register. Auto-correct features can introduce new errors (such as replacing a correct word with a wrong one). The informal conventions of digital communication are spreading to other genres, creating tension between the conventions of formal writing and the conventions of digital communication.

### Sentence combining as a pedagogical tool

Sentence combining exercises help students learn to avoid fragments and run-ons by teaching them how to combine clauses using subordination, coordination, and appropriate punctuation. Students are given a set of simple sentences and asked to combine them into more complex sentences. This exercise teaches students to identify clause boundaries and to choose the appropriate method for joining clauses. Research has shown that sentence combining exercises improve writing quality more than traditional grammar instruction.

### Connections to logic

The errors discussed in this unit have logical dimensions. Fragments present incomplete propositions as if they were complete. Run-ons conflate distinct propositions without showing their logical relationship. Dangling modifiers attribute actions to the wrong logical subject. Misplaced modifiers create scope ambiguities that affect the truth conditions of the sentence. Understanding the logical dimensions of these errors helps writers produce arguments that are not just grammatically correct but logically sound.

### Connections to rhetoric

Rhetorical effectiveness depends on clarity and precision. Fragments undermine clarity by presenting incomplete thoughts. Run-ons undermine precision by jamming too many ideas together. Dangling modifiers undermine credibility by creating unintentional comedy. Misplaced modifiers undermine precision by creating ambiguity. The study of these errors is therefore part of the broader study of rhetorical effectiveness.

### Connections to psychology

Psycholinguistic research has shown that these errors affect processing difficulty. Fragments require the reader to re-parse the sentence, increasing processing time. Run-ons require the reader to hold multiple propositions in working memory without adequate boundaries. Dangling modifiers create a moment of confusion when the reader realises the subject does not match. Misplaced modifiers create genuine ambiguity that the reader must resolve using context. These processing effects explain why these errors are perceived as errors: they increase the cognitive effort required to understand the text.

### Connections to education

Teaching these errors is a standard component of writing instruction at every level. The challenge is to move students beyond rote memorisation of rules and toward a grammatical understanding of why these errors occur and how to fix them. Students who understand clause structure can identify and fix fragments and run-ons. Students who understand modifier placement can identify and fix dangling and misplaced modifiers. The key is to teach grammatical principles, not just error patterns.


### Common errors and second language acquisition

Second language learners make systematic errors that reflect the grammatical structures of their first language. Understanding these transfer effects is essential for effective language teaching. Error analysis, a branch of applied linguistics, studies the patterns of errors that second language learners make and uses these patterns to develop targeted instruction. The errors discussed in this unit are among the most common in second language writing, and targeted instruction can significantly reduce their frequency.

### The comma splice and register

The comma splice is a register-sensitive error. In formal academic writing, it is almost always unacceptable. In literary fiction, it is common and often deliberate. In email and informal writing, it is so common that many readers do not notice it. The acceptability of the comma splice depends on the register, the audience, and the writer intent. Teaching the comma splice as a register-sensitive error, rather than as an absolute error, helps students make appropriate choices in different writing contexts.

### Dangling modifiers and passive voice

The relationship between dangling modifiers and passive voice is well documented. Passive voice constructions are more likely to produce dangling modifiers because the agent is removed from the sentence, making it harder to match the modifier to the appropriate subject. "Having been written in haste, the essay contained several errors" is technically a dangling modifier because "the essay" did not write itself. However, many readers find this sentence acceptable because the passive voice makes the agent understood from context. The safest approach is to use active voice with participial introductions.


### The dangling modifier in news writing

News writing is susceptible to dangling modifiers because of the need to compress information into tight prose. "Born in a small town, the book describes her childhood" is a dangling modifier because "the book" was not born. The fix is to restructure: "Born in a small town, she describes her childhood in the book." News editors are trained to catch dangling modifiers because they can create unintended humour that undermines the credibility of the news story.

### Common errors and the writing process

The writing process approach to instruction teaches that errors should be addressed during the editing phase, not during the drafting phase. During drafting, the writer focuses on generating ideas and organising them into a coherent structure. During editing, the writer focuses on fixing grammar, punctuation, and spelling errors. This approach reduces the anxiety associated with grammar instruction and allows writers to produce more fluent, confident prose. The errors discussed in this unit are most effectively addressed during the editing phase, when the writer can review each sentence for completeness, proper punctuation, and correct modifier placement.

### The comma splice and conjunctions

The comma splice can be fixed by adding a coordinating conjunction after the comma: "It was late, we went home" becomes "It was late, so we went home." The choice of conjunction determines the logical relationship between the clauses. "And" suggests addition. "But" suggests contrast. "So" suggests consequence. "Or" suggests alternative. "For" suggests reason. "Yet" suggests contrast with surprise. "Nor" suggests negative addition. The writer must choose the conjunction that most accurately represents the intended relationship.

### Fragments and the subordinate clause

The most common type of fragment is the subordinate clause standing alone. "Because it was raining." "Although she was tired." "If you want to come." These clauses begin with subordinating conjunctions and cannot stand alone as sentences. The fix is to attach them to an independent clause: "Because it was raining, we stayed inside." "Although she was tired, she kept working." "If you want to come, you should bring a jacket."

### Misplaced modifiers and prepositional phrases

Prepositional phrases are a common source of misplaced modifiers. "I saw the man with the telescope" is ambiguous: does "with the telescope" modify "the man" (the man has a telescope) or "saw" (I used a telescope to see the man)? The fix is to restructure the sentence: "With the telescope, I saw the man" or "I saw the man who had the telescope." Prepositional phrase attachment ambiguity is one of the most studied topics in computational linguistics because it is a major source of parsing difficulty for both humans and machines.

### The role of context in error identification

Whether a construction is an error often depends on context. "Because it was raining" is a fragment in isolation but a complete utterance in dialogue ("Why did you stay inside?" "Because it was raining."). "It was late, we went home" is a comma splice in formal writing but a normal sentence in informal speech. "Walking down the street, the trees looked beautiful" is a dangling modifier in strict formal writing but may be acceptable in descriptive prose where the temporal clause sets the scene. Context determines whether a construction is an error, a stylistic choice, or a conventional usage.
 Understanding the role of context in error identification is essential for effective editing and for teaching grammar in a way that respects the complexity of natural language.



### Misplaced modifiers and the principle of adjacency

The principle of adjacency states that a modifier should be placed as close as possible to the word it modifies. This principle explains why "she almost ate all the cookies" is ambiguous: "almost" is adjacent to "ate" but should be adjacent to "all" if the intended meaning is "she ate nearly all the cookies." The principle of adjacency is not a grammatical rule but a pragmatic principle that helps writers produce clear, unambiguous sentences.

### Common errors in technical documentation

Technical documentation requires extreme precision, and common errors can have serious consequences. A misplaced modifier in a technical specification can create ambiguity about the procedure being described. A fragment in a safety warning can leave the reader unsure about what action to take. A run-on in a set of instructions can make the procedure difficult to follow. Technical writers must be meticulous in avoiding these errors, and technical editing includes a specific check for each of the four errors discussed in this unit.

### Fragments and bulleted lists

Bulleted lists in presentations and reports often contain fragments. Whether this is acceptable depends on the context and the style guide. In presentations, fragments in bullet points are conventional and acceptable. In formal reports, the acceptability of fragments in bullet points depends on the style guide: some require complete sentences for each bullet, while others allow fragments. The key is consistency: all items in a list should follow the same pattern, either all fragments or all complete sentences.

### Common errors and writing anxiety

Many students experience writing anxiety because they are afraid of making grammar errors. This anxiety can be debilitating, preventing students from writing at all. Effective writing instruction addresses this anxiety by teaching students that errors are a normal part of the writing process and that revision is where errors are fixed. The goal is to separate the drafting phase (where errors are expected) from the editing phase (where errors are fixed). This separation reduces anxiety and allows students to write more freely.
 The errors discussed in this unit are most effectively addressed during the editing phase, when the writer can review each sentence systematically for completeness, proper punctuation, and correct modifier placement.



### Connections to literary analysis

Literary writers use fragments, run-ons, and dangling modifiers deliberately for effect. Ernest Hemingway used fragments to create a sense of immediacy and incompleteness. William Faulkner used run-ons to create a sense of stream-of-consciousness overflow. The analysis of deliberate errors is a legitimate tool of literary criticism, revealing the writer approach to narrative voice and pacing.

### Connections to editing and publishing

Professional editors spend a significant portion of their time fixing these four errors. In publishing, these errors are considered markers of unprofessional writing. Manuscripts that contain frequent fragments, run-ons, dangling modifiers, and misplaced modifiers are likely to be rejected, not because the ideas are bad but because the writing lacks the precision and control that professional publication requires.


## Historical context [Master]

The concept of the "sentence fragment" as an error is a product of the prescriptive grammar tradition that emerged in 18th-century English education. Before the standardisation of English grammar instruction, fragments were used freely in all registers of writing. The King James Bible (1611), one of the most influential texts in English, is full of fragments.

### The standardisation of sentence completeness

The distinction between complete and incomplete sentences was formalised by Lindley Murray in *English Grammar* (1795), which became the standard textbook in both England and America for decades. Murray established the requirement that a sentence express a "complete thought," a criterion that is surprisingly difficult to define precisely and that continues to cause confusion in writing instruction.

### The history of run-on sentences

Run-on sentences and comma splices were not consistently treated as errors until the 19th century. Earlier writers, including Shakespeare and the translators of the King James Bible, used comma splices freely. The strict prohibition emerged as punctuation became increasingly syntactic rather than elocutionary: as punctuation marks were reinterpreted as markers of grammatical structure rather than breathing pauses, the comma role as a clause separator became more rigidly defined.

### The history of dangling modifiers

The dangling modifier was first explicitly discussed as an error by 20th-century composition textbooks. Earlier grammarians did not systematically address the mismatch between introductory modifiers and main clause subjects. The concept relies on the notion of the logical subject of a non-finite clause, which was not articulated until the development of modern syntactic theory in the mid-20th century.

### The history of misplaced modifiers

Misplaced modifiers have been discussed since the 18th century, usually under the heading of "ambiguity." George Orwell rule "If it is possible to cut a word out, always cut it out" (*Politics and the English Language*, 1946) reflects the broader concern with precision and clarity that motivates modifier placement rules.

### The 18th century and prescriptive grammar

The 18th century was the period when the errors discussed in this unit were first codified as errors. Robert Lowth *A Short Introduction to English Grammar* (1762) established many of the rules that are still taught today. Lowth approach was prescriptive: he identified patterns that he considered incorrect and prescribed alternatives. This prescriptive tradition has been enormously influential in English education and continues to shape how grammar is taught in schools.

### The 19th century and composition pedagogy

The 19th century saw the development of composition pedagogy as a distinct field. Writing instruction moved from the teaching of classical rhetoric to the teaching of practical writing skills. The errors discussed in this unit became central to composition instruction because they were perceived as markers of poor writing. Textbooks devoted increasing attention to identifying and correcting fragments, run-ons, dangling modifiers, and misplaced modifiers.

### The 20th century and the process approach

The 20th century saw a shift from prescriptive grammar to the process approach to writing instruction. Instead of teaching grammar rules in isolation, the process approach taught writing as a recursive process of planning, drafting, revising, and editing. Errors were addressed during the editing phase, not during the drafting phase. This approach reduced the anxiety associated with grammar instruction but also reduced the amount of explicit grammar teaching. The result has been a generation of writers who can write fluently but who may not have the grammatical knowledge to identify and fix their own errors.

### Connections to philosophy of language

The errors discussed in this unit connect to philosophy through the concept of meaning. Fragments present incomplete meanings. Run-ons conflate distinct meanings. Dangling modifiers create unintended meanings. Misplaced modifiers create ambiguous meanings. These problems are not just grammatical; they are semantic and pragmatic. The study of common errors is therefore part of the broader philosophical investigation of how language creates meaning.

### Connections to computer science

Automated grammar checkers use computational linguistics to identify common errors. These systems use pattern matching, statistical models, and natural language processing to detect fragments, run-ons, dangling modifiers, and misplaced modifiers. The accuracy of these systems has improved significantly in recent years, but they still make errors, particularly with complex sentences where the error is ambiguous or where the "error" is actually a deliberate stylistic choice.
