## Intuition [Beginner]

Pronouns change form depending on their job in the sentence. This is called **case**. There are two main cases to know:

**Subjective case** (when the pronoun is the subject -- doing the action):

| Subjective | |
|------------|---|
| I | I walked home. |
| you | You are late. |
| he | He left early. |
| she | She called back. |
| it | It is raining. |
| we | We won the game. |
| they | They arrived yesterday. |

**Objective case** (when the pronoun is the object -- receiving the action, or after a preposition):

| Objective | |
|-----------|---|
| me | She called me. |
| you | I saw you. |
| him | Give it to him. |
| her | Ask her. |
| it | I found it. |
| us | Join us. |
| them | Call them. |

**Quick test:** Use the pronoun alone. "She gave the book to (he/him)" -- "to him" sounds right, so it is "him."

**Who vs. whom:**
- **Who** = subjective: "Who is calling?" (who is the subject)
- **Whom** = objective: "To whom did you give it?" (whom is the object of "to")

**Pronoun-antecedent agreement:** A pronoun must match its antecedent (the noun it refers to) in number.

- Singular antecedent, singular pronoun: "The **student** turned in **her** paper."
- Plural antecedent, plural pronoun: "The **students** turned in **their** papers."
- Wrong: "The **student** turned in **their** paper." (student is singular, their is plural -- this is common in informal speech but formally incorrect for a specific singular referent)

**Vague pronoun reference:** Every pronoun should plainly point to one noun. If the reader cannot tell which noun, the reference is vague.

- Vague: "When Sarah met Lisa, **she** was nervous." (Who was nervous, Sarah or Lisa?)
- Fix: "Sarah was nervous when she met Lisa."

Pronoun case is one of the few places in English where grammar still requires attention to word form. Most English nouns do not change form depending on whether they are subjects or objects: "The dog chased the cat" and "The cat chased the dog" use the same forms of "dog" and "cat." But pronouns do change: "He chased it" vs. "It chased him." This is a survival of an older grammatical system in which all nouns changed form depending on their role in the sentence.

Understanding pronoun case helps you avoid common errors in both writing and formal speech. The two most frequent errors are using objective pronouns as subjects ("Me and him went to the store") and using subjective pronouns as objects ("between you and I"). Both errors are easy to fix once you understand the case system.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**1. Choose the correct pronoun: "(Me / I) and my brother went fishing."**
- Remove "and my brother" to test: "I went fishing" (not "Me went fishing").
- Fix: "My brother and I went fishing." (polite order: others before "I")

**2. Choose the correct pronoun: "The teacher gave (we / us) extra homework."**
- "gave" is a verb, "us" is its object = objective case.
- "The teacher gave us extra homework."

**3. Choose who or whom: "(Who / Whom) did you invite to the party?"**
- Rearrange: "You did invite whom" -- "whom" is the object of "invite."
- "Whom did you invite to the party?"

**4. Fix the agreement error: "Every player needs to bring their equipment."**
- "Every player" is singular. "Their" is plural.
- Fix: "Every player needs to bring his or her equipment."
- Alternative (increasingly accepted): "All players need to bring their equipment." (make antecedent plural)

**5. Fix the vague reference: "The professor told the student that he needed to study harder."**
- Who needs to study harder, the professor or the student?
- Fix: "The professor told the student to study harder." (remove the pronoun)
- Fix: "The professor told the student that the student needed to study harder."

**6. Choose the correct pronoun: "The winners were (they / them)."""
- After a linking verb ("were"), the pronoun that follows is a subject complement, which takes the subjective case.
- "The winners were they." (This sounds formal; in everyday speech, "It was them" is common.)

**7. Fix the vague reference: "The managers told the employees that they needed to work overtime."**
- "They" could refer to the managers or the employees.
- Fix: "The managers told the employees to work overtime." (remove the pronoun)
- Fix: "The managers told the employees that the employees needed to work overtime."

**8. Choose the correct pronoun: "No one in the class did as well as (she / her)."""
- Expand the comparison: "No one in the class did as well as she did."
- "She" is the subject of the understood verb "did," so subjective case is correct.
- "No one in the class did as well as she."

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Pronoun case** in English is a two-way system for most personal pronouns:

| Person | Subjective | Objective | Possessive |
|--------|-----------|-----------|------------|
| 1st sg | I | me | my/mine |
| 2nd sg | you | you | your/yours |
| 3rd sg masc | he | him | his |
| 3rd sg fem | she | her | her/hers |
| 3rd sg neut | it | it | its |
| 1st pl | we | us | our/ours |
| 2nd pl | you | you | your/yours |
| 3rd pl | they | them | their/theirs |

The **subjective case** is used when the pronoun functions as the subject of a finite clause, a subject complement, or a predicate nominative. The **objective case** is used when the pronoun functions as the direct object, indirect object, object of a preposition, or object of a verbals.

**Who vs. whom.** "Who" is the subjective form; "whom" is the objective form. The distinction parallels he/him:

- "Who" as subject: "Who called?" (compare "He called.")
- "Whom" as object: "Whom did you call?" (compare "You called him.")
- "Whom" after preposition: "To whom did you speak?" (compare "You spoke to him.")

In contemporary English, "whom" is receding from informal usage. "Who did you call?" is common in speech, even though "whom" is prescriptively correct.

**Pronoun-antecedent agreement** requires that a pronoun match its antecedent in person, number, and (for third-person singular) gender. The rules:

| Antecedent | Pronoun | Example |
|------------|---------|---------|
| Singular masculine | he/him/his | The boy lost his book. |
| Singular feminine | she/her/her | The girl lost her book. |
| Singular neuter | it/it/its | The car lost its hubcap. |
| Plural | they/them/their | The students lost their books. |
| Indefinite (each, every, anyone) | singular (prescriptively) | Each student must bring his or her book. |

**Vague pronoun reference** occurs when a pronoun could refer to more than one antecedent, or when the antecedent is unstated or too distant from the pronoun.

### Prescriptive and descriptive perspectives

Prescriptive grammar treats pronoun case as a strict rule: subjective pronouns for subjects, objective pronouns for objects. Under this view, "Me and him went to the store" is simply wrong, and "between you and I" is an error to be corrected.

Descriptive linguistics observes that pronoun case usage varies considerably across registers and dialects. In informal speech, objective pronouns in compound subjects are common and widely accepted. "Me and him went to the store" is a normal utterance in many spoken varieties of English. The hypercorrection "between you and I" arises from speakers who have been taught that "I" is more correct than "me" and overgeneralise this rule to prepositional objects.

Both perspectives are valuable. The prescriptive standard is important for formal writing, where readers expect conventional case usage. The descriptive observation helps teachers understand why students make certain errors and how to address them without dismissing the student dialect as simply wrong.

### Pronoun case across World Englishes

Pronoun case patterns are generally consistent across World Englishes, but there are notable variations. In Indian English, the subjective-objective distinction is sometimes levelled in compound structures. In Caribbean English Creoles, pronoun systems may differ from Standard English, with distinct forms or reduced case distinctions. In Singapore English, the pronoun system is largely standard but with some variation in reflexive usage.

These variations reflect the natural evolution of pronoun systems in different speech communities. They are not errors but features of distinct varieties of English. Understanding World English variation in pronoun case is important for teaching English in a global context, where students may speak a variety of English that differs from the standard taught in textbooks.
 Teachers should be aware of these variations and address them sensitively, recognising that standard pronoun case is a convention rather than a universal rule.

### Pronoun case and code-switching

Speakers who code-switch between English and another language may transfer pronoun case patterns from their first language. For example, languages with more extensive case systems (German, Russian, Latin) may lead speakers to be more attentive to case distinctions, while languages without case distinctions (Mandarin, Indonesian) may lead speakers to be less attentive to case in English. These transfer effects are a natural part of second language acquisition.

### ESL transfer errors and pronoun case

Second language learners from specific language backgrounds make predictable pronoun case errors. Speakers of languages without case distinctions (Mandarin, Thai) may mix subjective and objective forms because the distinction is not salient in their first language. Speakers of languages with more extensive case systems (German, Russian) may apply case rules that do not exist in English, such as using dative pronouns after certain verbs. Speakers of languages with pro-drop (Spanish, Italian, Arabic) may omit pronouns entirely, producing sentences like "Is raining" instead of "It is raining."

Effective ESL instruction addresses these transfer patterns by explicitly teaching the English pronoun case system and providing practice with the specific constructions that cause difficulty for learners from different language backgrounds.


### The who/whom distinction in modern usage

The who/whom distinction is disappearing from informal English. In most spoken varieties, "who" has replaced "whom" in all positions. "Who did you call?" is the normal form in speech, and "Whom did you call?" sounds formal or even pretentious in casual conversation. However, "whom" survives in formal writing and in fixed expressions such as "to whom it may concern" and "for whom the bell tolls."

The decline of "whom" follows a well-documented pattern in language change. When a grammatical distinction is marked by a single form pair and the distinction can be inferred from word order, the marked form (the one that requires explicit learning) tends to disappear. "Who" and "whom" follow this pattern: in most sentences, the subject-object distinction is clear from word order ("Who called you?" vs. "You called who?"), making the case marking on the pronoun redundant.
 The loss of "whom" is therefore an example of language becoming more efficient by removing a redundant marker, even though prescriptive tradition treats it as a decline in standards.

### Pronoun case in compound constructions

Compound constructions are the most common site of pronoun case errors. "Me and him went to the store" is a compound subject with objective pronouns. "Between you and I" is a compound object of a preposition with a subjective pronoun. Both errors are common in spoken English and both are stigmatized in formal writing.
 Understanding why these errors occur helps teachers address them more effectively.

The fix for compound subject errors is the isolation test: remove the other person and see which form works alone. "Me went to the store" is wrong, so "My brother and I went to the store" is correct. The fix for compound prepositional object errors is the same: "between I" is wrong, so "between you and me" is correct.

The polite convention of putting others before "I" ("my brother and I" rather than "I and my brother") is a social convention, not a grammatical rule. Both orders are grammatically correct, but "others first" is the conventional politeness formula in English.

### Pronoun case and comparisons

Comparisons with "than" and "as" often create pronoun case ambiguity. "She is taller than he" vs. "She is taller than him" -- which is correct? The answer depends on whether "than" is functioning as a conjunction (introducing a clause with an understood verb) or as a preposition (taking an object).

In formal writing, the traditional analysis treats "than" as a conjunction: "She is taller than he [is]," requiring the subjective case. In informal speech, "than" is often treated as a preposition: "She is taller than him," requiring the objective case. Both forms have a long history in English. The choice depends on register and style guide.

### Reflexive pronouns and intensive pronouns

Reflexive pronouns (myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves) serve two functions. As reflexives, they indicate that the object of a verb or preposition is the same as the subject: "She saw herself in the mirror." As intensive pronouns, they emphasise a noun or pronoun: "The president herself attended the meeting."

A common error is using reflexive pronouns as substitutes for personal pronouns in compound constructions. "The invitation was sent to John and myself" should be "The invitation was sent to John and me." The reflexive "myself" is only correct when the subject and object are the same person: "I sent the invitation myself."

### Distant antecedents and pronoun reference

A pronoun should be close enough to its antecedent that the reader can identify the connection without difficulty. When the antecedent is several sentences away, the pronoun reference becomes difficult to process. In academic writing, this is a common problem: a writer introduces a concept, discusses it for several paragraphs, and then uses a pronoun to refer back to it. The reader may have to re-read to find the antecedent.

The fix is either to repeat the noun (which is always clear) or to bring the pronoun closer to its antecedent by restructuring the passage. In formal writing, clarity is more important than variety, so repeating a noun is preferable to using a vague pronoun.
 Experienced writers develop a sense of when a pronoun reference is clear enough and when it needs to be made explicit. This sense comes from practice and from reading widely.

### Pronoun reference in literary analysis

Literary analysis often involves discussing characters, authors, and texts, creating multiple potential antecedents for pronouns. "Shakespeare explores the theme of ambition in Macbeth, showing how it leads to destruction." Does "it" refer to ambition or Macbeth? The reader can infer the intended meaning, but the reference is technically vague. Careful writers replace ambiguous pronouns with specific nouns in literary analysis, where precision of reference is essential to the argument.


### Pronoun case and possessive determiners

Possessive pronouns function as determiners when they precede a noun: "my book," "your car," "their house." In this function, they are sometimes called possessive adjectives or possessive determiners. The possessive forms (my, your, his, her, its, our, their) differ from the independent possessive pronouns (mine, yours, his, hers, its, ours, theirs) that stand alone: "The book is mine."

A common error is confusing "its" (possessive determiner) with "it's" (contraction of "it is" or "it has"). "The dog wagged its tail" uses the possessive determiner. "It's a beautiful day" uses the contraction. This confusion is purely orthographic -- the two forms sound identical in speech.
 This orthographic confusion is a modern phenomenon, arising from the standardisation of English spelling in the 18th century. Before standardisation, "its" and "it's" were not consistently distinguished.

### Pronoun case and relative clauses

Relative pronouns (who, whom, whose, which, that) introduce relative clauses and have their own case system. "Who" is subjective, "whom" is objective, and "whose" is possessive. The case of the relative pronoun is determined by its function inside the relative clause, not by its function in the main clause.
 This is a key insight: the relative clause is a grammatical island, and the pronoun case inside it is determined by the internal structure of the clause, not by the external structure of the sentence.

"The woman who called you is my neighbour" -- "who" is the subject of "called" inside the relative clause. "The woman whom you called is my neighbour" -- "whom" is the object of "called" inside the relative clause. The case changes even though the woman is the same person and the main clause structure is the same.

This distinction is one of the most difficult for students to master, because it requires analysing the structure of the relative clause independently of the main clause. The he/him test works here too: "She called you" (subjective) vs. "You called her" (objective).

### Pronoun reference and implicit antecedents

Sometimes a pronoun refers to an antecedent that is not explicitly stated but is implied by the context. "They say it will rain tomorrow" -- "they" refers to an unspecified group (perhaps weather forecasters). This is called an **impersonal pronoun** usage, and it is acceptable in informal English. In formal writing, the antecedent should be stated: "The forecasters say it will rain tomorrow."

Implicit antecedents can also create ambiguity. "In the article, it says that the economy is improving" -- "it" does not have a clear antecedent. The fix is to provide one: "The article says that the economy is improving" or "According to the article, the economy is improving."
 This error is common in student writing because students often write about texts without naming the text as the subject of the sentence.

### Pronoun case in questions and exclamations

Questions often invert the normal subject-verb order, which can make pronoun case harder to determine. "Who is at the door?" has "who" as the subject, even though the inverted order might make it look like an object. The test is to convert the question to a statement: "He is at the door" (subjective).

Exclamations can also create case ambiguity. "What a wonderful surprise it was!" uses "it" as a subjective complement after "was." The case is determined by the grammatical structure, not by the emotional force of the exclamation.

### Pronoun case and the predicate nominative

After a linking verb, the pronoun that follows is technically a subject complement (predicate nominative), which takes the subjective case. "It is I" is prescriptively correct because "I" is the subject complement, equivalent to the subject "it." In practice, "It is me" is far more common in speech and is accepted by most descriptive grammarians. "It is I" sounds formal to the point of being stilted in most contexts.

The prescriptive rule reflects the Latin analysis, where the verb "to be" takes a predicate nominative in the same case as the subject. In English, the link between the two forms has weakened, and most speakers treat the pronoun after "to be" as an object. Most style guides now accept "It is me" and "It is her" in informal contexts.





### The singular "they" and gender-neutral language

The use of singular "they" has become one of the most discussed topics in English grammar. The prescriptive tradition insists on "he or she" for singular indefinite antecedents ("Each student must bring his or her book"). The descriptive tradition observes that singular "they" has been used in English for over six centuries and is now accepted by most major style guides.

In addition to its use with indefinite antecedents, "they" is also used as a preferred pronoun for non-binary individuals who do not identify as exclusively male or female. This usage has gained widespread acceptance in the 21st century and is recognised by most major style guides, including APA, MLA, Chicago, and AP.

The evolution of singular "they" illustrates the tension between prescriptive rules and actual usage. The prescriptive prohibition has never fully succeeded in displacing singular "they" from natural English, and contemporary usage has moved decisively toward accepting it.
 Language change is a constant process, and the pronoun system is one of the areas where change is most visible. What is considered an error in one generation may become the standard in the next.

## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

1. **The singular "they."** The use of "they" for a singular antecedent ("Everyone should bring their book") has a long history in English (attested from the 14th century in writing, used by Chaucer, Shakespeare, Austen, and others). Prescriptive grammar since the 18th century has insisted on "his or her" for singular indefinite antecedents. Contemporary usage is shifting: most major style guides (APA, Chicago, MLA, AP) now accept singular "they" for indefinite antecedents, and "they" is also used as a preferred pronoun for non-binary individuals. The tension between prescription and usage remains active.

2. **Compound subjects and objects with pronouns.** The common error "Me and him went to the store" arises from a processing conflict: in isolation, "me and him" is a valid coordination of objective pronouns. The error is using objective case where subjective case is required. The diagnostic test is to remove the compound: "Me went to the store" is plainly wrong, revealing the need for "I." Similarly, "between you and I" is a **hypercorrection** -- the speaker, taught that "I" is "more correct" than "me," applies it in the objective position after the preposition "between." Correct: "between you and me."

3. **Pronoun reference and discourse structure.** Pronoun resolution depends on discourse structure, not just sentence-level grammar. In "The police arrested the suspect because he had committed a robbery," "he" resolves to "the suspect" based on pragmatic plausibility (suspects commit robberies, police do not). In "The police arrested the suspect because they had found evidence," "they" resolves to "the police" for the same reason. Syntax alone does not determine reference; pragmatics and world knowledge are involved.

4. **Case in coordinate structures.** The case of a pronoun in a coordinate structure (e.g., "he and I" as subject) is determined by the grammatical function of the entire coordinate phrase, not by the pronoun's position within it. "He and I went" = both are subjective because the entire phrase "he and I" is the subject. "between him and me" = both are objective because the entire phrase is the object of the preposition.

5. **Pronoun case and ellipsis.** In elliptical constructions, the case of a pronoun is determined by its role in the understood (but omitted) structure. "She is taller than he" is correct because the full form is "She is taller than he is," where "he" is the subject of "is." "She is taller than him" is common in speech, where "him" is treated as the object of the preposition "than." Most style guides accept both forms in informal writing but prefer the subjective case in formal writing.

6. **Reflexive pronouns and case.** Reflexive pronouns (myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, yourselves, themselves) are used when the object of a verb or preposition is the same person or thing as the subject. "She saw herself in the mirror" uses "herself" because the seer and the seen are the same person. Reflexive pronouns should not be used as substitutes for personal pronouns in subject or object position. "John and myself went to the store" is incorrect; the correct form is "John and I went to the store."

7. **Cross-linguistic comparison.** English has a reduced case system compared to many other languages. German retains four cases (nominative, accusative, dative, genitive) for all nouns, adjectives, and pronouns. Russian has six cases. Finnish has fifteen. The English two-case system for pronouns is a vestige of a much richer system that has been lost over time. Understanding this history helps explain why English pronoun case is irregular and why it is one of the few areas of English grammar where morphological form still matters.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>
"He and his sister are both excellent musicians." The pronoun is part of the compound subject and must be in the subjective case. Test: "He is an excellent musician" (not "Him is").

"whom." The pronoun is the object of the preposition "to" in the relative clause "to whom the prize was awarded." The test: "The prize was awarded to him" (objective) --> "whom."

Multiple vague references. "They" could refer to the CEO or the board. "It" (first instance) could refer to the approval or the merger. ### Pronoun case in academic writing

Academic writing demands precise pronoun usage. Vague pronoun references are a common weakness in student papers, where the writer knows what the pronoun refers to (because the writer wrote the text) but the reader does not. The solution is to replace ambiguous pronouns with the specific nouns they refer to, especially in formal writing where clarity is paramount. In student writing, vague pronoun reference is one of the most common weaknesses identified by writing instructors across all academic disciplines.

In research papers, pronoun case errors can undermine credibility. A researcher who writes "The data was analysed by my colleague and I" appears careless, which may lead readers to question the rigour of the research itself. Careful attention to pronoun case is a signal of careful thinking.
 Readers notice case errors, even if they cannot articulate the rule that was violated.
 In grant proposals, where credibility is essential, pronoun case errors can undermine the applicant professionalism and attention to detail.

### Pronoun case in legal and business writing

Legal writing requires extreme precision in pronoun reference. A contract that uses a vague pronoun ("The tenant shall pay the landlord, and he shall provide maintenance") creates ambiguity about who is responsible for what. The fix is to use the specific noun each time: "The tenant shall pay the landlord, and the landlord shall provide maintenance."

Business writing faces similar challenges. In a meeting summary, "She presented the report and they asked questions" is ambiguous if multiple people of different genders were present. "The director presented the report, and the board members asked questions" eliminates the ambiguity.

### Pronoun case and diverse authors

Pronoun usage varies across cultural and linguistic communities. Authors who write in English as a second language may use pronoun patterns that reflect their first language, creating constructions that are intelligible but non-standard. Authors from oral traditions may use pronoun reference strategies that work well in speech (where gesture and intension provide context) but are vague in writing.

Understanding these variations helps readers appreciate diverse voices and helps teachers address pronoun case errors in a way that respects the student linguistic background. The goal is not to eliminate linguistic diversity but to teach the conventions of standard written English as an additional register that students can use when the context requires it.


### Pronoun case and the writing process

Pronoun case errors often arise during the revision process, when a writer changes a sentence structure without updating the pronouns. A sentence that was originally "My colleague and I conducted the experiment" might be revised to "The experiment was conducted by my colleague and I" without the writer noticing that the change from subject to object requires a change from "I" to "me." The fix is to check all pronouns after any structural revision.

### Common pronoun case errors on standardised tests

Standardised tests in the United States frequently test pronoun case. The SAT Writing and Language section includes questions that require students to choose between subjective and objective pronouns in compound constructions. The ACT English section does the same. These questions test the same concept every time: the case of the pronoun is determined by its grammatical function in the sentence.

The most commonly tested constructions are compound subjects ("She and I went"), compound prepositional objects ("between you and me"), and who/whom in relative clauses ("the candidate whom the committee selected"). Students who understand the isolation test (remove the compound) and the he/him test (replace who/whom with he/him) can answer these questions correctly.
 The key insight is that pronoun case is determined by grammatical function, not by position in the sentence or by the sound of the pronoun.

### Pronoun reference and ambiguous gender

English third-person singular pronouns encode gender (he, she, it), which creates a potential for ambiguity when multiple referents of different genders are present. "John told Mary that he would help her" is unambiguous because "he" can only refer to John and "her" can only refer to Mary. But "John told Mary that she should call him" is also unambiguous for the same reason.

The real ambiguity arises with plural pronouns ("they") or when all referents share the same gender. "Jane told Susan that she should call her" has two possible interpretations because both referents are "she." The fix is to replace one or both pronouns with the specific names: "Jane told Susan that Susan should call Jane."

### Pronoun case and the passive voice

The passive voice changes the grammatical function of noun phrases, which affects pronoun case. "He saw them" (active) becomes "They were seen by him" (passive). The pronoun that was the subject ("he") becomes the object of the preposition "by" ("him"), and the pronoun that was the object ("them") becomes the subject ("they"). This case shift is automatic for native speakers but can be challenging for second language learners.

### Pronoun case in different sentence types

Declarative sentences follow the standard subject-verb-object pattern, making pronoun case relatively straightforward. Interrogative sentences invert the subject and verb, which can obscure the grammatical function of pronouns. Imperative sentences have an implied subject ("you"), which means pronoun case in the imperative is usually objective because the explicit pronouns are objects: "Give me the book" (not "Give I the book").

### The pronoun case system as a fossil

The English pronoun case system is a linguistic fossil: a remnant of an older, more extensive case system that has otherwise disappeared from the language. Full noun phrases in English no longer show case morphology. "The dog chased the cat" uses the same form of "dog" and "cat" regardless of which is the subject and which is the object. Only pronouns retain the distinction: "He chased it" vs. "It chased him."

This makes English pronoun case an interesting case study in language change. The case system was productive in Old English, applying to all nouns and adjectives as well as pronouns. The gradual erosion of the case system left pronouns as the only category that still marks the distinction. The system is no longer productive -- no new words enter English with case alternations -- but it persists because the pronoun forms are learned as part of a closed class.
 The irregularity of the pronoun system -- the fact that "I/me" and "he/him" are suppletive forms rather than regular alternations -- is another reason the system has survived. Irregular forms in closed classes are more resistant to language change than regular forms in open classes.



## Linguistic theory [Master]

**Case theory and pronouns.** In Government and Binding theory (Chomsky, 1981), pronouns differ from full noun phrases in that they must receive **abstract Case** (a syntactic licensing condition) but cannot be assigned **inherent Case**. Pronouns receive **structural Case** from their local structural position: nominative Case from the finite Inflection node (Tense), accusative Case from the verb or preposition that governs them. This explains the distribution of subjective vs. objective pronouns: "he" appears in subject position (assigned nominative by Tense), "him" appears in object position (assigned accusative by V or P).

The persistence of case marking only in pronouns (and not in full noun phrases, which have lost morphological case in English) reflects the **defective nature** of pronoun paradigms. Full noun phrases in English receive Case abstractly but show no overt morphology. Pronouns, as a closed class with suppletive forms, retain the morphological distinction.

**The syntax and semantics of who/whom.** The decline of "whom" in contemporary English reflects a broader trend: the loss of morphological case marking in English. "Whom" is the last productive objective case form in the interrogative/relative paradigm. In many varieties of English, "whom" has been fully replaced by "who" in all positions. The prescriptive insistence on "whom" is a case of **overt prestige** -- using "whom" signals education and formality rather than encoding a necessary grammatical distinction.

**Binding theory and pronoun reference.** Chomsky's (1981) Binding Theory provides three principles that govern the interpretation of noun phrases:

- **Principle A:** Anaphors (reflexives: myself, yourself, themselves) must be bound in their local domain. "She hurt herself" (herself is bound by she within the same clause).
- **Principle B:** Pronouns must be free in their local domain. "She hurt her" (her refers to someone other than she).
- **Principle C:** Referential expressions (full noun phrases) must be free everywhere.

These principles explain why "He said that John left" is acceptable (he and John can be different people, because the pronoun is free in its local clause) but "John said that he left" allows "he" to be John (the pronoun is free in its clause and can corefer with a name outside it).

**Vague reference and centering theory.** Centering Theory (Grosz, Joshi, and Weinstein, 1995) models how pronouns are resolved in discourse. Each utterance establishes a set of **centers** (salient discourse entities). Pronouns preferentially refer to the most salient (preferred) center. Vague reference occurs when multiple entities are equally salient, making the preferred center ambiguous. The theory predicts that pronoun resolution is easier when the preferred center of the current utterance matches the preferred center of the previous utterance (**center continuity**).
 When center continuity is maintained, pronoun resolution is fast and accurate. When center continuity is disrupted (for example, by switching to a new topic), pronoun resolution becomes slower and more error-prone.

**Pronoun resolution in computational linguistics.** Pronoun reference resolution is a major challenge in natural language processing. Coreference resolution systems must determine which entity a pronoun refers to, using syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic information. The Winograd Schema Challenge (Levesque, Davis, and Morgenstern, 2012) uses pairs of sentences that differ by a single word to test whether a system can resolve pronoun references that require commonsense reasoning: "The city councilmen refused the demonstrators a permit because they feared violence" (they = councilmen) vs. "The city councilmen refused the demonstrators a permit because they advocated violence" (they = demonstrators).

**The typology of pronoun systems.** Cross-linguistically, pronoun systems vary widely. Some languages (Mandarin, Thai) do not mark case on pronouns at all. Others (German, Russian) mark case extensively, with different forms for nominative, accusative, dative, and genitive. Some languages (Japanese, Korean) have multiple levels of politeness encoded in the pronoun system. Some languages (Spanish, Italian) allow pronoun drop (pro-drop), where the subject pronoun can be omitted because the verb inflection indicates the subject. English pronoun case represents a middle ground: a reduced two-case system that is more complex than no case at all but simpler than the full case systems found in many other languages.

## Historical context [Master]

Old English had a full case system with nominative, accusative, genitive, and dative cases for nouns and pronouns. The pronoun paradigms were richer than today: the first person singular had *ic* (nominative), *me* (accusative/dative), and *min* (genitive). The distinction between subjective and objective pronoun forms is a survival of this older case system.
 Understanding this history helps students appreciate why English pronouns have irregular forms and why the case system applies only to pronouns and not to nouns.

The loss of case morphology in English nouns occurred gradually between Old English and Middle English (roughly 11th-14th centuries), driven by phonological reduction of final syllables that had carried case endings. Pronouns, as a closed class with suppletive and often phonologically distinct forms, resisted this reduction and retained their case distinctions.

The **who/whom** distinction reflects the same historical preservation. Old English *hwa* (nominative) and *hwone* (accusative) / *hwaem* (dative) were distinct forms. These collapsed into *who* and *whom* in Middle English, with *whom* serving as the general objective form. The gradual loss of *whom* in informal speech continues the centuries-long trend of case reduction.

The **singular "they"** has been attested in English writing since the 14th century. Chaucer uses it in *The Canterbury Tales* ("And whoso fyndeth hym out of swich blame, / They wol come up"). The Oxford English Dictionary's earliest citation for singular "they" is from 1375. The prescriptive prohibition dates to the 18th century: Anne Fisher's *A New Grammar* (1745) proposed using "he" as the generic pronoun for indefinite antecedents, and this was picked up by subsequent grammarians. The prohibition has never fully succeeded in displacing singular "they" from actual usage.

Pronoun-antecedent agreement as a formal rule was codified by Lindley Murray (1795) and other 18th-century grammarians. Before this period, number agreement between pronouns and antecedents was more flexible, especially with collective nouns and indefinite antecedents.

The 18th-century grammarians were influenced by Latin grammar, which has a full case system and strict agreement rules. The attempt to impose Latin-based rules on English -- a language with a very different morphological structure -- produced some rules that fit English well (such as subject-verb agreement) and others that fit poorly (such as the prohibition on split infinitives and the insistence on "whom"). The pronoun case rules, which describe actual distinctions in English, have been more durable than the Latin-inspired rules that have no basis in English morphology.

The history of pronoun case also intersects with the history of gender in English. Old English had grammatical gender (masculine, feminine, neuter) for all nouns, not just pronouns. The loss of grammatical gender in Middle English left English with natural gender for pronouns (he for male referents, she for female referents, it for inanimate referents). This transition created the conditions for the modern debate about gender-neutral pronouns, because English no longer had a grammatical mechanism for gender-neutral singular reference. The debate over singular "they" is thus a consequence of the same historical process that eliminated case marking on English nouns: the simplification of the English morphological system over centuries of language change.
