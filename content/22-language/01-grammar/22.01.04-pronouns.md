## Intuition [Beginner]

A pronoun is a word that stands in for a noun. Instead of saying "Sarah" every time, you say "she." Instead of repeating "the bicycle," you say "it." Pronouns save you from saying the same name over and over.

"Sarah picked up **her** coat. **She** put **it** on and left." Three pronouns: "her" (whose coat), "she" (who put it on), "it" (what she put on). Without pronouns: "Sarah picked up Sarah's coat. Sarah put the coat on and Sarah left." Clunky and repetitive.

The word "pronoun" literally means "for a noun" -- it steps in when a noun has already been named. Pronouns are some of the most frequently used words in English. In fact, the ten most common words in most English texts include several pronouns: *I, you, he, she, it, we, they, me, him, her*.

Pronouns do more than just replace names. They also tell you who is speaking (*I, we*), who is being spoken to (*you*), and who is being talked about (*he, she, it, they*). They show possession (*my, your, his, her, its, our, their*). They point to things (*this, that, these, those*). They ask questions (*who, what, which*). Each type of pronoun has a specific job.

## Visual [Beginner]

```
Person talking  |  Person talked to  |  Others

Possessive forms:
   my / mine          your / yours       his / her / hers / its
   our / ours         your / yours       their / theirs
```

"I" means the speaker. "You" means the listener. "He," "she," or "it" means someone or something else. "We" includes the speaker plus others.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Replace the repeated nouns with pronouns: "Tom found Tom's keys. Tom gave the keys to Tom's friend."

Step 1: Find the repeated nouns. "Tom" appears three times. "Keys" appears twice.
Step 2: Replace with pronouns. First mention keeps the noun. After that, use pronouns.

Result: "Tom found **his** keys. **He** gave **them** to **his** friend."

What this tells us: pronouns refer back to a noun that has already been named. That noun is called the **antecedent**. "Tom" is the antecedent for "he," "his," and "his."

Here is a second example: "The cat sat on the mat. The cat purred because the mat was warm."

Step 1: "The cat" appears twice, "the mat" appears twice.
Step 2: Replace with pronouns: "**It** purred because **it** was warm."

Wait -- "it" appears twice, and now the sentence is unclear. Does the second "it" refer to the cat or the mat? Was the cat warm, or was the mat warm? This is a **pronoun ambiguity problem**. To fix it, keep the noun for one of them: "It purred because the mat was warm" or "The cat purred because it was warm." Choosing which noun to keep depends on what you mean.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **pronoun** is a word that substitutes for a noun or noun phrase, typically referring to an entity already established in the discourse (the **antecedent**) or to the speaker, listener, or other participants in the speech act [ref: TODO_REF].

### Pronoun types in English

| Type | Function | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Personal** | Refer to persons or things | I, you, he, she, it, we, they, me, him, her, us, them |
| **Possessive** | Show ownership | my, your, his, her, its, our, their / mine, yours, hers, ours, theirs |
| **Reflexive** | Refer back to the subject | myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, themselves |
| **Demonstrative** | Point to specific things | this, that, these, those |
| **Interrogative** | Ask questions | who, whom, whose, which, what |
| **Relative** | Link clauses to nouns | who, whom, whose, which, that |
| **Indefinite** | Refer to non-specific entities | someone, anyone, nothing, everybody, each, all, some |
| **Reciprocal** | Express mutual action | each other, one another |

Each pronoun type occupies a specific syntactic position and serves a specific function. Personal pronouns function as subjects or objects. Possessive pronouns function as determiners (my book) or as independent noun equivalents (the book is mine). Reflexive pronouns appear as objects that corefer with the subject. Demonstrative pronouns can function as subjects (this is good), objects (I want that), or determiners (this book). Interrogative pronouns begin questions. Relative pronouns introduce dependent clauses.

### Person and number

English pronouns mark three **persons** (who is speaking):

- **First person**: the speaker (I, we)
- **Second person**: the listener (you)
- **Third person**: someone else (he, she, it, they)

And two **numbers**: singular (I, he, she, it) and plural (we, they). "You" is the same in both singular and plural in standard Modern English, though many dialects have developed plural forms: *y'all*, *you guys*, *youse*, *yinz*.

The first person singular "I" is always capitalised in English -- a convention that developed in the 13th century, likely because the single-letter word was easy to overlook in handwritten manuscripts. In many other languages, the first person pronoun is not capitalised: German "ich," French "je," Spanish "yo."

### Pronoun case

Pronouns change form depending on their grammatical role (**case**):

| Subjective | Objective | Possessive (determiner) | Possessive (independent) | Reflexive |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| I | me | my | mine | myself |
| he | him | his | his | himself |
| she | her | her | hers | herself |
| they | them | their | theirs | themselves |
| we | us | our | ours | ourselves |
| you | you | your | yours | yourself/yourselves |

Common error: using the objective form where the subjective is needed, or vice versa. "Him and I went" should be "He and I went" (both are subjects). "Between you and I" should be "between you and me" (both are objects of the preposition).

A useful trick: remove the other person and see which form sounds right. "Him went" sounds wrong, so "He and I went" is correct. "Between I" sounds wrong, so "between you and me" is correct.

### Ambiguity and antecedent clarity

Pronouns can be ambiguous when multiple possible antecedents exist. "When Sarah met Lisa, she was tired" -- who was tired? Sarah or Lisa? Skilled writers restructure to avoid ambiguity: "When Sarah met Lisa, Sarah was tired" or "Sarah was tired when she met Lisa."

This ambiguity is not a flaw in English -- it is a consequence of how pronouns work. A pronoun refers to an antecedent, but when two antecedents of the same gender and number are nearby, the reference becomes unclear. The solution is either to replace the pronoun with the noun or to restructure the sentence so that only one antecedent is available.

### Descriptive vs. prescriptive perspectives on pronouns

Prescriptive grammar rules about pronouns are among the most frequently taught and frequently violated. The rule "never end a sentence with a preposition" leads to advice like "between you and me" (not "between you and I"). The rule "use 'whom' for objects" leads to corrections like "Whom did you see?" (not "Who did you see?").

Descriptive linguistics observes that English speakers consistently use "who" for both subject and object in casual speech: "Who did you see?" is the natural form for most speakers. "Whom" has been retreating from casual English for centuries and now appears mainly in formal writing. Many linguists predict that "whom" will eventually disappear from English entirely, just as the distinct subject-object forms of nouns (he/him, she/her) may one day simplify further.

The singular "they" is another case where prescriptive and descriptive perspectives conflict. Prescriptive rules long insisted on "he" as the generic pronoun: "Every student should bring his book." Descriptive observation shows that speakers have used singular "they" for this purpose since the 14th century. The conflict is not about correctness but about social convention and the politics of language.

### World Englishes: pronoun variation

Pronoun systems vary across World Englishes. In Indian English, the pronoun "you" is often supplemented by respectful forms borrowed from Hindi and other Indian languages. A speaker might address an older person as "aap" (formal "you" in Hindi) even while speaking English, or use "ji" as an honorific suffix: "Yes, uncle-ji."

In Caribbean English, the pronoun "unnu" (from Igbo "unu," meaning "you plural") is used in Jamaica to address multiple people. In Singlish (Singapore English), "we" is sometimes replaced by "lor" or other discourse particles that carry pragmatic meaning beyond simple plural reference.

In South African English, pronoun usage reflects the multilingual context. Speakers may code-switch between English pronouns and pronouns from Zulu, Xhosa, or Afrikaans, creating hybrid forms that carry social meaning about identity, respect, and community membership.

### AAVE and pronoun patterns

African American Vernacular English uses pronouns in systematic ways that differ from Standard American English. One well-studied feature is **possessive reciprocity**: "They got each other's back" uses the reciprocal pronoun to express mutual loyalty in a way that is grammatically regular in AAVE.

AAVE also features **unstressed pronoun reduction**: "I'ma do it" (I am going to do it) contracts and reduces the pronoun-auxiliary sequence. "You done did it" uses the aspectual "done" with a pronoun subject to mark completive aspect. These patterns are not random errors but systematic features of a rule-governed variety.

Research by William Labov and others has shown that AAVE pronoun usage is as logical and consistent as Standard English pronoun usage, differing in specific rules but not in complexity or expressive power. The tendency to label AAVE pronoun patterns as "incorrect" reflects social bias rather than linguistic analysis.

### Cross-linguistic comparison

Pronoun systems vary dramatically across languages. Japanese has a complex system of first-person pronouns that encode social status and gender: *watashi* (formal, gender-neutral), *boku* (informal, typically male), *ore* (casual, masculine), *atashi* (casual, feminine). The choice of pronoun signals the speaker's identity and relationship to the listener.

Spanish maintains a distinction between informal and formal second person: *tu* (informal) vs. *usted* (formal). French has *tu* (informal) vs. *vous* (formal or plural). German has *du* (informal) vs. *Sie* (formal). These T-V distinctions (named after the Latin *tu* and *vos*) encode social hierarchy directly into the pronoun system.

Mandarin Chinese goes further, with multiple pronouns that encode social relationships: *wo* (I), *ni* (you informal), *nin* (you formal/respectful), *women* (we), *tamen* (they). The written forms use distinct characters, and the spoken forms differ in tone.

Some languages, including Japanese and Korean, allow pronouns to be omitted entirely when the referent is clear from context. In Japanese, "Tabeta" (ate) can mean "I ate," "you ate," "he ate," or "she ate" depending on context. This pro-drop feature means that pronouns are far less frequent in these languages than in English.

### ESL transfer errors with pronouns

Speakers of different first languages produce systematic pronoun errors in English:

- **Chinese speakers** may drop pronouns entirely: "Is very cold" instead of "It is very cold," because Mandarin is a pro-drop language.
- **Spanish speakers** may confuse gender: "She is a good man" because Spanish marks grammatical gender on nouns and the transfer carries over.
- **Japanese speakers** may use the wrong pronoun for social context, overusing formal pronouns or underusing them, because the social encoding of Japanese pronouns does not map onto English.
- **Arabic speakers** may overuse "he" as a generic pronoun because Arabic defaults to masculine for gender-unspecified reference.
- **Russian speakers** may confuse "he/she/it" for inanimate objects because Russian marks grammatical gender on all nouns, so "table" is "he" and "book" is "she."

Understanding these transfer patterns helps teachers address pronoun errors systematically.

### Pronouns in legal and formal writing

Pronouns have special significance in legal writing. The choice of pronoun in a contract, statute, or regulation can determine the scope of a legal obligation. "The tenant shall maintain the premises in good condition; he shall be responsible for all repairs" -- does "he" refer only to male tenants, or is it intended as generic? Modern legal drafting favours gender-neutral language: "The tenant shall maintain the premises in good condition and shall be responsible for all repairs" (no pronoun needed), or "The tenant shall maintain the premises in good condition; they shall be responsible for all repairs" (singular "they").

The pronoun "it" in legal writing can create ambiguity about whether a provision applies to a person or an entity. "The company shall file its annual report" -- "its" refers to the company. But "The board appointed a director and it approved the budget" -- does "it" refer to the board or the director? Legal drafters are trained to avoid such ambiguities by repeating the noun or restructuring the sentence.

In political discourse, pronoun choice is a rhetorical tool. "We the people" (first person plural) creates solidarity and shared identity. "They are destroying our country" (third person plural) creates an out-group. "You will pay for this" (second person) addresses the listener directly and creates confrontation. These uses are not accidental -- they are strategic choices that shape how audiences perceive issues and groups.

### Relative pronouns in detail

Relative pronouns (*who, whom, whose, which, that*) deserve special attention because they serve a dual function: they refer back to an antecedent AND they introduce a relative clause. In "The man who called you is waiting," "who" refers to "the man" (antecedent) and also introduces the clause "who called you."

The choice between "who" and "which/that" depends on whether the antecedent is a person or a thing. "The woman who called" (person) vs. "the book that I read" (thing). "Whose" can be used for both: "the student whose book" (person) and "the tree whose branches" (thing).

Restrictive relative clauses (with "that" or "who") limit the reference of the antecedent: "The students who studied passed" (only the studying students passed). Non-restrictive relative clauses (set off by commas, with "which" or "who") add extra information: "The students, who had all studied, passed" (all the students passed, and by the way, they had all studied). The distinction is semantic and syntactic: restrictive clauses are essential to the meaning; non-restrictive clauses are parenthetical.

### Demonstrative pronouns and discourse

Demonstrative pronouns (*this, that, these, those*) do more than point to physical objects. In discourse, they point to ideas, arguments, or entire stretches of text. "This is the key problem" -- "this" refers not to a physical object but to an abstract concept. "That argument is flawed" -- "that" points to a previously stated argument.

In academic writing, "this" and "these" are commonly used to refer back to previously mentioned concepts: "The results showed a significant increase in temperature. This finding supports the hypothesis." "This finding" is a demonstrative noun phrase that connects the current sentence to the previous one, creating textual cohesion.

The spatial dimension of demonstratives (*this* = near, *that* = far) extends to temporal and conceptual distance. "This week" (near in time) vs. "that week" (far in time). "This idea" (currently relevant) vs. "that idea" (previously mentioned or dismissed). The near-far distinction is a cognitive universal -- all languages have some form of demonstrative contrast, though the number of distinctions varies (some languages have three-way systems: near speaker, near listener, far from both).

## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

### Pronoun resolution in comprehension

When readers encounter a pronoun, they must determine its antecedent -- a process called **pronoun resolution**. Research in psycholinguistics shows that readers resolve pronouns rapidly, within 200-400 milliseconds of encountering the pronoun. The resolution process is influenced by several factors:

1. **Recency**: the most recently mentioned noun is the preferred antecedent. "John met Bill. He..." -- "he" is initially resolved to Bill (the most recent noun).
2. **Subject preference**: the subject of the previous sentence is preferred. "Bill was met by John. He..." -- "he" is more likely resolved to Bill (the subject).
3. **Gender and number**: the pronoun must agree with its antecedent in gender and number. "John met Mary. He..." -- "he" is resolved to John, not Mary.
4. **Semantic plausibility**: world knowledge constrains resolution. "John punished Bill because he..." -- "he" is resolved to John (people punish others, not the reverse).

These factors interact and sometimes compete, creating ambiguity that requires reanalysis. The study of pronoun resolution is central to both psycholinguistics and computational linguistics.

### Corpus frequency of pronouns

Pronouns are among the most frequent words in English. In the British National Corpus, the top 20 most frequent words include: *I* (rank 10), *you* (rank 14), *it* (rank 19), *he* (rank 24), *they* (rank 31), *we* (rank 38), *she* (rank 43), *me* (rank 47). Together, personal pronouns account for approximately 4-5% of all word tokens in written English, and even more in spoken English.

The frequency distribution is not even. First person pronouns (*I, we*) are more frequent in spoken English than in academic writing, where third person pronouns (*it, they*) dominate. This reflects the different functions of speech (self-expression) and academic writing (object reporting).

### Pronouns and gender

The relationship between pronouns and gender is one of the most actively discussed topics in contemporary linguistics. English third-person singular pronouns encode gender: *he* (masculine), *she* (feminine), *it* (non-human). This three-way distinction means that any reference to a person in the third person requires a gender choice.

For centuries, English used *he* as the default generic pronoun: "Every student should hand in his paper." This convention was challenged by feminist linguists in the 1970s and 1980s, who pointed out that generic *he* makes women invisible in language. Alternatives proposed included *he or she*, *s/he*, alternating between *he* and *she*, and singular *they*.

Singular *they* has emerged as the most widely adopted solution. It has a long history of use (Chaucer, Shakespeare, Austen, and many others used it), and it avoids both the gender bias of generic *he* and the awkwardness of *he or she*. Major style guides now accept singular *they*, including the APA, Chicago, and MLA guides.

Beyond generic reference, singular *they* is also used as a personal pronoun by non-binary and genderqueer people who do not identify as exclusively male or female. This usage is grammatically parallel to other singular pronouns and follows the same agreement patterns: "They are here" (not "they is here"), reflecting that *they* takes plural verb agreement even in singular use.

### Reflexive pronouns and emphatic use

Reflexive pronouns (*myself, yourself, himself, herself, itself, ourselves, themselves*) serve two functions. As **reflexives**, they indicate that the object of a verb is the same as the subject: "She hurt herself." As **intensive pronouns** (also called emphatic pronouns), they emphasise a noun: "The president himself attended the meeting."

A common error in formal writing is using reflexive pronouns where a personal pronoun is needed: "Please send the report to John or myself" should be "Please send the report to John or me." The reflexive form is only correct when the subject and object are the same entity.

## Linguistic theory [Master]

### Binding theory

Chomsky's **Binding Theory** (1981) explains pronoun distribution through three principles:

- **Principle A**: Reflexives (*himself, themselves*) must be **bound** within their local domain. "She hurt herself" works because "herself" is bound by "she" within the same clause. "She said that he hurt herself" is ungrammatical because "herself" reaches across a clause boundary.
- **Principle B**: Personal pronouns (*him, her, them*) must be **free** within their local domain. "She hurt her" works only if "her" refers to someone other than "she" -- the pronoun must not be bound locally.
- **Principle C**: Full noun phrases (names, descriptions) must be **free** everywhere. "She said that Mary left" cannot have "she" refer to Mary -- the full NP "Mary" cannot be bound by any pronoun.

These principles capture a wide range of grammaticality judgments with just three rules. Cross-linguistic variation in binding domains (some languages allow long-distance reflexives) is a central topic in comparative syntax.

### Pronouns and null subjects

English requires overt subjects: "It is raining" -- "it" has no referent but must appear. Languages like Italian, Spanish, and Mandarin allow **pro-drop** (null subjects): Italian "Piove" means "It is raining" with no subject pronoun. This parametric variation is captured by the **pro-drop parameter** (Rizzi 1982): languages differ in whether the subject position can be left phonologically empty when the verb carries enough inflectional information to recover the subject.

### The singular "they"

The use of "they" as a singular pronoun has a long history in English (attested from the 14th century in writing, including in Chaucer, Shakespeare, and Austen). It serves two functions:

1. **Generic reference**: "Everyone should bring their book" -- "their" refers to a person of unspecified gender.
2. **Specific non-binary reference**: "Alex said they would arrive at noon" -- "they" refers to a specific person who uses they/them pronouns.

Prescriptive grammarians of the 18th and 19th centuries objected to singular "they" and proposed "he" as the generic pronoun (a convention that itself introduced bias by subsuming all genders under masculine forms). Descriptive linguistics treats singular "they" as a well-established feature of English grammar with centuries of attested use.

### Pronouns as bound variables

In formal semantics, pronouns often function as **bound variables**. "Every student loves their teacher" has the logical form $\forall x(Student(x) \to Loves(x, teacher(x)))$, where "their" corresponds to the bound variable $x$. The pronoun is "bound" by the quantifier "every" in the same way that a variable is bound by a quantifier in logic.

This analysis explains why pronouns sometimes take "wide" or "narrow" scope relative to quantifiers. "Every student saw a professor and they admired her" -- "they" refers to the students (bound by "every"), but "her" can refer to a specific professor (free, referring to someone in the discourse context) or to the same professor each student saw (bound by "a"). The ambiguity is structural, parallel to scope ambiguities in logic.

### Connections

The binding constraints on pronouns parallel scope constraints on variables in formal logic -- a pronoun cannot "escape" its binding domain any more than a bound variable can escape its quantifier's scope.

### Pronouns and identity

Pronoun choice is deeply connected to personal and social identity. The choice between *tu* and *vous* in French, between *du* and *Sie* in German, or between first name and title+last name in English encodes social relationships: intimacy vs. formality, equality vs. hierarchy, solidarity vs. distance.

In English, the choice between *he*, *she*, and *they* for third-person singular reference encodes gender identity. When a person specifies their pronouns (e.g., "My pronouns are she/her"), they are providing information about their gender identity. Using the correct pronouns is a matter of respect and accuracy, just as using the correct name is.

The debate over pronouns is therefore not merely a grammatical question. It touches on questions of identity, respect, social recognition, and the relationship between language and the categories it encodes. Linguists study these questions using the tools of sociolinguistics, pragmatics, and discourse analysis.

### Pronouns in literature

Writers use pronouns strategically for narrative effect. First-person narration (*I*) creates intimacy and immediacy: the reader experiences the story through the narrator's eyes. Third-person limited (*he/she*) allows distance while maintaining a single character's perspective. Third-person omniscient (*he/she/they*) allows the narrator to move between characters.

Some writers exploit pronoun ambiguity for literary effect. In Henry James's novels, ambiguous pronoun references mirror the characters' uncertain perceptions of each other. In detective fiction, ambiguous pronouns can mislead the reader: "She saw him with the knife" -- who is "she" and who is "him"? The ambiguity creates suspense.

Virginia Woolf experimented with pronouns in *The Waves*, using "he" and "she" to follow the inner lives of six characters, shifting pronouns to shift perspective. The pronoun changes are the structural backbone of the novel, marking the transitions between one consciousness and another.

### Pronoun avoidance strategies

Some writers and speakers avoid pronouns for stylistic or cultural reasons. Academic writing sometimes overuses passive constructions to avoid first-person pronouns: "It was observed that..." instead of "We observed that..." The practice of avoiding "I" in academic writing is a relatively recent convention, dating from the late 19th century, when scientists adopted an impersonal style to convey objectivity. Earlier scientists like Newton and Darwin used first-person pronouns freely.

In business writing, pronoun avoidance can create cold, impersonal prose. "The undersigned wishes to inform the recipient that the matter has been resolved" is more distant than "I want to let you know that we have resolved the matter." The choice between pronoun-rich and pronoun-avoidant styles is not just a grammar question -- it reflects assumptions about the relationship between writer and reader.

### Demonstratives across languages

All languages have demonstrative pronouns, but the number of distinctions varies. English has a two-way system: *this* (near) vs. *that* (far). Spanish has a three-way system: *este* (near speaker), *ese* (near listener), *aquel* (far from both). Japanese has a three-way system: *kore* (near speaker), *sore* (near listener), *are* (far from both). Some indigenous languages of the Americas have four-way or even five-way demonstrative systems that distinguish visibility, altitude, and other spatial features.

The universality of demonstratives suggests that spatial reference is a fundamental cognitive category. All languages need a way to point to things in the shared environment, and demonstratives provide this function. The variation in the number of distinctions reflects different cultural and environmental priorities: speakers in some communities need finer spatial distinctions than others.

### Indefinite pronouns and negation

Indefinite pronouns (*someone, anyone, no one, everybody, nothing, everything*) interact with negation in systematic ways. In standard English, "double negation" is prohibited: "I don't see nothing" is prescriptively incorrect (should be "I don't see anything"). The prescriptive rule is that two negatives cancel each other out: "I don't see nothing" would logically mean "I see something."

However, double negation is a feature of many English dialects and of many other languages. In French, "ne...pas" is the standard negation pattern (two negative elements). In Spanish, "no...nada" is used: "No veo nada" (I don't see nothing = I don't see anything). In AAVE, double negation serves as emphasis, not logical cancellation: "I don't see nothing" means "I really don't see anything," not "I see something."

The prescriptive prohibition on double negation is based on logic, not on natural language use. Most of the world's languages use multiple negative elements, and the English double negative was standard until 18th-century grammarians imposed the logical rule. The rule is useful for formal writing but does not reflect the natural logic of everyday speech.

### Pronoun acquisition in children

Children acquire pronouns in a systematic order. First-person pronouns (*I, me, my*) are among the earliest words children produce, appearing around age 2. However, children often confuse subjective and objective forms (*I* vs. *me*) for several years: "Me want it" is a common child-language error. The distinction between *I* and *me* is a grammatical abstraction that takes time to master.

Third-person pronouns (*he, she, it, they*) appear later, around age 2-3, and children often use them before they have fully mastered gender agreement. A young child might say "he" for a female referent or "it" for a pet. Reflexive pronouns (*myself, yourself*) appear even later, around age 4-5, and children often overgeneralise them: "He saw hisself" instead of "He saw himself."

The acquisition of pronouns provides evidence for the innate basis of grammar. Children produce pronoun forms they have never heard (like "hisself") by applying regular rules, which suggests they are not simply imitating adult speech but are actively constructing grammatical rules. This argument, developed by Chomsky and others, is a cornerstone of generative linguistics.

### Pronouns in sign languages

Sign languages have pronoun systems that are functionally equivalent to spoken language pronouns but use spatial pointing rather than vocal words. In American Sign Language (ASL), the first-person pronoun is a point to the signer's chest. Second-person is a point to the addressee. Third-person is a point to the location in space where the referent has been established.

One remarkable feature of ASL pronouns is **spatial agreement**: when a referent is introduced, the signer assigns them a location in space. All subsequent pronouns referring to that person point to that location. This creates a spatial pronoun system that is more precise than spoken English pronouns in some ways, because each third-person referent has a unique spatial location, reducing ambiguity.

The existence of fully grammatical pronoun systems in sign languages is evidence that pronouns are a universal feature of human language, not tied to the auditory-vocal channel. Whether spoken or signed, all known languages have a way to refer to the speaker, the addressee, and third parties.

In philosophy, pronouns raise questions about reference and identity: how does a word like "he" or "she" pick out a specific person in the world? This is the problem of **anaphora** -- the linguistic mechanism by which pronouns refer back to antecedents. The study of anaphora connects linguistics to philosophy of language and to computational approaches to reference resolution.

In computer science, pronoun resolution is a core problem in natural language processing. Determining the antecedent of a pronoun in a text requires understanding both the syntactic structure of the sentences and the semantic relationships between the entities mentioned. State-of-the-art NLP systems use neural networks trained on large corpora to resolve pronouns, but the problem remains challenging for ambiguous cases.

### Coreference resolution

In computational linguistics, the task of determining which noun phrases in a text refer to the same entity is called **coreference resolution**. This includes pronouns (he, she, it) but also definite noun phrases (the man, the company) and names (John, Mr. Smith). A coreference chain for a text about a character named "Sarah" might include: "Sarah" ... "she" ... "her" ... "the young woman" ... "Sarah" ... "she."

Coreference resolution is essential for text understanding. A machine reading "John gave Mary a book. She loved it. He was happy" must determine that "she" = Mary, "it" = the book, and "he" = John. This requires syntactic parsing (identifying subject and object positions), semantic knowledge (books are things that can be loved), and discourse tracking (keeping track of which entities have been mentioned).

The Winograd Schema Challenge (Levesque et al. 2012) tests pronoun resolution using sentences that require world knowledge: "The trophy didn't fit into the brown suitcase because it was too large." What was too large -- the trophy or the suitcase? Humans know that if something doesn't fit because it's too large, the thing that doesn't fit is "it." So "it" = the trophy. But change "large" to "small" and the resolution flips: "it was too small" means the suitcase was too small. "It" = the suitcase. Resolving these correctly requires understanding the physical relationship between containers and their contents.

## Historical context [Master]

Old English had a richer pronoun system than Modern English, including distinct dual number forms (*wit* = "we two," *git* = "you two") and grammatical gender matching (the third-person pronoun agreed in gender with its antecedent). The loss of grammatical gender during the Middle English period (11th-15th centuries) simplified the system. The possessive "its" did not exist in early Modern English; Shakespeare used "his" for inanimate objects ("the sun, his beams"). "Its" first appears in print around 1600 and became standard by the 18th century.

The 18th century saw heated debates about pronouns. Anne Fisher's *A New Grammar* (1745) proposed "he" as the generic pronoun for mixed-gender reference, a convention that was adopted by subsequent grammarians and remained standard in formal writing until the late 20th century. The feminist critique of generic "he" in the 1970s and 1980s reopened the question, and the eventual adoption of singular "they" by major style guides represents one of the most significant pronoun changes in the history of English grammar.

The second-person pronoun "you" has its own complex history. Old English distinguished between *pu* (singular informal), *ge* (plural), and *eow* (plural object). Over time, the plural form *you* came to be used as a polite singular (like French *vous*), eventually displacing *thou* entirely. By the 18th century, "thou" survived only in religious language and regional dialects. The loss of the singular-plural distinction in "you" created a gap that regional dialects filled with new plural forms: *y'all* (American South), *youse* (New York, Ireland), *yinz* (Pittsburgh), *you guys* (widely used).

### The evolution of "thou" and "you"

The story of "thou" and "you" in English illustrates how pronoun systems change in response to social forces. In Middle English, *thou* was the singular informal pronoun and *ye/you* was the plural. Over time, following the French model of *tu/vous*, English speakers began using *you* as a polite singular form, reserved for social superiors and formal occasions. *Thou* became the intimate form, used with family, close friends, children, and social inferiors.

By the 16th century, the polite *you* had spread so widely that *thou* began to seem rude or excessively intimate in many contexts. Shakespeare's plays show the transition in progress: characters use *thou* to intimates and inferiors, *you* to equals and superiors, and the choice signals social relationships. By the 17th century, *you* had displaced *thou* in most contexts, and by the 18th century, *thou* survived only in religious language (thee/thou in Quaker worship and in some Bible translations) and in a few regional dialects.

The loss of *thou* created a gap in English: there was no longer a way to distinguish singular and plural "you" in standard English. Regional dialects filled this gap with new plural forms, as noted above. The cycle -- binary distinction lost, new forms created to restore it -- is a common pattern in language change. Some linguists predict that *y'all*, *you guys*, or *youse* may eventually become standard English, completing the cycle.

### Pronouns and power

Pronouns have always been entangled with power. The T-V distinction (informal vs. formal "you") encodes social hierarchy: using *tu* (informal) to a social superior in medieval France was an insult; using *vous* (formal) to a close friend was cold and distant. The choice of pronoun signalled the speaker's recognition of social rank.

In modern English, pronoun-related power dynamics appear in other forms. The refusal to use someone's specified pronouns is a form of social rejection -- it denies the person's stated identity. The use of generic *he* in legal and academic writing excluded women from the category of full persons for centuries. The adoption of singular *they* and other gender-inclusive pronoun practices is part of a broader shift toward language that includes all people.

In political rhetoric, pronoun choices frame relationships between leaders and citizens. "We will build a better future" (first person plural, inclusive) creates solidarity. "They are taking our jobs" (third person plural, exclusive) creates division. "I alone can fix it" (first person singular, exclusive) asserts individual authority. These patterns are studied in critical discourse analysis, a branch of linguistics that examines how language creates and maintains power relationships.

### Reciprocal pronouns and mutual reference

Reciprocal pronouns (*each other, one another*) express mutual action. "They love each other" means A loves B and B loves A. The two forms are largely interchangeable in modern English, though a traditional distinction holds that *each other* refers to two and *one another* to three or more. This distinction is not consistently observed by speakers and is not supported by corpus data.

Reciprocal pronouns raise interesting semantic questions. "They saw each other" means each saw the other. But "They described each other to the police" is more complex: did each describe the other, or did they collaboratively describe their mutual relationship? The ambiguity arises because the reciprocal pronoun can scope differently with respect to other arguments in the sentence.

In formal semantics, reciprocals are analysed using set theory: "They saw each other" is true if for every pair (a, b) in the set {they}, a saw b. Different strengths of reciprocity are possible: strong reciprocity (every pair), intermediate reciprocity (every member saw at least one other), and weak reciprocity (some saw some). The English reciprocal is ambiguous between these readings, and the resolution depends on context and world knowledge.

### Pronoun dropping and pro-drop revisited

The pro-drop parameter, mentioned earlier, has further implications. In pro-drop languages, the rich inflection on the verb provides enough information to identify the subject without an overt pronoun. Italian "parlo" (I speak) has a first-person singular ending *-o* that makes the pronoun *io* (I) unnecessary. English "speak" has no such ending, so the pronoun "I" is required.

However, English does allow pronoun dropping in certain registers. Diary and note style uses frequent subject drop: "Went to the store. Bought eggs. Came home." Casual conversation sometimes drops auxiliaries and subjects: "Going to the party?" means "Are you going to the party?" These restricted contexts for pronoun dropping are not the same as the systematic pro-drop found in Italian or Mandarin, but they show that the English pronoun system is not rigidly fixed.

### Pronouns in poetry and song

Pronouns are central to lyric poetry and songwriting. The first-person pronoun *I* is the default voice of the lyric poet, creating an intimate, confessional tone. Walt Whitman's "Song of Myself" begins: "I celebrate myself, and sing myself." The repeated pronoun establishes the poem's central concern with individual identity and experience.

In popular music, pronoun choices create emotional effects. "You" in a love song addresses the beloved directly, creating immediacy: "I can't help falling in love with you" (Elvis Presley). "We" creates shared identity: "We shall overcome" (civil rights anthem). "They" can create distance or opposition: "They say we're young and we don't know" (The Growlers).

The economic use of pronouns in poetry -- where every word carries weight -- makes pronoun ambiguity a poetic resource rather than a problem. When Emily Dickinson writes "I heard a Fly buzz -- when I died --," the "I" is both the poet and a dramatic persona, and the ambiguity is deliberate. Pronouns in literature are never merely grammatical devices; they are tools for constructing perspective, identity, and relationship.

Understanding pronouns -- their types, their syntactic constraints, their role in discourse, and their social significance -- is essential for reading carefully, writing precisely, and thinking critically about how language constructs identity and power.
