## Intuition [Beginner]

**Subject-verb agreement** means the verb in a sentence must match the subject in number. If the subject is singular (one thing), the verb takes a singular form. If the subject is plural (more than one), the verb takes a plural form.

In English, agreement shows up most plainly in the **present tense** with third-person subjects:

| Subject | Verb form | Example |
|---------|-----------|---------|
| He / She / It (singular) | verb + **-s** | She **runs**. |
| They / We / You (plural) | base verb | They **run**. |

The key rule: a singular subject takes **-s** on the verb; a plural subject does not.

This might seem backwards at first. When you add an -s to a noun, it becomes plural ("dog" becomes "dogs"). But when you add an -s to a present-tense verb, it becomes singular ("run" becomes "runs"). This is just how English works. The -s on the verb is not a plural marker -- it is an agreement marker that signals "this verb goes with a singular subject."

Think of it like a lock and key. The subject has a shape (singular or plural), and the verb has to fit that shape. A singular subject needs a singular verb. A plural subject needs a plural verb. When they match, the sentence sounds right. When they do not match, something sounds off: "He run to school" sounds wrong to most English speakers because the singular subject "he" does not match the plural verb form "run."

The good news is that agreement in English is simpler than in many other languages. In Spanish, every verb has six different forms for the present tense (one for each person and number combination). In English, most verbs have only two: the base form and the -s form. The only exception is the verb "be," which has three forms in the present tense: "am" (I), "is" (he/she/it), and "are" (you/we/they).

In the past tense, agreement is even simpler. For almost all verbs, there is only one form: "walked" works for every subject. "I walked, you walked, he walked, we walked, they walked." The verb "be" is again the exception: "I/he/she/it was" vs. "you/we/they were." So if you master the present-tense -s rule and the was/were distinction, you have covered almost all of English subject-verb agreement.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Apply agreement rules to fix each sentence.

**1. "The students in the lab conducts experiments."**
- Subject: *The students in the lab* -- head noun is *students* (plural).
- Verb should be plural: *conduct*.
- Corrected: "The students in the lab **conduct** experiments."

**2. "Each of the letters were delivered on time."**
- Subject: *Each* (singular indefinite pronoun).
- Verb should be singular: *was*.
- Corrected: "Each of the letters **was** delivered on time."

**3. "The committee are meeting tomorrow."**
- *Committee* is a collective noun. In American English it is treated as singular.
- Corrected (American): "The committee **is** meeting tomorrow."

**4. "The list of ingredients are on the counter."**
- Subject: *The list* (singular). The phrase "of ingredients" is a prepositional phrase modifying "list," not part of the subject head.
- Verb should be singular: *is*.
- Corrected: "The list of ingredients **is** on the counter."

A common trap: the noun right before the verb ("ingredients") is not the subject. The subject is "list." Words that come between the subject and the verb can trick you into choosing the wrong form. Always find the head noun of the subject phrase first, then make the verb agree with that head noun.

**5. "There is five students waiting outside."**
- The notional subject after "there" is "five students" (plural).
- Verb should be plural: *are*.
- Corrected: "There **are** five students waiting outside."

**6. "Macaroni and cheese are my favourite comfort food."**
- "Macaroni and cheese" is treated as a single dish, so it takes singular agreement.
- Verb should be singular: *is*.
- Corrected: "Macaroni and cheese **is** my favourite comfort food."

These examples show the main patterns you need to watch for: intervening prepositional phrases, indefinite pronouns, collective nouns, existential "there" constructions, and compound subjects that form single units. Each pattern has its own rule, and the key is to identify which pattern applies before choosing the verb form.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Subject-verb agreement in English is a syntactic dependency in which the finite verb inflects to match the **person** and **number** features of the subject NP. In practice, the overt morphological reflex in modern English is limited:

- **Third-person singular present:** verb + *-s* (e.g., *walks*, *has*, *is*)
- **Third-person plural present:** base form (e.g., *walk*, *have*, *are*)
- **Past tense of *be*:** *was* (singular) vs. *were* (plural)
- **All other past tense verbs:** no agreement distinction (*walked* is used for all persons and numbers)

The **subject** is structurally defined as the NP in the specifier of the tense phrase (TP) or, in traditional terms, the NP that the sentence is about.

### The full agreement paradigm

The complete present-tense agreement paradigm for English regular verbs is remarkably sparse compared to languages like Spanish or Russian:

| Person/Number | "walk" paradigm | "be" paradigm | "have" paradigm |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st singular (I) | walk | am | have |
| 2nd singular (you) | walk | are | have |
| 3rd singular (he/she/it) | walks | is | has |
| 1st plural (we) | walk | are | have |
| 2nd plural (you) | walk | are | have |
| 3rd plural (they) | walk | are | have |

Only "be" has a full paradigm with distinct forms for all persons and numbers. Regular verbs distinguish only third-person singular from everything else. This poverty of agreement morphology makes English unusual among Indo-European languages and is the result of centuries of phonological erosion.

### Intervening phrases and agreement

A prepositional phrase or other modifier between the subject and the verb does not change agreement. "The [list of items] is long" -- the verb agrees with *list*, not *items*. This rule is straightforward in principle but causes many errors in practice, because the noun closest to the verb ("items") exerts a strong attraction.

Psycholinguistic research has shown that speakers sometimes produce agreement errors when a plural noun intervenes between a singular subject head and the verb. This is called the **attraction effect**. Speakers are more likely to say "The key to the cabinets are missing" than "The key to the cabinet are missing," even though both are errors, because the plural "cabinets" attracts the verb toward plural agreement. The attraction effect demonstrates that agreement is not purely rule-based but is influenced by the processing of nearby words during sentence production.

### Indefinite pronouns and agreement

Words like *each*, *every*, *everyone*, *nobody*, *either*, *neither* are grammatically singular despite conceptual plurality. "Everyone has finished" (not *have*). These pronouns cause difficulty because they refer to multiple people or things conceptually, but grammatically they are singular.

The indefinite pronouns that take singular agreement include: each, either, neither, everyone, everybody, someone, somebody, anyone, anybody, no one, nobody, everything, something, anything, nothing. A useful memory aid: if the pronoun contains "one," "body," or "thing," it takes singular agreement.

The pronouns *both*, *few*, *many*, and *several* are always plural. The pronouns *some*, *any*, *none*, and *all* can be singular or plural depending on the noun they refer to: "Some of the water is gone" (singular, uncountable) vs. "Some of the students are here" (plural, countable).

### Collective nouns and agreement variation

Nouns like *committee*, *team*, *family*, *audience* denote a group. In American English they are typically singular ("The team is ready"). In British English plural agreement is common ("The team are ready"). This difference reflects a deeper difference in how the two varieties construe collective entities: American English prioritises the group as a single unit, while British English more readily construes the group as a collection of individuals.

Some collective nouns allow both singular and plural agreement depending on meaning. "The jury has reached a verdict" treats the jury as a single body making one decision. "The jury are arguing among themselves" treats the jury as individuals who disagree with each other. The choice of singular or plural agreement signals whether the speaker is thinking of the group as a unit or as separate members.

### Proximity agreement

With disjunctive coordinators (*or*, *nor*), the verb agrees with the closest subject: "Neither the dogs nor the cat **is** here" vs. "Neither the cat nor the dogs **are** here." This is the proximity rule, and it applies specifically to correlative conjunctions like "either...or" and "neither...nor."

With "and," the compound subject is normally plural: "The cat and the dog are here." But there are exceptions when the two nouns form a single conceptual unit: "Bacon and eggs is my favorite breakfast" treats "bacon and eggs" as one dish, not two separate items. Other examples: "macaroni and cheese," "law and order," "bread and butter," "fish and chips."

### Inverted sentences

When the verb precedes the subject, agreement still applies to the postverbal NP: "There **are** three options" (not *is*), because the notional subject *three options* is plural. Inverted sentences with "there" and "here" are common sources of agreement errors, because the verb appears before the subject and speakers sometimes match it to the first noun they encounter.

Questions also invert subject and verb: "Is the student ready?" (singular) vs. "Are the students ready?" (plural). In each case, the verb agrees with the subject that follows it, even though the normal subject-verb order is reversed.

### Titles and quoted phrases

Titles of books, films, and works of art take singular agreement even when they contain plural nouns: "The Hunger Games is a popular novel" (not *are*). Quoted phrases also take singular agreement: "'Children are our future' is a common saying" (not *are* again -- the quote is being treated as a single linguistic expression).

Measurements and amounts can be singular or plural depending on whether the focus is on the quantity as a unit or the individual items: "Five miles is a long walk" (treating five miles as a single distance) vs. "Five miles are marked on the map" (treating them as five separate mile segments).

### Descriptive vs. prescriptive perspectives

Prescriptive grammar insists on strict formal agreement: the verb must match the grammatical number of the subject, regardless of meaning. On this view, "The committee is meeting" is correct and "The committee are meeting" is an error (in American English).

Descriptive linguistics observes that speakers often produce sentences where the verb agrees with the notional (semantic) subject rather than the grammatical subject. "A group of students were protesting" is common in both speech and writing, even though "group" is formally singular. Descriptive grammarians treat this as a natural tension between formal and notional agreement, not as an error.

The prescriptive tradition has a point: consistent formal agreement makes sentences easier to parse because it provides a reliable structural signal. But the descriptive observation is also valid: notional agreement reflects how speakers actually think about the subject's meaning, and insisting on formal agreement in all cases can sometimes produce sentences that sound unnatural.

### World Englishes: agreement variation

Subject-verb agreement varies across World Englishes in systematic ways. In Indian English, collective nouns commonly take plural agreement: "The committee have decided" is standard in Indian English, reflecting the influence of Hindi and other Indian languages that do not distinguish singular and plural in the same way for collective entities.

In Caribbean English creoles, agreement morphology is often reduced or absent. "He walk to school" is grammatical in Jamaican Creole, which does not mark third-person singular agreement at all. This is not an error but a systematic feature of the creole's grammar, which has a simpler agreement system than Standard English.

In Nigerian English, proximity agreement is more common than in Standard British English: "The list of items are ready" is widely accepted. This reflects the influence of Nigerian languages, some of which do not have formal agreement systems and instead rely on word order and context to indicate subject-verb relationships.

### AAVE and agreement

African American Vernacular English optionally allows zero-marking on third-person singular present verbs: "He walk to school" instead of "He walks to school." This feature is systematic in AAVE -- it is not random omission. Research has shown that AAVE speakers use the zero-marked form more frequently in certain grammatical contexts (such as habitual or generic statements) and less frequently in others (such as narratives about specific events).

The absence of third-person singular -s in AAVE has roots in the contact linguistics of the early American South, where speakers of various West African languages acquired English under conditions of limited access. Several West African languages do not mark subject-verb agreement, and this feature was transferred into the developing creole and then into AAVE.

Prescriptive grammar treats zero-marking as non-standard, but linguistically it represents a systematic reduction in agreement morphology parallel to what happened in the history of English more broadly. Old English had rich agreement morphology that was gradually eroded over centuries. AAVE has simply carried this erosion one step further.

### Cross-linguistic comparison

English has one of the sparsest agreement systems among Indo-European languages. Spanish verbs agree with their subjects in person and number across all tenses and moods, producing dozens of distinct verb forms. Russian verbs agree in person, number, and gender in the past tense. German verbs agree in person and number, and German also has adjective agreement (adjectives inflect to match the noun they modify in case, gender, and number).

Languages outside the Indo-European family show a wide range of agreement patterns. Swahili has noun class agreement: verbs, adjectives, and other modifiers all carry prefixes that match the noun class of the head noun. Arabic has rich gender and number agreement, including a dual number (for exactly two things) in addition to singular and plural. Mandarin Chinese has no subject-verb agreement at all -- the verb form does not change regardless of the subject.

The cross-linguistic variation in agreement systems supports the view that agreement is not a necessary feature of human language but a parameter that varies from language to language. Languages with rich agreement systems can often drop the subject pronoun entirely (pro-drop), because the verb form alone identifies the subject. Spanish "caminamos" (we walk) does not need the pronoun "nosotros" because the verb ending identifies the subject. English, with its minimal agreement, cannot normally drop the subject: "walks" could only be third-person singular, but "walk" could be first-person singular, second-person, or any plural.

This correlation between agreement richness and pro-drop is known as the **pro-drop parameter** and has been influential in generative syntax. Languages fall along a spectrum: Mandarin has no agreement and no pro-drop, English has minimal agreement and no pro-drop, Spanish has rich agreement and pro-drop, and Italian has very rich agreement and extensive pro-drop. The connection between agreement and pro-drop suggests that agreement serves a functional purpose: it provides information about the subject that allows the subject to be omitted when context makes it recoverable.

### Agreement in sign languages

Sign languages also have agreement systems, though they operate in the visual-spatial modality rather than the auditory-vocal modality. In American Sign Language (ASL), verbs that take agreement move between spatial locations associated with the subject and object referents. The direction of movement encodes who is doing what to whom, serving the same function as spoken agreement morphology.

Research on agreement in sign languages has provided evidence that agreement is a fundamental property of human language, not tied to the auditory-vocal channel. The fact that both spoken and signed languages develop agreement systems suggests that the human language faculty has a built-in predisposition to encode relationships between arguments and predicates through systematic morphological marking.

### ESL transfer errors with agreement

Second-language learners of English face several predictable agreement challenges depending on their first language:

- **Speakers of languages with no agreement** (Mandarin, Thai, Vietnamese) frequently omit the third-person singular -s because their L1 does not require agreement marking. "She walk to school" is a common error.
- **Speakers of languages with rich agreement** (Spanish, Arabic, Russian) may overgeneralise agreement, producing forms like "I walks" or adding agreement markers where English does not use them.
- **Speakers of languages with different collective noun patterns** may use plural agreement with collective nouns: "The team are ready" is produced by many L2 speakers whose first language treats collectives as plural.
- **Speakers of pro-drop languages** (Spanish, Italian, Japanese) may produce sentences without subjects: "Is raining" instead of "It is raining."

These transfer errors are predictable from the differences between L1 and L2 agreement systems. Teachers who understand these patterns can target their instruction to address the specific challenges faced by speakers of different language backgrounds.

Research on L2 agreement acquisition has shown that the third-person singular -s is one of the last English morphemes to be acquired, even by advanced learners. This late acquisition may be due to the low perceptual salience of the -s ending (it is a single consonant, often unstressed) and the fact that its omission rarely causes misunderstanding. Learners can communicate successfully without it, so there is less pressure to acquire it than more communicatively important features.

Error analysis studies have also found that agreement errors increase with sentence complexity. Learners produce correct agreement in simple sentences ("She walks") but make errors in sentences with intervening phrases ("The list of items on the table need..."). This pattern mirrors the attraction effects found in native speaker production, suggesting that agreement computation is cognitively demanding for both native and non-native speakers.

## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

### Agreement with compound subjects

Compound subjects joined by "and" are normally plural: "The cat and the dog are friends." But when the two nouns form a single unit, singular agreement is used: "Bacon and eggs is my favorite breakfast." Other unitary expressions include "macaroni and cheese," "law and order," "bread and butter," "fish and chips," "salt and pepper."

When compound subjects are joined by "or" or "nor," the proximity rule applies: the verb agrees with the nearest subject. "Either the teacher or the students are responsible." "Neither the students nor the teacher is responsible." Notice how the verb changes depending on which subject is closer.

### Agreement with existential "there"

The existential construction "there is/are" causes frequent agreement errors. The rule is that the verb agrees with the notional subject that follows it: "There is a book on the table" (singular) vs. "There are books on the table" (plural). In casual speech, "there's" is often used with plural subjects ("There's three books on the table"), but this is considered non-standard in formal writing.

The contraction "there's" has become somewhat fossilised as a presentational particle, similar to French "il y a" (literally "it there has"), which is used regardless of the number of the following noun. This grammaticalisation process may eventually make "there's" acceptable with plural subjects in Standard English, but it has not done so yet in formal written contexts.

In academic and formal writing, it is important to use "there are" with plural subjects. However, many published writers use "there's" with plural subjects in dialogue and informal narration to create a natural, conversational tone. The choice between formal and informal agreement with existential "there" is a register decision, not a grammatical one in the strict sense.

### Agreement with quantities and measurements

Quantities and measurements present special agreement challenges. When the subject is a quantity ("five dollars," "two weeks," "three miles"), the verb is typically singular because the quantity is being treated as a single amount: "Five dollars is too much to pay." "Two weeks is not enough time." "Three miles is a reasonable distance."

However, when the focus is on the individual items rather than the quantity as a whole, plural agreement is used: "Five dollars were scattered on the floor" (individual dollar bills). "Two weeks have passed since I last saw her" (individual weeks counted). The distinction depends on whether the speaker construes the quantity as a unit or as a collection of individual items.

Mathematical operations take singular agreement: "Two plus two is four." "Five times three is fifteen." "Ten divided by two equals five." The operation is treated as a single mathematical statement, regardless of the numbers involved.

### Agreement with relative pronouns

When a relative pronoun is the subject of a relative clause, it agrees with its antecedent: "The students who **are** studying" (plural antecedent) vs. "The student who **is** studying" (singular antecedent). The verb in the relative clause must match the number of the noun that the relative pronoun refers to, not the number of any other noun in the sentence.

One of the hardest is "one of the [plural noun] who/that [verb]." Should the verb be singular (agreeing with "one") or plural (agreeing with the plural noun)? Standard usage favours plural agreement: "She is one of the students who **are** always prepared" -- because "who" refers to "students." However, some prescriptive authorities argue for singular agreement, treating "one" as the head. Both forms are attested in published writing.

This construction illustrates a genuine ambiguity in the grammar. If "who" modifies "students," then plural agreement is correct (she is one member of a group of students who are prepared). If "who" modifies "one," then singular agreement is correct (she is the one student who is prepared). In practice, the plural reading is more common and more natural, because it treats the relative clause as restricting the set of students rather than identifying a single individual.

### Agreement with proper nouns and foreign words

Proper nouns that end in -s (United States, United Nations, the Netherlands) are treated as singular in American English: "The United States has a large economy." "The United Nations is meeting this week." These names refer to single entities despite the plural form.

Foreign words and phrases used in English take agreement based on how they are construed. "Media" is plural in Latin but commonly singular in English: "The media is reporting the story" (singular construal, treating media as a collective entity). Some style guides insist on plural agreement with data ("The data are compelling"), but singular agreement is increasingly common ("The data is compelling"). This shift reflects the natural process by which foreign plurals are reanalysed as singular mass nouns in English.

### Agreement with fractions and percentages

Fractions and percentages take singular or plural agreement depending on the noun they modify: "Fifty percent of the water **is** gone" (singular, uncountable noun) vs. "Fifty percent of the students **are** here" (plural, countable noun). The fraction or percentage itself does not determine agreement -- the noun it modifies does.

With "a number of" and "the number of," a critical distinction applies: "A number of students **have** complained" treats "a number of" as a quantifier meaning "several," and the verb agrees with the plural noun "students." But "The number of students **has** increased" treats "number" as the genuine head, and the verb is singular. This distinction is one of the most frequently tested in formal grammar assessments.

## Linguistic theory [Master]

### Agreement in generative grammar

Agreement is a core phenomenon in morphosyntax and has been central to theories of the syntax-morphology interface. In **Government and Binding** theory (Chomsky 1981), agreement is mediated by the **AGR** node (later split into AGR-S and AGR-O), a functional head in the inflectional layer that carries person and number features and establishes a Spec-head relation with the subject.

In the **Minimalist Program**, the AGR node was eliminated (Chomsky 1995). Agreement is instead captured by the operation **Agree**, which establishes a feature-matching relation between a **probe** (typically T) and a **goal** (the subject NP) without requiring movement. The probe searches its c-command domain for an active goal with matching interpretable features, and uninterpretable features are valued and subsequently deleted at the interfaces.

This analysis raises questions about the direction of agreement. In most languages, the verb agrees with the subject (downward agreement). But some languages show upward agreement or even "hybrid agreement" where the controller varies depending on syntactic context. The Minimalist analysis treats agreement as a symmetric relation that is then resolved by independent principles, rather than an inherent property of either the subject or the verb.

### Corbett's agreement typology

**Corbett (2006)** in his typological work distinguishes **syntactic agreement** (mechanical, feature-driven) from **semantic agreement** (based on meaning). English collective nouns illustrate the tension: "The committee is unanimous" (syntactic: singular) vs. "The committee are arguing among themselves" (semantic: notionally plural).

Corbett's **Agreement Hierarchy** predicts that semantic agreement is more likely in attributive > predicative > relative pronoun > personal pronoun positions. This means that even speakers who use singular agreement with a verb ("The committee is...") may use plural agreement with a personal pronoun ("The committee... themselves"). The hierarchy has been confirmed across a wide range of languages and represents one of the most robust cross-linguistic generalisations about agreement.

Corbett also identifies the **Agreement Features Hierarchy**: number > gender > person. Languages that mark only one agreement feature mark number. Languages that mark two mark number and gender. Languages that mark all three mark number, gender, and person. English, with only number (and person in the limited case of "be"), sits at the bottom of this hierarchy.

### Notional vs. formal agreement

**Notional vs. formal agreement** has a long history. Traditional grammar emphasizes formal (grammatical) features, while functionalist approaches (Halliday 1994) treat agreement as a realization of the interpersonal metafunction -- the speaker's construal of the subject's referential status.

The tension between notional and formal agreement is not a flaw in the grammar but a genuine indeterminacy in how speakers conceptualise referents. When a speaker says "The team is ready," the speaker construes the team as a single entity. When a speaker says "The team are ready," the speaker construes the team as a collection of individuals. Both construals are valid, and the agreement system allows speakers to signal which construal they intend. This flexibility is a feature, not a bug.

### Agreement and processing

Psycholinguistic research on agreement has produced several important findings. The **attraction effect** (Bock & Miller 1991) shows that speakers produce more agreement errors when a plural noun intervenes between a singular subject and the verb. This effect is robust across languages and has been demonstrated in both production and comprehension experiments.

The **marking and morphing** model (Eberhard, Cutting & Bock 2005) proposes that agreement is computed through a combination of notional (semantic) number and grammatical (formal) number features. When the two conflict -- as with collective nouns -- speakers may produce either singular or plural agreement depending on which set of features is more salient in the current context.

### Agreement in dependency grammar

In **Dependency Grammar** (Tesniere 1959), agreement is treated as a relationship between a governor and a dependent, where the dependent's form is determined by the governor's features. The verb is the governor and the subject is the dependent (or vice versa, depending on the framework). Dependency approaches to agreement are widely used in computational linguistics because they can be efficiently computed.

### Agreement and language change

Agreement systems are not static -- they change over time. The history of English shows a consistent trend toward reduction in agreement morphology, from the rich system of Old English to the minimal system of Modern English. This trend is not unique to English; many Indo-European languages have undergone similar reductions (French has simpler agreement than Latin, and the Scandinavian languages have simpler agreement than Old Norse).

The question for Modern English is whether the third-person singular -s will continue to erode. Some linguists predict that it will eventually disappear, following the same trajectory as the Old English agreement endings. Others argue that the -s ending is stable because it serves a useful disambiguating function, helping listeners identify the subject of the sentence. The outcome depends on whether the communicative benefit of the -s ending outweighs the cognitive cost of producing it.

In AAVE and some Southern American English varieties, the reduction has already occurred. Third-person singular zero-marking is the norm in these varieties, suggesting that English can function perfectly well without the -s ending. The persistence of the -s in Standard English may be due more to social factors (its association with formal education and Standard English) than to grammatical necessity.

## Connections [Master]

This unit connects to the surrounding language sequence through prerequisites (22.01.03, 22.01.04) and successors (22.01.16). In the wider Babel Bible map, subject-verb agreement links to logic and formal semantics (where agreement is a syntactic reflex of semantic features), rhetoric and argument (where agreement errors undermine credibility), literary interpretation (where agreement choices create stylistic effects), and world/civics units where public language shapes law and institutions.

### Connections to formal semantics

In formal semantics, the number feature of the subject NP has semantic consequences. A singular NP ("the cat") presupposes a single referent, while a plural NP ("the cats") presupposes multiple referents. Subject-verb agreement is the syntactic reflex of this semantic distinction. When agreement fails, the sentence becomes harder to interpret because the syntactic signal does not match the semantic content. In formal writing, agreement errors can undermine the writer's credibility, leading readers to question the accuracy of the content as well as the grammar. This is why agreement is so heavily tested in academic settings and why professional writers proofread carefully for agreement errors.

### Connections to world and civics

Legal language is particularly sensitive to agreement because laws must apply precisely to their intended targets. An agreement error in a legal document could create ambiguity about whether a statute applies to a single entity or to multiple entities. "The defendant is entitled to..." unambiguously applies to one person. "The defendants are entitled to..." unambiguously applies to multiple people. The agreement marker removes ambiguity and ensures that the law is applied correctly.

In political discourse, agreement choices can subtly influence how audiences perceive groups. "The government has decided" treats the government as a unified actor, which may imply consensus. "The government have decided" (British English) treats the government as a collection of individuals, which may imply deliberation among different viewpoints. These subtle agreement differences can shape public perception of political events.

### Connections to language acquisition

Children acquiring English produce agreement errors in predictable patterns. Early child English shows frequent omission of the third-person singular -s: "He go" instead of "He goes." This error persists longer than many other grammatical features, sometimes until age 4-5. The persistence of this error may be due to the limited salience of the -s ending (it is a single consonant that is often unstressed) and the fact that English agreement is so sparse that children have limited evidence from which to infer the rule.

Research on child language acquisition has also found that children are more likely to produce correct agreement when the subject is a pronoun ("he runs") than when the subject is a full noun phrase ("the boy runs"). This pattern suggests that agreement with pronouns is learned earlier because pronouns provide a clearer, more consistent cue for the -s ending. Full noun phrases require the child to compute the number of the head noun, which is an additional step.

Bilingual children acquiring two languages simultaneously may show cross-linguistic influence in agreement. A Spanish-English bilingual child might produce "she cantan" (Spanish plural verb with English pronoun) or "they sings" (English singular verb with plural subject). These errors show that the two agreement systems are not fully separated in the child's developing grammar and that features from one language can temporarily influence the other.

### Connections to computational linguistics

Subject-verb agreement is a standard evaluation metric for natural language processing systems. The ability to detect and correct agreement errors is a benchmark for grammar checkers, and the ability to maintain correct agreement in generated text is a benchmark for language models. Agreement errors remain one of the most common errors in machine-generated text, especially in complex sentences with intervening phrases.

Recent large language models have shown significant improvement in agreement accuracy, but they still struggle with certain patterns. Sentences with long dependencies (where the subject and verb are far apart) and sentences with attractors (plural nouns between a singular subject and the verb) remain challenging. These difficulties mirror the challenges faced by human speakers and provide evidence that agreement computation is genuinely difficult, not just for machines but for the human language processing system.

### Connections to education

Subject-verb agreement is one of the most frequently taught and tested grammatical concepts in English language education. Standardised tests at every level (from elementary school assessments to the SAT and GRE) include questions that test agreement knowledge. The frequency of agreement testing reflects the practical importance of correct agreement in academic and professional writing.

However, research on grammar instruction has shown that teaching agreement rules in isolation (through worksheets and drills) is less effective than teaching agreement in the context of meaningful writing tasks. Students who learn agreement by editing their own writing retain the knowledge better than students who learn it through decontextualised exercises. This finding supports a pedagogical approach that integrates grammar instruction with writing instruction rather than treating grammar as a separate subject.

### Connections to literary style

Writers exploit agreement for stylistic effect. Collective nouns allow writers to shift between singular and plural construal: "The army marched through the valley. They were exhausted." The first sentence uses singular agreement ("army marched"), treating the army as a unit. The second sentence uses a plural pronoun ("they"), shifting to an individual construal. This shift is common in narrative fiction and demonstrates how agreement choices contribute to point of view and characterisation.

In poetry, agreement can be manipulated for rhythm or emphasis. A poet might write "The stars is shining" to create a dialectal or archaic effect. The agreement "error" is deliberate and signals that the speaker comes from a particular region or time period. In standard literary prose, however, correct agreement is expected, and agreement errors distract readers from the content and undermine the writer's credibility.

## Historical context [Master]

Old English had far richer agreement morphology than Modern English. Verbs agreed with their subjects in both **person** (1st, 2nd, 3rd) and **number** (singular, plural) across all tenses, and adjectives agreed with nouns in case, gender, and number. The progressive erosion of inflectional endings from Middle English onward (driven by language contact after the Norman Conquest, phonological reduction of unstressed syllables, and the loss of grammatical gender) left Modern English with the minimal agreement system we see today: essentially just the third-person singular *-s* in the present tense and the *was/were* distinction.

### The spread of -s

The spread of -s was gradual. In Shakespeare's plays (c. 1590-1613), both forms coexist: "He goes" and "He goeth" both appear, sometimes in the same play. The -s form was initially colloquial and informal, while -eth was more formal. Over time, -s became the standard in all registers except the most conservative religious language, where -eth persisted in phrases like "he maketh" in traditional liturgical texts.

The spread of northern -s to the south of England is a well-documented example of dialect diffusion. Several factors contributed to its spread: the increasing economic and political importance of northern England during the Industrial Revolution, the influence of Scots English (which used -s), and the general tendency for colloquial forms to replace formal ones over time. The replacement of -eth by -s was essentially complete by the 18th century, though -eth survived in religious and poetic contexts well into the 19th century.

### The influence of printing on agreement

The introduction of printing to England by William Caxton in 1476 had a significant effect on agreement. Printers needed to standardise spelling and grammar to make texts consistent, and this standardisation often favoured one agreement form over another. The -s ending was easier to print than the -eth ending (fewer letters), which may have contributed to its adoption in printed texts.

The standardisation of English grammar through printing also codified certain agreement rules as "correct" while treating regional variations as "incorrect." Before printing, variation in agreement was normal and accepted. After printing, the forms chosen by influential printers and grammar books became the standard, and other forms were stigmatised. This process of standardisation created the prescriptive tradition that still influences attitudes toward agreement today.

### The loss of the thou/thee distinction

Early Modern English had a second-person singular pronoun "thou" with its own agreement forms: "thou walkest," "thou art," "thou hast." The loss of "thou" in standard English (it survives in some dialects and in religious language) eliminated the first- and second-person singular agreement distinctions, leaving only the third-person singular as a productive agreement marker.

The loss of "thou" was driven by social factors. "You" was originally a plural form that came to be used as a polite singular address (like French "vous"). Over time, the polite form became the default, and "thou" retreated to intimate or derogatory use before disappearing from Standard English entirely. This sociolinguistic change had the grammatical consequence of further reducing the English agreement paradigm.

### Agreement in the digital age

Digital communication has introduced new agreement challenges. The singular "they" as a gender-neutral pronoun ("They are a talented writer") requires plural agreement with a singular referent. This usage has a long history in English (attested from the 14th century) but has become much more common in the 21st century as gender-neutral language has gained prominence. Style guides now widely accept singular "they" with plural agreement.

Social media language shows extensive variation in agreement. Posts in African American Vernacular English, Caribbean English, and other varieties use agreement patterns that differ from Standard English. Descriptive linguists see this variation as a natural feature of multilingual, multidialectal digital communication, while prescriptivists worry about the erosion of standard agreement norms.

The study of agreement in digital communication is a growing area of sociolinguistic research. Researchers have found that agreement variation in social media posts correlates with the writer's regional background, age, and social identity. The third-person singular -s is one of the most variable features in online English, with rates of omission varying from near-zero in formal posts to near-complete absence in casual posts by speakers of AAVE, Caribbean English, and other varieties with reduced agreement.
