## Intuition [Beginner]

**Voice** is about who or what gets to be the subject of the sentence. It tells you whether the subject *does* the action or *receives* the action.

- **Active voice:** The subject performs the action. "The dog **chased** the cat."
- **Passive voice:** The subject receives the action. "The cat **was chased** by the dog."

Notice how the same event can be described from either angle. The meaning is nearly the same, but the focus shifts. In the active sentence, the dog is the star. In the passive sentence, the cat is.

You form the passive with a form of **be** + **past participle**:

| Active | Passive |
|--------|---------|
| writes | is written |
| wrote | was written |
| is writing | is being written |
| has written | has been written |
| will write | will be written |

You can also include the original doer of the action using **by** + agent ("by the dog"), or you can leave it out entirely when the agent is unknown, obvious, or unimportant.


The passive voice is one of the most useful and most debated features of English grammar. Writers, teachers, and editors often say to avoid the passive voice, but this advice is oversimplified. The passive voice is not wrong -- it is a grammatical tool with specific uses. The key is knowing when to use it and when the active voice would be better.

Think of it this way: the active voice is like pointing a camera at the person doing something. The passive voice is like pointing the camera at the person or thing being affected. Both angles show the same event, but they focus on different participants. A good writer chooses the right angle for each sentence.

In everyday speech, the active voice is more common because it is direct and natural. "I ate the pizza" sounds more natural than "The pizza was eaten by me." But in scientific and academic writing, the passive voice is very common because the focus is on what was done, not who did it. "The solution was heated to 100 degrees" is standard in a lab report, even though "We heated the solution to 100 degrees" would also be grammatically correct.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Convert each active sentence to passive.

**1. "The committee approved the budget."**
- Object ("the budget") becomes subject.
- Verb "approved" (past simple) becomes "was approved" (was + past participle).
- Agent: "by the committee."
- Result: "The budget was approved by the committee."

**2. "Someone stole my bicycle."**
- Object ("my bicycle") becomes subject.
- Verb "stole" becomes "was stolen."
- Agent: we can omit "someone" because it is vague.
- Result: "My bicycle was stolen."

**3. "The company is launching a new product."**
- Object ("a new product") becomes subject.
- Verb "is launching" (present progressive) becomes "is being launched."
- Result: "A new product is being launched by the company."


**4. "Shakespeare wrote Hamlet."**
- Object ("Hamlet") becomes subject.
- Verb "wrote" becomes "was written."
- Agent: "by Shakespeare."
- Result: "Hamlet was written by Shakespeare."

**5. "The students have completed the assignment."**
- Object ("the assignment") becomes subject.
- Verb "have completed" (present perfect) becomes "has been completed."
- Result: "The assignment has been completed by the students."

**6. "Someone will announce the results tomorrow."**
- Object ("the results") becomes subject.
- Verb "will announce" (future) becomes "will be announced."
- Agent: we can omit "someone" because it is vague.
- Result: "The results will be announced tomorrow."

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Voice** is a grammatical category that indicates the relationship between the participants in an event (agent, patient, theme) and the grammatical roles (subject, object) assigned to them.

In English, voice is marked on the verb phrase:

- **Active voice:** The subject of the clause corresponds to the semantic agent. The verb appears in its standard form. Example: *The wind destroyed the roof.*
- **Passive voice:** The subject of the clause corresponds to the semantic patient or theme. The verb phrase takes the form **be** (inflected for tense and agreement) + **past participle**. The agent, if expressed, appears in an oblique **by**-phrase. Example: *The roof was destroyed by the wind.*

The passive applies to monotransitive, ditransitive, and complex transitive clauses:


Not all verbs can form passives. Only transitive verbs -- those that take a direct object -- can be passivised. Intransitive verbs like "sleep," "arrive," and "happen" cannot form passives because there is no object to promote to subject position. "He slept" cannot become "He was slept" because "slept" has no direct object. However, prepositional verbs like "sleep in" can form prepositional passives: "The bed was slept in" is grammatical because "the bed" is the object of the preposition "in."

Modal verbs combine with the passive in predictable ways. "Can fix" becomes "can be fixed." "Should clean" becomes "should be cleaned." "Must address" becomes "must be addressed." The modal precedes "be" + past participle: "The problem must be addressed immediately."

| Clause type | Active | Passive |
|-------------|--------|---------|
| Monotransitive | She **sent** the letter. | The letter **was sent** (by her). |
| Ditransitive | She **gave** him the book. | He **was given** the book. / The book **was given** to him. |
| Complex transitive | They **consider** her a genius. | She **is considered** a genius. |

With ditransitive verbs, either object can become the passive subject, producing two possible passive constructions (the **indirect object passive** and the **direct object passive**).

Agent omission is the norm rather than the exception in English passives. Corpus studies show that roughly 80% of English passive clauses lack an expressed agent.


### How to form the passive

The passive is formed with the appropriate form of the verb "be" plus the past participle of the main verb. The tense of the passive is carried by the form of "be," not by the main verb. "Is written" is present tense passive. "Was written" is past tense passive. "Will be written" is future tense passive. "Is being written" is present progressive passive. "Has been written" is present perfect passive.

Each of these forms corresponds to an active construction. "She writes the report" becomes "The report is written by her." "She wrote the report" becomes "The report was written by her." "She is writing the report" becomes "The report is being written by her." The tense changes, but the passive pattern (be + past participle) remains the same.

### The get-passive

English has a second passive construction using get instead of be: The

English has a second passive construction using "get" instead of "be": "The window got broken." "She got fired." "He got promoted." The get-passive is more informal than the be-passive and typically emphasises the event rather than the resulting state. It often carries connotations of adversity or benefit: "got broken" (adverse), "got promoted" (beneficial).

The get-passive also implies that the subject was somehow involved or affected in a way that the be-passive does not. "She got surprised" suggests the surprise happened to her directly, while "She was surprised" is more neutral. The get-passive is more common in spoken English and informal writing.

### Ditransitive passives

With ditransitive verbs (verbs that take two objects), either object can become the passive subject. "She gave him the book" has two passive forms: "He was given the book" (indirect object becomes subject) and "The book was given to him" (direct object becomes subject). Both are grammatically correct.

The choice between the two passives depends on what the writer wants to focus on. "He was given the book" makes the recipient the topic. "The book was given to him" makes the gift the topic. In practice, the indirect object passive is more common and more natural-sounding in English.

### Descriptive vs. prescriptive perspectives

Prescriptive grammar strongly favours the active voice. Style guides from Strunk and White to modern college writing handbooks advise writers to use the active voice as a general rule. The reasoning is that the active voice is more direct, more concise, and makes agency clear. "The committee made the decision" is shorter and more transparent than "The decision was made by the committee."

Descriptive linguistics observes that the passive voice is perfectly grammatical and serves important communicative functions. In scientific writing, the passive voice is conventional because it focuses on procedures and results rather than on the researchers who performed them. In legal writing, the passive voice is used to describe actions without specifying who performed them, which can be important for legal precision.

### ESL transfer errors with passive voice

ESL learners often transfer passive voice patterns from their first language. Speakers of languages that lack a passive construction may overuse the active voice in English, producing sentences like "Someone stole my car" when "My car was stolen" would be more natural. Speakers of languages with more frequent passive usage may overuse the passive in English, producing sentences like "It was decided that the meeting is postponed" when "We decided to postpone the meeting" would be more natural.

Common ESL errors with the passive voice include using the wrong auxiliary ("The car has stolen" instead of "The car was stolen"), failing to use the past participle ("The car was steal" instead of "The car was stolen"), and using the passive with intransitive verbs ("The accident was happened" instead of "The accident happened"). These errors reflect the complexity of the English passive construction, which requires correct auxiliary selection, participle morphology, and transitivity awareness.

### Passive voice in World Englishes

The passive voice is used differently across World Englishes. In Indian English, the passive voice is used more frequently than in British or American English, reflecting the influence of substrate languages where the passive is more common. In Singapore English, the passive may be expressed with different patterns. In Nigerian English, the passive voice may be used in contexts where British English would prefer the active voice.

These differences are not errors but features of distinct varieties of English. Each variety has developed its own conventions for using the passive voice, shaped by contact with local languages and by the communicative needs of its speakers. Understanding these differences is important for teachers of English as an international language.



The prescriptive bias against the passive voice reflects a preference for agency and transparency in writing, not a grammatical rule. The passive voice becomes a problem only when it is used to obscure agency inappropriately, not when it is used to shift focus appropriately.

### World Englishes: voice variation

Voice usage varies across World Englishes. In Indian English, the passive voice is used more frequently than in British or American English, particularly in formal and academic writing. This higher frequency may reflect the influence of Indian languages, some of which use passive-like constructions more extensively.

In Caribbean English creoles, the passive voice is often expressed through word order and context rather than through the be + past participle construction. In Nigerian English, the passive voice is sometimes used with intransitive verbs in ways that would be ungrammatical in Standard British English. These variations reflect the influence of local languages on English grammar.

### AAVE and voice

African American Vernacular English uses the passive voice similarly to Standard American English, but the get-passive is more common in AAVE than in formal Standard English: "He got caught" rather than "He was caught." This preference for the get-passive reflects the general tendency of spoken English to prefer "get" over "be" for passive constructions.

### Cross-linguistic comparison

Passive constructions vary significantly across languages. In Japanese, the passive is formed by adding the suffix "-rare" to the verb stem, and Japanese also has an adversative passive that indicates the subject was adversely affected. In Spanish, the passive can be formed with "ser" + past participle or with a reflexive construction. In Mandarin Chinese, the passive is marked by the particle "bei" before the agent.

In Spanish, the passive can be formed with "ser" + past participle or with a reflexive construction using "se." The reflexive passive is more common in everyday Spanish. In German, the passive uses "werden" + past participle, and there is also a stative passive using "sein" + past participle that describes a resulting state rather than an event. In Mandarin Chinese, the passive is marked by the particle "bei" before the agent, and traditionally had adversative connotations, though this has weakened in modern usage.

In ergative-absolutive languages like Basque and Inuktitut, passive-like constructions behave differently because the alignment of grammatical relations differs from nominative-accusative languages like English. The agent of a transitive verb is marked differently, which means that passive constructions involve different grammatical operations than in English.

### Impersonal passives

Some languages allow impersonal passives that do not promote any argument to subject position. In German, "Es wurde getanzt" means "It was danced" or "There was dancing." This construction passivises an intransitive verb without having a patient to promote. English does not generally allow impersonal passives, though constructions like "It is said that..." and "It is believed that..." function similarly by passivising a clausal complement without promoting a specific argument.

### ESL transfer errors with voice

Second-language learners face predictable challenges with the English passive. Speakers of languages without a distinct passive construction may avoid the passive entirely. A common error is overusing the passive in academic writing, producing sentences like "It was decided by the researchers that the experiment would be conducted" when the active "The researchers decided to conduct the experiment" would be clearer. Speakers of languages with different passive formations may transfer their L1 patterns into English, producing incorrect agent phrases or ungrammatical passive forms.

## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

1. **Agent promotion and patient demotion.** The passive takes the patient (direct object of the active clause) and promotes it to subject position. The agent is demoted to an oblique by-phrase or omitted entirely. This changes what the sentence is "about" -- its topic.

The passive achieves this promotion by restructuring the clause. In the active sentence The

2. **The get-passive.** English has a second passive construction using *get* instead of *be*: "The window got broken." The *get*-passive typically emphasizes the event rather than the resulting state and often carries connotations of adversity or benefit to the subject. It is more common in informal registers.

3. **Adjectival passive vs. verbal passive.** "The door was closed" is ambiguous: it can be a verbal passive (an event: someone closed the door) or an adjectival passive (a state: the door was in a closed condition). Tests for distinguishing them include modification by adverbs (*The door was tightly closed* favors adjectival) and the ability to add a by-phrase (*The door was closed by the janitor* forces a verbal reading).

4. **Middle voice and anti-passive.** English has constructions that resemble the passive but are not true passives: "This book sells well" (middle), "The car steers easily." These have active morphology but passive-like semantics -- the subject is the patient, but no agent is implied. Cross-linguistically, some languages mark a distinct **middle voice** for such constructions.

5. **When to prefer active voice.** In most writing contexts, active voice is preferred because it is direct, concise, and makes agency clear. Passive voice is appropriate when the agent is unknown, irrelevant, or deliberately backgrounded, or when the patient is the topic of the discourse.


### When to use passive voice

The passive voice is appropriate in several situations. First, when the agent is unknown: "My car was stolen" (you do not know who stole it). Second, when the agent is obvious or universal: "The criminal was arrested" (presumably by the police). Third, when the agent is unimportant: "The building was constructed in 1920" (who built it is not relevant). Fourth, when the patient is the topic: "The experiment was conducted over three days" (the experiment is what the paragraph is about). Fifth, when you want to avoid responsibility: "Mistakes were made" (no specific person is blamed).

In scientific writing, the passive voice is conventional in methods sections because the focus is on what was done, not who did it. "The samples were collected from three sites" is standard scientific prose. In legal writing, the passive voice is used for precision: "The defendant was found guilty" focuses on the verdict, not on who reached it.

### Passive voice and readability

Excessive use of the passive voice can make writing harder to read. Passive sentences are often longer than their active equivalents because they require an auxiliary verb and sometimes a by-phrase. They can also be vague about agency, leaving the reader wondering who performed the action.

A good rule of thumb is to use the active voice by default and switch to the passive only when there is a clear reason to do so. If you cannot identify a reason for using the passive, the active voice is probably the better choice. This is not a grammatical rule but a stylistic guideline that produces clearer, more engaging writing.


In practice, most professional writers use a mixture of active and passive voice. Academic papers typically use the passive in methods sections and the active in discussion sections. News articles use the passive when the agent is unknown and the active when the agent is newsworthy. Business reports use the passive for problems and the active for solutions. Skilled writers shift between voices as their message requires, without adhering rigidly to either extreme.

## Linguistic theory [Master]

The analysis of passive has been central to generative syntax since Chomsky (1957). In **Government and Binding** theory (Chomsky 1981), passive is treated as a **movement** operation: the object NP moves from its base position (object of V) to the subject position (Spec, IP), leaving behind a trace. The passive morpheme (*-en*) absorbs the verb's external theta-role (the agent) and its accusative Case-assigning ability, which is what forces the object to move -- it can no longer receive Case in situ.

In **Minimalism** (Chomsky 1995, 2000), the passive is analyzed as involving **smuggling** (Collins 2005): the passive participle's Voice head lacks the probe that would assign the external theta-role, and the object moves to Spec, T to satisfy the EPP feature of T. The by-phrase is analyzed as an adjunct introducing the suppressed external argument, sometimes treated as a PP headed by *by* with a nominal complement that receives the agent theta-role.

**Baker, Johnson, and Roberts (1989)** argue that the passive morpheme (*-en*) is itself a clausal subject argument that absorbs the external theta-role. Under this analysis, passive sentences are essentially ergative: the passive morpheme functions like a pronominal agent.

Cross-linguistically, passive constructions vary significantly. Some languages (Lithuanian, Latin) have both **personal** and **impersonal** passives. Impersonal passives can promote intransitive subjects: "It was slept." Others (German, Dutch) allow **prepositional passives** more freely than English. Languages with **ergative-absolutive** alignment (Basque, Inuktitut) have passive-like constructions that behave differently because the alignment of grammatical relations differs from nominative-accusative languages like English.

The functional-typological literature (Siewierska 1984; Keenan & Dryer 2007) classifies passives along dimensions of **agent prominence** (is the agent expressed?), **subject properties** (does the promoted NP acquire full subject properties?), and **verb generality** (which verbs can passivize?).

### Passive in construction grammar

Construction Grammar treats the passive as a construction -- a learned pairing of form and meaning. The passive construction carries the meaning "focus on the patient" and has the form "be + past participle (+ by-phrase)." On this view, the passive is not derived from the active by a transformational rule but is an independent construction that speakers learn alongside the active construction.

This approach explains why the passive has properties that cannot be predicted from the active alone. For example, the get-passive has connotations of adversity and benefit that are not present in the active. These connotations are part of the construction itself, not derived from the active voice. Construction Grammar captures these construction-specific meanings more naturally than transformational approaches.

### The passive in corpus linguistics

Corpus studies of the passive voice have produced several important findings. The passive is most common in academic writing and news reporting, and least common in conversation and fiction. Within academic writing, the passive is most frequent in methodology sections and least frequent in discussion sections. The most common passive verbs in academic writing are "be based on," "be associated with," "be used," and "be considered."

These corpus findings challenge the prescriptive advice to avoid the passive. The passive is not overused in most genres -- it is used appropriately in contexts where the patient is the topic. The problem is not the passive itself but the inappropriate use of the passive in contexts where the active voice would be more effective.

### Connections to formal semantics

In formal semantics, the passive voice is analysed as a mapping between thematic roles and grammatical functions. The passive alternation changes which argument is mapped to the subject position, but it does not change the underlying event structure. "The dog chased the cat" and "The cat was chased by the dog" describe the same event with the same participants.

### Connections to rhetoric

The passive voice is a powerful rhetorical tool. Politicians use it to avoid responsibility: "Mistakes were made" is more politically palatable than "I made mistakes." Scientists use it to establish objectivity: "The data were collected over six months" suggests standard, unbiased procedures. Understanding how the passive shifts focus and agency is essential for critical reading.

### Connections to language acquisition

Children acquire the passive voice relatively late compared to the active voice. English-speaking children begin producing active sentences around age 2, but productive use of the passive does not appear until age 4-5. The late acquisition reflects the passive's syntactic complexity: the child must understand that the subject is not the agent, produce the be + past participle construction, and optionally include a by-phrase.

### Connections to literature

Writers use the passive voice for specific literary effects. In mystery novels, the passive can conceal the agent: "The murder was committed between midnight and dawn" -- who did it? The passive withholds the agent's identity, creating suspense. In scientific fiction, the passive voice creates a clinical, detached tone.

### Connections to world and civics

Legal language relies heavily on the passive voice. Laws are written in the passive because they apply universally: "Taxes shall be paid by all employed persons." In political speech, the passive can obscure responsibility: "Revenue was redirected" avoids specifying who made the decision. Citizens who understand the passive voice can recognise when it is being used to hide agency.


### Connections to journalism

News reporting uses the passive voice strategically. Headlines often use the passive to focus on the affected party: "Mayor attacked during press conference" rather than "Man attacks mayor." The passive construction makes the mayor the topic. In crime reporting, the passive voice is used to describe victims: "A woman was assaulted on Main Street" focuses on the event and location rather than the perpetrator.

### Connections to computational linguistics

Passive voice detection is a standard task in natural language processing. Grammar checkers flag excessive passive use, and readability metrics penalise high passive-to-active ratios. Machine translation systems must correctly convert between active and passive voice when translating between languages with different voice conventions.

### Connections to education

Teaching the passive voice is a staple of English language education. Students learn to form the passive, convert between active and passive, and choose the appropriate voice for different contexts. Research shows that teaching students to recognise and revise unnecessary passive constructions improves writing clarity.

### Connections to critical discourse analysis

Critical discourse analysis examines how the passive voice is used to exercise and conceal power. When a government report states "Villages were destroyed in the military operation," the passive voice removes the military from the role of agent, making the destruction seem like a natural event rather than a deliberate action. Politicians, corporations, and institutions routinely use the passive voice to manage public perception, evade accountability, and present contested actions as inevitable or impersonal.

Recognising these patterns is a form of media literacy. When students learn to ask "Who is the agent?" every time they encounter a passive construction, they develop the ability to detect evasion and spin in public language. This critical reading skill is directly relevant to citizenship and civic engagement.

### Connections to philosophy of language

The passive voice raises philosophical questions about agency and responsibility. When we say "The window got broken," we describe an event without attributing it to an agent. Some philosophers argue that the passive voice reflects a worldview in which events happen rather than being caused by agents. The active voice, by contrast, reflects a worldview in which agents act and are responsible for their actions.

This philosophical dimension explains why the passive voice is so controversial in writing instruction. The debate is not just about grammar but about how we represent causation and responsibility in language. Writers who default to the passive voice may unconsciously adopt a perspective that obscures human agency, while writers who favour the active voice adopt a perspective that foregrounds it.

### Connections to psychology

Psychological research has shown that the passive voice affects how people assign blame and responsibility. In studies where participants read about accidents described in either the active or passive voice, those who read passive descriptions were less likely to assign blame to specific individuals. "The vase was broken" led to lower blame ratings than "Sarah broke the vase." This effect demonstrates that grammatical voice is not just a stylistic choice but a cognitive tool that shapes how we think about events and responsibility.

### Connections to translation

Translating the passive voice between languages requires careful attention to voice conventions. A sentence that uses the passive naturally in English may need to be translated using the active voice in a language where the passive is less common or carries different connotations. Translators must decide whether to preserve the voice of the original or adapt to the conventions of the target language. This decision is particularly important in legal and diplomatic translation, where the passive voice may be used for specific rhetorical purposes.

## Historical context [Master]

The English passive construction descends from the Old English passive, which used *wesan*/*beon* + past participle for action passives and *weorthan* + past participle for eventive passives. *Weorthan* ("to become") fell out of use as a passive auxiliary during the Middle English period, leaving *be* as the sole passive auxiliary.

### Old English passive origins

The passive construction in English has a long history that stretches back to Old English, where it was formed differently from Modern English. Old English used two auxiliary verbs to form the passive: "wesan" and "beon" (both ancestors of Modern English "be") were used for statal passives, while "weorthan" (meaning "to become") was used for eventive passives. The distinction between these two types of passive was important in Old English because it conveyed different aspectual meanings.

The loss of "weorthan" during the Middle English period simplified the passive system. By the 14th century, "be" had become the sole passive auxiliary in English. This simplification is part of the broader trend in the history of English toward reduced inflectional morphology and a more analytic grammatical structure.



The *get*-passive emerged in colloquial English around the 18th century and expanded in the 19th-20th centuries. It is still considered informal and is absent from most prescriptive writing guides, though it is fully grammatical in spoken English.

The prescriptive bias against passive voice in English writing is largely a 20th-century phenomenon. Fowler (1926) and Strunk & White (1959) both cautioned against overuse, and the advice became a staple of American composition pedagogy. However, corpus studies show that academic and scientific writing uses passive voice heavily (often 25-30% of finite clauses), particularly in methodology sections where the focus is on procedures rather than agents.


George Orwell's famous essay "Politics and the English Language" (1946) criticised the passive voice as a tool of political evasion, arguing that writers use it to obscure agency and avoid responsibility. Orwell's critique was influential but has been challenged by linguists who point out that the passive voice has many legitimate uses and that Orwell himself used it frequently.

### The prescriptive tradition and its critics

The prescriptive tradition against the passive voice has been criticised by linguists for several reasons. First, it often misidentifies what the passive voice is. Strunk and White, in their influential guide, give examples of "the passive voice" that are not actually passive constructions. Second, the advice to avoid the passive voice ignores the many legitimate uses of the passive, such as maintaining topic continuity, focusing on the patient, and describing processes where the agent is unknown or irrelevant. Third, the prescriptive tradition treats the passive voice as a sign of bad writing, when in fact the passive is used by skilled writers in every genre.

Linguists such as Geoffrey Pullum have argued that the advice to avoid the passive voice is among the most harmful pieces of prescriptive grammar advice, because it is based on a misunderstanding of what the passive is and how it functions in discourse. The passive voice is a grammatical tool, not a stylistic sin.

### Corpus evidence for passive frequency

Corpus linguistics has provided detailed data on how frequently the passive voice is used in different registers. The Longman Grammar of Spoken and Written English found that the passive voice accounts for approximately 25% of finite verb phrases in academic writing, 15% in news reporting, 8% in fiction, and only 2% in conversation. These figures show that the passive voice is a feature of formal, planned prose rather than spontaneous speech.

Within academic writing, the passive is most common in the hard sciences and least common in the humanities. Physics papers use the passive in roughly 30% of finite clauses, while English literature papers use it in only about 10%. This variation reflects different rhetorical conventions across disciplines rather than different levels of writing quality.





### Passive voice in scientific writing

Scientific writing has used the passive voice extensively since the 17th century, when the Royal Society of London established conventions for scientific reporting. In recent decades, some journals have shifted toward encouraging the active voice: "We conducted the experiment" is now accepted or even preferred. This shift reflects a growing recognition that the active voice is clearer and that using "we" does not undermine scientific objectivity.

### Voice conventions in different academic fields

Different academic disciplines have different voice conventions. In the natural sciences (physics, chemistry, biology), the passive voice has been the traditional standard for methods and results sections: "The solution was heated to 100 degrees Celsius." In the social sciences, the active voice is increasingly common: "We interviewed 50 participants." In the humanities, the active voice is standard, and the passive voice is used sparingly. These disciplinary differences reflect different attitudes toward the role of the researcher in the research narrative.

### The passive in patents and technical writing

Patent applications use the passive voice extensively to describe inventions in impersonal, universal terms. "A device is disclosed comprising a housing and a circuit board" uses the passive to describe the invention without reference to the inventor. This impersonal style is required by patent law and has influenced the style of technical writing more broadly.

Technical manuals often use the passive in instructional contexts: "The battery should be removed before servicing." This passive instruction is more formal than "Remove the battery before servicing" but less direct. Modern technical writing increasingly favours the active imperative for instructions because it is clearer and easier to follow.

### Passive voice and readability scores

Readability formulas like the Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level and the Gunning Fog Index penalise the use of the passive voice because passive sentences tend to be longer and more complex than active equivalents. A text with many passive constructions will receive a higher readability score (meaning it is harder to read) than the same text rewritten in the active voice.

However, readability formulas are imperfect measures. A text that uses the passive voice appropriately may be easier to understand than a text that forces every sentence into the active voice. The passive voice can improve readability when it maintains a consistent topic across sentences, reducing the cognitive effort required to follow the argument.

In academic writing, the passive voice often serves a topic-management function. If the paragraph is about a chemical compound, then making that compound the subject of every sentence (including passive sentences) helps the reader follow the argument. Rewriting these sentences in the active voice would shift the subject from the compound to various researchers, making the paragraph harder to follow even though each individual sentence would be shorter.



### Passive voice in business writing

Business writing has traditionally used the passive voice extensively, particularly in reports, memos, and formal correspondence. "Your complaint has been received and is being reviewed" is a standard business formulation that uses the passive to sound professional and impersonal. In recent years, business writing guides have encouraged a shift toward the active voice for clarity and directness: "We received your complaint and are reviewing it."

The shift from passive to active in business writing reflects a broader cultural change toward transparency and directness in professional communication. Modern business writing favours short, direct sentences that identify who is doing what. The passive voice is still used when the agent is unimportant or when a more formal tone is desired, but the default has shifted from passive to active.

### Passive voice in creative writing

In creative writing, the passive voice is generally avoided because it weakens the prose. "The dragon was slain by the knight" is less vivid than "The knight slew the dragon." The active voice creates stronger images and more dynamic sentences. However, skilled writers sometimes use the passive voice deliberately for specific effects -- to slow the pace, to create distance, or to shift focus to the affected character.

In mystery and detective fiction, the passive voice is used to conceal information. "The body was found in the library" does not specify who found it, allowing the author to reveal this information later. The passive voice creates a gap in the narrative that the reader is compelled to fill, driving engagement and suspense.

### Passive voice in journalism

Journalistic writing uses the passive voice in specific contexts. Crime reports use the passive when the perpetrator is unknown: "A bank was robbed on Main Street last night." Political reporting uses the passive to maintain neutrality: "The decision was made after extensive deliberation." Headlines use the passive to frontload newsworthy information: "New policy announced by the president" puts "new policy" first because it is the most newsworthy element.

The choice between active and passive in journalism is driven by news values rather than grammatical preference. Journalists learn to use the voice that best serves the story, placing the most important information at the beginning of the sentence.

### Passive voice in sports writing

Sports writing provides interesting examples of the passive voice because it often describes events where the agent is the focus. "The goal was scored in the 89th minute" uses the passive to focus on the goal rather than the scorer. "The record was broken three times this season" focuses on the record rather than the athletes who broke it. In sports commentary, the passive voice creates dramatic effect by delaying the identification of the agent.

### Passive voice in recipe and instruction writing

Recipes and instructions use the passive voice to describe steps without specifying who performs them. "The onions are sauteed until translucent" and "The form should be completed in black ink" are both passive constructions that describe a process rather than giving a direct command. This impersonal style makes the instruction feel universal and authoritative, as though it applies to anyone performing the task. Modern recipe and instruction writing has shifted toward the active imperative ("Saute the onions until translucent") for greater directness.



### The middle voice

English has constructions that resemble the passive but are not true passives. "This book sells well" has an active verb but a patient subject. "The car steers easily" works the same way. These are called middle constructions and they exist between active and passive. Some languages have a dedicated middle voice for such constructions.

The middle voice is important because it shows that the active-passive binary is not complete. There are constructions that are neither fully active nor fully passive. These middle constructions are common in advertising ("This cream applies smoothly"), product descriptions ("The fabric washes well"), and technical writing ("The software installs automatically").
These middle constructions are productive, meaning speakers can create new ones freely. "This new material cuts easily" is immediately understood even if the specific sentence has never been uttered. This productivity is evidence that the middle voice is a grammatical pattern in English, not merely a collection of fixed expressions.
Understanding the middle voice helps learners recognise that English voice is a spectrum rather than a binary choice between active and passive.



### Passive voice and gender

Research in feminist linguistics has examined how the passive voice is used to obscure gendered violence. Headlines like "Woman was attacked" use the passive voice to describe the victim while leaving the attacker unnamed. In contrast, headlines about crimes committed by women often use the active voice: "Woman attacks man." This pattern has been documented across multiple news outlets and reflects implicit biases in how the passive voice is deployed.

### Passive voice in contracts and legal documents

Legal documents use the passive voice extensively because it creates a sense of impersonal authority. "Taxes shall be paid" sounds more authoritative than "You shall pay taxes." The passive voice makes the law seem to come from an abstract authority rather than from specific legislators. This use of the passive voice has both practical benefits (generality) and rhetorical effects (authority and distance).

### Passive voice in the digital age

The internet has intensified the debate over passive voice. Grammar-checking tools flag passive constructions and suggest active alternatives. While these tools can help identify overuse, they sometimes encourage unnecessary changes. Not every passive sentence needs to be converted to active voice. The best writing uses both voices strategically, choosing each for its communicative purpose.

Automated grammar checkers like the one built into Microsoft Word and Grammarly flag passive constructions with a green underline and suggest active alternatives. While these tools can help writers identify overuse, they cannot determine whether a particular passive construction is appropriate in context. Writers who follow every suggestion from their grammar checker may end up with prose that is technically active but less effective than the original passive version.

The key insight is that voice is a rhetorical choice, not a grammatical error. The passive voice becomes a problem only when it is used habitually and thoughtlessly, without regard for its effects on clarity and emphasis. When used intentionally, the passive voice is a powerful tool for controlling information flow, managing emphasis, and achieving specific communicative goals.
