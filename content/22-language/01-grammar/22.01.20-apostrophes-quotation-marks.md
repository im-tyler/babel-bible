## Intuition [Beginner]

Two punctuation marks handle the jobs of showing ownership, shortening words, and quoting speech.

**Apostrophes (')** have two jobs:

1. **Possession** -- showing that something belongs to someone:
   - "The dog's bone" (the bone belongs to the dog)
   - "Sarah's book" (the book belongs to Sarah)
   - "The students' project" (the project of the students)

2. **Contractions** -- showing that letters have been removed:
   - "don't" = "do not" (the "o" in "not" is removed)
   - "it's" = "it is" (the "i" in "is" is removed)
   - "they're" = "they are"

**Quotation marks (" " or ' ')** have two jobs:

1. **Direct speech** -- showing exactly what someone said:
   - She said, "I will be there at noon."

2. **Titles of short works** -- articles, poems, songs, episodes:
   - I read the article "Climate Change and the Future" in today's paper.

**Common traps:**

| Wrong | Right | Why |
|-------|-------|-----|
| "The dog wagged it's tail" | "The dog wagged its tail" | "its" = possession, no apostrophe (like "his," "her") |
| "Your going to be late" | "You're going to be late" | "you're" = "you are" |
| "Their here already" | "They're here already" | "they're" = "they are" |
| "I saw three dog's" | "I saw three dogs" | Plurals do NOT take apostrophes |

The key rule: **apostrophes never make plurals.** "Apple's for sale" is always wrong (unless the apple owns something).
 Plurals are formed by adding -s or -es without any apostrophe. This is one of the most important rules in English punctuation.
 This rule applies to all nouns, numbers, and abbreviations. Apostrophes are for possession and contraction only.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**1. Add the correct apostrophe: "The boys bicycle was stolen"**
- One boy owns the bicycle.
- "The boy's bicycle was stolen."

**2. Add the correct apostrophe: "The students homework was late"**
- Multiple students' homework.
- "The students' homework was late." (plural possessive)

**3. Choose the correct word: "The dog lost (its / it's) collar"**
- We need the possessive form = "its" (no apostrophe).
- "The dog lost its collar."

**4. Choose the correct word: "(Your / You're) going to need an umbrella"**
- "You are going to need" = contraction = "You're."
- "You're going to need an umbrella."

**5. Punctuate: She said I will be there soon**
- Direct speech needs quotation marks and a comma.
- She said, "I will be there soon."

**6. Punctuate: He asked did you finish the report**
- Direct question inside quotation marks.
- He asked, "Did you finish the report?"


### More worked examples with apostrophes

**7. "The womens restroom is on the second floor"**
- "Women" is a plural that does not end in s, so the possessive adds s.
- "The women restroom is on the second floor."

**8. "I cant believe its already December"**
- "Cant" is a contraction of "cannot," so it needs an apostrophe.
- "Its" is a contraction of "it is," so it also needs an apostrophe.
- "I cannot believe it is already December."

**9. "The Jones family bought a new car"**
- "Jones" is a singular proper noun ending in s. Both "Jones" and "Jones" are acceptable.
- "The Jones family bought a new car."

**10. "She said the meeting is at three oclock"**
- "Oclock" is a contraction of "of the clock," so it needs an apostrophe.
- "She said the meeting is at three oclock."

### Common apostrophe patterns

The possessive apostrophe follows these patterns. For singular nouns, add s: "the dog bone," "the teacher desk." For plural nouns ending in s, add only an apostrophe: "the dogs bones," "the teachers lounge." For plural nouns not ending in s, add s: "the children toys," "the men room." For singular proper nouns ending in s, either s or s is acceptable: "James book" or "James book."

### Common quotation mark patterns

Quotation marks enclose direct speech: She said, "Hello." They enclose titles of short works: I read the article "Climate Solutions." They enclose words used metalinguistically: The word "dog" has three letters. They enclose scare quotes: The "expert" was wrong. They are NOT used for indirect speech: She said that she was leaving (no quotation marks).

### Quotation marks with other punctuation

The placement of punctuation with quotation marks follows different conventions in American and British English. American English places periods and commas inside quotation marks regardless of whether they belong to the quoted material: She said, "Hello." British English places them according to logical relevance: She said, "Hello". Question marks and exclamation points follow the same logic in both systems: inside if part of the quoted material, outside if part of the surrounding sentence.

### Prescriptive vs. descriptive perspectives

Prescriptive grammar teaches strict rules for apostrophes and quotation marks. Descriptive linguistics recognises that usage varies. The greengrocer apostrophe, while non-standard, is pervasive. Quotation mark conventions differ between American and British English. Understanding these differences helps writers choose appropriate conventions.

### World Englishes and apostrophes

Different varieties of English use apostrophes somewhat differently. In informal writing across all varieties, the greengrocer apostrophe remains common. In some Caribbean English creoles, apostrophes are used differently in contractions. In Indian English, apostrophes in abbreviations follow both British and local conventions.

### ESL challenges with apostrophes

ESL learners often struggle with apostrophes because many languages do not use them for possession or contraction. Speakers of languages without a possessive case may confuse the possessive apostrophe with the plural -s. The distinction between its and it is is a particular challenge because it violates the general pattern where apostrophe-s marks possession.

### Apostrophes in academic writing

Academic writing uses apostrophes for possession but avoids most contractions. Formal academic style requires writing out contractions: do not instead of do not, cannot instead of cannot. The possessive apostrophe is used freely for noun phrases.

### Quotation marks in academic writing

Academic writing uses quotation marks for direct quotations. Block quotations are set off without quotation marks. Scare quotes are used sparingly to signal contested terms. The conventions for punctuation placement with quotation marks are determined by the style guide being followed.

### Apostrophes in digital communication

In digital communication, apostrophes are often omitted in contractions. This omission is partly due to the inconvenience of typing on mobile devices and partly due to the informal register. Some argue the apostrophe in contractions is becoming optional in informal English.
 The gap between formal and informal apostrophe usage continues to widen as digital communication becomes more prevalent in everyday life.
 This trend is particularly pronounced in text messaging and social media, where speed and convenience take precedence over formal orthography.

### The apostrophe and possessive pronouns

One of the most confusing aspects of the apostrophe is the distinction between possessive pronouns and contractions that sound identical. "Its" (possessive) vs. "it is" (contraction). "Your" (possessive) vs. "you are" (contraction). "Their" (possessive) vs. "they are" (contraction). "Whose" (possessive) vs. "who is" (contraction). The pattern is consistent: possessive pronouns never take apostrophes, while contractions always do. The confusion arises because the regular possessive pattern for nouns uses an apostrophe ("the dog bone"), but possessive pronouns follow a different pattern that does not use apostrophes.

### Apostrophes with joint possession

When two people own something together, the apostrophe goes after the last name only: "John and Mary house." When they own separate things, each name gets an apostrophe: "John and Mary houses" (John has one house and Mary has a different one). This distinction is important for precise writing, particularly in legal and business documents where the difference between joint and separate ownership matters.

### Apostrophes in time expressions

Apostrophes are used in time expressions where the noun is understood but omitted. "a day work" (a day of work), "a week pay" (a week of pay), "a moment notice" (a moment of notice). These constructions are possessive in origin: the day owns the work, the week owns the pay. The apostrophe is required in formal writing, though it is sometimes omitted in informal contexts.

### Quotation marks and dialogue tags

Dialogue tags identify the speaker in a passage of dialogue. "Hello," she said. The dialogue tag "she said" is not part of the quotation and is not enclosed in quotation marks. The comma after "Hello" belongs to the sentence structure, not to the quotation, but American convention places it inside the quotation marks. Understanding the relationship between dialogue tags and quotation marks is essential for writing fiction and narrative prose.

### The apostrophe in brand names

Many brand names use or omit apostrophes in non-standard ways. "Levi Strauss" (no apostrophe in the possessive). "L.L. Bean" (no apostrophe). "Trader Joe" (possessive apostrophe). "Walgreens" (no apostrophe). These variations reflect branding decisions rather than grammatical rules. Writers should follow the official spelling of each brand name, regardless of whether it conforms to standard apostrophe rules.
 When in doubt, check the company website for the official spelling.

### Quotation marks and irony in digital communication

In digital communication, quotation marks are used to convey irony and sarcasm. "Sure, that sounds like a "great" idea." The scare quotes around "great" signal that the writer means the opposite. This use of quotation marks is a pragmatic device that relies on the reader understanding that the quoted word is not meant literally. The convention is well established in informal digital communication.
 The convention is so well established that it has been adopted by auto-correct features, which sometimes add scare quotes to words that the algorithm identifies as potentially ironic.

### Apostrophes and the double genitive

English has a construction called the double genitive, which combines the preposition "of" with a possessive form: "a friend of mine," "a book of hers." The apostrophe is required in the possessive form even though the preposition "of" already marks the possessive relationship. This construction is idiomatic and has no direct equivalent in many other languages. ESL learners often find it confusing because it seems redundant.

### Quotation marks and legal documents

Legal documents use quotation marks to indicate the exact text of a statute, regulation, or contract provision. The precision of quotation marks in legal writing is essential because the meaning of a legal document can depend on the exact words used. Legal drafting guides provide detailed rules for the use of quotation marks, including rules for nested quotations, block quotations, and quotations within parenthetical citations.

### The apostrophe and compound nouns

Compound nouns present special challenges for the apostrophe. "My mother-in-law house" raises the question: where does the apostrophe go? The rule is that the apostrophe goes at the end of the entire compound noun: "my mother-in-law house." "My brothers-in-law houses" follows the same principle: the apostrophe goes after the last word of the plural compound noun. These rules are not intuitive and must be learned explicitly.

### Quotation marks and translations

When quoting a translation, writers use quotation marks for the translated text even though the original words were in a different language. "He said, I am innocent." The quotation marks enclose the English translation, not the original words. If the original words are also given, they may be placed in parentheses after the translation: "He said, I am innocent."

### The apostrophe in music

Song lyrics often use apostrophes in contractions to match the rhythm of the music. "Cause" for "because," "round" for "around," "til" for "until." These contractions are conventional in popular music but would be considered informal in prose writing. The apostrophe signals to the reader that the word is a contraction, not a misspelling.

### Punctuation within quotations

When a quotation contains its own punctuation, the writer must decide whether to reproduce it exactly or to adapt it to the new context. In most cases, the original punctuation is reproduced within the quotation marks, and additional punctuation is added outside. "Did she say, I am leaving?" The question mark belongs to the enclosing sentence, not to the quotation, so it goes outside the quotation marks (in British English) or inside (in American English, depending on the style guide).


### The apostrophe in different dialects

Some English dialects use apostrophes differently from standard English. In some non-standard dialects, apostrophes are used in words that do not have them in standard English: "them" contracted to "em," "of" contracted to "o."" These non-standard apostrophe uses reflect phonological reductions that are part of the dialect grammar.

### Teaching quotation marks effectively

Teaching quotation marks involves teaching several distinct skills: recognising direct speech, placing quotation marks around the quoted words, punctuating the dialogue tag, and handling punctuation inside and outside the quotation marks. Each of these skills must be taught explicitly, because the conventions are not intuitive. Students often struggle with the placement of punctuation, particularly the difference between American and British conventions.

### The future of the apostrophe

The apostrophe faces an uncertain future. Digital communication has reduced its use in contractions. The greengrocer apostrophe remains pervasive despite centuries of prescriptive prohibition. Auto-correct features both help and hinder correct apostrophe use. Some linguists predict that the apostrophe will eventually be simplified or lost, while others believe it will persist because it serves a useful disambiguating function. The future of the apostrophe will be determined by the millions of writers who use or omit it every day.

### Quotation marks and indirect speech

Indirect speech does not use quotation marks. "She said that she was leaving" is indirect speech, reported without quotation marks because the exact words are not given. The shift from direct to indirect speech involves changes in pronouns ("I" becomes "she"), tense (present becomes past), and time expressions ("now" becomes "then"). Understanding the distinction between direct and indirect speech is essential for accurate reporting and for avoiding plagiarism.
 In academic writing, the failure to distinguish between direct and indirect speech can lead to accusations of misquoting or misrepresenting a source.

### Apostrophes in place names

Place names often contain apostrophes that reflect historical possessive forms. In recent decades, there has been a trend toward removing apostrophes from place names. The United States Board on Geographic Names has a policy of removing apostrophes unless required by law or custom. This policy reflects the broader trend toward simplification of English orthography.

### Quotation marks in email

In email, quotation marks are used for direct quotations and for titles. Scare quotes are common in email: The deadline is firm. The scare quotes signal doubt about whether the deadline is truly firm.

### The apostrophe and collective nouns

Collective nouns present challenges for apostrophe placement. The team victory takes an apostrophe because team is singular. The teams victory takes an apostrophe after the s because teams is plural. The ambiguity of collective nouns requires careful attention to context.

### Quotation marks and paraphrase

Paraphrasing involves restating ideas in your own words. A paraphrase does not use quotation marks. However, if distinctive phrases from the original are retained, they must be enclosed in quotation marks. The boundary between paraphrase and quotation requires judgment.
 In academic writing, the general rule is that any phrase of three or more consecutive words taken from a source must be enclosed in quotation marks. This rule is a guideline rather than an absolute standard, and the determination of when quotation marks are required depends on the distinctiveness of the original phrasing and the conventions of the academic discipline.

### Quotation marks in fiction vs. non-fiction

The conventions for quotation marks differ between fiction and non-fiction. In fiction, quotation marks are used primarily for dialogue, and the conventions for dialogue punctuation are well established. In non-fiction, quotation marks are used for direct quotations from sources, for scare quotes, and for titles. The conventions for non-fiction quotation marks are more varied and depend on the genre and the style guide being followed. Understanding these differences is important for writers who work in multiple genres.

### The apostrophe and email addresses

Apostrophes cannot be used in email addresses, URLs, or many digital identifiers. This restriction creates problems for people whose names contain apostrophes, such as OConnor and DArtagnan. In digital contexts, these names are typically written without apostrophes (OConnor, DArtagnan), which can create confusion about the correct spelling of the name. This digital limitation is one of the pressures contributing to the potential decline of the apostrophe in English.



## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Apostrophe.** The apostrophe serves two functions in standard English:

1. **Possessive marker.** The apostrophe + s ('s) marks the possessive (genitive) case of nouns. The rules:

| Noun type | Possessive form | Example |
|-----------|----------------|---------|
| Singular noun | add 's | the dog's bone |
| Plural noun ending in s | add ' only | the dogs' bone |
| Plural noun not ending in s | add 's | the children's toys |
| Singular proper noun ending in s | add 's or ' | James's book / James' book |

The choice between "James's" and "James'" is a matter of style guide. Chicago Manual of Style and MLA recommend 's. AP Style recommends the ' alone.

2. **Contraction marker.** The apostrophe marks the omission of one or more letters or numbers:
- don't (do not), can't (cannot), it's (it is), '99 (1999), rock 'n' roll

**Quotation marks.** Quotation marks enclose direct speech, titles of short works, and words used metalinguistically. The two systems:

| Style | Primary quotes | Nested quotes | Region |
|-------|---------------|---------------|--------|
| American | "..." | '...' | US |
| British | '...' | "..." | UK (traditional) |

**Punctuation placement with quotation marks.** The American convention places periods and commas inside closing quotation marks regardless of whether they belong to the quoted material. The British convention places them according to logical relevance (inside if part of the quote, outside if not). Question marks and exclamation points follow the same logic in both systems: inside if part of the quoted material, outside if part of the surrounding sentence.

## Key concepts [Intermediate+]

1. **The its/it's distinction.** "Its" is the possessive form of "it" (analogous to "his," "her," "their" -- none of which take apostrophes). "It's" is always a contraction of "it is" or "it has." This is the single most common apostrophe error in English. The confusion arises because the regular possessive pattern for nouns uses 's, but possessive pronouns (*my, your, his, her, its, our, their*) never do.

2. **The greengrocer's apostrophe.** Using an apostrophe to form a plural ("apple's," "CD's," "1980's") is called the **greengrocer's apostrophe** (from its prevalence on produce signs). It is non-standard in formal writing. Some style guides accept the apostrophe for clarity with certain plurals (letters: "mind your p's and q's"; abbreviations with periods: "Ph.D.'s"), but regular plurals of nouns never take apostrophes.

3. **Possessive vs. descriptive (attributive) noun phrases.** Not every noun before another noun is possessive. "A teachers' union" (the union belongs to teachers) uses the possessive. "A teachers college" (a college for teachers, not owned by them) uses the attributive form without an apostrophe. The distinction is semantic, not syntactic, and many such cases are disputed.

4. **Scare quotes.** Quotation marks around a word to signal distance, irony, or skepticism ("The so-called "expert" gave terrible advice") are called **scare quotes**. They are a pragmatic device with no syntactic function. Overused, they read as snide or non-committal. When a word is used in a non-literal or contested sense, italics or the phrase *so-called* often accomplish the same purpose more cleanly.

5. **Single quotation marks for titles and specialized uses.** Beyond nesting, single quotation marks appear in certain fields: philosophy uses them for mentioning words (the word 'dog' has three letters), linguistics uses them for glosses and meanings, and British publishing traditionally uses them as primary quotation marks.


### Quotation marks in different genres

Different genres use quotation marks differently. Fiction uses them primarily for dialogue. Journalism uses them for direct quotations from sources. Academic writing uses them for quotations from sources and for scare quotes. Legal writing uses them for quotations from statutes and precedents. Poetry uses them for various purposes, sometimes omitting them entirely. Understanding these genre conventions is important for effective communication.
 Each genre has its own conventions for quotation mark use, and writers who understand these conventions can use quotation marks more effectively in their own writing.

### The apostrophe in names and brands

The apostrophe in names and brand names often violates standard apostrophe rules. "St. John's" uses an apostrophe for possession. "Starbucks" has no apostrophe despite being a possessive form. "McDonald's" uses an apostrophe for possession. "Harrods" omits the apostrophe. These variations reflect historical accident, branding decisions, and the gradual simplification of English orthography. Writers should follow the official spelling of each name or brand, regardless of whether it follows standard apostrophe rules.
 The trend in modern branding is toward omitting apostrophes, partly for visual simplicity and partly because apostrophes can cause problems in URLs and digital identifiers.

### Apostrophes in legal writing

Legal writing uses apostrophes precisely because the difference between a possessive and a plural can affect legal meaning. "The defendants motion" (one defendant) versus "the defendants motion" (multiple defendants) is a meaningful distinction in legal context. Legal drafting guides devote considerable attention to apostrophe rules to ensure that legal documents are unambiguous.

### Scare quotes and irony

Scare quotes are quotation marks used around a word to signal that the writer does not endorse its usual meaning. "The expert gave terrible advice" signals that the writer questions whether the person is truly an expert. Scare quotes are a pragmatic device that can convey irony, skepticism, or distance. They should be used sparingly because overuse makes writing seem snide or non-committal. When a word is used in a non-literal sense, italics or the phrase so-called often accomplish the same purpose more cleanly.

### Quotation marks and dialogue in fiction

In fiction, quotation marks are the standard convention for marking dialogue in American and contemporary British English. "Hello," she said. "How are you?" Some writers, notably James Joyce and Cormac McCarthy, have experimented with omitting quotation marks for dialogue, creating a more fluid narrative style. The choice of whether to use quotation marks for dialogue is a stylistic decision that affects the pacing and intimacy of the narrative.

### The apostrophe and the plain language movement

The plain language movement advocates for clearer, simpler writing in government and business documents. One of its recommendations is to minimise the use of apostrophes where they create ambiguity. For example, "the employees benefits" could be read as a possessive (the benefits belonging to employees) or as a plural followed by a noun (benefits for employees). Plain language drafting avoids such constructions by rewriting: "the benefits provided to employees."
 "the benefits provided to employees." This recommendation reflects the principle that legal and business documents should be unambiguous, and that apostrophes can create ambiguity when the reader cannot determine whether a word is possessive or plural.
 and that punctuation should serve clarity above all else. When apostrophe usage creates confusion rather than clarity, rewriting the sentence is the better approach.

### Apostrophes in contractions: a detailed guide

English contractions fall into several patterns. Auxiliary verb + not: "do not" = "do not," "is not" = "is not," "will not" = "will not" (irregular). Pronoun + auxiliary verb: "I am" = "I am," "they are" = "they are," "she will" = "she will." Pronoun + have: "I have" = "I have," "they have" = "they have." Question word + auxiliary: "who is" = "who is," "what is" = "what is." Miscellaneous: "let us" = "let us," "of the clock" = "of the clock." Learning these patterns helps writers use apostrophes correctly in contractions.

### Quotation marks in different languages

Different languages use different quotation marks. English uses double quotes as primary. French uses guillemets. German uses low-99 quotes (below the line) and 66 quotes (above the line). Spanish uses angle quotes. Chinese and Japanese use bracket-like marks. These differences are important for translation and multilingual documents. When translating, quotation marks must be converted to the conventions of the target language.

### The apostrophe and language change

The apostrophe is a relatively recent addition to English orthography, and it may not be permanent. Some linguists have predicted that the apostrophe will eventually be lost from English, as it has been from many other languages that once used it. The loss of the apostrophe would simplify English orthography but would also create ambiguity (its vs. it is, were vs. were vs. where). Whether the apostrophe survives depends on whether writers continue to find it useful for distinguishing between possessives, plurals, and contractions.

### Teaching apostrophes effectively

Teaching apostrophes is one of the most challenging tasks in writing instruction. Students often learn the rules (apostrophe for possession and contraction, not for plurals) but continue to make errors because they have not internalised the grammatical distinctions that the rules encode. Effective instruction focuses on helping students distinguish between possessives, plurals, and contractions by identifying the grammatical function of each word. When students can say "this word shows possession" or "this word is a plural" or "this word is a contraction," they can apply apostrophe rules correctly.

### Quotation marks and plagiarism

Quotation marks play an important role in academic integrity. When a writer uses the exact words of a source, they must enclose those words in quotation marks and provide a citation. Failure to do so constitutes plagiarism, even if the source is cited. The absence of quotation marks around borrowed words is one of the most common forms of plagiarism in student writing. Teaching students to use quotation marks correctly is therefore not just a matter of punctuation but a matter of academic ethics.

### The apostrophe in poetry

Poets use apostrophes for elision to maintain metrical patterns: "oer" for "over," "tis" for "it is," "neer" for "never." These poetic contractions are different from everyday contractions because they are chosen for metrical rather than phonological reasons. In modern free verse, poetic contractions are less common, but they still appear in formal verse and in pastiches of older poetry.

### Scare quotes and media literacy

Understanding scare quotes is a component of media literacy. When a news article uses scare quotes around a term, it signals that the term is contested or that the writer does not endorse its usual meaning. "The treatment was "successful" in 30% of cases" suggests that the writer questions whether 30% counts as success. Recognising scare quotes helps readers identify the writer's attitude toward the information being presented.

### Block quotations

Block quotations are long quotations (typically more than 40 words in APA style, more than 4 lines in MLA style) that are set off from the main text by indentation. Block quotations do not use quotation marks because the indentation itself signals that the material is quoted. The conventions for block quotations vary by style guide, but the principle is the same: the formatting signals that the material is a direct quotation.

### Ellipsis with quotation marks

When omitting words from a quotation, writers use an ellipsis (...) to mark the omission. The ellipsis appears within the quotation marks: "The results were ... inconclusive." If the omission comes at the end of a sentence, the ellipsis is followed by a period: "The results were surprising...." The conventions for ellipsis with quotation marks vary by style guide, but the principle is that the ellipsis marks the omission and the quotation marks mark the boundaries of the quotation.

### Quotation marks in headlines

Newspaper headlines often use single quotation marks instead of double quotation marks to save space: Mayor says city is thriving instead of Mayor says "city is thriving." This convention is specific to journalism and does not apply to other genres. The use of single quotation marks in headlines is a space-saving measure that dates back to the era of narrow newspaper columns.

## Linguistic theory [Master]

**The apostrophe as orthographic marker of morphosyntactic category.** The apostrophe is unusual among punctuation marks in that it participates directly in the morphological system of English. The possessive s is a **clitic** -- a morpheme that attaches phonologically to the last word of a noun phrase rather than to the head noun alone. "The King of England crown" shows s attaching to "England," not "King." This distinguishes the English possessive from a simple suffix.

### The clitic nature of the possessive

The fact that the possessive is a clitic rather than a suffix has important grammatical consequences. Because the clitic attaches to the end of the entire noun phrase, it can follow modifiers, postpositions, and other elements that would not normally receive a suffix. "The President of the United States address" is grammatical because the clitic attaches to "States," the last word of the noun phrase "the President of the United States." This behaviour is predicted by the clitic analysis and cannot be explained by a simple suffix analysis.

### Contractions and syntactic change

The contraction apostrophe marks a phonological reduction with syntactic consequences. Contractions like "do not" and "cannot" are not merely shortened forms -- they have distinct syntactic distributions. "Are not I?" is the standard tag question for first person singular, but "Am not I?" is ungrammatical in standard English, and "Am I not?" is formal. The contraction has become the primary form. This is an example of how phonological reduction can lead to grammatical change.

### The loss of contractions in formal writing

The prohibition against contractions in formal writing is a prescriptive convention that does not reflect the grammar of most speakers. In speech, contractions are the norm, and their absence sounds formal or stilted. The prescriptive rule against contractions in formal writing developed in the 18th century as part of the broader project of standardising English grammar. Descriptive linguists recognise that contractions are fully grammatical in all registers except the most formal.

### Quotation marks and reported speech

The distinction between **direct** and **indirect** speech is fundamental to the grammar of quotation. Direct speech ("She said, I am leaving") presents a verbatim record and requires quotation marks. Indirect speech ("She said that she was leaving") integrates the reported clause into the syntax of the matrix sentence (note the shift from first to third person, present to past tense) and takes no quotation marks.

### The use-mention distinction

Quotation marks create a **mention** context rather than a **use** context, in the sense of the use-mention distinction from philosophy of language. The quoted words are presented as linguistic objects rather than being deployed for their referential content. This is why scare quotes work: they signal that a word is being mentioned (with ironic distance) rather than straightforwardly used. The use-mention distinction is important in logic, philosophy, and linguistics.

### Quotation marks in programming and markup

Quotation marks have been adopted by programming languages and markup languages for specific syntactic purposes. In most programming languages, quotation marks delimit string literals. In HTML and XML, quotation marks delimit attribute values. These programming uses are unrelated to the natural language functions of quotation marks, but they illustrate the cultural significance of punctuation conventions. The single quote and double quote have different functions in different programming languages, which can cause confusion for people who work with both natural and programming languages.

### Cross-linguistic quotation conventions

Not all languages use the same quotation marks. English uses double quotes as primary. French uses guillemets. German uses low-99 quotes. Chinese and Japanese use their own bracket-like quotation marks. These differences are important for translation and for multilingual documents. When translating between languages, quotation marks must be converted to the conventions of the target language.

### The evolution of quotation practices

Corpus studies show that quotation practices have changed significantly over the past century. Direct quotation has become more common in journalism and narrative prose, partly due to the influence of interview-based reporting. The conventions for punctuation placement with quotation marks remain a point of divergence between American and British English, reflecting different theories of whether punctuation belongs to the quoted material or the enclosing sentence.

## Connections [Master]

### Connections to philosophy

The use-mention distinction, which quotation marks encode orthographically, is central to the philosophy of language. Gottlob Frege, Bertrand Russell, and W.V.O. Quine all discussed the distinction between using a word and mentioning it. Quotation marks are the written language device that makes this distinction visible, allowing writers to present words as objects of discussion rather than as tools of reference.

### Connections to rhetoric

Quotation marks serve rhetorical functions beyond marking direct speech. Scare quotes signal irony, skepticism, or distance. Block quotations lend authority by presenting extended passages from authoritative sources. Epigraphs (quotations at the beginning of a work) set the tone and signal the intellectual context. These rhetorical uses of quotation marks are part of the broader system of persuasive writing.

### Connections to psychology

The processing of direct and indirect speech has been studied in psycholinguistics. Direct speech (with quotation marks) activates different brain regions than indirect speech, suggesting that readers process direct speech as if they were hearing the speaker's voice. This finding has implications for narrative writing: direct speech creates a more vivid, immersive experience than indirect speech.

### Connections to education

Teaching apostrophes and quotation marks is a standard component of writing instruction. The challenge with apostrophes is the persistent confusion between possessives, plurals, and contractions. The challenge with quotation marks is the complexity of punctuation placement rules, particularly the difference between American and British conventions. Effective instruction focuses on the grammatical principles behind the rules rather than rote memorisation.

### Connections to literary analysis

Literary writers use quotation marks in distinctive ways. Some writers, like James Joyce and Cormac McCarthy, omit quotation marks for dialogue, creating a more fluid narrative style. Others, like Jane Austen, use quotation marks precisely to distinguish dialogue from narration. The analysis of quotation mark usage is a legitimate tool of literary criticism, revealing the writer's approach to narrative voice and dialogue.
 The use or omission of quotation marks for dialogue is one of the most distinctive stylistic choices a fiction writer can make.

### Connections to journalism

Journalism uses quotation marks extensively for direct quotations from sources. The conventions for quotation in journalism are strict: direct quotations must be verbatim, and any changes or omissions must be marked with brackets or ellipses. Misuse of quotation marks in journalism, such as placing words in quotation marks that the source did not say, is a serious ethical violation.

## Historical context [Master]

The apostrophe was introduced into English in the 16th century, originally to mark **elision** -- the omission of a sound. Its earliest uses were for contractions: *tis* (it is), *oer* (over), *neer* (never). The use of the apostrophe for the possessive developed later, in the 17th century, by analogy with the contraction: the possessive ending *-es* (Old English *-es*) was reinterpreted as *e+s*, with the apostrophe marking an omitted letter. This is why the possessive apostrophe exists: it is historically an elision marker on a suffix that has since been further reduced.
 The apostrophe was essentially repurposed from marking elision to marking possession, creating the dual function that persists today.

### Old English and the genitive

Old English had a fully inflected genitive case that did not require an apostrophe. The apostrophe was introduced after the Old English period, when the genitive ending had been reduced to -s and was reinterpreted as a contraction. The historical development explains why the possessive apostrophe is an anomaly: it marks a morphological category (possession) using an orthographic device (the apostrophe) that was originally reserved for elision.

### The standardisation of apostrophe rules

The standardisation of apostrophe rules occurred in the 18th century. Before then, usage was inconsistent. Shakespeare used apostrophes for elision but not consistently for possession. Robert Lowth *A Short Introduction to English Grammar* (1762) helped regularise the rules, establishing the distinction between singular possessive (s) and plural possessive ().

### The greengrocer apostrophe in history

The greengrocer apostrophe is not a modern invention. Historical corpora show that apostrophes were used in plurals as early as the 17th century, particularly before *s* in foreign-derived words and abbreviations. The prescriptive prohibition against plural apostrophes solidified in the 19th century. Despite centuries of prescriptive prohibition, the greengrocer apostrophe remains pervasive in informal writing and signage.

### Quotation marks in manuscript culture

Quotation marks originated as **marginal marks** (called *diple*, from the Greek for "double") placed in the margins of manuscripts to highlight important passages, especially citations of scripture. By the late medieval period, the marks migrated into the text itself. The modern paired quotation mark developed in 16th-century English printing. Single quotation marks were originally the primary form in English; double quotation marks became primary in American English during the 19th century.

### American vs. British quotation conventions

The American convention of placing periods and commas inside quotation marks derives from the aesthetics of typesetting. In the era of handset type, a period or comma next to a quotation mark could create an ugly gap or damage the fragile pieces of type. Placing the punctuation inside the quotes was a practical solution that became a rigid convention. British English follows the logical convention of placing punctuation inside or outside based on whether it belongs to the quoted material.

### The apostrophe in the digital age

The apostrophe faces challenges in the digital age. Auto-correct features on smartphones often add or remove apostrophes inappropriately. URLs and email addresses cannot contain apostrophes, which creates problems for names and businesses that contain them. The greengrocer apostrophe persists in digital communication, and some linguists predict that the apostrophe may eventually be simplified or lost from English orthography.
 This prediction is based on the observation that the apostrophe is one of the most frequently misused punctuation marks, and that many languages function perfectly well without it. However, the apostrophe does serve a useful disambiguating function, and its loss would create ambiguities that writers would need to resolve in other ways.
 The future of the apostrophe is therefore uncertain, and its fate will be determined by usage patterns over the coming decades.

### Apostrophes and numbers

The apostrophe is used with numbers in specific contexts. Decades: "the 1980s" (no apostrophe in most style guides, though "the 1980s" was once common). Abbreviated years: "the class of 99" (apostrophe for omitted digits). Plurals of letters: "mind your ps and qs" (apostrophe for clarity, because "ps and qs" looks confusing without it). Plurals of abbreviations with periods: "two PhDs" (apostrophe for clarity). Regular plurals of numbers and abbreviations do not need apostrophes: "the 1990s," "three PhDs," "several CEOs."
: the 1990s, three PhDs, several CEOs. The trend in modern style guides is toward omitting apostrophes in plurals of numbers and abbreviations.

### Quotation marks and titles

The conventions for marking titles depend on the length of the work. Short works (articles, poems, songs, episodes, short stories) are enclosed in quotation marks. Long works (books, albums, films, TV series, journals) are italicised. The distinction is between works that are part of a larger whole (quotation marks) and works that stand alone (italics). Understanding this distinction helps writers punctuate titles correctly.

### The apostrophe and formal vs. informal register

The use of apostrophes differs between formal and informal registers. In formal writing, contractions are avoided: "do not" instead of "do not." In informal writing, contractions are the norm: "do not" sounds stiff and unnatural. The choice of whether to use contractions is one of the most visible markers of register in English writing. Writers who shift between formal and informal registers must be aware of the different conventions for apostrophe use in each register.

### The apostrophe and singular they

The possessive form of singular "they" is "their," which does not take an apostrophe: "Someone left their umbrella." This usage has become standard in English for referring to a person whose gender is unknown or irrelevant. The possessive "their" follows the same pattern as "his" and "her" -- possessive pronouns never take apostrophes.
 The singular they has been used in English for centuries and is attested in the works of Shakespeare, Austen, and many other canonical writers.

### Quotation marks in technical documentation

Technical documentation uses quotation marks for specific purposes: to introduce new terms, to indicate literal values, and to mark user interface elements. "Click the "Save" button." "Enter "yes" to continue." These conventions help the reader distinguish between instructions and the literal text that must be typed. In technical documentation, the conventions for quotation mark use are specified by the style guide of the organisation or the industry.

### The apostrophe and sign language

Sign languages do not use apostrophes, because they are visual-gestural languages without a written form. However, when sign language is transcribed using notation systems, apostrophes may be used to represent certain grammatical features. The absence of apostrophes in sign languages is a reminder that punctuation is a feature of written language, not of language in general.


### Connections to philosophy of language

The study of apostrophes and quotation marks connects to philosophy through the concept of reference. Possessive apostrophes mark a referential relationship between a possessor and a possessed entity. Quotation marks mark the boundary between use and mention, which is fundamental to the theory of reference. These connections show that punctuation is not merely orthographic but participates in the semantic and pragmatic systems of language.
