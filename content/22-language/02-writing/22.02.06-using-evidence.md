## Intuition [Beginner]

Evidence is the ground an argument stands on. Without it, you have opinions. With it, you have a case. But not all evidence is equal. A single dramatic story is not as strong as a controlled study. A quote from a celebrity is not as credible as a quote from a subject-matter expert. Learning to use evidence well means learning both how to find it and how to judge it.

Imagine you are building a house. Your claim is the roof -- the thing you want your reader to accept. Evidence is the foundation and walls that hold the roof up. If the foundation is weak (bad evidence), the roof falls. If the walls are missing (no explanation of how evidence supports the claim), the roof has nothing to rest on. Strong arguments need both solid evidence and solid reasoning that connects the evidence to the claim.

![Diagram: A house structure where the roof is labeled "Claim," the walls are labeled "Reasoning," and the foundation is labeled "Evidence."](/images/22-language/evidence-house.png)

Four main types of evidence appear in most writing:

1. **Statistical / quantitative:** Numbers, percentages, measurements. "Traffic fatalities decreased 23 percent after the law took effect." Strong when the data is from a reliable source; weak when cherry-picked or misleadingly presented.

2. **Expert testimony:** Statements from authorities in the relevant field. "According to Dr. Chen, a virologist at Stanford, the variant is no more severe than previous strains." Strong when the expert's field matches the claim; weak when the expert is speaking outside their area.

3. **Anecdotal / illustrative:** Specific stories or examples. "Maria, a single mother in Detroit, lost her job when the factory closed." Not proof of a general trend, but powerful for showing what a trend means in human terms.

4. **Analogical:** Comparisons to similar situations. "When Ireland taxed plastic bags in 2002, usage dropped 90 percent; a similar policy here would likely produce comparable results." Useful for prediction, but only if the situations are genuinely similar in relevant respects.

Good writers do not pick one type and rely on it exclusively. They combine types: statistics to show the scale, an anecdote to make it real, expert testimony to explain why it is happening.

The process of using evidence in writing has four steps: (1) **find** evidence that is relevant to your claim, (2) **evaluate** whether the evidence is credible and representative, (3) **select** the strongest pieces for your argument, and (4) **integrate** the selected evidence into your prose with context and explanation. Many writers focus on finding and skip evaluating, which leads to weak arguments supported by unreliable sources. Others focus on selecting and skip integrating, which produces papers full of quotations that never connect to the thesis.

Evidence also has a **scope** problem. A national statistic about education funding may not apply to your local school district. A study of college students may not generalize to working adults. Evidence is always evidence about something specific, and writers must be careful not to stretch it beyond what it actually shows. The scope of your claim should match the scope of your evidence. If your evidence is about one city, limit your claim to that city. If you want to make a national claim, find national evidence.

Evidence must be **integrated**, not dropped in. A quotation without context or explanation is not evidence -- it is decoration. After presenting evidence, explain what it means and how it supports your claim. The reader should never have to guess why a piece of evidence is there. Think of evidence as a guest at a party: you introduce the guest to your friends (context), let the guest speak (the quotation or data), and then continue the conversation by responding to what the guest said (explanation).

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Example 1: Integrating statistical evidence**

Weak (dropped in): *Climate change is serious. The temperature has risen 1.1 degrees. Something must be done.*

Strong (integrated): *Global average temperature has risen approximately 1.1 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels (NASA, 2023). While 1.1 degrees may sound small, it represents a massive energy imbalance: the additional heat absorbed by the planet is equivalent to detonating several Hiroshima-sized atomic bombs per second. This warming has already contributed to a 13 percent decline in Arctic sea ice extent per decade.*

The strong version names the source, explains why the number matters, and connects it to a concrete consequence.

**Example 2: Using anecdotal evidence responsibly**

Weak: *John lost his home in the hurricane, so climate change is destroying communities.*

The anecdote of one person does not prove a general claim about climate change. It illustrates what the general claim means at the human level.

Strong: *Hurricane Maria destroyed an estimated 400,000 homes in Puerto Rico (FEMA, 2018). Among those homes was the house where John Rodriguez had lived for thirty years. Rodriguez's story is not unique: it is one of hundreds of thousands of similar losses that together illustrate the human cost of increasingly severe weather events.*

The anecdote serves as illustration after the statistical scale is established.

**Example 3: Expert testimony used correctly vs. incorrectly**

Incorrect: *Dr. Smith says vaccines are dangerous.* (Who is Dr. Smith? What is their field? Are they a relevant expert?)

Correct: *According to Dr. Anthony Fauci, director of the National Institute of Allergy and Infectious Diseases from 1984 to 2022, the mRNA vaccines underwent the largest clinical trials in vaccine history and showed 94-95 percent efficacy at preventing symptomatic COVID-19.*

The correct version identifies the expert, their credentials, and their specific claim.

**Example 4: Avoiding cherry-picking**

Cherry-picked: *Economic growth was 4.2 percent in Q3, proving the policy works.*

Complete picture: *Economic growth spiked to 4.2 percent in Q3 but fell to 1.8 percent in Q4 and averaged 2.1 percent for the year -- roughly in line with the pre-policy average of 2.0 percent. The Q3 spike appears to reflect a one-time inventory adjustment rather than a sustained change in trend.*

**Example 5: Combining evidence types**

Consider how Ta-Nehisi Coates builds his argument in *Between the World and Me*. He does not rely on statistics alone or narrative alone. He combines historical data (redlining maps, homeownership rates) with personal narrative (his experience growing up in Baltimore) with expert analysis (citations to historians and sociologists). The result is an argument where each type of evidence reinforces the others: the statistics show the scale, the personal narrative shows the human impact, and the expert testimony provides the causal explanation.

**Example 6: Evaluating a graph**

Not all evidence comes in words. Suppose you encounter a bar chart showing that "violent crime doubled from 2020 to 2021." Before accepting this as evidence, check the y-axis. If the axis starts at 950 instead of 0, a rise from 980 to 1020 looks dramatic when it is actually a 4 percent increase. The graph is not technically wrong, but its visual presentation exaggerates the change. Strong writers evaluate visual evidence with the same rigor they apply to textual evidence.

**Example 7: Analogical evidence tested**

Claim: "Sweden's approach to drug policy should be adopted here because both countries have similar populations and economies."

Testing the analogy: Both countries do have developed economies, but they differ in healthcare system structure, cultural attitudes toward addiction, legal frameworks, and geographic factors. Sweden's policy may work well in its context but produce different results elsewhere. A responsible writer acknowledges these differences: "Sweden's approach offers useful lessons, but any adaptation would need to account for differences in healthcare infrastructure and cultural context."

**Example 8: Using evidence from a literary text**

When writing about literature, your primary evidence is the text itself. Weak: "Holden Caulfield is lonely." Strong: "Holden Caulfield's loneliness pervades *The Catcher in the Rye*. When he describes the feeling that 'you didn't know where the hell you were going' (Salinger, 61), the second-person pronoun collapses the distance between narrator and reader, making Holden's disorientation feel shared rather than observed. This technique recurs throughout the novel, most strikingly when..." The strong version quotes specific language, identifies the technique, and explains how the quoted passage supports the interpretive claim.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

**Evidence** is any material presented in support of a claim. In argumentative and expository writing, evidence functions as the **grounds** (in Toulmin's terms) upon which the argument's reasoning operates. The four major categories of evidence are:

1. **Statistical / quantitative evidence:** Numerical data derived from systematic observation, measurement, or sampling. Includes surveys, experiments, censuses, and meta-analyses. Evaluated by sample size, methodology, source credibility, and whether the data is presented in context or cherry-picked.

2. **Expert testimony:** Statements by individuals recognized as authorities in a relevant field. Evaluated by the expert's credentials, the relevance of their expertise to the specific claim, and whether their view represents a scholarly consensus or a minority position.

3. **Anecdotal evidence:** Specific instances, stories, or examples. Evaluated by representativeness (does this case typify the general pattern?) and by whether it is presented as illustration (legitimate) or as proof of a general claim (fallacious).

4. **Analogical evidence:** Reasoning from a known case to a similar case. Evaluated by the relevance and number of similarities between the cases and the absence of critical differences that would undermine the comparison.

**Evidence integration** follows a pattern sometimes called the "evidence sandwich" or "ICE" (Introduce, Cite, Explain):
- **Introduce:** Provide context (who, what, when, where) before the evidence.
- **Cite / Present:** State the evidence, with attribution.
- **Explain:** Show how the evidence supports the claim. Never assume the reader will make the connection independently.

**Cherry-picking** is the selective presentation of evidence that supports a predetermined conclusion while omitting evidence that contradicts it. It is an epistemic error (it misrepresents the state of knowledge) and a rhetorical error (it undermines credibility when the omission is discovered).

**The hierarchy of evidence** (from evidence-based medicine, applicable more broadly) ranks evidence types by reliability:
- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses (strongest)
- Randomized controlled trials
- Cohort studies
- Case-control studies
- Case series and case reports
- Expert opinion / anecdote (weakest)

This hierarchy is a guide, not an absolute rule. A well-designed observational study can be more informative than a poorly designed randomized trial.

**Source credibility** has multiple dimensions. A source's **expertise** refers to their qualifications in the relevant domain. A source's **trustworthiness** refers to their track record for accuracy and honesty. A source's **institutional affiliation** provides context (a university press has different review standards than a personal blog). A source's **peer review status** indicates whether the work has been evaluated by other experts. No single dimension guarantees credibility; effective evaluation considers all of them.

**The burden of evidence** falls on the person making the claim. The more extraordinary or controversial the claim, the stronger the evidence required. This principle, sometimes expressed as Hitchens's razor ("What can be asserted without evidence can be dismissed without evidence"), is a working guideline for both writers and readers.

**Evidence sufficiency** depends on the scope of the claim. A narrow claim ("This specific policy reduced emissions in this city") requires less evidence than a broad claim ("Carbon taxes reduce emissions universally"). Writers must calibrate the quantity and quality of their evidence to the ambition of their thesis. A five-page essay cannot support the same breadth of evidence as a monograph, and the thesis must be scaled accordingly.

**Corroboration** is the principle that evidence from multiple independent sources is stronger than evidence from a single source. If three independent studies reach the same conclusion, the conclusion is better supported than if only one study reaches it. Corroboration also helps detect errors: if one study finds an effect but five others do not, the single study may have methodological problems. Writers should seek corroborating evidence for important claims and should acknowledge when evidence is contested rather than unanimous.

**The distinction between evidence and reasoning** is fundamental but often blurred in practice. Evidence is the raw material (data, quotations, facts). Reasoning is the logical connection between the evidence and the claim. The statement "Test scores rose 15 percent after the new curriculum was introduced" is evidence. The statement "The 15 percent rise in test scores suggests that the new curriculum improved student learning" is reasoning. Both are necessary, but they serve different functions. Evidence provides the grounds; reasoning provides the movement from grounds to claim. Many weak arguments fail not because they lack evidence but because they lack explicit reasoning -- they present data and then jump to conclusions without explaining how the data supports the conclusion.

**Primary vs. secondary evidence** is a distinction that matters in research writing. Primary evidence comes from the original source: the experiment itself, the original text, the raw data, the eyewitness account. Secondary evidence comes from someone else's interpretation or summary of the primary evidence: a textbook's summary of an experiment, a critic's interpretation of a novel, a journalist's report on a study. Primary evidence is generally stronger because it has not been filtered through another interpreter's biases. However, secondary evidence is sometimes necessary (when the primary source is inaccessible) and can be valuable (when the secondary source provides expert analysis). Strong arguments use primary evidence when possible and secondary evidence when necessary, always with appropriate attribution.

## Key analysis [Intermediate+]

### Key concepts

- **Evidence types:** Statistical, expert testimony, anecdotal, analogical. Each has strengths and limitations; effective arguments combine types.
- **Evidence integration:** The practice of introducing, presenting, and explaining evidence so its relevance to the claim is explicit.
- **Cherry-picking:** Selecting only evidence that supports a conclusion while ignoring contradictory evidence. Undermines both accuracy and credibility.
- **Source credibility:** The trustworthiness of an evidence source, determined by expertise, institutional affiliation, peer review status, and track record.
- **Representativeness:** Whether a sample or example accurately reflects the larger population or pattern it is invoked to represent.
- **Evidence hierarchy:** A ranking of evidence types by reliability. Systematic reviews outrank expert opinion, but context matters.
- **Corroboration:** Whether multiple independent sources support the same finding. Single-source claims are inherently weaker than multi-source claims.
- **Evidence sufficiency:** The calibration of evidence quantity and quality to the scope of the claim being made.

### Diagnostics: common evidence problems

Weak evidence usage falls into recognizable patterns. Learning to diagnose these patterns helps writers revise their own work and evaluate others'.

**The dropped quotation.** A source is quoted but never explained. The reader sees the words but not why they matter. Remedy: after every quotation or data point, write one or two sentences explaining what the evidence means and how it supports the claim.

**The mystery source.** Evidence is attributed to "studies" or "experts" without naming the specific study or expert. "Studies show that..." is not evidence; "A 2019 meta-analysis published in *The Lancet* involving 42 trials and 30,000 participants found that..." is evidence. Specificity enables verification.

**The irrelevant expert.** A qualified authority is cited, but their expertise does not match the claim. A Nobel laureate in physics has no special authority on nutrition policy. Remedy: evaluate whether the expert's specific field matches the specific claim.

**The outdated source.** Evidence from 1995 is cited to support a claim about current conditions. In fast-moving fields (medicine, technology, climate science), evidence can become outdated within a few years. Remedy: check publication dates and seek the most current available evidence.

**The single-source claim.** A significant claim rests on one study or one expert. Single sources can be wrong, retracted, or misinterpreted. Remedy: corroborate important claims with at least one additional independent source.

**The misleading statistic.** Numbers are presented without context. "Crime rose 50 percent" sounds alarming, but if the baseline was two incidents and it went to three, the increase is statistically meaningless. Remedy: always provide context for numerical data (baselines, sample sizes, confidence intervals, comparison points).

**The false analogy.** Two cases are compared without acknowledging critical differences. "Singapore's healthcare system works well, so the United States should adopt the same model" ignores vast differences in population size, demographics, political structure, and existing infrastructure. Remedy: test analogies by identifying relevant differences as well as similarities.

### Rhetorical analysis of evidence in published arguments

Examining how published writers use evidence sharpens your own practice. Consider James Baldwin's "A Talk to Teachers" (1963). Baldwin combines personal narrative (his experience as a Black child in Harlem) with historical evidence (the history of American slavery and segregation) with statistical reference (educational disparities). Each type of evidence serves a distinct function: the personal narrative establishes his authority to speak (ethos), the historical evidence provides context and causal explanation, and the statistical evidence demonstrates scale. Baldwin does not merely stack evidence; he orchestrates it, using each type for what it does best.

Consider Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie's TED Talk "The Danger of a Single Story." Adichie uses anecdotal evidence as her primary mode -- she tells stories about her childhood in Nigeria, her experiences at American universities, and her encounters with stereotypes. She does not cite statistics or meta-analyses. Yet her argument is persuasive because she uses anecdotes not as proof of general claims but as illustrations of a pattern that the audience can recognize from their own experience. She generalizes cautiously and invites the audience to test her claims against their own knowledge. This is anecdotal evidence used responsibly: specific stories in service of a general insight, with acknowledgment that stories are not data.

Consider Chinua Achebe's "An Image of Africa: Racism in Conrad's *Heart of Darkness*." Achebe uses textual evidence (quotations from Conrad's novella) to support his claim that the work is racist. He presents specific passages, analyzes their language, and draws conclusions about their implications. This is close reading as evidence: the primary source is both the object of analysis and the evidence for the analytical claim. Achebe also cites historical evidence about the Belgian Congo to show that Conrad's portrayal distorted reality. The combination of textual and historical evidence makes his argument stronger than either type would alone.

Define "cherry-picking" and explain how a writer might inadvertently cherry-pick even without intending to deceive. Provide an example.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Cherry-picking is the selective presentation of evidence that supports a conclusion while omitting contradictory evidence. It can be deliberate (a partisan source highlighting only favorable data) or inadvertent. Inadvertent cherry-picking occurs when a writer's research process is biased: they search for evidence that confirms their working thesis (confirmation bias), they rely on a single source that presents only one side, or they stop researching as soon as they find enough support. Example: a student writing that "social media causes depression" searches for studies confirming this link, finds three, and cites them. They do not search for studies finding no link or finding that social media use correlates with improved social support. The result is a paper that presents the evidence as unanimous when it is not. The remedy is to deliberately seek out disconfirming evidence and to represent the range of findings accurately.

## Advanced results [Master]

The use of evidence in argument connects to ancient questions about the nature of proof. Aristotle distinguished between *entechnic* (intrinsic) proofs, which the rhetorician discovers through argument, and *atechnic* (extrinsic) proofs, which exist independently of the argument (witnesses, documents, contracts). This distinction maps roughly onto the modern difference between reasoning (how you connect evidence to claims) and evidence itself (the data you work with).

The **scientific revolution** transformed Western expectations about evidence. Before the seventeenth century, authority (citation of Aristotle, Scripture, or legal precedent) was a primary mode of proof. The empirical method, as articulated by Bacon and practiced by the Royal Society, established observation and experiment as the gold standard. This shift created the modern evidence hierarchy: empirical data outranks authority, and replicated studies outrank single findings.

In the twentieth century, the field of **informal logic** developed systematic accounts of evidence evaluation. The critical thinking movement, associated with Robert Ennis, John McPeck, and Richard Paul, argued that evaluating evidence quality is a core cognitive skill that education should develop. In composition studies, this led to pedagogical emphasis on source evaluation, especially in the context of research writing.

The rise of the internet and digital media has complicated evidence evaluation. Information abundance means that supporting evidence is available for virtually any claim, including false ones. The challenge has shifted from finding evidence to evaluating it. Pedagogical responses include the "CRAAP test" (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose), lateral reading strategies (checking what other sources say about a source), and the "SIFT" method (Stop, Investigate the source, Find better coverage, Trace claims to their origin).

In rhetoric and composition, the concept of **kairos** -- the timeliness and appropriateness of an argument for its moment -- applies to evidence as well. Evidence that was current and compelling ten years ago may be outdated. Evidence that is persuasive in one cultural context may carry no weight in another. Effective arguers attend not only to the logical strength of their evidence but to its rhetorical appropriateness for the specific audience and situation.

Postmodern and social-constructionist critiques question whether evidence can ever be "neutral." Thomas Kuhn's *The Structure of Scientific Revolutions* (1962) argued that what counts as evidence is shaped by the prevailing paradigm -- scientists working within different frameworks literally see different things. This critique does not entail that all evidence is equally valid, but it complicates the assumption that evidence speaks for itself independent of interpretive frameworks.

The relationship between evidence and **narrative** has received increasing attention. Scholars in the rhetoric of science, including Alan Gross and Jeanne Fahnestock, have shown that even in scientific writing, evidence is embedded in narrative structures: the experiment is a story with characters (researchers), a setting (laboratory), a conflict (the problem being investigated), and a resolution (the findings). Recognizing the narrative dimension of evidence does not undermine its reliability but reveals that the presentation of evidence always involves rhetorical choices about emphasis, order, and framing.

Genre-specific evidence conventions deserve attention. In the natural sciences, quantitative evidence from controlled experiments is the gold standard. In the humanities, textual evidence (close reading of primary sources) carries the most weight. In law, precedent (prior court decisions) and statutory interpretation are primary. In journalism, eyewitness accounts and documentary evidence are valued. In public policy, cost-benefit analyses and program evaluations are standard. A writer who understands evidence conventions in one field may need to learn different conventions when writing in another.

The concept of **evidence bandwidth** helps explain why different genres use different types of evidence. A five-paragraph essay has limited bandwidth: it can present and explain two or three pieces of evidence. A research paper has more bandwidth: it can engage with a dozen sources. A book-length monograph has extensive bandwidth: it can present entire bodies of evidence. The writer must calibrate both the ambition of the claim and the quantity of evidence to the available bandwidth. A common student error is to make a broad claim and then provide only enough evidence to support a narrow version of it.

The role of **visual evidence** has expanded with digital media. Charts, graphs, photographs, infographics, and data visualizations are all forms of evidence, and they have their own conventions and pitfalls. A misleading graph (truncated y-axis, selective time range, cherry-picked comparison) can distort evidence as much as a misleading quotation. Evaluating visual evidence requires its own set of critical skills: understanding how scale, color, and framing affect interpretation.

For **ESL writers**, evidence conventions in English academic writing may differ from conventions in their first language. Some cultures emphasize authority-based evidence (citing respected scholars) over empirical evidence. Some cultures value indirect evidence (building a case through implication rather than explicit statement). Some cultures treat personal experience as a legitimate form of evidence in academic argument, while English academic conventions typically subordinate personal experience to published research. ESL writers benefit from explicit instruction in the evidence conventions of English-language academic discourse, with acknowledgment that these conventions are cultural, not universal.

The concept of **testimonial injustice**, developed by philosopher Miranda Fricker, applies to evidence evaluation. Testimonial injustice occurs when a speaker's credibility is unfairly diminished because of prejudice (racism, sexism, classism). When a writer dismisses evidence from a source because of the source's identity rather than the quality of the evidence, the writer commits an epistemic error. Fricker's work reminds us that source credibility should be evaluated based on expertise and reliability, not on social identity. At the same time, credibility assessment must account for structural biases: sources from marginalized communities may have access to forms of evidence (lived experience, community knowledge) that mainstream sources lack.

The **replication crisis** in psychology and other sciences has profound implications for how writers use evidence. Beginning around 2010, researchers discovered that many published findings could not be replicated in new studies. The Open Science Collaboration's 2015 study found that only 36 percent of 100 published psychology experiments produced statistically significant results when repeated. This crisis revealed that published evidence is not always reliable evidence. Writers who cite scientific studies should look for replicated findings, attend to effect sizes (not just statistical significance), and be cautious about citing single studies, especially from fields affected by replication difficulties. The crisis has also led to reforms (pre-registration, open data, larger sample sizes) that are improving the reliability of new research.

The role of **metadata** in evidence evaluation is often overlooked. When was the study published? Who funded it? Was it peer-reviewed? What journal published it? Was the sample size adequate? What was the methodology? This contextual information helps writers assess the reliability of the evidence. A study published in a peer-reviewed journal with a large sample size and transparent methodology is stronger than a press release from an industry group citing an unpublished study with unknown methods. Writers should not only evaluate the evidence itself but also the circumstances of its production.

The concept of **evidence ecology** describes the interconnected system of evidence production and consumption. Evidence is produced by researchers, reported by journalists, summarized by textbook authors, and cited by student writers. At each stage, the evidence is filtered, interpreted, and potentially distorted. A researcher's nuanced finding becomes a journalist's sensational headline, which becomes a student's sweeping claim. Writers who understand this ecology can trace evidence back through the chain and evaluate it at each stage. This practice, sometimes called "upstream reading," helps writers avoid citing distorted versions of evidence.

The relationship between evidence and **narrative** has received increasing attention. Scholars in the rhetoric of science, including Alan Gross and Jeanne Fahnestock, have shown that even in scientific writing, evidence is embedded in narrative structures: the experiment is a story with characters (researchers), a setting (laboratory), a conflict (the problem being investigated), and a resolution (the findings). Recognizing the narrative dimension of evidence does not undermine its reliability but reveals that the presentation of evidence always involves rhetorical choices about emphasis, order, and framing.

The distinction between **absence of evidence** and **evidence of absence** is important for responsible argument. "There is no evidence that X causes Y" is not the same as "There is evidence that X does not cause Y." The first statement means that no study has found a link; the link may exist but be undetected. The second statement means that studies have actively looked for a link and failed to find one, which is stronger (but still not definitive) evidence against the link. Writers should be precise about this distinction and should not claim that lack of evidence disproves a claim.

The concept of **evidence resistance** describes the phenomenon where audiences reject well-supported evidence because it contradicts their existing beliefs. Research in cognitive psychology (Kahan et al., 2017) shows that politically motivated reasoning can cause people to dismiss strong evidence while accepting weak evidence that confirms their views. This does not mean evidence is futile, but it does mean that effective arguers must attend to how they present evidence as well as what evidence they present. Framing evidence in ways that do not threaten the audience's identity, providing narrative context alongside data, and acknowledging uncertainty can all reduce evidence resistance.

The ethics of evidence use extend beyond avoiding cherry-picking. Writers have an ethical obligation to represent evidence accurately (not distorting findings through selective quotation), to cite sources (giving credit where it is due), to acknowledge uncertainty (not presenting tentative findings as definitive), and to consider harm (not using evidence in ways that could harm vulnerable populations). These ethical principles are not just rules to follow; they reflect the fundamental purpose of evidence in argument: to help readers form accurate beliefs about the world.

The phenomenon of **evidence laundering** occurs when questionable evidence is given credibility by passing it through seemingly legitimate channels. A lobbying group funds a study, the study produces a favorable finding, the finding is reported in a press release, the press release is covered by news outlets, and the news coverage is then cited as evidence. At each stage, the evidence appears more credible than it actually is. Writers who trace evidence to its origin (as the SIFT method recommends) can detect this pattern and avoid citing laundered evidence.

**Statistical significance** and **practical significance** are different concepts that writers must distinguish. A study may find a statistically significant effect (unlikely to be due to chance) that is too small to matter in practice. Conversely, a meaningful practical effect may not reach statistical significance because the sample size is too small. When reporting statistical evidence, writers should report both the statistical significance and the effect size, and they should explain what the effect means in practical terms. A medication that reduces headache duration by a statistically significant 30 seconds is not practically useful; a tutoring program that produces a practically significant 2-grade-level improvement that does not reach statistical significance because only 15 students were studied deserves further investigation.

## Connections [Master]

This unit connects to the surrounding language sequence through prerequisites (22.02.05) and successors (22.02.09). In the wider Babel Bible map, using evidence also links to logic and formal semantics in philosophy, rhetoric and argument in writing, literary interpretation, and world/civics units where public language shapes law, institutions, and collective action. The cross-domain point is that language is not only a school subject; it is the medium through which definitions, evidence, authority, and disagreement become teachable.

For ESL writers, evidence conventions connect to contrastive rhetoric. Different academic cultures have different expectations about what counts as evidence, how much evidence is needed, and how evidence should be presented. Learning English academic evidence conventions is not learning "the right way" but learning one set of conventions among many.

The connection to **scientific reasoning** is significant. The evidence hierarchy taught in writing classes mirrors the evidence hierarchy used in scientific research. Writers who understand evidence types and evaluation criteria are better equipped to read scientific literature, evaluate popular science reporting, and make evidence-based decisions in daily life.

The connection to **media literacy** is immediate. Every day, citizens encounter claims supported by evidence (or lacking it) in news articles, social media posts, advertisements, and political speeches. The ability to evaluate that evidence -- to assess source credibility, detect cherry-picking, and distinguish strong from weak support -- is a civic skill that underpins informed participation in democratic society.

The connection to **literary analysis** may seem less direct but is real. Literary arguments use textual evidence (quotations from the work being analyzed) to support interpretive claims. The same principles of integration apply: quotations must be introduced, presented, and explained. The same pitfalls apply: cherry-picked quotations that ignore contrary passages, quotations dropped in without analysis, and evidence from unreliable sources (e.g., SparkNotes instead of the primary text).

The connection to **business and professional writing** is practical. In business, evidence takes the form of market data, financial reports, customer surveys, and case studies. A business proposal that makes claims without supporting evidence will not be funded. A marketing analysis that cherry-picks data will produce flawed recommendations. The principles of evidence use taught in this unit apply directly to professional contexts.

The connection to **quantitative reasoning** deserves emphasis. Using statistical evidence well requires not just finding numbers but understanding what they mean. Percentages without baselines, correlations presented as causation, and averages that hide important distributions are all common errors. Writers who develop basic quantitative literacy (understanding percentages, ratios, averages, margins of error, and the difference between correlation and causation) will use statistical evidence more effectively and evaluate others' use of it more critically.

The connection to **ethics and academic integrity** is direct. Proper evidence use is inseparable from honest attribution, accurate representation of sources, and transparent acknowledgment of limitations. Plagiarism (using evidence without attribution), fabrication (inventing evidence), and falsification (distorting evidence) are violations of academic integrity because they undermine the shared trust that makes evidence-based argument possible. When a reader cannot trust that the evidence is real and accurately represented, the entire argument collapses regardless of its logical structure.

The connection to **cross-cultural communication** is significant. Evidence that persuades in one culture may not persuade in another. In some East Asian academic traditions, evidence from authoritative texts carries more weight than empirical data. In some African intellectual traditions, communal testimony and proverbs function as forms of evidence. In some Indigenous knowledge systems, oral tradition and land-based knowledge are legitimate evidence that Western academic conventions may not recognize. Writers who work across cultural contexts must understand that evidence conventions are not universal but shaped by cultural values and epistemological assumptions.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The use of evidence has evolved alongside Western epistemology. In classical rhetoric, evidence (*pisteis*) included both logical argument and extrinsic proofs such as laws, witnesses, and documents. Roman law developed formal rules of evidence that influenced legal systems for centuries.

The medieval period saw the development of the scholastic method, which used evidence in the context of authoritative texts. A typical scholastic argument began with a question, cited authoritative sources on both sides, and resolved the question through reasoning grounded in those authorities. Evidence was textual and authority-based rather than empirical.

The scientific revolution introduced experimental evidence as a new standard of proof. Robert Boyle's air pump experiments (1660s) were significant not only for their findings but for establishing the practice of public, repeatable experimentation as a form of evidence. The Royal Society's motto, *Nullius in verba* ("Take nobody's word for it"), encapsulated the new emphasis on evidence over authority.

In the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, statistical evidence gained prominence. The development of probability theory, sampling methods, and statistical inference (Fisher, Neyman, Pearson) created tools for drawing conclusions from numerical data. The rise of social science brought statistical evidence into domains previously governed by qualitative argument.

In the twenty-first century, evidence evaluation has become both more important and more difficult. The internet provides instant access to vast quantities of information, but it also enables the rapid spread of misinformation. Fact-checking organizations, media literacy initiatives, and critical thinking curricula have emerged in response. In academic writing, the expectation for evidence has intensified: a research paper in 2025 is expected to engage with more sources, from more diverse perspectives, than was typical fifty years ago.

Non-Western evidence traditions offer important perspectives. The Nyaya school of Indian philosophy developed rigorous standards for evaluating evidence and inference, including a five-member syllogism that requires both general principle and specific instance. The Islamic intellectual tradition developed sophisticated standards for evaluating hadith (reports about the Prophet Muhammad), including chains of transmission (isnad) that function as a form of source credibility assessment. Confucian scholarship valued evidence from classical texts and historical precedent, with commentary traditions that required scholars to engage with prior interpretations before advancing new claims. African oral traditions use proverbs and communal memory as evidence, with community elders serving as living repositories of corroborated knowledge.

The concept of **bearing false witness** appears across multiple ethical and religious traditions as a prohibition against presenting false evidence. The Ninth Commandment ("You shall not bear false witness against your neighbor") is not only a moral injunction but an epistemological principle: the integrity of evidence is essential to the integrity of community. Similar prohibitions appear in Islamic law (bearing false testimony is a serious sin), in Confucian ethics (the superior person speaks truthfully and does not misrepresent), and in many Indigenous legal traditions (where false evidence can harm the entire community, not just individuals). These cross-cultural parallels suggest that the ethics of evidence use is not a Western or modern invention but a universal concern.

The philosophical question of whether evidence is **found** (discovered in the world) or **constructed** (shaped by the frameworks we bring to inquiry) remains contested. Realists argue that evidence exists independently of our theories and constrains what we can believe. Constructivists argue that evidence is always interpreted through theoretical lenses and that different frameworks produce different evidence. This debate has practical implications for writing: should writers present evidence as if it speaks for itself, or should they acknowledge the interpretive dimension? Most composition scholars advocate a middle position: evidence constrains interpretation (not anything goes), but evidence never speaks entirely for itself (interpretation is always involved).

The history of evidence in **legal contexts** provides a rich parallel. The Western legal tradition developed rules of evidence over centuries: what counts as admissible evidence, who is qualified to testify, how evidence should be weighed. The concept of "beyond a reasonable doubt" in criminal law sets a higher evidentiary standard than "preponderance of evidence" in civil law, reflecting the principle that the seriousness of a claim should determine the strength of evidence required. This principle applies to academic writing as well: the more consequential or controversial the claim, the stronger the evidence must be.

The development of **peer review** as a mechanism for evaluating evidence is relatively recent. While the Royal Society practiced forms of review in the seventeenth century, formal peer review as we know it emerged in the mid-twentieth century. Peer review is a quality-control mechanism: experts in the field evaluate evidence before it is published, checking for methodological soundness, accurate interpretation, and appropriate conclusions. Peer review is not infallible (reviewers can be biased, mistakes can be missed), but it provides a layer of credibility that non-reviewed sources lack. Writers should attend to whether their sources have been peer-reviewed and should be more cautious with non-reviewed evidence.
