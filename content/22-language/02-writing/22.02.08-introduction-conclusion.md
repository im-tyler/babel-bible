## Intuition [Beginner]

The introduction and conclusion are the most-read and least-written-well parts of most essays. The introduction either pulls the reader in or loses them. The conclusion either leaves a lasting impression or trails off. Both deserve more attention than they typically get.

Think of the introduction as a doorway and the conclusion as an exit. The doorway should be inviting: it should make the reader want to enter. The exit should be satisfying: the reader should leave with something valuable, not just relief that the essay is over.

![Diagram: An essay shown as a house. The front door is labeled "Introduction (Hook + Context + Thesis)" and the back door is labeled "Conclusion (Synthesis + Implication + Final Thought)." Inside, rooms represent body paragraphs.](/images/22-language/intro-conclusion-house.png)

**The introduction** has three jobs:
1. Get the reader's attention (the hook).
2. Provide necessary context or background.
3. State the thesis.

The standard pattern is the **funnel**: start broad, narrow down, end with the thesis. Open with something that makes the reader want to keep reading -- a surprising fact, a provocative question, a vivid anecdote, a bold claim. Then provide enough background so the reader can follow the argument. Then state the thesis.

**The conclusion** does NOT just restate the introduction. Its jobs:
1. Remind the reader of the argument's core point (but not by copying the thesis word for word).
2. Show why the argument matters -- the "so what?"
3. Leave the reader with something to think about.

Good conclusions do one or more of the following: point to implications, call the reader to action, suggest further questions, or return to the opening hook (creating a satisfying circular structure).

Things to avoid in introductions:
- "In this essay I will..." (Announce the topic; do not describe the essay.)
- "Since the dawn of time..." (Vague, overblown openings.)
- "Webster's dictionary defines X as..." (Padding, not a hook.)

Things to avoid in conclusions:
- Introducing new evidence or arguments. If it matters, it belongs in the body.
- "In conclusion..." (The reader can see it is the last paragraph.)
- Simply restating the thesis verbatim. The reader just read the whole essay; they do not need a photocopy of the first paragraph.

**A note on writing process:** Many experienced writers write the introduction *after* the body paragraphs. Why? Because writing is a process of discovery. You may not fully understand your argument until you have worked through the evidence and reasoning. If you write the introduction first, you may end up with a thesis that no longer matches what the body actually argues. The solution is to draft a working thesis, write the body, then return to the introduction and revise it so that it accurately reflects the developed argument. This is not cheating; it is good process.

**The "so what?" question** is your best friend when writing conclusions. After you have reminded the reader of your argument, ask yourself: "So what? Why should anyone care?" The answer to this question is your implication. If you cannot answer it, your argument may not be significant enough to sustain an essay. If you can answer it powerfully, your conclusion will resonate.

**Hooks must be relevant.** A hook that is merely attention-grabbing but has no connection to the argument is worse than no hook at all. The reader feels misled. The best hooks are not only surprising or vivid but also directly connected to the thesis. A surprising statistic about water pollution is a good hook for an essay about water policy. A surprising statistic about shark attacks is not (unless the essay is about shark attacks). Test every hook by asking: "Does this lead naturally to my thesis?"

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Example 1: Funnel introduction**

Hook: *In 1969, the Cuyahoga River in Ohio caught fire. Not a metaphorical fire -- the river, thick with industrial waste, literally burned for thirty minutes.*

Context: *The Cuyahoga fire became a symbol of the environmental degradation that prompted the creation of the Environmental Protection Agency and the passage of the Clean Water Act. For decades, these regulations reduced water pollution across the United States.*

Thesis: *Today, however, a new category of water contamination -- per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances, or PFAS -- threatens to undo much of that progress, and current regulations are not equipped to address it.*

The opening is vivid and specific. The context bridges to the current situation. The thesis states a clear, arguable claim.

**Example 2: Conclusion that returns to the hook**

If the essay opened with the Cuyahoga River fire, the conclusion might close by referencing it: *The Cuyahoga does not burn anymore. The river that once made national news for catching fire now supports fish populations and attracts kayakers. But the PFAS crisis shows that the fight for clean water is never finished -- it simply takes new forms. The question is whether we will respond as decisively to this generation's contamination as the previous generation did to theirs.*

**Example 3: What NOT to do**

Weak introduction: *In this essay, I will talk about water pollution. Water pollution is a big problem. There are many types of water pollution. I will discuss PFAS.* (Padding, no hook, announcement instead of engagement.)

Weak conclusion: *In conclusion, PFAS is bad. As I have shown in this essay, PFAS causes many problems. We should do something about it. Thank you for reading.* (Verbatim restatement, empty call to action, "in conclusion" flag.)

**Example 4: Conclusion with implication**

*The regulation of PFAS will cost industry billions of dollars. The cost of not regulating it -- in increased cancer rates, contaminated water supplies, and degraded ecosystems -- will cost far more. The history of environmental regulation in the United States is a history of industries claiming that compliance will destroy them, followed by compliance that does not destroy them. The question is not whether we can afford to regulate PFAS. It is whether we can afford not to.*

**Example 5: Introduction using a literary opening**

Hook: *James Baldwin once wrote, "Not everything that is faced can be changed, but nothing can be changed until it is faced." His words were about race in America, but they apply equally to the challenge of educational inequality.*

Context: *Despite decades of reform efforts and billions of dollars in funding, the achievement gap between students in wealthy and poor school districts has barely narrowed since it was first measured in the 1960s.*

Thesis: *The persistence of this gap suggests that the problem is not a lack of programs or funding but a structural feature of how American public education is financed through local property taxes, and meaningful reform requires dismantling that system entirely.*

**Example 6: Conclusion with further question**

*The evidence presented here demonstrates that property-tax-based school funding produces systematic inequality. But dismantling this system raises its own questions: What funding mechanism should replace it? How do we ensure that replacing local control with state or federal funding does not reduce community investment in schools? And in a political environment where even modest reforms face fierce opposition, what coalition could possibly build support for a change this fundamental? These questions do not undermine the case for reform; they define the terrain on which the next phase of the debate will be fought.*

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The **introduction** is the opening section of an essay or article, typically one or more paragraphs, whose functions are to establish the topic, engage the reader, provide necessary context, and state the thesis. Its formal structure follows a **funnel** pattern: from broad (the general topic or hook) to narrow (the specific thesis).

Components of an effective introduction:
1. **Hook:** An opening sentence or sentences designed to capture the reader's attention. Effective hooks include surprising facts, vivid anecdotes, provocative claims, and resonant quotations. Ineffective hooks include dictionary definitions ("Webster's defines..."), vague generalizations ("Throughout history..."), and meta-commentary ("In this essay I will...").
2. **Context / background:** Information the reader needs to understand the argument. May include historical context, key definitions, the current state of debate, or a summary of the problem.
3. **Thesis:** The essay's central claim, typically appearing as the last sentence of the introduction in academic writing.

The **conclusion** is the closing section of an essay, whose functions are to synthesize the argument, demonstrate its significance, and provide closure. Effective conclusions perform one or more of the following:

- **Synthesis:** Show how the essay's individual arguments combine to support the thesis. Not a point-by-point summary but an integration that reveals the whole.
- **Implication:** Explain what follows from the argument -- what it means for policy, practice, or further research.
- **Call to action:** Urge the reader to do something specific in response to the argument.
- **Further question:** Raise a question that the essay's argument opens but does not answer, suggesting directions for continued inquiry.
- **Return to hook:** Circle back to the opening image, anecdote, or fact, creating structural closure.

**Circular structure** (also called **frame structure**) is a technique in which the conclusion returns to the introduction's opening, creating a sense of completeness. The opening raises a question or presents a scene; the body answers the question or analyzes the scene; the conclusion returns to the opening with new understanding.

**The "so what?" test** is a diagnostic for conclusions. After stating the argument, ask: "So what? Why does this matter?" If the conclusion cannot answer this question, it is incomplete. The "so what?" answer is the implication -- the reason the reader should care about the argument. Every conclusion should pass this test.

**Introduction length** should be proportional to the essay. In a five-paragraph essay, one paragraph suffices. In a research paper, the introduction may be several paragraphs, including a literature review that establishes the current state of knowledge. In a book, the introduction may be a full chapter. The principle is that the introduction should be long enough to fulfill its functions (engage, contextualize, state thesis) and no longer.

**Conclusion length** follows similar proportionality. A conclusion that is too short suggests the writer ran out of things to say. A conclusion that is too long suggests the writer is padding. The conclusion should be long enough to synthesize the argument, state its implications, and provide closure -- typically 10-15 percent of the total essay length.

## Key analysis [Intermediate+]

### Key concepts

- **Funnel introduction:** Broad opening narrowing to thesis. The standard pattern for academic and expository writing.
- **Hook strategies:** Surprising facts, vivid anecdotes, provocative claims, resonant quotations, direct questions. The hook must be relevant to the argument, not merely attention-grabbing.
- **Thesis placement:** Typically the last sentence of the introduction. Placement signals to the reader that the context-setting is finished and the argument is beginning.
- **Synthesis in conclusion:** Integrating the essay's arguments into a coherent whole, not summarizing them point by point.
- **Implication / "so what?":** The conclusion's job of showing why the argument matters beyond the essay itself.
- **Circular / frame structure:** Opening and closing with the same image, anecdote, or question. Creates structural satisfaction.
- **Forbidden moves:** Announcing the essay ("In this paper I will..."), restating the thesis verbatim, introducing new evidence, padding with "In conclusion."

### Diagnostics: common introduction and conclusion problems

**The announcement introduction.** "In this essay, I will discuss X. First I will talk about A. Then I will talk about B. Finally, I will discuss C." This introduction tells the reader what the essay will do instead of engaging the reader in the topic. Remedy: replace the announcement with a hook that makes the reader want to know more about the topic.

**The dictionary definition opening.** "Merriam-Webster defines 'justice' as..." This is padding. The reader knows what justice means (or thinks they do). If the term is genuinely contested, the introduction should acknowledge the debate, not cite a dictionary. Remedy: open with a question, a puzzle, or a concrete example that illustrates the contested meaning.

**The overbroad opening.** "Throughout history, humans have struggled with the problem of X." This opening is so broad that it could introduce almost any essay on almost any topic. It wastes the reader's time. Remedy: start with something specific to your argument -- a particular event, a particular statistic, a particular case.

**The summary conclusion.** "In conclusion, this essay has argued that A, B, and C." A summary conclusion treats the reader as if they have not been paying attention. If the essay is well-structured, the reader already knows A, B, and C. The conclusion should add value by synthesizing, implicating, or questioning -- not by recapitulating.

**The new-evidence conclusion.** The writer introduces a new statistic, a new source, or a new argument in the conclusion. This violates the function of the conclusion (synthesis, not addition) and leaves the reader wondering why this evidence was not presented in the body where it could be properly developed. Remedy: if the evidence is important, move it to the body. If it is not important, delete it.

**The trailing-off conclusion.** The essay simply stops. The last paragraph restates the thesis and then ends without synthesis, implication, or final thought. The reader is left thinking, "Is that it?" Remedy: end with something that gives the reader a reason to remember the essay -- a vivid image, a provocative question, a return to the opening.

### Rhetorical analysis of introductions and conclusions in published writing

Consider the opening of James Baldwin's "A Talk to Teachers": *Let's begin by saying that we are living through a very dangerous time.* This is not a funnel; it is a direct address that establishes urgency and establishes Baldwin's authority (the "let's" is inclusive, placing speaker and audience on the same side). The opening does three things simultaneously: it names the stakes, it engages the audience, and it establishes the speaker's ethos. Effective openings in public argument often achieve this triple function.

Consider the conclusion of Martin Luther King Jr.'s "I Have a Dream" speech. King does not summarize his previous points. Instead, he shifts to a vision of the future -- a series of images of racial harmony that function as both implication (this is what the argument leads to) and emotional climax (this is what we are fighting for). The conclusion is not analytical; it is visionary. This demonstrates that conclusions need not be purely rational; they can also be aspirational.

Consider the opening of Toni Morrison's *Playing in the Dark*. Morrison begins with a personal anecdote about reading as a child, then narrows to her observation that American literature is haunted by the presence of Africanism. The opening is both personal (establishing ethos) and analytical (introducing the thesis). This combination is characteristic of literary-critical introductions, which often move from the writer's experience to the analytical claim.

## Advanced results [Master]

The introduction and conclusion correspond to two of the six parts of the classical oration as described by Cicero and Quintilian: the **exordium** (introduction) and the **peroratio** (conclusion). The exordium's function was to make the audience *benevoli* (well-disposed), *attenti* (attentive), and *dociles* (receptive to instruction). The peroratio had two functions: *recapitulatio* (summary of the argument) and *affectus* (emotional appeal, designed to move the audience to the desired response).

Cicero distinguished between the *exordium directum* (a straightforward opening appropriate when the audience is already favorable) and the *exordium insinuatio* (an indirect opening used when the audience is hostile or skeptical, in which the speaker subtly works their way into the audience's confidence before revealing the argument). This distinction has modern parallels: a writer addressing a sympathetic audience can open directly with the thesis, while a writer addressing a skeptical audience may need to build credibility and common ground before revealing the argument's direction.

The concept of **kairos** -- the opportune moment -- applies to introductions. An effective introduction establishes not only the topic but its timeliness: why this argument matters now. This is why the most effective hooks often reference current events, recent data, or pressing problems. They establish kairos.

Modern composition scholars have complicated the classical model. The process movement's emphasis on revision led to the observation that many experienced writers compose their introductions last, because they do not fully understand their argument until they have written the body. This practice inverts the reader's experience (thesis first) while respecting the writer's process (thesis last). The pedagogical implication is that the introduction should be taught as something to be drafted and revised rather than as a formula to be applied.

Genre studies have shown that introduction and conclusion conventions vary sharply across disciplines. In the natural sciences, introductions typically follow a "CARS" (Create a Research Space) model identified by John Swales: establish the territory (what is known), identify a gap (what is not known), and occupy the niche (what this paper will do). In the humanities, introductions are more likely to open with a textual or conceptual puzzle. Legal writing begins with a statement of the issue. Business writing opens with an executive summary. Teaching a single model (the funnel) across all genres misrepresents the diversity of rhetorical practice.

The relationship between introduction and **ethos** deserves attention. The introduction is where the writer establishes credibility with the reader. This happens not only through the thesis but through the choice of hook, the quality of the context, and the tone of the prose. A writer who opens with a surprising, well-sourced fact demonstrates knowledge and authority. A writer who opens with a dictionary definition demonstrates neither. The introduction is the reader's first impression of the writer's mind, and first impressions matter.

The conclusion's relationship to **pathos** has classical roots. The peroratio was the section where the orator was expected to appeal to the audience's emotions -- to move them to anger, pity, indignation, or resolve. Modern conclusions serve a similar function: they translate the argument's logical content into emotional stakes. A conclusion that ends with a vivid image, a moral challenge, or a vision of the future engages the reader's emotions as well as their intellect, making the argument more memorable and more likely to inspire action.

The concept of ** closure** in narrative theory applies to conclusions. Readers desire closure -- a sense that the argument has reached a natural ending, not merely stopped. Closure can be achieved through synthesis (showing how the parts fit together), through implication (showing where the argument leads), through circular structure (returning to the opening), or through a final image that crystallizes the argument's significance. A conclusion that provides closure satisfies the reader; a conclusion that does not leaves the reader unsettled, which may be the writer's intention (in arguments that are deliberately open-ended) but is usually experienced as a flaw.

Genre-specific introduction and conclusion conventions merit detailed attention. In **scientific papers**, the introduction follows the CARS model: establish the field, identify a gap, and state the paper's contribution. The conclusion (usually called "discussion" or "conclusions") interprets the findings, acknowledges limitations, and suggests future research. In **legal briefs**, the introduction states the issue and the client's position. The conclusion states the relief requested. In **business reports**, the introduction may be an executive summary. The conclusion states recommendations. In **literary analysis**, the introduction typically presents a textual puzzle or interpretive question. The conclusion reflects on the implications of the interpretation for understanding the text or the broader literary tradition.

The role of **audience awareness** in introduction and conclusion design is significant. An introduction for an expert audience can use technical language and assume background knowledge. An introduction for a general audience must translate technical concepts into accessible language. An introduction for a hostile audience must build common ground before revealing the thesis. An introduction for a sympathetic audience can state the thesis directly and move on. The writer who understands their audience can calibrate the introduction and conclusion to achieve maximum effect.

For **ESL writers**, introduction and conclusion conventions in English academic writing may differ from those in their first language. Some cultures prefer indirect openings that gradually approach the topic. English academic conventions favor relatively direct openings that state the topic and thesis within the first paragraph. Similarly, some cultures favor conclusions that express humility and invite correction, while English academic conventions favor conclusions that state the argument's implications with confidence. ESL writers benefit from explicit instruction in these conventions, with acknowledgment that they are cultural, not universal.

The digital environment has changed how introductions and conclusions function. Online readers scan rather than read linearly. They may read the introduction, skip to the conclusion, and then decide whether to read the body. This makes both sections more important and more compressed than in print. Web writing often uses subheadings, bullet points, and bold text to make the introduction and conclusion scannable. The principles remain the same (engage, contextualize, state thesis; synthesize, implicate, close), but the execution adapts to the medium.

The concept of **the writer's journey** provides a useful metaphor for the introduction-conclusion arc. The introduction is the departure: the writer and reader leave familiar ground and set out toward a destination (the thesis). The body is the journey: the evidence, reasoning, and counterarguments encountered along the way. The conclusion is the arrival: the destination is reached, and the writer looks back at where they have been and forward to where the journey leads. This metaphor captures the sense of movement that characterizes effective essays -- the feeling of going somewhere, not merely standing in place.

The concept of **paradoxical closure** deserves attention. Some of the most effective conclusions do not resolve the argument but deepen it. They end with a paradox, a tension, or a question that the argument has revealed but cannot answer. This kind of conclusion is not incomplete; it is honest. Some arguments lead to more questions, not fewer, and a conclusion that acknowledges this is more intellectually rigorous than one that pretends to have all the answers. Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie's *We Should All Be Feminists* ends not with a resolution but with an invitation: "A feminist is a man or a woman who says, 'Yes, there's a problem with gender as it is today and we must fix it, we must do better.'" This conclusion does not resolve the problem; it defines a stance toward it.

The relationship between introduction and **title** is worth noting. The title is the reader's first encounter with the text, and an effective title functions as a miniature introduction: it hints at the topic, the approach, and sometimes the thesis. Titles like "A Talk to Teachers" (Baldwin), "The Danger of a Single Story" (Adichie), and "An Image of Africa" (Achebe) prepare the reader for what follows. Academic titles typically follow a colon structure: a catchy first part followed by a descriptive second part ("Fires in the Mirror: A Case Study in Documentary Theater"). The title-introduction relationship is another dimension of beginning that deserves attention.

The phenomenon of **the cold open** -- starting in media res, without any context or preparation -- is a technique borrowed from narrative fiction that can be effective in certain kinds of essays. Instead of building up to the thesis, the cold open drops the reader into the middle of the argument and then provides context retrospectively. This technique is risky (the reader may be confused rather than intrigued) but powerful when executed well. It works best in essays that are primarily narrative or experiential, where the reader's initial confusion mirrors the writer's initial confusion and is resolved through the body of the essay.

The concept of **voice** in introductions connects this unit to 22.02.11 (Style and Voice). The introduction is where the writer's voice is first established, and the conclusion is where it is most fully expressed. A writer with a distinctive voice (Baldwin's prophetic urgency, Morrison's lyrical precision, Adichie's conversational intelligence) is recognizable from the first sentence. Developing a strong voice in the introduction and maintaining it through the conclusion is one of the hallmarks of accomplished writing.

## Connections [Master]

This unit connects to the surrounding language sequence through prerequisites (22.02.04, thesis statements) and successors (none declared). In the wider Babel Bible map, introduction and conclusion also links to logic and formal semantics in philosophy, rhetoric and argument in writing, literary interpretation, and world/civics units where public language shapes law, institutions, and collective action.

The connection to **thesis statements** (22.02.04) is immediate: the introduction contains the thesis, and the conclusion reflects on it. Writers who have mastered thesis construction will write stronger introductions and conclusions because the thesis itself is clear and arguable. Conversely, a vague thesis produces a vague introduction and a weak conclusion.

The connection to **paragraph structure** (22.02.02) is structural. Just as a paragraph has a topic sentence, supporting sentences, and a concluding sentence, an essay has an introduction, body paragraphs, and a conclusion. The essay-level structure mirrors the paragraph-level structure. Understanding this parallel helps writers see the introduction as a large-scale topic sentence and the conclusion as a large-scale concluding sentence.

The connection to **literary analysis** is worth noting. When writing about literature, the introduction typically presents a textual puzzle (a passage that seems contradictory, a character whose behavior is unexpected, a pattern that recurs without explanation). The conclusion typically reflects on what the interpretation reveals about the text as a whole or about the broader questions the text raises. Literary introductions and conclusions have their own conventions, but they follow the same principles as other academic writing: engage, contextualize, state the claim; synthesize, implicate, close.

The connection to **public speaking** is practical. The principles of effective introductions and conclusions apply equally to written essays and oral presentations. A speaker who opens with a compelling story and closes with a call to action is using the same techniques as a writer who opens with a vivid anecdote and closes with an implication. The cross-modal application of these principles reinforces their generality and importance.

The connection to **journalism** is significant. Journalistic leads (openings) and kickers (closings) follow conventions that overlap with academic introductions and conclusions. The "inverted pyramid" structure in news writing places the most important information first, which is a compressed version of the funnel introduction. Feature writing uses more elaborate hooks and circular closures. Writers who understand both journalistic and academic conventions can adapt their introduction-conclusion strategies to different genres.

The connection to **creative writing** may seem distant but is real. Short stories and novels have openings that must hook the reader and endings that must provide (or deliberately withhold) closure. The techniques differ (fiction uses character, dialogue, and scene; essays use evidence, reasoning, and analysis), but the underlying principle is the same: the opening creates expectations, the middle develops them, and the closing resolves or transforms them. Writers who practice both creative and analytical writing can learn from each form's approach to beginnings and endings.

The connection to **business writing** is practical. In business contexts, the "introduction" is often an executive summary -- a compressed version of the entire document that appears at the beginning so that busy readers can grasp the main points without reading the full report. The "conclusion" is often a recommendations section that tells the reader what to do with the information. These business conventions demonstrate the introduction-conclusion principles in a different register: engagement becomes concision, context becomes relevance, and implication becomes actionability.

The connection to **legal writing** is instructive. Legal briefs typically begin with a statement of the issue and the client's position (a compressed funnel). They end with a "prayer for relief" -- a specific statement of what the client wants the court to do (a form of call to action). The legal conventions demonstrate that introductions and conclusions are not merely literary devices but functional tools that serve specific purposes in specific contexts. Writers who understand the functional dimension of introductions and conclusions can adapt their strategies to any genre.

The connection to **persuasion psychology** is worth noting. Research on persuasion shows that audiences are most influenced by what they hear first (the primacy effect) and what they hear last (the recency effect). The introduction and conclusion occupy these privileged positions in the reader's memory. This research supports the classical emphasis on making the exordium and peroratio the strongest sections of the argument. A mediocre body sandwiched between a strong introduction and a strong conclusion may be more persuasive than a strong body framed by weak bookends.

The connection to **memory and retention** is significant. Cognitive research shows that readers remember the beginning and end of a text better than the middle. This means that the introduction and conclusion have disproportionate influence on what the reader takes away from the essay. Writers who understand this cognitive fact can invest more revision time in these sections, knowing that they will have the greatest impact on the reader's lasting impression of the argument.

The connection to **cross-cultural communication** is important for writers working in multilingual contexts. Different cultures have different expectations for how texts should begin and end. Some cultures prefer indirect openings that establish the writer's humility and the topic's importance before stating the thesis. Others prefer direct openings that state the thesis immediately. Some cultures favor conclusions that express gratitude and invite correction. Others favor conclusions that assert the argument's significance with confidence. Writers who understand these cultural differences can avoid misunderstandings and build trust with diverse audiences.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The classical oration's six-part structure (exordium, narratio, partition, confirmatio, refutatio, peroratio) dominated Western rhetorical education for two millennia. The exordium and peroratio were understood as the oration's emotional bookends: the exordium prepared the audience to listen sympathetically, and the peroratio moved them to act.

In the medieval and early modern periods, sermon structures inherited the classical pattern. The preacher's introduction established the text and its relevance; the conclusion applied the doctrine to the congregation's lives and called for repentance or virtue. This structure carried into secular prose: the essay form, as practiced by Montaigne and Bacon, features introductions that engage the reader and conclusions that deliver a final insight.

The modern academic introduction-conclusion pair emerged in the nineteenth century alongside the standardization of university writing instruction. The funnel introduction and summary conclusion became the default model, a simplification of the classical pattern that served the needs of mass education.

In the twentieth century, both the process movement and genre studies challenged the standardized model. Process theorists argued that introductions should be revised (often rewritten entirely) after the body is complete. Genre theorists showed that different disciplines have different introduction and conclusion conventions. And the rise of digital writing introduced new considerations: web readers scan introductions quickly and often skip to conclusions, making both sections more important and more compressed than in print.

The concept of the **hook** is relatively modern. While classical rhetoricians discussed ways to secure the audience's attention, the term "hook" and the pedagogical emphasis on crafting attention-grabbing openings emerged in twentieth-century composition textbooks, influenced by journalism's emphasis on the "lead" and advertising's emphasis on capturing attention in the first seconds.

Non-Western rhetorical traditions have different approaches to openings and closings. The Arabic rhetorical tradition, influenced by Quranic style, often opens with praise (of God, of the audience) before introducing the topic. The Confucian tradition values openings that establish the writer's relationship to the subject through reference to classical texts or personal experience. The Japanese essay tradition (*zuihitsu*) favors seemingly casual openings that gradually reveal their significance. These traditions remind us that the funnel introduction is culturally specific, not universal.

The African oral tradition provides another model for openings and closings. In many West African cultures, a speaker begins by greeting the audience and establishing their relationship to the community before introducing the topic. The closing typically includes a proverb or saying that crystallizes the speech's message. This structure values relational connection over informational efficiency, and it reminds us that introductions and conclusions serve social as well as rhetorical functions.

The Indian rhetorical tradition, particularly the Nyaya school, begins arguments by stating the thesis (*pratijna*) and then provides the reason, example, application, and conclusion. This structure is more direct than the classical Western exordium, reflecting a different set of assumptions about the audience's relationship to the speaker and the topic.

The philosophical question of **beginnings and endings** has been explored by thinkers from Aristotle (who discussed *archai*, or starting points, in both metaphysics and rhetoric) to Edward Said (whose *Beginnings: Intention and Method* examines how texts establish their starting points). Said argues that beginnings are not neutral; they encode assumptions about what counts as knowledge and who counts as a reader. This insight applies to essay introductions: the choice of hook, context, and thesis encodes assumptions about the reader's knowledge, interests, and values.

The relationship between introduction-conclusion structure and **the concept of the essay** itself is significant. The word "essay" comes from the French *essayer*, "to try" or "to attempt." Montaigne's essays were explorations, not arguments -- they meandered, digressed, and circled back. The modern academic essay, with its rigid introduction-body-conclusion structure, is a departure from this exploratory ideal. Some composition scholars argue that the formulaic introduction and conclusion have made student essays more predictable and less interesting. The tension between structure and exploration remains a live issue in writing pedagogy.

The phenomenon of **the false ending** -- a conclusion that appears to close the argument but then reopens it in the final sentence -- is a technique used by sophisticated writers. The false ending creates a sense of completion and then disrupts it, leaving the reader with a final provocation. This technique should be used sparingly; when it works, it produces a powerful sense of intellectual unfinishedness that keeps the reader thinking after the essay is over. Ta-Nehisi Coates's *Between the World and Me* uses this technique: the letter appears to close with reflections on his son's future, but the final pages reopen the question of whether that future is possible in a society structured by racism.

The history of the **concluding paragraph** in American education is instructive. In the mid-twentieth century, the standard advice was to "restate your thesis in different words." By the 1970s, composition scholars were criticizing this advice as reductive and urging more sophisticated conclusion strategies (synthesis, implication, further questions). By the 2000s, the consensus had shifted toward teaching multiple conclusion strategies and requiring students to choose the one most appropriate to their argument. This evolution reflects a broader shift in composition pedagogy from formulaic rules to rhetorical judgment.

The philosophical concept of **telos** (end or purpose) connects introduction and conclusion in a deeper way. Aristotle argued that every argument has a *telos* -- a purpose toward which it aims. The introduction establishes the *telos* (what the argument is trying to achieve). The body works toward it. The conclusion reveals whether the *telos* has been achieved and what it means that it has (or has not). This teleological view of argument reminds writers that introductions and conclusions are not merely formal requirements but expressions of the argument's fundamental purpose.

The relationship between introductions and **attention economics** has become increasingly important. In an age of information overload, readers make snap judgments about whether to continue reading. Studies of online reading behavior show that many readers never scroll past the first screen. This means the introduction carries an enormous burden: it must not only engage and contextualize but must convince the reader that the essay is worth the investment of time. This economic dimension of attention does not change the principles of good introduction writing (engagement, context, thesis) but it raises the stakes: a mediocre introduction that might have been tolerated in a print culture may be fatal in a digital culture.

The concept of **the extended introduction** in research writing deserves attention. In research papers, the introduction often includes a literature review -- a summary of what previous scholars have found and where the gaps remain. This extended introduction can span several pages and serves a dual function: it establishes the writer's credibility (by demonstrating knowledge of the field) and it justifies the current study (by showing that a gap exists). The CARS model (Create a Research Space) formalizes this structure: Move 1 establishes the territory, Move 2 identifies the gap, Move 3 occupies the niche. Research writers who master this model write introductions that simultaneously position their work within a scholarly conversation and demonstrate why that work is necessary.
