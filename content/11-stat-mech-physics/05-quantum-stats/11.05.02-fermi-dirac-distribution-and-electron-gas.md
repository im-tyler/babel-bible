## Intuition [Beginner]

Electrons, protons, and neutrons are **fermions**: particles with half-integer spin that obey the **Pauli exclusion principle**. No two identical fermions can occupy the same quantum state. Picture electrons in a metal filling up energy levels like seats in a theatre — each seat holds at most one person, and the seating is enforced by a fundamental law of quantum mechanics rather than social convention.

At absolute zero, electrons pack into the lowest available states. The energy of the highest occupied state is the **Fermi energy** $E_F$. Every state below $E_F$ is full; every state above is empty. At finite temperature the occupation becomes a smooth step: the **Fermi-Dirac distribution** gives the probability that a state at energy $E$ is occupied:

$$
n(E) = \frac{1}{e^{(E - E_F)/kT} + 1}.
$$

At $T = 0$ this is a sharp step: $n = 1$ for $E < E_F$ and $n = 0$ for $E > E_F$. At room temperature only electrons within about $kT$ of $E_F$ can change states — the rest are "frozen" below.

This explains why metals have a small electronic specific heat. A classical gas of $N$ electrons would carry specific heat $\tfrac{3}{2}Nk_B$, but in a metal only the fraction $kT/E_F$ of electrons near the Fermi surface can absorb thermal energy, giving $C_v \sim (kT/E_F)Nk_B$ — much smaller.

Copper has $E_F \approx 7$ eV. At 300 K, $kT \approx 0.026$ eV, so the thermal "fuzzy region" near $E_F$ is only about 0.4% of the full energy range.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a tall building with many floors. Each floor is an energy level, and each floor has exactly two apartments (spin-up and spin-down). The Pauli principle says at most one electron per apartment. At $T = 0$, tenants fill from the ground floor up — the building is full up to floor $E_F$ and empty above. The **Fermi surface** is the boundary between occupied and unoccupied floors.

Turn on a small amount of heat. Only the tenants on the top few floors near $E_F$ have enough energy to move to higher floors. Everyone else is stuck — there are no empty apartments nearby. This is why the electron gas in a metal contributes so little to heat capacity: almost all electrons are locked in place by the exclusion principle.

![Building analogy: a skyscraper filled from ground floor to E_F. Tenants near the top can move to empty higher floors; those on lower floors are blocked by occupied apartments above them.](/img/11.05.02-building-analogy-placeholder.svg)

## Worked example [Beginner]

**Copper: how many electrons can participate in thermal physics?**

Copper has one conduction electron per atom. The Fermi energy is $E_F \approx 7.0$ eV. At room temperature $T = 300$ K, the thermal energy is $kT \approx 0.026$ eV.

The fraction of electrons within $kT$ of the Fermi surface is roughly $kT / E_F = 0.026 / 7.0 \approx 0.004$, or about 0.4%. Only these electrons can change their state when the metal absorbs heat.

The classical prediction for the electronic specific heat would be $C_{\text{classical}} = \tfrac{3}{2}Nk_B$. The actual result from Fermi-Dirac statistics is smaller by the factor $kT/E_F$:

$$
C_v \approx \frac{\pi^2}{2}\,\frac{kT}{E_F}\,Nk_B \approx 0.006\,Nk_B.
$$

This is roughly 250 times smaller than the classical prediction — matching experimental measurements on metals.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Consider a system of non-interacting fermions that can occupy single-particle states labelled by $k$ with energies $\epsilon_k$. The **grand canonical ensemble** [11.04.01] for fermions differs from the bosonic case in one critical respect: each state $k$ can be occupied by at most one particle ($n_k \in \{0, 1\}$), enforced by the Pauli exclusion principle [12.01.02].

For a single state $k$, the grand partition function is

$$
\mathcal{Z}_k = \sum_{n_k=0}^{1} e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)n_k} = 1 + e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)},
$$

where $\beta = 1/(k_B T)$ and $\mu$ is the chemical potential. Since the states are independent, the full grand partition function factorises:

$$
\mathcal{Z}_{GC} = \prod_k \big(1 + e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)}\big).
$$

The grand potential $\Omega = -PV = -k_BT \ln \mathcal{Z}_{GC}$ gives

$$
\Omega = -k_BT \sum_k \ln\big(1 + e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)}\big).
$$

The pressure follows immediately: $P = k_BT \sum_k \ln(1 + e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)})/V$. In the continuum limit this becomes $P = (k_BT/V)\int g(\epsilon)\ln(1 + e^{-\beta(\epsilon - \mu)})\, d\epsilon$, which for the degenerate gas at $T = 0$ reduces to $P_0 = 2nE_F/5$.

The **mean occupation number** of state $k$ is

$$
\langle n_k \rangle = \frac{1}{\mathcal{Z}_k}\sum_{n_k=0}^{1} n_k\, e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)n_k} = \frac{e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)}}{1 + e^{-\beta(\epsilon_k - \mu)}}.
$$

This gives the **Fermi-Dirac distribution**:

$$
\boxed{\;\;f(\epsilon) = \frac{1}{e^{\beta(\epsilon - \mu)} + 1}.\;\;}
$$

At $T = 0$ the chemical potential equals the Fermi energy: $\mu(T=0) = E_F$. The distribution becomes a step function: $f = 1$ for $\epsilon < E_F$, $f = 0$ for $\epsilon > E_F$. At low temperatures ($kT \ll E_F$), $\mu \approx E_F$ and the distribution changes from 1 to 0 in a narrow band of width $\sim kT$ around $E_F$.

The **Fermi temperature** is defined as $T_F = E_F / k_B$. For typical metals, $T_F \sim 10^4$ K, so room temperature is a small perturbation.

### The free electron gas in a box

For $N$ free electrons in a box of volume $V$, the single-particle energies are $\epsilon_{\mathbf{k}} = \hbar^2 k^2 / (2m)$. The density of states per unit energy (counting spin degeneracy) is

$$
g(\epsilon) = \frac{V}{2\pi^2}\left(\frac{2m}{\hbar^2}\right)^{3/2} \sqrt{\epsilon}.
$$

At $T = 0$, the total number of electrons fixes $E_F$:

$$
N = \int_0^{E_F} g(\epsilon)\,d\epsilon = \frac{V}{3\pi^2}\left(\frac{2mE_F}{\hbar^2}\right)^{3/2},
$$

giving

$$
E_F = \frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\left(3\pi^2 n\right)^{2/3},
$$

where $n = N/V$ is the electron number density. The total energy at $T = 0$ is

$$
U_0 = \int_0^{E_F} \epsilon\,g(\epsilon)\,d\epsilon = \frac{3}{5}NE_F,
$$

and the pressure at $T = 0$ is $P_0 = 2U_0/(3V) = 2nE_F/5$ — the **degeneracy pressure** of the electron gas, which does not vanish even at zero temperature.

This zero-point pressure is a purely quantum-mechanical effect with no classical analogue. In a classical gas, $T = 0$ implies all particles at rest and zero pressure. For fermions, the Pauli principle forces electrons into progressively higher energy states even at $T = 0$, and the resulting kinetic energy produces a pressure. For copper ($n \approx 8.5 \times 10^{28}\,\text{m}^{-3}$, $E_F \approx 7$ eV): $P_0 \approx 3.8 \times 10^{10}\,\text{Pa} \approx 3.8 \times 10^5$ atm — a pressure comparable to the interior of Jupiter. This enormous pressure is contained by the electrostatic attraction between electrons and the ionic lattice; the balance between degeneracy pressure and Coulomb attraction determines the equilibrium density of the metal.

The Fermi velocity is $v_F = \sqrt{2E_F/m}$. For copper: $v_F \approx 1.57 \times 10^6$ m/s, or about 0.5% of the speed of light. The Fermi wave vector is $k_F = mv_F/\hbar = (3\pi^2 n)^{1/3} \approx 1.36 \times 10^{10}\,\text{m}^{-1}$. The Fermi wavelength $\lambda_F = 2\pi/k_F \approx 0.46$ nm is comparable to the interatomic spacing, confirming that the electron gas is deeply quantum-mechanical — the de Broglie wavelength of electrons at the Fermi surface is of the same order as the lattice constant.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The chemical potential $\mu$ is **not** in general equal to $E_F$. The Fermi energy is defined as $\mu(T=0)$. At finite temperature $\mu(T)$ decreases slightly below $E_F$ for a 3D gas. The distinction matters at the level of the Sommerfeld expansion.
- The FD distribution $f(\epsilon)$ is the mean occupation of a *single* state. When states are degenerate (e.g., spin), the total occupation of the energy level includes the degeneracy factor.
- The free electron gas model neglects electron-electron interactions and the periodic lattice potential. In real metals, band structure modifies the density of states substantially, but the Fermi-surface concept and the FD distribution remain valid — the Fermi surface becomes a surface in $\mathbf{k}$-space determined by the band dispersion rather than the free-particle parabola.
- "Degenerate" in this context means $T \ll T_F$, not related to energy-level degeneracy. A highly degenerate Fermi gas is one whose properties are dominated by the exclusion principle rather than thermal effects. The opposite regime $T \gg T_F$ is the classical limit where the FD distribution reduces to the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution and quantum effects become negligible.

- The free electron gas model neglects electron-electron interactions and the periodic lattice potential. In real metals, band structure modifies the density of states substantially, but the Fermi-surface concept and the FD distribution remain valid — the Fermi surface becomes a surface in $\mathbf{k}$-space determined by the band dispersion rather than the free-particle parabola. The effective mass approximation $m^*$ captures the band-structure renormalisation in a single parameter.

- The Fermi energy $E_F$ is sometimes called the Fermi level in semiconductor physics, but the two are not identical. The Fermi level is the chemical potential $\mu$ at any temperature, while $E_F$ is specifically $\mu$ at $T = 0$. In metals the distinction is negligible ($\mu(T) \approx E_F$ for $T \ll T_F$), but in semiconductors $\mu$ can differ dramatically from any band edge.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Sommerfeld electronic specific heat).** *For a free electron gas in three dimensions with $N$ electrons and Fermi energy $E_F$, the electronic specific heat at constant volume at low temperature ($kT \ll E_F$) is*

$$
\boxed{\;\;C_v = \frac{\pi^2}{2}\,\frac{k_BT}{E_F}\,Nk_B.\;\;}
$$

**Proof.** The total energy at temperature $T$ is

$$
U(T) = \int_0^{\infty} \epsilon\,g(\epsilon)\,f(\epsilon)\,d\epsilon,
$$

where $g(\epsilon) = C\sqrt{\epsilon}$ with $C = (V/2\pi^2)(2m/\hbar^2)^{3/2}$ and $f(\epsilon) = 1/(e^{\beta(\epsilon - \mu)} + 1)$. Define $h(\epsilon) = \epsilon\,g(\epsilon) = C\epsilon^{3/2}$. We need

$$
U(T) = \int_0^{\infty} h(\epsilon)\,f(\epsilon)\,d\epsilon.
$$

The **Sommerfeld expansion** evaluates integrals of the form $\int_0^{\infty} \phi(\epsilon)\,f(\epsilon)\,d\epsilon$ when $kT \ll E_F$. Integrating by parts:

$$
\int_0^{\infty} \phi(\epsilon)\,f(\epsilon)\,d\epsilon = -\int_0^{\infty} \Phi(\epsilon)\,f'(\epsilon)\,d\epsilon,
$$

where $\Phi(\epsilon) = \int_0^{\epsilon} \phi(\epsilon')\,d\epsilon'$. The derivative $-f'(\epsilon)$ is sharply peaked around $\epsilon = \mu$ with width $\sim k_BT$ and integrates to 1. Taylor-expanding $\Phi(\epsilon)$ around $\mu$:

$$
\Phi(\epsilon) = \Phi(\mu) + \Phi'(\mu)(\epsilon - \mu) + \tfrac{1}{2}\Phi''(\mu)(\epsilon - \mu)^2 + \cdots
$$

The zeroth term gives $\Phi(\mu) = \int_0^{\mu} \phi(\epsilon)\,d\epsilon$. The first-order term vanishes by symmetry of $f'$. The second-order term gives

$$
\int_0^{\infty} \phi(\epsilon)\,f(\epsilon)\,d\epsilon = \int_0^{\mu} \phi(\epsilon)\,d\epsilon + \frac{\pi^2}{6}(k_BT)^2\,\phi'(\mu) + O(T^4).
$$

Applying this to $U(T)$ with $\phi(\epsilon) = h(\epsilon) = C\epsilon^{3/2}$:

$$
U(T) = \int_0^{\mu} C\epsilon^{3/2}\,d\epsilon + \frac{\pi^2}{6}(k_BT)^2 \cdot \tfrac{3}{2}C\mu^{1/2} + O(T^4).
$$

The zeroth-order piece is $\tfrac{2}{5}C\mu^{5/2} = \tfrac{3}{5}N\mu$ (using $N = \tfrac{2}{3}C\mu^{3/2}$ at leading order). Similarly, applying the expansion to the particle number $N = \int g(\epsilon)\,f(\epsilon)\,d\epsilon$ gives $\mu = E_F[1 - O(T^2/E_F^2)]$, so at leading order in $T$ we may replace $\mu$ by $E_F$ in the correction term. Then:

$$
U(T) = \frac{3}{5}NE_F + \frac{\pi^2}{4}N\,\frac{(k_BT)^2}{E_F} + O(T^4).
$$

Differentiating with respect to $T$:

$$
C_v = \frac{\partial U}{\partial T} = \frac{\pi^2}{2}\,\frac{k_BT}{E_F}\,Nk_B + O(T^3). \qquad \square
$$

**Corollary.** The ratio $C_v/C_v^{\text{classical}} = (\pi^2/3)(kT/E_F) \ll 1$ at room temperature. For copper ($E_F \approx 7$ eV, $T = 300$ K): $C_v/C_v^{\text{classical}} \approx 0.006$. This resolves the century-old puzzle of why the Dulong-Petit law overestimates the specific heat of metals by counting all electrons as classical particles.

### The Sommerfeld expansion: general framework

The Sommerfeld expansion is a systematic procedure for evaluating integrals of the form

$$
I = \int_0^{\infty} \phi(\epsilon)\, f(\epsilon)\, d\epsilon
$$

where $f(\epsilon) = 1/(e^{\beta(\epsilon - \mu)} + 1)$ is the Fermi-Dirac distribution and $kT \ll E_F$. The expansion proceeds by defining $\Phi(\epsilon) = \int_0^{\epsilon} \phi(\epsilon')\, d\epsilon'$ and integrating by parts:

$$
I = \int_0^{\infty} \Phi'(\epsilon)\, f(\epsilon)\, d\epsilon = [\Phi(\epsilon) f(\epsilon)]_0^{\infty} + \int_0^{\infty} \Phi(\epsilon)\, (-f'(\epsilon))\, d\epsilon.
$$

The boundary terms vanish because $f(\infty) = 0$ and $\Phi(0) = 0$. The derivative $-f'(\epsilon) = \beta e^{\beta(\epsilon - \mu)}/(e^{\beta(\epsilon - \mu)} + 1)^2$ is sharply peaked around $\epsilon = \mu$ with width $\sim kT$ and integrates to 1. Taylor-expanding $\Phi(\epsilon)$ around $\mu$:

$$
\Phi(\epsilon) = \Phi(\mu) + \Phi'(\mu)(\epsilon - \mu) + \tfrac{1}{2}\Phi''(\mu)(\epsilon - \mu)^2 + \cdots
$$

Only even powers of $(\epsilon - \mu)$ survive the integration because $-f'(\epsilon)$ is an even function of $(\epsilon - \mu)$ to leading order. The moments are

$$
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (\epsilon - \mu)^{2n}(-f'(\epsilon))\, d\epsilon = \frac{2}{2n+1}\left(\frac{2}{\beta}\right)^{2n} \int_0^{\infty} \frac{x^{2n}}{e^x + 1}\, \frac{dx}{x} = c_{2n}(kT)^{2n},
$$

where $c_2 = \pi^2/3$, $c_4 = 7\pi^4/15$, and so on. The result is the Sommerfeld expansion:

$$
I = \int_0^{\mu} \phi(\epsilon)\, d\epsilon + \frac{\pi^2}{6}(kT)^2\, \phi'(\mu) + \frac{7\pi^4}{360}(kT)^4\, \phi'''(\mu) + \cdots
$$

This is the workhorse of low-temperature solid-state physics. It applies whenever $\phi$ is a smooth function and $kT \ll \mu$, which is satisfied for all metals at laboratory temperatures. The absence of odd powers of $T$ is a consequence of the particle-hole symmetry of the FD distribution.

### Worked example: Pauli paramagnetism

A free electron gas in a uniform magnetic field $B$ develops a net magnetisation because spin-up and spin-down electrons shift their Fermi energies by $\pm \mu_B B$ (where $\mu_B = e\hbar/(2m)$ is the Bohr magneton). The excess of spin-up over spin-down electrons near $E_F$ is $\delta N \sim g(E_F) \mu_B B$. The magnetisation is $M = \mu_B \delta N / V = \mu_B^2 g(E_F) B$, giving the **Pauli susceptibility**

$$
\chi_P = \mu_B^2\,g(E_F) = \frac{3}{2}\,\frac{N\mu_B^2}{V\,E_F}.
$$

This is temperature-independent at low $T$ (unlike the Curie law $1/T$ for local moments), because only electrons near $E_F$ can flip their spin, and their number does not change with $T$ at leading order.

### Worked example: degeneracy pressure in white dwarf stability

Consider a white dwarf of mass $M = 1\,M_\odot \approx 2 \times 10^{30}$ kg and radius $R \approx R_\oplus \approx 6.4 \times 10^6$ m. The number of electrons (assuming carbon/oxygen composition, approximately 0.5 electrons per unit mass) is $N_e \approx M/(2m_p) \approx 6 \times 10^{56}$. The electron density is $n_e = N_e/(4\pi R^3/3) \approx 4.4 \times 10^{36}\,\text{m}^{-3}$.

The Fermi energy is $E_F = (\hbar^2/2m_e)(3\pi^2 n_e)^{2/3}$. Computing: $(3\pi^2 \times 4.4 \times 10^{36})^{2/3} \approx (1.3 \times 10^{38})^{2/3} \approx 2.6 \times 10^{25}\,\text{m}^{-2}$. Then $E_F = (1.05 \times 10^{-34})^2 \times 2.6 \times 10^{25}/(2 \times 9.11 \times 10^{-31}) \approx 1.6 \times 10^{-14}\,\text{J} \approx 0.1$ MeV.

The Fermi momentum is $p_F = \sqrt{2m_e E_F} \approx \sqrt{2 \times 9.11 \times 10^{-31} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-14}} \approx 1.7 \times 10^{-22}\,\text{kg m/s}$, and $p_F/(m_e c) \approx 1.7 \times 10^{-22}/(2.7 \times 10^{-22}) \approx 0.6$. The electrons are mildly relativistic — the non-relativistic degeneracy pressure provides a reasonable estimate but the relativistic correction is significant, and for more massive white dwarfs the Chandrasekhar limit is approached.

The degeneracy pressure is $P_0 \approx 2n_e E_F/5 \approx 2 \times 4.4 \times 10^{36} \times 1.6 \times 10^{-14}/5 \approx 2.8 \times 10^{22}\,\text{Pa}$. The gravitational pressure is $P_{\rm grav} \sim GM^2/(4\pi R^4) \approx 6.67 \times 10^{-11} \times (2 \times 10^{30})^2/(4\pi \times (6.4 \times 10^6)^4) \approx 2.5 \times 10^{22}\,\text{Pa}$. The two pressures are comparable, confirming hydrostatic equilibrium. A more massive star would have higher $n_e$, pushing electrons further into the relativistic regime where degeneracy pressure weakens relative to gravity, until the Chandrasekhar limit is reached.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the Fermi-Dirac distribution satisfies $f(\epsilon) + f(-\epsilon) = 1$ when $\mu = 0$ (measuring energies relative to the chemical potential).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write out $f(-\epsilon) = 1/(e^{-\beta\epsilon} + 1)$ and add it to $f(\epsilon) = 1/(e^{\beta\epsilon} + 1)$. Find a common denominator.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$f(\epsilon) + f(-\epsilon) = \frac{1}{e^{\beta\epsilon} + 1} + \frac{1}{e^{-\beta\epsilon} + 1} = \frac{1}{e^{\beta\epsilon} + 1} + \frac{e^{\beta\epsilon}}{1 + e^{\beta\epsilon}} = \frac{1 + e^{\beta\epsilon}}{1 + e^{\beta\epsilon}} = 1$. ## The Fermi surface and Landau's Fermi liquid theory [Master]

The **Fermi surface** is the surface in momentum space $\mathbf{k}$ defined by $\epsilon(\mathbf{k}) = E_F$. For a free electron gas this is a sphere of radius $k_F = (3\pi^2 n)^{1/3}$. In a real metal, the periodic lattice potential breaks the spherical symmetry and the Fermi surface develops complex topography — sheets, necks, and open orbits — that encode the band structure. Experimental techniques such as de Haas-van Alphen oscillations measure the extremal cross-sectional areas of the Fermi surface directly.

**Landau's Fermi liquid theory** (1956) provides the framework for understanding why the free-electron picture works as well as it does, despite strong electron-electron interactions in metals. Landau postulated that the low-energy excitations of the interacting system are **quasiparticles** — long-lived excitations that are in one-to-one correspondence with the free-particle states, carrying the same quantum numbers but with renormalised parameters (effective mass $m^*$, effective $g$-factor, etc.).

The key insight is adiabatic continuity: if the interaction is turned on slowly, each free-particle state evolves into a quasiparticle state without crossing other states. The Pauli principle ensures stability: a quasiparticle near the Fermi surface can only decay by scattering into other states near $E_F$, and the phase space for such processes scales as $(\epsilon - E_F)^2$, giving quasiparticle lifetimes $\tau \sim 1/(\epsilon - E_F)^2$. The Fermi surface itself is **protected** by the Luttinger theorem: the volume enclosed by the Fermi surface is proportional to the total electron density, independent of interaction strength.

Landau introduced **interaction parameters** $F_l^s$ (symmetric) and $F_l^a$ (antisymmetric) characterising how quasiparticles interact in angular-momentum channel $l$. The specific heat, compressibility, and susceptibility are renormalised:

$$
\frac{C_v}{C_v^0} = \frac{m^*}{m} = 1 + \frac{F_1^s}{3}, \qquad \frac{\chi}{\chi_0} = \frac{m^*/m}{1 + F_0^a}, \qquad \frac{\kappa}{\kappa_0} = \frac{m/m^*}{1 + F_0^s}.
$$

Fermi liquid theory breaks down in one dimension (where the correct description is the **Luttinger liquid** with spin-charge separation) and in systems near a quantum critical point. Non-Fermi-liquid behaviour — linear-in-$T$ resistivity, logarithmic specific-heat divergence, anomalous power laws — is observed in many strongly correlated materials (cuprate superconductors, heavy fermion compounds) and is a major research frontier in condensed matter physics.

### The de Haas-van Alphen effect

The quantisation of electron orbits in a magnetic field $B$ causes the Fermi surface to slice through Landau levels as $B$ varies, producing oscillations in the magnetisation $M(B)$ known as the **de Haas-van Alphen (dHvA) effect**. The oscillation period $\Delta(1/B) = 2\pi e/\hbar A_{\text{ext}}$ directly measures the extremal cross-sectional area $A_{\text{ext}}$ of the Fermi surface perpendicular to the field direction. By rotating the field, the full Fermi surface topology can be mapped.

The Lifshitz-Kosevich formula gives the oscillatory part of the magnetisation:

$$
M_{\text{osc}} \propto \sum_{p=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{p^{3/2}} \sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi p F}{B} + \phi\right) R_T(p)\, R_D(p),
$$

where $F = \hbar A_{\text{ext}}/(2\pi e)$ is the dHvA frequency, $R_T = (\pi p k_B T m^*/(\hbar e B))/\sinh(\pi p k_B T m^*/(\hbar e B))$ is the thermal damping factor, and $R_D = \exp(-\pi p /(\omega_c \tau))$ is the Dingle damping factor. The effective mass $m^*$ is extracted from the temperature dependence of the oscillation amplitude, providing a direct measurement of quasiparticle renormalisation.

## Density of states in real metals [Master]

The free-electron density of states $g(\epsilon) \propto \sqrt{\epsilon}$ is modified by band structure. In the nearly-free-electron model, Bragg planes open energy gaps and the density of states develops **van Hove singularities** — logarithmic divergences in 2D and square-root cusps in 3D — at the band edges. The Fermi energy may fall near such a singularity, dramatically enhancing the electronic specific heat and the superconducting transition temperature.

The measured **Sommerfeld coefficient** $\gamma = C_v/T = (\pi^2/3)k_B^2 g(E_F)$ is conventionally written as $\gamma = \gamma_0(m^*/m)$ where $\gamma_0$ is the free-electron value. Heavy-fermion materials (e.g., CeAl$_3$, UPt$_3$) have $m^*/m \sim 10^2$--$10^3$, reflecting enormous quasiparticle masses from strong correlations. These systems lie at the boundary of Fermi liquid theory and often exhibit unconventional superconductivity.

The relationship between the electronic density of states and superconductivity is a central theme of the BCS theory: the superconducting transition temperature $T_c \sim \omega_D \exp(-1/(g(E_F)V))$ where $\omega_D$ is the Debye frequency and $V$ is the effective electron-electron attraction mediated by phonons. A van Hove singularity at $E_F$ can enhance $g(E_F)$ and thus $T_c$, explaining why some materials with unusual Fermi surface geometries are high-temperature superconductors.

## White dwarf stars as degenerate electron gas [Master]

A white dwarf is the remnant core of a low-to-intermediate mass star ($M \lesssim 8 M_\odot$), supported against gravitational collapse by electron degeneracy pressure. The typical mass is $\sim 0.6\,M_\odot$ and the radius is $\sim R_\oplus$, giving electron densities $n_e \sim 10^{36}\,\text{m}^{-3}$ and $E_F \sim 0.3$ MeV — well into the relativistic regime.

The equation of state has two limits. **Non-relativistic** ($p_F \ll m_e c$): $P \propto \rho^{5/3}$, supporting any mass at some radius. **Ultra-relativistic** ($p_F \gg m_e c$): $P \propto \rho^{4/3}$, and the pressure scales the same way as gravitational force with density. Equating the two pressures yields a unique mass — the **Chandrasekhar limit** $M_{\rm Ch} \approx 1.4\,M_\odot$ (Exercise 9). Above this mass, no stable white dwarf exists; the star collapses further to a neutron star or black hole.

### Neutron degeneracy pressure and neutron stars

When a white dwarf exceeds the Chandrasekhar mass, electron degeneracy pressure fails and the star collapses. At the resulting densities ($\rho \sim 10^{17}\,\text{kg/m}^3$, nuclear density), electrons inverse-beta-decay into neutrons via $e^- + p \to n + \nu_e$. The remaining neutrons form a degenerate Fermi gas with Fermi energy $E_F \sim 100$ MeV and Fermi temperature $T_F \sim 10^{12}$ K. The neutron degeneracy pressure $P \sim \hbar^2 n_n^{5/3}/m_n$ supports the star against further collapse.

A neutron star of mass $1.4\,M_\odot$ has radius $\sim 10$ km — comparable to a city. The equation of state involves strong nuclear interactions that modify the free-neutron degeneracy pressure significantly. The upper mass limit for neutron stars (the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff limit) is approximately $2-3\,M_\odot$, depending on the nuclear equation of state. Above this limit, even neutron degeneracy pressure fails, and the star collapses to a black hole.

The detection of gravitational waves from the neutron-star merger GW170817 (LIGO/Virgo, 2017) provided the first direct constraint on the neutron-star equation of state, ruling out extremely soft equations of state and confirming that neutron degeneracy pressure, supplemented by nuclear forces, supports these objects.

### Metals, insulators, and the band picture

The free-electron model treats conduction electrons as a Fermi gas in a box. Real solids have a periodic lattice potential that splits the free-electron energy levels into **bands** — ranges of allowed energies separated by **gaps** (forbidden energies). The Fermi energy's location relative to the band structure determines whether the solid is a metal, insulator, or semiconductor.

In a **metal**, the Fermi energy lies within a partially filled band. Electrons near $E_F$ can be excited to nearby empty states with arbitrarily small energy cost, giving high electrical and thermal conductivity. The FD distribution applies directly, with the band structure modifying the density of states $g(\epsilon)$ from the free-electron $\sqrt{\epsilon}$ to the band-specific form.

In an **insulator**, the Fermi energy lies in a band gap. The valence band below $E_F$ is completely filled, and the conduction band above is empty. At $T = 0$ there are no partially filled bands. At finite $T$, thermal excitation across the gap populates the conduction band, but for a gap $E_g \gg kT$ the occupation is exponentially suppressed: the number of thermally excited carriers is $\sim e^{-E_g/(2kT)}$, giving extremely small conductivity. Diamond ($E_g = 5.5$ eV) is an insulator at all accessible temperatures.

A **semiconductor** is an insulator with a small gap ($E_g \sim 0.1$–$2$ eV). Silicon ($E_g = 1.1$ eV) has a significant thermal carrier population at room temperature. Doping introduces states in the gap that shift the effective Fermi energy, controlling the carrier density and enabling transistor action.

The distinction between metals and insulators is a Fermi-surface phenomenon: metals have a Fermi surface (a surface in k-space separating occupied from unoccupied states), while insulators do not. The existence and topology of the Fermi surface is determined by the band filling — the number of electrons per unit cell — and the band structure. Topological insulators add a further classification: materials that are insulators in the bulk but have metallic surface states protected by topology.

## Connections [Master]

The Fermi-Dirac distribution connects to nearly every area of condensed matter and many-body physics:

- **Superconductivity** (BCS theory): The Cooper instability arises because electrons near the Fermi surface in the presence of an attractive interaction form bound pairs. The BCS ground state is a coherent superposition of occupied and unoccupied states near $E_F$, and the energy gap $\Delta$ opens at the Fermi surface.
- **Quantum Hall effect**: In two-dimensional electron gases, Landau quantisation discretises the density of states into delta-function-like levels. The integer and fractional quantum Hall effects are Fermi-surface phenomena in the presence of a strong magnetic field.
- **Nuclear physics**: Nucleons in a nucleus obey Fermi-Dirac statistics with $E_F \sim 38$ MeV. The nuclear Fermi gas model treats the nucleus as two degenerate Fermi gases (protons and neutrons) and explains the asymmetry term in the semi-empirical mass formula. The Fermi momentum $p_F \approx 250$ MeV/c is a fundamental parameter of nuclear structure, and the nuclear equation of state at high density (relevant to neutron stars and heavy-ion collisions) is a Fermi-gas problem with strong interactions.

- **Neutron stars**: At densities above nuclear saturation, electrons inverse-beta-decay into neutrons. The resulting neutron degeneracy pressure ($E_F \sim 100$ MeV) supports the star — a white dwarf supported by electron degeneracy becomes a neutron star supported by neutron degeneracy. The transition from electron to neutron degeneracy pressure marks the boundary between white dwarfs and neutron stars, and the upper mass limit for neutron stars (the Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff limit) is the analogue of the Chandrasekhar limit for neutron degeneracy.

- **Cross-reference to chemistry** [14.01.01]: The aufbau principle for atomic electron configurations is the single-atom limit of the FD distribution. Each orbital in an atom is a "state" that holds at most two electrons (spin up/down), and they fill from lowest energy upward — the molecular-orbital and crystal-field treatments in inorganic chemistry [16.03.02] inherit this structure. The periodic table itself is a consequence of the FD distribution applied to electrons in the hydrogen-like orbital basis, with the filling order determined by the energy ordering of the orbitals.

- **Condensed matter experiments**: Scanning tunnelling microscopy (STM) directly measures the local density of states at the Fermi surface. The tunnelling current $I \propto \int_0^{eV} g(E_F + \epsilon)[f(E_F + \epsilon) - f(E_F + \epsilon - eV)]\, d\epsilon$ probes the FD distribution near $E_F$. At low temperature the differential conductance $dI/dV$ is proportional to $g(E_F + eV)$, providing a direct image of the density of states — the technique that enabled the discovery of the energy gap in superconductors and the pseudogap in high-$T_c$ materials.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

The Fermi-Dirac distribution was discovered independently by **Enrico Fermi** (1926) and **Paul Dirac** (1926), both building on the new quantum statistics of Bose (1924) and Einstein (1924--25). Fermi's approach started from the Pauli exclusion principle and constructed the statistical mechanics of particles that cannot share a quantum state. Dirac's approach identified the connection between particle statistics and the symmetry of the quantum-mechanical wave function: fermions have antisymmetric wave functions ($\psi(1,2) = -\psi(2,1)$), bosons have symmetric ones. The spin-statistics theorem (proved rigorously by Pauli in 1940) established that particles with half-integer spin are fermions and particles with integer spin are bosons, connecting the FD distribution to the deepest structure of relativistic quantum field theory.

**Arnold Sommerfeld** (1928) applied the FD distribution to electrons in metals, resolving two major puzzles that had plagued the Drude model of electrical conduction since 1900. First, the Drude model predicted an electronic specific heat of $\tfrac{3}{2}Nk_B$ per mole, but measured values were 100 times smaller — the Sommerfeld treatment showed that only the fraction $kT/E_F$ of electrons near the Fermi surface can absorb thermal energy, giving $C_v \sim (kT/E_F)Nk_B$. Second, the Drude model could not explain the long mean free path of conduction electrons (hundreds of nanometres at room temperature, far exceeding the interatomic spacing); the FD picture, combined with Bloch's band theory (1928), showed that electrons in a perfect lattice propagate without scattering, and resistance arises from lattice vibrations (phonons) and impurities. Sommerfeld's free-electron treatment — the "Sommerfeld model" — remains the starting point for solid-state physics textbooks.

**Wolfgang Pauli** (1927) computed the paramagnetic susceptibility of a free electron gas (Pauli paramagnetism), showing that it is temperature-independent at low $T$ — in contrast to the Curie law $\chi \propto 1/T$ for local magnetic moments. The experimental observation that the magnetic susceptibility of alkali metals is nearly temperature-independent was a key confirmation of the FD description of conduction electrons.

**Lev Landau** (1956) generalized the theory to interacting fermions with his Fermi liquid theory, providing the conceptual framework for understanding why the non-interacting picture works so well despite the strong Coulomb repulsion between electrons in metals. Landau's key postulate — that the low-energy excitations of the interacting system are in one-to-one correspondence with free-particle states (quasiparticles) — is justified by adiabatic continuity: turning on interactions slowly deforms free-particle states into quasiparticle states without crossing other states. The Pauli principle ensures quasiparticle stability by restricting the phase space for decay. Landau's theory predicted the existence of zero sound in liquid $^3$He (confirmed experimentally in 1966) and established the renormalisation-group language later adopted for quantum critical phenomena.

**Subrahmanyan Chandrasekhar** (1931) showed that relativistic electron degeneracy cannot support stars above $\sim 1.4\,M_\odot$, predicting the existence of neutron stars and black holes decades before their observational confirmation. This result was controversial — Eddington publicly opposed it at a 1935 meeting of the Royal Astronomical Society, arguing that "there should be a law of Nature to prevent a star from behaving in this absurd way." Chandrasekhar did not receive the Nobel Prize for this work until 1983, and the Chandrasekhar limit is now a cornerstone of stellar astrophysics. The philosophical lesson is that simple statistical-mechanical arguments (the FD distribution applied to relativistic electrons in a gravitational field) can have profound consequences for the fate of cosmic objects.

**Philosophical note on exclusion and individuality.** The Pauli exclusion principle raises a subtle question about the individuality of particles. In classical physics, particles are distinguishable by their trajectories; in quantum mechanics, identical fermions are not merely hard to distinguish — they are *numerically identical* in the sense that exchanging two of them changes the sign of the wave function but no observable. The FD distribution encodes this: the occupation number of each state is 0 or 1, not because particles "avoid each other" as a matter of dynamics, but because the antisymmetric wave function vanishes when two fermions occupy the same state. Whether this is a dynamical principle or a kinematic constraint is a question in the philosophy of quantum mechanics that remains debated.
