## Intuition [Beginner]

A statistical-mechanics problem starts with a *small* system in contact with a *large* one. The small system might be a single molecule, a tiny crystal, a single qubit; the large one is a thermal reservoir — a glass of water, the laboratory air, the rest of the universe — that we will not track in detail. The reservoir is so big that whatever the small system does, the reservoir's temperature stays fixed at $T$. The question is: what is the probability that the small system is in any particular one of its microscopic states?

The answer is the **Boltzmann factor**. If the small system has a microstate $i$ with energy $E_i$, the probability of finding it in that state is proportional to

$$
P_i \;\propto\; e^{-E_i / k_B T},
$$

where $k_B$ is Boltzmann's constant. High-energy states are penalised exponentially. Cold reservoirs ($T$ small) make the penalty severe — only the lowest states have a chance. Hot reservoirs spread probability across many states.

The factor $e^{-E_i / k_B T}$ is unnormalised. To turn it into an actual probability you divide by the sum of all such factors, added over every state the system can occupy. That normaliser is the **partition function**, written $Z$. Writing the sum as $Z = e^{-E_0/k_B T} + e^{-E_1/k_B T} + e^{-E_2/k_B T} + \ldots$ and dividing through gives $P_i = e^{-E_i / k_B T} / Z$. Every state contributes one term.

A combination $\beta := 1/(k_B T)$ comes up so often we give it a name — the *inverse temperature*. Low $T$ means large $\beta$ and sharp penalties; high $T$ means small $\beta$ and broad sampling. In these units the Boltzmann factor is $e^{-\beta E_i}$, and the partition function is the same sum written $Z(\beta) = e^{-\beta E_0} + e^{-\beta E_1} + e^{-\beta E_2} + \ldots$ — one Boltzmann-weighted term for each state.

Why bother with $Z$? Because once you know $Z$ as a function of $\beta$, you know everything macroscopic. The average energy is the rate at which $\ln Z$ changes with $\beta$, with an overall minus sign — call this the "log-$Z$ slope". Writing it as the rate of change of $\ln Z$ with respect to $\beta$, multiplied by $-1$, gives the mean energy $\langle E\rangle$.

The energy fluctuations, the heat capacity, the magnetisation, the pressure — every thermodynamic observable is a derivative of $\ln Z$ with respect to some parameter ($\beta$, the box volume, an external field). The partition function is the bookkeeping that turns one number per microstate into the handful of numbers a thermometer reads off.

Two examples set the tone. First, a single coin that can lie heads (energy $0$) or tails (energy $\varepsilon$). Then $Z = 1 + e^{-\beta \varepsilon}$, and the average energy $\langle E \rangle = \varepsilon e^{-\beta \varepsilon} / (1 + e^{-\beta \varepsilon})$ smoothly interpolates between $0$ at low $T$ (the coin sits in heads) and $\varepsilon / 2$ at high $T$ (both faces equally likely). The heat capacity peaks at a temperature near $\varepsilon / k_B$ — the **Schottky anomaly** — because that is where the system has room to absorb energy by flipping heads into tails. Two-state behaviour and a peaked heat capacity is a generic fingerprint of a gap in the energy spectrum.

Second, a particle in a one-dimensional box. The energy levels are now infinitely many, but the same recipe applies: write down $Z$, take a log, differentiate. The result is the ideal-gas law $PV = N k_B T$ once you assemble $N$ such particles. Everything in thermodynamics — entropy, free energy, pressure, chemical potential — comes out of $Z$ by differentiation.

Picture a small box of energy levels next to a much bigger box labelled "reservoir at temperature $T$". A double-headed arrow between them stands for energy exchange. Across the small box are the energy levels $E_0 < E_1 < E_2 < \ldots$ as horizontal rungs. The Boltzmann factor $e^{-\beta E_i}$ is drawn as a bar to the right of each rung — long bars for low energies, short ones for high. Across the bottom is a histogram of these bar lengths after dividing through by $Z$; that is the canonical probability distribution.

A second panel shows the same picture at two temperatures. At low $T$ the bars decay quickly: the ground state takes almost all the probability and the histogram is a spike. At high $T$ the bars decay slowly: many states share probability roughly equally and the histogram is broad.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take a two-level system: one state at energy $0$, one at energy $\varepsilon > 0$. Place it in thermal contact with a reservoir at temperature $T$.

Step 1. Write down the partition function:

$$
Z(\beta) \;=\; e^{-\beta \cdot 0} + e^{-\beta \varepsilon} \;=\; 1 + e^{-\beta \varepsilon}.
$$

Step 2. Probabilities of the two states:

$$
P_0 \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\beta \varepsilon}}, \qquad P_1 \;=\; \frac{e^{-\beta \varepsilon}}{1 + e^{-\beta \varepsilon}}.
$$

Step 3. Mean energy:

$$
\langle E \rangle \;=\; 0 \cdot P_0 + \varepsilon \cdot P_1 \;=\; \frac{\varepsilon \, e^{-\beta \varepsilon}}{1 + e^{-\beta \varepsilon}} \;=\; \frac{\varepsilon}{e^{\beta \varepsilon} + 1}.
$$

Check: at $T \to 0$ (i.e., $\beta \to \infty$), $\langle E \rangle \to 0$ — the system sits in the ground state. At $T \to \infty$ ($\beta \to 0$), $\langle E \rangle \to \varepsilon / 2$ — equal occupation of both states.

Step 4. Heat capacity, $C_V := d\langle E\rangle / dT$. Using $\beta = 1/(k_B T)$ so $d\beta/dT = -1/(k_B T^2)$:

$$
C_V \;=\; k_B \, (\beta \varepsilon)^2 \, \frac{e^{\beta \varepsilon}}{(e^{\beta \varepsilon} + 1)^2}.
$$

This is the **Schottky anomaly**. It is zero at $T \to 0$ (no states reachable), zero at $T \to \infty$ (both states already maxed out), and peaks at a temperature where $k_B T \approx 0.42\, \varepsilon$. The bump in $C_V(T)$ is the signature of a gap of size $\varepsilon$ in the energy spectrum.

What this tells us: the partition function is a single function $Z(\beta)$ from which all the thermodynamics — probabilities, mean energy, heat capacity — flows by elementary calculus. A two-level system is the smallest meaningful example; everything later is more of the same with more states.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let a physical system have a set of microstates $\{i\}$ with energies $\{E_i\}$ (discrete spectrum for the moment) and let $\beta := 1/(k_B T)$ denote the inverse temperature, where $k_B = 1.380649 \times 10^{-23}\,\mathrm{J/K}$ is **Boltzmann's constant** (an exact defined quantity since the 2019 SI redefinition). The **canonical ensemble** is the probability distribution on microstates

$$
P_i \;=\; \frac{1}{Z(\beta)} \, e^{-\beta E_i}, \qquad Z(\beta) \;:=\; \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i},
$$

where $Z(\beta)$ is the **canonical partition function**. The distribution depends on $\beta$ and on the parameters $(V, N, \ldots)$ that label which Hamiltonian is being used; we write $Z(\beta, V, N)$ when the parameter dependence matters. For a continuous classical spectrum on phase space $\Gamma = \prod_i T^*Q_i$, the sum is replaced by a Liouville-measure integral with the Gibbs $1/N!$ correction for identical particles,

$$
Z(\beta, V, N) \;=\; \frac{1}{N!\, h^{3N}} \int_\Gamma e^{-\beta H(q, p)} \, d^{3N}q \, d^{3N}p,
$$

with $h$ Planck's constant making the integrand dimensionless. The math-side measure-theoretic version is developed in [08.01.01].

The canonical ensemble is the unique probability distribution on microstates that **maximises the Shannon entropy** $S_{\mathrm{Sh}} := -k_B \sum_i P_i \ln P_i$ subject to the constraints

$$
\sum_i P_i \;=\; 1 \quad \text{and} \quad \sum_i P_i E_i \;=\; U,
$$

where $U$ is the prescribed mean energy. Solving by Lagrange multipliers — introduce $\alpha$ for normalisation, $\beta$ for the energy constraint — gives $P_i = e^{-\alpha - \beta E_i}$; absorbing the constant into the normaliser identifies $e^{-\alpha} = 1/Z(\beta)$, and the multiplier $\beta$ is in one-to-one correspondence with $U$ via $\partial \ln Z/\partial \beta = -U$. The thermodynamic identification $\beta = 1/(k_B T)$ then comes from matching the canonical $S_{\mathrm{Sh}}$ to the thermodynamic entropy and using $1/T = (\partial S/\partial U)_{V, N}$ from the first and second laws of thermodynamics [11.01.NN, pending].

**Thermodynamic quantities from $Z$.** The bridge between the partition function and macroscopic thermodynamics runs through the **Helmholtz free energy**

$$
F(\beta, V, N) \;:=\; -k_B T \ln Z \;=\; -\beta^{-1} \ln Z,
$$

from which every other thermodynamic potential follows by Legendre transform and elementary identities:

$$
U \;=\; \langle E\rangle \;=\; -\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta} \;=\; F - T\frac{\partial F}{\partial T},
$$
$$
S \;=\; -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial T}\right)_{V, N} \;=\; k_B \ln Z + \frac{U}{T},
$$
$$
P \;=\; -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial V}\right)_{T, N} \;=\; k_B T \frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial V}, \qquad \mu \;=\; \left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial N}\right)_{T, V}.
$$

For specific heats at constant volume, $C_V = (\partial U/\partial T)_{V,N} = k_B \beta^2 (\partial^2 \ln Z/\partial \beta^2)$. The same identity reads

$$
\langle (\Delta E)^2 \rangle \;=\; \langle E^2 \rangle - \langle E \rangle^2 \;=\; \frac{\partial^2 \ln Z}{\partial \beta^2} \;=\; k_B T^2 \, C_V,
$$

so energy fluctuations and the heat capacity are the *same object* — a particular case of the **fluctuation-dissipation theorem** [11.08.NN, pending].

### Classical ideal gas

The ideal-gas Hamiltonian is $H = \sum_{i=1}^N |\mathbf{p}_i|^2/(2m)$. The phase-space integral factorises over the $N$ particles, with each particle's position integral giving a factor of the box volume $V$ and each momentum integral giving a Gaussian factor $(2\pi m / \beta)^{3/2}$. Including the $1/N! h^{3N}$ prefactor:

$$
Z(\beta, V, N) \;=\; \frac{1}{N!} \left(\frac{V}{\lambda_T^3}\right)^{\!N}, \qquad \lambda_T \;:=\; \sqrt{\frac{h^2}{2\pi m k_B T}} \;=\; h\sqrt{\frac{\beta}{2\pi m}},
$$

where $\lambda_T$ is the **thermal de Broglie wavelength**. The Helmholtz free energy is

$$
F \;=\; -k_B T \ln Z \;=\; -N k_B T \left[ \ln\!\left(\frac{V}{N\lambda_T^3}\right) + 1\right],
$$

using Stirling's approximation $\ln N! \approx N \ln N - N$ valid for the macroscopic $N$. From $F$ one reads off the ideal-gas equation of state $P = -\partial F/\partial V = N k_B T / V$, the **Sackur–Tetrode entropy** $S = N k_B \left[\ln(V/(N\lambda_T^3)) + 5/2\right]$, and the internal energy $U = (3/2)N k_B T$ — equipartition for three translational degrees of freedom. The classical limit $\lambda_T^3 \ll V/N$ (de Broglie wavelength much less than interparticle spacing) is when quantum statistics become unnecessary; the opposite regime is where Bose-Einstein or Fermi-Dirac statistics kick in [11.05.NN, pending].

### Equipartition

For a Hamiltonian of the form $H = \sum_k \alpha_k x_k^2$ in some canonical variables $\{x_k\}$ (positions, momenta, normal-mode coordinates) and arbitrary positive coefficients $\alpha_k > 0$, each quadratic degree of freedom contributes $\tfrac{1}{2}k_B T$ to the mean energy at temperature $T$ — the **equipartition theorem**. The argument is a Gaussian integral: in the canonical ensemble, $\langle \alpha_k x_k^2 \rangle = -\partial \ln Z / \partial \beta$ restricted to the $x_k$ subsystem, and $\int e^{-\beta \alpha_k x_k^2} dx_k \propto \beta^{-1/2}$ so $\ln Z$ contributes $-\tfrac{1}{2}\ln \beta$ per quadratic mode, hence $\tfrac{1}{2}k_B T$ each. Equipartition is the high-temperature limit of every quadratic system; at low temperature it fails because energy levels are no longer densely packed compared to $k_B T$, and modes "freeze out" — the historical signature that classical stat mech needed quantum corrections, dramatised in the heat-capacity-of-solids problem (Einstein 1907; Debye 1912).

### Quantum harmonic oscillator

For a single oscillator with energy levels $E_n = \hbar\omega(n + 1/2)$, $n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$, the partition function is a geometric series:

$$
Z_{\mathrm{HO}}(\beta) \;=\; \sum_{n=0}^\infty e^{-\beta \hbar\omega(n + 1/2)} \;=\; \frac{e^{-\beta \hbar\omega/2}}{1 - e^{-\beta \hbar\omega}} \;=\; \frac{1}{2 \sinh(\beta \hbar\omega/2)}.
$$

Mean energy:

$$
\langle E \rangle \;=\; -\frac{\partial \ln Z_{\mathrm{HO}}}{\partial \beta} \;=\; \frac{\hbar\omega}{2} + \frac{\hbar\omega}{e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1} \;=\; \hbar\omega\left(\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1}\right).
$$

The classical limit $k_B T \gg \hbar\omega$ recovers $\langle E\rangle \to k_B T$ (equipartition, one $\tfrac{1}{2}k_B T$ from position-quadratic, one from momentum-quadratic). The quantum limit $k_B T \ll \hbar\omega$ gives $\langle E\rangle \to \hbar\omega/2$ — zero-point energy plus exponentially small thermal occupation. The crossover temperature is $k_B T \sim \hbar\omega$. Heat capacity $C_V = k_B (\beta\hbar\omega)^2 e^{\beta\hbar\omega}/(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1)^2$ has the same Einstein form (1907) and resolves the low-temperature failure of equipartition.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The energy levels in $\sum_i e^{-\beta E_i}$ are *microstate* energies, not energy *eigenvalues* with their degeneracies absorbed. A doubly-degenerate level at energy $E$ contributes two terms $e^{-\beta E}$, not one. Equivalent presentations write $Z = \sum_n g_n e^{-\beta E_n}$ where the sum is over distinct levels $E_n$ with degeneracies $g_n$. State which form you mean.

- The canonical $1/N!$ in the classical phase-space partition function is the **Gibbs correction** required to make $F$ extensive ($F \propto N$ at fixed $V/N$). Omitting it produces $F \propto N \ln N$ — the **Gibbs paradox**. Conceptually $1/N!$ is the quotient by the symmetric-group action on identical particles; physically it is the residue of quantum indistinguishability surviving into the classical limit.

- The free energy used here is the **Helmholtz** free energy $F(T, V, N)$, the natural potential for fixed $(T, V, N)$. For fixed $(T, P, N)$ the relevant potential is the **Gibbs** free energy $G = F + PV$ (a Legendre transform in $V \to P$). For fixed $(T, V, \mu)$ it is the **grand potential** $\Omega = F - \mu N$ (Legendre in $N \to \mu$, the grand-canonical partition function). The canonical $F$ and the partition function $Z$ together determine all of these.

- The Helmholtz free energy is *not* the same as the internal energy $U$; the difference $U - F = TS$ is the entropic contribution. At low $T$ they coincide; at high $T$ $|F|$ greatly exceeds $U$.

- Energy fluctuations $\sqrt{\langle (\Delta E)^2 \rangle} = T\sqrt{k_B C_V}$ scale like $\sqrt{N}$ for a macroscopic system, whereas $\langle E\rangle$ scales like $N$. The relative fluctuation is $1/\sqrt{N}$ — vanishing in the thermodynamic limit. This is why canonical and microcanonical predictions agree macroscopically even though they differ at the level of distributions.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Thermodynamic-quantity identities from the partition function).** *Let $Z(\beta, V, N) = \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i(V, N)}$ be the canonical partition function of a system whose energy spectrum depends smoothly on the macroscopic parameters $(V, N)$, with the sum convergent for all $\beta > 0$. Then for each $(\beta, V, N)$,*

$$
\langle E \rangle \;=\; -\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta}, \qquad \langle (\Delta E)^2 \rangle \;=\; \frac{\partial^2 \ln Z}{\partial \beta^2} \;=\; k_B T^2 \, C_V,
$$

*and, defining $F := -k_B T \ln Z$,*

$$
S \;=\; -\frac{\partial F}{\partial T} \;=\; k_B \ln Z + \frac{\langle E\rangle}{T}, \qquad P \;=\; -\frac{\partial F}{\partial V} \;=\; k_B T \, \frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial V}.
$$

**Proof.** All four identities are differentiation of the absolutely convergent series $Z(\beta) = \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i}$, justified term-by-term by dominated convergence.

For mean energy, differentiate $Z$ in $\beta$:
$$
\frac{\partial Z}{\partial \beta} \;=\; \sum_i (-E_i) e^{-\beta E_i} \;=\; -Z\,\langle E\rangle, \qquad \text{so} \quad \frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial \beta} \;=\; -\langle E\rangle.
$$

For energy variance, differentiate once more:
$$
\frac{\partial^2 \ln Z}{\partial \beta^2} \;=\; \frac{\partial}{\partial \beta}\!\left[\frac{1}{Z}\sum_i (-E_i) e^{-\beta E_i}\right] \;=\; \frac{1}{Z}\sum_i E_i^2 e^{-\beta E_i} - \frac{1}{Z^2}\left(\sum_i E_i e^{-\beta E_i}\right)^{\!2} \;=\; \langle E^2 \rangle - \langle E\rangle^2.
$$
Using $C_V = \partial\langle E\rangle/\partial T = -k_B \beta^2 \, \partial\langle E\rangle/\partial \beta = k_B \beta^2 \, \partial^2 \ln Z/\partial \beta^2$ identifies the variance with $k_B T^2 \, C_V$.

For entropy, start from the Shannon expression evaluated on the canonical distribution $P_i = e^{-\beta E_i}/Z$:
$$
S_{\mathrm{Sh}} \;=\; -k_B \sum_i P_i \ln P_i \;=\; -k_B \sum_i P_i (-\beta E_i - \ln Z) \;=\; k_B \beta \langle E\rangle + k_B \ln Z \;=\; \frac{\langle E\rangle}{T} + k_B \ln Z.
$$
The thermodynamic identity $F = U - TS$ then gives $F = \langle E\rangle - T S_{\mathrm{Sh}} = -k_B T \ln Z$, so $S_{\mathrm{Sh}}$ is the thermodynamic entropy and $-\partial F/\partial T = S$ follows directly from $F = -k_B T \ln Z$ and the chain rule.

For pressure, the energy levels $E_i(V)$ depend on the box volume (think particle-in-a-box: shrinking $V$ raises $E_i$). Differentiating in $V$ at fixed $\beta$:
$$
\frac{\partial \ln Z}{\partial V}\bigg|_\beta \;=\; \frac{1}{Z}\sum_i (-\beta)\frac{\partial E_i}{\partial V} e^{-\beta E_i} \;=\; -\beta \left\langle \frac{\partial E_i}{\partial V} \right\rangle,
$$
and the thermodynamic identification $P = -\langle \partial E_i / \partial V \rangle$ (the canonical-ensemble average of the mechanical force per area) gives $P = k_B T \, \partial \ln Z/\partial V$. $\square$

The four identities are the entire content of canonical-ensemble thermodynamics. Any specific physical system reduces to: compute $Z$, take logarithms, differentiate. The rest of statistical mechanics is bookkeeping for which $Z$ to write down.

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the partition function $Z(\beta, V, N)$ of an $N$-particle classical ideal gas in a box of volume $V$. From $Z$, derive (a) the equation of state, (b) the internal energy, (c) the Helmholtz free energy.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The phase-space integral factorises over the $N$ particles and over position vs momentum. Each momentum integral is a 3D Gaussian.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$Z(\beta, V, N) = (V/\lambda_T^3)^N / N!$ where $\lambda_T = h\sqrt{\beta/(2\pi m)}$ is the thermal de Broglie wavelength.

(a) $P = k_B T \, \partial \ln Z/\partial V = N k_B T / V$, the ideal-gas law.

(b) $\langle E\rangle = -\partial \ln Z/\partial \beta = -\partial/\partial \beta [N \ln V - 3N \ln \lambda_T - \ln N!] = -N \partial(-\tfrac{3}{2}\ln(2\pi m/\beta h^2))/\partial \beta = (3/2)N/\beta = (3/2)N k_B T$. Three quadratic momentum modes per particle, $\tfrac{1}{2}k_B T$ each — equipartition.

(c) $F = -k_B T \ln Z = -k_B T [N \ln(V/\lambda_T^3) - \ln N!] \approx -N k_B T [\ln(V/(N\lambda_T^3)) + 1]$ using Stirling. Extensivity of $F$ (proportionality to $N$ at fixed $V/N$) requires the $1/N!$ — the Gibbs correction.

</details>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

For the quantum harmonic oscillator with energy levels $E_n = \hbar\omega(n + 1/2)$, compute $Z(\beta)$ in closed form. From it, compute the mean energy and heat capacity at all temperatures.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$Z$ is a geometric series. Use $\sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n = 1/(1-x)$ with $x = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$Z(\beta) = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega/2} \sum_{n=0}^\infty e^{-n\beta\hbar\omega} = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega/2} / (1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}) = 1/[2\sinh(\beta\hbar\omega/2)]$.

$\langle E\rangle = -\partial \ln Z/\partial \beta = (\hbar\omega/2) \coth(\beta\hbar\omega/2) = \hbar\omega/2 + \hbar\omega/(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1)$.

$C_V = \partial \langle E\rangle/\partial T = k_B (\beta\hbar\omega)^2 \, e^{\beta\hbar\omega} / (e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1)^2$, the **Einstein heat capacity**.

Limits: $T \to \infty$ gives $\langle E\rangle \to k_B T$ (equipartition: two quadratic degrees of freedom in $p$ and $q$) and $C_V \to k_B$. $T \to 0$ gives $\langle E\rangle \to \hbar\omega/2$ (zero-point energy) and $C_V \to k_B (\beta\hbar\omega)^2 e^{-\beta\hbar\omega} \to 0$ exponentially fast — modes freeze out.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Using the result of Exercise 4, show that the heat capacity of $N$ independent identical harmonic oscillators (e.g., Einstein's model of a crystal lattice) deviates from the classical Dulong-Petit value $C_V = 3 N k_B$ at low temperature, and identify the temperature scale at which the deviation becomes significant.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$C_V$ from Exercise 4 multiplied by $3N$. The deviation from $k_B$ per oscillator is significant when $\beta\hbar\omega$ is order unity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A 3D crystal lattice in the Einstein model has $3N$ oscillators (3 polarisations per site for $N$ sites). $C_V^{\mathrm{Einstein}} = 3 N k_B \, (\beta\hbar\omega)^2 e^{\beta\hbar\omega}/(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1)^2$. At high $T$: $C_V \to 3N k_B$, the Dulong-Petit law (one $k_B$ per oscillator from equipartition). At low $T$: $C_V \to 3 N k_B (\hbar\omega/k_B T)^2 e^{-\hbar\omega/k_B T}$ — exponential suppression. The crossover is at $k_B T \sim \hbar\omega$, defining the **Einstein temperature** $\Theta_E = \hbar\omega/k_B$. Real crystals follow $C_V \propto T^3$ at low $T$ (Debye) rather than exponential; the difference is that real lattice vibrations are *phonons* with a $\omega$-dependent density of states, not a single oscillator frequency.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that energy fluctuations in the canonical ensemble are $\langle (\Delta E)^2\rangle = k_B T^2 C_V$, and explain why this implies that the canonical and microcanonical ensembles give the same predictions for extensive thermodynamic quantities in the thermodynamic limit $N \to \infty$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate $\langle E\rangle = -\partial \ln Z/\partial \beta$ once more in $\beta$. Compare the size of $\sqrt{\langle (\Delta E)^2\rangle}$ to $\langle E\rangle$ as $N \to \infty$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\partial \langle E\rangle/\partial \beta = -\partial^2 \ln Z/\partial \beta^2 = -(\langle E^2\rangle - \langle E\rangle^2)$. Converting $\partial/\partial \beta = -k_B T^2 \, \partial/\partial T$ gives $\partial \langle E\rangle/\partial \beta = -k_B T^2 \, C_V$, so $\langle (\Delta E)^2\rangle = k_B T^2 \, C_V$.

For an extensive system, $\langle E\rangle \sim N$ and $C_V \sim N$ (both extensive), so $\sqrt{\langle (\Delta E)^2\rangle} \sim \sqrt{N}$. The relative fluctuation $\sqrt{\langle (\Delta E)^2\rangle}/\langle E\rangle \sim 1/\sqrt{N} \to 0$. In the canonical ensemble the energy is sharply peaked around $\langle E\rangle$, and the canonical distribution is concentrated on the microcanonical energy shell at $E = \langle E\rangle$ in the limit. The two ensembles thus produce the same intensive predictions (per-particle quantities, equation-of-state ratios), differing only in subleading $1/\sqrt{N}$ corrections to extensive quantities. This is **ensemble equivalence**.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

A classical particle in one dimension has Hamiltonian $H(q, p) = p^2/(2m) + (1/4)\lambda q^4$ with $\lambda > 0$ — a quartic anharmonic oscillator. Compute the canonical partition function $Z(\beta)$ as a one-dimensional integral, and show that $\langle E\rangle = (1/4)k_B T \cdot (\text{kinetic}) + (1/4)k_B T \cdot (\text{quartic-potential})$ — the **virial theorem** for power-law potentials. (Note: a quartic-position degree of freedom contributes $\tfrac{1}{4}k_B T$, not $\tfrac{1}{2}k_B T$.)

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The momentum integral is Gaussian. The position integral $\int e^{-\beta \lambda q^4/4} dq$ scales like $\beta^{-1/4}$, so $\ln Z$ contributes $-(1/4)\ln \beta$ from this mode.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$Z(\beta) = (1/h) \int e^{-\beta p^2/(2m)} dp \cdot \int e^{-\beta \lambda q^4/4} dq$. The $p$ integral is $\sqrt{2\pi m/\beta}$. The $q$ integral: substitute $u = (\beta \lambda/4)^{1/4} q$, so $du = (\beta \lambda/4)^{1/4} dq$, giving $\int e^{-u^4} du \cdot (\beta \lambda/4)^{-1/4} = \Gamma(1/4) \cdot 2^{-1/2} \cdot (\beta\lambda/4)^{-1/4} \cdot 4^{1/4}$. Collecting, $\ln Z = -\tfrac{1}{2}\ln \beta - \tfrac{1}{4}\ln \beta + \text{const}$, so $\langle E\rangle = -\partial \ln Z/\partial \beta = (1/2 + 1/4)/\beta = (3/4) k_B T$.

The contributions split: $\langle p^2/(2m)\rangle = (1/2) k_B T$ (Gaussian-quadratic equipartition) and $\langle \lambda q^4/4\rangle = (1/4) k_B T$ from the quartic degree of freedom. The general rule: a degree of freedom with Hamiltonian $\sim |x|^n$ contributes $k_B T/n$ to the mean energy, recovering the standard $\tfrac{1}{2}k_B T$ for $n = 2$ and giving $\tfrac{1}{4}k_B T$ for $n = 4$. This is the **generalised equipartition theorem** for power-law potentials.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the canonical distribution $P_i = e^{-\beta E_i}/Z$ is the unique probability distribution on a finite microstate space that maximises the Shannon entropy $S_{\mathrm{Sh}} = -k_B \sum_i P_i \ln P_i$ subject to $\sum_i P_i = 1$ and $\sum_i P_i E_i = U$ for a prescribed $U$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use Lagrange multipliers for the two constraints. Strict concavity of $-x \ln x$ gives uniqueness.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Introduce multipliers $\alpha$ for normalisation and $\beta$ for the energy constraint, and extremise $\mathcal{L} = -k_B \sum_i P_i \ln P_i - \alpha(\sum_i P_i - 1) - \beta(\sum_i P_i E_i - U)$. Setting $\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial P_i = 0$ gives $-k_B(\ln P_i + 1) - \alpha - \beta E_i = 0$, so $P_i = \exp(-1 - \alpha/k_B - \beta E_i/k_B)$. Absorbing the constants into a normaliser identifies $P_i = e^{-\beta' E_i}/Z(\beta')$ with $\beta' = \beta/k_B$ (we then call $\beta'$ the inverse temperature, i.e., what we have been calling $\beta$ throughout). The multiplier $\beta$ is fixed implicitly by $-\partial \ln Z(\beta)/\partial \beta = U$.

Uniqueness: $-x \ln x$ is strictly concave on $(0, 1)$, so $S_{\mathrm{Sh}}$ is a strictly concave function of $P$ on the simplex; the constraints are affine; a strictly concave function on a convex set has at most one maximum.

The result is the **Jaynes maximum-entropy principle** (Jaynes 1957): the canonical ensemble is the least biased probability distribution given knowledge of $U$ alone, in the precise information-theoretic sense that any other distribution with the same $U$ would smuggle in additional assumptions beyond the energy constraint.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

Consider two independent canonical systems $A$ and $B$ at the same temperature, with partition functions $Z_A(\beta)$ and $Z_B(\beta)$. Show that the partition function of the combined system $A \cup B$ is $Z_{A \cup B} = Z_A \cdot Z_B$, and hence that the Helmholtz free energies are additive: $F_{A \cup B} = F_A + F_B$. Explain why this multiplicative property is the structural reason $F$ is extensive.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A microstate of the combined system is a pair $(i, j)$ with $E_{(i,j)} = E^A_i + E^B_j$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A combined microstate is a pair $(i, j)$ where $i$ runs over $A$ and $j$ over $B$; the energy of $(i, j)$ is the sum $E^A_i + E^B_j$ because $A$ and $B$ are independent (non-interacting). Then

$$
Z_{A\cup B}(\beta) \;=\; \sum_{i, j} e^{-\beta(E^A_i + E^B_j)} \;=\; \left(\sum_i e^{-\beta E^A_i}\right)\!\left(\sum_j e^{-\beta E^B_j}\right) \;=\; Z_A(\beta) \cdot Z_B(\beta).
$$

Taking logarithms: $\ln Z_{A\cup B} = \ln Z_A + \ln Z_B$, and multiplying by $-k_B T$: $F_{A\cup B} = F_A + F_B$. So $F$ is additive over independent subsystems — this is what *extensive* means thermodynamically.

If $A$ and $B$ are identical copies of the same system, $F_{A \cup B} = 2 F_A$ — twice the free energy. Repeating with $N$ identical copies gives $F = N f$ for some per-system free energy $f$. The structural reason $F$ is extensive is that $Z$ is multiplicative over tensor-product systems, and $\ln$ converts product into sum. Interactions between $A$ and $B$ break the multiplicativity at order $\beta U_{AB}$, but for short-range interactions the corrections are surface terms ($\sim V^{2/3}$) compared to bulk extensive terms ($\sim V$), and $F$ remains extensive in the thermodynamic limit.

</details>

</aside>

## Phase-space and quantum formulations [Master]

The discrete-spectrum partition function $Z = \sum_i e^{-\beta E_i}$ is a special case. The two general formulations on which all of statistical mechanics rests are the **classical phase-space partition function**

$$
Z_{\mathrm{cl}}(\beta, V, N) \;=\; \frac{1}{N!\, h^{3N}} \int_{\Gamma_N} e^{-\beta H(q, p)} \, d^{3N}q \, d^{3N}p,
$$

where $\Gamma_N$ is the $N$-particle phase space (a $6N$-dimensional symplectic manifold, the $N$-fold product of single-particle phase spaces — see [09.04.02] for the symplectic structure on $T^*Q$), and the **quantum canonical partition function**

$$
Z_{\mathrm{qm}}(\beta) \;=\; \mathrm{Tr}\!\left(e^{-\beta \hat H}\right),
$$

for a Hamiltonian operator $\hat H$ bounded below on a separable Hilbert space, with the trace taken over an orthonormal basis. When $\hat H$ has discrete spectrum $\{E_n\}$ this reduces to $\sum_n e^{-\beta E_n}$; when the spectrum has a continuous part the trace is a spectral integral against the spectral measure of $\hat H$.

The factor $h^{3N}$ in $Z_{\mathrm{cl}}$ makes the measure dimensionless and is fixed by demanding agreement with $Z_{\mathrm{qm}}$ in the classical limit — the **Wigner correspondence**. The $1/N!$ is the **Gibbs correction** for identical particles: classical phase space treats particle labels as physical, while quantum mechanics treats identical particles as indistinguishable; the residue surviving the classical limit is the quotient by the symmetric group $S_N$, equivalent to dividing the partition function by $N!$. The rigorous measure-theoretic version of $Z_{\mathrm{cl}}$ on a general symplectic manifold is treated in [08.01.01] using the Liouville volume form $\omega^n/n!$ as the canonical measure on phase space; the Gibbs $1/N!$ is the quotient measure on $\Gamma_N / S_N$.

### Saddle-point derivation from the microcanonical ensemble

The microcanonical ensemble assigns equal probability to every microstate on the energy shell $\{E_i = E\}$. Let $\Omega(E)$ denote the number of microstates with energy $E$ (or, in the continuous case, the density of states). The microcanonical entropy is $S(E) = k_B \ln \Omega(E)$. The canonical partition function can be rewritten as an integral over the energy shell against the microcanonical density:

$$
Z(\beta) \;=\; \int dE \, \Omega(E) \, e^{-\beta E} \;=\; \int dE \, e^{[S(E)/k_B] - \beta E}.
$$

In the thermodynamic limit $N \to \infty$, both $S(E)$ and $E$ scale extensively as $N$, so the exponent $N \cdot [s(\epsilon)/k_B - \beta \epsilon]$ with intensive $s = S/N$ and $\epsilon = E/N$ becomes large. **Laplace's method** [02.05.05] evaluates the integral by its saddle point — the value $\epsilon^* = \epsilon^*(\beta)$ at which the exponent is stationary,

$$
\frac{1}{k_B} \frac{\partial s}{\partial \epsilon} \;=\; \beta, \qquad \text{equivalently} \qquad \frac{\partial S}{\partial E} \;=\; \frac{1}{T}.
$$

Around the saddle, expand to quadratic order; the result is

$$
Z(\beta) \;\sim\; e^{N[s(\epsilon^*)/k_B - \beta \epsilon^*]} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{N |s''(\epsilon^*)/k_B|}} \cdot (1 + O(1/N)).
$$

Taking the log and dividing by $N$: the **per-particle free energy** is $f(\beta) = -\beta^{-1}(s/k_B - \beta \epsilon)\big|_{\epsilon = \epsilon^*} = \epsilon^* - T s(\epsilon^*) = u - Ts$, the standard Legendre transform of the microcanonical entropy. This is the **Darwin-Fowler saddle-point derivation** of the canonical distribution (1922); the modern formulation is in terms of large deviations and Legendre duality. The fluctuation prefactor recovers the $\langle (\Delta E)^2\rangle = k_B T^2 C_V$ result of the Intermediate tier, and the $O(1/N)$ correction is the systematic difference between canonical and microcanonical expectations — vanishing in the thermodynamic limit. The macroscopic equivalence of the two ensembles is the statement that Legendre transforms of extensive thermodynamic potentials commute with the thermodynamic limit, an instance of a general principle in large-deviation theory.

### Grand-canonical ensemble and the Laplace-transform connection

If particle number is also exchanged with the reservoir, fix instead the chemical potential $\mu$ and sum over both microstates and particle numbers:

$$
\Xi(\beta, V, \mu) \;:=\; \sum_{N=0}^\infty e^{\beta \mu N} \, Z(\beta, V, N) \;=\; \sum_{N=0}^\infty \sum_{i \in N\text{-states}} e^{-\beta(E_i - \mu N)}.
$$

$\Xi$ is the **grand-canonical partition function**, related to $Z$ by a discrete Laplace transform in $N$. The grand potential is $\Omega = -k_B T \ln \Xi$; analogues of all canonical identities hold with $\mu \leftrightarrow N$ replacing $\beta \leftrightarrow E$. For systems with fluctuating $N$ — bosons, fermions, open systems — the grand-canonical ensemble is the natural starting point, and the canonical $Z$ at fixed $N$ is recovered by inverse Laplace transform in $\mu$ — a saddle-point evaluation gives the saddle $\mu^* = \mu^*(N)$ identifying $\mu$ as the standard chemical potential. The three ensembles — microcanonical, canonical, grand-canonical — sit in a Legendre-transform chain: $S(E, V, N) \leftrightarrow -F(T, V, N)/T \leftrightarrow -\Omega(T, V, \mu)/T$, with each transform fixing a different extensive variable in exchange for its conjugate intensive parameter.

### Mayer cluster expansion

For a classical gas with two-body interactions $U(r_{ij})$, write $H = \sum_i p_i^2/(2m) + \sum_{i < j} U(r_{ij})$ and decompose $e^{-\beta U_{ij}} = 1 + f_{ij}$ with $f_{ij} := e^{-\beta U_{ij}} - 1$ the **Mayer $f$-function**. Expanding the product $\prod_{i<j}(1 + f_{ij})$ generates a sum over graphs on the $N$ vertices, with each graph contributing an integral over its connected components. Re-summing yields the **cluster expansion**

$$
\ln \Xi \;=\; \sum_{l \geq 1} b_l(\beta) \, V \, z^l,
$$

where $z := e^{\beta \mu}/\lambda_T^3$ is the **fugacity** and $b_l$ are cluster integrals over $l$-particle connected diagrams. The expansion is the systematic small-$z$ (dilute-gas) corrections to ideal-gas behaviour, generating the **virial expansion** $P/(k_B T) = n + B_2(T) n^2 + B_3(T) n^3 + \cdots$ with virial coefficients $B_l$ explicit integrals over cluster diagrams. Mayer's expansion is the classical-gas analogue of perturbation theory in QFT — the same diagrammatic structure with Feynman rules replaced by cluster rules.

### Functional integral at finite temperature

For a quantum system with Hamiltonian $\hat H$ on the Hilbert space $L^2(\mathbb{R})$ (one particle for simplicity), the **Feynman-Kac formula** evaluates the trace by a path integral over loops of imaginary-time period $\beta\hbar$:

$$
Z_{\mathrm{qm}}(\beta) \;=\; \mathrm{Tr}(e^{-\beta \hat H}) \;=\; \int \mathcal{D}q(\tau) \, e^{-S_E[q]/\hbar},
$$

where the integral is over closed paths $q(0) = q(\beta\hbar)$ and the **Euclidean action** is $S_E[q] = \int_0^{\beta\hbar} d\tau \left[\tfrac{1}{2}m \dot q^2 + V(q)\right]$ with $\dot q := dq/d\tau$. This is the **finite-temperature path integral**: the quantum statistical-mechanics analogue of the Feynman path integral $\int e^{iS/\hbar}$, related to the real-time formulation by **Wick rotation** $t \to -i\tau$. The math-side treatment of the Euclidean path integral with rigorous measures (Wiener measure, Symanzik regularisation) lives in [08.07.01], and the quantum-classical correspondence by Wick rotation in [08.09.01]; at zero temperature ($\beta \to \infty$) this reduces to the ground-state vacuum expectation value. For field theories the same construction with $q \to \phi(x, \tau)$ gives Euclidean QFT at finite temperature, the apparatus underlying thermal QFT, finite-temperature gauge theory, and the cosmological derivations of the cosmic-microwave-background spectrum.

### Renormalization-group structure

The partition function as a function of the couplings $\{K_a\}$ in the Hamiltonian is the **generating functional** of statistical mechanics. Coarse-graining — the integration of short-wavelength modes — defines a flow on the space of couplings: $\{K_a\} \mapsto \{K_a'\}$ such that the partition function on the coarse-grained lattice equals that on the fine lattice, up to a regular function of the couplings. Fixed points of this flow are scale-invariant theories; their stability properties classify the universality classes of continuous phase transitions, and the eigenvalues of the linearised flow at a fixed point give the critical exponents. The full apparatus is developed on the math side in [08.04.01]–[08.04.04] and on the physics side in [11.07.NN, pending]; the canonical ensemble is the underlying object on which all of this acts. The deep structural fact is that the partition function and its derivatives are the universal observables that the RG flow preserves up to scheme dependence — every other quantity (correlation functions, susceptibilities, response functions) is computable from $Z$ as a derivative or correlator.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The canonical ensemble is Gibbs's invention. His *Elementary Principles in Statistical Mechanics* of 1902 [ref: TODO_REF Gibbs 1902] introduced the term *canonical distribution* (with the modular constant $\Theta = k_B T$) and developed its consequences as a self-contained programme — independent of Boltzmann's gas-kinetic motivations and aimed at a general thermodynamic formalism for arbitrary mechanical systems. Boltzmann's 1877 papers on the relation $S = k_B \ln W$ [ref: TODO_REF Boltzmann 1877] had supplied the entropic content, but it was Gibbs who packaged the construction into the ensemble framework still used today. The 1922 paper of Darwin and Fowler [ref: TODO_REF Darwin-Fowler 1922] gave the saddle-point derivation of the canonical from the microcanonical, putting the equivalence of ensembles on the analytic footing that the modern large-deviation reformulation (Lanford 1973, Ellis 1985) made precise.

The information-theoretic re-derivation is due to Jaynes (1957) [ref: TODO_REF Jaynes 1957]. The argument inverts the historical sequence: instead of constructing the canonical ensemble from physical reservoir-contact reasoning, Jaynes derives it as the unique maximum-entropy distribution consistent with knowledge of $\langle E\rangle$ — and identifies the multiplier $\beta$ with $1/(k_B T)$ post hoc by matching to thermodynamics. The maximum-entropy principle is one of the long-standing conceptual reformulations of statistical mechanics; its critics (Sklar, Earman) note that "least biased given $\langle E\rangle$" is a subjective epistemic principle that requires further argument to license objective physical claims. The maximum-entropy programme nevertheless extends, in its more applied incarnations, to image reconstruction, neural-network energy models, and the Bayesian inference of physical parameters.

The path-integral formulation at finite temperature is due to Feynman (1953, 1972) [ref: TODO_REF Feynman 1972], with the rigorous probabilistic version owed to Wiener's earlier construction of Brownian motion (1923) and to Kac's identification of $\mathrm{Tr}(e^{-\beta \hat H})$ with the Wiener integral of $e^{-\int V dt}$ (Feynman-Kac, 1949). The two are joined in the Euclidean formulation of QFT (Symanzik 1969; Osterwalder-Schrader 1973), which underwrites the modern computation of finite-temperature observables in particle physics, condensed matter, and cosmology.
