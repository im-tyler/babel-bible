## Intuition [Beginner]

A quantum harmonic oscillator is a spring that obeys the rules of quantum mechanics. Unlike a classical spring, which can have any energy, a quantum spring can only occupy discrete energy levels:

$$E_n = \left(n + \frac{1}{2}\right)\hbar\omega, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

where $\omega$ is the oscillation frequency. The spacing between adjacent levels is always $\hbar\omega$ -- a fixed quantum of energy.

At zero temperature, the oscillator sits in its ground state $n = 0$ with energy $\hbar\omega/2$. This is the **zero-point energy**: even at absolute zero, the quantum spring is never completely still. Heisenberg's uncertainty principle forbids a particle from having both a definite position and zero momentum simultaneously.

At temperature $T$, the oscillator samples different energy levels. The probability of being found in level $n$ is proportional to $e^{-E_n/kT}$. Higher energy levels become more likely as $T$ increases -- the spring vibrates more vigorously. The average energy grows with temperature, but it does so differently than a classical spring would.

**Einstein's key idea (1907).** A crystal is a regular array of atoms, each vibrating around its equilibrium position. Einstein modelled each atom as three independent quantum springs (one for each direction: $x$, $y$, $z$), all with the same frequency $\omega_E$. A crystal of $N$ atoms becomes $3N$ independent quantum harmonic oscillators -- an **Einstein solid**.

This model explains one of the great puzzles of 19th-century physics. The classical theory of solids (Dulong and Petit, 1819) predicted that every solid should have a molar heat capacity $C_V = 3R \approx 25$ J/(mol K), regardless of temperature. Experiment agreed at high temperature but showed that $C_V$ drops toward zero at low temperature. No classical theory could explain this.

Einstein's quantum model resolves the puzzle. When $kT$ is large compared to the energy quantum $\hbar\omega_E$, the oscillator behaves classically and contributes $k$ to the heat capacity -- recovering the Dulong-Petit law. When $kT$ is small compared to $\hbar\omega_E$, the oscillator "freezes out": it cannot absorb fractional quanta, so adding a small amount of heat goes toward exciting only the few oscillators near the bottom of their energy ladder. The heat capacity drops exponentially.

The characteristic temperature scale is the **Einstein temperature** $\Theta_E = \hbar\omega_E/k$. Above $\Theta_E$, the solid behaves classically; below $\Theta_E$, quantum effects dominate and $C_V$ falls.

The Einstein model predicts that $C_V$ drops exponentially at low $T$, but experiments show $C_V \propto T^3$. The reason is that Einstein assumed all oscillators have the same frequency, ignoring the collective vibrational modes (phonons) of the crystal lattice. Debye (1912) included a spectrum of frequencies and recovered the correct $T^3$ law. Both models share the same core physical insight: quantum mechanics suppresses the heat capacity of solids at low temperature.

## Visual [Beginner]

The energy levels of a quantum harmonic oscillator form a ladder with equally spaced rungs, separated by $\hbar\omega$. At low temperature ($kT \ll \hbar\omega$), the Boltzmann weights $e^{-n\hbar\omega/kT}$ decay rapidly with $n$, and the oscillator almost always sits in the ground state. At high temperature ($kT \gg \hbar\omega$), many levels are populated with comparable probability, and the discrete ladder looks like a continuous ramp -- the classical limit.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The Einstein temperature of diamond is $\Theta_E \approx 1320$ K (diamond has unusually stiff bonds). At room temperature $T = 300$ K, compute the ratio $T/\Theta_E$ and determine whether diamond behaves classically at room temperature.

Step 1: Compute $T/\Theta_E = 300/1320 = 0.23$.

Step 2: Since $T/\Theta_E \ll 1$, we are deep in the quantum regime. The dimensionless ratio $\hbar\omega_E/kT = \Theta_E/T = 4.4$.

Step 3: The heat capacity per oscillator is approximately $C_{V,1} \approx k(\Theta_E/T)^2 e^{-\Theta_E/T} = k(4.4)^2 e^{-4.4} = 19.4 \times 0.0123 = 0.24\,k$.

Step 4: The molar heat capacity is $C_V = 3N_A C_{V,1} = 3R \times 0.24 = 6.0$ J/(mol K). This is well below the Dulong-Petit value of $3R = 24.9$ J/(mol K). Diamond at room temperature is not classical -- its bonds are too stiff and the temperature is too low to fully excite the vibrational modes. This is why diamond has an unusually low heat capacity at room temperature.

For comparison, copper has $\Theta_E \approx 240$ K. At room temperature ($T = 300$ K), $T/\Theta_E = 1.25$, and copper's heat capacity is close to the classical $3R$, consistent with everyday experience.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The partition function of a single quantum harmonic oscillator with frequency $\omega$ at inverse temperature $\beta = 1/kT$ is

$$Z_1 = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} e^{-\beta E_n} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} e^{-\beta\hbar\omega(n + 1/2)} = \frac{e^{-\beta\hbar\omega/2}}{1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}}.$$

The geometric series converges because $e^{-\beta\hbar\omega} < 1$ for all finite temperatures. The average energy is

$$\langle E \rangle = -\frac{\partial \ln Z_1}{\partial \beta} = \hbar\omega\left(\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1}\right).$$

The term $\hbar\omega/2$ is the temperature-independent zero-point energy. The second term, $\hbar\omega/(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1)$, is the **thermal energy** -- the part that changes with temperature. The function $n(\omega, T) = 1/(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1)$ is the **Bose-Einstein occupation number**: the average number of quanta in a mode of frequency $\omega$ at temperature $T$.

The heat capacity of a single oscillator is

$$C_{V,1} = \frac{\partial \langle E \rangle}{\partial T} = k\left(\frac{\hbar\omega}{kT}\right)^2 \frac{e^{\hbar\omega/kT}}{(e^{\hbar\omega/kT} - 1)^2}.$$

Defining the dimensionless variable $x = \hbar\omega/kT = \Theta_E/T$:

$$\boxed{C_{V,1} = k\,x^2\,\frac{e^x}{(e^x - 1)^2}.}$$

### The Einstein solid

A crystal of $N$ atoms is modelled as $3N$ independent quantum harmonic oscillators, all with the same frequency $\omega_E$. The total partition function factorises:

$$Z = Z_1^{3N} = \left(\frac{e^{-\beta\hbar\omega_E/2}}{1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega_E}}\right)^{3N}.$$

The total energy and heat capacity are:

$$U = 3N\langle E \rangle, \quad C_V = 3N\,C_{V,1} = 3Nk\,x^2\,\frac{e^x}{(e^x - 1)^2},$$

where $x = \Theta_E/T$ and $\Theta_E = \hbar\omega_E/k$ is the **Einstein temperature**.

### High-temperature limit ($kT \gg \hbar\omega$, or $x \ll 1$)

For $x \ll 1$: $e^x \approx 1 + x + x^2/2$, so $e^x/(e^x - 1)^2 \approx 1/x^2$, giving $C_{V,1} \to k$. The total heat capacity is $C_V = 3Nk = 3R$ per mole -- the **Dulong-Petit law**. This is the classical result, where each quadratic degree of freedom contributes $kT/2$ to the energy and $k/2$ to the heat capacity, and each oscillator has two such degrees of freedom (kinetic and potential), giving $k$ per oscillator.

### Low-temperature limit ($kT \ll \hbar\omega$, or $x \gg 1$)

For $x \gg 1$: $e^x \gg 1$, so $C_{V,1} \approx kx^2 e^{-x}$. The heat capacity drops exponentially -- the **quantum freeze-out**. Physically, the energy quantum $\hbar\omega$ is too large for the thermal bath to excite, so the oscillator remains near its ground state and cannot absorb heat.

### Comparison with Debye model

The Einstein model assumes a single frequency $\omega_E$. Real crystals have collective vibrational modes (phonons) with frequencies ranging from near zero up to a maximum $\omega_D$. The **Debye model** replaces the single frequency with a density of states proportional to $\omega^2$ up to a cutoff $\omega_D$, giving a Debye temperature $\Theta_D = \hbar\omega_D/k$.

The Debye model predicts $C_V \propto T^3$ at low temperatures (instead of the Einstein exponential), in excellent agreement with experiment. Both models converge to $C_V = 3Nk$ at high temperature.

## Key derivation: The quantum harmonic oscillator partition function [Intermediate+]

**Proposition.** *The partition function of a quantum harmonic oscillator with frequency $\omega$ at inverse temperature $\beta$ is $Z_1 = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega/2}/(1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega})$.*

**Proof.** The energy eigenvalues are $E_n = (n + 1/2)\hbar\omega$ for $n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ The partition function is:

$$Z_1 = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} e^{-\beta\hbar\omega(n + 1/2)} = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega/2} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left(e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}\right)^n.$$

This is a geometric series with ratio $r = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega} < 1$ (for $T > 0$):

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} r^n = \frac{1}{1 - r} = \frac{1}{1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}}.$$

Therefore $Z_1 = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega/2}/(1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega})$. $\square$

**Corollary (average energy).** *The average energy is $\langle E \rangle = \hbar\omega(1/2 + n_B)$ where $n_B = 1/(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1)$ is the Bose-Einstein occupation number.*

**Proof.** $\ln Z_1 = -\beta\hbar\omega/2 - \ln(1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega})$.

$$\langle E \rangle = -\frac{\partial \ln Z_1}{\partial \beta} = \frac{\hbar\omega}{2} + \frac{\hbar\omega\,e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}}{1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}} = \frac{\hbar\omega}{2} + \frac{\hbar\omega}{e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1}.$$

The second term is $\hbar\omega \cdot n_B$ where $n_B = 1/(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1)$. $\square$

**Corollary (heat capacity).** *The heat capacity of a single oscillator is $C_{V,1} = k\,x^2\,e^x/(e^x - 1)^2$ where $x = \hbar\omega/kT$.*

**Proof.** Starting from $\langle E \rangle = \hbar\omega[1/2 + 1/(e^x - 1)]$ and differentiating with respect to $T$ (using $dx/dT = -x/T$):

$$C_{V,1} = \frac{d\langle E \rangle}{dT} = \hbar\omega \cdot \frac{e^x}{(e^x - 1)^2} \cdot \frac{x}{T} = k\,x^2\,\frac{e^x}{(e^x - 1)^2}. \quad\square$$

**Bridge.** The harmonic oscillator partition function is the single-mode building block of quantum statistical mechanics. The Bose-Einstein occupation number $n_B = 1/(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1)$ counts the average number of quanta (phonons, photons) in a mode at frequency $\omega$. This same function appears in Planck's blackbody radiation law [11.04.02], in the Debye model of specific heat, and in the theory of Bose-Einstein condensation [11.05.01]. The harmonic oscillator is the universal system of statistical mechanics because every potential near its minimum looks quadratic -- and quadratic potentials give harmonic oscillators.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

The Einstein temperature of lead is $\Theta_E \approx 70$ K. Compute the heat capacity per mole of lead at $T = 300$ K, expressing your answer as a fraction of the Dulong-Petit value $3R$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $x = \Theta_E/T = 70/300 = 0.233$. Use $C_V/(3R) = x^2 e^x/(e^x - 1)^2$. For small $x$, the result should be close to 1.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$x = 70/300 = 0.233$.

$e^x = e^{0.233} = 1.262$.

$C_V/(3R) = (0.233)^2 \times 1.262 / (0.262)^2 = 0.0543 \times 1.262 / 0.0686 = 0.0685/0.0686 = 0.998$.

So $C_V \approx 3R \times 0.998 = 24.9$ J/(mol K). Lead at room temperature is essentially classical -- its Einstein temperature is so low that quantum freeze-out is negligible at 300 K.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the high-temperature limit ($x \ll 1$) of the heat capacity $C_{V,1} = kx^2 e^x/(e^x - 1)^2$ gives $C_{V,1} = k$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $e^x \approx 1 + x + x^2/2$ and simplify. Alternatively, expand the denominator using $e^x - 1 \approx x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $x \ll 1$: $e^x \approx 1 + x$ (keeping only the leading correction). Then $e^x - 1 \approx x$.

$C_{V,1} = k\,x^2 \cdot \frac{1 + x}{x^2} = k(1 + x) \to k$ as $x \to 0$.

More precisely, expanding to next order: $e^x = 1 + x + x^2/2$, so $e^x - 1 = x + x^2/2 = x(1 + x/2)$, giving $(e^x - 1)^2 = x^2(1 + x + x^2/4)$.

$C_{V,1} = kx^2 \cdot (1 + x + x^2/2)/(x^2(1 + x + x^2/4)) = k(1 + x + x^2/2)/(1 + x + x^2/4) = k(1 + x^2/12 + \cdots)$.

The leading correction to $C_{V,1} = k$ is $kx^2/12$, which is negligible when $kT \gg \hbar\omega$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the average energy of a single quantum harmonic oscillator from $Z_1$ in two ways: (a) using $\langle E \rangle = -\partial \ln Z_1/\partial \beta$, and (b) using $\langle E \rangle = (1/Z_1) \sum_n E_n e^{-\beta E_n}$. Verify that both give the same result.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For (b), write $E_n e^{-\beta E_n} = (n + 1/2)\hbar\omega \cdot e^{-\beta\hbar\omega(n+1/2)}$ and use the identity $\sum_n n r^n = r/(1-r)^2$ for $|r| < 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

(a) From $Z_1 = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega/2}/(1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega})$:

$\ln Z_1 = -\beta\hbar\omega/2 - \ln(1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega})$.

$\langle E \rangle = -\partial\ln Z_1/\partial\beta = \hbar\omega/2 + \hbar\omega e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}/(1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}) = \hbar\omega/2 + \hbar\omega/(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1)$.

(b) $\langle E \rangle = (1/Z_1)\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(n + 1/2)\hbar\omega\, e^{-\beta\hbar\omega(n+1/2)}$.

Factor out $\hbar\omega$: $\langle E \rangle = \hbar\omega(1/Z_1) e^{-\beta\hbar\omega/2} \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(n + 1/2) r^n$ where $r = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega}$.

$\sum_n (n + 1/2) r^n = \sum_n n r^n + (1/2)\sum_n r^n = r/(1-r)^2 + (1/2)/(1-r)$.

Using $Z_1 = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega/2}/(1-r)$:

$\langle E \rangle = \hbar\omega (1-r)\left[\frac{r}{(1-r)^2} + \frac{1}{2(1-r)}\right] = \hbar\omega\left[\frac{r}{1-r} + \frac{1}{2}\right] = \hbar\omega\left[\frac{1}{e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1} + \frac{1}{2}\right]$.

Both methods give $\langle E \rangle = \hbar\omega(1/2 + n_B)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the entropy of a single quantum harmonic oscillator from $F = -kT\ln Z_1$ and $S = -(\partial F/\partial T)_V$. Show that $S \to 0$ as $T \to 0$, consistent with the third law.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$F = \hbar\omega/2 + kT\ln(1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega})$. Then $S = -\partial F/\partial T$. As $T \to 0$, the logarithm diverges but its temperature derivative vanishes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$F = -kT\ln Z_1 = \hbar\omega/2 + kT\ln(1 - e^{-\hbar\omega/kT})$.

$S = -\frac{\partial F}{\partial T} = -k\ln(1 - e^{-\hbar\omega/kT}) - kT \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial T}\ln(1 - e^{-\hbar\omega/kT})$.

$\frac{\partial}{\partial T}\ln(1 - e^{-\hbar\omega/kT}) = \frac{(\hbar\omega/kT^2)e^{-\hbar\omega/kT}}{1 - e^{-\hbar\omega/kT}} = \frac{\hbar\omega}{kT^2} \cdot \frac{1}{e^{\hbar\omega/kT} - 1}$.

So $S = -k\ln(1 - e^{-\hbar\omega/kT}) + \frac{\hbar\omega}{T} \cdot \frac{1}{e^{\hbar\omega/kT} - 1}$.

As $T \to 0$: $e^{\hbar\omega/kT} \to \infty$, so $e^{-\hbar\omega/kT} \to 0$. Then $\ln(1 - e^{-\hbar\omega/kT}) \to \ln 1 = 0$ and $1/(e^{\hbar\omega/kT} - 1) \to 0$. Therefore $S \to 0$.

The entropy of a single quantum oscillator vanishes at absolute zero -- the system is in a unique ground state, in accordance with the third law. The zero-point energy $\hbar\omega/2$ does not contribute to the entropy because it is temperature-independent.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

The Helmholtz free energy of the Einstein solid ($3N$ oscillators) is $F = 3N[\hbar\omega_E/2 + kT\ln(1 - e^{-\Theta_E/T})]$. Derive the equation of state $P = -(\partial F/\partial V)_T$ and show that $P$ depends on volume only through the dependence of $\omega_E$ on $V$. Argue that for an incompressible solid ($\omega_E$ independent of $V$), the pressure is constant.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$P = -\partial F/\partial V$ at constant $T$. The only volume-dependent quantity is $\omega_E(V)$. Use the chain rule.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$P = -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial V}\right)_T = -3N\left[\frac{\hbar}{2}\frac{d\omega_E}{dV} + kT \cdot \frac{e^{-\Theta_E/T}}{1 - e^{-\Theta_E/T}} \cdot \left(-\frac{\hbar}{kT}\frac{d\omega_E}{dV}\right)\right]$.

$P = -3N\frac{d\omega_E}{dV}\left[\frac{\hbar}{2} - \frac{\hbar}{e^{\Theta_E/T} - 1}\right] = -3N\frac{d\omega_E}{dV}\left[\frac{\hbar}{2} - \hbar\,n_B\right]$.

The pressure depends on volume only through $d\omega_E/dV$. If $\omega_E$ is independent of $V$ (perfectly rigid lattice), then $d\omega_E/dV = 0$ and $P = 0$ (or constant, if there is an external pressure).

For a real solid, $\omega_E$ increases under compression (stiffer bonds vibrate faster), so $d\omega_E/dV < 0$, giving $P > 0$ -- the lattice pushes back against compression. The Gruneisen parameter $\gamma = -d\ln\omega_E/d\ln V$ quantifies this relationship and is a material-dependent constant of order 1--3 for most solids.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

The Debye model replaces the single Einstein frequency with a density of states $g(\omega) = 9N\omega^2/\omega_D^3$ for $0 \le \omega \le \omega_D$ (and zero for $\omega > \omega_D$). Derive the low-temperature heat capacity $C_V = (12\pi^4/5)Nk(T/\Theta_D)^3$ from this density of states. This is the **Debye $T^3$ law**.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The total energy is $U = \int_0^{\omega_D} \hbar\omega\,n_B(\omega, T)\,g(\omega)\,d\omega$. At low $T$ ($kT \ll \hbar\omega_D$), the upper limit can be extended to infinity because $n_B$ is exponentially small for $\hbar\omega \gg kT$. Then use the integral $\int_0^{\infty} x^3/(e^x - 1)\,dx = \pi^4/15$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$U = \int_0^{\omega_D} \hbar\omega \cdot \frac{1}{e^{\hbar\omega/kT} - 1} \cdot \frac{9N\omega^2}{\omega_D^3}\,d\omega = \frac{9N\hbar}{\omega_D^3} \int_0^{\omega_D} \frac{\omega^3}{e^{\hbar\omega/kT} - 1}\,d\omega$.

Substituting $x = \hbar\omega/kT$ ($\omega = kTx/\hbar$, $d\omega = (kT/\hbar)\,dx$):

$U = \frac{9N\hbar}{\omega_D^3} \cdot \frac{(kT)^4}{\hbar^4} \int_0^{\Theta_D/T} \frac{x^3}{e^x - 1}\,dx$.

At low $T$ ($\Theta_D/T \gg 1$), extend the upper limit to $\infty$:

$U \approx \frac{9N(kT)^4}{(\hbar\omega_D)^3} \cdot \frac{\pi^4}{15} = \frac{9N\pi^4 k^4 T^4}{15(\hbar\omega_D)^3}$.

$C_V = \frac{dU}{dT} = \frac{36N\pi^4 k^4 T^3}{15(\hbar\omega_D)^3} = \frac{12\pi^4}{5} Nk\left(\frac{T}{\Theta_D}\right)^3$.

This is the Debye $T^3$ law. The power-law behaviour ($T^3$) comes from the quadratic density of states ($\omega^2$), in contrast to the Einstein model's exponential freeze-out. The Debye model agrees with experiment because low-frequency phonons -- which the Einstein model omits -- dominate the heat capacity at low temperature.

</details>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The quantum harmonic oscillator partition function involves a geometric series evaluation and logarithmic differentiation. The geometric series $\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} r^n = 1/(1-r)$ for $|r| < 1$ is available in Mathlib. The main formalization challenge is representing the average energy $\langle E \rangle = \hbar\omega(1/2 + 1/(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1))$ as a function of the real variables $\omega$, $\beta$, and the physical constants $\hbar$, $k$. The heat capacity formula $C_V = kx^2 e^x/(e^x - 1)^2$ requires differentiating the average energy with respect to temperature, which is manageable symbolically but demands careful handling of the chain rule through $x = \hbar\omega/kT$. The Einstein solid ($3N$ independent oscillators) is a product of identical partition functions, and its heat capacity follows from linearity. The asymptotic limits ($C_V \to k$ as $x \to 0$, $C_V \sim kx^2 e^{-x}$ as $x \to \infty$) are within Mathlib's scope for real analysis.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Equipartition theorem and the classical limit

The high-temperature limit of the quantum harmonic oscillator provides a clean derivation of the equipartition theorem for quadratic degrees of freedom. A classical harmonic oscillator with Hamiltonian $H = p^2/(2m) + m\omega^2 q^2/2$ has two quadratic terms. The classical partition function factorises:

$$Z_1^{\mathrm{cl}} = \frac{1}{h}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\beta p^2/(2m)} dp \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-\beta m\omega^2 q^2/2} dq = \frac{1}{h}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi m}{\beta}} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{\beta m\omega^2}} = \frac{kT}{\hbar\omega}.$$

The average energy is $\langle E \rangle^{\mathrm{cl}} = -\partial\ln Z_1^{\mathrm{cl}}/\partial\beta = 1/\beta = kT$. Each quadratic degree of freedom contributes $kT/2$ -- the equipartition theorem. The quantum result $\langle E \rangle = \hbar\omega(1/2 + 1/(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1))$ reduces to $kT$ in the limit $kT \gg \hbar\omega$ because $e^{\beta\hbar\omega} \approx 1 + \beta\hbar\omega$, giving $\hbar\omega \cdot 1/(\beta\hbar\omega) = kT$.

The zero-point energy $\hbar\omega/2$ is invisible to thermodynamics -- it contributes a constant to $F$ but vanishes from $S$, $C_V$, and all response functions. However, it is physically real: the Lamb shift, the Casimir effect, and the ground-state energy of molecular vibrations all depend on it.

### Path integral formulation

The partition function of a quantum harmonic oscillator has an elegant path-integral representation. In the Feynman path integral formulation, $Z_1 = \mathrm{Tr}\,e^{-\beta\hat{H}}$ can be written as a Euclidean path integral over periodic trajectories $q(\tau)$ with period $\beta\hbar$:

$$Z_1 = \oint \mathcal{D}q(\tau)\, \exp\left(-\frac{1}{\hbar}\int_0^{\beta\hbar} d\tau\, \left[\frac{m}{2}\dot{q}^2 + \frac{m\omega^2}{2}q^2\right]\right).$$

For the harmonic oscillator, this is a Gaussian functional integral and can be evaluated exactly. The result reproduces $Z_1 = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega/2}/(1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega})$. This calculation demonstrates that the path integral formulation of quantum statistical mechanics is a natural extension of classical phase-space integrals to quantum systems, with the classical limit recovered when $\hbar \to 0$.

The Euclidean action in the path integral has the same form as the classical action but with imaginary time $\tau = it$. The partition function is a weighted average over all periodic paths, where the weight is determined by the Euclidean action. For anharmonic potentials, the path integral cannot be evaluated in closed form, but perturbative and numerical methods are available. The harmonic oscillator thus serves as the reference system for perturbation theory in both quantum mechanics and quantum statistical mechanics.

### Phonons and lattice dynamics

The Einstein model treats each atom as vibrating independently. In reality, atoms in a crystal are coupled by interatomic forces, and the normal modes of vibration are collective excitations called **phonons**. The Hamiltonian for small vibrations of a crystal lattice is:

$$H = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{\mathbf{k}, s} \left[\frac{|P_{\mathbf{k},s}|^2}{m} + m\omega_s^2(\mathbf{k})|Q_{\mathbf{k},s}|^2\right],$$

where $\mathbf{k}$ is the wave vector, $s$ labels the phonon branch (longitudinal and transverse), and $Q_{\mathbf{k},s}$ and $P_{\mathbf{k},s}$ are normal-mode coordinates. Each normal mode is an independent quantum harmonic oscillator with frequency $\omega_s(\mathbf{k})$. The phonon dispersion relation $\omega_s(\mathbf{k})$ replaces the single Einstein frequency with a spectrum.

Acoustic phonons have $\omega \to 0$ as $|\mathbf{k}| \to 0$ (long-wavelength sound waves). These low-frequency modes dominate the low-temperature heat capacity, giving the Debye $T^3$ law. Optical phonons (in crystals with more than one atom per unit cell) have $\omega > 0$ at $|\mathbf{k}| = 0$ and behave more like Einstein oscillators -- they contribute an exponential freeze-out at low $T$.

The specific heat of a real crystal is a combination of these contributions. At very low $T$ ($T \ll \Theta_D$), the $T^3$ acoustic-phonon term dominates. At intermediate temperatures, optical phonons begin to contribute. At high $T$ ($T \gg \Theta_D$), all modes are fully excited and the Dulong-Petit limit is recovered regardless of the details of the phonon spectrum.

### Negative temperatures and population inversion

For a harmonic oscillator, $n_B = 1/(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1) > 0$ for all finite positive temperatures. However, if the population is inverted (higher energy levels more populated than lower ones), one can formally define a negative temperature through $e^{-\beta\hbar\omega} > 1$, giving $\beta < 0$. Negative temperatures are "hotter" than any positive temperature in the sense that energy flows from a negative-temperature system to a positive-temperature system. Population inversion is the basis of the laser: the gain medium is pumped to a negative-temperature state, and stimulated emission extracts coherent radiation.

**Synthesis.** The quantum harmonic oscillator is the foundational system of statistical mechanics because every bound system near equilibrium looks harmonic. The partition function $Z_1 = e^{-\beta\hbar\omega/2}/(1 - e^{-\beta\hbar\omega})$ encodes the full thermodynamic behaviour of a single vibrational mode, and the Einstein solid extends this to a macroscopic crystal by treating $3N$ oscillators as independent. The central physical insight is the energy quantum $\hbar\omega$: when $kT \gg \hbar\omega$, the system is classical (Dulong-Petit), and when $kT \ll \hbar\omega$, the system freezes out ($C_V \to 0$). The Bose-Einstein occupation number $n_B = 1/(e^{\beta\hbar\omega} - 1)$ bridges these limits and reappears wherever quantum harmonic oscillators arise: blackbody radiation [11.04.02], phonon physics, and Bose-Einstein condensation [11.05.01]. The Einstein model's single-frequency assumption is its key limitation; the Debye model's inclusion of a frequency spectrum gives the correct $T^3$ low-temperature law and connects this unit to the full theory of lattice dynamics and solid-state physics. The harmonic oscillator partition function is dual to the classical equipartition result [11.04.04]: the classical limit $C_V = k$ per oscillator is recovered from the quantum formula when $\hbar\omega/kT \to 0$, just as the Sackur-Tetrode entropy is recovered from quantum statistics in the classical limit $n\lambda^3 \to 0$.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition.** *The heat capacity of the Einstein solid is $C_V = 3Nkx^2 e^x/(e^x - 1)^2$ where $x = \Theta_E/T$ and $\Theta_E = \hbar\omega_E/k$. This heat capacity satisfies: (i) $C_V \to 3Nk$ as $T \to \infty$, (ii) $C_V \to 0$ exponentially as $T \to 0$, (iii) $C_V$ is monotonically increasing in $T$ for all $T > 0$.*

**Proof.** The total partition function for $3N$ independent oscillators is $Z = Z_1^{3N}$. The total energy is:

$$U = 3N\langle E \rangle = 3N\hbar\omega_E\left[\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{e^x - 1}\right].$$

$C_V = \frac{dU}{dT} = 3N\hbar\omega_E \cdot \frac{d}{dT}\left[\frac{1}{e^x - 1}\right]$.

Using $dx/dT = -\Theta_E/T^2 = -x/T$:

$\frac{d}{dT}\left[\frac{1}{e^x - 1}\right] = -\frac{e^x}{(e^x - 1)^2} \cdot \frac{dx}{dT} = \frac{e^x}{(e^x - 1)^2} \cdot \frac{x}{T}$.

$C_V = 3N\hbar\omega_E \cdot \frac{x e^x}{T(e^x - 1)^2} = 3Nk \cdot x^2 \frac{e^x}{(e^x - 1)^2}$.

(i) As $T \to \infty$: $x \to 0$, $e^x/(e^x - 1)^2 \to 1/x^2$, so $C_V \to 3Nk$.

(ii) As $T \to 0$: $x \to \infty$, $e^x/(e^x - 1)^2 \approx e^x/e^{2x} = e^{-x}$, so $C_V \approx 3Nk x^2 e^{-x} \to 0$ exponentially.

(iii) Monotonicity follows from $dC_V/dT > 0$ for all $T > 0$. Setting $f(x) = x^2 e^x/(e^x - 1)^2$, we need $df/dx < 0$ (since $C_V \propto f(x)$ and $x$ decreases with $T$). Computing $f'(x) = [2x(e^x - 1)^2 - x^2 e^x(e^x - 1) + 2x^2 e^x(e^x - 1)] / (e^x - 1)^3 \cdot e^x$. After simplification, $f'(x) < 0$ for all $x > 0$, confirming $C_V$ is monotonically increasing in $T$. $\square$

**Proposition.** *The entropy of the Einstein solid satisfies $S \to 0$ as $T \to 0$, consistent with the third law of thermodynamics.*

**Proof.** From the free energy $F = 3N[\hbar\omega_E/2 + kT\ln(1 - e^{-\Theta_E/T})]$ and $S = -(dF/dT)_V$:

$$S = -3N\left[k\ln(1 - e^{-\Theta_E/T}) + kT \cdot \frac{(\Theta_E/kT^2)e^{-\Theta_E/T}}{1 - e^{-\Theta_E/T}}\right].$$

As $T \to 0$: $e^{-\Theta_E/T} \to 0$, so $\ln(1 - e^{-\Theta_E/T}) \to 0$ and $e^{-\Theta_E/T}/(1 - e^{-\Theta_E/T}) \to 0$. Both terms in $S$ vanish, giving $S \to 0$. The ground state has $3N$ oscillators each in their lowest energy state ($n = 0$), so there is a unique microstate and $S = k\ln 1 = 0$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **[11.04.01]** The quantum harmonic oscillator partition function is a direct application of the canonical ensemble to a system with discrete energy levels.
- **[11.04.03]** The factorisation $Z = Z_1^{3N}$ uses the partition-function product rule for independent subsystems.
- **[11.04.02]** Blackbody radiation is a collection of photon modes, each a quantum harmonic oscillator. The average energy $\langle E \rangle = \hbar\omega\,n_B$ is the same expression that gives the Planck distribution.
- **[11.04.04]** The classical ideal gas heat capacity $C_V = 3Nk/2$ comes from three translational degrees of freedom. The Einstein solid heat capacity $C_V = 3Nk$ at high $T$ comes from three vibrational degrees of freedom, each with kinetic and potential contributions.
- **[11.05.01]** Phonons are bosons. The Debye model is a Bose gas of phonons with a density of states proportional to $\omega^2$.
- **[11.01.03]** The third law is satisfied: $S \to 0$ as $T \to 0$ for the Einstein solid (unique ground state).

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

In 1907, Albert Einstein -- two years after his annus mirabilis papers on Brownian motion, the photoelectric effect, and special relativity -- turned his attention to the specific heat of solids. The Dulong-Petit law ($C_V = 3R$ per mole) was known to fail at low temperatures, but no theoretical explanation existed. Einstein applied Planck's quantum hypothesis to the vibrations of atoms in a crystal. His model assumed that each atom vibrates independently with a single frequency, and that the energy of vibration is quantised in units of $\hbar\omega_E$.

Einstein's prediction -- that $C_V$ should decrease at low temperature and approach zero as $T \to 0$ -- was a triumph of early quantum theory. It provided one of the first pieces of evidence that quantisation applied beyond blackbody radiation, to material systems. The model correctly captured the qualitative behaviour but predicted an exponential drop in $C_V$ at low $T$, while experiments showed a $T^3$ dependence.

Peter Debye (1912) resolved this discrepancy by replacing Einstein's single frequency with a continuous spectrum of vibrational modes up to a cutoff frequency $\omega_D$, treating the crystal as an elastic continuum. The Debye model gives $C_V \propto T^3$ at low $T$ (from the acoustic phonon modes) and $C_V = 3Nk$ at high $T$, in excellent quantitative agreement with experiment. The Debye temperature $\Theta_D$ became a standard material parameter tabulated for every solid.

Max Born and Theodore von Karman (1912) independently developed a more detailed lattice dynamical theory that treated the crystal as a discrete periodic structure, yielding the phonon dispersion relations. Their approach is the foundation of modern solid-state physics, with applications ranging from thermal conductivity to superconductivity.

The Einstein and Debye models illustrate a recurring pattern in theoretical physics: a simple model that captures the essential physics (Einstein: quantised vibrations), followed by a refined model that includes additional structure (Debye: frequency spectrum), followed by a full treatment (Born--von Karman: discrete lattice dynamics). Each level of sophistication adds predictive power while preserving the core physical insight.

The Einstein solid also raises a conceptual point about modelling. The assumption that all oscillators have the same frequency is physically unrealistic -- real crystals have a wide spectrum of vibrational frequencies. Yet the model captures the essential physics: quantum mechanics suppresses the heat capacity at low temperature. The specific form of the suppression (exponential vs. power law) depends on the details of the frequency spectrum, but the qualitative behaviour ($C_V \to 0$ as $T \to 0$) follows from quantisation alone. This is an instance of a general principle: the universal features of a physical theory are often captured by the simplest model that incorporates the key new ingredient.
