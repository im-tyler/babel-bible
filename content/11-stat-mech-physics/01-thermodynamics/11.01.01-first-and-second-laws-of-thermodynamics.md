## Intuition [Beginner]

Energy is conserved. That is the first law of thermodynamics. Energy can move between forms — kinetic, potential, thermal, chemical — but the total never changes. In a thermodynamic system the bookkeeping is: the **internal energy** $U$ of a system changes by the heat added minus the work done. In symbols, $\Delta U = Q - W$.

Heat $Q$ is energy transferred because of a temperature difference. Work $W$ is energy transferred by any other means — pushing a piston, turning a shaft, running a current through a resistor. Both are transfers *across the boundary*. The first law says the change in what is *inside* equals what came in as heat minus what went out as work.

The second law adds a direction. In an isolated system — no heat in, no work out — the total energy is constant, but something else always increases: **entropy**, denoted $S$. Entropy measures how many microscopic arrangements are consistent with the macroscopic state you observe. A neat stack of cards has low entropy (few arrangements look "stacked"). The same cards scattered across the floor have high entropy (enormously many arrangements look "scattered").

The practical consequence: heat flows from hot to cold, never the other way on its own. A cup of coffee cools down in a room; the room never spontaneously donates thermal energy to heat the coffee back up. That asymmetry — the **arrow of time** — is what the second law gives you that Newton's laws do not. Newton's equations run equally well forwards and backwards. Thermodynamics does not.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a gas sealed in a cylinder with a movable piston. The gas molecules bounce around inside, colliding with each other and the walls. Each collision transfers a tiny impulse; the aggregate of all those impulses on the piston face is the **pressure** $P$. The piston's position fixes the **volume** $V$.

Add heat to the gas (hold a flame to the cylinder). The molecules speed up — average kinetic energy rises, so temperature rises. Some of that extra energy pushes the piston outward, doing work on whatever is outside. The first law tracks the split: $\Delta U = Q - W$. Some energy stays inside (higher $U$), some leaves as work.

Now remove the flame and insulate the cylinder. No more heat can enter or leave. The second law says entropy cannot decrease in this isolated setup. The gas will never spontaneously compress itself, push the piston back in, and cool down — that would lower entropy without external intervention.

![Cylinder with piston: gas molecules (small dots) exert pressure on the piston. Left: heat flows in (red arrow), gas expands, piston moves right, doing work. Right: insulated cylinder, entropy increases as gas equilibrates; spontaneous reverse motion is forbidden by the second law.](/img/11.01.01-cylinder-piston-placeholder.svg)

The picture encodes both laws: the first law governs the energy balance at the boundary, the second law governs which directions of change are allowed inside.

## Worked example [Beginner]

A gas in a cylinder absorbs $Q = 500\text{ J}$ of heat from a flame and does $W = 200\text{ J}$ of work pushing the piston outward.

Step 1. Apply the first law:

$$
\Delta U = Q - W = 500\text{ J} - 200\text{ J} = 300\text{ J}.
$$

The internal energy of the gas increased by 300 J. The remaining 200 J left as work.

Step 2. Now insulate the cylinder (no heat exchange: $Q = 0$) and let the gas expand until it has done exactly 300 J of work. The first law gives:

$$
\Delta U = 0 - 300\text{ J} = -300\text{ J}.
$$

The gas loses 300 J of internal energy and cools down. The initial 300 J of stored energy was completely converted to work.

Step 3. Could the gas instead have spontaneously absorbed 300 J from its cooler surroundings and heated back up, without any external input? The second law says no. Heat does not flow from cold to hot on its own. The coffee does not reheat itself by stealing thermal energy from the room.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **thermodynamic system** is specified by a set of **state variables** — quantities that depend only on the current equilibrium state, not on how the system arrived there. For a simple compressible system the fundamental state variables are: internal energy $U$, entropy $S$, volume $V$, temperature $T$, and pressure $P$. Additional variables (chemical potential $\mu$, particle number $N$, magnetic moment $M$, etc.) enter when the system has additional degrees of freedom beyond mechanical compression.

A **state function** is any quantity determined entirely by the state. $U$, $S$, $T$, $P$, $V$ are state functions. Heat $Q$ and work $W$ are *not* state functions — they depend on the path taken between states.

**First law (differential form).** For an infinitesimal change,

$$
dU = \delta Q - \delta W.
$$

The notation $dU$ is an **exact differential** (the change in a state function), while $\delta Q$ and $\delta W$ are **inexact differentials** (path-dependent). For a reversible process acting on a simple compressible system, $\delta Q_{\rm rev} = T\,dS$ and $\delta W_{\rm rev} = P\,dV$, giving the **fundamental thermodynamic relation**:

$$
\boxed{\;\;dU = T\,dS - P\,dV.\;\;}
$$

This equation is the backbone of thermodynamic manipulation. It is valid for any quasi-static (reversible) change of a simple system. The sign convention $-P\,dV$ reflects: positive $dV$ (expansion) means the system does work *on* the surroundings, reducing $U$.

**Second law.** There are two classical statements.

*Clausius statement:* No process is possible whose sole result is the transfer of heat from a colder body to a hotter body.

*Kelvin-Planck statement:* No process is possible whose sole result is the absorption of heat from a reservoir and the conversion of all of that heat into work.

Both statements are equivalent (each implies the other via a contradiction argument). The second law can be reformulated as the **entropy principle**: for any spontaneous process in an isolated system,

$$
\Delta S \geq 0,
$$

with equality if and only if the process is reversible. The function $S$ is a state function (this requires proof and is the content of Clausius's theorem).

**Entropy as a state function.** Clausius's theorem establishes that for any cyclic process, $\oint \delta Q / T \leq 0$, with equality for reversible cycles. This inequality guarantees the existence of a state function $S$ whose change between two equilibrium states $A$ and $B$ satisfies

$$
S(B) - S(A) \geq \int_A^B \frac{\delta Q}{T},
$$

with equality for reversible paths. The integral on the right is path-dependent; the entropy difference on the left is not.

**Statistical interpretation (Boltzmann).** The entropy of a macrostate with $\Omega$ compatible microstates is

$$
S = k_B \ln \Omega,
$$

where $k_B$ is Boltzmann's constant. This bridges the macroscopic second law to a microscopic counting argument: macrostates with more microstates are more probable, and isolated systems evolve toward more probable macrostates.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The first law $\Delta U = Q - W$ does **not** say that heat and work are forms of energy; it says they are *ways energy is transferred*. Internal energy $U$ is the energy; $Q$ and $W$ are boundary transfers.
- The second law does **not** say "entropy always increases everywhere." It says entropy of an *isolated* system increases. A refrigerator decreases the entropy of its interior, but the entropy increase of the hot kitchen more than compensates.
- The equation $dU = T\,dS - P\,dV$ holds only for *reversible* processes. For irreversible processes the inequality $dU < T\,dS - P\,dV$ may hold, but $U$, $S$, $V$ remain well-defined state functions for equilibrium states connected by any path.
- The Kelvin-Planck statement forbids converting heat *entirely* into work in a cyclic process. A single expansion stroke can convert heat entirely into work — but then the system is in a different state and cannot repeat without an external reset.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Carnot's theorem).** *No heat engine operating between two reservoirs at temperatures $T_H$ (hot) and $T_C$ (cold) is more efficient than a Carnot engine operating between the same reservoirs. All reversible engines operating between $T_H$ and $T_C$ have the same efficiency, the **Carnot efficiency***

$$
\eta_{\rm Carnot} = 1 - \frac{T_C}{T_H}.
$$

**Proof.** Suppose an engine $X$ has efficiency $\eta_X > \eta_{\rm Carnot}$. Run a Carnot engine *backwards* between the same reservoirs as a refrigerator, using the work output of $X$ to drive it. By hypothesis, $X$ produces more work per unit of heat absorbed from $T_H$ than the Carnot refrigerator requires per unit of heat dumped to $T_H$. The net effect of the combined machine: heat is transferred from $T_C$ to $T_H$ with no external work input (some work from $X$ drives the Carnot refrigerator, and there is work left over). This violates the Clausius statement of the second law. Contradiction.

Hence $\eta_X \leq \eta_{\rm Carnot}$ for any engine $X$.

For the converse, any reversible engine operating between $T_H$ and $T_C$ can be used in place of the Carnot engine in the argument above. If a reversible engine $R$ had $\eta_R < \eta_{\rm Carnot}$, run the Carnot engine backwards driven by $R$; the same contradiction follows. So all reversible engines have the same efficiency, equal to the Carnot efficiency.

The explicit value $\eta = 1 - T_C/T_H$ follows from computing the entropy balance for the four-step Carnot cycle (isothermal expansion at $T_H$, adiabatic expansion, isothermal compression at $T_C$, adiabatic compression back to the initial state): the total entropy change over the cycle is zero (reversible), giving $Q_H/T_H = Q_C/T_C$, hence $\eta = 1 - Q_C/Q_H = 1 - T_C/T_H$. $\square$

**Corollary.** *The existence of a universal temperature scale on which the Carnot efficiency is $1 - T_C/T_H$ is equivalent to the existence of the thermodynamic temperature scale. This is the basis of the Kelvin scale: $T$ is defined so that the efficiency of a Carnot engine depends only on the ratio $T_C/T_H$, independent of the working substance.*

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

An ideal gas undergoes an isothermal (constant-temperature) expansion. Does its internal energy change? Does its entropy change?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For an ideal gas, $U$ depends only on $T$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Internal energy does not change ($\Delta U = 0$) because $U$ depends only on $T$ for an ideal gas. Entropy increases ($\Delta S > 0$) because the gas expands into a larger volume, increasing the number of accessible microstates $\Omega$, and $S = k_B \ln \Omega$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

A Carnot engine operates between $T_H = 600\text{ K}$ and $T_C = 300\text{ K}$. It absorbs $Q_H = 1000\text{ J}$ per cycle. Compute the efficiency, the work output per cycle, and the heat rejected to the cold reservoir.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $\eta = 1 - T_C/T_H$, then $W = \eta Q_H$ and $Q_C = Q_H - W$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\eta = 1 - 300/600 = 0.5$. Work per cycle: $W = 0.5 \times 1000 = 500\text{ J}$. Heat rejected: $Q_C = Q_H - W = 500\text{ J}$.

The entropy balance checks: $\Delta S_{\rm hot} = -Q_H/T_H = -1000/600$ and $\Delta S_{\rm cold} = Q_C/T_C = 500/300 = 1000/600$, so total $\Delta S = 0$ (reversible cycle).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the Clausius statement and the Kelvin-Planck statement of the second law are equivalent. Specifically, show that violating one implies violating the other.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Assume a machine that violates Kelvin-Planck (converts heat entirely to work in a cycle). Use that work to drive a heat pump between the same reservoirs.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Kelvin-Planck implies Clausius:* Suppose a device violates Clausius by transferring heat $Q_C$ from cold to hot with no other effect. Connect a heat engine between the same two reservoirs that absorbs $Q_H$ from hot, rejects $Q_C$ to cold, and produces work $W = Q_H - Q_C$. The combined system absorbs $Q_H - Q_C$ from the hot reservoir, produces $W = Q_H - Q_C$ of work, and returns the cold reservoir to its original state. Net effect: heat from a single reservoir (hot) is converted entirely to work — violating Kelvin-Planck.

*Clausius implies Kelvin-Planck:* Suppose a device violates Kelvin-Planck by absorbing heat $Q$ from a single reservoir at $T_H$ and converting it entirely to work. Use this work to drive a refrigerator pumping heat from $T_C$ to $T_H$. Net effect: heat is transferred from cold to hot with no external work input — violating Clausius.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Two moles of an ideal gas expand freely into a vacuum (Joule expansion) from volume $V_i$ to $V_f = 2V_i$. No heat is exchanged and no work is done. Using $S = k_B \ln \Omega$, argue that the entropy increases. Compute $\Delta S$ if the gas has $N$ molecules.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Each molecule now has twice the volume to occupy. The number of spatial configurations doubles per molecule.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In free expansion, $\Delta U = 0$ (no heat, no work), and the temperature of an ideal gas is unchanged. But the available volume doubles. Each molecule has twice as many positional configurations, so $\Omega_f / \Omega_i = 2^N$. The entropy change is

$$
\Delta S = k_B \ln\frac{\Omega_f}{\Omega_i} = k_B \ln 2^N = N k_B \ln 2 = nR\ln 2,
$$

where $n$ is the number of moles and $R = N_A k_B$ is the gas constant. For two moles, $\Delta S = 2R\ln 2$. Positive, consistent with $\Delta S \geq 0$ for an isolated system. The process is irreversible (entropy increases), even though energy is conserved.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the relation $dS = dQ_{\rm rev}/T$ from the Carnot cycle. Specifically, show that for any reversible cyclic process, $\oint \delta Q_{\rm rev}/T = 0$, and conclude that $S$ defined by $dS = \delta Q_{\rm rev}/T$ is a state function.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Decompose an arbitrary reversible cycle into a sum of infinitesimal Carnot cycles. Each contributes zero to $\oint \delta Q/T$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take an arbitrary reversible cycle in the $P$-$V$ plane. Approximate it by a sequence of infinitesimal Carnot cycles: pairs of adiabats crossed by isotherms at temperatures $T_i$ and $T_{i+1}$. For each Carnot sub-cycle, $Q_{H,i}/T_{H,i} = Q_{C,i}/T_{C,i}$ (Carnot theorem), so $\delta Q_i/T_i$ summed around the sub-cycle is zero. Summing over all sub-cycles, internal adiabatic contributions cancel pairwise (adjacent cycles share an adiabat traversed in opposite directions), leaving

$$
\oint \frac{\delta Q_{\rm rev}}{T} = 0.
$$

A quantity whose integral around any closed path is zero defines a state function. Define $S$ by $dS = \delta Q_{\rm rev}/T$. Then $S(B) - S(A) = \int_A^B \delta Q_{\rm rev}/T$ is independent of path — entropy is a state function. This is **Clausius's theorem**.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

An engine claims to operate between reservoirs at $T_H = 400\text{ K}$ and $T_C = 200\text{ K}$ with efficiency $\eta = 0.6$. Is this possible? If not, what is the maximum possible efficiency?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare with the Carnot efficiency for these temperatures.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Carnot efficiency is $\eta_{\rm Carnot} = 1 - 200/400 = 0.5$. The claimed $\eta = 0.6 > 0.5$ violates Carnot's theorem. No engine between these reservoirs can exceed 50% efficiency. The claim is impossible.

This follows directly from Carnot's theorem: no engine is more efficient than a Carnot engine between the same reservoirs, and all reversible engines achieve exactly the Carnot efficiency. Any real (irreversible) engine has $\eta < \eta_{\rm Carnot}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

A system undergoes a reversible isothermal expansion at temperature $T$, absorbing heat $Q$. Then it undergoes a reversible adiabatic expansion to a lower temperature $T'$. Finally it is compressed isothermally at $T'$ and then adiabatically back to the initial state. Show that the efficiency of this Carnot cycle is $\eta = 1 - T'/T$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Track $Q_{\rm in}$ (absorbed at $T$) and $Q_{\rm out}$ (rejected at $T'$). The adiabatic legs contribute no heat. Use the entropy balance over the full cycle.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Label the four legs: (1) isothermal expansion at $T$: heat $Q_H$ absorbed, entropy change $\Delta S_1 = Q_H/T$. (2) Adiabatic expansion: $Q = 0$, $\Delta S_2 = 0$. (3) Isothermal compression at $T'$: heat $Q_C$ rejected, $\Delta S_3 = -Q_C/T'$. (4) Adiabatic compression: $Q = 0$, $\Delta S_4 = 0$.

Total entropy change over the cycle: $\Delta S_{\rm total} = Q_H/T - Q_C/T' = 0$ (reversible cycle). Hence $Q_C/Q_H = T'/T$. The work output is $W = Q_H - Q_C$, so

$$
\eta = \frac{W}{Q_H} = 1 - \frac{Q_C}{Q_H} = 1 - \frac{T'}{T}.
$$

The efficiency depends only on the reservoir temperatures, not on the working substance. This is the defining property of the thermodynamic temperature scale.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

For a general thermodynamic system with fundamental relation $dU = T\,dS - P\,dV$, prove that $(\partial T/\partial V)_S = -(\partial P/\partial S)_V$. This is one of the **Maxwell relations**.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$U$ is a state function, so $dU$ is exact. Apply the equality of mixed partial derivatives.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $U(S, V)$ is a state function, $dU = T\,dS - P\,dV$ is an exact differential. The coefficients satisfy $T = (\partial U/\partial S)_V$ and $-P = (\partial U/\partial V)_S$. Equality of mixed second partial derivatives gives

$$
\frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial V\,\partial S} = \frac{\partial^2 U}{\partial S\,\partial V},
$$

i.e., $\left(\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}\right)_S = -\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial S}\right)_V$.

This is the first Maxwell relation. The other three follow by applying the same argument to the thermodynamic potentials $H = U + PV$, $F = U - TS$, and $G = U + PV - TS$ developed in the next unit [11.01.02].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that for an isolated system composed of two subsystems initially at temperatures $T_1$ and $T_2$ (with $T_1 > T_2$) that are brought into thermal contact, the total entropy increases. Assume each subsystem has constant heat capacity $C$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the final equilibrium temperature, then compute $\Delta S$ for each subsystem using $dS = C\,dT/T$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Energy conservation: $C(T_1 - T_f) = C(T_f - T_2)$, giving $T_f = (T_1 + T_2)/2$.

Entropy change for subsystem 1 (cools from $T_1$ to $T_f$):

$$
\Delta S_1 = C\ln\frac{T_f}{T_1}.
$$

Entropy change for subsystem 2 (heats from $T_2$ to $T_f$):

$$
\Delta S_2 = C\ln\frac{T_f}{T_2}.
$$

Total entropy change:

$$
\Delta S = C\ln\frac{T_f}{T_1} + C\ln\frac{T_f}{T_2} = C\ln\frac{T_f^2}{T_1 T_2}.
$$

Since $T_f = (T_1 + T_2)/2$, we have $T_f^2 = (T_1 + T_2)^2/4 \geq T_1 T_2$ by the AM-GM inequality (equality only when $T_1 = T_2$). Hence $T_f^2/(T_1 T_2) \geq 1$ and $\Delta S \geq 0$, with equality only when $T_1 = T_2$ (no heat flow). This confirms the second law for this concrete scenario.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 11 (hard, symbolic).**

A heat pump (reverse Carnot engine) is used to heat a house. The outside temperature is $T_C = 270\text{ K}$ ($-3^\circ$C) and the inside temperature is $T_H = 293\text{ K}$ ($20^\circ$C). Compute the coefficient of performance (COP) of an ideal heat pump: $\text{COP} = Q_H / W$, where $Q_H$ is the heat delivered to the house and $W$ is the work input. How does this compare to resistive heating (COP = 1)?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For a Carnot heat pump, $Q_H/T_H = Q_C/T_C$ and $W = Q_H - Q_C$. Express COP in terms of the temperatures.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From the Carnot relations: $Q_C/Q_H = T_C/T_H = 270/293$. So $W = Q_H(1 - T_C/T_H) = Q_H \cdot 23/293$. The COP is

$$
\text{COP} = \frac{Q_H}{W} = \frac{1}{1 - T_C/T_H} = \frac{T_H}{T_H - T_C} = \frac{293}{23} \approx 12.7.
$$

An ideal heat pump delivers 12.7 J of heating for every 1 J of work input — far more efficient than resistive heating (1 J of heating per 1 J of electrical energy). Real heat pumps achieve COP $\approx 3$–$5$ due to irreversibilities, but they are still 3–5 times more efficient than resistive heating. This dramatic improvement in efficiency is a direct consequence of the second law: the heat pump does not *create* heat, it *moves* heat from the cold reservoir (outside) to the hot reservoir (inside), and the second law limits how much work this requires.

</details>

**Exercise 12 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the Clausius inequality $\oint \delta Q/T \leq 0$ implies that for any irreversible process from state $A$ to state $B$, $\Delta S > \int_A^B \delta Q/T$ (entropy generation is strictly positive). Show that this leads to the concept of the **entropy production** $\sigma = dS - \delta Q/T \geq 0$, and that $\sigma = 0$ only for reversible processes.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Consider a cyclic process: irreversible path from $A$ to $B$, followed by a reversible path from $B$ back to $A$. Apply the Clausius inequality to the full cycle.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take a cyclic process consisting of: (1) an irreversible path from $A$ to $B$, absorbing heat $\delta Q$ at temperature $T$; (2) a reversible path from $B$ to $A$, with $\delta Q_{\rm rev} = T\,dS$.

The Clausius inequality for the full cycle: $\oint \delta Q/T \leq 0$, i.e.,

$$
\int_A^B \frac{\delta Q_{\rm irr}}{T} + \int_B^A \frac{\delta Q_{\rm rev}}{T} \leq 0.
$$

The reversible integral equals $S(A) - S(B) = -\Delta S$. So:

$$
\int_A^B \frac{\delta Q_{\rm irr}}{T} - \Delta S \leq 0,
$$

hence $\Delta S \geq \int_A^B \delta Q_{\rm irr}/T$, with strict inequality for irreversible processes. Define the entropy production $\sigma = \Delta S - \int_A^B \delta Q/T \geq 0$. For a reversible process $\sigma = 0$; for an irreversible process $\sigma > 0$. The entropy production is the quantitative measure of irreversibility: it vanishes for equilibrium processes and is positive whenever dissipative processes (friction, heat flow across finite temperature differences, chemical reactions) occur.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 13 (hard, symbolic).**

The Sackur-Tetrode equation for the entropy of a monatomic ideal gas is

$$
S = Nk_B\left[\ln\left(\frac{V}{N}\left(\frac{4\pi mU}{3Nh^2}\right)^{3/2}\right) + \frac{5}{2}\right].
$$

From this fundamental relation, derive (a) the temperature $T = (\partial U/\partial S)_{V,N}$, (b) the pressure $P = -(\partial U/\partial V)_{S,N}$, and (c) the equation of state. Verify that you recover $PV = Nk_BT$ and $U = \frac{3}{2}Nk_BT$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $S$ in terms of $U$ and invert to find $(\partial S/\partial U)_{V,N} = 1/T$ and $(\partial S/\partial V)_{U,N} = P/T$. Use the chain rule for the inverse relation.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From the Sackur-Tetrode equation:

$$
\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial U}\right)_{V,N} = Nk_B \cdot \frac{3}{2U} = \frac{3Nk_B}{2U}.
$$

Since $1/T = (\partial S/\partial U)_{V,N}$: $T = 2U/(3Nk_B)$, so $U = \frac{3}{2}Nk_BT$.

$$
\left(\frac{\partial S}{\partial V}\right)_{U,N} = Nk_B \cdot \frac{1}{V} = \frac{Nk_B}{V}.
$$

Since $P/T = (\partial S/\partial V)_{U,N}$: $P = Nk_BT/V$, giving $PV = Nk_BT$.

The Sackur-Tetrode equation encodes all thermodynamic properties of the ideal gas. The first derivatives give $T$ and $P$ (the equation of state and the energy-temperature relation). The second derivatives give the heat capacity $C_V = \frac{3}{2}Nk_B$ and the compressibility $\kappa_T = 1/P$. This is an explicit demonstration that the fundamental relation $U(S, V, N)$ contains complete thermodynamic information — a central tenet of the postulatory approach of Callen.

## Caratheodory's axiomatic formulation [Master]

The classical statements of the second law (Clausius, Kelvin-Planck) are *negative* — they describe what cannot happen. Caratheodory (1909) gave a *positive* axiomatisation that makes the mathematical structure more transparent. The Caratheodory approach is preferred by mathematicians because it converts the second law from a statement about hypothetical engines into a theorem of differential geometry.

**Caratheodory's principle.** *In every neighbourhood of any equilibrium state, there exist states that cannot be reached by an adiabatic process.*

This innocuous-sounding geometric restriction on the set of reachable states turns out to be equivalent to the classical second law. The key mathematical content is the following.

Consider the differential form $\delta Q = dU + P\,dV$ (the first law written for a simple system, with $\delta Q$ being the inexact heat 1-form). Caratheodory's principle, combined with a theorem on Pfaffian forms, implies:

**Theorem (Caratheodory).** *If $\delta Q$ is a Pfaffian form on a manifold of equilibrium states and every neighbourhood of every point contains adiabatically inaccessible points, then $\delta Q$ admits an **integrating factor**: there exist functions $T$ and $S$ such that $\delta Q = T\,dS$, with $dS$ an exact differential.*

The integrating factor $T$ is the thermodynamic temperature, and $S$ is entropy. The second law reduces to the existence of this integrating factor. The monotonicity of entropy (entropy increases for spontaneous processes) follows from the sign convention that $T > 0$.

This formulation has two virtues. First, it makes the second law a statement about differential geometry — the Frobenius integrability condition on the heat 1-form — rather than a prohibition on engine designs. Second, it generalises: Caratheodory's framework extends to systems with additional work terms (magnetic, chemical, surface) without changing the core argument. The proof uses a result from the theory of Pfaffian differential forms: if a 1-form $\omega$ on an $n$-dimensional manifold has the property that every neighbourhood of every point contains points inaccessible by curves satisfying $\omega = 0$, then $\omega$ admits an integrating factor. The condition $\omega = 0$ defines the adiabatic hypersurface; the inaccessibility condition is Caratheodory's principle. The integrating factor turns the inexact heat form into an exact differential, which is entropy.

The equivalence with the Clausius and Kelvin-Planck statements is shown in detail in Callen Ch. 4 and in Landsberg, *Thermodynamics and Statistical Mechanics* (1978), Ch. 6. A modern pedagogical treatment appears in Pippard's *Classical Thermodynamics* (1957), which presents both the classical and Caratheodory formulations side by side and proves their equivalence explicitly using the properties of Pfaffian forms on two- and three-dimensional manifolds.

## Thermodynamic potentials and Maxwell relations [Master]

The fundamental relation $dU = T\,dS - P\,dV$ uses $S$ and $V$ as independent variables. Different experimental situations call for different independent variables. The **Legendre transforms** of $U$ produce the standard thermodynamic potentials.

**Enthalpy** $H = U + PV$:

$$
dH = T\,dS + V\,dP.
$$

Natural variables: $(S, P)$. Useful for constant-pressure processes.

**Helmholtz free energy** $F = U - TS$:

$$
dF = -S\,dT - P\,dV.
$$

Natural variables: $(T, V)$. Minimised at equilibrium for a system at constant $T$ and $V$.

**Gibbs free energy** $G = U + PV - TS = H - TS$:

$$
dG = -S\,dT + V\,dP.
$$

Natural variables: $(T, P)$. Minimised at equilibrium for a system at constant $T$ and $P$ (the most common experimental condition).

Each potential's exact differential yields a **Maxwell relation** by equality of mixed partial derivatives:

| Potential | Maxwell relation |
|-----------|-----------------|
| $U(S,V)$ | $(\partial T/\partial V)_S = -(\partial P/\partial S)_V$ |
| $H(S,P)$ | $(\partial T/\partial P)_S = (\partial V/\partial S)_P$ |
| $F(T,V)$ | $(\partial S/\partial V)_T = (\partial P/\partial T)_V$ |
| $G(T,P)$ | $-(\partial S/\partial P)_T = (\partial V/\partial T)_P$ |

These four relations allow the substitution of measurable quantities (equations of state, heat capacities) for hard-to-measure quantities (entropy derivatives). They are the workhorses of thermodynamic computation.

**Stability conditions.** Equilibrium requires the relevant thermodynamic potential to be a *minimum*, which imposes convexity/concavity conditions. For $F(T, V)$ at fixed $T$: $F$ is convex in $V$, giving $(\partial^2 F/\partial V^2)_T > 0$, i.e., $-(\partial P/\partial V)_T > 0$. This is the **mechanical stability condition**: the isothermal compressibility $\kappa_T = -V^{-1}(\partial V/\partial P)_T$ must be positive. Similarly, $C_V = T(\partial S/\partial T)_V > 0$ (thermal stability). Violation of stability signals a phase transition. The stability conditions are not independent — they are linked by Maxwell relations. For example, the Prigogine-Defay ratio relates the discontinuities in $C_P$, $\kappa_T$, and $\alpha$ at a phase transition, and its deviation from unity signals the breakdown of the simple two-phase description (as occurs in glass transitions).

## Third law and information-theoretic entropy [Master]

**Third law (Nernst heat theorem).** As $T \to 0$, the entropy change in any isothermal process approaches zero. Equivalently, the entropy of a system in internal equilibrium approaches a constant (conventionally zero) as $T \to 0$.

The third law has two consequences: (a) heat capacities vanish as $T \to 0$ (otherwise integrating $dS = C\,dT/T$ would give a logarithmic divergence), and (b) it is impossible to reach $T = 0$ in a finite number of steps. The statistical interpretation: at $T = 0$, the system occupies its unique ground state, $\Omega = 1$, and $S = k_B \ln 1 = 0$.

The unattainability of absolute zero (consequence b) follows from a simple argument. Suppose a process lowers the temperature from $T_1$ to $T_2 < T_1$ by an adiabatic demagnetisation step. The entropy decrease of the spin system is compensated by an entropy increase of the lattice phonons. As $T \to 0$, the entropy curves for different magnetic fields converge (third law), so each step produces a smaller and smaller temperature reduction, requiring an infinite number of steps to reach $T = 0$. This is not an engineering limitation — it is a fundamental thermodynamic bound, as absolute as the impossibility of a perpetual motion machine.

**Connection to information theory.** Shannon (1948) defined the entropy of a discrete probability distribution $\{p_i\}$ as

$$
H_{\rm Shannon} = -\sum_i p_i \ln p_i.
$$

For the uniform distribution over $\Omega$ microstates, $p_i = 1/\Omega$ and $H_{\rm Shannon} = \ln \Omega$. The thermodynamic entropy is then $S = k_B H_{\rm Shannon}$. This is not an analogy — it is an identification. Thermodynamic entropy *is* the Shannon entropy of the microstate distribution, scaled by $k_B$. The second law is then a statement about the tendency of probability distributions to spread under weak coupling.

The deep consequence: thermodynamics is a special case of statistical inference. The maximum-entropy principle (Jaynes, 1957) recovers all of equilibrium thermodynamics by maximising Shannon entropy subject to constraints (mean energy, mean particle number, etc.). The Lagrange multipliers enforcing the constraints are identified as $1/T$ and $-\mu/T$ (chemical potential over temperature). This perspective is developed further in [11.02.01] (Maxwell-Boltzmann) and [11.04.01] (canonical ensemble).

The information-theoretic interpretation also resolves a puzzle about the third law. The third law states $S \to 0$ as $T \to 0$, but the Shannon entropy of any probability distribution with finite support is non-negative, and is zero only for a point mass (a single microstate with probability 1). The third law is therefore the statement that the ground state of a quantum system is unique (non-degenerate) — or more precisely, that any ground-state degeneracy grows sub-exponentially with system size, so $S/N \to 0$ as $N \to \infty$. Glasses and other disordered systems violate this condition, having a finite residual entropy $S(0) > 0$ (the "configurational entropy" of many metastable minima), which is a direct experimental signature of the non-uniqueness of the glass ground state. The third law thus connects the macroscopic concept of zero-point entropy to the microscopic question of ground-state degeneracy, and its violations (residual entropy in CO, ice, and spin glasses) are among the most informative probes of disorder in condensed matter.

## Connections [Master]

- **Conservation laws [09.01.03]** provide the first law in its most general form: energy conservation as a consequence of time-translation symmetry via Noether's theorem [09.03.01]. The first law of thermodynamics is the application of energy conservation to macroscopic systems with the energy budget split into heat and work. The distinction between heat and work is not fundamental — both are energy transfers — but it is operationally essential because heat (disordered energy transfer) and work (ordered energy transfer) have different entropy implications: heat transfer at temperature $T$ carries entropy $Q/T$, while work carries zero entropy.

- **Noether's theorem [09.03.01]** connects continuous symmetries to conserved quantities. Energy conservation from time-translation invariance is the classical-mechanics precursor to the first law. The second law, however, has no Noether-type derivation — it is an independent physical principle that does not follow from any symmetry of the Hamiltonian. This asymmetry is profound: the first law is a theorem of mechanics, while the second law is a theorem of statistics. The two laws have fundamentally different epistemic status, which is why thermodynamics feels different from the rest of physics.

- **Multivariable chain rule [02.05.03]** is the computational tool behind every Maxwell relation and every partial-derivative manipulation in thermodynamics. The chain rule on the Legendre-transformed potentials generates the four Maxwell relations mechanically.

- **Legendre transform [05.00.03]** is the mathematical operation producing each thermodynamic potential from $U$ by swapping an independent variable. The Legendre transform of $U(S, V)$ with respect to $S$ gives $F(T, V)$; with respect to $V$ gives $H(S, P)$; with respect to both gives $G(T, P)$.

- **Thermodynamic potentials [11.01.02]** (next unit) takes the potentials introduced here and develops their extremisation principles, stability analysis, and applications to phase transitions.

- **Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution [11.02.01]** derives the velocity distribution of an ideal gas from the entropy-maximisation principle, using $S = k_B \ln \Omega$ as its starting point. The Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution is the microscopic consequence of the second law applied to an ideal gas: the equilibrium distribution is the one that maximises entropy subject to the constraint of fixed total energy.

- **Canonical ensemble [11.04.01]** replaces the microstate counting $\Omega$ by a Boltzmann-weighted sum over states, generalising $S = k_B \ln \Omega$ to the Gibbs entropy $S = -k_B \sum p_i \ln p_i$.

- **Chemical thermodynamics [14.06.01]** applies the first and second laws to molecular-scale reactions: free energies, equilibrium constants, and the thermodynamic driving force of chemical change all descend from the definitions of $U$, $S$, $T$, and the potentials. The second law is what determines which reactions are spontaneous: at constant $(T, P)$, the reaction proceeds in the direction that lowers $G$, which balances enthalpy ($\Delta H$, an energy term from the first law) against entropy ($\Delta S$, from the second law).

- **Oxidative phosphorylation [17.04.02]** is a biological application: the proton-motive force across the inner mitochondrial membrane converts the free energy of the electrochemical gradient into ATP. The efficiency ceiling is set by the Carnot-like constraint that the free-energy drop per proton must exceed the free energy of ATP synthesis — a direct consequence of the second law. Biological energy transduction is fundamentally constrained by the same thermodynamic limits that govern steam engines.

- **Black hole thermodynamics** (Bekenstein 1973, Hawking 1975) assigns entropy $S = k_B A/(4\ell_P^2)$ to a black hole event horizon of area $A$, where $\ell_P = \sqrt{G\hbar/c^3}$ is the Planck length. The generalized second law states that the total entropy (matter plus black hole) never decreases. This connection between gravity, quantum mechanics, and thermodynamics is one of the deepest results in theoretical physics and suggests that thermodynamic principles may be more fundamental than either gravity or quantum mechanics individually.

- **Cosmology and the arrow of time.** The second law, applied to the universe as a whole, implies that the universe began in a state of very low entropy (the Big Bang) and has been evolving toward higher entropy ever since. The cosmological arrow of time — the fact that the universe expands rather than contracts — is linked to but distinct from the thermodynamic arrow. Roger Penrose estimated that the initial entropy of the universe was lower than its current entropy by a factor of approximately $10^{10^{123}}$, an extraordinarily fine-tuned initial condition that remains unexplained by any fundamental theory.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

**Carnot (1824)** laid the foundation in *Reflexions sur la puissance motrice du feu* without invoking energy conservation (which was not yet established). Carnot analysed heat engines using the caloric theory — heat as a fluid — and derived the efficiency ceiling that bears his name. His key insight was that the efficiency depends only on the temperature difference, not on the working substance. Carnot's work was initially obscure (published in a limited edition of 600 copies) and was brought to wider attention by Clapeyron's 1834 reformulation using indicator diagrams — the $P$-$V$ plots still used in thermodynamics textbooks today.

**Joule (1840s)** established the mechanical equivalent of heat through a series of meticulous experiments: falling weights turning paddle wheels in water, electrical heating of resistors, gas compression. The result — that 4.184 Joules of mechanical work produce exactly one calorie of heat — demolished the caloric theory and established energy conservation as a quantitative principle. Joule's experiments are a paradigm of careful measurement overturning theoretical prejudice.

**Clausius (1850)** reformulated Carnot's work in terms of the first law (energy conservation, newly established by Joule, Mayer, and Helmholtz) and introduced entropy as a state function in 1865. The name — from the Greek *trope* (transformation) — encoded the idea that entropy measures the "unavailable" energy, the portion that cannot be converted to work. Clausius's 1865 paper ended with the famous couplet: "The energy of the universe is constant. The entropy of the universe tends to a maximum." These two sentences are the first and second laws in their most sweeping form.

**Kelvin (1851)** independently arrived at the second law and established the absolute temperature scale. The Kelvin scale is defined by the Carnot efficiency: $T$ is the temperature on which $\eta = 1 - T_C/T_H$ holds, independent of any material property. Kelvin's insight was that the Carnot cycle provides a universal thermometer — one that does not depend on the properties of any particular substance (mercury, alcohol, or gas). This is why the Kelvin scale is the fundamental temperature scale in physics.

**Boltzmann (1877)** gave the statistical interpretation $S = k_B \ln \Omega$, connecting macroscopic entropy to microscopic combinatorics. This was controversial: Loschmidt's reversibility objection (1876) asked how time-reversible mechanics can produce irreversible entropy increase, and Zermelo's recurrence objection (1896) noted that Poincaré recurrence contradicts monotonic entropy increase. Boltzmann's response — that recurrence times are astronomically large and that entropy increase is statistical, not absolute — anticipated the modern resolution. The Boltzmann equation (1872), which derives the time evolution of the velocity distribution function, provides the bridge between microscopic reversibility and macroscopic irreversibility through the $H$-theorem: a quantity $H$ (related to negative entropy) decreases monotonically, provided molecular chaos (the Stosszahlansatz) is assumed. The philosophical debate over whether the Stosszahlansatz is a physical assumption or a logical necessity continues to this day.

**Caratheodory (1909)** gave the axiomatic formulation: the second law as an integrability condition on the heat 1-form. This removed the need for engines, cycles, and anthropomorphic notions of "what cannot happen," replacing them with differential geometry. Ehrenfest and Lieb & Yngvason (1999) further refined the axiomatic approach, establishing the second law as a consequence of a natural ordering relation on thermodynamic states.

**Shannon (1948)** and **Jaynes (1957)** completed the information-theoretic unification. Shannon entropy $H = -\sum p_i \ln p_i$ is the same function as thermodynamic entropy up to the factor $k_B$. Jaynes showed that maximising Shannon entropy subject to constraints recovers all equilibrium thermodynamics, making the second law a theorem of statistical inference rather than a physical axiom. Jaynes' insight was that the thermodynamic formalism is not specific to physics — it applies to any situation where one must assign probabilities under incomplete information.

The philosophical tension between the time-reversal symmetry of fundamental mechanics and the irreversibility of thermodynamics remains a central topic in the philosophy of physics. The dominant view (Boltzmann's statistical interpretation, refined by the Boltzmann equation and the BBGKY hierarchy) holds that irreversibility emerges from the special choice of low-entropy initial conditions — the "past hypothesis" (Albert, 2000) — rather than from any fundamental asymmetry in the laws. An alternative view, advocated by Prigogine and collaborators, holds that irreversibility is fundamental and that the time-symmetric equations of mechanics are an idealisation that breaks down for sufficiently complex systems. This debate has practical consequences: if irreversibility is merely apparent (a consequence of coarse-graining), then microscopic fluctuations should occasionally produce entropy decreases in small systems — a prediction confirmed by fluctuation theorems (Evans & Searles, 1994; Gallavotti & Cohen, 1995) that quantify the probability of transient second-law violations in small systems over short time scales.
