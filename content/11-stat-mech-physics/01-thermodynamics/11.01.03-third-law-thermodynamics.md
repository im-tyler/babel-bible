## Intuition [Beginner]

The first law says energy is conserved. The second law says entropy tends to increase. The third law says something about what happens at the coldest possible temperature.

As you cool a system toward absolute zero — temperature $T$ approaching zero — the entropy $S$ approaches a minimum value. Not just "a" minimum, but *the* minimum: a universal floor that you can set to zero by convention. The third law states that the entropy change in any process between equilibrium states vanishes as the temperature approaches zero.

In practical terms, you can never actually reach absolute zero. You can get closer and closer — current experiments reach below one nanokelvin — but every step toward zero temperature becomes harder. The third law guarantees this: cooling a system by any finite process requires an entropy change, and as $T$ approaches zero, no entropy change is available to drive the cooling.

Three consequences follow immediately. Heat capacities must go to zero at $T = 0$ (otherwise you could extract entropy at arbitrarily low temperature). Thermal expansion coefficients must go to zero (otherwise you could use mechanical work to create an entropy change). And the Gibbs and Helmholtz free energies become indistinguishable from the internal energy as $T \to 0$, because the $TS$ term vanishes.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a plot of entropy $S$ versus temperature $T$ for a pure substance. The curve starts at the origin $(T = 0, S = 0)$ — this is the third law's content — and rises monotonically. Near $T = 0$ the curve is very flat: the entropy barely increases because the heat capacity is vanishingly small. At higher temperatures the curve climbs more steeply.

Now overlay a second curve: the entropy of the same substance in a different phase or under a different pressure. Both curves start at $S = 0$ when $T = 0$. Their difference — the entropy change for the transition between the two states — squeezes to zero at the origin. The two curves may diverge at higher temperatures, but they converge at $T = 0$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take a solid at very low temperature. Experimentally, the heat capacity at constant volume $C_V$ follows the Debye $T^3$ law:

$$C_V \;=\; \frac{12\pi^4}{5}\, N k_B \left(\frac{T}{\Theta_D}\right)^3,$$

where $\Theta_D$ is the Debye temperature (a material constant). The entropy is the accumulated sum of $C_V / T$ over all temperatures from zero to $T$. Substituting the Debye form and carrying out the sum:

$$S(T) \;=\; \frac{4\pi^4}{5}\, N k_B \left(\frac{T}{\Theta_D}\right)^3.$$

As $T \to 0$, the entropy $S \to 0$ — exactly as the third law requires. The heat capacity also vanishes ($C_V \sim T^3 \to 0$), consistent with the consequence that no finite heat capacity can persist to absolute zero.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $S(T, X)$ denote the entropy of a thermodynamic system at temperature $T$ in equilibrium state $X$, where $X$ denotes the remaining thermodynamic variables (volume, pressure, magnetic field, etc.).

**Nernst heat theorem (1906).** For any two equilibrium states $X_1$ and $X_2$ of a system in internal equilibrium,

$$\lim_{T \to 0^+} \left[S(T, X_2) - S(T, X_1)\right] \;=\; 0.$$

**Planck's stronger form (1911).** The entropy of every pure substance in internal equilibrium approaches zero as $T \to 0$:

$$\lim_{T \to 0^+} S(T, X) \;=\; 0.$$

Planck's form sets a universal entropy scale. The absolute entropy of a substance at temperature $T$ is then

$$S(T) \;=\; \int_0^T \frac{C(T')}{T'}\, dT',$$

where $C$ is the appropriate heat capacity and the integral is evaluated along a reversible path from $T = 0$ to $T$. The convergence of this integral requires $C(T) \to 0$ as $T \to 0$ — itself a consequence of the third law.

### Microscopic interpretation

The Boltzmann entropy $S = k_B \ln \Omega$ connects the third law to the ground-state degeneracy $\Omega_0$ of a quantum system. The third law requires $\Omega_0$ to be small — specifically, $\ln \Omega_0 / N \to 0$ as $N \to \infty$ for a system of $N$ particles. A non-degenerate ground state ($\Omega_0 = 1$, $S = 0$) satisfies this exactly. A ground state with a degeneracy that grows sub-exponentially in $N$ also satisfies it.

Systems that violate the strong form — so-called *frustrated* systems — have a residual entropy $S_0 > 0$ at $T = 0$. The classic example is ice, where proton disorder on the hydrogen-bond network produces $\Omega_0 \sim (3/2)^N$, giving $S_0 \approx N k_B \ln(3/2)$. Such systems do not violate the Nernst form (entropy differences still vanish at $T = 0$ for processes between states of the same material), but they violate Planck's stronger statement.

## Key derivation: Consequences of the third law [Intermediate+]

**Proposition.** The third law implies the following thermodynamic consequences:

1. **Vanishing heat capacities.** $C_V \to 0$ and $C_P \to 0$ as $T \to 0$.

2. **Vanishing thermal expansion.** $\alpha_V := V^{-1}(\partial V / \partial T)_P \to 0$ as $T \to 0$.

3. **Equivalence of free energies.** $G - H \to 0$ as $T \to 0$ (where $G$ is the Gibbs free energy and $H$ is the enthalpy).

**Proof of (1).** The entropy at constant volume is $S(T, V) = \int_0^T C_V(T', V) / T'\, dT'$. By the third law this integral converges. For the integral $\int_0^T (C_V / T') dT'$ to converge at its lower limit, $C_V / T'$ must be integrable near $T = 0$. If $C_V$ approached a non-zero constant $c$ as $T \to 0$, the integrand would behave as $c/T'$ near $T' = 0$, giving a logarithmic divergence. Hence $C_V \to 0$.

More precisely, the Debye model and experiment show $C_V \sim T^3$ for insulators and $C_V \sim T$ for metals at low $T$. Both vanish at $T = 0$.

**Proof of (2).** The Maxwell relation $(\partial S / \partial P)_T = -(\partial V / \partial T)_P$ gives $(\partial S / \partial P)_T = -V \alpha_V$. By the third law, $(\partial S / \partial P)_T \to 0$ as $T \to 0$ (since $S$ itself approaches a pressure-independent constant). Therefore $V \alpha_V \to 0$, and since $V$ remains finite, $\alpha_V \to 0$.

**Proof of (3).** The Gibbs free energy is $G = H - TS$. As $T \to 0$, the product $TS \to 0$ provided $S$ is bounded. Since $S \to 0$ by the third law and $S$ is continuous for systems in internal equilibrium, $TS \to 0$ and $G \to H$.

**Bridge.** The third law builds toward the low-temperature behaviour of quantum statistical systems — Bose-Einstein condensation [11.05.04] and the Fermi gas [11.05.05] — where the heat capacity vanishes as $T \to 0$ in precise quantitative agreement with these thermodynamic consequences. The key insight is that the third law is not merely a convention about entropy zero-points but a substantive physical constraint that appears again in the microscopic theory: the ground state of a quantum system has a degeneracy that does not grow exponentially with $N$, and this is dual to the thermodynamic statement that $C_V \to 0$. The foundational reason is that quantum mechanics quantises the energy levels, creating a gap between the ground state and the first excited state that suppresses entropy at low temperature.

A metal has low-temperature heat capacity $C_V = \gamma T + aT^3$, where $\gamma$ is the electronic specific-heat coefficient and the $T^3$ term is the lattice (Debye) contribution. Compute $S(T)$ and show both terms satisfy the third law.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$S(T) = \int_0^T \frac{\gamma T' + aT'^3}{T'} dT' = \int_0^T (\gamma + aT'^2)\, dT' = \gamma T + \frac{aT^3}{3}$.

Both the electronic term $\gamma T$ and the lattice term $aT^3/3$ vanish as $T \to 0$. The electronic linear term reflects the Fermi-Dirac statistics of conduction electrons [11.05.05]; the lattice cubic term reflects the Debye phonon spectrum [11.05.06].

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the Gibbs free energy and the Helmholtz free energy become equal at $T = 0$ for a system satisfying the third law.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $G = F + PV$ and the Maxwell relation to show $PV \to 0$ is not required; instead use $G = H - TS$ and $F = U - TS$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

We have $G = H - TS$ and $F = U - TS$. Their difference is $G - F = H - U = PV$. As $T \to 0$, both $G$ and $F$ approach their respective internal energies (since $TS \to 0$), but the difference $G - F = PV$ does not generally vanish. However, the *thermodynamic potentials* coincide in the sense that $G(T=0) = H(T=0)$ and $F(T=0) = U(T=0)$. The key point is that the $TS$ correction disappears, making the free energies equal to the corresponding energies at absolute zero.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Using the Maxwell relation $(\partial S / \partial V)_T = (\partial P / \partial T)_V$, show that the third law implies the pressure coefficient $(\partial P / \partial T)_V \to 0$ as $T \to 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The third law constrains how $S$ changes with $V$ at fixed $T$ as $T \to 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the third law, $S(T, V) \to 0$ for all $V$ as $T \to 0$. Therefore $(\partial S / \partial V)_T \to 0$ as $T \to 0$ (since $S$ is approaching a constant — zero — independent of $V$). By the Maxwell relation, $(\partial P / \partial T)_V = (\partial S / \partial V)_T \to 0$. This means the isochore $P(T, V)$ becomes flat as $T \to 0$: pressure ceases to depend on temperature at very low $T$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the unattainability principle: show that no finite number of adiabatic demagnetisation steps can cool a paramagnetic salt to $T = 0$. Assume the entropy at applied field $B$ and temperature $T$ satisfies $S(B, T) = S(0, T')$ for an adiabatic process connecting $(B, T)$ to $(0, T')$, and use the third law.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

An adiabatic process conserves entropy. Sketch the $S(T)$ curves for $B = 0$ and $B > 0$ and show they both approach $S = 0$ as $T \to 0$, so no finite step can reach $T = 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In adiabatic demagnetisation, the system starts at $(B_1, T_1)$ and is adiabatically (isentropically) reduced to $B_2 = 0$ at temperature $T_2$. Conservation of entropy gives $S(B_1, T_1) = S(0, T_2)$.

By the third law, $S(0, T) \to 0$ only as $T \to 0$. For the final temperature to be $T_2 = 0$, we need $S(B_1, T_1) = S(0, 0) = 0$. But $S(B_1, T_1) > 0$ for any $T_1 > 0$ (since entropy is monotonically increasing in $T$). So $T_2 > 0$ always.

Repeating the process: start at $(B_2, T_2)$ with $T_2 < T_1$ but $T_2 > 0$. Each step reduces $T$ but never to zero, because the entropy curves for $B > 0$ and $B = 0$ both approach zero only asymptotically. The temperature after $n$ steps satisfies $T_n > 0$ for all finite $n$. Absolute zero is unattainable by any finite number of steps — this is the unattainability statement of the third law.

</details>

</aside>

Ice Ih has a residual entropy $S_0 = N k_B \ln(3/2)$ due to proton disorder (Pauling's estimate). Show that this violates Planck's form of the third law but not the Nernst heat theorem. Compute $S_0$ in J/mol/K for one mole of ice.

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

A two-level system has energy levels $E_0 = 0$ and $E_1 = \epsilon$ with degeneracies $g_0$ and $g_1$ respectively. Compute the entropy at temperature $T$ and find the condition on $g_0$ and $g_1$ for the third law to hold (in Planck's form).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the partition function, then $S = k_B (\ln Z + \beta \langle E \rangle)$, and take $T \to 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The partition function is $Z = g_0 + g_1 e^{-\beta\epsilon}$. The average energy is $\langle E \rangle = g_1 \epsilon\, e^{-\beta\epsilon} / Z$. The entropy is

$$S = k_B\left[\ln Z + \frac{\beta g_1 \epsilon\, e^{-\beta\epsilon}}{Z}\right].$$

As $T \to 0$ ($\beta \to \infty$): $Z \to g_0$, $\langle E \rangle \to 0$, and $S \to k_B \ln g_0$. Planck's form ($S \to 0$) requires $g_0 = 1$: the ground state must be non-degenerate. If $g_0 > 1$, the system has residual entropy $S_0 = k_B \ln g_0$ at $T = 0$ — a violation of Planck's form but not necessarily of the Nernst theorem (since different macroscopic states may share the same ground-state degeneracy, making entropy differences vanish).

For the third law to hold in full generality for all substances, the overwhelming majority of pure crystalline solids have $g_0 = 1$. Frustrated systems with $g_0 > 1$ are the exception.

</details>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The third law has no formalization in Mathlib. The core mathematical content that would need to be formalised is:

1. A type `ThermodynamicState` carrying continuous functions $S$, $C_V$, $T$, $P$, $V$ satisfying thermodynamic identities (Maxwell relations, Euler relations).
2. The limit statement: for any two states in internal equilibrium, $\lim_{T \to 0^+} |S(T, X_1) - S(T, X_2)| = 0$.
3. Derived consequences: $C_V(T) \to 0$, $\alpha_V(T) \to 0$.
4. The unattainability principle as an impossibility result: no finite sequence of adiabatic processes reaches $T = 0$.

The obstruction is not deep mathematics but the absence of a thermodynamic-state infrastructure in Mathlib. Once such a structure exists with the appropriate continuity and monotonicity assumptions, the proofs are elementary limit arguments.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Simon's formulation and the role of internal equilibrium

The third law applies only to systems in *internal equilibrium*. A glass — a supercooled liquid frozen in a non-equilibrium configuration — can have a residual entropy at $T = 0$ that depends on its preparation history. Simon (1937) clarified that the third law applies to systems that have reached their thermodynamic equilibrium state, not to metastable or frozen non-equilibrium configurations.

This distinction matters operationally. The third law is sometimes stated as: *the entropy of a system in internal equilibrium is zero at absolute zero*. A glass is not in internal equilibrium, so its residual entropy does not constitute a violation. The rigorous formulation restricts attention to states on the equilibrium manifold — the set of states accessible by reversible (quasi-static) processes.

### Negative temperatures and the third law

Systems with bounded energy spectra (e.g., nuclear spins in a magnetic field) can realise *negative* absolute temperatures. At negative $T$, the population of the higher-energy state exceeds that of the lower-energy state. Entropy at negative $T$ is higher than at any positive $T$, and the third law still holds: as $T \to 0^+$, $S \to 0$. The passage from positive to negative $T$ goes through $T = \pm \infty$ (maximum entropy), not through $T = 0$. Negative temperatures do not violate the third law because they are *hotter* than any positive temperature — the system has more energy, not less.

### The third law in quantum statistics

For a quantum system with Hamiltonian $\hat{H}$ and energy eigenvalues $E_0 < E_1 \leq E_2 \leq \ldots$, the canonical partition function at inverse temperature $\beta$ is

$$Z(\beta) = \sum_n g_n\, e^{-\beta E_n},$$

where $g_n$ is the degeneracy of level $n$. The entropy is $S = k_B(\ln Z + \beta \langle E \rangle)$. As $T \to 0$, $\langle E \rangle \to E_0$ and $Z \to g_0\, e^{-\beta E_0}$, so

$$S \;\to\; k_B \ln g_0.$$

The third law (Planck form) holds if and only if $g_0 = 1$. For a system with a non-degenerate ground state and an energy gap $\Delta = E_1 - E_0 > 0$, the approach to zero entropy is exponentially fast: $S \sim g_1/g_0 \cdot e^{-\beta\Delta}$ for $k_B T \ll \Delta$.

For a gapless system ($\Delta = 0$, a continuum of excitations starting at $E_0$), the approach to zero entropy depends on the density of states. A phonon spectrum with $\rho(\epsilon) \sim \epsilon^2$ in three dimensions gives $S \sim T^3$; a photon spectrum with $\rho(\epsilon) \sim \epsilon^2$ gives $S \sim T^3$ as well. In both cases $S \to 0$, but the rate is power-law rather than exponential.

### Residual entropy and frustration

A *frustrated* system has competing interactions that cannot all be satisfied simultaneously. The classic examples are:

- **Spin ice** (Dy$_2$Ti$_2$O$_7$, Ho$_2$Ti$_2$O$_7$): magnetic moments on a pyrochlore lattice obey "two-in, two-out" ice rules, producing a residual entropy identical in form to that of water ice.
- **Geometrically frustrated antiferromagnets**: triangular or kagome lattice antiferromagnets where no spin configuration satisfies all anti-alignment constraints.
- **Structural glasses**: configurational disorder frozen in at the glass transition temperature $T_g$, with the residual entropy depending on the cooling rate.

These systems illustrate the boundary of the third law's applicability: the Nernst form ($\Delta S \to 0$) typically still holds, but Planck's absolute zero entropy fails because the ground-state manifold has macroscopic degeneracy.

**Synthesis.** The third law sits at the boundary between classical thermodynamics and quantum mechanics — it is the bridge from the macroscopic entropy concept to the microscopic ground-state structure. The central insight is that the third law encodes the quantum mechanical quantisation of energy: the gap between ground and first excited state is what forces entropy to zero at $T = 0$. This is dual to the statement that heat capacities vanish, which appears again in the Debye model [11.05.06] and the Fermi gas [11.05.05]. The foundational reason the third law holds for most (but not all) physical systems is that quantum mechanics provides a non-degenerate ground state separated by a finite gap from excited states; frustrated systems with degenerate ground states represent the exceptional case where the generalisation of the third law must be refined. Putting these together, the third law is the thermodynamic shadow of quantum mechanics: it is exactly the macroscopic manifestation of the quantum energy gap.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Simon's formulation).** *For any system in internal equilibrium, the entropy difference $\Delta S$ between any two equilibrium states at the same temperature vanishes as $T \to 0$. Equivalently, the isotherms in the $S$-$X$ plane (where $X$ denotes all other thermodynamic variables) converge as $T \to 0$.*

**Proof.** Let $S_1(T)$ and $S_2(T)$ be the entropies of two equilibrium states at temperature $T$. By the second law, any reversible isothermal process connecting the two states at temperature $T$ has entropy change $\Delta S(T) = S_2(T) - S_1(T)$. The heat absorbed is $Q = T \Delta S(T)$.

By the third law (Nernst form), $\lim_{T \to 0} \Delta S(T) = 0$. Hence $Q = T \Delta S(T) \to 0$ as well (since $T \to 0$ and $\Delta S$ is bounded). This means no isothermal process at $T = 0$ can transfer heat — the system is thermally inert at absolute zero.

For the stronger (Planck) form: if the ground state is unique ($g_0 = 1$), then $S(T) = \int_0^T C/T'\, dT'$ and the integral converges. Conversely, if the integral converges for every reversible path from $T = 0$, then $S(0)$ is well-defined and can be set to zero by convention. The convergence of $\int_0^T C/T'\, dT'$ is equivalent to $C(T)/T$ being integrable near $T = 0$, which requires $C(T) \to 0$ faster than $1/|\ln T|$ (a very weak constraint). In practice, all known materials have $C \sim T^n$ for $n \geq 1$ at low $T$, far stronger than the minimum requirement. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **[11.05.05]** The Fermi gas at low temperature has $C_V \sim T$, satisfying the third law with the linear coefficient $\gamma$ providing direct information about the density of states at the Fermi level.
- **[11.05.06]** The Debye model gives $C_V \sim T^3$ at low $T$, and the Debye temperature $\Theta_D$ governs the rate at which entropy approaches zero — the third law constrains the phonon density of states at low energy.
- **[11.06.01]** The ferromagnetic Ising model has a non-degenerate ground state (all spins up or all spins down, related by symmetry) with $S(T=0) = 0$, satisfying the third law; frustrated spin models provide counterexamples.
- **[11.01.02]** Thermodynamic potentials and Legendre transforms relate the third law's consequences to the behaviour of $G$, $F$, and $H$ at low temperature — all coincide at $T = 0$.
- **[11.04.01]** The canonical ensemble partition function $Z = \sum_n g_n e^{-\beta E_n}$ gives the microscopic mechanism: the ground-state degeneracy $g_0$ controls whether $S \to k_B \ln g_0$ or $S \to 0$.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Walther Nernst proposed the heat theorem in 1906 on the basis of extensive measurements of chemical equilibria at low temperatures. His original formulation was thermodynamic: the entropy change in any chemical reaction vanishes as $T \to 0$. Planck (1911) strengthened this to the statement that every pure substance has zero entropy at $T = 0$, giving an absolute entropy scale.

The third law occupies a curious position in thermodynamics. Unlike the first and second laws, which are empirical generalisations with no known exceptions, the third law has known exceptions (frustrated systems, glasses). Its validity is tied to quantum mechanics: a quantum system with a non-degenerate ground state automatically satisfies it, while a classical system — with a continuous phase space — has no mechanism to force $S \to 0$.

Einstein (1914) noted that the third law is essentially a quantum-mechanical statement about the behaviour of matter at low temperatures, not a purely thermodynamic law. This perspective was formalised by Simon (1930s), who distinguished between the unrestricted third law (which fails for glasses and frustrated systems) and the restricted third law (which applies only to systems in internal equilibrium and holds universally).

The unattainability principle — that $T = 0$ cannot be reached by any finite process — was shown by Belgiorno (2003) to be logically independent of the Nernst heat theorem under certain conditions, though in standard thermodynamics they are generally treated as equivalent.
