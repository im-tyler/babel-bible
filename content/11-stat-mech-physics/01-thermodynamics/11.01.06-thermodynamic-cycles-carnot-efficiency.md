## Intuition [Beginner]

A heat engine converts thermal energy into mechanical work. It absorbs heat from a hot reservoir, converts some of it to work, and dumps the rest into a cold reservoir. No engine can convert all the absorbed heat into work — some heat must always be wasted. The **efficiency** $\eta$ is the fraction of absorbed heat that becomes work.

The **Carnot cycle** is the most efficient possible engine operating between two temperatures. It uses only reversible steps (slow, quasi-static processes), and its efficiency depends only on the two temperatures:

$$\eta_{\rm Carnot} = 1 - \frac{T_c}{T_h}.$$

A Carnot engine working between $T_h = 500$ K and $T_c = 300$ K has efficiency $1 - 300/500 = 40\%$. No real engine can beat this. Real engines (Otto, Diesel) use irreversible steps (rapid combustion, sudden expansion) and are less efficient, but they produce more power per cycle.

## Visual [Beginner]

The Carnot cycle on a $P$-$V$ diagram consists of four steps: (1) isothermal expansion at $T_h$ (absorbing heat), (2) adiabatic expansion (cooling from $T_h$ to $T_c$), (3) isothermal compression at $T_c$ (dumping heat), (4) adiabatic compression (heating from $T_c$ to $T_h$). The cycle forms a closed loop; the enclosed area is the net work done per cycle.

![PV diagram of the Carnot cycle: two isotherms (at T_h and T_c) connected by two adiabats. The enclosed area equals the net work W = Q_h - Q_c. The Otto cycle is shown for comparison: two isochors (constant volume) connected by two adiabats, forming a taller but thinner loop.](/img/11.01.06-carnot-otto-cycles-placeholder.svg)

## Worked example [Beginner]

The **Otto cycle** (model of a petrol engine) has four steps: (1) adiabatic compression ($V_1 \to V_2$), (2) constant-volume heat addition ($V_2$, combustion), (3) adiabatic expansion ($V_2 \to V_1$, power stroke), (4) constant-volume heat rejection ($V_1$, exhaust). The compression ratio is $r = V_1/V_2$. For an ideal gas with heat capacity ratio $\gamma$:

$$\eta_{\rm Otto} = 1 - \frac{1}{r^{\gamma - 1}}.$$

For $r = 10$ and $\gamma = 1.4$ (air): $\eta = 1 - 1/10^{0.4} = 1 - 0.398 = 60.2\%$. Real petrol engines achieve about 25–35% due to friction, heat loss, and non-instantaneous combustion.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Carnot cycle

The Carnot cycle consists of two reversible isothermal processes and two reversible adiabatic processes:

1. **Isothermal expansion** at $T_h$: absorb heat $Q_h$ from hot reservoir.
2. **Adiabatic expansion**: temperature drops from $T_h$ to $T_c$.
3. **Isothermal compression** at $T_c$: reject heat $Q_c$ to cold reservoir.
4. **Adiabatic compression**: temperature rises from $T_c$ to $T_h$.

For an ideal gas, the work done in each step can be computed explicitly. The net work is $W = Q_h - Q_c$ and the efficiency is:

$$\eta_{\rm Carnot} = \frac{W}{Q_h} = 1 - \frac{Q_c}{Q_h} = 1 - \frac{T_c}{T_h}.$$

The equality $Q_c/Q_h = T_c/T_h$ follows from the fact that entropy change is zero over a complete reversible cycle: $Q_h/T_h - Q_c/T_c = 0$.

### Clausius inequality and entropy production

For any cyclic process (reversible or irreversible):

$$\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} \leq 0.$$

For an irreversible cycle, the entropy change of the universe (system plus reservoirs) is positive:

$$\Delta S_{\rm universe} = -\frac{Q_h}{T_h} + \frac{Q_c}{T_c} \geq 0.$$

The **entropy production** is $\sigma = \Delta S_{\rm universe} \geq 0$, with $\sigma = 0$ only for reversible processes.

### Key result: Carnot theorem

**Theorem (Carnot).** *No heat engine operating between two temperatures $T_h$ and $T_c$ can have efficiency greater than $1 - T_c/T_h$. Equality holds only for a reversible engine.*

The proof uses the second law: if an engine had $\eta > \eta_{\rm Carnot}$, you could couple it with a Carnot refrigerator to produce a perpetual-motion machine of the second kind (extracting net work from a single temperature).

**Bridge.** The Carnot theorem builds toward the theory of irreversible thermodynamics [11.02.04], where the entropy production rate is related to the fluxes (heat flow, particle flow) and forces (temperature gradients, chemical potential gradients) by linear response coefficients. This is exactly the content of the Onsager reciprocal relations. The foundational reason the Carnot efficiency is a universal bound is that it follows from the second law alone, independent of the working substance; putting these together with the fluctuation-dissipation theorem [11.02.04], irreversibility and entropy production are the macroscopic shadows of microscopic fluctuations.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

A Carnot refrigerator operates between $T_c = 260$ K (inside the fridge) and $T_h = 310$ K (room). Compute the coefficient of performance COP $= Q_c/W$.

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

COP $= T_c/(T_h - T_c) = 260/50 = 5.2$. For every joule of work input, the fridge removes 5.2 J of heat from its interior. This is why refrigerators are efficient: they move more heat than the work they consume.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (medium, symbolic).**

Derive the Diesel cycle efficiency $\eta = 1 - (1/r^{\gamma-1})[\rho^\gamma - 1]/[\gamma(\rho - 1)]$ where $r$ is the compression ratio and $\rho$ is the cutoff ratio (volume at end of combustion divided by volume at start).

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

The Diesel cycle: (1) adiabatic compression $1 \to 2$ ($V_1/V_2 = r$), (2) constant-pressure heat addition $2 \to 3$ ($V_3/V_2 = \rho$), (3) adiabatic expansion $3 \to 4$ ($V_4 = V_1$), (4) constant-volume heat rejection $4 \to 1$.

Heat added: $Q_{\rm in} = nC_P(T_3 - T_2)$. Heat rejected: $Q_{\rm out} = nC_V(T_4 - T_1)$.

Using the adiabatic relations: $T_2 = T_1 r^{\gamma-1}$, $T_3 = T_2 \rho = T_1 r^{\gamma-1}\rho$, $T_4 = T_3(V_3/V_4)^{\gamma-1} = T_1 r^{\gamma-1}\rho(\rho/r)^{\gamma-1} = T_1 \rho^\gamma$.

$\eta = 1 - Q_{\rm out}/Q_{\rm in} = 1 - C_V(T_1\rho^\gamma - T_1)/[C_P(T_1 r^{\gamma-1}\rho - T_1 r^{\gamma-1})] = 1 - [\rho^\gamma - 1]/[\gamma r^{\gamma-1}(\rho - 1)]$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

A steam power plant operates between $T_h = 800$ K and $T_c = 300$ K. The actual efficiency is 35%. Compute the second-law efficiency (ratio of actual to Carnot efficiency).

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

Carnot efficiency: $\eta_{\rm Carnot} = 1 - 300/800 = 0.625 = 62.5\%$. Second-law efficiency: $\eta_{\rm II} = 0.35/0.625 = 0.56 = 56\%$. The plant achieves 56% of the theoretical maximum. The remaining 44% of the Carnot potential is lost to irreversibilities (friction, heat transfer across finite temperature differences, combustion losses).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Compute the entropy produced per cycle by an irreversible engine that absorbs $Q_h = 1000$ J from $T_h = 500$ K, rejects $Q_c = 700$ J to $T_c = 300$ K, and produces $W = 300$ J of work.

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

Entropy production: $\sigma = -Q_h/T_h + Q_c/T_c = -1000/500 + 700/300 = -2 + 2.333 = 0.333$ J/K per cycle.

Check: $W = Q_h - Q_c = 300$ J (consistent). Efficiency: $\eta = 300/1000 = 30\%$. Carnot: $1 - 300/500 = 40\%$. The actual engine is less efficient, and the entropy production quantifies the gap.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the Carnot theorem using the second law: show that no engine operating between $T_h$ and $T_c$ can exceed $\eta_{\rm Carnot} = 1 - T_c/T_h$.

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose engine X has $\eta_X > \eta_{\rm Carnot}$. Then $W_X/Q_h > 1 - T_c/T_h$, so $Q_c^X = Q_h - W_X < Q_h T_c/T_h$.

Now couple engine X with a Carnot refrigerator C operating between the same two reservoirs. Engine X produces work $W_X$ which drives the refrigerator. The Carnot refrigerator absorbs $Q_c^C$ from the cold reservoir and dumps $Q_h^C = Q_c^C + W_X$ to the hot reservoir, with $Q_h^C/T_h = Q_c^C/T_c$.

Net heat extracted from the cold reservoir: $Q_c^C - Q_c^X > 0$ (since $Q_c^X < Q_h T_c/T_h = Q_c^{\rm Carnot}$ and the Carnot refrigerator removes more). Net heat deposited in the hot reservoir: $Q_h^C - Q_h$.

The combined system extracts heat from the cold reservoir and deposits it in the hot reservoir using no external work — a refrigerator requiring no energy input. This violates the Clausius statement of the second law (heat cannot spontaneously flow from cold to hot). Contradiction. Therefore $\eta_X \leq \eta_{\rm Carnot}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

An ideal gas undergoes a throttling (Joule-Thomson) expansion from $(P_1, T_1)$ to $(P_2, T_2)$ through a porous plug. Show that enthalpy is conserved ($H_1 = H_2$) and compute the entropy production $\Delta S = C_P\ln(T_2/T_1) - R\ln(P_2/P_1)$.

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

Throttling is a steady-flow process with no heat exchange ($Q = 0$) and no work ($W = 0$). The first law for steady flow gives $H_1 = H_2$ (enthalpy conservation). For an ideal gas, $H$ depends only on $T$, so $T_1 = T_2$ (no temperature change for an ideal gas).

The entropy change: $\Delta S = nC_P\ln(T_2/T_1) - nR\ln(P_2/P_1)$. For an ideal gas ($T_2 = T_1$): $\Delta S = -nR\ln(P_2/P_1) > 0$ since $P_2 < P_1$. The process is irreversible — entropy is produced even though enthalpy is conserved.

For a real gas (van der Waals), $H$ depends on both $T$ and $P$, so throttling does change the temperature. The Joule-Thomson coefficient $\mu_{JT} = (\partial T/\partial P)_H = (1/C_P)[T(\partial V/\partial T)_P - V]$ determines whether the gas cools ($\mu_{JT} > 0$) or heats ($\mu_{JT} < 0$).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The Curzon-Ahlborn efficiency $\eta_{CA} = 1 - \sqrt{T_c/T_h}$ describes the efficiency of an endoreversible engine at maximum power output. Show that $\eta_{CA} < \eta_{\rm Carnot}$ and compute both for $T_h = 1000$ K, $T_c = 300$ K.

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

$\eta_{\rm Carnot} = 1 - 300/1000 = 0.70 = 70\%$. $\eta_{CA} = 1 - \sqrt{0.3} = 1 - 0.548 = 0.452 = 45.2\%$.

$\eta_{CA} < \eta_{\rm Carnot}$ because $\sqrt{T_c/T_h} > T_c/T_h$ for $T_c < T_h$ (the square root of a fraction is larger than the fraction itself).

The Curzon-Ahlborn result assumes finite-rate heat transfer between the working fluid and the reservoirs. The Carnot efficiency is achieved only for zero power output (infinitely slow operation). At maximum power, the efficiency drops to $\eta_{CA}$, which agrees well with real power plant efficiencies (typically 35–50%).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

For a general cyclic process with working substance at temperatures $T(t)$ that varies over the cycle, show that the entropy production per cycle is $\sigma = \oint (1/T_{\rm res}(t) - 1/T(t))\,\delta Q \geq 0$ where $T_{\rm res}(t)$ is the reservoir temperature.

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

The entropy change of the working substance over a complete cycle is zero (it returns to its initial state): $\Delta S_{\rm system} = 0$. The entropy change of the reservoirs is $\Delta S_{\rm res} = -\oint \delta Q / T_{\rm res}$. The total entropy production is:

$\sigma = \Delta S_{\rm system} + \Delta S_{\rm res} = -\oint \frac{\delta Q}{T_{\rm res}}$.

Now, the entropy change of the working substance per cycle is also $\Delta S_{\rm system} = \oint \delta Q / T + \sigma_{\rm int} = 0$, where $\sigma_{\rm int} \geq 0$ is the internal entropy production from irreversibilities within the working substance.

Combining: $\sigma = \oint \frac{\delta Q}{T} - \oint \frac{\delta Q}{T_{\rm res}} = \oint \left(\frac{1}{T} - \frac{1}{T_{\rm res}}\right)\delta Q \geq 0$.

This is the general Clausius inequality. For a reversible process, $T = T_{\rm res}$ at every point, so $\sigma = 0$. For an irreversible process, $\sigma > 0$.

</details>

</aside>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The Carnot theorem is a consequence of the second law and the definition of thermodynamic temperature. Formalising it requires a thermodynamic-cycle framework with path integrals for heat and work, a definition of efficiency, and a proof that no cycle exceeds the Carnot bound. The mathematical content is elementary (algebraic manipulation of heat and work integrals), but the physical framework (distinguishing reversible from irreversible processes) requires domain-specific infrastructure.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Endoreversible thermodynamics

The Curzon-Ahlborn model (1975) treats the engine as internally reversible but with finite-rate heat transfer to the reservoirs. The working fluid temperatures are $T_{hw}$ (hot side) and $T_{cw}$ (cold side), with heat flow rates $\dot{Q}_h = \alpha(T_h - T_{hw})$ and $\dot{Q}_c = \beta(T_{cw} - T_c)$. Maximising power output gives $\eta_{CA} = 1 - \sqrt{T_c/T_h}$, which agrees well with real power plants.

### Entropy production minimisation

Bejan (1996) proposed entropy generation minimisation as a design principle for thermal systems. The entropy production rate $\dot{\sigma}$ quantifies the thermodynamic losses in a system. Minimising $\dot{\sigma}$ at fixed power output is equivalent to maximising the second-law efficiency. This approach is used in the design of heat exchangers, cooling systems, and chemical reactors.

### Stirling and Ericsson cycles

The Stirling cycle (two isotherms connected by two isochors, with regenerative heat storage) and the Ericsson cycle (two isotherms connected by two isobars, with regeneration) both achieve Carnot efficiency when the regeneration is perfect. In practice, imperfect regeneration reduces their efficiency below Carnot, but both offer advantages in specific applications (Stirling engines for solar thermal power, Ericsson cycles for gas turbines with intercooling and reheat).

**Synthesis.** Thermodynamic cycles build toward the microscopic theory of irreversibility [11.02.03, 11.02.04], where entropy production is related to the collision operator in the Boltzmann equation and the fluctuation-dissipation theorem. The central insight is that the Carnot efficiency is a universal bound that follows from the second law alone, independent of the working substance or the cycle details. This is dual to the microscopic irreversibility encoded in the H-theorem: entropy production is positive for any irreversible process, whether it is a macroscopic engine cycle or a microscopic collision cascade. The foundational reason the Carnot bound holds is that heat flows from hot to cold, and extracting work from a single temperature would violate this directionality. Putting these together, the Carnot cycle is the idealisation that separates the thermodynamic content (the universal efficiency bound) from the engineering details (the specific cycle shape), and this separation generalises to the Onsager reciprocal relations [11.02.04] where the linear-response coefficients of all irreversible processes are constrained by symmetry.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition.** *The efficiency of any reversible engine operating between two reservoirs at $T_h$ and $T_c$ is $\eta = 1 - T_c/T_h$, independent of the working substance.*

**Proof.** For a reversible engine, the total entropy change over one cycle is zero. The engine absorbs $Q_h$ from $T_h$ and rejects $Q_c$ to $T_c$. The entropy change of the hot reservoir is $-Q_h/T_h$ and of the cold reservoir is $Q_c/T_c$. The entropy change of the working substance is zero (cyclic process). So:

$$\frac{Q_c}{T_c} - \frac{Q_h}{T_h} = 0 \implies \frac{Q_c}{Q_h} = \frac{T_c}{T_h}.$$

Efficiency: $\eta = 1 - Q_c/Q_h = 1 - T_c/T_h$. The result depends only on the reservoir temperatures, not on the working substance. Any two reversible engines operating between the same temperatures have the same efficiency. $\square$

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Sadi Carnot published his *Reflexions sur la puissance motrice du feu* in 1824, before the concept of energy (first law) was formalised. His insight was that the efficiency of a heat engine depends only on the temperature difference, not on the working substance — a result he derived using the caloric theory of heat. Clausius (1850) and Thomson (Kelvin, 1851) rederived Carnot's result using the first and second laws, establishing the absolute temperature scale and the concept of entropy.

The Otto cycle (Nikolaus Otto, 1876) and Diesel cycle (Rudolf Diesel, 1892) model the thermodynamic processes in real internal combustion engines. Neither achieves Carnot efficiency because they involve irreversible processes (rapid combustion, finite-rate heat transfer), but they produce far more power per cycle than a Carnot engine operating at the same temperatures.
