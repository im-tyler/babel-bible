## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine a gas whose molecules are not uniformly distributed — say, there is a hot spot on the left and a cold spot on the right. Molecules carry energy, momentum, and particles as they move and collide. The **Boltzmann equation** describes how the distribution of molecular velocities $f(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{v}, t)$ evolves in time as molecules stream through space and collide with each other.

The equation has two parts. The **streaming term** accounts for molecules moving freely: a molecule at position $\mathbf{r}$ with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ will be at position $\mathbf{r} + \mathbf{v}dt$ a moment later. The **collision term** accounts for velocity changes due to binary collisions: when two molecules collide, they exchange momentum and energy, changing their velocities.

Boltzmann's great insight was the **H-theorem**: he defined a quantity H based on the distribution function f (weighted by its own logarithm, averaged over all velocities) and showed that H always decreases over time. The distribution f always evolves toward the equilibrium Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution, which minimises H. This was the first microscopic derivation of the second law of thermodynamics — and it sparked a debate about irreversibility that lasted decades.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture a cloud of gas molecules with a velocity distribution that is initially non-equilibrium (e.g., two streams of molecules moving in opposite directions). As time passes, collisions between molecules randomise the velocities, and the distribution relaxes toward the Maxwell-Boltzmann form (a Gaussian in each velocity component). The H-function decreases monotonically, like a ball rolling down a hill toward the equilibrium valley.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The **BGK (Bhatnagar-Gross-Krook) model** replaces the full collision operator with a simple relaxation term: the distribution $f$ relaxes toward the local Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution $f_0$ at a rate $1/\tau$:

$$\frac{df}{dt} = -\frac{f - f_0}{\tau},$$

where $\tau$ is the relaxation time. In the absence of spatial gradients (homogeneous gas), the solution is

$$f(t) = f_0 + (f(0) - f_0)\, e^{-t/\tau}.$$

The distribution relaxes exponentially to equilibrium with time constant $\tau = l/\bar{v}$ (the mean free time). This is the simplest model of approach to equilibrium and captures the essential physics: collisions drive the system toward equilibrium at a rate set by the collision frequency.

**Exercise (easy, multiple choice).**

The Boltzmann H-theorem states that:

A. $H$ is always constant
B. $dH/dt \geq 0$ (H increases)
C. $dH/dt \leq 0$ (H decreases)
D. $H$ oscillates

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

**Option C.** $dH/dt \leq 0$, with equality only when $f$ is the equilibrium Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution. The H-function is essentially the negative of the entropy density: $S = -k_B H$, so $dS/dt \geq 0$ (entropy increases). This is the microscopic second law.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The **Boltzmann transport equation** for a dilute gas of hard spheres is

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla_{\mathbf{r}} f + \frac{\mathbf{F}}{m}\cdot\nabla_{\mathbf{v}} f = C[f],$$

where $f(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{v}, t)$ is the single-particle distribution function, $\mathbf{F}$ is the external force, and $C[f]$ is the **collision operator**:

$$C[f] = \int d^3v_1 \int d\Omega\, |\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{v}_1|\, \sigma(\Omega)\,[f'f_1' - ff_1].$$

Here $f' = f(\mathbf{v}')$, $f_1 = f(\mathbf{v}_1)$, $f_1' = f(\mathbf{v}_1')$, and the primed velocities are the post-collision velocities. The **gain term** $f'f_1'$ counts collisions that increase $f$ at velocity $\mathbf{v}$; the **loss term** $ff_1$ counts collisions that decrease it.

### Key result: The H-theorem

**Theorem (Boltzmann).** *Define $H[f] = \int f \ln f\, d^3v$. Then $dH/dt \leq 0$, with equality if and only if $f$ is the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution.*

The proof uses the identity $\ln(f'/f_1') - \ln(f/f_1) \leq 0$ (from detailed balance) and shows that the collision integral is non-positive. The equilibrium distribution satisfies detailed balance $f'f_1' = ff_1$, which gives $f_0 \propto e^{-mv^2/(2kT)}$.

### BGK model

The **BGK approximation** replaces $C[f]$ with $-\nu(f - f_0)$ where $\nu = 1/\tau$ is the collision frequency and $f_0$ is the local Maxwellian with the same density, mean velocity, and temperature as $f$. This preserves the conservation laws (mass, momentum, energy) while simplifying the collision operator to a linear relaxation term.

**Bridge.** The Boltzmann equation builds toward the fluctuation-dissipation theorem [11.02.04], which provides an alternative derivation of transport coefficients from equilibrium time-correlation functions. This is exactly the Green-Kubo approach: the transport coefficients from the Chapman-Enskog solution of the Boltzmann equation agree with those from the Green-Kubo relations. The foundational reason the Boltzmann equation predicts irreversibility (the H-theorem) despite time-reversible microscopic dynamics is the **molecular chaos assumption** (Stosszahlansatz): the velocities of two colliding particles are assumed to be uncorrelated before the collision. This is the bridge between microscopic reversibility and macroscopic irreversibility. Putting these together, the H-theorem is the kinetic-theory derivation of the second law, and the BGK model captures its essential content in a simplified form.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution $f_0 = n(m/(2\pi kT))^{3/2}\exp(-mv^2/(2kT))$ satisfies the detailed balance condition $f_0(\mathbf{v}')f_0(\mathbf{v}_1') = f_0(\mathbf{v})f_0(\mathbf{v}_1)$ for elastic collisions (where $v^2 + v_1^2 = v'^2 + v_1'^2$).

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

$\ln[f_0(\mathbf{v})f_0(\mathbf{v}_1)] = 2\ln n + 3\ln(m/(2\pi kT)) - m(v^2 + v_1^2)/(2kT)$.

For elastic collisions, energy is conserved: $v^2 + v_1^2 = v'^2 + v_1'^2$. So $\ln[f_0(\mathbf{v})f_0(\mathbf{v}_1)] = \ln[f_0(\mathbf{v}')f_0(\mathbf{v}_1')]$. Hence $f_0(\mathbf{v})f_0(\mathbf{v}_1) = f_0(\mathbf{v}')f_0(\mathbf{v}_1')$, satisfying detailed balance. The collision operator vanishes: $C[f_0] = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove the H-theorem: show that $dH/dt \leq 0$ for a spatially homogeneous gas. Use the identity $(f'f_1' - ff_1)\ln(ff_1/(f'f_1')) \leq 0$.

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

For a homogeneous gas ($\nabla f = 0$, $\mathbf{F} = 0$): $\partial f/\partial t = C[f]$. So

$dH/dt = \int (1 + \ln f) C[f]\, d^3v$.

Write $C[f] = \int\int [f'f_1' - ff_1]\, W\, d^3v_1\, d\Omega$ where $W = |\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{v}_1|\sigma(\Omega)$. Then

$dH/dt = \int\int\int (1+\ln f)[f'f_1' - ff_1]\, W\, d^3v\, d^3v_1\, d\Omega$.

By the symmetry of the collision integral (swapping $(v,v_1) \leftrightarrow (v',v_1')$), one can show that

$dH/dt = \frac{1}{4}\int\int\int [f'f_1' - ff_1]\ln(ff_1/(f'f_1'))\, W\, d^3v\, d^3v_1\, d\Omega$.

Since $(x-y)\ln(y/x) \leq 0$ for $x, y > 0$ (with equality only for $x = y$), we have $dH/dt \leq 0$, with equality only when $ff_1 = f'f_1'$ (detailed balance).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the BGK collision operator conserves mass, momentum, and energy: $\int C_{BGK}[f]\, d^3v = 0$, $\int \mathbf{v}\, C_{BGK}[f]\, d^3v = 0$, $\int v^2\, C_{BGK}[f]\, d^3v = 0$.

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

$C_{BGK} = -\nu(f - f_0)$ where $f_0$ has the same moments as $f$:

$n = \int f\, d^3v = \int f_0\, d^3v$, $n\mathbf{u} = \int \mathbf{v}f\, d^3v = \int \mathbf{v}f_0\, d^3v$, $\frac{3}{2}nkT = \int \frac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u}|^2 f\, d^3v = \int \frac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u}|^2 f_0\, d^3v$.

So $\int C_{BGK}\, d^3v = -\nu(\int f\, d^3v - \int f_0\, d^3v) = 0$. Similarly for momentum and energy. The BGK operator conserves the collision invariants by construction.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (hard, symbolic).**

The Chapman-Enskog expansion writes $f = f_0 + \epsilon f_1 + \ldots$ where $\epsilon$ is the Knudsen number. At first order, show that $f_1 = -\tau(\partial f_0/\partial t + \mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla f_0)$ and that substituting into the moment equations recovers the Navier-Stokes equations with the transport coefficients $\eta = nkT\tau$, $\kappa = (5/2)nkT\tau/m$.

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

At order $\epsilon^0$: $C[f_0] = 0$ (equilibrium). At order $\epsilon^1$: $\partial f_0/\partial t + \mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla f_0 = C'[f_0]\cdot f_1$ where $C'$ is the linearised collision operator. For the BGK model: $C'[f_0]\cdot f_1 = -\nu f_1$, so $f_1 = -(1/\nu)(\partial f_0/\partial t + \mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla f_0) = -\tau(\partial f_0/\partial t + \mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla f_0)$.

The stress tensor from $f_1$: $\sigma_{ij} = \int m(v_i - u_i)(v_j - u_j) f_1\, d^3v$. Evaluating with the Maxwellian gradients gives $\sigma_{ij} = -\eta(\partial u_i/\partial x_j + \partial u_j/\partial x_i - \frac{2}{3}\delta_{ij}\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u})$ with $\eta = nkT\tau$.

The heat flux: $q_i = \int \frac{1}{2}m|\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u}|^2(v_i - u_i) f_1\, d^3v = -\kappa\,\partial T/\partial x_i$ with $\kappa = (5/2)nkT\tau/m$ (for a monatomic gas, the Prandtl number from BGK is Pr = 1, compared to the exact hard-sphere value Pr = 2/3).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (hard, symbolic).**

Discuss the Loschmidt paradox: if the microscopic dynamics are time-reversible, how can the Boltzmann equation predict irreversible behaviour? What is the role of the molecular chaos (Stosszahlansatz) assumption?

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

The Loschmidt paradox (1876): reversing all velocities at time $t$ would make the system retrace its path, so $H$ would *increase* — contradicting the H-theorem.

Resolution: the molecular chaos assumption assumes that the velocities of two particles about to collide are uncorrelated: $f_2(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v}_1) = f(\mathbf{v})f(\mathbf{v}_1)$. This is true at $t = 0$ (by hypothesis) but is not maintained by the exact dynamics — post-collision velocities are correlated. The assumption is justified in the dilute-gas limit ($n\lambda^3 \ll 1$) where three-body collisions are rare and correlations decay between successive binary collisions.

The Boltzmann equation is therefore an *approximate* description that becomes exact in the Boltzmann-Grad limit ($N \to \infty$, $a \to 0$, $Na^2 = \text{const}$). The irreversibility is not a property of the microscopic dynamics but of the approximation that discards correlations. This is the content of Lanford's theorem (1975): the Boltzmann equation is valid for a time $t < t_0/(5\lambda)$ where $t_0$ is the mean free time, i.e., for a finite but physically relevant time window.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

The linearised Boltzmann equation for small perturbations $\delta f = f - f_0$ in a homogeneous gas is $\partial\, \delta f/\partial t = L[\delta f]$ where $L$ is the linearised collision operator. Show that $L$ is self-adjoint in the weighted inner product $\langle g, h \rangle = \int (g h / f_0)\, d^3v$ and has non-positive eigenvalues.

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

The linearised collision operator is obtained by expanding $C[f_0 + \delta f] = C'[f_0]\cdot \delta f + O(\delta f^2)$. From the H-theorem proof, $L$ can be written in symmetric form:

$L[g] = -\frac{1}{2}\int\int\int f_0 f_{01} W [g'/f_0' + g_1'/f_{01}' - g/f_0 - g_1/f_{01}]\, d^3v_1\, d\Omega$

where $g = \delta f$. The inner product $\langle g, L[g]\rangle = \int (g L[g]/f_0)\, d^3v$ can be shown (by the same symmetrisation used in the H-theorem) to be non-positive:

$\langle g, L[g]\rangle = -\frac{1}{4}\int\int\int f_0 f_{01} W [g'/f_0' + g_1'/f_{01}' - g/f_0 - g_1/f_{01}]^2\, d^3v\, d^3v_1\, d\Omega \leq 0$.

The eigenvalues of $L$ are non-positive, and the zero eigenvalues correspond to the collision invariants ($1, \mathbf{v}, v^2$). This is the spectral property that guarantees relaxation to equilibrium.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The entropy production rate in the Boltzmann equation is $\dot{\sigma} = -k_B dH/dt \geq 0$. For the BGK model, show that $\dot{\sigma} = k_B\nu\int (f - f_0)(\ln f - \ln f_0)\, d^3v \geq 0$.

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

$H[f] = \int f\ln f\, d^3v$. $\dot{H} = \int (1 + \ln f)(\partial f/\partial t)\, d^3v = \int (1 + \ln f)(-\nu(f - f_0))\, d^3v = -\nu\int (f - f_0)(1 + \ln f)\, d^3v$.

Since $\int(f - f_0)\, d^3v = 0$ (mass conservation), the $1$ term drops: $\dot{H} = -\nu\int (f - f_0)\ln f\, d^3v$.

Now use the identity $\int(f - f_0)\ln f_0\, d^3v = 0$ (which follows from the conservation laws and the Maxwellian form of $f_0$). Adding: $\dot{H} = -\nu\int (f - f_0)(\ln f - \ln f_0)\, d^3v$.

Since $(x - y)(\ln x - \ln y) \geq 0$ for all $x, y > 0$ (with equality only for $x = y$), we have $\dot{H} \leq 0$, so $\dot{\sigma} = -k_B\dot{H} \geq 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

For small departures from equilibrium, the BGK equation gives a diffusion coefficient $D = kT\tau/m$ (Einstein relation). Show this by computing the mean-square displacement $\langle r^2(t)\rangle$ from the BGK equation and extracting $D$.

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

The velocity autocorrelation in the BGK model: from the homogeneous BGK equation for a tagged particle, $\partial f/\partial t = -\nu(f - f_0)$ where $f$ is the velocity distribution of the tagged particle. The velocity autocorrelation decays exponentially: $\langle \mathbf{v}(0)\cdot\mathbf{v}(t)\rangle = (3kT/m)e^{-t/\tau}$.

The mean-square displacement: $\langle r^2(t)\rangle = \int_0^t dt'\int_0^t dt''\, \langle \mathbf{v}(t')\cdot\mathbf{v}(t'')\rangle = 2\int_0^t (t - s)\langle\mathbf{v}(0)\cdot\mathbf{v}(s)\rangle\, ds$.

For $t \gg \tau$: $\langle r^2(t)\rangle = 2t\int_0^\infty \langle\mathbf{v}(0)\cdot\mathbf{v}(s)\rangle\, ds = 2t \cdot (3kT/m)\int_0^\infty e^{-s/\tau}\, ds = 6Dt$ where $D = (kT/m)\tau = kT\tau/m$. This is the Einstein relation $D = \mu kT$ with mobility $\mu = \tau/m$.

</details>

</aside>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The Boltzmann equation is an integro-differential equation for the distribution function $f(\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{v}, t)$. Formalising it requires: (1) the distribution function as a measurable function on $\mathbb{R}^3 \times \mathbb{R}^3 \times \mathbb{R}$; (2) the collision operator as a bilinear integral operator satisfying detailed balance; (3) the H-functional and its monotonicity. The H-theorem is a functional inequality of the form $(x-y)\ln(x/y) \geq 0$, which is provable in Mathlib. The main gap is the collision-operator framework.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Lanford's theorem

Lanford (1975) proved that the Boltzmann equation is the exact limit of the Liouville equation in the Boltzmann-Grad limit ($N \to \infty$, particle diameter $a \to 0$, $Na^2 = \text{const}$). The derivation is valid for a finite time $0 \leq t \leq C/\lambda$ (a fraction of the mean free time), during which most particles undergo at most one collision. This is the rigorous justification of the molecular chaos assumption.

### Hydrodynamic limits

The Boltzmann equation reduces to the Euler equations (inviscid fluid) in the limit $\epsilon \to 0$ (Knudsen number going to zero) at order $\epsilon^0$, and to the Navier-Stokes equations at order $\epsilon^1$ (Chapman-Enskog expansion). This establishes the connection between kinetic theory and continuum fluid mechanics.

### Entropy and irreversibility

The H-theorem provides the first microscopic derivation of the second law. The entropy production rate $\dot{\sigma} = -k_B dH/dt$ is non-negative, vanishing only at equilibrium. This connects the Boltzmann equation to the theory of irreversibility [11.02.04] and the fluctuation-dissipation theorem, where the entropy production rate is proportional to the product of fluxes and forces.

**Synthesis.** The Boltzmann equation builds toward the fluctuation-dissipation theorem [11.02.04] and the Green-Kubo relations, which provide an alternative route to transport coefficients. The central insight is that the H-theorem is the kinetic-theory embodiment of the second law: entropy increases because collisions randomise velocities, and the molecular chaos assumption is the link between time-reversible dynamics and irreversible macroscopic behaviour. This is dual to the information-theoretic interpretation of entropy: $H = -S/k_B$ is the information content of the distribution, and the H-theorem says information is lost (entropy increases) because collisions destroy correlations. The foundational reason the Boltzmann equation works is that in a dilute gas, most collisions are binary and uncorrelated; putting these together with the Chapman-Enskog expansion, the microscopic collision dynamics produce the macroscopic Navier-Stokes equations, generalising the mean-free-path estimates [11.02.02] to exact numerical prefactors.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition.** *The equilibrium solution of the Boltzmann equation is the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution $f_0 = n(m/(2\pi kT))^{3/2}\exp(-m|\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u}|^2/(2kT))$.*

**Proof.** The collision operator vanishes when detailed balance holds: $f(\mathbf{v}')f(\mathbf{v}_1') = f(\mathbf{v})f(\mathbf{v}_1)$ for all collisions. Taking logarithms: $\ln f(\mathbf{v}') + \ln f(\mathbf{v}_1') = \ln f(\mathbf{v}) + \ln f(\mathbf{v}_1)$. This is a conservation law: $\ln f$ is a linear combination of the collision invariants:

$$\ln f(\mathbf{v}) = A + \mathbf{B}\cdot\mathbf{v} + C|\mathbf{v}|^2.$$

The constants $A, \mathbf{B}, C$ are determined by the conditions: $\int f\, d^3v = n$ (density), $\int \mathbf{v}f\, d^3v = n\mathbf{u}$ (mean velocity), $\int |\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u}|^2 f\, d^3v = 3nkT/m$ (temperature). Solving gives $A = \ln[n(m/(2\pi kT))^{3/2}] - mu^2/(2kT)$, $\mathbf{B} = m\mathbf{u}/(kT)$, $C = -m/(2kT)$. Substituting recovers the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution. $\square$

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Boltzmann introduced his equation in 1872, provoking immediate objections. Loschmidt (1876) noted that time-reversal of the microscopic dynamics would reverse the sign of $dH/dt$ — the **reversibility paradox**. Zermelo (1896) noted that Poincare recurrence implies any system returns arbitrarily close to its initial state — the **recurrence paradox**. Both paradoxes were resolved by recognising that the H-theorem holds with probability approaching 1 for macroscopic systems (the thermodynamic limit), not with absolute certainty. The molecular chaos assumption selects the overwhelmingly probable direction of evolution.
