## Intuition [Beginner]

The renormalization group (RG) answers a deceptively simple question: what happens to a system when you zoom out? If you blur your vision and look at a lattice of spins from far away, individual spins become invisible. Instead, you see patches of aligned spins — **blocks**. The RG transformation replaces each block by a single effective spin.

After one zoom step, the system looks similar but has fewer spins, a larger lattice spacing, and different coupling constants. If you zoom out again and again, the coupling constants follow a trajectory — an **RG flow** — through the space of all possible Hamiltonians.

A **fixed point** of the RG is a Hamiltonian that looks the same at every scale. Fixed points organise the entire flow: trajectories are attracted to or repelled from them. Near a critical point, the system flows toward a fixed point, and its properties at that fixed point determine the universal critical behaviour.

Coupling constants that grow under the RG are **relevant** — they drive the system away from the fixed point and determine the phase. Coupling constants that shrink are **irrelevant** — they wash out at large scales and do not affect the critical behaviour. This classification explains universality: systems with the same relevant couplings but different irrelevant ones share the same critical exponents.

## Visual [Beginner]

Imagine the space of all possible Hamiltonians as a landscape. RG flow is a vector field on this landscape. Fixed points are special locations where the flow stops.

The critical surface is the set of Hamiltonians that flow to the critical fixed point. Any starting point on this surface ends up at the same fixed point — this is universality. Starting points off the surface flow away, either to the high-temperature (disordered) fixed point or the low-temperature (ordered) fixed point.

## Worked example [Beginner]

**1D Ising model: exact block-spin decimation.** Group pairs of spins $(s_1, s_2)$, $(s_3, s_4)$, $\ldots$ into blocks. Sum over the "inner" spins to get an effective Hamiltonian for the remaining spins:

$$e^{K'(s_1 s_3 - 1)} = 2\cosh(K(s_1 + s_3)).$$

The renormalised coupling is $K' = \frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K)$. The flow of $K$ under repeated decimation: $K \to K' < K \to K'' < K' \to \ldots \to 0$. The coupling always flows to zero — there is no phase transition in 1D, as expected.

If instead $K$ grew under decimation (which happens for sufficiently strong coupling in 2D), the flow would approach a nonzero fixed point $K^*$ with $K^* = \frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K^*)$, signalling a phase transition.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Block-spin transformation

Given a Hamiltonian $H(\{s_i\})$ on a lattice with spacing $a$ and $N$ sites, the block-spin transformation with scale factor $b > 1$ is:

1. **Block**: Group the $N$ sites into $N' = N/b^d$ blocks of linear size $ba$.
2. **Decimate**: For each block, define a block spin $S_I = f(\{s_i \in I\})$ (e.g., majority rule: $S_I = \text{sgn}(\sum_{i \in I} s_i)$).
3. **Coarse-grain**: Integrate out the original spins:

$$e^{-\beta' H'(S_I)} = \sum_{\{s_i\}} \prod_I \delta(S_I - f(\{s_i\}))\, e^{-\beta H(\{s_i\})}.$$

The result is a new Hamiltonian $H'$ on $N' = N/b^d$ sites with lattice spacing $ba$. The RG transformation is the map $\mathcal{R}: H \mapsto H'$.

### RG flow and fixed points

Applying $\mathcal{R}$ repeatedly generates the **RG flow**: $H_0 \to H_1 \to H_2 \to \ldots$. A **fixed point** $H^*$ satisfies $\mathcal{R}(H^*) = H^*$. The fixed-point Hamiltonian is scale-invariant — it looks the same at every length scale.

Linearising around $H^*$: write $H = H^* + \sum_i g_i O_i$ where $\{O_i\}$ are scaling operators and $g_i$ are coupling constants. Under the RG:

$$g_i' = b^{y_i} g_i + \text{higher order},$$

where $y_i$ are the **RG eigenvalues**. The classification is:

- **Relevant**: $y_i > 0$ ($g_i$ grows; drives the system away from $H^*$).
- **Irrelevant**: $y_i < 0$ ($g_i$ shrinks; washes out at large scales).
- **Marginal**: $y_i = 0$ ($g_i$ is unchanged to linear order; higher-order terms decide).

### Key result: Critical exponents from RG eigenvalues

The relevant eigenvalues at the critical fixed point determine the critical exponents:

$$\nu = 1/y_t, \qquad \eta = 2 - y_h, \qquad \alpha = 2 - d/y_t,$$

where $y_t$ is the eigenvalue of the thermal operator (the one conjugate to $T - T_c$) and $y_h$ is the eigenvalue of the magnetic field operator. All other exponents follow from scaling relations:

$$\beta = \frac{d - y_h}{2y_t}, \qquad \gamma = \frac{2y_h - d}{y_t}, \qquad \delta = \frac{y_h}{d - y_h}.$$

## Key derivation: 1D Ising decimation [Intermediate+]

**Proposition.** *The block-spin decimation of the 1D Ising model with nearest-neighbour coupling $K$ gives a renormalised coupling $K' = \frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K)$. The fixed point $K^* = 0$ is stable for all finite $K$, confirming the absence of a phase transition.*

**Proof.** The partition function for a 1D Ising chain is $Z = \sum_{\{s_i\}} e^{K\sum_i s_i s_{i+1}}$. Decimate every other spin: sum over $s_2, s_4, \ldots$ while keeping $s_1, s_3, \ldots$ fixed.

The sum over $s_2$ gives:

$$\sum_{s_2 = \pm 1} e^{K(s_1 s_2 + s_2 s_3)} = e^{K(s_1 + s_3)} + e^{-K(s_1 + s_3)} = 2\cosh(K(s_1 + s_3)).$$

For $s_1 = s_3 = +1$: $2\cosh(2K)$. For $s_1 = +1, s_3 = -1$ (or vice versa): $2\cosh(0) = 2$. For $s_1 = s_3 = -1$: $2\cosh(2K)$.

We want this to equal $C\, e^{K' s_1 s_3}$ for some constants $C, K'$. Setting $s_1 = s_3$: $C e^{K'} = 2\cosh(2K)$. Setting $s_1 = -s_3$: $C e^{-K'} = 2$. Dividing: $e^{2K'} = \cosh(2K)$, giving $K' = \frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K)$.

Flow: $K' < K$ for all $K < \infty$ (since $\cosh(2K) < e^{2K}$ for $K > 0$). So $K \to K' < K \to K'' < K' \to \ldots \to 0$. The fixed point $K^* = 0$ (infinite temperature, completely disordered) is the only stable fixed point. There is no nonzero fixed point, hence no phase transition. $\square$

**Bridge.** The 1D Ising decimation builds toward the 2D block-spin analysis and the epsilon expansion [11.07.03], where nonzero fixed points do exist. This is exactly the content of the lower critical dimension: 1D is below $d_\ell = 1$ for the Ising model (no phase transition at $T > 0$), while 2D is above $d_\ell$ and has a nonzero RG fixed point. The foundational reason the RG fixed point exists in 2D but not in 1D is that the domain-wall energy scales as the perimeter (length in 2D, single bond in 1D), which can overcome the entropic cost of creating a domain. Putting these together with the Wilson-Fisher fixed point [11.07.03], the block-spin picture provides the geometric intuition for why critical behaviour depends on dimensionality: the number of neighbours grows with $d$, and this changes the balance between energy and entropy at the fixed point.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Verify that $K^* = 0$ and $K^* = \infty$ are fixed points of the 1D Ising decimation $K' = \frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K)$.

<details class="exercise__hint><summary>Hint</summary>

A fixed point satisfies $K' = K$. Substitute $K = 0$ and $K = \infty$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

$K = 0$: $K' = \frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(0) = \frac{1}{2}\ln 1 = 0$. Fixed point confirmed.

$K = \infty$: $\cosh(2K) \to e^{2K}/2$, so $K' = \frac{1}{2}\ln(e^{2K}/2) = K - \frac{1}{2}\ln 2 \neq K$. So $K = \infty$ is NOT a fixed point of this transformation — the coupling decreases even at $K = \infty$.

Actually, for $K \to \infty$: $\frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K) \to K$ (since $\cosh(2K) \sim e^{2K}/2$ and $\frac{1}{2}\ln(e^{2K}/2) = K - \ln 2/2$). So $K' \to K - \ln 2/2 < K$. Even starting at infinite coupling, the decimation reduces it. The only true fixed point is $K^* = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

For the 1D Ising decimation with $K = 0.5$, compute $K'$, $K''$, and $K'''$ (three decimation steps). How many steps until $K < 0.01$?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

$K = 0.5$: $K' = \frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(1) = \frac{1}{2}\ln(1.543) = 0.217$.
$K' = 0.217$: $K'' = \frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(0.434) = \frac{1}{2}\ln(1.095) = 0.045$.
$K'' = 0.045$: $K''' = \frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(0.090) \approx \frac{1}{2}(0.090)^2/2 = 0.002$ (using $\cosh(x) \approx 1 + x^2/2$).

Three decimation steps bring $K$ from $0.5$ to $0.002 < 0.01$. The coupling decays rapidly — the 1D Ising chain has no phase transition because the fixed point at $K = 0$ attracts all finite couplings.

</details>

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Linearise the 1D Ising RG transformation around the fixed point $K^* = 0$ and find the RG eigenvalue $y_t$. Confirm that $\nu = 1/y_t$ gives the correlation length exponent $\nu = 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint><summary>Hint</summary>

For small $K$: $K' \approx \frac{1}{2}\ln(1 + 2K^2) \approx K^2$... wait, let me compute properly. $\cosh(2K) \approx 1 + 2K^2$, so $K' \approx \frac{1}{2}\ln(1+2K^2) \approx K^2$. This gives $y_t$ from $K' = b^{y_t} K$ with $b = 2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

For small $K$: $\cosh(2K) \approx 1 + 2K^2$, so $K' \approx \frac{1}{2}\ln(1 + 2K^2) \approx K^2$.

But the RG eigenvalue is defined by $K' = b^{y_t} K$ where $b$ is the scale factor (here $b = 2$, since we decimated every other spin). So $K^2 = 2^{y_t} K$, giving $y_t = \log_2(K)$. But $y_t$ should be a constant, not depending on $K$!

The issue is that $t = T - T_c$ is the relevant variable, not $K$. For the 1D Ising model, $T_c = 0$, so $t = T$. Since $K = J/(kT) = J/(kt)$, we have $t \propto 1/K$. The decimation acts on $t$ as $t' = t \cdot 2^{y_t}$ with $y_t = 1$ (from $K' \approx K^2$, so $t' \approx t/2$, giving $2^{y_t} = 2$, $y_t = 1$). Hence $\nu = 1/y_t = 1$.

The correlation length in 1D is $\xi = 1/|\ln\tanh K| \sim e^{2K} \sim e^{2J/kT}$ for large $K$, which diverges as $T \to 0$ with $\nu = 1$ (since $\xi \sim e^{A/T} \sim T^{-\nu}$ doesn't have a simple power law; the 1D Ising model has $T_c = 0$ and the divergence is exponential, not power-law).

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

For the Gaussian fixed point in $d$ dimensions, the RG eigenvalues are $y_t = 2$ (thermal) and $y_h = 1 + d/2$ (magnetic). Compute the critical exponents $\nu$, $\eta$, $\alpha$, $\beta$, $\gamma$, $\delta$ and verify they match mean-field values for $d = 4$.

Use $\nu = 1/y_t$, $\eta = 2 - y_h$, $\alpha = 2 - d\nu$, and the scaling relations.

$\nu = 1/y_t = 1/2$. $\eta = 2 - y_h = 2 - (1 + d/2) = 1 - d/2$. For $d = 4$: $\eta = 1 - 2 = -1$... That's not right. Let me reconsider.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the number of relevant operators at a critical fixed point determines the number of independent thermodynamic parameters needed to specify the critical point. Show that the Ising model has exactly two relevant operators (thermal and magnetic).

<details class="exercise__hint><summary>Hint</summary>

A critical point is a point in the phase diagram where the correlation length diverges. Each relevant operator defines a direction away from the critical surface.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

The phase diagram of the Ising model is parameterised by two variables: temperature $T$ and magnetic field $h$. A critical point occurs at a specific $(T_c, h_c)$. The critical surface is the set of all Hamiltonians that flow to the critical fixed point; it is a codimension-$n_r$ surface where $n_r$ is the number of relevant operators.

For the Ising universality class, there are exactly two relevant operators: the thermal operator $O_t$ (conjugate to $T - T_c$) with eigenvalue $y_t > 0$, and the magnetic operator $O_h$ (conjugate to $h$) with eigenvalue $y_h > 0$. All other operators are irrelevant.

The critical point is the intersection of the two codimension-1 surfaces $t = 0$ and $h = 0$, which is generically a single point. Moving away from this point in the $t$-direction (changing temperature) triggers the phase transition; moving in the $h$-direction (applying a field) destroys the transition by breaking the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry.

If there were $n_r$ relevant operators, the critical point would be the intersection of $n_r$ codimension-1 surfaces — a codimension-$n_r$ object. The number of independent thermodynamic parameters needed to locate the critical point is $n_r$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

The lower critical dimension $d_\ell$ is the dimension below which no ordered phase exists at $T > 0$. Show that $d_\ell = 1$ for the Ising model by using the RG: demonstrate that the only fixed point in $d = 1$ is the zero-coupling one ($K^* = 0$), while in $d = 2$ a nonzero fixed point exists.

<details class="exercise__hint><summary>Hint</summary>

In 1D, the decimation recursion $K' = \frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K)$ always reduces $K$. In 2D, the block-spin recursion has a nonzero fixed point.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

1D: The decimation recursion $K' = \frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K)$ is a monotonically increasing function of $K$, but $K' < K$ for all finite $K > 0$ (since $\cosh(2K) < e^{2K}$). The only fixed point with finite $K$ is $K^* = 0$. There is no nonzero fixed point, hence no phase transition. The ordered phase ($K = \infty$) exists only at $T = 0$, so $d_\ell = 1$.

2D: The block-spin recursion for the 2D Ising model (e.g., using the Niemeijer-van Leeuwen cumulant expansion) has a nonzero fixed point at $K^* > 0$. At this fixed point, the correlation length is infinite and a phase transition occurs. The exact critical coupling is $K_c = \frac{1}{2}\ln(1 + \sqrt{2}) \approx 0.4407$ (Onsager 1944).

The difference: in 1D, a domain wall costs energy $O(1)$ (breaking one bond) and has entropy $O(\ln N)$ (it can be placed at $N$ positions). The entropy always wins for $T > 0$. In 2D, a domain wall costs energy $O(N^{1/2})$ (proportional to the wall length) and has entropy $O(\ln N)$. The energy wins below $T_c$.

</details>

</aside>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The RG as a semigroup of transformations on a space of Hamiltonians is a purely mathematical object. Formalising it requires: (1) a type of Hamiltonians (or coupling-constant vectors) with a transformation map $\mathcal{R}$; (2) a fixed-point condition $\mathcal{R}(H^*) = H^*$; (3) a linearisation giving eigenvalues $y_i$ and a classification into relevant/irrelevant/marginal; (4) the connection between eigenvalues and critical exponents. The mathematics is standard dynamical-systems theory (fixed points, stability analysis), but the physical context (Hamiltonians, critical exponents) requires domain-specific infrastructure.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Niemeijer-van Leeuwen cumulant expansion

For the 2D Ising model with 2x2 blocks and majority rule, the exact block-spin transformation is intractable. The **Niemeijer-van Leeuwen (NvL) cumulant expansion** approximates it by expanding in powers of the inter-block coupling.

The effective Hamiltonian is $H' = H_0 + V$ where $H_0$ contains the intra-block couplings and $V$ contains the inter-block couplings. The RG recursion for the coupling constants is obtained from a cumulant expansion:

$$H' = \langle H_0 \rangle_0 + \langle V \rangle_0 + \frac{1}{2}(\langle V^2 \rangle_0 - \langle V \rangle_0^2) + \cdots,$$

where $\langle \cdot \rangle_0$ denotes an average with respect to the intra-block Hamiltonian $H_0$. The first cumulant generates the renormalised nearest-neighbour coupling; the second cumulant generates next-nearest-neighbour and four-spin couplings. Truncating at first order gives a reasonable approximation; including the second cumulant improves accuracy but introduces new coupling constants that complicate the flow.

### Exact RG in 1D and the lower bound on $d_c$

The 1D Ising decimation is one of the few exactly solvable RG transformations. It demonstrates that exact RG transformations can be computed for models with sufficiently simple topology. For the 2D Ising model, no exact real-space RG transformation is known (though the transfer matrix provides an exact solution by other means).

The **Migdal-Kadanoff** approximation is a systematic (but uncontrolled) real-space RG for the Ising model in arbitrary dimension. It gives exact results in $d = 1$ and $d = 2$ but becomes increasingly inaccurate for $d \geq 3$. Despite its approximations, it correctly identifies the lower and upper critical dimensions and gives a qualitative picture of the RG flow in all dimensions.

### Universality from the RG perspective

The RG explains universality in a precise way. Consider the space of all Hamiltonians with a given symmetry (e.g., $\mathbb{Z}_2$ for the Ising model). This space is infinite-dimensional (there are infinitely many coupling constants). The RG flow projects this infinite-dimensional space onto a finite-dimensional **critical surface** — the stable manifold of the critical fixed point. The dimension of the critical surface is the number of irrelevant operators. All Hamiltonians on the same critical surface share the same critical exponents because they all flow to the same fixed point.

Two systems are in the same universality class if they flow to the same fixed point under the RG. The universality class is determined by: (1) the spatial dimension $d$, (2) the symmetry of the order parameter, and (3) the range of the interaction (short-range vs long-range). These three pieces of information specify the relevant and marginal operators at the fixed point, and hence the critical behaviour.

**Synthesis.** The block-spin renormalization builds toward the epsilon expansion [11.07.03] and the classification of universality classes [11.07.04], which together provide the quantitative theory of critical phenomena. The central insight is that the RG fixed point is the mathematical object that embodies universality: all microscopic details are washed out by the flow, leaving only the relevant operators to determine the macroscopic critical behaviour. This is dual to the mean-field theory [11.06.02], which can be recovered as the Gaussian fixed point of the RG in $d > 4$. The foundational reason the RG works is that critical phenomena involve correlations at all length scales, and the RG systematically eliminates short-wavelength fluctuations while preserving the long-wavelength physics; putting these together, the RG is the natural language for scale-dependent phenomena, and the fixed-point classification of relevant and irrelevant operators is the structural reason why only a few parameters determine the critical behaviour of infinitely many different physical systems.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition.** *The 1D Ising decimation $K' = \frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K)$ has a unique stable fixed point at $K^* = 0$ and an unstable fixed point at $K^* = \infty$. The correlation length transforms as $\xi' = \xi/b$ where $b = 2$.*

**Proof.** The function $f(K) = \frac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K)$ is monotonically increasing, with $f(0) = 0$ and $f(K) < K$ for all $K > 0$ (since $\cosh(x) < e^x$ for $x > 0$). Therefore $f(f(K)) < f(K) < K$, and iterating drives $K$ monotonically to zero. The only finite fixed point is $K^* = 0$.

For the correlation length: under decimation with scale factor $b = 2$, the number of sites halves and the lattice spacing doubles. A correlation length measured in units of the old lattice spacing $\xi$ becomes $\xi' = \xi/b$ in units of the new spacing. Since $K' < K$, the effective temperature increases and the correlation length (in physical units) decreases. The fixed point $K^* = 0$ has $\xi = 0$ (infinite temperature, completely disordered), and the fixed point $K^* = \infty$ has $\xi = \infty$ (zero temperature, completely ordered).

The RG eigenvalue at $K^* = 0$: $f'(0) = \tanh(0) = 0$. So $y = 0$ to linear order — the thermal eigenvalue is zero at this fixed point, consistent with $T_c = 0$ (no phase transition at finite temperature). The next-order term gives $f(K) \approx K^2$, a nonlinear flow that reaches $K^* = 0$ in finite RG steps. $\square$

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Kadanoff (1966) proposed the block-spin picture as a heuristic argument for scaling: if the system is scale-invariant at the critical point, then grouping spins into blocks should produce a system of the same form but with renormalised parameters. Wilson (1971) formalised this into the renormalization group as a mathematical transformation on the space of Hamiltonians, with fixed points governing critical behaviour.

Wilson's 1971 papers and the subsequent Wilson-Kogut review (1974) are among the most influential works in theoretical physics. They provided the first systematic framework for computing critical exponents from first principles, explaining universality, and connecting critical phenomena to quantum field theory. Wilson was awarded the 1982 Nobel Prize for this work.

The philosophical content is significant: the RG shows that macroscopic physics is insensitive to most microscopic details. Only a few "relevant" parameters survive the coarse-graining process, and these determine the observable behaviour. This is a form of emergence — the macroscopic theory is simpler than the microscopic one, not because the microscopic details are irrelevant in principle, but because the RG systematically eliminates them.
