## Intuition [Beginner]

Near a phase transition, small changes in temperature produce enormous changes in behaviour. A magnet heated to its critical temperature $T_c$ loses its magnetization abruptly — yet every individual spin keeps flipping as before. What changes is the *correlation length*: the distance over which two spins still "feel" each other. Far from $T_c$ this range is short. At $T_c$ it diverges — every spin talks to every other spin, and the whole system acts as one.

The renormalization group (RG) is a strategy for understanding this. The idea: zoom out by grouping nearby spins into blocks and treating each block as a single effective spin. After zooming out, the system has fewer degrees of freedom but the same large-scale physics. The parameters (temperature, coupling strength) change as you zoom. If a parameter *grows* under zooming, it is **relevant** — it drives the phase transition. If it *shrinks*, it is **irrelevant** — microscopic details that wash out at large scales.

This explains **universality**: systems with wildly different microscopic physics can share identical critical behaviour, because only the relevant parameters survive the zoom. The liquid-vapour transition and the 3D Ising model have the same critical exponents because they share the same relevant operators.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider the 1D Ising chain: spins on a line, each coupled to its neighbour with strength $K = J/(k_B T)$. Decimate by removing every other spin: take three consecutive spins, average over (trace out) the middle one, and ask what effective coupling the two outer spins feel.

When the outer spins point the same way, the middle spin is "pulled" strongly in that direction and contributes a large Boltzmann weight. When the outer spins point opposite ways, the middle spin is conflicted and the weight is smaller. The ratio of these two weights determines a new coupling $K'$ between the surviving spins. The result is $K' = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K)$, which is always *smaller* than $K$. Each decimation step weakens the coupling. After many steps $K \to 0$: spins become independent and there is no long-range order. The 1D Ising model has no phase transition at any finite temperature, and the RG flow confirms this — the coupling flows to zero under repeated coarse-graining.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Kadanoff block spins

Partition the lattice into blocks of linear size $b$. Define a block spin $S_I = \mathrm{sgn}\!\big(\sum_{i \in I} s_i\big)$ for each block $I$. The RG transformation $\mathcal{R}_b$ maps the original Hamiltonian $H$ with couplings $\mathbf{K} = (K_1, K_2, \ldots)$ to a new Hamiltonian with renormalized couplings $\mathbf{K}' = \mathcal{R}_b(\mathbf{K})$. The partition function is preserved up to an overall constant:

$$
Z_N(\mathbf{K}) = Z_{N/b^d}(\mathbf{K}') \cdot C(\mathbf{K}),
$$

where $d$ is the spatial dimension. The correlation length transforms as $\xi(\mathbf{K}') = \xi(\mathbf{K})/b$.

### RG flow and fixed points

Iterating $\mathcal{R}_b$ generates an RG *flow* on the space of coupling constants. A **fixed point** $\mathbf{K}^*$ satisfies $\mathcal{R}_b(\mathbf{K}^*) = \mathbf{K}^*$. At a fixed point the system is scale-invariant: $\xi(\mathbf{K}^*)$ is either $0$ or $\infty$.

Linearise about a fixed point: write $\delta \mathbf{K} = \mathbf{K} - \mathbf{K}^*$ and compute the eigenvalues $\lambda_i$ and eigenvectors $\mathbf{e}_i$ of the linearised map $M_{ij} = \partial K_i'/\partial K_j\big|_{\mathbf{K}^*}$. The eigenvalues control the flow near the fixed point:

- **Relevant** ($\lambda_i > 1$): perturbation grows under RG, drives the system away from the fixed point.
- **Irrelevant** ($\lambda_i < 1$): perturbation shrinks, the system flows back to the fixed point.
- **Marginal** ($\lambda_i = 1$): higher-order analysis required.

Define the **scaling exponent** $y_i = \ln \lambda_i / \ln b$. Relevant operators have $y_i > 0$; irrelevant have $y_i < 0$.

### Critical exponents from RG eigenvalues

The critical exponents are determined by the RG eigenvalues at the fixed point. For the thermal eigenvalue $y_t$ (the relevant direction associated with $T - T_c$) and the magnetic eigenvalue $y_h$:

$$
\nu = 1/y_t, \qquad \alpha = 2 - d/y_t, \qquad \beta = (d - y_h)/y_t, \qquad \gamma = (2y_h - d)/y_t, \qquad \eta = 2 + d - 2y_h.
$$

These are **scaling relations**: they reduce the independent critical exponents to two numbers ($y_t$, $y_h$). The hyperscaling relation $\alpha = 2 - d\nu$ is automatic.

### The epsilon expansion

The Gaussian fixed point in $d > 4$ dimensions has $\phi^4$ as an irrelevant operator. At $d = 4$ it becomes marginal. Wilson and Fisher showed that for $d = 4 - \epsilon$ (with $\epsilon$ small), the $\phi^4$ coupling flows to a new **Wilson-Fisher fixed point** at $u^* = O(\epsilon)$. The critical exponents are computed as power series in $\epsilon$:

$$
\eta = \frac{\epsilon^2}{54} + O(\epsilon^3), \qquad \nu = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\epsilon}{12} + O(\epsilon^2), \qquad \ldots
$$

At one loop the Wilson-Fisher fixed point is $u^* = \epsilon \cdot 16\pi^2/(n + 8)$ for an $n$-component field.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Critical exponent $\alpha$ from the Wilson-Fisher fixed point in $d = 4 - \epsilon$).** *The specific-heat critical exponent $\alpha$ for the Wilson-Fisher fixed point of the $O(n)$-symmetric $\phi^4$ theory in $d = 4 - \epsilon$ dimensions is*

$$
\alpha = \frac{\epsilon(4 - n)}{2(n + 8)} + O(\epsilon^2).
$$

**Proof.** Start with the Landau-Ginzburg-Wilson Hamiltonian for an $n$-component field $\boldsymbol{\phi} = (\phi_1, \ldots, \phi_n)$:

$$
\mathcal{H} = \frac{1}{2}(\nabla \boldsymbol{\phi})^2 + \frac{1}{2}r\,\boldsymbol{\phi}^2 + \frac{u}{4!}(\boldsymbol{\phi}^2)^2,
$$

where $r \propto T - T_c$ and $u > 0$ is the quartic coupling. The upper critical dimension is $d_c = 4$; above $d_c$ mean-field theory is correct.

The one-loop beta function for $u$ in $d = 4 - \epsilon$ is

$$
\beta(u) = -\epsilon\, u + \frac{n + 8}{6}\,\frac{u^2}{(4\pi)^2}.
$$

Setting $\beta(u^*) = 0$ gives the Wilson-Fisher fixed point:

$$
u^* = \frac{6(4\pi)^2}{n + 8}\,\epsilon.
$$

The thermal eigenvalue (the derivative of the RG flow for $r$ evaluated at the fixed point) determines $y_t$. The RG equation for $r$ at one loop is

$$
r' = b^2\!\left[r + \frac{n + 2}{6}\frac{u}{(4\pi)^2}\,r\right],$$

where the bracket accounts for the one-loop self-energy correction. At the fixed point $u = u^*$, reading off the eigenvalue:

$$
\lambda_t = b^{2 - (n+2)\epsilon/(n+8)}.
$$

Therefore $y_t = 2 - (n+2)\epsilon/(n+8)$, and the correlation-length exponent is

$$
\nu = 1/y_t = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{n + 2}{4(n + 8)}\,\epsilon + O(\epsilon^2).
$$

By the hyperscaling relation $\alpha = 2 - d\nu$:

$$
\alpha = 2 - (4 - \epsilon)\!\left[\frac{1}{2} + \frac{(n+2)\epsilon}{4(n+8)} + O(\epsilon^2)\right].
$$

Expanding to first order in $\epsilon$:

$$
\alpha = 2 - 2 - \frac{(n+2)\epsilon}{2(n+8)} + \frac{\epsilon}{2} + O(\epsilon^2) = \frac{\epsilon(4 - n)}{2(n+8)} + O(\epsilon^2).
$$

For $n = 1$ (Ising universality class), $\alpha = \epsilon \cdot 3/18 + O(\epsilon^2) = \epsilon/6 + O(\epsilon^2)$, positive and small — the specific heat diverges weakly. For $n = 4$, $\alpha = O(\epsilon^2)$ to leading order (a near-logarithmic divergence). For $n > 4$, $\alpha < 0$ — the specific heat has a cusp rather than a divergence. $\square$

The sign and magnitude of $\alpha$ are direct fingerprints of the number of field components $n$, which is why measuring the specific-heat singularity experimentally constrains the universality class.

**Exercise 8 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify the 1D Ising decimation formula: show that $\sum_{s_i = \pm 1} e^{K s_{i-1} s_i + K s_i s_{i+1}} = C \cdot e^{K' s_{i-1} s_{i+1}}$ and derive $K' = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Evaluate the sum explicitly for the two cases $s_{i-1} s_{i+1} = +1$ and $s_{i-1} s_{i+1} = -1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

When $s_{i-1} s_{i+1} = +1$ (both spins parallel): the sum is $e^{2K} + e^{-2K} = 2\cosh(2K)$.

When $s_{i-1} s_{i+1} = -1$ (spins antiparallel): the sum is $e^{0} + e^{0} = 2$.

So $\sum_{s_i} e^{K s_{i-1} s_i + K s_i s_{i+1}} = 2\cosh(2K)$ when $s_{i-1} = s_{i+1}$ and $= 2$ when $s_{i-1} = -s_{i+1}$.

Write this as $C \cdot e^{K' s_{i-1} s_{i+1}}$ where $C e^{K'} = 2\cosh(2K)$ and $C e^{-K'} = 2$. Dividing: $e^{2K'} = \cosh(2K)$, so $K' = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K)$. The prefactor is $C = 2\sqrt{\cosh(2K)}$, which contributes an additive constant to the free energy but does not affect the coupling.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (medium, symbolic).**

Find all fixed points of the 1D Ising decimation map $K' = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K)$ and classify their stability.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A fixed point satisfies $K^* = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K^*)$. Check $K^* = 0$ and $K^* = \infty$. Evaluate the derivative $dK'/dK$ at each.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$K^* = 0$ (high-temperature fixed point):** $\tfrac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(0) = 0$. Check: $dK'/dK = \tanh(2K)$, so $dK'/dK\big|_{K=0} = 0 < 1$. This fixed point is **stable** — all couplings flow to zero under iteration. In the 1D Ising model there is no phase transition; every temperature maps to this fixed point.

**$K^* = \infty$ (zero-temperature fixed point):** $\tfrac{1}{2}\ln\cosh(2K) \to K$ as $K \to \infty$ (since $\cosh x \sim e^x/2$ for large $x$). Check: $dK'/dK\big|_{K \to \infty} = \tanh(\infty) = 1$. This fixed point is **marginally stable from below** — it takes infinitely many iterations to reach it from any finite $K$, but $K$ grows monotonically. It is not a true critical point because the correlation length at $K = \infty$ is infinite but the fixed point is not approached from both sides.

There are no other fixed points. The 1D Ising model has no finite-$T$ phase transition, and the RG flow reflects this: the only attractive fixed point is $K^* = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 10 (hard, symbolic).**

The 2D Ising model on a square lattice has an exact RG transformation due to Niemeijer and van Leeuwen (1974). At the simplest approximation (first-order cumulant expansion), the RG map for the nearest-neighbour coupling is $K' = 2K^2 + O(K^3)$. Find the non-zero fixed point, compute $y_t$ and the correlation-length exponent $\nu$, and compare with the exact Onsager value $\nu = 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The non-zero fixed point satisfies $K^* = 2(K^*)^2$. Solve, then compute $dK'/dK$ at $K^*$ and extract $y_t$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fixed points: $K^* = 0$ (high-$T$, disordered) and $K^* = 1/2$ (non-zero). At $K^* = 1/2$:

$$
\frac{dK'}{dK}\bigg|_{K=1/2} = 4K^*\big|_{K^*=1/2} = 2.
$$

For a block rescaling factor $b = \sqrt{2}$ (Niemeijer–van Leeuwen choice for the square lattice, blocks of $2 \times 2$ with majority rule), the thermal eigenvalue is $\lambda_t = 2$, so

$$
y_t = \frac{\ln \lambda_t}{\ln b} = \frac{\ln 2}{\ln \sqrt{2}} = 2.
$$

Therefore $\nu = 1/y_t = 1/2$. This is a crude approximation — the exact Onsager result is $\nu = 1$. The discrepancy comes from the first-order cumulant truncation, which neglects higher-order interactions generated by the RG. Including next-nearest-neighbour and four-spin couplings (second-order cumulant) gives $\nu \approx 0.9$, much closer to the exact answer.

</details>

## Momentum-shell RG and the Wilsonian programme [Master]

The block-spin RG is conceptually clear but cumbersome for computation. Wilson's **momentum-shell RG** is the field-theoretic version that makes systematic perturbation theory possible.

Start with a coarse-grained field theory for an $n$-component order parameter $\boldsymbol{\phi}(\mathbf{x})$ with Hamiltonian

$$
\mathcal{H}[\boldsymbol{\phi}] = \frac{1}{2}\!\int\! d^d x \left[(\nabla \boldsymbol{\phi})^2 + r\,\boldsymbol{\phi}^2\right] + \frac{u}{4!}\!\int\! d^d x\,(\boldsymbol{\phi}^2)^2.
$$

Decompose $\boldsymbol{\phi}$ into Fourier modes. The RG step consists of:

1. **Integrate out** the "fast" modes with $\Lambda/b < |\mathbf{k}| < \Lambda$ (the momentum shell).
2. **Rescale** momenta $\mathbf{k}' = b\mathbf{k}$ to restore the cutoff to $\Lambda$.
3. **Rescale** the field $\boldsymbol{\phi}' = b^{\zeta}\boldsymbol{\phi}$ to keep the gradient term normalised.

The rescaling exponent $\zeta = 1 + \eta/2$ is determined by demanding that the kinetic term stays canonical at the fixed point. The result is a set of **RG flow equations** for the couplings $(r, u, \ldots)$ as functions of the logarithmic rescaling parameter $\ell = \ln b$.

At one loop in $d = 4 - \epsilon$ the flow equations for the $O(n)$ model are:

$$
\frac{dr}{d\ell} = 2r + \frac{n+2}{6}\frac{u}{(4\pi)^2}\,r + \ldots, \qquad \frac{du}{d\ell} = -\epsilon\, u + \frac{n+8}{6}\frac{u^2}{(4\pi)^2} + \ldots
$$

The fixed point $u^* = 6(4\pi)^2\epsilon/(n+8)$ is the Wilson-Fisher fixed point. The Gaussian fixed point $u^* = 0$ is unstable for $d < 4$.

### The Callan-Symanzik equation

For a field theory at or near a fixed point, the two-point correlation function $G(\mathbf{k}; u, r, \Lambda)$ satisfies the **Callan-Symanzik equation**

$$
\left[\Lambda \frac{\partial}{\partial \Lambda} + \beta(u)\frac{\partial}{\partial u} + \gamma_\phi(u)\left(d/2 - 1 + \eta/2\right)\right] G = 0,
$$

where $\beta(u) = du/d\ell$ is the beta function and $\gamma_\phi$ is the anomalous dimension. At the Wilson-Fisher fixed point, $\beta(u^*) = 0$ and the anomalous dimension is $\eta = 2\gamma_\phi(u^*)$. This determines the critical two-point function $G(\mathbf{k}) \sim k^{-2+\eta}$ — the defining property of $\eta$.

### Upper and lower critical dimensions

The **upper critical dimension** $d_c$ is the dimension above which mean-field theory gives correct critical exponents. For short-range interactions with a scalar order parameter, $d_c = 4$. The **lower critical dimension** $d_l$ is the dimension below which there is no ordered phase at any finite temperature. For discrete symmetry (Ising), $d_l = 1$; for continuous symmetry ($O(n)$, $n \geq 2$), $d_l = 2$ (Mermin-Wagner theorem). The dimension of interest $d_l < d < d_c$ is the critical-dimensional window where non-mean-field universal behaviour exists.

### The c-theorem (Zamolodchikov)

In two dimensions, Zamolodchikov's **c-theorem** (1986) states that there exists a function $c(g)$ of the coupling constants that is non-increasing along RG flows and stationary only at fixed points. At a fixed point, $c$ coincides with the central charge of the associated conformal field theory. The theorem provides a rigorous notion of "irreversibility" of RG flow and constrains which fixed points can flow to which: a theory with central charge $c_{\rm UV}$ in the ultraviolet can flow only to a theory with $c_{\rm IR} \leq c_{\rm UV}$ in the infrared. In four dimensions, the analogous statement is the **a-theorem** (proven by Komargodski and Schwimmer, 2011).

### Conformal field theory at criticality

At a critical fixed point the system is scale-invariant and, in most physically relevant cases, conformally invariant. In $d = 2$ the conformal group is infinite-dimensional and the resulting **conformal field theories** (CFTs) are exactly solvable by representation-theoretic methods (Belavin-Polyakov-Zamolodchikov, 1984). The minimal models, classified by pairs of coprime integers $(p, p')$, provide an infinite family of solvable critical points. The 2D Ising model at $T_c$ is the minimal model $(4, 3)$ with central charge $c = 1/2$.

### The exact RG for the 2D Ising model

The 2D Ising model is the paradigmatic example where the RG programme closes completely. Onsager's exact solution (1944) gives $\alpha = 0$ (logarithmic divergence), $\beta = 1/8$, $\gamma = 7/4$, $\delta = 15$, $\nu = 1$, $\eta = 1/4$. The RG interpretation: the critical fixed point has two relevant eigenvalues $y_t = 1$ (thermal) and $y_h = 15/8$ (magnetic), reproducing all exponents via the scaling relations. The equivalence with the free-fermion CFT ($c = 1/2$) identifies the fixed point as a specific minimal model, and the exact S-matrix of the massive integrable field theory (Zamolodchikov, 1989) describes the RG flow away from the fixed point for $T \neq T_c$.

## Historical notes [Master]

The renormalization group has two independent origins.

**Kadanoff (1966)** introduced the block-spin picture as a physical argument for scaling and universality. The key insight was that the divergent correlation length at $T_c$ means no single microscopic scale dominates — the physics must organise itself by how it transforms under changes of scale. Kadanoff's paper ("Scaling laws for Ising models near $T_c$," *Physics* 2, 263) deduced the scaling form of the free energy and the scaling relations among critical exponents without computing any of them.

**Wilson (1971–1974)** turned Kadanoff's picture into a calculational tool. Wilson's key contributions were: (i) the momentum-shell RG, which makes the coarse-graining procedure systematic in field theory; (ii) the $\epsilon$-expansion, which provides a controlled perturbative access to the Wilson-Fisher fixed point; (iii) the connection to earlier renormalization in quantum field theory (Gell-Mann–Low, 1954), unifying the two notions of "renormalization." Wilson's 1971 papers ("Renormalization group and critical phenomena I and II," *Physical Review* B4, 3174 and 3184) and the 1974 review with Kogut (*Physics Reports* 12C, 75–199) are the foundational documents.

**Fisher (1974, 1998)** systematised the classification of scaling variables and the notion of "crossover" between fixed points. **Zamolodchikov (1986)** proved the $c$-theorem, establishing an irreversibility property of RG flows in 2D and connecting the RG programme to conformal field theory.

## Connections [Master]

**Quantum field theory and high-energy physics.** The Wilsonian RG was recognised by Wilson himself as the physical meaning of the Gell-Mann–Low renormalization group in quantum electrodynamics. The RG flow of the $\phi^4$ coupling in 4 dimensions — the beta function $\beta(g) = -\beta_0 g^3/(16\pi^2) + \ldots$ — is the origin of "asymptotic freedom" in non-Abelian gauge theories (Gross, Politzer, Wilczek, 1973). The conceptual framework is identical: the coupling runs with scale, and the direction of the flow determines the physics. In QFT the running is logarithmic rather than power-law (because $d = d_c = 4$), but the structure is the same.

**Quantum criticality and condensed matter.** At zero temperature, quantum fluctuations replace thermal fluctuations as the driver of phase transitions. The quantum-to-classical correspondence maps a $d$-dimensional quantum system at $T = 0$ to a $(d + 1)$-dimensional classical system, with imaginary time as the extra dimension. The RG framework carries over directly: the upper critical dimension of the quantum transverse-field Ising model is $d_c = 3$ (spatial dimensions), and the quantum critical exponents are those of the 4D classical Ising model. Hertz (1976) and Millis (1993) developed the quantum RG formalism.

**Statistical inference and machine learning.** The RG has been reinterpreted as a variational optimisation procedure (Li and Wang, 2018; Lenggenhager et al., 2020): coarse-graining is compression, and the RG flow minimises a variational free energy. Deep neural networks trained on Ising configurations learn representations that mirror the RG hierarchy (Mehta and Schwab, 2014). The mathematical content of this correspondence is still being clarified, but the structural analogy — relevant features survive compression, irrelevant ones are discarded — is precise.
