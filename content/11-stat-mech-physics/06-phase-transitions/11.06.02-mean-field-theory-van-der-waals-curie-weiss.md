## Intuition [Beginner]

Mean-field theory is the simplest approach to phase transitions. The idea: replace the complicated interactions between many particles with an effective average interaction. Each particle feels the "mean field" produced by all its neighbours, and this field depends on the state of the system as a whole.

For a magnet, each spin feels an effective magnetic field proportional to the average magnetisation of the whole system. If most spins point up, the effective field pushes each spin to point up — a positive feedback loop. Temperature provides the opposing force: thermal fluctuations randomise spins. The competition between ordering (from the mean field) and disordering (from temperature) produces a phase transition at a critical temperature $T_c$.

Below $T_c$, the ordering wins and a spontaneous magnetisation $m$ appears. Above $T_c$, disorder wins and $m = 0$. The magnetisation grows continuously from zero as you cool below $T_c$, following a power law $m \sim (T_c - T)^{1/2}$.

Mean-field theory gives the same critical exponents for the van der Waals fluid (liquid-gas transition) and the Curie-Weiss magnet (ferromagnetic transition). This universality is not a coincidence — the two systems share the same mathematical structure, with pressure playing the role of magnetic field and density playing the role of magnetisation.

## Worked example [Beginner]

For the Curie-Weiss model, the self-consistency equation is $m = \tanh(mT_c/T)$ where $T_c = Jz/k$ ($J$ is the coupling, $z$ is the number of neighbours). At $T = T_c/2$ (half the critical temperature):

$m = \tanh(2m)$.

Trying $m = 0.96$: $\tanh(1.92) = 0.9576$. Close but not exact. Trying $m = 0.958$: $\tanh(1.916) = 0.957$. Converging to $m \approx 0.957$.

Below $T_c$, the magnetisation rises quickly toward $m = 1$ (fully aligned). At $T = 0$, $m = 1$ exactly (all spins aligned, no thermal fluctuations).

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### Curie-Weiss model

The **Curie-Weiss Hamiltonian** for $N$ spins $s_i = \pm 1$ is

$$H = -\frac{J}{2N}\left(\sum_{i=1}^N s_i\right)^2 - h\sum_{i=1}^N s_i,$$

where $J > 0$ is the ferromagnetic coupling and $h$ is the external field. The factor $1/N$ ensures extensivity (the interaction energy scales as $N$, not $N^2$). Every spin interacts with every other spin equally — this is an infinite-range model, not a lattice model with nearest-neighbour interactions.

The partition function is

$$Z = \sum_{\{s_i\}} \exp\left[\frac{\beta J}{2N}\left(\sum_i s_i\right)^2 + \beta h \sum_i s_i\right].$$

Evaluating the sum by the **Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation** (a Gaussian linearisation trick):

$$Z = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{dx}{\sqrt{2\pi/N\beta J}} \exp\left[-\frac{N\beta J x^2}{2} + N\ln(2\cosh(\beta Jx + \beta h))\right].$$

In the thermodynamic limit $N \to \infty$, the integral is dominated by its saddle point, obtained by maximising the exponent:

$$x = \tanh(\beta Jx + \beta h).$$

Identifying $m = x$ (the magnetisation per spin), this is the **self-consistency equation**

$$\boxed{m = \tanh\left(\frac{T_c m}{T} + \frac{h}{kT}\right), \qquad T_c = \frac{J}{k}.}$$

### Mean-field free energy

The mean-field free energy per spin is

$$f(m) = \frac{1}{2}kT_c\, m^2 - kT\ln(2\cosh(T_c m/T + h/kT)).$$

For $h = 0$ and $T$ near $T_c$, expanding in powers of $m$:

$$f(m) \approx -kT\ln 2 + \frac{1}{2}k(T - T_c)\, m^2 + \frac{kT_c^3}{12T^2}\, m^4 + \cdots.$$

The equilibrium magnetisation minimises $f$. For $T > T_c$: the minimum is at $m = 0$. For $T < T_c$: the quartic term stabilises a minimum at $m \neq 0$.

### Key result: Mean-field critical exponents

Near $T_c$ with $h = 0$:

- **Order parameter**: $m \sim (T_c - T)^\beta$ with $\beta = 1/2$ for $T < T_c$.
- **Susceptibility**: $\chi = (\partial m / \partial h)_{h=0} \sim |T - T_c|^{-\gamma}$ with $\gamma = 1$.
- **Critical isotherm**: $h \sim m^\delta$ at $T = T_c$ with $\delta = 3$.
- **Specific heat**: discontinuity at $T_c$ with $\alpha = 0$ (jump, not divergence).

These are the **mean-field (Landau) critical exponents**, shared by the van der Waals gas, the Curie-Weiss model, and all mean-field theories regardless of microscopic details.

## Key derivation: Self-consistency and critical exponents [Intermediate+]

**Proposition.** *The Curie-Weiss self-consistency equation $m = \tanh(T_c m/T)$ has a non-zero solution for $T < T_c$, and the critical exponents are $\beta = 1/2$, $\gamma = 1$, $\delta = 3$.*

**Proof.** For $h = 0$: $m = \tanh(T_c m/T)$. Near $T_c$, $m$ is small, and $\tanh(x) \approx x - x^3/3$. So:

$$m \approx \frac{T_c m}{T} - \frac{1}{3}\left(\frac{T_c m}{T}\right)^3.$$

For $m \neq 0$, divide by $m$: $1 = T_c/T - (T_c/T)^3 m^2/3$. Let $t = (T_c - T)/T_c$. To first order in $t$: $T_c/T = 1 + t$, so $1 = 1 + t - (1+t)^3 m^2/3 \approx 1 + t - m^2/3$. Hence $t = m^2/3$, giving

$$m = \sqrt{3t} = \sqrt{3(T_c-T)/T_c} \sim (T_c - T)^{1/2}, \qquad \beta = 1/2.$$

For the susceptibility, add a small field $h$: $m = \tanh(T_c m/T + h/kT)$. Linearising in $h$: $\chi = (\partial m/\partial h)_{h=0} = (T_c\chi/T + 1)/(kT) / (1 - T_c/T)$... more carefully:

$\chi^{-1} = kT(1/T_c - 1/T)/(1/T_c) = k(T - T_c)$ for $T > T_c$. So $\chi = k/(T - T_c) \sim (T - T_c)^{-1}$, giving $\gamma = 1$.

For the critical isotherm ($T = T_c$, $h \neq 0$): $m = \tanh(m + h/kT_c)$. For small $m$ and $h$: $m \approx m + h/kT_c - m^3/3$, so $h/kT_c = m^3/3$, hence $h \sim m^3$ and $\delta = 3$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The mean-field critical exponents build toward the renormalization group [11.07.01, 11.07.02], which explains why mean-field exponents are universal and when they are correct. This is exactly the content of the Ginzburg criterion [11.06.04]: mean-field theory is self-consistent when fluctuations are small compared to the mean-field order parameter, which happens above the upper critical dimension $d_c = 4$. The foundational reason mean-field theory works at all is that the infinite-range interaction (every spin couples to every other spin) suppresses fluctuations by a factor $1/\sqrt{N}$, and in the thermodynamic limit fluctuations vanish entirely. This generalises to short-range interactions in high enough dimension ($d > 4$) where the coordination number is effectively infinite, putting these together with the Landau theory [11.06.03] provides the universal framework for all mean-field phase transitions.

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that the Curie-Weiss self-consistency equation $m = \tanh(T_c m/T)$ has $m = 0$ as a solution for all $T$, and that a non-zero solution exists for $T < T_c$ by checking the slope of $\tanh(T_c m/T)$ at $m = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint><summary>Hint</summary>

The non-zero solution bifurcates from $m = 0$ when the slope of $\tanh$ at the origin exceeds 1.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

At $m = 0$: $\tanh(0) = 0$, so $m = 0$ is always a solution. The slope of $\tanh(T_c m/T)$ at $m = 0$ is $T_c/T$. When $T_c/T > 1$ (i.e., $T < T_c$), the slope exceeds 1 and the tanh curve starts above the identity line, guaranteeing a non-zero intersection. When $T > T_c$, the slope is less than 1 and $m = 0$ is the only solution.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

The mean-field specific heat of the Curie-Weiss model has a discontinuity $\Delta C = 3/(2kT_c)$ per spin at $T_c$. Compute the jump.

<details class="exercise__hint><summary>Hint</summary>

$C = -T(\partial^2 f/\partial T^2)$. Use $f \approx -kT\ln 2 + k(T-T_c)m^2/2$ and $m^2 = 3(T_c-T)/T_c$ for $T < T_c$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

For $T > T_c$: $m = 0$, so $f = -kT\ln 2$ and $C = 0$ (on top of the constant background).

Wait, the internal energy per spin is $u = -(1/2)Jm^2$. For $T > T_c$: $u = 0$. For $T < T_c$: $u = -(1/2)J \cdot 3(T_c-T)/T_c = -(3J/2T_c)(T_c - T)$.

$C = du/dT = 3J/(2T_c) = 3k/2$ per spin (using $J = kT_c$). So the specific heat jump at $T_c$ is $\Delta C = 3k/2$ per spin. The exponent $\alpha = 0$ (discontinuity, not divergence).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the mean-field free energy $f(m) = a(T-T_c)m^2 + bm^4$ (with $a, b > 0$) reproduces the Curie-Weiss critical exponents. Minimise $f$ for $T > T_c$ and $T < T_c$.

<details class="exercise__hint><summary>Hint</summary>

The equilibrium $m$ satisfies $df/dm = 0$. Solve for $m$ in both regimes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

$df/dm = 2a(T-T_c)m + 4bm^3 = 0$. Solutions: $m = 0$ or $m^2 = a(T_c - T)/(2b)$.

For $T > T_c$: $m^2 < 0$ from the second solution, so the only real solution is $m = 0$ (paramagnetic phase).

For $T < T_c$: $m^2 = a(T_c - T)/(2b) > 0$, giving $m = \pm\sqrt{a(T_c-T)/(2b)} \sim (T_c - T)^{1/2}$, so $\beta = 1/2$.

The susceptibility: $\chi = 1/(d^2f/dm^2)|_{m=0}$. For $T > T_c$: $d^2f/dm^2|_{m=0} = 2a(T-T_c)$, so $\chi = 1/(2a(T-T_c)) \sim (T-T_c)^{-1}$, $\gamma = 1$. For $T < T_c$: $d^2f/dm^2|_{m=m_0} = 2a(T-T_c) + 12bm_0^2 = 2a(T-T_c) + 6a(T_c-T) = 4a(T_c-T)$, so $\chi = 1/(4a(T_c-T)) \sim (T_c-T)^{-1}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Map the van der Waals equation near the critical point to the Curie-Weiss model by identifying: $\Delta\rho \leftrightarrow m$, $\Delta P \leftrightarrow h$, $\Delta T \leftrightarrow T - T_c$. Show that both have the same Landau free energy form.

<details class="exercise__hint><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand the van der Waals equation in reduced variables near the critical point and compare with $f = a(T-T_c)m^2 + bm^4 - hm$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

Near the critical point, the van der Waals pressure (in reduced variables with $p = (P-P_c)/P_c$, $\phi = (v-v_c)/v_c$, $t = (T-T_c)/T_c$) expands as $p \approx 4t - 6t\phi - (3/2)\phi^3$. The Gibbs free energy per molecule is $g = g_c + \text{terms involving } p \text{ and } t$. Integrating $p$ with respect to $\phi$ gives a free energy of the form $f(\phi) = -4t\phi + 3t\phi^2 + (3/8)\phi^4$.

With the mapping $\phi \leftrightarrow -m$ (or $m = -(v - v_c)/v_c$, so $m$ measures the density deviation): $f(m) = 4tm + 3tm^2 + (3/8)m^4$. Near $T_c$, the coefficient of $m^2$ changes sign: for $T < T_c$ it is negative (ordering), for $T > T_c$ it is positive (disordering). The pressure term $4tm$ plays the role of the external field $h$. This is exactly the Landau form $f = hm + a(T-T_c)m^2 + bm^4$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the mean-field correlation length diverges as $\xi \sim |T - T_c|^{-1/2}$ near the critical point, giving the exponent $\nu = 1/2$.

<details class="exercise__hint><summary>Hint</summary>

The correlation function in mean-field theory satisfies $(k - \nabla^2)G(r) = \delta(r)$ where $k \propto |T - T_c|$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

In mean-field theory, the Ornstein-Zernike form of the correlation function is $G(r) \sim e^{-r/\xi}/r^{(d-1)/2}$ where $\xi = \sqrt{c/k}$, $c$ is a constant (the "stiffness"), and $k = a|T - T_c|$ is the coefficient of the quadratic term in the free energy. So $\xi \sim |T - T_c|^{-1/2}$, giving $\nu = 1/2$.

The hyperscaling relation $d\nu = 2 - \alpha$ gives $d \cdot (1/2) = 2 - 0 = 2$, so $d = 4$. Hyperscaling is satisfied only at $d = 4$ — the upper critical dimension. For $d < 4$, hyperscaling fails with mean-field exponents, indicating that fluctuations (neglected by mean-field theory) modify the critical behaviour.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Evaluate the Curie-Weiss partition function in the thermodynamic limit by the saddle-point method. Show that the free energy per spin is $f = -kT \min_x [\beta Jx^2/2 - \ln(2\cosh(\beta Jx + \beta h))]$.

<details class="exercise__hint><summary>Hint</summary>

Start from $Z = \int dx\, \exp(-Ng(x))$ where $g(x) = \beta Jx^2/2 - \ln(2\cosh(\beta Jx + \beta h))$. Apply Laplace's method as $N \to \infty$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

The Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation gives

$Z = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dx\, \sqrt{\frac{N\beta J}{2\pi}}\, \exp\left[N\left(-\frac{\beta Jx^2}{2} + \ln(2\cosh(\beta Jx + \beta h))\right)\right].$

As $N \to \infty$, the integral is dominated by the saddle point $x^*$ that maximises the exponent:

$\frac{d}{dx}\left[-\frac{\beta Jx^2}{2} + \ln(2\cosh(\beta Jx + \beta h))\right] = 0,$

$-\beta Jx + \beta J\tanh(\beta Jx + \beta h) = 0,$

$x = \tanh(\beta Jx + \beta h).$

This is the self-consistency equation with $m = x$. The free energy per spin is

$f = -\frac{kT}{N}\ln Z \to kT \cdot g(x^*) = \frac{J(x^*)^2}{2} - kT\ln(2\cosh(\beta Jx^* + \beta h)).$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Compute the mean-field critical exponents for the Blume-Capel model (spin-1 Ising model with spins $s_i = -1, 0, +1$ and Hamiltonian $H = -J\sum_{\langle ij\rangle}s_is_j + D\sum_i s_i^2 - h\sum_i s_i$) in the mean-field approximation.

<details class="exercise__hint><summary>Hint</summary>

The mean-field equation is $m = (2\sinh(\beta Jzm + \beta h))/(2\cosh(\beta Jzm + \beta h) + e^{\beta D})$ where $z$ is the coordination number.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

The mean-field partition function for one spin in effective field $h_{\rm eff} = Jzm + h$ is $Z_1 = e^{\beta h_{\rm eff}} + 1 + e^{-\beta h_{\rm eff}} = 2\cosh(\beta h_{\rm eff}) + e^{\beta D}$.

The magnetisation is $\langle s \rangle = \partial \ln Z_1 / \partial (\beta h_{\rm eff}) = 2\sinh(\beta h_{\rm eff})/(2\cosh(\beta h_{\rm eff}) + e^{\beta D})$.

Setting $m = \langle s \rangle$ with $h = 0$: $m = 2\sinh(\beta Jzm)/(2\cosh(\beta Jzm) + e^{\beta D})$.

Near the critical point ($m$ small): $m \approx 2\beta Jzm/(2 + e^{\beta D})$, so $T_c = 2Jz/(k(2 + e^{\beta_c D}))$. This is a self-consistent equation for $T_c$ because $\beta_c$ appears on both sides.

The critical exponents are the same mean-field values: $\beta = 1/2$, $\gamma = 1$, $\delta = 3$. The Blume-Capel model has the same universality class as the Ising model in mean-field theory. The crystal-field $D$ shifts $T_c$ but does not change the critical exponents — a manifestation of universality.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Show that the Ginzburg criterion for the validity of mean-field theory in $d$ dimensions is $|T - T_c|^{(4-d)/2} \gg 1$ (i.e., mean-field theory is valid for $d > 4$ or far from $T_c$). Use the fluctuation-dissipation theorem and the mean-field correlation length.

<details class="exercise__hint><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare the mean-square fluctuation $\langle(\delta m)^2\rangle \sim kT\chi/\xi^d$ (from the fluctuation-dissipation theorem) with the square of the mean-field order parameter $m^2 \sim |T-T_c|$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer><summary>Answer</summary>

The mean-field order parameter is $m^2 \sim |T - T_c|$. The mean-square fluctuation of the order parameter in a correlation volume $\xi^d$ is $\langle(\delta m)^2\rangle \sim kT\chi/\xi^d$ where $\chi \sim |T-T_c|^{-1}$ and $\xi \sim |T-T_c|^{-1/2}$.

So $\langle(\delta m)^2\rangle \sim |T-T_c|^{-1} \cdot |T-T_c|^{d/2} = |T-T_c|^{(d-2)/2}$.

Mean-field theory is self-consistent when $\langle(\delta m)^2\rangle \ll m^2$, i.e., $|T-T_c|^{(d-2)/2} \ll |T-T_c|$. This gives $|T - T_c|^{(4-d)/2} \gg 1$.

For $d > 4$: $(4-d)/2 < 0$, so $|T-T_c|^{(4-d)/2} \to \infty$ as $T \to T_c$. Mean-field theory is always valid near $T_c$ in $d > 4$.

For $d < 4$: $(4-d)/2 > 0$, so $|T-T_c|^{(4-d)/2} \to 0$ as $T \to T_c$. Mean-field theory always fails sufficiently close to $T_c$.

For $d = 4$: marginal case — logarithmic corrections to mean-field behaviour.

The upper critical dimension for the Ising universality class is $d_c = 4$.

</details>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

Mean-field theory requires: (1) a self-consistency equation as a fixed-point condition; (2) a proof that the fixed point bifurcates at a critical temperature; (3) extraction of critical exponents from power-series expansions near the bifurcation. The bifurcation analysis is within Mathlib's scope (implicit function theorem, Taylor expansion), but the physical context (magnetisation as an order parameter, critical exponents as observables) requires a domain-specific framework that does not exist.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Universality of mean-field exponents

All mean-field theories — Curie-Weiss, van der Waals, Landau, Bragg-Williams — give the same critical exponents $\beta = 1/2$, $\gamma = 1$, $\delta = 3$, $\alpha = 0$, $\nu = 1/2$, $\eta = 0$. This universality holds regardless of the microscopic details because mean-field theory replaces all interactions by an average field, erasing information about the lattice structure, the interaction range, and the dimensionality. The only remaining information is the symmetry of the order parameter (scalar for Ising, vector for Heisenberg) and the analytic structure of the free energy.

### Mean-field theory for the Heisenberg model

For a model with $O(n)$ symmetry (e.g., the Heisenberg model with $n = 3$), the mean-field free energy is $f = a(T-T_c)|\mathbf{m}|^2 + b|\mathbf{m}|^4$. The critical exponents are the same as for the Ising model ($\beta = 1/2$, etc.) because the quartic term still stabilises the ordered phase. The $O(n)$ symmetry affects the structure of fluctuations (Goldstone modes for $n \geq 2$ in $d \leq 2$, by the Mermin-Wagner theorem) but not the mean-field critical exponents.

### Relation to exact results

The 1D Ising model has no phase transition at $T > 0$ (exact result), yet mean-field theory predicts $T_c = Jz/k > 0$ for any coordination $z$. The failure occurs because 1D fluctuations are too strong. The 2D Ising model has $T_c > 0$ but with exponents $\beta = 1/8$, $\gamma = 7/4$, $\delta = 15$ — far from the mean-field values. The 4D Ising model (and above) has mean-field exponents, confirming the Ginzburg criterion.

**Synthesis.** Mean-field theory builds toward the renormalization group [11.07.01, 11.07.02], which explains both why mean-field exponents are universal and why they fail below $d = 4$. The central insight is that mean-field theory neglects spatial fluctuations, treating the whole system as uniform. This is dual to the Landau approach [11.06.03], which retains spatial variation but still treats fluctuations perturbatively. The foundational reason mean-field theory gives the correct exponents above $d = 4$ is that the coordination number grows with dimension, making the effective interaction range infinite in high enough dimension; putting these together, mean-field theory is the large-$d$ limit of the exact theory, and the Ginzburg criterion [11.06.04] quantifies the correction at finite $d$. The generalisation from mean-field to RG is the passage from uniform to scale-dependent order parameters, and this is exactly the content of the block-spin transformation [11.07.02].

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition.** *The Landau free energy $f(m) = a(T-T_c)m^2 + bm^4 - hm$ (with $a, b > 0$) reproduces all four mean-field critical exponents.*

**Proof.** Equilibrium: $\partial f/\partial m = 2a(T-T_c)m + 4bm^3 - h = 0$.

**Exponent $\beta$** ($h = 0$, $T < T_c$): $2a(T-T_c)m + 4bm^3 = 0$. Non-zero solution: $m^2 = a(T_c-T)/(2b)$, so $m \sim (T_c-T)^{1/2}$, $\beta = 1/2$.

**Exponent $\gamma$** ($h = 0$, $T > T_c$): Linearise in $h$: $m \approx h/(2a(T-T_c))$. So $\chi = m/h = 1/(2a(T-T_c)) \sim (T-T_c)^{-1}$, $\gamma = 1$.

**Exponent $\delta$** ($T = T_c$): $4bm^3 = h$, so $h \sim m^3$, $\delta = 3$.

**Exponent $\alpha$** ($h = 0$): For $T > T_c$, $m = 0$ and $C = -T\partial^2 f/\partial T^2 = 0$ (the free energy is independent of $T$ at $m = 0$). For $T < T_c$: $f = a(T-T_c)m^2 + bm^4 = a(T-T_c) \cdot a(T_c-T)/(2b) + b \cdot a^2(T_c-T)^2/(4b^2) = -a^2(T_c-T)^2/(4b)$. So $C = -T\partial^2 f/\partial T^2 = Ta^2/(2b)$ — a constant. The specific heat has a finite jump $\Delta C = T_c a^2/(2b)$ at $T_c$, giving $\alpha = 0$ (discontinuity). $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **[11.06.01]** The Ising model is the microscopic theory; mean-field theory is its approximate solution. The exact 2D Ising solution gives different exponents, demonstrating the limitations of mean-field theory.
- **[11.06.03]** Landau theory generalises mean-field theory by expanding the free energy in powers of the order parameter, providing a universal framework for all mean-field phase transitions.
- **[11.06.04]** The Ginzburg criterion determines when mean-field theory fails by comparing fluctuation magnitudes to the mean-field order parameter.
- **[11.07.01]** The renormalization group explains why mean-field exponents are correct above $d = 4$ and provides the corrected exponents for $d < 4$.
- **[11.01.05]** The van der Waals gas is the fluid analogue of the Curie-Weiss magnet; both are mean-field theories with identical critical exponents.

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

Pierre Weiss proposed the molecular-field hypothesis in 1907 to explain ferromagnetism. His insight was that each magnetic atom experiences an internal field proportional to the average magnetisation — a self-consistent condition that produces a phase transition. The Weiss theory correctly predicted the existence of a Curie temperature and the general shape of the magnetisation curve, though the critical exponents were later found to be inaccurate in two and three dimensions.

The Curie-Weiss model (an infinite-range Ising model) was introduced as a mathematically tractable version of the Ising model that can be solved exactly. Its partition function is computable by the Hubbard-Stratonovich transformation, and the thermodynamic limit can be taken rigorously. This makes it a benchmark for testing approximation methods and a starting point for understanding when mean-field theory is exact.

The connection between the van der Waals fluid and the Curie-Weiss magnet — the density-magnetisation mapping — was recognised by Landau (1937), who unified all mean-field theories into a single framework based on the order parameter and the symmetry of the free energy.
