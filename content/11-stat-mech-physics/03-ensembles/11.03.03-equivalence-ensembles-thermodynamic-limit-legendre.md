## Intuition [Beginner]

Consider a box of gas. You can describe it in three ways:

1. **Fixed energy (microcanonical).** The box is perfectly isolated. The total energy $E$ is locked in, the particle number $N$ is fixed, and the volume $V$ is fixed. You count every microscopic arrangement consistent with those constraints and define the entropy $S$ from the count. The temperature $T$ is a derived quantity: it measures how fast $S$ changes when you add a little energy.

2. **Fixed temperature (canonical).** The box sits in a heat bath. The temperature $T$ is held constant by exchanging energy with the bath, but $N$ and $V$ are still fixed. You weight each microscopic state by $e^{-E/(kT)}$ and normalise. The key quantity is the Helmholtz free energy $F = E - TS$, which acts as an "effective energy" at fixed temperature.

3. **Fixed temperature and chemical potential (grand canonical).** The box is connected to a reservoir that can exchange both energy *and* particles. The temperature $T$ and chemical potential $\mu$ are fixed by the reservoir. You weight each state by $e^{-(E - \mu N)/(kT)}$. The grand potential $\Omega = F - \mu N$ is the governing quantity.

Three different descriptions. Three different probability distributions. Three different thermodynamic potentials ($S$, $F$, $\Omega$).

**The central claim: for a macroscopic system — one with a large number of particles — all three descriptions give the same answers for observable quantities.** The pressure, the compressibility, the heat capacity, the equation of state — all agree. The three ensembles are equivalent in the thermodynamic limit.

Why? Because the probability distributions are incredibly sharp. In the canonical ensemble, the energy fluctuates — but the fluctuation $\Delta E$ grows only as $\sqrt{N}$ while the mean energy $\langle E \rangle$ grows as $N$. So $\Delta E / \langle E \rangle \sim 1/\sqrt{N}$, which is one part in $10^{12}$ for a mole of gas. The canonical energy is "almost fixed" — almost microcanonical. The same argument applies to particle-number fluctuations in the grand canonical ensemble: $\Delta N / \langle N \rangle \sim 1/\sqrt{N}$.

The three potentials are related by **Legendre transforms** — mathematical operations that swap one variable for its conjugate. The entropy $S(E)$ is a function of energy; its Legendre transform in $E$ gives $-F/(kT)$ as a function of $1/(kT)$. The free energy $F$ as a function of $N$ has its Legendre transform in $N$ giving $\Omega$ as a function of $\mu$. These transforms are exact in the thermodynamic limit; for finite systems there are correction terms that vanish as $N$ grows.

## Visual [Beginner]

Imagine three rooms, each containing the same gas, but set up differently:

- **Room A** has perfectly insulated walls. No heat in, no heat out. The energy is fixed. A dial on the wall shows the temperature — but it fluctuates slightly because the gas molecules redistribute their kinetic energy among themselves.

- **Room B** has walls that conduct heat to a vast thermal reservoir outside. The temperature is rock-steady. But a sensitive energy meter shows tiny fluctuations — the room gains and loses small amounts of energy from the reservoir.

- **Room C** has both a thermal connection and a small pipe to the reservoir, so molecules can drift in and out. Both temperature and chemical potential are steady, but both the energy and the particle number fluctuate.

Now make the rooms enormous — a swimming pool of gas, then a lake, then an ocean. In each room, the fluctuations become proportionally tinier. The energy dial in Room A steadies. The energy meter in Room B and the particle counter in Room C both show that $\Delta E / E$ and $\Delta N / N$ are heading toward zero. All three rooms converge to the same behaviour.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take an ideal gas with $N$ particles in volume $V$ at temperature $T$. Compare the energy predictions from the three ensembles.

**Microcanonical.** The energy is exactly $E = \frac{3}{2}NkT$. No fluctuations.

**Canonical.** The mean energy is $\langle E \rangle = \frac{3}{2}NkT$ — the same. The fluctuation is $\Delta E = \sqrt{\frac{3}{2}N}\,kT$. So the relative fluctuation is

$$\frac{\Delta E}{\langle E \rangle} \;=\; \sqrt{\frac{2}{3N}}.$$

For $N = 10^{23}$ (a mole), this is about $10^{-12}$ — utterly negligible. For $N = 10$ (a tiny cluster), it is about $0.26$ (26 percent) — enormous.

**Grand canonical.** The mean energy is again $\frac{3}{2}\langle N \rangle kT$, and both $E$ and $N$ fluctuate. But the relative fluctuations in both quantities scale as $1/\sqrt{N}$ and vanish for large $N$.

The message: for macroscopic systems, the choice of ensemble is a matter of convenience, not physics. You pick the one that makes your calculation easiest.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

### The thermodynamic limit

The **thermodynamic limit** is the mathematical procedure of taking the particle number $N$ and the volume $V$ to infinity while holding the density $\rho = N/V$ fixed:

$$N \to \infty, \quad V \to \infty, \quad \frac{N}{V} = \rho = \text{const}.$$

In this limit, the following intensive quantities have well-defined finite limits:

- **Entropy density:** $\displaystyle s(e) = \lim_{N,V \to \infty} \frac{S(E, V, N)}{Nk}$
- **Free energy per particle:** $\displaystyle f(T, \rho) = \lim_{N,V \to \infty} \frac{F(T, V, N)}{N}$
- **Grand potential density:** $\displaystyle \omega(T, \mu) = \lim_{V \to \infty} \frac{\Omega(T, \mu, V)}{V}$

The existence of these limits is a foundational result in mathematical statistical mechanics, established rigorously by Van Hove, Ruelle, and others for systems with short-range interactions.

### Legendre transform structure

The three thermodynamic potentials are connected by Legendre transforms [11.01.02]. Recall that the Legendre transform of a function $g(x)$ is $g^*(p) = \sup_x (px - g(x))$, where $p$ is the variable conjugate to $x$.

**Microcanonical to canonical.** The entropy $S(E)$ as a function of energy has the canonical free energy as its Legendre transform:

$$-\frac{F(T)}{kT} = \sup_E \left(\frac{S(E)}{k} - \frac{E}{kT}\right).$$

If $S(E)$ is concave, the supremum is achieved at the energy $E^*$ satisfying $\partial S / \partial E = 1/T$, and the transform is invertible.

**Canonical to grand canonical.** The Helmholtz free energy $F(T, N)$ as a function of particle number has the grand potential as its Legendre transform:

$$-\Omega(T, \mu) = \sup_N \left(\mu N - F(T, N)\right) = kT\,\ln \Xi.$$

The supremum is achieved at $N^*$ satisfying $\partial F / \partial N = \mu$.

The chain of Legendre transforms is:

$$S(E) \xrightarrow{\;\text{Legendre in } E\;} -\frac{F}{kT} \xrightarrow{\;\text{Legendre in } N\;} \frac{\Omega}{kT}.$$

Each transform swaps one extensive variable for its intensive conjugate: $E \leftrightarrow T$, $N \leftrightarrow \mu$.

### Convexity and concavity

A fundamental result:

- The **entropy density** $s(e)$ is a **concave** function of the energy density $e = E/N$: $s''(e) \leq 0$. Concavity follows from thermodynamic stability: the heat capacity $C_V = (\partial E / \partial T)_V$ is non-negative, which translates to $d^2 s / de^2 = -1/(T^2 c_V) \leq 0$ where $c_V = C_V / N$ is the specific heat per particle.

- The **Helmholtz free energy per particle** $f(T)$ is a **concave** function of $T$: $f''(T) \leq 0$. This follows because the specific heat satisfies $c_V = -T\, f''(T) \geq 0$.

- The **grand potential density** $\omega(T, \mu)$ is concave in both $T$ and $\mu$: $\partial^2 \omega / \partial \mu^2 = -(\partial \rho / \partial \mu)_T \leq 0$ (stability: the compressibility $\kappa_T$ is non-negative).

Concavity is the mathematical signature of thermodynamic stability. A system that violated concavity would be unstable: it would phase-separate or develop runaway fluctuations.

### Laplace's method and the saddle-point approximation

The connection between ensembles is made precise by **Laplace's method**. The canonical partition function is related to the microcanonical density of states $\Omega(E)$ by

$$Z(\beta) = \int_0^\infty \Omega(E)\, e^{-\beta E}\, dE.$$

Writing $\Omega(E) = e^{S(E)/k}$:

$$Z(\beta) = \int_0^\infty e^{S(E)/k - \beta E}\, dE.$$

Define $g(E) = S(E)/k - \beta E$. In the thermodynamic limit, both $S$ and $E$ are extensive (proportional to $N$), so $g(E) \sim N\,\phi(E/N)$ where $\phi$ is an intensive function. The integral is dominated by the maximum of $g(E)$, because deviations from the maximum are exponentially suppressed as $e^{-N \cdot (\text{positive constant})}$.

The maximum occurs at $E^*$ where $g'(E^*) = 0$, that is, $S'(E^*)/k = \beta$, which gives $T(E^*) = 1/(k\beta)$. Expanding $g(E)$ to second order around $E^*$:

$$g(E) \approx g(E^*) + \tfrac{1}{2}\, g''(E^*)(E - E^*)^2.$$

The Gaussian integral yields

$$Z(\beta) \approx e^{g(E^*)} \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{|g''(E^*)|}}.$$

Taking logarithms:

$$\ln Z(\beta) \approx g(E^*) + \tfrac{1}{2}\ln(2\pi) - \tfrac{1}{2}\ln|g''(E^*)|.$$

The first term, $g(E^*) = S(E^*)/k - \beta E^*$, is extensive (proportional to $N$). The logarithmic correction terms are of order $\ln N$. In the thermodynamic limit, the correction terms vanish relative to the leading term:

$$-\frac{F}{kT} = \ln Z(\beta) = \frac{S(E^*)}{k} - \beta E^* + O(\ln N).$$

Dividing by $N$ and taking $N \to \infty$:

$$-\frac{f}{kT} = \frac{s(e^*)}{k} - \beta e^*.$$

This is the Legendre transform relation between the free energy density $f$ and the entropy density $s$, now derived from Laplace's method. The derivation shows that the Legendre transform is exact in the thermodynamic limit, with corrections that vanish as $N \to \infty$.

### Relative fluctuations

The width of the Gaussian in Laplace's method gives the energy fluctuation:

$$(\Delta E)^2 = -\frac{1}{g''(E^*)} = \frac{k}{-S''(E^*)/k} = kT^2 C_V.$$

Since $C_V \sim N$ and $\langle E \rangle \sim N$:

$$\frac{\Delta E}{\langle E \rangle} = \frac{\sqrt{kT^2 C_V}}{\langle E \rangle} \sim \frac{\sqrt{N}}{N} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{N}}.$$

The same argument applies to particle-number fluctuations in the grand canonical ensemble, giving $\Delta N / \langle N \rangle \sim 1/\sqrt{N}$.

**Equivalence theorem (informal).** *In the thermodynamic limit, the microcanonical, canonical, and grand canonical ensembles yield identical values for all thermodynamic quantities (pressure, compressibility, heat capacity, and so on) for any system with short-range interactions and a concave entropy function.*

## Key derivation: Equivalence from Laplace's method [Intermediate+]

**Proposition.** *For a system with concave entropy density $s(e)$, the canonical free energy per particle $f(T)$ is the Legendre transform of $-Ts(e)$:*

$$f(T) = \inf_e \left(e - Ts(e)\right).$$

*The infimum is achieved at $e^*$ where $s'(e^*) = 1/T$, and the mapping $T \mapsto e^*$ is the inverse of $e \mapsto T(e) = 1/s'(e)$.*

**Proof.** The canonical partition function is

$$Z_N(\beta) = \int_0^\infty \Omega_N(E)\, e^{-\beta E}\, dE = \int_0^\infty e^{S_N(E)/k - \beta E}\, dE,$$

where $S_N(E) = k\ln\Omega_N(E)$ is the microcanonical entropy at particle number $N$. Introduce the energy per particle $e = E/N$ and the entropy per particle $s_N(e) = S_N(Ne)/(Nk)$:

$$Z_N(\beta) = N \int_0^\infty e^{N[s_N(e)/k - \beta e]}\, de.$$

As $N \to \infty$, the integrand is dominated by the maximum of $\phi(e) = s(e)/k - \beta e$ (where $s(e) = \lim_{N \to \infty} s_N(e)$). By concavity of $s(e)$, $\phi$ has a unique maximum at $e^*$ satisfying

$$\phi'(e^*) = \frac{s'(e^*)}{k} - \beta = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad s'(e^*) = k\beta = \frac{1}{T}.$$

Applying Laplace's method:

$$\frac{1}{N}\ln Z_N(\beta) \;\to\; \sup_e \left(\frac{s(e)}{k} - \beta e\right) = \frac{s(e^*)}{k} - \beta e^*.$$

Since $F = -kT\ln Z$:

$$\frac{f(T)}{kT} = -\frac{s(e^*)}{k} + \beta e^*,$$

and therefore $f(T) = e^* - Ts(e^*)$, the Legendre transform. The condition $s'(e^*) = 1/T$ ensures that $e^*$ and $T$ are conjugate variables. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Legendre transform connection between $s(e)$ and $f(T)$ is the mathematical backbone of thermodynamics [11.01.02]. Every thermodynamic potential is a Legendre transform of every other, and the choice of potential corresponds to the choice of which variables are held fixed. The equivalence of ensembles is the statistical-mechanical manifestation of this thermodynamic structure: the three ensembles are three different ways of accessing the same Legendre structure, and they converge in the thermodynamic limit because the Legendre transform is exact for concave functions.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that if the entropy density $s(e)$ is concave, then $f(T) = \inf_e(e - Ts(e))$ is concave in $T$. That is, show $\partial^2 f / \partial T^2 \leq 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $f = e^* - Ts(e^*)$ where $e^*(T)$ is determined by $s'(e^*) = 1/T$. Differentiate twice using the envelope theorem.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By the envelope theorem, $\partial f / \partial T = -s(e^*)$. Then $\partial^2 f / \partial T^2 = -s'(e^*)\,de^*/dT$. From $s'(e^*) = 1/T$, differentiating with respect to $T$: $s''(e^*)\,de^*/dT = -1/T^2$, so $de^*/dT = -1/(T^2 s''(e^*))$. Therefore $\partial^2 f / \partial T^2 = -\frac{1}{T} \cdot \frac{-1}{T^2 s''(e^*)} = \frac{1}{T^3 s''(e^*)}$. Since $s''(e^*) \leq 0$ (concavity), we get $\partial^2 f / \partial T^2 \leq 0$. Concavity of $s$ implies concavity of $f$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

The relative energy fluctuation in the canonical ensemble satisfies $(\Delta E)^2 / \langle E \rangle^2 = kT^2 C_V / \langle E \rangle^2$. For an ideal monatomic gas with $C_V = \frac{3}{2}Nk$ and $\langle E \rangle = \frac{3}{2}NkT$, show that this equals $2/(3N)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute and simplify the ratio.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$(\Delta E)^2 = kT^2 C_V = kT^2 \cdot \frac{3}{2}Nk = \frac{3}{2}N(kT)^2$.

$\langle E \rangle^2 = \left(\frac{3}{2}NkT\right)^2 = \frac{9}{4}N^2(kT)^2$.

$\frac{(\Delta E)^2}{\langle E \rangle^2} = \frac{3N(kT)^2/2}{9N^2(kT)^2/4} = \frac{3/2}{9N/4} = \frac{6}{9N} = \frac{2}{3N}$.

So $\Delta E / \langle E \rangle = \sqrt{2/(3N)} \to 0$ as $N \to \infty$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

For a one-dimensional Ising chain in zero field, the free energy per spin is $f(T) = -kT\ln(2\cosh(J/(kT)))$. Compute the entropy per spin $s = -\partial f / \partial T$ and verify that $s \geq 0$ for all $T > 0$ and $s \to \ln 2$ as $T \to \infty$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate $f(T)$ with respect to $T$ and evaluate the limits. Let $x = J/(kT)$ to simplify.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $x = J/(kT)$. Then $f = -kT\ln(2\cosh x)$. Differentiating:

$s = -\frac{df}{dT} = k\ln(2\cosh x) - kT \cdot \frac{d}{dT}\ln(2\cosh x) = k\ln(2\cosh x) - kT \cdot \frac{\sinh x}{\cosh x} \cdot \frac{d}{dT}\!\left(\frac{J}{kT}\right) = k\ln(2\cosh x) + x\tanh x \cdot k.$

So $s/k = \ln(2\cosh x) - x\tanh x$ (the sign works out after carrying the chain rule through).

As $T \to \infty$ ($x \to 0$): $\cosh x \to 1$, $\tanh x \to 0$, so $s \to k\ln 2$. This is the maximum entropy for a two-state system.

For all $T > 0$: the function $h(x) = \ln(2\cosh x) - x\tanh x$ satisfies $h(x) \geq 0$ for all $x$ (with equality only at $x = 0$ in the $T \to \infty$ limit). This follows from $\cosh x \geq e^{|x|}/2$, giving $\ln(2\cosh x) \geq |x| \geq x\tanh x$.

</details>

</aside>

Saddle point: $\phi'(e) = \frac{3}{2e} - \beta = 0$ gives $e^* = \frac{3}{2\beta} = \frac{3}{2}kT$.

Curvature: $\phi''(e) = -\frac{3}{2e^2}$, so $\phi''(e^*) = -\frac{3}{2}\cdot\frac{4\beta^2}{9} = -\frac{2\beta^2}{3}$.

Laplace's method gives:

$Z_N \approx e^{N\phi(e^*)}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{N|\phi''(e^*)|}} = e^{N\phi(e^*)}\sqrt{\frac{3\pi}{N\beta^2}}$.

$\ln Z_N = N\phi(e^*) + \frac{1}{2}\ln\!\left(\frac{3\pi}{N\beta^2}\right)$.

The correction per particle is $\frac{1}{2N}\ln\!\left(\frac{3\pi}{N\beta^2}\right) \to 0$ as $N \to \infty$, confirming that the Legendre transform $\frac{1}{N}\ln Z_N \to \phi(e^*) = s(e^*)/k - \beta e^*$ is exact in the thermodynamic limit.

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the equivalence of the canonical and grand canonical ensembles in the thermodynamic limit by showing that the particle-number distribution $P(N) = z^N Z_N(\beta) / \Xi(\beta, z)$ concentrates sharply. Write $\ln P(N) = \beta\mu N + \ln Z_N - \ln\Xi$, use $f(T, N) = -kT\ln Z_N$, and apply a saddle-point argument to show that the dominant contribution comes from a single value $N^*$ with $\Delta N / N^* \sim 1/\sqrt{N^*}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Define $\psi(N) = \beta(\mu N - f(T, N)) = \ln(z^N Z_N)$. The saddle point in $N$ satisfies $\mu = \partial f / \partial N|_{N=N^*}$, and the curvature gives the fluctuation.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $\psi(N) = \beta\mu N + \ln Z_N = \beta(\mu N - f(T,N))$ where $f(T,N) = -kT\ln Z_N$. Then $P(N) = e^{\psi(N)} / \Xi$.

In the thermodynamic limit, write $N = \rho V$ and $f(T, N) = N\tilde{f}(T, \rho)$ where $\tilde{f}$ is the free energy per particle. Then $\psi(N) = \beta N[\mu - \tilde{f}(T, N/V)]$.

The saddle point $N^*$ satisfies $\partial\psi/\partial N = 0$: $\beta\mu - \beta\,\partial f / \partial N|_{N^*} = 0$, giving $\mu = \partial f / \partial N|_{N^*}$.

The second derivative: $\psi''(N^*) = -\beta\,\partial^2 f / \partial N^2|_{N^*}$. By thermodynamic stability, $\partial^2 f / \partial N^2 = (\partial\mu / \partial N)_T > 0$ (compressibility is positive), so $\psi''(N^*) < 0$ — the saddle is a maximum.

The fluctuation is $(\Delta N)^2 = 1/|\psi''(N^*)| = kT\,(\partial\langle N\rangle / \partial\mu)_T$. Since $(\partial\langle N\rangle / \partial\mu)_T = N^2\kappa_T / V$ and $\kappa_T$ is intensive, $(\Delta N)^2 \sim N^*$, giving $\Delta N / N^* \sim 1/\sqrt{N^*} \to 0$.

The probability of significant deviations from $N^*$ is exponentially small: $P(N) \sim \exp(-c(N - N^*)^2 / N^*)$. The distribution converges to a delta function in the thermodynamic limit, and all thermodynamic quantities computed in the grand canonical ensemble agree with their canonical counterparts.

## Advanced results [Master]

### Large deviation principle

The deepest formulation of ensemble equivalence is through the **large deviation principle** (LDP) [37.07.02]. Let $E_N$ be the random energy in the canonical ensemble at inverse temperature $\beta$ with $N$ particles. The energy density $e_N = E_N/N$ satisfies a large deviation principle with rate function

$$I(e) = \beta e - \frac{s(e)}{k} + \sup_{e'}\left(\frac{s(e')}{k} - \beta e'\right) = \beta(e - e^*) - \frac{s(e) - s(e^*)}{k}.$$

This means $P(e_N \approx e) \asymp e^{-NI(e)}$ as $N \to \infty$. The rate function $I(e) \geq 0$ with equality at $e = e^*$, so the probability of observing any energy density other than $e^*$ is exponentially suppressed. This is the rigorous content of the saddle-point argument: the canonical ensemble concentrates on a single energy density, and that energy density is the one that maximises the microcanonical entropy subject to the temperature constraint.

The LDP formulation reveals that ensemble equivalence is a **variational principle**: the canonical ensemble maximises entropy subject to fixed expected energy (with $\beta$ as the Lagrange multiplier), and the microcanonical ensemble maximises entropy subject to fixed exact energy. In the thermodynamic limit, the constraint "fixed expected energy" and "fixed exact energy" become equivalent because the distribution concentrates.

For the grand canonical ensemble, a joint LDP holds for the pair $(e_N, \rho_N)$ with rate function

$$I(e, \rho) = \beta(e - \mu\rho) - \frac{s(e, \rho)}{k} + \sup_{e',\rho'}\left(\frac{s(e', \rho')}{k} - \beta(e' - \mu\rho')\right),$$

where $s(e, \rho)$ is the entropy density as a function of energy density and number density.

### Yang-Lee zeros and analyticity

The grand partition function $\Xi_N(z) = \sum_{k=0}^{N} a_k z^k$ is a polynomial in the fugacity $z$ for a finite system. The **Yang-Lee zeros** are the roots $z_1, z_2, \ldots$ of this polynomial. For a ferromagnetic system, all roots lie on the unit circle $|z| = 1$ in the complex $z$-plane (Lee-Yang circle theorem, 1952).

The thermodynamic limit $N \to \infty$ produces a dense set of zeros. If the zeros stay away from the positive real axis, then $\ln\Xi / N$ is an analytic function of real positive $z$, and the system has no phase transition. If the zeros accumulate and approach a point $z_c > 0$ on the real axis, then $\ln\Xi / N$ develops a singularity at $z_c$ — this is a phase transition.

The connection to ensemble equivalence is direct: non-analyticity of $\ln\Xi / N$ at $z_c$ means the grand potential density $\omega(T, \mu)$ has a singularity, which in turn means the Legendre transform from $\omega$ to $f$ or $s$ may fail to be invertible at that point. At a first-order phase transition, the microcanonical entropy $s(e)$ develops a linear segment (a "convex intruder"), and the canonical free energy $f(T)$ has a kink. The three ensembles remain equivalent in the sense that all three potentials describe the same phase diagram, but the relationship between them involves Maxwell-construction tie lines rather than point-to-point Legendre duality.

### Non-equivalence for long-range interactions

Ensemble equivalence can **fail** for systems with long-range interactions — those where the interaction potential decays slower than $r^{-d}$ in dimension $d$. Examples include:

- **Self-gravitating systems.** The gravitational potential $\Phi \propto 1/r$ is long-range in three dimensions. A self-gravitating gas can have negative specific heat in the microcanonical ensemble (adding energy decreases the temperature), which is impossible in the canonical ensemble. The two ensembles give inequivalent predictions for the temperature-energy relation.

- **Mean-field models.** The Curie-Weiss model (infinite-range Ising model with $J_{ij} = J/N$) is a mean-field approximation that retains ensemble equivalence because the $1/N$ scaling ensures thermodynamic stability. But if the coupling is stronger than $1/N$, equivalence can fail.

- **Two-dimensional vortices.** Point vortices in two dimensions interact via a logarithmic potential, which is long-range. The microcanonical and canonical descriptions can diverge, predicting different phase structures.

The mathematical criterion, due to Ruelle and Griffiths, is: ensemble equivalence holds if and only if the microcanonical entropy density $s(e)$ is concave. For short-range systems, concavity follows from subadditivity of the entropy (the entropy of a composite system is at least the sum of the entropies of the parts, minus a surface correction that vanishes in the thermodynamic limit). For long-range systems, subadditivity can fail, $s(e)$ can become non-concave, and the ensembles diverge.

When $s(e)$ is non-concave, the canonical free energy $f(T)$ is still the Legendre transform of $Ts(e)$, but the transform is no longer invertible — different energy densities map to the same temperature. The microcanonical ensemble is the "fundamental" description in this case, and the canonical ensemble provides a "coarse-grained" version that smooths out the non-concave features of $s(e)$ via the Legendre transform's convex hull operation.

### Griffiths-Ruelle lemma

The **Griffiths-Ruelle lemma** provides a sufficient condition for the thermodynamic limit to exist. For a system of particles in a domain $\Lambda$ with a stable and tempered pair potential, the partition function satisfies a submultiplicativity property

$$Z_{\Lambda_1 \cup \Lambda_2} \leq Z_{\Lambda_1} \cdot Z_{\Lambda_2}$$

(up to surface terms), which by Fekete's lemma guarantees that $\lim_{V\to\infty} (1/V)\ln Z_V$ exists. This is the foundation for the existence of the thermodynamic limit and, by extension, for ensemble equivalence. The conditions are:

1. **Stability:** The potential energy of any configuration of $N$ particles is bounded below by $-BN$ for some constant $B > 0$ independent of $N$.
2. **Tempering:** The interaction potential $\phi(r) \to 0$ sufficiently fast as $r \to \infty$ (specifically, $|\phi(r)| \leq C / r^{d+\epsilon}$ for some $\epsilon > 0$ in dimension $d$).

Systems that violate stability (e.g., the bare Lennard-Jones potential at short range, which diverges as $r^{-12}$) can be stabilised by a hard-core repulsion. Systems that violate tempering (e.g., gravity, $1/r$ in $d = 3$) are precisely those for which ensemble equivalence can fail.

The proof of submultiplicability uses the fact that for short-range interactions, the energy of the combined system $\Lambda_1 \cup \Lambda_2$ is at most the sum of the energies of $\Lambda_1$ and $\Lambda_2$ plus an interfacial correction proportional to the surface area between them. In the thermodynamic limit, the surface-to-volume ratio vanishes, and the correction disappears.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Equivalence of microcanonical and canonical ensembles).** *Let $s(e)$ be the thermodynamic-limit entropy density, assumed concave and differentiable. Let $\beta > 0$ and define $e^* = e^*(\beta)$ by $s'(e^*) = 1/T = k\beta$. Then:*

1. $-\beta f(\beta) = \sup_e (s(e)/k - \beta e) = s(e^*)/k - \beta e^*$ *(Legendre transform).*
2. *In the canonical ensemble at inverse temperature $\beta$, the energy density converges in probability to $e^*$ as $N \to \infty$.*
3. *The canonical variance $(\Delta e)^2 = kT^2 c_V / N \to 0$, where $c_V = -T s''(e^*) / k$ is the specific heat per particle.*

**Proof.** (1) is the Laplace-method result proved in the Key derivation above. For (2), consider the probability density of the energy density $e$ in the canonical ensemble:

$$p_N(e) = \frac{e^{N[s(e)/k - \beta e]}}{\int e^{N[s(e')/k - \beta e']}\, de'}.$$

Define $\phi(e) = s(e)/k - \beta e$. Since $s$ is concave and differentiable, $\phi$ has a unique maximum at $e^*$ with $\phi(e) < \phi(e^*)$ for $e \neq e^*$. By Laplace's method, $p_N(e) \to \delta(e - e^*)$ as $N \to \infty$ (in the sense of weak convergence of measures). This establishes (2).

For (3), the second-order expansion $\phi(e) \approx \phi(e^*) + \frac{1}{2}\phi''(e^*)(e - e^*)^2$ with $\phi''(e^*) = s''(e^*)/k$ gives a Gaussian approximation with variance $(\Delta e)^2 = -1/(N\phi''(e^*)) = -k/(Ns''(e^*))$. Using $s''(e^*) = -k/(T^2 c_V)$ where $c_V$ is the specific heat per particle: $(\Delta e)^2 = kT^2 c_V / N$. Since $c_V$ is intensive, $(\Delta e)^2 \to 0$ as $N \to \infty$. $\square$

**Proposition (Grand canonical-canonical equivalence).** *Under the same hypotheses, with the additional assumption that $f(T, \rho)$ is concave in the number density $\rho$, the grand canonical particle-number distribution concentrates at $\rho^* = \langle N \rangle / V$ satisfying $\mu = \partial f / \partial \rho|_{\rho=\rho^*}$, and the grand potential density $\omega = -P$ satisfies*

$$\omega(T, \mu) = f(T, \rho^*) - \mu\rho^*$$

*(Legendre transform in $\rho$).*

**Proof.** The grand partition function is $\Xi = \sum_N z^N Z_N(\beta, V)$ where $z = e^{\beta\mu}$. Writing $N = \rho V$ and using the thermodynamic limit of $f(T, \rho) = -kT \lim_{V\to\infty} \ln Z_N / N$:

$$\frac{1}{V}\ln\Xi = \sup_\rho \left(\beta\mu\rho - \beta f(T,\rho)\right).$$

The supremum is achieved at $\rho^*$ where $\mu = \partial f / \partial \rho|_{\rho^*}$, which follows from the concavity of $f$ in $\rho$. This gives $\omega = f(T,\rho^*) - \mu\rho^*$, the Legendre transform. The variance satisfies $\Delta\rho \sim 1/\sqrt{V}$, vanishing in the thermodynamic limit. $\square$

## Historical and philosophical context [Master]

The equivalence of ensembles has its roots in Gibbs's 1902 *Elementary Principles*, where he introduced the canonical and grand canonical distributions as computational tools and noted that they should agree with the microcanonical distribution for macroscopic systems. Gibbs did not provide a rigorous proof; his argument was based on the sharpness of the energy distribution, which he demonstrated for specific examples. The key conceptual insight — that three different probability measures on phase space produce the same macroscopic thermodynamics — was present in embryonic form but lacked mathematical precision.

Einstein (1902--1904) independently developed statistical mechanics from the microcanonical perspective, computing fluctuations and noting the connection between entropy and probability. His 1910 paper on critical opalescence was an early application of fluctuation theory, showing that the fluctuation-dissipation relation connects ensemble fluctuations to thermodynamic response functions. Einstein's approach emphasised the probabilistic interpretation of entropy, which would later become central to the large-deviation formulation.

The rigorous mathematical treatment began with Van Hove (1949), who proved the existence of the thermodynamic limit for systems with short-range interactions. Van Hove's insight was that the free energy per particle has a well-defined limit as the system size goes to infinity, because surface effects (which depend on the boundary) become negligible compared to bulk effects (which scale with the volume).

Ruelle's *Statistical Mechanics: Rigorous Results* (1969) established the modern framework. Ruelle proved that the thermodynamic limit of the entropy density exists and is concave for stable, tempered potentials, and that the Legendre transform connects the ensembles. His proof uses subadditivity: the entropy of a composite system is at least the sum of the entropies of its parts (minus an interfacial correction), and Fekete's lemma then guarantees the existence of the limit.

Lanford's 1973 lectures on entropy and equilibrium states deepened the connection to the variational principles of thermodynamics. Lanford showed that the canonical ensemble can be characterised as the unique probability measure that maximises entropy subject to a constraint on the expected energy — a variational characterisation that generalises to infinite-dimensional systems (Gibbs measures on lattices and Gibbsian random fields).

The large deviation perspective was developed by Varadhan (1966, 1984) and applied to statistical mechanics by Lanford, Ruelle, and Ellis. The key insight is that the microcanonical entropy *is* the large deviation rate function for the energy density, and the canonical ensemble is the exponential tilting that makes the most likely energy density coincide with the saddle point. Ellis's *Entropy, Large Deviations, and Statistical Mechanics* (1985) is the standard reference.

The breakdown of equivalence for long-range systems was studied by Thirring (1970), who showed that self-gravitating systems can have negative specific heat in the microcanonical ensemble but not in the canonical ensemble — the two ensembles give qualitatively different predictions. The mathematical conditions for equivalence versus non-equivalence were crystallised by Ellis, Haven, and Turkington (2000), who proved that equivalence holds if and only if the microcanonical entropy is concave, and that non-concave entropy leads to inequivalent ensembles with distinct phase structures.
