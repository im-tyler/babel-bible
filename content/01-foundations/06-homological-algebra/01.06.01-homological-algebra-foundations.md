## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine a shape built from points, line segments, and triangles glued along their edges. A boundary operator records, for each piece, the pieces on its rim: the boundary of a segment is its two endpoints, and the boundary of a triangle is its three edges. Going around a triangle and adding up its three edges, each inner edge cancels against a neighbour pointing the other way. So the boundary of a boundary is zero.

The reverse can fail. A closed loop of segments has no boundary — it returns to its start — yet no triangle may be present to fill it in. That gap, a closed loop with no filling, is a hole. Homology counts these holes. Exactness is the ideal situation where every closed loop does have a filling; homology measures exactly how far a chain falls short of being exact.

The same pattern — boundary of a boundary is zero, and we measure the leftover — reappears across all of higher algebra. In the algebraic setting it becomes the toolkit called homological algebra, and the holes become obstructions to solving equations that no amount of clever rearrangement can remove.

## Visual [Beginner]

A chain complex is a row of modules connected by boundary operators, each one handing the next what it could not kill.

![A row of modules C2, C1, C0 connected by downward boundary arrows d, with the im-of-one inside the ker-of-the-next shaded as the homology.](/img/homological-algebra-foundations-placeholder.svg)

The shaded overlap — what the incoming operator reaches but the outgoing operator does not catch — is the homology. Vanishing homology means the row is exact; non-vanishing homology is the obstruction the algebra must remember.

| Piece | Boundary operator sends it to |
|---|---|
| triangle (face) | its three edges, with orientation signs |
| segment (edge) | the difference of its two endpoints |
| point (vertex) | zero |

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take a triangle with vertices $v_0, v_1, v_2$, edges $e_{01}, e_{12}, e_{20}$, and a single face $f$. The boundary operator sends the face to its three oriented edges, and each edge to the difference of its endpoints.

Step 1. The boundary of the face is $e_{01} + e_{12} + e_{20}$.

Step 2. Apply the boundary operator again, to each edge. Edge $e_{01}$ becomes $v_1 - v_0$, edge $e_{12}$ becomes $v_2 - v_1$, and edge $e_{20}$ becomes $v_0 - v_2$.

Step 3. Add these: $(v_1 - v_0) + (v_2 - v_1) + (v_0 - v_2)$. The $v_1$ terms cancel, the $v_2$ terms cancel, and the $v_0$ terms cancel. The total is $0$.

Boundary of a boundary is zero. Now delete the face but keep the three edges. The loop $e_{01} + e_{12} + e_{20}$ still has boundary zero — it closes — yet no face remains to fill it. That unfilled loop is one homology class: one hole.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **chain complex** $(C_\bullet, d)$ over a ring $R$ is a sequence of $R$-modules and maps

$$
\cdots \longrightarrow C_{n+1} \xrightarrow{d_{n+1}} C_n \xrightarrow{d_n} C_{n-1} \longrightarrow \cdots
$$

with $d_n \circ d_{n+1} = 0$ for every $n$ [ref: TODO_REF Weibel Ch. 1]. The maps $d_n$ are **differentials** or boundary operators. The condition $d^2 = 0$ forces $\operatorname{im} d_{n+1} \subseteq \ker d_n$, so the quotient below is defined.

The $n$-th **homology** of $C_\bullet$ is

$$
H_n(C_\bullet) \;=\; \frac{\ker d_n}{\operatorname{im} d_{n+1}} \;=\; \frac{Z_n(C)}{B_n(C)},
$$

where $Z_n = \ker d_n$ are the $n$-cycles and $B_n = \operatorname{im} d_{n+1}$ are the $n$-boundaries. A complex is **exact** at $C_n$ when $H_n = 0$, equivalently $\operatorname{im} d_{n+1} = \ker d_n$. A cochain complex has differentials that raise degree, $d^n: C^n \to C^{n+1}$; its cohomology is $H^n = \ker d^n / \operatorname{im} d^{n-1}$.

A **chain map** $f_\bullet: C_\bullet \to D_\bullet$ is a family of module maps commuting with the differentials ($d^D_n f_n = f_{n-1} d^C_n$). Chain maps induce maps $H_n(f): H_n(C) \to H_n(D)$. Two chain maps $f, g$ are **chain homotopic** ($f \simeq g$) when there are maps $s_n: C_n \to D_{n+1}$ with $f_n - g_n = d^D_{n+1} s_n + s_{n-1} d^C_n$; chain-homotopic maps induce the same map on homology.

A chain map $f: C_\bullet \to D_\bullet$ is a **quasi-isomorphism** when every induced map $H_n(f): H_n(C) \to H_n(D)$ is an isomorphism. Quasi-isomorphism is weaker than isomorphism of complexes: two distinct complexes can carry the same homology and still differ degree by degree. The passage to the **derived category** in [04.03.11] formally inverts all quasi-isomorphisms, so that a quasi-isomorphism becomes a genuine isomorphism there; this is the move that lets derived functors be computed from any convenient resolution of the same object.

A sequence $A \xrightarrow{f} B \xrightarrow{g} C$ is **exact** at $B$ when $\operatorname{im} f = \ker g$. A **short exact sequence** is

$$
0 \longrightarrow A' \xrightarrow{i} A \xrightarrow{p} A'' \longrightarrow 0,
$$

exact at every term: $i$ is injective, $p$ is surjective, and $\operatorname{im} i = \ker p$. It **splits** when $A \cong A' \oplus A''$ compatibly with $i$ and $p$; equivalently there is a section $s: A'' \to A$ with $p \circ s = \operatorname{id}$, or a retraction $r: A \to A'$ with $r \circ i = \operatorname{id}$.

An $R$-module $P$ is **projective** when every diagram $P \to B \leftarrow C$ with $B \twoheadrightarrow C$ admits a lift $P \to C$; equivalently $\operatorname{Hom}_R(P, -)$ is exact. Dually, $I$ is **injective** when $\operatorname{Hom}_R(-, I)$ is exact, i.e. every map into a submodule extends. A category has **enough projectives** when every module is a quotient of a projective; $R\text{-Mod}$ always has enough projectives (every module is a quotient of a free module). A **projective resolution** of $M$ is an exact sequence

$$
\cdots \longrightarrow P_2 \longrightarrow P_1 \longrightarrow P_0 \longrightarrow M \longrightarrow 0
$$

with each $P_i$ projective [ref: TODO_REF Cartan-Eilenberg Ch. VI]. Injective resolutions are defined dually.

A covariant functor $F$ is **right exact** when it preserves cokernels; $-\otimes_R N$ is right exact. It is **left exact** when it preserves kernels; $\operatorname{Hom}_R(M, -)$ is left exact. The **derived functors** $L_n F$ (for right exact $F$, via projective resolutions) and $R^n F$ (for left exact $F$, via injective resolutions) measure the failure of $F$ to be exact, and the two workhorses are

$$
\operatorname{Tor}_n^R(M, N) = L_n(- \otimes_R N)(M), \qquad \operatorname{Ext}_R^n(M, N) = R^n \operatorname{Hom}_R(M, -)(N).
$$

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Not every complex is exact.* For $0 \to \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\times 2} \mathbb{Z} \to 0$ concentrated in degrees $1, 0$, the cokernel of $\times 2$ is $\mathbb{Z}/2$, so $H_0 \cong \mathbb{Z}/2$ — homology detects the obstruction, it does not vanish.
- *Tensor is not left exact.* From $0 \to \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\times n} \mathbb{Z}$, tensoring with $\mathbb{Z}/n$ gives $\mathbb{Z}/n \xrightarrow{\times n} \mathbb{Z}/n$, the zero map, so injectivity is lost. $\operatorname{Tor}_1^{\mathbb{Z}}(\mathbb{Z}/n, \mathbb{Z}/n) \cong \mathbb{Z}/n$ records this failure.
- *Projective need not be free.* Over many rings (rings of integers in number fields, or the tangent bundle of a sphere via Swan's theorem) projective modules exist that admit no basis; over a PID, projective coincides with free, which is the simple case.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Snake lemma).** Given a commutative diagram of $R$-modules with exact rows

$$
\begin{array}{ccccccc}
& \ker a & \to & \ker b & \to & \ker c & \\
 & \downarrow & & \downarrow & & \downarrow & \\
0 \to & A' & \xrightarrow{i} & A & \xrightarrow{p} & A'' & \to 0 \\
 & {\scriptstyle a}\downarrow & & {\scriptstyle b}\downarrow & & {\scriptstyle c}\downarrow & \\
0 \to & B' & \xrightarrow{j} & B & \xrightarrow{q} & B'' & \to 0 \\
 & \downarrow & & \downarrow & & \downarrow & \\
& \operatorname{coker} a & \to & \operatorname{coker} b & \to & \operatorname{coker} c &
\end{array}
$$

there is an exact sequence

$$
\ker a \to \ker b \to \ker c \xrightarrow{\delta} \operatorname{coker} a \to \operatorname{coker} b \to \operatorname{coker} c,
$$

in which the **connecting homomorphism** $\delta$ sends $x \in \ker c \subseteq A''$ to the class of $j^{-1}\bigl(b(p^{-1}(x))\bigr)$ in $\operatorname{coker} a$. The proof is a diagram chase checking exactness at each of the six positions [ref: TODO_REF Weibel Ch. 1].

To see how $\delta$ is built, chase an element $x \in \ker c$ through the diagram. Lift it along the surjection $p$ to an element $\tilde x \in A$ with $p(\tilde x) = x$. Apply $b$ and then $q$: since $q(b(\tilde x)) = c(p(\tilde x)) = c(x) = 0$, exactness of the bottom row places $b(\tilde x)$ in $\ker q = \operatorname{im} j$, so a unique $y \in B'$ has $j(y) = b(\tilde x)$. The class of $y$ in $\operatorname{coker} a$ is independent of the lift $\tilde x$, and that class is $\delta(x)$. This zig-zag — lift right, push down, pull back left — is the prototype of every connecting homomorphism in homological algebra, including the boundary map of the long exact sequence in homology and the Bockstein operations of [04.03.06].

The snake lemma is the algebraic engine behind the long exact sequence in homology: a short exact sequence of complexes $0 \to C' \to C \to C'' \to 0$ yields, by applying the snake lemma degree by degree, the long exact sequence

$$
\cdots \to H_n(C') \to H_n(C) \to H_n(C'') \xrightarrow{\delta} H_{n-1}(C') \to \cdots
$$

**Theorem (Five lemma).** Given a commutative diagram with exact rows

$$
\begin{array}{ccccccccc}
A_1 & \to & A_2 & \to & A_3 & \to & A_4 & \to & A_5 \\
{\scriptstyle f_1}\downarrow & & {\scriptstyle f_2}\downarrow & & {\scriptstyle f_3}\downarrow & & {\scriptstyle f_4}\downarrow & & {\scriptstyle f_5}\downarrow \\
B_1 & \to & B_2 & \to & B_3 & \to & B_4 & \to & B_5
\end{array}
$$

if $f_1, f_2, f_4, f_5$ are isomorphisms, then $f_3$ is an isomorphism.

*Proof.* Injectivity of $f_3$. Let $a_3 \in A_3$ with $f_3(a_3) = 0$. Then $f_4\,g_3(a_3) = g_3'\,f_3(a_3) = 0$ by commutativity, and since $f_4$ is injective, $g_3(a_3) = 0$. Exactness of the top row gives $a_3 = g_2(a_2)$ for some $a_2 \in A_2$. Commutativity yields $g_2'\,f_2(a_2) = f_3\,g_2(a_2) = f_3(a_3) = 0$, so $f_2(a_2) = \ker g_2' = \operatorname{im} g_1' = g_1'(b_1)$ for some $b_1$. Since $f_1$ is surjective, $b_1 = f_1(a_1)$, and $f_2\,g_1(a_1) = g_1'\,f_1(a_1) = g_1'(b_1) = f_2(a_2)$. Injectivity of $f_2$ gives $a_2 = g_1(a_1)$, whence $a_3 = g_2(a_2) = g_2\,g_1(a_1) = 0$. So $f_3$ is injective.

Surjectivity of $f_3$. Let $b_3 \in B_3$. Since $f_5$ is injective and $g_4'\,g_3'(b_3) = 0$, exactness gives $g_3'(b_3) = g_4'(b_4)$ for some $b_4$ by first using $f_5\,g_4'(b_3)$: $f_5\,g_4'(b_3) = g_4'\,g_3'(b_3) = 0$, hence $g_4'(b_3) = 0$ as $f_5$ injective. Thus $b_3 = g_2'(b_2)$ for some $b_2 \in B_2$. Surjectivity of $f_2$ gives $b_2 = f_2(a_2)$. Set $a_3 = g_2(a_2)$. Then $f_3(a_3) - b_3 = f_3\,g_2(a_2) - g_2'(b_2) = g_2'\,f_2(a_2) - g_2'(b_2) = 0$, so $f_3$ hits $b_3$. Hence $f_3$ is surjective, and an isomorphism. $\square$

**Bridge.** This diagram-chase lemma builds toward [04.03.01] (sheaf cohomology), where the long exact sequence is the engine that computes obstructions on spaces, and appears again in [04.03.06] (derived functors) as the mechanism by which a short exact sequence of sheaves yields a long exact sequence of cohomology groups. The foundational reason diagram chases recur is that exactness is a pointwise condition that nevertheless propagates globally along the arrows; this is exactly the pattern that makes homological algebra the universal language for measuring obstructions, and the bridge is that every derived functor is a systematic device for extending a half-exact functor to a fully exact one by adjoining these connecting maps.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Consider the chain complex $0 \to \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\times 4} \mathbb{Z} \to 0$ concentrated in degrees $1$ and $0$ (the degree-$1$ copy maps by $\times 4$ into the degree-$0$ copy). What is the order of $H_0$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$H_0 = \ker(C_0 \to 0)/\operatorname{im}(\times 4) = \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The degree-$0$ differential is the zero map, so $Z_0 = \mathbb{Z}$. The boundaries are $B_0 = 4\mathbb{Z}$. Hence $H_0 = \mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$, which has **order $4$**. Also $H_1 = \ker(\times 4) = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

State the exactness condition for a sequence $A \xrightarrow{f} B \xrightarrow{g} C$ at the middle term $B$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Exactness equates an image with a kernel.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The sequence is exact at $B$ precisely when $\operatorname{im} f = \ker g$. Equivalently, everything that $g$ kills is exactly what $f$ reaches, and nothing more.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

For a chain complex $(C_\bullet, d)$, show that the identity $d_n \circ d_{n+1} = 0$ implies $\operatorname{im} d_{n+1} \subseteq \ker d_n$, and conclude that the quotient $H_n = \ker d_n / \operatorname{im} d_{n+1}$ is defined.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Unpack what $d_n \circ d_{n+1} = 0$ says about an element $d_{n+1}(c)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For any $c \in C_{n+1}$, the identity gives $d_n(d_{n+1}(c)) = 0$, so $d_{n+1}(c) \in \ker d_n$. Hence $\operatorname{im} d_{n+1} \subseteq \ker d_n$, making $\operatorname{im} d_{n+1}$ a submodule of $\ker d_n$. The quotient $\ker d_n / \operatorname{im} d_{n+1}$ is therefore well-defined, and that quotient is $H_n(C_\bullet)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

Over $R = \mathbb{Z}$, let $C_1 = \mathbb{Z}$ and $C_0 = \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}$ with $d_1(1) = (2, 4)$, all other differentials zero. What is the order of the torsion subgroup of $H_0(C_\bullet)$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$H_0 = (\mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z})/\langle(2,4)\rangle$. Put the relation in Smith normal form: $\gcd(2,4)=2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

We have $Z_0 = \mathbb{Z}^2$ and $B_0 = \langle(2,4)\rangle$. Since $\gcd(2,4) = 2$, Smith normal form rewrites the single relation as $2$ acting on one summand, giving $H_0 \cong \mathbb{Z} \oplus \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$. The torsion subgroup is $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$, of **order $2$**.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $0 \to A \xrightarrow{i} B \xrightarrow{p} C \to 0$ be a short exact sequence. Prove that it splits if and only if there exists a section $s: C \to B$ with $p \circ s = \operatorname{id}_C$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Given a section, show $B = i(A) \oplus s(C)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If the sequence splits, $B \cong A \oplus C$ with $i(a) = (a,0)$ and $p(a,c) = c$, so $s(c) = (0,c)$ is a section. Conversely, given a section $s$ with $p \circ s = \operatorname{id}$, define $\varphi: A \oplus C \to B$ by $\varphi(a,c) = i(a) + s(c)$. It is injective: if $i(a) + s(c) = 0$, apply $p$ to get $c = 0$, then $i(a) = 0$ and injectivity of $i$ gives $a = 0$. It is surjective: for $b \in B$, write $b = [b - s(p(b))] + s(p(b))$; the bracket lies in $\ker p = \operatorname{im} i$. So $B \cong A \oplus C$ and the sequence splits.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, multiple choice).**

Which of the following is a projective $\mathbb{Z}$-module?

A. The torsion group $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$  
B. The rationals $\mathbb{Q}$  
C. Any free abelian group, such as $\mathbb{Z}^n$  
D. The Prüfer group $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Over a PID, projective coincides with free; free abelian groups are direct sums of copies of $\mathbb{Z}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**C. Any free abelian group such as $\mathbb{Z}^n$.** Over a PID (in particular over $\mathbb{Z}$), projective modules are exactly the free ones. The torsion groups $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$ and $\mathbb{Q}/\mathbb{Z}$ are not projective, and $\mathbb{Q}$, though torsion-free, is not free as an abelian group.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Using the free resolution $0 \to \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\times n} \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \to 0$, compute $\operatorname{Ext}^1_{\mathbb{Z}}(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z},\, \mathbb{Z})$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the contravariant functor $\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathbb{Z}}(-,\, \mathbb{Z})$ to the resolution and take cohomology of the resulting cochain complex in degree $1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Apply $\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathbb{Z}}(-,\,\mathbb{Z})$ to the resolution. Since $\operatorname{Hom}_{\mathbb{Z}}(\mathbb{Z}, \mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}$ (a homomorphism is determined by the image of $1$), the cochain complex becomes $0 \to \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\times n} \mathbb{Z} \to 0$ in cohomological degrees $0$ and $1$. Therefore

$$
\operatorname{Ext}^1_{\mathbb{Z}}(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z},\,\mathbb{Z}) = \operatorname{coker}(\mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\times n} \mathbb{Z}) = \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}.
$$

In particular $\operatorname{Ext}^1_{\mathbb{Z}}(\mathbb{Z}/n,\,\mathbb{Z}) \cong \mathbb{Z}/n$, the non-split extensions of $\mathbb{Z}/n$ by $\mathbb{Z}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Using the resolution $0 \to \mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\times m} \mathbb{Z} \to \mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z} \to 0$, show that

$$
\operatorname{Tor}_1^{\mathbb{Z}}(\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z},\, \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}) \;\cong\; \mathbb{Z}/\gcd(m,n)\mathbb{Z}.
$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply $- \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ to the resolution and take $H_1$ of the resulting complex. The kernel of multiplication by $m$ on $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ has order $\gcd(m,n)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Tensor the resolution with $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$. Since $\mathbb{Z} \otimes \mathbb{Z}/n \cong \mathbb{Z}/n$, the chain complex computing $\operatorname{Tor}$ is $0 \to \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\times m} \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \to 0$. Then

$$
\operatorname{Tor}_1^{\mathbb{Z}}(\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z},\,\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}) = \ker(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \xrightarrow{\times m} \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}).
$$

The kernel of multiplication by $m$ on $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ consists of the classes $[k]$ with $mk \equiv 0 \pmod n$, i.e. $n \mid mk$, equivalently $(n/\gcd(m,n)) \mid k$. This subgroup is cyclic of order $\gcd(m,n)$, hence $\operatorname{Tor}_1 \cong \mathbb{Z}/\gcd(m,n)\mathbb{Z}$. In particular $\operatorname{Tor}_1(\mathbb{Z}/n, \mathbb{Z}/n) \cong \mathbb{Z}/n$.

## Advanced results [Master]

The defining achievement of homological algebra is that every half-exact functor extends, via resolutions, to a sequence of derived functors connected by long exact sequences. Concretely, a left-exact covariant functor $F: \mathcal{A} \to \mathcal{B}$ between abelian categories, with $\mathcal{A}$ having enough injectives, gives right derived functors $R^n F$ defined by $R^n F(A) = H^n(F(I^\bullet))$ for any injective resolution $A \to I^\bullet$; the result is independent of the resolution chosen, because injective resolutions are unique up to chain homotopy [ref: TODO_REF Grothendieck Tohoku]. A short exact sequence $0 \to A' \to A \to A'' \to 0$ then yields the long exact sequence

$$
0 \to F(A') \to F(A) \to F(A'') \to R^1 F(A') \to R^1 F(A) \to R^1 F(A'') \to R^2 F(A') \to \cdots
$$

so $R^1 F$ precisely repairs the failure of $F$ to be right exact. The dual construction with projective resolutions gives the left derived functors $L_n F$ of a right-exact functor [ref: TODO_REF Cartan-Eilenberg Ch. V].

The two universal examples are $\operatorname{Ext}$ and $\operatorname{Tor}$. Over any ring, $\operatorname{Ext}_R^n(M, N)$ may be computed either from a projective resolution of $M$ (applying $\operatorname{Hom}_R(-, N)$) or from an injective resolution of $N$ (applying $\operatorname{Hom}_R(M, -)$); the two agree — this is the **balance** of $\operatorname{Ext}$, and $\operatorname{Tor}$ satisfies the analogous symmetry $\operatorname{Tor}_n^R(M, N) \cong \operatorname{Tor}_n^R(N, M)$ when $R$ is commutative [ref: TODO_REF Weibel §2.7]. In the lowest degree, $\operatorname{Ext}_R^1(M, N)$ classifies extensions of $M$ by $N$ up to the Yoneda equivalence relation: the split extension is the zero class, and every non-split short exact sequence $0 \to N \to E \to M \to 0$ is a non-zero element of $\operatorname{Ext}^1$.

Three structural facts make the theory usable. First, $\operatorname{Tor}_0^R(M, N) \cong M \otimes_R N$ and $\operatorname{Ext}_R^0(M, N) \cong \operatorname{Hom}_R(M, N)$, so the derived functors literally extend the original ones. Second, if $M$ has a projective resolution of length $\ell$, then $L_n F(M) = 0$ for $n > \ell$; over a PID every module has a length-$1$ resolution, so only $L_0$ and $L_1$ survive, which is why computations over $\mathbb{Z}$ reduce to a single map (Exercises 7 and 8). Third, a functor is exact precisely when all its higher derived functors vanish, restoring the clean case in which short exact sequences are preserved.

A second technique, **dimension shifting**, turns the long exact sequence into a recursive computation tool. Splicing the long exact sequence for $0 \to N \to E \to M \to 0$ with a resolution of $M$ expresses $R^n F(N)$ in terms of $R^{n-1} F$ of the next syzygy, stepping the degree down by one at the cost of replacing the module by a kernel of the resolution. Iterating reduces any $R^n F$ to a computation of $R^1 F$ on successive syzygies — which is why a projective resolution of length $\ell$ kills $R^n F$ for $n > \ell$, and why finitely generated modules over a regular local ring have bounded cohomological dimension.

When the input is not a single short exact sequence but a whole filtered complex, the long exact sequences assemble into a **spectral sequence**: a page $E_r^{p,q}$ of bigraded modules with differentials $d_r$ of bidegree $(r, 1-r)$ converging, under mild boundedness hypotheses, to the associated graded of the desired homology [ref: TODO_REF Weibel Ch. 5]. The two archetypes are the Grothendieck spectral sequence $R^p G \circ R^q F \Rightarrow R^{p+q}(G \circ F)$ for composed functors and the hypercohomology spectral sequence that computes sheaf cohomology from an open cover — the latter being the bridge from this unit to the Čech-to-derived-functor comparison in [04.03.03] and the vanishing theorems of [04.03.05].

**Synthesis.** Derived functors are the load-bearing construction of the whole subject: the snake lemma proved in the Key theorem builds toward [04.03.06] where $\operatorname{Ext}$ and $\operatorname{Tor}$ are computed by resolving either argument, the long exact sequence in homology appears again in [04.03.01] as the sheaf-cohomology sequence governing coherent sheaves on schemes, this is exactly the mechanism that converts a half-exact functor into a fully exact one, $\operatorname{Ext}^1$ classifying extensions generalises the split-versus-non-split dichotomy to all modules at once, the projective-versus-injective resolution story is dual to itself so that left and right derived functors are two faces of one construction, and the central insight is that homology measures the precise obstruction to exactness; putting these together, the bridge is that $\operatorname{Tor}$, $\operatorname{Ext}$, sheaf cohomology, and group cohomology are all instances of the single derived-functor construction.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Existence of projective resolutions).** In $R\text{-Mod}$, every module $M$ admits a projective resolution.

*Proof.* Every module is a quotient of a free module: choose a generating set $\{m_\alpha\}$ of $M$ and let $P_0 = \bigoplus_\alpha R$ surject onto $M$ by $e_\alpha \mapsto m_\alpha$; call the kernel $K_0$. Since free modules are projective, $P_0$ is projective. Now repeat: $K_0$ is itself a quotient of a free module $P_1$, giving $P_1 \twoheadrightarrow K_0$ with kernel $K_1$. Composing $P_1 \twoheadrightarrow K_0 \hookrightarrow P_0$ yields $d_1$. Iterating produces an exact sequence $\cdots \to P_2 \to P_1 \to P_0 \to M \to 0$ with each $P_i$ free, hence projective. $\square$

**Proposition ($\operatorname{Ext}^1$ classifies extensions).** For $R$-modules $M, N$, the set of equivalence classes of short exact sequences $0 \to N \to E \to M \to 0$ (the Yoneda equivalence) is in bijection with $\operatorname{Ext}_R^1(M, N)$.

*Proof sketch.* Fix a projective resolution $P_\bullet \to M$ and let $K_0 = \ker(P_0 \to M)$. Apply $\operatorname{Hom}_R(-, N)$ to the truncated resolution $0 \to K_0 \to P_0 \to M \to 0$; since $P_0$ is projective, $\operatorname{Hom}(P_0, N) \to \operatorname{Hom}(K_0, N) \to \operatorname{Ext}^1(M, N) \to 0$ is exact, so $\operatorname{Ext}^1(M,N) \cong \operatorname{Hom}(K_0, N)/\operatorname{im}\operatorname{Hom}(P_0, N)$. A short exact sequence $0 \to N \to E \to M \to 0$ is the pushout of $0 \to K_0 \to P_0 \to M \to 0$ along a map $K_0 \to N$; two maps give equivalent extensions exactly when they differ by an element of $\operatorname{im}\operatorname{Hom}(P_0, N)$, which is precisely the quotient. The split extension corresponds to the zero class. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Rings and modules [01.03.01].** Homological algebra lives inside the abelian category $R\text{-Mod}$ of modules over a ring [01.03.01]; every construction here — chain complex, exact sequence, $\operatorname{Tor}$, $\operatorname{Ext}$ — is a machine built on top of the modules and homomorphisms established in that prerequisite unit, and tensor products of modules are the input from which $\operatorname{Tor}$ is derived.

- **Sheaf cohomology [04.03.01].** The global-sections functor $\Gamma(X, -)$ on sheaves of modules is left exact but not exact, and its right derived functors $H^n(X, -) = R^n \Gamma(X, -)$ are sheaf cohomology [04.03.01]; the long exact sequence proved here is exactly the sheaf-cohomology long exact sequence that drives algebraic geometry.

- **Derived functors and Ext in geometry [04.03.06].** The $\operatorname{Ext}$ and derived-functor formalism developed here is specialised to coherent sheaves in [04.03.06], where $\operatorname{Ext}$ sheaves classify extensions of sheaves and underpin Serre duality; the abstract balance of $\operatorname{Ext}$ becomes a concrete geometric tool.

- **Derived categories [04.03.11].** Passing from individual derived functors to the derived category $\mathbf{D}(\mathcal{A})$ — the localisation of the category of complexes at quasi-isomorphisms [04.03.11] — packages all the long exact sequences here into a single triangulated structure in which $R F$ and $L F$ become exact functors between derived categories.

- **Galois and group cohomology [01.02.15].** Group cohomology $H^n(G, M)$ is the right derived functor of the $G$-invariants functor $M \mapsto M^G$ on $G$-modules, and specialises to Galois cohomology and Hilbert's theorem 90 in [01.02.15]; it is the most direct arithmetic application of the derived-functor machine.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Homological algebra was forged between 1940 and 1957 from three pressures: the need to organise the cohomology theories then proliferating in topology (singular, Čech, sheaf); the algebraic reformulation of extension problems in group theory and ring theory; and Grothendieck's insistence that the subject be re-derived inside an arbitrary abelian category rather than only module categories [ref: TODO_REF Grothendieck 1957]. The founding synthesis was Cartan and Eilenberg's 1956 *Homological Algebra*, which unified $\operatorname{Tor}$, $\operatorname{Ext}$, resolutions, and spectral sequences under a single diagrammatic discipline [ref: TODO_REF Cartan-Eilenberg 1956].

The 1957 Tohoku paper of Grothendieck then showed that the entire Cartan-Eilenberg machine depended only on the axioms of an abelian category and the existence of enough injectives, which made the theory portable to sheaves of modules and so planted the seed of modern algebraic geometry. Weibel's 1994 textbook and Gelfand-Manin's 2003 *Methods of Homological Algebra* record the further passage, in the late twentieth century, from derived functors to derived categories — a shift of viewpoint in which the long exact sequences proved here become shadows of a single triangulated structure.

The philosophical point is that homology replaces the unanswerable question "is this functor exact?" with the answerable one "how far does it deviate from exactness, in every degree?" The obstruction, once named and graded, becomes a computable invariant — and that move, from a yes/no failure to a measured invariant, is the move that makes homological algebra the lingua franca of modern geometry, topology, and number theory.
