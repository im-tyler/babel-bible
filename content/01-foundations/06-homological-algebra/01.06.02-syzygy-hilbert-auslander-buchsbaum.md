## Intuition [Beginner]

A module is hard to describe directly. The first move is to list its generators: a free module that maps onto it. But generators satisfy relations — equations the generators obey. Those relations form a new module, and to describe that one you list its generators, which obey relations among relations, and so on. This cascade is a **free resolution**.

Hilbert's syzygy theorem is the remarkable statement that over the polynomial ring in $n$ variables, this cascade always stops: after at most $n$ layers of relations, the next module is free. The number of variables is a hard ceiling on how tangled the relations can become. The first syzygy is the module of relations among generators; the second is relations among those; Hilbert says the chain terminates.

A **regular sequence** is a list of equations each of which genuinely cuts the module down — each is a non-zero-divisor on what is left. The **depth** counts how long such a chain can run. The Auslander-Buchsbaum formula is a conservation law: the length of the shortest resolution plus the depth always equals the depth of the ring itself.

Together these three results — the syzygy theorem, projective dimension, and the Auslander-Buchsbaum formula — explain why polynomial rings are homologically tame and why regular local rings behave like smooth points of a space.

## Visual [Beginner]

A resolution reads left to right as a staircase of free modules, each kernel feeding the next map; Hilbert's theorem caps the number of stairs, and the Auslander-Buchsbaum formula balances two quantities against the fixed depth of the ring.

![A staircase of free modules F_2 to F_1 to F_0 to M, each arrow labelled by the next syzygy kernel, terminating at layer n; below it a balance scale with pd(M) plus depth(M) on one side equal to depth(R) on the other.](/img/syzygy-hilbert-auslander-buchsbaum-placeholder.svg)

| Layer | Name | What it captures |
|---|---|---|
| $F_0 \to M$ | generators | a free cover of $M$ |
| $F_1 \to F_0$ | first syzygy | relations among the generators |
| $F_2 \to F_1$ | second syzygy | relations among the relations |
| $F_n \to F_{n-1}$ | $n$-th syzygy | Hilbert: stops by layer $n$ over $k[x_1,\dots,x_n]$ |

The balance line beneath the staircase is the conservation law: $\operatorname{pd}(M) + \operatorname{depth}(M) = \operatorname{depth}(R)$. Each extra layer of resolution trades for exactly one unit of depth.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take $R = k[x,y]$, polynomials in two variables, and the module $M = k$, the simplest module over $R$. It is generated by the single element $1$, so a free module $F_0 = R$ maps onto $k$ by sending each polynomial to its constant term.

Step 1. The kernel is the ideal of polynomials with zero constant term, which is $(x, y)$. This ideal needs two generators, $x$ and $y$, so the first syzygy layer is $F_1 = R \oplus R$ mapping onto $F_0$ by the row $(x\ y)$: a pair $(a, b)$ lands on $a x + b y$.

Step 2. The single relation between $x$ and $y$ is that $y\cdot x - x\cdot y = 0$. Written as a pair this is $(-y,\ x)$, so $F_2 = R$ maps into $F_1$ by the column $\binom{-y}{x}$.

Step 3. Check the composition: multiply the row $(x\ y)$ by the column $\binom{-y}{x}$. The result is $x\cdot(-y) + y\cdot x = -xy + xy = 0$. The composition vanishes, as required, and there are no further relations, so the resolution ends at $F_2$.

What this tells us: the resolution $0 \to R \to R^2 \to R \to k \to 0$ has length $2$, exactly the number of variables. Hilbert's syzygy theorem guarantees this is the worst case over $k[x,y]$.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $R$ be a ring and $M$ an $R$-module. A **free resolution** of $M$ is an exact sequence

$$
\cdots \longrightarrow F_2 \xrightarrow{d_2} F_1 \xrightarrow{d_1} F_0 \xrightarrow{\varepsilon} M \longrightarrow 0
$$

with each $F_i$ a free $R$-module [ref: TODO_REF Eisenbud Ch. 17]. The **$i$-th syzygy module** is $\operatorname{Syz}_i^R(M) = \ker d_{i-1}$ (with $d_{-1} = \varepsilon$ and $\operatorname{Syz}_0(M) = M$). Thus $\operatorname{Syz}_1(M) = \ker \varepsilon$ is the module of relations among the chosen generators of $M$, and the resolution builds $\operatorname{Syz}_{i+1}$ as a free cover of $\operatorname{Syz}_i$. Syzygies depend on the chosen cover, but **Schanuel's lemma** controls the ambiguity: given two short exact sequences $0 \to K \to P \to M \to 0$ and $0 \to K' \to P' \to M \to 0$ with $P, P'$ projective, there is an isomorphism $K \oplus P' \cong K' \oplus P$. Iterating, syzygies are well-defined up to projective summands.

The **projective dimension** of $M$ is

$$
\operatorname{pd}_R(M) \;=\; \inf\{\, n \ge 0 \;:\; M \text{ admits a projective resolution of length } n \,\},
$$

with $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) = \infty$ if no such resolution exists [ref: TODO_REF Weibel Ch. 4]. Equivalently $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) = \sup\{\, n : \operatorname{Ext}_R^n(M, N) \ne 0 \text{ for some } N \,\}$. Consequently $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) = 0$ iff $M$ is projective, and $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) \le n$ iff $\operatorname{Ext}_R^{n+1}(M, N) = 0$ for every module $N$. The **global dimension** of $R$ is $\operatorname{gldim} R = \sup_M \operatorname{pd}_R(M)$.

Let $I \subseteq R$ be an ideal and $M$ a finitely generated $R$-module with $IM \ne M$. A sequence $x_1, \dots, x_r \in I$ is **$M$-regular** if $x_i$ is a non-zero-divisor on $M/(x_1, \dots, x_{i-1})M$ for each $i$, and $M/(x_1, \dots, x_r)M \ne 0$. The **$I$-depth** of $M$ is the length of the longest $M$-regular sequence in $I$:

$$
\operatorname{depth}_I(M) \;=\; \sup\{\, r \;:\; \text{there exists an $M$-regular sequence of length } r \text{ in } I \,\}.
$$

For a Noetherian local ring $(R, \mathfrak m, k)$, write $\operatorname{depth}(M) = \operatorname{depth}_{\mathfrak m}(M)$. Two homological characterisations make depth computable without exhibiting a regular sequence [ref: TODO_REF Eisenbud Ch. 17-18]:

$$
\operatorname{depth}_I(M) \;=\; \inf\{\, n \ge 0 \;:\; \operatorname{Ext}_R^n(R/I,\, M) \ne 0 \,\},
$$

and, for local $(R, \mathfrak m, k)$, $\operatorname{depth}(M) = \inf\{\, n \;:\; \operatorname{Ext}_R^n(k, M) \ne 0\,\}$, equivalently $\operatorname{depth}_I(M) = n - \sup\{\, i : H_i(x_1, \dots, x_n;\, M) \ne 0\,\}$ where $x_1, \dots, x_n$ generate $I$ and $H_\bullet$ is Koszul homology. The inequality $\operatorname{depth}(M) \le \dim M$ always holds; when equality obtains, $M$ is **Cohen-Macaulay**.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Syzygies depend on the cover.* Over $R = k[x,y]$, the residue field $k$ has the minimal free cover $R \twoheadrightarrow k$, whose first syzygy is the ideal $(x,y)$; using the non-minimal cover $R \oplus R \twoheadrightarrow k$ instead produces a first syzygy with an extra free summand. Schanuel's lemma records exactly this freedom.
- *Order matters for regular sequences.* In $R = k[x,y]$, the sequence $x, y$ is $R$-regular, but $xy, x$ is not: modulo $xy$, the element $x$ is killed by $y \ne 0$, so $x$ is a zero-divisor on $R/(xy)$. The condition "non-zero-divisor on the successive quotient" is stronger than "each element nonzero".
- *Depth can be strictly smaller than dimension.* In $R = k[[x,y]]/(xy)$, the maximal ideal consists of zero-divisors (since $x \cdot y = 0$), so $\operatorname{depth}(R) = 0$ while $\dim(R) = 1$. The ring is not Cohen-Macaulay.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Auslander-Buchsbaum formula).** Let $(R, \mathfrak m, k)$ be a Noetherian local ring and $M$ a finitely generated $R$-module with $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) < \infty$. Then

$$
\operatorname{pd}_R(M) \;+\; \operatorname{depth}(M) \;=\; \operatorname{depth}(R). \qquad\text{[ref: TODO_REF Auslander-Buchsbaum 1957]}
$$

The proof rests on the **depth lemma**, the homological bookkeeping for a short exact sequence. For any short exact sequence $0 \to M' \to M \to M'' \to 0$ of finite $R$-modules,

$$
\operatorname{depth}(M') \;\ge\; \min(\operatorname{depth}(M),\, \operatorname{depth}(M'') + 1), \qquad
\operatorname{depth}(M'') \;\ge\; \min(\operatorname{depth}(M),\, \operatorname{depth}(M') - 1),
$$

which follows from the long exact sequence of $\operatorname{Ext}_R^\bullet(k, -)$ together with the identification $\operatorname{depth}(N) = \inf\{\, i : \operatorname{Ext}_R^i(k, N) \ne 0\,\}$ [ref: TODO_REF Eisenbud Prop. 18.4].

*Proof.* Induct on $d = \operatorname{pd}_R(M)$.

*Base case $d = 0$.* Then $M$ is projective, hence free over the local ring $R$ (a finite projective over a local ring is free). For a free module $F \cong R^{\oplus n}$, $\operatorname{Ext}_R^i(k, F) \cong \operatorname{Ext}_R^i(k, R)^{\oplus n}$, so $\operatorname{depth}(F) = \operatorname{depth}(R)$. The identity reads $0 + \operatorname{depth}(R) = \operatorname{depth}(R)$.

*Inductive step $d \ge 1$.* Choose a finite free cover $0 \to \Omega M \xrightarrow{\alpha} F \xrightarrow{\beta} M \to 0$. Splicing this short exact sequence with a projective resolution of $\Omega M$ of length $d - 1$ gives one of $M$ of length $d$, so $\operatorname{pd}_R(\Omega M) = d - 1$. The inductive hypothesis gives

$$
(d - 1) + \operatorname{depth}(\Omega M) \;=\; \operatorname{depth}(R), \tag{$\star$}
$$

hence $\operatorname{depth}(\Omega M) = \operatorname{depth}(R) - (d - 1) \le \operatorname{depth}(R)$.

Apply the depth lemma to $0 \to \Omega M \to F \to M \to 0$, using $\operatorname{depth}(F) = \operatorname{depth}(R)$:

$$
\operatorname{depth}(\Omega M) \;\ge\; \min(\operatorname{depth}(R),\, \operatorname{depth}(M) + 1), \tag{1}
$$
$$
\operatorname{depth}(M) \;\ge\; \min(\operatorname{depth}(R),\, \operatorname{depth}(\Omega M) - 1). \tag{2}
$$

First observe $\operatorname{depth}(M) < \operatorname{depth}(R)$. Suppose instead $\operatorname{depth}(M) \ge \operatorname{depth}(R)$. Then (2) forces $\operatorname{depth}(\Omega M) - 1 \ge \operatorname{depth}(R)$, i.e. $\operatorname{depth}(\Omega M) > \operatorname{depth}(R)$, contradicting $(\star)$. Hence $\operatorname{depth}(M) < \operatorname{depth}(R)$.

With $\operatorname{depth}(M) < \operatorname{depth}(R)$, inequality (1) reads $\operatorname{depth}(\Omega M) \ge \operatorname{depth}(M) + 1$, and inequality (2) becomes $\operatorname{depth}(M) \ge \operatorname{depth}(\Omega M) - 1$ (since $\operatorname{depth}(\Omega M) - 1 < \operatorname{depth}(R)$). Together these give $\operatorname{depth}(\Omega M) = \operatorname{depth}(M) + 1$. Substituting into $(\star)$,

$$
(d - 1) + (\operatorname{depth}(M) + 1) \;=\; \operatorname{depth}(R),
$$

that is, $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) + \operatorname{depth}(M) = \operatorname{depth}(R)$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This conservation law builds toward [04.03.06], where the $\operatorname{Ext}$ groups measuring depth here are the same derived functor that classifies extensions of coherent sheaves, and appears again in [04.02.07] as the homological engine behind dimension theory and regularity on schemes. The foundational reason the formula holds is that each syzygy layer of a minimal resolution over a local ring peels off exactly one unit of depth; this is exactly the equality $\operatorname{depth}(\Omega M) = \operatorname{depth}(M) + 1$ extracted from the depth lemma, and the bridge is that projective dimension and depth are two complementary measurements of a single module, trading off against the fixed depth of the ambient ring.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).**

Over the polynomial ring $R = k[x]$ in one variable, what is the largest possible projective dimension of a finitely generated $R$-module?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A polynomial ring in $n$ variables has global dimension $n$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

By Hilbert's syzygy theorem, $\operatorname{gldim}\, k[x] = 1$. Since $k[x]$ is a PID, every finitely generated module has a free resolution of length at most $1$. The answer is **$1$**.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, multiple choice).**

For an $R$-module $M$, the condition $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) = 0$ is equivalent to $M$ being which of the following?

A. the zero module  
B. projective  
C. injective  
D. finitely generated

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Projective dimension zero means a resolution of length zero exists.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. Projective.** A resolution of length zero is $0 \to P_0 \to M \to 0$ with $P_0$ projective, which forces $M \cong P_0$ to be projective. Conversely every projective module has such a resolution.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) \le n$ if and only if $\operatorname{Ext}_R^{n+1}(M, N) = 0$ for every $R$-module $N$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the characterisation of projective dimension as the largest degree in which $\operatorname{Ext}$ does not vanish, computed from any projective resolution.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take a projective resolution $P_\bullet \to M$. Then $\operatorname{Ext}_R^i(M, N) = H^i(\operatorname{Hom}_R(P_\bullet, N))$. If $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) \le n$, there is a resolution with $P_i = 0$ for $i > n$, so $\operatorname{Hom}_R(P_\bullet, N)$ is zero in degrees above $n$, giving $\operatorname{Ext}_R^{n+1}(M, N) = 0$ for all $N$. Conversely, if $\operatorname{Ext}_R^{n+1}(M, N) = 0$ for all $N$, take a resolution truncated at its $(n{+}1)$-st syzygy $K$; dimension shifting gives $\operatorname{Ext}^1(K, N) \cong \operatorname{Ext}^{n+2}(M, N) = 0$, so $K$ is projective and the resolution truncates to length $n$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

Let $R = k[x,y]$ and $k = R/(x,y)$. Using the Koszul resolution $0 \to R \xrightarrow{d_2} R^2 \xrightarrow{d_1} R \to k \to 0$ with $d_1 = (x\ y)$ and $d_2 = \binom{-y}{x}$, compute $\dim_k \operatorname{Ext}_R^2(k, k)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply $\operatorname{Hom}_R(-, k)$ and note that $x$ and $y$ annihilate $k$, so the induced differentials on $\operatorname{Hom}$ are zero.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Applying $\operatorname{Hom}_R(-, k)$ and using $\operatorname{Hom}_R(R, k) \cong k$ gives the cochain complex $0 \to k \xrightarrow{\delta^0} k^2 \xrightarrow{\delta^1} k \to 0$ in degrees $0, 1, 2$. The maps $\delta^0, \delta^1$ are dual to $d_1, d_2$, but their entries are $x, y$, which act as zero on $k = R/(x,y)$. Hence both maps are zero and $\operatorname{Ext}_R^2(k, k) = \operatorname{coker}(\delta^1) = k$. The dimension is **$1$**.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

In $R = k[x,y]$, show that $x, y$ is an $R$-regular sequence but $xy, x$ is not.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the second sequence, examine what $x$ does on the quotient $R/(xy)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$R = k[x,y]$ is a domain, so $x$ is a non-zero-divisor, and $R/(x) \cong k[y]$ is a domain, so $y$ is a non-zero-divisor on $R/(x)$. Hence $x, y$ is $R$-regular. For $xy, x$: $xy$ is a non-zero-divisor on the domain $R$, but on $R/(xy)$ the element $x$ is killed by the nonzero class of $y$, since $x \cdot y = xy = 0$ in $R/(xy)$. So $x$ is a zero-divisor on $R/(xy)$, and $xy, x$ is not $R$-regular.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="multiple-choice" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, multiple choice).**

Over a regular local ring $R$ of dimension $d$, the projective dimension of a finitely generated $R$-module is at most:

A. $0$  
B. $d$  
C. $2d$  
D. unbounded

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A regular local ring has finite global dimension equal to its Krull dimension.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B. $d$.** By the Auslander-Buchsbaum-Serre theorem, a regular local ring of dimension $d$ has global dimension $d$, so every finitely generated module has projective dimension at most $d$. The Auslander-Buchsbaum formula refines this: $\operatorname{pd}(M) = d - \operatorname{depth}(M) \le d$ since $\operatorname{depth}(M) \ge 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove Schanuel's lemma: if $0 \to K \xrightarrow{\alpha} P \xrightarrow{\beta} M \to 0$ and $0 \to K' \xrightarrow{\alpha'} P' \xrightarrow{\beta'} M \to 0$ are exact with $P, P'$ projective, then $K \oplus P' \cong K' \oplus P$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Since $P$ is projective and $\beta'$ is onto, lift $\beta: P \to M$ to a map $\gamma: P \to P'$ with $\beta' \gamma = \beta$, then build a map $K \oplus P' \to P$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Choose $\gamma: P \to P'$ with $\beta'\gamma = \beta$ (projectivity of $P$, surjectivity of $\beta'$). Define $\varphi: K \oplus P' \to P$ by $\varphi(k, p') = \alpha(k) + \gamma'(p')$ where $\gamma': P' \to P$ is a lift of $\beta'$ through $\beta$ (i.e. $\beta\gamma' = \beta'$, from projectivity of $P'$). Then $\varphi$ is onto: for $p \in P$, pick $p' \in P'$ with $\beta'(p') = \beta(p)$, so $p - \gamma'(p') \in \ker\beta = \operatorname{im}\alpha$, say $= \alpha(k)$, giving $p = \alpha(k) + \gamma'(p') = \varphi(k, p')$. Its kernel is $\{(\alpha^{-1}(-\gamma'(p')),\, p') : \beta'(p') = 0\} \cong K'$, so $0 \to K' \to K \oplus P' \to P \to 0$ is exact and splits (P projective), giving $K \oplus P' \cong K' \oplus P$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $R$ be a regular local ring and $M$ a finitely generated $R$-module. Show that if $M$ is Cohen-Macaulay, then $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) = \dim R - \dim M$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply Auslander-Buchsbaum and the Cohen-Macaulay equality $\operatorname{depth}(M) = \dim(M)$, together with the regularity of $R$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A regular local ring is itself Cohen-Macaulay, so $\operatorname{depth}(R) = \dim(R)$. Since $R$ is regular, $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) < \infty$, and the Auslander-Buchsbaum formula gives $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) = \operatorname{depth}(R) - \operatorname{depth}(M)$. If $M$ is Cohen-Macaulay, $\operatorname{depth}(M) = \dim(M)$. Substituting, $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) = \dim(R) - \dim(M)$, the codimension of the support of $M$. This is the link from the Auslander-Buchsbaum formula to intersection theory.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Hilbert's syzygy theorem.** Let $S = k[x_1, \dots, x_n]$. Every finitely generated $S$-module $M$ admits a free resolution of length at most $n$; equivalently $\operatorname{gldim}\, S = n$ [ref: TODO_REF Hilbert 1890]. The sharp example is the residue field $k = S/(x_1, \dots, x_n)$: the Koszul complex $K_\bullet(x_1, \dots, x_n;\, S)$ is a free resolution of $k$ of length $n$, so $\operatorname{pd}_S(k) = n$. The universal bound $\operatorname{pd}_S(M) \le n$ for all $M$ now drops out of the Auslander-Buchsbaum formula applied to the regular local ring $S_{\mathfrak m}$ at $\mathfrak m = (x_1, \dots, x_n)$: there $\operatorname{depth}(S_{\mathfrak m}) = n$, so $\operatorname{pd}_{S_{\mathfrak m}}(M_{\mathfrak m}) = n - \operatorname{depth}(M_{\mathfrak m}) \le n$, and localization cannot decrease what is already bounded. The graded version, closer to Hilbert's original statement, produces a *graded* free resolution of length $\le n$ with explicit Betti numbers read off from the Hilbert function of $M$.

**The Auslander-Buchsbaum-Serre theorem.** A Noetherian local ring $(R, \mathfrak m, k)$ is **regular** when $\mathfrak m$ can be generated by $\dim R$ elements, i.e. the embedding dimension $\mu(\mathfrak m)$ equals the Krull dimension. The Auslander-Buchsbaum-Serre theorem equates four conditions [ref: TODO_REF Serre 1955]:

- $R$ is regular;
- $\operatorname{gldim}(R) < \infty$;
- $\operatorname{pd}_R(k) < \infty$;
- $\operatorname{pd}_R(k) = \dim R$.

When these hold, $\operatorname{gldim}(R) = \dim R$. The direction "regular $\Rightarrow$ $\operatorname{pd}_R(k) < \infty$" is the Koszul resolution: if $\mathfrak m$ is generated by a regular sequence $x_1, \dots, x_d$, the Koszul complex resolves $k$ in length $d$. The reverse direction uses that a minimal free resolution of $k$ has $\operatorname{rank} F_i = \dim_k \operatorname{Tor}_i^R(k, k)$, and the inequality $\dim_k \operatorname{Tor}_1^R(k,k) = \mu(\mathfrak m) \ge \dim R$ together with the principal ideal theorem forces $\mu(\mathfrak m) = \dim R$ when $\operatorname{pd}_R(k)$ is finite. The theorem makes regularity — geometrically, the smoothness of a point — into a purely homological property, which is the entry point to regular morphisms in scheme theory.

**Minimal free resolutions.** Over a local ring $(R, \mathfrak m, k)$, a free resolution $F_\bullet \to M$ is **minimal** when each differential $d_i: F_i \to F_{i-1}$ has image inside $\mathfrak m F_{i-1}$, equivalently $d_i \otimes k = 0$. A minimal resolution is unique up to isomorphism of complexes, and its ranks satisfy the Betti-number formula $\beta_i(M) = \operatorname{rank} F_i = \dim_k \operatorname{Tor}_i^R(k, M)$. The Auslander-Buchsbaum formula then reads off the length of the minimal resolution as $\operatorname{pd}(M) = \operatorname{depth}(R) - \operatorname{depth}(M)$, and the resolution terminates exactly at the projective dimension. The Poincaré series $P_k^R(t) = \sum_i \dim_k \operatorname{Tor}_i^R(k, k)\, t^i$ is a fundamental invariant: for a regular local ring it is the polynomial $(1 + t)^{\dim R}$, and deviations from this polynomial measure how far $R$ sits from regularity.

**Syzygy theorem as a flatness and duality tool.** Over a regular local ring, the boundedness of projective dimension makes every finitely generated module a perfect complex, so derived duals $\mathbf{R}\!\operatorname{Hom}_R(M, -)$ commute with derived tensor in the derived category. The Auslander-Buchsbaum formula then specialises to the codimension formula of Exercise 8 for Cohen-Macaulay modules, which is the homological input to intersection multiplicities: Serre's intersection multiplicity $\chi(M, N) = \sum_i (-1)^i \operatorname{length}\, \operatorname{Tor}_i^R(M, N)$ is well-defined precisely because projective dimension is finite over a regular ring.

**Synthesis.** Hilbert's syzygy theorem, the projective-dimension formalism, and the Auslander-Buchsbaum formula build toward [04.03.06], where $\operatorname{Ext}$ computes both depth and sheaf extension groups, and the regular-local-ring characterisation appears again in [04.02.07] as the homological definition of a smooth point on a scheme; this is exactly the conservation law that ties resolution length to depth, the central insight is that a minimal resolution peels off one unit of depth per syzygy, the regular-sequence viewpoint generalises to the Koszul complex and complete intersections, the projective-versus-flat dimension story is dual to itself across the derived category, and the bridge is that regularity, finite global dimension, and finite projective dimension of the residue field are three faces of one condition; putting these together, polynomial rings and regular local rings are precisely the rings on which homological algebra terminates in bounded length.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Koszul complex resolves the residue field).** Let $S = k[x_1, \dots, x_n]$ and $\mathfrak m = (x_1, \dots, x_n)$. The Koszul complex $K_\bullet(x_1, \dots, x_n;\, S)$ is a free resolution of $k = S/\mathfrak m$ of length $n$.

*Proof.* The Koszul complex has $K_i = \bigwedge^i S^n$ (free of rank $\binom{n}{i}$) in degree $i$, with differential determined by exterior contraction against the vector $(x_1, \dots, x_n)$:

$$
d(e_{j_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge e_{j_i}) \;=\; \sum_{\ell=1}^{i} (-1)^{\ell - 1}\, x_{j_\ell}\, e_{j_1} \wedge \cdots \wedge \widehat{e_{j_\ell}} \wedge \cdots \wedge e_{j_i}.
$$

It is a chain complex of free $S$-modules of length $n$, with $H_0(K) = S/\mathfrak m = k$. The key fact is that $x_1, \dots, x_n$ is an $S$-regular sequence, which forces acyclicity in positive degrees: $H_i(K) = 0$ for $i \ge 1$. This is proved by induction on $n$. For $n = 1$, $K_\bullet$ is $0 \to S \xrightarrow{x_1} S \to 0$, acyclic in positive degree because $x_1$ is a non-zero-divisor. For the inductive step, identify $K_\bullet(x_1, \dots, x_n)$ as the mapping cone of the chain map "multiplication by $x_n$" on $K_\bullet(x_1, \dots, x_{n-1})$; the associated long exact homology sequence, together with the fact that $x_n$ is a non-zero-divisor on the homology of $K_\bullet(x_1, \dots, x_{n-1})$ (which is $k$ concentrated in degree $0$ by induction), yields $H_i(K_\bullet(x_1, \dots, x_n)) = 0$ for $i \ge 1$. Hence $K_\bullet \twoheadrightarrow k$ is a free resolution of length $n$, and $\operatorname{pd}_S(k) = n$. $\square$

**Proposition (Auslander-Buchsbaum-Serre, regularity $\Rightarrow$ finite global dimension).** If $(R, \mathfrak m, k)$ is a regular local ring, then $\operatorname{pd}_R(k) = \dim R$, and consequently $\operatorname{gldim}(R) = \dim R$.

*Proof sketch.* If $R$ is regular, $\mathfrak m$ is generated by a regular sequence $x_1, \dots, x_d$ with $d = \dim R$. Localising the Koszul complex of the previous proposition gives a free resolution of $k$ over $R$ of length $d$, so $\operatorname{pd}_R(k) \le d$. Minimality shows this resolution is minimal, so $\operatorname{Tor}_d^R(k, k) \cong k \ne 0$ and $\operatorname{pd}_R(k) \ge d$; hence equality. For any finitely generated $R$-module $M$, the Auslander-Buchsbaum formula gives $\operatorname{pd}_R(M) = d - \operatorname{depth}(M) \le d$, so $\operatorname{gldim}(R) \le d$, while $\operatorname{pd}_R(k) = d$ forces $\operatorname{gldim}(R) \ge d$. Thus $\operatorname{gldim}(R) = d$. The converse (finite $\operatorname{pd}_R(k)$ implies regularity) requires the inequality $\mu(\mathfrak m) = \dim_k \operatorname{Tor}_1^R(k,k) \le \operatorname{pd}_R(k)$ against Krull's principal ideal theorem; see [ref: TODO_REF Serre 1955]. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Homological algebra foundations [01.06.01].** Syzygies, free resolutions, and projective dimension are built directly on the chain complexes, exact sequences, and derived functors established in [01.06.01]; the projective dimension here is the length of the projective resolutions introduced there, and $\operatorname{Ext}$ — the right derived functor of $\operatorname{Hom}$ from that unit — is the device that computes both projective dimension and depth.

- **Rings and modules [01.03.01].** Free and projective resolutions live inside the module category of a ring from [01.03.01]; syzygies are submodules of free modules, and Schanuel's lemma is a statement about the ambiguity of short exact sequences of the kind classified there.

- **Commutative algebra foundations [01.05.01].** The Auslander-Buchsbaum formula operates over Noetherian local rings, the localisation and primary-decomposition machinery of [01.05.01]; the commutative algebra of Noetherian rings is the soil in which depth, regular sequences, and regular local rings grow.

- **Derived functors and Ext in geometry [04.03.06].** The $\operatorname{Ext}$ used here to measure depth and projective dimension is specialised to coherent sheaves in [04.03.06], where the same derived functor classifies sheaf extensions and underpins local-to-global spectral sequences on schemes.

- **Dimension theory on schemes [04.02.07].** The Auslander-Buchsbaum-Serre characterisation of regular local rings is the homological definition of a smooth point; in [04.02.07] it becomes the algebraic-geometric criterion for a scheme to be regular, connecting projective dimension to Krull dimension and the Nullstellensatz.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Hilbert proved the syzygy theorem in 1890 in *Über die Theorie der algebraischen Formen*, as the closing step of his reform of invariant theory; the word "syzygy," an astronomical term for a conjunction of bodies, had been borrowed into algebra by Sylvester to denote a relation among relations [ref: TODO_REF Hilbert 1890]. Hilbert's theorem showed that the "relations among relations among relations" over a polynomial ring must terminate in at most $n$ steps, a finiteness result that settled the generation of invariants and foreshadowed the modern theory of free resolutions. The homological reformulation had to await Cartan and Eilenberg's *Homological Algebra* (1956), which reframed syzygies as kernels in a long exact sequence.

The depth notion crystallised in the early 1950s through work of Rees, Samuel, and Auslander-Buchsbaum. The Auslander-Buchsbaum formula was announced in 1957 [ref: TODO_REF Auslander-Buchsbaum 1957], and the full equivalence between regularity of a local ring and finite global dimension is due independently to Auslander-Buchsbaum and Serre, the latter in his 1955 *Sur la dimension homologique des anneaux et des modules noethériens* [ref: TODO_REF Serre 1955]. Serre's paper introduced the Koszul complex — originally a tool of Koszul (1950) in the cohomology of Lie algebras — into commutative algebra, and proved that a local ring is regular exactly when its residue field has finite projective dimension. This made smoothness of a point a homological property, the entry point to Grothendieck's definition of a regular morphism in scheme theory. The Auslander-Buchsbaum-Serre theorem, the Koszul complex, and the Auslander-Buchsbaum formula together converted the finiteness Hilbert had observed in 1890 into the structural backbone of modern commutative algebra.
