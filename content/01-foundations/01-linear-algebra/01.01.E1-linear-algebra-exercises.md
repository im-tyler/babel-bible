## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Apostol Vol. 2 Chapters 1-5 build the linear-algebra spine: abstract linear spaces with subspace, span, independence, and dimension (Ch. 1); linear transformations, the rank-nullity theorem, and the matrix of a map in fixed bases (Ch. 2); the determinant as the unique multilinear alternating normalised function, with expansion and product rules (Ch. 3); eigenvalues, eigenvectors, the characteristic polynomial, and diagonalisability (Ch. 4); and the inner-product theory of Euclidean spaces — Gram-Schmidt, orthogonal projection, and the spectral theorem for symmetric operators (Ch. 5).

This pack collects ten problems drawn from those chapters — three easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a complete worked solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units rather than as a standalone development. The problems test operational competence: computing a dimension, applying rank-nullity, evaluating a determinant by structure rather than brute force, diagonalising a matrix, and orthonormalising a basis. Several deliberately reward a structural argument over a computational one, in Apostol's style.

Conventions follow the prerequisite units: $V$, $W$ denote finite-dimensional vector spaces over a field $\mathbb{F}$ ($\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$ unless stated); $T: V \to W$ a linear map; $\ker T$ and $\operatorname{im} T$ its kernel and image; $\chi_A(\lambda) = \det(\lambda I - A)$ the characteristic polynomial; $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$ an inner product.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one problem in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining nine follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead problem.** *Rank-nullity and the dimension of a sum.* Let $U, W$ be subspaces of a finite-dimensional space $V$. Prove $\dim(U + W) + \dim(U \cap W) = \dim U + \dim W$, and deduce that if $\dim U + \dim W > \dim V$ then $U \cap W \neq \{0\}$.

**Solution.** Let $\{e_1, \ldots, e_k\}$ be a basis of $U \cap W$, so $\dim(U \cap W) = k$. Since $U \cap W \subseteq U$, extend it to a basis $\{e_1, \ldots, e_k, u_1, \ldots, u_p\}$ of $U$, so $\dim U = k + p$. Likewise extend to a basis $\{e_1, \ldots, e_k, w_1, \ldots, w_q\}$ of $W$, so $\dim W = k + q$.

Claim: $B = \{e_1, \ldots, e_k, u_1, \ldots, u_p, w_1, \ldots, w_q\}$ is a basis of $U + W$. It spans, because any element of $U + W$ is a sum of a $U$-vector and a $W$-vector, each expressible in the listed vectors. For independence, suppose $\sum a_i e_i + \sum b_j u_j + \sum c_l w_l = 0$. Then $\sum c_l w_l = -\sum a_i e_i - \sum b_j u_j$ lies in $U$ (right side) and in $W$ (left side), so it lies in $U \cap W$, hence is a combination of the $e_i$. But the $w_l$ together with the $e_i$ form a basis of $W$, so the only way $\sum c_l w_l$ is a combination of the $e_i$ is $c_l = 0$ for all $l$. The relation reduces to $\sum a_i e_i + \sum b_j u_j = 0$, a relation among a basis of $U$, forcing all $a_i, b_j = 0$. So $B$ is independent.

Therefore $\dim(U + W) = k + p + q = (k + p) + (k + q) - k = \dim U + \dim W - \dim(U \cap W)$, which rearranges to the stated identity.

For the deduction: $\dim(U \cap W) = \dim U + \dim W - \dim(U + W) \geq \dim U + \dim W - \dim V > 0$, using $U + W \subseteq V$. A space of positive dimension contains a nonzero vector, so $U \cap W \neq \{0\}$. $\square$

This is the Grassmann dimension formula (Apostol Ch. 1 §1.13). The deduction is the standard pigeonhole on dimensions: two subspaces whose dimensions overcrowd the ambient space must meet nontrivially.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).** *Dimension of a polynomial subspace.*

Let $P_3$ be the space of real polynomials of degree $\leq 3$. Let $U = \{p \in P_3 : p(1) = 0\}$. Compute $\dim U$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$U$ is the kernel of the evaluation map $\operatorname{ev}_1: P_3 \to \mathbb{R}$, $p \mapsto p(1)$. Apply rank-nullity.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The evaluation map $\operatorname{ev}_1: P_3 \to \mathbb{R}$, $p \mapsto p(1)$, is linear. It is surjective (the constant polynomial $p \equiv c$ maps to $c$), so $\operatorname{im}(\operatorname{ev}_1) = \mathbb{R}$ has dimension $1$.

By rank-nullity, $\dim P_3 = \dim \ker(\operatorname{ev}_1) + \dim \operatorname{im}(\operatorname{ev}_1)$. Since $\dim P_3 = 4$ (basis $1, x, x^2, x^3$), we get $\dim U = \dim \ker(\operatorname{ev}_1) = 4 - 1 = 3$.

An explicit basis: $(x - 1), (x^2 - 1), (x^3 - 1)$, each vanishing at $x = 1$, and linearly independent.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Determinant by row operations.*

Compute $\det A$ for
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand along the first row, or clear the $(2,1)$ entry by a row operation first. Determinants are unchanged by adding a multiple of one row to another.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Expand along the first row:
$$
\det A = 2 \det\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} - 1 \det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} + 0.
$$
The first $2 \times 2$ determinant is $4 - 1 = 3$; the second is $2 - 0 = 2$. So $\det A = 2 \cdot 3 - 1 \cdot 2 = 6 - 2 = 4$.

This is the tridiagonal matrix with $2$ on the diagonal and $1$ off-diagonal; its determinants satisfy $D_n = 2 D_{n-1} - D_{n-2}$ with $D_1 = 2$, $D_2 = 3$, giving $D_n = n + 1$, consistent with $D_3 = 4$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, symbolic).** *Eigenvalues of a triangular matrix.*

Show that the eigenvalues of an upper-triangular matrix are exactly its diagonal entries.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The determinant of an upper-triangular matrix is the product of its diagonal entries. Apply this to $\lambda I - A$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $A = (a_{ij})$ be upper-triangular, so $a_{ij} = 0$ for $i > j$. Then $\lambda I - A$ is also upper-triangular, with diagonal entries $\lambda - a_{ii}$. The determinant of a triangular matrix is the product of its diagonal entries, so
$$
\chi_A(\lambda) = \det(\lambda I - A) = \prod_{i=1}^n (\lambda - a_{ii}).
$$
The roots of $\chi_A$ are the eigenvalues, and they are precisely $a_{11}, \ldots, a_{nn}$ (with algebraic multiplicity equal to the number of times each value appears on the diagonal). The same argument applies to lower-triangular matrices.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).** *Diagonalise a $2 \times 2$ matrix.*

Diagonalise
$$
A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}:
$$
find an invertible $P$ and diagonal $D$ with $A = P D P^{-1}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Find the eigenvalues from $\det(\lambda I - A) = 0$, then solve $(A - \lambda I)v = 0$ for each eigenvector. The columns of $P$ are the eigenvectors; $D$ holds the eigenvalues.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Characteristic polynomial: $\chi_A(\lambda) = (\lambda - 4)(\lambda - 3) - 2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = (\lambda - 5)(\lambda - 2)$. Eigenvalues $\lambda = 5, 2$.

For $\lambda = 5$: $(A - 5I) = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$, kernel spanned by $v_1 = (1, 1)$.

For $\lambda = 2$: $(A - 2I) = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, kernel spanned by $v_2 = (1, -2)$.

Then
$$
P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad D = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad A = P D P^{-1}.
$$
Check: $\det P = -3 \neq 0$, so $P$ is invertible and the diagonalisation is valid. Two distinct eigenvalues in a $2$-dimensional space always force diagonalisability.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).** *Rank of a product.*

For linear maps $S: U \to V$ and $T: V \to W$ between finite-dimensional spaces, prove $\operatorname{rank}(T \circ S) \leq \min(\operatorname{rank} S, \operatorname{rank} T)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\operatorname{im}(T \circ S) \subseteq \operatorname{im} T$ gives one bound. For the other, note $T \circ S = T|_{\operatorname{im} S} \circ S$ and rank cannot exceed the dimension of the domain of the second factor.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

First bound: every vector $(T \circ S)(u) = T(S(u))$ lies in $\operatorname{im} T$, so $\operatorname{im}(T \circ S) \subseteq \operatorname{im} T$. Taking dimensions, $\operatorname{rank}(T \circ S) \leq \operatorname{rank} T$.

Second bound: $\operatorname{im}(T \circ S) = T(\operatorname{im} S)$, the image of the subspace $\operatorname{im} S$ under $T$. The restriction $T|_{\operatorname{im} S}: \operatorname{im} S \to W$ is linear, and the dimension of the image of any linear map is at most the dimension of its domain. So $\operatorname{rank}(T \circ S) = \dim T(\operatorname{im} S) \leq \dim(\operatorname{im} S) = \operatorname{rank} S$.

Combining, $\operatorname{rank}(T \circ S) \leq \min(\operatorname{rank} S, \operatorname{rank} T)$. In matrix terms: $\operatorname{rank}(AB) \leq \min(\operatorname{rank} A, \operatorname{rank} B)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).** *Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are independent.*

Let $T: V \to V$ be linear with eigenvectors $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ for pairwise distinct eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k$. Prove $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ are linearly independent.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Induct on $k$. Apply $T$ to a dependence relation, and also multiply the relation by $\lambda_k$; subtract to eliminate $v_k$ and invoke the inductive hypothesis.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Induct on $k$. For $k = 1$, a single eigenvector is nonzero by definition, hence independent.

Assume the result for $k - 1$. Suppose $\sum_{i=1}^k c_i v_i = 0$. Apply $T$: since $T v_i = \lambda_i v_i$, we get $\sum_{i=1}^k c_i \lambda_i v_i = 0$. Multiply the original relation by $\lambda_k$: $\sum_{i=1}^k c_i \lambda_k v_i = 0$. Subtract:
$$
\sum_{i=1}^{k-1} c_i (\lambda_i - \lambda_k) v_i = 0
$$
(the $i = k$ term cancels). By the inductive hypothesis, $v_1, \ldots, v_{k-1}$ are independent, so each coefficient $c_i (\lambda_i - \lambda_k) = 0$. Since the eigenvalues are distinct, $\lambda_i - \lambda_k \neq 0$ for $i < k$, forcing $c_i = 0$ for $i = 1, \ldots, k-1$. The original relation collapses to $c_k v_k = 0$, and $v_k \neq 0$ gives $c_k = 0$. So all coefficients vanish.

Corollary: a map with $n = \dim V$ distinct eigenvalues is diagonalisable.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, numeric).** *Gram-Schmidt in $\mathbb{R}^3$.*

Apply Gram-Schmidt to $a_1 = (1, 1, 0)$, $a_2 = (1, 0, 1)$, $a_3 = (0, 1, 1)$ with the standard inner product to produce an orthonormal basis.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set $u_1 = a_1$, then subtract the projection of $a_2$ onto $u_1$, then subtract the projections of $a_3$ onto $u_1$ and $u_2$. Normalise at the end.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$u_1 = a_1 = (1, 1, 0)$, $\|u_1\|^2 = 2$.

$u_2 = a_2 - \frac{\langle a_2, u_1 \rangle}{\langle u_1, u_1 \rangle} u_1 = (1, 0, 1) - \frac{1}{2}(1, 1, 0) = (\tfrac{1}{2}, -\tfrac{1}{2}, 1)$, with $\|u_2\|^2 = \tfrac{1}{4} + \tfrac{1}{4} + 1 = \tfrac{3}{2}$.

$u_3 = a_3 - \frac{\langle a_3, u_1\rangle}{\|u_1\|^2} u_1 - \frac{\langle a_3, u_2\rangle}{\|u_2\|^2} u_2$. Here $\langle a_3, u_1\rangle = 1$ and $\langle a_3, u_2\rangle = -\tfrac{1}{2} + 1 = \tfrac{1}{2}$, so
$$
u_3 = (0,1,1) - \tfrac{1}{2}(1,1,0) - \frac{1/2}{3/2}(\tfrac12,-\tfrac12,1) = (0,1,1) - (\tfrac12,\tfrac12,0) - (\tfrac16,-\tfrac16,\tfrac13) = (-\tfrac{2}{3}, \tfrac{2}{3}, \tfrac{2}{3}).
$$
Normalising: $e_1 = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(1,1,0)$, $e_2 = \sqrt{\tfrac{2}{3}}(\tfrac12,-\tfrac12,1) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt6}(1,-1,2)$, $e_3 = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt3}(-1,1,1)$. One checks $\langle e_i, e_j\rangle = \delta_{ij}$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Vandermonde determinant.*

Prove that for scalars $x_1, \ldots, x_n$,
$$
\det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & x_1 & x_1^2 & \cdots & x_1^{n-1} \\ 1 & x_2 & x_2^2 & \cdots & x_2^{n-1} \\ \vdots & & & & \vdots \\ 1 & x_n & x_n^2 & \cdots & x_n^{n-1} \end{pmatrix} = \prod_{1 \leq i < j \leq n} (x_j - x_i).
$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Induct on $n$. Use column operations $C_k \mapsto C_k - x_1 C_{k-1}$ (from the right) to introduce a row of zeros except the first entry, then factor $(x_i - x_1)$ from each remaining row.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $V_n$ be the determinant. For $n = 1$, $V_1 = 1$ (empty product). For $n = 2$, $V_2 = x_2 - x_1$, matching.

Inductive step: perform the column operations $C_k \mapsto C_k - x_1 C_{k-1}$ for $k = n, n-1, \ldots, 2$ (in that order). The first row becomes $(1, 0, 0, \ldots, 0)$. Row $i$ becomes $(1,\, x_i - x_1,\, x_i^2 - x_1 x_i,\, \ldots) = (1,\, x_i - x_1,\, x_i(x_i - x_1),\, \ldots,\, x_i^{n-2}(x_i - x_1))$. These operations leave the determinant unchanged.

Expand along the first row (only the $(1,1)$ entry survives): $V_n$ equals the $(n-1) \times (n-1)$ determinant of the matrix with rows indexed $i = 2, \ldots, n$ and entries $x_i^{k}(x_i - x_1)$ for $k = 0, \ldots, n-2$. Factor $(x_i - x_1)$ out of row $i$:
$$
V_n = \prod_{i=2}^n (x_i - x_1) \cdot \det(x_i^{\,k})_{i=2,\ldots,n;\, k=0,\ldots,n-2} = \prod_{i=2}^n (x_i - x_1) \cdot V_{n-1}(x_2, \ldots, x_n).
$$
By the inductive hypothesis $V_{n-1} = \prod_{2 \leq i < j \leq n}(x_j - x_i)$. Combining gives $V_n = \prod_{1 \leq i < j \leq n}(x_j - x_i)$. The determinant is nonzero iff the $x_i$ are distinct — the basis for Lagrange interpolation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *Symmetric matrices have real eigenvalues.*

Let $A$ be a real symmetric $n \times n$ matrix. Prove every eigenvalue of $A$ is real, and that eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Work over $\mathbb{C}$ with the Hermitian inner product. Compare $\langle A v, v\rangle$ and $\langle v, A v\rangle$ using $A = A^T$ real. For orthogonality, compute $\langle A v_1, v_2\rangle$ two ways.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

*Real eigenvalues.* Let $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$ be an eigenvalue with eigenvector $v \in \mathbb{C}^n \setminus \{0\}$, so $Av = \lambda v$. Use the Hermitian inner product $\langle x, y\rangle = \sum \overline{x_i} y_i$. Then
$$
\lambda \langle v, v\rangle = \langle v, Av\rangle = \langle A^* v, v\rangle = \langle A^T v, v \rangle^{*}\!\!,
$$
and since $A$ is real symmetric, $A^* = \overline{A}^T = A$. Thus $\langle v, Av\rangle = \langle Av, v\rangle = \overline{\lambda}\langle v, v\rangle$. Equating, $\lambda \langle v,v\rangle = \overline{\lambda}\langle v,v\rangle$, and $\langle v,v\rangle > 0$ forces $\lambda = \overline{\lambda}$, so $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$.

*Orthogonality.* Let $Av_1 = \lambda_1 v_1$, $Av_2 = \lambda_2 v_2$ with $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$ (both real). Then
$$
\lambda_1 \langle v_1, v_2\rangle = \langle Av_1, v_2\rangle = \langle v_1, A v_2\rangle = \lambda_2 \langle v_1, v_2\rangle,
$$
using $A = A^T$ to move $A$ across the real inner product. Hence $(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2)\langle v_1, v_2\rangle = 0$, and $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$ gives $\langle v_1, v_2\rangle = 0$. This is the heart of the spectral theorem (Apostol Ch. 5): a real symmetric matrix has an orthonormal eigenbasis.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, proof).** *Cayley-Hamilton for a $2 \times 2$ matrix, and the trace-determinant form.*

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix $A$, prove the Cayley-Hamilton theorem $A^2 - (\operatorname{tr} A) A + (\det A) I = 0$ by direct computation, and use it to express $A^{-1}$ in terms of $A$ when $A$ is invertible.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The characteristic polynomial of a $2\times 2$ matrix is $\lambda^2 - (\operatorname{tr} A)\lambda + \det A$. Substitute $A$ for $\lambda$ and verify entrywise. For the inverse, multiply the identity by $A^{-1}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d\end{pmatrix}$, so $\operatorname{tr} A = a + d$, $\det A = ad - bc$. Compute
$$
A^2 = \begin{pmatrix} a^2 + bc & ab + bd \\ ca + dc & cb + d^2\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Then
$$
A^2 - (a+d)A + (ad - bc)I = \begin{pmatrix} a^2 + bc - (a+d)a + ad - bc & ab + bd - (a+d)b \\ ca + dc - (a+d)c & cb + d^2 - (a+d)d + ad - bc\end{pmatrix}.
$$
The $(1,1)$ entry is $a^2 + bc - a^2 - da + ad - bc = 0$; the $(1,2)$ entry is $ab + bd - ab - db = 0$; similarly $(2,1)$ and $(2,2)$ vanish. So $A^2 - (\operatorname{tr} A)A + (\det A)I = 0$.

If $A$ is invertible ($\det A \neq 0$), multiply by $A^{-1}$: $A - (\operatorname{tr} A)I + (\det A)A^{-1} = 0$, so
$$
A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A}\big((\operatorname{tr} A)I - A\big) = \frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a\end{pmatrix},
$$
recovering the standard $2 \times 2$ inverse formula from Cayley-Hamilton (Apostol Ch. 4 §4.16).

</details>

</aside>
