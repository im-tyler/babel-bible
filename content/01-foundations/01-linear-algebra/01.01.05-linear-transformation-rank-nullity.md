## Intuition [Beginner]

A linear transformation is a function between vector spaces that respects the two operations the spaces care about: adding two arrows and stretching one. Pick two arrows, add them, then apply the function — the result is the same as applying the function to each arrow first and adding afterwards. Stretch an arrow, apply the function — the result is the same as applying the function and then stretching by the same factor.

Two pieces of data determine the shape of a linear transformation. The kernel collects the arrows that get squashed to the origin: the function lands them all on top of zero. The image collects the arrows that the function actually reaches: it is the patch of the codomain the function paints over. Both pieces are themselves vector spaces.

The rank-nullity theorem balances a budget. Every dimension in the source space goes either into the kernel or into the image. If the source has 3 dimensions and the kernel grabs 1, the image gets the other 2.

## Visual [Beginner]

The left panel is a three-dimensional source space with three arrows: two that the linear transformation will keep, and one (along a dashed line) that the transformation will squash to the origin. The right panel is the codomain. The two surviving arrows reach a tilted plane through the origin — the image. The squashed arrow lands at the origin.

![A three-dimensional source space on the left with three basis arrows and a dashed kernel line; on the right, a tilted plane through the origin in the codomain spanned by the two arrows that survive the linear map, with the third arrow collapsed to the origin.](/img/linear-transformation-rank-nullity-placeholder.svg)

The dimensions on the left side add up. One dimension goes into the kernel and two dimensions go into the image: 1 plus 2 equals the source dimension 3.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Work in three-dimensional coordinate space. Define a function $T$ that sends $(x,y,z)$ to $(x+y,y+z)$.

Check linearity. For two inputs $(x_1,y_1,z_1)$ and $(x_2,y_2,z_2)$, the function on the sum is

$$
T(x_1+x_2,y_1+y_2,z_1+z_2)=((x_1+x_2)+(y_1+y_2),(y_1+y_2)+(z_1+z_2)),
$$

which equals $T(x_1,y_1,z_1)+T(x_2,y_2,z_2)$. Stretching by a scalar $c$ behaves the same way: $T(cx,cy,cz)=(cx+cy,cy+cz)=cT(x,y,z)$.

Find the kernel. The output is the origin when $x+y=0$ and $y+z=0$. Solving gives $x=-y$ and $z=-y$, so the kernel is the line of points of the form $y(-1,1,-1)$ as $y$ ranges over the reals. The kernel has dimension 1.

Find the image. Pick coefficients $a$ and $b$ in the codomain. Setting $T(a,0,b-a)=(a+0,0+(b-a))=(a,b-a)$ shows the function reaches every output of the form $(a,b-a)$. Reaching every $(a,b)$ instead asks for $T(a,b,0)=(a+b,b)$, and choosing instead $a$ and $b$ freely in the input shows every output is of the form $(\text{anything},\text{anything})$. The image is the entire two-dimensional codomain. The image has dimension 2.

Add the dimensions: 1 plus 2 equals 3. The source has dimension 3. The budget balances.

What this tells us: the kernel records what the function loses, the image records what the function reaches, and their dimensions sum to the source dimension every time.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $V$ and $W$ be vector spaces over a field $K$.

A **linear transformation** from $V$ to $W$ is a function $T:V\to W$ that satisfies the two preservation conditions

$$
T(u+v)=T(u)+T(v),\qquad T(cv)=cT(v),
$$

for all $u,v\in V$ and $c\in K$. The two conditions combine into the single condition $T(au+bv)=aT(u)+bT(v)$ for all $u,v\in V$ and $a,b\in K$. A linear transformation sends $0_V$ to $0_W$ because $T(0)=T(0+0)=T(0)+T(0)$ forces $T(0)=0$. Synonyms: *linear map*, *vector-space homomorphism*. The set of linear transformations $V\to W$ is itself a vector space over $K$, written $\mathcal L(V,W)$ or $\mathrm{Hom}_K(V,W)$ [ref: quantum-well Linear map.md].

The **kernel** of $T$ is the subset

$$
\ker T=\{v\in V:T(v)=0_W\}\subseteq V.
$$

It is a subspace of $V$: it contains $0_V$, and the preservation conditions show $au+bv\in\ker T$ whenever $u,v\in\ker T$ and $a,b\in K$ [ref: quantum-well Null space.md].

The **image** of $T$ is the subset

$$
\mathrm{im}\,T=\{T(v):v\in V\}\subseteq W.
$$

It is a subspace of $W$: it contains $T(0_V)=0_W$, and $aT(u)+bT(v)=T(au+bv)$ shows the image is closed under linear combinations.

The **rank** of $T$ is $\dim(\mathrm{im}\,T)$ and the **nullity** of $T$ is $\dim(\ker T)$ [ref: quantum-well Rank of a matrix.md]. Both are non-negative integers when $V$ is finite-dimensional, in which case the image is a subspace of $W$ with dimension at most $\dim V$.

Examples. The differentiation operator $D:K[x]_{\le n}\to K[x]_{\le n-1}$ on polynomials of degree at most $n$ is a linear transformation; its kernel is the constant polynomials (dimension 1), its image is all of $K[x]_{\le n-1}$ (dimension $n$), and the source dimension is $n+1$. The projection $\pi:K^3\to K^2$ given by $\pi(x,y,z)=(x,y)$ has kernel the $z$-axis (dimension 1) and image the entire codomain (dimension 2). The zero map $0:V\to W$ has kernel $V$ and image $\{0\}$. The identity map $\mathrm{id}:V\to V$ has kernel $\{0\}$ and image $V$.

Non-examples. The function $T(x,y)=(x+1,y)$ is not a linear transformation because it sends the origin to a non-zero point. The function $T(x,y)=(x^2,y)$ fails to preserve scalar multiplication because $T(cx,cy)=(c^2x^2,cy)$ in general differs from $cT(x,y)=(cx^2,cy)$. The function $T(x,y)=(|x|,|y|)$ fails additivity, since $T(-1,0)+T(1,0)=(1,0)+(1,0)=(2,0)$ but $T(-1,0)+T(1,0)\ne T(0,0)=(0,0)$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- A subset that contains $0$ and is closed under linear combinations is a subspace; however, the image of an arbitrary subset $S\subseteq V$ under $T$ need not equal $T(\mathrm{span}\,S)$ unless $S$ is itself a subspace.
- The kernel can be the whole source even when $V\ne 0$: the zero map has $\ker T=V$.
- The image can be the whole codomain even when $T$ is not injective: a projection $\pi:K^3\to K^2$ is surjective with non-zero kernel.
- Rank-nullity uses $\dim V$ on the left, not $\dim W$. Increasing the codomain does not change $\ker T$ or $\dim(\mathrm{im}\,T)$, so the identity is robust under enlargement of $W$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (rank-nullity).** Let $T:V\to W$ be a linear transformation between vector spaces over $K$, with $V$ finite-dimensional. Then

$$
\dim V=\dim(\ker T)+\dim(\mathrm{im}\,T).
$$

[ref: textbooks-extra Calculus Vol.2 - Multi-Variable Calculus and Linear Algebra with Applications (Tom Apostol).pdf]

**Proof.** Let $k=\dim(\ker T)$ and pick a basis $\{u_1,\ldots,u_k\}$ of $\ker T$. The kernel is a subspace of the finite-dimensional space $V$, so $k\le\dim V$ by the dimension comparison from [01.01.04]. Extend $\{u_1,\ldots,u_k\}$ to a basis $\{u_1,\ldots,u_k,v_1,\ldots,v_r\}$ of $V$, again by [01.01.04]. The number $r$ is then $\dim V-k$. The plan is to show $\{T(v_1),\ldots,T(v_r)\}$ is a basis of $\mathrm{im}\,T$, from which $\dim(\mathrm{im}\,T)=r$ and the identity $\dim V=k+r$ rearranges to the theorem.

Spanning. Let $w\in\mathrm{im}\,T$, so $w=T(v)$ for some $v\in V$. Expand $v$ in the basis of $V$:

$$
v=a_1u_1+\cdots+a_ku_k+b_1v_1+\cdots+b_rv_r,
$$

with $a_i,b_j\in K$. Apply $T$:

$$
w=T(v)=a_1T(u_1)+\cdots+a_kT(u_k)+b_1T(v_1)+\cdots+b_rT(v_r).
$$

Each $T(u_i)=0$ because $u_i\in\ker T$, so

$$
w=b_1T(v_1)+\cdots+b_rT(v_r),
$$

a linear combination of $T(v_1),\ldots,T(v_r)$. Hence $\{T(v_1),\ldots,T(v_r)\}$ spans $\mathrm{im}\,T$.

Linear independence. Suppose

$$
c_1T(v_1)+\cdots+c_rT(v_r)=0_W
$$

for some $c_1,\ldots,c_r\in K$. Pull the linear combination inside $T$ via linearity:

$$
T(c_1v_1+\cdots+c_rv_r)=0_W.
$$

The vector $c_1v_1+\cdots+c_rv_r$ therefore lies in $\ker T$, and so it is a linear combination of the basis $\{u_1,\ldots,u_k\}$ of the kernel:

$$
c_1v_1+\cdots+c_rv_r=d_1u_1+\cdots+d_ku_k,
$$

for some $d_1,\ldots,d_k\in K$. Rearranging,

$$
-d_1u_1-\cdots-d_ku_k+c_1v_1+\cdots+c_rv_r=0_V.
$$

This is a linear-dependence relation among the basis $\{u_1,\ldots,u_k,v_1,\ldots,v_r\}$ of $V$. Linear independence of that basis forces every coefficient to vanish, hence in particular $c_1=\cdots=c_r=0$. The list $\{T(v_1),\ldots,T(v_r)\}$ is therefore linearly independent in $W$.

Combining the two parts, $\{T(v_1),\ldots,T(v_r)\}$ is a basis of $\mathrm{im}\,T$, so $\dim(\mathrm{im}\,T)=r$. Since $\dim V=k+r$ by construction of the basis of $V$, the identity $\dim V=k+r=\dim(\ker T)+\dim(\mathrm{im}\,T)$ follows. $\square$

**Corollary (injectivity / surjectivity in finite dimensions).** Let $T:V\to W$ be linear with $\dim V=\dim W=n$ finite. Then $T$ is injective if and only if $T$ is surjective if and only if $T$ is an isomorphism.

**Proof.** Injectivity is equivalent to $\ker T=0$, hence to $\dim(\ker T)=0$. Surjectivity is equivalent to $\mathrm{im}\,T=W$, hence to $\dim(\mathrm{im}\,T)=n$. Rank-nullity reads $n=\dim(\ker T)+\dim(\mathrm{im}\,T)$, so $\dim(\ker T)=0$ if and only if $\dim(\mathrm{im}\,T)=n$. Each of those is in turn equivalent to $T$ being a bijection (a linear bijection is automatically a linear isomorphism, since the inverse of a linear bijection is linear). $\square$

**Bridge.** Rank-nullity is the dimension-counting half of the *first isomorphism theorem* for vector spaces: the same kernel-image data, when read as a quotient instead of a sum, yields the canonical isomorphism $V/\ker T\cong\mathrm{im}\,T$. The first isomorphism theorem reappears in the tensor algebra strand at [03.01.04], where the universal property of the tensor product packages bilinear maps as linear maps modulo a kernel. The same identity feeds the *Fredholm alternative* in functional analysis (the bounded-operator extension is reached in 02.11.* once Banach-space machinery is in place), where dimension counting is replaced by index counting for compact perturbations of the identity. Putting these together, dimension is a conserved quantity through any linear map: the codomain is not where the action is; what matters is what the map kills and what the map hits, and the source dimension is split exactly between those two.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Find a basis of the kernel and a basis of the image for the linear transformation $T:\mathbb R^3\to\mathbb R^3$ defined by $T(x,y,z)=(x+y+z,2x+2y+2z,3x+3y+3z)$, and verify rank-nullity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Notice that the three output coordinates are scalar multiples of $x+y+z$.

</details>

Each output coordinate is a multiple of $x+y+z$, so the image lies in the line spanned by $(1,2,3)$ in $\mathbb R^3$. Setting $x=1,y=z=0$ gives $T=(1,2,3)$, so the image equals that line and $\{(1,2,3)\}$ is a basis of $\mathrm{im}\,T$. The kernel is the set of $(x,y,z)$ with $x+y+z=0$. Solving gives the basis $\{(-1,1,0),(-1,0,1)\}$ of $\ker T$. Verification: $\dim(\ker T)+\dim(\mathrm{im}\,T)=2+1=3=\dim\mathbb R^3$.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $D:\mathbb R[x]_{\le 3}\to\mathbb R[x]_{\le 2}$ be the differentiation map on polynomials of degree at most 3. Find $\ker D$, $\mathrm{im}\,D$, and verify rank-nullity.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiation kills constants and lowers degree by exactly one whenever the degree is positive.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The kernel consists of constant polynomials, with basis $\{1\}$ and dimension 1. The image is all of $\mathbb R[x]_{\le 2}$: the polynomial $a_0+a_1x+a_2x^2$ is the derivative of $a_0x+\tfrac{a_1}{2}x^2+\tfrac{a_2}{3}x^3$, so the image is surjective onto the codomain. Its basis is $\{1,x,x^2\}$ and dimension 3. Rank-nullity check: $\dim\mathbb R[x]_{\le 3}=4=1+3=\dim(\ker D)+\dim(\mathrm{im}\,D)$.

</details>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Let $T:V\to W$ be a linear transformation between finite-dimensional vector spaces. Prove that $T$ is injective if and only if $\ker T=\{0_V\}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use linearity to relate the equation $T(u)=T(v)$ to the kernel.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $T$ is injective. If $v\in\ker T$, then $T(v)=0_W=T(0_V)$, so injectivity forces $v=0_V$. Hence $\ker T=\{0_V\}$. Conversely, suppose $\ker T=\{0_V\}$. If $T(u)=T(v)$, then $T(u-v)=T(u)-T(v)=0_W$, so $u-v\in\ker T=\{0_V\}$, hence $u=v$. Therefore $T$ is injective.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).**

Prove that the composition of two linear transformations is a linear transformation, and show that $\ker(S\circ T)\supseteq\ker T$ and $\mathrm{im}(S\circ T)\subseteq\mathrm{im}\,S$ whenever $T:U\to V$ and $S:V\to W$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Verify the two preservation conditions for the composition, then track elements through the kernels and images.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $u_1,u_2\in U$ and $a,b\in K$, $(S\circ T)(au_1+bu_2)=S(T(au_1+bu_2))=S(aT(u_1)+bT(u_2))=aS(T(u_1))+bS(T(u_2))=a(S\circ T)(u_1)+b(S\circ T)(u_2)$, so $S\circ T$ is linear. If $u\in\ker T$, then $T(u)=0_V$, so $(S\circ T)(u)=S(0_V)=0_W$, giving $u\in\ker(S\circ T)$. If $w\in\mathrm{im}(S\circ T)$, then $w=S(T(u))$ for some $u\in U$, so $w=S(v)$ where $v=T(u)\in V$, giving $w\in\mathrm{im}\,S$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

Let $T:V\to V$ be a linear operator on a finite-dimensional vector space. Prove that $T$ is injective if and only if $T$ is surjective if and only if $T$ is an isomorphism.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply rank-nullity with $W=V$ and use the dimension counts to chain the equivalences.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $n=\dim V$. Rank-nullity gives $n=\dim(\ker T)+\dim(\mathrm{im}\,T)$. Injectivity is equivalent to $\dim(\ker T)=0$ by the previous exercise; surjectivity is equivalent to $\dim(\mathrm{im}\,T)=n$ because the image is a subspace of $V$ and has the maximum possible dimension. The two conditions are equivalent because their sum is constant. A linear bijection has a linear inverse: if $T$ is bijective, define $T^{-1}$ pointwise; for $w_1,w_2\in V$ and $a,b\in K$, set $v_i=T^{-1}(w_i)$ and observe $T(av_1+bv_2)=aw_1+bw_2$, so $T^{-1}(aw_1+bw_2)=av_1+bv_2=aT^{-1}(w_1)+bT^{-1}(w_2)$. Hence $T$ is an isomorphism whenever it is injective or surjective.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).**

Let $T:V\to W$ be a linear transformation with $V$ finite-dimensional. Prove that $T$ induces an isomorphism

$$
\bar T:V/\ker T\xrightarrow{\;\cong\;}\mathrm{im}\,T,\qquad \bar T(v+\ker T)=T(v).
$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show $\bar T$ is well-defined, linear, injective, and surjective.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Well-defined: if $v+\ker T=v'+\ker T$, then $v-v'\in\ker T$, so $T(v-v')=0$ and $T(v)=T(v')$. Linear: $\bar T(a(v_1+\ker T)+b(v_2+\ker T))=\bar T((av_1+bv_2)+\ker T)=T(av_1+bv_2)=aT(v_1)+bT(v_2)=a\bar T(v_1+\ker T)+b\bar T(v_2+\ker T)$. Injective: if $\bar T(v+\ker T)=0$, then $T(v)=0$, so $v\in\ker T$ and $v+\ker T=0+\ker T$. Surjective: every element of $\mathrm{im}\,T$ has the form $T(v)=\bar T(v+\ker T)$. The map is therefore an isomorphism. The dimension count $\dim(V/\ker T)=\dim V-\dim(\ker T)$ from [01.01.04] together with the isomorphism recovers rank-nullity.

</details>

</aside>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

Mathlib models a linear transformation as `LinearMap`, the kernel as `LinearMap.ker`, the image as `LinearMap.range`, and the rank-nullity identity as `LinearMap.finrank_range_add_finrank_ker`. The companion file `Codex.Foundations.LinearAlgebra.RankNullity` records the statements used above and packages the rank-nullity identity in the Codex namespace.

```lean
import Mathlib.LinearAlgebra.Basis
import Mathlib.LinearAlgebra.Dimension.Basic
import Mathlib.LinearAlgebra.FiniteDimensional

variable {K V W : Type*} [Field K]
  [AddCommGroup V] [Module K V] [AddCommGroup W] [Module K W]

abbrev LinearTransformation := V →ₗ[K] W
abbrev kernel (T : V →ₗ[K] W) : Submodule K V := LinearMap.ker T
abbrev image  (T : V →ₗ[K] W) : Submodule K W := LinearMap.range T

theorem rank_nullity
    (T : V →ₗ[K] W) [FiniteDimensional K V] :
    Module.finrank K V
      = Module.finrank K (LinearMap.ker T)
      + Module.finrank K (LinearMap.range T) := by
  -- follows from `LinearMap.finrank_range_add_finrank_ker`
```

## Advanced results [Master]

**First isomorphism theorem.** For a linear transformation $T:V\to W$, the assignment $v+\ker T\mapsto T(v)$ is a well-defined linear isomorphism

$$
V/\ker T\xrightarrow{\;\cong\;}\mathrm{im}\,T.
$$

The construction is functorial: a commutative square of linear maps induces a commutative square of induced maps on quotients and images. In categorical language, $K\text{-Vect}$ is an abelian category in which every morphism factors uniquely as an epimorphism (the canonical projection $V\twoheadrightarrow V/\ker T$) followed by a monomorphism (the inclusion $\mathrm{im}\,T\hookrightarrow W$), with the middle term $V/\ker T\cong\mathrm{im}\,T$ recording the canonical image.

**Splitting in $K\text{-Vect}$.** Every short exact sequence of $K$-vector spaces

$$
0\longrightarrow A\xrightarrow{\;f\;}B\xrightarrow{\;g\;}C\longrightarrow 0
$$

splits. Concretely, picking a basis of $C$ and lifting each basis vector along $g$ yields a section $s:C\to B$ with $g\circ s=\mathrm{id}_C$, hence $B\cong f(A)\oplus s(C)\cong A\oplus C$. The corresponding statement in the category of finitely generated abelian groups is false: the sequence $0\to\mathbb Z\xrightarrow{2}\mathbb Z\to\mathbb Z/2\to 0$ has no section, since $\mathbb Z/2$ has no copy inside $\mathbb Z$. Splitting is therefore a feature of the field-coefficient case, not a feature of homological algebra in general.

**Module-theoretic generalisation.** Over a principal ideal domain $R$, a linear map between free modules of finite rank still satisfies a rank identity: $\mathrm{rank}\,F=\mathrm{rank}(\ker T)+\mathrm{rank}(\mathrm{im}\,T)$ when both sides are finite. Over an arbitrary commutative ring, the equality fails, since rank fragments into multiple invariants — minimal-generator count, Krull dimension, projective dimension. Even the basic rank-nullity statement requires the source module to be free; the kernel of a map between free modules need not itself be free over a non-PID.

**Banach-space generalisation: Fredholm operators and the index.** For a bounded linear operator $T:V\to W$ between Banach spaces with $\ker T$ and $\mathrm{coker}\,T=W/\overline{\mathrm{im}\,T}$ both finite-dimensional and $\mathrm{im}\,T$ closed, the **Fredholm index** is

$$
\mathrm{ind}(T)=\dim(\ker T)-\dim(\mathrm{coker}\,T).
$$

This integer is invariant under norm-continuous deformation of $T$ within the class of Fredholm operators, and additive under composition: $\mathrm{ind}(S\circ T)=\mathrm{ind}(S)+\mathrm{ind}(T)$. The finite-dimensional rank-nullity identity is the special case in which both $V$ and $W$ are finite-dimensional, where $\mathrm{ind}(T)=\dim V-\dim W$ regardless of $T$. The Atiyah-Singer index theorem (1968) computes $\mathrm{ind}(T)$ for an elliptic operator on a closed manifold from purely topological data of the symbol [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah, M. F. & Singer, I. M. — The index of elliptic operators I].

**Cohomological generalisation: Euler characteristic of a complex.** For a finite-dimensional chain complex of $K$-vector spaces $\cdots\to C^i\xrightarrow{\;d^i\;}C^{i+1}\to\cdots$ with $d^{i+1}\circ d^i=0$, repeated application of rank-nullity gives the identity

$$
\sum_i(-1)^i\dim C^i=\sum_i(-1)^i\dim H^i,
$$

where $H^i=\ker d^i/\mathrm{im}\,d^{i-1}$. The alternating sum is the Euler characteristic, and the identity is the homological-algebra incarnation of dimension as a conserved quantity.

**Synthesis.** Three threads weave together. The first is *dimension as conservation*: rank-nullity transports the source dimension across the map, splitting it between the lost part (kernel) and the reached part (image). The second is *coordinate-free factorisation*: every linear map factors canonically as a quotient followed by an inclusion, and the middle object is intrinsic to the map. The third is *invariance under deformation*: in the Banach-space setting, $\dim\ker$ and $\dim\mathrm{coker}$ each fluctuate under perturbation but their difference is rigid. Putting these together, the central insight is that the codomain is not where the action is. What matters is what the map kills and what the map hits, and the source dimension is split exactly between those two — finite-dimensionally by the integer identity, infinite-dimensionally by the index, cohomologically by the Euler characteristic.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Rank-nullity, redone in the universal-property style.** Let $T:V\to W$ be a linear transformation with $V$ finite-dimensional. The exact sequence

$$
0\longrightarrow\ker T\xrightarrow{\iota}V\xrightarrow{T}\mathrm{im}\,T\longrightarrow 0,
$$

where $\iota$ is the inclusion, is a short exact sequence of $K$-vector spaces. The Splitting Lemma in $K\text{-Vect}$ exhibits $V\cong\ker T\oplus\mathrm{im}\,T$ as $K$-vector spaces. Taking dimensions of both sides gives $\dim V=\dim(\ker T)+\dim(\mathrm{im}\,T)$. The basis-extension argument used in the Intermediate proof is the explicit splitting: the extended basis $\{u_1,\ldots,u_k,v_1,\ldots,v_r\}$ realises $V=\ker T\oplus\mathrm{span}\{v_1,\ldots,v_r\}$, and $T$ restricts to an isomorphism between the second summand and $\mathrm{im}\,T$.

**First isomorphism theorem in detail.** The map $\bar T:V/\ker T\to\mathrm{im}\,T$ is well-defined because $T(v_1)=T(v_2)$ when $v_1-v_2\in\ker T$. Linearity carries over from $T$. The map is injective because $\bar T(v+\ker T)=0$ forces $T(v)=0$, hence $v\in\ker T$, hence $v+\ker T=\ker T$. The map is surjective because every element of $\mathrm{im}\,T$ is $T(v)=\bar T(v+\ker T)$ for some $v$. Hence $\bar T$ is an isomorphism. Combining with the dimension formula $\dim(V/\ker T)=\dim V-\dim(\ker T)$ from [01.01.04] recovers rank-nullity from the isomorphism.

**Splitting Lemma in $K\text{-Vect}$.** For a short exact sequence $0\to A\xrightarrow{f}B\xrightarrow{g}C\to 0$ of $K$-vector spaces, pick a basis $\{c_\alpha\}$ of $C$. For each $c_\alpha$, choose a preimage $b_\alpha\in B$ with $g(b_\alpha)=c_\alpha$ (possible because $g$ is surjective). Define $s:C\to B$ on the basis by $s(c_\alpha)=b_\alpha$ and extend linearly. Then $g\circ s=\mathrm{id}_C$ on the basis, hence on all of $C$ by linearity. The pair $(f,s)$ realises $B\cong A\oplus C$: every $b\in B$ decomposes as $b=(b-s(g(b)))+s(g(b))$, with the first term in $\ker g=\mathrm{im}\,f$ and the second in $\mathrm{im}\,s$, and the decomposition is unique because $\mathrm{im}\,f\cap\mathrm{im}\,s=0$ (apply $g$ to both sides). The choice of basis of $C$ uses the existence of a basis for any vector space (Hamel basis in the infinite-dimensional case, by Zorn's lemma; finite basis in the finite-dimensional case).

**Failure of splitting outside $K\text{-Vect}$.** The short exact sequence of abelian groups

$$
0\longrightarrow\mathbb Z\xrightarrow{\;\cdot 2\;}\mathbb Z\xrightarrow{\;\bmod 2\;}\mathbb Z/2\longrightarrow 0
$$

does not split. A section $s:\mathbb Z/2\to\mathbb Z$ would have to send the non-zero element $1+2\mathbb Z$ to some $n\in\mathbb Z$ with $n\bmod 2=1$, while also satisfying $s(0)=0$ and the linearity condition $s(1+1)=s(0)=0$ in $\mathbb Z/2$, hence $2n=0$ in $\mathbb Z$, hence $n=0$. The two conditions are inconsistent. The obstruction is that $\mathbb Z/2$ contains 2-torsion that $\mathbb Z$ cannot absorb. This is the prototype obstruction in the $\mathrm{Ext}$ functor of homological algebra: $\mathrm{Ext}^1_{\mathbb Z}(\mathbb Z/2,\mathbb Z)=\mathbb Z/2$, and the non-zero class records the non-splitting sequence.

**Fredholm-index invariance under compact perturbation.** Stated without proof: if $T:V\to W$ is Fredholm and $K:V\to W$ is compact (norm-limit of finite-rank operators), then $T+K$ is Fredholm and $\mathrm{ind}(T+K)=\mathrm{ind}(T)$. The proof uses the parametrix construction — there exists $S:W\to V$ with $ST=I_V-K_1$ and $TS=I_W-K_2$ for compact $K_1,K_2$ — together with the fact that $I-K$ has finite-dimensional kernel and cokernel for any compact $K$, so its index is zero. Composition and additivity of the index then close the argument [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah, M. F. & Singer, I. M. — The index of elliptic operators I].

**Euler-characteristic identity for chain complexes.** Let $0\to C^0\xrightarrow{d^0}C^1\xrightarrow{d^1}\cdots\xrightarrow{d^{n-1}}C^n\to 0$ be a finite-dimensional chain complex with $d^{i+1}\circ d^i=0$. Define $Z^i=\ker d^i$, $B^i=\mathrm{im}\,d^{i-1}\subseteq Z^i$, and $H^i=Z^i/B^i$. Apply rank-nullity to $d^i:C^i\to C^{i+1}$:

$$
\dim C^i=\dim Z^i+\dim(\mathrm{im}\,d^i)=\dim Z^i+\dim B^{i+1}.
$$

Take the alternating sum $\sum_i(-1)^i\dim C^i=\sum_i(-1)^i\dim Z^i+\sum_i(-1)^i\dim B^{i+1}$. Reindex the second sum by $j=i+1$ to obtain $\sum_j(-1)^{j-1}\dim B^j=-\sum_j(-1)^j\dim B^j$, so

$$
\sum_i(-1)^i\dim C^i=\sum_i(-1)^i(\dim Z^i-\dim B^i)=\sum_i(-1)^i\dim H^i.
$$

The Euler characteristic of the complex equals the Euler characteristic of its cohomology.

## Connections [Master]

- **Subspace, basis, dimension [01.01.04]** — supplies the basis-extension lemma and the dimension formula $\dim(V/\ker T)=\dim V-\dim(\ker T)$ that the rank-nullity proof and the first isomorphism theorem both consume. Without [01.01.04], rank-nullity has no statement; with it, the proof reduces to a one-paragraph basis-tracking argument.

- **Tensor algebra [03.01.04]** — the universal property of the tensor product is, structurally, a rank-nullity statement: bilinear maps $V\times W\to U$ correspond to linear maps $V\otimes W\to U$ via passing through the kernel of the canonical bilinear factorisation. The same first-isomorphism-theorem identification $V/\ker T\cong\mathrm{im}\,T$ underlies the universal property and the dimension count $\dim(V\otimes W)=\dim V\cdot\dim W$ that connects multilinear algebra to the present unit.

- **Vector bundle [03.05.02]** — a morphism of vector bundles $T:E\to F$ over a fixed base $B$ has fibrewise kernels and images, and the rank-nullity identity holds in each fibre. When $T$ has constant rank, the kernel and image are themselves vector subbundles, generalising the linear-algebra picture to a parameterised family. The constant-rank hypothesis is essential: a non-constant-rank morphism has fibrewise kernels of jumping dimension, so the family of kernels is not a subbundle.

- **Differential forms [03.04.01]** — the exterior derivative $d:\Omega^k(M)\to\Omega^{k+1}(M)$ on a manifold $M$ is a linear map between infinite-dimensional vector spaces with $d^2=0$. The cohomology $H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}(M)=\ker d/\mathrm{im}\,d$ from [03.04.06] is the rank-nullity bookkeeping at level $k$; the Betti numbers $b_k=\dim H^k_{\mathrm{dR}}$ are the dimension counts the present unit's framework records.

- **Bilinear and quadratic forms [01.01.15]** — a bilinear form $B:V\times V\to K$ packages into a linear map $V\to V^*$ given by $v\mapsto B(v,\cdot)$. The kernel of that linear map is the radical of the form, and its image lies in $V^*$. Rank-nullity computes $\dim V=\dim(\mathrm{rad}\,B)+\mathrm{rank}\,B$, which is the dimension half of Sylvester's law of inertia.

- **Modular forms on $\mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ [21.04.01]** — the matrix elements $\gamma = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \in \mathrm{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ acting on the upper half-plane via Möbius transformations are special linear transformations of $\mathbb{R}^2$ with integer entries and determinant $1$. The two generators $S = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ and the relation $(ST)^3 = S^2 = -I$ are the linear-transformation backbone on which the entire arithmetic content of the modular group rests.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Hermann Grassmann gave the first systematic algebraic treatment of linear maps between extension systems in *Die lineale Ausdehnungslehre* (1844, Otto Wigand, Leipzig), introducing the dimension-tracking that the modern statement formalises. The vocabulary "rank" entered the literature through James Joseph Sylvester's 1851 paper *On the relation between the minor determinants of linearly equivalent quadratic functions* (Philosophical Magazine), where rank named the size of the largest non-vanishing minor of a matrix; the connection to dimension of the column space crystallised over the next half century. Georg Frobenius gave the modern matrix-rank framework in his 1877 memoir *Über das Pfaffsche Problem* (J. reine angew. Math. 82) [ref: textbooks-extra Calculus Vol.2 - Multi-Variable Calculus and Linear Algebra with Applications (Tom Apostol).pdf].

The rank-nullity theorem in its abstract vector-space form is part of Emmy Noether's homological program of the 1920s and 1930s. Noether's three isomorphism theorems for modules — published in *Ideale und Verbände in den allgemeinen abstrakten Theorien* (lectures at Göttingen, 1927–1928) and codified in van der Waerden's *Moderne Algebra* (1930) — gave the unified statement of which the vector-space rank-nullity is the field-coefficient case. The Atiyah-Singer index theorem (M. F. Atiyah and I. M. Singer, "The index of elliptic operators I", Annals of Mathematics 87 (1968), 484–530) extended the integer rank-nullity to a topological identity for elliptic operators on closed manifolds. Saunders Mac Lane and Garrett Birkhoff's *Algebra* (Macmillan, 1967) presented the unified module-theoretic version that subsumes rank-nullity, the first isomorphism theorem, and the structure theorem for finitely generated modules over a PID under a single categorical heading.
