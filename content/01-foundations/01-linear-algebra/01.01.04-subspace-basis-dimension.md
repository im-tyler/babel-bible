## Intuition [Beginner]

A subspace is a piece of a vector space that is itself closed under the same operations. Pick any two arrows that live inside it and add them: the sum stays inside. Stretch one arrow by any number: the result stays inside. The piece carries its own self-contained algebra.

In the everyday picture of three-dimensional space, the subspaces are the origin alone, every line through the origin, every plane through the origin, and the whole space. A line that misses the origin is not a subspace because adding two of its arrows lands somewhere off the line.

A basis is a smallest list of arrows that can build every other arrow by combining and scaling. Dimension counts how many arrows the basis needs.

## Visual [Beginner]

The blue parallelogram on the left is a plane through the origin sitting inside three-dimensional space. The red and blue arrows span that plane: every point in it is a combination of the two. The right panel shows three arrows that together span the whole surrounding space.

![A tilted plane through the origin with two basis arrows inside it; on the right, three orthogonal basis arrows for the surrounding three-dimensional space.](/img/subspace-basis-dimension-placeholder.svg)

The plane is a 2-dimensional subspace inside the 3-dimensional space. Two arrows are enough to reach every point of the plane. Three arrows are needed to reach every point of the whole space.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Work in the coordinate plane. Take the two arrows $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$.

Combining them with coefficients $3$ and $2$ produces

$$
3(1,0)+2(0,1)=(3,0)+(0,2)=(3,2).
$$

Combining them with coefficients $-1$ and $4$ produces

$$
-1(1,0)+4(0,1)=(-1,0)+(0,4)=(-1,4).
$$

Every point $(a,b)$ in the plane is reached as $a(1,0)+b(0,1)$. The two arrows reach every point and neither one is a multiple of the other. The list $\{(1,0),(0,1)\}$ is a basis of the coordinate plane, and the dimension of the plane is $2$.

What this tells us: a basis is a minimum-size kit that builds every other arrow.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $K$.

A **subspace** of $V$ is a subset $W\subseteq V$ that is non-empty, closed under addition, and closed under scalar multiplication. Equivalently, $W$ is non-empty and contains $au+bv$ whenever $u,v\in W$ and $a,b\in K$. A subspace inherits the vector-space structure of $V$ [ref: quantum-well Proof that the sum of subspaces is also a subspace.md].

A finite list $v_1,\ldots,v_n$ of vectors in $V$ is **linearly independent** when

$$
a_1v_1+\cdots+a_nv_n=0\implies a_1=\cdots=a_n=0,
$$

with $a_1,\ldots,a_n\in K$. The list **spans** $V$, written $V=\mathrm{span}\{v_1,\ldots,v_n\}$, when every vector in $V$ is a linear combination of the listed vectors.

A **basis** of $V$ is a list of vectors that is both linearly independent and spanning. Equivalent phrasings: a basis is a maximal linearly independent list, or a minimal spanning list. The vector space $V$ is **finite-dimensional** when it has a finite basis.

The **dimension** of a finite-dimensional vector space, written $\dim V$, is the number of vectors in any basis. Well-definedness — that every basis has the same number of elements — is the content of the key theorem below [ref: textbooks-extra Calculus Vol.2 - Multi-Variable Calculus and Linear Algebra with Applications (Tom Apostol).pdf].

Examples. The coordinate space $K^n$ has dimension $n$ with the standard basis $e_1,\ldots,e_n$ where $e_i$ has a $1$ in position $i$ and zeroes elsewhere. The space of polynomials of degree at most $n$ has dimension $n+1$ with basis $1,x,x^2,\ldots,x^n$. The zero space $\{0\}$ has dimension $0$ with the empty basis.

Non-examples. The set $\{(x,y)\in\mathbb R^2:x\ge 0\}$ is closed under addition and under non-negative scalar multiplication but not under multiplication by $-1$, so it fails to be a subspace. The set $\{(x,y)\in\mathbb R^2:x+y=1\}$ misses the origin and fails closure under addition. The list $(1,0),(2,0),(0,1)$ in $\mathbb R^2$ spans but is not linearly independent because $(2,0)=2(1,0)$, so it is not a basis.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Steinitz replacement).** Let $V$ be a vector space over $K$. Suppose $\{v_1,\ldots,v_n\}$ spans $V$ and $\{w_1,\ldots,w_m\}$ is linearly independent. Then $m\le n$, and there is a relabelling of the $v_i$ so that $\{w_1,\ldots,w_m,v_{m+1},\ldots,v_n\}$ also spans $V$ [ref: TODO_REF Steinitz, E. — Bedingt konvergente Reihen und konvexe Systeme].

**Proof.** Argue by induction on $m$.

Base case $m=1$. Since $\{w_1\}$ is linearly independent, $w_1\ne 0$. The spanning hypothesis writes $w_1=a_1v_1+\cdots+a_nv_n$ for scalars $a_i\in K$, and at least one $a_i$ is non-zero because $w_1\ne 0$. Relabel so that $a_1\ne 0$. Solving for $v_1$ gives

$$
v_1=a_1^{-1}w_1-a_1^{-1}a_2v_2-\cdots-a_1^{-1}a_nv_n.
$$

Every linear combination of $v_1,\ldots,v_n$ is therefore also a linear combination of $w_1,v_2,\ldots,v_n$. Hence $\{w_1,v_2,\ldots,v_n\}$ spans $V$, and $1\le n$ holds because $V$ contains the non-zero vector $w_1$, so $n\ge 1$.

Inductive step. Assume the result for $m-1$. Apply it to the spanning set $\{v_1,\ldots,v_n\}$ and the linearly independent set $\{w_1,\ldots,w_{m-1}\}$. The conclusion gives $m-1\le n$ and a relabelling so that $\{w_1,\ldots,w_{m-1},v_m,\ldots,v_n\}$ spans $V$.

Express $w_m$ in this spanning set:

$$
w_m=b_1w_1+\cdots+b_{m-1}w_{m-1}+c_mv_m+\cdots+c_nv_n,
$$

with $b_j,c_k\in K$. At least one $c_k$ is non-zero. Otherwise $w_m=b_1w_1+\cdots+b_{m-1}w_{m-1}$, which rearranges to

$$
b_1w_1+\cdots+b_{m-1}w_{m-1}+(-1)w_m=0.
$$

The coefficient $-1$ on $w_m$ is non-zero, contradicting the linear independence of $\{w_1,\ldots,w_m\}$.

Therefore some $c_k\ne 0$. The list $\{v_m,\ldots,v_n\}$ is non-empty, so $n\ge m$. Relabel so that $c_m\ne 0$. Solving for $v_m$ gives

$$
v_m=c_m^{-1}w_m-c_m^{-1}b_1w_1-\cdots-c_m^{-1}b_{m-1}w_{m-1}-c_m^{-1}c_{m+1}v_{m+1}-\cdots-c_m^{-1}c_nv_n.
$$

Every linear combination of $w_1,\ldots,w_{m-1},v_m,\ldots,v_n$ is therefore also a linear combination of $w_1,\ldots,w_m,v_{m+1},\ldots,v_n$. Hence $\{w_1,\ldots,w_m,v_{m+1},\ldots,v_n\}$ spans $V$. The induction is complete. $\square$

**Corollary (well-definedness of dimension).** Any two bases of a finite-dimensional vector space have the same number of elements.

**Proof.** Let $B=\{v_1,\ldots,v_n\}$ and $B'=\{w_1,\ldots,w_m\}$ be bases. Apply the replacement theorem with $B$ as the spanning set and $B'$ as the linearly independent set: $m\le n$. Apply it again with the roles reversed: $n\le m$. Hence $m=n$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The replacement theorem builds toward rank-nullity, where dimension counts solutions and image vectors against each other inside a single linear map. The dimension function appears again in tensor algebra, where $\dim(V\otimes W)=\dim V\cdot\dim W$ packages the multilinear structure into a single integer. The same dimension count organises differential forms, where the rank of $\Lambda^k V^*$ records how many independent $k$-fold wedge products fit inside an $n$-dimensional space. The same invariant feeds vector-bundle theory, where the rank of a bundle is the dimension of every fibre. Putting these together, dimension is the foundational invariant of finite-dimensional linear algebra: it classifies finite-dimensional vector spaces over a fixed field up to isomorphism, and it is the integer the rest of the strand reads off whenever a multilinear, exterior, or fibrewise construction is performed.

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Show that the polynomials $1,x,x^2$ are linearly independent over $\mathbb R$.

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Prove the dimension formula

$$
\dim(W_1+W_2)+\dim(W_1\cap W_2)=\dim W_1+\dim W_2
$$

for finite-dimensional subspaces $W_1,W_2$ of a vector space $V$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Start with a basis of $W_1\cap W_2$ and extend separately to bases of $W_1$ and of $W_2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\{u_1,\ldots,u_k\}$ be a basis of $W_1\cap W_2$. Extend to a basis $\{u_1,\ldots,u_k,v_1,\ldots,v_p\}$ of $W_1$ and to a basis $\{u_1,\ldots,u_k,w_1,\ldots,w_q\}$ of $W_2$. The combined list $\{u_1,\ldots,u_k,v_1,\ldots,v_p,w_1,\ldots,w_q\}$ spans $W_1+W_2$. To see independence, suppose $\sum a_iu_i+\sum b_jv_j+\sum c_lw_l=0$. Then $\sum c_lw_l=-\sum a_iu_i-\sum b_jv_j$ lies in $W_1\cap W_2$, so each $c_l=0$ by independence of the $w_l$ modulo $W_1\cap W_2$. The remaining relation reduces to a relation among the basis of $W_1$, forcing all $a_i=b_j=0$. Therefore $\dim(W_1+W_2)=k+p+q$, while $\dim W_1+\dim W_2=(k+p)+(k+q)$, and $\dim(W_1\cap W_2)=k$. The identity follows.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional vector space and let $W\subseteq V$ be a subspace. Prove that $\dim W\le\dim V$, with equality if and only if $W=V$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the replacement theorem to a basis of $W$ inside a basis of $V$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $\{w_1,\ldots,w_m\}$ be a basis of $W$ and $\{v_1,\ldots,v_n\}$ a basis of $V$. The basis of $W$ is linearly independent in $V$, and the basis of $V$ spans $V$. The replacement theorem gives $m\le n$, so $\dim W\le\dim V$. If $m=n$, then $\{w_1,\ldots,w_m\}$ is a maximal linearly independent set in $V$, hence a basis of $V$, hence spans $V$, so $W=V$. Conversely, if $W=V$ the dimensions agree.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Subspace lattice.** The subspaces of $V$ form a lattice under inclusion, with meet $W_1\cap W_2$ and join $W_1+W_2=\{w_1+w_2:w_1\in W_1,w_2\in W_2\}$. The dimension formula

$$
\dim(W_1+W_2)+\dim(W_1\cap W_2)=\dim W_1+\dim W_2
$$

is the modular identity that characterises this lattice [ref: TODO_REF Hoffman-Kunze — Linear Algebra]. The lattice is modular but generally not distributive: in $\mathbb R^2$, three distinct lines through the origin produce a sublattice on which the distributive law fails.

**Quotient and direct sum.** For a subspace $W\subseteq V$, the quotient space $V/W$ has dimension $\dim V-\dim W$. A choice of complement $W'$ realises the internal direct-sum decomposition $V=W\oplus W'$ and identifies $V/W$ with $W'$. The dimension count $\dim(W\oplus W')=\dim W+\dim W'$ holds by concatenating bases.

**Infinite-dimensional case.** Every vector space has a basis (a Hamel basis), assuming the axiom of choice; existence is proved by Zorn's lemma applied to the partially ordered set of linearly independent subsets [ref: quantum-well Finite dimensional vector spaces.md]. Any two Hamel bases of $V$ have the same cardinality, and that cardinal is $\dim V$. In an infinite-dimensional Banach space, a Hamel basis is uncountable, and the more useful objects are Schauder bases (allowing convergent infinite sums) or, in Hilbert spaces, orthonormal bases. These are categorically different objects: a Schauder basis depends on the topology and on a choice of summation order, while a Hamel basis is purely algebraic.

**Dimension as a categorical invariant.** The category $K\text{-Vect}^{\mathrm{fd}}$ of finite-dimensional vector spaces over $K$ is equivalent to the skeleton consisting of objects $K^n$ for $n\ge 0$, with morphisms $K^m\to K^n$ given by $n\times m$ matrices. Under this equivalence, isomorphism class corresponds to the natural number $\dim$, so $\pi_0$ of the underlying groupoid is $\mathbb N$. The Grothendieck group of the symmetric monoidal category $(K\text{-Vect}^{\mathrm{fd}},\oplus)$ is $K_0(K\text{-Vect}^{\mathrm{fd}})=\mathbb Z$, generated by the class $[K]$, and the dimension homomorphism $\dim:K_0\to\mathbb Z$ is an isomorphism.

**Module-theoretic generalisation.** Over a principal ideal domain, a finitely generated free module has a well-defined rank that plays the role of dimension; the structure theorem decomposes any finitely generated module into a free part and torsion. Over a general commutative ring, dimension fragments into multiple invariants — minimal generator count, Krull dimension, projective dimension — none of which agree in full generality. The clean coincidence available over a field is the algebraic content of "vector space".

**Synthesis.** This construction generalises the pattern fixed in the vector space unit, with the choice of basis selecting a coordinate system inside an otherwise unstructured object. Read in the opposite direction, dimension is the object that records the structure independently of the coordinates: the same invariant survives every change of basis. The replacement-theorem skeleton appears again in matroid theory, where the same exchange axiom abstracts the linear-independence pattern away from vector spaces and into a combinatorial framework. Putting these together, the central insight is that finite-dimensional linear algebra is governed by a single integer, and that integer is the only invariant separating one finite-dimensional space from another over a fixed field. The same invariant feeds vector-bundle rank, tensor-product dimension counts, exterior-algebra rank, and the discrete data of every Lie-group representation.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Existence of a basis (finite-dimensional case).** Suppose $V$ is spanned by a finite list $S=\{v_1,\ldots,v_n\}$. Iterate the following procedure: if some $v_i$ is a linear combination of the others, remove it; otherwise stop. The procedure terminates because the list shrinks at each step and stays a spanning set throughout. The final list is linearly independent and spans, hence a basis.

**Extension of an independent list to a basis.** Let $L=\{u_1,\ldots,u_k\}$ be linearly independent in a finite-dimensional $V$ with basis $B=\{v_1,\ldots,v_n\}$. Apply Steinitz replacement with $B$ as the spanning set and $L$ as the independent list: $k\le n$, and the relabelling of $B$ produces $\{u_1,\ldots,u_k,v_{k+1},\ldots,v_n\}$ spanning $V$. This list has $n$ elements and $\dim V=n$, so it is a basis [ref: textbooks-extra Calculus Vol.2 - Multi-Variable Calculus and Linear Algebra with Applications (Tom Apostol).pdf].

**Coordinate isomorphism.** A choice of ordered basis $(v_1,\ldots,v_n)$ defines

$$
\Phi:K^n\to V,\qquad (a_1,\ldots,a_n)\longmapsto a_1v_1+\cdots+a_nv_n.
$$

Spanning gives surjectivity. Linear independence gives $\ker\Phi=0$, hence injectivity. So $\Phi$ is an isomorphism, and every $n$-dimensional vector space over $K$ is isomorphic to $K^n$.

**Hamel basis existence (general case).** Let $\mathcal F$ be the family of linearly independent subsets of $V$, partially ordered by inclusion. Every chain $\mathcal C\subseteq\mathcal F$ has the union $\bigcup\mathcal C$ as an upper bound, which is linearly independent because any finite linear-dependence relation involves only finitely many elements and hence lies inside one member of the chain. Zorn's lemma gives a maximal element $B$. Maximality forces $\mathrm{span}\, B=V$: otherwise a vector $v\notin\mathrm{span}\, B$ extends $B\cup\{v\}$ to a strictly larger independent set. Hence $B$ is a basis.

**Equicardinality of Hamel bases.** Standard transfinite combinatorics shows that two Hamel bases $B,B'$ of an infinite-dimensional vector space satisfy $|B|=|B'|$. The proof reduces to a counting argument: each element of $B'$ is a finite linear combination of elements of $B$, the assignment is at-most countable-to-one, and cardinal arithmetic with infinite cardinals closes the comparison [ref: TODO_REF Halmos — Finite-Dimensional Vector Spaces].

## Connections [Master]

- **Vector space [01.01.03]** — supplies the underlying object on which the subspace lattice is built and the algebraic data the basis decomposes.

- **Bilinear and quadratic forms [01.01.15]** — measurement on a vector space pulls back through a basis to a Gram matrix, and the rank of that matrix records the dimension of the radical complement.

- **Tensor algebra [03.01.04]** — the dimension count $\dim(V^{\otimes k})=(\dim V)^k$ is the multilinear shadow of the present unit, and a basis of $V$ generates a basis of every tensor power.

- **Vector bundle [03.05.02]** — the rank of a bundle is the dimension of every fibre, and a local product chart pulls a basis of the fibre back to a frame on the base.

- **Differential forms [03.04.01]** — the rank of $\Lambda^k V^*$ is $\binom{n}{k}$, where $n=\dim V$, so the dimension function fixes how many independent $k$-forms exist on an $n$-manifold's cotangent space.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Hermann Grassmann gave the first systematic account of linearly independent generators and dimension in *Die lineale Ausdehnungslehre* (1844), formulating the algebra of $n$-dimensional extension long before the field-axiomatic vector-space concept stabilised [ref: TODO_REF Halmos — Finite-Dimensional Vector Spaces]. Giuseppe Peano's *Calcolo geometrico secondo l'Ausdehnungslehre di H. Grassmann* (1888) gave the modern axiomatic definition of a real vector space, including the closure conditions on subspaces and the dimension axiom in essentially their current form.

Ernst Steinitz proved the replacement theorem in 1913 in his memoir *Bedingt konvergente Reihen und konvexe Systeme* (J. reine angew. Math. 143). The theorem established well-definedness of dimension as a corollary of an exchange procedure and is the algebraic ancestor of the matroid exchange axiom; van der Waerden adopted it as the standard route to dimension in *Moderne Algebra* (1930), and Hermann Weyl's *Theorie der Gruppen und ihrer Darstellungen* (1928) presented the linear-algebraic content axiomatically as the foundation for representation theory. Georg Hamel's 1905 paper *Eine Basis aller Zahlen und die unstetigen Lösungen der Funktionalgleichung* $f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)$ used Zorn-style choice to construct what is now called a Hamel basis of $\mathbb R$ over $\mathbb Q$, exhibiting an additive function on $\mathbb R$ that is not linear and demonstrating the choice-dependence of bases in the infinite-dimensional setting.
