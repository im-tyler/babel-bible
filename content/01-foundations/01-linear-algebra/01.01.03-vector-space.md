## Intuition [Beginner]

A vector space is a place where adding arrows and stretching arrows always makes sense. If two arrows live in the same vector space, their sum lives there too. If an arrow is stretched by a number, the result also lives there.

The idea matters because it separates the rules from the picture. Arrows in the plane are vector-space examples, but so are lists of numbers, polynomials, signals, and solutions to many equations. Once the rules are shared, the same algebra works in all of these settings.

Think of a vector space as a workshop with two reliable tools: combine and scale.

## Visual [Beginner]

The blue and red arrows combine to make the green arrow. The purple arrow is stretched but stays in the same plane.

![A coordinate grid with vector addition and scalar stretching inside one vector space.](/img/vector-space-placeholder.svg)

The picture shows the two operations, not the full definition. A vector space is any collection where these operations obey the same basic rules.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Work in the usual coordinate plane. Take the arrow $(3,1)$ and the arrow $(-1,4)$.

Adding them means adding matching coordinates:

$$
(3,1)+(-1,4)=(2,5).
$$

Stretching the first arrow by $2$ gives

$$
2(3,1)=(6,2).
$$

Both results are still arrows in the coordinate plane.

What this tells us: the coordinate plane is closed under the two vector-space operations, addition and scaling.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $K$ be a field. A **vector space over $K$** is an abelian group $(V,+,0)$ together with a scalar multiplication map

$$
K\times V\longrightarrow V,\qquad (a,v)\longmapsto av,
$$

such that, for all $a,b\in K$ and all $u,v\in V$,

$$
a(u+v)=au+av,\qquad (a+b)v=av+bv,
$$

$$
(ab)v=a(bv),\qquad 1v=v.
$$

The elements of $V$ are **vectors**; the elements of $K$ are **scalars**. This is Mathlib's `Module K V` structure when `K` is a field [ref: quantum-well Vector spaces.md].

Standard examples include $K^n$, polynomial spaces $K[x]$, function spaces $X\to K$, and solution spaces of homogeneous linear equations. The one-element vector space exists over every field and contains only the zero vector.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (elementary consequences of the axioms).** Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $K$. For every $v\in V$ and every $a\in K$,

$$
0v=0,\qquad a0=0,\qquad (-1)v=-v.
$$

**Proof.** For the first identity, use distributivity over scalar addition:

$$
0v=(0+0)v=0v+0v.
$$

Add the additive inverse of $0v$ to both sides. The result is $0=0v$, so $0v=0$.

For the second identity, use distributivity over vector addition:

$$
a0=a(0+0)=a0+a0.
$$

The same cancellation gives $a0=0$.

For the third identity,

$$
v+(-1)v=1v+(-1)v=(1+(-1))v=0v=0.
$$

Thus $(-1)v$ is an additive inverse of $v$. The additive inverse is unique in the abelian group $(V,+,0)$, so $(-1)v=-v$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [01.01.15] (bilinear form / quadratic form), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [03.01.04] (tensor algebra). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the zero vector in a vector space is unique.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compare two candidate additive identities $0$ and $0'$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $0$ and $0'$ are both additive identities, then

0=0+0'=0'.

The first equality uses that $0'$ is an additive identity, and the second uses that $0$ is an additive identity.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Let $S$ be a nonempty subset of a vector space $V$. Show that if $S$ is closed under addition and scalar multiplication, then $S$ is a vector space with the inherited operations.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Most axioms are inherited from $V$. Use nonemptiness and scalar multiplication by $0$ to get the zero vector.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Choose $s\in S$. Since $S$ is closed under scalar multiplication, $0s=0$ lies in $S$. If $s\in S$, then $(-1)s=-s$ lies in $S$, so additive inverses are present. Addition and scalar multiplication stay in $S$ by assumption. The remaining identities hold because they already hold in $V$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Let $X$ be a set and let $K^X$ be the set of all functions $X\to K$. Define addition and scalar multiplication pointwise. Prove that $K^X$ is a vector space over $K$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Evaluate each vector-space axiom at an arbitrary point of $X$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For functions $f,g:X\to K$, define $(f+g)(x)=f(x)+g(x)$ and $(af)(x)=a f(x)$. The zero function sends every $x$ to $0$. Each axiom in $K^X$ is checked pointwise and becomes the corresponding field identity in $K$. Therefore $K^X$ is a vector space.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

Let $A$ be an $m\times n$ matrix over $K$. Prove that the solution set of $Ax=0$ in $K^n$ is a vector space.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use matrix linearity: $A(x+y)=Ax+Ay$ and $A(ax)=aAx$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The zero vector satisfies $A0=0$. If $Ax=0$ and $Ay=0$, then $A(x+y)=Ax+Ay=0+0=0$. If $a\in K$, then $A(ax)=aAx=a0=0$. The subset criterion from Exercise 4 applies.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

Every vector space has a basis, assuming the usual choice principle. For finite-dimensional spaces, a basis is a finite list of linearly independent vectors that spans the space; every vector has a unique coordinate expression in that basis. For arbitrary vector spaces, the same statement is proved by Zorn's lemma [ref: TODO_REF Halmos Ch. 1].

If $V$ has a basis indexed by $I$, then $V$ is isomorphic to the direct sum

$$
K^{(I)}=\{(a_i)_{i\in I}: a_i=0\text{ for all but finitely many }i\}.
$$

This identifies vector spaces over a fixed field by dimension: two vector spaces over $K$ are isomorphic exactly when their bases have the same cardinality.

The categorical form is also important. A vector space over $K$ is a module over the field $K$, and the category $\mathrm{Vect}_K$ is an abelian category with kernels, cokernels, direct sums, and exact sequences. The finite-dimensional subcategory is rigid monoidal under tensor product and duals.

**Synthesis.** This construction generalises the pattern fixed in [00.02.05] (function), with the symmetric data replaced by its skew or twisted analogue. Read in the opposite direction, the construction is dual to the metric story: complements and orthogonality are taken with respect to the bilinear datum of this unit, not a metric, and the resulting category of subobjects is the one the rest of the strand classifies. The central insight is that this datum identifies algebra with geometry: functions become vector fields, subspaces become quotients, and invariants become cohomology classes — and that identification is the engine driving every theorem downstream.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Basis classification in finite dimension.** Let $V$ be finite-dimensional. Any spanning list can be shortened to a basis by deleting vectors that are linear combinations of earlier vectors. Any linearly independent list can be extended to a basis by adding vectors from a fixed spanning list until the span is all of $V$. The exchange lemma proves that any two bases have the same number of elements, so dimension is well-defined [ref: TODO_REF Hoffman-Kunze §1].

**Coordinate isomorphism.** If $(v_1,\ldots,v_n)$ is a basis of $V$, define

$$
\Phi:K^n\to V,\qquad (a_1,\ldots,a_n)\mapsto a_1v_1+\cdots+a_nv_n.
$$

Spanning makes $\Phi$ surjective. Linear independence makes $\ker\Phi=0$, so $\Phi$ is injective. Therefore $\Phi$ is a vector-space isomorphism.

**Subspace criterion.** A nonempty subset $S\subseteq V$ is a subspace exactly when it is closed under linear combinations $au+bv$. If $S$ is a subspace, closure is inherited from scalar multiplication and addition. Conversely, closure under $au+bv$ gives $0\in S$ by choosing $a=0$, gives additive inverses by choosing $a=-1$, and gives closure under addition and scalar multiplication by choosing the other coefficients appropriately.

## Connections [Master]

- **Bilinear forms [01.01.15]** — require vector spaces before one can evaluate two vector inputs.

- **Tensor algebra [03.01.04]** — starts from a vector space and builds the free associative algebra generated by it.

- **Clifford algebra [03.09.02]** — is a quotient of the tensor algebra of a vector space by quadratic relations.

- **Fredholm operators [03.09.06]** — act on infinite-dimensional vector spaces with topology added.

- **Vector bundle [03.05.02]** — places one vector space over each point of a base space.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The abstract vector-space concept emerged from nineteenth-century linear equations, determinants, and geometry, then stabilized through early twentieth-century algebra. The modern axiomatic presentation treats vectors as elements of any set with addition and scalar multiplication satisfying structural laws, not as coordinate tuples alone [ref: TODO_REF Halmos Ch. 1].

Halmos's *Finite-Dimensional Vector Spaces* helped set the mid-century American style for coordinate-free linear algebra. Axler later emphasized linear maps and invariant subspaces while delaying determinants, a choice reflected in the modern definition-first route through vector spaces [ref: quantum-well Axler S., Gerhing F.W., Ribet K.A. Linear Algebra Done Right, Springer, 2nd edition, 1997.md].
