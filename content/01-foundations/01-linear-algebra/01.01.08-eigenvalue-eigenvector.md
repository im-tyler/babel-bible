## Intuition [Beginner]

An eigenvector of a linear map is an arrow the map does not rotate. Apply the map; the arrow comes back pointing along its original line, possibly longer, possibly shorter, possibly flipped to the opposite direction. The number recording how much the arrow has been stretched is the eigenvalue of that eigenvector. A stretch by a factor of 2 is an eigenvalue of 2; a flip and a stretch by 3 is an eigenvalue of $-3$; a fold flat onto the origin is an eigenvalue of 0.

Most arrows are not eigenvectors. A generic arrow gets rotated by the map and lands pointing somewhere new. Eigenvectors are the special arrows along which the map acts by a single scalar — the rotation is invisible because the arrow has no perpendicular component to rotate into.

A linear map can have several eigenvalues, each one labelling its own family of eigenvectors. Together, the eigenvalues record the stretching directions of the map and the stretch factors along each. They are the simplest pieces of information needed to describe what a linear map does.

Two pieces of data are encoded here. The line along which the map acts as a pure stretch is the *eigendirection*. The number recording the stretch factor along that line is the *eigenvalue*. The whole map decomposes into these directions whenever there are enough of them.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the two-by-two matrix

$$
A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Apply $A$ to the vector $(1, 0)$: the result is $(3, 0)$, which is $3$ times the input. So $(1, 0)$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $3$. Apply $A$ to $(0, 1)$: the result is $(0, -1)$, which is $-1$ times the input. So $(0, 1)$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $-1$.

Read the geometry. The map stretches everything along the horizontal axis by a factor of $3$ and flips everything along the vertical axis (a stretch by $-1$). Every arrow drawn in the plane breaks into a horizontal piece and a vertical piece; the map acts on each piece by its own scalar. A vector $(a, b)$ becomes $(3a, -b)$.

Now an off-diagonal example. Take

$$
B = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Apply $B$ to $(1, 0)$: the result is $(2, 0) = 2 \cdot (1, 0)$. So $(1, 0)$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $2$. Try to find a second eigenvector along $(0, 1)$: $B(0, 1) = (1, 2)$, which is not a scalar multiple of $(0, 1)$. The matrix $B$ has only one eigenvalue ($2$) and one eigendirection (the horizontal line), even though it is a two-by-two matrix.

What this tells us: a linear map may have a full set of eigendirections (one per dimension), in which case the map looks like pure stretching in those directions, or it may have fewer, in which case some shearing is involved and the map cannot be reduced to pure stretching.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $V$ be a vector space over a field $K$ and let $T: V \to V$ be a linear operator on $V$. A scalar $\lambda \in K$ is an **eigenvalue** of $T$ if there exists a non-zero vector $v \in V$ with

$$
T(v) = \lambda v.
$$

The non-zero vector $v$ is then called an **eigenvector** of $T$ for the eigenvalue $\lambda$. The condition $v \ne 0$ is part of the definition: without it every scalar would qualify as an eigenvalue because the equation holds at $v = 0$ for any $\lambda$ [ref: quantum-well md/Mathematical foundations/Algebra/Linear Algebra and Matrix Theory/Eigenvalues and eigenvectors.md].

For each $\lambda \in K$ the **eigenspace** of $T$ at $\lambda$ is the subspace

$$
E_\lambda = \ker(T - \lambda \cdot \mathrm{id}_V) = \{v \in V : T(v) = \lambda v\} \subseteq V.
$$

The eigenspace contains the zero vector and every eigenvector for $\lambda$. It is a subspace of $V$ because it is the kernel of a linear map [01.01.05]. The scalar $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $T$ exactly when $E_\lambda \ne \{0\}$.

The **characteristic polynomial** of $T$, when $V$ is finite-dimensional, is

$$
\chi_T(t) = \det(t I - T) \in K[t],
$$

where $I$ is the identity operator on $V$ and the determinant [01.01.07] is computed in the polynomial ring $K[t]$. Concretely, fix a basis of $V$ to represent $T$ by a matrix $A \in M_n(K)$; then $\chi_T(t) = \det(t I_n - A)$. Choice of basis is harmless: under change of basis $A \mapsto B A B^{-1}$, the multiplicativity of the determinant gives $\det(tI - B A B^{-1}) = \det(B (tI - A) B^{-1}) = \det A \cdot \det(tI - A) \cdot \det(B^{-1}) = \det(tI - A)$, recorded in Exercise 8 of [01.01.07]. The polynomial $\chi_T(t)$ is therefore an invariant of the operator class. It is monic of degree $n = \dim V$, with constant term $(-1)^n \det T$ and next-to-leading coefficient $-\mathrm{tr}\, T$.

**Eigenvalues are roots of the characteristic polynomial.** A scalar $\lambda \in K$ is an eigenvalue of $T$ if and only if $\chi_T(\lambda) = 0$. Indeed, $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue iff $\ker(T - \lambda I) \ne \{0\}$, which by the invertibility corollary in [01.01.07] is equivalent to $\det(\lambda I - T) = 0$, which is exactly $\chi_T(\lambda) = 0$.

The **algebraic multiplicity** of an eigenvalue $\lambda$ is its multiplicity as a root of $\chi_T(t)$. The **geometric multiplicity** of $\lambda$ is $\dim E_\lambda$. Both are positive integers; geometric multiplicity is always at most algebraic multiplicity, with strict inequality possible.

**Worked instances.** For the matrix $A = \mathrm{diag}(3, -1)$, the characteristic polynomial is $(t-3)(t+1)$ with two distinct simple roots; algebraic multiplicity equals geometric multiplicity (both $1$) for each eigenvalue. For the Jordan block $B = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$, the characteristic polynomial is $(t-2)^2$; the single eigenvalue $2$ has algebraic multiplicity $2$ but geometric multiplicity $1$ — the eigenspace $E_2 = \ker(B - 2I)$ is the kernel of $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, the span of $(1, 0)$ alone. For the planar rotation by angle $\theta$, $R_\theta = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$, the characteristic polynomial is $t^2 - 2\cos\theta \cdot t + 1$, with no real roots when $\theta \notin \pi \mathbb Z$; over $\mathbb C$ the roots are $e^{\pm i\theta}$ [ref: textbooks-extra Calculus Vol.2 - Multi-Variable Calculus and Linear Algebra with Applications (Tom Apostol).pdf].

**Diagonalisability.** The operator $T$ is **diagonalisable** if $V$ admits a basis consisting of eigenvectors of $T$. Equivalently, $V = \bigoplus_\lambda E_\lambda$ as a direct sum over eigenvalues. In a basis of eigenvectors, the matrix of $T$ is diagonal with the eigenvalues on the diagonal. Diagonalisability is the condition that the sum of geometric multiplicities equals $\dim V$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- The condition $v \ne 0$ in the eigenvalue definition is load-bearing. Drop it and every scalar becomes an eigenvalue of every operator.
- An operator on a real vector space may have no eigenvalues in the ground field: planar rotation by an angle other than $0$ or $\pi$ has $\chi_T(t) = t^2 - 2\cos\theta \cdot t + 1$ with no real roots. The same operator over $\mathbb C$ has eigenvalues $e^{\pm i\theta}$.
- Algebraic multiplicity is a property of the characteristic polynomial; geometric multiplicity is a property of the eigenspace. They can differ. The Jordan block $\begin{pmatrix} \lambda & 1 \\ 0 & \lambda \end{pmatrix}$ has $\lambda$ as an eigenvalue of algebraic multiplicity $2$ and geometric multiplicity $1$.
- Diagonalisability is not implied by having eigenvalues; it is implied by having enough independent eigenvectors. The Jordan block above has an eigenvalue but is not diagonalisable.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (linear independence of eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues).** Let $T: V \to V$ be a linear operator on a vector space $V$ over $K$. Let $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k \in K$ be distinct eigenvalues of $T$ and let $v_1, \ldots, v_k \in V$ be corresponding eigenvectors, with $v_i \ne 0$ and $T(v_i) = \lambda_i v_i$ for $i = 1, \ldots, k$. Then $\{v_1, \ldots, v_k\}$ is linearly independent.

[ref: textbooks-extra Calculus Vol.2 - Multi-Variable Calculus and Linear Algebra with Applications (Tom Apostol).pdf]

**Proof.** Induction on $k$.

*Base case $k = 1$.* A single non-zero vector $\{v_1\}$ with $v_1 \ne 0$ is linearly independent: a relation $c_1 v_1 = 0$ with $c_1 \in K$ forces $c_1 = 0$, since the field $K$ has no zero divisors.

*Inductive step.* Assume the result for $k-1$ distinct eigenvalues. Suppose

$$
c_1 v_1 + c_2 v_2 + \cdots + c_k v_k = 0
$$

for some scalars $c_1, \ldots, c_k \in K$. The plan is to apply the operator $T - \lambda_k I$ to both sides, observe that the term $v_k$ drops out, and use the inductive hypothesis on the resulting relation among $v_1, \ldots, v_{k-1}$.

Apply $T - \lambda_k I$ to both sides of the relation, using linearity:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^k c_i (T - \lambda_k I)(v_i) = (T - \lambda_k I)(0) = 0.
$$

For each $i$, $(T - \lambda_k I)(v_i) = T(v_i) - \lambda_k v_i = \lambda_i v_i - \lambda_k v_i = (\lambda_i - \lambda_k) v_i$. Substituting,

$$
\sum_{i=1}^k c_i (\lambda_i - \lambda_k) v_i = 0.
$$

The $i = k$ term is $c_k (\lambda_k - \lambda_k) v_k = 0$ and drops out, leaving

$$
\sum_{i=1}^{k-1} c_i (\lambda_i - \lambda_k) v_i = 0.
$$

By the inductive hypothesis, $\{v_1, \ldots, v_{k-1}\}$ is linearly independent, so each coefficient must vanish:

$$
c_i (\lambda_i - \lambda_k) = 0 \quad \text{for } i = 1, \ldots, k-1.
$$

The eigenvalues are distinct, so $\lambda_i - \lambda_k \ne 0$ for $i < k$; hence $c_i = 0$ for $i = 1, \ldots, k-1$. The original relation collapses to $c_k v_k = 0$, and $v_k \ne 0$ forces $c_k = 0$ as well. Every coefficient vanishes, so $\{v_1, \ldots, v_k\}$ is linearly independent. $\square$

**Corollary (sufficient condition for diagonalisability).** Let $T: V \to V$ be a linear operator on a finite-dimensional vector space $V$ over $K$ with $\dim V = n$. If $T$ has $n$ distinct eigenvalues in $K$, then $T$ is diagonalisable.

**Proof.** Let $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n \in K$ be the $n$ distinct eigenvalues and pick one eigenvector $v_i$ for each $\lambda_i$. By the theorem, $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ is a linearly independent set of $n$ vectors in $V$. Any linearly independent set of $\dim V$ vectors is a basis [01.01.04], so $\{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}$ is a basis of $V$ consisting of eigenvectors. The matrix of $T$ in this basis is diagonal with $\lambda_i$ in the $i$-th diagonal entry. $\square$

**Bridge.** The linear-independence-for-distinct-eigenvalues theorem is the load-bearing lemma from which most of finite-dimensional spectral theory descends.

*First synthesis*: combined with the fundamental theorem of algebra, it gives a generic diagonalisability statement — over $\mathbb C$, the characteristic polynomial of a generic $n \times n$ matrix has $n$ distinct roots, so a generic operator on a complex finite-dimensional vector space is diagonalisable, with the non-diagonalisable locus a proper algebraic subvariety of the matrix space.

*Second synthesis*: combined with the determinant identity $\chi_T(t) = \det(tI - T)$, it lets the entire spectral structure of $T$ be read off from the characteristic polynomial — the eigenvalues are the roots, the algebraic multiplicities are the root multiplicities, and the geometric multiplicities are the dimensions of the kernels of $T - \lambda I$ at each root.

*Third synthesis*: the same independence pattern, applied to generalised eigenspaces $\ker(T - \lambda I)^k$ instead of eigenspaces, yields the primary decomposition $V = \bigoplus_\lambda \ker(T - \lambda I)^{m_\lambda}$ where $m_\lambda$ is the algebraic multiplicity; this is the path to the Jordan canonical form, in which every operator over $\mathbb C$ becomes a direct sum of Jordan blocks $J_k(\lambda) = \lambda I_k + N_k$ with $N_k$ a single nilpotent shift.

*Fourth synthesis*: the spectral data carries the dynamics of iteration. The $m$-th iterate $T^m$ has the same eigenvectors as $T$ and eigenvalues $\lambda^m$, so the growth rate of orbits $\{T^m v\}$ is governed by the largest eigenvalue (the *spectral radius*), and the long-time behaviour of any linear dynamical system $v_{m+1} = T v_m$ — stability, oscillation, decay — is determined entirely by the location of the eigenvalues in the complex plane.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).**

The operator $T$ on $\mathbb R^3$ has eigenvalues $2$, $-1$, and $3$. Compute $\det T$ and $\mathrm{tr}\, T$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The determinant is the product of the eigenvalues (with multiplicity); the trace is the sum.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\det T = 2 \cdot (-1) \cdot 3 = -6$ and $\mathrm{tr}\, T = 2 + (-1) + 3 = 4$. The general identity reads $\chi_T(t) = t^n - (\mathrm{tr}\, T) t^{n-1} + \cdots + (-1)^n \det T$, so the trace is the sum of eigenvalues and the determinant is the product.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Find the eigenvalues and a basis of each eigenspace for the matrix

$$
A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Decide whether $A$ is diagonalisable.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\chi_A(t)$, factor it, and for each root solve $(A - \lambda I) v = 0$ for $v$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The characteristic polynomial is $\chi_A(t) = (t-1)^2 - 4 = t^2 - 2t - 3 = (t-3)(t+1)$, with eigenvalues $3$ and $-1$. For $\lambda = 3$: solve $(A - 3I) v = 0$, that is $\begin{pmatrix} -2 & 2 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix} v = 0$, giving $v = (1, 1)$ as a basis of $E_3$. For $\lambda = -1$: solve $(A + I) v = 0$, that is $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 2 \\ 2 & 2 \end{pmatrix} v = 0$, giving $v = (1, -1)$ as a basis of $E_{-1}$. The two eigenvalues are distinct, so by the corollary $A$ is diagonalisable, with diagonal form $\mathrm{diag}(3, -1)$ in the basis $\{(1,1), (1,-1)\}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the matrix

$$
B = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}
$$

has eigenvalue $3$ with algebraic multiplicity $2$ but geometric multiplicity $1$. Conclude that $B$ is not diagonalisable.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\chi_B(t)$ and then $\ker(B - 3I)$ explicitly.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The characteristic polynomial is $\chi_B(t) = (t-3)^2$, so $3$ is the only eigenvalue, with algebraic multiplicity $2$. The eigenspace $E_3 = \ker(B - 3I)$ is the kernel of $\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, which sends $(x, y) \mapsto (y, 0)$; the kernel is $\{(x, 0)\} = $ span$\{(1, 0)\}$, of dimension $1$. Geometric multiplicity is $1 < 2 = $ algebraic multiplicity. A diagonalisable $2 \times 2$ matrix has sum of geometric multiplicities equal to $2$, which fails here. So $B$ is not diagonalisable.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, proof).**

Let $T: V \to V$ be a linear operator on a finite-dimensional vector space $V$. Show that $0$ is an eigenvalue of $T$ if and only if $T$ is not injective.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Translate the condition "$0$ is an eigenvalue" into a statement about $\ker T$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The scalar $0$ is an eigenvalue of $T$ iff there exists $v \ne 0$ with $T(v) = 0 \cdot v = 0$, iff $\ker T \ne \{0\}$. By Exercise 5 of [01.01.05], $\ker T \ne \{0\}$ iff $T$ is not injective. Combining the two equivalences gives the result. As a corollary, $T$ is invertible iff $0$ is not an eigenvalue iff $\chi_T(0) = (-1)^n \det T \ne 0$ iff $\det T \ne 0$, consistent with the invertibility-via-determinant corollary of [01.01.07].

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).**

Let $T: V \to V$ be a linear operator and $\lambda$ an eigenvalue of $T$. Show that $\lambda^m$ is an eigenvalue of $T^m$ for every positive integer $m$, with the same eigenvectors.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Iterate the eigenvalue equation $T(v) = \lambda v$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $T(v) = \lambda v$ with $v \ne 0$. Then $T^2(v) = T(T(v)) = T(\lambda v) = \lambda T(v) = \lambda^2 v$ by linearity. Induction on $m$: assuming $T^{m-1}(v) = \lambda^{m-1} v$, compute $T^m(v) = T(T^{m-1}(v)) = T(\lambda^{m-1} v) = \lambda^{m-1} T(v) = \lambda^m v$. So $v$ is an eigenvector of $T^m$ with eigenvalue $\lambda^m$. The vector $v$ is still non-zero, so $\lambda^m$ is an eigenvalue of $T^m$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

Let $T: V \to V$ be a linear operator on a finite-dimensional vector space and let $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k$ be the distinct eigenvalues of $T$ in the ground field $K$. Show that the sum of eigenspaces is direct:

$$
E_{\lambda_1} + E_{\lambda_2} + \cdots + E_{\lambda_k} = E_{\lambda_1} \oplus E_{\lambda_2} \oplus \cdots \oplus E_{\lambda_k}.
$$

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Reduce to the linear independence theorem by picking a basis of each eigenspace.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The sum $\sum_i E_{\lambda_i}$ is direct iff every relation $\sum_i u_i = 0$ with $u_i \in E_{\lambda_i}$ forces each $u_i = 0$. Suppose such a relation holds; assume for contradiction that some $u_i \ne 0$, and let $I = \{i : u_i \ne 0\}$, of size at least $1$. Each $u_i$ for $i \in I$ is a non-zero element of $E_{\lambda_i}$, so an eigenvector of $T$ for the eigenvalue $\lambda_i$. The eigenvalues $\{\lambda_i\}_{i \in I}$ are distinct (as a subset of the distinct list $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k$). By the linear independence theorem, $\{u_i\}_{i \in I}$ is linearly independent. Yet $\sum_{i \in I} 1 \cdot u_i = 0$ contradicts linear independence. So every $u_i = 0$, and the sum is direct.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).**

Let $T: V \to V$ be a linear operator on a finite-dimensional vector space over an algebraically closed field $K$. Show that $T$ has at least one eigenvalue.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the fundamental theorem of algebra to the characteristic polynomial.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The characteristic polynomial $\chi_T(t) = \det(tI - T) \in K[t]$ is monic of degree $n = \dim V \ge 1$. Over an algebraically closed field, every non-constant polynomial has a root in $K$, so $\chi_T(t)$ has at least one root $\lambda \in K$. By the equivalence "$\lambda$ is an eigenvalue iff $\chi_T(\lambda) = 0$" from the formal-definition section, $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $T$. (Over a non-algebraically-closed field, eigenvalues may fail to exist: planar rotation over $\mathbb R$ at angle $\theta \notin \pi \mathbb Z$ has no real eigenvalues.)

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Cayley-Hamilton theorem.** Every operator satisfies its own characteristic polynomial. Concretely, for $T: V \to V$ on a finite-dimensional $K$-vector space with characteristic polynomial $\chi_T(t) \in K[t]$, the operator $\chi_T(T) \in \mathrm{End}_K(V)$ is the zero operator. Equivalently, for $A \in M_n(K)$,

$$
\chi_A(A) = A^n + c_{n-1} A^{n-1} + \cdots + c_1 A + c_0 I_n = 0,
$$

where $\chi_A(t) = t^n + c_{n-1} t^{n-1} + \cdots + c_0$. The standard proof routes through the adjugate identity from [01.01.07]: the matrix $tI - A$ over $K[t]$ satisfies $(tI - A) \cdot \mathrm{adj}(tI - A) = \chi_A(t) \cdot I_n$; expanding the adjugate in powers of $t$ and matching coefficients gives a telescoping identity that collapses to $\chi_A(A) = 0$.

**Minimal polynomial.** The set of polynomials $p \in K[t]$ with $p(T) = 0$ is an ideal of $K[t]$; the unique monic generator $m_T(t)$ is the **minimal polynomial** of $T$. By Cayley-Hamilton, $m_T(t)$ divides $\chi_T(t)$. The two polynomials have the same roots (the eigenvalues of $T$), but the multiplicities may differ: the multiplicity of $\lambda$ in $m_T$ is the size of the largest Jordan block of $T$ at $\lambda$, while the multiplicity in $\chi_T$ is the algebraic multiplicity (the sum of the sizes of all Jordan blocks at $\lambda$). The operator $T$ is diagonalisable iff $m_T(t)$ has distinct roots — that is, iff $m_T$ is square-free.

**Primary decomposition.** For an operator $T: V \to V$ on a finite-dimensional vector space over $K$, with $\chi_T(t) = \prod_i (t - \lambda_i)^{m_i}$ factoring over an algebraic closure, the **generalised eigenspace** at $\lambda_i$ is

$$
G_{\lambda_i} = \ker(T - \lambda_i I)^{m_i}.
$$

The space $V$ decomposes as $V = \bigoplus_i G_{\lambda_i}$, with each $G_{\lambda_i}$ invariant under $T$ and $\dim G_{\lambda_i} = m_i$. The restriction of $T$ to $G_{\lambda_i}$ has the single eigenvalue $\lambda_i$, and on $G_{\lambda_i}$ the operator $T - \lambda_i I$ is nilpotent of order $\le m_i$.

**Jordan canonical form.** Over an algebraically closed field $K$, every operator $T: V \to V$ on a finite-dimensional $K$-vector space is similar to a direct sum of **Jordan blocks**

$$
J_k(\lambda) = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda & 1 & & \\ & \lambda & \ddots & \\ & & \ddots & 1 \\ & & & \lambda \end{pmatrix} \in M_k(K),
$$

with $k \ge 1$ and $\lambda$ ranging over the eigenvalues of $T$. The decomposition refines the primary decomposition: on each generalised eigenspace $G_\lambda$, the restriction of $T - \lambda I$ is a nilpotent operator, and the Jordan block structure records its nilpotent type (the partition of $\dim G_\lambda$ by Jordan-block sizes). The multiset of block sizes at $\lambda$ is the **Segre characteristic** of $T$ at $\lambda$, and the Jordan-form classification is the conjugacy classification of $\mathrm{GL}_n(\mathbb C)$ — two matrices in $M_n(\mathbb C)$ are similar iff they have the same Jordan form up to block reordering [ref: TODO_REF Jordan, C. — Traité des substitutions et des équations algébriques].

**Spectral theorem (finite-dimensional).** Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional inner-product space over $\mathbb C$ (Hermitian) or $\mathbb R$ (Euclidean). A linear operator $T: V \to V$ is **self-adjoint** if $\langle Tu, v \rangle = \langle u, Tv \rangle$ for all $u, v \in V$. The spectral theorem says that every self-adjoint operator has real eigenvalues and an orthonormal basis of $V$ consisting of eigenvectors of $T$. Equivalently, the matrix of $T$ in an orthonormal basis is diagonalisable by a unitary (resp. orthogonal) change of basis, with real diagonal entries. The companion **real spectral theorem** says that real symmetric matrices are diagonalisable over $\mathbb R$ — a statement about $\mathrm{End}_{\mathbb R}(V)$ that, over the complex extension, follows from the Hermitian case.

**Banach- and Hilbert-space generalisation.** For a bounded linear operator $T$ on a Banach space $V$, the **spectrum** $\sigma(T) \subseteq \mathbb C$ is the set of $\lambda$ for which $T - \lambda I$ fails to be invertible. The spectrum partitions as

- the **point spectrum** $\sigma_p(T)$, eigenvalues in the classical sense ($T - \lambda I$ is not injective);
- the **continuous spectrum** $\sigma_c(T)$, $T - \lambda I$ is injective with dense but proper image;
- the **residual spectrum** $\sigma_r(T)$, $T - \lambda I$ is injective with non-dense image.

The resolvent map $\lambda \mapsto (T - \lambda I)^{-1}$ is an operator-valued analytic function on $\mathbb C \setminus \sigma(T)$, and the spectrum is a non-empty compact subset of the closed disc of radius $\|T\|$. For a self-adjoint operator on a Hilbert space, the spectrum is real, and the **spectral theorem** of Stone and von Neumann associates a projection-valued spectral measure $E$ on $\sigma(T)$ with $T = \int_{\sigma(T)} \lambda \, dE(\lambda)$ [ref: TODO_REF von Neumann, J. — Allgemeine Eigenwerttheorie Hermitescher Funktionaloperatoren]; the finite-dimensional spectral decomposition $T = \sum_\lambda \lambda P_\lambda$ over eigenprojections is the discrete-measure case.

**Spectral radius and dynamics.** The **spectral radius** of $T$ is $r(T) = \max_\lambda |\lambda|$, the largest absolute value of an eigenvalue. Gelfand's formula gives $r(T) = \lim_{m \to \infty} \|T^m\|^{1/m}$ in the Banach-space setting. The spectral radius controls the long-time behaviour of the discrete dynamical system $v_{m+1} = T v_m$: orbits decay to zero when $r(T) < 1$, are bounded when $r(T) \le 1$ with no eigenvalues on the unit circle producing Jordan blocks of size $\ge 2$, and grow exponentially when $r(T) > 1$. The same dichotomy in continuous time governs the stability of an equilibrium of $\dot v = T v$ via the location of eigenvalues relative to the imaginary axis.

**Frobenius eigenvalues in arithmetic.** The eigenvalues of geometric Frobenius acting on $\ell$-adic étale cohomology of a smooth projective variety over a finite field are algebraic integers whose absolute values, under every complex embedding, are integer powers of $\sqrt{q}$ (the **Weil conjectures**, proved by Deligne in 1974). This is the arithmetic incarnation of the eigenvalue concept: the spectral data of a single linear operator (Frobenius on cohomology) encodes the number of points of the variety over every finite extension of the base field, via the Lefschetz fixed-point formula. The same package — *eigenvalues of a canonical operator on a cohomology space*— recurs in representation theory (characters of Hecke operators), in number theory (Galois representations), and in mathematical physics (energy spectrum of a quantum Hamiltonian).

**Synthesis.** Several threads weave together. *First synthesis* — *eigenvalues as the principal invariants of a linear operator*: the entire similarity class of $T$ is recorded by its Jordan form, hence by the multiset of eigenvalues with their Segre characteristics; every coordinate-free polynomial in the entries of $T$ — the trace, determinant, characteristic polynomial coefficients — is a symmetric function of the eigenvalues. *Second synthesis* — *the characteristic polynomial as the bridge between linear algebra and algebra*: $\chi_T$ is a degree-$n$ polynomial whose roots carry the spectrum, but the polynomial itself lives in $K[t]$ and is amenable to factorisation, divisibility, and minimal-polynomial bookkeeping — so the spectral structure of $T$ is encoded entirely in the ideal $(\chi_T(t)) \subset K[t]$ and the module structure $V \cong K[t]/(p_1) \oplus \cdots \oplus K[t]/(p_r)$ for $p_i \mid p_{i+1}$ (the elementary divisors). *Third synthesis* — *self-adjointness as a guarantee of diagonalisability*: a self-adjoint operator on an inner-product space carries enough orthogonality data that the spectral theorem produces an orthonormal eigenbasis; this is the linear-algebra origin of the unitary representation theory of compact groups, the spectral decomposition of compact self-adjoint operators on Hilbert space, and the eigenfunction expansions of the Laplacian on a compact Riemannian manifold. *Fourth synthesis* — *spectrum as dynamical signature*: the location of the spectrum of an evolution operator in the complex plane determines the qualitative dynamics — exponential decay, oscillation, exponential growth — and is the linear-algebra core of stability theory for differential equations and discrete-time recurrences alike.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Cayley-Hamilton via the adjugate identity.** Work over a commutative ring $R$ and let $A \in M_n(R)$. The adjugate identity from [01.01.07] gives

$$
(tI - A) \cdot \mathrm{adj}(tI - A) = \chi_A(t) \cdot I_n
$$

as a matrix identity in $M_n(R[t])$. The entries of $\mathrm{adj}(tI - A)$ are polynomials of degree at most $n - 1$ in $t$; write

$$
\mathrm{adj}(tI - A) = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} B_k t^k, \quad B_k \in M_n(R).
$$

Write $\chi_A(t) = t^n + c_{n-1} t^{n-1} + \cdots + c_1 t + c_0$. Expanding the left side of the adjugate identity and matching coefficients of $t^k$ for each $k$ gives the system

$$
B_{n-1} = I_n, \quad B_{k-1} - A B_k = c_k I_n \text{ for } 1 \le k \le n-1, \quad -A B_0 = c_0 I_n.
$$

Multiply the $k$-th identity on the left by $A^k$ and sum from $k = 0$ to $n$ (with $B_n = 0$ formally). The left side telescopes:

$$
\sum_{k=0}^n A^k (B_{k-1} - A B_k) = -A^{n+1} B_n + B_{-1} = 0,
$$

interpreting $B_{-1} = 0$ from the boundary identity $-A B_0 = c_0 I_n$ (which contributes $A^0 \cdot (-A B_0) = c_0 I_n$ to the right side). The right side is $\chi_A(A) = A^n + c_{n-1} A^{n-1} + \cdots + c_0 I_n$. Equating, $\chi_A(A) = 0$. $\square$

**Minimal polynomial divides characteristic.** By Cayley-Hamilton, $\chi_T(T) = 0$, so $\chi_T$ belongs to the ideal $\{p \in K[t] : p(T) = 0\}$. The minimal polynomial $m_T$ is the monic generator of this ideal, hence divides $\chi_T$. Equality holds iff every elementary divisor of the $K[t]$-module $V$ (the structure-theorem decomposition) is a single power — iff $V$ is a cyclic $K[t]$-module, iff $T$ has a cyclic vector.

**Same roots.** Every root of $m_T$ is a root of $\chi_T$ because $m_T \mid \chi_T$. Conversely, every eigenvalue $\lambda$ of $T$ satisfies $T(v) = \lambda v$ for some non-zero $v$, so $m_T(T)(v) = m_T(\lambda) \cdot v$; since $m_T(T) = 0$ and $v \ne 0$, $m_T(\lambda) = 0$. The two polynomials have the same set of roots; the multiplicities differ as recorded.

**Diagonalisability iff minimal polynomial has distinct roots.** Suppose $m_T(t) = \prod_i (t - \lambda_i)$ is square-free with distinct roots $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k$. The polynomials $p_i(t) = \prod_{j \ne i} (t - \lambda_j) / \prod_{j \ne i} (\lambda_i - \lambda_j)$ satisfy $\sum_i p_i(t) = 1$ (Lagrange interpolation at the $\lambda_i$ with constant value $1$) and $(t - \lambda_i) p_i(t)$ is divisible by $m_T(t)$, so $(T - \lambda_i I) p_i(T) = 0$. Setting $P_i = p_i(T)$ gives $\sum_i P_i = I$ and $T P_i = \lambda_i P_i$. Each $P_i$ is a projection onto an eigenspace $E_{\lambda_i}$, and $V = \bigoplus_i E_{\lambda_i}$, so $T$ is diagonalisable. Conversely, if $T$ is diagonalisable with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k$, then $p(t) = \prod_i (t - \lambda_i)$ satisfies $p(T) = 0$ on each eigenspace, hence on $V$, so $m_T \mid p$; since $m_T$ has the same roots as $\chi_T$ and $p$ is square-free with those roots, $m_T = p$ is square-free.

**Primary decomposition.** Let $\chi_T(t) = \prod_i (t - \lambda_i)^{m_i}$ over an algebraic closure of $K$ (the eigenvalues $\lambda_i$ are distinct). The polynomials $q_i(t) = (t - \lambda_i)^{m_i}$ are pairwise coprime in $K[t]$. By the structure theorem for finitely generated modules over a PID, applied to $V$ as a $K[t]$-module with $t$ acting as $T$,

$$
V \cong \bigoplus_i K[t] / (q_i(t)^{e_i})
$$

for some exponents $e_i$; here $e_i \le m_i$ and the inclusion of the summand $K[t]/(q_i^{e_i})$ identifies with $G_{\lambda_i} = \ker(T - \lambda_i I)^{m_i}$. The direct-sum decomposition gives $V = \bigoplus_i G_{\lambda_i}$ with each $G_{\lambda_i}$ invariant under $T$. Restricting $T$ to $G_{\lambda_i}$, the operator $T - \lambda_i I$ is nilpotent because $(T - \lambda_i I)^{m_i}$ annihilates $G_{\lambda_i}$. Hence on $G_{\lambda_i}$, $T = \lambda_i I + N_i$ with $N_i$ nilpotent.

**Jordan canonical form.** On a generalised eigenspace $G_\lambda$ the operator $N = T - \lambda I$ is nilpotent. The structure theorem for nilpotent operators on a finite-dimensional vector space gives a basis in which $N$ takes the block-diagonal form $\bigoplus_k N_k$ with $N_k \in M_k(K)$ the standard nilpotent shift (1 on the superdiagonal, 0 elsewhere). Adding $\lambda I_k$ to each block gives $T$ on $G_\lambda$ in the form $\bigoplus_k J_k(\lambda)$ where $J_k(\lambda) = \lambda I_k + N_k$ is the standard Jordan block. Assembling over $\lambda$ gives the full Jordan canonical form $T = \bigoplus_{\lambda, k} J_k(\lambda)$. The multiset of block sizes at each $\lambda$ — the Segre characteristic — is uniquely determined by $T$; two operators are similar iff they have the same Jordan form up to permutation of blocks. The construction of the basis of $G_\lambda$ that exhibits $N$ in block form is the *Jordan chain* construction: pick a basis of $\ker N$, lift to a basis of $\ker N^2 / \ker N$ via $N$-preimages, and iterate.

**Spectral theorem for self-adjoint operators (finite-dimensional).** Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional inner-product space over $K \in \{\mathbb R, \mathbb C\}$ and let $T: V \to V$ be self-adjoint, $\langle Tu, v \rangle = \langle u, Tv \rangle$. The proof has three parts.

*Eigenvalues are real.* Let $\lambda$ be an eigenvalue with eigenvector $v \ne 0$. Compute $\lambda \langle v, v \rangle = \langle \lambda v, v \rangle = \langle Tv, v \rangle = \langle v, Tv \rangle = \langle v, \lambda v \rangle = \bar\lambda \langle v, v \rangle$. Since $\langle v, v \rangle \ne 0$, $\lambda = \bar\lambda$, so $\lambda \in \mathbb R$.

*Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.* Let $Tv = \lambda v$ and $Tw = \mu w$ with $\lambda \ne \mu$ (both real by the first part). Compute $\lambda \langle v, w \rangle = \langle Tv, w \rangle = \langle v, Tw \rangle = \langle v, \mu w \rangle = \mu \langle v, w \rangle$. Hence $(\lambda - \mu) \langle v, w \rangle = 0$, and $\lambda \ne \mu$ forces $\langle v, w \rangle = 0$.

*An orthonormal eigenbasis exists.* Induct on $\dim V$. The base case $\dim V = 1$ is immediate. Inductive step: over $\mathbb C$, the fundamental theorem of algebra gives a root $\lambda$ of $\chi_T$, hence an eigenvalue with unit-norm eigenvector $e_1$. Over $\mathbb R$, the complexification $T_{\mathbb C}$ on $V_{\mathbb C} = V \otimes_{\mathbb R} \mathbb C$ is self-adjoint with respect to the Hermitian extension of the inner product, so has a real eigenvalue by the complex case, and a real eigenvector by the conjugation symmetry of $V_{\mathbb C}$. In either case, set $W = e_1^\perp$, the orthogonal complement. The restriction $T|_W: W \to W$ is well-defined: for $w \in W$, $\langle Tw, e_1 \rangle = \langle w, T e_1 \rangle = \langle w, \lambda e_1 \rangle = \lambda \langle w, e_1 \rangle = 0$, so $Tw \in W$. The restriction is self-adjoint on $W$ (the inner product on $W$ is the restriction), and $\dim W = \dim V - 1$. The inductive hypothesis gives an orthonormal eigenbasis $e_2, \ldots, e_n$ of $W$; together with $e_1$, this is an orthonormal eigenbasis of $V$.

**Linear independence of eigenvectors, revisited functorially.** The Intermediate-tier inductive proof has a coordinate-free packaging via the primary decomposition: distinct eigenvalues give pairwise coprime polynomials $(t - \lambda_i)$, so the corresponding eigenspaces $E_{\lambda_i} = \ker(T - \lambda_i I)$ sit inside pairwise distinct primary components of the $K[t]$-module $V$, hence their sum is direct. The two proofs reflect the same arithmetic of $K[t]$ — pairwise coprimality of $(t - \lambda_i)$ — at different levels of abstraction.

## Connections [Master]

- **Determinant: axiomatic + expansion + properties [01.01.07]** — supplies the determinant identity $\chi_T(t) = \det(tI - T)$ that defines the characteristic polynomial of an operator, and the multiplicativity identity $\det(BAB^{-1}) = \det A$ that proves the characteristic polynomial is a similarity invariant. The adjugate identity from [01.01.07] is the engine of the standard Cayley-Hamilton proof in this unit; without the determinant unit's machinery, neither the characteristic polynomial nor Cayley-Hamilton has a workable definition or proof.

- **Linear transformation: kernel, image, rank-nullity [01.01.05]** — supplies the eigenspace as a kernel, $E_\lambda = \ker(T - \lambda I)$, and the dimension count that links algebraic and geometric multiplicity. The equivalence "$\lambda$ is an eigenvalue iff $T - \lambda I$ is not injective" is the rank-nullity equivalence "injective iff kernel is zero" combined with the determinant-based root characterisation of [01.01.07]; the present unit is where the two prerequisite threads converge into a single spectral invariant.

- **Inner-product space: orthogonality, Gram-Schmidt, spectral theorem [01.01.09]** — refines the diagonalisability content of this unit for self-adjoint operators on a finite-dimensional inner-product space, producing an orthonormal eigenbasis with real eigenvalues. The spectral theorem is the inner-product-space sharpening of the diagonalisability corollary of this unit, and the load-bearing case of the finite-dim spectral theory that generalises to the unbounded self-adjoint operators of functional analysis.

- **Unbounded self-adjoint operators on Hilbert space [02.11.03]** — the infinite-dimensional generalisation in which the spectrum of a self-adjoint operator becomes a closed subset of $\mathbb R$, decomposed into point, continuous, and (in the non-self-adjoint generalisation) residual spectrum, and the spectral measure replaces the discrete eigenprojection sum. The Stone-von Neumann spectral theorem in [02.11.03] is the functional-analytic incarnation of the finite-dim spectral theorem.

- **Linear ODE systems with constant coefficients [02.06.04]** — the solutions of $\dot x = A x$ for $A \in M_n(\mathbb C)$ are governed by the eigenvalues and Jordan structure of $A$: distinct eigenvalues give $\exp(\lambda_i t)$ modes, and a Jordan block of size $k$ at $\lambda$ adds polynomial-in-$t$ modes $t^j \exp(\lambda t)$ for $j < k$. Stability of the equilibrium $x = 0$ is governed by the location of the eigenvalues relative to the imaginary axis. The eigenvalue concept of this unit is the linear-algebra core of constant-coefficient ODE theory.

- **Discrete and continuous dynamical systems [05.01.01]** — for a discrete dynamical system $x_{n+1} = T x_n$ or a continuous system $\dot x = T x$ near an equilibrium, the eigenvalues of $T$ classify the local dynamics: stable equilibria correspond to all eigenvalues having $|\lambda| < 1$ (discrete) or $\mathrm{Re}\,\lambda < 0$ (continuous), unstable equilibria correspond to at least one eigenvalue outside that region, and bifurcations occur precisely when an eigenvalue crosses the boundary. The eigenvalue concept of this unit is the linear-algebra core of stability theory and bifurcation analysis.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The eigenvalue concept arose in mechanics. Joseph-Louis Lagrange, in *Mécanique analytique* (Paris, 1788), found that the small oscillations of a coupled mechanical system about an equilibrium decompose into independent harmonic modes governed by the roots of an auxiliary algebraic equation — what would now be the characteristic polynomial of the linearised motion. Pierre-Simon Laplace, in *Mécanique céleste* (Paris, 1799–1825, Vol. I, Book II), applied the same technique to the secular variations of planetary orbits, with the roots of the *équation séculaire* governing the long-term stability of the solar system. Augustin-Louis Cauchy's 1829 paper in his own *Exercices de Mathématiques* gave the first systematic treatment, proving in particular that the characteristic equation of a real symmetric matrix has only real roots — the prototype of the spectral theorem [ref: TODO_REF Cauchy, A.-L. — Sur l'équation à l'aide de laquelle on détermine les inégalités séculaires des mouvements des planètes].

Arthur Cayley introduced matrix notation in *A memoir on the theory of matrices* (Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society 148, 1858, 17–37), and stated what is now the Cayley-Hamilton theorem there, with a proof for $n \le 4$ and the remark that the general case would be left to the reader [ref: TODO_REF Cayley, A. — A memoir on the theory of matrices]. Ferdinand Georg Frobenius proved the general case in *Über lineare Substitutionen und bilineare Formen* (J. reine angew. Math. 84, 1878, 1–63), and developed the systematic invariant theory of the characteristic polynomial together with the elementary divisor theorem [ref: TODO_REF Frobenius, F. G. — Über lineare Substitutionen und bilineare Formen]. Camille Jordan's *Traité des substitutions et des équations algébriques* (Gauthier-Villars, Paris, 1870) gave the canonical-form classification that bears his name, in the algebraic-group-theory context of substitutions of variables in polynomial equations [ref: TODO_REF Jordan, C. — Traité des substitutions et des équations algébriques].

The infinite-dimensional extension is the work of David Hilbert, who in *Grundzüge einer allgemeinen Theorie der linearen Integralgleichungen* (Nachrichten von der Gesellschaft der Wissenschaften zu Göttingen, six papers from 1904 to 1910) developed the spectral theory of self-adjoint integral operators with continuous kernel and introduced the term *Spektrum*. The modern Hilbert-space formulation, with the spectral measure of an unbounded self-adjoint operator, is John von Neumann's *Allgemeine Eigenwerttheorie Hermitescher Funktionaloperatoren* (Mathematische Annalen 102, 1929, 49–131), written in support of the mathematical foundations of quantum mechanics: physical observables correspond to self-adjoint operators on a Hilbert space, and their possible measured values are the points of the spectrum [ref: TODO_REF von Neumann, J. — Allgemeine Eigenwerttheorie Hermitescher Funktionaloperatoren]. The English vocabulary *eigenvalue* and *eigenvector* are direct loans from von Neumann's German *Eigenwert* and *Eigenvektor*, themselves shortenings of *Eigenschaftswert* — characteristic value.
