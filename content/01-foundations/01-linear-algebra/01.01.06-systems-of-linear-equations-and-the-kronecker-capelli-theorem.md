## Intuition [Beginner]

A system of linear equations asks several demands of the same unknowns at once. Find numbers $x$, $y$, $z$ so that one combination of them equals 6, another equals 0, and a third equals 4. Each equation pins the unknowns to a flat sheet in space; the solution is wherever all the sheets meet. Three sheets can cross at a single point, share a whole line, or fail to share anything at all.

So a linear system has exactly one of three fates. It can have one answer, infinitely many answers, or no answer. The whole subject is about telling which fate you are in before grinding through the arithmetic, and about reading the answer's shape off the equations themselves.

The deciding quantity is how much genuinely new information each equation carries. If a fourth equation is just the first three added together, it tells you nothing fresh and cannot rule out a solution. If instead it demands something the first three already forbid, the demands clash and no answer survives. The count of independent demands is the rank, and comparing two ranks settles existence.

## Visual [Beginner]

Picture three flat planes floating in space, each one the solution sheet of a single equation. The left panel shows the lucky case: the three planes pierce one another at a single shared point, the unique solution. The middle panel shows three planes meeting along a common line — every point on that line solves all three, so there are infinitely many answers. The right panel shows three planes arranged like the sides of a triangular prism: each pair crosses in a line, but no point lies on all three at once, so there is no solution.

![Three panels of three planes each: left, planes meeting at one point (unique solution); middle, planes meeting along a shared line (infinitely many solutions); right, planes forming a prism with no common point (no solution).](/img/systems-of-linear-equations-and-the-kronecker-capelli-theorem-placeholder.svg)

The picture you land in depends only on how the equations lean against one another, not on the particular numbers on the right-hand side.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Solve the system
$$
x + y + z = 6,\qquad 2x - y + z = 3,\qquad x + 2y - z = 2.
$$

Start by removing $x$ from the second and third equations. Subtract twice the first equation from the second: $(2x - y + z) - 2(x + y + z) = 3 - 12$, which gives $-3y - z = -9$. Subtract the first equation from the third: $(x + 2y - z) - (x + y + z) = 2 - 6$, which gives $y - 2z = -4$.

Now work with the two smaller equations $-3y - z = -9$ and $y - 2z = -4$. From the second, $y = 2z - 4$. Put this into the first: $-3(2z - 4) - z = -9$, so $-6z + 12 - z = -9$, so $-7z = -21$, giving $z = 3$.

Back-substitute. With $z = 3$, the equation $y = 2z - 4$ gives $y = 2$. The first original equation $x + y + z = 6$ gives $x = 6 - 2 - 3 = 1$. Check all three: $1 + 2 + 3 = 6$, $2 - 2 + 3 = 3$, and $1 + 4 - 3 = 2$. Every equation holds.

What this tells us: by peeling off one unknown at a time, three tangled equations collapse into a staircase you can climb back up. The single answer $(1, 2, 3)$ is the one point where all three planes meet.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $K$ be a field. A **system of $m$ linear equations in $n$ unknowns** over $K$ is a list
$$
\sum_{j=1}^{n} a_{ij} x_j = b_i \qquad (i = 1, \ldots, m),
$$
with coefficients $a_{ij} \in K$ and right-hand sides $b_i \in K$. Collecting the coefficients into the **coefficient matrix** $A = (a_{ij}) \in K^{m\times n}$, the unknowns into $\mathbf x = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)^{\mathsf T} \in K^n$, and the right-hand sides into $\mathbf b = (b_1, \ldots, b_m)^{\mathsf T} \in K^m$, the system is the single matrix equation
$$
A\mathbf x = \mathbf b.
$$
The **augmented matrix** is the $m \times (n+1)$ matrix $[A \mid \mathbf b]$ obtained by adjoining $\mathbf b$ as a final column. The system is **consistent** (or **solvable**) if at least one $\mathbf x \in K^n$ satisfies it, and **inconsistent** otherwise. When $\mathbf b = \mathbf 0$ the system $A\mathbf x = \mathbf 0$ is **homogeneous**; the **zero solution** $\mathbf x = \mathbf 0$ is always present, and the substantive question is whether a nonzero solution exists.

Two readings of $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ organise the theory. The **column reading** writes $A\mathbf x = x_1 \mathbf a_1 + \cdots + x_n \mathbf a_n$, where $\mathbf a_j$ is the $j$-th column of $A$; consistency then means $\mathbf b$ is a linear combination of the columns, that is, $\mathbf b$ lies in the **column space** $\operatorname{Col}(A) = \operatorname{im}(A) \subseteq K^m$ [ref: quantum-well Rank of a matrix.md]. The **operator reading** treats $A$ as the linear map $K^n \to K^m$, $\mathbf x \mapsto A\mathbf x$, whose kernel $\ker A = \{\mathbf x : A\mathbf x = \mathbf 0\}$ is the **null space**, the solution set of the homogeneous system [ref: quantum-well Null space.md]. The **rank** of $A$ is $\operatorname{rank}(A) = \dim \operatorname{Col}(A)$, the dimension of the column space, equal also to the dimension of the row space and to the number of pivots in any row-echelon form of $A$ [ref: quantum-well Linear map.md].

A matrix is in **row-echelon form** when each nonzero row begins (reading left to right) with a leading entry strictly to the right of the leading entry of the row above, and all-zero rows sit at the bottom. It is in **reduced row-echelon form** when, additionally, every leading entry equals $1$ and is the only nonzero entry in its column. **Gauss-Jordan elimination** transforms a matrix to reduced row-echelon form using the three **elementary row operations**: swapping two rows, scaling a row by a nonzero element of $K$, and adding a multiple of one row to another. Each operation corresponds to left-multiplication by an invertible **elementary matrix**, so it preserves both the solution set of $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ and the ranks of $A$ and $[A \mid \mathbf b]$. The columns of $A$ containing leading entries are the **pivot columns**; their count is $\operatorname{rank}(A)$. The remaining columns are the **free columns**, and the unknowns they index are the **free variables**.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- Consistency is a property of the pair $(A, \mathbf b)$, not of $A$ alone. The same $A$ can give a solvable system for one $\mathbf b$ and an unsolvable one for another, depending on whether $\mathbf b \in \operatorname{Col}(A)$.
- More equations than unknowns ($m > n$) does not force inconsistency, and fewer ($m < n$) does not force consistency. A tall system can still be solvable if the extra rows are dependent; a short system $0 \cdot x = 1$ in one unknown is already unsolvable.
- "Unique solution" requires $\operatorname{rank}(A) = n$, the number of unknowns — not $\operatorname{rank}(A) = m$. A square system is the only case where these coincide.
- Adjoining $\mathbf b$ can raise the rank by at most $1$, never lower it, since $A$ is a submatrix of $[A \mid \mathbf b]$. So $\operatorname{rank}(A) \le \operatorname{rank}([A \mid \mathbf b]) \le \operatorname{rank}(A) + 1$ always.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Kronecker-Capelli).** Let $A \in K^{m\times n}$ and $\mathbf b \in K^m$. The system $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ is consistent if and only if
$$
\operatorname{rank}(A) = \operatorname{rank}([A \mid \mathbf b]).
$$
When it is consistent, the solution set is the affine set $\mathbf x_p + \ker A$, where $\mathbf x_p$ is any particular solution; its dimension as an affine set is $n - \operatorname{rank}(A)$. In particular the solution is unique exactly when $\operatorname{rank}(A) = n$.
[ref: textbooks-extra Calculus Vol.2 - Multi-Variable Calculus and Linear Algebra with Applications (Tom Apostol).pdf]

**Proof.** Write $\mathbf a_1, \ldots, \mathbf a_n$ for the columns of $A$, so $\operatorname{Col}(A) = \operatorname{span}\{\mathbf a_1, \ldots, \mathbf a_n\}$. The columns of $[A \mid \mathbf b]$ are $\mathbf a_1, \ldots, \mathbf a_n, \mathbf b$, so $\operatorname{Col}([A \mid \mathbf b]) = \operatorname{span}\{\mathbf a_1, \ldots, \mathbf a_n, \mathbf b\} = \operatorname{Col}(A) + \operatorname{span}\{\mathbf b\}$.

Consistency means $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ has a solution, that is, $\mathbf b = x_1\mathbf a_1 + \cdots + x_n \mathbf a_n$ for some scalars $x_j$, which is the statement $\mathbf b \in \operatorname{Col}(A)$. Now $\mathbf b \in \operatorname{Col}(A)$ holds if and only if adjoining $\mathbf b$ to the spanning set does not enlarge the span, that is, $\operatorname{Col}([A \mid \mathbf b]) = \operatorname{Col}(A)$, which holds if and only if these two subspaces have equal dimension, since one contains the other. Taking dimensions, $\operatorname{Col}(A) \subseteq \operatorname{Col}([A\mid\mathbf b])$ with equality of dimension forcing equality of the spaces; and equality of dimension is exactly $\operatorname{rank}(A) = \operatorname{rank}([A\mid\mathbf b])$. The two ranks are therefore equal if and only if the system is consistent. (If $\mathbf b \notin \operatorname{Col}(A)$, then $\mathbf b$ is independent of the columns of $A$, so $\operatorname{rank}([A\mid\mathbf b]) = \operatorname{rank}(A) + 1$.)

For the solution-set structure, suppose the system is consistent and let $\mathbf x_p$ be a particular solution, $A\mathbf x_p = \mathbf b$. If $\mathbf x$ is any solution, then $A(\mathbf x - \mathbf x_p) = A\mathbf x - A\mathbf x_p = \mathbf b - \mathbf b = \mathbf 0$, so $\mathbf x - \mathbf x_p \in \ker A$, giving $\mathbf x \in \mathbf x_p + \ker A$. Conversely, for any $\mathbf h \in \ker A$, $A(\mathbf x_p + \mathbf h) = \mathbf b + \mathbf 0 = \mathbf b$, so $\mathbf x_p + \mathbf h$ is a solution. The solution set is exactly $\mathbf x_p + \ker A$. By rank-nullity applied to $A : K^n \to K^m$ from [01.01.05], $\dim \ker A = n - \operatorname{rank}(A)$, which is the dimension of the affine solution set. The solution is unique precisely when $\ker A = \{\mathbf 0\}$, that is, when $n - \operatorname{rank}(A) = 0$, i.e. $\operatorname{rank}(A) = n$. $\square$

**Corollary (homogeneous and square cases).** The homogeneous system $A\mathbf x = \mathbf 0$ has a nonzero solution if and only if $\operatorname{rank}(A) < n$; in particular this holds whenever $m < n$ (fewer equations than unknowns). A square system ($m = n$) has a unique solution for every $\mathbf b$ if and only if $\operatorname{rank}(A) = n$, equivalently $\det A \ne 0$, equivalently $A$ is invertible.

**Proof.** The homogeneous system is always consistent (the zero solution), so the rank criterion is automatic; the substantive content is the size of $\ker A$, which is nonzero exactly when $n - \operatorname{rank}(A) > 0$, i.e. $\operatorname{rank}(A) < n$. If $m < n$, then $\operatorname{rank}(A) \le m < n$, forcing a nonzero solution. For the square case, $\operatorname{rank}(A) = n$ makes $A : K^n \to K^n$ injective by the kernel computation and hence surjective by rank-nullity from [01.01.05], so $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ has the unique solution $\mathbf x = A^{-1}\mathbf b$ for every $\mathbf b$; the equivalence with $\det A \ne 0$ is the invertibility criterion of [01.01.07]. $\square$

**Bridge.** The rank criterion *builds toward* the coordinate-free first isomorphism theorem of [01.01.05]: "$\mathbf b \in \operatorname{Col}(A)$" is the matrix face of "$\mathbf b \in \operatorname{im}(T)$", and the affine solution set $\mathbf x_p + \ker A$ is a coset of the kernel, the same object that the quotient $K^n/\ker A \cong \operatorname{im}(A)$ packages intrinsically. The same solvability bookkeeping *appears again in* the determinant unit [01.01.07] as Cramer's rule for the square nonsingular case, and *resurfaces in* the spectral and least-squares circle of [01.01.12], where an inconsistent $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ is replaced by the consistent normal equations $A^{\mathsf T} A\mathbf x = A^{\mathsf T}\mathbf b$. It *connects forward to* the Fredholm alternative of functional analysis, where the finite rank comparison becomes an orthogonality condition $\mathbf b \perp \ker A^{*}$ in infinite dimensions.

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

Let $A \in K^{m\times n}$. Prove that the map $A : K^n \to K^m$ is surjective if and only if $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ is consistent for **every** $\mathbf b \in K^m$, and that this holds if and only if $\operatorname{rank}(A) = m$.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Cramer's rule.** Let $A \in K^{n\times n}$ with $\det A \ne 0$ and $\mathbf b \in K^n$. The unique solution of $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ has components
$$
x_i = \frac{\det A_i}{\det A}, \qquad i = 1, \ldots, n,
$$
where $A_i$ is $A$ with its $i$-th column replaced by $\mathbf b$. The rule follows from the adjugate identity $A^{-1} = (\det A)^{-1}\operatorname{adj}(A)$ of [01.01.07]: writing $\mathbf x = A^{-1}\mathbf b$ and expanding the $i$-th component, $\sum_j (\operatorname{adj} A)_{ij} b_j = \sum_j C_{ji} b_j$ is precisely the cofactor expansion of $\det A_i$ along its $i$-th column. Cramer's rule is a closed-form solvability certificate, but as an algorithm it costs $n+1$ determinant evaluations and is, for numerical purposes, dominated by Gauss-Jordan elimination, which solves the same system in $O(n^3)$ field operations. Its value is structural and theoretical: it exhibits the solution as a rational function of the data with denominator $\det A$, which is what makes the solution depend continuously (indeed polynomially after clearing the denominator) on the entries away from the singular locus $\det A = 0$.

**The Fredholm alternative.** The Kronecker-Capelli dichotomy is the finite-dimensional shadow of the **Fredholm alternative**. For a square real system $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$, exactly one of two statements holds: either $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ has a solution for every $\mathbf b$ (the case $\ker A = \{\mathbf 0\}$), or the homogeneous system $A\mathbf x = \mathbf 0$ has a nonzero solution and then $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ is solvable if and only if $\mathbf b$ is orthogonal to every solution of the transposed homogeneous system $A^{\mathsf T}\mathbf y = \mathbf 0$. The orthogonality condition $\mathbf b \perp \ker A^{\mathsf T}$ is the metric refinement of "$\mathbf b \in \operatorname{Col}(A)$", using the identity $\operatorname{Col}(A) = (\ker A^{\mathsf T})^{\perp}$ valid in any finite-dimensional inner-product space. In the infinite-dimensional setting of compact operators on a Hilbert space — Fredholm's 1903 theory of integral equations $(\mathrm{I} - K)\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ — the same alternative governs solvability, with $\ker(\mathrm I - K)$ and $\ker(\mathrm I - K^{*})$ taking the place of the two null spaces, both finite-dimensional and of equal dimension.

**Conditioning.** Solvability is a yes/no question, but the *stability* of the answer under perturbation of the data is governed by the **condition number** $\kappa(A) = \|A\|\,\|A^{-1}\|$ of a square nonsingular $A$. If $A(\mathbf x + \delta\mathbf x) = \mathbf b + \delta\mathbf b$, then the relative error obeys $\|\delta\mathbf x\|/\|\mathbf x\| \le \kappa(A)\,\|\delta\mathbf b\|/\|\mathbf b\|$. A system can be exactly consistent yet practically unsolvable when $\kappa(A)$ is enormous: the column space is technically full but its columns are nearly dependent, so $\mathbf b$ sits near the boundary of $\operatorname{Col}(A)$ and tiny changes in $\mathbf b$ swing the solution wildly. This is the quantitative residue of the qualitative rank jump: rank deficiency is the limit $\kappa(A) = \infty$, and near-deficiency is large but finite $\kappa(A)$.

**Synthesis.** Four strands meet at the rank criterion. The first is *membership*: solvability of $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ is the single geometric fact $\mathbf b \in \operatorname{Col}(A)$, detected by the rank comparison because adjoining a vector to a spanning set raises the dimension exactly when the vector is external to the span. The second is *affine structure*: a consistent system's solution set is a coset of the kernel, so the homogeneous problem controls the multiplicity and the inhomogeneous data controls only the offset. The third is *duality*: the column-space condition has an orthogonal-complement form $\mathbf b \perp \ker A^{\mathsf T}$, the Fredholm alternative, which survives into the infinite-dimensional theory of integral equations where determinants no longer exist. The fourth is *quantification*: rank is a discrete invariant, but its failure is approached continuously through the condition number, which measures how close a solvable system sits to the inconsistent boundary. Together they recast the elementary question "does this system have a solution?" as a statement about one subspace containing one vector, and every later refinement — Cramer, Fredholm, least squares, conditioning — is a reading of that single containment.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (rank is well-defined: row rank equals column rank).** For any $A \in K^{m\times n}$, the dimension of the row space equals the dimension of the column space. Consequently the pivot count in any row-echelon form of $A$ is an invariant of $A$, justifying the notation $\operatorname{rank}(A)$ used throughout.

**Proof.** Let $r$ be the column rank, $\dim \operatorname{Col}(A)$, and let $\mathbf c_1, \ldots, \mathbf c_r \in K^m$ be a basis of the column space. Form the matrix $C \in K^{m\times r}$ with these as columns. Every column $\mathbf a_j$ of $A$ is a linear combination $\mathbf a_j = \sum_{k=1}^{r} c_{j}^{(k)} \mathbf c_k$, and collecting the coefficients into $R \in K^{r\times n}$ with $(R)_{kj} = c_j^{(k)}$ gives the factorisation $A = CR$. Reading $A = CR$ by rows, each row of $A$ is a linear combination of the $r$ rows of $R$, so the row space of $A$ is spanned by the $r$ rows of $R$ and has dimension at most $r$; thus the row rank is at most the column rank. Applying the same argument to $A^{\mathsf T}$, whose rows are the columns of $A$ and vice versa, the column rank is at most the row rank. The two are equal. Since elementary row operations preserve the row space (each new row is a combination of old rows, reversibly), they preserve its dimension, and in row-echelon form the nonzero rows are linearly independent and number exactly the pivots; so the pivot count equals the row rank, hence the common rank. $\square$

**Proposition (uniqueness of reduced row-echelon form).** Every matrix $A \in K^{m\times n}$ is row-equivalent to exactly one matrix in reduced row-echelon form. Hence the set of pivot columns, the rank, and the parametrisation of $\ker A$ by free variables are intrinsic to $A$, not artifacts of the elimination path.

**Proof.** Existence is the termination of Gauss-Jordan elimination: at each stage choose the leftmost column with a nonzero entry on or below the current pivot row, swap it up, scale it to a leading $1$, and clear the rest of its column; the process strictly advances the pivot column and pivot row and halts. For uniqueness, suppose $B$ and $B'$ are reduced row-echelon forms of $A$, so $B = EA$ and $B' = E'A$ for products $E, E'$ of elementary matrices; then $B' = E'E^{-1}B = MB$ with $M = E'E^{-1}$ invertible, and $B, B'$ have the same null space (row operations preserve solution sets), namely $\ker A$. Two reduced row-echelon matrices with the same null space agree: the pivot columns are determined as those columns not expressible as a combination of earlier columns — equivalently the columns where a new dependency among $\ker A$ relations is resolved — and this characterisation depends only on $\ker A$. Concretely, column $j$ is a pivot column if and only if the $j$-th standard relation is not forced by the relations among columns $1, \ldots, j-1$ that hold for all vectors of $\ker A$; and once the pivot columns coincide, the entries of a reduced row-echelon matrix in the free columns are exactly the coefficients expressing each free column in the basis of preceding pivot columns, again determined by $\ker A$ alone. Therefore $B = B'$. $\square$

**Proposition (solution-count trichotomy over a field).** Over a field $K$, a system $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ has either no solution, exactly one solution, or, when $K$ is infinite, infinitely many; when $K$ is finite of order $q$, a consistent system with $d = n - \operatorname{rank}(A)$ free variables has exactly $q^{d}$ solutions.

**Proof.** If inconsistent, there are no solutions. If consistent, the solution set is $\mathbf x_p + \ker A$ by the Kronecker-Capelli theorem, an affine copy of the vector space $\ker A$, so its cardinality equals $|\ker A|$. If $\operatorname{rank}(A) = n$, then $\ker A = \{\mathbf 0\}$ and the solution is unique. If $\operatorname{rank}(A) = r < n$, then $\ker A$ is a $K$-vector space of dimension $d = n - r \ge 1$, hence isomorphic to $K^{d}$ as a set; over an infinite field $K^d$ is infinite, and over a finite field of order $q$ it has $q^{d}$ elements. Translating by $\mathbf x_p$ does not change the count, so the consistent system has $q^d$ solutions in the finite case and infinitely many in the infinite case. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Linear transformation: kernel, image, rank-nullity [01.01.05]** — supplies the engine of this unit. The Kronecker-Capelli criterion is "$\mathbf b \in \operatorname{im}(A)$" and the solution-set dimension $n - \operatorname{rank}(A)$ is the nullity from rank-nullity; the unit specialises the abstract linear-map theory to the concrete matrix-and-augmented-matrix calculus, with the coefficient matrix $A$ read as the map $K^n \to K^m$.

- **Determinant [01.01.07]** — the square nonsingular case factors through the determinant: $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ has a unique solution for every $\mathbf b$ iff $\det A \ne 0$, and Cramer's rule expresses each coordinate as a ratio of determinants $\det A_i / \det A$. The determinant is the scalar gate that detects when the rank criterion is automatically satisfied for all right-hand sides at once.

- **Singular value decomposition [01.01.12]** — when $A\mathbf x = \mathbf b$ is inconsistent, the least-squares replacement minimises $\|A\mathbf x - \mathbf b\|$, solved by the normal equations $A^{\mathsf T} A \mathbf x = A^{\mathsf T}\mathbf b$ and stably computed through the SVD and the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse. The SVD also exposes the condition number $\kappa(A) = \sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$ that governs how a solvable system degrades toward the inconsistent boundary.

- **Eigenvalue and eigenvector [01.01.08]** — the eigenvalue equation $(A - \lambda I)\mathbf x = \mathbf 0$ is a homogeneous linear system, and $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue precisely when this system has a nonzero solution, i.e. when $\operatorname{rank}(A - \lambda I) < n$. The Kronecker-Capelli homogeneous criterion is the existence half of the spectral theory built in that unit.

- **Linear equations and the line [00.03.01]** — the two-equation, two-unknown case is the classification of how two lines in the plane meet, with the determinant $a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1$ as the unique-intersection test. The present unit generalises that planar classification to $m$ equations in $n$ unknowns and replaces the single determinant by the pair of ranks.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Systematic elimination predates its abstract justification by millennia: the Chinese *Jiuzhang Suanshu* (*Nine Chapters on the Mathematical Art*, compiled by the first century CE) solves systems by an array-reduction procedure essentially identical to what is now called Gaussian elimination. Carl Friedrich Gauss formalised the elimination method in his astronomical least-squares work of the early nineteenth century, and Wilhelm Jordan's textbook treatment in the 1880s fixed the reduced form that bears their joint name. The rank-based consistency criterion is the contribution of the 1870s. Eugène Rouché stated the solvability condition in terms of the vanishing of certain determinants in *Sur la discussion des équations du premier degré*, Comptes Rendus 81 (1875), 1050–1052 [ref: TODO_REF Rouché, E. — Sur la discussion des équations du premier degré]. Ferdinand Georg Frobenius gave the modern matrix-rank framing shortly after, which is why the result is called the Rouché-Frobenius theorem in much of the literature. The name "Kronecker-Capelli" honours Leopold Kronecker's rank formulation and Alfredo Capelli's textbook codification of the criterion in his *Lezioni di algebra complementare* (Naples, 1892) [ref: TODO_REF Capelli, A. — Lezioni di algebra complementare]. The several names of one theorem record a half-century in which the determinant-heavy nineteenth-century viewpoint was gradually replaced by the dimension-and-rank viewpoint of twentieth-century linear algebra; Georgi Shilov's *Linear Algebra* (1971 English translation) presents the criterion in the mature rank-theoretic form adopted here [ref: TODO_REF Shilov, G. E. — Linear Algebra (Dover, 1977 reprint of the 1971 Prentice-Hall translation)].
