## Intuition [Beginner]

A single symmetric matrix has a set of perpendicular axes along which it acts by pure stretching. The spectral theorem [01.01.13] finds them. But many problems in physics and statistics come with two such matrices at once, and the two sets of axes usually point in different directions. The question of this unit is whether one clever choice of coordinates can straighten out both matrices together.

The surprising answer is yes, as long as one of the two matrices measures a genuine size, meaning it is positive whenever you feed it a nonzero vector. That positive matrix gets used as a new ruler. Once you agree to measure lengths and angles with that ruler instead of the ordinary one, the second matrix turns out to have perpendicular stretch axes in the new sense, and those very axes diagonalise both matrices at the same time.

A vibrating system makes this concrete. Two masses joined by springs have a kinetic-energy matrix and a potential-energy matrix. Diagonalising both at once finds the special motions, called normal modes, in which the whole system oscillates at a single clean frequency, with every part moving in step.

## Visual [Beginner]

Two quadratic forms in the plane draw two ellipses centred at the origin. The first form, the positive-definite one, draws a reference ellipse; the second draws another ellipse, tilted differently. Ordinary perpendicular axes line up with at most one of them. The simultaneous-diagonalisation directions are the axes that look like the principal axes of both ellipses once you stretch the picture so the reference ellipse becomes a perfect circle.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take two symmetric matrices in the plane,

$$
A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad B = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Here $B$ is positive: feeding it any nonzero vector $(x, y)$ gives $2x^2 + y^2 > 0$. The generalised eigenvalues solve $\det(A - \lambda B) = 0$.

Step 1. Form $A - \lambda B = \begin{pmatrix} 3 - 2\lambda & 1 \\ 1 & 3 - \lambda \end{pmatrix}$.

Step 2. Set its determinant to zero: $(3 - 2\lambda)(3 - \lambda) - 1 = 0$.

Step 3. Multiply out: $9 - 3\lambda - 6\lambda + 2\lambda^2 - 1 = 2\lambda^2 - 9\lambda + 8 = 0$.

Step 4. Solve the quadratic: $\lambda = (9 \pm \sqrt{81 - 64})/4 = (9 \pm \sqrt{17})/4$. Numerically $\lambda_1 \approx 3.28$ and $\lambda_2 \approx 1.22$.

Step 5. Find the first generalised eigenvector from $A v = \lambda_1 B v$, that is $(A - \lambda_1 B) v = 0$. The top row gives $(3 - 2\lambda_1) x + y = 0$, so $y = (2\lambda_1 - 3) x \approx 3.56 x$. A direction is $v_1 \approx (1, 3.56)$.

Step 6. Check $B$-orthogonality of the two eigenvectors. The second eigenvector is $v_2 \approx (1, -0.56)$. Then $v_1^\top B v_2 = 2(1)(1) + (3.56)(-0.56) \approx 2 - 1.99 \approx 0$, confirming the eigenvectors are perpendicular when measured by $B$.

What this tells us: the two generalised eigenvalues are the values of $\lambda$ where $A$ and $\lambda B$ agree as forms in some direction, and the two eigenvectors are perpendicular in the ruler set by $B$, not in the ordinary ruler. Those directions are exactly the ones that diagonalise both forms at once.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $V$ be a finite-dimensional real vector space and let $A, B$ be **real symmetric bilinear forms** on $V$ in the sense of [01.01.15], with Gram matrices (also written $A, B$) in a fixed basis. Assume $B$ is **positive-definite**, written $B \succ 0$: $v^\top B v > 0$ for every $v \neq 0$. Symmetry means $A = A^\top$ and $B = B^\top$. All forms in this unit are real symmetric unless stated otherwise.

Because $B \succ 0$, the formula

$$
\langle u, v \rangle_B = u^\top B v
$$

defines a genuine inner product on $V$, the **$B$-inner product**, with norm $\|v\|_B = \sqrt{v^\top B v}$. Two vectors are **$B$-orthogonal** when $\langle u, v \rangle_B = 0$; a basis is **$B$-orthonormal** when its Gram matrix against $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_B$ is the identity. This makes $(V, \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_B)$ a finite-dimensional inner-product space in the sense of [01.01.09].

**Definition (generalised eigenvalue problem).** A scalar $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$ and a vector $v \neq 0$ form a **generalised eigenpair** of the ordered pair $(A, B)$ when

$$
A v = \lambda B v.
$$

The scalar $\lambda$ is a **generalised eigenvalue** and $v$ a **generalised eigenvector**. Equivalently $\lambda$ is a root of the **generalised characteristic polynomial**

$$
p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda B),
$$

and $v \in \ker(A - \lambda B)$. The formal object $A - \lambda B$, viewed as a one-parameter family of matrices, is the **matrix pencil** of the pair; it is **regular** when $p$ is not identically zero, which holds automatically here because $\det(A - \lambda B)$ has leading term $(-\lambda)^n \det B \neq 0$.

**Definition (simultaneous diagonalisation by congruence).** The pair $(A, B)$ is **simultaneously diagonalisable by congruence** when there is an invertible $P$ with

$$
P^\top B P = I, \qquad P^\top A P = \Lambda = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n).
$$

The transformation $X \mapsto P^\top X P$ is **congruence**, the change-of-variables law for bilinear forms [01.01.17], and it is the correct equivalence here: a substitution $v = P y$ sends the form $v^\top X v$ to $y^\top (P^\top X P) y$. Congruence is not similarity ($P^{-1} X P$); the two coincide only when $P$ is orthogonal, and the diagonalising $P$ below is generally not orthogonal.

**Definition (generalised Rayleigh quotient).** For $v \neq 0$,

$$
\rho(v) = \frac{v^\top A v}{v^\top B v}
$$

is the **generalised Rayleigh quotient** of $(A, B)$. It is well defined because $B \succ 0$ keeps the denominator positive, and it reduces to the ordinary Rayleigh quotient of [01.01.14] when $B = I$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- Positive-definiteness of $B$, not of $A$, is what the construction needs. The matrix $A$ may be indefinite; its generalised eigenvalues are then a mix of positive and negative reals, and the simultaneous diagonalisation still exists.
- The diagonalising $P$ is generally **not** orthogonal, so $P^\top A P$ is congruent but not similar to $A$. Reading off "eigenvalues of $A$" from $\Lambda$ is the error: the $\lambda_k$ are eigenvalues of the pencil, equivalently of $B^{-1} A$, not of $A$.
- If $B$ is merely positive-*semi*definite or indefinite, the pencil may be defective or singular. The pair $A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, $B = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$ has $\det(A - \lambda B) = \lambda^2 + 1$, with no real generalised eigenvalue; no real congruence diagonalises both.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (simultaneous diagonalisation of two forms, one definite; Shilov Ch. 10 [ref: TODO_REF]; Horn-Johnson §4.5 [ref: TODO_REF]).** *Let $A, B$ be real symmetric forms on an $n$-dimensional real vector space with $B \succ 0$. Then there is an invertible $P$ with*

$$
P^\top B P = I, \qquad P^\top A P = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n),
$$

*the columns of $P$ are $B$-orthonormal generalised eigenvectors, and the diagonal entries $\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n$ are the generalised eigenvalues, that is the roots of $\det(A - \lambda B) = 0$. All $\lambda_k$ are real.*

**Proof.** Equip $V$ with the $B$-inner product $\langle u, v \rangle_B = u^\top B v$, which is a genuine inner product because $B \succ 0$. Consider the linear operator $T = B^{-1} A$. It is **self-adjoint for $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_B$**: for all $u, v$,

$$
\langle T u, v \rangle_B = (B^{-1} A u)^\top B v = u^\top A^\top B^{-\top} B v = u^\top A v,
$$

using $A^\top = A$ and $B^{-\top} B = I$, and symmetrically $\langle u, T v \rangle_B = u^\top B (B^{-1} A v) = u^\top A v$. The two are equal, so $T = T^*$ in the $B$-inner product.

By the spectral theorem [01.01.13] applied to the self-adjoint $T$ on the inner-product space $(V, \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_B)$, there is a $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_B$-orthonormal basis $p_1, \ldots, p_n$ of eigenvectors, $T p_k = \lambda_k p_k$ with $\lambda_k \in \mathbb{R}$. The eigenvalue equation $B^{-1} A p_k = \lambda_k p_k$ rearranges to $A p_k = \lambda_k B p_k$, so each $p_k$ is a generalised eigenvector of $(A, B)$ with generalised eigenvalue $\lambda_k$.

Assemble the eigenvectors as the columns of $P = [\,p_1 \mid \cdots \mid p_n\,]$. The $B$-orthonormality $\langle p_i, p_j \rangle_B = p_i^\top B p_j = \delta_{ij}$ is exactly $P^\top B P = I$. For the other form,

$$
(P^\top A P)_{ij} = p_i^\top A p_j = p_i^\top B (B^{-1} A p_j) = p_i^\top B (\lambda_j p_j) = \lambda_j\, p_i^\top B p_j = \lambda_j \delta_{ij},
$$

so $P^\top A P = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$. The matrix $P$ is invertible because its columns are $B$-orthonormal, hence linearly independent (a dependence $\sum c_k p_k = 0$ paired in $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_B$ against $p_j$ forces $c_j = 0$).

Finally, the $\lambda_k$ are the generalised eigenvalues: from $P^\top B P = I$ and $P^\top A P = \Lambda$ one gets $A = P^{-\top} \Lambda P^{-1}$ and $B = P^{-\top} P^{-1}$, so

$$
\det(A - \lambda B) = \det\!\big(P^{-\top}(\Lambda - \lambda I) P^{-1}\big) = \det(P)^{-2}\, \det(\Lambda - \lambda I) = \det(P)^{-2} \prod_k (\lambda_k - \lambda).
$$

The roots are precisely $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$, all real, and ordering the eigenbasis by decreasing eigenvalue gives $\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n$. $\square$

**Bridge.** This theorem builds toward the small-oscillation theory of the Advanced results, where $A$ is the stiffness form, $B$ the mass form, and the generalised eigenpairs become normal modes with frequencies $\omega_k^2 = \lambda_k$; the same congruence reduction appears again in [01.01.15] as the metric-signature normalisation that $B$-orthonormalisation performs, since $P^\top B P = I$ is Sylvester's positive-definite case made explicit. The foundational reason the construction works is that a positive-definite $B$ is an inner product in disguise, so $B^{-1} A$ becomes self-adjoint and the spectral theorem [01.01.13] applies verbatim; this is exactly the move that converts a two-form problem into a one-operator problem. The generalised Rayleigh quotient $\rho(v) = (v^\top A v)/(v^\top B v)$ generalises the ordinary Rayleigh quotient of [01.01.14], with its critical values the $\lambda_k$. Putting these together, simultaneous diagonalisation is the statement that one congruence rounds out $B$ to the identity and squares up $A$ to a diagonal at the same time.

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the generalised eigenvalues of $(A, B)$ with $B \succ 0$ are exactly the ordinary eigenvalues of the symmetric matrix $C = L^{-1} A L^{-\top}$, where $B = L L^\top$ is a Cholesky factorisation.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute $B = L L^\top$ into $A v = \lambda B v$ and set $w = L^\top v$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $B = L L^\top$ with $L$ invertible. Starting from $A v = \lambda B v = \lambda L L^\top v$, multiply on the left by $L^{-1}$: $L^{-1} A v = \lambda L^\top v$. Set $w = L^\top v$, so $v = L^{-\top} w$ and $L^{-1} A L^{-\top} w = \lambda w$, that is $C w = \lambda w$ with $C = L^{-1} A L^{-\top}$. Since $A = A^\top$, $C^\top = L^{-1} A^\top L^{-\top} = C$, so $C$ is symmetric and its eigenvalues are real. The map $v \mapsto w = L^\top v$ is a bijection, so generalised eigenpairs of $(A,B)$ correspond one-to-one with eigenpairs of $C$. Rubric: full credit for the substitution, the symmetry of $C$, and the eigenvalue correspondence.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that generalised eigenvectors for distinct generalised eigenvalues are $B$-orthogonal: if $A u = \mu B u$ and $A v = \nu B v$ with $\mu \neq \nu$, then $u^\top B v = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $u^\top A v$ two ways, using each eigen-equation and the symmetry of $A$ and $B$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

From $A v = \nu B v$, $u^\top A v = \nu\, u^\top B v$. From $A u = \mu B u$ and symmetry, $u^\top A v = (A u)^\top v = \mu (B u)^\top v = \mu\, u^\top B v$ since $B = B^\top$. Subtracting, $(\mu - \nu)\, u^\top B v = 0$. As $\mu \neq \nu$, $u^\top B v = 0$, so $u$ and $v$ are $B$-orthogonal. Rubric: full credit for the two evaluations of $u^\top A v$ and the cancellation.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).**

For $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = I$, the generalised problem reduces to the ordinary one. Compute the largest generalised eigenvalue.

With $B = I$ the equation is $A v = \lambda v$. Factor the characteristic polynomial of $A$.

**$3$.** With $B = I$, $\det(A - \lambda I) = (2 - \lambda)^2 - 1 = (\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 3)$, so the eigenvalues are $1$ and $3$, and the largest is $3$. The generalised Rayleigh quotient becomes the ordinary one. Rubric: full credit for $3$.

</details>

Substitute $w = L^\top v$ with $B = L L^\top$ and check that $\rho(v)$ becomes the ordinary Rayleigh quotient of $C = L^{-1} A L^{-\top}$ at $w$, then invoke Courant-Fischer for $C$.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Factor $B = L L^\top$ and set $w = L^\top v$, a bijection of $V$. Then $v^\top B v = w^\top w$ and $v^\top A v = w^\top L^{-1} A L^{-\top} w = w^\top C w$ with $C = L^{-1} A L^{-\top}$ symmetric. So $\rho(v) = (w^\top C w)/(w^\top w) = R_C(w)$, the ordinary Rayleigh quotient of $C$. The map $v \mapsto w$ carries each $k$-dimensional subspace $S$ to a $k$-dimensional subspace $S' = L^\top S$, bijectively, and $\min_{0 \neq v \in S}\rho(v) = \min_{0 \neq w \in S'} R_C(w)$. Taking the maximum over $k$-dimensional subspaces on both sides and applying Courant-Fischer for the symmetric $C$,
$$
\max_{\dim S = k}\min_{0 \neq v \in S}\rho(v) = \max_{\dim S' = k}\min_{0 \neq w \in S'} R_C(w) = \lambda_k(C) = \lambda_k,
$$
since $\lambda_k(C)$ are the generalised eigenvalues by Exercise 3. Rubric: full credit for the congruence-to-orthonormal substitution, the subspace bijection, and the appeal to Courant-Fischer.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Give an explicit pair $(A, B)$ of real symmetric $2 \times 2$ matrices, with $B$ indefinite, that is **not** simultaneously diagonalisable by any real congruence, and justify the failure through the pencil $A - \lambda B$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Make $\det(A - \lambda B)$ a quadratic with no real roots, so there is no real generalised eigenvalue to diagonalise toward.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Take $A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $B = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$, both symmetric with $B$ indefinite. Then
$$
A - \lambda B = \begin{pmatrix} -\lambda & 1 \\ 1 & \lambda \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \det(A - \lambda B) = -\lambda^2 - 1 = -(\lambda^2 + 1),
$$
whose roots are $\pm i$, not real. If a real $P$ gave $P^\top B P = D_1$ and $P^\top A P = D_2$ both diagonal, the pencil $A - \lambda B = P^{-\top}(D_2 - \lambda D_1)P^{-1}$ would have real roots (the ratios of diagonal entries of $D_2$ and $D_1$), contradicting the complex roots above. So no real congruence simultaneously diagonalises this pair; the regular pencil has complex elementary divisors and falls under the Weierstrass classification rather than the definite-$B$ theorem. Rubric: full credit for a valid pair, the determinant computation, and the contradiction argument.

</details>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem (small oscillations; Lagrange 1788 [ref: TODO_REF]).** *Let a mechanical system with generalised coordinates $q \in \mathbb{R}^n$ have kinetic energy $T = \tfrac12 \dot q^\top B \dot q$ and potential energy $V = \tfrac12 q^\top A q$ near a stable equilibrium, with $B \succ 0$ the mass form and $A = A^\top$ the stiffness form. The linearised equations of motion $B \ddot q + A q = 0$ decouple in the simultaneous-diagonalisation coordinates $q = P y$ into $n$ independent harmonic oscillators*

$$
\ddot y_k + \lambda_k y_k = 0, \qquad k = 1, \ldots, n,
$$

*where the $\lambda_k$ are the generalised eigenvalues of $(A, B)$. Each $y_k$ is a normal coordinate; the $k$-th normal mode oscillates at angular frequency $\omega_k = \sqrt{\lambda_k}$ when $\lambda_k > 0$, and the equilibrium is stable exactly when every $\lambda_k > 0$, that is when $A \succ 0$.*

The substitution $q = P y$ with $P^\top B P = I$, $P^\top A P = \Lambda$ sends $T$ to $\tfrac12 \dot y^\top \dot y$ and $V$ to $\tfrac12 y^\top \Lambda y = \tfrac12 \sum_k \lambda_k y_k^2$, so the Lagrangian separates and the Euler-Lagrange equations become the decoupled set above. This is the mechanical content of simultaneous diagonalisation: the normal modes are the generalised eigenvectors, and the normal frequencies squared are the generalised eigenvalues.

**Theorem (generalised Rayleigh quotient and min-max; cf. [01.01.14]).** *With $\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n$ the generalised eigenvalues of $(A, B)$, $B \succ 0$, the generalised Rayleigh quotient $\rho(v) = (v^\top A v)/(v^\top B v)$ has range $[\lambda_n, \lambda_1]$ and*

$$
\lambda_k = \max_{\dim S = k}\ \min_{0 \neq v \in S} \rho(v) = \min_{\dim S = n-k+1}\ \max_{0 \neq v \in S} \rho(v).
$$

*The extremes $\lambda_1 = \max_v \rho(v)$ and $\lambda_n = \min_v \rho(v)$ are attained on the corresponding generalised eigenvectors.*

The Cholesky substitution $w = L^\top v$ identifies $\rho$ with the ordinary Rayleigh quotient of $C = L^{-1} A L^{-\top}$, so every result of [01.01.14] transports: the critical points of $\rho$ are the generalised eigenvectors, and the recursive deflation $\lambda_{k+1} = \max_{v\, \perp_B\, p_1, \ldots, p_k} \rho(v)$ uses $B$-orthogonality rather than ordinary orthogonality.

**Theorem (Weierstrass-Kronecker canonical form of a pencil; Weierstrass 1868 [ref: TODO_REF]; Kronecker 1890 [ref: TODO_REF]).** *Let $A - \lambda B$ be a pencil of real (or complex) symmetric matrices. If the pencil is **regular** ($\det(A - \lambda B) \not\equiv 0$), it is equivalent under strict congruence to a direct sum of blocks indexed by its **elementary divisors** $(\lambda - \lambda_i)^{m_i}$ and infinite elementary divisors $\mu^{m}$ (poles at $\lambda = \infty$, present when $B$ is singular). If the pencil is **singular** ($\det(A - \lambda B) \equiv 0$), additional **Kronecker minimal-index** blocks $L_\varepsilon, L_\eta^\top$ appear, encoding the row and column minimal indices of the singular part.*

When $B \succ 0$ the pencil is regular with $n$ finite linear elementary divisors $(\lambda - \lambda_i)$, no infinite divisors, and no minimal indices, which is the diagonalisable case of the definite theorem. The general theory is what governs the failure modes: a defective regular pencil (repeated $\lambda_i$ with $m_i > 1$) is not diagonalisable, and a singular pencil arises when $A$ and $B$ share a common null direction. The QZ algorithm of Moler and Stewart computes the generalised Schur form that realises this classification numerically.

**Theorem (Williamson's symplectic normal form; pointer).** *Let $A \succ 0$ be a positive-definite real symmetric $2n \times 2n$ matrix and $J = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & I_n \\ -I_n & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ the standard symplectic form. There is a symplectic $S$ (meaning $S^\top J S = J$) with*

$$
S^\top A S = \begin{pmatrix} D & 0 \\ 0 & D \end{pmatrix}, \qquad D = \operatorname{diag}(d_1, \ldots, d_n),\ d_k > 0,
$$

*and the $d_k$, the **symplectic eigenvalues**, are the positive parts of the eigenvalues of $i J A$.*

This is the simultaneous-normal-form theorem when the second structure is a symplectic form rather than a positive-definite form: instead of congruence by an arbitrary $B$-orthonormal $P$, the allowed transformations are symplectic, and the invariants are the symplectic eigenvalues. Williamson's theorem is the linear-algebra backbone of Gaussian quantum information and the metaplectic representation, exactly as the definite case underpins classical normal modes.

**Synthesis.** Simultaneous diagonalisation of two real symmetric forms, one positive-definite, is the spectral theorem [01.01.13] read in the inner product that the definite form supplies. A positive-definite $B$ is an inner product $\langle u, v \rangle_B = u^\top B v$; in that inner product the operator $B^{-1} A$ is self-adjoint, so it has a $B$-orthonormal eigenbasis, and the matrix $P$ of that basis achieves $P^\top B P = I$ and $P^\top A P = \Lambda$ at once. The diagonal entries are the generalised eigenvalues, the roots of $\det(A - \lambda B)$, equivalently the ordinary eigenvalues of the Cholesky-reduced $C = L^{-1} A L^{-\top}$, and the generalised Rayleigh quotient $\rho$ carries the entire Courant-Fischer min-max apparatus of [01.01.14] across the substitution $w = L^\top v$. The transformation is congruence, not similarity, which is exactly why the $\lambda_k$ are pencil invariants rather than eigenvalues of $A$, and why Sylvester's law of inertia rather than the characteristic polynomial of $A$ governs the signs.

Putting these together, the mechanical reading is Lagrange's normal-mode decomposition, with $A$ the stiffness, $B$ the mass, normal modes the generalised eigenvectors, and normal frequencies $\omega_k^2 = \lambda_k$; the definiteness of $B$ is the kinetic-energy positivity that makes the theory work, and its loss pushes the problem into the Weierstrass-Kronecker classification of general pencils. The same template, with the second structure a symplectic form, is Williamson's theorem and its symplectic eigenvalues. Across all of these the central insight is that one congruence rounds one form to the identity and squares the other to a diagonal, converting a coupled two-form problem into an uncoupled spectrum.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (existence and uniqueness up to $B$-orthogonal mixing).** *Let $A, B$ be real symmetric with $B \succ 0$. A simultaneous congruence diagonalisation $P^\top B P = I$, $P^\top A P = \Lambda$ exists; the diagonal $\Lambda$ is unique up to permutation, and within each generalised eigenspace the choice of $B$-orthonormal eigenvectors is free up to a $B$-orthogonal transformation.*

**Proof.** Existence is the Key theorem: $T = B^{-1} A$ is self-adjoint for $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle_B$, hence has a $B$-orthonormal eigenbasis, whose matrix $P$ satisfies the two identities. For the diagonal's uniqueness, suppose $P_1, P_2$ both diagonalise the pair, giving $\Lambda_1, \Lambda_2$. From $P_i^\top B P_i = I$ and $P_i^\top A P_i = \Lambda_i$, the pencil factors as $A - \lambda B = P_i^{-\top}(\Lambda_i - \lambda I)P_i^{-1}$, so $\det(A - \lambda B) = \det(P_i)^{-2}\prod_k(\lambda_k^{(i)} - \lambda)$. The roots of $\det(A - \lambda B)$ are basis-independent, so the multiset $\{\lambda_k^{(1)}\}$ equals $\{\lambda_k^{(2)}\}$, giving $\Lambda_1 = \Lambda_2$ up to permutation.

For the eigenvector freedom, fix a generalised eigenvalue $\mu$ with generalised eigenspace $E_\mu = \ker(A - \mu B)$. If $\{p_j\}$ and $\{p'_j\}$ are two $B$-orthonormal bases of $E_\mu$, the change-of-basis matrix $Q$ between them satisfies $Q^\top Q = I$ because both are $B$-orthonormal: $\delta_{ij} = \langle p'_i, p'_j\rangle_B = \sum_{k,l} Q_{ki} Q_{lj}\langle p_k, p_l\rangle_B = (Q^\top Q)_{ij}$. So $Q$ is orthogonal, and the choice within $E_\mu$ is exactly a $B$-orthogonal mixing. $\square$

**Proposition (inertia of the pencil and signs of the generalised eigenvalues).** *Let $A, B$ be real symmetric with $B \succ 0$, and let $\Lambda = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$ be the diagonal of generalised eigenvalues. The number of positive, negative, and zero $\lambda_k$ equals the inertia $(n_+, n_-, n_0)$ of $A$, the Sylvester signature of the form $A$ itself.*

**Proof.** By the Key theorem there is an invertible $P$ with $A = P^{-\top}\Lambda P^{-1}$, so $A$ and $\Lambda$ are congruent. Sylvester's law of inertia [ref: TODO_REF Sylvester 1852] states that the numbers of positive, negative, and zero diagonal entries of a real symmetric matrix are invariant under real congruence. Applying it to the congruence $A \cong \Lambda$, the inertia of $A$ equals the inertia of $\Lambda$, which counts the signs of the $\lambda_k$. In particular the equilibrium of the small-oscillation system is stable ($A \succ 0$) precisely when all $\lambda_k > 0$. $\square$

**Proposition (positivity of $B^{-1}A$'s eigenvalues forces simultaneous definiteness).** *If $A, B$ are real symmetric with $B \succ 0$, then $A \succ 0$ if and only if every generalised eigenvalue $\lambda_k > 0$, and $A \succeq 0$ if and only if every $\lambda_k \geq 0$.*

**Proof.** Diagonalise simultaneously, $A = P^{-\top}\Lambda P^{-1}$, $B = P^{-\top}P^{-1}$. For $v \neq 0$, set $y = P^{-1} v \neq 0$; then $v^\top A v = y^\top \Lambda y = \sum_k \lambda_k y_k^2$ and $v^\top B v = \sum_k y_k^2 > 0$. If all $\lambda_k > 0$, then $v^\top A v = \sum_k \lambda_k y_k^2 > 0$ for every $v \neq 0$, so $A \succ 0$. Conversely, if some $\lambda_j \leq 0$, take $v = P e_j$ (so $y = e_j$); then $v^\top A v = \lambda_j \leq 0$, so $A$ is not positive-definite. The semidefinite statement follows by replacing the strict inequalities throughout. $\square$

**Proposition (Cholesky reduction realises the diagonalising congruence).** *Let $B = L L^\top$ be the Cholesky factorisation ($L$ lower-triangular, invertible) and let $C = L^{-1} A L^{-\top}$, a real symmetric matrix with orthogonal eigendecomposition $C = U \Lambda U^\top$, $U^\top U = I$. Then $P = L^{-\top} U$ satisfies $P^\top B P = I$ and $P^\top A P = \Lambda$.*

**Proof.** Compute the $B$-form: $P^\top B P = U^\top L^{-1} (L L^\top) L^{-\top} U = U^\top (L^{-1} L)(L^\top L^{-\top}) U = U^\top U = I$. Compute the $A$-form: $P^\top A P = U^\top L^{-1} A L^{-\top} U = U^\top C U = U^\top (U \Lambda U^\top) U = \Lambda$, using $U^\top U = I$ twice. The eigenvalues in $\Lambda$ are the eigenvalues of $C$, which by the substitution $w = L^\top v$ are the generalised eigenvalues of $(A, B)$. This gives an explicit, numerically standard route to $P$: one Cholesky factorisation and one symmetric eigendecomposition. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

The construction is the spectral theorem [01.01.13] applied in the inner product supplied by the positive-definite form: where the spectral theorem diagonalises a self-adjoint operator in a fixed inner product, this unit chooses the inner product $\langle u, v\rangle_B = u^\top B v$ so that $B^{-1}A$ becomes self-adjoint, and the resulting eigenbasis diagonalises both forms. The reality of the generalised eigenvalues and the $B$-orthogonality of eigenvectors are the spectral theorem's reality and orthogonality conclusions, transported.

The generalised Rayleigh quotient $\rho(v) = (v^\top A v)/(v^\top B v)$ specialises the Rayleigh quotient and Courant-Fischer min-max of [01.01.14] through the Cholesky substitution $w = L^\top v$, which turns $\rho$ into the ordinary Rayleigh quotient of $C = L^{-1}AL^{-\top}$; every extremal and min-max statement there carries over, now with $B$-orthogonality replacing ordinary orthogonality in the deflation.

The congruence transformation $X \mapsto P^\top X P$ is the bilinear-form change-of-variables law of [01.01.17], and the sign count of the diagonalised pair is governed by Sylvester's law of inertia from [01.01.15]: the generalised eigenvalues inherit the signature of $A$, so the quadratic-form classification by inertia is exactly what determines stability of the associated mechanical equilibrium.

The same template recurs when the second form is symplectic rather than positive-definite, giving Williamson's normal form and the symplectic eigenvalues that organise Gaussian states in quantum information [12.07.03]; and the small-oscillation reading feeds the normal-mode analysis of coupled oscillators and lattice vibrations in classical and condensed-matter physics, where the generalised eigenvectors are the phonon polarisations and $\omega_k^2 = \lambda_k$ are the squared mode frequencies.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The simultaneous reduction of two quadratic forms grew out of mechanics before it became a chapter of linear algebra. Lagrange, in the *Mécanique analytique* (1788), analysed the small oscillations of a system about a stable equilibrium by writing the kinetic and potential energies as two quadratic forms and seeking coordinates in which both became sums of squares, so that the equations of motion separated into independent harmonic oscillators; the determinant condition he reached is the secular equation $\det(A - \lambda B) = 0$ that names the squared normal frequencies [ref: TODO_REF Lagrange 1788]. Sylvester's law of inertia (1852) supplied the invariant that the reduction respects: the signature of a real quadratic form is unchanged by real congruence, so the signs of the generalised eigenvalues are intrinsic [ref: TODO_REF Sylvester 1852].

The general theory of a pencil of forms is due to Weierstrass and Kronecker. Weierstrass (1868) classified a regular pencil $A - \lambda B$ by its elementary divisors, settling exactly when the pair is simultaneously diagonalisable and when it is defective [ref: TODO_REF Weierstrass 1868]. Kronecker (1890) completed the picture for singular pencils, introducing the minimal indices that measure the singular part beyond the Weierstrass divisors [ref: TODO_REF Kronecker 1890]. The positive-definite case singled out in this unit is the benign corner of that theory, where all elementary divisors are finite and linear and the pencil diagonalises by a real congruence. Shilov's *Linear Algebra* presents the definite case as the simultaneous-reduction theorem, and Parlett's *The Symmetric Eigenvalue Problem* (1980) developed the numerical side, the Cholesky reduction and the conditioning of the symmetric-definite generalised eigenproblem that underlies modern eigensolvers [ref: TODO_REF Parlett 1980].
