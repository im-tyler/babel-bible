## Intuition [Beginner]

Every fraction has a numerator and a denominator. The integers admit fractions like $3/4$ and $7/2$, but no integer $n$ satisfies $2 \times n = 1$. Localisation solves this by building a larger ring where chosen elements become invertible. Given a ring $A$ and a set $S$ of elements you want as denominators, the localisation $S^{-1}A$ is a new ring whose elements are fractions $a/s$ with $a$ in $A$ and $s$ in $S$, subject to cross-multiplication: $a/s$ equals $a'/s'$ when $s' a = s a'$.

Take $A = \mathbb{Z}$ and $S = \{1, 2, 4, 8, 16, \ldots\}$, the powers of $2$. The localisation $S^{-1}\mathbb{Z}$ consists of all fractions $a/2^n$ with $a$ an integer and $n \geq 0$. These are the dyadic rationals: numbers like $3/4$, $-5/8$, $7/2$. The element $2$ is now invertible with inverse $1/2$, but $3$ and $5$ are not invertible because they were not placed in $S$.

Localisation zooms in on one part of a ring by making selected elements invertible while leaving others untouched. Different choices of $S$ illuminate different structural features. Why does this concept exist? Localisation provides the mechanism for studying a ring one prime at a time, which is the foundation of local algebra and the bridge from rings to geometry.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take $A = \mathbb{Z}$ and $S = \{1, 3, 9, 27, 81, \ldots\}$, the powers of $3$. The localisation $S^{-1}\mathbb{Z}$ consists of fractions $a/3^n$.

Step 1. The integer $5$ maps to $5/1$ in $S^{-1}\mathbb{Z}$, which behaves exactly like $5$.

Step 2. The integer $3$ gains an inverse: $1/3$. This element did not exist in $\mathbb{Z}$; it is new. Similarly, $9$ has inverse $1/9$ and $27$ has inverse $1/27$.

Step 3. The fraction $6/9$ equals $2/3$ because $3 \times 6 = 18 = 9 \times 2$. Cross-multiplication confirms equality. Every fraction in this localisation can be written with a denominator that is a power of $3$.

What this tells us: localising $\mathbb{Z}$ at the powers of $3$ produces precisely the rational numbers whose denominators are powers of $3$, with the usual fraction arithmetic.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $A$ is a commutative ring with identity [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah-Macdonald 1969].

**Definition (Multiplicative set).** A subset $S \subseteq A$ is a *multiplicative set* if $1 \in S$ and $s, t \in S$ implies $st \in S$.

**Definition (Localisation).** The *localisation* of $A$ at $S$, written $S^{-1}A$, is the set of equivalence classes $a/s$ with $a \in A$, $s \in S$, where
$$
\frac{a}{s} = \frac{a'}{s'} \iff \exists\, t \in S:\ t(s' a - s a') = 0.
$$
The ring operations are
$$
\frac{a}{s} + \frac{a'}{s'} = \frac{s'a + sa'}{ss'}, \qquad \frac{a}{s} \cdot \frac{a'}{s'} = \frac{aa'}{ss'}.
$$

The canonical map $\iota\colon A \to S^{-1}A$ sends $a \mapsto a/1$. This is a ring homomorphism, and every $s \in S$ becomes a unit in $S^{-1}A$: the inverse of $s/1$ is $1/s$.

Two important special cases:

1. **Field of fractions.** If $A$ is an integral domain and $S = A \setminus \{0\}$, then $S^{-1}A$ is the field of fractions of $A$. For $A = \mathbb{Z}$ this gives $\mathbb{Q}$.

2. **Local ring at a prime.** If $\mathfrak{p}$ is a prime ideal of $A$ and $S = A \setminus \mathfrak{p}$, then $S^{-1}A$ is denoted $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$. This is a local ring (a ring with exactly one maximal ideal) whose maximal ideal is $\mathfrak{p} A_{\mathfrak{p}}$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *The equivalence relation needs the witness $t$.* When $A$ has no zero-divisors the condition simplifies to $s'a = sa'$. When zero-divisors are present, the witness $t \in S$ is required for transitivity of $\sim$.

- *The canonical map $\iota$ need not be injective.* If $s \in S$ satisfies $sa = 0$, then $\iota(a) = a/1 = 0/1$ because $s(1 \cdot a - 0 \cdot 1) = sa = 0$. Elements annihilated by something in $S$ vanish in the localisation.

- *If $0 \in S$ then $S^{-1}A = 0$.* Every fraction $a/s$ equals $0/1$ witnessed by $t = 0$. For this reason one always assumes $0 \notin S$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Universal property of localisation).** Let $A$ be a commutative ring, $S$ a multiplicative set, and $B$ a commutative ring. If $f\colon A \to B$ is a ring homomorphism such that $f(s)$ is a unit in $B$ for every $s \in S$, then there exists a unique ring homomorphism $\bar{f}\colon S^{-1}A \to B$ satisfying $\bar{f}(a/1) = f(a)$ for all $a \in A$.

*Proof.* **Existence.** Define $\bar{f}(a/s) = f(a)f(s)^{-1}$. This is well-defined: if $a/s = a'/s'$, there exists $t \in S$ with $t(s'a - sa') = 0$. Applying $f$ gives $f(t)\bigl(f(s')f(a) - f(s)f(a')\bigr) = 0$ in $B$. Since $f(t)$ is a unit in $B$, multiply by $f(t)^{-1}$ to obtain $f(s')f(a) = f(s)f(a')$. Multiplying both sides by $f(s)^{-1}f(s')^{-1}$ gives $f(a)f(s)^{-1} = f(a')f(s')^{-1}$.

The map $\bar{f}$ is a ring homomorphism:
$$
\bar{f}\!\left(\frac{a}{s} + \frac{a'}{s'}\right) = \bar{f}\!\left(\frac{s'a + sa'}{ss'}\right) = \frac{f(s')f(a) + f(s)f(a')}{f(s)f(s')} = \frac{f(a)}{f(s)} + \frac{f(a')}{f(s')}.
$$
Multiplicativity: $\bar{f}\bigl((a/s)(a'/s')\bigr) = f(aa')f(ss')^{-1} = f(a)f(s)^{-1}f(a')f(s')^{-1} = \bar{f}(a/s)\bar{f}(a'/s')$. Also $\bar{f}(1/1) = f(1)f(1)^{-1} = 1$.

On elements $a/1$: $\bar{f}(a/1) = f(a)f(1)^{-1} = f(a)$, so $\bar{f}$ extends $f$.

**Uniqueness.** Suppose $g\colon S^{-1}A \to B$ is another ring homomorphism with $g(a/1) = f(a)$. For any $a/s \in S^{-1}A$:
$$
g(a/s) = g\bigl((a/1)(1/s)\bigr) = g(a/1) \cdot g(1/s) = f(a) \cdot g(1/s).
$$
Since $g(s/1) \cdot g(1/s) = g(1) = 1$, we have $g(1/s) = g(s/1)^{-1} = f(s)^{-1}$. Therefore $g(a/s) = f(a)f(s)^{-1} = \bar{f}(a/s)$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The universal property builds toward [01.02.10] (tensor product of modules), where the localisation of a module $S^{-1}M$ is constructed as $S^{-1}A \otimes_A M$, and this is exactly the extension-of-scalars functor along $\iota\colon A \to S^{-1}A$. The foundational reason is that $S^{-1}A$ is the universal ring under $A$ that inverts $S$, and the central insight is that every homomorphism from $A$ to a ring where $S$ is inverted factors uniquely through $S^{-1}A$.

The bridge is between the ring-theoretic fraction construction and the module-theoretic tensor-product construction, and the pattern generalises to the localisation of quasi-coherent sheaves on a scheme [ref: TODO_REF Serre 1955].

**Exercise 2 (easy, multiple choice).**

Let $\mathfrak{p}$ be a prime ideal of a commutative ring $A$ and set $S = A \setminus \mathfrak{p}$. Which statement about $A_{\mathfrak{p}} = S^{-1}A$ is correct?

A. Every element of $A$ remains nonzero in $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$.

B. The maximal ideal of $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ is $\mathfrak{p} A_{\mathfrak{p}}$.

C. Elements of $\mathfrak{p}$ become invertible in $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$.

D. $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ is always a field.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$S = A \setminus \mathfrak{p}$: elements outside $\mathfrak{p}$ become invertible. What does this imply about the ideals of $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**B.** The maximal ideal of $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ is $\mathfrak{p} A_{\mathfrak{p}} = \{a/s : a \in \mathfrak{p},\, s \in S\}$. Statement A is false because elements annihilated by something in $S$ map to zero. Statement C is false: elements outside $\mathfrak{p}$ become invertible, not elements of $\mathfrak{p}$. Statement D is false: $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ is a local ring but need not be a field (e.g. $\mathbb{Z}_{(2)}$ is not a field).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $A$ be an integral domain with field of fractions $K = (A \setminus \{0\})^{-1}A$. Prove that for any multiplicative set $S \subseteq A \setminus \{0\}$, the localisation $S^{-1}A$ is the subring of $K$ consisting of elements $a/s$ with $s \in S$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show that the inclusion $S^{-1}A \hookrightarrow K$ is injective and identify its image.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Since $A$ is an integral domain, the equivalence relation simplifies to $a/s = a'/s'$ if and only if $s'a = sa'$. Both $S^{-1}A$ and $K$ use this same relation, and $S \subseteq A \setminus \{0\}$, so every fraction $a/s$ with $s \in S$ is an element of $K$.

The map $\varphi\colon S^{-1}A \to K$ sending $a/s \mapsto a/s$ (viewing the right-hand side in $K$) is an injective ring homomorphism: $\varphi(a/s) = 0$ in $K$ means $a = 0$ in $A$ (since $K$ is the field of fractions of an integral domain), hence $a/s = 0$ in $S^{-1}A$.

The image of $\varphi$ is $\{a/s \in K : s \in S\}$. This is a subring of $K$ because $S$ is multiplicative: if $s, s' \in S$ then $ss' \in S$, so sums and products of elements with denominators in $S$ again have denominators in $S$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, numeric).**

In the localisation of $\mathbb{Z}$ at $S = \{1, 5, 25, 125, \ldots\}$, write $7/25 + 3/5$ in the form $a/5^n$ with $n$ minimal. What is $a$?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Bring both fractions to a common denominator (a power of $5$), then add.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**22.** Compute $7/25 + 3/5 = 7/25 + 15/25 = 22/25$. Since $\gcd(22, 25) = 1$, the denominator $5^2 = 25$ is minimal: $22$ is not divisible by $5$, so the fraction cannot be written with a smaller power of $5$ in the denominator.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $A = k[x, y]$ be a polynomial ring over a field $k$ and let $\mathfrak{p} = (x)$. Describe the elements of the local ring $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ and identify its maximal ideal.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The multiplicative set is $S = A \setminus (x) = \{g \in k[x,y] : g(0,y) \neq 0\}$, the polynomials not divisible by $x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The localisation $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ consists of fractions $f(x,y)/g(x,y)$ where $g(0,y) \neq 0$ (equivalently, $g$ has a term independent of $x$). The maximal ideal is
$$
\mathfrak{p} A_{\mathfrak{p}} = \left\{\frac{f}{g} : f \in (x),\, g \notin (x)\right\} = \left\{\frac{x \cdot h(x,y)}{g(x,y)} : g(0,y) \neq 0\right\}.
$$
Every element of $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ not in $\mathfrak{p} A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ has $f \notin (x)$, meaning $f(0,y) \neq 0$, and is therefore invertible: $(f/g)^{-1} = g/f$. So $\mathfrak{p} A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ is the unique maximal ideal and $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ is a local ring.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, symbolic).**

Prove that if $\mathfrak{p}$ is a prime ideal of $A$ and $S = A \setminus \mathfrak{p}$, then $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ is a local ring with maximal ideal $\mathfrak{p} A_{\mathfrak{p}}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show that every element of $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ not in $\mathfrak{p} A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ is a unit.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

An element of $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ has the form $a/s$ with $a \in A$ and $s \in S = A \setminus \mathfrak{p}$. The element $a/s \in \mathfrak{p} A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ if and only if $a \in \mathfrak{p}$ (because $s \notin \mathfrak{p}$ and $\mathfrak{p}$ is prime).

If $a/s \notin \mathfrak{p} A_{\mathfrak{p}}$, then $a \notin \mathfrak{p}$, so $a \in S$. Therefore $s/a \in A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ and $(a/s)(s/a) = as/(sa) = 1/1$. Every element outside $\mathfrak{p} A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ is a unit.

An ideal that contains a unit equals the whole ring. So $\mathfrak{p} A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ is the unique maximal ideal of $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Let $A$ be a commutative ring, $S$ a multiplicative set, and $M$ an $A$-module. Define $S^{-1}M$ as the set of equivalence classes $m/s$ with $m \in M$, $s \in S$, where $m/s = m'/s'$ if $t(s'm - sm') = 0$ for some $t \in S$. Prove that $S^{-1}A \otimes_A M \cong S^{-1}M$ as $S^{-1}A$-modules.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Construct maps in both directions. The map $S^{-1}A \times M \to S^{-1}M$ sending $(a/s, m) \mapsto (am)/s$ is $A$-bilinear and induces a map from the tensor product. For the inverse, send $m/s \mapsto (1/s) \otimes m$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The map $\varphi\colon S^{-1}A \times M \to S^{-1}M$ defined by $(a/s, m) \mapsto (am)/s$ is $A$-bilinear: for $r \in A$, $\varphi(ra/s, m) = (ram)/s = r \cdot (am)/s = r\varphi(a/s, m)$, and $\varphi(a/s, m + m') = a(m+m')/s = am/s + am'/s = \varphi(a/s, m) + \varphi(a/s, m')$. By the universal property of the tensor product [01.02.10], $\varphi$ induces an $S^{-1}A$-linear map $\Phi\colon S^{-1}A \otimes_A M \to S^{-1}M$ with $\Phi((a/s) \otimes m) = (am)/s$.

Define $\Psi\colon S^{-1}M \to S^{-1}A \otimes_A M$ by $\Psi(m/s) = (1/s) \otimes m$. This is well-defined: if $m/s = m'/s'$ with witness $t \in S$, then $t(s'm - sm') = 0$, so $t(s'm - sm') = 0$ in $M$. In $S^{-1}A \otimes_A M$:
$$
(1/s) \otimes m - (1/s') \otimes m' = (s't)/(ss't) \otimes m - (st)/(ss't) \otimes m' = (1/ss't) \otimes (s'm - sm') \cdot t = 0.
$$

Checking the composites: $\Psi(\Phi((a/s) \otimes m)) = \Psi(am/s) = (1/s) \otimes (am) = (a/s) \otimes m$. Conversely, $\Phi(\Psi(m/s)) = \Phi((1/s) \otimes m) = m/s$. Both composites are identities on generators, so $\Phi$ is an isomorphism.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove Nakayama's lemma: let $A$ be a local ring with maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}$, let $M$ be a finitely generated $A$-module, and suppose $\mathfrak{m} M = M$. Then $M = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Let $m_1, \ldots, m_n$ be a minimal generating set for $M$. Since $\mathfrak{m} M = M$, write $m_n = r_1 m_1 + \cdots + r_n m_n$ with $r_i \in \mathfrak{m}$. Rearrange and use the fact that $1 - r_n$ is a unit in $A$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $m_1, \ldots, m_n$ be a minimal generating set for $M$, so $n \geq 1$ and no proper subset generates $M$. Since $\mathfrak{m} M = M$, the element $m_n$ can be written as
$$
m_n = r_1 m_1 + r_2 m_2 + \cdots + r_n m_n, \qquad r_i \in \mathfrak{m}.
$$
Rearranging: $(1 - r_n) m_n = r_1 m_1 + \cdots + r_{n-1} m_{n-1}$.

Since $r_n \in \mathfrak{m}$ and $A$ is local with maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}$, the element $1 - r_n$ lies outside $\mathfrak{m}$: if $1 - r_n \in \mathfrak{m}$ then $1 = (1 - r_n) + r_n \in \mathfrak{m}$, contradicting $\mathfrak{m} \neq A$. In a local ring, every element outside the maximal ideal is a unit, so $1 - r_n$ is invertible.

Multiplying by $(1 - r_n)^{-1}$:
$$
m_n = \frac{r_1}{1 - r_n} m_1 + \cdots + \frac{r_{n-1}}{1 - r_n} m_{n-1}.
$$
This expresses $m_n$ in terms of $m_1, \ldots, m_{n-1}$, contradicting minimality of the generating set unless $n = 0$, i.e. $M = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (Localisation of modules).** Let $A$ be a commutative ring, $S$ a multiplicative set, and $M$ an $A$-module. The localisation $S^{-1}M$ is an $S^{-1}A$-module with scalar multiplication $(a/s)(m/t) = (am)/(st)$. There is a canonical isomorphism $S^{-1}M \cong S^{-1}A \otimes_A M$ of $S^{-1}A$-modules.

The isomorphism sends $m/s \mapsto (1/s) \otimes m$; its inverse sends $(a/s) \otimes m \mapsto (am)/s$ [ref: TODO_REF Atiyah-Macdonald 1969].

**Theorem 2 (Exactness of localisation).** The functor $S^{-1}(-)\colon \mathbf{Mod}_A \to \mathbf{Mod}_{S^{-1}A}$ is exact. If $0 \to M' \xrightarrow{f} M \xrightarrow{g} M'' \to 0$ is an exact sequence of $A$-modules, then $0 \to S^{-1}M' \xrightarrow{S^{-1}f} S^{-1}M \xrightarrow{S^{-1}g} S^{-1}M'' \to 0$ is exact.

Equivalently, $S^{-1}A$ is a flat $A$-module: the functor $S^{-1}A \otimes_A -$ is exact. This is a consequence of the fraction-level description of kernels and images in $S^{-1}M$.

**Theorem 3 (Prime ideal correspondence).** The maps $\mathfrak{q} \mapsto \iota^{-1}(\mathfrak{q})$ and $\mathfrak{p} \mapsto \mathfrak{p}(S^{-1}A)$ give a bijection between prime ideals of $S^{-1}A$ and prime ideals of $A$ that do not meet $S$.

Under this correspondence, maximal ideals of $S^{-1}A$ correspond to prime ideals of $A$ that are maximal among those disjoint from $S$. In particular, when $S = A \setminus \mathfrak{p}$ for a prime $\mathfrak{p}$, the only maximal ideal of $A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ is $\mathfrak{p} A_{\mathfrak{p}}$.

**Theorem 4 (Nakayama's lemma).** Let $(A, \mathfrak{m})$ be a local ring and $M$ a finitely generated $A$-module. If $\mathfrak{m} M = M$, then $M = 0$. Equivalently, elements $m_1, \ldots, m_n$ generate $M$ if and only if their images generate $M/\mathfrak{m}M$ as a vector space over the residue field $A/\mathfrak{m}$.

The equivalence-of-generators formulation is the practical version: to check that elements generate $M$, it suffices to check that their images generate $M/\mathfrak{m}M$.

**Theorem 5 (Support of a module).** The *support* of an $A$-module $M$ is $\operatorname{Supp}(M) = \{\mathfrak{p} \in \operatorname{Spec} A : M_{\mathfrak{p}} \neq 0\}$. If $M$ is finitely generated, then $\operatorname{Supp}(M) = V(\operatorname{Ann} M)$, the set of primes containing the annihilator of $M$.

The support detects where a module is "visible" after localisation. For a finitely generated module over a Noetherian ring, the support is a closed subset of $\operatorname{Spec} A$ [ref: TODO_REF Eisenbud 1995].

**Theorem 6 (Local-to-global principle).** Let $M$ be a finitely generated module over a Noetherian ring $A$. The following are equivalent:

(i) $M = 0$.

(ii) $M_{\mathfrak{p}} = 0$ for every prime ideal $\mathfrak{p}$ of $A$.

(iii) $M_{\mathfrak{m}} = 0$ for every maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}$ of $A$.

Similarly, a homomorphism $f\colon M \to N$ of finitely generated modules over a Noetherian ring is injective (resp. surjective, bijective) if and only if $f_{\mathfrak{m}}$ is injective (resp. surjective, bijective) for every maximal ideal $\mathfrak{m}$.

**Synthesis.** The localisation functor is the foundational reason that local algebra reduces global questions about modules to computations at each prime ideal. The central insight is that exactness of $S^{-1}(-)$ [Theorem 2] makes localisation a flat base change, and this is exactly the mechanism that identifies local data at $\mathfrak{p}$ with the fibre of the structure sheaf on $\operatorname{Spec} A$. Putting these together with the support [Theorem 5] and the local-to-global principle [Theorem 6], the bridge is between the module-theoretic condition $M_{\mathfrak{p}} = 0$ for all $\mathfrak{p}$ and the global vanishing $M = 0$.

This is exactly the algebraic foundation of scheme theory: the assignment $\mathfrak{p} \mapsto A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ defines the structure sheaf on $\operatorname{Spec} A$, and the pattern generalises from rings to schemes, where quasi-coherent sheaves are locally given by modules and gluing is automatic by the exactness of localisation. The local-to-global principle appears again in [01.02.17], where Noetherianness is detected locally, and the pattern recurs in [01.02.11] where exactness of localisation turns short exact sequences of modules into short exact sequences of stalks.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (Exactness of localisation).** The functor $S^{-1}(-)$ is exact.

*Proof.* Let $M' \xrightarrow{f} M \xrightarrow{g} M''$ be exact at $M$, meaning $\operatorname{im} f = \ker g$. We show $S^{-1}(\operatorname{im} f) = S^{-1}(\ker g)$.

For any submodule $N \subseteq M$, the localisation $S^{-1}N$ is the image of $N$ under the map $M \to S^{-1}M$ sending $m \mapsto m/1$. Concretely, $S^{-1}N = \{n/s : n \in N,\, s \in S\}$.

Since $f(M') \subseteq \ker g$, every element $f(m')/s$ maps to $g(f(m'))/s = 0/s = 0$ under $S^{-1}g$. So $S^{-1}(\operatorname{im} f) \subseteq S^{-1}(\ker g)$.

Conversely, let $m/s \in S^{-1}(\ker g)$, so $m \in \ker g$ and $s \in S$. Then $m \in \operatorname{im} f$, say $m = f(m')$. So $m/s = f(m')/s = (S^{-1}f)(m'/s)$. Hence $S^{-1}(\ker g) \subseteq S^{-1}(\operatorname{im} f)$.

Therefore $S^{-1}(\operatorname{im} f) = S^{-1}(\ker g)$, and $S^{-1}M' \xrightarrow{S^{-1}f} S^{-1}M \xrightarrow{S^{-1}g} S^{-1}M''$ is exact at $S^{-1}M$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Nakayama's lemma — equivalence of generators formulation).** Let $(A, \mathfrak{m})$ be a local ring and $M$ a finitely generated $A$-module. Elements $m_1, \ldots, m_n$ generate $M$ if and only if their images generate $M/\mathfrak{m}M$ over $A/\mathfrak{m}$.

*Proof.* Let $N = A m_1 + \cdots + A m_n$ be the submodule generated by $m_1, \ldots, m_n$. The quotient $M/N$ satisfies $\mathfrak{m}(M/N) = (\mathfrak{m}M + N)/N$. If the images of $m_1, \ldots, m_n$ generate $M/\mathfrak{m}M$, then $M = \mathfrak{m}M + N$, so $\mathfrak{m}(M/N) = M/N$.

The module $M/N$ is finitely generated (since $M$ is). By Nakayama's lemma [Theorem 4], $\mathfrak{m}(M/N) = M/N$ implies $M/N = 0$, hence $M = N$.

Conversely, if $m_1, \ldots, m_n$ generate $M$, their images generate $M/\mathfrak{m}M$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Prime ideal correspondence).** The maps $\varphi\colon \{\text{primes of } S^{-1}A\} \to \{\text{primes of } A \text{ disjoint from } S\}$ defined by $\varphi(\mathfrak{q}) = \iota^{-1}(\mathfrak{q})$, and $\psi\colon \{\text{primes of } A \text{ disjoint from } S\} \to \{\text{primes of } S^{-1}A\}$ defined by $\psi(\mathfrak{p}) = \{a/s : a \in \mathfrak{p},\, s \in S\}$, are mutually inverse bijections.

*Proof.* First, $\psi(\mathfrak{p})$ is a prime ideal of $S^{-1}A$. It is an ideal because $(a/s)(b/t) = (ab)/(st)$ and $\mathfrak{p}$ is an ideal. It is proper because $1/1 \notin \psi(\mathfrak{p})$: if $1/1 = a/s$ with $a \in \mathfrak{p}$, then $t(s - a) = 0$ for some $t \in S$, giving $ts = ta \in \mathfrak{p}$; since $t \notin \mathfrak{p}$ (as $\mathfrak{p} \cap S = \emptyset$) and $\mathfrak{p}$ is prime, $s \in \mathfrak{p}$, contradicting $s \in S$. Primality: if $(a/s)(b/t) = (ab)/(st) \in \psi(\mathfrak{p})$, then $u \cdot ab \in \mathfrak{p}$ for some $u \in S$; since $u \notin \mathfrak{p}$ and $\mathfrak{p}$ is prime, $ab \in \mathfrak{p}$, hence $a \in \mathfrak{p}$ or $b \in \mathfrak{p}$.

Now check the composites. For $\varphi \circ \psi$: given a prime $\mathfrak{p}$ of $A$ with $\mathfrak{p} \cap S = \emptyset$,
$$
\varphi(\psi(\mathfrak{p})) = \{a \in A : a/1 \in \psi(\mathfrak{p})\} = \{a \in A : \exists\, s \in S,\, a' \in \mathfrak{p} \text{ with } a/1 = a'/s\}.
$$
The equality $a/1 = a'/s$ means $t(sa - a') = 0$ for some $t \in S$, so $tsa = ta' \in \mathfrak{p}$. Since $ts \notin \mathfrak{p}$, we get $a \in \mathfrak{p}$. Hence $\varphi(\psi(\mathfrak{p})) \subseteq \mathfrak{p}$. The reverse inclusion $\mathfrak{p} \subseteq \varphi(\psi(\mathfrak{p}))$ is immediate: if $a \in \mathfrak{p}$ then $a/1 \in \psi(\mathfrak{p})$ so $a \in \varphi(\psi(\mathfrak{p}))$.

For $\psi \circ \varphi$: given a prime $\mathfrak{q}$ of $S^{-1}A$,
$$
\psi(\varphi(\mathfrak{q})) = \{a/s : a \in \iota^{-1}(\mathfrak{q}),\, s \in S\}.
$$
If $a \in \iota^{-1}(\mathfrak{q})$ then $a/1 \in \mathfrak{q}$, so $a/s = (a/1)(1/s) \in \mathfrak{q}$ (since $1/s$ is a unit). Hence $\psi(\varphi(\mathfrak{q})) \subseteq \mathfrak{q}$. Conversely, if $a/s \in \mathfrak{q}$, then $a/1 = (a/s)(s/1) \in \mathfrak{q}$, so $a \in \iota^{-1}(\mathfrak{q})$, giving $a/s \in \psi(\varphi(\mathfrak{q}))$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Tensor product of modules [01.02.10].** The localisation of a module satisfies $S^{-1}M \cong S^{-1}A \otimes_A M$, making localisation a special case of extension of scalars along the canonical map $A \to S^{-1}A$. The tensor-hom adjunction then identifies $\operatorname{Hom}_{S^{-1}A}(S^{-1}M, S^{-1}N)$ with $S^{-1}\operatorname{Hom}_A(M, N)$ when $M$ is finitely presented, linking localisation to the homological algebra developed in the tensor product unit.

- **Hilbert basis theorem; Noetherian rings [01.02.17].** Localisation preserves Noetherianness: if $A$ is Noetherian then so is $S^{-1}A$, because every ideal of $S^{-1}A$ has the form $I(S^{-1}A)$ for some ideal $I$ of $A$, and the ascending chain condition on ideals of $A$ transfers to $S^{-1}A$. The local-to-global principle for Noetherian rings states that a module is zero if and only if it is zero after localising at every maximal ideal, and this closes the loop between the local and global finiteness conditions.

- **Exact sequence, snake lemma [01.02.11].** The exactness of $S^{-1}(-)$ means that applying localisation to any short exact sequence of $A$-modules produces a short exact sequence of $S^{-1}A$-modules. This is the ring-theoretic analogue of the flatness property for tensor products: localisation is an exact functor. When applied to the snake lemma diagram, localisation commutes with the connecting homomorphism, so the snake lemma descends to every localisation.

- **Field [01.01.01].** The field of fractions of an integral domain is the localisation at $S = A \setminus \{0\}$, recovering the construction of $\mathbb{Q}$ from $\mathbb{Z}$ as the simplest example. Every field is already local (the unique maximal ideal is $(0)$), so localisation at a prime ideal generalises the passage from an integral domain to its fraction field by allowing more selective inversion.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Grell 1927 introduced the construction of localisation for integral domains in *Beziehungen zwischen den Idealen verschiedener Ringe* [ref: TODO_REF Grell 1927], establishing that every homomorphism from a domain $A$ to a field in which a prescribed set of elements is invertible factors through the ring of fractions. Chevalley 1943--44 extended the construction to arbitrary commutative rings with zero-divisors in *On the theory of local rings* [ref: TODO_REF Chevalley 1944], introducing the modern form of the equivalence relation with the witness element $t \in S$ and proving the prime-ideal correspondence.

Serre 1955 made localisation the cornerstone of algebraic geometry in *Faisceaux algebriques coherents* [ref: TODO_REF Serre 1955], where the assignment $\mathfrak{p} \mapsto A_{\mathfrak{p}}$ defines the structure sheaf on $\operatorname{Spec} A$. The local-to-global principle for coherent sheaves — a module is zero if and only if all its stalks are zero — is the geometric expression of the algebraic local-to-global principle proved above. Nakayama's lemma, proved independently by Nakayama and Azumaya around 1950--51, became the primary tool for studying finitely generated modules over local rings.
