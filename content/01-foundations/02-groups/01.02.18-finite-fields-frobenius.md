## Intuition [Beginner]

A finite field is a number system with only finitely many elements, yet you can still add, subtract, multiply, and divide by anything nonzero. The clock arithmetic on the hours $0, 1, \dots, p-1$ for a prime $p$ is the smallest example; mathematicians write it as $\mathbb{F}_p$.

The surprise is that there are larger finite fields too. For every prime $p$ and every whole number $n \geq 1$, there is exactly one field with $p^n$ elements, written $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ (or $\mathbb{F}_q$ with $q = p^n$). These, and only these, are all the finite fields that exist.

The deepest feature is a built-in symmetry. In a field of characteristic $p$, the map that sends every element $x$ to $x^p$ reshuffles the elements while preserving all the arithmetic. This reshuffle is the **Frobenius**, and doing it $n$ times returns every element of $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ to itself. The Frobenius is the heart of this unit: it is both a symmetry of the field and the generator of its Galois group.

## Visual [Beginner]

The field $\mathbb{F}_4$ with four elements, built by adjoining a symbol $\alpha$ with $\alpha^2 = \alpha + 1$. The Frobenius map $x \mapsto x^2$ acts on the four elements as follows.

| element $x$ | $0$ | $1$ | $\alpha$ | $\alpha + 1$ |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Frobenius $x^2$ | $0$ | $1$ | $\alpha + 1$ | $\alpha$ |

![The four elements of F_4 arranged in a square, with curved arrows showing the Frobenius map swapping alpha and alpha+1 while fixing 0 and 1.](/img/finite-fields-frobenius-placeholder.svg)

The Frobenius swaps $\alpha$ and $\alpha + 1$ while fixing $0$ and $1$. Applying it twice returns every element to itself, so its order is $2$, the degree of $\mathbb{F}_4$ over $\mathbb{F}_2$.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the prime $p = 7$. The nonzero hours $1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6$ form the multiplicative system $\mathbb{F}_7^\times$. List the powers of $3$ modulo $7$:

$3^1 = 3$, $\; 3^2 = 9 = 2$, $\; 3^3 = 6$, $\; 3^4 = 18 = 4$, $\; 3^5 = 12 = 5$, $\; 3^6 = 15 = 1$.

Every nonzero value appears exactly once, so $3$ generates the whole cycle. The nonzero part $\mathbb{F}_7^\times$ is a single cycle of length $6 = 7 - 1$. A generator like $3$ is called a **primitive root**.

Now build a field with four elements from scratch. Start from $\{0, 1\}$ and add a new symbol $\alpha$ with the rule $\alpha^2 = \alpha + 1$. The four elements are $0, 1, \alpha, \alpha + 1$. Multiplying $\alpha$ by itself gives $\alpha + 1$, and multiplying once more gives $\alpha \cdot (\alpha + 1) = \alpha^2 + \alpha = 1$. So $\alpha$ cycles through all three nonzero elements, exactly like the primitive root $3$ above.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Fix a prime $p$. The **prime field** of characteristic $p$ is $\mathbb{F}_p = \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$. A **finite field** is a field with finitely many elements; any such field has characteristic $p$ for a unique prime $p$ and is a finite-dimensional vector space over $\mathbb{F}_p$, hence has $q = p^n$ elements for a unique $n \geq 1$. The notation $\mathbb{F}_q$ denotes the field with $q = p^n$ elements [ref: TODO_REF Lang §VIII].

The **Frobenius endomorphism** of a field $K$ of characteristic $p$ is the map

$$
\operatorname{Frob}_p : K \to K, \qquad x \mapsto x^p .
$$

It is a ring homomorphism because $(xy)^p = x^p y^p$ and the **freshman's dream** identity $(x + y)^p = x^p + y^p$ holds: every middle binomial coefficient $\binom{p}{k}$ for $1 \leq k \leq p-1$ is divisible by $p$, so it vanishes in characteristic $p$. On a finite field the Frobenius is injective, hence bijective, so it is a **field automorphism** [ref: TODO_REF Dummit-Foote §14].

For a finite extension $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p$ the **norm** and **trace** are

$$
N_{\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p}(x) = \prod_{i=0}^{n-1} x^{p^i} = x^{(p^n - 1)/(p-1)},
\qquad
\operatorname{Tr}_{\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p}(x) = \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} x^{p^i},
$$

the product and the sum of the Galois conjugates $x, x^p, x^{p^2}, \dots, x^{p^{n-1}}$ of $x$.

### Constructions

There are two standard ways to build $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$, and they agree by uniqueness. The **splitting-field construction** takes the splitting field of $x^{p^n} - x$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$; its $p^n$ distinct roots form the field. This is the construction that makes uniqueness immediate.

The **quotient construction** picks an irreducible polynomial $f \in \mathbb{F}_p[x]$ of degree $n$ and forms the quotient ring $\mathbb{F}_p[x]/(f)$. Because $f$ is irreducible and $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$ is a principal ideal domain, the ideal $(f)$ is maximal, so the quotient is a field; it has $p^n$ elements, with basis $1, \bar{x}, \bar{x}^2, \dots, \bar{x}^{n-1}$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$ where $\bar{x}$ is the image of $x$. This is the polynomial analogue of building $\mathbb{F}_p$ as $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$, and it is the construction the number-theory units invoke when they write $\mathbb{F}_q \cong \mathbb{F}_p[x]/(f)$ for $f$ irreducible of degree $n$.

The two constructions give isomorphic fields, but the isomorphism is not canonical: it depends on the choice of $f$ and on which root of $f$ the quotient element $\bar{x}$ is matched to. Different irreducible polynomials of the same degree $n$ yield different-looking models of the same field $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$, related by Frobenius-twisted change-of-basis maps.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- *Not every finite ring is a field.* $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ for composite $n$ has zero divisors, so it is not a field; finite fields exist only for prime-power orders.
- *Not every prime power gives several fields.* For each $q = p^n$ there is exactly one field of order $q$ up to isomorphism, and exactly one copy of it inside a fixed algebraic closure $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$.
- *The Frobenius is not the identity in general.* $\operatorname{Frob}_p$ fixes exactly $\mathbb{F}_p$ pointwise; on $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ it has order $n$, not $1$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Existence, uniqueness, and cyclicity).** For every prime $p$ and every $n \geq 1$ there exists a field $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ with $p^n$ elements. It is unique up to isomorphism (and unique inside a fixed algebraic closure $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$). Its multiplicative group $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}^\times$ is cyclic of order $p^n - 1$.

*Proof.* **Existence.** Let $f(x) = x^{p^n} - x \in \mathbb{F}_p[x]$, and let $K$ be its splitting field over $\mathbb{F}_p$. The formal derivative is $f'(x) = p^n x^{p^n - 1} - 1 = -1 \neq 0$, so $f$ is separable and has $p^n$ distinct roots in $K$. Let $R \subseteq K$ be the set of roots. The freshman's dream, iterated $n$ times, gives $(a + b)^{p^n} = a^{p^n} + b^{p^n}$; thus $a, b \in R$ implies $a + b \in R$. Likewise $(ab)^{p^n} = a^{p^n} b^{p^n}$, so $ab \in R$, and $(-a)^{p^n} = -a^{p^n}$ gives additive inverses, while $a \neq 0$ with $a^{p^n} = a$ gives $a^{p^n - 1} = 1$, a multiplicative inverse. So $R$ is a subfield of $K$ with $|R| = p^n$, proving existence.

**Uniqueness.** Let $L$ be any field with $p^n$ elements. Its unit group $L^\times$ has order $p^n - 1$, so every nonzero $a \in L$ satisfies $a^{p^n - 1} = 1$, hence $a^{p^n} = a$; the element $0$ satisfies this too. Therefore every element of $L$ is a root of $x^{p^n} - x$, so $L$ is contained in the splitting field $K$ and in fact equals the root set $R$. Any two fields of order $p^n$ are thus splitting fields of the same polynomial over $\mathbb{F}_p$, hence isomorphic, and there is a unique one inside $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$.

**Cyclicity of $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$.** Write $G = \mathbb{F}_q^\times$, a finite abelian group of order $q - 1$. By the structure theorem for finite abelian groups, $G \cong \mathbb{Z}/d_1 \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}/d_r$ with $d_1 \mid d_2 \mid \cdots \mid d_r$. Every element of $G$ then satisfies $x^{d_r} = 1$, since $d_r$ is a multiple of the order of every element; $d_r$ is the exponent of $G$. So all $q - 1$ elements of $G$ are roots of the polynomial $x^{d_r} - 1 \in \mathbb{F}_q[x]$. A nonzero polynomial of degree $d_r$ over a field has at most $d_r$ roots, giving $q - 1 \leq d_r$. But the exponent $d_r$ divides $|G| = q - 1$, so $d_r \leq q - 1$. Therefore $d_r = q - 1$, which forces $r = 1$, and $G \cong \mathbb{Z}/(q - 1)$ is cyclic. $\square$

**Bridge.** The cyclicity of $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ builds toward [21.01.05] primitive roots, where the prime case $\mathbb{F}_p^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}/(p-1)$ supplies a single generator (a primitive root) for the unit group modulo $p$, and appears again in [21.01.04] Fermat's little theorem, where $a^p \equiv a \pmod p$ is precisely the statement that the Frobenius fixes $\mathbb{F}_p$ pointwise. The foundational reason the unit group is cyclic is that a polynomial of degree $d$ over a field has at most $d$ roots, which forces the exponent of $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ up to $q - 1$; this is exactly the bridge from the field property to the cyclic-multiplicative-structure fact that underwrites the discrete logarithm. The pattern generalises from $\mathbb{F}_p$ to every $\mathbb{F}_q$ without the moduli restriction that limits $(\mathbb{Z}/n)^\times$, and putting these together, finite fields are the universal arena where multiplicative cyclic structure and Galois symmetry coincide.

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).**

In $\mathbb{F}_4 = \mathbb{F}_2(\alpha)$ with $\alpha^2 = \alpha + 1$, write out the multiplication table of the nonzero elements and confirm that $\mathbb{F}_4^\times$ is cyclic of order $3$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $\alpha \cdot \alpha$, $\alpha \cdot (\alpha + 1)$, and $(\alpha + 1)^2$, reducing by $\alpha^2 = \alpha + 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\alpha^2 = \alpha + 1$. Then $\alpha \cdot (\alpha + 1) = \alpha^2 + \alpha = (\alpha + 1) + \alpha = 2\alpha + 1 = 1$ in characteristic $2$. Also $(\alpha + 1)^2 = \alpha^2 + 1 = (\alpha + 1) + 1 = \alpha$. So the nonzero elements are $1, \alpha, \alpha + 1$ with $\alpha^3 = \alpha \cdot \alpha^2 = \alpha(\alpha + 1) = 1$. Hence $\mathbb{F}_4^\times = \langle \alpha \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/3$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).**

Verify the freshman's dream $(a + b)^3 = a^3 + b^3$ directly in $\mathbb{F}_3$, and explain why it expresses the additivity of the Frobenius.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand $(a + b)^3$ and examine the middle binomial coefficients modulo $3$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$(a + b)^3 = a^3 + 3a^2 b + 3ab^2 + b^3$. In characteristic $3$ the coefficients $3 = 0$, so the middle terms vanish and $(a + b)^3 = a^3 + b^3$. The Frobenius on a field of characteristic $3$ is $\operatorname{Frob}_3(x) = x^3$; this identity is precisely the statement that $\operatorname{Frob}_3(a + b) = \operatorname{Frob}_3(a) + \operatorname{Frob}_3(b)$, the additivity that makes the Frobenius a field homomorphism.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, symbolic).**

Let $\mathbb{F}_9 = \mathbb{F}_3(i)$ with $i^2 = -1$. Compute the Frobenius orbit of $i$ and identify the Frobenius as a familiar operation on $a + bi$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Frobenius in characteristic $3$ is $x \mapsto x^3$. Use Fermat's little theorem $a^3 = a$ for $a \in \mathbb{F}_3$ and $i^3 = i \cdot i^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For $a \in \mathbb{F}_3$, $a^3 = a$. Also $i^3 = i^2 \cdot i = (-1) i = -i$. So $\operatorname{Frob}_3(a + bi) = a^3 + b^3 i^3 = a - bi$. The Frobenius is complex conjugation on $\mathbb{F}_9$. Its orbit on $i$ is $i \mapsto -i \mapsto i$, of period $2 = [\mathbb{F}_9 : \mathbb{F}_3]$, and $\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_9 / \mathbb{F}_3) = \{\mathrm{id}, \operatorname{Frob}_3\} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that $\mathbb{F}_{p^m} \subseteq \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ (inside $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$) if and only if $m$ divides $n$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

View both as vector spaces over the smaller field, and use the order of the Frobenius on each.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

If $\mathbb{F}_{p^m} \subseteq \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$, then $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ is a vector space over $\mathbb{F}_{p^m}$ of some dimension $k$, so $p^n = (p^m)^k$ and $n = mk$. Conversely, if $m \mid n$, write $n = mk$. The field $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ is the splitting field of $x^{p^n} - x$. Since $(p^m)^k = p^n$, any element $a \in \mathbb{F}_{p^m}$ satisfies $a^{p^m} = a$, hence by iteration $a^{p^n} = a^{(p^m)^k} = a$, so $a$ is a root of $x^{p^n} - x$, giving $\mathbb{F}_{p^m} \subseteq \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that the norm $N : \mathbb{F}_{p^n}^\times \to \mathbb{F}_p^\times$ is a surjective group homomorphism.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Multiplicativity is immediate from the product formula. For surjectivity, compare the kernel size to the order of $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The product formula gives $N(xy) = \prod (xy)^{p^i} = \prod x^{p^i} \cdot \prod y^{p^i} = N(x) N(y)$, so $N$ is a homomorphism, and its values lie in $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$ because $N(x)^p = N(x)$ (the Frobenius permutes the conjugates). The kernel consists of $x$ with $x^{(p^n - 1)/(p-1)} = 1$, a polynomial of degree $(p^n - 1)/(p-1)$, so $|\ker N| \leq (p^n - 1)/(p-1)$. By the first isomorphism theorem the image has size $|\mathbb{F}_{p^n}^\times| / |\ker N| \geq (p^n - 1) / ((p^n - 1)/(p - 1)) = p - 1 = |\mathbb{F}_p^\times|$. The image lands in $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$, so it must equal all of $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (medium, symbolic).**

Show that the Frobenius $\operatorname{Frob}_p : \mathbb{F}_{p^n} \to \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ is an automorphism by proving its kernel is $\{0\}$, and conclude that every element of $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ has a unique $p$-th root in $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A field homomorphism has kernel an ideal; the only ideals of a field are $\{0\}$ and the whole field.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Frobenius is a ring homomorphism by the freshman's dream. Its kernel is an ideal of the field $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$, so it is either $\{0\}$ or the whole field. Since $\operatorname{Frob}_p(1) = 1^p = 1 \neq 0$, the kernel is not the whole field, so $\ker(\operatorname{Frob}_p) = \{0\}$ and the map is injective. An injective map from a finite set to itself is bijective, so $\operatorname{Frob}_p$ is an automorphism. In particular every $y \in \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ is $x^p$ for a unique $x$, namely $x = y^{p^{n-1}}$ (since $x^p = y$ and $x = y^{p^{n-1}}$ gives $x^p = y^{p^n} = y$).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove that every element of $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ is a root of exactly one monic irreducible polynomial over $\mathbb{F}_p$ whose degree divides $n$, and use this to derive the identity $\sum_{d \mid n} d \, I_p(d) = p^n$, where $I_p(d)$ is the number of monic irreducible polynomials of degree $d$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Factor $x^{p^n} - x$ into monic irreducibles over $\mathbb{F}_p$. Which irreducibles can appear, and how many roots does each contribute?

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The polynomial $x^{p^n} - x$ has $p^n$ distinct roots in $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$, namely every element of the field, and it is separable. An irreducible factor $g$ of degree $d$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$ divides $x^{p^n} - x$ iff $\mathbb{F}_{p^d} \subseteq \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$, which holds iff $d \mid n$ (Exercise 6). Each such $g$ then contributes its $d$ roots, all lying in $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$. Therefore $x^{p^n} - x = \prod_{d \mid n} \prod_{g \text{ monic irr.}, \deg g = d} g(x)$, and counting degrees gives $\sum_{d \mid n} d \, I_p(d) = p^n$. Möbius inversion yields $I_p(n) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{d \mid n} \mu(d) \, p^{n/d}$, so in particular $I_p(n) > 0$ for every $n$, proving the existence of irreducible polynomials of every degree.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

The classification is complete and striking: up to isomorphism, the finite fields are exactly the fields $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$, one for each prime $p$ and integer $n \geq 1$, and every finite field is **perfect** because the Frobenius is an automorphism (injective on a finite set). The algebraic closure $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$ is the union $\bigcup_{n \geq 1} \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$, and its subfield lattice is the divisor lattice: the subfields of $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ are exactly the $\mathbb{F}_{p^d}$ with $d \mid n$ [ref: TODO_REF Dummit-Foote §14].

The Galois group is always cyclic and canonically generated. Since every $x \in \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ satisfies $x^{p^n} = x$, the Frobenius satisfies $\operatorname{Frob}_p^n = \mathrm{id}$ on $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$; its fixed field is $\mathbb{F}_p$, so its order is exactly $n$. Thus $\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^n} / \mathbb{F}_p) = \langle \operatorname{Frob}_p \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/n$. More generally, the fixed field of $\operatorname{Frob}_p^k$ is $\mathbb{F}_{p^d}$ with $d = \gcd(k, n)$, so the Galois correspondence reduces exactly to the divisor lattice of $n$ [ref: TODO_REF Lang §VIII].

The norm and trace package the conjugates into arithmetic. The trace $\operatorname{Tr} : \mathbb{F}_{p^n} \to \mathbb{F}_p$ is an $\mathbb{F}_p$-linear, surjective map (its image is a nonzero $\mathbb{F}_p$-subspace, hence all of $\mathbb{F}_p$), and the nondegenerate bilinear form $(x, y) \mapsto \operatorname{Tr}(xy)$ identifies $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ with its dual as an $\mathbb{F}_p$-vector space. The norm $N : \mathbb{F}_{p^n}^\times \to \mathbb{F}_p^\times$ is a surjective group homomorphism (Exercise 7). Both are transitive in towers: $\operatorname{Tr}_{K/\mathbb{F}_p} = \operatorname{Tr}_{L/\mathbb{F}_p} \circ \operatorname{Tr}_{K/L}$ and similarly for the norm.

Because the Frobenius is an automorphism, every finite field is **perfect**: every irreducible polynomial over $\mathbb{F}_p$ is separable, so finite-field extensions are always Galois and the pathology of inseparability never arises. The algebraic closure $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p} = \bigcup_{n \geq 1} \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ inherits an automorphism $\operatorname{Frob}_p$ of infinite order, and the absolute Galois group $\operatorname{Gal}(\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}/\mathbb{F}_p)$ is the procyclic completion $\widehat{\mathbb{Z}} = \varprojlim_n \mathbb{Z}/n$, topologically generated by the Frobenius. The finite Galois groups $\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p) \cong \mathbb{Z}/n$ are its continuous quotients. This is the cleanest example of an infinite Galois group equipped with the Krull topology [ref: TODO_REF Lang §VIII].

The irreducible polynomials over $\mathbb{F}_p$ are counted by factoring $x^{p^n} - x$. Since its roots are precisely the elements of $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$, and a monic irreducible of degree $d$ divides it iff $d \mid n$, degree-counting gives $\sum_{d \mid n} d \, I_p(d) = p^n$, where $I_p(d)$ is the number of monic irreducibles of degree $d$. Möbius inversion yields $I_p(n) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{d \mid n} \mu(d)\, p^{n/d}$, which is positive for every $n \geq 1$, supplying the existence of irreducible polynomials of every degree that the quotient construction requires [ref: TODO_REF Lidl-Niederreiter 1997].

For computations one wants good bases. The **normal basis theorem** states that $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ admits a basis over $\mathbb{F}_p$ consisting of a single Frobenius orbit $\{\alpha, \alpha^p, \alpha^{p^2}, \dots, \alpha^{p^{n-1}}\}$; with such a basis the Frobenius acts by a cyclic permutation of coordinates, so raising to the $p$-th power becomes a coordinate shift. This underlies efficient hardware implementations of finite-field arithmetic and the optimal normal-basis standards used in elliptic-curve cryptography.

These structures drive the applications the number-theory units depend on. The cyclicity of $\mathbb{F}_p^\times$ is the existence of primitive roots [21.01.05]; the identity $a^p = a$ is Fermat's little theorem, the statement that the Frobenius fixes $\mathbb{F}_p$ [21.01.04]; and the discrete logarithm in $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ underwrites Diffie-Hellman key exchange and the Digital Signature Algorithm. Going further, the zeta function of $\mathbb{F}_q$ and the Weil conjectures count the $\mathbb{F}_{q^r}$-points of a variety by reading off the eigenvalues of the Frobenius acting on étale cohomology, with the elliptic-curve case treated in [04.04.03] [ref: TODO_REF Lidl-Niederreiter 1997].

**Synthesis.** Finite fields are the meeting point of three structures that the rest of the curriculum reads off separately: the multiplicative group $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ is cyclic (the foundational reason discrete logarithms and primitive roots exist, building toward [21.01.05]), the Galois group $\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p) \cong \mathbb{Z}/n$ is generated by a single automorphism (this is exactly the Frobenius, the finite-field shadow of complex conjugation), and the subfield lattice $\mathbb{F}_{p^d} \subseteq \mathbb{F}_{p^n} \Leftrightarrow d \mid n$ mirrors the divisor lattice of $n$. The pattern generalises to the Weil conjectures and the zeta function $\zeta_{\mathbb{F}_q}(t)$, where the Frobenius acts on cohomology and the point count is read off from its characteristic polynomial; the central insight is that the Frobenius is simultaneously a Galois element, a linear operator, and a cohomological eigenvalue. Putting these together with the norm and trace, finite fields become the universal testbed for arithmetic geometry: the bridge is that every counting problem over a finite field reduces to the fixed-point structure of a power of the Frobenius, appearing again in [04.04.03] elliptic curves, [21.05.01] Galois representations, and the Langlands program.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Fixed fields of the Frobenius).** Let $K = \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ inside $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$, and let $\sigma = \operatorname{Frob}_p$. For any $k \geq 1$, the fixed field $K^{\langle \sigma^k \rangle} = \{x \in K : x^{p^k} = x\}$ equals $\mathbb{F}_{p^d}$ where $d = \gcd(k, n)$. In particular $\sigma$ has order $n$ and $\operatorname{Gal}(K/\mathbb{F}_p) = \langle \sigma \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/n$.

*Proof.* An element $x \in K$ is fixed by $\sigma^k$ precisely when $x^{p^k} = x$, i.e. when $x$ is a root of $x^{p^k} - x$. Inside $\overline{\mathbb{F}_p}$ the roots of $x^{p^k} - x$ are exactly the elements of $\mathbb{F}_{p^k}$ (the unique subfield of that order). So $K^{\langle \sigma^k \rangle} = K \cap \mathbb{F}_{p^k} = \mathbb{F}_{p^n} \cap \mathbb{F}_{p^k}$. By Exercise 6, the intersection of $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ and $\mathbb{F}_{p^k}$ is the largest subfield contained in both, namely $\mathbb{F}_{p^d}$ with $d = \gcd(k, n)$ (since $\mathbb{F}_{p^a} \cap \mathbb{F}_{p^b} = \mathbb{F}_{p^{\gcd(a,b)}}$).

Setting $k = n$: $\sigma^n = \mathrm{id}$ on $K$ because every $x \in \mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ satisfies $x^{p^n} = x$, so the order of $\sigma$ divides $n$. Setting $k$ with $\gcd(k, n) = 1$ gives a fixed field of $\mathbb{F}_p$, so no smaller power of $\sigma$ fixes all of $K$; combined with the divisor-lattice description, the order of $\sigma$ is exactly $n$. The extension $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p$ is Galois (it is the splitting field of the separable polynomial $x^{p^n} - x$), so $|\operatorname{Gal}(K/\mathbb{F}_p)| = [K : \mathbb{F}_p] = n$. The cyclic subgroup $\langle \sigma \rangle$ already has $n$ elements, so $\operatorname{Gal}(K/\mathbb{F}_p) = \langle \sigma \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/n$. $\square$

**Corollary (Enumeration of subfields).** The subfields of $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ are in order-preserving bijection with the divisors of $n$: for each $d \mid n$ there is exactly one subfield, namely $\mathbb{F}_{p^d}$, and the Galois correspondence sends it to the unique subgroup $\langle \sigma^d \rangle \leq \langle \sigma \rangle$ of order $n/d$.

**Proposition (Finite subgroups of a field's unit group are cyclic).** Let $K$ be any field and $G \subseteq K^\times$ a finite subgroup of its multiplicative group. Then $G$ is cyclic.

*Proof.* Let $|G| = m$ and let $\lambda$ be the exponent of $G$, the least common multiple of the orders of its elements. Every $g \in G$ satisfies $g^\lambda = 1$, so every element of $G$ is a root of the polynomial $x^\lambda - 1 \in K[x]$. This polynomial has degree $\lambda$ and therefore at most $\lambda$ roots in the field $K$. Since all $m$ elements of $G$ are roots, $m \leq \lambda$. On the other hand $\lambda$ is the order of some element of $G$ (in a finite abelian group the exponent is attained), so $\lambda$ divides $m$ by Lagrange, giving $\lambda \leq m$. Hence $\lambda = m$, and an element of order $\lambda = m$ generates $G$, so $G$ is cyclic. $\square$

Applied to $K = \mathbb{F}_q$ with $G = \mathbb{F}_q^\times$ this recovers the cyclicity of order $q - 1$; applied to $K = \mathbb{F}_p$ it is the existence of primitive roots modulo $p$. The argument fails for $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^\times$ when $n$ is not a prime power precisely because $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$ is not a field, so a degree-$\lambda$ polynomial may have more than $\lambda$ roots there — the source of the moduli restriction in [21.01.05].

## Connections [Master]

- **Fields and Galois theory [01.04.01].** A finite field $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ is the splitting field of the separable polynomial $x^{p^n} - x$ over $\mathbb{F}_p$, so the general Galois correspondence of [01.04.01] specializes here to a perfect lattice-reversal between the subfields $\mathbb{F}_{p^d}$ and the subgroups of the cyclic group $\langle \operatorname{Frob}_p \rangle \cong \mathbb{Z}/n$.

- **Group [01.02.01].** The cyclicity of $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ and of $\operatorname{Gal}(\mathbb{F}_{p^n}/\mathbb{F}_p)$ are both statements that certain naturally-arising groups are cyclic; the polynomial-root-count argument is the prototype of "a finite subgroup of the multiplicative group of a field is cyclic," a group-theoretic fact in the language of [01.02.01].

- **Fermat, Euler, Wilson [21.01.04].** Fermat's little theorem $a^p \equiv a \pmod p$ is the statement $\operatorname{Frob}_p = \mathrm{id}$ on $\mathbb{F}_p$, and the freshman's dream $(a+b)^p = a^p + b^p$ used in Euler's induction proof is the additivity of the Frobenius; this unit is the precise target those number-theory arguments forward-reference.

- **Primitive roots and the structure of units [21.01.05].** The theorem that $\mathbb{F}_p^\times \cong \mathbb{Z}/(p-1)$, and hence that primitive roots exist modulo every prime, is the case $n = 1$ of the cyclicity of $\mathbb{F}_q^\times$ proved here; the same polynomial-degree argument lifts the result to every $\mathbb{F}_q$ without exception.

- **Congruences and the Chinese remainder theorem [21.01.03].** The construction $\mathbb{F}_q \cong \mathbb{F}_p[x]/(f)$ for $f$ irreducible of degree $n$ is the polynomial analogue of the modular arithmetic in [21.01.03], and the factorization of $f$ via the Frobenius decomposition is the polynomial Chinese remainder theorem applied to a single maximal ideal.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Évariste Galois introduced finite fields in his 1830 paper *Sur la théorie des nombres*, where he constructed the field $\mathbb{F}_{p^n}$ as congruences in $\mathbb{F}_p[x]$ modulo an irreducible polynomial of degree $n$ and observed that the nonzero elements form a cyclic group [ref: TODO_REF Galois 1830]. This was the first appearance of what are now called **Galois fields** (hence the notation $GF(p^n)$), and the Frobenius symmetry $x \mapsto x^p$ is implicit in his construction. Galois's insight was that irreducibility of the modulus is exactly the condition that makes division possible, the polynomial analogue of "$p$ prime makes $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ a field."

The classification theorem — that *every* finite field is a Galois field $\mathrm{GF}(p^n)$, unique for each prime $p$ and degree $n$ — was completed by Eliakim Hastings Moore in 1893 [ref: TODO_REF Moore 1893]. Moore's theorem closed the question of which finite fields exist: the order of a finite field is necessarily a prime power, and every prime power occurs uniquely. The American school then systematized the subject, culminating in Leonard Eugene Dickson's 1901 treatise *Linear Groups: With an Exposition of the Galois Field Theory*, the first book-length development of finite fields and the classical groups over them [ref: TODO_REF Dickson 1901].

The modern abstract treatment, phrasing finite fields as splitting fields of $x^{p^n} - x$ and the Frobenius as the canonical generator of the Galois group, is due to Emil Artin and the mid-twentieth-century algebra textbooks. The subject gained a second life through applications: Reed-Solomon codes (1960), finite-field cryptography (Diffie-Hellman 1976), and the Weil conjectures, where the Frobenius acts on cohomology and governs the point counts of varieties over finite fields.
