## Intuition [Beginner]

Imagine a system that evolves in time according to a fixed rule: a metal bar cooling, a population diffusing, a quantum particle drifting. If you know the state now, the rule hands you the state one second later, and two seconds later, and so on. Run the rule for $t$ seconds, then run it again for $s$ seconds, and you have run it for $t + s$ seconds total. This composition law is the whole idea of a semigroup: a family of "time-advance machines" $T(t)$, one for each duration $t \geq 0$, that stack by adding their times.

The cleanest example is the ordinary exponential. The scalar equation $u' = a u$ has solution $u(t) = e^{ta} u(0)$, and $e^{ta} e^{sa} = e^{(t+s)a}$. The number $a$ is the "rule"; the exponential turns it into a flow. For a vector of unknowns the rule becomes a matrix $A$ and the flow becomes the matrix exponential $e^{tA}$.

The leap is to let the state live in an infinite-dimensional space of functions, and let $A$ be a differential operator like the Laplacian. Now there is no convergent power series for $e^{tA}$, but the flow $T(t)$ still exists. The question this unit answers is: which rules $A$ generate a sensible flow?

## Visual [Beginner]

A semigroup is a one-way clock of operators. At time zero it does nothing; as time advances it pushes every starting state along its trajectory, and the pushes compose by adding times.

![A time axis with operators T(0)=identity, T(t), T(s), and T(t+s) shown as boxes; an arrow from a starting state f flows through T(t) then T(s) and lands at the same place as flowing through T(t+s), illustrating the composition law T(t)T(s)=T(t+s).](/img/c0-semigroups-hille-yosida-placeholder.svg)

The generator $A$ is the velocity of this flow at the starting line: it records how fast a state begins to move the instant the clock starts. Knowing the generator is knowing the rule; the flow is its time-integrated form.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the simplest infinite-dimensional flow: rigid translation. Let a state be a function $f(x)$ on the line, and let the rule be "shift to the right at unit speed". After time $t$ the machine returns $T(t)f$, the function whose value at $x$ is $f(x - t)$.

Step 1. Check the start. At $t = 0$ the shift is by zero, so $T(0)f = f$. The machine does nothing at time zero.

Step 2. Check composition. Shift by $t$, then by $s$. First $T(t)f$ has value $f(x - t)$ at $x$. Applying $T(s)$ shifts again: the result at $x$ is the previous function evaluated at $x - s$, namely $f((x - s) - t) = f(x - t - s)$. That is exactly $T(t + s)f$. So $T(t)T(s) = T(t + s)$.

Step 3. Find the velocity. Take $f(x) = x^2$ and watch the value at a fixed point $x = 3$. We have $T(t)f$ at $x = 3$ equal to $(3 - t)^2 = 9 - 6t + t^2$. Its rate of change at $t = 0$ is $-6$. The general rule: the starting velocity is minus the slope of $f$. The generator of right-translation is "minus the derivative".

What this tells us: a concrete flow (sliding a graph) hides a generator (a derivative operator). The flow is smooth in time even though the generator involves differentiation, the operation that usually roughens functions. The semigroup tames the operator.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Let $X$ be a Banach space [02.11.04]. A **strongly continuous semigroup** (or **$C_0$-semigroup**) is a family $\{T(t)\}_{t \geq 0}$ of bounded linear operators $T(t) \in \mathcal{B}(X)$ satisfying

$$
T(0) = I, \qquad T(t + s) = T(t)\, T(s) \quad (t, s \geq 0),
$$

together with the **strong continuity** axiom

$$
\lim_{t \to 0^+} \| T(t) x - x \| = 0 \quad \text{for every } x \in X.
$$

Strong continuity is weaker than norm continuity of $t \mapsto T(t)$; the heat semigroup is strongly but not norm continuous at $t = 0$.

A standard consequence is an exponential bound: there exist $M \geq 1$ and $\omega \in \mathbb{R}$ with

$$
\| T(t) \| \leq M e^{\omega t} \quad (t \geq 0) \quad [\text{ref: TODO\_REF Pazy 1983, §1.2}].
$$

When $M = 1$ and $\omega = 0$ the semigroup is a **contraction semigroup**: $\| T(t) \| \leq 1$.

The **infinitesimal generator** of the semigroup is the operator $A$ defined by

$$
A x = \lim_{t \to 0^+} \frac{T(t) x - x}{t},
$$

with domain $D(A)$ equal to the set of $x \in X$ for which this limit exists in the norm of $X$. The generator is in general an unbounded operator [02.11.03]: $D(A)$ is a proper dense subspace and $A$ is closed.

For $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$, the **resolvent set** $\rho(A)$ consists of those $\lambda$ for which $\lambda I - A : D(A) \to X$ is a bijection with bounded inverse, the **resolvent**

$$
R(\lambda, A) = (\lambda I - A)^{-1} \in \mathcal{B}(X).
$$

An operator $A$ is **dissipative** if for every $x \in D(A)$ there is a normalised duality pairing witnessing $\operatorname{Re}\langle A x, x^* \rangle \leq 0$; concretely, on a Hilbert space $A$ is dissipative iff $\operatorname{Re}\langle A x, x \rangle \leq 0$ for all $x \in D(A)$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Strong continuity is not norm continuity.* If $t \mapsto T(t)$ were norm continuous at $0$, then $A$ would be bounded and $T(t) = e^{tA}$ a convergent series. Genuine PDE semigroups (heat, wave) have unbounded generators precisely because they are only strongly continuous.

- *The generator is not defined everywhere.* Writing "$A = $ the derivative" hides the domain. The translation semigroup on $L^2(\mathbb{R})$ has generator $-\frac{d}{dx}$ on the domain $H^1(\mathbb{R})$, not on all of $L^2$. The same formula on a different domain is a different operator and may fail to generate a semigroup.

- *Dissipativity alone does not generate.* A dissipative operator generates a contraction semigroup only when a range condition also holds: $\operatorname{ran}(\lambda I - A) = X$ for some (hence all) $\lambda > 0$. Dropping the range condition leaves merely symmetric-looking operators that have no flow.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Hille-Yosida, contraction case).** A linear operator $A$ on a Banach space $X$ is the infinitesimal generator of a $C_0$-semigroup of contractions $\{T(t)\}_{t \geq 0}$ if and only if

1. $A$ is closed and densely defined, and
2. every $\lambda > 0$ lies in $\rho(A)$ and the resolvent satisfies

$$
\| R(\lambda, A) \| \leq \frac{1}{\lambda} \quad (\lambda > 0) \quad [\text{ref: TODO\_REF Yosida 1948}].
$$

*Proof.* ($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $A$ generates a contraction semigroup. Density of $D(A)$ and closedness of $A$ are standard properties of generators (proved in the Full proof set). For $\lambda > 0$ define

$$
R(\lambda) x = \int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t} T(t) x \, dt,
$$

a convergent Bochner integral since $\| e^{-\lambda t} T(t) x \| \leq e^{-\lambda t} \| x \|$. A direct manipulation using the semigroup law shows $R(\lambda) = (\lambda I - A)^{-1}$: for $x \in D(A)$, integrating by parts in $t$ against $\frac{d}{dt} T(t) x = T(t) A x$ gives $R(\lambda)(\lambda I - A)x = x$, and the reverse identity follows similarly. The contraction bound yields

$$
\| R(\lambda) x \| \leq \int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t} \| T(t) x \| \, dt \leq \| x \| \int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t} \, dt = \frac{\| x \|}{\lambda}.
$$

($\Leftarrow$) Assume (1) and (2). The construction is the **Yosida approximation**. For $\lambda > 0$ set

$$
A_\lambda = \lambda A R(\lambda, A) = \lambda^2 R(\lambda, A) - \lambda I \in \mathcal{B}(X),
$$

the second equality from $\lambda A R(\lambda, A) = \lambda (\lambda I - (\lambda I - A)) R(\lambda, A) = \lambda^2 R(\lambda, A) - \lambda I$. Each $A_\lambda$ is bounded, so the exponentials $e^{t A_\lambda}$ are defined by their power series and form uniformly continuous semigroups.

First, $\lambda R(\lambda, A) x \to x$ as $\lambda \to \infty$ for every $x \in X$. For $x \in D(A)$ one has $\lambda R(\lambda, A) x - x = R(\lambda, A) A x$, whose norm is $\leq \| A x \| / \lambda \to 0$; density of $D(A)$ and the uniform bound $\| \lambda R(\lambda, A) \| \leq 1$ extend the limit to all of $X$. Consequently $A_\lambda x = \lambda R(\lambda, A) A x \to A x$ for $x \in D(A)$.

Next, each $e^{t A_\lambda}$ is a contraction. Writing $A_\lambda = \lambda^2 R(\lambda, A) - \lambda I$,

$$
\| e^{t A_\lambda} \| = e^{-\lambda t} \| e^{t \lambda^2 R(\lambda, A)} \| \leq e^{-\lambda t} \, e^{t \lambda^2 \| R(\lambda, A) \|} \leq e^{-\lambda t} e^{\lambda t} = 1,
$$

using $\| R(\lambda, A) \| \leq 1/\lambda$. The bounded generators $A_\lambda$ and $A_\mu$ commute (both are functions of the commuting resolvents), so for $x \in D(A)$,

$$
\| e^{t A_\lambda} x - e^{t A_\mu} x \| = \left\| \int_0^t \frac{d}{ds}\big(e^{(t - s) A_\mu} e^{s A_\lambda} x\big)\, ds \right\| \leq \int_0^t \| e^{(t-s)A_\mu} e^{s A_\lambda} (A_\lambda - A_\mu) x \| \, ds \leq t \, \| A_\lambda x - A_\mu x \|.
$$

The right side tends to $0$ as $\lambda, \mu \to \infty$, uniformly for $t$ in compact intervals. Thus $T(t) x = \lim_{\lambda \to \infty} e^{t A_\lambda} x$ exists for $x \in D(A)$, uniformly on compacta, and extends by density and the uniform contraction bound to all $x \in X$. The limit family $\{T(t)\}$ inherits $T(0) = I$, the semigroup law, contractivity, and strong continuity from the approximants. A final computation identifies the generator of $\{T(t)\}$ with $A$: for $x \in D(A)$, $T(t)x - x = \lim_\lambda \int_0^t e^{sA_\lambda} A_\lambda x \, ds = \int_0^t T(s) A x \, ds$, whose derivative at $t = 0$ is $A x$. $\square$

**Bridge.** The Yosida approximation is exactly the device that builds toward every later existence theorem for evolution equations: it replaces an unbounded generator by bounded surrogates $A_\lambda$ whose exponentials are honest power series, then passes to the limit. This is the foundational reason the abstract Cauchy problem $u' = A u$ is well posed precisely when $A$ generates a semigroup, and it generalises the matrix exponential $e^{tA}$ of finite-dimensional linear ODE theory to operators with no convergent series. The resolvent bound $\|R(\lambda, A)\| \le 1/\lambda$ is dual to the contraction bound $\|T(t)\| \le 1$ through the Laplace transform $R(\lambda, A) = \int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t} T(t)\, dt$; this is exactly the analytic content the theorem extracts. The same circle of ideas appears again in [02.13.03] the heat semigroup $e^{t\Delta}$, where dissipativity of the Laplacian is the input, and the central insight that a flow is recovered from the velocity at the start carries into the analytic-semigroup theory of parabolic problems. Putting these together, Hille-Yosida is the gateway from static functional analysis to dynamics.

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).**

Let $A$ generate a $C_0$-semigroup. Show that for $x \in D(A)$ the orbit $t \mapsto T(t)x$ is continuously differentiable with $\frac{d}{dt} T(t) x = A T(t) x = T(t) A x$, so $u(t) = T(t)x$ solves the abstract Cauchy problem $u' = A u$, $u(0) = x$.

The second form shows $T(t)x \in D(A)$ with $A T(t)x = T(t)Ax$, so the right derivative is $A T(t) x = T(t) A x$. The left derivative agrees by a parallel estimate, and $t \mapsto T(t) A x$ is continuous, so $t \mapsto T(t)x$ is $C^1$ and solves $u' = A u$, $u(0) = x$. ## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

The expected formalization would introduce a `C0Semigroup X` structure bundling the family `T : ℝ≥0 → (X →L[𝕜] X)` with the identity, semigroup-law, and strong-continuity fields; define the generator as a densely defined operator on a submodule domain via the strong limit of difference quotients; build the resolvent `R(λ, A) = (λ • 1 - A)⁻¹` and the Laplace representation `R(λ, A) x = ∫ t in Ioi 0, exp (-λ * t) • T t x`; and state Hille-Yosida as the equivalence between generation and the resolvent bound `‖(R(λ, A))^[n]‖ ≤ M / (λ - ω)^n`, with the Yosida approximation `A_λ = λ • A ∘ R(λ, A)` as the constructive witness.

## Advanced results [Master]

**General Hille-Yosida and the power bound.** Dropping contractivity, $A$ generates a $C_0$-semigroup with $\| T(t) \| \leq M e^{\omega t}$ if and only if $A$ is closed, densely defined, $(\omega, \infty) \subset \rho(A)$, and

$$
\| R(\lambda, A)^n \| \leq \frac{M}{(\lambda - \omega)^n} \quad (\lambda > \omega,\ n \geq 1).
$$

The power on the resolvent is essential: a bound on $\| R(\lambda, A) \|$ alone controls $M = 1$ but not general $M$, because $R(\lambda, A)^n = \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{(n-1)!}\frac{d^{n-1}}{d\lambda^{n-1}} R(\lambda, A)$ encodes the $n$-fold iterated Laplace transform $\int_0^\infty \frac{t^{n-1}}{(n-1)!} e^{-\lambda t} T(t)\, dt$.

**Lumer-Phillips theorem.** A densely defined operator $A$ generates a $C_0$-semigroup of contractions if and only if $A$ is dissipative and $\operatorname{ran}(\lambda_0 I - A) = X$ for some $\lambda_0 > 0$. Dissipativity gives $\| (\lambda I - A) x \| \geq \lambda \| x \|$, hence injectivity and the resolvent bound; the range condition supplies surjectivity. This is the working criterion in PDE, because dissipativity is an energy estimate and the range condition is solvability of $\lambda u - A u = f$ [ref: TODO_REF Lumer-Phillips 1961].

**Stone's theorem.** On a Hilbert space, $A$ generates a $C_0$-group of unitaries $\{T(t)\}_{t \in \mathbb{R}}$ if and only if $A = iH$ with $H$ self-adjoint [02.11.03]. Then $T(t) = e^{itH}$, the time evolution of quantum mechanics; the Schrödinger group and the wave group are the canonical instances [ref: TODO_REF Stone 1932].

**Analytic semigroups and sectoriality.** An operator $A$ is **sectorial** of angle $\theta \in (0, \pi/2]$ if its resolvent set contains a sector $\Sigma_{\pi/2 + \theta} = \{ \lambda \neq 0 : |\arg \lambda| < \pi/2 + \theta \}$ and $\| R(\lambda, A) \| \leq M / |\lambda|$ there. Equivalently, $A$ generates a semigroup $T(t)$ that extends holomorphically to a sector in the complex $t$-plane and satisfies the smoothing estimate

$$
\| A T(t) \| \leq \frac{C}{t} \quad (t > 0).
$$

The estimate $\| A T(t) \| \leq C/t$ is the abstract form of parabolic smoothing: it says the semigroup instantly maps $X$ into $D(A)$, the analytic content of "the heat equation smooths". The Laplacian on $L^p(\mathbb{R}^n)$ is sectorial of angle $\pi/2$, so $e^{t\Delta}$ is analytic [02.13.03].

**Synthesis.** The four generation theorems are one structure seen from four angles, and putting these together exposes the central insight of the subject: a linear evolution is determined by the velocity field $A$ at the origin of time, and the analytic fingerprint of that field is its resolvent. Hille-Yosida reads generation off the iterated resolvent bound; Lumer-Phillips reads it off dissipativity, which is exactly the energy estimate a PDE supplies, so the abstract theory and the concrete a priori estimate are dual descriptions of the same flow. Stone's theorem is the foundational reason quantum dynamics is unitary: self-adjointness of the Hamiltonian is the generation condition for a group rather than a semigroup, and the reversibility of $e^{itH}$ generalises the finite-dimensional fact that skew-Hermitian matrices exponentiate to unitaries. Analytic-semigroup sectoriality, with its smoothing bound $\|A T(t)\| \le C/t$, is dual to dissipativity-plus-resolvent-decay and is exactly the abstract statement that parabolic flows regularise instantly; this is the bridge from the heat semigroup $e^{t\Delta}$ of [02.13.03] to the full nonlinear theory, where the generation theory of the linearisation appears again as the engine of fixed-point existence proofs for quasilinear and reaction-diffusion equations.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (the generator is densely defined and closed).** Let $\{T(t)\}$ be a $C_0$-semigroup with generator $A$. Then $D(A)$ is dense in $X$ and $A$ is a closed operator.

*Proof.* For $x \in X$ and $r > 0$ set $x_r = \frac{1}{r}\int_0^r T(s) x \, ds$, a convergent Riemann/Bochner integral by strong continuity. Then

$$
\frac{T(h) - I}{h} x_r = \frac{1}{h}\left( \frac{1}{r}\int_0^r T(s+h)x\, ds - \frac{1}{r}\int_0^r T(s)x\, ds \right) = \frac{1}{r}\cdot\frac{1}{h}\left( \int_r^{r+h} T(s)x\, ds - \int_0^h T(s)x\, ds \right).
$$

As $h \to 0^+$ the right side tends to $\frac{1}{r}(T(r)x - x)$ by continuity of $s \mapsto T(s)x$. Hence $x_r \in D(A)$ with $A x_r = \frac{1}{r}(T(r)x - x)$. Moreover $x_r \to x$ as $r \to 0^+$, again by strong continuity, so $D(A)$ is dense.

Closedness was established in Exercise 6 via $T(t)x_n - x_n = \int_0^t T(s) A x_n\, ds$: if $x_n \to x$ and $A x_n \to y$, the limit identity $T(t)x - x = \int_0^t T(s)y\, ds$ divided by $t$ and sent to $0$ gives $x \in D(A)$, $Ax = y$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (dissipativity yields the resolvent bound).** If $A$ is dissipative, then for every $\lambda > 0$ and $x \in D(A)$,

$$
\| (\lambda I - A) x \| \geq \lambda \| x \|.
$$

In particular $\lambda I - A$ is injective with $\| R(\lambda, A) \| \leq 1/\lambda$ whenever it is surjective.

*Proof.* By dissipativity choose a norming functional $x^* \in X^*$ with $\| x^* \| = 1$, $\langle x, x^* \rangle = \| x \|$, and $\operatorname{Re}\langle A x, x^* \rangle \leq 0$. Then

$$
\| (\lambda I - A) x \| \geq \operatorname{Re}\langle (\lambda I - A)x, x^* \rangle = \lambda \| x \| - \operatorname{Re}\langle A x, x^* \rangle \geq \lambda \| x \|.
$$

Injectivity is immediate. If $\lambda I - A$ is also onto, then for $y = (\lambda I - A)x$ we get $\| R(\lambda, A) y \| = \| x \| \leq \| y \| / \lambda$, i.e. $\| R(\lambda, A) \| \leq 1/\lambda$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (uniqueness of the semigroup from its generator).** A $C_0$-semigroup is determined by its generator: if two $C_0$-semigroups $\{T(t)\}$ and $\{S(t)\}$ have the same generator $A$, then $T(t) = S(t)$ for all $t \geq 0$.

*Proof.* Fix $t > 0$ and $x \in D(A)$. The map $s \mapsto S(t - s) T(s) x$ on $[0, t]$ is differentiable with derivative $-A S(t-s) T(s)x + S(t-s) A T(s) x = 0$, using that $S$ and $T$ have generator $A$ and that $T(s)x \in D(A)$. Hence the map is constant; comparing $s = 0$ and $s = t$ gives $S(t)x = T(t)x$. Density of $D(A)$ and boundedness of $S(t), T(t)$ extend the equality to all of $X$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Unbounded self-adjoint operators [02.11.03]** — Stone's theorem identifies generators of unitary groups with $iH$, $H$ self-adjoint; the spectral theorem there builds $e^{itH}$ and the deficiency-index theory decides which symmetric operators generate a group at all.

- **Banach spaces [02.11.04]** — the entire theory lives on a Banach space; the resolvent bound, the Bochner integral $\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t}T(t)\,dt$, and the uniform boundedness of $\{e^{tA_\lambda}\}$ are Banach-space estimates, and contractivity is measured in the Banach norm.

- **Heat equation, heat kernel, and Duhamel's principle [02.13.03]** — the heat semigroup $e^{t\Delta} f = \Phi(\cdot, t) * f$ is the prototype analytic semigroup; its generator is the Laplacian, dissipativity is the energy inequality, and Duhamel's principle is the variation-of-parameters formula $u(t) = T(t)u_0 + \int_0^t T(t-s)f(s)\,ds$ for the inhomogeneous Cauchy problem.

- **Poisson equation and fundamental solution [02.13.02]** — solving $\lambda u - \Delta u = f$ for the resolvent of the Laplacian is the elliptic range condition feeding Lumer-Phillips, linking semigroup generation to elliptic solvability.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The theory crystallised in 1948 when Einar Hille and Kosaku Yosida independently characterised the generators of strongly continuous contraction semigroups; Yosida's note in the *Journal of the Mathematical Society of Japan* introduced the approximation $A_\lambda = \lambda A R(\lambda, A)$ that bears his name [ref: TODO_REF Yosida 1948], and Hille's account appeared in the 1948 first edition of his Colloquium volume, expanded with Ralph Phillips in the 1957 *Functional Analysis and Semi-Groups* [ref: TODO_REF Hille 1957]. Marshall Stone had already proved the unitary-group case in 1932 [ref: TODO_REF Stone 1932], giving the mathematical foundation for the time evolution in von Neumann's formulation of quantum mechanics. Günter Lumer and Ralph Phillips recast generation through dissipativity in 1961 [ref: TODO_REF Lumer-Phillips 1961], producing the criterion that PDE practitioners use because dissipativity is an energy estimate. Analytic-semigroup theory grew from the parabolic regularity results of the 1950s and 1960s and reached its modern functional-analytic form in the monographs of Pazy and of Engel and Nagel.
