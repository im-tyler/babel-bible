## Intuition [Beginner]

A diffusion equation describes a quantity that smooths itself out as time runs forward: heat in a bar, a chemical seeping through a gel, a population spreading across a habitat. The companion unit on the heat equation handed you one such equation and one explicit formula, the spreading Gaussian, that solved it on an infinite bar with constant material. But most real diffusion happens in a bounded region, through a material whose conductivity varies from place to place and even drifts with time. There is no tidy Gaussian for that. The question becomes sharper: does a solution even exist, and is it the only one?

This unit answers yes by building the solution out of simple pieces instead of guessing a formula. Pick a handful of standard "shapes" that a function on the region can have, the lowest-pitched vibration modes of the region, and look for the best approximate solution that is a blend of just those few shapes. Each shape has a strength that changes over time, and plugging the blend into the diffusion rule turns the partial differential equation into an ordinary system of rate equations for those strengths, the kind of system that always has a solution. Then add more shapes, and more, and watch the approximations settle down toward an honest solution of the full equation.

Why should adding more shapes settle down rather than blow up? Because diffusion drains energy. At every instant the total "size" of the solution can only decrease, apart from whatever the source term pumps in, and a clean bookkeeping inequality turns that physical fact into a uniform numerical bound on every approximation at once, no matter how many shapes you used. A family of approximations that is uniformly bounded cannot run off to infinity; it must have a settling-down point, and that point is the solution.

This is the energy method, and the shape-by-shape construction is the Galerkin method. Together they give existence, uniqueness, and a guarantee that the solution depends continuously on the data, for diffusion equations far too general for any explicit formula. The price is that the solution is found in an averaged, weak sense first, exactly as in the companion unit on weak solutions of the steady-state problem; smoothness is recovered afterward.

## Visual [Beginner]

The picture to hold is a stack of approximations climbing toward a true solution, each one a blend of a fixed handful of standard shapes, all kept from blowing up by a single energy ceiling.

![A three-panel diagram. The left panel shows a bounded region with three simple standing-wave shapes drawn over it, labelled "mode 1, mode 2, mode 3 (fixed shapes)"; below them three dials labelled "strengths that change in time" feed into a box labelled "ordinary rate equations". The middle panel shows a ladder of curves stacked vertically, each labelled "blend of the first m shapes", with the curves getting closer together as you climb, and a dashed curve at the top labelled "the true weak solution"; a horizontal bar across all of them is labelled "one energy ceiling holds every rung". The right panel shows a curve of total energy starting at the initial value and sloping downward over time, with a small upward bump labelled "energy added by the source", over a baseline labelled "diffusion can only drain energy".](/img/galerkin-existence-parabolic-placeholder.svg)

Read the panels left to right. The left panel is the reduction: instead of searching among all functions, you search among blends of a few fixed shapes. The diffusion rule, restricted to those blends, becomes a closed system of ordinary rate equations for the time-varying strengths, and such systems are solvable by standard ordinary-differential-equation theory.

The middle panel is the limit. Each rung of the ladder uses more shapes than the one below, so each is a finer approximation. The rungs crowd together as you climb because a single energy ceiling, drawn as the horizontal bar, holds all of them at once; nothing on the ladder can escape upward. A bounded climbing family has a settling-down point, the dashed curve at the top, and that limit solves the full equation.

The right panel is the source of the ceiling. The total energy of any approximation starts at the energy of the initial data and, because diffusion only smooths and never sharpens, can only fall, except for the controlled amount the source term feeds in. That one-way energy budget is the whole reason the construction converges.

## Worked example [Beginner]

We run the shape-by-shape recipe by hand on the simplest bounded diffusion problem and watch the energy fall. Take the bar from zero to $\pi$, held at zero temperature at both ends, governed by the rule "rate of temperature change equals curvature", which is $u_t = u_{xx}$. Start with the initial temperature profile $u(x, 0) = 3\sin x + 4\sin 2x$, already a blend of two standard shapes.

Step 1. Choose the shapes. The natural shapes for a bar clamped at both ends are the standing waves $\sin x$, $\sin 2x$, $\sin 3x$, and so on. Each one keeps its shape under the diffusion rule and only changes in strength, which is exactly what makes the recipe close up neatly.

Step 2. Write the blend. Look for $u(x, t) = a_1(t)\sin x + a_2(t)\sin 2x$, with the two strengths $a_1(t)$ and $a_2(t)$ to be found. The starting strengths are $a_1(0) = 3$ and $a_2(0) = 4$, read straight off the initial profile.

Step 3. Turn the rule into rate equations. The curvature of $\sin kx$ is $-k^2\sin kx$. Matching the rate of change of each shape to its curvature gives two separate rate equations: $a_1'(t) = -1\cdot a_1(t)$ and $a_2'(t) = -4\,a_2(t)$. The shapes have decoupled into independent decays.

Step 4. Solve the rate equations. Each is simple exponential decay: $a_1(t) = 3e^{-t}$ and $a_2(t) = 4e^{-4t}$. So $u(x, t) = 3e^{-t}\sin x + 4e^{-4t}\sin 2x$. The higher shape, $\sin 2x$, decays four times faster, the precise statement that finer wrinkles smooth out first.

Step 5. Watch the energy fall. Measure energy by the total of $u^2$ across the bar. Because the shapes are orthogonal, this total is $\frac{\pi}{2}\big(a_1(t)^2 + a_2(t)^2\big) = \frac{\pi}{2}\big(9e^{-2t} + 16e^{-8t}\big)$. At time zero this is $\frac{\pi}{2}\cdot 25$. At any later time both terms are smaller, so the energy has strictly fallen, and it heads to zero as time runs on.

What this tells us: restricting to a few shapes turned the partial differential equation into a handful of decoupled decays we solved exactly, and the energy, measured as the total of $u^2$, only decreased. With no source feeding the bar, the energy ceiling is just its starting value, and every approximation stays under it forever. That falling energy is the engine that, in the general case, keeps the shape-by-shape approximations from blowing up as we add more shapes.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

Throughout, $\Omega \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is open and bounded, $T > 0$ is fixed, and the spatial operator at each time is the divergence-form second-order operator of [02.16.04],
$$L(t)u = -\sum_{i,j=1}^n \partial_i\big(a^{ij}(x,t)\,\partial_j u\big) + \sum_{i=1}^n b^i(x,t)\,\partial_i u + c(x,t)\,u,$$
with coefficients $a^{ij}, b^i, c \in L^\infty(\Omega \times (0,T))$, $a^{ij} = a^{ji}$, and uniform ellipticity $\sum_{ij} a^{ij}(x,t)\,\xi_i\xi_j \ge \theta\,|\xi|^2$ for a.e. $(x,t)$ and all $\xi \in \mathbb{R}^n$. The time-dependent bilinear form is
$$B[u, v; t] = \int_\Omega \Big(\sum_{ij} a^{ij}(\cdot,t)\,\partial_j u\,\partial_i v + \sum_i b^i(\cdot,t)\,(\partial_i u)\,v + c(\cdot,t)\,u\,v\Big)\,dx,$$
bounded and Gårding-coercive on $H^1_0(\Omega)$ uniformly in $t$ by the estimates of [02.16.04].

**Definition (Gelfand triple).** Let $V = H^1_0(\Omega)$ and $H = L^2(\Omega)$, with $V \hookrightarrow H$ continuous and dense. Identifying $H$ with its own dual $H^*$ through the Riesz map of [02.11.08], and composing with the dual of the inclusion $V \hookrightarrow H$, yields the **Gelfand triple** (or rigged Hilbert space)
$$V \hookrightarrow H \cong H^* \hookrightarrow V^*, \qquad V = H^1_0(\Omega),\ H = L^2(\Omega),\ V^* = H^{-1}(\Omega),$$
with both inclusions continuous and dense. The duality pairing $\langle \cdot,\cdot\rangle : V^* \times V \to \mathbb{R}$ extends the inner product of $H$: for $h \in H$ and $v \in V$, $\langle h, v\rangle = (h, v)_H$.

**Definition (Bochner spaces).** For a Banach space $X$ and $1 \le p \le \infty$, $L^p(0,T;X)$ is the space of (strongly measurable) functions $u : (0,T) \to X$ with $\|u\|_{L^p(0,T;X)} = \big(\int_0^T \|u(t)\|_X^p\,dt\big)^{1/p} < \infty$ (essential supremum for $p = \infty$); these are the **Bochner spaces** built on the Bochner integral of [24.01.01]. A function $u \in L^2(0,T;V)$ has **weak time-derivative** $u' \in L^2(0,T;V^*)$ if
$$\int_0^T \varphi'(t)\,u(t)\,dt = -\int_0^T \varphi(t)\,u'(t)\,dt \quad \text{in } V^*, \qquad \text{for all } \varphi \in C_c^\infty(0,T).$$
The **Bochner-Sobolev space** of solutions is
$$W(0,T) = \big\{\,u \in L^2(0,T;V) : u' \in L^2(0,T;V^*)\,\big\}, \qquad \|u\|_{W(0,T)}^2 = \|u\|_{L^2(0,T;V)}^2 + \|u'\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)}^2.$$

**Definition (weak solution of the parabolic problem).** Given $f \in L^2(0,T;V^*)$ and $g \in H$, a function $u \in W(0,T)$ is a **weak solution** of the initial/boundary-value problem
$$u_t + L(t)u = f \ \text{in } \Omega \times (0,T], \qquad u = 0 \ \text{on } \partial\Omega \times (0,T], \qquad u(\cdot,0) = g \ \text{on } \Omega,$$
if $u(0) = g$ in $H$ (a meaningful pointwise statement by the embedding theorem below) and
$$\langle u'(t), v\rangle + B[u(t), v; t] = \langle f(t), v\rangle \qquad \text{for all } v \in V \text{ and a.e. } t \in (0,T).$$

**Definition (Galerkin approximation).** Let $\{w_k\}_{k\ge 1} \subseteq V$ be the orthonormal-in-$H$, orthogonal-in-$V$ basis of Dirichlet eigenfunctions, $-\Delta w_k = \lambda_k w_k$ in $\Omega$, $w_k \in H^1_0(\Omega)$, supplied by the spectral theorem for the compact resolvent of [02.16.04]; $\{w_k\}$ is complete in both $H$ and $V$. The **$m$-th Galerkin approximation** is
$$u_m(t) = \sum_{k=1}^m d_m^k(t)\,w_k \in V_m := \mathrm{span}\{w_1,\dots,w_m\},$$
where the coefficient vector $d_m(t) = (d_m^1,\dots,d_m^m)$ solves the finite-dimensional ODE system
$$(u_m'(t), w_k)_H + B[u_m(t), w_k; t] = \langle f(t), w_k\rangle \quad (k = 1,\dots,m), \qquad d_m^k(0) = (g, w_k)_H.$$

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *$u'$ lives in $V^*$, not in $H$.* For a generic weak solution the time-derivative $u_t$ is only an element of $L^2(0,T;H^{-1})$; demanding $u' \in L^2(0,T;H)$ is a genuine regularity gain (it holds for the heat equation with $g \in V$ and $f \in L^2(0,T;H)$, the "improved" estimate) and fails in general. The whole point of the Gelfand triple is that the equation $u' + L u = f$ is an identity in $V^*$, not in $H$.

- *The pairing identity needs the embedding, not just integration by parts.* The formula $\frac{d}{dt}\,\tfrac12\|u(t)\|_H^2 = \langle u'(t), u(t)\rangle$ — the engine of every energy estimate — is valid precisely for $u \in W(0,T)$ and requires the Lions-Aubin embedding $W(0,T) \hookrightarrow C([0,T];H)$. Writing it down for $u \in L^2(0,T;V)$ alone is meaningless: such a $u$ has no pointwise-in-time values in $H$ and no initial trace.

- *Coercivity may only hold after a shift.* The Gårding inequality gives $B[u,u;t] \ge \beta\|u\|_V^2 - \gamma\|u\|_H^2$, not coercivity outright. The substitution $u = e^{\gamma t}\tilde u$ converts the equation for $\tilde u$ into one with the genuinely coercive form $B + \gamma(\cdot,\cdot)_H$; forgetting this shift makes the Grönwall step circular when $\gamma > 0$.

- *Compactness is needed only for nonlinear passage; the linear limit is purely weak.* For the linear equation, weak and weak-* limits of the Galerkin sequence suffice to pass to the limit in every term, because all terms are linear in $u_m$. The Aubin-Lions strong $L^2(0,T;H)$ compactness becomes essential only when a nonlinearity must be passed to the limit; invoking it for the linear problem is unnecessary, though harmless.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Galerkin existence and uniqueness).** Let $L(t)$ be uniformly elliptic with $L^\infty$ coefficients on $\Omega \times (0,T)$, so that $B[\cdot,\cdot;t]$ is bounded with constant $\Lambda$ and satisfies the Gårding inequality $B[u,u;t] \ge \beta\|u\|_V^2 - \gamma\|u\|_H^2$ uniformly in $t$. Then for every $f \in L^2(0,T;V^*)$ and $g \in H = L^2(\Omega)$ there exists a unique weak solution $u \in W(0,T)$ of the parabolic problem, and it obeys the a priori estimate
$$\|u\|_{L^\infty(0,T;H)} + \|u\|_{L^2(0,T;V)} + \|u'\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)} \le C\big(\|f\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)} + \|g\|_H\big),$$
with $C = C(\theta, \Lambda, \gamma, T)$ [ref: TODO_REF Evans 2010 §7.1] [ref: TODO_REF Lions 1961].

*Proof.* **Step 1 (the finite-dimensional system is solvable).** Fix $m$. Writing $u_m(t) = \sum_{k\le m} d_m^k(t) w_k$ and using $(w_k, w_\ell)_H = \delta_{k\ell}$, the Galerkin equations read $\dot d_m^k(t) + \sum_{\ell\le m} B[w_\ell, w_k; t]\,d_m^\ell(t) = \langle f(t), w_k\rangle$, a linear ODE system $\dot d_m = -E(t)\,d_m + F(t)$ with $E(t)_{k\ell} = B[w_\ell, w_k; t] \in L^\infty(0,T)$ and $F(t)_k = \langle f(t), w_k\rangle \in L^2(0,T)$. By the Carathéodory existence theorem for ODEs with $L^\infty$/$L^2$ coefficients there is a unique absolutely continuous $d_m$ on $[0,T]$ with the prescribed initial data, hence a unique $u_m \in H^1(0,T;V_m)$.

**Step 2 (energy estimate, uniform in $m$).** Multiply the $k$-th Galerkin equation by $d_m^k(t)$ and sum over $k \le m$; since $u_m(t) \in V_m$ this is exactly the test choice $v = u_m(t)$:
$$(u_m'(t), u_m(t))_H + B[u_m(t), u_m(t); t] = \langle f(t), u_m(t)\rangle.$$
The first term is $\frac{d}{dt}\,\tfrac12\|u_m(t)\|_H^2$. Gårding bounds the second from below by $\beta\|u_m\|_V^2 - \gamma\|u_m\|_H^2$, and the right side is estimated by $\|f\|_{V^*}\|u_m\|_V \le \tfrac{\beta}{2}\|u_m\|_V^2 + \tfrac{1}{2\beta}\|f\|_{V^*}^2$ via Young's inequality. Rearranging,
$$\frac{d}{dt}\,\|u_m(t)\|_H^2 + \beta\|u_m(t)\|_V^2 \le 2\gamma\|u_m(t)\|_H^2 + \tfrac{1}{\beta}\|f(t)\|_{V^*}^2.$$
Dropping the nonnegative $\beta\|u_m\|_V^2$ and applying the differential Grönwall inequality [ref: TODO_REF Grönwall 1919] gives, for $0 \le t \le T$,
$$\|u_m(t)\|_H^2 \le e^{2\gamma t}\Big(\|u_m(0)\|_H^2 + \tfrac1\beta\|f\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)}^2\Big) \le e^{2\gamma T}\Big(\|g\|_H^2 + \tfrac1\beta\|f\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)}^2\Big),$$
since $\|u_m(0)\|_H \le \|g\|_H$ ($u_m(0)$ is the $H$-orthogonal projection of $g$ onto $V_m$). This bounds $\|u_m\|_{L^\infty(0,T;H)}$. Integrating the rearranged inequality over $(0,T)$ and using the just-proved sup bound on the $2\gamma\|u_m\|_H^2$ term bounds $\int_0^T \beta\|u_m\|_V^2\,dt$, hence $\|u_m\|_{L^2(0,T;V)}$. Both bounds are independent of $m$.

**Step 3 (estimate on the time-derivative).** Fix $v \in V$ with $\|v\|_V \le 1$ and split $v = v_1 + v_2$ with $v_1 \in V_m$ the $H$-orthogonal projection and $v_2 \perp_H V_m$. Then $(u_m'(t), v)_H = (u_m'(t), v_1)_H = \langle f(t), v_1\rangle - B[u_m(t), v_1; t]$, so $|(u_m'(t), v)_H| \le (\|f(t)\|_{V^*} + \Lambda\|u_m(t)\|_V)\|v_1\|_V \le \|f(t)\|_{V^*} + \Lambda\|u_m(t)\|_V$, using $\|v_1\|_V \le \|v\|_V \le 1$. Taking the supremum over such $v$ identifies $u_m'(t) \in V^*$ with $\|u_m'(t)\|_{V^*} \le \|f(t)\|_{V^*} + \Lambda\|u_m(t)\|_V$, whence $\|u_m'\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)} \le \|f\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)} + \Lambda\|u_m\|_{L^2(0,T;V)}$, again bounded uniformly in $m$.

**Step 4 (passage to the limit).** Steps 2-3 give $u_m$ bounded in $L^2(0,T;V)$ and $u_m'$ bounded in $L^2(0,T;V^*)$. Both spaces are reflexive (Hilbert), so by Banach-Alaoglu / weak sequential compactness there is a subsequence with $u_m \rightharpoonup u$ in $L^2(0,T;V)$ and $u_m' \rightharpoonup u'$ in $L^2(0,T;V^*)$ (the weak limit of the derivatives is the derivative of the weak limit, since distributional differentiation is weakly continuous). Hence $u \in W(0,T)$. Fix $N$ and $\psi \in C^1([0,T])$ with $\psi(T) = 0$, and a fixed $w_k$ with $k \le N$. For $m \ge N$, multiply the Galerkin identity by $\psi$ and integrate by parts in time:
$$\int_0^T \big[(u_m, w_k)_H\,(-\psi'(t)) + B[u_m, w_k; t]\,\psi(t)\big]\,dt = \int_0^T \langle f, w_k\rangle\psi\,dt + (u_m(0), w_k)_H\,\psi(0).$$
Every term is linear and continuous in $u_m$ for the weak topologies, so passing $m \to \infty$ along the subsequence replaces $u_m$ by $u$ and $u_m(0)$ by $g$ (the projections converge to $g$ in $H$). The resulting identity holds for every $w_k$, and by density of $\{w_k\}$ in $V$ for every $v \in V$; undoing the integration by parts shows $\langle u'(t), v\rangle + B[u(t),v;t] = \langle f(t),v\rangle$ for a.e. $t$ and $u(0) = g$. Thus $u$ is a weak solution, and the a priori estimate is the limit of the uniform bounds by weak lower semicontinuity of the norms.

**Step 5 (uniqueness).** If $u$ is a weak solution with $f = 0$, $g = 0$, test the equation with $v = u(t)$ (legitimate by the embedding $W(0,T) \hookrightarrow C([0,T];H)$ and the pairing identity below): $\frac{d}{dt}\,\tfrac12\|u(t)\|_H^2 + B[u(t),u(t);t] = 0$, so $\frac{d}{dt}\|u\|_H^2 \le 2\gamma\|u\|_H^2$, and Grönwall with $\|u(0)\|_H = 0$ forces $\|u(t)\|_H \equiv 0$. Two solutions with the same data thus coincide. $\square$

**Bridge.** The energy estimate is the foundational reason the construction converges: the Gårding coercivity of [02.16.04], the same inequality that proved elliptic existence, now controls the time-integrated $V$-norm, while the $H$-norm of the solution is dominated for all time by the data through Grönwall — this is exactly the bounded coercive form of the stationary theory promoted to an evolution by integrating in time. The abstract Lax-Milgram solvability of the elliptic problem appears again here at every fixed time as the solvability of the Galerkin ODE matrix $E(t)$, and the whole scheme builds toward the semigroup picture, where the operator $-L$ generates the solution flow. Putting these together, the central insight is that a parabolic equation is an elliptic energy inequality integrated against time and closed by Grönwall, so existence costs no explicit kernel and no Fourier analysis; the bridge is that uniqueness and continuous dependence both fall out of the single identity $\frac{d}{dt}\tfrac12\|u\|_H^2 = \langle u', u\rangle$, which generalises the Beginner-tier falling-energy picture into a rigorous statement valid for variable, time-dependent coefficients.

**Exercise 7 (hard, symbolic).**

Prove the improved energy estimate. Suppose $g \in V = H^1_0(\Omega)$, $f \in L^2(0,T;H)$, and (for simplicity) $L = L^*$ symmetric time-independent coercive. Test the Galerkin equation with $v = u_m'(t)$ and deduce a bound on $\|u_m'\|_{L^2(0,T;H)}$ and $\|u_m\|_{L^\infty(0,T;V)}$ uniform in $m$, hence $u' \in L^2(0,T;H)$ and $u \in L^\infty(0,T;V)$ for the limit.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

With $v = u_m'$, the term $B[u_m, u_m']$ is $\frac{d}{dt}\,\tfrac12 B[u_m,u_m]$ when $B$ is symmetric and time-independent. The left side then produces $\|u_m'\|_H^2$ plus a perfect time-derivative; bound the right side by Young's inequality.

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Choosing $v = u_m'(t) \in V_m$ in the Galerkin identity gives $(u_m', u_m')_H + B[u_m, u_m'] = (f, u_m')_H$. Since $B$ is symmetric and time-independent, $B[u_m, u_m'] = \frac{d}{dt}\,\tfrac12 B[u_m, u_m]$. Absorbing $\tfrac12\|u_m'\|_H^2$ on the left, $\tfrac12\|u_m'\|_H^2 + \frac{d}{dt}\,\tfrac12 B[u_m,u_m] \le \tfrac12\|f\|_H^2$. Weak-* limits then give $u' \in L^2(0,T;H)$ and $u \in L^\infty(0,T;V)$, with $Lu = f - u' \in L^2(0,T;H)$, so $u(t) \in H^2 \cap H^1_0$ for a.e. $t$ by elliptic regularity.

## Advanced results [Master]

The Galerkin/energy existence theorem sits inside a wider structure. The semigroup viewpoint of Hille-Yosida recasts the autonomous case as an abstract Cauchy problem generated by $-L$; the Lions-Aubin compactness lemma upgrades weak convergence to the strong convergence needed for nonlinear and quasilinear problems; the variational Lions theorem replaces the eigenbasis by an arbitrary dense sequence and handles genuinely non-self-adjoint, time-dependent forms; parabolic regularity bootstraps the weak solution to a classical one; and the same apparatus, with the energy identity replaced by a conserved quantity, treats the hyperbolic (wave) equation. Each refines the Galerkin argument of the Intermediate tier.

**Theorem 1 (semigroup representation; autonomous case).** Let $L$ be time-independent, with $-L$ generating, via the Hille-Yosida theorem, a strongly continuous (in fact analytic) contraction-type semigroup $\{e^{-tL}\}_{t\ge 0}$ on $H$ [ref: TODO_REF Hille 1948] [ref: TODO_REF Yosida 1948]. Then the weak solution constructed by Galerkin coincides with the mild/semigroup solution
$$u(t) = e^{-tL}g + \int_0^t e^{-(t-s)L}f(s)\,ds,$$
the Duhamel/variation-of-parameters formula of [02.13.03] lifted to the abstract operator. Analyticity of the semigroup encodes the parabolic smoothing: for $t > 0$, $e^{-tL}$ maps $H$ into the domain of every power of $L$, so $u(t)$ is spatially smooth even for $g \in H$ only. The Galerkin energy estimate is, in this language, the dissipativity $\mathrm{Re}\,(Lv,v)_H \ge -\gamma\|v\|_H^2$ that Hille-Yosida requires of a generator.

**Theorem 2 (Aubin-Lions-Simon compactness).** Let $V \hookrightarrow\hookrightarrow H \hookrightarrow V^*$ with the first embedding compact. Then
$$\big\{\,u \in L^2(0,T;V) : u' \in L^2(0,T;V^*)\,\big\} = W(0,T) \hookrightarrow\hookrightarrow L^2(0,T;H)$$
compactly [ref: TODO_REF Aubin 1963] [ref: TODO_REF Lions 1969]. The proof interpolates: the compact embedding $V \hookrightarrow\hookrightarrow H$ makes a $W(0,T)$-bounded sequence precompact in $H$ at a.e. fixed time, and the uniform $V^*$-bound on $u'$ provides equicontinuity in time (an Ehrling-inequality argument: $\|v\|_H \le \epsilon\|v\|_V + C_\epsilon\|v\|_{V^*}$). This is the lemma that lets the Galerkin method pass nonlinearities to the limit: a quasilinear term $a(u_m)\nabla u_m$ converges because $u_m \to u$ strongly in $L^2(0,T;H)$, hence a.e. after a further subsequence.

**Theorem 3 (Lions' variational existence; general time-dependent forms).** Let $\{B[\cdot,\cdot;t]\}$ be a family of bounded bilinear forms on $V$, measurable in $t$, with the uniform Gårding inequality $B[v,v;t] \ge \beta\|v\|_V^2 - \gamma\|v\|_H^2$. For every $f \in L^2(0,T;V^*)$ and $g \in H$ there is a unique $u \in W(0,T)$ with $\langle u'(t),v\rangle + B[u(t),v;t] = \langle f(t),v\rangle$ for all $v \in V$, a.e. $t$, and $u(0) = g$ [ref: TODO_REF Lions 1961] [ref: TODO_REF Lions-Magenes 1972 Ch. 3]. The proof replaces the eigenbasis by any sequence $\{w_k\}$ dense in $V$ (no spectral theory needed) and runs the same energy estimate and weak-limit passage; the inf-sup structure of the space-time bilinear form on $L^2(0,T;V)$ supplies existence à la Babuška-Nečas [02.16.04]. This is the form in which the method generalizes to Navier-Stokes and to monotone-operator equations.

**Theorem 4 (parabolic regularity).** If, in addition, $g \in H^1_0(\Omega)$, $f \in L^2(0,T;L^2(\Omega))$, and $a^{ij} \in C^1$ with $\partial_t a^{ij} \in L^\infty$, then the weak solution satisfies $u \in L^\infty(0,T;H^1_0) \cap L^2(0,T;H^2)$ and $u' \in L^2(0,T;L^2)$, with
$$\mathrm{ess\,sup}_{[0,T]}\|u(t)\|_{H^1_0}^2 + \int_0^T \big(\|u\|_{H^2}^2 + \|u'\|_{L^2}^2\big)\,dt \le C\big(\|f\|_{L^2(0,T;L^2)}^2 + \|g\|_{H^1_0}^2\big)$$
[ref: TODO_REF Evans 2010 §7.1]. Higher regularity follows by differentiating the equation in $t$ and bootstrapping with the interior $H^2$ elliptic estimate of [02.16.04]; with smooth data and compatibility conditions the weak solution is the classical solution. The mechanism is the improved energy estimate (Exercise 7): testing with $u_m'$ rather than $u_m$ trades one time-derivative for one elliptic gain.

**Theorem 5 (the hyperbolic parallel).** For the second-order hyperbolic problem $u'' + L(t)u = f$ with $u(0)=g$, $u'(0)=h$, the same Galerkin scheme produces a unique weak solution $u \in L^\infty(0,T;V)$ with $u' \in L^\infty(0,T;H)$ and $u'' \in L^2(0,T;V^*)$. The energy estimate now tests with $u_m'$ and uses the conserved quantity $E_m(t) = \tfrac12\|u_m'(t)\|_H^2 + \tfrac12 B[u_m(t),u_m(t);t]$, whose time-derivative is controlled rather than sign-definite; there is no smoothing and no gain of regularity, the signature contrast between parabolic dissipation and hyperbolic conservation. The wave equation [02.13.03 successor chapter] is the constant-coefficient instance.

**Synthesis.** The energy estimate is the foundational reason the Galerkin scheme converges, and it is exactly the Gårding coercivity of [02.16.04] integrated against time and closed by Grönwall: the time-integrated $V$-norm is controlled by ellipticity while the $H$-norm is propagated by the data, so existence costs no kernel and no Fourier analysis. This is dual to the elliptic theory in a precise sense — the stationary Lax-Milgram solvability at each frozen time is what makes the Galerkin ODE matrix invertible, and the parabolic solution operator is the time-ordered product of these elliptic resolvents, which in the autonomous case is exactly the analytic semigroup $e^{-tL}$ generated by $-L$ through Hille-Yosida, the Duhamel formula of [02.13.03] lifted to operators. Putting these together, the central insight is that a parabolic equation is one energy identity, $\frac{d}{dt}\tfrac12\|u\|_H^2 = \langle u',u\rangle$, fed three different right-hand sides: tested against $u$ it gives existence and uniqueness, tested against $u'$ it gives the improved regularity that recovers the classical solution, and combined with the compact embedding of [02.16.04] through Aubin-Lions it gives the strong convergence that carries the whole method into the nonlinear world. The hyperbolic problem is the same scheme with dissipation replaced by conservation, so the bridge from this unit reaches simultaneously back to the elliptic existence theory it integrates and forward to the semigroup, regularity, and nonlinear evolution theories it generates.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition 1 (uniform energy estimate).** Under the hypotheses of the main theorem, the Galerkin approximations satisfy $\sup_m\big(\|u_m\|_{L^\infty(0,T;H)} + \|u_m\|_{L^2(0,T;V)} + \|u_m'\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)}\big) \le C(\|f\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)} + \|g\|_H)$.

*Proof.* Testing the Galerkin identity with $v = u_m(t)$ gives $\frac{d}{dt}\tfrac12\|u_m\|_H^2 + B[u_m,u_m;t] = \langle f, u_m\rangle$. Gårding and Young ($\langle f,u_m\rangle \le \tfrac{\beta}{2}\|u_m\|_V^2 + \tfrac{1}{2\beta}\|f\|_{V^*}^2$) yield $\frac{d}{dt}\|u_m\|_H^2 + \beta\|u_m\|_V^2 \le 2\gamma\|u_m\|_H^2 + \tfrac1\beta\|f\|_{V^*}^2$. Discarding $\beta\|u_m\|_V^2$ and applying Grönwall (Exercise 4) bounds $\|u_m\|_{L^\infty(0,T;H)}^2 \le e^{2\gamma T}(\|g\|_H^2 + \tfrac1\beta\|f\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)}^2)$. Reinstating $\beta\|u_m\|_V^2$ and integrating over $(0,T)$: $\beta\int_0^T\|u_m\|_V^2 \le \|u_m(0)\|_H^2 + 2\gamma\int_0^T\|u_m\|_H^2 + \tfrac1\beta\|f\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)}^2 \le C(\|g\|_H^2 + \|f\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)}^2)$ using the sup bound just proved. Finally $\|u_m'(t)\|_{V^*} \le \|f(t)\|_{V^*} + \Lambda\|u_m(t)\|_V$ (Step 3 of the theorem, via $H$-orthogonal projection onto $V_m$), so $\|u_m'\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)} \le \|f\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)} + \Lambda\|u_m\|_{L^2(0,T;V)}$, completing the bound. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (existence of a weak solution).** A subsequence of $(u_m)$ converges weakly in $L^2(0,T;V)$, with $u_m'$ converging weakly in $L^2(0,T;V^*)$, to a limit $u \in W(0,T)$ that is a weak solution with $u(0) = g$.

*Proof.* By Proposition 1 and reflexivity, extract $u_m \rightharpoonup u$ in $L^2(0,T;V)$ and $u_m' \rightharpoonup \chi$ in $L^2(0,T;V^*)$. For $\varphi \in C_c^\infty(0,T)$ and $v \in V$, $\int_0^T \varphi'(t)\langle u_m,v\rangle\,dt = -\int_0^T\varphi\langle u_m',v\rangle\,dt$; passing to the limit (weak convergence against the fixed test pairing) gives $\int_0^T\varphi'\langle u,v\rangle = -\int_0^T\varphi\langle \chi,v\rangle$, so $\chi = u'$ and $u \in W(0,T)$. Fix $k$ and $\psi \in C^1([0,T])$ with $\psi(T)=0$. For $m \ge k$, multiplying the Galerkin identity by $\psi$ and integrating by parts in time gives $\int_0^T\!\big[-(u_m,w_k)_H\psi' + B[u_m,w_k;t]\psi\big]dt = \int_0^T\langle f,w_k\rangle\psi\,dt + (u_m(0),w_k)_H\psi(0)$. Each term is weakly continuous in $u_m$; since $u_m(0) \to g$ in $H$ (projections of fixed $g$), the limit reads $\int_0^T\!\big[-(u,w_k)_H\psi' + B[u,w_k;t]\psi\big]dt = \int_0^T\langle f,w_k\rangle\psi\,dt + (g,w_k)_H\psi(0)$. By density of $\{w_k\}$ in $V$ this holds for all $v\in V$; choosing $\psi \in C_c^\infty(0,T)$ recovers the equation a.e., and comparing the boundary terms for general $\psi$ with $\psi(0)\ne 0$ forces $u(0) = g$. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (uniqueness and continuous dependence).** The weak solution is unique and depends continuously on $(f,g)$: $\|u\|_{C([0,T];H)} \le C(\|f\|_{L^2(0,T;V^*)} + \|g\|_H)$.

*Proof.* The estimate is the limit of Proposition 1 under weak lower semicontinuity, together with the embedding $W(0,T)\hookrightarrow C([0,T];H)$ (Exercise 8). For uniqueness, the difference $w$ of two solutions with the same data solves $w' + Lw = 0$, $w(0)=0$. The pairing identity (Exercise 3) gives $\frac{d}{dt}\tfrac12\|w(t)\|_H^2 = \langle w',w\rangle = -B[w,w;t] \le -\beta\|w\|_V^2 + \gamma\|w\|_H^2 \le \gamma\|w\|_H^2$. Grönwall with $\|w(0)\|_H = 0$ gives $\|w(t)\|_H = 0$ for all $t$, so the two solutions agree. $\square$

**Proposition 4 (the abstract pairing identity).** For $u \in W(0,T)$, $t \mapsto \|u(t)\|_H^2$ is absolutely continuous with $\frac{d}{dt}\|u(t)\|_H^2 = 2\langle u'(t), u(t)\rangle$ for a.e. $t$, and $W(0,T) \hookrightarrow C([0,T];H)$.

*Proof.* Smooth $V$-valued functions are dense in $W(0,T)$ (mollify in time after extending). For such $u$ the identity is the classical product rule, $u'(t)\in V \subseteq H$ making the pairing an inner product. For general $u \in W(0,T)$ take $u_j \to u$ in $W(0,T)$ smooth; then $\int_s^t 2\langle u_j',u_j\rangle\,d\tau = \|u_j(t)\|_H^2 - \|u_j(s)\|_H^2$. The integrand converges in $L^1$ (weak-times-strong in dual $L^2$ spaces), and the difference $\|u_j(t)\|_H^2-\|u_j(s)\|_H^2$ converges uniformly by the a priori bound of Exercise 8 applied to $u_j - u_k$, giving a Cauchy sequence in $C([0,T];H)$ whose limit is the continuous representative of $u$. Passing to the limit yields the identity and the embedding bound $\max_t\|u(t)\|_H \le C\|u\|_{W(0,T)}$. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- The spatial engine is the elliptic weak theory of [02.16.04]: the Gårding coercivity proved there from uniform ellipticity is the exact input to the parabolic energy estimate, and the Dirichlet eigenbasis driving the Galerkin scheme is the spectral decomposition of the compact resolvent built in that unit. This unit owns the *time-evolution* layer; [02.16.04] owns the *frozen-time elliptic solvability* that the Galerkin matrix inherits at each instant.

- The Bochner spaces $L^2(0,T;V)$, the weak time-derivative, and the Gelfand triple $V \subset H \subset V^*$ are built on the Sobolev-space and Bochner-integral framework of [24.01.01]; that unit supplies the duality $H^* \cong H$ and the trace giving meaning to the spatial boundary condition, while this unit assembles them into the solution space $W(0,T)$ and the Lions-Aubin embedding into $C([0,T];H)$.

- The constant-coefficient, whole-space heat equation of [02.13.03] is the explicitly solvable special case: its Gaussian heat kernel is the integral form of the abstract semigroup $e^{-tL}$ of Theorem 1, and its Duhamel formula is the variation-of-parameters representation that the Galerkin solution realizes for variable, bounded-domain coefficients where no kernel is available. This unit is the existence theory of which [02.13.03] is the one computable instance.

- The semigroup viewpoint of Theorem 1 is developed in its own right in [02.18.03] (Hille-Yosida and $C_0$-semigroups), which characterizes exactly which operators $-L$ generate the parabolic flow; the Galerkin energy estimate of this unit is the dissipativity hypothesis of that generation theorem, so the two units are the constructive and the generator-theoretic faces of the same evolution.

- The variational/minimization counterpart for the stationary problem is the direct method of [02.18.04], which finds elliptic solutions as energy minimizers; the parabolic flow of this unit is the gradient flow of that same Dirichlet energy, so the long-time limit of the parabolic solution converges to the minimizer, linking time-evolution existence to variational existence within the chapter.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The approximation by finitely many fixed shapes originates with Boris Galerkin's 1915 paper on the elastic equilibrium of rods and plates [ref: TODO_REF Galerkin 1915], which projected the equilibrium equations onto a finite set of admissible deflection functions; the idea itself traces to Walther Ritz's 1908 variational method, of which Galerkin's is the weak-form generalization not requiring an energy functional. The extension of the method from steady-state to time-dependent problems is due to Sandro Faedo, whose 1949 *Annali della Scuola Normale Superiore di Pisa* memoir [ref: TODO_REF Faedo 1949] introduced what is now called the Faedo-Galerkin method: project onto finitely many spatial modes, solve the resulting system of ordinary differential equations in time, and pass to the limit using a-priori energy bounds.

The functional-analytic completion belongs to Jacques-Louis Lions, whose 1961 *Équations différentielles opérationnelles* [ref: TODO_REF Lions 1961] cast parabolic and hyperbolic problems in the Gelfand-triple framework $V \subset H \subset V^*$ and proved existence for general time-dependent coercive forms by the variational method, with the trace and compactness theory developed jointly with Enrico Magenes [ref: TODO_REF Lions-Magenes 1972]. The compactness lemma that carries the method into nonlinear problems was given by Jean-Pierre Aubin in 1963 [ref: TODO_REF Aubin 1963] and extended by Lions [ref: TODO_REF Lions 1969]. The dual semigroup formulation rests on the Hille-Yosida generation theorem of Einar Hille [ref: TODO_REF Hille 1948] and Kōsaku Yosida [ref: TODO_REF Yosida 1948], independently proved in 1948, and the Grönwall inequality closing the energy estimate is Thomas Grönwall's 1919 lemma [ref: TODO_REF Grönwall 1919].
