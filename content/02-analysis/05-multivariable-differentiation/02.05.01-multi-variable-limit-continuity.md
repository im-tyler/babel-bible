## Intuition [Beginner]

A function of several variables takes a tuple of numbers as input and produces another tuple as output. A function from the plane to the line accepts a point $(x, y)$ and returns a single number; a function from $3$-space to the plane accepts $(x, y, z)$ and returns a pair $(u, v)$. The picture for the simplest case, two inputs and one output, is a surface sitting above the floor of the input plane.

A *limit* describes what happens as the input slides toward a target point. The limit of $f$ at a point $a$ equals $L$ when, no matter how the input approaches $a$, the output gets close to $L$. *Continuous* at $a$ adds one more requirement: the value the function actually takes at $a$ matches that limit. Visually, a continuous function gives a surface with no holes, no jumps, no torn edges.

The catch in two dimensions or more is that a point can be approached from many directions. A safe limit must agree along every approach — straight lines from the left, from the right, from above, along curves, along spirals. If two different approaches give two different answers, no limit exists.

## Visual [Beginner]

Two surfaces side by side. On the left, a smooth bowl shaped like $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$, dipping down to a single low point at the origin where the function value is $0$. On the right, a saddle-and-ridge shape for $g(x, y) = xy/(x^2 + y^2)$, with two arrows showing the line $y = x$ approaching the origin from a height of $1/2$ and the line $y = 0$ approaching from a height of $0$ — two different heights at the same input point.

![Two surfaces over the input plane: a smooth bowl that is continuous at the origin, and a saddle-shaped surface where two different straight-line approaches give two different limit values, so the limit at the origin does not exist.](/img/multi-variable-limit-continuity-placeholder.svg)

The left surface earns the label *continuous*. The right surface fails the limit test at the origin. The image is the visual signature of the central rule: a multi-variable limit must agree along every path of approach.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Take the specific function $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$ and the target point $(0, 0)$. Plug in: $f(0, 0) = 0$. Approach along the $x$-axis with $y = 0$: $f(x, 0) = x^2$, which goes to $0$ as $x$ goes to $0$. Approach along the $y$-axis with $x = 0$: $f(0, y) = y^2$, which goes to $0$. Approach along the diagonal $y = x$: $f(x, x) = 2 x^2$, which goes to $0$. Every approach gives $0$, and the actual function value is $0$, so $f$ is continuous at the origin.

Now take $g(x, y) = xy / (x^2 + y^2)$ at the same target point $(0, 0)$. The expression at the origin is $0/0$, which is not a number. Approach along $y = 0$: $g(x, 0) = 0$, which goes to $0$. Approach along $y = x$ with $x \neq 0$: $g(x, x) = x^2/(2x^2) = 1/2$, which stays at $1/2$. Two paths, two answers — the limit does not exist, and no choice of value at $(0, 0)$ fixes this. In two dimensions, "the function tends to a number" is a stronger requirement than in one, because every approach has to agree.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

The Euclidean norm on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is $\|x\| = \sqrt{x_1^2 + \cdots + x_n^2}$ for $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)$. The **open ball** of radius $r > 0$ centred at $a \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is $B_r(a) = \{x \in \mathbb{R}^n : \|x - a\| < r\}$. A subset $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is **open** if for every $a \in U$ some $r > 0$ has $B_r(a) \subseteq U$. A point $a$ is a **limit point** of $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ if every open ball around $a$ contains some point of $S$ different from $a$.

Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, let $a \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be a limit point of $S$, and let $f : S \to \mathbb{R}^m$. The **limit of $f$ at $a$ equals $L \in \mathbb{R}^m$**, written $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$, when

$$
\forall \varepsilon > 0 \ \exists \delta > 0 \ \forall x \in S : 0 < \|x - a\| < \delta \implies \|f(x) - L\| < \varepsilon.
$$

The function $f$ is **continuous at $a \in S$** when $a$ is either an isolated point of $S$ or a limit point of $S$ with $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$. Equivalently, for every $\varepsilon > 0$ some $\delta > 0$ gives $\|x - a\| < \delta \implies \|f(x) - f(a)\| < \varepsilon$. The function $f$ is **continuous on $S$** when it is continuous at every point of $S$. Following Apostol [ref: TODO_REF Apostol Ch. 8 §8.2–8.4].

A few standard reformulations are equivalent over $\mathbb{R}^n$ with its Euclidean norm.

- **Topological form.** $f$ is continuous on $S$ iff $f^{-1}(V)$ is open relative to $S$ for every open $V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$ [ref: TODO_REF Munkres §18].
- **Sequential form.** $f$ is continuous at $a$ iff for every sequence $(x_k)$ in $S$ with $x_k \to a$, the image sequence $f(x_k) \to f(a)$ [ref: TODO_REF Rudin Ch. 4].
- **Component form.** Writing $f = (f_1, \ldots, f_m)$ with $f_i : S \to \mathbb{R}$, the function $f$ is continuous at $a$ iff every component $f_i$ is continuous at $a$.

### Counterexamples to common slips

- **Equality on every line through $a$ does not imply the limit exists.** For $h(x, y) = x^2 y / (x^4 + y^2)$ extended by $h(0, 0) = 0$, every straight line through the origin yields the limit $0$, yet along the parabola $y = x^2$ the function equals $1/2$. The limit at the origin therefore does not exist.
- **Continuity in each variable separately does not imply joint continuity.** The function $g(x, y) = xy / (x^2 + y^2)$ extended by $g(0, 0) = 0$ is continuous in $x$ for each fixed $y$ and continuous in $y$ for each fixed $x$, yet it is not jointly continuous at the origin.
- **A removable singularity is not always removable.** If $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists, redefining $f(a)$ to that limit makes $f$ continuous at $a$. If the limit does not exist, no redefinition produces continuity.
- **The sequential criterion needs the Euclidean structure (or first-countability).** In a general topological space, sequential continuity can be strictly weaker than topological continuity. Over $\mathbb{R}^n$ they coincide because countable neighbourhood bases exist.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (sequential characterisation of continuity).** *Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, let $a \in S$, and let $f : S \to \mathbb{R}^m$. The function $f$ is continuous at $a$ if and only if for every sequence $(x_k)$ in $S$ with $\|x_k - a\| \to 0$, the image sequence $\|f(x_k) - f(a)\| \to 0$.*

**Proof.** ($\Rightarrow$) Assume $f$ is continuous at $a$, and let $(x_k)$ be a sequence in $S$ with $x_k \to a$. Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. By continuity, choose $\delta > 0$ so that $\|x - a\| < \delta$ implies $\|f(x) - f(a)\| < \varepsilon$ for $x \in S$. Since $x_k \to a$, choose $N$ with $\|x_k - a\| < \delta$ for all $k \geq N$. Then $\|f(x_k) - f(a)\| < \varepsilon$ for all $k \geq N$, so $f(x_k) \to f(a)$.

($\Leftarrow$) Argue by contraposition. Assume $f$ is not continuous at $a$. Then some $\varepsilon_0 > 0$ has the property that for every $\delta > 0$, some $x_\delta \in S$ satisfies $\|x_\delta - a\| < \delta$ and $\|f(x_\delta) - f(a)\| \geq \varepsilon_0$. Apply this with $\delta = 1/k$ for each positive integer $k$ to extract $x_k \in S$ with $\|x_k - a\| < 1/k$ and $\|f(x_k) - f(a)\| \geq \varepsilon_0$.

The sequence $(x_k)$ converges to $a$ because the gap $\|x_k - a\|$ is bounded above by $1/k$, and $1/k \to 0$ by the Archimedean property of $\mathbb{R}$ proved in [02.02.01]. The image sequence $(f(x_k))$ stays at distance at least $\varepsilon_0$ from $f(a)$ for every $k$, so it does not converge to $f(a)$. The contrapositive is established, so the converse direction holds. $\square$

**Bridge.** The sequential criterion is the bridge between four neighbouring frames. *First*, it transports the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition to the language of sequence convergence, the same translation that converts the completeness axiom (C) of [02.02.01] into the monotone convergence theorem. *Second*, it lifts to the general-topology characterisation in [02.01.02], where continuity is recast as "preimage of open is open"; over $\mathbb{R}^n$ the three forms — $\varepsilon$-$\delta$, sequential, topological — collapse into one because Euclidean space has countable neighbourhood bases. *Third*, it underpins the differential structure of [02.05.02]: the differential $df_a$ is defined as the linear map approximating $f$ at $a$, and that approximation is meaningful only when $f$ is continuous at $a$, since $\lim_{h \to 0} f(a + h) = f(a)$ is exactly what makes the remainder $o(\|h\|)$ control the function. *Fourth*, it furnishes the multivariable Heine-Cantor refinement: if $S$ is compact and $f$ is continuous, the same sequential mechanism produces uniform continuity, because no escape sequence can withhold a convergent subsequence. Read together, the four bridges identify continuity as the load-bearing notion that connects the order-completeness of the real line, the topological language of open sets, the linear-approximation framework of differentiation, and the compactness-driven uniformity that powers the existence theorems for integrals and ODEs.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).**

Show directly from the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition that the projection map $\pi_1 : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ given by $\pi_1(x, y) = x$ is continuous at every point.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use $|\pi_1(p) - \pi_1(a)| = |x - a_1|$ and bound this by $\|p - a\|$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix $a = (a_1, a_2) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ and $\varepsilon > 0$. Set $\delta = \varepsilon$. For any $p = (x, y)$ with $\|p - a\| < \delta$, the inequality $|x - a_1| \leq \sqrt{(x - a_1)^2 + (y - a_2)^2} = \|p - a\| < \delta = \varepsilon$ gives $|\pi_1(p) - \pi_1(a)| < \varepsilon$. So $\pi_1$ is continuous at $a$. The same argument applies to $\pi_2$. Rubric: full credit for the bound and the choice $\delta = \varepsilon$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).**

Show that if $f, g : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ are continuous at $a$, then $f + g$ and $f \cdot g$ are continuous at $a$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the sum, use the triangle inequality. For the product, add and subtract a cross term: $f(x) g(x) - f(a) g(a) = (f(x) - f(a)) g(x) + f(a)(g(x) - g(a))$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For the sum, given $\varepsilon > 0$, pick $\delta_1, \delta_2$ for $f, g$ with $\varepsilon/2$ and take $\delta = \min(\delta_1, \delta_2)$; then $|(f + g)(x) - (f + g)(a)| \leq |f(x) - f(a)| + |g(x) - g(a)| < \varepsilon/2 + \varepsilon/2 = \varepsilon$.

For the product, write $|f(x) g(x) - f(a) g(a)| \leq |f(x) - f(a)| \cdot |g(x)| + |f(a)| \cdot |g(x) - g(a)|$. Continuity of $g$ at $a$ supplies a neighbourhood on which $|g(x)| \leq |g(a)| + 1$. Choose $\delta_f$ small enough that $|f(x) - f(a)| < \varepsilon / (2(|g(a)| + 1))$ and $\delta_g$ small enough that $|g(x) - g(a)| < \varepsilon / (2 |f(a)| + 2)$. The minimum of these and the boundedness neighbourhood gives the required $\delta$. Rubric: full credit for both the sum and the product, partial for one.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, numeric).**

Let $g(x, y) = (x^2 - y^2) / (x^2 + y^2)$ for $(x, y) \neq (0, 0)$. Compute the directional limit along the line $y = mx$ as $(x, y) \to (0, 0)$, and report the value when $m = 2$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Substitute $y = mx$ and simplify before letting $x \to 0$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$-3/5$.** Along $y = mx$ with $x \neq 0$, $g(x, mx) = (x^2 - m^2 x^2) / (x^2 + m^2 x^2) = (1 - m^2)/(1 + m^2)$, independent of $x$. For $m = 2$, this is $(1 - 4)/(1 + 4) = -3/5$. The directional limit depends on the slope $m$, so the joint limit at the origin does not exist. Rubric: full credit for the answer plus the observation that different slopes give different limits.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, short-answer).**

Show that the function $h(x, y) = x^2 y / (x^4 + y^2)$ extended by $h(0, 0) = 0$ has limit $0$ along every straight line through the origin, yet has no limit at the origin.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

After checking lines, try the parabola $y = x^2$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Along $y = mx$ with $x \neq 0$, $h(x, mx) = x^2 \cdot mx / (x^4 + m^2 x^2) = mx^3 / (x^2 (x^2 + m^2)) = mx / (x^2 + m^2)$, which goes to $0$ as $x \to 0$ for every fixed $m$. Along the vertical line $x = 0$ the function is $0$. So every straight-line approach yields $0$.

Along the parabola $y = x^2$, $h(x, x^2) = x^2 \cdot x^2 / (x^4 + x^4) = x^4 / (2 x^4) = 1/2$, a constant nonzero value. So the parabolic approach yields $1/2$. Two different paths give two different values, so the joint limit at the origin does not exist. Rubric: full credit for both calculations and the conclusion.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, short-answer).**

Prove the *composition rule*: if $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ is continuous at $a$ and $g : \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^p$ is continuous at $f(a)$, then $g \circ f$ is continuous at $a$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Chain $\varepsilon$ through $g$ to get an intermediate tolerance, then chain that tolerance through $f$ to get $\delta$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Fix $\varepsilon > 0$. By continuity of $g$ at $f(a)$, choose $\eta > 0$ with $\|y - f(a)\| < \eta \implies \|g(y) - g(f(a))\| < \varepsilon$. By continuity of $f$ at $a$, choose $\delta > 0$ with $\|x - a\| < \delta \implies \|f(x) - f(a)\| < \eta$. Putting these together, $\|x - a\| < \delta \implies \|g(f(x)) - g(f(a))\| < \varepsilon$. So $g \circ f$ is continuous at $a$. Rubric: full credit for the chained-$\varepsilon$ argument with the intermediate tolerance $\eta$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, short-answer).**

Prove the *multivariable Heine-Cantor theorem*: if $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is compact (closed and bounded) and $f : K \to \mathbb{R}^m$ is continuous, then $f$ is uniformly continuous on $K$ — for every $\varepsilon > 0$ some $\delta > 0$ has $\|x - y\| < \delta \implies \|f(x) - f(y)\| < \varepsilon$ for *all* $x, y \in K$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Argue by contradiction. If $f$ fails to be uniformly continuous, build sequences $(x_k), (y_k)$ in $K$ with $\|x_k - y_k\| \to 0$ but $\|f(x_k) - f(y_k)\| \geq \varepsilon_0$, then use Bolzano-Weierstrass to extract convergent subsequences.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Assume $f$ is not uniformly continuous on $K$. Then some $\varepsilon_0 > 0$ has the property that for every $k \geq 1$ there exist $x_k, y_k \in K$ with $\|x_k - y_k\| < 1/k$ and $\|f(x_k) - f(y_k)\| \geq \varepsilon_0$.

The sequence $(x_k)$ lies in the bounded set $K$, so by Bolzano-Weierstrass it has a subsequence $(x_{k_j})$ converging to some $a \in \mathbb{R}^n$. Since $K$ is closed, $a \in K$. The companion subsequence $(y_{k_j})$ satisfies $\|y_{k_j} - a\| \leq \|y_{k_j} - x_{k_j}\| + \|x_{k_j} - a\| < 1/k_j + \|x_{k_j} - a\|$, which tends to $0$, so $y_{k_j} \to a$ as well.

By continuity of $f$ at $a$, $f(x_{k_j}) \to f(a)$ and $f(y_{k_j}) \to f(a)$. Hence $\|f(x_{k_j}) - f(y_{k_j})\| \to 0$, contradicting the lower bound $\|f(x_{k_j}) - f(y_{k_j})\| \geq \varepsilon_0$ that holds for every $j$. The assumption fails, and $f$ is uniformly continuous on $K$. Rubric: full credit for the contradiction setup, the Bolzano-Weierstrass extraction, and the matched-subsequence convergence.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="short-answer" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, short-answer).**

Show that the topological criterion ($f$ continuous iff $f^{-1}(V)$ is open for every open $V$) and the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ criterion are equivalent on $\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Open balls form a basis for the Euclidean topology on $\mathbb{R}^m$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

($\varepsilon$-$\delta$ implies topological.) Let $V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$ be open. Pick $a \in f^{-1}(V)$, so $f(a) \in V$. By openness, some $\varepsilon > 0$ has $B_\varepsilon(f(a)) \subseteq V$. By the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition, some $\delta > 0$ has $f(B_\delta(a)) \subseteq B_\varepsilon(f(a)) \subseteq V$, so $B_\delta(a) \subseteq f^{-1}(V)$. The point $a$ has an open ball inside $f^{-1}(V)$, so $f^{-1}(V)$ is open.

(Topological implies $\varepsilon$-$\delta$.) Fix $a$ and $\varepsilon > 0$. The ball $V = B_\varepsilon(f(a))$ is open in $\mathbb{R}^m$. By the topological criterion, $f^{-1}(V)$ is open in $\mathbb{R}^n$ and contains $a$, so some $\delta > 0$ has $B_\delta(a) \subseteq f^{-1}(V)$. Then $\|x - a\| < \delta$ implies $f(x) \in V$, that is $\|f(x) - f(a)\| < \varepsilon$. Rubric: full credit for both directions; partial credit for one.

</details>

</aside>

## Lean formalization [Intermediate+]

`lean_status: partial` — Mathlib provides `ContinuousAt`, `Continuous`, the metric-space `EuclideanSpace ℝ (Fin n)`, and the sequential characterisation through `Metric.continuousAt_iff_seq_tendsto`. The textbook-style packaging that puts the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ form, the sequential form, and the multivariable Heine-Cantor theorem under one named result is the Codex-facing gap.

```lean
import Mathlib.Topology.MetricSpace.Basic
import Mathlib.Topology.ContinuousOn
import Mathlib.Analysis.InnerProductSpace.EuclideanDist

namespace Codex.Analysis.MultiVariable

abbrev EuclR (n : ℕ) := EuclideanSpace ℝ (Fin n)

variable {n m : ℕ}

/-- The ε-δ form of continuity at a point for `f : ℝⁿ → ℝᵐ`. -/
example (f : EuclR n → EuclR m) (a : EuclR n) :
    ContinuousAt f a ↔
      ∀ ε > 0, ∃ δ > 0, ∀ x, dist x a < δ → dist (f x) (f a) < ε :=
  Metric.continuousAt_iff

/-- Sequential characterisation of continuity at a point. -/
example (f : EuclR n → EuclR m) (a : EuclR n) :
    ContinuousAt f a ↔
      ∀ x : ℕ → EuclR n,
        Filter.Tendsto x Filter.atTop (nhds a) →
        Filter.Tendsto (fun k => f (x k)) Filter.atTop (nhds (f a)) := by
  refine ⟨fun h x hx => h.tendsto.comp hx, ?_⟩
  intro h
  exact Metric.continuousAt_iff_seq_tendsto.mpr h
```

## Advanced results [Master]

**Multivariable Heine-Cantor.** *Let $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ be compact and $f : K \to \mathbb{R}^m$ continuous. Then $f$ is uniformly continuous on $K$.* Stated and proved as Exercise 6 by sequential extraction; the original 1872 statement of Heine [ref: TODO_REF Heine 1872] handled the single-variable case on a closed bounded interval, and Cantor in the same year gave the independent treatment. The multivariable extension uses the Bolzano-Weierstrass property of compact subsets of $\mathbb{R}^n$, which is the Euclidean specialisation of the Heine-Borel theorem.

**Composition continuity.** *If $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ is continuous and $g : \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^p$ is continuous, then $g \circ f$ is continuous.* Proved as Exercise 5 by the chained-$\varepsilon$ argument; equivalently, by the topological criterion, $(g \circ f)^{-1}(V) = f^{-1}(g^{-1}(V))$ is open whenever $V$ is open, since open sets pull back to open sets at each step.

**Tietze extension theorem.** *Let $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ be closed and let $f : A \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous and bounded. Then $f$ extends to a continuous $F : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ with the same bound.* The result is a metric-space specialisation of Urysohn's normality machinery; proof via successive approximation by continuous functions whose maximum-norm errors are halved at each stage [ref: TODO_REF Munkres §35]. The extension carries the same modulus of continuity in the compact case.

**Banach fixed-point theorem.** *Let $X$ be a non-empty complete metric space and let $T : X \to X$ be a contraction (Lipschitz constant $L < 1$). Then $T$ has a unique fixed point, and the iteration $x_{k+1} = T(x_k)$ converges to it from any starting point at geometric rate $L^k$.* Proved by showing $(x_k)$ is Cauchy via the geometric-series bound $\|x_{k+1} - x_k\| \leq L^k \|x_1 - x_0\|$. The theorem is the workhorse behind the inverse and implicit function theorems of [02.05.04] and the Picard-Lindelöf existence theorem for ODEs.

**Sequential vs topological continuity in general.** Sequential continuity equals topological continuity exactly when the domain is *first-countable*: every point has a countable neighbourhood basis. Metric spaces are first-countable, so over $\mathbb{R}^n$ the two criteria coincide. In a non-first-countable space (such as $\mathbb{R}$ with the cocountable topology, or an uncountable product of copies of $\{0, 1\}$), sequentially continuous functions need not be continuous. A *net* — a generalised sequence indexed by a directed set — restores the equivalence in full generality.

**Synthesis.** Four observations organise the unit. *First*, the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition, the sequential criterion, the topological criterion, and the component-by-component criterion are four faces of the same notion on $\mathbb{R}^n$, identical because of the Euclidean metric structure inherited from the real-number axioms. *Second*, the requirement that limits agree along every approach is the load-bearing difference between single-variable and multi-variable analysis: a function of one variable approaches a target along two sides of a number line; a function of several variables approaches along a continuum of paths, including straight lines, parabolic arcs, and worse. *Third*, compactness upgrades pointwise continuity to uniform continuity, and that uniformity is the source of every existence theorem that follows: Riemann integrability of continuous functions on closed bounded boxes, Picard-Lindelöf for ODEs, Stone-Weierstrass approximation. *Fourth*, the metric-space framework introduced by Fréchet in 1906 [ref: TODO_REF Fréchet 1906] generalises this entire picture to any space carrying a distance function, opening the door to function spaces, sequence spaces, and the Banach- and Hilbert-space technology of functional analysis. Taken together, these observations identify continuity as the single notion that bridges the order-completeness of [02.02.01], the topological language of [02.01.01–02], the linear approximation of [02.05.02], and the contraction-based existence theorems that drive [02.05.04] and ordinary differential equations.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Sequential characterisation.** Proved in §"Key theorem with proof" above.

**Multivariable Heine-Cantor.** Proved in Exercise 6 by a Bolzano-Weierstrass extraction.

**Composition continuity.** Proved in Exercise 5 by the chained-$\varepsilon$ argument.

**$\varepsilon$-$\delta$ equals topological.** Proved in Exercise 7 by the basis of open balls.

**Proposition (Bolzano-Weierstrass for $\mathbb{R}^n$).** *Every bounded sequence in $\mathbb{R}^n$ has a convergent subsequence.* The single-variable case follows from the completeness axiom (C) of [02.02.01] via the monotone-subsequence lemma. The $n$-dimensional case proceeds by induction on $n$: extract a subsequence of the first coordinates that converges (single-variable case applied to the bounded sequence of first components), then a sub-subsequence on the second coordinates, and so on for $n$ steps. The final sub-$n$-subsequence converges in every coordinate, hence in $\mathbb{R}^n$ by the component-form characterisation. $\square$

**Proposition (Banach fixed-point theorem).** *Let $(X, d)$ be a non-empty complete metric space and $T : X \to X$ with $d(T(x), T(y)) \leq L \cdot d(x, y)$ for some $L \in [0, 1)$ and all $x, y \in X$. Then $T$ has a unique fixed point $x^* \in X$, and the iterates $x_{k+1} = T(x_k)$ from any $x_0$ satisfy $d(x_k, x^*) \leq L^k d(x_0, x^*) / (1 - L)$.*

*Proof.* By induction $d(x_{k+1}, x_k) \leq L^k d(x_1, x_0)$. For $k < l$, the triangle inequality gives $d(x_k, x_l) \leq \sum_{j=k}^{l-1} d(x_{j+1}, x_j) \leq d(x_1, x_0) \cdot L^k (1 - L^{l-k})/(1 - L) \leq d(x_1, x_0) L^k / (1 - L)$. Since $L^k \to 0$ by the Archimedean property of $\mathbb{R}$ applied to $\log L < 0$, the sequence $(x_k)$ is Cauchy. By completeness of $X$, it converges to some $x^* \in X$. By continuity of $T$ (Lipschitz functions are continuous) and the recurrence $x_{k+1} = T(x_k)$, the limit satisfies $x^* = T(x^*)$. Uniqueness: if $y^*$ is another fixed point, $d(x^*, y^*) = d(T(x^*), T(y^*)) \leq L \cdot d(x^*, y^*)$, so $(1 - L) d(x^*, y^*) \leq 0$, forcing $d(x^*, y^*) = 0$ and $x^* = y^*$. $\square$

**Proposition (Tietze extension on $\mathbb{R}^n$, sketch).** Let $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ be closed and $f : A \to [-M, M]$ continuous. Build a sequence of continuous functions $g_k : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ with $|g_k| \leq (2/3)^k M / 3$ and $|f - \sum_{j \leq k} g_j| \leq (2/3)^{k+1} M$ on $A$. Each $g_k$ comes from Urysohn's lemma applied to the disjoint closed subsets where the running remainder is large positive and large negative. The series $\sum_k g_k$ converges uniformly on $\mathbb{R}^n$ to a continuous function $F$ with $F|_A = f$. Full proof in Munkres §35 [ref: TODO_REF Munkres §35]. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

**Topological space [02.01.01]** — the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition of multi-variable continuity is the metric-space specialisation of the topological "preimage of open is open" definition. The unit recovers the Euclidean instance from the topological framework, and the topological framework abstracts the unit's geometric content.

**Continuous map [02.01.02]** — the morphisms in the category $\mathbf{Top}$. Multi-variable continuous functions are the morphisms restricted to objects of the form $\mathbb{R}^n$ with the Euclidean topology. Sequential characterisation here equals the general categorical condition because $\mathbb{R}^n$ is first-countable.

**Real-number axioms [02.02.01]** — the Euclidean norm depends on the order-and-completeness structure of $\mathbb{R}$ in two essential ways: the square root used to define $\|x\|$ exists by the existence theorem for $n$-th roots (Exercise 5 of [02.02.01]), and the Archimedean property ensures that the witness sequences with $\|x_k - a\| < 1/k$ converge to $a$.

**Partial derivative and the differential [02.05.02]** (pending) — the differential at $a$ is the unique linear map approximating $f$ near $a$ to first order. Continuity at $a$ is necessary: if $f$ is not continuous at $a$, no linear map can satisfy $\lim_{h \to 0} (f(a + h) - f(a) - L(h)) / \|h\| = 0$. The unit supplies the foundational notion that makes the differential well-defined.

**Inverse and implicit function theorems [02.05.04]** (pending) — the proofs of both theorems use the Banach fixed-point theorem on a complete metric space, which is in turn an application of the sequential criterion of this unit. The contraction estimate produces a Cauchy sequence whose limit, by completeness, lands in the metric space; continuity of the contraction map ensures that the limit is the fixed point.

**Metric space [02.01.05]** — the multi-variable framework is one instance of the metric-space framework. Cauchy completeness, uniform continuity, the Banach fixed-point theorem, and the Heine-Cantor theorem all live at the metric level and specialise to the Euclidean case treated here.

**Banach space [02.11.04]** — completeness of $\mathbb{R}^n$ in the Euclidean norm makes it a finite-dimensional Banach space. The pattern "continuous linear map plus completeness implies existence by Banach fixed-point" recurs throughout functional analysis, and the Banach-Mazur game and open-mapping theorem at that level rest on the multi-variable continuity machinery of this unit.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Augustin-Louis Cauchy gave the first $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ formulation of limits and continuity in the 1821 *Cours d'analyse* [ref: TODO_REF Cauchy 1821], confined to functions of a single real variable. The conceptual move that makes the multi-variable case interesting — the recognition that a point can be approached from many directions, and that a candidate limit must agree along all of them — became visible in the lecture practice of Riemann and Weierstrass in the 1850s and 1860s, though it was rarely written down explicitly. Eduard Heine in 1872 [ref: TODO_REF Heine 1872] proved that continuity on a closed bounded interval is automatically uniform, the prototype of every later compactness-and-continuity theorem; Georg Cantor obtained the same result independently in the same year.

Maurice Fréchet's 1906 doctoral thesis [ref: TODO_REF Fréchet 1906] introduced the abstract metric space and the abstract notion of a topological structure on a set, subsuming the Euclidean case treated in this unit. Felix Hausdorff's 1914 axiomatisation, the Kuratowski closure-operator presentation of 1922, and Bourbaki's mid-century treatise [ref: TODO_REF Bourbaki Ch. I §7] consolidated continuity as the central morphism notion in topology, with the metric / Euclidean theory as one instance among many. The sequential criterion proved in this unit is the bridge between the Cauchy-Riemann-Heine prehistory and the Fréchet-Hausdorff topological present: it states the metric definition in terms that translate directly to the categorical one, and it identifies the specific structure (first-countability) that licenses the translation in general.
