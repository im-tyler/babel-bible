## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Apostol Vol. 2 Chapters 8-9 develop the differential calculus of functions $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$. Chapter 8 covers limits and continuity, partial derivatives, the distinction between existence of partials and differentiability, the differential $df_p$ as a linear map, the gradient and directional derivative, the Jacobian matrix, and the chain rule in matrix form. Chapter 9 adds the multivariable Taylor formula, the Hessian and sufficient conditions for extrema, constrained optimisation by Lagrange multipliers, and the implicit and inverse function theorems with full proofs.

This pack collects nine problems from those chapters — three easy, four medium, two hard — each with a hint and a complete worked solution. It tests operational competence: computing a Jacobian and applying the chain rule, locating and classifying critical points via the Hessian, running a Lagrange-multiplier optimisation, and applying the implicit and inverse function theorems to decide local solvability.

Conventions follow the prerequisite units: $Df(p)$ or $J_f(p)$ denotes the Jacobian matrix of $f$ at $p$; $\nabla f$ the gradient of a scalar field; $D_u f = \nabla f \cdot u$ the directional derivative along a unit vector $u$; $H_f$ the Hessian of second partials. A point is critical when $\nabla f$ vanishes there; the second-derivative test reads off definiteness of $H_f$.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one problem in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining eight follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead problem.** *Lagrange multipliers on a constraint.* Find the extrema of $f(x, y, z) = x + 2y + 3z$ on the sphere $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 14$.

**Solution.** The constraint set $g(x, y, z) = x^2 + y^2 + z^2 - 14 = 0$ is a compact sphere, and $f$ is continuous, so $f$ attains a maximum and minimum on it. At a constrained extremum where $\nabla g \neq 0$, the Lagrange condition holds: $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$ for some multiplier $\lambda$.

Compute $\nabla f = (1, 2, 3)$ and $\nabla g = (2x, 2y, 2z)$. The condition $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$ gives
$$
1 = 2\lambda x, \qquad 2 = 2\lambda y, \qquad 3 = 2\lambda z.
$$
With $\lambda \neq 0$ (since $\nabla f \neq 0$), solve $x = \frac{1}{2\lambda}$, $y = \frac{1}{\lambda}$, $z = \frac{3}{2\lambda}$. Substitute into the constraint:
$$
\frac{1}{4\lambda^2} + \frac{1}{\lambda^2} + \frac{9}{4\lambda^2} = \frac{1 + 4 + 9}{4\lambda^2} = \frac{14}{4\lambda^2} = 14,
$$
so $\lambda^2 = \frac{1}{4}$, $\lambda = \pm\frac{1}{2}$.

For $\lambda = \frac{1}{2}$: $(x, y, z) = (1, 2, 3)$, giving $f = 1 + 4 + 9 = 14$. For $\lambda = -\frac{1}{2}$: $(x, y, z) = (-1, -2, -3)$, giving $f = -14$.

The maximum is $14$ at $(1, 2, 3)$ and the minimum is $-14$ at $(-1, -2, -3)$. $\square$

Geometrically, $f = \langle (1,2,3), (x,y,z)\rangle$ is maximised when $(x,y,z)$ points along $(1,2,3)$; the extreme value $\pm\|(1,2,3)\|\cdot\sqrt{14} = \pm\sqrt{14}\cdot\sqrt{14} = \pm 14$ is the Cauchy-Schwarz bound, which the Lagrange method recovers analytically.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).** *Gradient and directional derivative.*

For $f(x, y, z) = x^2 y + y z^2$, compute $\nabla f$ at $(1, 2, 1)$ and the directional derivative there along $u = \tfrac{1}{3}(2, -1, 2)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\nabla f = (f_x, f_y, f_z)$. The directional derivative is $D_u f = \nabla f \cdot u$, valid because $u$ is a unit vector.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$f_x = 2xy$, $f_y = x^2 + z^2$, $f_z = 2yz$. At $(1, 2, 1)$: $\nabla f = (2\cdot 1\cdot 2,\; 1 + 1,\; 2\cdot 2\cdot 1) = (4, 2, 4)$.

The vector $u = \tfrac13(2, -1, 2)$ is a unit vector ($\|(2,-1,2)\| = 3$). So
$$
D_u f = \nabla f \cdot u = \tfrac13(4\cdot 2 + 2\cdot(-1) + 4\cdot 2) = \tfrac13(8 - 2 + 8) = \tfrac{14}{3}.
$$

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Jacobian of polar coordinates.*

For the map $g(r, \theta) = (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$, compute the Jacobian matrix $Dg$ and its determinant.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The columns (or rows) of $Dg$ are the partials of the component functions. The determinant is the area-scaling factor used in the polar change of variables.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

With $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$:
$$
Dg = \begin{pmatrix} \partial x/\partial r & \partial x/\partial \theta \\ \partial y/\partial r & \partial y/\partial \theta \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta \end{pmatrix}.
$$
$$
\det Dg = \cos\theta \cdot r\cos\theta - (-r\sin\theta)\cdot\sin\theta = r\cos^2\theta + r\sin^2\theta = r.
$$
The factor $r$ is the familiar $dx\,dy = r\,dr\,d\theta$ from polar integration.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, symbolic).** *Existence of partials does not imply differentiability.*

Let $f(x, y) = \frac{xy}{x^2 + y^2}$ for $(x, y) \neq (0, 0)$ and $f(0, 0) = 0$. Show both partials exist at the origin yet $f$ is not continuous there.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute $f_x(0,0)$ and $f_y(0,0)$ from the limit definition along the axes. For continuity, approach the origin along $y = x$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Along the $x$-axis, $f(x, 0) = 0$ for all $x$, so $f_x(0,0) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(h,0) - 0}{h} = 0$. By symmetry $f_y(0,0) = 0$. Both partials exist and equal $0$.

But $f$ is not continuous at the origin: along $y = x$ (with $x \neq 0$), $f(x, x) = \frac{x^2}{2x^2} = \frac12$, which does not tend to $f(0,0) = 0$ as $x \to 0$. Approaching along $y = 0$ gives the limit $0$ instead, so no joint limit exists.

A function with existing partials but no continuity cannot be differentiable (differentiability implies continuity). This is the standard cautionary example: partial derivatives alone are too weak; differentiability requires the linear-map approximation $f(p+h) = f(p) + df_p(h) + o(|h|)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).** *Chain rule in a coordinate change.*

Let $w = f(x, y)$ with $x = s^2 - t^2$, $y = 2st$. Express $w_s$ and $w_t$ in terms of $f_x$, $f_y$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$w_s = f_x x_s + f_y y_s$ and similarly for $w_t$, by the multivariable chain rule.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The partials of the inner map: $x_s = 2s$, $x_t = -2t$, $y_s = 2t$, $y_t = 2s$.

By the chain rule,
$$
w_s = f_x x_s + f_y y_s = 2s\, f_x + 2t\, f_y, \qquad w_t = f_x x_t + f_y y_t = -2t\, f_x + 2s\, f_y.
$$
In matrix form, $(w_s, w_t) = (f_x, f_y)\,Dg$ where $Dg = \begin{pmatrix} 2s & -2t \\ 2t & 2s\end{pmatrix}$ is the Jacobian of $(s,t)\mapsto(x,y)$. This is the map $z \mapsto z^2$ in the complex variable $z = s + it$; its Jacobian determinant $4(s^2 + t^2) = 4|z|^2$ is the conformal scaling factor.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).** *Classify critical points by the Hessian.*

Find and classify the critical points of $f(x, y) = x^3 + y^3 - 3xy$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Solve $\nabla f = 0$. At each critical point evaluate the Hessian $H_f = \begin{pmatrix} f_{xx} & f_{xy} \\ f_{xy} & f_{yy}\end{pmatrix}$; use $\det H_f$ and $f_{xx}$ to classify.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\nabla f = (3x^2 - 3y,\; 3y^2 - 3x) = 0$ gives $y = x^2$ and $x = y^2$, so $x = x^4$, i.e. $x(x^3 - 1) = 0$. Critical points: $(0, 0)$ and $(1, 1)$.

Hessian: $f_{xx} = 6x$, $f_{yy} = 6y$, $f_{xy} = -3$, so $H_f = \begin{pmatrix} 6x & -3 \\ -3 & 6y\end{pmatrix}$ with $\det H_f = 36xy - 9$.

At $(0,0)$: $\det H_f = -9 < 0$, a saddle point.

At $(1,1)$: $\det H_f = 36 - 9 = 27 > 0$ and $f_{xx} = 6 > 0$, so the Hessian is positive-definite — a local minimum, with $f(1,1) = 1 + 1 - 3 = -1$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).** *Implicit function theorem applied.*

Show that the equation $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 + \sin(xyz) = 4$ defines $z$ as a $C^1$ function of $(x, y)$ near the point $(1, 1, \sqrt{2})$ (where it holds, since $1 + 1 + 2 + \sin(\sqrt2)\cdot\ldots$ — see solution), and compute $\partial z/\partial x$ there.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the implicit function theorem: it applies when $\partial F/\partial z \neq 0$ at the point, where $F$ is the equation moved to one side. Then $\partial z/\partial x = -F_x / F_z$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Set $F(x, y, z) = x^2 + y^2 + z^2 + \sin(xyz) - 4$. We treat the point as a solution $p_0 = (x_0, y_0, z_0)$ of $F = 0$ near $(1, 1, \sqrt2)$ (the $\sin$ term is a small perturbation; the implicit function theorem is a local statement requiring only $F(p_0) = 0$ and $F_z(p_0) \neq 0$, so we carry $p_0$ symbolically).

Partials: $F_x = 2x + yz\cos(xyz)$, $F_z = 2z + xy\cos(xyz)$. At a point near $(1,1,\sqrt2)$, $F_z \approx 2\sqrt2 + \cos(\sqrt2) \neq 0$. Since $F$ is $C^1$ and $F_z(p_0) \neq 0$, the implicit function theorem gives a $C^1$ function $z = h(x, y)$ near $p_0$ with $F(x, y, h(x,y)) = 0$.

Differentiating $F(x, y, h) = 0$ in $x$: $F_x + F_z\, \partial z/\partial x = 0$, so
$$
\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{F_x}{F_z} = -\frac{2x + yz\cos(xyz)}{2z + xy\cos(xyz)},
$$
evaluated at $p_0$. The theorem guarantees local solvability and the formula; the explicit numeric value follows from substituting $p_0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, numeric).** *Lagrange multipliers with a product objective.*

Maximise $f(x, y) = xy$ subject to $x + y = 10$ with $x, y \geq 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Either substitute the constraint, or set $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$ with $g = x + y - 10$. Compare both routes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Lagrange: $\nabla f = (y, x)$, $\nabla g = (1, 1)$. The condition $(y, x) = \lambda(1, 1)$ gives $x = y = \lambda$. The constraint $x + y = 10$ then yields $x = y = 5$, and $f(5, 5) = 25$.

Check the endpoints: at $(10, 0)$ and $(0, 10)$, $f = 0$. So the maximum on the feasible segment is $25$ at $(5, 5)$.

This is the arithmetic-geometric mean inequality in disguise: for fixed sum $x + y$, the product $xy$ is maximised when $x = y$, giving $xy \leq \left(\frac{x+y}{2}\right)^2 = 25$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Inverse function theorem and local invertibility.*

Let $f(x, y) = (e^x \cos y,\; e^x \sin y)$. Show $f$ is locally invertible at every point, compute $\det Df$, yet explain why $f$ is not globally injective.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Compute the Jacobian determinant; the inverse function theorem applies wherever it is nonzero. For global injectivity, consider the effect of replacing $y$ by $y + 2\pi$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Jacobian:
$$
Df = \begin{pmatrix} e^x\cos y & -e^x\sin y \\ e^x\sin y & e^x\cos y\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \det Df = e^{2x}(\cos^2 y + \sin^2 y) = e^{2x} > 0
$$
for all $(x, y)$. Since $f$ is $C^1$ and $Df$ is invertible everywhere, the inverse function theorem gives a local $C^1$ inverse near every point.

But $f$ is not globally injective: $f(x, y + 2\pi) = (e^x\cos(y + 2\pi), e^x\sin(y+2\pi)) = f(x, y)$, so $f$ is $2\pi$-periodic in $y$ and identifies infinitely many points. This is the complex exponential $z = x + iy \mapsto e^z$, locally a diffeomorphism (nonzero derivative) but globally a covering map onto $\mathbb{C} \setminus \{0\}$. The inverse function theorem is inherently local — nonvanishing Jacobian gives local but never global invertibility on its own.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *Second-order Taylor and a degenerate critical point.*

For $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^4$, show the origin is a critical point where the Hessian is positive-semidefinite but singular, and prove directly that it is nonetheless a strict local minimum.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The Hessian test is inconclusive when $\det H_f = 0$. Argue directly from the form of $f$ near the origin instead of from the quadratic Taylor term.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

$\nabla f = (2x, 4y^3)$, which vanishes only at $(0, 0)$ — a critical point. The Hessian is $H_f = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 12y^2\end{pmatrix}$; at the origin $H_f = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}$, positive-semidefinite with $\det H_f = 0$. The second-derivative test is inconclusive.

The second-order Taylor expansion $f(x,y) = x^2 + o(|(x,y)|^2)$ loses the $y$-information, since $y^4$ is higher order. So we argue directly: for every $(x, y) \neq (0, 0)$,
$$
f(x, y) = x^2 + y^4 > 0 = f(0, 0),
$$
because $x^2 \geq 0$ and $y^4 \geq 0$ cannot both vanish unless $x = y = 0$. Hence the origin is a strict global (in particular local) minimum.

The lesson: a singular Hessian forces a direct or higher-order analysis. Here the quartic flat direction $y$ still curves upward, but only the fourth-order term detects it.

</details>

</aside>
