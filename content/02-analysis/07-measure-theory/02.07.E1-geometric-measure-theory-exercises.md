## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

The geometric integration theory of Whitney, sharpened by Federer and Evans-Gariepy, measures the size of irregular sets and the volume of non-smooth images. Its tools are the $s$-dimensional Hausdorff measure $\mathcal{H}^s$, built by the Carathéodory construction from diameters of small covering sets; the resulting Hausdorff dimension; Lipschitz maps, whose almost-everywhere differentiability is Rademacher's theorem; the area formula, which computes the $\mathcal{H}^m$-measure of the image of an injective Lipschitz map $f:\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$ ($m \le n$) by integrating the Jacobian; the coarea formula, its companion for $m \ge n$ that slices by level sets; and countably $m$-rectifiable sets, those covered up to $\mathcal{H}^m$-null error by countably many Lipschitz images of $\mathbb{R}^m$.

This pack collects ten exercises — three easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units. The exercises group loosely by topic: computing Hausdorff measure and dimension of explicit sets (easy); Lipschitz constants, Rademacher differentiability, and the area formula on graphs (medium); and the coarea formula and the structure of rectifiable sets (hard).

Conventions follow Evans-Gariepy: $\mathcal{H}^s$ is normalized so that $\mathcal{H}^n = \mathcal{L}^n$ (Lebesgue measure) on $\mathbb{R}^n$, with constant $\alpha(s) = \pi^{s/2}/\Gamma(s/2 + 1)$; the Jacobian of a linear map $L:\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$ ($m \le n$) is $\mathbf{J}L = \sqrt{\det(L^{\top}L)}$.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining nine follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *State the area formula for a Lipschitz map $f:\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$ with $m \le n$, and use it to derive the classical surface-area formula for the graph of a $C^1$ function $u:\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}$.*

**Solution.** The **area formula** states: for a Lipschitz map $f:\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$ with $m \le n$ and a Lebesgue-measurable set $A \subset \mathbb{R}^m$,
$$
\int_A \mathbf{J}f(x)\,d\mathcal{L}^m(x) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \#\bigl(A \cap f^{-1}(y)\bigr)\,d\mathcal{H}^m(y),
$$
where $\mathbf{J}f(x) = \sqrt{\det\bigl(Df(x)^{\top}Df(x)\bigr)}$ is the $m$-dimensional Jacobian (defined $\mathcal{L}^m$-a.e. by Rademacher's theorem), and $\#$ counts preimages. When $f$ is injective on $A$ the multiplicity is $1$, so the right side is $\mathcal{H}^m(f(A))$ — the $m$-area of the image.

Apply it to the graph map $f:\Omega \to \mathbb{R}^{m+1}$, $f(x) = (x, u(x))$, which is injective with
$$
Df = \begin{pmatrix} I_m \\ \nabla u^{\top} \end{pmatrix}, \qquad Df^{\top}Df = I_m + \nabla u\,\nabla u^{\top}.
$$
The matrix $I_m + \nabla u\,\nabla u^{\top}$ is a rank-one perturbation of the identity; by the matrix-determinant lemma $\det(I_m + \nabla u\,\nabla u^{\top}) = 1 + |\nabla u|^2$. Hence $\mathbf{J}f = \sqrt{1 + |\nabla u|^2}$, and the area formula gives
$$
\mathcal{H}^m(\mathrm{graph}\,u) = \int_{\Omega} \sqrt{1 + |\nabla u(x)|^2}\,d\mathcal{L}^m(x).
$$
This is exactly the elementary surface-area integral, now derived as a special case of the Lipschitz area formula. $\square$

The area formula is the engine of the whole subject: it reduces the geometric measure of a parametrized surface to an integral of a Jacobian over the parameter domain, and it extends verbatim to merely Lipschitz $f$ because Rademacher's theorem supplies $Df$ almost everywhere.

**Exercise 1 (easy, numeric).** *Hausdorff dimension of the middle-thirds Cantor set.*

Compute the Hausdorff dimension of the standard middle-thirds Cantor set $C \subset [0,1]$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$C$ is self-similar under two maps each contracting by $1/3$. The similarity dimension $s$ solves $2\cdot(1/3)^s = 1$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The Cantor set is the attractor of the iterated function system $\{x \mapsto x/3,\ x \mapsto x/3 + 2/3\}$: two similarities, each with ratio $r = 1/3$, satisfying the open-set condition. The similarity dimension $s$ solves $N r^s = 1$ with $N = 2$ pieces:
$$
2\cdot (1/3)^s = 1 \implies 3^s = 2 \implies s = \frac{\log 2}{\log 3} \approx 0.6309.
$$
For an IFS satisfying the open-set condition, the Hausdorff dimension equals the similarity dimension, and $0 < \mathcal{H}^s(C) < \infty$ at this exponent. So $\dim_{\mathcal{H}} C = \log 2 / \log 3$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, numeric).** *Hausdorff measure scaling.*

Let $A \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ and let $T(x) = \lambda x$ be a dilation by $\lambda > 0$. Express $\mathcal{H}^s(T(A))$ in terms of $\mathcal{H}^s(A)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A cover of $A$ by sets of diameter $\le \delta$ maps under $T$ to a cover of $T(A)$ by sets of diameter $\le \lambda\delta$, and $\mathrm{diam}\,T(U) = \lambda\,\mathrm{diam}\,U$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For any set $U$, $\mathrm{diam}\,T(U) = \lambda\,\mathrm{diam}\,U$. In the Carathéodory construction, the $s$-content of a cover $\{U_i\}$ contributes $\sum_i \alpha(s)(\mathrm{diam}\,U_i / 2)^s$; replacing each $U_i$ by $T(U_i)$ multiplies every diameter by $\lambda$, hence multiplies each summand by $\lambda^s$. The correspondence $\{U_i\} \leftrightarrow \{T(U_i)\}$ is a bijection between $\delta$-covers of $A$ and $\lambda\delta$-covers of $T(A)$. Passing to the infimum and then $\delta \to 0$,
$$
\mathcal{H}^s(T(A)) = \lambda^s\,\mathcal{H}^s(A).
$$
This is the homogeneity of Hausdorff measure under scaling — the reason $\mathcal{H}^s$ is the right gauge for self-similar sets and the source of the dimension-defining exponent.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, proof).** *Lipschitz maps do not raise Hausdorff dimension.*

Let $f:\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$ be Lipschitz with constant $L$. Show $\mathcal{H}^s(f(A)) \le L^s\,\mathcal{H}^s(A)$ for every $A \subset \mathbb{R}^m$, and conclude $\dim_{\mathcal{H}} f(A) \le \dim_{\mathcal{H}} A$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\mathrm{diam}\,f(U) \le L\,\mathrm{diam}\,U$. Push a $\delta$-cover of $A$ through $f$ to get an $L\delta$-cover of $f(A)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Lipschitz with constant $L$ means $|f(x) - f(y)| \le L|x - y|$, so for any $U$, $\mathrm{diam}\,f(U) \le L\,\mathrm{diam}\,U$. Given a cover $\{U_i\}$ of $A$ with $\mathrm{diam}\,U_i \le \delta$, the sets $\{f(U_i)\}$ cover $f(A)$ with $\mathrm{diam}\,f(U_i) \le L\delta$. Then
$$
\mathcal{H}^s_{L\delta}(f(A)) \le \sum_i \alpha(s)\Bigl(\tfrac{\mathrm{diam}\,f(U_i)}{2}\Bigr)^s \le L^s \sum_i \alpha(s)\Bigl(\tfrac{\mathrm{diam}\,U_i}{2}\Bigr)^s.
$$
Taking the infimum over $\delta$-covers gives $\mathcal{H}^s_{L\delta}(f(A)) \le L^s \mathcal{H}^s_\delta(A)$; letting $\delta \to 0$ yields $\mathcal{H}^s(f(A)) \le L^s\,\mathcal{H}^s(A)$.

If $s > \dim_{\mathcal{H}} A$ then $\mathcal{H}^s(A) = 0$, so $\mathcal{H}^s(f(A)) = 0$, hence $\dim_{\mathcal{H}} f(A) \le s$. Taking the infimum over such $s$ gives $\dim_{\mathcal{H}} f(A) \le \dim_{\mathcal{H}} A$. Lipschitz maps cannot increase Hausdorff dimension.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *Rademacher in one variable.*

State Rademacher's theorem and prove the one-dimensional case: a Lipschitz function $f:\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is differentiable $\mathcal{L}^1$-almost everywhere.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A Lipschitz function is absolutely continuous; invoke the Lebesgue differentiation theorem for the fundamental theorem of calculus, or use that monotone functions are differentiable a.e. after writing $f$ as a difference of monotone functions.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Rademacher's theorem.** A locally Lipschitz map $f:\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is differentiable at $\mathcal{L}^m$-almost every point.

**One-dimensional proof.** Let $f:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ be $L$-Lipschitz. Then $f$ is absolutely continuous: for disjoint intervals $(a_i, b_i)$, $\sum_i |f(b_i) - f(a_i)| \le L\sum_i (b_i - a_i)$, so the variation is controlled by total length and the absolute-continuity $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ condition holds with $\delta = \varepsilon/L$. By the fundamental theorem of calculus for absolutely continuous functions, $f$ is differentiable $\mathcal{L}^1$-a.e., $f' \in L^\infty$ with $|f'| \le L$ a.e., and $f(b) - f(a) = \int_a^b f'(t)\,dt$.

(Equivalently: $g(x) = Lx + f(x)$ and $h(x) = Lx - f(x)$ are both nondecreasing because $f$ is $L$-Lipschitz; monotone functions are differentiable a.e. by Lebesgue's theorem, so $f = \tfrac{1}{2L}(g - h)$ is differentiable a.e.) The higher-dimensional Rademacher theorem reduces partial-derivative existence to this one-variable statement along lines, then upgrades a.e. partials to full differentiability.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).** *Area of a paraboloid cap via the area formula.*

Use the area formula to compute the $\mathcal{H}^2$-measure of the graph of $u(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$ over the disk $\{x^2 + y^2 \le 1\}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For a graph, $\mathbf{J}f = \sqrt{1 + |\nabla u|^2}$. Here $|\nabla u|^2 = 4(x^2+y^2)$; switch to polar coordinates.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The graph map $f(x,y) = (x, y, x^2 + y^2)$ has $\nabla u = (2x, 2y)$, so $|\nabla u|^2 = 4(x^2 + y^2)$ and $\mathbf{J}f = \sqrt{1 + 4(x^2 + y^2)}$. By the area formula,
$$
\mathcal{H}^2(\mathrm{graph}\,u) = \int_{x^2+y^2\le 1} \sqrt{1 + 4(x^2 + y^2)}\,dx\,dy.
$$
In polar coordinates ($r \in [0,1]$, area element $r\,dr\,d\theta$):
$$
= \int_0^{2\pi}\!\!\int_0^1 \sqrt{1 + 4r^2}\,r\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{12}(1 + 4r^2)^{3/2}\Big|_0^1 = \frac{\pi}{6}\bigl(5^{3/2} - 1\bigr).
$$
Numerically $5^{3/2} = 5\sqrt5 \approx 11.1803$, so the area is $\tfrac{\pi}{6}(11.1803 - 1) \approx \tfrac{\pi}{6}(10.1803) \approx 5.330$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).** *Jacobian of a linear map.*

For a linear map $L:\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$ with $m \le n$, show the area-formula Jacobian $\mathbf{J}L = \sqrt{\det(L^{\top}L)}$ equals the product of the singular values of $L$, and interpret it geometrically.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the singular value decomposition $L = U\Sigma V^{\top}$ with $\Sigma$ carrying singular values $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_m \ge 0$. Compute $L^{\top}L$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write the singular value decomposition $L = U\Sigma V^{\top}$, where $U \in O(n)$, $V \in O(m)$, and $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times m}$ has the singular values $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_m \ge 0$ on its diagonal. Then
$$
L^{\top}L = V\Sigma^{\top}U^{\top}U\Sigma V^{\top} = V(\Sigma^{\top}\Sigma)V^{\top} = V\,\mathrm{diag}(\sigma_1^2,\ldots,\sigma_m^2)\,V^{\top}.
$$
So $\det(L^{\top}L) = \prod_{i=1}^m \sigma_i^2$ and $\mathbf{J}L = \sqrt{\det(L^{\top}L)} = \prod_{i=1}^m \sigma_i$.

Geometrically, $L$ maps the unit cube in $\mathbb{R}^m$ to an $m$-parallelepiped in $\mathbb{R}^n$ whose edges have lengths $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_m$ along the orthonormal directions $V e_i$; its $m$-dimensional volume is the product of those lengths, $\prod \sigma_i = \mathbf{J}L$. This is why $\mathbf{J}f(x)$ is the local volume-stretch factor of the area formula: it is the $m$-volume of the image of the infinitesimal unit cube under $Df(x)$.

</details>

</aside>

**Exercise 7 (medium, numeric).** *Coarea on a linear functional.*

Apply the coarea formula to $f:\mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$, $f(x) = x_3$, on the unit ball $B$, to check that integrating the $\mathcal{H}^2$-measure of the level disks recovers the volume of $B$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The coarea formula reads $\int_B \mathbf{J}f\,d\mathcal{L}^3 = \int_{\mathbb{R}} \mathcal{H}^2(B \cap f^{-1}(t))\,dt$, with $\mathbf{J}f = |\nabla f|$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Here $\nabla f = (0,0,1)$, so the coarea Jacobian is $\mathbf{J}f = |\nabla f| = 1$, and the left side is $\int_B 1\,d\mathcal{L}^3 = \mathcal{L}^3(B) = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi$.

The level set $f^{-1}(t) = \{x_3 = t\}$ meets $B$ in the disk of radius $\sqrt{1 - t^2}$ for $|t| \le 1$, with $\mathcal{H}^2$-measure $\pi(1 - t^2)$. The right side is
$$
\int_{-1}^{1} \pi(1 - t^2)\,dt = \pi\Bigl[t - \tfrac{t^3}{3}\Bigr]_{-1}^{1} = \pi\Bigl(\tfrac{2}{3} + \tfrac{2}{3}\Bigr) = \frac{4\pi}{3}.
$$
Both sides equal $\tfrac{4}{3}\pi$: the coarea formula reproduces the volume by integrating the areas of horizontal slices. This is the Lipschitz generalization of Fubini's theorem along the level sets of $f$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Coarea form of the layer-cake / Eilenberg inequality.*

Let $f:\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}$ be Lipschitz with constant $L$. Prove the Eilenberg inequality
$$
\int_{\mathbb{R}} \mathcal{H}^{m-1}\bigl(A \cap f^{-1}(t)\bigr)\,dt \le L\,\mathcal{L}^m(A)
$$
for measurable $A \subset \mathbb{R}^m$, as a corollary of the coarea formula.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The coarea formula gives equality with $|\nabla f|$ in place of $L$ inside the volume integral. Bound $|\nabla f| \le L$ a.e. by Rademacher.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The coarea formula for a Lipschitz $f:\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}$ states, for measurable $A$,
$$
\int_A |\nabla f(x)|\,d\mathcal{L}^m(x) = \int_{\mathbb{R}} \mathcal{H}^{m-1}\bigl(A \cap f^{-1}(t)\bigr)\,dt,
$$
where $\nabla f$ exists $\mathcal{L}^m$-a.e. by Rademacher's theorem. Since $f$ is $L$-Lipschitz, $|\nabla f(x)| \le L$ at every point of differentiability, hence $\mathcal{L}^m$-a.e. Therefore
$$
\int_{\mathbb{R}} \mathcal{H}^{m-1}\bigl(A \cap f^{-1}(t)\bigr)\,dt = \int_A |\nabla f|\,d\mathcal{L}^m \le \int_A L\,d\mathcal{L}^m = L\,\mathcal{L}^m(A).
$$
This is the Eilenberg inequality: the total $(m-1)$-area of the level sets of a Lipschitz function inside $A$ is controlled by the volume of $A$ times the Lipschitz constant. It is the technical workhorse behind many size estimates in geometric measure theory, including the proof that a Lipschitz function maps null sets to "slice-thin" families of level sets.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *A Lipschitz graph is rectifiable.*

Show that the graph $\Gamma = \{(x, u(x)) : x \in \mathbb{R}^m\}$ of a Lipschitz function $u:\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is a countably $m$-rectifiable set, and that $\mathcal{H}^m(\Gamma \cap K) < \infty$ for every compact $K$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Rectifiable means covered up to $\mathcal{H}^m$-null error by countably many Lipschitz images of $\mathbb{R}^m$. The graph map $f(x) = (x, u(x))$ is itself such an image.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A set $E \subset \mathbb{R}^{m+n}$ is **countably $m$-rectifiable** if there are Lipschitz maps $f_i:\mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^{m+n}$ with $\mathcal{H}^m\bigl(E \setminus \bigcup_i f_i(\mathbb{R}^m)\bigr) = 0$.

The graph map $f(x) = (x, u(x))$ is Lipschitz: $|f(x) - f(y)|^2 = |x-y|^2 + |u(x) - u(y)|^2 \le (1 + L^2)|x - y|^2$, so $f$ has Lipschitz constant $\sqrt{1 + L^2}$ where $L = \mathrm{Lip}(u)$. Its image is exactly $\Gamma$. Thus a single Lipschitz image covers $\Gamma$ with zero error, so $\Gamma$ is countably $m$-rectifiable.

Finiteness: for compact $K \subset \mathbb{R}^{m+n}$, $\Gamma \cap K \subset f(B)$ for a bounded set $B \subset \mathbb{R}^m$ (the projection of $K$). By the Lipschitz size estimate (Exercise 3), $\mathcal{H}^m(f(B)) \le (\sqrt{1+L^2})^m\,\mathcal{H}^m(B) = (1+L^2)^{m/2}\,\mathcal{L}^m(B) < \infty$. Hence $\mathcal{H}^m(\Gamma \cap K) < \infty$. Equivalently, the area formula (lead exercise) gives the exact value $\int_B \mathbf{J}f\,d\mathcal{L}^m$, with $\mathbf{J}f = \sqrt{\det(I + Du^{\top}Du)}$ bounded by $(1+L^2)^{m/2}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, proof).** *Approximate tangent plane of a rectifiable set.*

State what it means for a countably $m$-rectifiable set $E$ with $\mathcal{H}^m \llcorner E$ locally finite to have an approximate tangent plane $\mathcal{H}^m$-a.e., and explain why this fails for a purely unrectifiable set such as a positive-$\mathcal{H}^1$-measure subset of the plane that projects to null on every line.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

An approximate tangent plane at $x$ is an $m$-plane $P$ such that the rescaled measures $(\eta_{x,\lambda})_\#(\mathcal{H}^m \llcorner E)$ converge to $\mathcal{H}^m \llcorner P$ as $\lambda \to 0$, where $\eta_{x,\lambda}(y) = (y - x)/\lambda$. Purely unrectifiable sets have no blow-up limit concentrated on a plane.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a countably $m$-rectifiable $E$ with $\theta = \mathcal{H}^m \llcorner E$ locally finite, the **rectifiability structure theorem** gives: at $\mathcal{H}^m$-a.e. $x \in E$ there is an $m$-plane $T_x E$ (the approximate tangent plane) such that the blow-ups
$$
(\eta_{x,\lambda})_\#\,\theta \;\xrightarrow[\lambda \to 0]{}\; \mathcal{H}^m \llcorner T_x E \quad \text{weakly},
$$
where $\eta_{x,\lambda}(y) = (y-x)/\lambda$. Since $E$ is, up to null error, a countable union of Lipschitz graphs, and each Lipschitz graph has a true tangent plane a.e. (by Rademacher's differentiability of the defining function), the tangent planes patch together to give $T_x E$ almost everywhere.

For a purely $1$-unrectifiable set $E \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ with $0 < \mathcal{H}^1(E) < \infty$ — for instance a "four-corner" Cantor dust whose orthogonal projections onto $\mathcal{L}^1$-almost every line are null (Besicovitch) — no such tangent line exists at $\mathcal{H}^1$-a.e. point: the blow-up measures do not concentrate on any line, because the set looks the same (totally disconnected, projecting to null) at every scale. The Besicovitch-Federer projection theorem makes this precise: $E$ is purely unrectifiable iff its projection to $\mathcal{H}^1$-almost every line is $\mathcal{L}^1$-null, the exact opposite of the rectifiable case, where projections have positive measure in most directions. Thus the existence of approximate tangent planes a.e. characterizes rectifiability.

</details>

</aside>
