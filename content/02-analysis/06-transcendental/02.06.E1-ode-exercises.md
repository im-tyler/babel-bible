## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Apostol Vol. 2 Chapters 6-7 treat linear ordinary differential equations through the linear-algebra lens. Chapter 6 develops the first-order linear equation $y' + p(x)y = q(x)$ via the integrating factor, then the $n$-th-order constant-coefficient equation $Ly = 0$ whose solution space is an $n$-dimensional vector space governed by the characteristic polynomial of $L = p(D)$; it adds the Wronskian test for independence, and the inhomogeneous solution by undetermined coefficients and variation of parameters. Chapter 7 generalises to systems $\dot{x} = Ax$, solved by the matrix exponential $e^{tA}$, computed through diagonalisation or Jordan form.

This pack collects ten problems from those chapters — three easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a complete worked solution. It tests operational competence: solving a first-order linear equation, building the general solution of a constant-coefficient equation from roots of the characteristic polynomial, handling resonance in the forced case, testing independence with the Wronskian, exponentiating a matrix, and producing a power-series solution.

Conventions follow the prerequisite units: $D = d/dx$ is the differentiation operator; $L = p(D)$ a constant-coefficient operator with characteristic polynomial $p(r)$; the Wronskian of $y_1, \ldots, y_n$ is $W = \det(y_j^{(i-1)})$; $e^{tA} = \sum_{k\geq 0} t^k A^k / k!$ the matrix exponential.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one problem in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining nine follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead problem.** *Forced harmonic oscillator with resonance.* Solve $y'' + \omega^2 y = \cos(\omega t)$ with $y(0) = 0$, $y'(0) = 0$, where the forcing frequency matches the natural frequency $\omega$.

**Solution.** The homogeneous equation $y'' + \omega^2 y = 0$ has characteristic polynomial $r^2 + \omega^2 = 0$, roots $r = \pm i\omega$, so $y_h = c_1\cos(\omega t) + c_2\sin(\omega t)$.

For a particular solution, the forcing $\cos(\omega t)$ coincides with a homogeneous solution — this is resonance. Undetermined coefficients then requires multiplying the trial form by $t$: try $y_p = t(A\cos\omega t + B\sin\omega t)$. Differentiate:
$$
y_p' = A\cos\omega t + B\sin\omega t + t(-A\omega\sin\omega t + B\omega\cos\omega t),
$$
$$
y_p'' = -2A\omega\sin\omega t + 2B\omega\cos\omega t + t(-A\omega^2\cos\omega t - B\omega^2\sin\omega t).
$$
Then $y_p'' + \omega^2 y_p = -2A\omega\sin\omega t + 2B\omega\cos\omega t$ (the $t$-terms cancel). Matching $\cos(\omega t)$: $2B\omega = 1$, so $B = \frac{1}{2\omega}$; matching $\sin(\omega t)$: $A = 0$. Thus $y_p = \frac{t}{2\omega}\sin(\omega t)$.

General solution: $y = c_1\cos\omega t + c_2\sin\omega t + \frac{t}{2\omega}\sin\omega t$. Apply initial conditions. $y(0) = c_1 = 0$. Then $y' = c_2\omega\cos\omega t + \frac{1}{2\omega}\sin\omega t + \frac{t}{2}\cos\omega t$, and $y'(0) = c_2\omega = 0$, so $c_2 = 0$.

Final: $y(t) = \dfrac{t}{2\omega}\sin(\omega t)$. $\square$

The amplitude grows linearly in $t$ — the signature of resonance. Driving an undamped oscillator at its natural frequency feeds energy in coherently, and the response is unbounded. This is the canonical Apostol Ch. 6 resonance example.

## Exercises [Intermediate]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).** *First-order linear equation by integrating factor.*

Solve $y' + 2y = e^{-x}$ with $y(0) = 1$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The integrating factor is $\mu = e^{\int 2\,dx} = e^{2x}$. Multiply through and recognise the left side as $(\mu y)'$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Integrating factor $\mu = e^{2x}$. Multiply: $e^{2x}y' + 2e^{2x}y = e^{2x}e^{-x} = e^x$, i.e. $(e^{2x}y)' = e^x$. Integrate: $e^{2x}y = e^x + C$, so $y = e^{-x} + Ce^{-2x}$.

Apply $y(0) = 1$: $1 = 1 + C$, so $C = 0$. Thus $y = e^{-x}$.

Check: $y' = -e^{-x}$, and $y' + 2y = -e^{-x} + 2e^{-x} = e^{-x}$. Correct.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).** *Separable equation.*

Solve $y' = xy^2$ with $y(0) = 1$, and state the interval of existence.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Separate: $\frac{dy}{y^2} = x\,dx$. Integrate both sides, then solve for $y$ and apply the initial condition.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Separate variables: $\int y^{-2}\,dy = \int x\,dx$, giving $-\frac{1}{y} = \frac{x^2}{2} + C$. Solve: $y = \frac{-1}{x^2/2 + C} = \frac{-2}{x^2 + 2C}$.

Apply $y(0) = 1$: $1 = -2/(2C)$, so $2C = -2$, $C = -1$. Thus
$$
y = \frac{-2}{x^2 - 2} = \frac{2}{2 - x^2}.
$$
The solution blows up as $x \to \pm\sqrt2$, so the maximal interval of existence containing $0$ is $(-\sqrt2, \sqrt2)$. A nonlinear equation can have solutions that escape to infinity in finite "time" even when the right side is smooth everywhere.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (easy, symbolic).** *Constant-coefficient homogeneous equation.*

Find the general solution of $y'' - 5y' + 6y = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The characteristic polynomial is $r^2 - 5r + 6$. Distinct real roots $r_1, r_2$ give $y = c_1 e^{r_1 x} + c_2 e^{r_2 x}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Characteristic polynomial: $r^2 - 5r + 6 = (r - 2)(r - 3)$, roots $r = 2, 3$. The two roots are distinct and real, so
$$
y = c_1 e^{2x} + c_2 e^{3x}.
$$
The solutions $e^{2x}, e^{3x}$ are linearly independent (their Wronskian is $e^{5x}(3 - 2) = e^{5x} \neq 0$), and span the $2$-dimensional solution space.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, symbolic).** *Repeated root.*

Solve $y'' - 4y' + 4y = 0$ and explain the form of the second solution.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The characteristic polynomial has a double root. When $r$ is a root of multiplicity $2$, the solution space is spanned by $e^{rx}$ and $x e^{rx}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Characteristic polynomial: $r^2 - 4r + 4 = (r - 2)^2$, a double root $r = 2$. A single exponential $e^{2x}$ gives only a one-dimensional family, but the equation's solution space is two-dimensional, so a second independent solution is needed.

For a root $r$ of multiplicity $m$, the functions $e^{rx}, x e^{rx}, \ldots, x^{m-1}e^{rx}$ all solve $Ly = 0$. Here $m = 2$, so the general solution is
$$
y = (c_1 + c_2 x)e^{2x}.
$$
Verification that $xe^{2x}$ works: writing $L = (D - 2)^2$, we have $(D - 2)(xe^{2x}) = e^{2x}$ and $(D-2)^2(xe^{2x}) = (D-2)e^{2x} = 0$. The factor $x$ appears precisely because the operator has the repeated factor $(D - 2)$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).** *Undetermined coefficients (non-resonant).*

Find a particular solution of $y'' + y = 3e^{2x}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Since $e^{2x}$ is not a homogeneous solution (roots are $\pm i$), try $y_p = Ae^{2x}$ and solve for $A$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Homogeneous roots: $r^2 + 1 = 0$, $r = \pm i$, so $e^{2x}$ is not a homogeneous solution and the simple trial form works. Try $y_p = Ae^{2x}$: then $y_p'' = 4Ae^{2x}$, and
$$
y_p'' + y_p = 4Ae^{2x} + Ae^{2x} = 5Ae^{2x} = 3e^{2x}.
$$
So $5A = 3$, $A = \frac{3}{5}$. A particular solution is $y_p = \frac{3}{5}e^{2x}$.

The full general solution would be $y = c_1\cos x + c_2\sin x + \frac{3}{5}e^{2x}$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).** *Wronskian and Abel's identity.*

For $y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = 0$ with solutions $y_1, y_2$, prove the Wronskian $W = y_1 y_2' - y_2 y_1'$ satisfies $W' = -p(x)W$, hence $W(x) = W(x_0)\exp\!\big(-\!\int_{x_0}^x p\big)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Differentiate $W$ directly. The $y_i''$ terms can be replaced using the ODE, and most terms cancel.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Differentiate $W = y_1 y_2' - y_2 y_1'$:
$$
W' = y_1' y_2' + y_1 y_2'' - y_2' y_1' - y_2 y_1'' = y_1 y_2'' - y_2 y_1''.
$$
Each solution satisfies $y_i'' = -p y_i' - q y_i$. Substitute:
$$
W' = y_1(-p y_2' - q y_2) - y_2(-p y_1' - q y_1) = -p(y_1 y_2' - y_2 y_1') - q(y_1 y_2 - y_2 y_1) = -p W,
$$
since the $q$-term vanishes. This first-order linear equation $W' = -pW$ integrates to $W(x) = W(x_0)\exp\!\big(-\int_{x_0}^x p(t)\,dt\big)$ (Abel's formula).

Consequence: the Wronskian is either identically zero or never zero on the interval. So $y_1, y_2$ are linearly independent iff $W \neq 0$ at a single point.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (medium, numeric).** *Matrix exponential by diagonalisation.*

Compute $e^{tA}$ for $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 3\end{pmatrix}$, and write the solution of $\dot{x} = Ax$ with $x(0) = (1, 1)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Diagonalise $A = PDP^{-1}$; then $e^{tA} = P e^{tD} P^{-1}$ with $e^{tD}$ diagonal. The eigenvalues are the diagonal entries (triangular matrix).

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Eigenvalues $1, 3$ (triangular). For $\lambda = 1$: $(A - I) = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 2 \\ 0 & 2\end{pmatrix}$, eigenvector $(1, 0)$. For $\lambda = 3$: $(A - 3I) = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 2 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}$, eigenvector $(1, 1)$. So
$$
P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}, \quad P^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}, \quad e^{tD} = \begin{pmatrix} e^t & 0 \\ 0 & e^{3t}\end{pmatrix}.
$$
Then
$$
e^{tA} = P e^{tD} P^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} e^t & e^{3t} - e^t \\ 0 & e^{3t}\end{pmatrix}.
$$
With $x(0) = (1, 1)$: $x(t) = e^{tA}(1,1)^T = (e^t + e^{3t} - e^t,\; e^{3t}) = (e^{3t}, e^{3t})$. (Consistent: $(1,1)$ is the eigenvector for $\lambda = 3$, so the trajectory is pure $e^{3t}$ growth along it.)

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, numeric).** *Matrix exponential of a defective matrix.*

Compute $e^{tA}$ for the non-diagonalisable matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 2\end{pmatrix}$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $A = 2I + N$ with $N = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}$ nilpotent ($N^2 = 0$). Since $2I$ and $N$ commute, $e^{tA} = e^{2tI}e^{tN}$, and the series for $e^{tN}$ terminates.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Write $A = 2I + N$ with $N = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}$. Then $N^2 = 0$, so $e^{tN} = I + tN = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & t \\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}$ (series terminates). Because $2I$ commutes with $N$,
$$
e^{tA} = e^{2tI}e^{tN} = e^{2t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & t \\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} e^{2t} & t e^{2t} \\ 0 & e^{2t}\end{pmatrix}.
$$
The off-diagonal $t e^{2t}$ is the Jordan-block signature: a repeated eigenvalue with a single eigenvector produces a polynomial-times-exponential term, exactly the $x e^{rx}$ second solution from Exercise 4 in matrix form.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, symbolic).** *Power-series solution.*

Find the power-series solution of $y'' - xy = 0$ (the Airy equation) about $x = 0$ with $y(0) = 1$, $y'(0) = 0$, giving the recurrence and the first few terms.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $y = \sum_{n\geq 0} a_n x^n$, substitute, and match coefficients of $x^n$ to get a recurrence relating $a_{n+2}$ to $a_{n-1}$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $y = \sum_{n\geq0} a_n x^n$. Then $y'' = \sum_{n\geq0}(n+2)(n+1)a_{n+2}x^n$ and $xy = \sum_{n\geq1} a_{n-1}x^n$. The equation $y'' - xy = 0$ gives, matching $x^n$:
$$
(n+2)(n+1)a_{n+2} = a_{n-1} \quad (n \geq 1), \qquad 2a_2 = 0 \;(n = 0).
$$
So $a_2 = 0$, and $a_{n+2} = \dfrac{a_{n-1}}{(n+2)(n+1)}$.

Initial conditions: $a_0 = y(0) = 1$, $a_1 = y'(0) = 0$. The recurrence steps by $3$. Since $a_1 = 0$, the chain $a_1, a_4, a_7, \ldots$ all vanish; $a_2 = 0$ kills $a_2, a_5, a_8, \ldots$. Only the $a_0$-chain survives:
$$
a_3 = \frac{a_0}{3\cdot 2} = \frac{1}{6}, \qquad a_6 = \frac{a_3}{6\cdot 5} = \frac{1}{180}, \ldots
$$
giving $y(x) = 1 + \frac{x^3}{6} + \frac{x^6}{180} + \cdots$ — the Airy function $\mathrm{Ai}$-type even-index solution normalised to $y(0) = 1$, $y'(0) = 0$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 10 (hard, proof).** *Reduction to a first-order system and uniqueness.*

Show that the second-order equation $y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = 0$ is equivalent to a first-order linear system $\dot{u} = A(x)u$, and use this to argue that a solution with $y(x_0) = 0$, $y'(x_0) = 0$ must be identically zero.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Set $u = (y, y')$. Express $u'$ in terms of $u$. Then invoke uniqueness for linear first-order systems with a given initial condition.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Set $u_1 = y$, $u_2 = y'$, so $u = (u_1, u_2)$. Then $u_1' = u_2$ and $u_2' = y'' = -p y' - q y = -q u_1 - p u_2$. In matrix form,
$$
u' = A(x)u, \qquad A(x) = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ -q(x) & -p(x)\end{pmatrix}.
$$
This is a first-order linear system, equivalent to the original equation: any solution $y$ gives $u = (y, y')$ solving the system, and conversely the first component of any system solution solves the second-order equation.

For uniqueness: if $y(x_0) = 0$ and $y'(x_0) = 0$, then $u(x_0) = (0, 0)$. The constant zero function $u \equiv 0$ solves $u' = A(x)u$ with this initial value. With $p, q$ continuous, $A(x)$ is continuous, so the linear system satisfies the Picard-Lindelöf hypotheses and its solution with given initial data is unique. Therefore $u \equiv 0$, hence $y \equiv 0$. This is why the solution space is exactly two-dimensional: a solution is pinned down by the pair $(y(x_0), y'(x_0))$.

</details>

</aside>
