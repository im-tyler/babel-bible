## Intuition [Beginner]

The integral of a function measures the total area between its graph and the horizontal axis. Imagine a speed-versus-time graph: the total distance covered equals the area enclosed under the speed curve.

To approximate this area, stack rectangles underneath and on top of the curve. Rectangles that extend above the graph give an overestimate; rectangles that fall below give an underestimate. The true area lies between these two bounds.

When the overestimate and the underestimate are close — and the gap between them shrinks as the rectangles get thinner — the area is pinned to a single number. That number is the integral.

This concept exists because many quantities in mathematics and physics — distances from velocities, accumulated growth, probabilities, areas of regions — arise from adding up infinitely many contributions, and the rectangle-stacking method gives a rigorous foundation for that total.

## Visual [Beginner]

The picture shows a curve rising from left to right on the interval from $0$ to $2$. Two stacks of rectangles sit on this interval. The taller rectangles (shaded darker) extend above the curve — their total area overestimates the shaded region between the curve and the axis. The shorter rectangles (shaded lighter) fall below — their total area underestimates the same region.

![Upper rectangles overestimating and lower rectangles underestimating the area under a curve.](/img/step-function-darboux-integral-placeholder.svg)

As the rectangles become thinner, both the overestimate and the underestimate converge toward the true shaded area between the curve and the horizontal axis.

## Worked example [Beginner]

Consider $f(x) = x^2$ on the interval from $0$ to $2$. Divide into $4$ equal strips, each of width $1/2$.

Step 1. Upper rectangles: take the largest value of $f$ in each strip. Heights are $f(1/2) = 1/4$, $f(1) = 1$, $f(3/2) = 9/4$, $f(2) = 4$. Each has width $1/2$, so the upper total is $(1/4 + 1 + 9/4 + 4) \times 1/2 = 30/8 = 15/4$.

Step 2. Lower rectangles: take the smallest value of $f$ in each strip. Heights are $f(0) = 0$, $f(1/2) = 1/4$, $f(1) = 1$, $f(3/2) = 9/4$. The lower total is $(0 + 1/4 + 1 + 9/4) \times 1/2 = 14/8 = 7/4$.

Step 3. The true area lies between $7/4 = 1.75$ and $15/4 = 3.75$. Using thinner strips narrows this range. With $8$ strips the gap halves. The exact area is $8/3 \approx 2.667$, squeezed between the two estimates.

What this tells us: any bounded curve has its area trapped between an upper estimate and a lower estimate, and refining the rectangles forces both toward the same number.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **partition** $P$ of a closed interval $[a,b]$ is a finite ordered set $a = x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_n = b$. The subinterval lengths are $\Delta x_i = x_i - x_{i-1}$, and the **mesh** of $P$ is $\|P\| = \max_i \Delta x_i$.

A **step function** $\varphi$ on $[a,b]$ is constant on each open subinterval $(x_{i-1}, x_i)$ of some partition. If $\varphi = c_i$ on $(x_{i-1}, x_i)$, the integral of $\varphi$ is $\int_a^b \varphi\,dx = \sum_{i=1}^n c_i \Delta x_i$.

For a bounded function $f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ and a partition $P = \{x_0, \ldots, x_n\}$, set:
$$M_i = \sup_{x \in [x_{i-1},x_i]} f(x), \qquad m_i = \inf_{x \in [x_{i-1},x_i]} f(x).$$

The **upper Darboux sum** is $U(f,P) = \sum_{i=1}^n M_i \Delta x_i$ and the **lower Darboux sum** is $L(f,P) = \sum_{i=1}^n m_i \Delta x_i$.

The **upper integral** and **lower integral** are:
$$\overline{\int_a^b} f\,dx = \inf_P\, U(f,P), \qquad \underline{\int_a^b} f\,dx = \sup_P\, L(f,P),$$
where the infimum and supremum range over all partitions of $[a,b]$.

A bounded function $f$ is **Darboux integrable** on $[a,b]$ if the upper and lower integrals coincide. Their common value is the Darboux integral, written $\int_a^b f\,dx$.

### Counterexamples to common slips [Intermediate+]

- *Ignoring boundedness.* The definition requires $f$ to be bounded on $[a,b]$. An unbounded function has at least one subinterval where the supremum is infinite, so upper Darboux sums are undefined. For instance $f(x) = 1/x$ is not Darboux integrable on $(0,1]$.

- *Confusing mesh refinement with the infimum.* A partition $P'$ refines $P$ if $P \subseteq P'$. Refinement always lowers $U$ and raises $L$, but two unrelated partitions need not be comparable. The upper integral is an infimum over *all* partitions, not a limit as mesh tends to zero.

- *Assuming a single partition suffices.* A single partition can produce a small gap $U - L$ by coincidence. Integrability demands that for *every* tolerance $\epsilon > 0$, *some* partition achieves $U - L < \epsilon$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (Darboux integrability criterion).** A bounded function $f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is Darboux integrable if and only if for every $\epsilon > 0$ there exists a partition $P$ of $[a,b]$ with $U(f,P) - L(f,P) < \epsilon$.

*Proof.* ($\Rightarrow$) Suppose $f$ is Darboux integrable. Let $I = \int_a^b f\,dx$. Since $I = \inf_P U(f,P)$, for any $\epsilon > 0$ there exists a partition $P_1$ with $U(f,P_1) < I + \epsilon/2$. Since $I = \sup_P L(f,P)$, there exists a partition $P_2$ with $L(f,P_2) > I - \epsilon/2$. Let $P^* = P_1 \cup P_2$, the common refinement. Then:
$$L(f,P_2) \le L(f,P^*) \le U(f,P^*) \le U(f,P_1),$$
so
$$U(f,P^*) - L(f,P^*) \le U(f,P_1) - L(f,P_2) < \left(I + \frac{\epsilon}{2}\right) - \left(I - \frac{\epsilon}{2}\right) = \epsilon.$$

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose for every $\epsilon > 0$ there exists a partition $P$ with $U(f,P) - L(f,P) < \epsilon$. For any partition $P$, the chain $L(f,P) \le \underline{\int_a^b} f\,dx \le \overline{\int_a^b} f\,dx \le U(f,P)$ holds, so:
$$0 \le \overline{\int_a^b} f\,dx - \underline{\int_a^b} f\,dx \le U(f,P) - L(f,P) < \epsilon.$$
Since $\epsilon > 0$ is arbitrary, the upper and lower integrals coincide and $f$ is Darboux integrable. $\square$

**Theorem (Continuous functions are integrable).** If $f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous, then $f$ is Darboux integrable on $[a,b]$.

*Proof.* A continuous function on a closed bounded interval is bounded, so upper and lower sums are defined. By the Heine-Cantor theorem, $f$ is uniformly continuous on $[a,b]$: given $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $|f(x) - f(y)| < \epsilon/(b-a)$ whenever $|x - y| < \delta$.

Choose a partition $P$ with mesh $\|P\| < \delta$. On each subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$, the extreme value theorem gives points where $f$ attains $M_i$ and $m_i$, and these points are within distance $< \delta$, so $M_i - m_i < \epsilon/(b-a)$. Then:
$$U(f,P) - L(f,P) = \sum_{i=1}^n (M_i - m_i)\Delta x_i < \frac{\epsilon}{b-a}\sum_{i=1}^n \Delta x_i = \epsilon.$$
By the Darboux criterion, $f$ is integrable. $\square$

**Bridge.** The epsilon criterion builds toward [02.03.03], where convergence of infinite sums was decided by tail behaviour — here the "tail" is the gap $U - L$, and shrinking it to zero pins the integral. The foundational reason the criterion works is that the real numbers are complete [02.02.01]: every non-empty set bounded above has a least upper bound, which guarantees that the infimum of all upper sums and the supremum of all lower sums exist. This is exactly the mechanism that identifies the upper and lower integrals when their gap vanishes. The central insight is that integrability reduces to a single quantitative condition — for any tolerance, find a partition — and this condition appears again in the Lebesgue criterion below.

Prove that if $f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is monotone increasing, then $f$ is Darboux integrable on $[a,b]$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the uniform partition $P_n = \{a + i(b-a)/n : 0 \le i \le n\}$. Since $f$ is increasing, $M_i - m_i = f(x_i) - f(x_{i-1})$. Show that the sum telescopes.

## Advanced results [Master]

**Theorem 1 (Riemann-Darboux equivalence).** A bounded function $f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is Darboux integrable if and only if it is Riemann integrable, and the two integrals coincide.

A Riemann sum for $f$ with respect to a tagged partition $(P, \xi)$ is $S(f,P,\xi) = \sum_{i=1}^n f(\xi_i)\Delta x_i$ where $\xi_i \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]$. The function is Riemann integrable if these sums converge to a unique limit as $\|P\| \to 0$, independent of tag choices. Since $L(f,P) \le S(f,P,\xi) \le U(f,P)$ for any tagging, the two definitions agree when the Darboux condition holds.

**Theorem 2 (Continuous functions are integrable).** Every continuous $f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is Darboux integrable.

Proved in the Key theorem section above via uniform continuity and the Heine-Cantor theorem.

**Theorem 3 (Monotone functions are integrable).** Every monotone function $f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is Darboux integrable.

A monotone function has bounded variation and at most countably many discontinuities. The proof in the Full proof set uses the telescoping property of the Darboux gap.

**Theorem 4 (Lebesgue's criterion for Riemann integrability).** A bounded function $f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is Riemann-Darboux integrable if and only if the set of discontinuities of $f$ has Lebesgue measure zero.

This is the definitive characterisation. The forward direction constructs a partition covering the discontinuity set by intervals of total length less than $\epsilon$ and uses uniform continuity on the complement. The reverse direction applies the Darboux criterion on the continuity set.

**Theorem 5 (The Dirichlet function is not integrable).** The function $f : [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}$ defined by $f(x) = \mathbf{1}_{\mathbb{Q}}(x)$ is bounded but not Darboux integrable.

The Dirichlet function is discontinuous at every point. Its discontinuity set has full measure $[0,1]$, violating Lebesgue's criterion.

**Theorem 6 (Linearity).** If $f$ and $g$ are Darboux integrable on $[a,b]$ and $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$, then $f + g$ and $\alpha f$ are Darboux integrable with $\int_a^b (f+g)\,dx = \int_a^b f\,dx + \int_a^b g\,dx$ and $\int_a^b (\alpha f)\,dx = \alpha \int_a^b f\,dx$.

The scalar case follows from $\inf(\alpha S) = \alpha \inf S$ for $\alpha > 0$. The sum uses $\sup(f+g) \le \sup f + \sup g$ and the matching infimum inequality, then applies the Darboux criterion.

**Theorem 7 (Additivity over intervals).** If $f$ is Darboux integrable on $[a,b]$ and $c \in (a,b)$, then $f$ is integrable on $[a,c]$ and $[c,b]$, with $\int_a^b f\,dx = \int_a^c f\,dx + \int_c^b f\,dx$.

Any partition of $[a,b]$ containing the point $c$ decomposes into a partition of $[a,c]$ and one of $[c,b]$. The Darboux sums add and the integrability criterion carries over to each subinterval.

**Synthesis.** The Darboux framework is the foundational reason that the integral can be defined without choosing sample points — the upper and lower sums pin the value from both sides. The central insight is that integrability reduces to whether the gap $U(f,P) - L(f,P)$ can be made arbitrarily small, and this is exactly the refinement criterion that identifies the Darboux integral with the Riemann integral. Putting these together, the Lebesgue criterion shows that boundedness plus almost-everywhere continuity is both necessary and sufficient. The bridge is from approximation-by-rectangles to approximation-by-level-sets, and the pattern generalises to the Lebesgue integral, where step functions are replaced by simple functions and interval length by measure.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Proposition (Monotone functions are integrable).** Every monotone function $f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is Darboux integrable.

*Proof.* Assume $f$ is increasing (the decreasing case follows by considering $-f$ and linearity). Given $\epsilon > 0$, choose $n \in \mathbb{N}$ with $n > (f(b) - f(a))(b - a)/\epsilon$. Let $P_n$ be the uniform partition $x_i = a + i(b-a)/n$ for $0 \le i \le n$. Since $f$ is increasing, $M_i = f(x_i)$ and $m_i = f(x_{i-1})$ for each $i$. Then:
$$U(f,P_n) - L(f,P_n) = \sum_{i=1}^n (f(x_i) - f(x_{i-1})) \cdot \frac{b-a}{n} = \frac{b-a}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (f(x_i) - f(x_{i-1})).$$
The sum telescopes: $\sum_{i=1}^n (f(x_i) - f(x_{i-1})) = f(b) - f(a)$. Therefore:
$$U(f,P_n) - L(f,P_n) = \frac{(b-a)(f(b) - f(a))}{n} < \epsilon.$$
By the Darboux criterion, $f$ is integrable. $\square$

**Proposition (The Dirichlet function is not integrable).** Define $f : [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}$ by $f(x) = 1$ for $x \in \mathbb{Q}$ and $f(x) = 0$ for $x \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$. Then $f$ is bounded on $[0,1]$ but not Darboux integrable.

*Proof.* The function $f$ is bounded (it takes only the values $0$ and $1$). Let $P = \{x_0, \ldots, x_n\}$ be any partition of $[0,1]$. Since $\mathbb{Q}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}$, each subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$ contains a rational point, so $M_i \ge 1$. Since $\mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$ is dense, each subinterval contains an irrational point, so $m_i \le 0$. Combined with the bounds $0 \le f \le 1$, this gives $M_i = 1$ and $m_i = 0$ for every $i$. Hence:
$$U(f,P) = \sum_{i=1}^n 1 \cdot \Delta x_i = 1, \qquad L(f,P) = \sum_{i=1}^n 0 \cdot \Delta x_i = 0.$$
The upper integral is $\inf_P U(f,P) = 1$ and the lower integral is $\sup_P L(f,P) = 0$. Since $1 \ne 0$, $f$ is not Darboux integrable. $\square$

## Connections [Master]

- **Infinite series: convergence and the standard tests [02.03.03].** The convergence infrastructure for series — epsilon criterion, comparison arguments, control of tails — is the same machinery that drives the Darboux integrability criterion. Both require showing that a quantity can be made arbitrarily small by choosing a sufficiently fine approximation. The series unit provides the prerequisite analytical maturity for the integral.

- **Continuous map [02.01.02].** The Key theorem proves that every continuous function on a closed bounded interval is Darboux integrable. The proof relies on the Heine-Cantor theorem (uniform continuity of continuous maps on compact domains), which is the central result of the continuous-map unit. The integrability theorem is one of the deepest consequences of continuity in single-variable analysis.

- **Real-number axioms (ordered field) [02.02.01].** The Darboux construction depends on the completeness of $\mathbb{R}$: the upper integral is an infimum and the lower integral is a supremum, both of which exist only because every non-empty bounded-above subset of $\mathbb{R}$ has a least upper bound. Without completeness, the infimum of upper sums and the supremum of lower sums need not exist, and the integral cannot be defined.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

Riemann 1854 introduced the integral via tagged partitions in his Gottingen Habilitationsschrift [ref: TODO_REF Riemann1854], defining integrability by the convergence of Riemann sums as mesh tends to zero. Darboux 1875 reformulated the theory using upper and lower sums in *Ann. Sci. Ecole Norm. Sup.* (2) 4 [ref: TODO_REF Darboux1875], giving a cleaner equivalent definition that avoids the choice of sample points and directly exploits the completeness of $\mathbb{R}$.

Lebesgue 1901 proved the definitive characterisation in *C. R. Acad. Sci. Paris* 132 [ref: TODO_REF Lebesgue1901]: a bounded function on $[a,b]$ is Riemann-integrable if and only if its set of discontinuities has measure zero. This criterion explains why the Dirichlet function (discontinuous everywhere) fails integrability, and motivates the passage to the Lebesgue integral, which handles a larger class of functions by replacing partitions of the domain with partitions of the range.
