## Formal definition of the pack [Intermediate]

Whitney's geometric integration theory rebuilds the calculus of chains and forms so that Stokes' theorem survives for non-smooth objects. The starting algebra is the exterior power: $r$-vectors in $\Lambda_r \mathbb{R}^n$ and $r$-covectors in $\Lambda^r \mathbb{R}^n$ paired by the wedge product and the duality $\langle \alpha, \xi\rangle$. A current is a continuous linear functional $T$ on the space of compactly supported smooth $r$-forms; its boundary $\partial T$ is defined by $\partial T(\omega) = T(d\omega)$, dual to the exterior derivative, so that Stokes' theorem $T(d\omega) = \partial T(\omega)$ holds by definition. The size of a current is measured by its mass $\mathbf{M}$ and, for convergence and approximation, by the flat norm $\mathbf{F}$ (Whitney's $\flat$) and the sharp norm $\sharp$; flat chains are the completion of polyhedral chains in the flat norm. The Whitney extension theorem supplies the smooth functions whose level sets and graphs feed the construction.

This pack collects nine exercises — two easy, four medium, three hard — each with a hint and a full solution. It is meant to be read alongside its prerequisite units. The exercises group loosely by topic: exterior-algebra and wedge-product computations (easy/medium); mass, flat and sharp norms and the boundary operator (medium); and Stokes for currents, the constancy theorem, and the Whitney extension theorem (hard).

Conventions follow Whitney and Federer: $\mathcal{D}^r$ denotes compactly supported smooth $r$-forms and $\mathcal{D}_r$ its dual, the $r$-currents; mass is the comass-dual norm $\mathbf{M}(T) = \sup\{T(\omega) : \|\omega\|_{\text{comass}} \le 1\}$; the boundary lowers dimension by one, $\partial:\mathcal{D}_r \to \mathcal{D}_{r-1}$.

## Key theorem with full solution [Intermediate]

Before the pack proper, we work one exercise in full as an exemplar of the format. The remaining eight follow the same structure (problem, hint, full answer in `<details>` blocks).

**Lead exercise.** *Define the current $\llbracket M \rrbracket$ associated with a compact oriented smooth $m$-submanifold $M \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ with boundary, and prove that $\partial\llbracket M \rrbracket = \llbracket \partial M \rrbracket$.*

**Solution.** A compact oriented $m$-dimensional submanifold $M$ defines an $m$-current $\llbracket M \rrbracket \in \mathcal{D}_m(\mathbb{R}^n)$ by integration of forms:
$$
\llbracket M \rrbracket(\omega) = \int_M \omega, \qquad \omega \in \mathcal{D}^m(\mathbb{R}^n).
$$
This is linear in $\omega$ and continuous (bounded by $\mathbf{M}(\llbracket M \rrbracket) = \mathcal{H}^m(M) < \infty$ times the comass of $\omega$), so it is a genuine current. Its mass is the $m$-area of $M$.

The boundary of a current is **defined** by duality with the exterior derivative: $\partial T(\eta) = T(d\eta)$ for $\eta \in \mathcal{D}^{m-1}$. Applying this to $T = \llbracket M \rrbracket$:
$$
\partial\llbracket M \rrbracket(\eta) = \llbracket M \rrbracket(d\eta) = \int_M d\eta.
$$
By the classical Stokes theorem for the smooth oriented manifold-with-boundary $M$,
$$
\int_M d\eta = \int_{\partial M} \eta = \llbracket \partial M \rrbracket(\eta),
$$
where $\partial M$ carries the boundary orientation. Since this holds for every test form $\eta \in \mathcal{D}^{m-1}$, the currents agree: $\partial\llbracket M \rrbracket = \llbracket \partial M \rrbracket$. $\square$

This is the design principle of currents: the boundary operator is built as the adjoint of $d$ precisely so that Stokes' theorem becomes the definition rather than a theorem to be reproved. Two immediate consequences: $\partial^2 = 0$ (dual to $d^2 = 0$), and the mass of $\partial\llbracket M\rrbracket$ equals $\mathcal{H}^{m-1}(\partial M)$, the area of the boundary.

## Exercises [Intermediate]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, symbolic).** *Wedge of two 1-covectors.*

In $\mathbb{R}^3$ with coordinates $x, y, z$, compute $(dx + 2\,dy)\wedge(3\,dy - dz)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Expand bilinearly using $dx\wedge dx = 0$, $dy\wedge dy = 0$, and anticommutativity $\alpha\wedge\beta = -\beta\wedge\alpha$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Expand:
$$
(dx + 2\,dy)\wedge(3\,dy - dz) = 3\,dx\wedge dy - dx\wedge dz + 6\,dy\wedge dy - 2\,dy\wedge dz.
$$
Since $dy\wedge dy = 0$, the third term vanishes:
$$
= 3\,dx\wedge dy - dx\wedge dz - 2\,dy\wedge dz.
$$
This is the resulting $2$-covector, expressed in the standard basis $\{dx\wedge dy,\ dx\wedge dz,\ dy\wedge dz\}$ of $\Lambda^2\mathbb{R}^3$. Its coefficients $(3, -1, -2)$ are, up to sign convention, the components of the cross product of the two coefficient vectors $(1, 2, 0)$ and $(0, 3, -1)$ — the classical identification $\Lambda^2\mathbb{R}^3 \cong \mathbb{R}^3$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, symbolic).** *Decomposability via the wedge.*

Show that a $2$-covector $\omega \in \Lambda^2\mathbb{R}^4$ is decomposable (a single wedge $\alpha\wedge\beta$) iff $\omega\wedge\omega = 0$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Write $\omega = \sum_{i<j} c_{ij}\,dx_i\wedge dx_j$ and compute $\omega\wedge\omega$ in $\Lambda^4\mathbb{R}^4 \cong \mathbb{R}$; it is a Pfaffian.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In $\mathbb{R}^4$, $\omega = c_{12}\,dx_1 dx_2 + c_{13}\,dx_1 dx_3 + c_{14}\,dx_1 dx_4 + c_{23}\,dx_2 dx_3 + c_{24}\,dx_2 dx_4 + c_{34}\,dx_3 dx_4$ (wedges suppressed). Then
$$
\omega\wedge\omega = 2(c_{12}c_{34} - c_{13}c_{24} + c_{14}c_{23})\,dx_1 dx_2 dx_3 dx_4.
$$
The scalar $\mathrm{Pf}(\omega) = c_{12}c_{34} - c_{13}c_{24} + c_{14}c_{23}$ is the Pfaffian of the skew coefficient matrix.

If $\omega = \alpha\wedge\beta$ is decomposable, then $\omega\wedge\omega = \alpha\wedge\beta\wedge\alpha\wedge\beta = 0$ since $\alpha$ repeats. Conversely, if $\omega\wedge\omega = 0$ then $\mathrm{Pf}(\omega) = 0$, which is exactly the condition for the rank-$2$ skew matrix $(c_{ij})$ to have rank $2$ (not $4$); a rank-$2$ skew form is $\alpha\wedge\beta$ for a suitable pair, so $\omega$ is decomposable. Hence decomposability $\iff \omega\wedge\omega = 0$ in $\Lambda^2\mathbb{R}^4$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="symbolic" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, symbolic).** *Comass of a simple covector.*

Compute the comass $\|\omega\|^*$ of the $2$-covector $\omega = dx_1\wedge dx_2$ on $\mathbb{R}^4$, defined as the supremum of $\langle \omega, \xi\rangle$ over unit simple $2$-vectors $\xi$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

A unit simple $2$-vector is $\xi = u\wedge v$ with $u, v$ orthonormal. $\langle dx_1\wedge dx_2,\ u\wedge v\rangle$ is a $2\times 2$ minor; bound it by the orthonormality.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For orthonormal $u, v \in \mathbb{R}^4$, the pairing is the determinant of the projection onto the $(x_1, x_2)$-plane:
$$
\langle dx_1\wedge dx_2,\ u\wedge v\rangle = \det\begin{pmatrix} u_1 & v_1 \\ u_2 & v_2 \end{pmatrix} = u_1 v_2 - u_2 v_1.
$$
This is the signed area of the parallelogram spanned by the projections of $u$ and $v$ onto the $(x_1,x_2)$-plane. Since projection does not increase area and $u\wedge v$ has unit area, $|u_1 v_2 - u_2 v_1| \le 1$, with equality when $u, v$ lie in the $(x_1,x_2)$-plane (e.g. $u = e_1$, $v = e_2$). Hence
$$
\|\omega\|^* = \sup_{\xi}\langle\omega,\xi\rangle = 1.
$$
The comass of a simple unit covector is $1$; this is the dual norm that makes the mass $\mathbf{M}(T) = \sup\{T(\omega):\|\omega\|^*\le 1\}$ of the current $\llbracket M\rrbracket$ equal the area of $M$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).** *Boundary squares to zero for currents.*

Using the duality definition $\partial T(\eta) = T(d\eta)$, prove $\partial(\partial T) = 0$ for every current $T$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Apply the definition twice and use $d\circ d = 0$ on forms.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $T \in \mathcal{D}_r$ and let $\eta \in \mathcal{D}^{r-2}$ be any test form. Apply the boundary definition twice:
$$
\partial(\partial T)(\eta) = (\partial T)(d\eta) = T\bigl(d(d\eta)\bigr) = T(d^2\eta).
$$
On smooth forms $d^2 = 0$ (the exterior derivative satisfies $d\circ d = 0$), so $d^2\eta = 0$ and therefore $T(d^2\eta) = T(0) = 0$. Since $\eta \in \mathcal{D}^{r-2}$ was arbitrary, $\partial(\partial T) = 0$ as an element of $\mathcal{D}_{r-2}$.

The boundary operator on currents is nilpotent because it is the adjoint of the nilpotent exterior derivative. This makes $(\mathcal{D}_*, \partial)$ a chain complex dual to the de Rham cochain complex $(\mathcal{D}^*, d)$, and it is the abstract reason a boundary has no boundary, generalizing $\partial\partial M = \emptyset$ for manifolds.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="numeric" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, numeric).** *Flat norm of a small loop versus the disk it bounds.*

Let $T = \llbracket \partial D_\varepsilon\rrbracket$ be the boundary $1$-current of the disk $D_\varepsilon$ of radius $\varepsilon$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$. Estimate the flat norm $\mathbf{F}(T)$ and explain why it tends to $0$ as $\varepsilon \to 0$, even though the mass $\mathbf{M}(T)$ does not.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

The flat norm is $\mathbf{F}(T) = \inf\{\mathbf{M}(T - \partial S) + \mathbf{M}(S)\}$ over $S$. Take $S = \llbracket D_\varepsilon\rrbracket$, so $\partial S = T$ and the first term vanishes.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

The flat norm of an $r$-current is
$$
\mathbf{F}(T) = \inf\bigl\{ \mathbf{M}(T - \partial S) + \mathbf{M}(S) : S \in \mathcal{D}_{r+1}\bigr\}.
$$
Choose $S = \llbracket D_\varepsilon\rrbracket$, the disk filling the loop. Then $\partial S = T$, so $T - \partial S = 0$ and $\mathbf{M}(T - \partial S) = 0$. The remaining term is $\mathbf{M}(S) = \mathcal{H}^2(D_\varepsilon) = \pi\varepsilon^2$. Hence
$$
\mathbf{F}(T) \le \pi\varepsilon^2 \xrightarrow[\varepsilon\to 0]{} 0.
$$
By contrast the mass is the length of the loop, $\mathbf{M}(T) = 2\pi\varepsilon$, which also tends to $0$ but only linearly — and a *unit-radius* loop has $\mathbf{F} \le \pi$ while $\mathbf{M} = 2\pi$. The point of the flat norm is that small boundaries are flat-small because they bound small-area regions: it sees that a tiny loop is close to $0$ in the homological sense. This is why the flat norm, not the mass, is the right topology for the compactness and approximation theorems (a flat-Cauchy sequence of polyhedral chains has a flat-chain limit).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (medium, proof).** *Sharp norm bounds the flat norm.*

Recall Whitney's sharp norm $\sharp$ controls how a chain pairs with forms of bounded $C^1$-norm. Show $\mathbf{F}(T) \le \mathbf{M}(T)$ and explain the inclusion of norm topologies $\mathbf{M} \ge \mathbf{F} \ge \sharp$ (mass strongest, sharp weakest).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For $\mathbf{F} \le \mathbf{M}$, take $S = 0$ in the flat-norm infimum.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**$\mathbf{F}(T) \le \mathbf{M}(T)$.** In the infimum $\mathbf{F}(T) = \inf_S\{\mathbf{M}(T - \partial S) + \mathbf{M}(S)\}$, take the admissible choice $S = 0$: this gives $\mathbf{M}(T - 0) + \mathbf{M}(0) = \mathbf{M}(T)$. The infimum is at most this value, so $\mathbf{F}(T) \le \mathbf{M}(T)$.

**Ordering of topologies.** Mass measures a chain against all comass-$\le 1$ forms; the flat norm relaxes this by allowing a piece of the chain to be discarded as the boundary of a small-mass filling, so flat-small chains include all small-area boundaries that mass would still see as large. The sharp norm relaxes further: it tests against forms whose values *and* whose variation (Lipschitz/$C^1$ constant) are bounded, so it tolerates small geometric perturbations (a chain slightly translated is sharp-close to the original). Thus
$$
\mathbf{M}(T) \ge \mathbf{F}(T) \ge c\,\sharp(T)
$$
on bounded sets, with mass the finest and sharp the coarsest. Convergence in mass implies convergence in flat norm implies convergence in sharp norm, but none of the reverse implications holds — which is exactly why each norm exists: mass for size, flat for homological/compactness arguments, sharp for the most flexible continuity.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, proof).** *Stokes for a flat chain.*

Let $T$ be a flat $r$-chain (a limit of polyhedral chains in the flat norm) and $\omega$ a smooth $r$-form with $d\omega$ also smooth. Prove the Stokes identity $T(d\omega) = (\partial T)(\omega)$ persists from polyhedral chains to $T$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For polyhedral chains, $T(d\omega) = \partial T(\omega)$ is classical Stokes. Both sides are continuous in the flat norm; pass to the limit.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For a polyhedral $r$-chain $P$, classical Stokes gives $P(d\omega) = (\partial P)(\omega)$ — the sum over oriented simplices of $\int_\sigma d\omega = \int_{\partial\sigma}\omega$, with interior faces cancelling.

Let $P_k \to T$ in the flat norm, $\mathbf{F}(T - P_k) \to 0$. Two facts make the identity pass to the limit:

1. *Pairing is flat-continuous.* For a fixed smooth $\omega$ with $\|\omega\|^* \le c_0$ and $\|d\omega\|^* \le c_1$, the flat-norm definition gives $|S(\omega)| \le \max(c_0, c_1)\,\mathbf{F}(S)$ for any flat chain $S$. Hence $P_k(d\omega) \to T(d\omega)$ and $P_k(\omega) \to T(\omega)$.
2. *Boundary is flat-continuous.* The boundary operator is $1$-Lipschitz in the flat norm: $\mathbf{F}(\partial S) \le \mathbf{F}(S)$ follows from the definition (any filling for $S$ provides one for $\partial S$). So $\mathbf{F}(\partial T - \partial P_k) = \mathbf{F}(\partial(T - P_k)) \le \mathbf{F}(T - P_k) \to 0$, giving $(\partial P_k)(\omega) \to (\partial T)(\omega)$.

Therefore, passing to the limit in $P_k(d\omega) = (\partial P_k)(\omega)$,
$$
T(d\omega) = \lim_k P_k(d\omega) = \lim_k (\partial P_k)(\omega) = (\partial T)(\omega).
$$
Stokes' theorem holds for every flat chain. This is the payoff of Whitney's construction: the flat-norm completion is designed exactly so the two operations in Stokes — pairing with $d\omega$ and taking the boundary — are continuous, so the smooth identity survives the passage to non-smooth chains.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 8 (hard, proof).** *Constancy theorem in dimension one.*

Let $T$ be a $1$-current in an interval $(a, b) \subset \mathbb{R}$ with $\partial T = 0$ and locally finite mass. Show $T = c\,\llbracket (a,b)\rrbracket$ for a constant $c$ (the one-dimensional constancy theorem).

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

$\partial T = 0$ means $T(f') = 0$ for every compactly supported smooth $f$. Interpret $T$ as a distribution and use that a distribution with zero derivative is a constant.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

A $1$-current on $(a,b)$ acts on $1$-forms $\omega = g\,dx$ with $g \in C_c^\infty(a,b)$; write $T(g\,dx) = \langle \mu, g\rangle$ for a distribution $\mu$ (a measure, by the finite-mass hypothesis). A $0$-form (test function) is $f \in C_c^\infty(a,b)$, with $df = f'\,dx$.

The hypothesis $\partial T = 0$ means $0 = (\partial T)(f) = T(df) = T(f'\,dx) = \langle \mu, f'\rangle$ for all $f \in C_c^\infty(a,b)$. By definition of the distributional derivative, $\langle \mu, f'\rangle = -\langle \mu', f\rangle$, so $\langle \mu', f\rangle = 0$ for all $f$, i.e. $\mu' = 0$ as a distribution.

A distribution on an interval with zero derivative is a constant: $\mu = c$ for some $c \in \mathbb{R}$. (Proof: pick $f_0$ with $\int f_0 = 1$; any $g$ writes as $g = (\int g)f_0 + h'$ with $h \in C_c^\infty$, and $\langle \mu, g\rangle = (\int g)\langle\mu, f_0\rangle$ since $\langle\mu, h'\rangle = -\langle\mu', h\rangle = 0$; so $\langle\mu, g\rangle = c\int g$ with $c = \langle\mu, f_0\rangle$.) Therefore $T(g\,dx) = c\int_a^b g\,dx = c\,\llbracket(a,b)\rrbracket(g\,dx)$, i.e. $T = c\,\llbracket(a,b)\rrbracket$.

This is the simplest case of Federer's constancy theorem: a current of top dimension in a connected open set with zero boundary is a constant multiple of the ambient orientation current.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 9 (hard, proof).** *Whitney extension on a closed set.*

State the Whitney extension theorem for $C^1$ data and use it to show that any closed set $K \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ is the zero set of some $C^\infty$ function $f:\mathbb{R}^n \to [0,\infty)$.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

For the zero-set claim, the relevant Whitney result is the smooth Urysohn/zero-set construction: cover the open complement by countably many balls and sum bump functions. State the $C^1$ extension theorem, then give the zero-set corollary.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Whitney extension theorem ($C^1$ form).** Given a closed set $K \subset \mathbb{R}^n$, a function $f_0:K \to \mathbb{R}$, and a candidate "derivative" field $L:K \to (\mathbb{R}^n)^*$ satisfying the first-order Whitney compatibility condition
$$
f_0(y) - f_0(x) - L(x)(y - x) = o(|y - x|) \quad \text{uniformly as } |y-x|\to 0,\ x,y\in K,
$$
there exists $F \in C^1(\mathbb{R}^n)$ with $F|_K = f_0$ and $DF|_K = L$. The extension is built by interpolating $f_0$ across the complement using a Whitney partition of unity subordinate to a dyadic cube decomposition of $\mathbb{R}^n\setminus K$.

**Zero-set corollary.** Let $K$ be closed and $U = \mathbb{R}^n\setminus K$ open. Apply the Whitney cube decomposition of $U$: countably many dyadic cubes $\{Q_i\}$ with $\mathrm{diam}\,Q_i \approx \mathrm{dist}(Q_i, K)$ and a subordinate $C^\infty$ partition of unity $\{\varphi_i\}$, $\mathrm{supp}\,\varphi_i \subset 2Q_i \subset U$. Choose weights $c_i > 0$ small enough (depending on $\sup$-norms of derivatives of $\varphi_i$ on $Q_i$) that
$$
f = \sum_i c_i\,\varphi_i
$$
converges in $C^\infty$ on $U$; the controlled decay near $\partial U$ makes $f$ and all its derivatives extend by $0$ across $K$, giving $f \in C^\infty(\mathbb{R}^n)$ with $f > 0$ on $U$ and $f \equiv 0$ on $K$ (and one may take $f \ge 0$ throughout). Hence $K = f^{-1}(0)$: every closed set is the zero set of a nonnegative smooth function. This is the existence-of-smooth-cutoffs facet of Whitney's theory, the same machinery that supplies the smooth functions parametrizing the chains and currents of the rest of the pack.

</details>

</aside>
