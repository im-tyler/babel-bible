## Intuition [Beginner]

A *metric space* is a set together with a notion of distance between any two points — a function $d(x, y)$ that returns a non-negative real number, equals zero exactly when $x = y$, is symmetric in its inputs, and satisfies the triangle inequality.

That last condition — the triangle inequality $d(x, z) \leq d(x, y) + d(y, z)$ — is the geometric heart of the definition. It says: going through an intermediate point can never be shorter than going directly. Without it, "distance" wouldn't behave the way our spatial intuition requires.

From a metric you build a topology: a set is open if every point has a small ball around it contained in the set. Continuity, convergence, completeness, and compactness all then have natural metric formulations. Metric spaces are the most familiar topological spaces — and they are the bridge from set-theoretic topology to honest analytic geometry.

## Visual [Beginner]

A scattered set of points with a distance function. Any three points satisfy the triangle inequality: the direct distance from one to a third is no longer than going through the middle one.

![Three points in a metric space, with three pairwise distances satisfying the triangle inequality.](/img/metric-space-placeholder.svg)

The triangle inequality is what makes "balls" — sets of all points within a fixed distance of a centre — well-behaved. Open balls form a basis for the metric topology.

## Worked example [Beginner]

The plane $\mathbb{R}^2$ with the **Euclidean distance** $d((x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2)) = \sqrt{(x_1 - x_2)^2 + (y_1 - y_2)^2}$ is the prototype metric space.

But there are many metrics on $\mathbb{R}^2$:

- **Taxicab metric**: $d((x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2)) = |x_1 - x_2| + |y_1 - y_2|$ — distance you'd travel along a grid of streets.
- **Chebyshev metric**: $d = \max(|x_1 - x_2|, |y_1 - y_2|)$ — chessboard king's-move distance.
- **Discrete metric**: $d(p, q) = 1$ if $p \neq q$ and $0$ otherwise.

Each is a valid metric. The first three give *the same topology* on $\mathbb{R}^2$ (open sets and continuous functions agree), even though the actual distance values differ. The discrete metric gives a different topology, where every singleton $\{p\}$ is an open ball of radius $1/2$.

So distance and topology are linked but not identical: many metrics produce the same topology, and a topology can be metrisable in many ways.

## Formal definition [Intermediate+]

A **metric space** is a pair $(X, d)$ where $X$ is a set and $d : X \times X \to [0, \infty)$ is a function (the **metric** or **distance function**) satisfying, for all $x, y, z \in X$:

1. **Positive-definiteness**: $d(x, y) = 0 \iff x = y$.
2. **Symmetry**: $d(x, y) = d(y, x)$.
3. **Triangle inequality**: $d(x, z) \leq d(x, y) + d(y, z)$.

The **open ball** of radius $r > 0$ centered at $p \in X$ is $B(p, r) := \{x \in X : d(p, x) < r\}$.

The **metric topology** on $X$ is generated by open balls: a set $U \subseteq X$ is open iff for every $p \in U$ there is $r > 0$ with $B(p, r) \subseteq U$. This is a topology (axioms verified directly), making every metric space a topological space [02.01.01].

A topological space whose topology arises from some metric is called **metrisable**.

A sequence $(x_n)_{n \geq 1}$ in $(X, d)$ **converges to $x \in X$** if $d(x_n, x) \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. The sequence is **Cauchy** if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $N$ such that $d(x_n, x_m) < \varepsilon$ for all $n, m \geq N$. Every convergent sequence is Cauchy, but not every Cauchy sequence converges in the same space (e.g., the rational numbers under standard distance are not complete).

A metric space is **complete** if every Cauchy sequence converges (to a limit in the space).

A function $f : (X, d_X) \to (Y, d_Y)$ between metric spaces is:

- **Continuous** at $p$: for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there is $\delta > 0$ such that $d_X(x, p) < \delta \implies d_Y(f(x), f(p)) < \varepsilon$.
- **Uniformly continuous**: the $\delta$ can be chosen independently of $p$.
- **Lipschitz**: there is $K > 0$ with $d_Y(f(x), f(y)) \leq K \cdot d_X(x, y)$ for all $x, y$.
- **Isometry**: $d_Y(f(x), f(y)) = d_X(x, y)$ for all $x, y$.

## Key theorem with proof [Intermediate+]

**Theorem (the metric topology is a topology).** *Let $(X, d)$ be a metric space. The collection of subsets $\tau_d := \{U \subseteq X : \forall p \in U, \exists r > 0 \text{ s.t. } B(p, r) \subseteq U\}$ is a topology on $X$.*

**Proof.** Verify the three topology axioms:

*$\emptyset, X \in \tau_d$:* The empty set vacuously satisfies the condition. For $X$: any point $p$ has $B(p, 1) \subseteq X$. So both are open.

*Arbitrary unions:* Let $\{U_\alpha\}_{\alpha \in A} \subseteq \tau_d$. For $p \in \bigcup_\alpha U_\alpha$, $p \in U_\alpha$ for some $\alpha$, so there is $r > 0$ with $B(p, r) \subseteq U_\alpha \subseteq \bigcup U_\alpha$. Hence $\bigcup U_\alpha \in \tau_d$.

*Finite intersections:* Let $U_1, U_2 \in \tau_d$. For $p \in U_1 \cap U_2$, there are $r_1, r_2 > 0$ with $B(p, r_1) \subseteq U_1$ and $B(p, r_2) \subseteq U_2$. Set $r := \min(r_1, r_2) > 0$. Then $B(p, r) \subseteq B(p, r_1) \cap B(p, r_2) \subseteq U_1 \cap U_2$, so $U_1 \cap U_2 \in \tau_d$. $\square$

The proof is mechanical, but it's the proof: every metric space is a topological space via the metric topology. The converse — when does a topology come from a metric? — is the *metrisation problem*, addressed by Urysohn's metrisation theorem and its generalisations.

**Bridge.** The construction here builds toward [02.11.04] (banach space fundamentals), where the same data is upgraded, and the symmetry side is taken up in [02.11.08] (hilbert space). The defining pattern appears again in those units in a sharpened form, where the local data is glued or quotiented. Putting these together, the foundational insight is that the data of this unit gives the structural signature that the rest of the strand reads off.

## Exercises [Intermediate+]

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 1 (easy, proof).**

Show that the discrete metric $d(x, y) = 1$ if $x \neq y$, $d(x, y) = 0$ if $x = y$, satisfies the triangle inequality.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Check the four cases based on whether the three points coincide.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

For three points $x, y, z$: if all are distinct, $d(x, z) = 1$ and $d(x, y) + d(y, z) = 2$, so $d(x, z) \leq d(x, y) + d(y, z)$ holds. If $x = z$, $d(x, z) = 0 \leq d(x, y) + d(y, z)$. If $y = x$ (or $y = z$), $d(x, y) = 0$ (or $d(y, z) = 0$) and the right side is at least $d(x, z)$ since $d \leq 1$. All cases check.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="easy" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 2 (easy, proof).**

Show that every convergent sequence in a metric space is Cauchy.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the triangle inequality and convergence to bound $d(x_n, x_m)$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Suppose $x_n \to x$. Given $\varepsilon > 0$, pick $N$ with $d(x_n, x) < \varepsilon/2$ for $n \geq N$. For $n, m \geq N$,

$$
d(x_n, x_m) \leq d(x_n, x) + d(x, x_m) < \varepsilon/2 + \varepsilon/2 = \varepsilon.
$$

So $(x_n)$ is Cauchy.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 3 (medium, proof).**

Show that the Euclidean and taxicab metrics on $\mathbb{R}^n$ generate the same topology.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Show that every Euclidean ball contains a taxicab ball, and vice versa.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $d_2$ denote Euclidean and $d_1$ denote taxicab. By Cauchy-Schwarz / norm-equivalence, $d_2(x, y) \leq d_1(x, y) \leq \sqrt{n} \cdot d_2(x, y)$. So a Euclidean ball of radius $r/\sqrt{n}$ is contained in a taxicab ball of radius $r$, and a taxicab ball of radius $r$ is contained in a Euclidean ball of radius $r$. Each metric's open balls form a basis for the same topology.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 4 (medium, proof).**

Show that a Cauchy sequence with a convergent subsequence is itself convergent.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Use the triangle inequality with both the Cauchy bound and the subsequence-limit bound.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Let $(x_n)$ be Cauchy with subsequence $(x_{n_k}) \to x$. Given $\varepsilon > 0$, pick $N_1$ with $d(x_n, x_m) < \varepsilon/2$ for $n, m \geq N_1$, and $K$ with $d(x_{n_k}, x) < \varepsilon/2$ for $k \geq K$. For $n \geq N_1$, choose $k$ with $n_k \geq N_1$ and $k \geq K$. Then $d(x_n, x) \leq d(x_n, x_{n_k}) + d(x_{n_k}, x) < \varepsilon/2 + \varepsilon/2 = \varepsilon$. So $x_n \to x$.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="medium" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 5 (medium, conceptual).**

Why is the metric topology *not* always Hausdorff if the metric is replaced by a *pseudo-metric* (a function satisfying the metric axioms except that $d(x, y) = 0$ allows $x \neq y$)?

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Hausdorff requires the ability to separate distinct points by disjoint open sets.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

In a pseudo-metric space, two distinct points $x, y$ might satisfy $d(x, y) = 0$. Then every open ball around $x$ contains $y$, and vice versa, so $x$ and $y$ cannot be separated by disjoint opens. The topology fails to be Hausdorff. The remedy is to quotient by the equivalence $x \sim y \iff d(x, y) = 0$, which produces a genuine metric space (a *Kolmogorov quotient*).

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="proof" markdown="1">

**Exercise 6 (hard, proof).**

Prove that every metric space has a unique completion: a complete metric space $(\widetilde{X}, \widetilde{d})$ together with an isometric embedding $X \hookrightarrow \widetilde{X}$ whose image is dense.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Construct $\widetilde{X}$ as equivalence classes of Cauchy sequences in $X$.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

Construction: Let $\widetilde{X}$ be the set of equivalence classes of Cauchy sequences in $X$, where $(x_n) \sim (y_n)$ iff $d(x_n, y_n) \to 0$. Define $\widetilde{d}([x_n], [y_n]) := \lim_n d(x_n, y_n)$ (well-defined and limits exist by Cauchy-ness). The map $X \to \widetilde{X}$, $x \mapsto [\text{constant sequence } x]$ is an isometric embedding with dense image (every $[x_n]$ is the limit of constant sequences as $n \to \infty$).

Completeness: a Cauchy sequence in $\widetilde{X}$ has a representative whose diagonal Cauchy sequence in $X$ converges to a class in $\widetilde{X}$.

Uniqueness: any two completions are isometric via the identity map on the dense image, extended uniformly continuously to the entire completion.

</details>

</aside>

<aside class="exercise" data-difficulty="hard" data-type="conceptual" markdown="1">

**Exercise 7 (hard, conceptual).**

State the Banach fixed-point theorem and explain why it's central to metric-space theory.

<details class="exercise__hint"><summary>Hint</summary>

Contractions on complete metric spaces.

</details>

<details class="exercise__answer"><summary>Answer</summary>

**Banach fixed-point theorem.** Let $(X, d)$ be a complete metric space and $f : X \to X$ a *contraction*: there exists $K \in [0, 1)$ such that $d(f(x), f(y)) \leq K \cdot d(x, y)$ for all $x, y$. Then $f$ has a unique fixed point $x^\ast \in X$, and for any $x_0 \in X$, the sequence $x_0, f(x_0), f(f(x_0)), \ldots$ converges to $x^\ast$.

The theorem is the fundamental existence-uniqueness theorem for many problems: ordinary differential equations (Picard-Lindelöf), implicit function theorem, Newton's method convergence. It is essentially "iteration of contractions on complete spaces always succeeds." Completeness is essential: without it, the iteration could fail to converge to a point of $X$.

</details>

</aside>

## Advanced results [Master]

**Banach fixed-point theorem.** Stated in Exercise 7. Used throughout functional analysis (existence/uniqueness of solutions to integral and differential equations) and inverse-function-style theorems.

**Heine-Borel theorem.** A subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ is compact iff it is closed and bounded. Generalises to: in a metric space, a subset is compact iff it is *complete and totally bounded*.

**Arzelà-Ascoli theorem.** A subset $\mathcal{F}$ of $C(X, Y)$ (continuous functions from a compact metric space $X$ to a metric space $Y$) is relatively compact in the uniform topology iff it is bounded and *equicontinuous*. This is the foundational tool for compactness arguments in function spaces.

**Stone-Weierstrass theorem.** Continuous functions on a compact metric space can be uniformly approximated by elements of any subalgebra that separates points and contains a non-vanishing function.

**Baire category theorem.** A complete metric space cannot be written as a countable union of nowhere-dense sets. This is the foundation of the open mapping, closed graph, and uniform boundedness principles in functional analysis [02.11.01].

**Metrisation theorems.** Urysohn (1925): a regular Hausdorff space with a countable basis is metrisable. Nagata-Smirnov (1950s): a topological space is metrisable iff it is regular Hausdorff with a countably locally finite basis. These characterise *which* topological spaces arise from a metric.

**Hausdorff distance.** For two compact subsets $A, B$ of a metric space, the Hausdorff distance $d_H(A, B) := \max(\sup_{a \in A} d(a, B), \sup_{b \in B} d(b, A))$ makes the space of compact subsets a metric space itself. Foundation for geometric measure theory and shape analysis.

**Synthesis.** This construction generalises the pattern fixed in [02.01.01] (topological space), with the symmetric data replaced by its skew or twisted analogue. Read in the opposite direction, the construction is dual to the metric story: complements and orthogonality are taken with respect to the bilinear datum of this unit, not a metric, and the resulting category of subobjects is the one the rest of the strand classifies. The central insight is that this datum identifies algebra with geometry: functions become vector fields, subspaces become quotients, and invariants become cohomology classes — and that identification is the engine driving every theorem downstream.

## Full proof set [Master]

**Metric topology axioms.** Proved in §"Key theorem".

**Banach fixed-point theorem.** Define $x_n := f^n(x_0)$. Then $d(x_{n+1}, x_n) \leq K \cdot d(x_n, x_{n-1}) \leq K^n \cdot d(x_1, x_0)$. Sum: for $m > n$, $d(x_m, x_n) \leq \sum_{k=n}^{m-1} d(x_{k+1}, x_k) \leq K^n / (1 - K) \cdot d(x_1, x_0) \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. Cauchy in complete $X$, so converges to some $x^\ast$. Continuity of $f$ gives $f(x^\ast) = \lim f(x_n) = \lim x_{n+1} = x^\ast$. Uniqueness: if $f(y^\ast) = y^\ast$ too, $d(x^\ast, y^\ast) = d(f(x^\ast), f(y^\ast)) \leq K \cdot d(x^\ast, y^\ast)$, forcing $d(x^\ast, y^\ast) = 0$.

**Completion construction.** Proved in Exercise 6.

**Heine-Borel for $\mathbb{R}^n$.** A bounded subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$ has bounded sequences with subsequential limits (Bolzano-Weierstrass); a closed bounded set thus has every sequence contain a subsequence converging in the set, which is compactness in the metric topology. Conversely, compact subsets of metric spaces are closed (Hausdorff) and bounded (totally bounded → bounded).

**Baire (sketch).** For complete $X$ and a countable union of nowhere-dense sets $\bigcup F_n$, recursively construct a nested sequence of closed balls $\overline{B}_n \subseteq \overline{B}_{n-1}$ with $\overline{B}_n \cap F_n = \emptyset$ and radius $\to 0$. The sequence of centres is Cauchy, hence convergent (by completeness) to a point not in any $F_n$. So $\bigcup F_n \neq X$.

## Historical & philosophical context [Master]

The notion of a metric space was introduced by Maurice Fréchet in his 1906 thesis, generalising the Euclidean distance to abstract sets. Hausdorff's 1914 *Grundzüge der Mengenlehre* placed metric and topological spaces side by side, distinguishing the two. The early-twentieth-century insight was that *distance* and *neighbourhood* are different notions, and topology is the more general one.

The interplay between metric and topological structure is a recurring theme: complete metric spaces are particularly tractable (Banach fixed-point, Baire category), but not every topological space is metrisable. The 1925 Urysohn metrisation theorem and its 1950s refinements (Nagata-Smirnov, Bing) gave necessary and sufficient conditions for metrisability.

In modern analysis, metric spaces are the natural setting for fixed-point theorems, function-space approximation results, and (after Banach) the foundations of functional analysis. The completion construction abstracts the way the reals are built from the rationals — a process that recurs in $L^p$-spaces, distribution spaces, and many other constructions.
